# Bava Metzia 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Worker Eating Rights

### Mishnah Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אוֹכֵל פּוֹעֵל קִישּׁוּת אֲפִילּוּ בְּדִינָר, כּוֹתֶבֶת וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדִינָר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חַסָּמָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא יֹאכַל פּוֹעֵל יָתֵר עַל שְׂכָרוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין. אֲבָל מְלַמְּדִין אֶת הָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא רַעַבְתָן וִיהֵא סוֹתֵם אֶת הַפֶּתַח בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A laborer may eat cucumbers** while he works, and this is the *halakha* **even** if the amount he eats is equal in value **to a dinar;** or he may eat **dates, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if the amount he eats is equal in value **to a dinar. Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma says: A laborer may not eat more than** the value of **his wages, but the Rabbis permit** it, according to the strict letter of the law. **But one teaches a person not to be a glutton and thereby close the opening** to other job offers **in his face.** When people hear of his greed they will be reluctant to hire him.

The daf opens with the *mishnah*'s declaration that a worker may eat from the produce on which he labors even if the value of what he consumes far exceeds his wage. The *mishnah* gives two examples: *ochel po'el kishut afilu b'dinar* — a worker may eat cucumbers worth even a *dinar* — and similarly a *koseves*, a pressed date, even if worth a *dinar*. The point is not merely that he may eat; he may eat even more than he is being paid for his work.

### Eliezer vs. Sages

Rabbi Eliezer ben Chisma dissents: *lo yochal po'el yoter al s'charo* — the worker may not eat beyond the value of his actual pay. The Sages, however, permit it, though they add a moral instruction: *aval m'lamdin adam shelo yehei ra'avtan* — one ought to teach the worker not to be a glutton, for while he technically has the right, it is not in his long-term interest. *V'sosem es hapesach lifanav* — he will be slamming the door in his own face, since no employer will want to hire an overly voracious worker in the future. The permission stands, but wisdom counsels restraint.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ חֲכָמִים הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ אֲבָל מְלַמְּדִין: לְתַנָּא קַמָּא לֵית לֵיהּ ״מְלַמְּדִין״. לְרַבָּנַן אִית לְהוּ ״מְלַמְּדִין״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: The statement of **the Rabbis is** identical to the statement of **the first *tanna*.** A mishna would not repeat the exact same opinion. The Gemara explains: The practical difference **between them** concerns the statement: **But one teaches** a person not to be a glutton. **According to the first *tanna*, he does not** accept the notion that **one teaches** a person not to be a glutton. **According to the Rabbis, they do** accept this principle that **one teaches** a person not to be a glutton.

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: the Sages sound identical to the *Tanna Kamma*, the anonymous first voice of the *mishnah*, since both permit the worker to eat beyond his fee. The *gemara* resolves this by locating the genuine dispute: *ika beineihu aval m'lamdin* — the difference is precisely whether one adds the instruction about gluttony. The *Tanna Kamma* does not include this teaching — whether from indifference or from a view that it is not the community's business to instruct him — while the Sages hold that we do instruct him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּרַב אַסִּי. דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא שְׂכָרוֹ אֶלָּא לִבְצוֹר אֶשְׁכּוֹל אֶחָד – אוֹכְלוֹ. וְאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא בָּצַר אֶלָּא אֶשְׁכּוֹל אֶחָד – אוֹכְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead **that** the practical difference **between them** concerns a *halakha* taught **by Rav Asi. As Rav Asi says: Even if he hired him to harvest only one cluster,** the laborer **may eat. And Rav Asi** further **said: Even if he harvested only one cluster, he may eat it.**

The *gemara* then adds a second possibility: *iba'is eima ika beineihu d'Rav Assi* — alternatively, the *mishnah*'s debate may hinge on the position of Rav Assi, which the *gemara* now introduces as an independent matter that the *mishnah*'s disputants may implicitly be debating.

## Rav Assi's Cases

### Single Cluster Case

Rav Assi rules: *afilu lo secharo ela livtzor eshkol echad, ochlo* — even if one is hired only to harvest a single cluster of grapes, the worker may eat the entire cluster. The case is striking: the worker is hired precisely to pick that one cluster, and as he is picking it he pops it into his mouth. He can eat not merely more than his fee; he can eat the very produce he was hired to gather.

### Whole Day Case

Rav Assi adds a second ruling: *afilu lo batzar ela eshkol echad, ochlo* — even if the worker is hired to work the entire vineyard for the full day but has so far picked only a single cluster, he may eat that first cluster. At first glance this seems far less of a *chiddush* than the first case. After all, this worker is going to fill the owner's basket regardless; why should he be entitled to stuff himself before he has even gotten through a second cluster?

### Necessity of Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אִיכָּא לְמִיתַּב לְכֵלָיו שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיתַּב לְכֵלָיו שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת – אֵימָא לִיתֵּב בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר לֵיכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it was **necessary** for Rav Asi to state both of these *halakhot*, despite their apparent similarity. **As, had he taught us** only **this first one,** one might have thought that he may eat **because there is no** other food **to place in the homeowner's vessels,** as he was hired to harvest only a single cluster. The Torah permits him to eat, and if he is not allowed to eat that cluster, what else is there for him to eat? **But if there is** produce left over **to place in the homeowner's vessels,** as in the second case, one might **say** that **he should first place** some in the vessels **and then eat.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא – דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְקַיּוֹמֵי לְבַסּוֹף, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לְקַיּוֹמֵי לְבַסּוֹף – אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** Rav Asi **taught us** only **this** second case, one might have said that the reason he may eat is **that ultimately it is possible to fulfill** the requirement to place produce in the owner's vessels, i.e., he can eat and still perform the task. **But** in a situation **where ultimately it is not possible to fulfill** his task, since if he were to eat the only cluster he was hired to harvest there would be nothing left for him to do, one might **say** that he may **not** eat. Therefore, both *halakhot* are **necessary.**

The *gemara* explains why both cases are necessary. Had we heard only the first case — hired for one cluster — one could argue that the reason he eats the whole thing is simply *mishum d'lo ika lemsav l'keilei d'ba'al habayis*: there is nothing to put into the owner's basket anyway. Since it is a binary choice between the owner and the worker, the worker chooses himself, and that is defensible. But in the second case, *ika lemsav l'keilei d'ba'al habayis* — the owner's basket will be filled — and one might have said *eima leisav b'reisha v'hadar leichal*: first do your work, put the first cluster in the basket, and eat later. You should pace yourself rather than take the first cluster for yourself.

Conversely, had we only heard the second case — hired for the full day — one might understand why it is permitted: *d'efshar l'kayem l'shneihem l'vasof*, in the end the owner will get something too, so eating the first cluster seems harmless. But one might have thought that in the first case, where the owner will get nothing at all from the remaining harvest — *d'lo efshar l'kayem l'shneihem l'vasof* — perhaps the worker would not be allowed. Hence *tzricha*, both are necessary, and Rav Assi's ruling is that in either case, the worker may eat that first cluster.

The *gemara* then notes that perhaps the underlying dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and the Sages in the *mishnah* tracks this debate about Rav Assi's position.

## Hidden Scroll Debate

### Issi's View Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּרַב. דְּאָמַר רַב: מָצָאתִי מְגִילַּת סְתָרִים בֵּי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְכָתוּב בָּהּ: אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי תָבֹא בְּכֶרֶם רֵעֶךָ״ – בְּבִיאַת כׇּל אָדָם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the dispute of the mishna: **If you wish, say that the** practical difference **between** the opinions of the first *tanna* and the Rabbis concerns a *halakha* taught **by Rav. As Rav says: I found a concealed scroll,** a document that lists *halakhot* in shortened form so that they will not be forgotten. Rav discovered this document **in Rabbi Ḥiyya's house, and it was written in it: Isi ben Yehuda says** that with regard to the verse: **"When you come into your neighbor's vineyard** then you may eat grapes until you have enough at your own pleasure" (Deuteronomy 23:25), **the verse is speaking of the entry of any person** who passes alongside a vineyard, not only a laborer.

The *gemara* introduces yet another possible axis of the *mishnah*'s debate: *amar Rav matzati megilat setarim bei Rabbi Chiya* — Rav declared that he found a hidden scroll in the house of Rabbi Chiya. Rashi explains that such scrolls were concealed not out of fear of legal consequences but out of reluctance to have *Torah she-be'al peh* become a written, public, authoritative text akin to *Torah she-bichtav* — writing down the oral tradition was considered both intellectually and spiritually problematic, as it hardened and froze what was meant to remain fluid and living. Items recorded in such scrolls were sometimes radical positions. This one certainly was: *Issi ben Yehuda omer ki tavo b'kerem re'echa b'vi'at kol adam hakatuv medaber* — Issi ben Yehuda reads the verse "when you come into your fellow's vineyard" as referring to any person at all, not merely a hired worker. Anyone, according to this reading, may enter a friend's vineyard and eat.

### Rav Rejects Issi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב: לָא שְׁבַק אִיסִי חַיֵּי לְכׇל בְּרִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav said** in response: **Isi has not left any livelihood for any entity,** as many people might pass by and consume all the fruit of one's vineyard. The first *tanna* agrees with Rav, while the Rabbis accept Isi ben Yehuda's opinion that by right even one who is not a laborer may eat.

Rav reacted sharply: *lo shavki Issi chayei l'chol birya* — Issi's position would not allow anyone to survive. If any person could walk into any field and begin eating the produce, farming would become an impossibility. Not everyone fences in their fields — one does not fence in a field of wheat — and a society cannot function on such a basis.

### Kahana's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: דִּלְמָא בְּעוֹשִׂין בִּסְעוּדָּתָם, דְּעָבְדוּ וְאָכְלוּ? אָמַר לִי: אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְאִינִישׁ (לְאוֹגַר אָגוֹרֵי) [דְּנוֹגַר אֲגִירֵי] וְנִיקְטְפֵיהּ לְפַרְדֵּיסֵיהּ, וְלָא נֵיתוֹ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא (וְאָכְלוּ לֵיהּ).
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stated** this ***halakha* before Rav Kahana,** and I suggested that **perhaps** Isi ben Yehuda was referring to laborers **who perform** labor **for their meal; that** is, **they** voluntarily enter his vineyard to **perform** labor **and eat.** In other words, Isi ben Yehuda did not mean that anyone may help themselves to produce. Rather, if one chooses to perform labor in the vineyard of another, he may eat from his grapes even if he was not hired by the owner. Rav Kahana **said to me: Even so, a person prefers to hire** laborers **to pluck** the fruit of **his orchard, rather than** have **everyone come and eat it,** as he fears that people he did not hire might not perform the work properly.

Rav Ashi reports that he related this exchange to Rav Kahana, who offered a reinterpretation: *dilma b'osim b'seudatam d'avdi v'achli* — perhaps Issi ben Yehuda does not mean that a random passerby may simply enter and eat. Rather, his position may be that a person may self-appoint as a worker — seeing grapes that need to be picked, entering the vineyard, doing the work, and feeding himself in the process. It is not trespassing and eating; it is uninvited labor with incidental eating. This represents a middle ground between explicit employment and outright foraging.

### Ashi's Problem

Rav Ashi himself was not satisfied with this reinterpretation. Even granting Rav Kahana's reading, *nicha leh l'inish l'ogurei agurei u'lichdafei l'fardiseih* — any vineyard owner would vastly prefer to hire workers for pay and manage his orchard in an orderly fashion, *v'lo leisu kulei alma v'achlu leh* — rather than have a free-for-all in which anyone can wander in, declare themselves a worker, and eat from his crop. Even the softened version of Issi's position creates practical chaos. The debate in the *mishnah*, then, may reflect these competing orientations — the more liberal approach represented by the *Tanna Kamma* and the more conservative stance of the Sages.

## Mishlo vs. Mishamayim

### Core Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פּוֹעֵל מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, אוֹ מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In the case of **a laborer** who eats while performing labor, does **he eat from his own** property, i.e., is the food he eats in addition to his wages and therefore considered his private property, **or** does **he eat from** the property **of Heaven?** In other words, perhaps the Torah granted him the right to eat the food with which he works as a special privilege, but it does not belong to him.

The *gemara* now articulates one of the most important conceptual questions of the entire sugya: *po'el mishelo hu ochel o mishel Shamayim hu ochel?* — When a worker eats from the produce as he labors, is he eating what is effectively his own — a benefit treated as part of his wages, coming from the employer as part of the employment arrangement — or is he eating by virtue of a special right granted directly by the Torah, independent of his agreement with the employer?

The distinction matters practically and conceptually. If the right is *mishelo* — his own, a component of his salary — then it belongs to the same framework of negotiation between employer and employee, and it would presumably be alienable and assignable like any other form of compensation. If it is *mishel Shamayim* — given by Heaven — then the Torah itself grants this right to the worker while he works, and it belongs to him personally in a way that cannot be transferred or bargained away by others.

### Wage Limit Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? דְּאָמַר: תְּנוּ לְאִשְׁתִּי וּבָנַי. אִי אָמְרַתְּ: מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל – יָהֲבִינַן לְהוּ, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל – לְדִידֵיהּ זַכִּי לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, לְאִשְׁתּוֹ וּבָנָיו – לָא זַכִּי לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** raised by this dilemma? The Gemara answers: The difference is in a case **where he says:** I myself will not eat, but I will **give** the produce **to my wife and children** in my stead. **If you say** that **he eats from his own** property, **we give them** the food, as it belongs to him, **but if you say** that **he eats from** the property **of Heaven, the Merciful One entitles** the laborer **himself** to eat, **but the Merciful One does not entitle his wife and children** to do so. **What,** then, is the *halakha*?

The *gemara* proposes a *nafka mina*: what if the worker says *t'nu l'ishti u'vanai* — give this to my wife and children rather than to me? If the right is *mishelo* and part of his salary, he ought to be able to reassign it as he sees fit; if it is *mishel Shamayim*, the Torah gave it specifically to him and his family has no share in it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אוֹכֵל פּוֹעֵל קִישּׁוּת וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדִינָר, כּוֹתֶבֶת וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדִינָר. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל – אֲגִיר בְּדַנְקָא אָכֵיל בְּזוּזָא?! וְאֶלָּא מַאי – מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל, סוֹף סוֹף אֲגִיר בְּדַנְקָא אָכֵיל בְּזוּזָא! אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – רַחֲמָנָא זַכִּי לֵיהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי רַחֲמָנָא זַכִּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: **A laborer may eat cucumbers, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if the amount he eats is equal in value **to a dinar;** or he may eat **dates, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if the amount he eats is equal in value **to a dinar. If you say** that **he eats from his own** property, is it possible that **he was hired for one-sixth** of a dinar and yet **he may eat** an amount worth a whole **dinar?** Would the Torah have granted him ownership over such a large sum relative to his wages? The Gemara refutes this argument: **Rather, what** then will you say? Will you say that **he eats from** the property **of Heaven? Ultimately,** in that case too **he was hired for one-sixth** of a dinar **and** yet in practice **he may eat** an amount worth **a dinar. Rather, what have you to say? That the Merciful One entitles him** to eat more than his wages. **Here too,** one can likewise say that **the Merciful One entitles him** to possess more than his wages.

The *gemara* first attempts to prove from the *mishnah* itself that the right cannot be *mishelo*. The *mishnah* states that a worker may eat even if the value of his consumption exceeds his wage. If this were truly salary, the anomaly would be glaring: *oger b'danka achil b'zuza?* — one hired for a sixth of a *dinar* eats a *dinar*'s worth? Benefits cannot structurally exceed the base salary if they are understood as implicit compensation from the employer.

### Conclusion

The *gemara* then deflects this proof. Even granting the *mishel Shamayim* position, the same oddity formally exists — the worker still eats more than he earns, it is simply that the Torah grants this right. So the *gemara* concludes: *Rachmana zakkei lei* — the Torah grants him this right. And even if the right is conceived as *mishelo*, as part of the contractual relationship, one can still say *Rachmana zakkei lei* — the Torah permits this benefit to exceed the salary. The proof fails to resolve the question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חַסָּמָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא יֹאכַל פּוֹעֵל יוֹתֵר עַל שְׂכָרוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, וּמָר סָבַר מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another suggestion: **Come** and **hear** a proof from another statement from the mishna: **Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma says: A laborer may not eat more than** the value of **his wages, but the Rabbis permit** it. **What, is it not** the case that they **disagree with regard to this: That** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma, **holds** that **he eats from his own** property, and therefore he may not eat an amount worth more than he earns, **and** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **he eats from** the property **of Heaven?**

The *gemara* then tries to use the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Chisma and the Sages as a vehicle for the *mishelo* versus *mishel Shamayim* debate — Rabbi Eliezer holds *mishelo* and therefore a worker cannot eat beyond his wage; the Sages hold *mishel Shamayim* and therefore there is no ceiling. But the *gemara* rejects this as well: *lo d'chulei alma mishelo hu ochel* — perhaps both agree it is *mishelo*, and their disagreement turns instead on how to read the word *k'nafshecha* in the verse (*ki tavo b'kerem re'echa v'achalta anavim k'nafshecha save'echa*). One reading is that *k'nafshecha* conveys a moral teaching: since workers risk their lives — *badavar shemoseir nafsho alav* — climbing trees, doing dangerous labor, they deserve to eat without limit. The other reading derives from *k'nafshecha* an analogy between the worker and the worker's own throat: just as if one muzzles oneself there are no lashes, so too if one muzzles the worker there are no lashes — a purely technical *derasha* about the penalty for violation, unrelated to the ceiling on eating.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, וְהָכָא ״בִּכְנַפְשְׁךָ״ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: כְּנַפְשְׁךָ בִּדְבַר שֶׁמּוֹסֵר נַפְשׁוֹ עָלָיו,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No;** it is possible **that everyone agrees** that **he eats from his own** property, **and here they disagree with regard to** the meaning of a term in the verse: "When you come into your neighbor's vineyard, then you may eat grapes until you have enough **at your own pleasure [*kenafshekha*]"** (Deuteronomy 23:25). One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **holds that "at your own pleasure [*kenafshekha*],"** which literally means: In accordance with your soul, is referring **to a matter for which he hands over his soul,** i.e., the laborer acquires the fruit by virtue of the risks he accepts upon himself as part of his work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר סָבַר כְּנַפְשְׁךָ: מָה נַפְשְׁךָ, אִם חָסַמְתָּ – פָּטוּר, אַף פּוֹעֵל אִם חָסַמְתָּ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that the term **"*kenafshekha*"** means: Like your own person. **Just as** with regard to **your own person,** i.e., the owner, **if you muzzled** yourself, you are **exempt,** as you yourself do not have to eat, **so too,** with regard to **a laborer, if you muzzled** him, i.e., you did not allow him to eat, you are **exempt.** This indicates that there are cases in which a worker is not entitled to eat.

## Wife and Children Test

### Reassignment Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נָזִיר שֶׁאָמַר ״תְּנוּ לְאִשְׁתִּי וּבָנַי״ – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל – אַמַּאי אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ? הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם ״לָךְ לָךְ״ אָמְרִין נְזִירָא: ״סְחוֹר סְחוֹר לְכַרְמָא לָא תִּקְרַב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If **a nazirite who** is working in a vineyard **says: Give** the grapes **to my wife and my children,** as he is prohibited from eating them himself, **they do not listen to him. But if you say** that a laborer **eats from his own** property, **why** should **they not listen to him?** The Gemara answers: **There,** the reason is different, **due to** the well-known proverb concerning a nazirite: **Go, go, we say to a nazirite, go round, go round; do not approach a vineyard.** It is prohibited for a nazirite to eat any of the products of the vine. To keep a nazirite away from temptation, the Sages attempt to deter him from accepting work in a vineyard by not allowing him to give the fruit to his family. Consequently, this *halakha* is due to the concern about a possible transgression and has nothing to do with the rights of a laborer.

The *gemara* continues seeking a resolution. It introduces the case of a *nazir* who is working in a vineyard: since a *nazir* is forbidden to consume any grape product, he cannot eat the fruit himself, and so he says to the owner: *t'nu l'ishti u'vanai* — let my wife and children eat it instead. The *gemara* rules: *ein shomin lo* — one does not listen to him.

### Reassignment Forbidden

If the eating right were *mishelo* — part of his salary and his property to dispose of as he wished — why could he not simply redirect it to his wife and children? The inability to reassign is exactly the *nafka mina* the *gemara* was pursuing, and it seems to prove decisively that the right is *mishel Shamayim*: the Torah gave it to him personally while he works, and it does not extend to his family.

### Nazir Safeguard

The *gemara* deflects this proof, however, invoking the famous principle: *lech lech amrin nezira, schor schor l'charma lo tikrav* — go away, go away, we say to the *nazir*: round and round, do not even approach the vineyard. The fact that a *nazir* is working in a vineyard at all is already objectionable; the Rabbis therefore deny him the benefit he would otherwise have been able to redirect to his family, as a penalty for placing himself in an inappropriate situation. This is not a technical *gezeira* but rather a broader religious attitude — even where something is technically permissible, one should keep well away from dangerous spiritual territory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פּוֹעֵל שֶׁאָמַר ״תְּנוּ לְאִשְׁתִּי וּבָנַי״ – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל – אַמַּאי אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ? מַאי פּוֹעֵל – נָזִיר. וְהָתַנְיָא ״נָזִיר״, וְהָתַנְיָא ״פּוֹעֵל״! מִידֵּי גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי תַּנְיָין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: With regard to **a laborer who said: Give** the produce **to my wife and my children, they do not listen to him. But if you say** that **he eats from his own** property, **why** should **they not listen to him?** The Gemara refutes this argument: In this particular context, **what** is the meaning of **a laborer?** It means **a nazirite** laborer. The Gemara questions this response: **But isn't it taught** in one *baraita* concerning the case of **a nazirite, and isn't it taught** in another *baraita* concerning the case of **a laborer?** Apparently, these are two different *halakhot*. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Were** these *baraitot* **taught alongside one another,** such that one can deduce a *halakha* from the change in wording? These are two separate *baraitot*, and therefore no inference can be drawn from the difference in terminology, and both may be referring to a nazirite laborer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִנַּיִן לְפוֹעֵל שֶׁאָמַר ״תְּנוּ לְאִשְׁתִּי וּבָנַי״ שֶׁאֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶל כֶּלְיְךָ לֹא תִתֵּן״. וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי נָזִיר, אִי הָכִי מִשּׁוּם ״אֶל כֶּלְיְךָ לֹא תִּתֵּן״, מִשּׁוּם ״לָךְ לָךְ״ אָמְרִין נְזִירָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from another *baraita*: **From where** is it derived with regard **to a laborer who said: Give** the produce **to my wife and my children, that they do not listen to him? As it is stated: "But you shall not put any in your vessel"** (Deuteronomy 23:25). **And if you would say** that **so too,** this is referring to **a nazirite, if so,** the reason is not **due to** the verse: **"But you shall not put any in your vessel";** rather, **it is due to** the principle: **Go, go, we say to a nazirite,** do not approach a vineyard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְאַיְּידֵי דְּקָתָנֵי לַהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן פּוֹעֵל – קָא נָסֵיב לֵהּ קְרָא דְּפוֹעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **Yes, it is indeed so.** This *baraita* is discussing a nazirite, **and since** it **teaches** the *halakha* **by** utilizing the **language** of **a laborer,** without specifying that he is a nazirite, **it cites the verse** that is stated **with regard to a laborer.** In fact, the actual source for the *halakha* is a decree due to naziriteship, while the practice is permitted to any other laborer.

The *gemara* then encounters a *baraita* that speaks of a regular worker — not a *nazir* — who says *t'nu l'ishti u'vanai* and is likewise not accommodated. This seems to be the clean proof: *ein shomin lo* applies even to a regular worker, which can only be because the right is *mishel Shamayim*. The *gemara* attempts to argue that this *baraita* also actually refers to a *nazir* despite using the word *po'el*, and that the verse cited — *v'el kelyecha lo titein* ("you shall not place it into your vessel") — merely indicates the personal nature of the eating right. But this answer is plainly forced: if the case is a *nazir*, why describe him as a worker and cite a verse that applies universally? The *gemara*'s insistence on rejecting the obvious conclusion is strained, and the plain import of the sources is that the right is *mishel Shamayim* and therefore cannot be reassigned.

## Drying Figs Case

### Drying Extends Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל לְקַצּוֹת בִּתְאֵנִים
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 2:7): With regard to **one who hires a laborer to prepare figs for drying,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹכֵל וּפָטוּר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאוֹכֵל אֲנִי וּבְנִי״, אוֹ ״שֶׁיֹּאכַל בְּנִי בִּשְׂכָרִי״ – הוּא אוֹכֵל וּפָטוּר, וּבְנוֹ אוֹכֵל וְחַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **this** laborer **may eat and is exempt from** separating **tithe.** Since the Torah granted him permission to eat, he may do so while he is working without separating tithes, as is the case with regard to gifts due to the poor. But if the laborer stipulated: **On the condition that I and my sons may eat, or that my son may eat for my wages, he** himself **may eat and** is **exempt** from separating tithes, as he is permitted to eat by Torah law, **and his son may eat but** is **obligated** to separate tithes.

The *gemara* continues with a new case. Someone hires a worker to dry figs. An important principle governs the worker's right to eat: it extends only until *gmar melacha* — the completion of the work process. Rashi notes, and Tosafot discusses, that the determination of *gmar melacha* can depend on intention or, as Tosafot suggests, on the standard practice of the locale. In this case, since the owner has decided to dry the figs rather than eat them fresh, *gmar melacha* is deferred until the drying is complete. The worker hired for this process therefore still has a right to eat — *harei zeh ochel u'patur min hamaaser* — he eats and is exempt from *maaser*, since the produce has not yet reached *gmar melacha* and the right to eat applies before *maaser* obligations attach.

### Son's Obligation

The *mishnah* then addresses a stipulation: what if the worker says *al m'nat she'yochal ani u'vni* — on the condition that I and my son will eat — or that his son will eat in lieu of his wage? Rashi understands *bni bis'chari* to mean that the son eats and the value is deducted from the worker's hourly pay. Tosafot reads it differently: the son eats in the worker's stead, with the worker's pay unchanged. Either way, the son is eating. The ruling is: *hu ochel u'patur* — the worker himself eats and is exempt from *maaser*, but *u'vno ochel v'chayav* — his son eats and is *chayav* in *maaser*.

### Appearance Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, בְּנוֹ אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? אָמַר רָבִינָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּמִקָּח.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say he eats from his own** property, **why** is **his son obligated?** A son may eat from his father's table without rendering the food subject to tithes. **Ravina said:** The reason is **because it looks like a sale.** Although the produce belongs to the laborer by Torah law, when he makes a deal involving his son it has the appearance of a transaction. Therefore, he must separate tithes to avoid any misunderstanding on the part of observers.

The *gemara* asks: if the worker's eating is *mishelo* — part of his negotiated salary — why is the son *chayav* in *maaser*? The son is also eating pursuant to the same salary arrangement. Rav Pappa explains: *mishum d'mechzi k'mekach* — because it looks like a sale. When the worker himself is nibbling as he works, however one conceptualizes the underlying arrangement, it does not look like a transfer of ownership. But when the benefit passes to someone other than the worker — even if the arrangement is structurally the same — it has the appearance of a sale, and that appearance alone is sufficient to create at least a rabbinic obligation in *maaser*. This is not a technical question of ownership but one of appearances: eating directly does not look like a *mekach*, while eating by stipulation through another person does.

## Neta Revai Case

### Undisclosed Neta Revai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֲלִים לַעֲשׂוֹת בְּנֶטַע רְבָעִי שֶׁלּוֹ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יֹאכְלוּ. אִם לֹא הוֹדִיעָן – פּוֹדֶה וּמַאֲכִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites yet another relevant source: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (93a): In the case of **one who hires a laborer to perform** labor **with his fourth-year produce,** such laborers **may not eat** the fruit, as all fruit of the fourth year of a tree must be taken and consumed in Jerusalem. **And if he did not inform them** beforehand that they were working with fourth-year produce, they are considered to have been hired under false pretenses. Consequently, he must **redeem** the fruit **and feed them.**

The *gemara* introduces a further case. One hires a worker to labor among trees that are in their fourth year of growth — *neta revai* — outside of Jerusalem. The worker cannot eat the fruit because *neta revai* must be redeemed and consumed in Jerusalem; simply eating it in the field is not an option. The worker, naturally, cannot eat: *harei elu lo yochalu*. 

### Redemption and Feeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל – אַמַּאי פּוֹדֶה וּמַאֲכִילָן? אִיסּוּרָא לָא זַכִּי לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא! הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּמִקָּח טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that the laborer **eats from** the property **of Heaven, why** must the owner **redeem** the fruit **and feed them? The Merciful One** certainly **did not entitle them to** transgress **a prohibition.** Even if by Torah law the laborer is granted a personal right to eat, this applies only to permitted food. The Gemara explains: **There,** the reason is **because it looks like a mistaken transaction,** as they accepted employment under the assumption that they would be permitted to eat the fruit. He is therefore obligated to compensate them.

The problem arises when the employer failed to disclose this status to the workers: *v'im lo hodiam*. The workers arrived expecting to enjoy the usual benefit of eating while they worked. The employer's remedy is *podeh umachilan* — he must redeem the fruit and then feed it to them. He quantifies the value of what they would have eaten, redeems that amount of *neta revai* (which, like *maaser sheni*, can be redeemed with money), and compensates them accordingly.

### Mekach Taut Appearance

The *gemara* raises the conceptual difficulty. If the worker's right is *mishel Shamayim* — a right given directly by the Torah — then when that right does not exist (as with *neta revai*, where the Torah itself did not extend the right to this fruit), why is the employer obligated to compensate? The Torah never gave them anything here; *issura lo zachar leh Rachmana* — the Torah did not grant them an entitlement to forbidden fruit. If it were *mishelo*, part of the implicit contract between employer and employee, then the failure to provide the expected benefit would constitute a *mekach taut* — a defective transaction. But if it is *mishel Shamayim*, the employer can simply say: this was not between us; your grievance is with Heaven.

The *gemara* answers: *hatam mishum demichzei kemekach taut* — even in the *mishel Shamayim* framework, the situation has the appearance of a fraudulent transaction. The workers reasonably expected to benefit, they did not, and it looks as though they were deceived. Even without a direct contractual obligation, the appearance of deception must be remedied. This principle of *mechzi kemekach taut* will recur throughout the ensuing discussion.

## Wine Barrels Case

### Unsealed Barrels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: נִתְפָּרְסוּ עִגּוּלָיו, נִתְפַּתְּחוּ חָבִיּוֹתָיו – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, וְאִם לֹא הוֹדִיעָן – מְעַשֵּׂר וּמַאֲכִילָן. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל, אַמַּאי מְעַשֵּׂר וּמַאֲכִילָן? אִיסּוּרָא לָא זַכִּי לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: But now **state the latter clause** of that same mishna: If **his cakes,** in which he had earlier preserved his figs, **broke apart** and crumbled, so that they must be preserved once again, or if **his barrels** of wine **opened** and he hired laborers to reseal them, **these** laborers **may not eat.** The reason is that the figs and wine were already subject to tithes, from which point a laborer may not eat them. **And if** the owner **did not inform them** that it is prohibited for them to consume the food, **he must tithe** the food **and feed them. But if you say he eats from** the property **of Heaven, why must he tithe** the food **and feed them? The Merciful One** certainly **did not entitle them to** transgress **a prohibition.**

The *mishnah* continues with cases of a worker hired when *gmar melacha* has already occurred but the worker does not know it. If the owner's rounds of pressed figs collapse and scatter — *nitparzu igulav* — and a worker is hired to regather and repress them, or if the owner's barrels of wine come open — *nitpatechu chaviyotav* — and a worker is hired to reseal them, those workers have no right to eat: *harei elu lo yochalu*. The *gmar melacha* is complete; the eating right is extinguished.

But if the employer did not inform them — *v'im lo hodiam* — he must take *maaser* from the produce and feed them from it, compensating them for the benefit they expected and did not receive.

### Gmar Melacha Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּמִקָּח טָעוּת. בִּשְׁלָמָא נִתְפָּרְסוּ עִגּוּלָיו – מִיחֲזֵי כְּמִקָּח טָעוּת. אֶלָּא נִתְפַּתְּחוּ חָבִיּוֹתָיו, מַאי מִקָּח טָעוּת אִיכָּא? מִידָּע יָדַע דְּאִיטְּבִיל לְהוּ לְמַעֲשֵׂר!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Here too,** is it **because it looks like a mistaken transaction,** that explanation is not tenable. **Granted,** in the case **where his cakes broke apart,** this does **look like a mistaken transaction,** as the laborers were unaware that the figs had been preserved once already, and they mistakenly thought that the fruit had not yet reached the stage at which it would become subject to tithes. **But** with regard to the other case, when **his barrels opened, what mistaken transaction is there** here? **They certainly know that** the wine **had** already **been rendered untithed produce with regard to tithes,** as wine is subject to tithes as soon as it has been collected into the pit alongside the winepress.

Regarding the figs case, the deception is intelligible: the worker sees what appears to be unseparated, unprocessed produce and assumes *gmar melacha* has not occurred. But the wine barrel case presents a difficulty. The *gemara* asks: *mai mekach taut ika?* — what misunderstanding could a worker possibly have about sealed wine barrels? Wine reaches *gmar melacha* when it descends into the wine vat (*yayin misheyered labor*). Workers hired to reseal barrels obviously know they are dealing with finished wine. Where is the mistaken expectation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: שֶׁנִּתְפַּתְּחוּ חָבִיּוֹתָיו לַבּוֹר. וְהָתַנְיָא: יַיִן מִשֶּׁיֵּרֵד לַבּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said:** This is referring to a case **where his barrels opened** in such a manner that the wine once again fell **into the pit** from which it came. The laborers therefore assumed that the owner was not yet obligated to set aside tithes. The Gemara raises a difficulty against this explanation: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **wine** is immediately subject to tithes **from when it descends into the pit?**

Rav Sheshet resolves this: the case is *shenitpatchu chaviyotav labor* — the barrels broke and the wine spilled back into the cistern. The workers therefore see wine in a pit and think it has never left the vat — that *gmar melacha* has not yet occurred — and so they believe they will have a right to drink. Rashi clarifies that *nitpatechu* simply means the seal was removed, not that the barrels shattered. Once the *gemara* reframes the scenario as wine having returned to the cistern, the workers' mistake becomes comprehensible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר מִשֶּׁיְּקַפֶּה, דַּאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָא הֲוָה יָדְעִינַן. וְנֵימָא לְהוּ: אִיבְּעִי לְכוּ אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתַּיְיכוּ דִּלְמָא מְקַפֶּה! בְּאַתְרָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּנָגֵיד אִיהוּ מְקַפֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that wine is subject to tithes only **from when** one starts to remove the seeds and the waste **floats** to the top, which occurs after the wine has already descended into the pit. The reason for this *halakha* is **that** the laborers can **say to him: We did not know** that the wine had already been removed from the pit. The Gemara asks: **But let us say to them: It should have entered your minds** that **perhaps** its waste had already **floated.** The Gemara responds: The ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to a place where that** same **man who pulls** the wine from the pit **is** also the one **who floats** its waste. Consequently, it was reasonable for the laborers to assume that they had been hired to perform both tasks.

The *gemara* then challenges even this: the employer could say to the workers, *ibei lachon le'asukei adatayichu* — you should have realized that perhaps the lees were already removed; that was your risk to assess. But Rav Zvid, citing a *baraita* of Rabbi Oshaya, refines the positions: the *Tanna Kamma* holds *yayin misheyered labor veyekpeh* — wine reaches *gmar melacha* when it descends into the vat and the lees are removed there. Rabbi Akiva goes further: *misheyishleh bechaviyot* — not until the wine is poured into the barrels and the froth that rises in the barrels is also removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּתָנֵי רַב זְבִיד בִּדְבֵי רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: יַיִן מִשֶּׁיֵּרֵד לַבּוֹר וִיקַפֶּה, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיְּשַׁלֶּה בֶּחָבִיּוֹת. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא שֶׁלֹּא נִתְפַּתְּחוּ חָבִיּוֹתָיו לַבּוֹר, דַּאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָא הֲוָה יָדְעִינַן דִּמְשַׁלֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And** one can reach a different conclusion **now that Rav Zevid has taught** the following version of the above dispute, heard **from the school** of **Rabbi Hoshaya: Wine** is subject to tithes **from when it descends into the pit and** its waste **floats. And Rabbi Akiva says:** It is subject to tithes **from when he drains** the waste **from the barrels.** They would pour all of the wine into a barrel, before draining and removing the waste after fermentation. With this in mind, **you** can **even say that the barrels were not opened into the pit,** but simply opened up, **as** the laborers can **say to him: We did not know that he had** already **drained** the waste.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא לְהוּ: אִיבְּעִי לְכוּ אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתַּיְיכוּ דִּלְמָא מְשַׁלֵּי! בְּאַתְרָא דְּהָהוּא דְּשָׁרֵיק הָהוּא מְשַׁלֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **But let us say to them: It should have entered your minds** that **perhaps** its waste had already **been drained.** The Gemara answers: This is referring **to a place where that** same man **who plugs** the barrel with a stopper **is** also the one **who drains** its waste, and therefore they assumed they had been hired to perform both tasks.

According to Rabbi Akiva's position, *gmar melacha* does not occur until the barrels are sealed and the froth inside removed. Therefore, a worker hired to reseal an open barrel can legitimately believe that the froth has not yet been skimmed, and thus that *gmar melacha* has not occurred. The employer's response — that the worker should have figured this out — is defeated by the following fact: *ba'asra dehu desharik ihu mashli* — in that locale, the job of sealing the barrel and the job of removing the froth are typically done by the same person and packaged together. The worker hired to reseal therefore has a reasonable expectation that he would also be the one removing the froth, that *gmar melacha* had not yet occurred, and that he would be entitled to drink. Since the work was already completed by someone else, that expectation was disappointed, and the employer must compensate him — whether because it was *mishelo* and a genuine *mekach taut*, or because it is *mishel Shamayim* and it merely *mechzi kemekach taut*.

## Negotiation Rights

### Who Can Negotiate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קוֹצֵץ אָדָם עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ, עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, וְעַל יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן דַּעַת. אֲבָל אֵינוֹ קוֹצֵץ לֹא עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי בְּהֶמְתּוֹ – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (93a): **A man can stipulate on his own behalf** that he receive a certain increase in his wages instead of eating the produce with which he works, and similarly, he can stipulate this **on behalf of his adult son or daughter, on behalf of his adult** Canaanite **slave or** Canaanite **maidservant, or on behalf of his wife,** with their agreement, **because they have** the basic level of mental **competence,** i.e., they are legally competent and can therefore waive their rights. **But he cannot stipulate** this **on behalf of his minor son or daughter, nor on behalf of his minor** Canaanite **slave or** Canaanite **maidservant, nor on behalf of his animal, as they do not** have the basic level of mental **competence.**

A new *mishnah* takes up the question of who may negotiate away eating rights in advance. *Kotzetz adam al yedei atzmo* — a person may negotiate on his own behalf, forgoing the right to eat in exchange for higher pay. He may similarly negotiate *al yedei bno uvito hagedolim* — on behalf of his adult sons and daughters — and *al yedei avdo veshifchato hagedolim* — on behalf of his adult male and female slaves. He may also negotiate *al ha'isha* — on behalf of his wife — *mipnei sheyesh ba'an da'at*, because all of these individuals possess *da'at*: the adult understanding and autonomy needed to waive their rights and be represented in that waiver.

### Who Cannot Negotiate

The *mishnah* then states the limits. *Aval eino kotzetz al yedei beno uvito haketanim* — but he may not negotiate on behalf of his minor sons and daughters, *velo al yedei avdo veshifchato haketanim* — nor on behalf of his minor slaves, *velo al yedei behemto* — nor on behalf of his animal. The minors cannot give up their right because they lack the *da'at* required to waive a right; the animal presents a different category entirely. Rashi notes that muzzling one's own animal is already forbidden regardless of financial arrangement — it is not a right the owner can contract away, whether for his benefit or the animal's, because the prohibition against muzzling an animal at its labor is absolute and irrespective of any employment negotiation.

### Feeding Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בְּמַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אֵינוֹ קוֹצֵץ, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל – קְטַנִּים נָמֵי נִקּוֹץ לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this mishna: It might **enter your mind** that all these examples involve cases **where** the father, master, or husband, depending on the case, **provides** his children, slaves, or wife **with food,** and they assist him in his work. **Granted, if you say** that a laborer **eats from** the property **of Heaven,** it is **due to that** reason that **he may not stipulate** this on behalf of minors, as the Torah also entitled minors themselves to eat when they work, and they cannot waive their rights. **But if you say** that a laborer **eats from his own** property, and the food he consumes is a monetary obligation, **let him** be allowed **to stipulate on behalf of minors as well.** Since in a case where a father provides sustenance for his children he keeps the profits of their labor, he should be entitled to stipulate this concerning their payment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּשֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת. אִי הָכִי, גְּדוֹלִים נָמֵי! גְּדוֹלִים יָדְעִי וְקָא מָחֲלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a situation **in which** the father **does not provide** his children **with food.** Therefore, he does not keep the profits of their labor and has no right to make stipulations concerning the terms of their employment. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** in the case of **adult** children and slaves **too,** the father or master should not be able to stipulate in this manner. Since he does not provide them with food, why should he be able to waive their rights? The Gemara responds: **Adults are aware** of the stipulation **and forgive** their rights to the food. He can stipulate this only with their agreement.

The *gemara*'s first approach to explaining why minors' rights cannot be negotiated relies on an assumption: *u'ma'aleh lahen mezonot* — the father is feeding his children and minor slaves. When a father feeds his minor children, he is entitled to their labor and earnings. The *gemara* therefore reasons: if the eating right is *mishel Shamayim* — a right granted by Heaven — then the father simply has no entitlement to negotiate it away; it is not his to negotiate. But if the right is *mishelo*, part of the employment arrangement, then if the father is entitled to the minor's earnings he should be entitled to negotiate this benefit as well.

The *gemara* then deflects: *hacha bemai iskinan keshein ma'aleh lahen mezonot* — we are speaking of a case where the father is not feeding them. Since he is not feeding them, he is not entitled to their earnings, and therefore even if the eating benefit is *mishelo*, it is not *his* salary to negotiate. And as for adult dependents: *gedolim yad'ei v'kamachli* — adults understand and consent; they can authorize him to negotiate on their behalf and waive the right themselves, regardless of whether he feeds them.

### Rav Ushaya Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנָא רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: קוֹצֵץ אָדָם עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ וְעַל יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא עַל יְדֵי בְּהֶמְתּוֹ. וְעַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, אֲבָל לֹא עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים. וְקוֹצֵץ עַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הַכְּנַעֲנִים, בֵּין גְּדוֹלִים וּבֵין קְטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rabbi Hoshaya teach** in a *baraita*: **A person can stipulate on his own behalf** that he receive a certain increase in his wages instead of eating the produce with which he works, **and on his wife's behalf, but not on behalf of his animal; and** he can stipulate this **on behalf of his adult son or daughter, but not on behalf of his minor son or daughter; and he can stipulate** this **on behalf of his Canaanite slave or** Canaanite **maidservant, whether they are adults or minors.** This contradicts the ruling of the previous mishna that one cannot stipulate this on behalf of his minor slave.

Against this, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* of Rav Oshaya that distinguishes differently. That *baraita* holds: a person may negotiate for himself and his wife, but not his animal; he may negotiate for his adult children but not his minor children; and he may negotiate for his slaves — *bein gedolim bein ketanim*, both adult and minor. The *baraita* thus allows negotiation for minor slaves but not for minor children. If the explanation were simply about whether the father is feeding them, one would expect the same rule to apply to both minor children and minor slaves. The difference requires a different explanation.

## Slave Labor Entitlement

### Salary Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּמַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת, וּבְהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, וּמָר סָבַר מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל?! לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – בְּשֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת, וּבָרַיְיתָא – בְּמַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** correct to say that **this and that,** both sources, are referring to cases **where** the master **provides** the slaves **with food, and they disagree with regard to this: That** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the mishna, **holds** that a laborer **eats from his own** property, and therefore he can relinquish the rights of his minor slave, **and** one **Sage,** of the *baraita*, **holds** that a laborer **eats from** the property **of Heaven,** and therefore he cannot relinquish the rights of his minor slave? The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **No; everyone agrees** that a laborer **eats from his own** property, **and** it is **not difficult. Here,** in the mishna, it is referring to a case **where he does not provide** the slaves **with food,** as stated previously, **and** the ***baraita*** is referring to a case **where he provides them with food.**

The *gemara* attempts to read the dispute between the *mishnah* and the *baraita* of Rav Oshaya as turning on the *mishelo* versus *mishel Shamayim* question itself — and as operating under the assumption that the father is feeding both his minor children and his minor slaves. On the *mishelo* view, eating is part of the salary; since the father feeds them and is entitled to their earnings, he can negotiate the minor slave's eating benefit. On the *mishel Shamayim* view, the benefit is not salary at all and therefore cannot be negotiated.

### Logical Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ – בְּמַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת? אִי הָכִי קְטַנִּים נָמֵי נִקּוֹץ לְהוּ! צַעֲרַיְיהוּ דִּבְנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים לָא זַכִּי לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To what** case **did you interpret** the *baraita* to be referring? Did you interpret it as referring to a case **where he provides** the slaves **with food? If so, let him also** be allowed to **stipulate** on behalf of his **minor** children that they receive no food, as presumably the *baraita* is referring to a case where he provides his minor children with food. The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One does not entitle him to** waive **the suffering of his minor son and daughter.** His young children will suffer if they are prevented from eating the food they see before them.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty with this reading. The same *baraita* that allows negotiating minor slaves' rights refuses to allow it for minor children. If both groups are being fed, and if the *mishelo* view entitles the father to both their earnings and this benefit, why can he negotiate for the slave but not for his own minor son? Both are being fed; both have their earnings attributed to the father. The asymmetry requires explanation.

The *gemara* answers: *schariah de-vno u-vito ktanim lo zachu lei Rachmana* — the Torah (or by rabbinic enactment, as Tosafot notes, since the father's right to his children's earnings is primarily rabbinic) did not give the father the right to negotiate away a benefit that consists essentially of providing his children food. Even if he is entitled to their monetary earnings, he cannot strip them of the food they eat in the field. He may not profit by causing his minor children to go hungry. With minor slaves, no such restriction applies, and the *baraita* therefore allows the master to negotiate away even the minor slave's benefit.

### Entitlement Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לְמַתְנִיתִין – בְּשֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **To what** case **did you interpret** the *baraita* to be referring? Did you interpret it as referring to a case **where he does not provide** the slaves **with food?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין הָרַב יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יָכוֹל הָרַב לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **This works out well according to the one who says** that **a master cannot say to a slave: Work for me and I will not feed you,** i.e., he is obligated to provide the slave with a livelihood. For the purposes of the case at hand, this means that the master cannot stipulate that he is relinquishing his slaves' right to eat while performing labor, and therefore it works out **well. But according to the one who says** that **a master can say to a slave: Work for me and I will not feed you, what can be said?** He should be able to stipulate to that effect with regard to his minor slave, as he is entitled to all profits that result from the slaves' labor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּשֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה לָהֶן מְזוֹנוֹת, וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר יָכוֹל, וּמַר סָבַר אֵין יָכוֹל. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: יָכוֹל הָרַב, שָׁבֵיק מַתְנִיתִין וְעָבֵיד כְּבָרַיְיתָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** according to this opinion one must accept a different explanation: Both **this** mishna **and that** *baraita* are referring to a case **when he does not provide** the slaves **with food, and** the two tannaitic sources **disagree with regard to that** very issue. **As** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the *baraita*, **holds** that a master **can** say to a slave: Work for me and I will not feed you, **and** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the mishna, **holds** that **he cannot** do so. The Gemara is puzzled by this response: If so, **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says** that **a master can** say to his slave that he will not feed him, has **left** aside **the mishna and acted** and ruled **in accordance with the *baraita*.**

The *gemara* then introduces a further complication. The initial *mishnah* forbids negotiating for both minor children and minor slaves. The *baraita* permits it for minor slaves. One way to resolve this, as explored above, is that the *mishnah* speaks of a case where the father is not feeding them, and the *baraita* where he is. But this resolution works only if a master cannot demand a slave's labor without feeding the slave. If a master can demand labor without feeding — as one opinion holds — then even when not feeding, the master is entitled to the slave's earnings, and he should be able to negotiate the benefit either way.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela idei ve-idei be-she-ein ma'aleh lahem mezonot* — in both the *mishnah* and the *baraita*, the father is not feeding them. The dispute between the two sources reflects a disagreement about whether a master can demand a slave's labor without providing food. The *mishnah*, which does not allow negotiating minor slave rights, holds that a master cannot demand labor without feeding the slave, and therefore when he is not feeding them he has no entitlement to negotiate. The *baraita*, which allows it, holds that a master can demand labor regardless of whether he feeds the slave, and therefore even without feeding he is entitled to the slave's earnings and may negotiate them.

## Final Resolution

### Common Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל, וְלָא מָצֵי קָצֵיץ. וּמַאי ״קוֹצֵץ״ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא – מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara retracts the previous explanation in favor of another: **Everyone agrees** that a laborer **eats from** the property **of Heaven, and** even if a father or master provides his child or slave with food **he cannot stipulate** that the child or slave should not eat when performing labor, as the father or master has no rights over that which they consume. **And what is** the meaning of: **Stipulates, that Rabbi Hoshaya says** in the *baraita*? That does not mean, as in the mishna, that the master relinquishes the slaves' right to food; rather, he stipulates that they should eat **food** before they work, so that they will be too full to eat at a later stage.

The *gemara* now steps back and considers whether all of the above debate was predicated on the *mishelo* framework. It asks: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that a master is entitled to his slave's labor even without feeding him, would he then abandon the *mishnah* and rule like the *baraita*? The *gemara* finds this implausible — Rabbi Yochanan is consistently drawn toward the *stam mishnah* — and so it reframes the entire conclusion: *ela d'chulei alma min hashamayim hu ochel* — the final position of the *gemara* is that all authorities agree the eating right is *mishel Shamayim*, a right given by Heaven to the worker.

On the *mishel Shamayim* view, the eating right is not part of the employer-employee negotiation and does not belong to any third party such as a father or master. However, adults who hold this Heaven-given right are *benei da'at* and are capable of waiving it themselves. An adult can say: I choose not to exercise the right the Torah gave me, and in exchange I will receive greater monetary compensation. That exchange is valid because an adult has the capacity to waive his own rights.

### Adult Waiver

Minors, however, cannot waive a Heaven-given right. The Torah extended this right to them personally, and neither they nor their parents or masters can compromise it. This explains the consistent rule across both the *mishnah* and the *baraita*: in no configuration may a father or master negotiate away a minor's eating rights. The heaven-given entitlement of a minor is not alienable.

### Unresolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי בְּהֶמְתּוֹ תֶּבֶן נִקּוֹץ לַהּ! אֶלָּא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר – מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא אוֹכֵל, וּמָר סָבַר – מִשֶּׁל שָׁמַיִם הוּא אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If so, **in the corresponding** situation, **with regard to his animal,** there should **likewise** be no discussion at all because **he can stipulate** in this manner and distribute **straw for it** before it starts work, as everyone agrees that this is permitted. **Rather,** the Gemara retracts this interpretation and says that in fact **they disagree with regard to this: That** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the mishna, **holds** that a laborer **eats from his own** property, **and** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the *baraita*, **holds** that a laborer **eats from** the property **of Heaven.** This proves that this issue is in fact a dispute between *tanna'im*.

The *gemara* also revisits what *katzatz* — negotiating away eating rights — could even mean on the *mishel Shamayim* view. One suggestion is that it means feeding workers substantially beforehand so they are sated and will not want to eat during the work. But the *gemara* rejects this: on that interpretation, one should be able to do the same for one's animal by feeding it in advance, yet the *mishnah* clearly says one cannot negotiate for an animal. The *gemara* thus reasserts that *katzatz* means a genuine waiver in exchange for higher pay. And with the conclusion that the eating right is *mishel Shamayim*, such a waiver can be made only by those with full adult *da'at* on their own behalf.

The larger question — whether the eating right is ultimately *mishelo* or *mishel Shamayim* — is explicitly left without final resolution. The *gemara* has mapped the terrain, tested both positions against case after case, and arrived at a practical framework for who can waive and who cannot. But the foundational conceptual question remains open, and we conclude with it unresolved.
