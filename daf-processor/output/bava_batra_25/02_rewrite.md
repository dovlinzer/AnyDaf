# Bava Batra 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Threshing Floor

### Fixed vs. Ad Hoc

The *mishna* at the bottom of 24b continues the theme of maintaining appropriate distances between neighbors and begins with the rule: *merachikin et ha-goren ha-kavua min ha-ir chamishim ama* — a fixed, permanent threshing floor must be kept fifty *amot* from the town. The reason is straightforward: the chaff produced during threshing is dispersed by the wind and can blow into people's faces and fields. The requirement of *chamishim ama* creates a buffer large enough to prevent this.

The *mishna* continues: *lo ya'aseh adam goren kavua betoch shelo* — a person may not establish a fixed threshing floor within his own property *ela im ken yesh lo chamishim ama l'chol ruach*, unless he has fifty *amot* clear in every direction, so that chaff cannot drift into a neighboring field. The *mishna* then adds: *umerachik min nitiotav shel chaveiro umin niro k'dei shelo yazik* — one must also keep it sufficiently distant from his neighbor's plantings and from his neighbor's *nir* (a fallow field left to rest or to kill off weeds) so as not to cause damage.

### Abaye's Two Cases

The *gemara* immediately notices a tension: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa* — what distinguishes the opening clause from the closing one? If the rule is fifty *amot* in all directions, why does the *mishna* seem to add a separate requirement about plantings and fallow fields? Abaye resolves this by reading the *mishna* as containing two distinct cases. The opening clause addresses a *goren kavua*, a large, established threshing operation, which requires fifty *amot*. The closing clause, he argues, addresses a *goren she-eino kavua*, a non-fixed, ad hoc threshing floor, for which the required distance is assessed case by case based on what is necessary to avoid actual damage.

### Non-Fixed Operation

This raises a further question: *heichi dami goren she-eino kavua* — what precisely characterizes a non-fixed threshing floor, and why does it create less potential damage? Rav Yosef b'Rebbi Chanina answers: *kol she-eino zoreh b'rachat* — any operation that does not use a large winnowing implement. Without the large-scale vessel that scatters chaff widely, the reach of the dispersed material is more limited, and therefore the required distance is reduced accordingly.

### Rav Ashi's Reading

Rav Ashi reads the *mishna* differently. Rather than two separate cases, he holds that the *mishna* is presenting a single rule and then explicating its rationale. *Mai ta'ama merachikin goren kavua min ha-ir chamishim ama* — why fifty *amot* from the city? *K'dei shelo yazik* — in order not to damage the neighbor's plantings. On this reading, the closing clause of the *mishna* is not adding a new *halacha* but simply explaining the reasoning behind the one already stated.

### Baraita Confirmation

A *braita* is then cited in support: it teaches that just as one keeps the threshing floor fifty *amot* from the city, so too one keeps it fifty *amot* from a neighbor's gourds, cucumbers, plantings, and fallow field — and concludes *k'dei shelo yazik*, in order not to cause damage. This language parallels the *mishna* precisely. *Bishlama l'Rav Ashi nicha* — for Rav Ashi, this fits well: the *braita* treats the reference to plantings and fallow fields as an explication of the same fifty-*amah* rule, not a separate case. *Ela l'Abaye kashya* — but for Abaye, who reads the *mishna* as covering two distinct cases, the *braita* is difficult, since it presents everything as a single unified rule. The *gemara* concedes: *kashya* — it remains a difficulty for Abaye.

### Nir Damage Question

Having established the framework, the *gemara* pauses on a conceptual difficulty. *Bishlama mi-kishu'av u-mi-dilu'av d'azil avka v'ati b'libei u-matzi lei* — the harm to growing crops like gourds and cucumbers is understandable: the wind carries the dusty chaff into the field where it settles and interferes with the growing produce, absorbing nutrients and causing damage. *Ela mi-niro amai* — but if the field is a *nir*, a fallow field with nothing actively growing, why is there any concern? What harm does chaff blown into an unplanted field actually cause?

### Chaff as Manure

Rav Abba bar Zavda (and some attribute this to Rav Abba bar Zutra) resolves the question: if sufficient chaff accumulates, it decomposes and effectively becomes manure. One might think this is actually beneficial, and *Tosafot* addresses this directly: *af al gav she'aso'o galal, af al gav degalal mashbiach hakarka, kesheyesh kullah galal mazik* — while manure in moderation improves soil, if the entire field becomes nothing but decomposed chaff-turned-manure without anything planted, it is actually damaging to the land. This completes the explanation of why the fifty-*amah* requirement applies even with respect to a fallow field.

## Odorous Operations

### Nevelot, Kevarot, Burski

The *mishna* at the top of 25a turns to a different category of neighbor damage — not physical harm from dispersed particles, but harm through smell. Three types of operations must be kept fifty *amot* from the city: *nevelot* (carcasses, i.e., operations involving the storage or processing of animal carcasses), *kevarot* (cemeteries), and the *burski* (tannery). These are essentially zoning regulations — not disputes between individual neighbors but categorical restrictions on which kinds of operations may be located within a residential area at all. The concern with *nevelot* is specifically the stench of decomposition rather than any concern about the slaughter itself, which is processed quickly. The concern with the *burski* is similarly olfactory — tanneries generate powerful and persistent odors from the curing process.

### Tannery Eastward

Beyond the general fifty-*amah* requirement, the *mishna* adds a specific directional restriction: *ein osin burski ella l'mizrach ha'ir* — a tannery may only be established to the east of the town. The underlying reason is the prevailing wind pattern. *Rashi* explains that the wind from the east is weak, while *Tosafot* frames it in the reverse: the strong wind blows from the west. Either way, the practical result is the same — the dominant wind current travels from west to east, and by situating the tannery to the east of the city, the wind carries the smell away from the residential area rather than into it. This wind pattern, flowing consistently from west to east, corresponds to well-known meteorological patterns: westerly winds push weather systems eastward, which is also why eastbound flights experience headwinds while westbound flights are assisted by tailwinds.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees with the restriction to the east: *lechol ruach hu oseh chutz mi-ma'arava* — one may establish a tannery in any direction except the west, because placing it to the west would carry the stench directly into the city. North and south, he allows with greater latitude. He also requires the fifty-*amah* distance in all permitted directions.

### Gemara Clarifies Distance

The *gemara* asks how to read *Rabbi Akiva*'s position precisely. One possibility is that when he permits other directions, he permits building even within fifty *amot* — *lechol ruach hu oseh ve-somech, chutz min ha-ma'arav, umarchik chamishim amah* — meaning only the west requires fifty *amot* while other directions require no particular distance. The other reading is that *lechol ruach hu oseh umarchik chamishim amah* — all other directions require fifty *amot* as well, but *chutz min ha-ma'arav, she-eino oseh kelal* — the west is simply prohibited entirely.

A *braita* resolves the question: *Rabbi Akiva omer lechol ruach hu oseh umarchik chamishim amah, chutz min ha-ma'arav, she-eino oseh kol ikar, mipnei shehi tadira* — one may build in any direction as long as it is fifty *amot* away, except the west, where it may not be built at all, because the western wind is constant. The word *tadira* here — spelled with an *alef*, though *Tosafot* notes it is more consistently spelled with a *hei* — means "constant" or "regular." Both the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Akiva* thus agree that prohibited directions are absolutely prohibited, and permitted directions require fifty *amot*; they disagree only on how many directions are prohibited: the *tanna kamma* restricts the tannery to the east alone, while *Rabbi Akiva* prohibits only the west.

## Wind & Shechina

### Constant Wind Debate

The *gemara* now focuses on the word *tadira* — that the western wind is "constant." *Rava* poses the question to Rav Nachman: *mai tadira* — what does this mean? The obvious reading, the *pshat*, is that the western wind is the most prevalent wind, blowing regularly from west to east and thus most likely to carry odors into the city. But is that actually accurate?

### Four Daily Winds

This reading is challenged from a tradition cited in the name of Rav Chana bar Abba in the name of Rav: *arba ruchot menashvot bechol yom* — four winds blow every single day, and the northern wind is mixed with all of them. *She-ilmalei chen ein ha-olam mitkayem afilu sha'ah achat* — without the northern wind blended into every other wind, the world could not be sustained even for a moment.

### Southern Wind Harsh

Furthermore, *veruach deromit kashah mikullan* — the southern wind is the harshest of all. *Ve-ilmalei Ben Netz she-ma'amida, macharevet et ha-olam* — were it not for *Ben Netz*, the angel (whom *Rashi* identifies as the "son of the hawk," apparently a celestial being whose function is to restrain the southern wind), it would devastate the world entirely. The *gemara* cites a verse from Iyov: *ha-mibinatecha ya'aver netz yifros k'nafav l'teiman* — from your wisdom does the hawk spread its wings toward the south. Notably, this is already at least the second citation from Iyov in this *masechet* since the extended *aggadata* on that subject, a pattern worth tracking. The point of this tradition is that the northern wind, not the western, seems to be the most essential and ubiquitous — so the western wind's claim to being uniquely "constant" is not obviously supported by meteorological observation.

### Constant = Shechina

*Rava* therefore resolves the difficulty by reinterpreting *tadira* entirely. *Ela mai tadira — tadira bi-shechina* — it does not mean that the western wind is the most frequent; it means that the west is the location of the *shechina*, the Divine Presence. The prohibition on placing a tannery to the west is not primarily about wind patterns at all. It is about not situating a malodorous operation in the direction of God's presence.

### Sun Bowing West

This requires justification: why is the west the location of the *shechina*? The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *bo v'nachzik tov l'avotenu, she-hodi'unu makom tefilla* — let us acknowledge the debt we owe our ancestors for having taught us the proper direction of prayer and the location of the Divine Presence. The source is a verse in Nechemya — associated with the *Anshei Knesset Hagedola* — which states: *utzva ha-shamayim lecha mishtachavim* — the hosts of heaven bow down to you. Since the sun is among the hosts of heaven, and since it rises in the east and sets in the west, its arc of movement constitutes a bowing toward the west. The visual image is striking: imagine the sun's daily path as the gesture of prostration — rising in the east, reaching its height, and descending toward the west. The direction toward which it bows is the west, and therefore the *shechina* resides in the west.

This is a remarkable *gemara* for what it does not say. Two straightforward arguments from the *Beit HaMikdash* itself could have been cited: first, the *Kodesh Kodashim* is located to the west within the *Mikdash*; second, for communities in *Bavel* situated to the east of *Eretz Yisrael*, praying westward means praying toward *Yerushalayim*. The *gemara* references neither. Instead, it grounds the westward orientation entirely in a cosmic observation about the sun's movement — and cites a verse from a post-*churban* context, ironically from Nechemya, who was involved in the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *gemara* appears to be articulating a model of *tefilla* and Divine Presence that is deliberately decoupled from the *Mikdash* and from *korbanot*. In a post-*churban* reality, the *shechina* is not to be located by reference to *makom ha-mikdash* but by the movements of the heavens themselves. This is a powerful theological statement, and its near-silence on the *Beit HaMikdash* connection seems intentional.

### Departing Backwards

Rav Akha bar Yaakov challenges Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's proof. *V'dilma ke-eved she-noteil pras me-rabbo ve-chozer le-achorav u-mishtachaveh* — perhaps the imagery should be read differently: a servant who receives something from his master and then backs away, bowing as he retreats. If so, the sun's westward decline could represent departing from the *shechina* in the east while bowing backward as it goes — meaning the Divine Presence would be in the east, not the west. The *gemara* acknowledges: *kashya* — this is a valid difficulty, and the proof from the sun's movement is not airtight.

## Post-Churban Theology

### Shechina Bechol Makom

The *gemara* now introduces a position more radical still. Rabbi Oshaya holds that the *shechina* is not concentrated in any particular direction at all: *shechina bechol makom* — the Divine Presence is everywhere.

### Lightning Messengers

Rabbi Oshaya derives this from a verse: *atah hu Hashem levadecha, atah asita et ha-shamayim ve-et ha-aretz* — you alone, God, made heaven and earth. His argument proceeds from a comparison of divine and human messengers. A human king sends a messenger to a distant location; when the messenger completes the task, he must return to the king's location to report. This is because the king is in one place and the messenger must come back to that place to deliver his report. But God's messengers operate differently: *lemakom she-mishtalchim mi-sham machzirim shelichutan* — they deliver their report from wherever they were sent, because God is present everywhere and there is no single location to which they must return. The proof-text is again from Iyov: *ha-tishlach berakim ve-yelechu ve-yomeru lecha hineni* — you send your lightning bolts, they go forth, and they say to you: "here we are." The verse does not say *yavo'u ve-yomeru* — they came back and reported — but *vayelechu ve-yomeru* — they went and reported from there. *Melamed she-shechina bechol makom* — this teaches that the *shechina* is everywhere.

### Opposing Angels

The school of Rabbi Yishmael arrives at the same conclusion through a different route. A verse describes one angel leaving while another angel came *likrato* — to meet him, coming from the opposite direction. If the *shechina* were in a single location and all angels emanate from that place, a second angel following the first would be described as coming *acharav* — after him, from behind. The fact that the second angel came *likrato*, to meet him head-on from the opposite direction, implies that angels can originate from any direction — which in turn implies that the Divine Presence from which they come is present everywhere. *Melamed she-shechina bechol makom.*

### Rav Sheshet Anywhere

Rav Sheshet, who was blind, drew a practical *halachic* conclusion from this position. He instructed his attendant: *lechol ruchah ukman* — orient me in any direction when I stand to pray *Shemoneh Esrei*.

### Heretics Eastward

There was, however, one direction he excluded: *levar mi-mizrach* — do not face me eastward. Crucially, he clarified the reason: *de-lav mishum de-la it beih shechina* — not because the *shechina* is absent in the east; since *shechina* is everywhere, the east is no less valid than any other direction. Rather, *mishum de-mori beih minei* — because that is the practice of the heretics, and he did not want his posture to appear to endorse their custom. The identity of these *minim* who specifically pray eastward is not explained in the *gemara*, and the standard commentaries do not provide a clear answer. The most natural association for *minim* in Talmudic literature is with Jewish Christians, though a specific reason for an eastward prayer orientation in that context is unclear; sun-worshippers praying toward the rising sun is another possibility.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* makes explicit what the *gemara* has left implicit throughout this entire passage. *Tosafot* notes that all of these *amora'im* — those who hold *shechina bechol makom* and those who hold *shechina b'ma'arav* — appear not to accept the ruling cited in Berachot that one is obligated to pray toward *Yerushalayim*, based on the verse *veyitpallelu eilecha derech artzam*. If the westward orientation had simply been about *Eretz Yisrael* being to the west of *Bavel*, it would not apply universally — someone in America would not pray westward on that logic. But those who say the *shechina* is always in the west mean it universally, regardless of geographic relationship to *Eretz Yisrael*. And those who say *shechina bechol makom* — like Rav Sheshet — hold that one may pray in any direction at all, which straightforwardly contradicts the *Berachot* ruling.

*Tosafot* concludes that these *amora'im* follow the position of Rabbi Yishmael — *shechina bechol makom* — while those who say the *shechina* is specifically in the west align with a reading close to *Rabbi Akiva*'s position that the west is *nefishei hidira*, most regularly associated with divine presence. Only the final opinion to be discussed at the end of the *sugya* — that one in the north of *Eretz Yisrael* should pray southward — follows the *baraita* in Berachot that prayer must be directed toward *Eretz Yisrael*.

This makes the theology of the *sugya* unusually layered. A post-*churban* reality has generated competing responses: one group says God's presence is concentrated in the west, which has nothing to do with the destroyed *Mikdash*; another group says God's presence is everywhere, which similarly has nothing to do with *makom ha-mikdash*; and only the final voice in the *sugya* reasserts *Beit HaMikdash* orientation, though by that point framed around *Eretz Yisrael* rather than the *Mikdash* itself. This is worth pausing over. Just as *Torah* study becomes the universal locus of *kedushah* in the post-*churban* world — *ein laKadosh Baruch Hu b'olamo ella arba amot shel halacha bilvad* — so too the *shechina* is reconceptualized: *shechina bechol makom*, the divine presence accessible anywhere, rather than concentrated in a destroyed sanctuary. The irony that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi grounds this in a verse from Nechemya — written precisely in the context of rebuilding the *Beit HaMikdash* — makes the theological move all the more striking.

## Directions & Cosmology

### Directional Etymology

The *gemara*'s discussion of wind directions invites a broader treatment of directional terminology. There are two distinct sets of names for the cardinal directions in *lashon ha-kodesh*, and their etymologies reflect entirely different orientational frameworks.

The first set — *kedem* (east), *achor* (west), *teiman/yamin* (south), and *smol* (north) — derives from the orientation of a person facing the rising sun. A human being naturally orients toward the east, toward the light; hence the very word *orient* in English means "east," from the Latin for rising. With the east as *kedem* — one's front — the west naturally becomes *achor*, one's back. The right hand, when facing east, points south, giving *teiman* from *yamin*; the left hand points north, giving *smol*. *Tosafot* cites several scriptural proofs for this usage: the verse *achor va-kedem tzartani*, interpreted in Chagiga as referring to east and west; and *Aram mi-kedem u-Felishtim me-achor*, where *achor* means west. The word *acharei* in Devarim — *halo heimah b'ever ha-Yarden acharei derech mevo ha-shemesh* — similarly appears to mean simply "westward," toward where the sun sets.

*Tosafot* notes that this set of names can also be derived from the *shechina*'s perspective. If the *shechina* faces east (*p'nei ha-shechina l'mizrach*), then from the *shechina*'s vantage point the west is *achor*, the east is *kedem*, the south is *yamin*, and the north is *smol* — the same map, but oriented from the divine perspective rather than the human one.

### Wind Direction Names

The second set of directional names — *mizrach*, *ma'arav*, *darom*, *tzafon* — derives from the behavior of the sun. *Mizrach* comes from *zerichah*, the shining or rising of the sun: the east is where the sun shines forth. *Ma'arav* relates to *erev*, evening: the west is where night begins. *Darom* is the south because that is where the sun is at its zenith during daylight hours — rising in the east, reaching its height in the south, setting in the west. *Tzafon* is north because the sun is hidden (*tzafun*) from the north; the sun never travels into the northern hemisphere, so the north is the concealed, unilluminated direction. These two etymological systems thus reflect two different ways of mapping the world — one from the standpoint of a human being facing the sun, the other from the standpoint of the sun's daily movement through the sky.

### Portico Model

The *gemara* now presents two competing cosmological models for understanding how the sun returns from west to east each night. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *olam le-achsadrah hu domeh* — the world resembles a *portico*, a structure with three enclosed walls and one open side. In his model, the northern side of the world is not sealed. *Ve-chivan she-higia chama etzel keren ma'aravit tzafonit, nichsefet ve-olah le-ma'alah min ha-rakia* — when the sun reaches the northwest corner of the sky, it bends around and travels above the *rakia* to return to the east. The open northern side allows the sun to loop back without being enclosed, making the northern wall unnecessary.

### Tent Model

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees: *olam le-kuvah hu domeh* — the world is like a tent, enclosed on all sides. *Ve-ruach tzafonit mesovevet* — the northern side is in fact enclosed. *Ve-chivan she-chama maga'at le-keren ma'aravit tzafonit, mekapelet ve-chozeret achorei kippah* — when the sun reaches the northwest corner, it folds back and travels behind the *kippah*, the dome, to return. The proof-text from Kohelet is: *holech el darom ve-sovev el tzafon* — it goes to the south (by day, traveling the visible arc) and it circuits to the north (by night, traveling behind the enclosed dome). *Holech* refers to the sun's visible daytime movement beneath the dome; *sovev* refers to its hidden nighttime movement behind or around the dome. Since the sun does travel through the north during its hidden return journey, the dome must be sealed there as well to prevent light from coming through at night.

The earlier student question helpfully clarifies the key distinction: in Rabbi Eliezer's model, the sun at night reverses direction and returns through the south, never actually passing through the north — hence no northern seal is needed. In Rabbi Yehoshua's model, the sun continues in the same direction and loops back through the north — hence the northern enclosure is essential to prevent nocturnal illumination.

### Seasonal Variation

The *gemara* adds a further observation about the east-west axis: *elu pnei mizrach u-pnei ma'arav, she-pe'amim mesovevtan u-pe'amim mehalchtan* — with regard to the eastern and western faces of the world, sometimes the sun traverses them visibly (*mehalchtan*) and sometimes it traverses them hidden (*mesovevtan*). This refers to the variation between summer and winter.

### Rashi's Explanation

*Rashi* explains: on a long summer day (*beyom aroch shel tekufat Tammuz*), the sun rises from the northeast corner and sets in the northwest corner, meaning that a substantial portion of its east-west movement occurs visibly during daylight. On a short winter day, the sun rises from the southeast corner and sets in the southwest corner; its arc barely extends northward at all during the day, and *umerachikin balayla gimmel ruchot* — it covers three directions during the night, traversing north, east, and much of the eastern and western approach through the hidden path behind the *kippah*. Thus the same east and west directions are sometimes traversed in the visible daytime arc and sometimes in the hidden nocturnal arc, depending on the season.

## Post-Churban Reality

### Southern Wind Loss

Having concluded the cosmological discussions, the *gemara* turns to a series of statements from Rav Chisda (as transmitted by Rafram bar Pappa) that re-engage with the post-*churban* reality. *Mi-yom she-charav Beit HaMikdash, lo hugshama ruach dromit* — from the day the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed, the southern wind no longer brings the kind of abundant rainfall it once did. The proof-text: *va-yigzor al yamin va-ra'ev* — he decreed upon the right (i.e., the south) and there was hunger. *Va-yochal al smolo ve-lo save'u* — they ate from the left (the north) and were not sated. The first verse establishes that *yamin* (south) no longer provides abundance; the second demonstrates the equation of *yamin* with south and *smol* with north.

### Exile Consequences

A second statement from the same chain: *mi-yom she-charav Beit HaMikdash, ein ha-geshamim yordim me-otzar tov* — since the destruction, rain no longer falls from the "good storehouse." The verse cited, *yiftach Hashem lecha et otzaro ha-tov*, contains the phrase *lates matar artzecha* — to give rain upon your land. *Rashi* notes that the verse ties the *otzar tov* explicitly to Israel's dwelling upon its land. Thus the loss of rain from the *otzar tov* is not solely about the absence of the *Mikdash* but about exile itself: *bi-zman she-Yisrael shruyim al admasam, geshamim yordim me-otzar tov; bi-zman she-ein Yisrael shruyim al admasam, ein ha-geshamim yordim me-otzar tov*. When Israel dwells on its land in accordance with God's will, rain comes from the good storehouse; in exile, that channel is closed.

### Prayer Directions

Rabbi Yitzchak offers a striking practical teaching that reconnects directionality with *Beit HaMikdash* symbolism: *ha-rotzeh she-yechkam, yadrim; ve-she-ya'ashir, yatzpin* — one who wants wisdom should pray southward; one who wants wealth should pray northward.

### Beit HaMikdash Signs

The mnemonic: *shulchan ba-tzafon, u-menorah ba-darom* — in the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *shulchan* (table, bearing the *lechem ha-panim*) was positioned to the north and the *menorah* to the south. The table represents material sustenance — bread, wealth — and the *menorah* represents light and wisdom. One who prays in the direction of the *menorah* prays for wisdom; one who prays in the direction of the *shulchan* prays for wealth. This is a *Beit HaMikdash*-oriented framework, in marked contrast to the earlier portions of the *sugya* that ignored the *Mikdash* entirely.

### Wisdom to Wealth

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, however, says simply: *le-olam yadrim* — one should always pray southward. His reasoning: *mi-toch she-mis-chakim mis-asher* — wisdom leads to wealth, so if one prays for wisdom, wealth will follow. The verse supports this: *orech yamim bi-minah, u-vi-smolah osher ve-chavod* — in its right hand (*yamin* = south) are long days, and in its left hand (*smol* = north) are wealth and honor. The south thus yields wisdom *and*, through wisdom, wealth as well.

The *gemara* then raises an internal contradiction: *va-ha Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi amar shechina bi-ma'arav* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was also the one who said the *shechina* is in the west and that we should pray westward. How can he now be advocating southward prayer? The resolution: *di-metzadei itztidodei* — one angles oneself, orienting somewhat to the west and somewhat to the south simultaneously.

### Eretz Yisrael Orientation

The final ruling of the *sugya* is addressed by Rabbi Chanina to Rav Ashi: *ke-gon attun de-yasvin bi-tzfona de-Eretz Yisrael, adrimu* — you, who sit to the north of *Eretz Yisrael* (in *Bavel*), should pray southward. The source: *mi-tzafon tipasach ha-ra'ah al kol yoshvei ha-aretz* — from the north, evil shall open upon all inhabitants of the land, establishing that *Bavel* lies to the north of *Eretz Yisrael*. Therefore, one in *Bavel* prays southward — toward *Eretz Yisrael*.

The *sugya* thus ends by reasserting the *Eretz Yisrael* orientation that its earlier portions had conspicuously bypassed. *Tosafot*'s observation is borne out by the structure of the *sugya* itself: the early voices — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's sun-bowing argument, Rabbi Oshaya's and Rabbi Yishmael's omnipresence proofs, and Rav Sheshet's indifference to direction — all operate within a framework that does not depend on *makom ha-mikdash*. Only at the end does the *sugya* explicitly reintegrate *Beit HaMikdash* symbolism through Rabbi Yitzchak's *shulchan* and *menorah* mnemonic, and *Eretz Yisrael* orientation through Rabbi Chanina's ruling. The tension between these two theologies — *shechina bechol makom* and the continued primacy of *Eretz Yisrael* and the *Mikdash* as loci of divine encounter — runs through the entire passage and is left, characteristically, unresolved.

## Flax & Vegetables

### Flax Distance

The *mishna* concludes with several additional neighbor-damage rules. *Merachikin et ha-mishrah min ha-yerek* — one must keep the place where flax is soaked away from vegetables. Soaking flax generates significant odor, which damages neighboring produce.

### Rabbi Yosi Mustard

The *mishna* then records a case of mutual harm: *ve-et ha-chardal min ha-devorim* — mustard must be kept away from bees, because bees consume the mustard flowers and the resulting honey is adversely affected. The first opinion — by implication the *tanna kamma* — requires this distance. *Rabbi Yosi*, however, permits the mustard: *sheyachol lomar lecha, ad she-ata omer li harcheik chardalcha mi-dvorai, harcheik dvoracha mi-chardali* — he can respond: before you tell me to keep my mustard away from your bees, I can equally tell you to keep your bees away from my mustard, since they come and consume the flowers of my mustard plant.

### Mutual Harm

The underlying principle invoked by Rabbi Yosi is one of reciprocal harm: since each party is simultaneously *mazik* (the damaging party) and *nizak* (the damaged party) with respect to the other, neither has an unambiguous right to demand that the other relocate. One could theoretically argue the opposite — that each party *does* have the right to demand the other's removal — but Rabbi Yosi holds that mutual harm cancels the respective claims, and neither party can compel the other to move. This reflects a broader position of Rabbi Yosi's that appears in various forms throughout the tractate on the question of *al ha-mazik leharchik o al ha-nizak leharchik*. The *mishna* does not specify a distance for the mustard-and-bees case, and notably, the distance is left open — perhaps to be determined case by case, or perhaps fifty *amot* by analogy, though the text does not say so explicitly.
