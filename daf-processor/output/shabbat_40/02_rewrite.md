# Shabbat 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sheiya to Bishul

### Fire vs. Sun Heating

Having completed the discussion of *sheiya* issues, the daf now moves into the heart of *bishul* on Shabbat. The prior daf established a fundamental distinction: using fire to heat something is a potential *de'oraita* violation, whereas heating through the sun — *bishul chama* — is not a biblical concern.

### Toldos Chama/Or

The Bavli refines this distinction through the categories of *toldos chama* and *toldos or*. *Toldos or* — heat derived from fire rather than from fire itself — appears to carry potential biblical status, at least based on the case of placing an egg beside a hot water pot. *Toldos chama*, by contrast, is certainly not a biblical violation, though whether it carries rabbinic force is itself debated: since it can be confused with *toldos ha'or*, it may in practice be treated more stringently than direct solar heating.

### Egg/Kettle Case

The case of the egg placed beside a kettle of boiling water established a further principle: one need not place something directly upon a fire for *bishul de'oraita* to apply. Rav Yosef's reading of the Mishnah makes clear that proximity to an intense heat source is sufficient.

---

## Tverya Water Pipe

### Tverya Incident

The Mishnah records a case involving the people of Tverya who ran a pipe of cold water through the town's famous hot springs — *chamei Tverya* — in order to heat it. The rabbis ruled that this water must be treated as though it had been heated on Shabbat itself, and accordingly one may derive no benefit from it. If the infraction occurred on Shabbat, the water is forbidden for drinking and bathing alike. If the infraction occurred on Yom Tov, the water may be used for drinking but not for bathing the entire body.

The precise nature of the violation is somewhat ambiguous. One reading — which appears to be the Bavli's primary understanding — is that the pipe was set up *erev Shabbat*, creating a concern of *hatmanah*, insulation that is problematic even before Shabbat. An alternative reading, perhaps more naturally suggested by the Yerushalmi, is that the cold water was piped through on Shabbat itself and was actively heated on Shabbat through contact with the springs, constituting *toldos chama* in the most direct sense.

### Rav Ika Query

This leads to a difficulty. If the water was heated *erev Shabbat*, it should in principle be permitted — water heated before Shabbat, after all, may generally be used on Shabbat. Yet we know from elsewhere that even water heated *erev Shabbat* may not be used for bathing the entire body. At the same time, if we are speaking only of hands, face, and feet, that usage is permitted — so why should Yom Tov present any additional problem? The question then is: what is the intermediate category, something permitted when heated *erev Shabbat* but forbidden both on Shabbat and on Yom Tov?

Rav Ika bar Chanina resolves this by specifying that the case involves *lehishtatef pnei kol gufo* — pouring water over the entire body, a full-body shower rather than immersion in a bath — and that the ruling follows a specific Tanna in the following *beraisa*.

---

## Bathing Debate

### Showering Definition

The term *lehishtatef* denotes showering — pouring water over the whole body — as distinct from sitting in a bath. This definition is critical to understanding the three-way tannaitic dispute that follows.

### Three Positions

The *beraisa* records the following positions. Rabbi Meir rules most stringently: *lo yishtatef adam kol gufo bein bechamin bein betzonen* — a person may not pour water over his entire body whether in hot or cold water. The reasoning behind Rabbi Meir's prohibition of cold water is not entirely clear; it may be a concern that beginning with cold water will lead to hot water, or that it will lead to wringing out towels, or to some other prohibited activity. Rabbi Shimon is most lenient: full-body showering is permitted even with hot water, provided that water was heated before Shabbat — immersion in a bath remains forbidden, but showering is allowed. Rabbi Yehuda takes the middle position: *bechamin assur betzonen mutar* — hot water is forbidden, cold water is permitted.

### Resolve Tverya

The Tverya case is now resolved according to Rabbi Shimon. Ordinarily, water heated *erev Shabbat* may be used for a full-body shower per Rabbi Shimon's ruling. Since the rabbis decreed that the water from the Tverya pipe must be treated as though it were heated on Shabbat itself, it loses that leniency — one may neither drink it nor shower with it. Similarly, on Yom Tov, water heated on Yom Tov is permitted for hands and face but not for showering the whole body. Thus the case occupies a precise middle ground: had it genuinely been heated *erev Shabbat*, a shower would have been permitted per Rabbi Shimon; because it is treated as Shabbat-heated water, even the shower is forbidden; and on Yom Tov, even the permitted Yom Tov heating does not extend to the entire body.

## Yom Tov Heating

### Yom Tov Problem

This analysis raises a broader and more challenging question: why may one not heat water for bathing on Yom Tov at all? Unlike Shabbat, on Yom Tov fire is permitted for bodily needs. People who smoke, for example, have long claimed — and in some cases legitimately — that smoking qualifies as a *tzorach haguf*, a bodily need permitted on Yom Tov. Why, then, should heating water for a hot bath or shower be categorically forbidden? After all, bathing is surely a bodily need.

The concern that gave rise to the Shabbat restriction is clear enough: the institution of the public bathhouse created a situation in which, if hot bathing from pre-Shabbat water were permitted, bathhouse attendants would inevitably heat the water on Shabbat itself and claim it had been prepared beforehand. That structural concern, however, does not apply on Yom Tov in the same way, since fire is permitted. The Gemara proceeds to address this through a debate between the Rishonim.

### Ramban: Lo Pelug

The Ramban explains the Yom Tov prohibition as a *lo pelug*: since the rabbis prohibited hot bathing on Shabbat even from water heated before Shabbat, they made no distinction and extended the prohibition to Yom Tov as well. This is admittedly a striking *lo pelug*, given how many uses of fire and heat are permitted on Yom Tov. The Ramban's position means the restriction is purely rabbinic with no structural justification on Yom Tov itself — it is simply an undifferentiated blanket decree.

### Tosafot: Taanug

Tosafot, citing the Riva, offers a fundamentally different explanation. In the *tosafot* beginning *u'Beis Hillel matirin*, Tosafot writes *de-rechitza eina ela le-taanug ve-assura kmo mugmar mishum de-ein shava le-chol nefesh* — bathing the entire body in hot water is classified as a pleasure, a *taanug*, that is not universally shared, analogous to burning incense. Since it is *ein shava le-chol nefesh* — not a common bodily need of the general population — it falls outside the category of legitimate Yom Tov use. On Yom Tov one may perform labor for needs that are universal or near-universal; a luxury enjoyed by only a segment of the population does not qualify.

### Modern Practice

This Tosafot carries significant practical implications. If the Ramban is correct that the prohibition is a *lo pelug*, it is a rabbinic restriction with no sensitivity to changed circumstances. But if Tosafot is correct, the prohibition rests on an empirical assessment that hot bathing is not a common need — an assessment that is plainly different in the modern world, where regular bathing and showering are universal practice.

There are indeed *poskim* who rule accordingly, including the *Shemiras Shabbos ke-Hilchatah*, who in at least one extended footnote argues — though his position may evolve across editions — that a hot shower today, given that people shower regularly, should not be prohibited on Yom Tov. One must in any case be careful regarding *sechita* — the prohibition of wringing out — which applies to wet hair and towels; one should allow hair to drip dry or pat it dry rather than wringing it.

The Gemara's assumption that hot bathing on Yom Tov is self-evidently forbidden is thus one that contemporary *poskim* following Tosafot's reasoning may revisit.

A related and unresolved question concerns women who immerse in a *mikvah* on Friday night, typically in heated water. The full-body immersion in hot water would appear to raise the same concerns as bathing. While many *poskim* have noted the tension and offered various justifications — some suggesting that perhaps the water is merely warm rather than hot — the practice has not been discontinued. The implicit acceptance by *poskim* is itself a form of ruling, even if an acknowledged accommodation. Separately, Ashkenazic practice extends even to cold water: the thousand-year-old *minhag* among Ashkenazim is not to immerse the entire body even in cold water on Shabbat. This goes beyond anything in the Gemara, which limited the concern to hot water. Sephardim do not share this *minhag* and in practice will swim in a pool on Shabbat — a topic the daf will address later.

## Halacha L'Rav Yehuda

### Vessel vs. Ground

Rav Chisda introduces a distinction to help situate the tannaitic debate. He rules *machlokes b'kli* — the three-way dispute of Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Yehuda applies specifically when the water was heated in a vessel. *Aval b'karka divrei hakol mutar* — if the hot water is contained in the ground rather than in a portable vessel, all opinions would permit its use, since in-ground heating is less likely to lead to the appearance of, or actual, heating on Shabbat. The intuition is that a vessel of hot water is more easily associated with fire-heated water and therefore creates a greater concern that someone will heat it on Shabbat; ground-based heat seems less susceptible to that confusion.

### Challenge to Distinction

The Gemara challenges Rav Chisda immediately. The Tverya case — the very case that opened this discussion — involved water flowing through a pipe in the ground, clearly an in-ground scenario, and yet the rabbis forbade it. This overturns Rav Chisda's formulation. The Gemara revises his ruling: the debate in fact applies when the water is *b'karka* — in the ground — while water heated in a vessel is forbidden according to all opinions, since a portable vessel of hot water creates far greater concern of actual Shabbat heating.

### Halacha: Hands/Face/Feet

Rabba bar bar Chana reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*. The ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda's middle position. This is consequential: it means that on Shabbat, even water heated before Shabbat may not be used for a full-body shower — Rabbi Shimon's leniency is rejected — and certainly not for a bath. Only hands, face, and feet are permitted.

### Explicit vs. Inferred

Rav Yosef then asks Rabba bar bar Chana whether this ruling was received explicitly or inferred from a broader principle. *Mai klala* — what general principle might yield this inference? The candidate is the doctrine recorded in the name of Rav Yannai, citing Rav or Rabbi: *kol makom she-ata motzei shnayim cholekin ve-echad machriya, halacha k'divrei hamachriya* — whenever two opinions disagree and a third takes a compromise position, the *halacha* follows the compromise. Here, Rabbi Meir prohibits even cold water, Rabbi Shimon permits even a hot shower, and Rabbi Yehuda prohibits hot but permits cold — a textbook compromise position. One might therefore have inferred the ruling in Rabbi Yehuda's favor from this general doctrine.

There is a well-known exception to this principle, however: the case of *mikulei matlaniyot* — worn rags used for various household purposes — where Rabbi Eliezer was strict, Rabbi Yehoshua was lenient, and Rabbi Akiva offered a compromise. There the ruling does not follow Rabbi Akiva, for two reasons: first, Rabbi Akiva was himself a student of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua and therefore does not have the independent standing required for the *machriya* doctrine to apply; and second, Rabbi Akiva ultimately reversed his own compromise position and aligned with Rabbi Yehoshua. Tosafot here also notes at some length that not every middle position is a genuine compromise — a *machriya* must be a hybrid that incorporates elements of each extreme, not merely a position that happens to fall between them numerically.

A further question arises: even if the *machriya* doctrine applies in principle, perhaps it applies only when the dispute appears in a Mishnah, not in a *beraita*. The logic would be that Rabbi's editorial framing of a dispute in the Mishnah implicitly signals something about the preferred ruling, while a *beraita* carries no such editorial weight. Since the current dispute appears in a *beraita*, the inference from the *machriya* doctrine might not hold.

Rabba bar bar Chana resolves the inquiry: *ana be-feirush shemi li* — he heard it explicitly. The ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda regardless of these doctrinal questions. The practical upshot is clear: on Shabbat, bathing the whole body in hot water is forbidden even from water heated before Shabbat, and showering the whole body is equally forbidden.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav: Limb by Limb

The Gemara now explores the space between the extremes of full-body bathing and hands-face-feet. Rav rules: *le-machar rochetz bahem kol gufo eiver eiver* — one may wash the entire body limb by limb with water heated before Shabbat, washing one limb at a time until the whole body has been washed, as long as the entire body is not bathed simultaneously.

### Shmuel: Limited Areas

Shmuel disagrees sharply: *lo hutru lirkhotz ela panav yadav ve-raglav* — only the face, hands, and feet are permitted. Washing other parts of the body, whether sequentially or otherwise, is forbidden.

### Baraita Challenge

The Gemara challenges Rav's position. A *beraita* states: *chamim she-uchmu me-erev Shabbat, le-machar rochetz bahem panav yadav ve-raglav, aval lo kol gufo* — if water was heated before Shabbat, one may wash one's face, hands, and feet the next day, but not the whole body. Rav responds: *lo kol gufo be-vas achat* — the prohibition of the whole body means all at once; doing it limb by limb is still permitted. He further interprets the permission for *panav yadav ve-raglav* as meaning *ke-ein panav yadav ve-raglav* — individual limbs at a time, not the whole body simultaneously.

### Rava's Teaching

Rava presents Rav's position in a sharper formulation: *amar Rav rochetz bahem kol gufo u-meshayer eiver echad* — one may wash the whole body and merely leave out one limb. This would technically permit showering while leaving one arm outside, for example. The Gemara then marshals the same counter-evidence — that the *beraita* explicitly restricts the permission to face, hands, and feet, and that *eiver eiver* across the whole body is not permitted — and the Gemara declares *tiyuvta*: Rav's position is contradicted.

### Rabba's Practice

Rav Yosef asks Abaye whether Rabba — who had transmitted Rav's teaching in the more lenient formulation — actually practiced according to Rav's ruling. Abaye replies that he does not know. The Gemara asks: why is this even a question? Obviously Rabba would not have followed a position that had been refuted. The Gemara answers: perhaps Rabba had not heard the counter-evidence. Abaye himself testified that Rabba generally followed Rav's rulings in practice: *kol mili de-mar, avid ke-Rav* — all the master's practices accorded with Rav. The exceptions — three cases where Rabba followed Shmuel — were: removing *tzitzit* from one garment to another (*mattilin mi-beged le-beged*), lighting Chanukah candles from one flame to another (*madlikin mi-ner le-ner*), and ruling like Rabbi Shimon on dragging furniture (*halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon be-gireira*).

### Stringency/Leniency Rule

The Gemara responds with a crucial qualification: *ke-chumrei de-Rav avid, ke-kulei de-Rav lo avid* — Rabba followed Rav's stringencies, but not necessarily Rav's leniencies. Since the three cases where Rabba followed Shmuel were precisely cases where Shmuel was lenient, it is possible that Rabba only followed Rav unconditionally when Rav was strict. Here, Rav's position is the lenient one — permitting limb-by-limb washing — and therefore even if Rabba had not heard the counter-evidence, we cannot be certain he would have followed Rav. The question remains open.

The upshot, however, is clear from the Gemara's overall thrust: Rav's position is rejected. One may not wash the entire body limb by limb with hot water on Shabbat, nor may one perform the maneuver of washing the whole body while leaving a single limb out.

---

## Rama's Position

### Rama Allowance

Notwithstanding the Gemara's apparent restriction to hands, face, and feet, the Rama rules that washing other individual limbs is permitted, as long as one does not wash *kol gufo* — the entire body. The Rama's position is that the prohibition targets bathing or showering the whole body; individual limbs beyond the canonical three remain permissible. The distinction between the Rama's ruling and the case rejected by the Gemara is significant: the Gemara's rejected scenario involved washing each limb in sequence with the intent of covering the whole body, or — in Rava's sharper version — literally washing all but one limb. The Rama permits washing isolated individual limbs, stopping short of the totality that the decree was designed to prevent.

## Bathhouse Origin

### Sealed Bathhouse

Having established the rules, the Gemara now traces the historical origin of these restrictions. A *beraisa* discusses a bathhouse whose holes had been stopped up *erev Shabbat*. The bathhouses of the period were heated from below through vents; sealing those vents before Shabbat meant the water was not being heated on Shabbat itself. In such a case, *le-motzaei Shabbat rochetz bo miyad* — one may bathe immediately after Shabbat ends, since the water was not heated on Shabbat and thus carries no prohibition.

### Erev Yom Tov Closure

If the holes were stopped up *erev Yom Tov*, then on Yom Tov itself one may enter the bathhouse, perspire from the steam — *nichnas u-meize* — and then go into the outer chamber and pour cold water over oneself. The full bath in hot water is not permitted on Yom Tov, but benefiting from the steam — a *shvitz*, a sauna experience — is permitted, since the hot water generating the steam was covered with planks (*mechupin be-nesarim*), precluding any possibility of actual bathing. When the matter came before the Sages, they ruled even more leniently: even if the hot water is not covered with planks, the steam bath is permitted, as long as the person does not actually bathe in the hot water.

### Bnei Brak Incident

Rav Yehuda records a concrete incident illustrating this ruling: a story involving the bathhouse of Bnei Brak where the holes had been stopped up *erev Yom Tov*. The next day — perhaps the day following the Pesach Seder — Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya and Rabbi Akiva entered and *heziu bah*, took a steam bath. Afterward, *yatzu ve-nishtatefu be-veis ha-chitzon*, they went into the outer room and poured cold water over themselves. What made this permissible, the *beraisa* notes, was that the hot water was *mechupin be-nesarim* — covered with planks — so there was no possibility of anyone bathing directly in the hot water.

## Progressive Bans

### Initial Permission

The Gemara now provides a remarkable historical account — transmitted in the name of Bar Kapara via Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi via Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi — of how the restrictions on bathing developed in stages, each restriction generating a new loophole that was then closed, until a final reversal was required.

Initially the rule was simple: *be-techila hayu rochtzin be-chamin she-uchmu me-erev Shabbat* — water heated before Shabbat was permitted for full-body bathing on Shabbat. No restriction applied.

### Ban Bathing

*Hitchilu ha-balanim le-hachem be-Shabbat u-me-erev Shabbat huchamu* — the bathhouse attendants began heating the water on Shabbat and claiming it had been heated before Shabbat. This was the structural problem: the public institution of the bathhouse, with professional attendants whose livelihood depended on customers, created an environment where deceptive practice was endemic. The rabbis therefore prohibited hot bathing on Shabbat entirely — *asru et ha-chamin* — even from water genuinely heated before Shabbat. However, they permitted the steam bath — *hitiru et ha-zei'ah* — the *shvitz*, reasoning or assuming that this remained acceptable.

Logically, the distinction is difficult to justify: heating water for a steam bath requires the same fire as heating it for a bath. One possible explanation is that there was a social pressure to leave some avenue open — perhaps reflecting the Roman cultural reality in which bathing had become deeply embedded in daily life, and an absolute prohibition would be unsustainable.

### Ban Steam Bath

The next stage: *adayin hayu rochtzim be-chamin ve-omrim me-zi'in anachnu* — people would actually bathe in hot water and claim they were merely using the steam bath. Since the *shvitz* was permitted, bathers had a ready-made pretext. The rabbis therefore prohibited the steam bath as well — *asru lahem et ha-zei'ah*. In its place, they permitted *chamei Tverya* — the natural hot springs — since that water was not heated by fire at all, being warmed by geological heat or solar energy.

### Ban Hot Springs

In the third stage, *adayin hayu rochtzim be-chamei ha-ur ve-omrim be-chamei Tverya rachatznu* — people would bathe in fire-heated water and claim they had been to the hot springs. This pretense was at least geographically plausible for those in Tverya, though its application elsewhere is puzzling. The rabbis responded by prohibiting *chamei Tverya* as well — *asru lahem chamei Tverya* — and leaving cold water as the only permitted option. Rabbi Meir would have gone further and prohibited even cold water, as recorded earlier in the *beraita*.

### Permit Hot Springs

At this point *ra'u she-ein ha-davar omed lahem* — the Sages recognized that the public simply could not endure such a comprehensive prohibition. The expression *ein rov ha-tzibbur yecholim la'amod bo* is striking: it reflects a pragmatic willingness to reverse a rabbinic decree when it proves unenforceable and causes genuine public hardship. Accordingly, the rabbis reversed themselves and permitted *chamei Tverya*. Hot springs heated by natural means — *chama be-shel chamah* — are permitted. This ruling has significant implications in the Israeli context for the *dud shemesh*, the solar water heater, since water heated by solar energy is in the same category as *chamei Tverya* and is thus permitted for bathing.

The prohibition of the *shvitz*, the steam bath, however, remains in force and was not reversed. Rava notes further that walking through a large-city bathhouse incidentally — *metayel bahen* — without sitting in the steam is permitted, since the steam encountered in passing is incidental. By contrast, the bathhouses of small villages — *kfarim* — are small enough that even walking through them is tantamount to sitting in a sauna, as the steam is dense and concentrated, and this is therefore forbidden.

Rava adds that one who transgresses a rabbinic prohibition is appropriately called *avaryana* — a sinner — as the Gemara demonstrates from the very language of this *beraita*, which calls those who bathed while claiming to be taking a *shvitz* *ovrei aveira* — sinners — even though the underlying violation was purely rabbinic.

## Heating Liquids

### Warming Principle

The Gemara transitions to the direct question of *bishul* — cooking — on Shabbat, now addressing the precise thresholds that define forbidden heating of liquids. The operative rule is introduced through a *beraisa*: *mitchamem adam k'neged ha-medurah ve-yotze u-mishtatef be-tzonen* — a person may warm himself before a fire and then go out and pour cold water over himself, benefiting from the combination of body heat and cold water. However — *u-vilvad shelo yishtatef be-tzonen ve-yitchamem k'neged ha-medurah* — one may not first pour cold water over oneself and then stand before the fire, because this constitutes *mafshir mayim she-alav* — warming the water that is on one's body — which is either a *bishul* concern for the water itself or, per Tosafot's reading, is connected to the broader *gezeira* against bathing the whole body in hot water.

### Water Flask

A second *beraisa* addresses the flask of water: *meivi adam kiton shel mayim u-menicho k'neged ha-medurah* — a person may bring a flask of water and place it before the fire. The condition is *lo bishvil she-yechamu, ela bishvil she-tafig tzintan* — not in order for the water to become genuinely hot, which would constitute cooking, but merely to remove the chill. The line between permissible warming and forbidden *bishul* is defined by the concept of *yad soledet bo*, which will be elaborated momentarily.

The *beraisa* also addresses a hot towel, *aluntis*: one may heat a towel and place it on one's stomach on Shabbat. However, one may not bring a vessel of hot water — a *kumkum* — and place it on one's stomach on Shabbat, not only for the practical danger of spilling but because, per Rashi, it may spill and cover the whole body, amounting to bathing in hot water. Tosafot reads the concern differently: the prohibition relates to the risk of the water falling on the towel and leading to *sechita*, or alternatively to the concern that using a hot water bottle constitutes *refuah* — medicinal treatment — which is separately forbidden on Shabbat.

### Rabbi Yehuda: Oil

Rabbi Yehuda extends the discussion from water to oil: *mevia isha pach shel shemen u-menichato k'neged ha-medurah* — a woman may bring a flask of oil and place it before a fire. The condition again is *lo bishvil she-yevashel, ela bishvil she-yafsher* — not that it should cook, but only that it should liquefy, since oil left at room temperature in certain climates becomes semi-solid. The context suggests this may relate to using oil as a lotion applied to the hands, which would need to be in liquid form.

### Rabban Shimon: Oil

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes a more restrictive position on oil: *isha sacha yada shemen u-mitchamemet k'neged ha-medurah ve-sacha li-vnah ha-katan* — a woman may put oil on her hand and then hold it before the fire, and she may apply it to her child, without concern that the oil is heating up in the process. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel thus permits oil only when it is already on the body; placing oil directly before a fire, even to liquefy it, is problematic according to his view.

## Oil vs. Water

### Oil as Bishul

The Gemara raises the question explicitly: *shemen mahu le-tanna kamma* — what is the status of oil for the first opinion in the *beraita*, who spoke only of water and did not mention oil? Three positions among the Amoraim interpret the range of tannaitic views.

### Lenient: Oil OK

Rabba and Rav Yosef both rule *le-teira* — leniently. According to them, the first *tanna* holds that oil is entirely unproblematic: *shemen af al pi she-yad soledet bo mutar* — oil may be heated even to the point of *yad soledet bo* without constituting *bishul*. The reasoning is *kasavar tanna kamma shemen ein bo mishum bishul* — the first *tanna* holds that the category of *bishul* simply does not apply to oil when it is heated in isolation. Whereas heating water to *yad soledet bo* is meaningful because hot water is actively used — for tea, for cooking — heating oil by itself to that temperature is not a significant or meaningful act and therefore falls outside the definition of *bishul*.

### Strict: Oil Problem

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak reads the first *tanna* most strictly: even below *yad soledet bo*, heating oil constitutes *bishul*. On this reading, *kasavar shemen yesh bo mishum bishul ve-hafshara zo hi bishulah* — oil is subject to *bishul*, and merely liquefying it is already considered cooking. The practical implication is that the first *tanna* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel converge on the same prohibition but differ on the one permitted avenue: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits oil on the body held before the fire (*ke-l'achar yad*), while on Rav Nachman's reading of the first *tanna*, even that would be forbidden.

### Middle: Oil Limits

Rabbi Yehuda takes the middle position: *shemen yesh bo mishum bishul, ve-hafshara lo zo hi bishulah* — oil is subject to *bishul*, but merely liquefying it does not yet constitute *bishul*. Only heating oil to *yad soledet bo* crosses the forbidden threshold.

### Most Strict: Oil

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as noted, holds that *shemen yesh bo mishum bishul ve-hafshara zo hi bishulah* — even liquefying oil is considered *bishul* — and therefore prohibits placing oil directly before the fire entirely. The only permitted avenue for warming oil is to have it on the body and then stand before the fire.

The Gemara notes that on one reading, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the first *tanna* appear identical, both prohibiting even liquefaction. The Gemara resolves this: *ika beinaihu ke-l'achar yad* — the difference lies in the indirect scenario of oil on the body before the fire. The first *tanna* prohibits even that; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits it.

## Yad Soledet Bo

### Yad Soledet Intro

The Gemara now turns to the unifying halachic standard. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel establishes the definitive rule for both liquids: *echad shemen ve-echad mayim, yad soledet bo assur, ein yad soledet bo mutar* — whether oil or water, if heated to *yad soledet bo* it is forbidden; below that temperature it is permitted.

It is important to note what the Gemara is directly addressing here: the question of when heating a liquid itself constitutes *bishul* — the cooking of that liquid. The concept of *yad soledet bo* is subsequently applied by the *poskim* to the related but distinct question of whether a hot liquid is itself capable of cooking other foods placed in it, such as bread placed in hot soup. That broader application — relevant to *bishul achar bishul* and to *keli rishon* and *keli sheni* discussions — is a transfer of the standard developed here, not its primary context in this *sugya*. For solid foods, by contrast, the relevant threshold for *bishul* is functional edibility — whether the food has become *ma'achal ben Drusai* — since the transformation being tracked is a qualitative one. Liquids are different: since their "cooking" consists not in a qualitative change from raw to edible but in reaching a temperature of significance, *yad soledet bo* serves as the operative measure.

### Oil/Water Same

The ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel thus establishes parity between oil and water. Neither the leniency of Rabba and Rav Yosef — that oil is entirely exempt from *bishul* — nor the severity of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — that oil is subject to *bishul* even below *yad soledet bo* — is accepted. The same standard governs both: *yad soledet bo* triggers the prohibition, and below that threshold both are permitted.

### Infant Scald Test

Rachava defines *yad soledet bo* operationally: *kol she-kreiso shel tinok nichveit* — the temperature at which the stomach of an infant would be scalded. One might ask how this aligns with the more intuitive formulation — whether an adult can keep a hand in the water without recoiling. The *poskim* explain that the tests measure different things in a complementary way: the infant's stomach test applies to the briefest contact, even a momentary touch; the adult hand test asks whether one can sustain immersion for several seconds without being forced to withdraw. These two formulations, taken together, mark the threshold zone. Various *poskim* have attempted to translate *yad soledet bo* into precise Fahrenheit or Celsius values, with typical estimates falling somewhere between roughly 103 and 115 degrees Fahrenheit — but, as Rav Yehuda Herzl Henkin has argued, the Sages themselves defined this threshold experientially rather than numerically, and the proper test remains the hand in the water, not a thermometer reading.
