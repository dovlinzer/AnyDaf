# Sotah 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Akarah & Zkeinah

### Women Unable to Conceive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מַחְלוֹקֶת בַּעֲקָרָה וּזְקֵינָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states (24a): **A woman who was pregnant** with the child **of another** man at the time of her marriage…A sexually underdeveloped woman, who is incapable of bearing children [*ailonit*], and an elderly woman, and a woman who is incapable of giving birth for other reasons, neither collect payment of their marriage contracts nor drink the bitter water. Rabbi Elazar says: He can marry another woman and procreate through her; therefore these are considered permitted marriages, and the women can drink the bitter water. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The **dispute** between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Elazar is only **in** the case of **a barren woman and an elderly woman.**

Today's *daf* is 26, and we pick up on 25b with the topic of *muberet chaveiro*. The *mishnah* deals with women who cannot give birth — either because they are elderly (*zkeinah*) or infertile (*akarah*) — and their status with respect to the *sotah* procedure.

### Divorce Requirement

The *Chachamim* ruled that such a woman cannot be made to drink the *sotah* water, because she falls into a category of women whom the husband is obligated to divorce. The reason is that he must fulfill the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu*, and remaining married to a woman incapable of bearing children prevents this. To this, *Rabbi Eliezer* cogently responds: there is no need to divorce her, since he could simply marry another woman and thereby fulfill *pru u'revu*. The marriage to the infertile or elderly woman thus remains valid, and consequently he may administer the *sotah* water.

### Ailonit Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אַיְילוֹנִית — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא שׁוֹתָה וְלֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״, מִי שֶׁדַּרְכָּהּ לְהַזְרִיעַ, יָצְאָה זוֹ שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּהּ לְהַזְרִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard to **a sexually underdeveloped woman, all agree** that **she neither drinks** the bitter water **nor collects** payment of **her marriage contract.** This is **as it is stated** with regard to a *sota* who is found to be innocent of adultery: **"And she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed"** (Numbers 5:28), indicating that the *sota* ritual pertains only to **one whose way is to bear seed** and give birth, **excluding this** sexually underdeveloped woman, **whose way is not to bear seed.**

The *Gemara* then records the view of Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Bar Chana: *machloket be'akarah u'zkeinah* — the debate between the *Chachamim* and *Rabbi Eliezer* concerns only the *akarah* and the *zkeinah*. But regarding the *ailonit*, all agree — *divrei hakol* — that she does not drink and does not collect her *ketuvah*.

### Ailonit & Zera

The *ailonit* is a woman with pronounced male characteristics — she does not develop pubic hair or breasts, and it is physically impossible for her to conceive. The reason she is categorically excluded is not the *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu* but rather a separate derivation from the verse itself: *veniktah venizra zara* — "and she shall be cleansed and bear seed." The Gemara derives: *mi shedarkah lehazria* — the entire *sotah* category pertains only to a woman capable of bearing seed; *yatza zo she'ein darkah lehazria* — it excludes one for whom that is impossible.

Why is this different from an *akarah*? Rashi explains that the *ailonit*'s incapacity has existed from birth — it is a congenital condition, not a circumstantial one. The same logic would apply, presumably, to any woman born without the anatomical capacity to conceive. An *akarah*, by contrast, is someone who might have been expected to conceive; who knows, perhaps the waters themselves would effect a cure. The *ailonit*, however, is categorically outside the framework of *nizra zara* from the outset.

This emphasis on *zera* is worth pausing over. The *sotah* waters cause the guilty woman's body to deteriorate, or alternatively cause an innocent woman to conceive. The jealousy undergirding the entire *sotah* process is not merely about the act of intercourse itself but about the concern over whose child a woman might be carrying. The focus on *zera* throughout this *parsha* reflects that central preoccupation.

## Arusah & Muberet

### Kinuy Timing Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ וּמֵינֶקֶת חֲבֵירוֹ — לֹא שׁוֹתוֹת וְלֹא נוֹטְלוֹת כְּתוּבָּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **A woman who was pregnant** with the child **of another** man at the time of her marriage **and a woman who was nursing** the child **of another** man at the time of her marriage **neither drink** the bitter water **nor collect** payment of their **marriage contracts,** as their marriages were prohibited by rabbinic law. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge from a *beraita* that lists a series of cases. A man who performs *kinuy* — the formal warning of jealousy — directed at his *arusah* (betrothed woman) or his *shomeret yavam* (a widow awaiting *yibum*): if she was secluded with the suspected man (*stirah*) before the couple actually came together through *nisuim* or *yibum*, she cannot be made to drink and does not collect her *ketuvah*. This reflects the principle of *tachat ishah* — the *sotah* laws apply only once the couple is living a fully married life together. However, if the *stirah* occurred after *nisuim* or *yibum* were completed, then the earlier *kinuy* remains operative, as the *mishnah* has already taught.

### Muberet & Meneket

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ וּמֵינֶקֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְאִם נָשָׂא — יוֹצִיא וְלֹא יַחְזִיר עוֹלָמִית. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יוֹצִיא, וּכְשֶׁיַּגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ לִכְנוֹס — יִכְנוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** The reason for this is **as Rabbi Meir would say: A man may not marry a woman who is pregnant** with the child **of another** man **or a woman who is nursing** the child **of another** man, until twenty-four months pass after the baby's birth, so as to ensure that the woman will not become pregnant while the child needs to nurse. **And if he married** her, **he must divorce** her **and may never remarry** her, as the Sages penalized him for transgressing the prohibition. **And the Rabbis say: He must divorce** her, **and when his time to marry** her **arrives,** i.e., twenty-four months after the baby's birth, **he can marry** her again.

Another case where the *sotah* procedure does not apply: a man who married a woman still carrying (*muberet*) or nursing (*meneket*) a child from a previous relationship. This is the position of *Rabbi Meir*, who held that one may not marry a woman who is pregnant with or nursing another man's child — the concern being for the welfare of that child. The worry is that the new husband, whose investment in the child is limited, might be insufficiently careful in ways that could harm the child's health. *Rabbi Meir* maintained that if such a marriage was contracted in violation of this rule, the husband must divorce her (*yotzi*), and moreover, he may never remarry her (*ve'lo yachazir olamit*). Since in *Rabbi Meir*'s view the marriage must be dissolved, the *sotah* procedure cannot be administered.

### Remarriage After Nursing

The *Chachamim*, however, take a more lenient position: *yotzi u'ksheyagia zemana liknos yichnos* — he separates from her, but once the pregnancy or nursing period has concluded, he may return to her. Since the separation is temporary and not a permanent dissolution of the marriage, this woman retains her status within the marital framework, and she can accordingly be made to drink the *sotah* water once the relevant period has passed.

## Beraita Cases List

### Young Man & Old Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָרוֹבֶא שֶׁנָּשָׂא עֲקָרָה וּזְקֵינָה, וְאֵין לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים מֵעִיקָּרָא — לֹא שׁוֹתָה וְלֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל הוּא לִישָּׂא אַחֶרֶת וְלִפְרוֹת וְלִרְבּוֹת הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: In **the** case of a **young man who married a barren woman or an elderly woman, and he did not have a wife and children beforehand,** the woman **neither drinks nor collects** payment of her **marriage contract,** as it is prohibited for him to marry a woman with whom he cannot procreate. **Rabbi Elazar says:** This marriage is not forbidden, as **he can marry another** woman **and procreate through her,** and therefore she can drink the bitter water.

The *Gemara* now cites a comprehensive *beraita* that systematically lists cases in which the *sotah* procedure either does or does not apply. The first case involves a young man who married an *akarah* or *zkeinah* and has no children from a prior wife. In this situation, *lo shotah ve'lo notelet ketubatah* — she cannot be made to drink, and she does not collect her *ketuvah*. This aligns with the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*: since he is obligated to fulfill *pru u'revu* and has no other means of doing so, he must divorce this woman, and therefore the *sotah* framework is inapplicable.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

*Rabbi Eliezer* counters: *yachol lissa acheret velifrot heimena* — he can marry another woman and have children through her. There is therefore no requirement to divorce his current wife, and accordingly she can be made to drink the *sotah* water.

### Kinuy Post-Nisuim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַמְקַנֵּא לַאֲרוּסָתוֹ וּלְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁלּוֹ, וּמִשֶּׁכְּנָסָהּ נִסְתְּרָה — אוֹ שׁוֹתָה, אוֹ לֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּה. מְעוּבֶּרֶת וּמֵינֶקֶת עַצְמוֹ — אוֹ שׁוֹתָה, אוֹ לֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. הָרוֹבֶא שֶׁנָּשָׂא עֲקָרָה וּזְקֵינָה, וְיֵשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים — אוֹ שׁוֹתָה, אוֹ לֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **However,** in the case of **one who issued a warning to his betrothed, or to his** *yevama* while she was a **widow awaiting her *yavam*, and she secluded herself** with the other man **after he consummated** the marriage, **she either drinks** the bitter water **or does not collect** payment of her **marriage contract.** If **his own pregnant or nursing** wife becomes a *sota*, then despite the concern that the bitter water may harm the fetus, she **either drinks** the bitter water **or does not collect** payment of **her marriage contract.** In **the** case of a **young man who married a barren woman or an elderly woman, and he** already **had a wife and children** and was therefore permitted to marry his barren or elderly wife, the woman **either drinks** the bitter water **or does not collect** payment of her **marriage contract.**

The *beraita* continues: if a man performed *kinuy* toward his *arusah* or his *shomeret yavam* while she was still in that pre-marital status, but the *stirah* occurred only after they entered into full marriage (*nisuim* or *yibum*), then *o shotah o lo notelet ketubatah* — she either drinks, or she forfeits her *ketuvah* without drinking.

### Pregnant/Nursing Wife

If his own wife is pregnant with or nursing his own child, *o shotah o lo notelet ketubatah* — she can be made to drink. This case is presented almost as an obvious contrast to the earlier case: he has no reason to divorce her, and we assume he will attend to the health of his own child with appropriate care.

### Young Man Has Children

Similarly, if a young man married an *akarah* or *zkeinah* but already has children from another wife, *o shotah o notelet ketubatah* — there is no *pru u'revu* concern, the marriage stands, and she can be made to drink.

### Mamzer & Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשֶׁת מַמְזֵר לְמַמְזֵר, וְאֵשֶׁת נָתִין לְנָתִין, וְאֵשֶׁת גֵּר וְעֶבֶד מְשׁוּחְרָר, וְאַיְילוֹנִית — אוֹ שׁוֹתָה, אוֹ לֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּה. קָתָנֵי מִיהָא אַיְילוֹנִית, תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: With regard to **the wife of a *mamzer*** who is married **to a *mamzer*** in a permitted marriage, **and the wife of a Gibeonite** who is married **to a Gibeonite** in a permitted marriage, **and the wife of a convert or an emancipated slave, and a sexually underdeveloped woman,** if any of these women becomes a *sota* she **either drinks** the bitter water **or does not collect** payment of her **marriage contract,** as the marriage is permitted. After citing the entire *baraita*, the Gemara explains the difficulty: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that **a sexually underdeveloped woman** can drink the bitter water if the marriage is permitted, and this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Naḥman.**

The *baraita* then lists a series of valid, if unconventional, marriages: the wife of a *mamzer* married to a *mamzeret* or a *giyoret*; the wife of a *natin*; the wife of a *ger* or a freed slave (*eved meshuchrar*); and an *ailonit*. In all these cases, *o shotah o lo notelet ketubatah* — the woman can be made to drink. The *Gemara* will analyze each of these cases momentarily, but first it focuses on the inclusion of the *ailonit*.

## Ailonit Exception

### Beraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב נַחְמָן: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, וַאֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַיְילוֹנִית — לֹא שׁוֹתָה וְלֹא נוֹטֶלֶת כְּתוּבָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״ — מִי שֶׁדַּרְכָּהּ לְהַזְרִיעַ. יָצָאתָה זוֹ — שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּהּ לְהַזְרִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Naḥman** could have **said to you:** There **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to this matter, **and I state** my opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon Ben Elazar says: A sexually underdeveloped woman neither drinks nor collects** payment of her **marriage contract, as it is stated: "And she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed"** (Numbers 5:28), indicating that the *sota* ritual pertains only to **one whose way is to bear seed** and give birth, **excluding this** sexually underdeveloped woman, **whose way is not to bear seed.**

The *beraita* explicitly states that an *ailonit* can be made to drink — *katani miha ailonit*. This appears to be a direct contradiction to Rav Nachman, who held that all authorities agree an *ailonit* cannot drink.

### Tannaitic Dispute

Rav Nachman responds: *tanna'ei hi* — it is a dispute among *Tanna'im*. Rav Nachman aligns himself with one particular *tanna*: *ve'ana d'amri ki hai tanna* — I follow this *tanna*.

### R. Shimon ben Elazar

A *beraita* is then cited: *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* says an *ailonit* does not drink and does not collect her *ketuvah*, from the verse *veniktah venizra zara* — *mi shedarkah lehazria yatzah zo she'ein darkah lehazria*. This is precisely the reasoning articulated earlier by Rav Nachman: the *sotah* procedure is restricted to a woman for whom conception is a natural possibility, categorically excluding the *ailonit*.

### Rabbanan Interpret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה [זָרַע]״, שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה עֲקָרָה נִפְקֶדֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אִם כֵּן, יִסָּתְרוּ כׇּל הָעֲקָרוֹת וְיִפָּקְדוּ, וְזוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְלֹא נִסְתְּרָה הִפְסִידָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, what do they do with this** verse: **"And she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed"?** Since they hold that a sexually underdeveloped woman drinks the bitter water, what do they derive from the verse? The Gemara answers: **They require** it **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"And she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed"** (Numbers 5:28), indicates **that if she was barren, she will be remembered** and conceive a child; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yishmael said to him: If so, all the barren women will seclude themselves** with other men, **and they will be remembered** and conceive after drinking the bitter water and being found innocent; **but that** virtuous barren woman, who does not transgress the prohibition of seclusion, **since she does not seclude herself** with other men, **she loses** the opportunity to receive this blessing.

But how do the *Rabbanan* — who include the *ailonit* — read the same verse? They hold that *veniktah venizra zara* is required for a different teaching, recorded in another *beraita*: *Rabbi Akiva* understands the verse in its most literal sense — that if the woman was infertile (*akarah*) and is innocent, the waters will actually render her fertile. The verse, on his reading, promises a genuine blessing of childbirth where none existed before.

### R. Yishmael Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״, שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה יוֹלֶדֶת בְּצַעַר — יוֹלֶדֶת בְּרֶיוַח, נְקֵבוֹת — יוֹלֶדֶת זְכָרִים, קְצָרִים — יוֹלֶדֶת אֲרוּכִּים, שְׁחוֹרִים — יוֹלֶדֶת לְבָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael continues: **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "And she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed"** (Numbers 5:28)? This means **that if** in the past she **would give birth in pain,** from then on **she will give birth with ease;** if she gave birth to **females, she will** now **give birth to males;** if her children were **short, she will** now **give birth to tall** children; if her children were **black, she will give birth to white** children.

*Rabbi Yishmael* found this reading troubling. If it were true that the *sotah* waters cure infertility, one would create a perverse incentive: *im ken yistaru kol ha'akarot ve'yifkedu* — all infertile women would arrange for their husbands to perform *kinuy* and engineer a *stirah* so that they could drink and become fertile. Beyond the moral problem, *Rabbi Yishmael* notes the injustice: *ve'zo ho'il ve'lo nistarah hifsidah* — if an infertile woman who never went into seclusion with another man is thereby denied this miraculous cure, she has been treated unfairly for simply having remained faithful.

### Childbirth Benefits

Rather, *Rabbi Yishmael* proposes that the verse promises more modest improvements: *she'im haytah yoledet b'tzaar teled b'revach* — if she previously gave birth with great pain, she will now give birth with ease. *Nekeivot teled zecharim* — if she previously bore daughters, she will now bear sons, since sons were socially preferred in that context. *Ketzarim teled aruchim* — if her children were short, they will now be tall. *Shechorim teled levanim* — if they were dark-skinned, they will now be lighter-skinned. These details offer a striking sociological window into the values and aesthetic preferences of the time. *Rabbi Yishmael* was unconcerned that women would engineer the *sotah* process merely for these secondary benefits; the incentive is simply not strong enough. The radical incentive — moving from infertility to fertility — was the problem, not these more modest enhancements.

## Valid Marriages

### Mamzer Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשֶׁת מַמְזֵר לְמַמְזֵר. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אַפּוֹשֵׁי פְּסוּלִין לָא לַיפֵּשׁ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* in the *Tosefta* cited above states: **The wife of a *mamzer*** who is married **to a *mamzer*** in a permitted marriage… either drinks the bitter water or does not collect payment of her marriage contract. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** that **obvious?** Since their marriage is permitted, why should the *sota* ritual not apply? The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that she should not drink, since if she drinks and is found to be innocent of adultery, she is permitted to her husband. This is undesirable since their offspring are also *mamzerim*, and **we do not cause** the number of individuals **of flawed lineage to proliferate.** The *baraita* in the *Tosefta* therefore **teaches us** that this is not a concern, and the wife of a *mamzer* is permitted to drink.

The *Gemara* now returns to analyze the *beraita*'s list of cases. If a *mamzer* is married to a *mamzeret* or a *giyoret*, *peshita* — obviously it is a valid marriage. Why would we think otherwise? The concern might be: *afushei psulim lo lifshash* — perhaps one should not encourage a *mamzer* and *mamzeret* to remain married, given that their children will also be *mamzerim*. The *Gemara* therefore teaches that we are not concerned with this; people have a right to marry, and the *sotah* procedure applies to this couple.

### Ger Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשֶׁת גֵּר וְעֶבֶד מְשׁוּחְרָר וְאַיְילוֹנִית. פְּשִׁיטָא?
>
> **Translation:** The aforementioned *baraita* in the *Tosefta* states: **The wife of a convert or an emancipated slave, and a sexually underdeveloped woman** can drink the bitter water. The Gemara asks with regard to the wife of a convert or an emancipated slave, who also has the status of a convert: **Isn't** that **obvious?** Since their marriage is permitted, why should the *sota* ritual not apply?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְלֹא גֵּרִים, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי! ״וְאָמַרְתָּ״ — רִבּוּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that she does not drink, as the verse states: **"Speak to the children of Israel,** and say unto them: If the wife of any man goes astray, and acts unfaithfully against him" (Numbers 5:12). One might have inferred from this verse that the *sota* ritual applies only to those born as Jews **and not** to **converts;** the *baraita* in the *Tosefta* therefore **teaches us** that this is not so. The Gemara asks: Why not **say** that **indeed** the verse excludes converts? The Gemara answers: The subsequent term: **"And say** unto them" (Numbers 5:12) **is an amplification,** which serves to include converts.

The wife of a *ger* or *eved meshuchrar* — again, *peshita*, obviously. The concern is the verse *dabber el bnei Yisrael* — "speak to the children of Israel" — which might be read as excluding *gerim*. The *Gemara* responds that *ve'amarta aleihem* — "and you shall say to them" — serves as a *ribbuy*, an expansion, including *gerim* within the *parsha*. And an *eved meshuchrar* has the same status as a *ger*.

This formulation is worth noting carefully. The *Gemara* implies that a *ger* is not encompassed within the literal meaning of *bnei Yisrael* and requires a separate *ribbuy* to be included. It may be that the issue is specifically with *bnei Yisrael* — "children of Israel" — which focuses on patrilineal descent, rather than with *Yisrael* more broadly. Even so, the apparent need for an additional verse to include *gerim* is striking.

### Kohen's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן שׁוֹתָה כּוּ׳. אֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן שׁוֹתָה — פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה״ — אֲסוּרָה, הָא נִתְפָּשָׂה — מוּתֶּרֶת. וְזוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְנִתְפָּשָׂה אֲסוּרָה — אֵימָא לָא תִּשְׁתֵּה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **The wife of a priest drinks** the bitter water, and if she is found to be innocent of adultery she is permitted to her husband. The Gemara asks: Why does the mishna state: **The wife of a priest drinks? Isn't** that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that she does not drink, as the verse states: "And a man lay with **her…neither was she seized"** (Numbers 5:13). This indicates that if the *sota* was not seized **she is forbidden; however,** if she was **seized,** i.e., raped, she is **permitted** to her husband. **And** with regard to **this** woman, the wife of a priest, **since** even if she was **seized** she is **forbidden** to her husband, as a priest may not remain married to his wife if she was raped while they were married, one might **say** that the *sota* ritual does not apply to her, and she **does not drink.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that she does drink.

The case of the wife of a *kohen* returns us to the *mishnah*. *Peshita* — obviously she can drink. Why might one have thought otherwise?

### Rape & Kohen

The verse states *ve'hi lo nitpasa asura* — "and if she was not raped, she is forbidden" — implying that if she was raped, she is *permitted* to her husband. This *halakha* applies to ordinary women. But the wife of a *kohen* is subject to a more stringent rule: even if she was raped, she becomes forbidden to her *kohen* husband. Since the *pesukim* seem to presuppose that rape results in the woman remaining permitted, one might have thought the *parsha* does not apply to the *kohen*'s wife at all, and that she therefore cannot be made to drink. The *Gemara* teaches that she is nonetheless included in the *sotah* procedure.

### Kohen's Wife Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוּתֶּרֶת לְבַעְלָהּ. פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּמִתְנַוְּונָה. מִתְנַוְּונָה — הָא בַּדְקוּהָ מַיָּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: The wife of a priest drinks, **and** if she is found to be innocent of adultery, **she is permitted to her husband.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** that **obvious? Rav Huna says:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the woman's health **deteriorates** after she drinks the bitter water, and one might have thought that she is defiled. The Gemara asks: In the case of a woman whose health **deteriorates, hasn't the** bitter **water** already **evaluated** that **she** was unfaithful? The fact that her health deteriorates indicates that she is defiled and forbidden to her husband, and her death is delayed due to her merit in other matters.

Furthermore, once she drinks and the waters demonstrate her innocence, she is permitted to return to her *kohen* husband. This too seems obvious — *peshita*.

### Illness Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִתְנַוְּונָה דֶּרֶךְ אֵבָרִים. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָא זַנּוֹיֵי זַנַּאי, וְהָא דְּלָא בַּדְקוּהָ מַיָּא כִּי אוֹרְחַיְה[וּ] — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְאוֹנֶס זַנַּאי, וּלְגַבֵּי כֹּהֵן אֲסִירָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna is referring to a case **where her** health **deteriorates,** but not in the manner of a *sota*, who is afflicted in her belly and thighs (see Numbers 5:27). Rather, she is afflicted **by way of** other **limbs. Lest you say: This** woman **engaged in licentious intercourse, and** the fact **that the** bitter **water did not evaluate her in** the **usual manner** is **because she engaged in licentious intercourse under duress, and with regard to a priest,** even rape **renders her forbidden to her**  husband, the mishna therefore **teaches us** that the woman's deteriorating health does not indicate anything.

Rav Papa addresses a subtle case: a woman who is showing signs of physical deterioration — *misnavna*. We know that a guilty woman with great merit (*zechut*) may not suffer the full effects of the *sotah* waters immediately but will gradually decline in health over time. One might therefore think that a *kohen*'s wife showing such symptoms should not be permitted to return to her husband, as the symptoms might indicate delayed guilt. The *Gemara* responds: *b'misnavna derech evarim* — the ailments in question are not the characteristic symptoms associated with the *sotah*'s punishment. She has a headache, or various other bodily discomforts — but not the specific deterioration of the thigh and stomach that the *Torah* describes. These general symptoms do not prove guilt.

### Rape Symptoms

But if she is not showing those characteristic symptoms, what is the *chidush*? The *Gemara* explains: *ma d'teima hazinnai zannai* — one might have thought she actually did commit the act willingly, and the reason the waters are not producing their characteristic effect — *v'ha d'lo badku amaya* — is that the act was in fact performed under compulsion (*be-ones*). In the case of the *kohen*'s wife, compulsion makes no moral difference — she is forbidden to him regardless, since any intercourse with another man, even against her will, disqualifies the wife of a *kohen*. One might therefore speculate that the unusual symptom pattern is signaling exactly that: she was compelled, she is therefore forbidden to her *kohen* husband, and yet the waters do not produce the full effect because she bears no moral culpability.

The *Gemara* rules that we do not construct such reasoning out of thin air — *kamashma lan* that she remains permitted. It is worth noting, however, how troubling it is that the *Gemara* employs the term *zannai* — "she fornicated" — in a context where the woman was raped. The *Gemara* appears to use it as a shorthand for "she had intercourse with another man," but the terminology is jarring. A more neutral formulation — *de'ivla*, that she had intercourse — would have been more appropriate.

## Man's Role

### Wife of Seris

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשֶׁת סָרִיס שׁוֹתָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״מִבַּלְעֲדֵי אִישֵׁךְ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי לָאו בַּר הָכִי הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **The wife of a eunuch drinks.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** that **obvious?** Since their marriage is permitted, why should the *sota* ritual not apply? The Gemara replies: It is necessary **lest you say** that she does not drink, since **the Merciful One states** with regard to the *sota*: "But if you have gone astray while under your husband, and if you are defiled, and some man has lain with you **besides your husband"** (Numbers 5:20). This indicates that her husband had lain with her, **and this** husband, the eunuch, **is not capable of that.** The mishna therefore **teaches us** that the wife of a eunuch does drink the bitter water.

The *Gemara* continues: the wife of a *seris* — a man who cannot produce children — can be made to drink the *sotah* water. *Peshita* — obviously. Why might one have thought otherwise? The verse says *mibila'adei ishecha* — "other than your husband" — and one might think the *seris* is not in the category of a husband contemplated by the verse, since the concern over *zera* does not arise with him. The *Gemara* rejects this: he is capable of intercourse and erection, even if he cannot father children. Rashi clarifies that this is not a case of *patzua dakah* — a specific halachic category of genital injury — but simply a man who is infertile by some other means, and who is fully capable of normal marital life.

What is striking here is that the verse does include the language of *shichvat zera*: *ve'shachav ish otah shichvat zera* — "a man lay with her in the lying of seed." One might have thought this demands that the suspected paramour be capable of producing *zera*. The *Gemara* rules that this is not so — just as we concluded (against Rav Nachman's excluded position) that the *ailonit* is included even though she cannot bear *zera*, so too the *seris* is included even though he cannot produce it.

### Seris Does Kinuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל יְדֵי כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת מְקַנִּין. פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: A husband **can issue a warning** to his wife **with regard to all those with whom relations are forbidden,** e.g., her father or brother. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** that **obvious?**

Moreover, the *seris* himself can perform *kinuy*. And if a *seris* had intercourse with the daughter of a *kohen* (*bat kohen*), he can invalidate her — render her unable to eat *terumah* as a result of intercourse with a *pasul* individual, such as a *chalal*.

### Shachuf Kinuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקַנִּין עַל יָדוֹ, פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״וְשָׁכַב אִישׁ אֹתָהּ שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא לָאו בַּר הָכִי הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Shmuel's statement that **one can issue a warning with regard to** a *shaḥuf*, the Gemara asks: **Isn't** that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary, **lest you say** that one cannot issue a warning with regard to a *shaḥuf*, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And a man lay with her carnally [*shikhvat zera*]"** (Numbers 5:13), literally, a lying of seed, **and this** man **is not capable of that,** as he cannot ejaculate. Shmuel therefore **teaches us** that one can issue a warning with regard to a *shaḥuf*.

The same rules apply to a *shachuf* — a man whose body has deteriorated in a way that impairs his sexual function, but who retains some capacity for intercourse. Shmuel teaches that a *shachuf* does *kinuy*, and that intercourse with a *bat kohen* can similarly render her *pesulah*.

### Zera Not Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפוֹסֵל בִּתְרוּמָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל זַרְעוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: דְּאִית לֵיהּ זֶרַע — לִיחַלֵּל, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ זֶרַע — לָא לִיחַלֵּל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Shmuel's statement that a *shaḥuf* who engages in sexual intercourse with the daughter of a priest **disqualifies** her **from** partaking of ***teruma*: Isn't** that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that a *shaḥuf* does not disqualify the daughter of a priest from partaking of *teruma*, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah with regard to a priest: "And **he shall not disqualify his offspring** among his people" (Leviticus 21:15). One might infer from this verse that one **who** can **have offspring disqualifies** a woman from partaking of *teruma*, through forbidden sexual intercourse, and that one **who cannot have offspring does not disqualify** a woman from partaking of *teruma*. Shmuel therefore **teaches us** that this is not so. In any event, Shmuel states that one can issue a warning on account of a *shaḥuf*, unlike the initial interpretation of the mishna.

The *Gemara* makes explicit: *peshita* — obviously he can do *kinuy*. The potential objection would have been *im niphat ve'shachav ish otah shichvat zera* — the verse's mention of *shichvat zera* might have implied that the suspected paramour must be capable of ejaculation. The *Gemara* rules that the *zera* requirement is not operative here; *kamashma lan* that it does not matter whether the man in question can produce *zera*.

### Invalidation Regardless

Similarly, the *shachuf*'s ability to invalidate a *bat kohen* for *terumah* — *peshita*, obviously. The concern might have been: *lo yechalel zaro* — we learn that when a *chalal* has intercourse with a *bat kohen*, she and any offspring become *challal* and *chalalah* through the act. If the child — *zaro* — is the mechanism of *chillul*, perhaps a man who cannot produce *zera* cannot effect the *chillul*. The *Gemara* rules: *kamashma lan* that the disqualification applies regardless.

## Non-Jew & Behemah

### Non-Jew Kinuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְמַעוֹטֵי בְּהֵמָה, דְּאֵין זְנוּת בִּבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Since Rav Hamnuna's statement contradicts the suggestion that the mishna excludes a gentile, the Gemara asks: **Rather, what** does the term: And of one who is not a man, serve **to exclude? Rav Pappa says:** This serves **to exclude an animal, as** the concept of **licentiousness does not** apply **with regard to an animal.** Therefore, the *halakhot* of a *sota* do not apply in this case.

The *Gemara* returns to analyze who is excluded by the phrase *she'eino ish* — a "non-man." Having considered and dismissed various candidates, the discussion now turns to the *oved kochavim* (non-Jew). Rav Hamnuna teaches: a non-Jew can be the subject of a *kinuy* — a husband may warn his wife not to seclude herself with a particular non-Jew — and if she violated the warning, she can be made to undergo the *sotah* procedure. Furthermore, if a non-Jew had intercourse with a *bat kohen*, he renders her invalid for *terumah*.

### Already Forbidden

*Peshita* — obviously a non-Jew can be the subject of *kinuy*. The potential objection is drawn from the *pasuk*'s formulation about the woman becoming forbidden to the *ba'al* and to the *bo'el* simultaneously: this might suggest the entire *parsha* applies only when the act itself would create a new prohibition on the woman relative to the suspected man. But a Jewish woman is already forbidden to a non-Jew — *asura ve'kayma* — so perhaps the *parsha* does not apply?

### Non-Jew Invalidates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפוֹסֵל בִּתְרוּמָה — פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״וּבַת כֹּהֵן כִּי תִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ זָר״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: דְּבַר הֲוָיָה — אִין, דְּלָאו בַּר הֲוָיָה — לָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּפָסֵיל מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Rav Hamnuna's statement that a gentile who engages in sexual intercourse with the daughter of a priest **disqualifies** her **from** partaking of ***teruma*: Isn't** that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that he does not disqualify her, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And if a priest's daughter should be unto a strange man,** she shall not eat of that which is set apart from the holy things" (Leviticus 22:12), indicating that if a woman engages in sexual intercourse with one who is unfit for her, he disqualifies her from partaking of *teruma*. Since the term "should be unto" denotes marriage, one might say **that one who is eligible for betrothal, yes,** he disqualifies the woman; but a gentile, **who is not eligible for betrothal, does not** disqualify her. Rav Hamnuna therefore **teaches us that** a gentile **disqualifies** the woman from partaking of *teruma*, as one can learn **from** the ruling **of Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

The *Gemara* rules that the *parsha* does apply. Regarding the invalidation for *terumah*: *peshita*, obviously. The concern here is the verse *u'vat kohen ki siheye le'ish zar* — when a *bat kohen* is "with a foreign man," this effects her disqualification. One might have thought this refers only to a man she could theoretically be married to — someone with whom *havayah* (a binding marital relationship) is conceivable — and that a non-Jew, with whom marriage is categorically impossible, would fall outside this framework. *Kamashma lan* — we learn from Rav Hamnuna's ruling that she is nonetheless disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: מִנַּיִן לְנׇכְרִי וְעֶבֶד שֶׁבָּאוּ עַל הַכֹּהֶנֶת וְעַל הַלְּוִיָּיה וְעַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁפְּסָלוּהָ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבַת כֹּהֵן כִּי תִהְיֶה אַלְמָנָה וּגְרוּשָׁה״. מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אַלְמְנוּת וְגֵירוּשִׁין בָּהּ, יָצְאוּ נׇכְרִי וְעֶבֶד, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אַלְמְנוּת וְגֵירוּשִׁין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: From where** is it derived **that a gentile or a slave who engaged in sexual intercourse with the daughter of a priest or with the daughter of a Levite or with** the **daughter of an Israelite, disqualified her** from marrying a priest and from partaking of *teruma*? This is derived **as** it **is stated: "But if a priest's daughter should be a widow or a divorcée…**she returns to her father's house…she may eat of her father's bread" (Leviticus 22:13). This indicates that the daughter of a priest returns to eat of her father's bread, i.e., *teruma*, if she engaged in sexual intercourse with **one whose** marriage **to her has** the potential to end in **widowhood or divorce,** i.e., a Jew whom she is permitted to marry. This **excludes a gentile and a slave, whose** marriage **to her does not have** the potential to end in **widowhood or divorce,** as their betrothal is invalid.

The source for this is the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael*: how do we know that if a non-Jew or a slave had intercourse with a *kohenet*, a *leviyah*, or even a *bat Yisrael*, she is rendered *pesulah*? From the verse *u'vat kohen ki siheye almana u'grusha* — "and the daughter of a *kohen* who becomes a widow or a divorcee." *Mi she'yesh lo bah almanus ve'gerushin* — only a man in whose context widowhood and divorce are applicable; *yatza oved kochavim ve'eved she'ein lo bah almanus ve'gerushin* — this excludes the non-Jew and slave, for whom these concepts have no application, meaning that intercourse with such an individual always creates disqualification, since there is no permissible context in which such intercourse could occur without consequence.

### Behemah Exclusion

Rav Papa then provides the answer to what *she'eino ish* actually excludes: *behemah* — an animal. If a husband says "do not be secluded with this animal," that does not constitute a valid *kinuy*, because *ein znus b'vehemah* — there is no concept of *znus* (adulterous violation) with an animal. The act is certainly forbidden, but it does not fall within the category of *znus* that the *sotah parsha* presupposes.

### Korban Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא מִפַּרְזִקְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן אֵין זְנוּת בִּבְהֵמָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תָבִיא אֶתְנַן זוֹנָה וּמְחִיר כֶּלֶב וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava of Parzakya said to Rav Ashi: From where is this matter that the Sages stated** derived, that **licentiousness does not** apply **with regard to an animal?** Rav Ashi replied that it is **as it is written: "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot, or the price of a dog,** into the house of the Lord your God for any vow; for both of them are an abomination to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19). This verse prohibits one from sacrificing an animal that served as payment to a prostitute or as payment for the purchase of a dog.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: אֶתְנַן כֶּלֶב וּמְחִיר זוֹנָה — מוּתָּרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״, שְׁנַיִם וְלֹא אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a mishna (*Temura* 30a): In the converse cases, **the hire of a dog,** i.e., a kosher animal that was given to the owner of a dog as payment for engaging in intercourse with it, **and the price of a prostitute,** i.e., a kosher animal which served as payment in the purchase of a maidservant acquired for prostitution, **are permitted** to be sacrificed, **as** it **is stated** in the aforementioned verse: **For both of them.** This term indicates that only those **two** animals may not be sacrificed, i.e., those which served as the hire of a harlot and as the price of a dog; **and not four** animals, as the reverse cases are excluded from this *halakha*. This indicates that the concept of licentiousness does not apply with regard to animals, as the payment for intercourse with a dog is not considered payment for prostitution.

Rav Mi'Pashika asked Rav Ashi: what is the source for the principle that there is no concept of *znus* with a *behemah*? The answer comes from the verse *lo tavi esnan zona u'mechir kelev* — do not bring to the *Beit HaMikdash* an animal given as payment to a *zona* (prostitute) or acquired in exchange for a dog. A *beraita* derives: if the payment is an animal given in exchange for sleeping with a dog, or an animal given to purchase a *zona* (in Rashi's reading, to purchase a female slave), such an animal is in fact *permitted* as a *korban* — *shenayim ve'lo arba'ah*, only two [items are forbidden], not four. Since payment for intercourse with a dog is not considered *esnan* — the prohibited "harlot's fee" — this demonstrates that intercourse with a *behemah* is not conceptually *znus*, which is the principle applied here.

## Shichvat Zera

### Anal Intercourse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא ״שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״, לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״ — פְּרָט לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Shmuel states that one can issue a warning with regard to a *shaḥuf* even though he is unable to discharge semen. **But rather, why do I** need the verse to state: "And a man lay with her **carnally [*shikhvat zera*]"** (Numbers 5:13)? The Gemara answers: It is **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The term **"*shikhvat zera*" excludes something else.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: פְּרָט לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, ״מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״ כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** meant by the term: **Something else? Rav Sheshet said:** This **excludes** a case **where** the husband **issued a warning to** his wife not to engage in sexual intercourse **in an atypical manner,** i.e., anal intercourse, with another man, and teaches that this is not considered a valid warning. **Rava said to** Rav Sheshet: Intercourse **in an atypical manner** is considered sexual intercourse, as **it is written: "The cohabitations of a woman"** (Leviticus 18:22), indicating that there are two forms of sexual intercourse with a woman, vaginal and anal, and there is no halakhic differentiation between them.

The *Gemara* now asks: given that we have accounted for the exclusion of *behemah*, what work is the term *shichvat zera* doing in the verse? What does it come to exclude? A *beraita* answers: *shichvat zera perat l'davar acher* — it excludes "another matter." What is this "another matter"? Rav Sheshes explains: it excludes a case where the husband's *kinuy* was formulated specifically with reference to anal intercourse (*she'lo k'darkah*). Since that mode of intercourse does not involve the natural context of conception, it is not included within *shichvat zera*, and such a *kinuy* would be invalid.

### Rava's Position

Rava objects to this: *she'lo k'darkah mishkevei isha ksiv* — we know from the *Torah*'s treatment of forbidden sexual acts that anal intercourse between a man and a woman is halachically considered as much an act of intercourse as vaginal intercourse. The term *mishkevei isha* encompasses both modes. One could have argued that this is precisely why *shichvat zera* is needed — to limit the *sotah parsha* to the standard mode of intercourse, given the emphasis on *zera*. But since we have already established that neither the woman nor the suspected man need be capable of producing *zera*, it would be inconsistent to then exclude a mode of intercourse that is otherwise fully recognized as intercourse under *halacha*.

### Non-Intercourse Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: פְּרָט לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ דֶּרֶךְ אֵבָרִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: פְּרִיצוּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הִיא, וּפְרִיצוּתָא מִי אָסַר רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** It **excludes** a case **where** the husband **issued a warning to** his wife not to engage in intimate contact with another man **by way of** other **limbs,** as this is not considered sexual intercourse. **Abaye said to** Rava: That **is merely licentious** behavior, **and does the Merciful One render** a woman **forbidden** to her husband on account of merely **licentious** behavior, without sexual intercourse? Since this does not render her forbidden to her husband, it is obvious that if the husband issues a warning in this manner, violating the warning does not cause her to become a *sota*. The verse is therefore not required to exclude this case.

Rava therefore offers an alternative: *perat le'shikina lah derech evarim* — the verse excludes a case where the *kinuy* was directed at non-intercourse sexual acts: oral contact, or other forms of sexual behavior that do not constitute intercourse. Even though such acts are clearly a serious violation of the marital relationship, they would not trigger the *sotah* procedure and would not render the woman forbidden to her husband.

### Abaye on Kissing

Abaye challenges even this: *peritzuta be'alma hi* — these acts are mere licentiousness. They are obviously not intercourse. Why would I need a verse to exclude them? *Uperitzuta mi assar Rachmana?* — these acts do not make the woman forbidden to her husband in any case. There is simply no need for a verse to teach this. Abaye therefore proposes a different reading: the exclusion is directed at a *kinuy* concerning *neshikah* — a term encompassing the touching of the genitals — what the *Gemara* calls *hachnasat atarah* — the initial physical contact that precedes full intercourse. Though this is a hair's breadth from intercourse, if one holds that *hachnasat atarah* is not yet technically *bi'ah*, then it makes sense for a verse to be needed to exclude it.

### Kinuy Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פְּרָט לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ בִּנְשִׁיקָה. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַעֲרָאָה זוֹ הַכְנָסַת עֲטָרָה, אֲבָל נְשִׁיקָה — וְלֹא כְּלוּם הִיא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָתֵי קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי נְשִׁיקָה. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַעֲרָאָה זוֹ נְשִׁיקָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** The verse **excludes** a case **where** the husband **issued a warning to** his wife **with regard to** engaging in genital **contact** without actual penetration. The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that the definition of the **initial stage of intercourse is the insertion of the corona but** that genital **contact is nothing; this is** the reason **that the verse came to exclude** genital **contact. However, according to the one who says** that the definition of the **initial stage of intercourse is** genital **contact, what is there to say?** Why should this case be excluded from the *halakhot* of a *sota*?

However, the *Gemara* notes that this reasoning works only according to the opinion that *hara'ah* means *hachnasat atarah* — that the beginning of intercourse is defined by full penetration. According to the opinion that *hara'ah* means *neshikah* — that even initial external contact constitutes the commencement of intercourse — it would not make sense for the verse to exclude this, since it is effectively intercourse. Tosafot notes that this would be the point of the exclusion: it is an act of intercourse, but not one in the context of *zera*, and therefore outside the *parsha*. The *Gemara*, however, declines to go in that direction.

### Final Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם — לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ דֶּרֶךְ אֵבָרִים. וּמַהוּ דְּתֵימָא בִּקְפִידָא דְבַעַל תַּלְיָא רַחֲמָנָא, וּבַעַל הָא קָא קָפֵיד, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the verse serves **to** exclude a case **where** the husband **issued a warning to** his wife not to engage in intimate contact with another man **by way of** other **limbs. And** the verse explicitly excludes this case from the *halakhot* of a *sota*, **lest you say that** the woman is rendered a *sota* due to this warning, as **the Merciful One made** this *halakha* **dependent on** the **husband's objection, and** the **husband objects** to contact of this nature. The verse therefore **teaches us** that this is not considered a warning, as it does not involve sexual intercourse.

The *Gemara* therefore returns to Rava's answer: *le'olam le'shikina lah derech evarim* — the verse indeed excludes non-intercourse sexual acts. And in response to Abaye's challenge that such a *kinuy* is obviously ineffective, the *Gemara* clarifies that the novelty lies elsewhere: one might have thought that even without intercourse, the *sotah* procedure should apply because the husband has expressed explicit jealousy — *bikfida deba'al talya milta* — and he is plainly aggrieved. *Kamashma lan* that this is not so: without actual intercourse or a genuine concern about intercourse, the *parsha* of *sotah* does not operate. The husband's jealousy alone, directed at conduct that falls short of intercourse, is insufficient to invoke the *sotah* framework.
