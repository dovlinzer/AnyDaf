# Shabbat 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rings: Tumah vs Shabbat

### Collars & Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאַל תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד מִגָּלִיל הָעֶלְיוֹן אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁחוֹלְקִין בֵּין טַבַּעַת לְטַבַּעַת. אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא לֹא שָׁמַעְתָּ אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת. דְּאִי לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה, דָּא וְדָא חֲדָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain disciple from the Upper Galilee asked Rabbi Eliezer: I heard that one distinguishes between** one type of **ring and** another type of **ring.** However, I do not know with regard to what *halakha* this distinction is made. **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: Perhaps you only heard** that distinction **with regard to the matter of Shabbat;** a ring for adornment may be moved on Shabbat and other rings may not. **As,** with regard **to the matter of ritual impurity, this and that are one** and the same, and there is no distinction between them.

The previous amud had concluded a discussion of the *mishna*'s ruling that various *kolarim* — animal collars — may be immersed without being removed from the animal's neck. The *Gemara* had raised two difficulties. First, how can a collar be *tamei*, given that it is used for an animal rather than a person? The *Gemara* offered two responses: either the collar had originally served as a person's necklace and thereby retained its status as a *kli*, or it is considered a human implement insofar as a person uses it to control the animal. Second, why does the collar not constitute a *chatzitza* when it surrounds the animal's neck during immersion? Again, two answers were offered: either the collar was widened and therefore sits loosely, or it is fashioned as a chain with many gaps and therefore does not interpose.

### Student's Ring Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא? וְהָתְנַן: טַבַּעַת אָדָם טְמֵאָה, וְטַבַּעַת בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַטַּבָּעוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת! כִּי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ, נָמֵי דְּאָדָם קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard **to the matter of ritual impurity, are this and that one** and the same? Are all rings alike? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: **A ring** worn by **a person is ritually impure; however, the ring of an animal, and** that of **utensils, and all other rings** not worn by people **are ritually pure?** Apparently, a distinction is made between different types of rings with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity as well. **When** Rabbi Eliezer **was saying** that statement **to** the disciple, **he too was saying to him** that with regard to ritual impurity there is no distinction between different types **of** rings worn by **a person.**

In the midst of these discussions touching on both *tumah* of *kelim* and the laws of carrying on *Shabbat*, the *Gemara* introduces two illustrative exchanges. A student from the Upper Galilee asked Rabbi Eliezer: *shemati shecholkin bein tabaat l'tabaat* — I have heard that there is a distinction between different types of rings. Rabbi Eliezer replied: perhaps you heard this only with respect to *Shabbat* — *shema lo shamata le'inyan Shabbat* — for as will be established in the next chapter, a ring bearing a seal may not be worn on *Shabbat*, whereas a ring without a seal may be worn. That distinction, however, applies only to *Shabbat*. As for the laws of *tumah*, *d'leinyan tumah davar echad hi* — it is all the same *halacha*.

### Animal vs Human Rings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְאָדָם, דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא? וְהָתַנְיָא: טַבַּעַת שֶׁהִתְקִינָה לַחְגּוֹר בָּהּ מׇתְנָיו וּלְקַשֵּׁר בָּהּ בֵּין כְּתֵפָיו — טְהוֹרָה, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ טְמֵאָה אֶלָּא שֶׁל אֶצְבַּע בִּלְבַד! כִּי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ, נָמֵי דְּאֶצְבַּע קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard **to** rings worn by **a person**, **are this and that one** and the same? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A ring that one fashioned** into a buckle at the end of a belt **to wear it around his waist, or** into a clasp **to tie** garments **between his shoulders, is ritually pure?** The Sages **only said** that a ring is **ritually impure** with regard to a ring **worn on a** person's **finger.** Apparently, there is in fact a distinction between different rings worn by a person. **When** Rabbi Eliezer **was saying** that statement **to** the disciple, **he too was saying to him** that there is no distinction between different types of rings worn on a person's **finger.**

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this claim. Is it truly the case that all rings are identical for purposes of *tumah*? Surely the *mishna* teaches otherwise: *tabaat adam t'mea* — a ring made for a person is *tamei* as a *kli* — whereas *tabaat behemah v'kelim*, a ring made for an animal or for ornamentation of a vessel, *tehorot* — is *tahor*. There is plainly a distinction in *tumah* as well.

The *Gemara* resolves this: when Rabbi Eliezer said all rings are the same for *tumah*, he was speaking specifically about rings intended for human use, not animal rings or ornamental rings. The discussion therefore narrows to human rings.

### Metal & Wood Rings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְאֶצְבַּע דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא? וְהָתְנַן: טַבַּעַת שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת וְחוֹתָמָהּ שֶׁל אַלְמוֹג — טְמֵאָה. הִיא שֶׁל אַלְמוֹג וְחוֹתָמָהּ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת — טְהוֹרָה! כִּי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ, נָמֵי כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard **to** rings worn on a person's **finger, are this and that one** and the same? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: **A ring** made **of metal and its seal** is made **of coral, is ritually impure?** The primary component of the ring, metal, is the determining factor, and a metal utensil can become ritually impure. However, a ring that is made **of coral and its seal is** made **of metal is ritually pure.** Apparently, there is a distinction between different types of finger rings with regard to ritual impurity. **When** Rabbi Eliezer **was saying** that statement **to** the disciple, **he too was saying to him** that there is no distinction between different types of rings **that** are made **entirely of metal.**

But the *Gemara* presses further. Is it truly the case that all human rings are treated identically? A *mishna* teaches: *tabaat shel matechet v'chotama shel almog* — a metal ring with a cedar seal is *tamei*, because the ring is defined by the band that encircles the finger, and a metal *kli* is *tamei*. However, *shel almog v'chotama shel matechet* — a cedar ring with a metal seal is *tahor*, because the ring is then classified as a wooden vessel, and a wooden vessel is not *tamei* unless it has a receptacle, which this does not.

The *Gemara* again retreats: Rabbi Eliezer meant only that all metal finger rings are the same. And this, finally, is where the *Shabbat* distinction re-enters: all metal finger rings are indeed equivalent for *tumah*, but for *Shabbat*, whether the ring bears a seal or not determines whether one may carry it into the public domain.

### Seal on Rings

The upshot of the exchange is that the student had likely encountered the *Shabbat* distinction and misapplied it to *tumah*. The *Gemara* carefully threads through multiple layers — ring for animal versus human, belt-hook ring versus finger ring, metal versus wooden ring — before arriving at the precise scope of Rabbi Eliezer's claim. The ongoing comparison between *tumah* law and *Shabbat* law illustrates that while both depend on the definition of *kli*, the operative categories are not always identical.

## Needles: Tumah vs Shabbat

### Needle Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד שָׁאַל: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁחוֹלְקִין בֵּין מַחַט לְמַחַט. אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא לֹא שְׁמְעַתְּ אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, דְּאִי לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** that same disciple **asked: I heard that one distinguishes between** one type of **needle and** another type of **needle.** Still, I do not know with regard to what *halakha* this distinction is made. **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: Perhaps you only heard** that distinction **with regard to Shabbat.** With regard to the prohibition of carrying from a private to a public domain, or vice versa, there is a distinction between a needle with an eye, for which one is liable to bring a sin-offering, and one without an eye, for which one is not. **As, if** you were to suggest that the distinction is **with regard to ritual impurity, this,** a needle with an eye, **and that,** a needle without an eye, **are one** and the same, and there is no distinction between them.

The student posed a second question to Rabbi Eliezer: *shamati shecholkin bein machat lemachat* — I have heard that there is a distinction between types of needles. Rabbi Eliezer responded as before: perhaps you heard this only regarding *Shabbat* — *shema lo shamata le'inyan Shabbis* — for on *Shabbat*, carrying a standard needle with an eye constitutes a prohibited act of carrying, whereas a needle without an eye, fashioned as a decorative pin worn on a garment, functions as a *tachshit* — an ornament — and one who wears it is not liable. When it comes to *tumah*, however, *da veda achas hi* — both types of needle are treated the same.

### Eye in Needle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא? וְהָתְנַן: מַחַט שֶׁנִּיטַּל חוֹרָהּ אוֹ עוּקְצָהּ — טְהוֹרָה! כִּי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ בִּשְׁלֵימָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard **to the matter of ritual impurity, are this and that one** and the same? Are all needles alike? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: **A needle whose eye or whose point was removed is ritually pure,** as it is no longer fit for use? Apparently, there is a distinction between an intact needle and a broken one with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity. **When** Rabbi Eliezer **was saying** that statement **to** the disciple, he was referring **to a whole** needle. Indeed, there is no distinction between different types of whole needles with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* challenges this. Is it really true that all needles are the same for *tumah*? The *mishna* teaches: *machat shenital churah o uktzah* — if a needle has had its eye or its point removed, it is *tehorah*. A non-functional needle is no longer a *kli* and cannot become *tamei*. So needles are not universally equivalent for *tumah* purposes. The *Gemara* refines: Rabbi Eliezer meant only a complete, functional needle. Are all complete needles then the same?

### Rusty Needles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁלֵימָה דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא? וְהָתְנַן: מַחַט שֶׁהֶעֶלְתָה חֲלוּדָה, אִם מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַתְּפִירָה — טְהוֹרָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְמֵאָה. וְאָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: וְהוּא שֶׁרִישּׁוּמָהּ נִיכָּר! כִּי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, בְּשִׁיפָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to whole** needles, **are this and that one** and the same? Is there no distinction between them? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to **a needle that became rusty**; **if** the rust **inhibits the sewing,** the needle **is ritually pure; and if** it does **not** inhibit the sewing, **it is ritually impure. And the** Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai said: And that is** the *halakha* that the needle cannot become ritually impure not only when it is impossible to push the needle through the fabric, but even **when** the **mark** of rusty needle is **conspicuous** in the stitching. Apparently, there is a distinction between different types of whole needles. **When** Rabbi Eliezer **was saying** that statement **to** the disciple, **he was saying to him** that there is no distinction between different types **of** needles that were **smoothed** and filed. He was not referring to rusty needles.

Not necessarily. Another *mishna* teaches: *machat she'alsah chaludah* — a needle that has rusted — *im me'akev es hasefirah tehorah* — if the rust prevents sewing, it is *tehorah*; *ve'im lav tameia* — if not, it is *tamei*. The school of Rabbi Yannai further explained that this applies *vehu sherishumo nikar* — only if the rust leaves a noticeable mark when sewing, rendering the needle unusable. So even a complete needle may be *tahor* if corroded to the point of dysfunction. The *Gemara* refines again: Rabbi Eliezer meant a complete, polished needle.

### Polished Needles

Even this category requires examination.

### Holes & Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשִׁיפָא דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא? וְהָתַנְיָא: מַחַט בֵּין נְקוּבָה בֵּין אֵינָהּ נְקוּבָה מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן נְקוּבָה אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה בִּלְבַד!
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to smoothed** needles, **are this and that one** and the same? **Wasn't it taught in** a *baraita*: **A needle, whether it has an eye** and **whether it does not have an eye, may be moved on Shabbat? And we only said** that a needle **with an eye** is different **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **ritual impurity.** Apparently, there is a distinction between different types of smoothed needles with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity.

A *Baraita* teaches: *machat, bein nekuvah bein einah nekuvah, mutar letaltelah beShabbos* — a needle, whether it has a hole or not, may be moved on *Shabbat*. The reason: even a needle with an eye is classified as a *kli shemelachto le'issur*, an implement primarily used for a prohibited act (sewing). Such an implement may be moved *letzorech atzmo uletzorech mekomo* — for its own use or to clear its space.

### Needle Other Uses

Concretely: if one wishes to use the needle to pick one's teeth, that is a permitted use on *Shabbat*. As a *kli shemelachto le'issur*, it may be moved for a *letzorech hetter*, a permitted purpose, and so in either case — with or without an eye — *muktzah* does not apply.

### Hole Matters for Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַבָּיֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרָבָא — בְּגֻלְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't Abaye** already **interpret** that *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava** as referring **to unfinished** needles? If a needle is unfinished, and it has not been perforated to create an eye, it cannot become ritually impure because it is not yet a utensil. However, if the needle is finished, whether it has an eye and is used for sewing, or it does not have an eye and is used as a pin, it is regarded as a utensil and therefore can become ritually impure.

The *Baraita* then specifies: *velo amrinan nekuvah* — the distinction between a needle with a hole and one without is relevant *ela le'inyan tumah bilvad* — only for the laws of *tumah*. A needle with an eye is a *kli*; one without is not. This seems to directly contradict Rabbi Eliezer's claim that all needles are the same for *tumah*.

The *Gemara* resolves this through Abaye's explanation in the name of Rava: *begalmi* — the case of a needle without an eye being *tahor* refers to an unfinished needle, one still in the process of being fashioned, where the eye has not yet been formed. When Rabbi Eliezer said all needles are equivalent for *tumah*, he meant fully finished needles. Among finished needles, there are two varieties — one with an eye and one without (the pin-type ornament). For *tumah*, both finished types are treated as a *kli* and are *tamei*. For *Shabbat*, however, the ornamental pin may be worn without constituting carrying, while the sewing needle may not.

The broader lesson of both exchanges — rings and needles — is that the definition of *kli* is operative in multiple domains: *tumah*, *muktzah*, and the prohibition of carrying on *Shabbat*. Yet the precise application of that definition differs across these areas, and care must be taken not to import distinctions from one area into another.

## Animals & Shabbat Gear

### Mishna: Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חֲמוֹר יוֹצֵא בַּמַּרְדַּעַת בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִיא קְשׁוּרָה בּוֹ. זְכָרִים יוֹצְאִין לְבוּבִין. רְחֵלוֹת יוֹצְאוֹת שְׁחוּזוֹת, כְּבוּלוֹת וּכְבוּנוֹת. הָעִזִּים יוֹצְאוֹת צְרוּרוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹסֵר בְּכוּלָּן, חוּץ מִן הָרְחֵלִין הַכְּבוּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A donkey may go out** on Shabbat **with a saddlecloth** that protects it from the cold **when it is tied to** the animal, and there is no room for concern lest it fall. **Rams may go out *levuvin*. Ewes may go out *sheḥuzot, kevulot,* and *kevunot*.**All of these terms are discussed and explained in the Gemara. **She-goats may go out** with their udders **bound. Rabbi Yosei prohibits** the animals from going out **with all** of **these** items, as he considers them burdens, **except for the ewes that are *kevunot*.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which returns to the question of what animals may wear when going out on *Shabbat*. The governing principle is that an animal may bear items that serve to protect or control it, but not things deemed superfluous — those would constitute a *massa*, a burden. The *mishna* states:

A donkey — *chamor* — may go out with a *marda'at*, a saddle cloth, *bizman she'kshura bo* — when it is tied onto the animal. Rams may go out *bilvuvin* — bound, with either their genitals or their hearts covered, as a standard protective measure. Female sheep may go out in three modes: held up to allow the rams access, bound down to prevent it, or with their wool covered to protect it. Goats may go out *tzerurot* — bound — for reasons we will explore, primarily related to protecting their udders or nursing arrangements.

### Disputes on Gear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עִזִּים יוֹצְאוֹת צְרוּרוֹת לְיַבֵּשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לֵחָלֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Goats may go out** on Shabbat with their udders **bound to dry** their milk supply and discontinue their lactation, in order to facilitate conception. In that case, they are tied with a tight, permanent knot, and there is no concern lest it fall in the public domain. **However,** they may **not** go out with their udders bound **to** conserve their **milk,** as in that case they are bound loosely.

Rabbi Yose says *oser bekulan* — he forbids all of these cases — *chutz min ha'rechelim ha'kevunot*, with the sole exception of sheep whose wool is covered. Rabbi Yehuda draws a finer distinction: *izim yotzot tzerurot leyavesh aval lo lachalav* — goats may go out with their udders bound if the purpose is to cause them to dry up from lactation, but not if the purpose is to catch the milk and prevent it from going to waste. The significance of these distinctions will be unpacked as the *Gemara* addresses each case.

One might pause to reflect on what this entire chapter means in practice. To modern readers much of this *perek* seems remote — we are unfamiliar with the animals, the equipment, the terminology. Yet for *Rishonim* like *Tosafot*, who kept horses, cattle, and donkeys, these were live questions. And beyond the practical details, there is something striking about what this *perek* implies for the texture of *Shabbat* preparation. The *perek* sits in the middle of a larger sequence dealing with getting ready for *Shabbat*: lighting candles, managing food on the fire, and now — attending to one's animals. Every *Erev Shabbat*, before *Shabbat* began, one would need to go to the stable and remove from one's animals all the extraneous items they could not bear into *Shabbat*. The commandment not to have one's animals work on *Shabbat* extended to this careful preparation — freeing the animals, in a sense, to have their own form of *Shabbat*.

If one's animal went out into *reshut harabim* bearing a prohibited item, the violation would rest on the owner. The imperative to ensure this did not happen meant actively checking and readying one's livestock as part of the *Erev Shabbat* routine.

### Erev Shabbat Prep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְהוּא שֶׁקְּשׁוּרָה לוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: אֵין הַחֲמוֹר יוֹצֵא בַּמַּרְדַּעַת בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינָהּ קְשׁוּרָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel said**: **And** with regard to the *halakha* taught in our mishna that a donkey may go out on Shabbat with its saddlecloth, **that** only applies to a case **where** it **was tied to** the animal **from Shabbat eve. Rav Naḥman said:** The wording of **our mishna is also precise** in support of Shmuel's statement, **as it teaches** later in the chapter: **A donkey may not go out** into the public domain on Shabbat **with its saddlecloth when it is not tied to** its back.

The *Gemara* continues. Shmuel states: *ve'hu she'kshura me'erev Shabbat* — the permission for the donkey to go out with the saddle cloth applies only when it was tied onto the animal before *Shabbat* began. Rav Nachman adds that this can be inferred from the *mishna* itself, since the next *mishna* — which presents the mirror image of prohibited cases — states: *ein ha'chamur yotzei be'marda'at bizman she'eina kshura lo* — a donkey may not go out with a saddle cloth when it is not bound to it.

### Tied Before Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁאֵינָהּ קְשׁוּרָה לוֹ כְּלָל — פְּשִׁיטָא, דִילְמָא נָפְלָה לֵיהּ וְאָתֵי לְאֵתוּיֵי. אֶלָּא לָאו שֶׁאֵינָהּ קְשׁוּרָה מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא, שֶׁקְּשׁוּרָה לוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the meaning of that mishna: **What are the circumstances? If you say** that the later mishna is referring to a case where the saddlecloth **is not tied to** the animal **at all,** that is **obvious**. There is concern **lest** the saddlecloth **fall from** the animal **and** its owner will **come to bring it** and carry it four cubits in the public domain. **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where the saddlecloth is presently tied to the animal, but **it was not tied from Shabbat eve? By inference,** conclude **that the first clause,** i.e., our mishna, which permits the animal to go out with its saddlecloth, is referring to a case where the saddlecloth **was tied to** the animal **from Shabbat eve.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is the correct understanding.

The *Gemara* asks: what does this mean? If it means not tied at all, *peshita* — that is obvious — because it will likely fall off (*dilma nafla lei*) and one will come to carry it (*ve'ati le'atuyei*). Rather, it must mean tied, but not tied before *Shabbat* began. From which we infer — *michlal de'reisha* — that the permission in the first *mishna* means tied from *Erev Shabbat*. *Shema mina* — the inference is confirmed.

### Falling & Carrying

This discussion crystallizes an important structural point about the entire chapter. There are two distinct categories of prohibition regarding what animals (and, as we will see in the next chapter, people) may bear on *Shabbat*. The first is that the item itself does not qualify as a legitimate form of clothing or protection for that species, and therefore wearing it constitutes a form of carrying even while it remains on the body — just as wearing a knapsack is still carrying. The second is that the item would be permissible in principle, but there is concern it will fall off, leading one to inadvertently pick it up and carry it — a rabbinic concern. The first category involves a biblical violation; the second is rabbinic. Both are operative throughout this *perek*.

### Why Before Shabbat

Why does it matter specifically that the saddle cloth be tied before *Shabbat* begins? Several explanations are offered. Rashi suggests that tying it on *Shabbat* itself gives the act the character of placing a burden on the animal, not merely protecting it — and this definitional shift may be significant halachically, even if in other contexts the timing of placing an item does not ordinarily determine its category. *Tosafot* offers a similar reasoning framed in terms of appearance: tying it on *Shabbat* makes it look to onlookers as though one is preparing the animal to transport a load.

### Weekday Appearance

*Tosafot* adds a third explanation attributed to Rav Porat: *de'meichezi ke'ilu mitkaven le'holich behemah le'makom rachok* — it looks as though one intends to lead the animal on a long journey. This concern recurs at several points in the chapter and is best understood not merely as *maris ayin* — the concern about how an act appears to others — but as what might be called *uvda de'chol*, the creation of a distinctly weekday atmosphere on *Shabbat*. Standing in one's barn, saddling up the donkey, tying things onto it on *Shabbat* — that is precisely what one does during the week when preparing for a journey. Even if there is no issue of carrying, even if no *melacha* is involved, this kind of activity jars against the spirit of *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* therefore permits this only when the preparation was completed before *Shabbat* began.

## Saddle vs Cloth

### Backyard Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַסִּי בַּר נָתָן מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַהוּ לִיתֵּן מַרְדַּעַת עַל גַּבֵּי חֲמוֹר בְּשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוּתָּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מָה בֵּין זֶה לְאוּכָּף? אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi bar Natan raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav Ashi: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **placing a saddlecloth on a donkey on Shabbat** in a private domain in order to warm the donkey with no intention to take it into the public domain? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi **said to him: It is permitted.** Rav Asi bar Natan **said to him: What is the difference between this and a saddle**, which may not be moved on Shabbat? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi **remained silent** and did not answer.

The *Gemara* now asks a further question: even setting aside the issue of going out into *reshut harabim* — may one place a saddle cloth on a donkey on *Shabbat* within one's own backyard, where there is no carrying concern? Rashi clarifies that this is the precise question being posed. If the concern is purely about going into the street, tying before *Shabbat* addresses that. But what about the act of placing the cloth in the first place, even at home?

### Cloth vs Saddle

Rav Assi bar Natan posed this question to Rabbi Chiyya bar Rav Ashi. Rabbi Chiyya answered: *mutar* — it is permitted. Rav Assi pressed: *u'mah bein zu l'uchaf* — what is the difference between the saddle cloth and a saddle? A saddle may not be placed on the donkey even in the backyard. Rabbi Chiyya had no answer and fell silent.

### Saddle Muktzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: אוּכָּף שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי חֲמוֹר — לֹא יְטַלְטְלֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ, אֶלָּא מוֹלִיכָהּ וּמְבִיאָהּ בֶּחָצֵר וְהוּא נוֹפֵל מֵאֵילָיו. הַשְׁתָּא לִיטּוֹל אָמְרַתְּ לָא, לְהַנִּיחַ מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi bar Natan thought that Rabbi Ḥiyya was of the opinion that even a saddle may be placed on a donkey on Shabbat. **He,** therefore, **raised an objection** from a *baraita*: **A saddle that is on a donkey** on Shabbat, and its owner wishes to remove it, **he may not move it with his hand** to remove it; **rather, he walks** the animal **back and forth in the courtyard, and** the saddle **falls on its own. Now** even with regard **to removing** a saddle that is already on the animal's back, **you said no,** one may not move it; is prohibiting one **from placing** the saddle on the animal **necessary?**

Rav Assi then demonstrated the stringency of the saddle's status. If a saddle is already on a donkey, *lo yitlenu be-yado* — one may not simply remove it by hand, because the saddle itself is *muktzah*. To remove it, *molikho umevi'o be-chatzer ve-hi nofel me-eleiha* — one moves the donkey back and forth in the yard until it falls off on its own. The saddle's *muktzah* status makes clear that placing it on the animal is also forbidden. So why is the saddle cloth different?

### Feed Basket OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: שִׁבְקֵיהּ, כְּרַבֵּיהּ סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: תּוֹלִין טְרַסְקָל לִבְהֵמָה בְּשַׁבָּת, וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לְמַרְדַּעַת. וּמָה הָתָם דְּמִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג שְׁרֵי, הָכָא דְּמִשּׁוּם צַעַר — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to** Rav Asi: **Leave** Rabbi Ḥiyya, and do not raise an objection to his statement, as **he agrees with his teacher. As Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: One may hang a basket** with fodder **around** the neck of **an animal on Shabbat, and** by means of an ***a fortiori*** inference**,** derive that one may place **a saddlecloth** on an animal's back on Shabbat. What is the *a fortiori* inference? **Just as there,** placing the basket of fodder so that the animal can eat without bending down, **which is** done **for** the animal's **pleasure, is permitted**; **here,** placing the saddlecloth, **which is** done to prevent the animal from **suffering** from the cold, **all the more so** should be permitted.

Rabbi Zeira intervened on behalf of Rabbi Chiyya: *hana'ch le-shavtei ke-rabbeih sevira lei* — leave him alone, he holds like his teacher Rav. For Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi had himself transmitted in Rav's name: *tolin traska le-vehema be-Shabbos* — one may hang a feed basket on an animal on *Shabbat*. This refers to the kind of bucket that attaches to a horse's head, allowing it to eat without bending down. *Ve-kol she-ken le-marda'as* — and certainly one may put on a saddle cloth, which does something even more necessary for the animal by keeping it warm.

### Pleasure vs Pain

Rabbi Zeira elaborates on the logic: *u-mah hatam de-mishum ta'anug shari* — if putting on the feed basket, which merely makes the animal's eating more pleasant, is already permitted, then *hacha de-mishum tza'ar lo kol she-ken* — when the purpose is to relieve pain or cold, certainly it should be permitted. Reading *Tosafot*'s approach, the underlying question throughout these cases is: how much effort may one exert on *Shabbat* for the sake of one's animal, when the effort involves no *melacha*? The answer seems to be that effort to alleviate pain (*tza'ar*) is clearly sanctioned, and even effort for the animal's pleasure (*ta'anug*) may be permitted — at least according to Rav.

### Feed Basket No

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַרְדַּעַת מוּתָּר, טְרַסְקָל — אָסוּר. אֲזַל רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא דְרַב קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי אָמַר אַבָּא, לָא יָדַע בְּמִילֵּי דְשַׁבְּתָא וְלָא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: A saddlecloth is permitted;** however, **a basket** with fodder **is prohibited. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef went and said the *halakha* of Rav before Shmuel.** Shmuel **said to him: If Abba,** Rav, actually **said that, he knows nothing at all about matters of Shabbat.**

Shmuel, however, disagrees. *Marda'as mutar* — the saddle cloth is permitted; *ve-traska assur* — the feed basket is forbidden. It is an unnecessary exertion. When Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef conveyed Rav's position to Shmuel — that not only the saddle cloth but even the feed basket is permitted — Shmuel replied sharply: *lo yada be-millei de-Shabbata ve-lo kelum* — he knows nothing about the laws of *Shabbat*.

### Agreement on Cloth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סָלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נוֹתְנִין מַרְדַּעַת עַל גַּבֵּי חֲמוֹר בְּשַׁבָּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִישַׁר, וְכֵן תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַרְיוֹךְ בְּבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabbi Zeira ascended** to Eretz Yisrael **he found Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet who sat and said to him in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: One may place a saddlecloth on a donkey on Shabbat.** Rabbi Zeira **said to him:** You have spoken **well, and Aryokh explained** the matter **likewise in Babylonia.**

When Rabbi Zeira later went up to Eretz Yisrael, he found Rav Binyamin bar Yephet teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *nosnin marda'as al gabei chamor be-Shabbos* — one may place a saddle cloth on a donkey on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Zeira responded with *yasher*, an expression of approbation. The *Gemara* then notes that the same ruling was transmitted by *Arvach* — that is, Shmuel — in Bavel. But since Rav also permits the saddle cloth, why attribute the teaching specifically to Shmuel? The *Gemara* concludes that Shmuel's version of the ruling was distinguished by its ending: *ve'ein tolin traska be-Shabbat* — but one may not hang the feed basket on *Shabbat*. That final clause — permitting the cloth but prohibiting the basket — is what made Shmuel's formulation distinctive and worth attributing to him specifically.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַרְיוֹךְ״ מַנּוּ — שְׁמוּאֵל. וְהָא רַב נָמֵי אַמְרַהּ? אֶלָּא שַׁמְעֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ: וְאֵין תּוֹלִין טְרַסְקָל בְּשַׁבָּת, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִישַׁר, וְכֵן תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַרְיוֹךְ בְּבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is Aryokh?** It is **Shmuel. Didn't Rav also say** that one may place a saddlecloth on a donkey on Shabbat? With regard to a saddlecloth they agree. Why then did Rabbi Zeira attribute the ruling specifically to Shmuel? **Rather, he heard** Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet **conclude: However, one may not hang a basket** with fodder around the neck of an animal **on Shabbat.** It was that part of the statement that led him to say: You have spoken **well**, **and Aryokh explained** the matter **likewise in Babylonia.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת מַרְדַּעַת מוּתָּר, מַאי שְׁנָא מֵאוּכָּף? — שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאֶפְשָׁר דְּנָפֵיל מִמֵּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **In any case, everyone agrees that a saddlecloth is permitted.** The question arises: **How is** a saddlecloth **different from a saddle,** which may not even be removed from the donkey? If the concern is for the animal's suffering, why is it not permitted to remove the saddle? The Gemara answers: **It is different there, as it is possible for** the saddle **to fall on its own.** Therefore, there is no reason to permit its removal by hand.

The *Gemara* then notes that all opinions agree: *marda'at mutar* — the saddle cloth is permitted. The remaining question is what distinguishes it from the saddle. The *Gemara* offers one answer: *shani hasam d'efshar d'nafal mimaila* — the saddle is different because it is liable to fall off, and one may come to carry it. Rav Papa offers another answer: the saddle cloth is to warm the animal, and if the animal is cold that constitutes genuine *tza'ar* justifying the effort, whereas removing the saddle is ostensibly to cool the animal — and a donkey, people say, remains cold even in the heat of *Tekufat Tammuz* — so there is less urgency. Rashi clarifies that this second answer pertains specifically to *removing* the saddle, not placing it. Why placing the saddle is prohibited is perhaps better captured by the earlier intuition: a saddle, more than any other item, signals that one is preparing the animal for a journey or loading it with a burden, and that impression alone — whether framed as *muktzah* or as *uvda de'chol* — is sufficient to prohibit it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כָּאן לְחַמְּמָהּ, כָּאן לְצַנְּנָהּ. לְחַמְּמָהּ, אִית לַהּ צַעֲרָא. לְצַנְּנָהּ, לֵית לַהּ צַעֲרָא. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: חֲמָרָא אֲפִילּוּ בִּתְקוּפַת תַּמּוּז קָרִיר לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** There is a distinction between the two cases: **Here,** where the Sages permitted placing a saddlecloth on a donkey on Shabbat, it is **to warm** the animal. **There,** where the Sages prohibited removing a saddle, it is **to cool** the animal. Placing the saddlecloth **to warm** the animal is permitted because otherwise **it** experiences **discomfort** from the cold. However, removing the saddle **to cool** the animal is prohibited because the animal **does not** experience **discomfort** from excessive heat. **And that is** the folk saying **that people say: A donkey, even in the** summer **season of Tammuz, is cold.** Therefore, seeing to the animal's warmth is more important.

## Foals & Pain

### Baraita Foals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: לֹא יֵצֵא הַסּוּס בִּזְנַב שׁוּעָל, וְלֹא בַּזַּהֲרוּרִית שֶׁבֵּין עֵינָיו. לֹא יֵצֵא הַזָּב בַּכִּיס שֶׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא עִזִּים בַּכִּיס שֶׁבְּדַדֵּיהֶן, וְלֹא פָּרָה בַּחִסּוּם שֶׁבְּפִיהָ, וְלֹא סְיָיחִים בַּטְּרַסְקָלִין שֶׁבְּפִיהֶם לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְלֹא בְּהֵמָה בַּסַּנְדָּל שֶׁבְּרַגְלֶיהָ, וְלֹא בַּקָּמֵיעַ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא מוּמְחֶה — וְזֶה חוֹמֶר בַּבְּהֵמָה מִבָּאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the *Tosefta* to those who prohibit placing a basket with fodder around an animal's neck on Shabbat: **A horse may neither go out** into the public domain on Shabbat **with a fox's tail** that is placed as a talisman to ward off the evil eye **nor with** a string of **red wool that is** hung **between its eyes** as an ornament. **Neither may a *zav* go out with his pouch** that prevents his clothes from becoming sullied from his emissions, **nor goats with a pouch that is on their udders** so that they will not be scratched by stones, **nor a cow with the muzzle that is on its mouth, nor foals with baskets** of fodder **that are around their mouths into the public domain. And an animal** may **neither** go out **with** metal **shoes that are on its feet, nor with an amulet** that is placed on the animal to promote its good health, **even if** the amulet **has proved effective. And this is a stricture** that applies **to animals beyond** the strictures that apply **to people,** as a person is permitted to go out into the public domain with an amulet that has proved effective.

The *Gemara* cites a *Baraita* listing further cases: *lo yetzei ha-sus biznav shual* — a horse may not go out with a fox tail hung from its head as ornament; not with a red string between its eyes. These are superfluous decorations. The *Baraita* also teaches: *velo syachin b'traskalin shebefihem, l'reshut harabim* — young foals may not go out into the public domain with feed baskets attached to their mouths.

### Backyard OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: וְלֹא סְיָיחִין בִּטְרַסְקָלִים שֶׁבְּפִיהֶם לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים הוּא דְּלָא, הָא בְּחָצֵר — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. מַאי לַָאו, בִּגְדוֹלִים וּמִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג?!
>
> **Translation:** **In any case, it is taught** here: **Nor foals with baskets** of fodder **that are around their mouths into the public domain.** By inference: It is specifically **into the public domain** that they may **not** go with fodder baskets in their mouths; **however, in a courtyard,** they may **well** walk with a basket of fodder. **What? Is it not** referring **to large** foals around whose necks fodder baskets are hung **for** their **pleasure?**

The *Gemara* draws an inference: *l'reshut harabim hu delo* — it is specifically into the public domain that they may not go, *ha bechatzer shapir dami* — but in the courtyard it is fine. This supports Rav's position that the feed basket is permitted, at least in the backyard. The *Gemara* then asks: are we speaking of older foals, and the permission is based on *ta'anug* — mere pleasure — thereby proving Rav correct?

### Pain Justifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בִּקְטַנִּים וּמִשּׁוּם צַעַר: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי דּוּמְיָא דְּקָמֵיעַ. שְׁמַע מִינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No**, it is referring **to small** foals, **and** the baskets are hung **to** prevent their **discomfort.** The legs of a young foal are long and its neck is short. Consequently, eating from the ground is difficult. Hanging the fodder basket around its neck enables it to eat without bending down. The Gemara adds: **This is also precise** in the language of the *Tosefta*, **as it teaches** the case of the foals **similar to** the case of **an amulet** worn for healing purposes. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is the correct understanding.

The *Gemara* answers: *lo, biktannim* — no, we are speaking of very young foals. *U-mishum tza'ar* — and the reason is pain. A newborn foal has long legs and a relatively small head; bending down to eat requires significant effort and causes real discomfort. Attaching a feed basket to allow the foal to eat without bending is therefore not merely a convenience but a genuine alleviation of suffering. The *Gemara* confirms this reading: *dumya de-kame'a* — it is analogous to the amulet case, which will shortly be shown to involve relieving pain. *Shema mina* — the inference holds. This is consistent with Shmuel's framework: effort for *ta'anug* is forbidden, but effort for *tza'ar* is sanctioned.

## Amulets for Animals

### Amulets Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְלֹא בַּקָּמֵיעַ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא מוּמְחֶה. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: וְלֹא בַּקָּמֵיעַ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּמְחֶה. הָא מוּמְחֶה — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי! הָכָא נָמֵי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּמְחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: Nor** may an animal go out **with an amulet** on Shabbat, **even if** the amulet **proved effective.** The Gemara asks: **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: One may **not** go out on Shabbat **with an amulet that has not proved effective?** By inference: If the amulet **proved effective,** he may **well** do so. The Gemara answers: **Here too**, it is referring to an amulet **that has not proved effective.**

The *Gemara* returns to the *Baraita*: *velo b'kame'a, af al pi shehu mumcheh* — an animal may not go out with an amulet, even a tested one, and this is stricter than the rule for humans (*zeh chomer b'behemah mibe'adam*). The earlier *mishna*, by contrast, states that a person may not go out with an untested amulet — *she'eino mumcheh* — implying that a tested amulet is permitted for a person.

### Luck & Amulets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא מוּמְחֶה״ קָתָנֵי! מוּמְחֶה לְאָדָם וְאֵינוֹ מוּמְחֶה לִבְהֵמָה. וּמִי אִיכָּא מוּמְחֶה לְאָדָם וְלָא הָוֵי מוּמְחֶה לִבְהֵמָה? אִין, אָדָם דְּאִית לֵיהּ מַזָּלָא — מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ, בְּהֵמָה דְּלֵית לַהּ מַזָּלָא — לָא מְסַיַּיע לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Doesn't** the *baraita* **teach: Even if** the amulet **proved effective?** The *baraita* is referring to an amulet that **proved effective for a person, and did not prove effective for an animal.** The Gemara wonders: **Is there** an amulet that **proved effective for a person and is not effective for an animal?** Healing an animal should be easier than healing a person. The Gemara answers: **Yes,** an amulet **aids a person, who is under** the protection of **an** advocate **angel [*mazal*];** however, **it does not aid an animal, which is not under** the protection of **an** advocate **angel.**

The *Gemara* initially reads the *Baraita* as saying an animal cannot use even a tested amulet. But then it refines: *mumcheh le-adam ve-eino mumcheh le-behemah* — the amulet has been tested for humans but not for animals. An amulet tested to help a human will not necessarily help an animal. *Adam de-it lei mazla* — a person has fortune (*mazal*), and the amulet works in conjunction with that. *Behemah de-leit lah mazla* — an animal lacks that fortune, and so *lo mesaye lah* — the amulet does not help it. The rules are therefore structurally parallel: each species may use only an amulet tested for that species. This principle raises interesting broader questions about amulets and about the concept of *mazal*, which the *Gemara* leaves aside here.

### Shoes Stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי ״זֶה חוֹמֶר בַּבְּהֵמָה מִבָּאָדָם״? מִי סָבְרַתְּ אַקָּמֵיעַ קָאֵי? אַסַּנְדָּל קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **If so,** that the *baraita* is referring to an amulet that did not prove effective for an animal, but if the amulet proved effective, the animal may indeed go out into the public domain with it; **what** is the meaning of the phrase in the *Tosefta*: **And this is a stricture** that applies **to animals beyond** the strictures that apply **to people?** The *halakha* is the same with regard to both people and animals. If the amulet has proven effective, even an animal may go out with it on Shabbat. If it has not proven effective, even a person may not go out with it. The Gemara responds: **Do you hold** that **this** statement **is referring to an amulet? It is referring to a shoe;** an animal may not go out with a shoe on Shabbat, but a person may.

The *Gemara* asks: if the rules are parallel, in what sense is the animal's case stricter? The answer: *a-sandal kai* — the stringency refers to shoes, not to amulets. A person may go out wearing shoes; an animal may not, because the shoe is liable to fall off.

This concludes the discussion of amulets and introduces the general principle that certain items, however purposeful, remain prohibited for animals due to the risk of falling off and prompting carrying — the second category of rabbinic prohibition noted earlier.

## Animal Medicine

### No Anointing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: סָכִין וּמְפַרְכְּסִין לְאָדָם, וְאֵין סָכִין וּמְפַרְכְּסִין לַבְּהֵמָה. מַאי לַָאו, דְּאִיכָּא מַכָּה וּמִשּׁוּם צַעַר! לָא, דִּגְמַר מַכָּה וּמִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** that which was taught in a *baraita*: **One may smear** on **oil and scrape** off a scab on Shabbat **for a person, and one may not smear** on **oil and scrape** off a scab **for an animal. Is it not** referring here to a case **where there is a wound, and** he smears on oil **and** scrapes the scab **due to** the **discomfort** caused by the wound, and nevertheless it was permitted exclusively for a person and not for an animal? The Gemara rejects this argument: **No,** it is referring to a case **where the wound has** already **ceased** and healed, and he smears oil and scrapes **due to** the **pleasure** caused by the treatment.

The *Gemara* now examines a series of cases related to medical treatment for animals. The *Baraita* teaches: *sachin u-mefarchasin le-adam* — one may anoint and scrape a wound for a person, *ve-ein sachin u-mefarchasin le-behemah* — but one may not do so for an animal. The initial question is whether this prohibition applies even when there is a genuine wound causing pain — which would seem to contradict the principle that *tza'ar* justifies effort. The *Gemara* resolves this: the case is *di-gemar maka* — the wound has already healed, leaving only a scab. Scratching or scraping the scab is thus for *ta'anug* — pleasure — not to relieve genuine suffering. This is therefore consistent with Shmuel's ruling and does not extend the prohibition to cases of actual pain.

### Blood Clots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁאֲחָזָהּ דָּם, אֵין מַעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּמַיִם בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּצְטַנֵּן. אָדָם שֶׁאֲחָזוֹ דָּם, מַעֲמִידִין אוֹתוֹ בְּמַיִם בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּצְטַנֵּן. אָמַר עוּלָּא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם שְׁחִיקַת סַמְמָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** that which was taught in the following *baraita*: With regard to **an animal** suffering from heart **congestion** that restricts its **blood** supply and whose temperature has risen, **one may not stand it in water so that it will cool** off. However, with regard to **a person** suffering from heart **congestion** that restricts his **blood** supply, **one** may **stand him in water so that he will cool** off. Apparently, the suffering of an animal is of no concern. **Ulla said:** Here, the Sages issued **a decree** prohibiting all healing on Shabbat **due to the crushing of herbs** for medicinal purposes, which is prohibited by Torah law. The Sages prohibited cooling the animal in water lest one come to grind the ingredients used in the preparation of medicine.

A further case: *behemah she-achaza dam* — if an animal has a blood clot — *ein ma'amidin otah be-mayim bishvil she-titzztanen* — one does not immerse it in water to help dissolve the clot. But *adam she-achazo dam* — if a person has such a condition — *ma'amidin oto be-mayim bishvil she-yitztanen* — one may immerse him in water for relief. Why is the human case permitted but not the animal's?

### Spices Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי אָדָם נָמֵי! אָדָם נִרְאֶה כְּמֵיקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** the same decree should **also** apply in the case of **a person.** It should be prohibited to stand a sick person in water to cool him off due to the rabbinic prohibition against engaging in healing on Shabbat. The Gemara answers: In the case of **a person, it appears as** if he entered the water merely **to cool** off, not necessarily to cure an illness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי בְּהֵמָה נָמֵי נִרְאֶה כְּמֵיקֵר! אֵין מֵיקֵר לִבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so**, say in the case of **an animal as well** that **it appears as** if it entered the water merely **to cool** off**,** not necessarily to cure an illness. The Gemara answers: **An animal** does **not** typically enter the water on its own to **cool** off. Neither does one typically stand an animal in water to cool it off unless it serves some healing purpose. Apparently, due to a decree, the Sages were stringent and prohibited standing the animal in water even if it will die as a result.

Ulla explains: *gezeirah mishum shechikat samamanim* — it is a rabbinic decree because of the concern it will lead to grinding spices, i.e., preparing medicinal ingredients. The general rabbinic prohibition against taking medicine on *Shabbat* (*refuah*) exists specifically to prevent the grinding of medicinal spices. The question extends to various non-pharmacological forms of healing — stretches, massage, hydrotherapy — and whether they fall within this rabbinic concern. Here, the *Gemara* concludes that immersing an animal in water for therapeutic purposes is recognizably an act of *refuah* and is therefore prohibited by this decree.

For a person, however, *adam nireh ke-meikar* — a person getting into cool water appears to onlookers simply as cooling off, not as medical treatment. The therapeutic purpose is not apparent, and therefore the decree does not apply. Why not apply the same reasoning to an animal? *Ein meikar le-behemah* — people do not ordinarily immerse their animals in water for cooling; it is not a normal practice. Therefore, when one does it, it is obviously for therapeutic reasons, and the prohibition applies.

### Calling Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִבְהֵמָה מִי גָּזְרִינַן? וְהָתַנְיָא: הָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת חוּץ לַתְּחוּם, קוֹרֵא לָהּ וְהִיא בָּאָה. וְלָא גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵתוּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Do we** really **issue a decree for an animal? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If an animal **were standing beyond the** Shabbat **limit,** a situation in which it is prohibited to go fetch it, **he may call** the animal **and it will come** to him on its own? **And we do not issue a decree** to prohibit calling the animal, **lest he come to bring it** himself. Apparently, the Sages did not issue a decree in a case where one could incur a loss and there is no actual transgression committed. Here too, it should not be prohibited to stand his animal in water due to a decree lest he come to grind herbs and thereby violate a Torah prohibition.

The *Gemara* then asks: do we in fact apply such decrees to animals? A *Baraita* teaches: *hayeta omedet chutz litchum, koreh la ve'hia ba'ah* — if one's animal is outside one's *tchum*, one may call it and it will come. *Ve'lo gazrinan dilma ati le'atuyei* — and we do not worry that the owner will go outside the *tchum* to retrieve it. This suggests that we do not extend rabbinic precautions to animal-related scenarios.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה תְּחוּם שֶׁלָּהּ מוּבְלָע בְּתוֹךְ תְּחוּם שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ravina said:** No proof can be cited from this case, as here it is a situation **where** the animal's Shabbat **limit was subsumed within** the **limit** of its owner. The animal strayed beyond its own Shabbat limit, which is determined by the Shabbat limit of the shepherd entrusted with its herding. However, the animal remained within the Shabbat limit of its owner, which extended beyond that of the shepherd. Consequently, the owner is permitted to call the animal so that it will return on its own. Even if he forgets and goes out to fetch the animal, he will not have gone beyond his Shabbat limit. The fact that the animal itself went beyond its Shabbat limit is of no concern.

Ravina resolves the contradiction: the case in the *Baraita* is one where *tchum shelo muvla betoch tchum shelo* — the animal's legitimate space is embedded within the owner's space. The owner may walk to where the animal is. The only prohibition would be physically leading the animal back into the owner's area, since doing so would remove the animal from the space defined by its shepherd's location at the onset of *Shabbat*. Calling the animal and allowing it to come on its own avoids this. The concern in that case is thus minimal and specific, which is why no further decree was added. By contrast, the concern about *shechikat samamanim* is a broader, more general rabbinic category that does apply to animal treatment.

### Disputed Medicine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: שְׁחִיקַת סַמְמָנִין גּוּפָה תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁאָכְלָה כַּרְשִׁינִין לֹא יְרִיצֶנָּה בֶּחָצֵר בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּתְרַפֶּה, וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא מַתִּיר. דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The matter of the decree due to **crushing herbs is itself** subject to a dispute between **the *tanna'im*. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **an animal that ate vetch,** which caused a life-threatening case of constipation, **one may not run it** around **in the courtyard to loosen** its bowels due to the decree prohibiting healing. **Rabbi Oshaya deems** it **permitted.** Apparently, the *tanna'im* disagree whether or not healing is prohibited with regard to animals. The Gemara adds that **Rava taught:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Oshaya.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a further layer: *shechikat samamanim gufah tana'i hi* — the question of whether a given act falls within the prohibited category of *refuah* is itself a matter of *tannaic* dispute. A *Baraita* teaches: *behemah she'achlah karshinim* — if an animal has eaten vetch (a grain harmful to it) — *lo yeritzennah bechatzer bishvil she-titrapeh* — one may not jog it in the yard to help it pass the food, for that constitutes *refuah*.

### Jogging Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִים, אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** (Note: This text appears in Shabbat 54a. It is included here for continuity.)

However, *Rabbi Yoshia* — rendered here as *Rabbi Yoshi* — disagrees and permits it. *Rava* ruled: *halachah ke-Rabbi Yoshia*. Rashi explains that Rabbi Yoshia's leniency rests on the distinction that jogging is not a medicinal act but rather a physical form of care — *refuah* in a non-pharmacological sense — and therefore does not fall within the decree. As noted, this distinction carries significant practical implications for people as well: whether non-medicinal therapeutic activities such as physical exercise or stretching are permitted on *Shabbat* depends precisely on where one draws this line.

## Goat Coverings

### Udder Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּמִיהַדַּק, הָא דְּלָא מִיהַדַּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** This is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is referring to a pouch **that is** tied **tightly** to the udder. It is permitted because there is no concern that the pouch will fall. **That** *baraita* is referring to a pouch **that is not tightly** tied. It is prohibited because of the concern that the pouch will fall and a person will come to retrieve it.

The *Gemara* returns to the final case of the *mishna* and *Baraita*: goats going out with coverings over their udders. The *Baraita* states that they may not go out — *velo izin b'khis shebadadeihem* — with receptacles beneath their udders. Yet another *Baraita* says they may. Rav Yehuda resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha de'mehedak ve'ha de'lo mehedak* — there is no difficulty; the difference depends on whether the covering is tight or loose. Rashi's reading is that a loose covering is likely to fall off, leading one to carry it — the familiar second category of prohibition.

### Dry vs Catch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: תַּנָּאֵי שָׁקְלַתְּ מֵעָלְמָא? תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הָעִזִּים יוֹצְאוֹת צְרוּרוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹסֵר בְּכֻלָּן חוּץ מִן הָרְחֵילוֹת הַכְּבוּנוֹת, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עִזִּים יוֹצְאוֹת צְרוּרוֹת לְיַבֵּשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לֵיחָלֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Have you removed the *tanna'im* from the world?** This is subject to a disagreement between **the *tanna'im***, **as we learned** in our mishna: **She-goats may go out** with their udders **bound. Rabbi Yosei Rabbi Yosei prohibits** the animals from going out **with all** of **these** items, as he considers them burdens, **except for the ewes that are *kevunot*. Rabbi Yehuda says: Goats may go out** on Shabbat with their udders **bound to dry** their milk supply and discontinue their lactation in order to facilitate conception, as in that case, they are tied with a tight, permanent knot. **However,** they may **not** go out with their udders bound **to** conserve the **milk,** as in that case they are bound loosely. Apparently, there are *tanna'im* who rule leniently with regard to attaching pouches to the udders of goats and permit the practice, and others prohibit doing so.

Rav Yosef objects: one cannot simply pick two *tanna'im* at random and declare their views in conflict — the contradiction is between positions of the same *tanna'im*. The *Gemara* cites the *mishna* itself: Rabbi Yose forbids in all cases except sheep covering their wool; Rabbi Yehuda permits goats going out *tzerurot leyavesh* — bound to dry the udders — but not *lachalav* — not to catch the milk. Another resolution: both the permitting and prohibiting *Baraitot* reflect Rabbi Yehuda's position, and the distinction is precisely *leyavesh* versus *lachalav*.

### Antioch Goats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּעִזִּים שֶׁל אַנְטוֹכְיָא שֶׁהָיוּ דַּדֵּיהֶן גַּסִּין, וְעָשׂוּ לָהֶן כִּיסִין כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִסָּרְטוּ דַּדֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident involving the goats** belonging to the residents of **a house in Antioch whose udders were** especially **large** and they would drag along the ground. **And they made pouches for them so that their udders would not get scratched.**

The *Gemara* cites a teaching from Rabbi Yehuda himself: *ma'aseh be-izei beit antukhya she-hayu dadeihen gasim* — there was a story with the goats of Antioch whose udders were very large, and their nipples were so long that they dragged on the ground. Coverings were fashioned for them *kedei she-lo yisretu dadeihem* — so that the udders would not be scraped and wounded. This case is clearly a protective measure for the animal's benefit.

### Three Purposes

The significance of Rabbi Yehuda's distinction involves at least two considerations. First, tightness: a covering designed to dry the udder would be fastened tightly, reducing the risk of falling off. A covering designed to catch milk would be looser. Second, purpose: a covering that promotes drying serves the animal's welfare — an animal that is no longer nursing but continues to lactate may experience discomfort, and assisting the drying process is a genuine act of care for the animal. By contrast, a covering designed to catch milk serves the owner's economic interest, not the animal's needs, and therefore cannot be classified as protection for the animal. On this reading, it would constitute carrying.

The three purposes that emerge from the discussion — drying the udder, catching the milk, and protecting the udder from scraping — map onto distinct halachic categories. Drying: tight, for the animal's benefit, permitted. Catching milk: loose, for the owner's benefit, forbidden. Preventing scraping: clearly protective, analogous to a bandage, and seemingly permitted in all views. The tight-versus-loose distinction thus encodes not merely a practical concern about falling off, but a deeper judgment about whose interests the covering serves and whether it constitutes genuine protection for the animal or an economic device for the owner.
