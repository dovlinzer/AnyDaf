# Bava Kamma 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Shein & Regel

### Perek Overview

The daf begins at the bottom of 19b, where the *mishnah* opens the discussion of *shein* (the ox's tooth, representing damage through eating) as we transition from *regel* (damage through trampling). The perek as a whole, aptly named *Keitzad HaRegel*, focuses primarily on both *shein* and *regel* — where the first perek dealt with broad *klalim*, this one narrows in on the specifics of each category.

### Exemption Framework

With *regel*, the central discussion concerned *tzerorot* — pebbles kicked up in the course of walking — but a deeper question also emerged toward the end: what is the underlying basis for the exemption in *reshus harabbim*? When an animal walks through the public domain and causes damage in *reshus hayachid*, why is the owner exempt? Two distinct rationales surface in the *gemara*: one holds that the owner has a fundamental right to have his animal in the public domain, and therefore cannot be held liable for the normal consequences of its presence there; the other holds that the damaged party brought the harm upon himself through his own negligence in leaving his property exposed. This question — which is not merely academic but shapes the practical limits of the exemption — carries directly into the *sugyot* on *shein*, and we will continue to encounter it throughout.

---

## Shein Mu'edes

### Mishnah Text

The *mishnah* (19b) opens: *keitzad hashein mu'edes le'echol es harauy lah* — in what way does the animal's tooth establish a propensity for eating things fit for it? *Habeheimah mu'edes le'echol peiros u-yerakos* — an animal has a natural propensity to eat fruit and vegetables. These are normal foods and, when consumed in *reshus hanizak*, trigger full liability.

However, if the animal ate garments or vessels — *achla kesus u-keilim* — that is not the normal behavior of *shein*, and the owner pays only *chatzi nezek*, half damages. The eating is *meshunah*, abnormal.

### Meshunah as Keren

This raises a question the *gemara* addresses directly: when an animal eats in an unusual manner, is that abnormal *shein* still categorized as *shein* (merely an atypical instance of tooth-damage), or does it become a form of *keren* (deliberate or dangerous damage, normally paying half)? The *gemara*'s clear approach is the latter: because the eating is *meshunah*, it is treated as a type of *keren*, and the half-damage payment follows from that classification. The *gemara* does not seriously entertain the position that one could have an unusual *shein* that pays half by analogy to *keren* while remaining categorized as *shein*. The only category of normal *shein*-type damage that pays less than full is *regel* in the form of *tzerorot*.

### Negligence Question

The *mishnah* then states: *bameh devarim amurim, birshus hanizak* — when does liability apply? In the damaged party's domain. *Aval birshus harabbim patur* — but in the public domain, the owner is exempt. This is straightforward for normal *shein*. But a corollary emerges: if the abnormal eating is classified as *keren*, why should it be exempt in *reshus harabbim*? The Talmud's treatment of *keren* in the public domain will be addressed as the *gemara* develops. The *mishnah* adds an important new concept not applicable to *regel*: *ve-im nehenis meshalemes mah she-nehenis* — even where the owner is exempt from paying damages, if the animal derived benefit, the owner must pay for the benefit received. This concept does not apply to *regel* because *regel* produces no benefit for the animal's owner — the trampling yields nothing. But when an animal eats your grain in *reshus harabbim*, while you cannot sue for damage, you can demand reimbursement for the meal your animal received at your expense. This principle extends well beyond the laws of *nezikin* and will recur in many contexts.

## Benefit Payment

### Benefit vs. Damages

The concept of *ve-im nehenis meshalemes mah she-nehenis* introduces the important distinction between liability for damage and liability for benefit received. When we say an owner is exempt in *reshus harabbim*, we mean exempt from the *nezek* — the destruction of your property. But that exemption says nothing about the enrichment the owner received. The damaged party is not asking to be compensated for his loss; he is asking the animal's owner to return the value of the benefit conferred. This is a principle with broad application beyond *nezikin*: wherever one person is inadvertently enriched at another's expense — without any contract or agreement — the question arises whether restitution is owed.

### Valuation Difference

The *mishnah*'s formulation *mah she-nehenis* — pay what was benefited — raises the question of how that benefit is calculated, and the answer differs substantially from how damages are calculated. If the animal ate strawberries worth twenty dollars but the owner would normally have fed it hay worth two dollars, the damage payment would be twenty dollars. But the benefit payment may be only two dollars, since that is what the owner would actually have spent to feed his animal. The measure of benefit received is not the market value of what was consumed but rather how much the owner was actually enriched relative to his baseline expenditure — and if the food caused the animal harm, the benefit payment might be zero.

### Location-Based Cases

The *mishnah* then specifies the circumstances under which each type of payment applies. *Achla mitoch harachava* — if the animal ate from the middle of the street (*reshus harabbim*) — *meshalem mah she-nehenis*, the owner pays for the benefit. *Mitzidei harachava* — from the side of the street, closer to private property — *meshalem mah shehizika*, the owner pays full damages. *Mipisach hachanut* — from the front of the store — *meshalem mah she-nehenis*, benefit only. *Mitoch hachanut* — from inside the store — *meshalem mah shehizika*, full damages.

### Location Definition

An important question emerges from these cases: how do we define whether the eating occurred in *reshus harabbim* or in *reshus hanizak* when the animal is standing in one domain and its head reaches into another? The plain sense of the *mishnah* appears to be that what matters is where the food is and where the act of eating takes place — not where the animal's legs are standing. If the animal's head and the food are in one place, that is where the eating occurred, regardless of where the bulk of the animal stands.

One might ask: when the animal and the food are both in *reshus harabbim*, surely the food's owner bears some responsibility for leaving his produce exposed in a public thoroughfare. This is precisely the principle invoked to justify the exemption from damages: the owner of the produce was partly at fault. Nevertheless, the owner of the animal still received a meal at the other person's expense, and so even if damages are exempt, benefit payment remains. The question of contributory negligence removes the owner's liability in *tort* but does not extinguish the other party's right to be reimbursed for the value conferred.

The *gemara* notes that Rashi reads the *mishnah* through a specific lens — *mishum de'orchei lemichal hachi mishum hachi patur* — which ties the exemption in *reshus harabbim* to the normalcy of the eating, and implicitly treats unusual eating in the public domain as a form of *keren*. This reading will be revisited when the *gemara* takes up the question of *kopetzet* (jumping).

## Baraita Foods

### Food Examples

A *baraita* then elaborates on the category of *raui lah* — foods fit for the animal. If an animal entered *chatzer hanizak* and ate foods and drank liquids appropriate for it, it pays *nezek shalem*. A *chayah* (wild animal) that tore apart and ate meat likewise pays full damages, since that is its nature. But the *baraita* then extends the category: *u'farah she'achlah se'orin* — a cow that ate barley; *vachamor she'achal karshinin* — a donkey that ate leeks; *v'chelev she'likkeik es hashemen* — a dog that licked oil; *vachazir she'achal chatichah shel basar* — a pig that ate a piece of meat. In all these cases, the *baraita* rules *nezek shalem*. Although these foods are not the primary diet of those animals, they fall within an extended range of what the animal will eat.

### Hadachak Principle

Rav Pappa crystallizes the principle: *hashta de'amrat kol midei d'lav orchei v'achlah lei al yedei hadachak shmeih achilah* — now that you have established that anything not in the animal's normal diet but which it will consume under duress (*hadachak*) is still considered eating. Even food the animal would not ordinarily choose, if it would consume it when nothing else is available, constitutes *achilah* for the purposes of *shein*. This is the operative meaning of *raui lah*.

### Raui Lah Range

The range of *raui lah* therefore runs from what the animal eats normally and habitually at one end, to what it will eat even in extremis at the other. Anything within that range is still *shein* and triggers full liability. Only what falls entirely outside that range — what the animal would not consume even under duress — constitutes truly abnormal eating.

### Wild Foods Still Shein

Applying this principle: *hai shunra d'achal tamrei* — a cat that eats dates — and *vachamra d'achal binisa* — a donkey that eats fish — both fall within the extended category of *raui lah* and pay *nezek shalem*. These foods are unusual for the animal but within the range of what it would eat under extreme conditions.

## Bread & Basket

### Rav Yehuda Ruling

A practical case: a donkey ate bread and chewed up the basket that contained the bread. Rav Yehuda ruled *nezek shalem* for the bread and *chatzi nezek* for the basket — full damages for the bread as a normal food, and half damages for the basket as something the animal would not ordinarily eat, analogous to *kesus v'keilim*.

### Chewing Normal

The *gemara* immediately challenges this ruling: *keivan de'orchei l'meichal nahma orchei nami l'mipsai l'sala* — since it is normal for a donkey to eat bread, it is equally normal for it to chew through the basket in the process of getting to the bread. The act of destroying the basket is the natural, foreseeable accompaniment of eating bread from a basket. If so, why does Rav Yehuda impose only half damages for the basket?

### Damage Category

This raises an interesting question of categorization. Is the damage to the basket *shein*, *regel*, or *keren*? The animal is not eating the basket for nourishment, nor is it walking through it incidentally. Rather, it is tearing it open to access the bread inside. One might argue this is *keren* on the grounds that the animal has a *kavanah lehazik* — an intent to breach the container — to reach the food. Alternatively, one might classify it as *regel* by analogy to the rooster (*tarnegol*) case, where an animal causes incidental damage in the course of pursuing food. The *gemara* suggests that since the animal in this case opted while eating to go ahead and consume the basket as well, that constitutes a deliberate choice, rendering the basket-eating *meshunah* — which is why the ruling is half damages. The basket damage appears to fall closer to *regel* in its mechanism, even if the resulting liability is framed through the *meshunah* category.

## Turnip & Barrel

### Rava Turnip Ruling

At the top of 20a: a goat saw a turnip (*lipta*) resting on the mouth of a barrel. It climbed up, ate the turnip, and in the process broke the barrel. Rava ruled full liability for both — *nezek shalem* for the turnip and *nezek shalem* for the barrel.

### Normal Behavior

Rava's reasoning: *keivan de'orchei l'meichal lipta* — since it is normal for a goat to eat a turnip — *orcheia nami l'srouchi ul'misak* — it is therefore equally normal for it to climb up on a barrel to reach the turnip when the turnip is visible from below. The act of climbing is the natural, foreseeable means of access. This stands in contrast to the case of an animal eating food off a table, where the elevation of the table is treated as placing the food out of the normal range of access — making the eating *meshunah* and reducing liability to half. Here, however, climbing a barrel to reach visible food on its rim is considered *orcheia* (normal behavior), and so full liability applies. The barrel damage is presumably *regel* and the turnip is *shein*, both at *nezek shalem*.

## Domain Definition

### Neck Extension

The *gemara* now addresses a critical question of domain definition through a ruling of Ilfa: an animal standing in *reshus harabbim* that stretches out its neck and eats from food on the back of another animal (*gabi chaverta*) — the owner is liable. *Mai ta'ama? Gabi chavarta kechatzer hanizak dami* — the back of the other animal is treated as equivalent to *chatzer hanizak*, the damaged party's private domain.

From a formalist perspective this is straightforward: the other person's animal is his domain, and what rests on it belongs to that domain. Even from the negligence-based framework, one can argue that the owner of the eating animal ought to keep it from reaching into effectively another's domain — even in *reshus harabbim*, there is a basic obligation not to let your animal encroach on another's person and property.

### Animal Back Domain

Yet the *gemara* pushes back. Can we really call the back of an animal standing in *reshus harabbim* a *reshus hanizak*? The entire surrounding space is *reshus harabbim*. Under the animal's feet is *reshus harabbim*; above and around it is *reshus harabbim*. To carve out a small pocket of *reshus hanizak* in the middle of a continuous public space is not self-evident. This is analogous to the question of whether an object placed inside one's vessel in another's domain is acquired by the vessel's owner — whose domain governs when two domains overlap in a single space?

### Basket Support

The *gemara* attempts to support Ilfa's ruling from a *baraita*: *hayesa kupato mushleches le'achorav u'fashta tzavara v'achla mimena* — if someone was carrying an open basket on his back and an animal stretched out its neck and ate from it, the animal's owner is liable. This appears to confirm the principle that the contents of what someone carries on his person in *reshus harabbim* retain the status of *reshus hanizak*.

### Rashi Meshunah

The *gemara* deflects this as a proof, suggesting the *baraita* may be a case of *kopetzet* — an animal that jumped to reach the food — citing a principle from Rava, which will be addressed momentarily. Rashi reads *bekofetzet* as follows: *shekaftza v'achla al tzavaro she'ein darkah bekach vesoladat dekeren hi v'chayeves chatzi nezek ka'amar* — the animal jumped onto the neck of a person or another animal, which is not its normal behavior, making this a *toldah* (derivative) of *keren*, and therefore the liability is *chatzi nezek*, not *nezek shalem*.

On Rashi's reading, the *baraita* does not prove Ilfa's point about the back of an animal constituting *reshus hanizak*. Rather, the animal is liable because the jumping is *meshunah*, classifying the act as *keren*. This raises two related questions: first, what is the function of *kopetzet* — does it make the act *meshunah* (and thus *keren*), or does it instead strip away the normal exemption of *reshus harabbim* by showing that the food was effectively inaccessible and thus not negligently exposed? Second, why is the *gemara* reluctant to simply accept Ilfa's ruling as straightforward — that the other animal's back is *reshus hanizak*?

The reluctance may stem from exactly the domain problem identified above. Something on a normal ledge at the side of *reshus harabbim* presents a clear spatial boundary: here is public space, there is private. But the back of an animal surrounded entirely by public space resists easy categorization. *Kopetzet* may resolve the difficulty in one of two ways: either by creating *keren*-liability independent of the domain question (Rashi's reading), or by signaling that food placed truly out of reach — requiring a jump to access — can reasonably be treated as a domain unto itself, since ordinary animals in *reshus harabbim* would not encounter it in the normal course of their movement.

## Standing vs. Walking

### Yoshiya Rule

Rava's statement about *kopetzet* was made in the context of a ruling by Rav Yoshiya: *behemah bi'reshus harabbim halchah ve'achlah petura; amdah ve'achlah chayavet* — an animal in *reshus harabbim* that walked and ate while walking is exempt; an animal that stood and ate is liable.

### Rava Standing

The *gemara* immediately asks: both walking and standing are normal behaviors — why should standing change the liability? Rava answers: *bekofetzet* — "standing" here does not mean merely halting in place and lowering its head to eat. It means the animal spotted food that would normally be out of reach and jumped up to get it. Walking and incidentally eating food at ground level is entirely *orcheia*; jumping to access elevated food is not.

### Kopetzet Nature

This leaves open the precise legal mechanism by which *kopetzet* creates liability. On Rashi's reading, it is because jumping is *meshunah*, converting the act into a *toldah dekeren* and resulting in *chatzi nezek*. An alternative reading holds that *kopetzet* does not change the *category* of the damage but rather eliminates the normal exemption of *reshus harabbim*: because the food was positioned out of normal reach, the owner of the food was not negligent in placing it there, and therefore the animal's owner cannot invoke the usual *reshus harabbim* exemption. On this reading, the liability might be full *shein* rather than *chatzi nezek keren*. The *gemara* leaves the precise characterization somewhat open.

## Mitgalgel Case

### Zevid Question

Rav Zevid raises a question: *mitgalgel mahu* — what is the law when the food is rolling? The *gemara* identifies the case: a bundle of grain (*amir*) is in *reshus hayachid* and is rolling — or being rolled — from *reshus hayachid* to *reshus harabbim*. The *Rambam* and *Ra'avad* have the inverse *girsa*, with the bundle in *reshus harabbim* rolling toward *reshus hayachid*, but the underlying question is the same.

### Rashi Push

Rashi understands the case not as the bundle rolling under its own momentum, but as the animal pushing the bundle. The animal enters the owner's domain, pushes the food out into *reshus harabbim*, and then eats it there. The question is whether liability follows the location of the eating (in *reshus harabbim*, hence exempt) or the location where the animal first accessed the food (in *reshus hayachid*, hence liable). Rashi accordingly emends *mitgalgel* to *megalgel*, indicating the animal is actively rolling the bundle rather than the bundle moving on its own.

Intuitively, one would say the owner is *chayav*: the animal entered private domain without permission, removed property from protected space, and consumed it. That the final act of eating happened technically in *reshus harabbim* seems like it should be irrelevant. However, the strict formalist reading — *bi'er bisadeh acher*, damages are assessed at the place of consumption — points toward exemption. The *gemara* does not resolve this question cleanly.

### Bundle Half Cases

The *gemara* brings a *baraita* from Rabbi Chiyya: *masoi miktzato bifnim u'miktzato bachutz* — a bundle half inside *reshus hayachid* and half outside in *reshus harabbim*. *Achla bifnim chayaves, achla bachutz petura* — if the animal ate from the inside portion, it is liable; if from the outside portion, it is exempt. The *gemara* initially understands this as referring to two different outcomes for the same bundle — the animal pushed it entirely inside or entirely outside before eating. If pushed entirely inside and eaten there, liable; if pushed entirely outside and eaten in *reshus harabbim*, exempt — supporting the view that what matters is where the eating occurred, not where the bundle originated.

The *gemara* then rejects this interpretation: *lo eima achla mah shebifnim chayaves mah shebachutz petura* — the *baraita* simply means if the animal ate the portion physically located inside it pays, and if it ate the portion physically outside it is exempt, without any movement of the bundle at all. A further resolution: *ki ka'amar Rabbi Chiyya b'shilya de'aspasta* — Rabbi Chiyya is discussing long stalks of fodder that span both domains as a continuous object. Since part of the stalk is inside and part outside, eating the outside portion is considered eating in *reshus harabbim*, and eating the inside portion in *reshus hayachid*.

On Rashi's reading, the question of the rolling bundle remains: if the bundle began entirely in *reshus hayachid* and was pushed entirely into *reshus harabbim* before being eaten, does liability follow the origin or the eating? The *gemara* does not conclusively resolve this, though it suggests that where the bundle started half-outside, we are more inclined to follow the eating, since it was already partly in public space to begin with.

### Tosafot Rolling

Tosafot reads the *sugya* entirely differently. According to Tosafot, the *gemara*'s conclusion from elsewhere is clear that we follow the place of eating, not the place of taking. Tosafot therefore understands *mitgalgel* as the bundle rolling *on its own*, not being pushed by the animal. The scenario is: fruit is resting in *reshus hanizak* on an incline and rolling downhill toward *reshus harabbim*. The animal, standing in *reshus hanizak*, opens its mouth and catches the food before it reaches *reshus harabbim*. The question is whether to consider this eating in *reshus hayachid* (where animal and food are) or in *reshus harabbim* (where the food was destined to land).

This reading is striking — the animal is physically in *reshus hanizak*, the food is physically in *reshus hanizak*, and yet the *gemara* considers whether the eating might be treated as having occurred in *reshus harabbim* because that is where the food was heading. One possible rationale is rooted in *peshiat hanizak*: since the food was already on its way to *reshus harabbim* and the owner had effectively lost control of it, the owner's protection is compromised even before the food physically crosses the boundary. If it would inevitably have arrived in *reshus harabbim*, the owner can be said to have already forfeited the protection that *reshus hanizak* status would normally provide — and therefore the eating is treated as though it occurred in the unprotected public space.

## Kesus & Keilim

### Mishnah Garments

The *mishnah* states that if an animal ate garments or vessels — *kesus u'keilim* — it pays *chatzi nezek*, and this liability is, per the *mishnah*'s framing, only in *reshus hanizak*. By extension, in *reshus harabbim* the owner would be exempt even for this.

### Rav Akulu Exempt

This is surprising: eating garments is *meshunah* and classified as a type of *keren*. Normally *keren* in *reshus harabbim* carries liability (at least on some views). Why should eating garments in *reshus harabbim* be exempt? Rav states: *amar rav akulu* — even on both cases, eating garments and eating normal food alike, the owner is exempt in *reshus harabbim*.

### Kol HaMeshaneh

The reason: *kol hameshaneh u'va acher v'shina bo patur* — whoever does something abnormal, and another party comes and does something abnormal in response, the second party is exempt. This is a formulation of *peshiat hanizak* at an elevated level. Leaving garments in *reshus harabbim* is itself an abnormal act; the animal's equally abnormal response of eating those garments flows from the victim's own initial deviation from the norm. Because the victim initiated the abnormal chain of events, the animal's owner is exempt.

It is worth distinguishing *kol hameshaneh* from ordinary *peshiat hanizak*. Standard negligence means one failed to exercise due care in a situation where care was required — perhaps a normal act carried out without sufficient attention. *Kol hameshaneh* is a higher threshold: one actually violated the norms of appropriate behavior in a given space, doing something that should not have been done there at all. When that threshold is crossed, the victim loses the protection of *nezikin* law for the consequences that follow.

### Shmuel Dispute

Shmuel disagrees: *lo shanu ela peiros v'yarkos* — the exemption in *reshus harabbim* applies only to normal food (fruits and vegetables). *Aval kesut v'keilim chayavet* — if it ate garments, the owner is still liable even in *reshus harabbim*, because this is *keren* and *kol hameshaneh* does not rise to the level needed to eliminate the liability. Shmuel apparently holds that leaving garments in *reshus harabbim*, while perhaps not ideal, does not constitute the kind of norm violation that would trigger *kol hameshaneh*.

## Two Cows

### Reish Lakish Crouched

Reish Lakish applies the same principle to a case of physical damage: *shtei paros bi'reshus harabbim, achas revutza v'achas mehaleches* — two cows in *reshus harabbim*, one crouched down resting (an abnormal thing to do in a public thoroughfare) and one walking. *B'atas mehaleches bi'revutza petura* — if the walking cow kicks the crouching cow, even though kicking is a classic example of *keren*, the walking cow's owner is exempt.

### Abnormality Start

The reasoning follows *kol hameshaneh*: the crouching cow started the abnormal chain of events by lying down in the middle of *reshus harabbim*. The walking cow's owner cannot be held liable for the consequences of the other owner's initial norm violation.

### Yochanan Dispute

Rabbi Yochanan again stakes out the opposing position: *lo shanu ela peiros v'yarkos* — the *reshus harabbim* exemption applies only to eating fruit and vegetables. *Aval kesut v'keilim chayavet* — for garments, you are liable. And by extension, for *keren*-type damage like kicking, Rabbi Yochanan would presumably hold the walking cow liable.

### Norm vs. Negligence

The *gemara* asks: does Rabbi Yochanan reject the principle of *kol hameshaneh* entirely? *Lo, le'olam it lei* — no, he accepts the principle in general. He would agree, for instance, with Reish Lakish's case about the two cows. His distinction is that having garments on the ground in *reshus harabbim* — *kesut* — is not truly a violation of norms. *Avidi inshi de'manchi glimeihu u'mitpechi* — people regularly take off their coats for a moment and set them down in a public space to rest. That is within the range of acceptable behavior. *Aval behemah lav orcha* — but a cow crouching in the middle of the public thoroughfare is genuinely outside acceptable norms. So *kol hameshaneh* applies to the crouching cow but not to someone who briefly puts down a garment.

The distinction between *kol hameshaneh* and ordinary *peshiat hanizak* is significant. *Peshiat hanizak* means: you should have foreseen that something could happen to your property and you failed to protect it. That can apply even when what you did was entirely normal. *Kol hameshaneh* requires more: an actual violation of the behavioral norms of the space, not just insufficient precaution. It is the higher standard that completely removes the victim's legal protection for the consequences that follow.

## Benefit Valuation

### Rava Hay Value

The *gemara* now returns to the question of how to value the benefit payment. Rava offers one position: *dmei amir* — the owner pays the value of a bundle of hay. Even if the animal ate barley, the owner's actual saving is measured by what he would have spent on the animal's normal food, which is hay. The barley was more nutritious, but since the owner never would have purchased barley for his animal, his benefit is measured by what he saved, not by what was consumed.

### Wholesale Price

Rava is then cited for an alternative position: *dmei se'orin be'zol* — pay the price of barley at the wholesale (bulk) rate, which another passage establishes as roughly two-thirds of retail. On this view, since the animal actually received barley and barley provides more nourishment than hay, the owner's benefit is measured by the actual value of what was consumed — but at the wholesale price, since that represents the fair economic measure of the grain, not its inflated retail cost.

These two positions reflect a genuine conceptual tension: does the benefit payment track what the owner *saved* (his baseline expenditure on the animal's food), or what the owner *received* (the actual value of the superior nourishment)?

### Shimon Ben Yochai

A *baraita* supports the first view: Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai says *ein meshalemes ela d'mei amir bilvad* — you pay only the value of hay, nothing more.

### Benefit Estimate

A separate *baraita* supports the second view, aligned with Rava's alternative formulation: *im nehenis meshalemes mah she-nehenis. Keitzad? Achla kav o kavayim, ein omdim tishalem d'meihen* — do not simply calculate the market price of what was consumed. Rather, *ela omdim kama adam rotzeh le-ha'achil l'vimto davar ra'ui lah af al pi she-eino ragil* — assess how much a person would be willing to pay to give his animal something fit for it, even though it is not his normal feed. The measure is willingness-to-pay for the specific benefit received, not retail market price and not baseline expenditure.

This third measure is distinct from both alternatives: it is neither the hay value nor the wholesale barley price, but an estimate of how much the owner would have paid to procure this particular benefit for his animal, given that barley is good for it even if not its regular diet.

### Bad Food Exception

The *gemara* notes an exception: *achla chitin o davar hara lah, petura* — if the animal ate wheat or something that is actually bad for it, the owner pays nothing. Since the animal received no benefit — or was actively harmed — there is no *hana'ah* for which to pay.

## No Loss Benefit

### House Tenant

The *gemara* now shifts to a standalone and broadly applicable topic: *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser* — one party is benefited while the other incurs no loss. Does the benefiting party owe anything? The discussion is framed around Rav Chisda's presentation to Rami bar Chama: *hadar ba'chatzeir chaveiro she-lo mida'ato* — someone who lives in another's house without the owner's knowledge. Must he pay rent?

The four cases in this discussion are presented together, as different permutations of the same framework.

### No Benefit/Loss

The first case is where the house is not normally rented out and the tenant has somewhere else to stay at no cost. Here: *zeh lo neheneh v'zeh lo chaser* — neither benefit nor loss. The tenant could have stayed elsewhere for free and derives no real economic benefit; the owner loses nothing since he would not have collected rent. This case requires no analysis — obviously no payment is owed.

### Both Benefit/Loss

The second case: the house is actively on the market for rent and the tenant genuinely needs housing with nowhere else to go. Here: *zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser* — the tenant receives real economic benefit and the owner suffers real loss of rental income. This case also requires no analysis — the tenant clearly owes payment.

### Middle Cases

The interesting cases are in the middle. One middle case: the owner does not normally rent out the house, but the tenant genuinely needs housing. *Mai?* Can the tenant say "you wouldn't have earned anything from this house anyway, so you lost nothing, I owe nothing"? Or can the owner reply: "You received real benefit from my property, and you owe me for it"? This is the case of *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser* — the core unresolved question.

### Indirect Loss

The other middle case — not explicitly resolved in this passage — is where the owner is actively trying to rent the property, but the tenant did not need housing and could have stayed elsewhere for free. Tosafot holds that since the tenant received no real benefit (he could have obtained equivalent accommodation at no cost), and the owner's loss is only indirect (potential renters saw the house occupied and did not call), this constitutes *grama* — indirect causation — and the tenant is exempt. Most *rishonim*, including the Rosh, reject this. They hold that the tenant did receive real benefit by staying in the house, regardless of whether he could have stayed elsewhere. The fact that the owner has assigned a rental value to the space means the tenant effectively used something with a defined economic value. The *Rosh* treats this as *zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser* even if the tenant personally could have avoided the cost, because the benefit he received is real and the owner's loss of rental opportunity is real.

## Rami Answer Critique

### Mishnah Citation

Rami bar Chama responds to Rav Chisda's question by citing the *mishnah*: *im nehenis meshalemes mah she-nehenis* — if the animal derived benefit from eating your grain in *reshus harabbim*, it pays for the benefit. He presents this as an answer to the question of *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser*.

### Loss Case Noted

Rava dismisses this sharply: *kama lo cheili v'lo margish gavra de-marei saiyeh* — how fortunate is the one whose master has his back, because even though the answer was wrong, Rav Chisda accepted it. Rava's critique: *hai zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser* — the *mishnah*'s case is one of *zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser*. The animal eats your grain; you clearly lose your grain. Of course you are owed the benefit payment. That case answers nothing about *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser*.

### Real Question

The real question posed in the *beit midrash* the previous evening was: what is the *halachah* where one party benefits and the other loses nothing? Does benefit alone — without any corresponding loss to the other party — create an obligation of restitution? The *mishnah* about animal eating does not address this because there the owner always loses his grain.

### Public Fruit

Rami bar Chama apparently thought the *mishnah* could serve as an answer because fruit left in *reshus harabbim* is treated as effectively ownerless — *stam peiros bi'reshus harabbim afkurei mafkir lehu*. If the owner has effectively abandoned protection of his fruit by leaving it in the public domain, then perhaps even as he suffers the loss, it is as though he has no legitimate claim — making it structurally closer to *zeh lo chaser*. Rava rejects this reasoning: the owner has still lost real property, and the structural comparison to a *zeh lo chaser* scenario fails.

### Deferred Answer

The *gemara* concludes that the *mishnah* is simply a case of *zeh neheneh v'zeh chaser*, and the question of *zeh neheneh v'zeh lo chaser* remains open — to be taken up in the next session.
