# Rosh Hashanah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Adar's Length

### Adar Fixed Length

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ לְמָר עוּקְבָא: אֲדָר הַסָּמוּךְ לְנִיסָן — לְעוֹלָם חָסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** A ruling **was sent** from Eretz Yisrael **to Mar Ukva,** the Exilarch in Babylonia: **The Adar that** immediately **precedes Nisan is always short,** both in a regular year and in a leap year. But the first Adar in a leap year, which does not immediately precede Nisan, is sometimes full.

The discussion on daf 20 continues the broader inquiry into the lengths of certain months and whether the *shluchim* — the messengers sent to announce *Rosh Chodesh* — needed to travel for months whose length was in principle already fixed. The background assumption, which will be challenged, is that certain months preceding critical holidays were always twenty-nine days: *Elul* before *Tishrei*, and the *Adar* preceding *Nissan* — whether a standard *Adar* or *Adar Sheini* in a leap year — were always *chaser*, twenty-nine days. This would give communities a reliable basis for knowing when *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* and *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* would fall, even without receiving the messengers' report.

To set the stage, the *Gemara* on 19b brings a *drasha* transmitted by Rabbi Simai in the name of Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi: regarding the two *Adarim* of a leap year, if the court wished, it could make both of them full (*malei*, thirty days), both of them deficient (*chaser*, twenty-nine days), or one full and one deficient — and such was the practice in the *golah*. In the name of Rav, however, it was said that the proper approach is for the first *Adar* to be full and the second deficient, unless one has definite knowledge that the first *Adar* had been fixed at twenty-nine days in its proper time. On this view, the default assumption is thirty and twenty-nine.

Against this background, a tradition was sent to Mar Ukva: *Adar hasamuch leNissan le'olam chaser* — the *Adar* immediately preceding *Nissan* is always twenty-nine days, regardless of whether it is a regular *Adar* or an *Adar Sheini*.

### Witnesses & Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב נַחְמָן: עַל שְׁנֵי חֳדָשִׁים מְחַלְּלִין אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת — עַל נִיסָן וְעַל תִּשְׁרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman raised an objection** from what was taught in a mishna: Witnesses who saw the new moon **may desecrate Shabbat for** the fixing of the New Moon of **two months, for** the month of **Nisan and for** the month of **Tishrei,** due to the important Festivals that occur in them.

Rav Nachman challenges this position from our *Mishnah*: on two months, witnesses violate *Shabbat* to come and testify — for *Nissan* and for *Tishrei*, because the holidays of those months depend on getting the dates right. Since *Nissan* follows *Adar*, the difficulty is immediate: if *Adar* is always twenty-nine days, then *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* is automatically day thirty — fixed and known in advance. Why, then, would witnesses need to violate *Shabbat* to testify about it? Their testimony would be superfluous; the date is predetermined regardless of what they say.

### Mitzvah Lekadesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְעוֹלָם חָסֵר, אַמַּאי מְחַלְּלִינַן? מִשּׁוּם דְּמִצְוָה לְקַדֵּשׁ עַל הָרְאִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that the Adar immediately preceding Nisan is **always short, why should they desecrate** Shabbat? The court can calculate the time of the New Moon without witnesses. The Gemara answers: **Because it is a mitzva to sanctify** the New Moon **on the basis of** the testimony of witnesses who actually **saw** the new moon and not rely on calculations or established practices.

The *Gemara* initially deflects: *mishum d'mitzvah lekadesh al har'iyah* — there is a *mitzvah* to sanctify the new month on the basis of witness testimony, and for the sake of fulfilling that *mitzvah* properly, the witnesses violate *Shabbat* even when the date could technically be determined without them. On this reading, even if we know when *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* will be, we nevertheless want to anchor the sanctification in actual testimony.

This prompts an alternative formulation in the name of Rav Nachman — *ika de'amri*, some say — that actually the *Mishnah* can be read in the opposite direction, as support for the view that *Adar* is always twenty-nine. The logic runs as follows: if *Adar* is always twenty-nine days, and therefore we already know which day will be *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and that day happens to fall on *Shabbat*, we would still have the witnesses travel on *Shabbat* — because *mitzvah lekadesh al pi hare'iyah*, we want to perform the sanctification through testimony. This accounts neatly for why witnesses violate *Shabbat* for *Nissan* even if the date is fixed.

By contrast, if *Adar* is variable — sometimes twenty-nine, sometimes thirty — the *Gemara* asks why witnesses would ever need to violate *Shabbat* at all. If day thirty of *Adar* falls on *Shabbat* and the court has not yet fixed the month, could they not simply delay, leave the witnesses to come on Sunday, and declare Sunday — day thirty-one — as *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*? The court would avoid any violation of *Shabbat* and still conduct the sanctification based on testimony. The *Gemara* responds: this works only if day thirty falls on *Shabbat*. But the *Mishnah*'s case is that day thirty-one falls on *Shabbat* — that is, Friday is already day thirty, and if witnesses are to testify for that day, they must travel on *Shabbat*. At that point the court has no flexibility to delay; they cannot push *Rosh Chodesh* to day thirty-two. The witnesses must travel, and the case of violating *Shabbat* is necessary regardless.

### Rav Kahana's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּשֶׁהַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים מְחַלְּלִין אַף עַל כּוּלָּן, מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַקׇּרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raised an objection** from the continuation of the mishna, which teaches: **When the Temple was standing,** the witnesses **would desecrate** Shabbat **for** establishing the New Moon **even** for **all** the months, **in order to rectify the offering** of the New Moon, i.e., ensure that it is at the proper time.

Rav Kahana now presses the matter further. The *Mishnah* concludes: *keshehamikdash kayam mechallelin af akulahu mipnei takanat hakorban* — when the *Beit HaMikdash* stood, witnesses would violate *Shabbat* for every *Rosh Chodesh*, not only *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, because the *korbanot* of *Rosh Chodesh* needed to be brought on the correct day. This is Rav Kahana's proof: the fact that witnesses violate *Shabbat* is not, in principle, an expression of the abstract *mitzvah* to sanctify on the basis of testimony. If it were, they would violate *Shabbat* for every month, not only *Nissan* and *Tishrei*. The restriction to *Nissan* and *Tishrei* — in the period without the *Mikdash* — shows that witnesses violate *Shabbat* only when something practical hangs in the balance: the actual date must be uncertain and its determination must have concrete consequences.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּכוּלְּהוּ לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּמִצְוָה לְקַדֵּשׁ עַל הָרְאִיָּיה — נִיסָן וְתִשְׁרִי נָמֵי לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּמִצְוָה לְקַדֵּשׁ עַל הָרְאִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the fact that the reason that the witnesses are permitted to desecrate Shabbat for **all** the other months is **not because it is a mitzva to sanctify** the month based **on** testimony of witnesses who **saw** the new moon, the implication is that with regard to **Nisan and Tishrei as well,** the witnesses are permitted to desecrate Shabbat for another reason and **not because it is a mitzva to sanctify** the month based **on** the testimony of witnesses who **saw** the new moon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא זִמְנִין מָלֵא וְזִמְנִין חָסֵר, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְחַלְּלִינַן. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְעוֹלָם חָסֵר, אַמַּאי מְחַלְּלִינַן? תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that Adar **is sometimes full and sometimes short, due to that** reason the witnesses **may desecrate** Shabbat. **But if you say** that Adar **is always short, why should they desecrate** Shabbat? The court knows in advance when will be the New Moon of Nisan. The Gemara concludes: This is indeed **a conclusive refutation** to the proof brought by Rav Naḥman.

The *Gemara* draws the inference directly: *midekulahu lav mishum d'mitzvah lekadesh al pi hare'iyah* — from the fact that witnesses violate *Shabbat* for every month when the *Mikdash* stood, and only for *Nissan* and *Tishrei* when it did not, it follows that the reason for violating *Shabbat* is not the general desire to sanctify through testimony. It follows that the reason it applies to *Nissan* and *Tishrei* specifically is that the date is genuinely uncertain — and that means *Adar* cannot be fixed in advance. The *Gemara* concludes: *tiyuvta* — a decisive refutation. The position that *Adar* is always twenty-nine days is rejected. *Adar* can be either twenty-nine or thirty days, depending on testimony.

### Adar Variable

This is the *Gemara*'s conclusion for this section: *Adar* preceding *Nissan* is not invariably twenty-nine days. Its length was determined by when witnesses testified to having seen the new moon. The assumption of a fixed, predetermined *Adar* cannot be sustained.

## Elul 30 Days

### Ulla's News

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא עוּלָּא, אָמַר: עַבְּרוּהּ לֶאֱלוּל. אָמַר עוּלָּא: יָדְעִי חַבְרִין בַּבְלָאֵי מַאי טֵיבוּתָא עָבְדִינַן בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** § It is related that **when Ulla came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** This year **they added** an extra day to the month of **Elul. Ulla** continued and **said: Do our Babylonian colleagues understand what benefit we did for them?** We pushed off Rosh HaShana for a day, so that the Festival would not occur adjacent to Shabbat.

With the parallel assumption about *Elul* still in place — *miyemot Ezra va'eilach lo matzinu Elul me'ubar*, that from the days of Ezra onward *Elul* was never made thirty days — the *Gemara* now introduces a striking report. *Ki ata Ulla* — when *Ulla* came from Eretz Yisrael, he announced: *ibruhu l'Elul* — they made *Elul* thirty days. The confidence we might have had that *Elul* was always twenty-nine, providing certainty about when *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* would fall, has now been undermined as well. Just as *Adar* turns out not to be invariably twenty-nine, so too *Elul* was on at least one occasion extended to thirty days.

*Ulla* himself adds: *yadai chavrain b'Bavel mai tivuta avdinan b'hadaihu* — our colleagues in Babylon should know what a favor we did for them by making *Elul* thirty days. *Tosafot* sharpens the implicit objection: what exactly is the favor? From the Babylonian perspective, one might have preferred that *Elul* simply remain twenty-nine days without exception — that way *Rosh Hashanah* would be fully predictable, and there would be no need for a second day of *Yom Tov* observed in the diaspora due to uncertainty. If the calendar were perfectly fixed, the "favor" of adjusting *Elul* carries a hidden cost.

### Metaya & Yarka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טֵיבוּתָא? עוּלָּא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם יַרְקַיָּא. רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם מִתַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the **benefit** in having a weekday between Shabbat and a Festival? **Ulla said: Due to the vegetables** that would not be picked for two days and those picked beforehand that would no longer be fresh. **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina said: Due to the dead** who would not be buried for two days and consequently would begin to decompose.

The *Gemara* presses: *mai tivuta* — what was the benefit? Two answers are given. *Ulla amar mishum yarka* — *Ulla* himself says: because of vegetables. *Rav Acha bar Chanina amar mishum metaya* — Rav Acha bar Chanina says: because of dead bodies.

The concern of *metaya* — dead bodies — arises when *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* would fall on consecutive days. In such a case, there would be two full days on which burial is impossible: one cannot bury on *Shabbat* (even through non-Jews) and one cannot bury on *Yom Kippur*. In climates without refrigeration, two consecutive days of inability to bury a corpse poses a serious practical problem. The calendar is therefore adjusted — *Elul* is made thirty days — to prevent *Yom Kippur* from falling immediately adjacent to *Shabbat*.

The concern of *yarka* — vegetables — arises when *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* fall consecutively. Without refrigeration, if one picks fresh vegetables on Friday for *Shabbat*, and *Yom Tov* immediately follows, the vegetables will have wilted by the time *Yom Tov* arrives. Making *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* non-consecutive ensures that fresh vegetables can be gathered the day before each.

### Reason Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת אַחַר הַשַּׁבָּת. מַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם מִתַיָּא — מְעַבְּרִינַן. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם יַרְקַיָּא, לְאֵימַת קָא בָּעֵי לְהוּ — לְאוּרְתָּא, לְאוּרְתָּא טָרַח וּמַיְיתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** these two concerns? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is in the case where **Yom Kippur occurs** directly **after Shabbat.** According to **the one who said** that the reason is **due to the dead** that would go unburied for two days, the court **adds** an extra day to Elul so that Yom Kippur will not occur on Sunday. **But** according to **the one who said** that the reason is **due to the vegetables** that would not be fresh, there is no need to add an extra day to Elul. **When would he require** the vegetables? Only **in the evening** following Yom Kippur; and if Yom Kippur falls on Sunday, he can go out **in the evening** after the fast **and bring** fresh vegetables.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם יַרְקַיָּא, לְעַבְּרֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם מִתַיָּא! אֶלָּא: אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ יוֹם טוֹב הַסָּמוּךְ לַשַּׁבָּת בֵּין מִלְּפָנֶיהָ בֵּין מִלְּאַחֲרֶיהָ. מַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם יַרְקַיָּא — מְעַבְּרִינַן, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם מִתַיָּא — אֶפְשָׁר בְּעַמְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who says** that the reason is **due to vegetables,** the court **should** still **make** Elul **full due to the dead,** as this is also an important consideration. **Rather,** the practical difference **between them** is with regard to the case where **the Festival** of Rosh HaShana **occurs adjacent to Shabbat, either before it or after it.** According to **the one who said** that the reason is **due to the vegetables** that would wither, the court **adds** an extra day to Elul. **But** according to **the one who said** that the reason is **due to the dead** that would go unburied, there is no reason to make Elul full, because on a Festival **it is possible** to arrange that the dead be buried **by gentiles.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם מִתַיָּא, לְעַבְּרֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם יַרְקַיָּא! אֶפְשָׁר בְּחַמִּימֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to the one who said** that the reason is **due to the dead,** the court **should** still **add** an extra day to Elul **due to the vegetables.** The Gemara answers: According to him this is not an important consideration, **because it is possible** to soak the withered vegetables **in hot water** and thereby restore their freshness.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai beinayhu* — what practical difference does it make which reason one invokes? The case that distinguishes them is *Yom Kippur* falling immediately after *Shabbat* rather than before it. According to the one who says the concern is *metaya*, we would adjust the calendar to prevent that configuration. According to the one who says the concern is *yarka*, there is no vegetable issue on *Yom Kippur* itself, since one fasts on *Yom Kippur*; and after *Yom Kippur*, one can simply go pick fresh vegetables — *l'orta tarach u'maity* — so there is no need to adjust the calendar for that case.

The *Gemara* then observes that the *metaya* concern is so serious that even according to the one who says the primary reason is *yarka*, one would certainly also adjust for *metaya*. The real dispute, then, is over *yarka* alone. When *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* are consecutive — which, of course, occurs regularly in our experience — would one adjust the calendar? According to the *yarka* view, yes: *maavrin*, one adds a day to the previous month. According to the *metaya* view alone, no: *efshar b'amamei* — non-Jews can bury the dead on the first day of *Yom Tov*, so consecutive *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* does not create the two-day burial crisis. And as for vegetables on *Yom Tov*, *efshar b'chamimei* — one can keep them warm or heated to preserve them adequately. The vegetable concern is not severe enough to override the calendar.

As is well known, our calendar reflects the *metaya* position: *Yom Kippur* never falls on a Friday or a Sunday, but *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* do fall consecutively.

### Babylon's Climate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי: מַאי שְׁנָא לְדִידַן, אֲפִילּוּ לְדִידְהוּ נָמֵי! לְדִידַן חֲבִיל לַן עָלְמָא, לְדִידְהוּ לָא חֲבִיל לְהוּ עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, what is different** about those who live in Babylonia and those who live in Eretz Yisrael? Why did Ulla specifically say that adding an extra day to Elul was beneficial **to us,** in Babylonia; it was beneficial **to them as well.** The Gemara answers: **For us** in Babylonia the weather **is** very **hot,** and so vegetables wither and corpses decompose quickly. But **for them** in Eretz Yisrael, the weather **is not** as **hot,** and vegetables and corpses can be kept for two days.

One might ask why *Ulla* singles out the Babylonians as the recipients of this favor. The *Gemara* answers: *l'didan chavila lanu alma* — for us in Babylon, the world is intensely hot. The concerns that motivate the calendar adjustment — decomposing bodies and wilting vegetables — are far more acute in the humid heat of Babylon than in the more temperate, Mediterranean climate of Eretz Yisrael, where *lo chavilu alma* — the conditions are simply not as severe. The Babylonians, therefore, have particular reason to be grateful that the court in Eretz Yisrael adjusted *Elul* to prevent these problems. *Tosafot*'s irony remains unresolved by the *Gemara*: one might still prefer a fully predictable calendar to the benefits gained by the adjustment.

## Calendar Manipulation

### Adding Days Query

The previous discussion raises a deeper question: when the court makes *Elul* thirty days to prevent *Yom Kippur* from falling adjacent to *Shabbat*, are they doing so in response to the testimony of witnesses, or are they simply manipulating the calendar for their own reasons, independent of the moon? And if the latter, how can this be justified?

### Verse Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָתָנֵי רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: יָכוֹל כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּעַבְּרִין אֶת הַשָּׁנָה לְצוֹרֶךְ, כָּךְ מְעַבְּרִין אֶת הַחֹדֶשׁ לְצוֹרֶךְ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה לָכֶם רֹאשׁ חֳדָשִׁים״ — כָּזֶה רְאֵה וְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught above that for various reasons a month can have an extra day added, even if the new moon was seen on the night before the thirtieth. The Gemara challenges this assumption: **Is that so? But didn't Rabba bar Shmuel teach** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought **that just as** the court **adds** an extra month to **a year for** some pressing communal **need, so too,** the court **adds** an extra day to **a month for** some similar **need.** Therefore, **the verse states: "This month shall be to you the beginning of months"** (Exodus 12:2). This teaches that when there is a moon **like this, see** it **and sanctify** the month. When the new moon is seen, the month must be sanctified without delay.

The *Gemara* brings a *braita* taught by Rabba bar Shmuel: one might think that just as we intercalate the year — adding an extra month — for a need (*l'tzorech*), so too we might add a day to a month for a need, regardless of the moon. But the verse teaches: *hachodesh hazeh lachem rosh chodashim* — *kazeh re'eh v'kadesh*, see and sanctify. One must first see — that is, receive testimony — and only then sanctify. The calendar cannot be manipulated at will based purely on external concerns.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לְעַבְּרוֹ, כָּאן לְקַדְּשׁוֹ. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: יָכוֹל כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּעַבְּרִין אֶת הַשָּׁנָה וְאֶת הַחֹדֶשׁ לְצוֹרֶךְ, כָּךְ מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַחֹדֶשׁ לְצוֹרֶךְ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה לָכֶם״ — כָּזֶה רְאֵה וְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: This** is **not difficult: Here** it is speaking of **adding** an extra day to the month, although the new moon was seen on the thirtieth, which is permitted; whereas **there** it is speaking of **sanctifying** the month on the thirtieth, although the new moon was not yet seen, which is prohibited. **And this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** One **might** have thought **that just as** the court **adds to a year or a month for** some pressing communal **need, so too, a month can be sanctified** early on the thirtieth even without seeing the new moon. Therefore, **the verse states: "This month shall be to you the beginning of months,"** which teaches: Only when the moon appears **like this, see** it **and sanctify** the month, but not before.

Rava resolves the apparent tension: *lo kashya — kan l'avro kan lekadsho*. There are two distinct ways of overriding the astronomical testimony. *Le'avro* means delaying: witnesses appeared on day thirty and testified, but the court suppresses their testimony and makes the month thirty days, pushing *Rosh Chodesh* to day thirty-one. *Lekadsho* means advancing: the witnesses did not appear on day thirty, and the court sanctifies it as *Rosh Chodesh* without any testimony, making the previous month only twenty-nine days.

Rava's point is that the verse's requirement — *re'eh v'kadesh*, see then sanctify — limits only early sanctification. Doing it *le'avro*, a day late, still respects the basic procedure: witnesses testified, and the court sanctified, even if slightly delayed. But advancing — *lekadsho* — without prior testimony directly violates *kazeh re'eh v'kadesh*. Therefore, what the case of *Elul* demonstrates is permissible delay (*le'avro*), not impermissible early sanctification.

### Manipulation Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מְאַיְּימִין עַל הָעֵדִים עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ שֶׁנִּרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — לְעַבְּרוֹ, וְאֵין מְאַיְּימִין עַל הָעֵדִים עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ שֶׁלֹּא נִרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — לְקַדְּשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that this is **like that which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** The court **may intimidate witnesses** through rigorous and confusing examination, so that they will give inadmissible testimony **about the** new **moon that was seen at its** due **time,** the thirtieth of the month. This is so that the court **can add** an extra day to **it,** and the New Moon will be sanctified on the thirty-first day. **But** the court **may not intimidate witnesses** into giving testimony **about the new moon that was not seen on its** due **time, in order to sanctify** the New Moon early, on the thirtieth.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi articulates the limits that follow from this distinction: *me'aymin al ha-edim al ha-chodesh she-nir'ah bi-zmano le-avro* — the court may even intimidate witnesses who saw the moon at the expected time in order to silence them, thereby allowing the month to be extended. The court can actively suppress testimony in order to delay *Rosh Chodesh*. But: *v'ein me'aymin al ha-edim al ha-chodesh she-lo nir'ah bi-zmano le-kadsho* — the court may not intimidate witnesses to testify that they saw the moon a day before they actually did, in order to advance *Rosh Chodesh* to an earlier day. Suborning false testimony to enable early sanctification is categorically prohibited. The hierarchy, at this point in the *Gemara*, is: delay is permissible (and the court may even silence witnesses to achieve it); early sanctification is not, and procuring false testimony to enable it is certainly not.

### Rabbi Yochanan Opposite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי: הֱווּ יוֹדְעִין שֶׁכׇּל יָמָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָיָה מְלַמְּדֵנוּ: מְאַיְּימִין עַל הָעֵדִים עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ שֶׁלֹּא נִרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — לְקַדְּשׁוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא רָאוּהוּ, יֹאמְרוּ ״רָאִינוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Yehuda Nesia,** who was the *Nasi* of the Sanhedrin, **send** a message **to Rabbi Ami: Be aware that all the days of Rabbi Yoḥanan he would teach us** that **witnesses may be intimidated** into giving testimony **about the** new **moon that was not seen in its** due **time.** This was done **in order that** the new month **may be sanctified** early, already on the thirtieth. **Although** the witnesses **did not see** the moon, **they may say: We saw it.**

This position is now challenged. *Rav Yehuda Nesi'ah* — understood by *Tosafot* to be a later figure who was a contemporary of *Rabbi Ami*, not the immediate grandson of Rav Yehuda HaNasi — sent word to *Rabbi Ami*: be aware that throughout the lifetime of *Rabbi Yochanan*, he would consistently teach the exact opposite. *Me'aymin al ha-edim al ha-chodesh she-lo nir'ah bi-zmano le-kadsho* — the court would in fact intimidate witnesses to testify that they saw the moon a day early, even though they had not actually seen it, in order to advance *Rosh Chodesh*. *Af al pi she-lo ra'u, yomru ra'inu* — even though they did not see it, they would be made to say: we saw it. This is a striking position: not merely adjusting the calendar without witnesses, but actively procuring false testimony to do so.

### Abaye's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּנִיסָן וְתִשְׁרִי, הָא בִּשְׁאָר יַרְחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This** is **not difficult: This** ruling **is referring to Nisan and Tishrei,** which may be sanctified early in order to set the Festivals that occur in those months; **that** ruling is referring to **the other months,** which may not be sanctified early, even for some other pressing need.

Abaye resolves the contradiction between Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and Rabbi Yochanan by distinguishing between months: *ha be-Nisan u-ve-Tishrei, ha bi-shear yarchei* — one ruling applies to *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, the other to the remaining months. For *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, whose dates govern the holidays and carry the greatest practical weight, the court has maximum latitude: it will adjust the calendar in either direction, and if necessary will even procure false testimony to do so. For other months, the court will only delay — silencing witnesses and doing it late — but will not advance *Rosh Chodesh*, and certainly will not procure false testimony.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָא דְּתָנֵי רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל — אֲחֵרִים הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין בֵּין עֲצֶרֶת לַעֲצֶרֶת וְאֵין בֵּין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לָרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה אֶלָּא אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים בִּלְבַד. וְאִם הָיְתָה שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — חֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: That which Rabba bar Shmuel taught,** that the court may not lengthen or shorten the months for reason of communal need, follows the opinion of ***Aḥerim*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: ***Aḥerim* say: The** difference **between** the festival of ***Shavuot*** of one year and the festival of ***Shavuot*** of the following year, **and** similarly, **between Rosh HaShana** of one year and **Rosh HaShana** of the following year, **is only four days** of the week. There are 354 days in a lunar year, which are divided into twelve alternating months, six months that are thirty days long and six months that are twenty-nine days long. Therefore, every year is fifty weeks and four days long. **And if it was a leap year,** in which case the year is comprised of 383 days, or fifty-four weeks and five days, there is a difference of **five** days between them.

Rava offers a different resolution. The *braita* of Rabba bar Shmuel — which asserted that we may not adjust months at will and must be guided by testimony — does not represent the normative ruling. It is *Acherim hi* — it is the position of *Acherim*, identified as *Rabbi Meir*. The *braita* records: *Acherim omrim*, others say, *ein bein Atzeret l'Atzeret v'ein bein Rosh Hashanah l'Rosh Hashanah ela arba'ah yamim bilvad* — from one *Shavuot* to the next, or from one *Rosh Hashanah* to the next, the shift is only four days. In a leap year, five. This is because a standard Hebrew year of 354 days is fifty weeks with four days remaining; any given date shifts exactly four days from one year to the next (five in a leap year).

If one could confidently assert this four-day shift as invariable, it implies a perfectly predictable calendar — which is only possible if the calendar is not being adjusted at will. Rava reads this statement, however, as describing what would happen if the court always followed pure astronomical reality without any intervention. The *Gemara* reads it as the position of *Rabbi Meir* alone, who held that we do not manipulate the calendar at all. But the majority position is that we do adjust it as needed, and the statement of Rabba bar Shmuel that generated our difficulty is itself only the minority view of *Rabbi Meir*. According to the consensus, the calendar can be manipulated — extended, advanced, and in certain contexts even supported by suborned testimony — without restriction.

### Rav Dimi Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא מַתְנֵי אִיפְּכָא: מְאַיְּימִין עַל הָעֵדִים עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ שֶׁלֹּא נִרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — לְקַדְּשׁוֹ, וְאֵין מְאַיְּימִין עַל הָעֵדִים עַל הַחֹדֶשׁ שֶׁנִּרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — לְעַבְּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi from Neharde'a taught** the *baraita* in **the opposite** manner: The court **may intimidate the witnesses** into giving testimony **about the new moon that was not seen in its** due **time in order that** the new month **may be sanctified** early, already on the thirtieth. **But** the court **may not intimidate witnesses** so that they do not give acceptable testimony **about the** new **moon that was seen in its** due **time,** in order **to add** an extra day to the old month, so that the New Moon is sanctified on the thirty-first.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא — הַאי מִיחֲזֵי כְּשִׁקְרָא, הַאי לָא מִיחֲזֵי כְּשִׁקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for Rav Dimi's opinion? In **this** case, where the witnesses withhold testimony about what they saw and the New Moon is pushed off to the next day, it **looks like a lie,** as the moon might have been seen by others as well, and people will wonder why the New Moon was not sanctified the previous day. In **that** case, where the witnesses testify about something that they did not see and the New Moon is a day early, it **does not look like a lie,** as everybody knows that it is possible that only a few people saw the new moon.

Rav Dimi of Nehardea teaches the opposite order of preference from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *m'aimin al ha'edim al hachodesh shelo nira bizmano l'kadsho* — the court is more willing to procure false testimony to advance *Rosh Chodesh* than to silence witnesses and delay it. His reasoning turns on appearances: *ha mechzi k'shikra, ha lo mechzi k'shikra* — one scenario looks like a lie and the other does not.

The logic is as follows. If nobody actually saw the moon, but the court arranges for witnesses to testify that they saw it — and therefore declares today *Rosh Chodesh* — this will not be publicly apparent. A bystander who did not see the moon will simply assume it was visible and he missed it, or that conditions were different elsewhere. The court's manipulation is invisible. But if witnesses genuinely saw the moon and the court suppresses their testimony and announces that tomorrow — day thirty-one — will be *Rosh Chodesh*, bystanders who saw the moon clearly the night before will find this declaration absurd and transparently false. *Mechzi k'shikra* — it looks manifestly like a lie.

The irony, which the *Gemara* leaves standing, is striking: the court would prefer to procure actual false testimony if it does not look dishonest, over suppressing genuine testimony in a way that is visibly implausible. The concern with appearances of manipulation outweighs the concern with the underlying dishonesty of the manipulation itself.

The section closes with the conclusion that the court has broad latitude to adjust the calendar as needed. Whether one follows Abaye's distinction between *Nissan* and *Tishrei* versus other months, or Rava's position that the entire restriction belongs to the minority view of *Rabbi Meir*, the normative ruling allows calendar adjustment — including, under some views, the procurement of false testimony — when the need arises.

## Astronomy & Moon

### Shmuel's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יָכֵילְנָא לְתַקּוֹנֵי לְכוּלַּהּ גּוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel said: I am able to fix** the calendar **for the entire Diaspora** without witnesses. Shmuel was an expert on the movement of the celestial bodies and on the principles governing leap years and additional days added to months.

Having established that the calendar can be manipulated, the *Gemara* pivots to the astronomical expertise that underlies calendar determination. *Amar Shmuel*: *yachilna l'situnei l'chulei gola* — I could set up the calendar for the entire exile. *Shmuel*, renowned as a master of astronomy, is asserting that his command of the astronomical calculations was sufficient to establish the calendar independently, without relying on witness testimony from Eretz Yisrael — foreshadowing what ultimately occurred in the era of Hillel the Second.

### Before/After Chatzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי לִשְׁמוּאֵל: יָדַע מָר הַאי מִילְּתָא דְּתַנְיָא בְּסוֹד הָעִיבּוּר: נוֹלַד קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת אוֹ נוֹלַד אַחַר חֲצוֹת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּהָא לָא יָדַע מָר — אִיכָּא מִילֵּי אַחְרָנְיָיתָא דְּלָא יָדַע מָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abba, the father of Rabbi Simlai, said to Shmuel: Does the Master know** the meaning of **this statement, as it is taught** in a *baraita* dealing with **the secret of addition,** which discusses calendric calculations: Differentiate between when **the *molad* occurred before midday and** when **the *molad* occurred after midday? He said to him: No,** I do not know what this means. **He said to him: Since the Master does not know this, there are** probably **other matters that the Master does not know,** and therefore you must not establish a calendar, relying upon calculations that were made based on faulty or insufficient knowledge.

*Abba*, the father of Rabbi Simlai, put *Shmuel* to the test. He asked: do you know what is meant by the line taught in the *sod ha'ibbur* — the secret of intercalation, a cryptic compendium of the principles governing the calendar — *nolad kodem chatzot o nolad achar chatzot*? Does it matter whether the *molad* — the astronomical moment at which the moon moves out of conjunction — occurs before or after midday (*chatzot*)? And if so, why?

*Shmuel* admitted he did not know. *Abba* responded: if you do not know the meaning of this line, there must be many other things you do not know. The claim to be able to set up the calendar for the entire exile was premature.

### Night & Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא, שְׁלַח לְהוּ: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא לַיְלָה וָיוֹם מִן הֶחָדָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** As for the meaning of this obscure *baraita*, **when Rabbi Zeira went up** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, **he sent** back a letter to his colleagues in Babylonia: In order for a day to be sanctified as the New Moon, **it is necessary that** both **the night and the day be of the new** month. That is to say, the *molad* must occur before the beginning of the night.

When *Rav Zeira* ascended to Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted back the following teaching: *tzarich she-yehai layla v'yom min hachodesh* — it is required that both night and day belong to the month.

This is a cryptic formulation and attracts extensive commentary — the *Ba'al HaMa'or* (Rav Zerachyah HaLevi, twelfth century) devotes a substantial treatment to these few lines alone, which gives some measure of how dense and contested this passage is.

### Old Moon Visible

*Rashi* reads the principle *tzarich she-yehai layla v'yom min hachodesh* as follows. The moon is invisible for twenty-four hours around the time of conjunction: the old moon, moving in a counterclockwise direction relative to the sun, is visible only at the very end of the night — the last hours before sunrise — as it precedes the sun. It then sets before the sun and is invisible through the day. As it approaches the sun it becomes invisible entirely. After conjunction, the new moon moves out from behind the sun and becomes visible at the very start of night, immediately after sunset, then sets while it is still night.

Given this, *Rashi* connects the principle to the earlier discussion of calendar manipulation. Suppose witnesses saw the old moon during the night of day thirty, the day that has the potential of being sanctified as *Rosh Chodesh*. Even if the court wished to manipulate the calendar and declare that same day — that same daytime — as *Rosh Chodesh* based on alleged sighting of the new moon, this is impossible. People saw the old moon during the night just hours before. They know the new moon cannot have appeared in the intervening hours. The astronomical reality is publicly known, and the manipulation would be transparent — *mechzi k'shikra* in the most literal sense. The requirement that both night and day belong to the month thus sets an outer limit on calendar manipulation: the court cannot sanctify a day as *Rosh Chodesh* when the old moon was visibly present just hours earlier that same night.

### Visibility Prediction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ שֶׁאָמַר אַבָּא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: מְחַשְּׁבִין אֶת תּוֹלַדְתּוֹ. נוֹלַד קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁנִּרְאָה סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה. לֹא נוֹלַד קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלֹּא נִרְאָה סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what Abba, the father of Rabbi Simlai, said:** The *baraita* means as follows: **They calculate the *molad*;** if **the *molad* occurred before midday,** so that there are at least six more hours left of the day, **it is known that** the moon **will be visible close to sunset.** If, however, **the *molad* did not occur before midday,** so that there are fewer than six hours left of the day, **it is known that** the moon **will not be visible close to sunset.**

Returning to the question *Abba* posed to *Shmuel*: the significance of whether the *molad* falls before or after *chatzot* is now explained. The *Gemara* operates on the premise — stated explicitly though difficult to reconcile with precise astronomical observation — that once the moon has moved six hours out of conjunction, it will be visible near the time of sunset (*samuch l'shkiat hachamah*). On this assumption: if the *molad* occurs before midday, then six hours later it will be visible at or near sunset, meaning witnesses could conceivably see the new moon that same evening. If the *molad* occurs after midday, then six hours later the sun will have already set and the moon will be below the horizon; it cannot be visible that night.

The formula, therefore, is: *molad kodem chatzot — beyadua shenireh samuch l'shkiat hachamah*; *lo nolad kodem chatzot — beyadua shelo nireh samuch l'shkiat hachamah*. If the *molad* is before midday, it is known that the moon will be visible near sunset. If it is not before midday, it is known that it will not be visible near sunset.

### Contradict Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לְאַכְחוֹשֵׁי סָהֲדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the practical difference** that this statement makes? In any case, the court is dependent upon the testimony of witnesses. **Rav Ashi said:** This information is used **to refute the witnesses,** as if the witnesses claim that they saw the new moon at a time when it was not visible according to the calculations, they are clearly false witnesses.

*Rav Ashi* supplies the practical purpose of this astronomical knowledge: *l'achchushei sahadi* — to contradict witnesses. If witnesses claim to have seen the new moon at sunset on a given day, but the *molad* is known to have occurred after midday, their claim is astronomically impossible. The court can reject their testimony on that basis. Conversely, if the *molad* is known to have been before midday, the sighting is plausible. Astronomical calculation thus serves as a check on the integrity of witness testimony, not as a replacement for it.

### 24-Hour Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה שָׁעֵי מִכַּסֵּי סֵיהֲרָא. לְדִידַן — שֵׁית מֵעַתִּיקָא וְתַמְנֵי סְרֵי מֵחַדְתָּא, לְדִידְהוּ — שֵׁית מֵחַדְתָּא וְתַמְנֵי סְרֵי מֵעַתִּיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Naḥman said:** For **twenty-four hours the moon is covered,** i.e., not visible. This occurs between the last sighting of the old moon and the first sighting of the new moon. **For us,** in Babylonia, it is not visible for **six** hours **of the old** moon **and eighteen** hours **of the new; for them,** in Eretz Yisrael, it is not visible for **six** hours **of the new** moon **and eighteen** hours **of the old.**

*Rav Zeira* further transmits in the name of *Rav Nachman*: *kaf daled sha'ei machasei sihara* — the moon is concealed for twenty-four hours. This twenty-four-hour period is divided differently depending on location. *Ledidan* — for us in Eretz Yisrael — six hours of the old moon's invisibility and eighteen hours of the new moon's invisibility. *Ledidehu* — for them in Bavel — six hours of the new moon's invisibility and eighteen hours of the old moon's invisibility.

The practical application is again, as *Rav Ashi* states: *l'achchushei sahadi*, to contradict witnesses. If a witness in Eretz Yisrael claims to have seen the old moon within the last six hours before the new month, that is plausible; but if more than six hours have elapsed since the old moon was last visible, the claim is impossible. The figures differ for Bavel.

This passage is admittedly very difficult. Astronomically, the period during which the moon is invisible around conjunction is approximately thirty-six hours — nine degrees of arc in each direction at half a degree per hour — not twenty-four. Furthermore, the distinction between Eretz Yisrael and Bavel, which are approximately one time zone apart, would not seem to produce such a dramatic reversal in the invisibility periods for old versus new moon; they observe the same astronomical events with roughly an hour's difference.

### Geography Effect

One attempt at explanation runs as follows, on the *Gemara*'s own premise that six hours of separation from conjunction suffices for visibility. Consider the case where the *molad* occurs just after midday (*chatzot*). In this scenario, the new moon will become visible only six hours later, which by that point is after sunset — the moon is already below the horizon. The new moon is therefore invisible from the moment of conjunction until after the next sunrise: eighteen hours. The old moon, for its part, had been invisible for the final six hours before conjunction (as it was too close to the sun). So in this scenario: six hours of old moon invisibility, eighteen hours of new moon invisibility.

Now consider the opposite case: the *molad* occurs just before midday. The new moon becomes visible six hours later, right at sunset. The new moon is therefore invisible for only six hours. The old moon, last visible eighteen hours before conjunction, was invisible for eighteen hours. So in this scenario: eighteen hours of old moon invisibility, six hours of new moon invisibility.

The geographic explanation then proposes that the one-hour time difference between Bavel and Eretz Yisrael — Bavel being further east — shifts a *molad* that falls just before midday in Eretz Yisrael to just after midday in Bavel (or vice versa). This shift moves the scenario from one case to the other, reversing the distribution of six and eighteen hours between old and new moon. The two statements in the *Gemara* — *ledidan* six of the old and eighteen of the new, *ledidehu* six of the new and eighteen of the old — thus describe the same *molad* event as experienced from two different longitudes, where the one-hour offset is exactly sufficient to place the *molad* on opposite sides of midday.

The explanation is elegant, though it depends on the *Gemara*'s assumption that six hours from conjunction suffices for visibility — a figure that does not correspond to the actual astronomical requirement of approximately nine degrees of arc, representing closer to eighteen hours. Nonetheless, on the *Gemara*'s own terms, this geographic account coherently explains why the division of the twenty-four-hour concealment period is inverted between Eretz Yisrael and Bavel.
