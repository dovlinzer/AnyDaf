# Hullin 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kisuy HaDam & Humility

### Intro & Materials

We begin on the last line of 88b, with the intention of completing the discussion of *kisuy hadam* and then moving into the next prohibition relating to the eating of meat, namely *gid hanasheh*. Before turning to the *aggadeta*, it is worth noting a practical observation that arose in connection with the previous session's discussion of valid materials for *kisuy hadam*. The question had been what distinguishes valid materials from invalid ones — why, for instance, can one not use flour or metal filings? The suggestion offered was that invalid materials fall into one of two categories: those that are entirely non-absorbent, such as metal, and those that are so absorbent that they are transformed in the process, such as flour, which simply clumps together. Valid materials, by contrast, absorb without being fundamentally altered — dirt being the paradigmatic example. This framework, while not explicitly articulated as such in the *mishna*, does seem to align with the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the dominant view in the *gemara*.

### Abraham's Merit

The *gemara* now resumes with an *aggadic* passage, noting that while its original locus is in Masechet Sotah — where it arose naturally in context — it is quoted here because of its thematic connection to the *aggadeta* already introduced in our *sugya*. Rava says: in the merit of what Abraham our father said — *im michut ve'ad seroch naal*, "if from a thread to a shoe-strap" I will take nothing of yours (Genesis 14:23), his descendants merited two *mitzvot*: the blue thread of *tzitzit* (*chut shel techeilet*), which corresponds to the "thread," and the strap of *tefillin* (*retzuah shel tefillin*), which corresponds to the "shoe-strap."

The *gemara* continues by asking what kind of benefit is conferred by these *mitzvot*, since the broader context of the discussion has emphasized *mitzvot* from which a person derives some tangible benefit in this world.

### Tefillin Benefit

The *gemara* says: *bishelama retzuah shel tefillin* — the strap of *tefillin* is understandable. As the verse states, "And all the peoples of the earth shall see that the name of God is called upon you" (Deuteronomy 28:10), and we learned in a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer the Great said: this refers to the *tefillin shebarosh*, the head-*tefillin*, which bears the divine Name in the form of the letters *shin* and *dalet* on the *bayit* and the *kesher*. The end of that verse reads, *ve'yaru mimcha* — "and they shall fear you." The benefit, then, is that wearing *tefillin* causes the nations of the world to stand in awe of the one who bears them.

### Techeilet & Throne

The *gemara* then asks: but what is the benefit of the blue thread of *tzitzit*? Where is the practical benefit in this world? The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Meir: why was *techeilet* chosen over all other colors? Because *techeilet* resembles the sea, the sea resembles the sky, the sky resembles sapphire (*even sapir*), and sapphire resembles the *kisei hakavod*, the heavenly throne. The verse says, "And they saw the God of Israel, and under His feet was like the work of sapphire stone, and like the very heavens for purity" (Exodus 24:10) — showing that the heavens are like sapphire. And another verse says, "like the appearance of sapphire stone, the likeness of a throne" (Ezekiel 1:26) — establishing that sapphire is the color of God's throne.

Rashi understands the *gemara* as asking about practical worldly benefit, and answers that since the color of *techeilet* corresponds to God's throne, God, ever present upon His throne, continuously recalls the merit of *tzitzit* and that merit accrues to Israel. However, there is another and perhaps more natural reading: that the emphasis here is not on practical benefit but on the quality that makes these two *mitzvot* particularly meaningful — namely, their expression of an intimate connection between Israel and God. The name of God is upon us through *tefillin*; the color of God's throne is upon us through *techeilet*. This sense of direct relationship arguably fits the theme of Abraham more naturally, given that a central motif of Abraham's covenant is God's declaration, "I will be your God." Rashi's reading emphasizes practical benefit, but the *gemara* may simply be asking what makes these *mitzvot* stand out among all *mitzvot* — and the answer is that profound quality of closeness to the divine that they embody.

One student noted that the *midrash* also teaches that the *luchot* were made of sapphire — a point that could have reinforced the chain of associations but is not invoked here. Another observation raised is the degree of anthropomorphism in the passage — speaking of what stands beneath God's feet and the color of His throne. The *gemara* does not recoil from these images; they appear in the *Torah* itself and the *gemara* treats them straightforwardly. The point, though, seems ultimately to be about the spiritual reality of a connection to the divine, not a visual or physical description in any pagan sense.

### Stolen Property

Rabbi Abba says: *kasheh gezel hanachal* — stolen property that has already been consumed is particularly difficult. When one steals an object that remains intact, restitution is straightforward: return the object. But when the stolen item has already been used up or consumed, and the thief now feels genuine remorse and wishes to make restitution, the practical and psychological difficulty of doing so is enormous. The money may already be spent. The object no longer exists. Even the most righteous of people find themselves unable to make full restitution in such a case — *afillu tzadikim gemurim ein yekholin lehachazira*.

The proof-text is the verse in which Abraham, after refusing all the war booty, adds an exception: *biladai rak asher achlu hane'arim* — "only what the young men have eaten." The simple reading of the verse is that the fighters were entitled to their sustenance as wages of war. But the *gemara* reads it more sharply: Abraham was acknowledging that what his retinue had consumed was something he could not return, and so he was releasing the king of Sodom from any claim rather than being able to make whole. The language implies that this consumption had the quality of *gezel* — and even Abraham could not undo it.

### Humility Drasha

The *gemara* introduces the next passage with the statement of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yose HaGelili: whenever you find the words of Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Yose HaGelili in *Hagada*, make your ear like an *arparcheset* — a collecting bin into which grain is thrown in the mill — meaning, open yourself to receive everything he has to say. The *drasha* that follows is evidently the one being quoted in his name.

The verse reads: *lo merubchem mikol ha'amim chashak Hashem bachem* — "Not because you are greater than all the peoples did God desire you; rather, you are the fewest of all the peoples" (Deuteronomy 7:7). The *drasha* reads: God said to Israel, I desire you because *she'afilu besha'ah she'ani maspia lachem gedula*, even when I give you greatness, *atem mamitim atzmechem lefanai*, you make yourselves small before Me. The verse is re-read: God desires you not because you are *rav* — great — but because *ki atem hame'at* — you make yourselves *me'at*, small. Even when greatness is bestowed upon you, you do not treat yourselves as elevated. You remain humble.

The *gemara* enumerates the proof-cases. God gave greatness to Abraham, and Abraham said, *ve'anochi afar va'efer* — "I am dust and ashes." God gave greatness to Moses and Aaron, and they said, *venachnu mah* — "What are we? We are nothing." God gave greatness to David, and David said, *ve'anochi tola'at velo ish* — "I am a worm and not a man."

The idolaters are then contrasted. God gave greatness to Nimrod, and Nimrod said, "Come, let us build a tower to reach the heavens" — in *chazal*'s reading, it was Nimrod who led the *migdal bavel*. God gave greatness to Pharaoh, and Pharaoh said, *mi Hashem* — "Who is God that I should listen to Him?" God gave greatness to Sennacherib, and he said, "Who among all the gods of the lands has stood against me?" God gave greatness to Nebuchadnezzar, and he said, *e'eleh al bamotei av* — "I will ascend upon the high cloud." God gave greatness to Hiram king of Tyre, who had collaborated with Solomon in building the *beit hamikdash*, and he said, *moshav elohim yashavti belev yamim* — "I have sat in the dwelling of God, in the heart of the seas."

The pattern is striking: give the nations greatness and they believe themselves divine; give Israel greatness and their great ones speak of their own nothingness.

### Moshe & Aaron Merit

Rava — and some say Rabbi Yochanan — states: *gadol shene'emar beMoshe veAharon yoter mishene'emar beAvraham*. "Greater is what is said of Moses and Aaron than what is said of Abraham." The word "greater" here means more profound in its humility. Abraham called himself *afar va'efer* — dust and ashes — which at least is something. Moses and Aaron said *venachnu mah* — "What are we?" — nothing at all. Nothing is less than dust and ashes.

Rava — and some say Rabbi Yochanan — further states: *ein ha'olam mitkayem ela bishvil Moshe veAharon* — the world endures only on account of Moses and Aaron. The proof is a *gezeirah shavah*: the verse says *venachnu mah*, and the verse about creation says *toleh eretz al belimah* (Job 26:7) — God suspends the earth on *belimah*, which is read as *beli mah*, on "nothingness." Moses and Aaron said *mah* — we are nothing — and the world is suspended on that same *mah*, on their nothingness, on the merit of their humility.

A question arises: we invoke *zechut avot* on Rosh Hashana, but not typically *zechut Moshe veAharon*. The distinction is worth drawing carefully. *Zechut avot* is deeply intertwined with *brit avot* — the covenant God established with the Patriarchs, and God's repeated declaration *zacharti et briti*. It is a relational concept rooted in the foundational relationship between God and the Jewish people. The notion of *zechut tzadikim* — the merit of the righteous — is a different concept entirely, more akin to a spiritual endowment that future generations inherit and draw upon. Both are genuine concepts in the *gemara*, but they operate in different registers.

This passage also raises the question of the extreme nature of the humility being praised here. It is worth pausing over the fact that the *gemara* presents *venachnu mah* as surpassing *afar va'efer* in its humility — as if Abraham's expression was itself insufficient. The Rambam, deeply influenced by Aristotelian ethics in most areas of character development, makes an explicit exception when it comes to humility. Whereas Aristotle counseled a golden mean between pride and self-abasement, the Rambam in his *Hilchos Deos* and *Peirush HaMishnayot* explicitly calls for going to the extreme: *me'od me'od hevei shefal ruach*. This is not a Western value, and the *gemara* here reflects precisely that non-Western orientation. At the same time, this kind of humility does not mean denying one's accomplishments or pretending they did not occur. *Moshe anav mikol adam* — Moses was the most humble of all men — yet Moses did not deny what he had done. The humility the *gemara* speaks of is fundamentally about one's orientation toward God: not attributing greatness to oneself, not seeing oneself as the center of attention, acknowledging that whatever one has accomplished flows from gifts not of one's own making.

### Silencing Self

Rabbi Ila'a says: *ein ha'olam mitkayem ela bishvil mi shebolem et atzmo bish'at merivah* — the world endures only on account of one who stops his mouth at a time of quarrel, who does not give back an angry retort when someone says something hurtful. The proof-text is again *toleh eretz al belimah* — the world is suspended on *belimah*, on the one who is *bolem*, who seals his mouth.

Rabbi Abbahu says: *mi shemesim atzmo k'mi she'eino* — the world endures on account of one who makes himself as if he does not exist. The verse cited is *umitachat zero'ot olam* (Deuteronomy 33:27) — those who place themselves *mitachat*, underneath, who allow others to walk past them, they are the *zero'ot olam*, the everlasting support of the world.

### Valley Drasha

Rabbi Yitzchak asks: what is the meaning of the verse *ha'umnam elem tzedek tedaberun, mesharim tishpetu bnei adam* — "Is it indeed in silence that you speak righteousness; with justice do you judge the children of men?" (Psalms 58:2)? The *drasha* reads *ha'umnam* not as "in truth" but as a related form of *umanut* — craft, vocation. *Ma umanuto shel adam ba'olam hazeh?* What should be the primary endeavor of a person in this world? *Yasim atzmo ke'ilem* — make yourself like a mute person. Swallow the retort, don't engage in every quarrel.

One might ask: should one be silent even regarding *divrei Torah* — matters of Torah? The *gemara* answers: the verse says *tzedek tedaberun* — speak righteousness. Torah is called *tzedek*, and therefore one must speak up for Torah. And should one feel a sense of pride or self-satisfaction if he successfully defended the honor of Torah in an argument? Here the verse adds *mesharim tishpetu bnei adam* — and Rashi explains *mesharim* through the word *mishor*, a plain or valley: make yourself like a valley, low and level. Even when you must speak up for Torah, do not emerge from that exchange with a sense of superiority or self-righteousness.

## Ir Hanidachat & Kissing

### Ir Hanidachat Dust

Rabbi Zeira — and some say Rabba bar Yirmia — states: *mechasin be'afar Ir HaNidachat* — one may cover the blood using the dust of an *ir hanidachat*, a city condemned for communal idolatry, whose entire contents are to be burned. The question that immediately presents itself is that objects of an *ir hanidachat* are *isurei hana'ah* — forbidden for use or benefit — and we have been assuming throughout this *sugya* that one may not use objects that are *asurim behana'ah* even for the purpose of performing a *mitzvah*.

### Dirt vs. Ashes

*Ziri* clarifies: when we speak of *afar ir hanidachat*, we do not mean the ashes of things that were burned inside the city. Earlier in the *gemara* the words *afar* and *efer* were shown to be used interchangeably in certain contexts, so one might have assumed this refers to the burned residue. No — we are speaking of the ordinary dirt of the condemned city, not of its ashes.

### Disconnected Items

Why is the dirt of the city not itself forbidden in benefit? The verse says, *ve'et kol shelalah tikbotz el toch rechovah vesarafta* — "gather all of its spoil into the public square and burn it" (Deuteronomy 13:17). The principle derived is: only that which requires merely gathering and burning is included in the prohibition. The dirt of the city, however, would require uprooting (*telisha*), then gathering, then burning — it is not something that one simply gathers and burns. Moreover, there is a general principle that objects still connected to the ground cannot become *asurim*: if one worshipped the ground as *avodah zarah*, the ground itself does not become forbidden. The verse does say *vehayetah tel olam lo tibaneh* — the city shall never be rebuilt — but that does not extend to an outright prohibition on deriving all benefit from the soil. Accordingly, the dirt of an *ir hanidachat* is not *asur behana'ah*, and one may use it for *kisuy hadam*.

### Mitzvot Lav Lehanot

Rava, however, takes a different position. He holds that we are speaking of the ashes of objects that were burned, and yet the covering is valid. His reasoning: *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* — *mitzvot* were not given for the purpose of deriving benefit. This principle is not primarily a metaphysical statement about the nature of *mitzvot*; it is, more precisely, a statement about the nature of *isurei hana'ah*. When the Torah prohibits benefit from an object, it prohibits a certain kind of real-world, physical or monetary benefit — not religious or spiritual benefit. Fulfilling a *mitzvah* is not the kind of benefit that the prohibition of *hana'ah* is meant to prevent.

### Benefit Definition

To illustrate: there is a question whether one may study from a cadaver in order to acquire medical knowledge — is that "deriving benefit" from a *met*? The general answer is no, because the prohibition of *hana'ah* addresses physical or monetary benefit, not intellectual benefit. Similarly, the credit one receives for performing a *mitzvah* — the spiritual fulfillment — is not the kind of benefit that *isurei hana'ah* target. One can therefore, according to Rava, even use ashes from an *ir hanidachat* to cover blood.

It is worth noting a distinction that arises at the margins: smell is generally considered a form of physical pleasure and is treated as a form of *hana'ah* on some level. But aesthetic or auditory pleasure is not biblically prohibited. When we are speaking of objects with *issur hana'ah*, the operative definition of forbidden benefit is firmly in the domain of the physical and the monetary.

## Shofar & Lulav

### Shofar/Lulav Prohibition

Ravina was sitting and repeating this teaching of Rava. Rav Rechumi raised an objection. A *baraita* teaches: *shofar shel avodah zarah lo yitka bo* — do not blow a *shofar* of *avodah zarah*. Similarly, *lulav shel avodah zarah lo yitol* — do not take a *lulav* of *avodah zarah*. Both prohibitions exist as initial instructions.

### Non-fulfillment

The *gemara* initially understands the language of "do not" (*lo yitka*, *lo yitol*) to imply that if one did perform these actions, one would not have fulfilled the *mitzvah* — and this seems to be a direct contradiction of Rava's principle. If *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu*, then using these *isurei hana'ah* objects for a *mitzvah* should be permitted, and one should fulfill the obligation. The *gemara* notes that there is also a *baraita* that says explicitly: *taka lo yatza, natal lo yatza* — if one did blow, one did not fulfill the obligation; if one did take the *lulav*, one did not fulfill the obligation.

One might ask: if this is a contradiction to Rava's position, why doesn't the *gemara* simply say that one does fulfill the *mitzvah* and simultaneously transgresses the prohibition of *hana'ah*? The answer is that when the law presents a choice — either you fulfilled the *mitzvah* and transgressed, or you didn't fulfill the *mitzvah* and didn't transgress — we prefer the latter. It is better not to fulfill the *mitzvah* than to both sort of fulfill it and transgress. Hence the presumption in the *baraita* is non-fulfillment.

### Shiur Distinction

Rav Ashi resolves the apparent contradiction. *Hachi hashta* — how can you compare these cases? *Hasam shiura ba'ina* — there, by *shofar* and *lulav*, a *shiur* is required. A *shofar* must be a certain minimum size; a *lulav* must be a certain minimum length. An object dedicated to *avodah zarah* is subject to the rule *kol ha'omed lisreifah kesaruf dami* — everything that is destined to be burned is considered already burned. Or, framing it differently without relying on that principle: part of what a *shiur* demands is not merely a quantitative threshold, but that the object carry some significance. An object that is supposed to be destroyed — that is slated for the flames — has no significance whatsoever. It therefore cannot serve as a proper *mitzvah* object, not because of the *issur hana'ah*, but because its destined destruction undermines its halachic significance as an object.

### Blood Covering Logic

Here, by *kisuy hadam*, the situation is entirely different: *kol mah demichatat malei lechisui* — the more it is burned or ground up, the better it serves for covering the blood. The material used to cover blood has no requirement of *shiur* and no requirement of significance. Dirt, ashes, and excrement are all valid materials. The object used for covering has no independent halachic status as a *mitzvah* object in the way a *shofar* or *lulav* does. Therefore, the fact that it is slated for burning is no disqualification.

Rashi and Tosafot add a further dimension. Even in the case of *shofar* and *lulav*, the *gemara* indicated that one should not use them even *lechatchila* — initially — and even where the initial prohibition was resolved, there might still remain a concern of *bizuyot alav*, of treating the *mitzvah* in a disparaging manner, if one were to use an object that had been an object of *avodah zarah*. However, for *kisuy hadam*, no such concern arises. The material used to cover blood does not function as a *mitzvah* object in any dignified or formal sense. Dirt is dirt; the covering material does not require *kavod* as a *tashmish mitzvah*. There is therefore no reason to say that using dirt that was once the ground of an idolatrous site is a *bizayon* to the *mitzvah* — and if it was the actual ground itself that was worshipped, there would still be no problem using that soil for *kisuy hadam*.

### Church Conversion

This analysis has direct practical resonance today: there are many occasions when churches are converted into synagogues. The question of what may be used and what is prohibited within such a building — whether the *aron kodesh*, furnishings, the structure itself — is extensively discussed in *halacha*. God willing, when we reach the third *perek* of Megillah, where this topic arises, we will have the opportunity to explore it in depth.

## Gid HaNasheh Intro

### Gid HaNasheh Scope

We now turn to the new *perek* and the prohibition of *gid hanasheh*. The *mishna* opens by establishing the broad applicability of the prohibition: *noheig ba'aretz uvechutz la'aretz* — it applies both in the Land of Israel and outside it; *bifnei habayit veshelo bifnei habayit* — whether the Temple stands or not; and *bechullin uvemukdashim* — whether the animal is non-sanctified or a sanctified offering.

It is worth pausing to note the dramatic preamble that the sequence of this chapter represents. The *sugya* has been progressing through the entire process of preparing meat for consumption: first, the *shechita* had to be performed correctly; then one had to check for *tereifot*; then one had to determine whether there was a fetus in the womb; then, following *shechita*, one had to cover the blood if the animal was a *chayah* or a bird, not a *behemah*; and earlier, the prohibition of *shechting oto ve'et bno* on the same day had to be heeded. Now, having gotten through all of that, there remains one more prohibition before the meat may be eaten: the *gid hanasheh* must be removed. And following *gid hanasheh*, the *perek* after this will bring us to *basar bechalav*. It is curious, incidentally, that there is no *perek* directly dedicated to the removal of blood or the removal of *chelev* — perhaps because *chelev* was considered sufficiently obvious to identify and the blood-removal process sufficiently well known, but their absence from explicit *mishnayot* is notable.

### Anatomy & Location

The *mishna* continues: *beyerech hayamin uveyerech hasmol* — the prohibition applies to both the right and the left thigh. This will be the subject of debate. *Ve'eino noheig be'of* — it does not apply to birds. The reason is *mipnei she'ein lo kaf* — a bird does not have the *kaf hayarech*, the rounded socket of the hip joint that is referenced in the verse *asher kaf hayarech*. While birds do have the corresponding sinew, the anatomical structure that defines the prohibition — the rounded thigh-socket — is absent in birds.

### Fetus Dispute

The *mishna* states: *venoheig bashalil* — it applies to the fetus; *Rabbi Yehudah omer eino noheig bashalil* — Rabbi Yehudah says it does not. This dispute apparently concerns a nine-month fetus, where the *tanna kamma* — presumably Rabbi Meir — holds that a nine-month fetus has its own independent halachic status, and therefore its *gid hanasheh* is the *gid hanasheh* of a separate animal. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees, holding that even a nine-month fetus remains legally part of the mother and is not yet an independent animal, and therefore its sinew carries no separate prohibition.

### Butcher Trust

The *mishna* then states: *ve'ein hatavachim ne'emanim al gid hanasheh* — butchers cannot be trusted when they say they removed the *gid hanasheh* — in the words of Rabbi Meir. This is a striking ruling, because normally the principle of *ed echad ne'eman be'isurin* applies: one reliable person is trusted regarding matters of *kashrus*, even if they have a financial interest in the outcome. The reason butchers cannot be trusted here is that removing the *gid hanasheh* is exceptionally difficult and time-consuming. The labor involved in properly tracing and removing the sinew and all its tributaries is so demanding that the concern is not simply dishonesty but the practical reality that even a well-intentioned butcher may not have done it fully. The financial temptation compounds the problem. It should be noted that even where one cannot simply take the butcher's word, the alternative is not necessarily constant supervision — it may simply mean inspection after the fact. The anatomical complexity of the *gid* makes visual inspection challenging, particularly once the meat has been cut into pieces, but the principle is that one needs more than the butcher's assurance.

### Supervision Dispute

The *sages* disagree with Rabbi Meir: *vachachamim omrim ne'emanim alav ve'al hachelev* — butchers are trusted regarding the *gid hanasheh* and also regarding the removal of *chelev*, the forbidden fat. This ruling implicitly reminds us that the prohibition of *chelev* also requires active removal, even if no *perek* of this tractate is formally dedicated to it.

### Anatomy Display

The anatomy of the *gid hanasheh* is worth pausing over. The sinew is extremely long, running down the length of the thigh. What makes its removal so laborious is not just its length but its numerous tributaries — branching extensions that penetrate deep into the surrounding tissue — as well as a secondary branch that we will discuss as the *gemara* proceeds. One can readily understand why the *gemara* frames the removal as something that even committed and well-meaning butchers may fail to complete properly.

## Gid & Sanctified

### Sacrifices Question

The *mishna*'s inclusion of *mukdashim* — sanctified offerings — in the scope of *gid hanasheh* seems, at first glance, unremarkable. Tosafot observes, however, that there ought to be a *chiddush* — a novel teaching — in each clause of the *mishna*. What new information does the inclusion of *mukdashim* convey? The fact that *gid hanasheh* applies to an animal does not disappear simply because you sanctified that animal. The prohibition was already there. Why would sanctification change anything?

### Taste Question

The answer hinges on a question that will recur throughout this entire *perek*: does the *gid hanasheh* impart taste (*yesh begidim benoten taam*), or does it not (*ein begidim benoten taam*)? The question has significant practical and theoretical implications. On the practical side: if one cooked the thigh together with the *gid hanasheh*, may one remove the *gid* afterwards and eat the surrounding meat? This depends on whether the *gid* gave off sufficient taste to render the meat forbidden. On the theoretical side: if an animal is a *korban*, its flesh is forbidden to ordinary consumption. If the *gid* has taste, then eating the *gid* of a *korban* is not only a violation of *gid hanasheh* but also a violation of the prohibition against eating from a *korban*. If the *gid* has no taste, eating it provides no actual benefit from the *korban*, in which case — like eating the horn or hoof of a *korban* — the *korban* prohibition may not be violated.

### Interpretation

One possible reading of the *mishna* is that the *tanna* holds *yesh begidim benoten taam* — the *gid* does impart taste — and the *chiddush* of *mukdashim* is therefore: since the *gid* of a *korban* has taste, eating it means benefiting from the *korban*, and the *mishna* is telling us that the prohibition of *korbanot* now applies to the *gid* as well. If that is the *chiddush*, however, the *mishna* phrased it incorrectly. It should have said: the prohibition of *mukdashim* applies even to the *gid* — not that *gid hanasheh* applies to *mukdashim*. The *mishna*'s formulation (*noheg bemukdashim*) places the emphasis on the *gid hanasheh* prohibition, not on the *mukdashim* prohibition. This suggests the *tanna* holds the opposite: *ein begidim benoten taam* — the *gid* has no taste — and therefore eating the *gid* of a *korban* does not constitute deriving benefit from the *korban*. The *chiddush* is thus: even in *mukdashim*, where the entire animal is sanctified, the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* alone applies to the *gid*, and the *mukdashim* prohibition does not.

### Taste Evidence

But can the *tanna* really hold *ein begidim benoten taam*? The *gemara* notes that the very next *mishna* in this tractate — apparently by the same *tanna* — states: *yerech shenitbashal bah gid hanasheh, im yesh bah benoten taam harei zo asurah* — if you cooked a thigh together with the *gid hanasheh* and the *gid* imparted its taste, the thigh is forbidden. This *mishna* clearly presupposes that the *gid* can and does impart taste. So the *tanna* cannot hold *ein begidim benoten taam* as a categorical principle. The *gemara* must therefore find an alternative way to explain the *chiddush* of the *mukdashim* clause.

## Fetus in Sanctified

### Fetus Context

The *gemara* proposes a new reading: *ela hacha bivladot kodashim askinan* — we are dealing here with the offspring (*vlad*) of a sanctified animal. The scenario is as follows: a female animal is sanctified as a *korban* while already pregnant, or becomes pregnant after being sanctified. The fetus grows within a sanctified mother. According to the position that *vladot kodashim bimei iman hen kedoshim* — the offspring of sanctified animals are sanctified from the moment they begin to develop in the womb — the fetus acquires *kedushat korban* from the very earliest stage of its formation.

Now, if *gid hanasheh* applies to a fetus (following the *tanna kamma* against Rabbi Yehudah), and if both prohibitions — *gid hanasheh* and *mukdashim* — arise simultaneously at the moment the fetus begins to form, then the *chiddush* is clear: one might have thought that when two prohibitions arrive at exactly the same moment, one of them will be displaced. The *mishna* teaches that both apply — the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* takes effect along with the *mukdashim* prohibition.

James raises a sharper observation: the even more powerful *chiddush* would be if the *mukdashim* prohibition arrives *first*, and only later does the *gid hanasheh* prohibition take effect — because in that case, one might invoke the principle of *ein issur chal al issur* (a later prohibition cannot take effect upon an already-existing prohibition) and argue that the *gid* prohibition fails to apply. The *gemara* will indeed arrive at precisely this scenario.

### Fetus Clarification

The *gemara* raises a difficulty with the fetus reading. The *mishna* itself, in its later clause, introduces the application of *gid hanasheh* to a *shalil* as a matter of dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehudah. The fact that it is mentioned separately at the end of the *mishna* implies that the earlier clause about *mukdashim* was not already talking about a *shalil*. The *gemara* resolves this: we should understand the *mishna* as saying that while the preceding analysis does indeed concern a fetus of a *korban*, this is itself part of the very dispute — and the reader should be aware that Rabbi Yehudah disagrees with the underlying premise that *gid hanasheh* applies to a fetus at all.

### Corpse Evidence

The *gemara* then demonstrates from a *mishna* elsewhere that the scenario where *mukdashim* precedes *gid hanasheh* is not merely hypothetical — it is anatomically real. The *mishna* in the context of a *nazir* teaches that a *nazir* becomes obligated to shave and recommence his *nezirut* upon contact with a corpse. The list in that *mishna* includes both *al hames* (the whole corpse) and *al kezayit min hames* (a *kezayit* from a corpse). The question was asked there: if a *kezayit* suffices to generate *tum'ah*, then surely the whole corpse — which is many times larger — should also. Why state both? Rabbi Yochanan answers: the case of the whole corpse is necessary because there can exist a corpse that is smaller than a *kezayit*. How? A very early miscarriage — *lo nikre'u eivarav begidim*, the limbs have not yet been formed with sinews. At that stage, the entire fetus may be smaller than a *kezayit*.

What is critical for our discussion is the embedded teaching: at the very early stages of fetal development, the *gidim* — the sinews — do not yet exist. This means that when a fetus begins to develop within a sanctified mother, *kedushat korban* attaches to it from the outset, but the *gid hanasheh* prohibition cannot yet apply because the sinew does not yet physically exist. The order is therefore precisely what James anticipated: first the *korban* prohibition, then, later, the *gid* prohibition.

### Gid Timing Question

This creates a conceptual difficulty: if the *gid hanasheh* prohibition is supposed to apply to this fetus, but at the moment the fetus is sanctified the *gid* does not yet exist — how and when does the *gid* prohibition take effect? And once the *korban* prohibition is already in place, can the *gid* prohibition come and take effect upon it, given the principle of *ein issur chal al issur*?

## Ein Issur Chal

### Principle Stated

The principle of *ein issur chal al issur* states that a second prohibition cannot take effect upon an object already subject to a prior prohibition. If an object is already forbidden, a subsequent *issur* cannot "land" on it — it has nowhere to add, since the object is already prohibited.

### Application to Gid

The *gemara* arrives directly at this question in the scenario under discussion. The fetus is first sanctified as a *korban*. Then — anatomically and developmentally — the *gid hanasheh* comes into existence. At the moment the *gid* forms, can the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* take effect? Or does *ein issur chal al issur* prevent it, since the fetus is already *asur* as a *korban*?

### Pre-Sinai Weight

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *af al gav de'issur mukdashim kodem, asi issur gid chal alaihu* — even though the *korban* prohibition comes first, the *gid hanasheh* prohibition still takes effect. The reason: *sheken issuro noheg bivnei Noach* — the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* carries a special weight because it applied to *bnei Noach*. More precisely: it is not that *gid hanasheh* currently applies to non-Jews, but that it was in force *before Matan Torah*, prohibited to our ancestors when they were still in the category of *bnei Noach*. This pre-Sinaitic status gives it a unique weight — a kind of categorical primacy — that allows it to take effect even when a subsequent prohibition has already landed on the object.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot raises a sharp question: the whole analysis assumes that the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* applies to the fetus while it is still developing inside the sanctified mother. But we know from the earlier discussion of the *mishna* that there is a broad consensus — including both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah — that if the mother is *shechted*, the fetus is treated as a limb of the mother and the *gid* of the fetus carries no independent prohibition. So how can we say that the *gid* of the fetus is prohibited by *gid hanasheh* at all, given that as long as the fetus is in the mother's womb and the mother is eventually *shechted*, the fetus-*gid* will be permitted?

### Retroactive Application

Tosafot answers that the determination of whether the fetus is treated as an independent entity or as a limb of the mother depends on what ultimately happens to the mother. If the mother is *shechted* while the fetus is inside her, the *shechita* of the mother extends to the fetus, which is then treated retroactively as a limb of the mother throughout its development — and the *gid* prohibition never applied. But if the mother was never *shechted* — whether because the fetus was born alive after nine months, or because it was miscarried, or because it was removed — then since the mother's *shechita* never encompassed the fetus, the fetus is regarded retroactively as having been an independent entity throughout. In that case, looking backwards, the *gid* prohibition applied from the moment the sinew came into existence within the womb. The independence or dependence of the fetus is not determined at any single frozen moment during gestation, but is resolved by what ultimately happens at the time of the mother's *shechita* — or its absence.
