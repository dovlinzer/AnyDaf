# Bava Batra 176 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Liens: דאורייתא vs. דרבנן

### Ulla: Liens from Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – אֶחָד מִלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר, וְאֶחָד מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? שִׁעְבּוּדָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. וְאֶלָּא מַה טַּעַם אָמְרוּ מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶלָּא מִנְּכָסִין בְּנֵי חוֹרִין? מִשּׁוּם פְּסֵידָא דְלָקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA: Ulla says: By Torah law,** a creditor, **whether** in the case of **a loan with** a promissory **note or** the case of **a loan by oral** contract, can **collect** the debt **from liened property** that has been sold by the debtor subsequent to his receiving the loan. **What is the reason** for this? The property of a debtor at the time of the loan, even an oral loan, is **liened by Torah law. And what is the reason** the Sages **said** that one who gives **a loan by oral** contract can **collect** the debt **only from unsold** property? **Because of the loss** that would be incurred **by purchasers** of land if the seller's creditors could seize the land they have purchased.

We open the final *daf* of Bava Batra with the *gemara* that follows the *mishna* on 175b. The *mishna* established two foundational principles that have been guiding our discussion: that a *shtar chov* — a written loan document — creates liens on the borrower's property, while an oral loan backed only by witnesses but no written document does not. The *mishna* also addressed the *areiv*, the guarantor, who signs at the bottom of the *shtar* after the witnesses have already signed: according to Rabbi Yishmael, such a guarantor is obligated, but his obligation carries no liens against his property; according to Ben Nanas, he is not even obligated at all. The *gemara* now explores the theoretical underpinning of these distinctions through a dispute between Ulla and Rabba.

*Amar Ulla*: Ulla holds that, *dvar Torah* — as a matter of Torah law — whether a loan was made with a written document or whether it was made purely orally, the lender would be entitled to collect from encumbered property. Liens exist as a matter of biblical reality. The act of lending money, in and of itself, creates the legal phenomenon of *shibud* — an encumbrance on the borrower's assets. No additional instrument is required to bring that encumbrance into existence.

### Rabba: Liens Rabbinic On…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּה אָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – אֶחָד מִלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר וְאֶחָד מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶלָּא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? שִׁעְבּוּדָא לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. וּמַה טַּעַם אָמְרוּ מִלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּנְעוֹל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לֹוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says: By Torah law,** a creditor, **whether** in the case of **a loan with** a promissory **note or** the case of **a loan by oral** contract, can **collect** the debt **only from** the debtor's **unsold property. What is the reason** for this? The property of a debtor is **not liened by Torah law. And what is the reason** the Sages **said** that one who gives **a loan with** a promissory **note can collect** the debt **from liened property** that has been sold? **So as not to lock the door in the face of** potential **borrowers.** If one could not collect a debt by taking liened property that has been sold, people would be hesitant to put their money at risk by lending it.

Rabba disagrees entirely. According to Rabba, the Torah recognizes no category of liens at all. Whether the loan was documented in a *shtar* or was entirely oral, the Torah's default position is that a lender may collect only from *bnei chorin* — unencumbered property in the borrower's direct possession. It is worth noting, as Tosafot points out, that Rabba may be making an even more radical claim: that even if the document explicitly stipulated that the borrower's property should be encumbered, the Torah would recognize no such legal reality. The concept of a lien — the notion that a creditor holds a right in property even after it has been transferred to a third party — may simply not exist as a biblical category.

### Rationale & Policy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה נָמֵי! הָתָם לֵית לֵיהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that there is a desire to encourage people to lend money by granting greater power of collection to creditors, **a loan by oral** contract should **also** be collectible from liened property that has been sold. The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of a loan by oral contract, **it has no publicity** associated with it, so that purchasers often cannot find out about it, even after a thorough investigation, and it would be an unfair burden on them to have the property purchased by them seized in such cases.

The two positions lead to opposite accounts of rabbinic intervention, and this is where the policy logic becomes illuminating. According to Ulla, since liens exist by Torah law, the rabbis had to step in and *remove* them in certain cases. Specifically, they removed the lender's right to collect from encumbered property when the loan was made without a written document. Why? *Mishum pseda de-lekuchos* — to protect the purchasers. A buyer who purchases land from the borrower has no way of knowing about an undocumented oral loan and cannot reasonably be expected to have investigated it. The rabbis therefore protected such buyers by effectively erasing the Torah-based lien in cases where no written record existed. When a *shtar* was written, however, the buyers bear responsibility for their own loss: *hasam inhu de'afsidu anafshayhu* — they brought the loss upon themselves, since the public document gave them the means to find out.

According to Rabba, the logic runs in the opposite direction. Since there are no Torah-based liens at all, the rabbis had to *create* them. Their motivation: *kedei shelo tin'ol deles bifnei lovin* — so as not to lock the door in front of borrowers, or more precisely, in front of lenders. The Torah already provides many disincentives for lending money; the rabbis needed to encourage the practice by ensuring that lenders had a reliable mechanism for recovering their funds. They therefore instituted the rabbinic creation of liens — but only when the lender takes the step of writing a *shtar*, because a *shtar* that is publicized gives the lender a way to protect himself without unfairly harming innocent purchasers. The *gemara* pushes back: why not extend rabbinic liens even to oral loans, in order to maximize the encouragement of lending? The answer: *hasam leis lei kala* — an oral loan has no public voice; there is no way for purchasers to discover it, and so the *pseda de-lekuchos* concern would immediately reassert itself. Moreover, once the rabbis have given lenders the option of writing a *shtar* and thereby securing full protection, they need not provide additional protection for lenders who decline to use that instrument.

It is worth pausing here to observe what is at stake conceptually. Both positions ultimately produce the same practical legal framework: a written loan carries liens, an oral loan does not. Yet the *gemara* clearly believes the theoretical difference is not merely academic. The question of whether *shibuda de-oraita* or *lav de-oraita* carries real legal consequences — and this is illustrated through a challenge from the laws of inheritance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבָּה הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבָּה: גָּבוּ קַרְקַע – יֵשׁ לוֹ. גָּבוּ מָעוֹת – אֵין לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabba** really **say this? But doesn't Rabba say,** in the case of a firstborn son, that if he and his brothers **collected** a debt from **land, he has** the right to receive a double portion of that payment, but if **they collected** a debt from **money, he does not have** the right to a double portion? A firstborn son is entitled to a double share of inheritance of any property that was owned by his father at the time of his death, but not to profits or income that accrue to the estate after his death. If a debt owed to the father is collected from land after his death, Rabba said that the firstborn is entitled to a double portion, indicating that the land was considered in the father's possession even before he died, which indicates that Rabba holds that the property of a debtor is liened to a creditor by Torah law.

The *gemara* raises Rabba's own ruling elsewhere: *gavu karka yesh lo, gavu ma'ot ein lo* — if the heirs collect the outstanding debt in the form of land, a *bechor* (firstborn son) takes a double portion of that land; if they collect in cash, he does not. The principle governing a *bechor*'s double portion is that he collects *pi shnayim* only from property already in the estate at the father's death, not from property that subsequently flows into it — *ein ha-bechor notel pi shnayim b'ra'uy ke-va-muchzak*. The only way to explain why land collected after death is treated as if it was already part of the estate is to say that the estate always had a lien on it — its claws were already in that land — and therefore collection of it is not new property flowing in but the realization of an already-existing right. This logic presupposes *shibuda de-oraita*. But Rabba holds that liens are purely rabbinic! How could he issue such a ruling?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא אֵיפוֹךְ דְּרַבָּה לְעוּלָּא וּדְעוּלָּא לְרַבָּה, וְהָא אָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, בַּעַל חוֹב דִּינֵיהּ בְּזִבּוּרִית!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say:** The opinions as they were cited earlier are attributed incorrectly, and it is necessary to **reverse** the two opinions, and exchange the opinion of **Rabba for** that of **Ulla, and** that **of Ulla for** that **of Rabba,** there is a difficulty: **But doesn't Ulla say** elsewhere: **By Torah law** the ***halakha* of a creditor is** to collect his debt only **from inferior-quality land?**

The *gemara* considers reversing the positions — assigning Rabba's *bechor* ruling to Ulla and vice versa — but is blocked by independent evidence for Ulla's position. *V'ha'amar Ulla, dvar Torah, ba'al chov dino b'ziborit* — Ulla said explicitly that a creditor collects from grade-C property by Torah law. The Rashbam sees this as proof that Ulla holds liens exist biblically, since the creditor is going to property. Tosafot, however, is not fully satisfied with this proof, noting that the rule about *ziborit* may concern direct collection from the borrower himself — which does not necessarily implicate the concept of a lien, i.e., the right to collect from a third party who purchased the land. For the Rashbam, any right to the borrower's property reflects the lien concept; for Tosafot, the lien specifically refers to the right against third-party purchasers, which is a distinct and more demanding category.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבָּה – טַעְמָא דִּבְנֵי מַעְרְבָא קָאָמַר, וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the contradiction between Rabba's two statements: **Rather, Rabba was stating an explanation for** the opinion of the **people of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, cited earlier (125a), **but he himself does not hold accordingly,** as he holds that a firstborn son is not entitled to a double portion of a debt collected from land.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction in Rabba by explaining that when Rabba issued his *bechor* ruling, he was not expressing his own view. *Rabba ta'ama de-vnei Ma'arava ka'amar v'lav sevira lei* — Rabba was there articulating the position of the sages of the West, *bnei Ma'arava*, not his own opinion. Rabba himself would not apply the *bechor* double-portion rule to land collected after the father's death, because for Rabba there was no pre-existing lien that would make that land "already" part of the estate.

### Liens as Ownership

This brings us to a conceptual framework that, while not stated explicitly in the *gemara* itself, helps illuminate what is at stake in the debate. Several *rishonim* and *acharonim* suggest two distinct ways of conceptualizing what a lien actually is.

The first model treats a lien as nothing more than an abstract legal right: because Michael owes me money, I have a right that allows me, under certain conditions, to go collect from Chanan who purchased Michael's land. But this model can seem arbitrary. Why should the fact that Michael owes me money create any rights against Chanan's property?

The second model is more intuitive: a lien represents a form of partial ownership in the land itself. From the moment of the loan, I held a certain stake in Michael's property. When Chanan bought that land, he bought it already encumbered by my pre-existing rights. This model makes the lien far more comprehensible — and it also points toward what a *shtar* might actually be doing. A *shtar* is, in other contexts, an instrument for transferring property rights. Perhaps the *shtar chov* functions similarly: it transfers a partial right in Michael's property to me, the lender, creating genuine partial ownership rather than a mere abstract entitlement. This would explain why, under this framework, *shibud de-oraita* has real legal consequences for the *bechor*: if the estate always held partial ownership in the land through the biblical lien, then collection of that land is not new property flowing in — it is the estate reclaiming what was always, in a sense, already its own.

## Heirs vs. Purchasers

### Rav & Shmuel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין וְלֹא מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? שִׁעְבּוּדָא לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel both say:** In the case of **a loan by oral** contract, the creditor **can collect** the debt only from the one who took the loan, but **not from the heirs** of the debtor after his death, **and not from purchasers** of his property. **What is the reason** for this? They maintain that the property of a debtor is **not liened by Torah law.**

The *gemara* now introduces a related dispute with a third axis: the distinction between collecting from heirs and collecting from purchasers. This brings a new practical implication of the *shibud* debate into focus.

Rav and Shmuel both rule: *malveh al peh eino goveh lo min ha-yorshim ve-lo min ha-lekuchos* — an oral loan, with no written document, cannot be collected either from the borrower's heirs or from those who purchased the borrower's property. Their reasoning: *mai ta'ama, shibuda lav de-oraita* — since liens are not biblical, they exist only where the rabbis created them, and the rabbis created them only in the context of a written document. A lender who chose not to write a *shtar* forfeited the rabbinic protection entirely, including any claim against the heirs.

The logic here deserves reflection. One might have expected that even without a *shtar*, a lender could still collect from the heirs — after all, the concern that motivated the rabbis to remove liens for oral loans was the protection of innocent purchasers who paid money for the property not knowing of the undocumented debt. Heirs, by contrast, paid nothing; they simply received an inheritance. If the goal is to protect paying buyers, why should that protection extend to heirs who are not buying anything? This is precisely the argument one could construct from the *de-oraita* position: if liens exist biblically, there is no reason to protect heirs, and a lender should be able to collect from them even without a document. But Rav and Shmuel, who hold *shibuda lav de-oraita*, reason that since liens exist only where the rabbis explicitly created them — and the rabbis created them only when a *shtar* was written — without a *shtar* there is simply no lien to enforce against anyone, heirs included.

### R. Yochanan & Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – גּוֹבֶה בֵּין מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, וּבֵין מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? שִׁעְבּוּדָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish both say:** In the case of **a loan by oral** contract, the creditor **can collect** the debt **both from the heirs** of the debtor after his death **and from the purchasers** of his property. **What is the reason** for this? They maintain that the property of a debtor is **liened by Torah law.**

Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish take the diametrically opposite position: *malveh al peh goveh bein min ha-yorshim u-vein min ha-lekuchos* — an oral loan allows collection from both heirs and purchasers. Their reasoning is *shibuda de-oraita*: liens are biblical, and the rabbis did nothing to interfere with them. This position, as the *gemara* notes, flatly contradicts everything the *mishna* has established. If you can collect from purchasers even without a written document, the entire edifice of the *mishna*'s ruling that oral loans have no liens collapses.

### Well-Digger Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְנָפַל עָלָיו שׁוֹר וַהֲרָגוֹ – פָּטוּר. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם מֵת הַשּׁוֹר – יוֹרְשֵׁי בַּעַל הַבּוֹר חַיָּיבִים לְשַׁלֵּם דְּמֵי שׁוֹר לִבְעָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav and Shmuel from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 6:2): If **one was digging a pit in the public domain, and an ox fell on him and killed him** as he was in the pit, the owner of the ox is **exempt** from paying for the damage caused, as it is the one who dug the pit who is at fault. **Moreover, if** it occurred **that the ox died** as a result of the fall, **the heirs of the owner of the pit are liable to pay the value of the ox to its owner.** Compensation for damages is comparable to a loan by oral contract, and yet the *baraita* states that the heirs of the culpable party must pay it.

The *gemara* challenges Rav and Shmuel's position from a *baraita*. The case: someone who illegally digs a pit in the public thoroughfare, and an ox falls into the pit, killing the digger in the process while the ox also dies. The heirs of the pit-digger are liable to pay the owner of the ox for its value. But this debt — the obligation to pay damages — was never memorialized in any written document. How can the heirs be liable? According to Rav and Shmuel, without a *shtar*, you cannot collect from heirs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא אָמַר רַב: בְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ela says** that **Rav says:** The *baraita* is discussing a case **in which** the one who dug the pit **stood trial** for the damage before he died, and once judgment is rendered by a court, the resulting financial liability is comparable to a loan with a promissory note.

The *gemara* resolves this through increasingly strained constructions. Rav Ila citing Rav says: the case is where the pit-digger had not yet died when a court session was held and judgment was rendered against him — and a court ruling is equivalent to a written document.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״הֲרָגוֹ״ קָתָנֵי! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But it is taught** in the *baraita* that the ox **killed him** by falling on him. How then can one say that he stood trial? **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The *baraita* does not mean that the ox killed him instantly, but **that it rendered him one who has a wound that will cause him to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** and there was enough time before his death to try him and deem him liable to pay for damages.

The *gemara* objects: the *baraita* says the ox *killed* him. Rav Adda bar Ahava responds: he was rendered a *treifah* — fatally wounded but not yet dead — and survived long enough for a judgment to be issued.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, תָּנֵי תַּנָּא: ״מֵת וּקְבָרוֹ״! הָתָם – דְּיָתְבִי דַּיָּינֵי אַפּוּמָּא דְבֵירָא, וְחַיְּיבוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects that there is a different version of the *baraita* according to which this interpretation is not possible: **But doesn't Rav Naḥman say** that a certain ***tanna* taught** the *baraita* with a different formulation, stating that the one who dug the pit **died** from the impact of the ox **and** the ox in effect **buried him** in the ground at the bottom of the pit? In this scenario it would be impossible to take the one who dug the pit to court to stand trial. The Gemara answers: **There,** according to that second version, it is discussing a case **where judges sat at the opening of the pit and deemed liable** the one who dug the pit to pay for the damage before he died.

Then Rav Nachman offers a tradition that the *tanna* taught the case as one where the man died and was buried by the ox in the pit. To which the *gemara* answers: the *beit din* was sitting right at the lip of the well and issued their ruling before his last breath.

The *gemara* elsewhere offers an additional avenue of explanation: a debt whose obligation derives directly from Torah law — such as the obligation to pay damages — may be treated as if it were written in a document. *Milveh ha-ksuvah ba-Torah ki-ksuvah bi-shtar damya*. But the resolutions remain strained, and the middle position — that you can collect from heirs but not purchasers — is not yet stated here. Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish meanwhile stand in tension with the *mishna*, and the *gemara* does not issue a formal refutation of their view at this juncture.

## Heirs vs. Purchasers…

### Rav Papa's Halakha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הִלְכְתָא: מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – גּוֹבֶה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. גּוֹבֶה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּנְעוֹל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לֹוִין, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת – דְּלֵית לֵיהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: The *halakha*** is that when a creditor gives **a loan by oral** contract, he can **collect** the debt **from the heirs** of the debtor after his death, **but he cannot collect** the debt **from the purchasers** of the debtor's property. He can **collect** the debt **from the heirs so as not to lock the door in the face of** potential **borrowers. But he cannot collect** the debt **from the purchasers, as** a loan by oral contract **has no publicity** associated with it.

Rav Papa, on *daf* 176, issues the definitive ruling: *hilchesa, malveh al peh goveh min ha-yorshim ve-eino goveh min ha-lekuchos* — the halakha is that an oral loan allows collection from the heirs but not from purchasers.

Two reasons are given. Collection from the heirs is permitted *kedei shelo tin'ol deles bifnei lovin* — so as not to lock the door in front of lenders. Even though one could argue that a lender who chose not to write a *shtar* should have known the consequences, the scenario in which a borrower dies and his heirs receive the estate is a natural, predictable occurrence. It is fair to allow a lender to collect the debt in that scenario regardless of whether a document was written. The heirs, after all, have not expended any money; they are simply paying their father's debt out of an estate they received as a gift of inheritance. On the other side, collection from purchasers is forbidden — *de-leis lei kala* — because an oral loan has no public voice, and purchasers cannot be expected to have known about it.

As Tosafot notes, this split ruling does not resolve the underlying debate about whether *shibuda de-oraita* or *lav de-oraita*. Both sides can accommodate it: if *shibuda de-oraita*, then liens exist by default and the rabbis simply declined to erode them against heirs while removing them against purchasers. If *lav de-oraita*, then the rabbis instituted liens *kedei shelo tin'ol deles* even in the oral case when it comes to heirs, while declining to do so for purchasers. However, Tosafot observes that the *gemara* in the first chapter of Kiddushin explicitly associates this split ruling with the position of *shibuda de-oraita* — which makes intuitive sense, since if liens are biblical, there is a natural baseline from which the rabbis are making targeted exceptions, rather than creating entirely new rabbinic entitlements.

One further question arises: is a *shtar chov* itself a biblical institution? A *shtar* that effects a legal change — a *get*, a writ of manumission, a deed of sale — is certainly recognized by Torah law. But a *shtar* whose entire purpose is to serve as testimony, to create a written record of a transaction that has already occurred, may be only a rabbinic institution. This is a genuine debate among the *rishonim*, with the Ramban and Rambam holding differing views. If the *shtar chov* is itself only rabbinic, then the entire structure of rabbinic-versus-Torah liens takes on an additional layer: the instrument that triggers the rabbinic creation of liens may itself be a rabbinic creation.

## Document & Witness Rules

### Document + Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹצִיא עָלָיו כְּתַב יָדוֹ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב לוֹ – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If one **presents to** a debtor a document in the **handwriting of** the debtor stating **that he owes** money **to him,** but without witnesses signed on the document, the creditor **can collect** only **from unsold property.**

The *gemara* now proceeds to spell out a series of specific rules about when a loan document creates liens and when it does not. The baseline principle: both witnesses and a written document are required for liens to obtain. Without witnesses, a document alone does not create liens; without a document, witnesses alone do not create liens.

### Hand-Written IOU

*Hotzi alav ktav yado* — if the lender produces the borrower's own hand-written acknowledgment of the debt, he may use that to collect from the borrower directly, from *bnei chorin*. But this does not create liens on property sold to purchasers. A hand-written IOU, unsupported by independent witnesses, functions as evidence of the obligation but does not achieve the status of a full *shtar* with *shibud*.

### Beit Din Validation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבָּהּ בַּר נָתָן מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוּחְזַק כְּתַב יָדוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּחְזַק כְּתַב יָדוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶלָּא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Natan asked Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If the document in the **handwriting of** the debtor **was ratified in court** at a later time, **what** is the *halakha*? Would it then be possible to collect the debt from liened property that has been sold? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him** in reply: **Even if** the document in the **handwriting of** the debtor **was ratified in court,** the creditor **can collect** the debt **only from unsold property.**

A *she'ela* is posed by Rabba bar Rav Nachman to Rav Yochanan: *huchzak ktav yado be-veis din mai?* — what if the borrower's handwriting in the IOU was validated by a *beit din*, which affixed its seal confirming that the signature is authentic? Does this validation transform the document into one backed by witnesses, thereby creating liens?

The answer is no. Even if the *beit din* validates the borrower's handwriting, *eino goveh ela mi-nechasim bnei chorin* — he may still collect only from unencumbered property. The Rashbam explains that the *beit din*'s validation does not function as testimony to the loan itself; its purpose is only to prevent the borrower from later denying his signature. The *beit din* is not adding the weight of witness testimony to the *shtar*; it is simply removing the borrower's ability to claim forgery. The document remains exactly what it was before the validation, with no enhanced legal status vis-à-vis third-party purchasers.

### R. Elazar: Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין, וְאִם נִישֵּׂאת – הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: כָּתַב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ – וְאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים; יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – וְאֵין בּוֹ זְמַן; יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן – וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד; הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין, וְאִם נִישֵּׂאת – הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, אֶלָּא שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לָהּ בִּפְנֵי עֵדִים – כָּשֵׁר, וְגוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises an objection** from a mishna (*Gittin* 86a), which states: There are **three** types of **bills of divorce** that **are not valid** *ab initio*, **but if** the woman **marries** another man on the basis of one of these bills of divorce the lineage of **the offspring** from this marriage **is unflawed.** In other words, she is not considered to be a married woman who engaged in sexual intercourse with another man, which would impair the lineage of their child. **And they are these:** A bill of divorce that the husband **wrote in his handwriting but there are no** signatures of **witnesses in** the document at all; a bill of divorce where **there are** the signatures of **witnesses in** the document **but there is no date** written **in it;** and a bill of divorce where **there is a date** written **in it, but it contains only one witness. These are** the **three bills of divorce** that are **not valid** with regard to which the Sages said: **And if she marries,** the lineage of **the offspring is unflawed. Rabbi Elazar says: Even though there are no** signatures of **witnesses in** the document, **but he handed it to her in the presence of** two **witnesses,** it is **a valid** bill of divorce. **And** on the basis of this bill of divorce the woman can **collect** the amount written to her in her marriage contract even **from liened property** that has been sold. This indicates that even when no witnesses signed the document, the debt listed therein can be collected from liened property that has been sold, as long as witnesses saw the document being transferred to the creditor. All the more so a debt listed in a document in the handwriting of the debtor that has been ratified by a court should be eligible for collection from liened property that has been sold.

The *gemara* introduces a *baraita* about three kinds of invalid *gittin* that are nevertheless *kasher be-di'avad* — if the woman remarried, we recognize the divorce as effective and the child of the new marriage is not a *mamzer*. The three cases are: a *get* written in the husband's own handwriting with no witnesses; a *get* with witnesses but no date; and a *get* with a date but with only one witness.

Rabbi Elazar then offers his ruling: even if there are no witnesses signed on the document, if it was delivered in the presence of witnesses — *eidei mesira karsai*, witnesses to the act of delivery are determinative — the *get* is fully valid, and not only valid but *goveh mi-nechasim meshubadim*. If we apply this principle to a loan document, then even a completely unsigned *shtar* handed over to the lender in the presence of two witnesses would create liens, because those witnesses to the delivery are the witnesses of the *shtar*.

This is a striking ruling. The *kol* — the publicity — that the *shtar* creates is achieved not by the signatures inscribed in the document but by the act of witnessed delivery. Two witnesses who saw the document handed over become, in Rabbi Elazar's view, the effective witnesses of that *shtar*, capable of generating full *shibud*.

### Document Effectiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִשְּׁעַת כְּתִיבָה הוּא דְּשַׁעְבַּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There** it **is different, as** the husband **obligated himself** and his property immediately **from the time of** the **writing** of the document, as he knew at that time that the document would be transferred in the presence of witnesses. By contrast, in the case of the mishna here, the document in the handwriting of the debtor was written without the intent of ratifying it. It was only at a later date, as an afterthought, that it was ratified, and the terms of the obligation of the debt cannot be changed at that time.

This raises the obvious question for our earlier discussion: if witnesses present at the moment of delivery create liens even without anything written in the document, why does the subsequent validation of a hand-written IOU by a *beit din* — which involves three judges examining and certifying the document — fail to create liens?

The *gemara* answers with a principle of temporal priority: *shani hasam de-mishas kesiva hu de-shiabad nafshia* — in Rabbi Elazar's case, the *shibud* takes effect from the moment the *shtar* came into existence as a *shtar*. When does a *shtar* come into existence? At the moment it is handed over to the other side. And at that very moment, two witnesses were present and observing. The *shtar*, from its very inception as an operative document, had witnesses. The *shibud* attached simultaneously with the *shtar*'s creation.

By contrast, when Michael hands over his hand-written IOU and only later does the lender go to *beit din* to get the signature validated, the document became operative — was handed over and received — without any witnesses present at that moment. The witnesses, so to speak, arrived after the fact. Since the *shibud* must attach at the moment the *shtar* comes into being, and at that moment there were no witnesses, the subsequent validation cannot retroactively create a *shtar* that carries liens. The *beit din*'s seal does one thing: it prevents the borrower from denying that the signature is his. It does not transform the document into a *shtar* with *shibud*, because that would require witnesses present at the formative moment.

## Guarantor Obligations

### Before vs. After Witness…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָרֵב הַיּוֹצֵא לְאַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: קוֹדֶם חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. לְאַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In the case of **a guarantor whose** commitment **emerged,** i.e., was written, **after the signing of** the promissory **note,** the creditor can collect the debt only from unsold property of the guarantor. **Rav says:** If the guarantor's commitment is written **before the signing of the document,** the creditor can **collect** the debt from the guarantor **from liened property** that has been sold; if it is written **after the signing of the document,** the creditor can **collect** the debt only **from unsold property.**

The *gemara* returns to the case of the guarantor who signs on a *shtar*. Rav rules: if the guarantor's signature appears before the witnesses sign — that is, his commitment is embedded within the body of the document and the witnesses are then signing to attest to the whole — then the lender *goveh mi-nechasim meshubadim*, he has liens on the guarantor's property. If the guarantor's signature appears after the witnesses have already signed — as in the *mishna*'s case — then the lender *goveh mi-nechasim bnei chorin*, he can collect from the guarantor's unencumbered property but has no liens. This is essentially the ruling of Rabbi Yishmael in the *mishna*, now spelled out with its underlying rationale: the placement of the guarantor's commitment relative to the witnesses determines whether his obligation carries a *shtar* with *shibud*.

### Rav's Two Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִמְנִין אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ קוֹדֶם חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶלָּא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. קַשְׁיָא דְּרַב אַדְּרַב! לָא קַשְׁיָא;
>
> **Translation:** At other **times, Rav said: Even** if the guarantee is written **before the signing of the document,** the creditor can **collect** the debt **only from unsold property. The Gemara asks: There is** a difficulty resulting from the contradiction between one statement **of Rav to** the other statement **of Rav,** as they seem to contradict one another with regard to a guarantee that is written before the signing of the document. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult.**

Yet the *gemara* reports that Rav himself on other occasions ruled differently: *afilu kodem chitumei shtaros eino goveh ela mi-nechasim bnei chorin* — even if the guarantor signed before the witnesses, the lender still has no liens on the guarantor's property. This appears to be a direct contradiction within Rav's own rulings. The *gemara* announces: *la kashya* — this is no contradiction.

### Vav Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא דִּכְתִב בֵּיהּ ״פְּלוֹנִי עָרֵב״. הָא דִּכְתִב בֵּיהּ ״וּפְלוֹנִי עָרֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** This is **where it was written in** the document as follows: **So-and-so** is **a guarantor,** as this formulation is an independent statement, and the witnesses signing the document do not necessarily relate to that statement. And **that** case, where Rav says the creditor can collect the debt from liened property that has been sold, is **where it was written in** the document as follows: **And so-and-so** is **a guarantor.** The usage of the conjunctive: And, causes this clause to be an integral part of the document, so that the signatures of the witnesses relate to it as well.

The resolution turns on a single letter. If the *shtar* reads *ploni areiv* — "so-and-so is a guarantor" — then the guarantor's commitment stands as a separate statement, syntactically detached from the core content of the *shtar*. The witnesses who sign are signing to the main body of the document — the loan — not to this additional line about the guarantor. Consequently, even if the guarantor's name appears before the witnesses' signatures, his obligation does not have the status of a witnessed *shtar* and carries no *shibud*. But if the *shtar* reads *u'ploni areiv* — "and so-and-so is a guarantor," with a *vav* connecting it to what preceded — then the guarantor's commitment is syntactically integrated into one continuous statement. The witnesses are signing to the entirety of this unified document, including the guarantor's obligation, and therefore the guarantor's commitment carries *shibud*.

This is admittedly a formalistic distinction, and one that may leave the reader searching for the underlying policy. One possible explanation is an effort to protect the *areiv*: the guarantor is performing a generous act by standing up for the borrower, and the halakha perhaps wishes to limit the circumstances under which he will be fully exposed to liens on his own property. Another consideration is the protection of purchasers of the guarantor's property: the question is whether the *lekuchos* who buy from the guarantor have sufficient notice that his property is encumbered, and a tightly integrated *shtar* — marked by the conjunctive *vav* — may be seen as generating greater public notice than a loosely appended separate statement.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶלָּא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִב בֵּיהּ ״וּפְלוֹנִי עָרֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In **both this** case **and that** case, the creditor can **collect** the debt **only from unsold property.** The Gemara comments: **And** according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, this is the *halakha* **even if it is written in** the document: **And so-and-so** is **a guarantor.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: עֵדִים הַחֲתוּמִין עַל שְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם בְּגֵט – פָּסוּל; חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא עַל שְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם חָתְמוּ. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, לְדִידִי מִיפָּרְשָׁא לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״שְׁאִילוּ״ – פָּסוּל. ״וּשְׁאִילוּ״ – כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Gittin* 9:9): In a case where **witnesses are signed on a greeting** written **in a bill of divorce,** the bill of divorce is **not valid,** as **we are concerned** that **perhaps** the witnesses **signed on the greeting** alone, i.e., they did not intend to testify to the divorce, but only to verify that the greeting was written. **And Rabbi Abbahu said** concerning this *baraita*: **It was explained to me** personally **by Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself that it is only when the wording of the greeting begins: **Send a greeting** to so-and-so, that the bill of divorce is **not valid,** as this is an independent statement and it is possible that the signatures relate to it alone. But if the wording is: **And send a greeting** to so-and-so, the usage of the conjunctive: And, causes this clause to be an integral part of the document, and the bill of divorce is **valid.** This indicates that Rabbi Yoḥanan himself maintains that when the conjunction is used the signatures relate to the entire document.

Rava challenges this resolution by drawing a comparison to a *get* that is *pasul*. The case: a *get* that contains, at the end, the phrase *she'eilas shalom* — a greeting, asking how someone is doing — followed by the witnesses' signatures. We rule such a *get* invalid because we say the witnesses may have been signing only to the greeting, not to the body of the *get* itself — and testimony directed at a greeting has no legal effect on the *get*. Now Rabbi Abbahu clarifies: he heard this ruling from Rabbi Yochanan himself. If you say *she'ilu* — "ask after her" — the *get* is *pasul*. If you say *ve-she'ilu* — "and ask after her," with a *vav* — the *get* is *kasher*, because the *vav* makes it one continuous statement and the witnesses are signing to the whole.

The challenge to our resolution of Rav's two positions is this: in the *get* case, even a *vav* is insufficient to ensure that the witnesses are truly signing to the substance of the *get*, if there is extraneous matter present. So how can we say that a *vav* before the guarantor's name ensures that the witnesses are signing to his obligation? Surely the guarantor clause is less extraneous than a social greeting — it is legally significant content — but the comparison still raises a structural question.

### Unity of Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִב ״פְּלוֹנִי עָרֵב״. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב! אֵימָא: וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here too,** when did Rabbi Yoḥanan say that the creditor can collect the debt only from the guarantor's unsold property? **Where it was written: So-and-so** is **a guarantor.** If the conjunction is used, he can collect the debt even from liened property that has been sold. The Gemara objects: But **if so, this is** identical to the opinion **of Rav,** whereas Rabbi Yoḥanan was presented as disagreeing with Rav. The Gemara answers: That presentation was inaccurate. **Say** instead: **And so Rabbi Yoḥanan says** as Rav did.

The resolution, as articulated by Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, turns on the direction of the concern. In the *get* case, when you say *she'ilu* without a *vav*, you have two entirely separate units: the *get* itself, and the greeting. We worry that the witnesses signed only to the greeting — an absurd thing, but formally possible — because the greeting stands as its own isolated statement. When you add the *vav*, it becomes one unified statement, the concern disappears, and the *get* is valid.

In the guarantor case, the parallel holds: *ploni areiv* without a *vav* creates a syntactic break, and we treat the guarantor clause as a separate unit, not directly attested by the witnesses. *U'ploni areiv* with a *vav* unifies the entire document, and the witnesses are signing to everything including the guarantor's obligation. Rabbi Yochanan's position, properly understood, is therefore identical to Rav's: the *vav* makes the difference. What we initially understood as Rabbi Yochanan saying that even a *vav* is insufficient turns out to be a misreading; he too holds that with a *vav* the guarantor's obligation carries *shibud*. The *gemara* concludes: *eima ve-chein amar Rabbi Yochanan* — simply say that Rabbi Yochanan said the same thing as Rav.

## Guarantor Liability

### Case of R. Ishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates: **An incident** occurred where such a case **came before Rabbi Yishmael,** and he said: The creditor can collect the debt from unsold property of the guarantor, but not from liened property that the guarantor has sold to others.

The *gemara* turns to a narrative that illuminates the final rulings about the guarantor's liability. A case came before Rabbi Yishmael in which the guarantor had signed at the bottom of the *shtar*, after the witnesses. Rabbi Yishmael's analysis: the guarantor is obligated to pay but his obligation carries no *shibud*, no liens. The obligation exists — one can collect from his *bnei chorin* — but there is no *shtar* against him personally that would allow collection from property he has sold to others.

### Ben Nanas Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ בֶּן נַנָּס: אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים וְלֹא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. אָמַר לוֹ: לָמָּה? אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי הַחוֹנֵק אֶת אֶחָד בַּשּׁוּק, וּמְצָאוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ ״הַנַּח לוֹ [וַאֲנִי אֶתֵּן לָךְ]״ – פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא עַל אֱמוּנָתוֹ הִלְוָהוּ. אֶלָּא אֵיזֶה הוּא עָרֵב שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב? ״הַלְוֵהוּ, וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לָךְ״ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁכֵּן עַל אֱמוּנָתוֹ הִלְוָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Nannas said** to Rabbi Yishmael: The creditor **cannot collect** the sum from the guarantor at all, **not from liened property** that has been sold, **nor from unsold property. Rabbi Yishmael said to him: Why** not? Ben Nannas **said to him:** If **one was strangling someone in the marketplace,** demanding repayment of a loan, **and another** person **found him** doing so and **said to** the attacker: **Leave him** alone **and I will give you** the money he owes, the person who intervened is **exempt** from paying, **as** the creditor **did not loan** the money in the first place **based on his trust** of the one who intervened. **Rather, who is a guarantor who is obligated** to repay the loan he has guaranteed? One who tells the creditor before the loan takes place: **Lend** money to **him, and I will give you** the repayment, **as** in that case the creditor **did loan** the money **based on his trust** of the guarantor.

Ben Nanas disagreed fundamentally. He argued from a vivid and powerful analogy: imagine a creditor has grabbed a borrower by the throat — *chanok* — demanding payment. Another person steps forward and says: let him go, I will pay you. In such a case, says Ben Nanas, the bystander's promise is not a genuine acceptance of obligation. He is simply trying to stop the creditor's coercive pressure; his words do not reflect a considered decision to obligate himself. Therefore, when the guarantor signs at the bottom of an already-executed *shtar*, we infer that the loan had already been made, the lender was pressuring the borrower, and the guarantor stepped in to stop the pressure. His commitment is analogous to the bystander in the choking scenario — a reactive intervention, not a genuine acceptance of debt — and therefore he is not obligated at all.

### R. Ishmael's Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּחְכִּים – יַעֲסוֹק בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת; שֶׁאֵין לָךְ מִקְצוֹעַ בַּתּוֹרָה יוֹתֵר מֵהֶן, וְהֵן כְּמַעְיָין הַנּוֹבֵעַ. וְהָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּעֲסוֹק בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – יְשַׁמֵּשׁ אֶת שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן נַנָּס.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael** thereupon **said: One who wants to become wise should engage in** the study of **monetary law, as there is no greater discipline in the Torah, and it is like a flowing spring. And,** he added, **one who wants to engage in** the study of **monetary law should attend to,** i.e., become a disciple of, **Shimon ben Nannas.**

The *gemara* records that Rabbi Yishmael responded to Ben Nanas's argument with warm praise. He said: if you want wisdom, learn *dinei mamonos* — monetary law. And if you want to excel in *dinei mamonos*, apprentice yourself to Ben Nanas. The imagery Ben Nanas deployed was strikingly effective. Nonetheless:

### Actual Choking Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּחָנוּק – מָה לִי אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חָלוּק הָיָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אַף בְּחָנוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What** would **Rabbi Yishmael say to me about** the case of the debtor who was being **strangled,** as depicted by ben Nannas? Perhaps he would agree in that case, since the guarantee was given under duress, that it is not valid. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear, as Rabbi Yaakov says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yishmael disagreed** with ben Nannas **also in** the case of the debtor being **strangled.**

The *gemara* then asks: *be-chanok ma lei amar Rabbi Yishmael?* — what about a case of literal choking? Not merely a metaphorical situation of financial pressure, but one where the creditor has literally grabbed the borrower by the throat. Does Rabbi Yishmael say even then the guarantor who steps in has obligated himself? One might think that in a case of genuine physical coercion, the guarantor's words are nothing more than a reflexive attempt to stop the violence, with no legal weight.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ, אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּכִי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חָלוּק הָיָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אַף בְּחָנוּק, וַהֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ אַף בְּחָנוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And is the ***halakha* in accordance with** Rabbi Yishmael's opinion even in the case of the debtor being strangled, **or** is the ***halakha* not in accordance with his** opinion in that case? **Come** and **hear, as when Ravin came** he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yishmael disagreed** with ben Nannas **also in** the case of the debtor being **strangled, and** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** Rabbi Yishmael's opinion **also in** the case of the debtor being **strangled.**

The answer comes from a tradition transmitted by Rabbi Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *cholok haya Rabbi Yishmael af be-chanok* — Rabbi Yishmael would disagree with Ben Nanas even in a case of literal choking. Once a guarantor verbally accepts the obligation, regardless of what circumstances motivated him to speak those words, he has obligated himself. We do not look behind the words to evaluate the psychological state or external pressure that produced them. This is an interesting contrast to the earlier case where a guarantor for a *ketubah* is not liable because he is understood as simply trying to facilitate the marriage rather than genuinely accepting a debt. The distinction may rest on the principle of *lo plug*: we do not try to evaluate what motivated each individual guarantor; once the words of guarantee are spoken, the obligation attaches.

### Kinyan Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָנוּק, וְקָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ – מִשְׁתַּעְבֵּד. מִכְּלַל דְּעָרֵב בְּעָלְמָא – לָא בָּעֵי קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** If the debtor was being **strangled, and** in addition **an act of acquisition was performed with** the guarantor, the guarantor **becomes obligated** to repay the debt. The Gemara deduces: **By inference** it emerges **that a guarantor generally does not require an act of acquisition** to become obligated to pay.

The *gemara* now raises the question of *kinyan* — the formal act of acquisition that is typically required to create a binding legal commitment. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: chanok ve-kanu mi-yado* — in the choking scenario, where the guarantor steps in under pressure, his obligation is enforceable only if accompanied by a *kinyan*, a formal act such as the drawing of a handkerchief. The *gemara* derives from this, by implication, the converse principle: *mikhlal de-areiv be-alma lo ba'ei kinyan* — in a normal case, a guarantor does not require a *kinyan*. His verbal commitment alone is sufficient to create the obligation.

### Court-Appointed Guarantor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עָרֵב דְּבֵית דִּין הוּא דְּלָא בָּעֵי קִנְיָן, הָא בְּעָלְמָא בָּעֵי קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this **disagrees with** a statement of **Rav Naḥman, as Rav Naḥman says: It is** only **a guarantor** who undertakes a loan guarantee **in** the presence of **a court who does not require an act of acquisition; this** indicates that **generally,** a guarantor **requires an act of acquisition** in order to be obligated to pay.

Rav Nachman introduces a qualification. He rules: *areiv de-bei din hu de-lo ba'ei kinyan* — specifically a guarantor whose role is assigned or supervised by a *beit din* does not require a *kinyan*. The logic: the honor and gravity of being designated by the court gives the guarantor's commitment such seriousness and weight that we need not require a formal act to confirm it. *De-be-hahi d-heiman lei gamar u-mishta'abed lei* — by the trust the *beit din* places in him, he fully resolves to accept the obligation and binds himself. But Rav Nachman holds that in an ordinary case — *ha de-alma* — a *kinyan* is indeed required. This conflicts with the earlier derivation from Shmuel, which suggested that only the pressure-induced guarantee (*chanok*) requires a *kinyan*, while all other guarantors are bound by words alone.

### Final Halakha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: עָרֵב בִּשְׁעַת מַתַּן מָעוֹת – לֹא בָּעֵי קִנְיָן, אַחַר מַתַּן מָעוֹת – בָּעֵי קִנְיָן. עָרֵב דְּבֵית דִּין – לָא בָּעֵי קִנְיָן; דִּבְהָהִיא הֲנָאָה דִּמְהֵימַן לֵיהּ – גָּמַר וּמְשַׁעְבֵּד לֵיהּ. הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ גֵּט פָּשׁוּט, וּסְלִיקָא לַהּ מַסֶּכֶת בָּבָא בָּתְרָא
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that **a guarantor** who accepts responsibility for the loan **at the time of the giving of the money does not require an act of acquisition;** but if he accepts responsibility **after the giving of the money,** he **requires an act of acquisition.** Moreover, **a guarantor** who undertakes a loan guarantee **in** the presence of a **court does not require an act of acquisition, as** in return **for that gratification that** he experiences in that the court **trusts him, he resolves to obligate himself.** 
> 
> **[This concludes the Gemara and our study of the tractate.]**

The *gemara* closes the *masechet* with the definitive ruling: *ve-hilchesa, areiv be-shas matan maos lo ba'ei kinyan* — a guarantor who accepts his obligation at the time of the loan, when the money is actually being transferred, does not need a *kinyan*. At that moment, the loan is being made *because of his guarantee*. The lender is extending credit in reliance on the guarantor's word. The guarantor's promise has genuine operative force — it is the immediate cause of the lender's action — and therefore creates a binding obligation through words alone.

*Achar matan maos ba'ei kinyan* — after the loan has already been made, the guarantor who steps in requires a *kinyan*. Here the money was already lent independently of his commitment; he is intervening after the fact, perhaps to relieve pressure on the borrower, and his words lack the same operative force. A formal *kinyan* is required to demonstrate the seriousness of his intention. This is precisely the Ben Nanas type of scenario: the guarantor who signs at the bottom of the *shtar*, after the loan has been made and the witnesses have signed.

*Areiv de-bei din lo ba'ei kinyan* — a court-appointed guarantor never requires a *kinyan*, regardless of timing. The weight of the *beit din*'s designation substitutes for the formality of acquisition.

With this ruling, Bava Batra reaches its conclusion. *Hadran alach Gett Pashut, ve-salikka lach Masechet Bava Batra.*
