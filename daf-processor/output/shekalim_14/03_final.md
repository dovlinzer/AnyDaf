# Shekalim 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Temple Officials

### Opening remarks

Today's daf is 14, though we begin in the middle of 13a with the opening of a new *perek*. The shift in tone is immediately apparent: after the dense and often challenging discussions that preceded, the *gemara* now moves into a markedly different register. The *mishnah* and the *gemara* that follows offer something of a respite — a sustained look at the people and personalities who populated the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the remarkable things they did there.

### First Mishnah

The *mishnah* opens: *Elu hen hamemunim shehayu bamikdash* — these are the appointed ones, those who held some administrative position in the *Beis HaMikdash*. The context is entirely natural given where we have been: having discussed the collection of the *shekel*, what to do with the *shekel* funds, and how prices were set for the suppliers, we are now firmly in the world of *Beis HaMikdash* administration. The *mishnah* accordingly catalogs the various officials and what each one oversaw.

### Officials overview

*Yochanan ben Phinehas al hachotamos* — Yochanan ben Phinehas oversaw the *chotamos*, the receipt system. As we have already seen, this was the mechanism by which worshippers would pay money, receive a stamp or token, and then bring that token to a supplier who would provide them with wine and other *nesachim*. *Achiyah al hanesachim* — Achiyah then oversaw the *nesachim* themselves: one official handled the receipt, the other fulfilled it. *Matya ben Shmuel al hapisos* — Matya ben Shmuel oversaw the *pisos*, the lots drawn to determine which *kohein* would receive the more sought-after assignments. *Petachia al hakinim* — Petachia oversaw the bird sacrifices, an administratively challenging role that we will examine more closely when the *gemara* returns to him. People would bring money to a communal container, and the *Beis HaMikdash* would acquire the birds and offer the sacrifices within the same day — a demanding logistical undertaking.

### Petachiah = Mordechai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּתַחְיָה עַל הַקִּינִּים. בּוֹא וּרְאֵה מַה גָדוֹל הוּא כוֹחוֹ שֶׁלְאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ שֶׁהוּא פּוֹתֵחַ דְבָרִים וְדוֹרְשָׁן וְיוֹדֵעַ בְּשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Petaḥiah of the nests. Come and see how great the power of this man was, for he "had original ideas and explained them, and he knew seventy languages.

At this point the *mishnah* takes a striking turn: *Petachia zehu Mordechai* — this Petachia was in fact Mordechai. The reason he was called Petachia, the *mishnah* explains, is *shehaya poseach devarim vedorsham veyodeia beshiv'im lashon* — because he would open up closed matters, explicate difficult things, and knew seventy languages. There is something notable about the *gemara* performing this kind of *drash* on the name of a character mentioned within the *mishnah* itself, a technique more familiar from the Torah's own treatment of figures like Yisro with his multiple names. But a second, more pressing question arises: if Petachia is Mordechai, then we are dealing with a figure from the very first generation of the *Beis HaMikdash Hasheini*. Does that mean all the officials listed here served in that same early generation?

### Generation question

This question is real. The *mishnah* also mentions Elazar — presumably the biblical Elazar — who oversaw the *Parochet*, and Pinchas, who dressed the *Kohanim*, presumably the *Kohen Gadol*. These are figures one would associate with a period far earlier than Mordechai, and the identification compounds the difficulty of treating this list as a roster of contemporaries. One plausible resolution is that the line *Petachia hu Mordechai* is a later gloss, a secondary layer added to the *mishnah*, and that if it is removed, the remainder of the list can be understood as describing one particular generation — perhaps toward the end of the *Mikdash* period, overlapping with the early *tanna'im*. That would make considerably more sense. It is also worth noting that Elazar and Pinchas are the only two names in the *mishnah* that appear without a patronymic, without a *ben*, which itself sets them apart and may support the view that they represent a separate tradition. Additionally, Mordechai was not a *kohein*, introducing a tribal inconsistency if he is placed among the *Beis HaMikdash* officials in a straightforward reading. The goal of the *mishnah* in naming individuals at all seems primarily to convey the texture and variety of the different roles — and to give *kavod* to those who performed them.

### More officials

*Ben Achiya al cholei me'ayim* — Ben Achiya oversaw digestive ailments among the *kohanim*, an office that turns out to have real medical substance, as the *gemara* will explain. *Nachunya chofer shichin* — Nachunya dug wells. *Gevini kruz* — Gevini was the announcer. *Ben Gever al ne'ilas she'arim* — Ben Gever oversaw the locking of the gates at night; while the *levi'im* performed the actual guarding, he served as the head administrator. *Ben Babi memuneh al hapakiya* — Ben Babi oversaw the *pakiya*, the twisting of the wicks, as we will see. *Ben Arza al hatziltzal* — Ben Arza oversaw the chimes, the signal for the *levi'im* to begin their singing. *Hugras ben Levi al hashir* — Hugras ben Levi oversaw the choir itself.

### Beit Garmo & Avtinas

The *mishnah* then turns to two institutions of a different character: *Beis Garmo al ma'aseh lechem hapanim* and *Beis Avtinas al ha-ketores*. We have already encountered both of these families in earlier discussions, where the *ketores* was mentioned in connection with payments to *Beis Garmo* and *Beis Avtinas*. What distinguishes them from the administrative officials listed above is that these were *kohanim* households who held hereditary secrets — *Beis Garmo* knew how to prepare the *Lechem HaPanim*, and *Beis Avtinas* knew how to prepare the *ketores*. These were not administrative posts in the same sense; the knowledge lived within the family and was transmitted only within it. Finally, the *mishnah* lists *Elazar al haParochet* and *Pinchas al haMalbush*, who dressed the *kohanim* — and as noted, these two stand without patronymics, raising again the question of which generation is being described and whether all of these figures truly belong to the same historical moment.

## Merit debate

### Rav Chizkiyah debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹנָה. כְּתִיב לָכֵן אֲחַלֶּק לוֹ בָרַבִּים וְאֶת עֲצוּמִים יְחַלֵּק שָׁלָל. זֶה רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה שֶׁהִתְקִין מִדְרַשׁ הֲלָכוֹת וְהַגָּדוֹת. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים. אֵילּוּ אַנְשֵׁי כְנֶסֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה. מַה שֶׁהִתְקִין זֶה כְלָלִין וּפְרָטִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Jonah said, it is written: *Therefore, I shall distribute to him in public, and with the powerful he shall have part in booty*, that is Rebbi Aqiba who organized interpretation of Scripture, practice, and homiletics. But some say, this refers to the men of the Great Assembly, who organized principles and details.

The *gemara* opens with a dispute between Rav Chizkiyah and *Rabbanan*. *Chad amar kasherei dor vador ba limnot* — one position holds that the *mishnah* is not describing a single generation at all. Rather, it singles out, from all the generations in which the *Beis HaMikdash* stood, those individuals who were most deserving of recognition — the *kasherei*, the upstanding ones of each era. On this reading, the figures in the *mishnah* can come from any number of different historical periods, united only by the fact that they merit special mention. *Vecharna amar mi shehaya be'oto dor mana* — the other position is more prosaic: the *mishnah* simply lists whoever happened to be serving in the generation of the author of the *mishnah*. This is not a selection of the best from across history; it is merely an account of who held each post at one particular time.

### Yoma reference

The practical consequence of this debate emerges through a connection to a *mishnah* in *Yoma*. That *mishnah* discusses various individuals and families who contributed to the *Beis HaMikdash*, and it concludes with a distinction: *al harishonim hu omer zecher tzadik livracha* — about the former ones it says "the memory of the righteous is a blessing," while *aval acharonim hu omer shem reshaim yirkav* — about the latter ones it says "the name of the wicked shall rot." The *mishnah* in *Yoma* mentions *Beis Garmo* and *Beis Avtinas* among those who did not share their knowledge with others, placing them ambiguously between the clearly praiseworthy and the clearly blameworthy.

According to the position that our *mishnah* in *Shekalim* deliberately selects the *kasherei* of each generation, all those mentioned — including *Beis Garmo* and *Beis Avtinas* — fall under *zecher tzadik livracha*. According to the position that the *mishnah* simply records whoever was there, no particular merit is implied, and the *shem reshaim yirkav* of the *Yoma mishnah* would apply to them.

### Garmo/Avtinas status

The question of whether *Beis Garmo* and *Beis Avtinas* are to be counted among the righteous or the wicked is thus genuinely unresolved at this stage, and the *gemara* will return to it. The ambivalence is understandable: on the one hand, they performed their sacred tasks with skill and faithfulness; on the other, they refused to transmit that knowledge to anyone outside their families. Whether that refusal constitutes a failing serious enough to place them in the *shem reshaim yirkav* category, or whether it is mitigated by their genuine service, depends on which reading of our *mishnah*'s purpose one adopts.

## Organizing traditions

### Rabbi Akiva

The *gemara* continues with Rav Yona citing the verse from Isaiah: *lachen achalek lo barabim v'et atzumim yachalek shalal* — "therefore I will divide for him among the great, and with the mighty he will divide the spoil." *Zeh Rabbi Akiva* — this refers to Rabbi Akiva, *she'hitkin midrash halachot v'agadot*, who established — or organized — the *halachot* and the *aggadot*. The *gemara* does not define this precisely, but the weight of the description falls on the word *yachalek*, to divide and distribute: Rabbi Akiva took what preceded him in a disorganized state and sorted it into conceptual categories. *Tosafos* glosses *agadot* here as *midrash halacha*, which is noteworthy, since we do not ordinarily associate Rabbi Akiva's legacy with *aggadah* in the looser sense. The standard formulation of Rabbi Akiva's domain is rather the world of *nega'im* and *ohalot*. The precise meaning of what he "established" here remains worth examining.

### Knesset HaGedolah

A second opinion divides the verse differently. *V'yesh omrim elu anshei knesset hagedola* — some say the verse refers to the members of the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*. The question then becomes: *ela ma tiken* — what exactly did they establish? The answer given is *zeh klalot u'pratot* — they established the framework of *kelal* and *prat*, the general and the particular, the foundational hermeneutical categories of rabbinic legal reasoning. The *gemara* appears to read the two halves of the verse as referring to two different figures or groups: *achalek lo barabim* to Rabbi Akiva, and *v'et atzumim yachalek shalal* to the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah*. What is striking throughout is the consistent emphasis on *organization* as the great intellectual achievement. Whether it is Rabbi Akiva sorting *halachot* and *aggadot* into categories, or the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* structuring legal reasoning around *kelal* and *prat*, the creative act being celebrated is not simply the generation of new law but the arrangement and structuring of what was already there into a coherent, transmissible form.

### Sofrim & numbering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי אַבָּהוּ. כְּתִיב מִשְׁפְּחוֹת סוֹפְרִים יֹוֹשְׁבֵי יַעְבֵּץ. מַה תַלְמוּד לוֹמַר סוֹפְרִים. אֶלָּא שֶׁעָשׂוּ אֶת הַתּוֹרָה סְפוּרוֹת סְפוּרוֹת. חֲמִשָּׁה לֹא יִתְרוֹמוּ. חֲמִשָּׁה דְבָרִים חַיָיבִין בַּחַלָּה חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה נָשִׁים פּוֹטְרוֹת צָרוֹתֵיהֶן. שְׁלשִׁים וָשֵׁשׁ כְּרֵיתוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה. שְׁלשָׁה עָשָׂר דָּבָר בְּנִבְלַת הָעוֹף הַטָּהוֹר. אַרְבַּע אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין. אֲבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת אַרְבָּעִים חָסֵר אֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abbahu said, it is written: *The families of counters, dwellers of Yabeṣ.* Why does the verse say, *counters*? Because they formulated the teaching numbers, numbers. "Five shall not lift heave." "Fifteen women free their co-wives." "Thirty-six extirpations in the Torah." "Thirteen matters about the carcass of a pure bird." "Four main categories of torts." "Thirty-nine categories of work."

Rabbi Abbahu then cites the verse *mishpachat Sofrim yoshvei Yabetz* — the family of the *Sofrim* who dwell in Yabetz — and asks: *mah Talmud lomar Sofrim*? Why were the early generations of rabbis called *Sofrim*? The answer is famous: *ela she-asu et haTorah sfurot sfurot* — they made the Torah into numbers. They organized the material numerically, creating mnemonics that would allow the traditions to be preserved, remembered, and transmitted accurately.

### Numerical mnemonics

The *gemara* immediately offers a cascade of examples: *chamisha lo yitremu trumah* — five people may not separate *terumah*; *chamesh esreh nashim potrot tzaroteihen* — fifteen women exempt their co-wives from *yibbum*; *shloshim v'shesha kretot baTorah* — thirty-six *kretot* in the Torah; *shlosha asar davar ne'emru b'neveilat ha-of hatehorah* — thirteen things are said regarding the carcass of a *tahor* bird; *arba avot nezikin* — four main categories of damage; *avot melachot arba'im chaseir achat* — the *melachot* of *Shabbat* are forty minus one. These are all openings of *mishnayot*. The organizing principle is numerical: a number anchors each tradition, makes it memorable, and serves as a check on the integrity of transmission.

### Torah she-be-al-peh

What is genuinely illuminating about this *gemara* is its recognition that the process of *Torah she-be-al-peh* involves not merely the development or evolution of *halacha* but, in a crucial sense, the *organization* of the material. When the *gemara* says that the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* established *halachot*, it may mean that they took existing practices — things people were already doing — and articulated them as formal law. They looked at what was being observed and gave it shape and expression. And then the early rabbis took that articulated material and structured it further: thematically, numerically, mnemonically. The entire enterprise of *Torah she-be-al-peh* as we have received it is, on this account, inseparable from the labor of organization.

### Ezra the Scribe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי אָחָא. כְּתִיב לְעֶזְרָא הַכֹּהֵן הַסּוֹפֵר. מַה תַלְמוּד לוֹמַר סוֹפֵר. אֶלָּא כְשֵׁם שֶׁהָיָה סוֹפֵר בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה כָּךְ הָיָה סוֹפֵר בְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Aḥa said, *for Ezra the priest, the counter*. But just as he was counting in the Torah so he was counting in the words of the Sages.

The *gemara* then draws a parallel to Ezra: *le-Ezra ha-Kohen ha-Sofer* — Ezra the *Kohen* the *Sofer*. *Mah Talmud lomar Sofer*? *Ela keshem she-hayah Sofer be-divrei Torah kach hayah Sofer be-divrei Chachamim* — just as he was a *Sofer* in matters of Torah, so was he a *Sofer* in matters of the Sages. The *gemara* is saying that the same way the early rabbis numbered and structured their oral traditions, Ezra numbered the written Torah. This is the work we associate with the early Masoretes: counting how many *psukim* the Torah contains, how many times *hi* is written versus *hu*, how many times a word is spelled *maleh* versus *chaser*, what is the midpoint of the Torah in words and in letters. All of this serves to guarantee the accuracy and integrity of the text, exactly as the numerical mnemonics of the *mishnah* serve to preserve the oral tradition.

There is a deeper point embedded here about the very word *sefer* and the word *sofer*. The simplest sense of *sofer* derives from *lesaper* — to tell, to narrate — and a *sefer* is a book that tells a story. But *sofer* also means to count, *lispor*. The two meanings are not incidental to one another: embedded in the concept of a book is the idea of structure, numbering, organization. A *sefer* is not a random collection; it has *psukim*, it has sections, it has a counted and verifiable form.

## Bread parable

### Labor metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חַגַּיי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחָמָן. הָרִאשׁוֹנִים חָרְשׁוּ וְזָרְעוּ נִיכְּשׁוּ כִּיסְּחוּ עִידְּרוּ קָצְרוּ עִימִּירוּ דָּשׁוּ זָרוּ בָּרְרוּ טָחֲנוּ הִרְקִידוּ לָשׁוּ קִיטִּיפוּ וְאָפוּ. וְאָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ פֶּה לוֹכַל.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥaggai in the name of Rebbi Samuel bar Naḥman. The earlier generations ploughed, and sowed, and weeded, and trimmed, and hoed, cut, made sheaves, threshed, winnowed, milled, sifted, made dough, kneaded, and baked. But we do not even have a mouth to eat.

Rabbi Chagai in the name of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachman offers one of the most beautifully constructed images in this section of the *gemara*: *harishonim charshu v'zar'u nichshu kisschu idru katzru amru dashu zaru berru tachnu hirkidu lashu kitfu v'afu* — the early ones plowed, planted, weeded, hoed, harvested, gathered into piles, threshed, winnowed, sorted out the chaff, ground, sifted, kneaded, patted down the dough, and baked it. They did every single step required to transform raw grain into bread. They did everything necessary to make Torah accessible to us.

### Our struggle

*V'anu ein lanu mah le'echol* — and we have nothing to eat. After all that labor, after every stage of preparation was completed on our behalf, we still struggle to access the Torah. The image is not merely rhetorical. What came before the *mishnah*? What had to be done to the inchoate mass of oral traditions, practices, and teachings that preceded it in order to produce the organized, articulated, transmissible document we now simply open and read? We arrive at *daf yomi* each morning, open the *gemara*, and read a *mishnah* — and we rarely pause to ask how much labor was invested in getting that *mishnah* to the form it is now in. Just as one can purchase a loaf of bread without pausing to consider the full chain of agricultural and manufacturing labor behind it, one can study *mishnah* without reckoning with the generations of organizing, articulating, and structuring work that made it possible. The *gemara* insists that we pause and reckon.

## Angels & donkey

### Angel comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זְמִינָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי זְעוּרָא. אִין הֲווֹן קַדְמָאֵיי מַלְאָכִין אֲנָן בְּנֵי אֵינַשׁ. וְאִין הֲווֹן בְּנֵי אֵנַשׁ אֲנָן חֲמָרִין. אָמַר רִבִּי מָנָא. בְּהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא אָמְרִין. אֲפִילוּ לַחֲמַרְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי פִינְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר לָא אִידְמִינָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abba bar Zemina said in the name of Rebbi Ze`ira: If the earlier generations were angels, we are men; if they were men, we are donkeys. Rebbi Mana said, at that moment they said, we are not even comparable to the she-ass of Rebbi Phineas ben Yair.

This leads directly to the famous formulation: *in havon kadma'in malachin, anan benei enash* — if the earlier ones were like angels, then we are merely people. *V'in havon benei enash, anan chamorin* — and if they were people, then we are donkeys. Rabbi Mana adds: at that moment I said, *afilu k'chamorei d'Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair lo daminan* — we are not even comparable to the donkey of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair. This self-deprecating observation sets up the story that follows.

### Donkey joke

The quip is precise: it is not merely that we are donkeys. We are not even *good* donkeys. The donkey of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair sets an extraordinarily high bar — as the story will make clear.

### Donkey refuses food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמַרְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי פִינְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר גְּנַבְתָהּ לִיסְטָאֵי בַלֵּילִיָּא. עֲבְדַּת טְמִירָא גַבּוֹן תְּלָתָא יוֹמִין וְלֹא טַעֲמָה כְלוּם. בְּתַר תְּלָתָה יוֹמִין אִימְלָכוּן מַחְזַרְתָּהּ. אָמְרוּן. אַפְקוּנַהּ מִן הָכָא דְלָא תֵימוּת גָּבָּן. אַפְקוּנַהּ. אָזְלַת וְקָמַת לָהּ עַל תִּרְעָא דָמָרָהּ. שְׁרִיַית מְנַהֲקָה. אֲמַר לוֹן. פָּתְחוּן לָהָדָא עֲלִיבְתָא דְּאִית לָהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמִין דְּלָא טַעֲמָה כְלוּם. פָּתְחוּן לָהּ וְעָלַת לָהּ. אֲמַר לוֹן. יְהָבוּ לָהּ תֵּיכוּל. יְהָבוּן קוֹמָהּ סְעָרִין וְלָא בָעַת מִיכוּל. אָמְרוּן לֵיהּ. רִבִּי. לָא בָעַת מִיכוּל. אֲמַר לוֹן. מְתַקְּנִין אִינּוּן. אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ. אִין. אֲמַר לוֹן. וָאַרִימִיתוּן דְּמַיִין. אָמְרוּן לֵיהּ. לֹא כֵן אַלְפָּן רִבִּי. הַלּוֹקֵחַ זֶרַע לִבְהֵמָה קֶמַח לְעוֹרוֹת שֶׁמֶן לָאוֹר פָּטוּר מִן הַדְּמַאי. אֲמַר לוֹן. מַה נַעֲבִיד לָהָדָא עֲלִיבְתָא דְּהִיא מַחְמְרָה עָל גַּרְמָהּ סַגִּין. וָאַרִימוֹן דְּמַיִין וְאָכְלָת.
>
> **Translation:** Robbers stole the she-ass of Rebbi Phineas ben Yair at night. She was hidden with them for three days during which she did not eat anything. After three days, they took counsel to return her. They said, remove her lest she die with us. They let her out; she returned to her master's door and started braying. He said to them, open to that poor creature because for three days now she did not taste anything. They opened for her and she entered. He said to them, give her something to eat. They put oats before her but she did not eat. They said to him, rabbi, she does not want to eat. He asked them, did you put it in order? They said: Yes. He asked them, did you remove its *demay*? They said, did the rabbi not teach us: "He who buys seeds for animals, flour for tanning, oil for lighting, is free from *demay*"? He said to them, what can we do with this poor creature since she is very restrictive for herself? They removed the *demay* and she ate.

*Chamortei d'Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair, ganvuha listim b'leilya* — the donkey of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair was stolen by bandits in the middle of the night. *Avdat temira gavon tlata yomin* — they kept him hidden with them for three days. *V'lo ta'ama klum* — and the donkey refused to eat anything. After three days, *amru l'machzara* — they decided to return him. They did not want to have a dead donkey on their hands and then be found out. *Afikuha, azlat v'kamat lah al tar'a d'mareh* — they let him go, and he went and stood at the door of his master's home, braying. Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair said to those around him: *pitchun l'hada aluvta* — open up the door for this forsaken one. *D'it lah tlata yomin d'lo ta'ama klum* — it has gone three days without eating anything. He knew that his donkey would not eat away from home.

They opened the door and brought the donkey in. Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair said: *yehavun lah klum te'echal* — give him something to eat. They gave him barley, *v'lo ba'at l'meichal* — and he refused to eat it. They reported this to Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair. He asked: *metaknin inun* — have you properly prepared it, removing the stones and chaff? They said yes. He asked further: *v'harimtun demai* — have you separated the *demai*? The barley had been purchased from an *am ha'aretz*, and while one is normally permitted to feed an animal *demai* without separating *terumot u'ma'asrot* — since the obligation is only rabbinic and feeding animals is more lenient — they had indeed assumed they could do so. But Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair had taught them a *halacha*: *halokeiach zera lebeheimah* — if one purchases *demai* for planting, or for feeding animals, or flour for tanning leather, or oil for a lamp, *patur min hademai* — one is exempt from separating *terumas ma'aser*. So they had assumed, correctly by the letter of the law, that they could feed the donkey without separating.

### Donkey machmir

Yet the donkey refused. Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair's response is remarkable: *mai na'avid lah leha aluvta, de'ee machmera agarmah al atzmah* — what can we do for this poor creature? It is *machmir* on itself. The donkey, of its own accord, was applying a standard beyond what was halachically required of it. The wordplay is delightful: a *chamor* that is *machmir*. And the word *atzmah* — itself, literally "its bone" — echoes the Aramaic *garma*, also meaning bone and also meaning oneself, a linguistic coincidence that the *gemara* savors. They went, separated the *demai*, and then the donkey ate. Which also explains, of course, why it had refused to eat during its three days with the bandits — they were surely feeding it *tevel* and *gezel*. The donkey of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair was more scrupulous in its observance than we are. That is the point.

## Petachiah mysteries

### Petachiah greatness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹא וּרְאֵה מַה גָדוֹל הוּא כוֹחוֹ שֶׁלְאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ, פּוֹתֵחַ דְבָרִים וְדוֹרְשָׁן וְיוֹדֵעַ בְּשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן. תַּנֵּי. סַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׁנַיִם שֶׁיּוֹדְעִין לְדַבֵּר וְכוּלָּן רְאוּיִין לִשְׁמוֹעַ הֲרֵי זוֹ רְאוּיָה לְסַנְהֶדְרִין. שְׁלֹשָׁה הֲרֵי זוֹ בֵינוֹנִית. אַרְבָּעָה הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲכָמָה. וּבְיַבְנֶה הָיוּ בָהּ אַרְבָּעָה. בֶּן עַזַּאי וּבֶן זוֹמָא בֶּן חֲכִינַאִי וְרִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן מַתְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Come and see how great the power of this man was, for he "had original ideas and explained them, and he knew seventy languages. It was stated: A Synhedrion of which two know how to speak and all of them are able to hear is acceptable as Synhedrion. Three is average, four is wise, and in Jabneh there were four, Ben Azzai and Ben Zoma, Ben Ḥanikai and Rebbi Eleazar ben Matthew.

The *gemara* returns now to Petachia from the *mishnah*: *bo u'rei mah gadol hu kocho shel oso ish, poteiach devarim vedorsham* — come and see how great was the power of that man; he could open up hidden things and explicate them. And he knew *shiv'im lashon*, seventy languages.

### Sanhedrin languages

A *baraita* is cited about the language requirements for a *sanhedrin*: *sanhedrin she-yesh bo shnayim she-yecholim ledaber v'khulam reuyim lishmoa* — if among the seventy members of a *sanhedrin* two can speak all languages and the rest can at least understand them, *harei zu reuyah le-Sanhedrin*, that is the minimum for a valid *sanhedrin*. If three can speak all seventy languages, that is an average *sanhedrin*. Four speakers of all languages constitutes a *wise* *sanhedrin*.

### Yavneh scholars

*U'veYavneh hayu vo daled* — and at Yavneh there were four: Ben Azzai, Ben Zoma, Ben Chakhinai, and Rabbi Elazar ben Masya. These four could speak all seventy languages, making the *sanhedrin* of Yavneh a *chakhmah*, a wise one.

### Drought & omer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא. פַּעַם אַחַת יָבְשָׁה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא יָדְעוּ מֵהֵיכָן לְהָבִיא. וַהֲוָה תַמָּן חַד אִלֵּם דַּהֲוַה יְהִיב חַד יָדֵיהּ עַל גַּגּוֹת וְחָדָא יָדֵיהּ עַל צְרִיפָא. אַיְתוּנֵיהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּפְתַחָא. אֲמַר לָהוּ. אִי אֲתַר דְּמִיתְקָרֵי גַגּוֹת צְרִיפִין אוֹ צְרִיפִין גַּגּוֹת. אָזְלִין לְתַּמָּן וְאַשְׁכְּחוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said, once the Land of Israel was dry and they did not know from where to bring. There was there a mute person who put one hand on roofs and another on a shed. They brought him before Petaḥiah, who told them, is there a place called *Gaggot-Ṣerifin* or *Ṣerifin-Gaggot*? They went there and found.

Rav Chisda then relates: *pa'am achat yavsha Eretz Yisrael* — once there was a drought in the Land of Israel, and they did not know from where to bring the *omer*. There was a mute person who put one hand on a rooftop and one on the top of a lean-to structure. They brought him before Petachia. Petachia asked: is there a place called *Gagot Tzrifim* or *Tzrifim Gagot*? They went there and found grain suitable for the *omer*. Petachia had interpreted the mute person's mime as indicating a place name.

### Blight story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה בֵּירִבִּי בּוּן. פַּעַם אַחַת נִשְׂדַף כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ וְלֹא הָיוּ יוֹדְעִין מֵאֵיכָן לְהָבִיא. וַהֲוָוה תַמָּן חַד אִילֵּם וַהֲוָה יְהִיב יָדֵיהּ עַל עֵיְינֵיהּ וְיָדֵ͏יהּ עַל סוֹכְרָא. אַיְיתוּנֵיהּ גַּבֵּי פְתַחְיָה. אֲמַר לוֹן. אִית אֲתַר דְּמִיתְקָרֵי עֵין סוֹכֵר אוֹ סוֹכֵר עַיִן. וְאָזְלוֹן תַּמָּן וְאַשְׁכְּחוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Abun said, once the entire world was burned and they did not know from where to bring. There was there a mute person who put one of his hands over his eyes and one hand on a door lock. They brought him before Petaḥiah, who told them, is there a place called *`Ein-Sokher* or *Sokher-Ayin*? They went there and found.

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Bun tells a similar story: *pa'am achat nishtadef kol ha-olam kulo* — once there was a blight on the entire world, not only in Israel, and they again did not know where to find grain for the *omer*. A mute person placed one hand over his eyes and the other over the opening of a well. They brought him before Petachia, who asked: is there a place called *Ein Sokher* or *Sokher Ein*? They went there and found what they needed.

### Three women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים הֵבִיאוּ קִינֵּיהֶן. אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת. לְעֵינָתִי. וְְְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת לְיַמָּתִי. וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת. לְזִיבָתִי. זוֹ שֶׁאָמְרָה. לְעֵינָתִי. סָבְרִין מֵימַר. שׁוֹפַעַת כְּמַעֲיָין. אֲמַר לוֹן. בְּעֵיְינָהּ סְכָנָת. זוֹ שֶׁאָמְרָה. לְיַמָּתִי. סָבְרִין מֵימַר. שׁוֹפַעַת כַּיָּם. אֲמַר לוֹן. בְּיַמָּהּ סְכָנָת. זוֹ שֶׁאָמְרָה. לְזִיבָתִי. סָבְרִין מֵימַר. זָבָה מַמָּשׁ. אֲמַר לוֹן. זְאֵב בָּא לִיטּוֹל אֶת בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Three women brought their nests. One said, לְעֵינָתִי; and one said, לְיַמָּתִי, and one said, לְזִיבָתִי. About the one who said לְעֵינָתִי, they wanted to say, her menstrual flow is like a well. He said, she was dangerously sick in her eye. About the one who said לְיַמָּתִי, they wanted to say, her menstrual flow is like the sea. He said, she was in danger on the sea. About the one who said לְזִיבָתִי, they wanted to say, she was really a *zavah*. He told them, a wolf came to snatch her son.

Then: *Gimel nashim heviu kineihan* — three women brought their *kinim* to Petachia, who oversaw the bird offerings. This story illuminates, from a different angle, why someone of Petachia's unique abilities was needed for that post. The challenge was not only the logistical complexity of birds getting mixed up — the subject of an entire tractate *Kinnim* — but also the problem of discerning the *intent* behind the offerings brought.

### Eye danger

The first woman said *le-einati* — for my *einati*. *Savrin lemeimar shofat ke-ma'ayan* — the natural reading was that she had experienced a flow like a wellspring, meaning she had bled outside her regular menstrual period and was a *zavah*, who must bring a *korban*. Petachia said: *be-aynah sakanta* — no, she means she was in danger in her eye, her life was at risk, and she is bringing this as a *nedavah*, a voluntary offering. The difference is halachically significant: a *zavah* brings a *chatat* and an *olah*; a *nedavah* is simply an *olah*.

### Sea danger

The second woman said *le-yamati* — for my *yamati*. Again the obvious reading was *shofat ke-yam*, that she had a flow like an ocean, making her a *zavah*. Petachia said: *be-yama sakanta* — no, she was endangered at sea, and this too is a *nedavah*.

### Wolf danger

The third woman said *le-zivati* — for my flow. This one seemed unambiguous: *zavah mamash*, she is plainly a *zavah*. Yet Petachia said: *ze'ev ba litol et benah* — a wolf came to take her child, she was saved, and this too is a *nedavah*. In each case Petachia was reading something — body language, context, demeanor — that allowed him to reinterpret what appeared on the surface to be a declaration of *zavah* status as something else entirely. This is the gift that made him indispensable: the ability to open what was closed, to see what others could not perceive.

## Ben Achiah health

### Stomach ailments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן אֲחִיָיה עַל חוֹלֵי מֵיעַיִם. עַל יְדֵי שֶׁהָיוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים מְהַלְּכִין יְחֵיפִים עַל הָרִצְפָּה וְהָיוּ אוֹכְלִים בָּשָׂר וְשׁוֹתִין מַיִם הָיוּ בָאִים לִידֵי חוֹלֵי מֵיעַיִם. וַהֲוָה יְדַע אֲהֵיי דֵין חֲמַר טַב לִמְעַיָיא. וְהֵיי דֵין חֲמַר סְמַס לִמְעַיָיא.
>
> **Translation:** "Ben Aḥiyyah for indigestion." Since the Cohanim were walking barefoot on the floor, and were eating meat, and drinking water, they got indigestion. He knew which wine was good for the intestines and which was noxious for intestines.

*Ben Achiya* oversaw *cholei me'ayim* — digestive ailments among the *kohanim*. The *gemara* now explains why this was such a persistent problem.

### Diet problems

*Al yedei she-hayu ha-kohanim holchim yecheifim al ha-ritzpah* — because the *kohanim* went barefoot on the stone floors, and they had to consume enormous quantities of meat from the *korbanot* — all the *chatat* and *asham* offerings that were brought. Apparently in the *Beis HaMikdash* they drank only water, no other beverage being available to them. This combination — cold stone floors underfoot, vast meat consumption, and water as the sole drink — led chronically to *cholei me'ayim*, stomach ailments.

### Wine remedies

*Va-havei yeda* — and Ben Achiya knew which wine would be good for the stomach, whether for settling it or for acting as a laxative, and which wine would have the opposite effect. He had, in other words, a practical pharmacological expertise in applying the right wine to the specific condition the *kohein* was suffering from. The role was as medically substantive as any other on the list.

## Nechunya wells

### Well digging

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְחוּנְיָיה חוֹפֵר שִׁיחִין. שֶׁהָיָה חוֹפֵר שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת. וַהֲוָה יְדַע הֵיי דֵין כֵּיף מְקוֹרֵר מַיָא וְהֵיי דֵין כֵּיף אִית בֵּיהּ שַׁרְבְּרוּבֵי וְעַד מַטֵּי הֵן שַׁרְבְּרוּבִיתֵיהּ מַטָיָיה.
>
> **Translation:** "Onias the digger of ditches." He was digging ditches and caves and knew which rock cools the water and which rock was dry and how far its dryness extended.

*Nechunya chofer shichin* — Nechunya dug wells. *She-hayah chofer shichin u-me'arot* — he would dig wells and caves and other excavations to ensure access to water for the *Beis HaMikdash* and for people traveling the roads.

### Cold water

*Va-havei yeda hai dein keif mekorar* — he knew which type of rock, if you dug into it, would yield cold water, the kind of cold underground water that was needed.

### Hot water

And he knew *sharvruri* — where there were streams of hot water running through the earth, and how far down those streams ran.

### Son's death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי אָחָא. וּמֵת [בְּנוֹ] בַצָּמָא. אָמַר רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. מָאן דְּאָמַר דְּרַחֲמָנָא וַותְרָן יִתְוַותְרוּן בְּנֵי מָעוֹי. אֶלָּא מַאֲרִיךְ רוּחֵיהּ וְגָבֵי דִידֵיהּ. אָמַר רִבִּי אָחָא כְּתִיב וּסְבִיבָיו נִשְׂעֲרָה מְאֹד. מְדַקְדֵּק עִמָּהֶן כְּחוּט הַשְּׂעָרָה. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה. לֹא מִטַּעַם הַזֶּה. אֶלָּא מִן מַה דִכְתִיב וְנוֹרָא [הוּא] עַל כָּל סְבִיבָיו. מוֹרָאוֹ עַל הַקְּרוֹבִים יוֹתֵר מִן הָרְחוֹקִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Aḥa said, but [his son] died of thirst. Rebbi Ḥanina said, anybody who says that the All-Merciful is indulgent, his intestines shall dissolve themselves; for He is forbearing and then collects His due. Rebbi Aḥa said, it is written, *His surroundings are very hairy*. He is exact with them like a hair's width. Rebbi Yose said, not because of this reason, but because what is written, *He is awesome on His surroundings*, His fear on those near Him is greater than those far away.

*Amar Rabbi Eliezer: umet bno btzama* — Rabbi Eliezer observes: and yet Nechunya's son died of thirst. This is the theological shock at the heart of the passage. Here was a man whose entire life's work was ensuring that others had water, and his own son died without it.

### Divine justice

The *gemara* responds with the famous declaration of Rabbi Chanina: *man de'amar Rachmana vatran, yivaterun bnei me'ohi* — whoever says that God is a *vatran*, that He simply lets things slide and does not ultimately exact what is owed, his own stomach should be let go in kind. The point is not to be taken literally but to underscore with force: God is not a *vatran*. There is *middas hadin*. *Ela ma'arich ruchyei, vegabe di'dei* — God may be *erech apayim*, He may extend the time before collecting, but ultimately He collects what is owed. The answer to the theological problem, on this account, is that we must believe in *cheshbon shamayim* even when we cannot follow it. There is a just accounting; we simply do not always see how it works.

### God's account

What this means practically is that when someone appears to be flourishing despite their sins, one should not conclude that God has simply forgiven and forgotten. The patience of *erech apayim* is not the same as acquittal. God will ultimately collect. And correspondingly, when someone appears to be suffering despite their righteousness, one should not conclude that the suffering is unjust. There is an accounting we cannot always access.

### Hairbreadth measure

Rabbi Acha then offers a different angle through a *drasha*: the verse says *u-sevivav nis-ara me'od*, which literally means that around God there is a great whirlwind. But Rabbi Acha reads *nis-ara* from the root *se'ara*, hair: *medakdek imahem ke-chut ha-se'ara* — God is as precise with those who surround Him as a hairbreadth. The closer one is to God, the higher the standard to which one is held. A great *tzaddik* is measured with extraordinary precision — precisely because he is capable of being held to that standard, and precisely because his conduct reflects on how God is perceived by the world.

### Awe of closeness

Rabbi Yosi offers a distinct but related formulation. The verse *v'nora hu al kol sevivav* — "He is awesome upon all those who surround Him" — yields the interpretation: *mora-o al ha-krovim yoser min ha-rechokim* — His awe rests more heavily upon those who are close than upon those who are far. This is not simply a restatement of Rabbi Acha's point. Where Rabbi Acha emphasizes God's exacting precision as something imposed from without, Rabbi Yosi may be pointing to something that emerges from within the person of great closeness: the awe that one carries by virtue of proximity to the divine creates its own inner demand for a higher standard. The exact conceptual distinction between these two positions is subtle, but the difference may be between an externally imposed accountability and an internally generated one — the *tzaddik*'s own heightened consciousness of the divine, which demands more of him than the ordinary person's relationship to God demands of them.

One of the commentators notes a further dimension to the Nechunya story: because Nechunya was so deeply invested in the area of water — it was his defining virtue — the standard applied to him *within that domain* was extraordinarily high. Even a minor failing in precisely that area would be held against him with the precision of *ke-chut ha-se'ara*. This would explain not just that he was punished, but why the punishment came in the very domain of his greatness — a dimension that connects naturally to *midah k'neged midah*.

## Chasid daughter

### Chasid tragedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חַגַּיי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן. מַעֲשֶׂה בְחָסִיד אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה חוֹפֵר בּוֹרוֹת שֵׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת לָעוֹבְרִים וְלַשָּׁבִים. פַּעַם אַחַת הָיְתָה בִתּוֹ עוֹבֶרֶת לְהִינָּשֵׂא וּשְׁטָפָהּ נָהָר. וַהֲווֹן כָּל עַמָּא עָלְלִין לְגַבֵּיהּ בְּעַיָין מְנַחַמְתֵּיהּ וְלָא קִבֵּל עֲלוֹי מִתְנַחֲמָה. עָאַל רִבִּי פִינְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר לְגַבֵּיהּ בָּעֵי מְנַחַמְתֵּיהּ וְלָא קִבֵּל עֲלוֹי מִתְנַחֲמָה. אֲמַר לוֹן. דֵּין הִינּוֹ חָסִידֵיכוֹן. אָמְרִין לֵיהּ. רִבִּי. כָּךְ וְכָךְ הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה וְכָךְ וְכָךְ אִירַע. אָמַר. אֵיפְשַׁר שֶׁהָיָה מְכַבֵּד אֶת בּוֹרְאוֹ בַמַּיִם וְהוּא מְקַפְּחוֹ בַמַּיִם. מִיַּד נָפְלָה הֲבָרָה בָעִיר. בָּאָת בִּתּוֹ שֶׁלְאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ. אִית דְּאָמְרִין. בְּשׁוּכְתָּא אִיתְעֲרִיַית. וְאִית דְּאָמְרִין. מַלְאַךְ יָרַד כִּדְמוּת רִבִּי פִינְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר וְהִצִּילָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥaggai in the name of Rebbi Samuel bar Naḥman: It happened that a pious person used to dig cisterns, ditches, and caves for travellers. Once, his daughter travelled to marry and she was swept away by a river. All the people came to him and wanted to console him, but he refused to be consoled. Rebbi Phineas ben Yair came to him and wanted to console him, but he refused to be consoled. He told them, is that your pious man? They said to him: Rabbi, such and such he did, such and such happened to him. He replied: It is impossible that he would honor his Creator by water and He hurts him by water! Immediately, there started a rumor in the town that the man's daughter had returned. Some say, she got trapped in a hedge of thorns, others say that an angel came in the appearance of Rebbi Phineas ben Yair and saved her.

Rabbi Chagai in the name of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachman relates a *ma'aseh*: there was a *chasid* who throughout his life would dig wells, pits, and caves to provide water for travelers. On his daughter's wedding day, she was washed away by a river. The tragic irony is structurally identical to the Nechunya story: a man of extraordinary virtue in the domain of water is struck precisely through water, and on the happiest day of his family's life.

### Refuses comfort

The entire community came to console him. He refused to accept consolation, like Yaakov Avinu before him — and as with Yaakov, the parallel invites attention, since Yaakov's inconsolable grief turned out to rest on a misapprehension. Here too the *chasid*'s refusal to be comforted carries within it an implicit *ta'ana* against God's justice. He was not merely grief-stricken; he was, at some level, refusing to accept that what had happened was right.

### Pinchas questions

Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair came to console him and was also refused. He turned to those present and asked: *dein hu chasid'chum*? This is your *chasid*? He who refuses consolation, who refuses to accept God's justice, who maintains a *ta'ana* against the divine — this is the man you call righteous? They explained to him the full story: what the man had done throughout his life with water, and how his daughter had died.

### Pinchas refuses

Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair's response is remarkable: *efshar she-hayah mekhabed es boro b'mayim v'hu mekape'ach b'mayim* — is it possible that this man honored his Creator with water, and now God is going to cut him off through water? Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair refused to accept this as true. His refusal was not the same as the father's refusal. The father refused consolation because he believed the tragedy had occurred and could not accept it as just. Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair refused to believe the tragedy had occurred at all in the way it appeared.

### Daughter alive

*Miyad naflah havarah ba'ir* — immediately a commotion arose in the city. The daughter appeared, alive.

### Two readings

*Ait d'amri b'suchsa istabchis* — some say she had grabbed a branch as she was swept along by the river and survived. *V'ait d'amri malach yarad kidmus Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair v'hitzilah* — and some say an angel descended in the likeness of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair and saved her. The two versions carry different implications. If she survived naturally — grabbed a branch, was carried to safety — then Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair's theological confidence is vindicated: God truly would not do this. His intervention was declaratory, not causal. If an angel descended in his likeness, then his intervention was itself the cause of her survival; she would have died without it, and it was his refusal to accept the injustice that changed the outcome.

### Responses differ

This story is in deliberate dialogue with the Nechunya story that preceded it. In both cases, a great man dedicated to providing water is struck through water. In the Nechunya story, the answer given is that God is not a *vatran* — the accounting always works out, even if we cannot follow it, and we must accept the result with faith. The *chasid*'s first response — inconsolable grief, refusal to accept, implicit *ta'ana* — represents one human posture before inexplicable suffering. Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair represents a different one: not acceptance of injustice as a fact, but refusal to accept that this could be God's will, and insistence on finding another explanation. The difference between the father and Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair is not that one was angrier or less faithful. It is that the father accepted the tragedy as real and then could not reconcile it with divine justice, while Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair would not accept it as real in the first place. He refused to believe that God would do such a thing to such a person.

## Gevini announcer

### Announcements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּבִינֵי כָרוֹז. שֶׁהָיָה מַכְרִיז בְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ. מֶה הָיָה אוֹמֵר. הַכֹּהֲנִים לָעֲבוֹדָה וְהַלְּוִיִּם לְדוּכָן וְיִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַעֲמָד. אַגְרִיפַּס הַמֶּלֶךְ שָׁמַע קוֹלוֹ עַד שְׁמוֹנֶה פַרְסָאוֹת וְנָתַן לוֹ מַתָּנוֹת הַרְבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** "Gabinius the herald." He was announcing in the Temple. What did he say? "The Cohanim to the service, and the Levites to the podium, and Israel to stand by." King Agrippas heard his voice at a distance of eight parasangs and gave him rich gifts.

*Gvini kruz* — Gevini was the *kruz*, the announcer of the *Beis HaMikdash*. *She-aya machriz b'Beis HaMikdash* — he would make announcements to call everyone to their positions. His call was: *Imdu HaKohanim l'avoda* — *kohanim*, to the *avodah*; *Leviim l'duchanam* — *levi'im*, to your stations for the *shira*; *Yisrael l'maamadam* — and Israel, to the *maamad*, the communal representation that stood witness to the *korban tamid*.

### Loud voice

*Agrippas HaMelech shama kolo ad cheis parsa'os* — King Agrippas heard his voice eight *parsa'os* away. One may reasonably assume some element of exaggeration in the account, but the point is clear: his voice was extraordinary in its power and reach. *V'nasnu lo matanos harbeh* — and the king gave him many gifts, presumably as an expression of admiration and encouragement.

## Ben Gever gates

### Gate locking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן גֶּבֶר עַל נְעִילַת שְׁעָרִים. תִּירְגֵּם רַב קוֹמֵי דְבֵית רִבִּי שִׁילָה. קָרָא גַבְרָא. אַכְרִיז כָּרוֹזָא. אָמְרוּן לֵיהּ. קָרָא תַּרְנוּגְלָא. אֲמַר לוֹן. וְהָתַנִּינָן בֶּן גֶּבֶר. אִית לָךְ מֵימַר בַּר תַּרְנוּגְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** "Ben Gever for locking the doors." Rav explained for the House of Rebbi Shila, "when *gever* called as "when the herald proclaimed." They said to him, "when the rooster called." He said to them, did we not state "Ben Gever"? Could you say, "the son of the rooster"?

*Ben Gever al ne'ilas she'arim* — Ben Gever oversaw the locking of the Temple gates, the *levi'im* being the ones who actually performed the task of guarding.

### Krias hagever

The *gemara* records that Rav Kumi of the school of Rav Shila translated *krias hagever* — the phrase used elsewhere to denote the time for the *trumas hadeshen* — as *kra gavra*, "the man called out," meaning the announcer's proclamation. This connects *krias hagever* to the *kruz*, the announcer, rather than to the crowing of a rooster. When challenged — why not translate it as *kra tarnagola*, "the rooster crowed," which is how *gever* is usually understood to function in that phrase? — the reply is: but our *mishnah* speaks of a *Ben Gever*, a man named Ben Gever. Would you then call him *bar tarnagola*, the son of a rooster? The joke lands, and the point is made: the translator preferred to render *gever* as a human being, in keeping with the *mishnah*'s use of the name, rather than as a rooster.

## Ben Babi charity

### Wick twisting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן בֵּבָי עַל הַפָּקִיעַ. שֶׁהָיָה מְזַוֵיג אֶת הַפְּתִילוֹת. רִבִּי יוֹסֵה עָאַל לְכוּפְרָה. בְּעָא מַמְנִיָיא עֲלֵיהוֹן פַּרְנָסִין וְלָא קִבְּלוּן מִינֵּיהּ. עָאַל וְאָמַר קוֹמֵיהוֹן. בֶּן בֵּבָי עַל הַפָּקִיעַ. וּמַה אִם זֶה שֶׁנִּתְמַנֶּה עַל הַפְּתִילֹות זָכָה לְהִימָּנוֹת עִם גְּדוֹלֵי הַדּוֹר. אַתֶּם שֶׁאַתֶּם מִתְמַנִּין עַל חַיֵּי נְפָשׁוֹת לֹא כָל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** "Ben Bavai over the oakum, he was threading the wicks. Rebbi Yose went up to Kufra and wanted to appoint providers there, but they did not accept. He came and said before them: "Ben Bavai over the oakum." If this one had been appointed over the wicks and therefore merited to be counted with the leaders of his generation, you who are being appointed over the lives of people, so much more.

*Ben Babi al hapaki'a* — Ben Babi oversaw the *pakiya*, which was the twisting of the wicks, the *ptilos* used in the *menorah*.

### Parnasin refused

The *gemara* then relates that Rabbi Yosi came to the town of *kuphra* and sought to appoint *parnasin* — administrators for *tzedaka* distribution, people who would oversee how charity funds were managed and dispersed to the poor. They refused to accept the appointment.

### Parnasin honor

Rabbi Yosi addressed them: *zachah limanos im gedolei hador* — Ben Babi merited to be counted among the great ones of his generation in the *mishnah*, and he oversaw the twisting of wicks — not a task of monumental significance compared to the others listed. *Atem she-atem nimnaim al chayei nefashos* — you, by contrast, would be appointed to matters of life and death. The poor depend on those who administer charity. *Vechol sheken* — how much more, then, should you merit to be counted among the great. The argument is that the responsibility of *parnasin* is not a burden to be avoided but an honor that dwarfs many of the celebrated Temple roles; those who administer the welfare of the destitute are engaged in something of ultimate human importance.

## Ben Arza chimes

### Signal chimes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן אַרְזָה עַל הַצִּלְצָל. כַּהִיא דְתַנִּינָן תַּמָּן. הֵנִיף הַסְּגָן בַּסּוּדָרִים וְהִקִּישׁ בֶּן אַרְזָא בַּצִּלְצָל.
>
> **Translation:** "Ben Arza for the cymbal;" as we have stated there, "the executive officer waves a sheet and Ben Arza plays the cymbal."

*Ben Arza al hatziltzal* — Ben Arza oversaw the chimes, or the like of a triangle or gong, that served as the signal for the *levi'im* to begin their music. The *gemara* cites the relevant procedure: when the *tamid* offering was being brought, the *sagan* — the deputy High Priest — would wave with cloths at a distance to signal readiness. *V'heikish ben Arza al hatziltzal* — Ben Arza would then strike the chimes. And with that signal, the *levi'im* would commence their singing and instrumental music at the precise moment the offering was brought.

## Hugras song

### Choir overseer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּגְדַּס בֶּן לִֵוי עַל הַשִּׁיר. אָמַר רִבִּי אָחָא. נְעִימָה יְתֵירָה הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ. וְאָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל הוּגְדַּס בֶּן לֵוִי שֶׁהָיָה מַנְעִים אֶת קוֹלוֹ בַזֶּמֶר. וּכְשֶׁהָיָה נוֹעֵץ גּוֹדְלוֹ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו הָיָה מוֹצִיא כַמָּה מִינֵי זֶמֶר והָיוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים נִזְקָרִין לוֹ בַת רֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** "Hugdas ben Levi for the song." Rebbi Aḥa said, he knew a special melody. When he put his thumb in his mouth he produced all kinds of sounds and the heads of his fellow Levites were taken aback.

*Hugras ben Levi al hashir* — Hugras ben Levi, who was himself of Levitical lineage, oversaw the *shir*, the actual choral singing of the *levi'im*.

### Beautiful melody

*Amar ava neima yeseira haya yodeia* — it was said that he possessed an exceptionally beautiful melody. *V'amru alav al Hugras ben Levi she-haya mane'ima es kolo bezemer* — he was able to make his voice extraordinarily melodious in song, and to accompany himself with music.

### Kohanim amazed

*U-khshehaya no'es gudalo besoch piv, haya motzi kama minei zemer* — when he would place his thumb inside his mouth, he could produce many different types of remarkable sounds — something akin to a complex form of whistling or vocalization that allowed him to generate multiple voices or tones simultaneously. *V'hayu kol ha-kohanim nizrakim lo bevat rosh* — and all the *kohanim* would be startled, taken aback, by the sheer power and beauty of his music. The word *nizrakim* conveys something close to being physically moved or jolted by the experience. It is a fitting note on which to pause, capturing as it does the living reality of what the *Beis HaMikdash* was at its best: a place where the most gifted people dedicated their most extraordinary talents to the service of God.
