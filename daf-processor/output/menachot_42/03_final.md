# Menachot 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzitzit Construction

### Folding & Knotting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב רָמֵי אַרְבַּע, וְעָיֵיף לְהוּ מֵיעָף, וּמְעַיֵּיל לְהוּ בִּגְלִימָא, וְאָבֵיק לְהוּ מֵיבָק. קָסָבַר: בָּעֵינַן תְּמָנְיָא בִּגְלִימָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֶיהְוֵי ״גְּדִיל״ ״גְּדִילִים״ בִּמְקוֹם פְּתִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov would affix four** strings to the garment, **and he would** first **fold them** in half **and** then **insert them** at the point of the folds **into** the hole of **the garment,** so that on one side there were eight strings and on the other side there were four loops. He would then take the eight strings **and loop them** through the four loops and pull them tight, thereby attaching them to the garment. The Gemara explains that **he held** that **we require eight** strings when they are initially placed **in the garment, in order that there be twisted cord** and **"twisted cords"** (Deuteronomy 22:12), i.e., four doubled strings, **at the place,** i.e., the time, when he creates the **loose hanging string.**

The daf opens mid-discussion with Rav Acha bar Yaakov's method of constructing *tzitzit*. The Gemara records: *Rav Acha bar Yaakov rami arba ve-ayitz lehu mi-yayitz* — he placed four strings of *tzitzit* and folded them over, then inserted them into his garment (*u-meilo biglimei*) and knotted them (*va-avid lehu mivak*). The description suggests that he folded the strings before threading them through the hole — that is, he passed the folded loop through the opening, and then drew the loose ends through the loop itself, thereby creating a knot automatically. This contrasts with our contemporary practice, in which the open ends are passed through the hole first, the string folds over, and then a separate strand is used to tie the knot.

The Gemara explains Rav Acha bar Yaakov's reasoning: *kasavar ba'inan tamnaya deglimei* — he holds that eight strings are required in the garment. By inserting four strings doubled over, one achieves eight, fulfilling *gedil gedilim bi-mkom petil* — the requirement of *gedil gedilim* (derived from the linguistic doubling of the word), while the strings remain in the state of loose thread.

### Rambam's Latitude

This passage is the last in a series of *Gemarot* addressing the precise number of strings and manner of knotting, and it is striking how wide a range of possibilities the Gemara entertains — including three strings, four strings, and various other *shiurim* — without citing explicit *psukim* and without clearly declaring the alternative positions *pasul*. This range of opinions underlies the Rambam's position, which may come as a surprise: the Rambam maintains that *min ha-Torah* no specific number of strings is mandated, and the exact method of construction is similarly unspecified. What the Torah fundamentally requires is a recognizable fringe — some configuration of loose strings, *krichot* (wrappings), and knots — without fixing the precise quantities of each. This reflects the breadth of opinion in the Gemara itself, where the absence of firm *pesukim* and definitive rulings of *pesul* suggests genuine latitude.

### Talmud Range

Of course, not everyone accepts this reading. Many authorities understand that a specific configuration is required, and that while the Rambam may restrict the *de-Oraita* obligation to broad parameters, the normative ruling (*pesak*) follows a very precise method — four strings doubled over to eight. At the same time, even those who hold that the exact construction is fixed acknowledge that there are further criteria governing the *process* of making *tzitzit*, as the Gemara proceeds to discuss: whether the strings must be spun *lishmah*, whether the blue dye must be produced *lishmah*, and so on. So while the Rambam grants latitude on certain dimensions *min ha-Torah*, it is not a blanket freedom, and the subsequent passages introduce meaningful constraints on how the *tzitzit* must be produced.

## Blessing on Making

### Rav Nachman's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה רָמֵי חוּטֵי, וְקָא מְבָרֵךְ ״לַעֲשׂוֹת צִיצִית״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי ״צִיצִי״ [דְּקָא] שָׁמַעְנָא? הָכִי אָמַר רַב: צִיצִית אֵין צְרִיכָה בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman found Rav Adda bar Ahava affixing strings** to a garment **and reciting the blessing** that concludes: **To prepare ritual fringes [*tzitzit*].** Rav Naḥman **said to** Rav Adda bar Ahava: **What** is this ***tzitzi*** sound that **I hear? This is what Rav says: Ritual fringes do not require a blessing** when one attaches them to the garment.

The Gemara introduces a new topic with the account of Mar brei de-Ravina, who followed the standard practice — according to Rashi, four strings doubled over to eight. Then Rav Nachman found Rav Adda bar Ahava in the act of inserting *tzitzit* strings into his garment (*rami chutei*), and as he did so, Rav Adda was reciting a blessing: *asher kideshanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu la'asot tzitzit* — the blessing "to make *tzitzit*." Rav Nachman challenged him: *mai tzitzit shamina hacha* — what sort of blessing are you making here? You are not yet wearing the garment.

### Rav's Position

Rav Nachman then invokes a ruling: *hachi amar Rav — tzitzit ein tzarich beracha* — Rav holds that no blessing is recited at the moment of making the *tzitzit*, only when one actually wears the garment. As will become clear, this ruling is deeply intertwined with the broader question of the nature of the *mitzvah* — whether it is an obligation upon the *person* (*chovas gavra*) or upon the *garment* (*chovas mana*).

### Chisda's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, עָל רַב חִסְדָּא לְמִירְמֵא דְּרַב אַדְּרַב, וּמִי אָמַר רַב: צִיצִית אֵין צָרִיךְ בְּרָכָה? וְהָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לְצִיצִית בְּגוֹי שֶׁהִיא פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְעָשׂוּ לָהֶם צִיצִת״ – בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יַעֲשׂוּ וְלֹא הַגּוֹיִם יַעֲשׂוּ!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this statement of Rav, the Gemara relates that **when Rav Huna died, Rav Ḥisda went into** the study hall **to raise a contradiction** from one statement **of Rav to** another statement **of Rav,** as follows: **Did Rav** actually **say** that **ritual fringes do not require a blessing** when one attaches them to the garment? **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived that **ritual fringes** attached **by a gentile** are **unfit?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "Speak unto the children of Israel** and command them **that they prepare for themselves strings"** (Numbers 15:38). The Sages derive from here that **the children of Israel shall prepare** ritual fringes, **but the gentiles shall not prepare** ritual fringes.

When Rav Huna died, Rav Chisda arrived and posed a contradiction against Rav from Rav himself. Did Rav not rule — as transmitted by Rav Yehuda in Rav's name — that *tzitzit* made by a non-Jew are invalid? The source is the verse: *daber el Bnei Yisrael ve-asu lahem tzitzit* — "speak to the children of Israel and they shall make for themselves *tzitzit*" — from which we derive *Bnei Yisrael ya'asu ve-lo ovdei kochavim*: only Israelites may make valid *tzitzit*, not non-Jews. If the process of making *tzitzit* is restricted to Jews, argues Rav Chisda, then the manufacturing itself is a meaningful act — and one should therefore recite a blessing when performing it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מַאי רוּמְיָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: קָסָבַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁירָה בְּגוֹי – בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁפְּסוּלָה בְּגוֹי – בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But what** is the **contradiction** between these two statements of Rav? **Rav Yosef said: Rav Ḥisda held** that in the case of **any mitzva** for **which** the necessary item is **fit** when produced **by a gentile,** if it is produced **by a Jew, he does not need to recite a blessing.** Conversely, **any mitzva** for **which** the necessary item is **unfit** when produced **by a gentile,** if it is produced **by a Jew, he needs to recite a blessing** when he produces the item.

The Gemara first asks what kind of contradiction this actually is. Rav Yosef explains the underlying principle assumed by Rav Chisda: *kol mitzvah she-hechshera be-oved kochavim, be-Yisrael ein tzarich levarech* — any *mitzvah* object that a non-Jew could validly produce requires no blessing when a Jew produces it; conversely, *kol mitzvah she-pesula be-oved kochavim, be-Yisrael tzarich levarech* — if a non-Jew's involvement disqualifies the object, then when a Jew performs the act, a blessing is in order.

The logic behind this principle, according to Tosafot, is textual: you cannot say *asher tzivanu* — "Who commanded us" — if a non-Jew could also have done the same act, since the act would not be distinctively the fulfilment of a divine commandment. This explanation, however, is not entirely satisfying. First, it does not explain why a blessing is appropriate now at all if the *mitzvah* has not yet been performed. Second, God did in fact command Israel — the non-Jew's technical ability does not eliminate Israel's specific mandate.

The deeper question is the extent to which the *act of making* a *mitzvah* object constitutes a *mitzvah* act in its own right. The Netziv's general approach to *hechsherei mitzvah* that are encoded in the Torah itself is relevant here: when the Torah itself speaks of "making" the *mitzvah* object, the process of making may itself be a *mitzvah* act. There is perhaps no clearer example than *tzitzit*, where the *psukim* (*ve-asu lahem tzitzit*, *gedilim ta'aseh lach*) are all formulated around the act of making. When the Torah restricts who may perform this act — excluding non-Jews — it signals that the process matters, not just the end result. And when the process matters, a blessing is in order. This, then, is the force of Rav Chisda's challenge: since Rav himself rules that a non-Jew cannot make valid *tzitzit*, why would Rav then say no blessing is recited?

## Gmar Mitzvah Principle

### Sukkah Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוּכָּה מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ, תְּפִילִּין הָוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ.
> 
> הֲרֵי סוּכָּה דִּכְשֵׁירָה בְּגוֹי, דְּתַנְיָא: סוּכַּת גּוֹיִם, סוּכַּת נָשִׁים, סוּכַּת בְּהֵמָה, סוּכַּת כּוּתִיִּים, סוּכָּה מִכׇּל מָקוֹם – כְּשֵׁירָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא מְסוּכֶּכֶת כְּהִילְכָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that the *halakha* with regard to **a *sukka* supports** the opinion of Rav Ḥisda, who holds that when an item used for a mitzva can be created by a gentile, a Jew who creates it does not recite a blessing. And the *halakha* with regard to **phylacteries is a conclusive refutation of his** opinion.
>
> The Gemara explains: **A *sukka* is fit** even if it was built **by a gentile, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a booth** built by **gentiles, a booth** built by **women, a booth for domesticated animals, a booth** built by **Samaritans, a booth of any sort,** each is **fit** for use as a *sukka*, **provided that it is roofed in accordance with the *halakha*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּה לְעַצְמוֹ אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם שֶׁהֶחֱיָינוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה״, בָּא לֵישֵׁב בָּהּ אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לֵישֵׁב בַּסּוּכָּה״, וְאִילּוּ ״לַעֲשׂוֹת סוּכָּה״ לָא מְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if a *sukka* was built **by a Jew, he is not required to recite a blessing** upon its construction, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who constructs a *sukka* for himself recites: Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time.** When **he comes to sit in** the *sukka*, **he recites: Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has sanctified us through His mitzvot and commanded us to reside in the *sukka*.** The Gemara notes that the *baraita* indicates that he recites a blessing at the time of construction, **whereas he does not recite a blessing** including the words: **To construct a *sukka*,** which confirms the opinion of Rav Ḥisda.

*Sukkah* appears to support the principle. A *sukkah* may be built by a non-Jew, by women, by animals, or by Samaritans — *sukkah mi-kol makom kesheira*: however it is built, so long as it provides the proper shade (*u-vilvad she-tehei mesukekes ke-hilchasah*), it is valid. The validity is entirely about the end result — a shade structure — and the identity or intent of the builder is entirely irrelevant. Accordingly, when a Jew builds a *sukkah*, no blessing is recited over the building.

A *Tosefta* confirms this: *ha-oseh sukkah le-atzmo* — one who builds a *sukkah* for himself — recites a *shehecheyanu*, since that blessing relates to the joyful anticipation of the upcoming *mitzvah*. But he does not recite the standard *birkat ha-mitzvah* of *asher kideshanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu*. That blessing is recited only upon sitting in the *sukkah* (*ba leishev, ba omer... leishev ba-sukkah*), not upon building it. This is entirely consistent: since the process of building does not matter — only the end result — there is nothing *mitzvah*-like about the act of building, and no blessing is warranted.

This analysis connects to a related principle: a *shehecheyanu* may be recited from the moment one begins preparing for a *Yom Tov* mitzvah — when one binds the *lulav*, or when one builds the *sukkah*. This explains why on the second day of *Yom Tov* we recite *shehecheyanu* in *kiddush* but do not repeat it for the *mitzvah* of *lulav*: the *shehecheyanu* for *kidushat ha-yom* can only be said when it is actually *Yom Tov*, so if yesterday was not *Yom Tov*, today's *kiddush* requires its own *shehecheyanu*. But the *shehecheyanu* for the *mitzvot hayom* may be recited from the moment one begins preparing — binding the *lulav* or building the *sukkah* — and having done so on the first day, one does not repeat it on the second.

### Tefillin Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּפִילִּין תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ, וַהֲרֵי תְּפִילִּין דִּפְסוּלוֹת בְּגוֹי, דְּתָנֵי רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִפַּשְׁרוּנְיָא: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, תְּפִילִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת שֶׁכְּתָבָן מִין, כּוּתִי, גּוֹי, עֶבֶד, אִשָּׁה, וְקָטָן, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד – פְּסוּלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּקְשַׁרְתָּם״ ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״, כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בִּקְשִׁירָה יֶשְׁנוֹ בִּכְתִיבָה, כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּקְשִׁירָה אֵינוֹ בִּכְתִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, the *halakha* with regard to **phylacteries** is **a conclusive refutation of** Rav Ḥisda's opinion. **Phylacteries are unfit** when written **by a gentile, as it is taught** by **Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rava, of Pashronya: A Torah scroll, phylacteries, or *mezuzot* that were written by a heretic, a Samaritan, a gentile,** a Canaanite **slave, a woman, a minor, or a Jewish apostate [*meshummad*] are unfit, as it is stated: "And you shall bind them** for a sign on your arm…**and you shall write them** on the doorposts of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:8–9). From this juxtaposition, one can derive the following: **Anyone who is** included **in** the mitzva of **binding** the phylacteries, i.e., one who is both obligated and performs the mitzva, **is** included **in** the class of people who may **write** Torah scrolls, phylacteries, and *mezuzot*; and **anyone who is not** included **in** the mitzva of **binding is not** included **in** the class of people who may **write** sacred texts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, דִּשְׁלַח רַב חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַל תְּפִילִּין שֶׁל יָד אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין״, עַל תְּפִילִּין שֶׁל רֹאשׁ אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל מִצְוַת תְּפִילִּין״, וְאִילּוּ ״לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּפִילִּין״ לָא מְבָרֵךְ!
>
> **Translation:** **And** despite the fact that phylacteries written by a gentile are unfit, **a Jew** who writes them **does not have to recite a blessing. As Rav Ḥiyya, son of Rav Huna, sent** a ruling **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: On phylacteries of the arm** one **says** the blessing: **Blessed** are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, **Who has sanctified us through His mitzvot and commanded us to don phylacteries. On phylacteries of the head** one **says** the blessing: **Blessed** are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, **Who has sanctified us through His mitzvot and commanded us concerning the mitzva of phylacteries.** The implication of this is that one recites blessings only when he dons the phylacteries, **whereas** when he writes the phylacteries he **does not recite a blessing: To prepare phylacteries.**

*Tefillin*, however, appear to contradict Rav Chisda's principle — and from the other direction. The Gemara cites a *Baraita*: a *Sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, or *mezuzah* written by a *tzadoki* (or *min*), a *kuti*, a non-Jew, a slave, a woman, or a minor are all invalid — *pesulim*. The source for this exclusion is the verse *u-kshartem u-chtavtam* — "you shall bind them and you shall write them" — from which the principle is derived: *kol she-yeshno bi-kshira yesh bi-ketiva* — whoever is obligated in the *mitzvah* of wearing *tefillin* is eligible to write *mezuzot* (and by extension, all sacred texts); *kol she-eno bi-kshira eno bi-ketiva* — whoever is exempt from *tefillin* cannot write these objects. Thus a broad category of people — non-Jews, women, minors, slaves, and those who have excluded themselves from the community — cannot write *STaM* (*Sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzah*).

This is precisely the kind of case Rav Chisda said should require a blessing: the process is restricted to eligible Jews, so when a Jew performs it, he should bless. Yet the Gemara states explicitly — on the authority of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted by Rabbi Chiya son of Rav Huna — that no blessing is recited over *making* *tefillin*. The blessing is recited only at the moment of wearing: *al tefillin shel yad* one says *le-haniach tefillin*, and *al tefillin shel rosh* one says *al mitzvas tefillin*. But *la'asot tefillin* — over manufacturing them — *lo mevarech*.

Here, then, is the problem with Rav Chisda's principle: process clearly matters for *tefillin* — a non-Jew cannot write them — and yet no blessing is recited. Does this mean that simply because the process matters, a blessing is not necessarily required? Several observations are worth noting. First, the invalidation of those who write *STaM* may reflect not a concern about the *cheftza shel mitzvah* (the *mitzvah* object itself), but rather about what constitutes *kitvei kodesh* — sacred scripture. The criteria for what makes a document genuinely *kitvei kodesh* may differ from the criteria for making a *mitzvah* object, and the former does not necessarily generate a *birkat ha-mitzvah*. Second, even if process matters, an additional indicator may be required before a blessing can be recited — namely, that the process itself is in some sense a *mitzvah* act, not merely a necessary precondition for a *mitzvah* act. This distinction becomes the key to the Gemara's resolution.

### Gmar Mitzvah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: כׇּל מִצְוָה דַּעֲשִׂיָּיתָהּ גְּמַר מִצְוָה, כְּגוֹן מִילָה, אַף עַל גַּב דִּכְשֵׁירָה בְּגוֹי – בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, וְכׇל מִצְוָה דַּעֲשִׂיָּיתָהּ לָאו גְּמַר מִצְוָה, כְּגוֹן תְּפִילִּין, אַף עַל גַּב דִּפְסוּלוֹת בְּגוֹי – בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't this the reason** for the distinction between different mitzvot: For **any mitzva whose performance is the completion of** the **mitzva, such as circumcision, even though it is valid** when performed **by a gentile,** when it is performed **by a Jew he must recite a blessing. But** for **any mitzva where the performance of** a particular act **is not the completion of** the **mitzva, such as** writing **phylacteries,** where one does not complete the mitzva until he dons them, **even though it is not valid** when performed **by a gentile,** when it is performed **by a Jew he does not need to recite a blessing.**

The Gemara now provides the definitive principle: it is not the question of Jewish versus non-Jewish involvement that determines whether a blessing is recited. The determining factor is *gmar mitzvah* — whether the act of making *completes* the *mitzvah*.

*Kol mitzvah de-asiyata gmar mitzvah* — any *mitzvah* in which the very act of performing it brings the *mitzvah* to completion — such as *mila* — the one performing it must recite a blessing, even in a case where a non-Jew could theoretically have done it. The act achieves the goal; it is the *mitzvah* itself. *Ve-chol mitzvah de-asiyata lav gmar mitzvah* — any *mitzvah* in which the act of making does not complete the *mitzvah* — such as *tefillin* or *sukkah* — even if a non-Jew cannot perform it, no blessing is recited at the manufacturing stage, because the maker has not yet fulfilled the *mitzvah*.

Surveying all the cases: when one builds a *sukkah*, one has not yet fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* — sitting in it is the *mitzvah*. When one writes a *mezuzah* or manufactures *tefillin*, one has not yet fulfilled the *mitzvah* — affixing and wearing are the *mitzvah*. In none of these cases is a blessing recited during manufacture. The sole exception is *brit mila*: when you perform the *mila*, you are done. That is the *mitzvah*. And accordingly, the blessing is recited: *al ha-mila*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְצִיצִית, בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר חוֹבַת טַלִּית הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר חוֹבַת גַּבְרָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to** reciting a blessing when one attaches **ritual fringes** to a garment, the Sages **disagree about this:** One **Sage,** Rav Adda bar Ahava, **holds** that **it is an obligation** pertaining to the **cloak.** Therefore, when one attaches the ritual fringes he is completing the mitzva, and he should recite a blessing: To prepare ritual fringes. **And** one **Sage,** Rav Naḥman, citing Rav, **holds** that **it is an obligation** incumbent upon **the man.** Consequently, the mitzva is not complete until he wears the garment, and he should not recite a blessing when he attaches the ritual fringes to the garment.

This principle also resolves the original question about *tzitzit*. Rav rules that no blessing is recited at the time of making *tzitzit* — and this is entirely consistent with the *gmar mitzvah* rule. If the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* is *chovas gavra* — an obligation triggered when the person wears a four-cornered garment — then the moment of putting the strings on the garment is not *gmar mitzvah*; the *mitzvah* is fulfilled in the wearing. However, if the *mitzvah* is *chovas mana* — an obligation on the garment itself, such that every four-cornered garment one owns must have *tzitzit* on it even when sitting unworn in a drawer — then the moment one attaches the *tzitzit* to the garment, one has completed the *mitzvah*, and a blessing would indeed be appropriate.

The Gemara states explicitly: *be-ha kamipelgi* — this is the crux of the dispute about blessing. *Mar savar chovas tallis* — one holds the obligation falls on the garment; *u-mar savar chovas gavra* — the other holds the obligation falls on the person. The Keren Orah adds an important nuance: even the position of *chovas mana* would not deny the significance of wearing, since a *Tosefta* records that when one wears *tzitzit* one says *le-hitatef be-tzitzit*. The point is not that wearing is irrelevant, but that the emphasis on the garment itself — the act of attaching *tzitzit* to every four-cornered garment one possesses — is sufficient to constitute *gmar mitzvah*, and thus to warrant a blessing at that moment.

The underlying tension is present in the *psukim* themselves. On one hand, *ve-asu lahem tzitzit* and *gedilim ta'aseh lach* — "you shall make" — suggest that the *mitzvah* is fulfilled in the act of making and attaching. On the other hand, *ve-haya lachem le-tzitzit u-re'isem oto* — "they shall be for you as *tzitzit* and you shall see them" — focuses on the ongoing moment of wearing and beholding. This textual tension is precisely the *machlokes* about *chovas gavra* and *chovas mana*, and it explains why Rav — who here appears to hold *chovas gavra* — rules that no blessing is recited at the time of making.

## Non-Jew Making Tzitzit

### Chovas Dispute

The Gemara now returns explicitly to the question of whether a non-Jew can make *tzitzit*, framing it in the context of the broader conceptual debate. The Gemara records that *chovas gavra* and *chovas mana* are not positions of equal extremism. The view that the obligation is *chovas gavra* — on the person — does not mean one is required to seek out four-cornered garments and obligate oneself. It means that when one does wear such a garment, the obligation to ensure it bears *tzitzit* applies. This is why the episode of Rav Ketina being reproached by the angel — for wearing a garment without *tzitzit* — does not imply a positive obligation to purchase *tallitot*. The rebuke was directed at one who was living in a cultural context where four-cornered garments were standard and was maneuvering to avoid them — comparable to the person who declines to eat a meal specifically to avoid being obligated to recite *bensching* afterward. The major *Rishonim* rule uniformly that today, when our standard garments are not four-cornered, there is no *halachic* problem with not wearing such garments. One is encouraged to seek the *mitzvah*, but is not obligated to do so.

The position of *chovas mana*, by contrast, means that every four-cornered garment one owns — even one sitting unworn in a drawer — must have *tzitzit* attached. When one attaches them, the *mitzvah* is done. This is the position attributed to Shmuel, who refers to the obligation as resting upon the *kesusa* — the garment itself. And it is notable that Shmuel, who holds *chovas mana* and thus emphasizes the act of attachment as the *mitzvah* act, is also the one who adds the requirement of *lishmah* to the spinning — a natural corollary: if the making matters, its qualitative criteria matter as well.

### Non-Jew Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַתּוּן הָכִי מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: You teach this** *halakha* about gentiles attaching ritual fringes to a garment **in this manner,** citing Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav that the ritual fringes are invalid. Consequently, Rav Ḥisda raises a contradiction between this ruling and another ruling of Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲנַן הָכִי מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לְצִיצִית בְּגוֹי שֶׁכְּשֵׁירָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְעָשׂוּ לָהֶם צִיצִית״, יַעֲשׂוּ לָהֶם אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **We teach it in this way,** according to which there is no contradiction: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived that if **ritual fringes** are attached to a garment **by a gentile** they are **valid?** It is derived from **that** which **is stated: "Speak unto the children of Israel** and command them **that they prepare for themselves [*lahem*] strings"** (Numbers 15:38). From the fact that the verse does not merely state: That they prepare [*ve'asu*], but rather states "*ve'asu lahem*," which can be translated as: That they prepare for them, the indication is that even **others,** i.e., gentiles, **shall prepare** ritual fringes **for them.**

The Gemara then records a variant tradition. Rav Mordechai says to Rav Ashi: *atun hachi masnisu lah* — that is how you teach the tradition; *anan hachi masninan lah* — this is how we teach it. In this version, Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: *minayin le-tzitzit be-nachri she-hi kesheira* — how do we know that a non-Jew *may* make *tzitzit* validly? From the same verse: *daber el Bnei Yisrael ve-asu lahem tzitzit* — *ya'asu lahem acheirim*, others may make them for Israel. In this reading, the verse is understood to permit others — including non-Jews — to produce the *tzitzit* on behalf of Israel.

This reversal is striking. If non-Jews can make *tzitzit*, the act of making is de-emphasized — what matters is the end result, the *tzitzit* being on the garment. This also tends to align with the position of *chovas gavra*, where the obligation is essentially about the wearing rather than the making, and where the process of producing the strings carries less intrinsic *mitzvah* weight.

## Materials & Lishmah

### Invalid Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עֲשָׂאָן מִן הַקּוֹצִים, וּמִן הַנִּימִין, וּמִן הַגְּרָדִין – פְּסוּלָה, מִן הַסִּיסִין – כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If **one prepared** ritual fringes **from** threads that protrude from the fabric like **thorns [*kotzim*], or** if he prepared them **from threads [*nimin*]** that were used to sew the garment and remain attached to it, **or from the strings [*geradin*]** that hang from the bottom of a garment, the ritual fringes are **unfit,** as one must attach ritual fringes to a garment for the sake of the mitzva. But if he prepared ritual fringes **from swatches** of wool that were not spun for the sake of the mitzva, they are **fit.**

The Gemara now addresses the material requirements for *tzitzit* strings. A ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav states: strings made from *kotzin*, *nimin*, or *gardin* are invalid (*pesulah*). Rashi explains these as threads or strands left over from the weaving or sewing process — natural byproducts of the fabric itself that were never separately inserted as *tzitzit*. Their invalidity stems from the principle of *ta'aseh ve-lo min he-asuy*: the *mitzvah* requires an affirmative act of making — inserting separate strings into the garment — and one cannot fulfill it by designating threads that were already there as part of the fabric's construction. The Rambam, by contrast, understands these terms as referring to inferior grades of wool — wool of low quality or with much extraneous material — and reads the invalidity as a material quality standard.

### Shmuel Lishmah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר: אַף מִן הַסִּיסִין פְּסוּלָה, בָּעֵינַן טְוִיָּיה לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda continues: **When I stated this** *halakha* in the name of Rav **before Shmuel,** he **said** to me: **Even** ritual fringes tied **from swatches** of wool that were not spun for the sake of the mitzva are **unfit,** as **we require the spinning** of the string to be **for the sake of** the mitzva.

However, *min ha-sisin* — strings made from *sisin* — are valid. *Sisin* appears to refer to wool that has already been spun, perhaps by non-Jews. When this ruling was reported to Shmuel, he responded: *af min ha-sisin pesulah* — even *sisin* are invalid. Why? *Ba'inan teviyah lishmah* — the spinning of the strings must be done *lishmah*, for the sake of the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*. This is why commercial *tzitzit* strings carry certification indicating *teviyah lishmah*. Pre-spun wool purchased in the market, whatever its quality, lacks this designation and is therefore invalid according to Shmuel.

### Tefillin Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: צִיפָּן זָהָב, אוֹ שֶׁטָּלָה עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – פְּסוּלוֹת. עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – כְּשֵׁירוֹת, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא עִיבְּדָן לִשְׁמָן. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף עוֹר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה פְּסוּלוֹת, עַד שֶׁיְּעַבְּדֵן לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this dispute **is like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: If one took phylacteries and **coated them** with **gold or patched them** with the **skin of a non-kosher animal,** then **they are unfit.** But if one patched them with the **skin of a kosher animal,** then **they are fit, and** this is so **even though he did not prepare** the skin **for their sake,** i.e., for the sake of its use in a mitzva. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even** if he patched them with the **skin of a kosher animal they are unfit, until he prepares them for their sake.**

The Gemara draws a parallel to *tefillin* to explore whether a *lishmah* requirement applies to *mitzvah* objects more broadly. The case involves *tefillin* whose *batim* (boxes) were plated with gold or covered with the hide of a non-kosher animal — such *tefillin* are invalid. Covered with kosher animal hide they are valid, *af al pi she-lo ibdan lishmah* — even if the tanning of the leather of the *batim* was not performed *lishmah*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, however, rules that even kosher leather is invalid for the *batim* unless it was tanned *lishmah*.

The Gemara presents this as a *tannaitic* parallel to the dispute about spinning *tzitzit lishmah*: the question of whether *mitzvah* objects require their production to be performed *lishmah* is a general one, though its application varies. Notably, *sukkah* does not require *lishmah* — a *sukkah* built for animals is perfectly valid for human use. The application of *lishmah* to *tefillin* and *tzitzit* thus reflects something specific about these objects, not a universal rule.

A student raised the apt observation that the Torah's language matters here: where the Torah says *ve-asu* or *ta'aseh* — emphasizing the act of making — one would expect a *lishmah* requirement; where the Torah focuses instead on *ve-haya lachem* and *u-kshartem* — the wearing and wearing — the emphasis falls on the end state rather than the process. This is precisely the distinction Tosafot draws regarding *tzitzit*, citing the verse *gedilim ta'aseh lach* and *leshem chovcha* as the basis for the *lishmah* requirement in *tzitzit* specifically. One might add that by *tzitzit* more than anywhere else, the Torah's emphasis on making (*ve-asu lahem tzitzit*) is unambiguous in its formulation — which also underlies the argument that the *mitzvah* is *chovas mana* and that completion of the making is the *gmar mitzvah*.

## Dyeing Techeiles

### Dyeing Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָא תְּכֵילְתָּא הֵיכִי צָבְעִיתוּ לַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַיְיתִינַן דַּם חִלָּזוֹן וְסַמָּנִין, וְרָמֵינַן לְהוּ בְּיוֹרָה, וְשָׁקְלִינַן פּוּרְתָּא בְּבֵיעֲתָא, וְטָעֲמִינַן לְהוּ בְּאוּדְרָא, וְשָׁדֵינַן לַיהּ לְהָהוּא בֵּיעֲתָא, וְקָלֵינַן לֵיהּ לְאוּדְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda: How do you dye this sky-blue wool** to be used for ritual fringes? Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda **said to** Abaye: **We bring blood of a *ḥilazon* and** various **herbs and put them in a pot** and boil them. **And** then **we take a bit** of the resulting dye **in an egg** shell **and test it by** using it to dye **a wad** of wool to see if it has attained the desired hue. **And** then **we throw** away **that egg** shell and its contents **and burn the wad** of wool.

The Gemara turns to the detailed process of dyeing *techeiles* — the blue string required in *tzitzit*. Abaye asks Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda: *ha-techeilta heichi tzavishtu lah* — how do you dye your *techeiles*? The answer describes a multi-stage process: one takes the blood of the *chilazon* (*mashcheinan dam chilazon*) together with certain additives (*samanin*), places them in a boiling vat (*shraminan lehu be-yora*, *u-martachinan lei*), and heats the mixture. Then — and this is where the Gemara's legal interest focuses — one takes a small sample of the dye in an eggshell (*shaklina purta be-vita*) and tests it (*taamina lehu be-udra*) — that is, dips a small sample cloth to observe whether the dye has reached the proper depth of color and penetration. After testing, the eggshell with its remaining dye is discarded (*shadina lei le-hahu beita*) and the sample cloth is burned (*kalmina lei le-udra*).

The Gemara notably does not provide the specific proportions of ingredients — a gap that has frustrated modern attempts to reconstruct the *techeiles* process. What it does provide, in great detail, is the procedure for testing and the disposal of all materials used in testing. This emphasis is itself legally significant.

### Three Inferences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ טְעִימָה פְּסוּלָה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּבָעֵינַן צְבִיעָה לִשְׁמָהּ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ טְעִימָה פָּסְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Learn from** this statement **three** *halakhot*: **Learn from it** that wool that was dyed for the purpose of **testing** the dye and not for use as ritual fringes is **unfit** for ritual fringes. Consequently, one burns the wad of wool so that no one will use it for ritual fringes. **And learn from it that we require dyeing for the sake of** the mitzva. **And learn from it** that using dye for **testing renders** all the dye in that vessel **unfit.** Therefore, some of the dye is removed from the pot before it is tested.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ טְעִימָה פְּסוּלָה, הַיְינוּ צְבִיעָה לִשְׁמָהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מָה טַעַם קָאָמַר – מָה טַעַם טְעִימָה פְּסוּלָה? מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵינַן צְבִיעָה לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The *halakha* that wool dyed for the purpose of **testing** the dye is **unfit is** the same as the requirement of **dyeing for the sake of** the mitzva. It is only because the sky-blue strings must be dyed for the sake of the mitzva that wool dyed as a test is unfit for use as ritual fringes, so why are these stated as two *halakhot*? **Rav Ashi said:** The statement about learning three *halakhot* **employs** the style known as: **What is the reason,** and it means: **What is the reason** that wool that was dyed for the purpose of **testing** is **unfit?** It is **because we require dyeing for the sake of** the mitzva.

The Gemara says *shema mina tlat* — three inferences may be drawn from this procedure. First, *teima pesulah* — testing invalidates. The reason the sample is taken in an eggshell rather than testing directly in the vat is that testing in the vat would invalidate the entire contents. To avoid contaminating the vat, a small quantity is removed first. Second, *ba'inan teviyah lishmah* — spinning must be done *lishmah*. The reason the sample cloth must be burned rather than reused as *tzitzit* is that it was dyed for the purpose of testing, not for the purpose of *techeiles*, and therefore was dyed *shelo lishmah* — not for the sake of the *mitzvah*. This confirms the *lishmah* requirement for dyeing, parallel to the requirement for spinning. Third, *teima pasla* — the remnant of the dye after testing is itself invalid and would invalidate the vat if poured back in.

Rav Ashi then notes that the first and third inferences are really one and the same: the reason the tested cloth is invalid is that it was dyed *shelo lishmah*, and by the same logic the remnant dye is also tainted. So what was stated as three is really two — and the Gemara freely acknowledges this. This is an instance of the Gemara's well-known preference for presenting conclusions in threes, even when the underlying distinctions do not fully support three independent categories.

As for why the remnant dye would invalidate the vat, the working explanation is that the dye was used in creating an invalid product — cloth dyed *shelo lishmah* — and thereby became associated with that invalid use. The eggshell dye must therefore be discarded entirely, rather than returned to the vat, lest it compromise the otherwise valid contents.

### Dye Reuse Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: טְעִימָה פְּסוּלָה, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כְּלִיל תְּכֵלֶת״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן דַּהֲבַאי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ מַרְאֶה שֵׁנִי שֶׁבָּהּ כָּשֵׁר, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁנִי תוֹלָעַת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that the *halakha* that using dye for testing renders all the dye in the pot unfit is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: *Tekhelet* dye that was used for **testing** is **unfit, as it is stated** concerning the priestly vestments: "And you shall make the robe of the ephod **entirely of blue [*kelil tekhelet*]"** (Exodus 28:31), which indicates that the dye must be used exclusively for this purpose, i.e., this must be the first item it is being used to dye. This is **the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel.** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Dahavai says: Even a second appearance** caused by the dye **is fit,** meaning even if it is the second time that the dye is being used, it is still fit. **As it is stated** in the verse: **"And scarlet wool [*ushni tola'at*]"** (Leviticus 14:4), which is interpreted to mean that this may be the second [*sheni*] usage of the dye.

The Gemara then presents a *tannaitic* dispute on a related point. Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel holds that testing invalidates because of the verse *kelil techeiles* — "entirely *techeiles*" — implying that the dye is only valid in its pure, first-use state. Rabbi Yochanan ben Dahavai says that even *mareh sheini* — a second application or secondary use of the dye — is valid, deriving this from the verse *u-sheni tolaat* (the red thread used in certain Temple contexts), suggesting that secondary-grade or secondary-use material is not inherently disqualified.

### Kelil Techeiles

The precise meaning of *kelil techeiles* and *mareh sheini* is disputed between Rashi and Tosafot, with an important contribution from the Keren Orah. Rashi reads the dispute as being about first use versus second use of the dye: *kelil techeiles* means the dye may only be used once, and any remaining dye from a prior application is invalid. On this reading, Rabbi Yochanan ben Dahavai disagrees and permits a second use. Tosafot objects that this is without precedent — there is no known prohibition on using a large vat to dye multiple batches of *tzitzit* strings, and in ordinary practice one would certainly dye many strings from the same vat. Tosafot therefore explains that the invalidity is not about first versus second use per se, but about whether the *first* use was an invalid one: once a quantity of dye has been used in an invalid process — such as testing — the remainder is tainted by association with that invalid use. Rabbi Yochanan ben Dahavai would disagree and permit the remainder even after an invalid first use.

The Keren Orah, one of the few *acharonim* to have written a commentary specifically on Menachot, offers a different and particularly elegant reading. He argues that the issue of testing is a *quality indicator*: a truly superior *techeiles* dye does not need testing — one simply knows it is excellent. The very act of testing reveals that there is uncertainty about the dye's quality, and a dye that requires testing is, by definition, not *kelil techeiles* — not pure, first-grade *techeiles*. It is *mareh sheini* — second-grade. On this reading, the eggshell method is a workaround: by removing the sample from the vat before testing, one avoids testing the *vat itself*, so the vat's dye retains its first-grade status. The issue has nothing to do with *lishmah* on this reading; it is entirely about the quality of the dye as reflected by the need to test. Rabbi Yochanan ben Dahavai would then hold that even dye whose quality has been called into question — *mareh sheini* in this sense — remains valid.

Running through this entire passage is an implicit comparison to *bigdei kehuna* — the priestly garments — several of which were by definition *shatnez*, a mixture of wool and linen. The relevance of this comparison appears in the broader discussion of whether wearing *tzitzit* containing *techeiles* — wool — on a linen garment would constitute *shatnez*. Rabbeinu Tam holds, through comparison to *bigdei kehuna*, that once a garment is designated as a *tzitzit* garment, the *tzitzit* themselves do not render it *shatnez*, even when worn at night or by a woman not performing the *mitzvah*. The comparison to *bigdei kehuna* here thus carries thematic weight beyond the surface-level linguistic parallel.
