# Sanhedrin 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sennacherib's Defeat

### Divine Razor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאׇמְרוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יְגַלַּח ה׳ בְּתַעַר הַשְּׂכִירָה בְּעֶבְרֵי נָהָר בְּמֶלֶךְ אַשּׁוּר אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְשַׂעַר הָרַגְלָיִם וְגַם אֶת הַזָּקָן תִּסְפֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: Were the** following **verse not written, it** would have been **impossible to say it,** since it appears to be a desecration of the name of God, **as it is written: "On that day shall the Lord shave with a hired razor in the parts beyond the river the king of Assyria, the head, and the hair of the legs, and it shall also sweep away the beard"** (Isaiah 7:20). The blasphemous indication is that the Holy One, Blessed be He, will Himself shave Sennacherib.

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rabbi Abahu: *ilmalei mikra katuv iy efshar le'omro* — were it not that the verse was written, it would be nearly heretical to say the following. The verse in question is from Yeshayahu: *bayom hahu yigalach Hashem betaar hasechira be'evrei nahar bimelech Ashur et harosh use'ar haraglayim vegam et hazakan tispeh* — on that day God will shave with the hired razor, by way of the river, with the king of Assyria, the head and the hair of the legs, and even the beard He will destroy. The verse, taken plainly, depicts God Himself administering a shave to Sennacherib. Because such an anthropomorphic image might seem irreverent, Rabbi Abahu prefaces it with his caveat. The *gemara* then proceeds to narrate how this verse was actually fulfilled.

### God as Old Man

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא, וְאִדְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּגַבְרָא סָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי אָזְלַתְּ לְגַבֵּי מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב דְּאַיְיתִיתִינְהוּ לִבְנַיְיהוּ וּקְטַלְתִּינְהוּ, מַאי אָמְרַתְּ לְהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּהָהוּא פַּחְדָּא נָמֵי יָתֵיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל וְשַׁנִּי נַפְשָׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, this is the incident depicted in the verse: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, came and appeared to** Sennacherib **as an old man.** God **said to him: When you go to the kings of the east and the west whose children you brought and killed, what will you say to them?** Sennacherib **said to** the Holy One, Blessed be He: **That man,** referring to himself, **also sits** overcome **with the same fear,** as I do not know what to say to them. Sennacherib **said to** the Holy One, Blessed be He: **What should we do?** The Holy One, Blessed be He, **said to him: Go** and **change your** appearance so that they will not recognize you.

*Ata Kudsha Brich Hu ve'idmi lei k'gavra saba* — God came and appeared to Sennacherib in the form of an old man. The disguised figure said to him: you have been traveling throughout all the lands of the east and the west, you have brought with you the sons of all the other kings — all those princes who accompanied your great army — *vekataltinhu*, and you got them all killed. *Mai amrat lehu* — what are you going to say to these kings when they ask where their children are? Sennacherib replied that this was precisely his own fear — *hahu gavra bahu pachta nami yasiv*, that very thing is what he himself was afraid of. He then asked the disguised figure: *heichi naavid* — what should I do? The answer came: *zil veshani nafshech* — go and disguise yourself.

### Scissors Quest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אֵישַׁנֵּי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי מַסְפְּרָא וְאֶיגְזְיָיךְ אֲנָא. מֵהֵיכָא אַיְיתֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עוּל לְהָהוּא בֵּיתָא וְאַיְיתֵי. אֲזַל, אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ לְמַלְאֲכֵי שָׁרֵת וְאִידְּמוֹ לֵיהּ כְּגַבְרֵי, וַהֲווֹ קָא טָחֲנִי קַשְׁיָיתָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי מַסְפְּרָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: טְחוֹן חַד גְּרִיוָא דְּקַשְׁיָיתָא וְנִיתֵּן לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Sennacherib asked him: **With what shall I change** it? God **said to him: Go bring me scissors and I will shear you** Myself. Sennacherib asked: **From where should I bring** the scissors? The Holy One, Blessed be He, **said to him: Go to that house and bring** them. **He went** and **found ministering angels,** who **came and appeared to** Sennacherib **as men; and** the angels **were grinding date pits.** Sennacherib **said to them: Give me scissors.** The ministering angels **said to him: Grind a *se'a* of pits and we will give** it **to you.**

The question then arose: *b'mai eshani* — how exactly should he disguise himself? The old man replied: go fetch me a *mispara*, a scissors, and I will give you a haircut. Sennacherib asked where he might obtain scissors. He was directed to a certain house. When he arrived, he found the heavenly angels there, appearing to him as ordinary men, grinding *kashisa* — palm kernels. Sennacherib asked if he could borrow a scissors. The men replied: *tchon chad giyura dekashisa u'niten lach* — grind a measure of these seeds for us and we will give it to you. So Sennacherib — once the conqueror of nations — ground a measure of palm kernels, and they gave him the scissors. The episode is unmistakably humiliating, evoking something of the degradation visited upon Samson at the millstone, though the precise moral lesson of the narrative remains somewhat elusive.

### Beard on Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְחַן חַד גְּרִיוָא דְּקַשְׁיָיתָא וִיהַבוּ לֵיהּ מַסְפַּרְתָּא. עַד דַּאֲתָא, אִיחֲשַׁךְ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי נוּרָא. אֲזַל וְאַיְיתִי נוּרָא. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא נָפַח לֵיהּ, אִתְּלִי בֵּיהּ נוּרָא בְּדִיקְנֵיהּ. אֲזַל גַּזְיֵיהּ לְרֵישֵׁיהּ וְדִיקְנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He ground a *se'a* of pits and they gave him scissors. By** the time **he came** back with the scissors **it grew dark.** The Holy One, Blessed be He, **said to** Sennacherib: **Go** and **bring fire** to provide light. Sennacherib **went and brought fire. While he was fanning it, the fire ignited his beard** and **it spread** and **sheared his head and his beard.**

As Sennacherib was making his way back to the old man with the scissors, it had grown dark. The old man said he could not cut hair in the dark — *zil aytei nura*, go bring fire. Sennacherib went and obtained a light. *Bahadei dekanafach leih itli beih nura bedikneih* — as he was blowing on the flame to keep it going, the fire caught his beard. *Azal gazyah lereisheih vedikneih* — he then shaved off his own hair and beard so that his whole face would not catch fire. Rather than God performing the shave, Sennacherib ended up doing it himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אֲמַרוּ): הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וְגַם אֶת הַזָּקָן תִּסְפֶּה״. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: ״גָּרֵירְתֵּיהּ לְאַרְמָאָה שְׁפַר לֵיהּ, אַיתְלֵי לֵיהּ נוּרָא בְּדִיקְנֵיהּ וְלָא שָׂבְעַתְּ חוּכָא מִינֵּיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: This is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And it shall also destroy the beard"** (Isaiah 7:20). **Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: If you scorch a gentile** and **it is pleasant for him, ignite a fire in his beard and you will never tire of ridiculing him.** It means that if one does not protest when others ridicule him, they will escalate the ridicule. The adage is based upon this incident involving Sennacherib.

*Amru hainu dechsiv vegam et hazakan tispeh* — this, says the *gemara*, is the fulfillment of the verse: the beard will be *destroyed*, not merely shaved, for it was burned away by fire. The distinction in language — *tispeh*, destruction, rather than *yigalach*, shaving — is now explained by the manner in which it came to pass.

### Rav Papa's Saying

Rav Papa appends a popular saying: *hainu de'amri inshi gerirta l'arma'ah shapir leih* — this is why people say, singe an Aramean and it will be good for him. *Itli leih nura bedikneih velo shachach chucha mineih* — if his beard catches fire, his smile of relief will not leave his face, so grateful is he for the result. The precise significance of this folk saying in context is admittedly opaque.

### Recognition & Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל, אַשְׁכַּח דַּפָּא מִתֵּיבוּתָא דְּנֹחַ. אָמַר: הַיְינוּ אֱלָהָא רַבָּא דְּשֵׁיזְבֵיהּ לְנֹחַ מִטּוֹפָנָא. אֲמַר: אִי אָזֵיל הָהוּא גַּבְרָא וּמַצְלַח, מְקָרֵב לְהוּ לִתְרֵין בְּנוֹהִי קַמָּךְ. שְׁמַעוּ בְּנוֹהִי וְקַטְלוּהּ. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי הוּא מִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה בֵּית נִסְרֹךְ אֱלֹהָיו וְאַדְרַמֶּלֶךְ וְשַׂרְאֶצֶר בָּנָיו הִכֻּהוּ בַחֶרֶב וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Sennacherib **went and found a beam from Noah's ark,** from which he fashioned a god. **He said: This** beam **is the great god who delivered Noah from the flood. He said: If that man,** referring to himself, **goes and succeeds, he will sacrifice his two sons before you. His sons heard** his commitment **and killed him. This is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And it came to pass as he was worshipping in the house of Nisroch his god that Adrammelech and Sarezer, his sons, smote him with the sword,** and they fled to Ararat" (II Kings 19:37), where Noah's ark had come to rest. The Gemara explains that this interpretation is based upon the etymological similarity between *neser*, the Hebrew term for beam, and Nisroch, the god that Sennacherib fashioned from a beam.

*Azal ashchach dapa miteivuta deNoach* — Sennacherib, now making his way back to Assyria, found a plank from Noah's ark. *Amar hainu elaha raba deshizbeih leNoach mitufana* — he recognized it as belonging to the great God who had saved Noah from the flood. This recognition — perhaps drawing on the flood narratives preserved among ancient nations — prompted him to realize he had been fighting against the God of Israel. He then said: *ei azilna vehu gavra umatzlach* — if I return to my country and succeed, *mekarvna lehu letrin benohi kaimach* — I will offer my two sons as sacrifices before You.

### Sons Kill Father

*Shamu benohi vekatluhu* — his sons heard this vow and killed him. This provides the backstory to what the *pesukim* record explicitly: *vayehi hu mishtachaveh beit nisroch elohav veAdrammech veSharezer benohi hikuhu bacharev* — and it was as he was bowing down in the house of his god Nisroch that Adrammelech and Sharezer his sons struck him with the sword.

## Historical Cycles

### Angel Layla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּחָלֵק עֲלֵיהֶם לַיְלָה הוּא וַעֲבָדָיו וַיַּכֵּם וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתוֹ מַלְאָךְ שֶׁנִּזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ לְאַבְרָהָם, ״לַיְלָה״ שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַלַּיְלָה אָמַר הֹרָה גָבֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the war between the kings and Abraham mentioned above, the Gemara explains a verse: "And he pursued as far as Dan. **And he divided himself against them by night [*laila*], he and his servants, and smote them"** (Genesis 14:14–15). The term "by night" [*laila*] appears out of place in this verse. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: That angel that happened to** come and assist **Abraham** in that war, **Laila is his name, as it is stated: "And Laila said: A man-child is brought forth"** (Job 3:3), indicating that there is an angel named Laila.

With the story of Sennacherib's destruction now complete, the *gemara* draws a broader observation about the cyclical nature of Jewish history. The same heavenly forces that intervened against Sennacherib's armies echo the intervention on behalf of Abraham against the four kings, and point forward to the future wars of *Gog uMagog*. The *gemara* turns to the verse: *Vayechalek Aleihem Layla* — and the night divided against them, referring to Abraham's pursuit of the kings who had captured Lot.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *oto malach shenizdamen lo le-Avraham Layla shemo* — the angel who came to Abraham's assistance was named *Layla*, Night. The verse's word *layla* is therefore to be read not merely as the time of night but as the name of the angel who assisted. The proof is from a verse in Iyyov: *ve-ha-layla amar hora gaver* — and the Night said, a man has been conceived — where Night is personified and speaks, indicating it represents an angelic being.

### Night Fights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: שֶׁעָשָׂה עִמּוֹ מַעֲשֵׂה לַיְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִן שָׁמַיִם נִלְחָמוּ הַכּוֹכָבִים מִמְּסִלּוֹתָם נִלְחֲמוּ עִם סִיסְרָא״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: טָבָא דְּנַפָּחָא מִדְּבַר נַפָּחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says:** God **performed for him an act of night,** i.e., the stars in heaven waged war on his behalf, **as it is stated: "They fought from heaven; the stars in their courses fought against Sisera"** (Judges 5:20). **Reish Lakish said:** The statement of Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa, **the smith, is preferable to** the statement **of the son of the smith,** Rabbi Yoḥanan.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha offers a different reading. *She-asah imo ma'aseh layla* — rather than an angel named Night, the *layla* in Abraham's verse means that the night itself fought for him, just as we find by another episode: *Min ha-shamayim nilchamu ha-kochavim mi-mesilotam nilchamu im Sisra* — from the heavens the stars fought from their paths against Sisera. There too celestial forces — elements of the night sky — intervened against an enemy of Israel.

Reish Lakish notes a parallel with the splitting of the sea, where *velo karav zeh el zeh kol ha-layla* — the Egyptians and Israel did not approach one another throughout the night, and the destruction came at the close of the night.

*Tava de-Nafcha mi-bar Nafcha* — Reish Lakish then remarks that the explanation of Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha is better than that of Rabbi Yochanan, who is himself known as *bar Nafcha*. The second interpretation is preferable: it is not that some angel bears the name Night, but that the stars and forces of the night sky came to fight.

### Abraham at Dan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּרְדֹּף עַד דָּן״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כֵּיוָן שֶׁבָּא אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק עַד דָּן, תָּשַׁשׁ כֹּחוֹ. רָאָה בְּנֵי בָּנָיו שֶׁעֲתִידִין לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּדָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּשֶׂם אֶת הָאֶחָד בְּבֵית אֵל וְאֶת הָאֶחָד נָתַן בְּדָן״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the previous verse: **"And he pursued as far as Dan"** (Genesis 14:14), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Once that righteous** person, Abraham, **came to Dan, his strength weakened. He saw** through the Divine Spirit **that his descendants were destined to worship idols in Dan, as it is stated:** "And he fashioned two calves of gold…**and he set one in Bethel, and the other one he placed in Dan"** (I Kings 12:28–29).

*Vayirdof ad Dan* — and he chased them until Dan, in the north of the Land of Israel. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *keivan sheba oto tzaddik ad Dan tashish kocho* — when that righteous man, Abraham, reached Dan, his strength ebbed and he was unable to continue the pursuit. Why? *Ra'ah benei banav she-atidin la'avod avodah zarah be-Dan* — he saw prophetically that his descendants would one day worship *avodah zarah* in Dan, where Jeroboam would place one of the golden calves: *vayaseim et ha-echad be-Veit El ve-et ha-echad natan be-Dan*. The future sin of his descendants sapped Abraham's strength at that very location.

### Nebuchadnezzar at Dan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע לֹא נִתְגַּבֵּר עַד שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְדָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִדָּן נִשְׁמַע נַחְרַת סוּסָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **And even that wicked** person, Nebuchadnezzar, **did not prevail until he reached Dan, as it is stated: "From Dan the snorting of his horses is heard"** (Jeremiah 8:16).

*Ve-af oto rasha lo nitgaber ad she-higia le-Dan* — and similarly, that wicked one, Nebuchadnezzar, did not gain his full strength until he reached Dan, because he was coming to punish Israel for that very sin. *Shene'emar mi-Dan nishma nachrat susav* — as it is written, from Dan can be heard the snorting of his horses. All of Jewish history is interconnected: the strength Abraham lost at Dan is the very strength that Nebuchadnezzar gained there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: ״אַף עַל גַּב דְּשָׁלַח רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא מִנְּצִיבִין: הִזָּהֲרוּ בְּזָקֵן שֶׁשָּׁכַח תַּלְמוּדוֹ מֵחֲמַת אוֹנְסוֹ, וְהִזָּהֲרוּ בַּוְּורִידִין כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְהִזָּהֲרוּ בִּבְנֵי עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ שֶׁמֵּהֶן תֵּצֵא תּוֹרָה – כִּי הָא מִילְּתָא מוֹדְעִינַן לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says: Even though Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira sent** a statement **from Netzivin: Be vigilant with regard to** treating with deference **an elder who forgot his studies due to circumstances beyond his control, and be vigilant with regard to** cutting the **jugular veins** when slaughtering an animal **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and be vigilant with regard to** treating with respect **the children of ignoramuses, as from** some of **them Torah will emerge, we inform them** of a message **like this matter.**

The *gemara* then introduces a statement attributed to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira of Nitzivin, or alternatively Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lakish, transmitted by Rabbi Zeira: *hizaheru be-zaken she-shachach talmudo machamat onso* — be careful to continue showing respect to an elder scholar who has forgotten his learning due to old age and through no fault of his own. And equally: *ve-hizaheru bi-venei amei ha-aretz she-mehen tetzei Torah* — be careful regarding the children of the unlearned, for from them Torah may emerge.

The placement of this teaching at this juncture will become clear from the passage that follows, which the *gemara* now turns to.

## Jeremiah's Complaint

### Racer Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צַדִּיק אַתָּה ה׳ כִּי אָרִיב אֵלֶיךָ אַךְ מִשְׁפָּטִים אֲדַבֵּר אוֹתָךְ מַדּוּעַ דֶּרֶךְ רְשָׁעִים צָלֵחָה שָׁלוּ כׇּל בֹּגְדֵי בָגֶד. נְטַעְתָּם גַּם שֹׁרָשׁוּ יֵלְכוּ גַּם עָשׂוּ פֶרִי״. מַאי אַהְדַּרוּ לֵיהּ? ״כִּי אֶת רַגְלִים רַצְתָּה וַיַּלְאוּךָ וְאֵיךְ תְּתַחֲרֶה אֶת הַסּוּסִים וּבְאֶרֶץ שָׁלוֹם אַתָּה בוֹטֵחַ וְאֵיךְ תַּעֲשֶׂה בִּגְאוֹן הַיַּרְדֵּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Right would you be, Lord, were I to contend with You; yet will I reason with You: Why does the path of the wicked prosper; why are all who deal treacherously secure? You have planted them, they have also taken root; they grow, they also bring forth fruit"** (Jeremiah 12:1–2). **What did they respond to** Jeremiah from heaven? **"If you have run with the pedestrians, and they have wearied you, how can you contend with horses? And though in a land of peace you feel secure, how will you do in the wild country of the Jordan?"** (Jeremiah 12:5).

*Tzadik atah Hashem ki ariv eilecha ach mishpatim adaber otach madua derech reshaim tzalcha shalu kol bogdei vaged* — Righteous are You, O God, even when I contend with You; but I must speak justice with You: why does the path of the wicked succeed, why do all those who rebel against You have peace? *Netatam gam shorashu yelechu gam asu peri* — You have planted them and they have taken root, they have gone on and been fruitful. This is Jeremiah's complaint, read through the lens of the story of Nebuchadnezzar, who has just been shown gaining his strength at Dan. Why was Nebuchadnezzar permitted to succeed?

*Ma ahadru lei* — what did they respond to him? The answer comes from the very next verse: *ki et raglim ratzta vayelauch veeich titchareh et hasusim uve-eretz shalom atah voteiach veeich taaseh bigon ha-Yarden* — because you have run against foot-racers and they have wearied you, how do you think you can compete against horses? And in a land of peace you are confident — how much more will you struggle at the swelling of the Jordan?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשָׁל לְאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁאָמַר: יָכוֹל אֲנִי לָרוּץ שָׁלֹשׁ פַּרְסָאוֹת לִפְנֵי הַסּוּסִים בֵּין בִּצְעֵי הַמַּיִם. נִזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ רַגְלִי אֶחָד, רָץ לְפָנָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה מִילִין בַּיַּבָּשָׁה וְנִלְאָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וּמָה לִפְנֵי רַגְלִי כָּךְ, לִפְנֵי הַסּוּסִים – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וּמָה שְׁלֹשֶׁת מִילִין כָּךְ, שָׁלֹשׁ פַּרְסָאוֹת – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וּמָה בַּיַּבָּשָׁה כָּךְ, בֵּין בִּצְעֵי הַמַּיִם – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets the verse according to its straightforward meaning. This is **a parable of a person who said: I can run three parasangs before the horses in the swamplands. He encountered a pedestrian** and **ran before him** for **three *mil* on dry land and wearied.** The people **said to him: And if** running **before a pedestrian** you grew **so** weary, then if you were to run **before horses, all the more so** would you become weary. **And if** after running **three *mil*** you grew **so** weary, then if you were to run **three parasangs,** four times that distance, **all the more so** would you become weary. **And if** after running **on dry land** you grew **so** weary, then if you were to run **in the swamplands, all the more so** would you become weary.

The *gemara* offers a parable. A man boasted that he could run three *parsa'ot* against horses, even through a swamp. A foot-racer challenged him. They ran merely three *milin* on dry land — a quarter of the claimed distance — and the boaster grew weary. *Umah lifnei ragli kach lifnei susim al achat kama vechama* — if this is how you fare against a foot-racer, how much more would you lose against horses? *Umah gimmel milin kach gimmel parsaot al achat kama vechama* — if three *milin* weary you, how much more three *parsa'ot*? *Umah bayabasha kach bein bitzei hamayim al achat kama vechama* — and if dry land undoes you, how much worse through a swamp?

### Four Steps Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף אַתָּה: וּמָה בִּשְׂכַר אַרְבַּע פְּסִיעוֹת שֶׁשִּׁלַּמְתִּי לְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע, שֶׁרָץ אַחַר כְּבוֹדִי, אַתָּה תָּמֵיהַּ? כְּשֶׁאֲנִי מְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר לְאַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב שֶׁרָצוּ לְפָנַי כְּסוּסִים – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **So too, you,** Jeremiah: **And if with regard to** the **reward** for **four paces that I compensated that wicked person,** Nebuchadnezzar, **who ran in My honor, you are astonished** at its magnitude, **when I compensate Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who ran before Me like horses** throughout their lives, **all the more so** will their reward be great. This teaches the potency of even a minor mitzva.

*Af atah umah bishchar daled pesiot sheshilamti lo leoto rasha sheratz achar kevodi* — and so too with you, Jeremiah: if merely for the reward of four steps that this wicked man ran in My honor, I am granting him this power — that he becomes the instrument of Jerusalem's destruction — imagine what the reward will be for those who truly served Me. Note that it should be said that a marginal note in the *gemara* indicates that the *Midrash* and the *Yalkut* read three steps rather than four, which would align with the three *parsa'ot* of the parable.

The destruction of Jerusalem is not the *reason* Jerusalem is destroyed — that is the sins of Israel. But someone had to serve as the instrument, and Nebuchadnezzar was selected for that role as a reward for this act of honor toward God. Even for so small a thing, this is what he receives.

### Patriarchs' Reward

*Kesheni meshalem sachar le-Avraham Yitzchak ve-Yaakov sheratzu lefanai k'susim al achat kama vekama* — when I eventually get around to rewarding Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who ran before Me like horses, their reward will be incomparably greater. The argument, then, is one of proportionality. If the tiny and almost incidental act of Nebuchadnezzar earns him such power, consider what the accumulated merit of the patriarchs will ultimately yield.

### Jeremiah Consoled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״לַנְּבִיאִים נִשְׁבַּר לִבִּי בְקִרְבִּי רָחֲפוּ כׇּל עַצְמֹתַי הָיִיתִי כְּאִישׁ שִׁכּוֹר וּכְגֶבֶר עֲבָרוֹ יָיִן מִפְּנֵי ה׳ וּמִפְּנֵי דִּבְרֵי קׇדְשׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** This teaches the potency of even a minor mitzva. **That is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Concerning the prophets. My heart within me is broken; all my bones shake; I am like a drunken man and like a man whom wine has overcome, due to the Lord and due to His sacred words"** (Jeremiah 23:9).

*V'linviim nishbar libi b'kirbi* — and for the prophets — which Rashi understands here as a reference to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — my heart was broken within me, I could not perceive where their ultimate reward would come from. *V'rachafu kol atzmotai hayiti k'ish shikkor v'kagever avru yayin* — all my bones trembled, I was like a drunken man, like a man overcome by wine. *Mipnei Hashem umipnei divrei kodsho* — because of God and because of His holy words. After God's explanation, Jeremiah is consoled: the very scale of Nebuchadnezzar's reward for so little is itself a guarantee of the infinitely greater reward that awaits those who truly served God.

## Four Steps Story

### Baladan's Letter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי אַרְבַּע פְּסִיעוֹת מַאי הִיא? דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּעֵת הַהִיא שָׁלַח מְרֹדַךְ בַּלְאֲדָן בֶּן בַּלְאֲדָן מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל סְפָרִים וְגוֹ׳״. מִשּׁוּם ״כִּי חָלָה חִזְקִיָּהוּ וַיֶּחֱזָק״ שַׁדַּר לֵיהּ סְפָרִים וּמִנְחָה? אִין, לִדְרֹשׁ ״(אֶת) הַמּוֹפֵת אֲשֶׁר הָיָה בָאָרֶץ״. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁמֵּת בּוֹ אָחָז שְׁתֵּי שָׁעוֹת הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **these four paces** of Nebuchadnezzar, **what is** the incident to which the Gemara alludes? The Gemara answers that the incident is **as it is written: "At that time Merodach-Baladan, son of Baladan, king of Babylonia, sent a letter** and a gift to Hezekiah, as he had heard that he had been ill and was recovered" (Isaiah 39:1). The Gemara asks: **Due to** the fact that **Hezekiah had been ill and was recovered, he sent him a letter and a gift?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** and he did so in order **"to inquire of the wonder that was in the land"** (II Chronicles 32:31). **As Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** On **that day that** Hezekiah's father, **Ahaz, died,** daylight lasted **two hours,** ten hours shorter than the standard day, so that the wicked Ahaz would be buried hurriedly, without the pomp typically accorded kings.

*Hani arba pisiot mai* — but what exactly are these four steps? The *gemara* now explains. *Ba'eis hi shalach Merodach Baladan ben Baladan Melech Bavel sefarim* — at that time Merodach Baladan the son of Baladan, king of Babylonia, sent letters and a gift to Hezekiah when he heard that Hezekiah had recovered from his illness. Why was this worthy of such a diplomatic gesture? *Mishum d'chala Chizkiyahu vayechazak* — merely because a king was sick and recovered? The *gemara* explains that another verse in Divrei Hayamim adds: *lidrosh hamofes asher haya ba'aretz* — because a wonder had been performed in the land.

### Sun Clock Wonder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי חָלָה חִזְקִיָּהוּ וְאִיתְּפַח, אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא לְהָנָךְ עֲשַׂר שָׁעֵי נִיהֲלֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנְנִי מֵשִׁיב אֶת צֵל הַמַּעֲלוֹת אֲשֶׁר יָרְדָה בְמַעֲלוֹת אָחָז בַּשֶּׁמֶשׁ אֲחֹרַנִּית עֶשֶׂר מַעֲלוֹת וַתָּשׇׁב הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ עֶשֶׂר מַעֲלוֹת בַּמַּעֲלוֹת אֲשֶׁר יָרָדָה״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: חִזְקִיָּהוּ חֲלַשׁ וְאִיתְּפַח. אֲמַר: אִיכָּא גַּבְרָא כִּי הַאי וְלָא בָּעֵינָא לְשַׁדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ שְׁלָמָא? כְּתַבוּ לֵיהּ: ״שְׁלָמָא לְמַלְכָּא חִזְקִיָּה, שְׁלָם לְקַרְתָּא דִּירוּשְׁלֶם, שְׁלָם לֶאֱלָהָא רַבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And when Hezekiah fell ill and recovered, the Holy One, Blessed be He, restored those ten hours to** Hezekiah, **as it is written: "Behold, I will return the shadow of the dial, which descended on the sundial of Ahaz, ten degrees backward. So the sun returned ten degrees, by which it had gone down"** (Isaiah 38:8). Merodach **said to** his servants: **What is this** wonder that the day was extended? **They said to him: Hezekiah fell ill and recovered,** and the sun shone for him. Merodach **said: There is a man like that and I do not need to send him greetings?** The ministers of Merodach **wrote to** Hezekiah: **Greetings to King Hezekiah, greetings to the city of Jerusalem,** and **greetings to the great God.**

What was the wonder? Rabbi Yochanan explains: *oso hayom shemeis bo Achaz shti sha'os haya* — on the day that Achaz, the wicked father of Hezekiah, died, the sun set ten hours early; the day had only two hours of light. God did this so that people would not come out to eulogize the wicked king and make a spectacle of his funeral. Then, *v'ki chala Chizkiyahu v'ispach hadrinu Kudsha Brich Hu l'hanach eser sha'ei* — when Hezekiah fell ill and recovered, God extended the day by ten hours. Ten hours taken away for the father, ten hours restored for the son.

The source is explicit: *Hineni meshiv tzel hama'alos asher yarda b'ma'alos Achaz bashemesh achornit eser ma'alos* — behold I will return the shadow of the sun clock of Achaz backward ten units. *Vatishev hashemesh eser ma'alos b'ma'alos asher yorada* — and the sun returned ten gradations on the steps where it had descended.

When Baladan heard of this, he said: *ika gavra ki hai velo be'ina leshadurei lei shalma* — there is such a great man, that the sun itself reversed for him, and I have not sent him greetings? He instructed his scribes: write to Shalma Lemalka Chizkiyah — peace to King Hezekiah; *Shalma Lekarta De'yerushalayim* — peace to the city of Jerusalem; *Shalma Le'elaha Raba* — peace to the great God.

### Nebuchadnezzar Corrects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְבוּכַדְנֶאצַּר סָפְרֵיהּ דְּבַלְאֲדָן הֲוָה. הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא לָא הֲוָה הָתָם. כִּי אֲתָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הֵיכִי כָּתְבִיתוּ? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הָכִי כָּתְבִינַן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: קְרֵיתוּ לֵיהּ ״אֱלָהָא רַבָּא״ וְכָתְבִיתוּ לֵיהּ לְבַסּוֹף? אָמַר: אֶלָּא הָכִי כְּתוּבוּ: ״שְׁלָם לֶאֱלָהָא רַבָּא, שְׁלָם לְקַרְתָּא דִּירוּשְׁלֶם, שְׁלָם לְמַלְכָּא חִזְקִיָּה״. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: קַרְיָינָא דְּאִיגַּרְתָּא אִיהוּ לֶיהֱוֵי פַּרְוַונְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Nebuchadnezzar was the scribe of Baladan,** and **at that time he was not there. When he came** there **he said to** the other scribes: **How did you write** the king's message? **They said to him: We wrote this:** Greetings to King Hezekiah, greetings to the city of Jerusalem, and greetings to the great God, as we were commanded. Nebuchadnezzar **said to** the scribes: **You called him: The great God, and you wrote Him at the end** of the list of greetings? **He said: Rather, write this: Greetings to the great God, greetings to the city of Jerusalem,** and **greetings to King Hezekiah.** The scribes **said to** Nebuchadnezzar: **The one who reads the letter, let him be the messenger.** You gave the advice; you correct the text.

*Nevuchadnetzar Safra De'baladan Hava* — Nebuchadnezzar was Baladan's scribe at the time. *Hahi sha'ata lo hava hatam* — at that moment he was not present, having been sent on some errand. When he returned and heard what had been written, he said: *karitu lei Elaha Raba vekatvitu lei levasof* — you called their God the great God, and you put God at the end of the letter? If you truly acknowledge Him as great, He comes first — before Jerusalem, and certainly before Hezekiah. He directed them to rewrite the letter: *Shalma Le'elaha Raba, Shalma Lekarta De'yerushalayim, Shalma Lemalka Chizkiyah* — God first, then His city, then the king.

The scribes responded with a famous phrase: *karyana de'igarta ihu leihavei parvanka* — let the one who reads the proclamation be the one to execute it. You want the letter rewritten your way? You go deliver it yourself.

### Four Steps Run

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְהַט בָּתְרֵיהּ, כִּדְרָהֵיט אַרְבַּע פְּסִיעוֹת, אֲתָא גַּבְרִיאֵל וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִילְמָלֵא לֹא בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ, לֹא הָיָה תַּקָּנָה לְשׂוֹנְאֵיהֶם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuchadnezzar **pursued** the messenger to take the letter from him and revise it. **When he ran four paces,** the angel **Gabriel came and stopped his** pursuit. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: If Gabriel had not come and stopped his** pursuit **there would have been no remedy for the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people. Had Nebuchadnezzar succeeded in revising the letter, his reward would have been so great that he would have been able to destroy the Jewish people, as he desired.

*Rahat batrei karahat arba pesiyot* — Nebuchadnezzar ran after the messengers who had already been dispatched. He ran four steps — or, according to the *Midrash* and *Yalkut*, three steps — to catch up with them and redirect the letter. These are the four steps he ran in God's honor.

### Gabriel Stops Him

*Ata Gavriel ve'ukmei* — the angel Gabriel came and stopped him after those four steps. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *ilmale ba Gavriel ve'he'emido lo hava takana lesonayehem shel Yisrael* — had Gabriel not come and halted him precisely there, there would have been no remedy for Israel. If Nebuchadnezzar had been permitted to run further — a greater distance, a more sustained effort in God's honor — his reward would have been correspondingly greater and the destruction he could have wrought correspondingly worse. Gabriel therefore stopped him at exactly four steps, limiting both his merit and the scope of what he would be empowered to do.

Here a brief but memorable digression: on the day of the shiur the teacher had been overcharged fifty cents at a sidewalk fruit stand. A block and a half away, the vendor came running after him to return the change — a gesture reminiscent of stories told about the *Chafetz Chaim*. The relevance, as Rashi will soon make explicit, is the theme of *benei am ha-aretz* and the importance of not underestimating anyone's potential for honesty and greatness.

### Baladan's Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בַּלְאֲדָן בֶּן בַּלְאֲדָן״? אָמְרִי: בַּלְאֲדָן מַלְכָּא הֲוָה, וְאִישְׁתַּנִּי אַפֵּיהּ וַהֲוָה כִּי דְּכַלְבָּא. הֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּרֵיהּ עַל מַלְכוּתָא. כִּי הֲוָה כָּתֵיב, הֲוָה כָּתֵיב שְׁמֵיהּ וּשְׁמֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ ״בַּלְאֲדָן מַלְכָּא״. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּן יְכַבֵּד אָב וְעֶבֶד אֲדֹנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of: Merodach-**Baladan, son of Baladan?** If his name was Merodach it was not Baladan. The Sages **say: Baladan was a king, and his visage was transformed and became like that of a dog. His son was sitting on the throne** in his stead **and when he would write** a letter **he would write his name and the name of his father, King Baladan. That is** the meaning of that **which is written** with regard to the honor accorded by gentiles to their fathers: **"A son honors his father and a servant his master"** (Malachi 1:6).

*Mai Baladan ben Baladan* — why does Merodach Baladan share his father's name? The *gemara* explains: *Baladan malka hava v'ishtanei apay ve'hava ke'kalba* — Baladan was king, but his face changed and came to resemble a dog. His son took over the kingship. *Ki hava kasiv hava kasiv shemei u'shemei de'avuha* — whenever the son would write his own name, he would include his father's name alongside it, styling himself Baladan son of Baladan, as if his father were still reigning, in order to honor him. This fulfills the verse: *ben yechabeid av v'eved adonav* — a son honors his father and a servant his master.

### Son Honors Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בֵּן יְכַבֵּד אָב״ – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, ״וְעֶבֶד אֲדֹנָיו״ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבַחֹדֶשׁ הַחֲמִישִׁי בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ הִיא שְׁנַת תְּשַׁע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה לַמֶּלֶךְ נְבוּכַדְנֶאצַּר מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל בָּא נְבוּזַרְאֲדָן רַב טַבָּחִים עָמַד לִפְנֵי מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל בִּירוּשָׁלִִם וַיִּשְׂרֹף אֶת בֵּית ה׳ וְאֶת בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **"A son honors his father"** is referring to **that which we stated** with regard to Baladan. **"And a servant his master"** is referring to a verse, **as it is written: "And in the fifth month, on the tenth day of the month, which was the nineteenth year of Nebuchadnezzar, king of Babylonia, came Nebuzaradan, captain of the guard, who stood before the king of Babylonia, into Jerusalem. And he burned the House of the Lord, and the house of the king"** (Jeremiah 52:12–13).

The second part of that verse — *v'eved adonav* — is illustrated by Nebuzaradan. The relevant verse reads: *u'vachodesh hachamishi b'asor lachodesh bishnas tsha esrei shana l'melech Nevuchadnezzar melech Bavel ba Nevuzaradan rav tabachim lifnei melech Bavel b'Yerushalayim* — in the fifth month, on the tenth of the month, in the nineteenth year of Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylonia, Nebuzaradan the chief slaughterer came before the king of Babylonia in Jerusalem.

But this raises an obvious question: *u'mi salik Nevuchadnezzar l'Yerushalayim* — since when was Nebuchadnezzar in Jerusalem? The verse elsewhere records that Tzidkiyahu was brought to Nebuchadnezzar in *Rivlah* — which Rabbi Abahu identifies as Antioch — clearly outside the Land of Israel. Rav Chisda and Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi offer two resolutions. One holds that Nebuzaradan had the image of Nebuchadnezzar engraved on his chariot — *dmus dyukno hayesa chakuka* — so that wherever he went, it was as though Nebuchadnezzar were present. The other holds that *eimateih yetirah hayetah lo mimenu* — Nebuzaradan had such intense awe of Nebuchadnezzar that he always felt his master's presence before him, *v'domeh k'mi she'omed lefanav* — as though he were perpetually standing in front of him. Either by an external symbol or through internalized reverence, the servant honored the master.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי סְלֵיק נְבוּכַד נֶצַּר לִירוּשָׁלַיִם? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעֲלוּ אֹתוֹ אֶל מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל רִבְלָתָה״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: זוֹ אַנְטוֹכְיָא! רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי, חַד אָמַר: דְּמוּת דְּיוֹקְנוֹ הָיְתָה חֲקוּקָה לוֹ עַל מֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵימָה יְתֵירָה הָיְתָה לוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ וְדוֹמֶה כְּמִי שֶׁעוֹמֵד לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Nebuchadnezzar ascend to Jerusalem? But isn't it written** with regard to Zedekiah: "And they took the king, **and brought him up to the king of Babylonia, to Riblah"** (II Kings 25:6), **and Rabbi Abbahu says: This** place called Riblah is a reference to **Antioch.** Apparently, Nebuchadnezzar was in Antioch, not in Jerusalem. **Rav Ḥisda and Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi** resolved this apparent contradiction. **One says: An image of** Nebuchadnezzar's **likeness was engraved on** Nebuzaradan's **chariot,** and he regarded that image as though Nebuchadnezzar were actually there. **And one says:** Nebuzaradan **was in extreme fear of** Nebuchadnezzar, **and it was as though** Nebuzaradan **was** always **standing before** Nebuchadnezzar. That is an example of the honor of a servant to his master mentioned in the verse.

This, Rashi explains, is also the connection to the earlier teaching about the children of *amei ha-aretz*. The *gemara* will shortly note that descendants of Sennacherib and Haman sat and taught Torah. The point, as the *Maharsha* articulates it more fully, is not that one should honor those students because of their illustrious wicked ancestry, but rather that one should never give up on educating the children of bad homes. Hezekiah was the child of the wicked Achaz. The descendants of Sennacherib became *Shemayah and Avtalyon*. The message is: do not assume that a child's environment determines his fate. Teachers can influence students, and that responsibility must not be abandoned.

## Jerusalem's Destruction

### Iron Hammers Fail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: טְעוֹן תְּלָת מְאָה כּוּדַנְיָיתָא נַרְגָּא דְפַרְזְלָא דְּשָׁלֵיט בְּפַרְזְלָא שַׁדַּר לֵיהּ נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר לִנְבוּזַרְאֲדָן. כּוּלְּהוּ בְּלַעְתִּינְהוּ חַד דַּשָּׁא דִּירוּשְׁלֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פִּתּוּחֶיהָ יָּחַד בְּכַשִּׁיל וְכֵילַפֹּת יַהֲלֹמוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss the role of Nebuzaradan in the destruction of the Temple. **Rava says: Nebuchadnezzar sent to Nebuzaradan three hundred mules laden with iron axes that cut iron. All of them were incapacitated** in the attempt to breach **one gate of Jerusalem, as it is stated: "And now they pound its carved work together with hatchet and with hammers"** (Psalms 74:6).

Rava teaches: *t'ein t'las me'ah kudnayasa narga d'parzla d'shalt b'parzla shad'reih Nebuchadnezzar l'Nebuzaradan* — Nebuchadnezzar sent Nebuzaradan a burden of 300 mules laden with iron hammers so sharp they could break through iron itself. *Kulyahu balyu b'chada dasha d'Yerushalayim* — and all 300 were destroyed trying to break through a single gate of Jerusalem. As the verse says, *p'sachah yachad b'khishil v'kheyalfos yahalomun* — they came at its opening with hammers and axes and could not prevail.

### Heavenly Voice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי לְמִיהְדַּר, אָמַר: מִסְתְּפֵינָא דְּלָא לִיעְבְּדוּ בִּי כִּי הֵיכִי דַּעֲבַדוּ בְּסַנְחֵרִיב. נָפְקָא קָלָא וְאָמַר: ״שָׁוַור בַּר שָׁוַור, נְבוּזַרְאֲדָן, שְׁוַור דִּמְטָא זִימְנָא דְּמַקְדְּשָׁא חֲרִיב וְהֵיכְלָא מִיקְּלֵי״. פָּשׁ לֵיהּ חַד נַרְגָּא. אֲתָא מַחְיֵיהּ בְּקוֹפָא, וְאִיפְּתַח, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִוָּדַע כְּמֵבִיא לְמָעְלָה בִּסְבֻךְ עֵץ קַרְדֻּמּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuzaradan **sought to return** to Babylonia and **said: I am afraid.** I want to ensure **that they will not do to me just as they did to Sennacherib,** whose downfall was in Jerusalem. A Divine **Voice emerged and said: Leaper, son of a leaper; Nebuzaradan, take** the **leap, as the time has arrived for the Temple to be destroyed and the Sanctuary to burn. One ax remained for him** to use. **He went and struck** the gate **with the dull end** of the ax **and it opened, as it is stated: "He became known as the wielder of axes upward in a thicket of trees"** (Psalms 74:5). At the appropriate time the gate was breached as though the ax were cutting trees.

Nebuzaradan then sought to withdraw. Whether it was Nebuzaradan or Nebuchadnezzar who hesitated, the concern voiced was: *mistafina d'la l'avdu bi k'heicha d'avdu b'Sancheiriv* — I am afraid they will do to me what they did to Sennacherib. Then *n'phek kahlah* — a heavenly voice emerged and said: *sh'var bar sh'var* — leaper son of a leaper, *m'to zimna d'mikdash d'yechariv v'heichala d'yikli* — the time has come, the Temple is to be destroyed and the *heichal* to be burned. Left with a single hammer, Nebuzaradan struck the gate *b'kupah* — with the back of the hammer, not even the sharp edge — *v'iftach* — and it opened. What 300 iron hammers could not accomplish, one blow accomplished once God permitted it.

### Heavenly Trampling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה קָטֵיל וְאָזֵל, עַד דִּמְטָא לְהֵיכְלָא. אַדְלֵיק בֵּיהּ נוּרָא. גְּבַהּ הֵיכְלָא. דְּרַכוּ בֵּיהּ מִן שְׁמַיָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּת דָּרַךְ ה׳ לִבְתוּלַת בַּת יְהוּדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **He was proceeding and killing until he reached the Sanctuary.** When he reached the Sanctuary, **he ignited a fire in it. The Sanctuary rose,** seeking to enter Heaven so that it would not burn. **They trod upon it from Heaven** and returned it to its place, **as it is stated: "The Lord has trodden the virgin, the daughter of Judah, as in a winepress"** (Lamentations 1:15).

He then slaughtered his way through the city until he reached the *heichal* and set it alight. The flames rose toward heaven. *Darchu bei min shamaya* — from heaven they trampled down the fire, so that it would be unmistakably clear that this was a this-worldly destruction and not a heavenly one. As the verse says: *Gat darach Hashem livtulat bat Yehuda* — God has trampled upon the daughter of Judah as in a winepress. The trampling here refers to pressing down the flames.

### Nebuzaradan Humbled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא זִיחָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ. נְפַקָא בַּת קָלָא וַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: עַמָּא קְטִילָא קָטְלַתְּ, הֵיכְלָא קַלְיָא קְלֵית, קִימְחָא טְחִינָא טְחֵינְתְּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קְחִי רֵחַיִם וְטַחֲנִי קָמַח גַּלִּי צַמָּתֵךְ חֶשְׂפִּי שׁוֹבֶל גַּלִּי שׁוֹק עִבְרִי נְהָרוֹת״. ״חִטִּים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״קֶמַח״.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuzaradan **became haughty,** taking pride in his conquest. **A Divine Voice emerged and said to him:** Your haughtiness is unwarranted, as **you killed a nation** that was already **dead, you burned a Sanctuary** that was already **burned,** and **you ground flour** that was already **ground, as it is stated** with regard to Babylonia: **"Take millstones and grind flour; uncover your locks, tuck up the train, uncover the leg, pass over rivers"** (Isaiah 47:2). **It was not stated:** Grind **wheat, but** "grind **flour,"** indicating that all the destruction had already been wrought by God, and the role played by the enemy was insignificant.

When Nebuzaradan began to feel haughty at his conquest, *nafak bat kol ve'amra* — a heavenly voice came forth and said: *ama ketila katalt* — you have killed a nation that was already killed. *Heichala kalya kalit* — you have burned a Temple that was already burned. *Kimcha techina techinat* — you have ground flour that was already flour. Do not take any credit. The verse cited is: *Kechi rechain utchaini kemach* — take a millstone and grind flour. Prophesying the exile, the verse says *kemach* — flour — not wheat, because what you are grinding was already flour. Nebuzaradan was merely actualizing a divine decree that had already been issued.

### Zechariah's Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲזָא דְּמֵיהּ דִּזְכַרְיָה דַּהֲוָה קָא רָתַח. אָמַר לְהוּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דַּם זְבָחִים הוּא דְּאִישְׁתְּפִיךְ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַיְיתֵי וַאֲנַסֵּי אִי מִדְּמוּ. כַּסִּי וְלָא אִידְּמוֹ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: גַּלּוֹ לִי, וְאִי לָא סָרֵיקְנָא לְכוּ לְבִשְׂרַיְיכוּ בְּמַסְרֵיקָא דְּפַרְזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** When he reached the Sanctuary, **he saw the blood of Zechariah** the priest **boiling.** It had not calmed since he was killed in the Temple (see II Chronicles 24:20–22). Nebuzaradan **said to** the priests there: **What is this? They said to him: It is the blood of offerings that was spilled.** Nebuzaradan **said to them: Bring** animals **and I will test** to determine **if** the blood of the animals **is similar** to the blood that is boiling. **He slaughtered** the animals **and** their blood **was not similar** to the boiling blood. Nebuzaradan **said to** the priests: **Reveal** the source of that blood **to me, and if not I will comb your flesh with an iron comb.**

*Chaza dmyei deZecharya dehava ka ratach* — Nebuzaradan then saw the blood of Zechariah the prophet, which was still boiling. This Zechariah — a *kohen* and *navi* who had prophesied the destruction of Jerusalem and was killed by the people in the Temple courtyard for it — had blood that had never ceased to boil. This story is well known from the *Kinot* of Tisha b'Av.

Nebuzaradan asked the people what the boiling substance was. They told him it was blood of sacrifices that had been spilled. He slaughtered an animal and compared the blood — it was not the same. He threatened to flay their skin with an iron comb if they did not tell him the truth. They confessed: this was the blood of a *kohen* and prophet who prophesied against Israel about the destruction of Jerusalem, *vekatluhu* — and they killed him.

### Appeasement Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַאי כֹּהֵן וְנָבִיא הוּא, דְּאִינַּבִּי לְהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּחוּרְבָּנָא דִּירוּשְׁלֶם, וְקַטְלוּהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא מְפַיֵּיסְנָא לֵיהּ. אַיְיתִי רַבָּנַן, קָטֵיל עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְלָא נָח. אַיְיתִי דַּרְדַּקֵּי דְּבֵי רַב, קָטֵיל עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְלָא נָח. אַיְיתִי פִּרְחֵי כְהוּנָּה, קָטֵיל עִילָּוֵיהּ, וְלָא נָח. עַד דִּי קְטַל עִילָּוֵיהּ תִּשְׁעִין וְאַרְבְּעָה רִיבּוֹא, וְלָא נָח.
>
> **Translation:** The priests **said to** Nebuzaradan: **This** blood **is** the blood of **a priest and a prophet who prophesied for the Jewish people with regard to the destruction of Jerusalem and whom they killed. He said to** the priests: **I will pacify** the blood so the boiling will stop. **He brought the Sages** and **killed them over** the blood **and** its boiling **did not cease. He brought schoolchildren** and **killed them over** the blood **and** its boiling **did not cease. He brought young priests** and **killed them over** the blood **and** its boiling **did not cease.** He continued killing **until he killed 940,000** people **over** the blood, **and** its boiling **did not cease.**

*Ana mefayisne leh* — I will appease him, said Nebuzaradan. He brought the *Rabbanan* and slaughtered them over the blood — perhaps because as leaders they bore responsibility. The blood did not stop. He brought schoolchildren — innocent, surely offering their blood should suffice. Still it did not stop. He brought the young *Kohanim*. Still it did not stop. *Ad dikatlinhu tishim ve-arba'in ribo* — until he had slaughtered 940,000 people, and still the blood boiled.

### Zechariah's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרַב לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: זְכַרְיָה, זְכַרְיָה, טוֹבִים שֶׁבָּהֶן אִיבַּדְתִּים. נִיחָא לָךְ דְּאֶיקְטְלִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ? מִיָּד נָח.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuzaradan **approached** the blood and **said: Zechariah, Zechariah, the worthy among them I killed** on your behalf. **Is it satisfactory for you that I kill them all? Immediately** the boiling **ceased.**

Finally Nebuzaradan approached and said: *Zecharia Zecharia tovim shebahen ibadeti nicha lach de-aktilinnhu lechulhu* — Zechariah, Zechariah, I have destroyed the best of them; do you want me to kill them all? *Miyad nach* — immediately the blood stopped boiling.

### Nebuzaradan Repents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִרְהַר תְּשׁוּבָה בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: מָה הֵם שֶׁלֹּא אִיבְּדוּ אֶלָּא נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת כָּךְ, הָהוּא גַּבְרָא מָה תֶּיהְוֵי עֲלֵיהּ? עֲרַק, שַׁדַּר פּוּרְטֵיתָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ, וְאִיתְגַּיַּיר.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuzaradan **contemplated repentance. He said: If they, who caused only one person to perish,** gained atonement only after all **this** killing, then with regard to **that man,** referring to himself, **what will be** required **for him** to gain atonement? **He deserted** his army and **dispatched** a last **will to his house and converted.**

*Hirher teshuva bedatei* — Nebuzaradan began to contemplate *teshuva*. He said to himself: *mah hem she-lo ibdu ela nefesh achas kach* — these people killed only one innocent person, and look how God demanded such justice for that one life. *Hahu gavra mah tehei alei* — I, who have now killed hundreds of thousands in my misguided attempt to appease the blood — what will become of me? *Azal shadar priteita leveiteih ve-isgayar* — he sent a final testament to his house and converted.

### Righteous Convert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נַעֲמָן - גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב הָיָה, נְבוּזַר אֲדָן - גֵּר צֶדֶק הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Naaman** the Aramean (see II Kings, chapter 5) **was a *ger toshav*,** meaning that he accepted upon himself to refrain from idol worship but did not convert to Judaism. **Nebuzaradan was** a completely **righteous convert.**

The *gemara* then records: *Tanu Rabbanan* — the Rabbanan taught: Na'aman was a *ger toshav*, a Noahide who worshipped God but did not become part of the Jewish people. But *Nevuzaradan ger tzedek hayah* — Nebuzaradan became a full righteous convert.

### Descendants Learn Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל סִיסְרָא לִמְּדוּ תּוֹרָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל סַנְחֵרִיב לִימְּדוּ תּוֹרָה בָּרַבִּים. וּמַאן נִינְהוּ? שְׁמַעְיָה וְאַבְטַלְיוֹן. מִבְּנֵי בָּנָיו שֶׁל הָמָן לִמְּדוּ תּוֹרָה בִּבְנֵי בְּרָק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Among the descendants of Sisera** (see Judges, chapter 4) were those who **studied Torah in Jerusalem. Among the descendants of Sennacherib** were those who **taught Torah in public.** The Gemara asks: **And who are they?** The Gemara answers: They were **Shemaya and Avtalyon.** **Among the descendants of Haman** were those who **studied Torah in Bnei Brak.**

*Mibnei banav shel Sisra limdu torah biyerushalayim* — the descendants of Sisera taught Torah in Jerusalem. *Mibnei banav shel Sancheriv limdu torah berabbim* — the descendants of Sennacherib taught Torah to the masses. *U-man ninhu Shemaya ve-Avtalyon* — who were they? Shemayah and Avtalyon. *Mibnei banav shel Haman limdu torah bivnei brak* — and the descendants of Haman taught Torah in Bnei Brak. No matter what family one comes from, the descendants can become the greatest of Torah teachers.

### Descendants of Nebuchadnezzar Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף מִבְּנֵי בָּנָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע בִּיקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהִכְנִיסָן תַּחַת כַּנְפֵי הַשְּׁכִינָה. אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי שֶׁהֶחֱרִיב אֶת בֵּיתְךָ וְשָׂרַף אֶת הֵיכָלֶךָ תַּכְנִיס תַּחַת כַּנְפֵי הַשְּׁכִינָה? הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״רִפִּינוּ אֶת בָּבֶל וְלֹא נִרְפָּתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And even among the descendants of that wicked** person, Nebuchadnezzar, were those whom **the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to bring beneath the wings of the Divine Presence** and have them convert. **The ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe:** The **one who destroyed Your House and burned Your Sanctuary,** will **You introduce** him **beneath the wings of the Divine Presence?** The Gemara explains: **That is** the meaning of that **which is written: "We have healed Babylonia, but she is not healed"** (Jeremiah 51:9).

*Ve-af mibnei banav shel oso rasha bikesh hakadosh baruch hu lehachnisan tachas kanfei hashchina* — even the descendants of that wicked man, Nebuchadnezzar himself, God wished to bring under His wings. *Amru malachei hashareis lifnei hakadosh baruch hu* — but the ministering angels said before God: *Ribono shel olam mi she-hechriv es beischa ve-saraf es heichalecha tachnis attah tachas kanfei hashchina* — the one who destroyed Your house and burned Your Temple — You will bring him under Your wings?

*Hainu dechsiv ripeinu es bavel velo nispasa* — that is what is meant by the verse: I sought to heal Babylonia, but it was not healed, because ultimately the descendants of Nebuchadnezzar were not accepted. Yet the man who actually carried out the destruction — Nebuzaradan — was accepted as a convert. There is something striking in this contrast: the one who followed orders, who personally slaughtered hundreds of thousands, was accepted in *teshuva*; while the family of the one who gave the orders was not. The *gemara* leaves us to grapple with the question of who may be accepted after terrible deeds, and under what circumstances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: זֶה נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: אֵלּוּ נַהֲרוֹת בָּבֶל, וְתַרְגֻּמַהּ: צִינְיָיתָא צְרִידָתָא דְּבַבְלָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says: This** verse **is** a reference to **Nebuchadnezzar,** none of whose children converted. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** This is not a reference to a person; rather, **these are the rivers of Babylonia, and interpret it** as referring to **the bitter saltwater rivers of Babylonia.**

Ulla and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani dispute what the verse *ripeinu es bavel velo nispasa* refers to: Ulla says it refers to Nebuchadnezzar and his descendants; Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says it refers to the bitter rivers of Babylonia, which were never healed and whose water remained bitter.

## Ammon & Moab

### Bad Neighbors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: עַמּוֹן וּמוֹאָב שִׁיבָבֵי בִּישֵׁי דִּירוּשְׁלֶם הֲווֹ. כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁמְעִינְהוּ לִנְבִיאֵי דְּקָא מִיתְנַבְּאִי לְחוּרְבָּנָא דִּירוּשְׁלֶם, שְׁלַחוּ לִנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר: פּוֹק וְתָא. אָמַר: מִסְתְּפֵינָא דְּלָא לִיעְבְּדוּ לִי כְּדַעֲבַדוּ בְּקַמָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § On a related note, the Gemara describes the events that led to the destruction of the Temple. **Ulla says: Ammon and Moab were bad neighbors of Jerusalem. Once they heard the prophets who prophesied about the destruction of the Jerusalem, they sent to Nebuchadnezzar: Emerge** from your dwelling place **and come** conquer them. Nebuchadnezzar **said** to them: **I am afraid.** I want to ensure **that they will not do to me just as they did to** my **predecessors.**

*Ulla amar Ammon u'Moav shcheinei ra'im l'Yerushalayim havu* — Ulla said: Ammon and Moab were bad neighbors to Jerusalem. *Keivan d'sham'inhu l'nevi'ei d'ka mitnabu l'churbana d'Yerushalayim* — when they heard the prophets prophesying the destruction of Jerusalem, they seized the opportunity and sent word to Nebuchadnezzar: *puk v'ta* — come, this is your moment.

Nebuchadnezzar was reluctant: *mistafina d'la l'yavdu bi ki heichi d'avdu b'kama'ei* — I am afraid they will do to me what they did to the earlier ones, meaning Sennacherib and Sisera and those who had previously tried and failed to destroy Israel.

### Messages to Attack

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: ״כִּי אֵין הָאִישׁ בְּבֵיתוֹ הָלַךְ בְּדֶרֶךְ מֵרָחוֹק״, וְאֵין אִישׁ אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה״. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: בְּקָרִיבָא הוּא וְאָתֵי. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: ״הָלַךְ בְּדֶרֶךְ מֵרָחוֹק״. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: אִית לְהוּ צַדִּיקֵי דְּבָעוּ רַחֲמֵי וּמַיְיתוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ammon and Moab **sent to him** that it is written: **"For the *ish* is not at home; he is gone on a long journey"** (Proverbs 7:19), **and *ish*** is referring to **no** one **but the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "The Lord is an *ish* of war"** (Exodus 15:3). Nebuchadnezzar **sent to them** in response: **He is in a nearby** location, **and He will come.** They **sent to** Nebuchadnezzar: **"He has gone on a journey from afar"** (Proverbs 7:19). Nebuchadnezzar **said to them: They have righteous** among them **who will pray for mercy and bring Him** to return.

Ammon and Moab then sent him a series of reassurances, each quoting and interpreting verses from Mishlei. First: *ki ein ha'ish b'veito halach b'derech merachok* — God is not at home, He has gone on a long journey. The *gemara* notes that *ish* here means God, as in *Hashem ish milchama*. Nebuchadnezzar replied that God was probably nearby and would return when His people were in danger. They countered: *halach b'derech merachok* — no, He has gone on a long way journey and is not coming back soon. He replied that there were still righteous people who would cry out and bring God back. They countered with another verse: *tzror hakesef lakach b'yado* — He has taken His money with Him and is not coming back; and *kesef* means the righteous, as derived from a verse in Hoshea connecting *kesef* with the redemption from Egypt. Even the righteous have departed. Nebuchadnezzar replied that the people might repent and pray God back. They countered with a final verse: *l'yom hakisei yavo l'veito* — God has set a time for His return, and *kisei* means a designated time, as in *bakese l'yom chagenu*. He has set a time of seventy years before He returns. So for now He has entirely left and will not return for the foreseeable future. The time is set; this is the window.

### Winter & Mountains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: ״צְרוֹר הַכֶּסֶף לָקַח בְּיָדוֹ״, וְאֵין כֶּסֶף אֶלָּא צַדִּיקִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאֶכְּרֶהָ לִּי בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר כָּסֶף וְחֹמֶר שְׂעֹרִים וְלֵתֶךְ שְׂעֹרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Ammon and Moab **sent to** Nebuchadnezzar: **"He has taken a bundle of *kesef* with him"** (Proverbs 7:20), **and *kesef*** is referring to **nothing other than the righteous, as it is stated: "So I bought her to me for fifteen pieces of *kesef* and for a *kor* of barley and a half-*kor* of barley"** (Hosea 3:2). The inference is that God acquired the congregation of Israel due to the presence of righteous people among them, and Ammon and Moab sent a message to Nebuchadnezzar that God had already taken the righteous and they no longer offered protection.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לְהוּ: הָדְרִי רַשִּׁיעֵי בִּתְשׁוּבָה, וּבָעוּ רַחֲמֵי וּמַיְיתוּ לֵיהּ. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: כְּבָר קָבַע לָהֶן זְמַן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לְיוֹם הַכֶּסֶא יָבֹא בֵּיתוֹ״. אֵין כֶּסֶא אֶלָּא זְמַן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בַּכֵּסֶה לְיוֹם חַגֵּנוּ״. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: סִיתְוָוא הוּא, וְלָא מָצֵינָא דְּאָתֵי מִתַּלְגָא וּמִמִּיטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Nebuchadnezzar **sent to them:** Perhaps **the wicked will repent** and become righteous **and they will pray for mercy and they will bring Him** to return. Ammon and Moab **sent to** Nebuchadnezzar: **God already designated the time of their** redemption, **as it is stated: "On the day of the *keseh*, He will come home"** (Proverbs 7:20), and ***keseh*** is referring to **nothing other than** a designated **time, as it is stated:** "Sound a *shofar* at the New Moon, **at the *keseh* on the day of our feast"** (Psalms 81:4). Since there is a time designated for redemption, until then you can do as you please. Nebuchadnezzar **sent to them: It is winter** now **and I cannot come** and conquer Jerusalem **due to the snow and the rain.**

Nebuchadnezzar raised another objection: *sitva hu v'ramia tina d'ati etaltal m'mitra* — it is winter, the rains and snow will prevent a campaign. This hesitation is itself noteworthy — there is a certain reluctance throughout this passage that may account for the *gemara*'s willingness to consider some small measure of merit even in Nebuchadnezzar. Indeed, the siege did begin in *Tevet*, in winter, as we mark on *Asara b'Tevet*. They responded: *ta shina d'tura* — come by way of the mountains, and the mountains will shelter you from the elements, as the verse says: *shalchu char moshel eretz misela midbara el har bat tziyon* — send a messenger through the rock of the wilderness to the mountain of Zion.

### Graves as Quarters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: תָּא אַשִּׁינָּא דְּטוּרָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁלְחוּ כַר מוֹשֵׁל אֶרֶץ מִסֶּלַע מִדְבָּרָה אֶל הַר בַּת צִיּוֹן״. שְׁלַח לְהוּ: אִי אָתֵינָא, לֵית לִי דּוּכְתָּא דְּיָתֵיבְנָא בֵּיהּ. שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: קְבָרוֹת שֶׁלָּהֶם מְעוּלִּין מִפַּלְטֵירִין שֶׁלְּךָ, דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּעֵת הַהִיא נְאֻם ה׳ יוֹצִיאוּ אֶת עַצְמוֹת מַלְכֵי יְהוּדָה וְאֶת עַצְמוֹת שָׂרָיו וְאֶת עַצְמוֹת הַכֹּהֲנִים וְאֵת עַצְמוֹת הַנְּבִיאִים וְאֵת עַצְמוֹת יוֹשְׁבֵי יְרוּשָׁלִָים מִקִּבְרֵיהֶם וּשְׁטָחוּם לַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְלַיָּרֵחַ וּלְכֹל צְבָא הַשָּׁמַיִם אֲשֶׁר אֲהֵבוּם וַאֲשֶׁר עֲבָדוּם וַאֲשֶׁר הָלְכוּ אַחֲרֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Ammon and Moab **sent to him: Come on the peaks of mountains,** where the rain does not pool, **as it is stated: "Send the lamb to the ruler of the land from the peaks of the wilderness to the mount of the daughter of Zion"** (Isaiah 16:1). Nebuchadnezzar **sent to them: If I come** to Jerusalem, **I** will **have no place to dwell** while laying siege to the city. Ammon and Moab **sent to him: Their burial caves are superior to your palaces,** and you can clear the caves and dwell there, **as it is written: "At that time, says the Lord, they shall remove the bones of the kings of Judea, and the bones of his princes, and the bones of the priests, and the bones of the prophets and the bones of the inhabitants of Jerusalem from their graves; and they shall spread them before the sun and the moon and all of the hosts of heaven, whom they have loved, and whom they have served, and after whom they have walked"** (Jeremiah 8:1–2). Ultimately Nebuchadnezzar came to conquer Judea and removed the corpses to make room for his army.

Nebuchadnezzar objected further: *ee asina leit li dukhta di asvina beh* — if I come, I will have no place to quarter my troops. They replied: *kivros shelahem me'ulim mi-paltrin shelcha* — their graveyards are better than your palace. As Rashi explains, the Israelites buried in caves, and those cave-graves offered more than enough space to quarter an army.

The proof-text is: *ba-et ha-hi ne'um Hashem yotziu et atzmot malchei yehuda ve'et atzmot sarav ve'et atzmot hakohanim ve'et atzmot hanevi'im ve'et atzmot yoshvei yerushalayim mikivreihem ushetachum lashemesh velayarei'ach ulechol tzava hashamayim asher ahevum va'asher avadum va'asher halchu achareihem* — at that time God declared that they would take out the bones of the kings of Judah, and of their officers, and of the *Kohanim*, and of the prophets, and of the inhabitants of Jerusalem from their graves, and spread them before the sun and the moon and all the host of heaven — those heavenly bodies which the people had loved and served and followed. The bones are taken from the caves because Nebuchadnezzar's troops are quartered in those very caves, emptied out to make room.
