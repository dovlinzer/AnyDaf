# Sanhedrin 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sennacherib's Defeat

### Divine Razor

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rabbi Abahu: *ilmalei mikra katuv iy efshar le'omro* — were it not that the verse was written, it would be nearly heretical to say the following. The verse in question is from Yeshayahu: *bayom hahu yigalach Hashem betaar hasechira be'evrei nahar bimelech Ashur et harosh use'ar haraglayim vegam et hazakan tispeh* — on that day God will shave with the hired razor, by way of the river, with the king of Assyria, the head and the hair of the legs, and even the beard He will destroy. The verse, taken plainly, depicts God Himself administering a shave to Sennacherib. Because such an anthropomorphic image might seem irreverent, Rabbi Abahu prefaces it with his caveat. The *gemara* then proceeds to narrate how this verse was actually fulfilled.

### God as Old Man

*Ata Kudsha Brich Hu ve'idmi lei k'gavra saba* — God came and appeared to Sennacherib in the form of an old man. The disguised figure said to him: you have been traveling throughout all the lands of the east and the west, you have brought with you the sons of all the other kings — all those princes who accompanied your great army — *vekataltinhu*, and you got them all killed. *Mai amrat lehu* — what are you going to say to these kings when they ask where their children are? Sennacherib replied that this was precisely his own fear — *hahu gavra bahu pachta nami yasiv*, that very thing is what he himself was afraid of. He then asked the disguised figure: *heichi naavid* — what should I do? The answer came: *zil veshani nafshech* — go and disguise yourself.

### Scissors Quest

The question then arose: *b'mai eshani* — how exactly should he disguise himself? The old man replied: go fetch me a *mispara*, a scissors, and I will give you a haircut. Sennacherib asked where he might obtain scissors. He was directed to a certain house. When he arrived, he found the heavenly angels there, appearing to him as ordinary men, grinding *kashisa* — palm kernels. Sennacherib asked if he could borrow a scissors. The men replied: *tchon chad giyura dekashisa u'niten lach* — grind a measure of these seeds for us and we will give it to you. So Sennacherib — once the conqueror of nations — ground a measure of palm kernels, and they gave him the scissors. The episode is unmistakably humiliating, evoking something of the degradation visited upon Samson at the millstone, though the precise moral lesson of the narrative remains somewhat elusive.

### Beard on Fire

As Sennacherib was making his way back to the old man with the scissors, it had grown dark. The old man said he could not cut hair in the dark — *zil aytei nura*, go bring fire. Sennacherib went and obtained a light. *Bahadei dekanafach leih itli beih nura bedikneih* — as he was blowing on the flame to keep it going, the fire caught his beard. *Azal gazyah lereisheih vedikneih* — he then shaved off his own hair and beard so that his whole face would not catch fire. Rather than God performing the shave, Sennacherib ended up doing it himself.

*Amru hainu dechsiv vegam et hazakan tispeh* — this, says the *gemara*, is the fulfillment of the verse: the beard will be *destroyed*, not merely shaved, for it was burned away by fire. The distinction in language — *tispeh*, destruction, rather than *yigalach*, shaving — is now explained by the manner in which it came to pass.

### Rav Papa's Saying

Rav Papa appends a popular saying: *hainu de'amri inshi gerirta l'arma'ah shapir leih* — this is why people say, singe an Aramean and it will be good for him. *Itli leih nura bedikneih velo shachach chucha mineih* — if his beard catches fire, his smile of relief will not leave his face, so grateful is he for the result. The precise significance of this folk saying in context is admittedly opaque.

### Recognition & Vow

*Azal ashchach dapa miteivuta deNoach* — Sennacherib, now making his way back to Assyria, found a plank from Noah's ark. *Amar hainu elaha raba deshizbeih leNoach mitufana* — he recognized it as belonging to the great God who had saved Noah from the flood. This recognition — perhaps drawing on the flood narratives preserved among ancient nations — prompted him to realize he had been fighting against the God of Israel. He then said: *ei azilna vehu gavra umatzlach* — if I return to my country and succeed, *mekarvna lehu letrin benohi kaimach* — I will offer my two sons as sacrifices before You.

### Sons Kill Father

*Shamu benohi vekatluhu* — his sons heard this vow and killed him. This provides the backstory to what the *pesukim* record explicitly: *vayehi hu mishtachaveh beit nisroch elohav veAdrammech veSharezer benohi hikuhu bacharev* — and it was as he was bowing down in the house of his god Nisroch that Adrammelech and Sharezer his sons struck him with the sword.

## Historical Cycles

### Angel Layla

With the story of Sennacherib's destruction now complete, the *gemara* draws a broader observation about the cyclical nature of Jewish history. The same heavenly forces that intervened against Sennacherib's armies echo the intervention on behalf of Abraham against the four kings, and point forward to the future wars of *Gog uMagog*. The *gemara* turns to the verse: *Vayechalek Aleihem Layla* — and the night divided against them, referring to Abraham's pursuit of the kings who had captured Lot.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *oto malach shenizdamen lo le-Avraham Layla shemo* — the angel who came to Abraham's assistance was named *Layla*, Night. The verse's word *layla* is therefore to be read not merely as the time of night but as the name of the angel who assisted. The proof is from a verse in Iyyov: *ve-ha-layla amar hora gaver* — and the Night said, a man has been conceived — where Night is personified and speaks, indicating it represents an angelic being.

### Night Fights

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha offers a different reading. *She-asah imo ma'aseh layla* — rather than an angel named Night, the *layla* in Abraham's verse means that the night itself fought for him, just as we find by another episode: *Min ha-shamayim nilchamu ha-kochavim mi-mesilotam nilchamu im Sisra* — from the heavens the stars fought from their paths against Sisera. There too celestial forces — elements of the night sky — intervened against an enemy of Israel.

Reish Lakish notes a parallel with the splitting of the sea, where *velo karav zeh el zeh kol ha-layla* — the Egyptians and Israel did not approach one another throughout the night, and the destruction came at the close of the night.

*Tava de-Nafcha mi-bar Nafcha* — Reish Lakish then remarks that the explanation of Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha is better than that of Rabbi Yochanan, who is himself known as *bar Nafcha*. The second interpretation is preferable: it is not that some angel bears the name Night, but that the stars and forces of the night sky came to fight.

### Abraham at Dan

*Vayirdof ad Dan* — and he chased them until Dan, in the north of the Land of Israel. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *keivan sheba oto tzaddik ad Dan tashish kocho* — when that righteous man, Abraham, reached Dan, his strength ebbed and he was unable to continue the pursuit. Why? *Ra'ah benei banav she-atidin la'avod avodah zarah be-Dan* — he saw prophetically that his descendants would one day worship *avodah zarah* in Dan, where Jeroboam would place one of the golden calves: *vayaseim et ha-echad be-Veit El ve-et ha-echad natan be-Dan*. The future sin of his descendants sapped Abraham's strength at that very location.

### Nebuchadnezzar at Dan

*Ve-af oto rasha lo nitgaber ad she-higia le-Dan* — and similarly, that wicked one, Nebuchadnezzar, did not gain his full strength until he reached Dan, because he was coming to punish Israel for that very sin. *Shene'emar mi-Dan nishma nachrat susav* — as it is written, from Dan can be heard the snorting of his horses. All of Jewish history is interconnected: the strength Abraham lost at Dan is the very strength that Nebuchadnezzar gained there.

The *gemara* then introduces a statement attributed to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira of Nitzivin, or alternatively Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lakish, transmitted by Rabbi Zeira: *hizaheru be-zaken she-shachach talmudo machamat onso* — be careful to continue showing respect to an elder scholar who has forgotten his learning due to old age and through no fault of his own. And equally: *ve-hizaheru bi-venei amei ha-aretz she-mehen tetzei Torah* — be careful regarding the children of the unlearned, for from them Torah may emerge.

The placement of this teaching at this juncture will become clear from the passage that follows, which the *gemara* now turns to.

## Jeremiah's Complaint

### Racer Parable

*Tzadik atah Hashem ki ariv eilecha ach mishpatim adaber otach madua derech reshaim tzalcha shalu kol bogdei vaged* — Righteous are You, O God, even when I contend with You; but I must speak justice with You: why does the path of the wicked succeed, why do all those who rebel against You have peace? *Netatam gam shorashu yelechu gam asu peri* — You have planted them and they have taken root, they have gone on and been fruitful. This is Jeremiah's complaint, read through the lens of the story of Nebuchadnezzar, who has just been shown gaining his strength at Dan. Why was Nebuchadnezzar permitted to succeed?

*Ma ahadru lei* — what did they respond to him? The answer comes from the very next verse: *ki et raglim ratzta vayelauch veeich titchareh et hasusim uve-eretz shalom atah voteiach veeich taaseh bigon ha-Yarden* — because you have run against foot-racers and they have wearied you, how do you think you can compete against horses? And in a land of peace you are confident — how much more will you struggle at the swelling of the Jordan?

The *gemara* offers a parable. A man boasted that he could run three *parsa'ot* against horses, even through a swamp. A foot-racer challenged him. They ran merely three *milin* on dry land — a quarter of the claimed distance — and the boaster grew weary. *Umah lifnei ragli kach lifnei susim al achat kama vechama* — if this is how you fare against a foot-racer, how much more would you lose against horses? *Umah gimmel milin kach gimmel parsaot al achat kama vechama* — if three *milin* weary you, how much more three *parsa'ot*? *Umah bayabasha kach bein bitzei hamayim al achat kama vechama* — and if dry land undoes you, how much worse through a swamp?

### Four Steps Reward

*Af atah umah bishchar daled pesiot sheshilamti lo leoto rasha sheratz achar kevodi* — and so too with you, Jeremiah: if merely for the reward of four steps that this wicked man ran in My honor, I am granting him this power — that he becomes the instrument of Jerusalem's destruction — imagine what the reward will be for those who truly served Me. Note that it should be said that a marginal note in the *gemara* indicates that the *Midrash* and the *Yalkut* read three steps rather than four, which would align with the three *parsa'ot* of the parable.

The destruction of Jerusalem is not the *reason* Jerusalem is destroyed — that is the sins of Israel. But someone had to serve as the instrument, and Nebuchadnezzar was selected for that role as a reward for this act of honor toward God. Even for so small a thing, this is what he receives.

### Patriarchs' Reward

*Kesheni meshalem sachar le-Avraham Yitzchak ve-Yaakov sheratzu lefanai k'susim al achat kama vekama* — when I eventually get around to rewarding Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who ran before Me like horses, their reward will be incomparably greater. The argument, then, is one of proportionality. If the tiny and almost incidental act of Nebuchadnezzar earns him such power, consider what the accumulated merit of the patriarchs will ultimately yield.

### Jeremiah Consoled

*V'linviim nishbar libi b'kirbi* — and for the prophets — which Rashi understands here as a reference to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — my heart was broken within me, I could not perceive where their ultimate reward would come from. *V'rachafu kol atzmotai hayiti k'ish shikkor v'kagever avru yayin* — all my bones trembled, I was like a drunken man, like a man overcome by wine. *Mipnei Hashem umipnei divrei kodsho* — because of God and because of His holy words. After God's explanation, Jeremiah is consoled: the very scale of Nebuchadnezzar's reward for so little is itself a guarantee of the infinitely greater reward that awaits those who truly served God.

## Four Steps Story

### Baladan's Letter

*Hani arba pisiot mai* — but what exactly are these four steps? The *gemara* now explains. *Ba'eis hi shalach Merodach Baladan ben Baladan Melech Bavel sefarim* — at that time Merodach Baladan the son of Baladan, king of Babylonia, sent letters and a gift to Hezekiah when he heard that Hezekiah had recovered from his illness. Why was this worthy of such a diplomatic gesture? *Mishum d'chala Chizkiyahu vayechazak* — merely because a king was sick and recovered? The *gemara* explains that another verse in Divrei Hayamim adds: *lidrosh hamofes asher haya ba'aretz* — because a wonder had been performed in the land.

### Sun Clock Wonder

What was the wonder? Rabbi Yochanan explains: *oso hayom shemeis bo Achaz shti sha'os haya* — on the day that Achaz, the wicked father of Hezekiah, died, the sun set ten hours early; the day had only two hours of light. God did this so that people would not come out to eulogize the wicked king and make a spectacle of his funeral. Then, *v'ki chala Chizkiyahu v'ispach hadrinu Kudsha Brich Hu l'hanach eser sha'ei* — when Hezekiah fell ill and recovered, God extended the day by ten hours. Ten hours taken away for the father, ten hours restored for the son.

The source is explicit: *Hineni meshiv tzel hama'alos asher yarda b'ma'alos Achaz bashemesh achornit eser ma'alos* — behold I will return the shadow of the sun clock of Achaz backward ten units. *Vatishev hashemesh eser ma'alos b'ma'alos asher yorada* — and the sun returned ten gradations on the steps where it had descended.

When Baladan heard of this, he said: *ika gavra ki hai velo be'ina leshadurei lei shalma* — there is such a great man, that the sun itself reversed for him, and I have not sent him greetings? He instructed his scribes: write to Shalma Lemalka Chizkiyah — peace to King Hezekiah; *Shalma Lekarta De'yerushalayim* — peace to the city of Jerusalem; *Shalma Le'elaha Raba* — peace to the great God.

### Nebuchadnezzar Corrects

*Nevuchadnetzar Safra De'baladan Hava* — Nebuchadnezzar was Baladan's scribe at the time. *Hahi sha'ata lo hava hatam* — at that moment he was not present, having been sent on some errand. When he returned and heard what had been written, he said: *karitu lei Elaha Raba vekatvitu lei levasof* — you called their God the great God, and you put God at the end of the letter? If you truly acknowledge Him as great, He comes first — before Jerusalem, and certainly before Hezekiah. He directed them to rewrite the letter: *Shalma Le'elaha Raba, Shalma Lekarta De'yerushalayim, Shalma Lemalka Chizkiyah* — God first, then His city, then the king.

The scribes responded with a famous phrase: *karyana de'igarta ihu leihavei parvanka* — let the one who reads the proclamation be the one to execute it. You want the letter rewritten your way? You go deliver it yourself.

### Four Steps Run

*Rahat batrei karahat arba pesiyot* — Nebuchadnezzar ran after the messengers who had already been dispatched. He ran four steps — or, according to the *Midrash* and *Yalkut*, three steps — to catch up with them and redirect the letter. These are the four steps he ran in God's honor.

### Gabriel Stops Him

*Ata Gavriel ve'ukmei* — the angel Gabriel came and stopped him after those four steps. Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *ilmale ba Gavriel ve'he'emido lo hava takana lesonayehem shel Yisrael* — had Gabriel not come and halted him precisely there, there would have been no remedy for Israel. If Nebuchadnezzar had been permitted to run further — a greater distance, a more sustained effort in God's honor — his reward would have been correspondingly greater and the destruction he could have wrought correspondingly worse. Gabriel therefore stopped him at exactly four steps, limiting both his merit and the scope of what he would be empowered to do.

Here a brief but memorable digression: on the day of the shiur the teacher had been overcharged fifty cents at a sidewalk fruit stand. A block and a half away, the vendor came running after him to return the change — a gesture reminiscent of stories told about the *Chafetz Chaim*. The relevance, as Rashi will soon make explicit, is the theme of *benei am ha-aretz* and the importance of not underestimating anyone's potential for honesty and greatness.

### Baladan's Name

*Mai Baladan ben Baladan* — why does Merodach Baladan share his father's name? The *gemara* explains: *Baladan malka hava v'ishtanei apay ve'hava ke'kalba* — Baladan was king, but his face changed and came to resemble a dog. His son took over the kingship. *Ki hava kasiv hava kasiv shemei u'shemei de'avuha* — whenever the son would write his own name, he would include his father's name alongside it, styling himself Baladan son of Baladan, as if his father were still reigning, in order to honor him. This fulfills the verse: *ben yechabeid av v'eved adonav* — a son honors his father and a servant his master.

### Son Honors Father

The second part of that verse — *v'eved adonav* — is illustrated by Nebuzaradan. The relevant verse reads: *u'vachodesh hachamishi b'asor lachodesh bishnas tsha esrei shana l'melech Nevuchadnezzar melech Bavel ba Nevuzaradan rav tabachim lifnei melech Bavel b'Yerushalayim* — in the fifth month, on the tenth of the month, in the nineteenth year of Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylonia, Nebuzaradan the chief slaughterer came before the king of Babylonia in Jerusalem.

But this raises an obvious question: *u'mi salik Nevuchadnezzar l'Yerushalayim* — since when was Nebuchadnezzar in Jerusalem? The verse elsewhere records that Tzidkiyahu was brought to Nebuchadnezzar in *Rivlah* — which Rabbi Abahu identifies as Antioch — clearly outside the Land of Israel. Rav Chisda and Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi offer two resolutions. One holds that Nebuzaradan had the image of Nebuchadnezzar engraved on his chariot — *dmus dyukno hayesa chakuka* — so that wherever he went, it was as though Nebuchadnezzar were present. The other holds that *eimateih yetirah hayetah lo mimenu* — Nebuzaradan had such intense awe of Nebuchadnezzar that he always felt his master's presence before him, *v'domeh k'mi she'omed lefanav* — as though he were perpetually standing in front of him. Either by an external symbol or through internalized reverence, the servant honored the master.

This, Rashi explains, is also the connection to the earlier teaching about the children of *amei ha-aretz*. The *gemara* will shortly note that descendants of Sennacherib and Haman sat and taught Torah. The point, as the *Maharsha* articulates it more fully, is not that one should honor those students because of their illustrious wicked ancestry, but rather that one should never give up on educating the children of bad homes. Hezekiah was the child of the wicked Achaz. The descendants of Sennacherib became *Shemayah and Avtalyon*. The message is: do not assume that a child's environment determines his fate. Teachers can influence students, and that responsibility must not be abandoned.

## Jerusalem's Destruction

### Iron Hammers Fail

Rava teaches: *t'ein t'las me'ah kudnayasa narga d'parzla d'shalt b'parzla shad'reih Nebuchadnezzar l'Nebuzaradan* — Nebuchadnezzar sent Nebuzaradan a burden of 300 mules laden with iron hammers so sharp they could break through iron itself. *Kulyahu balyu b'chada dasha d'Yerushalayim* — and all 300 were destroyed trying to break through a single gate of Jerusalem. As the verse says, *p'sachah yachad b'khishil v'kheyalfos yahalomun* — they came at its opening with hammers and axes and could not prevail.

### Heavenly Voice

Nebuzaradan then sought to withdraw. Whether it was Nebuzaradan or Nebuchadnezzar who hesitated, the concern voiced was: *mistafina d'la l'avdu bi k'heicha d'avdu b'Sancheiriv* — I am afraid they will do to me what they did to Sennacherib. Then *n'phek kahlah* — a heavenly voice emerged and said: *sh'var bar sh'var* — leaper son of a leaper, *m'to zimna d'mikdash d'yechariv v'heichala d'yikli* — the time has come, the Temple is to be destroyed and the *heichal* to be burned. Left with a single hammer, Nebuzaradan struck the gate *b'kupah* — with the back of the hammer, not even the sharp edge — *v'iftach* — and it opened. What 300 iron hammers could not accomplish, one blow accomplished once God permitted it.

He then slaughtered his way through the city until he reached the *heichal* and set it alight. The flames rose toward heaven. *Darchu bei min shamaya* — from heaven they trampled down the fire, so that it would be unmistakably clear that this was a this-worldly destruction and not a heavenly one. As the verse says: *Gat darach Hashem livtulat bat Yehuda* — God has trampled upon the daughter of Judah as in a winepress. The trampling here refers to pressing down the flames.

### Nebuzaradan Humbled

When Nebuzaradan began to feel haughty at his conquest, *nafak bat kol ve'amra* — a heavenly voice came forth and said: *ama ketila katalt* — you have killed a nation that was already killed. *Heichala kalya kalit* — you have burned a Temple that was already burned. *Kimcha techina techinat* — you have ground flour that was already flour. Do not take any credit. The verse cited is: *Kechi rechain utchaini kemach* — take a millstone and grind flour. Prophesying the exile, the verse says *kemach* — flour — not wheat, because what you are grinding was already flour. Nebuzaradan was merely actualizing a divine decree that had already been issued.

### Zechariah's Blood

*Chaza dmyei deZecharya dehava ka ratach* — Nebuzaradan then saw the blood of Zechariah the prophet, which was still boiling. This Zechariah — a *kohen* and *navi* who had prophesied the destruction of Jerusalem and was killed by the people in the Temple courtyard for it — had blood that had never ceased to boil. This story is well known from the *Kinot* of Tisha b'Av.

Nebuzaradan asked the people what the boiling substance was. They told him it was blood of sacrifices that had been spilled. He slaughtered an animal and compared the blood — it was not the same. He threatened to flay their skin with an iron comb if they did not tell him the truth. They confessed: this was the blood of a *kohen* and prophet who prophesied against Israel about the destruction of Jerusalem, *vekatluhu* — and they killed him.

*Ana mefayisne leh* — I will appease him, said Nebuzaradan. He brought the *Rabbanan* and slaughtered them over the blood — perhaps because as leaders they bore responsibility. The blood did not stop. He brought schoolchildren — innocent, surely offering their blood should suffice. Still it did not stop. He brought the young *Kohanim*. Still it did not stop. *Ad dikatlinhu tishim ve-arba'in ribo* — until he had slaughtered 940,000 people, and still the blood boiled. Finally Nebuzaradan approached and said: *Zecharia Zecharia tovim shebahen ibadeti nicha lach de-aktilinnhu lechulhu* — Zechariah, Zechariah, I have destroyed the best of them; do you want me to kill them all? *Miyad nach* — immediately the blood stopped boiling.

### Nebuzaradan Repents

*Hirher teshuva bedatei* — Nebuzaradan began to contemplate *teshuva*. He said to himself: *mah hem she-lo ibdu ela nefesh achas kach* — these people killed only one innocent person, and look how God demanded such justice for that one life. *Hahu gavra mah tehei alei* — I, who have now killed hundreds of thousands in my misguided attempt to appease the blood — what will become of me? *Azal shadar priteita leveiteih ve-isgayar* — he sent a final testament to his house and converted.

The *gemara* then records: *Tanu Rabbanan* — the Rabbanan taught: Na'aman was a *ger toshav*, a Noahide who worshipped God but did not become part of the Jewish people. But *Nevuzaradan ger tzedek hayah* — Nebuzaradan became a full righteous convert.

### Wicked Descendants

*Mibnei banav shel Sisra limdu torah biyerushalayim* — the descendants of Sisera taught Torah in Jerusalem. *Mibnei banav shel Sancheriv limdu torah berabbim* — the descendants of Sennacherib taught Torah to the masses. *U-man ninhu Shemaya ve-Avtalyon* — who were they? Shemayah and Avtalyon. *Mibnei banav shel Haman limdu torah bivnei brak* — and the descendants of Haman taught Torah in Bnei Brak. No matter what family one comes from, the descendants can become the greatest of Torah teachers.

### Descendants Rejected

*Ve-af mibnei banav shel oso rasha bikesh hakadosh baruch hu lehachnisan tachas kanfei hashchina* — even the descendants of that wicked man, Nebuchadnezzar himself, God wished to bring under His wings. *Amru malachei hashareis lifnei hakadosh baruch hu* — but the ministering angels said before God: *Ribono shel olam mi she-hechriv es beischa ve-saraf es heichalecha tachnis attah tachas kanfei hashchina* — the one who destroyed Your house and burned Your Temple — You will bring him under Your wings?

*Hainu dechsiv ripeinu es bavel velo nispasa* — that is what is meant by the verse: I sought to heal Babylonia, but it was not healed, because ultimately the descendants of Nebuchadnezzar were not accepted. Yet the man who actually carried out the destruction — Nebuzaradan — was accepted as a convert. There is something striking in this contrast: the one who followed orders, who personally slaughtered hundreds of thousands, was accepted in *teshuva*; while the family of the one who gave the orders was not. The *gemara* leaves us to grapple with the question of who may be accepted after terrible deeds, and under what circumstances.

Ulla and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani dispute what the verse *ripeinu es bavel velo nispasa* refers to: Ulla says it refers to Nebuchadnezzar and his descendants; Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says it refers to the bitter rivers of Babylonia, which were never healed and whose water remained bitter.

## Ammon & Moab

### Bad Neighbors

*Ulla amar Ammon u'Moav shcheinei ra'im l'Yerushalayim havu* — Ulla said: Ammon and Moab were bad neighbors to Jerusalem. *Keivan d'sham'inhu l'nevi'ei d'ka mitnabu l'churbana d'Yerushalayim* — when they heard the prophets prophesying the destruction of Jerusalem, they seized the opportunity and sent word to Nebuchadnezzar: *puk v'ta* — come, this is your moment.

### Fear of Fate

Nebuchadnezzar was reluctant: *mistafina d'la l'yavdu bi ki heichi d'avdu b'kama'ei* — I am afraid they will do to me what they did to the earlier ones, meaning Sennacherib and Sisera and those who had previously tried and failed to destroy Israel.

### Messages to Attack

Ammon and Moab then sent him a series of reassurances, each quoting and interpreting verses from Mishlei. First: *ki ein ha'ish b'veito halach b'derech merachok* — God is not at home, He has gone on a long journey. The *gemara* notes that *ish* here means God, as in *Hashem ish milchama*. Nebuchadnezzar replied that God was probably nearby and would return when His people were in danger. They countered: *halach b'derech merachok* — no, He has gone on a long way journey and is not coming back soon. He replied that there were still righteous people who would cry out and bring God back. They countered with another verse: *tzror hakesef lakach b'yado* — He has taken His money with Him and is not coming back; and *kesef* means the righteous, as derived from a verse in Hoshea connecting *kesef* with the redemption from Egypt. Even the righteous have departed. Nebuchadnezzar replied that the people might repent and pray God back. They countered with a final verse: *l'yom hakisei yavo l'veito* — God has set a time for His return, and *kisei* means a designated time, as in *bakese l'yom chagenu*. He has set a time of seventy years before He returns. So for now He has entirely left and will not return for the foreseeable future. The time is set; this is the window.

### Winter & Mountains

Nebuchadnezzar raised another objection: *sitva hu v'ramia tina d'ati etaltal m'mitra* — it is winter, the rains and snow will prevent a campaign. This hesitation is itself noteworthy — there is a certain reluctance throughout this passage that may account for the *gemara*'s willingness to consider some small measure of merit even in Nebuchadnezzar. Indeed, the siege did begin in *Tevet*, in winter, as we mark on *Asara b'Tevet*. They responded: *ta shina d'tura* — come by way of the mountains, and the mountains will shelter you from the elements, as the verse says: *shalchu char moshel eretz misela midbara el har bat tziyon* — send a messenger through the rock of the wilderness to the mountain of Zion.

### Graves as Quarters

Nebuchadnezzar objected further: *ee asina leit li dukhta di asvina beh* — if I come, I will have nowhere to quarter my troops. They replied: *kivros shelahem me'ulim mi-paltrin shelcha* — their graveyards are better than your palace. As Rashi explains, the Israelites buried in caves, and those cave-graves offered more than enough space to quarter an army.

The proof-text is: *ba-et ha-hi ne'um Hashem yotziu et atzmot malchei yehuda ve'et atzmot sarav ve'et atzmot hakohanim ve'et atzmot hanevi'im ve'et atzmot yoshvei yerushalayim mikivreihem ushetachum lashemesh velayarei'ach ulechol tzeva hashamayim asher ahevum va'asher avadum va'asher halchu achareihem* — at that time God declared that they would take out the bones of the kings of Judah, and of their officers, and of the *Kohanim*, and of the prophets, and of the inhabitants of Jerusalem from their graves, and spread them before the sun and the moon and all the host of heaven — those heavenly bodies which the people had loved and served and followed. The bones are taken from the caves because Nebuchadnezzar's troops are quartered in those very caves, emptied out to make room.
