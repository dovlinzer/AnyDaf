# Chagigah 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Heavens & Angels

### Ma'on & Night Singing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָעוֹן — שֶׁבּוֹ כִּיתּוֹת שֶׁל מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, שֶׁאוֹמְרוֹת שִׁירָה בַּלַּיְלָה וְחָשׁוֹת בַּיּוֹם, מִפְּנֵי כְבוֹדָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹמָם יְצַוֶּה ה׳ חַסְדּוֹ וּבַלַּיְלָה שִׁירֹה עִמִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** ***Ma'on*,** habitation, **is where** there are **groups of ministering angels who recite song at night and are silent during the day out of respect for Israel,** in order not to compete with their songs, **as it is stated: "By day the Lord will command His kindness, and in the night His song is with me"** (Psalms 42:9), indicating that the song of the angels is with God only at night.

The Gemara's discussion of the seven heavens continues into 13b, where we arrive at *ma'on*, the fifth level in the ascending hierarchy of heavens. Just as the previous heaven, *zevul*, was identified as the place where the angel Michael offers *korbanos* in a celestial temple — drawing on verses from the *nevi'im* that associate *zevul* both with Solomon's *Beis HaMikdash* and with God's dwelling on high — we now ascend to *ma'on*.

*Ma'on shebo kito shel malachei hashareis she'omros shira balailah, vachashos bayom mipnei kevodan shel Yisrael.* Ma'on is where companies of ministering angels sing *shira* to God — but only at night. During the day, they are silent, in deference to the honor of the Jewish people, so that Israel may offer its own song and recite *Kaddish*. This, the Gemara explains, is the meaning of the verse: *yomam yitzaveh Hashem chasdo uvalailah shiro imi* — in the day God commands His kindness (allowing Israel to sing), while at night, His song is with Him (offered by the angels). It is worth noting that while the Gemara asserts the angels' song takes place specifically in *ma'on*, the source proving that *ma'on* is a heaven is the verse *hashkifa mima'on kodshecha min hashamayim* — "look down from your holy abode, from the heavens" — which identifies *ma'on* with the heavens, though precisely why the angelic singing is located there rather than elsewhere is asserted rather than derived from a verse.

### Torah Study at Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה בַּלַּיְלָה — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מוֹשֵׁךְ עָלָיו חוּט שֶׁל חֶסֶד בַּיּוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹמָם יְצַוֶּה ה׳ חַסְדּוֹ״. וּמָה טַעַם ״יוֹמָם יְצַוֶּה ה׳ חַסְדּוֹ״ — מִשּׁוּם ״וּבַלַּיְלָה שִׁירֹה עִמִּי״. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה שֶׁהוּא דּוֹמֶה לְלַיְלָה, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מוֹשֵׁךְ עָלָיו חוּט שֶׁל חֶסֶד לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא שֶׁהוּא דּוֹמֶה לְיוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹמָם יְצַוֶּה ה׳ חַסְדּוֹ וּבַלַּיְלָה שִׁירֹה עִמִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the aforementioned verse, **Reish Lakish said: Whoever occupies** himself **with Torah at night, the Holy One, Blessed be He, extends a thread of kindness over him by day, as it is stated: "By day, the Lord will command His kindness," and what is the reason** that **"by day, the Lord will command His kindness"? Because "and in the night His song,"** i.e., the song of Torah, **"is with me." And some say** that **Reish Lakish said: Whoever occupies himself with Torah in this world, which is comparable to night, the Holy One, Blessed be He, extends a thread of kindness over him in the World-to-Come, which is comparable to day, as it is stated: "By day, the Lord will command His kindness, and in the night His song is with me."**

Before returning to the main enumeration, the Gemara inserts a teaching of Reish Lakish. *Kol ha'osek baTorah balailah, Hakadosh Baruch Hu moshech alav chut shel chesed bayom* — whoever studies Torah at night, God draws upon him a thread of kindness during the day. The proof-text is the same verse: *yomam yitzaveh Hashem chasdo* — why? *Uvalailah shiro imi* — because his Torah was God's song in the nighttime. One must appreciate this teaching in its original context, when there was no artificial light and nighttime was simply when people slept. A person who sacrificed sleep to study Torah was going to exceptional lengths, and the deliberate use of one's discretionary hours reveals the true ordering of one's priorities. There is also a striking theological dimension here: Torah itself is called *shira*, song — a point that Rav Soloveitchik elaborated at length in his discussions of the relationship between *Torah* and *tefillah* as modes of connecting to God.

A second version of the teaching (*ika d'amri*) widens the frame: *kol ha'osek baTorah ba'olam hazeh* — anyone who studies Torah in this world, which is analogous to night, God extends upon him a thread of kindness in the world to come, which is analogous to day. Here the *olam hazeh* / *olam haba* parallel maps onto the night/day imagery of the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי כׇּל הַפּוֹסֵק מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וְעוֹסֵק בְּדִבְרֵי שִׂיחָה — מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ גַּחֲלֵי רְתָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַקּוֹטְפִים מַלּוּחַ עֲלֵי שִׂיחַ וְשֹׁרֶשׁ רְתָמִים לַחְמָם״. וּמְנָלַן דְּאִיקְּרִי שָׁמַיִם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַשְׁקִיפָה מִמְּעוֹן קׇדְשְׁךָ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the same matter, **Rabbi Levi said: Anyone who pauses from words of Torah to occupy himself with mundane conversation will be fed with the coals of the broom tree, as it is stated: "They pluck saltwort [*maluaḥ*] with wormwood [*alei siaḥ*], and the roots of the broom tree [*retamim*] are their food"** (Job 30:4). The exposition is as follows: Those who pluck, i.e., pause, from learning Torah, which was given upon two tablets, *luḥot*, which sounds similar to *maluaḥ*, for the purpose of *siaḥ*, idle chatter, are punished by having to eat coals made from "the roots of the broom tree." **And from where do we** derive **that** *Ma'on* **is called heaven? As it is stated: "Look forth from Your holy *Ma'on*, from heaven"** (Deuteronomy 26:15).

The Gemara then adds a related teaching from Rav Levi: *kol haposek midivrei Torah ve'osek bedivrei sicha* — whoever interrupts his Torah to engage in idle conversation, *ma'achilin oso gachalei retamim*, he is fed the burning coals of the broom tree — a particularly unpleasant image — as derived from the verse describing those who eat the roots of the broom tree as their bread. The Gemara then returns to the question of how we know *ma'on* is called *shamayim*, resolving it from the verse cited above. The preceding digression about learning at night, it turns out, was prompted by the verse that also proves the identity of *ma'on*.

### Machon & Weather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכוֹן — שֶׁבּוֹ אוֹצְרוֹת שָׁלֶג וְאוֹצְרוֹת בָּרָד, וַעֲלִיַּית טְלָלִים רָעִים, וַעֲלִיַּית אֲגָלִים, וְחַדְרָהּ שֶׁל סוּפָה [וּסְעָרָה], וּמְעָרָה שֶׁל קִיטוֹר. וְדַלְתוֹתֵיהֶן אֵשׁ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִפְתַּח ה׳ לְךָ אֶת אוֹצָרוֹ הַטּוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** ***Makhon*,** dwelling place, **is where there are storehouses of snow and storehouses of hail, and the upper chamber of harmful dews, and the upper chamber of drops, and the room of tempests and storms, and the cave of mist. And the doors** of all these are made of **fire.** How do we know that there are storehouses for evil things? **For it is stated: "The Lord will open for you His good storehouse,** the heavens" (Deuteronomy 28:12), which indicates the existence of a storehouse that contains the opposite of good.

The next heaven is *machon*. *Shebo otzaros shel sheleg ve'otzaros shel barad* — this is where God maintains storehouses of snow and hail. Beyond these, *machon* contains *aliyos telalim ra'im* — attics of destructive rains — as well as storehouses of *agalim* (destructive rain, per Rashi) and violent whirlwinds, and a cave filled with smoke, whose doors are made of fire. All the destructive meteorological forces in the world originate here.

The proof that God maintains such a storehouse is derived from the verse *yiftach Hashem lecha es otzaro hatov* — "God shall open for you His good storehouse" — which implies by contrast that there is also a bad storehouse, and that is *machon*. The connection of the word *machon* to the heavens is established from the verse *ve'ata sishma hashamayim machon shivtecha* — "You shall hear from the heavens, the established place of Your dwelling." Once again, while the identification of *machon* with the heavens is established textually, the precise assignment of these meteorological storehouses to *machon* specifically is asserted rather than derived.

### David's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי בִּרְקִיעָא אִיתַנְהוּ? הָנֵי בְּאַרְעָא אִיתַנְהוּ! דִּכְתִיב: ״הַלְלוּ אֶת ה׳ מִן הָאָרֶץ תַּנִּינִים וְכׇל תְּהוֹמוֹת אֵשׁ וּבָרָד שֶׁלֶג וְקִיטוֹר רוּחַ סְעָרָה עוֹשָׂה דְבָרוֹ״! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דָּוִד בִּיקֵּשׁ עֲלֵיהֶם רַחֲמִים, וְהוֹרִידָן לָאָרֶץ. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, ״לֹא אֵל חָפֵץ רֶשַׁע אָתָּה לֹא יְגוּרְךָ (בִּמְגוּרְךָ) רָע״. צַדִּיק אַתָּה ה׳, לֹא יָגוּר בִּמְגוּרְךָ רָע. וּמְנָלַן דְּאִיקְּרִי ״שָׁמַיִם״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַתָּה תִּשְׁמַע הַשָּׁמַיִם מְכוֹן שִׁבְתֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question: With regard to **these** things listed above, are they **located in heaven?** It is obvious that **they** are **located on the earth. As it is written: "Praise the Lord from the earth, sea monsters and all depths, fire and hail, snow and mist, stormy wind, fulfilling His word"** (Psalms 148:7–8). The verse seems to indicate that all these things are found on the earth. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: David requested mercy with regard to them,** that they should not remain in heaven, **and He brought them down to earth. He said before Him: Master of the Universe, "You are not a God that has pleasure in wickedness, evil shall not sojourn with You"** (Psalms 5:5). In other words, **You are righteous, O Lord.** Nothing **evil should sojourn in Your vicinity.** Rather, it is better that they remain close to us. **And from where do we** derive **that** this place **is called "heaven"? As it is written: "And You shall hear** in **heaven, the *Makhon* of Your dwelling"** (I Kings 8:39).

An important challenge presents itself: the Gemara asks, *hani berakia isnehu* — are these things really in the heaven? The verse in Tehillim says *halelu es Hashem min ha'aretz* — praise God from the land — and then lists sea monsters, the depths, fire, hail, snow, smoke, and whirlwinds all as praising from *ha'aretz*, the earth. So these forces are apparently on earth, not in the heavens.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav resolves the contradiction: *David bikesh aleihem rachamim vehoridan la'aretz* — David prayed for compassion on these things and brought them down from heaven to earth. David argued before God: *lo Eil chafetz resha ata, lo yigurcha ra* — You are not a God who desires evil, and evil cannot dwell with You. Therefore, these destructive forces should not remain in the heavens with God; they belong below. One might note that this prayer seems to be primarily for God's honor rather than for human benefit — though perhaps the logic is that once these forces are naturalized into the earth as ordinary meteorological phenomena, they are less directly attributable to divine displeasure and may be less spiritually weighty in their destructiveness.

### Aravot Heaven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲרָבוֹת — שֶׁבּוֹ צֶדֶק מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה, גִּנְזֵי חַיִּים וְגִנְזֵי שָׁלוֹם וְגִנְזֵי בְרָכָה, וְנִשְׁמָתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים, וְרוּחוֹת וּנְשָׁמוֹת שֶׁעֲתִיד[וֹת] לְהִיבָּרְאוֹת, וְטַל שֶׁעָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחֲיוֹת בּוֹ מֵתִים. צֶדֶק וּמִשְׁפָּט — דִּכְתִיב: ״צֶדֶק וּמִשְׁפָּט מְכוֹן כִּסְאֶךָ״. צְדָקָה — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּלְבַּשׁ צְדָקָה כַּשִּׁרְיָן״. גִּנְזֵי חַיִּים — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי עִמְּךָ מְקוֹר חַיִּים״. וְגִנְזֵי שָׁלוֹם — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרָא לוֹ ה׳ שָׁלוֹם״. וְגִנְזֵי בְרָכָה — דִּכְתִיב: ״יִשָּׂא בְרָכָה מֵאֵת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** ***Aravot*,** skies, is the firmament **that contains righteousness; justice; righteousness,** i.e., charity; **the treasuries of life; the treasuries of peace; the treasuries of blessing; the souls of the righteous; the spirits and souls that are to be created; and the dew that the Holy One, Blessed be He, will use to revive the dead.** The Gemara proves this statement: **Righteousness and justice** are found in heaven, **as it is written: "Righteousness and justice are the foundation of Your throne"** (Psalms 89:15); **righteousness, as it is written: "And He donned righteousness as armor"** (Isaiah 59:17); **the treasuries of life, as it is written: "For with You is the source of life"** (Psalms 36:10). **And the treasuries of peace** are found in heaven, **as it is written: "And he called Him the Lord of peace"** (Judges 6:24), implying that peace is God's name and is therefore found close to Him. **And the treasuries of blessing, as it is written: "He shall receive a blessing from the Lord"** (Psalms 24:5).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁמָתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיְתָה נֶפֶשׁ אֲדוֹנִי צְרוּרָה בִּצְרוֹר הַחַיִּים אֵת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״. רוּחוֹת וּנְשָׁמוֹת שֶׁעֲתִיד[וֹת] לְהִיבָּרְאוֹת — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי רוּחַ מִלְּפָנַי יַעֲטוֹף וּנְשָׁמוֹת אֲנִי עָשִׂיתִי״. וְטַל שֶׁעָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחְיוֹת בּוֹ מֵתִים — דִּכְתִיב: ״גֶּשֶׁם נְדָבוֹת תָּנִיף אֱלֹהִים נַחֲלָתְךָ וְנִלְאָה אַתָּה כוֹנַנְתָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The souls of the righteous** are found in heaven, **as it is written: "And the soul of my master shall be bound in the bundle of life with the Lord, your God"** (I Samuel 25:29). **Spirits and souls that are to be created** are found there, **as it is written: "For the spirit that enwraps itself is from Me, and the souls that I have made"** (Isaiah 57:16), which indicates that the spirit to be released into the world, wrapped around a body, is located close to God. **The dew that the Holy One, Blessed be He, will use to revive the dead** is found in heaven, **as it is written: "A bountiful rain You will pour down, God; when Your inheritance was weary, You confirmed it"** (Psalms 68:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁם אוֹפַנִּים וּשְׂרָפִים וְחַיּוֹת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, וְכִסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד, מֶלֶךְ אֵל חַי רָם וְנִשָּׂא שׁוֹכֵן עֲלֵיהֶם בָּעֲרָבוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״סוֹלּוּ לָרוֹכֵב בָּעֲרָבוֹת בְּיָהּ שְׁמוֹ״, וּמְנָלַן דְּאִיקְּרִי ״שָׁמַיִם״? אָתְיָא ״רְכִיבָה״ ״רְכִיבָה״, כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״סוֹלּוּ לָרוֹכֵב בָּעֲרָבוֹת״ וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״רוֹכֵב שָׁמַיִם בְּעֶזְרֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** in the firmaments, are the ***ofanim*,** the **seraphim,** the **holy divine creatures, and the ministering angels, and the Throne of Glory. The King, God,** the **living, lofty, exalted One dwells above them in *Aravot*, as it is stated: "Extol Him Who rides upon the skies [*Aravot*], Whose name is God"** (Psalms 68:5). **And from where do we** derive **that** *Aravot* **is called "heaven"?** This is **learned** by using a verbal analogy between two instances of **"rides"** and **"rides": Here, it is written: "Extol Him Who rides upon the skies [*Aravot*]," and there, it is written: "Who rides upon the heaven as your help"** (Deuteronomy 33:26).

We now arrive at *aravot*, the highest of the heavens — the heaven in which, as this week's *parsha* states, God rides: *solu larochev ba'aravot beka shemo*. The identification of *aravot* with *shamayim* is established through a *gezeirah shavah*: the same word *rochev* (to ride) appears both in *solu larochev ba'aravot* and in *rochev shamayim be'ezrecha*, linking *aravot* to *shamayim*.

What is found in *aravot*? The Gemara lists: *tzedek, mishpat, utzedaka* — justice, law, and righteousness; *ginzei chayim, ginzei shalom, ginzei bracha* — storehouses of life, peace, and blessing; *nishmatotan shel tzadikim* — the souls of the righteous; *ruach veruchos veneshamot she'atid lehibarot* — winds, spirits, and future souls yet to be created; and finally *tal she'atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu lehachayos bo meitim* — the dew God will use to resurrect the dead at the time of *techiyas hameitim*. Each of these is supported by a proof-text: righteousness and justice are derived from *tzedek umishpat machon kisse'echa* (the word *machon* here refers to *aravot*, since all of these are near God's throne), righteousness from God clothing Himself like armor, the storehouses of blessing from *yissa bracha me'et Hashem*, the souls of the righteous from the verse *vehayetah nefesh adoni tzerurah bitzror hachayim et Hashem Elokecha*, and the future souls from *ki ruach milfanai ya'atof uneshamot ani asiti*.

In *aravot* are also found the *ofanim*, *serafim*, *chayos hakodesh*, *malachei hasharet*, and the *kisei hakavod* — the divine throne itself. God, the living and exalted King, dwells above them all: *melech el chai veram venissa shochein aleihem*. A subtle point: the text says *me'aleihem*, above them — already introducing a separation between God and even the highest of celestial beings.

It is worth pausing to observe the structure of the seven heavens as a whole. The destructive meteorological forces — hail, snow, destructive winds — are stored immediately below the highest heaven. Everything malevolent is close to God but just one level below. This seems to be the Gemara's way of wrestling with the problem of evil: everything ultimately comes from God, yet the Gemara seeks to create some distance. David's prayer then pushes evil even further away, down to the earth. The contrast between the heaven of destructive forces and the heaven of righteousness, peace, life, and the souls of the righteous, all immediately in God's presence, is stark and deliberate.

### God's Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחֹשֶׁךְ וְעָנָן וַעֲרָפֶל מַקִּיפִין אוֹתוֹ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָשֶׁת חֹשֶׁךְ סִתְרוֹ סְבִיבוֹתָיו סוּכָּתוֹ חֶשְׁכַת מַיִם עָבֵי שְׁחָקִים״. וּמִי אִיכָּא חֲשׁוֹכָא קַמֵּי שְׁמַיָּא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״הוּא גָּלֵא עַמִּיקָתָא וּמְסַתְּרָתָא יָדַע מָה בַחֲשׁוֹכָא וּנְהוֹרָא עִמֵּהּ שְׁרֵא״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּבָתֵּי גַוָּאֵי, הָא בְּבָתֵּי בָרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And darkness and clouds and fog surround Him, as it is stated: "He made darkness His hiding place, His pavilion round about Him; darkness of waters, thick clouds of the skies"** (Psalms 18:12). The Gemara asks: **And is there darkness before Heaven,** i.e., before God? **But isn't it written: "He reveals deep and secret things, He knows what is in the darkness, and the light dwells with Him"** (Daniel 2:22), demonstrating that only light, not darkness, is found with God? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** verse, which states that only light dwells with Him, is referring **to** the **inner houses,** where there is only light; **that** source, according to which He is surrounded by darkness, is referring **to** the **outer houses.**

Yet if God dwells in *aravot* together with the angels, the souls of the righteous, the *kisei hakavod*, and all these things, a problem arises: God is described in other verses as surrounded by darkness. *Vehoshech ve'anan ve'arafel mekifin oto* — darkness and cloud and thick cloud surround Him. Yet another verse says *hu gale amikta umsatarta yada mah bachashocha venehora imei* — God knows what is in the darkness, and light is with Him. Does darkness or light attend God?

*Lo kashya* — no contradiction. *Ha bevivtei gavai, ha bevivtei barai* — one refers to the inner chamber, the other to the outer. God Himself is in an inner room where there is light; the darkness that surrounds God is in the outer room. This is the sense of *betei barai* and *betei gavai* — inner and outer chambers. Even within *aravot*, God's own essential presence is screened off behind darkness from everything else. The angels, the throne, the souls of the righteous — all of these occupy, as it were, the outer chamber, while God's innermost essence remains inaccessible, separated by cloud and darkness.

This inner/outer structure is evocative of the Temple: the *azarah* with its blood and animal offerings, then the *heichal* kept perpetually lit, and then the *kodesh hakodashim* — always dark except for the brief entrance of the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur. The same logic of nested sanctity and concealment applies in the heavenly realm. The Gemara seems deeply invested in maintaining the idea that evil is distant from God and that God's own essence remains forever hidden and separate.

## Limits of Knowledge

### Highest Rakia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: עוֹד רָקִיעַ אֶחָד יֵשׁ לְמַעְלָה מֵרָאשֵׁי הַחַיּוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּדְמוּת עַל רָאשֵׁי הַחַיָּה רָקִיעַ כְּעֵין הַקֶּרַח הַנּוֹרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: There is one more firmament** above these, which is **above the heads of the divine creatures, as it is written: "And over the heads of the divine creatures there was the likeness of a firmament, like the color of the terrible ice"** (Ezekiel 1:22).

Rav Acha bar Yaakov introduces a further remark: *od rakia echad yesh lema'alah merashei hachayos* — there is yet another *rakia* above the heads of the heavenly *chayos*. The verse in Yechezkel states *udemut al rashei hachaya rakia ke'ein hakerach hanora* — and above the heads of the heavenly angels is a *rakia* like awesome frost. This highest *rakia* is where God Himself dwells, separate even from the highest angels. The celestial beings described throughout the preceding passage — the *ofanim*, *serafim*, *chayos hakodesh* — are therefore not in God's immediate presence; there is still another *rakia* above them.

### Permitted Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן יֵשׁ לְךָ רְשׁוּת לְדַבֵּר, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — אֵין לְךָ רְשׁוּת לְדַבֵּר, שֶׁכֵּן כָּתוּב בְּסֵפֶר בֶּן סִירָא: ״בַּמּוּפְלָא מִמְּךָ אַל תִּדְרוֹשׁ, וּבַמְכוּסֶּה מִמְּךָ אַל תַּחְקוֹר. בַּמֶּה שֶׁהוֹרְשֵׁיתָ הִתְבּוֹנֵן, אֵין לְךָ עֵסֶק בַּנִּסְתָּרוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Until here, you have permission to speak; from this** point **forward you do not have permission to speak, as it is written in the book of Ben Sira: Seek not things concealed from you, nor search those hidden from you. Reflect on that which is permitted to you; you have no business with secret matters.**

This leads directly to the limits of permitted knowledge. *Ad kan yesh lecha reshut ledaber* — until this point, you may speak and discuss the contents of the various heavens. *Mikan va'eilach, ein lecha reshut ledaber* — from here on, you may not discuss what is beyond. The source for this boundary is a citation from the book of Ben Sira, which, while belonging to the *sefarim chitzoniyim*, always held a special place for *Chazal*: *bemufla mimcha al tidrosh, uvemechuseh mimcha al tachkor, bemah she'hursheita hitbonen, ein lecha esek benistarot* — do not investigate that which is beyond you, do not probe what is hidden, contemplate only what you have been given permission to understand, and do not involve yourself with hidden matters.

This passage distills the essential principle of our *mishnah* limiting the exploration of esoteric knowledge. It also resonates with a broader philosophical tradition. The Rambam, in his approach to divine attributes, denied not only anthropomorphisms but any positive descriptive language applied to God, on the grounds that all such language, drawn from human experience, fundamentally diminishes and misrepresents the divine. The Kabbalistic tradition, which employs vivid and dynamic imagery for God, addresses this by positing the *Ein Sof* — the infinite, about which nothing whatsoever can be said — and then describing only the *sefirot*, which are not the essence of God but emanations. In precisely this way, the Gemara here presents a highest *rakia* about which nothing can be said, and lower levels which may be discussed. The parallel is genuine and instructive.

## Nevuchadnezzar & Distance

### Heavenly Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: מָה תְּשׁוּבָה הֱשִׁיבַתּוּ בַּת קוֹל לְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר ״אֶעֱלֶה עַל בָּמֳתֵי עָב אֶדַּמֶּה לְעֶלְיוֹן״ — יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: רָשָׁע בֶּן רָשָׁע, בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל נִמְרוֹד הָרָשָׁע, שֶׁהִמְרִיד כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ עָלָיו בְּמַלְכוּתוֹ —
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said: What response did the Divine Voice provide to that wicked man,** Nebuchadnezzar, **when he said: "I will ascend above the heights of the clouds; I will be like the Most High"** (Isaiah 14:14), thereby intending to rise to heaven? **A Divine Voice came and said to him: Wicked man, son of a wicked man, descendant,** i.e., follower of the ways, **of Nimrod the wicked, who caused the entire world to rebel against Him during** the time of **his reign.**

A *baraita* is now introduced, presented by Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai, which continues the theme of the heavens but approaches it from a different direction — the immeasurable distance between humanity and God. *Mah teshuvah heishivatu bat kol le'oto rasha* — what answer did the *bat kol* give to that wicked man, namely Nevuchadnetzar, *besha'ah she'amar*, when he declared *eleh bamotei av adameh le'elyon* — I will ascend upon the high clouds and be like the Most High?

The *bat kol* responded: *rasha ben rasha* — wicked person, son of a wicked person. Tosafos notes that the phrase *ben beno shel Nimrod harasha* does not mean literal biological descent but spiritual lineage: Nevuchadnetzar was the spiritual grandson of Nimrod, *she'himrid kol ha'olam kulo alav bemalchuso* — who incited all of humanity to rebel against God. Nimrod's name, derived from the word *mered* (rebellion), and his role in the *migdal Bavel* as imagined by the Midrashic tradition, made him the paradigmatic figure of human arrogance against the divine. And here is Nevuchadnetzar standing in that tradition.

### Journey to God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה שְׁנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָדָם — שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְמֵי שְׁנוֹתֵינוּ בָהֶם שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה וְאִם בִּגְבוּרוֹת שְׁמוֹנִים שָׁנָה״. וַהֲלֹא מִן הָאָרֶץ עַד לָרָקִיעַ מַהֲלַךְ חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה, וְעוֹבְיוֹ שֶׁל רָקִיעַ מַהֲלַךְ חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה, וְכֵן בֵּין כׇּל רָקִיעַ וְרָקִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **How many are the years of man? Seventy years, as it is stated: "The span of our life is seventy years, or if we are strong, eighty years"** (Psalms 90:10). **Now is** there **not from the earth to the firmament a walking** distance **of five hundred years, and the thickness of the firmament** itself is **a walking** distance **of five hundred years, and a similar** distance exists **between each and every one of the firmaments?**

But the *bat kol*'s answer is not merely an insult — it is a demonstration of the profound absurdity of Nevuchadnetzar's claim. *Kamma shenosav shel adam* — how long does a human being live? *Shivim shana* — seventy years. *Ve'im bigevuros shemonim shana* — eighty at most.

Now consider the journey. *Min ha'aretz ad ha-rakia* — from the earth to the first heaven is a journey of 500 years. *Ve'ovyo shel rakia* — the thickness of that first heaven is another 500 years. And then *bein kol rakia verakia* — between each successive heaven is another 500 years of travel, and each heaven is 500 years thick. With seven heavens, one is already looking at thousands of years simply to traverse them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעְלָה מֵהֶן — חַיּוֹת הַקֹּדֶשׁ. רַגְלֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּם, קַרְסוּלֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, שׁוֹקֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, רְכוּבֵּי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, יַרְכֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, גּוּפֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, צַוְּארֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, רָאשֵׁי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, קַרְנֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן. לְמַעְלָה מֵהֶן — כִּסֵּא כָּבוֹד. רַגְלֵי כִּסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, כִּסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן, מֶלֶךְ אֵל חַי וְקַיָּם רָם וְנִשָּׂא שׁוֹכֵן עֲלֵיהֶם, וְאַתָּה אָמַרְתָּ: ״אֶעֱלֶה עַל בָּמֳתֵי עָב אֶדַּמֶּה לְעֶלְיוֹן״ — ״אַךְ אֶל שְׁאוֹל תּוּרָד אֶל יַרְכְּתֵי בוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** And **above them,** above all the firmaments, **are the divine creatures. The feet of the divine creatures correspond** in distance **to all** the firmaments; **the ankles of the animals correspond to all of them, the shins of the animals correspond to all of them, the knees of the animals correspond to all of them, the thighs of the animals correspond to all of them, the bodies of the animals correspond to all of them, the necks of the animals correspond to all of them, the heads of the animals correspond to all of them,** and **the horns of the animals correspond to all of them. Above them** is the **Throne of Glory: The feet of the Throne of Glory correspond to all of them, the Throne of Glory corresponds to all of them,** and the **living, almighty, lofty, exalted King dwells above them. And you,** Nebuchadnezzar, **say: "I will ascend above the heights of the clouds; I will be like the Most High"** (Isaiah 14:15), but the next verse states: **"Yet you shall be brought down to the netherworld, to the uttermost parts of the pit"** (Isaiah 14:15).

But that brings you only to the top of the seventh heaven. *Lema'alah me'hen*, above them, are the *Chayos HaKodesh*, the heavenly beasts. And here the distances become almost incomprehensible. *Raglei hachayos keneged kulam* — the feet of the heavenly beasts are equal in length to all that preceded. *Arkuv hachayos keneged kulam* — the ankles are equal to everything before that. The calves, *shokei hachayos*, equal to everything preceding; the knees, *rekhuvei hachayos*, equal to everything preceding; the thighs, *yarchei hachayos*, equal again; the body, *gufei hachayos*; the necks, *tzavarei hachayos*; the heads, *rashei hachayos*; and finally the horns, *karnei hachayos* — each segment equal to everything that came before it, accumulating into distances that strain any conception. And even then, one has not reached God — above the heavenly beasts stands the *kisei hakavod*, whose legs alone equal everything that preceded, and the throne itself equals all that came before. Only then, *melech el ram chai vekayam veram venissa shochein adei marom* — does one arrive at God.

So: *ve'ata amarta e'eleh al bamtei av adameh le'elyon* — and you said you would rise up on a cloud and be like the Most High? *Ach el sheol tured el yarketei vor* — no, you will descend to the lowest pit.

The passage is a vivid statement not merely of physical distance but of the absolute transcendence of God over any human claim to power or greatness.

Tosafos notes a relevant detail concerning the *kedushah* of Yitzchak HaKalir, one of the earliest *paytanim*, which includes the line *ve'hachayos asher hinam meruba'os bakisei, kaf regel chamesh me'ot vechamesh esrei* — the foot of a heavenly beast is 500 and 15. This seems inconsistent with the Gemara's reckoning. Some emend the text and remove the letter *vav*, reading it as 500 times 15, which would fit: seven heavens plus eight airspaces (including the space from the top of the seventh heaven to the *chayos*) equals 15 intervals, each 500 years — hence 500 times 15. Tosafos notes, however, that this emendation is unnecessary, because the Kalir did not base his *piyut* on the Bavli but on the Yerushalmi, which does give the measure of the foot of the heavenly beast as 500 and 15 *amos*. As Tosafos observes, in a number of places the Kalir followed the Yerushalmi rather than the Bavli — a pattern consistent with the *minhag* of Eretz Yisrael, from which Ashkenazic liturgical tradition descends through Italy. Tosafos goes further and claims that the Kalir was himself a *tanna* — specifically Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon — which is historically untenable, but reflects the enormous reverence in which he was held. The Ashkenazic tradition of *piyut* is rooted in the practice of Eretz Yisrael, and it is therefore natural that its greatest poet should reflect the Yerushalmi tradition.

## Teaching Secrets

### Ma'aseh Merkavah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּמֶּרְכָּבָה בְּיָחִיד. תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֲבָל מוֹסְרִין לוֹ רָאשֵׁי פְרָקִים. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֵין מוֹסְרִין רָאשֵׁי פְרָקִים אֶלָּא לְאַב בֵּית דִּין, וּלְכׇל מִי שֶׁלִּבּוֹ דּוֹאֵג בְּקִרְבּוֹ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, וְהוּא שֶׁלִּבּוֹ דּוֹאֵג בְּקִרְבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna, according to the Gemara's explanation: **Nor** may one expound **the** Design of the Divine **Chariot to an individual. Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: But one may transmit to him,** an individual, **the outlines** of this topic, leaving him to comprehend the rest on his own. **Rabbi Zeira said: One may transmit the outlines** of the Design of the Divine Chariot **only to the president of the court,** who needs to know them due to his wisdom and meritorious deeds, **and to anyone whose heart inside him is concerned,** i.e., one who is concerned about his sins and desires to achieve full repentance. **There are** those **who say** that this does not refer to two separate individuals, but to the president of the court, **whose heart inside him is concerned.**

The Gemara now transitions to the discussion of who may be taught these esoteric matters. The *mishnah* has already stated that *ma'aseh merkavah* may not be taught even to a single individual unless he is a sage who understands on his own. A *baraita* taught by Rabbi Chiya adds: *aval mosrin lo rashei perakim* — nevertheless, one may transmit to such a person the chapter headings, the broad outlines, even if he has not yet fully demonstrated independent mastery.

Rabbi Zeira goes further in restriction: *ein mosrin rashei perakim ela le'av beis din* — even the chapter headings may only be transmitted to the head of a *beis din*, someone of exceptional stature. *Ulechol mi shelibo do'eg bekirbo* — and to one whose heart is anxious within him. This second criterion is puzzling at first glance. Why should anxiety of heart qualify one to receive esoteric knowledge?

There are at least two ways to understand it. The simpler reading, reflected in the Steinsaltz commentary, is that *libo do'eg bekirbo* describes someone who is not inclined to frivolity, who has the appropriate *yiras shamayim* and genuine concern — perhaps concern about his sins and desire for *teshuvah* — such that he will not misuse what he learns. A second reading, which the lecturer suggests as a possibility, is more complex: the person *libo do'eg bekirbo* is someone already deeply troubled theologically, already wrestling with questions, already in danger of being lost. For such a person, engaging with esoteric matters is not more dangerous — it may be necessary. Withholding answers from someone who is urgently questioning may be worse than the risk of exposing them to difficult material. This connects to a broader debate: the Ra'avad famously criticized the Rambam for raising the question of divine foreknowledge and free will in the *Mishneh Torah* without providing a satisfying resolution, arguing that it was better to say nothing and leave people's *emunah* undisturbed. The Rambam's position implies that for those already troubled, engagement is necessary. The phrase *mi shelibo do'eg bekirbo* may encode that same tension.

### Five Qualities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין מוֹסְרִין סִתְרֵי תוֹרָה אֶלָּא לְמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה דְבָרִים: ״שַׂר חֲמִשִּׁים, וּנְשׂוּא פָנִים, וְיוֹעֵץ, וַחֲכַם חֲרָשִׁים, וּנְבוֹן לָחַשׁ״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין מוֹסְרִין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה לְגוֹי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא עָשָׂה כֵן לְכׇל גּוֹי וּמִשְׁפָּטִים בַּל יְדָעוּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: The secrets of the Torah may be transmitted only to one who possesses** the following **five characteristics: "The captain of fifty, and the man of favor, and the counselor, and the cunning charmer, and the skillful enchanter"** (Isaiah 3:3). **And Rabbi Ami said** further: **The words of Torah may not be transmitted to a gentile, as it is stated: "He has not dealt so with any nation, and as for His ordinances, they have not known them"** (Psalms 147:20).

Rabbi Ami further specifies: *ein mosrin sitrei Torah ela lemi sheyeish bo chamishah devarim* — the hidden matters of Torah may only be transmitted to one who possesses five qualities. These are derived from a verse in Yeshayahu: *sar chamishim, unisso panim, ve'yoetz, vechacham charashim, unevon lachash* — a leader of fifty, one of distinguished bearing, a counselor, one wise in hidden matters, and one who understands that which is whispered.

Rashi clarifies that *sitrei Torah* here encompasses not only *ma'aseh merkavah* but also *Sefer Yetzirah* — attributed by tradition to Adam Harishon and said to contain the knowledge by which a *golem* can be created — and *ma'aseh bereishis* as well. The category of esoteric Torah is thus broader than *ma'aseh merkavah* alone.

### Non-Jews & Torah

Rabbi Ami adds a further ruling: *ein mosrin divrei Torah le'akum* — one may not transmit Torah to non-Jews at all, setting aside entirely the question of *sitrei Torah*. The verse cited is *lo asah chein lechol goy umishpatim bal yeda'um* — He did not do this for any other nation, and His laws He did not make known to them. The Torah was given specifically to Israel, and its transmission to non-Jews is prohibited. The Gemara elsewhere states even more sharply that a non-Jew who studies Torah is *chayav misah*.

### Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תָּא אַגְמְרָךְ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה הַמֶּרְכָּבָה, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא קַשַּׁאי. כִּי קַשׁ — נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַסִּי: תָּא וְאַגְמְרָךְ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי זְכַאי גְּמִירְתַּהּ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רַבָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Rabbi Elazar: Come and I will teach you the Design of the** Divine **Chariot.** Rabbi Elazar **said to him:** I have **not** yet **aged** sufficiently, as one must be very settled in one's mind for these studies. **When he grew old, Rabbi Yoḥanan had** already **passed away. Rabbi Asi said to him: Come and I will teach you the Design of the** Divine **Chariot. He said to him: Had I merited, I would have learned** it **from Rabbi Yoḥanan, your teacher.** It therefore appears that I am unworthy of studying it.

Rabbi Yochanan once turned to Rabbi Elazar and said: *ta, amarkha bema'aseh merkavah* — come, let me teach you *ma'aseh merkavah*. It is a remarkable invitation. Rabbi Elazar declined, saying he was not yet old enough. When he finally reached the appropriate age, Rabbi Yochanan had already passed away, and the opportunity was lost. Rav Assi then offered to teach him *ma'aseh merkavah* in Rabbi Yochanan's stead, and Rabbi Elazar replied: *i zakai gemirna me-Rabbi Yochanan rabach* — had I merited, I would have learned it from Rabbi Yochanan your teacher. Since I did not merit that, I will not learn it from you. One may wonder whether Rabbi Elazar genuinely felt unworthy of this knowledge, or whether he simply did not wish to learn *ma'aseh merkavah* at all.

### Rav Yosef Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף הֲוָה גְּמִיר מַעֲשֵׂה הַמֶּרְכָּבָה. סָבֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא הֲווֹ תָּנוּ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: (לִיגְמוֹר לַן) [לַיגְמְרַן] מָר מַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַגְמְרוּן לִי מַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית. בָּתַר דְּאַגְמְרוּהּ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לַיגְמְרַון מָר בְּמַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ: תְּנֵינָא בְּהוּ ״דְּבַשׁ וְחָלָב תַּחַת לְשׁוֹנֵךְ״, דְּבָרִים הַמְּתוּקִין מִדְּבַשׁ וְחָלָב — יְהוּ תַּחַת לְשׁוֹנֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Yosef would study the Design of the** Divine **Chariot** and was familiar with the subject, whereas **the Elders of Pumbedita would study the act of Creation. They said to** Rav Yosef: **Let the Master teach us the Design of the** Divine **Chariot. He said to them:** You **teach me the act of Creation. After they taught him** that subject, **they said to him: Let the Master teach us the Design of the** Divine **Chariot. He said to them: We learned with regard to them** the secrets of the Torah: **"Honey and milk are under your tongue"** (Song of Songs 4:11), meaning that **matters that are sweeter than honey and milk should remain under your tongue.** In other words, one should not speak of such matters, and anyone who is familiar with them may not reveal them to others.

Rav Yosef had studied *ma'aseh merkavah*, while the elders of Pumbedita had studied *ma'aseh bereishis*. It is worth noting the difference in verbs: the Gemara says Rav Yosef *gemir* — a term associated with *gemara*, meaning deep analytical study — while the elders of Pumbedita *tannei*, a term associated with *mishnah*, indicating that they held the traditions as a repository of received knowledge, possibly *beraisos*, as Rashi suggests. This distinction may indicate the different levels of engagement required by each discipline.

The elders of Pumbedita said to Rav Yosef: *ligmaran mar ma'aseh merkavah* — teach us *ma'aseh merkavah*. He responded: *agmruni ma'aseh bereishis* — first teach me your *ma'aseh bereishis*. After they did so, they returned and asked again: now teach us *ma'aseh merkavah*. Rav Yosef replied with the verse *devash vechalav tachat leshonecha* — honey and milk under your tongue: *devarim hametukim midvash vechalav yehei tachat leshonecha* — things sweeter than honey and milk must be kept under your tongue. He had taken their *ma'aseh bereishis* but would not share his *ma'aseh merkavah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״כְּבָשִׁים לִלְבוּשֶׁךָ״, דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵן כִּבְשׁוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם — יִהְיוּ תַּחַת לְבוּשֶׁךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said:** It is derived from **here,** from the following verse: **"The lambs** [***kevasim***] **will be for your clothing"** (Proverbs 27:26), which he expounds as though it were written with the letter *shin*, *kevashim*, meaning concealed matters: **Things that constitute the concealed matters of the world should be under your clothing;** you should not reveal them.

Rabbi Abbahu cited an alternative verse: *devarim she'hein kivshono shel olam, yehei tachat levushecha* — the hidden things of the world, keep them close beneath your garment.

### Yechezkel Boundaries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: תְּנֵינַן בְּהוּ עַד ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי בֶּן אָדָם״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הֵן הֵן מַעֲשֵׂה הַמֶּרְכָּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** When the Elders of Pumbedita saw that Rav Yosef was not going to teach them, **they said to him: We have learned them,** the verses concerning the Design of the Divine Chariot written in the book of Ezekiel, **up to** the verse **"And He said to me, son of man"** (Ezekiel 2:1). **He said to them:** If so, **these** verses **are** the very essence of the **Design of the** Divine **Chariot,** as they provide the main details of the topic.

The elders then revealed to Rav Yosef that they were not entirely without *ma'aseh merkavah* — they had already learned it up to the verse *vayomer elai ben Adam* in the second chapter of Yechezkel. Rav Yosef responded: *hein hein ma'aseh merkavah* — that is itself *ma'aseh merkavah*; after Yechezkel 2 it is no longer *ma'aseh merkavah*. So in effect, they already possessed what they needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: עַד הֵיכָן מַעֲשֵׂה הַמֶּרְכָּבָה? רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: עַד ״וָאֵרֶא״ בָּתְרָא, רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: עַד ״הַחַשְׁמַל״. עַד ״וָאֵרֶא״ מַגְמְרִינַן, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מָסְרִינַן רָאשֵׁי פְרָקִים. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: עַד ״וָאֵרֶא״ מָסְרִינַן רָאשֵׁי פְרָקִים, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, אִם הוּא חָכָם מֵבִין מִדַּעְתּוֹ — אִין, אִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: **Until where is the Design of the** Divine **Chariot** related? **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Until the latter "And I saw"** (Ezekiel 1:27), not including the last verse. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Until** the word **"the electrum"** (Ezekiel 1:27). Neither of these opinions accord with Rav Yosef's opinion that the Design of the Divine Chariot continues until the end of the chapter. The Gemara answers: **Until "And I saw," we teach** those worthy of it; **from this** point **forward,** we **transmit** only the **outlines. There are** those **who say: Until "And I saw," we transmit the outlines; from this** point **forward, if he is wise** and **can understand of his own accord, yes,** we teach him. **If not,** we do **not** teach him even the outlines.

The Gemara then presses: precisely until where does *ma'aseh merkavah* extend? Rabbi states: *ad va'era batra* — until the second *va'era*, meaning until the very opening of the vision: *va'era, vehinei ruach se'arah ba'ah min hatzafon, anan gadol ve'esh mitlakachas, venogah lo saviv, umitochah ke'ein hachashmal mitoch ha'esh* — and I saw, and behold a whirlwind coming from the north, a great cloud with flashing fire, and a glow surrounding it, and from its midst like the *chashmal* from within the fire. Rashi explains that *ad va'era* means: starting from *va'era* onwards is where the actual *ma'aseh merkavah* begins.

But there is further division within the first chapter. Tosafos identifies the critical boundary as the *chashmal* — it is specifically the *chashmal* that marks the shift to the most restricted material. One view holds that until the *va'era* one may teach openly; from there one teaches only *rashei perakim*, chapter headings. Another view (*ika d'amri*) is more restrictive: until *va'era* one may teach chapter headings; from the *va'era* on, and specifically once the *chashmal* enters, one may teach only *im hu chacham mevin mida'ato* — if the student is wise enough to understand independently. The question is whether the division of difficulty is one-tiered or two-tiered within the first chapter.

### Chananya Preserves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּרַם זָכוּר אוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ לַטּוֹב, וַחֲנַנְיָה בֶּן חִזְקִיָּה שְׁמוֹ. אִלְמָלֵא הוּא — נִגְנַז סֵפֶר יְחֶזְקֵאל, שֶׁהָיוּ דְּבָרָיו סוֹתְרִין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. מֶה עָשָׂה? הֶעֱלוּ לוֹ שְׁלוֹשׁ מֵאוֹת גַּרְבֵי שֶׁמֶן, וְיָשַׁב בַּעֲלִיָּיה וּדְרָשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: Indeed, that man is remembered for good, and Ḥananya ben Ḥizkiya was his name,** because **were it not for him, the book of Ezekiel would have been suppressed.** Why did they wish to suppress it? Because they found **that its words contradicted the words of Torah,** as its later chapters contain many *halakhot* that appear not to accord with those of the Torah. **What did he do? They brought up to him three hundred barrels of oil,** for light and sustenance, **and he sat in an upper chamber and expounded it,** to reconcile its teachings with those of the Torah.

The mention of Yechezkel's text prompts an aside. Rav Yehuda states: *beram zachor oto ha'ish letov, veChannanaya ben Chizkiya shemo* — that man should be remembered for good, and his name was Chananya ben Chizkiya. *Ilmalei hu, nignaz sefer Yechezkel* — were it not for him, the book of Yechezkel would have been hidden away entirely. Two things made Yechezkel dangerous: the esoteric vision of the divine chariot, and the fact that the later chapters — dealing with the laws of the *mikdash* and the *kohanim* — appear to contradict verses in the Torah. *She'hayu devarav sotrim divrei Torah* — its words seemed to contradict the Torah. Chananya ben Chizkiya brought up 300 flasks of oil, ensconced himself in his attic, and worked through the contradictions until he had reconciled them all. Rather than suppressing the book, he preserved it within the canon by doing the hard intellectual work of harmonization.

### Child & Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתִינוֹק אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה קוֹרֵא בְּבֵית רַבּוֹ בְּסֵפֶר יְחֶזְקֵאל וְהָיָה מֵבִין בְּחַשְׁמַל, וְיָצְאָה אֵשׁ מֵחַשְׁמַל וּשְׂרָפַתּוּ, וּבִיקְשׁוּ לִגְנוֹז סֵפֶר יְחֶזְקֵאל. אָמַר לָהֶם חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן חִזְקִיָּה: אִם זֶה חָכָם — הַכֹּל חֲכָמִים הֵן?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: An incident** occurred **involving a youth who was reading the book of Ezekiel in the house of his teacher, and** he **was** able to **comprehend the electrum, and fire came out of the electrum and burned him. And they sought to suppress the book of Ezekiel** due to the danger it posed. **Ḥananya ben Ḥizkiya said to them: If this** youth happened to be **wise,** are **all** people **wise** enough to understand this book?

The Gemara then reports: *ma'aseh betinok echad she'haya korei beveit rabbo besefer Yechezkel* — a child was reading the book of Yechezkel in his teacher's house. *Vehaya mevin bachashmal* — and this child was brilliant enough to understand the secrets of the *chashmal*. *Veyatza esh mechashmal vesarafathu* — and fire came out of the *chashmal* and burned him. This incident led to renewed calls to suppress the book of Yechezkel entirely.

Chananya ben Chizkiya replied: *im zeh chacham, hakol chachamim* — if this child was wise enough to grasp it at such a young age, then everyone is wise, and the book would need to be hidden from everyone. But that cannot be the right conclusion. The child's case was exceptional precisely because such depth of understanding at so young an age is extraordinarily rare. Normally, either a person lacks the understanding to grasp the *chashmal*, or — if they possess that understanding — they are of the age and maturity to handle it safely. The Gemara confirms this: *shani yanuka delav mati zimnei* — the child's case is exceptional because he had not yet reached his proper time. The knowledge itself, in the hands of one who is both wise and of appropriate age, is not inherently destructive.

## Chashmal

### Chashmal Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חַשְׁמַל? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: חַיּוֹת אֵשׁ מְמַלְּלוֹת. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: עִתִּים חָשׁוֹת, עִתִּים מְמַלְּלוֹת. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַדִּיבּוּר יוֹצֵא מִפִּי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא — חָשׁוֹת, וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵין הַדִּיבּוּר יוֹצֵא מִפִּי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא — מְמַלְּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It refers to **speaking animals of fire.** Electrum [*ḥashmal*] is an acrostic of this phrase [***ḥayyot esh memallelot***]. **It was taught in a *baraita*: At times they are silent; at times they speak. When the divine speech emerges from the mouth of the Holy One, Blessed be He, they are silent; and when the divine speech does not emerge from the mouth of the Holy One, Blessed be He, they speak.**

We now turn to the substance of what the Gemara is willing to say about the *chashmal* itself, even as it acknowledges that this is the most restricted portion of *ma'aseh merkavah*. Rav Yehuda states at the top of 13b: *chayos esh memalelot* — the *chashmal* is an acronym: *chet-shin-mem-lamed*, standing for fiery beasts (*chayos esh*) that speak to one another (*memalelot*). Rashi adds that the fire emerges from their speech.

### Beasts' Speech

A *baraita* elaborates: *itim chashot, itim memalelot* — sometimes these fiery beasts are silent, and sometimes they speak. *Besha'ah shehadibbur yotzei mipi Hakadosh Baruch Hu, chashot* — when speech issues from God, the beasts fall silent. *Uvesha'ah she'ein hadibbur yotzei mipi Hakadosh Baruch Hu, memalelot* — when God is not speaking, the beasts themselves speak. There is something deeply evocative about this — a live, crackling, electric quality to the heavenly *chayos*, oscillating between silence before God and their own fiery speech. It is no coincidence that *chashmal* became the modern Hebrew word for electricity.

### Beasts Running

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַחַיּוֹת רָצוֹא וָשׁוֹב כְּמַרְאֵה הַבָּזָק״. מַאי ״רָצוֹא וָשׁוֹב״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כְּאוּר הַיּוֹצֵא מִפִּי הַכִּבְשָׁן. מַאי ״כְּמַרְאֵה הַבָּזָק״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: כְּאוּר הַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּין הַחֲרָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"And the divine creatures ran and returned like the appearance of a flash of lightning [*bazak*]"** (Ezekiel 1:14). **What is** the meaning of **"ran and returned"? Rav Yehuda said: Like fire that is emitted from a furnace,** whose flame is continuously bursting out and withdrawing. **What is** the meaning of **"like the appearance of a flash of lightning"? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: Like the fire that is emitted** from **between pieces of** **earthenware** used for refining gold, as an additional meaning ascribed to the word *bazak* is shards of earthenware.

The verse in Yechezkel describes *vehachayos ratzo vashov kemarei habazak* — the beasts running back and forth like the appearance of lightning. Rav Yehuda explains *ratzo vashov*: *ke'or hayotzei mipi hakivshan* — like the fire that comes out of the mouth of a furnace, flickering and moving rapidly in and out. Rav Yossi bar Chanina interprets *kemarei habazak* — like the appearance of lightning: *ke'or hayotzei mibein hacharsim* — like the light that emerges from between the pieces of pottery. Rashi describes the context: in the smelting of gold, a pottery floor with holes is placed above the fire, and the fire flashes in and out through those openings. This image captures the rapid, intermittent, and intensely alive quality of the heavenly beasts' movement.

## Divine Chariot Vision

### Whirlwind North

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וָאֵרֶא וְהִנֵּה רוּחַ סְעָרָה בָּאָה מִן הַצָּפוֹן עָנָן גָּדוֹל וְאֵשׁ מִתְלַקַּחַת וְנוֹגַהּ לוֹ סָבִיב וּמִתּוֹכָהּ כְּעֵין הַחַשְׁמַל מִתּוֹךְ הָאֵשׁ״. לְהֵיכָן אֲזַל? אֲמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁהָלַךְ לִכְבּוֹשׁ אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ תַּחַת נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע. וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם: בְּיַד אוּמָּה שְׁפָלָה מָסַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת בָּנָיו. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מִי גָּרַם לִי שֶׁאֶהְיֶה שַׁמָּשׁ לְעוֹבְדֵי פְסִילִים — עֲוֹנוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל הֵן גָּרְמוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And I looked and, behold, a stormy wind came out of the north, a great cloud, with a fire flashing up, so that a brightness was round about it; and out of its midst was like the color of electrum, out of the midst of the fire"** (Ezekiel 1:4). The Gemara poses a question: **Where did** that wind **go? Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: It went to conquer the entire world under the wicked Nebuchadnezzar. And why was all of this necessary?** Why was it necessary that the entire world be subjected to his dominion? **So that the nations of the world would not say: The Holy One, Blessed be He, delivered His children into the hands of a lowly nation.** Since it was already decreed that the kingdom of Israel would fall into the hands of Nebuchadnezzar, God made him into a great conqueror, so that Israel would not be ashamed of being defeated by him. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said** with regard to this: **Who caused Me to be an attendant to worshippers of molten images,** forcing Me to wage their wars? **It was the sins of Israel that led Me** to do so.

The vision of Yechezkel opens: *va'era, vehinei ruach se'arah ba'ah min hatzafon, anan gadol ve'esh mitlakachas* — and I saw a whirlwind coming from the north, a great cloud with flashing fire. Rashi notes that *tzafon*, the north, signifies Bavel — *mitzafon tipatach hara'ah*, evil opens from the north. The question is therefore natural: what was God's chariot doing going toward Bavel?

Rav Yehuda citing Rav explains: *she'halach lichbosh et kol ha'olam kulo tachat Nevuchadnetzar harasha* — the divine chariot went to empower Nevuchadnetzar to conquer the entire world. The *why* follows: *shelo yomru umot ha'olam, beyad umah shefelah masar Hakadosh Baruch Hu et banav* — lest the nations say that God handed over His people to a lowly and insignificant nation. The conquest of Israel was of such cosmic significance that it could only be carried out by the world's greatest conqueror, and for that to happen, divine power went to Bavel to empower Nevuchadnetzar.

### God's Reluctance

Yet this raised its own theological discomfort. *Amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu: mi garam li she'e'eseh shamash le'ovdei pesilim?* — God said: what caused Me to become an attendant to idol worshippers? What forced My divine chariot to go to Bavel and serve, in effect, the purposes of idol worship? *Avonoteihem shel Yisrael hein garmu li* — it was the sins of Israel that brought Me to this.

### Sandalphon Angel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וָאֵרֶא הַחַיּוֹת וְהִנֵּה אוֹפַן אֶחָד בָּאָרֶץ אֵצֶל הַחַיּוֹת״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַלְאָךְ אֶחָד שֶׁהוּא עוֹמֵד בָּאָרֶץ, וְרֹאשׁוֹ מַגִּיעַ אֵצֶל הַחַיּוֹת. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: סַנְדַּלְפוֹן שְׁמוֹ, הַגָּבוֹהַּ מֵחֲבֵרוֹ מַהֲלַךְ חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה. וְעוֹמֵד אֲחוֹרֵי הַמֶּרְכָּבָה וְקוֹשֵׁר כְּתָרִים לְקוֹנוֹ. אִינִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״בָּרוּךְ כְּבוֹד ה׳ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ״, מִכְּלָל דִּמְקוֹמוֹ לֵיכָּא דְּיָדַע לֵיהּ! דְּאָמַר שֵׁם אַתָּגָא וְאָזֵל וְיָתֵיב בְּרֵישֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Another verse in the same chapter states: **"Now as I beheld the divine creatures, behold, one wheel [*ofan*] was upon the earth near the divine creatures"** (Ezekiel 1:15). **Rabbi Elazar said:** This wheel is **a certain angel who stands on the earth and its head reaches the divine creatures. It was taught in a *baraita*:** This angel **is named Sandalfon, who is taller than his colleague by a distance of five hundred years, and he stands behind the** Divine **Chariot and weaves crowns for his Maker.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so?** Can crowns be woven for God? **But isn't it written: "Blessed be the Lord's glory from His place"** (Ezekiel 3:12), which proves **by inference** that **no one knows His place?** Therefore, how can crowns be woven for Him? Rather, it can be done **by saying a name for the crown, and** then the crown **goes and sits on** God's **head** of its own accord.

The vision continues: *va'era el hachayos, vehinei ofan echad ba'aretz etzel hachayos* — and I saw by the heavenly beasts, and behold, one *ofan* was on the earth beside the beasts. Rav Elazar interprets: this *ofan* is an angel who stands on the earth, yet *rosho magia etzel hachayos* — his head reaches all the way up to the heavenly *chayos*. Given the distances we have already traversed — hundreds of thousands of years of travel — this angel spans an incomprehensible height. A *baraita* names him: *Sandalphon shemo* — his name is Sandalphon. *Hagavo'ah mechaveiro mahalach chamesh me'ot shanah* — he is 500 years taller than all the other angels. *Ve'omed achorei hamerkavah* — he stands behind God's chariot.

### Crown-Bearing

*Vekosher ketarim lekono* — and Sandalphon weaves crowns for his Master. The Gemara pauses at this: *eini?* — is this really so? Is someone present directly with God, placing a crown on God's head? The answer draws on the verse *baruch kevod Hashem mimkomo* — blessed is the honor of God from His place. God's place is distinct from any other place; wherever God is, He is praised, but no one occupies God's place. How then does Sandalphon's crown reach God? *De'amar shem atga, ve'azal veyativ beriesha* — he inscribes God's name on the crown, and the crown rises on its own to the highest heaven and comes to rest on God's head. Sandalphon does not place the crown directly; the crown ascends by itself.

## Yechezkel vs Yeshayahu

### Graphic vs Simple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל שֶׁרָאָה יְחֶזְקֵאל, רָאָה יְשַׁעְיָה. לְמָה יְחֶזְקֵאל דּוֹמֶה — לְבֶן כְּפָר שֶׁרָאָה אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ, וּלְמָה יְשַׁעְיָה דּוֹמֶה — לְבֶן כְּרַךְ שֶׁרָאָה אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אָשִׁירָה לַה׳ כִּי גָאֹה גָּאָה״ — שִׁירָה לְמִי שֶׁמִּתְגָּאֶה עַל הַגֵּאִים. דְּאָמַר מָר: מֶלֶךְ שֶׁבַּחַיּוֹת — אֲרִי, מֶלֶךְ שֶׁבַּבְּהֵמוֹת — שׁוֹר, מֶלֶךְ שֶׁבָּעוֹפוֹת — נֶשֶׁר, וְאָדָם מִתְגָּאֶה עֲלֵיהֶן, וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִתְגָּאֶה עַל כּוּלָּן וְעַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said: All that Ezekiel saw,** the prophet **Isaiah saw** as well, but the latter did not find it necessary to describe his vision in such detail. **To what may Ezekiel be compared? To a villager who saw the king** and is excited by all the extravagances of the king's palace and everything it contains, as he is unaccustomed to them. **And to what may Isaiah be compared? To a city dweller who saw the king.** Such an individual focuses on the encounter with the king, and is oblivious to all the distractions. **Reish Lakish said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "I will sing to the Lord, for He is highly exalted"** (Exodus 15:1)? It is fitting to **sing to He Who is exalted above the exalted. As the Master said: The king of the beasts** is **the lion, the king of the domestic animals** is **the ox, the king of the birds** is **the eagle, and man is exalted** and lords **over them, but the Holy One, Blessed be He, is exalted above all of them and above the entire world,** as the creatures that appear in the Divine Chariot are the ox, the lion, the eagle, and man.

Rava states: *kol she'ra'ah Yechezkel ra'ah Yeshayahu* — everything that Yechezkel saw, Yeshayahu also saw. Both prophets received visions of the divine chariot. Yet Yeshayahu's account is brief and lacks the elaborate physical description of Yechezkel's vision. Why?

*Lemah Yechezkel domeh? Leven kfar she'ra'ah et hamelech* — to what can Yechezkel be compared? To a villager who caught sight of the king. The villager is overwhelmed: he describes the crown, the robe, the palace, the courtyard, every detail in breathless excitement, because the experience was extraordinary and unprecedented. *Ulemah Yeshayahu domeh? Leven krach she'ra'ah et hamelech* — and to what can Yeshayahu be compared? To a city-dweller who saw the king. Someone accustomed to royal proximity has little to say about it; it is unremarkable. Hence Yeshayahu's restrained account.

This is the *pshat* of the *parable*, which suggests that Yeshayahu was simply closer to God, more habituated to prophetic visions, and therefore did not need to elaborate. Rashi, however, takes the parable very literally: Yechezkel was the son of commoners, while Yeshayahu was of aristocratic lineage, related to royalty. Having been exposed to royal grandeur in his earthly life, Yeshayahu had less need to describe in detail what he encountered in his prophetic vision.

Tosafos offers a different angle: Yechezkel saw his vision *bechutz la'aretz*, in exile in Bavel, while Yeshayahu saw his in *Eretz Yisrael*. For Yechezkel, the very occurrence of such a vision outside the Land was astonishing — and also, no one in Bavel would believe him. Just as the villager who has seen the king must describe every detail to convince skeptical listeners that he was truly in the royal presence, so Yechezkel had to give exhaustive description precisely because his audience would be unlikely to believe that such a prophetic vision had occurred in *chutz la'aretz*.

### Four Faces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וּדְמוּת פְּנֵיהֶם פְּנֵי אָדָם וּפְנֵי אַרְיֵה אֶל הַיָּמִין לְאַרְבַּעְתָּם וּפְנֵי שׁוֹר מֵהַשְּׂמֹאל לְאַרְבַּעְתָּן וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאַרְבָּעָה פָנִים לְאֶחָד פְּנֵי הָאֶחָד פְּנֵי הַכְּרוּב וּפְנֵי הַשֵּׁנִי פְּנֵי אָדָם וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי פְּנֵי אַרְיֵה וְהָרְבִיעִי פְּנֵי נָשֶׁר״, וְאִילּוּ שׁוֹר לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: יְחֶזְקֵאל בִּיקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים, וַהֲפָכוֹ לִכְרוּב. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! קָטֵיגוֹר יֵעָשֶׂה סָנֵיגוֹר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to the animals of the Divine Chariot: **One verse states: "As for the likeness of their faces, they had the face of a man; and the four had the face of a lion on the right side; and the four had the face of an ox on the left side"** (Ezekiel 1:10). **And it is** also **written: "And each one had four faces: The first face was the face of the cherub, and the second face was the face of a man, and the third the face of a lion, and the fourth the face of an eagle"** (Ezekiel 10:14), **but it does not include** the face of **an ox** in this second list. **Reish Lakish said: Ezekiel requested mercy** with regard to **it,** i.e., the face of the ox, **and had it turned into a cherub. He said before Him** as follows: **Master of the Universe.** Shall **an accuser [*kateigor*] become a defender [*saneigor*]?** As the face of an ox recalls Israel's sin of the Golden Calf, it would be preferable for there to be a different face on the Divine Chariot.

The divine chariot's *chayos* had four faces, and the verse in Yechezkel catalogues them: *pnei adam upnei aryeh el hayamin le'arba'tam upnei shor mehasmol le'arba'tam* — the face of a human and the face of a lion on the right, the face of an ox on the left, and the face of an eagle. The significance of these four creatures is drawn out by Reish Lakish: *shira lemi shemitga'eh al hage'im* — it is a song to the One who is exalted above all the proud. *Melech shebachayos* — who is king among wild beasts? The lion. *Melech shebabehemos* — who is king among domesticated animals? The ox. *Melech sheba'ofos* — who is king among birds? The eagle (or vulture). *Ve'adam mitga'eh aleihem* — and the human being towers over all of them. *VeHakadosh Baruch Hu mitga'eh al kulan ve'al kol ha'olam kulo* — and God is exalted above all of them and above the entire world. Therefore God's chariot bears the faces of the four kings of creation — lion, ox, eagle, and human — because God is the King of kings over them all.

### Ox to Cherub

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כְּרוּב״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כְּרָבְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן בְּבָבֶל קוֹרִין לְיָנוֹקָא רָבְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״פְּנֵי הָאֶחָד פְּנֵי הַכְּרוּב וּפְנֵי הַשֵּׁנִי פְּנֵי אָדָם וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי פְּנֵי אַרְיֵה וְהָרְבִיעִי פְּנֵי נָשֶׁר״, הַיְינוּ פְּנֵי כְרוּב הַיְינוּ פְּנֵי אָדָם? אַפֵּי רַבְרְבֵי וְאַפֵּי זוּטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **"cherub"? Rabbi Abbahu said: Like a baby** [***keravya***], **for in Babylonia they call a baby *ravya*. Rav Pappa said to Abaye: However, if that is so, what is** the meaning of **that which is written: "The first face was the face of the cherub, and the second face was the face of a man, and the third the face of a lion, and the fourth the face of the eagle"? The face of a cherub is** the same as **the face of a man;** what is the difference between them? He replied: The difference is that the face of a man is referring to **a large face,** whereas the face of a cherub means the **small face** of a baby.

Yet a second verse in Yechezkel replaces the face of the ox with a *keruv*, a cherub. The ox is simply missing: *de'ishtavar lei chushbna* — it is not counted. How did this come to be?

*Yechezkel bikesh alav rachamim vehafacho likeruv* — Yechezkel prayed over the face of the ox and had it transformed into a cherub. He said before God: *Ribbono Shel Olam, kateigor na'aseh saneigor?* — should the accuser become the defender? The ox evokes the *cheit ha'eigel*, the sin of the golden calf. That sin should not be represented on God's own divine throne, serving as a constant reminder before God of Israel's greatest failure. Yechezkel prayed that the ox be replaced, and it was transformed into a *keruv*. This is a remarkable notion: the very appearance of the divine throne, the most transcendent object in existence, was altered by the prayer of a human being. And yet this parallels what we saw with David, whose prayer brought destructive forces down from the heavens to the earth. The Gemara presents a theology in which divine transcendence and human efficacy are not opposites — the human being's prayer can transform the very face of the heavenly throne.

### Cherub Meaning

*Mai keruv?* — what does *keruv* mean? Rabbi Abbahu states: *k'ravya* — like a child. *Sheken beVavel korin l'yanuka ravya* — for in Babylonia, they called a child *ravya*. A *keruv* has a child-like face, which is how we consistently imagine the cherubim: young, innocent, child-like countenances. This is the origin of the well-known image.

Rav Pappa raises a question to Abaye: another verse in Yechezkel lists the faces as *pnei hakeruv, pnei adam, pnei aryeh, pnei nesher* — cherub, human, lion, eagle. If a *keruv* is a child-like human face, then the *keruv* and the *adam* are essentially the same thing, and you still have only three distinct creatures. The answer: *apei ravrevei ve'apei zutrei* — there is an adult face and a young face. The face of the *adam* and the face of the *keruv* are both human, but one is the face of a grown person and the other is the face of a child — the two human faces that replaced the single face of the ox after Yechezkel's prayer.
