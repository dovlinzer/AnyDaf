# Temurah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Female Asham & Offspring

### Question on Female Asham

The *gemara* picks up five lines from the wide lines on 21b with a question posed by one *amora* to another: *ba'ei minei Rabbi Avin bar Chiya me'Rabbi Avin bar Kahana* — Rabbi Avin bar Chiya asked Rabbi Avin bar Kahana: if one consecrated a female animal for an *asham*, what is the law regarding her offspring? Can the offspring be brought as an *olah*?

The background to this question is as follows. When a female animal is consecrated for an *asham*, it cannot be brought as an *asham*, since an *asham* requires a male. The normal consequence for an *asham* that becomes derailed — whether through *temurah*, through the owner having achieved atonement through another animal, or otherwise — is that one waits for it to acquire a *mum*, redeems it, and uses the proceeds to purchase an *olah*. So the question becomes: once the offspring of this female *asham* happens to be male, why not simply bring the animal itself as an *olah* rather than waiting for a *mum* and using the proceeds?

### Earlier Discussion Refer…

The *gemara* observes that this very question appears to have been addressed in an earlier discussion: *tifshotei mid'Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina* — the matter can seemingly be resolved from the earlier teaching in which Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina weighed in on a structurally similar case. The earlier *mishna* discussed the *temurat olah* and its offspring, where Rabbi Eliezer maintained that once you arrive at a male you may bring it as an *olah*. One might assume the same reasoning applies here: a female consecrated for an *asham* produces a male offspring, and since derailed *asham* funds go toward an *olah*, why not bring the thing itself?

But the earlier discussion distinguished between the cases. There, when one consecrated a female for an *olah* — or when a male *olah* produced a *temurah* and then offspring — whenever one ultimately arrives at a male, the *kedusha* one is bringing it under (*olah*) matches the *kedusha* of the original. Here, however, the original *kedusha* was that of an *asham*, and what one now seeks to bring is an *olah*. Since the proposed *kedusha* of the derivative does not match the *kedusha* of its origin, Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina had implied that even Rabbi Eliezer would agree one cannot bring it.

The *gemara* then explains why the question is being asked afresh: *lo shmi'a lei* — Rabbi Avin bar Chiya was simply not party to that earlier discussion, and wished to reason through the matter himself.

### Avin's Response

Rabbi Avin bar Kahana's response cuts against the earlier reasoning: *benah yikrav olah* — the male offspring may indeed be brought as an *olah*. His reasoning is that Rabbi Eliezer's position should not be read so narrowly as to require that the derivative's *kedusha* match the original. Rather, *mishum d'chazi l'akrava* — the operative principle for Rabbi Eliezer is simply that once the animal is fit to be brought, you bring it. There is no requirement that it carry the identical *kedusha* as the original.

### Challenge from Mishnah

The *gemara* challenges this reading from our own *mishna*. Our *mishna* addressed the case of the offspring of a *temurat asham*, a structurally similar case — the original *kedusha* was *asham*, and now one is dealing with a derivative. In that *mishna*, Rabbi Eliezer says *vlad u'vlad vlad ad sof kol ha'olam yavi bidmeihem olah* — the offspring and their offspring until the end of time: use the proceeds for an *olah*. The *mishna* says *bidmeihen* — their monetary value is used; *atzman lo* — the animals themselves are not brought, even once one arrives at a male.

This appears to directly contradict the position that Rabbi Eliezer would allow bringing the animal itself. One might anticipate a simple resolution: our *mishna* is discussing the case of a *temurat asham*, where the *halacha* of waiting for a *mum* and using the proceeds is already established at the level of the *temurah* itself, and that *halacha* passes down to the offspring. Whereas the original question concerned a female consecrated directly for an *asham*, which never entered that category, and whose offspring might therefore be brought directly. The *gemara*, however, does not pursue this path.

### Resolving Contradiction

Instead, the *gemara* offers a different resolution: *k'gon she'yalda nekeiva* — perhaps our *mishna* is speaking of the scenario where only female offspring were produced. The *gemara* immediately presses: but the *mishna* says *vlad u'vlad vlad ad sof kol ha'olam* — across endless generations, did not even one male appear? The answer given is: *meshanyinan shinuyei d'chika b'Vavel* — this is one of those forced Babylonian-style answers. Yes, the scenario is that females were born for the entire lineage.

It is striking that the *gemara* itself frames this as a strained answer, characteristic of Babylonian *amoraim*, who were sometimes depicted by their colleagues in *Eretz Yisrael* as offering *kvetch* resolutions. Rabbi Avin bar Kahana appears to be an *amora* of *Eretz Yisrael*, and the framing here preserves that ambient tension in the tradition. The *gemara* notes that some versions contain an additional, marginally better answer — found in the *Shita Mekubetzet* — which reads the *mishna* as addressing the case of a female, with the male case simply left unaddressed rather than explicitly excluded. The *Gra* suggests yet another possibility, though the distinction between these resolutions is not dramatic. In either reading, the takeaway is that one cannot straightforwardly resolve the question of the female *asham*'s male offspring from this *mishna*, and Rabbi Avin bar Kahana's position — that the male offspring may be brought directly as an *olah* — retains its standing.

## Bechor & Maaser Rules

### Bechor/Maaser Intro

With those intricate discussions behind us, the *perek* concludes with a far more accessible sugya as the *gemara* transitions from *asham* and *olah* to *bechor* and *maaser*. A brief word of background is in order. The *halacha* of *bechor u'ma'aser* is different from all other *korbanot* in a fundamental way. The *pasuk* states *lo yimacher velo yiga'el* — they cannot be sold or redeemed. If they acquire a *mum*, you do not redeem them and transfer the value toward another *korban*. Instead, they retain their *kedusha*, are slaughtered outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, and are eaten — the *bechor* by the *kohen*, and *maaser* by any *Yisrael*.

The same principle extends to *temurah*. If one makes a *temurah* from a *bechor* or *maaser*, that *temurah* is not brought as a *korban*. The *gemara* derives this from *pesukim*, but the underlying logic is compelling on its own terms: *kedushah* of *bechor* and *kedushas ma'aser* cannot be transferred to an animal that is not inherently a firstborn or a tenth. Such a *temurah* can never achieve the full status of *bechor* or *maaser* and therefore cannot be brought on the altar. The resolution is the same as with the original animal: wait for a *mum*, then eat it outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Mishnah Text

The *mishna* states: *temurat habechor ve'hama'aser* — the *temurah* of a *bechor* and *maaser*, along with *vladan u'vlad vladan ad sof ha'olam* — their offspring and the offspring of their offspring until the end of time — *harei elu kebechor u'kema'aser*: they carry the same *kedusha* as *bechor* and *maaser*. This means not that they are brought as *korbanot*, but that they share the same *din*: *veyeachlu bemumam labe'alim* — one waits for a *mum* to appear and they are then eaten by their owners.

One point worth noting: *bechor* is by definition male, so there cannot be direct offspring of a *bechor* itself. But *maaser* can be female, allowing for direct offspring of the *maaser* animal. In either case — whether offspring of the *temurah* or direct offspring of a *maaser* — the same *halacha* applies: they are not brought as *korbanot*.

### Marketplace Restriction

The *mishna* then asks: *mah bein bechor u'maiser levein kol hakodashim* — what distinguishes *bechor* and *maaser* from all other *korbanot*? The answer: *shekol hakodashim nimkarin be'itliz venishchatin be'itliz venishkalin belitra* — all other *kodashim* that acquire a *mum* may be sold in the meat market, slaughtered in the meat market, and weighed out by the pound. *Chutz min habechor vehama'aser* — except for *bechor* and *maaser*.

The reasoning behind permitting the sale of other *kodashim* in the marketplace is essentially one of maximizing the transfer value. When an animal that was consecrated as a *korban* acquires a *mum*, one redeems its monetary value and uses those funds to purchase a replacement *korban*. Retail sale yields a higher price than wholesale, so *chazal* permitted even the indignity of selling it like ordinary meat — slaughtered and weighed in the market — because the proceeds benefit *hekdesh*. The greater the sale price, the greater the value transferred toward the next *korban*.

By contrast, *bechor* and *maaser* are never redeemed and their value is never transferred to another *korban*. The owner simply eats the animal himself. Since *hekdesh* gains nothing from a higher price, there is no basis to permit treating the animal with such disregard; we insist instead that it be sold privately, wholesale, in deference to the *kedusha* it still retains. One might add that unlike other *kodashim* whose *kedusha* has been transferred away at the time of redemption, *bechor* and *maaser* retain their *kedusha* throughout — an additional reason to handle them with greater care.

### No Redemption

The *mishna* continues: *veyesh lahem pidyon utmuratan pidyon* — all other *kodashim* that acquire a *mum* can be redeemed, and so too their *temurah* if it acquires a *mum*. *Chutz min habechor vehama'aser* — but *bechor* and *maaser* cannot be redeemed; a *mum* does not trigger redemption but simply permits eating outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Outside Eretz Yisrael

The *mishna* also states that all other *korbanot* may be brought from *chutz la'aretz*, whereas *bechor* and *maaser* may not be brought from outside *Eretz Yisrael* — though Rashi explains that *bedi'avad*, if they were brought unblemished, they are offered as *korbanot*. If they arrived with a *mum*, one eats them outside the *Beis HaMikdash* as one would in any case.

## Rabbi Shimon Explained

### Parnasa Argument

Rabbi Shimon explains why *bechor* and *maaser* should not be brought from *chutz la'aretz*: *shehabechor vehama'aser yesh lahem parnasa mimkomam* — *bechor* and *maaser* can be "sustained" where they are. Since they may be eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash* once they acquire a *mum*, they can be dealt with locally. If one is in *chutz la'aretz* and possesses a *bechor* or *maaser* that has a *mum*, or waits until it acquires one, there is no need to transport the animal to *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Other Korbanot Transfer

By contrast, all other *korbanot* — even if they acquire a *mum* — must have their value transferred and a replacement *korban* brought. There is no way to "resolve" the matter outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. Since those animals must come to ירושלים in any case, the *halacha* accepts them from *chutz la'aretz*.

This reasoning raises a question that remains somewhat open: it explains why an individual *in* *chutz la'aretz* might personally prefer to handle a *bechor* or *maaser* locally, but it does not fully explain why *we* — as a matter of *halacha* — prefer that they remain in *chutz la'aretz* rather than seize the opportunity to come to *Eretz Yisrael* and bring a *korban*. *Tosafot* addresses this, and one might conjecture a practical concern: during the *regalim*, when pilgrims gathered in *Yerushalayim*, the volume of *bechor* and *maaser* animals arriving from the *diaspora* would have been enormous, and the policy may have been designed to manage that logistical burden. This question will resurface; indeed, the first *mishna* of a later *perek* opens with the striking heading *keitzad maarimin al habechor* — how does one use a legitimate stratagem to avoid the obligation of *bechor* — itself a testament to how seriously *chazal* took finding workable solutions within the framework of *halacha*.

## Making Blemish Permitted?

### Rav Aha Question

The *gemara* now records a question posed in *Eretz Yisrael*: *hamatil mum b'tmurat bechor u'maaser mahu* — what is the law if one intentionally makes a *mum* in an animal that is the *temurah* of a *bechor* or *maaser*, or the *vlad* of a *maaser*? There is a well-known prohibition against deliberately blemishing *kodashim*, but in this case the animal cannot be brought as a *korban* under any circumstances. Does the prohibition against *matil mum b'kodashim* apply even where the animal's being brought as a *korban* is entirely foreclosed? Or does the prohibition only apply where blemishing would actually prevent a *korban* from being offered?

This is a practically significant question. If one is left with such an animal and cannot bring it as a *korban*, one would naturally prefer to induce a *mum* and eat it rather than maintaining the animal indefinitely. The question is whether doing so constitutes a transgression.

### Abaye's Daisy-Chain

Abaye responds by redirecting the question: *v'tiba'ei lach hamatil mum b'tshii maaser* — why didn't you ask the same question about the ninth animal counted in *maaser behema*? If one miscounted and declared the ninth animal the tithe, it is *kadosh v'eino karev* — it has *kedushat maaser* but cannot be brought as a *korban*, because it is not the actual tenth. Can one make a *mum* in that animal? If you did not think to ask that question — presumably because you took for granted that since it cannot be brought there is no prohibition — then apply the same reasoning here.

The comparison works in both directions: *d'rachmana miatinhu* — in the case of *tmuras bechor u'maaser*, the Torah excluded their *temurah* from being brought, stating *kodesh hem — hem kreivin v'ein tmuratan kriva*, only the original animals are brought, not their *temurah*. And *ha'asiri v'hotzi et hatshii* — for *maaser*, the Torah specifies the tenth, thereby excluding the ninth. In both cases, the Torah's own language establishes that these animals cannot be offered.

### Conclusion: Permitted

The *gemara* records a second version of this exchange — attributed to Rav Pinchas the son of Rav Chisda — in which the question was actually posed about the ninth animal of *maaser*, and the response redirected to *tmuras bechor u'maaser*. In either direction, the logic is the same: each case takes the other's conclusion for granted, and from that mutual assumption the *gemara* concludes that it is permitted to make a *mum* in these animals, since they cannot be brought as *korbanot* regardless.

The *gemara* itself acknowledges that this reasoning is somewhat circular — the *pesukim* cited establish only that these animals are not brought, not that making a *mum* in them is therefore permitted. The conclusion is essentially reading an assumption back out of itself. Nevertheless, as the *gemara* frames it, that is indeed the operative conclusion: where an animal categorically cannot be brought as a *korban*, the prohibition against making a *mum* does not apply. The practical *nafka mina* is significant: one need not wait indefinitely for a *mum* to appear naturally but may induce one directly.

### Tosafot Distinction

*Tosafot* notes that one might have expected the *gemara* to relate this question to a parallel discussion in *Menachot* concerning whether one may make a *mum* in an animal that already possesses a *mum*. In that case too, the animal cannot be brought as a *korban* — so why doesn't that discussion resolve our question? *Tosafot* explains that the cases are not equivalent, and their answers need not be the same.

Two distinctions are offered. First, in the *Menachot* case, the animal started with a full *kedusha* capable of producing a *korban* and only subsequently became blemished. That original *kedusha* may retain its full protective force even once the animal can no longer be brought. Here, by contrast, the *kedusha* of *tmuras bechor* and *vlad maaser* was never capable of producing a *korban* from the outset, and the prohibition may simply never have applied with full force. Second, from the opposite direction: in the *Menachot* case, adding a *mum* to an already-blemished animal does not transform an unblemished *korban* into a blemished one, so the concern about degrading a *korban* is absent — yet even there it might be prohibited. Here, the animal is unblemished and making a *mum* is a meaningful act — yet it may still be permitted, because the prohibition is tied to the possibility of the animal's being brought. The two cases thus present different profiles, and *Tosafot* concludes that while they raise analogous questions, their outcomes are not interdependent.

## Three Tannaic Opinions

### Contradiction Setup

The *gemara* now addresses an apparent contradiction regarding bringing *bechor* from outside *Eretz Yisrael*. Our *mishna* implies that *bedi'avad*, if a *bechor* was brought from *chutz la'aretz* while unblemished, it is offered as a *korban*. Yet a *mishna* in *Challa* records that a certain Antignos brought *bechorot* up from *Bavel* and they were refused — even *bedi'avad*, they would not accept it. Rav Chisda resolves the contradiction: *ha Rabbi Yishmael ha Rabbi Akiva* — these represent the positions of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva respectively. A *braita* cited in the name of Rabbi Yossi records three traditions he received from three different elders. We now examine each in turn.

---

## Rabbi Akiva

### Akiva's Distinction

Rabbi Akiva's position is: *yachol ya'aleh bechor michutz la'aretz la'aretz bizman sheBeis HaMikdash kayam veyakrivenu* — one might think a *bechor* could be brought from outside *Eretz Yisrael* when the *Beis HaMikdash* stands and offered as a *korban*, even *bedi'avad*. The Torah teaches otherwise from the verse *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha maaser degancha tiroshcha ve'yitzharecha u'bechorot bekarcha ve'tzonecha* — you shall eat before God your *maaser* of grain and wine and oil and the firstborn of your cattle and flock. The verse links *bechor* to *maaser dagan*, and from that link Rabbi Akiva derives: *mimakom she'atah maaleh maaser dagan atah maaleh bechor* — only from the place where you bring *maaser* of grain do you bring your *bechor*. And *maaser dagan* is an agricultural obligation tied to *Eretz Yisrael* — one does not bring *maaser dagan* from *chutz la'aretz*. Therefore one does not bring *bechor* from *chutz la'aretz* either, even *bedi'avad*.

---

## Three Tannaic Opinions…

### Rabbi Yishmael

Rabbi Yishmael's position is that one simply cannot bring a *bechor* nowadays — meaning during the period when the *Beis HaMikdash* does not stand — and the question he explores is whether *maaser sheni* may still be eaten in *Yerushalayim*.

### Logical Derivation

Rabbi Yishmael poses a *kal vachomer*-style derivation: *bechor ta'un hava'at makom u'maaser ta'un hava'at makom* — both *bechor* and *maaser* must be brought to *Yerushalayim*. Just as *bechor* cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash* — because it must be offered on the *mizbe'ach* — perhaps *maaser sheni* likewise cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash*. The *braita* considers and rejects two possible analogies. First: learn from *bechor* — but *bechor* requires sprinkling blood and offering *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*, which distinguishes it. Second: learn from *bikurim* — both *bikurim* and *maaser* require being brought to *Yerushalayim* without necessarily being burned on the *mizbe'ach*. But *bikurim* requires the ritual of *hanacha* — *vehinachto lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — a placement before the altar, connecting it to the *Beis HaMikdash*. *Maaser sheni* has no such ritual; perhaps its eating in *Yerushalayim* is entirely independent of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

The *talmud lomar* — the verse itself — resolves the matter: *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha maaser degancha...u'bechorot bekarcha ve'tzonecha*. The Torah places *maaser* and *bechor* side by side. *Hikish maaser le'bechor* — the verse creates an equation: just as *bechor* cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash*, so too *maaser sheni* cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash*. The conceptual question this leaves open — whether *maaser sheni*'s connection to *Yerushalayim* is an extension of the *Beis HaMikdash* or something independent that the Torah arbitrarily links to *bechor* — the *gemara* does not fully resolve. But *halachically* the link is firm.

---

## Rabbi Akiva…

### Akiva's Derivation

Rabbi Akiva uses the same verse — *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha maaser degancha...u'bechorot bekarcha ve'tzonecha* — but reads it in the opposite direction from Rabbi Yishmael. Where Rabbi Yishmael learns about *maaser* from *bechor*, Rabbi Akiva learns about *bechor* from *maaser*: *mimakom she'atah maaleh maaser dagan atah maaleh bechor* — the same place from which you bring *maaser* of grain is the place from which you bring *bechor*. Since *maaser dagan* is not brought from *chutz la'aretz*, neither is *bechor*. The two *tanna'im* thus use the same verse's equation in opposite directions to reach opposite rulings — Rabbi Yishmael concluding that *maaser sheni* follows *bechor* in requiring the *Beis HaMikdash*, Rabbi Akiva concluding that *bechor* follows *maaser dagan* in being limited to *Eretz Yisrael*.

The underlying rationale for Rabbi Akiva's position is conceptual. The obligations of *bechor* and *maaser behema* are not simply about the *kedusha* of the land in a narrow, soil-based sense. They are part of the agricultural cycle — the yearly products of national life in *Eretz Yisrael*. The Torah conceives of *Bnei Yisrael* as engaged in agriculture in their land, and *bechor* and *maaser* are expressions of that national reality. When one is living outside that reality, in *chutz la'aretz*, bringing *bechor* and *maaser* loses its proper context. A regular *korban* may appropriately be brought from anywhere, because its significance is not tied to the land's agricultural life. But *bechor* and *maaser* belong to the yearly product of the nation living in its land — and that is precisely what Rabbi Akiva is expressing when he says: bring *bechor* only from the same place from which you bring *maaser dagan*.

## Three Tannaic Opinions…

### Common Denominator Q

The *gemara* asks: why was the verse necessary at all? Could one not have derived the same conclusion from a *mah hatzad* — a common-denominator argument — between *bechor* and *bikurim*? Each was considered independently and rejected, but their shared features, after eliminating the respective distinguishing qualities, should yield the conclusion.

### Pesuk Necessity

Rav Ashi answers: *mishum de'ika lemeimar mah le'hatzad hashaveh shebahen sheyesh bahen tzad mizbe'ach* — one could argue that what *bechor* and *bikurim* have in common is precisely that they both involve some ritual on or at the *mizbe'ach*. *Bechor* has blood and *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*; *bikurim* has the *hanacha* ritual before the *mizbe'ach*. *Maaser sheni* has no such ritual whatsoever. One might therefore argue that the *Yerushalayim* eating of *maaser sheni* is genuinely independent of the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the common denominator fails. The verse is therefore necessary to establish the link explicitly.

This analysis crystallizes the conceptual question: how do we think about *Yerushalayim* vis-à-vis *maaser sheni*? Is its status an extension of the *Beis HaMikdash* — such that without a *Beis HaMikdash* it becomes irrelevant — or is *Yerushalayim* for *maaser sheni* a category unto itself, independent of the *Mikdash*? The *gemara* does not provide a full conceptual resolution; it simply establishes *halachically*, via the verse, that *maaser* follows *bechor* in requiring the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Kedusha Scenario

The *gemara* probes further with a sharper question. Rabbi Yishmael's discussion was premised on a situation where it is taken for granted that one cannot eat *bechor* nowadays but it is not immediately obvious whether the same is true of *maaser sheni*. How is this possible? Either the *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim* and the *makom hamikdash* still exists — in which case one should theoretically be able to bring *bechor* as well, simply by building a *mizbe'ach* — or it does not exist, in which case *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim* is obviously irrelevant.

The *gemara* presses: *de'i salka da'atach kedusha rishona kidsha lesha'atah velo kidsha le'atid lavo* — if the *kedusha* has lapsed, then even the question about *maaser sheni* should not arise. And if the first *kedusha* remains *le'atid lavo*, then *afilu bechor nami* — even *bechor* should theoretically be possible. So where exactly is the question located?

The *gemara* resolves this by proposing a specific scenario. We do assume — following the view that the first *kedusha* endures — that *Yerushalayim* and the *makom hamikdash* retain their *kedusha* today. In theory, one could build a *mizbe'ach* and offer *korbanot*, as is well known from the relevant discussion in later authorities. But there is a specific *halacha* that prevents eating the meat of a *bechor* even in a case where the blood has already been sprinkled. The scenario: imagine a *bechor* was brought to the *Beis HaMikdash* on the very last day, the blood was sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach* — and then the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed. The meat is still present the next day. Why can one not eat it?

### Blood-Meat Unity

*Keivan de'ee issa ledam lavar zvika hu, asi basar yalif midam* — since if one now wanted to slaughter the animal, it could not be done with *zerikat hadam*, and *basar* is derived from *dam*: meat takes its permissibility from the ability to perform the blood service. At the moment when the blood can no longer be sprinkled on a *mizbe'ach*, eating the meat is prohibited, even if the blood was already sprinkled while the *Beis HaMikdash* stood. This establishes a *halacha* — not merely a practical impossibility — that one cannot eat *bechor* at a time when the *mizbe'ach* no longer functions. From that *halacha* regarding *bechor*, one then derives via the verse's equation that *maaser sheni* likewise cannot be eaten when the *mizbe'ach* is absent.

The *gemara* then raises the objection of Rabbi Yochanan's rule: *kol hatorah kulah lemedin lamed min lamed chutz mikodshim* — throughout the entire Torah one may do a "daisy-chain" derivation, learning from B to C because one learned from A to B — except in the case of *kodshim*. Here we have a double derivation: from the inability to do *zerikat hadam* to the prohibition on eating the *bechor*'s meat (*dam* to *basar*), and from there to *maaser sheni*. Since *bechor* is *kodshim*, one cannot daisy-chain within it. The answer: *maaser chullin hu* — *maaser sheni*, for the purposes of this rule, is not considered *kodshim* of the altar. There is a broader debate about whether *maaser sheni* is *mamon gavoah* or *mamon hedyot*, but either way it is not *korbanot hekdesh*, and therefore the daisy-chain prohibition does not apply to the final link. However, the objection can be reformulated: even if C (*maaser sheni*) is not *kodshim*, A and B (*dam* and *basar* of *bechor*) are — and if one says the prohibition on daisy-chaining follows the status of A and B rather than C, the problem remains. The *gemara* resolves this: *basar vedam chada milsa hi* — the blood and the meat of a *bechor* are fundamentally one unit. Deriving the meat's status from the blood's status is not a daisy-chain at all; it is an internal application within a single legal entity. Thus the derivation stands, and the conclusion of Rabbi Yishmael is firmly grounded.

## Rabbi Akiva…

### Agricultural Basis

Rabbi Akiva's conceptual basis for excluding *bechor* from *chutz la'aretz* reflects a broader understanding of what *bechor* and *maaser behema* represent. These obligations belong to the agricultural cycle — the yearly produce of national life in *Eretz Yisrael*. The Torah conceives of *Bnei Yisrael* as engaged in agriculture within their land, and *bechor* and *maaser* are expressions of that national-agricultural reality. One who is living outside that framework, in *chutz la'aretz*, is not participating in the context that gives these obligations their meaning. A regular *korban* may be brought from anywhere because its significance is not bound to the land's agricultural life. But *bechor* and *maaser* belong to the yearly product of the nation in its land. That is the logic of Rabbi Akiva's derivation: bring *bechor* only from the same place from which you bring *maaser dagan*.

## Ben Azzai Position

### Ben Azzai Question

The third tradition cited by Rabbi Yossi is that of Ben Azzai. Ben Azzai addresses yet another question derivable from the verse's equation of *maaser* and *bechor*. He asks: *yachol maaleh maaser sheni yochlenu bechol haroeh* — perhaps when bringing *maaser sheni* to *Yerushalayim*, one need not limit oneself to within the walls but may eat it anywhere visible from *Yerushalayim* — the area known as *kol haroeh*. The concept of *kol haroeh* is familiar in other contexts: cities visible from a walled city read the *Megillah* on the fifteenth. Might *Yerushalayim*'s status for *maaser sheni* extend similarly beyond its walls?

### Logical Derivation

Ben Azzai attempts a logical derivation: *bechor ta'un hava'at makom u'maaser ta'un hava'at makom* — both *bechor* and *maaser* must be brought to *Yerushalayim*. Just as *bechor* is eaten *lifnim min hachoma* — within the walls — perhaps *maaser* follows suit and is also limited to within the walls.

### Mizbe'ach Distinction

The derivation is rejected: *mah l'bechor sheken ta'un matan damim ve'imurim legabei mizbe'ach* — *bechor* has blood and *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*, which ties it to the walls as an extension of the *Beis HaMikdash*. *Maaser sheni* has no such *mizbe'ach* connection — perhaps its *Yerushalayim* definition is not the same as *bechor*'s.

### Verse Link

The verse connecting *maaser* to *bechor* then resolves the question: *makish maaser le'bechor — mah bechor ne'echal lifnim min hachoma af maaser ne'echal lifnim min hachoma* — just as *bechor* is eaten within the walls, so too *maaser sheni* is limited to within the walls. The Torah's equation forecloses the possibility of extending *maaser*'s domain to *kol haroeh*.

### Walls vs. Kol HaRoeh

The *gemara* asks: what prompted Ben Azzai to even raise the idea of *kol haroeh*? Where does that concept get its foothold? If the *mishna* already establishes that eating within the walls is the rule, why would anyone think to extend it?

### Shilo Precedent

The source is a *mishna* in *Zevachim* which establishes the difference between the period when the *Mishkan* stood at *Shilo* and the period of *Yerushalayim*: *ela shebeShilo ochlin kodashim kalim u'maaser sheni bechol haroeh* — at *Shilo*, one ate *kodashim kalim* and *maaser sheni* wherever visible from the *Mishkan*. *Uvi'Yerushalayim lifnim min hachoma* — in *Yerushalayim*, only within the walls. The reason *Shilo* used *kol haroeh* is that *Shilo* had no walls; *kol haroeh* served as the functional substitute for the walls that did not exist. Since at *Shilo* the extended area was included, one might have thought to apply that same principle to *Yerushalayim* even within the era of walls.

### Wall Limitation

The *gemara* concludes: *kol haroeh* at *Shilo* was a *bedi'avad* substitute for walls that did not exist. In *Yerushalayim*, where walls were consecrated with a formal *hakdashah*, the walls define the parameters — for *maaser* no less than for *korbanot*. The concept of *kol haroeh* served its purpose only where a wall-defined boundary did not exist. Once *Yerushalayim* has walls, the eating of *maaser sheni* follows the *halacha* of *bechor* and is confined to within those walls.

## Bechor Year Validity

### Third Opinion Psul

A third opinion (*acherim*) raises yet another question one might derive from the verse's equation of *maaser* and *bechor*: *yachol yehei bechor she'avar shenaso kifsulei hamukdashim veyipasel* — perhaps a *bechor* that has passed its first year becomes *pasul*, like other *psulei hamukdashin*. The Torah says *lifnei Hashem Elokecha tochlenu shana veshana* — you shall eat it before God year by year. This might indicate not merely an obligation to bring the *bechor* within the first year, but a definition: an animal older than one year is no longer a valid *bechor korban*.

### Maaser Connection

This is rejected via the same verse: *makish bechor le'maaser* — the Torah connects *bechor* to *maaser*. Just as *maaser* does not become *pasul* from one year to the next — indeed, the Torah contemplates eating *maaser* over multiple years, including the *biur ma'aser* clearing process every third year — so too *bechor* does not become *pasul* after its first year. The obligation to bring it within the year remains, but failure to do so does not render the animal an invalid *korban*.

### Shana BeShana

The other *tanna'im* in the *braita* — who use the verse's equation for other purposes (establishing the parameters of *Yerushalayim* for *maaser* or the limits of bringing from *chutz la'aretz*) — derive the rule that *bechor* is not *pasul* after a year from a different reading of the same phrase *shana veshana*: *yesh em labechor she'eino nifsal mishana lachaverta* — the *bechor* remains valid from one year to the next.

### Two Days Eaten

Having used *shana veshana* for the *pasul* question, the *acherim* still need a source for the rule that a *bechor* may be eaten for two days. The *gemara* establishes that a *bechor* is *ne'echal lishnei yamim velaila echad* — eaten for two days and one night. The *acherim* derive this from the phrase *shana veshana* itself: *erev Rosh Hashanah ve'Rosh Hashanah* — one can eat a *bechor* across two consecutive years by beginning on the eve of Rosh Hashanah and continuing into Rosh Hashanah itself, thus spanning two days across two calendar years.

### Two Days Source

For the other *rabbanan* — who already used *shana veshana* to teach that *bechor* is not *pasul* after a year — the source for eating over two days comes from elsewhere: *amar kra yiheyeh lach kehazeh* — the Torah says the *kohen* shall receive the *bechor* as he receives the *chazeh hatnufa*, the wave breast of a *shelamim*. Since the *chazeh hatnufa* of a *shelamim* is eaten for two days and one night, the same applies to *bechor*.

*Hadran alach Kol Pesulei HaMukdashin.*
