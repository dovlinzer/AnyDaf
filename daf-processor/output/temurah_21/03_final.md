# Temurah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Female Asham & Offspring

### Question on Female Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר חִיָּיא מֵרַבִּי אָבִין בַּר כָּהֲנָא: הִפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם, בְּנָהּ מַהוּ שֶׁיִּקְרַב לְעוֹלָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin bar Ḥiyya inquired of Rabbi Avin bar Kahana:** If one **designated a female** animal **as a guilt offering** and it gave birth to a male, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **its offspring is sacrificed as a burnt offering?**

The *gemara* picks up five lines from the wide lines on 21b with a question posed by one *amora* to another: *ba'ei minei Rabbi Avin bar Chiya me'Rabbi Avin bar Kahana* — Rabbi Avin bar Chiya asked Rabbi Avin bar Kahana: if one consecrated a female animal for an *asham*, what is the law regarding her offspring? Can the offspring be brought as an *olah*?

The background to this question is as follows. When a female animal is consecrated for an *asham*, it cannot be brought as an *asham*, since an *asham* requires a male. The normal consequence for an *asham* that becomes derailed — whether through *temurah*, through the owner having achieved atonement through another animal, or otherwise — is that one waits for it to acquire a *mum*, redeems it, and uses the proceeds to purchase an *olah*. So the question becomes: once the offspring of this female *asham* happens to be male, why not simply bring the animal itself as an *olah* rather than waiting for a *mum* and using the proceeds?

### Earlier Discussion Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And let him resolve** the inquiry **from** the statement **of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, who said** that **Rabbi Elazar concedes.** Rabbi Avin bar Ḥiyya **never heard** that statement.

The *gemara* observes that this very question appears to have been addressed in an earlier discussion: *tifshotei mid'Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina* — the matter can seemingly be resolved from the earlier teaching in which Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina weighed in on a structurally similar case. The earlier *mishna* discussed the *temurat olah* and its offspring, where Rabbi Eliezer maintained that once you arrive at a male you may bring it as an *olah*. One might assume the same reasoning applies here: a female consecrated for an *asham* produces a male offspring, and since derailed *asham* funds go toward an *olah*, why not bring the thing itself?

But the earlier discussion distinguished between the cases. There, when one consecrated a female for an *olah* — or when a male *olah* produced a *temurah* and then offspring — whenever one ultimately arrives at a male, the *kedusha* one is bringing it under (*olah*) matches the *kedusha* of the original. Here, however, the original *kedusha* was that of an *asham*, and what one now seeks to bring is an *olah*. Since the proposed *kedusha* of the derivative does not match the *kedusha* of its origin, Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina had implied that even Rabbi Eliezer would agree one cannot bring it.

The *gemara* then explains why the question is being asked afresh: *lo shmi'a lei* — Rabbi Avin bar Chiya was simply not party to that earlier discussion, and wished to reason through the matter himself.

### Avin's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנָהּ קָרֵב עוֹלָה. הַיְיא מַאי? עַד כַּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֶלָּא מַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה, דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי אָשָׁם דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ — אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹדֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **What is this? Rabbi Elazar says** in the mishna (18b) that the offspring itself is offered as a burnt offering **only** when **he designates a female** animal **as a burnt offering** and then it gives birth to a male, due to the fact **that there is burnt offering status** for a bird that is the same sex as **its mother. But with regard to** a female animal that was designated as **a guilt offering** and subsequently gave birth to a male, **where there is no guilt offering status** for animals that are the same sex **as its mother, even Rabbi Eliezer concedes** that its offspring is not sacrificed at all.

Rabbi Avin bar Kahana's response cuts against the earlier reasoning: *benah yikrav olah* — the male offspring may indeed be brought as an *olah*. His reasoning is that Rabbi Eliezer's position should not be read so narrowly as to require that the derivative's *kedusha* match the original. Rather, *mishum d'chazi l'akrava* — the operative principle for Rabbi Eliezer is simply that once the animal is fit to be brought, you bring it. There is no requirement that it carry the identical *kedusha* as the original.

### Challenge from Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: וְלָדָן וְלַד וְלָדָן עַד סוֹף כׇּל הָעוֹלָם יָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן עוֹלָה — בִּדְמֵיהֶן — אִין, עַצְמוֹ — לֹא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Avin bar Ḥiyya **raised an objection to** Rabbi Avin bar Kahana from the mishna: Rabbi Elazar says that in the case of **the offspring** of a female animal that was designated as a guilt offering or the offspring of the substitute of a guilt offering, **and the offspring of their offspring until the end of all time,** they graze until they become blemished, and then they are sold, and the owner **brings** an individual **burnt offering with** the **money** received for **their** sale. The Gemara infers: **With** the **money** received for **their** sale, **yes,** he brings a burnt offering, but the offspring **itself** is **not** sacrificed as a burnt offering.

The *gemara* challenges this reading from our own *mishna*. Our *mishna* addressed the case of the offspring of a *temurat asham*, a structurally similar case — the original *kedusha* was *asham*, and now one is dealing with a derivative. In that *mishna*, Rabbi Eliezer says *vlad u'vlad vlad ad sof kol ha'olam yavi bidmeihem olah* — the offspring and their offspring until the end of time: use the proceeds for an *olah*. The *mishna* says *bidmeihen* — their monetary value is used; *atzman lo* — the animals themselves are not brought, even once one arrives at a male.

This appears to directly contradict the position that Rabbi Eliezer would allow bringing the animal itself. One might anticipate a simple resolution: our *mishna* is discussing the case of a *temurat asham*, where the *halacha* of waiting for a *mum* and using the proceeds is already established at the level of the *temurah* itself, and that *halacha* passes down to the offspring. Whereas the original question concerned a female consecrated directly for an *asham*, which never entered that category, and whose offspring might therefore be brought directly. The *gemara*, however, does not pursue this path.

### Resolving Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כַּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֶלָּא מַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה, דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי אָשָׁם דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ — אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹדֶה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּשֵׁם עוֹלֶה עַל אִמּוֹ, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲזֵי לְהַקְרָבָה, וְהָא נָמֵי חֲזֵי לְהַקְרָבָה. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּלְדָה נְקֵבָה, וְעַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם לֹא אוֹלִיד חַד זָכָר?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְשַׁנֵּינָא [לָךְ] שִׁינּוּיֵי דְּחִיקִי בַּבְלָאֵי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּלְדָה נְקֵבוֹת עַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **What are we dealing with here?** It is a case **where** the female designated as a guilt offering or as the substitute of a guilt offering **gave birth to a female,** which cannot itself be offered as a guilt offering. Rabbi Avin bar Kahana responds: **I answer forced answers,** in the manner of the **Babylonians,** and say that the mishna is referring to a case **where it gave birth** to **females,** and those females also gave birth to females, **until the end of time.**

Instead, the *gemara* offers a different resolution: *k'gon she'yalda nekeiva* — perhaps our *mishna* is speaking of the scenario where only female offspring were produced. The *gemara* immediately presses: but the *mishna* says *vlad u'vlad vlad ad sof kol ha'olam* — across endless generations, did not even one male appear? The answer given is: *meshanyinan shinuyei d'chika b'Vavel* — this is one of those forced Babylonian-style answers. Yes, the scenario is that females were born for the entire lineage.

It is striking that the *gemara* itself frames this as a strained answer, characteristic of Babylonian *amoraim*, who were sometimes depicted by their colleagues in *Eretz Yisrael* as offering *kvetch* resolutions. Rabbi Avin bar Kahana appears to be an *amora* of *Eretz Yisrael*, and the framing here preserves that ambient tension in the tradition. The *gemara* notes that some versions contain an additional, marginally better answer — found in the *Shita Mekubetzet* — which reads the *mishna* as addressing the case of a female, with the male case simply left unaddressed rather than explicitly excluded. The *Gra* suggests yet another possibility, though the distinction between these resolutions is not dramatic. In either reading, the takeaway is that one cannot straightforwardly resolve the question of the female *asham*'s male offspring from this *mishna*, and Rabbi Avin bar Kahana's position — that the male offspring may be brought directly as an *olah* — retains its standing.

## Bechor & Maaser Rules

### Bechor/Maaser Intro

With those intricate discussions behind us, the *perek* concludes with a far more accessible sugya as the *gemara* transitions from *asham* and *olah* to *bechor* and *maaser*. A brief word of background is in order. The *halacha* of *bechor u'ma'aser* is different from all other *korbanot* in a fundamental way. The *pasuk* states *lo yimacher velo yiga'el* — they cannot be sold or redeemed. If they acquire a *mum*, you do not redeem them and transfer the value toward another *korban*. Instead, they retain their *kedusha*, are slaughtered outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, and are eaten — the *bechor* by the *kohen*, and *maaser* by any *Yisrael*.

The same principle extends to *temurah*. If one makes a *temurah* from a *bechor* or *maaser*, that *temurah* is not brought as a *korban*. The *gemara* derives this from *pesukim*, but the underlying logic is compelling on its own terms: *kedushah* of *bechor* and *kedushas ma'aser* cannot be transferred to an animal that is not inherently a firstborn or a tenth. Such a *temurah* can never achieve the full status of *bechor* or *maaser* and therefore cannot be brought on the altar. The resolution is the same as with the original animal: wait for a *mum*, then eat it outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תְּמוּרַת הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר, וְלָדָן וְלַד וְלָדָן עַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ כִּבְכוֹר וּכְמַעֲשֵׂר, וְיֵאָכְלוּ בְּמוּמָן לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the substitute of a firstborn** offering **and** the substitute of an animal **tithe** offering, and the **offspring** of those substitutes and **the offspring of their offspring until the end of time,** the halakhic status of **these** animals **is like** that of **a firstborn** offering **and like** that of an animal **tithe** offering in that they must be treated with sanctity: They graze until they become blemished, **and** at that point **they may be eaten in their blemished** state, the substitute of the firstborn by the priests and the substitute of the animal tithe **by** their **owners.** They are not sacrificed upon the altar like the original firstborn and animal tithe offerings.

The *mishna* states: *temurat habechor ve'hama'aser* — the *temurah* of a *bechor* and *maaser*, along with *vladan u'vlad vladan ad sof ha'olam* — their offspring and the offspring of their offspring until the end of time — *harei elu kebechor u'kema'aser*: they carry the same *kedusha* as *bechor* and *maaser*. This means not that they are brought as *korbanot*, but that they share the same *din*: *veyeachlu bemumam labe'alim* — one waits for a *mum* to appear and they are then eaten by their owners.

One point worth noting: *bechor* is by definition male, so there cannot be direct offspring of a *bechor* itself. But *maaser* can be female, allowing for direct offspring of the *maaser* animal. In either case — whether offspring of the *temurah* or direct offspring of a *maaser* — the same *halacha* applies: they are not brought as *korbanot*.

### Marketplace Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה בֵּין בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר לְבֵין כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים? שֶׁכׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים נִמְכָּרִים בָּאִיטְלֵז, וְנִשְׁחָטִין בָּאִיטְלֵז, וְנִשְׁקָלִין בְּלִיטְרָא, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the practical difference **between a firstborn** offering **and** an animal **tithe** offering **and all the** other **sacrificial animals?** The difference is **that all the** other **sacrificial animals** that were blemished and redeemed **are sold in the butchers' market [*ba'itliz*]**, **and slaughtered in the butchers' market, and weighed** and sold **by the *litra*,** in the manner that non-sacred meat is slaughtered and sold. This is the case with regard to all consecrated animals **except for the firstborn and** animal **tithe** offerings, which are sold only from the home and not by the *litra*.

The *mishna* then asks: *mah bein bechor u'maiser levein kol hakodashim* — what distinguishes *bechor* and *maaser* from all other *korbanot*? The answer: *shekol hakodashim nimkarin be'itliz venishchatin be'itliz venishkalin belitra* — all other *kodashim* that acquire a *mum* may be sold in the meat market, slaughtered in the meat market, and weighed out by the pound. *Chutz min habechor vehama'aser* — except for *bechor* and *maaser*.

The reasoning behind permitting the sale of other *kodashim* in the marketplace is essentially one of maximizing the transfer value. When an animal that was consecrated as a *korban* acquires a *mum*, one redeems its monetary value and uses those funds to purchase a replacement *korban*. Retail sale yields a higher price than wholesale, so *chazal* permitted even the indignity of selling it like ordinary meat — slaughtered and weighed in the market — because the proceeds benefit *hekdesh*. The greater the sale price, the greater the value transferred toward the next *korban*.

By contrast, *bechor* and *maaser* are never redeemed and their value is never transferred to another *korban*. The owner simply eats the animal himself. Since *hekdesh* gains nothing from a higher price, there is no basis to permit treating the animal with such disregard; we insist instead that it be sold privately, wholesale, in deference to the *kedusha* it still retains. One might add that unlike other *kodashim* whose *kedusha* has been transferred away at the time of redemption, *bechor* and *maaser* retain their *kedusha* throughout — an additional reason to handle them with greater care.

### No Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ לָהֶן פִּדְיוֹן, וְלִתְמוּרוֹתֵיהֶן פִּדְיוֹן, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in addition, all sacrificial animals that became blemished **are subject to redemption** through sale, at which point the money becomes sacred and the animal becomes non-sacred, **and their substitutes** are also subject **to redemption** through sale.This is true for all consecrated animals, **except for the firstborn and** animal **tithe** offerings, which are not subject to redemption.

The *mishna* continues: *veyesh lahem pidyon utmuratan pidyon* — all other *kodashim* that acquire a *mum* can be redeemed, and so too their *temurah* if it acquires a *mum*. *Chutz min habechor vehama'aser* — but *bechor* and *maaser* cannot be redeemed; a *mum* does not trigger redemption but simply permits eating outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Outside Eretz Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָאִין מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר, שֶׁאִם בָּאוּ תְּמִימִים — יִקְרְבוּ, וְאִם בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין — יֵאָכְלוּ בְּמוּמָן לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** all sacrificial animals **come** to be sacrificed in the Temple even **from outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **except for the firstborn and** animal **tithe** offerings, **which** should not be brought from outside Eretz Yisrael *ab initio*. But **if they came unblemished, they are sacrificed** in the Temple like a regular firstborn or animal tithe offering coming from Eretz Yisrael; **and if they are blemished** animals, **they may be eaten in their blemished** state, the firstborns by the priests and the animal tithes **by** their **owners.**

The *mishna* also states that all other *korbanot* may be brought from *chutz la'aretz*, whereas *bechor* and *maaser* may not be brought from outside *Eretz Yisrael* — though Rashi explains that *bedi'avad*, if they were brought unblemished, they are offered as *korbanot*. If they arrived with a *mum*, one eats them outside the *Beis HaMikdash* as one would in any case.

## Rabbi Shimon Explained

### Parnasa Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם שֶׁהַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר יֵשׁ לָהֶן פַּרְנָסָה מִמְּקוֹמָן, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנּוֹלַד בָּהֶם מוּם — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: What** is the **reason** for this last difference between them? It is **that the firstborn and** animal **tithe** offerings **have a remedy in their place** outside Eretz Yisrael, as they can graze until they become blemished and then can be eaten there. It is not necessary to bring them to Eretz Yisrael in order to eat them. **But** with regard to **all other sacrificial animals, even if a blemish develops in them, these** animals remain **in their sanctity,** and one must redeem them and bring another offering with the money of their redemption. Therefore, when they are unblemished it is proper to bring these animals themselves to Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbi Shimon explains why *bechor* and *maaser* should not be brought from *chutz la'aretz*: *shehabechor vehama'aser yesh lahem parnasa mimkomam* — *bechor* and *maaser* can be "sustained" where they are. Since they may be eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash* once they acquire a *mum*, they can be dealt with locally. If one is in *chutz la'aretz* and possesses a *bechor* or *maaser* that has a *mum*, or waits until it acquires one, there is no need to transport the animal to *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Other Korbanot Transfer

By contrast, all other *korbanot* — even if they acquire a *mum* — must have their value transferred and a replacement *korban* brought. There is no way to "resolve" the matter outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. Since those animals must come to ירושלים in any case, the *halacha* accepts them from *chutz la'aretz*.

This reasoning raises a question that remains somewhat open: it explains why an individual *in* *chutz la'aretz* might personally prefer to handle a *bechor* or *maaser* locally, but it does not fully explain why *we* — as a matter of *halacha* — prefer that they remain in *chutz la'aretz* rather than seize the opportunity to come to *Eretz Yisrael* and bring a *korban*. *Tosafot* addresses this, and one might conjecture a practical concern: during the *regalim*, when pilgrims gathered in *Yerushalayim*, the volume of *bechor* and *maaser* animals arriving from the *diaspora* would have been enormous, and the policy may have been designed to manage that logistical burden. This question will resurface; indeed, the first *mishna* of a later *perek* opens with the striking heading *keitzad maarimin al habechor* — how does one use a legitimate stratagem to avoid the obligation of *bechor* — itself a testament to how seriously *chazal* took finding workable solutions within the framework of *halacha*.

## Making Blemish Permitted?

### Rav Aha Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב עַזָּא: בָּעַן בְּמַעְרְבָא — הַמֵּטִיל מוּם בִּתְמוּרַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא קְרִיבָן — לָא מִיחַיַּיב, אוֹ דִילְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּקָדְשׁוּ — מִיחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Azza said** that **they inquired in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **one who inflicts a blemish on the substitute of a firstborn** offering **or** on the substitute of an animal **tithe** offering? **Do we say** that **since they are not sacrificed** on the altar like the actual firstborn or animal tithe offering, one who does this **is not liable,** as the prohibition against inflicting a blemish on a sacrificial animal applies only when one thereby disqualifies the animal from the altar, and that is not the case here; **or perhaps since they are sanctified, he is liable** for inflicting a blemish?

The *gemara* now records a question posed in *Eretz Yisrael*: *hamatil mum b'tmurat bechor u'maaser mahu* — what is the law if one intentionally makes a *mum* in an animal that is the *temurah* of a *bechor* or *maaser*, or the *vlad* of a *maaser*? There is a well-known prohibition against deliberately blemishing *kodashim*, but in this case the animal cannot be brought as a *korban* under any circumstances. Does the prohibition against *matil mum b'kodashim* apply even where the animal's being brought as a *korban* is entirely foreclosed? Or does the prohibition only apply where blemishing would actually prevent a *korban* from being offered?

This is a practically significant question. If one is left with such an animal and cannot bring it as a *korban*, one would naturally prefer to induce a *mum* and eat it rather than maintaining the animal indefinitely. The question is whether doing so constitutes a transgression.

### Abaye's Daisy-Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְתִיבְּעֵי לָךְ הַמֵּטִיל מוּם בִּתְשִׁיעִי שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר! אֶלָּא מַאי שְׁנָא תְּשִׁיעִי, דְּלָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לָךְ? דְּרַחֲמָנָא מַעֲטֵיהּ, ״עֲשִׂירִי״ — לְהוֹצִיא הַתְּשִׁיעִי. הָכִי נָמֵי, רַחֲמָנָא מַעֲטִינְהוּ, ״לֹא תִפְדֶּה קֹדֶשׁ הֵם״ — הֵם קְרֵיבִין, וְאֵין תְּמוּרָתָן קְרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava bar Rav Azza: **And let the dilemma be raised** in a case of **one who inflicts a blemish on** other sacrificial animals, such as **the ninth** animal counted while selecting the animal **tithe** offering, which was mistakenly declared to be the tenth. This animal is consecrated in that one is prohibited to work it or shear it until it becomes blemished, but it may not be sacrificed upon the altar. **Rather, what is different** about the **ninth** animal, which one called the tenth, **that you did not raise the dilemma** about it? It is **because the Merciful One excluded it,** with the verse: **"The tenth** shall be holy to the Lord" (Leviticus 27:32), which serves **to exclude** from the altar **the ninth** that was mistakenly declared to be the tenth. One may derive from here that one who inflicts a blemish upon this ninth animal is not liable. **Here, too,** with regard to the substitute of a firstborn, **the Merciful One excluded it** from the altar: "But the firstborn…**you shall not redeem; they are holy"** (Numbers 18:17). This indicates that **they,** the firstborns themselves, **are sacrificed, but their substitute is not sacrificed,** and it may be derived from here that one is also not liable for inflicting a blemish upon the substitute of a firstborn.

Abaye responds by redirecting the question: *v'tiba'ei lach hamatil mum b'tshii maaser* — why didn't you ask the same question about the ninth animal counted in *maaser behema*? If one miscounted and declared the ninth animal the tithe, it is *kadosh v'eino karev* — it has *kedushat maaser* but cannot be brought as a *korban*, because it is not the actual tenth. Can one make a *mum* in that animal? If you did not think to ask that question — presumably because you took for granted that since it cannot be brought there is no prohibition — then apply the same reasoning here.

The comparison works in both directions: *d'rachmana miatinhu* — in the case of *tmuras bechor u'maaser*, the Torah excluded their *temurah* from being brought, stating *kodesh hem — hem kreivin v'ein tmuratan kriva*, only the original animals are brought, not their *temurah*. And *ha'asiri v'hotzi et hatshii* — for *maaser*, the Torah specifies the tenth, thereby excluding the ninth. In both cases, the Torah's own language establishes that these animals cannot be offered.

### Conclusion: Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק מַתְנֵי לַהּ הָכִי: אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב עַזָּא, בָּעַן בְּמַעְרְבָא: הַמֵּטִיל מוּם בִּתְשִׁיעִי שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְתִיבְּעֵי לָךְ הַמֵּטִיל מוּם בִּתְמוּרַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר! אֶלָּא מַאי שְׁנָא תְּמוּרַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר דְּלָא מִיבַּעְיָא לָךְ? דְּרַחֲמָנָא מַעֲטִינְהוּ, ״קֹדֶשׁ הֵם״ — הֵן קְרֵיבִין וְאֵין תְּמוּרָתָן קְרֵיבָה. תְּשִׁיעִי שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי רַחֲמָנָא מַעֲטֵיהּ, ״הָעֲשִׂירִי״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַתְּשִׁיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak teaches** that statement in **this** way, in the opposite manner to the previous version: **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Azza, said** that **they inquired in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: In the case of **one who inflicts a blemish upon the ninth** animal counted while selecting the animal **tithe** offering, **what is** the *halakha*? Abaye **said to him: And let the dilemma be raised** with regard to **one who inflicts a blemish upon the substitute of a firstborn** offering **or** the substitute of an animal **tithe** offering. **Rather, what is different** about **the substitute of a firstborn or** the substitute of an animal **tithe that you did not raise the dilemma** about them? It is **because the Merciful One excluded them,** as it is stated: **"They are holy,"** which indicates that **they** themselves **are sacrificed, but their substitutes are not sacrificed.** With regard to **the ninth** animal counted while selecting the animal **tithe as well, the Merciful One excluded it,** as it is stated: **"The tenth,"** which serves **to exclude** from the altar **the ninth** that was mistakenly declared to be the tenth.

The *gemara* records a second version of this exchange — attributed to Rav Pinchas the son of Rav Chisda — in which the question was actually posed about the ninth animal of *maaser*, and the response redirected to *tmuras bechor u'maaser*. In either direction, the logic is the same: each case takes the other's conclusion for granted, and from that mutual assumption the *gemara* concludes that it is permitted to make a *mum* in these animals, since they cannot be brought as *korbanot* regardless.

The *gemara* itself acknowledges that this reasoning is somewhat circular — the *pesukim* cited establish only that these animals are not brought, not that making a *mum* in them is therefore permitted. The conclusion is essentially reading an assumption back out of itself. Nevertheless, as the *gemara* frames it, that is indeed the operative conclusion: where an animal categorically cannot be brought as a *korban*, the prohibition against making a *mum* does not apply. The practical *nafka mina* is significant: one need not wait indefinitely for a *mum* to appear naturally but may induce one directly.

### Tosafot Distinction

*Tosafot* notes that one might have expected the *gemara* to relate this question to a parallel discussion in *Menachot* concerning whether one may make a *mum* in an animal that already possesses a *mum*. In that case too, the animal cannot be brought as a *korban* — so why doesn't that discussion resolve our question? *Tosafot* explains that the cases are not equivalent, and their answers need not be the same.

Two distinctions are offered. First, in the *Menachot* case, the animal started with a full *kedusha* capable of producing a *korban* and only subsequently became blemished. That original *kedusha* may retain its full protective force even once the animal can no longer be brought. Here, by contrast, the *kedusha* of *tmuras bechor* and *vlad maaser* was never capable of producing a *korban* from the outset, and the prohibition may simply never have applied with full force. Second, from the opposite direction: in the *Menachot* case, adding a *mum* to an already-blemished animal does not transform an unblemished *korban* into a blemished one, so the concern about degrading a *korban* is absent — yet even there it might be prohibited. Here, the animal is unblemished and making a *mum* is a meaningful act — yet it may still be permitted, because the prohibition is tied to the possibility of the animal's being brought. The two cases thus present different profiles, and *Tosafot* concludes that while they raise analogous questions, their outcomes are not interdependent.

## Three Tannaic Opinions

### Contradiction Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם בָּאוּ תְּמִימִין כּוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס הֶעֱלָה בְּכוֹרוֹת מִבָּבֶל וְלֹא קִבְּלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הָא — רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that although the firstborn and animal tithe offerings should not be brought from outside Eretz Yisrael *ab initio*, **if they came unblemished,** they may be sacrificed. With regard to this issue, the Gemara **raises a contradiction:** There was an incident where **ben Antigonus brought up firstborn** animals **from Babylonia** in order to sacrifice them **and they did not accept them** in the Temple. **Rav Ḥisda said:** That is **not difficult: This** statement in the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** while **this** case of ben Antigonus is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.**

The *gemara* now addresses an apparent contradiction regarding bringing *bechor* from outside *Eretz Yisrael*. Our *mishna* implies that *bedi'avad*, if a *bechor* was brought from *chutz la'aretz* while unblemished, it is offered as a *korban*. Yet a *mishna* in *Challa* records that a certain Antignos brought *bechorot* up from *Bavel* and they were refused — even *bedi'avad*, they would not accept it. Rav Chisda resolves the contradiction: *ha Rabbi Yishmael ha Rabbi Akiva* — these represent the positions of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva respectively. A *braita* cited in the name of Rabbi Yossi records three traditions he received from three different elders. We now examine each in turn.

## Rabbi Akiva

### Akiva's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מִשּׁוּם שְׁלֹשָׁה זְקֵנִים, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֶה אָדָם מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה וְיֹאכְלֶנּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael disagreed over this matter: **Rabbi Yosei says three matters in the name of three elders:** Rabbi Yishmael, Rabbi Akiva, and Ben Azzai, each of whom issued a different statement with regard to a firstborn and second tithe. **Rabbi Yishmael says** that one **might** have thought that **a person may bring up second tithe in the present** day to Jerusalem **and eat it in Jerusalem.**

Rabbi Akiva's position is presented through a discussion of second tithe's connection to the firstborn. We now examine the opinions in detail.

### Logical Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: בְּכוֹר טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם, וּמַעֲשֵׂר טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם. מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת. לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּבְכוֹר — שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִים לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, תֹּאמַר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר דְּלָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **But** the opposite conclusion should be derived through **logical inference** from the *halakha* of a firstborn: **A firstborn requires** that it be **brought** to a specific **place,** i.e., Jerusalem, **and** second **tithe requires** that it be **brought** to a specific **place. Just as a firstborn is eaten only in the presence of the Temple, so too,** second **tithe is eaten only in the presence of the Temple.** This comparison can be refuted: **No, if you said** that this is true **with regard to a firstborn,** that it may be eaten only in the presence of the Temple, **as it requires placing the blood and sacrificial portions** of the offering **upon the altar, shall you** also **say** that this is the case **with regard to** second **tithe,** for **which** this **is not** required?

Rabbi Yishmael poses a *kal vachomer*-style derivation: *bechor ta'un hava'at makom u'maaser ta'un hava'at makom* — both *bechor* and *maaser* must be brought to *Yerushalayim*. Just as *bechor* cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash* — because it must be offered on the *mizbe'ach* — perhaps *maaser sheni* likewise cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash*. The *braita* considers and rejects two possible analogies. First: learn from *bechor* — but *bechor* requires sprinkling blood and offering *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*, which distinguishes it. Second: learn from *bikurim* — both *bikurim* and *maaser* require being brought to *Yerushalayim* without necessarily being burned on the *mizbe'ach*. But *bikurim* requires the ritual of *hanacha* — *vehinachto lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — a placement before the altar, connecting it to the *Beis HaMikdash*. *Maaser sheni* has no such ritual; perhaps its eating in *Yerushalayim* is entirely independent of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: בִּיכּוּרִים טְעוּנִין הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם, וּמַעֲשֵׂר טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם. מָה בִּיכּוּרִים אֵין נֶאֱכָלִין אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵין נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת. מָה לְבִיכּוּרִים, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין הַנָּחָה, תֹּאמַר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר דְּלָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rather, perhaps you will **say** that the *halakha* of eating second tithe in Jerusalem in the present is derived from a comparison to first fruits: **First fruits require** that they be **brought** to a specific **place,** i.e., Jerusalem, **and** second **tithe requires** that they be **brought** to a specific **place. Just as first fruits may be eaten only in the presence of the Temple, so too,** second **tithe may be eaten only in the presence of the Temple.** But this comparison is also flawed, as **what** is unique **about first fruits?** They are unique in **that they require placement** before the altar, as it is stated: "And the priest shall take the basket out of your hand, and set it down before the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 26:4). Perhaps it is for this reason that they must be eaten in the presence of the Temple. **Shall you say** that this is also the case with regard **to** second **tithe, which is not** obligated in placement before the altar?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ... מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְתִירוֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וּבְכוֹרוֹת בְּקָרְךָ וְצֹאנֶךָ״, הִקִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂר לִבְכוֹר: מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵין נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states** with regard to second tithe: **"And you shall eat before the Lord your God** in the place that He shall choose to cause His name to dwell there, **the tithe of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil, and the firstborn of your cattle and sheep"** (Deuteronomy 14:23). This verse **compares** second **tithe to the firstborn: Just as a firstborn is eaten only in the presence of the Temple, so too,** second **tithe is eaten only in the presence of the Temple.** This concludes the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.

The *talmud lomar* — the verse itself — resolves the matter: *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha maaser degancha...u'bechorot bekarcha ve'tzonecha*. The Torah places *maaser* and *bechor* side by side. *Hikish maaser le'bechor* — the verse creates an equation: just as *bechor* cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash*, so too *maaser sheni* cannot be eaten without the *Beis HaMikdash*. The conceptual question this leaves open — whether *maaser sheni*'s connection to *Yerushalayim* is an extension of the *Beis HaMikdash* or something independent that the Torah arbitrarily links to *bechor* — the *gemara* does not fully resolve. But *halachically* the link is firm.

## Rabbi Akiva Derivation

### Akiva's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֶה אָדָם בְּכוֹר מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים וְיַקְרִיבֶנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְתִירוֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וּבְכוֹרוֹת בְּקָרְךָ וְצֹאנֶךָ״ — מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲלֶה מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן, אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה בְּכוֹר. וּמִמְּקוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן — אִי אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה בְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites the second statement of Rabbi Yosei from the elders, in the continuation of the *baraita* above: **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that **a person may bring up a firstborn from outside Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael **when the Temple is standing and sacrifice it.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God,** in the place which He shall choose to cause His name to dwell there, **the tithe of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil, and the firstborn of your herd and of your flock"** (Deuteronomy 14:23). Rabbi Akiva derives from here that only **from the place that you bring up the tithe of grain** to Jerusalem, i.e., from Eretz Yisrael, **may you bring up a firstborn** to the Temple as an offering. **But from a place that you may not bring up the tithe of grain,** i.e., outside Eretz Yisrael, **you may not bring up a firstborn** from there.

Rabbi Akiva uses the same verse — *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha maaser degancha...u'bechorot bekarcha ve'tzonecha* — but reads it in the opposite direction from Rabbi Yishmael. Where Rabbi Yishmael learns about *maaser* from *bechor*, Rabbi Akiva learns about *bechor* from *maaser*: *mimakom she'atah maaleh maaser dagan atah maaleh bechor* — the same place from which you bring *maaser* of grain is the place from which you bring *bechor*. Since *maaser dagan* is not brought from *chutz la'aretz*, neither is *bechor*. The two *tanna'im* thus use the same verse's equation in opposite directions to reach opposite rulings — Rabbi Yishmael concluding that *maaser sheni* follows *bechor* in requiring the *Beis HaMikdash*, Rabbi Akiva concluding that *bechor* follows *maaser dagan* in being limited to *Eretz Yisrael*.

The underlying rationale for Rabbi Akiva's position is conceptual. The obligations of *bechor* and *maaser behema* are not simply about the *kedusha* of the land in a narrow, soil-based sense. They are part of the agricultural cycle — the yearly products of national life in *Eretz Yisrael*. The Torah conceives of *Bnei Yisrael* as engaged in agriculture in their land, and *bechor* and *maaser* are expressions of that national reality. When one is living outside that reality, in *chutz la'aretz*, bringing *bechor* and *maaser* loses its proper context. A regular *korban* may appropriately be brought from anywhere, because its significance is not tied to the land's agricultural life. But *bechor* and *maaser* belong to the yearly product of the nation living in its land — and that is precisely what Rabbi Akiva is expressing when he says: bring *bechor* only from the same place from which you bring *maaser dagan*.

## Common Denominator Question

### Contradiction Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֶיהְדַּר דִּינָא, וְלֵיתֵי בְּ״מָה הַצַּד״! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן צַד מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **But let the inference return** and compare second tithe to both the firstborn and first fruits together, **and let** the *halakha* of second tithe **be derived by** analogy from **the common element [*bema hatzad*]** of the two sources: Just as the firstborn and first fruits both require that they be brought to a specific place and are not eaten in the present time, so too, second tithe, which also requires that it must be brought to a specific place, should not be eaten in the present time. **Rav Ashi said:** The *halakha* of second tithe cannot be derived in this manner, **because it can be said: What** is notable **about the common element of these** cases? They are notable in **that they have an aspect** of their *halakha* that involves **the altar:** A firstborn must have its blood and sacrificial portions placed upon the altar, and first fruits must be placed before the altar, whereas no obligation with regard to the altar applies to the second tithe at all.

The *gemara* asks: why was the verse necessary at all? Could one not have derived the same conclusion from a *mah hatzad* — a common-denominator argument — between *bechor* and *bikurim*? Each was considered independently and rejected, but their shared features, after eliminating the respective distinguishing qualities, should yield the conclusion.

Rav Ashi answers: *mishum de'ika lemeimar mah le'hatzad hashaveh shebahen sheyesh bahen tzad mizbe'ach* — one could argue that what *bechor* and *bikurim* have in common is precisely that they both involve some ritual on or at the *mizbe'ach*. *Bechor* has blood and *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*; *bikurim* has the *hanacha* ritual before the *mizbe'ach*. *Maaser sheni* has no such ritual whatsoever. One might therefore argue that the *Yerushalayim* eating of *maaser sheni* is genuinely independent of the *Beis HaMikdash*, and the common denominator fails. The verse is therefore necessary to establish the link explicitly.

## Kedusha Scenario Analysis

### Kedusha Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, לָא שְׁנָא בְּכוֹר וְלָא שְׁנָא מַעֲשֵׂר בְּנֵי הֲבָאָה נִינְהוּ. וְאִי קָסָבַר קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְלֹא קִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, אֲפִילּוּ בְּכוֹר נָמֵי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** According to Rabbi Yishmael, a firstborn is not eaten in the present day, but with regard to second tithe he is uncertain about the *halakha*. The Gemara asks: **And what does he hold? If he holds** that the **initial consecration** both **sanctified** Jerusalem **for its time and sanctified** Jerusalem **forever,** including the period after the destruction of the Temple, then **a firstborn is no different and** second **tithe is no different,** as both **are capable of being brought** to Jerusalem: In the case of the firstborn, an altar may be built upon which it may be offered, while second tithe may be eaten in Jerusalem, and the presence of the Temple is not required for either one. **And if he holds** that the **initial consecration sanctified** Jerusalem **for its time but did not sanctify** Jerusalem **forever,** and he is still uncertain about the status of second tithe, then **raise the dilemma even** with regard to the **firstborn.**

The *gemara* probes further with a sharper question. Rabbi Yishmael's discussion was premised on a situation where it is taken for granted that one cannot eat *bechor* nowadays but it is not immediately obvious whether the same is true of *maaser sheni*. How is this possible? Either the *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim* and the *makom hamikdash* still exists — in which case one should theoretically be able to bring *bechor* as well, simply by building a *mizbe'ach* — or it does not exist, in which case *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim* is obviously irrelevant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר, קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְלֹא קִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּזְרַק דָּמוֹ שֶׁל בְּכוֹר בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, וְחָרַב הַבַּיִת, וַעֲדַיִין בְּשָׂרוֹ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, he holds** that the **initial consecration sanctified** Jerusalem **for its time but did not sanctify it forever, and here we are dealing with** a case **where the blood of a firstborn** offering **was sprinkled** upon the altar **before the Temple** was destroyed, **and** then **the Temple was destroyed, but its flesh was still in existence.**

The *gemara* resolves this by proposing a specific scenario. We do assume — following the view that the first *kedusha* endures — that *Yerushalayim* and the *makom hamikdash* retain their *kedusha* today. In theory, one could build a *mizbe'ach* and offer *korbanot*, as is well known from the relevant discussion in later authorities. But there is a specific *halacha* that prevents eating the meat of a *bechor* even in a case where the blood has already been sprinkled. The scenario: imagine a *bechor* was brought to the *Beis HaMikdash* on the very last day, the blood was sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach* — and then the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed. The meat is still present the next day. Why can one not eat it?

### Blood-Meat Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִי אִיתֵיהּ לְדָם לָאו בַּר זְרִיקָה הוּא, אָתֵי בָּשָׂר יָלֵיף מִדָּם, וְאָתֵי מַעֲשֵׂר וְיָלֵיף מִבְּכוֹר. וּמִי יָלְפִינַן בְּקָדָשִׁים מֵהֲדָדֵי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לְמֵדִין לָמֵד מִן הַלָּמֵד, חוּץ מִן הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵין לְמֵדִין לָמֵד מִן הַלָּמֵד! מַעֲשֵׂר חוּלִּין הוּא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּתַר לָמֵד אָזְלִינַן, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בָּתַר מְלַמֵּד אָזְלִינַן, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בָּשָׂר וָדָם חֲדָא מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Since if the blood is** still **there** and had not yet been sprinkled upon the altar, it is **not subject to sprinkling,** as the Temple has been destroyed, and consequently **the meat** of the firstborn offering may not be eaten. This *halakha* **comes** and is **derived from** the *halakha* of **the blood,** based upon the juxtaposition in the following verse: "You shall sprinkle their blood against the altar… and their flesh shall be yours" (Numbers 18:17–18). This teaches that the meat may be eaten only when the blood is fit to be sprinkled upon the altar. By contrast, there is no such juxtaposition with regard to second tithe, and therefore Rabbi Yishmael remains uncertain whether it may be eaten in the present time in Jerusalem. The Gemara asks: **And can we derive** the *halakhot* of **sacrificial** food **from one another? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** In **the entire Torah, we may derive** that which is **derived from** a *halakha* that was itself **derived** from another source, **except for** the case of **sacrificial** animals, **where** we **do not derive** that which is **derived from** that which was already **derived** from another source. The Gemara answers: Second **tithe is** considered **non-sacred.** Therefore, this derivation does not pertain to consecrated items. The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who said** that **we follow** the *halakha* that is **derived.** If it is a consecrated item, it may not be derived from another *halakha* that was derived from another source, but if it is non-sacred, such as second tithe, it may be derived in this manner. **But according to the one who said** that **we follow** the *halakha* that **teaches, what is there to say?** In this case the *halakha* that teaches, i.e., the source of the derivation, is the meat of the firstborn, which is a consecrated item that is derived itself from the status of the blood of the firstborn. The Gemara answers: The status of **the meat and the blood** of a firstborn offering **are one matter.** Therefore, the meat is not considered as derived from another *halakha*.

*Keivan de'ee issa ledam lavar zvika hu, asi basar yalif midam* — since if one now wanted to slaughter the animal, it could not be done with *zerikat hadam*, and *basar* is derived from *dam*: meat takes its permissibility from the ability to perform the blood service. At the moment when the blood can no longer be sprinkled on a *mizbe'ach*, eating the meat is prohibited, even if the blood was already sprinkled while the *Beis HaMikdash* stood. This establishes a *halacha* — not merely a practical impossibility — that one cannot eat *bechor* at a time when the *mizbe'ach* no longer functions. From that *halacha* regarding *bechor*, one then derives via the verse's equation that *maaser sheni* likewise cannot be eaten when the *mizbe'ach* is absent.

The *gemara* then raises the objection of Rabbi Yochanan's rule: *kol hatorah kulah lemedin lamed min lamed chutz mikodshim* — throughout the entire Torah one may do a "daisy-chain" derivation, learning from B to C because one learned from A to B — except in the case of *kodshim*. Here we have a double derivation: from the inability to do *zerikat hadam* to the prohibition on eating the *bechor*'s meat (*dam* to *basar*), and from there to *maaser sheni*. Since *bechor* is *kodshim*, one cannot daisy-chain within it. The answer: *maaser chullin hu* — *maaser sheni*, for the purposes of this rule, is not considered *kodshim* of the altar. There is a broader debate about whether *maaser sheni* is *mamon gavoah* or *mamon hedyot*, but either way it is not *korbanot hekdesh*, and therefore the daisy-chain prohibition does not apply to the final link. However, the objection can be reformulated: even if C (*maaser sheni*) is not *kodshim*, A and B (*dam* and *basar* of *bechor*) are — and if one says the prohibition on daisy-chaining follows the status of A and B rather than C, the problem remains. The *gemara* resolves this: *basar vedam chada milsa hi* — the blood and the meat of a *bechor* are fundamentally one unit. Deriving the meat's status from the blood's status is not a daisy-chain at all; it is an internal application within a single legal entity. Thus the derivation stands, and the conclusion of Rabbi Yishmael is firmly grounded.

## Ben Azzai Position

### Ben Azzai Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֶה אָדָם מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וְיֹאכְלֶנּוּ בְּכׇל הָרוֹאֶה? וְדִין הוּא: בְּכוֹר טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם, וּמַעֲשֵׂר טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם — מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates the third statement in the *baraita*, which discusses second tithe: **Ben Azzai says** that one **might** have thought that he may **bring up second tithe and eat it in any** place **that overlooks** Jerusalem.**But** could this matter not be derived through **logical inference: A firstborn** offering **requires** that it be **brought** to the **place,** i.e., Jerusalem, **and** a second **tithe requires** that it be **brought** to the **place. Just as a firstborn** animal **is eaten only within the walls** of Jerusalem, **so too,** second **tithe is eaten only within the walls** of Jerusalem.

The third tradition cited by Rabbi Yosei is that of Ben Azzai. Ben Azzai addresses yet another question derivable from the verse's equation of *maaser* and *bechor*. He asks: *yachol maaleh maaser sheni yochlenu bechol haroeh* — perhaps when bringing *maaser sheni* to *Yerushalayim*, one need not limit oneself to within the walls but may eat it anywhere visible from *Yerushalayim* — the area known as *kol haroeh*. The concept of *kol haroeh* is familiar in other contexts: cities visible from a walled city read the *Megillah* on the fifteenth. Might *Yerushalayim*'s status for *maaser sheni* extend similarly beyond its walls?

### Logical Derivation

The derivation is rejected: *mah l'bechor sheken ta'un matan damim ve'imurim legabei mizbe'ach* — *bechor* has blood and *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*, which ties it to the walls as an extension of the *Beis HaMikdash*. *Maaser sheni* has no such *mizbe'ach* connection — perhaps its *Yerushalayim* definition is not the same as *bechor*'s.

### Verse Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִבְכוֹר — שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, תֹּאמַר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר דְּלָא?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ... מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְתִירוֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וּבְכוֹרוֹת בְּקָרְךָ וְצֹאנֶךָ״ — מַקִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂר לִבְכוֹר: מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** This inference may be refuted: **What** is unique **about a firstborn?** It is unique in **that it requires the placement of the blood and sacrificial portions upon the altar. Shall you** also **say** that this is the case **with regard to** second **tithe, where** that **is not** required? Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God,** in the place which He shall choose to cause His name to dwell there, **the tithe of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil, and the firstborn of your herd and of your flock."** The verse **juxtaposes** second **tithe** and the **firstborn,** to teach that **just as the firstborn is eaten only within the walls, so too,** second **tithe is eaten only within the walls.**

The verse connecting *maaser* to *bechor* then resolves the question: *makish maaser le'bechor — mah bechor ne'echal lifnim min hachoma af maaser ne'echal lifnim min hachoma* — just as *bechor* is eaten within the walls, so too *maaser sheni* is limited to within the walls. The Torah's equation forecloses the possibility of extending *maaser*'s domain to *kol haroeh*.

### Walls vs. Kol HaRoeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ דְּקָאָמַר ״יָכוֹל״? אָמַרְתָּ הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן: אֵין בֵּין שִׁילֹה לִירוּשָׁלַיִם אֶלָּא שֶׁבְּשִׁילֹה אוֹכְלִין קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּכׇל הָרוֹאֶה, וּבִירוּשָׁלַיִם לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה, וְכֵן קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם לַיְתֵי מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּכׇל הָרוֹאֶה? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **What is difficult for** ben Azzai **that** prompted him to **say:** One **might** have thought that it is permitted to eat second tithe in any place that overlooks Jerusalem? The Gemara explains: **You** can **say** that it is difficult for him **since we learned** in a mishna (*Megilla* 9b): **The difference between** the Tabernacle in **Shiloh and** the Temple in **Jerusalem is only that in Shiloh one eats offerings of lesser sanctity,** e.g., individual peace offerings, thanks offerings, and the Paschal lamb, **and** also **the second tithe, in any place that overlooks** Shiloh,**but in Jerusalem** one eats those consecrated items only **within the walls.** **And likewise, offerings of the most sacred order** are eaten only in the area **within the hangings,** which surrounded the courtyard in the Tabernacle in Shiloh, which was equivalent to the area within the surrounding wall in the Temple courtyard in Jerusalem. **Lest you say** that even **in Jerusalem, they may bring second tithe** and eat it **in any** location **that overlooks** the walls, since, unlike the firstborn animal, second tithe does not possess any unique stringency, the verse **teaches us** that this is not so, as taught in the mishna.

The source is a *mishna* in *Zevachim* which establishes the difference between the period when the *Mishkan* stood at *Shilo* and the period of *Yerushalayim*: *ela shebeShilo ochlin kodashim kalim u'maaser sheni bechol haroeh* — at *Shilo*, one ate *kodashim kalim* and *maaser sheni* wherever visible from the *Mishkan*. *Uvi'Yerushalayim lifnim min hachoma* — in *Yerushalayim*, only within the walls. The reason *Shilo* used *kol haroeh* is that *Shilo* had no walls; *kol haroeh* served as the functional substitute for the walls that did not exist. Since at *Shilo* the extended area was included, one might have thought to apply that same principle to *Yerushalayim* even within the era of walls.

### Wall Limitation

The *gemara* concludes: *kol haroeh* at *Shilo* was a *bedi'avad* substitute for walls that did not exist. In *Yerushalayim*, where walls were consecrated with a formal *hakdashah*, the walls define the parameters — for *maaser* no less than for *korbanot*. The concept of *kol haroeh* served its purpose only where a wall-defined boundary did not exist. Once *Yerushalayim* has walls, the eating of *maaser sheni* follows the *halacha* of *bechor* and is confined to within those walls.

## Bechor Year Validity

### Third Opinion Psul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: יָכוֹל יְהֵא בְּכוֹר שֶׁעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ כִּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, וְיִפָּסֵל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְתִירוֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ״, מַקִּישׁ בְּכוֹר לְמַעֲשֵׂר, מָה מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, אַף בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Rabbi Yosei cites one more statement in the *baraita* with regard to a firstborn: The mishna (*Bekhorot* 26b) states that a firstborn animal is eaten from one year to the next, i.e., within its first year, as it is stated: "You shall eat it before the Lord your God year by year" (Deuteronomy 15:20). Rabbi Yosei adds that **others say:** One **might** have thought that **a firstborn whose first year has passed should** have the same status **as disqualified consecrated** animals **and** therefore **be disqualified.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And you shall eat before the Lord your God…**the tithe of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil,** and the firstborn of your herd and of your flock." The verse thereby **juxtaposes a firstborn** animal **to** second **tithe,** which teaches that **just as** second **tithe is not disqualified from** one **year to the next, so too, a firstborn** animal **is not disqualified from** one **year to the next.**

A third opinion (*acherim*) raises yet another question one might derive from the verse's equation of *maaser* and *bechor*: *yachol yehei bechor she'avar shenaso kifsulei hamukdashim veyipasel* — perhaps a *bechor* that has passed its first year becomes *pasul*, like other *psulei hamukdashin*. The Torah says *lifnei Hashem Elokecha tochlenu shana veshana* — you shall eat it before God year by year. This might indicate not merely an obligation to bring the *bechor* within the first year, but a definition: an animal older than one year is no longer a valid *bechor korban*.

This is rejected via the same verse: *makish bechor le'maaser* — the Torah connects *bechor* to *maaser*. Just as *maaser* does not become *pasul* from one year to the next — indeed, the Torah contemplates eating *maaser* over multiple years, including the *biur ma'aser* clearing process every third year — so too *bechor* does not become *pasul* after its first year. The obligation to bring it within the year remains, but failure to do so does not render the animal an invalid *korban*.

### Shana BeShana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבָּנַן, דְּמַפְּקִי לְהוּ לְטַעְמָא אַחֲרִינָא, מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ מְנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ מִ״לִּפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תֹאכְלֶנּוּ שָׁנָה בְשָׁנָה״, לִימֵּד עַל הַבְּכוֹר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the Rabbis,** i.e., Rabbi Yishmael, Rabbi Akiva, and ben Azzai, **who expound** this verse **for another explanation, from where do they** derive that a firstborn animal is not disqualified **from** one **year to the next?** The Gemara answers: **They derive it from** the verse **"You shall eat it before the Lord your God year by year."** The formulation "year by year" indicates two years, thereby **teaching with regard to a firstborn** animal **that it is not disqualified from** one **year to the next.**

The other *tanna'im* in the *braita* — who use the verse's equation for other purposes (establishing the parameters of *Yerushalayim* for *maaser* or the limits of bringing from *chutz la'aretz*) — derive the rule that *bechor* is not *pasul* after a year from a different reading of the same phrase *shana veshana*: *yesh em labechor she'eino nifsal mishana lachaverta* — the *bechor* remains valid from one year to the next.

### Two Days Eaten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלַאֲחֵרִים, לִפְנֵי ״ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תֹאכְלֶנּוּ שָׁנָה בְשָׁנָה״, מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: יוֹם אֶחָד מִשָּׁנָה זוֹ, וְיוֹם אֶחָד מִשָּׁנָה אַחֶרֶת, לִימֵּד עַל הַבְּכוֹר שֶׁנֶּאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וָלָיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **the others,** who derive this *halakha* from the juxtaposition of the firstborn and second tithe, with regard to **what do they interpret** the verse: **"You shall eat it before the Lord your God year by year"?** The Gemara answers: **It is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the time for the consumption of a firstborn animal: The phrase "year by year" teaches that there is a manner in which it may be eaten over two years: During **one day of this year, and** during **one day of the next year.** The verse therefore **teaches with regard to a firstborn** offering **that it may be eaten for two days and one night** in between.

Having used *shana veshana* for the *pasul* question, the *acherim* still need a source for the rule that a *bechor* may be eaten for two days. The *gemara* establishes that a *bechor* is *ne'echal lishnei yamim velaila echad* — eaten for two days and one night. The *acherim* derive this from the phrase *shana veshana* itself: *erev Rosh Hashanah ve'Rosh Hashanah* — one can eat a *bechor* across two consecutive years by beginning on the eve of Rosh Hashanah and continuing into Rosh Hashanah itself, thus spanning two days across two calendar years.

### Two Days Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, דְּנֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וָלָיְלָה אֶחָד מְנָא לְהוּ? אָמַר קְרָא: ״יִהְיֶה לָּךְ כַּחֲזֵה הַתְּנוּפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** Rabbi Yishmael, Rabbi Akiva, and ben Azzai, **from where do they** derive the *halakha* **that** a firstborn offering **may be eaten for two days and one night?** The Gemara answers that **the verse states** with regard to the firstborn: "And their flesh **shall be yours, like the breast of the waving** and the right thigh, it shall be yours" (Numbers 18:18). The repetition of the expression "It shall be yours" teaches that one may eat the firstborn for one more day than a standard thanks offering, which may be eaten only for one day and one night.

For the other *rabbanan* — who already used *shana veshana* to teach that *bechor* is not *pasul* after a year — the source for eating over two days comes from elsewhere: *amar kra yiheyeh lach kehazeh* — the Torah says the *kohen* shall receive the *bechor* as he receives the *chazeh hatnufa*, the wave breast of a *shelamim*. Since the *chazeh hatnufa* of a *shelamim* is eaten for two days and one night, the same applies to *bechor*.
