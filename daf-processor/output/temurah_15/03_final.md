# Temurah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Sacrifices

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְלֵימָא מָר שַׁלְמֵי פֶסַח, דְּאִי שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר — נִידָּר וְנִידָּב הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Dimi, when he cited this statement in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **But let the Master say** that the phrase "and your peace offerings" serves to include the **peace offering** that is brought together with **a Paschal offering.** This offering is sacrificed on the fourteenth of Nisan by a large group of people when they will not receive enough meat from their Paschal offering to feed them all. The suggested derivation from the verse is that if a peace offering separated for this purpose was not sacrificed on the fourteenth of Nisan, it may be brought during the intermediate days of the Festival. Abaye further adds: It is more reasonable to include this peace offering, **as, if** the verse is referring to **the peace offering of a nazirite, it is** already included by the verse in the categories of offerings that are **vowed or contributed** voluntarily.

The daf begins six lines down on 14b, where Abaye poses a question against a *baraita* that had been cited in the course of a broader discussion about the differences between *korbanot tzibur* and *korbanot yachid*. That earlier discussion had led the *Gemara* to quote a lengthy *baraita* based on the verse *u'minchatam u'niskeihem*, which catalogued the various *korbanot* that could be brought on *Chol HaMoed*. Among the words of that verse, *nidreichem u'nidvoteichem* — "your vows and your free-will offerings" — was understood to cover all voluntary sacrifices, leaving the remaining words of the verse to cover obligatory ones. The *baraita* used one set of words to include the *korbanot* of a *nazir*, reading *u'shalmeichem* as referring to *shalmei nazir*.

Abaye's challenge is pointed: why derive the *shalmei nazir* from that word at all? If the *shalmei nazir* are in the category of *neder u'nedavah*, they are already covered by *nidreichem u'nidvoteichem*. And if that is so, the remaining word would be better used to include *shalmei pesach* — the *shlamim* brought to accompany the *korban pesach* so that one may eat *al hasova*, satisfied, before consuming the *pesach* itself. The *shalmei pesach* carries a more genuinely obligatory character, since the requirement to eat *al hasova* is itself grounded in obligation. The *shalmei nazir*, by contrast, originates entirely in a voluntary act — the person chose to undertake *nezirut*, and that initial choice was a *neder*.

### Neder vs. Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָתַנְיָא זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא נִידָּב וְנִידָּר — קָרֵב בְּבָמַת יָחִיד, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נִידָּב וְנִידָּר — אֵינוֹ קָרֵב בְּבָמַת יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye elaborates: **As isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **This is the principle: Any** offering **that is vowed or contributed** voluntarily, e.g., a burnt offering or a peace offering, **is sacrificed on a private altar. And** any offering **that is not vowed or contributed** voluntarily **may not be sacrificed on a private altar.**

This is not merely a narrow question of *parshanut* but opens onto a broader issue: are the *korbanot* of a *nazir* to be categorized as *neder* or as *chovah*? On one hand, once a person has accepted *nezirut*, the sacrifice is obligatory — there is no choice about whether to bring it. On the other hand, the entire structure of *nezirut* began with an act of will, with a *neder*, and perhaps that originating character defines the *korbanot* as belonging to the *neder* category throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן: הַמְּנָחוֹת וְהַנְּזִירוֹת קְרֵיבִין בְּבָמַת יָחִיד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר! סְמִי מִכָּאן ״נְזִירוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And we learned** in another *baraita*: **The meal offerings and the** offerings of **a nazirite are sacrificed on a private altar; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir.** It is clear from these *baraitot* that the peace offering of a nazirite belongs in the category of offerings that are vowed or contributed voluntarily. If so, there is no need for it to be included separately by the verse. Rav Dimi replied to Abaye: **Delete** the phrase: Offering of **a nazirite from here,** i.e., from the *baraita* that considers it an offering that is vowed or contributed voluntarily. Only the nazirite vow itself is classified as voluntary; once the vow has been uttered, the ensuing offerings are obligatory.

The *Gemara* brings a *baraita* to demonstrate that the *korbanot* of a *nazir* are indeed considered *neder u'nedavah*: *zeh haklal — kol she'neder u'nedavah kariv b'vamat yachid, v'she'eino neder u'nedavah eino kariv b'vamat yachid*. The rule governing what may be offered on a private altar (*bamas yachid*), in the era before the *Beit HaMikdash* was built, is that only free-will sacrifices may be brought there — obligatory sacrifices, including an individual's *chatat*, may not. The *Gemara* then cites the *Mishnah*: *hamenachos v'hanezirus krevim b'vamat yachid* — *minchot* and the *korbanot* of *nezirut* may be brought on a *bamas yachid*. This is the view of Rabbi Meir, and it shows that *nezirut* sacrifices fall into the *neder u'nedavah* category.

The *Gemara* then considers the other side: could there be an opinion that *nezirut* sacrifices are *not* considered *neder u'nedavah*? One response is simply *samei mikan nezirus* — cross out the word *nezirus* from the *Mishnah* — implying that perhaps others disagree with Rabbi Meir on this classification. But Abaye is not satisfied. He insists that the categorization of *nezirut* as *neder* is supported not merely by rabbinic sources but, as we are about to see, by a careful reading of *pesukim*.

### Private Altar Rule

The *baraita* establishes the general principle: free-will offerings may be brought on a *bamas yachid*, while obligatory ones may not. Crucially, the *Mishnah* cited by the *Gemara* places *nezirut* offerings firmly in the free-will column by ruling that they are valid on a private altar. This ruling by Rabbi Meir anchors the discussion. Even if we were to cross out the word *nezirus* and acknowledge that there may be other views, the structural logic remains: the question of whether *nezirut* constitutes a *neder* or a *chovah* is precisely what determines whether its offerings may be brought on a *bamas yachid*.

### Avshalom's Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה וַיֹּאמֶר אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵלְכָה נָּא וַאֲשַׁלֵּם אֶת נִדְרִי אֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתִּי לַה׳ בְּחֶבְרוֹן כִּי נֵדֶר נָדַר עַבְדְּךָ בִּגְשׁוּר בַּאֲרָם לֵאמֹר אִם יָשִׁיב יִשִׁיבֵנִי ה׳ לִירוּשָׁלָיִם וַעֲבַדְתִּי אֶת ה׳.
>
> **Translation:** To prove from *pesukim* that *nezirut* is treated as a *neder*, Abaye points to the story of Avshalom. The verse in Shmuel reads: **"And it came to pass at the end of forty years, that Absalom said to the king: Please let me go and pay my vow, which I have vowed to the Lord, in Hebron. For your servant vowed a vow** while I dwelled at Geshur in Aram, saying: If the Lord shall indeed bring me back to Jerusalem, then I will serve the Lord."** The rabbis understand Avshalom to have been a *nazir olam*, a *nazir* for life, whose practice was to bring his *korbanot* and shave his hair every thirty days. And the verse explicitly calls this a *neder*.

The *Gemara*'s initial reading of the verse is that Avshalom was heading to Chevron to *offer* his *korbanot* there — that is, to bring his *nazir* sacrifices on a *bamas yachid* in Chevron. This would directly prove that *nezirut* offerings were treated as *neder u'nedavah*, permissible on a private altar. At that time, the central *Mishkan* was in Givon, not in Chevron, so Avshalom's bringing his *korbanot* in Chevron could only be explained if those offerings were of the *neder* type.

The verse, however, includes an important detail that complicates this reading. As Michael pointed out from the full *pasuk*: *ki neder nadar avdecha b'shivti b'Geshur* — "the vow I made while I was in Geshur." Avshalom came from Geshur. So if the vow was made in Geshur, why does the verse say he is going to fulfill it *b'Chevron*? One answer offered is *lo ikar nidro* — the verse does not mean that Chevron is where he undertook the *nezirut*, but rather that *asher nadarti l'Hashem b'Chevron* means he is going to Chevron to fulfill it there. This maintains the original inference: he is bringing his *korbanot* on a private *bamah* in Chevron.

### Chevron Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אַעִיקַּר נִדְרוֹ אָמְרִי. עִיקַּר נִדְרוֹ בְּחֶבְרוֹן הֲוָה? וַהֲלֹא בִּגְשׁוּר הֲוָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** Absalom did not go to Hebron to sacrifice his nazirite offerings. Rather, Absalom actually **said that** he undertook **the principal vow** to be a nazirite when he was in Hebron. The Gemara asks: **Was his principal vow** to be a nazirite in fact uttered **in Hebron? But wasn't** the vow made when Absalom was **in Geshur?** After all, the verse states explicitly: "For your servant vowed a vow while I dwelled at Geshur."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן: לֹא הָלַךְ אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶלָּא לְהָבִיא כְּבָשִׂים מֵחֶבְרוֹן. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי תֵּימָא לְאַקְרוֹבֵי הוּא דְּאָזֵיל — שָׁבֵק יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וְאָזֵיל וּמַקְרֵיב בְּחֶבְרוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa said, and some say** that it was **Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan** who said: The verse means that **Absalom went to Hebron only** in order **to bring sheep** specifically from there. The Gemara adds that **this also stands to reason, as, if you say that** Absalom **went** to Hebron **to sacrifice** his offering, would he have **abandoned Jerusalem and gone to sacrifice in Hebron?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי? לְהָבִיא כְּבָשִׂים מֵחֶבְרוֹן? הַאי ״אֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתִּי לַה׳ בְּחֶבְרוֹן״, ״מֵחֶבְרוֹן״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan's answer: **But rather, what** is our explanation of the verse? That Absalom went **to bring sheep from Hebron?** If so, **this** verse that states: "Please let me go and pay my vow, **which I have vowed to the Lord, in Hebron"** (II Samuel 15:7), **should** instead **have** stated: Which I have vowed to the Lord **from Hebron.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם לְאַקְרוֹבֵי, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: אַמַּאי שָׁבֵק יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וּמַקְרֵיב בְּחֶבְרוֹן? תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ גִּבְעוֹן, דְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ הוּא! אֶלָּא, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוּתְּרוּ הַבָּמוֹת — כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּבָעֵי מַקְרֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains that **actually** Absalom did go to Hebron **to sacrifice** his peace offering as a nazirite. **And that** which is **difficult for you,** i.e., **why** Absalom **abandoned Jerusalem and sacrificed** his offering **in Hebron,** should not pose a difficulty for you; rather, it is the question of why Absalom did not sacrifice his offering in **Gibeon** that **should pose a difficulty for you, as** at that time the Tabernacle and the communal altar were in Gibeon, and **it was a sanctified place.** Why, then, did Absalom go to Hebron rather than Gibeon? **Rather, since the** private **altars were permitted,** he was permitted to **sacrifice wherever he wished,** and he chose to go to Hebron. There was no reason for him to choose to go to Gibeon rather than any private altar.

The *Gemara* offers a second interpretation: *lo l'Chevron ela l'havi kevasim miChevron* — he was not going to Chevron to bring the *korbanot*, but to obtain the animals there. Chevron was known for fine sheep, and he would fetch the lambs there and then bring his offerings in Givon. The *Gemara* notes that this reading is actually compelling on its own terms: *hachi nami mistabra* — it makes good sense. If he was going to bring on a *bamas yachid*, why travel to Chevron at all? He could bring them right there in Yerushalayim.

This raises an interesting implicit question: does the *Gemara* here assume that Yerushalayim already held some elevated status even before the *Beit HaMikdash* was built? Perhaps — it may simply mean that there is no point *schlepping* when you could bring it immediately where you are. But the implication may also be that Yerushalayim already occupied a higher position in the spiritual hierarchy, even in the era of *heter bamos*.

The *Gemara* then pushes back on the "going to fetch lambs" reading: *asher nadarti l'Hashem b'Chevron* should read *miChevron mibayei* — if the intention were that he was obtaining animals *from* Chevron, the verse should have said something like "from Chevron," not "in Chevron." So the *Gemara* returns to the plain reading: *ella l'olam l'akruvei hu d'azil* — he was truly going to Chevron to bring the offering. And the question of why not in Yerushalayim is answered: *keivan shehutra bamos, kol heichah d'ba'ei makri* — once private altars were generally permitted, there was no inherent preference of one location over another. In a period of *heter bamos*, every location was, in principle, equally valid. There was no hierarchy of place for offerings that were permissible on a *bamas yachid*. Whatever personal reason Avshalom had for preferring Chevron, there was no *halachic* reason to prefer Yerushalayim.

The *Gemara* thus concedes the point. The plain reading of the *pesukim* supports Abaye's position: Avshalom brought his *nazir korbanot* in Chevron, on a private *bamah*, and this is entirely consistent with the rule that *neder u'nedavah* type offerings may be brought on a *bamas yachid*. The *nezirut* sacrifice is accordingly classified as *neder*, not *chovah*, and Abaye's challenge to the *baraita* stands. No satisfactory answer emerges for why the word *ushalmeikem* should be used to derive the *nezirut korban* rather than the *shalmei pesach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה, לְמַאן? תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי נְהוֹרַאי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה״ שֶׁשָּׁאֲלוּ לָהֶם מֶלֶךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: אוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה שֶׁשָּׁאֲלוּ לָהֶם מֶלֶךְ, אוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה עֲשִׂירִית שֶׁל שְׁמוּאֵל הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states that Absalom submitted his request to his father "at the end of forty years." The Gemara asks: **Forty years, according to whose** counting, i.e., forty years from when? It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Nehorai says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua:** The verse is referring **to the end of forty years** from **when** the Jewish people **requested for themselves a king,** in the days of Samuel (see I Samuel, chapter 8). **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **that year when they requested for themselves a king, that year was the tenth** year of the leadership **of Samuel.**

The *Gemara* then turns briefly to the question of what is meant by *miketz arba'im shanah* — the forty-year marker in the Avshalom episode. It cannot refer to forty years of David's reign, since the rebellion occurred earlier. A *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Nehorai citing Rabbi Yehoshua explains that the forty years are counted from when Israel first asked for a king. Breaking it down: ten years of Shmuel's leadership on his own, one year of joint leadership of Shmuel and Shaul, two years of Shaul's sole reign, and then thirty-six or thirty-seven years of David's reign, arriving at forty from the start of the monarchic process. The variation in the count — whether thirty-six or thirty-seven — is the subject of considerable textual discussion in both Rashi and Tosafot, but the essential point is that the forty years mark from the very beginning of the institution of kingship, lending the episode of Avshalom's rebellion a wider resonance: barely a generation into the experiment of monarchy, and already its dangers were manifest.

## Chattat Rules

### Individual Chattat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חַטַּאת הַיָּחִיד שֶׁכִּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ — מֵתָה, וְשֶׁל צִבּוּר — אֵינָהּ מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai that there are five sin offerings that are unfit for sacrifice on the altar and have no remedy and are therefore left to die. They are: The offspring of a sin offering; the substitute for a sin offering; a sin offering whose owner died; a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another sin offering, when the original sin offering was lost or stolen and later recovered; and a sin offering whose year has passed. The mishna continues the discussion of the distinction between individual and communal offerings. **An individual sin offering whose owner achieved atonement** with another sin offering after it was lost is left to **die, but** in the case of **a communal** sin offering it **is not** left to **die.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to a *Mishnah* dealing with the category of *chataot* that must be left to die. The reason this subject arises here is directly connected to the overarching framework of *Temurah*: the opening *Mishnah* of the tractate enumerated the differences between *korbanot tzibur* and *korbanot yachid*, one of which was the capacity to make a *temurah*. The present discussion asks whether the *halacha* of *chataot* that must die also differs along these lines.

The *Mishnah* states: *chatat hayachid shekiperu ba'aleha — meisa*. If a person designated an animal as his individual *chatat*, it was lost, he brought a replacement to atone, and then the original was found — the original must be left to die. It has no further use; it cannot be offered.

### Communal Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תָּמוּת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה מָצִינוּ בִּוְלַד חַטָּאת, וּבִתְמוּרַת חַטָּאת, וּבְחַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ — דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בְּיָחִיד וְלֹא בְּצִבּוּר, אַף שֶׁכִּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ וְעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ — בְּיָחִיד דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים וְלֹא בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Even a communal sin offering **shall** be left to **die. Rabbi Shimon says: Just as we found with regard to the offspring of a sin offering, and with regard to the substitute for a sin offering, and with regard to a sin offering whose owner died,** that **these matters apply to an individual** sin offering **and not to a communal** sin offering, **so too,** in the cases of a sin offering **whose owner achieved atonement** with another sin offering, **and** a sin offering **whose** first **year has passed,** the **matters** are **stated with regard to an individual** sin offering, **and not with regard to a communal** sin offering.

The *Mishnah* then draws a contrast: *v'shel tzibur einah meisa* — a communal *chatat* in analogous circumstances does not die. It is instead left to graze (*tir'eh*) until it develops a blemish (*mum*), at which point it can be redeemed. The question of what is done with the proceeds is left somewhat open in the *Mishnah* itself. Rashi understands the animal to graze until redeemed, though the precise application of the redemption money is a matter worth exploring.

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *tamus* — the communal *chatat* must also die, just like the individual one. This is the *machloket* in the *Mishnah*.

### Five Categories

Rabbi Shimon, who appears to be the *tanna kamma*, explains the principled basis for distinguishing communal and individual *chataot*. He begins from the five categories of *chatat* that must be left to die: *(1) vlad chatat* — an offspring of a *chatat*; *(2) tmuras chatat* — an animal exchanged for a *chatat*; *(3) meisu ba'aleha* — the owners died; *(4) niskapru b'acher* — the owners were atoned for through another animal; *(5) avar shnasah* — it passed its first year.

Rabbi Shimon observes that three of these five categories simply never occur with a communal *chatat*. A *tzibur chatat* is always male, so there can never be a *vlad chatat*. A *tzibur* cannot make a *temurah* — as established in the framework of this entire tractate — so there can never be a *tmuras chatat*. And a *tzibur*, as a corporate entity, never truly dies, so there is never a case of *meisu ba'aleha*. Since three of the five categories are structurally inapplicable to a *tzibur*, Rabbi Shimon argues that the entire framework of the five categories was formulated with the *yachid* in mind. The remaining two categories — *niskapru b'acher* and *avar shnasah* — are also, therefore, said only of the *yachid*, not the *tzibur*.

Rashi explains Rabbi Shimon's logic as follows: the rule that five categories of *chatat* must die is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. Clearly that *halacha* was stated with reference to the *yachid*, because by the *tzibur* only two categories could ever occur, not five. A *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* articulated in the framework of five cannot simply be exported to a different context where the framework collapses, and you cannot apply the individual's *halachot* to the *tzibur* at all.

### Found Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַהוּ אוֹמֵר חַטָּאת ״יָבִיא״? מִנַּיִן לְמַקְדִּישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה קַיֶּימֶת, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת — מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה יָבִיא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״חַטָּאת״. יָכוֹל יָבִיא שְׁתֵּיהֶן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יְבִיאֶנָּה״ — אַחַת וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **What is** the verse teaching when it **says:** "And if **he bring** a lamb as his offering for **a sin offering,** he shall bring it a female without blemish" (Leviticus 4:32)? **From where** is it derived with regard **to one who separated his sin offering and it was lost, and he separated another in its place and the first one was found alive, and they both stand** fit to be sacrificed, **from where** is it derived **that he may bring whichever of them he wishes? The verse states: "A sin offering."** One **might** have thought that **he** must **bring both of them.** Therefore, the continuation of **the verse states: "He shall bring it."** This teaches that he must bring **one** of the animals **but not two** of them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹתָהּ שְׁנִיָּה מָה תְּהֵא עָלֶיהָ? אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא, תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תִּרְעֶה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: תָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if so, **that second** animal, **what will be with it? Rav Hamnuna says** that **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is **left to graze** until it becomes unfit, and then it is redeemed and the money is used to purchase a gift offering. **Rabbi Shimon says:** It **shall** be left to **die,** as it is an individual sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another sin offering.

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* that seems to generate a surprising complication. The verse *im keves yavi korbano lechatas* is expounded: if you sanctify an animal as a *chatat*, it is lost, you sanctify a replacement, and then the original is found still alive — from where do we know that you may bring whichever one you choose? The verse says *yavi* — you shall bring — emphasizing your agency, indicating you may select either. But perhaps you should bring both? The verse later says *yivienah* in the singular — just one, not two. The second animal then has the status of a *chatat shekiperu ba'aleha b'acheres* — and the *baraita* records a dispute about what becomes of it.

Rav Hamnuna reports: Rabbi Yehuda says *tireh* — let it graze; Rabbi Shimon says *tamus* — let it die. This is deeply puzzling. In the *Mishnah*, both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon seemed to agree that an individual *chatat shekiperu ba'aleha* must die, differing only about the communal case. Yet here Rabbi Yehuda says the individual *chatat* should graze, and after reversing the names the *Gemara* finds Rabbi Shimon in the same position. How can either of them contradict what the *Mishnah* implies?

### Timing Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בַּאֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה, כָּאן — בַּאֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains: **Actually, do not reverse** the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon in the *baraita*, **and it** is **not difficult. Here,** where Rabbi Yehuda says in the *baraita* that the second animal must be left to graze, it is referring **to** an animal **lost at the time of the separation** of a substitute to take its place, but the original animal was found before the second was sacrificed. Since the animal was found before the other was sacrificed, it is left to graze. **There,** in the mishna where Rabbi Yehuda says that the animal must be left to die, it is referring **to** an animal that was still **lost at the time of atonement,** i.e., when the substitute was sacrificed.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty with a crucial distinction: *kan ba'avudah bish'as hafrashah, kan ba'avudah bish'as kaparah*. The two sources are addressing different situations based on *when* the original animal was found. The *Mishnah*'s ruling — that the *chatat* must die — applies when the original was found *after* the replacement had already been offered. In that case, atonement has been fully achieved through the second animal, and the first is entirely displaced; it is *chatat shekiperu ba'aleha*, and it must die. The *baraita*'s ruling, by contrast, applies when the original was found *before* the replacement was offered. In that situation, both animals are simultaneously available candidates. When the owner then proceeds with the replacement and sets aside the original, that original does not necessarily have to die — there is a difference in its status because it was never fully superseded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Both **this** *baraita* **and that** mishna are referring to an animal that was lost only **at the time of the separation** of the second animal, **and** yet it is **not difficult. Here** in the mishna, **Rabbi Yehuda** rules that the animal must be left to die **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi in a mishna (22b), as he maintains that even if the animal was lost only when the second animal was separated and was found by the time it was sacrificed, it must still be left to die. **There** in the *baraita*, **Rabbi Yehuda** says that the animal is left to graze **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis** in that same mishna. The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and rule that the animal is left to die only if it was not yet found when the replacement animal was sacrificed.

The logical asymmetry here is difficult to articulate neatly, since the case *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — but the general structure can be seen: when both animals are simultaneously present and a conscious choice is made, the rejected one retains some vestigial standing that it lacks when it was simply displaced after the fact.

An alternative resolution proposed by the *Gemara* is that even if both texts deal with animals found *bish'as hafrashah*, they can be reconciled by noting that Rabbi Yehuda himself holds different positions depending on whose underlying framework he is following. When Rabbi Yehuda in the *Mishnah* says *tamus*, that is according to the opinion of Rabbi — who rules that even if the original is found before the replacement is brought, it must die. When Rabbi Yehuda says *tireh* in the *baraita*, that is according to the *Rabbanan*, who rule that finding it before the replacement has been offered saves it from the status of mandatory death. The underlying dispute between Rabbi and the *Rabbanan* over this precise timing question thus accounts for the apparent inconsistency in Rabbi Yehuda's rulings across the two texts.

## Ein Tzibur Meisa

### Rav Papa Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַטַּאת צִיבּוּר שֶׁכִּפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים מֵתָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that Rabbi Yehuda says: Even a communal sin offering shall be left to die if the community has already achieved atonement through another animal. The Gemara asks: **But is there one who said** that **a communal sin offering whose owners achieved atonement** with another sin offering after it was lost is left to **die?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: ״וְהַבָּאִים מֵהַשְּׁבִי בְנֵי הַגּוֹלָה הִקְרִיבוּ עוֹלוֹת... פָּרִים בְּנֵי בָקָר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר... אֵילִים תִּשְׁעִים וְתִשְׁעָה, כְּבָשִׂים שִׁבְעִים וְשִׁבְעָה, שְׂעִירֵי חַטָּאת שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר — הַכֹּל עוֹלָה לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Similarly, Rabbi Yosei said:** It is stated with regard to those who returned from Babylonia in the days of Ezra: **"The children of the captivity that came out of exile sacrificed burnt offerings** to the God of Israel, **twelve bulls** for all Israel, **ninety-six rams, seventy-seven lambs, twelve goats for a sin offering; all this was a burnt offering unto the Lord"** (Ezra 8:35).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַטָּאת מִי קָרְבָה עוֹלָה? אָמַר רָבָא: כִּי עוֹלָה, מָה עוֹלָה אֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת — אַף חַטָּאת אֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: עַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הֱבִיאוּם, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁעָשׂוּ בִּימֵי צִדְקִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first analyzes this verse: **But is** it possible for **a sin offering** to be **sacrificed** as **a burnt offering? Rava said:** The verse means that it was all performed in the manner **of a burnt offering: Just as a burnt offering may not be eaten, so too,** that **sin offering** was **not eaten. As Rabbi Yosei would say: They brought these** twelve sin offerings **for** the sin of **idol worship; and Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** They were brought **for the** sin of **idol worship they committed in the days of** King **Zedekiah.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין, לְמַאן דְּאִית לֵיהּ חַטַּאת צִיבּוּר שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ — מֵתָה, אִית לֵיהּ נָמֵי חַטַּאת צִיבּוּר שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ — מֵתָה, וְהָא הָכָא דְּאִיכָּא דְּמֵתוּ בַּעֲלֵיהּ, וְקָא קָרְבָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty concerning which it cited this verse: It might **enter our mind** to think that **according to the one who holds** that **a communal sin offering whose owners achieved atonement** with another sin offering is left to **die,** he **also holds** that **a communal sin offering whose owners died** is left to **die. But here,** with regard to the offerings brought by the returning exiles, **this is** a case **of** a communal sin offering **whose owners died,** as the sin was committed in the time of Zedekiah, in the First Temple period, whereas the offerings were brought several generations later by those returning to rebuild the Second Temple. **And** yet **they were sacrificed.** This proves that a communal sin offering whose owners achieved atonement with another sin offering is not left to die.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַטַּאת צִיבּוּר שֶׁכִּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ מֵתָה, חַטַּאת צִיבּוּר שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ אֵינָהּ מֵתָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַצִּבּוּר מֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** in response: **Even according to the one who said** that **a communal sin offering whose owners achieved atonement** with another sin offering is left to **die,** he agrees that **a communal sin offering whose owners died is not** left to **die.** This is **because a community does not die.**

With the individual *chatat* framework now clarified, the *Gemara* turns to the communal case. A *baraita* cited by Rabbi Yossi introduces the verse from Ezra describing the return of the exiles to Eretz Yisrael: *verabim mehakohanim vehaleviyim verashei ha'avos hazkenim*... They brought burnt offerings: *parim shenem asar, eilim tish'im v'shisha, kevasim shiv'im v'shivah, tzfirei chatat shenem asar*. Twelve oxen and twelve he-goats — the oxen as *olot*, the goats as *chatat* — followed by the statement *hakol olah l'Hashem*, which Rava reads not as a general expression of offering but in its technical sense: all of it was burnt as an *olah*. How could the *chatat* goats be burnt as an *olah*? Rava explains: *mah olah einah nechelet, af chatat einah nechelet* — just as an *olah* is not eaten, so too this *chatat* was not eaten. These were *chataot hanisrafot*, burnt outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, of the type associated with *par helam davar shel tzibur* — the communal sin-offering brought when *Beit Din* ruled incorrectly and the community transgressed on the basis of that ruling.

The twelve oxen and twelve he-goats correspond to the twelve tribes. The sin for which they were atoning was *avodah zarah* — the *pasuk* in *Bamidbar* in *Parshas Shelach*, *v'im me'einei ha'eidah ne'esta lishgagah*, connecting to a *shgagah* committed by the entire community in matters that represent *kol mitzvos Hashem*, understood by the *Gemara* to refer to *avodah zarah*. Specifically, as Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel, this was for the *avodah zarah* committed in the time of Tzidkiyahu, in the final period of the first *Beit HaMikdash*.

The *Gemara* now raises its central question: if one were to hold that a *chatat tzibur shekiperu ba'aleha* must die — the position of Rabbi Yehuda in the *Mishnah* — then one would presumably also hold that a *chatat tzibur shemeisu ba'aleha* must die. But here, the original sinners — those who worshipped *avodah zarah* in the time of Tzidkiyahu — were dead by the time of Ezra's return, some seventy years later. Yet they brought the *chatat*. This would seem to require us to say that even when the *ba'alim* of the *tzibur* have died, the *chatat* does not die with them. Tosafot asks a sharp question: the very structure of the *Mishnah* takes for granted that *meisu ba'aleha* never applies to a *tzibur*, since a *tzibur* never dies. What was the *havah amina* that it might? Tosafot answers: the *Mishnah*'s assumption may only have been that *meisu ba'aleha* does not apply so long as *some* of the original members remain. The question posed here is whether even when *all* the original members have been replaced — as may have been the case by Ezra's time — the *tzibur* is still considered the same entity.

Rav Papa rules: *afilu man de'amar chatat tzibur shekiperu ba'aleha meisah* — even one who holds that a communal *chatat* whose *ba'alim* were atoned elsewhere must die — *chatat tzibur shemeisu ba'aleha einah meisah* — that same authority would never say the communal *chatat* dies when its *ba'alim* die.

### Corporate Entity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַב פָּפָּא הָא? אִי נֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״תַּחַת אֲבוֹתֶיךָ יִהְיוּ בָנֶיךָ״, אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ יָחִיד נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does Rav Pappa derive this** statement? **If we say** it is **because it is written: "Your sons shall be instead of your fathers"** (Psalms 45:17), i.e., it is considered as though the fathers are alive, **if so,** then this should apply **even** to **an individual as well.** In other words, sons should be able to sacrifice the sin offerings of their late fathers.

The reason is: *ein tzibur meisa*. A community does not die. The *Gemara* notes that the better textual reading is *ein tzibur meisa* — treating the community as a single entity — rather than *ein tzibur meisin* — which would treat it as a collection of individuals. The conceptual point is precisely this: the *tzibur* is not a set of specific persons. It is a corporate entity, and that entity persists regardless of whether its membership has changed. כלל ישראל remains כלל ישראל across generations, even if not a single original member survives.

### Rosh Chodesh Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, שֶׁאֵין הַצִּיבּוּר מֵתִים, מִשְּׂעִירֵי רְגָלִים וְרָאשֵׁי חֳדָשִׁים, דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: אַיְיתִינְהוּ מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. וְדִלְמָא מִתוּ מָרַיְיהוּ דְּהָנֵי זוּזֵי? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין הַצִּבּוּר מֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is** Rav Pappa's **reasoning** for his statement **that a community does not die.** It is derived **from** the *halakha* of **the goats** sacrificed on **pilgrimage Festivals and** on **New Moons, as the Merciful One states: Bring them from the** funds of the **collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber,** where they kept the half-shekels donated every year in the month of Adar, with which communal offerings were purchased. The Gemara explains: **But perhaps the owners of these coins** that were used to purchase these offerings **have died** in the meantime between the month of Adar and when the offerings are sacrificed throughout the year. If so, how can a sin offering be brought on behalf of some of its owners who have already died? **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** this *halakha* that **a community does not die?**

The *Gemara* proposes a stronger basis: the ongoing practice of bringing *chataot* for *Rosh Chodesh* and the *musafim* from *trumat halishka*, the funds collected in *Nissan* from all of Israel. By the time *Rosh Chodesh Iyar* arrives — or *Pesach*, or any subsequent *musaf* — some contributors will have died. Yet the *chatat* of the *musaf* is brought without any concern that a portion of its ownership has lapsed. *Ve'amar Rachmanah mitrumat halishka* — the Torah says to bring it from *trumat halishka* — and we do not say that those whose coins funded the animal have died and therefore the *korban* is a *chatat shemeisu ba'aleha*. This demonstrates the principle of *ein tzibur meis* in a regular, recurring context.

The limitation of this proof is acknowledged: it demonstrates only that when *some* contributors have died, the *tzibur* is still considered intact. It does not prove that the principle holds when the *majority* — let alone *all* — of the original members have died.

### Ezra's Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי אַקְרוּבִינְהוּ לְהָנֵי חַטָּאוֹת — אַחַיֵּי אַקְרֻבִינְהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרַבִּים מֵהַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם וְרָאשֵׁי הָאָבוֹת הַזְּקֵנִים אֲשֶׁר רָאוּ אֶת הַבַּיִת הָרִאשׁוֹן בְּיׇסְדוֹ זֶה הַבַּיִת... בּוֹכִים בְּקוֹל גָּדוֹל וְרַבִּים בִּתְרוּעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead a different answer in response to the earlier difficulty: The sin offerings for idolatry brought by the returning exiles were not in fact sacrificed for people who had died. Rather, **when they sacrificed these sin offerings** for the idolatry committed in the time of Zedekiah, **they sacrificed them for the living,** i.e., for those survivors who had worshipped idols in the time of Zedekiah and were still alive many decades later and had returned to rebuild the Second Temple. **As it is written: "But many of the priests, Levites, and heads of fathers' houses, the old men that had seen the first house standing on its foundation, wept with a loud voice when this house** was before their eyes; **and many shouted aloud** for joy" (Ezra 3:12).

The *Gemara* therefore returns to the Ezra episode in an attempt to demonstrate the principle in a more extreme case. The verse reads: *verabim mehakohanim vehaleviyim verashei ha'avos hazkenim asher ra'u et habayit harishon beyasdo zeh habayit bochim bekol gadol verabim bitru'ah*. There were many priests, Levites, and elders who had seen the *first* Beit HaMikdash with their own eyes who were now witnessing the founding of the second. They wept bitterly at the contrast. This shows that there *were* survivors from before the *churban* — people alive at the time of Tzidkiyahu's *avodah zarah* who were still present at Ezra's return. Thus, the *Gemara* now considers whether the whole case of Ezra's *korbanot* might simply reflect continuity through surviving members rather than proving anything about a fully replaced community.

### Crying vs. Shofar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא הָנָךְ מִיעוּטָא? לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דִּכְתִיב ״וְלֹא הִכִּירוּ הָעָם בַּתְּרוּעָה וְשִׂמְחָה לְקוֹל בְּכִי הָעָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But perhaps those** who remained and remembered the First Temple were **the minority,** in which case they should have each brought individual sin offerings, rather than a communal sin offering. The fact that they brought communal sacrifices indicates that the sin offering was not brought only on behalf of those few who remained. The Gemara explains: **You cannot say** that they were the minority, **as it is written** in the following verse: **"So that the people could not discern the noise of the shout of joy from the noise of the weeping of the people;** for the people shouted with a loud shout, and the noise was heard afar off" (Ezra 3:13). This verse shows that the people who cried because they remembered the First Temple were not a small minority.

The verse concludes: *ve'ein ha'am makirim kol tru'at hasimcha lekol bechi ha'am* — the people could not distinguish the sound of the joyful *shofar* blasts from the sound of the weeping, because the weeping was so loud it overwhelmed the rejoicing. The *Gemara* reads this as evidence that the weepers — those who had witnessed the first *Beit HaMikdash* — were a *majority*, since only a majority could drown out the joy of those who had no such memory.

This is a striking conclusion given the seventy-year gap. The *Gemara*, however, concedes that this inference is difficult to sustain. As we know from other episodes — the spies, for example — one does not need a numerical majority to overwhelm a crowd emotionally. A small number of older eyewitnesses could easily reduce an entire assembled community to tears. The *Gemara* ultimately cannot resolve the difficulty fully: it cannot definitively prove the principle of *ein tzibur meis* for cases where the majority, let alone the entirety, of the original members has been replaced.

Nevertheless, the *Gemara* remains committed to the principle. And practically speaking, the narrow case that *was* proven — from *Rosh Chodesh* and *musaf* — is sufficient for virtually all real applications. When dealing with a *chatat* animal still alive, the *tzibur* whose *ba'alim* have partially turned over is the standard case, and for that the *Rosh Chodesh* proof holds.

## Horaat Sha'ah

### Intentional Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי מַקְרְבִי לְהוּ? וַהֲרֵי מְזִידִין הֲווֹ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But how could they sacrifice** sin offerings for the sin of idolatry? After all, **they were intentional** idol worshippers, and a sin offering is brought only by one who sins unwittingly. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in response: **It was a provisional edict** issued in exigent circumstances, according to which they were permitted to bring sin offerings even for intentional sins.

The *Gemara* then presses an underlying difficulty in the Ezra narrative. The *avodah zarah* committed in the time of Tzidkiyahu was not *b'shogeg* — it was not accidental. It was willful, deliberate transgression. Normally a *chatat* can only be brought for an unintentional sin. How then could the returning exiles bring a *chatat* for intentional *avodah zarah*?

### Special Exception

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *horaat sha'ah* — it was a temporary exception, a one-time dispensation that does not reflect the ordinary *halacha*.

### Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי — בִּשְׁלָמָא פָּרִים וּשְׂעִירִים כְּנֶגֶד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים, אֶלָּא כְּבָשִׂים כְּנֶגֶד מִי? אֶלָּא הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that **this also stands to reason, as, if you do not say so,** one can object as follows: **Granted,** they sacrificed twelve **bulls and goats,** since each tribe must bring a communal sin offering, as stated in the Torah (Numbers, chapter 15), and these offerings **correspond to** the **twelve tribes. But to what** do **the** ninety-six **sheep correspond? Rather,** it must be that **it was a provisional edict.**

The purpose of this *horaat sha'ah* deserves attention. The act of bringing *korbanot* upon returning from exile was not merely ritualistic. It was a symbolic break from the past, a public acknowledgment of the sins of the first *Beit HaMikdash* era and a declaration of intent: we take responsibility, and things will be different now. This is precisely what the prophets had called for at the close of the first *Beit HaMikdash* period, when *avodah zarah* was among the gravest failures of the generation of Tzidkiyahu.

The *Gemara* confirms this reading with the observation that the twelve oxen and twelve goats, corresponding to the twelve *shevatim*, structured the *korban* as one of *par helam davar shel tzibur* — brought on behalf of the entire community. And the extra animals — ninety-six rams and seventy-seven sheep — whose precise correspondence is not explained, were also part of this *horaat sha'ah*. The *Gemara* simply notes *horaat sha'ah* and does not try to account for every detail.

There is an additional conceptual layer here. By accepting the designation of *horaat sha'ah*, the *Gemara* technically undermines some of the earlier proof for *ein tzibur meis*. If the entire act was a special exception, one cannot draw standard *halachic* inferences from it. The *Gemara* does not press this point, and one might argue that even within a *horaat sha'ah*, the very act of taking communal responsibility for past sins implies a strong sense of continuity. If the community could simply say "that was a different generation, we bear no connection," there would be no basis for the statement of responsibility at all. The act of *horaat sha'ah* itself is premised on the idea that this community is continuous with the one that sinned.

## Torah Transmission

### Complete Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: מִשֶּׁמֵּת יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה וְיוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹחָנָן אִישׁ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, בָּטְלוּ הָאֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת. אִישׁ שֶׁהַכֹּל בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § Earlier the Gemara mentioned the *halakha* of a sin offering whose owner died, which was one of the *halakhot* forgotten during the mourning period for Moses (see 16a). On this topic the Gemara says that **we learned** in a mishna **there** (*Sota* 47a): **From** the time **when Yosef ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida and Yosef ben Yoḥanan of Jerusalem died, the clusters [*eshkolot*] ceased,** i.e., they were the last of the clusters. The Gemara asks: What is the meaning of *eshkolot*? It means **a man who contains all [*ish shehakol bo*],** i.e., both Torah and mitzvot.

The *Gemara* concludes the daf with a *Mishnah* from Sotah cited in the context of lamenting the greatness that has been lost. The *Mishnah* states: *mishemet Yosef ben Yoezer ish Tzereda v'Yosef ben Yochanan ish Yerushalayim, batlu ha'eshkolot* — when Yosef ben Yoezer and Yosef ben Yochanan died, the *eshkolot* ceased. These figures lived in the earliest pre-*Tannaitic* period, even before the *Zugot*. What is an *eshkol*? The *Gemara* glosses it as *ish shehakol bo* — a person in whom everything is found.

Rashi's definition is worth pausing over. The three qualities Rashi identifies are: *Torah, yirat chet, u'gemilut chasadim* — Torah knowledge, a deep fear of sin, and acts of lovingkindness. The precision of this triad is instructive. Intellectual mastery of Torah is necessary but not sufficient; one can possess vast *talmudic chachmah* without the accompanying *yirat chet* — that genuine religiosity, humility, and sense of standing before God that keeps knowledge from becoming merely cerebral. Nor is the inner dimension alone enough; *gemilut chasadim* insists on responsibility toward other people. A complete person integrates all three.

### Lost Halachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל אֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת שֶׁעָמְדוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִימוֹת מֹשֶׁה עַד שֶׁמֵּת יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר — הָיוּ לְמֵדִין תּוֹרָה כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — לֹא הָיוּ לְמֵדִין תּוֹרָה כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: All** the **clusters who stood** at the head **of the Jewish people, from the days of Moses until Yosef ben Yo'ezer died, would study Torah** in the manner **of Moses, our teacher. From that** point **forward they would not study Torah** in the manner **of Moses, our teacher.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים הֲלָכוֹת נִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ בִּימֵי אֶבְלוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה! דְּאִישְׁתְּכֻח לְהוּ — אִישְׁתְּכֻח, וְדִגְמִירָן לְהוּ — הֲווֹ גְּמִירִי כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel said: Three thousand *halakhot* were forgotten during the days of mourning for Moses.** This suggests that the Sages who came immediately after Moses did not study Torah in the same manner as Moses. The Gemara answers: Those *halakhot* **that they forgot, were forgotten, but** with regard to those *halakhot* **that they studied, they would** continue to **study** in the manner **of Moses, our teacher.**

The *Gemara* then introduces an apparent challenge to the notion that the period from Moshe until Yosef ben Yoezer was one of perfect Torah transmission. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *shloshet alafim halachot nishtakchu b'yimei evlo shel Moshe* — three thousand *halachot* were forgotten during the mourning period following Moshe's death. If so, the generation immediately after Moshe was already imperfect. How can we describe that period as one of learning Torah *k'Moshe Rabbeinu*?

### Mesorah Standard

The *Gemara* resolves the question with a crucial distinction: *ahani d'ishtakach ishtakach* — those that were forgotten were forgotten; *u'd'gamir havu g'miri k'Moshe Rabbeinu* — but those that were retained and transmitted were preserved with complete faithfulness, exactly as Moshe himself had received and taught them. The standard being invoked here is not omniscience but fidelity of transmission. To learn Torah *k'Moshe Rabbeinu* means to be a perfect conduit of the *mesorah* — to pass on what one received without alteration, without substitution, without the intrusion of one's own reconstructions.

This points to a distinction that the *Gemara* will develop further in subsequent discussion: the difference between *mesorah* — perfect preservation of received tradition — and the use of one's own intellect to recover or reconstruct what may have been lost. The great figures from Moshe until Yosef ben Yoezer excelled at the former. What changed after Yosef ben Yoezer was not that *halachot* were necessarily forgotten or that Torah learning collapsed, but that the chain of perfectly faithful transmission was no longer maintained at the same standard. The discussion of this distinction will continue on the following *daf*.
