# Berakhot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Haseiba & Group Blessings

### Opening Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ. אַחַר הַמָּזוֹן — אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם. וְהוּא אוֹמֵר עַל הַמּוּגְמָר, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מְבִיאִין אֶת הַמּוּגְמָר אֶלָּא לְאַחַר סְעוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally: If **wine came before them during the meal, each and every** diner **recites a blessing** over the wine **for himself.** If the wine came **after the meal, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all. And he,** who recited the blessing over the wine, also **says** the blessing **over the incense** [***mugmar***], **although they only bring the incense** to the diners **after the meal.**

We begin on daf 43, picking up from the bottom of 42b. The *mishnah* had established that only when people are reclining together to eat bread may one person recite *hamotzi* on behalf of the entire group. The difficulty was a *braita* that appeared to permit this even when the group merely sits, without reclining. The *gemara* resolved the tension by introducing the concept of *kove'ia makom*: if the group explicitly designated a place by saying "let us go eat bread over there," that act of joint designation functions as an equivalent to *haseiba* and allows one person to bless for the group.

### Mishnah-Braita Tension

The underlying question is whether *haseiba* is purely a formal social convention — a matter of choreography defining the character of the meal — or whether it also reflects something about the subjective inner intention of each participant. The *mishnah* requires *haseiba*; the *braita* permits even sitting. This is the tension the *gemara* must resolve.

### Resolution: Designated Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כְּגוֹן דְּאָמְרִי ״נֵיזִיל וְנֵיכוֹל לַחְמָא בְּדוּךְ פְּלָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** With regard to those walking along the road, it was **in a case where they said: Let us go and eat in such-and-such a place.**

The *gemara*'s resolution is that when the group designates a shared location for eating, this designation creates the equivalent of *haseiba*. Even without physical reclining, the verbal act of "let us go eat bread in that place" constitutes the group as a group. In one reading, the social construct of *haseiba* itself — independent of any individual's inner state — accomplishes this. In another reading, it is precisely the *kavanah* expressed through that declaration that reframes the gathering: the participants are understood to have intended to constitute themselves as a collective, and this is what allows a single representative to speak for all.

### Group vs Individual

This points to a deeper principle articulated explicitly by the *Ritva*: *birchat ha-nehenin* and *birkat ha-mazon* are fundamentally different from other *berachot* in which one person can discharge another's obligation. Since these blessings concern a highly personal, subjective experience of pleasure and benefit, each individual would ordinarily be required to recite his own *berachah*. The exception arises only when the eating is understood not as a collection of individual experiences but as the shared experience of a unified group. In that case, the leader of the group does not speak as a private individual on behalf of other private individuals; he speaks as the representative of the collective. *Haseiba*, or its equivalent, is what accomplishes this reframing — moving the focus from "I am personally experiencing this benefit" to "we, as a group, are having this experience."

## Death of Rav & Resolution

### Rav's Death & Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב, אֲזוּל תַּלְמִידֵיהּ בָּתְרֵיהּ. כִּי הָדְרִי, אָמְרִי: נֵיזִיל וְנֵיכוּל לַחְמָא אַנְּהַר דָּנָק. בָּתַר דְּכָרְכִי, יָתְבִי וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״הֵסַבּוּ״ דַּוְקָא תְּנַן, אֲבָל יָשְׁבוּ לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרִי ״נֵיזִיל וְנֵיכוֹל רִיפְתָּא בְּדוּכְתָּא פְּלָנִיתָא״ — כִּי הֵסַבּוּ דָּמֵי? לָא הֲוָה בִּידַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, the Gemara relates: **When Rav died, his students went after his** casket to the city where he was to be buried. **When they returned, they said: Let us go and eat bread on** the banks of **the Dannak River. After they ate, they sat, and raised a dilemma: Did we learn** in the mishna **specifically** if **they reclined,** it is considered a joint meal; **however,** if they merely **sat** together, **no,** it is not considered a joint meal? **Or perhaps, since they said: Let us go and eat in such-and-such a place,** it is considered **as if they reclined? It was not within their capability** to resolve this dilemma.

The *gemara* presents a vivid narrative. After *Rav* died, his students followed the funeral bier to bury him. On their way back, they said: "Let us go eat bread at the bank of the river Danak." After they ate, they sat and a question arose among them: does the *mishnah*'s requirement of *haseiba* apply strictly — meaning that since they were sitting on a riverbank and not actually reclining on Roman dining couches, one person cannot bless for the group — or, since they had declared "let us go eat bread in that place," is that equivalent to *haseiba* and sufficient to allow one to bless for all?

They did not know the answer. The *gemara* then records that Rav Adda bar Abba rose and turned his torn garment around to its reverse side — since the students had already rent their garments for their teacher — and rent it again. He declared: "*Rav* has died, and we have not even finished learning from him how to make blessings over food." The act is extraordinary: he rent his garment twice in expression of a grief that was not merely about personal loss, but about the felt inadequacy of what had been transmitted.

### Anonymous Elder's Help

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָם רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אַהְדַּר קִרְעֵיהּ לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ, וּקְרַע קְרִיעָה אַחֲרִינָא. אֲמַר: נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב, וּבִרְכַּת מְזוֹנָא לָא גָּמְרִינַן. עַד דַּאֲתָא הָהוּא סָבָא, רְמָא לְהוּ מַתְנִיתִין אַבָּרַיְיתָא, וְשַׁנִּי לְהוּ: כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרִי ״נֵיזִיל וְנֵיכוֹל לַחְמָא בְּדוּךְ פְּלָן״ — כְּהֵסַבּוּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** and **reversed** his cloak, so that **his tear** which he had rent in mourning of Rav was **behind him, and** in mourning, **he rent another tear** in his garment. **He said: Rav is dead, and we have not** yet **learned** the *halakhot* of the **Grace after Meals. Until, this elder came and raised a contradiction** from **the mishna to the *baraita*,** as cited above, **and he resolved it for them: Since they said: Let us go and eat in such-and-such a place,** it is considered **as if they reclined.**

Then a certain elder — *hahu saba* — arrived and presented the contradiction between the *mishnah* and the *braita* to them, and resolved it: once a group declares "let us go eat bread in that place," that designation is like *haseiba*, and one person may bless for all. This is precisely the resolution we have already seen at the beginning of the discussion. The students' question was thereby answered — not by their master, who was gone, but by an anonymous figure.

It bears noting that some authorities understand every instance of the phrase *hahu saba* in the *gemara* as a reference to *Eliyahu HaNavi*, and reading it that way would suggest that Elijah appeared specifically because the students were so grief-stricken, to offer them some comfort by resolving their difficulty.

### Narrative Significance

The narrative arc carries a subtle counter-message. The profound sense of loss — the tearing of garments twice over, the feeling that essential learning had been lost with *Rav* — is real and not minimized. And yet an anonymous figure, or perhaps wisdom arising from within the community itself, is capable of resolving a genuine *halachic* question. One need not have it all transmitted from one's *rebbe*; careful attention to the *mishnah* and the *braita*, and the willingness to think through their tension, can also yield the answer. The loss of a master is irreplaceable, but the tradition is not solely dependent on any single chain of transmission.

## Wine & Haseiba Debate

### Rav's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵסַבּוּ, אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פַּת דְּבָעֵי הֲסִבָּה, אֲבָל יַיִן לָא בָּעֵי הֲסִבָּה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן נָמֵי בָּעֵי הֲסִבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **If they were reclined, one recites a blessing** on behalf of them all. **Rav said: This** *halakha* **was only taught** with regard to **bread that** it **requires reclining** to enable one to recite a blessing on behalf of them all. **However, wine does not require reclining. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even wine requires reclining as well.**

The *gemara* now continues: *heiseibu echad mevarech* — when they reclined, one person blesses for all. This is the rule we have been discussing. *Rav* says: this applies only to bread (*pas*), which requires *haseiba*. For wine, *haseiba* is not required; one person may recite *borei pri ha-gafen* for the group even without formal reclining. The basis for this appears to be the end of the *mishnah* itself, which speaks of the wine served after the meal — the post-meal drinking — and states that one person blesses for the group, without mentioning *haseiba* at all. The implication is that for wine, the social conventions of group drinking were themselves sufficient to constitute a collective experience, without the need for the structured Roman dining posture.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees: even wine requires *haseiba*. Without *haseiba*, one person cannot bless for the group even over wine.

### Two Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פַּת — דְּמַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה. אֲבָל יַיִן — לָא מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן נָמֵי מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rav said:** The mishna **only taught that reclining is effective** and enables one to recite a blessing on behalf of them all, with regard to a group eating **bread. However,** with regard to a group drinking **wine, reclining is ineffective** and each individual must recite a blessing for himself. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** with regard to **wine, reclining is effective.**

The *gemara* then records a second version of *Rav*'s position, which carries a subtly different nuance. In the first version, *Rav* said that wine does not *require* (*ba'ei*) *haseiba* — meaning it functions without it. In the second version, *Rav* said that *haseiba* does not *help* (*mahani*) for wine — meaning it is never effective for wine, even when present. These are importantly different claims. In the first version, wine can constitute a group experience through social convention alone, and *haseiba* is simply unnecessary. In the second version, the institution of *haseiba* is so intrinsically connected to the bread meal — to the formal Roman dining posture — that it has no conceptual weight when applied to drinking wine independently. Even if people recline while drinking, that does not rise to the level of the socially recognized institution of *haseiba*, which is a feature of the formal meal around bread.

## Tosefta: Order of Seudah

### Pre-Meal Customs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כֵּיצַד סֵדֶר הֲסִבָּה? — אוֹרְחִין נִכְנָסִין וְיוֹשְׁבִין עַל גַּבֵּי סַפְסָלִין וְעַל גַּבֵּי קָתֶדְרָאוֹת עַד שֶׁיִּכָּנְסוּ כּוּלָּם. הֵבִיאוּ לָהֶם מַיִם — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נוֹטֵל יָדוֹ אַחַת. בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ. עָלוּ וְהֵסַבּוּ וּבָא לָהֶם מַיִם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נָטַל יָדוֹ אַחַת, חוֹזֵר וְנוֹטֵל שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו. בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד בֵּרַךְ לְעַצְמוֹ, אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on a *Tosefta*: **What is the order of reclining** at a meal? The **guests enter and sit upon benches and chairs [*katedraot*] until all are assembled.** Afterward, **they brought them water and each and every one washes one hand** in which to hold the cup of wine. When **wine came before them** prior to the meal, **each and every one recites a blessing** over the wine **for himself.** Then, when **they entered and reclined** on the divans for the meal itself, **and water came before them, despite** the fact **that each and every one** already **washed one hand, they wash both hands again** prior to the meal, so that they will be able to eat with both hands. If **wine came before them** during the meal, **despite** the fact **that each and every one** already **recited a blessing for himself, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all.**

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *Tosefta* describing the full order of *haseiba* — and this *Tosefta* turns out to be a remarkable document that is as much descriptive of ancient Greco-Roman dining customs as it is prescriptive *halacha*.

The *Tosefta* begins: guests enter and sit on benches and chairs — *safsal* and *katedraot*. They would not immediately recline on couches. They waited until everyone had gathered. Then water was brought, and each person washed one hand. This was a customary practice, perhaps because they would be holding the wine cup in that hand. Then wine was brought before the meal began, and each person made *borei pri ha-gafen* for himself individually, because at this pre-meal stage the group had not yet constituted itself as a collective through reclining.

### Meal Begins

Then they went up into the main dining hall. Once there, even though each person had already washed one hand, each one again washed both hands — this time fulfilling the *halachic* requirement of *netilat yadayim* before bread. The one-hand washing before was a social practice; this two-hand washing is the obligatory *halachic* act before eating bread. Interestingly, the *Tosefta* that begins by asking "what is the order of *haseiba*?" proceeds directly to the wine at this point, skipping over the actual moment of reclining and eating bread. Wine is then served during the meal. Although each person had made his own blessing on the pre-meal wine, now, the *Tosefta* says, one person makes *borei pri ha-gafen* for the entire group.

### Post-Meal Wine

This naturally raises the question of how one is making yet another *borei pri ha-gafen* during the meal if the pre-meal *borei pri ha-gafen* was meant to cover wine throughout. *Tosafot* explains, based on *Rashi*, that since the guests moved from one room to another — entering the dining hall — this constitutes a *shinui makom*, a change of location, which necessitates a new *berachah*. This principle has ramifications for many situations in which someone begins eating or drinking in one place and moves to another, and the *Tosafot* opens significant questions about what counts as a disqualifying change of place and what conditions — such as being under the same roof, or having it explicitly in mind — might prevent the need for a new blessing. We will not pursue those questions here, but the underlying reasoning is clear.

### Tosefta-Mishnah Contradiction

What is striking is that the *Tosefta* directly contradicts the *mishnah* on the question of who blesses for wine during the meal. The *mishnah* says that during the meal each person blesses for himself, while one person blesses for the group for the post-meal wine. The *Tosefta* says the opposite: during the meal one person blesses for the group, and before the meal each person blesses individually. The *gemara* does not smooth over this contradiction immediately; it will engage with it in the following discussion.

## Resolving Tosefta

### Guests Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַאֵיךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר רַב לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פַּת דְּבָעֵי הֲסִבָּה, אֲבָל יַיִן לָא בָּעֵי הֲסִבָּה — קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא! שָׁאנֵי אוֹרְחִין דְּדַעְתַּיְיהוּ לְמִיעְקַר.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **according to that version** that **Rav said: This** *halakha* **was only taught** with regard to **bread that** it **requires reclining** to enable one to recite a blessing on behalf of them all. **However, wine does not require reclining; the first clause** of the *Tosefta*, which taught that each guest recites a blessing over the wine for himself, **is difficult.** The Gemara answers: **Guests are different, as** when they are sitting in the hall prior to the meal, **their intention is to leave** and enter the dining room. Therefore, while there, their drinking together is not considered a joint meal.

The *gemara* now addresses how the *Tosefta* can be reconciled with each of the two versions of *Rav*'s position.

According to the version in which *Rav* holds that wine does not *require* *haseiba* — meaning that one person can ordinarily bless for the group over wine without *haseiba* — the pre-meal stage of the *Tosefta* is a difficulty. If wine does not need *haseiba*, why does each person bless for himself before the meal? The *gemara* answers: *shani orchin de-dato'ehu le-me'akar* — guests are different, because their intention is to uproot themselves. They know they are about to move to a different room. This pre-meal gathering is entirely temporary; no one is *kove'ia makom* here for the purpose of drinking. Since there is no genuine fixing of a place to drink, this moment does not constitute a drinking group, and therefore each person blesses individually. In all other contexts, though, group wine drinking would not require *haseiba*.

### Context of Bread Haseiba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַאֵיךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר רַב לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פַּת, דְּמַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה, אֲבָל יַיִן לָא מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה — קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִגּוֹ דְּקָא מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה לְפַת — מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ הֲסִבָּה לְיַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to the version** that **Rav said:** The mishna **only taught that reclining is effective** and enables one to recite a blessing on behalf of them all, with regard to a group eating **bread. However,** with regard to a group drinking **wine, reclining is ineffective** and each individual must recite a blessing for himself; **the latter clause** of the *Tosefta*, which taught that when drinking wine when reclining, one recites a blessing on behalf of all, **is difficult.** The Gemara responds: **There it is different, as since reclining is effective** and enables one to recite a blessing on behalf of them all **for bread, reclining is effective for wine** as well.

According to the second version — in which *Rav* holds that *haseiba* does not help for wine at all and can never constitute a wine-drinking group — the *Tosefta*'s statement that one person blesses for the group during the meal is the difficulty. If *haseiba* never works for wine, how can there be a group *berachah* on wine during the meal?

The *gemara* answers with a principle of contextual inclusion: *dimigo de-mahani lei haseiba le-pas, mahani lei haseiba le-yayin* — since *haseiba* is operative for the bread, it is operative for the wine as well. The *haseiba* of the bread meal defines the entire gathering as a structured collective experience. The wine, served in the midst of a formally constituted bread meal, inherits the character of that meal. It is not the wine that defines the group; it is the bread meal that defines the group. But once you are sitting together as a formal bread-eating collective, the wine consumed within that framework is likewise experienced collectively, and one person may bless for all. By itself, wine cannot trigger the institution of *haseiba*. But when wine appears within the context of an already-established *haseiba* for bread, it partakes of that same character.

## Ben Zoma on Wine

### Ben Zoma's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן. שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת בֶּן זוֹמָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ, לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן — אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם? אָמַר לָהֶם: הוֹאִיל וְאֵין בֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה פָּנוּי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If **wine came before them during the meal,** each and every diner recites a blessing over the wine for himself. If the wine came after the meal, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all. The *Tosefta* relates: **They asked Ben Zoma: Why did** the Sages **say:** If **wine came before them during the meal, each and every** diner **recites a blessing** over the wine **for himself;** but if the wine came **after the meal, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all? He said to them:** This is **because** while eating, **the throat is not available.** If one recites a blessing on behalf of them all, he would be forced to wait until they all had finished eating and readied themselves to drink the wine together (*Tosafot*). To prevent imposing upon them, the Sages ruled that there is no need to recite the blessing together.

The *gemara* now takes up the *mishnah*'s own position — which is the reverse of the *Tosefta*'s — that during the meal each person blesses for himself, while after the meal one person blesses for the group. *Ben Zoma* was asked about this: what is the reason that during the meal each person must make his own *borei pri ha-gafen*, while for the post-meal wine one person may do so for all?

### Choking Explanation

One explanation of this answer is practical and concerns risk: if a designated person were to recite *borei pri ha-gafen* for the group while the others were still eating, those others would feel compelled to answer *amen* and might do so while food was still in their mouths, with some risk of choking. Therefore the practice is that each person blesses for himself, at a moment of his own readiness, rather than being obligated to respond to another's blessing while still in the midst of eating.

### Group Experience Need

A second explanation runs deeper and connects back to the fundamental principle of group *berachot*: for one person to bless for the group, the group must be in a shared experiential state — all poised to receive the same benefit at the same moment. If one person recites *borei pri ha-gafen* for everyone while some still have food in their mouths and throats, those people are not actually ready to drink. They are not in the same experiential position as the one making the blessing. They cannot drink at this moment. Therefore there is no true shared experience over which a single *berachah* could encompass them all. After the meal, however, when the food has been removed and everyone's throat is clear, the group can genuinely be in the same state of readiness, and one person's *berachah* can represent the collective.

### Shared Experience

This second explanation reveals the full logic of the entire discussion: a *berachah* recited on behalf of a group requires that the group share an experiential state at that moment. *Haseiba* and *kove'ia makom* define the group structurally; but even within a defined group, there must be a genuine shared readiness for the benefit being blessed over. *Tosafot* raises the obvious question: what if no one happens to have food in his mouth at a given moment during the meal? Should the rule not apply? *Tosafot*'s conclusion is that the sages established a general rule that was not applied case by case. For our purposes — and *Tosafot* speaks here in terms of contemporary practice in thirteenth-century *Ashkenaz* — the only meaningful form of group experience that survives is one anchored in the context of bread, and *haseiba* as a formal institution no longer governs the way it once did in the Roman dining world.

## Wine & Incense Blessings

### Convention & Hand-Washing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהוּא אוֹמֵר עַל הַמּוּגְמָר כּוּ׳. מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״וְהוּא אוֹמֵר עַל הַמּוּגְמָר״, מִכְלָל דְּאִיכָּא עֲדִיף מִינֵּיהּ, וְאַמַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And he,** who recited the blessing over the wine, also **recites** the blessing **over the incense,** although they bring the incense only after the meal. The Gemara asks: **From** the fact **that **the mishna** teaches: And he recites** the blessing **over the incense, it can be inferred** that **there is** someone who should **take precedence over him,** to recite the blessing. That is why the mishna must emphasize that it is he who recites the blessing. **And** **why** does the one who recited the blessing over the wine nevertheless recite the blessing over the incense?

The *mishnah* states that the same person who recites *borei pri ha-gafen* for the post-meal wine also recites the *berachah* on the *mugmar* — the incense that was burned after the meal. The *gemara* asks: why does this particular person receive both honors? The answer the *gemara* gives is that the *mishnah*'s formulation implies that there was already someone more deserving to receive the second honor, yet the person who made the *berachah* on the wine received it anyway. The reason: *ho'il ve-hu natal yadav techilah ba-achronah* — since he was the first to wash his hands for *mayim acharonim*, the post-meal hand washing, he becomes the one designated for what follows. He who performs *mayim acharonim* first is, in a sense, already in the batter's box for the next ritual act.

This seems surprising: the *mishnah* spoke of his reciting the blessing on wine, not of his washing first. The *gemara*'s reasoning assumes a link — that washing first for *mayim acharonim* led to the wine *berachah*, which then carries over to the *mugmar berachah* — even though the *mishnah* itself did not explicitly mention the hand washing.

### Mayim Acharonim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְהוּא נָטַל יָדָיו תְּחִלָּה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: הַנּוֹטֵל יָדָיו תְּחִלָּה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה — הוּא מְזוּמָּן לַבְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Since he washed his hands first after** the meal, prior to Grace after Meals, he also recites the blessing over the incense, as the washing of hands after the meal and the burning of the incense serve similar functions. This **supports** the opinion **of Rav, as Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi says** that **Rav says: He who washes his hands first after** the meal **is designated to recite the blessing** of Grace after Meals.

This is supported by a teaching of *Rav Chiyya bar Ashi* in the name of *Rav*: *ha-notel yadav techilah ba-achronah hu mezuman li-verachah* — the one who first performs *mayim acharonim* is designated for the *berachah*. There is a principle, similar in spirit to *ha-matchil be-mitzvah omrim lo gemor* — the one who begins a mitzvah is told to complete it — by which an initiating act in one ritual domain confers a kind of standing or priority in the next.

### Rav Adda Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי בִּסְעוֹדְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַב: קוּם מְשִׁי יְדָךְ. חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה מְרַתֵּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: בַּר פַּחֲתֵי, עַיֵּין בְּבִרְכַּת מְזוֹנָא קָאָמַר לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav and Rabbi Ḥiyya were seated before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi at a meal. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to Rav: Stand and wash your hands.** Rabbi Ḥiyya **saw** that Rav **was trembling,** as Rav thought that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was criticizing him for eating too much or for having dirty hands. **Rabbi Ḥiyya said** to Rav: **Son of noblemen, he is telling you **to** review Grace after Meals.** As you will be the one reciting Grace after Meals, he told you to wash your hands first.

The *gemara* illustrates this with a narrative. *Rav* and *Rabbi Chiya* were sitting before *Rabbi* (Yehuda HaNasi) at a meal. *Rabbi* said to *Rav*: "Go wash your hands." *Rav* was alarmed — had he come to the table with unclean hands? Had *Rabbi* noticed something unseemly? *Rabbi Chiya* saw his distress and said to him: "*Bar pachti* — what he means is that he wants to give you the honor of leading *birkat ha-mazon*," and since the one who washes first for *mayim acharonim* is designated for the *berachah*, this is *Rabbi*'s way of conferring that honor upon *Rav*. The parallel to our *mishnah* is clear: washing hands first for *mayim acharonim* carries with it a designation for the leading role in what follows, whether that is *birkat ha-mazon* or the *mugmar berachah*.

## Blessing on Smell

### Rabbi Zeira's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רָבָא בַּר יִרְמְיָה: מֵאֵימָתַי מְבָרְכִין עַל הָרֵיחַ — מִשֶּׁתַּעֲלֶה תִּמְרָתוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרָבָא בַּר יִרְמְיָה: וְהָא לָא אֲרַח? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, ״הַמּוֹצִיא לֶחֶם מִן הָאָרֶץ״ דִּמְבָרֵךְ וְהָא לָא אֲכַל! אֶלָּא דַּעְתֵּיהּ לְמֵיכַל, הָכָא נָמֵי דַּעְתֵּיהּ לְאוֹרוֹחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the blessing over incense, the Gemara proceeds to discuss various *halakhot* that deal with blessings recited over scents. **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rava bar Yirmeya said: From when does one recite the blessing over the scent** of incense? **From when its column** of smoke **rises** after the incense has been placed upon the coals. **Rabbi Zeira said to Rava bar Yirmeya: But** at that point, **he has not** yet **smelled it.** Rava bar Yirmeya **said to him: And according to your reasoning,** the blessing: **Who brings forth bread from the earth, that one recites** before eating bread, at that point, **he has not yet eaten** from it. **Rather,** in that case, one recites the blessing when **he intends to eat; here too, he intends to smell.**

The *gemara* now turns to a new and significant topic: when exactly does one make a *berachah* on the aroma of incense? *Rabbi Zeira* transmitted in the name of *Rav*: the *berachah* is made from the moment the column of smoke rises up — *mi-she-ta'aleh timraso*.

### Before or After?

*Rabbi Zeira* himself then challenged this: but at that moment one has not yet smelled it. The interlocutor responds with an analogy: when one makes *hamotzi lechem min ha-aretz*, one has not yet eaten the bread — yet we make the *berachah* before eating. The answer is that one's intention is to eat, and the *berachah* is made in anticipation of the benefit. So too here, one's intention is to smell the incense, and the *berachah* is made in anticipation.

The exchange appears obvious on its face, but it carries an implicit and important subtext.

### Nehenin vs Shevach

The deeper question is whether the *berachah* on smell is a *birkat ha-nehenin* — a blessing made before deriving benefit, as is the rule for food and drink, where it is forbidden to derive benefit from this world without first making a *berachah* — or whether it is a *birkat ha-shevach*, a blessing of praise, like the blessing one makes upon seeing a rainbow or blossoming trees, which is made after or during the experience, not in anticipation of it.

The distinction is significant. A *birkat ha-nehenin* functions as a kind of permission structure: before the benefit, one must acknowledge God; only then may one partake. A *birkat ha-shevach* is different in character: one has an aesthetic or spiritual experience, and the *berachah* articulates the praise that experience evokes. The *berachah* on sight — seeing beautiful trees, a rainbow, or extraordinary natural phenomena — is clearly in the latter category. No one holds one's hand over one's eyes until making a *berachah*, then removes it to look. One sees the thing and then praises God.

The question here is where smell falls along this spectrum. Smell is not like eating: it does not fill one's stomach, does not sustain the body in the same way. In some sense it is more like an aesthetic experience than a nutritive one. If that is so, perhaps one should smell first and bless afterward, as with sight. But the position encoded in the *gemara* — and this is the ruling we follow — is that smell is treated as a *birkat ha-nehenin*: the *berachah* is made before smelling, in anticipation of the benefit, on the analogy of *hamotzi* before eating. One piece of evidence in favor of treating it as *nehenin* is the presence of something physical and substantial — the column of smoke, the incense material — that more closely resembles an object of consumption than a purely visual or aesthetic experience.

*Tosafot*, grappling with these questions, reaches the conclusion that if one is uncertain which *berachah* to make on a given aroma, one might fall back on *she-hakol*, treating it as any other case of doubt in *birchat ha-nehenin*. Another view cited by *Tosafot* is that the generic *berachah* in the realm of *besamim* is *borei minei besamim*, and that this serves as the equivalent of *she-hakol* for aromas. That *Tosafot* entertains the possibility of *she-hakol* itself underscores that the category of smell is fully understood as falling within *birchat ha-nehenin*.

### Meorei Ha-Esh Example

By contrast, a well-known parallel case clarifies the distinction between the two types. *Borei meorei ha-esh* — the blessing on fire on *motzei Shabbat* — is a *birkat ha-shevach*, not a *birkat ha-nehenin*. The *mishnah* says one does not make the *berachah* until one benefits from its light — *ein mevarchin ad she-ye'otu le-oro*. This means one looks at the flame first, derives benefit from seeing it, and then makes the *berachah*. The practice of looking at one's fingernails during the *berachah* on fire, as if deriving a specific benefit at that moment, follows from this: the *berachah* comes after or during the experience. One who makes the *berachah* before looking and then looks is following the structure of *birkat ha-nehenin* — which would be a mistake in this case, since *borei meorei ha-esh* is a *birkat ha-shevach*. This parallel helps delineate why the question about smell is so pointed: if smell were categorized as *shevach*, one would smell first and then bless; if *nehenin*, one blesses first and then smells. The ruling is that smell is *nehenin*.

## Types of Incense Blessings

### General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּאַבָּא בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר זְעֵירִי: כׇּל הַמּוּגְמָרוֹת מְבָרְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן ״בּוֹרֵא עֲצֵי בְּשָׂמִים״ חוּץ מִמּוּשְׁק, שֶׁמִּין חַיָּה הוּא, שֶׁמְּבָרְכִין עָלָיו ״בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי בְּשָׂמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Abba bar Naḥmani, said** that **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rav said, and some say that Rav Ḥisda said** that **Ze'iri said: Over all the incense one recites: Who creates fragrant trees, except for musk,** which **is** extracted **from a living creature,** and over which **one recites: Who creates various spices.**

*Rav Chisda* taught in the name of *Rav* — with an alternative attribution to *Zeiri*: all *mugmarot* (types of incense) receive the *berachah* of *borei atzei besamim* — the blessing on aromatic woods — with one exception: musk. Musk, which derives from animal glands or excretions rather than from plant matter, receives instead *borei minei besamim* — a more generic blessing on types of aromatic things, which functions as the *she-hakol* of the *besamim* world.

### Braita Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵין מְבָרְכִין ״בּוֹרֵא עֲצֵי בְּשָׂמִים״ אֶלָּא עַל אֲפַרְסְמוֹן שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי, וְעַל אֲפַרְסְמוֹן שֶׁל בֵּית קֵיסָר, וְעַל הַהֲדַס שֶׁבְּכָל מָקוֹם! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** based on what was taught in a *baraita*: **One only recites: Who creates fragrant trees, over the balsam from the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and over balsam from the house of Caesar and over myrtle everywhere.** According to the previous statement, one recites that blessing over all types of incense. The Gemara comments: Indeed, it is a **conclusive refutation.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this with a *braita*: one only makes *borei atzei besamim* on *afarsamon* (balsam) — and specifically on the balsam of the house of *Rebbe* and of the house of Caesar (both of whom apparently held private royal orchards of this extraordinarily valuable plant, which in its time was said to be worth its weight in gold) — and on *hadas* (myrtle) everywhere. Nothing else, according to this *braita*, would receive *borei atzei besamim*. This appears to contradict the broad ruling that all *mugmarot* receive that *berachah*. The *gemara* declares this a *tiyuvta* — a refutation.

### Resolution of Contradiction

Though not fully explicated in the Sefaria text provided, *Tosafot* notes that the *Ba'al Halachot Gedolot* did not have the word *tiyuvta* in his text at all, and instead presents a different version of the *gemara* that harmonizes the sources. The reading he proposes is: the *braita* should be understood as saying "myrtle and anything similar to it." That is, the *braita* uses *hadas* as a paradigm example, not as an exclusive list. The principle derived is: *kol she-ikaro la-rei'ach* — anything whose primary purpose in society is its aroma. This excludes items such as quinces and apples (*tapuchim* and *chavushim*), which do have pleasant aromas but whose primary social function is as food. For those, one does not say *borei atzei besamim*. For any aromatic wood or plant whose defining characteristic is its aroma — not its food value or other utility — one does say *borei atzei besamim*, provided also that it has the quality of *etz* (to be further discussed below). This resolution also implies that *borei atzei besamim* can be said even when the material is being burned as incense, not merely when it is present in its natural state.

*Tosafot* further addresses a practical case: a person who enters a house filled with various *besamim* and is uncertain whether what he is smelling comes from an *etz* or from *adamah*. Some authorities say he should make *she-hakol* — since on all things, if one made *she-hakol*, one has discharged one's obligation. The view of *Rabbeinu Moshe* is that he should make *borei minei besamim*, which functions as the generic default in the world of *besamim*. This debate itself further reflects the question of exactly how to classify *birchat ha-nehenin* on smell.

### Balsam Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב יִצְחָק: הַאי מִשְׁחָא דַאֲפַרְסְמוֹן מַאי מְבָרְכִין עִלָּוֵיהּ? אָמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״בּוֹרֵא שֶׁמֶן אַרְצֵנוּ״. אָמַר לֵיהּ: בַּר מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה דַּחֲבִיבָא לֵיהּ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מַאי? אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״בּוֹרֵא שֶׁמֶן עָרֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Yitzḥak: This balsam oil, what blessing does one recite over it?** Rav Yitzḥak **said to him, this is what Rav Yehuda said:** One recites: **Who creates the oil of our land,** as balsam only grew in Eretz Yisrael, in the Jordan valley. Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Except for Rav Yehuda, for whom Eretz Yisrael was** extremely **beloved** and who therefore mentioned it in his blessing, **what** blessing does **everyone** else recite over balsam oil? **He said to him: This is what Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** One recites: **Who creates pleasant oil.**

*Rav Chisda* asked *Rav Yitzchak*: what *berachah* does one make on oil of balsam — *mishcha de-afarsamon*? The answer attributed to *Rav Yehuda* is *borei shemen artzeinu* — who creates the oil of our land, *Eretz Yisrael* — reflecting *Rav Yehuda*'s well-known love of the Land of Israel and his tendency to weave references to it wherever possible. The *gemara* gently notes this and asks: for everyone else, who perhaps does not share *Rav Yehuda*'s particular attachment, what would the *berachah* be? *Rabbi Yochanan* is cited: *borei shemen areiv* — who creates pleasant oil. These appear to have been specific formulations for a specific substance of extraordinary value.

### Costus & Oil-Infused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הַאי כְּשַׁרְתָּא מְבָרְכִין עִלָּוֵיהּ ״בּוֹרֵא עֲצֵי בְּשָׂמִים״, אֲבָל מִשְׁחָא כְּבִישָׁא — לָא. וְרַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִשְׁחָא כְּבִישָׁא, אֲבָל מִשְׁחָא טְחִינָא — לָא. נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ מִשְׁחָא טְחִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Over costus,** a spice, **one recites: Who creates fragrant trees, but** over **oil** that was **pressed** with spices to absorb their scents, **no,** one does not recite that blessing. **And Rav Kahana said: Even** over **oil pressed** with spices, one recites: Who creates fragrant trees, **but not** over **oil** into which spices were **ground. The Sages of Neharde'a say: Even** over **oil** into which spices were **ground,** which is of even lower quality, one recites this blessing.

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* rules that *kushta* (costus, a type of spice) receives *borei atzei besamim*. However, *mishcha kevisha* — oil in which aromatic material has been soaked so that the oil absorbs the aroma, with the original substance removed — does not receive a *berachah*, because the aromatic thing itself is no longer *be-ayin*, physically present. This raises a question directly relevant to modern perfumes and essential oils: if the original substance is no longer present and only its absorbed scent remains, does a *berachah* apply at all?

*Rav Kahana* disagrees and holds that even *mishcha kevisha* — oil that has had the aromatic material soaked in it — receives *borei atzei besamim*. But even he would agree that *mishcha techina* — where the aromatic material is ground up and mixed into the oil without being properly steeped — does not qualify, because in that form the aromatic quality has not been effectively released into the oil and the scent is not significant. The scholars of *Nehardea* go further still, ruling that even ground-up material mixed into the oil suffices. The running question throughout is: at what point has the aromatic substance sufficiently manifested itself — either as a physical object *be-ayin* or as a potent aromatic presence — to warrant a *berachah*?

## What Counts as Etz?

### Stalks as Etz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: הַאי סִמְלָק — מְבָרְכִין עִלָּוֵיהּ ״בּוֹרֵא עֲצֵי בְּשָׂמִים״. אָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב: הָנֵי חִלְפֵי דְיַמָּא — מְבָרְכִין עֲלַיְיהוּ ״בּוֹרֵא עֲצֵי בְּשָׂמִים״. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״וְהִיא הֶעֱלָתַם הַגָּגָה וַתִּטְמְנֵם בְּפִשְׁתֵּי הָעֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: Over this jasmine [*simlak*], one recites: Who creates fragrant trees. Rav Ḥananel said** that **Rav said: Over these spikenards,** which grow next to **the sea, one recites: Who creates fragrant trees. Mar Zutra said: What is the verse** from which we derive that even a plant with soft stalks can be called a tree? From the verse: **"She had taken them up to the roof and hidden them under the trees of flax"** (Joshua 2:6); evidently, even stalks of flax are called "trees."

Having established the general rule that *borei atzei besamim* applies to aromatic trees and woody plants, the *gemara* turns to the question of what constitutes an *etz* for this purpose. The definition here is broader than the one used for determining whether to say *borei pri ha-etz* over fruit. For fruit, the operative criterion is whether the plant survives from year to year and its main stalk persists; an annual plant producing food receives *borei pri ha-adamah* regardless of how high it grows. Here, for *besamim*, the definition extends to things that grow on stalks.

*Mar Zutra* offers a scriptural proof: in the Book of *Yehoshua*, the verse describing *Rachav* hiding the spies uses the phrase *be-fishtei ha-etz* — in the flax stalks — where the word *etz* is applied to a stalk. This proves that stalks can be called *etz*. Therefore, aromatic plants that grow on stalks may still receive *borei atzei besamim*, even if they would not be considered trees in the conventional sense. It is notable that this broader definition does not carry over into the domain of fruit blessings: barley and wheat, which also grow on stalks, receive *adamah*, not *ha-etz*. The criterion for *etz* in the *besamim* context is evidently more inclusive.

### Daffodil Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא אָמַר: הַאי נַרְקוֹם דְּגִינוּנִיתָא — מְבָרְכִין עִלָּוֵיהּ ״בּוֹרֵא עֲצֵי בְשָׂמִים״. דְּדַבְרָא — ״בּוֹרֵא עִשְׂבֵי בְשָׂמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiya said: Over this garden daffodil one recites: Who creates fragrant trees,** while over a wild daffodil that grows **in the field,** one recites: **Who creates fragrant plants.**

A distinction is drawn between the garden daffodil (*narkum de-ganisa*) and the field daffodil (*de-davra*). The garden daffodil receives *borei atzei besamim*; the field daffodil receives *borei ispei besamim* — the blessing on aromatic herbs or grasses. The precise botanical or legal distinction between the two is not entirely clear from the *gemara*. It may be that the cultivated garden variety, by virtue of cultivation, acquires a more substantial or persistent form that rises to the level of *etz*, while the wild field variety does not. Whatever the underlying principle, the *sugya* makes clear that drawing the line between *atzei besamim* and *ispei besamim* requires careful attention to the nature of each specific plant.

### Flowers & Perennials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָנֵי סִיגְלֵי — מְבָרְכִין עֲלַיְיהוּ ״בּוֹרֵא עִשְׂבֵי בְשָׂמִים״. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: הַאי מַאן דְּמוֹרַח בְּאֶתְרוֹגָא אוֹ בְּחַבּוּשָׁא אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ שֶׁנָּתַן רֵיחַ טוֹב בַּפֵּירוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: Over** fragrant **violets one recites: Who creates fragrant plants. Mar Zutra said: One who smells a citron [*etrog*] or a quince recites: Blessed…who gave pleasant fragrance in fruits.**

Similarly, violets (*siglei*), which are straightforward flowers with no woody component, receive *borei ispei besamim*. On the other hand, jasmine (*samlok*) and a species identified as spikenard growing near the sea receive *borei atzei besamim*, presumably because they grow on stems or branches sturdy enough to qualify as *etz* in the sense established above. Taken together, these rulings show that the line between *atzei* and *ispei besamim* tracks something like: does this plant have a sufficiently persistent, woody, or stalk-like structure? A perennial bush or plant with persistent stems may qualify; a flower that blooms annually from the ground does not.

### Fruit Blessings

*Mar Zutra* adds: if one smells an *etrog* or a quince (*chavush*), both of which have a pleasant natural fragrance, the *berachah* is *baruch she-natan rei'ach tov ba-perot* — who has given a pleasant aroma to fruit. The reason is that these are primarily fruits; their defining social function is as food. Despite their lovely smell, they are not *besamim* in the primary sense, and so neither *atzei* nor *ispei besamim* applies. The blessing acknowledges instead the *Creator*'s gift of pleasant fragrance to fruits — a distinct formulation appropriate to this category. The example of *etrog* may also be significant because *etrog* is not typically eaten raw, which might lead one to think it is not primarily a fruit; but it remains fundamentally a fruit, and the blessing reflects that.

## Blossoming Trees Blessing

### Trees Blessing Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דְּנָפֵיק בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן וְחָזֵי אִילָנֵי דְּקָא מְלַבְלְבִי, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ שֶׁלֹּא חִיסֵּר בְּעוֹלָמוֹ כְּלוּם וּבָרָא בּוֹ בְּרִיּוֹת טוֹבוֹת וְאִילָנוֹת טוֹבוֹת לְהִתְנָאוֹת בָּהֶן בְּנֵי אָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** On a related topic, the Gemara cites that **Rav Yehuda said: One who goes out during Nisan and sees trees that are blossoming recites: Blessed…who has withheld nothing from His world, and has created in it beautiful creatures and trees for human beings to enjoy.**

The *gemara* now makes a seamless transition from blessings on smell to a related blessing recited in the spring. *Rav Yehuda* taught: one who goes out in the month of *Nissan* and sees trees that are blossoming recites the following: *Baruch she-lo chisar be-olamo klum u-bara bo beriyot tovot ve-ilanot tovim le-hitna'ot bahem benei adam* — Blessed is One who has not withheld anything from His world and who created in it good creatures and good trees for human beings to take pleasure in.

### Scriptural Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁמְּבָרְכִין עַל הָרֵיחַ? — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּל הַנְּשָׁמָה תְּהַלֵּל יָהּ״. אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה נֶהֱנֵית מִמֶּנּוּ וְאֵין הַגּוּף נֶהֱנֶה מִמֶּנּוּ? — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה הָרֵיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Zutra bar Toyvia said that Rav said: From where is it derived that one recites a blessing over scent? As it is stated: "Let every soul praise the Lord" (Psalms 150:6). He explains the verse: What is it from which the soul derives benefit and the body does not derive benefit from it? You must say: That is scent. Even over items from which only the soul derives benefit, one must recite a blessing and praise God.

From where is it known that one makes a *berachah* on smell at all? *Rav Zutra bar Tuvia* in the name of *Rav* derives it from a verse: *kol ha-neshamah tehalel Yah* — all souls will praise God. The *gemara* reads this as pointing to something that the soul benefits from (*neshamah nehenet mimenu*) but the body does not — and that is smell. This derivation is itself noteworthy: rather than grounding the obligation in the general principle that it is forbidden to derive benefit from this world without a *berachah* — which is the standard foundation of *birchat ha-nehenin* — the *gemara* cites a specific verse and specifically distinguishes smell as a benefit to the soul rather than to the body.

### Nature of Smell

This distinction — *ha-neshamah nehenet mimenu ve-ein ha-guf nehena mimenu* — is central to the entire dialectic that has run through this *sugya*. Smell is not like food, which sustains the body; it is not like drink, which quenches thirst and fills the stomach. It is not even quite like sight, which delivers aesthetic experience but leaves no trace on the body whatsoever. Smell occupies a middle position: it is physical enough — there is a substance, a column of smoke, a present object — that it more closely resembles *nehenin* than *shevach*. And yet the *gemara*'s own proof-text underscores that it is not the same bodily *hana'ah* as food. In the end, the ruling is that smell is treated as *birchat ha-nehenin* — the *berachah* comes before one smells. But the *gemara*'s citation of this verse, and its emphasis that the body does not derive the same kind of benefit as from food, preserves the sense that smell is a sui generis category, neither fully reducible to *nehenin* nor fully equivalent to *shevach*.

## Oil & Myrtle Customs

### Oil & Myrtle Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הֵבִיאוּ לְפָנָיו שֶׁמֶן וַהֲדַס, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַשֶּׁמֶן וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַהֲדַס. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַהֲדַס וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַשֶּׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If **they brought before him** both scented **oil and** a **myrtle** branch, **Beit Shammai say: One recites a blessing over the oil** first **and over the myrtle** branch **thereafter**. **And Beit Hillel say: One recites a blessing over the myrtle** branch first **and over the oil thereafter.**

The *gemara* returns to the customs of the *seudah*. At the end of the meal, oil and myrtle were brought out for the guests, and *berachot* were made on each.

### Shammai-Hillel Debate

*Beit Shammai* holds: one first makes the *berachah* on the oil and then on the myrtle. *Beit Hillel* holds: one first makes the *berachah* on the myrtle and then on the oil. The nature of this debate — which blessing has priority when two distinct items requiring separate blessings are presented simultaneously — has echoes in other *Beit Shammai*–*Beit Hillel* disputes, such as the order of *borei pri ha-gafen* and *mekadesh ha-Shabbat* in *kiddush*. The underlying logic in each case concerns some principle of precedence or importance, though the exact nature of the argument here is not immediately transparent.

### Rabban Gamliel's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: אֲנִי אַכְרִיעַ. שֶׁמֶן — זָכִינוּ לְרֵיחוֹ וְזָכִינוּ לְסִיכָתוֹ, הֲדַס — לְרֵיחוֹ זָכִינוּ לְסִיכָתוֹ לֹא זָכִינוּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמַּכְרִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Gamliel said: I will decide this dispute in favor of the opinion of Beit Shammai, that one should recite a blessing over the oil first, as it is more significant. With regard to **oil; we are privileged** to enjoy **its fragrance and we are privileged** to enjoy it by **anointing** ourselves with **it.** With regard to the **myrtle** branch; **we are privileged** to enjoy **its fragrance, we are not privileged** to enjoy it by **anointing** ourselves with **it. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **the decisor,** Rabban Gamliel.

*Rabban Gamliel* intervened to decide the matter: oil is superior to myrtle because oil confers two forms of benefit — one benefits from its aroma (*rei'ach*) and also from anointing one's body with it (*sichato*). Myrtle provides only the benefit of aroma. Since oil provides a more comprehensive benefit, it takes precedence, and the *berachah* on oil comes first — in agreement with *Beit Shammai*.

### Halacha Ruling

*Rabbi Yochanan* rules: the *halacha* follows the one who cast the deciding vote (*ha-machria*). Since *Rabban Gamliel* decided in favor of *Beit Shammai*'s position, the *halacha* follows *Beit Shammai* — an uncommon ruling, given that the general rule is *halacha ke-Veit Hillel*.

### Rav Pappa's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא. אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ שֶׁמֶן וַהֲדַס. שְׁקַל רַב פָּפָּא בָּרֵיךְ אַהֲדַס בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר בָּרֵיךְ אַשֶּׁמֶן. אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמַּכְרִיעַ? אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּבֵית הִלֵּל. וְלָא הִיא, לְאִשְׁתְּמוֹטֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa happened** to come **to the house of Rav Huna, son of Rav Ika. They brought before him** both scented **oil and** a **myrtle** branch. **Rav Pappa took and recited a blessing** over the **myrtle branch first and then recited a blessing** over the **oil.** Rav Huna **said to him:** And **does the Master not hold** that **the *halakha* is in accordance** with the opinion of **the decisor?** If so, you should have recited a blessing over the oil first. Rav Pappa said: **Rava said the following: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel.** The Gemara comments: **That is not so,** as Rava did not issue that ruling. **Rather, Rav Pappa did** this **in order to extricate himself** from an unpleasant situation and justify his conduct.

*Rav Pappa* visited the home of *Rav Huna the son of Rav Ika*, and when oil and myrtle were brought before them, *Rav Pappa* took the myrtle and blessed on it first — following *Beit Hillel*. His host challenged him: does the master not hold that the *halacha* follows the *machria*, and therefore we should bless on the oil first like *Beit Shammai*? *Rav Pappa* replied: this is what *Rava* said — the *halacha* follows *Beit Hillel*. The *gemara* then comments: this was not actually so. *Rav Pappa* was maneuvering to extricate himself from the embarrassment of having been caught in what appeared to be a halachic error — he had just read the passage teaching that one should cast oneself into a fiery furnace rather than embarrass another person in public, and now he sought a way to avoid his own embarrassment by attributing a position to *Rava*.

### Rava's Explanation

The *gemara* notes that *Rava* had indeed said, in a different context, that the principle *halacha ke-divrei ha-machria* applies only when the *machria* offers a genuine compromise position — a middle path between the two disputants. When a third authority simply sides with one of the two positions without offering any new synthesis, that does not constitute a true *machria*, and the principle does not apply. On the basis of that teaching of *Rava*, one would in fact arrive at the conclusion that the *halacha* follows *Beit Hillel* — not because *Rava* ever said so explicitly about this case, but because *Rava*'s ruling about what qualifies as a *machria* means that *Rabban Gamliel*'s vote does not trigger the principle. *Rav Pappa*, therefore, was not fabricating from whole cloth, but he did slightly misrepresent the position by saying "*Rava* says the *halacha* follows *Beit Hillel*," when *Rava* never said that directly.

## Oil & Wine Order

### Oil-Wine Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הֵבִיאוּ לִפְנֵיהֶם שֶׁמֶן וְיַיִן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אוֹחֵז הַשֶׁמֶן בִּימִינוֹ וְאֶת הַיַּיִן בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַשֶּׁמֶן וְחוֹזֵר וּמְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹחֵז אֶת הַיַּיִן בִּימִינוֹ וְאֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַיַּיִן וְחוֹזֵר וּמְבָרֵךְ עַל הַשֶּׁמֶן, וְטָחוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַשַּׁמָּשׁ. וְאִם שַׁמָּשׁ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הוּא — טָחוֹ בַּכּוֹתֶל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁגְּנַאי לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם לָצֵאת לַשּׁוּק כְּשֶׁהוּא מְבוּשָּׂם.
>
> **Translation:** **Our Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **they brought before them** both scented **oil and wine, Beit Shammai say: He grasps the oil in his right** hand, since he recites a blessing over the oil first, **and the wine in his left** hand. **He recites a blessing over the oil and then he recites a blessing over the wine. Beit Hillel say** the opposite: **He grasps the wine in his right** hand **and the oil in his left** hand. **He recites a blessing over the wine and then he recites a blessing over the oil. And** after he has recited a blessing over the oil and anointed his hands with it, **he smears** it **on the head of the servant** so that his hands will not remain perfumed. **And if the servant is a Torah scholar, he smears** the oil **on the wall, as it is unbecoming for a Torah scholar to go out perfumed into the marketplace.**

The *gemara* next records a *baraita* about oil and wine at the end of the meal. *Beit Shammai* holds: one holds the oil in the right hand and the wine in the left hand, makes the *berachah* on the oil first, and then on the wine. *Beit Hillel* holds the reverse: wine in the right hand and oil in the left, and the *berachah* on wine is made first.

### Wiping on Shamash

After the oil is applied to one's hands, the custom was to wipe the excess oil on the head or hair of the *shamash* — the attendant serving at the meal. The idea seems to be that the *shamash* benefits from receiving the remaining aromatic oil. However, if the *shamash* is a *talmid chacham*, one does not wipe the oil on his head; instead, one wipes it on the wall.

### Unseemly for Scholars

The reason: it is unseemly (*genai*) for a *talmid chacham* to go out into the marketplace perfumed. This ruling sets up the following broader discussion of conduct befitting a Torah scholar.

## Six Unfitting Things

### Perfumed in Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים גְּנַאי לוֹ לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם: אַל יֵצֵא כְּשֶׁהוּא מְבוּשָּׂם לַשּׁוּק, וְאַל יֵצֵא יְחִידִי בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאַל יֵצֵא בְּמִנְעָלִים הַמְטוּלָּאִים, וְאַל יְסַפֵּר עִם אִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק, וְאַל יָסֵב בַּחֲבוּרָה שֶׁל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ, וְאַל יִכָּנֵס בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Tangential to the mention of conduct unbecoming a Torah scholar, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Six things are disgraceful for a Torah scholar: He may not go out perfumed into the marketplace; he may not go out** of his house **alone at night; he may not go out** wearing **patched shoes; he may not converse with a woman in the marketplace; he may not recline** and participate in a meal **in the company of ignoramuses; and he may not be the last to enter the study hall.**

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: six things are unbecoming of a *talmid chacham*. The first is going out perfumed into the marketplace. *Rabbi Yochanan* explains: this applies in a place where people are suspected of *mishkav zachar* — homosexual activity — because going about perfumed in such a place would arouse suspicion that one was signaling one's availability. *Rav Sheshet* qualifies: this applies only to having the perfume on one's clothing. If it is on one's body, the sweat will cause it to evaporate, and there is no concern. *Rav Pappa* adds: hair, however, is like clothing in this respect — the oil on one's hair will remain apparent. Others say hair is like the body — it will be absorbed and will not be a problem.

### Alone at Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַל יֵצֵא כְּשֶׁהוּא מְבוּשָּׂם לַשּׁוּק: אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁחֲשׁוּדִים עַל מִשְׁכַּב זָכוּר. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּבִגְדוֹ, אֲבָל בְּגוּפוֹ — זֵיעָה מְעַבְּרָא לֵיהּ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּשְׂעָרוֹ כְּבִגְדוֹ דָּמֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי. וְאַל יֵצֵא יְחִידִי בַּלַּיְלָה: מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא קְבִיעַ לֵיהּ עִידָּנָא, אֲבָל קְבִיעַ לֵיהּ עִידָּנָא — מִידָּע יְדִיעַ דִּלְעִידָּנֵיהּ קָא אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the statements of the *baraita*. **He may not go out perfumed into the marketplace; Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This prohibition only applies **in a place where they are suspected of homosexuality.** One who goes out into the marketplace perfumed, will arouse suspicion. **Rav Sheshet said: We only said this with regard to his clothing** that was perfumed, **but with regard to his body,** it is permitted, as **his sweat causes the fragrance to dissipate. Rav Pappa said:** In this regard, **his hair is** considered **like his clothing. And some say: His hair is** considered **like his body.** The second is **he may not go out** of his house **alone at night because of suspicion** of promiscuity. **And** therefore **this was only** prohibited **if he does not have a set time** during the night to study with his teacher, **but** if **he has a set time,** everyone **knows that he is going to** study with his teacher at **his set time,** and they will not be suspicious of him.

The second is going out alone at night — because of *cheshad*, suspicion. People will assume one has a clandestine rendezvous. This applies only if one has no fixed appointment at night. If one has a regular nightly commitment — such as a fixed time to study with a teacher — people know this and there is no suspicion.

### Patched Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל יֵצֵא בְּמִנְעָלִים הַמְטוּלָּאִים: מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: גְּנַאי הוּא לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁיֵּצֵא בְּמִנְעָלִים הַמְטוּלָּאִים. אִינִי? וְהָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא נָפֵיק! אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: בִּטְלַאי עַל גַּבֵּי טְלַאי. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּפַנְתָּא, אֲבָל בְּגִילְדָּא — לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וּבְפַנְתָּא לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּאוֹרְחָא, אֲבָל בְּבֵיתָא — לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, אֲבָל בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He may not go out** wearing **patched shoes.** This **supports** the statement of **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said: It is disgraceful for a Torah scholar to go out** wearing **patched shoes.** The Gemara challenges: **Is that so? Didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** himself **go out** wearing patched shoes? **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, said:** It is only prohibited when the shoe has **a patch upon a patch. And we only said this with regard to** patches on **the upper** part of the shoe **but** if the patch is **on the sole, this does not** apply. **And with regard to** patches on **the upper** part of the shoe, **we only said this** when he is out **on the road, but in his house, we need not** be concerned. **And we only said this with regard to the summer** when the patches would be visible to all, **but during the rainy season,** when the mud obscures the view of the patches, **we need not** be concerned.

The third is going out in shoes that have patches. *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* states this explicitly: it is unbecoming for a *talmid chacham* to go out in patched shoes. The *gemara* then asks: but *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* himself went out in patched shoes! *Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman* answers: the problem is specifically *talai al gav talai* — patches upon patches. A single patch on a shoe is unremarkable; but when one has multiple layers of patching, the shoe becomes unseemly. Furthermore, even multiple patches are only a problem on the main body of the shoe (*befanta*); on the heel or sole (*gilda*), it is not a concern. And even on the main body of the shoe, it is only a problem when going out; at home, it matters not at all. Finally, it is only a problem in summer (*yemot ha-chamah*); in the rainy season, wearing worn-out shoes is practical and unremarkable.

### Speaking with Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל יְסַפֵּר עִם אִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא אִשְׁתּוֹ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֲפִילּוּ הִיא אִשְׁתּוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא בִּתּוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא אֲחוֹתוֹ — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַכֹּל בְּקִיאִים בִּקְרוֹבוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **He may not converse with a woman in the marketplace. Rav Ḥisda said: Even if she is his wife. This was also taught** in a *baraita*: One may not converse with a woman in the market, **even if she is his wife, even if she is his daughter and even if she is his sister,** for **not everyone is well-versed in** the identity of **his female relatives** and they will suspect that he is talking to women who are not his relatives.

The fourth is speaking with a woman in the marketplace. *Rav Chisda* rules: this applies even to one's own wife. The *baraita* confirms: even one's wife, even one's daughter, even one's sister — because not everyone knows who one's relatives are, and people will form suspicious impressions about the identity of the woman one is speaking with.

### Avoiding Am Ha'Aretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל יָסֵב בַּחֲבוּרָה שֶׁל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ: מַאי טַעְמָא? — דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאִמְּשׁוֹכֵי בָּתְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He may not recline** and participate in a meal **in the company of ignoramuses.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason? Perhaps he will be drawn after them** and emulate them.

The fifth is reclining in a group of *amei ha-aretz* in the manner of *haseiba*. The reason: one may come to follow their ways. Association with those who are unlearned and who do not conduct themselves with the standards of *Torah* scholarship will, over time, influence one's own conduct and standards.

### Last to Arrive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל יִכָּנֵס בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״פּוֹשֵׁעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **He may not be the last to enter the study hall.** The Gemara explains: **Because they** will **call him negligent,** in other words, careless and lazy.

The sixth is being the last to enter the *beit ha-midrash* — because one will be called *poshea*, negligent. There must always be someone who arrives last; it should not be the *talmid chacham*.

### Large Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים, אַף לֹא יַפְסִיעַ פְּסִיעָה גַּסָּה: דְּאָמַר מָר, פְּסִיעָה גַּסָּה נוֹטֶלֶת אֶחָד מֵחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מִמְּאוֹר עֵינָיו שֶׁל אָדָם. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לַהְדְּרֵיהּ בְּקִדּוּשָׁא דְבֵי שִׁמְשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say he may not take long strides, as the Master said: A long stride takes** away **one five-hundredth of a person's eyesight.** The Gemara asks: **What is his remedy** if he took long strides? The Gemara responds: **He can restore it** by drinking the wine of ***kiddush* on** Shabbat **eve.**

Some add a seventh: do not take large steps. The *gemara* explains the reasoning: taking large steps removes one five-hundredth of a person's eyesight. The remedy for this is the wine of *kiddush*, which reverses the loss. This is framed as a health concern rather than a matter of propriety — though it is interesting to note that the prohibition on large steps overlaps with the concern about running on *Shabbat*, and the remedy through *kiddush* wine creates a certain symmetry.

### Haughty Bearing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל יְהַלֵּךְ בְּקוֹמָה זְקוּפָה: דְּאָמַר מָר הַמְהַלֵּךְ בְּקוֹמָה זְקוּפָה אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, כְּאִילּוּ דּוֹחֵק רַגְלֵי שְׁכִינָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״מְלֹא כׇל הָאָרֶץ כְּבוֹדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And he may not walk with an upright posture,** but slightly bowed, **as the Master said: One who walks with an upright posture** and in an arrogant manner, **even four cubits,** it is **as if he is pushing away the feet of the Divine Presence,** as **it is written: "The entire world is full of His glory"** (Isaiah 6:3).

And finally: do not walk with an upright and haughty bearing (*kumah zekufah*). The reason is severe: one who walks even four *amot* with an exaggerated, arrogant posture is considered as if he were pushing away the feet of the *Shekhinah* — for as it is written, *melo chol ha-aretz kevodo* — the entire earth is filled with God's glory. To walk with self-important haughtiness through a world permeated by the divine presence is to assert one's own centrality in a space that belongs to God.
