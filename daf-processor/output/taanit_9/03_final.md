# Taanit 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Siman Klala & Omens

### Friday Rain Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁנִים כִּשְׁנֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ, וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת — אֵינָן אֶלָּא סִימַן קְלָלָה. הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: קָשֶׁה יוֹמָא דְמִיטְרָא כְּיוֹמָא דְּדִינָא. אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: אִי לָא דִּצְרִיךְ לִבְרִיָּיתָא, בָּעֵינַן רַחֲמֵי וּמְבַטְּלִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Even** in **years like the years of Elijah,** when God decreed that no rain would fall, if **rain falls on Shabbat eves it is nothing other** than **a sign of a curse,** as the rain disrupts the preparations for Shabbat. **This is** the same as that **which Rabba bar Sheila said: A rainy day is as difficult as a judgment day. Ameimar** even **said: Were** it **not** for the fact **that** rain is needed **by people, we would pray for mercy and** to **annul it,** due to the nuisances that rain causes.

The Gemara opens this section by shifting from the importance of rain — what brings it, what causes it to cease — to a series of observations about rain as a *siman klala*, a sign of a curse. The emphasis here is not that rain itself constitutes a *klala*, but rather that the timing or circumstances of its arrival can carry negative religious symbolism. Rain is seen as a vehicle through which God communicates with us, and even a beneficial rain can, under the wrong conditions, carry an ominous message.

Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *afilu shanim kishnei Eliyahu* — even if the years have been like the years of Eliyahu, meaning years of terrible drought, and then the rains finally arrive — if they fall on *erev Shabbat*, that is still nothing but a *siman klala*. Rashi explains the difficulty: preparations for Shabbat require going outdoors, and rain on a Friday creates genuine inconvenience. The Gemara reinforces this with a statement attributed to Rava bar Shila: a day of rain can be as difficult and disruptive as a day when the courts are in session — the crowds, the commotion, the general inconvenience. It is quite striking that after years of drought, one would have grounds to complain that rain finally came — but on a Friday. The point, again, is not that the rain is bad, but that God chose to send it precisely on a Friday, and that timing is the *siman klala*.

### Rain Complaint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָא דִּצְרִיךְ לִבְרִיָּיתָא, בָּעֵינַן רַחֲמֵי וּמְבַטְּלִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Were it not for the fact that rain is needed by people, we would pray for mercy and annul it.

The Gemara then records a more sweeping, discordant voice on the matter. It quotes a statement that if rain were not so necessary for the survival of the world — if it were not indispensable to *briyata*, to creation itself — one would simply pray that rain never come at all. This is someone whose primary experience of rain is the inconvenience it causes. This is a fascinating outlier within the sustained discussion of rain's importance and religious grandeur — a dissenting voice complaining about the very thing the Gemara has been celebrating.

### Shabbat Sun Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: שֶׁמֶשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת — צְדָקָה לַעֲנִיִּים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזָרְחָה לָכֶם יִרְאֵי שְׁמִי שֶׁמֶשׁ צְדָקָה וּמַרְפֵּא״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ פְּרוּטָה שֶׁבַּכִּיס מִתְבָּרֶכֶת בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָתֵת מְטַר אַרְצְךָ בְּעִתּוֹ וּלְבָרֵךְ אֵת כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Sun on Shabbat is charity for the poor,** who are then able to enjoy the outdoors without suffering from cold. **As it is stated: "But for you who fear My name, the sun of righteousness shall arise with healing in its wings"** (Malachi 3:20). **And Rabbi Yitzḥak** further **said: The day of the rains is great, as even a *peruta* in** one's **pocket is blessed on it, as it is stated: "To give the rain of your land in its due time, and to bless all the work of your hand"** (Deuteronomy 28:12).

Rabbi Yitzchak adds another dimension to this picture: *shemesh b'Shabbat tzedaka le'aniyim* — sunshine on Shabbat is an act of charity and righteousness to the poor. The verse cited is: *vezarcha lachem yirei shemi shemesh tzedaka u'marpe* — and the sun of righteousness will shine for those who fear My name, with healing in its wings. The precise connection to the poor is not entirely spelled out, but Rashi seems to suggest that the poor, who have no field and no outdoor obligations on Shabbat, can simply enjoy a pleasant day. Unlike the landowner who dreads the inconvenience of Friday rain, the poor person can relax in the sun, walk somewhere pleasant, and perhaps even be the beneficiary of others' generosity — as one might put it, a sunny Shabbat puts people in a good mood and they are more inclined to share.

## Bracha Samui

### Hidden Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵין הַבְּרָכָה מְצוּיָה אֶלָּא בְּדָבָר הַסָּמוּי מִן הָעַיִן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְצַו ה׳ אִתְּךָ אֶת הַבְּרָכָה בַּאֲסָמֶיךָ״. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֵין הַבְּרָכָה מְצוּיָה אֶלָּא בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין הָעַיִן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְצַו ה׳ אִתְּךָ אֶת הַבְּרָכָה בַּאֲסָמֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** apropos blessings, **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: A blessing is found only in an object that is hidden [*samui*] from the eye,** not in an item visible to all, as public miracles are exceedingly rare. **As it is stated: "The Lord will command His blessing upon you in your barns [*ba'asamekha*]"** (Deuteronomy 28:8). Rabbi Yitzḥak's exposition is based on the linguistic similarity between *samui* and *asamekha*. Likewise, **the school of Rabbi Ishmael taught: A blessing is found only in an object that is not exposed to the eye, as it is stated: "The Lord will command His blessing upon you in your barns."**

Rabbi Yitzchak continues with one of his most famous teachings: *ein habracha metzuya ela bedavar hasamui min ha'ayin* — blessing is only found in something hidden from the eye. The verse cited is *yetzav Hashem itcha et habracha be'asamecha* — God should command the blessing upon you in your storehouse. The word *be'asamecha*, "in your storehouse," is read as playing on the word *samui*, hidden, concealed from view. The implication is that *bracha* operates beneath the surface; once something is identified, quantified, and publicly visible, it becomes harder for blessing to take hold. This is the same logic underlying the custom of saying *keina hora* — there is a concern that publicizing and pointing out one's good fortune invites the *ayin hara* and removes it from the domain of hidden, growing blessing.

The idea runs deeper than superstition. If God's blessing is to operate, it is more naturally accommodated in a space of ambiguity — where a miraculous augmentation of what one has would not require an obvious violation of the laws of nature. If you count your money and have exactly a thousand dollars, and tomorrow you have two thousand, that is a manifest miracle. But if you never measured, the blessing can work invisibly. The *Tana devei Rabbi Yishmael* states this slightly differently: *ein habracha metzuya ela bedavar she'ein ha'ayin sholetet bo* — blessing is found only where the eye has no dominion. Again, the same verse supports the point.

One might note an interesting counterreading of Rabbi Yitzchak's collected statements. Rather than asserting that God operates through supernatural hidden channels, one could read him as arguing precisely the opposite — that God works through the broad, natural strokes of history and meteorology, and that those who claim to see God's hand being specially revealed in particular events are mistaken. On this reading, the blessing in the granary and the blessing that comes with rain are both thoroughly naturalistic: when rain falls, the entire economy is stimulated, agricultural productivity improves, consumer demand rises, and the macroeconomy benefits — hence even the money in your pocket is blessed. God's blessing works through nature, not in spite of it, and the admonition against measuring is an admonition against claiming that you can isolate and quantify divine intervention.

### Day of Rain

The Gemara further establishes that *gadol yom geshamim*, great is a day of rain — not merely in the metaphorical comparisons we have seen elsewhere, but in terms of the concrete blessing it brings — *she'afilu peruta shebakis mitbarechet bo*, that even the coin in one's pocket is blessed on a day of rain. The verse cited is *latet matar artzecha be'ito u'levarech et kol ma'aseh yadecha* — to give rain to your land in its season and to bless all the work of your hands. The rain is not merely an agricultural event; it is accompanied by a general divine favor that extends to all endeavors. Whether one understands this literally or economically, the point is the same: rain signifies God's connection to the land and His favor to the people, and that favor radiates outward to all of one's activities.

### Measuring Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לָמוֹד אֶת גׇּרְנוֹ, אוֹמֵר: ״יְהִי רָצוֹן מִלְּפָנֶיךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ שֶׁתִּשְׁלַח בְּרָכָה בְּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֵנוּ״. הִתְחִיל לָמוֹד, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ בְּרָכָה בַּכְּרִי הַזֶּה״. מָדַד וְאַחַר כָּךְ בֵּירַךְ — הֲרֵי זוֹ תְּפִלַּת שָׁוְא, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַבְּרָכָה מְצוּיָה לֹא בְּדָבָר הַשָּׁקוּל, וְלֹא בְּדָבָר הַמָּדוּד, וְלֹא בְּדָבָר הַמָּנוּי, אֶלָּא בְּדָבָר הַסָּמוּי מִן הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who enters to measure** produce **in his granary recites: May it be Your will, Lord our God, that You send a blessing upon the work of our hands.** After he has **begun to measure, he recites: Blessed** is He **who sends a blessing upon this pile.** If one first **measured and afterward recited the blessing, it is a prayer** in **vain, as a blessing is not found either in an object that is weighed or in an object that is measured or in an object that is counted,** as these would constitute open miracles. **Rather,** a blessing is found only **in an object that is hidden from the eye.**

A *Tanu Rabbanan* passage spells out the practical implications of the *bracha samui* principle with great clarity. One who enters to measure his granary should say beforehand: *Yehi ratzon milfanecha Hashem Elokeinu shetishlach bracha bema'aseh yadeinu* — may it be Your will, God, to send blessing upon the work of our hands. Once he begins measuring, he may say *Baruch hasholeach bracha bakari hazeh* — blessed is the One who sends His blessing into this mound of grain. But if one has already finished measuring and only then recites a blessing that there should be increase — that is a *tefillat shav*, a futile prayer, *lefi she'ein habracha metzuya lo bedavar hashakul velo bedavar hamadud velo bedavar hamanoi ela bedavar hasamui min ha'ayin* — because blessing is not found in something weighed, measured, or counted, only in something hidden from the eye.

The logic here is precise: before the measurement, God could in principle effect an increase that would not register as a blatant supernatural intervention. Once the granary is measured and the quantity is known, any further increase would be an obvious miracle — a violation of the natural order that God, while capable of performing, does not do. The parallel to the Vilna Gaon's discussion of *tefillat shav* in Berakhot is apt: one who hears of a disaster and prays it did not happen in his house is not praying for the impossible — the Gra holds God *could* change the past — but for something God simply will not do once the event is fixed and known. The principle extends, incidentally, to the prohibition against counting people directly, which connects to the phrase *veyidgu larov* — like fish hidden from the eye, and the general principle that quantifying blessing tends to diminish it.

## Rain Metaphors

### Kibbutz Galuyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִבּוּץ, גְּיָיסוֹת, צְדָקָה, מַעֲשֵׂר, פַּרְנָס סִימָן. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים כְּיוֹם קִבּוּץ גָּלִיּוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוּבָה ה׳ אֶת שְׁבִיתֵנוּ כַּאֲפִיקִים בַּנֶּגֶב״, וְאֵין ״אֲפִיקִים״ אֶלָּא מָטָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֵּרָאוּ אֲפִקֵי יָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites five statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan, in accordance with the following **mnemonic: Ingathering; armies; charity; tithe; sustainer. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The day of the rains is as great as the day of the ingathering of the exiles, as it is stated: "Turn our captivity, O Lord, as the streams in the dry land"** (Psalms 126:4), **and "streams"** means **nothing other** than **rain, as it is stated: "And the streams of the sea appeared"** (II Samuel 22:16).

The Gemara introduces here a *siman* — a mnemonic device — for the series of statements that follows: *kibbutz, geshamim, tzedaka, parnasa*. The first: Rav Yochanan teaches that *gadol yom hageshamim k'yom kibbutz galuyot* — great is a day of rain like the day of the ingathering of the exiles. The verse cited is *shuva Hashem et sheviteinu k'afikim baNegev* — return our captivity, God, like the *afikim* of the Negev. The *afikim* are identified as rain, supported by the verse *vayera'u afikei yam* — the streams of the ocean were seen. Rashi understands *afikei yam* as *motzai yam*, the outflows of the sea. More simply, when rain falls in the Negev and rushes into the dry *wadis*, it creates sudden, powerful flash floods — the *nachal* which is bone dry becomes a roaring torrent in moments. The comparison is apt: the return of the exiles, like water pouring into a desert *wadi*, is sudden, overwhelming, and transformative.

### Armies Rest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ גְּיָיסוֹת פּוֹסְקוֹת בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּלָמֶיהָ רַוֵּה נַחֵת גְּדוּדֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The day of the rains is great, as even armies stop fighting on it** due to the rain and mud. **As it is stated: "Watering its ridges abundantly; settling down its furrows [*gedudeha*]"** (Psalms 65:11).

Rav Yochanan adds: *gadol yom hageshamim she'afilu gaysot paskot bo* — great is a day of rain in that even armies cease their activities on that day. Whether this means they stop marching, stop fighting, or simply rest, the verse supports the reading: *tlameha ravvei nachat gdudeha* — you have saturated its furrows, and its troops have rested. The *gdudim* here, the "troops," may also be read as referring to the riverbanks — when the rain saturates the furrows, even the armies are stilled.

### Poskei Tzedaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הַגְּשָׁמִים נֶעֱצָרִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל פּוֹסְקֵי צְדָקָה בָּרַבִּים וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נְשִׂיאִים וְרוּחַ וְגֶשֶׁם אָיִן אִישׁ מִתְהַלֵּל בְּמַתַּת שָׁקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** further **said: Rain is withheld only due to those who pledge charity in public but do not give** it, **as it is stated: "As vapors and wind without rain, so is he who boasts of a false gift"** (Proverbs 25:14).

Rav Yochanan further teaches that *ein hageshamim ne'etzarim ela bishvil poskei tzedakah barabbim v'ein notnim* — rain is withheld only on account of those who publicly pledge charity and then fail to fulfill their pledge. The verse cited is *nesi'im veruach vegeshem ayin, ish mithallel bematat shaker* — clouds and wind but no rain: a man boasting of gifts he will never give. The parallel is precise and direct: the *poskei tzedakah*, those who make public promises of charity, are like clouds that promise rain. There is wind, there are clouds, there are all the signs of a great downpour — but the rain never comes. The lack of rain is thus a *middah keneged middah* reflection of the lack of follow-through in human generosity.

## Aser Te'aser

### Tithes Wealth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר״ — עַשֵּׂר בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּתְעַשֵּׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "A tithe shall you tithe [*te'aser*]"** (Deuteronomy 14:22)? This phrase can be interpreted homiletically: **Take a tithe [*asser*] so that you will become wealthy [*titasher*],** in the merit of the mitzva.

Rav Yochanan expounds the verse *aser te'aser* — you shall surely tithe — as a play on words: *aser bishvil shetit'asher*, give tithes in order that you shall become wealthy. The doubling of the root *ayin-shin-reish* encodes both the obligation and the reward: tithe and you will become rich. This sits within the broader framework the Gemara has been developing — the correspondence between human action, particularly in giving to God and to others, and God's response in the form of material blessing. Whereas the earlier discussions made this correspondence through the metaphor of rain, here the equation is stated directly and without meteorological mediation.

It is worth noting what is implied by removing rain from the equation. Rather than focusing on the symbolic and religious resonance of rain, the discussion pivots to the bottom line: give your ten percent and you will get more back. This framing makes the principle immediately practical and also more easily generalizable beyond agriculture.

### Test God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְיָנוֹקָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִימָּא לִי פְּסוּקָיךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמַאי ״עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַשֵּׂר בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּתְעַשֵּׁר, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל נַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan found** the **young son of Reish Lakish. He said to** the boy: **Recite to me your verse,** i.e., the verse you studied today in school. The boy **said to him: "A tithe shall you tithe."** The boy further **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **But** what is the meaning of this phrase: **"A tithe shall you tithe"?** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** The verse means: **Take a tithe so that you will become wealthy.** The boy **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **From where do you** derive that this is so? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Go** and **test** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִי שְׁרֵי לְנַסּוֹיֵיהּ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תְנַסּוּ אֶת ה׳״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: חוּץ מִזּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָבִיאוּ אֶת כׇּל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר אֶל בֵּית הָאוֹצָר וִיהִי טֶרֶף בְּבֵיתִי וּבְחָנוּנִי נָא בָּזֹאת אָמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אִם לֹא אֶפְתַּח לָכֶם אֵת אֲרֻבּוֹת הַשָּׁמַיִם וַהֲרִיקֹתִי לָכֶם בְּרָכָה עַד בְּלִי דָי״.
>
> **Translation:** The boy **said to him: And is it permitted to test the Holy One, Blessed be He? But isn't it written: "You shall not test the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 6:16)? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** the boy that **Rabbi Hoshaya said as follows:** It is prohibited to test God in any way, **except in this** case of tithes, **as it is stated: "Bring the whole tithe into the storeroom, that there may be food in My house, and test Me now by this, said the Lord of hosts, if I will not open for you the windows of heaven, and pour out for you a blessing that there shall be more than sufficiency"** (Malachi 3:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עַד בְּלִי דָי״? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב: עַד שֶׁיִּבְלוּ שִׂפְתוֹתֵיכֶם מִלּוֹמַר ״דַּי״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוָת מָטֵי הָתָם לְהַאי פְּסוּקָא, לָא הֲוֵית צְרִיכְנָא לָךְ וּלְהוֹשַׁעְיָא רַבָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** In relation to the above verse, the Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"That there shall be more than sufficiency [*ad beli dai*]"? Rami bar Ḥama said** that **Rav said:** It means that the abundance will be so great **that your lips will be worn out [*yivlu*],** similar to the word *beli*, **from saying enough [*dai*].** Returning to the above incident, the Gemara adds that the boy **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: Your claim appears explicitly in a verse. **If I had arrived there,** at this verse, **I would not have needed you or Hoshaya your teacher,** as I could have understood it on my own.

The Gemara here presents a charming and instructive exchange. Rav Yochanan encounters the young son of Reish Lakish — who was also Rav Yochanan's nephew, since Reish Lakish had married Rav Yochanan's sister. Reish Lakish had already died by this point. Rav Yochanan asks the child: tell me the verse you are learning. The child responds: *aser te'aser* — I am learning the verse about tithes. Rav Yochanan explains the play on words: *aser bishvil shetit'asher* — give tithes in order to become wealthy. The child pushes back: how do you know? That is a nice *drash*, but what is the proof it actually works? Rav Yochanan answers simply: go ahead, try it — *zil nasei*.

The child's response is sharp: is it permissible to test God? Does not the verse say *lo tenasu et Hashem* — do not test God? Rav Yochanan replies in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: this is the one exception — *chutz mizo*. The verse in Malachi states explicitly: *havi'u et kol hama'aser el beit ha'otzar... uvchanuni na bazot amar Hashem Tzeva'ot im lo eftach lachem et arubot hashamayim vaharicoti lachem bracha ad bli dai* — bring all the tithes to the storehouse, test Me in this, says the Lord of Hosts, and I will open the windows of heaven and pour out blessing without limit. God Himself invites the test in this single instance.

What does *ad bli dai* — blessing without limit — mean? Ami bar Chama says in the name of Rav: *ad sheyivlu siftoteichem milomar dai* — until your lips grow weary from saying "enough." There will be so much blessing that you will exhaust yourself declaring it sufficient.

The child then delivers a pointed response: had I already reached that passage in Malachi in my learning, I would not have needed you or your teacher Rabbi Oshaya to derive it. The *peshat* of the verse says it outright. One person might read this as intellectual enthusiasm — here is a child so engaged in *Torah* that he confidently engages his uncle's scholarship. Another might read it as somewhat sharp, even *chutzpadik*. As will become clear shortly, this child may carry some emotional baggage toward his uncle that colors the exchange.

The passage about *aser te'aser* leads naturally into a broader discussion of the medieval institution of *maaser kesafim* — tithing one's income — which Tosafot cites in the name of the *Sifrei*, though this passage does not appear in the *Sifrei* we have. The *Sifrei* as quoted by Tosafot derives from the words *et kol* — "all of your tithe" — an obligation that extends beyond grain to include *ribbit u'prakmatya vechol davar shemarviach bo* — interest, commerce, and all other income-generating activities. A clear sign that this is a medieval formulation rather than a tannaic one is the very first item in the list: *ribbit*, charging interest. The practice of Jews making money through *ribbit* was essentially a medieval phenomenon. This passage does not appear in any *Sifrei* we possess, and while some *Rishonei Ashkenaz* argued that *maaser kesafim* has biblical or rabbinic force, the overwhelming consensus is that it is a *minhag*. Nevertheless, it represents a remarkable act of halakhic creativity — the transference of a central agricultural and theological obligation into a context where the original framework no longer applies: neither residence in *Eretz Yisrael* nor an agrarian economy. Aggadic statements about the consequences of giving and withholding *maaser* — that it enriches and impoverishes — serve as a powerful motivational framework for observing a practice that, technically speaking, carries no formal *halachic* obligation.

### Mishlei Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְיָנוֹקֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּיָתֵיב וְאָמַר: ״אִוֶּלֶת אָדָם תְּסַלֵּף דַּרְכּוֹ וְעַל ה׳ יִזְעַף לִבּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another story about the precociousness of this child. **And furthermore,** on a different occasion **Rabbi Yoḥanan found** the **young son of Reish Lakish, when he was sitting** and studying **and** he was **reciting** the verse: **"The foolishness of man perverts his way, and his heart frets against the Lord"** (Proverbs 19:3).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְקָא מַתְמַהּ. אָמַר: מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דִּכְתִיבִי בִּכְתוּבֵי דְּלָא רְמִיזִי בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? אָמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ הָא מִי לָא רְמִיזִי, וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּצֵא לִבָּם וַיֶּחֶרְדוּ אִישׁ אֶל אָחִיו לֵאמֹר מַה זֹּאת עָשָׂה אֱלֹהִים לָנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan sat down and wondered** aloud about this verse, **saying: Is there anything that is written in the Writings that is not alluded to in the Torah** at all? I cannot think of any hint of this idea in the Torah itself. The child **said to him: Is that to say** that **this** idea is really **not alluded to** in the Torah? **But isn't it written,** with regard to Joseph's brothers: **"And their heart failed them and they turned trembling to one to another, saying: What is this that God has done to us?"** (Genesis 42:28).

On another occasion, Rav Yochanan encounters Reish Lakish's son sitting and reciting the verse: *ivelet adam tesalef darko, ve'al Hashem yiza'af libo* — the foolishness of a man perverts his own path, yet he directs his anger at God. Rav Yochanan is struck by this and sits wondering: is there anything written in the *Kesuvim* that is not already hinted to in the *Torah*? Before Rav Yochanan can answer, the child responds for him: of course this is hinted to in the *Torah* — the verse says *vayeitzei libam vayechredu ish el achiv leimor mah zot asah Elokim lanu* — the brothers' hearts sank and they trembled, saying to one another: what has God done to us? Here are the brothers, suffering the consequences of their own act of selling Yosef, and yet directing their distress at God — precisely the dynamic described in Mishlei.

## Rebi Student

### Reish Lakish Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּל עֵינֵיהּ וַחֲזָא בֵּיהּ, אָתְיָא אִימֵּיהּ אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: תָּא מִקַּמֵּיהּ, דְּלָא לֶיעְבַּד לָךְ כְּדַעֲבַד לַאֲבוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Impressed by the youth's wisdom, Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised his eyes and stared at the boy.** At this point, the boy's **mother came and took him away, saying to him: Come away from** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **so that he does not do to you as he did to your father.**

Rav Yochanan lifts his eyes and looks at the boy. His mother — Rav Yochanan's sister and Reish Lakish's widow — comes and takes the child away, saying to him: *ta mikamei delo le'avid lach k'da'avad le'avuch* — go away from him, lest he do to you what he did to your father. There is a well-known narrative in which Rav Yochanan directed a look at Reish Lakish that caused Reish Lakish's death. Rav Yochanan, evidently displeased by the child's sharp responses, gave the boy such a look — and the mother, recognizing what this could mean, intervened to remove her son from danger.

This episode illuminates the earlier exchange about *aser te'aser* in a new light. The boy's confident, even confrontational tone toward his uncle — *I wouldn't have needed you or your teacher to figure that out* — may reflect not merely intellectual enthusiasm but genuine emotional charge. A child whose mother believes his uncle killed his father can hardly be expected to be uniformly deferential. And yet the mother herself evidently felt her son had crossed a line, since she rushed to remove him before the consequences became fatal.

The Gemara here places this story in a broader thematic context that has been developing throughout the daf: the relationship between a *rebbi* and a student, and what happens when that relationship is strained. What is striking is the contrast with how God relates to human beings through rain. On the one hand, God — as we have seen — will bring rain even for the sake of a single individual, is infinitely precise in His provision, and responds with abundant blessing to even minimal human gestures like giving *maaser*. On the other hand, human teachers — Rav Yochanan looking at Reish Lakish, and now at Reish Lakish's son — respond to perceived disrespect with devastating and fatal consequence. If there is an implicit theological argument being made here, it may be that God's relationship to human beings is characterized by a compassion and patience that human relationships, even among the greatest sages, cannot always approximate.

## Yachid vs. Rabbim

### Individual Rain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָטָר בִּשְׁבִיל יָחִיד, פַּרְנָסָה בִּשְׁבִיל רַבִּים. מָטָר בִּשְׁבִיל יָחִיד, דִּכְתִיב: ״יִפְתַּח ה׳ לְךָ אֶת אוֹצָרוֹ הַטּוֹב לָתֵת מְטַר אַרְצְךָ״. פַּרְנָסָה בִּשְׁבִיל רַבִּים, דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנְנִי מַמְטִיר לָכֶם לֶחֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** § After this brief digression, the Gemara turns to the fifth in the series of statements by Rabbi Yoḥanan concerning rain. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rain** falls even **for the sake of an individual,** in response to the petition of a single person in need of rain, whereas a blessing of **sustenance** comes only **for the sake of many. Rain** falls even **for the sake of an individual, as it is written: "The Lord will open for you His good treasure,** the heavens, **to give the rain of your land"** (Deuteronomy 28:12). The fact that this verse is written in the second person singular demonstrates that rain can fall even for the sake of an individual. Rabbi Yoḥanan further proves that **sustenance** comes **for the sake of many, as it is written: "Behold I will cause to rain bread from the heavens for you"** (Exodus 16:4). Here, God is referring to the people in the plural form.

Rav Yochanan teaches: *matar bishvil yachid, parnasa bishvil rabbim* — God will bring rain even for the sake of a single individual, but general *parnasa* — broad sustenance and economic wellbeing — only comes when the masses deserve it. Rain for an individual: the verse says *yiftach Hashem lecha et otzaro hatov latet matar artzecha* — God shall open for you, in the singular, His good storehouse, to give the rain of your land. *Parnasa* for the masses: the verse says *hineni mamtir lachem lechem min hashamayim* — I will rain down for you, in the plural, bread from the heavens.

This distinction is notable in the context of the larger tractate. Taanit is fundamentally about communal fasting in response to the withholding of rain — rain as a communal concern, its absence as a communal affliction. Yet here we see that the positive dimension of rain — rain actually falling — can be for the merit of a single individual. A recurrent theme in the tractate, one that will be developed further, is the role of *yechidim*, exceptional individuals like Choni HaMa'agel, who pray for rain and are answered even before the community's formal prayers take effect.

### Three Providers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה פַּרְנָסִים טוֹבִים עָמְדוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֵלּוּ הֵן: מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּמִרְיָם. וְשָׁלֹשׁ מַתָּנוֹת טוֹבוֹת נִיתְּנוּ עַל יָדָם, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: בְּאֵר, וְעָנָן, וּמָן. בְּאֵר — בִּזְכוּת מִרְיָם, עַמּוּד עָנָן — בִּזְכוּת אַהֲרֹן, מָן — בִּזְכוּת מֹשֶׁה. מֵתָה מִרְיָם — נִסְתַּלֵּק הַבְּאֵר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתָּמׇת שָׁם מִרְיָם״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וְלֹא הָיָה מַיִם לָעֵדָה״, וְחָזְרָה בִּזְכוּת שְׁנֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Three good sustainers rose up for the Jewish people** during the exodus from Egypt, and **they are: Moses, Aaron and Miriam. And three good gifts were given** from Heaven **through their agency, and these are they:** The **well** of water, the pillar of **cloud, and** the **manna.** He elaborates: The **well** was given to the Jewish people **in the merit of Miriam;** the **pillar of cloud** was **in the merit of Aaron;** and the **manna in the merit of Moses.** When **Miriam died** the **well disappeared, as it is stated: "And Miriam died there"** (Numbers 20:1), **and it says thereafter** in the next verse: **"And there was no water for the congregation"** (Numbers 20:2). **But** the well **returned in the merit of both** Moses and Aaron.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת אַהֲרֹן — נִסְתַּלְּקוּ עַנְנֵי כָּבוֹד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע הַכְּנַעֲנִי מֶלֶךְ עֲרָד״, מָה שְׁמוּעָה שָׁמַע? שָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּת אַהֲרֹן וְנִסְתַּלְּקוּ עַנְנֵי כָּבוֹד, וּכְסָבוּר נִיתְּנָה לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְהִלָּחֵם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּרְאוּ כׇּל הָעֵדָה כִּי גָוַע אַהֲרֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** When **Aaron died** the **clouds of glory disappeared, as it is stated: "And the Canaanite, the king of Arad heard"** (Numbers 33:40). **What report did he hear? He heard that Aaron had died and the clouds of glory had disappeared, and he thought that** the Jewish people were no longer protected by Heaven and therefore **he had been given permission to go to war against the Jewish people. And this** disappearance of the clouds **is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And all the congregation saw that [*ki*] Aaron was dead"** (Numbers 20:29).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״וַיִּרְאוּ״, אֶלָּא ״וַיִּירָאוּ״. כִּדְדָרֵישׁ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״כִּי״ מְשַׁמֵּשׁ בְּאַרְבַּע לְשׁוֹנוֹת: אִי, דִּלְמָא, אֶלָּא, דְּהָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: Do not read** the verse as: **"And they saw [*va'yiru*]"; rather,** read it as: **And they were seen [*va'yera'u*],** as the clouds which had concealed the Jewish people were temporarily removed. This is **as Reish Lakish taught. As Reish Lakish said:** The term ***ki*** actually **has** at least **four** distinct **meanings: If; perhaps; but; because,** or that. According to this interpretation, the verse would be rendered: And all the congregation was seen, because [*ki*] Aaron was dead.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם בִּזְכוּת מֹשֶׁה, מֵת מֹשֶׁה נִסְתַּלְּקוּ כּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאַכְחִד אֶת שְׁלֹשֶׁת הָרֹעִים בְּיֶרַח אֶחָד״, וְכִי בְּיֶרַח אֶחָד מֵתוּ? וַהֲלֹא מִרְיָם מֵתָה בְּנִיסָן, וְאַהֲרֹן בְּאָב, וּמֹשֶׁה בַּאֲדָר! אֶלָּא, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּתְבַּטְּלוּ שָׁלֹשׁ מַתָּנוֹת טוֹבוֹת שֶׁנִּתְּנוּ עַל יָדָם, וְנִסְתַּלְּקוּ כּוּלָּן בְּיֶרַח אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Both** the well and the clouds of glory **returned in the merit of Moses.** However, when **Moses died all of them disappeared. As it is stated: "And I cut off the three shepherds in one month"** (Zechariah 11:8). **But did** the three shepherds really **die in one month? Didn't Miriam die in** the month of **Nisan, and Aaron in Av and Moses in Adar? Rather,** this verse **teaches** us **that** with the death of Moses the **three good gifts that were given through their agency were annulled, and** all three gifts **disappeared in one month,** which made it seem as though all three leaders had died at the same time.

A *Beraita* is cited as a challenge to this principle. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda teaches that three great leaders — *parnasim* — stood up for the Jewish people: Moshe, Aharon, and Miriam. Three great gifts were given through them: the *be'er*, the well, in the merit of Miriam; the *amud anan*, the pillar of cloud, in the merit of Aharon; and the *man*, the manna, in the merit of Moshe.

When Miriam died, the well disappeared — as inferred from the juxtaposition: *vatamot sham Miriam* followed immediately by the verse stating there was no water for the congregation. The connection of Miriam to water runs deep throughout the *Torah*, and her association with the nurturing, life-giving aspects of water is fitting. The well returned in the merit of the two remaining leaders — Moshe and Aharon — as evidenced by God's command to both of them to speak to the rock and bring forth water.

When Aharon died, the *ananei hakavod*, the clouds of glory, departed. This is derived from the verse *vayishma HaKena'ani Melech Arad* — the Canaanite king of Arad heard something and came to attack. What did he hear? That Aharon had died and the clouds had departed, leaving Israel exposed and apparently unprotected. Rabbi Abbahu adds: do not read *vayiru* — they saw that Aharon had died — but *vayyere'u* — they became visible, they were exposed, *because Aharon had died*, employing the principle that *ki* can carry the meaning of "because" (*deha*), one of its four usages as enumerated by Reish Lakish.

When Moshe died, all three gifts ceased simultaneously. The verse *va'achchid shloshet haro'im beyerach echad* — I will destroy the three shepherds in one month — is cited to confirm this. The apparent difficulty is that Miriam died in Nisan, Aharon in Av, and Moshe in Adar — hardly one month. The resolution: all three *gifts* ceased in a single month. This implies that Moshe had restored all three gifts — the well, the clouds, and the manna — during his lifetime, so when he died, all three disappeared at once.

### Moshe Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אַשְׁכְּחַן פַּרְנָסָה בִּשְׁבִיל יָחִיד! שָׁאנֵי מֹשֶׁה, כֵּיוָן דִּלְרַבִּים הוּא בָּעֵי — כְּרַבִּים דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty from this *baraita*. **Apparently, we find** that **sustenance** can come **for the sake of an individual,** as the *baraita* states that sustenance in the form of manna came for the sake of Moses. The Gemara answers: **Moses is different, since he requested** the manna **for many,** and therefore he was considered **like many,** not as an individual.

The relevance of this *Beraita* to Rav Yochanan's teaching becomes clear. If *parnasa* only comes for the *rabbim*, how can we account for the fact that the *man* came in the merit of Moshe — a single individual? The Gemara resolves: *shani Moshe, kivan delarabim hu ba'ei kerabim dami* — Moshe is different, because he is needed for the masses, he is considered equivalent to the masses. A leader of Moshe's stature is, in a legal and theological sense, coextensive with the community he leads. Rain or *parnasa* granted in his merit is therefore effectively granted for the *rabbim*. The principle of *yachid* applying to rain thus holds: for an individual of non-extraordinary standing, God will still bring rain, but general *parnasa* requires communal merit.

## Rebi Student (II)

### Three Shepherds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ וְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אִידִי וְרַב חִיָּיא מִוּוֹסְתַּנְיָא הֲווֹ שְׁכִיחִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, כׇּל אֵימַת דַּהֲוָה אָמַר לְהוּ שְׁמַעְתָּא וְלָא הֲוָה מִסְתַּבְּרָא לְהוּ, הֲווֹ מְרַמְּזִי אַהֲדָדֵי. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a story concerning the aforementioned verse from Zechariah. **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ, Rav Shmuel bar Idi, and Rav Ḥiyya from Vastanya were** often **found before Rava,** as they were among his most distinguished students. **When Rava died, they came before Rav Pappa** to learn from him. However, as also they were great Sages, **whenever Rav Pappa would say a *halakha* that did not** sound **reasonable to them, they would gesture to each other** that Rav Pappa was not equal in stature to Rava. Rav Pappa **was offended** by their behavior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַקְרְיוּהּ בְּחֶלְמֵיהּ: ״וָאַכְחִד אֶת שְׁלֹשֶׁת הָרֹעִים״. לִמְחַר, כִּי הֲווֹ מִיפַּטְרִי מִינֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לֵיזְלוּ רַבָּנַן בִּשְׁלָמָא.
>
> **Translation:** A verse **was read to** Rav Pappa **in a dream: "And I cut off the three shepherds in one month"** (Zechariah 11:8). **The next day, when they took their leave from him,** Rav Pappa **said to them: May the Rabbis go in peace,** a hint that this would be their final parting. Rav Pappa thought that the three Sages would die as punishment for their behavior and he would never see them again.

The Gemara now revisits the verse *va'achchid shloshet haro'im beyerach echad* in a strikingly different context — not to discuss the three leaders of Israel in the desert, but to frame an episode involving Rav Papa and three of his students. Rav Huna bar Manoach, Rav Shmuel bar Idi, and Rav Chiyya miVastanya had studied regularly before Rava. After Rava died, they came to learn from Rav Papa. Every time Rav Papa would teach a *sugya*, and it was not coming across clearly or sharply to them, they would signal one another — a look exchanged that communicated wordlessly: this is not what it was like with Rava. The unspoken comparison was devastating.

Rav Papa became deeply despondent — *chalash datei* — weighed down by the repeated experience of not measuring up in the eyes of these students. Then, in a dream, the verse was read to him: *va'achchid shloshet haro'im* — I will destroy the three shepherds. The heavenly message, whatever its exact theological valence, was that these three students would be removed. The next morning, when they took their leave, Rav Papa said: *leizil rabanan beshalma* — the rabbis should go in peace. Whether this constitutes a blessing or simply a farewell is debated. A marginal note suggests Rav Papa blessed them to protect them from the consequences implied by his dream; others find this reading overly apologetic, and the plain sense is a certain resignation to the outcome.

The episode reinforces a pattern visible throughout the daf: the rabbi-student relationship, when the student's behavior tilts toward disrespect or condescension, leads to severe and fatal results. Human relationships among the greatest sages operate with a kind of hair-trigger sensitivity that God's relationship to human beings — expressed through the patient, individual-level provision of rain — does not.

### Shimi Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי הֲוָה שְׁכִיחַ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, הֲוָה מַקְשֵׁי לֵיהּ טוּבָא. יוֹמָא חַד חַזְיֵיהּ דִּנְפַל עַל אַפֵּיהּ, שַׁמְעֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: רַחֲמָנָא לִיצְּלַן מִכִּיסּוּפָא דְשִׁימִי. קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ שְׁתִיקוּתָא, וְתוּ לָא אַקְשִׁי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: **Rav Shimi bar Ashi was** often **found before Rav Pappa** and **would raise many objections against** the opinions of Rav Pappa. **One day** Rav Shimi bar Ashi saw Rav Pappa **fall on his face** after prayer. **He heard him say: May the Merciful One save me from the embarrassment of Shimi, and** as a result Rav Shimi bar Ashi **resolved to be silent and not to raise any further objections** against Rav Pappa, as he saw how greatly they pained his teacher.

A related episode involving Rav Shimi bar Ashi provides a cautionary counterpoint. Rav Shimi bar Ashi regularly studied before Rav Papa and regularly posed sharp *kashyot* against him — question after strong question. One day, Rav Shimi saw Rav Papa fall on his face — visibly overwhelmed — and overheard him say: *Rachmana litzlan mikisufa deShimi* — God save us from the embarrassment of Shimi. Rav Shimi now understood precisely what it could mean for God to save Rav Papa from such embarrassment. He immediately took upon himself a vow of silence — *kibeil alei shitikusa* — and asked no more questions. He chose to suppress his intellectual engagement entirely rather than risk what his questions might bring upon him.

The juxtaposition of these two stories makes the stakes vivid. Students who signal their contempt for their teacher — even without words — face elimination. A student who recognizes what his sharp questions are doing to his teacher chooses silence as the only safe path. In both cases, the relationship between *rebbi* and *talmid* is portrayed as one of extraordinary power imbalance, where the teacher's dignity must be guarded at enormous personal cost.

## Precise Rain

### One Blade Grass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סָבַר מָטָר בִּשְׁבִיל יָחִיד, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִנַּיִן לַמָּטָר בִּשְׁבִיל יָחִיד, דִּכְתִיב: ״שַׁאֲלוּ מֵה׳ מָטָר בְּעֵת מַלְקוֹשׁ ה׳ עֹשֶׂה חֲזִיזִים וּמְטַר גֶּשֶׁם יִתֵּן לָהֶם לְאִישׁ עֵשֶׂב בַּשָּׂדֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of rain. **And Reish Lakish also maintains** that **rain** can fall **for the sake of an individual, as Reish Lakish said: From where** is it derived **that rain** falls even **for the sake of an individual? As it is written: "Ask of the Lord rain at the time of the last rain; even of the Lord who makes thunderclouds, and He will give them showers of rain; for a man grass in the field"** (Zechariah 10:1).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לַכֹּל — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לְאִישׁ״. וְתַנְיָא: אִי לְאִישׁ, יָכוֹל לְכׇל שְׂדוֹתָיו — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שָׂדֶה״. אִי שָׂדֶה, יָכוֹל לְכׇל הַשָּׂדֶה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֵשֶׂב״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that rain falls **for** the sake of **all** the Jewish people. **The verse** therefore **states: "For a man,"** i.e., for the needs of an individual **And it was** further **taught** in a *baraita*: **If** rain falls **"for a man,"** one **might** have thought that the rain is **for all his fields.** Therefore **the verse states: "Field,"** which indicates that at times it rains on only one field. **If** it rains on one **field,** one **might** have thought that the rain is **for the entire field.** This is why **the verse states: "Grass";** rain can fall for the sake of even a single plant in the field.

The Gemara returns to the theme of divine precision in rainfall. Reish Lakish derives from the verse *sha'alu meHashem matar b'eis malkosh, Hashem oseh chazizim u'matar geshem yitein lahem l'ish eisev basadeh* — ask God for rain in the season of the late rains; God makes *chazizim* and gives rain; He gives to each man *eisev* in the field — that rain can come even for a single individual. The word *l'ish* — to a man, in the singular — establishes this.

A *Beraita* then refines the point further. Perhaps *l'ish* means all of his fields — all of one individual's land will receive rain even if the community does not deserve it? No: *talmud lomar sadeh* — a single field. Perhaps the whole field? *Talmud lomar eisev* — even a single blade of grass. The rain can be precise to the level of a single blade of grass. This is not simply fanciful aggadah; it reflects the experiential reality that rain can be highly localized, that one area receives rainfall while an adjacent area does not, that a cloud can drop its water on a very specific patch of ground. Theologically interpreted, this means God's providence in rain is not broad and indiscriminate but exquisitely calibrated to need.

### Garden Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב דָּנִיאֵל בַּר קַטִּינָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהִיא גִּינְּתָא. כׇּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה אָזֵיל וְסָיַיר לַהּ. אָמַר: הָא מֵישְׁרָא בָּעֲיָא מַיָּא, וְהָא מֵישְׁרָא לָא בָּעֲיָא מַיָּא. וַאֲתָא מִיטְרָא וְקָמַשְׁקֵי כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּמִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ מַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like** the practice **of Rav Daniel bar Ketina,** who **had a certain garden. Every day he would go and inspect it,** to see what it needed. He would **say: This bed requires water and this bed does not require water, and rain would come and water everywhere that required water,** but nowhere else.

A vivid illustration is provided by the story of Rav Daniel bar Katina. He had a garden, and every day he would walk through it and inspect each furrow: *hai mishra ba'ei maya* — this bed needs water; *veha mishra lo ba'ei maya* — this bed does not. Then the rain would come and water exactly those places that needed water and none of those that did not. What is remarkable about this account is the confidence embedded in it: Rav Daniel would make his assessment, and the rain would follow accordingly, as though responding to his survey. The image suggests something beyond mere meteorological coincidence — a man so attuned to his land and to divine providence that his judgment and God's provision move in alignment.

### Chazizim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״ה׳ עֹשֶׂה חֲזִיזִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל צַדִּיק הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ חֲזִיז בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. מַאי ״חֲזִיזִים״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: פּוֹרְחוֹת. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סִימָן לַמָּטָר — פּוֹרְחוֹת. מַאי פּוֹרְחוֹת? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עֵיבָא קְלִישָׁא תּוּתֵי עֵיבָא סְמִיכְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the aforementioned verse, the Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"The Lord** Who **makes thunderclouds"? Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** This **teaches** that for **every righteous person, the Holy One, Blessed be He, prepares a separate thundercloud.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the precise meaning of **"thunderclouds"? Rav Yehuda said: Flying** clouds. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A sign of** approaching **rain is flying** clouds. The Gemara asks: **What** are **flying** clouds? **Rav Pappa said:** A flying cloud is **a thin cloud under a thick cloud.**

The Gemara now asks: what are the *chazizim* mentioned in the verse? Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina explains: the verse teaches that for every individual *tzaddik*, God makes a distinct, individual *chaziz* — a particular flash, a particular sign. Rain is not simply God's general gift to the world at large; within the rain, each righteous individual has something addressed specifically to him. This is the fullest expression of *hashgacha pratis* in the context of rainfall: not only can rain come for a single individual, but within any rain event, each *tzaddik* receives something tailored to him alone.

## Rain Signs

### Porchot Drizzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: נְהִילָא מִקַּמֵּי מִיטְרָא — אָתֵי מִיטְרָא. בָּתַר מִיטְרָא — פָּסֵיק מִיטְרָא. מִקַּמֵּי מִיטְרָא — אָתֵי מִיטְרָא. וְסִימָנָיךְ: מַהוֹלְתָּא. דְּבָתַר מִיטְרָא — פָּסֵיק מִיטְרָא. וְסִימָנָיךְ: חַרְיָא דְעִיזֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that there is another sign of imminent rain: **Drizzle before rain** means that **rain is coming.** However, drizzle that falls **after rain** is a sign that the **rain is stopping.** Rav Yehuda provides an analogy by way of explanation: Drizzle **before rain** means that **rain is coming, and your mnemonic is a sieve:** Just as small quantities of flour drop from the sieve even before one begins to actively sift it, so too drizzle falls before rain. Conversely, drizzle **that** falls **after rain** signifies that the **rain is stopping, and your mnemonic is goat dung.** A goat's initial droppings are large, whereas its latter droppings are small.

Rav Yehuda teaches that the *chazizim* in the verse are *porchos* — thin clouds. Rav Papa explains: *eiva klisha tusei eiva smikta* — a thin, wispy cloud beneath a thick, heavy cloud. This formation is a sign of significant rainfall to come.

Rav Yehuda adds a related observation about *nehila* — a drizzle, a thin rain. A drizzle before the main rain comes — *mikmi matra ati matra* — is a sign that the rain will be good and substantial, like a sieve where the fine material passes through first before the heavier substance follows. A drizzle after the rain — *basar matra pasik matra* — means the rain is about to stop, like the diminishing output at the end of a goat's excretion (the Gemara's rather earthy comparison). In each case, the *porchot* or *nehila* serve as interpretive signals about what the rain will do.

### Ulla Bavel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבָבֶל, חֲזָא פּוֹרְחוֹת, אֲמַר לְהוּ: פַּנּוּ מָאנֵי, דְּהַשְׁתָּא אָתֵי מִיטְרָא. לְסוֹף לָא אָתֵי מִיטְרָא. אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּמְשַׁקְּרִי בַּבְלָאֵי, הָכִי מְשַׁקְּרִי מִיטְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that when **Ulla happened to** come **to Babylonia, he saw flying** clouds. **He said to** the local residents: **Put away** your **vessels, as** the **rain is coming now. Ultimately,** despite the presence of flying clouds, **rain did not fall. He said: Just as Babylonians are liars, so too, their rains are liars,** as flying clouds are a reliable sign of rain in Eretz Yisrael, but not in Babylonia.

Ulla once came to Bavel and observed these *porchos* — either the thin clouds or the drizzle. He told the people around him: *pnu manei de'hashta ati matra* — clear out your vessels, bring everything indoors, a heavy rain is about to fall. But the rain did not come. Ulla's response was pointed: *ke'eichi de'meshakrei Bavla'ei hachi meshakrei matra'ei* — just as the Babylonians are liars, so too their rain is a liar. The signs that portend rain in one place apparently do not operate reliably in another. But one might also note, as the Gemara tacitly invites us to, that it is rather more convenient to blame the rain — or the people — than to simply acknowledge a misprediction.

The Gemara then draws an explicit parallel: just as the *poskei tzedakah* who publicly promise charity and never deliver are like clouds that promise rain and produce nothing, so too Ulla's experience in Bavel — all the signs of rain and none of the substance — mirrors the character he perceives in its inhabitants.

### Dates Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבָבֶל, חָזֵי מְלָא צַנָּא דְתַמְרֵי בְּזוּזָא. אֲמַר: מְלָא צַנָּא דְּדוּבְשָׁא בְּזוּזָא, וּבַבְלָאֵי לָא עָסְקִי בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? בְּלֵילְיָא צַעֲרוּהוּ, אָמַר: מְלָא צַנָּא דְסַכִּינֵי בְּזוּזָא, וּבַבְלָאֵי עָסְקִי בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts another incident that occurred when **Ulla happened to** come **to Babylonia.** He **saw a basket full of dates** on sale **for** one **dinar, and** he said: One can buy **a basket full of honey dates for a dinar, and** yet these **Babylonians do not occupy themselves with Torah.** In a place where excellent food is so inexpensive, and where there is no need to engage in hard labor for one's material needs, the inhabitants should be able to occupy themselves with Torah. Ulla himself ate many dates, but **during the night they caused him pain** and diarrhea. **He** subsequently **said: A basket full of knives for a dinar, and** yet somehow these **Babylonians** are able to **occupy themselves with Torah,** despite the trouble these dates cause.

A second episode involving Ulla in Bavel: he saw a basket full of dates for sale for only a *zuz* — a remarkably low price. He was astonished at the abundance and cheapness of food: *mala tana d'duvsha b'zuza* — a basket full of something as sweet as honey for a *zuz*! And with such easy living available to them, how is it that the Babylonians do not immerse themselves more fully in *Torah* study? That night, however, the dates caused him considerable digestive distress. His view shifted: *mala tana d'sachina b'zuza* — it is as though a basket of knives were available for a *zuz*, such danger lurks in this plenty. And he revised his earlier complaint: given what this food does to you, the fact that the Babylonians manage to learn *Torah* at all is actually remarkable.

The self-revision is charming, but the underlying tension is real. Ulla's initial assumption — easy material conditions should produce more *Torah* learning — and his revised conclusion — privation might actually sharpen dedication — reflect a genuine debate about the relationship between material ease and spiritual productivity. Life in Bavel was materially more comfortable than in *Eretz Yisrael*, and whether that comfort helped or hindered *Torah* study was a live question. The observation also captures an overarching structural feature of this entire daf: the constant interleaving of rain and *Torah* study as paired themes, each illuminating the other. Rain can be a blessing or an obstacle; *Torah* can flourish in privation or be undermined by plenty. Both are nuanced, contextual, and dependent on the person, the place, and the inner posture — the *kavana* — of those involved.

## Water Cycle

### Ocean Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מִמֵּימֵי אוֹקְיָינוֹס הוּא שׁוֹתֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵד יַעֲלֶה מִן הָאָרֶץ וְהִשְׁקָה אֶת כׇּל פְּנֵי הָאֲדָמָה״. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וַהֲלֹא מֵימֵי אוֹקְיָינוֹס מְלוּחִין הֵן! אָמַר לוֹ: מִתְמַתְּקִין בֶּעָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the source of rain. It **is taught** in a *baraita* **that Rabbi Eliezer says: The entire world drinks from the waters of the ocean [*okeyanos*],** i.e., evaporated ocean water is the source of rain. **As it is stated: "And there went up a mist from the earth and watered the whole face of the ground"** (Genesis 2:6). **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: But the waters of the ocean are salty,** whereas rainwater is sweet. Rabbi Eliezer **said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: The waters **are sweetened in the clouds,** before they fall to the earth.

A *Beraita* now turns to the cosmological question of where rain actually comes from. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *kol olam kulo m'mei Okeanos hu shoteh* — the entire world drinks from the waters of the ocean. The water cycle as he understands it is essentially correct: evaporation from the ocean leads to cloud formation, which leads to rainfall, which eventually finds its way back to the ocean. The verse cited is *v'eid ya'aleh min ha'aretz v'hishka kol p'nei ha'adama* — a cloud rises from the ground and waters the entire earth. The ocean's waters rise, form clouds, and fall as rain.

### Heavenly Waters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מִמַּיִם הָעֶלְיוֹנִים הוּא שׁוֹתֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִמְטַר הַשָּׁמַיִם תִּשְׁתֶּה מָּיִם״, אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְאֵד יַעֲלֶה מִן הָאָרֶץ״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָעֲנָנִים מִתְגַּבְּרִים וְעוֹלִים לָרָקִיעַ, וּפוֹתְחִין פִּיהֶן כְּנוֹד, וּמְקַבְּלִין מֵי מָטָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָזֹקּוּ מָטָר לְאֵדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** In contrast, **Rabbi Yehoshua says: The entire world drinks from the upper waters, as it is stated: "And it drinks water as the rain of heaven comes down"** (Deuteronomy 11:11). The *baraita* asks: **But** according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, **how do I uphold** the verse: **"And there went up a mist from the earth"?** Rabbi Yehoshua could answer that this verse **teaches that the clouds grow stronger, and rise to the firmament, and open their mouths like** a leather **bottle, and receive the rain waters** from above, **as it is stated:** "For He draws away the drops of water, **which distill rain from His vapor"** (Job 36:27).

Rabbi Yehoshua challenges: *m'mei Okeanos meluchin hein* — ocean water is salty; how can it become the fresh rainwater we drink? Rabbi Eliezer responds: *mematkin b'avim* — they are sweetened in the clouds. This is, in essence, a correct description of evaporation: the water evaporates and leaves the salt behind, emerging as fresh water in the clouds.

Rabbi Yehoshua, however, holds an entirely different cosmology: *kol olam kulo m'mayim elyonim hu shoteh* — the entire world drinks from the waters above the *rakia*, the firmament, which separated the upper and lower waters at the time of creation. Rain does not originate in the ocean at all, but descends from the waters stored above the heavens. The verse supporting this is *l'matar hashamayim tishteh mayim* — from the rain of the heavens you shall drink water.

How then does Rabbi Yehoshua understand the verse about the cloud rising from the ground? *Melamed she'ananim mitgavrin v'olin l'rakia* — the clouds strengthen and ascend to the *rakia*, *u'potechin pihem k'nod* — they open their mouths like a waterskin or canteen, *u'mekablin m'mei matar* — and they receive the rainwater. On this view, the clouds are not carriers of evaporated ocean water but rather empty vessels that ascend to the heavens, are filled with water from above the *rakia*, and then descend to deliver it.

### Sieve Clouds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנוּקָּבוֹת הֵן כִּכְבָרָה, וּבָאוֹת וּמְחַשְּׁרוֹת מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חַשְׁרַת מַיִם עָבֵי שְׁחָקִים״. וְאֵין בֵּין טִיפָּה לְטִיפָּה אֶלָּא כִּמְלֹא נִימָא, לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁגָּדוֹל יוֹם הַגְּשָׁמִים כְּיוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ בּוֹ שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the clouds **are perforated like a sieve, and they come and sprinkle water onto the ground, as it is stated: "Gathering of waters, thick clouds of the skies"** (II Samuel 22:12). **And between each and every drop there is only a hairbreadth,** and yet each drop emerges individually. This serves **to teach you that the day of rains is as great as the day on which Heaven and Earth were created,** i.e., rainfall is as miraculous as creation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֹשֶׂה גְדֹלוֹת וְאֵין חֵקֶר״, וּכְתִיב: ״הַנֹּתֵן מָטָר עַל פְּנֵי אָרֶץ״, וּכְתִיב לְהַלָּן: ״הֲלוֹא יָדַעְתָּ אִם לֹא שָׁמַעְתָּ אֱלֹהֵי עוֹלָם ה׳ אֵין חֵקֶר לִתְבוּנָתוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״מֵכִין הָרִים בְּכֹחוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated,** with regard to the creation of the world: **"Who does great things past finding out"** (Job 9:10), **and** as an example of this **it is written: "Who gives rain upon the earth"** (Job 5:9–10). **And it is written below: "Have you not known? Have you not heard that the everlasting God, the Lord,** the Creator of the ends of the earth, does not faint and is not weary; **His discernment is past finding out"** (Isaiah 40:28). The repetition of "past finding out" indicates that rainfall is as wondrous as the creation of the world. The Gemara adds: **And it is written** in a psalm that deals with rainfall: **"Who by Your strength sets fast the mountains;** Who is girded about with might" (Psalms 65:7).

The verse *yazoku matar l'eido* is cited: God will pour out rain to His cloud. And: *u'menuvavos hein k'kivara* — the clouds are perforated like a sieve. The water does not fall in a single rush but in individual drops, as through the many small holes of a sieve. *V'ein bein tipa l'tipa ela kemlo nima* — between one drop and the next there is only a hairbreadth of separation. The clouds thus function as a kind of celestial delivery system: filled above and dispensing below through their many small perforations.

Rabbi Yehoshua's view — that rain comes from the waters above the *rakia* — connects naturally to the theme of *maaseh bereishit*, the cosmology of creation, in which God separated the waters above from the waters below. On his reading, rain is not part of a natural water cycle rooted in the earth, but a continuous act of divine provision drawing on the primordial waters stored in heaven. It is accordingly Rabbi Yehoshua who concludes with the declaration that *gadol yom hageshamim k'yom she-nivreu bo shamayim va'aretz* — great is a day of rain like the day on which the heavens and earth were created — supported by the verse *ose gadolot v'ein cheker*, God does wonders without limit, and by the verses connecting rain and divine incomprehensibility. For Rabbi Yehoshua, to interpret rain as miraculous is inherent to his cosmology: the clouds ascend, receive the waters of creation from above the firmament, and deliver them to the earth.
