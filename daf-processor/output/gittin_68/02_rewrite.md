# Gittin 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kordyakos & Da'as

### New Perek Intro

Daf 68 marks the beginning of a new *perek*, one that is distinguished by an extended engagement with *aggadah* before returning to *halakha*. The daf in fact opens mid-discussion from the final lines of 67b, transitioning seamlessly into its first *mishnah*.

### Kordyakos Case

The *mishnah* introduces the case of *mi she'achazo kordyakos* — one who is seized with *kordyakos*, a condition that renders a person not in his right mind. The Gemara will eventually characterize it as a kind of spirit or demon, though it more likely refers to a specific illness producing mental incapacity. The governing principle is clear: such a person lacks *da'as*, and therefore when he says *kisvu get l'ishti* — write a *get* for my wife — *lo amar klum*, his words are legally meaningless. The appointment of *shlichus* requires genuine mental capacity, and that capacity is here absent.

### Retraction Valueless

The *mishnah* then presents a more striking case: *amar kisvu get l'ishti v'achazo kordyakos v'chazar v'amar lo tichtovenu* — he first said "write a *get* for my wife" while in his right mind, was then seized with *kordyakos*, and in that altered state retracted his instruction. The ruling is that *ein devarav ha'achronim klum* — his subsequent retraction is legally null. This is a considerable *chiddush*. One might have thought that the retraction, even if expressed during an episode of *kordyakos*, creates at minimum a *safek* sufficient to halt the writing of the *get*. Yet the Gemara rules that the wife is *vaday megureshes* — certainly divorced — and the retraction is treated as entirely without weight. The *shlichus* was validly constituted at the outset, and a declaration made while bereft of *da'as* cannot undo it.

This pair of cases illuminates a fundamental principle: what *halakha* requires is *da'as*, not merely verbal expression. One may speak without *da'as* — the first case — or one may lack the ability to speak while possessing full *da'as* — as the *mishnah* goes on to demonstrate. The two scenarios are mirror images of one another.

The *mishnah* then addresses *nishtatek* — one whose speech was silenced, whether through physical ailment or some other cause. If others ask him, "Shall we write a *get* for your wife?" and he nods, one tests him three times: *im amar al lav lav v'al hen hen* — if he can consistently signal no for things that are no and yes for things that are yes — then *yichtevu v'yitnu*, a *get* may be written and delivered. This procedure establishes two things simultaneously: that his signals can be interpreted reliably, and that his inability to speak does not reflect any deeper mental impairment.

### Kordyakos Definition

The Gemara then asks *mai kordyakos* — what precisely is this condition? Shmuel answers: *d'nachirei chamra chadta d'matzarta* — it is triggered by new wine fresh from the press. This appears to mean that the wine sets off something larger than ordinary intoxication, since the Gemara could simply have described the person as *shikor*, a drunk. The Gemara explicitly notes this: *v'lisni mi she'nashcho yayin chadash* — why not just say "one affected by new wine"? The answer is: *ha kamashma lan d'ha rucha kordyakos shema* — the text is teaching us that this particular spirit or illness has a name: *kordyakos*.

### Amulets & Names

*L'mai nafka mina?* — why does it matter that it has a name? *L'kameia* — because if one wishes to write an amulet against this affliction, one must know the name of the *shed* being warded off. The name is thus halakhically significant, not merely academically interesting.

## Medical Treatments

### Kordyakos Remedy

Immediately following the question of the amulet, the Gemara pivots to medical treatment — a striking juxtaposition that moves from the metaphysical to the practical. *Mai asusa* — how does one heal *kordyakos*? The prescription is *bisra sumka agumre* — lean red meat on coals — *vechamra marka* — and diluted wine.

### Sunstroke Treatment

Abaye then introduces a series of remedies transmitted in the name of his nursemaid, whom he calls *em*, mother — as Abaye was orphaned and reserved this term of endearment for the woman who raised him. For *shimsha bas yoma*, a sunstroke or fever of a single day, the remedy is a flask of water. If the condition persists for two days, *sikurei* — bloodletting is required. If it has lasted three days, one returns to the same regimen prescribed for *kordyakos*: lean meat on coals and diluted wine.

### Chronic Fever Cure

For *shimsha atikta* — a chronic or long-standing fever — the treatment is considerably more involved. One takes a black hen, cuts it lengthwise and widthwise, shaves the middle of one's head, and places the hen on the shaved area, leaving it there *ad demisrach* — until it begins to decompose. One then descends into the water, submerging up to the neck, remaining until faint, then swimming out and sitting down. If this does not suffice, one eats cress and repeats the water immersion in the same manner.

### Chills Remedy

The treatment for chills — *talga* — is characterized by Rashi as a condition of cold. As a kind of counterpart to the fever remedy, one prescribes *bisra shamina agumre* — fat meat on coals — and *vechamra chaya* — undiluted wine, the reverse of what is recommended for *kordyakos*.

### Rav Amram Story

Rav Amram Chasida suffered at the hands of the servants of the *Reish Galusa*'s household. The *Reish Galusa*, the political head of Babylonian Jewry, was not always surrounded by the most upright people, and Rashi explains that Rav Amram drew their hostility precisely because *mipnei shehaya chasid uparush umachmir aleihem be'isurin* — he was a pious and disciplined man who rebuked the household for its transgressions. In retaliation, they forced him to lie in the snow.

The next day, when they offered to make amends by bringing him whatever he wished, Rav Amram employed reverse psychology. Knowing that *hani kol de'amina lehu mafich apchi* — whatever he asked for they would give him the opposite — he requested lean meat on coals and diluted wine. They promptly brought him fat meat on coals and undiluted wine, which was precisely the remedy for chills that he required.

### Yalta & Bath

When Yalta, the learned wife of Rav Nachman and a member of the *Reish Galusa*'s family, heard of Rav Amram's condition, she took matters into her own hands. She brought him to the bathhouse and had him immerse in the waters until they turned red — the blood and fatty residue of the prescribed meat, Rashi explains, working their way out of his body. His flesh afterward showed markings that Rashi likens to coins, what we might describe as blotches, perhaps consistent with frostbite.

## Rav Sheshet & Trust

### Decline the Meal

The narrative shifts to Rav Sheshet, who was blind, and his interactions with the *Reish Galusa*'s household. When the *Reish Galusa* asked him why he had declined to eat at the household's table, Rav Sheshet replied without hesitation: *delo ma'alu avdei dechasidi eiver min hachai* — your servants are not trustworthy; they are suspect of tearing a limb from a living animal rather than producing properly prepared meat. The *Reish Galusa* challenged him: *mi yeimar* — who says so? Rav Sheshet replied: *hashta machveina lach* — I will demonstrate it to you now.

### Three-Leg Test

Rav Sheshet instructed his attendant to slip into the kitchen and steal one leg from the animal being prepared. When the servants then brought him a dismembered lamb, he had them place before him a full spread. They came with three legs, having failed to notice that one was missing. Rav Sheshet remarked: this animal had three legs? When they went and tore a leg off of another living animal to make up the count, Rav Sheshet had his attendant place the stolen leg on the table as well. He then observed: so this animal had five legs? The deception was fully exposed.

The episode illustrates with painful clarity the necessity of *mashgiach temidi* — constant supervision. Kosher establishments may operate with full integrity under normal conditions, yet the question is what happens when a shipment does not arrive, when a substitution must be made under pressure. The temptation to cut corners in such moments is precisely the vulnerability that requires constant oversight.

### Mashgiach Role

The *Reish Galusa*, rather than accepting Rav Sheshet's point as definitive, offered an alternative: let the preparations be done in front of Rav Sheshet himself, and he may supervise and eat. Rav Sheshet agreed to this arrangement.

### Dangerous Bone

A plate was set before him, and meat was brought out and placed at his place along with *ristana dechancha* — a particular bone near the thigh region which, Rashi explains, carries a small embedded bone capable of causing choking. The servants, now hostile after being publicly humiliated, were evidently hoping this bone would lodge in Rav Sheshet's throat. He sensed the danger before placing the meat in his mouth — apparently suspicious that they might attempt something — felt the piece carefully, identified it as the dangerous bone, and quietly wrapped it in his handkerchief, saying nothing.

When the meal ended, the servants accused him of having stolen a silver cup, using the charge as a pretext to search his belongings and expose what was wrapped in his handkerchief. They declared triumphantly: *chazi mar d'lo meichal ka be'ei ela l'tzaurin* — you see, you never intended to eat with us; you only came here to cause trouble.

### Disease Detection

Rav Sheshet responded that he had in fact eaten from the meat, but had detected from its taste that the piece he set aside had the flavor of *chivra* — a diseased, leprous quality. The servants denied this, insisting the animal had no such condition. Rav Sheshet told them: *bidku b'duchtei* — go examine the specific location on the animal where that piece came from, for Rav Chisda taught that *uchma b'chivra v'chivra b'uchma l'kishuta hu* — black spots on a white animal or white spots on a black animal indicate a diseased, *treif*-like condition. They examined the animal and found that Rav Sheshet was correct. Once again, the servants had been feeding guests from an animal with a problematic condition, and once again Rav Sheshet had caught them.

### Pit Trap Escape

As Rav Sheshet was departing from the *Reish Galusa*'s household, the servants dug a pit in his path and covered it with a mat, hoping he would lie down on it — as they suggested he do — and fall in. Rav Chisda, who was walking behind Rav Sheshet and could see, cleared his throat audibly — *nachar lei meachorei* — as a covert warning, since he apparently could not speak openly without risk.

Rav Sheshet, alerted, turned to a child traveling with him and asked: *psuk li psukecha* — tell me the verse you are studying. The child recited a verse from Shmuel 2: *neteh lecha al yeminecha o al semolecha* — turn to your right or to your left. Rav Sheshet understood the message and asked his attendant what he saw ahead. The attendant said: *tsifta deshadya* — a mat thrown on the ground. *Hadar minei* — go around it, Rav Sheshet instructed, and they did.

Afterward, Rav Chisda asked: how did you know there was danger? Rav Sheshet explained: your throat-clearing alerted me; the verse the child recited fit the situation exactly; and *de'chasidi avdei dela ma'alu* — I knew these servants were not to be trusted. Putting all those signs together, the conclusion was obvious.

## Demons & Shamir

### Singers vs Demons

The Gemara now turns to the world of *shedim*, entering through a verse in *Kohelet*: *asiti li sharim vesharot veta'anugot benei adam shida veshidot* — I made for myself male and female singers and all pleasures of humanity, *shida veshidot*. The Gemara parses the verse: *sharim vesharot* — male and female singers — refers to various types of music; *ta'anugot benei adam* — the pleasures of people — refers to pools and bathhouses. But what are *shida veshidot*?

Here the Gemara records a divergence between the Babylonian and Palestinian traditions. In Bavel, *hacha targimu shida veshidtin* — they translated it as male and female demons. In *Eretz Yisrael*, *amrei shidta* — the word denotes a kind of palanquin, a carriage. This divergence is not merely lexical; it reflects a broader cultural difference. The world of demons was far more prominent in the Babylonian *Gemara* than in the *Yerushalmi*, shaped by the surrounding Persian and Zoroastrian culture in which demonic figures played a significant role. The Roman milieu of *Eretz Yisrael* posed its own spiritual dangers, but demons were not their primary framework.

### Types of Demons

Rav Yochanan declares: *shlosh me'ot minei shedim hayu be'shichin* — there were three hundred different types of *shedim* in the place called Shichin. As for *shida* itself, Rav Yochanan says he does not fully know what it is — Rashi offering two readings: either that the nature of the *shed* remains uncertain to him, or that *shida* is the name of the mother or chief of all the *shedim*, and that identity he cannot determine.

### Building Challenge

The Gemara now explains why *Shlomo HaMelech* needed *shedim* at all. The verse in connection with the building of the *Beit HaMikdash* states that it was constructed with *even shlema masa* — whole, undressed stone — reflecting the prohibition of *lo tanif aleihem barzel* — you shall not raise iron over them. The rabbis therefore asked: how could the stonework of the Temple be accomplished without metal tools? The answer involves the *shamir* — a worm-like creature possessed of the capacity to split stone, which Moshe had used for the engraving of the names on the stones of the *ephod*, where similarly the stone had to remain whole while the letters were inscribed. The *shamir* would trace the path along the surface and the stone would crack along that line without being chipped, leaving the stone intact yet marked.

The *halakhic* question of whether iron implements were used outside the Temple precincts — at the quarry — and prohibited only within them is noted as a discussion among the *poskim*, but the Gemara's *aggadic* framework proceeds on the basis that the *shamir* was the operative solution.

### Finding Shamir

*Amru lei rabbanan* — the sages advised Shlomo: *ika shamira d'ayti Moshe l'avnei ephod* — there is the *shamir* that Moshe had for the stones of the *ephod*. But where is it now? *Heichi ishtakach?* They recommended: take a male and a female *shed* — *ayti shida v'shidtin* — press them against one another — *kavshinhu ahadadi* — cause them affliction — and *efshar d'yadei umaglu lach* — perhaps they will know and reveal it to you.

Shlomo had ordinary *shedim* at his disposal; capturing the king of demons would require a more elaborate stratagem. So he seized male and female *shedim* and coerced them. They responded: *anan lo yadinan* — we do not know. But, they said, *dilma Asmedai malka deshidi yada* — perhaps Ashmadai, the king of the *shedim*, knows. Shlomo asked: where is he? *Isei betur ploni* — at such and such a mountain. And here is what he does, which you may use to capture him.

### Ashmadai's Routine

Ashmadai digs for himself a pit — *karya lei bera* — fills it with water — *umali lei maya* — and covers it with a great rock — *umachsei lei betinra* — so that no one may tamper with his water supply. He seals it with his signet — *vachasmei begushpanka*. Every day he ascends to the heavenly *yeshiva* — *salik l'rakia v'gamar mesivta d'rakia* — and then descends to study in the earthly *yeshiva* as well — *v'gamar mesivta d'ara*. This dual engagement reflects the intermediate nature of *shedim*: they are partly human and partly divine, as the Gemara in *Berachot* notes that they resemble humans in three ways and angels in three. Having studied in both realms, Ashmadai returns, inspects his seal, uncovers the cistern, drinks, reseals, and departs.

## Capturing Ashmadai

### Well Scheme

Shlomo dispatched Benayahu ben Yehoyada, equipping him with a chain bearing the divine name — *shalshelta d'chakika aleh shem* — a signet ring also engraved with the divine name, bundles of wool, and skins of wine. Benayahu went to the mountain. He dug a pit below Ashmadai's cistern — *khara beira mittata'ei* — and opened a channel between them so the water from the upper cistern drained into the lower pit. He then stopped up the channel with wool — *vastminu b'gavvei d'amra* — so that the flow would not be visible. He then dug a pit above the cistern — *v'khara beira m'eila'ei* — and poured wine into it — *v'shafkhinu l'chamra* — so that the wine flowed down into the now-empty cistern. He then covered everything over so that no disturbance would be visible. He climbed a tree and waited.

### Wine Temptation

When Ashmadai returned, he inspected his seal, found it untampered, uncovered the cistern — and found wine where water had been. He immediately began to recite verses he had learned: *leitz hayayin homeh shechar v'chol shogeh bo lo yechkam* — wine is a mocker, strong drink is a brawler, and whoever is led astray by it will not be wise. And he cited the verse from Hoshea: *znus v'yayin v'tirosh yikach lev* — licentiousness and wine take away the heart. *Lo ishtei* — he would not drink. Yet *ki tzachi* — thirst overcame him, and *lo sagya leh* — he could not withstand it. He drank, became sated, and fell asleep.

It is worth noting the irony: just as new wine triggered the condition of *kordyakos* in a human being — diminishing his *da'as* — here the king of the *shedim* succumbs to wine despite his full awareness of its danger. And Ashmadai's refusal to drink was itself ironic: he was citing verses from *Kohelet*, the very book authored by Shlomo, whose name he would soon usurp.

### Chain Capture

Benayahu descended from the tree, approached, threw the chain around Ashmadai — *shada beh shalshelta* — and locked it. When Ashmadai woke and struggled — *hava ka mifarkes* — Benayahu told him: *shma d'marach alach* — the name of your Master is upon you. God's name on the chain held him, and he could not break free.

### Journey & Kindness

Benayahu now led Ashmadai back toward Shlomo. During the journey, as Ashmadai walked alongside or before his captor, he brushed against a palm tree and knocked it down — demonstrating the enormous physical force he contained even while restrained. He came close to a house and knocked it down as well simply by passing. When he came near the small hut of a widow who came out and implored him, he was moved by her entreaty — *itchanena leh* — and bent his enormous frame in the opposite direction to avoid harming her dwelling. In doing so he strained and broke a bone. He then cited the verse from *Kohelet*: *ulashon raka tishbor garem* — a soft tongue breaks a bone. He had been appeased by her gentle words, and look what it cost him.

## Ashmadai's Nature

### Acts of Kindness

Benayahu then began to ask Ashmadai about the curious behaviors he had observed along the way. Why, when you saw a blind man going astray on the path, did you set him right? Ashmadai replied: *machrezei aleih birki'a* — it was announced in heaven that *tzaddik gamur hu* — he was a completely righteous person, and *u'man de'avid leih nicha nafshia zachi le'alma de'ati* — anyone who did something good for him would merit a share in the world to come. Ashmadai, it seems, was not moved by compassion alone; he was moved by the knowledge, gained through his daily study in the heavenly *yeshiva*, of who this person was and what reward awaited those who helped him.

### Mortality Signs

Why, then, did you set right the drunk man who was wandering off the path? Here Ashmadai's answer was the reverse: *machrezei aleih birki'a de'rasha gamur hu* — it was announced in heaven that he was a completely wicked person. By doing him a kindness, Ashmadai ensured that he would receive his reward in this world — *ki heichi de'leikhalu le'alma* — so that in the world to come he would face only punishment, with no remaining merit to soften it. This reflects the classical *Chazal* understanding of *rasha ve'tov lo* — the wicked who prospers in this world is receiving his payment here, leaving nothing for the next.

The connection to *Kohelet* runs deep here. *Kohelet* wrestles with the apparent equivalence of *tzaddik* and *rasha*, the shared fate of the righteous and the wicked. Ashmadai's explanations pierce through that surface equivalence: the blind *tzaddik* and the wayward *rasha* receive the same outward act of assistance, but for entirely opposite reasons, with entirely opposite eschatological consequences.

Why did you weep at the wedding celebration? *Ba'ei mimat gavra be'go tlatin yomei* — the bridegroom is going to die within thirty days of the wedding. *U'va'ya mintar leih yavam katan* — and the bride will be bound in *yibbum* to a young brother-in-law who is still a baby, and she will wait thirteen years until he reaches adulthood to receive *chalitza* or *yibbum*. This too resonates with *Kohelet*'s *tov lalechet el beit avel milechet el beit mishteh* — better to go to the house of mourning than to the house of feasting, for that is the end of all humanity. The wedding celebration conceals a coming tragedy that Ashmadai, with his heavenly knowledge, could already see.

Why did you laugh when the man ordered shoes to last for seven years? *Hahu shiva yomei leit leih masanei le'shava shanin ba'ei* — that man will not live seven more days; he is ordering shoes for seven years. Once more the theme: human beings make plans over vast horizons while they stand at the very edge of mortality.

Why did you laugh at the magician performing his divinations? *De'hava yativ avei gaza de'malka* — because he was sitting directly above a royal treasure buried in the earth beneath him, and he was attempting through his magic to discover hidden things — unable to find the treasure that lay immediately below him. *Liksum mai de'ika tuteih* — he should have tried to divine what was directly underfoot.

All of Ashmadai's observations during the journey function collectively as a kind of walking commentary on *Kohelet*: the vanity of human plans, the hidden decree of heaven, the tragic arc between celebration and mourning, the irony of the wise man who cannot see what is before him.

## Solomon's Fall

### Testing Power

Ashmadai was kept in Shlomo's presence even after the *shamir* had been obtained — perhaps to extract further heavenly knowledge, perhaps simply because Shlomo was reluctant to release him. One day they were sitting together, and Ashmadai posed a challenge. He cited the verse: *ke'to'afot re'em lo* — like the *to'afot* of the *re'em* are to God. The interpretation given is that *to'afot* refers to the ministering angels, and *re'em* refers to the *shedim*. So God is praised for his mastery over both angels and demons. Ashmadai then asked: *mai ravuteichu minan* — what makes you so much greater than us? How is it that God is praised for his dominion over you and over us together? You want to see how great I am? Take the chains off me. Give me your signet ring. *Ve'achvei lach ravutai* — I will show you my greatness.

### Casting Away

Shlomo removed the chains. He handed over the ring. Ashmadai swallowed Shlomo — or, as some versions read, swallowed the ring — spread one wing to the heavens and the other to the earth, spanning the full distance between them, and cast Shlomo *arba me'ah parsa* — four hundred *parasangs* away.

### Kohelet Realized

At that moment, Shlomo said the words of *Kohelet*: *mah yitron l'adam bechol amalo she'ya'amol tachas hashemesh* — what profit does a person have from all his labor under the sun? He had amassed everything — music, pleasure, wisdom, dominion over demons and kings — and a single moment of foolishness had stripped it all away. The verse *vezeh hayah chelki mikol amali* — this was my portion from all my labor — was interpreted by Rav and Shmuel as referring to what he had left: one opinion says *maklo*, his staff alone; the other says *gundo*, his cloak. After all his wealth, this was his portion.

## Solomon's Fall (II)

### Beggar King

Shlomo now wandered from door to door. *Kol heicha demati amar* — wherever he arrived, he would declare: *ani Kohelet hayisi melech al Yisrael biYerushalayim* — I, Kohelet, was once king over Israel in Jerusalem. The word *hayisi* — I used to be — is itself poignant: his very name had shifted; he was no longer Shlomo but Kohelet, the gatherer, the philosopher, now asking in exile what meaning underlies all human striving. The philosophizing of *Kohelet* takes on a new dimension here — it was born not from detached wisdom but from devastating loss. Only once he had been stripped of everything did the deep questions of the book become truly his own.

Meanwhile, Ashmadai was impersonating him in the palace.

## Impersonation Discovered

### Sanhedrin Inquiry

When Shlomo came before the Sanhedrin, the rabbis took note of something that did not fit the profile of genuine madness. *Shoteh bechada milta lo sarich* — a person who is genuinely deranged does not act sanely in all matters except one. Yet this man was entirely coherent in every regard, and his only apparent aberration was his insistence that he had once been Shlomo HaMelech. The sages recognized this inconsistency as grounds for investigation.

### Detection Methods

The rabbis asked Benayahu: *kabei lach malka legabei* — has the king summoned you recently? Benayahu replied that he had not been called to see the king in some time. They then sent word to the queens asking whether the king had visited them. The queens replied that yes, he had come. The rabbis then instructed the queens: *bidku bechar'ei* — examine his feet. The queens reported back that he keeps his feet covered at all times, wearing slippers in bed. Furthermore, they noted two profoundly disturbing behaviors: he was demanding relations even during *niddah*, and he had made advances toward Bat Sheva, his own mother. Something was deeply wrong.

### Restoration & Fear

The real Shlomo was brought back. He was given a signet ring and a chain engraved with the divine name — the same mechanism that had originally bound Ashmadai. When Shlomo entered, Ashmadai saw him and fled.

### Guards & Defense

Yet even after Ashmadai's flight, Shlomo was left with an ongoing fear — *havah lei bi'asusa minei* — a lasting dread of what Ashmadai could do. This explains the verse in *Kohelet*: *hinei mitaso shel Shlomo shishim giborim saviv lah migiborei Yisrael kulam achuzei cherev melumadei milchama ish charbo al yerecho mipachad baleilos* — behold, the bed of Shlomo, sixty warriors surrounding it from among the heroes of Israel, all grasping swords, trained in war, each man with his sword on his thigh because of the fear of the nights. That fear was the lingering shadow of Ashmadai.

---

## Final Status

### King or Beggar

The Gemara closes with a dispute between Rav and Shmuel. *Chad amar melech vehedyot* — one holds that Shlomo was first a king and then became a commoner, and never again regained his throne. *Vechad amar melech vehedyot umelech* — the other holds that he was king, then commoner, then king again, meaning the restoration described above represents a genuine return to his former status.

According to the first opinion, Shlomo spent the rest of his life wandering, declaring to anyone who would listen that he had once been king over Israel — a *Kohelet* indeed, gathering the meaning of everything he had lost, asking *mah yitron* with no answer forthcoming. According to the second opinion, the story has a restoration, though one shadowed permanently by fear. Either way, the lens through which the Gemara reads *Kohelet* is now fully in view: the book is not merely abstract philosophy, but the utterance of a man who had touched the heights of human achievement and learned from catastrophic personal experience that it could vanish in a moment.
