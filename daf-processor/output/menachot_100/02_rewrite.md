# Menachot 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lechem HaPanim Setup

The daf opens at 100a, still within the *mishnah* treating *lechem hapanim*. The *mishnah* presents three distinct scenarios of procedural failure in the arrangement of the showbread and its accompanying frankincense, and the Gemara examines the consequences of each.

### Bread Sat, Incense Sun

The first case: the *kohanim* placed the bread on the *shulchan* on *Shabbos* but delayed placing the frankincense (*levona*) until Sunday. When the *bezichin* — the two bowls of frankincense — were then offered up on the following *Shabbos*, the offering is *pasul*. As Rashi explains: *demichusar zman hu shelo haya al hashulchan ela shishi yamim* — it is lacking in its proper time, since the frankincense had rested on the table for only six days rather than the required seven. A prerequisite for the *bezichin* to be offered is that they have remained on the *shulchan* for a full week. Because this condition was not met, the *levona* was not brought as a valid *matir*, and consequently the bread never attained the status of being permitted for consumption. The *mishnah* therefore rules that *ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul notar vetamei*: a *pigul* intention at the time of offering has no effect, since *pigul* applies only when the *matir* was offered *kemitzvaso*; *notar* does not apply since the bread was never rendered fit to be eaten; and a *tamei* person who consumed it incurs no *kares*, since *kares* for eating sacred food applies only when the food had attained a state of permissibility — which this bread never did.

One might ask: why not simply offer the *bezichin* on Sunday, giving them a full week before the following *Shabbos*? The answer is two-fold. The *bezichin* may only be offered on *Shabbos*, not on a Sunday. Furthermore, since the bread was placed at the correct time — on *Shabbos* — it would no longer be valid by the time a Sunday *bezichin* offering could complete the required seven-day period from the bread's placement.

### Both Sat, Incense Late

The second case: both the bread and the *bezichin* were placed on the *shulchan* correctly, on *Shabbos*, but the frankincense was then offered late — *l'achar hashabbos*, after *Shabbos* had ended. Again the verdict is *psula*: the *matir* was not offered *kemitzvaso*, and the same consequences follow regarding *pigul*, *notar*, and *tumah*.

### Both Sun, Wait Solution

The third and most instructive case: both the bread and the *bezichin* were placed on the *shulchan* on Sunday — that is, entirely after *Shabbos*. If one then attempted to offer the *bezichin* on the following *Shabbos* (six days later), the offering would again be *pasul*, since the full seven days had not elapsed. Here, however, the *mishnah* offers a resolution: *yani'chenu l'Shabbos haba'ah* — leave the bread on the table until the *Shabbos* after that. And *she'afilu hi al hashulchan yamim rabim ein bekach klum* — even if it sits on the *shulchan* for many days, that causes no problem.

This ruling requires careful explanation. It does not mean that extended time on the *shulchan* is unconditionally harmless. Rather, since the bread was placed on Sunday — *shelo bizmano*, at the wrong time — the *shulchan* never sanctified it and the clock never began ticking. The clock only begins when the bread is placed on *Shabbos*, which is its proper time. Therefore, when the bread sits through that first *Shabbos* without having been placed correctly, only at the next *Shabbos* does its placement become meaningful, the *shulchan* sanctify it, and the seven-day count commence. The *bezichin* can then be offered on the *Shabbos* following that — thirteen days in total from the original Sunday placement.

This stands in contrast to the first case. There, the bread was placed on *Shabbos* and the clock began immediately. No amount of waiting past the initial *Shabbos* could remedy the *bezichin* being placed a day late. Here, the clock never started, so waiting costs nothing and the next *Shabbos* provides the proper initiation point.

## Tamid Timing & Prep

### Daylight Check

The *Gemara* quotes a *mishnah* from Tamid: *amar lahem hamemuneh, tze'u ur'u im higiya zman shechita* — the overseer would send someone out to verify that the time for *shechita* of the daily *tamid* offering had arrived. When the scout reported that the east had become light, he would say *barkai* — an expression evoking the breaking light of dawn. *Matia ben Shmuel* is quoted as saying that when he himself served as overseer, he would ask the scout: *he'ir pnei kol hamizrach ad shebeChevron* — has the entire eastern sky brightened, as far as one can see toward Chevron? Only upon receiving confirmation would he proceed. This was a deliberate double-check, not an impulsive reaction to any flash of light.

### Moonlight Mistake

The reason for such caution is historical. On one occasion, *me'or halevana* — the moonlight — rose brilliantly and the *kohanim* mistook it for dawn. They slaughtered the *tamid*, only to discover the error. The offering had been *shechted* at night and was therefore invalid; it had to be taken out to *beit hasreifah* to be burned. In addition, the *kohen gadol* was brought to *beit hatvilah* — a detail that Rashi understands as most likely reflecting a *Yom Kippur* context — though that matter is tangential here.

### Temple Purity

The general rule that then follows describes the purity standards maintained in the *Beis HaMikdash*: any *kohen* who had defecated was required to immerse in a *mikvah*, while one who had urinated was required to wash his hands and feet. This was not a strict *halachic* requirement triggered by *tumah* per se, but rather the standard of preparation that was observed before performing the *avodah* in the Temple.

## Mincha at Night

### Night Slaughter

The father of Rabbi Avin (*avuha d'Rabbi Avin*) taught a *braita*: *lo zo bilvad* — not only is an animal slaughtered at night invalid, but *afilu olas ha'of shenimleka balaila umincha shenikmitza balaila taitzei leveit hasreifah* — even a bird offering whose *melikah* was performed at night, or a *mincha* whose *komets* was taken at night, must go out to *beit hasreifah*. The *Gemara* accepts the ruling about the bird without difficulty: *mishum delo efshar lehahadura* — there is no way to undo a *melikah* and restore the bird to life. But the *mincha* presents a genuine question. Why can one not simply return the *komets* to its vessel and take it again by day?

### Handful Sanctification

The answer given by the same authority who taught the *braita* is: *klei sharet mekadeshin shelo bizmanam* — the service vessels sanctify even outside their proper time. When the *kohen* placed the *komets* into a *kli sharet* at night, even though night is not the valid time for this act, the vessel nonetheless imparted a degree of sanctification to the *komets*. As a result, the *komets* has been designated and can no longer simply be returned to the *mincha* and re-taken. Without this principle, one could have argued that when the *kli sharet* is used *shelo bizmano*, nothing meaningful has occurred and the *komets* retains no distinct status. The *chiddush* is that this assumption is wrong.

### Temurah Challenge

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a teaching in Temurah: *kol hakarev bayom kadosh bayom* — whatever is offered in the day is sanctified in the day; *balaila kadosh balaila* — if an offering is valid at night, it is sanctified at night; *bein bayom uvein balaila* — if it is valid either way, the vessel sanctifies it either way. The plain implication is that the *kli sharet* functions precisely when the offering can be brought: for a normal daytime *korban*, the vessel works by day but not by night. This directly contradicts the claim that vessels sanctify *shelo bizmanom*.

### Vessel Timing Rule

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *eino kadosh l'hikarev aval kadosh l'hipasel* — the *komets* taken at night is not sanctified to the degree that it may be offered, but it is sanctified to the degree that it may be rendered *pasul*. This is, as the analysis frankly acknowledges, the worst of both worlds. Because the act was performed at the wrong time, the *komets* cannot be offered up. But because it was placed in a *kli sharet*, it has nonetheless acquired its identity as a designated *komets* — a *komets psula* — and may no longer be returned to the *mincha* for a fresh taking by day. The vessel did not elevate the offering to fitness, but it did lock in its identity and its disqualification.

## Bread Vessel Rules

### Zeira Challenge

Rabbi Zeira now raises a sharp challenge that brings the discussion back to our *mishnah* about *lechem hapanim*. He returns to the third case: bread and *bezichin* placed on the *shulchan* on Sunday, and the *bezichin* offered on the following *Shabbos* — invalid, because only six days have elapsed. The *mishnah*'s solution was to wait until the *Shabbos* after that, noting that bread sitting on the *shulchan* for many days creates no problem, because the clock never started ticking when the bread was placed *shelo bizmano*.

Rabbi Zeira's challenge: if we accept the principle that *klei sharet mekadesh shelo bizmanom* — at least to the extent of rendering something *pasul* — then why does the Sunday placement not begin the clock? The *shulchan* is itself a *kli sharet*. When the bread is placed on it on Sunday, even though that is the wrong time, it should nonetheless sanctify the bread sufficiently to render it *pasul*, just as the *komets* vessel rendered the nighttime *komets* unable to be re-taken. If so, by the time the bread has been on the table for eight days, it should be considered to have already been designated and subsequently disqualified — which would mean that even the "wait until next *Shabbos*" solution collapses.

Rava acknowledges that the question is excellent: *man dekamosif shapir kamosif* — the one who raises this addition raises it well. Nevertheless, he notes that the father of Rabbi Avin was citing an established *braita*, not inventing a position. That *braita* unambiguously states that a *mincha* taken at night cannot be rectified — which only makes sense on the assumption that the *kli sharet* functions *shelo bizmano* at least to create disqualification. So both the *braita* and the *mishnah* must somehow be reconciled.

### Night vs Days

The resolution: *kasavar laila ein mechusar zman, hayamim mechusarin zman* — night is not considered "lacking in its proper time," whereas the intervening days are considered fully lacking. Night, specifically the night leading into the day on which an offering could be brought, is close enough to the proper time that when the *kli sharet* operates at night, it operates within a period that is conceptually adjacent to the day of *avodah*. The offering is *pasul* because *avodah* cannot be performed at night, but it is not *mechusar zman* — not so far removed from proper time that the vessel's act is entirely without meaning. Therefore, the nighttime taking of the *komets* locks in the *komets*'s identity.

By contrast, placing the bread on the *shulchan* six days before *Shabbos* is *mechusar zman* in the full sense. The *shulchan* is not acting *shelo bizmano* in a mild way — it is acting at a completely wrong time, and therefore no sanctification of any kind occurs. The clock does not start, no disqualification takes hold, and the bread waits inertly until it reaches a proper *Shabbos*.

### Friday Night Problem

This resolution, however, generates a new problem. If we say that "the days" lack proper time but "night" does not, then consider what happens as the bread sits on the *shulchan* from Sunday through the week. By the time Friday night arrives — the night of *erev Shabbos* — the bread has been on the table for six days, and Friday night is the nighttime preceding the very *Shabbos* on which the bread ought to have been placed. That night is not *mechusar zman*, by the logic just established. So why does Friday night not begin the clock — sanctifying the bread, starting the seven-day count, and causing it to become *pasul* before the second *Shabbos* that the *mishnah* relies upon?

### Clock Prevention

Two answers are offered. Rava says: *beshekodmo v'silayko* — in practice, before Friday night arrived, the *kohanim* would remove the bread from the *shulchan* temporarily, preventing Friday night from triggering any sanctification. They would keep it elsewhere and return it to the *shulchan Shabbos* morning, ensuring that the first *Shabbos* on which the bread rested on the table was the second *Shabbos* after its placement — the one the *mishnah* intends.

Mar Zutra, and some say Rav Ashi, offers an alternative: *afilu tayma beshelo kodmo v'silayko* — even if the bread was never removed before Friday night, *keivan desiduro shelo kemitzvato naaseh kemi shesidro hakof* — since the original placement was so far removed from its proper time, the entire act of placement is treated as though it were performed by a monkey, that is, as entirely meaningless. The bread on the *shulchan* from Sunday is not the product of a valid act of *sidur*; it is simply present there without consequence. The *kli sharet* cannot operate on something whose placement was so fundamentally improper as to be considered no act at all.

This second answer illuminates a deeper principle. In the case of the *mincha* at night, the act of taking the *komets* was close enough to its proper time — the night immediately preceding the day — that it was a meaningful act. The *kli sharet* responded to that meaningful act by imparting at least the sanctification of *pasul*. But an act so far removed from its proper time — like placing the bread six days early — is not merely *shelo bizmano* in a technical sense. It is so disconnected from any notion of proper performance that it registers as no act at all, and no *kli sharet* can respond to a non-act.

## Two Loaves Duration

### Two Loaves Timing

The final *mishnah* of the *perek* turns to two related topics: the *shtei halechem* of *Shavuos* and the *lechem hapanim*. Regarding the *shtei halechem*: they are eaten no fewer than two days after baking and no more than three — *ein pachos mishnayim v'lo yoter al shlosha*. The *mishnah* explains: since baking does not override *Yom Tov* — *eino docheh Yom Tov* — the loaves must be baked on *erev Yom Tov*, yielding a minimum of two days (baking day and eating day). If *Yom Tov* falls on Sunday, one cannot bake on *Yom Tov* or on *Shabbos*, so baking must occur on Friday — yielding three days: Friday, *Shabbos*, and Sunday.

It should be noted: the clock for the *shtei halechem* does not begin when the loaves are baked. It begins when the accompanying *korban* is *shechted*. Similarly, the baking itself does not trigger sanctification that would create a problem of *linah* or premature disqualification.

### HaPanim Timing

The *lechem hapanim* follows the same structural logic but with a longer duration: *ein pachos mitish'ah* — no fewer than nine days from baking, *velo yoter al echad asar* — no more than eleven. The same principle applies: baking is not *docheh Shabbos*, so it must be done on *erev Shabbos*, and the loaves are eaten the following *Shabbos*. Counting both the day of baking and the day of eating gives nine days.

If *Yom Tov* falls on *erev Shabbos* (Friday), one cannot bake on that *Yom Tov*, so baking is moved to Thursday — yielding ten days. If *Rosh Hashanah* falls on Thursday and Friday, baking cannot occur on either day of *Yom Tov*, so it is done on Wednesday, and the bread is eaten the following *Shabbos* — eleven days total.

Rashi notes that when *Rosh Hashanah* is Thursday-Friday, the *Shabbos* on which the bread is eaten falls out on *Yom Kippur*, and asks how the bread can be consumed then. His answer is that it is eaten on *motzaei Shabbos*, still within the eleven-day window since the bread does not become *pasul* until Sunday morning. Tosafot takes a different approach: at the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*, when *Rosh Hashanah* was observed over two days, the second day of *Rosh Hashanah* was the first of *Tishrei*, making the arithmetic work differently — though that is a broader discussion.

The *mishnah* concludes this section by stating explicitly: *ve'eino doche lo es haShabbos velo es Yom Tov* — baking neither the *shtei halechem* nor the *lechem hapanim* overrides *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*. That the *lechem hapanim* baking does not override *Shabbos* is self-evident; the case of *Yom Tov* is more interesting, particularly regarding the *shtei halechem*, which are to be eaten on *Yom Tov* itself.

### Shimon Exception

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then quotes Rabbi Shimon ben HaSgan with a dissenting view: *doche es haYom Tov ve'eino doche es Yom Tzom* — baking does override *Yom Tov*, but it does not override *Yom Kippur*. According to this opinion, one may bake the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuos* itself to eat them on *Shavuos*. Furthermore, this applies even to the *lechem hapanim*: if *Yom Tov* falls before *Shabbos*, one may bake on *Yom Tov* in preparation for a *Shabbos* as many as eight or nine days later. The one exception is *Yom Kippur*, which cannot be overridden even for this purpose.

## Baking Override

### Offerings Category

Ravina offers an analytical framework to understand why, according to the first opinion in the *mishnah*, one cannot bake the *shtei halechem* on *Yom Tov*. One might have supposed that the prohibition is only *miderabanan*: biblically, cooking for food (*ochel nefesh*) is permitted on *Yom Tov*, and the *shtei halechem* will ultimately be eaten by the *kohanim*, so perhaps it qualifies. The *Rabbanan*, on this reading, would have prohibited it out of concern that people would defer their obligations — *shema yishehe* — postponing their *nedarim* and *nedavot* until *Yom Tov* if *Yom Tov* baking for a *korban* context were permitted. But Ravina argues that the prohibition is *mid'oraita*, not *miderabanan*: when an act of cooking or baking is done fundamentally for the purpose of a *korban* — even if the *kohanim* will eat the result — it is not classified as *lachem*, "for you," and therefore does not fall within the *Yom Tov* permit of *ochel nefesh*. The *Torah*'s permission is for cooking that is oriented directly toward human eating; something performed as a *korban* act, with eating as a downstream consequence, does not qualify.

### Shavuot Proof

The proof for this reading is the *shtei halechem* themselves. The concern of *shema yishehe* — postponing one's obligations — would have no relevance to the *shtei halechem*, since they can only be brought on *Shavuos* and there is nowhere to defer them. If the prohibition were merely *miderabanan* for reasons of *shema yishehe*, no such prohibition could logically apply to the *shtei halechem*. Yet the *mishnah* plainly states *ve'kasani eino doche lo es haShabbos velo es Yom Tov* — the *shtei halechem* baking does not override *Yom Tov*. This can only be explained if the prohibition is *mid'oraita*: baking for a *korban* purpose is categorically outside the *Yom Tov* permission, regardless of any rabbinic concern about deferral.

What makes the position of Rabbi Shimon ben HaSgan particularly striking is its breadth: he permits not only baking on *Yom Tov* for *Yom Tov* itself, but also baking on *Yom Tov* in preparation for a *Shabbos* that falls many days later. Beyond the question of baking a *korban* item on *Yom Tov*, this also touches on the principle — parallel to the concerns addressed by *eiruv tavshilin* — of cooking from *Yom Tov* for *Shabbos*.

## Mincha Redemption

### Pre-Vessel Redemption

A new *mishnah* opens the next *perek*: *hamenchos v'hanesachim shenitme'u ad shelo kidshu b'kli yesh lahem pidyon* — if a *mincha* or *nesachim* became *tamei* before being placed into a *kli sharet*, they may be redeemed. At this stage, the flour, oil, or wine has been designated for sacred use but not yet placed in a service vessel. The sanctity it holds is *kedushat damim* — sanctity of monetary value. Just as one who declares his animal or property consecrated to the Temple creates an obligation to transfer that item's monetary value to the sanctuary treasury, so too these offerings, before the *kli* step, are sanctified only in terms of their worth. Something with only *kedushat damim* can be redeemed: its *kedushah* transfers to the redemption money, and the original item is released.

### Post-Vessel No Redemption

Once the *mincha* or *nesachim* have been placed in a *kli sharet*, the situation changes entirely: *mishekidshu b'kli ein lahem pidyon* — after sanctification by the service vessel, they cannot be redeemed. The vessel's sanctification elevates them to *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity. At this level, the item's sacred identity is not merely a matter of monetary value; the item itself is sacred in a substantive sense. Something with *kedushat haguf* cannot be redeemed. Even if it subsequently becomes *tamei* — making it impossible to offer — it still cannot be redeemed. The questioner cannot say: since I cannot use it in its *tamei* state, allow me to redeem it and purchase replacement ingredients. The *mishnah* answers: no. Once in the *kli*, the door to redemption is closed.

### Exception List

The *mishnah* then lists a separate category: *ha'ofot v'ha'etzim v'halevona v'chlei sharet* — birds, wood, frankincense, and service vessels themselves. These *mishenitme'u ein lahem pidyon* — once they become *tamei*, they cannot be redeemed. The stated reason is *shelo ne'emar pidyon ela bevehema* — the Torah's language of redemption was stated only in the context of animals.

This list, placed in the middle of the *mishnah*, appears somewhat puzzling at first glance. The opening and closing of the *mishnah* fit together coherently: *minchos* and *nesachim* before a *kli sharet* can be redeemed, after the *kli sharet* they cannot, because the *Torah* only provided a redemption mechanism for an animal with a *mum*. But why single out birds, wood, *levona*, and *kli sharet* in the middle? The *Gemara* will clarify this.

## Why Vessel Matters

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel teaches: *va'afilu hein tehorim nifdim* — the *minchos* and *nesachim* may be redeemed before being placed in a *kli sharet* even if they are *tahor*, even if no *tumah* has occurred. The *mishnah* framed the case in terms of *tumah* — the items became *tamei* before being placed in the *kli* — but that framing should not be read as establishing *tumah* as a prerequisite for redemption. The *Gemara* itself immediately notes: *pshita, nitmu tnan* — is this not obvious? The *mishnah* already states that *tamei* items can be redeemed before the *kli* step. The implicit question is, what is Shmuel adding?

Shmuel's point is a careful reading of the *mishnah*'s structure. One might have read the opening clause as establishing a *chiddush* in the other direction: specifically when the items have become *tamei* before being put in a *kli sharet*, they may be redeemed — but if they are *tahor*, even before the *kli* step, perhaps they may not. Shmuel rejects this. The *mishnah* uses the *tamei* scenario not because *tamei* is the threshold, but because it wants to contrast it with the *sefa*: *mishekidshu b'kli ein lahem pidyon* — once in the *kli*, no redemption, even if they are now *tamei*. In order to create that contrast, the *reisha* phrases its case in terms of *tamei* items. But in actual *halacha*, before the *kli* step, *kedushat damim* items can be redeemed regardless of *tumah* status.

*Va'idi dekaba'i lemisni seifa* — since the *mishnah* needed to teach the *sefa*, which is the genuine *chiddush* — namely, that even *tamei* items cannot be redeemed once they have acquired *kedushat haguf* through the *kli sharet* — it correspondingly framed the *reisha* in the *tamei* scenario for parallel structure. But Shmuel clarifies the actual rule.

### Body Sanctity

The *Gemara* then asks: is it not obvious that once the items are in the *kli sharet* they cannot be redeemed? *Pshita, kedushat haguf ninhu* — they have intrinsic sanctity; of course they cannot be redeemed. The *Gemara* answers: *itzterich* — no, this needed to be stated. One might have argued as follows: consider an animal sanctified as a *korban*. It has *kedushat haguf* from the moment of sanctification. Yet if it develops a *mum*, it can be redeemed. So perhaps, by analogy, these items — which also have *kedushat haguf* after the *kli* step — can likewise be redeemed when they develop a problem analogous to a *mum*, namely *tumah*. After all, in both cases the item with *kedushat haguf* has become unfit for use. Why not permit redemption in both situations?

The *mishnah* answers by rejecting the analogy. The allowance for redeeming an animal with a *mum* is a specific Torah provision; it does not establish a general principle that any item with *kedushat haguf* may be redeemed when it becomes unfit. The language the *Torah* uses regarding redemption was addressed specifically to animals — *shelo ne'emar pidyon ela bevehema* — and does not extend to *minchos*, *nesachim*, or the other items in the list.

### Animal Blemish

The distinction between animals and other sacred items runs deeper. An animal sanctified as a *korban* acquires *kedushat haguf* immediately upon the verbal act of designation, well before any service vessel is involved. When the animal is later *shechted*, the knife — itself a *kli sharet* — is applied, but the animal's *kedushat haguf* preceded this. Strikingly, however, something that acquires its *kedushat haguf* through placement in a *kli sharet* — like a *mincha* in a service bowl — cannot be redeemed once that *kli* step has occurred, even if it subsequently becomes *tamei*. The animal, despite having *kedushat haguf*, remains redeemable when blemished; the post-*kli* *mincha* does not. The *Gemara* acknowledges a certain asymmetry here: why should the *kli sharet* step be more restrictive for a *mincha* than the equivalent stage is for an animal? The *Gemara* notes that it simply takes this as given — *dimekli sharet lo ashkachan demifrak* — we have never found anything that was redeemed after its sanctification by a *kli sharet*. The animal's *kedushat haguf* exists prior to any *kli sharet* involvement, so the redemption provision for *ba'al mum* applies there; once anything, animal or otherwise, enters the *kli sharet* stage, redemption is foreclosed.

### Textual Basis

The *mishnah*'s conclusion — *shelo ne'emar pidyon ela bevehema* — points to the *pasuk* in Vayikra 27 as the source for the entire framework. The verse that provides for redemption of sacred animals is the textual basis from which the Rabbis derive that redemption is limited to animals. Everything else with *kedushat haguf*, whether *minchos*, *nesachim*, birds, wood, *levona*, or *kli sharet*, is outside the scope of that provision.

### Blemish Derasha

The *Gemara* concludes by identifying precisely where the *Torah* calls a blemished animal *tamei*. The relevant *pasuk* reads: *ve'im kol behema tmeia asher lo yakrivu mimena korban l'Hashem* — if it is an impure animal from which a *korban* cannot be brought to God. The *Gemara* asks: is this verse speaking about an actually *tamei* animal, such as a non-kosher species, or is it speaking about a blemished animal?

A nearby verse already addresses a genuinely *tamei* animal: *ve'im babehema hatmeia ufada be'erkecha* — if it is an impure animal, it may be redeemed at its assessed value. Since that verse already covers the literally *tamei* case, the first verse must be serving a different purpose. The phrase *asher lo yakrivu mimena korban l'Hashem* — from which a *korban* to God cannot be brought — is the key. This describes not a non-kosher species (for which the prohibition needs no elaboration), but rather a kosher animal that has become unfit for the *mizbe'ach* due to a *mum*. The animal belongs to a species that could normally be offered, but *this particular animal* cannot be brought — because it is blemished. That is what the *Torah* means by calling a *ba'al mum* "impure."

A further limitation is derived from the phrase *asher lo yakrivu*: the prohibition must be absolute — "from which they will not bring." This excludes a *mum over*, a transient blemish that will heal. Since such an animal *can* eventually be offered, it does not meet the criterion of absolutely not being *karev*, and therefore the Torah's redemption provision does not apply to it. Only a permanent *mum* — one that definitively prevents the animal from ever being brought as a *korban* — allows for redemption of a sanctified animal.

The *Gemara*'s identification of the *pasuk* and the *derasha* that follows thus ties the entire discussion together: the Torah's redemption mechanism was constructed specifically for blemished animals and does not carry over to *minchos*, *nesachim*, or the other categories listed in the *mishnah*. The contrast between animals and everything else is not a rabbinic fence but a biblical boundary, and the *mishnah*'s enumeration of birds, wood, *levona*, and *kli sharet* as a separate group will be parsed further in the next *daf*.
