# Shabbat 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Angels Challenge Torah

### Angels Question Gift

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *be-sha'ah she-alah Moshe la-marom* — when Moshe ascended on high to receive the *Torah*, the heavenly angels, the *malakhei ha-sharet*, said before the Holy One Blessed be He: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, what is this person born of woman, *yilud ishah*, doing among us? What is this mortal doing here?

God replied: *lekabbel Torah ba* — he has come to receive the *Torah*. The angels then pressed their objection.

### Torah Belongs in Heaven

The angels said: *hamudah genuzah she-genuzah lecha t'sha me'ot shiv'im ve-arba'ah dorot kodem she-nivra ha-olam* — this precious, hidden treasure that was concealed for You 974 generations before the world was created, *atah mevakesh litnah le-vasar va-dam* — You wish to give it to flesh and blood? They cited the verse: *Mah enosh ki tizkerennu u-ven adam ki tifkedennu* — what is a person that You should remember him, a human being that You should take notice of him? And they concluded with: *Hashem Adoneinu mah addir shimcha be-chol ha-aretz asher tenah hodecha al ha-shamayim* — let Your glory, let the *Torah* which is Your glory, remain in heaven. There is enough of Your greatness manifest in the earth; keep this most glorious thing above.

### 974 Generations

The number 974 requires some unpacking. The generation that received the *Torah* was the 26th generation of human history: ten generations from Adam to Noach, ten from Noach to Avraham, and then Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, Levi, Amram, and Moshe. Since the *Torah* was meant to be given after a thousand generations, 974 generations were effectively compressed or set aside.

More fundamentally, this framing sets up a profound theological point: the *Torah* is not merely a set of instructions for how people should live their lives. It is something that preceded *beri'at ha-olam*, the creation of the world entirely — something that belongs, in a certain sense, in the divine realm rather than in the physical one. This is the weight behind the angels' argument, and it is precisely what Moshe will have to contend with in his response.

### Moshe Defends Humans

God told Moshe: *hachzer lahem teshuvah* — go and give them an answer. Moshe demurred: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, I am afraid they will burn me up with the breath of their mouths — how can I possibly argue with angels? God told him: hold onto the throne of My glory, *achoz be-chisse kevodi*, and give them your response. The *Gemara* cites the verse *me'achez penei kisse parshaz alav anano*, and explains *parshaz* as an acronym: *pireish Shaddai ziv* — God spread the radiance of His presence over Moshe and shielded him, so that he could stand and respond.

Moshe then addressed God directly — asking, as it were, rhetorically — what is written in this *Torah* that You are giving me? *Anochi Hashem Elohecha asher hotzeiticha me-eretz Mitzrayim* — I am the Lord your God who took you out of the land of Egypt. Moshe turned to the angels: Did you go down to Egypt? Were you enslaved to Pharaoh? If so, why should the *Torah* be for you? It does not speak to your experience; it speaks to ours.

He continued: what else is written in it? *Lo yiheyeh lecha* — you shall have no other gods. Are you living among nations who worship idols, that you need to be commanded against idolatry? What else? *Zachor et yom ha-Shabbat le-kadsho* — remember the Sabbath day to sanctify it. Do you perform labor during the week that you require rest? What else? *Lo tissa* — do not take God's name in vain. Do you engage in commerce, *masa u-matan*, that you might swear falsely in a business dispute? What else? *Kabeid et avicha ve-et imecha* — honor your father and mother. Do you have parents? What else? *Lo tirtzach, lo tin'af, lo tignov* — do not murder, do not commit adultery, do not steal. Is there jealousy among you? Is there an *yetzer ha-ra* between you? None of this applies to you. The *Torah* was made for beings who face these temptations and these realities.

### Angels Agree

*Miyad hodu lo l'Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — immediately all the angels agreed with God that He was right to wish to give the *Torah* to human beings. Notably, they agreed not with Moshe but with God — Moshe had directed his remarks toward God, even while making his points about human experience. The *Gemara* cites the verse that follows in the same *perek* of *Tehillim*: *Hashem Adoneinu mah addir shimcha be-chol ha-aretz* — the second time the verse is written, it does not include the phrase "give Your glory to heaven," because by then the angels had conceded: it should not remain bound up above. *Ve-ilu tenah hodecha al ha-shamayim lo ketiv* — that phrase no longer appears.

## Moshe's Gifts

### Angels' Gifts

The *Gemara* continues: *kol echad ve-echad na'asah lo oheiv* — every one of those angels became a dear friend to Moshe, and each one gave him something, some gift or secret teaching. The verse is cited: *alita la-marom shavita shevi lakachta matanot ba-adam* — You ascended on high, You took captivity captive, You received gifts from people. *Bishkhar shekru'cha adam* — as reward for their having called you merely a human being, you received gifts from them.

Even the angel of death gave Moshe a secret teaching, as the *Gemara* derives from the verse: *vayiten et ha-ketoret va-yechapper al ha-am* — Aharon placed the incense and atoned for the people when the plague broke out. Moshe sent Aharon to do this, and indeed *vaya'amod bein ha-meitim u-vein ha-chayyim va-te'atzar ha-maggeifah* — he stood between the dead and the living and the plague ceased. How did Moshe know that the *ketoret* would stop a *maggeifah*? *I lav d'amar lei* — had the angel of death not told him this secret, *mi hava yada* — how would he have known? It must be that this knowledge came to him from the angel of death during that very encounter in heaven.

### Ascending to Take

This *midrash* is a striking one. It underscores, first, that the *Torah* is not merely a communication from God to human beings about how to live — it is something with an eternal, supernal nature, something so spiritual that one could argue it does not even belong in a physical world. The angels make that argument, and Moshe has to demonstrate that the *Torah* also contains these other dimensions, the dimensions that speak directly to mortal existence. Yet Moshe's having to argue for it in the first place captures something important: the *Torah* as *hamudah genuzah*, as a hidden divine treasure, remains at its core something that transcends mere practical instruction.

The *Maharsha* notes that the same verses — *mah enosh ki tizkerennu* — appear in another *midrash* as well, in the context of the angels objecting to the very creation of human beings. Why create *basar va-dam*? What value do they have? The parallel is not coincidental. One could read the two *midrashim* as related stages of a single argument: the angels first objected to creating human beings at all, and then objected to giving them the *Torah*. But the *Maharsha* suggests that the value of creating human beings lies precisely in their capacity to strive for the divine — and the giving of the *Torah* is the culmination of that process. You create something that has both a physical and a spiritual component, and you give it the *Torah* as the means of actualizing its spiritual dimension.

The *Maharsha* further connects this to the image of Moshe holding onto the *kisse*, the throne of glory. What entitles human beings to receive the *Torah*? Not that they are purely physical — in that case the angels would be entirely correct. What entitles them is precisely that they can reach toward the divine. *Achoz be-chisse kevodi* — the fact that we can hold onto, can aspire toward, the throne of glory is what makes us appropriate recipients of the *Torah*, whose very role is to help us actualize the divine within us.

There is also something evocative in the phrase *alita la-marom shavita shevi* — you ascended on high and took as spoils — which calls to mind the broader human motif of ascending to take something from a realm where resistance is met. The resistance here, it must be emphasized, comes from the angels; it is God who wishes to give the *Torah*. That distinction matters greatly. The angels represent the argument that something so wholly spiritual cannot or should not be brought into the physical world. God's insistence that it should be given represents the counter-truth: that the *Torah* bridges both realms and belongs, ultimately, with those who can reach for the divine even while dwelling in the physical.

## Angels Challenge Torah…

### Rashi on 974

Rashi explains the 974 generations as follows: *b'otan tsha me'ot ve-shiv'im ve-arba'ah dorot she-kadmu Torah la-olam* — in those 974 generations that preceded the *Torah*'s entry into the world. The *Torah* speaks of *davar tziva le-elef dor* — a word He commanded for a thousand generations — meaning that a thousand generations should have existed before the *Torah* was given. But *ra'ah ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu she-ein ha-olam mitkayem be-lo Torah kol kach* — God saw that the world could not sustain itself for so long without the *Torah* — *ve-he'eviran ve-lo bera'an* — so He set aside those 974 generations and did not create them, instead bringing the world into existence just 26 generations before the giving of the *Torah*. Without the *Torah*, humanity would have descended into chaos; the generations of Noach demonstrate well enough what can happen to a world left without divine guidance for too long.

## Satan's Failed Quest

### Satan Searches

*Ve-amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — the same Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught: when Moshe descended from before the Holy One Blessed be He, having won his case and received the *Torah*, the *Satan* came before God and asked: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, where is the *Torah*? It emerges that somehow the *Satan* had missed the entire exchange — *Tosafot* brings a *midrash* indicating that God had distracted the *Satan* and sent him elsewhere during those events.

God replied: *Netatiha la-aretz* — I gave it to the earth. The *Satan* took this literally, and went to the earth. He asked: *Torah heikhan hi*? Where is the *Torah*? The earth replied: *Elohim hevin darkah ve-hu yada et mekoma* — God understands its path; ask Him where it is. So the *Satan* went to the sea. The sea said: *Ein imadi* — it is not with me. He went to the depths: *Ein bi* — it is not in me. The *Gemara* cites all of this from the relevant verses in *Iyov*: *Tehom amar lo vi hi ve-ha-yam amar ein imadi* — the depths say it is not within me, the sea says it is not with me. *Avaddon va-mavet amru be-oznenu shamanu shim'ah* — destruction and death say, we have heard only a report of it. The *Gemara* does not develop the reference to *Avaddon va-mavet* further, leaving it as a final indication that the *Torah* exceeds even the broadest expanses of the physical world.

### Satan to Moshe

The *Satan* returned to God and reported: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, I searched throughout all the earth, through land and sea and the depths, and I cannot find it. God said: *Lech etzel Ben Amram* — go to the son of Amram. The choice of the name *Ben Amram* is pointed: it emphasizes the entirely mortal, human nature of the person being directed to, *yilud ishah* — born of a woman, a child of ordinary people.

### Moshe Declines

The *Satan* went to Moshe and asked: *Torah she-natan lecha ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu heikhan hi* — the *Torah* that God gave to you, where is it? Moshe responded: *Ve-chi mah ani she-natan li ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu Torah* — who am I that God should have given me the *Torah*? He did not deny having it — notice — but he refused to claim it as his own.

God then said to Moshe: *Moshe badai attah* — Moshe, are you being deceptive? You are making it sound as though you do not have it. Moshe replied before God: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, *hamudah genuzah yesh lecha* — this is the very same language the angels had used — this precious hidden treasure that you have, *she-attah mishta'ashea bah be-chol yom* — with which You take pleasure every day, which is almost God's companion in its intimacy of relationship. *Ani ahazik tovah le-atzmi* — should I take credit for myself? The *Torah* was not given because of me or because we deserved it. It was God's choice to give it. I will not present myself as its spokesman or claim personal credit for its being in our possession.

### Torah Named After

God responded: *ho'il u-mi'atat atzmecha* — since you have made yourself small, since you refused to take personal credit for this gift, *tikra al shimcha* — it will be called by your name. *Zikhru Torat Moshe avdi* — remember the *Torah* of Moshe my servant.

This is a striking reversal. The message of this *midrash*, bracketing for a moment the role of the *Satan*, is to underscore once again that the *Torah* in its most essential nature is something supernal, belonging to the divine realm. We cannot claim that we deserve it, that Moshe was so brilliant he earned it. Ultimately it was God's decision to give it, and Moshe's humility in refusing to claim credit is precisely what makes him worthy of having his name attached to it forever.

What remains somewhat difficult is the specific role the *Satan* plays in this narrative. Why is it the *Satan* who is searching? One compelling reading offered is that the *Satan* is making a different kind of argument from the one made by the angels: the earth, the sea, and the depths cannot contain the *Torah* — it is vaster than the entire physical world, which means that any finite human being who thinks of himself as full, as a repository deserving of credit, can never truly hold it. Only one who considers himself empty — who is genuinely humble — can continue to absorb and receive *Torah* without limit. The *Satan* goes to all these vast physical expanses and finds nothing; only when he reaches the man who says "who am I?" does he find where the *Torah* truly resides. In this reading, the *Satan*'s journey through the breadth of creation is itself the demonstration that the *Torah* cannot be contained by the physical world, and that it is human humility — the quality Moshe exemplifies — that creates the vessel capable of receiving it.

## Sixth Hour Confusion

### Sixth Hour Means

*Ve-amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: what is meant by the verse *vayar ha-am ki boshesh Moshe* — the people saw that Moshe had delayed? Do not read *boshesh* as "delayed," but rather *ba'u sheish* — the sixth hour had arrived. When Moshe ascended to the heavens, he told Israel: at the end of forty days, *be-techilat sheish* — at the beginning of the sixth hour of the day, I will return.

### Satan Confused Time

At the end of those forty days, the *Satan* came and *irvev et ha-olam* — he confused the world. He said to the people: where is Moshe your teacher? They said: he went up on high. He said to them: the sixth hour has come and he has not yet returned. They did not pay attention to him. He said: he is dead — and still they did not pay attention. Then *hera lahem demus mitato* — he showed them some vision of Moshe's funeral bier in the sky, and it was then that they turned to Aharon and said *ki zeh Moshe ha-ish* — this man Moshe — pointing, as it were, at what they had seen.

The *irvev et ha-olam* — the confusion of the world — may well refer to the *Satan* having darkened the sky or made it cloudy, so that it was genuinely difficult to determine exactly what time it was. Around midday, when the sun is near its apex, the difference between the eleventh hour and the beginning of the sixth can be hard to discern. This is the reading of *Tosafot*. Rashi, however, reads it differently: Rashi argues that a mere hour's confusion could not account for the events that follow, since the making of the *eigel* and Moshe's descent only occurred the following morning. Rashi therefore suggests that the *Satan*'s confusion was not about the hour of the day but about whether the first day counted among the forty — that is, whether the count had already completed or not. The difficulty with Rashi's reading is that it sits uneasily with the *midrash*'s explicit emphasis on the sixth hour.

## Names of Sinai

### Sinai Etymology

One of the *rabbinic* students asked Rav Kahana: have you heard what the name *Har Sinai* signifies? Rav Kahana replied that *Sinai* read backwards yields *nes* — it is the mountain on which miracles were done for Israel. The student objected: if so, it should have been called *Har Nissai*. Rav Kahana then suggested: perhaps *Sinai* relates to *siman* — a good sign was made there for Israel. Again the student objected: then it should have been *Har Simannai*. At this point Rav Kahana suggested that the student go instead to Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, *d'ma'aini be-aggadata* — who engage carefully with *aggadata* and will give him a satisfying answer.

### Five Wilderness Names

Rav Chisda and Rabba the son of Rav Huna both say: what is *Har Sinai*? It is *har she-yardah sin'ah la-akum alav* — the mountain on account of which enmity descended upon the nations of the world, because they are now jealous that we have the *Torah*.

Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina teaches that there are five names for the wilderness. *Tosafot* notes that the *Gemara* is about to identify five locations which are not clearly the same place in the *Torah*, but we proceed here treating them as a unified set, each name capturing a different dimension of the event. The wilderness is called *Midbar Tzin* — *tzin* like *tzivui*, commandment, because that is where Israel was commanded. It is called *Midbar Kadesh* — *kadesh* like *kidush*, sanctification, because Israel was sanctified there through receiving the *Torah*. It is called *Midbar Kedemot* — the wilderness of that which is ancient and primordial, because the *Torah* which preceded the world was given on it. It is called *Midbar Paran* — *paran* like *paru ve-ravu*, because Israel was fruitful there: Rashi explains, citing *dechol echad nit'abrah ishto zachar be-mitzvat shuvu lachem le-oholeichem* — each man's wife became pregnant with a male child when they returned to their tents after the period of separation at *Har Sinai*. Rashi himself adds *ve-lo yadana heicha remizah* — he does not know precisely where this is hinted at in the text, but the *Gemara* appears to assume it. And the fifth is *Midbar Sinai* — the same theme as the *Har Sinai* name, *she-yardah sin'ah la-akum alav*.

### Chorev Desolation

*U-mah shemo* — and what is the actual proper name of the mountain? *Chorev shemo* — its name is Chorev. This is challenged by Rabbi Abbahu, who holds that the proper name of the mountain is in fact *Har Sinai* — and all the *drash* notwithstanding, it is simply the name. According to Rabbi Abbahu, the name *Har Chorev* is descriptive rather than proper: *lamah nikra Har Chorev* — why is it called *Har Chorev*? *She-yardah churvah la-akum alav* — because desolation descended upon the nations of the world on account of it, since we received the *Torah* there, which was good for us and ultimately difficult for them.

This introduces a larger theme — present in other contexts as well, notably the *Gemara* in *Avoda Zara* — about the relationship between the nations and the *Torah*. At the time of *yemot ha-Mashiach*, God will call the nations to account for not having observed the commandments; they will respond that they were never offered the *Torah*. The name *Chorev*, with its connotation of desolation befalling the nations, is an allusion to this entire complex of questions about our relationship with the non-Jewish world.

## Red Strand/Sin

### Grammar of Verse

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishnah*'s discussion of the red strand tied to the *se'ir ha-mishtaleiach*, the scapegoat, which is derived from the verse *im yihyu chata'eichem ka-shanim ka-sheleg yalbinu* — even if your sins are like crimson, they will become white as snow. A grammatical question is raised: the verse uses *ka-shanim* — like crimson things, plural — yet contrasts this with *ka-sheleg*, snow, in the singular. Similarly, it does not say *ka-shlagim*, snows in the plural. Rabbi Yitzchak explains the irregularity.

### Deep Sin Cleansing

God says to Israel: *im yihyu chata'eichem ka-shanim halalu* — even if your sins were as long-standing as *mi-sheishet yemei Bereishit* — from the six days of creation itself, from the very beginning of the world — *ve-ad achshav* — until now — *ka-sheleg yalbinu* — I will whiten them. The point is not only that sins which are scarlet red in their severity can be cleansed, but that sins which are ancient and deeply rooted — ongoing across the full sweep of human history — can also be forgiven. The grammatical irregularity in the verse is thus pressed into expressing a temporal depth: not just deeply sinful, but long-sinning.

## Judgment Scene

### Forefathers Judge

Rava then interprets the verse *le-chu na ve-nivachah yomar Hashem* — come now and let us reason together, says God — *im yihyu chata'eichem ka-shanim ka-sheleg yalbinu*. Several grammatical anomalies are noted: it says *le-chu*, go, rather than *bo*, come; and it says *yomar Hashem*, God will say, rather than *amar Hashem*, God said. From these Rava derives: *le-atid lavo* — in the future time, God will say to Israel: go, *etzel avoteichem* — to your forefathers — *ve-yochi'chu etchem* — and they will rebuke you for your sins.

### Forefathers' Failures

Israel will respond: *Ribbono Shel Olam*, to whom shall we go? To Avraham? He was told *yado'a teda* — know that your descendants will be strangers — and he did not plead for compassion on our behalf. To Yitzchak? He blessed Eisav with dominion, saying *ka-asher tarid u-farakta ulo me-al tzavvarecha* — when you throw off their yoke — meaning he accepted Eisav's having dominion over us at certain times, and did not intercede for us. To Yaakov? God told him *Anochi eirei'd imcha Mitzrayimah ve-Anochi a'alcha gam alo* — I will go down with you and bring you up — acknowledging a long descent into exile ahead, and Yaakov did not plead for us either. Each of the *avot* failed to advocate for Israel at a critical moment, and so Israel cannot be confident of receiving compassion from their judgment.

### God as Judge

*Etzel mi nelech* — to whom then shall we go? *Achshav yomar Hashem* — You, God, be our judge. We place our trust in You alone.

### God's Compassion

*Ho'il u-telitem atzmechem bi* — since you have placed yourselves entirely in My hands, since you were unwilling to defer to anyone else and committed yourselves fully to My judgment — *im yihyu chata'eichem ka-shanim ka-sheleg yalbinu* — I will have compassion on you and cleanse you of your sins.

The larger message of this *drash* is somewhat difficult to assess simply, because it casts Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov in a surprisingly unflattering light. Perhaps the point is less about the *avot* themselves and more about the nature of *teshuvah*: true return requires that we give ourselves over entirely to God's judgment, without holding back or seeking more comfortable advocates. The sentiment finds its parallel in *Tachanun*, where we say *nafla na ve-yad Hashem* — let us fall into the hand of God — the same instinct to surrender judgment to God rather than to human mediators.

## Isaac Bears Burden

### Sin Calculation

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: what is meant by the verse *ki Atah Avinu ki Avraham lo yedanu ve-Yisrael lo yakiranu, Atah Hashem Avinu go'aleinu me'olam shemecha* — You are our Father, for Avraham does not know us and Israel does not recognize us; You, Hashem, are our Father, our Redeemer from eternity. The same theme continues in the parallel narrative: in the future, God will say to Avraham that his children have sinned. Avraham will say: perhaps I should refer this to Yaakov, who truly suffered through *tza'ar gidul banim*, the pain of raising children — he will have compassion. God then says to Yaakov: your children have sinned. Yaakov says: *yimachu al kdushat shemecha* — let them be blotted out for Your holy name if they have sinned.

At this God declares: *lo be-savei ta'ama ve-lo be-dardakei eitza* — not in the elders is there sense, and not in the young is there counsel. Avraham is too old; Yaakov is too young. God turns to Yitzchak.

God says to Yitzchak: your children have sinned against Me. Yitzchak replies: *Ribbono Shel Olam* — *banai* — my children? And not *banecha* — Your children? When they said *na'aseh* before *nishma* — when they accepted the *Torah* before even hearing all of its contents — You called them *beni bechori Yisrael*, My son, My firstborn. When they do what pleases You, they are Your children; now that they have sinned, they are only mine?

Yitzchak then proceeds to make a careful accounting of how much Israel could actually have sinned.

### Sin Calculation

*Kama shnotav shel adam* — how long is a human life? Seventy years. Take away the first twenty, *d'lo anashta aleihu* — for which God does not administer punishment, since full moral culpability only begins at twenty. That leaves fifty. Take away twenty-five years of *leiluta* — nighttime, when one is at home and not exposed to the same temptations — leaving twenty-five. Take away twelve and a half years for time spent in *tefilah*, eating, and attending to bodily needs. What remains is twelve and a half years — the maximum time across a full lifetime during which Israel could actually have sinned against God in any meaningful way.

### Isaac's Offer

*Im ata sovel et kulam, mutav* — if You are willing to bear all of that burden of sin, that is the best outcome. *Ve-im lav* — and if not — *palga alai u-palga alecha* — let us split it, half upon me and half upon You, and we can be *mocheil* to one another.

### Akeidah Merit

And if one insists on saying the full weight of sin falls on Yitzchak — *ha karivti nafshi kamach* — I offered up my entire self before You at the *Akeidah*. The willingness to give everything entitles him to secure atonement for all of Israel.

### Praise to God

*Patchu ve-amru* — they then opened and said: *ki Atah Avinu* — You are our Father — and they directed this at Yitzchak, who had been willing to advocate for them. *Ki Avraham lo yedanu ve-Yisrael lo yakiranu* — Avraham and Yaakov did not recognize or advocate for us, but you did. Yitzchak responds: *ad she-atem mekalsin li, kalsu le-Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — while you are praising me, you should be praising the Holy One Blessed be He. *U-machvei lahem Yitzchak le-Hakadosh Baruch Hu be-einav* — and Yitzchak indicated with his eyes toward God. *Miyad nas'u eineihem la-marom* — immediately they raised their eyes heavenward and said: *ki Atah Hashem Avinu go'aleinu me'olam shemecha* — You, Hashem, are our Father and our Redeemer. The *pasuk* is thus read as a progression: first Israel praises Yitzchak as their true father given his intercession — *ki Atah Avinu* — while Avraham and Yaakov did not come to their aid; and then Yitzchak redirects them, so that the verse concludes with *Atah Hashem Avinu*, directed entirely at God.

## Jacob's Merit

### Chains to Kindness

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*: it was fitting that Yaakov our father should have descended to Egypt in chains of iron, *b'shalshela'ot shel barzel*. The decree of *gei-yiheyeh zar'acha* — that Israel would be strangers in a foreign land — would logically have entailed being taken down as slaves, in chains. *Ella she-zechuto garamah lo* — but his merit transformed it into something far gentler.

### Ropes of Love

The verse states: *be-chevlei adam emshachem be-avotot ahavah* — in human ropes I will draw them, in bonds of love. *Va-ehyeh lahem ke-merimei ol al lecheyeihem* — I was like one who lifts the burden from their jaw. *Ve-at elav ochil* — I inclined toward them so that they could bear it. God brought Yaakov down to Egypt not in iron chains but gently, as a father lifting a burden from a child's face. This note concludes the *aggadic* section on something of a rehabilitative note regarding Yaakov, who had been cast in an unflattering light in the preceding *midrash*.

## Shiur Measures

### Wood Shiur

The *Gemara* now returns to the business of the *mishnah* — the *shiurim*, the minimum measures that determine liability for carrying various materials on *Shabbat*. *Ha-motzi etzim* — one who carries out wood on *Shabbat* is liable if he carried enough wood *ke-dei le-vashal beitzah kalah* — sufficient to cook a light egg, meaning a hen's egg, specifically to fry it with oil, which is a small amount — equivalent roughly to the size of a fig.

### Spice/Dye Shiur

*Tavlin* — spices: the minimum measure is *ke-dei letabel beitzah kalah* — enough to season this same light egg, a very small quantity, a pinch of salt, a pinch of pepper. *U-mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — different spices combine with one another: if one has a small amount of salt and a small amount of pepper, neither sufficient individually, they combine to reach the *shiur* together. Shells of nuts, shells of pomegranates, *istis* and *puah* — two types of plants — all used to produce dyes — are liable at the amount *ke-dei litzboa bahem beged katan pi sevachah* — sufficient to dye a small garment that would fit over the opening of a hairnet.

### Cleansing Shiur

*Mei raglayim* — urine — *neter u-vorit*, *kamulya va-ashlag* — all various cleansing agents — are liable at the amount *ke-dei lechabbes beged katan pi sevachah* — sufficient to launder that same small garment at the opening of a hairnet. Rabbi Yehuda says: the measure is *ke-dei le-ha'avir et ha-ketem* — sufficient to remove a menstrual stain from a small garment. Whether this represents a smaller or larger quantity than the preceding measure is left without explicit resolution.

### Reed Shiur

*Kaneh* — a reed — is liable at the size *ke-dei la'asot kulmos* — sufficient to fashion a quill for writing. *Im hayah av o merusas* — if it was too thick or if it was broken and could not be used to make a quill — then the measure reverts to *ke-dei levashel beitzah kalah she-be-vetzim* — enough to cook the lightest egg, scrambled in a pan — which is the standard measure for firewood.

### Standard Use Rule

The *Gemara* notes that this ruling about a broken reed might have seemed obvious: since it cannot be used as a quill, of course one measures it only as firewood. The *chiddush* might be thought to concern only the broken reed, since it has no other use. But what about ordinary wood, which could potentially be used for other small purposes — for instance, as a tooth in a key? One might have thought that since wood could serve such functions, even a minimal quantity would suffice to establish liability. The *Gemara* teaches that this is not so: *kol shehu* — an arbitrarily small amount — is not the standard. The standard use of wood is as firewood, and the *shiur* is determined by that standard use: *ke-dei levashel beitzah kalah*.
