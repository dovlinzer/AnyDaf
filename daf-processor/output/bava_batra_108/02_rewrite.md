# Bava Batra 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Charitz Structure

### Boundary Ditches

Before turning to *Perek Yesh Nochlin*, it is worth completing the discussion from the previous day regarding the *charitz* and the *ben charitz*. The *mishnah* had established that when a field is sold, the purchaser bears the obligation to designate from the land he has acquired an area for a *charitz* — an outer ditch — a *ben charitz* — a narrower inner ditch — and a *geder*, a fence or wall. To illustrate: if a seller divides a field and sells half to Reuven, Reuven now bears the responsibility to dig the *charitz* and *ben charitz* and to construct the *geder*.

The *gemara* at the close of 107b states: *tana: charitz mibachutz u-ven charitz mibifnim* — the outer element is the *charitz* and the inner element is the *ben charitz*. Both are situated on the exterior side of the *geder*, such that the order from the outside in is: *charitz*, *ben charitz*, and then *geder*.

### Measurements

The *charitz* is six *tefachim* wide. The *ben charitz* is three *tefachim* wide. Between the two ditches there must be one *tefach* of space. So the total boundary structure consists of a six-*tefach* outer ditch, a one-*tefach* gap, a three-*tefach* inner ditch, and then the wall — amounting to a substantial buffer zone before the field itself begins.

### Animal Prevention

The *gemara* explains the rationale for both ditches. One might ask: why not simply dig the *charitz* alone and position the wall there? The *gemara* answers: *aidei de-ravach ka-eima begaveih ve-kafetza* — because the *charitz* is six *tefachim* wide, a larger animal could stand within it and use that position to leap over the fence. The effectiveness of the ditch depends precisely on its creating an awkward gap in front of the wall, but a sufficiently wide ditch defeats this purpose by providing a stable launching position.

One might then ask the reverse: why not simply dig the *ben charitz* alone, a narrow ditch that the animal cannot stand inside? To this the *gemara* responds: *aidei de-katin ka-eima asfata ve-kafetza* — because it is so narrow, an animal could stand right next to it and jump over. Therefore both ditches are necessary. The *charitz* prevents the animal from getting close enough to the wall, and the *ben charitz* prevents the animal from finding solid footing immediately adjacent to the wall. This is a principle analogous to what we know from *Kilayim*, where the minimum spacing of a fence is designed to prevent a baby goat from passing through — *lavud* being three *tefachim*, corresponding to the width of a baby goat.

### Seller Obligation

A note in some texts suggests that, given these requirements, the seller must provide the buyer with an additional ten *tefachim* of land to account for the space consumed by this boundary structure. The source is not attributed to a specific authority, but it appears to reflect at least one view among the *rishonim*. The *gemara* itself does not state this explicitly. Alternatively, one could understand it as a matter of pricing: the seller, knowing that this obligation will consume part of the purchased area, prices the land accordingly.

One might raise the question of why the obligation falls upon the buyer at all — why not permit the buyer to waive his own protection? The most plausible answer is that the construction of boundary ditches became a binding convention, and like much of communal infrastructure, once universally adopted it protects not only one field but the entire surrounding area. As Robert Frost observed, good fences make good neighbors; if every field maintains a proper boundary, animals do not spread from one field to the next.

### Irrigation Use

It bears mentioning that in *Bavel*, these ditches served a secondary function as irrigation channels. This was already relevant in the earlier *sugyot* of the fourth and fifth *perakim*, where questions arose about the precise boundaries being sold — which *metzarim* are included, how to account for diagonal lines, and how to maximize access to a river or an irrigation ditch. So while the *gemara* here is primarily focused on the animal-prevention function of the *charitz* structure, the dual utility of such ditches as irrigation channels would have been well understood in context.

## Nachala Concept

### Inheritance Model

With that, we can say *hadran alach Beit Kor* and turn to *Perek Yesh Nochlin* with a brief introduction to the *mitzvah* — or *mitzvot* — of *nachalot*. The Torah does not presume a world in which a person's property becomes ownerless upon death or reverts to some form of common ownership. Rather, the Torah's model is that property should transfer to the deceased's family members. The entire system of *nachalot* is built on this foundational premise.

### Nachala Etymology

One of the notable etymological observations about the word *nachala* is its connection to the word *nachal*, a stream. Rabbi Israel Moshe Chazzan — one of the great Sephardic rabbinic figures of the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, who served as Chief Rabbi in Rome and in Israel — wrote an extended responsum on a complex case of *nachalot* and devoted considerable attention to this etymology. He observes that the word *nachala* evokes a stream flowing downhill. The general principle of inheritance is that it flows downward — from parent to child — just as water flows downhill. But what happens when the path is blocked? The stream backs up and finds a new channel, flowing perhaps laterally or even slightly upward before making its way down again. This captures the halachic structure of inheritance: property flows first to children, and if there are no children, it reverts upward to the father or across to siblings and then down through their lines.

### Mitzvot Count

*Rambam*, *Ramban*, and *Saadia Gaon* disagree about how many *mitzvot* are contained within the laws of *nachalot*. The *Rambam* counts a single *mitzvah* — *dinei nachalot* — which he enumerates as *mitzvah* 248, the last positive *mitzvah* in his *Sefer HaMitzvot*. In his understanding, this is a *mitzvah* incumbent upon the *beit din* to adjudicate cases of inheritance correctly.

*Ramban* objects, arguing that there is also a *mitzvat lo ta'aseh* — a prohibition against the father improperly favoring one son over another with respect to the *bechor's* double portion: *lo yivaker ben ha'ahuvah al pnei ven hasnu'ah babechor*. That yields a second *mitzvah*. *Rasag* — Saadia Gaon — in his poem on the 613 *mitzvot* appears to count a third *mitzvah* as well: *nachalat habat*, the daughter's right of inheritance when there are no sons. The *Rambam* presumably subsumes all three within his single *mitzvah*, while *Rasag* counts them as three distinct *mitzvot*: the general law of *nachalot*, the prohibition against improperly assigning or denying the *bechor's* double portion, and the daughter's inheritance.

### Modern Wills

A final introductory point of practical importance: all of the foregoing concerns what happens when inheritance takes place automatically, without prior arrangement. But of course, many people today write wills and establish other mechanisms for distributing their estates. It is essential to keep in mind that a will should not only be civilly binding in the secular legal system but should also carry halachic validity. Otherwise, one risks a collision between the halachic rules of inheritance and the designations one has made. Every case of *nachalot* involves death — sometimes multiple deaths — and, as with *Masechet Yevamot*, one becomes somewhat accustomed to discussing families in which tragedy has struck. One of the greatest practical sources of conflict in many families is precisely inheritance, and the halachic framework exists in part to bring order to what might otherwise become deeply divisive disputes.

## Mishna Categories

### Four Categories

The *mishnah* opens with an elegant four-part structure. *Yesh nochalim u-manchilim* — there are those who both inherit and bequeath, meaning that within a given relationship the inheritance can flow in either direction depending on who dies first. *Ve-yesh nochalim ve-lo manchilim* — there are those who inherit but cannot bequeath upward or across. *Manchilim ve-lo nochalim* — there are those who can bequeath but cannot inherit. And *lo nochalim ve-lo manchilim* — there are relationships where neither inheriting nor bequeathing is possible. The *mishnah* then provides three examples for each category.

### Father-Son Case

In the category of *nochalim u-manchilim* — those who both inherit and bequeath — the *mishnah* lists: *ha-av et ha-banim*, the father with respect to his sons; *ve-ha-banim et ha-av*, the sons with respect to their father; and *ve-ha-achim min ha-av*, paternal brothers. The first two items together illustrate that the father-son relationship is bilateral: if the father dies, the sons inherit; if a son predeceases the father, the father inherits from the son. The relationship works in both directions.

Paternal brothers are also in this category: if one brother dies without sons, his property passes to his brother — this is precisely the setting in which *yibbum* arises. The *pasuk* states *yakum al shem achiv ha-met* — the child born of the *yibbum* union shall carry the name of the deceased brother. There is a *machloket tannaim* in *Masechet Yevamot* over whether *yakum al shem* means the child must literally be named after the deceased brother, or whether *le-nachala* — for purposes of inheritance — is the operative meaning. The *halacha* follows the view that it refers to inheritance, though at least one *amora* holds that *ein mikra yotzei midei peshuto* — the plain meaning of the *pasuk* cannot be entirely displaced — suggesting the child should also be named accordingly. In practice, *yibbum* is exceedingly rare today. *Rav Ovadia Yosef* attempted to encourage the Sephardic community, in keeping with the *psak* in *Shulchan Aruch*, to prefer *yibbum* over *chalitza*, whereas Ashkenazic practice has universally preferred *chalitza*. That effort met with considerable resistance, and the present state of practice in Israel is uncertain.

### Mother Cases

The *mishnah* then lists the category of *manchilim ve-lo nochalim* — those who can bequeath but cannot inherit: *ha-ishah et baneha*, a woman whose property passes to her sons; *ve-ha-ishah et ba'alah*, a woman whose property passes to her husband; and *achei ha-em*, the brothers of a woman. These relationships allow property to flow in one direction only — downward or across — but not back up or in the other direction.

In the category of *nochalim ve-lo manchilim* — those who inherit but cannot bequeath upward — the *mishnah* lists *ha-ish et imo*, a man inheriting from his mother; *ha-ish et ishto*, a man inheriting from his wife; and *bnei achayot*, the sons of two sisters. In this last case, if two sisters each have their own family lines, the nephew can inherit from his aunt — the property flows in that direction — but the aunt cannot inherit from her nephew, because his property will pass through his father's side. The structure is deliberately non-parallel: the *bnei achayot* example focuses on a different generation than the *ha-ishah et baneha* example, and the *gemara* will have work to do in explaining why these examples are not strictly redundant.

### Maternal Brothers

Finally, *ve-ha-achim min ha-em* — maternal brothers — fall into the category of *lo nochalim ve-lo manchilim*. Two half-brothers who share only a mother cannot inherit from one another, because each one will inherit through his own father's family. *Nachala* is fundamentally structured along the patriarchal family line. Even when daughters or sisters inherit from one another, it is because they remain connected through a common male ancestor. The maternal bond alone does not create an inheritance relationship in halachic terms.

## Order Question

### Why Order

The *gemara* opens its analysis of the *mishnah* with a pointed structural question. Since the relationship of father and sons is listed under *nochalim u-manchilim* — meaning inheritance flows both ways — why does the *mishnah* list *ha-av et ha-banim* before *ha-banim et ha-av*? They represent the same bilateral relationship; both directions are subsumed under the same category. The *gemara* phrases this as: *mai shena de-katani ha-av et ha-banim be-reisha? litni ha-banim et ha-av be-reisha?* — what is the reason the *mishnah* lists the father-to-sons direction first? It could equally well have listed the sons-to-father direction first.

### Tragic First?

The *gemara* answers: *chada de-atchulei be-poreanuta lo matchilinan* — we do not begin with that which is a *poreanuta*, a tragic circumstance. When a son predeceases his father and the father inherits from the son, that represents a disruption of the natural order. The *Rashbam* notes that while every case of *nachalot* involves death and can be spoken of as tragic, there is nonetheless a crucial distinction. Children burying their parents is *darko shel olam* — the normal course of life. A parent burying a child is an inversion of that natural order and is therefore far more painful. So the *mishnah* places the father-to-sons direction first, even though both directions are equally valid, in order not to open with the more tragic case.

### Derasha vs Tragic

The *gemara* continues with a *ve-od* — an additional argument — and this one is particularly striking. There is a broader principle across *Shas*, seen for instance at the opening of *Masechet Yevamot*, that a *tanna* tends to place the case derived by *drasha* — scriptural exposition from *Torah she-be-al peh* — before the case that is explicit in *Torah she-bi-chtav*. The *tannaim* had such devotion to *Torah she-be-al peh* that they preferred to give it primacy in the arrangement of the *mishnah*.

Now, it is explicit in the *pasuk* that a son inherits from his father: *ish ki yamut u-ven ein lo* — when a man dies and has no son — and *ve-ha'avartem et nachalato le-vito* — his inheritance passes to his daughter. The case of the father inheriting from the son, by contrast, is derived by *drasha*, not stated explicitly. According to the general principle, then, the *tanna* ought to have placed the *sons-to-father* direction first, since it is the one derived through *drasha* and therefore more beloved. Yet the *mishnah* does not do this. Why? Because doing so would mean opening with a case of *poreanuta* — a parent inheriting from a predeceased child. So the *tanna* faces two competing considerations: the pull of *Torah she-be-al peh* favoring the *drasha*-derived case first, and the principle of not beginning with tragedy. The latter wins, and the explicit *pasuk* case — father bequeathing to sons — appears first.

## Hierarchy From Pesukim

### Father Priority

Having established the order within the *mishnah*, the *gemara* now turns to the *drasha* itself: *de-tanya: she'ero — zeh ha-av* — the *pasuk*'s word *she'ero*, meaning "his relative," is interpreted to refer to the father. This teaches that *ha-av kodem le-achim* — the father takes priority over brothers as an heir. That is, if a man dies and his father is still living, the inheritance goes to the father before it goes to any brother.

### Closest First

From here the *gemara* derives a general principle: *talmud lomar hakorov — korov korov kodem* — the phrase "his close relative" (*she'ero hakorov elav*) teaches that whichever relative is the closest inherits first. The word *hakorov*, with the definite article, implies precedence: the *most* proximate heir takes precedence over more distant ones. Every subsequent question in this sugya is an application of this principle — the *gemara* must determine, in any given configuration of potential heirs, who ranks as the closer relative.

### Script Sources

The *pesukim* themselves provide relatively sparse guidance. Three principles can be extracted: first, a son inherits from his father; second, if there is no son, the daughter inherits; and third, if there are no children at all, the inheritance reverts to the father's relatives — brothers or more distant paternal kin — in order of proximity. The derivation of everything beyond these three basic rules depends on the principle of *korov korov kodem* and on various *drashot*.

## Son vs Brother

### Relative Distance

With the principle of *korov korov kodem* established, the *gemara* confronts its first major question: how do we know that a son is a closer relative than a brother? Both are potential heirs in the event of death without children in the direct line. The question is: if a man dies, does his property go to his son or to his brother first — assuming, for purposes of the analysis, that the father is already deceased?

### Role Filling

The *gemara* answers by appealing to two independent areas of *halacha* in which a son fills his father's role: *marbeh ani et ha-ben sheken kam tachat aviv le-ye'uda u-sdei achuza* — I give weight to the son because in two domains the son stands in his father's place. The language *kam tachat aviv* — he stands in his father's shoes — is evocative. It points to one of the fundamental conceptual questions surrounding *nachala*: does inheritance mean that property changes hands, or does it mean that a person steps into another's role? These may be two distinct models, and different contexts within the laws of *nachalot* may reflect different underlying theories.

The first domain is *ye'udah*. At the opening of *Parshas Mishpatim*, the Torah describes the case of the *ama ha-ivriya*, the young woman who has been indentured to work. Part of the arrangement of *ye'ud* is the possibility of marriage: the woman may be designated as a wife either for the master who purchased her or for his son — *o le-vno ye'adena*. The son thus literally fills his father's place in this capacity, stepping into the *ye'udah* relationship that the father had established.

### Brother Yibum

The second domain is *sdei achuza*, ancestral fields. The son inherits his father's role with respect to the ancestral portion in a manner that differs from the inheritance of ordinary purchased property, and this "role-filling" character of the inheritance distinguishes it. In contrast, the *gemara* notes that the brother fills a role in *yibbum*: *marbeh ani et ha-ach sheken kam tachat achiv le-yibbum* — I give weight to the brother because in the domain of levirate marriage the brother stands in for his deceased brother. When one brother dies without children, the surviving brother enters into the role of the deceased by marrying his widow.

### Yibum Limitation

However, the *gemara* immediately qualifies this: *klum yesh li yibbum ela be-makom she-ein ben* — there is no *yibbum* except precisely where there is no son. If the deceased had a son — or even a daughter, or if his wife is pregnant — there is no *yibbum*. This is a decisive piece of evidence. The *gemara* states: *ta'ama de-ika hai pircha, ha lav hachi hava amina ach adif* — it is only because of this consideration that I would not have said the brother is the superior heir. Without the fact that *yibbum* is inapplicable in the presence of a son, one might have argued that the brother's role in *yibbum* reflects a relationship even stronger than that of the son. The very fact that *yibbum* is displaced by the existence of a son demonstrates that the son's claim is paramount.

### Two Against One

The rhetorical structure of the *gemara*'s conclusion is: *teipuk lei de-hacha tartei u-de-hacha chada* — in two arenas the son enjoys the "standing in his father's place" relationship (*ye'udah* and *sdei achuza*), while the brother enjoys it in only one (*yibbum*). Two against one: the son is the closer relative. *Tosafot* note that there are in fact additional cases on both sides that the *gemara* does not cite here, so the ratio is not strictly two-to-one in the sources as a whole. But the *gemara* chose these three examples to make the point.

## Property Flow

### Downhill Flow

The *gemara* articulates a general organizing principle of the entire *nachala* system: inheritance flows downhill to children before it flows uphill to parents or sideways to siblings. Rabbi Israel Moshe Chazzan's metaphor of the stream captures this precisely. The default direction of *nachala* is always toward the next generation.

### Daughter Case

This is illustrated by the case of a daughter. If a man dies leaving no sons, his property passes to his daughter. And if that daughter then dies, her property will pass to her own children — it continues to flow downward. So long as there is a downstream heir, the property moves in that direction.

### Revert to Father

Only when there is no downstream heir does the property revert upward. If a daughter inherits from her father but dies without children and unmarried, the property must then flow back up and across — to her father's family, to an uncle, or to whatever paternal relative stands next in line by the principle of *korov korov kodem*. This is the "backing up" that Rabbi Israel Moshe Chazzan describes: when the stream's downhill path is blocked, the water reverses course and finds a different channel, but it will always seek to flow downward again wherever possible.

The *gemara* leaves the continuation — specifically the further analysis of *sdei achuza gufa*, the distinctive character of ancestral fields in the inheritance calculus — for the following *daf*, where it will examine whether the *sdei achuza* argument is itself an independent proof or simply a restatement of the same principle already established through *ye'udah*.
