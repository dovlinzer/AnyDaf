# Eruvin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kora & Mechitza

### Kora Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָבוֹי שֶׁנִּפְרַץ מִצִּידּוֹ — בְּעֶשֶׂר, מֵרֹאשׁוֹ — בְּאַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** if it was breached **from its side,** the side wall of the alleyway, carrying within the alleyway is prohibited if the breach is **ten** cubits wide. But if it was breached **from its front,** the wall that faces the public domain, carrying within the alleyway is prohibited even if the breach is only **four** handbreadths wide.

We pick up at the very bottom of 6b. Before proceeding, it is worth recalling the themes from the previous session, as they recur throughout today's material. The central question surrounding the *kora* — the beam placed at the entrance of a *maboi* — was whether its primary function is that of a *heker*, a marker or sign meant to be noticeable, or whether it functions as a *mechitza*, a form of virtual wall. This distinction has far-reaching implications for how we understand the legal standing of these rabbinic constructs.

### Lechi Width

A related question applies equally to the *lechi*, the vertical post. Tosafos, in the brief note titled *Arba Amos* on 5b, addresses a seemingly paradoxical ruling: when a *lechi* becomes too wide, it loses its status as a *lechi* and requires an additional *lechi* in its place. Tosafos asks how this could be according to the view that a *lechi* functions as a *mechitza* — surely a wider post would only constitute a better wall, not a worse one? Tosafos answers: *mikol makom heker kzat bayinan* — even according to the view that a *lechi* functions as a *mechitza*, a minimal element of *heker* is still required. The lesson is that these categories are not binary. Something may be primarily a *mechitza* while retaining a secondary dimension of *heker*, or vice versa. Both the *kora* and the *lechi* inhabit this ambiguous middle ground.

### Maboi Size

The other major topic from the previous session was the minimum size of a *maboi* that qualifies for the rabbinic leniencies governing carrying. The question was whether the minimum length is *arba tefachim* — a quite small measurement — or *arba amos*, a full four cubits. This debate was argued back and forth and remains a backdrop for the discussion that follows.

## Side Break Size

### Eser Amot Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מִצִּידּוֹ בְּעֶשֶׂר — דְּאָמַר פִּתְחָא הוּא, מֵרֹאשׁוֹ נָמֵי נֵימָא פִּתְחָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **What is the difference** such that carrying is prohibited due to a breach **from the side** only if the breach is **ten** cubits? This is **because** you **say** that up to ten cubits **it is** deemed **an entrance.** If the breach is **in the front, let us also say it is an entrance,** and carrying should be permitted if the breach is less than ten cubits.

With these themes in mind, we turn to the ruling of Rav Chana bar Rava in the name of Rav: *maboi shenifratz mitzido be-eser* — if a *maboi* has a breach in its side wall, a gap of up to *eser amot* is tolerable. This is consistent with a principle encountered previously: since the entrance of a *maboi* may itself be as wide as *eser amot*, a gap of that size in a wall can be treated as an opening rather than a break. Furthermore, as was discussed previously, when a side wall of the *maboi* suffers a breach but a stub of wall at least *arba tefachim* wide remains at the corner near the front, a gap of up to *eser amot* in the breach can be accommodated.

### Front Break Arba

However, the ruling continues: *me-rosho be-arba* — at the head of the *maboi*, the front opening, the tolerance is only four. The natural question is what "four" refers to here, since the front of the *maboi* is by definition open. Rashi explains the scenario: the original opening of the *maboi* was wider than *eser amot*, necessitating the construction of an additional wall to narrow it to an acceptable width. The question then becomes how stable that constructed wall must be. And the ruling is striking: even a gap as small as *arba tefachim* in this wall renders it invalid. It cannot tolerate any break larger than *arba tefachim*, whereas the side wall could tolerate a gap of up to *eser amot*. Two questions immediately arise: why is such a tiny gap problematic, and why should the front be so much stricter than the side?

### Corner Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּפְרַץ בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית, דְּפִתְחָא בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית לָא עָבְדִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** We are dealing with **a case where the breach is in a corner.** Since **people do not make an entrance in a corner,** a breach of this kind cannot be viewed as an entrance, and if the breach is larger than four handbreadths it must be sealed.

The Gemara poses the question directly: *mai shna mitzido be-eser* — why on the side is a gap of up to ten tolerated, *d'amar pitcha hu*, because we interpret it as an opening? *Me-rosha nami neima pitcha hu* — why not say the same of a gap at the front? Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua answers by specifying the scenario: *k'gon shenifratz b'keren zavit* — what we are dealing with is a breach at the corner. And *pitcha b'keren zavit lo avdi inish* — people do not place doorways at corners. A gap at a corner cannot be reclassified as a doorway, and therefore it retains the legal character of a breach rather than an opening. That is why even *arba tefachim* — significant enough to count as a gap, yet not capable of being construed as a doorway given its location — is problematic. Below *arba tefachim*, there is a degree of tolerance, analogous to the principle of *lavud*, where continuity is assumed; but once the gap reaches *arba tefachim* and cannot be reread as an entrance, it constitutes a clear breach. The same logic appeared in an earlier context regarding a *chatzer* opening into a *maboi* at the corners: corner breaches are categorically different from frontal openings.

### Rav Huna Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה בְּאַרְבָּעָה. וְכֵן אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא: לָא תִּפְלוֹג עִלַּאי דְּרַב אִיקְּלַע לְדַמְחַרְיָא וַעֲבַד עוֹבָדָא כְּווֹתִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַב בִּקְעָה מָצָא וְגָדַר בָּהּ גָּדֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna** disagreed with Rav Ḥanin bar Rava and **said:** There is no distinction between the side and the front, for in **both this** case **and that,** a breach of up to **four** handbreadths is allowed. **And so Rav Huna said to Rav Ḥanan bar Rava: Do not dispute me, as Rav** himself **arrived at** a place called **Damḥarya and performed an action,** i.e., issued a practical ruling, **in accordance with my** opinion. Rav Ḥanan bar Rava **said** in response **to him:** No proof can be brought from that incident, for in that case **Rav found an** unguarded **valley and fenced it in,** i.e., Rav saw the need to add a safeguard and was therefore stringent in this case. His ruling, however, was not generally applied.

This leads to the opposing opinion. Rav Chana bar Rava's ruling — that the side of the *maboi* tolerates a gap of up to *eser amot* — was transmitted in the name of Rav. But Rav Huna takes a far stricter position: *echad zeh v'echad zeh be-arba* — both the front and the side tolerate only *arba tefachim*. This is a position that was alluded to in yesterday's material, though it may have been easy to lose track of amid the back and forth.

Rav Huna confronts Rav Chanan bar Rava directly: *lo tiphal galai* — do not disagree with me. He argues that Rav himself, when he visited the place called Macharya, ruled in practice that a gap invalidated the *maboi* — a gap that was apparently no more than *arba tefachim*. How then can Rav Chana bar Rava cite Rav in support of the *eser amot* position?

Rav Chana bar Rava's response is elegant: *Rav bika matza ve-gadar ba-geder* — Rav found an open field and built a fence around it. This is a well-known metaphor for a rabbinic stringency enacted not because the law strictly requires it, but because the community's level of knowledge or scrupulousness was insufficient to be trusted with the full latitude of the law. The word choice is apt: we are discussing breaks in walls, and here Rav is said to have demanded more wall — a stricter standard — precisely because of the realities of that particular place. In other words, Rav's ruling at Macharya was a local, contextual stringency, not a statement of the underlying *halacha*. The *eser amot* tolerance remains the general principle in Rav's name; the stricter ruling was a *geder*, a protective fence.

### Bent Maboi Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִיתְּמַר: מָבוֹי עָקוֹם — רַב אָמַר: תּוֹרָתוֹ כִּמְפוּלָּשׁ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: תּוֹרָתוֹ כְּסָתוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Rav Huna's opinion stands to reason, as it was stated** that the *amora'im* disagree about the following issue: With regard to **a crooked,** L-shaped **alleyway** that opens onto the public domain at both ends, **Rav said: Its law is like** that of an alleyway that is **open** on two opposite sides, and it must be treated in a manner suitable for such an alleyway, i.e., an opening in the form of a doorway must be constructed at both ends, or else such an opening must be constructed at the point where the two arms of the alleyway meet and a side post or a cross beam must be placed at each end. **And Shmuel said: Its law is like** that of an alleyway that is **closed** on one side, and all that is necessary is a side post or a cross beam at each end.

The Gemara then reports that Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak finds Rav Huna's position more logically compelling — *kevatei de-Rav Huna mistabra* — and derives support from a separate Talmudic discussion about a bent *maboi*, which requires a brief detour.

## Bent Maboi

### Rav-Shmuel Bent

A *mavoy akum* is a *maboi* configured at a right angle — rather than running straight from one end to the other, it bends ninety degrees. The standard picture drawn by Rashi is simple: one corridor leads into another at a perpendicular angle, and the far end of the second corridor opens into a *reshut ha-rabim*. The practical question is how to treat such a configuration for the purposes of the *lechi* and *kora* leniencies. 

By way of context: a *mavoy mefulash* — a straight-through alley open at both ends — cannot be addressed simply with a *lechi* or *kora*; it has only two walls and is treated more stringently. A *mavoy satum* — a dead-end alley — has three walls and can be addressed with a *lechi* or *kora* at its one opening. The bent *maboi* raises a question about which category applies.

Rav says *toraso kemefulash* — it is treated like a straight-through *maboi*, meaning the *lechi* and *kora* leniencies do not suffice on their own. Shmuel says *toraso kesatum* — it is treated like a dead-end *maboi*, and a *lechi* or *kora* at the opening is sufficient. Rashi presents two understandings of Shmuel's position: one in which a single *lechi* or *kora* at the outer opening suffices, since each leg of the bend can be considered independently as a dead-end; another in which some marker is needed at the inner corner as well. The discussion of precisely which end requires the *lechi* or *kora* is part of this analysis, though the consensus is that only one is needed.

### Bent Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר — בְּהָא לֵימָא שְׁמוּאֵל תּוֹרָתוֹ כְּסָתוּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the particular circumstances of the case: **With what are we dealing? If you say** that the width of the alleyway at the point of the turn is **more than ten** cubits wide, **in this** case, **would Shmuel say that its law is like** that of an alleyway that is **closed** on one side? With an opening of that size, it must be considered like an alleyway that is open on both ends.

The Gemara now uses this dispute to adjudicate the earlier debate. *Bemai askinan* — what are the circumstances of the bent *maboi* disagreement? *Eleima beyoser me-eser* — if the width of the arms of the bend exceeds *eser amot*, then how could Shmuel say it is like a dead-end? The break in the side wall created by the perpendicular corridor would already exceed *eser amot* and would be clearly intolerable even by the most lenient standard. So the dispute must be operating in a case where the width of each corridor is *eser amot* or less.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו בְּעֶשֶׂר, וְקָאָמַר רַב תּוֹרָתוֹ כִּמְפוּלָּשׁ, אַלְמָא פִּירְצַת מָבוֹי מִצִּידּוֹ בְּאַרְבָּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, are we not** dealing with a case where the width of the alleyway at the point of the turn is **ten** cubits or less, **and Rav** nonetheless **said** that **the law** of such an alleyway **is like that of** an alleyway that is **open** on both ends. **Apparently, a breach in the side** wall **of an alleyway** renders it prohibited to carry even if it is only **four** handbreadths wide, in accordance with the opinion of Rav Huna.

Now the Gemara focuses on Rav's position: *decamar Rav toraso kemefulash* — Rav says the bent *maboi* is treated as open-ended. If Rav genuinely held that a side break of less than *eser amot* is always tolerable, why would the fact that one corridor opens perpendicularly into the other cause the entire configuration to be treated as a *maboi mefulash*? There is an obvious answer: because the corridor does not merely create a break in the wall — it connects all the way through to a *reshut ha-rabim*. It is not an isolated gap but a passage through which the public moves. The Gemara states this explicitly: *shani hasam dekabaki rabbim* — that case is different because the masses pass through. A throughway to a *reshut ha-rabim* creates a more serious problem than a simple gap in a wall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא, שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּקָא בָּקְעִי בַּהּ רַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥanan bar Rava** argues that the cases cannot be compared: **It is different there,** in the case of the L-shaped alleyway, **for many people cross through** the opening from one arm to the other. Since in practice the alleyway is open to regular traffic, the ruling is stringent even with regard to a small breach.

The Gemara therefore concludes that no proof about Rav's general view on side breaks can be derived from the bent *maboi* case. The argument then runs in reverse as well: the Gemara challenges the view that even *arba tefachim* is problematic by pointing to the ruling of Rav Ami and Rav Assi — who taught that a gap of up to *eser amot* in the front wall is tolerable as long as *arba tefachim* of wall remains at the corner. This was discussed in the previous session.

## Side Break Size (II)

### Gedudim Foundation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַב הוּנָא סָבַר אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא בָּקְעִי בַּהּ רַבִּים, מַאי שְׁנָא מִדְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי? הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא גִּידּוּדֵי, הָכָא דְּלֵיכָּא גִּידּוּדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Can it be **inferred** from this **that Rav Huna holds** that **even if many people** do **not cross through** the opening, a breach of four handbreadths still prohibits carrying? **What is the difference** between this case and the case of the ruling of **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** cited earlier, that an upright board of four handbreadths suffices in order to allow a breach of up to ten cubits? The Gemara answers: **There, there are remnants of a wall** that render it difficult to pass through the breach, and therefore that breach does not annul the partitions. However, **here, there are no remnants of a wall.**

The Gemara resolves this tension by distinguishing between the presence and absence of *gedudim* — foundation remnants of the original wall. The ruling of Rav Ami and Rav Assi, permitting a gap of up to *eser amot*, applies where the original wall has been breached but its foundations are still partially present. Those remnants serve two functions: they maintain a degree of physical and visual continuity with the original wall, and they impede easy passage through the gap, preventing it from functioning as a convenient thoroughfare.

By contrast, the stricter position — that even *arba tefachim* is a problem — applies when there is no foundation at all. A clean gap, with nothing remaining of the original wall, can no longer claim the residual continuity that *gedudim* provide.

The picture that emerges from the entire discussion is thus more nuanced than any single ruling suggests. Both positions — *eser amot* and *arba tefachim* — come with exceptions. Even the authority who holds that a side break may be up to *eser amot* concedes that a gap feeding directly into a *reshut ha-rabim* is categorically more problematic. Even the authority who holds that *arba tefachim* is the maximum concedes that when *gedudim* remain, the tolerance extends to *eser amot*. The underlying *machlokes* concerns the baseline case: a gap in the side wall of a *maboi* with no remaining foundations and no direct connection to a *reshut ha-rabim*. There, the debate is whether a gap of up to *eser amot* is tolerable or whether anything exceeding *arba tefachim* constitutes a disqualifying breach.

One additional factor enters the analysis at this stage and will become important later: the concern with a breach is not solely about continuity of the wall but also about the creation of an additional entrance or exit. If the *lechi* or *kora* is understood at least in part as a *heker* — a marker designating the single point of entry and exit — then a gap of *arba tefachim* is significant not merely as a hole in the wall but because it is large enough to function as an independent entrance. This would create an unmarked point of access that the *lechi* or *kora* at the main opening does not address. This framing, already anticipated by Rashi and Tosafos, will recur in the discussions that follow.

On the specific question about the front wall: all authorities appear to agree that a breach at the corner is problematic at *arba tefachim*, since, as established, corners are not places where doorways are made.

## Reshut Harabim

### Four Domains

We now turn to a new and critical topic. The Gemara in Shabbat 6a — notably, the same daf number in a parallel tractate — lays out the foundational taxonomy of domains for Shabbat: *arba reshuyot l'Shabbat* — four domains. The *reshut ha-yachid* is the private domain. The *reshut ha-rabim* is the public domain. The *karmelit* is an intermediate domain governed by rabbinic prohibition. And the *makom patur* is a place exempt from any prohibition.

The definition of *reshut ha-rabim* is then specified. *Eizehu reshut ha-rabim — stratia u-platya gedola* — a major thoroughfare and a large public square. And also *mevo'ot ha-mefulashin* — alleys open at both ends. As the discussion of the *maboi* configuration already suggested, a side street that connects two major thoroughfares at both ends is itself classified as a *reshut ha-rabim*, in part because of the heavy traffic it bears and in part because it lacks the three-wall structure required for a more protected domain.

## Eruv Mechanisms

### Tzurat Hapetach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מְעָרְבִין דֶּרֶךְ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים? עוֹשֶׂה צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח מִכָּאן, וְלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה מִכָּאן. חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: עוֹשֶׂה דֶּלֶת מִכָּאן וְדֶלֶת מִכָּאן וּכְשֶׁהוּא יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס נוֹעֵל. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עוֹשֶׂה דֶּלֶת מִכָּאן, וְלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How does one render a public thoroughfare fit** for carrying **by** means of **an *eiruv*? He constructs an** opening in the **form of a doorway from here,** on one side of the thoroughfare, **and a side post or a** cross **beam from here,** on the other side. **Ḥananya** disagrees and **says:** This is the subject of an early dispute between *tanna'im*, for **Beit Shammai say: He constructs a door from here,** on one side, **and a door from here,** on the other side, **and when he exits and enters, he must lock** the door. It is not sufficient to construct a symbolic door; rather, there must be a door that actually closes. And **Beit Hillel say: He constructs a door from here,** on one side, **and a side post or a** cross **beam from here,** on the other side.

A *baraita* asks: *keitzad me'arvin derech reshut ha-rabim* — how does one make an *eruv* around a *reshut ha-rabim*? Before proceeding, a terminological clarification is essential. What we commonly call an *eruv* today — the wires on telephone poles and the posts beneath them — is more precisely a *tzurat ha-petach*, a doorframe-shaped construction that functions as a virtual wall. The *eruv* proper, in Talmudic usage, refers to the communal joining together, typically symbolized by a shared loaf of bread or *matzah*, that allows the residents within a defined space to carry as if they share one domain. The *baraita*'s question — how does one make an *eruv* around a *reshut ha-rabim* — therefore means: what construction is necessary to transform a *reshut ha-rabim* into an enclosed space within which the *eruv* can then operate?

The *baraita* answers: *oseh tzurat ha-petach mikan ve-lechi o kora mikan* — one makes a *tzurat ha-petach* on one side and a *lechi* or *kora* on the other. This seems relatively manageable. The logic is that a *reshut ha-rabim* traversing a walled city already has walls on two sides; the only gaps to close are the two openings at either end. A *tzurat ha-petach* on one end and a *lechi* or *kora* on the other would appear to complete the enclosure. Rashi's illustration is the familiar image of a major colonnaded street bisecting a walled city, with gates at each end — precisely the configuration visible in the famous mosaic map of Yerushalayim in the Cardo.

A *tzurat ha-petach* is always a stronger construction than a *lechi* or *kora*, which is itself only a partial *tzurat ha-petach*. So the *baraita*'s ruling seems to offer a workable approach to enclosing a *reshut ha-rabim*.

## Reshut Harabim (II)

### 600,000 Criterion

Before the Gemara continues, a crucial criterion embedded in Rashi's commentary must be examined. Rashi, at every occurrence of the term *reshut ha-rabim* on this page, adds: a city in which 600,000 people (*shishim ribo*) are present. This criterion does not appear in the Gemara text itself. Tosafos, in his comment on *keitzad me'arvin derech reshut ha-rabim*, cites Rashi's full definition — a *reshut ha-rabim* is sixteen *amot* wide, open from one gate to the other (*mi-sha'ar le-sha'ar*), and has 600,000 people — and then notes that the same criterion appears in the *Halachot Gedolot* and the Geonim. The requirement of 600,000 originates with the *Geonim*, not with the Gemara proper.

The practical consequence of this criterion is enormous. It allowed the *Rishonim* — and authorities in subsequent generations — to argue that in their time, there were no cities with 600,000 people traversing these streets, and therefore no genuine *reshut ha-rabim* existed. This opened the door to making *eruvin* where, under a stricter definition, it would be impossible.

### Eruv Possibility

Whether this criterion is halachically binding is itself a major *machlokes* among the *Rishonim*. Those who accept it can argue that virtually no modern city contains a true *reshut ha-rabim*, clearing the way for *eruvin* to be constructed with *tzurat ha-petach* alone. Those who reject it are left with a far more demanding standard, and the question of whether an *eruv* is even possible in a given city becomes much more fraught.

The 600,000 figure is itself subject to further debate. Does it mean that 600,000 people actively traverse the specific street in question? Does it refer to the total population of the city, on the assumption that the street is available to all of them? And if it means active daily usage, the traffic counts of even the busiest streets in major cities become relevant data. The George Washington Bridge, by some counts the busiest crossing in the state of New York, carries roughly 300,000 vehicles per day, with the vast majority single-occupancy. Streets discussed in the context of neighborhood *eruvin* — such as those in Flatbush — fall far short of even that figure. The pedestrian count for Times Square on an average day, however, may approach the relevant threshold, which explains why that area is not currently included within an *eruv*. All of these contemporary applications flow directly from the *Geonim*'s threshold introduced here.

## Eruv Mechanisms (II)

### Beit Shammai-Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מִי מִיעָרְבָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִי שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים מִשְּׁנֵי צִידֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, עוֹשֶׂה לֶחִי מִכָּאן וְלֶחִי מִכָּאן, אוֹ קוֹרָה מִכָּאן וְקוֹרָה מִכָּאן, וְנוֹשֵׂא וְנוֹתֵן בָּאֶמְצַע. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְעָרְבִין רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a fundamental question: **Can a public domain be rendered fit** for carrying **by** means of **an *eiruv*? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Furthermore, Rabbi Yehuda said:** **One who has two houses** opposite each other **on the two sides of the public domain,** and he wishes to carry from one house to the other on Shabbat via the public domain, **he may place a side post from here,** on one side of one of the houses, **and** an additional **side post from here,** on the other side. **Alternatively,** he may place **a** cross **beam from here,** from one end of one house, **and** an additional cross **beam from here,** from the other side of the house, **and** then **he may carry** objects **and place** them in the area **between** them, for in this manner he turns the middle area into a private domain. The Rabbis **said to him: One cannot render a public domain fit** for carrying **by** means of **an *eiruv* in this manner.** Apparently, there is no way to establish an absolute public domain fit for carrying by means of an *eruv*.

The *baraita* continues. *Chananya* introduces a *machlokes* between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. According to Beit Shammai: *oseh delet mikan v'delet mikan* — one must construct actual doors on both sides, and moreover, *u-keshehu yotze ve-nichnas no'el* — when one enters and exits, one must close those doors each time. This is an extraordinarily stringent standard, effectively requiring that the major thoroughfare be sealed off upon every individual act of passage. According to Beit Hillel: *oseh delet mikan ve-lechi o kora mikan* — one side requires a real door, and the other side may be addressed with a *lechi* or *kora*. Beit Hillel does not explicitly require closing the door each time, which is a meaningful leniency over Beit Shammai.

### Gate Closure Need

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא בְּכָךְ הוּא דְּלָא מִיעָרְבָא, הָא בִּדְלָתוֹת מִיעָרְבָא, וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, אִילְמָלֵא דַּלְתוֹתֶיהָ נִנְעָלוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה — חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים! וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: הָנֵי אֲבוּלֵּי דְמָחוֹזָא אִילְמָלֵא דַּלְתוֹתֵיהֶן נִנְעָלוֹת — חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions its previous conclusion: **And if you say** that it is only **in this manner,** by way of a side post or a cross beam, **that** a public domain **cannot be rendered fit** for carrying, **but by** means of **doors** it can be **rendered fit for carrying. But** this is not true, as **didn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said: With regard to **Jerusalem, were it not for** the fact that **its doors are locked at night, one would be liable for** carrying in **it** on Shabbat, **because** its thoroughfares are regarded **as a public domain?** This shows that the presence of a door is not sufficient to render it permitted to carry in a public domain; rather, the door must actually be locked. **And** similarly, **Ulla stated:** With regard to **the** city **entrances [*abbulei*] of Meḥoza,** which meet the criteria for a public domain, **were it not for the fact that their doors are locked, one** would be **liable for** carrying in **them, because** they are regarded as **a public domain.** Apparently, without the actual locking of doors it is impossible to establish a public domain fit for carrying by means of the symbolic partitions of a side post or a cross beam. If so, how can the Sages in the *baraita* argue about how to establish a public domain fit for carrying?

At this point the Gemara interjects with a far more fundamental challenge: *reshut ha-rabim mi mi'arva* — since when is it possible to make an *eruv* in a *reshut ha-rabim* at all? The Gemara cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi Yehuda described how someone with two houses on opposite sides of a *reshut ha-rabim* could carry between them: *oseh lechi mikan ve-lechi mikan o kora mikan ve-kora mikan* — a *lechi* or *kora* at both ends of the intervening stretch. The Sages rejected this outright: *ein me'arvin reshut ha-rabim be-chach* — this is not sufficient to make an *eruv* in a *reshut ha-rabim*. Rabbi Yehuda's position, the Gemara notes elsewhere, is that two walls already suffice *de-oraita* to remove an area from *reshut ha-rabim* status — a view not accepted by the *halacha*.

The Gemara then anticipates an objection: perhaps the Sages only rejected *lechi* and *kora*; a door or *tzurat ha-petach* might suffice? The Gemara answers with two dramatic examples. Rabbah bar Bar Hana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: had the gates of Yerushalayim not been locked at night, one would be liable for carrying in the main streets as in a *reshut ha-rabim* — despite the fact that Yerushalayim was a walled city with gates. The walls and the gates themselves are insufficient; what transforms the status is that the gates were actually locked for part of the day.

### Locked Gates

Ulla adds the example of the gates of Mechuza: *elmale daltoteihen ninalot* — had they not been locked, one would be liable for carrying within as in a *reshut ha-rabim*. And Rashi, consistent throughout the page, appends at each of these examples that these cities had 600,000 inhabitants. The principle is clear: a genuine *reshut ha-rabim* cannot be enclosed for *eruv* purposes through any form of virtual construction — not *tzurat ha-petach*, not *lechi*, not *kora*, and not even unlocked doors. Only the actual closing of gates for part of the day accomplishes the transformation. This makes an *eruv* in a true *reshut ha-rabim* essentially impossible for any community that is not living within a walled, gated city whose gates are actually locked.

## Side Streets

### Baraita Reread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: כֵּיצַד מְעָרְבִין מְבוֹאוֹת הַמְפוּלָּשִׁין לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, עוֹשֶׂה צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח מִכָּאן וְלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **Rav Yehuda said:** The wording of the *baraita* must be emended so that **this is what it says: How does one render alleyways that** are not themselves public domains but are **open** on two opposite sides **into the public domain fit** for carrying **by** means of **an *eiruv*? He constructs an opening** in the **form of a doorway from here,** on one side of the alleyway, **and a side post or a** cross **beam from here,** on the other side.

How then does the earlier *baraita* — which appeared to offer a workable formula of *tzurat ha-petach* on one side and *lechi* or *kora* on the other — make any sense? Rav Yehuda resolves the difficulty through a careful rereading of the *baraita*'s language. The phrase *keitzad me'arvin derech reshut ha-rabim* does not mean "how does one make an *eruv* in the *reshut ha-rabim* itself." It means: how does one make an *eruv* for streets that lead through the *reshut ha-rabim* — that is, the *mevo'ot ha-mefulashin le-reshut ha-rabim*, the side streets that feed into the *reshut ha-rabim*. The more lenient formula — *tzurat ha-petach* on one end and *lechi* or *kora* on the other — applies not to the major thoroughfare itself but to the cross streets that connect to it.

This reinterpretation is significant. The side streets are classified as *reshut ha-rabim* not because they independently meet all the criteria of a *reshut ha-rabim* but because they feed into one. Their status is, as it were, derivative. It is therefore appropriate that the conditions for enclosing them are more lenient. With a *tzurat ha-petach* at one end providing a virtual third wall — since the physical walls of the buildings on either side already constitute two walls — and a *lechi* or *kora* at the other, the side street is effectively enclosed. The *tzurat ha-petach* functions as a complete wall for these purposes. Once three walls are in place, a *lechi* or *kora* suffices for the fourth.

### Rav-Shmuel Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר. רַב אָמַר: הִילְכְתָא כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כַּחֲנַנְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* differed on how the *halakha* is to be decided with regard to this issue. **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **the** anonymous **first *tanna*** of the *baraita*, and it is sufficient to have the form of a doorway on one side and a side post or cross beam on the other side in order to render it permitted to carry in an alleyway that is open on two opposite sides to the public domain. **And Shmuel says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥananya,** following the position of Beit Hillel, who also require a door on one side.

Within this framework of side streets, there is a further dispute between Rav and Shmuel. Rav rules *halacha ke-tanna kamma* — like the more lenient first opinion in the *baraita*, which requires only a *lechi* or *kora* on each end of the side street, without even a *tzurat ha-petach*. Shmuel rules *halacha ke-Chananya* — like Chananya, who frames the matter as a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, with the accepted ruling according to Beit Hillel requiring at least one actual door.

### Door vs. Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לַחֲנַנְיָה אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל, אוֹ אֵין צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: בְּדִידִי הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וְאָמַר לִי שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages concerning the position of Beit Hillel: According **to Ḥananya, in accordance with** the opinion **of Beit Hillel, must** this door **be locked or need it not be locked? Come** and **hear** a proof from that **which Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said,** who, as mentioned earlier, rules in accordance with Beit Hillel: The door **need not be locked. And similarly, Rav Mattana said** that **Shmuel said:** The door **need not be locked. Some say** that **Rav Mattana said: A case** involving this very issue **happened to me, and Shmuel said to me:** The door **need not be locked.**

This raises an important question: according to Chananya following Beit Hillel, one side requires a real door. Must that door be closed? The Gemara records multiple formulations in Shmuel's name — transmitted by Rav Yehuda and by Rav Nachman — all converging on *ein tzarich lin'ol*: the door does not need to be closed. A further tradition from Rav Masna, who reported a direct ruling from Shmuel in an actual case, also concludes that closing is not required. The *halacha* we follow in practice is Rav's more lenient ruling — *lechi* or *kora* without even requiring a door — but the discussion in Shmuel's name at least establishes that even under the stricter position, the door need not actually be closed during use.

### Neharde'a Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב עָנָן: צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל אוֹ אֵין צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל? אֲמַר לְהוּ: תָּא חֲזִי הָנֵי אֲבוּלֵּי דִּנְהַרְדָּעָא דְּטִימָן עַד פַּלְגַיְיהוּ בְּעַפְרָא, וְעָיֵיל וְנָפֵיק מָר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **They raised a dilemma before Rav Anan** with regard to this issue: **Need** the door **be locked or need** it **not be locked? He said to them: Come** and **see these** city **entrances of Neharde'a** that open on two opposite sides into the public domain, the gateways of **which were filled up halfway with earth,** so that the doors themselves could not possibly be locked. **Mar Shmuel** regularly **goes in and out** through them, **but has never said anything to** the people of Neharde'a about them. This shows that it is not necessary for the doors to be locked.

The question of closing the door is pressed further by the testimony concerning the gates of Neharde'a. Rav Anan was asked whether the door must be closed or not. He answered by describing what he had seen: the gates of Neharde'a had been so filled up with dirt that they could not fully close — they were blocked up to half their height — and yet Mar Shmuel had walked in and out of those streets without objection, neither stopping people from carrying nor indicating that anything was amiss. This would appear to demonstrate that actual closure is not required.

### Ability Close

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: הָנָךְ מְגוּפוֹת הֲוַאי. כִּי אֲתָא רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: פַּנְּיוּהּ לְעַפְרַיְיהוּ. לֵימָא קָסָבַר רַב נַחְמָן: צָרִיךְ לִנְעוֹל? לָא, כֵּיוָן דִּרְאוּיוֹת לִנְעוֹל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין נִנְעָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana** rejected this proof and **said: Those** doors in Neharde'a **were** partially **blocked,** and therefore there was no need to lock them, but in general, the door of an alleyway that opens on both sides into the public domain must be locked. The Gemara relates that **when Rav Naḥman came** to Neharde'a, **he said: Clear away the earth,** so that the doors can be locked. The Gemara attempts to understand Rav Naḥman's instruction: **Let us say that Rav Naḥman holds** that the door of an alleyway that is open on two opposite sides **must be locked.** The Gemara explains: **No,** this is not proof. In order for carrying to be permitted in such an alleyway, it is enough that the doors **be fit to be locked, even if they are not** actually **locked.**

Rav Kahana, however, resists this proof. *Hanach megupos havay* — those gates were partially closed. The dirt blocking them constituted a partial obstruction, and perhaps a partially closed gate suffices to meet the standard; the example therefore cannot prove that a fully unclosed and unobstructed gate is acceptable.

The matter is then clarified by a report of Rav Nachman: *panyu l'afreih* — he ordered the dirt removed. The Gemara asks whether this means Rav Nachman holds that the door must be capable of being closed. The answer is: *keivan de-rauya li-nna'el af al pi she-eina* — as long as the door is capable of being closed, it is sufficient even if it is not actually closed. The operative requirement is not closure in practice but closability in principle.

## Summary

### Hierarchy

The discussion on this *daf* establishes a clear hierarchy of requirements depending on the nature of the domain in question. If one is dealing with a genuine *reshut ha-rabim* — defined by the Geonim's criterion as a thoroughfare sixteen *amot* wide, open from gate to gate, in a city of 600,000 — then an *eruv* is effectively impossible unless one is in a walled city whose gates are actually locked for part of the day. No *tzurat ha-petach*, no door, no *lechi* or *kora* will suffice on its own.

If one is dealing with a side street that feeds into a *reshut ha-rabim* — a *mavoy mefulash le-reshut ha-rabim* — the requirements are more lenient. At minimum, according to the position of Rav, a *lechi* or *kora* at each end suffices. According to Shmuel following Chananya and Beit Hillel, at least one side requires a real door, though that door need not be closed, only closable.

If one is dealing with a space that is not a *reshut ha-rabim* at all — either because the 600,000 criterion is not met or because the area otherwise lacks the characteristics of a major public thoroughfare — then a *tzurat ha-petach* at each opening can serve as a complete virtual wall, and the *eruv* can be constructed entirely through this mechanism.

### UWS Example

The direct application of these principles can be seen in the construction of the eruv on the Upper West Side of Manhattan. The builders of that *eruv* did not rely solely on *tzurat ha-petach* for every boundary. Recognizing the possibility that certain streets might be argued to meet the threshold of a genuine *reshut ha-rabim*, they installed physical structures — collapsible fabric constructions — at key points along the perimeter, designed to be pulled out and physically block the street. Once a year, at approximately three in the morning, these structures were deployed, effectively closing off the boundary streets for that moment. This annual act of closure satisfied, at least in principle, the requirement that the openings be capable of being closed — and perhaps more than merely capable, since the closure was actually performed. The structures remained in place throughout the year to demonstrate their closability. This practice, undertaken under the authority of the municipal government with the support of Mayor Koch overriding objections from the Parks and Transportation Departments, is a direct and vivid application of the *Gemara*'s requirement that even for side streets connected to a *reshut ha-rabim*, the boundary construction must reflect the possibility of controlling passage — whether through actual closure, as Beit Shammai demands, or at least through closability in principle, as the *halacha* ultimately requires.
