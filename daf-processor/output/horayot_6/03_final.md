# Horayot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Avoda Zara Korban

### Par Sheni Exegesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּפַר שֵׁנִי בֶן בָּקָר תִּקַּח לְחַטָּאת״? אִם לְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֵם שְׁנַיִם, וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וַעֲשֵׂה אֶת הָאֶחָד חַטָּאת וְאֶת הָאֶחָד עֹלָה לַה׳״! [אֶלָּא] יָכוֹל [תְּהֵא] נֶאֱכֶלֶת חַטָּאת לַלְוִיִּם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּפַר שֵׁנִי״ – שֵׁנִי לָעוֹלָה, מָה עוֹלָה לָא נֶאֱכֶלֶת, אַף חַטָּאת לֹא נֶאֱכֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "And a second young bull shall you take as a sin-offering"** (Numbers 8:8), in the context of the purification of the Levites in the aftermath of the sin of the Golden Calf? **If** this is written **to teach that they are two,** i.e., that there is an additional bull sacrificed, **isn't it already stated: "And sacrifice the one as a sin-offering, and the other as a burnt-offering, unto the Lord"** (Numbers 8:12)? **Rather,** one **might** have thought that the **sin-offering** that is sacrificed **for the Levites shall be eaten** like other sin-offerings. To counter this, **the verse states: "And a second bull,"** to underscore that it is **second to the burnt-offering** and similar to it: **Just as a burnt-offering is not eaten, so too,** this **sin-offering is not eaten.**

The discussion opens with a *brayta* cited three lines from the bottom of 5b. Rabbi Shimon asks: what is the *talmud lomar* of the phrase *par sheni* — "the second ox" — in the passage in *Behaalotecha* describing the dedication of the *Levi'im* to the service of the *Mishkan*? Two *parim* are brought, one as a *chatas* and one as an *olah*. Since the *Torah* has already stated explicitly "you shall make one of them a *chatas* and one an *olah*," we already know there are two animals. What, then, is added by the word *sheni*?

Rabbi Shimon answers: one might have thought this *chatas* is an ordinary *chatas* that is eaten — specifically, eaten by the *Kohanim*. Therefore the *Torah* says *par sheni*, meaning it is paired with the *olah*: just as the *olah* is not eaten, so too this *chatas* is not eaten. This is a special *chatas* whose blood is brought on the inner *mizbe'ach* and whose meat is not consumed.

### Ezra Account Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: ״הַבָּאִים מֵהַשְּׁבִי הַגּוֹלָה הִקְרִיבוּ [עֹלוֹת] לֵאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל פָּרִים שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר וְגוֹ׳ הַכֹּל עוֹלָה״. הַכֹּל עוֹלָה סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁחַטָּאת עוֹלָה! אֶלָּא הַכֹּל כָּעוֹלָה: מָה עוֹלָה לֹא נֶאֱכֶלֶת – אַף חַטָּאת לֹא נֶאֱכֶלֶת. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הֱבִיאוּם, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁעָשׂוּ בִּימֵי צִדְקִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, Rabbi Yosei said** that it is stated with regard to those who returned to Zion from Babylonia in the days of Ezra: "The children of the captivity **who came out of exile sacrificed burnt-offerings to the God of Israel, twelve bulls** for all Israel, ninety-six rams, seventy-seven lambs, twelve goats as a sin-offering; **all this was a burnt-offering** unto the Lord" (Ezra 8:35). The question arises: Does it **enter your mind** to say: **"All this was a burnt-offering"?** Is it **possible that a sin-offering** is **a burnt-offering? Rather,** say: **All this** was **like a burnt-offering. Just as a burnt-offering is not eaten, so too,** this **sin-offering is not eaten, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It was as atonement **for idol worship** that **they brought them; and Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It was atonement **for the idol worship that they practiced during the days of Zedekiah.**

Rabbi Yose then draws on the same principle. A *pasuk* in Ezra (8:35) describes those who returned from the Babylonian exile bringing *korbanot*: twelve oxen as *olot* on behalf of all Israel — representing the twelve tribes — ninety-six rams, seventy-seven lambs, and twelve goats as a *chatas*. The verse then concludes: *hakol olah* — "all of it is an *olah*."

The *Gemara* immediately objects: *hakol olah salka deitach*? How can everything be an *olah* when the text just identified the twelve goats as a *chatas*? The answer is that *hakol ke'olah* — everything is *like* an *olah*: just as the *olah* is not eaten, so the *chatas* is not eaten.

What *chatas* is this? A *brayta* states: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, al avodat kochavim hevi'u* — they were brought for *avoda zara*. The *korban* that the *Torah* prescribes in Bamidbar 15 for the entire *tzibur* when it sins through *avoda zara* — based on a *psak* of the *Beis Din HaGadol* — consists of a *par* as an *olah* and a *se'ir* as a *chatas*. Here, twelve tribes sinned, yielding twelve *parim* as *olot* and twelve *se'irim* as *chataot*. And since the verse calls everything "like an *olah*," we derive that this *chatas* is not eaten — its blood is brought on the inner *mizbe'ach*.

*Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Shmuel*: this was for the *avoda zara* committed in the days of *Tzidkiyahu*, before the exile, and the atonement is now being brought seventy years later upon the return in the time of the Second *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Twelve Korbanot Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לְהָנֵי שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חַטָּאוֹת כְּגוֹן דְּחָטְאוּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים, דְּמַיְיתוּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׂעִירִים. אִי נָמֵי דְּחָטְאוּ שִׁבְעָה שְׁבָטִים וּשְׁאָרָא אִינָךְ בִּגְרִירָה. וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, כְּגוֹן דְּחָטְאוּ אַחַד עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים, דְּמַיְיתוּ אַחַד עָשָׂר שְׂעִירִים וְאִידַּךְ דְּבֵית דִּין. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר: בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִין וְלֹא צִבּוּר, שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ? כְּגוֹן דְּחָטְאוּ וַהֲדַר חָטְאוּ וַהֲדַר חָטְאוּ, עַד תְּרֵיסַר זִימְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted, according to Rabbi Yehuda,** whose opinion is cited in the mishna, **you find** liability for **these twelve sin-offerings** in a case **where twelve tribes sinned** by engaging in idol worship, **as** in that case **they bring twelve goats. Alternatively,** you find liability in a case **where seven tribes sinned, and the rest of these** tribes that did not sin are **drawn** after the majority of tribes that sinned and each brings a sin-offering. **And according to Rabbi Shimon as well, you find** liability for these twelve sin-offerings in a case **where eleven tribes sinned, as** in that case **they bring eleven goats and the other** goat is brought **by the court. But according to Rabbi Meir, who says** that **the court brings** an offering **and** the **public** does **not,** and only one bull is sacrificed, **how do you find this** a case of liability to bring **twelve offerings?** The Gemara answers: It is in a case **where they sinned, and then sinned** again, **and then sinned** again, **until** they sinned **twelve times.**

The *Gemara* proceeds to examine how twelve *korbanot* can be accounted for under the various *Tana'im*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the *korban* for *par he'elem davar shel tzibur* is brought separately by each *shevet*, twelve *korbanot* arise naturally: if all twelve tribes sinned with *avoda zara*, each tribe brings its own *se'ir* as a *chatas* and its own *par* as an *olah*. Even if only seven tribes sinned, the remaining ones are "dragged along" as the *Mishna* taught — and Rabbi Yehuda still arrives at twelve.

For Rabbi Shimon, who holds that both the *tzibur* and *Beis Din* bring separately, twelve is also attainable: eleven tribes sinned, bringing eleven *se'irim*, plus one additional *se'ir* for the *Beis Din* — a total of twelve.

### Rabbi Meir's Count

According to Rabbi Meir, who holds that only *Beis Din* brings and not the *tzibur*, reaching twelve is more difficult. There is only one *Beis Din*; how do you generate twelve *korbanot*? The *Gemara* answers: *kegon dechatu vehadar chatu vehadar chatu* — they sinned, then sinned again, then sinned again. That is, *Beis Din* issued a ruling, the *tzibur* sinned, they realized their error, then *Beis Din* ruled again, they sinned again, and so on — twelve times. This requires imagining twelve separate cycles of erroneous ruling, sin, and recognition, which is itself a puzzling construction, as the *Gemara* will shortly note.

### Dead Sinners Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מַיְיתֵי לְהוּ הָנְהוּ דְּחָטְאוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't those who sinned** by engaging in idol worship during the time of Zedekiah and the Babylonian exile already **die?** How can their descendants bring a sin-offering on their behalf? The status of those animals is that of a sin-offering whose owner has died, which is disqualified from sacrifice on the altar.

The *Gemara* presses further. *Ve-ha meitu lehu hane d'chatu* — but those who sinned have already died. The *avoda zara* was committed in the days of *Tzidkiyahu*, over seventy years before this *korban* is being brought. How can a *chatas* be offered on behalf of people who are no longer alive?

*Rashi* consistently reads the problem through the lens of *chatas she'metu ba'aleha* — if the owner of a designated *chatas* dies, the animal cannot be brought. The *Rosh*, however, notes that this framing is not precise: no animal was designated at the time of *Tzidkiyahu*, and those animals would have died along with their owners. The real question, as he frames it, is a more fundamental one: can one bring a *korban chatas* at all on behalf of people who are already dead?

There is yet another difficulty the *Gemara* largely sets aside: even granting the framework, in the time of *Tzidkiyahu* only two and a half tribes remained — *Yehuda*, *Binyamin*, and *Levi*. All the other tribes had been exiled by *Sancherev* roughly a century earlier. The number twelve therefore does not fit in any straightforward historical sense. As the *Gemara* will conclude, this was all *hora'at sha'ah* — a special ruling that does not conform to ordinary *halachah*.

## Korban for Dead

### Ein Tzibur Meis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כִּי גְּמִירִי חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ בְּמִיתָה – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּיָחִיד, אֲבָל לֹא בְּצִבּוּר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מִיתָה בְּצִבּוּר. מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַב פָּפָּא הָא? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב: ״תַּחַת אֲבוֹתֶיךָ יִהְיוּ בָנֶיךָ״, אִי הָכִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּיָחִיד נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: When** it **is learned** as a tradition that the fate of **a sin-offering whose owners have died** is to allow the animal **to die** without its being sacrificed, **this matter** applies specifically **with regard to an individual** who died **but not with regard to a congregation, because there is no death with regard to a congregation;** the entity of the congregation remains even when specific members die. The Gemara asks: **From where does Rav Pappa derive this? If we say** that he derives it **from** that **which is written: "Instead of your fathers shall be your sons"** (Psalms 45:17), indicating that as long as the sons are alive it is as though the fathers are alive, then **if so,** the same should be true **even with regard to an individual as well,** and sons should be able to sacrifice the sin-offerings of their dead fathers.

*Rav Papa* teaches: *ki g'miri chatas she'meitu ba'aleha be'mitah, hani mili be'yachid, aval lo be'tzibur, lefi she'ein tzibur metah* — the principle that a *chatas* whose owner has died cannot be brought applies only to an individual, not to a *tzibur*, because the *tzibur* does not die.

*Rashi* reads this narrowly: the *halachah* of *chatas she'metu ba'aleha* simply does not apply to a *tzibur*. But the *Rosh* presses for a broader reading. The issue here is not that designated animals outlived their owners, since no animals were designated in the time of *Tzidkiyahu*. The deeper question is whether a *korban chatas* may be brought on behalf of people who have already died. *Rav Papa*'s answer, on the *Rosh*'s reading, is that when one brings on behalf of a *tzibur*, one does not look at the specific individuals who constitute it at any given moment. The *tzibur* is a corporate entity that carries its history across generations. When members die and their children live on, it is still the same community — and a *korban* may be brought on behalf of that continuing entity.

### Chatas Shemetu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דּוּקְיָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא מִשָּׂעִיר דְּרֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ, דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: מַיְיתֵי מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. וְהָא מַיְיתֵי לְהוּ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, וְהָנָךְ דְּפָיְישִׁי הֵיכִי מַיְיתוּ! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ בְּצִבּוּר קְרֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the inference of Rav Pappa is from the goat of the New Moon, as the Merciful One states: Bring** those sin-offerings **from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber,** where the half-shekels contributed by the Jewish people every Adar are stored. **But haven't some of the Jewish people died** since Adar? If so, **how can those who remain bring** a sin-offering if some of the owners of the offering have died? **Rather, learn from it** that **a sin-offering whose owners have died may be sacrificed in** the case of **a communal** offering.

The *Gemara* asks: where does *Rav Papa* derive this? One suggested source is the *pasuk* "your children shall rise in place of their fathers" — implying generational continuity. But the *Gemara* rejects this: if generational continuity were the basis, even an individual's son should be able to bring a *chatas* on behalf of his deceased father, which we do not permit.

The *Gemara* therefore looks instead to the *se'ir* of *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Se'ir Rosh Chodesh

The *se'ir* of *Rosh Chodesh* is a *chatas* brought from the funds of the *trumat ha'lishkah* — the collection taken at the beginning of the year. Between the time the funds were collected and the time the *korban* is actually brought each month, some of the contributing Israelites will inevitably have died. Yet the *korban* continues to be brought. *Alla shema mina: chatas she'meitu ba'aleha be'tzibur kreiva* — we derive that even when some of the owners have died, the *korban* may still be brought when it is a *korban* of the *tzibur*.

### Eglah Arufah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא מֵהָכָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּפֵּר לְעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר פָּדִיתָ ה׳״ – רְאוּיָה כַּפָּרָה זוֹ שֶׁתְּכַפֵּר עַל יוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, מִדִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר פָּדִיתָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the reason** for the opinion **of Rav Pappa is from here, as it is written** in the confession recited by the Sages during the rite of the heifer whose neck is broken: **"Atone for Your people Israel whom You have redeemed, Lord"** (Deuteronomy 21:8). **This atonement is fit to atone** even **for those who emerged from Egypt, from** the fact **that it is written: "Whom You have redeemed."** The reference is to those whom God redeemed from Egypt, even though they died long ago.

The *Gemara* therefore turns to a different source: the *eglah arufah*. The *pasuk* says, "atone for Your people Israel whom You have redeemed" — and the *Gemara* derives that this *kaparah* is effective even for those who left Egypt, long since dead. This suggests that communal atonement can function even for those who no longer live.

### Returning Generation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא מִי הֲווֹ חַיִּים? אִין, הָכִי נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרַבִּים מֵהַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם וְרָאשֵׁי הָאָבוֹת וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But here,** in the case of those returning from Zion, **are** any of the people who worshipped idols during the time of Zedekiah **alive** when the offering is sacrificed? The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so** that there were people from that period still alive, **as it is written: "Many of the priests and Levites and heads of patrilineal houses,** the elders that had seen the first house standing on its foundation, wept with a loud voice when this house was before their eyes. And many shouted aloud for joy" (Ezra 3:12).

The *Gemara* again objects: *mi dami*? By *eglah arufah*, the rite is performed primarily for those who are alive, and it reaches those who are already dead only incidentally — *migo d'mechaper achayei, mechaper nami amisim*. But in the case of the returnees, those for whom the *korban* is being brought — the generation of *Tzidkiyahu* — are entirely dead. How can the *eglah arufah* serve as a model?

The *Gemara* resolves this by establishing that, in fact, many members of the previous generation were still alive among the returnees. A *pasuk* in Ezra 3 records that many elder *Kohanim*, *Levi'im*, and tribal leaders who had seen the First *Beis HaMikdash* in its founding were present at the laying of the foundation of the Second — weeping aloud at the contrast. The *Gemara* asks: perhaps they were only a minority? The following *pasuk* answers this: the sound of crying overwhelmed the shouts of rejoicing — which implies that those who wept were the more numerous group. Thus the majority of those present had lived in the era of the First *Beis HaMikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא מוּעָטִין הֲווֹ וְלָא רַבִּים הֲווֹ! הָכְתִיב: ״(וְלֹא הִכִּירוּ בְּקוֹל) [וְאֵין הָעָם מַכִּירִים קוֹל] תְּרוּעַת הַשִּׂמְחָה לְקוֹל בְּכִי הָעָם [וְגוֹ׳] וְהַקּוֹל נִשְׁמַע עַד לְמֵרָחוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps those** elders **were few** in number, **and were not many,** and the majority of those present consisted of people who were not alive during the days of Zedekiah. The Gemara answers: **Isn't it written: "And the people could not discern the noise of the shout of joy from the noise of the weeping of the people;** as the people shouted with a loud shout, **and the noise was heard far off"** (Ezra 3:13)? This indicates that the weeping elders who survived from the era of Zedekiah outnumbered the younger people.

This somewhat narrows *Rav Papa*'s originally sweeping principle. *Ein tzibur meit* — the *tzibur* does not die — is sustained, but the *Gemara* grounds it in the concrete historical fact that most of the returnees belonged to the earlier generation. The *Gemara* has not fully proven that atonement can be brought on behalf of an *entirely* replaced community many generations removed; it has accepted the principle in somewhat narrower parameters, while noting that this more expansive reading remains a powerful conceptual idea, akin to later questions about communal responsibility across generations.

## Hora'at Sha'ah

### Hora'at Sha'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מְזִידִין הֲווֹ! הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי – ״אֵילִים תִּשְׁעִים וְשִׁשָּׁה כְּבָשִׂים שִׁבְעִים וְשִׁבְעָה״, כְּנֶגֶד מִי? אֶלָּא הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה, הָכָא נָמֵי הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't** those who engaged in idol worship in the era of Zedekiah do so **intentionally?** Sin-offerings are brought for unwitting, not intentional sins. The Gemara answers: **It was a provisional edict** issued in exigent circumstances to enable them to sacrifice a sin-offering to atone for an intentional sin. The Gemara comments: **So too it is reasonable, as if you do not say so,** then with regard to these **"ninety-six rams, seventy-seven lambs,"** to **what** do they **correspond? Rather,** the sacrifice of those rams and lambs **was a provisional edict. Here too,** concerning sin-offerings for intentional transgressions, **it was a provisional edict.**

The *Gemara* delivers its final blow to the attempt to fit the *Ezra* account into standard *halachah*: *ve-ha mezidin havu* — but they worshipped *avoda zara* deliberately! The entire framework of *par he'elem davar shel tzibur* for *avoda zara* applies only when the sin arose from an erroneous *psak* of *Beis Din* acted upon *beshogeg*. The *avoda zara* of the generation of *Tzidkiyahu* was manifestly *bemezid*.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *hora'as sha'ah haysah* — it was a special ruling for that time, outside the parameters of ordinary *halachah*. This is reinforced by the sheer impossibility of accounting for the ninety-six rams and seventy-seven lambs in any standard framework: *keneged mi* — what category do those correspond to? The answer is simply that the entire episode was a *hora'at sha'ah*, divinely inspired, broadly representative of the twelve tribes, but not a direct application of *halachah*. This also frees us from the pressure to reconcile the varying counts for Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Meir, or the problem that most of the twelve tribes had already been exiled long before *Tzidkiyahu*.

### Meis Tzibur/Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מֵת אֶחָד מִן הַצִּבּוּר – חַיָּיבִין, אֶחָד מִבֵּית דִּין – פְּטוּרִין. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַב, רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִים וְלֹא צִבּוּר, הִלְכָּךְ מֵת אֶחָד מִן הַצִּבּוּר – חַיָּיבִין, דְּהָא קָאֵים כּוּלֵּיהּ בֵּית דִּין, מֵת אֶחָד מִבֵּית דִּין – פְּטוּרִין, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּת אֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּתָּפִין, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § On a related note, **the Sages taught:** If the court unwittingly issued a ruling and the congregation performed a transgression on the basis of that ruling, and before the offering was brought **one** member **of the public died,** they are **liable** to bring an offering. If **one** member **of the court** died, they are **exempt.** The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who stated this *halakha*? **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Yirmeya said** that **Rav said: It is Rabbi Meir, who says:** The **court, and not the public, brings** the offering. **Therefore,** if **one** member **of the public died** the court is **liable, as the entire court** remains **intact.** If **one** member **of** the **court died** the court is **exempt, as** the *halakha* here **is** like the *halakha* of **a sin-offering that one of the partners** who co-own the offering **died, and due to that** reason the court is **exempt.**

A *brayta* is now cited on daf 6a: if one of the *tzibur* dies between the time *Beis Din* realizes the sin and the time the *korban* is brought, they are still obligated. If one of *Beis Din* dies, they are exempt. The *Gemara* asks whose view this reflects. *Rav Chisda* citing *Rabbi Zeira* citing *Rabbi Yirmeya* states: it is the view of Rabbi Meir — *d'amar beis din maytin v'lo tzibur* — who holds that it is *Beis Din* that bears the obligation, not the *tzibur*. Therefore, if one of the *tzibur* dies, the entire *Beis Din* is still present and the obligation stands; the *tzibur* members are not the *be'alim* of the *korban*. But if one of *Beis Din* dies, the *korban* becomes a *chatas* whose owner has died — a *chatas she'meis echad min ha'shutfin* — and they are exempt.

*Rashi* notes that while *shutfus* does not ordinarily apply to a *chatas* — one cannot simply jointly commission a *chatas* with a friend for a shared sin — there are specific cases where the *Torah* establishes a group obligation, among them the *Beis Din* here and the *Kohanim* in the *korban* of *Yom Kippur*. The term *shutfus* is used because this is not the entire *kahal*, merely a defined group.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְנוֹקְמַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: בֵּית דִּין עִם הַצִּבּוּר, מֵת אֶחָד מִן הַצִּבּוּר – חַיָּיבִין, דְּאֵין צִבּוּר מֵתִים. מֵת אֶחָד מִבֵּית דִּין – פְּטוּרִין, כִּדְאָמְרִינַן דְּחַטָּאת שׁוּתָּפִין הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this: And let us establish** this *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says:** The **court,** together **with the public,** brings the offering. Therefore, if **one** member **of the public died** the court is still **liable, as** the **public does not die.** But if **one** member **of** the **court died** the court is **exempt, in accordance with** that **which we say, that it is a sin-offering** belonging to **partners.**

*Rav Yosef* challenges this: the *brayta* could equally reflect the view of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that both *Beis Din* and the *tzibur* bring separately. Under Rabbi Shimon, if one of the *tzibur* dies, the *tzibur's korban* is still brought — *she'ein tzibur mesa* — since the *tzibur* as a corporate entity continues. But if one of *Beis Din* dies, the *Beis Din's* own *korban* cannot be brought — because *Beis Din*, unlike the *kahal*, is not an abstract continuing entity but a defined group of specific individuals, and their *korban* thus constitutes a *chatas shutfim* whose owner has died.

The *Gemara* here draws a pointed distinction. The *kahal* functions like a corporation: its members may change, yet it persists. *Halachah* does not generally recognize corporations, but for the *kahal* it makes an exception. *Beis Din*, by contrast, is specifically those individuals; they are the *be'alim* of that *korban*, and if one of them dies, the *korban* cannot proceed.

### Abaye: Chatas Tzibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: חַטַּאת שׁוּתָּפִין אֵינָהּ מֵתָה, דְּתַנְיָא: פַּר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים שֶׁאָבְדוּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶם – יָמוּתוּ כּוּלָּן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יִרְעוּ, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין חַטַּאת צִבּוּר מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **We heard with regard to Rabbi Shimon that he says: A sin-offering** belonging to **partners is not** left to **die, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where **a bull and a goat of Yom Kippur were lost, and one designated other** animals **in their place,** and then the lost animals were found, **they shall all** be left to **die;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say: They shall graze** until they develop a blemish, **due to** the fact **that a communal sin-offering is not** left to **die.** The bull sacrificed on Yom Kippur belongs to the priests, and is therefore a sin-offering belonging to partners; nevertheless, Rabbi Shimon holds that its status is not that of a bull whose owners died.

*Abaye* disagrees with *Rav Yosef*'s suggestion. He points out that Rabbi Shimon explicitly holds *chatas shutfim einah mesa* — that even a group's *chatas*, not just that of the entire *klal*, does not die if one member is gone. The source is a *brayta*: if the *par* and *se'ir* of *Yom Kippur* are lost and replacements are designated, and then the originals are found, all must be left to die — because the *kohanim* and *Am Yisrael* have already received atonement through the substitutes (*nitkapru ba'aleha b'acher*). That is the position of Rabbi Yehuda. But *Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say they are left to graze* — because *ein chatas tzibur mesa*. When it is a *chatas tzibur*, the principle of *chatas metah* does not apply, so the animals are left to graze until they become blemished and are then redeemed.

Since Rabbi Shimon applies this principle even to the *Kohanim* of *Yom Kippur* — a group, not the entire *klal* — he would presumably apply it equally to *Beis Din*. Therefore, under Rabbi Shimon, even if one of *Beis Din* dies, the *chatas* could still be brought. The *brayta*'s ruling that death of a *Beis Din* member creates exemption cannot reflect Rabbi Shimon's view, and must reflect Rabbi Meir's alone.

## Tzibur vs. Shutafim

### Kohanim as Kahal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: כֹּהֲנִים קָא אָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי כֹּהֲנִים, דְּאִיקְּרוּ קָהָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל הַכֹּהֲנִים וְעַל כׇּל עַם הַקָּהָל יְכַפֵּר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to** Abaye: **Priests, you say? Priests are different, as they are characterized as a congregation** in and of themselves, **as it is written: "And for the priests and for all the people of the congregation he shall atone"** (Leviticus 16:33).

*Rav Yosef* resists *Abaye*'s conclusion. Even granting that Rabbi Shimon holds *chatas tzibur* does not die, one cannot automatically extend this to *Beis Din*. The *Kohanim* to whom Rabbi Shimon applied this principle in the *Yom Kippur* case are different: *shani kohanim de'ikru kahal* — the *Kohanim* are themselves designated as a *kahal*. The proof: a *pasuk* states "he shall atone for the *Kohanim* and for all the people of the *kahal*" — the juxtaposition implies that *Kohanim* themselves bear the status of a *kahal*. Rabbi Shimon's application of *ein chatas tzibur meta* to the *Kohanim* therefore derives from their recognized status as a *kahal*, not from any broader principle that the rule applies to any group. *Beis Din* has no such status and therefore the principle would not extend to it.

### Achuza Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב: שִׁבְטוֹ שֶׁל לֵוִי לָא אִיקְּרוּ קָהָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנְנִי מַפְרְךָ וְהִרְבִּיתִךָ וּנְתַתִּיךָ לִקְהַל עַמִּים וְגוֹ׳״. כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אֲחוּזָּה – אִיקְּרִי קָהָל, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֲחוּזָּה – לָא אִיקְּרִי קָהָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa, son of Rabbi Ya'akov, said: The tribe of Levi is not characterized as a congregation, as it is written: "Behold, I will make you fruitful and multiply you, and I will make of you a congregation of peoples;** and I will give this land to your descendants after you for an everlasting possession" (Genesis 48:4). It is derived from here that **any** tribe **that has** an ancestral **possession** bequeathed to it **is characterized as a congregation, and any** tribe **that does not have** an ancestral **possession** bequeathed to it **is not characterized as a congregation.** The tribe of Levi has no ancestral land, and the priests who are from the tribe of Levi have no ancestral land.

The *Gemara* now asks: if *Levi* is excluded from the *kahal* and is not a tribe for these purposes, we are left with only eleven tribes. The *Gemara*'s answer is well-known: *Abaye* cites *Yaakov*'s declaration that "*Efraim u'Menashe shall be to me like *Reuven* and *Shimon*" — the two sons of *Yosef* are elevated to the status of independent tribes, restoring the count to twelve.

*Rava* raises a qualification: a *pasuk* states "*beshem acheihem yikaru binachalasam*" — that *Efraim* and *Menashe* are called by their brothers' names for the purpose of inheritance. The implication is that their tribal status holds only regarding a portion in the land, not for other purposes. The *Gemara* pushes back: are they not counted as separate tribes for their arrangement around the *Mishkan* — each under its own flag? The *Gemara* responds: *kenachalasan kach chaniyasan* — their arrangement in the encampment corresponds to their arrangement in the land, in order to give honor to the flags. This may imply that the separation is consequential only as an extension of the *achuza* principle, but in practice the *Gemara* acknowledges that they are functionally counted as two distinct tribes in the arrangement of the *degalim* and the listing of the *nesi'im*.

### Twelve Tribes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, חָסְרִי לְהוּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״אֶפְרַיִם וּמְנַשֶּׁה כִּרְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן יִהְיוּ לִי״. אָמַר רָבָא, וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״עַל שֵׁם אֲחֵיהֶם יִקָּרְאוּ בְּנַחֲלָתָם״, לְנַחֲלָה הוּקְשׁוּ וְלֹא לְדָבָר אַחֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so, the twelve tribes are lacking** in number, as without the tribe of Levi there are only eleven. **Abaye said** that it is stated: **"Ephraim and Manasseh shall be like Reuben and Simeon to me"** (Genesis 48:5), and they are counted as two tribes. **Rava said: But isn't it written: "After the name of their brethren shall they be called in their inheritance"** (Genesis 48:6), indicating that it is **with regard to inheritance** that **they are likened** to tribes, **but not with regard to another matter?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָא חֲלוּקִין בַּדְּגָלִים! כְּנַחֲלָתָן כָּךְ חֲנִיָּיתָן, כְּדֵי לְחַלֵּק כָּבוֹד לַדְּגָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And** are they **not** considered independent tribes concerning other matters as well? **But aren't they separate with regard to the banners** under which the Jewish people traveled in the wilderness? There were three tribes represented by each banner, and the three tribes under the banner of Manasseh were Manasseh, Ephraim, and Benjamin. The Gemara answers: **In accordance with** the division of the tribes according to **their inheritances, so was** the division of the tribes in **their encampment** around the Tabernacle. This was **in order to honor the banners,** so that there would be three tribes affiliated with each banner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חֲלוּקִים בַּנְּשִׂיאִים! הָהוּא, לַחֲלוֹק כָּבוֹד לַנְּשִׂיאִים. דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹמֹה עָשָׂה שִׁבְעָה יְמֵי חֲנוּכָּה, וּמָה רָאָה מֹשֶׁה לַעֲשׂוֹת שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר יְמֵי חֲנוּכָּה? כְּדֵי לַחֲלוֹק כָּבוֹד לַנְּשִׂיאִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But aren't they separate with regard to** the matter of **princes** of the tribes, as the tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh had separate princes? The Gemara answers: **That** was **in order to honor the princes,** and there is no proof that they are two different tribes with regard to other matters. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Solomon set seven days of dedication** of the Holy Temple, **and what did Moses see** that led him **to set twelve days of dedication** for the Tabernacle? He did so **in order to honor the** tribal **princes,** so that the prince of each tribe would bring his offering on his own day. Due to the honor each prince deserved, Ephraim and Manasseh are considered two tribes.

As *Ramban* observes, whenever the *Torah* enumerates the tribes, it always arrives at twelve — but through two different methods: sometimes *Levi* is counted and *Efraim*/*Menashe* are treated as one, and sometimes *Efraim* and *Menashe* are counted separately and *Levi* is excluded. Each context determines which method is used. The *Gemara* cites the further proof that *Shlomo* held seven days of *chanukah* for the *Beis HaMikdash*, while *Moshe* held twelve days for the *chanukat haMishkan* — *kedei lachlok kavod la'nesi'im* — to give honor to each *nasi* his own day, which presupposes twelve separate tribes.

### Five Chata'ot Metot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת מֵתוֹת: וְלַד חַטָּאת, וּתְמוּרַת חַטָּאת, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתָהּ. וְאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר וְלַד חַטָּאת בְּצִבּוּר, שֶׁאֵין צִבּוּר מַפְרִישִׁין נְקֵבָה. וְאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר תְּמוּרַת חַטָּאת בְּצִבּוּר, שֶׁאֵין תְּמוּרָה בְּצִבּוּר. וְאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ בְּצִבּוּר, שֶׁאֵין צִבּוּר מֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** There are **five sin-offerings** that are left to **die,** as they may not be sacrificed: **The offspring of a sin-offering** born after its mother was consecrated as a sin-offering; **the substitute of a sin-offering,** which assumes sacred status but may not be sacrificed; **a sin-offering whose owners have died; a sin-offering whose owners'** sin was **atoned** for with another offering; **and a sin-offering whose** first **year has passed,** as a sin-offering may be brought only within the animal's first year. **And you cannot state** a case of **the offspring of a communal sin-offering, as** the **congregation does not designate a female** as a sin-offering, **And you cannot state** a case of **a substitute of a communal sin-offering, as there is no substitute for a communal** offering. **And you cannot state** a case of **a sin-offering whose owners have died in** the case of **a communal** sin-offering, **as** the **public does not die.**

The *Gemara* returns to the question of *chatas tzibur* by way of a *brayta* in which Rabbi Shimon enumerates five categories of *chataot* that must be left to die. These are: (1) *vlad chatas* — the offspring of a *chatas*, which bears *kedushah* but is not designated for any particular sin and cannot be brought; (2) *tmuras chatas* — the animal exchanged for a *chatas* through the act of *temura*, which likewise acquires *kedushah* without purpose; (3) *chatas she'meitu ba'aleha* — a *chatas* whose owner has died; (4) *chatas she'nitkapru ba'aleha b'acher* — a *chatas* whose owners have received atonement through a substitute animal; and (5) *chatas she'avrah shnatah* — a *chatas* that has passed its first year and is no longer eligible. A well-known mnemonic for these five is *ve'timnah* — each letter recalling one category.

### Indivisible Unit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ וְשֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתָהּ – לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ, יָכוֹל יָמוּתוּ? אָמַרְתָּ: יִלְמוֹד סָתוּם מִן הַמְפוֹרָשׁ, מַה מָּצִינוּ בִּוְלַד חַטָּאת וּתְמוּרַת חַטָּאת וְשֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ – בְּיָחִיד דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים וְלֹא בְּצִבּוּר, אַף שֶׁנִּתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ וְשֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתָהּ – בְּיָחִיד דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים וְלֹא בְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a sin-offering **whose owners'** sin was **atoned** for **and** a sin-offering **whose** first **year has passed, we did not hear** whether the *halakha* applies to communal sin-offerings as well. One **might** have thought they too **shall** be left to **die. You say: One shall derive** the **vague from the explicit. What did we find with regard to the offspring of a sin-offering, the substitute of a sin-offering, and** a sin-offering **whose owners died?** We found that these matters **are stated** only **with regard to an individual** sin-offering, **but not with regard to a communal** sin-offering. **So too,** these matters of **a sin-offering whose owners'** sin was **atoned** for **and** a sin-offering **whose** first **year has passed are stated** only **with regard to an individual** sin-offering, **but not with regard to a communal** sin-offering. Likewise, one may derive that an individual's sin-offering must be left to die, while a sin-offering owned by partners is not left to die.

Rabbi Shimon then observes that by a *tzibur*, three of these five categories simply cannot arise. *Vlad chatas* is inapplicable because whenever the *Torah* prescribes a *chatas* for the *tzibur* it is always a male animal — a *tzibur* never designates a female. *Tmuras chatas* is inapplicable because *temura* only takes effect when performed by an individual; when a *tzibur* attempts it, nothing happens. *Chatas she'meitu ba'aleha* is inapplicable because *ein tzibur meitim* — the *tzibur* does not die. The remaining two categories — *nitkapru ba'aleha* and *avrah shnatah* — could in principle occur even with a *tzibur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּחַד מָקוֹם גְּמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does one derive** the *halakha* of the **possible from** the **impossible?** Some of these cases are by their very nature not relevant to communal sin-offerings. One cannot derive the *halakha* from them with regard to cases that could be relevant to communal sin-offerings. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Shimon learns** all these *halakhot* of sin-offerings that are left to die as a tradition **in one place,** i.e., as a single *halakha*. In cases where not all the categories of sin-offerings left to die apply, no category of sin-offerings that are left to die applies.

Rabbi Shimon reasons that since these five categories were stated together as a unified group, and since three of them categorically do not apply to a *tzibur*, the entire group of five does not apply to a *tzibur*: *Rebbe Shimon bechad makom gmar* — he derives the inapplicable from the applicable as a single indivisible unit.

*Rashi* explains: we know these five were enumerated together as one group. Since the group as a whole cannot apply to a *tzibur* — because three of its members are categorically absent — the entire *halachah* of *chataot metot* does not take hold in the context of *tzibur*.

The *Tosafot HaRosh* draws out an important implication: this same logic extends beyond the *kahal* to any group. Even *shutafim* — partners, or a defined group like *Beis Din* — cannot perform *temura*; *temura* is only for an individual. Therefore, as soon as even one of the five categories fails to apply, the entire set is treated as inapplicable. This means that according to Rabbi Shimon, the principle of *ein chatas tzibur meta* extends not only to *Klal Yisrael* as a whole but to any group — *Kohanim*, *Beis Din*, or any other *shutafim* — wherever the structural conditions that gave rise to the category of *chataot metot* cannot be fully replicated.

The *Gemara* thus confirms that *Abaye*'s earlier reading of Rabbi Shimon's view — that even a group's *chatas* does not die — is grounded in this broader structural principle, and that the *brayta* exempting a *Beis Din* member's death from triggering the rule must accordingly be the position of Rabbi Meir alone.

## Kohen Gadol Intro

### Kohen Gadol Chiyuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הוֹרָה כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ לְעַצְמוֹ שׁוֹגֵג וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג – מֵבִיא פַּר. שׁוֹגֵג וְעָשָׂה מֵזִיד, מֵזִיד וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהוֹרָאַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ לְעַצְמוֹ כְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין לַצִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where **an anointed priest,** i.e., the High Priest, **issued** an erroneous **ruling for himself** permitting performance of an action prohibited by Torah law, if he issued the ruling **unwittingly and** then **unwittingly performed** the transgression in accordance with his ruling, he is liable to **bring a bull** as a sin-offering for an unwitting transgression by the anointed priest. If he issued the ruling **unwittingly, and performed** the transgression **intentionally,** or if he issued the ruling **intentionally and performed** the transgression **unwittingly,** he is **exempt** from liability to bring any offering, **as** there is a principle: The legal status of **the ruling of an anointed priest for himself is like** that of **the ruling of the court for the** general **public.** Therefore, the High Priest is liable to bring the bull as a sin-offering for his unwitting transgression in a case when the court would be liable to bring the bull as a communal sin-offering for an unwitting transgression performed by the general public.

With *Hadran alach perek rishon*, the first *perek* — governing the *Beis Din HaGadol* — concludes. The *Mishna* opening the second *perek* addresses the *Kohein Gadol*. The *Torah*'s language of "the anointed *Kohein*" is understood to create an obligation structurally parallel to that of *Beis Din*: just as the communal *korban* requires both an erroneous ruling by *Beis Din* and an act carried out by the *tzibur* on the basis of that ruling, so too the *Kohein Gadol's* liability requires both a mistaken ruling and an act carried out by him personally on the basis of that ruling. He functions simultaneously as *Beis Din* and as the *kahal*.

The *Mishna* states: *horu kohein mashiach l'atzmo, shogeig ve'asah shogeig — meivi par* — if the *Kohein Gadol* ruled mistakenly for himself that *cheilev* was permitted and then ate it inadvertently, he brings a *par*. This is the paradigm case where both components — *hora'ah beshogeg* and action *beshogeg* — coincide.

### Psak and Action

The *Mishna* then distinguishes the mixed cases. If he ruled *beshogeg* but acted *bemezid*, he does not bring a *korban*, because the action was willful. If he ruled *bemezid* — knowingly issuing an incorrect ruling — but then performed the act *beshogeg* — for example, he deliberately ruled that a certain type of *dam* was permitted, then inadvertently consumed that *dam* without recalling his ruling — he is likewise *patur*. Without both the *hora'ah beshogeg* and the action flowing from that *hora'ah*, there is no *chiyuv*. This is the meaning of the closing formulation: *shehoras kohein mashiach l'atzmo kehoras beis din vetzibur* — his ruling for himself is equivalent to the ruling of *Beis Din* combined with the action of the *tzibur*; both must be present.

### Forgotten Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שׁוֹגֵג וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג – מֵבִיא פַּר. פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוֹרָה, וְשָׁכַח מֵאֵיזֶה טַעַם הוֹרָה, וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁטָּעָא אָמַר: הֲרֵינִי עוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת הוֹרָאָתוֹ, דְּמַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ מִתְיְדַע לֵיהּ, [שֶׁמָּא] הָדַר בֵּיהּ – כְּמֵזִיד דָּמֵי וְלָא לִחַיַּיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: If he issued the ruling **unwittingly and** then **unwittingly performed** the transgression in accordance with his ruling, he is liable to **bring a bull.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** This is certainly a case of an unwitting transgression, for which the Torah deems him liable to bring an offering. **Abaye said: What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where he issued** an erroneous **ruling and forgot the reason that** was the basis upon which **he issued the ruling, and at the moment that he erred** and performed the transgression, **he said: I am hereby performing** this action **with** my **ruling in mind. As, lest you say: Since if the reason** that was the basis upon which he issued the ruling **became known to him** at that moment, **perhaps he would have retracted his** ruling, therefore if he nevertheless performed his transgression, his status is **like** that of **an intentional** transgressor, **and let him not be liable** to bring an offering; to counter this, the *tanna* **teaches us** that this too is a case of an unwitting transgression.

The *Gemara* observes that the opening case — *shogeig ve'asah shogeig meivi par* — seems self-evident. *Abaye* clarifies that it addresses a non-trivial scenario: *dehoreh veshachach mizeh ta'am horeh* — he ruled that a certain thing was permitted but subsequently forgot the reason behind his ruling. When the moment came to act, he said to himself: I recall that I ruled this way, and I trust my own earlier judgment, even though I no longer remember my reasoning — and he proceeded on that basis.

One might think this does not qualify as acting on his own *psak*: *keivan de'ilu mityada leh shema hadar bei* — had he remembered his reasoning, he might well have reversed himself upon realizing his error. If so, acting on a forgotten ruling is perhaps *kemeizid dami* — more akin to acting willfully on one's own rather than acting on an authoritative ruling. The *Gemara* therefore teaches *kamashma lan* — this does count as acting on his own *psak*. Proceeding in reliance on one's own earlier ruling, even when the precise reasoning has been forgotten, is sufficient to satisfy the requirement of *shogeig ve'asah shogeig* and generate an obligation to bring the *korban*.
