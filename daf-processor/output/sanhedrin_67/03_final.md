# Sanhedrin 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mesit & Witness Rules

### Mesit Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּסִית – זֶה הֶדְיוֹט, וְהַמֵּסִית אֶת הַהֶדְיוֹט. אָמַר: יֵשׁ יִרְאָה בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, כָּךְ אוֹכֶלֶת, כָּךְ שׁוֹתֶה, כָּךְ מְטִיבָה, כָּךְ מְרֵיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the case of **an inciter,** listed among those liable to be executed by stoning, **this is an ordinary person,** not a prophet. **And** it is referring to **one who incites an ordinary person** and not a multitude of people. What does the inciter do? **He says: There is an idol in such and such a place,** which **eats like this, drinks like this, does good** for its worshippers **like this,** and **harms** those who do not worship it **like this.**

The daf opens at the very top, continuing the list of sins punishable by *skila*. The *mishnah* begins with the *mesit* — the person who seduces, induces, or entices another to commit *avodah zarah*. The *mishnah* qualifies him as a *hedyot*, a commoner, and the *gemara* will explain the significance of this term. The implicit contrast in the first clause is with a *navi sheker* — a false prophet who incites to idolatry — since, as we will see in the *gemara*, Rabbi Shimon holds that a *navi sheker* who induces idolatry receives *chenek* rather than *skila*.

The second clause, which specifies that the *mesit* entices *the individual*, contrasts not with a *navi* but with the *madiach* — the one who entices an entire city. There is thus a three-tiered structure: the *mesit* seduces an individual, the *madiach* seduces an entire city, and the *ir hanidachat* is the city that has been fully seduced, whose inhabitants are all executed and whose buildings are destroyed entirely. The first clause of the *mishnah* contrasts with the *navi sheker*; the second is understood to contrast with the *madiach*, which is the subject of the next *mishnah*.

What constitutes the act of enticement? The *mishnah* describes it as follows: the *mesit* says, "There is a god in such-and-such a place — here is what it eats, here is what it drinks, here is how it benefits its worshippers, here is how it harms those who transgress." He promotes and extols the idol.

### Hidden Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת שֶׁבְּתוֹרָה, אֵין מַכְמִינִין עֲלֵיהֶם, חוּץ מִזּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states a principle with regard to the *halakha* of an inciter: With regard to **all of those** mentioned **in the Torah** who **are liable to** receive **the death** penalty, if there are no witnesses to their transgressions, the court **does not hide** witnesses in order **to** ensnare and punish **them, except for this** case of an inciter.

The *mishnah* then states a sweeping rule: *kol chayavei misos shebatorah ein machminim aleihem chutz mizzo* — for all other capital offenses in the Torah, one may not have witnesses lie in wait. The entire framework of capital law is designed to make execution extraordinarily difficult: witnesses must be present, they must administer *hatra'ah* (prior warning), the accused must acknowledge the warning with an *af al pi chen* and then immediately proceed to commit the act. All of these safeguards ensure that the person acted with full awareness and genuine intent.

The *mesit* is the sole exception. Because what he does is considered so grievous — it is not enough for him to sin himself, he must go and corrupt others — the Torah itself declares *lo sachmol alav velo sechasseh alav*, "do not have compassion for him and do not conceal it." Therefore, witnesses may be hidden against him, no prior warning need be administered, and there is no requirement that the conditions of formal testimony in *beis din* be fulfilled in the ordinary way.

### Two-Witness Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: הֵן עֵדָיו, וּמְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לְבֵית דִּין וְסוֹקְלִין אוֹתוֹ. אָמַר לְאֶחָד: הוּא אוֹמֵר ״יֵשׁ לִי חֲבֵירִים רוֹצִים בְּכָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: If the inciter **said** his words of incitement **to two** men, **they are his witnesses, and** he does not need to be warned before the transgression; **they bring him to court and stone him.** If **he said** his words of incitement **to one** man alone, that man's testimony would not be sufficient to have the inciter executed. Therefore **he says** to the inciter: **I have friends who are interested in this;** tell them too. This way there will be more witnesses.

The *mishnah* proceeds to the case where the *mesit* speaks to two people directly: *amar lishnayim, hen edav* — the very two people he attempts to seduce become the witnesses against him. They may bring him to *beis din* and he is stoned. The question naturally arises: how do we establish that he acted with *mezid* without formal *hatra'ah*? It seems that in this case, the ordinary requirement to demonstrate intentionality may be modified — the context of deliberate seduction itself serves as the operative evidence. Some *mefarshim* hold that even here a form of dissuasion would be offered, though others argue that were the target to give any such response before two witnesses, the *mesit* would recognize the trap and retreat — not out of *teshuvah*, but out of self-preservation — and then proceed to entice the next person. The *mishnah* therefore permits, in the two-witness case, proceeding without the usual formalities.

### Wall Concealment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה עָרוּם וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְדַבֵּר בִּפְנֵיהֶם, מַכְמִינִין לוֹ עֵדִים אֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר. וְהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״אֱמוֹר מַה שֶּׁאָמַרְתָּ בְּיִחוּד!״ וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר לוֹ. וְהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֵיאַךְ נַנִּיחַ אֶת אֱלֹהֵינוּ שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם וְנֵלֵךְ וְנַעֲבוֹד עֵצִים וַאֲבָנִים?״ אִם חוֹזֵר בּוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה מוּטָב, וְאִם אָמַר: ״כָּךְ הִיא חוֹבָתֵנוּ, כָּךְ יָפֶה לָנוּ״ – הָעוֹמְדִין מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר מְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לְבֵית דִּין וְסוֹקְלִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **If** the inciter **is cunning, and** he knows that **he cannot speak in front of** two men, the court **hides witnesses for him behind the fence** so that he will not see them, **and** the man whom the inciter had previously tried to incite **says to him: Say what you said** to me when we were **in seclusion. And the other** person, the inciter, **says to him** again that he should worship the idol. **And he says to** the inciter: **How can we forsake our God in Heaven and go and worship wood and stones? If** the inciter **retracts** his suggestion, **that is good. But if he says: This** idol worship **is our duty; this** is what **suits us,** then **those standing behind the fence bring him to court and have him stoned.**

A more complex scenario follows: *amar le'echad* — the *mesit* approaches only a single individual, and that individual, unwilling to be enticed, wishes to see the *mesit* brought to justice. He tells the *mesit*: "I have friends who would also like to hear your ideas," thereby creating the possibility of two witnesses. But what if the *mesit* is clever — *im hayah arum* — and suspects something, or simply never speaks his enticements in the presence of more than one person at a time? The *mishnah* prescribes: *machminim lo eidim achorei hageder* — witnesses are hidden behind a wall, so that the *mesit* believes he is in private.

The procedure is carefully choreographed. The person being targeted says to the *mesit*: "Tell me again what you were saying before, now that we are alone" — *emor mah she'amarta li b'yichud* — either meaning "now that we are in private" (as Rashi explains) or "tell me clearly again." The *mesit* then says: let us go worship such-and-such *avodah zarah*. The target responds: "How could we abandon our God in heaven and go worship wood and stone?" — *heich naniach et eloheinu shebashamayim v'neilech v'na'avod eitzim va'avanim?* If the *mesit* recants, *mutav* — good. But if he doubles down and says, "No, that is indeed what we are obligated to do," then *ha'omdim me'achorei hageder*, the witnesses behind the wall bring him to *beis din* and he is stoned.

This second scenario differs from the first in an important respect: a form of *hatra'ah* has effectively been administered. By responding "how could we abandon our God?", the target has attempted to dissuade the *mesit* and made clear the gravity of what is being proposed. While this does not follow the formal structure of *hatra'ah* — the witnesses behind the wall are not identified, and the language of *chayav mitah* is not used — it is at minimum sufficient to establish *mezid*: if the *mesit* then reaffirms his position, he is clearly acting with full intentional awareness.

### Avodah Zarah Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: ״אֶעֱבוֹד״, ״אֵלֵךְ וְאֶעֱבוֹד״, ״נֵלֵךְ וְנַעֲבוֹד״; ״אֶזְבַּח״, ״אֵלֵךְ וְאֶזְבַּח״, ״נֵלֵךְ וְנִזְבַּח״; ״אַקְטִיר״, ״אֵלֵךְ וְאַקְטִיר״, ״נֵלֵךְ וְנַקְטִיר״; ״אֲנַסֵּךְ״, ״אֵלֵךְ וַאֲנַסֵּךְ״, ״נֵלֵךְ וּנְנַסֵּךְ״; ״אֶשְׁתַּחֲוֶה״, ״אֵלֵךְ וְאֶשְׁתַּחֲוֶה״, ״נֵלֵךְ וְנִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* of an inciter includes **one who says: I shall worship** idols, or one of the following statements: **I shall go and worship** idols, or: **let us go and worship** idols, or: **I shall sacrifice** an idolatrous offering, or: **I shall go and sacrifice** an idolatrous offering, or: **Let us go and sacrifice** an idolatrous offering, or: **I shall burn** incense as an idolatrous offering, or: **I shall go and burn** incense, or: **Let us go and burn** incense, or: **I shall pour** an idolatrous libation, or: **I shall go and pour** a libation, or: **Let us go and pour** a libation, or: **I shall bow** to an idol, or: **I shall go and bow,** or: **Let us go and bow.**

The *mishnah* concludes with an enumeration of the specific acts that constitute *hasatah* — enticement. *Ha'omer e'avod* ("I will worship"), *eilech v'e'avod* ("I will go and worship"), *neilech v'na'avod* ("let us go and worship") — and the same formula applied to each of the four primary acts of idolatrous worship: *zivuch* (animal sacrifice), *kitor* (burning incense), *nisuch* (pouring libation), and *hishtachavayah* (prostration). Even the formula *eilech v'e'avod* — "I will go and worship," without explicitly inviting the other person along — constitutes *hasatah*, because when someone enthusiastically declares his intention to go worship an idol before you, the implication is that you will follow.

A subtlety remains: the opening of the *mishnah* describes the *mesit* as one who talks up the idol — recounting its powers and habits — while the closing section specifies the direct declaration of worshipping. It is not entirely clear whether the full act of *hasatah* requires both components together, or whether either alone suffices. The *gemara* does not fully resolve this point.

## Gemara Attributions

### Mishna Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמֵּסִית זֶה הֶדְיוֹט. טַעְמָא דְּהֶדְיוֹט, הָא נָבִיא בְּחֶנֶק. וְהַמֵּסִית אֶת הַהֶדְיוֹט, טַעְמָא דְּיָחִיד, הָא רַבִּים בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: With regard to the case of **an inciter, this is an ordinary person.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** he is executed by stoning is **that he is an ordinary person, but** if he is **a prophet** he is executed **by strangulation,** not by stoning. The mishna states further: **And** it is referring to **one who incites an ordinary person.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** he is executed by stoning is **that** he incited **an individual, but** if he subverted **a multitude** of people, he is executed **by strangulation.**

The *gemara* opens by noting that the *mishnah* describes the *mesit* as the *hedyot* — the commoner. The implication, on one reading, is that a *navi sheker* who incites to idolatry does not receive *skila* but rather *chenek*. Similarly, the contrast with the individual — *hamesit et hahedyot* — implies that one who seduces the many receives *chenek* rather than *skila*.

The *gemara* concludes: *masnitin mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *mishnah* follows the view of Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: נָבִיא שֶׁהֵדִיחַ – בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּחֶנֶק. מַדִּיחֵי עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת – בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, **whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A prophet who subverted** others to participate in idol worship is executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says:** He is executed **by strangulation.** Likewise, **the subverters of an idolatrous city** are executed **by stoning. Rabbi Shimon says: By strangulation.**

The *baraisa* is cited: a *navi* who acted as a *madiach* — according to the *chachamim* — receives *skila*; Rabbi Shimon says *chenek*. Those who seduce the *ir hanidachat* — according to the *chachamim* — receive *skila*; Rabbi Shimon says *chenek*. So it is Rabbi Shimon who holds that both the *navi sheker* who incites to idolatry and the seducers of the *ir hanidachat* receive the lighter penalty of strangulation, while the *chachamim* maintain *skila* across all categories. Intriguingly, Rabbi Shimon's position assigns the *lesser* penalty to the *navi sheker* and *madiach* relative to the *mesit*.

But the *gemara* immediately raises an objection: *eima seifa* — look at the end of the *mishnah*. The next *mishnah* begins: *hamadiach, ze ha'omer neilech v'na'avod avodat kochavim*. And as Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav, *midichei ir hanidachat shanu kan* — the word *madiach* here refers to the seducers of the *ir hanidachat*. If the *madiach* of an *ir hanidachat* also receives *skila*, then the *mishnah* as a whole cannot follow Rabbi Shimon. We would have a *mishnah* that begins like Rabbi Shimon and ends like the *chachamim*.

### Ravina's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ רַבָּנַן הִיא, וְלָא זוֹ אַף זוֹ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says: The entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** the *tanna* **teaches** the mishna employing the style of: **Not** only **this** but **also that.** In other words, the mishna should be explained as follows: Not only is one who incites an individual executed by stoning, but even one who subverts an entire city is executed by stoning.

Ravina resolves this: *kulla Rabbanan hi* — the entire *mishnah* reflects the view of the *chachamim*. The opening clause is not to be read as a contrast implying that other categories receive a different punishment. Rather, it follows the principle of *velo zu af zu* — the *mishnah* is not narrowing the law but expanding it. It says: here is the *mesit*, an individual who entices a single person — he receives *skila*. And other categories? The *navi sheker* is a different category, but he too receives *skila*. The *madiach* of an *ir hanidachat*, as the next *mishnah* states, also receives *skila*. The *mishnah* is mapping the landscape of the categories, not excluding the others from *skila*.

### Rav Pappa View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כִּי קָתָנֵי ״מֵסִית זֶה הֶדְיוֹט״, לְהַכְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: When** the mishna **teaches** with regard to one who **incites** that **this is** referring to **an ordinary person,** it is not indicating that a prophet is not included in this *halakha*. Rather, it is referring **to** the **hiding** of witnesses behind a fence in order to ensnare the inciter, as his life is treated with contempt and derision, as though he were an ordinary person, i.e., a simpleton.

Rav Pappa offers an alternative reading. He accepts that Ravina has correctly identified the structure — *mesit* enticing an individual receives *skila*, *madiach* of an *ir hanidachat* in the next *mishnah* also receives *skila* — but he addresses a remaining gap: where does the *mishnah* address the contrast between a *mesit* who is a commoner and one who is a *navi*? Ravina's reading does not seem to resolve this.

Rav Pappa says: when the *mishnah* describes the *mesit* as a *hedyot*, it is not contrasting him with a *navi* at all. The word *hedyot* here means: we treat him with contempt. *Lerachmana* — the Torah calls him a commoner to communicate the halachic posture we adopt toward him: *machminim alav*, we hide witnesses against him, we treat him without the ordinary dignity of the *hatra'ah* process. The *baraisa* that follows elaborates: you light a candle in the inner chamber, you seat the witnesses in the outer chamber in the dark — so they can see him and hear his voice, but he cannot see them — and the procedure of the *mishnah* plays out. So the point of calling him a *hedyot* is not categorical distinction but rather the license to use the entrapment method.

## Mechashef Category

### Real vs. Illusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְכַשֵּׁף – הָעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה חַיָּיב, וְלֹא הָאוֹחֵז אֶת הָעֵינַיִם. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שְׁנַיִם לוֹקְטִין קִשּׁוּאִין, אֶחָד לוֹקֵט פָּטוּר וְאֶחָד לוֹקֵט חַיָּיב. הָעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה חַיָּיב, הָאוֹחֵז אֶת הָעֵינַיִם פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The warlock** is also liable to be executed by stoning. **One who performs** a real **act** of sorcery **is liable, but not one who deceives the eyes,** making it appear as though he is performing sorcery, as that is not considered sorcery. **Rabbi Akiva says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua:** For example, **two** people can each **gather cucumbers** by sorcery. **One** of them **gathers** cucumbers and he is **exempt, and** the other **one gathers** cucumbers and he is **liable.** How so? **The one who performs** a real **act** of sorcery is **liable,** and **the one who deceives the eyes is exempt.**

The *mishnah* continues: *hamachshef* — the sorcerer. The Torah in *Shemot* uses the feminine form, *mechashefah*, but the rule applies equally to men and women. The *mishnah* defines the culpable *mechashef* as one who performs a genuine act — *ha'oseh ma'aseh chayav* — as opposed to *ha'oches et ha'einayim*, the illusionist who deceives the eye. If someone uses genuine witchcraft or sorcery to effect a real change in the world, he is liable for execution. If he is merely an illusionist who makes something appear differently than it is, he is not *chayav mitah*. A striking implication of this distinction — one that runs throughout this section of the *gemara* — is that *chazal* understood real witchcraft to be an actual, effective phenomenon, not merely a superstition.

### Akiva Example

The example illustrates this distinction perfectly. Rabbi Akiva, citing Rabbi Yehoshua, offers an illustrative case: *shnayim loktin kishuin* — two people appear to wave their hands and magically harvest cucumbers from the patch. One is liable and one is exempt. How can this be? *Ha'oseh ma'aseh chayav* — the one who actually performed a real act using sorcery is liable. *Ve'ha'oches et ha'einayim patur* — the one who merely created the illusion of picking cucumbers without actually altering anything is exempt.

### Punishment Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיתָתָן בַּמֶּה? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מְכַשֵּׁפָה לֹא תְחַיֶּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״לֹא תְחַיֶּה כׇּל נְשָׁמָה״. מָה לְהַלָּן בְּסַיִיף, אַף כָּאן בְּסַיִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** manner is **their death** sentence administered? **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: It is stated here: "You shall not allow a witch to live," and it is stated there,** with regard to the conquest of the Canaanites: **"You shall allow nothing that breathes to live"** (Deuteronomy 20:16). **Just as there,** the Canaanites were to be killed **by** the **sword** (see Numbers 21:24), **so too here,** the execution of a witch is administered **by** the **sword.**

A *baraisa* is then cited addressing the nature of the *mechashef*'s punishment. The Torah says *mechashefah lo techayeh* but does not specify the mode of execution. How do we determine which of the four death penalties applies?

Rabbi Yossi HaGlili argues by *gezerah shavah*: just as the Torah uses the phrase *lo techayeh* by *mechashefah*, it also uses *lo techayeh kol neshama* in the context of warfare against the enemy, where the mode is *sayif* — the sword. Therefore, the punishment for a *mechashef* is also death by the sword.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מְכַשֵּׁפָה לֹא תְחַיֶּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״אִם בְּהֵמָה אִם אִישׁ לֹא יִחְיֶה״. מָה לְהַלָּן בִּסְקִילָה, אַף כָּאן בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: It is stated here: "You shall not allow a witch to live," and it is stated there,** with regard to Mount Sinai: "No hand shall touch it, for he shall be stoned, or thrown down; **whether it be animal or man, it shall not live"** (Exodus 19:13). **Just as there,** the verse speaks **of stoning, so too here,** a witch is executed **by stoning.**

Rabbi Akiva disagrees and argues his own *gezerah shavah*: the phrase *lo yichyeh* appears at Har Sinai — *yaro yerare o sakel yisakel lo yichyeh* — in a context that explicitly mentions *skila*. Therefore, by connecting *mechashefah lo techayeh* to *lo yichyeh* at Har Sinai, the punishment is *skila*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲנִי דַּנְתִּי ״לֹא תְחַיֶּה״ מִ״לֹּא תְחַיֶּה״, וְאַתָּה דַּנְתָּ ״לֹא תְחַיֶּה״ מִ״לֹּא יִחְיֶה״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili **said to him: I derived** the meaning of the verse **"You shall not allow** a witch **to live" from** the verse **"You shall allow nothing** that breathes **to live"** via a verbal analogy between two similar phrases, **but you derived** the meaning of the verse **"You shall not allow** a witch **to live" from** the verse **"It shall not live,"** which is a less similar phrase.

Rabbi Yossi pushes back: I derived *lo techayeh* from *lo techayeh* — the exact same phrase. You are combining *lo techayeh* with *lo yichyeh*, which is a different formulation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲנִי דַּנְתִּי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶן הַכָּתוּב מִיתוֹת הַרְבֵּה, וְאַתָּה דַּנְתָּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִגּוֹיִם, שֶׁלֹּא רִיבָּה בָּהֶן הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא מִיתָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: I derived** a *halakha* concerning **Jews from** a *halakha* concerning **Jews, with regard to whom the verse included many** types of **death** penalties. Therefore, the fact that the expression "It shall not live" refers to stoning when stated with regard to Jews is especially significant. **But you derived** a *halakha* concerning **Jews from** a *halakha* concerning **gentiles, with regard to whom the verse included only one** type of **death** penalty, namely, decapitation.

Rabbi Akiva responds on two fronts. First, both of his cases involve a *Yisrael* — a Jew — whereas Rabbi Yossi's comparison draws from the *lo techayeh* of warfare against non-Jews. Second, since there are multiple possible death penalties for Jews — *skila*, *sreifa*, *hereg*, *chenek* — the specification of one at Har Sinai is meaningful and informative about which penalty applies in analogous cases. When it comes to non-Jews, there is only ever one penalty, *sayif*, making the comparison less instructive for determining which of multiple Jewish penalties to select. Our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Akiva: *skila*.

### Ben Azai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר ״מְכַשֵּׁפָה לֹא תְחַיֶּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר ״כׇּל שֹׁכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה מוֹת יוּמָת״. סְמָכוֹ עִנְיָן לוֹ, מָה שׁוֹכֵב עִם בְּהֵמָה בִּסְקִילָה – אַף מְכַשֵּׁף בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Ben Azzai says** that **it is stated: "You shall not allow a witch to live"** (Exodus 22:17), **and it is stated** in the following verse: **"Whoever lies with an animal shall be put to death"** (Exodus 22:18). The fact that the Torah **juxtaposes this matter to that** matter is **to** teach that **just as** one who **lies with an animal** is executed **by stoning** (see Leviticus, chapter 20), **so too, a warlock** is executed **by stoning.**

Ben Azai offers an independent derivation. The Torah juxtaposes the verse about the *mechashefah* with the verse *kol shochev im behema mot yumat* — one who commits bestiality shall be put to death. *Samchu inyan lo* — the *parshiyos* are juxtaposed. Just as bestiality carries *skila* (which Ben Azai assumes is established elsewhere), so too the *mechashef* receives *skila* by virtue of the juxtaposition.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּמָכוֹ עִנְיָן לוֹ, נוֹצִיא לָזֶה בִּסְקִילָה? אֶלָּא, אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי בִּכְלַל מְכַשְּׁפִים הָיוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצְאוּ? לְהַקִּישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן וְלוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי בִּסְקִילָה – אַף מְכַשֵּׁף בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this derivation, **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: And because** the Torah **juxtaposes this matter with that** matter, **shall we take this** person **out to be stoned?** Should he be sentenced to the most severe type of capital punishment on that basis? **Rather,** the source is as follows: **A necromancer and a sorcerer were** included **in the** general **category of warlocks, and why were they singled out** from the rest, with their prohibition and punishment stated independently? This was done in order **to draw an analogy to them and say to you: Just as a necromancer and a sorcerer** are executed **by stoning, so too, a warlock** is executed **by stoning.**

Rabbi Yehuda objects: *vechi mipnei shesamchu inyan lo notzei ze beskila?* — just because they are juxtaposed we are going to bring this person out and stone him? Rashi explains *notzei* as removing him from the other penalty categories to assign him the most severe, though there is also a powerful visual dimension to the phrase: can we really execute someone by stoning on the basis of a textual adjacency?

This objection is the source for the general rule that Rabbi Yehuda is not *doresh smuchin* — he does not derive law from the mere juxtaposition of passages, except in Sefer Devarim, which is a separate matter.

Rabbi Yehuda offers his own derivation instead: *ov v'yidoni bichlal machashifim hayu* — *ov* and *yidoni* are themselves types of sorcery, subsumed within the category of *mechashef*. The Torah nevertheless singles them out — *velama yatzu? lehakish lahen* — in order to allow a *hekesh*, a comparison, back to the general category. Since *ov* and *yidoni* are punished by *skila*, and they are linked by *hekesh* to *mechashef*, the *mechashef* too is punished by *skila*.

### Two Kesuvim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי, לִיהְווֹ אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda as well, let** the punishment with regard to **a necromancer and a sorcerer be** considered **two verses that come as one,** i.e., that teach the same matter, **and** therefore the *halakha* of other cases cannot be derived from it, according to the principle that **any two verses that come as one do not teach** about other cases.

The *gemara* then presses: if Rabbi Yehuda does not *doresh smuchin*, and instead relies on the principle that when a case is singled out from a general category it teaches back to the whole — this is the rule of *davar sheyatza min haklal lelamed, lo lelamed al atzmo yatza ela lelamed al haklal kulo yatza* — then both *ov* and *yidoni* were singled out. That gives us *shnei kesuvim habayim ke'echad* — two verses making the same point. And the rule is that when two verses make the same point, one does not derive a general principle from them; each instance is treated as its own isolated exception.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה: עֲדָא אָמְרָה, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zekharya says: This means** that **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **two verses that come as one do teach** about other cases.

This objection, however, actually confirms something about Rabbi Yehuda's position: *kasavar Rabbi Yehuda, shnei kesuvim habayim ke'echad melamdim* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that even when two verses are singled out together to make the same point, they *do* teach the general principle. This is a known position of Rabbi Yehuda documented elsewhere in the *gemara*, and its source is precisely this passage.

## Theology & Shed Powers

### Heavenly Powers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָן כְּשָׁפִים? שֶׁמַּכְחִישִׁין פָּמַלְיָא שֶׁל מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Why** is sorcery **called *keshafim*? Because** it is an acronym for: **Contradicts the heavenly entourage [*shemakhḥishin pamalia shel mala*].** Sorcery appears to contradict the laws of nature established by God.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: *lama nikra sheman mechashefim?* — why are sorcerers called *mechashefim*? He reads it as an acronym: *shemacheshim pamalya shemala* — they weaken the heavenly household, the divine retinue above. Witchcraft is understood as an assault on the order maintained by the heavenly powers, a subversion of the natural and divine order.

This raises a sharp theological question. If the sorcerers weaken the *pamalya shemala*, does that mean that witchcraft actually constrains God?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵין עוֹד מִלְבַדּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ לִדְבַר כְּשָׁפִים. הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲוָת קָא מְהַדְּרָא לְמִשְׁקַל עַפְרָא מִתּוּתֵי כַּרְעֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: אִי מִסְתַּיַּיעְתְּ, זִילִי עֲבִידִי. ״אֵין עוֹד מִלְבַדּוֹ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "To you it was shown, so that you should know that the Lord is God; **there is none else besides Him"** (Deuteronomy 4:35). **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** This is true **even** with regard **to a matter of sorcery;** sorcery is ineffective against a righteous person. The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who was attempting to take dust from under the feet of Rabbi Ḥanina** in order to perform sorcery on him and harm him. Rabbi Ḥanina **said to her: If you succeed, go** and **do** it. I am not concerned about it, as **it is written: "There is none else besides Him."**

The *gemara* addresses this through a story about Rabbi Chanina. A woman was attempting to gather the dust from beneath his feet — apparently in order to perform sorcery against him. Rabbi Chanina told her: if you succeed, go and do it — *ein od milvado ksiv*, the Torah has written "there is none besides God." Rabbi Chanina's trust in God was absolute: whatever power witchcraft might have, God transcends it entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָן מְכַשְּׁפִים? שֶׁמַּכְחִישִׁין פָּמַלְיָא שֶׁל מַעְלָה. שָׁאנֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דִּנְפִישׁ זְכוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Why** are sorcerers **called *mekhashefim*? Because** it is an acronym for: **Contradicts the heavenly entourage.** This indicates that one should be wary of sorcery. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥanina is different, as his merit is great,** and sorcery certainly has no effect on such a righteous person.

The *gemara* then asks: but Rabbi Yochanan said that sorcerers *do* weaken the heavenly powers — implying that witchcraft works! The resolution: *shani Rabbi Chanina denafish zchusei* — Rabbi Chanina was different; his merit was exceptional. The general assumption, the *gemara* makes clear, is that witchcraft does operate effectively under normal circumstances. It is only for a person of Rabbi Chanina's extraordinary spiritual stature that God would override the ordinary functioning of those forces. For everyone else, the assumption must be that these powers operate — just as one cannot step off a cliff and rely on *ein od milvado*. Rabbi Chanina was making a bold theological statement rooted in his personal trust in God, not a general halachic claim that witchcraft never works.

### Egyptian Magic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַיְיבוּ בַּר נַגָּרֵי אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: ״בְּלָטֵיהֶם״ – אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה שֵׁדִים, ״בְּלַהֲטֵיהֶם״ – אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה כְשָׁפִים. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֵת לַהַט הַחֶרֶב הַמִּתְהַפֶּכֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aivu bar Nagri says** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that in the verse: "And the magicians of Egypt did in that manner **with their secret arts [*belateihem*]"** (Exodus 7:22), **these** words **are** describing **acts of** employing **demons,** which are invisible, and their actions are therefore hidden [*balat*]. With regard to the similar term **"*belahateihem*"** (Exodus 7:11), **these are acts of sorcery,** which sorcerers perform themselves, without using demons. **And likewise it says: "And the flaming [*lahat*] sword that turned every way"** (Genesis 3:24), referring to a sword that revolves by itself.

Rabbi Aivo bar Nagri, citing Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, addresses the terminology used for the Egyptian magicians in the Torah. When the text says *belateihem* — without the letter *heh* — this refers to *maasei shedim*, the work of demons. The root *lat* suggests concealment or invisibility; the magicians summoned *shedim*, lesser spirits, to perform their operations. When the text says *belahateihem* — with the *heh*, the root *lahat* meaning flash or flame — this refers to *maasei keshafim*, direct magical powers not mediated through *shedim*. The verse *ve'et lahat hacherev hamitpachet* — the flashing of the turning sword — is cited in support: the flashing motion evokes this kind of direct sorcerous power.

### Vessel Particularity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּקָפֵיד אַמָּנָא – שֵׁד, דְּלָא קָפֵיד אַמָּנָא – כְּשָׁפִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** A sorcerer **who is particular about** using a certain **utensil** for his sorcery is employing **a demon;** one **who is not particular about** using a certain **utensil** is performing an act of **sorcery.**

Abaye offers a practical diagnostic: *dekaphid amana shed, delo kaphid amana keshafim* — if the practitioner is very particular about the specific vessels and implements used, this indicates *shedim* are at work. One may recall from the *gemara* in Berachot the elaborate specifications — a black cat, the daughter of a black cat, first-born, and so forth — all of that specificity is characteristic of *shedim*, who apparently require precise conditions to be invoked. If the practitioner is indifferent to the vessels, the operation is direct *keshafim*.

### Keshafim Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הִלְכוֹת כְּשָׁפִים כְּהִלְכוֹת שַׁבָּת, יֵשׁ מֵהֶן בִּסְקִילָה, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן מוּתָּר לְכַתְּחִלָּה. הָעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה – בִּסְקִילָה, הָאוֹחֵז אֶת הָעֵינַיִם – פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר. מוּתָּר לְכַתְּחִלָּה – כִּדְרַב חֲנִינָא וְרַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: כׇּל מַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא הֲווֹ עָסְקִי בְּהִלְכוֹת יְצִירָה, וּמִיבְּרֵי לְהוּ עִיגְלָא תִּילְתָּא, וְאָכְלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: The *halakhot* of sorcery are like the *halakhot* of Shabbat,** in that their actions can be divided into three categories: **There are** some **of them** for which one is liable to be executed **by stoning, and there are** some **of them** for which one is **exempt** from punishment by Torah law **but** they are **prohibited** by rabbinic law, **and there are** some **of them** that are **permitted *ab initio*.**

Abaye then articulates a tripartite framework, comparing the laws of *keshafim* to the laws of Shabbat. Some forms of sorcery carry *skila* — just as deliberate *melachah* on Shabbat. Some are *patur aval assur* — exempt from court penalty but still forbidden, analogous to rabbinically prohibited labor on Shabbat. And some are *mutar lechatchilah* — entirely permitted from the outset.

**The one who performs** a real **act** of sorcery is liable to be executed **by stoning. One who deceives the eyes is exempt** from punishment, **but** it is **prohibited** for him to do so. What is **permitted *ab initio*** is to act **like Rav Ḥanina and Rav Oshaya: Every Shabbat eve they would engage in** the study of **the *halakhot* of creation, and a third-born calf would be created for them, and they would eat it** in honor of Shabbat.

What falls into the *mutar lechatchilah* category? The example is Rav Chanina and Rav Oshaya. Every *erev Shabbat* they would study *Hilchot Yetzirah* — the laws of creation, understood by some to refer to the *Sefer Yetzirah* — and would thereby create a *igla tilta*, a fine third-born calf, which they would eat on Shabbat. This is the permitted category. The operative distinction appears to be: if one employs divine names and divine creative power, it is not in the category of forbidden *keshafim*; if one employs the power of demons or magic, it is. Rashi at one point suggests the calf came into being automatically through the learning, but the context — deliberately studying to produce Shabbat food — strongly implies intentionality, and the distinction rests on the source of power invoked.

### Creation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ דְּקַרְנָא, דְּנָפֵיץ וְשָׁדֵי כְּרִיכֵי דְשִׁירָאֵי מִנְּחִירֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I saw Karna's father** perform a magic trick in **which he would blow** his nose **and cast rolls of silk from his nostrils** by deceiving the eye.

Rav Ashi adds an observation: *chazeina lei le'avuha deKarna* — he once saw the father of Karna, evidently a practitioner of illusion or sleight of hand, who blew his nose and silk scarves came streaming out of his nostrils — a classic illusionist's trick performed, apparently, in an unusual fashion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ הַחַרְטֻמִּם אֶל פַּרְעֹה אֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים הִיא״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִיכָּן שֶׁאֵין הַשֵּׁד יָכוֹל לִבְראוֹת בְּרִיָּה פָּחוֹת מִכִּשְׂעוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And the magicians said to Pharaoh: This is the finger of God"** (Exodus 8:15), **Rabbi Eliezer says:** It is derived **from here that a demon cannot create an entity smaller than** the size of **a barley grain.** Consequently, the magicians were not capable of duplicating the plague of lice, and they realized that this was not an act of sorcery but was performed by God.

The *gemara* then returns to the Egyptian sorcerers and the plague of lice. When the *chartumim* said *etzba Elokim hi* — "it is the finger of God" — they acknowledged they could not replicate it. Why not? Rabbi Elazar derives from here: *ein hashed yachol livrot biryah pachos mikis'orah* — a *shed* cannot create a creature smaller than a barley kernel. The lice were too small for the *shedim* to gather or create, hence the magicians' failure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָאֱלֹהִים! אֲפִילּוּ כְּגַמְלָא נָמֵי לָא מָצֵי בָּרֵי. הַאי מִיכְּנִיף לֵיהּ, וְהַאי לָא מִיכְּנִיף לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: By God! They cannot create even** an entity as large **as a camel.** They do not create anything. Rather, **they can gather these** large animals, leading them from one place to another, **but they cannot gather those** small animals.

Rav Pappa takes a more sweeping position: *ha'Elokim, afilu kegamla nami lo matzei barei* — in God's name, the *shedim* cannot create anything at all, not even something as large as a camel. The question of size is beside the point. The distinction is not about size but about *kinuf* — gathering. *Hanei michnaf lehu, vehanei lo michnaf lehu* — what the magicians using *shedim* can do is send the *shedim* to gather existing matter from elsewhere and bring it together. What they cannot do is create *ex nihilo*. The lice were too small and too numerous to be gathered from elsewhere, which is why the magicians failed. Rabbi Elazar and Rav Pappa thus disagree about whether *shedim* can create at all: Rabbi Elazar holds they can, but are limited by size; Rav Pappa holds they cannot create anything and can only gather what already exists.

## Witchcraft Examples

### Rav's Camel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא, דְּשַׁקְלֵיהּ לְסַפְסִירָא וְגַיְּידֵיהּ לְגַמְלָא, וּטְרַף לֵיהּ בְּטַבְלָא, וְקָם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְבָתַר הָכִי, דָּם וּפַרְתָּא מִי הֲוַאי? אֶלָּא, הַהִיא אֲחִיזַת עֵינַיִם הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to Rabbi Ḥiyya: I myself saw a certain Arab who took a sword and sliced a camel and** then **beat a drum [*betavla*], and** the camel **arose** from the dead. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: Was there blood and excretion afterward** in that place, which flowed from the camel when it was sliced? **Rather,** since there was none, **that was** clearly **a deception of the eyes** and not sorcery.

Rav told Rabbi Chiya: *le-didi chazi hahu taya* — I once saw a certain nomadic Arab who took his sword, chopped his camel in half, banged on a bell, and the camel rose back up whole. Rabbi Chiya immediately asked: afterward, was there blood and excrement on the ground? Presumably there was not. Therefore, Rabbi Chiya concluded: *ella hi achizat einayim havei* — it was pure illusion. The camel was never actually cut; the whole scene was a deception of the eye.

### Alexandria Donkey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְעֵירִי אִיקְּלַע לַאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם. זְבַן חַמְרָא. כִּי מְטָא לְאַשְׁקוֹיֵיהּ מַיָּא, פְּשַׁר וְקָם גַּמְלָא דְּאוּסְקָנִיתָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו זְעֵירִי אַתְּ, לָא הֲוָה מַהְדְּרִינַן לָךְ. מִי אִיכָּא דְּזָבֵין מִידֵּי הָכָא וְלָא בָּדֵיק לֵיהּ אַמַּיָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ze'eiri happened** to come **to Alexandria of Egypt. He bought a donkey. When he was about to give it** water **to drink** the magic **thawed** when the donkey touched the water and it was revealed that it was not a donkey, **and it turned into the plank of a bridge.** The ones who sold it to him **said to him: If you were not Ze'eiri,** a distinguished person, **we would not refund you** the money for the donkey. **Is there** anyone **who buys an item here and does not examine it** first **with water?** Since sorcery was widespread there, anyone who bought an item examined it in order to find out if it was affected by sorcery, and if one did not examine an acquired item by exposing it to water and it turned out to be under a spell, he suffered the loss.

Zeiri came to Alexandria in Egypt and purchased a donkey. When he brought it to drink water, the magic reversed itself — for water, it appears, is the agent that dissolves these kinds of spells — and the donkey became a plank of wood. He had been sold an enchanted board. He went to reclaim his money, and the sellers told him: if you were not the distinguished Zeiri, we would not give you your money back. Anyone who buys anything in Alexandria without first testing it with water is simply not exercising due diligence. The marketplace in Egypt is one where *keshafim* are rife, and the prudent buyer knows to test.

### Inn Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַנַּאי אִיקְּלַע לְהָהוּא אוּשְׁפִּיזָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַשְׁקְיַן מַיָּא. קָרִיבוּ שְׁתִיתָא. חֲזָא דְּקָא מְרַחֲשָׁן שִׂפְוָותַהּ, שְׁדָא פּוּרְתָּא מִינֵּיהּ. הֲווֹ עַקְרַבֵּי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא שְׁתַאי מִדִּידְכוּ, אַתּוּן נָמֵי שְׁתוֹ מִדִּידִי. אַשְׁקְיַיהּ, הֲוַאי חַמְרָא. רַכְבַהּ, סָלֵיק לְשׁוּקָא. אֲתָא חֲבֶרְתָּהּ, פְּשַׁרָה לַהּ. חַזְיֵיהּ דְּרָכֵיב וְקָאֵי אַאִיתְּתָא בְּשׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: A man named **Yannai arrived at a certain inn. He said to** the innkeepers: **Give me water to drink. They brought** him **flour mixed with water. He saw that the lips of** the innkeeper woman **were moving,** and he **cast a bit of** the drink to the ground, and it **turned into scorpions,** and he understood that the innkeepers performed sorcery on the drink. Yannai **said to them: I drank from yours; you too drink from mine,** and he also performed sorcery on the drink. **He gave** it to **her to drink** and **she turned into a donkey. He rode upon her** and **went to the marketplace. Her friend came** and **released her** from the sorcery, **and** people **saw him riding on a woman in the marketplace.**

A figure named Yanai came to an inn and ordered water. A wheat-and-water mixture was brought. He noticed that the barmaid's lips were moving — a telltale sign of an attempt at sorcery — and he quietly poured out a small amount of the liquid. Scorpions emerged from it, revealing what had been hidden in the drink. He then announced to the proprietors: "I drank your water; now you drink mine" — and served them a drink over which he had performed his own enchantment. The woman was transformed into a donkey. He rode her into the marketplace. Her friend came, apparently aware of what had happened, and dissolved the spell with water. Yanai was then seen riding on a woman in the middle of the marketplace — the story is not without its disturbing dimensions — and the episode ends there.

### Marketplace Exposure

The donkey-woman episode described above — the transformation, the public exposure, and the dissolution of the spell by water — illustrates both the operation of *keshafim* in these stories and the recurring role of water as the agent of reversal. In both the Alexandria donkey and the inn incident, water is what breaks the enchantment, a detail the *gemara* presents matter-of-factly as practical knowledge.

## Frog Plague

### Frog Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּעַל הַצְּפַרְדֵּעַ וַתְּכַס אֶת אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: צְפַרְדֵּעַ אַחַת הָיְתָה, הִשְׁרִיצָה וּמִלְּאָה כׇּל אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם. כְּתַנָּאֵי: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר, צְפַרְדֵּעַ אַחַת הָיְתָה וּמִלְּאָה כׇּל אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: עֲקִיבָא, מָה לְךָ אֵצֶל הַגָּדָה? כַּלֵּה מִדַּבְּרוֹתֶיךָ וְלֵךְ אֵצֶל נְגָעִים וְאֹהָלוֹת. צְפַרְדֵּעַ אַחַת הָיְתָה, שָׁרְקָה לָהֶם וְהֵם בָּאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated with regard to the plagues of Egypt: **"And the frog came up and covered the land of Egypt"** (Exodus 8:2). Noting that the term "the frog" is written in the singular, **Rabbi Elazar says:** At first **it was one frog; it spawned and filled the entire land of Egypt** with frogs. The Gemara comments: This matter is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*: Rabbi Akiva says: It was one frog, and it** spawned and **filled the entire land of Egypt** with frogs. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said to him: Akiva, what are you doing** occupying yourself **with** the study of ***aggada*?** This is not your field of expertise. **Take your statements to** the tractates of ***Nega'im* and *Oholot*.** In other words, it is preferable that you teach the *halakhot* of the impurity of leprosy and the impurity imparted in a tent, which are among the most difficult areas of *halakha* and are within your field of expertise. Rather, the verse is to be understood as follows: **It was one frog; it whistled to** the other frogs, **and they** all **came** after it.

Returning briefly to the world of Egyptian plagues, the *gemara* addresses the verse *va-ta'al ha-tzefardea va-techas et eretz Mitzrayim* — the frog (singular) arose and covered the land of Egypt. Rabbi Elazar states: *tzefardea achat haytah* — there was one frog, and it propagated, multiplying its young until they filled all of Egypt.

The *gemara* notes this is actually a *tannaitic* dispute. Rabbi Akiva holds: there was one frog, and from it all of Egypt was filled — all of the frogs came from this single progenitor. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah said to him: *Akiva, mah lecha etzel aggadah?* — Akiva, what are you doing with *aggadah*? Stay in your lane. *Kelach midiburecha, v'lech etzel Negaim v'Oholot* — stop speaking about these matters and go to *Negaim* and *Oholot*, the most complex areas of halachic reasoning, where your expertise lies.

### Rebuke

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's own reading: there was one frog, yes — but not that all the frogs propagated from it biologically. Rather, this one frog let out a croak, *sharka lahem*, and that called all the other frogs to come. The frog was a leader that summoned the masses, not a single organism that spawned them all.

Why was Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah so exercised by Rabbi Akiva's reading? The text does not make fully explicit what troubled him so deeply. The two positions may not appear so dramatically different — one emphasizes miraculous biological propagation, the other a miraculous form of leadership and ingathering. Perhaps Rabbi Elazar felt that the *aggadic* exposition required a more careful interpretive hand, and that the domain of *aggadah* belonged to its own specialists, while Rabbi Akiva's true mastery lay in the rigors of *Negaim* and *Oholot*.
