# Menachot 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Shimon & Possibili…

### Reu'uy Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? בִּשְׁלָמָא פָּרָה, עוֹמֶדֶת לִפְדּוֹת הִיא, שֶׁאִם מָצָא אַחֶרֶת נָאָה הֵימֶנָּה – מִצְוָה לִפְדּוֹתָהּ. אֶלָּא הָנֵי מְנָחוֹת, מִצְוָה לִפְדּוֹתָן?! וְהָא לָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, דְּמִצְוָה לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, וְאִי בָּעֵי זָרַק, וְקָתָנֵי דְּאֵין מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** How can these cases be compared? Granted, with regard to the red heifer, it is considered to be an item that stands to be redeemed, since if he found another animal choicer than it, there is a mitzva to redeem the first one and purchase the choicer one with the money. But is there a mitzva to redeem these meal offerings? But there is a case where sacrificial meat remained overnight before the sprinkling of the blood occurred, where there was a mitzva to sprinkle the blood the day before, and if he had wanted he could have sprinkled it, and the offering would not have been disqualified. And yet, Rabbi Shimon teaches in the baraita that sacrificial meat that remained overnight is not susceptible to the ritual impurity of food, even though it should have been considered fit for consumption on the day the offering was slaughtered, as the blood stood to be sprinkled and there was a mitzva to sprinkle it.

We pick up approximately fifteen lines down on 102a, at the line beginning with the word *havi* — *havi zorik lo amrinan* — in the middle of the discussion concerning Rabbi Shimon's position on *tum'as ochlin*. The question at stake is what counts as food for the purposes of susceptibility to *tumah*. Rabbi Shimon holds that if something is halachically prohibited from being eaten or from which benefit may be derived — *asur b'hana'ah* — it does not qualify as food. However, he carves out an important exception: something that was *ra'uy*, fit or theoretically capable of being eaten at some point, retains its status as food. His examples include *basar b'chalav* where the meat and milk were each independently edible before mixing, the flour of a *mincha* before it was sanctified, and even something consecrated such as the *parah adumah* that could have been redeemed. In all these cases, the theoretical availability of permitted consumption is sufficient to confer the status of food.

### Pigul & Blood-Throwing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא לָן מַמָּשׁ, וַאֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דְּפַיגֵּל בִּזְרִיקָה. דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּרֵיק – אָמְרִינַן, אִי בָּעֵי הֲוָה זָרֵיק – לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi said: I related this discussion in the presence of Rav Naḥman and explained Rabbi Shimon's opinion differently: Even if you say that the case in the baraita is one in which the meat was actually left overnight and there was time to sprinkle the blood during the day, and even if you say that he rendered the offering piggul at the time of the sprinkling of the blood rather than during the slaughtering, Rabbi Shimon does not consider those to be cases in which the offering had a time when it was fit for consumption. Rabbi Shimon said only that if he had wanted, he would have redeemed it, and therefore an item that stands to be redeemed is treated as if it were already redeemed. Redemption is simple and requires only a verbal statement. According to Rabbi Shimon we do not say that if he had wanted, he would have sprinkled it, i.e., that the sprinkling of the blood and similar actions that stand to take place are treated as having taken place already.

The debate now turns to a sharper question: does Rabbi Shimon extend this principle of *ra'uy* to cover cases where something would require an affirmative act — specifically, the throwing of blood — in order to become fit for consumption? Consider a *korban* that was *shechted* and whose blood, when sprinkled on the *mizbeiach*, was accompanied by a thought of *pigul*. Such an animal could never be eaten: not as a living animal, not as meat after *shechita* once the blood was thrown with *pigul* intent. Yet there was a brief window — between *shechita* and the invalid sprinkling — during which one could theoretically have thrown the blood correctly. The question is whether *i bei zorik*, the theoretical possibility that the blood could have been thrown properly, is sufficient under Rabbi Shimon's framework to consider this animal food.

### Rav Ashi's Distinction

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this discussion before Rav Nachman and offered the following resolution: even granting Rabbi Shimon's position in its broadest form, there is a meaningful distinction between two kinds of theoretical possibility. When redemption is effected verbally — *i bei porik*, if one could simply redeem it with words — that trivial accessibility is enough to establish the item as *ra'uy* and thus as food. But when becoming fit for consumption would require an actual transformative act, such as the proper throwing of blood, we do not apply the principle of *i bei havi zorik*. Mere theoretical feasibility of blood-throwing does not suffice to confer food status.

## Me'ilah & Sha'at Heter

### Sha'at Heter Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כֹּל שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ, וְכֹל שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to Rav Ashi's opinion from a mishna (Me'ila 2a): Rabbi Yehoshua states a principle about the misuse of offerings that became disqualified: With regard to any offering that had a time that it was permitted for consumption by the priests before it became disqualified, one is not liable for misusing it, and with regard to any offering that did not have a time that it was permitted for consumption by the priests before it became disqualified, one is liable for misusing it. Misuse of consecrated property applies only to offerings that are considered fully reserved for God. Once the priests are permitted to partake of the offering it is no longer categorized as consecrated property.

The Gemara now challenges Rav Ashi's position by invoking a general rule stated by Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua articulates a broad principle: *kol shehayesa lo sha'as heter lakohanim ein mo'alin bo* — anything that had a period during which it was permissible for the *kohanim* to eat it is no longer subject to *me'ilah*; and conversely, anything that never had such a period remains subject to *me'ilah*. The relevance is immediate: just as the question of food status for *tum'as ochlin* turns on whether something was fit to be eaten, so too the question of *me'ilah* turns on whether something was permissible to the *kohanim*. And in both cases, the operative question is whether we require actual permissibility or whether theoretical possibility suffices.

### Examples of Heter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזֶהוּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים? שֶׁלָּנָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמֵאת, וְשֶׁיָּצְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua clarifies: And what is a disqualified offering that had a time that it was permitted for consumption by the priests? Examples of such a situation are as follows: When, after the blood was sprinkled, the meat of the offering was left overnight; or when the meat of an offering became ritually impure; or when an offering left the Temple courtyard. One is not liable for misuse in these cases, since the meat of these offerings became permitted to the priests once the blood was sprinkled and only subsequently was it disqualified.

Rabbi Yehoshua's rule is then illustrated with examples. What constitutes a case where something *had* a period of being permitted to the *kohanim*? The Gemara answers: *she-lana, she-nitme'ah, she-yatz'ah* — meat that slept overnight and became *notar*, or that became *tamei*, or that was taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. In each of these scenarios, the meat was at some point — after the *zericas hadam*, the proper sprinkling of blood — genuinely permissible for the *kohanim* to eat, even though something subsequently rendered it forbidden. Conversely, a *korban* that was *shechted* with a thought of *pigul*, or slaughtered outside its proper time or place, or whose blood was received or thrown by disqualified *kohanim*, never passed through such a window of permissibility.

### Theoretical vs. Actual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא רֵישָׁא שֶׁלָּנָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמֵאת, וְשֶׁיָּצְאָה. מַאי לָאו לָנָה מַמָּשׁ, וְהָכָא דְּאִי בָּעֵי הֲוָה זָרֵיק הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי דְּאֵין מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara addresses the objection to Rav Ashi's opinion: In any event, the first clause teaches that meat of an offering that was left overnight, and meat that became impure, and meat that left the courtyard all had a time when they were permitted to the priests. What, is it not referring to a case where it was actually left overnight, i.e., both the blood and the meat of the offering, and here the case is an instance of: If he had wanted, he could have sprinkled the blood, and for that reason the mishna teaches that one is not liable for misusing it? It is considered as having had a time that it was permitted to the priests since he could have sprinkled the blood during the day, and therefore the offering is treated as if the sprinkling already happened, counter to Rav Ashi's claim that such reasoning does not apply with regard to the sprinkling of the blood.

The Gemara raises the possibility that the phrase *sha'as heter lakohanim* — "a time when it was permitted to the *kohanim*" — does not require that the blood was actually thrown and the meat was genuinely accessible. Rather, the *sha'as heter* might mean only that it was theoretically possible: *i bei havi zorik*, had the blood been thrown correctly, it could have been eaten. On this reading, even a case where the blood slept overnight without ever having been sprinkled could qualify, because in theory one could have thrown the blood. This would directly challenge Rav Ashi's contention that theoretical blood-throwing does not suffice.

### Actual Throwing Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, רְאוּיָה לָצֵאת, וּרְאוּיָה לִטַּמֵּא, וּרְאוּיָה לָלִין. אֲבָל לִינָה מַמָּשׁ, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמוֹעֲלִין! הַאי ״כׇּל (שהיה) [שֶׁהָיְתָה] לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים״ וְ״כֹל שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: No, the mishna is referring to cases where the meat left the courtyard at a time when it was fit to leave, and the meat became impure when it was fit to become impure, and was left over when it was fit to be left over, i.e., the mishna is discussing cases where these occurred after the blood was sprinkled, rendering the meat fit to be consumed by the priests. For that reason it was not subject to the halakhot of misuse of consecrated property. But according to this, what is the halakha with regard to a case where the whole offering, including the blood, was actually left overnight? Is that indeed a case where one is liable for misusing consecrated property, as the priests never had a time when it was permitted to consume the meat? If so, those statements in the mishna: Any offering that had a time that it was permitted for consumption by the priests, and any offering that did not have a time that it was permitted for consumption by the priests, are imprecise. They indicate that the critical factor is whether the meat had a time that it was potentially permitted, even if it was ultimately disqualified.

The Gemara says: *lo* — no. And it explains: *re'uyah latzet, re'uyah letamei, re'uyah lelin* — the cases of *yetzia*, *tumah*, and *linah* should be understood as cases where the meat was in a state where it was susceptible to those *pesulim*. Rashi explains this to mean that in the cases cited by Rabbi Yehoshua, the blood was actually thrown. The meat had become fully *kasher* — genuinely fit for consumption by the *kohanim* — and it was only subsequently that a *psul* of *linah*, *yotze*, or *tumah* occurred. The language *she-lana, she-nitme'ah, she-yatz'ah* refers to those *pesulim* occurring at a stage when the meat was legitimately available, which presupposes that the blood had already been sprinkled. This is, admittedly, a somewhat difficult read of the text, and Rashi and Tosafot each work to unpack it — but the essential point is that the Gemara understands the *Mishnah* to be describing scenarios where blood-throwing actually occurred, not merely where it could hypothetically have occurred.

It is also worth noting that the Gemara implicitly assumes Rabbi Yehoshua's principle must be consistent with Rabbi Shimon's framework — a point that itself warrants reflection, though the Gemara does not develop it further.

### Grammar & Hypothetical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מְעִילָה אַטּוּמְאָה קָא רָמֵית? מְעִילָה מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשָּׁה וְלָאו קְדוּשָּׁה הִיא, לְבָתַר דְּפָקְעָה לַהּ קְדוּשְּׁתֵיהּ, בְּמַאי הָדְרָא רָכְבָא לַהּ? טוּמְאָה מִשּׁוּם אוּכְלָא וְלָאו (מִשּׁוּם) אוּכְלָא הִיא, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִי בָּעֵי זָרֵיק מָצֵי זָרֵיק לֵיהּ – מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ אוּכְלָא, וּמְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין. הֵיכָא דְּאִי בָּעֵי (מָצֵי) זָרֵיק לָא מָצֵי זָרֵיק – לָא מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ אוּכְלָא, [וְלָא] מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav Ashi says: Are you raising a contradiction between the halakhot of misuse of consecrated property and the halakhot of ritual impurity? These cannot be compared, as liability for misuse of consecrated property is due to the sanctity or lack of sanctity of an item, i.e., on whether it is classified as fully reserved for God. Therefore, after the sanctity of the offering has lapsed, which occurs when the blood is ready to be sprinkled, as at that point it is already regarded as permitted to the priests, how can it return and be inured in it? But with regard to impurity, the offering's susceptibility to the impurity of food is due to whether it is considered food or not considered food. Therefore, in any case where if he wants to sprinkle the blood he could sprinkle it, it is only in sprinkling the blood that he grants the meat the status of food, and then it is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food. But in a case where if he wants to sprinkle the blood he cannot sprinkle it for some reason, and the offering is subsequently disqualified, he does not grant it the status of food, since it never became permitted to eat and it therefore is not susceptible to the impurity of food.

The Gemara then pushes further on the language. If the determining factor is whether the blood was *actually* thrown — making the meat genuinely permissible to the *kohanim* at that moment — then Rabbi Yehoshua's formulation is curious. He says *kol shehayesa lo*, "anything that *had* a time," using the past tense. If actual permissibility is what matters, why phrase it in the past? The formulation should have been *kol sheyesh lo sha'as heter*, "anything that *has* a time" — framing the moment of permissibility as the operative present fact rather than a past historical occurrence. The past tense, the Gemara suggests, sounds more like a hypothetical: something that *could have had* such a time, not something that was actually and concretely permissible. Rashi reads this grammatical nuance as an argument that the Gemara was initially tempted to read *sha'as heter* as covering merely theoretical possibility. On that reading, the past tense captures a moment that was available in principle but never actualized. Based on this grammatical pressure, the Gemara is inclined to concede that by *me'ilah*, theoretical possibility may well suffice — while maintaining that the case of food status for *tum'as ochlin* operates differently.

## Me'ilah vs. Food Status

### Kedushah & Possibility

Rav Ashi responds to this challenge directly: *me'ilah tuma ramit* — can you really draw a comparison between *me'ilah* and *tumah*? These are categorically different questions. *Me'ilah* depends on the degree of *kedushah* the object carries. Once the *kedushah* is lowered — *depaka lei kedushtei*, once the sanctity flies off, so to speak — *me'ilah* ceases. And once lost, that *kedushah* level does not return: *u-vemai hadra rachva lah*. The crucial point is that the theoretical possibility of blood-throwing is sufficient to weaken the *kedushah*. The moment it becomes conceivable — within human reach — that the blood could be thrown and the meat could be eaten, the *kedushah* begins to slip from its highest register. And once it has slipped, there is no mechanism to restore it. So by *me'ilah*, theoretical possibility changes the status in a real and permanent way.

### Food Requires Actuality

When it comes to *tum'as ochlin*, however, the question is categorically different: *mishum ochla velav ochla hi* — we are asking whether this is food. The *girsa* in the *Shita Mekubetzes* and the emendation offered by the *Bach* help clarify the text here: the principle as Rav Ashi intends it is that only where the blood was actually thrown — *d'izrik* — is the meat rendered food. Where the blood has not been thrown, the theoretical possibility that it *could* have been thrown does not suffice to grant food status. The formulation *kol heicha de'i ba'ei zarku mashvei lei ochla* would read as "wherever one could have thrown, that makes it food," which proves too much; the corrected reading is that actual throwing is what makes it food, and without it, it remains non-food regardless of theoretical accessibility.

### Losing vs. Gaining States

Rashi articulates the underlying principle that explains the asymmetry: it is easier to *lose* a state than to *gain* one. Something that is *kadosh* and sits at a high level of *kedushah* — as soon as the door is opened, as soon as it becomes conceivable that the blood could be thrown and the meat could be accessed, the *kedushah* begins to descend. That theoretical opening is enough for the item to slip from its elevated status. But to *gain* the status of food — for something that is not actually edible to be reclassified as food — mere theoretical possibility is insufficient. One cannot simply say that because the blood *could* have been thrown and the item *could* have been eaten, it therefore counts as food. The asymmetry between losing and gaining a halachic state explains why *me'ilah* responds to theoretical possibility while food status does not.

## Asham Talui Case

### Asham Discovery Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמֵּבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁלֹּא חָטָא – אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁחַט נוֹדַע לוֹ, יֵצֵא וְיִרְעֶה בָּעֵדֶר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב וְיִמָּכֵר וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יַקְרִיב, שֶׁאִם אֵינוֹ בָּא עַל חֵטְא זֶה הֲרֵי הוּא בָּא עַל חֵטְא אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection from a mishna (Karetot 23b) to Rav Ashi's opinion that with regard to susceptibility to the impurity of food, blood that is ready to be sprinkled is not considered as if it were sprinkled: With regard to one who brings a provisional guilt offering to be sacrificed, because he is uncertain as to whether he committed a sin that requires a sin offering (see Leviticus 5:17–19), and later it becomes known to him that he has not sinned, the status of the offering is as follows: If it became known to him that he had not sinned before the offering was slaughtered, the consecrated ram should go out and graze in the flock as a non-sacred animal, as the consecration was performed in error. This is the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: it is treated as a guilt offering that was disqualified and it should graze until it becomes blemished, and then it is sold, and its money that is received from the sale is allocated for communal gift offerings. Rabbi Eliezer says: It should be sacrificed in its current state, since if it does not come to atone for this sin, it will come for a different sin, as he certainly committed some sin of which he is unaware.

The Gemara now introduces a *Mishnah* from Kerisot as a potential challenge. Someone brings an *asham talui* — the contingent guilt offering brought when one is uncertain whether he committed a sin requiring a *chatas*. The classic case: two pieces of meat were before him, one permitted and one forbidden *cheilev*, and he ate one without knowing which. In a state of genuine doubt, he is obligated to bring an *asham talui* to atone provisionally, with the understanding that if he later discovers he did eat the *cheilev*, he must bring a full *korban chatas*. He sanctifies an animal for this purpose. Now suppose that before the *shechita*, he discovers that in fact he did not sin — someone clarifies which piece he ate, and it was the permitted one. In that case, *im ad shelo nishchat noda lo*, if the discovery comes before *shechita*, Rabbi Meir says: *yeitzei veyeireh ba'eder* — let the animal go back into the flock. The sanctification was made in error. Since the *asham talui* was only ever predicated on the state of doubt, and that doubt is now resolved, the sanctification retroactively has no basis. The Sages, however, disagree: *yeireh ad sheyista'eiv veyimmacher veyiplu damav lindava* — the animal retains its *kedushah*, must be allowed to develop a blemish, be sold, and the proceeds directed to a voluntary *olah*. Their reasoning is that at the moment of the vow, one presumably intended to bring a *korban* in any eventuality, even if ultimately it would take the form of a donation rather than an obligatory offering.

### Nullification Post-Shech…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁנִּשְׁחַט, נוֹדַע לוֹ – הַדָּם יִשָּׁפֵךְ, וְהַבָּשָׂר יִשָּׂרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** If, after the provisional guilt offering was slaughtered, it became known to him that he had not sinned, the blood collected in a cup to sprinkle on the altar should be spilled into the Temple courtyard drain and the meat should be burned in the place of burning, in accordance with the halakhot of a disqualified offering.

Rabbi Eliezer takes yet a different approach: bring it regardless. Since an *asham talui* does not require a highly specific concrete possibility of sin — and since people inevitably have some exposure to undetected transgression — the animal may serve as *asham talui* for some other potential sin. After the *shechita*, however, the situation changes. If one discovers *after* slaughter that he had not sinned, *hadam yishafeich vehabasar yisaref* — the blood is spilled and the meat is burned. The reason is that it is now essentially *chullin b'azarah*. Even according to the *chachamim*, who hold that the animal retains *kedushah*, Tosafot in Kerisot explains that the sanctification was never for the animal itself to serve as an *olah* but rather for its proceeds to go toward one; and since it was *shechted* as an *asham* without valid obligation, and its alternative identity as an *olah* does not apply to the animal itself, it is in any case a *korban pasul*. Alternatively, Tosafot suggests that once *shechita* takes place with the identity of an *asham*, that identity cannot be retroactively changed — even if the worshipper had in mind that it might alternatively serve as an *olah*.

### Rabbi Yosei's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִזְרַק הַדָּם – הַבָּשָׂר יֵאָכֵל, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ הַדָּם בַּכּוֹס – יִזָּרֵק וְהַבָּשָׂר יֵאָכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** If the blood was already sprinkled on the altar when it became known to him that he had not sinned, the meat of the offering is eaten by the priests in the normal manner. And Rabbi Yosei says: Even if he discovered that he had not sinned while the blood was still in the cup, it is sprinkled on the altar and the meat is eaten.

If, however, the blood was actually sprinkled on the *mizbeiach* before the discovery — *nizrak hadam* — and only afterward does one learn he had not sinned, then *habasar yeiachel* — the meat is eaten. This is because at the moment of *zerikah*, the offering was being brought in a genuine state of doubt, and was therefore brought under valid obligation. The post-facto discovery does not invalidate what was properly done. Rabbi Yosei goes further: *afilu hadam bekos yizraik vehabasar yeiachel* — even if the blood is still in a cup and has not yet been thrown, and even though the discovery has now revealed that there was no obligation, one may nevertheless proceed to throw the blood and eat the meat. This is the striking position that now engages the Gemara.

### Rava's Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֲמָרָהּ, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִזְרוֹק – כְּזָרוּק דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** And Rava says in explanation of Rabbi Yosei's opinion: Rabbi Yosei stated this ruling in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, who says that for any blood that stands to be sprinkled on the altar, it is as if it has already been sprinkled. Therefore, once the blood is in the cup and is ready to be sprinkled, the meat is permitted as though the blood already had been sprinkled. This statement contradicts Rav Ashi's opinion that with regard to the status of the meat as food, an offering whose blood stands to be sprinkled is not necessarily considered as though it has already been sprinkled.

Rava explains Rabbi Yosei's reasoning by attributing it to the school of Rabbi Shimon: *Rabbi Yosei b'shitas Rabbi Shimon amar, de'amar kol ha'omed lizrok k'zaruk dami* — Rabbi Yosei follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds that anything that is positioned to be thrown is considered as if it has already been thrown. On this reading, the moment the blood is in a cup and the *zerikah* could technically be performed, it is treated halachically as if the *korban* has already been properly offered, and the meat may therefore be eaten. This would represent a robust application of Rabbi Shimon's principle — and notably, it would apply to *gaining* a status, not merely losing one. If the possibility of throwing makes it as though the *korban* has been brought, then the meat gains the status of something that may be eaten. This constitutes a challenge to the distinction Rav Ashi drew between losing *kedushah* (where theoretical possibility suffices) and gaining food status (where it does not).

## Kli Sharet Alternative

### Kli Sharet Receives Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא? אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּקָסָבַר כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַפְּסוּלִין לְכַתְּחִילָּה לִיקְרַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Is that the reason for Rabbi Yosei's opinion? They say in the West, Eretz Yisrael, in the name of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina, that this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei: His reasoning is that he holds that a service vessel sanctifies disqualified offerings to be sacrificed on the altar *ab initio*, and in this case the blood was already in the service vessel.

The Gemara responds by offering an alternative explanation of Rabbi Yosei's position that does not rely on Rabbi Shimon at all. *Midi taima* — the real reason, as transmitted in the West in the name of Rabbi Yosei bar Chanina, is not the principle of *kol ha'omed lizrok k'zaruk dami*, but rather: *dikesavar klei shares mikadshin et hapsulin l'chatchila likarev* — Rabbi Yosei holds that a *kli shares*, a sanctified service vessel, has the capacity to sanctify even *pesulim*, at least in certain configurations, sufficiently to permit the offering to go forward.

### Safety in Kli Sharet

Rashi qualifies this as applying specifically to a relatively *weak* or attenuated *psul* — such as the one that pertains here, where the *asham* was brought in genuine doubt and only afterward did one discover there was no obligation. The *psul* that affects this offering is not a *psul* of the animal itself or of the act of *shechita*, but rather a change in the worshipper's obligation status. In such a case, once the blood has been caught in the *kli shares*, it is considered, in Rashi's framing, to have "arrived home safely." The *kli shares* creates a kind of protective threshold: now that the blood is properly contained within the vessel, even the subsequent emergence of a *psul* does not prevent the *zerikah* from proceeding. This is analogous to the principle that certain *pesulim*, once an offering has ascended the *mizbeiach*, do not require it to be brought down. Here too, once the blood is in the *kli shares*, one proceeds to complete the *avoda*. On this explanation, Rabbi Yosei's position does not commit us to Rabbi Shimon's broad principle, and the scope of *kol ha'omed lizrok k'zaruk dami* remains in question.

## Notar & Parah Adumah

### Burn Obligation Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: מִדְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִזְרוֹק כְּזָרוּק דָּמֵי, כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִשְׂרוֹף נָמֵי כְּשָׂרוּף דָּמֵי – נוֹתָר וּפָרָה אַמַּאי מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין? עַפְרָא בְּעָלְמָא נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: Since Rabbi Shimon said that for any blood that stands to be sprinkled on the altar it is as if it has already been sprinkled, and for any item that stands to be burned it is as if it is already burned, why does he hold, as the Gemara mentioned previously (101b), that meat from an offering that is leftover, and the meat of a red heifer that is not yet burned on its pyre, are both susceptible to the ritual impurity of food, since there was a time that they were fit for consumption by the priests? They are merely dust, as they stand to be burned, and therefore should no longer retain the status of food.

Rav Ashi then raises a further problem. If one grants that Rabbi Shimon really does hold *kol ha'omed lizrok k'zaruk dami* in its fullest sense — that something fit to have its blood thrown is treated as if the blood has been thrown — then by parallel logic one might also say *kol ha'omed lisrof k'saruf dami*, that something which must be burned is treated as if it has already been burned, i.e., as if it is already ash. This creates an immediate difficulty: *notar* and *parah adumah* each carry an obligation to be burned, yet we said earlier that both are susceptible to *tum'as ochlin* — they are treated as food. If the obligation to burn them makes them theoretically equivalent to ash, why should they count as food at all? *Afra b'alma nihu* — they should be considered mere dust.

### Chibat HaKodesh Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חִיבַּת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מַכְשַׁרְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana said to Rav Ashi in response: Nevertheless, regard for the sanctity of sacred property renders them susceptible to the impurity of food.

Rav Ashi responds: *chibas hakodesh machshartan* — the special relationship of sanctity that pertains to things within the *Mikdash* confers upon them a food-like status that overrides normal considerations. Even where something would not ordinarily qualify as food — whether because it is theoretically equivalent to ash or for some other reason — the elevated association with the *Mikdash* is itself sufficient to render such items susceptible to *tumah*. This principle was already encountered several *dapim* earlier in the tractate, in the context of wood, *levonah*, and other items that acquire susceptibility to *tumah* by virtue of their sanctified context rather than by their intrinsic edibility.

### Tamei vs. Pasul Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: נְהִי דְּמַהְנְיָא לְהוּ חִיבַּת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ לְאִיפְּסוֹלֵי דְּגוּפֵיהּ, לִיקְּרוֹיֵי טָמֵא נָמֵי, לְמִימְנֵי בֵּיהּ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי?
>
> **Translation:** Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Granted that the regard for the sanctity of sacred property is effective in order to disqualify the meat itself if it becomes impure, but is it also considered impure to the extent that one counts first- and second-degree with regard to it, as indicated by the language: Susceptible to the ritual impurity of food? Accordingly, if the meat came into contact with a primary source of impurity, it would have first-degree impurity and it would subsequently transfer second-degree impurity to an item that comes into contact with it.

Ravina asks a pointed follow-up question to Rav Ashi: *mihedi mehani lei chibas hakodesh lifsulei d'gufei* — I can understand that *chibas hakodesh* operates to make something more susceptible to *tumah* such that if a *tamei* object contacts it, it becomes *pasul*. But would you go so far as to say it becomes genuinely *tamei*, capable of transmitting *tumah* onward — to count *rishon* and *sheni* degrees of *tumah* removal? These are two distinct categories in the laws of *tumah v'taharah*: *tamei* means the object has absorbed *tumah* and can transmit it further; *pasul* means it is disqualified but the chain of transmission stops there. If *notar* and *parah adumah* are only considered food-like by virtue of *chibas hakodesh*, and not intrinsically, the strong language of *metamei tumas ochlin* — *transmitting* food *tumah* — seems to overclaim.

### Reish Lakish Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּבָעֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צָרִיד שֶׁל מְנָחוֹת, מוֹנִין בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי, אוֹ אֵין מוֹנִין בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי?
>
> **Translation:** If that were the case, one could resolve the dilemma that Reish Lakish raises: With regard to a dry portion of flour taken from one of the meal offerings that has not come into contact with a liquid and is therefore susceptible to impurity due only to regard for its sanctity, does one count first- and second-degree impurity with it, or does one not count first- and second-degree impurity with it? Since Reish Lakish's inquiry is unresolved, presumably the same uncertainty applies here.

Ravina observes that this question connects to one already posed by Reish Lakish: *tzarid shemen menachot* — if a *mincha* offering dried out and the oil never reached a portion of the flour, that dry flour was never rendered susceptible to *tumah* through contact with liquid in the ordinary way. Yet by virtue of *chibas hakodesh*, it gains susceptibility. Reish Lakish's question was: when this flour becomes *tamei* through *chibas hakodesh*, does the transmission proceed as *rishon* and *sheni* — i.e., is it genuinely *tamei* in the full sense — or does the chain stop, making it only *pasul*? If we could resolve the question about *notar* and *parah adumah* — accepting that *chibas hakodesh* renders them truly *tamei* in the language of *metamei tumas ochlin* — that would appear to answer Reish Lakish's question as well.

### D'Rabbanan Level

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, כִּי קָאָמְרִינַן – דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: When Reish Lakish raises the dilemma, it is with regard to the status of the dry mass of the meal offering by Torah law, as consecrated items are burned only when rendered impure by Torah law. When we said that leftover meat and the meat of the red heifer are susceptible to the impurity of food, we were inquiring about the status of the leftover meat and of the red heifer by rabbinic law, and therefore nothing can be derived from the dilemma raised by Reish Lakish.

The Gemara declines to accept this resolution. Reish Lakish was asking about the status *d'Oraisa* — whether at the level of Torah law, *chibas hakodesh* produces genuine *tumah* with full transmission capacity. The answer being discussed here, the Gemara clarifies, operates only at the *d'Rabbanan* level: *ki ka'amina d'Rabbanan* — whatever the precise Biblical framework, at the Rabbinic level we are willing to say that *chibas hakodesh* renders an item genuinely *tamei* in the full sense, including transmission. This resolves the apparent tension in using the language *metamei tumas ochlin* for items whose food status derives only from their sanctified context: at the Rabbinic level, that context is sufficient to activate the full vocabulary of *tumah* transmission, even if the Biblical analysis might operate differently.

## Mincha Vow Cases

### Vessel Type Mismatch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי בְּמַחֲבַת״ וְהֵבִיא בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, ״בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת״ וְהֵבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת – מַה שֶּׁהֵבִיא הֵבִיא, וִידֵי חוֹבָתוֹ לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of one who says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering prepared in a shallow pan, and he brought a meal offering prepared in a deep pan instead; or if he said: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering prepared in a deep pan, and he brought a meal offering prepared in a shallow pan instead, the meal offering that he brought, he brought as a voluntary meal offering, but he has not fulfilled his obligation that he undertook with his vow and he must therefore bring another meal offering.

The Gemara transitions to the next *Mishnah*, which was cited already at the beginning of the tractate. The *Mishnah* presents a series of cases involving the specification of a *mincha* offering. In the first, someone takes a vow — *harei alai* — to bring a *mincha* in a *machavat*, a shallow pan, and instead brings it in a *marcheshet*, a deep pan, or vice versa. The ruling: *mah sheheivi heivi veyedei chovato lo yatza* — what he brought was brought as a valid offering, but he has not fulfilled his vow obligation. The *mincha* is accepted as a valid donation; the vow obligation remains outstanding. The vow was specific as to the vessel type, and bringing a different vessel type — even with the same amount of flour — does not satisfy it.

### Sanctification Mismatch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זוֹ לְהָבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת״, וְהֵבִיא בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת; ״בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת״, וְהֵבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת – הֲרֵי זוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** If he said: This tenth of an ephah of flour is a meal offering to bring in a shallow pan, and he brought it prepared in a deep pan instead; or if he said: This tenth of an ephah of flour is a meal offering to bring in a deep pan, and he brought a meal offering prepared in a shallow pan, this offering is not valid, because he did not fulfill what he had stated concerning that tenth of an ephah of flour.

The second case: if he says *zo lehavi bamachavat* — I am sanctifying *this* flour to bring as a *minchat machavat* — and then brings it as a *minchat marcheshet*, or the reverse, *harei zo pesula* — it is invalid. When the flour itself is sanctified with a specific designation, using it in a manner inconsistent with that designation creates a mismatch between the *kedushah* and its execution. The result is not merely an unfulfilled vow but an invalid offering.

### Quantity Mismatch Unfulf…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵי עָלַי שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת לְהָבִיא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד״, וְהֵבִיא בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים, ״בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים״, וְהֵבִיא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד – מַה שֶּׁהֵבִיא הֵבִיא, וִידֵי חוֹבָתוֹ לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of one who says: It is incumbent upon me to bring one meal offering of two tenths of an ephah in one vessel, and he divided it and brought it in two vessels, removing a handful from each; or if he says: It is incumbent upon me to bring two tenths of an ephah for two meal offerings in two vessels, and he brought one meal offering of two tenths of an ephah in one vessel and removed one handful from it, then the meal offering that he brought, he brought as a voluntary meal offering, but he has not fulfilled his obligation.

Similarly, if someone takes a vow to bring two *esronot* of flour in two separate vessels — two *menachot* of one *issaron* each — and instead brings both in one vessel, or takes a vow to bring them in one vessel and brings them in two, the ruling again is *mah sheheivi heivi veyedei chovato lo yatza*: the offering brought is valid on its own terms as a donation, but the obligation remains unfulfilled. The specificity of the vow — both the number of *esronot* and the number of vessels — is part of its definition, and a departure from either dimension means the obligation is not discharged.

### Sanctified Configuration…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵלּוּ בִּכְלִי אֶחָד״, וְהֵבִיא בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים; ״בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים״, וְהֵבִיא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** If he says: These two tenths of an ephah before me are a meal offering in one vessel, and he divided them and brought them in two vessels, removing a handful from each; or if he says: These tenths of an ephah are two meal offerings in two vessels, and he brought them in one vessel, both of these offerings are not valid, because in both cases he deviated from the number of handfuls that he vowed to remove.

When, however, the flour itself is sanctified with a specific configuration — *elu bikli echad*, this flour to be brought in one vessel, and one then brings it in two, or the reverse — *harei elu pesulim*, the offerings are invalid. This is somewhat surprising. One might have thought that the question of one vessel versus two is a procedural detail without substantive halachic import. The *Mishnah* teaches otherwise. Rashi explains: if you said two *menachot* and brought one, you have effectively presented a *mincha yeteira* — an oversized offering — since what should have been two separate *menachot* with two *kematzim* burnt on the *mizbeiach* now has only one *komets* taken from a combined mass. And if you said one and brought two, each offering is a *mincha chaseira* — each is half of what it was supposed to be. These structural changes mean the offering is fundamentally different from what was sanctified, and it is therefore *pasul*. This is admittedly a *chiddush*: the configuration of vessels is treated as part of the essential identity of the sanctification, not merely a technical preference.

### Reminder Reframes Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵי עָלַי שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת לְהָבִיא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד״, וְהֵבִיא בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״בִּכְלִי אֶחָד נָדַרְתָּ״. הִקְרִיבָן בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים – פְּסוּלִין, בִּכְלִי אֶחָד – כְּשֵׁרִין.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of one who says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering of two tenths of an ephah in one vessel, and he divided them and brought them in two vessels, and others said to him: You vowed to bring a meal offering in one vessel, then if he sacrificed the two tenths of an ephah in two vessels they are not valid even as voluntary meal offerings, and he must bring another meal offering to fulfill his obligation. His failure to respond and explain that it was not his intention to fulfill his vow with this offering indicates that he does intend to fulfill his vow with it. Since he deviated from his vow, the offering is not valid. If he sacrificed the two tenths of an ephah in one vessel after he was reminded, it is valid, as he fulfilled his vow.

The final case in the *Mishnah* is the most intriguing. Someone takes a vow to bring two *esronot* in one vessel and then, while walking to the *Beis Hamikdash*, places them in two vessels instead. A bystander says to him: *b'kli echad nadarta* — you vowed to bring it in one vessel. He ignores the comment and proceeds to offer it in two vessels. The ruling: *pesulim* — invalid. And conversely, if someone vowed two *esronot* in two vessels and was bringing it in one, and a bystander says *bishnei keilim nadarta* — you vowed two vessels — and he proceeds to offer it in one, it is similarly invalid. Yet if, upon hearing the reminder, he corrects his approach and aligns the offering with his vow, it is *kasher*.

Rashi's explanation of why the reminder creates invalidity is that the bystander's statement frames the act in the context of the vow. The worshipper's silence and continuation are interpreted not as a statement that this is an independent offering disconnected from the vow, but rather as an implicit acknowledgment that he is attempting to fulfill his vow — improperly. What he brings is thus assessed as an attempted fulfillment of his vow, and since it does not conform to its terms, it is not merely an unrelated valid donation but an invalid offering.

This explanation, it must be admitted, is puzzling. The vow was about the worshipper's personal obligation, not about the flour. The flour was sanctified for a *mincha* without any designation of one vessel or two. How can the intervention of bystanders retroactively impose an identity on the offering that makes it *pasul*? If someone vowed a sheep *olah* and brought a cow *olah*, we would say: thank you for the cow *olah*, it is valid, but your obligation for a sheep *olah* remains. Why, here, does the verbal reminder transform a valid offering into an invalid one? This remains an open question, as Rashi and Tosafot do not provide a fully satisfying resolution.

## Vow vs. Sanctification

### Both Cases Necessary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא. דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר ״בְּמַחֲבַת״ וְקָא מַיְיתֵי בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּמַחֲבַת, וְאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, אֵימָא יְדֵי נִדְרוֹ נָמֵי יָצָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָךְ, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא פָּלֵיג לַהּוֹ, אֲבָל הָתָם דְּלָא פָּלֵיג בֵּיהּ – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites cases where one vowed to bring a meal offering prepared in a shallow pan and instead brought one prepared in a deep pan and vice versa, as well as cases where one vowed to bring two tenths of an ephah in one vessel and instead brought them in two vessels and vice versa. In all these cases, the offering is accepted but he has not fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara comments: And it is necessary for the mishna to mention both types of changes from the initial vow. It is necessary because had the tanna taught us only this first case, one might think that perhaps this individual does not fulfill his obligation because he said: In a shallow pan, and he brought it instead in a deep pan. But here, where he changes the number of offerings but both this, the offering specified in his vow, and that, the offering that he actually brought, are in a shallow pan, or both this and that are in a deep pan, I would say that he has indeed fulfilled his vow, as the difference in number of offerings brought is not significant. Therefore, the tanna taught the second case as well, to teach that the change in the number is in fact significant. And conversely, had the tanna taught us only this case, where there was a discrepancy between the number of offerings he vowed to bring and the number he actually brought, one might think that he does not fulfill his obligation only because he divided the two tenths of an ephah that were supposed to be brought together. But there, where he changed the type of pan but did not divide the flour to be used, I would say that it is not a case where he failed to fulfill his obligation. Therefore, it was necessary for the tanna to teach that where he changes the type of pan, he does not, in fact, fulfill his obligation.

The Gemara opens its analysis of the *Mishnah* by asking why both the vessel-type case (*machavat* versus *marcheshet*) and the quantity case (one vessel versus two) are necessary. The answer follows a standard Talmudic pattern: each case teaches something the other does not. *Iy ashmi'inan hach kmaisa* — had we only been taught the first case, one might have thought the invalidity results specifically because the type of pan is integral to the character of the *mincha*: a *minchat machavat* and a *minchat marcheshet* are defined by the Torah as distinct offerings with their own *parshiyos*, and vowing one and bringing the other is therefore inherently a mismatch. One might then suppose that when someone vows a *mincha* of a certain quantity in a certain number of vessels, the number of vessels is not so defining — after all, *idi v'idi machavat* and *idi v'idi marcheshet*, the flour is being prepared in the same type of pan either way — and the quantity configuration is merely incidental. *Eima de-nidron nami yeitzei* — perhaps the vow is substantially fulfilled. The second case teaches that even the number of vessels is sufficiently integral to the vow that a departure from it means the vow is not fulfilled. Conversely, had only the quantity case been taught, one might have thought the deficiency results specifically because altering the number of vessels changes the amount of flour being offered in each unit, making each *mincha* either oversized or undersized. But in the case of *machavat* and *marcheshet*, where the total amount of flour is unchanged, perhaps the vow is fulfilled. The *Mishnah* therefore needs both cases to teach that each dimension — vessel type and vessel number — independently defines the vow.

### Kli Sharet Non-Sanctifyi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַה שֶּׁהֵבִיא – הֵבִיא, וִידֵי נִדְרוֹ לֹא יָצָא. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף יְדֵי נִדְרוֹ נָמֵי יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a baraita a case similar to that of the mishna: If one brings a meal offering somewhat different from that which he vowed to bring, then the one that he brought, he brought as a voluntary meal offering, but he has not fulfilled his vow. Rabbi Shimon says: He has even fulfilled his vow, as Rabbi Shimon maintains that the type of pan or the number of offerings is not significant.

A *baraita* is then cited that bears on the case where flour is sanctified with a specific configuration. The *baraita* states that the *kli shares* does not sanctify the flour with a fixed identity prior to the offering. The relevance: if someone designated flour as *elu bikli echad* — this flour in one vessel — and then placed it in two vessels, why not simply recombine it before the *avoda*? The *baraita* suggests that the *kli shares* does not yet lock in its identity in a way that would prevent correction. The implication seems to be that even after placing the flour in an incorrect vessel, the situation could be remedied.

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ לְהָבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת – וְהָא תַּנְיָא: לֹא קִידְּשׁוּם כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא קִידְּשׁוּם לִיקְרַב, אֲבָל קִידְּשׁוּם לִיפָּסֵל. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁקְּבָעָן בִּשְׁעַת נֶדֶר, אֲבָל בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if he says: This tenth of an ephah of flour is a meal offering to bring in a shallow pan, and he brought a meal offering prepared in a deep pan instead, it is not valid. The Gemara asks: But isn't it taught in a baraita that in this case the service vessel does not consecrate the flour, since he brought it in a different service vessel than he had vowed? Therefore, the meal offering is still non-sacred and can be used. The Gemara answers that the baraita should be understood according to that which Abaye says: Under such circumstances, the service vessel does not sanctify them with regard to being sacrificed on the altar, but it does sanctify them in order to become disqualified. And Abaye says an additional comment about the previous case: The Sages taught in the mishna that in the case of a change in the type of meal offering, the meal offering is not valid only when he assigned it at the time of the vow. But if at the time of the vow he simply said: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering, and at the time of the designation of the fine flour as a meal offering he specified a particular type of meal offering, he is not obligated to bring that type, and if he brought a different type, he has still fulfilled his obligation.

Abaye reconciles the *baraita* with the *Mishnah*'s ruling of *pesulim*: *lo kadshem likarev aval kadshem lipassel* — the *kli shares* does not make the offering *kasher* for presentation, but it does fix its identity sufficiently to establish invalidity. This is a well-known distinction in halachic analysis. Once the flour is in the *kli shares*, its identity is crystallized — it cannot be reconfigured without that configuration becoming its defining characteristic. If that configuration is misaligned with how it was sanctified, the *kli shares* has locked in the misalignment, making it *pasul*, even though it has not yet made it valid for offering in the positive sense.

Abaye adds a further important principle: *lo shanu ela shekeva'am besha'as neder* — the rule that misalignment creates invalidity applies only when the specific configuration was established at the time of the *neder* itself. *Aval besha'as hafrasha lo* — but if the configuration was specified only at the time of separating the flour (*hafrasha*) and not at the time of the original vow, then that subsequent specification does not define the identity of the offering in the same binding way. The reason: the *neder* is the generative act that defines the obligation, and the identity it creates is what governs. When someone vows simply to bring a *mincha* and then, at the time of *hafrasha*, specifies that it will be a *marcheshet* or a *machavat* or will be in two vessels, that specification at *hafrasha* does not carry the same halachic force. If the offering is ultimately brought in a different configuration than specified at *hafrasha* but consistent with what was left open at the time of the *neder*, there is no *psul*. This principle is confirmed by a parallel transmission: Rabbi Yitzchak bar Chanina in the name of Rabbi Yosei in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states the same rule — *lo shanu ela shekeva'am besha'as neder aval besha'as hafrasha lo, ka'asher nadarta velo ka'asher hifrashta*.

### Reminder Problem Unresol…

This principle makes the final case of the *Mishnah* all the more perplexing. In that case, the worshipper took a personal vow obligation — he never sanctified the flour in a specific configuration. He vowed to bring two *esronot* in one vessel or two vessels and then walked toward the *Beis Hamikdash* with the flour arranged differently. The bystander's reminder framed the act as an attempt to fulfill the vow, and the Gemara rules it *pasul* as a result. But if the time of *neder* is what fixes identity, and the *neder* here was simply a personal obligation without a specific flour-vessel designation, how does the bystander's oral comment at the time of delivery suddenly impose a specific identity on the offering — one that then makes it *pasul* for being misaligned? The flour was never sanctified with any configuration. The vow was abstract. Nothing at the time of *neder* fixed a vessel identity. Why should what amounts to a verbal exchange at the moment of approaching the *mizbeiach* now redefine the nature of the *mincha* and render it invalid? Rashi and Tosafot do not resolve this difficulty, and we leave it as an open question.
