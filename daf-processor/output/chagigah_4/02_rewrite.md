# Chagigah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Amon & Moab

### R. Eliezer's Report

We pick up approximately six lines before the wider lines begin on the page — about twelve lines from the bottom, where the text reads *ma ta'am*. We were in the middle of a discussion that featured two strikingly different approaches: that of Rabbi Eliezer and that of Rabbi Yehoshua, dealing with what happens when their students came and reported what was transpiring in the *beis medrash*. The heart of the dispute concerned Rabbi Yehoshua's eagerness to hear a multiplicity of voices — that multiplicity itself being part of what *Torah she-be'al peh* is about — whereas Rabbi Eliezer held that Torah transmission is only about the straight tradition. Rabbi Eliezer was therefore deeply offended that his students had held a debate, taken a vote, and reached a conclusion on a matter about which he possessed a direct tradition. The student who reported this to him had his eyes fall out — an arresting detail, given that the surrounding discussion concerns a blind person and *aliyah l'regel* and *mitzvas re'iyah*, though whether there is any intentional echo there is difficult to say.

### Amon/Moab Rules

The substance of what was decided — the tradition Rabbi Eliezer claimed to possess — is now stated: Amon and Moab, the trans-Jordanian territories, do not observe the *shemita* year, yet they do observe *trumos u-ma'asros* and give *ma'aser ani* during the *shemita* year. The Gemara then asks: *ma ta'am* — what is the reason?

### Kedusha Rishona

The answer is that many cities were conquered by those who came up from Egypt in the time of Yehoshua and were sanctified with the sanctity of the land of Israel, but they were not re-sanctified in the time of Ezra. Therefore they do not technically possess *kedushah* and are not bound by the agricultural *mitzvos* of *trumos u-ma'asros* and *shemita* — *mipnei she-kedusha rishona kidesha l'sha'ata v'lo kidesha l'asid lavo*, because the first sanctity in the time of Yehoshua was not for all time. (This question of whether *kedusha rishona* was permanent or temporary was discussed in Megillah and is rooted in *pesukim* that speak of the return to the land — the re-entry in the time of Ezra being compared to the original entry, such that a second conquest would be necessary to re-sanctify the land. The related question of whether all *shemita* today is *derabbanan* is itself a major debate, which we will not unpack here.)

### Cities for Poor

The Gemara adds a further and specific point: certain cities were *intentionally* left outside the re-sanctification — *kedei she-yismachu aleihem aniyim bashevi'is*, in order that the poor should be able to rely on them during the *shemita* year.

### Shemita Paradox

This Gemara is a striking and ironic one. What it is saying, in effect, is that if you were to observe *shemita* in those territories, the poor would actually be worse off. The idea that *shemita* benefits the poor — by leaving produce accessible to all — is undermined by the very requirement to let the land lie fallow. If the land is not being worked, what is there for the poor beyond whatever grows wild? So the best arrangement for the poor turns out to be the opposite of *shemita*: work the land, and then give a tenth of the produce as *ma'aser ani* to the *aniyim*. Since the first sanctity of those territories was not permanent and the re-sanctification never took place, the subsequent observance of these agricultural laws was only *derabbanan*, not *de'oraisa* — and therefore the Rabbis had the authority to determine what would apply. They ruled accordingly: since *shemita* is not practiced there, the land is worked, and *ma'aser ani* is given, ensuring that the poor of the land of Israel have what to live off of during a year when the land of Israel itself lies fallow.

This Gemara beautifully captures a deep internal tension within *shemita*: the value of leaving the land fallow operates at cross purposes with the value of sustaining the poor. The same tension plays out in various modern *shemita* mechanisms — *heter mechira*, *pruzbul*, and related devices — all of which reflect the reality that some of the values embedded in *shemita* can pull in opposite directions.

### Eyes Restored

After this discussion, the Gemara records in a *baraita*: *le'achar shenityashevah da'ato* — after Rabbi Eliezer had calmed down, he said, *yehi ratzon sheyachzir einay Yosei limkomam* — may it be God's will that Yosei's eyes be restored to their place — *vachazaru* — and they returned to their sockets. The story is grotesque, but it is told in the immediate context of *mitzvas re'iyah*, and that thematic frame is hard to ignore.

## Shoteh Definition

### Three Behaviors

The Gemara now moves from the *cheresh* to the *shoteh*. This discussion, though brief, is an important one for thinking about how the Gemara defines someone who is not of sound mind and therefore lacks halakhic legal responsibility. *Eizehu shoteh* — who is a *shoteh*? Three observable behaviors are listed: *hayotze yachidi balaylah* — one who goes out alone at night (telling us something about the society in which they lived, where no normal person ventures out alone at night due to wild animals and robbers); *vehalan beveis hakevaros* — one who sleeps in cemeteries; *vehamakarea et kesuto* — one who tears his own garments for no apparent purpose.

On this list, two positions are recorded. Rav Huna says: *ad sheyehu kulam bevat achat* — only if he does all three together does he qualify as a *shoteh*. Rabbi Yochanan says: *afilu be'achat mehen* — even one of them suffices.

### R. Yochanan

The Gemara then presses: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? The force of the question is this: if the person is doing any one of these acts *derech shtut* — in a way that clearly indicates irrational behavior — then why would Rabbi Yochanan need more than one? And if he is doing them in a way that does not indicate irrationality, even all three together should not suffice. So what exactly is each opinion maintaining?

### Derech Shtut

The Gemara resolves: *le'olam deka avid lehu derech shtut* — we are indeed talking about a person who does these things in a manner that appears irrational. But the Gemara then introduces an important qualification: even when the act looks irrational on its face, there may yet be an explanation we have not considered.

### Alt Explanations

Take the case of one who sleeps in a cemetery. It looks completely irrational — but perhaps, says the Gemara, *kedei shetishre alav ruach tumah hu deka avid*, he is trying to attract an evil spirit, to engage in some form of black magic. Within a worldview in which spirits can be summoned and manipulated, this is not irrational at all. Similarly, the one who goes out alone at night — *emor gandanrifas achadei*, perhaps *gandanrifas* has taken hold of him. Rashi understands this as either an overwhelming anxiety attack or a severe fever that drives him to seek fresh air; in either case, an unusual but not necessarily irrational circumstance might explain the behavior. And the one who tears his garments — perhaps he is simply an absentminded person, so lost in his thoughts that he does not notice what his hands are doing, the way some people ruin a tie or unravel a seam mid-conversation without any awareness. So in each individual case, the behavior looks irrational, yet an explanation is at least conceivable.

### Pattern Evidence

This is where Rav Huna's position gains its force. The Gemara says: *keivan de'avdinhu lekulhu* — once he has done all three, what single explanation could account for three completely different seemingly irrational acts? The Gemara compares this to the case of an ox that gored a donkey, a camel, and another ox — *vena'aseh muad lakol* — and we establish that it is a habitual gorer with no discrimination about its targets. The common denominator across three different categories is itself the evidence. Just as three gorings of different animals establishes an indiscriminate pattern, three different irrational acts — each explicable on its own but not together — establish a general pattern of incompetence. This is Rav Huna's logic: any single act might have an innocent explanation; the composite of three distinct acts, each of a different type, admits only one general explanation — the person is not of sound mind.

Rabbi Yochanan's position, requiring only one act, sets a considerably lower threshold and is for that reason more troubling: one does not want too low a threshold for placing someone in this category.

It is worth noting what the Gemara is consistently trying to do here: as a legal system, it wants to focus on observable activities and to fit the analysis into a framework it already knows — the laws of *muad* — in order to make the determination replicable. And yet the Gemara's own question, *ei de'avid derech shtut ei de'lo avid derech shtut*, reveals that the real driver is contextual judgment. Each case has to be assessed on its own. There is a tension between the desire for concrete, checkable criteria and the recognition that no fixed list can substitute for genuine evaluation of the individual.

This also connects to the broader question of what these three behaviors actually are. They read as folk definitions — the kinds of things people in that society would point to and say, "that is what crazy looks like." The Gemara then takes those folk definitions and tries to impose a formal halakhic structure on them, using the logic of *muad* and *tam*. Whether the formal structure can fully bear the weight of the question is itself something the Gemara leaves in tension. One might ask, for example, about someone who is schizophrenic and moves between states — *itim shoteh itim shafui* — a case the Gemara does engage with elsewhere. Or about someone in a manic episode who is compulsively spending money — which connects directly to the next source.

### Wasting Money

Rav Pappa cites a *baraita* from the tractate of *Trumos* that adds a further criterion: *eizehu shoteh zeh me'abed kol mah shenosnim lo* — who is a *shoteh*? One who destroys or wastes everything given to him. You hand the person a hundred dinars and he throws it in the street. Here, a single act of a particular type — destroying one's own property — is sufficient to establish the status of *shoteh*.

### Teiku

This prompts the Gemara to ask: would Rav Huna, had he heard this *tosefta*, have reversed his position? The question is framed carefully. Perhaps he would have reversed himself only with respect to the case most analogous to this one — *mikarea kesuto*, the tearing of garments, since tearing one's garment is a form of destroying one's own property, similar to wasting money. If so, he would have said: that one act alone suffices, while going out at night and sleeping in a cemetery still require the composite of all three. Or perhaps, once he conceded that a single act of this type suffices, he would have generalized and said any one of the three is sufficient by itself. The Gemara leaves the question unresolved: *teiku*.

The *Tosafos* cites a line from the Yerushalmi that adds further texture. The Yerushalmi records a debate parallel to the Bavli's, but then contains the statement of Rabbi Avin: *mistabra k'Rabbi Yochanan b'chada mim'abed mah shenosnim lo* — it makes sense to rule like Rabbi Yochanan at least in the case of *me'abed mah shenosnim lo*, because *afilu shoteh shebishotim eino me'abed mah shenosnim lo* — even the most mentally impaired person does not casually throw away everything given to him. That act, in other words, is so beyond any conceivable rational explanation that even one instance is diagnostic in a way the other behaviors are not.

What the Gemara ultimately leaves us with is this: the key question is always whether the act is being done *derech shtut* or not. Observable criteria and formal lists are the legal system's attempt to provide clarity, but the Gemara itself acknowledges that some criteria may be individually sufficient while others are not, and that the individual circumstances of each case cannot be fully replaced by a mechanical checklist.

## Tumtum/Androgynous

### Zacur Exclusion

The Gemara now addresses two categories of ambiguous or third-category sex: *tumtum* and *androgynus*. A *tumtum* is a person whose genitalia are present but covered by skin due to a birth defect or similar condition — we cannot see them and therefore cannot determine the person's sex. An *androgynus* is a person who actually has both male and female genitalia. Both are not obligated in the *mitzvas re'iyah*.

The Gemara opens with a *baraita*: *zachar* — from the word *z'chorkha*, "your males" — *l'hotzi et ha'nashim*, to exclude women. *Z'chorkha*, the possessive "your males," *l'hotzi tumtum v'androgynus*, to exclude a *tumtum* and an *androgynus*. And then the word *kol*, "all" — *kol z'chorkha l'rabot et ha'ketanim* — to include minor sons. The Gemara proceeds to unpack each of these derivations.

First, why does the verse need to tell us women are excluded? The Gemara notes that *aliyah l'regel* and bringing an *olat re'iyah* is a *mitzvat aseh shehazman grama* — a positive time-bound commandment — and women are generally exempt from time-bound positive commandments. So we should have been able to derive the exemption of women from that general principle without needing an explicit verse. The Gemara answers: *itztrich* — the verse is necessary. Without it, we might have applied a *gezeirah shavah* from *hakhel*, where the Torah explicitly includes men, women, and children in the assembly. We might have reasoned: just as there women are obligated, so here too women should be obligated. The verse therefore comes to teach us that they are not. It should be noted — and the *Rambam* already points this out — that the general principle of *mitzvat aseh shehazman grama* is wrong approximately half the time. It is more descriptive than predictive, and it tends to appear at the *stam* level of the Gemara as a retrospective question rather than a reliable rule.

Additionally, the Yerushalmi records that women are indeed obligated in *aliyah l'regel* itself, derived from *hakhel* — they are simply not obligated to bring the *korban*. So the exclusion operates specifically at the level of the *olat re'iyah*, not at the level of appearing in Jerusalem altogether.

### Androgynus Logic

The Gemara then turns to the exclusion of *androgynus*. *Bishlama androgynus itztrich* — I understand why a verse is needed here. Without an explicit exclusion, *salka daitakh amina* — I would have thought: *ho'il v'it lei tzad zachrut*, since this person has male genitalia as well as female, there is an aspect of maleness present that might suffice to obligate them. *Kamashma lan* — the verse teaches otherwise: *d'beriyah bifnei atzma hu* — an *androgynus* is their own category, neither male nor female, and therefore not included within the narrow definition of "male" operative here.

This is worth pausing on. The recognition in the Gemara that the world is not simply divided male and female — that there may be a third category, a person who has both male and female genitalia — is a striking feature of this discussion. The Gemara is focused exclusively on observable genitalia and not on other markers of gender, but the recognition of a third category is nonetheless remarkable. Not everyone agrees that *androgynus* represents a genuine third category; some maintain the person is male or female and we simply cannot determine which. But the possibility of a third category is taken seriously here.

The *androgynus* presents a fascinating complexity across different halakhic contexts. *Tosafos* notes an opinion in the Gemara that an *androgynus* would receive a *bris milah* on *Shabbos* — because the presence of male genitalia and an *orlah* is sufficient to establish an absolute obligation in *bris milah*, even if it does not establish that the person is unambiguously male in all respects. The category sometimes pulls toward inclusion as male and sometimes toward exclusion, and here it pulls toward exclusion. The logic is that for *olat re'iyah*, the definition of "male" is drawn narrowly enough that having only a partial male identity does not suffice.

### Safek Problem

The Gemara then asks about *tumtum*: *ela tumtum sfeka hu* — a *tumtum* is simply a case of doubt, male or female we just do not know. *Mi itztarich kra lemi'utei sfeka* — why would you need a verse to exclude a case of doubt? We know how to handle doubt in *halakha*.

But this raises an immediate problem. If someone came to a rabbi with a doubt about whether they are obligated in a *mitzvah de'oraisa*, the answer would generally be: do it. The principle of *sfeka de'oraisa lechumra* — in cases of biblical doubt, we are strict — would seem to require including a *tumtum* in the obligation. The Gemara's question is therefore itself somewhat surprising: why would we need a verse to *exclude* a doubtful case, rather than a verse to *include* it?

The *Rambam* resolves this by maintaining that the principle of *sfeka de'oraisa lechumra* is itself only *derabbanan* — it is a rabbinic rule to be strict in cases of biblical doubt. According to the Torah itself, one could be lenient in a case of doubt. On that view, a *tumtum* would not be obligated by default, and you would need a verse to establish that the doubt resolves toward obligation rather than exemption. *Tosafos*, by contrast, raises a further complication: why not simply have the *tumtum* bring an *olah* regardless of the doubt, since an *olah* that is not brought as an *olat re'iyah* is still permitted as a voluntary offering? *Tosafos* works through why that would not be straightforward, but the point illustrates that the question of how to treat the *tumtum*'s doubt is genuinely complex and not obviously resolved in favor of exemption.

### Covered Genitalia

*Itztarich kra lemi'utei sfeka, amar Abaye keshebeitzo mibachutz* — the verse is needed for the following specific case, as Abaye explains: a person whose scrotum is externally visible but whose shaft is covered, the rest of the male genitalia not being exposed. In this case, there is no actual doubt about the person's sex — we can see the scrotum and know the person is male. Yet because the genitalia are not fully exposed and visible, the person does not fully qualify as *zachar* for purposes of this particular obligation.

This is a genuinely unusual ruling. For virtually all other halakhic purposes this person is categorized as male without question. The word *zachar* in the Torah is relatively rare — the Torah more typically speaks of *ish*, a man — and *zachar* may carry a more specifically anatomical connotation that demands a more literal standard of observable maleness. Here, even where we know what the genitalia are, the fact that they are not fully exposed is enough to exclude the person from the narrow category of *zachar* operative in this *sugya*.

This connects to a broader pattern in the discussion: the Gemara consistently demands completeness and unambiguity. The obligation of *olat re'iyah* seems to require, in the Gemara's reading, that the obligated person be in the most fully realized and unambiguous version of every relevant category — fully male, fully free, fully able-bodied, fully pure. Anyone who falls short of that complete, unambiguous realization, in any one of those dimensions, is excluded. The Gemara grounds each exclusion in textual derivation, but the cumulative picture is of a halakhic norm that centers an idealized, complete type, and exempts or excludes those who fall outside it in any respect. This raises genuinely troubling questions — which we will return to in what follows — about the difference between exemption from an obligation and effective exclusion from a communal experience.

## Minors/Chinuch

### Chinuch Status

The Gemara now addresses minors. The word *kol* — "all of your males" — *lerabot et ha'ketanim*: the inclusive language comes to include minor children. But the Mishnah explicitly excludes a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan*. There is an apparent contradiction.

Abaye resolves it: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. One case concerns a *katan shehigia lechinuch* — a minor who has reached the age of training — and the other case concerns a *katan shelo higia lechinuch* — a minor who has not yet reached that age. Abaye could have resolved the contradiction more simply, by saying: if the child can make the journey, he is obligated; if not, he is excluded. That reading would have made the minor's inclusion biblical. But Abaye deliberately does not take that path. Instead he introduces the rabbinic concept of *chinuch* — the obligation of a father to train his children in the performance of *mitzvos*.

The consequence is significant: *katan shehigia lechinuch derabbanan hee* — any obligation assigned to a minor who has reached the age of *chinuch* is rabbinic, not biblical. The verse's inclusive language is then recharacterized: *v'kra asmachta be'alma* — the verse is not the source of the obligation but merely an *asmachta*, a textual support for a rabbinic enactment. One would not have suspected this from the Mishnah alone, which reads as though a minor's obligation might be biblical, nor from the verse itself. The Gemara forces this reframing because it cannot accept a genuine biblical obligation for a minor.

This reframing carries practical consequences. For instance: if a minor brings a *korban* for *olat re'iyah* on the first day of *yom tov*, and then reaches *bar mitzvah* during *chol ha-moed*, must he bring another *korban* now that he is biblically obligated? If his earlier *korban* was brought only under a *chinuch* obligation, does it count toward his new biblical obligation? These are the kinds of questions the reframing generates, and they are not merely theoretical.

The verse's inclusive language is then redirected: *ela v'kra lemai ata* — if the verse does not come to include minors in a biblical sense, what does it come to include?

## Smelly Tradesmen

### Three Trades

The Gemara answers that the verse comes to include what is taught in the name of *Acherim*: *hamkameitz*, one who handles dung — Rashi explains this is for purposes such as working leather or fertilizing; *vehamtzareif nechoshet*, one who smelts copper down in the copper mines, an extremely malodorous occupation; *vehabursi*, a tanner. All three are *peturim min ha're'iyah* — exempt from appearing.

### Odor Rationale

The reason given is derived from the phrase *kol z'chorkha* — all your males. *Mi sheyachol la'alot im kol z'chorkha* — one who can come up *together with* all your males, one with whom other people would be willing to travel. *Yatz'u elu she'einam re'uyim la'alot im kol z'chorkha* — these three are excluded because nobody would want to travel with them, and no one would want to be downwind of them on the pilgrimage road.

### Pilgrimage Cohesion

This is a striking hermeneutical move. The exemption here has no formal grounding in a particular physical incapacity or halakhic status of the person. It is entirely relational — it concerns how others experience the person's presence. And the rationale makes implicit sense if we understand *aliyah l'regel* not merely as an individual act but as a communal one. A pilgrimage is not simply a large number of individuals each going independently to the same place. People travel together, in groups, in communities; the journey itself is a shared experience. Someone who cannot integrate into that communal journey — not because of any unwillingness on their part, but because of what their occupation has done to them — stands apart. The verse's inclusive phrase, "all your males," is read as implicitly requiring the capacity to travel as part of that collective.

The hermeneutical strategy here is fascinating in its own right: a verse that could be read inclusively is turned, through the phrase *im kol z'chorkha*, into an exclusionary criterion. One might ask whether the verse should instead be read as correcting the social tendency to marginalize these workers — insisting that they too must come. The Gemara reads it the other way: precisely because they cannot easily join the group, they are exempt.

Some *rishonim* raise a further question about these three occupations: whether the odor is temporary and can be removed by bathing before the *aliyah*, or whether after years in such trades the smell becomes permanently absorbed into the skin and cannot be fully removed. The practical implications for when the exemption applies depend on how one answers that question.

There is a deeper issue here as well, and it connects to what runs through this entire *sugya*. The cumulative list of exemptions — women, slaves, minors, the lame, the blind, the elderly, the *tumtum*, the *androgynus*, those with partially covered genitalia, and now those who smell of their trade — creates an implicit portrait of who the ideal *oleh l'regel* is. Each exclusion defines the category by its edges, and the center of the category begins to look like a very narrowly defined type. The Gemara is using legal exemption language, but the social effect of that language is to identify who belongs most fully to the communal experience and who stands at its margins. One does not want to be too quick to impose modern categories onto ancient texts, but the pattern is hard to ignore, and it raises serious questions about what the halakhic category of exemption does and does not imply for inclusion in the broader communal and religious life.

## Slaves & Women

### Adon Logic

The Gemara now addresses women and unfreed slaves together. The exclusion of women is already established. The question is: *ela avadim m'na lan* — from where do we exclude slaves? Normally, slaves share the same level of obligation as women — so why not simply derive the exemption of slaves from the exemption of women without an independent verse?

Rav Huna provides an answer rooted in a specific verse: *el pnei ha'adon Hashem* — to appear before the Master, the Lord. *Mi she'ein lo ela adon echad* — only one who has but one master — God — is obligated. *Yatza zeh sheyesh lo adon acher* — a slave, who has another human master controlling his choices and movements, cannot in the relevant sense "appear before God" as a fully free agent. The slave's legal subordination to a human master creates a structural incompatibility with appearing before the divine Master.

### Women/Slave Parallel

The Gemara then asks: *hai l'ma li kra* — why was a verse even needed? We already know the general rule: any *mitzvah* that a woman is obligated in, a slave is obligated in; and any *mitzvah* that a woman is exempt from, a slave is exempt from. Since women are exempt here, slaves should be exempt as a matter of course.

This parallel is not entirely arbitrary. Both women and slaves, for different reasons, are not fully independent legal agents in the world of the Gemara. An unmarried adult woman is theoretically entirely free, but in practice a woman would typically move from her father's house to her husband's house, and the extent to which she is a fully autonomous agent is limited in various ways. The linkage between these categories reflects something real about the social structure the Gemara inhabits, even if it is deeply uncomfortable from a contemporary perspective.

### Half/Half Case

Ravina resolves the question by explaining that the verse is needed for a specific and unusual case: *mi shechatzyo eved v'chatzyo ben chorin* — the half-slave, half-free person. How does one arrive at such a status? One might be owned by two partners, one of whom freed his share while the other did not. This person is partly free and therefore already obligated in *mitzvos* to some degree. One might have thought that the portion of him that is free makes him at least partially obligated in *olat re'iyah*. The verse *el pnei ha'adon* comes to exclude even this case.

The Gemara confirms this reading from the precise language of the Mishnah: *nashim va'avadim she'einam meshuchrarim* — women and *unfreed* slaves. The specification "unfreed" would be redundant if we simply meant any slave. The word *she'einam meshuchrarim* — not fully freed — indicates we are dealing with a case of incomplete freedom, i.e., the half-and-half case. And the requirement once again is completeness: one must be totally free, not partially free, to fall within the obligation.

## Physical Barriers

### Regalim Meaning

The Gemara now addresses physical impediments: lameness, blindness, illness, and old age. The *baraita* derives the exemptions from the word *regalim* — the pilgrimage term, rooted in *regel*, foot or leg. *Prat leba'alei kavin* — to exclude those who have a prosthesis, those who do not have a functioning leg. *Davar acher*: *regalim prat lecheiger* — also to exclude the lame whose leg does not function, *v'choleh* — the ill, *v'suma* — the blind, *v'zaken* — the elderly, *v'she'eino yachol la'alot beraglav* — and anyone who cannot physically ascend on their own legs.

### Blind Exclusion

The inclusion of a blind person in a list otherwise concerned with the physical capacity to walk might seem surprising — a blind person's legs function normally. But as the Gemara implies here, a blind person would need a walking stick to navigate, cannot walk independently without assistance, and cannot make the ascent on their own. It is worth noting that this rationale for excluding the blind — inability to navigate the journey — is distinct from the rationale for excluding one who is blind in one eye that the Gemara will provide shortly, which is textual and involves the language of *yeira'eh yeira'eh*. The same category of blindness generates different exclusionary rationales depending on which verse and which aspect of the *mitzvah* is operative.

### Generic Unable

The phrase *v'she'eino yachol la'alot beraglav* functions as a generic residual category. Given this, one might ask: why bother listing the specific cases of lame, blind, ill, and elderly at all? The generic formulation would cover them. The Gemara implicitly acknowledges this: perhaps the specific cases are examples, and what we are really meant to take away is the general principle — if you cannot ascend on your own legs, you are exempt. The question of whether the specific listing matters halakhically remains.

### Pampered Person

Rava adds one further category to this list: *mefanki* — someone who is extremely pampered or fastidious. Rashi explains the specific case: *she'ein holchin belo min'al* — one who refuses to walk without shoes — *v'ein adam nichnas har habayis bemin'alo* — and one is not permitted to enter *Har haBayis* while wearing shoes. The connection to the verse is then supplied: *mi bikeish zos miyadchem remos chatzerai* — "who asked you to trample in My sanctuary?" — God does not want the sanctuary trampled by shoes, and therefore someone who would refuse to remove their shoes is not obligated to come. Rashi's reading focuses narrowly on shoe-wearing specifically, rather than the more general idea of a person who demands to be carried everywhere and cannot endure the ordinary physical demands of the pilgrimage.

The larger point, again, is one of completeness and capacity: the obligation of *olat re'iyah* applies only to those who are able to fulfill it in the full, normal, unassisted way. Any physical impediment — whether structural, situational, or even attitudinal — removes the obligation.

## Impure/Uncircumcised

### Tamei Logic

Two final categories are addressed: the *tamei* and the *arel*. The *tamei* person is exempt. The verse states: *uvasa shama v'heiveisa shama* — you will come there and bring there. *Kol she'yeshno b'viyah yeshno b'hava'ah* — anyone who can appear is obligated to bring; *v'chol she'eino b'viyah eino b'hava'ah* — anyone who cannot appear is not obligated to bring.

### Korban Purpose

One might ask: why not simply send the *korban* through an agent — *shaliach* — even if you cannot appear yourself? The Gemara's answer reveals a crucial conceptual point. The *mitzvah* is *olat re'iyah* — an offering of *appearance*. The *korban* is not a substitute for appearing; it is the concretization of appearing. The Rabbis have given the obligation of *re'iyah* — appearing before God at the Temple — a specific form: you appear by bringing a *korban*, and through the *korban* your presence is expressed. But this means the *korban* and the appearance are linked: the *korban* represents the one who brings it precisely because that person has appeared. If the person cannot appear at all — is *tamei* and therefore halakhically barred from entering — there is no appearance to represent, and the *korban* has no standing as an *olat re'iyah*.

This raises an interesting boundary question. Suppose a person is in Jerusalem but simply chooses not to ascend to the Temple — they have their *shaliach* bring the *olat re'iyah* on their behalf. Have they discharged their obligation? Presumably the obligation would be considered fulfilled, since they *could* have appeared; the *korban* can represent their presence because they were in principle present and available. But the *tamei* person is in a different situation: the incapacity is not a matter of choice but of halakhic status, and therefore the *korban* cannot stand in for an appearance that is legally impossible.

This line of reasoning also generates the following observation: if the *tamei* person is exempt from the *korban* because they cannot appear, the same logic would apply to the blind person, the lame person, and the others — they too are exempt from the *korban* because their inability to appear in the full halakhic sense means the *korban* cannot represent their appearance. The exemption, in all these cases, runs through the concept of appearance rather than being a direct exemption from a *korban* obligation.

### Arel=Tamei

The Gemara then asks about the *arel* — the uncircumcised man. *Minalan* — from where do we derive his exemption? He is not permitted in the *Beis haMikdash*. The Gemara answers: *ha manni Rabbi Akiva hi* — this ruling follows Rabbi Akiva, who derives from the *baraita*: *ish ish* — the doubled phrase in the context of which *kohanim* may eat *terumah*. The repetition *ish ish* teaches that not only a *tamei kohen* may not eat *terumah*, but even an *arel kohen* is excluded — the *arel* is treated *k'tamei*, like a *tamei*. Rabbi Akiva generalizes this principle: wherever *tamei* is excluded from Temple-related matters, *arel* is excluded as well.

### One Eye Blind

The Gemara closes with a ruling attributed to Rabbi Elazar ben Divai in the name of Rabbi Yehuda: a person blind in one eye is exempt. The source is the classic *gezeirah shavah* on the word *yeira'eh*: the Torah writes *yeira'eh* — "he shall be seen" — and *yireh* — "he shall see." *Kedereich she-ba liros kakh ba leheira'os* — just as one comes to see, so too one comes to be seen. *Mah ba liros be-shtei einav* — just as one comes to see with both eyes, *af leheira'os be-shtei einav* — so too one comes to be seen with both eyes, as it were — God sees the person with both divine "eyes." Therefore, *af leheira'os be-shtei einav* — one must be able to see with both of one's own eyes to be obligated. A person blind in one eye, even though they require no walking stick and can make the physical journey without difficulty, is nevertheless exempt from the *korban olat re'iyah* — not because of any physical incapacity to travel, but because they cannot fulfill the bilateral seeing that the verse requires.

This concludes the Gemara's enumeration of categories exempt from the *olat re'iyah*.
