# Menachot 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Minchas Chotei

### Kohen's Sin Mincha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים כְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל – מָה מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל נִקְמֶצֶת, אַף מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים נִקְמֶצֶת. אִי מָה מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל נִקְמֶצֶת וּשְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין, אַף מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים נִקְמֶצֶת וּשְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "לַכֹּהֵן כַּמִּנְחָה", לַכֹּהֵן כַּמִּנְחָה, וְלֹא לָאִשִּׁים כַּמִּנְחָה. הָא כֵּיצַד? קוֹמֶץ קָרֵב בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְשִׁירַיִם קְרֵיבִין בְּעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of the **priests** as **equivalent to** the status of **a meal offering of a sinner** brought **by an Israelite. Just as** with regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by an Israelite, a handful is removed, so too,** with regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of the **priests, a handful is removed.** The *baraita* challenges this: **If** so, then say as follows: **Just as** with regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by an Israelite, a handful is removed and its remainder is eaten, so too,** with regard to the **meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of the **priests, a handful** should be **removed and its remainder eaten.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And the remainder shall be **the priest's, as the meal offering,"** which is interpreted to mean that **with regard to** the rite performed by **the priest,** his meal offering is **like the meal offering** of the Israelite, **but** it is **not like the meal offering** of the Israelite with regard to consumption **by the fires** of the altar. **How** is **this** possible? In what ways does the meal offering of a priest resemble that of an Israelite in some respects and not in others? The priest's **handful is sacrificed by itself,** like that of the Israelite, **and the remainder is sacrificed by itself,** unlike those of the Israelite, which are eaten.

The daf opens by picking up at the very bottom of Ayin Gimel Amud Beis. The underlying question is one that runs through the entire opening sugya: the Torah establishes that when a Kohen brings a Mincha, the entire offering is burned — there is no Kmitza and no eating of the remainder. But that rule was formulated with a standard Mincha in mind, one that functions somewhat like an Olah. What happens when the Mincha is a *minchas chotei* — a sin-offering — where there is no logical parallel to burning the whole thing, since it is functioning as a type of Chatas? The Tanna Kamma rules that you burn the whole thing regardless. Rabbi Shimon disagrees: you perform a Kmitza, burn the Kmitza separately, and burn the remainder separately. Both agree, then, that everything is ultimately burned — the *minchas chotei* Shel Kohanim is not excluded from the general category of *minchas kohen*. The dispute is whether it retains the structure of a *minchas chotei* by requiring a Kmitza.

### Scriptural Analysis


The Gemara asks: Mena Hanei Mili? Where is this derived from? Detanu Rabbanan — our Rabbis taught from the pasuk in *vayikra hei*: V'hayisaho *lakohen kamincha* — "and it shall be for the *kohen like* a Mincha." This pasuk appears at the conclusion of the Olah V'Yored passage, which describes the situation of one who cannot afford birds and brings instead an Asiris Ha'eifah Soles — a tenth of an ephah of fine flour — as a Chatas. The passage ends: V'chiper Alav Hakohen Al Chata'aso Asher Chata Me'achas Me'eleh V'nislach Lo V'hayisaho *lakohen kamincha*. Even though this Mincha functions as a Chatas, the Torah concludes that it is "like a Mincha." What does that phrase come to teach?

The Beraisa offers two possible readings. The first is *shetei avodah kashera bo* — that the word Lakohen teaches that even if this is the Kohen's own Chatas, he may still perform the Avodah for it himself, a previously unestablished principle. The second possibility: O Eino Ela L'hatir *minchas chotei* Shel Kohanim — perhaps the pasuk comes to permit the Kohen to eat the remainder of his own *minchas chotei*, treating it like an ordinary Minchas Yisrael where the Kohen eats the Shirayim. If that reading were correct, how would one reconcile it with the general pasuk V'chol *minchas kohen* *kalil tihyeh* Lo Seiachel — every Mincha of a Kohen shall be fully burned and not eaten? That general prohibition, one might argue, applies only to B'minchas Nedavaso — his free-will Mincha — whereas by his Chovaso, his obligatory Mincha brought for a sin, perhaps it may be eaten.

### Kamincha Interpretation

To resolve this, the Beraisa invokes the word Kamincha. The argument runs as follows: had the Torah said only Lakohen — that it belongs to the Kohen — one might have read it as granting him the right to eat the remainder. But the addition of Kamincha, "like a Mincha," forces a comparison. The question is: like which Mincha? Like the Kohen's own standard Mincha — that is, his Minchas Nedava, which is fully burned. The derivation is Makish Chovaso L'nedavaso — his obligatory Mincha is compared to his free-will Mincha. Mah Nedavaso Einah Ne'echeles, just as a free-will Mincha of a Kohen is not eaten but fully burned, Af Chovaso Einah Ne'echeles, so too his obligatory Mincha is not eaten. The reading is thus: Kamincha points to the Kohen's own standard Mincha, meaning it is fully burned, while Lakohen teaches that he retains the right to perform the Avodah himself.

### Separate Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר "וְהָיְתָה לַכֹּהֵן כְּמִנְחָתוֹ"? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא "כַּמִּנְחָה"! אֶלָּא לְהַקִּישׁ מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים כַּמִּנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל – מָה מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל נִקְמֶצֶת, אַף מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים נִקְמֶצֶת. אִי מָה מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל נִקְמֶצֶת וּשְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין, אַף מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים נִקְמֶצֶת וּשְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "לַכֹּהֵן כַּמִּנְחָה" – לַכֹּהֵן כַּמִּנְחָה, וְלֹא לָאִשִּׁים כַּמִּנְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** In disagreeing with the previous interpretation, **Rabbi Shimon said: Is it stated: And it shall be the priest's, as his meal offering? But it states only: "As the meal offering,"** referring to the meal offering of a non-priest. **Rather,** this verse serves **to compare** the meal offering of a sinner brought by a priest as **equivalent to** the status of **a meal offering of a sinner** brought **by an Israelite. Just as** with regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by an Israelite, a handful is removed, so too,** with regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of **the priests, a handful is removed.** The challenge: **If** so, then say as follows: **Just as** with regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by an Israelite, a handful is removed and its remainder is eaten, so too,** with regard to the **meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of the **priests, a handful** should be **removed and its remainder eaten.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And the remainder shall be **the priest's, as the meal offering,"** which means: **with regard to** the rite performed by **the priest,** his meal offering is **like the meal offering** of the Israelite, **but** it is **not like the meal offering** of the Israelite with regard to consumption **by the fires** of the altar.

Rabbi Shimon pushes back. Vechi Ne'emar Vehaya Lakohen Kaminchaso — does the Torah say "like his Mincha," in the possessive, which would force the comparison to the Kohen's own Minchas Nedava? V'hallo Lo Ne'emar Ella Kamincha — it says only "like a Mincha," without the possessive. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon reads Kamincha differently: *lehakish *minchas chotei* shel kohanim kaminchas chotei* Shel Yisrael — to connect the *minchas chotei* of a Kohen to the *minchas chotei* of a Yisrael. Not to a Minchas Nedava, but to the parallel *minchas chotei* that a Yisrael would bring.

Rabbi Shimon does not go so far as to say the Kohen may eat the remainder — the general pasuk prohibiting any Kohen from eating his Mincha is still operative. But at minimum, the comparison to the *minchas chotei* of a Yisrael teaches that a Kmitza is performed and the Kometz is separated from the remainder. Mah *minchas chotei* Shel Yisrael Nikmetzes, just as the *minchas chotei* of a Yisrael requires a Kmitza, Af *minchas chotei* Shel Kohanim Nikmetzes, so too the *minchas chotei* of a Kohen requires a Kmitza.

One might then ask: should we draw the equation all the way? Ei Mah *minchas chotei* Shel Yisrael Nikmetzes V'shireiha Ne'echalin — just as a *minchas chotei* of a Yisrael has Kmitza and the remainder is eaten, perhaps Af *minchas chotei* Shel Kohanim Nikmetzes V'shireiha Ne'echalin, so too the *minchas chotei* of a Kohen has Kmitza and the remainder is eaten. The Beraisa rejects this: Talmud Lomar V'hayisaho *lakohen kamincha*. The word Lakohen teaches that the Kohen treats it in the manner of a Mincha — meaning, from the Kohen's perspective, he performs the Avodah as he would for any *minchas chotei*, with a Kmitza. But *velo le'ishim kamincha* — with respect to the fire, it is not like a standard *minchas chotei*. The fire takes everything. The formulation is striking: for the Kohen, Kamincha — it is like a Mincha; for the Ishim, it is not like that Mincha.

The upshot is that both the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Shimon agree that the Kamincha of this pasuk is the decisive word, but they dispute whether it refers to the Kohen's own Minchas Nedava (Chachamim) or to the *minchas chotei* of a Yisrael (Rabbi Shimon). The Tanna Kamma reads it as one unified unit to be burned in full; Rabbi Shimon reads it as having the structural form of a *minchas chotei* — with a Kmitza — while still burning everything.

### Kohen's Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא שֶׁתְּהֵא עֲבוֹדָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה בּוֹ, מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא, מִנַּיִן לַכֹּהֵן שֶׁבָּא וּמַקְרִיב קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו בְּכׇל עֵת וּבְכׇל שָׁעָה שֶׁיִּרְצֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וּבָא בְּכׇל אַוַּת נַפְשׁוֹ וְשֵׁרֵת". אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי – דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בָּא עַל חֵטְא, אֲבָל דָּבָר שֶׁבָּא עַל חֵטְא – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** According to the opinion of the first *tanna* in the *baraita* cited earlier, a priest may perform the rites of a meal offering of a sinner that he brings for himself based on the verse "And the remainder shall be the priest's, as the meal offering." The Gemara now asks: With regard to **this** *halakha* **that its rite is valid** when performed **by him,** is it **derived from here?** It is **derived from there,** another verse cited in a *baraita*, which states: **From where** is it derived **with regard to a priest that** he may **come and sacrifice his offerings at any time and at any hour that he desires,** even when it is not during the period of his priestly watch? **The verse states:** "And if a Levite comes from any of your gates from all of Israel, where he lives, **and comes with all the desire of his soul** to the place which the Lord shall choose, **then he shall serve** in the name of the Lord his God, as all his brothers the Levites do, who stand there before the Lord" (Deuteronomy 18:6–7). The Gemara answers: **If** the *halakha* were derived **from there,** the verse in Deuteronomy, **I would say: This statement,** that a priest may perform his rites whenever he chooses, applies only to **a matter that does not come** to atone **for a sin,** as the verse is referring to an offering that he desires to bring. **But** with regard to a **matter that comes** to atone **for a sin, I would say** that he may **not** perform the rite himself. Therefore, the *halakha* of the meal offering of a sinner must be derived from the verse in Leviticus: "And the remainder shall be the priest's, as the meal offering."

The Gemara then turns to the other element derived from the pasuk — the right of a Kohen to perform the Avodah for his own korban. Hashata Avoda Ksheira Bo — now that we know a Kohen may perform the Avodah of his own *minchas chotei*, do we learn this principle from here, from the word Lakohen? Mei'hacha Nafka? The Gemara objects: we already know from another source. A Beraisa asks, Minayin L'kohen She'ba U'makriv Korbonosav B'chol Eis U'v'chol Sha'ah She'yirtzeh — how do we know a Kohen always has the option of bringing his own personal Korbanos and performing their Avodah himself? This relates to the system of rotating mishmaros — each family of Kohanim served in a fixed weekly rotation, and within each week the sub-families had their days. Nevertheless, for one's own Korbanos, a Kohen always retains the right to serve. This is derived from the pasuk in Devarim: U'ba B'chol Avas Nafsho V'sheireis — and he shall come whenever his soul desires and serve.

If we learn this principle from there, why is it also needed here, from the *minchas chotei*? *im mei'hasam* Hava Amina Hanei Mili Davar She'ein Ba Al Cheit — from that pasuk alone, one might have thought the rule applies only to a Korban that is not sin-related. A free-will Korban, which a Kohen brings by choice, seems more naturally one over which he retains personal control. Aval Davar Sheba Al Cheit, Eima Lo — but for a sin-related Korban, one might have thought the Kohen may not serve for himself. The intuition is significant on two levels. First, there is something fitting about autonomy in voluntary giving; you are acting from choice and so can also choose how to present it. Second, and more compellingly, there is a structural logic to the *beis haMikdash* where someone else performs atonement on your behalf. If anywhere that logic should apply, it is precisely when the Kohen himself is the sinner — that he would need another to bring his Korban before God. Therefore, the pasuk of Kamincha is needed to teach that even for a sin-related Mincha, the Kohen may perform his own Avodah.

### Devarim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא נָמֵי מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: "וְכִפֶּר הַכֹּהֵן עַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַשֹּׁגֶגֶת בְּחֶטְאָה בִשְׁגָגָה", מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַכֹּהֵן מִתְכַּפֵּר עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ! אִי מֵהָהוּא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי – בְּשׁוֹגֵג, אֲבָל בְּמֵזִיד – לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is **this** *halakha* that a priest may perform the rite of his own sin offering **also derived from here,** i.e., the verse: "And the remainder shall be the priest's, as the meal offering"? It is **derived from there: "And the priest shall effect atonement for the soul that sins unwittingly, when he sins unwittingly"** (Numbers 15:28). The term "for the soul that sins unwittingly" **teaches that the priest** may **effect atonement** even **through himself,** when he performs the rite. The Gemara answers: **If** the *halakha* were derived **from that** latter verse, **I would say: This statement,** permitting him to effect his own atonement, applies when the offering is brought **for** a sin committed **unwittingly. But** with regard to an offering brought **for** a sin committed **intentionally,** the priest may **not** effect his own atonement. Therefore, the verse that states: "And the remainder shall be the priest's, as the meal offering," **teaches us** that the priest may perform the rite even for an offering that he brings for an intentional sin.

The Gemara presses further. Ela Ma'ata Avoda She-bachutz, Aima Lo? Even granting the above, what about Avodah performed outside the Mikdash — does the Kohen have the right to bring his own Korban even there? The Gemara notes: Ha Nami Mehacha Nafka — we learn even that from a different pasuk, namely V'chiper Hakohen Al Hanefesh Hashogeges *b'chet bishgaga*, from the passage in Bamidbar Tet-Vav about a certain Chatas. This pasuk is read as Melamed She-hakohen Mitkaper Al Yedei Atzmo — teaching that the Kohen may bring his own Chatas and achieve atonement through his own act.

But then: I Me-hahu, if the principle came only from that pasuk, Hava Amina Hani Mili B'shogeg — one might have thought it applies only when the sin was committed B'shogeg, in error. The pasuk explicitly says Bishgaga — in error — and there is a certain logic to saying: if the sin was accidental, the Kohen is less morally compromised and so may present himself before God. Aval B'meizid, Lo — but if the sin was intentional, perhaps he has no right to bring his own Korban. The *minchas chotei* is therefore needed as an additional source because it is part of the Olah V'Yored framework, which includes certain cases of B'meizid — notably Zadon Shvua, a deliberate false oath in the context of Shvuas Eidus. In those cases as well, the Kohen may bring his own Korban. The word B'meizid in the printed text carries a dalet marking it as a later gloss — Rashi did not have those words — and it appears to have been a marginal note by a later editor identifying the relevant case, subsequently incorporated into the text.

## Kohen's Own Korban

### Sin Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים נִקְמֶצֶת, וְהַקּוֹמֵץ קָרֵב בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, וְהַשִּׁירַיִם קְרֵיבִין בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַקּוֹמֶץ קָרֵב בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְהַשִּׁירַיִם מִתְפַּזְּרִין עַל בֵּית הַדֶּשֶׁן. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: הָוֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, בֵּית הַדֶּשֶׁן דְּהֵיכָא? אִי דִּלְמַעְלָה – הַיְינוּ אֲבוּהּ, אִי דִּלְמַטָּה – יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁקָּרֵב לְמַטָּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: דִּלְמָא לְאִיבּוּד? אַחִיכוּ עֲלֵיהּ, וְכִי יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁקָּרֵב לְאִיבּוּד?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon that a handful of the meal offering of a sinner brought by a priest is separated and offered upon the altar, it **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **the meal offering of a sinner** brought **by** one of **the priests, a handful is removed; and the handful is sacrificed by itself, and the remainder is sacrificed by itself. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: The handful is sacrificed by itself, and the remainder is scattered upon the place of the ashes.** With regard to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan discusses it** and asks: **To which place of the ashes** in the Temple is he referring? **If** he is referring to the one **that is above,** upon the altar, his opinion **is** identical to that of **his father,** Rabbi Shimon, who stated that the remainder is offered and burned upon the altar, after which the ashes are swept onto the ash heap upon the altar. If so, according to both opinions, the remainder is taken to the same place. And **if** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, is referring **to** the ash heap **that is below** the altar, that is difficult: Do **you have** any **item that is sacrificed below** the altar? **Rabbi Abba said to** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: **Perhaps** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, means that the remainder is scattered there **to be wasted.** When the Sages heard this, **they laughed at him,** saying: **But** do **you have** any **item that is sacrificed** as part of the Temple service in order **to be wasted?**

The Gemara now presents a second Beraisa. Rabbi Shimon holds that the Kometz of the *minchas chotei* Shel Kohanim is brought on its own and the Shirayim are burned on their own. His son, Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon, goes further: Hakometz Kareiv Be-atzmo V'hashirayim Mitpazrim Al *beit hadeshen* — the Kometz is brought by itself, but the remainder is scattered on the *beit hadeshen*. The reasoning: you cannot eat the Shirayim because it is a *minchas kohen*; you cannot burn them on the Mizbeach as an act of Hakrava because you have already performed the Kmitza and only the Kometz is offered. So what do you do with the Shirayim? You scatter them on the *beit hadeshen*.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba reports that Rabbi Yochanan raised a question about this: *beit hadeshen de-heicha* — which *beit hadeshen* is meant? I De-le-ma'ala — if it means the ash pile on top of the Mizbeach where things are burned and ashes collect, then Hainu Avuha — this is the same position as Rabbi Shimon his father. You are effectively saying the Shirayim are burned on the Mizbeach. I De-le-mata — if it means the lower *beit hadeshen*, the place at ground level in the Azarah: Rashi understands this as the area beside the ramp where ashes were deposited after Trumat Hadeshen in the morning, while Tosafot understands it as the place in the Azarah where *parim hanisrafim* V'se'irim Hanisrafim are burned. Either way, the act is not one of Hakrava — it is disposal.

Rabbi Abba responds: *dilma le-ibbud* — perhaps it simply means you destroy it. The Shirayim cannot be eaten, cannot be offered, so you destroy them. The Gemara mocks this answer — Achichu Alei — asking: *ve-chi yesh lecha davar she-karev le-ibbud*? Is there any case where something is Lechatchila brought specifically to be destroyed? Yet this objection is itself puzzling, because the parallel to *parim hanisrafim* — where the blood is offered inside, portions burned on the Mizbeach, and the remaining meat destroyed — seems a reasonable comparison. Nevertheless, the Gemara treats it as an anomalous category.

### Bagegel/Bameizid


The position of Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon thus creates a genuinely unusual structure: the Kometz is offered on the Mizbeach as normal, while the Shirayim occupy a kind of limbo — they may not be eaten, they may not be offered, and the only resolution is destruction. This is, as noted, the core tension his position occupies.

### Philosophy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין: "כׇּל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה לֹא תֵאָכֵל" – לַאֲכִילָה הִקַּשְׁתִּיהָ, וְלֹא לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that there are consecrated items that are intentionally wasted. With regard to such items, **the father of Rabbi Avin teaches: "And every meal offering of the priest shall be offered in its entirety; it shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 6:16). Why must this verse, which follows those discussing the High Priest's daily griddle-cake offering, state that it shall not be eaten, after it already states that it is entirely offered? The verse teaches: **I have likened** the meal offering of a sinner brought by a priest to the daily griddle-cake offering brought by the High Priest **with regard to eating,** insofar as it is not eaten, **but** I have **not** likened it **with regard to another matter,** i.e., that the meal offering of a sinner is not burned in the manner of the griddle-cake offering.

The Gemara then cites a Beraisa attributed to the father of Rabbi Avin: *kol *minchas kohen* *kalil tihyeh* lo se'achel* — every Mincha of a Kohen shall be Kalil, shall be complete, it shall not be eaten. This pasuk in Tzav comes immediately after the Torah makes explicit what is done with the *minchas chavitin* of the Kohen Gadol — half brought in the morning, half in the afternoon — and the Torah states there Chok Olam L'Hashem Kalil Tuktar, burn it all. Then follows the general statement: *v'chol *minchas kohen* *kalil tihyeh* lo se'achel*.

The key observation is that the general pasuk does not use the language Kalil Tuktar — fully burn it — but only *kalil tihyeh lo se'achel*: it shall be Kalil, it shall not be eaten. The Beraisa notes: L'achila Hikishtih V'lo L'davar Acher — the pasuk equates it to eating, and nothing else. It says do not eat it, but it does not explicitly command burning it fully. This is the textual basis from which Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon derives his middle position. He holds that a Kmitza is taken based on the earlier pasuk; the Shirayim cannot be eaten because of *lo se'achel*; they need not be burned as a full offering because the pasuk does not say Kalil Tuktar; and therefore they are destroyed. The prohibition is against eating — and nothing more can be derived from the text.

### Position Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא אָמַר? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכִי קָא אָמַר – "כׇּל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן ... לֹא תֵאָכֵל" – חוֹבָתוֹ, "כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה" – נִדְבָתוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: סַכִּינָא חֲרִיפָא מַפְסְקָא קְרָאֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: "כׇּל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה" – נִדְבָתוֹ, "לֹא תֵאָכֵל" – חוֹבָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is he saying,** as the verse states explicitly that it must be entirely offered? **Abaye said** that **this is what** he **is saying:** This is how the verse is to be read: **"Every meal offering of the priest…shall not be eaten,"** when the offering is sacrificed to fulfill **his obligation,** such as the meal offering of a sinner. It is neither eaten nor completely burned, but rather placed on the ash heap. When the verse states: **"Shall be offered in its entirety,"** this is referring to **his gift** offering, which is completely burned in the manner of a griddle-cake offering. **Rava said to him:** This reading is like a **sharp knife cutting the verses** to pieces, as it interprets the beginning and end of the verse together, ignoring the middle. **Rather, Rava says:** When the verse states: **"Every meal offering of the priest shall be offered in its entirety,"** this is referring to **his gift** offering, which is entirely offered. When the verse states that it **"shall not be eaten,"** this is referring to **his obligatory** meal offering, which is neither eaten nor entirely offered.

*mai ka'amar* — what exactly is Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon saying through this pasuk? Amar Abaye Hachi Ka'amar — Abaye explains the reading as follows: the pasuk Kol *minchas kohen* *lo se'achel* addresses the Chovato, the obligatory Mincha — do not eat it — while *kalil tihyeh* addresses the Nedavaso, the free-will Mincha — burn it fully. The free-will Mincha, like an Olah, is fully burned; the obligatory *minchas chotei* is not eaten but also not burned as a full offering — its Shirayim are destroyed on the floor.

Rashi and Tosafot both note the derivation's internal logic: if the Torah wanted only to say "burn it fully," it would have been sufficient to say so without adding *lo se'achel* — of course you are not eating something that is fully burned. The redundancy teaches that there are two distinct categories: one that is burned fully (the Minchas Nedava) and one that is neither eaten nor burned fully (the *minchas chotei*), with the remainder destroyed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא? מִסְתַּבְּרָא נִדְבָתוֹ הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן תְּדִירָה, לָא חָטֵי, בְּסִים רֵיחֵיהּ. אַדְּרַבָּה, חוֹבָתוֹ הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן עִשָּׂרוֹן חוֹבָה! הָנָךְ נְפִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why should the verse be read that way? **I** can **reverse** the interpretation and say that the priest's obligatory meal offering is entirely offered, and his gift offering shall be neither eaten nor entirely offered. The Gemara answers: It **stands to reason** that **his gift offering should be included** in the phrase "shall be offered in its entirety," **as** it resembles the griddle-cake offering and differs from the meal offering of a sinner in several respects, namely, **that** it is **frequent,** that the priest bringing it **did not sin,** and that **its scent is fragrant,** as it is mixed with oil, and frankincense is added to it. The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** the verse **should** be interpreted **to include his obligatory** meal offering, **as** the obligatory meal offering is similar to the griddle-cake offering in that, unlike the gift offering, it comprises **a tenth** of an ephah and bringing it is **an obligation.** The Gemara responds: Because **these** similarities between the gift offering and the griddle-cake offering **are** more **numerous** than those between the obligatory offering and the griddle-cake offering, it is logical that the verse is comparing the gift offering to the griddle-cake offering.

Rava objects: Sakina Charifa Mafska Krai — a sharp knife that divides the pasuk? You are taking half the pasuk as addressing the *minchas chova* and the other half as addressing the Minchas Nedava. Yet the objection is immediately puzzling, because Rava himself proceeds to do the exact same thing. Ela Amar Rava — rather, says Rava: Kol *minchas kohen* *kalil tihyeh* addresses the Nedavaso, burn it fully; *lo se'achel* addresses the Chovaso, do not eat it — but also do not burn it. As Rashi and Tosafot observe, the only difference between Abaye and Rava is the order in which the pasuk is read: Abaye reads it in reverse (Chovah then Nedava), while Rava reads it in the natural forward sequence. The substantive halakhic positions are identical.

V'eipoich Ana — the Gemara raises the possibility of reversing the assignments: perhaps it is the *minchas chotei* that is burned fully and the Minchas Nedava whose Shirayim are destroyed. The Gemara responds that it is more sensible to say the Nedava is compared to the *minchas chavitin*: Mistavra Nedavaso Hvei L'ribuyei, the free-will Mincha should be equated with the Chavitin because Shekein Tadira — both are recurring (the Nedava can come at any time), *lo choti* — neither comes for a cheit, and *basim reichei* — both have a pleasing odor because both contain oil, whereas the *minchas chotei* has no oil, and the phrase *reiach nichoach* is never applied to the *minchas chotei*, reflecting not only the absence of oil but the fact that it is not framed as a positive offering.

The Gemara raises the counter-argument: Aderaba — perhaps the obligatory *minchas chotei* should be the one compared to the Chavitin, since Shekein Isaron — both are limited to one Isaron of flour, unlike the Minchas Nedava which may be larger, and Chovah — both are obligatory. The Gemara responds: Hanei Nefishan — the points of similarity with the Nedava are more numerous (three versus two), and therefore we conclude that it is the free-will Mincha that is burned fully while the Minchas Chotei's Shirayim are handled differently.

It is worth noting that this entire analysis belongs to Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon. The Chachamim hold there is simply no difference — both the *minchas chotei* and the Minchas Nedava of a Kohen are fully burned in the same manner. The more nuanced and arguably more persuasive positions are those of Rabbi Shimon and his son, who recognize that a *minchas chotei*, which is structurally parallel to a Chatas, should at least preserve the Kmitza structure that defines that category.

## R. Elazar Position

### Beit Hashen Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי "כׇּל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה לֹא תֵאָכֵל", מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עֶלְיוֹנָה בְּ"כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר", וְתַחְתּוֹנָה בְּ"לֹא תֵאָכֵל". מִנַּיִן לִיתֵּן אֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה, וְאֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "כָּלִיל" "כָּלִיל" לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "כָּלִיל", וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן "כָּלִיל", מָה לְהַלָּן בְּ"כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר", אַף כָּאן בְּ"כָּלִיל תׇּקְטָר". וּמָה כָּאן לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ, אַף לְהַלָּן לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § After discussing the opinions of Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, the Gemara asks: **And** as for the opinion of **the Rabbis** that no handful is removed from any of the meal offerings of the priests, with regard to **this** verse: **"And every meal offering of the priest shall be offered in its entirety; it shall not be eaten," what do they do with it?** How do they interpret the superfluous phrase: "It shall not be eaten"? The Gemara answers: **They require it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning the griddle-cake offering, the verse states: "It shall be entirely smoked for the Lord" (Leviticus 6:15). From this verse and the verse that follows it, which states: "And every meal offering of the priest shall be offered in its entirety; it shall not be eaten," **I have** derived **only** that the griddle-cake offering mentioned **above,** in the first verse, is subject **to: "It shall be entirely smoked," and** that other meal offerings of priests mentioned **below,** in the second verse, are subject **to: "It shall not be eaten."** **From where** is it derived **to apply** the prohibition **that was said about this** case **to that** case, **and** the prohibition **that was said about that** case **to this** case, to teach that the priests' meal offerings shall be entirely smoked and the griddle-cake offering shall not be eaten? **The verse states: "Entirely,"** in the first verse, and: **"Entirely,"** in the second, to be utilized **as a verbal analogy: It is stated here,** with regard to the priests' meal offerings: **"Entirely," and it is stated there,** with regard to the griddle-cake offering: **"Entirely."** **Just as there,** the offering is subject **to** the prohibition of: **"It shall be entirely smoked," so too here,** the offerings are subject **to** the prohibition of: **"It shall be entirely smoked." And just as here,** with regard to the meal offering of the priest, the verse serves **to impose a prohibition upon eating it, so too there,** with regard to the griddle-cake offering, the verse serves **to impose a prohibition upon eating it.**

The Gemara then asks: V'rabbanan Hai *kol *minchas kohen* *kalil tihyeh* lo se'achel* — if the Chachamim do not split this pasuk to address two different types of Mincha, what do they do with it? Miba'u Lei Lachada Tanya — they use it for a different teaching entirely. The Beraisa states: Ain Li Ela Elyonah B'kalil Taktir V'tachtonah B'lo Se'achel — from the earlier texts alone, I know only two separate things: the *minchas chavitin* has a positive commandment to be fully burned (Kalil Taktir), while the general *minchas kohen* has a negative prohibition against eating (*lo se'achel*). But I do not yet know whether each prohibition extends to the other case: does the *minchas chavitin* also carry a Lav against eating, and does the general *minchas kohen* also carry a positive Mitzvah to fully burn it?

Minayin Liten Es Ha'amur Shel Zeh Be-zeh V'es Ha'amur Shel Zeh Be-zeh — how do I know to apply the content of each pasuk to the other?

### Leibbud Defense


Talmud Lomar Kalil Kalil Le-*gezeirah shavah* — the word Kalil appears in both places, in the context of the *minchas chavitin* (Kalil Tuktar) and in the context of the general *minchas kohen* (*kalil tihyeh*). Through this *gezeirah shavah*: Mah Lalan B'kalil Taktir Af Kan B'kalil Taktir — just as by the *minchas chavitin* there is a positive Mitzvah to fully burn it, so too for every *minchas kohen* there is a positive Mitzvah to fully burn it. U'mah Kan Liten Lo Sa'aseh Achilaso Af Lalan Liten Lo Sa'aseh Achilaso — and just as by a general *minchas kohen* there is a negative prohibition against eating it, so too by the *minchas chavitin*. Rather than using the pasuk to distinguish between two categories of Mincha — as Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon does — the Chachamim use it to unify all Minchos of a Kohen, establishing both a positive obligation to fully burn and a negative prohibition to eat, for every case.

## Chachamim Position

### Kalil Tihyeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבִינָא: כֹּהֵן שֶׁאָכַל מִן הָאֵימוּרִין, מָה הוּא? לָאו דְּזָרוּת לָא קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי, כִּי קָא מִבַּעְיָא לִי לָאו דְּ"כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה", מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the prohibition against eating from certain offerings derived from the verse that states: "Shall be offered in its entirety; it shall not be eaten," **Ravina asks:** In the case of **a priest who ate from the sacrificial portions, what is** the *halakha*? As to whether this priest violates **the prohibition of a non-priest** eating certain types of sacrificial meat, expressed in the verse: "No non-priest shall eat of the sacrificial meat" (Leviticus 22:10), **I do not raise the dilemma. When I raise the dilemma,** it is with regard to the **prohibition of: "Shall be offered in its entirety;** it shall not be eaten." **What** is the *halakha*? Is this prohibition applicable only to the meal offering of a priest mentioned in the verse, or does it apply to a priest who eats from the sacrificial portions as well?

The question is not about the Lav of Zarut, which applies to a non-Kohen who eats Kodshim; Ravina is asking something different. Regarding the Lav de-Zarut — *lo kamibaya li* — that is obvious, because even though he is a Kohen, vis-à-vis things that are burned on the Mizbeach he is considered a Zar, a foreigner with no right to eat them. Ki Kamibaya Li *lav d'chalil tihyeh* — the question is whether there is a separate, generalized Lav derived from *kalil tihyeh lo se'achel*. Does that prohibition extend beyond the context of a *minchas kohen* to anything that is designated to be fully burned on the Mizbeach? Tosafot notes that the question brackets the issue of Me'ilah entirely, focusing specifically on this additional Lav.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַהֲרֹן לְרָבִינָא: תָּא שְׁמַע דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא בְּ"כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה", לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aharon said to Ravina: Come** and **hear** an answer to this question from that **which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to **every** sacrificial item, not just the meal offering of a priest, such as the sacrificial portions, **which is** included **in** the category of **"shall be offered in its entirety"** that requires it to be burned, the verse serves **to impose a prohibition against eating it.**

Rav Aron responds to Ravina: Ta Shema D'tanya — come hear, for a Beraisa teaches: Rabbi Eliezer Omer *kol shehu b'chalil tihyeh* Liten Lo Ta'aseh Al Achilato — Rabbi Eliezer says that anything governed by the rule of *kalil tihyeh* carries a negative prohibition against eating it. Therefore, if a Kohen eats from things that are to be fully burned on the Mizbeach, he transgresses this Lav, beyond the transgression of Zarut. What emerges from Rabbi Eliezer's position is that the prohibition of *lo se'achel* is not confined to the *minchas kohen* specifically but extends to any item governed by the principle of Kalil — anything that shall be wholly burned. Tosafot has a broader discussion reconciling this with other Gemaras that seem to limit the transgression of Zarut to items that a Kohen is himself permitted to eat; but in any case, what Rabbi Eliezer's ruling establishes here is the existence of this generalized Lav.

## Imurim Question

### Positive/Negative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, וּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים – לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְאֵין בָּהֶן לַכֹּהֲנִים. וּבָזֶה יִפָּה כֹּחַ מִזְבֵּחַ מִכֹּחַ הַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The meal offering of priests, the meal offering of the anointed priest,** i.e., the High Priest, **and the meal offering** brought with **libations** that accompany burnt offerings and peace offerings are burned in their entirety **on the altar, and there is no** part **of them for the priests. And in** the case of those offerings, **the power of the altar is greater than the power of the priests.**

The Mishnah presents two contrasting categories. Minchas Kohanim U'minchas Kohen Mashiach U'minchas Nesachim L'mizbeach — the Mincha of an ordinary Kohen, the *minchas chavitin* of the Kohen Gadol, and the *minchas nesachim* that accompanies a free-will animal Korban — all of these go entirely to the Mizbeach. *v'ein bahem l'kohanim* — the Kohanim receive nothing from them. U'vaze Yafe Ko'ach Mizbeach Mikoach Kohanim — in this category, the Mizbeach has the greater portion over the Kohanim.

The Gemara immediately asks: V'tu Leika? Are there no other things that go entirely to the Mizbeach? *v'ha'ika olah* — what about an Olah? The answer: Ika Or L'kohanim — the Olah is not fully to the Mizbeach; the Kohanim receive the hide. V'ha'ika Olat Ha'of — what about a bird Olah, which goes entirely on the Mizbeach with no hide to distribute? The answer: Ika Mor'ah V'notzah — the crop and feathers are cut out and thrown beside the Mizbeach, so it does not all go on the fire. *v'ha'ika nesachim* — what about the wine of Nesachim, which goes entirely to the Mizbeach? L'shitin Azli — the wine goes into the caverns or underground channels; it does not burn on the Mizbeach itself.

### Ravina's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי "בָּזוֹ"? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמִּתְנַדֵּב יַיִן מְבִיאוֹ וּמְזַלְּפוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי אִישִּׁים, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דִּלְשִׁיתִין אָזְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what** is meant when the mishna emphasizes: **And in** the case of those offerings, the power of the altar is greater than the power of the priests, indicating that there are other items that one might have thought are burned entirely on the altar as well? The Gemara answers: The mishna's wording serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said: One who donates wine brings it and pours it over the fires** of the altar, such that it is offered in the manner of an animal offering and not as a libation. Therefore, the mishna's wording **teaches us that** wine donated in this manner **goes to the drainpipes,** unlike according to the opinion of Shmuel that it is entirely burned.

U'mai Baze — why then does the Mishnah emphasize that these are the unique cases? *l'afukei mi'd'shmuel* — to exclude the opinion of Shmuel. D'amar Shmuel Ha-minadev Yayin Meivi U-mezalfo Gavye Ishim — Shmuel holds that if one donates wine to the Mizbeach, he sprinkles it directly on the fire. If Shmuel were correct, donated wine would constitute another category of something burned fully on the Mizbeach. The Mishnah, read as covering all cases in the world of Korbanos rather than being limited to the context of Minchos alone, excludes this position. Kamashma Lan D'le-shitin Azlei — only the items enumerated in the Mishnah go fully on the Mizbeach; donated wine is not one of them. It goes into the Shitin, like all wine of Nesachim.

### R. Eliezer Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמִּתְנַדֵּב שֶׁמֶן – קוֹמְצוֹ, וּשְׁיָרָיו נֶאֱכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: With regard to another ruling, the mishna **supports** a different statement **of Shmuel. As Shmuel said: One who donates oil** to the Temple **removes a handful** and sacrifices it on the altar, **and its remainder is eaten** by the priests. Shmuel's ruling is in accordance with the mishna, which does not list a donation of oil as one of the offerings given completely to the altar.

Having excluded one position of Shmuel, the Gemara notes that the Mishnah also supports a different position of his. *misayei lei l'shmuel* D'amar Shmuel Ha-minadev Shemen Komtzo Ve-shiyaro Ne'echalin — Shmuel holds that if one donates oil to the Mizbeach, one performs a Kmitza on the oil and the remainder is eaten by the Kohanim, treating donated oil like a Mincha. If the halakha were that donated oil is burned entirely on the Mizbeach, then it would constitute an additional case of something fully going to the Mizbeach, and the Mishnah's enumeration would be incomplete. Since the Mishnah presents its list as exhaustive — at least within the domain of edible Kodshim — and donated oil is not on the list, this supports Shmuel's position that donated oil undergoes a Kmitza-like process and the remainder goes to the Kohanim.

The entire phenomenon is remarkable: the Torah lists specific Korbanos one may bring, and the intuition is that one cannot simply ad-lib an offering of shemen. Yet the sugya establishes not only that one can donate oil, but that such a donation is treated as a quasi-Mincha, with a Kmitza performed on oil — a striking and unusual procedure.

### Abaye/Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים – נֶאֱכָלִין לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְאֵין בָּהֶם לַמִּזְבֵּחַ. וּבָזֶה יִפָּה כֹּחַ הַכֹּהֲנִים מִכֹּחַ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA (continued): **The two loaves,** i.e., the public offering on Shavuot of two loaves baked from new wheat, **and the shewbread,** i.e., the twelve loaves that were placed on the sacred Table in the Sanctuary each Shabbat, **are eaten by the priests, and there is no part of them** burned **on the altar. And in** the case of those offerings, **the power of the priests is greater than the power of the altar.**

The Mishnah then presents the contrasting category: *shtei halechem v'lechem hapanim* Ne'echalin L'kohanim *v'ein bahem l'mizbeach* — the two loaves of Shavuot and the *lechem hapanim* of every Shabbos are eaten entirely by the Kohanim, and nothing from them goes to the Mizbeach. For the *shtei halechem*, the Mizbeach receives the blood of the *shenei kivshei atzeret* — the two lambs — but none of the bread itself, since it is Chametz and Chametz cannot be placed on the Mizbeach. For the *lechem hapanim*, the Mizbeach receives the Levona on the Shulchan, but none of the bread itself. U'vaze Yafe Ko'ach Hakohanim Mikoach Hamizbeach — in this case the Kohanim have the greater portion over the Mizbeach.

The Gemara asks: V'tu Leika? Are these the only cases where the Kohanim receive everything? V'ha'ika Chatas Ha'of — a bird Chatas is eaten entirely by the Kohanim, with no Eimurim going to the Mizbeach. The answer: Amar Ika Dama — the blood is placed on the Mizbeach, even if in a trivial amount. V'ha'ika Log Shemen Shel Metzora — the log of oil of the Metzora is largely consumed by the Kohanim, yet Ika Matnosav — there are sprinklings from that oil directed toward the *kodesh kodashim*, and additional applications to the ear, thumb, and toe of the Metzora, so the full amount does not go to the Kohen.

U'mai Bazu — why then does the Mishnah enumerate these as special cases? L'afukei Mi-man D'amar *shtei halechem* Haba'os Bifnei Atzman Li-sreifa Azlan — there is an opinion that if the *shenei kivshei atzeret*, the lambs that accompany the *shtei halechem*, cannot be brought, the loaves themselves are burned, since without the lambs there is no mechanism to offer the loaves and they cannot go on the Mizbeach as Chametz. The Mishnah excludes this view: Kamashma Lan D'bazu Yafeh Ko'ach Kohanim L'olam — in this category, the Kohanim always receive the *shtei halechem* fully, even in the absence of the lambs. What exactly constitutes the act of Hakrava in that case is not fully resolved, but the Mishnah's ruling is read to cover even that unusual circumstance, somewhat reminiscent of Bikkurim, where there is no burning on the Mizbeach — only a waving and declaration — and yet it is considered a valid act of Hakrava.

## R. Elazar Position…

### All to Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת הַנַּעֲשׂוֹת בִּכְלִי, טְעוּנוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁמֶן: יְצִיקָה, וּבְלִילָה, וּמַתַּן שֶׁמֶן בִּכְלִי קוֹדֵם לַעֲשִׂיָּיתָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All the meal offerings that are prepared in a vessel,** e.g., the offerings prepared in a pan or deep pan, **require three placements of oil,** listed here in the reverse order of their placement: **Pouring** oil on the cakes after they have been cooked, **and mixing** oil into the flour, **and placement of oil into the vessel prior to preparation** of the meal offerings.

The next Mishnah introduces a new topic: Kol Haminchot Hana'asot B'kli Te'unot *shalosh matanot shemen* — every *mincha made* in a vessel requires three applications of oil. The Mishnah lists them in reverse order: Yetzika, pouring or anointing; U'velila, mixing; and U'matan Shemen Bakli Kodem La'asiyatan, placing oil in the vessel before making it. Logically the sequence is: first you put oil in the vessel, then you mix the flour with the oil, then you drizzle oil on top — a sequence analogous to preparing a recipe, with the pan oiled first, the dough mixed, and the finished product dressed.

### All to Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלּוֹת בּוֹלְלָן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: סוֹלֶת. הַחַלּוֹת טְעוּנוֹת בְּלִילָה, וּרְקִיקִין – מְשִׁיחָה. כֵּיצַד מוֹשְׁחָן? כְּמִין כִּי, וְהַשְּׁאָר נֶאֱכָלִין לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the meal offerings that come as loaves, it is after the flour has been baked into **loaves** that one breaks them into pieces and **mixes them** with oil; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say:** It is with **fine flour,** before the baking, that one mixes the oil. Although **the loaves** of the meal offering baked in an oven **require mixing** of their flour with oil, **wafers** do not require mixing, but rather a **smearing** of oil on them after baking. **How does one smear** oil on **them?** He does so in a shape **similar** to the Greek letter **chi,** Χ, **and the rest** of the oil remaining after smearing is **eaten by priests.**

The Mishnah then addresses a distinction in how oil is applied to two types of baked offerings. *hachallos te'unot belila* — the Challos, referring here to the Challos of the Ma'afeh Tanur, the oven-baked Mincha described in the Torah as Challos Matzot Belulot Bashemen, require mixing with oil. V'harkikin Meshicha — the Rekikin, the wafers, described in the Torah as Rkikei Matzot Meshuchim Bashemen, require anointing with oil. *keitzad moshchan* — how does one anoint them? K'min Kai — in the shape of a Greek chi, that is, an X-shaped mark across the wafer.

The Mishnah adds: Bolelan — you mix them with oil. This is the position of Rabbi, who understands, as Rashi explains, that after the Challos are baked and hardened, you break them up and mix them again with oil. *vachachamim omrim* Solet — the Chachamim hold you mix with oil at the flour stage, before baking. This dispute concerns whether the second application of oil to the Challos occurs before or after baking — a question whose full elaboration is reserved for subsequent discussion.
