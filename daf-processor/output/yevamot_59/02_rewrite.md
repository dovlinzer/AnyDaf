# Yevamot 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Intro

### Kiddushin/Nissu'in Review

The discussion on this daf flows directly from the *sugya* that concluded the previous day's learning — namely, the case of a *kohen gadol* who betroths a woman when she is still a *ketannah* and the question of what happens if she becomes a *bogeret* before the marriage is consummated. The core issue was whether the governing moment is the *kiddushin* or the *nissu'in*. The *gemara* had attempted to resolve this from *pesukim* but ultimately concluded that she would need to be a *ketannah* at the time of *nissu'in* as well — though a different rule may apply in the parallel case where it is the man who changed status, having performed *kiddushin* as an ordinary *kohen* and subsequently becoming a *kohen gadol*. In that scenario, she might be assessed according to her state at the time of *kiddushin*.

### Kohen Gadol Restrictions

What this earlier discussion established is the existence of special *halachot* governing the marriage of a *kohen gadol* that go beyond those applying to an ordinary *kohen*. One of them is clear from the text of the *pasuk* — *ishah bi-vsuteha yikkach*, he shall take a woman in her virginity — namely, the *mitzvah* to marry a *besulah*. Less transparent from the *pesukim*, yet taken for granted by the *gemara*, is the requirement that she be a *ketannah* or at least a *na'arah* at the time of marriage, and not a *bogeret* — a woman who has reached full physical maturity, presumed to occur from the age of twelve and a half. Whether this requirement applies at the moment of *kiddushin* or *nissu'in* is itself a question the *gemara* has been wrestling with.

### Widow/Mature Prohibition

With that background in place, the *mishnah* addresses these issues directly. *Kohen gadol lo yissa almanah* — the *kohen gadol* may not marry a widow. This is explicit in the *Torah*. And the *mishnah* clarifies: *bein almanah min ha-erusin bein almanah min ha-nissu'in* — it makes no difference whether she is a widow from *erusin*, and therefore presumably still a virgin, or a widow from *nissu'in*. Either way, the *Torah*'s prohibition applies.

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-lo yissa bogeret* — he may not marry a *bogeret*. The three stages of female maturity bear repeating: a *ketannah* is under twelve years and one day; from twelve years and one day to twelve and a half she is a *na'arah*; from twelve and a half onward she is a *bogeret*, fully physically mature, and a *kohen gadol* may not marry her — though this is nowhere made explicit in the *pesukim*. To this, *Rabbi Elazar ve-Rabbi Shimon makhshirin bi-vogheret* — they declare a *bogeret* permissible.

The *mishnah* also records, in brackets whose precise *girsa* is uncertain, the further restriction of *ve-lo yissa mukat etz* — he may not marry a woman who has been, literally, smitten by wood. This refers to a woman whose hymen has been ruptured not through any act of intercourse but through some other physical activity — something having been inserted vaginally, or more commonly in practice, rupture resulting from general physical activity. Whether this clause is stated *divrei ha-kol* or attributed specifically to Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon is itself a textual question.

What this *mishnah* immediately sets in motion is a fundamental inquiry: what is the definition of virginity for these purposes? Is it about the state of the hymen, or is it about sexual experience? The two are not always identical. One can imagine an act of intercourse that does not rupture the hymen, and conversely, a rupturing of the hymen that has nothing to do with intercourse. The *gemara* turns itself to this question at once.

## Virginity Definition

### Three Maturity Stages

Before entering the debate between the *tanna'im*, the *gemara* first dispenses with the question of the *almanah*. The *baraita* cites the *pasuk almanah lo yikkach*, and one might have thought to limit this prohibition to a widow from *nissu'in* — on the model of Tamar, *shevi almanah beit avikh*, who had been in a full *nissu'in* relationship with the brothers and had therefore had marital relations. Perhaps all *almanot* should be read through that lens? No, the *gemara* responds. Because the *Torah* juxtaposes *almanah* with *gerushah* — *et eleh lo yikach* — and a *gerushah* has no such limiting precedent. A *gerushah* applies whether from *erusin* or *nissu'in*, and so the same is true of an *almanah*. The prohibition on marrying an *almanah* or a *gerushah* concerns their status, not the question of virginity; that concern is addressed by the separate *pasuk*, *ishah bi-vstuleha*.

Turning to the *bogeret*: the *gemara* cites the *baraita* — *v'hu ishah bi-vstuleha yikkach*. Why does the *Torah* use the unusual phrase *bi-vstuleha* rather than simply *besulah yikkach*? The extra formulation, according to *Rabbi Meir*, comes to exclude the *bogeret* — *prat le-bogeret she-kalu lah betuleha* — she whose *betulim* have dissipated. *Tosafot* explains, and the *gemara* itself makes clear, that this does not mean the hymen has entirely disappeared, but that it has somehow thinned out or is no longer in its original state. The anatomical question of whether the hymen actually changes during puberty is genuinely complex — contemporary medical professionals tend to resist the binary framing of "there or not there," since perforation is a matter of degree even in younger women. Nevertheless, the *gemara*'s assumption is that by the completion of puberty, the hymen is in a meaningfully different state than it was before.

### Rabbi Meir Hymen View

Rabbi Meir's position is anchored entirely in the physical reality of the hymen. Had the *Torah* simply said *besulah yikkach*, *besulah* would be an inclusive term — *afilu miktzat betulim mashma* — even if there is only some portion of the hymen present, she would qualify. The word *bi-vstuleha*, with its intensifying formulation, demands something more — *ad de-ika kol betulim* — the hymen must be in its complete, unchanged state, as it exists prior to puberty.

And then there is the second dimension of the word *bi-vstuleha*. If the *bet* prefix comes to narrow the category — demanding that the hymen be entirely intact — it also simultaneously comes to broaden it in another direction. Specifically, *kedarkah ve-lo shelo kedarkah*: the extra *bet* of *bi-vstuleha* teaches that the concern applies only to vaginal intercourse. Anal intercourse is not a problem. This is quite striking, but within Rabbi Meir's framework it is perfectly coherent. His entire focus is the anatomical state of the hymen — not the question of sexual experience. If a woman has had anal sex, that act has no impact on the hymen, and she remains, in his view, a *besulah*. Conversely, she could be a *bogeret* whose hymen has changed simply through the process of maturation, and she would be disqualified. The criterion is purely physical: the state of the hymen.

The implications follow inexorably. *Mukat etz* is a problem — something has physically ruptured the hymen, even without intercourse. Anal sex is not a problem — there is no impact on the hymen. And since the only criterion is anatomical, the restriction is extremely narrow in one direction and extremely demanding in another: she must be a girl who has not yet matured, whose hymen is entirely intact, and in whom nothing whatsoever has caused any physical rupture. But she could, in principle, have had sexual relations — so long as they had no physical impact on the hymen.

### Eliezer/Shimon Sex View

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon reason in precisely the opposite direction. Had the *Torah* said simply *besulah*, that alone would imply — *besulah sheleimah mashma* — a complete, intact hymen. The word *bi-vstuleha*, with its expanded formulation, moves in the opposite direction: *afilu miktzat betulim* — even if the hymen is not in its original state, even if she is a *bogeret*, she still qualifies. Their concern is not with the precise anatomical condition of the hymen, but with whether the woman has had sexual intercourse.

But then *bi-vstuleha* must be doing something in the restrictive direction as well. If it is broadening the definition of *besulah* to include even a *bogeret*, it simultaneously narrows it by establishing that *bein kedarkah bein shelo kedarkah* — both vaginal and anal intercourse disqualify her. This is equally logical from their perspective: since the criterion is about whether she has had sex, not about the hymen, the act of anal intercourse renders her a non-virgin, even if it has had no impact on the hymen whatsoever. The question of *mukat etz* then becomes more nuanced. If the *mishnah*'s *mukat etz* prohibition is attributed to Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, they would be operating with two distinct criteria simultaneously: one relating to the hymen having been physically compromised, and one relating to whether she has had sex. Since both criteria exist, neither needs to be taken to an extreme. The hymen criterion does not need to demand pristine anatomical perfection — that would be Rabbi Meir's extreme. And there is still a category in which a woman is not a *besulah* even without sexual intercourse, namely *mukat etz*.

The structure of the debate can be stated simply: Rabbi Meir holds it is entirely about the hymen, not about sex. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon hold it is about sex — and either entirely so, or about sex combined with a more moderate standard regarding the hymen — but in any event not about the hymen alone.

## Anal Sex Debate

### Rav Yehudah Anal Sex

*Amar Rav Yehudah amar Rav: niv'alah shelo kedarkah pesulah li-kehunah*. Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav rules that a woman who has had anal intercourse is disqualified for marriage to a *kohen* — which in context means specifically a *kohen gadol*. This is precisely the position one would intuit: of course a woman who has had intercourse, even anal, is no longer a virgin in any meaningful sense. What the *gemara* has already shown is that according to Rabbi Meir, the opposite is true — since Rabbi Meir focuses exclusively on the hymen. Rav's ruling, then, reflects the position of Rabbi Eliezer, and the *gemara* will shortly say as much: *ve-Rav de-amar ke-Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Rava's Challenge

*Mativ Rava*. Rava raises a challenge from the *baraita* dealing with the rapist's obligation to marry his victim: *ve-lo tihyeh le-isha* — she shall be his wife — applies only to a woman who is *ra'uyah lo*, halachically fitting for him. *Prat le-almanah le-kohen gadol, u-gerushah ve-chalutzah le-kohen hedyot* — if a *kohen gadol* rapes an *almanah*, or an ordinary *kohen* rapes a *gerushah* or *chalutzah*, the obligation to marry does not apply, since these women are forbidden to them.

The *gemara* asks: how exactly did this *kohen gadol* rape this *almanah*? *Ileima kedarkah* — if it was vaginal intercourse — then why must the *baraita* invoke the fact that she is an *almanah* at all? *Teipokei lei mishum de-hava lei be'ulah* — before any question of her status as a widow, she is already a *be'ulah* by virtue of his own act. The act of rape itself, even though it was *be'ulat atzmo*, has made her a non-virgin, and therefore she is disqualified from marrying a *kohen gadol* on that ground alone. Why does the *baraita* need to say she is an *almanah*?

*Ela shelo kedarkah* — it must be that the *kohen gadol* raped her *shelo kedarkah*, anally. And in that case, according to Rav's ruling, she would still be a *be'ulah* — yet the *baraita* needs to find a separate ground for her disqualification, namely that she is an *almanah*. This demonstrates that *shelo kedarkah* does not make her a *be'ulah*. *Ha mani Rabbi Meir* — this *baraita* must be reflecting Rabbi Meir's position. *Ve-Rav de-amar ke-Rabbi Eliezer* — and Rav is ruling in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer.

But now the *gemara* presses further. If we are operating within Rabbi Eliezer's framework, *mai irya mishum be'ulah*? Why need we discuss the category of *be'ulah* and the specific restrictions of a *kohen gadol* at all? *Teipokei de-hava lei zonah* — there is a far more sweeping issue: a woman who has had intercourse outside of marriage is, according to Rabbi Eliezer, a *zonah*, and is forbidden even to an ordinary *kohen*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *penui ha-ba al ha-penuya asah zona* — a single man who has intercourse with a single woman outside the context of marriage renders her a *zonah*. So within Rabbi Eliezer's world, if the woman has had anal intercourse with some man outside of marriage, she is not only a *be'ulah* for purposes of the *kohen gadol*'s restriction; she is a *zonah* forbidden to every *kohen*. The question of *kohen gadol* and *be'ulah* is then superfluous.

### Bestiality Non-Sex

To resolve the difficulty, *Rav Yosef* offers a striking suggestion: *kegon she-niv'alah li-vehemah* — the case involves a woman who was penetrated by an animal. Through this, the *gemara* seeks to identify a scenario in which a woman has had an act of penetration — making her a *be'ulah* — but has not engaged in *zenut* in the ordinary sense, and is therefore not a *zonah* according to Rabbi Eliezer. The reasoning is that *zenut* is a category defined by human fornication. An act with an animal falls outside this category entirely — it is not considered sexual in the relevant halachic sense for purposes of *issur zona*, even as it might still bear on her status as a *be'ulah* by virtue of the physical act itself. Of course, this itself raises a question: if we say the act is not considered "sex" for purposes of *zona*, on what basis do we say it is considered sex for purposes of *be'ulah*? The two seem to rest on the same question of whether the act constitutes a sexual act.

### Abaye Objection

*Amar Abaye: mimman nafshach* — make up your mind. *Ee be'ulah havya, zonah nami havya* — if you consider an act of intercourse with an animal to make her a *be'ulah*, on the grounds that it is an act of sex, then it also makes her a *zonah*. *Ve-ee zonah lo havya, be'ulah nami lo havya* — and if it is not considered an act of sex for purposes of *zona* because the act with an animal is categorically different, then for the same reason it does not make her a *be'ulah* either. Since we are no longer discussing the hymen — the *gemara* is now firmly in the territory of anal intercourse, where the hymen is not the issue — the only question is whether this constitutes "sex." One cannot answer that question differently depending on which *issur* is under discussion. Abaye's objection is accepted.

### Mukat Etz Response

Rava responds with the case of *mukat etz*: here is a woman who is not a *besulah* — the hymen is ruptured — yet she is certainly not a *zonah*. One might have thought the same logic could be applied to the case of *shelo kedarkah* — perhaps the act of anal intercourse or penetration by an animal is considered to have done something to the hymen, not to have constituted "sex," and on that physical basis she is disqualified, while remaining free of the *zona* status. But the *gemara* immediately forecloses this. *Im ken ein lach ishah she-kesheirah li-kehunah she-lo na'asis mukat etz al yedei tzeror* — if every physical intrusion into the body, even one that bears no relationship to sexual intercourse, were to disqualify, then wiping oneself with a stone — the ancient practice — would render every woman a *mukat etz*. This is clearly impossible.

Therefore, when the *gemara* discusses *shelo kedarkah*, it is not discussing something that physically ruptures the hymen. It is discussing an act of sex. And once it is an act of sex, Abaye's objection stands: you cannot say it is sex for purposes of *be'ulah* and not sex for purposes of *zona*. The two are the same question. The *gemara* accepts this conclusion, and the challenge of how to construct a case in which a woman is a *be'ulah* according to Rabbi Eliezer's framework without also being a *zonah* according to that same framework remains unresolved — until Rabbi Zeira offers his solution, which begins the next section.

## Me'ona Case

### Rabbi Zeira Solution

*Ella amar Rabbi Zeira: bi-mema'enet* — Rabbi Zeira's answer is that the case involves a woman who exercises *mi'un*, the right of refusal. Here is the scenario: a *ketannah*, a minor girl, was married off by her mother and brother through a rabbinic *kiddushin* — *kiddushei ketanah*. She lived with her husband and they had intercourse. However, the intercourse was *shelo kedarkah* only. Now she turns twelve, reaches majority, and exercises *mi'un* — she declares that she does not wish this marriage and dissolves it. Since the *kiddushin* was only rabbinic in the first place, and she exercises her right of refusal, the marriage is retroactively undone. She is not an *almanah* and not a *gerushah* — there is no legal aftermath of the marriage. She is, retroactively, as though she was never married.

Now examine her status. On the one hand, since she was in a marriage — even a rabbinic one — when the intercourse took place, it was not *bi'at zenut*. Even Rabbi Eliezer concedes that a legally and socially recognized marriage, even a rabbinic one, removes the act from the category of fornication. She is therefore not a *zonah*. On the other hand, since the marriage has been retroactively dissolved, she is regarded as never having been married, and she may marry a regular *kohen*. Yet she has had intercourse *shelo kedarkah*, and according to Rav's ruling in the name of Rav Yehudah, that makes her a *be'ulah*. She therefore cannot marry a *kohen gadol*, even though she is not a *zonah*. This is precisely the case needed: a *be'ulah* who is not a *zonah*, within Rabbi Eliezer's framework.

### Retroactive Marriage

There is a celebrated observation of Rav Chaim regarding this case of *mi'un* that deserves careful attention, because it reveals a conceptual tension at the heart of the solution. The *mah nafshach* presses itself: if the marriage is retroactively undone, then at the time of intercourse she was retroactively not married — in which case the sex was non-marital, and Rabbi Eliezer should declare her a *zonah* after all. Conversely, if we say the sex was marital, should she not be a *gerushah* of some rabbinic variety, rather than being fully available to an ordinary *kohen*?

Rav Chaim resolves this by articulating the principle of *mikan u-le-haba le-mafrei'a*: moving forward, retroactively she was never married; for things that already occurred, we treat them as having happened within the context of marriage. In other words, the retroactivity operates in different directions depending on the question being asked. For any new question that arises — her status going forward — we say she was never married. For events that already occurred — the sexual acts that took place during the relationship — we say those happened within a marriage. The marriage exists yesterday but not tomorrow. This allows the *be'ulah* status and the absence of *zona* status to coexist without contradiction.

### Agunah Application

This *gemara* has considerable relevance to contemporary discussions about solutions to the *agunah* problem, particularly proposals involving *hafka'at kiddushin* or *kiddushin al tenai* — conditional marriage. One argument raised against such proposals is the principle *ein adam oseh be'ilato bi'at zenut* — no one would want the *kiddushin* to be conditional, because if the condition retroactively dissolves the marriage, all the intercourse that occurred within it is retroactively rendered non-marital fornication. This argument draws on the idea that in the *gemara*'s world, the only institution defining the relationship was the religious institution of marriage. If that is undone, nothing remains.

A counter-argument, however, emerges from the case of *mema'enet*. Even a rabbinic *kiddushin* — *kiddushei ketanah*, arranged by the mother and brother — is sufficient to define the relationship as non-fornication, *de-oraita*, for purposes of the *zona* prohibition. In the contemporary context, even where a *halachic* marriage is retroactively dissolved, a civil marriage recognized legally and communally remains in place. One might argue, building on the logic of *mema'enet*, that this civil recognition is sufficient to prevent the retroactive dissolution from rendering past intercourse as *bi'at zenut* — since the relationship was embedded in a recognized legal and social structure, even if not a *halachic* one. This is, to be clear, a complex and contested question, but the *sugya* of *mema'enet* is a genuine and relevant data point in that conversation.

## Bestiality Evidence

### Bestiality Permitted

*Amar Rav Shimi bar Chiya: niv'alah li-vehemah kesheirah li-kehunah*. Having established that *bi'ah shelo kedarkah* — notwithstanding its non-impact on the hymen — nonetheless renders a woman a *be'ulah* and disqualifies her from marrying a *kohen gadol*, the *gemara* now returns to the case of bestiality. Rav Shimi bar Chiya declares that a woman who has been penetrated by an animal is *kesheirah li-kehunah* — fit to marry a *kohen gadol*. This is the ruling that was anticipated in the earlier exchange: since an act with an animal is categorically different from a sexual act in the halachic sense, it neither renders her a *be'ulah* nor a *zonah*. Rashi notes that the discussion here presumes *kedarkah*, vaginal penetration, which would normally rupture the hymen — and this ruling therefore implies that the position regarding *mukat etz* is lenient, or that the case is *shelo kedarkah*. Either way, the act is not considered an act of sex.

### Tannaitic Non-Human

*Tanya nami hachi* — a *baraita* teaches similarly: *niv'alah le-mi she-eino ish* — if a woman was penetrated by one who is not a man, *af al pi she-bi-sekilah* — even though the offense carries the death penalty of stoning, *kesheirah li-kehunah* — she is fit for *kehunah*. The severity of the prohibition does not transform the act into an act of sex for these purposes. It remains categorically sui generis, and therefore does not affect her status with regard to virginity or the *zona* prohibition. The question is raised whether the phrase *she-eino ish* is a euphemism for an animal, or could it encompass other scenarios — such as an act with a minor, whose sexual act may or may not be considered a full *bi'ah* depending on age. The *gemara* understands the *baraita* to be speaking by way of euphemism, with an animal as the intended referent.

### Heitlu Story

When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael* he brought the following incident: *ma'aseh be-na'arah achat be-Heitlu* — a story about a young woman in a place called Heitlu, who was cleaning herself, *she-hayetah mekabedet et ha-bayit* — this is understood as a euphemism for wiping herself — and a wild Cyprian dog attacked her from behind and penetrated her, *va-hikhshirah Rebbe li-kehunah* — and Rebbe ruled that she was fit to marry a *kohen gadol*. Since the act was committed by an animal, it does not constitute an act of intercourse in the halachic sense, and she retains her eligibility. Whether the penetration was vaginal or anal is a question the *gemara* leaves open; in either case, Rebbe's ruling stands.

The *gemara* then asks: *le-kohen gadol mi hava* — was there actually a *kohen gadol* in Rebbe's time to whom this would be practically relevant? *Ela re'uyah le-kohen gadol* — the meaning is that she is theoretically fit for a *kohen gadol*, even if the ruling had no immediate practical application.

### Esnan Zonah Derivation

*Amar lei Rava mi-Parquin le-Rav Ashi: heicha amur Rabbanan ein zenut li-vehemah* — where do the Rabbis derive that bestiality does not constitute *zenut*? The source is the *pasuk*: *lo tavi esnan zonah u-mechir kelev beit Hashem elokecha* — do not bring a gift of a prostitute or the purchase price of a dog into the House of God. The *halacha* derived from the first clause is that a sheep given to a prostitute as payment for her services cannot be brought as a *korban*. And *mechir kelev* — whether read in its plain sense as the price paid for a dog, or understood by the Sages quite literally as the price paid to engage in an act with a dog — teaches that if a sheep is exchanged for an act of bestiality, that sheep *is* permitted for use as a *korban*.

This is established in the *mishnah*: *esnan kelev u-mechir zonah mutarin* — the payment given for a dog and the payment received for a *zonah* are permissible. *She-ne'emar gam sheneihem* — the *Torah* says "the two of them," meaning these two specific things, *shenayim ve-lo arba* — two, not four. The exclusion of the price paid for bestiality demonstrates that the act is not categorized as *zenut* at all. Were it considered *zenut*, the sheep would be forbidden as *esnan zonah*. Since it is not forbidden, the *Torah* is implicitly declaring that the act falls entirely outside the category of sexual fornication. The *gemara* acknowledges one could make other distinctions — perhaps the exclusion is for some different reason — but the *gemara* reads the exclusion as reflecting the categorical distinction: bestiality is simply not the same type of act as human intercourse. Calling it something else may be unavoidable, but placing it within the same legal category as *zenut* cannot be done.

## Aseh Violation Chalal

### Rape/Seduction Debate

The *mishnah* and the *gemara* now turn to the consequences of a *kohen gadol*'s violations of the *mitzvat aseh* of *ishah bi-vstuleha yikkach*. The *baraita* states: *anusas atzmo u-mefutas atzmo* — if a *kohen gadol* raped or seduced a woman, she has now become a *be'ulat atzmo*, a woman whose loss of virgin status is owing to his own prior act. He is forbidden to marry her — he violates the *mitzvat aseh* of marrying a virgin — but *im nasa nasa*: if he did marry her, the marriage stands.

The *baraita* then distinguishes this case from that of *anusas chaveiro u-mefutas chaveiro* — a woman who was raped or seduced by another man. Here too the prohibition applies, since she is no longer a *besulah*, and the *mitzvat aseh* is violated if he marries her. But the legal consequence is sharper: *ve-im nasa*, if he did marry her, *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov omer ha-vlad chalal* — the child is a *chalal*, stripped of *kedushat kehunah*. *Ve-chachamim omrim ha-vlad kasher* — the Sages say the child is entirely valid. This is a debate about whether a child conceived through a violation of an *aseh* — as opposed to a *lav* — in the laws of *kehunah* has the status of a *chalal*.

### Marriage Divorce

Before proceeding to the *chalal* debate, the *gemara* addresses the meaning of *im nasa nasa* in the case of *be'ulat atzmo*. *Amar Rav Huna amar Rav: u-motzi be-get* — yes, the marriage stands, but Rav adds: and he must give her a *get*. The *gemara* notes the apparent redundancy: what *chiddush* is there in saying the marriage is legally binding? Even when a *kohen* violates a *lav* — marrying a *gerushah*, for instance — the marriage is binding *bedi'avad* and requires a *get*. Why single this case out?

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov*: the *chiddush* lies in the law of *knas*. When a man seduces a woman, he either marries her or pays the penalty fine. If he chooses to marry her, the marriage exempts him from paying. In a case involving a *lav* violation, such a marriage would not be considered a licit marriage for this purpose, and would not exempt from the *knas*. Here, since the violation is only of an *aseh*, the marriage does exempt from the *knas*. That is the substance of *im nasa nasa* — the marriage is legally operative and counts. Nevertheless, rabbinically, the situation cannot be permitted to continue. The *get* must be given.

### Toleration Problem

The *gemara* then cites a tradition in the name of Rav and Rabbi Yochanan: *bogeret u-mukat etz lo yissa, ve-im nasa nasa* — a *kohen gadol* should not marry a *bogeret* or a *mukat etz*, but if he did, the marriage stands. The *gemara* asks: if the principle is *im nasa nasa* and the marriage continues, why not say the same about *be'ulat atzmo*? Why not tolerate the situation *bedi'avad* in that case too?

The answer given is *sofa lihyot bogeret tachtav* and *sofa lihyot mukat etz tachtav*: in the cases of *bogeret* and *mukat etz*, the point of prohibition is the moment of marriage, not the ongoing state of being married to a non-*besulah*. After all, every woman a *kohen gadol* marries will eventually not be a *besulah*, and the *Torah* does not prohibit that ongoing state. Therefore, *bedi'avad*, we can tolerate the situation. But in the case of *be'ulat atzmo*, the problem traces back to general inappropriate behavior — a *choteh niskar* concern — and therefore the situation cannot be rabinically sanctioned even after the fact. The ongoing presence of the *issur* is a factor at the societal level, even if the question of whether he transgresses *biblically* on an ongoing basis is more complex.

One might ask: does the *kohen gadol* transgress the *aseh* only at the initial moment of marriage, or does he continue to transgress throughout the marriage? The *gemara*'s framing suggests the transgression is concentrated in the initial act, since any woman becomes a *be'ulah* after that first act — yet Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's position, discussed next, implies the violation continues. This remains an open and significant question in the *issurei kehunah* framework.

### Chalal from Aseh

The *baraita* states the dispute: Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov says the child of such a union is a *chalal*; the Sages say the child is valid. The *gemara* notes that this applies to the case of *anusas chaveiro u-mefutas chaveiro* — the woman made a *be'ulah* by another man — and the child could be conceived well into the marriage, long after the first act. Since Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov holds the child is a *chalal* even then, it implies he views the *issur* as continuous throughout the marriage.

### Rav Ashi Chalal

One approach to explaining Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's ruling was proposed earlier: perhaps his position aligns with Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that premarital sex renders a woman a *zonah*, forbidden even to an ordinary *kohen*. If she is a *zonah*, the child's *chalal* status derives from the *zona* prohibition, not from the *aseh* of marrying a *besulah*. The *gemara* rejects this because Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's rulings are described as *kav ve-naki* — few but reliable — such that we rule by them. But Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rav explicitly rules that *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Eliezer on the *zona* issue. If Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's ruling depended on Rabbi Eliezer's position, we would be forced to accept Rabbi Eliezer's ruling as well. Since we do not, this explanation is rejected: *kashya*.

*Amar Rav Ashi*: the simpler explanation of the debate is that it concerns *yesh chalal mi-chayvei aseh* — whether a *chalal* can be produced by a violation of a *mitzvat aseh*, as opposed to a *lav*. *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov savar yesh chalal mi-chayvei aseh* — he holds that violating even an *aseh* produces a *chalal* child. *Ve-Rabbanan savri ein chalal mi-chayvei aseh* — the Sages hold that *chalal* status requires a *lav* violation.

The textual support for Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov is the *pasuk*: *almanah u-gerushah ve-chalala zona et eleh lo yikach ki im besulah me-amav yikach ishah, ve-lo yechalel zaro be-amav* — and he shall not desecrate his seed. Reading the two *pesukim* together, any violation — including the *aseh* of *ki im besulah yikach* — produces a *chalal*. The Sages counter that *eleh* — "these" — is a limiting term: the word *eleh* interrupts the series and marks off only the *lavin* as generating *chalal* status; the *aseh* of *ki im besulah* is in a separate category and does not produce *chalal*. Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, in response, reads *eleh* as doing something different: excluding a woman who is a *niddah* from generating *chalal* status. Even though violating the *issur karet* of *niddah* is a far weightier offense, since it is not a marital *issur kehunah* about status — it is temporary and situational — it does not produce a *chalal*. The word *eleh* carves out that exclusion, not the exclusion of *aseh* violations. The *gemara* notes that this position of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov leaves a structural difficulty — *kashya* — that is left unresolved.

## Priestly Tumah Sister

### Betrothed Sister Debate

The *gemara* now opens a new but closely related topic: the obligation of a *kohen* to become *tamei* for his virgin sister. The *Torah* states: *le-achoto ha-besulah ha-kerova elav asher lo hayetah le-ish* — for his sister the *besulah*, close to him, who has not been to a man. The *baraita* records a dispute: *Rabbi Meir ve-Rabbi Yehudah omrim metamei lah* — a *kohen* does become *tamei* for his sister even if she is *arusah*; *Rabbi Yosei ve-Rabbi Shimon omrim ein metamei lah* — he does not.

### Sex Excludes Tumah

On the question of *anusah u-mifutah* — a sister who has been raped or seduced and has had sexual intercourse — *divrei ha-kol ein metamei lah*: all authorities agree the *kohen* may not become *tamei* for her. The *pasuk*'s language is plain: *besulah asher lo hayetah le-ish*. Intercourse has occurred; she is no longer *besulah* in the relevant sense.

### Mukat Etz Tumah

The question of *mukat etz* in this context is whether a sister whose hymen has been ruptured without intercourse is still within the category of *besulah* for purposes of *tumah*. *U-mukat etz ein metamei lah divrei Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Shimon holds that the *kohen* does not become *tamei* for a *mukat etz* sister. *She-hayah Rabbi Shimon omer: re'uyah le-kohen gadol metamei lah; she-einah re'uyah le-kohen gadol ein metamei lah* — he links the criterion directly to the *kohen gadol*'s marriage eligibility standard. Since a *mukat etz* is unfit for a *kohen gadol*, she is also not in the category for *tumah* by an ordinary *kohen*.

### Bogeret Tumah

*U-vogeret metamei lah divrei kol adam* — a *bogeret* sister: the *kohen* does become *tamei* for her, and this is the unanimous position. This is striking, because Rabbi Meir himself — who ordinarily holds that a *bogeret* is no longer a *besulah* for purposes of the *kohen gadol*'s marriage — agrees here that the *kohen* becomes *tamei* for a *bogeret* sister.

### Standard Reconciliation

How can Rabbi Meir hold two different standards simultaneously — *bogeret* is not a *besulah* for marriage to a *kohen gadol*, yet she is a *besulah* for *tumah* by a *kohen* for his sister? The *gemara* addresses this directly. *Mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Meir ve-Rabbi Yehudah* — why do Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah say the *kohen* remains *metamei* even for an *arusah*? They interpret the *pesukim* as follows: *le-achoto ha-besulah — prat le-anusah u-mifutah*. *Besulah* excludes a woman who has had intercourse. One might then have thought to exclude *mukat etz* as well, since anatomically the hymen is compromised. But *talmud lomar asher lo hayetah le-ish* — the additional phrase *who has not been to a man* defines what *besulah* means in this context: *mi she-havayatah al yedei ish* — one whose rupture was caused by a man. Only that case is excluded. A *mukat etz*, whose rupture has no human sexual cause, is not excluded.

*Ha-kerovah elav — le-rabbos arusah*: the word *kerovah*, close to him, is interpreted as including even an *arusah*, since she is still within his familial orbit. *Eilav — le-rabbos et ha-bogeret*: the word *eilav*, to him, includes even a *bogeret*.

The *gemara* then asks a pointed question: why does the *baraita* need a separate *derasha* to include a *bogeret*, given that Rabbi Meir himself holds *besulah afilu miktzat betulim mashma* — *besulah* includes even one with a partial *hymen*? The answer is that without this *derasha*, one might have applied a *gezerah shavah* — learning *besulah besulah* from the *kohen gadol* context, where a *bogeret* is excluded — and imposed the same restriction here. Therefore the *derasha* is necessary to establish that the two contexts operate by different standards. The same Rabbi Meir who demands anatomical perfection of the hymen for the *kohen gadol*'s marriage, and for whom *mukat etz* is disqualifying in that context, rules here — in the context of a *kohen*'s *tumah* for his sister — entirely by the criterion of sex, not by the state of the hymen. In this context, *bogeret* poses no problem, *mukat etz* poses no problem, and *arusah* poses no problem: the only disqualifying factor is intercourse. The two domains, while sharing terminology, operate by entirely distinct principles.

The discussion of Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon's opposing position — that an *arusah* is excluded from the *tumah* obligation — is introduced at the close of the *shiur*, where the *baraita*'s *derasha* of *le-achoto ha-besulah prat la-arusah* begins to be analyzed. That analysis will be taken up in the following day's learning.
