# Pesachim 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamei Grapes & Sanctity

### Tamei Grapes Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַעֲלָה. הָכָא נָמֵי — מַעֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this is merely **a higher standard,** as by Torah law the water may be drawn with one vessel and sanctified in another vessel. The Sages added that this water must be sanctified in the same vessel in which it was drawn from the spring, and they found support for their opinion in the Torah. **Here too,** the Sages established **a higher standard** for a consecrated object that became ritually impure, as they decreed that it cannot be ritually purified through insertion in the ground.

Our discussion picks up at the very bottom of 34b, where we need a few lines to wrap up the previous sugya before proceeding to the new chapter. By way of reminder: the Gemara had been building toward a statement of Rabbi Yochanan that if one has *tamei* grapes, it is possible to extract the grape juice in a way that does not render the juice *tamei* — by pressing less than a *kebeitzah* at any given moment, so that at no point does sufficient mass exist to transfer *tumah* to the liquid. An entire daf had been devoted to the buildup toward that ruling.

The other central discussion of 34b concerned a Mishnah teaching that if one plants *tamei terumah*, the *tumah* departs but the *terumah* status remains problematic. The difficulty is obvious: if the *tumah* is gone, why should the *terumah* remain an issue? The Gemara offered several answers, ultimately settling on a teaching attributed to Reish Lakish — or presented as something known in Eretz Yisrael but not in Bavel — that we maintain different standards for holy items and non-holy items. For non-holy items, the principle of *zreia* applies: plant it in the ground and the *tumah* departs, or immerse it in a *mikveh* (the same conceptual language is used), and similarly the *tumah* is removed. But if the item was holy and became *tamei*, this "trick" does not apply. Hence, *terumah*, which possesses sanctity, does not shed its problematic status merely through planting, even though the *tumah* technically departs for other purposes.

### Zreia & Mikveh Tricks

This same distinction — that certain purification mechanisms do not apply to sanctified items — was stated regarding water designated for *nisuch hamayim*: once such water became *tamei* after being sanctified, it cannot be immersed in a *mikveh* to become *tahor*. The principle, then, is that the "tricks" which work for ordinary *chulin* do not function for items possessing sanctity.

### Nisuch Hamayim Status

The question now before the Gemara is whether this elevated status — which prevents the use of such purification mechanisms — applies only to items at the highest level of *kedushah*, such as water that has been placed into a *kli sharet*, or whether it extends even to items that have been sanctified verbally but not yet placed in a *kli sharet*, which represents a lower level of *kedushah*.

### Oral vs Vessel Sanctity

The Gemara pursues this question by examining what exactly was sanctified and at what level. Items designated for *korbanot* that have been sanctified verbally but not yet placed in a *kli sharet* have not attained their full level of *kedushah*. Would we still say that even such items are treated with the special stringency that bars these purification tricks?

### Rabbi Yochanan Grapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ, דְּרָכָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁן — טְהוֹרִים. הִקְדִּישָׁן וְאַחַר כָּךְ דְּרָכָן — טְמֵאִין. וְהָא עֲנָבִים דִּקְדוּשַּׁת פֶּה נִינְהוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **grapes that became ritually impure, if one trod on them and afterward consecrated them, they are pure.** According to this opinion, the wine inside the grape does not become impure from the grape itself. However, if **he consecrated** the grapes **and afterward pressed them, they are impure,** because the *halakha* is especially stringent with regard consecrated items. **And yet** with regard to **grapes which are** only **consecrated through speech,** as the wine/grapes offered on the altar are not brought in a sacred vessel, **even so, the Sages imposed a higher** standard such that these grapes become impure after they have been consecrated.

To address this, the Gemara introduces a statement of Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: if grapes that were *tamei* were pressed *first* and then sanctified, the resulting juice is *tahor*. But if they were sanctified first and then pressed, the juice is *tamei*. The Gemara notes that these grapes must be *kedushat peh* — sanctified only verbally, not yet through a *kli sharet* — and yet the Rabbis maintained the stringency. This would suggest that even oral sanctification suffices to trigger the special restrictions.

However, Rav Yosef objects: when Rabbi Yochanan spoke of grapes that were sanctified, he was not referring to grapes designated for altar wine. Rather, he was referring to grapes of *terumah* — and by *terumah*, the only mode of sanctification is verbal, which is already the *highest* available level of *kedushah* for *terumah*. Hence we do not allow tricks to undo *tumah* once *terumah* has been sanctified. But for items designated for the altar that have not yet been placed in a *kli sharet* — perhaps even there we would not extend the stringency. The question thus remains unresolved at that level.

### Kebeitzah & Tumah Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרָכָן — וַאֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא?! וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ — דּוֹרְכָן פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement with regard to wine pressed from impure grapes: The phrase **if one tread upon them** is stated without qualification, indicating that the wine is ritually pure **even** if he pressed **many** grapes at once. **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this? Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** With regard to **grapes that became ritually impure grapes, one** should **tread on them less than an egg-bulk at a time.** When there is less than an egg-bulk of grapes, they do not impart ritual impurity.

The Gemara then turns back to Rabbi Yochanan's statement that one may press grapes with the juice remaining *tahor*, and asks: did he not limit this to doing less than a *kebeitzah* at any moment? How can the earlier statement speak without such a qualification? The Gemara offers two resolutions. The first: here too the rule is that one must press less than a *kebeitzah* at any time. The second resolution is more illuminating: when Rabbi Yochanan said that one must press less than a *kebeitzah* at a time, that was addressing a situation where the grapes had touched a *rishon* and were therefore a *sheni*. A *sheni* that contacts liquid elevates that liquid to *rishon* — hence the need for caution. But in the other statement, where the grapes had touched only a *sheni* and were therefore a *shlishi*, if those grapes are unsanctified *chulin*, the liquid they produce cannot be elevated beyond a *sheni* even in the worst case, and in fact non-sanctified food cannot become a *shlishi* at all. So the concern about the juice is entirely absent, and the distinction between pressing before and after sanctification has nothing to do with the *kebeitzah* trick — rather, it turns on the much simpler point that unsanctified *shlishi* grapes cannot render liquid problematic at all, whereas once sanctified, by *kodshim* one can go all the way to *revi'i*, so the entire calculus changes.

### Rava's Comparisons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: ״וְנָתַן עָלָיו מַיִם חַיִּים אֶל כֶּלִי״ — שֶׁתְּהֵא חִיּוּתָן בִּכְלִי. ״וְנָתַן״, אַלְמָא תְּלוּשִׁין נִינְהוּ. וְהָא מְחוּבָּרִין נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We, too, have learned** that the Sages created higher standards with regard to consecrated items. As it was taught that the verse says with regard to the red heifer: "And for the impure they shall take the ashes of the burning of the sin-offering, **and he shall put flowing water into a vessel"** (Numbers 19:17), which teaches that the flowing water from the spring **should flow** directly **into the vessel** in which it will be sanctified. On the other hand, the verse says **"and he shall put,"** meaning that the water should be poured into the vessel. **Apparently** the water **is detached, but it is** clearly **attached** to the spring, as it was previously stated that the water must flow directly into the vessel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: טָבַל וְעָלָה — אוֹכֵל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. הֶעֱרִיב שִׁמְשׁוֹ — אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה. בִּתְרוּמָה אִין, בְּקָדָשִׁים לָא. אַמַּאי, טָהוֹר הוּא? אֶלָּא מַעֲלָה. הָכִי נָמֵי — מַעֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said: We, too, learned** a similar statement in a *baraita*: When the period of ritual impurity of a *zav* or leper has been completed, and he **immersed** during the day **and emerged,** he may **eat tithes** immediately. Once **the sun set for him, he** may **eat *teruma*.** Rav Shimi bar Ashi infers from this statement: **With regard to *teruma*, yes,** he may eat it; however, **with regard to consecrated** food, **no,** he may not. Given that conclusion, Rav Shimi bar Ashi asks: **Why** is there a difference between *teruma* and consecrated foods? After all, **he is ritually pure. Rather,** the Sages established a **higher standard** for consecrated food, and permitted one to eat them only after sacrificing the requisite purification offering. **Here too,** the Sages established **a higher standard** for the ritual purity of consecrated liquids.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר״, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָעֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה. עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה בְּנֵי אִיטַּמּוֹיֵי נִינְהוּ?! אֶלָּא מַעֲלָה. הָכָא נָמֵי — מַעֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We, too, learned** another case where the Sages established a higher standard. The verse says: "And the flesh that touches any ritually impure thing shall not be eaten; it shall be burnt with fire. And as for the flesh, anyone who is ritually pure may eat from it" (Leviticus 7:19). **And the flesh** comes **to include the wood** in the Temple **and the frankincense.** The Gemara asks: **Do wood and frankincense** have the **capacity to become ritually impure,** given that they are neither food nor drink? **Rather,** the Sages established **a higher standard** for these objects due to their sanctity; **here too,** the Sages established **a higher standard** for the ritual purity of consecrated liquids.

Rava then offers a series of comparisons to demonstrate that we regularly find the Torah imposing heightened standards on *kodesh* items — and that this principle therefore supports the ruling that such purification tricks are unavailable once an item is sanctified.

His first comparison involves the *parah adumah*: the verse states that live spring water must be placed into the vessel (*el keli*), indicating that the water must be drawn directly into the mixing vessel. Yet another verse uses language suggesting the water was already drawn and then placed. Rava resolves the tension by noting that the requirement to draw directly into the vessel is a *ma'alah* — a special stringency — imposed because of the item's sanctified character.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi adduces another comparison: one who immerses in a *mikveh* while in a state of *tumah* becomes *tahor* at nightfall for the purpose of *terumah*, but remains unfit to eat *kodshim* until the following morning and the bringing of a *korban*. Why, if one is already *tahor*? The answer again is *ma'alah* — the Torah imposes a stricter standard for *kodshim* than for *terumah*.

Rav Ashi adds yet another comparison: the verse's use of *ve-habasar* (and the meat) is expounded to include *etzim* and *levonah* — wood and frankincense — within the category of things that can receive *tumah* when sanctified, even though ordinarily *tumah* does not adhere to wood or incense. This is possible, the Gemara concludes, because *ma'alah* — *tumah* adheres more strongly to sanctified items.

These comparisons are admittedly loose. They range from the question of whether *zreia* removes *tumah* from *terumah*, to whether a *kebeitzah* trick works, to whether a *korban* is needed to complete *tahara*, to whether wood can receive *tumah*. The Gemara is drawing broad analogical support for the general proposition that *kodesh* items operate under heightened rules — and that this principle underlies the various restrictions under discussion. With that, the lengthy excursus on *tumah*, *terumah*, and the related topics that arose from a Mishnah discussing the eating of *chametz terumah* on Pesach comes to a close.

## Transition to Matzah

### Topic Shift

We now encounter a radical shift in the tractate. Until this point — for the first thirty-odd pages — the discussion has centered almost entirely on the *issur* of owning *chametz*: the obligation of *bedikah*, the prohibition of owning *chametz* from *chatzot* onward, the requirement to destroy *chametz* and by what method, the time from which eating is forbidden, and the status of *chametz she-avar alav ha-Pesach*. Even the discussions about a non-Jew who owns *chametz* and the permissibility of benefit after Pesach were all about ownership of *chametz*. The entire first section of the tractate has revolved around that theme.

Now we shift — and it is a very radical shift — to the positive *mitzvot* of Pesach: the obligations of *matzah* and *maror*. We will return to *chametz* later, but for now a new chapter begins.

### Mishnah Grains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח: בְּחִטִּים, בִּשְׂעוֹרִים, בְּכוּסְּמִין וּבְשִׁיפוֹן וּבְשִׁיבּוֹלֶת שׁוּעָל. וְיוֹצְאִין בִּדְמַאי וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁנִּטְּלָה תְּרוּמָתוֹ, וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁנִּפְדּוּ. וְהַכֹּהֲנִים בַּחַלָּה וּבַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **These are** the types of grain **with which a person fulfills his obligation** to eat *matza* **on the** first night of **Passover: With wheat, with barley, with spelt [*kusmin*], with rye [*shifon*], and with oats [*shibbolet shu'al*]. And one fulfills** his obligation by eating not only *matza* made from properly tithed grains, but even **with** *matza* made from **doubtfully tithed produce, and** *matza* made **with first tithe from which its *teruma* was** already **taken,** or **second tithe and consecrated food that were redeemed. And priests** may eat *matza* prepared **from *ḥalla*,** the portion of dough that is given to priests, **or with *teruma*,** as priests are permitted to eat these portions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא בְּטֶבֶל, וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁלֹּא נִטְּלָה תְּרוּמָתוֹ, וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדּוּ. חַלּוֹת הַתּוֹדָה וּרְקִיקֵי נָזִיר, עֲשָׂאָן לְעַצְמוֹ — אֵין יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן. עֲשָׂאָן לִמְכּוֹר בַּשּׁוּק — יוֹצְאִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** one may **not** fulfill one's obligation to eat *matza* made **with untithed produce, nor with** *matza* made from the **first tithe from which its *teruma* was not separated, nor with** *matza* made either from the **second tithe, nor** from **consecrated** grain **that was not redeemed.** With regard to one who prepared **loaves of** *matza* that are brought with **a thanks-offering,** or to the **wafers** brought **by a nazirite,** the Sages drew the following distinction: If **he prepared them for himself,** then he **does not fulfill** his obligation to eat *matza* **with them.** However, if **he prepared them to sell them in the market** to those who require these loaves or wafers, **one fulfills** the obligation to eat *matza* **with them.**

The Mishnah states: *Elu devarim she-adam yotzei bahen yedei chovato be-Pesach* — these are the things with which one fulfills his obligation on Pesach. With *chitim* (wheat), with *se'orim* (barley), with *kusmin*, with *shifon*, and with *shibolet shual*. One may also fulfill the obligation using *demai*, and with *maaser rishon* from which *terumah* has been taken, and with *maaser sheni* and *hekdesh* that have been redeemed. *Kohanim* may use *challah* and *terumah* for their *matzah*. One does not fulfill the obligation with *tevel*, with *maaser rishon* from which *terumah* has not been taken, or with *maaser sheni* and *hekdesh* that have not been properly redeemed.

## Five Grains Identified

### Five Species Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: כּוּסְּמִין — מִין חִיטִּין, שִׁיבּוֹלֶת שׁוּעָל וְשִׁיפוֹן — מִין שְׂעוֹרִין. כּוּסְּמִין — גּוּלְבָּא, שִׁיפוֹן — דִּישְׁרָא, שִׁיבּוֹלֶת שׁוּעָל — שֻׁבְילֵי תַעֲלָא. הָנֵי אִין, אוֹרֶז וְדוֹחַן — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara identifies the species mentioned in the mishna. One of the Sages **taught** in the *Tosefta*: **Spelt** is **a type of wheat,** while **oats [*shibbolet shu'al*] and rye [*shifon*]** are **a type of barley.** The Gemara translates the lesser-known species into the vernacular Aramaic: **Spelt** is called ***gulba*; rye** is ***dishra*;** and **oats** are ***shevilei ta'ala*.** The Gemara infers: **These** species, **yes,** they may be used for *matza*; however, **rice [*orez*] and millet [*doḥan*], no,** they may not be used.

The Gemara immediately explains the relationship among these five grains. *Kusmin*, it says, is *min chitin* — a type of wheat. *Shibolet shual* and *shifon* are *min se'orim* — types of barley. The Gemara also provides Aramaic equivalents: *kusmin* is called *gulba*, *shifon* is *dishra*, and *shibolet shual* is *shivile ta'ala* — literally "fox-paths," a direct rendering of the Hebrew *shibolet shual*, evoking the image of the rows along a fox's tail.

### Rashi Identification

The standard translations given for these three secondary grains, largely following Rashi, are: *kusmin* as spelt, *shifon* as rye, and *shibolet shual* as oats. These are the identifications that have shaped traditional practice and popular understanding for centuries.

However, a closer examination reveals that these identifications are not without difficulty.

### Felix's Identification

Dr. Yehuda Felix, the author of the comprehensive study of flora and fauna in the Mishnah, subjects these identifications to modern botanical analysis, with significantly different conclusions. Regarding *shifon*, which Rashi translates as rye (*sigla*), Dr. Felix notes that rye was unknown in the land of Israel in antiquity and therefore cannot be correct. He concludes that *shifon* is rather *Triticum spelta* — spelt — which resembles barley in many respects and has historically been used as an alternative to barley for animal feed, and which the Gemara itself characterizes as a type of barley.

That shifts the identification of *kusmin*, which Rashi had translated as spelt. Since spelt is now assigned to *shifon*, *kusmin* must be something else. Dr. Felix concludes that it is *kusemet* — emmer wheat — one of the earliest cultivated forms of wheat, well attested in the land of Israel from the First Temple period onward. The Gemara's characterization of *kusmin* as *min chitin* (a type of wheat) fits this identification very well.

That leaves *shibolet shual*. Rashi translates it as oats. Under Dr. Felix's system, since spelt and emmer wheat have already been assigned, and rye cannot be included because it was unknown in ancient Israel, *shibolet shual* is identified as a form of two-row barley.

The result is that according to Dr. Felix, the three secondary grains are emmer wheat, spelt, and two-row barley — and neither rye nor oats belongs on the list.

### Celiacs & Oats Issue

The practical stakes of this disagreement are significant, particularly regarding oats. Oats do not contain gluten — the protein that gives dough its elasticity, allows it to be kneaded and shaped, and is present in all the other grains on the list. The absence of gluten from oats provides a structural basis for Dr. Felix's conclusion that oats do not belong among the five grains: the common denominator of all five species appears to be the presence of gluten, which also underlies the capacity to produce genuine *chametz* in the sense of dough that rises and releases gas.

The consequence for practice is acute. People suffering from celiac disease who cannot tolerate gluten rely on oat-based *matzah* to fulfill their Pesach obligation, precisely because oats — following Rashi's identification — are included among the five grains. If Dr. Felix is correct and oats are not actually one of the five grains, celiacs would have no grain with which to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* and would be exempt altogether.

### Auerbach vs Elyashiv

Hebrew Wikipedia, under the entry on *chamisha minei dagan*, records a telling episode. Professor Felix relates that on the basis of his botanical identification, Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach began reciting *shehakol* — rather than *mezonot* — on his morning oatmeal, treating oats as a non-grain food much as one would treat rice or corn. He maintained this practice for two years. Rav Shlomo Elyashiv then objected sharply, arguing that one cannot overturn a Jewish custom stretching back to the time of the *Rishonim* simply on account of a single scholar's academic findings.

This episode crystallizes a fundamental tension in *halacha*: to what degree may scientific discovery revise legally canonized meanings? The Gemara's understanding of the Mishnah — even if it may not have been the Mishnah's original intent — becomes binding through the process of *psak*. *Halacha* operates within legal realities that do not automatically recalibrate in response to botanical or archaeological evidence, however compelling. There is also an eminently practical dimension: *paskening* that oats are a valid grain enables celiacs to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* on Pesach. The normative conclusion remains that oats are included among the five grains.

## Rice & Hamitz Defined

### Rice Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: אָמַר קְרָא ״לֹא תֹאכַל עָלָיו חָמֵץ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים תֹּאכַל עָלָיו מַצּוֹת״. דְּבָרִים הַבָּאִים לִידֵי חִימּוּץ — אָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּמַצָּה, יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין בָּאִים לִידֵי חִימּוּץ, אֶלָּא לִידֵי סִירְחוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that *matza* cannot be prepared from rice or millet, **derived? Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said, and likewise** a Sage **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught, and likewise** a Sage **of the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov taught** that **the verse states: "You shall eat no leavened bread with it; seven days you shall eat with it *matza*,** the bread of affliction" (Deuteronomy 16:3). This verse indicates that only with **substances which will come to** a state of **leavening, a person fulfills his obligation to** eat ***matza* with them,** provided he prevents them from becoming leavened. This **excludes these** foods, i.e., rice, millet, and similar grains, **which,** even if flour is prepared from them and water is added to their flour, **do not come to** a state of **leavening but to** a state of **decay [*siraḥon*].**

The Gemara states: *hani in, orez ve-dochen lo* — these five grains yes, but rice (*orez*) and millet (*dochen*) are not included. From where is this derived?

### Biblical Source

Rav Shimon ben Lakish cites a verse from Devarim, and the same teaching is attributed to the school of Rabbi Yishmael and the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. The verse reads: *lo tochal alav chametz, shivat yamim tochal alav matzot* — do not eat *chametz* on it; for seven days eat *matzot*. The exegetical principle is *devarim habaim lidei chimutz adam yotzei yedei chovato b'matzah* — only things that are capable of becoming *chametz* can serve as *matzah* when they are not allowed to ferment. Rice and millet are excluded because *she-ein ba'in lidei chimutz ela lidei sirchon* — they do not arrive at *chimutz* (fermentation/rising), but rather at *sirchon* (decomposition or spoilage).

### Chimutz Definition

This raises an immediate question: rice does ferment. Its sugars break down, and in fact rice vinegar exists as a product of that process. So what precisely does the Gemara mean when it says rice does not come to *chimutz*?

Commentators and scholars point to two possible distinctions. The first is gluten: the five grains contain gluten, which binds and elasticizes the dough, enabling it to be kneaded and shaped. Rice lacks gluten. The second — and perhaps more intuitive — explanation relates to what *chimutz* means from a practical, experiential standpoint: not simply the biochemical process of fermentation, but the visible leavening of dough — the release of gas, the puffing and rising that occurs when water is combined with flour. This is what *chametz* means to the lay observer, and this is what does not happen with rice. If you prepare a dough from rice flour and leave it on the table, you do not observe the same dramatic rising that occurs with wheat flour dough. That seems to be the line the Gemara is drawing. It does not mean that no chemical decomposition occurs in rice; it means that the characteristic *machmitz* phenomenon — the rising of dough — is not replicated.

This distinction also underlies the broader *Ashkenazi chumra* of *kitniyot*. While that is a separate discussion, it developed in part because rice and grains are easily confused (both can be milled into flour and formed into dough), and because rice sold in the market was sometimes adulterated with actual grain. That gave rise to the Ashkenazi practice of avoiding *kitniyot* — but the underlying *halacha* as established by the Gemara is that rice is not one of the five grains and does not produce genuine *chametz*.

### R. Yochanan Nuri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, דְּאָמַר: אוֹרֶז מִין דָּגָן הוּא, וְחַיָּיבִין עַל חִימּוּצוֹ כָּרֵת. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹסֵר בְּאוֹרֶז וְדוֹחַן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקָּרוֹב לְהַחְמִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, who said: Rice is** a full-fledged **type of grain, and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating it in **its leavened** state, like other types of grain. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri prohibits** eating cooked **rice and millet** on Passover, **due to** the fact **that they are close to being leavened.**

The Gemara notes that the Mishnah does not accord with the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who holds that *orez min dagan hu* — rice is a type of grain. According to him, one is liable to *karet* for eating leavened rice on Pesach, and one may fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* with rice. A *braita* confirms: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri forbids rice and millet dough that has not been baked immediately, because *she-karov l'hachmitz* — it is close to becoming *chametz*. He further rules that one who lets it become *chametz* is liable to *karet*, and if one bakes it in time, it can be used for *matzah*. The Mishnah, and normative *halacha*, do not follow this view.

### Karov vs Gamur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ ״שֶׁקָּרוֹב לְהַחְמִיץ״? דְּקָדֵים וּמַחְמִיץ, אוֹ דִילְמָא: ״קָרוֹב לְהַחְמִיץ״ — הָוֵי, חָמֵץ גָּמוּר — לָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars who were studying this issue. What is the meaning of the expression: **That it is close to becoming leavened?** Does this mean **that it will be leavened** even **quicker** than wheat or barley? **Or** does it **perhaps** mean that **it is** only **close to being leavened,** i.e., it resembles leavened bread, but **it is not full-fledged leavened bread.** In other words, perhaps Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri maintains that these grains cannot in fact become full-fledged leavened bread, and one is not punished with *karet* for eating them on Passover. However, he rules that one should nevertheless avoid eating them, due to their similarity to leavened bread.

The Gemara raises a further question about Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's use of the phrase *karov l'hachmitz* (close to fermenting). Does this mean that rice ferments even faster than regular flour — making it *more* stringent — or does it mean that rice comes close to *chametz* but does not reach the status of *chametz gamur* (full *chametz*), placing it instead in the category of *chametz nukshah* (incompletely leavened)? The *braita*'s explicit statement that one incurs *karet* and may use rice for *matzah* resolves the question: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri regards rice as producing full *chametz*, not merely a quasi-*chametz*. The phrase *karov l'hachmitz* means that it ferments quickly, not that it falls short of full fermentation.

## Fruit Juices

### Reish Lakish Liquids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עִיסָּה שֶׁנִּילּוֹשָׁה בְּיַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ — אֵין חַיָּיבִין עַל חִימּוּצָהּ כָּרֵת. יָתֵיב רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, וְיָתֵיב רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין וְקָא מְנַמְנֵם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Reish Lakish said:** With regard to **dough that was kneaded with wine, oil, or honey, one is not liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating it in **its leavened** state, since these liquids will not cause the dough to be leavened. **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, were sitting before Rav Idi bar Avin, and Rav Idi bar Avin was sitting and dozing** as his students conversed. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa: What is the reason of Reish Lakish,** who maintains that one is not liable to receive *karet* for this type of leavening?

We now arrive at another major category directly relevant to contemporary practice. Rabbah bar bar Chana cites Reish Lakish: *issa she-nilsha be-yayin u-ve-shemen u-ve-devash* — dough that was kneaded with wine, oil, and honey — that is, with *mei peiros* (fruit juices) rather than water — *ein chayavin al chimutza karet*. Even if such dough is left and allowed to sit, one does not incur *karet*, because it does not produce genuine *chametz*. The characterization *matzah ashira* (rich matzah) enters here as well.

### Matzah Capability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תֹאכַל עָלָיו חָמֵץ [וְגוֹ׳]״. דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּמַצָּה — חַיָּיבִין עַל חִימּוּצוֹ כָּרֵת. וְהָא, הוֹאִיל וְאֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהּ יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ, דְּהָוְיָא לַיהּ מַצָּה עֲשִׁירָה — אֵין חַיָּיבִין עַל חִימּוּצָהּ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa **said to him: The verse states: "You shall eat no leavened bread with it;** seven days you shall eat with it *matza*" (Deuteronomy 16:3). In light of the juxtaposition of leavened bread and *matza*, Reish Lakish compares these two types of bread: With regard to those **substances** through **which a person fulfills his obligation to** eat ***matza*, one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating them in their **leavened** state. **And** with regard to **this** dough, which was kneaded with wine, oil, or honey, **since a person does not fulfill his obligation** to eat *matza* **with it,** as **it is** called **rich,** i.e., enhanced, ***matza*, one is not liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating it in **its leavened** state on Passover. *Matza* is called the bread of affliction, or the poor man's bread, a description that does not apply to dough prepared with wine, oil, or honey.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua poses the question to Rav Pappa: what is Reish Lakish's reasoning? The answer offered initially draws on the verse *lo tochal alav chametz, shivat yamim tochal alav matzot*. The argument is framed in the reciprocal direction from the one used earlier: not only can only things capable of becoming *chametz* serve as *matzah*, but conversely, only things capable of serving as *matzah* become full *chametz* carrying the penalty of *karet*. In formula: *devarim she-adam yotzei yedei chovato ba-hem be-matzah, chayavin al chimutzan karet* — only things from which one can fulfill the *mitzva* of *matzah* carry the full penalty for *chametz*.

### Matzah Ashirah

Why can dough kneaded with fruit juices not serve as *matzah*? Because one of the defining criteria of *matzah* is *lechem oni* — the bread of poverty. A dough enriched with wine, oil, and honey is no longer poor person's bread; it is *matzah ashira*, rich matzah. Since it cannot fulfill the *mitzva* of *matzah*, the argument runs, its becoming leavened does not carry the full severity of *chametz*.

This is a striking argument. It implies that the extra criteria of *matzah* — criteria added on top of the basic requirement that it be made from fermentable grain — somehow retroactively affect whether the leavened version of that dough constitutes serious *chametz*. That is a counterintuitive claim.

### Dissolved Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: הִמְחָהוּ וּגְמָעוֹ, אִם חָמֵץ הוּא — עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְאִם מַצָּה הוּא — אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּאֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּמַצָּה, וְחַיָּיבִין עַל חִימּוּצוֹ כָּרֵת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, raised an objection to Rav Pappa:** We learned with regard to one who took a baked item, **dissolved it in water, and swallowed** this mixture on Passover: **If** the baked item **is leavened bread,** he is **punished with *karet*; and if it is *matza*, a person does not fulfill his obligation** to eat *matza* **on Passover** with this food. The reason one does not fulfill the obligation to eat *matza* is because swallowing in this manner is not considered an act of eating. **But here** this is a case **where a person does not fulfill his obligation to** eat ***matza*** with this dissolved food, **and** nevertheless **he is liable** to receive ***karet* for** eating it in **its leavened** state. This ruling conflicts with Rav Pappa's general principle.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua challenges this reasoning. He cites a case: *ha-mechahu u-gema'o* — if one takes *chametz* and dissolves it completely in water and then swallows it, one is still liable to *karet* for eating *chametz*. Yet this dissolved *chametz* cannot possibly fulfill the *mitzva* of eating *matzah* — there is no *akhila* (eating) that would count for the *mitzva*, and the Gemara elsewhere would likely apply the principle that one who dissolves *matzah* and swallows it does not fulfill *achilas matzah*. So here we have a case where the very same substance is full *chametz* with *karet*, and yet cannot serve as *matzah*. The logical framework — that only things capable of *matzah* can be full *chametz* — is thereby refuted.

### Mei Peiros Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּעַר בְּהוּ רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, אָמַר לְהוּ: דַּרְדְּקֵי, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִשּׁוּם דְּהָווּ לְהוּ מֵי פֵירוֹת, וּמֵי פֵירוֹת אֵין מַחְמִיצִין.
>
> **Translation:** At this point **Rav Idi bar Avin woke up,** due to their discussion, and **said to them: Children, this is the reasoning of Reish Lakish:** One is not liable to receive *karet* for eating dough kneaded with oil or honey, **because** oil and honey **are** considered **fruit juices,** **and fruit juice does not** cause dough to be **leavened.** Therefore, dough prepared with these liquids is not considered full-fledged leavened bread.

At this point, Rav Idi bar Avin, who had been dozing during the exchange, wakes up and addresses the two students: *dardekei* — children. The true reason for Reish Lakish's ruling, he explains, is altogether different: *mishum de-havei lei mei peiros, u-mei peiros ein machmitzin* — because the dough was kneaded with fruit juices, and fruit juices simply do not cause *chimutz*. The ruling has nothing to do with the logical reciprocity between *matzah* and *chametz*. It is a straightforward empirical-halakhic principle: *mei peiros* do not ferment dough in the manner that produces *chametz*. This appears to have at least partial empirical support: the presence of sugars and other compounds in fruit juices alters the fermentation process and may inhibit the gas-releasing, dough-rising phenomenon that characterizes *chimutz*.

### Rishonim Positions

The question then becomes: what precisely does *mei peiros ein machmitzin* mean? Reish Lakish said one is not liable to *karet* — which could mean either that the dough becomes only *chametz nukshah* (quasi-*chametz*) and not *chametz gamur*, or that it does not become *chametz* at all. There is contradictory evidence across various sugyas, which gave rise to two basic positions among the *Rishonim*.

One position holds that fruit juices without water produce at least quasi-*chametz* (*chametz nukshah*), and therefore one must still be careful — baking immediately avoids the problem, but if the dough sits, it becomes at least rabbinically problematic *chametz*, perhaps even more quickly than standard dough. A second, very prominent position — that of Tosafot — distinguishes between two scenarios: fruit juices with water added, and fruit juices without water. When no water is added, the dough simply does not become *chametz* at all, and one need not rush to bake it. When water is added alongside the fruit juice, the dough does become *chametz* — and possibly even faster than usual.

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

The *Shulchan Aruch* rules in accordance with the second position: dough kneaded with pure fruit juice and no water does not become *chametz*, there is no urgency to bake it, and it may be eaten on Pesach without restriction. This is analogous to the status of rice according to the *ikkar halacha* — permissible, though it cannot fulfill the *mitzva* of *matzah* because it does not meet the criterion of *lechem oni*.

The *Rama*, predictably given the broader *Ashkenazi* approach to Pesach stringencies — the same approach that produced the *kitniyot* practice — rules that the *minhag* is to be *machmir*: even without water added, fruit juices may produce quasi-*chametz*, and therefore the practice among *Ashkenazim* is to avoid dough made with fruit juices on Pesach except for the sake of a *choleh* (someone who is ill) or very young children.

### Egg Matzot

Eggs raise a separate but related question: some *Rishonim* suggested that eggs might be subject to stricter rules than other fruit juices, because egg-based dough becomes considerably thicker and therefore might be more prone to *chimutz*. The normative consensus, however, is to treat eggs exactly as other fruit juices. Egg *matzot* are therefore in the same category as *matzah ashira*: the *Shulchan Aruch*'s ruling would permit them, but the *Ashkenazi minhag* following the *Rama* restricts them to the sick and to very young children — and in practice, even baked egg *matzot* are avoided by *Ashkenazim* except in those circumstances.

The irony is worth noting. The entire question of *mei peiros ein machmitzin* concerns whether the dough, if left unbaked, becomes *chametz*. Once it is actually baked into *matzah*, that concern does not apply in the same way. In principle, baked egg *matzah* should be no more problematic than water-based *matzah*. And yet, just as plain corn is forbidden to *Ashkenazim* on Pesach while plain wheat is obviously not, the stringency has taken on a life of its own such that even baked egg *matzot* are avoided except by those with special need.

## Technical Cases

### Demai Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא בִּדְמַאי וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר וְכוּ׳. דְּמַאי הָא לָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ! כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי מַפְקַר לְנִכְסֵיהּ הָוֵי עָנִי וְאוֹכֵל דְּמַאי — הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns to the cases of the mishna. The mishna taught that one can **fulfill** his obligation to eat *matza* **with doubtfully tithed produce and** with produce **of** the **first tithe,** from which *teruma* has been separated. The Gemara asks: Can one really fulfill his obligation with **doubtfully tithed produce?** It is **not suitable for him** to eat. The Sages prohibited the eating of doubtfully tithed produce that has not been tithed. The Gemara answers: **Since, if one** so **desires** he may **renounce** all **his property,** thereby becoming a poor person who is permitted to eat doubtfully tithed produce, **now too it is suitable for him,** even though he did not declare his property ownerless. Consequently, if he used doubtfully tithed produce as *matza*, he has fulfilled the mitzva after the fact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: מַאֲכִילִין אֶת הָעֲנִיִּים דְּמַאי, וְאֶת אַכְסַנְיָא דְּמַאי. וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תָּנָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מַאֲכִילִין אֶת הָעֲנִיִּים דְּמַאי וְאֶת הָאַכְסַנְיָא דְּמַאי, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מַאֲכִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: **One** may **feed the poor doubtfully tithed produce, and** he may also feed Jewish **soldiers [*akhsanya*]** who lodge with him **doubtfully tithed produce. And Rav Huna said** that it **was taught** that **Beit Shammai say: One** may **neither feed the poor doubtfully tithed produce, nor** feed **soldiers doubtfully tithed produce. And Beit Hillel say: One** may **feed** these groups doubtfully tithed produce. This mishna indicates that doubtfully tithed produce is not prohibited to the same extent as entirely untithed produce, as in certain situations the prohibition pertaining to doubtfully tithed produce does not take effect.

The Mishnah ruled that one fulfills the obligation of *matzah* using *demai* — grain purchased from an *am ha-aretz* about which there is doubt whether *terumos u-maasros* have been separated. The difficulty is obvious: if one may not eat *demai* (at least rabbinically), how can grain that cannot be eaten fulfill an eating-based *mitzva*?

The Gemara resolves this with a striking argument. In theory, one could renounce all one's possessions, become a pauper, and then be fully permitted to eat *demai* — because the Rabbis were lenient with regard to paupers, given that the prohibition of *demai* is only rabbinic, and the biblical presumption (*rov*) is that *terumos u-maasros* have in fact been separated. The Mishnah elsewhere confirms: *ma'achilin et ha-aniyim demai* — one may feed paupers *demai*, and similarly one may feed quartered soldiers (*achsanya*) *demai*, even according to Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai disagreed, but the normative ruling follows Beit Hillel.

The deeper conceptual point may be this: where do we ever find a category of *ma'achalos assuros* — forbidden foods — that paupers are specifically permitted to eat? We would never say, for instance, that a rabbinically forbidden meat-and-milk mixture is permitted for the poor. The fact that the Rabbis made such a specific leniency for paupers with *demai* indicates that *demai* does not have the character of genuinely forbidden food. It is technically restricted but not in the same qualitative category as *issur*, and therefore it does not disqualify *matzah* made from it.

### Ma'aser First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁנִּטְּלָה תְּרוּמָתוֹ וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! דְּכֵיוָן שֶׁנִּטְּלָה תְּרוּמָתוֹ חוּלִּין הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that one can fulfill the obligation to eat *matza* with produce of the **first tithe from which its *teruma*,** the *teruma* of the tithe, **was taken** and given to a priest. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious, as, since its *teruma* has been taken, it is non-sacred** food? What novel element is taught by this statement?

The Mishnah also permits *maaser rishon* from which *terumas ma'aser* has been taken. The Gemara objects: this is obvious — once the Levite has given the tenth-of-a-tenth to the Kohen, the remaining *maaser rishon* is fully *chulin* and anyone may eat it. Why state it?

### Levi Beat Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא — שֶׁהִקְדִּימוֹ בְּשִׁיבֳּלִים, וְנִטְּלָה הֵימֶנּוּ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר וְלֹא נִטְּלָה הֵימֶנּוּ תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, וְכִדְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach the *halakha* in a case where a Levite **preceded** the priest while the grain was still **on** its **stalks.** Ordinarily, the Levite would be given his first tithe only after the priest had taken the *teruma gedola*; however, in this case the Levite took his portion before the priest. **And the *teruma* of the tithe was taken** by the Levite from his tithe so that he would be permitted to eat the tithe; however, **the *teruma gedola* was not taken from** the produce at all. **And** the novel element of the mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Abbahu.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁהִקְדִּימוֹ בְּשִׁיבֳּלִים — פָּטוּר מִתְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַהֲרֵמֹתֶם מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמַת ה׳ מַעֲשֵׂר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר״. מַעֲשֵׂר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה וּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: First tithe** in which the Levite **preceded** the priest while the grain was still **on the stalks is exempt from *teruma gedola*,** even though the Levite thereby reduces the amount of grain the priest receives. **As it is stated:** "Moreover you shall speak to the Levites and say to them: When you take from the children of Israel the tithe which I have given you from them as your inheritance, **and you shall set apart from it a gift for the Lord, a tithe from the tithe"** (Numbers 18:26). This verse teaches that the Levite is obligated to set apart **a tithe from the tithe,** i.e., the *teruma* of the tithe, **and not *teruma gedola* and the *teruma* of the tithe from the tithe.**

The *chiddush* applies to a special case: where the Levite "beat out" the Kohen by approaching the grain's owner while the grain was still in the sheaves (*shibbolim*) — before it had been threshed, winnowed, and processed into a pile. At that stage, the grain has not yet reached the point of full obligation in *terumot u-maasros* (the stage known as *miruach* — the smoothing over of the pile — has not occurred). The owner gave the Levite his *maaser* at that early stage. The Levite then took *terumas maaser* — the tenth-of-a-tenth — from his portion and gave it to the Kohen. But *terumah gedolah* was never taken from this grain, either before or after the Levite received his portion.

One might think that since *terumah gedolah* was never separated, the grain remains *tevel*. But Rabbi Abbahu cites Reish Lakish: *maaser rishon she-hikdimo be-shibbolim, patur mi-terumah gedolah*. When the Levite takes his *maaser* while the grain is still in the sheaves — before it has become fully obligated — he is exempt from *terumah gedolah*. The verse states *ma'aser min hama'aser amarti lach* — I told you to take a tenth of a tenth, and not also *terumah gedolah u-terumas maaser min hama'aser*. Once the Levite received his portion before full obligation set in, he escapes the requirement of *terumah gedolah* entirely.

### Rav Pappa's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אֲפִילּוּ הִקְדִּימוֹ בִּכְרִי נָמֵי לִיפְּטַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עָלֶיךָ אָמַר קְרָא ״מִכֹּל מַתְּנֹתֵיכֶם תָּרִימוּ אֵת כׇּל תְּרוּמַת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: However, if** that **is so, even** if the Levite **preceded** the priest after the kernels of grain were removed from the stalks and placed **in a pile,** the Levite should not have to separate *teruma gedola* in this case either. Abaye **said to him: With regard to your** claim, **the verse states: "From all that is given to you, you shall set apart all of that which is the Lord's *teruma*,** of all the best of it, even its consecrated portion" (Numbers 18:29). This verse teaches that the Levites must designate a portion of the gifts they receive and give it to the priests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ?! הַאי אִידְּגַן, וְהַאי לָא אִידְּגַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** to lead you to require the separation of *teruma gedola* from first tithe that was taken from grain in piles and not from first tithe that was taken from grain on stalks? Abaye answers: **This** produce, after being threshed and placed into piles, is completely processed and **has become grain; and that** produce on the stalk **did not** yet **become grain,** and the Levite is therefore exempt from separating *teruma gedola* from it. Produce that has been arranged in a pile is called grain by the Torah and is given to the priest. Once it is classified as grain, the right of the priest takes effect and the Levite is required to separate *teruma gedola* from it.

The Gemara then poses a challenge. Rav Pappa asks Abaye: if the Levite escapes the *terumah gedolah* obligation by beating the Kohen to the grain, why limit this to the case where the grain was in sheaves? Even if the Levite came after the grain had already been piled and processed (*bikari*) but still before the Kohen arrived, should he not similarly be exempt? Abaye responds by citing a second verse: *mi-kol matnoséichem tarimu et kol terumat Hashem* — from all your gifts you shall give all of the *terumos*. This verse indicates that sometimes the Levite must give all *terumos* from his *maaser*, including *terumah gedolah*.

The resolution: the distinction is whether the grain had already reached the point of full obligation. When the Levite takes his *maaser* while the grain is in sheaves — *lo idgan*, not yet fully processed — no obligation in *terumah gedolah* ever attached to the portion he received. But if he received his *maaser* after the grain had been winnowed, threshed, and piled — *idgan*, it has already become grain subject to *ma'aser* — the obligation in *terumah gedolah* had already taken effect, and the Levite cannot escape it merely by collecting his portion before the Kohen does.

The Mishnah's ruling that *maaser rishon* from which *terumah* has been taken is valid for *matzah* therefore applies even to this unusual case where the Levite escaped *terumah gedolah* by collecting his portion while the grain was still in sheaves.

### Ma'aser Sheni Redeemed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁנִּפְדּוּ וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁנָּתַן אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְלֹא נָתַן אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ. וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאֵין חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that one can fulfill his obligation to eat *matza* with **second-tithe** grain **and** with **consecrated** grain **that was redeemed.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case, as consecrated grain that has been redeemed is non-sacred produce. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case where the consecrated property was not completely redeemed, i.e., **where one gave** payment for **the principal,** the value of the tithe, **but he did not give** payment for the additional **fifth** due when redeeming consecrated items. **And** the mishna **teaches us** that failure to add **the fifth does not invalidate** the redemption. Although there is an obligation to pay this additional fifth, the neglect of this duty does not prevent the grain from becoming non-sacred.

The Mishnah permits *maaser sheni* and *hekdesh* that have been redeemed. Again the Gemara asks: is this not obvious? Once redeemed, the sanctity is removed and the item is ordinary *chulin*. The *chiddush*, the Gemara explains, lies in a case where the redemption was technically deficient — specifically, where one gave the principal value (*keren*) without adding the extra fifth (*chomesh*) that is normally required when redeeming *maaser sheni* or *hekdesh*. The Mishnah teaches that the absence of the *chomesh* does not invalidate the redemption: *ein chomesh me'akev* — the fifth does not block the removal of sanctity. As long as the principal was paid, the *maaser sheni* or *hekdesh* is sufficiently redeemed to be treated as *chulin* and may be used for *matzah*.

### Kohanim Challah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּהֲנִים בְּחַלָּה וּבִתְרוּמָה וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מַצָּה שָׁוָה לְכׇל אָדָם בָּעֵינַן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — ״מַצּוֹת״ ״מַצּוֹת״ רִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the mishna further taught that **priests** can fulfill their obligation **with** *matza* of ***ḥalla* and with *teruma*.** The Gemara again asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case. Since a priest is permitted to eat *ḥalla* and *teruma*, he can fulfill his obligation to eat *matza* with them. The Gemara responds: This ruling is nevertheless necessary, **lest you say** that **we require *matza*** that may be eaten **equally by anyone,** which would mean that *matza* that may not be eaten by regular Israelites is prohibited to priests as well. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that the repetition of the words **"*matzot*," "*matzot*"** (Deuteronomy 16:3, 8) comes to **amplify,** i.e., one can fulfill one's obligation to eat *matza* even with foods that may be eaten only by specific people.

The Mishnah's ruling that *kohanim* may use *challah* and *terumah* for their *matzah* obligation might also have been questioned: perhaps *matzah* must be universally edible — *shaveh le-chol adam*. The Gemara rejects this requirement. The verse uses the plural *matzot*, which serves to include even items not edible by all. As long as the item is edible by the one eating it, that is sufficient. Since *challah* and *terumah* are fully edible for *kohanim* — indeed, that is precisely what they are designated for — *kohanim* may use them to fulfill the *mitzva* of *matzah*.

### Forbidden Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא בְּטֶבֶל וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, בְּטֶבֶל טָבוּל מִדְּרַבָּנַן שֶׁזְּרָעוֹ בְּעָצִיץ שֶׁאֵינוֹ נָקוּב.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **However,** one may **not** fulfill this obligation **with untithed produce.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case, as it is always prohibited to eat *tevel*. The Gemara explains: **No, it is necessary** to teach this *halakha* in a case **where** it is considered **untithed produce by rabbinic** law, and by Torah law the produce is permitted. For instance, this is the case with regard to grain **that one sowed in an unperforated flowerpot.** Anything grown disconnected from the ground is not defined as produce of the ground, and its owner is exempt by Torah law from tithing it. However, by rabbinic law, grain sowed in **an unperforated flowerpot** is considered untithed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁלֹּא נִטְּלָה תְּרוּמָתוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁהִקְדִּימוֹ בִּכְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** It was also taught in the mishna: **And** one does **not** fulfill his obligation **with** *matza* from produce **of** the **first tithe, whose *teruma*,** *teruma* of the tithe, **was not taken.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that this is the case, as this produce may not be eaten. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach this with regard to a case **where** the Levite **preceded** the priest after the kernels of grain were placed **in a pile** but before *teruma gedola* was separated from the produce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כְּדַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְשַׁנִּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Lest you say, as Rav Pappa said to Abaye,** that in that case too, the Levite should be exempt from the requirement to separate *teruma gedola* from this produce, the *tanna* of the mishna **teaches us, as** Abaye **responded** to Rav Pappa, that there is a difference between a case when the grain was on the stalks and when it was collected in a pile. Therefore, one may not eat this produce before separating *teruma gedola* from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדּוּ וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** It was further taught in the mishna: **And** one does **not** fulfill his obligation to eat *matza* **with second-tithe** produce **or consecrated** grain **that was not redeemed.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that, too, **obvious,** as it is prohibited to eat these foods?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּנִפְדּוּ, וּמַאי לֹא נִפְדּוּ — שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדּוּ כְּהִלְכָתָן: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁפְּדָאוֹ עַל גַּב אֲסִימוֹן, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְצַרְתָּ הַכֶּסֶף״ — דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ צוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** this is speaking of a case **where they were redeemed, and what** is the meaning of the expression: **They were not redeemed?** It means **that they were not redeemed properly,** e.g., **second-tithe** grain that **was redeemed with an unminted coin [*asimon*]. As the Merciful One says** with regard to the redemption of the second tithe: "And you shall turn it into money, and bind up the money in your hand, and go to the place which the Lord your God shall choose" (Deuteronomy 14:25). The phrase **"and bind up [*ve'tzarta*] the money"** indicates that the produce must be exchanged for **an object that has a form [*tzura*],** not unminted metal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁחִילְּלוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְנָתַן הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **consecrated** property, we are dealing with a case **where he redeemed it** by exchanging it **for land instead of money, as the Merciful One says: "And he will give** the fifth part of **the money** of your valuation unto it **and it shall be assured to him"** (see Leviticus 27:19). If one redeemed consecrated property with land rather than money, the consecrated status is not transferred to the land.

The Mishnah then lists the items with which one cannot fulfill the obligation. One cannot use real *tevel* — untithed grain. The Gemara notes that this is obvious. The *chiddush* applies to *tevel de-rabbanan* — specifically grain grown in a flowerpot without a hole in the bottom (*atzitz she-eino nakuv*), which is only rabbinically obligated in *maasros* since it is not truly connected to the ground. Even this rabbinic *tevel* disqualifies *matzah*, since it cannot be eaten by anyone — not even paupers, unlike *demai* — and therefore lacks the quality of edible food required.

One cannot use *maaser rishon* from which *terumah* has not been taken. The Gemara again: this is obvious — it is *tevel*. The *chiddush* is the mirror image of the permitted case above: here the Levite collected his *maaser* after the grain had already been piled (*bikari*), and therefore after the obligation in *terumah gedolah* had already attached. Even though the Levite beat the Kohen in the sense of collecting his portion first, the prior attachment of the *terumah gedolah* obligation means the grain remains *tevel* until *terumah gedolah* is separated. As Abaye explained to Rav Pappa, the verse *mi-kol matnoséichem tarimu et kol terumat Hashem* requires full separation in such a case.

Finally, *maaser sheni* and *hekdesh* that have not been properly redeemed cannot be used. The cases of deficient redemption here are: *maaser sheni* redeemed on an unminted coin (*asimon*), which the verse *ve-tzarta ha-kesef* — bind the money — excludes (only properly minted coins with an image suffice); and *hekdesh* redeemed on land rather than on moveable property, which the verse *ve-natan ha-kesef ve-kam lo* requires.

### Overall Principle

The overall principle that emerges from these cases is coherent: if the food is edible — even if only by a specific group, like *kohanim*, or only in a technical or contingent way, like *demai* — then it may be used for *matzah*. If it is forbidden to eat, even rabbinically, it cannot fulfill the *mitzva* — and this even in cases where the prohibition is technical or the redemption merely deficient. The further question of *why* forbidden food cannot fulfill the *mitzva* of *matzah* — whether it is derived from a verse or from the general principle of *mitzva ha-ba'ah be-aveirah* — will be addressed as the discussion continues.
