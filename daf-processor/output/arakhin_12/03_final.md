# Arakhin 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shirah Without Korban

### Shirah Basic Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, וַיֹּאמֶר: ״חִזְקִיָּהוּ לְהַעֲלוֹת הָעוֹלָה עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וּבְעֵת הֵחֵל הָעוֹלָה הֵחֵל שִׁיר ה׳ וְהַחֲצוֹצְרוֹת עַל יְדֵי כְּלֵי שִׁיר דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a verse: **"And Hezekiah commanded to offer the burnt offering upon the altar, and when the burnt offering began, the song of the Lord began also, and the trumpets, together with the instruments of David king of Israel**…And Hezekiah the king and the princes commanded the Levites to sing praises unto the Lord" (II Chronicles 29:27–30).

The *gemara* on the bottom of 11b has been laying out the basic principle governing the *shirah* of the *levi'im*: one performs it in conjunction with an *olat chovah* of the *tzibur* — that is, the *temidim* and the *musafim*. The question under discussion is whether one would also perform it for a communal *olah* that is a free-will offering. To address this, the *gemara* introduces a *braita* that will serve as the focus of the remainder of the discussion.

### Meritorious Days Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְגַלְגְּלִין זְכוּת לְיוֹם זַכַּאי וְכוּ׳. אָמְרוּ: כְּשֶׁחָרַב הַבַּיִת בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב הָיָה, וּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת הָיָה, וּמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה, וּמִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ שֶׁל יְהוֹיָרִיב הָיְתָה, וְהָיוּ כֹּהֲנִים וּלְוִיִּם עוֹמְדִים עַל דּוּכָנָן וְאוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה, וּמָה שִׁירָה אָמְרוּ? ״וַיָּשֶׁב עֲלֵיהֶם אֶת אוֹנָם וּבְרָעָתָם יַצְמִיתֵם״, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיקוּ לוֹמַר ״יַצְמִיתֵם ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ״ עַד שֶׁבָּאוּ גּוֹיִם וּכְבָשׁוּם, וְכֵן בַּשְּׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara has still not proven whether or not a communal voluntary burnt offering must be accompanied with song. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the following *baraita*. **Rabbi Yosei says: A fortunate** matter **is brought about on an auspicious day, and a deleterious** matter **on an inauspicious day.** As the Sages **said: When the Temple was destroyed for the first** time, **that day was the Ninth of Av,** a date on which several calamities had already occurred; **and it was the conclusion of Shabbat,** i.e., it was on the day after Shabbat, a Sunday; **and it was the year after a Sabbatical** Year; **and it was the week of the priestly watch of Jehoiarib; and the priests and Levites were standing on their platform and singing song. And what song were they singing?** They were singing the verse: **"And He brought upon them their own iniquity, and He will cut them off in their own evil"** (Psalms 94:23). **And they did not manage to recite** the end of that verse: **"The Lord our God will cut them off," before gentiles came and conquered them. And likewise,** the same happened **when the Second** Temple was destroyed.

The *braita* opens with the view of Rabbi Yosi: *megalgelin zechut l'yom zakai v'chovah l'yom chayav* — good things happen on meritorious days and bad things on inauspicious ones. The *braita* then offers evidence for the second half of this principle. When the First *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed, it was *Tisha B'Av* — the same day the spies returned with their report, which is presumably the original reason it was designated a *yom chayav*. It was also *motzei Shabbat*, a Sunday, and *motzei shevi'it*, the first year of the *shemita* cycle. The coincidence of those latter two details seems to carry its own ominous resonance as the beginning of a new era of *galut*, even if not directly tied to the notion of *yom chayav*.

### Temple Destruction Story

The *braita* continues: it was the cycle of *Yehoyariv* among the *kohanim*, and the *kohanim* and *levi'im* were standing on their *duchan* and singing *shirah*. One might note that it is slightly unusual to say the *kohanim* were on the *duchan* — that platform is really associated with the *levi'im* — but the *braita* includes them. The *shirah* they were singing was *vayashov aleihem et onam u'vera'atam yatzmitem* — "God will return upon them their iniquity; in their evil He will cut them off." The words that were coming out of their mouths were remarkably — even chillingly — apt for the moment. And they did not even complete the verse, *yatzmitem Hashem Elokeinu*, before the enemies came and conquered them. The *braita* concludes: *v'chen bashniyah* — the same thing happened in the Second *Beit HaMikdash*, which was also destroyed on *Tisha B'Av*, on a Sunday, in the first year of the *shemita* cycle.

### No Tamid Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי שִׁירָה מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיהּ? אִילֵּימָא דְּעוֹלַת חוֹבָה, מִי הֲוָה? בְּשִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר בְּתַמּוּז בָּטַל הַתָּמִיד! וְאֶלָּא דְּעוֹלַת נְדָבָה — וְהָא תָּנֵי רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא דְּלָא צְרִיכָא! אֶלָּא לָאו דִּנְסָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **This song, what was its purpose? If we say that** it accompanied **an obligatory burnt offering, was there** any obligatory communal burnt offering sacrificed at that time? **The daily offering had ceased** to be brought **on the seventeenth of Tammuz,** three weeks before the Ninth of Av. **Rather,** it would seem that this song accompanied **a voluntary burnt offering.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, teach that** a voluntary burnt offering **does not require** song? **Rather, is it not** the case **that** the song accompanied **libations** brought without an animal offering? This would prove that the Levites sing when a wine libation is brought, even without an animal offering.

The *gemara* now raises its central question: *hai shirah mai avidetei?* — what was this *shirah* doing there at all? Normally one performs *shirah* in conjunction with the *tamid* or the *musaf*. But it was *Tisha B'Av*, so there was no *musaf*. And as for the *tamid* — *b'Yud-Zayin b'Tammuz batel hatamid*: already from the 17th of Tammuz, under siege and without sufficient animals, they had ceased to bring the *korban tamid*. How could *shirah* have been performed without it?

The *gemara* suggests: perhaps it was *d'olat nedavah* — perhaps a single animal happened to be available, not enough to maintain the regular *tamid* (which requires a sheep), but they found a calf — *ben bakar krei b'alma hu d'isrami lehu* — and brought a free-will communal *olah*. If so, this would constitute proof that *shirah* may be performed even when the *olah* is not the fixed obligatory one. The *gemara* then asks: but *v'tisbarah* — does this reasoning really hold up? If they lacked sufficient animals for the *tamid*, how did they happen to have an animal for a free-will *olah*? The *gemara* answers: *ha lo kashya* — that is not difficult. The *tamid* requires a specific species; a *ben bakar*, a calf, cannot serve as the *tamid* and might well have been available.

### Duchan Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אָשֵׁי: וְתִסְבְּרָא?! שִׁירָה דְּיוֹמֵיהּ ״לַה׳ הָאָרֶץ וּמְלוֹאָהּ״, ״וַיָּשֶׁב עֲלֵיהֶם אֶת אוֹנָם״ בְּשִׁירָה דְּאַרְבְּעָה בְּשַׁבָּא הוּא! אֶלָּא אִילְיָיא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דִּנְפַל לְהוּ בְּפוּמַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said, and some say** it was **Rav Ashi** who said: **And can you understand** that this song was recited over any offering? **The song of the day** for Sunday, which is when the *baraita* says that the Temple was destroyed, is the psalm beginning with the verse: "A psalm of David. **The earth is the Lord's, and the fullness thereof"** (Psalms 24:1). And yet the verse that the *baraita* says that the Levites were singing: **"And He brought upon them their own iniquity," is in the song for Wednesday,** not the song for Sunday. **Rather, it was merely** a portentous **lamentation that came into their mouths,** not a song recited over an offering. Consequently, no proof can be offered from this *baraita*.

However, Rava (and some say Rav Ashi) raises a deeper objection: *v'tisbarah* — does this even cohere? The *shir shel yom* for Sunday is *la'Hashem ha'aretz u'mlo'ah*, yet they were singing *vayashov aleihem et onam*, which is the Wednesday *shirah*. The whole thing was already out of order. *Ailaya b'alma hu d'nafal lehu b'pumaihu* — Rashi explains this as a *kinah*, a dirge: it simply fell from their mouths, not as a proper *shir shel yom* but as an almost instinctive lament for what was about to befall them. At this stage, the *gemara* initially understands this to mean they were not even performing the official *shirah* of the *levi'im* at all — they were simply anticipating the destruction.

But the *gemara* immediately rejects this reading: *v'ha omdin al haduchan katani* — the *braita* explicitly states they were standing on the *duchan*, which indicates they were performing official *shirat levi'im*. The fact that they sang the wrong *shir* might mean they erred, but they were still engaged in formal *shirah*. And if so, the original question returns in full force: how were they performing formal *shirah* without a *tamid*?

### Reish Lakish Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״עוֹמְדִים עַל דּוּכָנָן״ קָתָנֵי! כִּדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אוֹמֵר שִׁירָה שֶׁלֹּא עַל הַקׇּרְבָּן. אִי הָכִי, בִּנְסָכִים נָמֵי לֵימָא! נָפֵיק מִינֵּיהּ חוּרְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* that the Levites were **standing on their platform** near the altar, which is the place where they sing to accompany offerings? The Gemara answers: This can be explained **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish. As Reish Lakish says:** The Levites are permitted to **recite a song** on the platform even when it is **not for an offering.** The Gemara asks: **If so, let** them **also recite** a song **for libations** that are brought without an animal offering, even if that is not required. The Gemara answers: This could **result in a mishap,** as the Levites might assume that just as singing for libations which are brought without an animal offering is optional, so too, singing for libations that accompany an animal offering is also optional.

The *gemara* answers: *k'de'Reish Lakish*, for Reish Lakish teaches, *omer shira belo korban* — one may perform *shirah* even without a *korban*. According to Reish Lakish, the *levi'im* have a *reshut* — permission — to sing *shirah* at any time within the *Beit HaMikdash*, regardless of whether a *korban* is being offered. The *chiyuv* to do so is tied to the *tamid* and *musaf*, but the *reshut* is not so restricted.

### Free Will Problem

The *gemara* then presses: if that is so — *ei hachi*, if they were permitted to sing *shirah* at any time — then the entire opening question about whether *shirah* may accompany a communal free-will *olah* dissolves. If one has *reshut* to perform *shirah* at all times, certainly one may do so during a communal *olat nedavah* as well. Why was that ever in doubt?

The *gemara* answers: *nafka d'meicharei churban* — it would lead to bad consequences. Rashi explains: if *shirah* is performed alongside a free-will *olah*, people will come to understand that doing so is a *reshut* and not an obligation. They may then come to treat *shirah* even by the *olat tamid* more casually and fail to observe it properly when it is in fact obligatory. Therefore, even though there is a general *reshut* to perform *shirah* at all times, we prefer not to perform it alongside a communal free-will *olah* so as not to blur the distinction between obligatory and optional contexts.

The *gemara* then proceeds to resolve the original question. Rav Mari, son of Rav Pinchas, teaches a *braita* on the verse *al olosaychem v'al zivchei shalmeychem*, which the *gemara* has been treating as parallel to the *shirah* of the *levi'im*. The *braita* reasons: just as the *shalmei tzibur* — the two lambs of *Shavuot*, which are *kodshei kodashim* — have a fixed communal time, so too the *olah* must have a fixed communal time. This limits the *shirah* to occasions when there is a fixed, obligatory communal *olah* — meaning the *tamid* and *musaf* — along with the *shalmei tzibur* of *Shavuot*.

## Delayed Nesachim

### Independent Nesachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: נְסָכִים הַבָּאִים בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן טְעוּנִין שִׁירָה אוֹ אֵין טְעוּנִין שִׁירָה? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: ״מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה אֶלָּא עַל הַיַּיִן״ — אָמְרִינַן, אוֹ דִלְמָא עַל אֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה אָמְרִינַן, אַשְּׁתִיָּיה לְחוֹדַהּ לָא אָמְרִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **libations that are brought by themselves,** without an animal offering, **do they require song** on the part of the Levites as the libations are poured on the altar, **or do they not require song?** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: Should it be assumed that **since Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: From where** is it derived that **one recites a song** of praise in the Temple **only over wine,** it can be inferred that **we recite** songs whenever wine is poured on the altar? **Or perhaps we recite** songs only **upon the eating and drinking** of the altar, i.e., when both an animal offering is burnt and a libation is also poured upon the altar, but **upon the drinking** of the altar **alone, we do not recite** songs.

The *gemara* raises a new question: *nesachim haba'im bifnei atzman* — if *nesachim* are brought independently, *te'unim shira o ein te'unim shira?* — do they require *shirah* or not? The question has two poles. On one side, Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan that *ein omrim shira ela al hayayin* — *shirah* is said specifically over wine. That might suggest that even wine brought by itself would warrant *shirah*. On the other side, perhaps *akhila u'shtiya be'inan* — we require the full combination of food and drink, meaning wine paired with a *korban*, not wine alone.

### Postponed Wine Theory

The difficulty, of course, is that the entire preceding discussion established that *shirah* is only performed for a fixed communal *olah*. What could possibly justify asking whether *nesachim* on their own warrant *shirah*? Rashi and Tosafot both address this by explaining that the question concerns *nesachim* that are *postponed* — the *halacha* permits the *nesachim* of a *korban* to be brought the following day, or even some days later. The question is therefore: if the *nesachim* of Sunday's *korban tamid* were delayed and are now being brought on Wednesday, does the act of pouring this wine — which is indeed connected to an obligatory communal *olah*, albeit one offered days earlier — warrant *shirah*?

And if so, which *shir* would be recited? The assumption is that one would say the *shir shel yom* of the current day — Wednesday's *shir* — not Sunday's. The rationale is that the obligation was not to recite a specific number of *shirot*; rather, the problem was the absence of *shirah* accompanying *that korban*. Now that the wine is being poured, the *shirah* accompanies the pouring, and the day's *shir* applies. This is comparable to the principle in *tefilla* where missing *ya'aleh v'yavo* at *mincha* of *Rosh Chodesh* does not generate an obligation to recite a specific *Rosh Chodesh* text — the deficiency was in the *tefilla* itself, not in a quota of *ya'aleh v'yavo* recitations.

### Braita Revisited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְגַלְגְּלִין זְכוּת לְיוֹם זַכַּאי וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to resolve the dilemma through a *baraita* cited earlier. **Come** and **hear: Rabbi Yosei says** that **a fortunate** matter **is brought about on an auspicious day,** and a deleterious matter on an inauspicious day, e.g., the Ninth of Av, on which several tragedies have already occurred. As the Sages said: When the Temple was destroyed for the first time, that day was the Ninth of Av; and it was the conclusion of Shabbat; and it was the year after a Sabbatical Year.

To resolve the question, the *gemara* returns to the same *braita*: *megalgelin zechut l'yom zakai v'chovah l'yom chayav*. The *braita* states that on the day the First *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed, the *levi'im* were performing *shirah*. The *gemara* asks again: *hai shira mai avidteih?* What justified any *shirah* at that moment? *Ilima de'olat chovah* — if it was tied to the *tamid* — *mi hava?* They had already ceased bringing the *tamid* from the 17th of Tammuz. *Ela lav denisachin* — it must have been, then, that they were performing *shirah* over *nesachim* brought independently — wine being poured without an accompanying *korban* at that moment. If so, this would demonstrate that independent *nesachim* do indeed warrant *shirah*.

### Delayed Theory Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אָשֵׁי: וְתִסְבְּרָא?! שִׁירָה דְּיוֹמֵיהּ ״לַה׳ הָאָרֶץ וּמְלוֹאָהּ״, ״וַיָּשֶׁב עֲלֵיהֶם אֶת אוֹנָם״ בְּשִׁירָה דְּאַרְבְּעָה בְּשַׁבָּא הוּא! אֶלָּא אִילְיָיא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דִּנְפַל לְהוּ בְּפוּמַּיְיהוּ. וְהָא ״עוֹמְדִים עַל דּוּכָנָן״ קָתָנֵי! כִּדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אוֹמֵר שִׁירָה שֶׁלֹּא עַל הַקׇּרְבָּן. אִי הָכִי, בִּנְסָכִים נָמֵי לֵימָא! נָפֵיק מִינֵּיהּ חוּרְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said, and some say** it was **Rav Ashi** who said: **And can you understand** that this song was recited over any offering? **The song of the day** for Sunday, which is when the *baraita* says that the Temple was destroyed, is the psalm beginning with the verse: "A psalm of David. **The earth is the Lord's, and the fullness thereof"** (Psalms 24:1). And yet the verse that the *baraita* says that the Levites were singing: **"And He brought upon them their own iniquity," is in the song for Wednesday,** not the song for Sunday. **Rather, it was merely** a portentous **lamentation that came into their mouths,** not a song recited over an offering. Consequently, no proof can be offered from this *baraita*. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* that the Levites were **standing on their platform** near the altar, which is the place where they sing to accompany offerings? The Gemara answers: This can be explained **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish. As Reish Lakish says:** The Levites are permitted to **recite a song** on the platform even when it is **not for an offering.** The Gemara asks: **If so, let** them **also recite** a song **for libations** that are brought without an animal offering, even if that is not required. The Gemara answers: This could **result in a mishap,** as the Levites might assume that just as singing for libations which are brought without an animal offering is optional, so too, singing for libations that accompany an animal offering is also optional.

The *gemara* immediately rejects this inference. Rava, and some say Rav Ashi, returns to the core objection: *v'tisbarah*? The *shir shel yom* for Sunday was *la'Hashem ha'aretz u'mlo'ah*, yet they were singing *bishira de'arba beshabat* — the Wednesday *shirah*. Everything was already disordered. *Ela eiva b'alma de'afnu bepumaihu* — it was simply a dirge that fell from their mouths; this was not a regulated halakhic act at all. But then the *gemara* reinvokes the *duchan*: they were standing on the platform, which means it was formal *shirah*. So *k'de'Reish Lakish* — they were exercising the general *reshut* to perform *shirah* at any time, not because of *nesachim* specifically. And if that is the explanation, *benesachim nami leima* — one should say they had that *reshut* equally well when pouring independent *nesachim*. But *nafik minei churba* — the same concern about confusion applies, so we do not derive any proof from this incident regarding independent *nesachim*. The question of whether independent *nesachim* require *shirah* thus remains unresolved, and the *gemara* moves on.

What we have established, then, is this: the obligation to perform *shirah* is tied to fixed communal *olot* — the *tamid* and *musaf*, and possibly the *shalmei tzibur* of *Shavuot*. However, the *levi'im* possess a general *reshut* to perform *shirah* at any time in the *Beit HaMikdash*, even without a *korban*, in keeping with the view of Reish Lakish. The principle of *ein omrim shira ela al hayayin* is therefore not absolute; it describes the primary obligatory context but does not prohibit *shirah* at other times.

## First Temple Shmita Year

### Which Shmita Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְגַלְגְּלִין זְכוּת לְיוֹם זַכַּאי וְכוּ׳. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, בְּמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית מִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנָה לְגָלוּתֵנוּ, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ, בְּאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה אַחַר אֲשֶׁר הוּכְּתָה הָעִיר״! אֵיזוֹ הִיא שָׁנָה שֶׁרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה בֶּעָשׂוֹר לַחֹדֶשׁ? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself** cited in the *baraita*. **Rabbi Yosei says: A fortunate** matter **is brought about on an auspicious day,** and a deleterious matter on an inauspicious day. The Gemara asks: **Can you find** such a possibility, that when the Temple was destroyed **for the first time** it was **in the year after a Sabbatical** Year? **But isn't it written** in a verse that Ezekiel experienced a prophecy **"in the twenty-fifth year of our captivity, at the beginning of the year, on the tenth day of the month, in the fourteenth year after the city was smitten"** (Ezekiel 40:1)? **Which** is the **year when the beginning of the year is on the tenth of the month? You must say** that **this** is referring to **the Jubilee,** which begins on Yom Kippur, the tenth of Tishrei.

We now turn to the second major subject of the *daf*: the calendrical calculations that the *braita* demands. The *braita* stated that the First *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed on *motzei shevi'it* — the first year of the *shemita* cycle. The *gemara* asks: *mi mishkachat lei?* Does this actually work out?

### Yechezkel's 25th Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בְּחַד בְּשָׁבוּעַ חֲרוּב, מֵחַד בְּשָׁבוּעַ לְחַד בְּשָׁבוּעַ — תַּמְנֵי, לְחַד בְּשָׁבוּעַ אַחֲרִינָא — חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה הָוְויָין.
>
> **Translation:** **But if it enters your mind** to say that the Temple was **destroyed in the first** year **of the Sabbatical cycle,** it is impossible that the Jubilee, which follows a Sabbatical Year, occurred on the fourteenth year after the destruction of the Temple: Counting **from the first** year **of the Sabbatical cycle to** the next **first** year **of the Sabbatical cycle,** one arrives at year **eight.** If one continues to count **to the first** year **of** yet **another Sabbatical cycle, it is the fifteenth** year after the year of the destruction of the Temple, not the fourteenth year.

The *gemara* challenges the claim from a *pasuk* in *Yechezkel*: *b'esrim v'chamesh shana l'galutenu* — Yechezkel says it is the 25th year of our exile. Now, Yechezkel was not exiled with the *churban*; he was exiled together with *Yehoyachin* eleven years before the destruction. The verse continues: *berosh hashana be'asor lachodesh* — on *Rosh Hashana* on the tenth of the month. And it adds: *b'arba esre shana achar asher huketa ha'ir* — fourteen years after the city was struck. So the 25th year of Yechezkel's exile was simultaneously the 14th year after the *churban*. This means Yechezkel was exiled eleven years before the destruction.

### Yom Kippur Yovel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: בְּאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה אַחַר שָׁנָה שֶׁהוּכְּתָה הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Ravina said:** The meaning of the verse is that it was **in the fourteenth year after the year that the city was smitten.** The year of the destruction is not counted as year one of the fourteen years; rather, year one was the following year. The destruction took place in the thirty-sixth year of the Jubilee cycle, and therefore the Jubilee occurred fourteen years later.

*Eizehu shana she-Rosh Hashana be'asor lachodesh?* — which year has *Rosh Hashana* on the tenth of the month? *Hevei omer: zeh yovel* — it must be the *yovel* year. In the *yovel* year, *Yom Kippur* functions as a kind of formal *Rosh Hashana*, since it is the day on which the *yovel* is fully inaugurated — the *shofar* is blown, slaves go free, and lands revert to their original owners. The *gemara* elsewhere notes that from the actual *Rosh Hashana* of the *yovel* year until *Yom Kippur*, slaves could not yet be worked but had not yet been fully released. *Yom Kippur* thus serves as a *rosh hashana* for the *yovel*'s practical effects. This tells us that fourteen years after the *churban* was a *yovel* year, and the *yovel* year is the first year of a new *shemita* cycle.

Now, if the *churban* itself occurred in year one of a *shemita* cycle and one counts both endpoints — from year one of the cycle in which the *churban* occurred to year one of a later cycle — the first cycle accounts for seven years, and adding a full additional cycle brings one to year fifteen. But the verse says it was only year fourteen. This means we are counting fourteen years *after* the *churban* year, not treating the *churban* year itself as year one of the count. In that case, one might ask whether the *churban* should not then be placed in year two of the cycle rather than year one.

### Yechezkel Exile Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר מָר: גָּלוּ בְּשֶׁבַע, גָּלוּ בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה. גָּלוּ בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה, גָּלוּ בִּתְשַׁע עֶשְׂרֵה. מִשַּׁב וְעַד תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי — חַד סְרֵי, וַחֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה — עֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁית הָוְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: Now, **from the seventh** year, when the first exile occurred, **until the eighteenth** year, when the second exile occurred, in conjunction with the Temple's destruction, is **eleven** years, not counting the year of the destruction, **and** an additional **fifteen** years, until the date mentioned in the verse in Ezekiel, **equals twenty-six** years from the first exile, not twenty-five.

To resolve this the *gemara* invokes a tradition: *galu b'sheva galu b'shmona*, *galu b'yud-chet galu b'yud-vet* — there was an exile in year 7/8 and another in year 18/19. These paired designations reflect two different counting systems. The exile of *Yehoyachin* (with Yechezkel) is called year 7 from the *kibush Yehoyakim* — the year Nebuchadnezzar subdued Yehoyakim — and simultaneously year 8 from Nebuchadnezzar's accession to the throne, since in year 2 of his reign he conquered Yehoyakim. Similarly, the *churban* is called year 18 from *kibush Yehoyakim* and year 19 of Nebuchadnezzar's reign.

The *gemara* notes that Nebuchadnezzar conquered Ninveh in his first year of reign and conquered Yehoyakim in his second. Using either counting system, there are eleven years between the exile of Yechezkel and the *churban*. The *churban* thus falls in year 12 if one counts the exile of Yechezkel as year 1 and counts both endpoints — since 7 to 18 (counting endpoints) yields not 12 but fewer, and the *gemara* works through the arithmetic carefully, demonstrating that 25 can be reached only by excluding the year of the *churban* itself from both counts.

### Destruction Year Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבִינָא: וּלְדִידָךְ מִי נִיחָא? מִכְּדֵי גָּלוּ נָמֵי בִּתְשַׁע עֶשְׂרֵה, מִשַּׁב וְעַד תְּשַׁסְרֵי — תַּרְתֵּי סְרֵי, וְאַרְבַּע סְרֵי — עֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁית הָוְיָין! אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר? לְבַר מִשַּׁתָּא דִּגְלוֹ בַּהּ. לְדִידִי נָמֵי — לְבַר מִשַּׁתָּא דִּגְלוֹ בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Ravina** could have **said to you: And according to you,** who maintains that the fourteen years mentioned in the verse include the year of the destruction of the Temple, **does it work out well? Now, they were also exiled in the nineteenth** year, and it was then that the Temple was destroyed. **From the seventh** year **until the nineteenth** year is **twelve** years, **and** an additional **fourteen** years until the date mentioned in the verse in Ezekiel **equals twenty-six** years from the first exile, not twenty-five. **Rather, what have you to say?** That the twenty-five years are counted **separate from the year when they were exiled** for the first time. **According to my** opinion **also,** I can say that the twenty-five years are **separate from the year when they were exiled** for the first time.

The answer, stated by Ravina, is: *arba asra shana achar shana shecharva ha'ir* — we count 14 years *after* the year the city was destroyed, not including that year in the tally. Similarly, the 11-year gap between Yechezkel's exile and the *churban* does not include the *churban* year itself. So the calculation is: 11 years before the *churban* (not counting the *churban* year) plus 14 years after the *churban* (not counting the *churban* year) equals 25. The *churban* year is excluded from both segments. This preserves the claim that the *churban* occurred in year one of a *shemita* cycle, because from one year-one to the next (at an interval of 14 years not counting endpoints) produces a number consistent with the verse's testimony of 14.

A challenge arises: given that the *churban* is described as year 18/19, does the alternative reading — that year 19 was a *separate* exile event rather than merely an alternative name for the same year — create an additional problem? Ravina addresses it: *ela mai it lach lemeimar levar mishta degalu bah* — in whatever version of the counting one uses, one excludes the year of the exile itself. Applying this consistently, the arithmetic converges on 25, and the conclusion stands: *mi savar shalosh galuyos havyan?* — there were not three separate exiles. Year 7 and year 8 are the same year counted two ways; year 18 and year 19 are the same year counted two ways. The tradition is describing two exile events, not three.

### 18-19 Year Verification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, תְּשַׁסְרֵי לְרָבִינָא קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **In any case,** the assertion of the *baraita* that the destruction of the Temple occurred in the **nineteenth** year is **difficult for Ravina,** because there are eleven years from the first exile until the year of the destruction, not counting the year of the exile or the year of the destruction, and then an additional fifteen years, including the year of the destruction, until the date mentioned in the verse in Ezekiel, which is a total of twenty-six years, not twenty-five.

An additional verification follows. The *gemara* says: if one takes the span from year 7 (of *kibush Yehoyakim*) to year 18 — which is 11 years — and then adds the 14 years after the *churban*, one arrives at 25 only because the *churban* year itself is excluded from both counts. Had it been included in either, the result would be 26. This confirms the logic of Ravina's solution.

### One Year Dual Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ שָׁלֹשׁ גָּלִיּוֹת הֲוַי? גָּלוּ בְּשֶׁבַע לְכִיבּוּשׁ יְהוֹיָקִים, שֶׁהִיא שְׁמוֹנֶה לִנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר, גָּלוּ בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה לְכִיבּוּשׁ יְהוֹיָקִים, שֶׁהִיא תְּשַׁע עֶשְׂרֵה לִנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Do you hold** that **there were three** separate **exiles,** one in the seventh or eighth year, a second one in the eighteenth year, and a third one in the nineteenth year? In truth there were only two exiles: **They were exiled in the seventh** year **from** Nebuchadnezzar's **subjugation of Jehoiakim, which was the eighth** year **of Nebuchadnezzar's** reign. **They were** then **exiled** a second time, when the Temple was destroyed, **in the eighteenth** year **from the subjugation of Jehoiakim, which was** actually in **the nineteenth** year **of Nebuchadnezzar's** reign. Consequently, there were eleven years from the first exile to the destruction of the Temple, not counting the year of the exile, and another fourteen years until the verse in Ezekiel, which equals a total of twenty-five years.

The *gemara* concludes by clarifying what may seem paradoxical: year 18 and year 19 are not two distinct years but a single year designated by two different names depending on the reference point of the count. The same applies to year 7 and year 8. The tradition speaks of two exiles — the exile of *Yehoyachin* and the *churban* — not of four separate deportations. Each is simply recorded under two concurrent numbering systems that were in use simultaneously.

## Second Temple Shmita Year

### Second Temple Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בַּשְּׁנִיָּה. וּשְׁנִיָּה בְּמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית מִי מִשְׁתְּחַס לַהּ? מִכְּדִי בַּיִת שֵׁנִי כַּמָּה קָם? אַרְבַּע מְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים. אַרְבַּע מְאָה — תְּמָנְיָא יוֹבֵלֵי, אַרְבֵּסְרֵי — תְּרֵי שָׁבוּעַ, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ שֵׁית, הָוֵה לֵיהּ בְּשִׁיתָּא בַּשָּׁבוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the *baraita*, which teaches: When the Temple was destroyed for the first time, that day was the Ninth of Av; and it was the conclusion of Shabbat; and it was the year after a Sabbatical Year; **and likewise,** the same happened **when the Second** Temple was destroyed. The Gemara asks: **Can you find** such a possibility, that the **Second** Temple was destroyed **in the year after a Sabbatical Year? Now,** for **how many** years did **the Second Temple stand?** It stood for **420** years. **Four hundred** years include exactly **eight Jubilees,** as the Jubilee cycle is fifty years. An additional **fourteen** years consist of **two Sabbatical cycles.** There are **six** years **remaining** during which the Temple stood, which means that the last year **was the sixth** year **of the Sabbatical cycle,** and therefore when the Temple was destroyed the following year it was a Sabbatical Year, not the year after the Sabbatical Year.

Having established that the First *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed on *motzei shevi'it*, the *gemara* turns to the second part of the *braita*: *v'chen bashniyah* — the same was true of the Second *Beit HaMikdash*. *V'shniyah b'motzei shevi'is mi mishtachas lah?* — can one actually demonstrate that the Second *Beit HaMikdash* was also destroyed in the first year of a *shemita* cycle?

### 420 Year Duration

The *gemara* begins from a received tradition: the Second *Beit HaMikdash* stood for 420 years — *arba me'os v'esrim*. This is simply a *mesorah*, not derived here from external historical sources.

### Eight Yovels Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: ״שְׁנַת חֲמִשִּׁים עוֹלָה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן״, אַיְיתִי תְּמָנְיָא מִתְּמָנְיָא יוֹבֵלֵי, וְהָנֵי שֵׁית — הָוֵי אַרְבֵּיסַר, אִישְׁתְּכַח דִּבְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית חֲרוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita* stated? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **the fiftieth year is counted for here and for there,** i.e., it is both the Jubilee Year and the first year of the next Sabbatical cycle and Jubilee cycle, and therefore each Jubilee cycle is only forty-nine years rather than fifty years. Consequently, when calculating the number of years of the Sabbatical and Jubilee cycles until the destruction of the Second Temple, **bring** an additional **eight** years **from the eight** complete Jubilee cycles during which the Temple stood. The eight years **and those six** years that remained according to the previous calculation **equal fourteen** years, which are two complete Sabbatical cycles. Therefore, **it is found that** the Second Temple **was destroyed in the year following the Sabbatical** Year.

Now the *gemara* counts by *yovel* units of 50. Since the *shemita* cycle includes a *yovel* year that stands apart from the regular seven-year rotation, one must count in units of 50 to properly determine where in the cycle the destruction falls. 420 years contains eight complete *yovels* (8 × 50 = 400), leaving 20 years. Those 20 years divide into two complete *shemita* cycles of 7 years each (14 years), with 6 years remaining. *Pashu lehu shesh* — 6 years are left over. The *churban* of the Second *Beit HaMikdash* would therefore fall in year 6 of a *shemita* cycle — *havu lei beshisa beshavu'a* — not in year 1. The *braita*'s claim appears to be falsified.

### Rabbi Yehuda 49 Years

*Ha mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the *gemara* proposes that this portion of the calculation follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *shnas chamishim oleh lekan ulekan*: the *yovel* year is simultaneously year 50 of the outgoing cycle and year 1 of the incoming cycle. Under this view, one does not count separate standalone *yovel* years; every year falls within a standard seven-year *shemita* cycle, and 420 years divided evenly by 7 yields exactly 60 cycles. The *churban* thus falls perfectly on *motzei shemita* — the end of year 7 and the threshold of year 1. Moreover, if one applies Rabbi Yehuda's view retroactively to the eight *yovel* years that the first method treated as standalone: those eight years, when counted as part of a cycle, add eight extra years to what was previously year 6 of the cycle, yielding year 14 — which, in a 7-year cycle, is equivalent to year 7, i.e., *motzei shemita*. So Rabbi Yehuda's approach makes the Second *Beit HaMikdash* calculation work.

### Method Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ! דְּתַנְיָא: שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר יוֹבֵלוֹת מָנוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִשֶּׁנִּכְנְסוּ לָאָרֶץ וְעַד שֶׁיָּצְאוּ, וְאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ מָנוּ, שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן — נִמְצָא בַּיִת חָרֵב בִּתְחִילַּת יוֹבֵל, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא ״בְּאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה אַחַר אֲשֶׁר הֻכְּתָה הָעִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But **if** one explains the *baraita* in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** the first clause of the *baraita* is problematic, as **you do not find** that **the first** time the Temple was destroyed was in the year after the Sabbatical Year. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The Jewish people counted seventeen Jubilees from when they entered Eretz** Yisrael **until they left,** when the First Temple was destroyed. **And you cannot say** that **they counted from the time they entered, because if you say this, the result is** that **the Temple was destroyed at the beginning of the Jubilee** cycle, **and you do not find** that the Jubilee Year was **"in the fourteenth year after the city was smitten"** (Ezekiel 40:1).

But the *gemara* now points out a glaring contradiction. *Ei Rabbi Yehuda berishona lo mishkachas lei* — if the answer for the Second *Beit HaMikdash* requires Rabbi Yehuda's 49-year cycle, then Rabbi Yehuda's method will not work for the First *Beit HaMikdash*. For the First *Beit HaMikdash*, the *gemara* had just demonstrated on the previous *amud* that a *yovel* year occurred fourteen years after the *churban*, which only makes sense on the assumption of 50-year cycles. Under Rabbi Yehuda's system of pure 49-year cycles, the First *Beit HaMikdash* calculation breaks down. The two halves of the same *braita* thus appear to require contradictory assumptions about the length of the *yovel* cycle. This is the central tension that the remainder of the *daf* must resolve.

## Historical Interruption…

### 17 Yovels Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא צֵא מֵהֶם שֶׁבַע שֶׁכִּיבְּשׁוּ, וְשֶׁבַע שֶׁחִילְּקוּ, וְאַתָּה מוֹצֵא ״בְּאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה אַחַר אֲשֶׁר הֻכְּתָה הָעִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, remove from them** the **seven** years **when they conquered** the land **and** the **seven** years **when they divided** the land, as they did not start counting the first Jubilee cycle until after those events. **And you** then **find** that the Jubilee Year was **"in the fourteenth year after the city was smitten."**

To understand the difficulty more precisely, the *gemara* invokes a *braita*: *shva esrei yovlot manu Yisrael mishenichnesu la'aretz v'ad sheyatzu* — Israel counted seventeen *yovlot* from when they entered the land until they left it. Seventeen times 50 is 850. This number can be verified: the verse in *Melakhim* states that the *Beit HaMikdash* was built 480 years after the Exodus, meaning 440 years after the entry into the land. The First *Beit HaMikdash* stood for 410 years, so the total is 440 plus 410 = 850 years from entry into the land to the *churban*. This matches seventeen *yovlot* of 50 years each exactly. But the *gemara* notes a complication: *v'ee ata yachol lomar misha'ah shenichnesu manu* — one cannot say they began counting from the moment of entry, because if they had, the seventeen cycles would place the *churban* precisely at the beginning of a *yovel* year, contradicting the *pasuk* we cited earlier showing that the *yovel* fell fourteen years *after* the *churban*.

### Conquest Division Years

The resolution is: *tzei meihem sheva shekavshu v'sheva shechilku* — subtract from those 850 years the seven years of conquest and the seven years in which the land was divided among the tribes. Only after the land was fully conquered and apportioned among the tribes did it attain the status of *Eretz Yisrael* in the halakhic sense required for *yovel* to apply. So the *yovel* count began only fourteen years after entry. This introduces an automatic 14-year offset, which is precisely why a *yovel* year fell fourteen years *after* the *churban* rather than at the time of the *churban* itself: the cycles were already shifted by 14 years from the outset.

This resolution, however, works perfectly only under 50-year cycles. Under Rabbi Yehuda's 49-year cycles, the seventeen *yovlot* yield 17 extra years that were previously being counted as standalone *yovel* years but are now folded into the seven-year rotation. *Ika shivsrei mishivsrei yovlei* — you have those 17 surplus years, and they push the *shemita* position of the *churban* three years later in the cycle, to year 3 or year 4. The mathematics no longer support the *braita*'s claim that the First *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed on *motzei shevi'it*, and the *pasuk* about the 14-year *yovel* offset also ceases to function. So the contradiction stands: the Second *Beit HaMikdash* seems to require Rabbi Yehuda, but the First *Beit HaMikdash* does not.

### Sancheriv Hiatus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ שְׁנֵי דְּאַגְלִינְהוּ סַנְחֵרִיב, עַד דַּאֲתָא יִרְמְיָה אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ — לָא קָחָשֵׁיב לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Throughout **those years from** when **Sennacherib exiled** the ten tribes **until Jeremiah came and returned them** to their land, **they did not count** the Jubilee cycle, as the Jubilee applies only when all twelve tribes are in their ancestral lands. When the exiles returned, a new Jubilee cycle was started, and the Temple was destroyed thirty-six years later, in the year after a Sabbatical Year.

The *gemara* offers its first resolution: *hanehu shani d'aglinhu Sancheriv ad d'asi Yirmiyahu v'ahadrinhu lo kachashiv lehu* — the years during which Sancheriv exiled the ten tribes, until Yirmiyahu restored them, are not counted in the *shemita* cycle. The *halacha* requires that *yovel* is only observed when all of the inhabitants of the land are present — *kol yoshveha aleha*. When Sancheriv sent the ten tribes into exile, the condition for *yovel* no longer obtained, and the *shemita* cycle effectively went on hiatus.

### Yirmiyahu Mission Reset

According to the *gemara*, there is a *midrash* that when the *Sefer Torah* was found in the *Beit HaMikdash* during the reign of Yoshiyahu and he sought prophetic guidance, he did not turn to Yirmiyahu — because Yirmiyahu was away on a mission. That mission was to bring back the ten tribes from the exile of Sancheriv so that *yovel* could be reinstated. It was only when Yirmiyahu completed this mission and the exiled tribes returned — or were at least brought back into some relationship with the land — that the counting of *shemita* and *yovel* resumed. Rashi works out the mathematics: from the time of Yoshiyahu's restoration and Yirmiyahu's mission until the *churban* is an exact number of *shemita* cycles, landing at year 1, with the *yovel* falling fourteen years later as the *pasuk* indicates. So according to this answer, using Rabbi Yehuda's 49-year cycles and accounting for the hiatus under Sancheriv, both the First and Second *Beit HaMikdash* calculations can be made to work.

### Rabbis 50-Year Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם רַבָּנַן, וְכִי קָתָנֵי ״וְכֵן בַּשְּׁנִיָּה״ — אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a second answer to its question about the statement of the *baraita* which equates the destructions of the First Temple and the Second Temple. **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually,** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and when it teaches: And likewise,** the same happened **when the Second** Temple was destroyed, it is not asserting that the Temple was destroyed in a year after a Sabbatical Year, because in fact the Second Temple was destroyed during a Sabbatical Year. Rather, it is referring **to the other** details the *baraita* provides with regard to the timing of the destruction, i.e., that it occurred on the Ninth of Av and on a Sunday.

Alternatively — *iba'is eima* — one may simply maintain the Rabbis' position of 50-year cycles throughout. Under this reading, the First *Beit HaMikdash* calculation works seamlessly, as we demonstrated. As for the *braita*'s statement that the same was true for the Second *Beit HaMikdash* — *v'chi tana v'chen bashniyah, ashara* — the *braita* does not mean that every detail was identical. It means that many things were similar: the date of *Tisha B'Av*, the fact that it was a Sunday, and so forth. But it does not imply that the Second *Beit HaMikdash* was also destroyed in the first year of a *shemita* cycle. Under 50-year cycles and 420 years of the Second *Beit HaMikdash*, the destruction fell in year 6 of the cycle — that is simply not the same. *Hachi nami mistabra* — and in fact this makes sense, as the *gemara* is about to demonstrate.

## Second Temple Differences

### No Yehoyariv Watch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, מִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ שֶׁל יְהוֹיָרִיב בַּשְּׁנִיָּה מִי הֲוַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** to conclude that not all of the details pertaining to the destruction of the First Temple also apply to the destruction of the Second Temple. **As, if you do not say so,** then with regard to the statement that the destruction occurred during the week of the priestly watch of Jehoiarib, **was the priestly watch of Jehoiarib present during** the time of **the Second** Temple?

The *gemara* supports the view that the Second *Beit HaMikdash* destruction could not have precisely mirrored the First by raising a separate point: *mishmarto shel Yehoyariv bashniya mi hava?* — since when was there a watch of *Yehoyariv* in the Second *Beit HaMikdash*?

### Four Mishmarot Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אַרְבַּע מִשְׁמָרוֹת עָלוּ מִן הַגּוֹלָה — יְדַעְיָה וְחָרִים פַּשְׁחוּר וְאִימֵּר, עָמְדוּ נְבִיאִים שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶם וְחִלְּקוּם לְעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע מִשְׁמָרוֹת, בְּלָלוּם וּנְתָנוּם בַּקַּלְפִּי, בָּא יְדַעְיָה וְנָטַל חֶלְקוֹ וְחֵלֶק חֲבֵירָיו שֵׁשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the watch of Jehoiarib did not exist during the Second Temple era? As it is taught: Only **four priestly watches ascended from the** Babylonian **exile,** while the other twenty stayed in Babylonia. And these are the watches who returned: The descendants of **Jedaiah, Harim, Pashḥur, and Immer. The prophets among** those who returned **arose and divided** these four families **into twenty-four watches.** They achieved this as follows: They wrote the names of these new twenty-four watches on pieces of paper, **mixed them up, and put them in a receptacle [*kalpi*]** from which lots were drawn. A representative from the family of **Jedaiah came and drew his portion and the lot of** five **other** watches, for a total of **six.**

As a *braita* teaches: *arba mishmarot alu min hagola* — of the twenty-four *mishmarot* of *kohanim*, only four families returned from the exile in Babylon. Those four were *Yedaya, Charim, Pashchur*, and *Imar*. The difficulty in persuading Jews to leave the relative comfort of exile for the rebuilding of the land meant that the vast majority of priestly families did not return.

### Lottery Redistribution

The prophets of that era — *Chaggai, Zecharya*, and *Malachi* — wanted to preserve the institution of twenty-four *mishmarot*. They therefore took the slips designating all twenty-four *mishmarot*, placed them in a vessel, and conducted a lottery: *bilulum v'nasnu bekalpi*.

### Yedaya Represents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא חָרִים וְנָטַל חֶלְקוֹ וְחֵלֶק חֲבֵירָיו שֵׁשׁ, וְכֵן פַּשְׁחוּר וְכֵן אִימֵּר. הִתְקִינוּ נְבִיאִים שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶם שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ יְהוֹיָרִיב רֹאשׁ מִשְׁמָרוֹת עוֹלֶה — לֹא יִדָּחֶה יְדַעְיָה מִמְּקוֹמוֹ, אֶלָּא יְדַעְיָה עִיקָּר וִיהוֹיָרִיב טָפֵל לוֹ, אֶלָּא אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Harim came and** also **drew his portion and the lot of** five **other** watches, for a total of **six. And likewise Pashḥur, and likewise Immer. The prophets among them stipulated that even** if the descendants of **Jehoiarib,** who originally **headed the priestly watches, would ascend** to Eretz Yisrael, **Jedaiah would not be demoted from its place** as the first of the watches. **Rather,** the watch of **Jedaiah** would retain **precedence, and Jehoiarib** would be **subordinate to it.** The Gemara concludes: **Rather,** when the *baraita* equates the details pertaining to the destruction of the two Temples, it is referring **to other** details, i.e., that both were destroyed on the Ninth of Av and on a Sunday, but it is not equating them with regard to all the details.

*Yedaya* drew first and obtained his own name — which Rashi regards as a miraculous occurrence — and then drew five additional slips, representing five of the *mishmarot* that had not returned. He would henceforth represent his own family and those five others. Each of the four returning families similarly drew themselves and five others, so that the institution of twenty-four *mishmarot* was maintained in structure if not in genealogy. The prophets then established: even if *Yehoyariv* — the head of all the *mishmarot* — were to return later and claim his rightful position, *lo yidacheh Yedaya mimkomo* — *Yedaya* would not be displaced from his place. *Yedaya ikar v'Yehoyariv tafel* — *Yedaya* is now primary; *Yehoyariv* is subordinate.

### Returnee Rights Loss

Those who failed to return when it mattered forfeited their institutional standing. A descendant of *Yehoyariv* who arrived years after the fact, however distinguished his lineage, would find that his family's slot was now permanently occupied.

### Similarities Not Identical

This demonstrates that the destruction of the Second *Beit HaMikdash* could not have been precisely identical to that of the First in every detail the *braita* enumerates. There was no *mishmar* of *Yehoyariv* as such in the Second *Beit HaMikdash*; one might argue that the *mishmar* then serving was *standing in for Yehoyariv*, but the *gemara* reads the *braita* literally and concludes that it was not the same. Similarly, the *shemita* cycle year was not the same. The *braita*'s phrase *v'chen bashniyah* means: in many significant respects the destruction was similar — the date of *Tisha B'Av*, the day of the week, the terrible appropriateness of the moment — but not that every detail was identical. It was *ashara*, approximate, similar in character but not a point-for-point replica.
