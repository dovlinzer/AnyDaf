# Yoma 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Terumat HaDeshen Debate

### Intro: Tanna Kamma vs R. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִלְבַּשׁ״? לְהָבִיא מִצְנֶפֶת וְאַבְנֵט לַהֲרָמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת בִּגְדֵי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, שֶׁכְּשֵׁירִין לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט. אָמַר רַבִּי: שְׁתֵּי תְשׁוּבוֹת בְּדָבָר, חֲדָא: דְּאַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לֹא זֶה הוּא אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט. וְעוֹד: בְּגָדִים שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמַּשְׁתָּ בָּהֶן קְדוּשָּׁה חֲמוּרָה, תִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן קְדוּשָּׁה קַלָּה?! אֶלָּא, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִלְבַּשׁ״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשְּׁחָקִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** that the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it was taught in a *baraita* that the Torah states: "And the priest shall put on his linen garment, and his linen trousers shall he put on his flesh" (Leviticus 6:3). The words "shall he put on" seem superfluous, since these same words were already stated earlier in the verse. Therefore, the Torah could have sufficed with saying: "And linen trousers **on his flesh." What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Shall he put on"?** This extra expression comes **to include** the donning of the **mitre and** the **belt,** which are not mentioned here explicitly, for **the removal** of the ash; this is the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Dosa says:** The extra expression comes **to include the** permissibility of the **High Priest's clothes** that he wears **on Yom Kippur,** which are linen garments identical to those of the common priest, to teach that **they are acceptable** to be used afterward by **common priests** in their service. In other words, the expression teaches that the High Priest's garments need not be permanently retired from service after Yom Kippur, unlike the opinion of another Sage, as will be explained below. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: There are two refutations** against Rabbi Dosa's interpretation: **One** is that **the belt of** the **High Priest** that he wears on Yom Kippur is made only of linen and **is not** identical to **the belt of** the **common priest,** which, in Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion, is made of wool and linen. Therefore, it is impossible for the High Priest's Yom Kippur garments to be used by a common priest. **And furthermore,** with regard to **garments that you used** to perform the services of **the most severe sanctity,** i.e., the services performed by the High Priest on Yom Kippur, can it be that **you will** then **use them** to perform **services of lesser sanctity** by a common priest? **Instead** of this, a different interpretation must be said. **What,** then, is the meaning when **the verse states** the superfluous words **"shall he put on"?** The phrase comes **to include worn out** garments, teaching that as long as they have not become tattered they may be used for Temple services.

The daf begins with the Gemara's declaration of *nima ketana'ei* — let us see whether this matter is connected to a *tannaitic* dispute. The issue at hand concerns a debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer regarding the *terumat hadeshen*, the priestly service of removing ashes from the altar.

By way of background: every morning, the *kohen* performed a ritual removal of a small amount of ashes from the top of the *mizbe'ach*, placing them beside the altar's ramp. This is the *terumat hadeshen* proper. Separately, the Torah states: *vefashat et begadav velavash begadim acherim vehotzi et hadeshen el michutz lamachaneh el makom tahor* — "he shall remove his garments and put on other garments, and carry the ashes outside the camp to a pure place." According to the simple reading of the *pesukim*, this is one continuous act. According to *Chazal*, however, these are two distinct acts. The daily morning ritual involves removing only a small amount. The second act — changing clothes and transporting the ashes outside the *mikdash* — was performed only when needed, when the accumulated ashes had to be cleared away, a kind of periodic removal.

### Two Acts of Ash Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִנִּיחָם שָׁם״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁטְּעוּנִין גְּנִיזָה. רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: רְאוּיִם הֵן לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהִנִּיחָם שָׁם״ — שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתַּמַּשׁ בָּהֶן יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with another *baraita* connected to this debate. With regard to the garments of the High Priest, the Torah states: "And Aaron shall go into the tent of meeting, and he shall take off the linen garments that he had put on when he went into the Sanctuary, **and shall leave them there"** (Leviticus 16:23). This verse **teaches that** the linen garments worn by the High Priest during the Yom Kippur service **require storing away,** i.e., they may not be used again. **Rabbi Dosa says:** They do not have to be stored away, because although they may not be used again by the High Priest on a subsequent Yom Kippur, **they are acceptable** for use **for a common priest. And what,** then, is the meaning when **the verse states: "And shall leave them there,"** which implies that they are not to be used again? It means **that** the High Priest himself **may not use them** on a **subsequent Yom Kippur** for service in the Holy of Holies; it does not mean that they may not be used at all.

This second act, the *hotza'at hadeshen*, is essentially a garbage removal: cleaning out the excess ashes from the *mizbe'ach*. The Torah's phrase *vefashat et begadav* — "he shall remove his garments" — introduces a change of clothing before carrying the ashes out.

What does he change into? The *tanna kamma* says he changes into other *bigdei kehunah* — priestly garments — simply of lesser quality. One would not wish to soil one's regular priestly garments with ash-removal work, so dedicated, less fine garments were set aside for this purpose. They are still *bigdei kehunah*, however, merely not the finest. This is a significant detail: if the clothing requirement is still *bigdei kehunah*, it strongly implies that this act is still an *avodah* of the *mikdash*. The question therefore poses itself in stark terms: is taking out the ashes an *avodah*?

### Garment Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא. לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: צְרִיכָא קְרָא לְרַבּוֹיֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא צְרִיכָא קְרָא לְרַבּוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: It is possible to say that this is **not** the subject of debate between these two *tanna'im*. Rather, **everyone** agrees that the removal of ashes **is** a bona fide Temple **service** requiring all four garments, **and here they disagree about** a different point, which is **this: One Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **a** derivation from the **verse is necessary** to **include** the mitre and belt, which are not mentioned explicitly in the verse. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Dosa, **holds that** since the removal of the ash is a bona fide Temple service it is obvious that all four garments are required, so a derivation from **a verse** to include the other two garments **is not necessary.** Accordingly, both *tanna'im* are in agreement that the removal of ash is a bona fide service and requires all four priestly garments.

This question of garments becomes the pivot of the debate. The *tanna kamma* holds that the *kohen* who performs the *hotza'at hadeshen* wears four *bigdei kehunah*, and that a *kohen* with a blemish — a *ba'al mum* — may not perform it. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, holds that even a *ba'al mum* may perform the ash removal. While Rabbi Eliezer does not directly address the garment question, his ruling about the *ba'al mum* carries an unmistakable implication: since a *ba'al mum* is disqualified only from performing a genuine *avodah*, allowing him to perform this act signals that Rabbi Eliezer does not regard it as an *avodah* in the full halachic sense.

### Is Ash Removal Avodah?

The central question thus crystallizes: is the *hotza'at hadeshen* — the occasional clearing of accumulated ashes — classified as an *avodah*? The *tanna kamma* appears to say yes: it requires four *bigdei kehunah* and an unblemished *kohen*. Rabbi Eliezer appears to say no: even a *ba'al mum* may perform it.

This *baraita* debate is then taken up by two *Amoraim*, Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, who raise a further question: does the same dispute apply not only to the *hotza'at hadeshen* but also to the daily morning ritual of the *terumat hadeshen* itself?

### Yochanan vs Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּהוֹצָאָה, כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בַּהֲרָמָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּהוֹצָאָה, אֲבָל בַּהֲרָמָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל עֲבוֹדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* taught that Rabbi Eliezer derived from the word other that blemished priests are eligible for the task of carrying out the ashes, while the first *tanna* derived a different teaching from those words. The Gemara clarifies the scope of the dispute between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Eliezer. **Reish Lakish said: Just as** there is **a dispute** between Rabbi Eliezer and the first *tanna* **with regard to carrying** the ashes **out** of the camp, **so too, there is a dispute with regard to the removal of the ashes** from the altar. Rabbi Eliezer maintains that the removal of the ashes may also be performed by blemished priests, while the first *tanna* disagrees. **But Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The **dispute** is only **with regard to carrying** the ashes **out** of the camp, **but all agree that the removal** of the ashes **is a** bona fide Temple **service** that cannot be performed by blemished priests.

One would naturally assume that the daily morning *terumat hadeshen* is universally regarded as an *avodah*. After all, a lottery — a *payis* — was held among the *kohanim* each morning specifically to determine who would perform it, suggesting considerable dignity and formal status. Rabbi Yochanan therefore reads the *tannaitic* dispute narrowly: the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer concerns only the *hotza'at hadeshen*, the occasional clearing-out. The morning *terumat hadeshen* is not in dispute; everyone agrees it is an *avodah*, requires four *bigdei kehunah*, and may not be performed by a *ba'al mum*.

Reish Lakish disagrees, and his position is considerably more radical.

### Reish Lakish Radical View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? אָמַר לָךְ: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, יֵשׁ לְךָ עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁירָה בִּשְׁנֵי כֵלִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason behind** the opinion **of Reish Lakish?** Reish Lakish could have **said to you: If it enters your mind** that the removal of the ashes **is a** bona fide Temple **service,** you are faced with the following difficulty: **Do you have** any Temple **service that may be performed with** only **two garments** rather than the full set of four vestments worn by the priests? In the Torah's description of the garments worn to remove the ashes it says: "And the priest shall put on his linen garment, and his linen trousers shall he put on his flesh" (Leviticus 6:3).

Reish Lakish argues that Rabbi Eliezer's leniency extends even to the daily morning *terumat hadeshen*. According to Reish Lakish's reading of Rabbi Eliezer, even that daily ritual — the one celebrated with a morning lottery — is not an *avodah*, and could therefore be performed by a *ba'al mum*. This is a striking claim. The entire elaborate *payis* procedure, with all its solemnity, would be surrounding an act that Rabbi Eliezer, in Reish Lakish's understanding, does not classify as a formal *avodah* at all.

### Two Garments = Not Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בְּכֻתּוֹנֶת וּמִכְנָסַיִם, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְמִצְנֶפֶת וְאַבְנֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what is the explanation for **Rabbi Yoḥanan's** opinion? In fact, the priest is required to wear all four priestly garments. **The Merciful One reveals** in the Torah that the priest must wear **the tunic and the trousers** like any other service so that one would not think that taking out the ashes may be performed in regular, non-sacred clothes. Once the Torah has made this point and mentioned these two specific garments, **the same is true** for the other two garments as well, i.e., **the mitre and the belt.**

Reish Lakish's proof derives from the *pesukim* themselves. The Torah describes the *terumat hadeshen* as follows: *velavash hakohen mido bad umichnesei bad yilbash al besaro* — "the *kohen* shall wear his linen tunic, and linen breeches shall he wear upon his flesh." The verse mentions only two garments: the linen cloak and the linen breeches. The belt and the turban are conspicuously absent. Reish Lakish reads this as significant: the Torah does not require the full four-garment set for the *terumat hadeshen*. Since a genuine *avodah* requires all four *bigdei kehunah*, the fact that only two are mentioned indicates this is not a full *avodah*.

The Gemara then cites a *baraita* (*nima ketana'ei*) that illuminates the debate. The *baraita* asks: why does the verse say *yilbash* ("he shall wear") in addition to *al besaro* ("upon his flesh")? Could it not simply have said *al besaro*? The word *yilbash* is redundant and must therefore teach something additional.

Rabbi Yehudah answers: *lehavi mitznefet ve-avneit leharama* — to include the turban and the belt in the *terumat hadeshen*. On his reading, the Torah mentions only two garments but the extra word *yilbash* teaches us that all four are required. This clearly implies that the *terumat hadeshen* is a genuine *avodah*.

Rabbi Dosa, however, offers a different interpretation of the same word *yilbash*. He reads it as permitting a *kohen hedyot* to wear the garments that had been worn by the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. The *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* wears the same four-white-garment ensemble: linen tunic, linen breeches, linen belt, and linen turban — identical to those of the ordinary *kohen*. Rabbi Dosa argues that the word *yilbash* implies reuse: these garments can be worn by another *kohen* for the *terumat hadeshen*.

*Rebbe* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) rejects this on two grounds. First, *de-avneito shel kohen gadol lo zeh hu avneito shel kohen hedyot* — the belt worn by the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* is pure linen, whereas the ordinary *kohen*'s belt is *kilayim* (a wool-and-linen blend, permitted in the Temple context). They are different garments and cannot simply be substituted. Second, *bigadim shenishtamesh bahen kedusha chamura teshamesh bahen kedusha kala* — garments used for a weightier level of sanctity (the *Yom Kippur* service of the *kohen gadol*) cannot then be used for a lighter level of sanctity (an ordinary daily service). *Rebbe* therefore rules that the *yilbash* teaches something else entirely: *lerabbot hashachakim* — even worn or faded garments remain valid for priestly service. The Torah does not insist on fresh, brand-new garments each time.

The Gemara then addresses the *pasuk* regarding after *Yom Kippur*, where it says *vehinicham sham* — "and he shall leave them there." *Rebbe* understands this as *melamed sheteunim genizah* — the garments of the *kohen gadol* used on *Yom Kippur* must be stored away (buried) and never reused, not for another *Yom Kippur* and not for any other service. Rabbi Dosa, who permits reuse, understands *vehinicham sham* as teaching only that these garments may not be used for a *subsequent* *Yom Kippur*. Each *Yom Kippur* demands its own set of new garments — the significance is in the uniqueness of each *Yom Kippur*, not in the sanctity of the garments as such. The garments themselves, on this reading, carry no inherent prohibition against reuse in ordinary priestly service throughout the year.

Having surveyed these opinions, the Gemara asks: does this *baraita* debate reflect a disagreement about whether *terumat hadeshen* requires all four garments? Perhaps Rabbi Dosa and *Rebbe* — who deploy the word *yilbash* for purposes other than teaching the four-garment requirement — hold that only two garments are needed, consistent with Reish Lakish's position that it is not a full *avodah*.

The Gemara rejects this inference decisively: *dekulei alma avodah hi* — everyone agrees it is an *avodah*. The debate between Rabbi Yehudah on the one hand and Rabbi Dosa and *Rebbe* on the other is not about whether four garments are required — they all agree four garments are required — but only about whether the word *yilbash* is needed to teach this. According to Rabbi Yehudah, without *yilbash* one might have thought two garments suffice, and *yilbash* is therefore needed to teach us four. According to Rabbi Dosa and *Rebbe*, no verse is needed for this; it is obvious that a full *avodah* requires all four garments, and therefore *yilbash* must be teaching something else — either the reusability of the *kohen gadol*'s garments (Rabbi Dosa) or the validity of worn garments (*Rebbe*).

### Tosafot on Reish Lakish

*Tosafot* presses the implications of Reish Lakish's position further. According to Reish Lakish, it is not merely that *Rabbi Eliezer* regards *terumat hadeshen* as a non-*avodah*; rather, Reish Lakish holds that even according to the *tanna kamma*, the *terumat hadeshen* does not require all four *bigdei kehunah*. Since only two are required, it cannot be a full *avodah* by anyone's reckoning. The entire debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer, on this reading, is only about whether it is appropriate to allow a *ba'al mum* to perform the act — not about whether the act is technically classified as an *avodah*. Even the *tanna kamma* would agree that technically, a non-*kohen* could perform it; the *tanna kamma*'s concern is only that allowing a *ba'al mum* to do so simply *pasht nisht* — it is unseemly, even if not formally prohibited.

*Tosafot* notes that this raises a related question: if it is not an *avodah*, why does the *kohen* wear *bigdei kehunah* at all? This leads into a separate *sugya* about whether *bigdei kehunah nitnu lehanos bahem o lo nitnu lehanos bahem* — whether priestly garments may be used for purposes other than formal *avodah*. That discussion, while connected, need not be pursued here.

### Positions Compared

To summarize the landscape of opinions: Rabbi Yochanan reads the *tannaitic* dispute as narrowly as possible. The daily morning *terumat hadeshen* is uncontroversially an *avodah* — everyone agrees on this, it requires an unblemished *kohen* and all four *bigdei kehunah*. The only debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer concerns the *hotza'at hadeshen*, the occasional clearing-out, where the *tanna kamma* calls it an *avodah* and Rabbi Eliezer does not.

Reish Lakish offers the most radical view. In his reading, Rabbi Eliezer's position extends even to the daily morning *terumat hadeshen*, which he too regards as a non-*avodah*. More than that, *Tosafot* explains that according to Reish Lakish, even the *tanna kamma* does not regard *terumat hadeshen* as a full *avodah* in the sense requiring four garments — the entire dispute is only about the appropriateness of allowing a *ba'al mum* to perform an act that, technically speaking, is not a formal Temple service at all.

The Gemara, through its analysis of the *baraita*, restores the natural order: *dekulei alma avodah hi*. Everyone agrees the *terumat hadeshen* is an *avodah*; the word *yilbash* is deployed differently by different *tannaim* precisely because they all take for granted that all four garments are required.

## Amount of Ashes

### Rabbi Avin's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אָבִין: תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן בְּכַמָּה? מִתְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר יָלְפִינַן לַהּ, אוֹ מִתְּרוּמַת מִדְיָן יָלְפִינַן לַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״וְהֵרִים״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״וְהֵרִים״. מָה לְהַלָּן — בְּקוּמְצוֹ, אַף כָּאן — בְּקוּמְצוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Avin raised a dilemma: How much** ash must be removed in order to fulfill the mitzva of **removal of the ashes? Do we derive it from the *teruma* of the tithe,** the portion that the Levite sets aside for the priest, in which case one hundredth of the total is separated, **or do we derive it from the donations** that were set aside from the spoils of the war with **Midian, where** one five-hundredth was taken from the spoils of war (see Numbers 31:28)? **Come** and **hear** a teaching with regard to this dilemma. **As Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** in a *baraita* that **it is stated here: "And he shall take up** the ashes" (Leviticus 6:3), **and it is said elsewhere,** with regard to a meal-offering: **"And he shall take up** a handful of the choice flour of the meal-offering" (Leviticus 6:8). **Just as there,** the amount he removes is **a handful** of flour, **so too, here,** he removes **a handful** of ash. The amount of ashes removed from the altar is therefore not a fixed percentage of the total ash.

The Gemara now pivots from the question of the *avodah*'s status to a different question about the *terumat hadeshen*: *terumat hadeshen bekamah* — how much ash is actually removed in the daily morning ritual? This question applies specifically to the daily ritual, not the periodic clearing-out; when the altar had to be cleaned of accumulated ashes, the *kohen* simply removed as much as was necessary. But the morning *terumat hadeshen* involved a precise, defined quantity. Rabbi Avin poses the question.

### Proposed Sources

Three potential sources are proposed, each yielding a different quantity. The first is *terumat ma'aser* — the *terumah* that the *levi'im* separate from their *ma'aser*, which constitutes one-tenth of one-tenth, a tithe of the tithe. Perhaps the morning *terumat hadeshen* similarly involves one-tenth of the ashes on the altar. The second proposed source is *terumat Midyan*, the allocation set aside from the spoils of the war against Midyan, which was apportioned at one-fiftieth for one group and one-five-hundredth for another. Perhaps the ash removal follows one of these fractions. *Tosafot* notes that ordinary *terumah* (the standard separation from grain) and *challah* are not considered, since those carry no fixed proportion — one may give more or less — whereas the above two sources involve specific, fixed quantities, making them more plausible analogues.

### Kometz Measure

The resolution is provided by a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya. The Torah uses the word *veheirim* — "he shall lift" — in the context of the *terumat hadeshen*, and the same word *veheirim* appears in connection with the *kometz* of the *mincha* offering, where the *kohen* takes a handful from the flour. By means of a *gezerah shavah*, the measure for the ash removal is derived from the *kometz* of the *mincha*: just as the *kohen* takes a handful (*kometz*) of the *mincha*, so too he removes a handful's worth of ash in the morning *terumat hadeshen*. As Rashi notes, since one cannot plunge a hand into the still-hot coals and embers of the altar fire, the *kohen* estimates the equivalent of a *kometz* — according to Rashi, at least a *kometz* — and removes that amount, placing it beside the ramp.

## Four Avodat Categories

### Rav's Four Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אַרְבַּע עֲבוֹדוֹת זָר חַיָּיב עֲלֵיהֶן מִיתָה: זְרִיקָה, וְהַקְטָרָה, וְנִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם, וְנִיסּוּךְ הַיַּיִן. וְלֵוִי אָמַר: אַף תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן. וְכֵן תָּנֵי לֵוִי בְּמַתְנִיתֵיהּ: אַף תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav said:** Although a non-priest may not perform any Temple service, **there are** only **four** Temple **services** for which **a non-priest is liable to** receive the punishment of **death** by God's hand for doing so. They are: **Sprinkling** sacrificial blood on the altar, **and burning** incense or parts of sacrificial animals on the altar, **and** pouring out **the water libation** on the altar on the festival of *Sukkot*, **and** pouring out **the wine libation** on the altar. **And Levi said:** This is true **also** for **the removal of the ashes. And similarly, Levi taught in his** collection of ***baraitot*: The removal of ashes** is **also** included among those services for which a non-priest incurs the death penalty if he performs them.

Having established that the *terumat hadeshen* is a genuine *avodah*, the Gemara now moves to a broader and consequential question: not merely whether ash removal is an *avodah*, but what category of *avodah* it belongs to. Rav introduces a classification: *arba avodos zar chayav aleihem mita* — there are four Temple services for which a non-*kohen* (*zar*) who performs them incurs *heavenly* death (*mita bidei shamayim*). The Torah states: *vehazar hakarev yumat* — "the foreigner who draws close shall die." While it is clear that a *zar* may not perform any Temple service at all, there is a distinction between lesser violations (which are forbidden but do not carry the death penalty) and the most weighty category of violations that the verse specifically assigns a capital sanction.

### The Four Listed

Rav's four are: *zerikat hadam* (throwing the blood of a sacrifice against the altar), *haktarah* (burning — whether limbs of an *olah*, the innards of any *korban*, or the burning of incense), *nisuch hamayim* (the water libation performed on *Sukkot*), and *nisuch hayayin* (the wine libation that accompanied any *korban* requiring a *mincha*). These four, and only these four, carry the sanction of death for a *zar* who performs them.

### Levi Adds HaDeshen

Levi disagrees with Rav's list and adds a fifth: *af terumat hadeshen* — even the daily morning ash removal. This is a striking position. Earlier in the *sugya*, Reish Lakish had argued that a non-*kohen* could technically perform the *terumat hadeshen*. Now Levi goes to the opposite extreme: not only is *terumat hadeshen* an *avodah* (consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's view, which the Gemara endorsed), but it belongs to the most severe category, such that a *zar* who performs it is deserving of death. As the Gemara notes, Levi also taught this in a *baraita*: *af terumat hadeshen* is included among the *avodot* for which a *zar* incurs *mita*.

## Rav's Two Criteria

### Avodah Tammah Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְרַב — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ אִתְּךָ תִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת כְּהוּנַּתְכֶם לְכׇל דְּבַר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וּלְמִבֵּית לַפָּרוֹכֶת וַעֲבַדְתֶּם עֲבוֹדַת מַתָּנָה אֶתֵּן אֶת כְּהוּנַּתְכֶם וְהַזָּר הַקָּרֵב יוּמָת״. ״עֲבוֹדַת מַתָּנָה״ — וְלֹא עֲבוֹדַת סִילּוּק, ״וַעֲבַדְתֶּם״ — עֲבוֹדָה תַּמָּה, וְלֹא עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason for Rav's** opinion? **As it is written: "And you and your sons with you shall keep your priesthood in everything pertaining to the altar and to that within the veil; and you shall serve; I give you the priesthood as a service of gift; and the common man that draws near shall be put to death"** (Numbers 18:7). Rav interprets this verse as follows: **"A service of gift"** indicates a service that involves giving, i.e., placing something on the altar, **and not a service** that involves **removal** from the altar, to the exclusion of removing the ashes. **"And you shall serve [*va'avadtem*]"** is interpreted as referring to **a service that is complete [*avoda tamma*]** on its own, such as sprinkling the blood, **and not** a service that is not complete, i.e., **a service that** is only a preparatory step and **has another service after it** that completes its purpose, such as slaughtering the animal or collecting its blood, which are only preparatory steps leading up to the sprinkling of the blood on the altar.

The Gemara now probes the reasoning behind these positions. *Mai ta'ama d'Rav* — what is Rav's reason for limiting the capital category to four and excluding *terumat hadeshen*?

The answer is built on two separate *derashot* from the same *pasuk*: *ve-atah u-vanecha itcha tishmeru et kehunaschem lechol devar hamizbe'ach umibeis laparochet va'avadetem avodat matanah etein et kehunaschem vehazar hakarev yumat* — "And you and your sons with you shall guard your priesthood for every matter of the altar and for what is within the curtain, and you shall serve — a service of gift I give your priesthood — and the stranger who draws near shall die."

The word *va'avadetem* — "and you shall serve" — yields the first criterion through a *derashah*: *avodah tammah* — a completing service, one that is the final stage of a process. This excludes interim services. One might ask: surely *kabalat hadam* (receiving the blood) and *holachat hadam* (carrying the blood to the altar) are also services that require a *kohen* — why are they not on the list? The answer is that those are intermediate stages. Only the final act — the act after which nothing further remains to be done with that element — qualifies as *avodah tammah* and carries the capital sanction for a *zar*. Throwing the blood on the altar is the final stage of the blood service; burning the sacrificial portions is the final stage of the fire service; the libations are final acts unto themselves.

### Placing Not Removing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי — רַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא ״לְכׇל דְּבַר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. וְרַב — הַהוּא לְאֵתוֹיֵי שֶׁבַע הַזָּאוֹת שֶׁבִּפְנִים וְשֶׁבִּמְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what is the reason for the opinion of **Levi?** Why does he include the removal of ashes? According to him, **the Merciful One includes** this service by adding **"in everything pertaining to the altar,"** which teaches that all actions performed on the altar, including the removal of ashes, are significant and are prohibited to a non-priest on pain of death. The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rav** learn from the phrase "in everything pertaining to the altar"? The Gemara answers: According to him, the word "everything" in **that** phrase comes **to include the seven sprinklings that are** performed **inside** the Sanctuary, when the blood of certain offerings is sprinkled on the veil of the Holy of Holies, **and** the seven sprinklings of oil **of the leper,** which are also performed inside the Sanctuary. Rav learns from the word "everything" that if a non-priest were to perform any of these actions he would be liable to receive the death penalty, despite the fact that they are not performed on the altar.

The second criterion is derived from the words *avodat matanah* — "a service of giving/placing." The play is on the root *natan* — to give, to place. The *derashah*: *matanah litein* — an *avodah* that involves placing something. *Velo avodat siluk* — and not an *avodah* that involves removing something. The *terumat hadeshen*, though it may satisfy the first criterion (it is a final act with nothing further required of those ashes), fails the second: it is an act of removal, not of placement.

### HaDeshen Is Siluk

Rav therefore holds that *terumat hadeshen* does not belong to the capital category, not because it lacks finality but because it is *avodat siluk* — a service of removal — rather than *avodat matanah* — a service of placing. Both criteria must be met: the service must be a concluding act (*avodah tammah*) and it must involve placing something, not removing it (*avodat matanah velo avodat siluk*). *Terumat hadeshen* satisfies only one of the two; it therefore stands outside the capital category.

## Levi's Position

### All Altar Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי — נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״דְּבַר״ וְ״כׇל דְּבַר״. וְרַב — ״דְּבַר״ וְ״כׇל דְּבַר״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **Levi** derive these cases? The Gemara answers: **He derives** them **from** the superfluous wording of the text. As the entire phrase **"pertaining** to the altar" is superfluous, he derives from this the inclusion of the removal of the ashes. Additionally, the expression **"everything pertaining"** implies a further inclusion, from which he derives the internal sprinklings mentioned above. **And** what does **Rav** learn from this superfluous wording? Rav does **not derive** anything particular from the distinction between the expressions **"pertaining** to the altar" and **"everything pertaining** to the altar."

Levi, who includes *terumat hadeshen* in the capital category, reads the *pasuk* differently. He focuses on the phrase *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* — "for every matter of the altar." For Levi, this phrase is expansive: it encompasses all final-stage Temple services relating to the altar, whether they involve placing or removing. *Terumat hadeshen* is unquestionably connected to the altar and is unquestionably a concluding act — and that, according to Levi, is sufficient.

### Scope Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״לְכׇל דְּבַר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ — כָּלַל, ״עֲבוֹדַת מַתָּנָה״ — פָּרַט, כְּלָל וּפְרָט. אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט: עֲבוֹדַת מַתָּנָה — אִין, עֲבוֹדַת סִילּוּק — לָא! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּלְמִבֵּית לַפָּרוֹכֶת וַעֲבַדְתֶּם״, ״אֶל מִבֵּית לַפָּרוֹכֶת״ הוּא דַּעֲבוֹדַת מַתָּנָה וְלֹא עֲבוֹדַת סִילּוּק. הָא בַּחוּץ — אֲפִילּוּ עֲבוֹדַת סִילּוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about Levi's position: **But say** that the verse should be interpreted as follows: **"In everything pertaining"** is a **generalization,** and **"a service of gift"** is a **specification,** indicating a case of **a generalization** followed by **a specification.** One of the principles of hermeneutics states that in such cases, **the generalization includes only what** is mentioned explicitly **in the specification.** Following that rule, one would conclude: **A service of giving,** i.e., placing on the altar, **yes,** this is included, but **a service of removal** is **not** included. This presents a difficulty for Levi. The Gemara responds that **the verse states: "To that within the veil; and you shall serve;** I give you the priesthood as a service of gift" (Numbers 18:7), indicating that it is only with regard to services that are performed **within the veil,** i.e., in the Holy of Holies, **that there is** a distinction between services, and a non-priest who performs **services of giving** there, such as sprinkling the blood inside the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur, is subject to the death penalty, **but not** one who performs **services of removing** there, such as the removal of the censer from the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur. **This** leads to the conclusion that when it comes to services performed **outside** of the Holy of Holies, a non-priest would be liable if he performed any service, **even a service of removal,** such as the removal of ashes from the altar.

The Gemara presses the dispute. If Levi reads *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* as including all altar-related *avodot* whether of placement or removal, what, in Levi's view, does *avodat matanah* teach? For Rav, *avodat matanah* restricts the category to placement-*avodot*; if Levi does not use it for this restriction, what does he do with it?

Levi's answer: *avodat matanah* is not deployed to distinguish placing from removing; instead, its teaching is redirected to the phrase *umibeis laparochet* — "and within the curtain." Rav uses the phrase *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* to include things that are not done on the altar itself but are still final-stage placement-*avodot* — specifically, *sheva hazayot shebifnim veshebimetzora*, the seven sprinklings performed inside the *kodesh hakodashim* on *Yom Kippur* and the sprinklings of oil associated with the *metzora*'s purification. These involve placing (sprinkling) but not on the altar proper. According to Rav, the phrase *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* extends the capital category to include them even though they are not performed on the altar.

Levi, however, derives this extension from *devar vechol devar* — from the fact that the verse uses *kol* ("every") in the phrase *lechol devar hamizbe'ach*. The word *kol* includes the sprinklings not on the altar. This frees *avodat matanah* in Levi's reading to serve a different function: establishing that within the enclosed interior spaces (*mibeis laparochet*), only placement-*avodot*, not removal-*avodot*, carry the capital sanction. Outside, *terumat hadeshen*-style removal is included in the capital category; inside, only placement qualifies.

Rav, for his part, does not accept a *derashah* from *devar vechol devar* — the doubling of the noun to teach two inclusions. He does not expound the *kol* as a separate teaching; for him, *avodat matanah* does the entire work of excluding removal, and the verse's scope does not change depending on interior versus exterior location.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״וַעֲבַדְתֶּם״ נָמֵי, ״אֶל מִבֵּית לַפָּרוֹכֶת״ הוּא דַּעֲבוֹדָה תַּמָּה וְלֹא עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ עֲבוֹדָה. הָא בַּחוּץ — אֲפִילּוּ עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ עֲבוֹדָה! ״וַעֲבַדְתֶּם״ הֲדַר עָרְבֵיהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is **so** that the limitations of the verse apply only to those services performed in the Holy of Holies, one should say that the phrase: "**And you shall serve [*va'avadtem*],"** from which it is derived that one is liable only for a service that is complete [*avoda tamma*] on its own and not incomplete, should **also** be similarly limited to services performed **to what is within the veil.** In that case, the liability of the non-priest, which is limited to cases where he performs **a complete service and** does **not** apply if he performs **a service that** is only preparatory and **has another service after it** that completes its purpose, should apply only to services performed in the Holy of Holies. **But** for services performed **outside** the Holy of Holies, a non-priest should be liable **even** if it is **a service that** is incomplete and **has a service after it.** The Gemara answers: The phrase: "**And you shall serve [*va'avadtem*],"** which begins with the conjunction *vav*, meaning: And, indicates that **the verse goes back and combines** the service performed within the veil to services performed outside of it. This teaches that with regard to this *halakha* there is no difference between a service performed outside and a service performed inside.

The Gemara raises a formal objection to Levi's reading. If *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* (general) is followed by *avodat matanah* (specific), one should apply the hermeneutical principle of *klal ufrat*: the general statement is limited by the specific one that follows it. A general statement followed by a specification means only what the specification includes — namely, placement, not removal. How, then, can Levi insist that *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* includes removal-*avodot* like *terumat hadeshen*?

The Gemara's answer turns on the syntax of the *pasuk*. The phrase *umibeis laparochet va'avadetem avodat matanah* connects *avodat matanah* specifically to *mibeis laparochet* — the interior space within the curtain. It is only there — within the *heichal* and *kodesh hakodashim* — that the specification of *avodat matanah* (placement, not removal) applies. Outside, the general phrase *lechol devar hamizbe'ach* stands unqualified, and removal-*avodot* are included.

But the Gemara immediately presses: if Levi restricts *avodat matanah* to *mibeis laparochet*, should he not equally restrict *avodah tammah* to *mibeis laparochet*? If *va'avadetem* teaches *avodah tammah*, and Levi reads *va'avadetem* as connected specifically to *umibeis laparochet*, then the requirement of *avodah tammah* would only apply inside — meaning outside, even an intermediate-stage service could generate capital liability for a *zar*. That cannot be right.

The Gemara resolves this with a structural observation: *va'avadetem hadar arbei kra* — the *va'avadetem* doubles back and reconnects to the broader context. The *vav* (the conjunctive "and") at the beginning of *va'avadetem* ties it to the wider framework of the *pasuk*, maintaining *avodah tammah* as a universal criterion. The restriction of *avodat matanah* to *mibeis laparochet* is a bracketing specific to that phrase alone; *avodah tammah*, by virtue of the *vav*, remains a general requirement throughout.

The upshot is as follows. Rav has two criteria that apply universally: the service must be a concluding act (*avodah tammah*), and it must involve placement (*avodat matanah*), not removal (*avodat siluk*). Levi has one universal criterion — *avodah tammah* — and one criterion that applies only inside the roofed interior spaces: within the *heichal* and *kodesh hakodashim*, the service must also be placement, not removal. Outside, *avodah tammah* alone suffices, and *terumat hadeshen*, which is the final act of ash-removal from the altar, qualifies.

## Menorah & Avodah Test

### Menorah Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: עֲבוֹדַת סִילּוּק בַּהֵיכָל, מַהוּ? לִפְנִים מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ, אוֹ לְחוּץ מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ? הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: ״מִבֵּית״, ״וּלְמִבֵּית״.
>
> **Translation:** According to Levi, if a non-priest performs a service involving removal within the veil, i.e., in the Holy of Holies, he does not incur the death penalty, but if he performs a service involving removal outside, such as the removal of the ashes from the external altar, he is liable to the death penalty. **Rava raised a dilemma** relating to Levi's approach: **What** would be the *halakha* with regard to **a service of removal** performed **in the Sanctuary** chamber that is before the Holy of Holies, such as removing the burnt incense from the inner altar, or removing burnt wicks and leftover oil from the candelabrum? Do **we compare** such an act **to** a service performed **inside** the Holy of Holies, so that he would be exempt from the death penalty, **or** do **we compare** it **to the outer** service? Rava himself **went back and resolved** the dilemma: Had the Torah said only: **Within** the veil, it would have been understood that it is referring only to actions performed in the Holy of Holies. But since the Torah says: **And to what is within** the veil, the added conjunction: And, teaches that it is referring to something else besides the Holy of Holies, i.e., the Sanctuary.

The Gemara now tests the *avodah tammah* criterion by working through the preparation of the *menorah* step by step, asking at each stage whether that stage constitutes the concluding act.

Rava poses the question: *avodat siluk beheichal mahu* — what is the rule for a removal-*avodah* performed within the *heichal* (as distinct from the *kodesh hakodashim*)? Is the *heichal* treated like the interior (*mibeis laparochet*), requiring both *avodah tammah* and the placement criterion? Or is it treated like the exterior (*bachutz*), where *avodah tammah* alone suffices and even removal can generate liability? The Gemara concludes from the verse's language — *umibeis laparochet*, which can be read as the combined *umibeit* — that the interior criterion applies to both the *kodesh hakodashim* and the *heichal*. Anywhere under roof within the *mikdash* building, a removal-*avodah* would not qualify for the capital category.

This has a direct application to the *menorah*, whose service takes place in the *heichal*.

### Cleaning to Lighting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, זָר שֶׁסִּידֵּר אֶת הַשׁוּלְחָן לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא סִידּוּר בָּזִיכִין. סִידֵּר בָּזִיכִין לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא סִילּוּק וְהַקְטָרָה. זָר שֶׁסִּידֵּר אֶת הַמְנוֹרָה לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא נְתִינַת פְּתִילָה. נָתַן פְּתִילָה לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא נְתִינַת שֶׁמֶן. נָתַן שֶׁמֶן לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא הַדְלָקָה. הִדְלִיק לִיחַיַּיב! הַדְלָקָה לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **However, if** it is **so** that a non-priest is liable for performing any service involving placing, as established above, **a non-priest who set up the arrangement** of wood on the altar **should be liable,** since that is a service involving placing. The Gemara answers: **There is** still **the** mitzva of the **arrangement of two logs** on the altar that remains to be done, so that setting up the arrangement of wood is not a service that is complete on its own. The Gemara asks: If so, a non-priest who **arranged the two logs should be liable.** The Gemara answers: **There is** still **the arrangement of limbs** of offerings on the fire that remains to be done, so placing the two logs is also not considered a service that is complete on its own. The Gemara asks further with regard to the notion that a non-priest is liable for performing a service that is complete on its own that takes place in the Sanctuary: If so, **a non-priest who arranges the** lamps of the **candelabrum should be liable.** The Gemara rejects this: **There is** still the **placing of the wicks** in the lamps that remains to be done, so arranging the lamps is not considered a service that is complete on its own. The Gemara asks: If so, a non-priest who **placed a wick** in the candelabrum's lamps **should be liable.** The Gemara answers: That too is not a service that is complete on its own, as **there is** still the necessity of **placing the oil.** The Gemara asks: If so, a non-priest who **placed the oil should be liable.** The Gemara answers: That too is not a service that is complete on its own, as **there is** still the service of **lighting** that needs to be done. The Gemara asks: If so, if a non-priest **who lit** the lamps **should be liable.** The Gemara answers: **Lighting** the lamps **is not** considered a bona fide Temple **service,** since in doing so nothing is done to the candelabrum itself.

The Gemara now proceeds through the stages of *menorah* preparation. After cleaning the *menorah*, the *kohen* places new wicks — should a *zar* be liable for this? No: there is still the placing of the oil to follow. Should he be liable for placing the oil? No: there is still the *hadlakah*, the actual lighting, to follow. At each stage the same logic applies — *it is not yet done*. The process continues until one arrives at the lighting itself.

### Lighting Not Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתְנוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן אֵשׁ עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְעָרְכוּ עֵצִים עַל הָאֵשׁ״, לִימֵּד עַל הַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אֶלָּא בְּכֹהֵן כָּשֵׁר וּבִכְלִי שָׁרֵת! הַצָּתַת אֲלִיתָא — עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, הַדְלָקָה — לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** kindling a fire really **not** considered a Temple service? **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"And the sons of Aaron the priest shall put fire upon the altar and lay out wood in order upon the fire"** (Leviticus 1:7), which **teaches that the kindling of chips [*alita*]** added to keep the altar's fire going must **be performed only by a proper priest and** that he must be wearing **the priestly garments?** This shows that kindling is considered a service. The Gemara answers: **Kindling** the **chips is a** bona fide **service,** but **lighting** the candelabrum is **not** a bona fide **service.**

When the Gemara finally arrives at *hidlik le-hayyav* — should a *zar* be liable for lighting the *menorah*? — the answer is both surprising and striking: *hadlakah lav avodah hi* — the lighting of the *menorah* is not an *avodah*. This is counterintuitive in the extreme. The lighting of the *menorah* is the moment of the *menorah*'s purpose; a whole *parasha* begins *beha'alotecha et haneirot el mul penei hamenorah*. Yet precisely there, the Torah does not specify *benei Aharon hakohanim*; nowhere in the context of the *menorah* lighting does the Torah restrict the act to *kohanim*. And because it is not formally classified as an *avodah*, a *zar*'s performing it does not carry the capital sanction — and more than that, as the Rambam will explain, a *zar* may perform it outright.

### Hatzat Proof

A *baraita* confirms the distinction. The verse states: *venatnu benei Aharon hakohen esh al hamizbe'ach ve-archu etzim al ha-esh* — "and the sons of Aaron the *kohen* shall place fire on the altar and arrange wood on the fire." There, the Torah says *benei Aharon hakohanim*. The *baraita* derives: *limed al hatzatat alita she-lo te-he ela bekohen kasher uvicheli sharet* — this teaches that the kindling of the initial twig-fire (*hatzatat alita*) must be performed by an unblemished *kohen* using sanctified vessels. The kindling of the altar-fire twigs is an *avodah*. The lighting of the *menorah* is not.

---

## Rambam Synthesis

### Wood as Haktarah

The Rambam draws out the full implications of the Gemara's analysis. He rules that *hakatarah* — the burning of sacrificial material — encompasses not only the offering itself but also the wood arranged for the fire. *Ketzad haktarah? Vehamesidar shenei gezirei etzim al hama'arachah harei hu kemaktir evarim vehayav mita she-ha-etzim korban hen* — one who arranges the two logs of wood on the pyre is like one who burns the sacrificial limbs, and if a *zar* does so he is deserving of death, because the wood itself has the status of a *korban*. Elsewhere the Gemara even raises the question of salting the wood just as one salts sacrificial offerings, so closely is the wood identified with the sacrificial act.

---

## Menorah & Avodah Test…

### Light vs. Burning

The Gemara's resolution of the *menorah* paradox turns on a fundamental distinction. The lighting of altar-wood twigs (*hatzatat alita*) is part of the burning process; it participates in the *haktarah* of the offerings that will be consumed by that fire. Its purpose is to burn. By contrast, the lighting of the *menorah* — *hadlakah* — is not aimed at burning anything. The wicks and oil are consumed, yes, but consumption is merely the necessary means; the purpose is *light*. The *menorah*'s function is illumination, not combustion, and because the act of lighting aims at creating light rather than performing a burning-service, it is not an *avodah* in the halachic sense that would restrict it to an unblemished *kohen* performing it under the capital sanction.

---

## Rambam Synthesis…

### Non-Kohen May Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא בַּעֲלֵי זְרוֹעוֹת דְּחָמְסִי וְעָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** [This brief statement appears in the Sefaria text at Yoma 24b as part of the discussion regarding the lotteries and is not a new independent anchor for this essay section.]

The Rambam takes this Gemara to its logical conclusion, going further than the Gemara's bare statement. He rules: *vechen hadlakat haneirot kesherah bezarim* — the lighting of the *menorah* is valid even if performed by a non-*kohen*. This is not merely an observation that a *zar* is not liable for capital punishment if he lights it; the Rambam holds that *lechatchilah*, a *zar* may legitimately perform this act. The *kohen* must perform the cleaning (*hatavah*) of the *menorah*, which is an *avodah*, but the actual kindling may be done by anyone.

### Removed & Lit Outside

The Rambam extends this ruling even further. *Vehotziam lachutz mutar lezar lehadlikan* — the *menorah* may be taken out of the *heichal* and a non-*kohen* may light it there. One would never have imagined that the *menorah* could be lit outside its proper place in the *heichal*, yet the Rambam rules that this too is permitted, precisely because *hadlakah* is not an *avodah* — the locational requirements for *avodot* simply do not apply to it. The Rambam derives this directly from the Gemara's determination that lighting the *menorah* is not classified as a Temple service at all.

---

## Wood-Stacking Test

### Wood Logs Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, זָר שֶׁסִּידֵּר אֶת הַמַּעֲרָכָה לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא סִידּוּר שְׁנֵי גְּזִירֵי עֵצִים. סִידֵּר שְׁנֵי גְזִירִין לִיחַיַּיב! אִיכָּא סִידּוּר אֵבָרִים. וְהָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זָר שֶׁסִּידֵּר שְׁנֵי גְּזִירֵי עֵצִים חַיָּיב! בְּהָא פְּלִיגִי, מָר סָבַר: עֲבוֹדָה תַּמָּה הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו עֲבוֹדָה תַּמָּה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **However, if** it is **so** that a non-priest is liable for performing any service involving placing, as established above, **a non-priest who set up the arrangement** of wood on the altar **should be liable,** since that is a service involving placing. The Gemara answers: **There is** still **the** mitzva of the **arrangement of two logs** on the altar that remains to be done, so that setting up the arrangement of wood is not a service that is complete on its own. The Gemara asks: If so, a non-priest who **arranged the two logs should be liable.** The Gemara answers: **There is** still **the arrangement of limbs** of offerings on the fire that remains to be done, so placing the two logs is also not considered a service that is complete on its own. The Gemara asks: But didn't Rav Asi say that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A non-priest who arranged the two pieces of wood is liable? This shows that placing the two logs is a complete service and contradicts Rav's statement that a non-priest is liable only if he performs the four services that he mentioned above. The Gemara answers: Indeed, Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree on this** point. **One Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that the placement of the two logs **is a service that is complete** on its own, as the arrangement of limbs that follows is not considered a continuation of the setting up of the wood; **and one Sage,** Rav, **holds** that the placement of the two logs **is not** considered **a service that is complete** on its own, since it is followed by the arrangement of the limbs on the wood.

Having established that the *hatzatat alita* (kindling the altar-twigs) is an *avodah* while *hadlakah* (lighting the *menorah*) is not, the Gemara turns to the arrangement of the two logs (*shnei gezirei etzim*) placed on the *ma'arachah* each morning. The question is: does this act constitute an *avodah tammah* — a concluding, final-stage service — or is it an interim step?

---

## Wood-Stacking Test…

### Two Interpretations

The Gemara records a dispute on this point. One position holds that the arrangement of the two logs is an *avodah tammah* — it is the culmination of the fire-preparation process. From the perspective of the fire itself, once the wood is arranged the fire-service is complete; the *korbanot* that will subsequently be offered are a separate process. On this view, a *zar* who performs this act is *chayav* (as the position of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan). The second position holds that the wood arrangement is only an interim step — it is preparatory to the burning of the *korbanot*, which is the true purpose and conclusion. The wood is never an end in itself; it exists only to receive and consume the offerings. This position reads the entire sequence — wood arrangement, carrying of limbs, burning of limbs — as a single continuous process, and the wood arrangement falls in the middle of it.

## Baraita Support

### Rav's Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: עֲבוֹדוֹת שֶׁזָּר חַיָּיב עֲלֵיהֶם מִיתָה: זְרִיקַת דָּם בֵּין לְפָנִים בֵּין לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים, וְהַמַּזֶּה בְּחַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וְהַמְמַצֶּה, וְהַמַּקְטִיר בְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף, וְהַמְנַסֵּךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין מַיִם, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין יַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the disagreement between Rav and Levi and notes: **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav,** and **it was taught** in a second *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Levi. It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav:** These are the **services for which a non-priest** who performs them **is liable to** receive the penalty of **death** by God's hand: One who performs **the sprinkling of blood, whether inside** the Sanctuary; on the golden altar or at the veil; **or inside the innermost** chamber, the Holy of Holies, on Yom Kippur; or outside on the main altar; **and one who sprinkles** blood in the case of a **bird sin-offering; and one who squeezes** the blood of **a bird burnt-offering** on the wall of the altar **or burns** the bird on the altar; **and one who pours out three *log* of water** on the altar for the *Sukkot* water libation **or three *log* of wine** on the altar for an ordinary libation. The removal of ashes from the altar is not listed here.

The Gemara closes by noting that each position — Rav's and Levi's — finds support in a *baraita*. A *baraita* aligning with Rav lists the *avodot* for which a *zar* incurs *mita* without including *terumat hadeshen*: *zerikat hadam*, whether performed inside the *heichal*, within the *kodesh hakodashim*, or at various other points; the squirting of blood in the bird-*chatat* (*hamaza bechatas ha'of*); the pressing-out of the remaining blood (*hamimatzei*); the burning of the bird-*olah* (*hamaktir b'olas ha'of*) — these being variants of the blood and burning categories; and the libation (*hamenasech*) of three *log* of water or three *log* of wine. This list corresponds precisely to Rav's four categories (blood-throwing, burning, water libation, wine libation) without adding *terumat hadeshen*.

### Levi's Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי: עֲבוֹדוֹת שֶׁזָּר חַיָּיב עֲלֵיהֶן מִיתָה: הַמֵּרִים אֶת הַדֶּשֶׁן, וְשֶׁבַע הַזָּאוֹת שֶׁבִּפְנִים, וְשֶׁבִּמְצוֹרָע, וְהַמַּעֲלֶה עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ בֵּין דָּבָר כָּשֵׁר בֵּין דָּבָר פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Levi:** These are the **services for which a non-priest** who performs them **is liable to** receive the penalty of **death** by God's hand: **One who removes the ashes** from the altar; one who performs **the seven sprinklings that are** performed **inside** the Sanctuary **or** the sprinklings **of the leper; and one who raises up** an offering **onto the altar, whether it is** a **proper** offering **or** a **disqualified** one. In this *baraita*, the removal of ashes is listed.

A second *baraita* aligns with Levi. This *baraita* places *terumat hadeshen* at the head of the list — *hamayrim et hadeshen* — and proceeds to include *sheva hazayot shebifnim veshebimetzora* (the seven sprinklings of blood performed inside the *kodesh hakodashim* on *Yom Kippur*, and the oil sprinklings of the *metzora*), and *hamaleh al gabei mizbe'ach bein davar kasher bein davar pasul* — one who burns something on the altar, whether the item is valid or invalid, provided it is something that, once placed on the altar, is permitted to remain there.

The *sugya* thus concludes with the two positions each anchored in *tannaitic* tradition. The underlying debate is about the scope of *avodah tammah* — how broadly or narrowly to define what constitutes a "completing service" — and whether *avodat siluk* (removal) can ever meet that definition outside the interior of the *mikdash* building. And the great *chiddush* with which the *daf* ends: the lighting of the *menorah*, which one would have supposed to be the quintessential *avodah*, turns out not to be an *avodah* at all.
