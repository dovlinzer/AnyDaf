# Temurah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sanctifying Skin

### Abaye's Question

The daf opens by completing the series of questions that grew out of the previous discussion about sanctifying a limb or part of an animal. The *gemara* at the bottom of 11b records that Abaye posed the following question to Rabba, his teacher: if one sanctifies only the skin (*ora*) of an animal — without sanctifying the animal as a whole — may one put the animal to work? The *shita mekubetzet* notes that this question may apply even according to Rabbi Yosi, who generally holds that sanctifying a part of an animal causes the whole to become sanctified, since the skin is never itself offered as a *korban* and therefore may occupy a conceptually distinct status. But regardless of which *tanna* the question is framed under, the basic issue is this: given that only the skin is sanctified, does working the animal constitute a prohibited use of sanctified property?

### Fetus Analogy

The *gemara* brings a proof: *ta shma* — if one sanctifies the fetus of an animal, the mother is permitted for shearing but forbidden for work, because working weakens the fetus (*d'kachish ubar she'bah*), whereas shearing has no effect on the fetus. The implicit suggestion is that the same logic would apply to the case of the skin: working might affect the skin, and therefore be forbidden, while shearing would not.

### Rabbinic vs Biblical

Rav Yosef, however, pushes back. He suggests that the prohibition on working the mother when only the fetus is sanctified may be only *m'derabanan* — of Rabbinic origin — since the act of working is not technically directed at the sanctified object itself. If so, the case of the fetus cannot serve as a proof for whether working an animal whose skin alone is sanctified is *biblically* prohibited, since the fetus case may itself only be Rabbinic. This is the thrust of Abaye's question: what is the biblical status?

### Working Impact

The counter-argument is then raised: if the prohibition in the fetus case is only Rabbinic, why did the Rabbis not extend the decree to shearing as well? The answer is that the Rabbis issued a *gezeira* specifically because working weakens the fetus, whereas shearing does not. Since shearing causes no harm to the fetus, there was no reason to include it in the decree. The parallel to the skin case is left somewhat open. Rashi explains that working an animal can affect the skin — *avoda machish lei l'ora* — though precisely why working would impact the skin more than shearing is not fully developed in the *gemara*. One plausible reading is that a yoke placed on the animal directly presses against the hide. Regardless, the *gemara* has established that the question of the skin parallels the fetus question structurally, and that the proof from the fetus case is inconclusive because the entire prohibition there may be Rabbinic.

## Unsanctified Fetus

### Fetus Status

The *gemara* continues with Abaye now addressing a new set of questions to Rav Yosef. The case: the mother was sanctified as a *shelamim* while the fetus was not sanctified — either because Rabbi Yochanan permits one to explicitly exclude the fetus when sanctifying the mother, or because the animal was not yet pregnant at the time of sanctification and we hold that *vladei kodashim* only become sanctified at birth. The mother is then *shechted* inside the Temple as a *korban*. The question Abaye poses: has one transgressed the prohibition of *chullin ba-azarah*?

### Act vs Result

The question is conceptually sharp. The physical act of *shechita* was performed on the mother, not on the fetus. Yet the *halachic* effect of *shechita* — rendering the animal *shechted* — applied to the fetus by virtue of its being inside the mother. The fetus was not sanctified, so it is *chullin*. The act of *shechita* was not performed on it, but the result of *shechita* was. Is it the act or the result that determines whether one has performed *shechita* on *chullin* inside the *Beis HaMikdash*?

### Pasuk-Based Answer

Rav Yosef responds not by resolving the conceptual question directly, but by citing the *pasuk*. The verse from which the prohibition of *chullin ba-azarah* is derived states that if the place is far from you, *then* you may slaughter there — implying that non-sanctified animals are to be slaughtered outside the Temple. But in this case, the mother was a *shelamim* and *had* to be slaughtered inside the Temple. Since the *shechita* was required to occur in the Temple, one cannot be said to have violated the prohibition. As Rashi frames it: *lo yachol lishchot behemah zo ela ba-azarah* — this animal could only be slaughtered in the Temple. The *Torah* prohibits only what could have been done elsewhere. This answers the question on a technical level without directly adjudicating whether it is the act or the result of *shechita* that is legally operative.

### Reversed Fetus

Abaye then presents the mirror image of the previous case. Here the mother is *chullin* and the fetus is a *shelamim* — sanctified directly, as Rabbi Yochanan permits — and the mother is *shechted outside* the Temple. The act of *shechita* was performed on the mother, which is *chullin*, but the result rendered the fetus — a *korban* — effectively slaughtered. Has one violated the prohibition of *shechitas kodashim bachutz*, slaughtering sanctified animals outside the Temple? Again, the same conceptual tension arises: the act was performed on the *chullin*, not on the *kodashim*, but the *kodashim* was in practice slaughtered.

### Fit for Mishkan

Rav Yosef again answers by *pasuk*, this time citing the verse *v'yevi'um la-Hashem* — bring them to God — which implies that the prohibition of *shechitas kodashim bachutz* applies only to something fit to be brought as a *korban* at the *ohel moed*. The fetus, while still *in utero*, was not yet fit to be brought as a *korban*. Therefore, one is exempt on technical grounds. An alternate formulation of the same principle is stated directly: *ra'uy l'pesach ohel moed chayavin alav bachutz* — only what is fit for the entrance of the *ohel moed* triggers the prohibition. In both cases, Rav Yosef sidesteps the fundamental question of whether it is the act or the result of *shechita* that matters, answering instead with a limiting principle: neither case meets the threshold for a violation.

## Derivatives Rule

### Overview

Having concluded the discussions that grew out of the opening *mishnayot* of the *masechet* — who may perform *temurah*, on which animals, including questions about fetuses and limbs — the *gemara* now turns to a series of *mishnayot* that are grouped together by structural similarity rather than by direct subject matter. These *mishnayot* share a common theme: a secondary or derivative status does not generate a further secondary status of the same kind. *Temurah* is one of the cases in this list, which is why it appears in this *masechet*, but the series includes topics ranging from *terumah* and *chametz* to *mikvaot*, *mei chatat*, and *beit ha-peras*.

### Meduma

The first case is *meduma* — a mixture of *terumah* and *chullin* that is not *batel* because the ratio is less than one in a hundred. If a unit of *terumah* falls into fifty units of *chullin* and the mixture is forbidden, and then one unit from that mixture falls into another fifty units of *chullin*, how much *terumah* is in the second mixture? One could reason that the unit drawn from the first mixture was entirely forbidden and should be treated as entirely *terumah*, thereby making the second mixture forbidden as well. But the *mishnah* rules: *ein hameduma medama'as ela lefi cheshbon* — the mixture does not render the next batch forbidden except in proportion. Only the actual fraction of *terumah* in the unit drawn from the first mixture counts; you calculate the math and determine whether the second mixture still has enough *terumah* to be forbidden.

### Mechometz

The same formulation applies to *mechometz* — a mixed yeast (containing both *chullin* and *terumah* yeast) that falls into dough and causes fermentation. Normally, the fact that a small amount of something causes fermentation — and thereby exerts an effect on the dough — could prevent *bitul* even if the quantity is below the usual threshold. But the *mishnah* says: *ein hamuchometz mechametz ela lefi cheshbon*. It is only according to proportion: you examine how much of the yeast is actually *terumah*, and only if the *terumah* portion alone could have caused fermentation does it matter.

### Drawn Water

Similarly, *mayim she'uvim* — drawn water — invalidates a *mikvah* if three *lugin* of it enter before the *mikvah* has reached forty *se'ah*. The *mishnah* states that this too operates *lefi cheshbon* — according to proportion or calculation. The precise meaning of this phrase will be unpacked in the *gemara*.

### Mei Chatat

*Mei chatat* — the waters of purification prepared from the ashes of the *parah adumah* — cannot make other water into *mei chatat*. The only valid preparation is ashes placed with water; one cannot take an existing mixture of *mei chatat* and use it to sanctify fresh water. This case is structurally closest to *temurah*: just as a *temurah* derives its sanctity from the original *korban* and cannot pass that sanctity to yet another animal, so too the *mei chatat* mixture, however potent, cannot transmit its status to additional water without the original ingredients — *eifer* and water.

### Beit Ha-peras

A *beit ha-peras* — a field plowed over a grave, which scatters bone fragments and creates a zone of potential *tum'ah* — does not make another field into a *beit ha-peras*. The designation requires a grave having been plowed over in that location; a field contaminated by proximity to an existing *beit ha-peras* does not acquire the status independently.

### Double Terumah

*Terumah* cannot be taken twice from the same grain. If one separated *terumah* once, a second attempt to separate *terumah* from the same produce does not produce valid *terumah*. The act of designation has already been performed; the product of an attempted second *hafrashat terumah* does not acquire *terumah* status.

### Temurah Rule

Finally, *temurah* cannot make *temurah*, and a *vlad kodashim* — an animal born from a sanctified animal — cannot make *temurah*. The *mishnah* records this as the position of the *chachamim* in their debate with Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda holds that once a *vlad kodashim* is born, it can perform *temurah*; the *chachamim* respond that only what was directly sanctified — the original *hekdesh* — can create *temurah*, not what derived its sanctity through birth. Some manuscripts include the additional phrase that *temurah* itself also cannot make *temurah*, underscoring that the principle applies to both forms of derivative sanctity.

The conceptual unity of the list is now visible. *Temurah* sanctity derives from the original *korban*; a *temurah* cannot pass its sanctity forward to yet another animal. A *vlad kodashim*'s sanctity derives from its mother; it too cannot perform *temurah*. The pattern mirrors what we saw with *beit ha-peras*, *mei chatat*, and the others: a derivative status does not generate a further degree of the same status. That structural parallel is why these *mishnayot* are grouped together in this *masechet*, even though most of them do not directly concern *temurah*.

## Meduma Debate

### Sages Position

Having laid out the list, the *gemara* now examines each case in turn. On the question of *meduma*, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *mishnah*'s ruling that *meduma* affects the next mixture only in proportion is *not like Rabbi Eliezer* — *de-lo k'Rabbi Eliezer*. The position of the *chachamim* is that you follow the actual mathematics.

### Rabbi Eliezer Strict

The proof text is a *mishnah* in *terumah*: if a *se'ah* of *terumah* fell into fewer than a hundred [units of *chullin*] — so that it is not *batel* — and the whole became *meduma*, and then a unit from that *meduma* fell elsewhere, what is the status of that second mixture? *Rabbi Eliezer omer: medame'a k'terumah vadai* — Rabbi Eliezer says it is treated as pure *terumah*, as though the unit that fell into the second mixture was entirely *terumah*. The entire mixture having been forbidden, Rabbi Eliezer treats each unit extracted from it as if it were fully *terumah*, making the second mixture forbidden at the same ratio as if actual *terumah* had fallen into it. This is a stringent position: the forbidden status of the first mixture has, in effect, been transferred in full to each unit that exits it. This echoes the general principle of *chatichah na'aseis neveilah* found in *hilchos kashrus* — the whole piece becomes intrinsically forbidden — though there it operates on a Rabbinic level and the analogy is not exact, since in the *meduma* case everything is *min b'mino* and the amount is less than one in sixty.

### Sages Proportional

The *chachamim* reject this. *She'ani omer se'ah shenaflah hi she'altah* — I apply the logic that the very *se'ah* that fell in is the same one that fell out. Or, in a simpler formulation, the mixture is forbidden only proportionally, and when you take a unit from it, only the proportional fraction of *terumah* within that unit is considered to have fallen into the second mixture. The second mixture therefore has far less than a problematic amount of *terumah* and is *batel*. The *gemara* here does not simply identify the anonymous *mishnah* as being against Rabbi Eliezer — it also illuminates the debate fully, demonstrating exactly how the *chachamim*'s principle of *lefi cheshbon* is expressed in that *mishnah* in *terumah*.

## Yeast Fermentation

### Yeast Mixture Case

The *gemara* turns to *mechometz*. Rabbi Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the ruling about *mechometz* in our *mishnah* is also not like Rabbi Eliezer. The relevant case: yeast made of *chullin* and yeast made of *terumah*, mixed together, fell into a batch of dough. Neither the *chullin* yeast alone nor the *terumah* yeast alone was sufficient to cause fermentation; only together did they have that effect. The amount of *terumah* yeast is below one in a hundred, so it would ordinarily be *batel*. But the fact that yeast causes fermentation — exerts a tangible effect on the dough — can prevent *bitul*. The question is whether the *terumah* yeast, by participating in the fermentation even though it could not have done so alone, is considered to have had its impact and is therefore not *batel*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Yeast

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *achar acharon ani ba* — I follow the last one. If the two yeasts fell in sequentially rather than simultaneously, the second one to fall in is the one that tipped the scales and caused the fermentation to occur. If that was the *terumah* yeast, the dough is forbidden. If the *terumah* fell in first, before it had anything to combine with, it had no effect at that moment and is therefore *batel*. Rashi's analysis of a *gemara* elsewhere extends this: even if the two fell in simultaneously, as long as the *terumah* yeast was not the first to fall, it is considered to have participated in the fermentation and creates a problem.

### Sages Yeast

The *chachamim* disagree: *bein shenaphal b'techilah bein shenaphal b'sof* — regardless of whether the *terumah* yeast fell in first or last, *l'olam eino oser ad sheyehei bo k'dei l'hachmitz* — it is never forbidden unless the *terumah* yeast alone was sufficient to cause fermentation. This is the principle of *ze v'ze gorem*: when two causes together produce an effect but neither would have done so independently, the *chachamim* hold that *ze v'ze gorem mutar* — the involvement of the forbidden item does not render the result forbidden, since it could not have acted alone.

### Cheshbon Meaning

What, then, does *lefi cheshbon* mean in the context of *mechometz* in our *mishnah*? According to Tosafot's reading — which is the more straightforward approach, as opposed to Rashi's more complex interpretation — it means: examine the yeast mixture and determine what fraction of it is *terumah* yeast. Only if that *terumah* fraction, taken on its own, would have been sufficient to cause fermentation does it prevent *bitul*. If the *terumah* yeast needed the *chullin* yeast to accomplish anything, then *lefi cheshbon* it is insufficient and the *terumah* is *batel*.

## Drawn Water

### Three Log Rule

The *mishnah* states that drawn water invalidates a *mikvah lefi cheshbon*. To understand this, it is necessary to recall the basic rule: three *lugin* of *mayim she'uvim* — water that has been drawn in a vessel — invalidates a *mikvah* that has not yet reached forty *se'ah* of valid water. By way of orientation, a *revi'it* is a quarter of a *log*, and the standard *revi'it* used for *kiddush* is approximately three and a half ounces; a *log* is therefore roughly fourteen ounces, and three *lugin* amounts to approximately forty-two ounces — somewhat more than three standard cups. This relatively modest quantity, falling into an incomplete *mikvah*, invalidates it.

The standard practice in modern *mikvaot* addresses this concern by maintaining two connected bodies of water: a sealed pit containing forty *se'ah* of collected rainwater, connected through a small opening to a separate pool in which people actually immerse. Because the rainwater pit was already a complete, valid *mikvah* before the second pool was added, any amount of piped water added to the second pool cannot invalidate — the connection to the kosher *mikvah* saves it. The pipes themselves are not considered *keilim* — receptacles — because they lack a bottom; they simply channel water through continuously without holding it. The pit in the ground is similarly not a *kli* because it is in the ground, not a detached vessel. When the *mikvah* is cleaned and the water replaced, care must be taken regarding residual water, a point that becomes halachically significant and will be addressed below.

The question now is what *lefi cheshbon* means in this context. A natural reading would parallel the *meduma* case: if water from an invalidated *mikvah* overflows into a second *mikvah*, only the proportional fraction of drawn water in that overflow is considered *she'uvim*, not the entire overflow. But the *gemara* understands the phrase differently. The attribution comes first.

### Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *mishnah*'s rule is *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*. The source is a *Tosefta* in Mikvaot: *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov omer* — a *mikvah* that already contains twenty-one *se'ah* of rainwater may have the remaining nineteen *se'ah* filled by drawing water with a vessel from one's shoulder and pouring it into the *mikvah*, provided the water runs along the ground before entering — and it is nevertheless valid. The reasoning: *she-ha-rubah metaheret bi-revi'it u-ve-hamshachah* — the majority [of kosher water] combined with the drawn water running along the ground (*hamshacha*) renders the *mikvah* valid. 

The key condition is twofold: first, the majority of the forty *se'ah* must already consist of kosher rainwater — at least twenty-one *se'ah*. Second, the drawn water may not be poured directly into the *mikvah* but must run along the ground before entering, making it somewhat more "natural" and less directly a product of human conveyance. The underlying principle appears to be that what invalidates a *mikvah* is not an intrinsic flaw in the water itself but rather the manner in which the *mikvah* is constituted — if the foundational majority of water was rainwater and the drawn water merely supplemented it through a process that was not entirely direct, the *mikvah* retains its character as a naturally formed body of water rather than a manufactured one.

### Opposing View

This being an implicitly lenient ruling — drawn water in the minority, combined with *hamshacha*, is permitted — one might assume the *Rabbanan* take a stricter position. The *gemara* initially considers this inference: *mikhlal de-rabbanan savri* that *hamshacha* would not work here and any drawn water, even if in the minority, would invalidate. But then Ravin arrived from Eretz Yisrael and reported in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the opposing position is actually *more* lenient: *she-hu ve-she-shichu kulo tahor* — even if one drew all of the water in this manner it would be valid. If so, our *mishnah* cannot be identified as lenient relative to the *Rabbanan*, since the *Rabbanan* are more lenient still. The *gemara* therefore concludes that this reading leaves the *mishnah* attributable to neither the *Rabbanan* nor Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — *lo Rabbanan ve-lo Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Rava Vessels

Rava therefore offers a different interpretation: *lefi cheshbon kelim* — "according to calculation" means according to the *number of vessels*. This shifts the entire frame of analysis.

### Yosef ben Choni

The source is a *Tosefta*: the *chachamim* rule that three *lugin* of drawn water invalidate a *mikvah* regardless of how many vessels the water was distributed among — whether it fell in from two vessels, three vessels, four, or five, it does not matter. Yosef ben Choni disagrees: *bi-shnayim u-vi-shelosha kelim posel et ha-mikvah, be-arba ve-chamisha ein posel et ha-mikvah* — two or three vessels invalidate, but four or five do not.

Why should the number of vessels matter? The logic appears to be that the *psul* of *mayim she'uvim* is not a flaw in the water's composition but a consequence of the *mikvah* having been formed through deliberate human effort. What constitutes a "meaningful act" of drawing? Only when each individual act of pouring contributed a significant quantity — at least a *log* per vessel. If the three *lugin* were spread across four or five vessels, each act contributed less than a *log* and was therefore not a halachically significant act of formation. The aggregate still totals three *lugin*, but no single act of drawing was substantial enough to leave its mark on the *mikvah*'s character. This reading assumes roughly equal distribution across vessels, though the *mishnah* does not spell this out explicitly.

*Lefi cheshbon*, then, according to Rava, means: do not automatically say that any three *lugin* of drawn water invalidates; calculate according to the number of vessels and the significance of each individual act.

### Practical Application

The ראב"ד — Rav Avraham bar David — is cited (via the *Tosefot Yom Tov*) as ruling in accordance with Yosef ben Choni, and draws an important practical inference: when one empties a *mikvah* to clean it, removing the water with a vessel, some residual water inevitably remains — splashing back from the bucket, dripping on the floor. This water may technically be *mayim she'uvim* and might accumulate to three *lugin* on the floor before new rainwater fills the *mikvah*. The ראב"ד rules that there is no concern, since in the course of cleaning, the water falling back was spread across many removals — each removal contributed only drops, not a *log* per vessel. Thus even if the residual water on the floor exceeds three *lugin* in total, it was never drawn three *lugin* at a time, and according to Yosef ben Choni it does not invalidate. Some *poskim*, however, are *machmir* and make a small hole in the cleaning bucket so that the water is never fully "held" — and thus never technically *she'uvim* — even as it is removed.

A separate and significant halakhic question addressed in passing is whether *hamshacha* can be performed on the *majority* or even the entirety of the water in a *mikvah*. This was a pressing practical question for communities that could not obtain rainwater easily. Tosafot, finding that the implication of even one *gemara* line suggesting *kulo* — the entire *mikvah* — conflicts deeply with numerous *mishnayot* in *Taharot*, reads that line restrictively, arguing that it can only mean cases where forty *se'ah* of valid rainwater are present somewhere in the mixture regardless. The *Rishonim* debated this extensively, and the question of how we rule (*posken*) depends both on the correct textual reading and on reconciling this passage with the *mishnayot* in Seder Taharot — a tractate that, lacking a *gemara*, relies on analysis of the *mishnayot* themselves, the *Tosefta*, and the incidental citations of those passages in the *Bavli* and *Yerushalmi*.

## Mei Chatat Rule

### Basic Rule

The *mishnah* states: *ein mei chatat osim mei chatat* — *mei chatat* cannot make *mei chatat*. The mixture of water and ashes of the *parah adumah* cannot serve as a source for preparing new *mei chatat*; one cannot simply add the existing mixture to fresh water and thereby sanctify the fresh water. The valid preparation requires introducing ashes (*eifer*) to water — not *mei chatat* to water.

### Attribution

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: this ruling is not like Rabbi Shimon — *d'lo k'Rabbi Shimon*.

### Rabbi Shimon Lenient

A *Tosefta* in Parah records the dispute: *hikdim afar l'mayim pasul* — if one puts the ashes in the vessel first and then adds the water, it is invalid. Rabbi Shimon holds that it is valid: *Rabbi Shimon machshir*. Note that the text here uses *afar* (dirt) rather than *eifer* (ashes), because the same *gezeira shava* connects the *parah adumah* to the *sotah* ritual, where the verse uses *afar*. Rabbi Shimon notes: is this really "dirt"? No, it is ashes. The Torah used the word *afar* in the context of the *parah adumah* in order to create a *gezeira shava* with the *sotah* verse — *ne'emar kan afar, ve-ne'emar lehan afar* — "dirt" is mentioned both here and there. Just as for the *sotah* the ideal procedure is water first and then dirt, the same ideal applies for *parah adumah*. But the *gezeira shava* cuts both ways for Rabbi Shimon: just as the *parah adumah* permits ashes before water (as Rabbi Shimon will now establish), so too the *sotah* permits dirt before water. Either order is acceptable.

### Verse Analysis

The question then is: how does Rabbi Shimon know that ashes-before-water is valid for *parah adumah*? *Trei krayi ksivi* — two separate formulations are written. One phrase is *v'natan alav mayim chayim el keli* — the simple reading of this *pasuk* is: take the ashes, then put water on them into a vessel — implying ashes come first. The other reading draws on only the end of that same verse: *mayim chayim el keli* — living water into a vessel — which implies the water must be the first thing to enter the vessel. Rabbi Shimon reads both phrases as independently valid options: either put the ashes first and then the water (*v'natan alav*), or put the water first into the vessel (*mayim chayim el keli*). Either is acceptable.

### Sages Water First

The *chachamim* insist on water first, deriving this from *mayim chayim el keli* and treating it as the definitive instruction (*seifa de-kra davka* — the end of the verse determines). As for *v'natan alav* — "and he put upon it" — they read *alav* not as a statement of sequence (ashes first, then water on top), but as an instruction about mixing: *le-arvan* — you must mix them well together. The word *alav* merely ensures thorough combination, not a specific order. How do the *chachamim* know that water-first is the definitive rule? *Mima matzinu be-chol makom machshir lemala* — from the general principle that the *machshir*, the activating ingredient, is put on top. Since for *sotah* it is explicit that the water is the base and the dirt is placed upon it, the same structure governs *parah adumah*: water first, ashes on top.

It is notable that Rabbi Shimon's reading is arguably the more straightforward *pshat* of the verse, yet the *chachamim* reinterpret *alav* as referring to mixing rather than sequence. The *gemara*'s attribution of our *mishnah* as opposing Rabbi Shimon emphasizes the aspect of *matan eifer* — the ashes must be added to the water — which implies the water precedes the ashes, contrary to Rabbi Shimon's permissive ruling.

## Beit Ha-peras

### Mishnah Position

The *mishnah* states: *ein beit ha-peras oseh beit ha-peras* — one *beit ha-peras* does not create another *beit ha-peras*. The *gemara* immediately identifies this as not like Rabbi Eliezer — *matnitin de-lo ke-Rabbi Eliezer*.

### Rabbi Eliezer

The source *mishnah*: *Rabbi Eliezer omer beit ha-peras oseh beit ha-peras* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that a *beit ha-peras* can make another field into a *beit ha-peras*. The scenario is that a field was already plowed over a grave — creating a *beit ha-peras* — and then that field was plowed further into a neighboring field.

### Definition

The fundamental rule of *beit ha-peras* is established by a *braita*: *ha'choresh et ha-kever oseh beit ha-peras milo ma'ane me'ah amah* — one who plows over a grave creates a *beit ha-peras* of a full *ma'ane*, one hundred *amot* (approximately 150 feet). This is the assumed radius of scatter: when a grave is plowed over, bone fragments may have been dispersed up to a hundred *amot* in the direction of plowing, and *kohanim* may not traverse the field. If the grave was at the edge of the field, the *beit ha-peras* extends a hundred *amot* in each direction of plowing. The *gemara* notes that on this underlying rule — that plowing from a *beit ha-peras* into a neighboring field contaminates that neighboring field up to a hundred *amot* — even the *Rabbanan* seem to concede, as Rav Dimi reported in the name of Reish Lakish: *shalosh sadot u'shtei ma'anot* — three fields and two *ma'anot*, meaning the contamination radiates outward to the adjacent fields in the direction of plowing. The *Rabbanan* apparently agree that the second field is affected.

### Tosafot Interpretation

Tosafot resolves the apparent inconsistency: the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Rabbanan* is not actually about the immediately neighboring field — both agree it is impacted. The debate concerns the *third* field. If the *beit ha-peras* status of the second field is treated as a genuine, independent *beit ha-peras* in its own right, then plowing from it into a third field would create yet another *beit ha-peras* — and so on, potentially without limit. This is what Rabbi Eliezer holds: *beit ha-peras oseh beit ha-peras*, meaning the second field's status is primary and self-generating, capable of producing further generations of *beit ha-peras*. The *chachamim* say no: the second field's impurity is derivative of the original plowed grave; it is a *beit ha-peras* only in a secondary sense and cannot in turn make a third field into a *beit ha-peras*. The chain stops at the first remove.

This is the structural parallel that places this case in the list: just as *temurah* cannot make *temurah*, and a *vlad kodashim* cannot make *temurah*, because derivative sanctity does not generate further derivative sanctity — so too a *beit ha-peras* created by proximity to an original *beit ha-peras* cannot produce yet another *beit ha-peras*. The *machlokes* between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Rabbanan* here is therefore precisely analogous to the *machlokes* about *temurah*: does a derivative status become primary and self-perpetuating, or does it remain secondary and terminate there?
