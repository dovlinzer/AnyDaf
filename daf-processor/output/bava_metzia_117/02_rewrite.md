# Bava Metzia 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Abba's Tenant Ques…

### Tenant Alone or Shared

The daf opens at the very last line of 116b, where Rabbi Abba bar Memel poses a series of questions bearing on the *mishna* we have been studying. That *mishna* addresses a case in which a person rents a second story from the owner of the ground floor, and the roof of the ground floor — which is simultaneously the floor of the second story — breaks through. The *mishna* grants the upstairs tenant the right to descend and live in the ground floor until the owner repairs the damage. The entire house is conceptualized as *meshubad* — encumbered — in favor of the tenant's right to reside in the upper story, and it is on the basis of that encumbrance that the tenant may demand alternative accommodations below.

Rabbi Abba bar Memel's first question, which opens 117a, is: *kishu dar levado hu dar kemekara oh dilma shneihem darin?* — when the tenant relocates to the ground floor, does he live there alone, as he did when he occupied the upper story in isolation, or do both the tenant and the owner live together on the ground floor? The owner might reasonably argue: even granting that the entire house is *meshubad* to your right of residence, that encumbrance was never intended to go so far as to exclude me from my own home. Such an understanding would be implicit in the rental arrangement regardless of how the contract is worded — *ada'ata de-afkinun lo aguri lakh*, I would never have agreed to rent to you on terms that would deprive me of a place to live.

### Roof vs. Front Door Entr…

If we assume that both parties are permitted to cohabit on the ground floor, a second question follows naturally: *kishu mishtamesh derekh petakhim mishtamesh oh derekh gag mishtamesh?* — when the tenant uses the ground floor during this interim period, does he enter through the front door like any ordinary ground-floor resident, or must he enter by way of the roof — that is, by climbing the exterior stairs to the second story and descending through the breach in the floor?

The owner might insist that the tenant keep to the rooftop entrance: *mekara derekh gag hashta nami derekh gag* — just as when you lived upstairs you entered through the roof, so too now. The tenant, however, might counter: *aliya ve-yerida lo kibeili alai* — I agreed to climb stairs to enter my apartment, but I never agreed to climb up the stairs, traverse the second story, and descend again through a hole in the floor. Since you are obligated to provide me with habitable accommodations, you cannot impose upon me an access arrangement more burdensome than the one I originally accepted.

One should note that the *gemara* employs the construction *im timtza lomar* — "if you assume this is the case" — at each stage of Rabbi Abba's inquiry. The *rishonim* and *poskim* have observed that when the *gemara* is willing to grant one premise in order to pose the next question, this may itself constitute a reason to rule in accordance with that premise — and accordingly, traditional editions of the text mark each *im timtza lomar* with a small letter indicating how we *pasken*.

### Multiple Stories Scenario

The third question involves a building with a ground floor, a second story, and a third story: *shtei aliyot zo gabi zu mahu?* — what is the law when there are two upper stories stacked one atop the other? The *gemara* considers two sub-cases. If the third story's floor collapses, the tenant who lived there may descend to the second story — or, if the entire building is *meshubad*, perhaps all the way to the ground floor. Conversely: *ipcha tachtonah mahu lesalko legamrei be'elyonah* — if the second story's floor breaks through, can the owner direct the tenant upward to the third story instead of downward, telling him, "You already agreed to climb one flight of stairs; climbing two is not substantively different"? Or does the tenant have a valid claim that *chad aliyah kabbil alach shtei aliyot lo kabbil alach* — he accepted the burden of one flight of stairs, not two? The *gemara* concludes with *teiku* — the questions remain unresolved.

## Plaster Responsibility D…

### Rabbi Yosi Position

The *mishna* records a dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yosi regarding who bears responsibility for replacing the roof-floor. The *tanna kamma* holds that the owner — the landlord — must repair the floor in its entirety. Rabbi Yosi distinguishes between components: *hatachton noten et hatikrah* — the owner of the ground floor provides the structural beams, but the responsibility for the plaster belongs to the tenant living above.

This reflects a familiar landlord-tenant boundary: what constitutes the landlord's structural obligation, and what falls within the tenant's responsibility for the conditions of his own living space? The *tanna kamma* holds that the owner must restore the entire floor. Rabbi Yosi holds that the owner need only provide the framework — the beams — while the plaster, which is applied on top, is the upper tenant's concern.

### Roof Materials Definition

The *gemara* asks: *mai tikrah* — what precisely are the structural materials constituting the roof-floor that the owner must provide? Rabbi Yosi Berabbi Chanina enumerates *kanim, sano'im, ve-sitni* — reeds, mats, and wooden beams. Reish Lakish says the reference is to *levachin* — wooden planks. The *gemara* resolves that these two opinions do not disagree: *mar ki atreih u-mar ki atreih* — each authority describes the standard building materials of his own locale.

## Water Damage Plaster Case

### Two Owners Water Case

The *gemara* now introduces a related case. There were two people living in the same building — *chad ilai ve-chad tatai*, one on the upper floor and one on the lower — and *ifchit ma'azivah*, the plaster of the dividing floor cracked. *Ki mashi yadeih ilai azli maya u-mazki le-tatai* — when the upper resident would wash his hands, the water would seep through the cracked plaster and drip down, damaging the lower resident. A not uncommon situation.

The question of who bears responsibility for repair is disputed by two *amora'im*: *Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba amar elyon metakken* — it is the upper resident's obligation to fix the plaster, while *Rabbi Ila'i in the name of Rabbi Chiyya Berabbi Yosi* holds the opposite — *tachton metakken*, the lower resident must repair it. A mnemonic is provided: *ve-Yosef hurad mitzraimah* — the name Yosef alludes to Rabbi Yosi (via the name Yosef), and *hurad*, "was brought down," corresponds to *tachton*, below. Thus the tradition associated with Rabbi Yosi holds that it is the lower resident who must make the repair.

### Rashi: Two Owners vs. La…

Rashi makes an important clarificatory note at this point: unlike the *mishna*, which concerns a landlord-tenant relationship, this case involves two *separate owners* — one who owns the upper floor and one who owns the lower. Rashi emphasizes this distinction deliberately, because if the case were identical to our *mishna*, the ruling would follow automatically from the *mishna*'s dispute between Rabbi Yosi and the *tanna kamma*. The fact that this is a case of two owners rather than a landlord and tenant makes it a somewhat different scenario — and the *gemara* will now explore how the debate between Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba and Rabbi Ila'i maps onto the broader principled debate.

## Damager vs. Damagee Prin…

### Bava Batra Parallel Deba…

The *gemara* proposes: *leima Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba ve-Rabbi Ila'i be-plugeta de-Rabbi Yosi ve-rabbanan ka-miflegi* — perhaps the dispute between Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba and Rabbi Ila'i precisely parallels the dispute between Rabbi Yosi and the Rabbis in our *mishna*. The conceptual framework being proposed is as follows. When the plaster is understood as existing solely to prevent water from the upper floor from damaging the lower floor, the question of who must provide it reduces to a classic question in the laws of *nezikin*: when two parties are each operating legitimately within their own domain, and one's activity indirectly causes harm to the other, who bears the responsibility to prevent that harm?

According to the position that *al ha-mazik le-harchik et atzmo* — it is the damager who must distance himself from the one being harmed — the upper resident, whose water is seeping downward, is the *mazik*. He must provide the plaster to prevent his activity from causing harm. According to the opposing view, *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* — it is the damaged party who must protect himself — then the lower resident, if he does not wish to suffer the dripping water, must take steps to repair the floor beneath him.

This is a classic *Bava Batra* framework: if a neighbor is having a barbecue and the smoke drifts into your yard, who must move? The party generating the activity in their own domain, or the one receiving the harm? This dispute maps directly onto the question of who plasters the cracked floor.

### Tree and Well Case

The *gemara* then raises a sharp objection. If this is indeed a straightforward expression of the debate between Rabbi Yosi and the Rabbis on *mazik* versus *nizak*, then we would expect Rabbi Yosi to hold *al ha-mazik le-harchik et atzmo* — meaning the upper resident (the *mazik*) must plaster. But we know from an explicit *mishna* in *Bava Batra* that Rabbi Yosi holds the opposite position.

The *mishna* in *Bava Batra* teaches: *marchikin et ha-ilan min ha-bor esrim ve-chamish amot* — a tree must be planted at least 25 *amot* from a cistern or well, because the roots will grow and damage the walls or floor of the cistern. For a carob tree or a sycamore, the distance increases to 50 *amot*, as their roots extend further — and this applies whether the tree is above, below, or alongside the cistern, since the roots travel beneath the floor of the cistern as well.

*Im ha-bor kodem kotzetz ve-notein damim* — if the well was dug first, and the tree was planted afterward in violation of the required distance, the owner of the tree must cut it down, though the owner of the well must compensate him. The compensation reflects the fact that ultimately, the tree owner was acting within his own domain; it was merely the passage of time — the gradual growth of roots — that caused the damage. *Ve-im ha-ilan kodem lo yakotz* — if the tree was planted first, the subsequent well owner has no claim whatsoever.

Rabbi Yosi's position in that *mishna* is striking: *af al pi she-ha-bor kodemes la-ilan lo yakotz* — even if the well predated the tree, the tree owner cannot be compelled to cut it down. *She-ze chofer be-toch shelo ve-ze nota be-toch shelo* — this one digs in his own domain, and that one plants in his own domain. If the indirect consequences of what I do in my yard eventually reach your yard and harm you, that is not my responsibility; if you do not wish to be harmed, it is incumbent upon you to protect yourself. This is explicitly *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* — Rabbi Yosi sides with the *nizak* bearing responsibility, not the *mazik*.

### Rabbi Yosi's Damagee Pri…

This creates an apparent contradiction. If Rabbi Yosi holds *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* — that the party at risk of harm must distance himself — then in our *mishna*, where Rabbi Yosi says the upper tenant must provide the plaster, he would be imposing the obligation on the *mazik*, which is precisely the position he rejects. And symmetrically, the Rabbis in *Bava Batra* hold *al ha-mazik le-harchik et atzmo*, yet in our *mishna* they seem to place the plastering obligation on the lower owner — the *nizak*.

The positions in each context appear inverted relative to the other. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the debate between Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba and Rabbi Ila'i in the water damage case does correctly parallel the *Bava Batra* debate of Rabbi Yosi and the Rabbis — *al ha-mazik* versus *al ha-nizak*. But this is a case involving two separate owners and water damage, whereas the debate in our *mishna* is about something else entirely.

### Water Damage vs. Bava Ba…

Before turning to what our *mishna*'s debate actually concerns, the *gemara* raises an additional complication. Even granting that the water damage case between two owners follows the *Bava Batra* framework, there is a refinement: *modeh Rabbi Yosi be-girei dilei* — Rabbi Yosi concedes that when one's action directly and immediately causes harm to another, like arrows shot from one's property into a neighbor's, even he would hold the *mazik* responsible. The principle *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* applies only where the damage is indirect and time-lapsed — as in the case of the tree whose roots slowly grow and eventually undermine a neighbor's cistern.

The *gemara* therefore challenges: is the water damage case really analogous to the tree? When someone washes their hands and the water seeps through the floor and drips below — isn't that direct, immediate damage, like arrows? The *gemara* responds: *de-psiki maya ve-hadar naflei* — it is a case where the water first accumulates and is slowly absorbed into the cracking plaster before finally dripping through. This delayed absorption means it is not a direct act of damage in the manner of *girei dilei*, but rather a time-lapsed, indirect form of harm. Therefore the *Bava Batra* framework applies, and Rabbi Yosi's *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* position can consistently hold the lower resident responsible.

## Roof Strength vs. Floor…

### Rabbis: Plaster = Streng…

With the water damage case properly assigned to the *Bava Batra* debate, the *gemara* now identifies the real dispute animating our *mishna*. The disagreement between Rabbi Yosi and the Rabbis here concerns not the *mazik-nizak* framework, but rather the function of the plaster itself: *be-chozek tikra ka-miflegi* — they debate whether applying plaster is part of making the roof strong.

The Rabbis hold: *ma'aziva achzukei tikra hu ve-achzukei tikra al ha-tachton remei* — the plaster is what structurally reinforces the roof and floor. Wood beams or planks alone leave a wobbly, incomplete structure. A truly solid floor requires plaster to bind and seal the material. Therefore, providing the plaster is part of the landlord's fundamental obligation to deliver a functional roof; the *tanna kamma* assigns this cost to the owner of the lower floor.

### Rabbi Yosi: Plaster = Sm…

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *ma'aziva ashvei gumot hu ve-ashvei gumot a-elyon remei* — the plaster serves only to level and smooth the surface for the upper resident's comfort and use. Assuming the structural elements — the beams or planks — are solid and properly secured, the floor is already functionally complete. The plaster is an amenity: it fills in the gaps, produces a smooth walking surface, and makes the living conditions pleasant. In the language of landlord-tenant law, this falls within the domain of what the tenant provides for his own living space, not what the landlord owes as a matter of structural repair. Hence Rabbi Yosi places the plastering obligation on the upper tenant.

### Gemara Challenge to Expl…

This explanation is coherent, but it prompts a further *gemara* challenge. If in our *mishna* Rabbi Yosi holds that the plaster is merely for smoothness and is therefore the upper tenant's responsibility, and if separately Rabbi Yosi holds *al ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo*, one might ask: what about water damage from the upper floor? Even if we accept Rabbi Yosi's view on plaster in the *mishna*, shouldn't his *Bava Batra* principle lead him, in the water damage case between two owners, to place responsibility on the lower resident rather than the upper — thereby holding consistently that the *nizak* must protect himself?

This is precisely what the *gemara* has been working toward. The answer lies in the distinction already established: the water damage case between two separate owners tracks the *al ha-mazik / al ha-nizak* debate. Our *mishna*'s dispute between Rabbi Yosi and the Rabbis is a separate question — about the nature of the plaster — that does not involve the *mazik-nizak* framework at all. Rashi's insistence that the water damage case involves two owners, not a landlord and tenant, is what keeps these two debates from collapsing into one.

### Slow Water Absorption

The *gemara*'s resolution — that water damage through cracked plaster qualifies as indirect harm because *de-psiki maya ve-hadar naflei*, the water is absorbed gradually before finally dripping through — is what allows the *Bava Batra* framework of *al ha-mazik / al ha-nizak* to govern that case without contradiction. The upper resident's washing is not analogous to shooting arrows; the harm unfolds over time through an intermediate process of absorption. This is consistent with Rabbi Yosi's general principle that when operating in one's own domain with delayed, indirect consequences, it is the *nizak* who must take protective steps.

## Broken House Scenario

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *mishna* now turns to a scenario involving two separate owners rather than a landlord and tenant: *ha-bayit ve-ha-aliya shel shenayim she-naflu* — the ground floor and the upper story each belong to different owners, and the building collapsed. *Amar ba'al ha-aliya le-va'al ha-bayit livnot* — the upper-floor owner tells the ground-floor owner to rebuild his floor so that the second story can then be rebuilt on top of it. The ground-floor owner refuses — perhaps because he has other accommodations or lacks funds.

The *tanna kamma* rules that *harei ba'al ha-aliya boneh et ha-bayit ve-dar be-tocha ad she-yiten lo et yetzi'otav* — the upper-floor owner may go ahead and finance the construction of the ground floor, live in it himself, and remain there until the ground-floor owner reimburses his expenses. He is not entitled to demand the full increase in property value — it may well be that he paid a builder one hundred thousand and the property is now worth a hundred and fifty thousand — but he is entitled to recover precisely what he laid out. His right to do this flows from his status as one who has a legitimate interest in the property and who steps in when the other party fails to fulfill his responsibility.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *af zeh dar be-toch shel chaveiro tzarich le-ha'alot lo sachar* — this person is living in his fellow's house and must therefore pay rent for it. Even though the upper-floor owner paid for the construction, the ground floor belongs to the other party. By living in it, the upper-floor owner is deriving benefit from someone else's property, and this is something Rabbi Yehuda considers impermissible.

The question is framed using the familiar categories of *zeh neheneh ve-zeh chaser* — one benefits while the other suffers a loss — and *zeh neheneh ve-zeh lo chaser* — one benefits while the other loses nothing. We will see the *gemara* analyze this further. For now, Rabbi Yehuda's prescription is: *ela ba'al ha-aliya boneh et ha-bayit ve-et ha-aliya* — the upper-floor owner must finance the construction of both the ground floor and the second story, all the way up to the roof — *ve-yoshev bah ad she-yitten lo yetzi'osav* — and then he may reside in the *upper* story until the ground-floor owner repays him. Tosafot prefers the reading *ve-yoshev bah*, meaning he lives in the second story. He may not live in the ground floor at all; that belongs to the other owner and he has no right to benefit from it.

Rashi notes that once the upper-floor owner has a habitable second story, he could also permissibly live on the ground floor under certain conditions — since if he could just as well live upstairs, living downstairs does not constitute *zeh neheneh* in a meaningful sense, as he is gaining nothing he could not otherwise have. Tosafot challenges this, pointing out from earlier in the *gemara* that living on the ground floor carries a genuine advantage — one avoids the exterior staircase — and therefore does constitute a benefit.

## Three Benefit Cases

### House Building Benefit

Rabbi Yochanan states: *bi-shlosha mekomos shana lanu Rabbi Yehuda assur le-adam she-yehene mi-mamon chaveiro* — in three places Rabbi Yehuda teaches that a person is forbidden to derive benefit from another's money. This needs some unpacking. We are not speaking of outright theft or conversion. These are cases where one might plausibly argue that the legal framework permits the benefit — and yet Rabbi Yehuda insists that it is improper.

Whether this reflects a strict halachic principle or an ethical imperative that then shapes the *halacha* is a question addressed by Rashi and Tosafot. Tosafot's framing is particularly pointed: the underlying law would in many of these cases seem to permit the benefit, but because it is ethically unseemly to profit from another's property without that person's genuine acquiescence, Rabbi Yehuda intervenes to restructure the outcome.

The first case is our *mishna* itself. The upper-floor owner built the ground floor and would otherwise live in it until reimbursed. Rabbi Yehuda forbids this: you have no right to live in property that belongs to another, even property you paid to construct.

### Dyer and Wool Case

The second case: *ha-nosein tzemer le-tzeva li-tzboa lo adom ve-tzeva'o shachor* — a person gives wool to a dyer to be dyed red, and the dyer dyes it black (or the reverse: given to be dyed black, dyed red). Rabbi Meir rules: *nosein lo d'mei tzemaro* — the dyer pays the owner the value of the wool and acquires it. The rationale is *shinui koneh* — a transformative change in the object effects a transfer of ownership. The dyer did something wrong, but by effecting an irreversible transformation without the owner's consent, he has effectively acquired the object; he pays for the raw material and the matter is closed.

Rabbi Yehuda rules otherwise: *im ha-shevach yeseira al ha-hotza'a nosein lo et ha-hotza'a* — if the enhanced value of the dyed wool exceeds the dyer's expenses (the dye, the labor, etc.), the owner pays only the expenses and takes back his wool, now more valuable. *Ve-im ha-hotza'a yeseira al ha-shevach nosein lo et ha-shevach* — if the expenses exceeded the improvement, the owner pays the dyer the value of the improvement and takes back the wool, ensuring the dyer neither profits nor suffers a net loss.

The logic, as Rashi explains it: if we followed Rabbi Meir and allowed the dyer to simply keep the wool (paying only for the undyed raw material), the dyer would be benefiting from the fact that this person brought him the wool and set the entire transaction in motion. That is an improper benefit from another's property, and so Rabbi Yehuda restructures the outcome to prevent it.

### Debt and Promissory Note

The third case: *mi she-para miktzat chovo ve-hishlish et shtaro* — a debtor who has paid a portion of a debt and has the *shtar* (promissory note) deposited with a third party as a form of security arrangement. The scenario: Reuven owes Shimon one hundred dollars. Reuven pays twenty-five and, rather than receiving a receipt, proposes the following: the *shtar* — which records the full hundred — is given to Levi as a neutral custodian. Reuven says to Levi: if I pay Shimon the remaining seventy-five by next Monday, you will return the *shtar* to me, confirming only seventy-five is outstanding. But *ve-im eini noten lecha mi-kan ve-ad zman ploni ten lo shtaro* — if I fail to pay Shimon by next Monday, give him back the *shtar*, and I will owe the entire hundred again (with the twenty-five previously paid treated as a gift).

The question is whether this conditional arrangement is binding. *Higia zman ve-lo natan* — the deadline arrives and Reuven does not pay. Rabbi Yosi rules *yitein* — Levi must hand the *shtar* back to Shimon, who may then collect the full hundred. Rabbi Yehuda rules *lo yitein* — no, the condition is not binding, and Levi does not return the *shtar*. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, Reuven owes only what he actually owes, and the conditional promise to forfeit his prior payment is not enforceable.

Once again, the concern is improper benefit: the arrangement as structured would allow Shimon to collect a hundred when only seventy-five is genuinely owed, profiting from a conditional promise that Reuven made under duress. Rabbi Yehuda, following his broader principle, refuses to validate such an outcome.

## Case-by-Case Legal Princ…

### Benefit + Damage Principle

The *gemara* pushes back on Rabbi Yochanan's unified framing, arguing that each of Rabbi Yehuda's three rulings may rest on its own distinct legal principle rather than a single overarching ethical norm. In the case of the broken house: *dilma ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Yehuda hacha ela mishum de-ika shachrurita* — perhaps Rabbi Yehuda rules here only because there is a concrete element of *zeh neheneh ve-zeh chaser*. The person living in the newly built house blackens and wears the walls through ordinary habitation. When the ground-floor owner ultimately reclaims the house, he receives a structure already lived in and somewhat deteriorated. This is not a case where one benefits while the other loses nothing — the ground-floor owner suffers a genuine loss in the condition of his property. The obligation to pay rent flows straightforwardly from *me-ikar ha-din* — the basic legal rule that one does not benefit at another's expense — rather than from any special ethical principle of Rabbi Yehuda's.

### Changer Has Lower Hand

In the dyer case, the *gemara* proposes: *mishum de-ka meshani* — Rabbi Yehuda rules as he does because the dyer changed the agreement. There is a well-established principle, *kol ha-meshaneh yado al ha-tachtonah* — whoever deviates from the terms of an arrangement is in the weaker position and cannot dictate the outcome. The ruling against the dyer flows not from any ethical concern about improper benefit, but from the standard contractual principle that the party who departs from the agreed terms bears the consequences. *Shinui* here is not *koneh* — it does not transfer ownership — because the dyer was not an authorized agent of change.

### Asmachta Not Binding

In the debt case, the *gemara* explains: *u-mi she-para miktzat chovo nami havei asmachta* — the conditional forfeiture of the prior payment is an *asmachta*, a conditional promise made without genuine expectation or full commitment to follow through. A person who says "if I fail to do X by a given date, I will owe double" is making a commitment he does not truly believe he will need to honor; such a commitment lacks the requisite mental resolve — *gemirat da'at* — to be legally binding. *U-shaminan lei le-Rabbi Yehuda de-amar lo kani* — and we know from elsewhere that Rabbi Yehuda holds that an *asmachta* does not effect an acquisition. The ruling is therefore a direct application of the *asmachta* doctrine, not a novel ethical principle about improper benefit.

Rashi's comment here is particularly pointed: he indicates that this very *halacha* — that Rabbi Yehuda rules the *shtar* is not returned — is in fact the source from which we derive Rabbi Yehuda's position on *asmachta*. The case is not a corollary of a known principle; it is the textual basis for the principle itself.

## Building Modifications R…

### Ground Floor Modifications

Rav Aha bar Adda in the name of Ulla now turns to a related question: what structural modifications may the ground-floor owner make when rebuilding, and what changes may the upper-floor owner make to his own story?

Regarding the ground floor: *tachton ba li-shanot* — if the ground-floor owner wishes to alter the construction from the original. *Be-gvil shomin lo* — if he wishes to replace the original stones with *gvil*, large unhewn stones, we listen to him. Unhewn stones are wider and thicker than finished, hewn stones; they make for a stronger and more solid foundation, which is beneficial for the upper story resting on it. *Be-gvil be-gozit ein shomin lo* — but if he wishes to move in the opposite direction, from *gvil* to *gozit* (hewn, finished stones), we do not listen to him. *Gozit* stones may be more aesthetically refined, but they are not as wide, and the resulting foundation is weaker. The upper-floor owner has a right to expect no less structural integrity than he originally had.

Similarly: *be-kfisim shomin lo, be-lveinim ein shomin lo* — if the ground-floor owner wishes to build with *kfisim*, half-bricks, we listen to him. Two half-bricks with plaster between them produce a wall that is actually wider and stronger than a standard full brick (*lveina*). So switching from standard bricks to *kfisim* strengthens the structure; this is permitted. The reverse — switching from *kfisim* to the single standard brick — is not permitted, as it weakens the walls.

In roofing: *le-sachech be-arazim shomin lo, be-shikmim ein shomin lo* — cedar (*arazim*), the *arzei ha-levanon* of classical fame, is a stronger and more durable roofing material than sycamore (*shikmim*). The ground-floor owner may switch to cedar, which only benefits the structure; he may not switch away from cedar to sycamore.

Window placement: *le-ma'et be-chalonot shomin lo, le-harbot be-chalonot ein shomin lo* — fewer windows create thicker, stronger walls, which is better for the load-bearing capacity of the structure supporting the upper floor. So reducing windows is permitted; increasing windows weakens the walls and is not permitted.

Ceiling height: *le-hagbiha ein shomin lo, le-ma'et shomin lo* — a taller ceiling on the ground floor means the upper floor is elevated higher, requiring the tenant to climb more stairs, and may affect the structural dynamics of the building. Therefore raising the ceiling is not permitted. Lowering the ceiling — giving the upper-floor owner less height to climb and placing the upper floor closer to the ground — is permitted.

### Second Floor Modifications

The rules governing the upper-floor owner's modifications run in the opposite direction on most counts, reflecting the different structural considerations: the concern here is that the upper story not become heavier or place greater downward pressure on the ground floor below it.

*Be-gozit shomin lo, be-gvil ein shomin lo* — the upper-floor owner may switch to hewn stones (*gozit*), which are lighter; he may not switch to unhewn stones (*gvil*), which are heavier and would impose additional load on the lower structure. *Be-kfisim ein shomin lo, be-lveinim shomin lo* — he may not switch to the double-half-brick construction (heavier), but may switch to single standard bricks (lighter). *Be-arazim ein shomin lo, be-shikmim shomin lo* — cedar is heavier and stronger; he may not add the weight of cedar roofing, but may switch to the lighter sycamore. *Le-harbot be-chalonot shomin lo, le-ma'et be-chalonot ein shomin lo* — more windows mean lighter walls, less downward pressure; this is permitted. Fewer windows mean heavier walls; not permitted.

### Structural Change Principle

The underlying principle emerges clearly: a change that strengthens the structure or lightens the load from below is permissible; a change in the opposite direction — one that weakens the foundation or adds weight — may not be imposed on the other party. One should note that the rule about ceiling height is the single point where both owners share the same restriction: *le-hagbiha ein shomin lo, le-ma'et shomin lo* applies identically to both the ground-floor and upper-floor owners. Raising the overall height of either story is not permitted; reducing it is. In every other category, the permitted and forbidden directions are mirror images of each other.

## Land Ownership Division

### Rabbi Noson Ratio

When neither party has the ability or desire to rebuild, a question arises about the underlying land rights. This question assumes the two owners never explicitly apportioned ownership of the land itself — perhaps they each inherited a share, or jointly purchased the building with an agreement to divide it by floor, without ever specifying who owns what portion of the ground. A *baraita* records the dispute. *Rabbi Noson omer tachton notel shnei chalakim ve-elyon shlish* — Rabbi Noson holds that the ground-floor owner has a two-thirds share of the land, while the upper-floor owner has a one-third share.

### Rabbis Ratio

*Ve-chachamim omrim tachton shlosha chalakim ve-elyon revi'a* — the Rabbis hold that the ground-floor owner has three-quarters of the land and the upper-floor owner has only one-quarter. Both opinions agree that the ground-floor owner holds the larger share, given his greater proximity and connection to the land itself, but the precise fractions are disputed.

### Rava's Conclusion

*Amar Rava: nakat Rabbi Noson be-yadcha* — Rava rules that one should accept Rabbi Noson's position and *pasken* accordingly. The justification: *de-dayana hu ve-nachis le-umka de-dina* — Rabbi Noson served as the head of the *beit din* and penetrated to the depths of monetary law. His ruling is therefore the authoritative one on this question.

### Impact on Rights Explanation

Rashi explains the conceptual basis for Rabbi Noson's one-third allocation to the upper floor: because the presence of a second story shortens the effective lifespan of the ground floor by roughly a third — the additional weight and structural stress imposed by the upper story reduces the durability of the lower — this demonstrates that the upper-floor owner exercises a degree of claim over the property equivalent to a third of its total value. Rashi's formulation leaves some room for further analysis of exactly how to connect the impact on structural longevity with proportional land ownership.

Tosafot approaches the question differently, and from a different angle. Tosafot notes that this entire allocation may seem surprising: one might naturally assume that the ground-floor owner *is* the owner of the property in the fullest sense, and that the upper-floor owner merely owns the structure of the second story and the right to access it — but holds no independent stake in the underlying land. The *chiddush* of the *gemara*'s conclusion, then, is that since the second story exerts a real and measurable impact on the ground floor, this impact itself generates a form of proprietary right. The upper-floor owner is not merely a leaseholder of an upper structure; he has a genuine fractional interest in the land — whether that fraction is one-third per Rabbi Noson, or one-quarter per the Rabbis.
