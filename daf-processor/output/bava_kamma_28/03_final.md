# Bava Kamma 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Adam HaMazik & Negligence

### Negligence Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁטִּינְּפוּ כֵּלָיו בַּמַּיִם, אֲבָל הוּא עַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר; קַרְקַע עוֹלָם הִזִּיקַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: They taught** that the owner of the jug is liable **only when the clothes of** the one who slipped **were soiled by the** dirty **water. But** if the person **himself** was injured, the owner of the jug is **exempt,** as it is **the ground** that **caused his injury,** not the jug or the water.

The opening discussion recaps several essential principles established in the preceding material concerning the levels of negligence required — or not required — for liability under the category of *adam hammazik*. With respect to *nezek*, the *gemara* indicates that even an act of pure *ones* — complete absence of negligence — generates liability. The additional payments associated with personal injury — *tza'ar*, *nezek* in the narrower sense, and *refu'i* — require at least some degree of negligence, specifically *ruach metzuyah*, a commonly prevailing wind or recognizable circumstance. Crucially, however, *kavanah* — actual intent — is not necessary for these. Only *boshet* requires a higher threshold, namely intention to act as a *mazik*, though not necessarily the specific intention to cause embarrassment.

With this background established, the *gemara* opened the new *perek* and presented the case of a person who breaks a pitcher in *reshut harabbim*. Under the principle *ein darkan shel bnei adam lehistakel badrachim* — people do not ordinarily watch where they are going — such a person may be exempt from liability. The precise scope of this exemption turns on whether one normally watches where one walks; in circumstances where that is not the norm, or where conditions make it impossible, the person who broke the pitcher is exempt.

### Tosafot vs. Ramban

This raises an immediate tension with the principle of *adam mu'ad le'olam* — that a person is always forewarned and therefore always liable for his actions. Two major *rishonic* positions resolve the tension differently.

*Tosafot* holds that *adam mu'ad le'olam* does not mean absolute liability regardless of circumstances. Rather, it means only that one need not rise to a level of full, deliberate negligence — but a total *ones* still exempts. *Tosafot* thus softens the force of the principle and allows for exemption when negligence is genuinely absent.

The *Ramban*, by contrast, maintains that *adam mu'ad le'olam* holds in full — no negligence whatsoever is required. The exemption in the case of the broken pitcher arises from an entirely different source.

### Peshiat HaNizak

According to the *Ramban*, the question of whether people ordinarily watch where they are going is not introduced to establish that the *mazik* had some minimum level of negligence. Since the *Ramban* holds that no negligence is required at all, there is nothing to establish on that front. Rather, the inquiry is directed at the *nizak* — the injured party. If it is commonly known that people do not look where they are going, then placing a pitcher in *reshut harabbim* is itself an act of *peshiat hanizak* — contributory negligence on the part of the victim. The pitcher-owner should have anticipated that ordinary pedestrians walk without watching closely, and the resulting exemption flows from that negligence, not from any deficiency in the *mazik*'s fault.

## Avid Inish Dina Lenafshei

### Rav Yehuda vs. Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִתְּמַר, רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: לָא עָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: עָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it was stated,** that **Rav Yehuda says: A person may not take justice into his own** hands, whereas **Rav Naḥman says: A person may take justice into his own** hands.

The *gemara* now turns to the critical principle of *avid inish dina lenafshei* — whether a person may take the law into his own hands. The occasion for this discussion is a case in which someone drew water from a well on a day that was not his designated turn, and the other party struck him with a shovel. The question is what fine, if any, the one who struck with the shovel must pay. Rav Nachman ruled that no fine is owed, because the person was acting under the license of *avid inish dina lenafshei* — protecting his own interests and enforcing his own rights directly.

The *gemara* presents the dispute formally: *Rav Yehuda amar lo avid inish dina lenafshei* — a person may not take the law into his own hands. *Rav Nachman amar avid inish dina lenafshei* — a person may.

### Makom Psida Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּעָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Where there is** an imminent **loss** that will be suffered if the injured party does not take action, **everyone agrees that a person may take justice into his own** hands.

Before delineating the precise disagreement, the *gemara* establishes a point of consensus: *heicha de'ika psida, kulei alma lo pligi de'avid inish dina lenafshei* — where there is a genuine loss, meaning that waiting to pursue the matter through the courts would result in an irrecoverable or irreversible harm, everyone agrees that one may take the law into one's own hands. The urgency of preventing actual loss justifies unilateral action.

### Leika Psida Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי – הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא; רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: לָא עָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, דְּכֵיוָן דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא – לֵיזִיל קַמֵּיהּ דַּיָּינָא. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: עָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, דְּכֵיוָן דִּבְדִין עָבֵיד – לָא טָרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree** only **when there is no** imminent **loss** that will be suffered. **Rav Yehuda says** that **a person may not take justice into his own** hands, **because since there is no loss, he should go before the judge** to have him enforce the law. **Rav Naḥman says** that **a person may take justice into his own** hands. **Since he is acting lawfully,** as he is clearly in the right, **he need not trouble** himself to go before the judge to have him enforce the law.

The dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Nachman applies specifically to the case of *leika psida* — where there is no imminent loss, meaning one could in principle go to court and fully recover any damages after the fact. Rav Yehuda holds that in this scenario one may not act unilaterally: *keivan de'leika psida leizil kamei dayana* — since there is no loss, go before a judge. Rav Nachman disagrees: *keivan de'bedin avid lo tarach* — since the person is acting entirely within his legal right, we do not impose on him the burden and effort of litigating the matter in court. Implicit in this debate is the question of whether the costs, time, and effort involved in court proceedings themselves constitute a *psida* sufficient to justify self-help. That seems, in fact, to be precisely what is being contested.

## Ben Bag Bag's Case

### Honest Retrieval

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן בַּג בַּג אוֹמֵר: אַל תִּיכָּנֵס לַחֲצַר חֲבֵרְךָ לִיטּוֹל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, שֶׁמָּא תֵּרָאֶה עָלָיו כְּגַנָּב. אֶלָּא שְׁבוֹר אֶת שִׁינָּיו, וֶאֱמוֹר לוֹ: שֶׁלִּי אֲנִי נוֹטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Bag Bag says: Do not enter another** person's **courtyard** secretly **to take what is** rightfully **yours without permission, lest you appear to him as a thief** trying to steal his property. **Rather, break his teeth,** i.e., take it by force, **and say to him: I am taking what is mine.**

Rav Kahana raises a challenge from a *baraita* quoting Ben Bag Bag: *al tikanes l'chatzer chaveircha litol et shelcha shelo birshut* — do not sneak into someone else's courtyard to retrieve your own object without permission. *Shema tera'eh alav k'ganav* — you will appear like a thief, and conducting oneself in a stealthy, deceptive manner is improper both in terms of character and communal perception. Rather, Ben Bag Bag says, *shvor shinav v'emor lo sheli ani notel* — break his teeth and tell him openly: I am taking what is mine. The lesson is that self-help is permitted when conducted honestly and openly, even if it involves the use of force. Sneaking and deception, even in pursuit of a just claim, are prohibited; directness and transparency, even if forceful, are permitted.

This case is notable because, unlike the shovel at the well, this appears to be a scenario of *leika psida* — there is no evidence that leaving the object in the other person's possession would cause ongoing financial loss — yet force is still apparently sanctioned.

### Minority Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עִמָּךְ?! בֶּן בַּג בַּג יְחִידָאָה הוּא, וּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** You can keep this question **to yourself,** as it poses no difficulty at all. **Ben Bag Bag** states **an individual** opinion in this regard, **and the Rabbis disagree with him.**

The *gemara* responds to the challenge by pointing out that Ben Bag Bag is a *yechida'ah* — a lone, minority opinion — and that the rabbis may well disagree with him. The fact that Ben Bag Bag permits the use of force in retrieving one's own property, even in a case without imminent loss, does not necessarily reflect normative halachic consensus.

A further important observation emerges from examining these cases together. In every instance where *avid inish dina lenafshei* is invoked — whether the well, the house entry, the physical confrontation — the other side suffers some tangible harm: injury to his person, damage to his property, or both. This is what makes the principle legally significant. If one's object were simply lying in plain sight and recovery required no violation of the other party's rights, there would be no question — one simply takes it back. The entire issue of *avid inish dina lenafshei* arises precisely because the act of retrieval involves some harm to the opposing party: trespassing, striking, damaging property. The permissibility of causing that harm is what is being adjudicated.

### Rav Yannai's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: מַאי ״שְׁבוֹר אֶת שִׁינָּיו״ – בַּדִּין. אִי הָכִי, ״וֶאֱמוֹר לוֹ״?! ״וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! ״שֶׁלִּי אֲנִי נוֹטֵל״?! ״שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא נוֹטֵל״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai says** an alternative response to Rav Kahana's objection: **What** does the *baraita* mean by the phrase: **Break his teeth?** It means sue him **in judgment. If so,** the subsequent statement in the *baraita*: **And say to him,** is inaccurate. It **should have** stated: **And they,** the court, **say to him. Likewise, the wording: I am taking what is mine,** is inaccurate. Since it is the court who makes this statement, the *baraita* **should have** stated: **He is taking what is his.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this poses **a difficulty** for Rav Yehuda's version of the response.

Rav Yannai attempts to preserve Ben Bag Bag's opinion while reading it more conservatively. He proposes that *shvor shinav* — break his teeth — is not meant literally. Rather, it means *b'din*: drag him before the court. Do not sneak in like a thief, but bring the matter openly to adjudication.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reading: if the intended meaning is to bring the case to court, the language *sheli ani notel* — I am taking what is mine — is peculiar. If it were the court acting on the person's behalf, one would expect the formulation *shelo hu notel* — the court is recovering what is his — not *I* am taking what is mine. The *gemara* pronounces this reading *kashya* — difficult — and the plain sense of Ben Bag Bag's words remains that he envisions direct, forceful, honest retrieval, not court proceedings.

## Force in Self-Help

### Ox on Ox Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שׁוֹר שֶׁעָלָה עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ לְהוֹרְגוֹ, וּבָא בַּעַל הַתַּחְתּוֹן וְשָׁמַט אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ, וְנָפַל עֶלְיוֹן וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. מַאי, לָאו בְּמוּעָד – דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an alternative proof for Rav Naḥman's opinion. It is stated in a *baraita*: With regard to **an ox that climbed on the back of another** ox **to kill it, and the owner of the** ox on the **bottom came and removed his** ox, **and the** ox **on top** consequently **fell and died,** the owner of the ox on the bottom is **exempt** from paying for the dead ox. **What, is** this ruling **not** stated **with regard to** a case where the attacker is **a forewarned** ox, **where there is no loss** to the owner of the bottom ox, since even if the owner of the ox on the bottom does not remove his ox, he will eventually receive full restitution from the owner of the belligerent ox? Yet he is exempt from paying restitution for causing the death of the belligerent ox. Evidently, he had the right to take justice into his own hands.

The *gemara* brings a further case: *shor she'alah gabei chaveiro lehorgo* — an ox climbs on top of another ox with the apparent intention of killing the bottom one. The owner of the bottom ox, faced with imminent loss, pulls his ox out from underneath, causing the top ox to fall and die. The ruling is *patur* — he is exempt — because he was simply protecting his own property.

The *gemara* then asks: in what case does this apply? Presumably, it must be discussing even a *mu'ad* — an ox that had been forewarned, such that the owner of the bottom ox could theoretically recover his full damages in court. If it were only a *tam*, whose owner would recover merely half, the financial loss would be apparent and the exemption would be easy to explain under the *makom psida* principle. But if it is a *mu'ad*, full recovery in court is available, and the exemption implies that *avid inish dina lenafshei* applies even where there is *leika psida*.

### Minimal Force Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בְּתָם – דְּאִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא. אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: דְּחָפוֹ לָעֶלְיוֹן וָמֵת – חַיָּיב. וְאִי בְּתָם, אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לְשׇׁמְטוֹ, וְלֹא שְׁמָטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** the ruling is **with regard to** a case where the attacker is **an innocuous** ox, **where there is a loss** for the owner of the ox on the bottom if he does not save it, since the owner of an innocuous ox pays for only half the damages resulting from his ox's attack. The Gemara asks: **If so, say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: If, instead of removing his own ox, the owner of the ox on the bottom **pushed the one on top and it died,** he is **liable. And if** it is referring **to an innocuous** ox, **why** is he **liable?** He is defending his property so as not to incur a loss. The Gemara answers: He is liable **because he should have removed** his ox from underneath the belligerent ox, **and he did not remove it.** Instead, he pushed the belligerent ox and caused it to die. Although one may take justice into his own hands, if he damages another person's property when he could have protected his own property in a harmless manner he is liable.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps the case concerns a *tam* — and therefore there is indeed a *psida* (the owner of the bottom ox would recover only half), which is why the intervention is permitted. The *gemara* then turns to the second clause of that *baraita*: *dechaf'o l'elyon u-met, chayav* — if instead the owner of the bottom ox *pushed the top ox off*, causing it to fall and die, he is *chayav*. If the entire case concerns a *tam*, with a genuine *psida* at stake, why does pushing the top ox create liability? Why should the mode of response matter?

The *gemara* answers: *she'hayah lo l'shamto v'lo shamto* — even in the case of a *tam*, where a real loss was at stake, the owner should have pulled the bottom ox out rather than pushed the top one off. This is a striking ruling. It introduces the principle that *avid inish dina lenafshei* does not confer unlimited license. Even when one is justified in taking protective action, one must use the minimum necessary force or the least damaging available method. Pulling out one's own ox is the act of someone protecting his property. Pushing the other ox off is something more — it is a direct act against the other party's animal that goes beyond what self-protection required. When an alternative was feasible, resorting to the more aggressive option strips the act of its protective character and redefines it as unauthorized damage.

A natural question arises: is the distinction really about which act causes more damage? Perhaps pulling the bottom ox out would in fact cause the same result. The better answer appears to be that the distinction is conceptual and definitional. When a feasible alternative exists, the choice of the more aggressive method makes it impossible to characterize the act purely as protecting one's own property. The act then bears the quality of attacking the other party's ox, and for that one is liable.

The earlier case of striking with a shovel or Ben Bag Bag's "break his teeth" involved scenarios where no gentler alternative was available — the only path to protection was through the forceful act. Here, the alternative of pulling the bottom ox out was genuinely feasible. The *hayah lo l'shamto* formulation of the *gemara* implies that where such an alternative is not feasible, even pushing the top ox would presumably be permitted.

### Conceptual Basis

This brings into focus a deeper question about the conceptual underpinning of *avid inish dina lenafshei*. One approach — natural to the framework of *Bava Kamma* — is that the license is grounded in the right to protect one's own property. Just as one may use force to repel a thief in the act of stealing, so too may one use force to recover property that is currently being wrongfully withheld. The continued possession of one's object by another is itself a kind of ongoing theft, and force employed to end that situation is force in defense of one's own property.

On this view, the principle applies when there is a concrete object belonging to the claimant that is in another's possession. It would not extend to cases where one is merely seeking monetary compensation — where the original property has been consumed or replaced and what remains is an obligation to pay. Compelling payment is not recovering one's property; it is enforcing an obligation, and that requires the courts. This limiting principle, associated with the *Mordechai*, makes sense of why the cases in the *gemara* universally involve specific tangible objects and also explains why *avid inish dina lenafshei* cannot justify, say, entering someone's house and removing cash equivalent to a debt owed.

The *Rambam*, by contrast, takes a strikingly different approach in *Hilchos Sanhedrin*. He observes that while a *beis din* conventionally requires three judges, *me'ikar hadin* — as a matter of strict law — a *beis din* of one is valid. And that, the *Rambam* says, is the conceptual basis of *avid inish dina lenafshei*: a person may act as a *beis din* of one. This framing moves the principle entirely out of the domain of property protection and into the domain of adjudication. On the *Rambam*'s view, the person is not merely defending his belongings — he is actually exercising judicial authority. The peculiarity of this position is obvious: a litigant is ordinarily disqualified as a *karov eitzel atzmo*, someone who is too closely related to himself to be objective. Yet the *Rambam* apparently considers the self-adjudication valid. The implications may also be broader: if the basis is acting as a one-man *beis din* rather than protecting concrete property, then perhaps even compensation claims — not only recovery of specific objects — could fall within the scope of *avid inish dina lenafshei*.

There is an important practical corollary to consider across all these cases. The filling of the *chatzer* with barrels of wine and oil, for instance: the owner of the *chatzer* may break them going in and out, *meshabeir v'yotzei l'veito u'meshabeir v'nichnas l'havi zechuyosav*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains this as the *chatzer*-owner needing to enter and exit in order to access his home or retrieve documents for litigation. The inability to use one's home could itself be characterized as a *hefsed*. The cases of the *eved Ivri* who refuses to leave and the related discussion of the *eved ganva* introduce scenarios where the *psida* arises from the threat that a slave who has lost his fear of the master will begin stealing — *arei de'idna hava aimta demarei aleh, hashta leit lei aimta demarei aleh*. Yet another case — the *eved* to whom a *shifcha Kena'anit* had been given — involves not property protection at all but the prevention of an ongoing *aveira*, which the *gemara* treats as a separate category, not properly within the scope of the *avid inish dina lenafshei* discussion.

The case of the pitchers filling the *reshut harabbim* also bears on this question. The *gemara* cites the principle that one who accidentally trips on a pitcher left in the public domain is exempt — *ta'ama denitkal bah, ha shibrah chayav*: the reason the *gemara* specifies *nitkal* (accidentally tripped) is that intentional breakage would render one liable. Rav Zevid in the name of Rava explains: even intentional breakage is permitted — the *gemara* specifies *nitkal* only because the *seifa* of the *mishna* deals with injury to the one who trips, and that liability applies specifically when the person accidentally stumbled. If one intentionally broke the pitchers — because the *reshut harabbim* was blocked — one cannot then claim damages from the pitcher's owner for injuries sustained in the act of breaking. *Dehu azik anafsheih* — one brought the injury upon oneself. But the underlying permission to break through the blocked *reshut harabbim* is affirmed. Whether that constitutes a case of *makom psida* or not is itself an interesting question — one's ability to traverse *reshut harabbim* might well be considered a form of *psida* when blocked — but the *gemara*'s analysis confirms the permission regardless.

## Woman Defending Husband

### Seizing Privates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְקַצֹּתָה אֶת כַּפָּהּ״ – מָמוֹן. מַאי, לָאו בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר? לֹא, שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*, which relates to the case of a woman who, during a fight between her husband and another man, grabs the other man's genitals: The verse that states: **"Then you shall cut off her hand"** (Deuteronomy 25:12), should not be taken literally; rather, it is referring to **monetary restitution. What, is it not** referring to a case **where she cannot save** her husband from his attacker **by a means other** than grabbing the attacker's genitals, and nevertheless she is punished? This indicates that one may not take justice into his own hands. The Gemara answers: **No,** the verse is referring to a case **where she can save** him **by other means.** Otherwise she is exempt from paying restitution.

The *gemara* now invokes the *mishna* in the context of *boshet* — the passage from Devarim concerning a woman who, while her husband is being beaten by another man, intervenes by seizing the attacker's private parts. The *Torah* says *v'katzotah et kappah* — her hand shall be cut off — which the *gemara* understands not literally but as the obligation to pay *boshet*, the payment for causing embarrassment. The obvious question the passage raises: what exactly did she do wrong? She was protecting her husband. Should this not be a case of *avid inish dina lenafshei*?

The *gemara* asks: *mai lav b'she'eina yechola lehatzil al yedei davar acher* — is this not speaking of a case where she had no other means of stopping the attacker? If so, this should plainly qualify as *avid inish dina lenafshei*, and she should be exempt from paying *boshet*.

It is worth pausing here over a related question. Before this point, the discussion of *avid inish dina lenafshei* had raised the issue of whether the principle extends to causing personal injury to another, not merely property damage. Ben Bag Bag's *shvor shinav* — break his teeth — suggests that it does. Now a further question emerges: if someone is about to beat you and you would be entitled afterward to collect *tza'ar*, *nezek*, *shevet*, *refu'i*, and *boshet* — all five heads of *chavel* damages — is that situation considered *makom psida* or *leika psida*? Intuitively, personal injury seems to belong in the category of *makom psida* — one's body is not a financial asset from which one recovers even if compensated. Yet the *gemara* apparently treats this as a *leika psida* scenario, which is puzzling. Perhaps the *gemara* is focusing specifically on the *boshet* inflicted on the attacker, which is purely an embarrassment claim and not physical harm to the defending woman or her husband.

### Necessity Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר – פְּטוּרָה? אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״וְשָׁלְחָה יָדָהּ״ – פְּרָט לִשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין; לִפְלוֹג וְלִתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּשֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר, אֲבָל אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר – פְּטוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if she cannot save** him **by other means,** is it possible that **she** is **exempt? If so, instead of teaching** in **the latter clause** of that *baraita* that the expression mentioned in the previous verse: **"And extended her hand"** (Deuteronomy 25:11), **excludes an emissary of the court,** who is authorized to act in this manner and is therefore exempt from paying restitution, **let** the *baraita* **distinguish and teach within** the case under discussion in the verse **itself,** as follows: **In what** case **is this statement** that the wife is liable **said?** It is **in** a case **where she can save** her husband **by another means. But** if **she cannot save** him **by other means, she** is **exempt.**

The *gemara* answers its own question: *lo b'sheyachol lehatzil al yedei davar acher* — no, the *mishna* is speaking of a case where she *could* have rescued her husband through some less drastic means. Since a less harmful alternative was available and she did not avail herself of it, she overstepped the bounds of what *avid inish dina lenafshei* permits. This directly parallels the principle established in the ox-on-ox case: even legitimate self-help is bounded by the requirement to employ the minimum necessary force. Exceeding what necessity demands transforms the act from *din* — lawful self-help — into excess, for which one is liable.

The *gemara* then asks: if that is so, why does the *seifa* of that *baraita* need to specifically exempt a *shliach beis din* — a court officer who performs the same act? If even the woman herself would be exempt when she had no alternative, why mention the court officer separately as a special exemption? The *gemara* responds: *hachi nami ka'amar* — the *baraita* is indeed saying both things. *B'meh devarim amurim b'sheyachol lehatzil al yedei davar acher* — when is she liable? When she could have acted through less drastic means. *Aval eino yachol lehatzil al yedei davar acher, na'asa yada k'shliach beis din u'ptura* — but when she had no other option, her hand becomes like that of a *shliach beis din* and she is exempt.

### Shliach Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּשֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר, אֲבָל אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר – נַעֲשָׂה יָדָהּ כִּשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין, וּפְטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That is also what** the *baraita* **is saying: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **in** a case **where she can save** him **by other means. But** if **she cannot save** him **by other means, her hand is rendered like an emissary of the court, and she is exempt.**

This resolution carries a significant conceptual implication. When a person acts within the proper parameters of *avid inish dina lenafshei* — necessity, no available alternative, minimum required force — that person is deemed to be acting as an agent of the court. This framing provides striking support for the *Rambam*'s understanding of the principle: *avid inish dina lenafshei* is not merely a pragmatic license to protect property, but is genuinely characterized as a form of judicial action. The person is, in a real sense, functioning as *beis din*.

One might object: she is protecting her husband, not herself — how is this *l'nafshei*, for herself? The answer presumably lies in the concept of *ishto k'gufo* — a spouse is regarded as part of oneself — such that protecting one's husband constitutes protecting oneself. This simultaneously raises the question of third parties. If the basis of *avid inish dina lenafshei* is the right to protect one's own property or interests, then acting on behalf of an unrelated third party — retrieving someone else's object from a wrongful possessor — would not be covered, even if one knows with certainty that the object belongs to the third party. The *l'nafshei* is essential and limiting. On the *Rambam*'s view, however — where the person is functioning as a one-person *beis din* — the scope might conceivably extend further, though that too would risk producing total social disorder. An additional curiosity follows from the *Rambam*'s framing: the *gemara* concludes that the woman is acting as *shliach beis din*, and the *Rambam* says she is acting as *dan yachidi* — a sole judge. The possibility of a woman serving as *dayan* in monetary cases is implied, which is itself a remarkable point.

## Public Thoroughfare Case

### Path Rerouting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיְתָה דֶּרֶךְ הָרַבִּים עוֹבֶרֶת בְּתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ; נְטָלָהּ, וְנָתַן לָהֶם מִן הַצַּד – מַה שֶּׁנָּתַן נָתַן, וְשֶׁלּוֹ לֹא הִגִּיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof for Rav Yehuda's opinion from a mishna (*Bava Batra* 99b): In a case **where a public thoroughfare would pass through** one's **field,** and **he appropriated it and** instead **gave** the public an alternative route **on the side** of his property, the *halakha* is that the route **that he has given** is the route **that he gave** them, and they may use it. **But his** property, which he appropriated, **has not reached him,** i.e., he cannot appropriate it for his personal use since it was already in public use.

The *gemara* continues with another case: *harei she'haysa derech harabbim u'overet b'toch sadeihu* — a public thoroughfare was passing through one's private field. The landowner, wishing to eliminate the path cutting through the middle of his land, unilaterally seized it and *nasan lahem min hatzad* — gave the public an alternate path along the edge of his field. The ruling of the *mishna* is: *ma shenasan nasan v'shelo lo higi'o* — whatever path he gave them, they keep, but his original path they also retain. The public does not lose their rights to the original path simply because the owner offered them an alternative.

The *gemara* asks: if *avid inish dina lenafshei* applies, why can the owner not simply stand at the original path and physically bar anyone who tries to use it? He did a swap — the public gets the new path — so why can he not enforce the abandonment of the old one by force?

### Gezeira Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִתֵּן לָהֶם דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava: It is** a rabbinic **decree, lest he give them a circuitous route.** Since the public has the right to pass through his field, as the Gemara will soon explain, the owner cannot appropriate their thoroughfare without giving the public a similar alternative route. Therefore, the Sages decreed that he cannot appropriate it without their consent, lest he provide an inadequate route.

Rav Zevid in the name of Rava answers: *gezeira shema yitjen lahem derech akalason* — it is a rabbinic safeguard. Even if this particular landowner offered an equivalent alternate path, we are concerned that other landowners will similarly reroute public paths but give circuitous or inconvenient alternatives — *derech akalaton*. Because of that concern, we do not permit any self-help enforcement of a rerouting, even where the swap was genuinely fair. The public retains its rights in the original path as a blanket policy.

### Rav Ashi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן לָהֶם דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כֹּל מִן הַצַּד – דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן הוּא, קְרוֹבָה לָזֶה וּרְחוֹקָה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where he** actually **gave them a circuitous route.** If one in fact gives the public a straight path, he need not surrender his rights, and he may take justice into his own hands by blocking the original thoroughfare. **Rav Ashi said: Any** route **on the side** that the property owner might give them **is** necessarily considered **a circuitous route,** as **it is close for this** person, **and it is far for that** person. While some will benefit from the change, it will be detrimental for others.

Rav Ashi disagrees with the need for a *gezeira*. He holds that the *baraita* itself is describing precisely the case of a roundabout path: *kegon shenasan lahem derech akalason* — the case involves a situation where he gave them a *derech akalaton* to begin with. And more fundamentally, *kol min hatzad derech akalason hu* — any path that differs from the original is inherently a *derech akalaton*. Why? *Karov lezeh v'rachok lezeh* — any rerouting will be more convenient for some users and less convenient for others. Since there is no such thing as a genuinely equivalent alternate path — every shift imposes a burden on at least some members of the public — no rerouting can be considered a fair swap, and the public never loses its rights to the original path.

### Public Acquisition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי שֶׁלּוֹ לֹא הִגִּיעוֹ? לֵימָא לְהוּ: שְׁקוּלוּ דִּידְכוּ, וְהַבוּ לִי דִּידִי! מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב יְהוּדָה – דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מֵיצַר שֶׁהֶחֱזִיקוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים – אָסוּר לְקַלְקְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, why has his** property **not reached him?** If he cannot make the change, why can he not reclaim the path that he intended to turn over to the public? **Let him say to them: Take your** original route **and give me** back **my** property, so that he will not lose both pieces of land. The Gemara answers that he cannot reclaim it **due to Rav Yehuda's** statement, **as Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **a path that the masses established** as a public thoroughfare, **it is prohibited to destroy it** for them. Therefore, since the public already began using the alternative route, the owner cannot appropriate it from them.

This creates a further complication: if the swap was not legally valid, why does the public retain the alternate path that the landowner gave them? The *gemara* responds with a ruling of Rav Yehuda: *meitzar shehecheziku bo rabbim assur lekalkelo* — a path or boundary that the public has already begun using cannot be taken away from them. Once the public actually travels the alternate route, they acquire rights to it by virtue of that use, akin to a principle of eminent domain through public use. The result is that the landowner finds himself bound on both counts: the public never relinquished the original path (because the swap was invalid), and once they began using the new path, they acquired it as well. It should be noted that this form of *mitzar shehecheziku bo rabbim* does not, in classical *halacha*, automatically carry with it a right to compensation for the landowner.

## Peah Case

### Alternate Side Taking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁהִנִּיחַ פֵּאָה מִצַּד אֶחָד, וּבָאוּ עֲנִיִּים וְנָטְלוּ מִצַּד אַחֵר – זֶה וָזֶה פֵּאָה. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ עָבֵיד אִינִישׁ דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, אַמַּאי זֶה וָזֶה פֵּאָה? לִנְקוֹט פַּזְרָא וְלִיתֵיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an alternative proof from a *baraita*: With regard to **the owner** of a field **who left produce in the corner of the field, which is given to the poor [*pe'a*], on one side** of the field, **and poor people came and took from another side, this** produce **and that** produce are both considered ***pe'a*. And if you say that a person may take justice into his own** hands, **why** are **this and that** both considered ***pe'a*? Let him take a stick and sit** in his field on the side from which the poor people are taking produce without his permission, and let him warn them to take only from the side that he allocated.

The *gemara* brings one final case: *ba'al habayis shehiniach pe'ah mitzad echad* — a landowner designated *pe'ah* on one side of his field. The poor came and took *pe'ah* from the other side of the field. The ruling is *zeh va'zeh pe'ah* — both are considered *pe'ah*. The *gemara* again asks: if *avid inish dina lenafshei* applies, why does the landowner not simply stand at the unauthorized side and prevent the poor from taking from there, insisting they take only from the designated side? *Lincot pazra v'leisav* — take a shovel and sit there and drive them off.

### Momentary Peah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי ״זֶה וָזֶה פֵּאָה״ – לְפוֹטְרָן מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. כִּדְתַנְיָא: הַמַּפְקִיר אֶת כַּרְמוֹ, וְהִשְׁכִּים בַּבֹּקֶר וּבְצָרוֹ – חַיָּיב בְּפֶרֶט וּבְעוֹלֵלוֹת וּבְשִׁכְחָה וּבְפֵאָה, וּפָטוּר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** In terms of monetary law he can prevent them from taking *pe'a* from the side that he did not allocate. **What** is meant by the ruling of: **This** produce **and that** produce are both considered ***pe'a*?** It means that they are considered *pe'a* **with regard to exempting** both portions of produce **from tithes.** Just as the portion that the poor people took is exempt from tithes, so is the portion that he allocated initially. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who renounces ownership of his vineyard and arose early in the morning** before anyone else took possession of it **and harvested it,** the one who renounces ownership **is obligated in** the mitzva of **individual fallen** grapes left for the poor [***peret***], **and in** the mitzva of **incompletely formed clusters** of grapes left for the poor [***olelot***], **and in** the mitzva of **forgotten** clusters of grapes left for the poor, **and in** the mitzva of ***pe'a*,** the four gifts to the poor that the Torah requires one to give from a vineyard (see Leviticus 19:9–10). **But he is exempt from the** mitzva to **tithe** his produce, because this requirement does not apply to an ownerless field. Likewise, in the above case the portion of the field that was allocated for *pe'a* is exempt from tithes even after the owner reclaims it, as *pe'a* is considered ownerless property in this regard.

Rava resolves this: *mai zeh va'zeh pe'ah liftur min hama'aser* — the ruling that "both are *pe'ah*" does not mean the landowner is obligated to allow the poor to take from both sides. It means that what they took from the unauthorized side was *momentarily* in the status of *pe'ah*. The landowner is entirely entitled to protect the side he had not designated — to use self-help to prevent the poor from taking from it — and he should not be understood as obligated to surrender both portions. The legal significance of the temporary *pe'ah* status is narrower: once produce had momentarily been designated or treated as *pe'ah* — even briefly — the landowner is exempt from *ma'aser* on that produce when he reclaims it. A *baraita* confirms this: one who declared his vineyard *hefker* overnight and then rose early to reclaim it before anyone else took it is *chayav* in *peret*, *olelot*, *shichecha*, and *pe'ah* — the gifts to the poor — but *patur* from *ma'aser*. Similarly, here, the produce that momentarily had *pe'ah* status carries with it a *ma'aser* exemption, even once the owner reasserts possession. The case therefore has no bearing on whether self-help is permitted — it confirms it is — but only on the *ma'aser* consequences of the brief *pe'ah* status.

## General Principle & Limitations

### Rishonim Consensus

The *gemara*'s treatment of all these cases points in one direction. The ruling among the *rishonim* is overwhelmingly that *avid inish dina lenafshei* applies *afilu b'makom de'leika psida* — even where there is no imminent irreversible loss. One need not show that waiting for the courts would result in unrecoverable harm; the license to act unilaterally in protection of one's rightful property or interest exists even when court recovery would theoretically be available.

### Concrete Objects

Yet the principle requires limits, and the *Mordechai* articulates an important one. All of the cases in the *gemara* involve a specific, concrete, tangible object belonging to the claimant — an ox being seized, an object sitting in someone's courtyard, a path being blocked. The *Mordechai* draws from this a limiting principle: *avid inish dina lenafshei* applies to the recovery or protection of concrete property. It does not extend to the enforcement of monetary obligations or compensation claims. If one seeks not the return of a specific object but payment of a debt or indemnification for a loss already suffered, the matter belongs before a court. One may not help oneself to the equivalent in cash from the debtor's wallet under the banner of *avid inish dina lenafshei*. The reason is both conceptual — compensation is not the same as protecting one's property — and practical: permitting self-help in the recovery of monetary compensation would generate total anarchy, with everyone stealing from everyone else and claiming to be merely collecting what they are owed.

### Rashba's Caveat

The *Rashba* was asked a pointed question: a person knows with complete certainty that he is right in a legal dispute — the other side has clearly stolen from him — but he has lost his documentation and cannot prove the claim in court. May he forge the documentation in order to win the case? After all, he knows he is entitled to the outcome; why not *avid inish dina lenafshei* justify securing it by any available means?

The *Rashba*'s answer was emphatic. This is completely forbidden. Forging court documents does not constitute the exercise of self-help in protecting one's property — it constitutes the subversion of the judicial system itself. It transforms *din* and *emet* into a mockery. Whatever license *avid inish dina lenafshei* confers, it is bounded by the integrity of the legal system and cannot be invoked to justify acts that fundamentally undermine the authority and function of the courts.

### Court Authority Balance

The tension inherent in *avid inish dina lenafshei* is thus a tension between two legitimate concerns: the individual's right to protect what is his without being forced through the burdens and delays of litigation on one hand, and the preservation of court authority and social order on the other. Several parameters emerge from the *gemara* and the *rishonim* to manage this tension. Self-help must involve a concrete object belonging to the claimant. The force used must be the minimum necessary — no excessive damage, no action against persons or property beyond what retrieval requires. The license applies to one's own interests, not those of third parties. And the whole framework breaks down entirely where it shades into conduct — like forgery — that corrodes the foundations of the legal system.

Particularly instructive is the distinction between cases of preventing imminent harm and cases of recovering property already in another's hands. Preemptive self-help, where damage has not yet occurred and one is acting to stop it, is in many ways the clearest case — analogous to one's right to repel a thief *ba bemachteres*. Recovery of property already in another's possession raises more complex questions, especially when time has passed and the dynamics of possession have shifted. And allowing physical injury to another person's body — even at the level of *shvor shinav* — is itself a significant *chiddush*, bounded as it is by the requirement that no less drastic means was available.

## Proof in Court

### Burden of Proof

A critical practical question follows from the entire discussion: does the permission granted by *avid inish dina lenafshei* eliminate the obligation to prove one's case afterward? The answer is no. *HaMotzi mChaveiro alav hare'ayah* — the burden of proof always rests on the claimant — remains fully operative. The fact that one acted in self-help does not in itself establish that the action was justified.

### Injured Party Remedy

If the person whose property was damaged or whose person was harmed — whether by the striking of a shovel, the breaking of teeth, or any other act of self-help — brings the matter to court, the person who acted must prove that his action was warranted. He must demonstrate that the object was his, that the other party was wrongfully withholding it, and that the force used was within the parameters that *avid inish dina lenafshei* permits. Only if he succeeds in proving all of this will he be exempt from liability for the harm he caused. *Avid inish dina lenafshei* confers the license to act, not immunity from subsequent judicial scrutiny. As a practical matter, however, this does shift the dynamics: by the time the matter reaches court, the person who acted has already recovered his object and is in possession of it. The burden of initiating litigation now falls on the injured party, and the costs of *tircha* — effort, time, legal fees — have been transferred to him.

## Pitcher in Reshut HaRabim

### Mishna Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִשְׁבְּרָה כַּדּוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהוּחְלַק אֶחָד בַּמַּיִם אוֹ שֶׁלָּקָה בְּחַרְסִית – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּמִתְכַּוֵּין – חַיָּיב, בְּאֵינוֹ מִתְכַּוֵּין – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one's **jug broke in the public domain and** another person **slipped in the water** from the jug and was injured from the fall, **or** if he **was injured by the shards** of the broken jug, the owner of the jug is **liable. Rabbi Yehuda says: In** a case where the owner of the jug acted **with intent,** he is **liable,** and **in** a case where he acted **without intent,** he is **exempt.**

The *gemara* now introduces a new *mishna*: *nishbar kado b'reshut harabbim* — a pitcher broke in the public domain — *v'huchlak echad b'mayim* — and someone slipped on the water — *o shelaka b'charisit* — or was injured by a shard of the broken pitcher. In such cases, *chayav* — the owner of the pitcher is liable. The broken pitcher has effectively created a *bor* in the public domain — a hazard that the owner is responsible for. One question is what degree of negligence is required for the pitcher's breaking: is the owner liable even if it broke entirely by *ones*, or is some negligence necessary? The *mishna*'s ruling seems to place this squarely in the category of *bor*.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies the ruling: *bim'khaven chayav v'eino m'khaven patur* — if the owner intentionally broke the pitcher, he is liable; if unintentionally, he is exempt. Intentionality here, as a threshold for liability, suggests a stricter standard than the default rule — whether it means literally trying to break the pitcher, or some degree of deliberate negligence, is a question the *gemara* will examine. In any case, the *mishna* presents this as a case that sounds like *bor*.

## Water: Shor or Bor? (II)

### Vessel vs. Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: מִכְּדֵי אַבְנוֹ וְסַכִּינוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ – מִבּוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ, וְכוּלָּן אֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן: ״שׁוֹר״ – וְלֹא אָדָם, ״חֲמוֹר״ – וְלֹא כֵּלִים. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן קְטָלָא, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין – אָדָם חַיָּיב, וְכֵלִים פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda continued: **When I stated this** ruling of Rav **before Shmuel, he said to me: After all, we derive** the cases of damage caused by leaving **one's stone, one's knife, or one's load** in the public domain **from** the case of **one's pit, and I** therefore **read,** i.e., apply, **with regard to all of them** the inference of the Sages from the verse: "And an ox or a donkey fall therein" (Exodus 21:33), that the one who dug the pit is liable only if what incurred damage is **an ox, but not a person,** or **a donkey, but not vessels. And this statement applies with regard to** a situation where the person is **killed,** i.e., if one fell into a pit and was killed, the person who dug it is exempt from paying restitution, since the verse refers exclusively to an animal that was killed. **But with regard to damage,** the digger of the pit is **liable** to pay restitution for injury to **a person, but exempt** from paying restitution for damage to **vessels,** for which no distinction between death and injury can be applied.

Rav Yehuda quotes Rav's ruling: *lo shanu ela she'tinfu kelav b'mayim* — the case of liability concerns a situation where the water stained the person's clothing. *Aval hu atzmo patur* — but if the person himself slipped and fell and was injured, the owner of the pitcher is exempt. The reason: *karka olam hizzikato* — the person was injured by the ground itself, not by the water.

This ruling connects to a foundational debate about the classification of objects left in *reshut harabbim* that still belong to their owner. Rav holds — as established earlier in the tractate — that such objects retain their character as the owner's property. An owned object that causes damage is categorized as *shor*, not *bor*. By contrast, Shmuel holds that any object left in *reshut harabbim* that causes damage is classified as *bor*, regardless of whether the owner retains ownership.

This distinction has direct consequences for the *mishna*'s case. The category of *bor* carries the rule that one is exempt for damage to vessels — *bor* liability applies to personal injury and animal deaths, but not to broken objects. If the spilled water is classified as *shor* — because the owner never relinquished ownership — then vessel damage (a stained garment) generates liability. If it is classified as *bor*, vessel damage is exempt. Rav, who says it is a *shor*, can explain why the *mishna* holds one liable for the stained garment. Shmuel, who says it is *bor*, would need to find another reading, since under *bor* the garment damage is categorically exempt.

### Karka Olam

Rav's further ruling — that personal injury from slipping on the water is exempt because of *karka olam* — requires careful analysis. One might assume that the *chiyyuv* of *bor* functions simply as a stumbling block: the hazard causes the person to trip, and the subsequent impact with the ground is the predictable consequence. On that view, it should not matter that the ground, rather than the water itself, inflicts the injury — the hazard set the whole sequence in motion.

But Rav does not accept this reasoning. His position is that a *shor* — and here the water is a *shor* because the owner retains ownership — generates liability only when the *shor* itself directly causes the damage. If the ox pushes someone who then falls and is injured on the ground, the injury occurred from the ground, not from the ox's direct contact, and the owner is exempt. The same logic applies here: the water caused the person to slip, but the injury was inflicted by the ground. Since the water itself did not directly injure the person, there is no liability.

It should be noted that the analysis of *karka olam* in the context of *bor* is itself more complex. In the paradigmatic case of *bor*, a person falls into the pit and is injured by hitting the pit's floor — which is also *karka olam*. Yet the *bor* owner is liable. This suggests that the *chiyyuv* of *bor* might be understood differently: some *rishonim* suggest the liability derives not from the physical impact but from the *hevel* — the compressed air — within the pit, which is conceptually distinct from the surrounding ground. These complexities will be developed further in the *gemara*, but for now the operative principle in Rav's ruling is that the water as *shor* must itself directly cause the damage.

## Shmuel: Always Bor

### Shmuel's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב – הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ – מָמוֹנוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how would **Rav** answer this difficulty? The Gemara answers: **This** presumption that liability for damage caused by one's stone, knife, or load is derived from the category of Pit, thereby exempting him from payment for damage to vessels, **applies** only in a case **where he renounced ownership of them. But** in a case **where he did not renounce ownership of them, it is** considered to be like any other case where **his property** causes damage. Therefore, he is liable to pay for damage caused to the vessels.

When this ruling was reported before Shmuel, he rejected Rav's distinction. Shmuel invokes a principle: *avna v'sakina u'massa mibor lamadnu* — the stone, the knife, and the load that one leaves in *reshut harabbim* — the paradigm cases of hazards left in public — are all derived from the laws of *bor*. Whether one owns them or not, they are categorized as *bor*. Shmuel reads the *pasuk*: *dekulan ani karei behun shor v'lo adam chamor v'lo kelim* — all of these cases are governed by the rule of *bor*: one is liable for the death of an ox or donkey, but exempt for killing a person and exempt for damage to vessels.

### Liability Despite Karka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן קְטָלָא, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן נְזָקִין, אָדָם חַיָּיב וְכֵלִים פַּטְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And this statement applies with regard to** a situation where the person is **killed,** i.e., if one fell into a pit and was killed, the person who dug it is exempt from paying restitution, since the verse refers exclusively to an animal that was killed. **But with regard to damage,** the digger of the pit is **liable** to pay restitution for injury to **a person, but exempt** from paying restitution for damage to **vessels.**

Shmuel adds a clarification: *v'hani milei le'inyan ketala* — the exemption for a *person* in *bor* applies specifically to death. *Aval le'inyan nezikin, adam chayav v'kelim patturim* — for injury (as opposed to death), one is liable for harm to a person, but remains exempt for damage to vessels. A vessel that is broken is effectively destroyed — *sheviratan zo hi mitatan* — and the law treats vessel damage in *bor* as equivalent to death, for which a *bor* owner is categorically exempt.

Thus Shmuel's reading of the *mishna* is necessarily about personal injury, not about damaged garments. Since the water is always *bor*, and *bor* is exempt for vessel damage, the *mishna*'s case of liability must refer to the person being injured — falling on the ground after slipping on the water. And Shmuel holds that even though the ground inflicted the actual injury — *karka olam* — liability still attaches, because the *bor* created the hazard that caused the fall.

## Water: Shor or Bor? (II)

### Mafkir Determination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״וְנָפַל שָׁמָּה שּׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר״; ״שׁוֹר״ – וְלֹא ״אָדָם״, ״חֲמוֹר״ – וְלֹא כֵּלִים. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר וְכֵלָיו וְנִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ, חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו וְנִתְקָרְעוּ – חַיָּיב עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וּפָטוּר עַל הַכֵּלִים. הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה – לְאַבְנוֹ וְסַכִּינוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ שֶׁהִנִּיחָן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִזִּיקוּ. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הֵטִיחַ צְלוֹחִיתוֹ בְּאֶבֶן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Oshaya raises an objection** from a *baraita* discussing Pit: It is derived from the verse: "And if a man shall open a pit, or if a man shall dig a pit and not cover it, **and an ox or a donkey fall therein"** (Exodus 21:33), that the digger is liable to pay restitution only if what incurred damage is **an ox but not a person,** or **a donkey but not vessels. From here** the Sages **stated** that if **an ox with its equipment fell into** a pit, and the ox was wounded **and** the equipment **broke;** or if **a donkey with its equipment** fell in, and the donkey was wounded **and** the equipment **tore,** then the one who dug the pit is **liable** to pay restitution **for** any injury sustained by **the animal but exempt** from paying restitution **for the** damaged **equipment. To what** case **is this similar?** It is similar **to** the case of **his stone, or his knife, or his load that he left in the public domain, and they caused damage. Therefore,** if one **dropped his flask on a stone** that was left in the public domain, breaking the flask, the owner of the stone is **liable.**

Rav now responds to Shmuel's challenge with a decisive clarification: *hani milei heicha de'afkirinhu* — the principle that such objects are categorized as *bor* applies when the owner made them *hefker* — formally relinquished ownership. *Aval heicha delo afkirinhu, mamono hu* — but if the owner retained ownership, the object remains his property and is classified as *shor*. This *mafkir* / non-*mafkir* distinction is the backbone of Rav's entire position throughout this *sugya*. When one breaks a pitcher in *reshut harabbim* and does not renounce ownership of the spilled water, the water is one's property — a *shor* — and vessel damage (stained clothing) generates liability. When the owner did relinquish ownership, the water becomes *bor*, vessel damage is exempt, but personal injury creates liability.

## Baraita: Objects in Public Domain

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא קַשְׁיָא לְרַב, וְסֵיפָא קַשְׁיָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל! וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ הִיא גּוּפָא – קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא פָּטוּר, וְסֵיפָא חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **The first clause** of this *baraita* is **difficult according to** the opinion of **Rav,** since it compares the cases of a stone, a knife, and a load to the case of Pit, and it does not distinguish between a situation in which he renounced ownership of them or one where he did not. **And the last clause** of the *baraita*, which deems the owner of a stone in the public domain liable to pay restitution for the damage to the broken flask, is **difficult according to** the opinion of **Shmuel.** According to him, the owner should be liable only for causing injury and not for damaging vessels. The Gemara responds to this objection: **And according to your reasoning,** the *baraita* **itself should pose a difficulty for you,** since **the first clause** states that one is **exempt** from liability for damage to vessels, **and the last clause** states that he is **liable.**

The *gemara* now examines a *baraita* that seems to speak to both sides of the debate simultaneously. The *baraita* compares cases of *bor* — citing the rule from the *pasuk* that *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo kelim* — with cases of a stone, knife, or load left in *reshut harabbim*, and then proceeds to say: *im hitiach tzluchito b'even chayav* — if one's vessel struck the stone and broke, the owner of the stone is *chayav*. 

This produces an apparent internal contradiction. The *baraita* first categorizes the stone as analogous to *bor* — which is exempt for vessel damage — and then immediately holds the stone-owner liable for a broken vessel. The *gemara* also clarifies the proper logical relationship: it is not that *bor* is like a stone and a knife, but that a stone and a knife are like *bor*, since *bor* is the primary category derived from the *Torah*.

The *gemara* notes that the *baraita* is difficult regardless of which position one takes: *reisha kashya l'Rav* — the beginning troubles Rav, because the *baraita* treats the stone as *bor*, whereas Rav would say it is *shor* (assuming the owner retained ownership). *V'seifa kashya l'Shmuel* — the end troubles Shmuel, because the *baraita* makes the stone-owner liable for broken vessels, whereas Shmuel holds that stones in *reshut harabbim* are always *bor* and therefore always exempt for vessel damage. And indeed the *baraita* is internally contradictory — *hi gufa reisha patur v'seifa chayav* — the beginning implies *patur* on vessels and the end says *chayav*.

### Rav's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַב מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ. רַב מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – כְּשֶׁהִפְקִירָן, אֲבָל לֹא הִפְקִירָן – חַיָּיב. לְפִיכָךְ הֵטִיחַ צְלוֹחִיתוֹ בְּאֶבֶן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav resolves** the contradiction according **to his** line of **reasoning, and Shmuel resolves** it according **to his** line of **reasoning. Rav resolves** it according **to his** line of **reasoning** as follows: **In what** case **is this statement said?** Concerning what case does the *baraita* rule that a stone, a knife, and a load are analogous to Pit, exempting their owner from liability for breakage to vessels caused by them? It is **where he renounced ownership of them. But** if **he did not renounce ownership of them he is liable. Therefore,** if one **dropped his flask on a stone** belonging to another person in the public domain, the owner of the stone is **liable** to pay for the damage to the flask.

*Ela Rav metaretz l'ta'amei v'Shmuel metaretz l'ta'amei* — each *amora* resolves the *baraita* according to his own framework. Rav's resolution is clean: *bameh devarim amurim keshehifkiram* — when is the stone or knife a *bor*? When the owner was *mafkir* — relinquished ownership. *Aval lo hifkiram, chayav* — if he retained ownership, it is a *shor*, and he is liable for vessel damage. The *baraita* is therefore not self-contradictory: the first clause, which treats the objects as *bor*, describes the case where the owner declared them *hefker*; the second clause, which imposes liability for the broken vessel, describes the case where ownership was retained and the objects are accordingly classified as *shor*.

### Shmuel's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ אַבְנוֹ סַכִּינוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ – כְּבוֹרוֹ דָּמֵי; לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל נִזְקֵי כֵּלִים בְּבוֹר, לְפִיכָךְ הֵטִיחַ צְלוֹחִיתוֹ בְּאֶבֶן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel resolves** the contradiction according **to his** line of **reasoning: Now that you said** that the cases of **one's stone, one's knife, and one's load are similar to one's pit,** then **according to Rabbi Yehuda, who deems** one **liable** to pay **for damage** caused **to vessels by** falling into **a pit** that he dug, one is **therefore liable** in a case where someone **dropped his flask on** a **stone** belonging to him, and the flask broke.

Shmuel's resolution is more strained. Since Shmuel holds that all these objects are *bor* regardless of ownership, he cannot use the *mafkir* distinction to reconcile the *baraita*. Instead, Shmuel says: *hashta d'amrat avno sakino u'masa'o k'bor dami, Rabbi Yehuda d'mechayev al nizkei kelim b'bor* — now that we have established that a stone, knife, and load are classified as *bor*, the clause that holds one liable for broken vessels reflects the minority opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that a *bor* owner does pay for damage to vessels. *Lefichach hitiach tzluchito b'even chayav* — therefore, if one's vessel broke on the stone, one is liable, under Rabbi Yehuda's view that *bor* encompasses vessel damage. This is admittedly a difficult reading of the *baraita*, since it attributes the vessel liability not to the specific circumstances (a retained-ownership stone) but simply to an alternative *tannaitic* position on *bor* in general, with no indication in the text itself that the *baraita* shifts between normative and minority views. Rav's resolution, by contrast, allows the *baraita* to read as a unified, internally coherent ruling.

The *sugya* thus concludes with two interconnected debates between Rav and Shmuel. The first concerns the fundamental classification of owned objects left in *reshut harabbim*: Rav holds that a retained-ownership object is *shor*; Shmuel holds it is always *bor*. The second flows directly from the first: is one liable for the personal injury of someone who trips on the object and hits the ground — *karka olam*? Rav says no, because neither the *shor* nor the *bor* framework generates liability for harm inflicted by the ground itself, unless the owner relinquished the object (making it *bor*) in which case vessel damage drops out and injury to persons remains. Shmuel says yes — even though the *karka olam* inflicted the physical harm, the hazard created by the *bor* is sufficient to generate liability for the person injured. These two positions each force a particular reading of the *mishna*, and each encounters difficulty in the *baraita* — difficulties that each *amora* resolves according to his own consistent framework.
