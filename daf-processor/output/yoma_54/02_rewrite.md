# Yoma 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Walking Backwards

### Kohen Gadol Exiting

Today's daf is 54. We begin on 53b, where the previous discussion centered on the manner in which the *kohen gadol* would walk backwards out of the *kodesh kodashim*, and how that practice was then transposed: first to walking backwards when departing from one's *rebbi* — a transference of the sense of the sacred center from the *Mikdash* to Torah and its representative — and then further still to a direct encounter with God, namely the three steps backward taken upon completing *shmoneh esrei*.

### Three Steps Backward

The *Gemara* on 53b introduces a *tanya*: *hamispalel tzarich sheyafsiya shalosh pesiot leachora* — one who prays must step three steps backward upon concluding. Afterward, he recites *oseh shalom*. The *tanna* adds a strikingly harsh consequence: *veim lo asa ken, ra'uy shelo hitpalel* — if he did not do this, it would have been better for him not to have prayed at all. Rashi, citing the phrase *ra'uy hayo lo im lo hitpalel kemit'to*, does not substantially alter the plain meaning: the manner in which one ends the prayer affects the entire character of the prayer, rendering the whole worthless if the conclusion is improper.

### Direction of Greeting

In the name of Shmaya, the *tanna* teaches that one should give *shalom* first to the right and then to the left — *shenoten shalom liyamin veachar kach lismol*. The proof text is *mimino esh das lamo*, indicating that God's right hand is associated with the giving of Torah. A second verse is added: *yipol mitzidcha elef urevava miminecha* — a thousand shall fall at your side, but a myriad at your right. The *Gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps the preference for the right in the first verse merely reflects the ordinary human practice of using the right hand? The second verse answers this, since it concerns not an act of giving but a general framework of divine favor toward the right.

### Rava Corrects Abaye

Rava observed Abaye turning first to his own right when reciting *oseh shalom bimromav*. He corrected him: *mi savart liyamin didach?* — do you think it means your right? No. *Lismol didach dehu yemino shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — one should first turn to one's own left, which is God's right. Since one is departing from God's presence, giving *shalom* to the right means orienting toward God's right — which, from the worshipper's perspective, is to one's own left. This is indeed the practice observed today.

### Three Steps & Bowing

Chiya brei deRav Huna reported that he observed Abaye and Rava take the three backward steps with a single prostration — *dipsilu shalosh pesiot bechriyah achas* — remaining bowed throughout the steps rather than stepping and bowing separately. This connects to the earlier discussion of Rabbi Eliezer, who would prostrate himself upon the ground until Rabbi Yochanan had departed.

This concludes the *Gemara*'s treatment of walking backwards — a fascinating intersection of *Mikdash* practice and *tefilla*. The discussion transitions naturally to a related intersection: the *kohen gadol*'s brief prayer in the *heichal* upon exiting the *kodesh kodashim*.

## Kohen Gadol's Prayer

### Short Prayer & Weather

Returning to the *Mishna*, the text states: *umitpalel tefilla ketzara bevayis hachitzon* — he would recite a short prayer in the outer chamber. The *Gemara* asks: *mai mitzlei* — what would he actually say? Rava bar Rav Adda and Ravin bar Rav Adda both report in the name of Rav: *Yehi ratzon milifanecha Hashem Elokeinu shetehei shana zo geshuma ushchuna* — may it be Your will that this year be rainy and hot.

### Shchuna Interpretation

The *Gemara* immediately questions the inclusion of *shchuna*: is an overly hot year a desirable thing? *Ella eima* — rather, the prayer should be read conditionally: *im shchuna tehei geshuma* — if it is going to be too hot, may it at least also be rainy, to offset the heat. This was fundamentally a concern about the crops and the agricultural livelihood of the people, not personal comfort. It is worth noting that the liturgical prayer of Yom Kippur, recited after the *avodah*, expands this into a considerably longer paragraph — but the *Gemara* here identifies this as the essential core.

The fact that, of all possible petitions, this concern about weather is singled out speaks to the reality of an agricultural society: the single most pressing matter for national welfare was whether the year would bring adequate rain.

### National Concerns Prayer

Rav Acha brei deRava adds, in the name of Rav Yehuda, a concluding section to the prayer that extends beyond meteorological concerns. Notably, the language shifts at this point from Hebrew into Aramaic.

### Dominion of Yehuda

The extended prayer includes: *lo ya'adi avidi shultana mibei Yehuda* — may dominion not be removed from the house of Yehuda. This reflects the belief that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was a direct descendant of David HaMelech, and that even during the period of the *Bayis Sheni*, when the Jewish people lived under foreign rule, they retained a degree of political autonomy through the Davidic line of the *nasi*. The *kohen gadol*, representing the nation at a national level, would pray that this measure of self-governance not be taken away. The petition is a powerful one: it frames the preservation of Jewish political identity as a matter of collective religious concern.

### Economic Sufficiency

The prayer continues: *velo yihiyu amcha Yisrael tzrichim leparnas ze mize* — may Your people Israel not need to support one another for *parnassa*. This broadens the prayer from weather to economic well-being. The *Maharsha* connects this to a famous *Gemara* in Berachos in which David's advisors suggest that, in a time of universal poverty, the people should simply do commerce with one another — to which the response is that if everyone is poor, there is no new money entering the system. The prayer is therefore for a genuinely productive year, not merely an internal redistribution of existing resources — what one might call a positive balance of payments.

### National vs. Individual

The prayer concludes: *velo tikanes lefanecha tefilas ovrei drachim* — and may the prayers of those traveling on the roads not enter before You. Travelers, for obvious reasons, pray that it not rain. But their self-interested, individual petitions are of far lesser weight than the national need for proper rainfall. The *kohen gadol* explicitly asks that God not heed those private concerns when they conflict with collective welfare.

## Chanina ben Dosa

### Rain on the Road

The *Gemara* now introduces a story about *ovrei drachim* and prayer. Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa was traveling on the road — *Rebbi Chanina Ben Dosa hava ka azil be-orcha* — when rain began to pour down upon him. He prayed: *Ribbono Shel Olam, kol olam kulo be-nachas ve-Chanina be-tza'ar?* — Master of the Universe, all the world is at ease, and Chanina alone is in distress? *Pasak mitra* — and the rain stopped.

### Rain Reversal

When he arrived home, he prayed again: *Ribbono Shel Olam, kol olam kulo be-tza'ar ve-Chanina be-nachas?* — all the world is now suffering because on my account You stopped the rain, and I alone am comfortable? *Ata mitra* — and the rain resumed. The story is striking: certain figures — among the *tanna'im* and *amora'im* — possessed an immediacy of divine responsiveness such that God would follow their word in both directions. The logic of Chanina ben Dosa's conduct is coherent: if God will equally hear him when he arrives home and asks for the rain to resume, he can permissibly pray to have it stopped while still on the road.

### Rav Yosef's Question

Rav Yosef raises the pointed rhetorical question: *mai ahani lei tzelutei de-kohen gadol legabei Rebbi Chanina Ben Dosa?* — what value does the *kohen gadol*'s prayer have in a world where Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa exists? The implication is that Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa's direct line to Heaven renders the formal institutional prayer almost superfluous.

## Long Prayer Concern

### Fear of Death

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* about a *kohen gadol* who extended his prayer at length — *she-herich bitfilato*. The *kohanim* outside began to discuss whether to send someone in after him; they feared he might have died inside the *kodesh kodashim*. Just as they were about to enter, he emerged.

### Defense of Prayer

They asked him: *mipnei mah herachta bitfilatcha* — why did you pray for so long? He replied: *kashe be-eineichem she-hitpalalti aleichem ve-al Beis Hamikdash she-lo ye-charev?* — Is it problematic in your eyes that I prayed on your behalf and for the *Beis Hamikdash* that it should not be destroyed?

This is an interesting moment. One wonders at what point during the *Bayis Sheni* they genuinely feared its destruction, or whether the *Gemara* is projecting backwards, imagining that even in the time of the *Bayis Sheni* there was awareness of the precariousness of the *Mikdash*'s continued existence.

### Warning Against Length

The *kohanim* replied: *al targil la'asot ken* — do not make a habit of this. They cited an existing teaching: *lo hayah ma'arich bitfilato kedei she-lo le-hav'it et Yisrael* — he was not supposed to extend his prayer, so as not to frighten the people of Israel. The citation formula *she-harei shaninu* suggests they were quoting the literary corpus of the *Mishna*, which is slightly anachronistic, though the underlying principle clearly predates its formulation. The lesson is clear: even the most sincere and self-sacrificing prayer — offered for the sake of the people and the *Mikdash* itself — is inappropriate when its prolonged duration causes the congregation genuine distress. Being a *tzaddik* for others does not override the obligation not to alarm them.

## Aron's Fate Dispute

### Shtiyah Stone

The *Mishna* says: *mishenitlo ha-Aron* — from the time the *aron* was removed. Notably, it does not say *mishe-nignaz*, from the time it was hidden, but *mishe-nital*, from the time it was taken — language that implies it was carried away, not concealed in place. This leads to the position that the *aron* was exiled to Babylonia.

Before engaging the dispute directly, the *Mishna* introduces a related detail: *even hayetah sham mi-yemot nevi'im rishonim* — there was a stone there from the days of the early prophets, *ve-shtiyah hayetah nikreit* — and it was called the *Shtiyah* stone, the foundation stone. It stood *gavoha min ha-aretz shalosh etzba'ot* — three fingerbreadths above the ground.

Several questions arise regarding this stone. Was the *aron* placed upon it? Was it always visible, or was it beneath the ground during the period of the *aron* and only exposed after? The *Gemara* does not resolve these physical questions, and the dimensions invite further discussion: three fingerbreadths is barely an inch — quite modest — and the rock associated with the Dome of the Rock is considerably more imposing. Various sources do identify the *Even Shetiyah* with that rock, and archaeological analysis of the *Har Habayit* dimensions provides *halachic* authorities a basis for determining the approximate location of the *azarah* — hence the existence of sanctioned tours that can navigate around the *azarah* boundaries. But the precise correlation remains uncertain. The *Gemara* itself records only that upon this stone the *kohen gadol* would place the incense, and that it served as a substitute for the *aron* in the ritual of the *Bayis Sheni*.

The subsequent *Mishnayos* detail the *kohen gadol*'s actions after leaving the *kodesh kodashim*: he retrieves the blood of the ox from the one who had been stirring it, re-enters to sprinkle — *achat lemalah vesheva lematah* — one above and seven below, without intending to strike high or low specifically but rather *kematzlif*, with a flicking or whipping motion. He counts: *achat, achat ve'achat, achat veshtayim, achat veshalosh* — and so on through seven. He exits, places the blood on one of the two gold pedestals in the *heichal* — a dispute exists between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda as to whether there were one or two such pedestals — then performs the identical procedure with the blood of the goat, and subsequently mixes the two bloods together, pouring from vessel to vessel to combine them thoroughly.

### Four Positions

Returning to the language *mishe-nital*, the *Gemara* says: *tana ke-man de-amar aron galah le-vavel* — the *tanna* of the *Mishna* holds like the opinion that the *aron* was exiled to Babylonia. A *baraita* is then cited presenting four positions on the fate of the *aron*.

### Eliezer: Exiled

Rabbi Eliezer says: *aron galah le-vavel* — the *aron* was exiled to Babylonia. The proof text is *va-yeviehu vavel im klei chemdat beis Hashem* — Nevuchadnezzar brought him to Babylonia together with the most desirous vessels of the House of God. What is the most desirous vessel? The *aron*. A second verse is added: *va-yeitzei mi-bat Tzion kol hadarah* — from the daughter of Zion all her glory departed. The *Gemara* reads *hadarah* as *chadarah* — the innermost chamber. What is found in the innermost chamber? The *aron* with the *luchot*. Thus two verses together establish that the *aron* was taken into exile.

### Shimon: Buried

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai says: *aron bimkomo nignaz* — the *aron* was buried in its place, beneath the floor of the *kodesh kodashim*. His proof is from the verse: *va-ya'arichu ha-badim min ha-kodesh al pnei ha-dvir* — the tops of the poles extended from the *kodesh* to the face of the *dvir* — and *va-yihyu sham ad ha-yom hazeh* — and they were there until this day. If the poles were still in place until the time of the verse's writing, the *aron* never moved; it must have been concealed in place.

### Ulla's Report

This position conflicts, however, with a report from Ulla. Ulla states that Rabbi Matya ben Charash once asked Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — when they were together in Rome — whether, given that Rabbi Eliezer teaches us from two verses that the *aron* was taken to Babylonia, what Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai himself maintained. Rabbi Shimon replied that he holds *aron bimkomo nignaz*, as demonstrated by the verse about the poles remaining *sham ad ha-yom hazeh*. Thus the *Braitah* and Ulla's report disagree about Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position: the *braitah* attributes to him the view that the *aron* was exiled, while Ulla's account presents him as holding that it was buried in place. Both versions stand without resolution.

### Rabba's Challenge

Rabba challenged Ulla's reasoning: *mah mashma* — what exactly does *va-yihyu sham ad ha-yom hazeh* prove? Does every occurrence of *ad ha-yom hazeh* in the *Tanach* imply permanence and continuity for all time?

### Counter-Example

Rabba marshals a counter-example. The verse states: *ve-es ha-yevusi yoshev Yerushalayim lo horishu benei Binyamin va-yeshev ha-yevusi es benei Binyamin be-Yerushalayim ad ha-yom ha-zeh* — the Jebusites dwelling in Jerusalem were not driven out by the children of Binyamin, and the Jebusites dwelt with the children of Binyamin in Jerusalem until this day. Does this mean that *benei Binyamin* remained continuously in Jerusalem? A *baraita* shows otherwise. Rabbi Yehuda teaches that for fifty-two years no man passed through Yehuda — the land was entirely desolate. This figure is derived from the *gematria* of *behema* (בהמה), which equals fifty-two, tied to the verse describing the utter desolation of the land. Additionally, Rabbi Yosei derives from a *gezeirah shava* on the word *bris* that for seven years the land suffered *gofris va-melach* — sulfur and salt — rendering it entirely untillable. Yet the verse had said *benei Binyamin* remained *ad ha-yom ha-zeh*. Clearly, then, *ad ha-yom ha-zeh* does not guarantee uninterrupted continuity.

### Sham Significance

The *Gemara* offers a response: *hacha chtiv sham* — in the verse about the *aron*'s poles, it says *va-yihyu **sham** ad ha-yom hazeh*, with the word *sham* — "there." The counter-example regarding the Jebusites and *benei Binyamin* lacks this word. The word *sham*, it is argued, implies continuity of presence — they remained precisely there, without displacement. However, this distinction is itself then challenged. The verse about the five hundred men of Binyamin who went to Har Seir and struck the remnant of Amalek similarly concludes *va-yeshvu **sham** ad ha-yom hazeh* — yet after Sancheiriv came and displaced all the nations (*va-asir gevulot amim*), they certainly were no longer there. Thus *sham* does not in fact guarantee permanence. *Tyuvta* — the challenge stands, and the support from this verse is withdrawn.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position that the *aron* is buried in place may still be correct, but it can no longer be derived from this verse.

### Chamber of Wood

Rav Nachman introduces a fourth position: the *chachamim* say that the *aron* was hidden in the *lishkat dir ha'etzim* — the chamber of wood, where timber was stored for the *mizbe'ach*. It was concealed beneath the floor of that chamber, not beneath the *kodesh kodashim* and not carried into exile.

### Priest's Discovery

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds that the *Mishna* of *Shekalim* — studied before *Yoma* — actually corroborates this. A certain *kohen* was going about his business in that chamber and noticed that a section of the floor looked different from the others — Rashi explains that one tile was slightly raised, suggesting the ground had been disturbed. He went to tell his companion what he had found, but *velo hispik ligmor et hadavar* — he was not able to finish saying what it was before *ad sheyatza nishmato* — his soul departed. They thereby knew definitively that the *aron* was concealed in the *lishkat ha'etzim*.

### Wormy Wood Account

A *tanna devei Rabbi Yishmael* gives an alternative, more elaborate account. Two *kohanim* with physical blemishes — who were therefore unfit for the *avodah* — were assigned the task of removing wormy wood from the chamber's stores, going through the timber piece by piece. The axe of one of them slipped and fell — and a fire emerged from beneath the floor and consumed it. From this, too, it was known that the *aron* was hidden there — an event not unlike, as one might observe, the Indiana Jones motif of lethal sacred discovery.

## Poles & Parochet

### Seen/Not Seen

Rav Yehuda points out a textual contradiction. One verse states *va-yir'u rashei ha-badim* — the tops of the poles were seen. Another states *ve-lo yera'u ha-chutzah* — they were not seen outside. How can both be true? The resolution: *nirin ve'ein nirin* — they were seen, yet not fully seen.

### Lengthening Poles

A *baraita* elaborates. One might have thought that *va-yir'u rashei ha-badim* means merely that the poles were visible in their normal position alongside the *aron*. But the *kodesh kodashim* was 20 by 20 *amot*, and the *aron* itself was only two and a half *amot* long. Even with the staves inserted, in their ordinary centered position they would not have reached the front wall or the curtain. Therefore *talmud lomar va-ya'arichu ha-badim* — they were lengthened, meaning they were shifted forward. The *Maharsha* understands this not as a miraculous elongation but as a physical repositioning: the staves were slid through their rings toward the front of the *aron* so that their tips extended toward the entrance curtain. One might then think the staves broke through — but *ve-lo yera'u ha-chutzah* — they were not visible outside. *Ha keitzad?* How then? *Dochkin uvoltin veyotzin ba-parochet* — they pressed against and protruded through the fabric of the *parochet*, creating visible protrusions without actually penetrating to the exterior.

### Poles as Breasts

The *Gemara* describes what this looked like: *ve-nir'in ki-shnei dadei isha* — they appeared like the two breasts of a woman. The supporting verse is from *Shir Hashirim*: *tzror ha-mor dodi li bein shadai yalin* — a bundle of myrrh my beloved is to me; he rests between my breasts.

This imagery is, of course, extraordinary, and it opens the door to what follows. *Shir Hashirim* deploys not merely romantic but deeply erotic language to describe the relationship between *Bnei Yisrael* and God — calling upon the most powerful of human drives, the desire for union and intimacy, and translating it into the encounter between Israel and the Divine. This is not unique in the history of religious literature — mystical and erotic poetry intersect in various Eastern traditions and in Sufi Islam — but within the Jewish corpus it is *Shir Hashirim* that carries this freight most explicitly, and it is the imagery of that work that now begins to animate the *Gemara*'s description of the *aron* and the *kodesh kodashim*.

The connections are striking. The word *mor* — myrrh — is an ingredient of the *ketoret*, the incense. The *ketoret* was placed between the *badim* — the poles of the *aron*. *Bein shadai yalin* — he rests between my breasts — maps almost precisely onto this act. The *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* enters the innermost chamber bearing the *ketoret* and places it *bein ha-badim*. The language *heviani ha-melech chadarav* — the king has brought me into his chambers — resonates with the *kohen gadol*'s entry, and notably, the chamber-word *cheder* was already invoked earlier when the *Gemara* read *hadarah* as *chadarah* to identify what the Babylonians took from the innermost room. Even the technical legal term for unauthorized entry into the *kodesh kodashim* — *bi'ah reikanit*, an empty entry — uses the word *bi'ah*, which is also the biblical term for conjugal union. The erotic register is not incidental; it is structurally embedded in the *Gemara*'s entire treatment of the *kodesh kodashim*.

What is also notable is the gender inversion in these passages. In most of *Shir Hashirim* and in most rabbinic metaphor, God is the man and *Bnei Yisrael* is the woman. Here, God becomes the beloved — the one entered, the one approached — the *aron* protruding like breasts through the curtain. The *kohen gadol* approaches. The imagery is of the most intimate penetration of the innermost, and the *Gemara* presents this not as scandal but as the ultimate expression of sacred relationship.

## Keruvim & Erotica

### Exposing Keruvim

Rav Ketina teaches: *be-sha'ah she-Yisrael olim la-regel, megalin lahem et ha-parochet* — when Israel would go up for the pilgrimage festivals, they would draw back the *parochet*. Even though the people were not permitted inside the *heichal*, the gates of the *heichal* and the *azarah* were aligned, so that with the curtains of each successive doorway drawn aside, one could see straight into the *kodesh kodashim* from the *azarah*.

### Keruvim Intertwined

What they were shown was this: *umar'im lahem et ha-kruvim she-hayu me'urim zeh ba-zeh* — they were shown the *keruvim* intertwined with one another. The *keruvim* — the two angelic or human forms atop the *aron* — were not standing apart. Miraculously, they were found intertwined, like lovers. The pilgrims were told: *re'u chibatchem lifnei ha-Makom ke-chibat zachar u-nekevah* — see how beloved you are before God, like the love between male and female. The phrase *zachar u-nekevah* — male and female — is notably more physical and corporeal than the softer *ish ve-ishah*, and the imagery is unmistakably erotic.

### Maturation & Exposure

A question immediately arises: is not this exposure itself a violation of the essential privacy of what takes place in the *kodesh kodashim*? Is not the entire point that the most intimate occurs in the most concealed space? How can the very intimacy be put on display?

### Bride Metaphor

Rav Chisda raises a related challenge. The *Torah* states *ve-lo yavo'u lir'os ke-vala et ha-kodesh* — they shall not come to see as the holy is being swallowed up, referring to the prohibition against the *Levi'im* witnessing the sacred vessels being wrapped before transit. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav explains this as applying when the vessels were being placed into their protective coverings. If such extreme privacy applied in the *Mishkan*, how can the *Gemara* describe public exposure of the *keruvim*?

Rav Nachman answers with a *mashal*: it is like a bride. *Kol zeman she-hi be-veit aviha, tzenu'ah mi-ba'alah* — as long as she is in her father's house, even after *kiddushin*, she maintains modesty with her husband. *Keivan she-ba'ah le-veit chamiah, einah tzenu'ah mi-ba'alah* — once she enters the domain of her father-in-law's household, at the stage of *nisu'in*, she no longer maintains that same modesty with her husband.

The application: in the time of the *Mishkan*, *Bnei Yisrael* and God had just been married. The relationship was new, and God — here cast explicitly as the woman — required privacy, as a new bride would. Hence the prohibition on viewing the vessels. By the time of the *Beis Hamikdash*, they had been living together for centuries. The relationship had matured, and it was now appropriate, on occasion, to draw back the curtain and allow the people to witness the intimacy of God's love for them.

There is an additional dimension worth noting in the *mashal*. The phrase *veit chamiah* — the father-in-law's house — carries the implication that *God* is now entering *our* domain, *our* land, *our* *Har Habayit*. In an ancient patriarchal framework, the wife moves to the husband's family home. If God is the wife and *Bnei Yisrael* is the husband, then God's *Shekhinah* dwelling on *Har Habayit* is God moving into our land, our home — making the *Mikdash* less the exclusive domain of the Divine and more a shared space in which *Bnei Yisrael* are *ba'al ha-bayis*. This sociological dimension is genuinely empowering, even as it operates within a patriarchal symbolic framework.

### Divorce & Remarriage

A student raises the question: what about the episode of the *kohen* who died upon discovering the *aron*'s location in the *lishkat ha'etzim*? If the mature relationship permits exposure, why was he struck down?

Rav Nachman's answer: *nisgarsha kamart?* — you are speaking of after the divorce. *Nisgarsha chazru le-chibasan ha-rishonah* — after a divorce and remarriage, the relationship reverts to its initial stage. After the *Churban Bayis Rishon*, the relationship was sundered. The *Bayis Sheni* represents a remarriage — a new beginning — and therefore the early-stage modesty returns. The *kohen* in the *Bayis Sheni* who came upon the hidden *aron* was approaching the most intimate space at a time when privacy had been re-established. The fire was not merely a protection of a physical secret; it was the restoration of appropriate sacred boundaries.

## Wall Carvings

### Keruvim Images

The *Gemara* now asks a structural question. The discussion of the poles protruding through the *parochet* and the *keruvim* intertwined — to which *Beis Hamikdash* does each refer? If it is the *Bayis Rishon*, there was no *parochet* in the same sense — there was a wall, though the doorway did have a hanging curtain. If it is the *Bayis Sheni*, there were no *keruvim* — the actual statues of the *keruvim* rested atop the *aron*, and the *aron* was absent from the *Bayis Sheni*.

The *Gemara* resolves this through a series of distinctions. Rav Zeira in the name of Rav reports that there were thirteen *parochos* in the *Mikdash*: seven corresponding to the seven gates of the *azarah*, one at the entrance to the *heichal*, one at the entrance to the *ulam*, two at the *dvir* — the inner wall of the *kodesh kodashim* — arranged with openings facing in opposite directions, and two more on the upper story of the *kodesh kodashim*, used for entry when maintenance work was needed above. The point is that even in the *Bayis Rishon*, where the *kodesh kodashim* was sealed by a wall, the doorway in that wall was hung with a curtain — which is where the poles would have protruded.

### Carved Decorations

Rav Acha bar Yaakov then argues that the discussion of the *keruvim* being shown to the pilgrims refers to the *Bayis Sheni*, where actual *keruvim* statues were absent — but where images of *keruvim* were present. He cites the verse: *ve-es kol kiros ha-bayis misav pituhe miklayos kruvim ve-simaros u-feturei tzitzim ve-tzipa zahav miyashar al ha-michuka* — all the walls of the house were covered with engraved braided carvings of *keruvim*, palm trees, and open flowers, and gold was inlaid into the carvings. So even in the *Bayis Sheni*, these carved images lined the inner walls.

### Man Intertwined

The verse continues, and Rabba bar Shila interprets the phrase *ke-ma'ar ish ve-livya* as *ke-ish ha-me'urav be-livyah shelo* — like a man intertwined with his consort. Even the carved *keruvim* on the walls depicted them in an embrace. Thus even in the *Bayis Sheni*, pilgrims could be shown these images and told: see how beloved you are before God.

### Desecration

Reish Lakish provides the tragic counterpoint. When the non-Jews entered the *heichal* — in the context of the *Bayis Sheni*'s destruction — they saw the *keruvim* intertwined, *me'uravim zeh ba-zeh*. They took them out into the marketplace — *hotziu'um la-shuk*. They transported what was most interior, most private, most sacred into the most public and commercial of spaces.

### Loss of Reverence

And they said: *Yisrael hallalu she-birchasam bracha ve-kilalasam klala — ya'asku bi-dvarim hallalu?* — this Jewish people, whose blessings are genuine blessings and whose curses are genuine curses — they should be engaging in *these* things, with sexuality in the *kodesh kodashim*? *Miyad hizilum* — immediately they denigrated them.

### Intimacy vs. Defilement

The proof text is from *Eikhah*: *kol mechabdeha hizziluha ki ra'u ervatah* — all who honored her have made her contemptible, because they saw her nakedness. The citation from *Eikhah* may point toward the destruction of the *Bayis Rishon*, in which context the actual *keruvim* statues atop the *aron* would have been the objects removed — a possibility more viscerally disturbing, and perhaps even more powerful as an image of desecration.

The final irony is profound. Sexual intimacy between husband and wife can be the most powerful, the most holy, and the most sacred of human experiences — but removed from its context, exposed in the marketplace, stripped of relationship and meaning, it becomes pornographic. The *keruvim*' embrace in the *kodesh kodashim*, within the living relationship between *Bnei Yisrael* and God, was the most exalted expression of that love. Taken out by those who had no part in that relationship, no framework for understanding it, and carried into the *shuk*, it was immediately reduced to scandal and used as a pretext for contempt.

The *Gemara* thus ends with a powerful contrast: all of the elaborate procedures that govern entry into the innermost chambers — the *kohen gadol*, the *Yom Kippur* service, the curtains and the incense and the walking backwards — all of it exists to protect and sanctify that innermost moment of union. The *churban* did not merely destroy the *Mikdash*; it violated the most intimate of sacred spaces and dragged its contents into the marketplace. *Kol mechabdeha hizziluha ki ra'u ervatah.*
