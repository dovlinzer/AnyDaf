# Ketubot 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Wives & Oaths

### Four Wives Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיָה נָשׂוּי אַרְבַּע נָשִׁים וָמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה קוֹדֶמֶת לַשְּׁנִיָּה, וּשְׁנִיָּה לַשְּׁלִישִׁית, וּשְׁלִישִׁית לָרְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who was married** to **four women and died, the** woman he married **first precedes the** woman he married **second** in claiming her marriage contract, **the second** precedes **the third, and the third** precedes **the fourth.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 93b introduces the case of *mi shehaya nasuy arba nashim umes* — a man who was married to four wives sequentially and then died. Unlike the preceding *mishna*, which dealt with the hypothetical scenario of a man who married multiple wives on the exact same date — so that all their liens were simultaneous — the present *mishna* addresses the normal, real-world situation in which the wives were married one after the other, creating sequential liens on the husband's estate. The governing rule is therefore straightforward: *harishona kodemes lashniya, veshniya lashlishit, veshlishit leriviit* — the first has priority over the second, the second over the third, and the third over the fourth.

### Oaths Between Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרִאשׁוֹנָה נִשְׁבַּעַת לַשְּׁנִיָּה. וּשְׁנִיָּה לַשְּׁלִישִׁית, וּשְׁלִישִׁית לָרְבִיעִית, וְהָרְבִיעִית נִפְרַעַת שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** And** the **first** wife **takes an oath to the second** that she has taken nothing from the jointly owned properties of the estate in an unlawful manner, **and** the **second** takes an oath **to the third, and** the **third to the fourth. The fourth** wife **is paid** her share **without** having to take **an oath.**

When each wife comes to collect, the *mishna* imposes an oath requirement. The general rule is that anyone collecting from orphans — whether a creditor or a widow — must swear that none of the debt has already been paid. The rationale is that the orphans, had the father been alive, might have contested the claim and asserted partial or full payment; the orphans, particularly if minors, are not positioned to investigate their father's papers and press that claim themselves. Rashi assumes that the *mishna* here is dealing with adult orphans, who are in principle capable of making their own claims and examining the father's records — and therefore would not ordinarily compel an oath from a claimant arriving with a signed *shtar*. Nevertheless, the *mishna* establishes a distinct obligation: wife one must swear to wives two, three, and four, because those later wives can say that by exercising her lien and seizing property, she may be impacting assets that would otherwise flow to them. If part of her *ketubah* had already been paid, she would be seizing property that should rightfully reach them.

Tosafot raises the natural question of whether this oath obligation applies equally whenever any creditor collects and thereby affects other creditors with later liens. The answer may well be no in general, but the unique dynamic here — all the wives are widows of the same man, all their claims were triggered simultaneously by his death, and they all appear before us as a collective group — creates a relational context that generates the mutual oath obligation among them.

### Fourth Wife Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן נַנָּס אוֹמֵר: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא אַחֲרוֹנָה נִשְׂכֶּרֶת? אַף הִיא לֹא תִּפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Nanas says: Should she gain** this advantage merely **because she is last?** After all, she too is being paid from property that would otherwise go to the orphans. Rather, **she too is not paid without an oath.**

The *mishna* continues: *veriviit nifres shelo bishvua* — the fourth wife collects without any oath. Since she is last in line, no subsequent creditor is being harmed by her collection. The adult orphans, as noted, are in a position to press their own claims if they believe they have been wronged, so no oath is owed to them either. The fourth wife thus collects in peace, which might seem like a small consolation for receiving whatever is left at the end.

### Ben Nanas Disagrees

Ben Nanas disagrees. His position is that the mere fact of being last in line does not exempt wife four from taking an oath. The *gemara* will clarify precisely why — but his objection rests on the possibility that one of the fields seized by an earlier wife may turn out not to have belonged to the husband, leaving an earlier wife's debt still uncollected and potentially requiring her to look to the fourth wife's field for satisfaction.

### Same-Day Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ יוֹצְאוֹת כּוּלָּן בְּיוֹם אֶחָד — כׇּל הַקּוֹדֶמֶת לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ אֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת זָכְתָה. וְכָךְ הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִין בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם שָׁעוֹת. הָיוּ כּוּלָּן יוֹצְאוֹת בְּשָׁעָה אַחַת, וְאֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא מָנֶה — חוֹלְקוֹת בְּשָׁוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** However, **if all of** the marriage contracts **were issued on the same day, whichever** wife's marriage contract **precedes** that of **another, even by a single hour, has acquired** the right to be paid first. **And so,** the practice **in Jerusalem** was that **they would write the hours** when the documents had been signed on the documents, in order to enable the document holder to demonstrate that his or her document preceded that of another. If **all** the contracts **were issued in the same hour and there is only one hundred dinars** from which to pay all of them, all of the women **divide** the money **equally.**

The *mishna* addresses the scenario where all the *ketubot* bear the same date. Even where the marriages occurred on the same day, priority can still be established if the hour is known: *kol hakodemes l'chavertah afilu sha'ah achas zachsa* — even one hour's precedence is sufficient to establish priority. The *mishna* notes that in Jerusalem it was the practice to record the hour of the transaction in the document — *v'chach hayu kosvin b'Yerushalayim sha'os* — precisely to enable this kind of fine-grained determination. If, however, all the documents share the same hour with no further differentiation, and the estate is insufficient to satisfy all claims, then *cholekot b'shaveh* — they divide equally — with the detailed proportional rules of the previous *mishna* applying as needed.

## Oath Debate Analysis

### Shmuel's Framing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת אַחַת מֵהֶן שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ. וּבְבַעַל חוֹב מְאוּחָר שֶׁקָּדַם וְגָבָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do** the first *tanna* and ben Nanas **disagree? Shmuel said:** The case is **where it is discovered that one of the** fields in the estate **is not his** field, e.g., the husband had stolen it from someone else. Consequently, it is likely that the field will be repossessed, and if it is used to pay the marriage contract of one of the first three wives, that wife stands to lose out. **And they disagree with regard to a creditor** whose promissory note was dated **later** than that of another creditor, and yet **he collected** his debt **before** the other creditor, leaving nothing for the other creditor to collect. This is parallel to the case of the wives if the fourth wife collects her marriage contract and then one of the earlier wives loses the field she has been paid.

The *gemara* opens its analysis with the question *b'mai kamifligi* — what precisely is the dispute between the *Tana Kamma* and Ben Nanas regarding whether the fourth wife must take an oath? Shmuel's answer, which Tosafot's reading of the *gemara* makes most sense, is this: the case at issue is one where it emerges — or where there is reason to believe — that one of the fields seized by wife one, two, or three was not actually the husband's property. The husband may have sold it innocently after purchasing stolen goods, or there may have been some other defect in his title. In any event, one of the earlier wives has seized a field that turns out not to have been validly his, meaning she has not in fact collected her *ketubah* debt through that seizure and will need to look elsewhere for satisfaction.

Rashi reads the scenario as one where this has already been established as fact. Tosafot, whose reading the lecturer follows as more consistent with the flow of the *gemara*, understands the debate as a general one: in ordinary circumstances, whenever wives one through three have taken their fields, does wife four need to take an oath because of the *possibility* that one of those earlier fields may turn out to have been problematic? On that reading, the debate is about the structural obligation in a normal case, not about what happens after a defect has been proven.

The underlying legal question that drives the debate is framed by the *gemara* as *u-va'al chov me'uchar she-kadum v-gava kamifligi* — they dispute what happens when a later creditor jumped ahead and collected, and it subsequently turns out that an earlier creditor had a superior lien. Does the later creditor get to keep what she collected, or must she return it?

A vivid illustration: if a man died and two creditors both have liens on his estate — one lien dating back ten years, the other more recent — and the later creditor moved quickly and seized the only available field before the earlier one acted, can the earlier creditor come and demand the field back? Or does the later creditor's prior seizure give her a right to retain it?

### Tana Kamma Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: מַה שֶּׁגָּבָה לֹא גָּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The first *tanna* holds** that **what** the creditor **has collected, he has not** fully **collected,** i.e., he will have to give up the property he collected so that the creditor with the earlier promissory note can collect his debt. Similarly, if the property given to one of the first three wives is repossessed and there is nothing left for her to collect, the fourth wife will have to relinquish the property that she had been paid to accommodate the wife who preceded her.

The *Tana Kamma* holds *mah she-gava lo gava* — what the later creditor took, she did not truly take. She cannot keep the field against an earlier creditor's superior claim. Applied to the wives: if wife four seized the last available field and it subsequently turns out that wife two, whose earlier field was defective, has an unsatisfied claim with an earlier lien, wife four must surrender her field to wife two. Given that outcome, there is no reason for wife four to swear to the earlier wives. She can never hurt their interests by holding her field, because if any of them ever need it, they are simply entitled to take it from her. An oath would serve no function.

### Ben Nanas Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן נַנָּס סָבַר: מַה שֶּׁגָּבָה גָּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And ben Nanas holds that what** the creditor **has collected, he has collected,** i.e., it is not taken from him in order to pay the earlier creditor. Consequently, according to the first *tanna*, there is no need for the fourth wife to take an oath before she collects the property, because whatever she collects can be taken from her in order to pay the other wives. According to ben Nanas, since the property the fourth wife collects cannot be taken from her, she must take an oath that she is collecting this property legally in order to ensure that none of the other wives will lose out because of what she collects.

Ben Nanas holds *mah she-gava gava* — what wife four took, she may keep. If wife two comes and says her field was defective and she has an earlier lien, wife four can respond that she already collected her field and is entitled to retain it. In that case, wife four's retention of the field does affect the earlier wives' ability to satisfy their debts. It is therefore appropriate to require her to swear that her own *ketubah* has not already been paid — because her keeping the field while potentially owing a debt to an earlier wife creates real harm to that wife's interests, and the oath is designed to address exactly that harm.

### Strip-Mining Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אָמַר) רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא — מַה שֶּׁגָּבָה לֹא גָּבָה. וְהָכָא בְּחָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תַּכְסִיף קָמִיפַּלְגִי,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Everyone agrees** that **what** the later creditor **has collected, he has not collected,** i.e., it may be repossessed by the earlier creditor. Rather, **they disagree here** as to whether **we are concerned** that **perhaps she will deplete** the field and cause its value to depreciate.

Rav Nachman offers an entirely different framing of the dispute. He holds that *d'chulei alma mah she-gava lo gava* — in truth, everyone agrees that wife four cannot keep the field if an earlier wife's lien proves superior. The dispute is not about whether she keeps the field, but about whether she will *tachsif* — strip-mine or devastate — the field while it is in her possession. Knowing she will eventually have to surrender it, she has every incentive to extract maximum value from it in the interim: selling off timber, exhausting the soil, or otherwise depleting whatever she can while the field is under her control.

### Psychological Deterrent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תַּכְסִיף. וּמַר סָבַר: לָא חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תַּכְסִיף.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** ben Nanas, **holds** that **we are concerned** that **perhaps she will deplete** the field. If she is not required to take the oath, she will understand that her hold on the land is uncertain, as it is possible that one of the other wives will repossess it. Consequently, she will try to reap the maximum benefit from the field in the short term without investing in the field for the long term, and thereby depleting the field. The Sages therefore imposed an oath upon the fourth wife. **And** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds that we are not concerned** that **perhaps she will deplete** the field and we can assume that it will retain its original value. Therefore, there is no reason to impose an oath upon the fourth wife.

This framing makes Ben Nanas's position both more surprising and more ingenious. According to Rav Nachman, the reason Ben Nanas wants wife four to take an oath is psychological: by requiring her to swear to the earlier wives that her *ketubah* has not been paid, we induce her to believe that she will be entitled to keep the field. After all, from her perspective, why would the court make her take an oath to the other wives unless she had some real claim to retain the field against their demands? She will reason that the oath is a precondition to keeping what she collected — and therefore she will handle the field responsibly, as a person who believes she will be its long-term owner, rather than strip-mining it as someone who knows she is a short-term custodian.

This is, as noted, a striking use of the oath mechanism. The oath is being imposed not because she is legally obligated to take it — everyone agrees she will have to return the field — but because making her take it creates a false impression that will deter destructive behavior. One might ask whether this involves a kind of deception. The response is that no real disservice is being done to the wife: all she is being asked to do is swear that she is entitled to collect what she is collecting. If along the way that oath causes her to believe she can keep the field, and if that belief in turn leads her to preserve the field's value, those are ancillary benefits. The oath itself is simply an affirmation of her legitimate claim to collect.

It is worth noting that Tosafot raises the broader question of why this kind of oath requirement — structured around the possibility of interconnected harm among multiple creditors — appears only in the context of a *ketubah* collection and not in ordinary creditor scenarios. The question does not receive a fully resolved answer, but the unique relational structure of the multiple-widow scenario, where all claims arise simultaneously from one death and all claimants are before us together, distinguishes it from the general creditor case.

## Partners & Representation

### Implicit Representation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הָנֵי תְּרֵי אַחֵי וּתְרֵי שׁוּתָּפֵי דְּאִית לְהוּ דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵי חַד, וַאֲזַל חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ לְדִינָא — לָא מָצֵי אִידַּךְ לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ לָאו בַּעַל דְּבָרִים דִּידִי אַתְּ. אֶלָּא שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** In a case of **two brothers or two partners who have legal** proceedings **against** another **individual, and one of them went to** attend to the **legal** proceedings **against him** and lost, **the other** brother or partner **cannot say** to the litigant: **I am not legally answerable to you,** i.e., I am not bound by the verdict because I was not represented in the legal proceedings. **Rather,** the brother or partner who appeared in court is considered to have **acted as his agent.**

The *gemara* cites a teaching by Rav Huna in the name of the elder Abaye — a *tanna* whose teaching frames the question of oaths to adult orphans — and then transitions to a related case. The teaching concerns *hanei trei achei vetrei shutfei* — two brothers who have inherited an undivided estate and are therefore effectively partners in it, or simply two partners in a jointly owned field. If a third party initiates a court case against the estate and only one of the partners appears to defend — *veazal chad mineihu bahadei ledina* — can the absent partner, upon learning of the outcome, say to the winning claimant: *at lav ba'al devarim didi at* — you never litigated against me, you only litigated against my partner's half, and I'm entitled to my own day in court?

Rav Huna's position is *shelichusei avad* — the partner who appeared was implicitly acting as the agent of the whole estate, and the absent partner cannot subsequently disavow the result. The one who appeared in court represented the entire jointly held property, not merely his own half.

### Why Not All Wives?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִקְּלַע רַב נַחְמָן לְסוּרָא. שַׁיְילוּהִי: כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא מַאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ, מַתְנִיתִין הִיא: הָרִאשׁוֹנָה נִשְׁבַּעַת לַשְּׁנִיָּה, וּשְׁנִיָּה לַשְּׁלִישִׁית, וּשְׁלִישִׁית לָרְבִיעִית. וְאִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹנָה לַשְּׁלִישִׁית לָא קָתָנֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁלִיחוּתַהּ עָבְדָה?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman** once **happened** to come **to Sura. They asked him: What** is the *halakha* in **a case like this** one presented by Rav Huna, where only one of the two brothers or partners attends the court proceedings? **He said to them: It is** taught in **a mishna: The** woman he married **first takes an oath to the** woman he married **second, the second to the third, and the third to the fourth.** But **it does not teach** that **the first** wife takes an oath **to the third** or the fourth. **What is the reason? Is it not due to** the fact that when the second wife requires the first to take an oath, **she is acting as** the third wife's **agent** as well, since they both share the same concern regarding the first wife?

Rav Nachman, visiting Sura, was asked about this rule. He responded that it can be derived from the *mishna* itself. The *mishna* says wife one swears to wife two, wife two to wife three, wife three to wife four — but does not say that wife one must also swear directly to wife three and wife four, even though wife one's collection affects their interests as well. Why the omission? It would seem to be because wife two, when she receives wife one's oath, is implicitly representing the interests of wives three and four as well.

### One Oath Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם שְׁבוּעָה לְאֶחָד וּשְׁבוּעָה לְמֵאָה, הָכָא אָמַר: אִילּוּ אֲנָא הֲוַאי, טָעֵינְנָא טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Is it comparable? There,** in the case of the mishna, **an oath to one is** equal to **an oath to one hundred,** and there is no need for the first wife to take multiple oaths about the same matter. **Here,** however, in the case of the brothers or business partners, the second brother or partner can **say: Had I been** there, **I would have** presented **a more** convincing **claim.**

The *gemara* immediately questions this inference. Perhaps the reason wife one need not swear separately to wives three and four has nothing to do with representation at all — it may simply be that *shvua le-echad shvua le-me'ah*, an oath made once is effective for everyone. The oath, once sworn, functions as evidence: it demonstrates to any interested party that the swearer would not have taken God's name in vain if she were lying. That evidentiary value does not depend on who was in the room when the oath was taken. There is no need to repeat the same oath to multiple parties.

The *gemara* had initially explored the possibility that oaths operate more in the mode of personally satisfying the other party — giving them the subjective assurance of having received a direct commitment — in which case each wife would need her own oath. But the *gemara*'s resolution rejects this model: a single oath suffices for all, so the *mishna*'s structure tells us nothing about implicit representation.

### Context & Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא אִיתֵיהּ בְּמָתָא, אֲבָל אִיתֵיהּ בְּמָתָא — אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵיתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **We said** that this doubt is taken into account **only** if the second brother or partner **is not in town** when the legal proceedings take place. **However,** if **he is in town, he should come** to court to participate in the legal proceedings, and if he fails to do so, it is clear that he is content to allow his brother or partner to represent him in court.

Returning to the question of partners, the *gemara* refines Rav Huna's position. The rule that the appearing partner implicitly represents the whole is not categorical. Rather: *d'lo ite b'mata* — the rule applies when the absent partner was not in the city at the time of the court proceedings. *Aval ite b'mata, ibai leh l'meisei* — but if he was in the city, why didn't he show up? His presence in town and his failure to appear can be read as tacit authorization of his partner to represent the entire estate. The principle, then, is not a blanket presumption of representation, but a contextual one: one must look at the circumstances to determine whether the absent partner implicitly conferred authority on the one who appeared. If he was elsewhere and genuinely unaware, he may be entitled to his own proceeding. If he was present in town, the context suggests he acquiesced in the other's representation. As the *gemara* makes clear, these are not rigid categorical rules; context governs, and the de facto assumption is that each partner represents only his own share unless circumstances indicate otherwise.

## Same-Day Documents

### Rav: Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: שְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת הַיּוֹצְאִים בְּיוֹם אֶחָד, רַב אָמַר: חוֹלְקִין, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שׁוּדָא דְּדַיָּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that in a case of **two deeds that are issued,** i.e., dated, **on the same day,** e.g., where an individual gave or sold the same item to two different people, **Rav said: They divide** it between them, as it is impossible to determine who it belongs to, **and Shmuel said:** The item is awarded according to **the discretion [*shuda*] of the judges.**

The *gemara* now takes up a case structurally similar to the *mishna* — two claims on the same asset arising from the same date — but meaningfully different. In the *mishna*'s scenario, both wives' claims are fully valid; the problem is simply insufficient assets. Here the question is different: two people present deeds of sale for the same field, both dated on the same day. Only one of them can be the true owner — the seller could not have sold the same field twice. One of the documents, in some sense, reflects a genuine transaction and the other does not, even if we cannot determine which is which.

The question is how a court should adjudicate this. Rav rules *cholkin* — divide the field. This is striking, because unlike the *mishna*'s scenario, splitting here does not match any possible reconstruction of the facts. The field was sold either entirely to A or entirely to B; it was never half sold to each. Yet given that we have no way to determine the truth, Rav holds that division is the most judicious resolution available, even if it is guaranteed to be factually wrong.

### Shmuel: Guess

Shmuel rules *shuda de-dayni* — the judges should exercise their judgment and award the field to one party. Shmuel's basic point is that the court should try to match what actually happened, even if it cannot be certain of success. Rav's split guarantees being fifty percent wrong every time. Shmuel would rather aim for the full truth — knowing the attempt might fail entirely — than settle for a compromise that is always partially incorrect.

Rashi and Tosafot understand *shuda de-dayni* differently. Rashi holds that the judges should look at all available circumstances — whose story coheres better, who seems more credible, what other evidence can be assembled — and make their best informed guess. Tosafot, by contrast, understands *shuda de-dayni* as a genuinely arbitrary exercise of judicial authority: the judges simply award it to whomever they choose, without reference to any evidentiary considerations. On Tosafot's reading, the decision is essentially a crapshoot, and its legitimacy derives not from its accuracy but from the court's authority to resolve intractable disputes.

### Rav=Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **Rav said** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said** that **signatory witnesses** on the document **effect** the transaction? Here, since the seller or the giver of the field did not ask the signatory witnesses to note the exact time, it implies that he wished to give it to two people, but did not want to reveal that he was giving it to both of them.

The *gemara* proposes that Rav's position aligns with that of Rabbi Meir, who holds *eidei chasima karsi* — it is the witnesses who sign the document who give it legal effect. The debate about *eidei chasima* versus *eidei mesira* originates in the law of *gittin*: is a *get* made effective by the witnesses who sign it, or by the witnesses who observe its delivery? Rabbi Meir holds it is the signatories. On that view, the operative legal reality of a document is entirely defined by its content — by what the signatures attest — not by when or to whom it was physically handed over. Since both documents in our case say the same thing and bear the same date, they carry identical legal weight. There is no extrinsic fact — such as which was delivered first — that could differentiate them. From Rabbi Meir's perspective, both documents took effect simultaneously and in contradiction to each other, so there is a genuine sense in which both parties acquired equal and simultaneous claims. Division is therefore not merely a fallback for uncertainty; it reflects an underlying legal reality in which neither party holds an unambiguous superior claim.

### Shmuel=R. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who said** that **witnesses of the transmission effect** the transaction, i.e., the act of transferring the legal document to the beneficiary causes the transaction to take effect. Therefore, the fact that the two documents bear the same date is of no consequence because the documents were presumably not given to their beneficiaries simultaneously, and the property belongs exclusively to the individual who received his document first. Consequently, there is no reason to divide the property.

Shmuel's position aligns with Rabbi Elazar, who holds *eidei mesira karsi* — it is the witnesses of delivery who make the document legally effective. On this view, what matters is not what the document says but when it was actually handed over. One of the two buyers received his deed before the other, and that buyer is the true owner. The documents' identical dates are legally irrelevant; the truth lies in the sequence of delivery. Since there is one real owner and one who is not, the court should try to determine which is which, rather than split what is in principle an undivided whole.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַב כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּגִיטִּין. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר: אַף בִּשְׁטָרוֹת. מִכְּלָל דְּרַב סָבַר בִּשְׁטָרוֹת לָא! אֶלָּא: מְחַוַּורְתָּא רַב כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can you really establish** that the opinion **of Rav** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar? Didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar with regard to bills of divorce?** And Rav Yehuda related further: **When I said this** *halakha* **in the presence of Shmuel, he said:** The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar **even with regard to** other legal **documents** as well. **By inference,** it is apparent **that Rav holds** that with regard to other legal **documents, no,** the *halakha* is not in accordance with Rabbi Elazar. **Rather, it is clear** that **Rav** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and Shmuel** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.**

The *gemara* confirms this alignment through a ruling of Rav Yehuda: Rav Yehuda reported that Rav stated *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer b'gittin* — we rule like Rabbi Elazar in the laws of *gittin*. When Rav Yehuda reported this before Shmuel, Shmuel added *af b'shtaros* — not only in *gittin* but in all documents. This implies that Rav ruled like Rabbi Elazar only in *gittin*, not in documents generally — and therefore in the context of commercial deeds, Rav follows Rabbi Meir. The *gemara* concludes: *ela machvarta, Rav k'Rabbi Meir u'Shmuel k'Rabbi Elazar* — it is clear that Rav follows Rabbi Meir and Shmuel follows Rabbi Elazar.

### Braita Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת הַיּוֹצְאִים בְּיוֹם אֶחָד חוֹלְקִין, תְּיוּבְתָּא דִשְׁמוּאֵל. אָמַר לָךְ שְׁמוּאֵל: הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וַאֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: In the case of **two deeds that are issued** dated **the same day,** the recipients of the deeds **divide** the property equally. Is this not **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Shmuel?** The Gemara answers that **Shmuel** could have **said to you:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and I said** my opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.**

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *braita*: *shnei shtaros hayotzim b'yom echad, cholkin* — two documents issued on the same day, divide. This appears to directly contradict Shmuel. Shmuel responds that the *braita* follows Rabbi Meir. But the *gemara* presses: if the *braita* followed Rabbi Meir, why does its concluding clause rule that the one who received the document first acquires the field — *zeh shemasar lo kanah*? That ruling reflects Rabbi Elazar's principle that delivery determines acquisition, not Rabbi Meir's. And yet even while operating under Rabbi Elazar's framework — where there genuinely is one true owner determined by sequence of delivery — the *braita* still says to divide. This poses a problem for Shmuel's position that under Rabbi Elazar you try to identify the real owner rather than split.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: כָּתַב לְאֶחָד וּמָסַר לְאַחֵר — זֶה שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ קָנָה. וְאִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אַמַּאי קָנָה? הָאָמַר עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **If** this *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, say the latter clause** of that same *baraita*: If **he wrote** a deed **to one** individual **and** then **transmitted it to another** individual, **the one to whom** the deed **was transmitted has acquired** the property. **If** the *baraita* is following the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, why** did the latter individual **acquire** the property? **Didn't** Rabbi Meir **say** that **the signatory witnesses** on the document **effect the transaction** and not the witnesses to its transmission?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יַחְלוֹקוּ, וְכָאן אָמְרוּ: מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצֶה הַשָּׁלִישׁ, יַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The *baraita* cited above is entirely in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar. However, there **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to money whose ownership is uncertain, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where an individual sent a sum of money to another via a messenger, and by the time the messenger arrived, the intended recipient had died, and in the meantime, the individual who had sent the money also died, the *tanna'im* disagree about what to do with the money. **The Rabbis say:** The heirs of the sender and the heirs of the intended recipient **should divide** the money. **And here,** in Babylonia, **they said: The third** party, i.e., the messenger, **can do as he pleases** with the money, a ruling that is comparable to the solution of leaving the decision to the discretion of the judges.

Shmuel's ultimate response is that the question of whether to split or to exercise judicial discretion is itself a *tannaitic* dispute that runs independently of the Rabbi Meir / Rabbi Elazar debate. A further *braita* is cited involving a *shaliach* sent to deliver money to a party who had since died, whose sender had also died, leaving it unclear which estate the money belonged to. The sages say the agent should divide it between the two sets of heirs; the Babylonian tradition says the agent may do as he sees fit. This parallels the split-versus-decide question, demonstrating that both approaches have *tannaitic* support and that Shmuel can maintain his position as one legitimate opinion within that dispute.

## Rami & Ukva Case

### Competing Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִמֵּיהּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא כְּתַבְתִּינְהוּ לְנִכְסַהּ לְרָמִי בַּר חָמָא בְּצַפְרָא, לְאוּרְתָּא כְּתַבְתִּינְהוּ לְמָר עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא. אֲתָא רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, אוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּנִכְסֵי. אֲתָא מָר עוּקְבָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּנִכְסֵי. אֲתָא רַב שֵׁשֶׁת לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד מָר הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד מָר הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **the mother of Rami bar Ḥama wrote** a deed **in the morning** transferring ownership of **her property to Rami bar Ḥama,** and **in the evening she wrote** another deed transferring **her property to** another of her sons, **Mar Ukva bar Ḥama. Rami bar Ḥama came before Rav Sheshet** and the latter **established his** right **to the property. Mar Ukva,** his brother, **came before Rav Naḥman** and the latter **established his** right **to the property. Rav Sheshet came before Rav Naḥman** and **said to him: What is the reason that the Master did this,** i.e., why did you issue this ruling? Rav Naḥman **said to him: And what is the reason that the Master did this,** i.e., why did you rule as you did?

The *gemara* now presents a concrete application. The mother of Rami bar Chama and Mar Ukva bar Chama — two brothers — wrote over her property to Rami bar Chama in the morning and then, the same halachic day toward evening, wrote it over again to Mar Ukva bar Chama. Each son presented his document to a different court: Rami bar Chama came before Rav Sheshet, who awarded him the estate; Mar Ukva came before Rav Nachman, who awarded it to him. Rav Sheshet then appeared before Rav Nachman to challenge his ruling.

### Written Date Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּקְדֵים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם יָתְבִינַן דְּכָתְבִינַן שָׁעוֹת? אֶלָּא מָר מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שׁוּדָא דְּדַיָּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to him: Because** Rami bar Ḥama's deed **preceded** that of Mar Ukva. Rav Naḥman **said to** Rav Sheshet: **Is that to say** that **we are sitting in Jerusalem, that we write the hours** on our legal documents? The *halakha* is that in any place where the hours are not recorded on legal documents, it does not matter when during the day a document was written. Rav Sheshet asked Rav Naḥman: **But what is the reason that the Master did this,** ruling as you did? Rav Naḥman **said to him:** It was **the discretion of the judges,** i.e., I ruled this way since it seemed to me that this is the way the mother wanted it.

Rav Sheshet argued that Rami bar Chama's document came first — it was written and given to him in the morning — and therefore he should have priority. Rav Nachman rejected this: *atu be-Yerushalayim yasvinan de-kasvinan sha'os* — do we sit in Jerusalem where we write the hours into the document? Both documents bear the same date. We have no recorded indication in either document of when during the day the transaction took place. Since we do not record hours, we cannot go by who received it first; the two documents are on their face identical in date.

### Shuda De-Dayni Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא נָמֵי שׁוּדָא דְּדַיָּינֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲדָא, דַּאֲנָא דַּיָּינָא וּמָר לָאו דַּיָּינָא. וְעוֹד, מֵעִיקָּרָא לָאו בְּתוֹרַת הָכִי אֲתֵית לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to** Rav Naḥman: **I also** applied the principle of **the discretion of the judges** and ruled as I did. Rav Naḥman **said to him: One** response to your point is **that I am a judge, and the Master is not a judge,** as Rav Sheshet did not serve in the official capacity of a judge. **Furthermore, at the outset, you did not arrive at** your conclusion **for this** reason, but due to your own theory with regard to the dating of the documents, which proved to be incorrect.

Rav Sheshet challenged this on two grounds. Rav Sheshet argued that he too had exercised *shuda de-dayni* when awarding the estate to Rami bar Chama, and therefore his prior ruling should stand. Rav Nachman responded with two counters. First: *ana dayana u'mar lav dayana* — I am a qualified judge and you are not. As Rashi explains, Rav Nachman was the supreme judicial authority of his generation, appointed and recognized by the relevant political and rabbinic institutions. The exercise of *shuda de-dayni* is not available to every judge.

### Tosafot Limitations

Tosafot articulates the tradition underlying this point with precision. The tradition, transmitted in the name of Rabbeinu Chananel, is that *shuda de-dayni* applies *davka be-mekarkei* — only with respect to land, not movable property. And it applies *davka dayan mumche ke-Rav Nachman be-doro* — only to the supreme expert judge of the generation, as Rav Nachman was in his time. Rav Sheshet, despite being an undeniably great authority, did not meet that standard. On Tosafot's reading, then, Rav Nachman's point is not merely that he outranks Rav Sheshet, but that Rav Sheshet fundamentally lacked the standing to invoke *shuda de-dayni* in the first place. The power is not a prerogative of any qualified judge; it is reserved exclusively for the singular supreme authority.

This dovetails with Tosafot's broader understanding that *shuda de-dayni* is a genuinely arbitrary exercise of judicial power — not a reasoned best-guess, but an authoritative pronouncement whose legitimacy flows from the judge's supreme standing. Precisely because such an unbound discretion would be dangerous in lesser hands, it is restricted to the one judge in each generation whose stature and experience make it trustworthy.

### Conscious Exercise

Rav Nachman's second response to Rav Sheshet's claim of having also exercised *shuda de-dayni* is equally important: *mei-ikara lav be-torat hachi asit lah* — when you awarded the estate to Rami bar Chama, you were not acting in the mode of *shuda de-dayni* at all. You gave it to him because you believed he deserved it — because his document came first, in the morning. You were attempting a normal legal adjudication, reasoning that prior receipt of the document confers priority. That was a mistaken judgment: since the hours were not recorded, we cannot go by the sequence of delivery. But it was an attempted legal ruling, not an exercise of *shuda de-dayni*. *Shuda de-dayni* requires a conscious, explicit invocation of judicial authority — the judge must be deliberately saying: I am empowered to award this to whomever I see fit, and I award it to X. That is not what Rav Sheshet did. One cannot retroactively reframe a failed legal ruling as an exercise of *shuda de-dayni*. The distinction matters: *shuda de-dayni* is a deliberate assertion of a judge's sovereign discretion, not a label applied after the fact to justify a decision that turned out to lack a proper legal basis.

## Ambiguous Dates

### Documents Compared

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ תְּרֵי שְׁטָרֵי דַּאֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף. חַד הֲוָה כָּתוּב בְּחַמְשָׁא בְּנִיסָן, וְחַד הֲוָה כָּתוּב בֵּיהּ בְּנִיסָן סְתָמָא. אוֹקְמֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְהָהוּא דְּחַמְשָׁא בְּנִיסָן בִּנְכָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident in which an individual wrote two deeds about the same piece of property: There were **these two deeds that came before Rav Yosef.** In **one** deed, **it was written** that the owner of the field sold it to a particular individual **on the fifth of Nisan, and in** the other **one it was written** that he sold the same property to someone else **in Nisan, without specifying** on which day in Nisan the sale took place. **Rav Yosef established** that **the one** whose deed said **the fifth of Nisan** had the right **to the property.**

The *gemara* presents one final case. Two documents were brought before Rav Yosef. One bore the date *chamisha b'Nissan* — the fifth of Nissan — with full specificity. The other simply said *Nissan*, without specifying which day of the month.

### Specific Date Wins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִידַּךְ: וַאֲנָא אַפְסֵיד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ יָדְךָ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, אֵימָא בַּר עֶשְׂרִים וְתִשְׁעָה בְּנִיסָן אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The other** claimant **said to** Rav Yosef: **Should I lose?** After all, it is possible that my deed was written prior to the other deed. Rav Yosef **said to him: You are at a disadvantage,** because there is no specific date in your deed, allowing one to **say** that **your deed is from the twenty-ninth of Nisan.** Since you have no way to prove otherwise, the property is awarded to the one who has a more specific date recorded in his deed.

Rav Yosef awarded the field to the holder of the specifically dated document — *ukmi Rav Yosef le-hahu de-chamsha be-Nissan be-nechasim*. The logic is that the ambiguous document leaves open the possibility that the transaction it records occurred anywhere from the first to the twenty-ninth of Nissan. The holder of the ambiguous document may well have a claim that predates the fifth of Nissan — but equally, he may have a claim that postdates it. Given this uncertainty, his document places him in the weaker position, *at yadcha al hatachtona* — he cannot prove that his date precedes the other's. Perhaps his document was executed on the twenty-ninth of Nissan. The specific document establishes its date clearly; the ambiguous one does not. The specific claimant prevails.

### Reimbursement Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְנִכְתּוֹב לִי מָר טִירְפָא מֵאִיָּיר וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The man said to him: Let the Master write me a document of authorization to repossess** liened property of the seller from anyone who purchased property from him **from** the first of the month of **Iyyar and on.**

The claimant holding the ambiguous document accepted the ruling but then raised the question of reimbursement. He had paid for the field, and now the court had awarded it to someone else. He proposed to exercise liens on other fields that the original seller had sold to third parties, arguing that even in the worst-case interpretation of his document — that it was executed at the very end of Nissan — he had a lien predating the first of Iyar. He therefore asked the court to write him a *tirfa* — a court order authorizing collection — from the first of Iyar onward, so that he could pursue those other buyers for reimbursement.

### Buyer Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָכְלִי לְמֵימַר לָךְ: אַתְּ בַּר חַד בְּנִיסָן אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him:** A purchaser **can say to you: Your** deed is from **the first of Nisan,** so that the field that you purchased is rightfully yours and it is the other man, whose deed was dated on the fifth of Nisan, took it illegally. Therefore, you should take possession of that field rather than repossessing other property.

Rav Yosef explained that those other buyers could turn this request against the claimant. They could say: *at bar Nissan at* — you are a Nissan person; that field was actually sold to you in Nissan, and it is therefore still yours. The fact that a court awarded it to someone else does not change the underlying reality, which they maintain is that the seller validly conveyed the field to the ambiguous-document claimant. Since the field is actually his, he has suffered no real loss and is not entitled to reimbursement from us. This is a strikingly counterintuitive result: a court determination that he does not own the field is apparently not conclusive against third-party buyers who claim to know the true facts and use that knowledge to defeat his reimbursement claim.

### Power of Attorney

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? נִכְתְּבוּ הַרְשָׁאָה לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **what is his remedy?** The Gemara answers: **Let** the deed holders **write** a document of **authorization to each other.** If the individual whose deed was written on the fifth of Nisan authorizes the other individual to repossess property on his behalf, then he will be able to repossess property sold after the end of Nisan, because regardless of when his deed was written and whose deed was written first, he now has the right to repossess liened property.

The solution proposed by the *gemara* is *harsha'a*: the two claimants must grant each other power of attorney. Specifically, the holder of the specific-date document — who was awarded the field — must grant the other claimant his power of attorney to pursue reimbursement from the other buyers. Here is the logic: the buyers insist that the field truly belongs to the ambiguous-document claimant, not to the person the court awarded it to. If so, it is the court-awarded party — who "officially" holds the field as far as the legal record is concerned — who has a cognizable reimbursement claim against those buyers. The ambiguous-document claimant cannot collect from them in his own name, because they say the field was really his all along. But armed with the other party's power of attorney, he can present himself as collecting on that party's behalf — and since the buyers acknowledge the court's awardee as the one who "lost" the field to them, they cannot deny his representative's claim. Through *harsha'a*, the ambiguous-document claimant can thus recover the value of what was taken from him, channeling the reimbursement claim through the party whose standing the buyers are forced to recognize.
