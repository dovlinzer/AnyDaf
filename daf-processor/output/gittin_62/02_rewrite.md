# Gittin 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Food Entrustment

### Swapping Food Concern

We pick up at the bottom of 61b, wrapping up the fifth *perek* of *HaNizakin*. The broader context of this *perek* concerns things done for *darchei shalom* — ways of peace — and the *Gemara* has been exploring the interactions between a *chaver* and an *am ha'aretz* in matters of *tumah*, *taharah*, and *terumot u'ma'asrot*. The particular question at hand is whether we are genuinely unconcerned, as a general matter, that an *am ha'aretz* will swap out food entrusted to him for his own inferior produce.

The *Gemara* challenges this presumption: *v'hatanya* — is it not taught in a *Tosefta* from *Taharot* that seems to imply real concern about the reliability of an *am ha'aretz* and his household?

### Wife Grinding Grain

The *Tosefta Taharot* teaches: *eshet chaver tochenet im eshet am ha'aretz bizman she'hi t'meiah aval lo bizman she'hi t'horah* — the wife of a *chaver*, one who is scrupulous in the laws of *tumah v'taharah*, may grind grain together with the wife of an *am ha'aretz* when she herself is *t'meiah*, but not when she is *t'horah*.

The logic is as follows. When the wife of the *chaver* is *t'meiah* — whether as a *niddah* or for any other reason — she is habituated at home to avoid direct contact with the grain, and she will carry that habit with her here. She will not cause the grain to become *tamei*. But when she is *t'horah*, she may absent-mindedly handle the food, or even pop a morsel into her mouth — and that food will not have had *terumot u'ma'asrot* taken from it. To prevent that inadvertent consumption, the *Tosefta* forbids the arrangement when she is *t'horah*.

### R. Shimon ben Elazar

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar takes a stricter position: *af bizman she'hi t'meiah lo titchan* — even when the wife of the *chaver* is *t'meiah*, she should not grind together with the wife of the *am ha'aretz*. His concern is *mipnei she'chavertah notenet lah ochel* — because the wife of the *am ha'aretz*, appreciating the shared labor, may well offer her friend some of the food as a gesture of collegiality. That social offering, however well-intentioned, could lead the wife of the *chaver* to eat produce from which *ma'asrot* have not been separated. So even the passive concern of absent-minded eating is compounded here by the active pressure of a friend's hospitality.

### Concern of Theft

The *Gemara* now draws out a troubling implication of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's reasoning. If the wife of the *am ha'aretz* is prepared to offer food to her friend without her husband's explicit permission, she is effectively stealing from him — since the food belongs to her husband, and he has not authorized the gift. As Rashi formulates it: *chasheida eshet am ha'aretz lignov mishel ba'alah u'latet l'chavertah*. And if this woman is capable of such theft, the *Gemara* asks: *hashta mignav ganva chilufei lo mechalfa* — if she will steal from her husband to give to her friend, surely she would swap out food entrusted to her if she preferred it to her own. How, then, can the *Gemara* maintain the general presumption that we need not worry about swapping?

### Rationalizing Offer

Rav Yosef resolves the difficulty: *hatam nami moreh v'amrah* — even there, in the case of the *Tosefta*, the woman is not stealing in any straightforward sense. She is *moreh hetter*, rationalizing her action as permitted. Her reasoning is captured in the principle *tora m'dashi k'achil* — just as the Torah forbids muzzling an ox while it threshes, so too one who labors together with another ought to be able to benefit from the produce. In her mind, her friend is working alongside her, and it is only natural and proper to share. This rationalization does not transfer to the case of straightforward swapping, where no such logic applies. A person will engage in behavior they can morally reframe; they will not as readily commit what they recognize, without any rationalization available, as simple theft.

## Challah & Terumah

### Challah Restriction

We now turn to a new but related topic, beginning three lines from the top of 62a. Rav Yossi ben Meshullam testified in the name of Rav Yochanan — his brother — who said in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Chasma: *ein osin challas am ha'aretz b'taharah* — one may not separate *challah* from the dough of an *am ha'aretz* while in a state of *taharah*.

### Tamei Assumption

The case is that of a professional bread-kneader who is a *chaver* and is currently *tahor*. An *am ha'aretz* brings him dough and asks him to knead it, bake *challot*, and while he is at it, please perform *hafrashat challah* — separate out the requisite portion for the *kohen*. This seems like an opportunity for a praiseworthy act: a *chaver*, *tahor*, taking *challah* on behalf of an *am ha'aretz* who may not know how.

The problem, however, is that the flour delivered by the *am ha'aretz* is assumed to be *tamei* — flour in the possession of an *am ha'aretz* carries that presumption. If the *chaver* separates *challah* while in a state of *taharah*, the *kohen* who receives it will see that it was separated by this known *chaver*, and will naturally assume the *challah* is *tahora*. But it is not — it derives from *tamei* dough. The *kohen* may then eat *tamei challah* under the mistaken impression that it is *tahora*, which is a serious transgression. This is why the procedure is forbidden when the kneader is *tahor*.

### Dough Alternative

There is, however, a permitted path: *aval osin issas chulyav b'taharah* — one may take the flour from the *am ha'aretz* and make it into dough while in a state of *taharah*. The critical distinction is that flour does not yet have the status of food susceptible to *tumah*; it is only once water is added and the dough forms that *tumah* can take hold. If the *am ha'aretz* delivers the flour — before any liquid contact — the kneader who is *tahor* can transform it into dough himself, in *taharah*, and then separate *challah* in *taharah*. The resulting *challah* genuinely is *tahorah*, and the *kohen* will not be misled.

### Unusual Vessels

Once the *challah* is separated, the task of delivering it to the *kohen* falls to the *am ha'aretz*, and a new concern arises: will he touch it and render it *tamei*? The solution given is *v'menicha b'kefishah o b'anchusah* — the separated *challah* is placed in a *kefishah* or an *anchusah*. Rashi explains that these are unusual, distinctive vessels — something like a worn flask or, according to the *Geonim*, a flat wooden board. These peculiar containers serve a specific purpose: when the *am ha'aretz* comes to retrieve his bread and the separated *challah*, he takes both together in those vessels — *notel es shteihen v'ein choshesh* — and we are not concerned that he will touch the *challah*, for reasons the *Gemara* will shortly explain.

### Terumah Olives

A parallel set of rules applies to the separation of *terumah* from olives. *V'ein osin trumas zeisav b'taharah* — similarly, one may not press olives that have already been crushed by an *am ha'aretz* and then separate *terumah* while *tahor*. The olive-pressing industry involved two stages: first, crushing the olives with a large stone wheel to begin releasing the oil, and then pressing them under sustained pressure to extract the remainder. Once the olives have been crushed and the liquid begins to emerge, contact between the liquid and the olive renders the produce susceptible to *tumah*, just as water does to flour. Olives delivered after crushing are therefore already *tamei*, and the same deception of the *kohen* would result if *terumah* were separated from them while the presser is *tahor*.

The alternative: *aval osin zeisim chulyav b'tahara* — one may take the whole, uncrushed olives from the *am ha'aretz* before any liquid contact has occurred, perform the crushing oneself in *taharah*, and thereby produce *terumah* that is genuinely *tahorah*. *V'notel mimenu k'dei terumah u'minicha bikli chaver* — one takes the portion designated as *terumah* and places it in one's own vessel — a vessel of the *chaver* that is *tahor* — and the *am ha'aretz* takes both portions when he comes to collect, without concern that he will touch the *terumah* improperly.

## Parnassa Logic

### Livelihood Allowance

The *Gemara* now asks: *v'ta'ama mai* — why do we permit these procedures at all? Even in the cases that are technically permitted, we should be concerned that the *am ha'aretz* will touch the separated *challah* or *terumah* and render them *tamei*. What justifies proceeding?

The answer comes from Rabbi Yochanan: *mishum k'dei chayei d'gaval u'mishum k'dei chayei d'badad* — for the sake of the livelihood of the bread-dough kneader and the sake of the livelihood of the olive presser. The vast majority of the population were *amei ha'aretz*, not *chaveirim*, and a professional kneader or presser who could also perform the required separations would have a significant business advantage. The halachic accommodations were crafted specifically to enable these artisans to make a living — an allowance analogous in spirit to the *darchei shalom* concessions of our *Mishnah*, though here the rationale is *k'dei chayei* rather than social harmony.

### Both Cases Needed

*V'tzricha* — the *Gemara* establishes that both cases, the kneader and the olive presser, must be explicitly taught; neither can be inferred from the other.

Had the *Gemara* told us only about the dough-kneader, one might have understood the dispensation specifically because *d'lo nafish agrei* — his profit margin is very thin. It is entirely understandable that we would extend special accommodations to someone whose livelihood is marginal. But *aval badad d'nafish agrei* — the owner of the olive press, by contrast, earns a considerably larger profit during the pressing season, and one might have thought we need not be so solicitous of his commercial interests. Hence the olive presser's case must also be stated.

Conversely, had the *Gemara* told us only about the olive presser, one might have justified the allowance on account of *d'lo shechi'ach lei* — the pressing season is brief. It is true he earns well during those weeks, but that limited seasonal income must support him the entire year, and so perhaps a special accommodation is warranted. *Aval gaval d'shechi'ach lei* — but the bread-kneader works year-round and has ongoing income, and one might have thought he requires no special accommodation. Therefore *tzricha* — both must be stated, to make clear that the dispensation applies in either situation.

### Tevel Warning

The *Gemara* now turns to the mechanism by which the halachic concerns are actually addressed. The statement *amar mar* reintroduces the case of *challah*: you take the *challah* in *taharah*, place it in the unusual vessels, and the *am ha'aretz* takes both the baked bread and the *challah* without your having to worry.

But the *Gemara* probes: *v'leichosh dilma naga bah* — why should we not be concerned that he will touch it? The answer is *d'amrinan lei* — we tell him: *chazi ei nagat bah hadar l'tivla* — if you touch this, everything will revert to being *tevel* and forbidden to eat. The *am ha'aretz*, though not punctilious about *tumah v'taharah*, is presumed to care about *tevel*. After all, the general principle is *rov amei ha'aretz me'asrin hen* — most *amei ha'aretz* do in fact take *terumos u'ma'asros*. Certainly one who has gone to the trouble of asking someone to separate *challah* on his behalf demonstrates that he cares. So we issue this warning — even if it is not literally accurate — and the fear of causing his bread to revert to *tevel* will deter him from touching the *challah*.

### Visual Reminder

The *Gemara* pushes further: *v'leichosh dilma lo ichpas lei* — but perhaps this particular *am ha'aretz* does not care? *Hashta l'sikuni k'michavein michpas lo ichpas lei* — this is readily answered. The very fact that he came to have *challah* taken for him demonstrates he cares. If he cared enough to request the separation, he cares enough to be deterred by the warning that touching it will ruin everything.

The *Gemara* then raises a more pointed difficulty for the case of *terumah* from olives. In the *challah* case, the separated portion is placed in the unusual vessels — *kefishah* or *anchusah* — which serve as a *hikeira*, a visual cue. When the *am ha'aretz* reaches for his produce and sees these distinctive containers, he is reminded of the warning and keeps his hands off. *Bishlama hasam is lei hikeira* — the unusual vessel functions as an ongoing visual reminder. But in the case of *terumah*, the text said to place it in *kli chaver*, a vessel of the *chaver*, which need not be unusual or distinctive at all. *Hacha mai hikeira is lei* — what visual marker will remind him? Without something to jog his memory, he may simply open the vessel and help himself, forgetting the warning entirely.

### Vessel Types

The *Gemara* resolves this by clarifying what is meant by *kli chaver*: *d'manach lei b'chlei glalim b'chlei avanim b'chlei adamah* — one places the *terumah* in vessels made of dried dung, stone, or unfinished earthenware. These materials cannot become *tamei* under any circumstances — unlike pottery, they do not receive *tumah*. This serves a dual purpose: first, even if he touches the vessel, the *terumah* inside will not become *tamei* through the vessel's contact; and second, these are sufficiently unusual containers that they function as a visual reminder, just as the distinctive vessels did in the *challah* case.

The *Gemara* then asks: *ee hachi mai irya yad d'chaver* — if the point is simply that the vessels cannot become *tamei*, why must they belong to a *chaver*? Even vessels of an *am ha'aretz* made of these same materials would serve equally well. *Hachi nami ka'amar* — the *Gemara* concedes the point entirely. The intention of *kli chaver* is not ownership by a *chaver* specifically, but vessels *she're'uyim l'hishtamesh bahen chaver* — vessels suitable for use by a *chaver*, meaning vessels that cannot become *tamei*. Even vessels belonging to an *am ha'aretz* would qualify, provided they are of the materials that cannot receive *tumah*.

## Non-Jews Shemitah

### Machzikin Definition

We now come to the final portion of the *Mishnah* in the fifth *perek*, which states: *machzikin yedei ovdei kochavim b'shvi'is* — one may *machzikin*, strengthen or give encouragement to, non-Jews who are working the land during *shemitah*. This is partly a matter of *darchei shalom*, but perhaps more essentially a practical necessity: since Jews cannot work the land during *shemitah*, the labor of non-Jews may be essential for the community's sustenance. Encouraging them is therefore not merely a social nicety.

### Hoeing Prohibition

The *Gemara* asks what precisely *machzikin* entails. The initial assumption might be that it means working alongside them, similar to the *Mishnah*'s earlier allowances of lending implements or grinding grain. The *Gemara* challenges this: *v'amar Rav Dimi bar Shishna mishmei de'Rav* — Rav Dimi said in the name of Rav that *ein odrin im akum bishviit* — one may not hoe together with a non-Jew during *shemitah*.

This ruling raises a larger question — one that the *Gemara* and *Tosafot* address with implications for the *heter mechira*. The underlying question is *yesh kinyan le'akum le'hafkiya miyad trumot u'maasrot b'Eretz Yisrael* — does non-Jewish ownership of land in Eretz Yisrael affect its *shemitah* status sufficiently to permit working it? Those familiar with the *heter mechira* will recognize this as precisely the principle upon which that dispensation rests. And yet this *Gemara* indicates that one may not hoe together with a non-Jew even on his own land.

*Tosafot* addresses the related *Gemara* in *perek zeh borer* concerning a *kohen* plowing during *shemitah* who claims to be an *agiston*. Rashi there explains that the *agiston* is a hired hand of a non-Jew, and the non-Jew's ownership of the land makes it permissible. *Tosafot* rejects this reading: our *Gemara* explicitly forbids working alongside a non-Jew during *shemitah*, and there is no compelling reason to distinguish between paid and volunteer labor.

*Tosafot* offers several alternative explanations. First, the *agiston* case may involve *sakanat nefashot* — if the farmers failed to pay the king's tax, their lives would be at risk, and *pikuach nefashot* overrides *shemitah*. Second, perhaps that *Gemara* holds, in contrast to ours, that non-Jewish ownership does indeed *hafkiya*, remove the *shemitah* prohibition — meaning these two *Gemarot* represent competing positions, and the *posek* who rules like the other *Gemara* may permit working land owned by a non-Jew. Third, perhaps even according to our *Gemara*, since *shemitah* in the present era is *derabbanan*, there is room for leniency in specific circumstances — such as paying a tax — even though as a general matter the prohibition remains.

These final two explanations of *Tosafot* are directly significant for the *heter mechira* debate.

### Encouragement Examples

Returning to the *Gemara*'s resolution: *lo tzricha le'meimar lehu achzukei be'alma* — what *machzikin* means in the *Mishnah* is simply saying *achzukei*, a word of encouragement, akin to *yasher koach*. This is illustrated by the examples that follow. *Ki had de'Rav Yehuda amar lehu achzuka* — when Rav Yehuda saw people working the land during *shemitah* he said *yasher koach*. *Rav Sheshet amar lehu ashrasa* — Rav Sheshet offered a similar word of encouragement, meaning something like "more power to you."

### Shalom Etiquette

The *Mishnah*'s final clause states that one asks after the well-being of non-Jews — *sho'alin bishlomam mipnei darchei shalom* — but one does not say *shalom aleichem* twice. Rashi understands this as a general statement, not limited to the *shemitah* context: when one encounters a non-Jew in ordinary circumstances, one may greet him with *shalom aleichem* — as illustrated by Rav Chisda, who made a point of going out to greet non-Jews — but one should not be *kofel shalom*, doubling the greeting with excessive enthusiasm. Rav Kahana would say *shlama lemar*, "peace to the master," as a courteous greeting.

### Achzuki Meaning

*Amar Rav Geviba lo nitzrecha ela leyom chagam* — Rav Geviba clarifies that the *Mishnah*'s clause about *sho'alin bishlomam* refers not to *shemitah* at all, but to non-Jews' festival days. As taught in a *braita*: do not enter a non-Jew's home on his festival to offer well-wishes — that would be too encouraging of his religious celebration. But if you encounter him in the marketplace, you may address him, *noten lo besafa rafa uvichoved rosh* — in a soft tone, with a certain gravity and restraint, without warmth or enthusiasm. The overall framework thus emerges: during *shemitah*, give genuine *achzuki* encouragement; year-round, a standard courteous greeting is fine; on their festivals, a measured and understated acknowledgment is permitted but must be kept at a minimum.

## Gneiva Story

### Gneiva Greeting

The *Gemara* now relates a memorable episode. Rav Huna and Rav Chisda were sitting together when Gneiva — a figure who appeared at the beginning of the tractate in a troubling context, having caused great distress to Mar Ukva and eventually being led away in chains — passed by. One said to the other: *nekum mikamei de'bar oryan hu* — let us rise before him, for he is a scholar. The other demurred: *lo umikmi palga nekum* — should we stand for someone who is a divisive troublemaker?

While they were debating, Gneiva approached them, and he greeted them with *shlama lechu malchi, shlama lechu malchi* — "Peace to you, O kings; peace to you, O kings." This scene is placed here not incidentally: the *Gemara* has just been discussing *kefilat shalom*, and here Gneiva demonstrates precisely that — doubling his greeting and addressing them with regal honor.

### Kings Justification

They asked him: *mena lach de-rabbanan ikru malchi* — where do you get the idea that sages are called kings? He cited the *pasuk* in Mishlei where the Torah speaks in its own voice: *bi melachim yimlochu* — through me kings have their reign. The implication is that true kingship is derived from Torah; those who bear and transmit Torah are in that sense kings. They then asked: *u'mena lach de-kafliinan shlama le-malchi* — and where do you know that one doubles the greeting to a king?

### Double Shalom Source

He replied: *de-amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav* — I have the tradition from Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, who derived from a *pasuk* that one doubles the *shalom* to a king. The *pasuk* in which the spirit takes hold of Amashai, head of the troops, and he declares to David: *lecha David ve-imecha ben Yishai shalom shalom lecha* — "Peace, peace to you, David." The word *shalom* appears twice, establishing the practice of *kofel shalom* before a king. This man, it turns out, was not merely a *bar oryan* in a superficial sense — he knew the traditions of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav and could source them precisely.

### Animal Feeding

The rabbis, apparently impressed, invited him to share a meal with them: *litim mar midi* — would you like to taste something? He declined, citing another ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *asur le-adam she-yitim klum ad she-yiten ma'achal le-vehemto* — it is forbidden for a person to taste anything before feeding his animals. The source: the *pasuk* says first *ve-natati esev be-sadecha le-vehemtecha* — I will give grass in your field for your animals — and only afterward *ve-achalta ve-savata* — you shall eat and be satisfied. The sequence in the *pasuk* is instructive: animals are fed before people eat.

The story is a striking one with which to close the fifth *perek*. Here was a figure who, from what we knew of him, seemed to represent social disorder. Yet he turned out to be deeply learned, respectful of the rabbis in the most formal and considered way — addressing them as kings, doubling his greeting with full awareness of its source — and possessed of an admirable personal ethic in the treatment of his animals. One might see in this a quiet commentary on the theme of *darchei shalom* that has animated the entire *perek*: the pursuit of a society in which people are treated with respect and consideration is not only a rabbinic prescription but a human aspiration. It is expressed not just in formal rules about lending implements or greasing the wheels of commerce, but in how one enters a room, addresses those one encounters, and orders one's own behavior even in private matters like feeding one's animals before sitting down to eat.

## Get Retraction

### Receive—Retractable

We now turn the page and begin the sixth *perek*, sometimes called *Ha-omer* and sometimes *Ha-itkabel*, after its second and more distinctive word. We are back to the central subject matter of *Gittin*.

The *Mishnah* opens: *ha-omer hitkabel zeh get le-ishti* — if a husband says to someone, "Receive this *get* for my wife," or *o holech get zeh le-ishti* — "Deliver this *get* to my wife" — then *im rotze lachazor yachzor* — if he wishes to retract, he may do so, up until the moment the *get* is actually delivered.

The reason: the wife did not appoint this person as her *shaliach*. To be divorced is assumed to be a detriment to the wife, and an act to one's detriment cannot be performed on one's behalf by an agent one never appointed. This person can function as the husband's agent to bring the *get*, but the divorce is not final until the wife actually receives it. The word "receive" is therefore reread as an instruction to deliver — *hitkabel v'holech* — make her receive it by bringing it to her; but in either formulation, the husband retains the ability to retract.

### Wife's Shaliach

If, however, the wife herself appoints a *shaliach* and instructs him: *hitkabel li giti* — receive my *get* on my behalf — then *im ratza lachazor lo yachzor* — once the husband places the *get* in this agent's hands, he cannot retract. The wife's *shaliach le'kabbalah* has received the *get*, and from that moment the divorce is effective. The rationale is the inverse of the first case: divorce, whatever its effect on the wife, is something she has affirmatively sought and for which she has formally appointed an agent.

### Take & Deliver

*U'l'fichach* — therefore, if the wife's *shaliach* presents himself and the husband wishes to preserve a window to retract, he may do so by recasting the arrangement. If the husband says *ee efshi shetikabel lah, ela holech v'ten lah* — "I do not want you to receive this on her behalf; rather, take this and deliver it to her" — he thereby converts the agent from a *shaliach le'kabbalah* of the wife into a *shaliach* of the husband for delivery. The divorce is then not effective until the *get* is placed in the wife's hand, and the husband retains the right to retract until that moment: *im ratza lachazor yachzor*.

### R. Shimon View

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes a broader view: *af ha-omeret tol li giti im ratza lachazor lo yachzor* — even if the wife says to the *shaliach* not "receive" but "take my *get* for me," this too constitutes a valid appointment as *shaliach le'kabbalah*, and the husband cannot retract once the *get* is handed over. The *chachamim* presumably disagree — "take it for me" is not equivalent to "receive it for me," and the wife's ability to effect her divorce turns on using the proper formulation — but Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that *tol* is interpreted as *kabbalah*, and the husband is bound.

## Holech vs. Zachi

### No Kabbalah

The *Gemara* opens its analysis. Rav Acha brei de-Rav Avya said to Rav Ashi: *ta'ama d'lo shavitia ihi shaliach l'kabbalah* — the reason the husband can retract in the first case of the *Mishnah* is because the wife never made this person a *shaliach le'kabbalah*. The *Mishnah*'s teaching of the case where the husband says "receive this *get* for my wife" — which obviously cannot work immediately, since the recipient is no one's *shaliach* for *kabbalah* — is instructive for what it implies: we are told the wife did not appoint this person as her agent.

### Holech Effectiveness

This raises a question about the second case. *Ha shavitia ihi shaliach l'kabbalah* — had the wife made this person her *shaliach le'kabbalah*, would the first case clearly be effective with no possibility of retraction? Certainly. But what about the second case — *holech v'ten lah*, take this and deliver it to her? If the wife had made the agent a *shaliach le'kabbalah*, and the husband then said not "receive this" but "take this to her," is the *get* immediately effective? Or does the husband's language of *holech* effectively demote the agent from *shaliach le'kabbalah* to *shaliach holacha* — an agent for conveyance rather than for receipt?

### Holech Zachi

The inference proposed is: since the first case in the *Mishnah* would obviously work if the wife had made this person a *shaliach le'kabbalah*, the *Mishnah* must be stating its rule for both cases in order to teach something about the second case. And if the *Gemara* infers that the second case — *holech v'ten lah* — would also be immediately effective when the wife had made the agent a *shaliach le'kabbalah*, this tells us something significant: *holech kezachi dami* — saying "take this" to someone is equivalent to saying "take possession of this on behalf of another." The word *holech* is not merely an instruction for physical conveyance; it transfers ownership. Those familiar with earlier discussions in the tractate will recall the debate over whether *zachi* — an instruction to acquire on behalf of another — is effective immediately; here, *holech* is proposed to carry the same force.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi rejects the inference. *Lo amina lach holech lav kezachi dami* — I do not accept that *holech* is like *zachi*. And if that is so, then the second case in the *Mishnah* would not be immediately effective even if the wife had made the agent a *shaliach le'kabbalah*. But then what is the purpose of the first case — *hitkabel get zeh le-ishti* — which seems to be stated as a *chiddush*? If the answer is simply "he is not a *shaliach le'kabbalah*," that is self-evident. Why does the *Mishnah* need to tell us this?

### Itkabel Chidush

The resolution: *itkabel* is a genuine *chiddush* in its own right. *D'salka daitecha amina, ho-ilu ba'al lav bar shavei shaliach le-kabala hu* — one might have thought that since the husband has no power to appoint a *shaliach le'kabbalah* on his wife's behalf, the formula *hitkabel get zeh le-ishti* is entirely ineffective. When the *get* is ultimately delivered to the wife, one might have thought it doesn't work at all — the husband said "receive," but he cannot make this person a *shaliach le'kabbalah*, so perhaps the entire transaction fails. The *Mishnah* therefore teaches: *de-itkabel ve-holech kama* — "receive it" means "bring it to her and have her receive it." Even though the husband used the language of *kabbalah*, we interpret his intent as an instruction for delivery. The *chiddush* is not that it fails immediately — of course it does, there is no *shaliach le'kabbalah* — the *chiddush* is that when the *get* reaches the wife, it does work, because *hitkabel* is understood as an authorization to deliver.

The question of whether *holech* is like *zachi* — and what consequences flow from that for cases where the wife has made the agent a *shaliach le'kabbalah* — remains open, and will be taken up as the *Gemara*'s analysis of this *perek* continues.
