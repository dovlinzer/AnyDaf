# Kiddushin 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Dates

### Intro: Dates & Shaveh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר לָהּ: "בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וּבָזוֹ". אֲבָל אָמַר לָהּ: "בְּאֵלּוּ" – אֲפִילּוּ אוֹכֶלֶת נָמֵי מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, כִּי קָא אָכְלָה – מִדְּנַפְשַׁהּ קָאָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna **taught** that if she ate the dates one by one their value is not added together **only if he said to her** that she is betrothed: **With this** one, **and with this** one, **and with this** one, which indicates that she is not betrothed until she has received them all. **But** if **he said to her** that he is betrothing her: **With these,** i.e., with all of them together, **even** if she **is eating** them one by one she is **also betrothed** if their combined value is one *peruta*. The reason is that **when she eats, she eats of her own** food. Once she has accepted the dates as money for betrothal, they become her own property and she is betrothed.

The discussion opens in the context of the *mishnah* dealing with a man who counts off individual dates — or similar objects — one by one, saying "marry me with this, and with this, and with this," and the question whether each date constitutes a separate act of *kiddushin* or whether they are combined into one act, such that collectively they might satisfy the requirement of *shaveh perutah*. We pick up on the bottom of 46b, approximately twenty lines from the bottom, at the line beginning with the word *bechamna*.

### Money: Gift or Loan?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֲחוֹתוֹ, רַב אָמַר: מָעוֹת חוֹזְרִים. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מָעוֹת מַתָּנָה. רַב אָמַר: מָעוֹת חוֹזְרִים – אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ, וְגָמַר וְנָתַן לְשׁוּם פִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** In the case of **one who betroths his sister** by giving her money, **Rav says: The money** of the betrothal must be **returned** by his sister, as this betrothal does not take effect. **And Shmuel says:** This **money** is considered to be **a gift** that she may keep. The Gemara clarifies their respective opinions. **Rav says: The money** must be **returned,** since **a person knows that betrothal does not take effect with his sister, and he decided to give** the money to her **for the sake of a deposit.**

At this point the *gemara* temporarily moves away from the question about counting off the dates and raises a different question: if a man performs *kiddushin* and for some reason the *kiddushin* does not take effect, must the woman return the money? Is the money to be treated as a gift — a *matanah* — or as a *pikadon*, an entrusted object held in trust? Obviously, if he stated his intention explicitly, there is no question. The issue is how to interpret the transaction legally when nothing further was said.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר: מָעוֹת מַתָּנָה – אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ, וְגָמַר וְנָתַן לְשׁוּם מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel maintains:** The **money** is considered to be **a gift,** because **a person knows that betrothal does not take effect with his sister, and he decided to give** the money to her **for the sake of a gift.**

The *gemara* frames the question as a debate between Rav and Shmuel, using the case of a man who betroths his sister — a *kiddushin* that everyone understands is entirely invalid. Does she return the money or may she keep it? Rav holds she must return it, which seems to be the straightforward position: why should we read into the transaction a new gift? Shmuel, however, holds that she may keep it.

### Challah Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבִינָא: הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַלָּתוֹ קֶמַח – אֵינָהּ חַלָּה, וְגָזֵל בְּיַד כֹּהֵן. וְאַמַּאי גָּזֵל בְּיַד כֹּהֵן? נֵימָא: אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישִׁים חַלָּה קֶמַח, וְנָתַן לְשֵׁם מַתָּנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raises an objection** from a mishna (*Ḥalla* 2:5): In the case of **one who separates his *ḥalla*,** the portion of dough that must be given to a priest, from **flour,** before it has been made into dough, the portion he has separated **is not *ḥalla*.** Since the Torah states: "Of the first of your dough you shall set apart" (Numbers 15:20), *ḥalla* can be separated only from dough. **And** if the priest fails to return the flour it is considered **stolen property in the priest's possession.** Ravina asks: **And why** is it **stolen property in the priest's possession?** In this case too, **let us say** as Shmuel does: **A person knows that one cannot separate *ḥalla*** from **flour, and he gave** the flour **for the sake of a gift.**

The *gemara* challenges Shmuel's position from a case involving one who separates *challah* from flour — that is, before it has become dough — and gives it to a *kohen*. This is similarly a transaction that everyone should understand is ineffective. The *halakhah* is that the *kohen* must return the flour; he does not get to keep it. This appears to support Rav's position.

### Shmuel's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּנָפֵיק חוּרְבָּה מִינַּהּ – זִימְנִין דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן פָּחוֹת מֵחָמֵשׁ רְבָעִים קֶמַח, וְהַאי אָלֵישׁ לֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וְקָסָבַר נִתַּקְּנָה עִיסָּתוֹ, וְאָתֵי לְמֵיכְלַהּ בְּטִיבְלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There it is different.** The priest must return the flour **because** otherwise a **ruinous** situation may **emerge from it.** How so? **Sometimes the priest has,** on his own, **less than five-fourths** of a *kav* of **flour,** i.e., he has less than the amount necessitating the separation of *ḥalla*, **and** he also has **this** flour, which gives him a total of more than five-fourths of a *kav*, the amount necessitating the separation of *ḥalla*. **He will knead** all **this** flour **together and** will **think his dough has been made ready** with regard to *ḥalla*, since he did not have enough of his own flour to require the separation of *ḥalla* and he had added flour that had been separated as *ḥalla* to it. **And he will** then **come to eat it in its untithed** state, as the flour he received was not in fact *ḥalla*. Therefore, the Sages required him to return the flour.

The *gemara* explains that according to Shmuel, the reason the *kohen* must return the flour is not because of any general principle about invalid transactions, but rather to prevent confusion about the law. If the *kohen* keeps the flour, the *ba'al habayit* or the *kohen* himself might come to think the *challah* separation was valid, leading to further violations. The flour is therefore returned as a way of reinforcing that it was not effective *challah*. Shmuel's broader position — that recipients may generally keep what was given in such contexts — remains intact; it is only the specific concern about legal confusion that mandates return here.

### Alternative: 2nd Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיהְוֵי תְּרוּמָה, וְלֹא תֵּאָכֵל עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא עָלֶיהָ חַלָּה מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question based on a comparison to a similar case: **But** why must the priest go to the effort of returning the flour to its owner? **Let** the separated flour **be** considered ***teruma*,** i.e., *ḥalla*, **and it should not be eaten** by the priest **until he removes *ḥalla* for it from somewhere else,** in order to make it ready to be eaten.

The *gemara* then asks: within the logic of Shmuel's reasoning, why not find an intermediate solution? Let the *kohen* keep the flour, but instruct him to separate *challah* again from it — or, equivalently, once it becomes dough. That would signal to everyone that the original separation was invalid, and the *kohen* would still benefit from keeping the flour. The *gemara* supports this suggestion by noting that the *kohen* would be willing to listen (*metzayet*) to such an instruction.

### Why Alt. Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּתְרֵי מָנֵי – צָאֵית, בְּחַד מָנָא – לָא צָאֵית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to things that are **in two** separate **containers,** the priest **will listen.** Since the priest is well aware of the difference between a perforated and a non-perforated pot, he will accept the ruling of the Sages to separate *teruma* an additional time. With regard to something that is **in one container, he will not listen.** He does not see any difference between receiving flour or dough, and he will not accept the ruling of the Sages to separate *ḥalla* an additional time. Consequently, they required him to return the flour.

The problem, says the *gemara*, is the *ba'al habayit*. Even if the *kohen* would listen to us and separate *challah* a second time, the *ba'al habayit* will not see that second separation. From his perspective, he gave flour, the *kohen* kept it, and the transaction is done. He will now believe his own obligation has been discharged and will proceed to eat his remaining dough while it is still *tevel* — untithed produce. This creates a serious violation. Therefore, returning the flour to the *ba'al habayit* is the only way to prevent the confusion.

### Owner's Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ: אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישִׁים חַלָּה קֶמַח! יוֹדֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ. יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישׁ חַלָּה קֶמַח, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ, דְּסָבַר: טַעְמַיְיהוּ מַאי – מִשּׁוּם טִירְחָא דְכֹהֵן וְטִירְחָא דְכֹהֵן אַחֵילְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** that **a person knows that one may not separate *ḥalla*** from **flour?** How can the priest make such an error? The Gemara answers: **He knows** the *halakha* **but he does not know** the reason for the *halakha*. **He knows that one cannot separate *ḥalla*** from **flour, but he does not know** the reason, **as he thinks: What** is **the reason** one may not separate *ḥalla* from flour? **Due to the labor of the priest,** i.e., to prevent the priest from having to knead it himself. And as far as **the labor of the priest** goes, he thinks: **I have relinquished** the right to have the non-priest knead it for me.

The *gemara* then raises what seems like an obvious objection: surely everyone knows that one does not separate *challah* from flour before it becomes dough — the *ba'al habayit* knows this! The *gemara* responds with a subtle but important distinction: *yodeia v'eino yodeia* — he sort of knows, but not fully. He knows the norm is not to do it. However, he does not know the underlying reason. He assumes the prohibition is merely a matter of not burdening the *kohen* with the extra work of turning flour into dough, and since the *kohen* here was willing to accept the flour (*tircha d'kohen kablah*), he may conclude that *bedi'avad* — after the fact — the separation might have been valid. Because his understanding is incomplete and his error is therefore plausible, we must return the flour to correct any possible misconception.

### Hydroponic Plants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיהְוֵי תְּרוּמָה, וְיַחְזוֹר וְיִתְרוֹם. מִי לָא תְּנַן: מִשֶּׁאֵינוֹ נָקוּב עַל הַנָּקוּב – תְּרוּמָה, וְיַחְזוֹר וְיִתְרוֹם! הָא אוֹקֵימְנָא: בִּתְרֵי מָאנֵי צָאֵית, בְּחַד מָנָא לָא צָאֵית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara makes a suggestion based on a comparison to a similar case: **But let it be *teruma*,** i.e., *ḥalla*, **and he should separate *teruma* again. Didn't we learn** a similar idea in a mishna (*Demai* 5:10): If one separated *teruma* **from** produce grown in **a non-perforated** pot **for** the produce of **a perforated** pot, it is ***teruma*** by rabbinic law, **but he must separate *teruma* again** to render the produce grown in the perforated pot ready to eat. The Gemara answers: **We have** already **established** that with regard to things that are **in two** separate **containers,** one **will listen,** but with regard to something that is **in one container, one will not listen.**

The *gemara* continues exploring alternative solutions. It asks: why not tell the *ba'al habayit* that the *kohen* may keep the flour, but that the *ba'al habayit* must separate *challah* a second time? This is modeled on a known case: one who takes *terumah* from hydroponic plants intending it to apply to produce grown in the soil. In that case, the *kohen* keeps the hydroponic produce, and the owner is instructed to separate a second time. So too here, perhaps the *ba'al habayit* can be instructed to separate again. The *gemara* rejects this parallel: in the hydroponic case, the two items are *trei minei* — fundamentally different categories — and for that reason the owner accepts that the first separation did not cover the second type and willingly separates again. But in the case of flour and dough, it is essentially the same substance. There is no reason apparent to the owner why he should have to separate a second time if the *kohen* is keeping the flour. He will not listen, and the desired effect will not be achieved.

### Challenge: Same Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא צָאֵית?! וְהָתְנַן: הַתּוֹרֵם קִישּׁוּת וְנִמְצָא מָרָה, אֲבַטִּיחַ וְנִמְצֵאת סָרוּחַ – תְּרוּמָה, וְיַחֲזוֹר וְיִתְרוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this assumption: **And will** one **not listen** to a ruling to separate *teruma* a second time from a single container? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 3:1): In the case of **one who separated a cucumber** as ***teruma*** to give to a priest, **and** that cucumber **was found** to be so **bitter** that it was inedible, or if he separated **a melon and it was found** to be **spoiled,** his separation is still ***teruma*, but he must separate *teruma* again?**

The *gemara* presses further: is it really true that a person will not listen to a second separation when the same type of object is involved? Is there no precedent for that?

### Watermelon Precedent

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

The *gemara* answers its own challenge from a *mishnah*: one who separated a gourd (*kishut*) as *terumah* and it turned out to be bitter, or a watermelon and it turned out to be sour (*avatiach ve-nimtzet saruach*) — the *kohen* keeps what was given, and the owner must separate again. Here it is all the same category — all watermelons — yet we say the separation was effective and the owner must repeat it. This would seem to show that even from a single type, people do listen to a second separation.

### Distinction: Biblical Va…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תְּרוּמָה מְעַלְּיָא הִיא, מִדְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as it is full-fledged *teruma* by Torah law,** and even if he does not listen and separate *teruma* again, no Torah law will be violated.

The *gemara* distinguishes this case. Even in the watermelon situation, it is not clear that people will actually comply with the instruction to separate again. But it does not matter, because even if they do not listen, the first *terumah* is *min ha-Torah* valid — it is biblically effective *terumah de-Oraita*. Since the sour watermelon is still, at least nominally, somewhat edible, the separation takes effect *bedi'avad*. If they fail to separate again, we are not left with a complete violation, because the first separation was already biblically sound. The flour case is entirely different — there the first separation was not valid at all, and if the owner does not separate again, the dough will be eaten as *tevel*. Therefore, the watermelon precedent does not apply.

### Leviticus Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעַאי: מִנַּיִן לַתּוֹרֵם מִן הָרָעָה עַל הַיָּפָה שֶׁתְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְלֹא תִשְׂאוּ עָלָיו חֵטְא בַּהֲרִימְכֶם אֶת חֶלְבּוֹ" – אִם אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, נְשִׂיאוּת חֵטְא לָמָּה? מִכָּאן לַתּוֹרֵם מִן הָרָעָה עַל הַיָּפָה שֶׁתְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** What is the source from which it is extrapolated that it is full-fledged *teruma* by Torah law? It is **from** a statement **of Rabbi Ilai, for Rabbi Ilai says: From where** is it derived **that one who separates *teruma* from poor-quality** produce **for superior-quality** produce, i.e., in order to fulfill the obligation of separating *teruma* from the high-quality produce, **that his *teruma* is *teruma*? As it is stated** with regard to *teruma*: **"And you shall bear no sin by reason of it, seeing that you have set apart from it the best thereof"** (Numbers 18:32). The verse is saying to give the best part as *teruma*, and one who gives a bad portion has committed a transgression. Nevertheless, the verse indicates that the separated produce is *teruma*; **if it were not sacred** as *teruma* **why** would one **bear a sin?** If one's action were to no effect, he has not sinned. **From here** it is derived **that** if **one separates *teruma* from poor-quality** produce **for superior-quality** produce, **his *teruma* is *teruma*.**

How do we know that separating from the inferior on the superior — *min ha-ra al ha-yafeh* — takes effect *bedi'avad*? Rabbi Ila derives this from the verse: "You shall not bear sin when you take the *chelbo* — the best of it — as *terumah*" (Leviticus). The implication is that if you do not take from the best — that is, if you take from the inferior on the superior — you will have sinned. As the *gemara* reasons, the concept of bearing sin (*nesi'at cheit*) only makes sense if the act was legally effective. If the bad-on-good separation had no legal force whatsoever, there would be nothing to bear sin over: one tried to do something wrong but achieved nothing. The fact that the *pasuk* speaks of *cheit* demonstrates that the act did take hold — it was a valid but sinful *terumah*.

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* raises a significant question here, since there is an entire discussion in *Temurah* about the general principle of *kol milta de-amar Rachmana la ta'avid i avid mahani o lo mahani* — whether an act performed in violation of a Torah prohibition takes legal effect. The cases vary: some prohibited acts do achieve their intended legal effect (*arayot*, for instance, are *chal*), while others do not (*issurei lav* generally are not *chal*). *Tosafot* asks how the *gemara* here can draw the inference it does without accounting for that general debate. He explains that even according to those who hold that a prohibited act may be punishable without taking effect, in this specific case the *gemara* understands from the structure of the *pasuk* itself that the only way the actor incurred a *cheit* is if the *terumah* was in fact *chal*. The generalization is fair, but the broader question is independently debated.

In summary: according to Shmuel, if someone betroths his sister, she gets to keep the money, because in principle parties may retain what was given in an invalid transaction. The *kohen* must return the flour only because specific concerns about legal confusion — on the part of either the *kohen* or the *ba'al habayit* — require it, and none of the proposed intermediate solutions adequately addresses those concerns.

## Back to Mishnah

### Bezo Vezo Vezo Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר לָהּ: "בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וּבָזוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** The mishna **taught** that if she ate the dates one by one their value is not added together **only if he said to her** that she is betrothed: **With this** one, **and with this** one, **and with this** one, which indicates that she is not betrothed until she has received them all.

Rava now returns to the *mishnah* at the top of 47a. The question is: when does the rule apply that if she eats individual dates as they are handed to her, those eaten dates cannot be counted toward the *shaveh perutah*? Rava specifies: this is only where the man said *bezu uvezu uvezu* — "with this, and with this, and with this" — designating each date separately as he gives it.

### Eating: Last Date Only

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

In the *bezu uvezu uvezu* case, if she eats each date as he hands it to her, the problem is that the act of *kiddushin* is not complete until the man finishes his statement. Each date eaten before that final moment has already been consumed — it is no longer present when the act of *kiddushin* is definitively concluded. Those dates are therefore analogous to a *milveh*: they were given, but they are no longer around at the legally significant moment. The only date that counts is the last one — the one still in existence when his statement concludes — and that final date must itself be worth a *perutah*.

### Collective: All Combined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אָמַר לָהּ: "בְּאֵלּוּ" – אֲפִילּוּ אוֹכֶלֶת נָמֵי מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, כִּי קָא אָכְלָה – מִדְּנַפְשַׁהּ קָאָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **he said to her** that he is betrothing her: **With these,** i.e., with all of them together, **even** if she **is eating** them one by one she is **also betrothed** if their combined value is one *peruta*. The reason is that **when she eats, she eats of her own** food. Once she has accepted the dates as money for betrothal, they become her own property and she is betrothed.

The case is different, says Rava, when the man says *ba'elu* — "marry me with these" — using a collective formulation while indicating the entire group of dates in his hand. He then hands them to her one by one and she eats each as it is given. Even so, she is *mekudeshes*. Why? Because the moment he completed the statement *ba'elu*, the entire group was already designated as *kesef kiddushin*. The act of *kiddushin* was definitionally complete at that moment. Everything she subsequently receives, she receives as her own property — *ki ka'achla midida ka'achla*, when she eats it, she eats what is already hers. There is no *milveh* problem, because there is no longer any ambiguity about whether these dates are *kesef kiddushin*. They were so designated before she received the first one.

This is the precise significance of the *chiddush*: in the *bezu uvezu uvezu* scenario, where each date is described individually and the act of *kiddushin* is not complete until the end, the earlier dates are consumed before the transaction is finalized and cannot be counted collectively. In the *ba'elu* scenario, all dates are declared as a unit at the outset, and even if each one is worth only a fifth of a *perutah*, they combine to satisfy the requirement — because they were all labeled as *kesef kiddushin* before any was given.

### Baraita: Ba'elu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּאַלּוֹן בְּרִמּוֹן וּבֶאֱגוֹז" אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לָהּ: "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּאֵלּוּ", אִם יֵשׁ בְּכוּלָּן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. "בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וּבָזוֹ", אִם יֵשׁ בְּכוּלָּם שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. "בָּזוֹ" – נְטָלַתּוּ וַאֲכָלַתּוּ, "בָּזוֹ" – נְטָלַתּוּ וַאֲכָלַתּוּ, וְעוֹד "בָּזוֹ" וְעוֹד "בָּזוֹ" – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava:** If one said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with** the fruit of **an oak tree,** i.e., an acorn, **with a pomegranate, and with a nut, or if he said to her: Be betrothed to me with these, if combined they are worth one *peruta*, she is betrothed. But if not, she is not betrothed.** If he said to her: Be betrothed to me **with this** one, **and with this** one, **and with this** one, **if combined they are worth one *peruta*, she is betrothed. But if not, she is not betrothed.** If he said to her: Be betrothed to me **with this** one, and **she took it and ate it; with this** one, and **she took it and ate it; and also with this** one **and also with this** one, **she is not betrothed unless one of them is worth one *peruta*.** This concludes the *baraita*.

The *gemara* notes that a *baraita* supports Rava's position. The *baraita* reads: if a man says to a woman, "marry me with an acorn, a pomegranate, and a nut" (*hitkadshi li be'alon be'rimon u've'egoz*) — or if he says *ba'elu*, "with all of these" — and collectively they are worth a *perutah*, she is *mekudeshes*; if not, she is not. Then the *baraita* continues to the case of *bezo uvezu uvezu* with eating: if she took each and ate it as it was given, she is *mekudeshes* only if one of them is worth a *perutah*.

### Question: Ba'elu?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי "בְּאַלּוֹן בְּרִמּוֹן בֶּאֱגוֹז", הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ: "אוֹ בְּאַלּוֹן, אוֹ בְּרִמּוֹן, אוֹ בֶּאֱגוֹז", אִם יֵשׁ בְּכוּלָּן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת? וְהָא "אוֹ" קָאָמַר! וְאֶלָּא "בְּאַלּוֹן וּבְרִמּוֹן וּבֶאֱגוֹז" – הַיְינוּ "בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וּבָזוֹ"! אֶלָּא לָאו דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: "בְּאֵלּוּ", הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לָהּ "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּאֵלּוּ" – מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָאו בְּ"אֵלּוּ" עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to prove Rava's ruling from the *baraita*. **What are the circumstances** in **this** first case, where he said: **With** the fruit of **an oak tree, with a pomegranate, with a nut? If we say that he said to her: Either with** the fruit of **an oak tree, or with a pomegranate, or with a nut,** why is the *halakha* that **if combined they are worth one *peruta* she is betrothed? But didn't he say: Or,** which indicates that she is betrothed with only one of them, and that one item should need to be worth one *perutah*? **But rather,** if we say that he said to her: **With** the fruit of **an oak tree, and with a pomegranate, and with a nut** together, **that is** the same as saying: **With this** one, **and with this** one, **and with this** one. That *halakha* is stated in the subsequent clause of the *baraita*, and would be redundant. **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where he said to her:** Be betrothed to me **with these? But from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches: Or if he said to her: Be betrothed to me with these, it may be inferred that** in **the first clause we are not dealing with** a situation where he said: **These.**

The *gemara* asks: in the first case of the *baraita* — the acorn, pomegranate, and nut — what exactly is the case? If the man said "either with the acorn, or with the pomegranate, or with the nut" (*o be'alon o be'rimon o be'egoz*), how could it suffice for them to be collectively worth a *perutah*? He spoke in the disjunctive — he named one at a time. But if we read it as simply listing them together (*be'alon be'rimon u've'egoz*), then that is identical to *bezu bezu bezu* — individual labeling — not a *ba'elu* formulation. So in what sense is the opening case of the *baraita* a *ba'elu* case?

### First Case: No Distincti…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּירוּשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ: "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּאַלּוֹן בְּרִמּוֹן בֶּאֱגוֹז", כֵּיצַד? כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר לַהּ "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּאֵלּוּ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with the explication of the *baraita*: Rather, it is to be understood as an initial, general statement, which the *tanna* **is** then **explaining,** as follows: If one says to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with** the fruit of **an oak tree, with a pomegranate, with a nut;** the *baraita* pauses and explains the circumstances of the case: **How so? For example, when he** gave her these three items and **said to her: Be betrothed to me with these.**

The *gemara* resolves this: the first clause of the *baraita* (*be'alon be'rimon u've'egoz*) is simply an example of how *ba'elu* works — it is explaining the *ba'elu* case by specifying what kind of items might be included. The formulation is *perusha kampfaresh* — an explanatory gloss, not a distinct case. Understood this way, the *baraita*'s structure becomes clear: first it presents the *ba'elu* scenario and rules that collective worth suffices; then it presents the *bezu* scenario with eating and rules that one individual date must be worth a *perutah*. The eating clause appears only in connection with the *bezu bezu bezu* case — not the *ba'elu* case — which is precisely what Rava argued.

### Rav Ami vs. Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ רֵישָׁא, לָא קָא מְפַלֵּיג בֵּין אוֹכֶלֶת לְמַנַּחַת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כֹּל הֵיכָא דַּאֲמַר לַהּ "בְּאֵלּוּ" כִּי קָא אָכְלָה – מִנַּפְשַׁהּ קָא אָכְלָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **While** in contrast to this, **in the first clause** of the *baraita* the *tanna* **does not distinguish between** a woman who **is eating** the items **and one who is placing** them in her possession. In either case, she is betrothed. You can **conclude from** the *baraita* that **any** case **where he said to her: With these,** if they were worth one *peruta* combined, then **when she is eating** them **she is eating of her own** food. It is not considered a loan. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that Rava's statement is correct.

One might ask: why was it necessary to attribute this *baraita* to Rava's position at all, given that the same point was made yesterday by Rav Ami? To understand this, one must recall the prior discussion. The *mishnah* presented two main structures: first, where each *kiddushin* was a separate statement — *hitkadshi li bezo, hitkadshi li bezo, hitkadshi li bezo* — and second, where the man said *bezu uvezu uvezu*, treating them as a group. The *mishnah* then added the case of *ochelet* — eating — and the question was: to which of the two structures does the eating clause belong?

Rav Ami said that *ochelet* modifies the *bezu uvezu uvezu* case. When she eats each date as it is named individually in one continuing statement, those eaten dates become *milveh*-like, and only the last one counts. This is what the *baraita* makes explicit: *bezo natla ve'achaltu, bezo natla ve'achaltu* — she took and ate, she took and ate — and the conclusion is that one of them must be a *shaveh perutah*.

Rav and Shmuel, by contrast, held that *ochelet* modifies the first structure — the case of separate *hitkadshi li* statements. On their reading, each *hitkadshi li bezo* is already a complete and independent act of *kiddushin*, and the eating clause teaches only the *chiddush* that even if she derives immediate benefit by eating the date, the *kiddushin* still requires a *shaveh perutah*. But this is no real *chiddush*: of course if any one of the separate acts is worth a *perutah* it works. And indeed the *baraita*'s language — explicitly describing eating date by date within a single continuing sequence — clearly tracks Rav Ami's reading, not Rav and Shmuel's.

### Baraita: Rabbi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי הִיא, דַּאֲמַר: לָא שְׁנָא "כְּזַיִת כְּזַיִת", וְלָא שְׁנָא "כְּזַיִת וּכְזַיִת", פְּרָטָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *halakha* of the *baraita* taught? **It is** that of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who says** with regard to the laws of *piggul*, which is an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume an olive-bulk of it after its appointed time or outside the boundaries of where it must be consumed: There is **no difference** whether he says that he intends to consume: **An olive-bulk** after its time, **an olive-bulk** outside its boundaries, i.e., without employing the word: And; **or** whether he said that he intends to consume **an olive-bulk** after its time **and an olive-bulk** outside its boundaries, i.e., employing the word: And. Both expressions **are** considered **individual** statements, since he is treating each statement separately, and in neither case does he intend to combine the two amounts. According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, when one says: With this and with this, each statement is distinct. Consequently, although she derives immediate benefit by eating the dates she receives, she is betrothed only if one of them is worth one *perutah* on its own.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by attributing the *baraita* to the position of Rebbi. Rebbi holds — in a parallel *sugya* concerning *pigul* intentions (*kol ke-zayit ke-zayit u-ke-zayit u-k-zayit trata ha-vei*) — that even when the conjunctive *vav* is used to link items, each is still treated as a separate act. It does not matter whether one says *ke-zayit ke-zayit* or *ke-zayit u-ke-zayit*; each remains independent. Analogously, according to Rebbi, even *bezu uvezu uvezu*, despite the connecting *vav*, is treated not as a collective but as a series of separate acts of *kiddushin*.

This means that the eating clause in the *baraita*, though textually attached to the *bezu uvezu uvezu* sequence, is functioning according to Rebbi's framework where each *bezo* is a separate act — like the *hitkadshi li bezo, hitkadshi li bezo* case of Rav and Shmuel. The *chiddush* is then: not that one must wait for the last date, but that as long as any one of them was worth a *perutah* when accepted, that act of *kiddushin* is valid. The *baraita* accordingly does not require specifically the *last* date to be a *perutah*, but *one of them* — because each acceptance was its own independent transaction. In this way, the *baraita* can be reconciled with Rav and Shmuel's reading after all, provided we understand it as authored according to Rebbi's view.

## Mekadesh B'Milveh

### Basic Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוֶה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, מִלְוָה לְהוֹצָאָה נִיתְּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says:** With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed,** since **a loan is given to be spent.** Consequently, from the moment the money is lent it no longer belongs to the lender, and he cannot betroth a woman with it.

The *gemara* now turns to a sustained discussion of a fundamental question: *ha-mekadesh be-milveh* — can a man betroth a woman using a debt she owes him? She owes him a hundred dollars; he says, keep the money, you are *mekudeshes* to me. He has not given her anything new — he has merely remitted an existing obligation. One might argue that the benefit she receives from the forgiveness of the debt (*mechilat ha-chov*) has a real monetary value, and that value could function as *kesef kiddushin*. But that would be a different act entirely — he would need to say explicitly, "marry me with the value of my waiving this debt" (*be-hana'at mechilat milveh*). What he has actually said is, "keep the money you owe me, and with that money you are *mekudeshes* to me." The question is whether that formulation is effective.

It is worth noting a contrast that clarifies the issue: if instead of lending money, he had lent her an object — say, a phone — and then said "keep the phone and be *mekudeshes* to me with it," that would work, because the object remains legally his throughout the loan. Money is entirely different, and that difference is the crux of the upcoming analysis.

### Rav's Position

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

Rav rules: *ha-mekadesh be-milveh einah mekudeshes* — one who betroths a woman with a loan does not effect valid *kiddushin*. The reason is the principle *milveh le-hotza'ah nitnah* — a loan is given to be spent. As soon as the money leaves the lender's hand, it is no longer his. The borrower owns that specific currency outright; all that remains in the lender's domain is an abstract right to repayment.

### Money vs. Object

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

The *chiddush* here is the sharp distinction between money and other objects. When a person lends money — even if the borrower has not yet spent it, even if the bills are still sitting in the borrower's hand — those bills are no longer the lender's property. He cannot demand them back. He cannot give them to someone else. He has nothing concrete left: only an abstract debt owed to him. This is *milveh le-hotza'ah nitnah*: the money is given with the understanding that it will be spent, and legally it transitions to the borrower's ownership immediately upon receipt. By contrast, a borrowed object (*shoel*) remains the lender's property throughout the period of the loan — the lender can demand its return, and the object itself is still his to dispose of. If he says to the woman, "keep the object I lent you and be *mekudeshes* to me," he is genuinely giving her something that was his. With money, he is not.

### Mechirah Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוֶה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר מִלְוָה לְהוֹצָאָה נִיתְּנָה, וּמָר סָבַר מִלְוָה לָאו לְהוֹצָאָה נִיתְּנָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it was taught in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed, and some say she is betrothed. What, is it not** that they **disagree with regard to this:** One **Sage holds** that **a loan is given to be spent, and** one **Sage holds** that **a loan is not given to be spent?**

The *gemara* introduces an apparent contradiction. A *mishnah* dealing with land purchase (*mechirah*) seems to indicate that *kiddushin be-milveh* can work. If Rav's principle is absolute — the loan money is never the lender's to give — then it should fail equally in any transactional context, whether *kiddushin* or sale of land. Why, then, does the *mishnah* in *mechirah* suggest otherwise?

### Special Case: Short Paym…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא?! אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְשָׁוִים בְּמֶכֶר שֶׁזֶּה קָנָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִלְוָה לְהוֹצָאָה נִיתְּנָה, בְּמַאי קָנֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this suggestion: **And** how can **you understand** it that way? **Say the latter clause** of that same *baraita*: **And they agree with regard to** the case of **a sale that he acquires** it. Although they disagree as to whether one can betroth a woman with a loan he has given her, they agree that a lender can purchase an item from the debtor in exchange for forgiving the money he has lent him. **If you say** that **a loan is given to be spent, with what has he acquired** it? There is no money with which to effect an acquisition. No proof can be derived from this *baraita*, which cannot be understood as stated.

Rav Nachman resolves this by introducing a completely different scenario. The *mishnah* in question is not addressing the classic *mekadesh be-milveh* case that Rav ruled on. Rather, says Rav Nachman citing Rav Huna, it deals with a case of *maneh chaser* — a short payment. A man said to a woman, "marry me with a hundred dollars (*maneh*)," she took the money and counted it, and it came up five short — only ninety-five. The missing five is in the nature of a *milveh*: he owes her the remainder. Does the *kiddushin* work?

### Debate: Embarrassment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הוּנָא חַבְרִין מוֹקֵים לַהּ בְּמִילֵּי אוּחְרֵי, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָמַר לַהּ: "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה" וְנִמְצָא מָנֶה חָסֵר דִּינָר. מָר סָבַר: כְּסִיפָא לַהּ מִילְּתָא לְמִיתְבְּעֵיהּ. וּמָר סָבַר: לָא כְּסִיפָא לַהּ מִילְּתָא לְמִיתְבְּעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: Our colleague** Rav **Huna interprets** the *baraita* as referring **to other matters** and not as referring to a straightforward case of a loan. **And with what are we dealing here?** A case **where he said to her: Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars, and it was found** to be **one hundred dinars less** one **dinar,** the missing dinar is considered to be a loan that he has taken from her. One **Sage,** who said the woman is not betrothed, **holds** that **the matter is embarrassing for her,** preventing her **from claiming** the final dinar from him, and since he has failed to fulfill his statement she is not betrothed. **And** one **Sage,** who said that she is betrothed, **holds** that **the matter is not embarrassing for her** and is not preventing her **from claiming** the final dinar. He is therefore considered to have fulfilled his statement and borrowed one dinar from her, which he will repay in due course, but she is nevertheless betrothed.

This is the subject of the *tanna'im*'s debate. One opinion holds that the *kiddushin* is invalid because the woman is *kesifa lah milta le-mitba'ei* — she is embarrassed to demand the remaining five dollars from her betrothed. Because of this social pressure, even if she said yes, she did not fully and genuinely accept the arrangement. Her consent was tainted by the fact that she felt unable to press her legitimate claim.

### Gmar Da'at Principle

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

The other opinion holds that she is not embarrassed to ask, and therefore her acceptance reflects genuine *gmar da'at* — a complete and decisive resolve. The underlying principle is that *kiddushin* requires that the woman truly and fully accept what is being offered. If she is being placed in a position where she cannot freely assert her rights, her apparent consent lacks the necessary decisiveness. If, on the other hand, she genuinely trusts she will receive what is owed and freely agrees, the *kiddushin* is valid despite the shortfall.

This also explains why the case of land purchase (*karka*) works according to both opinions. In a commercial transaction between two parties who are not entering a marriage, neither would hesitate to sue for the outstanding amount. There is no *kesifa* in a business deal; the buyer will unambiguously demand what is owed. Therefore, in that context, *gmar da'at* is unquestionable and the transaction succeeds.

### Threshold Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמָנֶה" וְנָתַן לָהּ דִּינָר – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת וְיַשְׁלִים, לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ? אָמְרִי: מָנֶה חָסֵר דִּינָר כְּסִיפָא לַהּ מִילְּתָא לְמִיתְבְּעֵיהּ, מָנֶה חָסֵר תִּשְׁעִים וָתֵשַׁע לָא כְּסִיפָא לַהּ מִילְּתָא לְמִיתְבְּעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But rather,** with regard to **that which Rabbi Elazar says:** If one said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with one hundred dinars, and he gave her** one **dinar, she is betrothed and he must** later **finish** giving the full sum; **let us say** that **his statement** is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** The Sages **say** to distinguish between the cases: Where he gave her **one hundred dinars less a dinar, the matter is embarrassing for her,** preventing her **from claiming** the final dinar from him, since he has given her almost the entire sum. But if he gave her **one hundred dinars less ninety-nine, the matter is not embarrassing for her** and is not preventing her **from claiming** the rest of the dinars. Therefore, all would agree that she is betrothed in the latter case.

The *gemara* then explores the thresholds of this principle. If the shortfall is enormous — say, ninety-nine dollars short out of a hundred — she will obviously demand the rest, since the discrepancy is so glaring that embarrassment is out of the question. In that case, *gmar da'at* is present and the *kiddushin* may work. If the shortfall is only five dollars out of a hundred, the embarrassment argument applies. These threshold cases help define when *kesifa* operates and when it does not.

### Rav Elazar Case

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

The *gemara* now raises the case stated by Rav Elazar: a man says "marry me with a hundred dollars" (*hitkadshi li be-maneh*) and gives her only one dinar — a small down payment — saying he will complete the payment later. Does this work?

Rav Elazar rules: *harei zu mekudeshes ve-yashlim* — she is *mekudeshes*, and he should complete the payment. The *gemara* asks: does this accord with only one side of the *tanna'im* debate? If giving ninety-five and owing five might not work because of *kesifa*, does giving only one dinar and owing the rest pose the same problem?

The *gemara* answers no: the two situations are inverse in the relevant dimension. When he gave ninety-five and owed five, the shortfall is small and the woman is embarrassed to ask for it — *kesifa* is operative. When he gave only one dinar and owes ninety-nine, the gap is so enormous that there is absolutely no expectation that she would absorb the loss. She will obviously press for the remainder — *lo kesifa lah milta* — and therefore her *gmar da'at* in accepting the *kiddushin* is unimpaired. Rav Elazar's case thus sidesteps the *tanna'im* debate entirely and is acceptable to both opinions.

### Pikadon Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה "הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּפִקָּדוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדֵךְ" וְהָלְכָה וּמְצָאַתּוּ שֶׁנִּגְנַב אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד, אִם נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנּוּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וּבְמִלְוָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנּוּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִלְוָה הֲרֵי הִיא כְּפִקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of Rav from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who says to a woman: Be betrothed to me with the deposit that I have in your possession, and she went and found that** the deposit had been **stolen or that** it had been **lost, if** the **value of one *peruta* of it remains** she is **betrothed** with that amount. **And if not, she is not betrothed,** since there is nothing to effect the betrothal. **But** if he said to her that he is betrothing her **with a loan** that he had given her, **she is betrothed, even though** the **value of one *peruta* of it does not remain. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Meir: A loan is like a deposit.**

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* about *pikadon*: if a man says to a woman, "marry me with the entrusted object (*pikadon*) that I have in your possession" — say, a bag of grain she is watching for him — and she agrees, and then goes to retrieve it and discovers it was stolen or lost. If something worth a *perutah* remains, she is *mekudeshes*; if nothing remains, she is not. By contrast, continues the *baraita*, in the case of a *milveh* — a loan — even if nothing of the original money remains, she is *mekudeshes*.

This *baraita* is striking, because it directly contradicts Rav's position. According to Rav, even with the full amount of the loan still in her possession, *kiddushin be-milveh* does not work — *milveh le-hotza'ah nitnah*. The *baraita*, however, seems to say the *milveh* case works even better than the *pikadon* case.

### Baraita Positions

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

The *baraita* thus presents two views. The first (the *tanna kamma*) says: *pikadon* requires a *perutah* to remain, but *milveh* works even with nothing left. This is the opposite of Rav's position. The second view, that of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in the name of Rabbi Meir, is that *milveh* is like *pikadon*: something of the original money must remain for the *kiddushin* to work.

Both views are, to varying degrees, in tension with Rav. One says *milveh* works completely (even better than *pikadon*); the other says it works if some money remains — still more permissive than Rav, who says it fails even when the entire amount is still in her possession.

### Text Corruption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְתִסְבְּרַאּ הָא מְתָרַצְתָּא? הָא מְשַׁבַּשְׁתָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: And** how can **you understand** it that way? Is **this** *baraita* fully **explainable? It is corrupted** and cannot be cited as a proof.

Rava intervenes with a striking move: *hamishabeshta hi* — the text of this *baraita* is corrupt. He argues that the *baraita* as received cannot be internally coherent. His argument proceeds as follows: what is the *pikadon* case? If she accepted liability for the object (*kibla aleha achrayus*), then if it is lost or stolen she owes the man the money — making her, in effect, a *milveh* debtor. In that case, the *pikadon* scenario where nothing is left should work just as well as the *milveh* case, and the *baraita*'s distinction collapses. If she did not accept liability (*lo kibla aleha achrayus*), then she owes him nothing when the object is lost, and indeed the *kiddushin* can only work if something physical remains. But in that case, why does the *baraita* draw the contrast to *milveh* at all? It should simply distinguish within the *pikadon* case itself: if she accepted liability, it works even without a remainder; if not, it requires a remainder. The fact that the *baraita* instead contrasts *pikadon* with *milveh* in the *sefa* reveals an internal incoherence, demonstrating that the text has been corrupted.

### Corrected Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי פִּקָּדוֹן הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקַבִּיל עֲלַיהּ אַחְרָיוּת, הַיְינוּ מִלְוָה. אִי דְּלָא קַבִּיל עֲלַיהּ אַחְרָיוּת – אִי הָכִי אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וּבְמִלְוָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, נִיפְלוֹג וְנִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁלֹּא קִבְּלָה עָלֶיהָ אַחְרָיוּת, אֲבָל קִבְּלָה עָלֶיהָ אַחְרָיוּת, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! אֶלָּא, תָּרֵיץ הָכִי: וּבְמִלְוָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** He explains why the *baraita* must be corrupted: **What are the circumstances of this deposit** discussed in the *baraita*? **If she assumed** financial **responsibility** to repay the owner **for it** if it is stolen or lost, it **is** the same as **a loan,** as even if it is entirely lost she must still repay its value. **If she did not assume** financial **responsibility for it,** then **if so, rather than teaching in the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **But** if he betroths her **with a loan** that he had given her, **she is betrothed despite** the fact **that** the **value of one *peruta* of it does not remain; let him distinguish and teach** the distinction **within the** case **itself,** as follows: **In what** case **is this statement said,** that she is not betrothed if less than the value of one *peruta* remains from the deposit? **If she did not assume** financial **responsibility upon herself** for it. **But** if **she assumed** financial **responsibility upon herself, even though** the **value of one *peruta* did not remain from it, she is betrothed.** **Rather,** since the wording of the *baraita* cannot remain as is, **answer this** way: **But** if he betroths her **with a loan** that he had given her, **she is not betrothed, despite** the fact **that** the **value of one *peruta* of it remains.**

Rava therefore proposes a corrected reading of the *baraita*: *ela tani hachi* — read it this way instead. In the case of *milveh*, even if something of the original amount remains (*af al pi she-nishtayer heimena shaveh perutah*), she is *einah mekudeshes*. This is the exact reversal of the *baraita*'s original formulation, and it aligns precisely with Rav's ruling: *milveh le-hotza'ah nitnah* — the loan money is entirely the borrower's from the moment it is given, and even if the bills have not yet been spent and are still physically present, the lender has no ownership claim over them and cannot use them to effect *kiddushin*.

### Shimon b. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִלְוָה הֲרֵי הִיא כְּפִקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Meir: A loan is like a deposit.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, citing Rabbi Meir, maintains his counter-position: *milveh harei hu ke-pikadon* — a loan is like an entrusted object. If some of the original money remains, it can function as *kesef kiddushin*. This is a principled disagreement with the *tanna kamma*'s position as corrected by Rava, and by extension with Rav. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that the loan money does not instantly and completely transfer to the borrower; while some of it persists in its original physical form, it retains some connection to the lender's ownership and can therefore serve as a vehicle for *kiddushin*.

## Loan Ownership Debate

### Two Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אָמַר רַבָּה: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן בְּבֵי רַב דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: בְּמִלְוָה בִּרְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים לַחֲזָרָה וְהוּא הַדִּין לְאוּנְסִין קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: מִלְוָה בִּרְשׁוּת לֹוֶה קָיְימָא, וְהוּא הַדִּין לָאוֹנָסִים. וּמָר סָבַר: מִלְוָה בִּרְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים קָיְימָא, וְהוּא הַדִּין לָאוֹנָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to what **principle** **do they disagree? Rabba said: I found the scholars in the study hall of Rav who were sitting and saying: They disagree with regard to** the issue of whether **a loan** the debtor had not yet begun to spend is **in the possession of** the **owner with regard to** the possibility of **retraction** of the loan by the lender. **And the same is true,** i.e., they also disagree, **with regard to** who bears responsibility for **accidents.** **As** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds: A loan stands in the possession of the debtor,** i.e., even if it has not been spent, the lender cannot demand the return of the money. **And the same is true with regard to** responsibility for **accidents,** i.e., if the money is lost, it is considered lost from the debtor's possession and he bears responsibility for it. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, **holds: A loan** that the debtor has not yet begun to spend **stands in the possession of the owner, and the same is true with regard to** responsibility for **accidents.** He can therefore betroth a woman with the money he has already lent her, provided that she has not yet begun to spend it.

The *gemara* now examines the underlying conceptual dispute that drives the *mekadesh be-milveh* debate. There are two views on when exactly a loan transitions out of the lender's ownership. According to the first view — which is Rav's — the transfer is immediate: the moment the money enters the borrower's hand, it belongs to the borrower fully. *Milveh le-hotza'ah nitnah* means not merely that the money is destined to be spent, but that it is already no longer the lender's at the instant of transfer. According to the second view — associated with Shmuel's formulation — the money only becomes the borrower's when he actually begins to spend it (*hotza'ah*). Until that moment, the bills remain, in some meaningful sense, the lender's property.

### Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ: לָאוֹנָסִים – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבִרְשׁוּת לֹוֶה קָיְימִי, מַאי טַעְמָא – לָא גָּרְעָא מִשְּׁאֵלָה, מָה שְׁאֵלָה דְּהָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ חַיָּיב בָּאוֹנָסִים, מִלְוָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. אֶלָּא הָכָא מִלְוָה בִּרְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים לַחֲזָרָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **And I said to them: With regard to accidents, everyone agrees** that it **stands in the possession of the debtor** and he is responsible for the money. **What is the reason?** A loan of money is **no worse than borrowing** an item. **Just as** in the case of **borrowing** an item, **whereby** the item is **returned** to its owner **intact** and yet the debtor **is liable for accidents,** as explicitly stated in the Torah, is it **not all the more so** that with regard to **a loan,** which the debtor spends and repays with other money, it should be considered in the debtor's possession and he should bear responsibility for it? **Rather, here** the practical difference **between them** concerns the question of **a loan in the possession of the owner with regard to** the possibility of **retraction** of the loan. The first *tanna* is of the opinion that he cannot retract the loan, whereas Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that he can do so.

If Shmuel's view is correct — that the lender retains ownership until spending begins — then *mekadesh be-milveh* could potentially work as long as none of the money has yet been spent. The lender would be giving the woman something that is genuinely still his: those specific bills, which have not yet crossed into the borrower's ownership by virtue of being spent. This is what Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's position (*milveh ke-pikadon*) is premised on: since the money may still be the lender's property, it can serve as *kesef kiddushin* just as an object in a *pikadon* relationship can.

### Retraction Rights

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

Rava reports that he found the Rabbis of Rav's academy (*rabbanan de-vei Rav*) sitting and discussing this very point. They offered a different way to frame the debate: *milveh bi-reshut be'alim le-chazarah* — the question is whether the loan, while still in the borrower's possession unspent, remains within the lender's domain for purposes of *retraction* (*chazarah*). Can the lender say, "I changed my mind, give me back my twenty dollars"? One opinion holds that he can — the loan is still *bi-reshut be'alim* before being spent, and just as a lender can recall an unspent loan, he can also use those funds as *kesef kiddushin*. The other opinion holds that the transfer is immediate and irrevocable: from the moment of lending, the money is out of the lender's domain entirely, and he has no right of retraction.

### Sheol Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַשּׁוֹאֵל קוּרְדּוֹם מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – קְנָאוֹ. לֹא בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ. לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But rather,** with regard to **that which Rav Huna says:** In the case of **one who borrows an ax from his friend** for a certain period of time, if **he chops** wood **with it he has acquired it** in the sense that the lender cannot demand its immediate return. If **he has not chopped** wood **with it he has not acquired it. Let us say** that **this *halakha*** that Rav Huna **stated** is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** According to the explanation of Rabba, it would be a dispute between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.

Rava challenges this framing by raising the case of *shoel* — one who borrows an object (not money). A *shoel* is fully liable (*chayav be-onassin*) from the moment he takes possession of the object, even though the object itself remains the lender's property throughout the loan. The *gemara* notes: *lo gara mi-shoel* — the money-borrower should certainly not be less liable than the object-borrower. Just as the *shoel* accepts full liability for loss and *oness* (force majeure) at the moment of taking possession — even though the lender could in principle demand the item's return — so too the money-borrower should be fully liable from the moment he receives the funds, regardless of whether ownership has technically transferred.

### Refined Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּמִלְוָה, דְּלָא הָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ. אֲבָל בִּשְׁאֵלָה, דְּהָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – אִין, לֹא בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, they disagree only with regard to a loan, which is not returned intact.** Since there is no need for the debtor to return the same money to the lender, this means that the money has been transferred to the debtor's domain, and the first *tanna* holds that the lender may not retract the loan. **But with regard to** the **borrowing** of an item, for example the ax, **which is returned intact, everyone agrees** that if **one chopped with it, yes,** he has acquired it and is responsible for it. If **he has not chopped with it, he has not acquired it.**

Rava therefore corrects the Rabbis' framing. The debate cannot be about liability for *onessin* — everyone agrees the borrower is liable for any loss from the moment he receives the money. A *shoel* accepts liability as soon as he takes possession, even before he begins using the item, and the money-borrower is certainly no less liable. The debate, Rava clarifies, must therefore be exclusively about *chazarah* — retraction rights — and its implication for *kiddushin*: can the lender reclaim the specific bills before they are spent, thereby still owning them in a sense that allows him to give them as *kesef kiddushin*?

### Abstract vs. Physical

This section contains no new anchored Talmudic text requiring insertion.

This brings the discussion to its sharpest formulation. At what moment does the concrete, physical ownership of the specific currency dissolve into an abstract debt obligation? Everyone agrees that this transition occurs — certainly before the last coin is spent, and certainly before the obligation is discharged. The question is whether it occurs immediately upon transfer of the bills (*milveh le-hotza'ah nitnah*, Rav's view), or only when the borrower actually begins spending the money (Shmuel's view, and the basis for Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's leniency). Once the money is fully abstracted into a debt — regardless of how much currency remains physically in the borrower's possession — the lender can no longer marry the woman with it, because he has nothing concrete left to give. The entire *sugya* of *mekadesh be-milveh* thus turns on this precise question of when abstract debt replaces physical ownership.
