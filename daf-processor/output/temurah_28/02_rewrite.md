# Temurah 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kol HaAsurin Intro

### New Perek Overview

Today's *daf* is 28, the beginning of a new *perek*: *kol ha'asurin*. This represents a significant shift. At the end of the previous *perek*, we finally arrived at the mechanics of how the act of *temurah* is performed. Now we turn to a very different conversation — what happens when certain forbidden animals become mixed up with other animals. It is not immediately evident how this relates to Masechet Temurah, but it is very relevant to other issues throughout rabbinic literature.

The *perek* opens with the principle: *kol ha'asurin legabei mizbei'ach* — all animals that are forbidden to be brought to the altar. These are categories we will see enumerated momentarily: things that cannot be brought as a *korban*. The scenario the *mishnah* addresses is when one such forbidden animal becomes mixed up with a herd of others. Can any of those animals now be brought as a *korban*, given that any one of them might be the forbidden one?

### Forbidden Animals List

The *mishnah* provides the following list of animals that cannot be brought as a *korban*. First, *ha'rovei'a ve-hanirva* — an animal that participated in an act of bestiality, whether in the active or passive role. Second, *ha'muktzeh* — here the term carries none of its familiar Shabbat meaning; rather it refers to an animal set aside for *avodah zarah*, designated to be worshipped but not yet actually worshipped — and *ha'ne'evad*, an animal that was actually worshipped as an idol. Third, *etnan u-mechir* — a gift given to a prostitute (*etnan*) or an animal exchanged for a dog (*mechir kelev*); both are explicitly excluded from use as a *korban* by the Torah. Fourth, *ha'kilayim* — a crossbreed, since it is neither sheep nor goat. Fifth, *ha'tereifah* — an animal with an internal wound that would render it a *tereifah*. The *gemara* later grapples with how one would know an animal was a *tereifah* in a case where it has become mixed with others; it discusses a scenario involving a particular type of puncture whose *tereifah* status depends on the cause. Sixth, *yotzei dofen* — an animal born by Cesarean section.

### Non-Nullification Rule

All of these cannot be brought as a *korban*. The *mishnah* then states the core ruling: *oserin b'chol she-hu* — even in the smallest amount, a single forbidden animal in a mixture renders the entire mixture forbidden. Even if one animal that cannot be brought as a *korban* becomes mixed into a herd of a hundred perfectly valid animals, none of those animals may be used as a *korban*.

### Mixture Scenarios

There are different possible scenarios that this *mishnah* may be addressing. One scenario is that all the animals are *chullin* — ordinary, non-sanctified animals. In that case, when the forbidden animal becomes mixed in, none of them can subsequently be used as a *korban*. One might ask: if they are all *chullin*, what is the practical concern? The answer depends on the specific forbidden animal involved. For a *tereifah*, since you cannot identify which animal it is, you cannot eat any of them — a practical prohibition on eating in addition to the *korban* restriction. For most of the other cases on the list, however, the prohibition is specifically for the altar; they remain permissible to a layperson for ordinary use or consumption.

A second scenario is that the animals were already *kodshim* — sanctified animals designated as *korbanot* — and a forbidden animal became mixed in among them. In that case the problem is not merely that you cannot bring them; you have a herd of consecrated animals, *chatos* or *shelamim* or whatever they may be, now compromised by the mixture. The resolution in that case is to let them all graze until they develop a blemish, at which point they can be redeemed. The logic of redemption in that scenario — which is discussed in Zevachim — is that you declare: if this is the *korban*, I am redeeming it; if it is the forbidden animal, it simply drops out. The *mishnah* in Zevachim addresses the case where the animals are already *kodshim* when the mixture occurs; our *mishnah* here deals, presumably, with the case where they are still *chullin*.

### Conceptual Basis

What is the underlying reason that the forbidden animals are not subject to *bitul* — nullification? The *mishnah* seems to suggest, along with the parallel *mishnah* in Zevachim, that something distinctive about the *korban* context is what creates the stringency: because these animals are destined for sacred use, we cannot gloss over the presence of a problematic animal even when it is one among many.

But there is another possible conceptual basis that has nothing to do with the *korban* context at all: the fact that they are *ba'alei chayim* — living beings. Inanimate objects, when mixed into a larger group of similar objects, can in some sense lose their distinctiveness and identity; the concept of *bitul* functions on the premise that the forbidden item has been effectively absorbed and no longer stands apart. But living creatures are different. A live animal never loses its identity in a mixture. Even when you cannot locate it, it is still there, still itself, still significant. We will see in the *gemara* how these two conceptual bases — the special stringency of the *korban* context and the irreducible individuality of living things — interact and where each applies.

A further conceptual wrinkle concerns the relationship between the forbidden status of these animals for the altar and their status for lay use. The question arises: are these animals forbidden because they are *asur b'hana'ah* — forbidden in benefit — or are they forbidden specifically and only as *korbanot*? This matters considerably. Take for example *rove'a* and *nirva* — animals involved in bestiality. If there were not two witnesses to the act, or if the case has not yet been adjudicated in *beit din*, the animal has not yet been sentenced to death and is not yet forbidden to a layperson. It is, however, already forbidden for the altar. The *gemara* will address these scenarios explicitly.

Similarly, regarding an animal that was worshipped as *avodah zarah*: there is a principle that only inanimate objects can become defined as idols for the purposes of *issur hana'ah*. The *gemara* elsewhere explores this in striking terms — if someone worships a mountain, the mountain does not become forbidden in benefit; you cannot render the earth prohibited. The same logic extends to living animals: because an animal has independence and is not simply a discrete, complete, static object, it does not become *asur b'hana'ah* even if it was worshipped. It is, however, still an object that was worshipped, and therefore cannot be used as a *korban*. This produces a somewhat ironic result: the animal itself is not forbidden in benefit, yet the inanimate adornments placed upon it — the gold and silver used to decorate it — can become *asur b'hana'ah*, even though the animal they adorn is not. We will return to this in greater detail when the *gemara* addresses it directly.

## Temurah vs Zevachim

### Redundancy Question

The *gemara* immediately raises a question about the *mishnah*'s core ruling — *v'ossrin b'chol she-hu*, that forbidden animals render a mixture forbidden even in the smallest proportion. *Tanina* — we have already taught this in a *mishnah* in Zevachim! That *mishnah* states: *kol hakodashim shenis'arvu b'chatas meisos o b'shor haniskal* — all *korbanot* that became mixed with *chatos meisos* (sin-offerings that must die) or with *shor haniskal* (an ox sentenced to be stoned) — *afillu echad b'ribo* — even one in ten thousand — *yamusu* — they all must die. So the principle of non-nullification in a mixture involving a forbidden animal already appears in Zevachim. Why must it be taught again here?

Before addressing that question, the *gemara* first resolves an apparent difficulty with the Zevachim *mishnah* itself. Its plain reading sounds as though one valid *korban* is getting mixed in among a herd of forbidden things — which would obviously be problematic. The *gemara* clarifies: *hachi katani* — the intended meaning is the reverse. One forbidden animal — a *chatas* that must die, or a *shor haniskal* — becomes mixed into a group of *korbanot*. Even if the problematic animal is one among ten thousand valid sacrifices, *yamusu* — they all must die. With that clarification, both *mishnayot* are teaching what appears to be the same principle: one problematic animal mixed into ten thousand valid ones renders them all forbidden. Why teach it twice?

### Chullin vs Kodashim

The *gemara* initially suggests that perhaps each case introduces a distinctive element. Perhaps one could have distinguished between them: *hasam hu de'assurei hana'ah* — in Zevachim, we are dealing with a *chatas* that must die or a *shor haniskal*, which are things forbidden in benefit even for a layperson. It is obvious that something *asur b'hana'ah* would render a mixture forbidden even in a vast quantity. But here in our *mishnah*, these are animals whose prohibition is specifically for the *korban* — they are not forbidden to a layperson. One might have thought that in such a case, *bitul* would apply. So each *mishnah* teaches a *chiddush* that could not be derived from the other alone.

The *gemara* then notes, however, that even the case of *rove'a v'nirva* appears in Zevachim — the Zevachim *mishnah* continues and says that if animals used in bestiality became mixed into a herd of *korbanot*, they should graze until they develop a blemish, be sold, and a new *korban* purchased with the proceeds of the most valuable animal. So the same *halacha* of non-nullification by *rove'a v'nirva* appears there as well. The *gemara* assumes that in both contexts we are speaking of cases without a *beit din* ruling, so the animals are not yet *asur b'hana'ah*, only forbidden for the altar. The same *halacha*, the same scenario — why repeat it?

Rav Kahana reports that he raised this question before Rav Shimi bar Ashi, who offered the following answer.

### Before/After Sanctification

*Chada b'chullin v'chada b'kodashim* — one *mishnah* deals with *chullin* and the other with *kodashim*. This distinction is evident from context. In Zevachim the animals are explicitly already *korbanot* — they were already consecrated when the forbidden animal became mixed in. In our *mishnah*, the animals are not yet sanctified; they are ordinary animals that one might have used as *korbanot*, and the mixture occurred while they were still *chullin*.

### Need for Both Cases

Why does this distinction necessitate teaching the rule in both places? Because, the *gemara* explains, had we only been taught the case in Zevachim — where the mixture occurred among already-consecrated animals — one might have reasoned as follows: *kodashim* that have been contaminated by contact with something forbidden are deeply compromised; the sanctity of the *korban* and the stigma of the forbidden thing are in direct tension, and the result is that none of them can be used. But by *chullin* — ordinary, non-sanctified animals — before anyone was thinking about *korbanot*, one unknown *yotzei dofen* mixed into a herd, with no altar context and no sanctity yet present — perhaps in that case we would allow *bitul* to operate, and only later when someone actually wants to sanctify one and bring it as a *korban* would the prohibition come into effect. Perhaps the stigma attaches only once sanctification is in view. *Kamashma lan* — our *mishnah* teaches that even then, even in the *chullin* stage, the mixture is already compromised for *korban* purposes and *bitul* does not apply.

Conversely, had we only been taught the *chullin* case in our *mishnah*, we would not have learned the practical remedy that applies when the animals are already *kodshim* — namely, letting them graze until a blemish develops and then redeeming them. The Zevachim *mishnah* teaches that corrective procedure. Each *mishnah* thus contributes something the other does not.

## Two Non-Nullification Ru…

### Issurei Hana'ah Rule

The *gemara* continues and points out that the principle of non-nullification also appears in the context of *chullin* — ordinary, non-*korban* purposes — and specifically in connection with things that are *asur b'hana'ah*, forbidden in benefit. This is a distinct and independent basis for non-nullification that operates in an entirely different domain from the *korban* considerations we have been discussing.

### Avodah Zarah Mishnah

The *gemara* cites the relevant *mishnah* from Avodah Zarah: *v'eilu asurin v'osrin b'chol she-hu* — the following things are forbidden and render a mixture forbidden even in the smallest amount. The list includes: *yayin nesech* — wine used for idolatrous libations (not *stam yeinam*, which is merely the ordinary wine of a non-Jew and is subject to its own separate prohibition; *yayin nesech* specifically is wine actively used in idolatrous service); *avodah zarah* itself — not animals in this context, but inanimate objects of idol worship; *tziporei metzora* — the birds used in the purification ritual of the *metzora*, which are *asur b'hana'ah*; *orot levuvin* — animal skins found with a circular hole cut near the heart, indicating the skin came from an animal slaughtered for idolatrous purposes, in which the internal organs were extracted through that opening in a specific idolatrous rite; *se'ar nazir* — the hair of a *nazir*, which must be burned beneath the pot in which his *shelamim* is cooked and is *asur b'hana'ah*; *peter chamor* — the firstborn of a donkey, which is *asur b'hana'ah* and may not be redeemed; *basar b'chalav* — meat and milk that were actually cooked together. This last item requires clarification: it does not mean that a drop of milk fell into a meat dish, in which case the milk would be *batel* in sixty. Rather, it means that the dish itself has already become *basar b'chalav* — meat and milk were actively cooked together — and then that cooked mixture became mixed with a thousand other dishes that were not cooked with dairy. In that case, the fully-formed *basar b'chalav* is never *batel*, even in ten thousand times its volume. The list continues: *shor haniskal*, the ox sentenced to be stoned; *eglah arufah*, the calf whose neck is broken in the ritual performed when a murderer is unknown; *chullin she-nishchetu b'azarah*, non-sacred animals slaughtered in the Temple courtyard, which are *asur b'hana'ah*; and *sa'ir hamishtaleach*, the goat sent to *Azazel* on Yom Kippur. All of these share the common denominator of being *asur b'hana'ah*, and the ruling is that they are never *batel* even in the largest mixture.

### Two Separate Rules

The *gemara* now states that you need both teachings — our *mishnah* and the Avodah Zarah *mishnah* — because they address fundamentally different categories. *Tzricha* — each one is necessary.

Had we only been taught the Avodah Zarah *mishnah*, we might have concluded: *de'issurei hana'ah lo boteli* — the reason things are never *batel* is specifically because they are *asur b'hana'ah*. That is the operative principle — something so fundamentally forbidden that no one may derive any benefit from it cannot be reduced to insignificance through mixture with permissible things. But by the cases in our *mishnah* — a C-section birth, a crossbreed — these are not *asur b'hana'ah* at all. They are only problematic for the *korban*. Perhaps for such things, *bitul* would apply? *Kamashma lan* — our *mishnah* teaches that even for specifically *korban*-related prohibitions, there is no *bitul*.

Conversely, had we only been taught our *mishnah*, one might have argued: *legavoah* — perhaps this is a uniquely rigorous standard that applies only to sacred things destined for the altar, because the *mizbei'ach* demands perfection in a way that ordinary life does not. Something merely distasteful or marginal that the altar rejects might still be perfectly *batel* for lay purposes. *Kamashma lan* — the Avodah Zarah *mishnah* teaches that even for lay purposes, things that are *asur b'hana'ah* are never *batel*.

The upshot: there are two independent principles of non-nullification. First, anything categorically excluded from use as a *korban* — whether or not it is *asur b'hana'ah* — renders a mixture forbidden for *korban* purposes and is never *batel* in that context, whether the mixture occurred while the animals were still *chullin* or after they were already *kodshim*. Second, anything that is *asur b'hana'ah* is never *batel* even for ordinary lay purposes. These are two separate rules, and each had to be taught independently.

### Live Animals Factor

One should not, however, take this as a complete account of the law of *bitul* in mixtures involving animals. The *gemara* in Zevachim actually develops the discussion further and points out that for most of the cases involving animals in our *mishnah*, even setting aside the *korban* consideration, *bitul* would not apply for an entirely independent reason: *ba'alei chayim* — living creatures — are never *batel*, even for non-*korban* purposes. A live animal never loses its individual identity in a mixture; it is always significant, always present, always itself. This principle may apply even in contexts that have nothing to do with sanctity or sacrifice.

The practical implication is that what may appear here as a ruling distinctive to the *korban* context is in many cases actually a more general principle about living things. The *gemara* in Zevachim notes that for most of these cases, you would not even need the *korban* argument to arrive at the conclusion of non-*bitul*, because the living nature of the animals alone would preclude it. What remains unique to the *korban* context may be the situation after *shechitah*, once the animal is no longer alive. These are the full contours of the question, and the *gemara* elsewhere — in Zevachim and Avodah Zarah — introduces additional factors that qualify and expand upon the picture presented here.

## Bestiality Source

### How Bestiality Forbidden

Having established the rules of non-nullification, the *gemara* now turns to examining the scriptural sources for each of the forbidden categories on the *mishnah*'s list. The first question: *rove'a v'nirva minalan d'psuli legavoah* — how do we know that animals involved in bestiality are forbidden for the altar?

The question must be understood precisely. Once a *beit din* has issued a ruling that the animal is to be stoned, the animal is *asur b'hana'ah* — forbidden in benefit — for everyone, and its exclusion from altar use follows automatically from a general principle that anything a layperson cannot eat or benefit from cannot be brought as a *korban*. The question here concerns the case before any such *beit din* ruling — perhaps because there were not two witnesses, or because the case has not yet been adjudicated. In that situation, the animal is not yet forbidden in benefit, yet our *mishnah* teaches it cannot be used as a *korban*. Where does that exclusion come from?

The *gemara* cites a *braisa* that derives it from the verse: *adam ki yakriv mikem korban la'Hashem, min habehemah min habakar u'min hatzon takrivu et korbanchem* — if you bring a *korban* to God, from the animals, from the cattle or from the sheep, you shall bring your offering. The *gemara* treats multiple elements of this verse as exclusionary: each *min* ("from") signals a subset, not the whole category. *Min habehemah lehotzi rovei'a v'nirva* — "from the animals" serves to exclude animals used in bestiality.

### Kal VaChomer Attempt

But the *gemara* immediately challenges the need for any scriptural derivation: *velo dino* — could this not be derived through logical inference? The proposed *kal vachomer* runs as follows: if an animal with a physical blemish (*baal mum*) — which has had no sin committed through it, which bears only a physical defect and no moral or religious stigma — is nonetheless disqualified from the altar, then surely an animal through which an act of bestiality was committed — a genuine moral and religious failing — should certainly be forbidden. The reasoning is intuitively compelling.

### Counter-Example

The *gemara* resists this, however, by pointing to a counter-example: *choresh b'shor v'chamor yochiach* — consider an animal that was used in the forbidden labor of plowing with an ox and a donkey together (*kilayim* of labor). A sin was committed through that animal; yet it remains permitted for use as a *korban*. This demonstrates that a sin having been committed by means of an animal does not automatically disqualify it from the altar. The *kal vachomer* from *baal mum* is therefore not watertight.

### Refined Kal VaChomer

The *gemara* refines the argument: *mah l'choresh b'shor v'chamor shein chayav misah* — the counter-example of the ox-donkey plowing can be distinguished, because that prohibition does not carry the death penalty. In the case of bestiality, both the human and the animal are subject to the death penalty. The severity of the sin is categorically different, and therefore the animal's disqualification from the altar might plausibly follow even by logic. The *gemara* concedes this point: *tol lach mah sheheiveisa* — fine, the refined argument works. If there are two witnesses and the matter goes to *beit din*, the animal will be put to death anyway and is obviously forbidden. So from that case alone the *kal vachomer* might indeed succeed.

### One Witness Cases

But the concession is limited. *Al pi eid echad o al pi baalim minalan* — what about cases where there is only one witness? Or where the owner himself admits it? Without two witnesses, there is no death penalty on the animal, and without a *beit din* ruling, the animal is not *asur b'hana'ah*. Can the *kal vachomer* extend to this case? Perhaps the prohibition is specifically because of the death penalty that the animal would be subject to, and without that death penalty, perhaps there is no disqualification. That is precisely why the *pasuk* is necessary — to teach that even without a *beit din* ruling, even without two witnesses, the animal cannot be used as a *korban*.

### Rav Shimi Logic

Rav Shimi offers an alternative logical derivation that would not have required a *pasuk*. He constructs the following *kal vachomer*: by a *baal mum*, removing two witnesses cannot make it permissible for eating — a blemished animal is not forbidden in benefit even without testimony — and yet the testimony of even a single witness is sufficient to disqualify it from being brought as a *korban*. By contrast, with *rove'a v'nirva*, the testimony of two witnesses *would* make the animal forbidden in benefit — the full death penalty would apply. Therefore, is it not logically necessary that even the testimony of a single witness should be sufficient to disqualify it from the *korban*? The *a fortiori* logic is: the *korban* disqualification, which is the weaker consequence, should follow even from lesser testimony, given that the graver consequence — *issur hana'ah* — follows from the stronger testimony.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

The *gemara* rejects this reasoning as well. Rav Ashi responds: *meikara dinach pircha* — the *kal vachomer* can be challenged from its very root. The challenge: *mah l'baal mum sheken mumu nikar* — a physical blemish is visible. One might argue that what makes a physical blemish significant enough to trigger *korban* disqualification even from one witness's testimony is precisely that the defect is apparent and manifest. It can be seen; it changes the animal's visible presentation. But bestiality leaves no visible mark on the animal; its past history is entirely invisible and unknown from looking at it. One might therefore argue that specifically aesthetic and visible imperfection is what the altar cares about, not an invisible moral history. And without being able to meet that criterion, the *kal vachomer* from *baal mum* cannot prove the case. That is why *talmud lomar* — the verse teaches explicitly: *min habehemah lehotzi rovei'a v'nirva*. The scriptural exclusion is necessary because the *kal vachomer* is vulnerable to this challenge.

The purpose of this entire exercise is not merely to account for why a *pasuk* was needed. It illuminates where the prohibition of *rove'a v'nirva* fits conceptually: there is a compelling logic that it constitutes a kind of moral-religious blemish on the animal, yet that logic cannot be fully proven independently, because a moral blemish and a physical blemish are qualitatively different — one is visible and one is not — and it is the *pasuk* that bridges that gap.

## Worshipped Animals Source

### Cattle Exclusion

The *gemara* continues to work through the exclusions embedded in the verse. *Min habakar* — "from the cattle" — *lehotzi hane'evad* — serves to exclude an animal that was actually worshipped as *avodah zarah*. Even here, the *gemara* is addressing the case without a formal idolatrous context that would trigger *issur hana'ah* for the animal itself — because, as noted earlier, animals do not become *asur b'hana'ah* through being worshipped. The animal may be eaten by a layperson. Yet it cannot be brought as a *korban*, and the *pasuk* teaches this.

### Why Scripture Needed

The *gemara* asks: why is a *pasuk* needed? Could this not also be derived by *kal vachomer*? The proposed inference: if *etnan u-mechir* — a gift to a prostitute or an animal exchanged for a dog — are forbidden for the altar despite the fact that there is no concept of forbidden adornments attaching to them (the adornments of *etnan* and *mechir* remain permissible even while the animals themselves are forbidden as *korbanot*), then surely an animal that was worshipped, whose adornments are forbidden even for a layperson, should itself certainly be forbidden for the *korban*.

### Reverse Kal VaChomer

But the *gemara* answers: *o chiluf* — without a *pasuk*, one could construct the exact opposite *kal vachomer*. Without any scriptural guidance, one would start from the assumption that a worshipped animal — which does not become *asur b'hana'ah* because it is a living creature — is permissible as a *korban*. From that starting point, one could construct a *kal vachomer* in the opposite direction: *etnan u-mechir*, which are themselves forbidden for the *korban*, nonetheless have permissible adornments. A worshipped animal, which I would have assumed is permissible for the *korban*, should certainly have permissible adornments as well — *kal vachomer* from *etnan u-mechir*. Without a *pasuk* establishing that the animal itself is forbidden, the argument based on adornments could be run entirely backward.

### Object vs Animal Worship

The *gemara* addresses why one would have assumed the animal is permissible: because the prohibition of *issur hana'ah* that applies to *avodah zarah* extends only to inanimate objects — things that are discrete, complete, static objects whose identity can be fully defined and captured by a human act of designation. Living animals, like the earth and things attached to it, have an independence that prevents them from being fully captured by such designation. Therefore, without a *pasuk*, one would simply have said the animal remains *mutar b'hana'ah* and, presumably, permissible for the *korban*.

### Adornments Resolution

At this point the *gemara* challenges the framing: *im ken bitalta lo tachmod kesef v'zahav aleihem v'lakachta lach* — but we already know from the verse "do not covet the gold and silver upon them and take it for yourself" that the adornments of idols are forbidden. This being the case, you could not have run the reverse *kal vachomer* to make the adornments permissible — the *pasuk* locks in their forbidden status. And if the adornments are forbidden, the *kal vachomer* from *etnan u-mechir* runs in the expected direction: something whose adornments are forbidden is itself certainly forbidden as a *korban*.

### Alternate Adornments View

The *gemara* responds: *ani mekayamnu* — I could have reconciled the *pasuk* about adornments differently. *Lo tachmod kesef v'zahav* applies specifically to adornments of inanimate objects — *bedavar she'ein bo ruach chayim* — in which case the object itself is *asur b'hana'ah*, and therefore anything used to adorn it shares that status. But by a living animal — *bedavar sheyesh bo ruach chayim* — since the animal itself is not *asur b'hana'ah* because it is a living creature, one might have argued that the adornment prohibition does not extend to it either: *ho'il v'hen mutarin* — since the animals are permitted, *yachol yihiyu tzipuyav mutarin* — perhaps the adornments would be permissible as well. Without a *pasuk* establishing that the animal itself is forbidden as a *korban*, I would have had no anchor from which to derive the adornments' forbidden status; the adornments prohibition might have been limited to inanimate idols. And from that position, I would have run the *kal vachomer* the opposite way: the animal is permitted, and therefore its adornments are certainly permitted, just as with *etnan u-mechir*.

This is why the *pasuk* — *min habakar lehotzi et hane'evad* — is indispensable. Without it, the question of whether the worshipped animal is permissible as a *korban* would have been genuinely unresolvable by logic alone.

As for the practical *halacha*: both Rashi and Tosafot affirm that the result is indeed somewhat paradoxical. When an animal is worshipped as *avodah zarah*, the animal itself is not *asur b'hana'ah* — it may be eaten by a layperson — yet it cannot be brought as a *korban*. Its inanimate adornments, however, are *asur b'hana'ah*. The animal is recognized as a worshipped object — the proof being that it cannot serve as a *korban* — but because it is a living creature, it does not become forbidden in benefit, a special *halacha* that does not extend to the inanimate objects placed upon it.

## Bestiality vs Goring

### Why Both Needed

Having established several exclusions from the verse *min habehemah min habakar u'min hatzon*, the *gemara* returns to an earlier question. The *mishnah* mentioned both *rove'a v'nirva* and *nogei'ach* — an ox that has gored and killed. Rabbi Shimon raises the question: *im ne'emar rovei'a lamah ne'emar nogei'ach im ne'emar nogei'ach lamah ne'emar rovei'a* — why is it necessary to mention both? In both cases, a sin occurred involving the animal; in both cases, if the matter goes to *beit din*, the animal will be put to death. Why can each not be derived from the other?

### Bestiality Non-Willful

The answer: *lefi sheyesh barovei'a mah she'ein benogei'ach v'yesh benogei'ach mah she'ein barovei'a* — each possesses a distinctive characteristic that the other lacks, such that neither can be derived from the other. What is unique to *rovei'a*? *Sheharovei'a oseh ones k'ratzon* — by *rove'a*, even if the animal participates non-willfully, it is still liable to death. If a person forces himself upon an animal, the animal is not a willing participant — yet the Torah imposes the death penalty regardless. One might have thought this makes the case *lighter* — the animal was, after all, a victim. Yet the opposite is true: the Torah's imposition of death even on the non-willing participant makes this a category of weighty prohibition, and one might therefore have concluded that only this category is excluded from the altar while the goring animal, which requires willful action, is not. Therefore *nogei'ach* must be stated explicitly.

### Goring Liability

And what is unique to *nogei'ach*? *Shenogei'ach meshaleim et hakofer* — when an animal gores and kills after having been designated a *mu'ad* (an established gorer), the owner must pay *kofer*, an atonement payment, reflecting his culpable negligence in the death of another person. This owner liability and the social dimension of harm attached to the goring animal creates a different kind of gravity that is absent in the case of *rove'a v'nirva*, where no one is killed and no financial liability falls on the owner.

### Each Explicitly Needed

Each case therefore has a distinctive feature that might have led one to think it alone warrants exclusion while the other does not. *Hutzrach lomar rovei'a v'hutzrach lomar nogei'ach* — both must be stated explicitly because neither can be inferred from the other.

## Moral Blemish Source

### Corruption Concept

The *gemara* now introduces an alternative *braisa* with an entirely different framework for deriving the forbidden status of these animals. Rather than learning from the exclusionary *min* forms in *adam ki yakriv*, this *tanna* learns from a different verse: *ki moshchasam bahem mum bam* — "for their corruption is in them, their blemish is in them." The *gemara* introduces this as: *chasorei mechasra v'hachi katani* — the text of the *mishnah* is incomplete and should be read as follows: *minayan she-hen asurin* — how do we know that all these animals are forbidden for the altar? *Talmud lomar: ki moshchasam bahem mum bam*.

### Physical Blemish for Redemption

A separate but important *halacha* is introduced alongside this derivation. These animals — *rove'a v'nirva* and the rest of the list — are compared to *kodshim she-kadma mum over le-hekdeshan* — animals sanctified after a transient blemish had already preceded their consecration. Such animals never attain *kedushat ha-guf*, the full bodily sanctity of a valid *korban*; they have only monetary sanctity. The comparison to a transient blemish (rather than a permanent one) is significant: it teaches that *utzrikhei mum kavua lifdos aleihem* — a permanent blemish is required in order to redeem them. Even though these animals are forbidden, their exclusion is not so total as to dispense with the ordinary requirement of a blemish for redemption. If someone consecrated such an animal after the disqualifying event — say, after it had been used in bestiality — it acquires a certain sanctity that requires proper redemption, and that redemption still requires the animal to develop a blemish.

### Sexual Sin and Idolatry

The *gemara* then explains how the house of Rabbi Yishmael applies the verse about *hashchasah* — corruption — to the full range of forbidden animals. The derivation rests on a hermeneutical principle articulated by the *tannaim* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *kol makom she-ne'emar hashchasah eino ela devar ervah v'avodat kokhavim* — whenever the Torah uses the language of *hashchasah* (corruption), it refers either to sexual sin or to idolatry.

### Pesuk Sources

The two sources supporting this principle are: for sexual sin — *ki hishchis kol basar darko al ha-aretz* — "all flesh had corrupted its way upon the earth," which *chazal* understand (likely also from the word *basar*) as referring to sexual immorality, a moral corruption of conduct. For idolatry — *pen tishchisun va-asitem lachem pesel temunat kol samel* — "lest you act corruptly and make for yourselves a carved image in the likeness of any figure." Corruption here means the corruption that consists in abandoning God and turning to idolatry.

### Moral Blemish Concept

What is conceptually striking about this derivation is the framing it implies. By reading *mum bam* — "blemish in them" — through the lens of *hashchasah* — corruption — the verse is understood not as referring to physical defect at all, but to moral-religious failure. Sexual sin (*gillui arayot*) and idolatry (*avodah zarah*) constitute a *mum* — a blemish — that is not visible on the body of the animal but is real and disqualifying nonetheless. It is, in the terminology we might use, a moral blemish, an ethical and religious defect rather than a physical one.

This conceptual framing is striking and important. It echoes the earlier discussion of why a *kal vachomer* from physical blemish could not fully succeed: a moral blemish is genuinely different in character from a physical blemish. Here, the school of Rabbi Yishmael makes that conceptual link explicit — the prohibition of these animals flows from a category of *mum* that is moral and religious in nature, even if it leaves no physical mark. Nevertheless — and this is crucial — the conceptual framing does not alter the operative *halacha*: a physical blemish is still required for redemption. The idea of a moral blemish is a framework for understanding the prohibition, not a replacement for the technical requirements.

## Two Pesukim Reconciliati…

### Corruption Pesuk Use

Having presented two tannaitic approaches — one learning from *min habehemah min habakar u'min hatzon* and one learning from *ki moshchasam bahem mum bam* — the *gemara* now asks: how does each *tanna* deal with the *pasuk* that the other uses? Specifically, if the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives the forbidden status of *rove'a v'nirva* and *ne'evad* from the *hashchasah* verse, what does it do with the *min habehemah* verse?

### Aesthetic Issues

*Mibei lei perat l'choleh u-le-zaken u-le-mezuham* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael uses the *min habehemah* forms to teach a different category of exclusion altogether: animals that are physically offensive or aesthetically unacceptable even without having a technical *mum*. A very old animal about to collapse, a chronically sick animal, or an animal with a foul smell — these are not formally blemished in the halakhic sense, yet they are clearly unsuitable for the altar. The multiple *min* exclusions in the verse are used to derive that these aesthetic categories are also disqualified.

### Different Categories

In the other direction: the *tanna kamma* — who used the *min habehemah* forms for *rove'a v'nirva* and the rest — derives the exclusion of the aesthetically offensive animals from a different set of *min* words. When the Torah continues further in the passage and specifies *min hatzon min hakavasim u'min ha-izzim* — "from the flock, from the sheep and from the goats" — those additional *min* forms provide the scriptural basis for excluding *choleh, zaken, u-mezuham*. And what does the school of Rabbi Yishmael do with those additional *min* words? There the *gemara* applies the principle *dibra Torah kilshon bnei adam* — the Torah sometimes speaks in ordinary human idiom, repeating or elaborating not to add legal content but simply as natural speech. Those words are not treated as carrying additional *derashot*.

The resolution thus is clean: the two tannaitic schools draw on the same pool of verses but divide them differently. The *tanna kamma* uses *min habehemah min habakar u'min hatzon* for moral-religious disqualifications (*rove'a, ne'evad*, etc.) and uses *min hatzon min hakavasim u'min ha-izzim* for aesthetic disqualifications. The school of Rabbi Yishmael uses the *hashchasah* verse for the moral-religious disqualifications and uses *min habehemah min habakar u'min hatzon* for the aesthetic ones, treating the later *min* forms as ordinary speech. Each approach is internally consistent, and each draws on scripture to cover the full range of forbidden categories.
