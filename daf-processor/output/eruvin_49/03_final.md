# Eruvin 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Courtyards

### Mishnah: A-B, B-C

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: רְשׁוּת אַחַת מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת לִשְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת. אֲבָל לָא שְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹת לִרְשׁוּת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This** statement in the mishna, that objects may be carried from either of the outer courtyards into the middle courtyard and also from the middle courtyard into either of the outer courtyards, **is the statement of,** i.e., in accordance with the opinion of, **Rabbi Shimon. But the Rabbis say: One domain serves two domains.** That is to say, it is permitted to carry objects from either of the outer courtyards into the inner one, as no prohibition is imposed upon the outer courtyards, given that both established an *eiruv* with the middle courtyard. **But two domains do not serve one domain,** meaning that it is prohibited to carry objects from the middle courtyard into either of the two outer courtyards.

The daf begins at the bottom of 48b, resuming the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *rabbanan* regarding a scenario involving three courtyards — A, B, and C — arranged in a line, where A and B have made a joint *eruv* and B and C have made a separate joint *eruv*. Rabbi Shimon, as stated in the Mishnah, holds that A and B may carry within their shared zone and B and C may carry within their shared zone. The *gemara* on 48a had explained that the *rabbanan* disagree out of concern that permitting A to carry into B's space will eventually lead to A carrying into C's space — a *gezeirah* based on likely confusion. The *gemara* now returns to that disagreement and explores whether a different, more principled rationale might be at work.

### Rav: One Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לִי:
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda relates: **When I recited** this teaching **before Shmuel, he said to me:**

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: "This is the position of Rabbi Shimon — but the *chachamim* say: *reshut achat m'shameshet l'shtei reshuyot*, one domain may serve two domains, *v'lo shtei reshuyot m'shameshet l'reshut achat*, but two domains may not serve one domain." The formulation is concise and somewhat opaque, which is why the *Tosafot* there is particularly extensive.

### Rashi: B's Contradiction

Rashi explains this principle as follows. If we focus on courtyard B — the middle courtyard that participates in both *eruvin* — the problem arises when B attempts to carry outward in both directions simultaneously: toward A and toward C. That pulling in two opposite directions constitutes a conceptual contradiction. B has an incoherent identity; it cannot belong to two different *reshuyot* at once. Symbolically, we can say that B is the shared *reshut* and can serve A, and it can serve C — but B itself cannot reach into A and simultaneously reach into C. A and C, however, face no such contradiction. From A's perspective, there is a simple A-B relationship with no contradictory pull; the same holds for C. The implication is that A and C may carry into B, but B may not carry outward in either direction.

This is, as the lecturer notes, a fundamentally different rationale from what the *gemara* offered yesterday. Rather than a *gezeirah* — a preventive decree lest A eventually carry into C — this is a principled conceptual objection: B has an inherently contradictory identity when its *eruv* affiliations pull it in two directions at once.

### Tosafot: Gezeirah

Tosafot, however, rejects Rashi's reading and maintains consistency with the *gemara*'s earlier reasoning: the *rabbanan*'s objection is a *gezeirah* lest A's objects find their way through B and ultimately into C. But Tosafot's *gezeirah* runs in precisely the opposite direction from Rashi's permissive ruling. According to Tosafot, B may carry into A and B may carry into C — movements that do not raise the concern of A reaching C. The problematic direction is A carrying into B or C carrying into B, because objects that A deposits in B's space may eventually migrate toward C. The *gezeirah* is thus aimed at preventing A (and C) from entering B's shared zone, rather than preventing B from moving outward.

To clarify: according to Rashi, A and C may enter B but B may not go outward; according to Tosafot, B may go outward toward A or C but A and C may not enter B — exactly the reverse — all for the same underlying concern about A ultimately reaching C, though understood from opposite directions.

At this point, a question arises about the nature of the prohibition: is this an *issur hotza'ah*, an act of carrying in a halachic sense? The answer is that this entire discussion operates within the domain of *reshut hayachid* — private space — and the concern is not a biblical prohibition of carrying but rather the rabbinic institution of *eruv chatzeirot*. Without a valid *eruv*, carrying from one distinct *reshut hayachid* into another constitutes a violation of the rabbinically enacted *eruv chatzeirot* framework, not the *issur hotza'ah* that involves *reshut harabim*.

### Shmuel: All Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שְׁלָשְׁתָּן אֲסוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **This** teaching, that carrying objects from either of the outer courtyards into the middle courtyard is permitted, is **also the statement of,** i.e., in accordance with, the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. But the Rabbis say: All three** courtyards **are prohibited,** that is to say, carrying is prohibited from any of the courtyards to any of the others.

The *gemara* continues: *ki amrisah kamei de-Shmuel* — when Rav Yehuda reported this ruling of Rav to Shmuel, Shmuel responded: *af zu divrei Rabbi Shimon* — even this, the position that one direction is permitted, falls within Rabbi Shimon's view. *Ve-chachamim omrim shloshan asurot* — the *chachamim*, Shmuel insists, forbid all three: A cannot go into B, B cannot go into A, C cannot go into B, B cannot go into C. No movement between any of the three courtyards' shared zones is permitted.

What this means, to be precise, is that Rav's position — permitting movement in at least one direction — is actually Rabbi Shimon's position, not the *rabbanan*'s. The *rabbanan*, according to Shmuel, are absolute in their prohibition. Any carrying across the shared zones is forbidden, regardless of direction. Each courtyard can still carry within its own private space, but the zones created by the joint *eruvin* cannot be used at all.

One might ask how to read the phrase *af zu divrei Rabbi Shimon*. Rashi understands it as a limitation on Rabbi Shimon: you thought Rabbi Shimon permits all directions, but in fact even this narrower permissive reading — only one direction — is already Rabbi Shimon's position. Tosafot reads it differently and more elegantly: the idea that any direction is acceptable is the exclusive province of Rabbi Shimon. The *chachamim*, by contrast, permit nothing. This avoids the difficulty with the Mishnah, which seems to present Rabbi Shimon as broadly permissive in both directions, making it awkward to read Rav's one-direction ruling as a limitation on Rabbi Shimon rather than as the *rabbanan*'s position.

The *gemara* also notes: one might ask, given that all three are forbidden, why were these *eruvin* made at all? The answer is simply that each courtyard remains entitled to carry within its own zone — A within A, B within B, C within C. The problem arises only in the shared zones created by the joint *eruvin*. The point is that what seemed like an expansion of permitted space has, according to the *chachamim*, produced no permitted shared zone whatsoever.

### Baraita: Shared Zone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אַלִּיבָּא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה לְשָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ וּפְתוּחוֹת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, עֵירְבוּ שְׁתַּיִם עִם הָאֶמְצָעִית — זוֹ מְבִיאָה מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ וְאוֹכֶלֶת, וְזוֹ מְבִיאָה מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ וְאוֹכֶלֶת. זוֹ מַחְזֶרֶת מוֹתָרָהּ לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ, וְזוֹ מַחְזֶרֶת מוֹתָרָהּ לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שְׁלָשְׁתָּן אֲסוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel. Rabbi Shimon said: To what is this comparable?** It is comparable **to three courtyards that open into one another, and that** also **open into a public domain. If the two** outer courtyards **established an *eiruv* with the middle** one, a resident of one of the outer courtyards **may bring** food **from a house** in that courtyard **and eat** it in the middle courtyard, **and** likewise a resident of the other courtyard **may bring** food **from a house** in that courtyard **and eat** it in the middle courtyard. And similarly, **this** resident **may bring leftovers** from the house where he ate **back into** the **house** in that courtyard, **and that resident may bring leftovers** from the house where he ate **back into** the **house** in this courtyard. **However, the Rabbis say: All three** courtyards **are prohibited.**

The *gemara* confirms this with a *baraita*: *tani kavatei d'Rav Yehuda aliba d'Shmuel*. Rabbi Shimon himself speaks in this *baraita*, drawing an analogy to the three courtyards. He describes the case of three courtyards open to one another and to a public street, where A and B have joined and B and C have joined. A person from A may bring food from their house into B's zone and eat; a person from C may do the same. And the leftover food may be returned home. The *baraita* then states: *aval chachamim omrim shloshan asurot* — but the *chachamim* say: all three are forbidden. This confirms Shmuel's reading with complete clarity: the *chachamim* forbid any use of the shared zones in any direction.

## Courtyard/Streets

### Two Streets Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא שְׁמוּאֵל לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָצֵר שֶׁבֵּין שְׁנֵי מְבוֹאוֹת, עֵירְבָה עִם שְׁנֵיהֶם — אֲסוּרָה עִם שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **Shmuel follows his** line of **reasoning** that he used elsewhere, as **Shmuel said:** With regard to **a courtyard that is between two alleyways,** if that courtyard **established an *eiruv* with both** alleyways, **it is prohibited with both of them.** Since the residents of the two alleyways are prohibited to carry from one to the other and the *eiruv* enables the residents of the two alleyways to carry in the courtyard, it is prohibited to carry from the courtyard into the alleyways, so that the residents of the alleyways do not transfer objects from one alleyway to the other via the courtyard.

The *gemara* now transitions — *v'azda Shmuel l'taimei*, Shmuel follows his own reasoning — to a new case, which at first seems to introduce additional complexity but ultimately becomes somewhat clearer conceptually. The case: a courtyard (*chatzer*) opens onto two *mavo'ot* — two alleyways or streets — one on each side. These streets have other courtyards opening into them, and those collections of courtyards ordinarily need to make a *shituf mavo'ot* in order to share the alleyway. The middle courtyard opens onto both streets and must be considered in relation to each.

### Eruv Both/Neither

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא עֵירְבָה עִם שְׁנֵיהֶם — אוֹסֶרֶת עַל שְׁנֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If the courtyard **did not establish an *eiruv* with either** alleyway, **it prohibits** one to carry in **both of them.** Since the residents of the courtyard were accustomed to utilizing both alleyways and did not establish an *eiruv* with either alleyway, the result is that each alleyway has a courtyard that did not establish an *eiruv*, which prohibits carrying from the courtyard into either alleyway.

The *gemara* states: *erva im shneihem asura im shneihem* — if the middle courtyard made an *eruv* with both streets (analogous to the A-B and B-C case), it is forbidden to use either street's shared zone. This follows directly from Shmuel's ruling of *shloshan asurot*: by affiliating with both streets simultaneously, the middle courtyard creates the same three-way tension, and no shared zone can function.

The second clause: *lo eirva im shneihem oseret al shneihem* — if it made no *eruv* at all with either street, it nonetheless prevents both streets from functioning properly. Why? Because the middle courtyard is physically present in that space. The principle of *shituf mavo'ot* requires that all courtyards opening into a given alleyway must participate together. The middle courtyard, by virtue of its location, is considered part of the group of courtyards belonging to whichever street it is primarily associated with. If it did not join in the *eruv* of that street's courtyards, the entire street's *eruv* is invalidated — just as in a single courtyard, if even one household fails to participate, the *eruv chatzeirot* of the entire courtyard is void.

### Primary Street Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה בְּאֶחָד רְגִילָה, וּבְאֶחָד אֵינָהּ רְגִילָה, זֶה שֶׁרְגִילָה בּוֹ — אָסוּר, וְזֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְגִילָה בּוֹ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If, however, the residents of the courtyard were **accustomed to utilizing** only **one** alleyway, while **they** are **not accustomed to utilizing one** alleyway, then with regard to the alleyway **which** they are **accustomed to utilizing,** it **is prohibited** to carry there, as the residents of the courtyard did not establish an *eiruv* with it. **But** with regard to the alleyway **which** they are **not accustomed to utilizing,** it **is permitted** to carry there, as the residents of the courtyard are not considered residents of that alleyway.

How do we determine which street the middle courtyard is primarily associated with? The *gemara* provides a straightforward initial criterion: *hayta b'achat regila u-v'achat eina regila* — if the residents habitually use one street and not the other, then that street of habitual use (*regila*) is the one the courtyard is considered part of. *Ze she-regila bo asur* — that street is forbidden if the middle courtyard was not included in its *eruv*. *Ve-ze she-eina regila bo mutar* — but the other street, which the courtyard does not habitually use, is not considered the courtyard's natural affiliation, and so the courtyards of that second street may make their *eruv* without it.

### Redefine via Eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: עֵירְבָה עִם שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְגִילָה בּוֹ, הוּתַּר רְגִילָה לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said:** With regard to residents of a courtyard who **established an *eiruv* with** the alleyway **which** they **were not accustomed to utilizing,** the alleyway **which** they **were accustomed to utilizing is permitted** to establish an *eiruv* **on its own** without the courtyard. The residents of the courtyard have demonstrated their intention to use the other alleyway, despite their not being accustomed to doing so.

Rabbah bar Rav Huna then introduces an important qualification: *irva im she-eina regila bo, hutra regila l'atzmo*. The default affiliation is the street of habitual use — but it is not irrevocable. If the middle courtyard proactively chose to make an *eruv* with the courtyards of the street it *does not* habitually use, that act of voluntary affiliation is recognized. The result is that the middle courtyard is now considered part of the *other* group, and the street it normally uses is free to make its own *eruv* without including the middle courtyard at all.

The ability to redefine one's affiliation by actively choosing to make an *eruv* with a different group reflects a broader principle: while physical habit and geography establish a default, deliberate legal action can override that default.

### Exclusion Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אִם עֵירְבָה רְגִילָה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְזֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְגִילָה בּוֹ לֹא עֵירַב, וְהִיא עַצְמָהּ לֹא עֵירְבָה — דּוֹחִין אוֹתָהּ אֵצֶל שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְגִילָה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba bar Rav Huna said** that **Shmuel said: If** the alleyway **which** the residents of the courtyard **were accustomed to utilizing established an *eiruv* on its own** without the courtyard, **while** the alleyway **which** they **were not accustomed to utilizing did not establish an *eiruv*, and** also the courtyard **itself did not establish an *eiruv*** with either alleyway, **we divert** the residents of the courtyard to use the alleyway **which** they are **not accustomed to utilizing.** This is because there is one alleyway in which it is prohibited to carry due to the lack of an *eiruv*, and a second alleyway in which it is permitted to carry; while it is prohibited for the residents of the courtyard to carry. As explained above, were they to utilize the alleyway which they are accustomed to utilizing, the other residents of the alleyway would also be prohibited to carry from their courtyards into the alleyway, despite having established an *eiruv* for their own alleyway. However, if they use the other alleyway, the residents of that alleyway will not lose anything; since they did not establish an *eiruv*, it is prohibited for them to carry in that alleyway regardless.

Rabbah bar Rav Huna then adds, in the name of Shmuel, a further case: *im irva regila le'atzmo ve-zeh sheina regila bo lo eirev ve'atzmo lo eirva* — if the courtyards of the habitually used street made their own *eruv* without including the middle courtyard, and neither those courtyards of the secondary street nor the middle courtyard itself made any *eruv* with the secondary street, we face a situation where both streets have failed to produce a functioning *eruv*. The habitually used street's *eruv* is void because the middle courtyard was excluded; the secondary street's *eruv* was never made at all.

In this situation: *dochin oto eitzel sheina regila bo* — we push the middle courtyard toward the secondary street, attributing it to the group it does not normally use. The logic is that by doing so, we harm nobody. The secondary street anyway has no *eruv* — whether or not the middle courtyard is considered part of it makes no practical difference to those residents. But by attributing the middle courtyard to the secondary street, we remove it from the primary street group, which allows those courtyards' *eruv* to stand.

### Midat Sodom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְגוֹן זֶה כּוֹפִין עַל מִדַּת סְדוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **In a case such as this, one compels** another to refrain from behavior **characteristic of Sodom.** We force a person to waive his legal rights in order to prevent him from acting in a manner characteristic of the wicked city of Sodom. If one denies another use of his possessions, even though he would incur no loss or damage by granting use of his property, his conduct is considered to be characteristic of Sodom. The courts may sometimes compel such a person to waive his legal rights.

The *gemara* closes this section by noting: *uve-khegon zeh kofin al midat Sodom* — in a case like this, we compel a party not to act in the manner of Sodom. *Midat Sodom* is the posture of "mine is mine and yours is yours" — refusing to allow another to benefit from something even when doing so costs the refuser nothing. The residents of the secondary street might object to having the middle courtyard attributed to their group, since they didn't ask for it. But since they suffer no harm whatsoever from this attribution — they cannot use their street in any event — they have no standing to refuse. The principle that one cannot invoke *midat Sodom* to block a costless benefit to another applies here: the middle courtyard is assigned to the secondary street, freeing the primary street's *eruv*.

## Eruv Foundations

### Makpid: No Eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמַּקְפִּיד עַל עֵירוּבוֹ — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. מָה שְׁמוֹ — עֵירוּב שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **one who is particular about his *eiruv*,** i.e., that the other people should not eat of the food he contributed, **his *eiruv* is not a** valid ***eiruv*.** After all, **what is its name? Joining [*eiruv*] is its name,** indicating that it must be jointly owned [*me'urav*] by all the participants in the *eiruv*. If one person does not allow the other participants to eat of it, it does not belong to all of them and cannot be called an *eiruv*.

The *gemara* now pivots — without leaving the context of *eruv chatzeirot* — to a series of fundamental conceptual statements about the nature and requirements of *eruv*. This discussion, though embedded in a *perek* otherwise devoted to *eruv techumin*, serves as a central exposition of the theoretical underpinnings of *eruv chatzeirot*.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: hamakpid al eiruvo ein eruvo eruv* — if a resident who contributed bread to the communal *eruv* declares that he insists his bread not be touched — that it remain exactly as he deposited it and that no one eat from it — his *eruv* is invalid. By maintaining this insistence on separation, he has undermined the *eruv*'s foundational purpose. And why? *Eiruv shemo* — its name is *eruv*. The legal conclusion is derived, strikingly, from the name of the institution itself.

### Rashi: Mixed/Pleasant

Rashi offers two complementary explanations. First, *eruv* derives from *me'urav* — mixed together, unified — as opposed to kept apart and separate. A person who insists his bread remain untouched is preserving a symbolic separateness that directly contradicts the *eruv*'s purpose. But Rashi adds a second dimension: *eruv* also relates to *areiva*, meaning pleasant or agreeable — *shutfus nocha ve'areiva*, a partnership that is comfortable and pleasant. The *eruv* is meant to create a sense of communal goodwill among neighbors, a feeling that they share their space and belongings with warmth. One who insists on separation not only refuses to be *me'urav* in the practical sense but also undermines the *areiva*, the spirit of neighborly pleasantness that the institution is designed to cultivate. The inference of *halachah* from the name of an institution is itself noteworthy.

### Rabbi Chanina: Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּקְרָא מֵאַנְשֵׁי וַרְדִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** Even in that case, **his *eiruv* is a** valid ***eiruv*, however,** that person **is called** one **of the men of Vardina.** The men of Vardina were renowned misers, meaning that he is considered to be like them.

Rabbi Chanina disagrees: *eruvo eruv* — even if the person insists on separation, the *eruv* is valid. However, *ella she'nikra me'anshei vardina* — he will be called one of the people of Vardina, a place apparently known in the Talmudic world for its miserliness. Rabbi Chanina's position reflects a pragmatic view of community: real communities are imperfect. A grudging, reluctant participation still counts as participation. One need not require an ideal spirit of generosity for the legal act to be valid. This is also, as the *gemara* implicitly notes, thematically continuous with the preceding discussion of *midat Sodom* — a miser may be condemned morally, but the law does not always nullify his acts.

### Cholok: Two Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַחוֹלֵק אֶת עֵירוּבוֹ — אֵינוֹ עֵירוּב. כְּמַאן? כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, דְּתַנְיָא: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁגָּבוּ אֶת עֵירוּבָן וּנְתָנוּהוּ בִּשְׁנֵי כֵלִים, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין זֶה עֵירוּב, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda** also **said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **one who divides his *eiruv*** into two parts, his *eiruv* **is not a** valid ***eiruv*.** This is for the aforementioned reason that, by definition, an *eiruv* needs to be indicative of joining, and this *eiruv* is separated into different parts. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Shmuel state this teaching? Could it be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **five** people who **collected their *eiruv* and placed it in two** separate **utensils, Beit Shammai say: This is not a** valid ***eiruv*, whereas Beit Hillel say: This is an *eiruv*.** It does not stand to reason that Shmuel would follow Beit Shammai, whose opinion is not accepted as normative law.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: ha-cholek et eruvo eino eruv* — beyond the case of insisting no one touch his bread, if a person physically separates his contribution by placing it in a distinct basket while all the other contributions are together, that too invalidates the *eruv*. The *gemara* asks: like whom is this ruling? Like Beit Shammai? For we have a *baraita*: *chamisha she-gav'u et eruvam ve-nasnu bishnei keilim* — five people collected their *eruv* and placed it in two vessels. Beit Shammai says it is not an *eruv*; Beit Hillel says it is.

The *gemara* answers: *afilu teima Beit Hillel* — even according to Beit Hillel, Shmuel's ruling stands. The reason Beit Hillel permitted division into two vessels is specific: *d'malyan lemana ve'aytar* — they tried to place everything in one vessel but it did not fit, and the overflow necessitated a second vessel. Beit Hillel permits this because the division was forced, not chosen. But *heicha d'palgei miflag* — where one person deliberately ensures his bread is kept in its own separate container, that is categorically different: it is an intentional act of self-distinction, and even Beit Hillel would agree it undermines the *eruv*.

### Two Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בֵּית הִלֵּל: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי בֵּית הִלֵּל הָתָם אֶלָּא דְּמָלְיָין לְמָנָא וְאִיַּיתַּר, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּפַלְגֵיהּ מִיפְלָג — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even if you say** that Shmuel stated his opinion in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel, Beit Hillel stated** their opinion **only there, where the** first **utensil was filled and there was** still some food **left over,** and therefore, some of the leftover food had to be placed in a second utensil. **But where they divided it** from the outset, even Beit Hillel agree that the *eiruv* is **not** valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי, לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָתָם — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָפֵיד. אֲבָל הָכָא — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּפַלְגֵיהּ מִיפְלָג, אֲבָל הָתָם — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need two** rulings that are based on the same principle, i.e., that an *eiruv* must demonstrate joining? The Gemara answers: Both rulings were **necessary. As, had** the Gemara **taught us** the ruling only **there,** with regard to one who is particular about his *eiruv*, one might have said that the *eiruv* is not valid **because** the person **is particular** and expressly does not desire that his *eiruv* be eaten by others. **However here,** with regard to one who divides the *eiruv* into different parts, one might **say** that his portion should **not** be considered as separated from the rest. **And had** the Gemara **taught us** the ruling only **here,** with regard to one who divides his *eiruv*, one might have said that the *eiruv* is not valid **because he divided it up,** thereby physically separating himself from the others. **However there,** with regard to one who is particular about his *eiruv*, one might **say** that his portion should **not** be considered as separated from the rest, since no act of separation was performed. Consequently, both rulings were **necessary.**

The *gemara* asks: *ve-tarti lamma li?* Why did Shmuel need to state both cases — the *makpid* who insists on non-touching, and the one who physically separates his bread? The answer is: *tzrichi* — both are needed. Had Shmuel only stated the case of the *makpid*, one might have thought that the invalidity is specifically because the person's explicit insistence creates a psychological separation, whereas physical placement in a separate vessel, without that explicit declaration, might not be problematic — perhaps he is fine with others eating from it, and the physical separation alone is less significant. Conversely, had Shmuel only stated the case of physical separation, one might have thought the problem is the tangible partition, but where everything is physically together and he merely verbally insists, perhaps that verbal insistence alone is insufficient to break the *eruv*. Both cases are necessary to teach that each form of separation — attitudinal and physical — independently invalidates the *eruv*.

### Owner Contribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה בְּבֵי מַעְצַרְתָּא דְּבֵי רַב זַכַּאי: מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל ״הַחוֹלֵק אֶת עֵירוּבוֹ — אֵינוֹ עֵירוּב״? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּיִת שֶׁמַּנִּיחִין בּוֹ עֵירוּב — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִיתֵּן אֶת הַפַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to Rav Yehuda in the olive press in Rav Zakkai's house: Did Shmuel** actually **say** that in the case of **one who divides his *eiruv*, it is not a** valid ***eiruv*? Didn't Shmuel say** elsewhere: **The house in which the *eiruv* is placed need not contribute bread** for the *eiruv*.

*Amar lei Rav Abba le-Rav Yehuda be-vei matzrata* — Rav Abba raised a challenge to Rav Yehuda, Shmuel's transmitter, at the location of a certain olive press. Did Shmuel really say that splitting one's contribution invalidates the *eruv*? But Shmuel himself taught: *bayit she-menichin bo eruv ein tzarich litten et ha-pas* — the house in which the *eruv* is deposited, its owner need not contribute bread to the *eruv*.

The *gemara* initially considers the reason: perhaps it is because we treat the bread stored in the homeowner's bread basket elsewhere in the house as though it were already combined with the *eruv* bread — *keivan de-manach be-sal ke-man de-manach hacha dami*, since it is placed in his vessel it is as if placed right here. If so, we see that physically separated bread — the homeowner's bread in another location — is treated as unified with the *eruv* bread. Why, then, cannot the person insisting on a separate basket claim the same logic?

### All at Eruv Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין פַּת, מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּכוּלְּהוּ הָכָא דָּיְירִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda **said to him: There** Shmuel validates the *eiruv* **although there is no bread** in the house in which the *eiruv* is deposited. And **what is the reason** for his ruling? It is **because** by placing food in a particular house, **all** the residents of the courtyard **are** regarded **as living here.**

Rav Yehuda answers the challenge with a sharper principle. The homeowner need not contribute bread — not because his bread elsewhere in the house is deemed virtually present, but rather: *de-kulhu hacha dayrei* — they are all living here. The act of depositing the *eruv* in Charlie's house transforms that house into the symbolic dwelling place of all the courtyard's residents for *Shabbat*. They are all, in a sense, residing with Charlie. Therefore Charlie need not contribute; his house is his contribution — the locus of everyone's symbolic presence.

This principle is significant and somewhat surprising. One might have expected Shmuel — who so strongly emphasized unification and communal pleasantness — to have promoted the image of a truly equal collective. But this ruling introduces a more hierarchical image: rather than a corporate community of equal partners, the *eruv* makes everyone a guest in the homeowner's residence. The homeowner is set apart not by miserliness but by serving as the host around whom everyone else is gathered.

## Two Eruv Models

### Kinyan Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עֵירוּב מִשּׁוּם קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: An *eiruv*** that is deposited in a house is effective **due to** the principle of **acquisition,** as each person who contributes a portion of food acquires the right to a certain use of the residence and is considered one of its residents.

*Amar Shmuel: eruv mishum kinyan* — Shmuel presents a formal theoretical account of the *eruv*: it functions as an act of acquisition. When residents contribute bread and deposit it in the homeowner's house, they are transacting — giving bread and receiving in exchange a legal share in the homeowner's property. This acquisition is what unifies all participants' domains into a single *reshut*.

This is, as the lecturer notes, a strikingly strange idea, and seems at odds with Shmuel's earlier emphasis on communal warmth and pleasantness.

### No Money: Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תֹּאמַר: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין קוֹנִין בְּמָעָה — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת. הֵיכָא דְּעֵירַב, מִיהוּ לִקְנֵי! גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ מָעָה עִיקָּר, וְזִמְנִין דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ מָעָה, וְלָא אָתֵי לְאִיעָרוֹבֵי בְּפַת, דְּאָתֵי עֵירוּב לְאִיקַלְקוֹלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say: Why** then **can one not acquire** this right **through** payment of a coin such as **a *ma'a*,** but rather only through bread? It is **because** a *ma'a* **is not** always **available on Shabbat eve,** as many people spend all of their available money for the necessities of Shabbat, and it is difficult to find money available at that hour. The Gemara asks: If so, according to Shmuel's opinion, in a case **where he established an *eiruv*** with money, **it should nonetheless acquire,** i.e., be valid. According to his opinion, there is no fundamental reason to invalidate the acquisition of rights in the residence through the payment of money, yet there is no indication that this position is valid. The Gemara answers: Even Shmuel did not permit one to establish an *eiruv* with money, due to **a decree lest** people **say that a *ma'a* is essential, and sometimes a *ma'a* will not be available, and they will not come** to prepare **an *eiruv* with bread, and** the halakhic category of ***eiruv* will be forgotten.**

If the *eruv* is fundamentally a transaction, why must it be conducted with bread? Why not use money — a *ma'ah*, for instance? The *gemara* answers: *mipnei she'eino matzui be'arvei Shabbatot* — because money is typically unavailable on *erev Shabbat*, when people are not handling their finances. Therefore the Sages instituted bread as the medium. But this raises a further question: if someone went ahead and used money *b'diavad*, why would it not work? The *gemara* responds with a *gezeirah*: *shema yomru mah ikar, ve-zimnin de-la shechiach ma'ah, ve-lo asi le-eiruvi be-fas* — lest people conclude that money is the primary vehicle, and when money is unavailable, they will have forgotten about bread and will fail to make the *eruv* at all. The exception would come to swallow the rule. Within the transactional framework, this concern has a certain internal logic: if the *eruv* is primarily about acquisition, monetary transactions are the most natural form, and institutionalizing that could erode the bread-based practice entirely.

### Dirah Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: עֵירוּב מִשּׁוּם דִּירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba** disagreed with Shmuel and **said: An *eiruv*** is effective **due to** the principle of **residence.** Each person who contributes a portion of food is considered as if he resides, for that Shabbat, in the residence in which the food is deposited.

*Rabbah amar: eruv mishum dirah* — Rabbah offers a competing account that many find far more intuitive, and which resonates with a point made earlier in the discussion. Just as in *eruv techumin*, where a person designates their *Shabbat* dwelling by placing bread at a specific location — the bread marks the place where one symbolically begins *Shabbat* — so too in *eruv chatzeirot*: the bread placed in the homeowner's house marks that house as the communal dwelling place for *Shabbat*. Everyone is symbolically living there together. The homeowner's exemption from contributing bread flows naturally from this: his house is where they all dwell, so the act of residence is his contribution.

### Three Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: כְּלִי, וּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְקָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical, halakhic difference **between these** two understandings? The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the question of whether an *eiruv* may be established with **a utensil.** If an *eiruv* is effective based on the principle of acquisition, in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel, then one should be able to establish an *eiruv* with a utensil; whereas, this would not constitute a valid *eiruv*, according to the opinion of Rabba. **And** another practical difference between them is with regard to whether an *eiruv* may be established with food that is **less than the value of a *peruta*.** According to Shmuel's opinion, this would not be a valid *eiruv*, as there is no acquisition with something less than the value of a *peruta*; whereas according to Rabba's opinion, since an *eiruv* is effective by establishing a person's residence, this can be done even with an amount of food worth less than a *peruta*. **And** there is another practical difference between them with regard to the question whether **a minor** may collect the *eiruv* from the residents of the courtyard and deposit it in one of the houses. According to Shmuel's opinion, this would not be a valid *eiruv*, for a minor cannot serve as an agent to effect acquisition, whereas according to Rabba's opinion, the *eiruv* is valid, as the food itself establishes the common residence for all the residents.

The *gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu?* — what is the practical difference between these two models? *Icha beinaihu kli u-fachos mi-shaveh perutah ve-katan* — three cases:

First, *kli* — a vessel. According to the *kinyan* model, an *eruv* could in principle be made by contributing any item of value — not just bread but even a garment — since what matters is the transactional exchange. According to the *dirah* model, only food, which symbolizes dwelling and eating, would qualify; a garment would not represent shared residence.

Second, *fachos mi-shaveh perutah* — bread worth less than a *perutah*. According to the *kinyan* model, a transaction requires something of monetary value; bread worth less than a *perutah* cannot constitute a valid acquisition. According to the *dirah* model, the bread symbolizes a meal and a dwelling-place, not a financial exchange, and its monetary value is irrelevant.

Third, *katan* — a minor. A minor cannot be a party to a legal transaction, so according to the *kinyan* model a minor's contribution is invalid — and, as Rashi understands it, a minor cannot even serve as the agent who deposits the bread. According to Tosafot, under the *kinyan* model a minor could not serve as the homeowner (he cannot transfer ownership rights to others) but might still contribute his own bread. Under the *dirah* model, the symbolic presence of a minor in the dwelling place is unproblematic.

The *kinyan* model is ultimately difficult to reconcile with the broader texture of Shmuel's statements, which consistently emphasize unification, communal pleasantness, and the spirit of shared dwelling rather than transactional exchange.

### Halacha: Rabbah

Tosafot, in the passage beginning *ve-katan*, states explicitly: *ve-nire de-halachah ke-Rabbah de-amar eruv mishum dirah* — the ruling follows Rabbah, who holds that *eruv* functions on the basis of *dirah*, shared dwelling. This is supported by citations from other *gemarot* that speak of the *eruv* operating with items worth less than a *perutah* and with other details consistent only with the *dirah* model.

### One Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה: לְדִידָךְ קַשְׁיָא וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל קַשְׁיָא. הָא תַּנְיָא: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁגָּבוּ אֶת עֵירוּבָן, כְּשֶׁהֵם מוֹלִיכִין אֶת עֵירוּבָן לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר — אֶחָד מוֹלִיךְ לְכוּלָּן. הוּא נִיהוּ דְּקָא קָנֵי, וְתוּ לָא. הוּא נִיהוּ דְּקָא דָּיַיר, וְתוּ לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rabba: It is difficult according to your** opinion that an *eiruv* is effective based on the principle of residence, **and it is difficult according to** the opinion **of Shmuel** that it is effective based on the principle of acquisition. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **five** people **who collected their *eiruv*, when they take their *eiruv* elsewhere,** in order to establish an *eiruv* together with another courtyard, **one** person may **take it** there **for all of them.** This indicates that **it is** only **that person who acquires** rights, **and nobody else,** and **it is** only **that person who gains residence, and nobody else.** In that case, how can the others rely on this *eiruv*?

Abaye raises a challenge to both models: *ha tanya — chamishah she-gav'u et eruvam, keshe-molichin et eruvam le-makom acher, echad molich le-kulan* — when five people from one courtyard wish to join the *eruv* of a neighboring courtyard, a single representative may bring all five contributions. How does this work on either model? Under the *kinyan* model, only that one representative acquires a share in the neighboring courtyard's property — the other four do not. Under the *dirah* model, only the representative symbolically takes up residence in that courtyard — not the others. Either way, the other four seem excluded.

### Shlichut Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא לְדִידִי קַשְׁיָא, וְלָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל קַשְׁיָא — שְׁלִיחוּת דְּכוּלְּהוּ קָא עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba said **to him:** It is **neither difficult according to my** opinion, **nor** is it **difficult according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** as, the person who takes the *eiruv* **acts as an agent,** effecting acquisition or determining residence **on behalf of all of them.**

The answer is straightforward: *lo le-didi kashya ve-lo le-Shmuel kashya — shlichut de-kulhu ka-avid* — it is not a difficulty for Rabbah's model or for Shmuel's model. When Reuven walks over to the neighboring courtyard on behalf of his courtyard, he acts as their *shaliach* — their agent. The principle of *shlichut* applies regardless of which underlying model governs the *eruv*: whether the act is an acquisition or a symbolic act of dwelling, an agent can perform it on behalf of all. This is an ordinary application of *dinei shlichut* and presents no theoretical difficulty.

### Rabbi Shimon Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the case of the three courtyards addressed above, **Rabba said** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** that it is permitted to carry from the middle courtyard into either of the two outer ones; and vice versa, however, it is prohibited to carry from one outer courtyard to the other.

*Amar Rabbah amar Rav Chama bar Guria amar Rav: halachah ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the ruling follows Rabbi Shimon in the three-courtyard case. Despite all the discussion of the *chachamim*'s position and Shmuel's stricter reading, the practical *halachah* is that A and B may carry among themselves and B and C may carry among themselves — carrying may not cross from A to C, but the intermediate shared zones function as Rabbi Shimon permits.

## Journey/Nightfall

### Traveling Home

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁבָּא בְּדֶרֶךְ וְחָשְׁכָה לוֹ, וְהָיָה מַכִּיר אִילָן אוֹ גָדֵר, וְאָמַר: ״שְׁבִיתָתִי תַּחְתָּיו״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was coming along the way** on Shabbat eve, **and it grew dark** while **he** was traveling, **and he was familiar** with **a tree or a fence** located two thousand cubits from his current location, and two thousand cubits from his house, **and he said: My residence is beneath** that tree, rather than in his present location, **he has not said anything,** as he did not establish a fixed location as his residence.

The *gemara* transitions back to the primary subject of the *perek* — *eruv techumin* — with a new Mishnah. *Mi she-ba ba-derech ve-chashkah lo* — a person who is traveling and nightfall overtakes him while still on the road, close to home but not yet there, with roughly four thousand *amot* remaining. He would like to be able to walk home after *Shabbat* begins, but doing so would require extending his *techum* beyond the standard two thousand *amot* from wherever he begins *Shabbat*.

### Midpoint Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבִיתָתִי בְּעִיקָּרוֹ״ — מְהַלֵּךְ מִמְּקוֹם רַגְלָיו וְעַד עִיקָּרוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, וּמֵעִיקָּרוֹ וְעַד בֵּיתוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה. נִמְצָא מְהַלֵּךְ מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** If, however, he said: **My residence is at** the tree's **trunk,** he acquired residence there, and he **may** therefore **walk from the place he is standing to the trunk** of the tree **two thousand cubits** away, **and from the trunk** of the tree **to his house,** an additional **two thousand cubits. Consequently, he walks after nightfall** a total of **four thousand cubits.**

If he is aware of a tree or wall located at the midpoint between him and his home — that is, roughly two thousand *amot* away from where he stands and two thousand *amot* from his house — he has an opportunity. *Ve-amar shevitati tachtav* — if he declares, "My *Shabbat* begins beneath that tree," but no more than that — *lo amar klum* — it is of no effect whatsoever. Why? Because he has not been sufficiently specific. The space beneath a tree's canopy is not a single point. To establish a valid *eruv techumin* by verbal designation, one must say *shevitati be-ikaro* — my *Shabbat* begins at the root, meaning the trunk, of the tree. That specificity constitutes a defined location.

Having designated the tree's trunk as his *Shabbat* starting point: *m'haleich mi-makom raglav ad ikaro alpayim amah* — he may walk the two thousand *amot* from where he currently stands to the trunk of the tree. *U-mi-ikaro be-veiso alpayim amah* — and from the trunk he may walk two thousand *amot* to his house. *Nimtza m'haleich mi-chasheicha arba'at alafim amah* — in total, even after *Shabbat* has begun, he can travel four thousand *amot* in a straight line and reach home.

This raises an interesting question: a standard *eruv techumin* requires either that bread be physically deposited at the desired location or that the person be physically present there. Here, neither is the case — the person is two thousand *amot* away and no bread is placed at the tree. The *gemara* implies this is a leniency granted specifically to one caught mid-journey: since he is not at home and cannot place bread, a verbal designation alone is accepted. It is a notable exception to the ordinary requirements.

### Where Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ מַכִּיר, אוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּקִי בַּהֲלָכָה, וְאָמַר ״שְׁבִיתָתִי בִּמְקוֹמִי״ — זָכָה לוֹ מְקוֹמוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **If one is not familiar** with a tree or any other noticeable landmark, **or if he is not an expert in the *halakha*,** unaware that residence can be established from a distance, **and he said: My residence is at my** current **location,** then his presence at **his** current **location acquires for him** the right to walk **two thousand cubits in each direction.**

If the traveler does not know of any suitable tree at the midpoint, or is unfamiliar with these laws and therefore does not know to make such a designation, the Mishnah offers an alternative: *ve-amar shevitati bi-mekomi* — he may declare, "My *Shabbat* begins where I stand." He will not reach home, but he avoids being restricted to the four *amot* that would otherwise apply to someone without any *Shabbat* designation. *Zachah lo mekomo alpayim amah le-chol ruach* — he is granted two thousand *amot* in every direction from where he stands. Tosafot notes that even without any verbal declaration, if a person is awake and present when *Shabbat* begins, the place where he stands is automatically his *Shabbat* starting point, and he has two thousand *amot* from there in every direction.

### Circle/Square

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲגוּלּוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְרוּבָּעוֹת, כְּטַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּהְיֶה נִשְׂכָּר לַזָּוִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The manner in which the two thousand cubits are measured is the subject of a tannaitic dispute. These cubits are measured **circularly,** i.e., as a circle with a radius of two thousand cubits; this is **the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigenos. And the Rabbis say:** These are measured **squarely,** i.e., **as a square tablet,** with each side measuring four thousand cubits, **so that he gains the corners.** He is permitted to walk from the middle to the corners of the square as well, a distance of approximately 2,800 cubits.

The Mishnah records a dispute about how to measure those two thousand *amot*. *Agulot, divrei Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* — they are measured as a circle, with the person at the center and two thousand *amot* as the radius. *Ve-chachamim omrim: meruba'ot* — the *chachamim* say they are measured as a square, *ke-tavla meruba'at, ke-dei she'yehei niskar la-zaviyot* — like a square tablet, so that the person gains the additional space in the corners beyond what the circle would grant. When the *chachamim* permit a square, there is also a further question of orientation: in which direction does one align the square? If it is a circle, orientation is irrelevant; but for a square, the direction of alignment will determine exactly which space falls within the *techum*.

### Poor Person Eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: הֶעָנִי מְעָרֵב בְּרַגְלָיו. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא עָנִי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר, לֹא אָמְרוּ מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל עַל הֶעָשִׁיר, שֶׁלֹּא יֵצֵא וִיעָרֵב בְּרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** the meaning of that **which** the Sages **said: The pauper establishes an *eiruv* with his feet,** i.e., one who does not have the bread required to establish an *eiruv* may walk anywhere within his Shabbat limit and declare: This is my residence, and his Shabbat limit is measured from that location. **Rabbi Meir said: We have** this leniency in effect **only** for **a pauper,** who does not have food for two meals. However, one who has bread may only establish residence with bread. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This leniency is in effect for **both a pauper and a wealthy person.** The Sages **said** that **one establishes an *eiruv* with bread only** in order **to be lenient with the wealthy** person, **so that he** need **not** exert himself and **go out and establish an *eiruv* with his feet.** Instead, he can appoint an agent to place bread for him in that location. This, however, does not negate the option of personally going to that location in order to establish residence without bread.

The Mishnah concludes this section by referencing an earlier teaching: *ve-zo hi she'amru he'ani me'areiv be-raglov* — this case of a traveler caught on the road is the scenario the Sages had in mind when they taught that a poor person may make an *eruv* with his feet — that is, by being physically present at the desired location — without needing to deposit bread. The traveler is effectively a "poor person" in this regard: he has no access to bread for this purpose.

*Amar Rabbi Meir: ein lanu ela ani* — Rabbi Meir holds that personal presence as a substitute for bread is permitted only where there is no alternative, as in genuine poverty or the traveler's predicament. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: echad ani ve-echad ashir* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that personal presence is always valid, even when bread is available. *Lo amru me'arvin be-pas ella le-hakel al he'ashir she-lo yeitzei ve-ye'areiv be-raglov* — the use of bread was enacted only as a convenience for the wealthy, so they would not need to go out and stand at the desired location at nightfall. But since the bread symbolizes being present, if one actually wishes to be present in person, that is at least as good as the bread — and according to Rabbi Yehuda, this option is always open.

## Vague Designation

### Rav: Worthless

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם״? אָמַר רַב: לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם כׇּל עִיקָּר, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְתַחְתָּיו שֶׁל אִילָן לָא מָצֵי אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that one who declares his intention to establish residence beneath a tree, without specifying the precise location, has not said anything. The Gemara asks: **What is** the precise meaning of **he has not said anything?** **Rav said: He has not said anything at all,** and has failed to establish residence anywhere, and **he may not even go to** the place **beneath** that **tree.** His failure to specify a particular location prevents him from establishing residence beneath the tree. The fact that he sought to establish residence someplace other than his present location prevents him from establishing residence at his present location. Accordingly, he may walk no more than four cubits from the place that he is standing.

*Mai lo amar klum?* — The *gemara* now examines the consequences of the failed designation "under the tree" without specification. What precisely does the Mishnah mean when it says this amounts to nothing? *Amar Rav: lo amar klum kol ikar — d'afilu le-tachtav shel ilan lo matzi azil* — Rav holds that the failed designation is completely worthless: the person cannot even reach the space under the tree. The reasoning, as Rashi explains, is that by saying "under the tree" the person made clear that he did not wish to begin *Shabbat* where he was standing. But because he failed to specify where under the tree, he has not effectively designated any place at all. Having rejected his current location and failed to establish any alternative, he has no *Shabbat* starting point whatsoever, and is therefore confined to four *amot* — completely stuck.

### Shmuel: Reach Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם לְבֵיתוֹ, אֲבָל לְתַחְתָּיו שֶׁל אִילָן מָצֵי אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: He has not said anything** with regard to going **to his home,** if it is two thousand cubits past the tree; **however,** with regard to the area **beneath the tree,** if its bough is entirely within two thousand cubits of his present location **he may** indeed **go** there.

Shmuel takes a more lenient position: *lo amar klum le-veiso* — the failed designation means only that he cannot reach all the way home. *Aval le-tachtav shel ilan matzi azil* — but he can reach the space under the tree.

### Chamor Gamal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַעֲשֶׂה תַּחְתָּיו שֶׁל אִילָן חַמָּר גַּמָּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** when we learned in the mishna that he did not establish residence, it means that the legal status of the area **beneath the tree becomes** comparable to both **a donkey driver,** who walks behind the animal and prods it, **and a camel driver,** who walks before the animal and leads it in the sense that the tree is pulling him in both directions. Since he did not specify a particular location as his residence, any part of the area beneath the tree could be the place where he established residence.

The *gemara* explains Shmuel's reasoning through the image of *chamor gamal*: the space under the tree becomes like the driver caught between a camel and a donkey pulling in opposite directions. The space beneath the tree's canopy — let us say it extends ten *amot* on each side, totaling twenty *amot* — is a zone of uncertainty. The person said his *eruv* begins "under the tree" but did not say where. We therefore treat the starting point of his *eruv* as uncertain: it might be at the far northern edge of the canopy, or at the far southern edge.

### Safek Space Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לִמְדּוֹד מִן הַצָּפוֹן — מוֹדְדִין לוֹ מִן הַדָּרוֹם, בָּא לִמְדּוֹד מִן הַדָּרוֹם — מוֹדְדִין לוֹ מִן הַצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, if **he comes to measure** two thousand cubits **from the north** of the tree in order to ascertain whether or not he may go to his home, because of the uncertainty with regard to the precise location where he established residence, **one measures** the distance **for him** stringently **from the south.** And likewise, if **he comes to measure** the distance to his home **from the south, one measures** the distance **for him from the north.**

This uncertainty works against him in every direction. *Ba limdod min ha-tzafon, modedin lo min ha-darom* — if he wishes to measure his two thousand *amot* northward, we say: perhaps your *eruv* began at the southern edge of the canopy, so we measure from there — giving you two thousand *amot* from the south edge, meaning you lose twenty *amot* in the northward direction. *Ba limdod min ha-darom, modedin lo min ha-tzafon* — and if he wishes to go south, we measure from the northern edge, and he again loses twenty *amot*. The entire space beneath the canopy is treated as a *safek* zone: in whichever direction he attempts to go, we assign his starting point to the far edge in the opposite direction, and he loses the full width of the canopy from his effective *techum*.

The consequence: if his home is exactly two thousand *amot* from the nearest edge of the canopy, he cannot reach his door, because we measure his two thousand *amot* from the far edge of the canopy — which is twenty *amot* further away. So Shmuel agrees that he cannot get all the way home. But unlike Rav, Shmuel does not strip him of the ability to reach the tree at all. He can get there; the problem is at the far end.

### Rabbah on Rav

The *gemara* on the opening of daf 50 spells out the reasoning behind Rav's stricter position. *Amar Rabbah: mai ta'ama de-Rav? Mishum de-lo mesayem atrei* — because he did not designate a specific location. Not designating does not create a *safek* between two possible points, as Shmuel would have it. Rather, the absence of specification means the designation has no legal existence at all. The person made clear, by his declaration, that he did not want to begin *Shabbat* at his current position; but since he never legally established any alternative place, he effectively begins *Shabbat* nowhere — and is left with only four *amot*. More on this will follow in the next session.
