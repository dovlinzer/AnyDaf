# Bava Kamma 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chometz Post-Pesach

### Intro: Chometz Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָמֵץ וְעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** If one robbed another of **leavened bread, and Passover elapsed over it,** and it is therefore prohibited to derive benefit from it, the robber **says to** his victim: **That which is yours is before you.**

We pick up on 98b, approximately ten lines down in the medium-sized text. The opening ruling concerns *chometz she'avar alav ha-Pesach* — leavened bread over which Pesach has passed. If someone stole *chometz* before Pesach and Pesach then passed, rendering it completely worthless, he may nonetheless return the physical object and declare *harei she-lecha lefanecha* — "behold, your object is before you." The primary Biblical obligation is not to pay back the monetary value of a stolen item but to return the object itself: *v'heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal*. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is that this obligation is fulfilled even when the object has become worthless, so long as it remains physically identical to what was taken.

### Object vs. Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא אוֹמְרִין בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״?
>
> **Translation:** **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **with regard to items from which benefit is forbidden** one **says: That which is yours is before you?**

The *gemara* asks: *man tana omer be-issurei hana'ah harei she-lecha lefanecha?* — who is the *tanna* who holds that even when an object is forbidden in benefit and entirely worthless, one may still say "behold, your object is before you" and thereby discharge the obligation of return?

### Rabbi Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוֹר שֶׁהֵמִית; עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – מְכָרוֹ מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ, שְׁחָטוֹ – בְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר, הֶחְזִירוֹ שׁוֹמֵר לִבְעָלָיו – מוּחְזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 5:4): If there was **an ox that killed** a person and is consequently liable to be stoned, and **before** the court **sentenced it** the owner **sold** the ox, the **sale** is valid; if **he consecrated it,** the **consecration** is valid; if **he slaughtered it, its flesh is permitted;** if **a bailee returned it to its owner, it is returned.**

Rav Chisda answers: it is Rabbi Yaakov. The source is a *brayta*: *shor she-heimis* — an ox that killed a person. Before a *gmar din*, before the ox has officially acquired the status of a *shor haniskal*, if one sells it the sale is valid, if one consecrates it the consecration takes effect, and if one slaughters it the meat is permitted, as Rashi notes — because these acts prevent the ox from ever attaining the *shor haniskal* status. The *brayta* thus identifies an opportunity to act prior to that status crystallizing.

### Braita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ – מְכָרוֹ אֵינוֹ מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ אֵינוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ, שְׁחָטוֹ – בְּשָׂרוֹ אָסוּר, הֶחְזִירוֹ שׁוֹמֵר לִבְעָלָיו – אֵינוֹ מוּחְזָר. רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אַף מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ, הֶחְזִירוֹ שׁוֹמֵר לִבְעָלָיו – מוּחְזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: By contrast, **from when it was sentenced** to be stoned it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, and if the owner **sold it,** the **sale** is **not** valid; if **he consecrated it,** the **consecration** is **not** valid; if **he slaughtered it, its flesh is forbidden;** if **a bailee returned it to its owner, it is not returned,** as once it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, the ox is worthless. **Rabbi Ya'akov says: Even after it was sentenced,** if **a bailee returned it to its owner, it is returned.**

Once *gmar din* has occurred and the ox formally becomes a *shor haniskal*, the situation reverses entirely: any sale is void, any consecration is invalid, and the meat of a slaughtered ox is forbidden even if it was not actually stoned.

### Shor HaNiskal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: אוֹמְרִין בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין אוֹמְרִין בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda completes his analysis: **What, is it not that they disagree about this** issue, **that Rabbi Ya'akov holds** that **with regard to items from which benefit is forbidden** one can **say: That which is yours is before you, and the Rabbis hold** that **with regard to items from which benefit is forbidden** one **cannot say: That which is yours is before you?**

Crucially for our discussion, the *brayta* rules that if a *shomer* was watching such an ox and the ox attained *shor haniskal* status while in the *shomer*'s care, the *shomer* may return it to its owner before *gmar din* — that is a valid return. But *mi-she-nigmar dino*, once *gmar din* has occurred, if the *shomer* attempts to return the ox to the owner and say "here is your object," that is *not* a valid return. The ox is *asur be-hana'ah* and entirely worthless; returning it does not discharge the obligation. Rabbi Yaakov, however, disagrees and holds that *af mi-she-nigmar dino*, even after *gmar din*, returning the ox counts as a valid return. So it appears that Rabbi Yaakov and the Rabbis are debating the very question of whether one can say *harei she-lecha lefanecha* regarding an object that is *asur be-hana'ah*.

## Gmar Din B'Fano

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אָמְרִינַן בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״; דְּאִם כֵּן, נִפְלְגוּ בְּחָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: הָכָא בְּגוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו קָא מִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said to him** in response: **No.** It may be **that everyone** agrees that **with regard to items from which benefit is forbidden** one can **say: That which is yours is before you,** and the ruling of the mishna is also in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **As if so,** i.e., if it were true that the dispute in the *baraita* between the Rabbis and Rabbi Ya'akov concerns the general question of returning an item from which benefit is forbidden, **they should disagree with regard to** the case of **leavened bread on Passover** and whether it can be returned as is, since this is a clear case of an item from which benefit is forbidden. **Rather, Rabba said: Here,** in the *baraita*, **they disagree with regard to** a different issue: Can **an ox be sentenced in its absence?**

Rava rejects this framing. He argues that the debate between Rabbi Yaakov and the Rabbis is not about *issurei hana'ah* in general. On the contrary, the principle that one may say *harei she-lecha lefanecha* by *issurei hana'ah* is universally agreed upon — *de-khulei alma amrinan be-issurei hana'ah harei she-lecha lefanecha*. As evidence, Rava notes: if the dispute truly concerned whether one can say *harei she-lecha lefanecha* by *issurei hana'ah*, they should have framed the debate around *chometz ha-Pesach*, the simpler and more straightforward case. The fact that the debate is specifically located in the *shor haniskal* case — which introduces additional complications — demonstrates that the underlying question is something unique to *shor haniskal*.

What is that unique complication? *Hacha be-gomrin dino shel shor she-lo be-fanav kamifligi* — the debate is whether *beis din* can render a *gmar din* on the ox without the ox being physically present.

### Rabbis' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין גּוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אַיְיתִיתֵיהּ נִיהֲלַיה – הֲוָה מַעְרֵיקְנָא לֵיהּ לְאַגְמָא, הַשְׁתָּא מְסַרְתֵּיהּ בְּיַד מַאן דְּלָא מָצֵינָא לְאִישְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains the dispute: **The Rabbis hold** that **an ox may not be sentenced in its absence,** and therefore the bailee is liable to pay. The reason is **that** when the ox is returned after its sentencing, the owner may **say to** the bailee: **Had you brought** the ox **to me** before sentencing, **I would have chased it away into the marsh,** preventing the sentencing from taking place. **Now,** since you did not return it to me before it was given to the court, **you have given it to one with whom I cannot litigate,** as the court was sure to sentence it. Therefore you must pay me and not return the ox.

The Rabbis hold *ein gomrin dino shel shor she-lo be-fanav* — *beis din* cannot conduct the final judgment unless the ox is present. Consequently, the *gmar din* that gave the ox its forbidden status could only have occurred because the *shomer* allowed the ox to be seen by the court.

### Shomer Cannot Return

The logic of the Rabbis' position is as follows: the owner can now say to the *shomer*, "Had you returned it to me before *gmar din*, I would have hidden it away in the swamps — *mavrikna lei le-agma* — and it never would have become a *shor haniskal*. Now, through your negligence, you have handed it over to an authority — *beis din* — against whom I have no legal recourse." The *shomer*'s negligence in allowing the ox to come before *beis din* is what caused the irreversible harm. That is why the return is invalid.

### Rabbi Yaakov Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: גּוֹמְרִין דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבַדִי לֵיהּ? סוֹף סוֹף הֲוָה גָּמְרִי לֵיהּ (דִּינָא) [לְדִינֵיהּ] שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov holds** that **an ox may be sentenced** even **in its absence,** and the claim of the owner of the ox is not accepted. The reason is **that** the bailee may **say to** the owner in response: **What did I do** to the ox? **Ultimately it would have been sentenced in its absence** and rendered forbidden.

Rabbi Yaakov counters: *gomrin dino shel shor she-lo be-fanav* — *beis din* can conduct *gmar din* even without the ox present. Therefore the owner cannot blame the *shomer*, because even if the *shomer* had returned the ox beforehand, *beis din* would have proceeded to *gmar din* regardless. The *shomer* did not cause the harm, and the return is therefore valid.

### Negligence Question

This raises an obvious difficulty. Even accepting the Rabbis' position that the *shomer*'s negligence contributed to the outcome — why should negligence matter at all? The principle of *harei she-lecha lefanecha* is precisely that one returns the *same object*, regardless of what has befallen it. The *gazlan* who failed to return the *chometz* before Pesach was also negligent in not returning it in time. The *shomer* was arguably negligent in allowing the ox to gore someone in the first place. Why should negligence disqualify the return when the object is physically the same?

### Tosafot: Visible Status

This question was already treated earlier in Bava Kamma, as both Rashi and Tosafot address it. Tosafot draws an analogy to the earlier principle of *pesulto malkhus* — when something is rendered invalid by governmental or communal decree, that can be treated as a visible damage even if the object has not physically changed. Tosafot applies the same reasoning here: since *beis din* is physically holding the ox, and its being held by *beis din* is a necessary and visible component of its *shor haniskal* status, the status is as though externally visible. Rashi attempts a different explanation — a combination of negligence and non-visible damage that renders return impossible — which is closer to the plain meaning of the *gemara*'s language but harder to fully understand conceptually. Either way, the *gemara* is asserting that the *shor haniskal* presents a unique set of considerations involving the degree of negligence and the visibility conferred by *beis din*'s seizure of the animal — and that this debate would not apply to *chometz she'avar alav ha-Pesach*.

## Braita Evidence

### Rav Chisda Asks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנֵית מִידֵּה בְּאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident: **Rav Ḥisda,** who stated that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Ya'akov and not the Rabbis, **found Rabba bar Shmuel** and **said to him: Did you learn anything** with regard to the *halakhot* of returning stolen **items from which benefit is forbidden?**

Having established Rava's distinction, the *gemara* now records an exchange that tests it empirically. Rav Chisda — who had been the one to suggest that the *shor haniskal* debate reflected a broader dispute about *issurei hana'ah* — encounters Rabba bar Shmuel and asks: *tanisa midi be-issurei hana'ah* — have you taught any *brayta* concerning *issurei hana'ah* that could help us determine whether the debate about *shor haniskal* is purely local, or whether it would extend to *chometz ha-Pesach* as well?

### Torah's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, תְּנֵינָא: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵלָה״; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזַל״? יַחֲזִיר כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּזַל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Shmuel **said to him: Yes, we learned** a *baraita*: The verse states: "Then it shall be, if he has sinned, and is guilty, **that he shall restore the item** that he robbed" (Leviticus 5:23). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "That he robbed"?** It means that the robber must **return the same** item **that he robbed.**

Rabba bar Shmuel replies that he has. The *brayta* derives from the phrase *v'heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal* — "and he shall return the stolen object that he stole" — that the obligation is to return the item in the state it was at the time of the theft, *k'ein she-gazal*.

### Returnability Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: גָּזַל מַטְבֵּעַ וְנִפְסַל, פֵּירוֹת וְהִרְקִיבוּ, יַיִן וְהֶחְמִיץ, תְּרוּמָה וְנִטְמֵאת, חָמֵץ וְעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח, בְּהֵמָה וְנֶעֶבְדָה בָּהּ עֲבֵירָה, וְשׁוֹר עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From here** the Sages **stated** that if one **robbed** another of **a coin and** it **was invalidated,** or of **produce and** it **rotted,** or of **wine and** it **fermented,** or of ***teruma* and** it **became ritually impure,** or of **leavened bread and Passover elapsed over it,** or of **an animal and a sin was performed with it, or** of **an ox that had not been sentenced, he** can **say to** the robbery victim: **That which is yours is before you.**

From this the *brayta* enumerates cases where the thief can say *harei she-lecha lefanecha* and thereby discharge his obligation, despite the object's worthlessness. These include: *gazal matbe'a ve-nifsal* — he stole a coin and it was invalidated; *peiros ve-hirkivu* (or according to some textual readings, *ve-henitnasakh* — wine that was poured as *yayin nesech*, rendering it *asur be-hana'ah* without any visible change); *yayin ve-hikrim* — wine that turned slightly; *terumah ve-nitme'at* — *terumah* that became *tamei*; *chometz she'avar alav ha-Pesach*; *behemah ve-ne'evda bah aveirah* — an animal used in an act of *aveirah*, which becomes unfit for the altar; and *shor ad she-lo nigmar dino* — an ox before *gmar din*. In all these cases, one may say *harei she-lecha lefanecha*, and this satisfies the Torah's requirement.

### Shor-Only Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – אִין, מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ – לָא? רַבָּנַן; וְקָתָנֵי: חָמֵץ וְעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח – אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ, לָא תֵּימָא לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Who did you hear say** that **before the ox was sentenced** it **can be** returned, but **after it was sentenced it cannot?** It is **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Ya'akov in the *baraita*; **and** this *baraita* **teaches** that if one robbed another of **leavened bread and Passover elapsed over it, he** can **say to** the robbery victim: **That which is yours is before you.** This disproves the analysis of Rav Ḥisda, as even the Rabbis agree that an item from which benefit is forbidden is returned as is. Rav Ḥisda **said to** Rabba bar Shmuel: **If you find** the Sages, **do not say anything to them,** i.e., do not publicize that I erred.

Critically, the *brayta* permits *harei she-lecha lefanecha* for *chometz* — listed without any qualification — while for the *shor haniskal* it specifies *ad she-lo nigram dino*: only before *gmar din*, not after. This structure demonstrates precisely what Rava claimed: the restriction in the *shor haniskal* case is particular to *gmar din* and does not reflect a general principle about *issurei hana'ah*. For all other *issurei hana'ah* on the list, including *chometz*, the return is valid unconditionally.

### Rav Chisda Loses

The *gemara* observes: who holds that a *shor haniskal* may be returned only before *gmar din* and not after? The Rabbis — who are the ones who restrict the *shomer*'s return after *gmar din*. And yet even those same Rabbis, as this *brayta* reflects, agree that *chometz she'avar alav ha-Pesach* — listed alongside the other cases — may be returned with *harei she-lecha lefanecha*. This confirms Rava's position and refutes Rav Chisda's original suggestion that they were debating the broader principle of *issurei hana'ah*.

Rav Chisda's response to being proven wrong is memorable: *ei mashkechuhu lehu, lo teimru lehu ve-lo midi* — if you encounter others from the *beis medrash*, tell them nothing. Do not publicize that I was refuted. This raises interesting questions about intellectual honesty, which the *gemara* leaves without comment.

A question arises in passing: is there not something troubling about the very premise of the Rabbis' position — that the owner could have hidden the ox in the swamp to evade *beis din*'s judgment? The ox had, after all, killed a person, and the institution of *shor haniskal* is a form of judicial accountability. The *gemara* never voices disapproval of such a strategy. One can note that in human capital cases, *chazal* themselves — Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon — famously declared that had they sat on the Sanhedrin, no one would ever have been executed, for they would always have found a loophole. The case of the *shor* is not a human life, but it raises the same underlying question about the relationship between the individual and the judicial process when the matter is, in some sense, between a person and God rather than a strictly interpersonal claim.

## Craftsmen Liability

### Rav Assi's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָתַן לָאוּמָּנִין לְתַקֵּן, וְקִלְקְלוּ – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם. נָתַן לְחָרָשׁ שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל לְתַקֵּן, וְקִלְקֵל – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם. וְהַבַּנַּאי שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו לִסְתּוֹר אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל, וְשִׁיבֵּר הָאֲבָנִים אוֹ שֶׁהִזִּיקָן – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם. הָיָה סוֹתֵר מִצַּד זֶה וְנָפַל מִצַּד אַחֵר – פָּטוּר; וְאִם מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **gave** items **to craftsmen to fix and they damaged** them, the craftsmen are **liable to pay** for the damage. For example, if one **gave a chest, a box, or a cabinet to a carpenter to fix, and he damaged** it, he is **liable to pay. And a builder who committed to demolish a wall and** while demolishing it he **broke the stones, or who damaged them,** is **liable to pay.** If **he was demolishing on this side** of the wall, **and** the wall **fell from another side** and caused damage, he is **exempt** from liability. **But if** a stone fell and caused damage **due to** the force of **the blow,** he is **liable.**

With the preceding discussion concluded, the *gemara* pivots somewhat from the theme of *gezeilah* — theft and its ramifications — and introduces a set of cases involving craftsmen who damage objects entrusted to them. In some respects this topic belongs more naturally to the first half of Bava Kamma, dealing with *mazik* liability, and indeed the *gemara* will eventually bring us back to the direct *mazik* analysis. For now, the *mishnah* lays the groundwork.

### Two Cases

The *mishnah* teaches: *Nasan le-ummanin le-taken ve-kilkelu, chayavin le-shalem* — if one gave items to craftsmen to repair and they ruined them, they are liable to pay. *Nasan le-charash shidah teivah u-migdal le-taken ve-kilkel, chayav le-shalem* — if one gave a carpenter a box, chest, or cabinet to fix and he broke it, he must pay. The *mishnah* continues: a builder who was commissioned to knock down a wall and in the process broke the stones or damaged them must pay. But if stones fell on a *different* side of the wall from where he was working — *ha-soser mi-tzad zeh ve-nafal mi-tzad acher* — he is exempt. If, however, those stones fell as a direct result of his striking the wall, *machmas ha-makkah*, he is liable.

### Rav Assi's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּתַן לְחָרָשׁ שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל – לִנְעֹץ בָּהֶן מַסְמֵר, וְנָעַץ בָּהֶן מַסְמֵר וְשִׁיבְּרָן; אֲבָל נָתַן לְחָרָשׁ עֵצִים לַעֲשׂוֹת שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל, וְעָשָׂה מֵהֶן שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל וְשִׁיבְּרָן – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one gave a carpenter a chest, a box, or a cabinet to fix, and he damaged it, the carpenter is liable to pay for the damage. **Rav Asi says:** The Sages **taught** that a carpenter is liable to pay damages **only** in a case where one **gave the carpenter a chest, a box, or a cabinet to drive a nail into them,** i.e., he gave the carpenter complete vessels to repair, **and he drove the nail into them and broke them. But** if one **gave wood to a carpenter to build a chest, a box, or a cabinet, and he built a chest, a box, or a cabinet from** the wood, **and** before giving it to the owner the carpenter **broke them,** he is **exempt** from paying for the damage caused to these vessels, and must pay only for the damage caused to the wood.

Rav Assi refines the liability rule. *Lo shanu ela she-nasan le-charash shidah teivah u-migdal li-no'otz bah masmer ve-na'atz bah masmer ve-shibran* — the case of liability in the *mishnah* is specifically where one gave the craftsman an already-constructed box and asked him to bang a nail into it, and in doing so he shattered it. The craftsman improved nothing; he simply destroyed what was given to him. But: *aval nasan le-charash eitzim la'asot mehem shidah teivah u-migdal, ve-asah mehem shidah teivah u-migdal ve-shibran, patur* — if one gave the carpenter raw wood with instructions to build a box, and the carpenter built a fine box worth many times the wood's value, and then in hammering in the final nail shattered the finished product, the craftsman is *exempt* from paying for the destroyed box.

This is a striking ruling. The owner handed over wood worth twenty dollars; the craftsman built something worth two hundred dollars and then destroyed it. Why does the craftsman not owe for the full value of the box he ruined?

### Uman Koneh B'Shvach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אוּמָּן קוֹנֶה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this? It is because **a craftsman acquires** ownership rights **through** the **enhancement of the vessel.** The craftsman is considered to have acquired the vessel through his work, which enhances its value, and it remains in his possession until he returns it to the owners. Consequently, if he damages the vessel in any way, he is damaging his own item, and must return only the value of the raw materials to the owners.

The answer: *uman koneh be-shevach kli* — the craftsman acquires ownership of the improvement in the vessel until the moment he actually returns it to the owner.

### Payment Framework

The conceptual framework underlying this principle is significant. We typically think of a craftsman as someone we hire to work on our property: the wood remains ours throughout, the craftsman adds labor, and we pay for the labor. Under this intuitive model, the finished box is entirely the owner's from the moment it is built, and destroying it would make the craftsman fully liable.

But *uman koneh be-shevach kli* proposes a fundamentally different framing. When you commission someone to build you a box from your raw materials and agree to pay fifty dollars, what you are actually doing — in the *gemara*'s conceptualization — is agreeing to *purchase* from the craftsman the box that he will create. He will build a box, that box belongs to him until delivery, and you have contracted to buy it from him at an agreed price. Payment is not for labor; it is a purchase price for an object the craftsman currently owns. As one might observe, this is conceptually parallel to the earlier discussions in this *perek* about *shinui koneh* — the way transformation confers ownership — though the *gemara* here approaches the question from the angle of commissioned work rather than theft.

A natural question follows: if the craftsman owns the box until delivery and you are buying it from him, and then the box is destroyed before delivery — you owe him nothing for the box he destroyed? Correct, because if the transaction is a sale, you cannot simultaneously demand payment for destroying "your" box. If you wish to claim the box was yours — and therefore the craftsman owes you for a two-hundred-dollar loss — you must first acknowledge that you contracted to pay him for it, in which case you owe the fee. If he does not collect the fee, you owe him for the wood at most. You cannot have it both ways.

## Dyer Case Proof

### Wool & Dye Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נָתַן לָאוּמָּנִין וְקִלְקְלוּ – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם. מַאי, לָאו דְּיָהֵיב לְהוּ עֵצִים? לָא, שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to contradict Rav Asi's statement: **We learned** in the mishna that if one **gave** items **to craftsmen** to fix **and they damaged** them, they are **liable to pay** for the damage. **What, is it not** referring to a case **where he gave them wood,** and they nevertheless pay the owner the value of a vessel? The Gemara responds: **No,** it is referring to a case where he gave them **a chest, a box, or a cabinet** to repair.

The *gemara* attempts to bring proof for *uman koneh be-shevach kli* from a *brayta* concerning a dyer. *Ha-nosein tzemer le-tzaba ve-hikdicha yora* — one who gives wool to a dyer, and the vat causes it to burn and be destroyed. The ruling: *nosein lo demei tzamro* — the dyer pays the owner for the value of the wool. The *gemara* asks: does he not also pay for the *shevach* — the improvement? If the wool was first dyed, improving in value, and only then destroyed, he apparently pays only the base value of the wool, not the improved dyed value. Does this not prove that *uman koneh be-shevach kli* — the dyer owned that improved value, so he owes you only what you owned (the raw wool)?

### Shmuel's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִקְדִּיחוֹ בִּשְׁעַת נְפִילָה, דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁבָחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said:** This is not a proof. **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the wool **was burned at the moment of falling** into the cauldron, before the dye had taken hold, so **that there is no enhancement.**

Shmuel objects. *Hacha be-mai askinan she-hikdicha be-sha'as nefillah* — perhaps we are dealing with a case where the wool was destroyed at the very moment it fell into the vat, before any improvement occurred at all. There was simply no *shevach* to pay for. Shmuel adds: had it been destroyed *after* absorbing the dye and being improved, what would the ruling be? In that case the dyer would pay *demei tzamro ve-shivcho* — for both the wool and its improvement — which would imply that *uman* is *not* *koneh be-shevach kli*. So Shmuel's explanation actually suggests he holds the opposite of *uman koneh be-shevach kli*.

### Dye Provision Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ שְׁמוּאֵל: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּצֶמֶר וְסַמָּנִין דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, וְצַבָּע אֲגַר יְדֵיהּ הוּא דְּשָׁקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this statement. **Shmuel** could have **said to you: With what are we dealing here** in the mishna? We are dealing with a case **where the wool and herbs** used in the dye both **belong to the homeowner, and the dyer is taking** only the **payment of his hand,** i.e., the wages for his labor, and nothing else. In this case, the craftsman does not acquire ownership rights through the enhancement of the vessel, but in a case where the craftsman provides the materials, he does acquire such rights.

But the *gemara* presses further. If Shmuel holds that in the standard case the dyer pays for the full improved value, we would expect the *brayta* to say he pays for the wool *and* the dye. The *brayta* says only the wool. The *gemara* then introduces a qualification: *ke-gon de-tzemer ve-simanim de-ba'al ha-bayit de-tzaba agar yadeih hu de-shakeil* — the case may be one where the owner supplied both the wool and the dye, and the dyer was therefore receiving only the fee for his handiwork, his *schar yadav*. When the owner supplies all the raw materials — including the dye — the dyer is conceived not as a craftsman creating something of his own but as a hired laborer. Under those circumstances, the framework of *uman koneh be-shevach kli* may not apply at all; he is simply a wage-earner.

The intuition behind this distinction is not entirely straightforward. After all, a carpenter might provide only his skill while the owner provides wood and nails, yet we might still think of the result as the carpenter's creation. The *gemara* appears to treat the dye, as the key transformative raw material in dyeing, as the dividing line: if the craftsman brings his own dye, he is creating something new that he then sells; if the owner supplies the dye, the craftsman is merely performing a service. One might further suggest that the provision of all raw materials by the owner positions the craftsman more as an extension of the owner's own activity rather than an independent producer.

The *gemara* pushes back even on this, however: if the owner supplied the dye, we would expect the dyer to owe the owner for the destroyed dye as well — yet that is not what the *brayta* says. So the attempted explanation creates its own difficulties.

### Shmuel Equivocates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי צַמְרוֹ וְסַמָּנִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא שְׁמוּאֵל דַּחוֹיֵי קָא מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if the homeowner's herbs were also ruined by the dyer, the mishna **should have** said that the dyer **gives** the owner the **value of his wool and herbs,** not just the value of his wool. **Rather, Shmuel is** merely **dismissing** the Gemara's proof with regard to the statement of Rav Asi that a craftsman acquires a vessel through its enhancement, by saying that the mishna could be understood otherwise. He does not, however, state his own opinion on this matter.

In the end, *Shmuel dechuya ka-midachei leh* — Shmuel is simply deflecting. He is not taking a definitive position on *uman koneh be-shevach kli*; he is only demonstrating that the *brayta* cannot serve as proof either way. The dyer case remains inconclusive.

## Kiddushin Case

### Garment Pickup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַנּוֹתֵן טַלִּיתוֹ לְאוּמָּן; גְּמָרוֹ וְהוֹדִיעוֹ – אֲפִילּוּ מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תָלִין״. נְתָנָהּ לוֹ בַּחֲצִי הַיּוֹם – כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁקְעָה עָלָיו הַחַמָּה, עוֹבֵר עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another suggestion: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who gave his garment to a craftsman,** and the craftsman **concluded** the work **and notified** the owner that the work was complete, **even** if the owner delays paying the craftsman **from now until ten days** henceforth, **he does not violate, for this** delay, the prohibition **of:** "You shall not oppress your neighbor, nor rob him; the wages of a hired worker **shall not abide with you all night** until the morning" (Leviticus 19:13). If the craftsman **gave** the garment **to him at midday,** then **once the sun has set** and the owner has not paid him, the owner **does violate, for this** delay, the prohibition **against delaying** the payment of wages.

A clearer proof emerges from a ruling about *bal talin*. A *mishnah* teaches: if one gives a cloak to a craftsman to work on, and the craftsman finishes and informs the owner — even if the owner waits ten days (or any extended period) to come and collect it, the owner does not transgress *bal talin*. The owner's obligation to pay does not begin until the moment of physical pickup.

### Bal Talin Rule

The prohibition of *lo salin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — do not allow the wages of a hired worker to remain unpaid overnight — is triggered by the act of receiving the completed work. *Nasano lo be-chatzi ha-yom, keivan she-shaka ha-chama, over alav mishum bal talin*: if the craftsman delivers the item in the middle of the day, the owner must pay by sunset or transgress the prohibition.

### Bal Talin Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אוּמָּן קוֹנֶה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי, אַמַּאי עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And if it enters your mind** to say that **a craftsman acquires** ownership rights **through** the **enhancement of the vessel, why does** the owner **violate** the prohibition **against delaying** the payment of wages? It is as if the craftsman acquired the garment, and the payment is considered to be a purchase of the garment by the owner, rather than a wage.

Here is where this ruling intersects with our question. If *uman koneh be-shevach kli* — if the craftsman truly owns the improvement and the transaction is conceived as a sale rather than a fee for services — then there should *never* be a violation of *bal talin*. The prohibition applies to *pe'ulat sachir*, the wage of a hired worker. But if the payment is not a wage but a purchase price for an object the craftsman owns, then delaying payment is simply being slow to pay for a sale, not withholding a worker's wages. One does not transgress *bal talin* for being late in paying for a car purchase. Yet the *mishnah* clearly contemplates that *bal talin* can be violated here — it only delays the clock until pickup. This implies that the payment *is* conceptualized as a wage for services rendered, not as a purchase price for an object owned by the craftsman.

This is a striking point — and all the more so because the *mishnah* is speaking of fixing a garment, not creating something from raw materials. Even a modest repair job is, under the *uman koneh be-shevach kli* principle, conceived as the craftsman owning the improvement. Yet *bal talin* still applies, which would seem to undercut the principle.

### Smoothing Wool

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: בְּגִרְדָּא דְסַרְבָּלָא, דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁבָחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, said:** The *baraita* is stating the *halakha* **with regard to the laundering of a thick garment, where there is no enhancement** of the garment. Therefore, the craftsman does not acquire it.

The *gemara* addresses this through Rav Mari b'rei de-Rav Kahana. He suggests the case involves *gardi de-sarvala* — someone who smoothed the rough surface of a thick garment — where the improvement is so minor as to be almost negligible. The *gemara* initially entertains the idea that there might be no *shevach* at all, but ultimately rejects this: *le-mai yahavei lei? de-reicha, keivan de-reicha hainu shevach* — whatever the reason you gave it to him, even the softening of the fabric constitutes an improvement, a *shevach*.

### Shvach vs. Kli

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, לְמַאי יַהֲבַהּ נִהֲלֵיהּ – לְרַכּוֹכֵי; כֵּיוָן דְּרַכְּכֵיהּ, הַיְינוּ שְׁבָחָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַגְרֵיהּ לְבִיטְשֵׁי – בִּיטְשָׁא בִּיטְשָׁא בְּמָעֲתָא, דְּהַיְינוּ שְׂכִירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, to what** end **did** the owner of the garment **give it to** the craftsman? He gave it to him in order **to soften** it. **Once he has softened it, that is its enhancement,** and the craftsman has therefore acquired it. The Gemara responds: **No;** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* in a case **where** the owner **hired** the craftsman **for treading,** i.e., to forcefully tread on the garment in water until it softens, with the owner paying the craftsman **a *ma'a*** coin **for each tread.** The difference is **that this is** considered **hired** labor, where the craftsman is paid based on the amount of times he performed an action, and not contractual labor, where he is paid based on the outcome, in this case, a softened garment.

This exchange, however, clarifies the precise formulation of the principle. The *gemara* makes clear it is not *uman koneh be-shevach kli* in the sense of owning the entire vessel or object. Rather, it is *uman koneh be-shevach* — the craftsman owns only the *improved value*, not the object itself. The car was worth ten thousand dollars before the repair; now it is worth eleven thousand. The craftsman owns that incremental thousand-dollar value. He does not own the car. Your nine-dollar shirt is now worth ten dollars after softening; the craftsman owns that one-dollar increment. The payment you agreed upon is, in this conceptualization, the price you are paying to *purchase from him* that improvement — at whatever rate you negotiated, which might be less or more than the improvement's market value.

This is an abstract mode of analysis, but it explains why *bal talin* can still apply: even conceding *uman koneh be-shevach*, the craftsman's ownership of a small increment of value does not transform the entire transaction into a commercial sale. The worker is still, in a fundamental sense, working for you, and withholding the agreed payment can still constitute a violation of *bal talin* — even if we simultaneously conceptualize an abstract ownership of the improvement.

## Wage vs. Improvement

### Stomping Wages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאי דִּסְלֵיק אַדַּעְתִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּלָא אַגְרֵיהּ לְבִיטְשֵׁי, מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת – דִּבְעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: קַבְּלָנוּת, עוֹבֵר עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״, אוֹ אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר? וַאֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: עוֹבֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And with regard to what entered our minds initially, that** the owner **did not hire** the worker **for treading** but rather he hired him like a craftsman based on the assumption that the garment would be returned laundered, this **supports** the opinion **of Rav Sheshet. As they asked Rav Sheshet:** If one hires **a contractor,** who is not paid an hourly wage but is paid upon the completion of his task, and the one who hired him does not pay him on the day that he completes the work, does he **violate, for this** delay, the **prohibition against delaying** the payment of wages, **or** does he **not violate** the prohibition? **And Rav Sheshet said to them:** He does **violate** the prohibition.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by introducing a cleaner case. *Lo tzricha de-agrei le-vatsha* — what if you hired someone explicitly to stomp on the fabric? *Batsha batsha be-ma'ata* — you agreed to pay a small amount for each act of stomping. That is unambiguously *sechirus* — hourly or per-action wages — with no element of commissioned improvement. Here, the person is clearly a wage-earner, not a contractor producing an end product, and *bal talin* plainly applies. The *gemara* had been seeking a case where *uman koneh be-shevach kli* might operate yet *bal talin* would still apply; the stomping case provides a clean example where *uman koneh be-shevach kli* is irrelevant by construction, since the payment is explicitly per action and not for an end product.

### Rav Sheshet's Question

This case bears on a question posed by Rav Sheshet: does *bal talin* apply to *kablanut* — contracting? If I hire you to complete a defined job, not by the hour but for a fixed fee upon completion, and I delay payment — do I transgress *bal talin*?

### Kablanut Bal Talin

One might think that if *uman koneh be-shevach kli*, then the payment for contracting is a purchase price, not a wage, and *bal talin* does not apply. Rav Sheshet held that one *does* transgress *bal talin* even in *kablanut*, suggesting either that *uman* is not *koneh be-shevach kli* or that *bal talin* applies beyond its most literal domain. The *gemara* raises the question of whether Rav Sheshet's position conflicts with Rav Assi's ruling about the craftsman who built from raw materials.

### Service vs. Improvement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת פְּלִיגָא אַדְּרַב אַסִּי? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַחָא: בִּשְׁלִיחָא דְאִיגַּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** based on this statement **that Rav Sheshet disagrees with** the statement **of Rav Asi,** who holds that a craftsman acquires ownership rights through the enhancement of the vessel, and his payment is not considered to be a wage? **Shmuel bar Aḥa said:** Rav Sheshet is discussing a specific type of contractor, and he stated his ruling **with regard to an agent** tasked with the delivery **of a letter,** in which case, since the contractor's only task is to deliver the letter, there is no enhancement through which he might acquire ownership rights. Consequently the prohibition against delaying the payment of wages applies.

The resolution is offered by Shmuel bar Acha: *be-shlicha de-igarta* — consider a case of someone hired to deliver a letter. Here there is no physical object being improved. The messenger provides a pure service; there is no *shevach kli* whatsoever. And yet this is clearly a form of *kablanut* — you are paying for the completion of a task, not by the hour. In such a case, there is no *uman koneh be-shevach kli* to muddy the analysis, and Rav Sheshet's question about whether *bal talin* applies to *kablanut* stands on its own, entirely independent of the ownership-of-improvement framework. Rav Sheshet held that yes, *bal talin* applies — suggesting the obligation extends to service-based contracting even where no physical improvement is made.

## Gold Jewelry Kiddushin

### Jewelry Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי? ״עֲשֵׂה לִי שֵׁירִים, נְזָמִין וְטַבָּעוֹת, וְאֶקַּדֵּשׁ לָךְ״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ מָמוֹן לְיָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the statement of Rav Asi, that a craftsman acquires ownership rights through the enhancement of the vessel, is the **subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it is taught in a *baraita*: If a woman gave gold to a goldsmith, instructing him: **Fashion bracelets, earrings, or rings for me, and I will be betrothed to you** as payment for your work, then **once he has fashioned them she is betrothed;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: She is not betrothed until money enters her possession.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *tannaitic* dispute that intersects directly with the question of *uman koneh be-shevach kli*. A woman says to a man: *asei li shirim nezamim ve-taba'ot ve-kadeish li bach* — take my gold, fashion it into necklaces, rings, and nose rings, and with those finished pieces of jewelry, betroth me. The question is whether the betrothal — *kiddushin* — takes effect.

### Rabbi Meir Position

**Rabbi Meir** rules: *keivan she-asam, mekudeshet* — once the man has fashioned the jewelry, she is betrothed. The implicit reasoning appears to be that the man has given the woman something of value — either the improved jewelry itself, or the improved value he created in her gold, or something equivalent — and that constitutes a valid act of *kiddushin*.

### Rabbis Position

**The Rabbis** disagree: *einah mekudeshet ad she-yehei mamon mi-yado* — she is not betrothed until actual property passes from his hand into hers. Merely completing the work is insufficient.

### First Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מָמוֹן״? אִילֵּימָא אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן, מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן לָא?! אֶלָּא בְּמַאי [כּוּ] מִקַּדְּשָׁא? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא, מַאי ״מָמוֹן״ – מָמוֹן אַחֵר;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is this **money** mentioned by the Rabbis? **If we say** it means **that** very **money,** i.e., the rings she ordered, then **by inference Rabbi Meir holds** that even **that** very **money is not** required in order for the betrothal to take effect. **But with what** does **he betroth her?** He has given her nothing but the jewelry. **Rather, it is obvious.** To **what money** are the Rabbis referring? They must be referring to when he betroths her via **other money,** i.e., the payment she owes him for his service.

The *gemara*'s first reading of this dispute is that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis are debating whether *uman koneh be-shevach kli*. If the craftsman owns the improved value, then the man has something to give the woman — he owns a portion of her gold in its improved form, and by handing it over he is giving her that owned value. That constitutes *kiddushin*. If *uman* does not own the *shevach*, he has nothing to give her from his own domain; all he has done is discharge a labor obligation, and one cannot betroth a woman with a debt she owes you (*milveh*).

The *gemara* notes a subtlety: even according to Rabbi Meir, the jewelry must apparently be physically handed over — *mamon mi-yado* — which raises a question about why physical transfer is necessary if the man already owns the improvement. One possible answer is that he might have done the work on the object while it remained in her possession — if, for instance, the work was done on her premises without his ever taking custody of the gold. In that case, he could not have acquired the improvement through *kinyan*, and the question of *uman koneh be-shevach kli* may be moot regardless.

### Revised Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסַבְרוּהָ דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף; וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara prefaces its explanation of the connection to the issue at hand by delineating two assumptions: **And** those who say that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis are engaging in a dispute with regard to the issue of a craftsman acquiring ownership rights through the enhancement of a vessel **assumed** that **everyone** agrees that the obligation to pay **a wage** is incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end,** i.e., the obligation to pay for a service begins when the hired party commences work, and the sum owed increases as he proceeds. The fact that he is not paid from the time he commences work establishes the wages as a debt. Consequently, the wages now have the status of a loan. **And** they also assumed that **everyone** agrees that with regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan** that **she is not betrothed.**

The *gemara* ultimately revises this first reading. *De-khulei alma ein uman koneh be-shevach kli* — perhaps everyone agrees that the craftsman does *not* own the improvement. The debate is instead about something else entirely: *be-yeshna le-sekhirus mi-techila ve-ad sof kamifligi* — they disagree about whether wages accrue incrementally throughout the work or only become due at the moment of completion.

### Rabbi Meir: Final Wages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: אֵין לִשְׂכִירוּת אֶלָּא לְבַסּוֹף, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יֵשׁ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir holds** that the obligation to pay **a wage** is incurred **only at the end** of the period for which he was hired, i.e., the owner is obligated to pay only when the work is complete, and therefore the goldsmith's wages do not have the status of a loan, but of a sum of money that she becomes obligated to give him at that time. If he gives her the jewelry without asking for that money, it is as though he gave the money from his wages to her, and she may be betrothed with it. **And the Rabbis hold** that the obligation to pay **a wage** is incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end,** and therefore his wages have the status of a loan, and she cannot be betrothed with them and must be given additional money.

**Rabbi Meir** holds *ein le-sekhirus ela le-vasof* — the wage obligation arises only at the point of completion. Throughout the work, the owner owes the craftsman nothing. When the job is finished and the craftsman says, "I give you my labor as a gift rather than charging you," that gift has value — it is something concrete he is providing at that moment — and it can function as the object of *kiddushin*, worth at least a *perutah*.

### Rabbis: Accruing Wages

**The Rabbis** hold *yeshna le-sekhirus mi-techila ve-ad sof* — the wage accrues continuously throughout the work. By the time the job is complete, the owner has been accumulating a debt to the craftsman with every passing moment. When the craftsman says at the end, "I am not collecting my fee, accept my labor as a gift," what he is really forgiving is a *milveh* — a debt. And *ha-mekadesh be-milveh einah mekudeshet* — betrothal with a debt owed to you does not create valid *kiddushin*.

## Milveh U'Pruta

### Rava's Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף, וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֵין אוּמָּן קוֹנֶה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** another interpretation: **Everyone** agrees that the obligation to pay **a wage** is incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period a craftsman is hired **to** its **end; and everyone** agrees that with regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed; and everyone** agrees that **a craftsman does not acquire** ownership rights **through** the **enhancement of the vessel.**

Rava proposes a unified account that cuts across all three of the previously offered explanations. He holds that certain things are agreed upon by everyone: *de-khulei alma yeshna le-sekhirus mi-techila ve-ad sof* — wages accrue throughout, and by the time the work is done, what exists is an accrued debt. *De-khulei alma mekadesh be-milveh einah mekudeshet* — everyone agrees you cannot betroth a woman with a debt. *De-khulei alma ein uman koneh be-shevach kli* — and everyone agrees the craftsman does not acquire ownership of the improvement. With all three of those propositions agreed upon, what is left to debate?

### Overshadowing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוֹסִיף לָהּ נוֹפֶךְ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה – דַּעְתַּהּ אַפְּרוּטָה, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה – דַּעְתַּהּ אַמִּלְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he added a jewel [*nofekh*] of his own for her. Rabbi Meir holds** that if a man betroths a woman with **a loan and one *peruta*, her mind is** focused **on the *peruta*.** Therefore, in this case the jewel serves as the betrothal money. **And the Rabbis hold** that if a man betroths a woman with **a loan and one *peruta* her mind is** focused **on the loan,** so the loan serves as the betrothal money, and if one betroths a woman with a loan, she is not betrothed.

The remaining dispute is about *milveh u-perutah* — the case where a man simultaneously forgives a debt *and* gives the woman something extra, something concrete and personal, as the instrument of *kiddushin*. In our case, the man was commissioned to make jewelry, completed the task, and then added his own personal extra flourish — *nofech mi-shelo* — something beyond what was agreed, a small personal artistic addition that was not part of the contract.

### Rabbi Meir: Extra Matters

**Rabbi Meir** holds *milveh u-perutah daata a-perutah* — when there is both a debt being forgiven and a concrete item being given, a person's attention and intent focus on the tangible, present gift. The debt being forgiven does not overshadow the concrete *perutah* being given. Accordingly, the woman can be betrothed by that extra personal improvement that the man contributed of his own initiative.

### Rabbis: Debt Overshadows

**The Rabbis** hold *milveh u-perutah daata a-milveh* — when the debt is the dominant element, the concrete item is psychologically overshadowed. The woman knows that the primary transaction here is the forgiveness of a hundred-dollar debt; the extra little touch cannot transform that dynamic into a valid act of *kiddushin*.

## Work Betrothal Dispute

### Past Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמָּךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis disagree **in the dispute** between **these *tanna'im*. As it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Kiddushin* 3:4): If one says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **with the payment for which I have worked for you, she is not betrothed,** as the payment is a loan, since she already owes him this money. But if he says: Be betrothed to me **with the payment for which I will work for you, she is betrothed,** as from the moment he is entitled to the money, he gives it to her for her betrothal. **Rabbi Natan says:** If he says: Be betrothed to me **with the payment for which I will work for you, she is not betrothed,** as Rabbi Natan holds that the obligation to pay a wage is incurred continuously from the beginning of the period he was hired to its end, which means that upon the completion of the labor it is a loan, **and all the more so** if he says: Be betrothed to me **with the payment for which I have worked for you.**

The *gemara* identifies this dispute as parallel to a debate among other *tannaim*. The case: a man says to a woman, *be-sachar she-asiti imach* — "I betroth you in exchange for the work I have already done for you." She is not betrothed. The work was done, the debt accrued, the man is forgiving a *milveh* — and one cannot be *mekadesh* with a *milveh*.

### Future Work

If instead he says *be-sachar she-e'eseh imach* — "in exchange for work I will do for you" — she *is* betrothed. Here the first *tanna* holds that this works.

### Rabbi Natan Rejects Both

**Rabbi Natan** disagrees: *be-sachar she-e'eseh imach einah mekudeshet ve-khol shekein be-sachar she-asiti imach* — neither the future-work formulation nor the past-work formulation creates valid *kiddushin*.

### Extra Improvement Except…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא אוֹמֵר, בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: בֵּין ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמָּךְ״, וּבֵין ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאִם הוֹסִיף לָהּ נוֹפֶךְ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* cites a third opinion: **And Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says: Actually they said** that the *halakha* is that regardless of **whether** he said: **With the payment for which I have worked for you, or whether** he said: **With the payment for which I will work for you, she is not betrothed. But if he added a jewel of his own for her, she is betrothed.**

However, Rabbi Natan concedes: *aval im hosif lah nofech mi-shelo, mekudeshet* — if the craftsman added a personal extra improvement beyond what was contracted, she is betrothed with that addition.

### Wages Accrual Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְרַבִּי נָתָן? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שְׂכִירוּת. בֵּין רַבִּי נָתָן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dispute: **What** difference **is there between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Natan?** The difference **between them** is with regard to **a wage:** Is the obligation incurred continuously or only upon the completion of the work? The difference **between Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi** is the issue of **a loan and one *peruta*.** Rabbi Natan holds that if a man betroths a woman with a loan and one *peruta* her mind is focused on the loan, and in this case his jewel is disregarded; and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that her mind is focused on the *peruta*, in this case the jewel, and she is betrothed with the jewel.

The *gemara* identifies what divides the first *tanna* from Rabbi Natan: *ika beinaihu sekhirus* — the question of wage accrual. The first *tanna* holds that the obligation to pay arises only at the end — *ein le-sekhirus ela le-vasof* — so "work I will do for you" is a genuine gift of future labor, not a preemptive forgiveness of an accruing debt. Rabbi Natan holds *yeshna le-sekhirus mi-techila ve-ad sof*, so by the time the work is completed, even future-promised work has become an accrued debt. As for the case of adding a personal extra improvement, the dispute between Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is precisely the *milveh u-perutah* question: does the extra concrete personal contribution overshadow the underlying debt, or does the debt overshadow the extra contribution?

## Return to Mazik

### Groundwork Complete

At this point a great deal of groundwork has been laid. We have worked through the *shinui* analysis of stolen goods, the conditions under which *harei she-lecha lefanecha* applies even to *issurei hana'ah*, and the *uman koneh be-shevach kli* principle with its implications for *bal talin*, *kablanut*, and *kiddushin*. In one sense, this has been the equivalent of Jughead's lawnmower: the *gemara* needed all of this to get to where it was actually going. The *mishnah* on craftsmen who damage objects is fundamentally a *mazik* issue — under what circumstances is a craftsman who damages something in the course of his work treated as a *mazik* and held to full liability — and that analysis awaits.

### Next: Grama

The *gemara* will now return to that direct *mazik* question and to the cases of *grama* — indirect damage — that were anticipated in the preceding pages. Rav Assi's new ruling, which appears at the top of the next amud, opens: *ha-me'abed kradomo shel chaveiro, amar lei ha kardumach ve-zil* — one who loses his fellow's axe says to him, "Here is your axe, go." The *gemara* immediately asks *ke-man* — like which *tanna* does this ruling accord? *Leima Rabbi Meir hi* — shall we say this is Rabbi Meir's view, who judges cases of *dina de-garmi*, the laws of indirect causation of damage? This exchange will open the final major discussion of the daf.
