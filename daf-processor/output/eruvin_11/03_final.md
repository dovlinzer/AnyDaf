# Eruvin 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Doorframe & Eruv

### Mishna: Tzurat Hapetach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁרָחָב מֵעֶשֶׂר — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט. אַשְׁכְּחַן צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח דְּמַהְנְיָא בְּרַחְבּוֹ, וַאֲמַלְתְּרָא דְּמַהְנְיָא בְּגׇבְהוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the entrance to the alleyway **has** an opening in the **form of a doorway,** then, **even if it is wider than ten** cubits, **one need not diminish** its width. The Gemara comments: **We find** that an opening in the **form of a doorway is effective** to permit carrying in an alleyway **with** regard to **its width,** i.e., when its entrance is more than ten cubits wide, **and** that **a cornice is effective with** regard to **its height,** i.e., when it is more than twenty cubits high.

The daf begins at the very bottom of 10b, returning once again to the *mishna*. The *mishna* has already established that a *mavoi* cannot be higher than twenty *amot* or wider than ten *amot*, and now turns to the question of how to remedy those deficiencies. The key principle introduced is: *im yesh lo tzurat hapetach* — if the opening possesses a doorframe, consisting of two posts with a beam across the top, then *af al pi sherachav me'eser ein tzarich lema'et* — even if it is wider than ten *amot*, one need not reduce it. The *tzurat hapetach* defines the entire opening as a doorway by virtue of its frame, regardless of width.

### Modern Eruv Setup

This ruling carries enormous practical significance, for it is precisely this principle upon which contemporary *eruv* construction relies. The standard method of creating an *eruv* in a city involves affixing beams against telephone poles and using the telephone wire as the upper beam, thereby forming *tzurot hapetach* of vast dimensions that encircle an entire urban area. The legitimacy of this practice rests squarely on the assumption that a *tzurat hapetach* remains valid even when wider than ten *amot*.

### Rambam's Limits

It should be noted, however, that the *Rambam* rules that a *tzurat hapetach* does not function for openings wider than ten *amot*. According to his position, the construction of a contemporary *eruv* in the standard manner would simply not be permissible — an extraordinarily stringent consequence. The *Rambam*'s source for this ruling will become clear as the *Gemara* unfolds.

## Doorframe Efficacy

### Height Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיפְּכָא מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע דְּתַנְיָא: מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא גָּבוֹהַּ מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה יְמַעֵט, וְאִם יֵשׁ לוֹ צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* in **the opposite** situation? Does an opening in the form of a doorway also serve to permit carrying in an alleyway that is more than twenty cubits high? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** the answer to this question, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: If the cross beam placed over the entrance to **an alleyway is higher than twenty cubits, one must diminish** its height, **but if** the entrance **has an opening in the form of a doorway, he need not diminish it.**

The *Gemara* on 11a opens by noting what is already established: *ashkachan tzurat hapetach d'mahani berachvah* — we have seen that a *tzurat hapetach* helps when the *mavoi* is too wide. Likewise, from the earlier discussion at the beginning of the *masechet*, we saw that an *amaltara* — the decorative cornice carved into the upper corners of a frame — helps when the *mavoi* is too high, exceeding twenty *amot*, since the ornamentation draws the eye upward and thereby provides the necessary visual recognition. The *Gemara* now asks: *ipcha mai* — what of the reverse cases? Will a *tzurat hapetach* help when the *mavoi* is too high, and will an *amaltara* help when it is too wide?

The logic behind the uncertainty is instructive. The problem of excessive height is arguably one of visibility — the cornice addresses it by making the threshold visually salient. It is not obvious that a *tzurat hapetach* would resolve this, since the *tzurat hapetach* functions by defining the space as a doorway, not by drawing the eye. Conversely, a cornice that addresses a visibility problem may not help when the issue is that the opening is simply too expansive to be conceptualized as a door.

A *baraita* is then cited: *mavoi she'hu gavo'ah me'esrim amah yema'et* — a *mavoi* exceeding twenty *amot* in height must be lowered; *v'im yesh lo tzurat hapetach eino tzarich lema'et* — but if it has a doorframe, it need not be. This is a striking ruling. Perhaps the reasoning is that if the frame itself ascends the sides of the opening, it encourages the viewer to look all the way up. Alternatively, perhaps the entire framework of excessive height is not fundamentally about visibility at all, but rather about defining the space as a doorway — and a *tzurat hapetach* accomplishes exactly that, irrespective of height.

### Reciprocal Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַלְתְּרָא בְּרׇחְבּוֹ מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא גָּבוֹהַּ מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה יְמַעֵט, וְהָרָחָב מֵעֶשֶׂר יְמַעֵט, וְאִם יֵשׁ לוֹ צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט, וְאִם יֵשׁ לוֹ אֲמַלְתְּרָא אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the effect of **a cornice with** regard to the need to diminish the alleyway's **width?** Does the cornice render the alleyway fit for one to carry within it, even if the entrance is more than ten cubits wide? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** an answer **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: If the cross beam placed over the entrance to **an alleyway is higher than twenty cubits, one must diminish** its height, **and** if the alleyway is **wider than ten** cubits, **one must diminish** its width. **However, if** the entrance to the alleyway **has an opening in the form of a doorway, he need not diminish it, and,** similarly, **if it has a cornice, he need not diminish it.**

The *Gemara* then poses the question in the other direction: *amtzur berachvo mai* — does the cornice help when the problem is excessive width? The *baraita* cited lists the cases: a *mavoi* too high must be lowered, a *mavoi* too wide must be narrowed, but if there is a *tzurat hapetach*, no reduction is needed — and similarly if there is an *amaltara*, no reduction is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו אַסֵּיפָא: לָא, אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof it wishes to adduce from this *baraita*: **What, is** this statement with regard to the cornice **not** referring **to the latter** clause, i.e., the case of an alleyway that is wider than ten cubits, proving that a cornice can render an alleyway otherwise too wide fit for one to carry within it? The Gemara refutes this argument: **No,** this statement is referring **to the first** clause of the *baraita*, that a cornice is effective for an alleyway more than twenty cubits high, but it tells us nothing about one that is more than ten cubits wide.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai lav aseifa* — is this not referring to the final case, meaning the cornice helps when the *mavoi* is too wide? The *Gemara* deflects: *lo, areisha* — not necessarily. Perhaps the *tzurat hapetach* is speaking to the too-wide case, while the *amaltara* is speaking to the too-high case, and the passage tells us nothing about whether a cornice resolves excessive width. The argument for saying it should not is compelling: there is no obvious logic by which a decorative cornice element would address the fundamental problem that an opening is too expansive to count as a doorway.

### Combined Solutions

What the *Gemara* has established with confidence is that a *tzurat hapetach* works not only when the *mavoi* is too wide, but even when it is too high — a conclusion of great practical relevance for the use of *tzurot hapetach* in modern *eruv* construction.

### Tosafot Challenge

*Tosafot* raises an interesting question at this point. Why, *Tosafot* asks, would one need a *tzurat hapetach* to solve the problem of excessive height? After all, a *tzurat hapetach* consists of a post on either side and a beam above. If there are posts on both sides, those posts themselves constitute a *lechi* — a side post — and a *lechi* should be sufficient for a *mavoi*. Why does it matter that the beam is higher than twenty *amot*?

*Tosafot* offers two answers. The first is that perhaps the posts are too thin to qualify as a valid *lechi* according to some opinion that requires a minimum thickness. The second, and more significant answer, is that the *baraita* may be addressing a scenario where a *lechi* alone would not suffice in the first place — such as an outdoor space or a broader configuration where a full *tzurat hapetach* is required and a *lechi* would be inadequate. In such a case, the point is precisely that even a *tzurat hapetach* — which might have been thought insufficient at a height greater than twenty *amot* — remains valid.

## Rav's Restriction

### Rav's Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְנְיֵיהּ, צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the same issue, **Rav Yehuda would teach** the *baraita* to **Ḥiyya bar Rav before Rav** as follows: If the entrance to an alleyway that is wider than ten cubits has an opening in the form of a doorway, **he need not diminish** its width. Rav **said to him: Teach him** that the correct version of the *baraita* is: **He must diminish** its width.

The *Gemara* now introduces a major dispute. *Matni lah Rav Yehuda le-Chiyya bar Rav kamei de-Rav* — Rav Yehuda was teaching *Chiyya bar Rav*, the son of Rav, in the presence of Rav himself, and he taught our *mishna* as it stands: *eino tzarich lema'et* — that if there is a *tzurat hapetach*, one need not reduce the opening. Rav intervened and corrected him: *atneiye tzarich lema'et* — teach it to him the other way, that even with a *tzurat hapetach*, one must still reduce the opening. According to Rav, the *mishna*'s ruling that a *tzurat hapetach* exempts one from reduction does not apply when the opening exceeds ten *amot*; the *tzurat hapetach* simply does not function in that case. This, as noted, is the ruling adopted by the *Rambam*.

### Rav Yosef's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִדִּבְרֵי רַבֵּינוּ נִלְמַד: חָצֵר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ פְּתָחִים וְחַלּוֹנוֹת — אֵינָהּ נִיתֶּרֶת בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: From the statement of our teacher,** Rav, who said that the entrance to an alleyway must be diminished even if it has an opening in the form of a doorway, **we will learn** that with regard to **a courtyard,** the walls of **which are mostly entrances and windows, it is not permitted** to carry within it even **by** having an opening in **the form of a doorway.** Even if the entrances have an opening in the form of a doorway, this does not render a mostly breached courtyard wall into a closed wall.

Rav Yosef proceeds to draw a further implication from Rav's position: *midivrei Rabbeinu nilmad* — from the words of our master we can derive that *chatzer she-rubba petachim ve-chalonot* — a courtyard the majority of whose perimeter consists of doorways, windows, and openings — presents a condition of *parutz merubba al ha-omed*, where more is breached than stands. Can such a courtyard be enclosed by means of a *tzurat hapetach*? Rav Yosef argues that from Rav's ruling it follows that *einan nitarot be-tzurat hapetach* — even if each individual opening is less than ten *amot*, a *tzurat hapetach* does not resolve the problem of majority exposure.

One should pause here to appreciate the stakes. Contemporary *eruvin* rely not only on the leniency that a *tzurat hapetach* can span more than ten *amot*, but also on the leniency that a *tzurat hapetach* can serve to enclose a space that is overwhelmingly open — where the vast majority of the perimeter consists of nothing but air. According to Rav and Rav Yosef's reading of him, both of these leniencies are denied.

### Logic of Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא — הוֹאִיל וְיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר אוֹסֵר בְּמָבוֹי, וּפָרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד אוֹסֵר בֶּחָצֵר. מָה יוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר הָאוֹסֵר בְּמָבוֹי — אֵינוֹ נִיתָּר בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח, אַף פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד הָאוֹסֵר בֶּחָצֵר — אֵינוֹ נִיתָּר בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason? Since** an opening of **more than ten** cubits **renders it prohibited** for one to carry **in an alleyway, and** likewise when the **breached** segment of a wall that **is greater than its standing** segment **renders it prohibited** for one to carry **in a courtyard,** the following claim can be made: **Just as,** according to Rav, in the case of an opening **more than ten** cubits wide **that renders it prohibited** for one to carry **in an alleyway,** carrying in the alleyway **is not permitted by the form of a doorway** in the opening, **so** too a case where **breached** segment of a wall **is greater than its standing** segment **that renders it prohibited** for one to carry **in a courtyard,** carrying in the courtyard **is not permitted by the form of a doorway** in the opening.

What is the logical basis for Rav's position? The *Gemara* articulates it: *ho'il ve-yoter me-eser oser be-mavoi* — a gap of more than ten *amot* is inherently problematic in the context of a *mavoi*, and *parutz merubba al ha-omed oser be-chatzer* — majority exposure is inherently problematic in the context of a *chatzer*. Rav's argument by analogy is: just as a *tzurat hapetach* cannot resolve a gap exceeding ten *amot*, so too it cannot resolve the condition of majority exposure. Both are fundamental structural deficiencies that a *tzurat hapetach*, however elegantly formed, cannot overcome.

One can understand this conceptually. The *tzurat hapetach* functions by defining a space as a doorway. One could argue that if an opening is too large, it simply cannot be credibly characterized as a doorway regardless of the frame around it — it is a breach, not a door. By extension, when the majority of the enclosure is breached, even individually valid *tzurot hapetach* may not suffice to transform the overall space into an enclosed domain.

And yet the conceptual distinction between the two problems is also worth noting. The objection to a gap wider than ten *amot* is local: this specific opening is too large to be called a doorway. The objection of *parutz merubba al ha-omed* is global: there is too much unenclosed space overall. Once one installs a *tzurat hapetach* in a particular gap and successfully defines that gap as a doorway, it is no longer treated as a breach but as a passage through a wall — and if every gap receives a valid *tzurat hapetach*, the problem of majority exposure disappears. This is the argument for saying that *parutz merubba al ha-omed* can be resolved by *tzurot hapetach* even if the too-wide problem cannot.

### Gemara Pushback

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר הָאוֹסֵר בְּמָבוֹי — שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הִתַּרְתָּ בּוֹ אֵצֶל פַּסֵּי בִירָאוֹת לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, תֹּאמַר בְּפָרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד הָאוֹסֵר בֶּחָצֵר — שֶׁכֵּן הִתַּרְתָּ אֵצֶל פַּסֵּי בִירָאוֹת לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **What** is the basis for comparison to an opening that is **more than ten** cubits wide **that renders it prohibited** for one to carry **in an alleyway?** It is not permitted by having an opening in the form of a doorway **because you did not permit** an opening of that size **with regard to** the case of upright **boards surrounding a well, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** Can **you say** the same **in a** case where the **breached** segment of a wall is **greater than the standing** segment **that renders it prohibited** for one to carry **in a courtyard,** that carrying in the courtyard will not be permitted by the form of a doorway? That situation is clearly not as severe a problem, **as you permitted** carrying **with regard to** upright **boards surrounding a well according to everyone.** Consequently, no comparison can be made between the case of an opening wider than ten cubits in an alleyway and a partition in which the breached segment is greater than the standing segment in a courtyard.

The *Gemara* pushes back against Rav Yosef's inference. The argument is as follows: the reason a *tzurat hapetach* might not help for a gap exceeding ten *amot* is that we are particularly strict about large gaps. The evidence for this strictness comes from the *patzei birot* — the corner posts surrounding a public well. According to Rabbi Meir, even in the case of the *patzei birot*, where leniency regarding majority exposure is granted by universal consensus, the individual gaps may not exceed ten *amot*. This shows that the ten-*amot* limit is a deeply embedded strictness, cutting across other leniencies.

By contrast, the problem of *parutz merubba al ha-omed* is one where we are comparatively lenient. The *patzei birot* themselves demonstrate this: the entire structure consists of only four corner posts with the majority entirely open, yet everyone agrees it is valid. *Tomar be-farutz merubba al ha-omed* — will you make the same strict argument when the problem is majority exposure? *Ha-oseh be-chatzer, sheken hutra etzlo patzei birot, ledivrei hakol* — this is a recognized area of leniency, so perhaps a *tzurat hapetach* can indeed resolve it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: דְּפָנוֹת הַלָּלוּ שֶׁרוּבָּן פְּתָחִים וְחַלּוֹנוֹת מוּתָּר, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports** this opinion that the form of a doorway is ineffective in a case where the breached segments of a wall are greater than its standing segments: With regard to the area enclosed by **these walls that most of them** consist of **entrances and windows,** it is **permitted** to carry on Shabbat therein, **provided that the standing** segments **are greater than the breached** segments.

The *Gemara* then attempts to bring a proof that a *tzurat hapetach* cannot resolve majority exposure from a passage about walls with numerous doorways and windows. The *Gemara* cites a teaching that walls with many openings are acceptable *u'vilvad she-omed merubba al ha-parutz* — provided that the solid portion predominates. This seems to imply that if the majority were openings, even doorways with *tzurot hapetach*, it would not be valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁרוּבָּן סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? — אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶן פְּתָחִים וְחַלּוֹנוֹת, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first analyzes the wording of the *baraita*: The Gemara analyzes the formulation of the *baraita*: **Can it enter your mind that** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where most of** the walls are entrances and windows? If so, the standing segments are not greater than the breached segments. **Rather,** emend the *baraita*: Carrying in the area enclosed by these walls **to which he added** many **entrances and windows is permitted, provided that the standing** segments **are greater than the breached** segments. Apparently, if the breached segments are greater than the standing segments, carrying is not permitted even if the breaches are in the form of doorways.

Rav Kahana deflects this by explaining that the teaching refers to *pichai shimai* — ruined or deformed openings that lack a proper frame. The debate between Rav Rechumi and Rav Yosef as to the precise meaning of *pichai shimai* — whether it means lacking proper side posts or lacking a lintel — converges on the conclusion that such openings do not constitute valid *tzurot hapetach* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בְּפִיתְחֵי שִׁימָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said** that his is not an absolute proof: **When this** *baraita* **was taught,** it was taught **with regard to broken entrances** [*pitḥei shima'ei*] that lack the proper form of doorways.

If one actually installs proper *tzurot hapetach*, the teaching presents no obstacle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי פִּיתְחֵי שִׁימָאֵי? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב רְחוּמִי וְרַב יוֹסֵף. חַד אָמַר: דְּלֵית לְהוּ שִׁקְפֵי, וְחַד אָמַר: דְּלֵית לְהוּ תִּיקְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are broken entrances? Rav Reḥumei and Rav Yosef disagreed** on the matter. **One said that they do not have** proper **doorposts, and** the other **one said that they do not have lintels** above the openings.

The *Gemara* ultimately notes: *v'af Rav Yochanan savar lah l'ha de-Rav* — even Rabbi Yochanan seems to hold like Rav, that a *tzurat hapetach* does not work for a gap wider than ten *amot*. The narrative that follows will both illustrate and complicate this claim.

## Vineyard Case

### Beit Chortan Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַב. דְּאָמַר רָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד מִבִּקְעַת בֵּית חוֹרְתָן שֶׁנָּעַץ אַרְבָּעָה קוּנְדֵּיסִין בְּאַרְבַּע פִּינּוֹת הַשָּׂדֶה, וּמָתַח זְמוֹרָה עֲלֵיהֶם. וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים, וְהִתִּירוּ לוֹ לְעִנְיַן כִּלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And even Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that** opinion **of Rav,** that an opening in the form of a doorway does not permit carrying if the opening is more than ten cubits wide. **As Ravin bar Rav Adda said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** There was **an incident involving a person from the valley of Beit Ḥortan who stuck four poles** [***kunddeisin***] into the ground **in the four corners of** his **field, and stretched a vine over them,** creating the form of a doorway on each side. He intended to seal the area so that he would be permitted to plant a vineyard in close proximity without creating a forbidden mixture of diverse kinds in a vineyard. **And the case came before the Sages, and they permitted him** to consider it sealed **with regard to diverse kinds.**

A story is introduced via the chain: *d'amar Ravin bar Rav Adda amar Rav Yitzchak* — Ravin bar Rav Adda says in the name of Rav Yitzchak: there was a certain man from the valley of *Beit Chortan* who drove four posts into the four corners of a field and stretched a vine across the tops of them — *shenatz arba kundisin b'arba pinot hasadeh u-matach zmora aleihem* — thereby creating, in effect, four *tzurot hapetach* enclosing a space that was entirely open. His intention was to use this enclosure to permit planting of *kilayim*, diverse species, within the space immediately adjacent to his vines. This is an astonishing application, for it means using *tzurot hapetach* to enclose a space that is, in its entirety, exposed on all sides — precisely the structure we use today for urban *eruvin*.

### Kilayim vs Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ לְעִנְיַן כִּלְאַיִם כָּךְ הִתִּירוּ לוֹ לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְכִלְאַיִם הִתִּירוּ לוֹ, לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת לֹא הִתִּירוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish said: Just as they permitted him** to consider it sealed **with regard to diverse kinds, so** too **they permitted him** to consider it sealed **with regard to Shabbat,** i.e., they permitted carrying within this area. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: With regard to diverse kinds, they permitted him** to consider it sealed, however, **with regard to Shabbat, they did not permit him** to do so.

Reish Lakish then drew the broader conclusion: *keshem she-hitiru lo l'inyan kilayim kach hitiru lo l'inyan Shabbat* — just as they permitted this for *kilayim*, so too would they permit it for *Shabbat*. Rabbi Yochanan disagreed: *l'kilayim hitiru lo, u'l'inyan Shabbat lo hitiru lo* — for *kilayim* it was permitted, but not for *Shabbat*.

The *Gemara* now asks: what was the critical feature of the case that generated this dispute? Two possible explanations present themselves. First, perhaps the vine was wrapped around the side of the posts rather than stretched directly over the top — *min hatzad* — in which case the issue is not the width of the enclosure but the position of the beam. The *Gemara* immediately rejects this as the basis of Reish Lakish's leniency: Rav Chisda has already ruled that *tzurat hapetach she-asah min hatzad lo asah ve-lo klum* — a *tzurat hapetach* in which the upper element runs along the side rather than directly over the top is entirely worthless, and Reish Lakish could not have endorsed it. Therefore, the vine must have run over the top — *al gaban* — and the dispute must have concerned something else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא מִן הַצַּד, וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ מִן הַצַּד לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **With what** case **are we dealing** here? **If you say** that he draped the vines on the posts **from the side,** rather than on top of them, **didn't Rav Ḥisda say** with regard to Shabbat: If **one constructed** an opening in **the form of a doorway from the side, he has done nothing?**

The *Gemara* considers whether the gap was less than or more than ten *amot*. If it was less than ten, there is no apparent reason for Rabbi Yochanan to invalidate it for *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* therefore suggests: *ela lav beyoser mi-eser* — the gap must have exceeded ten *amot*, and this is the crux of the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan: does a *tzurat hapetach* remain valid for *Shabbat* when the opening is wider than ten *amot*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עַל גַּבָּן. וּבְמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּעֶשֶׂר — בְּהָא לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּשַׁבָּת לָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that he set the vines **on top of** the posts. **And in what** circumstances did Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish disagree? **If you say** that the posts were set at a distance **of ten** cubits from each other, **would Rabbi Yoḥanan say in that** case that **with regard to Shabbat,** they did **not** permit him to consider the area sealed? Everyone agrees that the form of a doorway is effective for an entrance that is only ten cubits wide.

### Reconciling Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו — בְּיָתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't** it referring to a case **where** the posts were **more than ten** cubits apart? Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan agrees with Rav, that an opening in the form of a doorway does not permit carrying if the original opening is wider than ten cubits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם בְּעֶשֶׂר וּמִן הַצַּד — וּבִדְרַב חִסְדָּא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **No, actually** it is referring to a case **where** the posts were **ten** cubits apart, **and** the person attached the vines to the posts **from the side. And** Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree with regard to** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda.** Reish Lakish maintains that the form of a doorway is effective even when the horizontal cross beam is attached to the vertical posts from the side, and Rabbi Yoḥanan agrees with Rav Ḥisda that a form of a doorway is ineffective for the purpose of carrying on Shabbat when constructed in such a manner.

The *Gemara* then discovers a contradiction internal to each authority's position. A separate *baraita* presents Reish Lakish saying — not in his own name, but in the name of Rabbi Yehuda b'Rabbi Chanina — that a certain type of braiding or stringing (*pe'ah*) works for *kilayim* but not for *Shabbat*. Rabbi Yochanan in this same passage says it works for neither. This contradicts their positions in the *Beit Chortan* case, where Reish Lakish permitted both and Rabbi Yochanan permitted *kilayim* but not *Shabbat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּרְמִי דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אַדְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: פֵּיאָה מוּתֶּרֶת לְעִנְיַן כִּלְאַיִם, אֲבָל לֹא לְשַׁבָּת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כִּמְחִיצּוֹת לְשַׁבָּת דְּלָא, כָּךְ מְחִיצּוֹת לְכִלְאַיִם דְּלֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But** it is possible to **raise a contradiction** between this statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan and** another statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan; and** it is possible to **raise a contradiction** between this statement **of Reish Lakish and** another statement **of Reish Lakish. As Reish Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥanina:** **A braid** of vines plaited around poles to form a partition **is permitted with regard to diverse kinds,** i.e., it is considered a partition that renders planting grapevines in close proximity to other crops permitted, **but not with regard to Shabbat. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Just as** such a braid is **not** considered a **partition with regard to Shabbat, so** too it is **not** considered **a partition with regard to diverse kinds.** Their opinions in the dispute here apparently contradict their opinions in the dispute cited above.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אַדְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דִידֵיהּ, הָא — דְרַבֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** the apparent contradiction between one statement **of Reish Lakish** and the other statement **of Reish Lakish** poses **no difficulty, as this** statement, according to which such a braid of vines is an effective partition even with regard to Shabbat, reflects **his** own opinion; **that** statement, according to which it is an effective partition only with regard to diverse kinds, reflects the opinion **of his teacher,** Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥanina. **However,** the apparent contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan and** the other statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** poses **a difficulty.**

The resolution for Reish Lakish is straightforward: *ha deidei ha derabeih* — in the first instance he spoke in his own name; in the second, he transmitted the opinion of his teacher. These need not be consistent. The contradiction in Rabbi Yochanan's position is more difficult.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: הָתָם — עַל גַּבָּן, הָכָא — מִן הַצַּד, שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי מִן הַצַּד, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that **there,** where Rabbi Yoḥanan ruled that a braid of vines is an effective partition with regard to diverse kinds, it is referring to a case where the vines were placed **on top of** the posts, while **here,** where he rules that it is ineffective even with regard to diverse kinds, it is referring to a case where they were attached to the posts **from the side,** it works out **well. However, if you say** that both **this and that** are cases where the vines were attached **from the side, what is there to say?**

The *Gemara* proposes: if we say that in the first case (where Rabbi Yochanan permitted *kilayim* but not *Shabbat*) the vine was *al gaban*, running over the top, then his objection to *Shabbat* would be because the opening exceeded ten *amot*. In the second case (where he permits neither), the vine was *min hatzad*, running along the side — and that disqualifies even *kilayim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי מִן הַצַּד: הָתָם — בְּעֶשֶׂר, הָכָא — בְּיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** both **this and that** are cases where the vines were attached to the side posts **from the side. There,** where Rabbi Yoḥanan ruled that the braid is an effective partition with regard to diverse kinds, it is referring to a case **where** the poles were only **ten** cubits apart; **here,** where he rules that it is ineffective even with regard to diverse kinds, it is referring to a case **where** the poles were **more than ten** cubits apart.

### Beam Position Key

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין עֶשֶׂר לְיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר — דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה הַמַּעֲשֶׂה, שֶׁהָלַךְ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֵצֶל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבָּקִי בְּהִלְכוֹת כִּלְאַיִם, וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב בֵּין הָאִילָנוֹת וּמָתַח זְמוֹרָה מֵאִילָן לְאִילָן. וְאָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, אִי גְּפָנִים כָּאן מַהוּ לִזְרוֹעַ כָּאן? אָמַר לוֹ: בְּעֶשֶׂר מוּתָּר, בְּיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say that we distinguish between** an opening of **ten** cubits **and** an opening of **more than ten** cubits? **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Reish Lakish: That is not** the way that **the incident** transpired. **As Rabbi Yehoshua went to Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri to study Torah, even though** Rabbi Yehoshua himself was an **expert in the *halakhot* of diverse kinds and found him sitting among the trees, and** Rabbi Yehoshua **stretched a vine from one tree to another and said to him: Rabbi, if there are grapevines here,** in the enclosed area, **what** is the *halakha* with regard to **sowing** diverse kinds of seeds **here,** on the other side of the partition? Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **said to him:** In a case **where** the trees are only **ten** cubits apart, it is **permitted;** however, **where** they are **more than ten** cubits apart, it is **prohibited.**

The *Gemara* then considers whether the debate in the first case could equally be read as concerning the position of the vine rather than the width of the opening: perhaps even in the *Beit Chortan* case the vine ran along the side, and it is *that* which Rabbi Yochanan objected to for *Shabbat*, while Reish Lakish was lenient enough to permit a *min hatzad* arrangement for *Shabbat*. If so, the second case, where the vine was *min hatzad* and larger than ten *amot*, would explain why even Reish Lakish — who in his own name permits *min hatzad* for *Shabbat* — would say it fails for *kilayim* when it exceeds ten *amot*, following the stricter view transmitted from his teacher.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא עַל גַּבָּן יוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר אָסוּר — וְהָתַנְיָא: הָיוּ שָׁם קָנִין הַדּוֹקְרָנִין וְעָשָׂה לָהֶן פֵּיאָה מִלְמַעְלָה, אֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר מוּתָּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the case: **With what are we dealing** here? **If you say** that the vines were placed **on top of** the trees, when they are **more than ten** cubits apart **is it prohibited? But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to diverse kinds: **If there were forked reeds there and he plaited a braid** of vines **above them,** then **even** if the reeds were set **more than ten** cubits apart, **it is permitted?** With regard to diverse kinds, the form of a doorway when properly constructed is certainly effective.

The *Gemara* brings proof for the conclusion that *kilayim* is indeed more lenient than *Shabbat* in this domain: *hatanya, hayu sham kanim ha-dukranin ve-asah lahen pe'ah milmala, afilu b'yoter me-eser mutar* — an explicit *baraita* states that posts with a cord running over the top permit planting of diverse species even when the width exceeds ten *amot*. This demonstrates that for *kilayim*, the ten-*amot* threshold does not apply when the upper element runs directly over the top.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו מִן הַצַּד, וְקָאֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּעֶשֶׂר מוּתָּר, יוֹתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר אָסוּר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** is it **not** referring to a case where he attached the vines to the trees **from the side, and he is saying to him: In** a case where the trees are only **ten** cubits apart, it is **permitted;** however, **in** a case where the trees are **more than ten** cubits apart, **it is prohibited?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that there is a distinction between poles that are ten cubits apart and poles that are more than ten cubits apart, and that this distinction resolves the contradiction between the two statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

Given this explicit permission for *kilayim* at widths exceeding ten *amot*, the distinction drawn by Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri — who said that up to ten is permitted and beyond ten is forbidden — must be referring to a case where the cord ran *min hatzad*. Only in that degraded configuration does the width become significant even for *kilayim*. The *Gemara* concludes: *ela lav shma mina ela lav min hatzad* — derive from this that when the restriction applies, it is because the cord ran along the side, and even there, up to ten is acceptable and beyond ten is not. *Shma mina* — this is a sound conclusion.

The overall picture that emerges: for *kilayim*, a classic *tzurat hapetach* with the beam running directly overhead is valid regardless of width. When the beam runs *min hatzad*, the width becomes relevant even for *kilayim*: below ten *amot* it may still work; above ten *amot* it does not. For *Shabbat*, the requirement that the beam run directly overhead is absolute. Whether a gap exceeding ten *amot* disqualifies a properly formed *tzurat hapetach* for *Shabbat* remains the great dispute: Rav says it does, the unqualified *mishna* says it does not, and Rabbi Yochanan's position resists the *Gemara*'s attempts to align it definitively with Rav. The *Rambam* rules with Rav; current practice follows the *mishna*'s plain reading, and our *eruvin* depend on both the leniency of width exceeding ten *amot* and the leniency of majority exposure.

## Archway & Mezuzah

### Rav Sheshet Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ מִן הַצַּד — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם. וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח שֶׁאָמְרוּ, צְרִיכָה שֶׁתְּהֵא בְּרִיאָה כְּדֵי לְהַעֲמִיד בָּהּ דֶּלֶת, וַאֲפִילּוּ דֶּלֶת שֶׁל קַשִּׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now examines **the matter itself** with regard to Rav Ḥisda's statement cited above. **Rav Ḥisda said:** If **one prepared** an opening in **the form of a doorway from the side,** placing the horizontal cross beam to the sides, rather than on top, of the vertical posts, **he has not done anything.** **And Rav Ḥisda** also **said: The** opening in the **form of a doorway of which** the Sages **spoke must be strong enough to mount a door in it, and even** if it is merely a flimsy **door of straw.**

Returning to the question of what a *tzurat hapetach* must look like, the *Gemara* records Rav Chisda's rulings on the matter. First: *tzurat hapetach she-asah min hatzad lo asah ve-lo klum* — a *tzurat hapetach* in which the upper element runs along the side of the posts rather than directly over their tops is entirely invalid. Second: *tzurat hapetach she-amru tzrichah she-tehei briyah k'dei le-amid bah delet* — the doorframe must be sufficiently sturdy to support a door, should one be leaned against it. Notably, however, even a door made of straw would suffice — what is excluded is only a structure so flimsy it would collapse under any weight.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח צְרִיכָה הֶיכֵּר צִיר. מַאי הֶיכֵּר צִיר? אָמַר רַב אַוְיָא: אַבְקָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Yannai: The** opening in the **form of a doorway requires a mark** in the doorpost **for hinges.** The Gemara asks: **What is a mark for hinges? Rav Avya said: Loops [*avkata*]** into which the hinge is inserted, so that it will be possible to mount a door in the doorway.

Reish Lakish adds in the name of Rabbi Yannai an even more demanding requirement: *tzurat hapetach tzrichah heker tzir* — the doorframe must exhibit a recognizable hinge socket. Rav Avya explains that this means something resembling the bracket or loop that would receive a door's hinge — a physical indication that a door could actually be mounted. For Rabbi Yannai, the *tzurat hapetach* must not be merely symbolic but must genuinely resemble a functional doorframe to the point of having this feature.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא לְתַלְמִידֵי דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲמַר מָר מִידֵּי בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לֹא אָמַר וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Aḥa, the son of Rav Avya,** once **found the students of Rav Ashi** and **said to them: Did the Master,** Rav Ashi, **say anything with regard to** an opening in **the form of a doorway? They said to him: He said nothing,** implying that an indication for hinges is unnecessary.

The *Gemara* then records that Rav Acha the son of Rav Avya found the students of Rav Ashi and asked them: *amar mar midi be-tzurat hapetach* — did the master say anything specific regarding criteria for a valid *tzurat hapetach*? They replied that he had said nothing. From the master's silence, one infers that no special requirements — including *heker tzir* — are imposed beyond the basic structure. We rule accordingly: *heker tzir* is not required, though the requirement of structural soundness (*briyah*) is upheld.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: צוּרַת הַפֶּתַח שֶׁאָמְרוּ, קָנֶה מִכָּאן וְקָנֶה מִכָּאן וְקָנֶה עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן. צְרִיכִין לִיגַּע, אוֹ אֵין צְרִיכִין לִיגַּע? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֵין צְרִיכִין לִיגַּע. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: צְרִיכִין לִיגַּע.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** a *baraita*: **The form of a doorway of which they spoke** consists of **a reed from here,** on one side, **and a reed from there,** on the opposite side, **and a reed on top of them.** The Gemara asks: **Need** the lower reeds reach high enough **to touch** the upper reed, **or do they not need to touch** it? **Rav Naḥman said: They do not need to touch** it; **and Rav Sheshet said: They need to touch** it.

Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet dispute whether the side posts must physically touch the upper beam. Rav Nachman rules they need not (*ein tzrichin liga*); Rav Sheshet rules they must (*tzrichin liga*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל רַב נַחְמָן וַעֲבַד עוֹבָדָא בֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ רַב גַּדָּא: זִיל, שְׁלוֹף שְׁדִינְהוּ. אֲזַל, שְׁלַף, שְׁדִינְהוּ. אַשְׁכְּחוּהוּ דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, חַבְשׁוּהוּ. אֲזַל רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, קָם אַבָּבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּדָּא, פּוֹק תָּא! נְפַק וַאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Naḥman went** ahead **and performed an action in the house of the Exilarch in accordance with his** own **opinion.** He constructed an opening in the form of a doorway such that the upper reed was not in contact with the lower reeds. **Rav Sheshet said to his attendant, Rav Gadda: Go, remove** those reeds and **throw them** away. The attendant **went, removed** the reeds, and **threw them** away. Members **of the Exilarch's court found him** and **imprisoned him** for destroying the form of a doorway that permitted them to carry. **Rav Sheshet went** and **stood at the door** of the prison, and **called** out **to him: Gadda, go out** and **come** to me. The Exilarch's men released him, and **he went out and came** to Rav Sheshet.

Rav Nachman enacted his ruling in the house of the *Reish Galuta*, constructing a *tzurat hapetach* without the posts making contact with the beam above. Upon learning of this, Rav Sheshet instructed his attendant, Rav Gadda, to go and remove the *eruv* — a remarkable act of contravening a colleague's ruling in the house of the *Reish Galuta*. The household caught Rav Gadda in the act and had him imprisoned. Rav Sheshet then went to the door and called out to him by name, and Rav Gadda emerged — an episode recounted with an unmistakable air of the extraordinary. This Talmudic incident stands, incidentally, as a striking precedent for the dismantling of an *eruv* one regards as halachically invalid.

### Kippa Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת לְרַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּנֵי מָר מִידֵּה בְּצוּרַת הַפֶּתַח? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, תְּנֵינָא: כִּיפָּה, רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב בִּמְזוּזָה, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאִם יֵשׁ בְּרַגְלֶיהָ עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁהִיא חַיֶּיבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Sheshet** once **found Rabba bar Shmuel** and **said to him: Did the Master teach anything with regard to** the *halakhot* of **the form of a doorway? He said to him: Yes, we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an arched gateway, Rabbi Meir deems** the owner **obligated to** affix **a *mezuza*, and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt. However, they** both **agree that if its supports,** the vertical sides of the gate before it arches, **are ten** handbreadths high, **that** the gate **requires** a *mezuza*.

Rav Sheshet later asks Rabbah bar Shmuel whether he knows any relevant *baraitot* bearing on the *tzurat hapetach* discussion. He is told: *tanina kippah* — we have taught the case of an arch. The *baraita* specifies that the minimum dimensions for a valid doorframe — whether for the purpose of *mezuzah* or *tzurat hapetach* — are ten *tefachim* in height and four *tefachim* in width. This is why the posts erected for a *tzurat hapetach* must reach a minimum height of ten *tefachim*; below this they do not constitute a doorway.

### Meir vs Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים אִם גְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה וְאֵין בְּרַגְלֶיהָ שְׁלֹשָׁה, אִי נָמֵי: יֵשׁ בְּרַגְלֶיהָ שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְאֵין גְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה — וְלֹא כְּלוּם. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּיֵשׁ בְּרַגְלֶיהָ שְׁלֹשָׁה וּגְבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה, וְאֵין רְחָבָה אַרְבָּעָה, וְיֵשׁ בָּהּ לָחוֹק לְהַשְׁלִימָהּ לְאַרְבָּעָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים.
>
> **Translation:** In order to explain the dispute, **Abaye said: Everyone agrees that if** the entire arch **is ten** handbreadths **high, but its supports are less than three** handbreadths high, or, **alternatively,** if **its supports are three** handbreadths high but the entire arch **is less than ten** handbreadths **high,** the arch requires **no** *mezuza* **at all.** Both of these gateways lack the requisite parameters of the form of a doorway to require a *mezuza*. **Where they disagree is in** a case where **the supports are three** handbreadths high **and** the entire arch is **ten** handbreadths **high,** and at the height of ten handbreadths the arch **is less than four** handbreadths **wide; however, there is** room **to carve out** the area **to complete it to four** handbreadths, so that the opening of the arch measures four handbreadths wide and ten handbreadths high. Abaye explains the dispute: **Rabbi Meir holds** that **one carves** out the area **to complete** the four handbreadths, i.e., the arch is considered as though it has already been carved out, and the opening has the necessary dimensions. **And the Rabbis hold** that **one does not carve out** the arch **to complete** the four handbreadths. Since the opening is not actually four handbreadths wide at a height of ten handbreadths, no *mezuza* need be affixed.

The debate centers on a structure that begins as the correct four *tefachim* wide for the first three *tefachim* of height, but then curves inward in an arch, so that by the time it reaches ten *tefachim* in height it is no longer four *tefachim* wide. Rabbi Meir holds the structure valid; the Sages hold it invalid.

Abaye clarifies the parameters of the dispute. All agree that if the arch begins to curve before the structure reaches three *tefachim* off the ground, there is nothing of value — you do not even have the beginnings of a doorframe. All agree likewise that if the total height does not reach ten *tefachim*, the structure is invalid regardless of what happens below. The debate is restricted to the case where the first three *tefachim* are straight and four *tefachim* wide, and the full height reaches ten *tefachim*, but at the ten-*tefach* point the width has narrowed due to the arch.

### Conceptual Framework

The key question is whether one can invoke the principle of *chokekim le-hashlim* — conceptually "carving out" the material to complete a valid form. Rabbi Meir holds that one can: *Rabbi Meir savar chokekim le-hashlim*. Since the arch contains enough material that, in theory, one could carve it into a straight-sided rectangle meeting all the required dimensions, and since the base already has the correct proportions, the structure is treated as if it had already been so completed. The beginning of the correct form exists; the potential for the full form is present; and that potential is sufficient to confer the status of a valid doorway. Rabbi Meir's reasoning parallels the principle *kol ha-omed lizrok k'zaruk dami* — the potential state is treated as realized. The Sages (*Rabbanan savar ein chokekim le-hashlim*) do not accept this: if the actual dimensions at the critical point are insufficient, no conceptual carving saves it.

### Disconnected Posts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ — לָא תֵּימָא לְהוּ לְבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא וְלָא מִידֵּי מֵהָא מַתְנִיתָא דְכִיפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to** Rabba bar Shmuel: **If you find them, do not say to** the members of **the Exilarch's household anything with regard to this *baraita* of an arched gateway,** as it is proof against my opinion.

This debate has direct bearing on the question of whether the side posts of a *tzurat hapetach* must physically touch the upper beam. According to Rabbi Meir, even where the posts do not technically connect to the top of the structure — since the archway in his scenario is valid despite the actual walls not forming a flush rectangle — the doorframe can still be recognized as valid by virtue of its form and potential. The arch, in which the straight lower section does not physically reach the curved upper section in the rectangular sense, is analogous to a *tzurat hapetach* in which the posts do not literally touch the beam above. Rabbi Meir's ruling thus lends support to Rav Nachman's position that physical contact between posts and beam is not required.

This is precisely what Rabbah bar Shmuel pointed out to Rav Sheshet — and Rav Sheshet, recognizing that this *baraita* constituted evidence against his own ruling, instructed that the *matnita de-kippah* not be disclosed to the household of the *Reish Galuta*, lest it further undermine his position in an already fraught situation.

The conclusion that emerges from practice: the side posts need not physically touch the upper beam. What is required is that the posts be positioned directly beneath the beam — not off to the side — and that the overall structure possess the shape and sufficient stability of a doorframe.

## Lechi & Kora

### Shammai vs Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֶכְשֵׁר מָבוֹי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לְחָיַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a basic dispute with regard to the method of **rendering an alleyway fit** for one to carry within it on Shabbat. **Beit Shammai say:** Both **a side post and a** cross **beam** are required. **Beit Hillel say: Either a side post or a** cross **beam. Rabbi Eliezer says:** Two **side posts** are required, one on each side of the alleyway.

The *Gemara* moves to a new *mishna* addressing the question of *hechsher mavoi* — what is required to render a *mavoi* valid for carrying. Beit Shammai says: *lechi ve-korah* — both a side post and a horizontal beam. Beit Hillel says: *o lechi o korah* — either one suffices. Rabbi Eliezer says two *lechayayim* are required, one on each side.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, שֶׁהוּא נִיתָּר אוֹ בְּלֶחִי אוֹ בְּקוֹרָה. עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל רָחָב מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְעַד עֶשֶׂר, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: עַל זֶה וְעַל זֶה נֶחְלְקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In the name of Rabbi Yishmael, one student said before Rabbi Akiva: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about an alleyway that is less than four cubits** wide, as they both agree **that** carrying **is rendered permitted by either a side post or a** cross **beam. With regard to what did they disagree? It is with regard to** an alleyway that is **wider than four cubits, and up to ten** cubits wide; **as Beit Shammai say:** It requires both **a side post and a** cross **beam. And Beit Hillel say:** It requires **either a side post or a** cross **beam. Rabbi Akiva said** to the disciple: It is not so, as **they disagree** both **about this** case, i.e., an alleyway that is less than four cubits wide, **and about that** case, i.e., an alleyway that is between four and ten cubits wide.

A certain student adds in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, before Rabbi Akiva, that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree at all for a *mavoi* narrower than four *amot* — for such a *mavoi*, either a *lechi* or a *korah* suffices according to all. Their dispute was only for widths between four and ten *amot*. Rabbi Akiva demurs, maintaining that the dispute applied even when the width was less than four *amot*.

### Closed-End Mavoi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כַּחֲנַנְיָה וְלָא כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הֶכְשֵׁר מָבוֹי סָתוּם כֵּיצַד? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Before clarifying the various opinions in the mishna, the Gemara seeks to determine: **In accordance with whose** opinion was this mishna taught? Apparently, it is **neither in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥananya, nor in accordance with** the unattributed opinion of **the first *tanna*** of the *baraita*, who disagree about an alleyway that is open to a public domain on two opposite sides. The dispute is whether the form of a doorway on one end and a side post and a cross beam on the other end suffice to render it permitted for one to carry within it, or whether actual doors are required, at least on one end. However, they both agree that a side post and a cross beam alone are not effective. Since at this point the Gemara assumes that the dispute in the mishna between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel applies to all alleyways, whether closed on one side or open on two opposite sides to the public domain, these opinions reflect an entirely different position. **Rav Yehuda said** that **this is what** the mishna **is saying: How is a closed alleyway rendered fit** for one to carry within it on Shabbat? **Beit Shammai say:** It requires both **a side post and a** cross **beam. And Beit Hillel say: Either a side post or a** cross **beam.**

One might object that this *mishna* cannot apply to a *mavoi* open at both ends (*mavoi mefulash*), since such a *mavoi* would require at minimum a *tzurat hapetach* at one end — and therefore a *lechi* or *korah* alone would be insufficient regardless of the opinion. Rav Yehuda resolves this directly: the *mishna* is addressing specifically *hechsher mavoi satum* — the case of a closed-end *mavoi*, one that is enclosed on three sides and open at only one end. For such a *mavoi*, the question is what is required at the single open end. That is where the dispute of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel applies.

### Wall vs Heker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, לְמֵימְרָא דְּקָא סָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: אַרְבַּע מְחִיצּוֹת דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. לָא, לִזְרוֹק — מִשָּׁלֹשׁ הוּא דְּמִיחַיַּיב, לְטַלְטֵל — עַד דְּאִיכָּא אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the basis of each opinion. **Beit Shammai say:** It requires both **a side post and a** cross **beam. Is that to say** that **Beit Shammai hold** that in order for an area to be considered a private domain, **four partitions are** required **by Torah law?** Since a side post with a cross beam qualifies as a partition, the fact that they do not permit carrying within an alleyway without a side post indicates that they maintain that a private domain requires four partitions. The Gemara rejects this argument: **No,** there is no proof, as one cannot conclude the parameters for a private domain based on the number of walls required to permit carrying. As with regard to the Torah prohibition **to throw** an object into a private domain from the public domain, **once** an enclosed area has **three** partitions, **one is liable** by Torah law. However, to permit one **to carry** an object within a private domain, the Rabbis decreed that it is not permitted **until there are** partitions on all **four** sides.

The *Gemara* then probes the conceptual stakes of the Shammai-Hillel dispute. If Beit Shammai requires both a *lechi* and a *korah*, this would appear to indicate that together they constitute a fourth wall — *arba mechitzot de-oraisa*, that biblically four walls are required. The *Gemara* pushes back: not necessarily. Biblically, perhaps three walls suffice to constitute a *reshut ha-yachid* such that throwing an object from a *reshut ha-rabim* into it violates the prohibition. What Beit Shammai's requirement of both a *lechi* and a *korah* accomplishes is the rabbinical requirement for carrying (*le-taltel*) — and they require this combination because together they define the fourth side as a genuine *mechitza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים אוֹ לֶחִי אוֹ קוֹרָה. לֵימָא קָא סָבְרִי בֵּית הִלֵּל שָׁלֹשׁ מְחִיצּוֹת דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? לָא, לִזְרוֹק — מִשְּׁתַּיִם הוּא דְּמִיחַיַּיב, לְטַלְטֵל — עַד דְּאִיכָּא שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to draw an inference from that which **Beit Hillel say: Either a side post or a** cross **beam** is required. **Is that to say** that **Beit Hillel hold that** at least **three partitions** are required **by Torah law,** and that an area with fewer is not considered a private domain? The Gemara rejects this argument as well: **No** proof can be cited from here. With regard to the Torah prohibition **to throw** an object into a private domain from the public domain, **once** an enclosed area has merely **two** partitions, **one is liable** by Torah law. However, to permit one **to carry** an object within a private domain, the Rabbis decreed that it is not permitted **until there are** partitions on **three** sides. A cross beam and a side post do not function as partitions but merely as conspicuous markers, so that one does not mistakenly carry outside the alleyway.

Conversely, if Beit Hillel requires only one or the other, this suggests that a single element does not constitute a wall but rather a *heker* — a symbolic marker. Even so, the *Gemara* notes, this need not imply that biblically one needs only three walls; one could push further and say biblically even two walls could define a *reshut ha-yachid*, with the rabbinic requirement for carrying being three.

What matters conceptually is the following: the *Gemara* understands the Beit Shammai-Beit Hillel dispute as precisely the debate that was raised earlier in the *masechet* — does a *lechi* or *korah* function *mishum mechitza*, as a wall, or *mishum heker*, as a mere visual marker? The *Gemara*'s analysis here suggests that requiring both (*lechi ve-korah* together) is consistent with the *mechitza* reading, while requiring only one or the other is consistent with the *heker* reading. Crucially, however, the *Gemara* emphasizes that this conceptual distinction does not settle the question of biblical *reshut* status: the biblical threshold for *reshut ha-yachid* may be lower than what either position demands rabbinically for carrying, and the Shammai-Hillel dispute pertains to the rabbinic level of permission, not to the biblical definition of domain.
