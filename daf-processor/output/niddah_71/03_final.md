# Niddah 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beit Shammai Death Status

### Dead Niddah Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים [וְכוּ׳]. מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? אִי נֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״וַתִּתְחַלְחַל הַמַּלְכָּה״, וְאָמַר רַב: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁפֵּרְסָה נִדָּה. הָכָא נָמֵי, אַגַּב בִּיעֲתוּתָא דְּמַלְאֲכָא דְמוֹתָא — חָזְיָא, וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן שֶׁחֲרָדָה מְסַלֶּקֶת אֶת הַדָּמִים! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: פַּחְדָּא — צָמֵית, בִּיעֲתוּתָא — מְרַפְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Beit Shammai say:** The status of all women when they die is as though they were menstruating women. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai? If we say** that their opinion is **due to** that **which is written: "Then the queen was exceedingly distressed [*vatitḥalḥal*]"** (Esther 4:4), this is difficult. The Gemara first explains the derivation. **And Rav said:** This **teaches that she began to menstruate** out of fear, as the cavities [*ḥalalim*] of her body opened. **Here too,** when a woman is about to die, **due to the fear of the Angel of Death she sees** blood. This is difficult, as **didn't we learn** in a mishna (39a) **that trepidation eliminates** the flow of menstrual **blood?** The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult. Anxiety** generated by extended worry **contracts** the muscles and prevents the blood from flowing, but sudden **fear relaxes** the muscles and causes the blood to flow.

We open on 71a, approximately six lines into the daf, with the words *beit shammai omrim*. The subject is a debate already introduced in the preceding material: when a woman who is a *niddah* dies — or, correspondingly, when a man who is a *zav* or a woman who is a *zavah* dies — does the *tumah* of *niddah*, *zav*, or *zavah* persist beyond death in addition to the *tumah* of the corpse itself? The practical consequence is significant: a living *niddah* or *zav* renders impure through *mishkav u-moshav* — objects beneath them, even through multiple layers of cushions and coverings. If that status persists at death, the corpse continues to convey this heightened *tumah*. The *gemara* notes that this falls under the narrow subcategory of *even masama*, though the Rambam understands it as expressing the central principle that anything the body weighs upon is *tamei*, as if the person were still alive. The *gemara*'s rationale for maintaining this status is that we cannot be certain the person is truly dead — perhaps they have merely fainted. And since the *gemara* states that one does not cease to worry about misdiagnosis until the flesh has decomposed, this passage has been invoked in later halachic literature — particularly the discussions that arose roughly two centuries ago — regarding the danger of burying a person who may still be alive, and the customs of *minhag metei yerushalayim* that bear on that question.

### Fear vs. Shock Analysis

The *gemara* now asks: *mai ta'ama de-beit shammai* — what is Beit Shammai's reasoning? The *gemara* first proposes that Beit Shammai derives their position from the verse *va-tizchalchal ha-malkah* — Queen Esther trembled — from which Rav teaches *melamed she-paris niddah*, that the word *va-tizchalchal* suggests a seeping or flowing, indicating that she became a *niddah* at that moment. The connection to the word *mechalchelet* — something that seeps through — reinforces this reading. On this basis, one might argue that here too, *agav bisusa de-malach ha-maves* — due to the shock of the Angel of Death — the woman bleeds and thereby becomes a *niddah* at the moment of death.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning. We have learned in a *mishnah* that *charada mesalekes es ha-damim* — fear causes blood to be suppressed — as illustrated by the case of a woman hiding in a cave from marauding troops, who need not be concerned about her period, since fear prevents bleeding. So which is it: does fear cause bleeding or prevent it?

### Pachda/Bisusa Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: פַּחְדָּא — צָמֵית, בִּיעֲתוּתָא — מְרַפְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **This** is **not difficult. Anxiety** generated by extended worry **contracts** the muscles and prevents the blood from flowing, but sudden **fear relaxes** the muscles and causes the blood to flow.

The *gemara* resolves this tension cleanly: *pachda tzamiz* — fear constricts — while *bisusa marafya* — shock causes relaxation. Rashi explains that *pachda* refers to an ongoing state of fear, while *bisusa* denotes a sudden, overwhelming startlement: *behalus pitom she-adam ro'eh o shome'a nival*, the abrupt shock of something one suddenly sees or hears, which causes the muscles to relax rather than contract, potentially inducing bleeding. Fear of an impending calamity produces the physiological effect of suppressing flow; acute shock produces the opposite.

One might wonder, however, how this distinction applies to the Esther narrative. If *va-tizchalchal ha-malkah* refers to her hesitation before entering the court of Achashveirosh — an event she had planned and steeled herself for — it would seem to be *pachda*, not *bisusa*. In fact, as was correctly noted, the verse describes Esther's reaction not to her entry into the court but to her first hearing of the decree against the Jews — an abrupt, shattering piece of news, a classic case of sudden shock. With that clarification, the *bisusa* framework fits the verse precisely, and the *gemara*'s initial inference is coherent.

### Men Cannot Be Zav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִקְּרִי כָּאן ״מִבְּשָׂרוֹ״, וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת אוֹנְסוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** But this is difficult, as **I will apply here** the principle stated with regard to a *zav*: "An issue **out of his flesh"** (Leviticus 15:2) renders a man impure, **but not** an issue **due to circumstances beyond his control.** If a man has a discharge of *ziva* due to an illness or some accident, he does not become impure. Consequently, if he has a discharge due to fear after seeing the Angel of Death, he would not become ritually impure.

Even granting this resolution, there is a deeper problem with Beit Shammai's position. The debate applies not only to women but equally to men: *kol ha-nashim metim zavim* — Beit Shammai holds that all men die as *zavim*. If the reasoning were that the shock of impending death causes a discharge, this would be untenable by men, because the Torah requires that a *zav*'s flow emerge *mi-besaro* — from within himself, naturally — and not *mi-chamat ones*, not due to some external cause. Even if we were to say that the shock of the *malach ha-maves* caused a man to discharge, that discharge would be externally induced and would not confer *zav* status. Beit Shammai's position would collapse for men entirely.

### Baraita on Clothes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, כִּדְתַנְיָא: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מַטְבִּילִין כֵּלִים עַל גַּבֵּי נִדּוֹת מֵתוֹת, וְהָיוּ נִדּוֹת חַיּוֹת מִתְבַּיְּישׁוֹת — הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַטְבִּילִין עַל גַּבֵּי כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל נִדּוֹת חַיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the reason** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai** is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **At first they would** ritually **immerse** all the **utensils** that had been used **by** women who **died** while **menstruating,** which had thereby contracted ritual impurity even if the utensils did not touch the women after their death. **And** as a result, **the living menstruating women were embarrassed,** as they saw that the status of a menstruating woman is so severe that it remains even after death. The Sages therefore **instituted that one must immerse** the utensils which had been used **by all** dying **women, due to the honor of living menstruating women.**

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ella ta'ama de-beit shammai ke-de-tanya* — the real reasoning of Beit Shammai is as found in a *baraita*. And that *baraita* is the subject of the next section.

## Clothes Immersion

### Women's Clothes

The *baraita* opens: *barishona hayu matbilin kelim al gabei niddot metot* — originally they would immerse garments that had been worn by women who died as *niddot*. The phrasing requires clarification: this does not mean immersing clothes while they are literally on top of the corpse. Rather, the case is one where a woman had been wearing clothes during the days she was a *niddah* and she then died. The clothes she had been wearing are *tamei* by virtue of contact with a *niddah*. If her survivors wish to use those garments, they must first be immersed in a *mikveh*. Importantly, these are not the clothes she wore at the moment of death — those would in any case be *tamei* through contact with a corpse — but clothes she wore during the preceding days or weeks while she was a *niddah*.

The frequency with which a woman is a *niddah* made this a genuinely common scenario. A woman who was in her *niddah* period when she died — or who died in childbirth, a tragically common occurrence — would have clothing that required immersion. Upon her death, her daughters or others wishing to use those garments would take them to the *mikveh*.

### Universal Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מַטְבִּילִין עַל גַּבֵּי זָבִין מֵתִין, וְהָיוּ זָבִים חַיִּים מִתְבַּיְּישִׁין — הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַטְבִּילִין עַל גַּבֵּי כׇּל הָאֲנָשִׁים, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל זָבִים חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **at first they would immerse** all the utensils that had been used by ***zavin*,** men suffering from gonorrhea, **who died,** as the utensils had thereby contracted ritual impurity even if the utensils did not touch the men after their death. **And** as a result, **the living *zavin* were embarrassed.** Therefore, the Sages **instituted that one must immerse** the utensils that had been used **by all** dying men, **due to the honor of the living *zavin*.**

The *gemara* describes what follows: *ve-hayu niddot chayot mitbaishos* — and the living *niddot* were embarrassed. Rashi explains: *she-afilu be-mitatan hen meshunot mi-kol ha-adam* — even in death, they are treated differently from everyone else. Other people die and their clothes are simply buried with them or distributed without special ceremony; a woman who died as a *niddah* has her clothes singled out for *mikveh* immersion, publicly advertising her *niddah* status even post-mortem. The Rabbis' response was to universalize the practice: *she-yihu matbilin al gabei kol ha-nashim mipnei kevodan shel niddot chayot* — they enacted that all women's clothes be immersed after death, for the dignity of the living *niddot*, so that no individual is singled out.

### Men's Clothes Too

The *baraita* then describes a parallel development: *barishona hayu matbilin al gabei zavin metim* — originally they would immerse the clothes of men who had died as *zavim* — and the living *zavim* were embarrassed. The enactment was extended: *she-yihu matbilin al gabei ha-kol mipnei kevodan shel zavin chayim* — to immerse the clothes of all people, of every description, to spare the *zavim* from being singled out. Now no one is distinguished: neither women from women, nor men from women, nor *niddot* from non-*niddot*, nor *zavim* from everyone else.

### Niddah vs. Zav Frequency

This universalization is striking when one considers the asymmetry of the two phenomena. *Niddah* is a regular, monthly occurrence for most women; *zav* status in men is a rare pathology. It would seem disproportionate to enact a universal policy on account of a rarely occurring condition. Yet the enactment was made, and its result — treating men and women identically in this regard — addresses the concern that had been raised: why single out women? Now the practice applies equally to all.

Tosafot observes that in *Moed Katan* the practice appears to be universally accepted, not merely the position of Beit Shammai. Tosafot reads the word *barishona* as indicating that originally Beit Hillel had applied the immersion requirement only to the clothes of *niddot*, and subsequently reversed course to align with Beit Shammai — who had already taken the more universalizing position — precisely because of the concern for dignity. This reading is notable: we typically associate Beit Hillel with sensitivity toward human dignity, yet in this instance it was Beit Shammai who first moved to eliminate the stigmatizing distinction. There is a comparable case, worth noting in passing, involving a person who is half-slave and half-free, where Beit Shammai similarly showed greater proactivity than Beit Hillel.

It is also worth noting the broader pattern: the Rabbis modified halachic practice in multiple areas out of sensitivity to social embarrassment. Burial customs were standardized — the wealthy were no longer buried in elaborate dress — so that the poor would not be humiliated. Similarly, the practice described in connection with the *fifteenth of Av*, where all young women would borrow identical white garments, was designed so that no one would be embarrassed by her inability to afford finer clothing. The application of this principle to *tumah* law here is another instance of that same moral instinct embedded in normative halachah.

## Blood at Death

### Woman's Vaginal Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמֵּתָה, וְיָצְאָה מִמֶּנָּה רְבִיעִית דָּם — מְטַמְּאָה מִשּׁוּם כֶּתֶם, וּמְטַמְּאָה בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman who died, and** after her death **a quarter**-*log* **of blood emerged from her** body, although the blood emerged after death, it **transmits ritual impurity** by touching and carrying, **due to** the impurity of the **spot** of blood of a menstruating woman. This impurity as blood of menstruation applies to any amount of blood she emits, despite the *halakha* that generally, the blood of a corpse transmits impurity only if it is at least a quarter-*log* in volume. **And** as it is a quarter-*log* of blood, **it transmits impurity in a tent,** as it is the blood of a corpse.

Having dealt with the status of the dead *niddah* and *zav*, the *gemara* now turns to a related but distinct question: what is the *tumah* status of blood that emerges vaginally from a woman as she is dying? The case is: *ha-isha she-mesa ve-yatza mimena revi'it dam* — a woman dies, and a *revi'it* of blood emerges from her body vaginally. A *revi'it* — approximately a quarter of a *log*, roughly three and a half ounces — is the critical threshold for blood of a corpse to convey *tumas ohel*: anyone under the same roof as a *revi'it* of blood from a corpse becomes *tamei*. If the blood came out after death, only a *revi'it* creates *ohel* tumah. If the blood came out while she was alive, even a small amount constitutes *dam niddah* and conveys *tumas ketem*.

The difficulty is that we do not know when this blood came out.

### Both Ketem & Ohel

The first position in the *mishnah* gives the blood both stringencies: *metame mishum ketem u-metame ba-ohel* — it is *tamei* as a *ketem*, because we treat it as possibly having come out while she was alive and thus constituting *dam niddah* even in the smallest quantity; and it is *tamei* as *ohel tumah*, because we treat it as possibly having come out after death, making it blood of a corpse in a quantity sufficient to affect those under the same roof.

### After Death Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה מִשּׁוּם כֶּתֶם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנֶּעֱקַר מִשֶּׁמֵּתָה. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּיוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל מַשְׁבֵּר וָמֵתָה, וְיָצְאָה מִמֶּנָּה רְבִיעִית דָּם שֶׁהִיא מְטַמְּאָה מִשּׁוּם כֶּתֶם. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לְפִיכָךְ אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** That quarter-*log* of blood **does not transmit impurity due to** the impurity of the **spot** of blood of a menstruating woman, **because that** blood **was displaced after she died. And Rabbi Yehuda concedes in** the case of a woman who is **sitting** in childbirth **on the travailing chair [*mashber*] and she died, and a quarter***-log* **of blood emerged from her** body, **that** this blood **transmits ritual impurity due to** the impurity of the **spot** of blood of a menstruating woman. In that case, the blood was displaced while she was still alive. **Rabbi Yosei said: For that** reason, that quarter-*log* of blood **does not transmit impurity in a tent,** as it did not come from a corpse.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *eino metame mishum ketem* — he does not apply *tumas ketem* to this blood. His reasoning is *mipnei she-ne'ekar mi-she-mesa* — the blood only began flowing after she died. He therefore treats it solely as blood of the dead, which conveys *ohel tumah* (when there is a *revi'it*) but not *tumas ketem*.

### Childbirth Exception

Rabbi Yehuda himself, however, concedes an exception: *u-modeh Rabbi Yehuda be-yoledet she-mesa ve-yatza mimena revi'it dam* — a woman who dies in the course of childbirth, and from whom a *revi'it* of blood emerges — there he agrees that the blood is *tamei mishum ketem*. His reasoning is that in a woman dying in childbirth, the blood began flowing during her life, before the moment of death, and therefore carries the status of *dam niddah* even in a small amount.

### Ketem Excludes Ohel

Rabbi Yosi draws out the logical implication of Rabbi Yehuda's concession: *lefikach einah metame ba-ohel* — therefore, in the childbirth case, it should not convey *ohel tumah*. If we are acknowledging that the blood came out during her lifetime, it is *dam niddah*, not *dam mes*; it cannot simultaneously be treated as corpse blood requiring a *revi'it* for *ohel tumah*. The positions thus line up as follows: the first *tanna* applies both stringencies in all cases; Rabbi Yehuda applies only *ohel tumah* in the ordinary case, but both *ketem* and *ohel* in the childbirth case; and Rabbi Yosi argues that once the childbirth blood is treated as *ketem*, consistency demands that it be stripped of *ohel tumah* status.

## Uterine Blood Source

### Makor Makomo Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר, אַף עַל גַּב דְּנֶעֱקַר דָּם מִשֶּׁמֵּתָה, מְטַמְּאָה מִשּׁוּם כֶּתֶם? אָמַר רַבִּי זְעֵירִי: מָקוֹר מְקוֹמוֹ טָמֵא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that according to Rabbi Yehuda a quarter-*log* of blood that emerges from a woman after death does not transmit impurity due to the impurity of a spot of blood of a menstruating woman, despite the fact that it came from the uterus. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **By inference** one might think **that the first *tanna* holds** that **even though the blood was displaced after she died, it renders** one **ritually impure due to** the impurity of a blood **spot.** This is not reasonable, as a dead woman cannot attain the status of a menstruating woman. **Rabbi Ze'eiri said:** Even the first *tanna* agrees that blood from the uterus which leaves the body after death does not impart impurity as a spot. Rather, the difference **between** the opinions of the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yehuda is whether the **place of** a woman's **source,** i.e., her uterus, **is impure,** and therefore any blood that passes through there is impure, even if it left her body after death.

The *gemara* examines the first *tanna*'s position more carefully. The *gemara* infers from Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning — which explicitly relies on the timing of emission relative to death — that the first *tanna* holds something more fundamental: *af al gav de-ne'ekar dam mi-she-mesa metame mishum ketem* — even if we agree that the blood was emitted only after death, the first *tanna* still imposes *tumas ketem*. This is counterintuitive: how can blood that came out of a dead body constitute *dam niddah*?

Zeiri resolves this: *makor mekomo tamei* — the principle that the uterus itself renders its blood *tamei* as *dam niddah*, regardless of the woman's living status at the moment of emission. This is a recurring principle in *Niddah*: blood that originates from the *makor* carries *niddah* status by virtue of its anatomical source. The woman does not become a *niddah* — she is dead, and one condition for *niddah* status is being alive — but the blood itself, having emerged from the uterus, is treated as *dam niddah*. Rabbi Yehuda rejects this principle and holds that blood emitted after death is simply blood of a corpse, nothing more.

### Corpse Tumah Only

Rabbi Yehuda's position is thus internally consistent. In the ordinary case, blood emitted after death is *dam mes* — at most *ohel tumah* in the presence of a *revi'it* — and carries no *ketem* status. The uterine origin of the blood is irrelevant; what matters is the moment of emission.

### Mixed Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר בְּאֹהֶל נָמֵי מְטַמֵּא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: דַּם תְּבוּסָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And Rabbi Yehuda concedes** in the case of a woman who was sitting in childbirth when she died, that the quarter-*log* of blood which emerged from her body transmits the ritual impurity of the spot of the blood of a menstruating woman. And Rabbi Yosei said: For that reason, that quarter-*log* of blood does not transmit impurity in a tent. The Gemara asks: **By inference,** one might conclude **that the first *tanna*,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that the blood which emerges from a woman who died during childbirth **also transmits impurity in a tent.** This cannot be correct, as the blood left her while she was still alive. **Rav Yehuda said:** The difference **between** Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei is with regard to **blood of submission** discharged from a body at the time of death, which contains a mixture of blood that leaves a person when he is still alive and blood that emerges after his death. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the blood which emerges from a woman who dies during childbirth is blood of submission, which transmits impurity in a tent by rabbinic law. Rabbi Yosei maintains that it is clear that this blood emerged from her body before she died, and therefore it is not blood of submission.

The second debate — the childbirth case — operates on different grounds. When a woman dies in the act of giving birth, blood flows both during her life and after her death. We cannot isolate which drops belong to which phase. Rabbi Yehuda concedes that this blood carries *tumas ketem* because some of it demonstrably flowed while she was alive. The *gemara* clarifies: Rabbi Yehuda extends both *ketem* and *ohel* to this case because he understands the blood as an instance of *dam t'fusa* — blood whose timing is uncertain, flowing across the boundary of life and death — and he therefore imposes both stringencies.

### Dam T'fusa Concept

The *gemara* clarifies the conceptual crux of the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda in the childbirth case involving the category of *dam t'fusa*. This is blood that flows from a dying person across the threshold of death, part of it while alive, part after death, in uncertain proportion. The following sections examine this category in detail.

## Dam T'fusa Details

### Murdered Person Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ דַּם תְּבוּסָה? פֵּירֵשׁ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הָרוּג שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנּוּ דָּם בֵּין בְּחַיָּיו בֵּין בְּמוֹתוֹ, סָפֵק בְּחַיָּיו יָצָא, סָפֵק בְּמוֹתוֹ יָצָא, סָפֵק בְּחַיָּיו וּבְמוֹתוֹ — זֶהוּ דַּם תְּבוּסָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **What is blood of submission,** which imparts ritual impurity (see 62b)? **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yehuda, explained:** The case is one of a person who was **killed, from whom** a quarter-*log* of **blood emerged,** a flow which began **while he was alive and** continued **after his death,** and it is **uncertain** whether a full quarter-*log* **emerged while he was alive** and therefore does not transmit ritual impurity, and it is **uncertain** whether **it emerged** out **after his death,** in which case it imparts ritual impurity by Torah law, and it is **uncertain** whether the quarter-*log* emerged partly **while he was alive and** partly **after his death. That is blood of submission.** **And the Rabbis say** that this is not blood of submission, but a case of uncertain ritual impurity by Torah law, as it might all have emerged after death. Therefore, one applies the principle that **in a private domain** a case of **uncertainty** is considered to be **ritually impure,** whereas **in a public domain** a case of **uncertainty** is considered to be **ritually pure.**

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* that defines and illustrates the category of *dam t'fusa* directly: *eizehu dam t'fusa?* — what is *dam t'fusa*? Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Yehuda explains: the paradigm case is a *harug* — a person who has been killed — *she-yatza mimenu dam bein be-chayav bein be-moso* — from whom blood flows both during his life and after his death. The blood flows when he is stabbed, and continues to flow after death. We cannot determine *safek be-chayav yatza, safek be-moso yatza* — whether any particular drop emerged during life or after death — and perhaps some came out during each phase.

The significance of *revi'it* is central here. For blood of a corpse to convey *tumas ohel*, one requires a full *revi'it*. If even a small drop of the total *revi'it* was emitted during the man's lifetime, then there is no longer a complete *revi'it* of corpse blood, and there is no biblical *ohel tumah*. The question is whether the rabbinic category of *dam t'fusa* — which imposes a *revi'it* requirement for *ohel tumah* even in this case of mixed-timing blood — is the appropriate framework, or whether the situation triggers biblical *safek tumah* with its own rules.

The Chachamim respond to Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Yehuda by arguing: if there is genuine uncertainty whether a full *revi'it* of blood came out entirely after death, then in a private domain (*reshus ha-yachid*) the doubt is resolved stringently and the blood is *tamei* biblically, while in a public domain (*reshus ha-rabbim*) it would be *tahor*. They deny, in other words, that *dam t'fusa* is merely a rabbinic category — the moment a full *revi'it* might have come out after death, there is a potential biblical *tumah* at stake.

The rabbinic-only category of *dam t'fusa* must therefore be defined more precisely: it applies only when we know with certainty that some of the blood began flowing during the person's life — so that a full *revi'it* of post-mortem blood is definitively excluded — and yet the flow continued after death, creating a mixture whose precise composition is unknown. In that defined scenario there is no biblical issue, but the Rabbis nonetheless treat the blood as if it were *dam mes* requiring a *revi'it* for *ohel tumah*.

### Flow Continuity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ דַּם תְּבוּסָה? הָרוּג שֶׁיָּצָא הֵימֶנּוּ רְבִיעִית דָּם בְּחַיָּיו וּבְמוֹתוֹ, וַעֲדַיִין לֹא פָּסַק, סָפֵק רוּבּוֹ בְּחַיָּיו וּמִיעוּטוֹ בְּמוֹתוֹ, סְפֵק מִיעוּטוֹ בְּחַיָּיו וְרוּבּוֹ בְּמוֹתוֹ — זֶהוּ דַּם תְּבוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is blood of submission?** The case is one of a person who has been **killed from whom a quarter**-*log* of **blood emerged while he was alive and** continued emerging **after his death and has still not stopped** coming out. It is clear that a full quarter-*log* did not emerge after his death, but it is **uncertain** whether **the majority** emerged **while he was alive and the minority after his death,** and equally **uncertain** whether **the minority** emerged **while he was alive and the majority after his death. That is blood of submission.**

A critical element of the *dam t'fusa* definition — according to the Chachamim's formulation — is that the blood was flowing continuously: *ve-adayin lo pasak* — and the flow had not yet stopped. The *baraita* specifies this: *safek rubo be-chayav u-mi'uto be-moto, safek mi'uto be-chayav ve-rubo be-moto* — we do not know if the majority of the *revi'it* came out during life and only a minority after death, or vice versa. Tosafot explains why this uncertainty about majority matters: one might think that if the majority of the blood came out during the person's lifetime, we would not impose the stringency of *dam t'fusa* — after all, the *mes* blood is only a minor component. The *baraita* teaches otherwise. As long as the flow is continuous — beginning in life and continuing uninterrupted into death — the entire volume is treated with *dam mes* stringency, regardless of what proportion came from each phase. The continuity of flow is what unifies the blood into a single halachic entity.

### Dripping Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָרוּג שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנּוּ רְבִיעִית דָּם, וְהָיָה מוּטָּל בַּמִּטָּה וְדָמוֹ מְטַפְטֵף לְגוּמָּא — טָמֵא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַטִּפָּה שֶׁל מִיתָה מְעוֹרֶבֶת בּוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים מְטַהֲרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן נִפְסָק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In the case of one who has been **killed from whom a quarter***-log* of **blood emerged, and he was lying in a bed and his blood was dripping into a hole** in the ground, the blood in the ground **is ritually impure, because the drop of death is mixed with it. And the Rabbis deem it ritually pure, because** each **first** drop and every other **first** drop is **separated** from the other drops of blood, i.e., it did not flow in a steady stream. Since the blood initially emerged while he was alive and is pure, every drop of blood which emerged after his death is nullified when it falls into that blood, and therefore the entire puddle of blood is ritually pure.

Rabbi Yehuda introduces a variant: *harug she-yatza mimenu revi'it dam, ve-haya mutal be-mittah, ve-damo metatef le-gumatz* — a murdered person on a bed, from whom exactly a *revi'it* drips drop by drop into a pit below him. This blood is also *tamei*, says Rabbi Yehuda, *mipnei she-tipah shel mitah meurevet bo* — because the drop that came out at the moment of death is mixed in with the rest.

The Chachamim, however, disagree: *mipnei she-ha-rishon rishon nifsak* — because when blood drips one drop at a time, each drop is separated from the previous one. The blood that came out during life was already in the pit before each subsequent drop arrived. Each new drop of post-mortem blood, as it falls into the accumulated pool, becomes *batel* — nullified — by the larger volume of blood already present. Therefore the Chachamim rule the collected blood *tahor*: through the sequential mechanism of *rishon rishon batel*, the post-mortem drops are successively nullified, and there is never, halachically speaking, a significant presence of *dam mes* in the mixture.

### Rishon Nullification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה! רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: אֵין דָּם מְבַטֵּל דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The Rabbis are saying well to Rabbi Yehuda;** how does he respond to their claim? The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as he said: Blood does not nullify blood.**

The principle of *rishon rishon batel* is not unique to this context; the same logic applies in other areas of halachah involving the gradual admixture of prohibited substances.

### Blood Immunity

The *gemara* notes that this response — the Chachamim's nullification argument — is compelling, and asks: *shapir ka'amri rabbanan le-Rabbi Yehuda* — why doesn't Rabbi Yehuda accept it? The answer is: *Rabbi Yehuda leta'amei* — Rabbi Yehuda follows his own established ruling — *de-amar ein dam levatel dam* — that blood cannot nullify blood. The principle of *bittul be-rov*, nullification by majority, does not apply when the substance being added is identical in kind to the medium. As other *gemarot* generalize: *min be-mino lo batil* — a substance is not nullified by its own kind. Blood falling into blood cannot be said to dissolve into anonymity. Rabbi Yehuda thus holds that the dripping post-mortem blood retains its identity and cannot be nullified by the pre-existing pool.

## Crucifixion Case

### Crucifixion Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: צָלוּב עַל הָעֵץ, שֶׁדָּמוֹ שׁוֹתֵת לָאָרֶץ, וְנִמְצָא תַּחְתָּיו רְבִיעִית דָּם — טָמֵא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְטַהֵר, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: טִפָּה שֶׁל מִיתָה עוֹמֶדֶת לוֹ עַל גַּב הָעֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of one who is **crucified on wood, whose blood** does not drip but **runs** down the wood **to the earth, and a quarter***-log* **of blood is found underneath him,** the blood is **ritually impure. Rabbi Yehuda deems** the blood **ritually pure.** He explains his reasoning: **As I say** that the quarter-*log* of blood found underneath the body came out while he was still alive, and **the drop of** blood that came out after his **death remains on the wood.**

Rabbi Shimon introduces a further application of *dam t'fusa*: *tzaluv al ha-eitz* — a person who has been crucified — *she-damo shotes ve-yotzei* — whose blood flows and drips continuously — *ve-nimtza tachtav revi'it dam* — and a *revi'it* of blood is found beneath him. The case reflects the lived reality of Roman-era Palestine, where crucifixion was not a singular historical event but a recurring instrument of state terror. The question is whether this collected *revi'it* conveys *ohel tumah*.

The question is posed to both the Chachamim and Rabbi Yehuda. Their positions are stated together but rest on distinct reasoning.

### Chachamim Tahor

### Death Blood Isolated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, נֵימָא אִיהוּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ: שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר ״טִפָּה שֶׁל מִיתָה עוֹמֶדֶת עַל גַּב הַמִּטָּה״! שָׁאנֵי מִטָּה, דִּמְחַלְחֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. **And Rabbi Yehuda, let him say to himself** with regard to one who was killed on a bed and his blood is dripping into a hole in the ground: **As I say** that all the blood found underneath the body emerged while he was still alive and **the drop of** blood that came out after his **death remains on the bed.** Why does Rabbi Yehuda deem the blood ritually impure in such a case? The Gemara answers: The case **of the bed is different, as** the blood **seeps** through the bed onto the floor, whereas in the case of one who is crucified some of the blood may remain on the tree.

Rabbi Yehuda rules this blood *tahor*: *she-ani omer tipah shel mitah omedes al gav ha-eitz* — I say that the drop that came out at the moment of death remained on the wood of the cross itself and did not fall into the pool below. Since it is possible that none of the post-mortem blood mixed into the accumulated *revi'it*, one cannot invoke *dam t'fusa*. All of this is, it must be remembered, in the rabbinic domain — we already know this is not a full *revi'it* of pure post-mortem blood, so there is no biblical concern. But Rabbi Yehuda is unwilling to extend even the rabbinic category of *dam t'fusa* to a case where the *mes* blood may never have entered the mixture at all.

### Porous Material Effect

One might ask: why did Rabbi Yehuda not apply the same argument in the dripping-blood-on-a-bed case? Why not say there too that the post-mortem drop remained on the bed rather than mixing with the pool? The *gemara* answers: *shani mittah de-mechalchela* — a bed is different because it is porous, and blood seeps through it. The porous material ensures that every drop penetrates and mixes into the pool below. There, Rabbi Yehuda concedes that the post-mortem drop definitely entered the mixture, which is why he rules *tamei* in the bed case. But the wood of a cross does not allow blood to seep through in the same way, so the post-mortem drop may well have remained on the surface.

## Childbirth Purity

### Childbirth Dam T'fusa

Returning to the case of the woman dying in childbirth, the *gemara* now applies the *dam t'fusa* framework to explain the full range of positions.

### First Opinion

The first *tanna* treats the blood as *dam t'fusa*: blood that began flowing during life and continued after death. Because it is *dam t'fusa*, he imposes both stringencies — *tumas ketem* and *tumas ohel* — treating the blood as simultaneously *dam niddah* and *dam mes*.

### Mixed Timing View

Rabbi Yehuda agrees that in the childbirth case the blood began in life and some came out in death. That is precisely why he concedes *tumas ketem* here — he recognizes the blood's pre-mortem origins — and why he also imposes *ohel tumah*: the continuation of the flow into death, on the *dam t'fusa* model, gives the blood a double status.

## Post-Birth Purity

### Preparing Pesach Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: הַיּוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל דַּם טוֹהַר הָיְתָה מְעָרָה מַיִם לְפֶסַח.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Initially** the Sages **would say,** with regard to a woman who gave birth and observed the seven or fourteen days of impurity for a male or female child, respectively, and then immersed in a ritual bath and **who is observing** the period **of the blood of purity,** that **she would pour water** from a vessel in her hands **to** rinse **the Paschal offering.** Although it is permitted for such a woman to engage in intercourse with her husband, her halakhic status is like that of one who immersed that day and the sun has not yet set. She therefore assumes second-degree ritual impurity and confers third-degree ritual impurity upon *teruma* with which she comes into contact. Consequently, she may touch the vessel, as second-degree ritual impurity does not render a vessel impure. She may not touch the water, as it is designated for rinsing the Paschal offering and therefore it is treated with the sanctity of sacrificial food, which is rendered impure by second-degree ritual impurity.

We now turn to a new *mishnah* that addresses the *tumah* and *tahara* status of a woman during her *yamei tahara* — the post-partum purity period of 33 days following the birth of a boy, or 66 days following the birth of a girl. During this period, any bleeding she experiences does not render her a *niddah*. The *mishnah* opens: *barishona hayu omrim ha-yosheves al dam tahor hayesah ma'areh mayim la-pesach* — originally they said that a woman sitting in her days of *dam tahor* was permitted to prepare the water used for the *korban pesach*.

This is a somewhat surprising leniency, given that the woman during this period is categorized as a *tevul yom* in an extended sense — she has already immersed in a *mikveh* after the initial *niddah* phase, but until the conclusion of the 33 or 66 day period she cannot handle *kodshim*, since she retains the status of a *sheini le-tumah* with respect to sacred items. One might think, therefore, that she should not be permitted to handle water destined for use in preparation of the *korban pesach*.

### Tevul Yom Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַגַּע טְמֵא מֵת לְקָדָשִׁים, כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אַף כִּטְמֵא מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **then said:** Her status **is like** that of one who came into **contact with one impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse,** i.e., one with first-degree ritual impurity, who renders **consecrated** items impure. But with regard to all non-sacred items, even non-sacred items treated with the sanctity of sacrificial food, such as the water used to rinse the Paschal offering, she has second-degree ritual impurity. Consequently, it is permitted for her to touch not only the vessel, but the water inside it as well, **in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai say:** The status of the woman is **even like** that of one who is **impure** due to contact with **a corpse,** who is a primary source of ritual impurity and renders even a non-sacred vessel impure.

The tension is as follows. A *sheini* who comes into contact with liquid ordinarily makes that liquid a *rishon*. However, a *tevul yom* has a more attenuated status: contact between a *tevul yom* and ordinary *chullin* liquid raises no concern. The question is whether the water being prepared for the *pesach* sacrificial process is to be treated as *kodshim* liquid — in which case even a *tevul yom* would render it *tamei* — or as *chullin* liquid, in which case there is no problem whatsoever.

The original position held that *chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh ke-kodesh dami* — when *chullin* is prepared under the conditions of *kodesh* purity, it acquires a quasi-*kodesh* status. On that basis, the water being prepared for the *pesach* was treated with the strictures of *kodshim*, and the woman could not touch it directly. She could, however, handle the vessels — because the vessels themselves retained *chullin* status and were not subject to the elevated rules of *kodshim*. Her practical contribution was therefore limited: she could pour water from one vessel to another, but not place her hands in direct contact with the water.

### Rishon Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' מְעָרָה — אִין, נוֹגַעַת — לָא. אַלְמָא: חוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ — כְּקֹדֶשׁ דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: A woman who gave birth, observed the days of impurity, immersed in a ritual bath, and who is now observing the period of the blood of purity **would pour** water from a vessel in her hands to rinse the Paschal offering. The Gemara infers: Pouring from a vessel, **yes,** this is permitted, but she may **not touch** the water itself. **Apparently, non-sacred** produce, e.g., even liquid such as the water for rinsing the Paschal offering, **that was prepared according to** the level of **ritual purity** required **for sacrificial** food, i.e., with the same stringencies as required for sacrificial food, **is considered like sacrificial** food.

*Chazru lomar*: they retracted. The later position was *harei hi ke-maga tamei mes le-kodshim* — for purposes of *kodshim* specifically, she is treated as though she had touched someone who touched a corpse, effectively as a *rishon le-tumah* for sacred items. This represents a stringency with respect to *kodshim* — she is now treated more severely when she handles genuinely sacred items. But the retraction also carries a leniency: because *chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh* is no longer treated as *kodshim*, the water prepared for the *pesach* is ordinary *chullin*, and she may now handle it directly.

### Chullin Tahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַגַּע טְמֵא מֵת לְקָדָשִׁים — לְקָדָשִׁים אִין, לְחוּלִּין לָא, אַלְמָא: חוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ לָאו כְּקֹדֶשׁ דָּמוּ!
>
> **Translation:** But now **say the latter clause** of the mishna: The Sages **then said:** Her status **is like** that of one who came into **contact with one impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse,** i.e., one with first-degree ritual impurity, who renders **consecrated** items impure. This indicates that **with regard to consecrated** items, **yes,** she renders them impure, but **with regard to non-sacred** items, e.g., the water used to rinse the Paschal offering, she does **not** render them impure. **Apparently, non-sacred** produce **that was prepared according to** the level of **ritual purity** required **for sacrificial** food **is not considered like sacrificial** food.

The *gemara* observes that the two positions involve not merely a change in her personal status but a change in how the water itself is categorized. The original position treated the water as having the stringencies of *kodshim* (hence she could not touch it). The later position treats the water as pure *chullin* (hence she can). The *gemara* identifies this as a tension: *re'isha* seems to imply the water is like *kodshim*, while *seifa* implies it is not. The resolution is attributed to Abba Shaul — the *mishnah* reflects Abba Shaul's position — who holds that a *tevul yom* has *rishon* status only *le-kodshim*, not for *chullin*.

### Pouring Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? אַבָּא שָׁאוּל הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: טְבוּל יוֹם תְּחִילָּה לַקֹּדֶשׁ, לְטַמֵּא שְׁנַיִם וְלִפְסוֹל אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the latter clause of **the mishna? It is** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Abba Shaul says:** With regard to **one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed, he has the status of one who is impure with **first-**degree impurity **with regard to sacrificial** food, in **that** he **renders two** items of sacrificial food **impure, and disqualifies one** additional item. In other words, the first item of sacrificial food that he touches assumes the status of second-degree impurity. A second item that comes into contact with the first item assumes third-degree impurity. A third item that comes into contact with the second assumes fourth-degree impurity and is therefore disqualified from being eaten, but it does not impart impurity to other items.

Thus, in the original position, she could pour the water — managing the vessels — but not touch it. In the later position, she can touch the water freely, because it is purely *chullin* and she is only a *tevul yom*. The practical upshot, from the perspective of the final halachah, is that this woman can participate in preparing the *pesach* waters in a meaningful way.

## Permitted Actions

### Maaser & Challah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' וּמוֹדִים שֶׁהִיא אוֹכֶלֶת בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, וְקוֹצָה לָהּ חַלָּה, וּמַקֶּפֶת וְקוֹרָא לָהּ שֵׁם, וְאִם נָפַל מֵרוּקָּהּ וּמִדַּם טׇהֳרָהּ עַל כִּכָּר שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And** Beit Shammai **concede** to Beit Hillel that a woman observing the days of purity **partakes of** second-**tithe** produce, as it is permitted for one who immersed that day to eat second-tithe produce. **And she separates** part of **her** dough as ***ḥalla*, and she draws** the vessel with the part of the dough that she separated **near** the rest of the dough, **and she designates it** with the **name** of *ḥalla*. **And** Beit Shammai further concede to Beit Hillel **that if** a drop **from her saliva or from the blood of purity fell onto a loaf of *teruma*,** the loaf **is ritually pure,** as any liquid discharged from the body of one who immersed that day is ritually pure.

The *mishnah* continues: *u-modim she-hi ochelet be-ma'aser* — all opinions agree that during the *dam tahor* period she may eat *ma'aser*. She is, at most, a *sheini le-tumah* in her *tevul yom* status, and a *sheini* may eat *ma'aser*. The *gemara* confirms this from the general rule: *taval ve-alah ochel be-ma'aser* — a *tevul yom* who has immersed may eat *ma'aser*.

She may also separate *challah* — *ve-kotzah lah challah*. The reasoning requires some explanation. *Challah* separated from dough has the status of *terumah*, and she cannot touch *terumah* directly as a *sheini*. However, the procedure is as follows: she physically separates the portion intended for *challah* before it has been designated as such — at that point it is still pure *chullin*, and she may handle it freely. She then brings the separated piece close to the main body of dough — *u-makefet* — fulfilling the requirement of *mitzvah litrom min ha-mukaf*, that *terumah* should ideally be separated from adjacent quantities.

### Virtual Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹרָא לָהּ שֵׁם, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: נִגְזוֹר, דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְמִנְּגַּע בַּהּ מֵאַבָּרַאי? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **And she designates it** with the **name** of *ḥalla*. The Gemara explains that it is necessary for the *tanna* to teach this *halakha*, as it might **enter your mind to say: Let us decree lest she comes to touch** the *ḥalla* **from the outside.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that there is no such decree.

With the small piece of future *challah* physically adjacent to the larger dough, she then designates it as *challah* — *ve-koreit lah shem* — while no longer touching it. The designation creates the *challah* status at the moment she is not in contact with it. This is a form of virtual separation: the physical proximity establishes the necessary connection to the parent dough, while the verbal designation at a moment of non-contact avoids the problem of a *sheini* touching *terumah*. One might worry that after the designation she might inadvertently touch the newly created *challah* — the *gemara* notes this concern but dismisses it as insufficient to prohibit the practice.

### Spit & Blood Safe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נָפַל מֵרוּקָּהּ וּמִדַּם טׇהֳרָהּ עַל כִּכָּר שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר. דִּתְנַן: מַשְׁקֵה טְבוּל יוֹם מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצְאִין מִמֶּנּוּ (כְּמַשְׁקִין) [כְּמַשְׁקֶה] הַנּוֹגֵעַ בָּהֶם, וְאֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ אֵינָן מְטַמְּאִין, חוּץ מִמַּשְׁקֵה הַזָּב שֶׁהוּא אַב הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And** Beit Shammai concede to Beit Hillel that **if** a drop **from her saliva** or from the blood of purity fell onto a loaf of *teruma*, that the loaf is ritually pure, as any liquid discharged from the body of one who immersed that day is ritually pure. The Gemara explains that the reason is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Tevul Yom* 2:1): The *halakha* with regard to **liquids** that emanate **from one who immersed himself that day** from his state of ritual impurity **is like** that of **liquids that he touches. And** both **these and those,** the liquids that emanated from him and the liquids that he touched, **do not transmit ritual impurity.** This is the *halakha* **except for liquids** that come **from a *zav*, who is a primary source of ritual impurity.**

*Ve-nafal me-rokah u-mi-dam tahor al kikar shel terumah, she-hu tahor* — if her saliva or blood from the *dam tahor* period falls on *terumah*, the *terumah* remains *tahor*. The reason: although she is a *sheini le-tumah*, her bodily fluids in this state — spit and blood — do not convey *tumah* to the *terumah*. She is not an active *niddah* or *zav* at this stage; those statuses, which would render bodily fluids as *av ha-tumah*, do not apply to her now.

### Tevul Yom Limited

The *gemara* clarifies from a *mishnah*: *mashkeh tevul yom ke-mashkin ha-nogea bahen* — the bodily fluids of a *tevul yom* have the same status as liquids that the *tevul yom* has touched. And since a *tevul yom* who touches non-*terumah* liquid has no effect on it, the bodily fluids also have no effect. The exception is *mashkeh ha-zav* — the bodily fluids of an active *zav* or *niddah*, which are an *av ha-tumah* — but this woman is no longer in that category.

## Final Immersion

### Beit Shammai Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: צְרִיכָה טְבִילָה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה טְבִילָה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** There is another dispute between the *tanna'im* with regard to a woman who completed her days of purity. **Beit Shammai say:** Her immersion at the end of the days of impurity does not render it permitted for her to enter the Temple or to partake of *teruma*; rather, **she requires immersion** even **at the conclusion** of the days of purity. **And Beit Hillel say: She does not require immersion at the conclusion** of the days of purity to render it permitted for her to partake of *teruma*, as the immersion at the end of the days of impurity is sufficient.

At the conclusion of the 33 or 66 day period, when the woman's *tevul yom* status fully expires and she becomes completely *tehorah*, Beit Shammai holds *tzrichah tvilah ba-acharonah* — she is required to immerse in a *mikveh*. This immersion is not strictly necessary to effect her *tahara* — she becomes fully *tahor* automatically at the close of the period — but Beit Shammai requires it as a formal demarcation of the change in status.

### Beit Hillel Not Required

Beit Hillel holds *einah tzrichah tvilah ba-acharonah* — she is not required to immerse at the end. The transition to full *tahara* is automatic, and no ceremonial immersion is mandated.

### Rav Katina Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' דְּאָמַר מָר: טָבַל וְעָלָה — אוֹכֵל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: טְבוּל יוֹם אָרוֹךְ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that Beit Shammai concede to Beit Hillel that the woman observing the days of purity partakes of second-tithe produce. The Gemara explains that the reason is **as the Master said:** When the period of ritual impurity of a *zav* or leper has been completed, and he **immersed** during the day **and ascended** from the ritual bath, he **may partake of** second **tithe** immediately. **Rav Ketina said:** The difference **between them** is with regard to the status of **one who immersed that day,** i.e., at the end of his time of impurity, and **a long** time has passed since the immersion. Beit Shammai hold that as a long time has passed, i.e., thirty-three days after the birth of a boy or sixty-six days after the birth of a girl, she may not rely on that immersion. Beit Hillel maintain that she may still rely on that initial immersion.

*Amar Rav Katina: tevul yom aroch ika beinaihu* — what is at stake between them, says Rav Katina, is the concept of an "extended *tevul yom*." An ordinary *tevul yom* immersed in the morning and is fully *tahor* by nightfall; after a normal night-and-day cycle the transition is obvious and automatic. But this woman's *tevul yom* status extends for 33 or 66 days. At the end of that extended period, one might argue that the formal act of immersion is necessary precisely because the prolonged duration could cause confusion — people might forget exactly when the period ended, or what her current status is. A visible act of immersion would mark the transition unambiguously.

### Rashi Clarification

Rashi, drawing on a parallel discussion elsewhere in the *gemara*, notes that even Beit Hillel agrees that the woman should immerse before handling *kodshim* — the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concerns specifically immersion before handling *terumah*. Beit Shammai requires that formal immersion before she may resume handling *terumah*; Beit Hillel does not.
