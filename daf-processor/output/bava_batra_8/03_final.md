# Bava Batra 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gates & Walls

### Guard's Gate Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹת בֵּית שַׁעַר וָדֶלֶת לֶחָצֵר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא כׇּל הַחֲצֵרוֹת רְאוּיוֹת לְבֵית שַׁעַר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The residents of a courtyard **can compel** each inhabitant of that courtyard **to** financially participate in the **building of a gatehouse and a door to the** jointly owned **courtyard. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** disagrees and **says: Not all courtyards require a gatehouse.**

The daf opens by picking up from the *mishna* on 7b, transitioning from the obligation to build a dividing wall within a shared courtyard — motivated by concerns of *hezek re'iyah*, the prohibition against invading a neighbor's visual privacy — toward a broader framework of communal responsibility. The *mishna* establishes that members of a shared *chatzer* bear mutual obligations to protect their common space: *kofin oso livnos beis sha'ar u'deles le'chatzer* — one forces any member of the courtyard to participate in building a guard's gate and a door for the *chatzer*, protecting it both from passersby who might look in and from potential intruders.

### Shimon ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא כׇּל הַחֲצֵרוֹת רְאוּיוֹת לְבֵית שַׁעַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** disagrees and **says: Not all courtyards require a gatehouse.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents: *lo kol ha-chatzerot re'uyot le-veis sha'ar* — not every courtyard actually requires a guard's gate. As the *Gemara* will clarify, his reasoning appears connected to the *hezek re'iyah* concern: a courtyard that does not open directly onto the main public thoroughfare presents a lesser degree of exposure, and therefore a lesser need for such a structure.

### City Wall Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹת לָעִיר חוֹמָה וּדְלָתַיִם וּבְרִיחַ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא כׇּל הָעֲיָירוֹת רְאוּיוֹת לְחוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the residents of a city **can compel** each inhabitant of that city **to** contribute to the **building of a wall, double doors, and a crossbar for the city. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** disagrees and **says: Not all towns require a wall.**

The *mishna* then moves from the courtyard to the *ir*, the town. Here too the principle of compulsion applies: *kofin oso livnos le'ir chomah u'dlatayim u'vriach* — one forces the members of a town to contribute toward building a wall, doors, and a crossbar to secure the town. The terminology is worth noting: an *ir* is a town, smaller than a *kerach* (a city) and larger than a *kefar* (a village). Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel again limits the obligation — *lo kol ha-ayarot re'uyot le-choma* — not every town requires a wall. In the *braita*, his view is refined: a town near the border (*sefar*) genuinely needs a wall, while one far from the border does not. The Rabbis, characteristically, are more cautious: *zimnin de-mikru ve-atu gaisai* — troops can arrive even in places not on the frontier, and therefore the need for defensive walls may arise anywhere. Notably absent from this discussion is any mention of protection against thieves, robbers, or wild animals — the concern is framed specifically in terms of military threat.

### Residency Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה יְהֵא בָּעִיר וִיהֵא כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר? שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. קָנָה בָּהּ בֵּית דִּירָה – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this latter obligation, the mishna asks: **How long** must one live **in the city to be** considered **like** one of **the people of the city** and therefore obligated to contribute to these expenses? **Twelve months.** But if he **bought** himself **a residence in** the city, **he is immediately** considered **like** one of **the people of the city.**

The *mishna* then addresses the threshold of belonging: *kama yehei ba-ir v'yehei ke'anshei ha-ir* — how long must one reside in a town before being considered one of its inhabitants and therefore obligated in its communal taxes? The answer is twelve months. An exception applies to one who has purchased a permanent dwelling: *kanah bah beis dirah harei hu ke'anshei ha-ir mi-yad* — purchasing a home makes one an inhabitant immediately, regardless of duration of residence.

This section raises questions that the text itself leaves conspicuously open. The *mishna* identifies certain communal needs — a *beis sha'ar*, a *chomah* — that in some cases can be imposed on all residents by compulsion. But the precise mechanism of communal governance is not spelled out. Who decides when the obligation arises? Is it a majority vote? Elected representatives? The *mishna* gestures toward these questions without resolving them.

## Gates & Poor Access

### Pious Sage Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמֵימְרָא דְּבֵית שַׁעַר מְעַלְּיוּתָא הִיא?! וְהָא הָהוּא חֲסִידָא דַּהֲוָה רְגִיל אֵלִיָּהוּ דַּהֲוָה מִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵיהּ; עֲבַד בֵּית שַׁעַר, וְתוּ לָא מִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** making **a gatehouse is beneficial? But** wasn't there **that pious man, with whom** the prophet **Elijah was accustomed to speak,** who **built a gatehouse, and** after-ward Elijah **did not speak with him again?**

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with a sharp challenge: *lemeimra de-beis sha'ar maylusa hi?* — is the *mishna* then asserting that a guard's gate is simply a good thing? But there is a famous story that complicates this. A certain *chasid* — a pious sage — was accustomed to regular conversations with *Eliyahu Hanavi*. When this man built a *beis sha'ar* for his courtyard, Elijah ceased speaking with him entirely. Rashi explains: the gate prevented the poor from entering the courtyard to seek *tzedakah*. A structure that serves the residents' privacy and security simultaneously walls out those who depend on their generosity.

### Multiple Resolutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא מִגַּוַּאי, הָא מִבָּרַאי. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא מִבָּרַאי, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ דֶּלֶת, הָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ דֶּלֶת. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ דֶּלֶת, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ פּוֹתַחַת, הָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ פּוֹתַחַת. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ פּוֹתַחַת, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּפוֹתַחַת דִּידֵיהּ מִגַּוַּאי, הָא דְּפוֹתַחַת דִּידֵיהּ מִבָּרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** This is **not difficult: This,** the case presented in the mishna, is referring to a gatehouse built **on the inside** of the courtyard, in which case the poor can at least reach the courtyard's entrance and be heard inside the courtyard; **that,** the story of the pious man and Elijah, involves a gatehouse that was built **on the outside** of the courtyard, completely blocking the poor's access to the courtyard's entrance. **And if you wish, say** instead that in **both** cases the gatehouse was built **outside** the courtyard, **and** yet this is **not difficult:** In the one case, **there is a door** to the gatehouse, so that the poor cannot be heard inside the courtyard, while in the other case **there is no door. Or if you wish, say** that in **both** cases **there is a door, and** still this is **not difficult:** In the one case, **there is a key** needed to open the door, and the key is not available to the poor people, whereas in the other case, **there is no key** needed. **Or if you wish, say** that in **both** cases **there is a key** needed, **and** even so this is **not difficult:** In the one case **the key is on the inside,** so that the poor cannot reach it, while in the other case of the mishna, **the key is on the outside.**

The *Gemara* offers a series of resolutions to this tension, each carefully distinguishing configurations of the gatehouse that either preserve or eliminate access for the *ani*.

The first resolution: *lo kashya ha mi-gavai ha mi-barai* — there is no contradiction, because the cases differ based on whether the gatehouse is positioned on the inside or outside of the courtyard. Rashi explains that a gatehouse placed on the inside, behind the door, fully blocks the *ani* from entering. A gatehouse on the outside, by contrast, allows the *ani* at minimum to enter the structure itself and make his presence known, even without gaining full access to the courtyard.

The second resolution: *i ba'is eima, ha ve-ha mi-barai* — assume both gatehouses are on the outside; the question then becomes whether there is a door behind the gatehouse. If there is a door, the *ani* is still blocked from the interior. If there is no such door, the *ani* can enter the gatehouse and seek assistance there.

The third resolution: even if both cases have a door behind the gatehouse, the question becomes whether there is an opening (*putcha*) allowing entry into the gatehouse itself. Without such an opening, access is fully denied.

The fourth resolution: even if there is an opening in both cases, the crucial question is whether that opening faces inward or outward — *ha de-putcha didei mi-gavai ha de-putcha didei mi-barai*. If the opening faces inward, only those already inside the courtyard can access the gatehouse; those approaching from the outside cannot enter. If it faces outward, the approaching *ani* at least has a portal through which to make himself known and request help.

The deeper issue this series of resolutions surfaces is the irreducible tension at the heart of communal infrastructure: how does one protect residents — their property, their privacy — without foreclosing access for those who depend on communal generosity? The *Gemara* does not resolve this tension definitively; it maps the range of physical configurations that navigate it.

One question this raises is how Rashi knew the story was specifically about the *ani* being excluded. No explicit explanation is provided in the text itself, and it may reflect either a received tradition or the only available inference from the narrative's logic.

It is also worth clarifying a potential source of confusion: this entire discussion about the *beis sha'ar* and the *ani* concerns the gatehouse of the *chatzer* — the courtyard — not the gatehouse of the town. The town gate is a different structure: a large gate with heavy doors and a crossbar. The *ani* moving through a town goes from courtyard to courtyard seeking *tzedakah*; it is the courtyard gate that blocks or permits his access.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא כָּל חֲצֵרוֹת רְאוּיוֹת לְבֵית שַׁעַר; אֶלָּא חָצֵר הַסְּמוּכָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – רְאוּיָה לְבֵית שַׁעַר, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ סְמוּכָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – אֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לְבֵית שַׁעַר. וְרַבָּנַן – זִימְנִין דְּדָחֲקִי בְּנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְעָיְילוּ וְאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Not all courtyards require a gatehouse. Rather, a courtyard that adjoins the public domain requires a gatehouse** to prevent people from peering in. **But** a courtyard **that does not adjoin the public domain does not require a gatehouse.** The Gemara asks: **And** why don't **the Rabbis** make this distinction? The Gemara answers: Even if a courtyard does not adjoin the public domain, **people in the public domain sometimes are forced** toward the courtyard due to crowding in the public domain, **and come and enter** the courtyard.

The *braita* then restates Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling about courtyards: *lo kol ha-chatzerot re'uyot le-veis sha'ar; ella chatzer ha-semucha le-reshut ha-rabim re'uya le-veis sha'ar* — only a courtyard adjacent to the public thoroughfare genuinely needs a guard's gate. The Rabbis again push back: even a courtyard set back from the main road may sometimes attract unauthorized visitors — *zimnin de-mishtakchei benei reshut ha-rabim ve-ayilu ve-yasvu* — and therefore all courtyards may reasonably be subject to this obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹת לָעִיר כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת לָעִיר דְּלָתַיִם וּבְרִיחַ. וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא כָּל הָעֲיָירוֹת רְאוּיוֹת לְחוֹמָה; אֶלָּא עִיר הַסְּמוּכָה לַסְּפָר – רְאוּיָה לְחוֹמָה, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ סְמוּכָה לַסְּפָר – אֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לְחוֹמָה. וְרַבָּנַן – זִימְנִין דְּמִקְּרוּ וְאָתֵי גְּיָיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that the residents of a city **can compel** each inhabitant of that city **to build double doors and a crossbar for the city. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Not all cities require a wall. Rather, a city that adjoins the** state **border requires a wall, whereas** a city **that does not adjoin the** state **border does not require a wall.** The Gemara asks: **And** why don't **the Rabbis** make this distinction? The Gemara answers: Even if a city does not adjoin the border, **it sometimes happens that** invading **troops come** into the area. Therefore, it is always good for a city to be protected by a wall.

The *mishna*'s concluding section on city walls is then elaborated in parallel. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that only towns near the border (*sefar*) require walls; towns far from the frontier do not. The Rabbis hold that troops can appear anywhere and that no town is categorically exempt. This raises a broader governance question left unanswered by both *mishna* and *Gemara*: if twenty percent of the residents want a *beis sha'ar* and eighty percent do not — or vice versa — who decides?

## Taxation Principles

### Per Person vs. Wealth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֶׁהֵן גּוֹבִין, לְפִי נְפָשׁוֹת גּוֹבִין, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְפִי שֶׁבַח מָמוֹן גּוֹבִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי מָמוֹן גּוֹבִין; וְאֶלְעָזָר בְּנִי, קְבַע בָּהּ מַסְמְרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar asked Rabbi Yoḥanan: When** the residents of the city **collect** money to build a wall, **do they collect based on** the number of **people** living in each house, **or perhaps they collect based on the** net **worth** of each person? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: They collect based on the** net **worth** of each person, **and Elazar, my son,** you shall **fix nails in this,** i.e., this is an established *halakha*, and you must not veer from it.

With the framework of communal obligation established, the *Gemara* turns to the question of how the costs are to be distributed. *Ba'ei minei Rabbi Elazar mi-Rabbi Yochanan: keshen govin, lefi nefashot govin o dilma lefi shevach mamon govin* — when the town collects for communal needs, does it collect per capita, or proportional to wealth?

To frame the options: a per-capita levy is regressive — every person pays the same absolute amount regardless of means. A proportional levy — everyone paying the same percentage of their wealth — is a flat tax. A truly progressive tax, where rates increase with wealth, is not explicitly discussed. Rabbi Yochanan's answer is clear: *lefi mamon govin* — according to wealth. And he adds, with finality, *de-Elazar beni kava bah masmerot* — my son Elazar has hammered this in with nails; it is not an open question.

### Proximity vs. Income

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֶׁהֵן גּוֹבִין, לְפִי קֵירוּב בָּתִּים הֵן גּוֹבִין, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְפִי מָמוֹן גּוֹבִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי קֵירוּב בָּתִּים הֵן גּוֹבִין; וְאֶלְעָזָר בְּנִי, קְבַע בָּהּ מַסְמְרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rabbi Elazar asked Rabbi Yoḥanan: When they collect** money to build a wall, **do they collect based on the proximity of the houses** to the wall, so that those people who live closer to the wall pay more? **Or perhaps they collect based on the** net **worth** of each person. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: They collect based on the proximity of the houses** to the wall, **and Elazar, my son,** you shall **fix nails in this.**

A second version of the query is then introduced. Here it is already assumed that the tax is not regressive; the question shifts to whether the flat-wealth tax is the correct model, or whether proximity to the wall — and thus degree of benefit derived — is the more relevant factor. *Keshen govin, lefi keruv batim hen govin o dilma lefi mamon govin* — do we assess based on how close one's house is to the city wall, and therefore how much one benefits from it, or purely based on wealth? Rabbi Yochanan rules: *lefi keruv batim hen govin* — based on proximity to the wall. And again: *de-Elazar beni kava bah masmerot*.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot raises the obvious difficulty: if the second ruling is that we follow proximity, what happened to the first ruling that we follow wealth? Are these contradictory? Tosafot, citing Rabbeinu Tam, resolves this by treating the two principles as operating simultaneously. *Nosim aniyim krovim yoter mi-rechokim* — poor residents who live closer to the wall pay more than poor residents who live farther away. *Ve-chen ashirim krovim yoter me-ashirim rechokim* — wealthy residents who live closer pay more than wealthy residents who live farther. But crucially — *aval ashirim rechokim nosim yoter mi-aniyim krovim* — a wealthy resident who lives far from the wall still pays more than a poor resident who lives close to it. Wealth remains the more dominant factor; proximity modulates within each economic stratum, but does not override the primacy of ability to pay.

This produces a nuanced taxation model: both wealth and derived benefit are relevant variables, but they are weighted differently, with wealth carrying greater weight.

Tosafot then introduces a further principle that deepens the analysis considerably. The nature of the communal need may itself determine which factor predominates. *Keivan de-ein sakanat nefashot lo azlinan ela batar mamon* — since the primary purpose of a city wall is to protect property rather than lives (Tosafot understands that troops coming across the border are primarily raiders, not killers), we treat the wall as primarily an economic benefit, and therefore those with more property are the primary beneficiaries and must pay more accordingly. Tosafot cites a parallel ruling in tractate *Hagozel* (the later chapter of Bava Kamma) regarding a caravan that is attacked by troops in the desert: *mechashevin lefi mamon* — costs are allocated according to wealth, precisely because the troops came for money, not lives.

But *aval heicha de-tau ba-midbar* — if people are wandering lost in the desert and their lives are genuinely at risk, Tosafot rules that in that case we calculate *af lefi nefashot* — also per capita — because now every life is equally at stake and the per-person element returns as a meaningful factor, even if wealth still retains some role. Tosafot himself does not fully commit to a pure per-capita rule even in life-threatening situations; he describes a balance.

The deeper question Tosafot is navigating is this: when we say *lefi mamon*, do we mean this because those with more money are better able to pay, or because those with more property benefit more from the protection? Tosafot seems to say: both, and the mix depends on what the communal expenditure is actually for. This is a remarkable early articulation of the distinction between ability-to-pay and benefit-based theories of taxation.

## Rabbis & Tax Exemption

### Nesi'ah & Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה רְמָא דְּשׁוּרָא אַדְּרַבָּנַן. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: רַבָּנַן לָא צְרִיכִי נְטִירוּתָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶסְפְּרֵם מֵחוֹל יִרְבּוּן״ – אֶסְפְּרֵם לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לְצַדִּיקִים – דִּנְפִישִׁי מֵחָלָא; הַשְׁתָּא כּוּלְּהוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״כַּחוֹל אֲשֶׁר עַל שְׂפַת הַיָּם״, צַדִּיקִים עַצְמָם ״מֵחוֹל יִרְבּוּן״?!
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Rabbi Yehuda Nesia** once **imposed** payment of the tax for **the wall** even **on the Sages. Reish Lakish said to him: The Sages do not require protection, as it is written:** "How precious are your dear ones to me, O God…**If I should count them, they are more in number than the sand"** (Psalms 139:17–18). **If I should count whom? If we say** this is referring to **the righteous,** and the verse is saying **that they are greater in number than the** grains of **sand,** this is difficult. **Now** if **it is written about all of Israel: "As the sand which is upon the seashore"** (Genesis 22:17), can **the righteous themselves,** who are a part of Israel, **be greater in number than the** grains of **sand?** How can they possibly outnumber the grains of sand upon the seashore?

Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah (the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not to be confused with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi himself) imposed a tax for the city wall upon the rabbis. Resh Lakish objected: *rabbanan lo tsrikhei netiruta* — rabbis do not need the protection that the wall provides, because their Torah learning protects them. Since the basis for the tax obligation, as established above, is that one benefits from the protection, those who do not benefit are not obligated.

Resh Lakish's proof is from Psalms: *asaprem mechol yirbuyun* — "I will count them; they will multiply more than the sand." The *Gemara* first raises a difficulty with this reading: if even the entirety of Israel is compared to the sand of the sea, how can the righteous — a subset — be greater than the sand? The resolution: *asaprem le-ma'aseihem shel tsadikim, mechol yirbuyun* — we count not the righteous themselves but their righteous deeds, which are indeed more numerous than the sand. From this the *Gemara* builds a *kal va-chomer*: sand, though less numerous than the deeds of the righteous, nonetheless holds back the sea, functioning as a wall. The deeds of the righteous, which are far more numerous, must surely provide even greater protection. Therefore, *talmidei chachamim* have their own divine protection and require no city wall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶסְפְּרֵם לְמַעֲשֵׂיהֶם שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים, מֵחוֹל יִרְבּוּן. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה חוֹל, שֶׁמּוּעָט – מֵגֵין עַל הַיָּם; מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים, שֶׁהֵם מְרוּבִּים – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁמְּגִינִּים עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this** is what the verse **is saying:** If **I should count the deeds of the righteous, they are greater in number than the** grains of **sand. And** it follows by **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If the** grains of **sand, which are fewer** in number, **protect** the shore from **the sea,** barring it from flowing inland (see Jeremiah 5:22), **do not all the more so the deeds of the righteous, which are greater** in number, **protect them?** Consequently the Sages do not need additional protection.

When this argument came before Rabbi Yochanan, he said to Resh Lakish: why did you not use the verse from *Shir HaShirim* — *ani choma ve-shadai ke-migdalot* — I am a wall and my breasts are like towers?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תֵּימָא לֵיהּ מֵהָא: ״אֲנִי חוֹמָה וְשָׁדַי כַּמִּגְדָּלוֹת״; ״אֲנִי חוֹמָה״ – זוֹ תּוֹרָה, ״וְשָׁדַי כַּמִּגְדָּלוֹת״ –
>
> **Translation:** **When** Reish Lakish **came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and reported the exchange to him, Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: What is the reason** that **you did not quote this** verse **to him: "I am a wall and my breasts are like towers"** (Song of Songs 8:10), which may be explained as follows: **"I am a wall"; this** is referring to **the Torah. "And my breasts are like towers";**

*Ani choma zu Torah; ve-shadai ke-migdalot elu talmidei chachamim* — "I am a wall" refers to Torah, and "my breasts are like towers" refers to Torah scholars. This is a more direct image: Torah itself is the wall protecting the city, and the scholars are the towers of that wall. Of course, this protection seemingly applies only to the scholars themselves; the rest of the town still requires a physical wall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, סָבַר לַהּ כִּדְדָרֵשׁ רָבָא: ״אֲנִי חוֹמָה״ – זוֹ כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, ״וְשָׁדַי כַּמִּגְדָּלוֹת״ – אֵלּוּ בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **these are Torah scholars,** and towers do not require additional protection? The Gemara comments: **And Reish Lakish,** who did not cite this verse, **holds** in accordance with the way that **Rava expounded** the verse: **"I am a wall"; this** is referring to **the Congregation of Israel. "And my breasts are like towers"; these are the synagogues and study halls.**

Resh Lakish explains why he did not use this verse: he follows Rava's interpretation of the same passage. *Ani choma zu Knesset Yisrael; ve-shadai ke-migdalot elu batei knesiyot u-vatei midrashot* — "I am a wall" refers to the entire congregation of Israel, and "my breasts are like towers" refers to synagogues and study halls. In Rashi's reading, this interpretation holds that the *chomah* is not about individual scholars being protected, but about the entire Jewish people constituting a unified barrier against assimilation among the nations — *godrin atzman mil-hitame bein ovdei kochavim*. This is a sweeping statement about communal spiritual identity, but in Rava's reading the verse cannot be used to exempt scholars specifically from the wall tax.

### Poll Tax Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא רְמָא כְּרָגָא אַרַבָּנַן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: עֲבַרְתְּ אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וְאַדִּנְבִיאֵי וְאַדִּכְתוּבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It is similarly related that **Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda** once **imposed** payment of **the** poll **tax [*karga*]** even **on the Sages. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to him: You have transgressed** the words of **the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings.**

The *Gemara* then presents a parallel episode. Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda imposed a *karga* — a poll tax, or head tax — upon the rabbis. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak protested vigorously: *avarta d'Oraita v'd-Nevi'ei v'd-Ktuvai* — you have transgressed obligations rooted in the Torah, in the Prophets, and in the Writings.

### Biblical Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – דִּכְתִיב: ״אַף חֹבֵב עַמִּים כׇּל קְדֹשָׁיו בְּיָדֶךָ״ – אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאַתָּה מְחַבֵּב עַמִּים – כׇּל קְדוֹשָׁיו יִהְיוּ בְּיָדֶךָ. ״וְהֵם תֻּכּוּ לְרַגְלֶךָ״ – תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמְּכַתְּתִים רַגְלֵיהֶם מֵעִיר לְעִיר וּמִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, ״יִשָּׂא מִדַּבְּרוֹתֶיךָ״ – לִישָּׂא וְלִיתֵּן בְּדִבּוּרוֹתָיו שֶׁל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** You have transgressed the words of **the Torah, as it is written: "Even when He loves the peoples, all His holy ones are in Your hand"** (Deuteronomy 33:3), which is understood to mean that **Moses said to the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, even when You hold the** other **nations dear** and grant them dominion over Israel, let **"all His holy ones,"** meaning the Torah scholars, **be** exclusively **in Your hand** and free from the authority of the nations, and therefore be exempt from paying taxes. The continuation of that verse can also be understood as referring to Torah scholars, as it states: **"And they sit [*tukku*] at Your feet,** receiving Your words" (Deuteronomy 33:3), and **Rav Yosef teaches: These are Torah scholars who pound [*mekhatetim*] their feet from city to city and from country to country to study Torah; "receiving [*yissa*] Your words," to discuss [*lissa velitten*] the utterances of God.**

The Toraitic violation derives from Devarim: *af chovev amim kol kedoshav be-yadecha* — even when God shows favor to the other nations, His holy ones remain in His hands. The *Gemara* reads this as Moshe's plea before God: *ribbono shel olam, afilu be-sha'at she-atah mechabev amim, kol kedoshav yiheyu be-yadecha* — even when You empower the nations over Israel, protect Your righteous ones. Rav Yosef adds: *ve-heim tuku le-raglecha* — "they have nestled at Your feet" — refers to *talmidei chachamim* who wear out their legs traveling from city to city and from country to country *lilmod Torah*, in order to engage in *give-and-take* of Torah discourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדִּנְבִיאֵי – דִּכְתִיב: ״גַּם כִּי יִתְנוּ בַגּוֹיִם עַתָּה אֲקַבְּצֵם, וַיָּחֵלּוּ מְּעָט מִמַּשָּׂא מֶלֶךְ וְשָׂרִים״ – אָמַר עוּלָּא: פָּסוּק זֶה בִּלְשׁוֹן אֲרָמִית נֶאֱמַר: ״אִי תָּנוּ״ כּוּלְּהוּ – ״עַתָּה אֲקַבְּצֵם״, וְאִם ״מְעַט״ מֵהֶם – ״יָחֵלּוּ מִמַּשָּׂא מֶלֶךְ וְשָׂרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** And you have transgressed the words of **the Prophets, as it is written: "Though they have hired lovers [*yitnu*] among the nations, now I will gather them, and they will begin to be diminished by reason of the burden of kings and princes"** (Hosea 8:10). With regard to this verse, **Ulla says:** Part of **this verse is stated in the Aramaic language;** the word *yitnu* should be understood here in its Aramaic sense: To learn. And the verse should be interpreted as follows: **If all** of Israel **learns** Torah, **I will gather them** already **now; and if** only **a few of them** learn Torah, **they will be excused from the burden** imposed **by kings and princes.** This indicates that those who study Torah should not be subject to paying taxes.

The violation from the Prophets comes from Hoshea: *gam ki yitnu va-goyim ata akabtzem; va-yachelu me'at mi-massa melech ve-sarim*. Ulla offers a creative reading: the word *yitnu* should be read through its Aramaic cognate *tena* — to learn, as in *Mishnah*. The verse then means: *i tanu kulhu* — if all of them are engaged in Torah study, I will gather them now (the *mashiach* will come). And *ve-im me'at meihem* — if only a small number are learning, *va-yachelu me'at mi-massa melech ve-sarim* — those few will be relieved, exempted, from the burden of kings and princes. The word *va-yachelu* is read as related to *lo yachal devaro* — to void, to exempt from an obligation. This is a meaningfully different kind of argument: it is not claiming that scholars are protected and therefore do not benefit from a wall; it is claiming that scholars simply deserve to be freed from taxation as a matter of honor — a point that has broader applicability than protection-based exemptions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדִּכְתוּבֵי – דִּכְתִיב: ״מִנְדָּה בְלוֹ וַהֲלָךְ, לָא שַׁלִּיט לְמִרְמֵא עֲלֵיהֹם״; וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״מִנְדָּה״ – זוֹ מְנָת הַמֶּלֶךְ, ״בְּלוֹ״ – זוֹ כְּסַף גֻּולְגָּלְתָּא, ״וַהֲלָךְ״ – זוֹ אַרְנוֹנָא.
>
> **Translation:** And furthermore, you have transgressed the words of **the Writings, as it is written: "It shall not be lawful to impose tribute, impost or toll upon them"** (Ezra 7:24), i.e., upon the priests and Levites who serve in the Temple. This *halakha* would apply to Torah scholars as well. **And Rav Yehuda says: "Tribute"; this** is referring to **the king's portion,** a tax given to the king. **"Impost"; this** is referring to **the head tax. "Toll"; this** is referring to **a tax [*arnona*]** paid with property that was imposed from time to time.

The violation from the Writings comes from Ezra: *minida, belo, ve-halach, lo shalit le-mirma aleihem* — it shall not be lawful to impose various forms of taxation upon the temple servants. Rav Yehuda identifies these three taxes: *minida* is *m'nas hamelech*, the king's portion; *belo* is *kesef gulgalta*, the head tax; and *halach* is the *arnona*. Rashi identifies the *arnona* as an income tax on produce and animals collected annually; other authorities identify it as a levy for provisioning the king's retinue as it passes through. In either reading, the point is clear: communal leaders and religious figures have a recognized exemption from royal taxation.

The distinction between the earlier arguments and these latter two is significant. The earlier proofs — that Torah protects scholars, that divine favor shields them — are specifically relevant to taxes for physical protection. They argue that scholars do not need the benefit and therefore are not obligated. The latter proofs — from the Prophets and the Writings — argue for a more general exemption from taxation as a dignitary matter, not predicated on lack of benefit. A *nafka mina* between these two types of arguments will emerge shortly.

### Tax Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַכֹּל לַאֲגַלֵּי גָפָא, אֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי; אֲבָל רַבָּנַן – לָא צְרִיכִי נְטִירוּתָא. הַכֹּל לְכַרְיָא פַּתְיָא, אֲפִילּוּ מֵרַבָּנַן; וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא נָפְקִי בְּכָלוֹזָא, אֲבָל נָפְקִי בְּכָלוֹזָא – רַבָּנַן לָאו בְּנֵי מִיפָּק בְּכָלוֹזָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: All** of the city's residents must contribute **to** the building and upkeep of **the city gates [*le'aglei gappa*],** and for this purpose money is collected **even from orphans. But the Sages do not require protection** and are therefore exempt from this payment. **All** of the city's residents must contribute **to the digging of cisterns [*lekarya patya*],** and for this purpose money is collected **even from the Sages,** since they too need water. The Gemara comments: **And we said** this **only when** the people are **not** required to **go out en masse [*be'akhluza*]** and do the actual digging, but are obligated merely to contribute money for that purpose. **But if** the people are required to **go out en masse** and actually dig, **the Sages** are **not** expected to **go out** with them **en masse,** but rather they are exempt from such labor.

The *Gemara* now draws the critical distinction that organizes all the preceding material. *Hakol l'aglei gappa, afilu mi-yatmei* — everybody, even orphans, must contribute to the gates of the city. They are all protected by and benefit from those gates. *Rabbanan lo tsrikhei netiruta* — but the rabbis are exempt, because they do not need the protection.

*Hakol l'karya patya, afilu me-rabbanan* — everybody, including the rabbis, must contribute to the digging of wells. Rabbis drink water too; they derive tangible benefit, and therefore they must pay. The sole qualification: *delo nafik be-achluza* — this applies only to a monetary contribution. If the modality of the communal effort is not a tax but a public muster — *be-achluza*, a public call where everyone physically participates in the digging — then *rabbanan lo bnei mapakei be-achluza ninhu* — it is beneath the dignity of Torah scholars to appear in a public labor gang. They are exempt from personal physical participation, but not from financial contribution.

*Klala de-milta* — the governing principle is this: *kol milta de-it lehu hana'ah mineih, afilu mi-yatmei ve-afilu me-rabbanan* — anything from which they derive benefit, even orphans and even rabbis must contribute. The sole exception is protection-based taxes: because the rabbis' protection comes from their Torah, they are not beneficiaries of physical security measures and therefore bear no obligation to fund them.

This framework has clear contemporary resonance. The claim that *rabbanan lo tsrikhei netiruta* — that Torah protects scholars from physical harm — has historically been invoked in the *Chareidi* community to argue that full-time Torah scholars are exempt from military service in Israel. But this argument is more complicated than it appears. As Rav Aharon Lichtenstein argued in his famous essay *Ve-Zot Torat He-Hesder*, the argument proves far too little if it is applied selectively. If a scholar genuinely lives at a level of *emuna* at which he trusts entirely in divine protection — not locking his door, not worrying about employment, not carrying insurance — then the claim has integrity. But if the same scholar takes every ordinary precaution in daily life and invokes *rabbanan lo tsrikhei netiruta* only when it comes to army service, the argument is not being applied consistently.

More fundamentally, Rav Lichtenstein observed that the *Gemara*'s entire framework concerns tax obligations that arise from being a beneficiary of a service. One pays for the wall if one benefits from the wall. One is exempt if one does not benefit. But military service is categorically different: it is not undertaken to protect oneself. It is undertaken out of an obligation to protect others — framed either as *Milchemet Mitzvah* or as *pikuach nefesh* toward fellow Jews. One cannot say "I don't need to be protected, therefore I need not serve," because the obligation to serve is not premised on one's own need for protection. Just as no one would say "I am a rabbi, therefore I need not drive someone to the hospital on *Shabbat* in a life-threatening situation," the exemption from benefiting from a wall cannot translate into an exemption from an obligation owed to others.

A related argument has been offered within the *Chareidi* community itself: that Torah learning provides protection not merely for the scholar personally but for all of *Am Yisrael*. This is a genuine reframing of the issue — moving away from "I don't need protection" toward "my Torah learning is my contribution to the collective protection of Israel." This is a more substantive argument, though it does not derive from this *Gemara* directly; it reframes the *Gemara*'s logic rather than applying it straightforwardly.

## Rabbi's Warehouse Story

### Yonatan's Deception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי פָּתַח אוֹצָרוֹת בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת, אָמַר: יִכָּנְסוּ בַּעֲלֵי מִקְרָא, בַּעֲלֵי מִשְׁנָה, בַּעֲלֵי תַלְמוּד, בַּעֲלֵי הֲלָכָה, בַּעֲלֵי הַגָּדָה; אֲבָל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ אַל יִכָּנְסוּ. דָּחַק רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עַמְרָם וְנִכְנַס. אָמַר לוֹ: ״רַבִּי, פַּרְנְסֵנִי!״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״בְּנִי, קָרִיתָ?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״לָאו״. ״שָׁנִיתָ?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״לָאו״. ״אִם כֵּן, בַּמָּה אֲפַרְנְסֶךָ?״ [אָמַר לוֹ:] ״פַּרְנְסֵנִי כְּכֶלֶב וּכְעוֹרֵב״. פַּרְנְסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi once **opened** his **storehouses** to distribute food **during years of drought. He said: Masters of Bible, masters of Mishna, masters of Talmud, masters of *halakha*, masters of *aggada* may enter** and receive food from me, **but ignoramuses should not enter. Rabbi Yonatan ben Amram,** whom Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did not know, **pushed** his way in, **and entered,** and **said to him: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **sustain me.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: My son, have you read** the Bible? Rabbi Yonatan ben Amram **said to him,** out of modesty: **No.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi continued: **Have you studied** Mishna? Once again, Rabbi Yonatan ben Amram **said to him: No.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi then asked him: **If so, by what** merit **should I sustain you?** Rabbi Yonatan ben Amram **said to him: Sustain me like a dog and like a raven,** who are given food even though they have not learned anything. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was moved by his words and **fed him.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a series of dramatic confrontations over whether *talmidei chachamim* — Torah scholars — are exempt from communal taxes. Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah (the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not to be confused with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi himself) imposed a tax for the city wall upon the rabbis. Resh Lakish objected: *rabbanan lo tsrikhei netiruta* — rabbis do not need the protection that the wall provides, because their Torah learning protects them. Since the basis for the tax obligation, as established above, is that one benefits from the protection, those who do not benefit are not obligated. However, the *Gemara* also presents stories of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi himself in his approach to community welfare and *tzedakah*. In the famous narrative that follows, we see both his initial stringency and his ultimate magnanimity.

### Yonatan Revealed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דִּנְפַק יְתֵיב רַבִּי וְקָא מִצְטַעַר, וְאָמַר: אוֹי לִי שֶׁנָּתַתִּי פִּתִּי לְעַם הָאָרֶץ! אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי: שֶׁמָּא יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עַמְרָם תַּלְמִידְךָ הוּא, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לֵיהָנוֹת מִכְּבוֹד תּוֹרָה מִיָּמָיו? בָּדְקוּ וְאַשְׁכַּח. אָמַר רַבִּי: יִכָּנְסוּ הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **After** Rabbi Yonatan **left, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **sat, and was distressed, and said: Woe is me, that I have given my bread to an ignoramus.** His son, **Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said to him: Perhaps he was your disciple Yonatan ben Amram, who never in his life wanted to** materially **benefit from the honor** shown to the **Torah? They investigated** the matter **and found** that such was the case. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi then **said: Let everyone enter,** as there may also be others who hide the fact that they are true Torah scholars.

After Yonatan left, Rabbi sat in distress: *oy li she-natati pitti le-am ha-aretz* — woe to me, I gave my bread to an illiterate person.

Rav Shimon, Rabbi's son, then said to him: *shema Yonatan ben Amram talmidecha hu, she-eino rotzeh leihanot mi-kvod Torah mi-yamav* — perhaps this is your own student Yonatan ben Amram, who has always refused to derive any personal benefit from the honor of Torah. Investigation confirmed this. Upon learning the truth, Rabbi reversed his policy entirely: *yikanesu ha-kol* — let everyone enter.

The interpretive texture here is rich. The *Gemara* follows a reading in which Rabbi was not necessarily wrong to feel troubled — his discomfort was not resolved by discovering that his general policy was mistaken, but specifically by learning that this particular man had deliberately concealed his scholarship, because he refused to benefit from Torah's honor. It was Yonatan's own principled choice to present himself as unlearned that occasioned the situation; Rabbi was not deceived in any morally problematic sense. Once Rabbi understood what had happened, his decision to open the food to everyone followed — though the exact reasoning for this reversal is not fully spelled out. One reading is that the encounter prompted Rabbi to reconsider his categorical policy; another, perhaps more textually accurate, is that the story does not actually establish that the categorical policy was wrong, since Rabbi's relief was specifically tied to discovering that his food had not in fact gone to an *am ha-aretz*.

### Illiteracy & Calamity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דַּאֲמַר רַבִּי: אֵין פּוּרְעָנוּת בָּא לָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Commenting on Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion, the Gemara notes that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi conformed **to his** standard line of **reasoning,** as **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Suffering comes to the world only due to ignoramuses.**

The *Gemara* then explains Rabbi's original position more sharply. *Rebbi leta'ameih, de-amar Rebbi: ein pur'anut ba'ah la-olam ella bishvil ammei ha-aretz* — Rabbi goes according to his own reasoning, as he held that calamity comes to the world specifically because of the unlearned. This frames his original reluctance not as a preference for Torah scholars in the allocation of limited charity, but as a genuine conviction that *ammei ha-aretz* are a source of harm to society, and that supporting them is therefore not straightforwardly a benefit to the community.

### King's Crown Tax

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּהַהוּא דְּמֵי כְלִילָא דִּשְׁדוֹ אַטְּבֶרְיָא, אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: ״לִיתְּבוּ רַבָּנַן בַּהֲדַן״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״לָא״. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: ״עָרְוקִינַן״. [אֲמַר לְהוּ:] ״עֲרוֹקוּ״. עֲרַקוּ פַּלְגֵיהוֹן, דַּלְיוּהּ פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like** the incident of **the crown tax [*kelila*] that was imposed on** the residents of the city of **Tiberias.** The heads of the city **came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and said to him: The Sages should contribute** along **with us.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: No,** the Sages are exempt. **They said to him:** Then **we will run away** and the entire burden will fall on the Torah scholars. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: Run away** as you please. **Half** of the city's residents **ran away.** The authorities then **waived half** the sum that they had initially imposed on the city.

A further story illustrates the dynamics of the rabbi-versus-*am ha-aretz* social structure in the context of taxation. A tax for the king's crown (*dmei kelila*) was levied on the town of Tiberias. The townspeople came to Rabbi and asked that the rabbis contribute together with them. Rabbi refused. The townspeople threatened to leave the city rather than bear the entire burden. Rabbi said: leave, what do I care? Half the population departed. The tax authorities reduced the levy by half, asking the remaining residents to pay the remainder. Those residents again came to Rabbi and again asked the rabbis to contribute. Rabbi again refused. Again they threatened to leave. Again he said: leave. The entire remaining population left. Only a single launderer remained, a non-rabbi who was not in a position to leave. The tax collectors placed the entire remaining levy upon him. He too fled. At that point, *paka kelila* — the tax dissolved. The authorities did not impose the levy on the rabbis alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ הָנְהוּ פַּלְגָא קַמֵּי דְּרַבִּי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: ״לִיתְּבוּ רַבָּנַן בַּהֲדַן״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״לָא״. ״עָרְוקִינַן״. ״עֲרוּקוּ״. עֲרַקוּ כּוּלְּהוּ, פָּשׁ הַהוּא כּוֹבֵס. שַׁדְיוּהּ אַכּוֹבֵס. עֲרַק כּוֹבֵס, פְּקַע כְּלִילָא. אָמַר רַבִּי: רְאִיתֶם שֶׁאֵין פּוּרְעָנוּת בָּא לָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **The half** of the population that remained in the city then **came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, and **said to him: The Sages should contribute** along **with us.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: No,** the Sages are exempt. They said to him: Then **we** too **will run away.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said to them: **Run away** as you please. **They all ran away,** so that only **one launderer was left** in the city. The authorities **imposed** the entire tax **on the launderer. The launderer** then **ran away** as well. **The crown tax was** then **canceled** in its entirety. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: You see** from this **that suffering comes to the world only due to ignoramuses,** for as soon as they all fled from the city, the crown tax was completely canceled.

This story is striking: it reflects a reality — whether of Roman-era Palestine or of other periods — in which religious scholars were in practice not required to pay royal taxes, at least in certain circumstances. The *Gemara* reads Rabbi's behavior as consistent with his view that the rabbis bear no obligation to contribute to taxes for physical protection, and further that the *ammei ha-aretz* bear the burden of communal taxation in a way that the rabbis do not.

The coda attributed to Rabbi (or according to some, to Shmuel bar Shilat in the name of Rav): *ilmalei ammei ha-aretz, lo hayinu meshalem kol kach* — were it not for the *ammei ha-aretz*, the rabbis would not be made to pay such taxes at all. The irony is pointed: it is precisely the economic activity of the unlearned — they produce the income, grow the food, do the labor — that generates the taxable base from which the crown demands payment.

The question of what happened to the large middle category — those who had learned some *pesukim* or *Mishnah* but were not *rabbanan* proper — is left unaddressed. The *Gemara* shifts between *rabbanan* and *ammei ha-aretz* as if these exhaust the population, when in reality the bulk of the community occupies intermediate positions.

One final observation: the *Rashash* notes that Rabbi's sequential questions — *karita? shanita?* — reveal that in the Talmudic period, one could know *Mishnah* without having properly learned *Tanakh*. The fact that Rabbi asked about *Mishnah* even after Yonatan claimed not to have read *pesukim* shows that the two were not assumed to be sequential prerequisites. The *Rashash* uses this to respond to those in later generations who mocked rabbis for being learned in *Gemara* while ignorant of *Tanakh*: this phenomenon is not novel.

## Residency Periods

### Caravan Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה יְהֵא בָּעִיר וִיהֵא כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהִי: הַחַמֶּרֶת וְהַגַּמֶּלֶת הָעוֹבֶרֶת מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, וְלָנָה בְּתוֹכָהּ וְהוּדְּחָה עִמָּהֶן, הֵן בִּסְקִילָה, וּמָמוֹנָן פָּלֵט.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And how long** must one live **in the city to be** considered **like** one of **the people of the city?** Twelve months. **And we raise a contradiction** from what is taught in a *baraita*: In the case of **a donkey caravan or a camel caravan that was journeying from place to place, and it lodged inside** an idolatrous city, **and its** members **were led astray** along **with** the other residents of the city, and they too engaged in idol worship, **they,** the members of the caravan, are liable to death **by stoning** like ordinary individual idolaters, **and their property escapes** destruction, i.e., they are not treated like the residents of an idolatrous city, who are liable to death by the sword and whose property is destroyed.

The *Gemara* now revisits the twelve-month threshold of the *mishna* and discovers that the picture is more complex. A *braita* seems to suggest that thirty days, not twelve months, can suffice for some purposes of membership in a community. The case: *ha-chameret ve-hagamelet ha-overet mi-makom le-makom* — a caravan of donkeys or camels passing from one town to another — stopped overnight in a particular city. The next day, that city was condemned as an *ir ha-nidachat* (a city that had gone over to idol worship), and the caravan members had participated in the forbidden worship.

What is their status? Are they individuals guilty of idolatry — in which case they are executed by stoning and their property is preserved — or are they members of the *ir ha-nidachat* — in which case they are executed by the sword and their property is destroyed? The *braita* rules: *hen bi-sekila u-mamonam palat* — if they merely passed through, they are treated as individuals: stoning and property preserved. *Ve-im shehu sham sheloshim yom, hen be-sayif u-mamonam avad* — but if they had been there for thirty days, they are treated as members of the city: execution by the sword and property destroyed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נִשְׁתַּהוּ שָׁם שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – הֵן בְּסַיִיף, וּמָמוֹנָן אָבֵד!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And if** the caravan members **had remained** in that city for **thirty days, they** are liable to death **by the sword and their property is destroyed,** just as it is for the rest of the residents of the city. This seems to indicate that once an individual has lived in a city for thirty days, he is already considered one of its residents.

So thirty days makes one a *yoshev ha-ir* — a dweller in the city — for at least some legal purposes.

### Graduated Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא לִבְנֵי מָתָא, הָא לְיָתוֹבֵי מָתָא. כִּדְתַנְיָא: הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר; כׇּל שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּהָא שָׁם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ – אָסוּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – מוּתָּר. מִיּוֹשְׁבֵי הָעִיר; כׇּל שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּהָא שָׁם שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – אָסוּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן – מוּתָּר לֵיהָנוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is **not difficult. This** period, i.e., twelve months, is required in order to be considered one of the **members of the city;** and **that** period, i.e., thirty days, suffices in order to be considered one of the **residents of the city. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who is prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from the people of a** particular **city** is **prohibited from deriving benefit from anyone who has stayed there** for **twelve months,** but it is permitted for him to derive benefit from anyone who has stayed there for **less** time **than that.** By contrast, if he prohibited himself by way of a vow from deriving benefit **from the residents of a** particular **city,** he is **prohibited from deriving benefit from anyone who has stayed there** for **thirty days,** but **it is permitted for him to derive benefit from** anyone who has stayed there for **less** time **than that.**

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction between thirty days and twelve months by distinguishing two categories: *livnei masta* — being a person of the city, *anshei ha-ir* — and *li-yishuvei masta* — merely dwelling in the city. The *ir ha-nidachat* text uses the language *yoshvei ha-ir ha-hi* — dwellers of that city — which is satisfied by thirty days. But the *mishna*'s twelve-month threshold is for becoming *anshei ha-ir*, a full civic member who bears the city's tax obligations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְכׇל מִילֵּי מִי בָּעִינַן שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ? וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – לְתַמְחוּי, שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים – לְקוּפָּהּ, שִׁשָּׁה – לִכְסוּת, תִּשְׁעָה – לִקְבוּרָה, שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר – לְפַסֵּי הָעִיר! אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי תְּנַן נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין ״שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ״ – לְפַסֵּי הָעִיר תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do we require** that one lives in a city for **twelve months for all matters? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If one lives in city for **thirty days,** he must contribute **to** the **charity platter** from which food is distributed to the poor. If he lives there for **three months,** he must contribute **to** the charity **box.** If he lives there for **six** months, he must contribute **to** the **clothing** fund. If he lives there for **nine** months, he must contribute **to** the **burial** fund. If he lives there for **twelve** months, he must contribute **to** the **columns of the city [*lepassei ha'ir*],** i.e., for the construction of a security fence. **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: When we learned twelve months in the mishna, we learned** that with regard to contributing **to** the **columns of the city,** money used for protecting and strengthening the city, but not for other matters.

A *braita* then introduces an even more graduated timeline, revealing that communal obligations accrue incrementally based on duration of residence. *Sheloshim yom le-tamchui* — thirty days establishes an obligation to contribute to the *tamchui*, the daily communal food fund for the poor. *Shlosha chodashim le-kuppa* — three months establishes an obligation to the *kuppa*, the weekly charitable distribution. *Shisha le-chesut* — six months, and one must contribute to the communal fund for clothing the poor. *Tisha li-kvura* — nine months, and one must contribute to the fund for providing burial for the poor. *Shneim asar le-passei ha-ir* — and only after twelve months does one bear an obligation to contribute to the city walls.

This is a remarkably detailed and humane structure. The community recognizes not only the *tamchui* and *kuppa* — which are well known — but also organized collections for clothing and burial. These are not acts of individual generosity alone; they are structured communal obligations. And the graduated timeline reflects a principle of progressive inclusion: as one becomes more deeply embedded in a community, more obligations attach, culminating in the most permanent and costly obligation of all — the physical infrastructure of the city wall.

Why is the timeline structured this way? One possibility is that the later obligations are more financially demanding, and it is equitable to wait until residency is more clearly permanent before imposing them. But there may also be a conceptual distinction: the earlier obligations (food, clothing, burial) are about the *ani* — they flow from membership in a community of mutual care. The wall obligation, by contrast, flows from being a *beneficiary* of a physical service. The twelve-month threshold for the wall may reflect a judgment that only after a year does one's stake in the city's physical security justify the levy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל לְפַסֵּי הָעִיר, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי; אֲבָל רַבָּנַן – לָא, דְּרַבָּנַן לָא צְרִיכִי נְטִירוּתָא. אֲמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְשׁוּרָא וּלְפָרָשָׁאה וּלְטֻרְזִינָא – אֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי; אֲבָל רַבָּנַן – לָא צְרִיכִי נְטִירוּתָא. כְּלָלָא דְמִילְּתָא: כׇּל מִילְּתָא דְּאִית לְהוּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ – אֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All** are required to contribute **to** the **columns of the city, and** money is collected for that purpose **even from orphans. But the Sages** are **not** required to contribute, **since the Sages do not need protection. Rav Pappa said:** Money is collected **even from orphans for the** city **wall, for the** city **horseman, and for the guard [*uletarzina*]** of the city armory, **but the Sages do not require protection. The principle of the matter** is: Money is collected **even from orphans** for **anything from which they** derive **benefit.**

Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan confirms: the twelve months in the *mishna* is specifically about *passei ha-ir* — the city walls. The other obligations follow their own timelines.

Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan then states: *hakol le-passei ha-ir, va-afilu mi-yatmei* — everyone must contribute to the city walls, even orphans. They live within the walls; they benefit from the protection; they must pay. *Aval rabbanan lo* — but the rabbis are exempt. The reasoning is, once again, *derabanan lo tsrikhei netiruta*.

Rav Pappa extends this to a list of related items: *le-shura, u-le-parsha'ah, u-le-tarzina, afilu mi-yatmei* — for repairing the wall, for the horsemen-guards who patrol the perimeter, and for the keeper of the armory — all of these require contributions from everyone, including orphans. And once again: *aval rabbanan lo tsrikhei netiruta*.

The governing principle is then stated comprehensively: *kol milta de-it lehu hana'ah mineih, afilu mi-yatmei ve-afilu me-rabbanan* — anything from which they derive benefit, all must contribute, without exception. The only exemption is protection-related taxes, from which rabbis are excluded precisely because they do not, in the *Gemara*'s framework, benefit from physical protection.

## Orphans & Charity

### Orphan Charity Tax

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה רְמָא צְדָקָה אַיַּתְמֵי דְּבֵי בַּר מָרִיּוֹן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָתָנֵי רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה: אֵין פּוֹסְקִין צְדָקָה עַל הַיְּתוֹמִים – אֲפִילּוּ לְפִדְיוֹן שְׁבוּיִם! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לְאַחְשׁוֹבִינְהוּ קָא עָבֵידְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is reported that **Rabba imposed** a contribution to a certain **charity on the orphans of the house of bar Maryon. Abaye said to him: But didn't Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda teach: One does not impose a charity** obligation **on orphans even for** the sake of **redeeming captives,** since they are minors and are not obligated in the mitzvot? Rabba **said to him: I did this to elevate them** in standing, i.e., so that people should honor them as generous benefactors; not in order that the poor should benefit.

Rabba placed a *tzedakah* assessment upon the orphans of the household of Bar Marion. Abaye objected, citing a *baraita*: *ein poskin tzedakah al ha-yetomim afilu li-pidyon shvuyim* — one may not impose a *tzedakah* levy upon orphans, even for *pidyon shvuyim* — the redemption of captives, which is among the weightiest communal obligations. The principle is clear: while orphans can be compelled to pay for things that benefit them, *tzedakah* — by definition directed at benefiting others — cannot be extracted from orphans.

### Status in Society

Rabba justifies his imposition on different grounds: *ana le-achshuvinehu ka aveidna* — I am doing this for the orphans' own benefit. Contributing to *tzedakah* raises their standing in the community. An orphan who gives is perceived as a full and dignified member of society; one who only receives, or who is entirely exempted from communal obligations, occupies a lesser social position.

This argument has a parallel in the *Tosefta*, which teaches that *tzedakah* is collected from gentiles together with Jews *mipnei darchei shalom* — for the sake of social harmony. Even the act of being collected from, of being expected to contribute, confers a form of social dignity and equality. The *Tosefta*'s point is that excluding someone from the obligation to give can itself be a form of marginalizing them.

One might ask: if this reasoning — that contributing to *tzedakah* dignifies the giver — is sound, why does the *baraita* single out *pidyon shvuyim* as something that cannot be imposed on orphans? What makes that case different? The *Gemara* does not resolve this tension directly.

Ifra Hormiz, the mother of the Persian king Shavor Malka, sent a pouch of dinars to Rav Yosef with a request: *le-havei le-mitzvah rabbah* — use this for a great *mitzvah*. Rav Yosef sat and deliberated: what is the greatest *mitzvah*? Abaye said to him: the answer follows from the *baraita* itself. Since *ein poskin tzedakah al ha-yetomim afilu li-pidyon shvuyim* — we do not impose *tzedakah* on orphans even for *pidyon shvuyim* — *shma mina de-pidyon shvuyim mitzvah rabbah hi* — we may infer that *pidyon shvuyim* is the great *mitzvah* par excellence, the one whose weight alone makes it the subject of an explicit exclusion. Rav Yosef accordingly allocated the funds to *pidyon shvuyim*. The *Gemara* will continue with a broader exploration of *pidyon shvuyim* and *tzedakah* — which will occupy the coming pages.
