# Megillah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rosh Chodesh Tevet

### Multiple Scrolls Setup

The daf opens with a case requiring multiple *sifrei Torah*: when *Rosh Chodesh Tevet* falls on *Shabbat*, one encounters the unusual convergence of *Rosh Chodesh* and *Chanukah* on the same *Shabbat*, creating the well-known situation of three *sifrei Torah*. The *Gemara* earlier addressed the *Shabbat* scenario, and now turns to the weekday case.

### Rav Yitzchak's View

When *Rosh Chodesh Tevet* falls on a weekday, *Rav Yitzchak Naphcha* rules that one reads three *aliyot* devoted to *Rosh Chodesh* and one *aliyah* devoted to *Chanukah*. Since weekday *Rosh Chodesh* calls for three *aliyot* and *Chanukah* for three as well, the question is how to divide the four total *aliyot* that result from their coincidence. *Rav Yitzchak Naphcha* gives the preponderance to *Rosh Chodesh*: three readings from the *Rosh Chodesh* portion and the fourth *aliyah* from the *Chanukah* reading — the *nesi'im*.

### Rav Dimi's View

*Rav Dimi miHaifa* takes the contrary position: three *aliyot* are devoted to *Chanukah* and one to *Rosh Chodesh* — specifically the *musaf shel Rosh Chodesh* passage.

### Tadir Principle

*Rav Mani* weighs in to support *Rav Yitzchak Naphcha*, invoking the principle of *tadir veshe'eino tadir tadir kodem*: since *Rosh Chodesh* is the more frequent occurrence, it takes precedence, and accordingly the majority of the reading is devoted to it.

*Rav Avin*, however, argues that *Rav Dimi* is in fact the more logical position. His reasoning inverts the *tadir* logic: it is precisely *Rosh Chodesh* that causes the fourth *aliyah* to exist at all, since on a normal weekday *Chanukah* has only three *aliyot*. If *Rosh Chodesh* is responsible for generating the extra *aliyah*, it follows that this fourth *aliyah* — *revi'i beRosh Chodesh ba'ei mikri* — should itself be the *Rosh Chodesh* reading.

### Rav Yosef vs Rava

*Rav Yosef* rules that one does not give significant weight to *Rosh Chodesh* — meaning *Chanukah* is the dominant reading. *Rava* rules the opposite: one does not give weight to *Chanukah*, and *Rosh Chodesh* remains primary. The final *halakha* follows *Rava*: *ein mashgichin beChanukah veRosh Chodesh ikkar* — three *aliyot* from the *Rosh Chodesh* reading and the fourth *aliyah* from *Chanukah*.

## Shekalim/Ki Tissa

### Shekalim Before Ki Tissa

The *Gemara* now addresses what happens when *Parshat Shekalim* falls on the *Shabbat* of *Va'ata Tetzaveh* — the *Shabbat* immediately preceding *Ki Tissa*, whose opening section constitutes the *Shekalim* reading. *Rav Yitzchak Naphcha* proposes a tidy solution: read six *aliyot* from *Va'ata Tetzaveh* through to *Ki Tissa*, and use the seventh *aliyah* to read the beginning of *Ki Tissa* — namely, the *Shekalim* passage — through *Ve'asita*. The cycle of *Va'ata Tetzaveh* is thereby completed and the special reading is incorporated as the seventh *aliyah*.

### Abaye's Solution

*Abaye* objects. He argues that this arrangement will confuse people: *ukmi hu deka mokmi hatam* — they will simply think the *baal keri'ah* decided to venture a bit further into next week's *parsha*, and will not recognize that the seventh *aliyah* constitutes a purposeful *Parshat Shekalim* reading. It is worth noting that this objection itself illuminates the degree of flux in *parasha* divisions during the Talmudic period, particularly given the *Yerushalmi*'s three-year cycle.

*Abaye* therefore proposes a different approach: the sixth *aliyah* reads through the end of *Ki Tissa* up to *Ve'asita* — that is, the reader does not stop at the end of *Va'ata Tetzaveh* but carries into *Ki Tissa* — and then the seventh *aliyah* doubles back and re-reads the same *Shekalim* passage from the beginning of *Ki Tissa* through *Ve'asita*. This doubling back is analogous in function to the role of the *ba'al maftir*, though the *Gemara* here operates on the assumption that the special reading is assigned to *shvi'i* rather than *maftir* — a point connected to the broader question of whether *maftir* is *oleh min ha-minyan*.

### Supporting Tanya

A *tanya* corroborates *Abaye*'s approach. The *baraita* states: if *Parshat Shekalim* falls on a *Shabbat* adjacent to *Ki Tissa* — whether immediately before or immediately after — *korin otah ve-koflah*, one reads it and repeats it. While this at first seems to address only the case of *Shekalim* falling before *Ki Tissa* (confirming *Abaye*'s read-and-repeat method), *Rav Yitzchak Naphcha* challenges *Abaye*: the *baraita* speaks of both before and after. If *Shekalim* falls *after* *Ki Tissa*, there is no meaningful repetition to be done within that *Shabbat*'s reading. The *Gemara* resolves this: *koflah be-shabbasos* — the doubling refers not to repeating within a single *Shabbat*'s laining but to the fact that when *Shekalim* and *Ki Tissa* are adjacent weeks, the opening section of *Ki Tissa* will be read on two consecutive *Shabbatot* — once as part of the regular *parsha* cycle and once as the *Shekalim* reading.

### Shekalim on Ki Tissa

The final case: *Parshat Shekalim* falls directly on the *Shabbat* of *Ki Tissa* itself. *Rav Yitzchak Naphcha*, consistent with his general reluctance to repeat passages, proposes a striking solution: skip over the *Shekalim* section entirely, start the reading from *Ve'asita*, read through to *Vayakhel* for the first six *aliyot*, and then assign the seventh *aliyah* to go back and read *Ki Tissa* through *Ve'asita*. The problem is severe: *Abaye* objects that *hashta amri le-mafre'a hu de-kari* — people will say the Torah is being read out of order, and the *Mishnah* has already established that one may not read the Torah out of order. A section was skipped and only returned to afterward; regardless of the rationale, it constitutes reading backwards.

*Abaye* therefore rules: start from the very beginning of *Ki Tissa*, read six *aliyot* through to *Vayakhel*, and then the seventh *aliyah* returns to the opening of *Ki Tissa* and re-reads the *Shekalim* portion. The *tanya* cited earlier confirms this: *chala lihiyot be-Ki Tissa atzmah — koreh otah ve-koflah* — when *Shekalim* falls on *Ki Tissa* itself, one reads the passage and then repeats it.

## Special Parshiyot Calend…

### Rosh Chodesh Mid-Week

The *Gemara* now turns to the broader calendrical framework: how to ensure that the four special *parshiyot* fall in their proper places relative to *Purim*, *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and *Pesach*. The *Shekalim* reading must precede *Rosh Chodesh Adar* in order to give the community sufficient time to bring their *shekalim* before the money-changers set out their tables.

The *Mishnah* states: *chal lihiyot betoch ha-Shabbat, makdimim le-Shabbat she-avrah* — if *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls during the week, one reads *Shekalim* on the preceding *Shabbat*.

### Rav vs Shmuel

The case under dispute is *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falling on *Erev Shabbat* — Friday. Do we apply the same rule and read *Shekalim* on the *Shabbat* six days earlier, or does the proximity to *Shabbat* allow us to push the reading off to the following *Shabbat*?

*Rav* rules *makdimin*: even when *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on Friday, one reads *Shekalim* the preceding *Shabbat*, because otherwise one will not have the full fourteen days for people to bring their *shekalim* before the money-changers open for business. *Shmuel* rules *me'acharin*: one may push the reading to the following *Shabbat*. His reasoning: if *Rosh Chodesh* is on the 1st of *Adar* and that day is a Friday, then the 15th is also a Friday. The money-changers would not open their tables on *Erev Shabbat*; *shulchanot lo nafki ad chad be-Shabta* — they would wait until Sunday. Therefore in such a month, even postponing the reading by one day still leaves adequate time before the tables open, and *hilkach me'achirin* — one may therefore delay.

### Baraita Interpretation

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve the dispute from a *baraita*. The *Mishnah* itself says *chal lihiyot betoch ha-Shabbat, makdimin* — and the *Gemara* first asks whether this implies even the case of *Erev Shabbat*. The response: *lo, betoch ha-Shabbat davka* — the *Mishnah* means strictly in the middle of the week, not on the edge.

The *Gemara* then cites the *baraita* directly: *eizo hi Shabbat rishona — kol she-chal Rosh Chodesh Adar lihyot be-tokhah, va-afilu be-erev Shabbat* — the first special *Shabbat* is any *Shabbat* where *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls during its week, even if it falls on *Erev Shabbat*. This appears to be a proof for *Rav*: just as *betoch ha-Shabbat* means one reads *Shekalim* the preceding *Shabbat*, so too *afilu Erev Shabbat* — even on a Friday — one reads it the preceding *Shabbat*.

*Shmuel* counters by reading the *baraita* differently. He argues the text should be read as *bah* rather than *be-tokhah*: the first *Shabbat* is not any *week* in which *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls, but the *Shabbat* on which *Rosh Chodesh Adar* itself falls — or, if *Rosh Chodesh* fell on *Erev Shabbat*, the *Shabbat* immediately following it. The school of *Shmuel* (*tana de-vei Shmuel*) likewise formulates it as *bah*. The simple reading of the *baraita*, however, favors *Rav*.

### R. Shimon on Erev Shabbat

A *tannaitic* dispute is then cited: *mesargin le-Shabbatot divrei Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi* — one may insert breaks (skip *Shabbatot*) between the special readings, according to *Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi*. *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* disagrees and says *ein mesargin* — one does not skip. He qualifies: when does he say one does not skip? *Bizman she-chal lihyot be-Erev Shabbat* — when *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on *Erev Shabbat*. In that scenario, following *Shmuel*'s position and reading *Shekalim* the following *Shabbat* (the 2nd of *Adar*), the subsequent *Shabbatot* fall on the 9th, 16th, 23rd, and 30th — a perfect alignment requiring no skips. *Zachor* falls before *Purim*, *Parah* follows *Purim*, *Hachodesh* precedes *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and everything works without gaps.

*Eimatai ani omer ein mesargin* — *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* is saying: this is the *one* scenario in which the calendar works out naturally without any skipping, and that is precisely when *Rosh Chodesh* falls on *Erev Shabbat*. In all other cases — when it falls in the middle of the week — one must read *Shekalim* the preceding *Shabbat* (which may be in *Shevat*), and a skip will inevitably be required to bring *Zachor* to just before *Purim*. The debate between *Rav* and *Shmuel* thus maps onto the dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi* and *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*.

The *halakha* is ruled in all cases like *Rav*.

## Zachor & Purim

### Rav Advances

The same structural question arises regarding *Parshat Zachor*: what happens when *Purim* falls on *Erev Shabbat* — Friday? *Rav* rules *makdimin* — one reads *Parshat Zachor* the preceding *Shabbat*, well in advance.

### Shmuel Postpones

*Shmuel* rules *me'acharin* — one may postpone *Zachor* to the *Shabbat* following *Purim*, i.e., the day after. Again, *Shmuel* is entirely consistent with his position throughout this discussion.

### Order of Remembrance

*Rav*'s reasoning is clear: *ki heichi de-lo tikdam asiya lizchira* — one cannot allow the performance of the obligation (*Purim*, representing *mechiyat Amalek*) to precede the remembrance. The reading of *Zachor*, which establishes the obligation of remembering and combating *Amalek*, must chronologically precede the *Megillah* reading that enacts that memory. To read *Zachor* after *Purim* inverts the proper sequence.

### Mukafin Argument

*Shmuel* counters: *keivan de-ika mukafin de-avdi ba-chamishah asar* — since there are walled cities (*mukafin*) that observe *Purim* on the 15th, and the *Shabbat* following a Friday *Purim* would coincide with their observance, *asiya ve-zchira behadi hadadi ka'asya* — at least for them, the action and remembrance occur simultaneously. This grounds *Zachor* on the same day as *Purim*, giving it some residual relationship to the obligation even for those who observed on the 14th. The argument is admittedly unusual, as the *mukafin* are a minority, but it is *Shmuel*'s consistent reasoning.

### Baraita Proof

The *Gemara* attempts to prove the matter from the *Mishnah*, which states *uva-shniyah Zachor* — the second special *Shabbat* is *Zachor*. The first scenario of the *Mishnah* is *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falling on *Shabbat*, which means *Purim* falls on the 14th, which is *Erev Shabbat* — precisely the case under dispute. The *Mishnah* says the second reading is *Zachor*, implying it is read the *Shabbat* before *Purim* even in that scenario — a proof for *Rav*.

*Rav Pappa* deflects: *mai shniyah — shniyah le-hafsaka* — when the *Mishnah* says "the second *Shabbat*," it does not refer specifically to the scenario where *Rosh Chodesh* is on *Shabbat*. Rather, it means the second in sequence after the skip: in those cases where a *Shabbat* must be skipped between *Shekalim* and *Zachor*, the second special *Shabbat* — after the gap — is *Zachor*. The *Mishnah* is not speaking to the particular case of *Rosh Chodesh* on *Shabbat* implying *Purim* on *Erev Shabbat*.

The *Gemara* then brings the full *baraita* parallel to the one cited for *Shekalim*: *eizo hi Shabbat shniyah — kol she-chal Purim lihyot be-tokhah, va-afilu be-Erev Shabbat* — the second special *Shabbat* is any week in which *Purim* falls, even if *Purim* falls on *Erev Shabbat*. This again reads naturally as a proof for *Rav*: just as the *betoch* language for *Shekalim* meant any week containing *Rosh Chodesh*, so the parallel formulation for *Zachor* means any week containing *Purim*, even a Friday — and one reads *Zachor* the preceding *Shabbat*. *Shmuel* again reads *bah* rather than *be-tokhah*, and the school of *Shmuel* likewise transmits *bah*, reversing the *baraita*'s apparent meaning. The simple reading of the *baraita* supports *Rav*.

### Purim on Shabbat

The *Gemara* then addresses the case where *Purim* itself falls on *Shabbat*. For *Shmuel*, there is no novelty: his reading of the *baraita* already indicated that when *Purim* falls *on* *Shabbat*, that *Shabbat* is *Zachor*. But what would *Rav* say? If his concern is that remembrance must *precede* performance, would he insist on reading *Zachor* a full week in advance even when *Purim* is on *Shabbat* — meaning *Zachor* would be read simultaneously with *Purim*?

*Rav Huna* rules that in this case *ledivrei hakol ein makdimin* — all agree one does not advance. Reading *Zachor* on *Shabbat Purim* itself is not reading it *after* the obligation; it is reading it *on the same day*, which satisfies *Rav*'s concern.

*Rav Nachman* disagrees: *adayin hi machlokes* — the dispute persists even in this case. *Rav* requires *Zachor* to be read strictly *before Purim*, not merely simultaneously, and would insist on reading it the previous *Shabbat*.

A statement in *Rav*'s name is then cited: *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* says in the name of *Rabbi Abba* says in the name of *Rav* — when *Purim* falls on *Shabbat*, *makdim ve-korin be-Shabbat she-avrah Zachor* — one reads *Zachor* the preceding *Shabbat*. This confirms *Rav Nachman*'s reading: *Rav* demands genuine priority, not mere simultaneity.

It should be noted that in our fixed calendar, *Purim* cannot fall on *Shabbat* in regular cities; this scenario applies only to *Yerushalayim* and walled cities observing *Purim* on the 15th.

## Parah Adumah

### Third Shabbat Definition

The *Gemara* now turns to the third special *Shabbat*, *Parshat Parah*. The *baraita* defines it as *kol she-smucha le-Purim me-achareiha* — any *Shabbat* immediately following *Purim*.

### Before Rosh Chodesh

*Rav Chama bar Chanina* offers a different formulation: *Shabbat ha-smucha le-Rosh Chodesh Nissan* — the *Shabbat* immediately preceding *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. This seems to conflict with the *baraita*'s definition, since the *Shabbat* immediately before *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* is more naturally associated with *Parshat Hachodesh*.

### Reconciliation

The *Gemara* resolves: *ve-lo pligi* — they do not actually dispute. The two definitions apply to different calendar scenarios.

When *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* falls on *Shabbat*, *Parshat Hachodesh* is read on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* itself. In that case the *Shabbat* immediately preceding it (the 23rd) is *Parah*, and the 16th would be skipped. Here *Parah* is defined not as the *Shabbat* right after *Purim* (the 15th being *Shushan Purim*) but as the *Shabbat* adjacent to *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* — consistent with *Rav Chama bar Chanina*.

In all other scenarios, the *Shabbat* before *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* serves as *Hachodesh* and *Parah* falls the week before that, which is the *Shabbat* immediately following *Purim* (and *Shushan Purim* — for *Parah* will not be read on the 15th either). Both participants correctly observe that *Parah* will never fall during *Purim* itself or *Shushan Purim*; it is always the immediate *Shabbat* after both days have passed, with the sole exception noted above.

## Four Parshiyot

### Complete Baraita

The *Gemara* now quotes the complete *baraita* encompassing all four special *Shabbatot*. When *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on *Shabbat*, one reads *Ki Tissa* (the *Shekalim* section) with a *haftarah* from the episode of *Yehoyada ha-Kohen* — also about the *shekalim*. The *baraita* then defines each *Shabbat* in turn:

The first special *Shabbat* (*Shekalim*): *kol she-chal Rosh Chodesh Adar lihyot be-tokhah, va-afilu be-erev Shabbat* — any week in which *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls, even on *Erev Shabbat*; one reads *Ki Tissa* and reads the *haftarah* of *Yehoyada*.

The second special *Shabbat* (*Zachor*): *kol she-chal Purim lihyot be-tokhah, va-afilu be-erev Shabbat* — any week in which *Purim* falls, even on *Erev Shabbat*; the *haftarah* is *Pakadeti* — the passage from *Shmuel* recounting the war against *Amalek*.

The third special *Shabbat* (*Parah Adumah*): *kol she-smucha le-Purim me-achareiha* — the *Shabbat* immediately following *Purim* (and *Shushan Purim*, as qualified); the *haftarah* is *Ve-zarakti aleikhem* from *Yechezkel*.

The fourth special *Shabbat* (*Hachodesh*): *kol she-chal Rosh Chodesh Nissan lihyot be-tokhah, va-afilu be-erev Shabbat* — any week in which *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* falls, even on *Erev Shabbat*, one reads *Hachodesh hazeh* the preceding *Shabbat*; the *haftarah* is *Ko amar Hashem Elokim ba-rishon be-echad la-chodesh* from *Yechezkel*. When *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* falls on *Shabbat* itself, one reads *Hachodesh* directly on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*.

### Rashi's Mnemonic

The *halakha* is ruled like *Rav* in all cases. *Rashi* provides a mnemonic for determining which *Shabbatot* to skip based on when *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls: *Zavadu, Baro, Gad, Ve-haya*. In our fixed calendar, *Rosh Chodesh Adar* can only fall on *Shabbat*, Monday, Wednesday, or *Erev Shabbat* (Friday), and the mnemonic encodes the skipping pattern for each scenario.

### Three Scenarios

When *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on *Shabbat* (the 1st): the *Shabbatot* are the 1st, 8th, 15th, 22nd, and 29th. *Shekalim* is read on the 1st, *Zachor* on the 8th, the 15th is skipped (since one does not read *Parah* on *Purim* or *Shushan Purim*), *Parah* on the 22nd, and *Hachodesh* on the 29th — a clean, elegant alignment with no double-skip.

When *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on Monday: *Shabbat* is the 6th (since the 1st is Monday, *Shabbat* is the 6th), then 13th, 20th, 27th. Since *Rosh Chodesh* did not fall on *Shabbat*, *Shekalim* is read the preceding *Shabbat* (back in *Shevat*), the 6th is skipped, *Zachor* on the 13th, *Parah* on the 20th, and *Hachodesh* on the 27th.

When *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on Wednesday: *Shabbat* is the 4th, then 11th, 18th, 25th. *Shekalim* is read the *Shabbat* before the 4th (skipping the 4th), *Zachor* on the 11th, *Parah* on the 18th, and *Hachodesh* on the 25th — all before *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*.

When *Rosh Chodesh Adar* falls on *Erev Shabbat* (Friday): *Shabbat* is the 2nd, then 9th, 16th, 23rd, and *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* on the 30th. Here two skips are required. *Shekalim* is read before the 2nd, the 2nd is skipped, *Zachor* on the 9th before *Purim*, the 16th is skipped (to bring *Parah* adjacent to *Hachodesh*), *Parah* on the 23rd, and *Hachodesh* on the 29th. This is the scenario in which, when *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* falls on *Shabbat*, *Hachodesh* is read directly on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* itself, and *Parah* is aligned to the *Shabbat* immediately before *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* rather than immediately after *Purim*.

The reason *Rosh Chodesh* cannot fall on Thursday is simply the structure of the fixed Jewish calendar — those are the only permissible days.

## Four Parshiyot…

### Divine Name Note

A brief but practically important observation arises: *Rashi*'s calendar presentation uses numbers including 15 and 16. The convention of writing 15 as *yud-heh* (10+5) is avoided because *yud-heh* constitutes a divine name. Many *Rishonim*, however, insist that only the 15th requires this avoidance, since *yud-heh* is indeed a divine name; the 16th written as *yud-vav* does not carry the same concern, as *yud-vav* is not a divine name. Therefore in many *Rishonim* the 16th is written as *yud-vav*, not as 16 (*tet-zayin*).

## Regular Parshiyot

### Return to Order

On the fifth *Shabbat* following the sequence of special *parshiyot*, the *Gemara* states *bachamishi chozrin le-ke-sidran* — one returns to the normal order of readings. The question is: what is the "normal order" that has been interrupted?

### Rav Ami vs Yirmiyah

*Rav Ami* says *le-seder parshiyot* — one returns to the normal sequential reading of the weekly *parsha*. His position implies that during the special *Shabbatot*, the regular *parsha* was not read at all, only the special reading. (How one would divide such a reading into seven *aliyot* is admittedly unclear.) *Rabbi Yirmeya* says *le-seder haftarot hu chozer* — one returns to the normal *haftarot*. His position aligns with the *Bavli*'s general assumption throughout this discussion: that the regular *parsha* continues to be read and only the *haftarah* is changed, or the special *Shekalim* (and *Zachor* etc.) reading is added as *shvi'i* or *maftir*.

*Abaye* states that the position of *Rav Ami* is the more logically coherent one.

### Weekday vs Shabbat

The *Gemara* cites the *Mishnah* in support: *le-chol mafsikin* — for all special occasions one interrupts the normal reading — *le-Rashei Chodashim, le-Chanukah, le-Purim, le-Ta'aniyot, u-le-Ma'amadot, le-Yom HaKippurim*. This is the proof for *Rav Ami*: *bishlama* if you have been interrupting the reading of the *Torah* itself (the *parsha*), then the formulation "one interrupts" makes perfect sense — and crucially, two items on this list, *ta'aniyot* and *ma'amadot*, cannot happen on *Shabbat*. These are weekday events. On a weekday there is no *haftarah* to interrupt; what one is interrupting is the Torah reading itself.

The response for *Rabbi Yirmeya* — *ha ke-d-itah ve-ha ke-d-itah* — is that each case operates in its own context. When the special occasion falls on a weekday (*ta'aniyot*, *ma'amadot*, *Chanukah* on a Monday), one interrupts the actual Torah reading. When it falls on *Shabbat*, one preserves the *parsha* and changes only the *haftarah*. The simpler reading of the *Mishnah*, however, clearly supports *Rav Ami* — that the interruption refers to the Torah reading itself.

The *Gemara* presses *Rabbi Yirmeya*'s position further: if one is trying to preserve the normal reading as much as possible (as *Rabbi Yirmeya* holds for *Shabbat*), why not do the same for *ta'aniyot* — read the normal weekday portion in *Shacharit* and reserve the special *ta'anit* reading for *Mincha*? The *Gemara* responds: this proves *Rav Huna*'s statement. *Rav Huna* says *mi-safra kinufya* — in the morning of a *ta'anit*, the community is occupied gathering and assessing communal conduct (*milei de-mata*), taking stock of what needs to be corrected, and beginning the process of communal *teshuvah*. *Abaye* elaborates: from morning until midday one examines the affairs of the community; from midday until evening one devotes a quarter of the day to Torah reading and *haftarah*, and the final quarter to *tefillah*, confessions, and supplication. The source is the *pasuk* in *Ezra-Nechemiah*: *va-yikre'u be-sefer torat Hashem Elokeihem revi'it ha-yom* — they read in the Torah a quarter of the day — *u-revi'it mitvadim u-mishtachavim* — and another quarter confessing and bowing. The morning is not about ritual but about moral accounting; therefore the Torah reading cannot be split between morning and afternoon and must take place entirely in the afternoon. The *inyana de-yoma* in *Shacharit* and the *ta'anit* portion in *Mincha* is not a viable option.

## Festival Readings

### Festival Parshiyot

The final *Mishnah* of *Masechet Megillah* catalogues the Torah readings for the festivals and special occasions throughout the year. On *Pesach* one reads from *Parshat Moadot* in *Parshat Emor* in *Vayikra* — *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled*. On *Shavuot* (*Atzeret*) one reads *shiva shavuot*. On *Rosh Hashanah* — *ba-chodesh ha-shvi'i*, the section in *Emor* dealing with the first of the seventh month. On *Yom Kippur* — *Acharei Mot*, the *avodah* section. On the first day of *Sukkot* one reads from *Parshat Moadot she-be-Torat Kohanim* — the large section in *Emor* — *shor o chesev o ez*, covering all the festival *korbanot*. On *Chol Ha-Moed* one reads from *korbanot ha-chag* — the specific *Sukkot* offerings — from *Pinchas*, not from *Emor*.

The *Mishnah* does not specify *Chol Ha-Moed Pesach*, a point the *Gemara* will address in the *baraita*.

### Other Special Days

On *Chanukah* one reads from the *nesi'im* section in *Nasso* in *Bamidbar*. On *Purim* one reads *Vayavo Amalek*. On *Rosh Chodesh* — *u-ve-rashei chodsheikhem*. For the *ma'amadot* — the representatives of the people who stood in correspondence with the *kohanim* serving in the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* — one reads from *ma'aseh bereishit*, as we learned in *Masechet Ta'anit*. On *ta'aniyot* one reads *brachot u-klalot* — the section of blessings and curses, which *Rashi* identifies as *Bechukotai*. This is notable, since the full *Masechet Ta'anit* was studied without encountering this ruling, nor is it common practice today. The logic is clear, however: on fast days occasioned by drought or communal suffering, reading the *klalot* is meant to heighten awareness of consequence and intensify the call to *teshuvah*.

### Klalot Rule

One does not divide the *klalot* between multiple *aliyot*: *ein mafsikin ba-klalot — ela echad korei et kullan*. A single reader reads the entire section of curses; it is not split among several *olim*.

### Weekday Readings

Monday, Thursday, and *Shabbat Mincha* readings follow the normal sequential *parsha* but do not count toward the division for the following *Shabbat*. One does not reason that since, for example, *kohen* and *levi* were already called to *aliyot* earlier in the week, one may begin on *Shabbat* with *shlishi*.

### Source Pasuk

The *Mishnah* closes with the *pasuk* that grounds this entire framework: *Vayidaber Moshe et mo'adei Hashem el b'nei Yisrael* — and *Moshe* spoke of the appointed times of God to the children of Israel. The institution of reading about each *mo'ed* during its appointed time is derived from this verse: *mitzvatan she-yihyu korin kol echad va-echad bi-zmano* — it is their obligation to read about each one in its proper time, linking *kri'at ha-Torah* to the rhythm of the sacred calendar.
