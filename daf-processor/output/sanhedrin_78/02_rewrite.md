# Sanhedrin 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Definition of Murder

### Grama vs. Direct Act

Today's daf is 78. We pick up from eight lines above the bottom of 77b, at the *itmar* passage already introduced in the previous session. A brief review of the preceding material is in order, as it established several fundamental principles that the current daf builds upon.

The foundational question running through this entire *sugya* is: what constitutes an act of murder? Causation alone—what the Talmud calls *grama*, indirect causation—is insufficient for criminal liability. As with other areas of *halacha*, where *grama* damages are exempt from payment, so too in the laws of murder: you must perform a direct act, a *ma'aseh*. The question is what qualifies as such.

### Mitzamtzem Principle

The *gemara* establishes that you need not hold the victim with your bare hands until death arrives. The principle of *mitzamtzem*—holding someone in a position where, at the very moment of your act, the lethal agent is actively working upon them—suffices. If you bind someone and place them in the sun, that binding constitutes an act of murder even if you walk away and the victim dies hours later, because at the moment of the act, the sun's heat is already exerting its lethal force upon the victim's body. Similarly, holding someone underwater or pressing someone into a fire constitutes a direct act even if you release them and death follows.

An interesting question arises at the margins: if one were to lock a person inside a burning house—never physically touching the victim, merely controlling the surrounding environment—would that count as a direct act? The answer is not entirely resolved by the *gemara*, and the proper scope of this principle remains a matter requiring careful attention.

### Non-Direct Acts

The contrasting category involves situations in which, at the moment of your act, the lethal force is not yet bearing upon the victim. The clearest example is binding someone and leaving them in front of a lion: the lion may well attack the moment you depart, but at the moment of your act, the lion is not killing them. The *Rambam* describes such a person as a *rotzaich*—a murderer in the moral sense—but halachically it is not an actionable act of murder subject to capital punishment.

Similarly, throwing someone into a body of water and then removing the ladder they could use to escape: at the moment of throwing, the person could theoretically have survived. The *gemara* groups this with related cases—pulling away a shielding cover, or various actions taken around the victim rather than upon them—as instances that fall short of a prosecutable act of murder.

### Energy Transfer

The *gemara* then moves to a closely related question: must it be your own body that performs the act? The answer is no. One's *energy*—propelling a rock, shooting an arrow—is treated as an extension of the actor. Even when the object rebounds off a wall and strikes the victim from a new direction, the energy driving it is still entirely the thrower's; the wall merely redirected it. Provided the requisite intent and *hasra'ah* are present, such an act renders the perpetrator *chayav*.

## Koach Rishon/Sheni

### Sluice Gate Case

With this background established, the *gemara* introduces the *itmar* passage of Rav Pappa: if someone binds a victim in front of a dam and opens the sluice gates, releasing the stored water which then rushes down and kills the victim, this is considered equivalent to shooting an arrow—*girei deideih hu*, his arrows. The conceptual novelty here is significant. When you shoot an arrow, you transfer your kinetic energy into the projectile. Here, you have not physically propelled the water at all; you have merely released its latent potential energy. Yet that act of releasing potential energy is treated as though it is your energy driving the water.

### Degrees of Power

This rule, however, is qualified: *hani milei bekoach rishon*—this applies only when the victim is struck by the primary, first-stage force of the released water. But if the victim is positioned further away, so that the water must undergo a secondary motion—for example, the initial rush downward is *koach rishon*, but then the water continues flowing horizontally and strikes the victim—that subsequent motion is *grama be'alma*, mere indirect causation, and no longer constitutes a direct act of murder.

Rashi's description of *koach rishon* and *koach sheni* is not a perfectly precise physical taxonomy but rather a working framework: the first immediate consequence of releasing the stored energy is attributable to the actor; downstream consequences of the water simply continuing to flow are not.

### Immediacy Question

The line between *grama* and *ma'aseh* is genuinely blurred here, and that ambiguity is important well beyond the laws of murder. Consider a Rube Goldberg-style contraption: a trapdoor is opened, a ball falls, strikes the floor, rebounds at an angle, and kills the victim. If the first fall constitutes *koach rishon* and the rebound is *koach sheni*, then perhaps the actor is exempt. Yet if the actor had physically thrown the ball and it rebounded, we would say it is entirely his energy—merely redirected. What distinguishes releasing potential energy from physically throwing? Some element of immediacy appears to be doing important conceptual work. When you open the trapdoor and the ball immediately strikes the victim below, that might qualify; when the ball undergoes further intermediate stages, that breaks the chain.

### Shabbat Applications

This tension has major practical implications for the laws of *Shabbat*. When one flips a light switch and an incandescent bulb illuminates instantly, the experiential reality is that one turned on the light. At a physical level, one closed a circuit, which permitted electrical current to flow through a wire, which heated a filament. Strictly analyzed, is this *grama* or *ma'aseh*? The practical tendency is to treat immediate results as direct acts regardless of the intermediate physical steps—if it happens right now at the experiential level, we consider it your act. But the underlying conceptual question, informed by this *sugya*, is the subject of serious discussion in the *poskim* dealing with electricity, button-activated machinery (such as the question of whether pressing a button to operate a machine constitutes making *matzos*), and a range of modern technological applications. Even if the *halacha lepesika* tends toward treating immediate results as direct, the theoretical framework remains contested.

## Rock Angled Downward

### Rock Trajectory

The *gemara* continues with a further case from Rav Pappa: *zarak tsror lemala ve'azla litsadadim*—someone throws a rock upward and, as it descends, it comes down at an angle and strikes and kills a victim. We are dealing with an intentional act; without intent the entire question of *chiyuv retzicha* would not arise. What is the force that brings the rock to the victim?

### Energy Challenge

Mar bar Rav Ashi challenges Rav Pappa's position that the actor is *chayav*. His objection is elegant: *mishum dekocho hu*—you say this is the actor's force? If the actor's energy were still operating, the rock should have continued moving in the direction it was thrown—upward and outward—rather than falling. The very fact that it descends demonstrates that the actor's energy has been exhausted and what is pulling it down is gravity. *Ve'i lav kocho hu*—and if it is not the actor's force driving it, then absent any horizontal component, it should simply fall straight down. So which is it: whose force is bringing this rock to the victim?

### Weakened Energy

The *gemara* answers: *ela koach kocho hu*—it is the actor's *weakened* secondary force. The actor's original energy is what imparted the horizontal component to the projectile. That horizontal force did not simply vanish; it persists in a weakened form, redirected now by the combined action of the actor's horizontal energy and gravity's downward pull. The actor's force is thus still *involved* in bringing the rock to the victim, even if it is no longer the dominant force.

### Fire Analogy

The *rishonim* reach for an analogy from the laws of fire. The *gemara* elsewhere describes fire as *koach acher me'urav bo*—another force is intertwined in it. When one kindles a fire and the wind carries it to a person, one made the lethal energy and the wind delivered it. There is in fact a dispute among the authorities as to whether, if one intended a fire to spread to a particular location and it did so, that constitutes *retzicha*. But perhaps a partnership between one's force and another force—wind, gravity—does not sever the chain of causation when one's own force remains a contributing factor in the delivery of the lethal blow.

### Gravity Partnership

The case of the angled rock raises a philosophically striking point. It is not, strictly speaking, the actor's energy that kills—it is gravity that creates the downward force causing the fatal impact. The actor's contribution is only the horizontal vector, bringing the rock to the victim's location. Yet because the actor's force is still partially operative in the terminal phase, the actor is *chayav*. By contrast, if the rock were thrown straight up and fell straight down, the actor's force would be entirely dissipated on the way up, and the descending rock would have *none* of the actor's energy remaining in it. In that scenario, although the actor created the situation—exactly as opening a sluice gate and creating potential energy—the actor would be *patur*, because what brought the stone down was entirely gravity with no residual contribution from the actor's horizontal energy.

This creates a counterintuitive result: the actor who throws a rock at an angle, with only a partial contribution of his energy to the fatal trajectory, is *chayav*; whereas the actor who throws straight up and whose rock then falls directly down—a case where the actor equally created the entire potential energy situation—is *patur*. The distinguishing factor appears to be whether any component of the actor's original force remains operative at the moment the stone strikes the victim.

## Multiple Assailants

### Ten Sticks Case

The *gemara* now turns to a *Tanna* teaching: ten people struck a victim with ten sticks—*bein bevat achat bein bezeh achar zeh*—whether simultaneously or sequentially—and the victim died. This is the *sugya*'s version of *Murder on the Orient Express*.

### Simultaneous vs. Sequent…

The first *tanna* rules that all are *peturin* in either case. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira disagrees: when the blows were delivered sequentially, *ha'acharon chayav*—the last one is liable—because he finished the victim off. The simultaneous case is more intuitively clear: if each individual blow was not sufficient to kill by itself and the aggregate force killed collectively, no individual can be identified as the murderer, in precisely the same way that the principle of *shinaim she'asuhu* operates on *Shabbat*. The parallel with the individual capacity question is instructive in either direction.

### Shinaim She'asuhu

On *Shabbat*, if two people carry out an object together—say, a sofa—the question of liability depends on whether each person was individually capable of carrying it out alone. The principle of *shinaim she'asuhu* holds that when neither person was individually capable and they acted jointly, each individual did not technically perform the prohibited act. The ruling is that even in the case of *zeh yachol vezeh eino yachol*—one who could and one who could not—the *halacha* appears to be *patur* in both configurations. Whether two individuals were each independently capable or neither individually capable, the Talmudic framework finds grounds for exemption in either direction: if both could do it individually, one can argue each committed an act of murder, or one can argue that neither was *necessary* since the other would have done it anyway. If neither could do it alone, one can argue the collective act was committed, or one can argue no individual committed the act.

### Blame Deflection

The deeper point that Rashi articulates is that the discussion here is not primarily about whether the victim is still legally a "full life" by the time the later blows are struck. The victim, having been beaten by the first nine, is not a *treifa* in the technical halachic sense—his vital organs have not been severed. He is, in some sense, a *gosses bidei adam*, dying as a result of human violence. The issue is not the victim's bodily status; the victim's life is still legally full. Rather, the question is whether any single individual can be *blamed* for the death when each can point to all the others. In a sequential attack, the last assailant says: the nine before me equally would have killed him. Each can deflect responsibility to the others, and thus no individual is held liable.

Rabbi Yochanan notes that both the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira derive their positions from the same verse: *ve'ish ki yakkeh kol nefesh adam*—if a man smites any soul. The Rabbanan read *kol nefesh* as requiring a *complete* soul—meaning the victim must not already be in a state of dying from prior acts of murder directed at him. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira reads *kol nefesh* as *kol dehu nefesh*—any remnant of a soul suffices, and since the victim still had some life remaining when the last blow was struck, the last attacker is liable.

## Treifa and Gosses

### What is Treifa

Having introduced the phrase *kol nefesh*, the *gemara* now sharpens the distinction between two categories. A *treifa* is a person (or animal) with an internal injury of the sort that *halacha* recognizes as compromising the subject's status as a "full life." Not every terminal condition qualifies. A person dying of cancer or of natural causes has full legal status as a living person. *Treifa* refers to a specific set of internal injuries to vital organs or systems that *halacha* treats as categorically different—such that the victim, though alive, is no longer a full *nefesh* for purposes of the laws of murder.

### Gosses Status

A *gosses*, by contrast, is one who is on the very threshold of death—perhaps with only minutes to live. The *gosses*, however, retains full legal status. Killing a *gosses* is fully prosecutable as murder, regardless of how imminent the natural death would have been. This principle has obvious resonance in contemporary discussions of assisted death, where the argument that "the person was going to die imminently anyway" carries no halachic weight whatsoever.

Rava states: *hakol modim behoreig et hatreifa shehu patur*—everyone agrees that killing a *treifa* exempts the perpetrator from capital punishment. And conversely, killing a *gosses*—who remains a full life—renders one fully *chayav*. The contested case lies between these two.

### Classification Debate

The debate concerns the victim of a beating who has been struck by nine prior assailants and is now dying from the cumulative effect of those blows, but has not sustained any injury that would technically render him a *treifa*—his vital organs remain intact. Is such a person classified as a *treifa bidei adam* or as a *gosses bidei shamayim*?

One view compares this victim to a *treifa*: an act was done to him—*itavid bei ma'aseh*—a violent, murderous act at that, and therefore subsequent assailants can say the prior beating already categorically changed his status. The other view distinguishes: a *treifa* is defined by the severing of vital organs—*mechateich simanim*—which this victim lacks. Without that physiological criterion, he remains legally alive, and whoever deals the final blow should be *chayav*.

### Rashi on Murder Acts

Rashi's reading clarifies that the argument from the first position—that the victim is analogous to a *treifa* because *itavid bei ma'aseh*—is not really a claim about the victim's bodily status at all. It is a claim about the distribution of culpability. The prior assailants performed acts of murder against this victim. The later assailant can therefore say: don't blame it on me; the others also would have killed him. It is not that his life has been halachically diminished; it is that the act of killing cannot be uniquely attributed to the last person when so many others bear equal or greater causal responsibility.

### Vital Organs

The *gemara* then clarifies the counter-position: *treifa mechateich simanim*—a true *treifa* is defined by the severing of vital anatomical structures—whereas in this case, *lo mechatchei simanin*, those structures remain intact. Therefore, considered purely as a matter of "is this a full life?", the answer is yes, and the tenth assailant should in principle be *chayav*. The difficulty is solely the question of who gets blamed when everyone can point to everyone else.

The *gemara* presents a *Tanna* teaching before Rav Sheshet: if the first assailant's blow was non-fatal—*ein bo k'dei l'hamit*—and a second person's blow killed the victim, obviously the second is *chayav*. But the interesting case is where the first blow *was* fatal—*yeish bo k'dei l'hamit*—and a second person struck and killed the victim. That second person is *chayav*, and this is consistent with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's position that the last one in a sequential attack is liable.

## Treifa Murderer

### Treifa Murder Rules

Having established the rules for killing a *treifa*, Rava now addresses the inverse: a *treifa* who commits murder. If a *treifa* kills someone *bifnei beit din*—in the presence of the court—he is *chayav* and is executed. If the killing occurred *shelo bifnei beit din*—outside the court's direct observation—he is *patur*.

### Community Removal

The basis for liability when the court witnesses the act is the verse *uviarta hara mikirbecha*—you shall remove the evil from your midst. Tosafot notes that this reflects the principle of *lo tehei shemia gedola me're'iyah*: what the court itself observes does not require formal testimonial procedure in the usual sense, though various technical conditions (such as whether it was seen by day or night) introduce additional complications.

### Unvalidatable Testimony

The reason for exemption when the murder occurred outside the court's presence is: *d'haviyah lei eidut she'atah yachol lehazimah*—the testimony against him cannot be rendered into testimony of *zomemim*. The rule is that any testimony which cannot in principle be invalidated through the process of *hazamah*—the refutation of witnesses by proving they were elsewhere—is not legally valid testimony at all. *V'khol eidut she'atah yachol lehazimah lo shemah eidut.*

### Zomemim Logic

The logic is as follows. Witnesses testify that this *treifa* committed murder. If those witnesses were to be proven *zomemim*—false witnesses conspiring to cause the condemned's death—the punishment for *zomemim* is that we do to them what they intended to do to the accused: *ka'asher zamam*. But they were conspiring to take the life of a *treifa*, which halachically is not a full life. We cannot execute the *zomemim* in return, because that would not constitute equal recompense—we would be taking full lives in exchange for a non-full life. Since the witnesses cannot be subjected to the *zomemim* penalty, their testimony is categorically inadmissible, and without admissible testimony the *treifa* cannot be convicted.

## Treifa and Sex Act

### Treifa Not Corpse

Rava now extends his analysis. If a man commits a forbidden sexual act upon a *treifa*, he is *chayav*—fully liable. And if the *treifa* is the perpetrator, the same rule as above applies: *bifnei beit din chayav, shelo bifnei beit din patur*, for the same reasons of *uviarta hara mikirbecha* and *eidut she'i atah yachol lehazimah*.

### Limited Treifa Status

The *gemara* asks why this case needed to be stated—it appears to simply replicate the earlier ruling. The answer is that there is in fact a genuine *chiddush* here. One might have thought that since killing a *treifa* is treated as though the victim is not a full life, perhaps a *treifa* is halachically analogous to a corpse in *all* legal contexts, including that of prohibited sexual acts. If so, committing a forbidden act with a *treifa* might be no different from the case of one who engages with a corpse, which does not constitute a standard violation of the prohibition.

The *gemara* rejects this. The exemption from murder liability when the victim is a *treifa* is a *din* specific to the laws of murder. It does not mean that a *treifa* is treated as a corpse for all other halachic purposes. The basis: *ka mashma lan de-mishum hana'ah hu*—the prohibition of forbidden sexual acts is grounded in the physical pleasure of the act, and that pleasure is equally present with a living *treifa*. Hence full liability obtains.

### Other Payos Example

The *chiddush* is in fact broader than the sexual act context. To illustrate: if one were to remove the *peyos* from a *treifa*'s head, one would be fully liable for violating that prohibition, just as with any living person. One does not say "it is like cutting the *peyos* of a corpse." The *treifa*'s diminished status is operative only within the specific domain of the laws of murder, not as a general halachic reclassification of the person as dead.

## Witnesses & Treifa

### Witnesses vs. Treifa

Rava states: witnesses who testified against a *treifa* and were subsequently proven *zomemim* are not executed. This simply restates the principle underlying the earlier ruling: since they were conspiring to take a non-full life, we cannot take their lives as *ka'asher zamam* would require, and so the *zomemim* penalty cannot apply.

### Treifa Witnesses

A new and more complex case: what if the witnesses themselves are *treifos*, and they testify against a regular, fully living person? If they are proven *zomemim*, can they be executed? On its face, it seems they should be: they were conspiring to take a full life, and therefore their lives—even though not full lives—can be taken as *ka'asher zamam*, since the punishment would be *less* severe than what they sought to inflict, not more so. On this reading, Rava holds that such witnesses would be executed.

### Rav Ashi Position

Rav Ashi disagrees: *afilu edei treifa she-huzmu ein neheragim*—even *treifa* witnesses who are proven *zomemim* are not executed.

### Zomemim Logic

Rav Ashi's reasoning unfolds through a chain of logic. In order for the *zomemim* penalty to apply to these *treifa* witnesses, someone would have to come and refute them—proving they were elsewhere and thus lying. Those refuting witnesses would thereby be "making *zomemim*" out of the *treifa* witnesses. But can *those* refuting witnesses themselves be made into *zomemim*? No—because they are testifying against *treifa* witnesses, and as established, witnesses testifying against a *treifa* cannot be turned into *zomemim* (since the *zomemim* penalty cannot be imposed equally). Since somewhere in the chain the mechanism of *hazamah* breaks down, the *treifa* witnesses' testimony cannot be accepted in the first place, and they cannot be executed.

## Shor Treifa

### Rava on Shor

The *gemara* next considers the case of an ox that is a *treifa*—a category well-established in the laws of *kashrut*—that kills a person. Does it become a *shor haniskal*, an ox that must be stoned?

Rava holds: yes, the *treifa* ox becomes a *shor haniskal*. The *treifa* status of an animal does not diminish the act of killing; it only creates the procedural problem of *eidut she'i atah yachol lehazimah*, which pertains to human testimony about a human actor. An ox has no such procedural concern. However, Rava adds that if the *owner* of the ox is a *treifa*, the ox would *not* be stoned.

### Verse Connection

The basis is the verse: *hashor yisakel vegam be'alav yumat*—the ox shall be stoned and also its owner shall die. The *gemara* derives from this linkage: *kol heichi dekarina bei vegam be'alav yumat, karina bei hashor yisakel*—wherever we can say "the owner shall die" (i.e., the owner, had the owner committed the killing directly, would be subject to execution), we also apply "the ox shall be stoned." And wherever we cannot say the owner would be executed—such as when the owner is a *treifa*, whose conviction is blocked by *eidut she'i atah yachol lehazimah*—we do not stone the ox. The ox's fate is thus entirely governed by the owner's theoretical executability.

### Rav Ashi Ox View

Rav Ashi takes this further: *afilu shor treifa nami sheharag patur*—even a *treifa* ox that kills is not stoned. His reasoning differs from Rava's in a fundamental way. The *shor haniskal* procedure requires a court of twenty-three, because the ox is judged analogously to a human murderer. Rava views the ox as an extension of the owner: since we could not execute the owner, we do not stone the ox. Rav Ashi views the ox as *itself* being treated as the murderer. And since, were the ox a human *treifa*, it could not be executed—due to *eidut she'i atah yachol lehazimah*—so too the ox, viewed through the lens of a human murderer, is not stoned. The distinction is whether the ox's status follows from the owner's executability (Rava) or from the ox's own treated-as-human status (Rav Ashi).

## Animals as Killers

### Sicked Dog

The *gemara* now addresses cases of using living creatures as instruments of killing. If one *sics* a dog on a person—*shises bo et hakelev*—and the dog kills the victim, the person who sent the dog is exempt from capital punishment. The reason is straightforward: the dog performed the act, not the person. Even though the person is the clear causal agent, *grama* is not sufficient for murder liability.

### Snake Fangs Debate

A more complex scenario: a person physically takes a snake and presses its fangs into another person's arm. Rabbi Acha bar Yaakov notes that this is the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Chachamim.

### Rabbi Yehuda Snake

According to Rabbi Yehuda, *eres nachash bein shinav omed*—the venom is already positioned at the tip of the fangs, ready to inject. When you press the fangs into the victim, *you* are the one delivering the poison. The snake is, in effect, a poison-tipped knife in your hand. Therefore, *makish besayif*—the one who presses the fangs is treated like one who uses a sword—and the snake itself is not subject to *sekilah* (stoning), because the snake did not independently act.

### Chachamim Snake

The Chachamim disagree: *eres nachash me'elav hu meki*—the snake releases the venom on its own, even after you press its fangs against the victim. The snake's voluntary release of the venom—even if involuntary in a conscious sense—is a separate act that interposes between the person's action and the victim's death. Therefore, *lefichach nachash biskilah*—the snake itself becomes subject to *sekilah* as a killer—and *umakish patur*—the person who pressed the fangs is exempt—because in the end it was the snake's act, not his, that caused death. This raises the same questions discussed in Bava Kamma regarding a *shor ha'itztadin*—an ox that is in some sense compelled to act—and whether the concept of a willful killing can be attributed to a snake that has no apparent conscious deliberation.

### Gun Intermediary

The comparison to a firearm is instructive. When one pulls a trigger, one does not directly propel the bullet; one ignites a charge that propels it. If the snake is seen as analogous to the gunpowder—a mechanism that one activates and which then independently discharges—then the Chachamim's position makes sense. The animacy of the snake introduces an additional element: since the snake is a living being capable (in some sense) of acting or not acting, its role as an independent agent is more salient than that of gunpowder. Yet the question of whether a snake truly has any "choice" in the matter when its fangs are pressed against flesh is one the *gemara* does not fully resolve.

## Stone/Fist Case

### Initial Assessment

The *mishna* now introduces a pivotal case: *hamakeh et chaveiro bein be'even bein be'egrof ve'amduhu lemitah vehakal mima shehayah*—one struck a fellow with a stone or a fist and the blow was assessed as potentially lethal (*amduhu lemitah*), and then the victim appeared to be recovering. Subsequently the victim relapsed and died. The first *tanna* holds the perpetrator *chayav*: the act was assessed as lethal at the outset, and it did ultimately cause death. Both conditions are satisfied.

### Rabbi Nechemiah

Rabbi Nechemiah disagrees: the perpetrator is *patur*—exempt—since the interim recovery demonstrates that the blow did not in fact have the capacity to kill, and what happened afterward was a new, secondary complication rather than the direct continuation of the initial act.

### Two Conditions

This debate clarifies a principle that runs throughout the entire *sugya*: liability for murder requires two things. First, the blow must be assessed at the time of the act as having the capacity to kill (*amud lemitah*). Second, the victim must actually die from it. Both conditions must be established. If the blow was assessed as non-lethal but the victim died—no liability. If the blow was assessed as lethal but the victim recovered—no liability. The controversy between Rabbi Nechemiah and the Chachamim concerns only the case where both endpoints seem to be present (lethal assessment, actual death) but there is an intervening period of recovery.

### Verse Interpretation

Rabbi Nechemiah derives his position from the verses in Shemot. The *Torah* states: *ki yakeh ish et re'ehu be'even o be'egrof velo yamut venafal lemishkav, im yakum vehithalech bachutz al mishanto, venikah hamakeh*. The opening of the verse describes someone striking another with a stone or fist—exactly the language of our *mishna*. But the verse then says *im yakum vehithalech bachutz*—if he rises and walks outdoors—and then exempts the striker.

Rabbi Nechemiah asks: why would the verse need to tell us that if the victim is walking around, the striker is exempt? That is self-evident. Rather, *vehithalech bachutz* must be describing a more nuanced scenario: the victim was assessed as mortally wounded (*amduhu lemitah*), then appeared to improve (*hekel mima shehayah*), seemed to be recovering (*im yakum vehithalech*), and subsequently relapsed and died (*ve'achar mikan hichbid vamet*). In that case, *venikah hamakeh*—the striker is acquitted—because the moment of recovery interrupted the causal chain.

## Custody Principle

### Custody Rule

The Rabbanan derive a different teaching from the same verse. They argue that the formulation *venikah hamakeh*—"the striker shall be cleansed/released"—implies a prior state of being held in custody. Specifically: while the victim's fate remains undetermined, the perpetrator must be held in custody until we know whether he will be prosecuted for murder or merely for monetary damages. *Venikah hamakeh* then means: once the outcome is determined and he is not liable for murder, he is released.

### Source Debate

Rabbi Nechemiah objects: I already know to hold the person in custody from the case of the *Mekoshesh*—the wood-gatherer on *Shabbat*—as it says *vayanichu oto bemishmar*, they held him in custody. Therefore, the verse about the striker must be teaching something else—namely, Rabbi Nechemiah's rule about interim recovery.

### Mkoshesh

The Rabbanan respond that the *Mekoshesh* case cannot serve as a general precedent for holding a potential capital defendant in custody. In the *Mekoshesh* case, Moshe already knew from the principle of *mechalleliha mot yumat* that the *Mekoshesh* deserved death; what was unclear was merely the *form* of execution—*ki lo furash lahem mah ye'aseh lo*. It is natural and obvious that you hold someone in custody when you know they will be executed; the uncertainty was only procedural. But in the case of the striker, the entire question of whether he will be executed at all is unresolved while the victim lives. The verse is needed to teach us that even in that situation—where we do not yet know if a capital crime was committed—we hold the perpetrator in custody.

### Megadef

Rabbi Nechemiah counters: then I could learn from the *megadef*—the blasphemer. In that case, the verse says *lifrosh lahem al pi Hashem*—God should clarify for them—indicating that the appropriate disposition was entirely unknown, including even whether a capital crime had occurred. Yet they held the blasphemer in custody. That precedent can establish the general rule.

### General Rule

The Rabbanan reject this as well: *megadef hora'at sha'ah haytah*—the blasphemer case was an exceptional ruling, not a normative precedent. Rashi explains that the blasphemer was executed even though it had not yet been explicitly declared a capital offense—indeed, one could argue that without prior warning (*hasra'ah*) and without an established death penalty, the case was in multiple respects anomalous. Because that entire proceeding was a *hora'at sha'ah*, a one-time ruling, it cannot serve as the basis for a general rule that courts hold suspects pending determination of their victims' fate. Hence the verse about the striker is necessary to establish this rule.

## Two Assessments

### Verse Facts

Having worked through the dispute over the source for the custody requirement, the *gemara* now examines what Rabbi Nechemiah and the Chachamim each do with the two possible readings embedded in the verse. Rabbi Nechemiah reads the verse as encoding two distinct scenarios—two types of assessment—in a single passage.

### Scenario One

Scenario one: *amduhu lemitah vechaya*—the blow was assessed as lethal and the victim lived. Obviously the striker is exempt. No controversy here.

### Scenario Two

Scenario two: *amduhu lemitah, hekel mima shehayah, ve'achar kach hichbid vamet*—assessed as lethal, the victim seemed to recover, then relapsed and died. In this case, Rabbi Nechemiah says the striker is exempt because the moment of recovery broke the chain.

### Rabbis Reading

The Rabbanan, who use the verse for the custody requirement, need a different reading of the "two facts" embedded in it. Their second case is one where the victim was initially assessed as likely to survive (*amduhu lechayim*) and then unexpectedly died. In that case too, the striker is exempt—*venikah hamakeh*—because the threshold condition (lethal assessment at the time of the act) was never met. For Rabbi Nechemiah, this second scenario needs no verse: it is obvious that if the initial assessment was non-lethal, no capital liability can arise, since one of the two required conditions was never satisfied. From the very moment of the initial assessment as non-lethal, the striker left any capital proceeding as acquitted—*sharei yatza mibeis din zakai*. The verse would be entirely superfluous, and therefore Rabbi Nechemiah redeploys it for his teaching about interim recovery.

Everyone agrees on the two fundamental conditions: the blow must have been assessed as potentially lethal, and the victim must actually die. If either condition fails—assessed as non-lethal but victim dies, or assessed as lethal but victim survives—there is no capital liability. The sole controversy between Rabbi Nechemiah and the Chachamim is what legal weight to assign to an intervening period of apparent recovery before eventual death.

---

## Payment for Injury

### First Braita

The *gemara* now introduces a *Braita* that adds a further dimension to Rabbi Nechemiah's position. When a victim who was assessed as mortally wounded subsequently appears to recover, a second assessment is triggered—this time not for capital liability but for monetary damages (*omed sheni lemamon*). Since it now appears the blow was not in fact fatal, it is reclassified as an injury, and the perpetrator owes compensation accordingly.

### Monetary Assessment

This second assessment for monetary damages is calculated not from the moment of the apparent recovery but from the moment of the original beating. The relevant comparison is between the victim's state of full health before the beating and his condition now that he appears to be recovering. One does not calculate the improvement from near-death to injured; one calculates the total injury from healthy to the current state.

### Relapse Ruling

According to Rabbi Nechemiah, if the victim subsequently relapses and dies, the perpetrator pays the monetary damages assessed at the time of the recovery but is not executed for murder. The moment of apparent recovery definitively reclassified the act as an injury, and nothing that happens afterward reverses that reclassification.

### Chachamim

The Chachamim disagree: *ein omed achar omed*—there is no second assessment following the first. Once the initial assessment established the blow as lethal, that assessment stands. If the victim ultimately dies, the initial lethal assessment is confirmed and the perpetrator is executed. The apparent interim recovery does not trigger a legal reclassification and does not shift the proceeding from capital to monetary.

---

## Detailed Rules

### Reassess to Living

The *gemara* closes with a second *Braita* that provides a more granular statement of Rabbi Nechemiah's position. If the blow was initially assessed as lethal (*amduhu lemitah*) but the victim then appears to recover, the court reassesses and treats the situation as though the victim will live (*omdin oto lechayim*)—and the monetary damages assessment is made at this point.

### No Retroactive

However, the converse does not apply: if the victim was initially assessed as likely to survive (*amduhu lechayim*), and then unexpectedly dies, there is no retroactive reassessment to treat the blow as lethal. The initial non-lethal assessment is final, and the striker is exempt from capital liability.

### Payment Timing

If the victim was assessed as fatally wounded, then recovered and was reassessed as likely to survive, and then suffered a relapse and died, the payment is calculated based on the moment of the recovery assessment—the intermediate reassessment that classified the victim as injured rather than mortally stricken. That is the operative legal moment for purposes of computing the monetary compensation.

### Attribution

The *gemara* notes that this second *Braita* is presented anonymously—*stama*—and aligns it with Rabbi Nechemiah's position. Although Rabbi Nechemiah's view is formally a *da'at yachid*, a minority opinion, a number of these anonymous *braisos* operate according to his framework, suggesting that his reading of the verse and his legal principles had broader resonance than one might expect from a lone dissent.
