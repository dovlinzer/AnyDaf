# Nazir 56b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah & Mikdash Entry

### Rabbi Eliezer's Ruling

The *perek* concludes with two *mishnayot* that are considerably less intense than the intricate debates we have been navigating throughout this chapter regarding the various forms of *tumas meis* and their consequences for the *nazir*. The first of these opens with a sweeping principle attributed to Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: any *tumah min hameis* for which a *nazir* shaves his head — thereby nullifying his earlier *nezirut* — is also the category of *tumah* for which one incurs liability upon entering the *Beit Hamikdash* while in that state of *tumah*. Conversely, any *tumah min hameis* for which the *nazir* does not shave carries with it no liability for *bias mikdash*. The two categories mirror each other precisely.

It is worth recalling the context: *tumas meis* is unusual in that it is tolerated even within *Machaneh Leviyah* — one may proceed as far as *Ezras Nashim* — and the liability we are discussing applies only upon actual entry into the *Azarah* itself. With that in mind, the principle the *mishnah* is establishing becomes clear: whatever constitutes a *nazir*-invalidating *tumah* is also the threshold for *Mikdash* liability.

### Rabbi Meir's Challenge

Rabbi Meir pushes back sharply. His argument is straightforward: even granting that there exist forms of *tumas meis* that do not require the *nazir* to shave — and we spent much of this *perek* cataloguing those cases — these are still forms of *tumah* derived from contact with the dead. How can they possibly be less severe than touching a *sheretz*, a dead rodent? Any *tumah* whatsoever generates liability upon entering the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *mishnah* is not discussing the rabbinically derived categories such as *skachos*, *praos*, or *Beis Hapras*, but rather cases that are assumed to be biblically rooted — cases that, despite their biblical status, are nonetheless exceptions to the rule of *nazir megalayach*. Rabbi Meir insists that the *nazir* exception cannot translate into an exception for *bias mikdash*; the person is still *tamei meis*, and that is enough to be liable.

### Beraitah Discussion

The *Gemara* is less interested in the substance of this debate than in a secondary observation: how did Rabbi Eliezer come to state this principle in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua? A *braisa* is introduced in which Rabbi Eliezer recounts that when he traveled to Ardaskaya, he found Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peser Rosh sitting and presenting his analysis before Rabbi Meir — and the content of that teaching was precisely the principle articulated in our *mishnah*: the categories of *tumah* that trigger *nazir megalayach* are the same that trigger liability for *bias mikdash*, and those that do not trigger the former do not trigger the latter. When Rabbi Meir responded with his challenge — again invoking the comparison to *sheretz* — it was Rabbi Eliezer who intervened.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Source

Rabbi Eliezer turned to Rabbi Meir and asked: are you familiar with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Mamo, and do you know how weighty an authority he is? Rabbi Meir replied that he was. Rabbi Eliezer then stated: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Mamo transmitted this ruling to me in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua — the *stam* Rabbi Yehoshua, that is, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya — and the content of that tradition is precisely this principle. With that citation, Rabbi Eliezer gave backing to the position of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peser Rosh and confirmed the ruling in our *mishnah*.

This raises an obvious question: if Rabbi Eliezer received the tradition through Rabbi Yehoshua ben Mamo, why does our *mishnah* present it simply as Rabbi Eliezer saying it in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua, skipping the intermediate link entirely?

## Tradition Transmission

### Kadmaya and Batraye

The *Gemara* draws from this episode a principle about how chains of tradition are reported. From the fact that Rabbi Eliezer states a tradition as going directly from himself to Rabbi Yehoshua — omitting Rabbi Yehoshua ben Mamo, the authority from whom he actually received it — we learn that when a tradition passes through multiple links, it is sufficient and indeed standard practice to cite only the *kadmaya* and the *batraye*, the originator and the final transmitter. The intermediate authorities — the *metziaye* — need not always be enumerated.

### Nachman Example

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a parallel illustration. Nachman haLavlur is quoted as saying: this is what I received from Rabbi Measha, who received it from his father, who received it from the *zugot* — the early paired authorities of the Second Temple era, such as Hillel and Shammai and those before them — who received it from the *neviim*, and this constitutes a *halakha leMoshe miSinai* going back to Moshe Rabbeinu. The specific content concerns planting dill and mustard seed in two or three separate locations in one's field, and the requirement to leave *peah* — a corner portion — independently for each section rather than one combined corner for the whole.

The precise details belong to the laws of *peah*, but the point the *Gemara* draws on is the structure of the transmission: Nachman haLavlur gives a chain that proceeds from himself through several generations back to the *neviim*.

### Omitted Authorities

The *Gemara* immediately asks: but where in this chain are Yehoshua and Chalev? We know from *Pirkei Avot* that Moshe transmitted the Torah to Yehoshua, Yehoshua to the *zekenim*, and the *zekenim* to the *neviim* — yet Nachman haLavlur's chain jumps directly from the *neviim* to earlier links without specifying Yehoshua and the *zekenim*. This confirms, *shema mina*, that one need not enumerate every intermediate step in a chain of tradition. Some steps may be skipped while still faithfully representing the lineage.

## Rabbi Akiva's Logic

### Kal Vachomer Argument

The next *mishnah* shifts to a debate between Rabbi Akiva and his teacher Rabbi Eliezer, and it is notable that the discussion is introduced with the same formula used earlier: *danti lifnei*, I laid out my argument before him. Rabbi Akiva formulates the following *kal vachomer*: a bone the size of a barley grain does not cause *tumah* through *ohel* — it lacks the power to render someone *tamei* by being in the same covered space — and yet a *nazir* must shave if he touches or carries such a bone. Now, a *revi'is* of blood — a quarter-*log* — does cause *tumah be'ohel*. Should it not follow, by an inference *a fortiori*, that a *nazir* who touches or carries a *revi'is* of blood should certainly be obligated to shave?

The elegance of the argument is worth pausing over. Rabbi Akiva is careful not to argue that the *revi'is* should cause *tumah be'ohel* for the *nazir* — that would require a different *kal vachomer*. He argues only about *maga* and *masa*, touch and carrying. If the little bone — which cannot even cause *ohel tumah* — is sufficient to obligate a *nazir* to shave when touched, then a *revi'is* of blood, which can cause *ohel tumah*, should certainly be sufficient to obligate shaving when touched. The existing rule held that shaving was triggered only from a half-*log* of blood, and Rabbi Akiva was pressing: why demand a half-*log* even for *maga*?

### Rabbi Eliezer Rejects

Rabbi Eliezer's response was terse: *mah zeh Akiva, ein danim mi-kal vachomer* — what are you doing, Akiva? Logical inference has no place here. These are arbitrary rules, and this is not the arena for your *kal vachomers*. He neither validated the argument nor explained why it was inapplicable; he simply dismissed it. This reaction reflects something fundamental about Rabbi Eliezer's orientation toward Torah: he famously never stated anything he had not received from his teacher, and the application of human logic to these received rules struck him as fundamentally misplaced.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Validates

Rabbi Akiva was unsatisfied with this response and brought the argument to Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua's reply was strikingly different in tone. He said: *yafeh amarta* — you have argued well. The argument is sound. But: *ella kach amru halacha* — nevertheless, this is the received tradition, and it stands as it is.

The distinction in approach is instructive. Both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua arrive at the same bottom line — the *kal vachomer* does not change the *halacha*. But Rabbi Yehoshua validated the argument's internal logic while explaining why it nonetheless cannot override the tradition. Rabbi Eliezer simply rejected the enterprise of reasoning altogether. This dynamic reflects a broader tension running through the tradition between those who see *Torah she-be'al peh* primarily as the custodianship and transmission of received law, and those who see it as an ongoing intellectual engagement with text and logic. Rabbi Eliezer was paradigmatically the former; Rabbi Akiva — who brought unprecedented creativity and derivation to *halacha* — represented the latter. It is no coincidence that Rabbi Eliezer was more dismissive.

## Halakha L'Moshe Question

### Which Exception?

The *Gemara* now raises a pointed question: when Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua say that the *kal vachomer* is inappropriate because this is a *halacha*, what exactly is the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* being referenced? The *Gemara* presents two possibilities. One: the *halakha leMoshe miSinai* is the rule that even a bone the size of a barley grain — which lacks the power of *tumas ohel* — is sufficient to obligate a *nazir* to shave. Under this reading, the bone is the anomaly, and you cannot build a *kal vachomer* on an anomaly. Two: the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is the rule that a *revi'is* of blood causes *tumas ohel* at all. Under this reading, the *revi'is* of blood is the anomaly, and it cannot be invoked as the stronger horn of a *kal vachomer*.

The reasoning behind the second possibility draws on the verse: *al kol nafshot meis lo yavo*. As *Tosafot* explains, a plain reading of the *psukim* might imply that the minimum for *tumas meis* is a half-*log* of blood — because *nafshot* in the plural suggests an amount sufficient for two lives, which would be two *revi'is*, totaling a half-*log*. On this reading, the *revi'is* of blood causing *tumas ohel* is itself a *chiddush*, a departure from what the verses suggest, and therefore cannot serve as the stronger premise of a *kal vachomer*.

### Bone as Exception

The *Gemara* resolves the question by citing a *braisa* explicitly: the rule that a bone the size of a barley grain obligates a *nazir* to shave is the *halacha leMoshe miSinai*. The *revi'is* of blood is not a *halacha leMoshe miSinai* in this context; Rabbi Akiva was attempting to derive it by *kal vachomer* from the bone. But since the bone is itself a designated exception — a *halacha leMoshe miSinai* — it cannot serve as the basis of derivation.

### Kal Vachomer Principle

This brings us to a broader principle established here: *ein danin kal vachomer me-halacha* — one cannot construct a *kal vachomer* using a case that is itself labeled as a *halacha leMoshe miSinai*. The reason is conceptual. A *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is, by its nature, a sui generis received tradition — it is not intended to convey a larger principle from which further law may be derived. The entire enterprise of *kal vachomer* depends on the premise that the stronger case is stronger because of some underlying logic. When a rule is categorized as a *halacha leMoshe miSinai*, it stands outside that logical framework. It is simply tradition, not a proposition whose implications can be extended by human reasoning.

One might ask why this should be so — surely a tradition received at Sinai carries the highest possible authority, and if anything one might expect it to be the most reliable premise from which to derive. The answer seems to lie in the nature of the two modes of *halacha*. When we engage *pesukim* through the thirteen hermeneutical principles, we are working within a system of intellectual derivation where each ruling participates in a larger logical discourse. *Halacha leMoshe miSinai*, by contrast, is pure received tradition, standing apart from that discourse. To borrow its specific ruling as a logical stepping stone would be to misappropriate it — treating as a logical proposition something that is, by definition, a tradition unto itself.

## Two Nazirims Safek

### Safek Setup

The next *mishnah* presents a new scenario. There are two *nezirim*, and a third person informs them: I saw one of you become *tamei* — perhaps he walked unknowingly into a burial ground — but I do not know which one of you it was. Each of the two *nezirim* is now a *safek nazir tamei*, a *nazir* of doubtful *tumah* status.

### Solution with Stipulation

The *mishnah* provides a practical resolution for this predicament. Since one of the two is definitely obligated in a *korban tumah* and the other is definitely obligated in a *korban tahara*, they do not each need to bring both sets independently with stipulations. Rather, they bring their respective sets together and stipulate: if I am the one who was *tamei*, then my *korban* is the *korban tumah* and yours is the *korban tahara*; and if I am the one who was *tahor*, then mine is the *korban tahara* and yours is the *korban tumah*. Because one of each is definitively owed, the full set of obligatory *korbanot* — including the *chatat ha-of* for *tumah* — can be brought without resorting to a *nedava* framework.

This approach is considerably more efficient than what a single *safek tamei nazir* would face. A single individual in that state cannot bring a full *korban tumah* set, since he may not be obligated, and faces additional complications around the *asham*, which cannot be brought either *misafek* or *bitnai*. Having a partner in the same predicament allows the full sets to be brought and directed appropriately.

---

## Two Nazirims Safek…

### Two Periods

Following the initial *korban* sequence, the two *nezirim* count an additional thirty days. One of them has just concluded — if he was the *nazir tahor*, he is now finished. But the other — if he was the *nazir tamei* — must now complete his reconstituted *nezirut*. At the conclusion of the second thirty-day period, again one of them is definitively obligated in a full *korban tahara*, and again a stipulation is issued: whoever is the *tamei* one, the earlier set thirty days ago was his *korban tumah*; whoever is the *tahor* one, the earlier set was his *korban tahara*. And now, the *korban tahara* brought at day sixty belongs to whichever of them still needs it. In this way, the full halakhic obligations of both possible scenarios are discharged without either person needing to know his own status.

## Safek Tumah Domains

### Sotah Principle

The *Gemara* raises an unexpected question on the entire framework of the *mishnah*: why are the two *nezirim* treated as *safek tamei* at all? The principle governing *safek tumah* — learned from the case of the *Sotah* — is: *safek tumah birshut hayachid, safeiko tamei; safek tumah birshut harabbim, safeiko tahor*. Doubtful *tumah* in a private domain is treated as *tamei*; doubtful *tumah* in a public domain is treated as *tahor*. This principle, derived conceptually from the *Sotah* case — where *v'nistra*, she went into seclusion, implies that only two parties were present — applies whenever a doubt arises in a setting of two people.

### Three Make Public

If there were two *nezirim* plus the witness who observed the incident, that is three people. Three people constitute a *reshut harabbim* for the purposes of this rule. And if it is a *reshut harabbim*, then *safeiko tahor* — both *nezirim* should be ruled *tahor*, and the entire elaborate procedure of the *mishnah* would be unnecessary. This is a striking problem.

*Tosafot* raises a further complication from the other direction. Even if we were to apply the *reshut hayachid* principle, the *halacha* in that case does not merely treat the person as *safek tamei* — it treats him as *vadai tamei*, definitively *tamei*, until cleared. Yet the entire *mishnah* operates within a framework of *safek*, not *vadai*. *Tosafot* resolves this by noting that since it is impossible for both of them to be *vadai tamei* — only one actually is — we cannot apply the *vadai* consequence to both simultaneously. The situation therefore remains in the realm of *safek*, even in a *reshut hayachid*, because the absolute ruling cannot be consistently applied.

### Distance Witness

The *Gemara* resolves the domain question through Rabba bar bar Huna: the case concerns a witness who was standing at a distance from the two *nezirim*. He observed them from afar — perhaps he saw a piece of a corpse thrown in their direction, or simply could not make out who was who — and did not share the same space. The two *nezirim* were therefore in a *reshut hayachid* unto themselves, since the witness was not with them in that enclosed space. This reading is reinforced by Rav Ashi, who notes that the precise language of the *mishnah* — *eini yodeia eizeh mikem*, I do not know which one of you — implies not that the witness forgot what he saw, but that he never knew in the first place, which fits naturally with the scenario of observing at a distance and being unable to identify which individual was involved.

## Shaving Problem

### Hakafa Concern

The *Gemara* now raises a difficulty with the mechanics of the *mishnah*'s solution. Both *nezirim* shave at the end of each thirty-day period. But if one of them is *tahor*, he has no obligation to shave the second time. He shaves anyway — to discharge the possible obligation of the one who is *tamei*. Yet in doing so, he shaves all the hair of his head. This raises the concern of *hakafa*: the prohibition of *lo takifu pe'at rosheichem*, not to cut the sidelocks in a circular manner. Even setting aside the question of whether this person's *nezirut* is over, does shaving all the hair of one's head violate this prohibition?

The *Gemara* notes that the *giluach* itself is not *me'akeiv* the completion of *nezirut* — one's *nezirut* ends regardless of whether one shaves. So one might argue: why not simply have both *nezirim* shave only once at day thirty, and forgo the second shaving, thereby avoiding any possible transgression? The *mishnah* nevertheless prescribes shaving twice. The question is how that is permitted.

### Shmuel's Answer

Shmuel resolves the difficulty: the *mishnah* is dealing with a case of a woman or a minor. A woman is excluded from the prohibition of *hakafa* because that prohibition is linked to the prohibition against shaving the beard — and since women do not typically have beards, they are outside the category entirely. Anyone excluded from the prohibition of shaving the beard is correspondingly excluded from the prohibition of *hakafa* of the sideburns. Thus, if the two *nezirim* are women, there is no *hakafa* concern, and both may shave freely at both junctures.

### Full Head Alternative

The *Gemara* challenges this: why did Shmuel resort to the case of a woman or minor at all? Another solution was available: perhaps shaving the entire head does not constitute a violation of *hakafa* in the first place. *Lo takifu* means to make a circular cut — to create a hairline that encircles the head. If one shaves everything and is left entirely bald, there is no hairline at all, and therefore no *hakafa* in any meaningful sense.

### Shmuel's Position

Since Shmuel does not offer this answer and instead specifies the case of a woman or minor, we may infer — *shema mina* — that Shmuel holds *hakafat kol harosh shemah hakafa*: shaving the entire head does constitute the prohibited *hakafa*. Under ordinary circumstances, an adult male who shaves all the hair of his head without halakhic justification violates this prohibition. The only way to permit the second shaving, therefore, is to situate the case where the individuals are not subject to the prohibition at all — hence women or minors.

## Four Shavings Case

### Mishnah Setup

Mar Zutra reports that Shmuel's discussion is presented not in connection with our *mishnah* but with a subsequent one, which we encountered in a recent *daf*: a *nazir* who is *safek tamei* and *safek metzora*. Such a person must spread out his obligations over sixty days and performs four separate shavings across that period. As a *metzora*, he shaves at the onset of his *tahara* process and again a week later at its conclusion; as a *nazir tamei*, he shaves at the end of his *tumah* period and again at the completion of his reconstituted *nezirut*. If he is *safek* in both categories, the four shavings are spaced across sixty days to accommodate all possibilities. Yet in each case, the possibility that a given shaving is unnecessary raises the same *hakafa* concern.

### Same Question Applied

The question and resolution follow the same logic. The *Gemara* asks why we permit an individual who may not be obligated to shave repeatedly, given the prohibition of *hakafa*, and Shmuel's answer is the same: *be-ishah ve-katan*, a woman or a minor. Since Shmuel requires this qualification rather than relying on the argument that shaving the full head is not *hakafa*, the inference stands: Shmuel maintains that shaving the entire head does constitute *hakafa* and is prohibited for an adult male under ordinary circumstances.

## Shaver Independence

### Adult Shaver Liable

At this point Rav Huna introduces a position that cuts against the implicit assumption in Shmuel's answer. Shmuel resolved the *hakafa* concern by making the *nazir* a woman or minor — implying that if the *shavee* is exempt from the prohibition, the shaving is permissible. Rav Huna challenges this framing: *ha-makif et ha-katan harei hu chayav* — one who shaves a minor is nonetheless liable. The prohibition of *lo takifu* operates independently for the *shaver*: if the shaver is an adult male, he transgresses regardless of who the *shavee* is, even if the *shavee* is a minor or a woman entirely outside the prohibition.

### Wife Shaving Issue

Rav Adda bar Ahava immediately challenges Rav Huna: if an adult male transgresses as a shaver regardless of who the *shavee* is, how does your wife shave your children? Their sidelocks are evidently being cut. Who is doing it?

### Woman Exempt

Rav Huna replies: *Chova* — his wife's name — shaves them. Since a woman is excluded from the prohibition of shaving the beard, she is correspondingly excluded from the prohibition of *hakafa* as the shaver. The shaver's liability tracks whether the shaver is in the prohibited category, not whether the *shavee* is.

### Role Rules

This yields a clear structure. An adult male cannot occupy either position: he may not be the shaver, and he may not be the *shavee*. Women and minors may occupy either position — shaver or *shavee* — without transgressing. The key restriction is that both parties in the act must be free of the prohibition: an adult male as shaver violates the prohibition regardless of who sits in the chair; an adult male as *shavee* similarly implicates a transgression. But a woman may shave a minor, a minor may shave a woman, and any combination that excludes an adult male in either role is permissible.

### Rav Adda's Curse

Rav Adda bar Ahava's response to Rav Huna's answer was, to say the least, pointed. He exclaimed: *tikberinehu Chova le-vaneia* — may Chova bury her children. The *Rosh* softens this, reading it as a rhetorical expression of outrage: does she want to bury her children? Is this what she is doing to them? Yet whether spoken as a curse or an exclamation, the *Gemara* records its effect. For all the years of Rav Adda bar Ahava's lifetime, Rav Huna had no surviving children. The words, however they were intended, carried force — a theme the *Gemara* returns to repeatedly in its understanding of how the utterances of scholars reverberate beyond their speaker's intention.
