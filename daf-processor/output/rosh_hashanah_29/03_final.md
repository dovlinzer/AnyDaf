# Rosh Hashanah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mitzvot, Kavanah, Bal To…

### La'atzeis vs. La'avor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא, ״זִכְרוֹן תְּרוּעָה״ כְּתִיב, וְהַאי מִתְעַסֵּק בְּעָלְמָא הוּא — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר רָבָא: מִצְוֹת אֵין צְרִיכוֹת כַּוָּונָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** with regard to a *shofar*, **it is written: "A memorial of blasts"** (Leviticus 23:24), which might have been understood as requiring conscious intent, **and this one was merely acting unawares,** without having any intent whatsoever of performing the mitzva. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that the absence of intent does not invalidate fulfillment of the mitzva, even in the case of *shofar*. The Gemara concludes: **Apparently, Rava maintains** that the fulfillment of **mitzvot does not require intent.** That is to say, if one performs a mitzva, he fulfills his obligation even if he has no intention of doing so.

The *daf* before us is 29, with which we finish the third *perek* and enter into the fourth and final *perek* of *Rosh Hashanah*. We pick up three lines from the bottom of 28b, in the midst of a discussion of several central issues that *Rava* has introduced across the preceding pages. One was *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* — whether deriving benefit from fulfilling a *mitzvah* constitutes a technical violation of the prohibition against *hana'ah* from certain objects. A related but distinct example helps clarify the stakes: dissecting a corpse for medical knowledge is permitted even though such knowledge has enormous value, because it does not constitute *hana'ah* in the relevant technical sense.

A second issue was whether *kavanah* is required to fulfill a *mitzvah* — *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah*. All of the evidence from the *Mishnayot* seemed to require *kavanah*, understood as an awareness and intent that what one is performing is a *mitzvah* act. *Rava* disagrees, arguing that the *Mishnayot* speak only of intent to perform the act itself, not of labeling it as a *mitzvah*. Finally, the discussion transitioned into the issue of *bal tosif* and *bal tigra* — the prohibition against adding to or subtracting from a *mitzvah*. If *mitzvot* require no *kavanah*, it should be alarmingly easy to transgress *bal tosif*, since anything done during the time of a *mitzvah* might automatically become incorporated into it — as in the case of sleeping in a *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret*.

Now *Rava* attempts to reconcile these tensions. He states: *la'atzeis lo ba'ei kavanah* — to fulfill one's obligation, one does not need *kavanah*. But *la'avor ba'ei kavanah* — to transgress *bal tosif*, one does need intent. The reasoning is elegant. Taking a *lulav* and *etrog* on *Sukkot* is self-defining as a *mitzvah* act; no special intent is required for it to count. But if one holds a pencil in the same hand, nothing about holding a pencil is objectively a *mitzvah* act. To incorporate it into the *mitzvah* and thereby transgress *bal tosif*, one would need *kavanah* — the explicit intent to expand the *mitzvah* act to include it. Similarly, sleeping in a *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret* is not, by itself, an act that is self-evidently a *mitzvah*; one might simply be sleeping outdoors. To transgress *bal tosif* by extending the seven-day *mitzvah* into an eighth day, one would have to consciously frame that act as a *mitzvah* act.

### Lulav & Intentionality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָיָה עוֹבֵר אֲחוֹרֵי בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה בֵּיתוֹ סָמוּךְ לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְשָׁמַע קוֹל שׁוֹפָר אוֹ קוֹל מְגִילָּה, אִם כִּוֵּן לִבּוֹ — יָצָא, וְאִם לָאו — לֹא יָצָא. מַאי לָאו, אִם כִּוֵּן לִבּוֹ לָצֵאת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection: **Come** and **hear** that which we learned in our mishna: If **one was passing behind a synagogue, or his house was adjacent to the synagogue, and he heard the sound of** the ***shofar* or the sound of the Scroll** of Esther, **if he focused his heart, he has fulfilled** his obligation, **but if not, he has not fulfilled** his obligation. **What, is it not** that **he focused his heart to fulfill** his obligation, and if he failed to do so, he has not fulfilled his duty, therefore implying that the fulfillment of mitzvot requires intent?

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this formulation. It invokes the earlier case of *matan damim* — the application of blood — in which *Rabbi Yehoshua* held there is a problem of *bal tosif* even without apparent *kavanah* to expand the *mitzvah*. If *Rava's* principle is correct, *Rabbi Eliezer* could simply have responded that there is no *kavanah* present and therefore no violation. This is in fact one possible reading of *Rabbi Eliezer's* statement: *lo ne'emar bal tosif ela k'shehu l'atzmo* — *bal tosif* is only operative when one performs the expansion on its own terms; here the additional applications are the product of the bloods having become mixed, leaving the *kohen* no choice. That resolution may be precisely what *Rabbi Eliezer* intended.

Nevertheless, the *Gemara* treats this as a significant challenge to *Rava's* formulation and presses further.

### Bizmano vs. Shelo Bizmano

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱמַר לוֹ, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: מִצְוֹת אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עֲלֵיהֶן אֶלָּא בִּזְמַנָּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** This is **because I say** that **mitzvot can be transgressed only** by adding to them **in their** prescribed **times.** But if one adds to a mitzva outside of the period of obligation for the mitzva, there is no violation of the prohibition against adding to mitzvot. On the Eighth Day of Assembly there is no longer a mitzva to sleep in the *sukka*. Therefore, sleeping in the *sukka* on that day does not constitute a prohibited act.

*Rava* now refines his position: *la'atzeis lo ba'ei kavanah* — to fulfill one's obligation, no intent is needed. But to transgress *bal tosif*, the critical variable is *bizmano* — whether the act is performed during the time of the *mitzvah*. If it is *bizmano*, then *lo ba'ei kavanah* — even without intent, something can naturally become incorporated into the *mitzvah* act, and one may transgress *bal tosif*. Thus in the case of *matan damim*, the additional applications occur *bizmano*, within the performance of the *mitzvah* itself, and therefore become incorporated without any conscious intent to expand it. *Aval shelo bizmano* — not during the time of the *mitzvah*, as in sleeping in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret* — nothing defines that act as part of the *mitzvah*, and therefore *ba'ei kavanah*: explicit intent is required to incorporate it and thereby transgress *bal tosif*.

This resolves several of the earlier cases. The extra blessing of *birkat kohanim* involves *kavanah*, while the blood application does not, but is *bizmano*. The *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret* is neither accompanied by *kavanah* nor is it *bizmano*, and therefore presents no *bal tosif* concern.

### Types of Kavanah

This resolution, however, opens a further question: does *lo ba'ei kavanah bizmano* mean that literally everything one does while performing the *mitzvah* is automatically incorporated? That cannot be right — if one happens to be wearing a ring or holding one's glasses while taking the *lulav*, one surely has not transgressed *bal tosif*. The answer lies in clarifying what kind of *kavanah* is under discussion at each stage. When *Rava* says *mitzvot lo ba'ei kavanah* — that *mitzvot* do not require *kavanah* — he means one need not specifically intend to perform a *mitzvah*; intent to do the act itself suffices to render one *yotzei*. Similarly, *la'avor bizmano lo ba'ei kavanah* means one need not intend to expand the *mitzvah* consciously, but one does still have to intend to do the act in a way that makes it incorporated into what is being performed. The person applying blood four times is aware that he is making those applications, even if he did not intend to expand the *mitzvah*. The person who is wearing a ring is not intending to incorporate the ring into the *lulav* act at all. The distinction, then, between *kavanah* to frame something as an expanded *mitzvah* act — which one does not need *bizmano* — and *kavanah* to do the act in a way that connects it to the *mitzvah* performance — which one still needs — is where the precise and admittedly narrow line must be drawn.

## Kavanah LeHotzi

### Rav Zeira's Shofar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: אִיכַּוַּן וּתְקַע לִי. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: מַשְׁמִיעַ בָּעֵי כַּוָּונָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the intent required in order to fulfill the mitzva of *shofar*, **Rabbi Zeira said to his servant: Have intent** to sound the *shofar* on my behalf **and sound** it **for me.** The Gemara infers: **Apparently,** Rabbi Zeira **maintains** that **he who sounds** the *shofar* for others **is required to have intent** to discharge the hearer's obligation.

The *Gemara* now introduces a new dimension of *kavanah* through the instruction of *Rav Zeira* to his attendant: *ikaven u'tka li* — have *kavanah* when you blow the *shofar* and blow for me. This instruction adds something beyond the question of *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah*. Even granting *Rava's* view that *mitzvot* do not require *kavanah* to render oneself *yotzei*, blowing the *shofar* for another person introduces a distinct requirement: *kavanah lehotzi* — the intent not merely to blow, but specifically to help discharge the other person's obligation. It is not enough that the blower intends this as a *mitzvah* act of *tkias shofar* in general. For the act to become the other person's act — to connect to them — the blower must consciously intend to link that blowing to this specific listener. This is the principle of *ad sheyichavein shomeia umashmia* — both the hearer and the one causing the sound to be heard must have *kavanah*, but the *kavanah* required of the *mashmia* is specifically *kavanah lehotzi*.

### Shomea & Mashmia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיָה עוֹבֵר אֲחוֹרֵי בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה בֵּיתוֹ סָמוּךְ לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְשָׁמַע קוֹל שׁוֹפָר אוֹ קוֹל מְגִילָּה, אִם כִּוֵּן לִבּוֹ — יָצָא, וְאִם לָאו — לֹא יָצָא. וְכִי כִּוֵּן לִבּוֹ מַאי הָוֵי? הֵיאַךְ לָא קָא מִיכַּוֵּין אַדַּעְתָּא דִּידֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the mishna: If **one was passing behind a synagogue, or his house was adjacent to the synagogue, and he heard the sound of** the ***shofar* or the sound of** the **Scroll** of Esther being read, **if he focused his heart** to fulfill his obligation, **he has fulfilled** his obligation, **but if not, he has not fulfilled** his obligation. It may be asked: **And,** according to Rabbi Zeira, even **if** the hearer **focused his heart, what of it? The other one,** i.e., the one sounding the *shofar*, **did not focus his intent** to sound the *shofar* **with him in mind?** If indeed the intent of the one sounding the *shofar* is required, how does the passerby fulfill his obligation?

A *baraita* is then cited in challenge: *niskavein shomeia velo niskavein mashmia, niskavein mashmia velo niskavein shomeia, lo yatza — ad sheyichavein shomeia umashmia*. Both must have intent; if either is lacking, the obligation is not discharged. The *Gemara* observes that this *baraita* describes the intent of the *mashmia* in parallel with the intent of the *shomea*: just as the hearer need only intend to fulfill his own obligation, so apparently the blower need only intend to fulfill his own obligation. No additional, distinct *kavanah lehotzi* seems to be required of the blower — the *baraita* speaks of them in identical terms. This is a compelling challenge to *Rav Zeira's* requirement.

### Overhearing Behind Shul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בִּשְׁלִיחַ צִיבּוּר עָסְקִינַן, דְּדַעְתֵּיהּ אַכּוּלֵּיהּ עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with the representative of the community,** i.e., one **who** sounds the *shofar* for the entire congregation and **has everyone in mind.** He does not sound it for a specific individual, but rather on behalf of the entire community, and therefore anyone who hears him sound the *shofar* fulfills his obligation.

The *Gemara* raises a related case. Someone was walking behind a *beis haknesses* or living in an adjacent house and happened to hear the *shofar* or *megillah*: *im kivein libo yatza, v'im lav lo yatza* — if he had intent, he fulfilled his obligation; if not, he did not. The *Gemara* immediately notes the problem: even if this incidental listener had intent, what does it matter? *Lo kamichavein adaata dideih* — the blower had no awareness of this person at all and certainly had no intent to discharge his obligation.

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the case of the *shliach tzibbur*: we are speaking here of the one who blows or reads in an official congregational capacity. When one serves in that role, the nature of the position itself carries a blanket intent — *dedaateih achulei alma*, his intent extends to whoever hears, anyone in the world who needs to be *yotzei* with this performance. He does not enumerate individuals; he understands he is performing the *mitzvah* for the community, and that extends to whomever is within hearing range.

### Shliach Tzibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִתְכַּוֵּן שׁוֹמֵעַ וְלֹא נִתְכַּוֵּן מַשְׁמִיעַ, נִתְכַּוֵּן מַשְׁמִיעַ וְלֹא נִתְכַּוֵּן שׁוֹמֵעַ — לֹא יָצָא, עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוֵּן שׁוֹמֵעַ וּמַשְׁמִיעַ. קָתָנֵי מַשְׁמִיעַ דּומְיָא דְשׁוֹמֵעַ. מָה שׁוֹמֵעַ — שׁוֹמֵעַ לְעַצְמוֹ, אַף מַשְׁמִיעַ — מַשְׁמִיעַ לְעַצְמוֹ, וְקָתָנֵי לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection: **Come** and **hear** that which was taught in a *baraita*: If **the hearer** of the *shofar* **had intent, but the sounder** of the *shofar* **did not have intent,** or if **the sounder** of the *shofar* **had intent, but the hearer did not have intent, he has not fulfilled** his obligation, **until** both **the hearer and the sounder have intent.** The *baraita* **teaches** the *halakha* governing **the sounder** of the *shofar* **in similar fashion to** the *halakha* governing **the hearer.** From this it may be inferred that **just as the hearer hears for himself,** having intent to fulfill his own obligation, **so too, the sounder sounds for himself,** having intent to fulfill his own obligation, and not that of others. **And** the *baraita* **teaches** that if the sounder did not have this intent, the hearer **has not fulfilled** his obligation. But this indicates that if the sounder had intent to sound the *shofar* for himself, he need not have intent to sound it for others, therefore contradicting Rabbi Zeira's opinion.

This distinction between the *shliach tzibbur* and a private individual is made explicit by *Rabbi Yosi*. In the same *baraita* tradition, *mashmia lefi darko* — the blower performs it in his usual manner without special intent — is clarified as follows: *bameh devarim amurim — bishliach tzibbur*. This standard applies to the *shliach tzibbur*, for whom ordinary, unstated intent suffices precisely because his role presupposes he is blowing for others. *Aval beyachid — lo yatza ad sheyitkaven shomea umashmia*: but a private individual blowing for someone else requires explicit *kavanah lehotzi* — he must specifically intend to discharge that person's obligation. While the earlier *baraita* appeared to describe the intent of *mashmia* and *shomea* in identical terms, *Rabbi Yosi's* distinction makes clear that a private individual who wishes to serve as the means through which another person fulfills the *mitzvah* must have an active, directed intent to that effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוֹמֵעַ — שׁוֹמֵעַ לְעַצְמוֹ, וּמַשְׁמִיעַ — מַשְׁמִיעַ לְפִי דַּרְכּוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּשְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר, אֲבָל בְּיָחִיד — לָא יָצָא, עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוֵּין שׁוֹמֵעַ וּמַשְׁמִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The hearer hears for himself, and the sounder sounds** the *shofar* **in his** usual **way,** i.e., he need not intend to sound it for the sake of the hearer. **Rabbi Yosei said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It was said in the case of a **representative of the community. But** in the case of **an** ordinary **individual,** the hearer **does not fulfill** his obligation **until** both **the hearer and the sounder have intent** to discharge the hearer's obligation, as argued by Rabbi Zeira.

In practical terms, this applies at the *Shabbat* table as well. A person making *Kiddush* for others who has not specifically thought of those present should at minimum express a blanket intent to discharge the obligation of anyone who hears and wishes to be *yotzei*.

### Two Levels of Intent

This raises a pointed question: can one simultaneously hold both *Rava's* position that *mitzvot einan tzerichot kavanah* and the requirement of *kavanah lehotzi*? The answer is that these are two categorically different questions. When one performs the *mitzvah* act oneself — taking the *lulav*, blowing the *shofar* — the question is whether the absence of conscious *mitzvah*-intent prevents the act from counting. *Rava* says it does not; the act itself is sufficient. But when one wishes to fulfill one's obligation through someone else's act, an entirely different problem arises: what makes that other person's act one's own? The blower is performing the act; the listener is not. For the blowing to be constituted as the listener's act, some connecting link must exist — and that link is the *kavanah lehotzi* of the blower, the conscious intent to extend that act toward the listener. Without it, the sounds produced are simply sounds; they do not become the listener's *mitzvah* act. These are thus two distinct layers of the *kavanah* question, and it is entirely coherent to hold both simultaneously.

As for the practical *halacha*: the *rishonim* and *poskim* debate the rule for *mitzvot tzerichot kavanah*. The general tendency is to require *kavanah* for *de'oraita* obligations, while for *derabanan* obligations there is more room to rule *bedi'avad* that they do not require it — though the matter remains subject to significant dispute. The requirement of *kavanah lehotzi*, by contrast, is applied across the board wherever one person seeks to discharge another's obligation.

The discussion of *kavanah lehotzi* also raises the question of which *mitzvot* are even susceptible to the mechanism of discharging another's obligation through one's own performance. The answer is that this is fundamentally limited to auditory *mitzvot* — *brachot*, *mikra megillah*, *tkias shofar* — governed by the principle of *shomea ke'oneh*, that hearing is as if one has spoken. Yet even within this category, nuance remains. It is relatively straightforward to understand why hearing words of *tefillah* or *brachot* and responding *amen* is as if one said them — one heard the words, assented to them, and is thus connected to them. It is somewhat more difficult to understand how hearing the sounds of the *shofar* is equivalent to having placed the ram's horn to one's lips. Similarly, the *mitzvah* of *krias megillah* involves not merely hearing words but reading them from a physical scroll — whether hearing them read constitutes reading them oneself is a question that generates debate among the *rishonim*. The common thread that unifies *shofar*, *megillah*, and *brachot* under *shomea ke'oneh* is their auditory character, even if the precise mechanism differs across the categories.

## Moshe's Hands & Serpent

### Hands of Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״וְהָיָה כַּאֲשֶׁר יָרִים מֹשֶׁה יָדוֹ וְגָבַר יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״, וְכִי יָדָיו שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה עוֹשׂוֹת מִלְחָמָה אוֹ שׁוֹבְרוֹת מִלְחָמָה? אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִסְתַּכְּלִין כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה וּמְשַׁעְבְּדִין אֶת לִבָּם לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם — הָיוּ מִתְגַּבְּרִים, וְאִם לָאו — הָיוּ נוֹפְלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Incidental to the discussion of the required intent when sounding the *shofar*, the mishna cites the verse: **"And it came to pass, when Moses held up his hand, that Israel prevailed;** and when he let down his hand, Amalek prevailed" (Exodus 17:11). It may be asked: **Did the hands of Moses make war** when he raised them **or break war** when he lowered them? **Rather,** the verse comes **to tell you** that **as long as the Jewish people turned their eyes upward and subjected their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they prevailed, but if not, they fell.**

The *perek* concludes with an *aggadeta* that grows directly from the preceding discussion of *kavanah*. The *Mishnah* cites the verse: *Vehaya ka'asher yarim Moshe yado vegavar Yisrael, vechaasher yaniach yado vegavar Amalek* — when *Moshe* raised his hand, *Yisrael* prevailed; when he lowered it, *Amalek* prevailed. The *Mishnah* immediately poses its rhetorical question: *vechi yadav shel Moshe osos milchamah o shores milchamah?* Did *Moshe's* hands make the war, or break the war? What kind of power is being ascribed here?

The answer: *ella lomar lecha* — to tell you that whenever *Yisrael* would look upward at the raised hands and would direct their hearts to their Father in Heaven — *meshabdin es libam la'avihem shebashamayim* — they would prevail; and when they would not, they would fall.

### Serpent on Pole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״עֲשֵׂה לְךָ שָׂרָף וְשִׂים אוֹתוֹ עַל נֵס וְהָיָה כׇּל הַנָּשׁוּךְ וְרָאָה אוֹתוֹ וָחָי״, וְכִי נָחָשׁ מֵמִית, אוֹ נָחָשׁ מְחַיֶּה? אֶלָּא: בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל מִסְתַּכְּלִין כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה וּמְשַׁעְבְּדִין אֶת לִבָּם לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם — הָיוּ מִתְרַפְּאִין, וְאִם לָאו הָיוּ נִימּוֹקִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, you can say:** The verse states: **"Make for yourself a fiery serpent, and set it upon a pole; and it shall come to pass, that everyone that is bitten, when he sees it, he shall live"** (Numbers 21:8). Once again it may be asked: **Did the serpent kill, or did the serpent preserve life? Rather, when the Jewish people turned their eyes upward and subjected their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they were healed, but if not, they rotted** from their snakebites.

The same interpretive move is applied to a second story: *aseh lecha saraf vesim oto al nes*, make a fiery serpent and place it upon a pole, and anyone who was bitten would look at it and live. Again the rhetorical question is posed: *vechi nachash meimis o nachash michayeh?* Can a serpent bring death, or can a serpent bring life? And again the same answer: when *Yisrael* would look upward and subjugate their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they would be healed; when they would not, they would waste away.

### Removing Magic

This *aggadeta* operates on two levels simultaneously. On one level, it is making a point about the religious significance of *kavanah* that goes well beyond its halachic parameters. The preceding discussion treated *kavanah* as a technical question of what is required for an act to count, and whether intent is needed to transgress *bal tosif*. Here, the *Mishnah* enlarges the picture: *kavanah* — understood as directing one's heart toward God — is what gives a physical act its religious meaning and its capacity to connect one to the divine. The act without that orientation is empty; the act with it becomes a conduit for relationship with *avihem shebashamayim*.

On a second level, the *Mishnah* is actively demystifying narratives that could be read very differently. Both stories — the hands of *Moshe* and the serpent on the pole — could easily be read as accounts of direct magical efficacy. One might conclude that certain physical acts or objects carry inherent power, and the Torah is cataloguing them. This reading would be deeply at odds with the Torah's fundamental opposition to magic, incantation, and necromancy. The *Mishnah* therefore reframes these stories entirely: the object or gesture was never the operative force. It was always the *kavanah* — the interior act of turning toward God — that was doing the work. Whether this reframing constitutes the *pshat* of the *pesukim* is a larger question, but the interpretive intent of the *Mishnah* is unmistakable.

## Bar Da'at Requirement

### Obligation Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן אֵין מוֹצִיאִין אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּיָּיב בַּדָּבָר — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to its halakhic discussion, the mishna continues: **A deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor** who sounds the *shofar* **cannot discharge the obligation on behalf of the community. This is the principle** with regard to similar matters: **Whoever is not obligated to** do **a** certain **matter cannot discharge the obligation on behalf of the community.**

The *Mishnah* now establishes a principle that was already implicit in the preceding discussion of *kavanah lehotzi*: *cheresh, shote, v'katan ein motzi'in es harabim yedei chovasan* — a deaf-mute, a *shote* (a person not anchored in rational reality, with a fuller definition reserved for discussion elsewhere), and a minor cannot discharge the public from its obligations. The governing principle is stated explicitly: *ze haklal — kol she'eino mechuyav badavar eino motzi es harabim yedei chovasan* — whoever is not themselves obligated in a matter cannot be the one through whom the community discharges its obligation.

### Cheresh, Shote, Katan

All three of these categories — *cheresh*, *shote*, and *katan* — share the halachic status of not being *bar da'as*, not being persons of legal understanding and capacity. It is worth noting that the category of *cheresh* carries a complex modern history: the advent of sign language and the demonstrated cognitive capacities of deaf individuals led to a significant reconsideration of this category in later halachic literature. But within the framework of the *Gemara*, these three are treated as formally outside the circle of halachic obligation, and one cannot discharge the obligation of the *tzibbur* through the act of someone who does not themselves bear that obligation.

## Who's Obligated Baraita

### General Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בִּתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר. כֹּהֲנִים וּלְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. גֵּרִים וַעֲבָדִים מְשׁוּחְרָרִים. וְטוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **All are obligated to sound the *shofar*: Priests, Levites, and** ordinary **Israelites; converts; freed slaves; a *tumtum*,** i.e., one whose sexual organs from birth are concealed or are so undeveloped that it is impossible to determine whether the individual is male or female; **a hermaphrodite [*androginos*],** i.e., one with both male and female reproductive organs; and a **half-slave, half-freeman.**

A *baraita* now enumerates those who are obligated in *tkias shofar*: *hakol chayavin bitkias shofar — Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim, geirim, va'avadim meshuchrarim* — all are obligated: *Kohanim*, *Levi'im*, *Yisraelim*, converts, and freed slaves. To this list are added *tumtum*, *androgynous*, and *mi shechetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin* — the *tumtum*, the *androgynous*, and the half-slave, half-free person — because in each of these cases there is some aspect of the individual that may carry the relevant obligation.

### Tumtum Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְטוּם — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא לֹא אֶת מִינוֹ, וְלֹא אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ. אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — מוֹצִיא אֶת מִינוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ. מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא לֹא אֶת מִינוֹ, וְלֹא אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **A *tumtum*** who sounds the *shofar* **cannot discharge** the obligation of **one of his kind,** i.e., a fellow *tumtum*, since men are bound by the obligation, whereas women are not, and it is possible that the sounder is female and the hearer is male, **nor** can he discharge the obligation **of one who is not of his kind,** an ordinary man or woman. **A hermaphrodite can discharge** the obligation of **one of his kind,** a fellow hermaphrodite, since if the sounder is treated as a female, the hearer is also considered a female, **but** he **cannot** discharge the obligation of **one who is not of his kind. One who is half-slave and half-freeman cannot discharge** the obligation of **one of his kind,** as the slave component of the sounder cannot discharge the obligation of the free component of the hearer, **and** he certainly **cannot** discharge the obligation of **one who is not of his kind,** i.e., a completely free individual.

The *baraita* states: *tumtum eino motzi lo es mino v'lo es she'eino mino* — a *tumtum* cannot discharge another *tumtum* and cannot discharge someone who is clearly male. A *tumtum* is a person whose genitalia are concealed, and the question of whether the individual is male or female is therefore empirically unknown. Given that women are not obligated in *tkias shofar*, and given that one *tumtum* blowing for another *tumtum* creates the possibility that a female is attempting to discharge a male, the discharge fails: the part of the *tkiah* generated by what may be a female cannot satisfy the obligation of what may be a male.

An *androgynous* — a person possessing both male and female primary anatomy — can discharge another *androgynous* but cannot discharge a clearly male person. The distinction here is conceptually important. A *tumtum's* status is an empirical unknown: every *tumtum* may be male or female, depending on what lies beneath, and this may differ from case to case. An *androgynous*, by contrast, presents a question of legal classification that applies uniformly to the entire category: all *androgynous* individuals share the same halachic status, whatever that status turns out to be. The three possibilities are that they are classified as male, as female, or as a distinct third category. Whichever classification is correct applies equally to all members of the category — and therefore one *androgynous* can discharge another, since they share the same classification. However, none of these three possibilities is equivalent to a fully male individual, and therefore an *androgynous* cannot discharge a clear male. It is worth noting that even here, the female exemption from *shofar* is functioning as a background premise throughout these discussions without being explicitly stated — a striking feature of how the *Mishnah* and *baraita* are structured.

### Half-Slave Status

*Mi shechetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin eino motzi lo es mino v'lo es she'eino mino* — the half-slave, half-free person cannot discharge another half-slave and cannot discharge a fully free male. The reasoning for the inability to discharge another half-slave is that the *tkiah* produced by a half-slave is itself of a compromised halachic character: the portion of the blow generated by the *eved* component of the individual cannot serve as a *tkias mitzvah* for the *ben chorin* component of the listener. Since the *tkiah* is not a complete and uncompromised *tkias mitzvah*, it cannot satisfy the full obligation of even another half-slave.

### Self-Discharge Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בִּתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר, כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! אִי הָנֵי לָא מִיחַיְּיבִי — מַאן מִיחַיְּיבִי?!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above in the *baraita*: **All are obligated to sound the *shofar*: Priests, Levites, and** ordinary **Israelites.** The Gemara asks in astonishment: Isn't that **obvious? If these** people **are not obligated** to perform the mitzva, **who** then **is obligated** to perform it?

*Rav Huna* initially rules that the half-slave, half-free person can at least discharge his own obligation — *le'atzmo motzi*. *Rav Nachman* challenges this: what distinguishes the inability to discharge another from the inability to discharge oneself? The same structural problem applies internally. *Lo ati tzad avdus didey u'mafki tzad cheirus didey* — the slave-component of his own *tkiah* cannot discharge the free-person component of himself. The *tkiah* is indivisible; one cannot say that only the part of the blow that comes from the free aspect of himself is the operative *tkiah*. The entire act is compromised. *Rav Nachman* therefore concludes: *af le'atzmo eino motzi* — even his own obligation he cannot discharge through his own blowing. A *baraita* is cited in confirmation: *mi shechetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin af le'atzmo eino motzi*.

## Blessings Discharge

### All Brachos Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי אַהֲבָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הַבְּרָכוֹת כּוּלָּן, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיָּצָא — מוֹצִיא. חוּץ מִבִּרְכַּת הַלֶּחֶם וּבִרְכַּת הַיַּיִן, שֶׁאִם לֹא יָצָא — מוֹצִיא, וְאִם יָצָא — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Continuing the discussion of performing an obligation on behalf of others, **Ahava, son of Rabbi Zeira, taught** the following *baraita*: With regard to **all the blessings, even if one** already recited a blessing for himself and has consequently **fulfilled** his own obligation, **he can** still recite a blessing for others and thereby **discharge** their obligation, as all Jews are responsible for one another. This is true with regard to all blessings **except for the blessing** recited **over bread and the blessing** recited **over wine,** both before and after their consumption. With regard to these blessings, **if he has not** yet **fulfilled** his own obligation, **he can discharge** the obligation of others as well, **but if he** already **fulfilled** his own obligation, **he cannot discharge** the obligation of others, as these blessings are recited in appreciation of physical enjoyment, and can only be recited by one who is actually deriving pleasure at the time.

A *baraita* is taught by Abba, son of *Rav Zeira*: *kol habrachos kulan, af al pi sheyatza motzi* — all *brachot*, even if one has already fulfilled the obligation oneself, one may repeat the *bracha* to discharge another's obligation. The principle that one who has already been *yotzei* can nonetheless be *motzi* others applies broadly to *brachot* as a category.

### Bnei Hanaa Exception

There is, however, an exception: *chutz mibirchas halechem u'birchas hayayin* — except for *birkat halechem* (*Hamotzi*) and *birkat hayayin* (*Borei Pri HaGafen*). These are *birchat hanehenin* — blessings of benefit, rooted in the fact that the one reciting them is about to derive physical benefit from food or drink. *V'im lo yatza motzi* — if one has not yet made the *bracha* for oneself, one can make it and thereby discharge another as well. But *v'im yatza, eino motzi* — if one has already said *Hamotzi* or *Borei Pri HaGafen* for oneself, one cannot say it again for someone else. A *birchat hanehenin* is not framed as a *mitzvah* one is performing on behalf of another; it is anchored in the speaker's own benefit. If that benefit is not a present reality for the speaker, the *bracha* has no valid footing.

### Integrated Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּרְכַּת הַלֶּחֶם שֶׁל מַצָּה, וּבִרְכַּת הַיַּיִן שֶׁל קִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּחוֹבָה הוּא — מַפֵּיק, אוֹ דִלְמָא: בְּרָכָה לָאו חוֹבָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the blessing over bread** that is recited before eating ***matza*** at the Passover seder **and the blessing over wine** recited as part **of the sanctification of the day** of Shabbat or a Festival, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara analyzes the question: Do we say that **since there is an obligation** to recite these blessings due to the mitzva involved, therefore **one can discharge** the obligation for others, even if he himself has already fulfilled his obligation? **Or perhaps** we say that **the blessing** itself **is not an obligation,** but rather the obligation lies in the eating and drinking, and the blessing is recited over one's physical enjoyment; therefore, if he already fulfilled his own obligation, he cannot recite the blessing for others, as he derives no pleasure at this time.

*Rava* raises a question about the intersection of these two categories: what of *birchat hanehenin* that has become incorporated into a *birchat hamitzvah* — specifically, *birchat halechem shel matzah* and *birchat hayayin shel kiddush hayom*? When one says *Hamotzi* on *matzah* at the *Pesach Seder*, or *Borei Pri HaGafen* as part of *Kiddush*, is this still a *birchat hanehenin* to which the restriction applies? Or since the obligation of *Kiddush* and the *mitzvah* of *matzah* are operative, has the *birchat hanehenin* been absorbed into the *birchat hamitzvah* frame, rendering it one capable of being repeated to discharge another even after one has already fulfilled it oneself?

The question can be sharpened further. *Kiddush* can theoretically be made without *Borei Pri HaGafen* — if one had already recited *Borei Pri HaGafen* on wine before *Shabbat* began, as sometimes occurs when *Purim* falls on *Erev Shabbat*, the *halacha* in *Pesachim* is *pores mapah umekadesh*, and *Kiddush* would begin from the second *bracha*, since the *birchat hanehenin* has already been covered. Similarly, in a theoretical case where one had already said *Hamotzi* on *matzah* that does not qualify for the *mitzvah*, the subsequent eating of *mitzvah*-*matzah* would require only *al achilas matzah*, not *Hamotzi* again, since the *hana'ah* dimension was already covered. This means that even within *Kiddush* and the *Seder*, the *Hamotzi* and *Borei Pri HaGafen* are not intrinsically part of the *mitzvah* obligation — they are present because one is about to benefit, and if that dimension is already covered, they drop out.

### Rav Ashi Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב פַּפֵּי, הֲוָה מְקַדֵּשׁ לַן. וְכִי הֲוָה אָתֵי אֲרִיסֵיהּ מִדַּבְרָא, הֲוָה מְקַדֵּשׁ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** an answer to this question from what **Rav Ashi said: When we were** studying **in the school of Rav Pappi, he would recite *kiddush* for us, and when his tenants would arrive from the field he would recite *kiddush*** once again **on their behalf.** Therefore, it is clear that one may recite *kiddush* on behalf of others, including the blessing that is recited over the wine, even if he himself has already fulfilled his own obligation.

*Rav Ashi* reports: *ki havainan bei Rav Pappa, hava mekadesh lan* — when we were in the house of *Rav Pappa*, he would make *Kiddush* for them. *Vechi hava ati ariseih midabra, hava mekadesh lei* — and when his sharecropper came in from the field, he would make *Kiddush* for him as well, including *Borei Pri HaGafen*, without distinguishing between the two instances. Since *Rav Pappa* did not skip the *Borei Pri HaGafen* even when reciting *Kiddush* for the sharecropper after having already made *Kiddush* for himself — and since the sharecropper was presumably also drinking the wine and needed *Borei Pri HaGafen* — this serves as a practical ruling that the *Borei Pri HaGafen* embedded in *Kiddush* can be recited to discharge another's obligation even after one has already fulfilled one's own. The *birchat hanehenin* in that context is treated as having been absorbed into the *birchat hamitzvah*.

This ruling has interesting practical ramifications. The question of what happens when one is serving as *mesader kiddushin* before *birchat eirusin* — where the custom is to say *Borei Pri HaGafen* on wine, though the *Gemara* never makes this a formal requirement and the *halacha* is that it can be done without wine — is one that careful *mesadrei kiddushin* are alert to. Since in that context the *Borei Pri HaGafen* was never packaged into a formal *birchat hamitzvah*, those making the *bracha* without personally drinking face a *birchat hanehenin* without personal *hana'ah*. One solution practiced by those aware of this concern is to place a small amount of wine on one's fingers and taste it after handing the cup to the groom.

Separately, it should be noted that *Borei Pri HaGafen* on *Shabbat* morning operates differently from *Borei Pri HaGafen* on *Friday night*. On *Shabbat* morning, the *Kiddush* is essentially the practice of beginning one's meal with wine, without the full *mitzvah* character of *Friday night Kiddush*. Therefore, the *Borei Pri HaGafen* said on *Shabbat* morning is truly a straight *birchat hanehenin*, and one who has already said it cannot repeat it for someone else. If one made *Kiddush* at *shul* and subsequently returns home, one makes a new *Borei Pri HaGafen* not because one is repeating *Kiddush* in some enriched sense but simply because one is now drinking wine anew in a different setting, and a new *birchat hanehenin* is required.

### Chinuch Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לָא יִפְרוֹס אָדָם פְּרוּסָה לָאוֹרְחִין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן אוֹכֵל עִמָּהֶם, אֲבָל פּוֹרֵס הוּא לְבָנָיו וְלִבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ כְּדֵי לְחַנְּכָן בְּמִצְווֹת. וּבְהַלֵּל וּבַמְּגִילָּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיָּצָא — מוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One should not break bread** and recite a blessing **for guests unless he is eating with them,** so that he is obligated to recite a blessing for himself. **But he may break** bread **for his children and for** the other **members of his household** and recite the blessing, **in order to educate them to** perform **the mitzvot,** so that they know how to recite a blessing. **And with regard to *hallel* and the Scroll** of Esther, the *halakha* is that **even if he** already **fulfilled** his obligation, **he can** still **discharge** the obligation of others.

The *baraita* continues: *lo yifros adam prusah le'orchin ela im kein ochel imahem* — one should not break bread for guests unless one is eating with them. This states the basic rule for *Hamotzi* as a *birchat hanehenin*: without personal benefit, one may not say it for others. *Aval pores hu levanav ulevnei beiso kedei lechancham bemitzvot* — but one may break bread for one's children and household members in order to train them in *mitzvot*. For the sake of *chinuch*, one may say a full *bracha* — even a *birchat hanehenin* — even without personally fulfilling the obligation or without the personal *hana'ah* that would normally be required. This principle has important practical application: when training children to recite *brachot* or perform *mitzvot*, one may recite the complete *bracha* together with or for them, rather than substituting an imperfect formulation.

### Hallel & Megillah

Finally: *u've'hallelu u've'megilla af al pi sheyatza motzi* — for *Hallel* and *megillah*, even if one has already fulfilled one's own obligation, one may repeat the performance and the associated *brachot* to discharge another person's obligation. In practice, when helping someone fulfill the *mitzvah* of *megillah*, we would ideally have the person recite the *brachot* themselves and have the reader read the *megillah* on their behalf. But if they are unable, the reader may make the *brachot* again even after having already fulfilled his own *mitzvah*.

## Rosh Hashanah & Shabbat

### Mishnah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹם טוֹב שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת — בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ הָיוּ תּוֹקְעִין, אֲבָל לֹא בַּמְּדִינָה. מִשֶּׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, הִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי שֶׁיְּהוּ תּוֹקְעִין בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ בֵּית דִּין. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא הִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי אֶלָּא בְּיַבְנֶה בִּלְבַד. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֶחָד יַבְנֶה, וְאֶחָד כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the **Festival day of Rosh HaShana that occurs on Shabbat, in the Temple they would sound** the *shofar* as usual. **However,** they would **not** sound it **in the** rest of the **country** outside the Temple. **After the Temple was destroyed, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted that** the people **should sound** the *shofar* on Shabbat **in every place where there is a court** of twenty-three judges. **Rabbi Elazar said: Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted** this practice **only in Yavne,** where the Great Sanhedrin of seventy-one judges resided in his time, but nowhere else. **They said to him:** He instituted the practice **both** in **Yavne and** in **any place where there is a court.**

The fourth and final *perek* of *Rosh Hashanah* opens: *Yom Tov shel Rosh Hashanah she'chal lihyot b'Shabbos* — the *Yom Tov* of *Rosh Hashanah* that falls on *Shabbat*. One might ask why the *Mishnah* adds *Yom Tov shel* — why not simply say *Rosh Hashanah she'chal lihyot b'Shabbos*? For *Sukkot* and *Pesach*, the qualifier *Yom Tov* distinguishes the *Yom Tov* day from *Chol HaMoed*; but *Rosh Hashanah* has no *Chol HaMoed*, so the phrase seems redundant. It may simply be a stylistic parallel to the formula used for the other *yamim tovim*. But it is also possible that it alludes back to the opening *Mishnah* of the *masechet* — *arba'ah rashei shanim hem*, there are four *rosh hashanahs* — and signals that we are now speaking specifically of the *Yom Tov shel Rosh Hashanah*, not of the other three.

This is an appropriate moment to note how strikingly the *masechet* has, in its structure, downplayed the centrality of *Rosh Hashanah* as *Yom Tov*. The opening *Mishnah* treats *Rosh Hashanah* as one of four calendrical markers. The second *Mishnah* mentions it as one of four times the world is judged — *arba'ah perakim bashana ha'olam nidon* — without evoking its *Yom Tov* character. *Kiddush HaChodesh* then dominates the first *perek*, extends through the second, and bleeds into the third. Even the third *perek*, when it finally addresses *Rosh Hashanah* practices, does so without naming *Rosh Hashanah* explicitly — it simply says *kol hashofarot kesherim*. The Torah itself never calls this day *Rosh Hashanah*; and one might have expected *Chazal* in this *masechet* specifically to heighten its identity as *Yom HaDin*. Instead, this *Mishnah* is effectively the first place in the entire *masechet* where *Rosh Hashanah* is addressed squarely as a *Yom Tov*.

### Temple vs. Outside

The *Mishnah* states: *ba'Mikdash hayu tokin, aval lo ba'medina* — in the *Mikdash* they would blow the *shofar* on *Shabbat*, but not outside. What "outside" means requires some interpretation: it could mean outside the *Mikdash* compound within *Yerushalayim*, or it could mean outside *Yerushalayim* entirely. Given that the *Mishnah* later indicates that *Yerushalayim* had a wider radius for permissible blowing than *Yavneh*, the more likely reading is that *ba'medina* refers to places outside of *Yerushalayim*.

### Rabban Yochanan's Edict

After the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*: *hiskin Rabban Yochanan ben Zakai yihyu tokin b'chol makom sheyesh bo Beis Din* — *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakai* enacted that they would blow wherever there was a *Beis Din*. It is worth noting that the *Mishnah* does not frame this as *zecher laMikdash*, a commemoration of *Mikdash* practice. The *Mishnah* simply states that the practice was continued in the presence of a *Beis Din*, without characterizing it as memorial. Whether that framing is implied will be revisited in the *Gemara*.

### Rabbi Elazar Dispute

*Rabbi Elazar* disputes the breadth of *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakai's* enactment: *lo hiskin Rabban Yochanan ben Zakai ela be'Yavne bilvad* — the enactment applied only to *Yavneh*, the central *Beis Din* and the seat of Jewish leadership after the *Churban*, not to every *Beis Din* everywhere. The *Chachamim* respond: *echad Yavne v'echad kol makom sheyesh bo Beis Din* — whether *Yavneh* or any place with a *Beis Din*, the enactment applies equally.

### Jerusalem Advantage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד זֹאת הָיְתָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם יְתֵירָה עַל יַבְנֶה, שֶׁכׇּל עִיר שֶׁהִיא רוֹאָה, וְשׁוֹמַעַת, וּקְרוֹבָה, וִיכוֹלָה לָבוֹא — תּוֹקְעִין. וּבְיַבְנֶה לֹא הָיוּ תּוֹקְעִין אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna adds: **And Jerusalem** in earlier times **had this additional superiority over Yavne** after Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted this practice, **for** in **any city whose** residents **could see** Jerusalem **and hear** the sounding of the *shofar* from there, **and** which **was near** to Jerusalem **and** people **could come** to Jerusalem from there, **they** would **sound** the *shofar* there as well, as it was considered part of Jerusalem. **But in Yavne they would sound** the *shofar* **only in the court** itself, not in the surrounding cities.

Beyond the dispute between *Rabbi Elazar* and the *Chachamim*, the *Mishnah* adds: *v'od zos hayisa Yerushalayim yesira al Yavne* — *Yerushalayim* had an additional advantage over *Yavneh*. Any city in the environs of *Yerushalayim* — one that could see *Yerushalayim*, could hear its *shofar*, and was close enough that its inhabitants could come — they too would blow on *Shabbat*. In *Yavneh*, by contrast, blowing was restricted to those immediately present at the *Beis Din* itself. *Yerushalayim's* gravitational pull thus extended the permissibility of *Shabbat* blowing outward to its surrounding towns in a way that *Yavneh's* authority did not.

## Shofar on Shabbat Problem

### Verses Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי בַּר לַחְמָא אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״שַׁבָּתוֹן זִכְרוֹן תְּרוּעָה״. וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״יוֹם תְּרוּעָה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּשַּׁבָּת, כָּאן — בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where** are **these matters;** from where is it derived that the *shofar* is not sounded on Shabbat? **Rabbi Levi bar Laḥma said** that **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina said: One verse says,** with regard to Rosh HaShana: **"A solemn rest, a memorial of blasts"** (Leviticus 23:24), which indicates that one should merely remember the *shofar* without actually sounding it. **And another verse says: "It is a day of blowing for you"** (Numbers 29:1), i.e., a day on which one must actually sound the *shofar*. This apparent contradiction is **not difficult: Here,** the verse in which the *shofar* is only being remembered but not sounded, is referring **to a Festival that occurs on Shabbat; there,** the verse in which the *shofar* is actually sounded, is referring **to a Festival that occurs on a weekday.**

The *Gemara* now asks the foundational question: *minan hanei milei* — what is the source for the idea that blowing *shofar* on *Shabbat* is problematic at all? *Levi bar Lachma* citing *Rabbi Chama bar Chanina* offers the following: one verse says *shabbason zichron teruah* — a day of rest, a *remembrance* of blasting — implying that on this day there is only a memorial of the blasts, not actual blasting. Another verse says *yom teruah yihyeh lakhem* — it shall be for you a day of actual blasts. *Lo kashya* — there is no contradiction: the verse referring to remembrance applies when *Rosh Hashanah* falls on *Shabbat*, while the verse of actual blasting applies when *Rosh Hashanah* falls on a weekday. The two verses thus encode a biblical distinction between *Rosh Hashanah* on *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashanah* during the week.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא, בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ הֵיכִי תָּקְעִינַן? וְעוֹד: הָא לָאו מְלָאכָה הִיא, דְּאִצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This explanation is difficult, for **if** the distinction between Shabbat and the rest of the week applies **by Torah law, how does one sound** the *shofar* on Shabbat **in the Temple?** If it is prohibited to sound the *shofar* on Shabbat, it should be prohibited everywhere. **And furthermore,** there is an additional problem with this explanation: Although the Sages prohibited sounding a *shofar* and playing other musical instruments on Shabbat, by Torah law sounding a *shofar* **is not a prohibited labor** on Shabbat such **that a verse is necessary to exclude** it when Rosh HaShana occurs on Shabbat.

*Rava* challenges this directly: *im mid'Oraisa* — if the prohibition is biblical, *hi b'Mikdash heichi takinan* — how could they blow in the *Mikdash* at all? If the Torah itself prohibits blowing on *Shabbat*, no rabbinic or practical consideration can override it in any venue, not even the *Mikdash*. Furthermore, *halav melakha hi?* — and in any case, is blowing *shofar* even a *melakha*? *D'tana d'vei Shmuel*: the school of *Shmuel* teaches from the verse *kol melekhes avoda lo ta'asu* that blowing the *shofar* (*tkias shofar*) and removing baked bread from the walls of an oven (*ridyat hapas*) are excluded from the category of prohibited *melakha* — they are highly skilled acts (*chokhma*) but not *melakha* in the halachic sense. If blowing *shofar* is not *melakha*, there would be no biblical basis for its prohibition on *Shabbat* to begin with — and therefore no need for a verse to exclude it.

### Carrying Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי שְׁמוּאֵל: ״כׇּל מְלֶאכֶת עֲבוֹדָה לֹא תַּעֲשׂוּ״, יָצְתָה תְּקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר וּרְדִיַּית הַפַּת, שֶׁהִיא חָכְמָה וְאֵינָהּ מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof for this last claim: **As** a Sage **of the school of Shmuel taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to the verse that prohibits performing prohibited labor on Festivals: **"Any prohibited labor of work you shall not perform"** (Numbers 29:1). This comes **to exclude** from the category of prohibited labors **the sounding of the *shofar* and the removal of bread** from the oven, each of **which is a skill and not a labor,** and therefore they are not included in the category of prohibited labor. Apparently, sounding the *shofar* is not prohibited by Torah law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מִישְׁרֵא שְׁרֵי, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּגְזוּר בֵּיהּ כִּדְרַבָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בִּתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר, וְאֵין הַכֹּל בְּקִיאִין בִּתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִטְּלֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ וְיֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל הַבָּקִי לִלְמוֹד, וְיַעֲבִירֶנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: By Torah law one is permitted** to sound the *shofar* on Rosh HaShana even on Shabbat, **and it was the Sages who decreed** that **it** is prohibited. This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba, as Rabba said: All are obligated to sound the *shofar*** on Rosh HaShana, **but not all are experts in sounding the *shofar*.** Therefore, the Sages instituted **a decree** that the *shofar* should not be sounded on Shabbat, **lest one take** the *shofar* **in his hand and go to an expert to learn** how to sound it or to have him sound it for him, **and** due to his preoccupation **he** might **carry it four cubits in the public domain,** which is a desecration of Shabbat.

*Rava* therefore offers a different resolution: *mid'Oraisa mishra sharei* — biblically, blowing *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah Shabbat* is entirely permitted. The prohibition is *derabbanan*. *Rabba* explains the rabbinic reasoning: *hakol chayavin bitkias shofar v'ein hakol beki'im bitkias shofar* — everyone is obligated in *tkias shofar*, but not everyone knows how to blow it. *V'im yitlenu b'yado v'yeilekh etzel baki lilmod, v'ya'avirenu arba amos b'reshus harabim* — one would take the *shofar* in hand and carry it to an expert to learn how to blow it, and in doing so would carry it four *amot* in a *reshus harabim*, violating *Shabbat*. To prevent this from happening, the Rabbis decreed that *shofar* is not blown on *Shabbat* — a protective *gezeirah* against the likely transgression of carrying.

### Lulav & Megillah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְלוּלָב, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִמְגִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this is** also **the reason for** the rabbinical decree that **the palm branch** [***lulav***] may not be taken on Shabbat, **and this is** likewise **the reason for** the decree that **the Megilla** of Esther may not be read on Shabbat. The Sages were concerned that one might carry the *lulav* or the Megilla four cubits in the public domain to take it to an expert who will teach him the proper manner to perform these mitzvot.

*Rabba* extends this same rationale to account for two other *mitzvot* that are similarly not performed on *Shabbat*: *v'hainu ta'ama d'lulav v'hainu ta'ama d'megilla* — the same concern underlies the rabbinic prohibition against taking the *lulav* and reading the *megillah* on *Shabbat*. In each case, the worry is that people will carry the object — *lulav*, *megillah*, *shofar* — to an expert, and the fear of that *Shabbat* violation leads the Rabbis to prohibit the *mitzvah* performance entirely on *Shabbat*.

### Mikdash Exemption

The rabbinic protective decree does not apply in the *Beis HaMikdash*. This is a broader principle: the Rabbis did not extend their protective *gezerot* into the *Mikdash* context. Therefore, the *Mikdash* continued to blow even on *Shabbat*, not because of a biblical privilege but because the rabbinic *gezeirah* was inapplicable there.

### Yerushalmi View

The *Yerushalmi* takes a different approach. It does not reject the biblical reading offered at the outset of the *Gemara's* discussion. Rather, the *Yerushalmi* appears to maintain that there genuinely is a biblical distinction between *Rosh Hashanah* on *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashanah* on a weekday — that the two verses do in fact encode a real biblical differentiation — and it engages directly with that distinction rather than resolving it away in favor of a purely rabbinic explanation.
