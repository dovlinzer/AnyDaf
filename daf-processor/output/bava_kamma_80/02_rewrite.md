# Bava Kamma 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Takkanot: Animals & Traps

### Small Animals in EY

The daf opens at the bottom of 79b with a *mishna* that concludes *Perek Meruba*, capping off an extended discussion of damages and theft with a set of *takkanot* — rabbinic enactments designed to protect the public's property and to preserve the quality of the land. The *mishna* states: *ein megadlin behema dakka be-Eretz Yisrael* — one may not raise *behema dakka* (small domesticated animals, specifically sheep and goats) in Eretz Yisrael. The concern is straightforward: sheep and goats are grazing animals that can devastate vegetation, and in a land like Eretz Yisrael, where arable ground is precious and not abundant, their destructive potential is especially acute. The prohibition is therefore categorical within Eretz Yisrael itself, though it does not extend everywhere: one may raise small animals in Syria (*Suriya*) — an area that in the *mishna*'s period had significant Jewish settlement — and in the *midbarot* of Eretz Yisrael, which refers not to desert wastelands but to the relatively sparse, semi-arid areas such as the Judean hills, where vegetation grows only seasonally and the damage from grazing is correspondingly limited. Anyone who has driven through the Judean hills and seen Bedouin shepherds moving their flocks across the hillsides knows exactly the terrain the *mishna* envisions.

### Chickens & Tumah

The *mishna* continues with a second prohibition: *ein megadlin tarnegolin be-Yerushalayim mipnei ha-kodashim* — one may not raise chickens in Jerusalem on account of the *kodashim*. Chickens peck indiscriminately through garbage and refuse, and in a city saturated with sacrificial meat and other consecrated foods, they present an unacceptable risk of rendering *kodashim tamei*. The prohibition extends further: *kohanim* throughout all of Eretz Yisrael may not raise chickens, because *kohanim* outside Jerusalem regularly handle *terumah*, which must be kept *tahor*, and chickens roaming through refuse will inevitably introduce *tumah* into those precincts.

### Pigs Prohibited

The *mishna* then states: *ein megadlin chazirin be-chol makom* — one may not raise pigs anywhere. Notably, this prohibition is not geographically limited to Eretz Yisrael; it applies universally. The *gemara* will explain the specific reasoning, and it is worth observing that the categorical nature of the enactment — encompassing all locations — distinguishes it from the *behema dakka* prohibition, which is rooted in concern for the land.

### Dogs Must Be Chained

The *mishna* adds: *lo yegadel adam et ha-kelev ela im ken haya kashur be-shalshelet* — one may not raise a dog unless it is chained. An earlier *mishna* in the tractate addressed related concerns, and here too the danger is twofold: a dog on the loose may bite and injure someone, and *Rashi* notes as well that its barking can frighten people — a concern with specific application to pregnant women, where sudden fright can cause a miscarriage.

### Pigeon Traps Distance

Finally, the *mishna* prohibits setting traps for pigeons (*nishovin le-yonim*) within thirty *ris* of any inhabited area. Thirty *ris*, according to *Rashi*, equals four *mil* — roughly four kilometers. The rationale is that within that radius one is liable to inadvertently trap domesticated pigeons belonging to others. Wild pigeons are fair game, but at close range the risk of capturing someone else's birds is too great, and so the traps must be placed beyond that distance.

## Small vs Large Animals

### Behema Daka/Gassa

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita* that both echoes and refines the *mishna*. It reiterates the prohibition on raising *behema dakka* in Eretz Yisrael but permits doing so *be-chorshin she-be-Eretz Yisrael* — in the forests of Eretz Yisrael. (Here a brief clarification is warranted: in the *mishna*, the term *midbarot* refers to these semi-arid open areas, not to forests; the *baraita* uses the term *chorshin* — forests — as a parallel permitted zone. The lecturer acknowledges having inadvertently conflated the two earlier.) In Syria, the *baraita* continues, the prohibition does not apply even in settled areas — *be-Suriya afilu be-yishuv* — since the level of concern for the Syrian landscape does not rise to the level that prompted the Eretz Yisrael enactment. And it goes without saying — *ve-ein tzarich lomar be-chutz la-aretz* — that in the Diaspora generally, where not even *Suriya*'s quasi-sanctity applies, there is no restriction at all.

A second *baraita* echoes the same framework, specifying the Judean *midbar* and the *midbar* near the border of *Akko* as examples of the permitted zones within Eretz Yisrael itself.

Crucially, this *baraita* also draws a fundamental distinction: even those who prohibited *behema dakka* explicitly permitted *behema gassa* — large animals such as cattle, donkeys, and horses. The question is why no such prohibition was extended to large animals.

### Import Rationale

The *baraita* supplies its own explanation: *ein gozrin gezeirah al ha-tzibur ela im ken rov ha-tzibur yecholim la'amod bah* — a rabbinic decree is not issued against the community unless the majority of the community is capable of withstanding it. In the case of small animals, the decree is sustainable: *behema dakka efshar le-havi mi-chutz la-aretz* — sheep and goats can be imported, so the wool, milk, cheese, and meat that one might seek from them can be obtained without raising them domestically. But large animals — cows for plowing, horses for riding, donkeys and mules for labor — cannot realistically be prohibited: *behema gassa ee efshar le-havi mi-chutz la-aretz*. These animals are essential for agriculture and transport in ways that cannot simply be outsourced through importation, and a decree the community cannot bear is not a valid decree.

### Yom Tov Purchase

The *baraita* then carves out a narrow leniency within the prohibition on *behema dakka*: one is permitted to purchase small animals and keep them for up to thirty days before *Yom Tov* or before a son's wedding feast. The reasoning is that *Yom Tov* was the occasion on which people would typically eat meat — perhaps a lamb or a kid — and requiring someone to purchase the animal only on *Yom Tov* eve would be unreasonable. A thirty-day window is therefore permitted, without this being classified as "raising" the animal in the prohibited sense.

### Thirty-Day Limit

The *baraita* adds an important qualification: *u-vilvad she-lo yisheh ha-achronah she-bahem shloshim yom* — one may not allow the last of the animals to remain for thirty days. The *gemara* clarifies what this means. One might have thought: if I purchased an animal shortly before *Yom Tov*, and *Yom Tov* passes, I still have days remaining in my thirty-day window — perhaps I may continue to hold it afterward. The *gemara* rejects this: *kivan de-nafak lei regel lo miba'ei lei le-shahahei* — once *Yom Tov* is over, the justification for holding the animal evaporates. The thirty-day window is not a free-standing license; it exists specifically in relation to the upcoming occasion, and once that occasion has passed, continued possession is prohibited.

The *gemara* extends this leniency to a butcher (*tabbach*), who may purchase and slaughter immediately, or purchase and hold the animal briefly, again subject to the same thirty-day outer limit before the relevant event. *Rashi* understands the phrase *aguna* here — the "last" animal remaining — as referring to thirty days before the market (*shuk*), while *Tosafot* understands it more simply as a thirty-day ceiling regardless of market day, meaning that once all but the last animal have been sold or slaughtered, the remaining one must also be disposed of within thirty days.

### Rabban Gamliel Ruling

A related episode involves Rabban Gamliel's students, who asked him: *mahu la'asot* — what may one do regarding keeping small animals? He replied: it is permitted. The students seemed surprised — had the *mishna* not prohibited raising *behema dakka*? The *gemara* clarifies that the students were asking a more refined question: is it permissible not to raise them in the full sense, but simply to purchase them and keep them on hand temporarily? Rabban Gamliel confirmed this is permitted — with one critical condition: *u-vilvad shelo tetze ve-tireh ba-eder* — as long as the animal is not taken out to graze. One may feed it indoors, but it may not be brought out to pasture. Rabban Gamliel's own vivid formulation: *ela kosherah be-kar'ei ha-mitah* — you tie it to the legs of the bed. *Tosafot* notes this was the common practical method of keeping an animal indoors without grazing, and that the essential rule is the prohibition on grazing, not a literal requirement of the bed-tying arrangement. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* actually differ in their framing of Rabban Gamliel's ruling: according to *Tosafot*, Rabban Gamliel is simply clarifying the terms of the existing *Yom Tov* leniency — you may hold it for thirty days, but not graze it. According to *Rashi*, Rabban Gamliel is being more expansive, suggesting that even absent a special occasion one may keep a small animal indefinitely, as long as it is not grazed.

## Chasid & Goat Story

### Fresh Milk Remedy

The *gemara* now presents a vivid narrative that throws these rulings into relief. A certain righteous person (*chasid echad*) fell ill with a heart condition and consulted physicians, who prescribed a striking remedy: he must drink boiling-fresh milk — *chalav rotei'ach* — from morning to morning, meaning either directly from the animal or expressed and consumed immediately, while still at the animal's body temperature. A goat was brought for him and tied to the legs of his bed — precisely the arrangement Rabban Gamliel had described.

### Visitors' Recoil

When friends came to visit, they saw the goat tied at his bedside and recoiled. *Chazru le-achoreihem* — they stepped back — and said: *listim mezuyan be-veito shel zeh* — there is an armed bandit in this man's house. The imagery is pointed: the *gemara* in *Meruba* treats these grazing animals as metaphorical armed robbers ravaging the landscape. How could this righteous man harbor such a creature? They hesitated to enter.

Yet when they investigated his life, *lo matzu bo ela oto avon shel otah ez* — they found no transgression other than this goat. And on his deathbed, the *chasid* himself acknowledged: *yodei'a ani she-ein bi avon ela avon otah ez she-avarti al divrei chaveirai* — I know I have no sin except for this goat, through which I went against the words of my companions. The *gemara* does not spell out the halachic status of his action, and one might wonder whether he was in genuine medical need and could have invoked considerations akin to *pikuach nefesh*. But the narrative seems to operate on the level of *midat chasidut* — the standard of piety to which a *chasid* holds himself — and against that standard, even this fell short. The label *chasid echad* itself signals that for someone at that level of personal conduct, this was a meaningful failing.

## Rabbi Yishmael Story

### Wealth Destroyed

Rabbi Yishmael offers a complementary narrative drawn from his own family. His father's household (*beit aba*), he reports, were among the wealthy *ba'alei batim* of the Upper Galilee. And what caused their ruin? Two practices: *she-hayu morin ba-chorshin* — that they would graze their animals in the forests, and *she-danim dinei mamonot be-yachid* — that they would adjudicate monetary disputes as a single judge. The second infraction is a departure from proper procedure, even if technically defensible in certain circumstances. The first seems surprising at first glance: grazing in forests, as we have seen, is explicitly permitted.

### Field Damage

The resolution follows immediately. Even though they had forests near their homes — and grazing in those forests was indeed permitted — *sadeh ketana hayta ma'avirin derech aleha* — there was a small field between the forest and the road, and the animals would cross through it. Whether the goats and sheep were grazing in that small field or merely trampling through it without permission, they were causing agricultural damage to another's property. This failure to be sufficiently careful about crossing through private land — even a small one, even a path — was the transgression that brought about the ruin of his father's house. The lesson is one of punctiliousness: permission to graze in forests does not license casual incursion onto neighboring fields.

## Repentance & Exceptions

### Shepherd Teshuva

The *gemara* shifts to explore leniencies extended to those in difficult transitional situations. A shepherd who does *teshuva* — presumably one who has been grazing animals on others' lands and now wishes to make amends — is not required to liquidate everything immediately and repay all at once. Rather, *mocher al yad al yad* — he sells gradually, in stages. The reasoning is practical and humane: forcing a desperate seller onto the market all at once both depresses the price he receives and risks leaving him destitute. The approach parallels modern bankruptcy restructuring — creditors are repaid over time in a manner that neither ruins the debtor nor cheats those owed restitution.

### Ger Inherits Animals

Similarly, a *ger* who inherits dogs or pigs from his non-Jewish father finds himself in possession of animals whose continued ownership is prohibited. Here the situation is even more sympathetic: he did nothing wrong. Nevertheless, he is not compelled to liquidate immediately. *Ein mechayvin oto limkor miyad — ela mocher al yad al yad* — he is permitted to sell gradually so that he can obtain fair market value and organize his finances without being driven to ruin.

### Vow Timing

A parallel leniency applies to one who made a *neder* to purchase a house or marry a woman in Eretz Yisrael. Perhaps the language of the *neder* implies some urgency. Nevertheless, *ein mechayvin oto likach miyad* — one does not compel immediate fulfillment. The person may wait *ad she-yimtza ha-hogenet lo* — until he finds the house that is genuinely right for him, or the woman who is genuinely suited to him. One is not required to take the first available option simply because the *neder* is pending.

### Woman's Vow

A story illustrates just how far this principle extends. A certain woman, under domestic pressure — apparently a widow or divorcée struggling to care for a young child — declared in desperation: *kol mi she-yavo eini machzirato* — whoever comes and proposes to me, I will not refuse. One might think such a sweeping declaration obligates her to accept any suitor whatsoever. Indeed, *u-kaftzu aleha bnei adam she-einam mehuganim* — unsuitable men immediately rushed to propose. When the matter came before the sages, they ruled: *lo nitkavna zo ela le-hagon lah* — her intent was clearly restricted to an appropriate match. Even in a case where a woman explicitly declared she would refuse no one, the sages interpreted her vow as conditionally restricted to suitable proposals. This reinforces the principle: if even an explicit, seemingly unconditional declaration is interpreted with reference to appropriateness, certainly a *neder* to buy a house or marry in Eretz Yisrael requires no more than waiting for a proper opportunity.

## Wild/Domestic Animals

### Permitted Animals

The *gemara* introduces a *baraita* from Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar that carves out explicit exceptions to the restrictions on small animals. He permits keeping *klavim kufrin* (village dogs), cats (*chatulim*), monkeys (*kofin*), and *chuldot sna'im* — a specific type of small creature — because *she-asuyim le-naker et ha-bayit*: these animals serve the household function of clearing out vermin, rodents, and pests. Since they perform a genuine domestic service, they are permitted despite the general prohibition on small animals.

### Genets

The *chuldot sna'im* require some identification. The Koren *gemara* identifies the creature as the common genet — a small animal biologically similar to a mongoose, roughly the size of a cat, with a long tail and very short legs, typically dwelling in forested mountain terrain. Its flexibility allows it to climb trees and rocks with ease, and it preys on small birds and rodents. Because of its great dexterity in catching mice, it was commonly kept in households as a form of pest control. This identification illuminates why Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar groups it with cats and dogs as a permitted household animal.

### Khuldot Sna'im

The *gemara* asks: *mai chuldot sna'im* — precisely what is this creature? Rav Yehuda says: *shartza khartza* — that is its name, where *khartza* may refer to a species of thorn bush or a related habitat marker. An alternate formulation gives the name as *shartza kharza*. The *gemara* then offers a description: *de-ktinei shakei* — it has small or short legs, and *ve-ra'ei beini vardinei* — it grazes among thorn bushes. A further distinction is then drawn: this creature differs from an ordinary *shartza* in the shape of its legs. The ordinary *shartza* has broad, flat thighs such that it appears to slither, which is what classifies it as a *sheretz* in the more technical sense. The *chuldot sna'im*, by contrast, has thinner legs and moves more like a standard animal, giving it a different character and removing it from the *sheretz* classification. Whatever the precise zoological identification, the functional point is clear: these are useful household pest-controllers, and as such are permitted.

## Babylonia Enactment

### Babylonia Status

The *gemara* records a striking statement: *Rav Yehuda amar Rav: asinu atzmenu be-Vavel ke-Eretz Yisrael le-vehema dakka* — we have treated ourselves in Babylonia as if we were in Eretz Yisrael with regard to small animals. The sages of Babylonia extended the prohibition on *behema dakka* to apply in Bavel as well, even though Bavel was not Eretz Yisrael, held no equivalent *kedusha*, and was home to the majority of Jews in the Talmudic period. The question of what *asinu atzmenu* means is worth pausing over: does it signal a new enactment made at a specific historical moment, or a recognition that this was always the correct status of Bavel? In the parallel discussion in *Gittin* regarding a different matter, the framing suggests the latter — that Bavel's status is claimed to be equivalent to Eretz Yisrael even from the time of the *mishna*. Here, however, the plain reading suggests a new decision.

### Rav Huna Curse

The application of this enactment was not without friction. Rav Adda bar Ahava confronted Rav Huna directly: *didach mai* — what is your situation? You have sheep and goats in your possession — how can you permit yourself that which has been prohibited? Rav Huna replied: *didan ka mantera lehu Chova* — my wife Chova watches over them, implying she maintains supervision to ensure they do not go out to graze improperly. Rav Adda bar Ahava responded harshly: *Chova tikberinhu le-vnah* — may Chova bury her children. This was a devastating curse. *Tosafot* attempts to soften it slightly, reading the statement as a practical warning — if Chova is spending all her time watching the sheep, who is watching the children? But regardless of the gloss, the words carried halachic and spiritual weight. The *gemara* reports: *kula shanei de-Rav Adda bar Ahava lo ikayam zara le-Rav Huna me-Chova* — for all the years that Rav Adda bar Ahava remained alive after uttering those words, not one child of Rav Huna born from Chova survived. This is a devastating coda, and the *gemara* records it not so much to adjudicate the underlying halachic dispute as to underscore the gravity of careless speech — even speech that is arguably warranted.

An alternative version (*ika de-amrei*) attributes the original statement — *asinu atzmenu be-Vavel ke-Eretz Yisrael* — to Rav Huna himself, citing it in the name of Rav, and adds: *mi-ki ati Rav le-Vavel* — from when Rav came to Babylonia, that is when we began treating ourselves in this regard as Eretz Yisrael. On this reading, it was Rav's arrival in Bavel that initiated the change, and Rav Huna was transmitting Rav's ruling — while apparently failing to observe it himself.

## Cat Incident & Ruling

### Hierarchy at Bris

The *gemara* introduces one of the more memorable episodes of the *daf*. Rav, Shmuel, and Rav Assi all arrived at a *shevu'at ha-ben* — which *Rashi* identifies as a *pidyon haben*, while *Tosafot*, following Rabbeinu Tam, identifies it as a celebratory meal for the birth of a son, suggesting this may be the source of the custom of a *shalom zachor*. The three were among the most eminent Amoraim of their generation, and a question of precedence arose at the entrance. Rav's status was highest, followed by Rav Assi, with Shmuel below both — yet Rav would not enter before Shmuel. The *gemara* explains: *Rav milta be'alma hu de-avid lei li-Shmuel mishum hahu ma'aseh dilaitya* — Rav was showing Shmuel an exceptional courtesy because of a prior incident in which he had accidentally cursed Shmuel, and thereafter treated him with conspicuous deference as a form of ongoing atonement. Since Rav deferred to Shmuel, and Shmuel deferred to Rav Assi, and Rav Assi deferred to Rav, no one would cross the threshold first. The solution was to have Shmuel — who, in the actual hierarchy, was in last place — hang back, so that Rav could enter first, followed by Rav Assi, with Shmuel coming last, thus reflecting the proper order without anyone having to push past a peer.

### White Cat Attack

While they were sorting out this arrangement — *adahachi ve-hachi* — a cat suddenly attacked the baby, biting off his hand. *Rashi* and the context suggest this was no ordinary house cat; the violent nature of the attack implies a larger or unusually aggressive feline. Rav immediately went out and taught: *chatul mutar le-hargo* — a cat may be killed; *ve-assur le-kaymo* — and it is forbidden to keep one; *ve-ein bo mishum gezel* — and one who takes it commits no *gezel* (theft); *ve-ein bo mishum hashavas aveidah* — and one who finds it has no obligation to return it to its owner.

### Gemara Questions

The *gemara* subjects this ruling to immediate scrutiny. If killing a cat is permitted, is it not already obvious that keeping one is forbidden? The *gemara* answers: one might have thought that while killing is permitted — meaning society has license to destroy a dangerous animal — the individual owner retains the right to keep it, and no prohibition attaches to the ownership itself. Rav therefore had to state explicitly that ownership is *assur*. Similarly, if there is no *gezel* involved in taking the cat, one might assume it is also obvious that there is no obligation to return it. Ravina resolves this: the novel ruling concerns the cat's *skin* after it has been killed. One might have thought that even if the cat itself need not be returned, its valuable pelt must be restored to the owner. Rav teaches that even the skin need not be returned — since the owner was forbidden to possess the cat in the first place, his claim over its derivative value is extinguished as well.

### Black vs White Cats

This ruling immediately runs into a *baraita*: we just learned that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar explicitly permits keeping cats (*chatulim*) for the purpose of household pest control. How can Rav prohibit cats entirely? The *gemara* resolves the tension: *lo kashya — ha be-uchma ha be-chivra* — the distinction is between black cats and white cats. Black cats are permissible to keep; white cats are the dangerous ones and subject to Rav's prohibition.

### Cat Colors Explained

But a further difficulty arises: the cat that attacked the baby in Rav's story is identified by the *gemara* as black. If black cats are safe, how does Rav's ruling emerge from that incident? The *gemara* responds: *hasam uchma bar chivra hava* — that particular cat was black but born from a white parent, and therefore retained the dangerous qualities of the white lineage.

This generates a secondary question: Ravina himself had asked — and not resolved — the status of *uchma bar chivra*, a black cat born from a white one. If Ravina's question was open, how can the *gemara* treat Rav's case as settled? The *gemara* distinguishes: Ravina's unresolved question concerned *uchma bar chivra bar uchma* — a black cat whose parent was white but whose grandparent was black, raising uncertainty about whether the black lineage reasserts itself. The cat in Rav's incident was *uchma bar chivra bar chivra* — black, but born from a line of white cats, making the dangerous character much more clear-cut. The *gemara* acknowledges, however, that how one is meant to know the precise lineage of a particular cat is not entirely clear. The bottom line is that black cats from a stable black lineage are permitted for pest control, while white cats and their offspring — even when black in appearance — are dangerous, forbidden to keep, and may be killed.

## Opportunity & Welfare

### Boils & Doors

A chain of teachings attributed to Rabbi Acha bar Papa in the name of Rabbi Abba bar Papa in the name of Rabbi Adda bar Papa (with variant traditions substituting Rabbi Chiya bar Papa and Rabbi Chanina bar Papa) introduces several apparently disparate rulings that are linked by thematic association. The first: *matri'in al ha-chichuch be-Shabbat* — one may sound an alarm (*matri'in*) or call out even on *Shabbat* on account of *chichuch*, a spreading blister or boil, out of concern that a contagious outbreak may be developing in the community. According to *Rashi*, *matri'in* refers to sounding a *shofar*; according to *Tosafot*, the distinction between *matri'in* and *tzo'akin* may have more to do with the day on which one calls out than with the instrument used. This discussion is more fully developed in *Masechet Ta'anit*.

The second teaching: *ve-delet ha-ne'elet lo be-meheira tiftach* — a door that has been closed will not quickly open again. One must seize opportunity when it presents itself.

A *baraita* seems to challenge the first ruling: certain plagues — including *chichuch*, locusts, flies, hornets, gnats, snakes, and scorpions — would not warrant *matri'in* but only *tzo'akin*. The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — kan be-lach kan be-yavesh* — the distinction depends on whether the affliction is moist or dry. The moist, weeping type of boil is more dangerous and warrants the stronger response even on *Shabbat*. This is supported by a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: the *shchin* that God brought upon Egypt was moist on the outside and dry on the inside — the worst possible combination — as implied by the verse describing it as *shchin av'bu'ot* (Shemot 9:9), boiling and sprouting sores. That type of affliction can be the occasion for calling out even on *Shabbat*.

### Door Metaphors

The teaching about the closed door that does not quickly open is then interpreted in several ways by various Amoraim. Mar Zutra says it refers to *smicha*: the chain of rabbinic ordination stretching back to Moshe Rabbeinu was at certain periods in acute jeopardy of being broken, and if one had the opportunity to receive *smicha* and missed it, that door might not open again soon. Rav Ashi gives a more general formulation: *kol ha-marei'in lo lo be-meheira meitivin lo* — one to whom things have gone badly will not quickly find them going well again. Rav Acha mi-Difti gives the most pessimistic version: *le-olam ein meitivin lo* — one who has missed the opportunity will never find it again. The *gemara* notes, however, that *lav hi* — that is not the authoritative formulation; it represents Rav Acha mi-Difti's personal experience. *Tosafot* observes that in the story underlying Rav Acha mi-Difti's remark, he himself initially said the door would not *quickly* reopen — and only after he never did recover the lost position did he revise his teaching to "never." It is a quietly poignant footnote about how experience colors one's rulings.

## Deeds on Shabbat

### Writing Deed

The *gemara* now arrives at the ruling most directly relevant to the broader theme of *yishuv Eretz Yisrael*: *ha-lokeach bayit be-Eretz Yisrael kosvin alav ono afilu be-Shabbat* — one who purchases a house in Eretz Yisrael may have the deed written even on *Shabbat*. This is the reason the preceding teaching about closed doors was cited here: if an opportunity to acquire property in Eretz Yisrael presents itself and one hesitates, it may not present itself again.

### Settlement Priority

The *gemara* immediately questions the ruling: *be-Shabbat salka da'atach*? Can you literally write on *Shabbat*? Writing is a *melacha de-oraita*. The answer, as *Rava* stated in a parallel context, is: *omer le-nochri ve-oseh* — one instructs a non-Jew to write the deed, and the non-Jew performs the act. Normally, *amira le-nochri* — instructing a non-Jew to perform *melacha* on *Shabbat* — is itself a rabbinic prohibition (*shvut*). But here, *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael lo gazru bah rabanan* — for the sake of settling Eretz Yisrael, the rabbis did not apply the usual *gezeira*, and the *amira le-nochri* is permitted.

### Amira Exception

This ruling opens a broader halachic question: under what conditions is *amira le-nochri* permissible? The *Bahag* (*Ba'al Halachot Gedolot*), quoted by *Tosafot*, takes a radical position: *amira le-nochri* is permitted even for a *de-oraita* in the context of *mitzvot* such as *bris milah* — or at least for *machshirei bris milah*, the preparatory acts surrounding a *bris*, on the grounds that *bris milah* itself overrides *Shabbat*. Some opinions extend this further to *tzorchei rabim* — communal needs. The opposite extreme would not permit *amira le-nochri* even for a *shvut de-shvut be-makom mitzvah*. The normative consensus is that *amira le-nochri* is permitted for a rabbinic prohibition (*de-rabanan*) when a *mitzvah* is at stake, but not for a *de-oraita* prohibition — and the case of *yishuv Eretz Yisrael* is treated as a specific exception to that rule.

## Town Pathways

### Four Directions

The *daf* closes with a teaching of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *ha-lokeach ir be-Eretz Yisrael kofin oto likach lo derech me-arba ruchoseha* — one who purchases an entire town in Eretz Yisrael is compelled to also acquire pathways leading to and from it in all four directions, *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* — for the sake of settling the land. The underlying concern, it seems, is that if the roads and paths surrounding the town are held by others who might close them off, the town becomes inaccessible — which would undermine the very purpose of Jewish settlement. By compelling the purchaser to acquire the pathways as well, the rabbinic enactment ensures that the town remains genuinely viable and connected to the surrounding landscape, and that the settlement cannot be strangled by a hostile neighbor who controls the access routes.
