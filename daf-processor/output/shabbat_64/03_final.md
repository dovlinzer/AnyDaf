# Shabbat 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah: Garments & Sackcloth

### Arig & Tachshit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָרִיג כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — טָמֵא, וְתַכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — טָמֵא. אָרִיג וְתַכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — טָמֵא. מוּסָף שַׂק עַל הַבֶּגֶד שֶׁטָּמֵא מִשּׁוּום אָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A woven fabric of any size** can become **ritually impure, and an ornament of any size** can become **ritually impure.** An object that is half **woven** fabric **and** half **ornament** of **any size** can become **ritually impure.** And **a sack is added** to the category of **garment; it** too **is ritually impure due to woven fabric.**

The daf opens at the very bottom of 63b, where the Gemara has turned from the general topic of what may be worn on Shabbat into an accompanying discussion of *tumah ve-taharah*. The *Tanu Rabbanan* begins: *arig kol shehu tamei* — a woven piece of clothing, even the smallest size, is *tamei*. Normally we speak of a minimum size of three by three fingers, or three by three *tefachim*, depending on whether one is dealing with a remnant of a larger garment. The present case concerns an item made from the outset to be very small — a tiny wrap around a finger, for instance — and for such an item even the smallest size suffices.

The *braita* continues: *ve-tashchit kol shehu tamei* — an ornament, whether made of metal or wood, not a woven cloth, likewise becomes *tamei* at the smallest size. And then: *arig ve-tashchit kol shehu tamei* — a combination of the two, say a brooch with a woven string attachment, is also *tamei* at the smallest size.

### Tosafot: Kol Shehu

This last clause raises a question that Tosafot addresses directly. If each of the two components — the ornament and the weave — is already *tamei* at *kol shehu*, why does the *braita* need to specify the combination? The answer Tosafot gives is that there is a minimum size even to a *kol shehu*; not every infinitesimal thing qualifies. It is therefore possible that each component alone is only half of a *kol shehu*, and only when combined do they together constitute a full *kol shehu*. That combined item is also *tamei*. That is Tosafot's reading of the *braita*.

### Rava's Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אָרִיג כׇּל שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא — מֵ״אוֹ בֶגֶד״. תַּכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא — מִצִּיץ. אָרִיג וְתַכְשִׁיט כׇּל שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא — מִ״כׇּל כְּלִי מַעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in explanation of the *baraita*: **A woven fabric of any size is ritually impure** as derived **from** the phrase **"or a garment." An ornament of any size is ritually impure,** as derived **from** the *halakhot* of **the frontplate. A woven fabric and an ornament of any size is ritually impure,** as derived **from** that which is stated: "And Moses and Elazar the priest took the gold from them, **all vessels with which labor is done"** (Numbers 31:51). Any object that can be utilized for any action falls into the category of: All vessels with which labor is done.

The Gemara then asks for the biblical sources underlying each of these rulings. Rava answers: *arig kol shehu tamei* is derived from the verse in the context of *sheratzim*, which reads *o beged* — "or a garment." The word *o* is extra, and from it we derive that even the tiniest garment, a *kol shehu*, becomes *tamei*. The ruling that a small ornament is *tamei* derives from *tzitz*, the priestly headplate. And the ruling that a hybrid of ornament and weave is *tamei* derives from the verse in the context of Midian, which states *kol kli ma'aseh* — all articles fashioned for a purpose. The inclusive word *kol* encompasses even a hybrid vessel.

### Midian Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: הָהוּא בְּמִדְיָן כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** **One of the Sages said to Rava: That** verse **is written with regard to Midian.**

One of the rabbis challenged Rava: that verse is written in the context of Midian, where the booty had been exposed to corpses. This may reflect the strictures of *tumat meit*, a more severe category — perhaps one cannot apply it to the lighter *tumot*. Tosafot raises an additional point: perhaps the verse is not about *tumah* at all, but simply about cataloguing the spoils of war, with no legal implication for the laws of *tumah*.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמַר ״כְּלִי״ ״כְּלִי״ מֵהָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **He derived from** the word **vessel** written **there,** with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity imparted by a corpse, and the word **vessel** written with regard to the *halakhot* of other impurities.

The Gemara's resolution is a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *kli* appears in the context of *sheretz* and also in the context of Midian. By drawing this verbal equation, whatever we learn about a *kli* in the Midian passage — that even a hybrid vessel is included — applies equally to the laws of *tumat sheretz*. This establishes that hybrid vessels become *tamei* through contact with a dead rodent.

### Sackcloth Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּסָף שַׂק עַל הַבֶּגֶד שֶׁטָּמֵא מִשּׁוּם אָרִיג. אַטּוּ בֶּגֶד לָאו אָרִיג הוּא?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מוּסָף שַׂק עַל הַבֶּגֶד, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ אָרִיג — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita* that **a sack is added** to the category of "**garment"; it** too **is ritually impure due to woven fabric.** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that a garment is not a woven fabric?** Rather, the statement should be emended and **say as follows: A sack** made from goat hair **is added to the category of garment; even though it is not woven it** can nevertheless become **ritually impure.**

The *braita* concludes with a statement that initially seems strange: *mosif sak al ha-beged* — sackcloth, made from goat's hair, is more strict than a regular garment in that it is *tamei* as a woven item. But a standard *beged* is also *tamei* as a woven item. What additional stringency is being attributed to sackcloth?

### Sackcloth Reinterpreted

The Gemara resolves this by reinterpreting the statement: sackcloth is more strict than a regular garment in that it is *tamei* even though it is not actually woven. It falls within the category of a garment even absent the quality of *arig*. Sackcloth, it emerges, was not produced through a classic weaving of interlaced threads — it was made by a different process, perhaps a twining or twisting — and yet it still carries the *tumah* of a woven garment. The sackcloth is treated as if it were a garment even without being one in the technical sense.

### Twisted Hair Necklace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁכֵּן עָנִי קוֹלֵעַ שָׁלֹשׁ נִימִין וְתוֹלָה בְּצַוַּאר בִּתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what is** a garment made of unwoven goat hair **suitable? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Since a poor person** occasionally **braids three** goat **hairs and hangs it on his daughter's neck** as an ornament.

This raises the natural question: for what practical purpose would one use goat's hair that has not been woven at all? Rabbi Yochanan answers: a poor person would take three strands of goat's hair, twist them together into a sort of braided cord, and hang it around his daughter's neck as an inexpensive necklace. Even such a minimally processed item — twisted but not woven — falls within the category and is *tamei*.

### Baraita: Tuvui V'Arig

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שָׂק״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שַׂק, מִנַּיִין לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַקִּילְקְלִי וְאֶת הַחֲבָק — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ שָׂק״. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אֶת הַחֲבָלִים וְאֶת הַמְּשִׁיחוֹת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שָׂק״. מָה שַׂק טְוִוי וְאָרִיג — אַף כֹּל טְוִוי וְאָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a detailed halakhic exposition of that verse in a different *baraita*. From the fact that the verse mentioned **sack, I have only** derived that **a** whole **sack** can become ritually impure. **From where** is it derived **to include** even **reins [*kilkeli*] and a saddle band** fastened under the horse's belly in the category of those objects that can become ritually impure? **The verse states: "Or sack";** "or" teaches that the verse is referring to items similar to a sack as well. **I might** have thought, on that basis, **that I** should **include** even **the ropes and** measuring **cords. The verse states: "Sack," just as a sack is spun and woven, so too, everything that is spun and woven** can become ritually impure. Ropes and measuring cords are not made from spun threads, and they are certainly not woven.

A new *braita* now focuses on sackcloth specifically, extending the analysis. The *braita* begins: *ein li ela sak* — I know only sackcloth itself. From where do I learn to include other items?

### Horse Straps Inclusion

The *braita* asks: *minayin l'rabot et ha-kilkali ve-et ha-chavak* — how do I know to include the *kilkali* and *chavak*? Rashi identifies these as straps used on horses — a breast strap and a girth, various types of horse tack made from goat's hair. These are not human garments at all. The *braita* answers from the word *o* in the verse *o sak*: the inclusive *o* extends the category to include these horse straps, which are made of goat's hair even if fashioned for an animal.

### Ropes Exclusion

One might then think to include even *chavalim* and *meshichot* — ropes made of goat's hair. Rashi explains that these ropes were used primarily for measuring, and crucially they had not been twisted at all; they were simply straight lengths of fiber, not processed in any way resembling normal fabric production. Surely even those should be included? The *braita* answers from the word *sak* itself: *ma sak tavui ve-arig af kol tavui ve-arig* — just as sackcloth involves twisting and weaving, so anything covered by this category must involve at least twisting or weaving. Raw, untwisted ropes are excluded. The picture that emerges is: the material requirement is met by goat's hair (and we will see, other animal hairs); the processing requirement demands that the fibers be at minimum twisted together. Straight, unprocessed strands do not qualify.

### Dead Body Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר בְּמֵת: ״וְכׇל כְּלִי עוֹר וְכׇל מַעֲשֵׂה עִזִּים וְגוֹ׳ תִּתְחַטָּאוּ״ — לְרַבּוֹת הַקִּילְקְלִי וְאֶת הַחֲבָק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Now, it says with regard to** the *halakhot* of ritual impurity imparted by **a corpse:** "And every garment **and all that is made of skins and all work of goats'** hair and all things made of wood **you shall purify"** (Numbers 31:20). This verse comes **to include reins and the band** under the horse's belly within the category of: All work of goats' hair. They too can become ritually impure.

The *braita* then moves from *tumat sheretz* to *tumat meit*. By a corpse, the relevant verse reads: *ve-chol kli or ve-chol ma'aseh izim titchata'u* — you shall purify any leather vessel and anything made of goat's hair. The inclusive phrase *kol ma'aseh izim* — anything made of goat's hair — similarly includes the *kilkali* and *chavak*. And the same question arises: might it include even the untwisted ropes?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אֶת הַחֲבָלִים וְאֶת הַמְּשִׁיחוֹת? וְדִין הוּא: טִימֵּא בַּשֶּׁרֶץ, וְטִימֵּא בַּמֵּת. מָה כְּשֶׁטִּימֵּא בַּשֶּׁרֶץ — לֹא טִימֵּא אֶלָּא טְוִוי וְאָרִיג, אַף כְּשֶׁטִּימֵּא בַּמֵּת — לֹא טִימֵּא אֶלָּא טְוִוי וְאָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** I **might** have thought **that I** would **include even the ropes and** thin **cords** in this category. The Gemara begins with a logical analysis. **And it** may be inferred **logically** to the contrary, that a rope cannot become impure. The verse **deemed impure** an object that came in contact **with a creeping animal, and it deemed impure** an object that came in contact **with a corpse. Just as when it rendered** an object **impure from** contact **with a creeping animal it only rendered impure** objects **spun and woven,** as stated above; **so too, when it rendered impure** an object from contact **with a corpse, it only rendered impure** objects **spun and woven.**

The *braita* proposes learning the exclusion by analogy from the *sheretz* case: there we established through *o sak* that only twisted or woven items are *tamei*. Apply the same split to *tumat meit*. The Gemara intervenes: can this analogy simply be drawn?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵן אִם הֵיקֵל בְּטוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ שֶׁהִיא קַלָּה, נָקֵיל בְּטוּמְאַת הַמֵּת שֶׁהִיא חֲמוּרָה?! — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בֶּגֶד״ וָ״עוֹר״ ״בֶּגֶד״ וָ״עוֹר״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** There is room to distinguish: Are these **indeed** comparable? **If** the Torah **was lenient with regard to the ritual impurity of** an object that came in contact **with a creeping animal, which is a less severe** form of impurity, saying that ropes do not become impure from contact with that form of ritual impurity, **will we be lenient with regard to ritual impurity** imparted by **a corpse, which is** more **severe?** Perhaps, since impurity imparted by a corpse is more severe, even objects not woven and spun, e.g., ropes, become ritually impure from contact with it. Therefore, **the verse states garment and leather, garment and leather to** establish **a verbal analogy.**

*Tumat sheretz* is a lighter category — only a single day's *tumah* — and it is precisely because it is light that it might be more selective about which garments it renders *tamei*. But *tumat meit*, which is far more severe, might encompass a broader range of objects. The argument by analogy can be challenged.

### Logical Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר ״בֶּגֶד״ וָ״עוֹר״ בְּשֶׁרֶץ וְנֶאֱמַר ״בֶּגֶד״ וָ״עוֹר״ בְּמֵת. מָה בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר הָאָמוּר בְּשֶׁרֶץ — לֹא טִימֵּא אֶלָּא טְוִוי וְאָרִיג, אַף בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר הָאָמוּר בְּמֵת — לֹא טִימֵּא אֶלָּא טְוִוי וְאָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** The term **garment and leather is stated with regard to** ritual impurity imparted by **a creeping animal:** "And whatever any of them falls upon when they are dead will be impure whether it be any vessel of wood, or a garment, or leather, or sack, whatever vessel it be with which any work is done it must be put into water and it will be impure until evening, then it will be clean" (Leviticus 11:32). **And garment and leather is stated with regard to** ritual impurity imparted by **a corpse. Just as garment and leather stated with regard to a creeping animal only rendered impure** objects that are **spun and woven, so too, garment and leather stated with regard to a corpse only rendered impure** objects that are **spun and woven.**

The logical critique is sharpened further. One could construct a *kal va-chomer* in either direction: since even the lighter *tumah* of a rodent applies to twisted goat's hair items, certainly the stricter *tumah* of a corpse should apply. The *braita* rejects this reasoning by pointing out its vulnerability: *tumat sheretz* is characterized as more pervasive in certain respects — a rodent transmits *tumah* from the size of a lentil, whereas a corpse requires the size of an olive. Since the Torah treats rodents as *tamei* in cases where an analogous dead body would not be, one cannot assume the laws of garments flow automatically from one category to the other. Logical derivation — whether by analogy or by *kal va-chomer* — cannot bridge this gap.

It is worth pausing here. This pattern is characteristic of *midrashei halacha* — works like Sifra and Sifrei — where the text repeatedly entertains a logical derivation, constructs it in full, and then demolishes it, ultimately returning to a formal hermeneutical proof. One purpose of this process is to clarify the genuine distinctions between categories. Another, perhaps deeper, purpose is to demonstrate the limits of unaided human logic: one cannot simply reason one's way to Torah law. That requires a *pasuk*.

### Beged V'Or Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר הָאָמוּר בְּמֵת — טִמֵּא כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה עִזִּים, אַף בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר הָאָמוּר בְּשֶׁרֶץ — טִמֵּא כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה עִזִּים.
>
> **Translation:** Utilizing the same verbal analogy, one could say: **And just as garment and leather stated with regard to a corpse rendered impure any** object that is the **work of goats'** hair, **so too, garment and leather stated with regard to a creeping animal rendered impure any** object that is the **work of goats'** hair.

The *braita* therefore concludes: *beged ve-or l'gezeirah shavah* — the phrase *beged ve-or* appears both in the context of *sheretz* and in the context of *meit*. This is not an analogy subject to challenge; it is a *gezeirah shavah*, a formal verbal equation. Just as by *sheretz* only twisted or woven goat's hair items are *tamei*, so too by *meit* only twisted or woven items are *tamei*. And conversely, just as by *meit* the verse says *kol ma'aseh izim* — a very inclusive expression — that inclusiveness flows back through the *gezeirah shavah* to *sheretz* as well.

### Bidirectional Gleichung

The *gezeirah shavah* thus operates in both directions simultaneously. From the *meit* passage, the inclusive *kol ma'aseh izim* ensures maximum breadth; from the *sheretz* passage, the limiting word *sak* ensures the requirement of twisting or weaving. One applies to the other in a single ironclad equation.

### Animal Hairs Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דָּבָר הַבָּא מִן הָעִזִּים, מִנַּיִין לְרַבּוֹת דָּבָר הַבָּא מִזְּנַב הַסּוּס וּמִזְּנַב הַפָּרָה? — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ שָׂק״.
>
> **Translation:** **I have only** derived from this verbal analogy that **an object that comes from goats** can become ritually impure; **from where** do I derive **to include an item that comes from a horse's tail or from a cow's tail? The verse states: Or a sack,** and anything like a sack, i.e., these other items as well.

But the *braita* now asks a further question: so far we have been speaking of goat's hair. From where do we learn to include items made from the tail of a horse or the tail of a cow? These are hairs rather than wool — biologically quite different from what one would weave into standard fabric. The *braita* answers from the word *o* in *o sak*: the inclusive *o* extends the category beyond goat's hair to other animal hairs as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ לְקִילְקְלִי וַחֲבָק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't you** already **derive** ritual impurity with regard to **reins and a saddle band** from this verse? How can ritual impurity for items that come from a horse's tail and a cow's tail be derived from the same verse?

This raises a difficulty. We already used the word *o* to include the horse straps — the *kilkali* and *chavak*. How can the same word now do double duty to include other animal hairs?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִקַּמֵּי דְּתֵיתֵי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתְיָא גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה אִיַּיתַּר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That applies only before the verbal analogy was cited; now that the verbal analogy was cited,** the verse **is rendered extraneous.** The fact that any item that falls in the category of: "And all work of goats' hair," can become ritually impure is derived from the verbal analogy. Reins and a saddle bands are included in the category of work of goats' hair. Therefore, they need not be derived from that phrase. Consequently, a different *halakha* can be derived from that extraneous phrase: Objects that come from a horse's tail or a cow's tail can become ritually impure.

The Gemara answers: *hanei milei mikami de-leitei gezeirah shavah* — we used *o* to include the horse straps before we had the *gezeirah shavah*. Once we have made the *gezeirah shavah* between *sheretz* and *meit*, the inclusive *kol ma'aseh izim* from the *meit* passage does the work of including the horse straps. The word *sak* from the *sheretz* passage supplies the limiting principle. Since the *o* is no longer needed for its original function, it becomes available to teach the inclusion of other animal hairs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁרֶץ, בְּטוּמְאַת מֵת מִנַּיִין?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And I have** derived that an object made from a horse's tail can become impure **only with regard to a creeping animal;** however, **with regard to a corpse, from where** is this derived?

And then the final step of the argument closes the loop: now that other animal hairs are established as included by *sheretz*, the *gezeirah shavah* carries that ruling across to *meit* as well. One cannot derive it by *kal va-chomer* — the Gemara demonstrates, again, that such reasoning can be challenged from either direction — and so one must rely on the *gezeirah shavah*.

### Mufneh Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוּפְנֶה. דְּאִי לָאו מוּפְנֶה, אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְשֶׁרֶץ שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it must be that the words garment and leather are **free.** Those terms must be superfluous in their context. The Torah included those terms for the express purpose of establishing the verbal analogy. A verbal analogy that is based on otherwise extraneous terms cannot be logically refuted. **Because if** these terms are **not free,** the verbal analogy **can be refuted: What** is unique **to a creeping animal?** Its ritual impurity is stringent **in that** it **renders** objects **ritually impure** even **by means of** contact with **a lentil-bulk** of a creeping animal. That is not the case with regard to a corpse, which is less severe in that it renders objects ritually impure only by means of contact with an olive-bulk of a corpse. Unless the terms are free, the analogy can be refuted.

The Gemara then turns to a metatextual point that underlies the entire *gezeirah shavah*. A *gezeirah shavah* built on shared phrases that were not superfluous in their original contexts can, in principle, be challenged by a strong logical argument. Only if the shared phrases are *mufneh* — extra, serving no other function than to enable the *gezeirah shavah* — is the equation ironclad and immune to rational critique. Here the Gemara establishes that the phrase *beged ve-or* is indeed *mufneh* on both sides.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאיֵי אִפְּנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֵי. מִכְּדֵי שֶׁרֶץ אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל שֶׁרֶץ״, וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע: ״וְכׇל בֶּגֶד וְכׇל עוֹר אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה עָלָיו שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״ — ״בֶּגֶד״ וָ״עוֹר״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּשֶׁרֶץ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Indeed [*la'ei*], they are free.** The Gemara proves that the terms garment and leather are extraneous in their context. **Now, since** ritual impurity from contact with **a creeping animal is juxtaposed to** ritual impurity from contact with **semen, as it is written:** "And whoever touches anything that is impure by the dead or a **man from whom semen is emitted"** (Leviticus 22:4), **and juxtaposed to that** is the verse: **"Or whoever touches any creeping animal** which makes him impure, or a person who may make him impure with any impurity that he has" (Leviticus 22:5). **And it is written** in the *halakhot* **of** the ritual impurity of **semen: "And every garment and every hide on which the semen is** must be washed with water and will be impure until evening" (Leviticus 15:17). Since the verses appear next to each other, the *halakhot* of each can be derived from the other. Consequently, the words **garment and leather, which the Torah wrote with regard to a creeping animal, why do I need** them? The relevant *halakha* could be derived from the *halakhot* of seminal impurity. **Learn from it** that garment and leather were mentioned **to render them free.**

The reasoning proceeds as follows. A *sheretz* is compared by the Torah to *shichvat zera* — seminal emission — based on the juxtaposition of *ish asher yiga be-chol sheretz* immediately after *ish asher tetzei mimenu shichvat zera*. Since the Torah compares them, what causes *tumah* by *shichvat zera* applies to *sheretz*. And the verse by *shichvat zera* already states *ve-chol beged ve-chol or* — any garment or leather becomes *tamei*. Therefore the *beged ve-or* that appears in the *sheretz* passage could have been derived from *shichvat zera* without being stated explicitly. It is superfluous — *mufneh*. By precisely parallel reasoning, the Torah similarly compares *tumat meit* to *shichvat zera*, juxtaposing *ha-noge'ah be-chol tamei nefesh* with *o ish asher tetzei mimenu shichvat zera*. Once again, *beged ve-or* could have been derived for *meit* from *shichvat zera*, and so the explicit statement of *beged ve-or* in the *meit* passage is likewise superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד הוּא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד לְמֵידִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמֵידִין וּמְשִׁיבִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And still, it is free only from one side** of the verbal analogy. Although the terms garment and leather stated with regard to ritual impurity imparted by a creeping animal are extraneous in their context, and the relevant *halakha* could have been derived in another manner, those terms stated with regard to ritual impurity imparted by a corpse are not extraneous in their context. This verbal analogy is only free from one side. **It** works out **well according to the** opinion of the **one who said,** with regard to a verbal analogy that is **free from** only **one side, one can derive from it and cannot refute** it logically. **However, according to** the opinion of **the one who said** that **one can derive from** a verbal analogy of this kind **and one can refute** it logically, **what can be said?**

### Ironcladness of Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמֵת נָמֵי אִפְּנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֶה: מִכְּדִי מֵת אִתַּקַּשׁ לְשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנֹּגֵעַ בְּכׇל טְמֵא נֶפֶשׁ אוֹ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״, וּכְתִיב בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע: ״וְכׇל בֶּגֶד וְכׇל עוֹר״ — ״בֶּגֶד״ וָ״עוֹר״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּמֵת לְמָה לִי? — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Garment and leather stated **with regard to** impurity imparted by a **corpse are also free. Now, since a corpse is juxtaposed with semen, as it is written: "And whoever touches anything that is impure by the dead or a man whose semen is emitted from him"** (Leviticus 22:4); **and it is stated with regard to semen: "And every garment and every hide"** (Leviticus 15:17); the terms **garment and leather, which the Torah wrote with regard to** ritual impurity imparted by **a corpse, why do I** need them? **Learn from it** that they are mentioned in order **to render them free.** These terms are extraneous in their context, and were written for the purpose of the verbal analogy with the *halakhot* of creeping animals.

Since *beged ve-or* is *mufneh* on both sides — both in the *sheretz* passage and in the *meit* passage — the *gezeirah shavah* is fully ironclad. It does not merely carry the suggestion of equivalence between two cases that happen to share similar language; both shared phrases were placed there for the express purpose of enabling the equation. No logical challenge can break it. The entire edifice — which types of goat's hair are *tamei*, why the twisted but unspun ropes are excluded, why other animal hairs are included — rests on this foundation. And the methodological point is as striking as the legal conclusion: human logic, even when it looks quite persuasive, cannot substitute for this kind of formal derivation. The *gezeirah shavah* says what logic cannot securely establish on its own.

## Midian Booty & Gaze

### Agil & Kumaz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר מָצָא כְלִי זָהָב אֶצְעָדָה וְצָמִיד טַבַּעַת עָגִיל וְכוּמָז״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״עָגִיל״ — זֶה דְּפוּס שֶׁל דַּדִּין. ״כּוּמָז״ — זֶה דְּפוּס שֶׁל בֵּית הָרֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets verses written with regard to the Midianite war discussed above: **"And we have brought an offering before the Lord what every man has gotten of jewels of gold, chains, and bracelets, rings, *agil*, and *kumaz*,** to make atonement for our souls before the Lord" (Numbers 31:50). **Rabbi Elazar said: *Agil* is a mold** in the shape of a woman's **breasts** worn over them as an ornament. ***Kumaz* is a mold** in the shape **of the womb.**

Having introduced the Midian passage as a source, the Gemara now turns to examine those verses directly — a discussion relevant both to the *tumah* analysis already completed and to the larger theme of women's jewelry that runs through the entire chapter. The verse in question enumerates the jewelry that the *nesi'im* brought as a voluntary offering after the war: *etz'ada ve-tzamid taba'at agil ve-chumaz* — bracelets, bangles, rings, *agil*, and *kumaz*.

Rabbi Elazar identifies the last two items. *Agil*, he says, is *d'fus shel dadim* — a kind of form or ornament worn over the breasts. *Kumaz* is *d'fus shel beis ha-rechem* — an ornament worn directly over the pubic region. Both, in other words, were worn directly against the body in deeply intimate locations — analogous, one might note, to the body piercings and intimate jewelry worn in various cultures, including today.

### Kumaz Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דִּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן ״מָחוֹךְ״ — דָּבָר הַמֵּבִיא לִידֵי גִּיחוּךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: מִגּוּפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, ״כּוּמָז״ — כָּאן מְקוֹם זִימָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: If so, that is** the reason **that we translate** *kumaz* into Aramaic as ***maḥokh*,** meaning **an item that leads to foolishness. Rabba said to him: This** meaning **is learned from the verse itself; *kumaz*** is an acronym for: **Here [*kan*]** is the **place of [*mekom*] lewdness [*zimma*].**

Rav Yosef says: if that is what *kumaz* means, then I now understand the Aramaic translation, which renders *kumaz* as *machoach*. He connects *machoach* to the word *gichuch* — laughter, or more precisely, levity and licentiousness. Rashi glosses this as *leitzanut*. Rabbah then adds that the meaning is embedded in the Hebrew word itself: *kumaz* is an acronym of *kan makom zimah* — here is the place of licentiousness. The ornamentation of the most intimate parts of the body in this manner was understood as serving a context of sexual license.

### Hirhur Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקְצֹף מֹשֶׁה עַל פְּקוּדֵי הֶחָיִל״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אָמַר לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁמָּא חֲזַרְתֶּם לְקִלְקוּלְכֶם הָרִאשׁוֹן? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״לֹא נִפְקַד מִמֶּנּוּ אִישׁ״. אָמַר לָהֶן: אִם כֵּן כַּפָּרָה לָמָּה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם מִידֵי עֲבֵירָה יָצָאנוּ, מִידֵי הִרְהוּר לֹא יָצָאנוּ! מִיָּד ״וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Later in that chapter, it is written: **"And Moses was angry with the officers of the host,** the captains over thousands, and captains over hundreds, who came from the battle" (Numbers 31:14); **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **Moses said to Israel: Perhaps you have returned to your original sinful** behavior, when you sinned with the daughters of Moab and Midian at Shittim? **They said to him: "Not one man of us is missing"** (Numbers 31:49), we remain as wholesome in deed as we were. **He said to them: If so, why** do you need **atonement?** The princes brought these ornaments to atone for their souls. **They said to him: If we have emerged from the grasps of** actual **transgression, we have not emerged from the grasps of thoughts** of transgression. **Immediately,** they decided: **"And we have brought an offering before the Lord."**

Against this backdrop, the Gemara turns to the narrative surrounding the offering. When the Israelite soldiers first returned from Midian, Moshe grew angry upon seeing that they had kept the Midianite women alive — precisely the women who had seduced Bnei Israel into sin. Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha explains: Moshe said to Israel, *shema chazartem le-kilkulchem* — perhaps you have returned to your corrupt ways, suggesting they had an interest in the surviving women. Some fifty verses later, when the *nesi'im* bring their voluntary offering, they address this implicit accusation directly. They declare: *lo nifkad mimenu ish* — not one of our men was unaccounted for. Moshe responds: then why are you bringing an offering of atonement? They answer: *im midei aveira yatzanu, midei hirhur lo yatzanu* — if we escaped actual transgression, we did not escape lustful thought. They had not acted, but they had harbored *hirhur*, and for that they sought *kapara*.

This is a striking passage. The question of how the Gemara treats *hirhur* — whether the concern is its tendency to lead to *zera le-vatala* or seminal emission, or whether it is problematic in itself — is a broader topic that runs through many *sugyot*. Here it is treated quite plainly: *hirhur* without any accompanying act is grounds for bringing an atonement offering.

### Male Gaze Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: מִפְּנֵי מָה הוּצְרְכוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר כַּפָּרָה — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁזָּנוּ עֵינֵיהֶם מִן הָעֶרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: For what reason did Israel in that generation require atonement? Because** **they nourished their eyes from nakedness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מִפְּנֵי מָה מָנָה הַכָּתוּב תַּכְשִׁיטִין שֶׁבַּחוּץ עִם תַּכְשִׁיטִין שֶׁבִּפְנִים? — לוֹמַר לָךְ: כׇּל הַמִּסְתַּכֵּל בְּאֶצְבַּע קְטַנָּה שֶׁל אִשָּׁה כְּאִילּוּ מִסְתַּכֵּל בִּמְקוֹם הַתּוּרְפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse that lists the ornaments, **Rav Sheshet said: For what reason did the verse list outer ornaments,** i.e., a bracelet, **with inner ornaments,** i.e., a *kumaz*? **To tell you** that **anyone who gazes upon a woman's little finger** is considered **as if he gazed upon her** naked **genitals.** The atonement was for the sin of looking.

Rav Sheshet then asks: why does the Torah list all of the jewelry together — finger rings alongside the intimate body ornaments? To teach us, he answers, that *kol ha-mistakel be-etzba ketana shel isha ke-ilu mistakel be-makom ha-torfa* — anyone who gazes sexually at a woman's little finger, it is as though he gazes at her most private area. The point is not about what body part is visible or covered. The problem is the quality of the gaze itself. A lustful, objectifying look — *zanu eineihem* — is problematic regardless of where it is directed. From a halachic standpoint this is a concern about *hirhur* and the continuous provocation of the *yeitzer hara*; from what we would today call a feminist perspective, it addresses the male gaze and the objectification of women. The Gemara's framing is different from the modern one, but the underlying concern has a recognizable family resemblance. And it is the man's responsibility for how he is looking, not a demand that women cover their fingers.

The jewelry was brought as a *korban* and presumably melted down for use in the *Beis Ha-Mikdash*. One might ask whether there is a concern about using items so associated with licentiousness for sacred purposes, and whether the transformation through melting changes the object's identity. This connects to broader discussions elsewhere about items associated with — though not technically constituting — *avoda zara*, and whether a genuine physical transformation suffices to permit their use. The Gemara here does not address this question directly.

## Permissible Items

### Hair Extensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יוֹצְאָה אִשָּׁה בְּחוּטֵי שֵׂעָר, בֵּין מִשֶּׁלָּהּ בֵּין מִשֶּׁל חֲבֶירְתַּהּ בֵּין מִשֶּׁל בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna continues to discuss those items with which it is permitted to go out and those items with which it is prohibited to go out on Shabbat. **A woman may go out with strands of hair** that she put on her head, **whether** they are **from her own** hair that she made into a wig, **or whether** they are **from** the hair of **another, or whether they are from** the hair of **an animal.**

Having catalogued what is forbidden, the *Mishna* now turns to what a woman may wear. She may go out *be-chutei se'ar* — with hair extensions, whether woven from her own cut hair, from the hair of another woman, or from animal hair. The concern throughout this chapter has been that a woman will remove an item in a public domain to show her friends, thus carrying it without intent. Hair, however, is not a *chatzitza* for *mikvah* immersion, and so we are not concerned that she will remove it to immerse.

### Head Ornaments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְטוֹטֶפֶת, וּבְסַרְבִּיטִין בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן תְּפוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a woman may go out **with** an ornament called ***totefet*, and with *sarvitin* when they are sewn** and will not fall.

She may go out with a *totafet* and with *sarvitin* — types of head ornaments — *bizman she-tefurin be-kavul*, when they are sewn into her head covering, her cap. Since they are woven into the hat, she will not remove them without also removing the hat, which she would not do in public.

### Headscarves & Wigs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכָבוּל וּבְפֵאָה נׇכְרִית לֶחָצֵר. בְּמוֹךְ שֶׁבְּאׇזְנָהּ, וּבְמוֹךְ שֶׁבְּסַנְדָּלָהּ, וּבְמוֹךְ שֶׁהִתְקִינָה לְנִדָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** She may go out on Shabbat **with** a **woolen cap or with a wig to the courtyard,** although not to the public domain. **And** likewise she may go out **with a cloth that is in her ear, and with a cloth in her sandal, and with a cloth that she placed due to her menstrual** status.

She may likewise go out wearing a *kavul* — a type of scarf or hairnet — or a *pea nachrit*, a wig. These, however, are permitted only *la-chatzer*, in the courtyard — not necessarily in the public domain. The full scope of this limitation will be explored in the Gemara shortly.

### Medical Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפִילְפֵּל, וּבְגַלְגַּל מֶלַח, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּיתָּן לְתוֹךְ פִּיהָ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּתֵּן לְכַתְּחִלָּה בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאִם נָפַל — לֹא תַּחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** She may go out on Shabbat **with pepper, or with a grain of salt, or anything placed in her mouth** for healing or for preventing bad odor, **as long as she does not put** these objects in her mouth **for the first time on Shabbat. And if it fell out she may not replace it.**

The *Mishna* continues: a woman may go out with a cloth in her ear — presumably for an ear ailment — with a cloth in her sandal to protect against calluses, or with a cloth prepared for her *niddah* flow. The last of these raises a question in the early authorities. Rashi explains the purpose as preventing her garments from becoming soiled. Tosafot objects: an item meant to protect a garment and not the body does not have the status of clothing and should not be permitted. Tosafot therefore understands the cloth as protecting the woman's body directly, which gives it the character of a worn item and makes it permissible.

### Mouth Items

The *Mishna* further permits a woman to go out with pepper, salt, or similar items in her mouth, apparently as a remedy for bad breath. The formulation *ve-chol davar she-nittan le-toch piha* — anything placed in her mouth — suggests a broad category.

### Mouth Items Restriction

However, the *Mishna* immediately qualifies this: she may not place these items in her mouth *le-chatchila* on Shabbat itself, and if something fell out, she may not replace it. Tosafot offers two explanations for this restriction. One is that placing a remedy in one's mouth on Shabbat resembles taking medicine, and taking medicine on Shabbat is rabbinically prohibited. Tosafot himself finds this explanation difficult, because if the concern were medicine, one would presumably be permitted to replace a remedy that was already in use before Shabbat began, yet the *Mishna* forbids even that. The second explanation is therefore more convincing to Tosafot: the concern is not medicine but appearance — people who see a woman place something in her mouth and then step outside will suspect she is simply finding a clever method to carry something. This distinction between the two explanations has practical ramifications for how we approach things like mouthwash on Shabbat — whether they fall into a category of medicine or of something else entirely.

### False Tooth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁן תּוֹתֶבֶת, שֵׁן שֶׁל זָהָב — רַבִּי מַתִּיר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **A false tooth** as well as (Ramban) **a gold tooth, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **permits** going out with it, **and the Rabbis prohibit** doing so.

Finally, a woman may go out wearing a false tooth. A golden tooth, however, is subject to a dispute: Rabbi permits it, while the Sages prohibit it — apparently because a golden tooth is something she might be inclined to remove and show her friends in the middle of the street, which would constitute carrying.

## Hair Categories Logic

### All Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן דִּידַהּ מִשּׁוּום דְּלָא מְאִיס, אֲבָל חֲבֶירְתַּהּ דִּמְאִיס — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that a woman may go out with different strands of hair. The Gemara comments: **And it is necessary** to cite all of the cases. **If** the mishna **taught us only** with regard to **her own** hair, I would have said that she may go out with it **because it is not repulsive,** as it is her own hair; therefore, there is no concern lest she come to remove the strands and carry them in the public domain. **However,** the hair **of another, which is repulsive** and a different color from hers, **say no,** she may not go out with it, due to concern lest she be embarrassed, remove it, and come to carry it in the public domain.

The Gemara asks why all three categories of hair extension must be specified separately — the woman's own cut hair, another woman's hair, and animal hair. Could one not have been inferred from another?

### Age Mismatch Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן דַּחֲבֶירְתַּהּ — דְּבַת מִינַהּ הוּא, אֲבָל דִּבְהֵמָה לָאו בַּר מִינַהּ הוּא אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna **taught us** that she is permitted to go out with the hair **of another,** I would have said that she may go out with it because **it is** hair **of her own kind.** Therefore, it is not repulsive in her eyes and she will not come to remove it. **However,** the hair **of an animal,** since **it is not of her own kind, say no,** she may not go out with it due to concern lest she remove it. Therefore, **it is necessary** to cite all three cases.

The Gemara constructs a series of arguments to show why each case requires an independent ruling. If only the woman's own hair were mentioned, one might think it is permitted because wearing one's own hair is natural and unsurprising — *d'lo ma'is*, it is not unattractive or incongruous. But another woman's hair might strike people as odd or inappropriate, and she might therefore be embarrassed by it and inclined to remove it in public. Conversely, if only another woman's hair were mentioned as permissible — because at least it is human hair — one might think animal hair, which is from a different species, would generate more embarrassment and a greater likelihood of removal. Each case addresses a distinct concern. The Gemara then specifies a further nuance: a young woman wearing an old woman's white hair, or an old woman wearing a young woman's darker hair, raises its own concerns about color mismatch and the social pressure to remove the incongruous hair.

### Resolution Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תֵּצֵא יַלְדָּה בְּשֶׁל זְקֵנָה וּזְקֵנָה בְּשֶׁל יַלְדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in the *Tosefta*: It is permitted **as long as a girl does not go out with** the hair **of an elderly woman, and an elderly woman** does not go out **with** the hair **of a girl.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא זְקֵנָה בְּשֶׁל יַלְדָּה — שֶׁבַח הוּא לָהּ. אֶלָּא יַלְדָּה בְּשֶׁל זְקֵנָה אַמַּאי? גְּנַאי הוּא לָהּ! אַיְּידִי דִּתְנָא זְקֵנָה בְּשֶׁל יַלְדָּה, תְּנָא נָמֵי יַלְדָּה בְּשֶׁל זְקֵנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted,** the Gemara cited the case of **an elderly woman** who goes out **with** the hair **of a girl,** as it is a reasonable scenario because **it is flattering for her** to look young. **However, why** would **a girl** go out **with** the hair **of an elderly woman?** Since **it is demeaning for her** to appear elderly, it is an unlikely scenario. The Gemara answers: **Since** the mishna **taught** the case of **an elderly woman with** the hair **of a girl, it also taught** the improbable case of **a girl with** the hair **of an elderly woman.**

The Gemara resolves which of these cases is the non-obvious one requiring explicit mention. It is obvious that an old woman wearing a young woman's hair — which looks flattering — might lead her to show it off and remove it. Less obvious is that a young woman wearing an old woman's hair — which is incongruous and unflattering — would also be prohibited. One might think that since it looks so unattractive on her, she would never be motivated to show it off to friends. The *braita* must therefore tell us explicitly: even there, the concern applies, perhaps because the embarrassment of being seen with it creates a different motivation to remove it. The reason the *braita* mentions both: *aidi de-tani zeikeinah be-shel yaldah, tana nami yaldah be-shel zeikeinah* — since it teaches the case of the old woman wearing young hair, it teaches the reverse as well, even though one is more obvious than the other.

## Courtyard Debate

### Rav's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכָבוּל וּבְפֵאָה נׇכְרִית לֶחָצֵר. אָמַר רַב: כׇּל שֶׁאָסְרוּ חֲכָמִים לָצֵאת בּוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אָסוּר לָצֵאת בּוֹ לֶחָצֵר — חוּץ מִכָּבוּל וּפֵאָה נׇכְרִית.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that a woman may go out **with a woolen cap or with a wig to the courtyard. Rav said:** With regard to **all** ornaments and garments with **which the Sages prohibited going out into the public domain** on Shabbat, **it is** also **prohibited to go out with** them **into the courtyard** due to the concern lest she forget and go out to the street, **with the exception of a woolen cap and a wig.**

The *Mishna* specifies that the *kavul* and *pea nachrit* are permissible *la-chatzer* — in the courtyard. This formulation prompts the Gemara to ask what exactly the courtyard's status is vis-à-vis everything else discussed in the chapter. Rav rules: *kol she-asru chachamim la-tzeis bo le-reshus ha-rabim asur la-tzeis bo la-chatzer* — everything that the Sages forbade in the public domain is likewise forbidden in the courtyard. The concern is that wearing something in the courtyard will lead her to walk out into the street. Rashi specifies that this applies even to a courtyard with an *eruv*.

### Exceptions

The only exceptions are the *kavul* and the *pea nachrit* that the *Mishna* explicitly permits in the courtyard. According to Rav, this means that the general rule — what is forbidden in the public domain is also forbidden in the courtyard — applies to everything discussed in the chapter, and the *Mishna* is specifically carving out these two items as exceptions.

### Anani Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲנָנִי בַּר שָׂשׂוֹן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר: הַכֹּל כְּכָבוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Anani bar Sason said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: All** ornaments have the same legal status **as a woolen cap** and may be worn into the courtyard.

Rabbi Anani bar Sasson, citing Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose, rules differently: *ha-kol ki-kvula* — everything is permitted in the courtyard, just as the *kavul* is. According to this view, the prohibition throughout the chapter was directed at the public domain, and the courtyard was never subject to it. The *Mishna*'s mention of the *kavul* and *pea nachrit* as permissible in the courtyard is not carving out exceptions — it is simply providing examples of the general principle.

### Rav vs. Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: בְּכָבוּל וּבְפֵאָה נׇכְרִית לֶחָצֵר. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב — נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי עֲנָנִי בַּר שָׂשׂוֹן קַשְׁיָא! רַבִּי עֲנָנִי בַּר שָׂשׂוֹן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְמַאן קָאָמַר לֵיהּ? — מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי תַּנָּא הוּא וּפְלִיג.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna that it is permitted to go out **with a woolen cap or a wig into the courtyard. Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav** the matter works out **well,** as the mishna allows one to go out into a courtyard only with a woolen cap and a wig. **However, according to the opinion of Rabbi Anani bar Sason,** it is **difficult.** The Gemara answers: **In whose name did Rabbi Anani bar Sason say** his *halakha*? **In the name of Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosei, and Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosei is a *tanna* and,** as such, has the authority **to dispute** the determination in the mishna.

The Gemara points out the difficulty this creates. If, according to Rabbi Anani, the prohibition was never extended to a courtyard, then mentioning the *kavul* and *pea nachrit* as permissible in the courtyard is redundant — of course they are permitted there, as is everything else. The Gemara resolves this by noting that Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose is a *tanna* who explicitly disagrees with the *Mishna*'s framework. He holds that the entire chapter's prohibitions were limited to the public domain, and the courtyard was always permissible.

### Ulla's Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, מַאי שְׁנָא הָנֵי? אָמַר עוּלָּא: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְגַּנֶּה עַל בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rav, what is different** about **these,** the woolen cap and the wig, that the mishna permitted going out into the courtyard with them? **Ulla said: So that she will not become unappealing to her husband.**

According to Rav, who does distinguish between different items, Ulla explains why specifically the *kavul* and *pea nachrit* are permitted in the courtyard while other items are not: *kedei she-lo titganei al ba'alah* — so that she not appear unattractive to her husband. Even in the courtyard, she must be permitted to wear something presentable, because the alternative poses a risk to the marital relationship.

### Akiva & Shalom Bayit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְהַדָּוָה בְּנִדָּתָהּ״ — זְקֵנִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים אָמְרוּ שֶׁלֹּא תִּכְחוֹל וְלֹא תִּפְקוֹס וְלֹא תִּתְקַשֵּׁט בְּבִגְדֵי צִבְעוֹנִין, עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְלִימֵּד: אִם כֵּן — אַתָּה מְגַנָּהּ עַל בַּעְלָהּ, וְנִמְצָא בַּעְלָהּ מְגָרְשָׁהּ. אֶלָּא מַה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַדָּוָה בְּנִדָּתָהּ״ — בְּנִדָּתָהּ תְּהֵא עַד שֶׁתָּבֹא בַּמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"And of her that is sick in her menstrual status [*niddata*]"** (Leviticus 15:33), **the Elders** of the **early** generations **said** that this verse comes to teach us that the menstruating woman should be distanced from her husband in all senses, like a person ostracized [*menudeh*] by the Sages. This includes **that she may not paint** her eyes **blue**, **and she may not rouge [*pokeset*]** her face, **and she may not adorn herself with colorful clothing. Until Rabbi Akiva came and taught: If** you do **so, you are making her unappealing to her husband, and her husband will** consequently **divorce her.** Therefore, extreme strictures should not be instituted. **Rather, what** is the meaning of that which **the verse states: "And of her that is sick in her menstrual status"? She shall remain** prohibited **in her menstrual status** even after the flow of blood has stopped **until she immerses in the water** of a ritual bath.

This is supported by a *braita*: *ve-ha-davah be-niddata* — regarding a woman who is *niddah*, the early sages ruled that she should not apply eye shadow, rouge, or any jewelry, and should not wear colored garments. But Rabbi Akiva came and taught: if you do this, you will make her repulsive to her husband, and the husband will divorce her. Rather, the verse means only that she retains the legal status of *niddah* until she immerses in the *mikvah* — nothing in the verse mandates making her physically unattractive. Rabbi Akiva's sensitivity here — and his known personal history of a deeply romantic attachment to his wife — lends particular weight to his position. The principle established is clear: *shalom bayit* is a halachic value that limits the application of certain stringencies.

## Later Authorities

### Tosafot's Ruling

Having established the range of opinions within the Gemara, we now turn to how the later authorities resolved the question in practice — and this is where the discussion becomes particularly striking. Tosafot rules in accordance with Rabbi Anani bar Sasson: *nira de-halacha ke-Rav Anani* — it appears that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Anani, that everything is permitted in the courtyard. The reasoning is twofold: Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose is a *tanna* who explicitly argues, and when there is doubt in a *de-rabbanan* matter, we rule leniently.

### Karmelit Solution

Tosafot then takes an additional step. Rashi had understood that the courtyard discussion referred to a courtyard with an *eruv*. Tosafot disagrees: we are speaking even of a courtyard without an *eruv*. And as for the public domain itself — Tosafot acknowledges that throughout this chapter we have learned that certain items may not be worn in a *reshus ha-rabim*, and one might think that even with an *eruv* the concern applies. But Tosafot notes a further consideration: *ein lanu reshus ha-rabim gamur* — we do not have a genuine *reshus ha-rabim* today. All of the streets in our communities are really only a *karmelit*. Since the rules governing a *karmelit* are rabbinic rather than biblical, and since we rule that everything is permissible in a courtyard without an *eruv*, all of our streets may be treated similarly. Women are therefore permitted to wear rings and all jewelry.

### Rabbeinu Tam

Rabbeinu Tam articulates this position explicitly: a courtyard without an *eruv* functions as the baseline permissible domain, and since all of our streets have the status of a *karmelit* — lacking either the width of eighteen *amot* or the 600,000 travelers that would constitute a true *reshus ha-rabim* — everything that would be permitted in a courtyard without an *eruv* is permitted everywhere. This is the classic Rabbeinu Tam move: an established common practice that appears to flout an entire chapter of Gemara is shown, upon analysis, to be technically defensible.

### Shulchan Aruch

The Shulchan Aruch compiles the full range of opinions. First, anything forbidden in the public domain is also forbidden in a courtyard without an *eruv*. Second, Rashi's stricter position that it is forbidden even in a courtyard with an *eruv*. Third, the view of Tosafot that everything is permitted even in a courtyard without an *eruv*. After setting out the positions, the Shulchan Aruch acknowledges the practical reality.

### Reality vs. Halacha

Women routinely wore rings and all manner of jewelry. Three justifications are offered. The first is pragmatic and somewhat uncomfortable: they are simply not going to listen, and since this is rabbinic, it is better that they transgress unknowingly than knowingly — *mutav yihyu shogegot*. The second is the Tosafot-Rabbeinu Tam position: they are acting in accordance with the lenient opinion, which holds that all of our domains are equivalent to a *karmelit* or a courtyard without an *eruv*, and in such domains everything is permitted.

### Rema: Sociological

The Rema adds a third justification that is qualitatively different from the other two — and it is, in the words of the shiur, one that is remarkable to encounter. The original rabbinic concern was that a woman would remove her jewelry in the street to show her friends, and carrying an item that way would be prohibited. The Rema notes: *de-achshav she-shchachi tachshitin ve-yotzim afilu be-chol* — today, jewelry is so common that women wear it even on weekdays. *Ve-leika le-michash dilma shalfa u-machvya* — there is therefore no longer any reason to fear she will remove it to display it. *Kemo be-yameihem she-lo hayu regilim latzeis bahem rak be-Shabbat* — in earlier times, women only wore jewelry on Shabbat, and the novelty made it something one might show off. But when jewelry is an ordinary part of daily dress, no one removes it in the middle of the street.

This reasoning is of an entirely different character from the *karmelit* solution. The *karmelit* argument leaves the structure of the rabbinic prohibition in place and finds a jurisdictional escape. The Rema's argument says the prohibition itself no longer applies — not because of any technical legal consideration, but because the sociological reality that grounded the original decree no longer exists. Changed social conditions have changed the *halacha*.

The Shulchan Aruch closes by noting that all of this applies to the rabbinic prohibitions. Anything the Gemara treats as a biblical concern — items considered carrying rather than wearing — obviously remains prohibited regardless of sociological shifts or the *karmelit* analysis. But in practice, the entire chapter we have been studying was, as the Rema observes, effectively superseded by changed realities, and the later authorities worked — through a combination of legal reasoning and honest acknowledgment of practice — to bring the two into alignment.
