# Menachot 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Owners & Kohanim

### Tenufah Intro

The daf opens mid-*mishna*, picking up from 61b at the passage beginning *v'imurei shalmei yachid* — the *imurim* of a private *shelamim* together with the *chazeh* and *shok*, which require *tenufah*. The *mishna* had already established the broad framework of *tenufah*: who performs it, who owns the sacrifice, and how the two roles interact. The broader Talmudic discussion has emphasized throughout that the *torah* places the obligation squarely on the owners, while the *gemara* also established that the *kohanim* place their hands underneath the owners' hands during the waving.

### Tosafot Chatzitzah

Tosafot addresses the question of why the *kohen*'s hands do not constitute a *chatzitzah* — an interposition — between the owner and the offering. One possibility Tosafot entertains is that the owners' hands are positioned at the outer rim, with the *kohanim* below. Another suggestion is that the *kohanim* are placed directly beneath the owners. Either way, Tosafot offers an additional resolution: since the primary performer of the *tenufah* is ultimately the owner, the *kohen*'s involvement is secondary and does not constitute a *chatzitzah* in the legally disqualifying sense. This point bears directly on the *gemara* passage we are about to examine.

### Women & Non-Jews

The *mishna* states that even a woman's *korban* requires *tenufah*, yet it qualifies this by saying *b'yisrael v'lo ba'acherim* — among Jews, not others. The plain reading of this exclusion refers to non-Jews, a category whose relationship to various Temple obligations — *hagashah*, *semicha*, *levona*, and the like — has already been discussed in preceding pages. The *gemara* opens by asking: *mai ka'amar* — what precisely does this phrase mean?

### Rav Yehuda

Rav Yehuda reinterprets the *mishna* as follows: both men's and women's *korbanot* require *tenufah* — *korbanan ta'un tenufah*. The *mishna* is not saying that the woman herself performs the waving. Rather, the actual act of *tenufah* — *tenufas atzmah* — is performed only by *yisrael* and not by women. This reading introduces a sharp distinction: the *korban* of a woman obligates *tenufah*, but the woman cannot be the one to perform it.

This creates an immediately notable tension. The category *yisrael* is here being used in contrast to *nashim*, a usage one finds elsewhere in the *beit hamikdash* terminology — the *ezrat nashim* is contrasted not with an *ezrat gvarim* but with the *ezrat yisrael*, which is itself a striking linguistic fact. Beyond that, the substance of the ruling raises an obvious question: if the entire point of *tenufah* is the owner's presentation of the offering to God — as the *torah* itself emphasizes with the phrase *yadav taviena l'hanifo oso*, "his hands shall bring it to wave it" — how can the *kohen* perform it on the woman's behalf?

## Women Excluded

### Braita Text

A *braita* is cited: *bnei yisrael menifim v'ein ovdei kochavim menifim, bnei yisrael menifim v'ein hanashim menifot* — Jews perform *tenufah*, not non-Jews; Jews perform *tenufah*, not women.

### Rav Yoshiya Q

Rav Yoshiya immediately raises what is the natural challenge to this ruling. We have a precedent, he notes, in *semicha*: the *torah* distinguished between the *korban* of a male Jew, that of a non-Jew, and that of a woman in the context of *semicha*, such that women's *korbanot* and non-Jews' *korbanot* do not require *semicha*. Should we not similarly conclude that a woman's *korban* requires no *tenufah* at all — meaning not that someone else does it, but that the obligation is altogether absent, just as *semicha* is absent?

---

## Women Excluded…

### Gemara Response

The *gemara* rejects this line of reasoning by drawing a fundamental distinction between *semicha* and *tenufah*. When it comes to *semicha*, the act is performed exclusively by the owners — there is no other participant. If women cannot perform *semicha*, then for women's *korbanot* there is simply no *semicha* at all. The logic of exclusion in *semicha* reflects the nature of *semicha* itself: it is an act of ownership designation, and if the owner cannot do it, nobody can. *Tenufah*, by contrast, is an act in which the *kohanim* participate alongside the owners. Since *tenufah* involves the *kohanim* as well, it remains possible to exclude women from the act while still requiring *tenufah* for their *korbanot* — the *kohen* can perform it on the woman's behalf.

This distinction maps onto a deeper conceptual difference between the two acts. *Semicha* is fundamentally about symbolizing ownership — the owner leans on the animal, identifying it as his. If the owner cannot perform it, the act loses its entire meaning, and there is nothing left to do. *Tenufah*, however, is a ritual that belongs to the broader process of *hakrava*, of bringing the offering before God. While the *torah* emphasizes the owner's role, *tenufah* is not exclusively an ownership act in the same way *semicha* is. Many of the core acts of *hakrava* are performed by *kohanim*, and so it is conceptually coherent to say the waving is done by the *kohanim* without violating the nature of the rite.

A word should be said here about Jacob Milgrom's analysis, which is directly relevant. On *semicha* specifically, Milgrom argues — consistent with what the *gemara* itself says — that *semicha* is fundamentally an act of asserting ownership. He supports this from the pattern of which sacrifices do *not* require *semicha*: a *mincha*, a bird-offering, and a *korban tzibbur*. A *mincha* or bird-offering can be held entirely in one's hand — the ownership is manifest in the very act of holding it, so no additional symbolic gesture is needed. An animal walked into the *beit hamikdash* is no longer obviously "yours" in the same tactile sense, and so *semicha* serves to assert: this is my *korban*. A *korban tzibbur*, by definition, involves no single private owner to assert ownership, so *semicha* is simply irrelevant. Milgrom does not cite the *gemara*, but his approach aligns closely with how it frames the question here.

### Gender Patterns

It is worth pausing to note a recurring pattern: whenever one encounters a direct, tactile contact between a person and the *korban* in a ritual act, that is precisely where women are being excluded. The same dynamic appears in both *semicha* and *tenufah*. There is clearly a common denominator at work, a structural limitation on women's direct ritual engagement with the *korban*. The question of whether women are *permitted* — even if not *obligated* — to perform *semicha* is itself a *tannaitic* dispute, and the matter is similarly debated in the *braita* regarding *tenufah*.

### Women in Temple

What seems to be reflected here is a broader sociological reality of the *beit hamikdash*: women had access to the Temple precincts, but their designated space — the *ezrat nashim* — was explicitly distinct from the *ezrat yisrael*, the space of primary ritual activity. The very contrast in terminology between *yisrael* and *nashim* in the Temple's architecture mirrors the contrast one finds in these legal texts. Women could bring *korbanot*, but they were not regarded as primary participants in the *azara*'s ritual activity. This is an uncomfortable reality to sit with, and it has obvious implications: the model of *mechitza* based on the *ezrat nashim* is, as a result, a deeply problematic model — it encodes not merely separation but a hierarchy of access to the sacred space. The *gemara*'s language here reflects that hierarchy directly.

## Owners & Kohanim…

### Semicha vs Tenufah

The *gemara*, having established this conceptual divide, now proceeds to describe how the *tenufah* is physically performed. The *kohen* places his hands beneath the hands of the owners — *kohen mani'ach yado tachat yad habe'alim u'menif* — and both wave together. Whether the *kohanim* are positioned directly beneath or slightly adjacent, the combined act constitutes a valid *tenufah*.

The key asymmetry remains: because *semicha* is exclusively an act of the owners, excluding a category of people from *semicha* eliminates *semicha* for their *korban* entirely. Because *tenufah* involves the *kohanim* as co-participants, excluding a category of people from *performing* *tenufah* does not eliminate *tenufah* from their *korban* — the *kohen* steps in as the performing agent.

### Milgrom Analysis

Milgrom's treatment of *tenufah* itself is illuminating. He notes that the *torah* at times interchanges *tenufah* and *terumah*, as in the phrase *chazeh hatnufa veshok hatruma*. His reading is that *terumah* is essentially a verbal or conceptual designation of something as given to God — it is the act of separating and declaring, which can be accomplished without a physical gesture. *Tenufah* functions similarly, but is always *lifnei Hashem* — performed within the sacred precincts — and is therefore expressed through a physical act of elevation and waving rather than verbal designation. The reason *tenufah* is required, he argues, is because these items — particularly the *kodashim kalim* — are not fully designated to God from the outset. With a *shelamim*, for example, the *gemara* elsewhere teaches that there is no *me'ila* on *kodashim kalim* until after *shechita* and until the *eimurim* are placed on the *mizbe'ach*, because until that point the offering remains substantially under the owner's domain. *Tenufah* serves as the symbolic act of taking something that was the owner's and formally presenting it to God.

This reading aligns well with the *torah*'s insistence on *yadav taviena* — the owner's hands bring it — and explains why *tenufah* is demanded precisely for those items that are not yet fully removed from private ownership. Milgrom does acknowledge that the *menachot* associated with the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* are *kodshei kodashim* and seem to present a more complex case, but his general thesis is that the essence of *tenufah* is this act of transfer — from the human owner to God — carried out through physical gesture in the sacred space. That being said, the *gemara* here does two things simultaneously: it reads the *mishna* as limiting women's participation in *tenufah* (a reading that is, as noted, at least debatable as *pshat* in the *mishna* itself, though supported by the *braitot*), and then justifies that reading by contrasting *tenufah* with *semicha* in a way that, arguably, sits in tension with Milgrom's emphasis on *tenufah* as ownership-transfer.

## Converts & Slaves

### Derivation

A second *braita* is introduced: *bnei yisrael* — is this the only category included? What about converts and freed slaves, who are now fully Jewish? The *braita* derives their inclusion from the word *hamakriv* — "the one who brings" — which appears in the relevant *pasuk* and is understood as broadening the category beyond *bnei yisrael* in its narrowest reading. Yet one might counter that *hamakriv* refers to the *kohen* who offers the sacrifice on the *mizbe'ach*, not to the owner at all. The *braita* resolves this by noting that the *pasuk* also says *yadav taviena* — "his hands shall bring it" — which clearly refers to the owner, not the *kohen*. Therefore *hamakriv* must be extending the category of owners to include converts and freed slaves.

### Tosafot Q

Tosafot raises a sharp question here: ordinarily, we assume that converts and freed slaves are included in any obligation that applies to Jews. Why would we need a special derivation to include them? Tosafot's answer is that the *gemara* is operating from a maximally narrow baseline assumption — that by default, only a naturally born Jewish male is included, requiring explicit derivations to extend the obligation to converts, freed slaves, or anyone else. The inclusivity of converts and freed slaves here is thus not taken for granted but affirmatively derived.

## Physical Tenufah

### Parts Arrange

The *gemara* now describes the physical mechanics of the *tenufah* when performed on the actual sacrificial components of a *shelamim* — the *eimurim* (fats), *chazeh*, and *shok*. The *eimurim* are placed on the owner's palm — directly on the hand, since this is not done with a *kli sharet* — and the *chazeh* and *shok* are placed on top of the *eimurim*. One naturally wonders both about the practical challenges of this arrangement and why the *eimurim* go below rather than above.

### Bread Role

Whenever bread accompanies the *chazeh* and *shok* in the *tenufah* — as occurred at the *miluim*, the inaugural offerings — the bread is placed on top of everything else, above the meat. The *gemara* attributes this observation to Rav Pappa, who identifies the *miluim* as the one instance in which a *shelamim* is waved together with bread, making it the model case for this arrangement.

### Pesuk Conflict

The rationale for placing the *eimurim* below is drawn from a *pasuk*: *shok haterumah v'chazeh hatnufah al ishei hachalavim yavi'u* — the thigh and breast shall be brought *on* the fire-offerings of the fats, indicating that the meat goes above and the fats below. But a second *pasuk* states *et hachalavim al hachazeh yevi'enu* — bring the fats *on* the breast — which would seem to reverse the arrangement. The two *pesukim* directly contradict each other on which element is on top.

### Abaye Flip

Abaye resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between two stages of the process. When the *kohen* first brings the pieces from the slaughterhouse to the owner, the meat is on the bottom and the fats are on top — this satisfies the second *pasuk*. When it is then placed in the owner's hands for *tenufah*, the arrangement is flipped: fats below, meat above — satisfying the first *pasuk*. A third *pasuk* — *vayashimu et hachalavim al hachazot*, the fats were placed on the breasts — describes yet a further stage: after *tenufah*, the pieces are handed to another *kohen* to bring to the *mizbe'ach*, and at that point they are again inverted so that the fats end up on top, as befits their being placed on the *mizbe'ach*'s fire. The practical logic is clear: the fats, which are destined for the *mizbe'ach*, should be on top when approaching it so that they can be placed on the fire without needing to extract them from under the meat.

### Three Kohanim

The *gemara* notes an implication of Abaye's three-stage solution: the process involves three separate *kohanim*, one for each stage of handling. The *gemara* endorses this not merely as a technical consequence but as a value in itself, grounded in the principle of *berov am hadrat melech* — "in the multitude of people is the king's glory." It is preferable to involve more participants in a sacred act, as it increases the honor rendered.

## Shtei Halechem

### Source Context

The *mishna* had stated that the two loaves of *shavuot* — the *shtei halechem* — together with the two lambs are waved by the *kohen*. The *torah* describes this as *v'henif hakohen otam al lechem habikkurim*, waving them together with the bread of the first fruits.

### Pesuk Ambig

A *braita* is introduced that surfaces a contradiction in the relevant *pesukim* parallel to the one we just saw regarding the *eimurim* and meat. One *pasuk* seems to indicate that the lambs are placed on the bread — *yachol yanich kevasim al gabei halechem*. Another *pasuk* states *al shnei kevasim* — which would imply the bread is placed on the lambs. The text is thus, in the *braita*'s words, *nishtakel hakatuv* — the *pasuk* is evenly weighted, pointing in both directions simultaneously, and it is impossible to determine from the *pasuk* itself which element goes on top.

The *braita* therefore turns to a normative principle: wherever else we find bread waved together with other sacrificial items, the bread goes on top. Following Rav Pappa's earlier identification, the model case is again the *miluim*, where the *torah* places the bread last in the sequence before saying *v'hanafta otam tenufah lifnei Hashem* — and the *chachamim* understood from this that the bread is on top. So here too, the default should be bread on top.

This resolves which arrangement is standard, but it does not explain why the *torah* uses contradictory language. The three *tannaim* who follow take different approaches precisely to address this.

### Rabbi Yosei

Rabbi Yosei ben Hamishulam maintains that the lambs go on top. He preserves this position by reading *al shnei kevasim* — "on the two lambs" — not as a physical description of placement but as a relational statement: the bread is designated as coming *together with*, or *connected to*, the two lambs specifically, as distinct from the other seven lambs brought as part of the *musaf* offerings on *shavuot*. The *torah* is establishing a unique pairing — the *shtei halechem* are linked specifically to the *shnei kevasim* and not to the larger collection of animals. The word *al* here means "in relation to" or "connected to," not "on top of." This reading reconciles the contradictory *pesukim* while preserving the lambs-on-top arrangement.

### Chanani Sandwich

Chanani ben Chakinai proposes a more creative solution. The *shtei halechem* are placed between the thighs of the lambs — *bein yarchoteihem shel kevasim*. This arrangement physically satisfies both *pesukim* simultaneously: the bread is *on* the lambs because it rests between a thigh above, and the lambs are *on* the bread because the other thigh lies above the other loaf. Both statements are true simultaneously because the bread is sandwiched.

### Decorum Obj

Rabbi rejects Chanani's solution firmly. *Lifnei melech basar vadam ein osim ken* — one would never present a gift to a human king by stuffing the bread between the legs of the animals. Certainly, *lifnei melech malchei hamelachim Hakadosh Baruch Hu al achat kama v'khamah* — before the King of kings this is all the more inappropriate. Decorum before God demands a more dignified arrangement.

### Rabbi Side

Rabbi's own position is that the bread and lambs are placed side by side — *mani'ach zeh betzad zeh u'menif* — and waved together in that configuration. The word *al* in both *pesukim* does not mean "on top of" but rather "adjacent to" or "beside." Both *pesukim* can therefore be read as saying that each is placed beside the other, without physical layering. This removes the contradiction entirely.

### Al Meaning

The *gemara* then cites Rav Chisda and Rav Hamnuna (with the standard note that it is uncertain which one said it to the other) to the effect that Rabbi is consistent with his broader linguistic position: *Rebbi letamei*, Rabbi goes according to his own reasoning, for Rabbi elsewhere interprets *al* as meaning *besamuch* — nearby, not literally on top.

The proof-text is drawn from the *lechem hapanim*: the *pasuk* says *v'natata al hamareches levona zakha*, "you shall place *on* the arrangement *levona*." But the *levona* is not placed on top of the stacked loaves — it is placed on the table next to them. There, *al* clearly means adjacent. Rabbi adds another proof: *v'sakota al ha'aron et haparochet* — "the curtain shall provide shade *on* the *aron*." The *parochet* does not lie literally on top of the *aron*; it stands beside it and casts its shade upon it. Here too *al* means proximity rather than direct contact. This confirms that Rabbi's reading of *al* as *besamuch* has consistent textual grounding.

## Tenufah Motions

### Cosmic Offering

The *gemara* turns to the physical motions of *tenufah*: *moli'ach u'mevi ma'aleh u'morid* — forward and back, up and down. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the forward-and-back motion is directed toward *lemi she-haruchot shelo* — the One who controls the winds; the up-and-down motion toward *lemi she-hashamayim ve-ha'aretz shelo* — the One who controls heaven and earth. This framing aligns naturally with Milgrom's understanding: *tenufah* is fundamentally an act of giving something over to God, and the waving in all directions — toward every wind, toward heaven and earth — is a physical enactment of that total dedication. The directions of the motion are a kind of cosmic presentation.

### Bad Weather

The *gemara* then introduces an alternative formulation from the West, *Eretz Yisrael*, attributed to Rabbi Chama bar Ukva in the name of Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina. In this version, the forward-and-back motion is performed *kedei latzor ruchot ra'ot* — in order to restrain the bad winds; the up-and-down motion *kedei latzor tlalim ra'im* — to restrain the bad rains (*tlalim* here carrying the sense of adverse precipitation generally). This is a notably different emphasis: rather than a theological statement about giving to God, the waving is here understood as having an apotropaic, protective function — warding off destructive natural forces.

This framing is striking, even somewhat surprising. One might have expected the formulation to be positive — praying for good winds, for beneficent rains. Why is it cast in the negative, as a protection against harm? This question deserves serious attention. One possible answer: the *shtei halechem* and the *omer* are agricultural sacrifices, which naturally carry a double valence — gratitude for the present harvest together with an implicit petition for the future. And *tenufah* in this context may encode both. Yet the *gemara* frames it specifically as preventative.

### Remnant Value

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Avin draws a striking practical lesson from this: *zot omeret she'arei mitzvah me'akvin et hapuranuyot* — from here we learn that even the "remnants" of a *mitzvah*, the supplementary elements of an obligation rather than its core, are sufficient to forestall punishment and hold back destructive forces. *Tenufah* is not required for one to have fulfilled the sacrificial obligation — it is, in some sense, an addendum to the *korban*. Yet even this element, precisely because it functions as a *mitzvah*, carries protective power. The lesson extends far beyond the *beit hamikdash*: even partial or secondary observances carry weight and matter.

### Lulav Model

Rava extends the principle: *v'chen lulav* — the same holds for the lulav waving on *sukkot*. The same motions, in the same directions, carry the same meaning and the same preventative effect against bad winds and rains. This connection is natural: *sukkot* is the other great agricultural festival, the time when the liturgy shifts to *mashiv haru'ach u'morid hagashem* and when the communal *bakashah* for rain begins. The *tenufah* of the *shtei halechem* and the *omer* serves as the template — the halachic and conceptual model — for the waving of the *lulav*.

The emphasis on averting the negative rather than soliciting the positive may also be better understood in the *sukkot* context: at that season the concern is precisely about timing and excess — one does not want rain during *sukkot* itself, as it would be a sign of divine displeasure, even as one prays for it to come in its proper season thereafter. Perhaps that is why the tradition articulates the gesture as warding off harm rather than inviting blessing.

### Rav Acha

Rav Acha bar Yaakov would wave his *lulav* with an aggressive stabbing or thrusting motion — *mamti lei u'mayti lei* — and would declare: *giri be'einei d'sitna* — "an arrow in the eyes of the *satan*." The image is vivid: he is wielding the *lulav* as a weapon against the evil force, not merely performing a liturgical gesture. The *gemara*, however, rejects this approach: *v'lav milta hi* — this is not the right way to do it, because *mishum d'ati l'igruyei bei* — it is liable to provoke and incite the *satan* rather than neutralize him. One does not pick a direct fight with demonic forces; caution and indirection are the appropriate posture. The mention of *ruach ra'ah* — the bad wind or evil spirit — in this context connects to the same vocabulary used in discussions of *netilat yadayim* and similar protective rituals, where *ruach ra'ah* denotes a demonic presence rather than merely a natural force.

## Communal Tenufah

### Communal Rule

A *braita* teaches that the *zivchei shalmei tzibbur* — the communal *shelamim*, which refers exclusively to the *shnei kevasim* brought with the *shtei halechem* on *shavuot* — require *tenufah* both before and after *shechita*. The pre-*shechita* waving is explicit in the *pesukim* — it is the waving of the live lambs together with the bread that we have been discussing. The post-*shechita* waving is the subject of the *braita*: *te'unin tenufah l'achar shechitah*, and it is done *kemot shehen* — with the animal as it is, meaning the full carcass. This is the position of Rabbi. The *chachamim* disagree, holding that after *shechita* one waves only the *chazeh* and *shok*, as with a private *shelamim*.

The disagreement is immediately puzzling on its face. With a standard private *shelamim*, the *kohen* receives the *chazeh* and *shok* while the *mizbe'ach* receives the *chelev*. The *chazeh* and *shok* are thus distinct and distinctive — they are the portion that goes to the *kohen*, and waving them enacts the transfer. But with the *shnei kevasim*, the *kohanim* eat the entire animal. There is nothing halachically distinctive about the *chazeh* and *shok* here. So the *chachamim*'s position of limiting the post-*shechita* waving to *chazeh* and *shok* seems difficult to justify.

### Hermeneutic Q

Rav Chisda and Rav Hamnuna (again with uncertainty as to which one addressed which) identify the debate as turning on a general hermeneutic question: *don minah u'minah* versus *don minah v'okei b'asrah*. When one derives a law for case A from case B, does one also import the details and context of case B (*don minah u'minah* — learn from it and from it), or does one derive only the general principle from B and then apply that principle within the natural context of A (*don minah v'okei b'asrah* — learn from it and establish it in its place)?

### Rabbi vs Sages

This framing sets up the two positions. The source from which the post-*shechita* waving of the *shnei kevasim* is derived is the private *shelamim*, which also requires *tenufah* after *shechita*. The question is: when we apply that principle to the *shnei kevasim*, do we take along the full context of the private *shelamim* (including the limitation to *chazeh* and *shok*), or do we merely carry over the principle (post-*shechita* waving is required) and then determine its parameters according to the internal logic of the *shnei kevasim*?

### Sages Logic

The *chachamim* hold *don minah u'minah*: learn the law from the private *shelamim* and take the full context along. Just as there the waving is limited to *chazeh* and *shok*, so too here. The original context follows the rule into its new setting.

### Rabbi Logic

Rabbi holds *don minah v'okei b'asrah*: derive the principle — post-*shechita* waving — from the private *shelamim*, but then establish its application within the natural logic of the *shnei kevasim*. What is the underlying principle that determines *which parts* get waved in a private *shelamim*? It is not an arbitrary selection: one waves the portion being given to the *kohen*. That is the internal logic of the private *shelamim*'s *tenufah*. Apply that logic in the context of the *shnei kevasim*, where everything goes to the *kohanim*, and you wave the entire animal. Rabbi's position thus has the advantage of coherence — it makes the same principle operative in both cases, applying it to the facts on the ground in each context.

### Rav Papa

Rav Pappa offers a different reading of the dispute. He argues that in fact everyone holds *don minah u'minah* — everyone agrees one derives the law together with its original context. The question is: what exactly is the "context" of the private *shelamim's tenufah*? If one reads the context narrowly — the specific parts waved are *chazeh* and *shok* — then one limits the *shnei kevasim* post-*shechita* waving to *chazeh* and *shok*. But if one reads the context more deeply — the underlying principle is that one waves whatever goes to the *kohen* — then applying *that* context to the *shnei kevasim* yields waving of the entire animal, since the entire animal goes to the *kohanim*. According to Rav Pappa, this is what Rabbi is doing: he applies *don minah u'minah* but reads the contextual principle at a deeper level of abstraction, and at that level the principle leads to waving the whole animal.

### Ravina

Ravina proposes the opposite resolution. According to Ravina, everyone actually holds *don minah v'okei b'asrah* — everyone derives the principle from the private *shelamim* and then applies it in the new context of the *shnei kevasim*. Placing the law in the context of the *shnei kevasim*, where the entire animal goes to the *kohanim*, should by rights lead everyone to wave the whole animal. So why do the *chachamim* limit it to *chazeh* and *shok*? Because there is an explicit *pasuk* that narrows it: *et chazeh hatnufah v'et shok haterumah lakachti mei'et bnei yisrael mizivchei shalmeihem* — "from their *shelamim*" — the use of the plural *shalmeihem* is understood to include *shalmei tzibbur* within the *pasuk*'s scope, explicitly restricting the post-*shechita* waving of all *shelamim* — private and communal alike — to *chazeh* and *shok*. Rabbi, who disagrees, does not accept this derivation. Of the two positions, Rabbi's is conceptually the more coherent: it is strange to limit the *shnei kevasim*'s post-*shechita* waving to *chazeh* and *shok* when those parts have no distinctive status here.

## Three Types

### Framework

Rabbi Shimon introduces a systematic framework. There are three categories of sacrifices, each possessing two of three possible ritual characteristics — *semicha*, *tenufah chaim* (waving while alive), and *tenufah shechutim* (waving after *shechita*) — but lacking the third. No single category has all three.

### Private

The private *shelamim* requires *semicha*, which is performed while the animal is alive, and *tenufah* after *shechita* — the waving of the *chazeh* and *shok*. It does not require *tenufah* while alive.

### Communal

The *shnei kevasim* of *shavuot* require *tenufah* both while alive — as explicitly stated in the *torah* — and after *shechita*, as derived from the private *shelamim*. They do not require *semicha*, since *semicha* is categorically inapplicable to communal sacrifices.

### Asham

The *asham* of the *metzora* requires *semicha* — because it is a private *korban* — and *tenufah* while alive — as stated in the *torah*. It does not require *tenufah* after *shechita*: it is an *asham*, not a *shelamim*, and so the derivation that extends post-*shechita* waving from the private *shelamim* to the communal *shnei kevasim* does not reach it.

### Kal Vachomer

The *gemara* attempts to derive each missing characteristic by *kal vachomer*. Take the private *shelamim*: it has *semicha* but no *tenufah chaim*. The communal *shnei kevasim* have *tenufah chaim* but no *semicha*. A *kal vachomer* suggests: if the communal sacrifice, which lacks the "weight" of *semicha*, nevertheless requires *tenufah chaim*, surely the private *shelamim*, which has the additional stringency of *semicha*, should also require *tenufah chaim*. Similarly, one can attempt the reverse: if the private *shelamim*, lacking *tenufah chaim*, still has *semicha*, surely the communal *shnei kevasim*, which have *tenufah chaim*, should also have *semicha*.

### Pesuk Limits

Both *kal vachomer* arguments are rejected by explicit *pesukim*. In the case of *tenufah chaim* for the private *shelamim*: the *pasuk* describing the waving of the live *shnei kevasim* uses the word *otam* — "them" — which is interpreted as an exclusion: only they are waved alive, not the analogous private *shelamim*. In the case of *semicha* for the communal sacrifice: the *gemara* cites the tradition — *gimiri* — that there are only two instances of *semicha* on communal sacrifices, and the *shnei kevasim* on *shavuot* is not among them. The general rule is that *semicha* does not apply to communal sacrifices, with the notable exceptions of the *par he'elem davar shel tzibbur* — the bull brought when the *sanhedrin* rules incorrectly — and the *sa'ir hamishtale'ach*, the scapegoat of *yom kippur*. Rashi identifies these two exceptions. What they share is not ownership per se but a dimension of collective *sin* and *responsibility*: the *zikney ha'edah* perform *semicha* on the communal bull as an expression of their accountability for having misled the public, and the *sa'ir hamishtale'ach* involves a direct transfer of sin. These are structurally different from ordinary communal *musaf* offerings, where there is no ownership to assert and no sin to transfer. The system is therefore not arbitrary: *semicha* tracks meaning, and where the relevant meaning is absent, *semicha* is absent.

## Partnership

### Partners

A *braita* concludes: if five people brought a single *korban* as partners — something possible with a *shelamim*, which may be brought in joint ownership — one person may perform the *tenufah* on behalf of all. The notion that one representative can wave for the group introduces the principle of representation in *tenufah*.

### Women's Rep

From this principle, the *braita* draws a direct implication for women's *korbanot*: *veha'isha, kohen menif al yadeiha* — when a woman brings a *korban*, the *kohen* performs the *tenufah* on her behalf. The parallel to the partnership case is deployed here to justify the *kohen*'s representative role. One should note, however, that the parallel is not perfect: in the partnership case, the person performing *tenufah* is himself one of the owners. The *kohen* performing *tenufah* for the woman is not an owner at all. This is a meaningful leap, though the *gemara* treats it as a natural extension.

### Remote Owner

The principle is extended further still: *v'chein hashole'ach korbanotav mimedinat hayam, kohen menif al yado* — if someone sends a *korban* from overseas, unable to be present, the *kohen* performs *tenufah* on his behalf. This introduces the concept of *shlichut* — representation or agency — in *tenufah*. The logic is consistent: since *tenufah* is not understood as purely an act of ownership-assertion (unlike *semicha*), it can be performed by an agent. The owner's presence, while normally integrated into the act, is not so essential that *tenufah* cannot proceed in his absence. No analogous accommodation exists for *semicha*: if the owner of such a *korban* were overseas and could not perform *semicha*, there would simply be no *semicha*. The contrast once again underscores the different nature of the two acts.

### Tosafot Concern

Tosafot raises a serious concern about the case of the *korban* sent from overseas. *Semicha* is a real obligation for a male owner of an appropriate *korban*. If someone sends his *korban* from *medinat hayam* and the *kohen* performs *tenufah* in his absence, that is unproblematic — *tenufah* can be delegated. But how is it permissible to bring a *korban* that requires *semicha without semicha* having been performed? With women, the answer is that they cannot perform *semicha* and so the obligation is structurally inapplicable to them. But a male owner of a private *shelamim* is obligated in *semicha*. Can he simply send his animal to Yerushalayim without having performed *semicha* on it? Tosafot finds this troubling, noting that other *gemara* passages reflect a real reluctance to bring a *korban* without the owner being physically present to perform *semicha*. He ultimately acknowledges that the practice of sending *korbanot* from abroad is attested, but the tension he identifies is genuine and instructive.

## Milgrom Appendix

### Hagashah Symbol

A final observation, offered as a postscript to the preceding day's material: Milgrom provides a compelling reading of *hagashah* — the presentation of the *mincha* to the *mizbe'ach* — that illuminates Rabbi Shimon's position discussed there. He begins from the literary context in which *mincha* appears in *Vayikra*: the *torah* presents the *mincha* as an offering parallel to the *olah*, essentially a grain substitute when one cannot afford an animal. The *olah* is burned entirely; the *mincha*, by contrast, is only partially burned on the *mizbe'ach* while the remainder is eaten by the *kohanim*. The *hagashah* — the act of pressing the entire *mincha* against the *mizbe'ach* — serves to symbolically place the whole *mincha* on the *mizbe'ach*, demonstrating that conceptually the entire offering is dedicated to God and is as if being consumed by the *mizbe'ach*. The *kohanim*'s subsequent eating of the remainder is, on this reading, analogous to the *mizbe'ach*'s consumption — both partake of what has been fully dedicated.

### No Hagashah

This framework elegantly accounts for Rabbi Shimon's ruling that a *minchat kohanim* — a *mincha* brought by a *kohen*, which is burned entirely with no portion remaining for eating — does not require *hagashah*. The symbolic function of *hagashah* is to demonstrate, through the gesture of placing the whole *mincha* against the *mizbe'ach*, that it is all being given over even though in practice only part will be burned. When the entire *mincha* is in fact being burned, there is nothing to demonstrate — the reality speaks for itself, and the symbolic gesture is unnecessary. The *hagashah* requirement is thus precisely calibrated to cases where the outward act of offering requires this additional declarative moment to clarify what is actually happening.
