# Ketubot 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Onah & Domestic Duties

### Maaseh Yadayim Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹשָׂה בַּצֶּמֶר. בְּצֶמֶר אִין, בְּפִשְׁתִּים לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna says that a wife must **make** thread **from wool.** The Gemara infers: She **must** make thread **from wool,** but she is **not** obligated to do so **from flax.**

The previous discussion addressed *maaseh yadayim* and a wife's domestic duties. The Mishnah now turns to the husband's corresponding obligation: *onah*, the conjugal duty. The Mishnah had first dealt with a man who takes a vow not to have relations with his wife, giving him one or two weeks. It then addresses a list of circumstances in which a husband may be absent from home for extended periods without his wife's express permission — Torah students for a month, workers for a week. Even though a husband's *onah* obligation may be more frequent, occasional travel for business or to visit one's *rebbe* is permitted within certain limits. The Mishnah then enumerates the frequency of *onah* based on a man's profession, concluding with the phrase *divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — a formulation that signals an implicit dispute, to which the Gemara will return.

### Onah Frequency Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹרְחָא דְמִילְּתָא כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב: חֹדֶשׁ כָּאן, וְחֹדֶשׁ בַּבַּיִת. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְכׇל דְּבַר הַמַּחְלְקוֹת הַבָּאָה וְהַיּוֹצֵאת חֹדֶשׁ בְּחֹדֶשׁ לְכֹל חׇדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה״. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חֹדֶשׁ כָּאן וּשְׁנַיִם בְּבֵיתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֹדֶשׁ יִהְיוּ בַלְּבָנוֹן שְׁנַיִם חֳדָשִׁים בְּבֵיתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains its query: Although a man can legally make any agreement with his wife to limit her conjugal rights, **how much** is an acceptable **manner for this matter? Rav said:** The husband may spend **a month here,** in the study hall, **and** then must spend **a month at home.** The allusion to this is **as it is stated** with regard to reserve units serving in King David's army: **"In any matter of the courses, which came in and went out month by month throughout all the months of the year"** (I Chronicles 27:1). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He may spend **one month here,** in the study hall, **and** then **two** months **in his home, as it is stated** with regard to workers who worked in the construction of the Temple: **"A month they were in Lebanon, and two months at home"** (I Kings 5:28).

The Gemara at the opening of 62a — roughly ten lines down at the asterisk — takes up the *orcha d'milsa* passage from earlier: even where a husband has obtained his wife's permission for extended absence, what conduct is appropriate? This question is itself striking, because one might assume that legal permission settles the matter entirely. The Gemara insists otherwise.

### Rashi on Permission

The first *Rashi* on 62a is indispensable here. On the phrase *orcha d'milsa*, Rashi writes: *derech eretz shelo yisa alav cheit, v'afilu hu yachol l'pattosa shetitein lo reshut* — proper conduct demands that he not bear the burden of a sin, even if he is capable of persuading his wife to grant permission. There is permission and there is permission. The husband may be able to talk his wife into consenting to something she would not truly have chosen. Rashi's language — *lo yisa alav cheit* — is powerful: this is not merely a matter of sensitivity or etiquette, but carries the weight of sin. It is an important reminder that *halakha* and morality do not always fully overlap, and that legal entitlement does not exhaust one's moral responsibility.

### Talmud Torah vs Home

The Gemara had already quoted two opinions: Rav's view that a man may be away one month and home one month, and Rabbi Yochanan's view that he may be away one month but must remain home two months. These positions are supported by respective *psukim*. What will emerge across today's *daf* is an ongoing reflection on the tension between a man's obligations at home — whether specifically *onah* or simply his presence and availability to his wife — and his obligation toward *talmud torah*. This tension has contemporary resonance wherever people are consumed by their work or their religious pursuits at the expense of their domestic responsibilities. In the Gemara's context, however, the competing obligations are both religiously freighted, which makes the tension sharper and more interesting. The dynamic will recur throughout the *daf* in different registers, with different voices landing differently on the question of where the balance lies.

## Sighing Breaking the Body

### Rav: Half the Body

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֲנָחָה שׁוֹבֶרֶת חֲצִי גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה בֶן אָדָם הֵאָנַח בְּשִׁבְרוֹן מׇתְנַיִם וּבִמְרִירוּת תֵּאָנַח״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos a dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to the construction of the Temple, the Gemara cites another dispute between them. **Rav said: Groaning breaks half of a person's body, as it is stated: "Groan, therefore, you son of man, with the breaking of your loins, groan so bitterly"** (Ezekiel 21:11), which indicates that groaning breaks half of one's body, down to his loins.

Just as we had the dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan regarding how long a man may be absent, we now encounter a parallel dispute between the same two authorities on a different matter. Rav states: *anachah shever chatzi gufo shel adam* — sighing breaks half of a person's body. The proof-text is from Yechezkel, where God instructs the prophet: *he'anach beshivron motnayim* — moan with the breaking of the loins. Since the verse specifies the loins — the middle of the body — Rav infers that sighing breaks only half.

### Rabbi Yochanan: Full Body

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף כׇּל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה כִּי יֹאמְרוּ אֵלֶיךָ עַל מָה אַתָּה נֶאֱנָח וְאָמַרְתָּ אֶל שְׁמוּעָה כִּי בָאָה וְנָמֵס כׇּל לֵב וְרָפוּ כׇל יָדַיִם וְכִהֲתָה כׇל רוּחַ וְכׇל בִּרְכַּיִם תֵּלַכְנָה מַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that groaning breaks **even a person's entire body, as it is stated: "And it shall be, when they say to you: Why are you groaning? That you shall say: Due to the tiding, for it comes, and every heart shall melt, and all hands shall be slack, and every spirit shall be faint, and all knees shall drip with water"** (Ezekiel 21:12).

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *af kol gufo shel adam* — sighing breaks the entire body. His proof-text continues from the same prophetic context: *venamas kol lev, verafu kol yadayim, vechahatah kol ruach, vechol birkayim telechnah mayim* — all hearts melt, all hands weaken, all spirit dims, all knees dissolve into water. The imagery encompasses the whole person.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״בְּשִׁבְרוֹן מׇתְנַיִם״! הָהִיא דְּכִי מַתְחֲלָא — מִמׇּתְנַיִם מַתְחֲלָא. וְרַב נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״וְנָמֵס כׇּל לֵב וְרָפוּ כׇל יָדַיִם וְכִהֲתָה כׇל רוּחַ״! שָׁאנֵי שְׁמוּעָה דְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, דְּתַקִּיפָא טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why doesn't **Rabbi Yoḥanan also** say that it breaks half of one's body? **Isn't it written: "With the breaking of your loins,"** implying that it does not break the entire body? The Gemara answers: **This** does not mean that the breakage only reaches the loins, but rather **that when** the sigh **begins** to affect a person, **it begins from his loins.** The Gemara asks: **And** why doesn't **Rav also** say that it breaks the entire body? **Isn't it written: "And every heart shall melt, and all hands shall be slack, and every spirit shall be faint,"** which indicates that groaning causes the entire body to break? The Gemara answers: **The news with regard to** the destruction of **the Temple is different, as it is extremely crushing** and causes great anguish, but in general a sigh causes only half of the body to break.

The Gemara presses: how does Rabbi Yochanan handle the verse about the loins, which seems to specify only half the body? He answers: *hahi de'chi matchala mimotnayim matchala* — that is the starting point; the breakdown begins in the loins but spreads to destroy the whole body. And how does Rav handle the more encompassing verse? He says: *shani shemuat Beis HaMikdash detakifa tuva* — the destruction of the Temple is exceptional in its power to devastate. Ordinary sighing does not have that extreme effect.

### Traveler Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי דַּהֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. לָא אִימְּצִי גּוֹי לְסַגּוֹיֵי בַּהֲדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אַדְכְּרֵיהּ חוּרְבַּן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נְגִיד וְאִיתְּנַח, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא אִימְּצִי גּוֹי לְסַגּוֹיֵי בַּהֲדֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו אָמְרִיתוּ ״אֲנָחָה שׁוֹבֶרֶת חֲצִי גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִילְּתָא חַדְתִּי, אֲבָל הָא דַּשְׁנַן בָּהּ — לָא. דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: (דְּמַלְּפִי) [דְּמַלְּפָא] תִּכְלֵי — לָא בָּהֲתָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **a certain Jew and a gentile were walking along the road together. The gentile could not keep up with the Jew,** who was walking faster, and **he** therefore **reminded him of the destruction of the Temple** in order to make the Jew feel sorrowful and slow down. **The Jew sighed and groaned, but even so the gentile could not keep up with him,** as the Jew was still walking faster. The gentile **said to him: Don't you say that groaning breaks half of a person's body?** Why didn't it affect you? **He said to him: This applies only** with regard to **a new** sorrowful **affair, but this,** from **which we have** suffered **repeatedly** and to which we have become accustomed, does **not** affect us as much, **as people say: One who is used to being bereaved** of her children **does not panic [*bahata*]** when one of them dies, and similarly, one who is used to a tragedy is not as devastated when being reminded of it.

The Gemara then offers a story to illustrate the distinction between acute and habituated grief. A Jew and a non-Jew were walking together. The non-Jew could not keep pace with the Jew, who was walking quickly. The non-Jew then reminded his companion of the destruction of the Temple. The Jew sighed and slowed — yet even so, the non-Jew still could not keep up. The non-Jew said: didn't you just learn in *Gemara Kesuvos* that a sigh breaks half a person's body? The Jew replied: *hanei milei milta chadeta* — that is when the report is new, something freshly devastating. *Aval ha de'dashnan bah* — but this, which we have heard again and again, we have become inured to. People say: a woman who has suffered many miscarriages does not feel the same terror at the next one, because experience has numbed the initial shock. So too, the accumulated suffering visited upon Jews by non-Jews means that revisiting ancient tragedies no longer carries the same physiological impact.

### Sighing & Absence

This parallel dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan seems at first glance to be an independent literary unit, but there is a clear thematic connection to the earlier debate. Rav, who permits a man to be away from home for half the time, effectively says: your wife is sighing and distressed at home — *chatzi gufo*, she will survive it. Rabbi Yochanan, who insists the man be home two months out of three, says: no, *kol gufo shel adam* — the impact of your absence can be entirely destructive, and you cannot permit that. How one assesses the impact of one's absence on a spouse is inevitably filtered through prior assumptions about marriage and *talmud torah*. And the closing note of the story — once you are used to it, it is not so devastating — could plausibly be read as a Babylonian response to the critique of Eretz Yisrael: here in Bavel, our wives are used to it.

## EY vs Bavel Dispute

### Monogamy vs Polygamy

The divergence between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan connects to a broader and deeper dispute between Eretz Yisrael and Bavel — one that touches on the very nature of marriage. Eretz Yisrael was already a monogamous society; Bavel permitted polygamy.

### Marriage Intrinsic Value

This institutional difference reflects a difference in how marriage itself was understood. In Eretz Yisrael, marriage appears to have been valued intrinsically. Statements like *kol adam she'eino shari belo isha shari belo tova* — anyone who dwells without a wife dwells without goodness — and the biblical *lo tov heyos ha'adam levado* reflect a sense that marriage is essential to human flourishing for its own sake. In Bavel, by contrast, marriage was understood more instrumentally: a man marries so that he has a legitimate outlet for his desires, which then frees him to devote himself to *talmud torah* without improper thoughts.

### Marriage Before/After To…

This difference manifests directly in the famous dispute in Kiddushin about whether a man should first marry and then learn *torah*, or first learn and then marry. In Bavel, the ruling is to marry first — once you have a wife, your desires are channeled appropriately, and you can go learn. In Eretz Yisrael, the ruling is to learn first, because once you are married — *reichaim betzavaro* — you have domestic responsibilities that you cannot simply set aside. You had better finish your learning before you take on those obligations.

### Rabbi Yochanan EY Positi…

It is therefore entirely unsurprising that Rabbi Yochanan — the great Amora of Eretz Yisrael — insists on two months home and one month away, while Rav, working within the Babylonian framework, permits equal time. The sighing debate is not peripheral; it reflects the same underlying disagreement about what marriage demands and how seriously the husband's absence weighs on the wife. This is a real dispute between two civilizational orientations toward the relationship between marriage and *talmud torah*, not merely a technical disagreement about frequency.

## Occupational Classification

### Tayalim Daily

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַטַּיָּילִין בְּכׇל יוֹם. מַאי ״טַיָּילִין״? אָמַר רָבָא: בְּנֵי פִירְקֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאן דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״שָׁוְא לָכֶם מַשְׁכִּימֵי קוּם מְאַחֲרֵי שֶׁבֶת אוֹכְלֵי לֶחֶם הָעֲצָבִים כֵּן יִתֵּן לִידִידוֹ שֵׁנָא״, וְאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק: אֵלּוּ נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמְּנַדְּדוֹת שֵׁינָה מֵעֵינֵיהֶם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וּבָאוֹת לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ בְּנֵי פִירְקֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna said that **men of leisure** must engage in marital relations with their wives **every day.** The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by the term **men of leisure? Rava said:** These are **students** of Torah who go daily to review **their lectures** at a local study hall and return home each evening. **Abaye said to him:** Wives of Torah scholars are **those about whom it is written: "It is vain for you to rise early and sit up late, you that eat the bread of toil, so He gives to His beloved in sleep"** (Psalms 127:2), **and Rabbi Yitzḥak said** in explanation of this verse: **These are the wives of Torah scholars who deprive their eyes of sleep in this world and reach the life of the World-to-Come.** This indicates that Torah scholars exert themselves greatly in their studies and are not home in the evenings, **and you say** that the **students** reviewing **their lectures** are men of leisure, whose wives have conjugal rights for every night?

The Mishnah states: *hatayalin bechol yom* — the *tayalin*, whoever they are, have a daily *mitzvat onah* when the wife is not in a state of *niddah*. There is an important background consideration here: in the era of the Gemara, *niddah* lasted approximately one week per month, whereas today it extends to nearly two weeks, which proportionally reduces the applicable *onah* obligation.

The Gemara asks: *mai tayalin*? Rava answers: *bnei pirkei* — those who come and study at a local *yeshiva* and return home each day. Because they are not worn out by physical labor and are at home each night, their *mitzvat onah* is daily, assuming the wife is interested.

### Wives Torah Students

Abaye objects. He cites the verse *shav lachem mashkimei kum, me'acharei shevet, ochlei lechem ha'atzavim* — it is vain for you to rise early, to sit up late, to eat the bread of toil — and the continuation: *ken yitein liyidido shena* — God will provide for His beloved in sleep. Rav Yitzchak applies this to the wives of Torah scholars: *eilu neshoteihem shel talmidei chachamim shemenadedot shena me'eineihem* — these are the wives of Torah scholars who chase sleep from their eyes in this world and thereby merit the life of the world to come. These women are working through the night to support their husbands' learning. If the wives are losing sleep because of their domestic labors and the husbands are returning home only late at night, how can one describe the daily frequency as the norm? Abaye therefore concludes that the *tayalin* must refer to something else — specifically to people like Rav Shmuel bar Shilat, as Rav taught.

### Wealthy Independents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּדְרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת, דְּאָכֵיל מִדִּידֵיהּ, וְשָׁתֵי מִדִּידֵיהּ, וְגָנֵי בְּטוּלָּא דְאַפַּדְנֵיהּ, וְלָא חָלֵיף פְּרִיסְתְּקָא דְמַלְכָּא אַבָּבֵיהּ. כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר: כְּגוֹן מְפַנְּקִי דְמַעְרְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** The mishna should be explained **in accordance with the opinion of Rav, as Rav said:** This is referring to a man **such as Rabbi Shmuel bar Sheilat, who ate his own** food, **drank his own** drinks, **slept in the shade of his own house, and the king's tax collector [*peristaka*] did not pass by his door,** as they did not know that he was a man of means. A man like this, who has a steady income and no worries, is called a man of leisure. **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said: For example, the** wealthy, **pampered** men **in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, are called men of leisure. Due to the time they have available and the richness of their diet, they have the ability to satisfy their wives every night.

The correct referent for *tayalin* is the independently wealthy person who lives modestly off his own means. *Kegon Rav Shmuel bar Shilat de'achal midideih* — he eats and drinks from his own resources and sleeps in the shade of his own home, *velo chalif peristaka demalka avaveih* — and the king's tax collector does not pass his door, because he conducts himself inconspicuously and thus avoids the attention of the government. This person has no need to labor strenuously, no financial obligations to others, and no demands on his time and energy. For such a person, the *mitzvat onah* applies daily.

### Ravin on Pampered

When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he offered a parallel formulation: *kegon mefankei dema'arava* — like the pampered ones of the West, meaning Eretz Yisrael. The term *mefunack* suggests not spoiled in a pejorative sense, but independently sustained — wealthy individuals who do not work for a living, consistent with the category described above.

### Bathhouse Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ הֲוָה קָאֵי בֵּי בָאנֵי. הֲווֹ סָמְכִי לֵיהּ תְּרֵי עַבְדֵי. אִיפְּחִית בֵּי בָאנֵי מִתּוּתֵיהּ, אִיתְרְמִי לֵיהּ עַמּוּדָא, סְלֵיק וְאַסְּקִינְהוּ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה קָסָלֵק בְּדַרְגָּא, הֲווֹ סָמְכִי לֵיהּ רַב אַמֵּי וְרַב אַסִּי. אִיפְּחִתָא דַּרְגָּא תּוּתֵיהּ, סְלֵיק וְאַסְּקִינְהוּ. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּהָכִי, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִיסְמְכֵיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִם כֵּן מָה אַנִּיחַ לְעֵת זִקְנָה?
>
> **Translation:** To illustrate this point, the Gemara relates two incidents demonstrating the health and strength of the inhabitants of Eretz Yisrael: **Rabbi Abbahu was** once **standing in the bathhouse and two slaves were supporting** his walking. **The bathhouse collapsed under him** and was destroyed. **He found a pillar,** stood on it and **got out, and pulled them** both **up** with him. Similarly, **Rabbi Yoḥanan was** once **going up stairs,** and **Rav Ami and Rav Asi were supporting** him. **The stair collapsed under him, but he went up and pulled them** both **up** with him. **The Sages said to him: Since** it is clear that you are **so** strong, **why do you need** people **to support you? He said to them: If so,** if I were to expend all my strength now, **what will I leave for** myself in my **old age?**

At this point the Gemara provides two vivid anecdotes that illustrate what it means to be among the *mefankei dema'arava*. Rabbi Abbahu was in a bathhouse being supported by two servants — presumably because of his considerable physical bulk, which, as has been noted in scholarship on Babylonian imaginings of the sages of Eretz Yisrael, may reflect an orientalist projection of what wealthy and prominent figures looked like. The floor of the bathhouse collapsed beneath him, but he managed to catch himself on one of the supporting pillars — the kind visible at sites like Metzada, where the floor of the bathing area was suspended above the heated space below. Not only did he save himself, but he pulled both servants up as well. Similarly, Rabbi Yochanan was ascending steps, supported by Rav Ami and Rav Assi, when a step broke beneath him. He too pulled himself up and brought his attendants with him. When the rabbis asked why, if they were so strong, they needed to be supported in the first place, the reply was: *im ken ma ani mani'ach lezikna* — if I use my strength now, what will I preserve for old age?

These stories are presented in connection with the discussion of *mefankei dema'arava*, but they carry an additional resonance worth noting. Later in the *daf* we will encounter a story in which a roof collapses beneath a husband who has neglected his wife — a collapse that results in his death. Here, by contrast, things breaking underneath carries no such moral weight; the rabbis escape, even saving others. The contrast matters. The rabbis of Eretz Yisrael — Rabbi Yochanan, Rabbi Abbahu — are not the ones abandoning their wives. Their world is one of greater attentiveness to domestic obligation, which is precisely why the same image of collapse means something entirely different in their stories.

## Workers & Onah

### Workers Local vs Travel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַפּוֹעֲלִים שְׁתַּיִם בַּשַּׁבָּת. וְהָתַנְיָא: הַפּוֹעֲלִים אַחַת בַּשַּׁבָּת! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּעוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה בְּעִירָן, כָּאן בְּעוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַפּוֹעֲלִים שְׁתַּיִם בַּשַּׁבָּת, בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּעוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה בְּעִירָן, אֲבָל בְּעוֹשִׂין מְלָאכָה בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת — אַחַת בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna said: The set interval for **laborers** to fulfill their conjugal obligations to their wives is **twice a week.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: For **laborers, once a week? Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** This is **not difficult: Here,** the case is **where they work in their own city. There,** the case is **where they work in another city. This is also taught** in the *Tosefta* (5:6): For **laborers, twice a week. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is **when they work in their own city, but when they work in another city,** the set interval for their conjugal obligations is **once a week.**

The Mishnah states *v'hapolim shtayim bashabbat* — workers twice a week. But a *Braita* teaches *poel achas bashabbat* — once a week. Rabbi Yosi berabbi Chanina resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya kan be'osei melacha b'irono kan be'osei melacha b'ir acheres* — there is no difficulty; it depends on whether they are working in their own town or in a different town. Workers employed locally have more energy, return home each night, and thus fulfill *onah* twice weekly. Workers who commute to another town, even if they return the same day, are more worn out and are reduced to once a week. The *Braita* then confirms this distinction explicitly.

### Donkey Drivers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחַמָּרִים אַחַת בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna said: The set interval for **donkey drivers** is **once a week,** and for other professions it is even less frequent.

*Hachamorim achas bashabbat* — donkey drivers are obligated once a week.

## Neder & Divorce

### One Week Neder Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן לְאַבָּיֵי: אִיכְּפַל תַּנָּא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן טַיָּיל וּפוֹעֵל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, אַכּוּלְּהוּ. וְהָא שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan said to Abaye: Did the *tanna* go to all that trouble just to teach us** the *halakha* for **a man of leisure and** for **a laborer?** According to the set intervals given for conjugal obligations, it seems that the *halakha* that one who vowed to prohibit his wife from conjugal relations for longer than a week must divorce her is referring only to a man of leisure or a laborer, whose set interval for conjugal relations is less than that period. However, for other people, whose set interval is once a month or even less frequent, there should be no need to divorce the wife, since the vow does not deprive her of conjugal rights for longer than she would have been deprived anyway. **He said to him: No,** the *tanna* taught us a *halakha* **with regard to all of them,** not only a man of leisure or a laborer. He asked him: **But** with regard to a sailor **it said** that the set interval for conjugal relations is **six months;** why, then, should he have to divorce her if he vowed to forbid these relations for only a week?

Rabba bar Rav Chanan raises a question to Abaye. The Mishnah teaches that if a man takes a *neder* not to have relations with his wife, he must divorce her after one week according to Beis Hillel. But looking at the Mishnah's own classification of *onah* frequencies, very few men had an obligation more frequent than once a week — only the independently wealthy (daily) and local workers (twice weekly). Most men were obligated once a week or less. So if a man takes a *neder* for one week, what has he really withheld? For someone obligated once a week, he has missed exactly one *onah*. And for camel drivers or sailors, whose obligation falls far less frequently, a one-week *neder* withholds nothing at all. Why should such a *neder* compel divorce?

### Bread in Basket

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ לְמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is well known that **one who has bread in his basket is not comparable to one who does not have bread in his basket.** On a fast day, one who does not have bread available in his basket suffers more than one who does have bread available and knows that he will be able to eat later. In this case as well, when a woman knows that marital relations are forbidden to her due to a vow, her suffering from waiting for her husband to return is increased.

Abaye replies: *eino domeh mi sheyesh lo pas besalo l'mi she'ein lo pas besalo* — one who has bread in his basket is not comparable to one who does not. The operative factor is not the frequency of actual relations, but the wife's knowledge that her husband is *legally available* to her at any moment. When he is merely traveling for work or absent for legitimate reasons, there is no barrier in principle — he could return, the opportunity exists. But a *neder* creates an actual legal prohibition. Even for someone whose normal *onah* frequency is once every six months, the existence of a formal *neder* — which forecloses the possibility of intimacy — constitutes a sufficient rupture of the marital bond to warrant divorce after one week. The absence of the bread from the basket, not merely its infrequency, is what matters.

## Job Change

### Occupation Switch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן לְאַבָּיֵי: חַמָּר וְנַעֲשֶׂה גַּמָּל, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רוֹצָה אִשָּׁה בְּקַב וְתִיפְלוּת מֵעֲשָׂרָה קַבִּין וּפְרִישׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Hanan said to Abaye: If a donkey driver** who is already married wants to **become a camel driver, what** is the *halakha*? Is he permitted to change his profession in order to earn more money from his work, even though this will mean he reduces the frequency with which he engages in conjugal relations with his wife? **He answered him: A woman prefers a *kav*,** i.e., modest means, **with conjugal relations to ten *kav* with abstinence.** Consequently, he is not allowed to change his profession without her permission.

Rabba bar Rav Chanan poses a further question to Abaye: *chamor vena'aseh gamal mai* — what if a donkey driver wishes to become a camel driver? Moving from donkey driving to camel driving means longer journeys, greater absence from home, and a corresponding reduction in *onah* frequency. May he make this change without his wife's permission?

### Wife's Preference

The answer: *rotzah ishah be-kav ve-tiflut me-asarah kabin u-frishut* — a woman prefers one measure with *tiflut* over ten measures with abstinence. The word *tiflut* is somewhat loaded — it connotes something trivial or insubstantial, not a fully dignified expression of marital intimacy. The Gemara could have stated the principle in a more straightforward way: a man has a primary obligation to his wife and may not unilaterally reduce his presence. Instead, it frames the point through a somewhat unflattering characterization of women's preferences. The underlying *halakhic* conclusion is nonetheless clear: the husband may not unilaterally switch to an occupation that diminishes his availability to his wife without her consent.

### Rosh Exception

The Rosh adds an important qualification to this principle: if the proposed change is not a different occupation but a commitment to *talmud torah*, the husband does not require his wife's permission. The obligation and value of *talmud torah* stands apart from the ordinary calculus of occupational change, and a husband is entitled to pursue it even if it increases his absence, without seeking her approval for that specific transition.

## Sailors & Rabbi Eliezer

### Sailors Six Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַסַּפָּנִים אַחַת לְשִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אָמַר רַב בְּרוֹנָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַתַּלְמִידִים יוֹצְאִין לְתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת. אָמַר רָבָא: סָמְכוּ רַבָּנַן אַדְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה וְעָבְדִי עוֹבָדָא בְּנַפְשַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: For **sailors,** the set interval for conjugal relations is **once every six months. This is the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rav Berona said** that **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that **Rav said: This is the statement of Rabbi Eliezer, but the Rabbis say: Students may leave** their homes **to study Torah** for as long as **two or three years without permission** from their wives. **Rava said: The Sages relied on Rabbi Adda bar Ahava's** opinion **and performed an action** like this **themselves,** but the results were sometimes fatal.

*Ha-sapanim achat le-shishah chodashim — divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — sailors, once every six months — this is the position of Rabbi Eliezer. Rav Bruna said in the name of Rav: *halakhah ke-Rabbi Eliezer* — we rule in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer.

### 2-3 Year Absence

Rav Adda bar Ahava said in the name of Rav: *zu divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — this is the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer specifically. But the Sages say: *ha-talmidim yotzin le-Talmud Torah be' ve-gimel shanim shelo bi-reshut* — Torah students may go out for two or three years to study without express permission from their wives. This reveals that the debate implicit in the phrase *divrei Rabbi Eliezer* at the end of the Mishnah is not primarily about the general classification of occupational *onah* frequencies. It is specifically about how long a Torah student may be absent from home. Rabbi Eliezer's position, as understood by the Gemara, is that the framework of the Mishnah applies — a month away without permission. The opposing Sages permit two or three years.

It should be noted in passing that there is another possible axis of dispute with Rabbi Eliezer that the Gemara does not pursue here but which is developed elsewhere: how to conceptualize the fundamental nature of *onah* itself. Rabbi Eliezer understands *onah* as a calendar-based frequency. An alternative understanding — and there are *poskim* who hold this represents the *de-Oraita* obligation — is that *onah* is contextual and demand-based: a man is obligated to be available to his wife whenever she wishes. This reading may be reflected in Rabbi Yehoshua's position. But the Gemara here directs the debate toward the question of extended absence for *talmud torah*, and that is where it proceeds.

### Students' Self-Reliance

This passage flows directly into the following, but the distinct message of Rabbi Adda's statement about student absences needs to be held separate before the examples that follow:

Rava states: *samchu Rabanan ad'Rav Adda bar Ahava va'avdu uvda benafshayhu* — the rabbinic students relied on Rav Adda bar Ahava's articulation of the Sages' position and ruled for themselves accordingly, permitting themselves extended absence from home. Rashi presses the phrase *benafshayhu* — "with their own souls" — beyond the ordinary sense of ruling for oneself. He reads it as: they acted, but in doing so they were risking their own lives. The story that follows demonstrates exactly this.

## Rav Rechumi Story

### Rashi Risk Interpretation

The Ra'avad reads *va'avdu uvda benafshayhu* differently — not as risking one's life, but simply as ruling for themselves in a self-determined way. On the Ra'avad's reading, the position of the Sages permitting two to three years represents the operative *halakha*, and the students were entitled to rely on it. Rashi, by contrast, injects a note of moral caution: you may have the legal right, but you are playing with fire.

### Annual Return Delayed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב רְחוּמִי הֲוָה שְׁכִיחַ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא בְּמָחוֹזָא, הֲוָה רְגִיל דַּהֲוָה אָתֵי לְבֵיתֵיהּ כֹּל מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי. יוֹמָא חַד מְשַׁכְתֵּיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא. הֲוָה מְסַכְּיָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ: הַשְׁתָּא אָתֵי, הַשְׁתָּא אָתֵי. לָא אֲתָא. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתַּהּ, אַחִית דִּמְעֲתָא מֵעֵינַהּ. הֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּאִיגָּרָא, אִפְּחִית אִיגָּרָא מִתּוּתֵיהּ וְנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as** it is related **about Rav Reḥumi, who would commonly** study **before Rava in Meḥoza: He was accustomed to come** back **to his home every** year on the **eve of Yom Kippur. One day he was** particularly **engrossed in** the ***halakha*** he was studying, and so he remained in the study hall and did not go home. **His wife was expecting him** that day and continually said to herself: **Now he is coming, now he is coming.** But in the end, **he did not come. She was distressed** by this and **a tear fell from her eye.** At that exact moment, Rav Reḥumi **was sitting on the roof. The roof collapsed under him and he died.** This teaches how much one must be careful, as he was punished severely for causing anguish to his wife, even inadvertently.

*Ki ha de'Rav Rechumi* — like the story of Rav Rechumi. The name itself — *Rechumi*, cognate with *rachamim*, compassion — is resonant. He would regularly study with Rava in Mechuza. His practice was to return home once a year, on *Erev Yom Kippur*. One year, *yoma chad mashchateih shema'ta* — he became absorbed in a particularly engaging topic of learning and the learning stretched on.

### Wife's Tear Collapses

His wife was waiting at home, looking out repeatedly: *hashta ati, hashta ati* — now he will come, now he will come. He did not come. *Chalash da'ata* — she despaired. *Achit dima'ta me'eina* — a single tear fell from her eye. He was sitting on the roof. *Ifchit igra mituteih* — the roof collapsed beneath him, and *venach nafsheih* — he died.

Here again we have something collapsing beneath a rabbi — but this collapse is entirely different from the bathhouse stories above. There the collapse was incidental, almost comic, and the rabbis emerged stronger. Here a single tear causes the roof to give way and the husband dies. The juxtaposition is stark, and the connection to the earlier discussion of sighing — *anacha shoveret gufo shel adam* — is unmistakable. Something did break; it was not the wife's body, but the roof, and the husband beneath it.

### Ra'avad vs Rashi

The Ra'avad, consistent with his more permissive reading, largely sets aside the cautionary implications of this story. He acknowledges that in principle the *halakha* permits two to three years of absence, but draws a practical lesson from the narrative: even if you are legally entitled to be away, make every effort to keep the one commitment you have made — come home on *Erev Yom Kippur* if that is what you promised. The Rif does not bring Rav Adda bar Ahava's ruling at all, holding that the *halakha* follows Rabbi Eliezer — which limits permissible unauthorized absence to one month, not two or three years. The Ramah rules like Rav Adda bar Ahava, based on the fact that the Gemara reports that the students *relied* on his ruling, treating it as the operative position. And the Ra'avad attempts to reintegrate both the legal entitlement and the moral responsibility: yes, we *pasken* like the Sages for two or three years — but let the story of the collapsing roof temper how you exercise that entitlement.

## Torah Scholars Onah

### Friday to Friday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹנָה שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים אֵימַת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. ״אֲשֶׁר פִּרְיוֹ יִתֵּן בְּעִתּוֹ״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב הוּנָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב נַחְמָן: זֶה הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § **When** is **the** ideal **time for Torah scholars** to fulfill their conjugal obligations? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The appropriate time for them is **from Shabbat eve to Shabbat eve,** i.e., on Friday nights. Similarly, it is stated with regard to the verse **"that brings forth its fruit in its season"** (Psalms 1:3): **Rav Yehuda said, and some say** that it was **Rav Huna, and some say** that it was **Rav Naḥman: This** is referring to one **who engages in marital relations,** bringing forth his fruit, **from Shabbat eve to Shabbat eve.**

The Mishnah discussed the *onah* for Torah scholars in terms of how long they may be absent without permission, but it does not specify the actual frequency of their *onah* obligation. The Gemara addresses this directly. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *me'erev Shabbos l'erev Shabbos* — from Shabbat eve to Shabbat eve, meaning Friday night.

### Oneg Shabbat

Rashi explains: *shehu leil ta'anug u'shvita ve'hana'at haguf* — Friday night is a night of pleasure, rest, and bodily enjoyment, making it the appropriate and natural time for *onah* as part of *oneg Shabbos*. This is also significant in light of certain sectarian positions of the *Bayit Sheni* period that prohibited marital relations on Shabbat. The Gemara here affirms not only that Shabbat night is permitted, but that it is the designated *onah* time for Torah scholars. Rav Yehuda — and this is also cited in the names of Rav Huna and Rav Nachman — adds: *ze hamishamesh mitaso me'erev Shabbos l'erev Shabbos* — one who maintains relations on a weekly Friday-night cycle fulfills the verse *asher piryo yiten be'ito*, the fruit given in its proper time, which also implies that children conceived in this manner and at this time will be born well.

## Yehuda Bar Chiya

### Weekly Returns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, חַתְנֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי, הֲוָה אָזֵיל וְיָתֵיב בְּבֵי רַב, וְכׇל בֵּי שִׁמְשֵׁי הֲוָה אָתֵי לְבֵיתֵיהּ, וְכִי הֲוָה אָתֵי, הֲוָה קָא חָזֵי קַמֵּיהּ עַמּוּדָא דְנוּרָא. יוֹמָא חַד מְשַׁכְתֵּיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא. כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חָזֵי הָהוּא סִימָנָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כְּפוּ מִטָּתוֹ, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵי יְהוּדָה קַיָּים לֹא בִּיטֵּל עוֹנָתוֹ. הֲוַאי כִּ״שְׁגָגָה שֶׁיּוֹצָא מִלִּפְנֵי הַשַּׁלִּיט״, וְנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is related further that **Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya** and **son-in-law of Rabbi Yannai, would go and sit in the study hall, and every** Shabbat eve **at twilight he would come to his house. When he would come,** Rabbi Yannai **would see a pillar of fire preceding him** due to his sanctity. **One day he was engrossed in** the ***halakha*** he was studying, and he stayed in the study hall and did not return home. **When Rabbi Yannai did not see that sign** preceding him, **he said to** the family: **Turn his bed over,** as one does at times of mourning, since he must have died, reasoning that **if Yehuda were alive he would not have missed his set interval** for conjugal relations and would certainly have come home. What he said **became "like an error that proceeds from a ruler"** (Ecclesiastes 10:5), **and** Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, **died.**

Yehuda the son of Rabbi Chiya — and son-in-law of Rabbi Yanai — would go and sit in the house of his *rebbe*. Every *Erev Shabbat*, at nightfall — as Tosafot notes, *bei shimshai* refers to the *bein hashmashos* of Friday evening — he would return home. And when he would return, *hava kachazi amuda d'nura* — a pillar of fire would be seen preceding him, marking his arrival.

### Missed Sign

One Friday, *yoma chad mashcheih shema'ta* — he became absorbed in his learning and did not return. When the members of the household did not see the usual sign — the pillar of fire — Rabbi Yanai, his father-in-law, said to the household: *kifu mitasu* — overturn the bed, the sign of mourning and *aveilus*. His reasoning: *she'ilmalei Yehuda kayam, lo bital onaso* — if Yehuda were still alive, he would never have shirked his obligation and missed coming home. Therefore, his failure to appear could only mean that he had died.

### Death from Word

*Hava kishgaga sheyotzei milifnei hashalit* — it was like an error that goes out from the presence of a ruler, words spoken by an important person that take on the force of reality. And indeed, *venach nafsheih* — Rav Yehuda died.

### Duty Message

This story is striking in its severity. The wife is not described as weeping; there is no tear, no visible suffering. The mere fact that the husband failed to fulfill his *onah* obligation — that he was not home on *Erev Shabbat* as he always had been — was itself treated as proof of death and triggered a verbal decree that became reality. The message is unambiguous: the *onah* of Torah scholars is not optional or subject to casual revision, even for a single week. This stands in sharp contrast to the earlier stories about twelve-year absences, and the range of stories presented in this *daf* reflects genuinely different valuations of how far *talmud torah* may impinge on domestic responsibility.

## Rabbi Judah's Son

### Bride Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אִיעֲסֵק לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ בֵּי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, כִּי מְטָא לְמִיכְתַּב כְּתוּבָּה נָח נַפְשַׁהּ דִּרְבִיתָא. אָמַר רַבִּי: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם פְּסוּלָא אִיכָּא? יְתִיבוּ וְעַיִּינוּ בְּמִשְׁפָּחוֹת, רַבִּי אָתֵי מִשְּׁפַטְיָה בֶּן אֲבִיטַל, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא אָתֵי מִשִּׁמְעִי אֲחִי דָוִד.
>
> **Translation:** It is related further that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **arranged for his son** to marry a daughter of **the household of Rabbi Ḥiyya. When he came to write the marriage contract, the girl died. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Is there, Heaven forbid, some disqualification** in these families, as it appears that God prevented this match from taking place? **They sat and looked into the families'** ancestry and found that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **was descended from Shefatya ben Avital,** the wife of David, whereas **Rabbi Ḥiyya was descended from Shimi, David's brother.**

The Gemara now records that Rebbi — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — arranged a match for his son with the family of Rabbi Chiya, to one of Rabbi Chiya's daughters. When they came to write the *kesuvah*, the bride died.

### Yichus Problem

Rebbi was disturbed: *chas v'shalom, psulei ika* — is there perhaps some disqualification, some problem with this union, that caused it to be prevented? They sat and examined the lineages. Rebbi descended from Shefatya ben Avital — Avital being one of David's wives — establishing his descent from the royal house of David. Rabbi Chiya descended from Shimi, David's brother — an honorable lineage, but not the royal line itself. This mismatch of *yichus* was identified as the source of the problem, and the bride's death was understood as a providential intervention preventing an unsuitable match.

### Second Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל אִיעֲסֵק לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ בֵּי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן זִימְרָא. פְּסַקוּ לֵיהּ תַּרְתֵּי סְרֵי שְׁנִין לְמֵיזַל בְּבֵי רַב.
>
> **Translation:** **He went and arranged for his son** to marry a daughter **of the household of Rabbi Yosei ben Zimra. They agreed for him** that they would support him for **twelve years to go to study in the study hall.**

Rebbi then arranged a match for his son with the family of Rabbi Yossi ben Zimra.

## 12 Years Pre-Marriage

### Bride Walks Forward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחְלְפוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: נִיהְווֹ שֵׁית שְׁנִין. אַחְלְפוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֶיכְנֹיס וַהֲדַר אֵיזִיל. הֲוָה קָא מִכְּסִיף מֵאֲבוּהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנִי, דַּעַת קוֹנְךָ יֵשׁ בָּךְ — מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב: ״תְּבִיאֵמוֹ וְתִטָּעֵמוֹ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּתִיב: ״וְעָשׂוּ לִי מִקְדָּשׁ וְשָׁכַנְתִּי בְּתוֹכָם״.
>
> **Translation:** It was assumed that he would first go to study and afterward get married. **They passed** the girl **in front of** the groom and when he saw her **he said: Let it be** just **six years. They passed her in front of him** again and **he said to them: I will marry her** now **and then go** to study. **He was** then **ashamed** to see **his father,** as he thought he would reprimand him because when he saw the girl he desired her and could not wait. His father placated him and **said to him: My son, you have your Maker's perception,** meaning you acted the same way that God does. The proof for this is that **initially it is written: "You bring them and plant them** in the mountain of Your inheritance, the place that You, O Lord, have made for You to dwell in" (Exodus 15:17), which indicates that God's original intention was to build a Temple for the Jewish people after they had entered Eretz Yisrael. **And ultimately it is written: "And let them make Me a Sanctuary, that I may dwell among them"** (Exodus 25:8), i.e., even while they were still in the desert, which indicates that due to their closeness to God, they enjoyed greater affection and He therefore advanced what would originally have come later.

The agreement reached with Rabbi Yossi ben Zimra's family was that Rebbi's son would be supported for twelve years of study in *yeshiva* before the marriage. This reflects the Eretz Yisrael approach: learn first, then marry, so that you are not compelled to abandon your new wife for the *beis medrash*. However, the *kallah*'s family moved to accelerate matters. They had the bride walk before him — a pointed reminder of what was waiting for him — as if to ask: are you certain you want to wait?

### Marry Then Learn

He responded: *nava shish shanin* — perhaps six years would suffice. They had her walk before him again. At that point he said, *ichnis v'hadar eizil* — I will marry now, and then go to learn. This represents a capitulation to the Babylonian model: marry first, then pursue *talmud torah*. He was embarrassed before his father, who had envisioned twelve years of unencumbered study.

### Biblical Timeline

His father reassured him: *beni, da'as koncha yesh becha* — my son, you think like your Creator. He drew an analogy from the *Shirat HaYam*: the verse first says *t'vi'eimo v'sita'eimo behar nachalatcha* — bring them and plant them on the mountain of Your inheritance — meaning the people would enter the land and only then build the Temple. Yet God was so eager that He immediately said *v'asu li mikdash v'shachanti b'tocham* — make Me a sanctuary and I will dwell among them — advancing the union. So too, the son should not feel that his choice was wrong.

### Return Wife Sterile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל יְתֵיב תַּרְתֵּי סְרֵי שְׁנֵי בְּבֵי רַב. עַד דַּאֲתָא, אִיעֲקַרָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ. אָמַר רַבִּי: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד, נְגָרְשָׁהּ — יֹאמְרוּ: עֲנִיָּיהּ זוֹ לַשָּׁוְא שִׁימְּרָה. נִינְּסִיב אִיתְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי — יֹאמְרוּ: זוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְזוֹ זוֹנָתוֹ. בְּעָא עֲלַהּ רַחֲמֵי וְאִיתַּסִּיאַת.
>
> **Translation:** After his wedding **he went and sat for twelve years in the study hall. By the time he came back his wife had become infertile,** as a consequence of spending many years without her husband. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: What** should we **do? If he will divorce her,** people **will say: This poor woman waited** and hoped **for naught. If he will marry another woman** to beget children, people **will say: This one,** who bears him children, **is his wife and that one,** who lives with him, **is his mistress.** Therefore, her husband **pleaded** with God **to have mercy on her and she was cured.**

He went and studied for twelve years in *yeshiva*. When he returned — *ad d'ata itkar d'vattayhu* — his wife had become sterile. Apparently he had not been present to fulfill his marital obligations; the marriage, though contracted, had been effectively abandoned during those twelve years. He had sought the best of both models and wound up satisfying neither: he married, but then behaved as if he had not.

### Rav Solutions

Rav considered the options: *nigrisha* — should he divorce her and marry another woman to have children? But then people would say: *ani azo l'shav shimra* — this poor woman waited in vain for twelve years. Should he take a second wife to have children, as was possible in Babylonian practice? Then people would say: *zu ishto v'zu zonoso* — this is his wife and this is his mistress. Neither option was acceptable.

### Prayer & Cure

So *ba'ei lei rachamei v'isis* — Rav prayed on her behalf, and she was healed. The problem was resolved through prayer. This reflects a distinctively Babylonian sensibility: yes, things went wrong, real harm was done — but the *Eibishter* can help, and solutions exist. The story does not minimize the consequences of neglecting domestic obligation, but neither does it leave the situation as irreparable tragedy.

## Rabbi Chanina Story

### Rabbi Shimeon Delay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן חֲכִינַאי הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל לְבֵי רַב בְּשִׁילְהֵי הִלּוּלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיעַכַּב לִי עַד דְּאָתֵי בַּהֲדָךְ. לָא אִיעַכַּב לֵיהּ. אֲזַל יְתֵיב תְּרֵי סְרֵי שְׁנֵי בְּבֵי רַב. עַד דְּאָתֵי, אִישְׁתַּנּוֹ שְׁבִילֵי דְמָתָא וְלָא יְדַע לְמֵיזַל לְבֵיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥananya ben Ḥakhinai went to the study hall at the end of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai's wedding feast.** Rabbi Shimon **said to him: Wait for me until I** can **come with you,** after my days of celebration are over. However, since he wanted to learn Torah, **he did not wait** and **went and sat for twelve years in the study hall. By the time he came back, all the paths of his city had changed and he did not know how to go to his home.**

Rabbi Chanina ben Chachinai was preparing to travel to his *rebbe*'s *yeshiva*. He was departing *b'shilhei hillula d'Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* — at the conclusion of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's wedding celebrations. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai asked him to wait until the wedding week was over so they could travel to the *yeshiva* together. This request itself gestures toward the Eretz Yisrael sensibility: even one committed to *talmud torah* is expected to complete his week of marriage before departing.

### Chanina Proceeds

Rabbi Chanina did not wait. He went immediately and sat in the *yeshiva* for twelve years. We hear nothing further of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — presumably he spent more time at home before eventually departing, which the story implies was the appropriate path.

### Lost Roads

After twelve years, Rabbi Chanina returned — but *ishtannu shivilei d'massa* — all the roads of the city had changed and he could not find his way home. He sat by the riverbank, disoriented, like someone waking after a very long absence from a world that had moved on without him.

### Hears Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל יְתֵיב אַגּוּדָּא דְּנַהֲרָא. שְׁמַע לְהַהִיא רְבִיתָא דַּהֲווֹ קָרוּ לַהּ: ״בַּת חֲכִינַאי, בַּת חֲכִינַאי, מַלַּי קוּלְּתִיךְ וְתָא נֵיזִיל״. אֲמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַאי רְבִיתָא דִּידַן. אֲזַל בָּתְרַהּ. הֲוָה יְתִיבָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ קָא נָהֲלָה קִמְחָא, דַּל עֵינַהּ חֲזִיתֵיהּ, סְוִי לִבַּהּ, פְּרַח רוּחַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He went** and **sat on the bank of the river and heard people calling to a certain girl: Daughter of Ḥakhinai, daughter of Ḥakhinai, fill your pitcher and come up. He said: I can conclude from this that this is our daughter,** meaning his own daughter, whom he had not recognized after so many years. **He followed her** to his house. **His wife was sitting and sifting flour. She lifted her eyes up, saw him** and recognized him, **and her heart fluttered** with agitation and **she passed away** from the emotional stress.

He overheard people calling out to a young woman: *bas Chachinai, bas Chachinai, malli kulskha v'sa nizil* — daughter of Chachinai, fill your basket and let's go. He said: I can infer from this that this girl is ours — she is my daughter. He followed her home.

### Wife Dies Shock

His wife was sitting inside, working with flour. She lifted her eyes, saw her husband who had been gone for twelve years — and *savyei libba* — her heart seized. *Parach rucha* — her spirit departed. She died from the shock of his sudden reappearance.

### Prayer Revives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, עֲנִיָּיהּ זוֹ זֶה שְׂכָרָהּ?! בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי עֲלַהּ וְ[אַ]חֲיַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥananya **said before** God: **Master of the universe, is this the reward of this poor woman? He pleaded for mercy for her and she lived.**

He cried out: *Ribbono Shel Olam, aniya zo, zeh secharah* — Master of the Universe, is this her reward? He prayed for her, and she was revived. And here an important observation must be made: whereas in the story of the Rav Rechumi the husband died as a consequence of his neglect, in this story of Rabbi Chanina ben Chachinai it is the wife who suffers — and even then, she is revived through prayer. The husband does not bear the ultimate consequence. The asymmetry is significant: even in a story that is ostensibly cautionary, the man who abandons his wife for twelve years is not the one who pays with his life.

## Rabbi Chama Story

### Advance Notice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר בֵּיסָא אֲזַיל יְתֵיב תְּרֵי סְרֵי שְׁנֵי בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא. כִּי אֲתָא, אֲמַר: לָא אֶיעֱבֵיד כִּדְעָבֵיד בֶּן חֲכִינַאי. עָיֵיל יָתֵיב בֵּ[י] מִדְרְשָׁא, שְׁלַח לְבֵיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama bar Bisa went and sat for twelve years in the study hall. When he came** back to his house, **he said: I will not do what the son of Ḥakhinai,** who came home suddenly with tragic consequences for his wife, **did. He went and sat in the study hall** in his hometown, **and sent** a message **to his house** that he had arrived.

Rabbi Chama bar Bissa went and studied in the *beis medrash* for twelve years. When it was time to return, he said to himself: *lo e'evid ke-Rabbi Chanina ben Chachinai* — I will not do what Rabbi Chanina ben Chachinai did. He would not simply appear at home after twelve years and risk killing his wife with shock. Instead, he settled in the *yeshiva* at the edge of his hometown and sent word to his household that he was returning, giving his wife time to prepare herself emotionally.

### Son Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּרֵיהּ, יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ. הֲוָה קָא מְשַׁאֲלֵיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא, חֲזָא דְּקָא מְחַדְּדִי שְׁמַעְתָּתֵיהּ, חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: אִי הֲוַאי הָכָא, הֲוָה לִי זֶרַע כִּי הַאי. עָל לְבֵיתֵיהּ, עָל בְּרֵיהּ, קָם קַמֵּיהּ. הוּא סָבַר לְמִשְׁאֲלֵיהּ שְׁמַעְתְּתָא קָא בָעֵי, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ: מִי אִיכָּא אַבָּא דְּקָאֵים מִקַּמֵּי בְּרָא?
>
> **Translation:** While he was sitting there **his son Rabbi Oshaya,** whom he did not recognize, **came and sat before him.** Rabbi Oshaya **asked him** questions about ***halakha*, and** Rabbi Ḥama **saw that the *halakhot* of** Rabbi Oshaya **were incisive,** i.e., he was very sharp. Rabbi Ḥama **was distressed and said: If I had been here** and had taught my son **I would have had a child like this.** Rabbi Ḥama then **went in to his house** and **his son went in** with him. Rabbi Ḥama then **stood up before him** to honor a Torah scholar, since **he thought** that **he wanted to ask him a matter of *halakha*. His wife said to him: Is there a father who stands up before his son?**

His son Rabbi Oshaya came to the local *beis medrash* — not knowing, of course, that the scholar who had arrived was his father — and began studying with him. Rabbi Chama was immediately struck by the young man's sharpness and depth. *Chalash da'ateih* — he despaired and grew despondent, thinking: *i havai hacha, havai li zara ki hai* — had I stayed at home, I could have raised and educated a son as sharp as this. Of course, it was his son. He came into his home, and his son followed him inside — Rabbi Chama still did not recognize him, and rose to honor the distinguished visitor.

### Three-Bound Cord

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: ״הַחוּט הַמְשׁוּלָּשׁ לֹא בִּמְהֵרָה יִנָּתֵק״ — זֶה רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר בֵּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rami bar Ḥama read** the verse **about him: "A threefold cord is not quickly broken"** (Ecclesiastes 4:12). **This** is referring to **Rabbi Oshaya, son of Rabbi Ḥama bar Bisa,** as he represented the third generation of Torah scholars in his family.

His wife said to him: *mi ika abba dkai mikmei bera* — is there a father who stands up for his own son? At that moment he realized who the young man was. He cited the verse about him: *hachut hameshulash lo bimehera yinatek* — a three-ply cord is not easily broken — for here was a chain of Torah scholarship: Rav Bissa, his father Rabbi Chama bar Bissa, and now his son Rabbi Oshaya, each a great *talmid chacham*. The Torah had passed through the generations despite — or perhaps alongside — the extended absences.

The message of this story, like the one before it, is genuinely mixed. There is a poignancy to the moment: Rabbi Chama did not know his own son. He had missed twelve years of his son's development and formation. Yet the ending does not dwell on that loss. The *hachut hameshulash* passage frames the outcome triumphantly. The Torah continuity is intact, the son is remarkable, and the family is reunited. The Gemara is not pretending there are no costs to twelve years of absence — the estrangement, the strangeness of not recognizing one's own child — but the overall arc of these stories, told through a Babylonian lens, tends toward resolution and even vindication.

## Rabbi Akiva Story

### Akiva Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא רָעֲיָא דְּבֶן כַּלְבָּא שָׂבוּעַ הֲוָה, חֲזִיתֵיהּ בְּרַתֵּיה דַּהֲוָה צְנִיעַ וּמְעַלֵּי, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִי מִקַּדַּשְׁנָא לָךְ, אָזְלַתְּ לְבֵי רַב? אֲמַר לַהּ: אִין, אִיקַּדַּשָׁא לֵיהּ בְּצִינְעָה וְשַׁדַּרְתֵּיהּ. שְׁמַע אֲבוּהָ, אַפְּקַהּ מִבֵּיתֵיהּ אַדְּרַהּ הֲנָאָה מִנִּכְסֵיהּ. אֲזַל יְתֵיב תְּרֵי סְרֵי שְׁנִין בְּבֵי רַב. כִּי אֲתָא, אַיְיתִי בַּהֲדֵיהּ תְּרֵי סְרֵי אַלְפֵי תַּלְמִידֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates: **Rabbi Akiva was the shepherd of ben Kalba Savua,** one of the wealthy residents of Jerusalem. The **daughter** of Ben Kalba Savua **saw that he was humble and refined. She said to him: If I betroth myself to you, will you go to the study hall** to learn Torah? **He said to her: Yes. She became betrothed to him privately and sent him** off to study. **Her father heard** this and became angry. **He removed her from his house** and took a **vow** prohibiting her from **benefiting from his property.** Rabbi Akiva **went** and **sat for twelve years in the study hall. When he came** back to his house **he brought twelve thousand students** with him.

We now come to the most famous story of the *daf*, which extends the twelve-year framework to twenty-four. Rabbi Akiva was the shepherd of Kalba Savua, one of the wealthiest men in Jerusalem. Kalba Savua's daughter perceived that he was *tznia u-mali* — modest and of fine character. She said to him: if I agree to marry you, will you go and study in *yeshiva*? He agreed. *Ikdashalah betzina* — he married her in secret. *Ve-shadrateih* — she sent him off to learn. When the father heard, he expelled her from his home and took a vow forbidding her from deriving any benefit from his considerable wealth, furious that his daughter had married this ignoramus.

### First 12 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְעָה דְּבֵיתְהוּ, הֲוָת קָא נָפְקָא לְאַפֵּיהּ. אֲמַרוּ לַהּ שִׁיבָבָתָא: שְׁאִילִי מָאנֵי לְבוֹשׁ וְאִיכַּסַּאי. אֲמַרָה לְהוּ: ״יוֹדֵעַ צַדִּיק נֶפֶשׁ בְּהֶמְתּוֹ״. כִּי מָטְיָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, נְפַלָה עַל אַפַּהּ, קָא מְנַשְּׁקָא לֵיהּ לְכַרְעֵיהּ. הֲווֹ קָא מְדַחֲפִי לַהּ שַׁמָּעֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: שִׁבְקוּהָ, שֶׁלִּי וְשֶׁלָּכֶם — שֶׁלָּהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** and as he approached **he heard an old man saying to** his wife: **For how long** will you lead** the life of a **widow of a living man,** living alone while your husband is in another place? **She said to him: If he** would **listen to me, he would sit** and study **for another twelve years.** When Rabbi Akiva heard this **he said: I have permission to do** this. **He went back and sat for another twelve years in the study hall. When he came** back **he brought twenty-four thousand students with him. His wife heard and went out** toward him **to greet him. Her neighbors said: Borrow some clothes and wear them,** as your current apparel is not appropriate to meet an important person. **She said to them: "A righteous man understands the life of his beast"** (Proverbs 12:10). **When she came to him she fell on her face and kissed his feet. His attendants pushed her** away as they did not know who she was, and **he said to them: Leave her** alone, as **my** Torah knowledge **and yours is** actually **hers.**

Rabbi Akiva went and learned in *yeshiva* for twelve years. When he was about to return, he overheard an old man speaking to his wife: *ad kama kamitbartat almanut chayut* — how long will you live the life of a living widow? She replied: *i ledidi tzaias, yativ treisar shani achrinei* — if my husband would listen to me, he should stay there another twelve years. At that, Rabbi Akiva said: *bi-reshut ka-avadna* — I am acting with her permission. And he turned back.

This moment is worth pausing over. Tosafot notes that this marks a significant shift in the Gemara's overall arc. Near the beginning of the *daf*, the concern was precisely that a husband might pressure his wife into granting permission she did not truly wish to give. Here, the wife is the one actively urging the husband to continue learning. The difference between the stories is not only one of different legal opinions or cultural frameworks, but also of different types of wives and different types of marriages — ones in which the wife is a genuine, enthusiastic partner in the enterprise of *talmud torah*, not merely a reluctant or resigned participant. Many *kollel* wives today inhabit exactly this kind of partnership — though how much is intrinsic and how much is shaped by the surrounding culture is a question the Gemara does not answer.

### Return with Students

Rabbi Akiva returned after another twelve years — twenty-four in total — bringing with him twenty-four thousand students. His wife heard and came out to greet him.

### Wife Knows Soul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לַהּ שִׁיבָבָתָא: שְׁאִילִי מָאנֵי לְבוֹשׁ וְאִיכַּסַּאי. אֲמַרָה לְהוּ: ״יוֹדֵעַ צַדִּיק נֶפֶשׁ בְּהֶמְתּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Her neighbors **said: Borrow some clothes and wear them,** as your current apparel is not appropriate to meet an important person. **She said to them: "A righteous man understands the life of his beast"** (Proverbs 12:10).

Her neighbors urged her: *shili mani levushei ve-ichasi* — borrow some fine clothes and dress properly to greet your husband. She replied: *yodea tzaddik nefesh behemto* — the righteous man knows the soul of his animal. She was describing herself through this verse, which carries a somewhat discordant note — she speaks of herself as a beast, in a very self-effacing way, though one recalls that her name was Rachel, which is itself the word for ewe. The language is striking in its degree of submission, whatever the underlying intention.

### Wife Partnership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מָטְיָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, נְפַלָה עַל אַפַּהּ, קָא מְנַשְּׁקָא לֵיהּ לְכַרְעֵיהּ. הֲווֹ קָא מְדַחֲפִי לַהּ שַׁמָּעֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: שִׁבְקוּהָ, שֶׁלִּי וְשֶׁלָּכֶם — שֶׁלָּהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **When she came to him she fell on her face and kissed his feet. His attendants pushed her** away as they did not know who she was, and **he said to them: Leave her** alone, as **my** Torah knowledge **and yours is** actually **hers.**

When she reached him and fell at his feet — *nafla al apa, ka-menashka* — kissing his feet, his attendant moved to push her away, as it was unseemly. Rabbi Akiva stopped him: *shabkuna, sheli ve-shelachem shelah hu* — leave her; everything that is mine and yours is hers. The partnership he articulates is complete. All the Torah, all the students, all the achievement — she shares in it equally.

### Father Reconciles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע אֲבוּהָ דַּאֲתָא גַּבְרָא רַבָּה לְמָתָא, אָמַר: אֵיזִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֶפְשָׁר דְּמֵפַר נִדְרַאי. אֲתָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדַּעְתָּא דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה מִי נְדַרְתְּ? אָמַר לוֹ: אֲפִילּוּ פֶּרֶק אֶחָד, וַאֲפִילּוּ הֲלָכָה אַחַת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הוּא. נְפַל עַל אַפֵּיהּ וְנַשְּׁקֵיהּ עַל כַּרְעֵיהּ וִיהַב לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא מָמוֹנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the meantime **her father heard that a great man came to the town. He said: I will go to him. Maybe he will nullify my vow** and I will be able to support my daughter. **He came to him** to ask about nullifying his vow, and Rabbi Akiva **said to him: Did you vow thinking that** this Akiva **would become a great man? He said to him:** If I had believed he would know **even one chapter or even one *halakha*** I would not have been so harsh. **He said to him: I am he.** Ben Kalba Savua **fell on his face and kissed his feet and gave him half of his money.**

Kalba Savua heard that a great *talmid chacham* had arrived in the city and went to seek an annulment of the vow he had made years earlier forbidding his daughter from his wealth. He did not yet know that this great scholar was his son-in-law. Rabbi Akiva asked him: when you made that vow, would you have made it even if you had known that she would marry a great Torah scholar? Kalba Savua replied: *afilu perek echad afilu halacha achas* — even if he had known one chapter, one *halacha* of Torah, I would never have made the vow. Rabbi Akiva then revealed: *ana hu* — I am the man. Kalba Savua fell on his face, kissed his feet — mirroring his daughter's gesture — and gave him half his wealth. The story that began with exile from wealth ends with its restoration, and more. Twelve plus twelve years of *talmud torah*, a wife's genuine consent, and the outcome is students, wealth, and reconciliation.

### Daughter & Ben Azai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרַתֵּיה דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עֲבַדָא לֵיהּ לְבֶן עַזַּאי הָכִי. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: רְחֵילָא בָּתַר רְחֵילָא אָזְלָא, כְּעוֹבָדֵי אִמָּא כָּךְ עוֹבָדֵי בְּרַתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Akiva's daughter did the same** thing **for ben Azzai,** who was also a simple person, and she caused him to learn Torah in a similar way, by betrothing herself to him and sending him off to study. **This** explains the folk saying **that people say: The ewe follows the ewe; the daughter's actions are the same as her mother's.**

*Barteih de-Rabbi Akiva avdah lei le-Ben Azai hachi* — Rabbi Akiva's daughter made the same arrangement with Ben Azai, who is elsewhere known as one whose *toraso umnasao* — his Torah was his entire occupation, such that he famously did not marry. Here his daughter of Rabbi Akiva made a similar pact with him. *Ve-hainu d'amrei inshi, racheila basar racheila azla* — that is what people say: one sheep follows another. The name *Rachel* is the word for ewe, and Rabbi Akiva's wife was named Rachel. *Ke-ovdei ima kach ovdei barta* — as the mother did, so the daughter.

## Rav Yosef Story

### Early Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא שַׁדְּרֵיהּ אֲבוּהִי לְבֵי רַב לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף. פְּסַקוּ לֵיהּ שֵׁית שְׁנֵי. כִּי הֲוָה תְּלָת שְׁנֵי, מְטָא מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי, אָמַר: אֵיזִיל וְאֶיחְזִינְהוּ לְאִינָשֵׁי בֵּיתִי. שְׁמַע אֲבוּהִי, שְׁקַל מָנָא וּנְפַק לְאַפֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוֹנָתְךָ נִזְכַּרְתָּ?
>
> **Translation:** On the same subject it is related: **Rav Yosef, son of Rava, was sent by his father to the study hall** to learn **before** the great Sage **Rav Yosef.** They **agreed** that **he** should sit for **six years** in the study hall. **When three years had** passed, **the eve of Yom Kippur arrived** and **he said: I will go and see the members of my household,** meaning his wife. **His father heard and took a weapon,** as if he were going to war, **and went to meet him.** According to one version **he said to him: Did you remember your mistress,** as you are abandoning your studies to see a woman?

Rav Yosef the son of Rava was sent by his father to learn in the *yeshiva* of Rav Yosef. They agreed on six years — not twelve. After three years, it was *Erev Yom Kippur*, and he thought to return home for a visit before completing his six years. He got up and came home early.

### Father Attacks

His father heard that his son was returning before the agreed six years were up. He took a weapon — *shakol mana* — and went out to confront him. He said: *zonachta nizkarta* — you remembered your prostitute? The implication: you were so consumed by your desire to see your wife that you broke your commitment to learning. An alternative version: *yonachta nizkarta* — you remembered your dove — which is less harsh but still dismissive, still insisting the son should not have returned early.

### Yom Kippur Fast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיטְּרוּד. לָא מָר אִיפְּסִיק, וְלָא מָר אִיפְּסִיק.
>
> **Translation:** Since both father and son were involved in an argument, **they were preoccupied** and **this Master did not eat the cessation** meal before Yom Kippur **and that Master** also **did not eat the cessation** meal that day.

In the confrontation that ensued, *itrid* — they became so agitated and absorbed that *lo mar ifsach ve-lo mar ifsach* — neither the father nor the son managed to eat their *seudah mafsekes*, the final meal before the *Yom Kippur* fast. The *Yom Kippur* connection recurs pointedly throughout the *daf*: Rav Rechumi came home once a year on *Erev Yom Kippur* and died when he failed to do so; Yehuda bar Rabbi Chiya came home every *Erev Shabbat*; here the son is coming home after three years and is met with anger for returning too early.

### Concluding Message

The *daf* ends with this confrontation. And the implicit final message is one that the *yeshivot* in Eretz Yisrael sometimes invoke — but which the Gemara itself uses to draw a different conclusion. The stories of Rabbi Akiva involve twelve years and then another twelve years. Those in the *yeshiva* world who cite such stories to justify extended unbroken absence sometimes present twelve-plus-twelve as equivalent to twenty-four. But they are not the same. Rabbi Akiva *returned* after the first twelve years — he came home, heard his wife's voice urging him to go back, and only then went back. He did not simply disappear for twenty-four years without contact. The final story of Rav Yosef's son makes the same point in reverse: a son who tries to come home after three of six years is prevented from doing so — but a six-year agreement is a six-year agreement, not a twelve-year one. The structures and the commitments matter, and the Gemara's rich and sometimes contradictory collection of stories does not resolve easily into a single ruling, but it leaves the attentive reader with a sense that both poles — the obligations of *talmud torah* and the obligations of marriage — demand to be taken with full seriousness, and that collapsing one into the other comes at a cost.
