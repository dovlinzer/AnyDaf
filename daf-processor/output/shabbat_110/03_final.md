# Shabbat 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Foods & Drinks

### Mishna Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין אוֹכְלִין אֵיזֹבְיוֹן בְּשַׁבָּת לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַאֲכַל בְּרִיאִים, אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא אֶת יוֹעֶזֶר, וְשׁוֹתֶה אַבּוּבְרוֹאֶה. כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין — אוֹכֵל אָדָם לִרְפוּאָה, וְכׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִין — שׁוֹתֶה, חוּץ מִמֵּי דְקָלִים וְכוֹס עִקָּרִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן לִירוֹקָה. אֲבָל שׁוֹתֶה הוּא מֵי דְקָלִים לִצְמָאוֹ, וְסָךְ שֶׁמֶן עִיקָּרִין שֶׁלֹּא לִרְפוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not eat *eizoveyon* on Shabbat because healthy people do not eat it,** and therefore it is clear that anyone eating it is doing so for its medicinal value. **However, one may eat** a plant called ***yo'ezer* and may drink *abuvro'e*.** Furthermore, **all** types of **food** that healthy people eat **may be eaten by a person** even **for medicinal** purposes. **And one may drink** all **drinks except for water from palm trees and a *kos ikarin* because they are** known as a remedy for **jaundice.** Therefore, it is prohibited to drink them on Shabbat for curative purposes. **However, one may drink palm tree water** on Shabbat **in order** to quench **his thirst, and one may smear *ikarin* oil** on himself **for non-medical purposes.**

The daf begins on 109b, picking up with the reading of the *mishna*. The *mishna* states: *ein ochlin ezovon b'Shabbos l'fi she'eino ma'achal b'ri'im* — one may not eat *ezovon* on Shabbat, because it is not something that healthy people eat. Since the only person who would consume *ezovon* is someone eating it for medicinal purposes, it is treated as an obvious *refuah* and therefore prohibited.

By contrast, the *mishna* permits eating *yo'ezer*, because *yo'ezer* is sometimes consumed for non-medicinal purposes as well. Similarly, one may drink *abuvroa* for the same reason — it is consumed under ordinary circumstances and is not exclusively a remedy. The *mishna* then states the general rule: *kol ha'ochalim ochel adam l'refuah* — one may eat all foods even for medicinal purposes, provided those foods are of the kind that people generally eat, and similarly one may drink all beverages. The exceptions are *mei dekalim* (water of palm trees) and *cos ikrin* (a cup infused with plant roots), which are excluded because they are exclusively medicinal — specifically, for jaundice. However, if one drinks *mei dekalim* out of thirst, that is permitted. Similarly, one may use oil with roots if it is not for the purposes of *refuah*.

### Intent & Practice

The *mishna*'s logic requires attention. Two conditions must converge for a prohibition to apply: first, the substance must be the type of thing standardly used for *refuah*; second, one's personal motivation must also be medicinal. If either condition is absent — either because the item is not exclusively medicinal in standard usage, or because the individual's intent is something other than healing — then no prohibition applies. The *gemara* notes that we are not concerned here with *maris ayin* (the appearance of impropriety).

## Plant Identification

### Ezov Identification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵזוֹב — ״אַבְרָתָה בַּר הֲמַג״, אֵיזֹבְיוֹן — ״אַבְרָתָה בַּר הִינְג״. עוּלָּא אָמַר: מָרְוָא חִיוָּרָא. עוּלָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ מָרְוָא חִיוָּרָא. אֲמַר: הַיְינוּ אֵזוֹב דִּכְתִיב בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. רַב פַּפֵּי אָמַר: שׁוּמְשׁוּק. אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב פַּפֵּי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דִּתְנַן: מִצְוַת אֵזוֹב שְׁלֹשָׁה קְלָחִין וּבָהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה גִבְעוֹלִין, וְשׁוּמְשׁוּק הוּא דְּמִשְׁתַּכְחָא הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef said:** Unspecified **hyssop** mentioned in the Torah is called ***abarta bar hamag*** in Babylonia, and ***eizoveyon*** is called ***abarta bar hing*. Ulla said:** The hyssop mentioned in the Torah is **white sage.** The Gemara relates: **Ulla happened** to come **to the house of Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda. They brought white sage before him. He said: This is the hyssop that is written in the Torah. Rav Pappi said:** Hyssop is the root called ***shumeshuk*. Rav Yirmeya of Difti said: In accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Pappi it is reasonable, as we learned** in a mishna: **The mitzva of hyssop** is that one take **three branches each with three stems, and *shumeshuk* is** the species **that is found with that** configuration of three stems on each of its branches.

The *gemara* opens by addressing a botanical question: which plant is the *ezov* mentioned in the Torah when it describes dipping *agudat ezov* in contexts such as the blood of Pesach or the *tahara* of the *metzora*? Rav Yosef says it is *Abarta bar Chama* — a specific variety of *abarta*, which is generally understood as hyssop. The *ezovon* mentioned in the *mishna*, however, is a related but distinct variety: *Abarta bar Hinag*. Ulla disagrees and says the *ezov* of the Torah is *Maru chivra* — white *maru*, roughly identifiable as white sage.

### Ulla & Shumshuk

When Ulla visited the home of Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, they brought before him this white *maru*. He declared: "This is the *ezov* written in the Torah." Rav Papa, however, offers a third identification: the *ezov* of the Torah is *shumshuk*. Rav Yirmeyah of Difti says that the position of Rav Papa is most plausible, citing a *mishnaic* rule that the *mitzvah* of *ezov* requires three stalks, each of which has three stems. It is *shumshuk* specifically that grows in precisely this configuration — three stems emerging from each stalk — and therefore it best fits the botanical description required for the *mitzvah*.

## Parasite Remedies

### Tapeworm Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי אָכְלִי לֵיהּ — לְקוּקְאיָנֵי. בְּמַאי אָכְלִי לֵיהּ — בִּשְׁבַע תַּמְרֵי אוּכָּמָתָא. מִמַּאי הָוְיָא — מִקִּימְחָא דִשְׂעָרֵי בְּמָנָא דַּחֲלֵיף עֲלֵיהּ אַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **For what purpose do** people **eat it?** It is eaten **for** curing **intestinal worms. And with what is it eaten?** It is eaten **with seven black dates. From what do** worms **come into being** in the intestines? They come into being **from** eating **barley flour from a vessel that held** the flour **for forty days** since it was ground.

Returning to the *mishna*, the *gemara* asks: for what condition would someone eat *ezovon*? The answer is *kukani* — which Rashi identifies as intestinal tapeworms. What would one eat it with? With seven black dates (*sheva tamrei uchmasa*). How does one come to contract this intestinal worm? From barley flour (*kimcha d'sha'arei*) that has had forty days pass over it without being eaten or properly preserved. The precision of these details — seven dates, forty days — is notable, and raises the question of how much of this reflects empirical observation versus other assumptions about the workings of illness and remedy.

### Liver Worm Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אוֹכֵל הוּא אֶת יוֹעֶזֶר. מַאי יוֹעֶזֶר? פּוּתַּנְק. לְמַאי אָכְלִי לַהּ — לְאַרְקָתָא. בְּמַאי אָכְלִי לַהּ — בִּשְׁבַע תַּמְרֵי חִיוּוֹרָתָא. מִמַּאי הָוְיָא — מֵאוּמְצָא וּמַיָּא אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא, וּמִבִּישְׂרָא שַׁמִּינָא אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא, וּמִבִּישְׂרָא דְתוֹרָא אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא, מֵאַמְגּוּזָא אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא, וּמִגִּירֵי דְרוּבְיָא אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא וּמִשְׁתֵּי מַיָּא אַבָּתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **However, one may eat** the ***yo'ezer*** plant. The Gemara asks: **What is *yo'ezer*?** The Gemara answers: It is the vegetable known as ***potnak***. **For what is it eaten?** It is eaten **to** cure **liver worms. With what is it eaten?** It is eaten with **seven white dates. From what do** worms **come into being** in the liver? **From** eating **raw meat with water on an empty** stomach, **and from** eating **fatty meat on an empty** stomach, **and from** eating **ox meat on an empty** stomach, **and from** eating **nuts on an empty** stomach, **and from eating fenugreek shoots on an empty** stomach **and drinking water thereafter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לִיבְלַע תַּחְלֵי חִיוּוֹרָתָא. וְאִי לָא — לִיתִּיב בְּתַעֲנִיתָא, וְלַיְתֵי בִּישְׂרָא שַׁמִּינָא וְלִישְׁדֵּי אַגּוּמְרֵי, וְלִימֵיץ גַּרְמָא, וְלִיגַמַּע חַלָּא. וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: חַלָּא לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָשֵׁי לְכַבְדָּא. וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי גִּירְדָּא דַאֲסִינְתָּא דִּגְרִידָא מֵעִילַּאי לְתַתַּאי, וְלָא מִתַּתַּאי לְעִילַּאי, דִּילְמָא נָפְקָא אַיְּידֵי פּוּמֵּיהּ, וְלִישְׁלְקַהּ בְּשִׁיכְרָא בֵּי שִׁיבָבֵי, וּלְמָחָר נִסְכְּרִינּוּן לְנִקְבִין דִּידֵיהּ וְלִישְׁתֵּי, וְכִי מִפְּנֵי — מִפְּנֵי אַפְּשִׁיחָא דְּדִקְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** one has liver worms but does **not** have any *potnak* handy, or if one has taken *potnak* and it did not help, **he should swallow white cress. And if** he does **not** have that or if it did not help him, **he should sit in fast, and take fatty meat and place it on coals and suck** the **bone and swallow vinegar. And some say:** One should **not** drink **vinegar because it is harmful to the liver. And if** none of those options are available, **let** one **take the sawdust that was scraped** from the shell **of a thorn bush from top to bottom, and not from bottom to top,** as **perhaps** then the worms **will come out through his mouth. And let** one then **boil** the scrapings **in beer at twilight and the next day close his nostrils and drink** it. **And when he defecates, he should defecate on the trunk of a palm tree.**

The *mishna* then states that one may eat *yo'ezer*, which the *gemara* identifies as *putna* — a term that does not translate neatly. This remedy is used for *urktza*, which Rashi identifies as liver worms. It is eaten with seven white dates (*sheva tamrei chivarta*). The *gemara* then asks how one contracts *urktza*: from eating meat that has been charred on coals and drinking water on an empty stomach; from fatty meat on an empty stomach; from ox meat on an empty stomach; from nuts on an empty stomach; from the leaves of fenugreek on an empty stomach; or from drinking water immediately afterward.

If this initial treatment with *yo'ezer* is not effective, one should swallow white cress (*tachli chivarta*). If that fails, one should fast, then obtain fatty meat — the very food implicated as a cause — char it on coals, suck the marrow from a bone, and swallow vinegar. Some say not to use vinegar, since vinegar is hard on the liver.

If that still fails, one should obtain the scraped bark of a particular bush, being careful that the scraping goes from top to bottom and not bottom to top. The directionality matters: if the bark is scraped the wrong way, the worm will exit upward through the mouth rather than downward, which is dangerous. This bark should be boiled in beer — either at dusk or in a neighbor's house, since Rashi offers both interpretations of *bei shivvei*, with the latter suggesting that the fumes may be harmful to the one preparing it. The next day, one should seal one's orifices (Rashi says to pinch the nose), drink the liquid without inhaling it, and then relieve oneself at the base of a palm tree so the worm is expelled from the body.

### Medical Knowledge

This raises the question of why such material is preserved here at all. At this point in the *sugya*, it no longer bears directly on the laws of Shabbat — it is simply folk medicine. Yet it appears here in the name of sages and was transmitted and copied by the scribes as authoritative content. The answer lies in how the rabbis conceived of their role: if a rabbi believed he possessed reliable traditional knowledge that could preserve the health of those who listened to him, he would feel it was within the scope of his responsibility to transmit that knowledge. Just as a modern rabbi might stand before a congregation on Rosh Hashanah and urge people to check their smoke detectors — crossing what some might consider a boundary between religious and practical guidance — the rabbis here saw the welfare of their communities as falling within their purview. The *stama* of the *gemara* evidently agreed and incorporated this material. It is worth noting that in contemporary *yeshiva* settings, these medical passages are generally not treated with the same normative weight as *halachic* rulings.

## Poison & Snake Cures

### Water Poison Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹתִין אַבּוּבְרוֹאֶה. מַאי אַבּוּבְרוֹאֶה? חוּמַטְרִיָּא. מַאי חוּמַטְרִיָּא? חוּטְרָא יְחִידָאָה. לְמַאי עָבְדִי לַהּ — לְגִילּוּיָא. וְאִי לָא — לַיְתֵי חַמְשָׁא כְּלִילֵי וְחַמְשָׁא כּוֹסָתָא דְשִׁיכְרָא, וְנִישְׁלוֹקִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי עַד דְּקַיְימָא אַאַנְפָּקָא, וְנִישְׁתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned the mishna: **And one may drink *abuvro'e*** on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **What is *abuvro'e*?** The Gemara answers: It is the plant known as ***ḥumtareya.*** The Gemara asks: **What is *ḥumtareya*?** It is that which is called **the lonely staff.** The Gemara asks: **For what** purpose **is it used?** It is used **for** one who drank **exposed** water from which we suspect a snake drank and left behind its venom. **And if** one does **not** have the lonely staff, or if it was ineffective, **let** him **take five roses and five cups of beer and cook them together until there is a quarter** of a *log* of liquid **and** then **drink** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִימֵּיהּ דְּרַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי עֲבַדָה לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא חַד כְּלִילָא וְחַד כּוֹסְתָּא דְשִׁיכְרָא. שְׁלַקָה וְאַישְׁקִיתֵיהּ, וּשְׁגַרָא תַּנּוּרָא וּגְרַפְתֵּיהּ, וְאוֹתִיבָה בֵּיהּ לְבֵינְתָּא בְּגַוֵּויהּ, וּנְפַק מִינֵּיהּ כְּהוּצָא יַרְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami's mother made** this remedy **for a certain man** who had drunk exposed water suspected of having venom in it. She took **one rose and one cup of beer** and **she boiled** them. **She gave it to him to drink, and lit the oven, and swept out the coals, and placed a brick inside it** on which he could sit and not get burned. **And something** that appeared **like a green leaf emerged,** i.e., he vomited the venom.

### Snake Poison Case

The mother of Rav Acha bar Imi made this remedy — in the proportional amounts of one rose to one cup of beer — for a certain man. She boiled it, gave it to him to drink, then lit the oven, swept it out so it would not be burning hot, and placed a brick inside so he could sit without direct contact with the oven floor, which would have burned him. She essentially created a heated room. As a result, something yellow-green came out of him — *k'hutza yarka* — which represented the poison being expelled.

### Snake Venom Options

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַוְיָא אָמַר: רְבִיעֵתָא דַחֲלָבָא מֵעִיזָּא חִיוָּרְתָּא. רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר: לַיְיתֵי אֶתְרוֹגָא חֲלִיתָא וְלִחַיְּיקֵיהּ וְלִימַלְּיֵיהּ דּוּבְשָׁא, וְלוֹתְבֵהּ בֵּי מִילְלֵי דְנוּרָא, וְלֵיכְלֵיהּ. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר, מֵי רַגְלַיִם בְּנֵי אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם: בַּרְזִינָא — לְזִיבּוּרָא, רְבִיעֵתָא — לְעַקְרַבָּא, פַּלְגָא רִיבְעָא — לְגִילּוּיָא, רִיבְעָא — אֲפִילּוּ לִכְשָׁפִים מְעַלּוּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲנִיגְרוֹן, וַאֲבַנְגַּר, וְתִירְיָיקָה — מְעַלּוּ בֵּין לְגִילּוּיָא בֵּין לִכְשָׁפִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avya said:** The remedy for one who has swallowed venom is to drink **a quarter** of a *log* **of milk from a white goat. Rav Huna bar Yehuda said: Let** one **bring a sweet citron and make a hole in it, and fill it with honey, and place it among burning coals, and eat it. Rabbi Ḥanina said: Urine that is forty days old** is an effective remedy for several maladies: **A very small cupful** is beneficial in treating **a hornet** sting. **A quarter** of a *log* is beneficial in treating **a scorpion** bite. **Half a *log*** is effective in treating one who drank **exposed** water. **A *log* is even effective** in counteracting **witchcraft. Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Water in which spinach was boiled **[*anigron*]**, **and** water in which *banagri* grass was boiled **[*avangar*]**, **and balsam are effective for both** one who drank **exposed** water **and** as an antidote **for witchcraft.**

Rav Avya says: take a *revi'is* of milk from a white goat. Rav Huna bar Yehuda says: take a sweet *etrog*, bore a hole in it, fill it with honey, place it among glowing coals, and eat it. Rav Chanina says: use urine that is forty days old. A small amount (*bar zini*) is effective for wasp stings; a *revi'is* for scorpion stings; half a *log* for uncovered water; and a full *log* is so potent that it even works against witchcraft. Rav Yochanan mentions *anigron* — which Rashi identifies as a liquid produced from cooking various vegetables such as radishes or beets — together with *avangar* and *tzirika* (which some identify as balsam). These are effective against both the effects of uncovered water and witchcraft.

### Snake Bite Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאן דִּבְלַע חִיוְיָא — לוֹכְלֵיהּ כְּשׁוּתָא בְּמִילְחָא, וְלַירְהֲטֵיהּ תְּלָתָא מִילֵּי. רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּבְלַע חִיוְיָא. אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּפַרָּשָׁא. אוֹכְלֵיהּ כְּשׁוּתָא בְּמִילְחָא וְאַרְהֲטֵיהּ קַמֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא מִילֵּי, וּנְפַק מִינֵּיהּ גּוּבֵּי גּוּבֵּי. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי בְּלַע חִיוְיָא, אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּפַרָּשָׁא. אוֹכְלֵיהּ כְּשׁוּתָא בְּמִילְחָא וְאַרְהֲטֵיהּ קַמֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא מִילֵּי וּנְפַק מִינֵּיהּ גּוּבֵּי גּוּבֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **One who swallowed a snake should be fed hops in salt, and** then he should be made to **run** a distance of **three *mil*.** The Gemara relates: **Rav Shimi bar Ashi saw a person who swallowed a snake,** and Rav Ashi **appeared** to that person **as a horseman.** Rav Shimi **fed him hops with salt and made him run in front of him for three *mil*, and** the snake **came out of him in pieces. Some say** that **Rav Shimi bar Ashi** was the one who **swallowed a snake,** and **Elijah came** and **appeared to** Rav Ashi **as a horseman. He fed him hops with salt and made him run in front of him for three *mil*, and** the snake **came out of him in pieces.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאן דְּטַרְקֵיהּ חִיוְיָא — לֵיתֵי עוּבָּרָא דַחֲמָרָא חִיוָּרְתִּי, וְלִיקְרְעֵיהּ, וְלוֹתְבֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּכַח טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who was bitten by a snake should have the fetus of a white donkey brought** to him**, and it should be torn open and placed on** the snakebite. The Gemara says: **And this applies** only when the mother does **not** have a **condition that will cause it to die within twelve months** [***tereifa*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר קַשָּׁא דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא דְּטַרְקֵיהּ חִיוְיָא, הֲוָה תְּלֵיסְרֵי חֲמָרֵי חִיוָּרָתָא בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, קַרְעִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ, וְאִישְׁתְּכַחוּ טְרֵיפָה. הֲוַאי חֲדָא בְּהָהוּא גִּיסָא דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, עַד דְּאָזְלִי מַיְיתִי לַהּ אַכְלַהּ אַרְיָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: דִילְמָא חִיוְיָא דְּרַבָּנַן טַרְקֵיהּ, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אָסְוָתָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּפֹרֵץ גָּדֵר יִשְּׁכֶנּוּ נָחָשׁ״. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִין רַבִּי, דְּכִי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב, גְּזַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר בִּיסְנָא דְּלֵיכָּא דְּלַימְטֵי אַסָּא וְגִידְמֵי לְבֵי הִילּוּלָא בְּטַבְלָא, וַאֲזַל אִיהוּ אַמְטִי אַסָּא וְגִידְמֵי לְבֵי הִילּוּלָא בְּטַבְלָא. טַרְקֵיהּ חִיוְיָא, וּמִית.
>
> **Translation:** Jewish **official** in **Pumbedita that was bitten by a snake. There were thirteen white donkeys in Pumbedita** and **they tore them all** open **and they were found to be *tereifot*. There was one** donkey **on the other side** of **Pumbedita,** and **until they went to bring it, a lion ate it. Abaye said to them:** Since all of these things have happened, **perhaps a snake of the Rabbis bit him, for which there is no cure, as it is written:** "One that digs a pit will fall into it, and **one who breaches a fence will be bitten by a snake**" (Ecclesiastes 10:8). Perhaps this person violated a rabbinic decree, also known as a fence, and was bitten by a snake as punishment. That bite has no cure. **They said to him: Yes, my teacher,** it is so, **as when Rav died Rav Yitzḥak bar Bisna ruled that one may not bring myrtle and** palm **branches to a wedding hall to** accompany **bells, and he went** and **brought myrtle and** palm **branches to the wedding hall with bells.** Therefore, **a snake bit him. And** it is reported that ultimately the official **died.**

If someone has actually swallowed a snake, they should be fed hops in salt and made to run three *mil*. The *gemara* then relates the story of Rav Shimi bar Ashi, who encountered a man who had swallowed a snake. He disguised himself as a horseman, made the man eat hops in salt, and chased him on horseback, compelling him to run three *mil*, after which the snake came out in pieces. According to another version, it was Rav Shimi bar Ashi himself who had swallowed the snake, and it was Elijah the prophet who appeared to him in the guise of a horseman, administered the hops and salt, and made him run three *mil* until the snake came out in clumps.

For a snakebite, one should obtain the fetus of a white donkey, cut it open, and apply it to the bite. This remedy is only valid if the mother was not a *treifa*.

The *gemara* then tells a striking story. A Jewish officer of the king in Pumbedita was bitten by a snake. There were thirteen white donkeys in Pumbedita — surely, it seemed, one could be used. They opened all of them and found all of them to be *treifos*. There was one more donkey on the other side of Pumbedita, but by the time they went to retrieve it, a lion had eaten it. Every avenue was blocked.

### Snake Bite Cause

Abaye said to those who were trying to help: perhaps it was not an ordinary snake that bit him — perhaps it was the *chivya d'rabanan*, the snake of the rabbis, for which there is no remedy. He cited the verse: *u'poretz geder yishchenu nachash* — one who breaks through the fence will be bitten by a snake — a passage used as rhetorical shorthand for violating rabbinic norms and fences.

They replied: indeed, that may be so. When Rav was dying, Rav Yitzchak bar Bisna issued a decree that no one should bring *hadasim* and palm branches to a wedding feast. This officer had gone ahead and done precisely that — not a formal *issur d'rabbanan*, but a localized rabbinic enactment, a particular *geder*. And yet he had not shown it the proper respect. The snake bit him and he died. This fits with other passages in the *gemara* on the same theme, and is explicitly not the ironic kind of story — as the lecturer notes, this is not the *gemara* about Shmuel failing to show Rav where the bathroom was. Here, Rav was being actively honored through this enactment, and the officer violated it.

### Snake Avoidance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאן דְּכַרְכֵיהּ חִיוְיָא — לִינְחוֹת לְמַיָּא וְלִיסְחוֹף דִּיקּוּלָא אַרֵישֵׁיהּ וּלְהַדְּקֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ, וְכִי סָלֵיק עִילָּוֵיהּ — לִישְׁדְּיֵהּ לְמַיָּא וְלִיסְלוֹק וְלֵיתֵי. הַאי מַאן דְּמִיקַּנֵּי בֵּיהּ חִיוְיָא, אִי אִיכָּא חַבְרֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ — לַירְכְּבֵיהּ אַרְבַּע גַּרְמִידֵי, וְאִי לָא — לִישְׁוַאר נִגְרָא, וְאִי לָא — לִיעְבַּר נַהֲרָא, וּבְלֵילְיָא — לוֹתְבֵיהּ לְפוּרְיֵהּ — אַאַרְבַּע חָבְיָתָא, וְנִיגְנֵי בֵּי כוֹכְבֵי, וְלַיְיתֵי אַרְבְּעָה שׁוּנָּרֵי וְלֵיסְרִינְהוּ בְּאַרְבְּעָה כַּרְעֵי דְפוּרְיֵיהּ, וְלַיְיתֵי שַׁחֲפֵי וְלִישְׁדֵּי הָתָם, דְּכִי שָׁמְעִי קָלֵיהּ אָכְלִי לֵיהּ. הַאי מַאן דְּרָהֵיט אַבָּתְרֵיהּ — לִירְהוֹט בֵּי חָלָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional information about the dangers posed by snakes and how to deal with them. **One whom a snake encircled should descend into water and place a basket on** his **head and remove** the snake slowly **from him** into the basket. **And once the snake goes into** the basket, **let** him **throw it into the water and climb and emerge. One at whom a snake is angry** and is being pursued by a snake, **if he has another with him, let** him **ride him four cubits. And if not, let** him **jump** over **a ditch. And if not, let** him **cross a river. And at night let** him **place his bed on four barrels and sleep** outside beneath **the stars. And let** one **bring four cats and let** one **tie them to the four legs of the bed. And let** one **bring twigs and branches and throw them there so that when** the cats **hear the sound** of the snake crawling **they will eat it. One who is being pursued** by a snake, **let** him **run in sand** because a snake cannot move as quickly in sand as a person can.

If a snake has wrapped itself around a person, one should go down into water, place a basket (*dikula*) on one's head, and tilt the basket toward the snake so it slides into the basket, then throw the basket into the water and climb out.

If a snake is pursuing someone — Rashi explains that the snake tracks a person by scent from the ground — and there is a companion nearby, have the companion carry you for four *amos*, so the snake can no longer smell your footsteps. If that is not possible, jump over a stream of water; the snake may lose your scent or be unable to follow. If that fails, cross a river. At night, if you fear a snake will come to you while you sleep, raise your bed on four barrels so it is elevated from the ground, sleep under the open sky so the snake cannot come through the roof, and tie four cats to the four posts of the bed. Scatter woodchips around the bed: when the snake tries to slither through them, the cats will hear the sound and attack it.

If a snake is actively running after someone, one should run through sand, which apparently impedes the snake's pursuit.

### Snakes & Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אִיתְּתָא דְּחָזְיָא חִיוְיָא, וְלָא יָדְעָה אִי יָהֵיב דַּעְתֵּיהּ עִילָּוַהּ אִי לָא יָהֵיב דַּעְתֵּיהּ עִילָּוַהּ — תִּשְׁלַח מָאנַהּ וְתִשְׁדֵּי קַמֵּיהּ, אִי מִכְרַךְ בְּהוּ דַּעְתֵּיהּ עִילָּוַהּ, וְאִי לָא — לָא יָהֵיב דַּעְתֵּיהּ עִילָּוַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A woman who is seen** by **a snake and does not know whether it has directed his attention** toward **her or whether it has not directed his attention** toward **her, she should remove her garment and throw it before** the snake. **If** the snake **wraps itself in** the garments, it is an indication that **it has directed his attention** toward **her; and if not,** it is an indication that **it has not directed his attention** toward **her.**

If a woman sees a snake and does not know whether the snake has taken an interest in her — a theme rooted in the imagery of the *midrash* about the serpent's desire for Chavah in *Gan Eden* — she should remove her garments and throw them before the snake. If the snake wraps itself within the clothing, this indicates it is indeed drawn to her. If not, it is not. Should it prove that the snake is attracted to her, one option suggested is for her to have relations with her husband in front of the snake, so it understands she is spoken for. Some reject this, arguing it would only inflame the snake's desire further. Rather, she should take something from her hair and her nails and throw them at it, saying: *dishtana ana* — I am a *niddah*. There seems to be operating here a sense that the things associated with a woman in the state of *niddah* carry some deterrent or even demonic power that would repel the snake.

### Internal Snake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּהּ תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ קַמֵּיהּ. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּתָקֵיף לֵיהּ יִצְרֵיהּ! אֶלָּא תִּשְׁקוֹל מִמַּזְּיַהּ וּמִטּוּפְרַהּ וְתִשְׁדֵּי בֵּיהּ, וְתֵימָא: ״דִּישְׁתָּנָא אֲנָא״. הַאי אִיתְּתָא דְּעָיְלִי בַּהּ חִיוְיָא, לִיפַסְּעוּהָ וְלוֹתְבוּהָ אַתַּרְתֵּי חָבְיָתָא, וְלַיְתֵי בִּישְׂרָא שַׁמִּינָה וְלִישְׁדֵּי אַגּוּמְרֵי, וְלַיְתֵי אַגָּנָא דְּתַחְלֵי וְחַמְרָא רֵיחְתָנָא וְלוֹתְבוּ הָתָם, וְלִיטְרוֹקִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וְלִינְקוֹט צְבָתָא בִּידֵהּ, דְּכִי מוֹרַח רֵיחָא — נָפֵיק וְאָתֵי, וְלִישְׁקְלֵיהּ וְלִיקְלְיֵיהּ בְּנוּרָא, דְּאִי לָא — הָדַר עִילָּוַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is her remedy** so the snake will leave her alone? **She should have relations** with her husband **before** the snake. **Some say:** If she has relations in front of the snake, **all the more so that its desire will become stronger. Rather, she should take from her hair and her nails and throw them at** the snake, **and say** the following **to it** as an incantation: **I am a menstruating woman [*dishtana*]. A woman whom a snake has entered, let them spread her legs and place her on two barrels, and let them bring fatty meat and throw it onto coals. And let them bring her a bowl of cress and fragrant wine and place them there and mix them together. And** she should **take tongs in her hand, as when** the snake **smells** the **fragrance** it **emerges. And** then one **should take** the snake **and burn it in the fire, as if** it is **not** burned, **it will come back onto her.**

If a snake has entered into a woman's body, she should straddle two barrels with her legs spread, bring fatty meat and set it to burn on coals, and place a vessel of cress (*tachlei*) and aromatic wine underneath her. The aromatic meat and wine are intended to draw the snake out by scent. She should hold a pair of tongs in her hand: when the snake smells the aroma and emerges, she can grab it with the tongs and burn it in the fire — because if she does not do this, it will re-enter.

## General Principle

### Permitted Foods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין כּוּ׳. כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי טְחוֹל לַשִּׁינַּיִם וְכַרְשִׁינִין לִבְנֵי מֵעַיִים. כׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִין לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מֵי צְלָפִין בְּחוֹמֶץ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: מַהוּ לִשְׁתּוֹת מֵי רַגְלַיִם בְּשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: ״כׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִין שׁוֹתֶה״, וּמֵי רַגְלַיִם לָא שָׁתוּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **All** types of **food** that healthy people eat may be eaten by one eating them for medicinal purposes on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: The phrase: **All foods, what** does it come **to include?** The Gemara answers: It comes **to include spleen for** healing **teeth** and **vetch for** healing **intestines,** although they are not common foods. We also learned in the mishna: And one may drink all **drinks** on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: The phrase: **All drinks, what** does it come **to include?** The Gemara answers: It comes **to include** mixing **water in which capers have soaked, with vinegar. Ravina said to Rava: What** is the ruling with regard to **drinking urine on Shabbat?** Rava **said to him: We** already **learned** in the mishna: **One may drink all drinks, and people do not drink urine** and is not considered a drink. It is only consumed for medical purposes and is therefore prohibited.

Returning to the *mishna*'s general statement — *kol ha'ochalim* — the *gemara* asks: what does this phrase come to include? It comes to include eating spleen for the benefit of one's teeth, and *karshinin* (vetch) for stomach ailments. These are foods that, while unusual, are not exclusively medicinal — people do eat them under ordinary circumstances — and therefore one may consume them even with a medicinal intent.

A brief but practically significant digression: the question of vitamins on Shabbat is relevant here. In contemporary *halachic* discussion, most *poskim* tend to define the scope of these prohibitions as narrowly as possible, given that the original concern about *shchikat samamanim* (grinding medicines) is largely inapplicable today. Many hold that vitamins do not fall under the category of *ochel* or *refuah* in the relevant sense. Interestingly, this connects to a separate question: vitamins generally do require a *hechsher*, unlike many medications.

### Beverages Permitted

The *mishna*'s phrase *kol hamashkin* — all beverages — comes to include drinking *mei tzalpin* in vinegar, which Rashi identifies as a caper fruit preparation. All such beverages are permitted even for medicinal purposes, with the exception of *mei dekalim* and *cos ikrin*.

### Urine Question

Ravina asked Rava: what is the law regarding drinking urine on Shabbat? Urine had been cited earlier as an effective remedy for multiple conditions. Rava replied: the *mishna* itself already teaches *kol hamashkin shosseh* — and *mei raglayim* (urine) is not something people drink. The implication is that *mashkeh* is not merely any liquid in physical form, but a potable beverage — something considered drinkable in normal human usage. Since people do not ordinarily drink urine, it does not fall under the category of *mashkeh*, and drinking it for medicinal purposes would constitute a prohibited *refuah*. One might ask why Ravina raised the question at all given that the *mishna* already prohibited *mei dekalim* on medicinal grounds — the distinction he may have sought to draw was that urine, unlike palm water, is not a recognized beverage in any sense. In any case, Rava's answer settles the matter on definitional grounds.

### Water Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִמֵּי דְקָלִים. תָּנָא: חוּץ מִמֵּי דְקָרִים. מַאן דִּתְנָא ״מֵי דְקָרִים״ — שֶׁהֵם דּוֹקְרִים אֶת הַמָּרָה, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״מֵי דְקָלִים״ — שֶׁיּוֹצְאִין מִן שְׁנֵי דִקְלֵי. מַאי מֵי דְקָלִים? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בְּרוֹנָא: תַּרְתֵּי תָּלֵאי אִיכָּא בְּמַעְרְבָא, וְנָפְקָא עֵינָא דְמַיָּא מִבֵּינַיְיהוּ. כָּסָא קַמָּא — מְרַפֵּי, אִידַּךְ — מְשַׁלְשֵׁל, וְאִידַּךְ — כִּי הֵיכִי דְּעָיְילִי הָכִי נָפְקִי. אָמַר עוּלָּא: לְדִידִי שְׁתֵי [לִי] שִׁיכְרָא דְּבַבְלָאֵי, וּמְעַלֵּי מִינַּיְיהוּ. וְהוּא דְּלָא רְגִיל בֵּיהּ אַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: One may drink anything on Shabbat **except for palm tree water [*mei dekalim*].** It was **taught** slightly differently in the *Tosefta*: **Water that stabs [*mei dekarim*].** The Gemara explains: **The one who taught water that stabs** means **that the** waters **pierce the gall bladder; and the one who said palm tree water** means **that they come from two palm trees.** In order to explain **what palm tree water** is, **Rabba bar Beruna said: There are two palm trees in Eretz Yisrael, and a spring of water emerges from between them. The first cup** one drinks of this water **loosens** the intestines, **another** cup **causes diarrhea, and another,** a third cup, **just as it entered** as water, **so** too **it emerges. Ulla said: For me, I drink Babylonian beer, and** it **is more effective than** palm tree water in causing diarrhea. The Gemara comments: **And this is** true. It is effective for the stomach when the person who drinks it **has not become accustomed to** beer **for forty days.**

An alternative version of the *mishna* reads not *mei dekalim* (palm tree water) but *mei dekarim* — water of piercing. This refers to a liquid that pierces through the gallbladder, used medicinally for gallbladder conditions. According to those who maintain the reading *mei dekalim*, Rabbah bar Bar Chana explains that this refers to a specific spring in Eretz Yisrael that emerges between two particular palm trees. The first cup from this spring loosens the bowels; additional cups cause diarrhea; and any food consumed afterward passes straight through. Ulla adds that in his experience, Babylonian mead was more effective than even this water as a purgative — though only if one has not been drinking it for the previous forty days.

## Seasonal Remedies

### Zeysum Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: זֵיתוֹם הַמִּצְרִי תִּילְתָּא שְׂעָרֵי, וְתִילְתָּא קוּרְטְמֵי, וְתִילְתָּא מִילְחָא. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: תִּילְּתָא חִיטֵּי, וְתִילְתָּא קוּרְטְמֵי, וְתִילְתָּא מִילְחָא וְכַמּוֹנָא, וְסִימָנָיךְ — סִיסָאנֵי. וְשָׁתֵי לְהוּ בֵּין דִּבְחָא לַעֲצַרְתָּא, דִּקְמִיט — מְרַפֵּי לֵיהּ, וְדִרְפֵי — קָמֵיט לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** Water that stabs is **Egyptian *zitom*,** which is made from **one-third barley, and one-third saffron, and one-third salt. Rav Pappa said:** It is **one-third wheat, and one-third saffron, and one-third salt and cumin. And** this is **your mnemonic** to remember which said *zitom* is made from barley: A basket which is called ***sisanei*,** a word with two *samekhs.* This word alludes to the fact that Rav Yosef, who has a *samekh* in his name, is the one who says that Egyptian *zitom* is made from barley [*se'orim*], which has the letter *sin. Sin* is interchangeable with *samekh*. **And one** should **drink it between Passover and *Shavuot*. For one whose** intestines **are blocked,** it will **loosen** his intestines and cure **him; and** for one **whose** bowels are **loose,** it will **block him** and cure him as well**.**

Rav Yosef describes the preparation of Egyptian *zeysum*: one third barley, one third saffron, one third salt.

### Variant & Memory

Rav Papa gives a variant: one third wheat instead of barley, along with one third saffron and one third salt. To remember which sage says which version, there is a mnemonic: the word *sisani* contains two *samechs*. Rav Yosef says barley (*sa'arei*), which begins with a *samech*, and Rav Yosef's name is recalled by pairing it with the double-*samech* of the mnemonic.

### Pesach-Shavuot

This preparation is drunk between Pesach and Shavuot — *bein divcha l'atzarta*, between the Passover sacrifice and *Shavuos*. For those suffering from constipation (*de-kami*), it loosens the bowels; for those suffering from looseness, it has the opposite, binding effect. The effectiveness seems to be tied to the spring season.

## Cos Ikrin Formula

### Cos Ikrin Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוֹס עִקָּרִין. מַאי כּוֹס עִקָּרִין? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לַיְיתֵי מַתְקַל זוּזָא קוּמָא אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרָיָא, וּמַתְקַל זוּזָא גַּבְיָא גִּילָא, וּמַתְקַל זוּזָא כּוּרְכְּמָא רִישְׁקָא, וְלִישְׁחֲקִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** we also learned about a ***kos ikarin*** in the mishna. The Gemara asks: **What is a *kos ikarin*? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Let** one **bring the weight of a *zuz* of Alexandrian gum, and a weight of a *zuz* of alum, and a weight of a *zuz* of garden saffron, and let** one **grind them together.**

The *mishna* mentioned *cos ikrin* as one of the prohibited medicinal drinks. The *gemara* now defines what it is. Rabbi Yochanan says: take the weight of a *zuz* of Alexandrian *quma* — which Rashi identifies as a tree sap — the weight of a *zuz* of *gavya gila*, which Rashi identifies as alum, and the weight of a *zuz* of *korkema rishka*, which Rashi identifies as saffron (specifically garden saffron). Grind all three together.

### Three Ingredients

The procedure **for** treating **a *zava*** is that she should drink these **three** ingredients **with wine,** and she will be cured of her emission **and will not become barren. For** treating **jaundice** one should drink **two** of these ingredients **with beer; however, one will become sterile** from it.

### Two Ingredients

For jaundice (*yerokona*), one takes only two of the three ingredients and mixes them in beer. However, this two-ingredient combination carries a significant side effect: it causes sterilization.

## Zavah Treatments

### Remedy 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְזָבָה תְּלָתָא בְּחַמְרָא, וְלָא מִיעַקְרָא. וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי תְּלָתָא קְפִיזֵי שַׁמְכֵי פָרְסָאֵי, וְנִישְׁלוֹק בְּחַמְרָא, וְנַשְׁקְיַיהּ, וְנֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** It was said that **for** treating **a *zava*,** she should drink these **three** ingredients **with wine** and she will be healed from her emission **and will not become barren. And if** it is **not** effective, **let one bring three vessels full of Persian onions, and boil them in wine, and give it to her to drink. And we say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If the *cos ikrin* was not effective for the *zava*, the *gemara* prescribes a series of escalating remedies. The first: take three *kafizei* (which Rashi indicates are large vessels holding three *lugin* each) of Persian onion (*somki parsai*), boil it in wine, and have her drink it. One should then say to her: *kumi mi-zovayich* — rise up from your flow. The verbal command accompanying the remedy is a recurring element throughout this treatment series.

### Remedy 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לוֹתְבַהּ אַפָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים, וְלַנְקְטַהּ כָּסָא דְחַמְרָא בִּידַהּ, וְלַיְתֵי אִינִישׁ מֵאֲחוֹרַהּ וְלִיבַעֲתַהּ, וְלֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **seat her** at a **crossroads, and she should take a cup of wine in her hand, and let a person come from behind her and frighten her and say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If that was not effective, have the woman sit at a crossroads holding a cup of wine. A person should come from behind her and startle her — an intervention that resembles remedies for conditions like hiccups. One should again say to her: *kumi mi-zovayich*.

### Remedy 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי בּוּנָא דְכַמּוֹנָא וּבוּנָא דְמוֹרִיקָא וּבוּנָא דְשַׁבְּלִילְתָּא, וְנִישְׁלוֹק בְּחַמְרָא, וְנַשְׁקְיַיהּ, וְנֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is also **not** effective, **let one bring** her **a fistful [*buna*] of cumin, and a fistful of saffron, and a fistful of fenugreek, and cook** them **in wine, and give it to her to drink, and say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If that was not effective, take a handful of cumin (*kamona*), a handful of saffron (*morika*), and a handful of fenugreek (*shavlisa*), boil them in wine, and have her drink it. Say to her: *kumi mi-zovayich*.

### Remedy 4

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי שִׁיתִּין שִׁיעֵי דְּדַנָּא, וְלַשְׁפְּיַהּ, וְלֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let one bring sixty barrel seals, soak them, and then spread it** on her **and say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If that was not effective, take sixty seals of a barrel — the number sixty may be hyperbolic — and place them on her, perhaps with them soaked in water according to Rashi's interpretation, and say: *kumi mi-zovayich*.

### Remedy 5

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי פְּשִׁיטְנָא, וְלִישְׁלוֹק בְּחַמְרָא, וְלִשְׁפְּיַהּ, וְנֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let one bring *pashtina*,** a type of grass, **boil it in wine, and then spread it** on her **and say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If that was not effective, take a grass called *pashtina* and rub it on her, saying: *kumi mi-zovayich*.

### Remedy 6

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי חַרְנוּגָא דְּהִיגְתָּא רוֹמִיתָא, וְלִיקְלֵי, וְלִיסְּבַהּ בְּשַׁחֲקֵי דְכִיתָּנָא בְּקַיְיטָא וּבְשַׁחֲקֵי דַעֲמַר גּוּפְנָא בְּסִיתְוָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let one bring a thistle** that grows **on Roman thorns and burn it, and place its** ashes **in rags from linen clothing in the summer and in rags from cotton clothing in the winter.**

If that was not effective, take *charnoga* — which Rashi describes as a grass that grows among thorn bushes — burn it, and apply the ashes with worn-out linen garments in the summer or with worn-out cotton garments in the winter.

### Remedy 7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לִיכְרֵי שְׁבַע בֵּירֵי, וְלִיקְלֵי בְּהוּ שְׁבִישָׁתָא דְעׇרְלָה, וְלַינְקְטַהּ כָּסָא דְחַמְרָא בִּידַהּ, וְלוֹקְמַהּ מֵהָא וְלוֹתְבַהּ אַהָא, וְלוֹקְמַהּ מֵהָא וְלוֹתְבַהּ אַהָא, וְאַכֹּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא לֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let one dig seven pits and burn young** grape **shoots in them** from vines that are ***orla*, and place a cup of wine in her hand and raise her up from that** pit **and sit her in** another pit, **and raise her from that** pit **and sit her in this** pit, **and raise her from that** pit **and sit her in this** other pit. **And each and every** time **say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If that was not effective, dig seven holes and burn in each one a young shoot of an *orlah* grapevine — a vine in its first three years. Have her hold a cup of wine in her hand, and move her from hole to hole, sitting in each one. At each hole, one should say to her: *kumi mi-zovayich*.

### Remedy 8

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי סְמִידָא, וְלֵיסְכַהּ מִפַּלְגָא לְתַתַּאי, וְלֵימָא לַהּ: קוּם מִזּוֹבִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let one bring fine flour and place it on the bottom half of her** body **and say to her: Stop** emitting **your discharge.**

If that was not effective, take fine flour (*samida*) and apply it from her waist downward. Say to her: *kumi mi-zovayich*.

### Remedy 9

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי בֵּיעֲתָא דְנַעָמִיתָא, וְלִיקְלֵי, וְלִיסְּבַהּ בְּשַׁחֲקֵי דְכִיתָּנָא בְּקַיְיטָא וּבְשַׁחֲקֵי דַעֲמַר גּוּפְנָא בְּסִיתְוָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let** one **bring an ostrich egg and burn it, and place its** ashes **in linen rags during the summer, and in cotton rags during the winter.**

If that was not effective, take an ostrich egg, burn it, and apply the ashes with worn-out linen garments in the summer or worn-out cotton garments in the winter.

### Remedy 10

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לִיפְתַּח לַהּ חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא לִשְׁמַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is also **not** effective, **let** one **open a barrel of wine for her,** and let her drink it.

If that was not effective, open barrels of wine for her — specifying that the opening is in her name. Wine, as a red liquid, appears throughout these remedies; one senses a logic of sympathetic connection between the red wine flowing freely and the flow within the woman ceasing. When the barrels are opened, declare that they are being opened for this woman.

### Remedy 11

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לִנְקִיט שְׂעָרְתָּא דְּמִשְׁתַּכְחָא בִּכְפוּתָא דְּכוּדַנְיָא חִיוָּרְתָּא, אִי נָקְטָה חַד יוֹמָא — פָּסְקָה תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי, וְאִי נָקְטָה תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי — פָּסְקָה תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי, וְאִי נָקְטָה תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי — פָּסְקָה לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let** one **hold a barley** grain **that is found in the dung of a white donkey. If she holds it for one day** her discharge **will cease for two days; and if she holds it for two days it will cease for three days, and if she holds it for three days it will cease forever.**

If that was not effective, take a barley bean found in the excrement of a white donkey. If she holds it for one day, the flow stops for one day; for two days, two days; if she holds it for three days, the flow stops permanently.

## Jaundice Treatments

### Jaundice Two Ingredients

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְיַרְקוֹנָא תְּרֵין בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וּמִיעֲקַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **For jaundice,** one should drink **two** of the three ingredients mentioned together **with beer, and one becomes sterile** from it.

The *gemara* now returns to the two-ingredient *cos ikrin* for jaundice, underscoring that while it is effective, it does carry the side effect of sterilization. This raises an immediate *halachic* question, addressed in the following section, about whether such a remedy may be used at all.

### Remedy 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי רֵישָׁא דְשִׁיבּוּטָא דְּמִילְחָא וְלִישְׁלוֹק בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וְלִישְׁתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And if** one does **not** have these ingredients, i.e., Alexandrian gum, saffron, and alum, or if the remedy did not work, **let** one **bring the head of a salted *shibuta*** fish **and boil it in beer and drink** it.

If the *cos ikrin* was not effective for jaundice, take the head of a *shibuta* fish in salt, boil it in beer, and drink it.

### Remedy 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי מוֹנִינֵי דְקַמְצֵי. וְאִי לֵיכָּא מוֹנִינֵי דְקַמְצֵי — לַיְיתֵי מוֹנִינֵי דִּנְקִירֵי, וְלִיעַיְילֵיהּ לְבֵי בָנֵי וְלִישְׁפְּיֵיהּ. וְאִי לֵיכָּא בֵּי בָנֵי — לוֹקְמֵיהּ בֵּין תַּנּוּרָא לְגוּדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let** one **bring grasshopper brine** and drink it. **And if one does not have grasshopper brine, let** one **bring brine** of **small birds, and enter the bathhouse, and smear himself** with it. **And if there is no bathhouse, have him stand between the oven and the wall** and sweat out his disease.

If that was not effective, take the brine or juice of grasshoppers — or, if that is unavailable, the juice of small birds — and bring it into a bathhouse to use with the steam. If there is no bathhouse available, stand the patient between an oven and a wall, using the retained heat in the same way. The theme of heat and sweating recurs across many of these jaundice remedies.

### Remedy 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיְּחַמְּמֶנּוּ — יְקַנְּחֶנּוּ בִּסְדִינוֹ. רַב אַחָה בַּר יוֹסֵף חַשׁ בֵּיהּ, עֲבַד לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא, וְאִיתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who seeks to warm** this patient up **should wrap him** tightly **in his sheet. Rav Aḥa bar Yosef was sick with** this disease, **and Rav Kahana performed this** remedy **for him and he was healed.**

Rabbi Yochanan says: if you want to warm the patient, wrap him well in a cloak. Rashi offers the variant reading that one might use the garment of someone who had the same illness. The *gemara* relates that Rav Acha bar Yosef suffered from jaundice, and Rav Kahana performed this wrapping treatment for him — with the result that he recovered.

### Remedy 4

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי תְּלָתָא קְפִיזִי תַּמְרֵי פָּרְסְיָיתָא, וּתְלָתָא קְפִיזֵי דְקִירָא דְּנִישְׁתְּרוּפֵי, וּתְלָתָא קְפִיזֵי אַהֲלָא תּוֹלְעָנָא, וְלִישְׁלוֹקִינְהוּ בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וְלִישְׁתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And if** this remedy is **not** effective, **let** one **bring three vessels of Persian dates, and three vessels of dripping wax [*kira*], and three vessels of red aloe, and boil them in beer and drink** it.

If that was not effective, take three *kafizei* of Persian dates, three *kafizei* of wax that floats on the surface of a beehive, and three *kafizei* of red aloe (*ahala*). Boil them together in beer and drink.

### Remedy 5

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי עִילָא בַּר חֲמָרָא, וְלִיגַלַּח מְצִיעֲתָא דְרֵישָׁא, וְלִישְׁבּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּמָא מֵאַפּוּתֵיהּ, וְלוֹתְבֵיהּ אַרֵישֵׁיהּ, וְלִיזְדְּהַר מֵעֵינֵיהּ דְּלָא לִיסַּמֵּי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this remedy is **not** effective, **let** one **bring a donkey foal, and shave the middle of its head, and let blood from the** donkey's **forehead, and place it on his** own **head. And he should take care** not to let the blood enter **his eyes lest it blind him.**

If that was not effective, obtain a donkey foal, shave the middle of its head, and take the blood that flows from its forehead. Apply this blood to the head of the jaundice patient. One must be careful that it does not get into the patient's eyes, as it can cause blindness.

### Remedy 6

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי רֵישָׁא דְּבַרְחָא דְּמַנַּח בְּכִיבְשָׁא, וְלִישְׁלוֹק בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וְלִישְׁתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let** one **bring a pickled ram's head and boil it in beer and drink** it.

If that was not effective, take a ram's head that has been preserved in vinegar, boil it in beer, and drink it.

### Remedy 7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר חוּטְרָנָא, וְלִיקְרְעֵיהּ, וְלוֹתְבֵיהּ אַלִּיבֵּיהּ. וְאִי לָא — לַיְיתֵי כַּרָּתֵי מִכַּבְתְּוָתָא דְּמֵישָׁרֵי. הָהוּא טַיָּיעָא דְּחַשׁ בֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְגִינַּאי: שְׁקוֹל גְּלִימַאי וְהַב לִי מֵישָׁרָא דְכַרָּתֵי. יְהַב לֵיהּ וְאַכְלַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹשְׁלַן גְּלִימָיךְ וְאֶיגְנֵי בֵּיהּ קַלִּי. אִיכְּרַךְ גְּנָא בֵּיהּ. כַּד אִיחַמַּם וְקָם, נְפַל פּוּרְתָּא פּוּרְתָּא מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** this is **not** effective, **let** him **bring a striped something else,** i.e., a pig, **and tear it open and place it on his heart. And if** this is **not** effective, **let** him **bring leeks from the middle of the row,** which are very sharp. It is reported that **a certain Arab was sick with** jaundice. **He said to the gardener: Take my cloak and give me a row of leeks** in exchange. The gardener **gave him** the row **and he ate it.** The Arab **said to** the gardener: **Lend me your cloak and I will sleep in it for a short while.** The gardener gave it to him and **he wrapped himself in it and slept. When he got hot and stood up** pieces **fell from** the cloak **bit by bit** because the cloak had become very hot from the Arab's sweat. The Arab tricked the gardener, who ultimately received nothing.

If that was not effective, take what the *gemara* calls *davar acher* — the circumlocution for pig, since there is a taboo around naming it directly — tear it open and place it on the patient's heart.

If that was not effective, take cress (*karti*) from the middle row of a garden. The *gemara* then tells the story of a nomad who was suffering from jaundice. He told the gardeners: take my cloak and give me that row of cress. They agreed. After eating the cress and beginning to recover — the remedy, like the others, seemed to work through heat and sweating — he said: let me borrow back the cloak so I can sleep in it. He wrapped himself up and slept. When the fever broke and he heated up, the cloak was destroyed in the process. He had thus obtained the remedy and the use of the cloak for free. One could argue the gardeners had recourse under the principle of *hashoel chayav b'onsin* — that a borrower is liable for accidents — but the *gemara* leaves that aside.

## Sterilization Halakha

### Sterilization Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְיַרְקוֹנָא תְּרֵין בְּשִׁיכְרָא, וּמִיעֲקַר. וּמִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא, מִנַּיִין לַסֵּירוּס בָּאָדָם שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבְאַרְצְכֶם לֹא תַעֲשׂוּ״ — בָּכֶם לֹא תַעֲשׂוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discussed the remedy **for jaundice,** saying that one should drink **two** of the ingredients mentioned together **with beer, and one becomes sterile** from it. The Gemara asks: **And is it permitted** to cause sterility? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that castration of a man is prohibited? The verse states:** "Those whose testicles are bruised, or crushed, or torn, or cut, shall not be offered to the Lord, **and you shall not do this in your land"** (Leviticus 22:24), meaning that **you shall not do it to yourselves;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina.** Apparently, it is prohibited to castrate a man.

The *gemara* now turns to a direct *halachic* question raised by the two-ingredient *cos ikrin* for jaundice: since it causes sterilization, how can it be permitted at all? The *baraita* is cited: from where do we know that sterilizing a human being is prohibited? From the verse that prohibits bringing castrated animals as offerings and states *uv'artzchem lo ta'asu* — you shall not do this in your land — from which the *gemara* derives *uvachem lo ta'asu* — and you shall not do it among yourselves. This is the position of Rabbi Chanina.

### Intent & Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי — הֵיכָא דְּקָא מִיכַּוֵּין, הָכָא — מֵעַצְמוֹ הוּא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיְּסָרֵס תַּרְנְגוֹל — יִטּוֹל כַּרְבׇּלְתּוֹ, וּמִסְתָּרֵס מֵאֵלָיו. וְהָאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: רָמוּת רוּחָא הוּא דִּנְקִיטָא לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא בְּסָרִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** prohibition **applies** only in a case **where one intends** to castrate. **Here,** in the cure for jaundice, the sterility **happens on its own,** incidental to the treatment. Proof is cited from that which **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who seeks to castrate a rooster should remove its comb and it will become castrated on its own.** Incidental castration is permitted. The Gemara rejects the proof. **Didn't Rav Ashi say: It is arrogance that it assumes** when it has its comb, and when the comb is removed it becomes depressed and no longer procreates. However, it is not actually castrated. **Rather,** apparently this remedy for jaundice is permitted only for **one who is castrated** and for whom causing sterility is not a concern.

Rabbi Chanina himself, however, draws a distinction. The prohibition applies only where one acts with the *specific intention* of sterilizing. Here, the sterilization is a side effect — *me'elav*, it happens on its own — while the patient's goal is to heal from jaundice. As support, Rabbi Yochanan's statement is cited: if one wishes to sterilize a rooster, one removes its comb, and the rooster becomes sterile on its own. The implication drawn is that this constitutes an indirect act: one is not acting on the reproductive organs directly, but on something else, and the sterilization follows as a natural consequence. This raises a difficult conceptual question: does this mean that intentionally drinking something known to cause sterility is permitted as long as one is not physically acting on the body in the relevant area? The argument seems to permit the remedy precisely because the sterilization is indirect and incidental — yet the person who drinks the *cos ikrin* for jaundice knows full well the side effect. The *gemara* treats this as a meaningful distinction without fully resolving the tension.

### Rav Assi

Rav Assi challenges this reasoning from a different direction. He argues that removing a rooster's comb does not actually cause biological sterilization at all. Rather, the rooster is so proud of its comb that when the comb is removed, its spirit is broken — *ramus rucha hu* — and for psychological rather than physiological reasons, it ceases reproductive activity. If so, this example cannot serve as a proof that indirect sterilization is permitted. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *bisari* — the remedy is only permitted for someone who is already sterile. The implication is that the *halachic* permission is tightly circumscribed: knowing that a treatment will sterilize a fertile person is not something one may treat as merely incidental. This question about the parameters of sterilization — its biblical source, the role of intent, and the limits of indirectness — will continue to be examined.
