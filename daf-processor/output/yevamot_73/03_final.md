# Yevamot 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arel & Maaser Intro

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: עָרֵל מַהוּ בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר? כִּי הֵיכִי דְּיָלֵיף פֶּסַח מִמַּעֲשֵׂר לְעִנְיַן אֲנִינוּת, יָלֵיף נָמֵי מַעֲשֵׂר מִפֶּסַח לְעִנְיַן עֲרֵלוּת, אוֹ דִלְמָא: חָמוּר מִקַּל יָלֵיף, קַל מֵחָמוּר לָא יָלֵיף.
>
> **Translation:** **They raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet:** In the case of **an uncircumcised** man, **what is** the *halakha* **with regard to** his eating second **tithe?** Can one claim that **just as** the *halakha* governing the **Paschal lamb is derived from** the *halakha* governing second **tithe with regard to acute mourning,** in that an acute mourner, who may not eat second tithe, is likewise prohibited from partaking of the Paschal lamb, so **too,** the *halakha* concerning second **tithe is derived from** the *halakha* concerning the **Paschal lamb with regard to lack of circumcision,** in that an uncircumcised man, who may not partake of the Paschal lamb, is likewise prohibited from eating second tithe? **Or perhaps** the *halakha* governing the **stringent** case of the Paschal lamb **is derived from** the *halakha* governing the **lenient** case of second tithe, but the *halakha* concerning the **lenient** case of second tithe **is not derived from** the *halakha* concerning the **stringent** case of the Paschal lamb, and therefore it is prohibited for an uncircumcised man to partake of the Paschal lamb, but he may eat second tithe.

The sugya opens with a new question posed to Rav Sheshet: *arel mahu bema'aser* — can an *arel*, an uncircumcised person, eat *ma'aser*? This question arises in the midst of the chapter of *Ha'Arel*, which deals broadly with the legal disabilities of an uncircumcised man. Just before this, the *gemara* had been discussing whether an *arel* can perform the sprinkling of the ashes of the *parah adumah*, a question in which two opinions were recorded. One view, attributed to Rabbi Akiva, held that wherever a *tamei* person is disqualified on account of personal status rather than the transmission of *tuma*, an *arel* is likewise disqualified. Rava disagreed, but the position remained significant. Now the discussion shifts to a new and somewhat more obscure question: can an *arel* eat *ma'aser*?

### Maaser Sheni Status

The *ma'aser* at issue is specifically *ma'aser sheni*, the second tithe, for a straightforward reason. *Ma'aser rishon* goes to the *Levi*, and *ma'aser ani* is a gift distributed to the poor — neither of these carries any inherent *kedusha*. They are simply agricultural gifts, and the produce itself has no sanctified status.

### Types of Maaser

*Ma'aser rishon* is given to the *Levi* and *ma'aser ani* to the *ani*; neither imposes restrictions on the produce itself. The only *ma'aser* that possesses a quality of *kedusha* is *ma'aser sheni*, and it is therefore the only one that could plausibly be subject to the same restrictions as *terumah*. We will also encounter, later in the discussion, the anomalous position of Rabbi Meir regarding *ma'aser rishon*, but the default assumption is that *ma'aser sheni* alone carries sanctity.

### Maaser Sheni Laws

What is the nature of *ma'aser sheni*'s *kedusha*? It must be eaten in Yerushalayim. If one does not wish to bring the produce itself to Yerushalayim, it may be redeemed — its sanctity is transferred onto coins, which are then brought to Yerushalayim and spent on food, drink, or anointing, that is, on things that are directly consumed or used by the body. According to Rashi, *ma'aser sheni* may only be used for these direct bodily benefits. It cannot be used in commercial transactions; it is not at the owner's disposal in the way ordinary property is.

### Kal Vachomer Analysis

Given this *kedusha*, the question takes shape. We already know that an *arel* cannot eat *terumah* — that is the very subject of this chapter, derived from a *pasuk*. The questioners now ask: do we extend this to *ma'aser sheni*? The *gemara* frames the question around a potential inference. We know that an *onen* — a mourner in the immediate period following a relative's death — cannot eat *ma'aser*, derived from the phrase *lo achalti be'oni mimenu* in the *viduy ma'aser*. From that, the *gemara* elsewhere learned that an *onen* cannot eat the *korban Pesach*. The logic moves from the lighter (*ma'aser*) to the weightier (*Pesach*): if even *ma'aser* bars an *onen*, certainly the *korban Pesach* does. Can we run this logic in reverse and say: since an *arel* cannot eat *Pesach*, therefore an *arel* cannot eat *ma'aser*?

The *gemara* raises the objection: *chamir mikal yalif, kal michamir lo yalif* — an inference from the lighter to the weightier works, but you cannot necessarily reverse it and infer from the weightier to the lighter. The *korban Pesach* is *kodshim*; it is far more weighty than *ma'aser sheni*. The fact that an *arel* is forbidden from *Pesach* does not by itself establish that he is forbidden from the lighter *ma'aser sheni*. Tosafot notes that the question may turn on whether the link between *Pesach* and *ma'aser* is a *kal vachomer* or a *hekesh* — a formal textual equation — since a *hekesh* is not directional in the same way.

### Rav Sheshet Answers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: הַתְּרוּמָה וְהַבִּכּוּרִים חַיָּיבִים עֲלֵיהֶן מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וַאֲסוּרִין לְזָרִים, וְהֵן נִכְסֵי כֹהֵן, וְעוֹלִין בְּאֶחָד וּמֵאָה, וּטְעוּנִין רְחִיצַת יָדַיִם וְהֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ. הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בַּתְּרוּמָה וּבַבִּכּוּרִים, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to them: You** already **learned** the answer to this question in a mishna (*Ḥalla* 1:9): The following *halakhot* apply to both ***teruma* and** the **first fruits** of the new harvest, which must be given to the priests: If a priest ate of them while in a state of ritual impurity, **he is liable** to the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven; **and** if a non-priest ate of them unintentionally, he must restore the value of the produce he ate, adding **one-fifth** of its value as a fine; **and they are** both **forbidden to non-priests. And they are** considered in all regards **the** private **property of the priest** to whom they were given; **and they are nullified,** i.e., rendered permitted for consumption by non-priests, **in** a mixture of **one hundred and one,** when there are at least one hundred parts of permitted food for each part of *teruma* or first fruits; **and they** both **require washing of the hands** before they may be eaten; **and** if a priest is ritually impure, he may not eat of *teruma* or first fruits, even after immersion, until after **sunset** on the day of his immersion. All of **these** *halakhot* apply **to *teruma* and first fruits, which is not** the case **with** regard to second **tithe.**

Rav Sheshet responds to those who asked him: *tenituha* — we have already learned the answer. He directs them to a *mishna* in *Bikkurim*.

## Mishna Distinctions

### Mishna Introduction

The *mishna* in *Bikkurim* lists the ways in which *terumah* and *bikkurim* share laws that do not apply to *ma'aser sheni*. Everything enumerated in this first list is a stringency or a property that *terumah* and *bikkurim* share and that *ma'aser sheni* does not.

### Bikkurim Hybridity

Before proceeding, it is worth noting the somewhat surprising structure of this *mishna*. Intuitively, one might have expected *bikkurim* to be grouped with *ma'aser sheni* rather than with *terumah*. Both *bikkurim* and *ma'aser sheni* involve bringing produce to a central location — *ma'aser sheni* to Yerushalayim, *bikkurim* to the *Beit HaMikdash* — and both involve a formal declaration. Yet the first list groups *bikkurim* with *terumah*. The reason is that *bikkurim*, like *terumah*, is given to the *kohen* and may only be eaten by *kohanim*; a non-*kohen* who eats it is liable. So *bikkurim* is genuinely a hybrid: it shares properties with *terumah* by virtue of its priestly ownership and restriction, and it shares properties with *ma'aser sheni* by virtue of being brought to Yerushalayim and requiring a declaration. This duality explains why both lists exist.

### Mita & Chomesh

*Haterumah vehabikkurim chayavim aleihem mita vechomesh.* Both *terumah* and *bikkurim* carry the penalty of *mita* — death at divine hands — for a non-*kohen* who eats them, as derived from *umetu vo ki yechalelehu*. *Bikkurim* derives this law from the linkage to *terumah* through the phrase *terumat yadecha*. Alongside *mita*, there is the *chomesh*: if a non-*kohen* eats *terumah* or *bikkurim* unintentionally, he must return the principal to the *kohen* plus an additional fifth. These penalties obviously have no application to *ma'aser sheni*, since non-*kohanim* eat *ma'aser sheni* routinely.

### Property Rights

The *mishna* continues: *shehen nichsei kohen* — they are the property of the *kohen*. This may seem self-evident given that one gives them to the *kohen*, but it carries a meaningful legal consequence that is actually a leniency of *terumah* and *bikkurim* relative to *ma'aser sheni*. Because *terumah* is the *kohen*'s property, he may dispose of it as he sees fit within the constraints of its sanctity. He can, for example, use it as a medium of exchange — bringing it to a shopkeeper and purchasing goods with it. The shopkeeper, a non-*kohen*, may not eat the *terumah*, but he can trade it onward to another *kohen* or otherwise realize its value. The *terumah* has a diminished market value because of its restrictions — it can only be consumed by *kohanim*, must be kept *tahor*, and so forth — but it is nonetheless treated as genuine transferable property. A *kohen* could even use *terumah* to effect *kiddushin* with a woman, telling her *harei at mekudeshet li b'terumah zo*, and once she is fully married and eats *terumah* as a *kohen*'s wife, that is valid.

### Maaser Restriction

*Ma'aser sheni* is fundamentally different. It cannot be used in *sechorah* — commercial transactions — at all. It may only be directly consumed: eaten, drunk, or used for anointing. The owner has no freedom to trade it or use it as a medium of exchange. There is a further, deeper question about *ma'aser sheni*'s legal status: Rabbi Meir holds that *ma'aser sheni* is *mammon gavoha* — it belongs to the divine realm — while the *Chachamim* hold it is *mammon hedyot* — the owner's property, albeit with *kedusha* and attendant restrictions. This debate has practical consequences for questions of ownership such as liability when an animal purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money causes damage. The point for our purposes is that whatever one holds about ultimate ownership, the prohibition against using *ma'aser sheni* in transactions is clear.

### Bittul Ratios

A further distinction: *v'erev echad bemeah* — *terumah* and *bikkurim* are only *batel*, nullified, when mixed with other produce if the other produce constitutes at least one hundred times their volume. This is a special stringency the Rabbis applied to *terumah* — and extended to *bikkurim* — as well as to *orlah* and *kilayim*. The rationale that Rashi implies is that *terumah* is so potent in concept, since even a tiny amount of *terumah* can exempt an enormous pile of grain from the obligation, that it does not become *batel* at normal proportions. One needs a hundred-to-one ratio. *Ma'aser sheni*, by contrast, follows the ordinary rule, which according to Rashi is *rov* — a simple majority — though perhaps *shishim* might apply as well.

### Hand Washing

*Teunim rechitzat yadayim* — eating *terumah* and *bikkurim* requires prior washing of hands. This is because the Rabbis declared hands to be at the level of *sheni letumah* — a secondary source of impurity — and since *terumah* can be made *tamei* by a *sheni*, one must wash before handling it. *Ma'aser sheni* does not carry this requirement, reflecting its lower level of *kedusha*.

### Tumah Levels

*Ve-ha'arev shemesh* — the requirement of waiting until nightfall after immersion. A person who was *tamei* and immersed in a *mikveh* is not fully *tahor* until sunset. In the interval — after immersion but before nightfall — he has the status of a *sheni letumah*. He may therefore not touch *terumah* or *bikkurim*, but he may touch *ma'aser sheni*.

The broader *halakhic* picture here is worth sketching. A primary source of *tuma* — an *av hatuma*, such as a dead animal — can make a person, food, or a vessel *tamei*. A person so made *tamei* becomes a *rishon letumah* and can in turn make food *tamei*. That food becomes a *sheni*, and a *sheni* can make *terumah tamei*, rendering it a *shlishi*. A *shlishi* in *terumah* can then make *kodshim tamei*. This chain of *tuma* transmission operates precisely because *terumah* has a higher level of *kedusha* — it is restricted to *kohanim*, and violating it carries *mita*. *Ma'aser sheni* does not have this elevated *kedusha*, and therefore a *sheni letumah* cannot make *ma'aser sheni tamei* in the same way. The hand-washing and *ha'arev shemesh* requirements follow from this: since one's hands have the status of *sheni*, and since a *sheni* can make *terumah* into a *shlishi*, one washes before touching *terumah*. This does not apply to *ma'aser sheni*.

### Burning When Tamei

The final distinction in this first list, and one that will receive extensive analysis later: when *terumah* becomes *tamei*, the correct procedure is to burn it and derive benefit from it as fuel — the classic case discussed in the *mishna* of *Bameh Madlikin* involving *shemen sereifa*. By contrast, when *ma'aser sheni* or *bikkurim* becomes *tamei*, one may not burn it for fuel and benefit from it. Similarly, eating *tamei* *ma'aser* incurs *malkot*, lashes, since it violates a *lo ta'aseh*, whereas eating *tamei* *terumah* does not incur lashes.

## Non-Exhaustive Lists

### Arel Absence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא — נִיתְנֵי: עָרֵל אָסוּר בָּהֶן, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר! תְּנָא וְשַׁיַּיר. וּמַאי שַׁיַּיר דְּהַאי שַׁיַּיר?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet resumes his argument: **And if it is so** that one who is uncircumcised may eat second tithe, **let** the *tanna* also **teach: It is prohibited for an uncircumcised man** to eat of *teruma* and first fruits, **which is not** the case **with** regard to second **tithe.** The Gemara rejects this argument: This is no proof, as the *tanna* of the mishna **taught** only some of the differences between *teruma* and first fruits on the one hand and second tithe on the other, **and he omitted** others. The Gemara asks: The mishna is sometimes not exhaustive, but it never omits only one case. **What** other difference **did he omit that** you say that **he** also **omitted this** difference?

The *gemara* now attempts to draw a proof from this *mishna*. If an *arel* is permitted to eat *ma'aser sheni*, then the prohibition against an *arel* eating *terumah* and *bikkurim* — which is the subject of this entire chapter — would constitute another point of distinction between *terumah*/*bikkurim* on the one hand and *ma'aser sheni* on the other. The *mishna* could have said: *arel assur bahem*, an *arel* is forbidden in *terumah* and *bikkurim*, *mah she'ein kein bema'aser*, but not in *ma'aser sheni*. Since the *mishna* did not include this as a point of difference, the implication seems to be that an *arel* cannot eat *ma'aser sheni* either — otherwise it would have been mentioned as a distinction. This appears to be a strong proof.

The *gemara*'s response: *tana veshayar* — the list is not exhaustive; things were left off. One cannot infer anything from an absence in an incomplete list. But this requires substantiation. *Uma'i shayar de-hai shayar* — what else was left off, such that we can say this too was left off? The *gemara* needs to identify at least two omissions to establish that the list is genuinely non-exhaustive, since if only one item were missing, one should simply have included it.

### Second List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁיַּיר דְּקָא תָנֵי סֵיפָא: יֵשׁ בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר וּבַבִּכּוּרִים מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּתְּרוּמָה, שֶׁהַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְהַבִּכּוּרִים טְעוּנִין הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם, וּטְעוּנִין וִידּוּי, וַאֲסוּרִין לְאוֹנֵן, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. וְחַיָּיבִין בְּבִיעוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He omitted** the following, **as he teaches** in **the latter clause** of the mishna: **There are** *halakhot* that apply to second **tithe and first fruits, which is not** the case **with** regard to ***teruma*. As** second **tithe and first fruits require** that they be **brought to** a particular **place,** Jerusalem, where they must be eaten, whereas *teruma* may be consumed in all places; **and they** both **require a declaration,** as a declaration must be made on the last day of Passover in the fourth and seventh years of the Sabbatical cycle, stating that one's agricultural obligations with regard to tithes have been fulfilled properly, and so too a declaration must be made when first fruits are brought to the Temple; **and they are forbidden to an acute mourner; and Rabbi Shimon permits** an acute mourner to partake of first fruits. **And they** both **require eradication** before Passover in the fourth and seventh years of the Sabbatical cycle if one failed to bring them beforehand; **and Rabbi Shimon exempts** first fruits from the obligation of eradication.

The *gemara* points to the *seifa* — the second half of the *mishna*, which lists the ways in which *ma'aser sheni* and *bikkurim* are similar to each other and different from *terumah*. This second list includes: the requirement to bring them to a specific place — *ma'aser sheni* to Yerushalayim, *bikkurim* to the *Beit HaMikdash*; the requirement of *viduy*, a formal declaration — the *bikkurim* declaration of *Arami oved avi*, and the *ma'aser sheni* declaration including *lo achalti be'oni mimenu*; the prohibition against an *onen* eating them, derived from *lo achalti be'oni mimenu*; and the requirement of *biur* — clearing them out at the end of the three-year cycle.

Regarding the last two items, the *mishna* records a dispute. The *tanna kamma* holds that the *onen* and *biur* restrictions, which clearly apply to *ma'aser sheni*, also apply to *bikkurim*, since the *pasuk lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* links *ma'aser sheni* and *bikkurim* together. Rabbi Shimon disagrees and exempts *bikkurim* from both, holding that *bikkurim*'s fundamental identity is that of *terumah*, not *ma'aser*.

### Omitted Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ אָסוּר לְבַעֵר מֵהֶן בְּטוּמְאָה, וְאוֹכְלָן בְּטוּמְאַת עַצְמָן לוֹקֶה, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּתְּרוּמָה — לָא קָתָנֵי. אַלְמָא תָּנֵי וְשַׁיַּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And yet** second tithe and first fruits differ from *teruma* in that **it is prohibited to burn** the former and benefit from the burning, even when the produce is **in** a state of **ritual impurity** and therefore unfit to be eaten, e.g., one may not burn impure oil for light; **and one who eats them when they themselves,** the second tithe and first fruits, **are ritually impure is flogged,** even though he himself is ritually pure, **which is not** the case **with** regard to ***teruma*;** and these differences the *tanna* **does not teach. Apparently,** the *tanna* of the mishna **taught** certain differences between the cases **and omitted** others. Therefore, the omission of the *halakha* governing an uncircumcised man does not prove that an *arel* may eat second tithe.

The *gemara* observes that this second list also left things off — and not just one thing. Two items that belong on this list, in that they apply to *ma'aser sheni* (and arguably to *bikkurim*) but not to *terumah*, are missing. First: *assur leva'er mehem betumah* — when *ma'aser sheni* becomes *tamei*, one may not burn it for fuel, whereas *terumah* that becomes *tamei* is burned. Second: *ve'ochlan betumat atzman lokeh* — if one eats *tamei* *ma'aser sheni*, one receives *malkot*, lashes, since the prohibition derives from *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha*, a *lo ta'aseh*, whereas the prohibition on eating *tamei* *terumah* is derived from a positive commandment and therefore does not carry lashes.

Both of these omissions are rooted in the same *pasuk* — *lo bi'arti mimennu betamei*, from the *viduy ma'aser*. That verse teaches: do not consume it in a state of *tuma*, whether you were *tamei* and the *ma'aser* was *tahor*, or you were *tahor* and the *ma'aser* was *tamei*. The word *bi'arti* encompasses both burning for fuel and eating. Neither applies to *tamei* *ma'aser sheni*, in stark contrast to *terumah*.

### Inference Prevention

The demonstration that the second list is also non-exhaustive establishes the general point: these are not complete enumerations. Both the first list (*terumah* and *bikkurim* against *ma'aser sheni*) and the second list (*ma'aser sheni* and *bikkurim* against *terumah*) leave things off. One may therefore not infer from the absence of a distinction that no such distinction exists. The failure to mention the *arel* as a point of difference between *terumah*/*bikkurim* and *ma'aser sheni* proves nothing. Perhaps an *arel* can eat *ma'aser sheni*, and the *mishna* simply left that distinction off an incomplete list.

To make this vivid: the entire thrust of the attempted proof was that since the *mishna* did not say "an *arel* cannot eat *terumah* and *bikkurim* but can eat *ma'aser sheni*," this must mean the *arel* is treated the same way by *ma'aser sheni*. The *gemara* dismantles this inference by showing that both lists are admittedly truncated, and therefore silences cannot bear the weight of legal conclusions.

## Onen & Biur

### Pasuk Linkage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲסוּרִין לְאוֹנֵן, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. מְנָא לְהוּ — דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְתִירֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וְגוֹ׳ וּתְרוּמַת יָדֶךָ״, וְאָמַר מָר: ״תְּרוּמַת יָדֶךָ״ — אֵלּוּ בִּכּוּרִים, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ בִּכּוּרִים לְמַעֲשֵׂר: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר אָסוּר לְאוֹנֵן — אַף בִּכּוּרִים אָסוּר לְאוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** § Having cited the mishna, the Gemara proceeds to discuss it. It was taught in the *baraita* that second tithe and first fruits **are forbidden to an acute mourner; and Rabbi Shimon permits** an acute mourner to partake of first fruits. The Gemara asks: **From where do** the Rabbis derive that first fruits are forbidden to one who is in acute mourning? **As it is written: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain, or of your wine, or of your oil…nor the offering of your hand"** (Deuteronomy 12:17), **and the Master said: "The offering [*teruma*] of your hand," these are** the **first fruits. And first fruits are juxtaposed** in this verse **to** second **tithe: Just as** the second **tithe is forbidden to an acute mourner, so too, first fruits** are **forbidden to an acute mourner.**

The *gemara* now returns to examine the debates embedded in the *mishna*'s second list. It asks: what is the source for the *tanna kamma*'s view that the *onen* and *biur* rules apply to *bikkurim* as well as to *ma'aser sheni*? The answer is the *pasuk*: *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha ma'aser deganecha tiroshcha veyitzharecha u'terumat yadecha*. This verse prohibits eating certain things "in your gates" — meaning outside Yerushalayim. Taken at face value the verse is puzzling, since *ma'aser rishon* is given to the *Levi* in one's own city and *terumah* is eaten by *kohanim* throughout the land. The *gemara* therefore clarifies: the *ma'aser* that cannot be eaten "in your gates" is *ma'aser sheni*, which must be eaten in Yerushalayim. And the *terumat yadecha* refers to *bikkurim*, as the tradition has it: *terumat yadecha elu bikkurim*. The verse thus links *ma'aser sheni* and *bikkurim* together.

### Tanna Kamma Onen

From this linkage, the *tanna kamma* derives: just as an *onen* cannot eat *ma'aser* — as stated explicitly in the *viduy* declaration *lo achalti be'oni mimennu* — so too an *onen* cannot eat *bikkurim*, since they are joined in the same *pasuk*.

### Rabbi Shimon Onen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: תְּרוּמָה קְרִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא, מָה תְּרוּמָה מוּתֶּרֶת לְאוֹנֵן — אַף בִּכּוּרִים מוּתָּרִים לְאוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon** counters that since **the Merciful One calls** first fruits **"*teruma*,"** the *halakha* governing them is similar to that which governs *teruma*: **Just as *teruma* is permitted to an acute mourner, so too, first fruits** are **permitted to an acute mourner.**

Rabbi Shimon rejects this extension. His argument is that *bikkurim*'s identity is fundamentally that of *terumah* — *terumah karinah Rachmana*. Although *bikkurim* appears in the same verse as *ma'aser sheni*, that linkage reflects the coincidence of both being brought to Yerushalayim, not a substantive equation of their *kedusha*. The basic character of *bikkurim* — given to the *kohen*, eaten only by *kohanim*, carrying *mita* and *chomesh* — aligns it with *terumah*. And *terumah* is permitted to an *onen*. Therefore *bikkurim* is also permitted to an *onen*. It is worth noting that Rabbi Shimon's position is the more intuitively compelling one: since every substantive legal property of *bikkurim* tracks *terumah*, it is difficult to uproot it from that identity on the basis of a single verse that places it in proximity to *ma'aser sheni*.

### Biur Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיבִין בְּבִיעוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר. מָר מַקֵּישׁ וּמָר לָא מַקֵּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** both second tithe and first fruits **require eradication** before Passover in the fourth and seventh years of the Sabbatical cycle; **and Rabbi Shimon exempts** first fruits from the obligation of eradication. **One Sage,** the Rabbis, **juxtaposes** first fruits to second tithe: Just as second tithe is subject to eradication, so too are first fruits. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **does not juxtapose** the two *halakhot* and derive one from the other.

The same logical structure governs the *biur* dispute. The *tanna kamma* holds that just as *ma'aser sheni* must be cleared from the house in the third year of the *shemita* cycle — derived from *bi'arti hakodesh min habayit* — so too must *bikkurim*, on the basis of the same *pasuk* linkage. Rabbi Shimon exempts *bikkurim* from *biur*, for the same reason: *bikkurim*'s identity is *terumah*, and *terumah* has no such *biur* requirement. Rabbi Shimon adopts a minimalist stance throughout — he will link *bikkurim* to *ma'aser sheni* only where the connection is entirely obvious, and in all other respects he insists on *bikkurim*'s identity as *terumah*.

## Lo Ba'er Baraita

### Lo Ba'arti Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָסוּר לְבַעֵר מֵהֶן בְּטוּמְאָה, וְאוֹכְלָן בְּטוּמְאַת עַצְמָן לוֹקֶה, מְנָלַן — דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא בִעַרְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ בְּטָמֵא״, בֵּין שֶׁאֲנִי טָמֵא וְהוּא טָהוֹר, בֵּין שֶׁאֲנִי טָהוֹר וְהוּא טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated above that **it is prohibited to burn** second tithe and first fruits even when they are **in** a state of **ritual impurity, and** that **one who eats them when they themselves,** the second tithe and first fruits, **are ritually impure is flogged,** even though he himself is ritually pure. **From where do we** derive these *halakhot*? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says:** The verse with regard to the confession of tithes states: **"I did not consume of it while impure"** (Deuteronomy 26:14), a general statement that means **whether I,** the one who ate it, was **ritually impure, and** the tithe **was ritually pure,** or **whether I was ritually pure and** the tithe **was ritually impure.** Regardless, the tithe may not be consumed through burning or eating in a state of impurity.

The *gemara* now examines the source for the two items omitted from the second list — the prohibition on burning *tamei* *ma'aser sheni* and the prohibition (with attendant *malkot*) on eating it when *tamei*. A *baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Shimon expounds: *lo bi'arti mimennu betamei* — I did not consume it in a state of *tuma*. This covers two scenarios: *bein she-ani tamei v'hu tahor*, whether I was *tamei* and the *ma'aser* was *tahor*, or *bein she-ani tahor v'hu tamei*, whether I was *tahor* and the *ma'aser* itself had become *tamei*. The word *bi'arti* is understood to include both burning for fuel and eating. Therefore: if the *ma'aser* became *tamei*, one may not burn it for fuel and may not eat it. One must simply set it aside and allow it to decompose.

### Tumah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא מוּזְהָר עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ. טוּמְאַת הַגּוּף בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״נֶפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תִּגַּע בּוֹ וְטָמְאָה עַד הָעָרֶב וְלֹא יֹאכַל מִן הַקֳּדָשִׁים כִּי אִם רָחַץ בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּמָּיִם״!
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* adds: **And where one is** actually **warned against eating** second tithe in a state of impurity, this **I do not know.** Although it is obvious from the verse that this is prohibited, the source for the prohibition is unclear. The Gemara is puzzled by this last statement: The prohibition against eating second tithe when one is in a state of **ritual impurity of the body** is **explicitly written,** as it is stated with regard to the ritual impurity imparted by a creeping animal: **"The soul that touches it shall be impure until the evening, and he shall not eat from the holy things unless he has washed his flesh in water"** (Leviticus 22:6). This is referring to second tithe, as will be explained later (74b).

The *baraita* then proceeds to address the prohibition on eating *ma'aser* when *tamei*, and at this point the *gemara* interrupts. The *baraita* poses the question *va'hiecha muzhar al achilato* — how do we know eating *tamei* *ma'aser* is prohibited? — and answers *eini yodea*, I do not know, before citing the *pasuk* as the source. The *gemara* interjects: what do you mean you don't know? The case where a person is *tamei* and the *ma'aser* is *tahor* is written explicitly in the Torah — *nefesh asher tiga bo vetame'ah ad ha'erev velo yokhal min hakodashim* — a *tamei* person may not eat *kodashim*, and *ma'aser sheni* is clearly included in *kodashim*.

### Eating Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: טוּמְאַת עַצְמוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ הַטָּמֵא וְהַטָּהוֹר יַחְדָּו כַּצְּבִי וְכָאַיָּל״, וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֲפִילּוּ טָמֵא וְטָהוֹר אוֹכְלִין עַל שׁוּלְחָן אֶחָד בִּקְעָרָה אַחַת וְאֵינָן חוֹשְׁשִׁין, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: הָהוּא דַּאֲמַרִי לָךְ הָתָם ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ״ — הָכָא לָא תֵּיכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rather, **this is** the **dilemma he is raising: From where** is it derived that one may not eat it when the tithe **itself** is in a state of **ritual impurity? The verse states: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain"** (Deuteronomy 12:17), **and later it states** with regard to offerings that have been disqualified: **"You shall eat it within your gates, the impure and the pure alike, as the gazelle and as the hart"** (Deuteronomy 15:22). **And** a Sage **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Even a ritually impure and a ritually pure** person **may eat** together **on one table** and **out of one bowl without concern. And the Merciful One states: That which I said to you there,** with regard to disqualified offerings: **"You shall eat it within your gates,"** means regardless of whether it is the individual or the meat that is impure; but **here,** with regard to second tithe, **you may not eat** it in that state. From here it is derived that one may not eat second tithe when the tithe itself is ritually impure.

The Gemara then clarifies the *baraita*'s actual question: *tumat atzmo minalan* — what the *baraita* was asking about is not the case where the person is *tamei* but the *ma'aser* is *tahor*; that is well established. The question is: how do we know that one may not eat *ma'aser sheni* that is itself *tamei*, when the person is *tahor*? This is a different and subtler question, since when an object becomes *tamei* it has lost its pristine state and one might have thought the prohibition is less severe.

The answer comes from the *pasuk* *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha ma'aser deganecha* — you may not eat your *ma'aser* in your gates. This is then read in light of another verse: *bish'arecha tochelenu* — you shall eat it in your gates — which appears in the context of *kodashim* that became *pasul* and were redeemed. The Torah says that once redeemed, such *kodashim* may be eaten in your gates, *hatamei vehatahur yachdav*, by the *tamei* person and the *tahor* person together, just as one eats a deer or an ibex — animals that can never be *korbanot* and hence carry none of the restrictions of *kodashim*. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught this explicitly: even a *tamei* and *tahor* person can eat this redeemed *kodshim* from the same plate without concern.

The logic of the derivation is elegant. Eating "in your gates" in the Torah represents a mode of unrestricted consumption: outside Yerushalayim, without the limitations of *kedusha*, including the ability to eat even in a state of *tuma*. When the Torah says about *ma'aser sheni* *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha*, it is not merely saying "don't eat it outside Yerushalayim." It is also saying: do not eat your *ma'aser sheni* in the manner of eating "in your gates" — do not eat it as you eat things that carry no *kedusha*, do not eat it *betumah*. Therefore *ma'aser sheni* that has itself become *tamei* may not be eaten by a *tahor* person.

### Terumah Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּתְּרוּמָה. מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא בִעַרְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ בְּטָמֵא״ — מִמֶּנּוּ אִי אַתָּה מַבְעִיר, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַבְעִיר שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה שֶׁנִּטְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated above that one is prohibited from burning second tithe and first fruits even when they are ritually impure, **which is not** the case **with** regard to ***teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that this *halakha* does not apply to *teruma*? **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: As the verse** with regard to the declaration of tithes **states: "I did not consume of it while impure"** (Deuteronomy 26:14), and the words "of it" teach that **of it,** i.e., tithe, **you may not burn** when it is impure, **but you may burn** and derive benefit from **the oil of *teruma* that has become ritually impure.**

This rule — do not burn *tamei* *ma'aser sheni* for fuel, do not eat it when it is *tamei* — stands in contrast to *terumah*. When *terumah* becomes *tamei*, the established practice is precisely to burn it and benefit from it as fuel. How do we know this? The *gemara* cites Rabbi Abahu citing Rabbi Yochanan, who derives it from *mimennu* in the verse *lo bi'arti mimennu betamei*: the word *mimennu* — "from it" — implies a limitation, indicating that while you cannot burn *ma'aser sheni* when it is *tamei*, you can burn oil of *terumah* that has become *tamei*.

## Kal Vachomer

### KV Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: מִמֶּנּוּ אִי אַתָּה מַבְעִיר, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַבְעִיר שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל קֹדֶשׁ שֶׁנִּטְמָא! לָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? וּמָה מַעֲשֵׂר הַקַּל אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״לֹא בִעַרְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ בְּטָמֵא״ — קֹדֶשׁ חָמוּר לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But say** perhaps as follows: **Of it you may not burn, but you may burn** and derive benefit from **consecrated oil that became ritually impure.** The Gemara refutes this suggestion: That possibility is unacceptable. Is it **not an *a fortiori*** inference? **If** with regard to **the tithe, which is lenient, the Torah said: "I did not burn of it while impure,"** then with regard to **consecrated** oil, **which is** more **stringent,** is it **not all the more so** prohibited to burn them while ritually impure?

This ruling raises an immediate difficulty. *Ma'aser sheni* is the lighter item, and yet one cannot burn it when it is *tamei*. *Terumah* is weightier — restricted to *kohanim*, carrying *mita* and *chomesh* — and yet one may burn it. Is this not backwards? Someone raises the *kal vachomer*: if *ma'aser*, which is the lighter item (*ma'aser hakal*), may not be burned when *tamei* because the Torah says *lo bi'arti mimennu betamei*, how much more so should *kodshim* — which is weightier — not be burnable when *tamei*?

### Counter KV

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא! הָא כְּתִיב ״מִמֶּנּוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara retorts: **If so,** then with regard to ***teruma* as well,** say that **it is an *a fortiori*** inference, as *teruma* is certainly more sacred than tithes. If it is prohibited to benefit from second tithe while it is burning, all the more so would it be prohibited to benefit from *teruma* while it is burning. The Gemara answers: **Isn't it written "of it"?** From there it is derived that there is an item excluded from the prohibition against burning in a state of ritual impurity.

But if *kodshim* cannot be burned, then *terumah* should also be caught in the same *kal vachomer*: if *ma'aser* cannot be burned, *terumah* cannot either, since *terumah* is more weighty than *ma'aser*.

### Mimenu Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ? מִסְתַּבְּרָא קֹדֶשׁ לָא מְמַעֵיטְנָא שֶׁכֵּן פנקעכ״ס: פִּגּוּל, נוֹתָר, קׇרְבָּן, מְעִילָה, כָּרֵת, וְאָסוּר לְאוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** that led you to conclude that "of it" comes to exclude *teruma*? Perhaps it comes to exclude consecrated items. The Gemara replies: **It is reasonable** that **I do not exclude consecrated items** from the prohibition against benefiting from their burning, as with regard to consecrated items there are many stringent elements. Their Hebrew acronym is ***peh*, *nun*, *kuf*, *ayin*, *kaf*, *samekh*,** which is a mnemonic for the following stringencies that apply to consecrated items and not to *teruma*: ***Piggul*:** If, during one of the rites involved in the sacrifice of an offering, i.e., slaughter, receiving the blood, bringing it to the altar, or sprinkling it on the altar, the priest or the one bringing the offering entertains the thought of eating the offering at a time that is unfit for eating, the offering is thereby invalidated. ***Notar*:** Meat of an offering that remained beyond its allotted time may not be eaten and must be burned. **Offering [*korban*]:** It is an offering to God. **Misuse** of consecrated objects **[*me'ila*]:** One who unwittingly derives benefit from consecrated items is required to bring a guilt-offering for misuse of consecrated objects. ***Karet*:** The punishment of one who eats consecrated items while ritually impure is *karet*. **Forbidden to an acute mourner [*asur le'onen*]:** An acute mourner is prohibited from eating consecrated items. None of these *halakhot* apply to *teruma*. Therefore, consecrated items are more stringent than *teruma*, and accordingly they are not excluded from the prohibition against deriving benefit while ritually impure.

The response: *haktiv mimennu* — the Torah writes *mimennu*, "from it," which must be limiting something. The word implies that while you cannot burn *ma'aser* when *tamei*, something else can be burned. The question is then *uma ra'ita* — given that the *kal vachomer* would extend to everything more weighty than *ma'aser*, and given that *mimennu* must exclude something, what justifies excluding *terumah* and permitting its burning while leaving *kodshim* subject to the prohibition?

### Weighting Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, תְּרוּמָה לָא מְמַעֵיטְנָא שֶׁכֵּן מחפ״ז: מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וְאֵין לָהּ פִּדְיוֹן, וַאֲסוּרָה לְזָרִים! הָנָךְ נְפִישָׁן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כָּרֵת עֲדִיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument. **On the contrary, it is *teruma* that I would not exclude** from the prohibition, **as** with regard to *teruma* there are many stringent elements, represented by the acronym ***mem*, *ḥet*, *peh*, *zayin*,** which is a mnemonic for the following: **Death [*mita*]:** One who is prohibited from eating *teruma* but ate it intentionally is liable to the punishment of death at the hand of Heaven. **One-fifth [*ḥomesh*]:** A non-priest, to whom *teruma* is forbidden, who unwittingly ate *teruma* is obligated to pay its value to the priest plus one-fifth of the sum. **And** *teruma* **does not have** the possibility of **redemption [*pidyon*]:** Once it is sanctified, *teruma* may not be redeemed and rendered non-sacred. **And it is forbidden to non-priests [*zarim*].** These stringencies do not apply to consecrated items. The Gemara answers: **Those** stringencies that apply to consecrated items **are** more **numerous** than those that apply to *teruma*. Therefore, it is appropriate to be more stringent with consecrated items and exclude impure *teruma* from the prohibition against deriving benefit from it while it is burned. **And if you wish, say** instead a different reason, without counting the number of stringencies: Consecrated items are more stringent because one who eats them while ritually impure is liable to receive ***karet*,** which **is more severe** than death at the hand of Heaven, the punishment in the case of *teruma*.

The *gemara* answers: it makes sense not to exclude *kodshim* from the prohibition because *kodshim* is the weightier item overall, and we do not extend leniencies to weightier items. *Terumah*, though weightier than *ma'aser sheni*, is lighter than *kodshim*, so it is *terumah* that receives the leniency of being burnable when *tamei*.

### Kodashim Weights

How do we know *kodshim* is weightier than *terumah*? The *gemara* uses the mnemonic *pinkhas* — spelling out the stringencies of *kodshim*: *pigul* (improper intent during the *avodah* renders it forbidden), *nosar* (leaving it past its time), the fact that it is brought within the *Beit HaMikdash* itself, *meila* (misuse of *kodshim* is a distinct prohibition), *kares* (eating *tamei* *kodshim* incurs *kares*), and it is *assur le'onein* — forbidden to an *onen*.

### Terumah Weights

The *gemara* then entertains the counterargument: *terumah* has its own stringencies. The mnemonic *machpaz*: *mita* — a non-*kohen* who eats *terumah* is liable to death at divine hands; *chomesh* — he must add a fifth if he ate it unintentionally; *ein lah pidyon* — *terumah* cannot be redeemed, whereas consecrated animals that acquire a *mum* can be redeemed; *asurah lezarim* — while some *korbanot* such as *shelamim* may be eaten by non-*kohanim*, *terumah* may never be eaten by a non-*kohen*. These are genuine stringencies of *terumah* that *kodshim* does not share.

### Kares Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹכְלָן בְּטוּמְאַת עַצְמָן לוֹקֶה, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּתְּרוּמָה. מִילְקָא הוּא דְּלָא לָקֵי, הָא אִיסּוּרָא אִיכָּא — מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ״ — לָזֶה וְלֹא לְאַחֵר. וְלָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה — עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** It was further stated above that **one who eats** second tithe and first fruits **when they themselves,** the second tithe and first fruits, **are ritually impure is flogged, which is not** the case **with** regard to ***teruma*.** The Gemara infers from this that **it is lashes that he does not receive** when he eats *teruma* that is ritually impure; **however,** the transgression of **a prohibition is** involved. **From where do we** derive that this is prohibited? **The verse** referring to offerings that have been disqualified **states: "You shall eat it within your gates"** (Deuteronomy 15:22). The word "it" indicates that it is only **this,** disqualified offerings, that may be eaten in a state of impurity, **but not another** type of consecrated food. This is a prohibition that is derived by inference from a positive mitzva, i.e., it is not stated in the Torah in the form of a prohibition. **And** there is a principle that **a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva** is classified as **a positive mitzva,** for which no lashes are administered.

The *gemara* resolves the tension in two ways. First, quantitatively: *hanech nefishan* — the stringencies of *kodshim* are more numerous, six to four. Second, and more incisively: *kares adifa* — *kares* is a more severe punishment than *mita biyedei shamayim*. The fact that eating *tamei* *kodshim* incurs *kares*, while eating *terumah* incurs only *mita biyedei shamayim*, decisively tips the scales in favor of *kodshim* being weightier overall. Therefore, the leniency to burn when *tamei* is given to *terumah* — which is the intermediate item, less weighty than *kodshim* but more weighty than *ma'aser sheni* — and not to *kodshim*.

## Non-Exhaustive Lists (II)

### Rav Ashi's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מֵרֵישָׁא נָמֵי שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ דִּתְנָא וְשַׁיַּיר — מִדְּלָא קָתָנֵי וְנוֹהֲגִין בִּשְׁאָר שְׁנֵי שָׁבוּעַ, וְאֵין לָהֶם פִּדְיוֹן. מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara demonstrated earlier that no proof can be adduced from the mishna cited by Rav Sheshet, as the *tanna* of that mishna taught only some of the differences between second tithe and *teruma*. **Rav Ashi said: From the first part** of the mishna **as well, you** can **conclude that he taught** certain differences **and omitted** others, **from** the fact that **he does not teach** the following additional difference: *Teruma* and first fruits **apply in all the years of the seven-year** Sabbatical cycle, including the third and the sixth years, **and they do not have** the possibility of **redemption,** as once they are sanctified they may not be redeemed and rendered non-sacred. **This is not** the case **with** regard to **second tithe,** which applies only in the first, second, fourth, and fifth years of the cycle and which can be redeemed. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from here** that the *tanna* did not list all of the differences between second tithe and *teruma*.

Rav Ashi now offers a more economical solution to the earlier proof attempt. Recall that the entire discussion of the second list and its omissions was mobilized to show that the first list is non-exhaustive — that the absence of *arel* from the list of distinctions between *terumah*/*bikkurim* and *ma'aser sheni* cannot be taken as proof that an *arel* may eat *ma'aser sheni*. Rav Ashi points out that one need not look to the second list at all. Even from the beginning of the first list — *mereisha* — one can see that things were left off. *Midelo katani venohagin bish'ar shenei shavua* — the *mishna* did not say that *terumah* and *bikkurim* apply in all years of the *shemita* cycle while *ma'aser sheni* applies only in the third and sixth years. That is a major point of difference and it was not listed. *Ve'ein lahem pidyon* — nor did the *mishna* say that *terumah* and *bikkurim* cannot be redeemed, while *ma'aser sheni* can be redeemed by transferring its *kedusha* to coins. Both of these are prominent distinctions between *terumah*/*bikkurim* and *ma'aser sheni*, and neither appears on the list. *Shema mina* — one learns from this, already from the beginning of the *mishna*, that the list is non-exhaustive. The entire detour through the second list, while illuminating, was unnecessary for this specific point.

## Baraita on Arel

### Baraita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִשְׁתַּיְּירוּ בּוֹ צִיצִין הַמְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַמִּילָה — הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל לֹא בַּתְּרוּמָה, וְלֹא בַּפֶּסַח, וְלֹא בַּקָּדָשִׁים, וְלֹא בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר. מַאי לָאו מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן, לָא — מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its discussion of whether or not an uncircumcised man is prohibited from eating second tithe. **Come** and **hear** a proof with regard to this question from the following *baraita*: If **shreds** of flesh **that invalidate the circumcision** remain after the foreskin was removed, **one may not eat *teruma*, nor the Paschal lamb, nor sacrificial food, nor tithe. What, is it not** referring to **tithe of produce,** and so the dilemma is resolved? The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **No,** the tithe mentioned in this *baraita* is **animal tithe.** The *baraita* teaches that the meat of the animal tithe was forbidden to one who is uncircumcised.

The *gemara* now brings a stronger challenge. *Ta shema* — come and hear. A *baraita* teaches that in the case of *nishtayeru tzitzin hame'akvin et hamilah* — pieces of flesh that should have been removed in the *brit mila* were left in place — the person is still considered an *arel*. And the *baraita* then enumerates what this person may not eat: *harei zeh eino ochel lo bitrumah velo bePesach velo bekodshim velo bema'aser*. He may not eat *terumah*, he may not eat *Pesach*, he may not eat *kodshim*, and he may not eat *ma'aser*. This is an apparently explicit source that an *arel* cannot eat *ma'aser*. The *gemara* asks: does this not mean *ma'aser* of grain, i.e., *ma'aser sheni*?

### Maaser Behema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה הַיְינוּ קָדָשִׁים? וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, מִי לָא תְּנַן פֶּסַח וְקָתָנֵי קָדָשִׁים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **Animal tithe is** the same as **sacrificial food,** as it too is an offering the meat of which is eaten by the animal's owner; why would the *tanna* single it out? The Gemara counters: **And according to your reasoning** that the *baraita* would not have singled out specific offerings, **didn't we learn** in the *baraita* that an uncircumcised man may not eat the **Paschal lamb, and** yet **it** also **teaches** that the same *halakha* applies to **sacrificial food?**

The *gemara* offers a first deflection: *ma'aser behemah* — the *ma'aser* referred to is the animal tithe, which is a *korban*. When an animal is designated as *ma'aser behemah*, it has the status of *kodshim*. So the *baraita* is not adding a new category at all; it is simply another way of saying the *arel* cannot eat *kodshim*. The *gemara* makes this explicit: *ma'aser behemah hainu kodshim* — the animal tithe is *kodshim* and was already covered.

But this explanation is strained. If *ma'aser behemah* is already subsumed under *kodshim*, why mention it separately? The *gemara* points out that this problem mirrors an analogous one in the *baraita* itself: *mi lo tenan Pesach vetani kodshim* — the *baraita* already mentions *Pesach* and also mentions *kodshim*. Isn't *Pesach* already subsumed under *kodshim*? The *gemara* answers that both are necessary: had the *baraita* mentioned only *Pesach*, one might have thought the prohibition on an *arel* applies only to *Pesach* specifically, where the *pasuk* states *kol arel lo yokhal bo*; had it mentioned only *kodshim*, one might have thought the word *kodshim* refers specifically to *Pesach*. Mentioning both clarifies that *kodshim* means all *korbanot* and that *Pesach* is merely one instance. But this analysis makes the redundancy of *ma'aser behemah* all the more glaring.

### Rabbi Meir View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא פֶּסַח וְקָדָשִׁים צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי תְּנָא פֶּסַח — מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲרֵלוּת בְּפֶסַח כְּתִיבָא, אֲבָל קָדָשִׁים — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי תְּנָא קָדָשִׁים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי קָדָשִׁים — פֶּסַח. אֶלָּא מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר לְזָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Granted,** it is **necessary** to mention both **the Paschal lamb and sacrificial food. As, if** the *baraita* had **taught** only the *halakha* in the case of **the Paschal lamb,** one might have said that an uncircumcised man may not eat the Paschal lamb **because** the disqualification resulting from **lack of circumcision** is **written** explicitly **with regard to the Paschal lamb, but** with regard to other **sacrificial food,** concerning which the Bible says nothing about circumcision, one might **say** that there is **no** such prohibition. **And** conversely, **if** the *baraita* had **taught** only the *halakha* with respect to **sacrificial food, I would say: What** is meant here by **sacrificial food?** This is referring specifically to **the Paschal lamb,** concerning which the prohibition with regard to an uncircumcised man is stated explicitly, but other sacrificial food is permitted to him. **But why do I** need **animal tithe** to be mentioned at all? It is no different than any other sacrificial food. In that case, the tithe mentioned in the *baraita* is referring to second tithe. The Gemara suggests a different refutation of this proof: **Rather,** the tithe mentioned in the *baraita* is referring to **first tithe,** the one-tenth of the produce that is given to the Levites, **and** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said** that **first tithe is forbidden to common** Israelites. Since first tithe is forbidden to an ordinary Israelite, it may similarly be forbidden to an uncircumcised man. However, there is no proof from here that second tithe is forbidden to one who is uncircumcised, as second tithe is permitted even to ordinary Israelites.

The *gemara* therefore offers a second explanation: the *ma'aser* in the *baraita* refers to *ma'aser rishon* according to the position of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir holds the anomalous view that just as *terumah* is forbidden to non-*kohanim*, *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to non-*Leviyim*. Under this view, *ma'aser rishon* carries a restriction analogous to that of *terumah*, confining its consumption to the *Levi* just as *terumah* is confined to the *kohen*. The *gemara* suggests that the *baraita* may be invoking this position: an *arel* cannot eat *ma'aser rishon* according to Rabbi Meir, for the same reason he cannot eat *terumah* — these are items restricted to a particular class of people and the *arel*'s status creates an additional disqualification.

### Inconclusive

The *gemara*'s conclusion is notably tentative. Even with the *baraita* in hand — which states explicitly that an *arel* may not eat *ma'aser* — it has not been definitively established that an *arel* cannot eat *ma'aser sheni*. The *baraita* can be explained as referring either to *ma'aser behemah* (a form of *kodshim*) or to *ma'aser rishon* according to Rabbi Meir's distinctive position that treats it similarly to *terumah*. Neither of these is *ma'aser sheni*. The original question — *arel mahu bema'aser* — thus remains unresolved, and the *gemara* leaves it so.
