# Shabbat 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shiurim: Wine & Blood

### Shiurim Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵינָא: הַמּוֹצִיא יַיִן — כְּדֵי מְזִיגַת כּוֹס, וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: כְּדֵי מְזִיגַת כּוֹס יָפֶה. וְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִין בִּרְבִיעִית״. וְרָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל חַמְרָא דְּלָא דָּרֵי עַל חַד תְּלָת מַיָּא לָאו חַמְרָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **learned** that in the mishna: **One who carries out** undiluted **wine** is only liable if he carries out a measure **equivalent to** the wine typically **diluted** for **a cup. And it was taught with regard to** the mishna: A measure **equivalent to** the wine **diluted** for **a significant cup** of wine. **And it was taught in the latter** clause of the mishna: **And** the measure that determines liability for carrying out **all other liquids** is **a quarter** of a *log*. Since a quarter of a *log* is the significant measure for liquids, that is also the significant measure for diluted wine. **And Rava** is consistent with **his** line of **reasoning,** as **Rava said: Any wine that does not hold one** part wine diluted by **three** parts **water is not wine.** Wine must be sufficiently potent to be capable of being diluted with water three times its measure.

The daf under discussion is 77, and we continue within the perek of *Hamotzi Yayin*, which addresses the various *shiurim* for carrying foods and liquids on Shabbat. By way of orientation: for human foods, the governing measure is always a *gerogeres* (a dried fig's bulk). For liquids, different *shiurim* apply. Wine, when diluted, requires enough to constitute a full *revi'is* — specifically, according to Rav, a quarter of a *revi'is* of undiluted wine, given a three-to-one dilution ratio. This was somewhat contested by Abaye, who appeared to favor a two-to-one ratio, which would have implied a different minimum. With this background in place, we pick up in the middle of 77a.

### Dried Wine: Kezayis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: יָבֵשׁ בִּכְזַיִת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי נָתָן. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי נָתָן וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד. רַבִּי נָתָן — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים מִקּוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּמֵחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. דַּם נְבֵלָה — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מְטַהֲרִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מְטַמְּאִין. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַף כְּשֶׁטִּמְּאוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל, לֹא טִמְּאוּ אֶלָּא בְּדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רְבִיעִית, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לִקְרוֹשׁ וְלַעֲמוֹד עַל כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** in a *Tosefta*: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **congealed** wine on Shabbat is **an olive-bulk;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Natan. Rav Yosef said: Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said the same thing. Rabbi Natan's** statement is **that which we** just **stated. And** the statement of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** is **as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Six matters are among the leniencies of Beit Shammai and among the stringencies of Beit Hillel.** They include **the blood of a carcass. Beit Shammai deem** it **ritually pure,** as, in their opinion, only the flesh of a carcass imparts impurity; **and Beit Hillel deem** it **ritually impure. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said: Even when Beit Hillel deemed** it **impure, they deemed impure only** a measure of **blood that contains a quarter** of a *log*, **since it can congeal and constitute an olive-bulk.** Rav Yosef draws a parallel between the two opinions. Both *tanna'im* agree that a quarter of a *log* of liquid congeals to the size of an olive-bulk.

The Gemara introduces a *tana*: *yavesh bikezayis divrei Rebbi Noson* — if wine has dried out completely, Rabbi Noson holds that the relevant measure is a *kezayis*, since the substance is now solid. This is immediately puzzling: solid foods are governed by the measure of a *gerogeres*, not a *kezayis*. Why then does dried wine go by a *kezayis*? Presumably, the answer lies in the fact that it can be re-diluted and restored to wine, so it retains something of its liquid character — yet Rabbi Noson still assigns it a *kezayis*.

Rav Yosef observes that this position of Rabbi Noson is identical to one articulated by Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda: *amru davar echad* — they said the same thing. To establish this identification, the Gemara cites a *braisa* from tractate *Ediyos*, where Rabbi Yehuda reports six matters in which Beit Shammai was lenient and Beit Hillel strict. (Rashi notes parenthetically that there are in fact more than six such matters, but Rabbi Yehuda was testifying to the specific tradition before him in Ediyos, not offering an exhaustive catalogue.) One of these six concerns *dam neveilah* — the blood of a ritually slaughtered animal that was improperly killed. The meat of a *neveilah* is *tamei*; Beit Shammai holds the blood is *tahor*, while Beit Hillel holds it is *tamei* as well — whether because blood is treated equivalently to meat, or on the basis of a particular *pasuk* (as Tosafot discusses).

On the question of quantity, Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda states: *af kishitmu Beit Hillel, lo tim'u ela b'dam sheyesh bo revi'is* — even when Beit Hillel declares it *tamei*, they only do so for blood amounting to a *revi'is*, because that is the quantity which can congeal into a *kezayis*. The reasoning is that blood is *tamei* as a species of meat, and meat of a *neveilah* is *tamei* in a *kezayis*. Therefore, the liquid form is measured by the amount that will yield a *kezayis* of solid — which is a *revi'is*.

The symmetry is striking: Rabbi Noson says that wine which has dried into a *kezayis* solid was originally a *revi'is* of liquid; Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda says that blood which amounts to a *revi'is* of liquid is *tamei* because it can congeal into a *kezayis* of solid. Both affirm a conversion relationship between one *revi'is* of liquid and one *kezayis* of solid. The Gemara therefore identifies them as advancing the same underlying position.

### Density & Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דִּילְמָא לָא הִיא. עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן הָכָא דִּכְזַיִת בָּעֵי רְבִיעִית — אֶלָּא בְּיַיִן דִּקְלִישׁ, אֲבָל בְּדָם דִּסְמִיךְ — כְּזַיִת לָא בָּעֵי רְבִיעִית. אִי נָמֵי, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָתָם דִּכְזַיִת סַגִּי לֵיהּ בִּרְבִיעִית — אֶלָּא בְּדָם דִּסְמִיךְ, אֲבָל יַיִן דִּקְלִישׁ — כְּזַיִת הָוֵי יוֹתֵר מֵרְבִיעִית, וְכִי מַפֵּיק פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת לִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Perhaps that is not** the case, as **Rabbi Natan said** that **a quarter** of a *log* **is required only here, with regard to wine,** which **is liquid; however, with regard to blood, which is viscous,** in order to produce an **olive-bulk** when congealed, **a quarter** of a *log* **is not required.** Less than a quarter of a *log* of blood congeals into an olive-bulk. **Alternatively, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said that** in order to create a congealed **olive-bulk, a quarter** of a *log* **is sufficient only there, with regard to blood,** which is **viscous; however, wine,** which **is liquid,** in order to create a congealed **olive-bulk, more** than a **quarter** of a *log* of wine is required. **And,** therefore, even **when he carries out less than an olive-bulk** on Shabbat, **let him be liable.** There is no proof that the two tannaitic opinions are parallel.

Abaye challenges whether these two authorities can truly be regarded as making the same claim. His objection is grounded in the physical properties of the substances involved.

*Dilma lo hi* — perhaps they cannot be equated. Abaye argues: Rabbi Noson's ruling applies to wine (*d'kalish* — thin, not very viscous). But blood (*d'samich* — thick, more viscous) is a far denser substance. If a *revi'is* of thin wine yields a *kezayis* of dried matter, it does not follow that a *revi'is* of thick blood would yield the same. Less than a *revi'is* of blood — perhaps half, perhaps two-thirds — might already constitute a *kezayis* of congealed solid. So the two rulings cannot simply be read as expressing the same physical conversion ratio.

Conversely, Abaye argues from the other direction: if Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda holds that a *revi'is* of blood is the threshold because it can congeal into a *kezayis*, then applying that logic to wine (which is thinner), a *kezayis* of dried wine would actually represent *more* than a *revi'is* of original liquid. This means one would be *chayav* for carrying out even less than a full *kezayis* of dried wine. In short: neither ruling straightforwardly implies the other, because the *metzi'us* — the physical reality — of different liquids is genuinely different.

This Talmudic exchange points toward a broader conceptual tension. One might ask: at what point does the measure cease to track practical usability and become a formal legal category that operates by its own internal rules? This question surfaced earlier in the tractate as well — by the case of mixed grain types, where the governing question was whether the minimum *shiur* was met, not whether any animal would actually eat the mixture. The same dynamic appears here: once the measure has been assigned, the system operates with its own logic, and the question of whether the substance is genuinely useful in this form becomes secondary.

A related difficulty: if we are speaking of undiluted wine that has dried out, the starting point should have been a *quarter* of a *revi'is*, not a full *revi'is*, since undiluted wine requires only that smaller measure. Why does Rabbi Noson cite a full *revi'is*?

### Rashi: Category Shift

Rashi addresses this directly. He explains: *de-me-ikara havei beih revi'is u-ve-karush revi'is sheleima be'inan de-lav bar meziga* — although the wine may originally have been the denser, undiluted variety requiring only a quarter *revi'is*, once it dries out it is no longer eligible for *mezigah* (the act of diluting wine for drinking). It has departed from the category of concentrated wine-to-be-diluted, and therefore can no longer be governed by the *shiur* of that category. It must now be measured by the standard applicable to ordinary, fully potable wine — and that *shiur* is a full *revi'is*.

This is a notable conceptual move: the drying of the wine does not merely change its physical state; it shifts its legal category. It leaves category A (*yayin chamur*, undiluted wine ready to be mixed) and enters category B (standard wine ready for drinking). Rashi is explicit that the criterion for this shift is the loss of *mezigah* eligibility. What is equally notable is what Rashi does *not* say: he does not ask whether this *kezayis* of dried wine is actually useful or functional. The question is not one of usability but of categorical placement. The substance has been removed from one legal box and placed in another — and within that new box, the *shiur* of a *revi'is* applies. The formalization of the *shiurim* has, in this instance, taken over from the functional reasoning that originally generated them.

## Aleph-Ayin & Aramaic

### G'miya Spelling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָלָב כְּדֵי גְמִיעָה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״כְּדֵי גְמִיאָה״, אוֹ ״כְּדֵי גְמִיעָה״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: ״הַגְמִיאִינִי נָא מְעַט מַיִם מִכַּדֵּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **milk** is **equivalent to** that which is **swallowed [*kedei gemia*]** in one gulp. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is the correct version of the mishna: ***Kedei gemia*** with an *alef* **or *kedei gemia*** with an *ayin*? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It should be written with an *alef* as we find in the Torah: "And he said: **Give me to swallow [*hagmi'ini*** with an *alef* ] **please, a little water from your jug"** (Genesis 24:17).

The Gemara opens the next topic — the *shiur* for milk — with the phrase *chalav kedei g'miya* (milk in the amount of a swallow), and immediately raises a textual question: is the word *g'miya* spelled with an *alef* or an *ayin*? This may seem an unusual digression, but it reflects genuine linguistic uncertainty. For Ashkenazim, for whom *ayin* has lost its guttural quality and sounds identical to *alef*, this question might appear trivial — but in a world where *ayin* was pronounced gutturally, the two letters were meaningfully distinct, and the distinction mattered for scribal and oral traditions alike.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the question by citing the episode in Bereishit where Eliezer asks Rivkah for water: *hagmi'ini na me'at mayim mikadech* — the word for "let me swallow" appears there spelled with an *alef*. This supports the *alef* spelling. (It is worth noting that the word as it appears in the Mishnah may have been spelled with an *ayin*, yet the Gemara uses the biblical spelling to determine the correct form — an interesting deployment of the written Torah to arbitrate oral-tradition orthography.)

### Spelling Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״גַּרְאִינִין״, אוֹ ״גַרְעִינִין״? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא: ״וְנִגְרַע מֵעֶרְכֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** In the mishna at the end of the previous chapter we learned that the measure that determines liability for carrying out foods is calculated without their seeds [*garinin*]. Is the correct version ***garinin*** with an *alef* **or *garinin*** with an *ayin*? **Rava bar Ulla said:** It is with an *ayin*, since it stems from the same root as: **"And it shall be subtracted [*venigra*** with an *ayin*] **from your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:18).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״אוֹמְמוֹת״, אוֹ ״עוֹמְמוֹת״? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: ״אֲרָזִים לֹא עֲמָמֻהוּ בְּגַן אֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Do we describe dimming coals as ***omemot*** with an *alef* **or *omemot*** with an *ayin*? **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said:** It is written with an *ayin*, as it is stated: **"The cedars in the garden of God could not hide it [*amamuhu*** with an *ayin*]" (Ezekiel 31:8).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״מְאַמְּצִין״ תְּנַן, אוֹ ״מְעַמְּצִין״. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״וְעֹצֵם עֵינָיו מֵרְאוֹת בְּרָע״.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to closing the eyes of the dead, **did we learn** in the mishna ***me'amtzin*** with an *alef*, **or *me'amtzin*** with an *ayin*? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is written with an *ayin* as it is stated: **"And closes [*ve'otzem*** with an *ayin*] **his eyes from seeing evil"** (Isaiah 33:15).

The Gemara, now launched on this topic, raises several further spelling disputes. Are the seeds excluded from the *shiur* for carrying food spelled *garinim* with an *alef* or an *ayin*? Rabbah bar Ulla derives the correct spelling from the verse *venigra me'erkecha* — "and it shall be deducted from your valuation" — reasoning that seeds are things that are discarded or deducted, and the root there contains an *ayin*. The derivation is admittedly creative: the word in its biblical context has nothing to do with seeds, but the Gemara is searching for etymological grounding.

Then: is the word *omomos* — describing charcoal that is smoldering or sputtering, neither fully alight nor extinguished — spelled with an *alef* or an *ayin*? Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi cites the verse *arazim lo amamuhu began Elohim* — "the cedars did not overshadow it in the garden of God" — where Rashi explains *amamuhu* means "darkened." Since smoldering charcoal is blackened, this etymological connection supports the *ayin* spelling.

Finally, the word for closing one's eyes — as in the ruling about whether one may close the eyes of a dying person on Shabbat (a question touching on *muktzah* and the prohibition against hastening death) — is it spelled with an *alef* or an *ayin*? Rav Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan cites *ve-otzem einav me-r'ot bera* — "and one who closes his eyes from seeing evil" — where the word clearly appears with an *ayin*.

### Scribal Tradition

The Gemara's method here is revealing. In each case, the orthographic question is resolved by citing a biblical verse — from both Torah and *Nach* — on the assumption that the scribal tradition of the written text is reliable enough to arbitrate spelling disputes in the oral tradition. This reflects a broader confidence in the integrity of scribal transmission: not only the Torah scroll, which was subject to the most rigorous preservation, but even the books of *Nevi'im* and *Ketuvim* were preserved in scrolls that the sages consulted and trusted.

Scholars have noted that the community of scribes in antiquity was in certain respects distinct from the community of sages, with its own traditions, skills, and custodianship of the text — not only the craft of writing but the textual tradition itself. The Gemara's assumption that a biblical verse can settle an orthographic dispute in a Mishnaic term presupposes that both traditions, scribal and rabbinic, were understood to preserve authentic and reliable transmission.

The very fact that these *alef-ayin* questions arise at all is itself significant. One would never confuse an *alef* with a *chet*, because those are quite dissimilar. But *ayin* and *alef* are readily confused when the *ayin* has lost its guttural pronunciation — and the Gemara elsewhere mentions that in Beit She'an, a cosmopolitan city in Eretz Yisrael with significant Greek cultural influence, people were known to pronounce their *ayins* as *alefs*. It seems plausible that in Bavel as well, the guttural *ayin* was beginning to erode — and these textual disputes may be a linguistic symptom of precisely that process.

## Shiurim: Liquids

### Milk, Egg, Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא חָלָב שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה כְּדֵי גְמִיאָה. חָלָב שֶׁל אִשָּׁה וְלוֹבֶן שֶׁל בֵּיצָה — כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן בִּמְשִׁיפָא שֶׁל קִילוֹר. [קִילוֹר] כְּדֵי לָשׁוּף בְּמַיִם. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּדֵי שִׁיפָה, אוֹ כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה וְשִׁיפָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to carrying out milk, **the Sages taught:** One who carries out **the milk of an animal** on Shabbat is liable only for a measure **equivalent to** that which is **swallowed** in one gulp. The measure that determines liability for carrying out **a woman's milk and the white of an egg** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to place on** the part of **an eye bandage** upon which the ointment is placed directly **on the wound.** And the aforementioned **eye bandage** must be **sufficiently** large to use it **to rub** both his eyes **with water. Rav Ashi raised a dilemma:** Is the measure that determines liability for the bandage **equivalent to** that with which water can be **rubbed** on both eyes, **or** is it **equivalent to** that which can be **held and rubbed?** Is the part of the bandage held in his hand when applying the bandage to his eyes also taken into account? No resolution was found for this dilemma. Therefore, **let it stand** unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבַשׁ כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן עַל הַכָּתִית. תָּנָא: כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן עַל פִּי כָתִית. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: ״עַל כָּתִית״ — אַפּוּמָּא דְּכוּלַּהּ כָּתִית, אוֹ דִילְמָא אַמּוּרְשָׁא קַמָּא דְּכָתִית, לְאַפּוֹקֵי הוּדְרָנָא דְּלָא — תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **honey** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to place on a sore** caused by chafing. A *tanna* **taught** in a *Tosefta*: The precise measure is **equivalent to** that which is **placed on the opening of a sore,** i.e., on the wound itself. **Rav Ashi raised a dilemma:** Does the term **on a sore** mean the measure of honey spread **on the opening of the entire sore; or, perhaps** it means the measure spread **on the primary protuberance** of the **sore, to the exclusion of the surrounding** area upon which he does **not** spread honey? No resolution was found for this dilemma either. Therefore, **let it stand** unresolved.

The Gemara now presents a *baraisa*: one who carries out (*hamotzi*) milk of an animal — the measure is *kedei g'miya*, the amount of a swallow. The measure for a woman's milk (*chalav shel isha*) and for the white of an egg (*loven shel beitza*) is *kedei liten b'mashifa shel kilor* — enough to apply to the smear on an eye patch (*kilor*). The distinction is instructive: animal's milk was commonly drunk, and so it receives the drinking measure. A woman's milk, by contrast, was not typically expressed and stored — it went directly from mother to infant — and so the only context in which it would be carried externally was medicinal, specifically for use on this type of eye patch. The same applies to egg white, which was similarly applied to these patches for therapeutic purposes. Hence both are measured by the therapeutic use rather than a consumption measure.

### Eye Patch (Kilor)

The Gemara then addresses the measure for the *kilor* patch itself: *kedei lishof b'mayim* — enough to apply water to it. This formulation is somewhat puzzling. Rashi explains the measure as the amount used for two eyes — but why not simply describe it as the size required to function as an eye patch? Perhaps, as one might suggest, the patch was used not merely as a covering but as an applicator — held in the fingers and used to apply the liquid directly to the eye. In that reading, the relevant dimension is the patch's capacity to serve as an applicator: large enough to hold water on one portion while leaving another portion free to grip.

Rav Ashi raises a further question: is the minimum size *kedei shipa* — enough to apply the liquid — or *kedei achiza v'shipa* — enough both to grip and to apply, meaning the patch must be large enough to accommodate fingers holding it in addition to the surface area used for application? The Gemara concludes *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

### Honey for Wounds

For honey, the *tana* teaches: *kedei liten al pi katit* — enough to place on the opening of a wound. Rashi understands *katit* as a type of scraping or abrasion. The measure is not the entire surface area of the surrounding irritation but the wound opening itself. Rav Ashi again poses a question: does *al pi katit* refer to the mouth of the entire wound — the full area of injury, excluding only the surrounding inflamed tissue — or does it refer to the first raised prominence of the wound, excluding even the broader injured area? Once again the Gemara concludes *teiku*.

These unresolved questions are not without practical significance — at least theoretically, the *nafkah mina* concerns the obligation to bring a *korban chatat* for inadvertent violation. One might also observe the broader point being illustrated: these *shiurim*, at least in the present context, do not appear to be received as rigid *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. Rather, the Gemara is actively working them out through reasoning about typical use — which raises in turn the question, already flagged in the earlier discussion of mixed grains, of the relationship between the conceptual basis for a *shiur* and its subsequent formalization as an autonomous legal category.

## Creation & Purpose

### Nothing in Vain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבָּרָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּעוֹלָמוֹ לֹא בָּרָא דָּבָר אֶחָד לְבַטָּלָה. בָּרָא שַׁבְּלוּל — לְכָתִית. בָּרָא זְבוּב — לְצִירְעָה. יַתּוּשׁ — לְנָחָשׁ. וְנָחָשׁ — לַחֲפָפִית. וּסְמָמִית — לְעַקְרָב. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ — מַיְיתֵי חֲדָא אוּכָּמָא וַחֲדָא חִיוָּרָא, וְשָׁלְקִי לְהוּ וְשָׁיְיפִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** On the subject of sores, the Gemara cites a statement referring to one of the methods for healing a sore. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Everything that the Holy One, Blessed be He, created in His world, He did not create anything for naught. He created a snail** as a remedy **for a sore; He created a fly** to be crushed and spread as a remedy **on a wasp** sting; He created a **mosquito** as a remedy **for a snake** bite; **and** He created the **snake** itself as a remedy **for a** skin **rash; and** He created **a gecko** as a remedy **for a scorpion** bite. The Gemara explains: **How does** one **implement** the remedy? **He brings** two geckos, **one black and one white, cooks them, and spreads** the resulting ointment on the affected area.

The Gemara now turns in a markedly different direction — one that may seem surprising given the halachic context, but whose connection will become clear. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *kol ma shebara ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu b'olamo, lo bara davar echad l'vatala* — everything that the Holy One Blessed Be He created in His world, He created nothing in vain. Each creature, each natural phenomenon, serves a purpose. The connection to the immediately preceding material is subtle: we have just been discussing honey applied to wounds, and the Gemara is about to explain which creatures were created to provide cures for various injuries.

### Creatures & Cures

The Gemara proceeds: *bara shavlul l'katit* — He created the slug for an abrasion. One applies the slug (or its secretion) to a scraped or abraded wound. *Bara zvuv l'tzira* — He created the fly for a wasp sting; crushed flies were applied to the site of a wasp bite. *Yatush l'nachash* — the mosquito for a snake bite; crushed mosquito was applied to snakebite wounds. *Nachash l'chafafit* — the snake for *chafafit*, which Rashi explains as a type of boil or *shechin*; some preparation from the snake — whether venom or otherwise — served as treatment. *V'samamit l'akrav* — and the *samamit* (understood by Rashi and the primary tradition as a spider, though Steinsaltz identifies it as a gecko) for a scorpion sting.

For the last case, the Gemara elaborates on the preparation: *maiti chada uchma v'chada chivra* — one takes one black specimen and one white specimen of the *samamit*, boils them together, and applies the mixture to the scorpion sting.

The theological claim animating this passage is explicit: every creature serves a purpose. The Maharsha grapples with the obvious challenge: what of creatures that cause pain and harm — what is their purpose? His answer is that creatures capable of causing suffering serve a different but equally intentional divine purpose: they allow a person to atone through *yisurim* (suffering) in this world rather than in the next. The Gemara's focus here, however, is on the positive benefit these creatures provide — the specific medical functions that justify their existence from a human-centered perspective.

One might press the point further: is the argument that everything on earth must benefit human beings? The Gemara seems to be making precisely that claim, at least with respect to these creatures. But as one might note, this is a markedly anthropocentric framing. The psalmist's declaration that *hashamayim mesaprim kevod El* — the heavens declare God's glory — implies a different register of divine purpose, one that extends well beyond human utility. The Gemara here is answering a narrower question in a narrower way: these creatures, which might seem superfluous or merely irritating, actually serve concrete medical functions.

### Five Fears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, חֲמִשָּׁה אֵימוֹת הֵן אֵימַת חַלָּשׁ עַל גִּבּוֹר: אֵימַת מַפְגִּיעַ עַל הָאֲרִי, אֵימַת יַתּוּשׁ עַל הַפִּיל, אֵימַת סְמָמִית עַל הָעַקְרָב, אֵימַת סְנוּנִית עַל הַנֶּשֶׁר, אֵימַת כִּילְבִּית עַל לִוְיָתָן. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַאי קְרָא — ״הַמַּבְלִיג שֹׁד עַל עָז״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these creatures, the Gemara cites that which **our Sages taught: There are five dreads,** i.e., **dread** that **the weak** cast **over** the **mighty: The dread of the *mafgia*,** a small creature, **over the lion; the dread of the mosquito over the elephant; the dread of the gecko over the scorpion; the dread of the swallow over the eagle; the dread of the *kilbit*,** a small fish, **over a whale. Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: What** is **the verse** that alludes to these matters? As it is written: **"He that causes destruction [*shod*] to flash upon the mighty,** so that destruction comes upon the fortress" (Amos 5:9), which is interpreted as: He who lifts the downtrodden [*shadud*] over the mighty.

The Gemara continues with another teaching: *chamisha eimos hen* — there are five instances of fear, all falling under the heading of *eimat chalash al gibbor* — the fear of the weak upon the strong. This phrase functions as a general rubric for what follows, rather than as a sixth example in its own right: in each case, a smaller or apparently weaker creature causes fear in a larger, more powerful one.

The five are: *eimat mafgia al ari* — the fear of the *mafgia* upon the lion. Rashi describes this as a small creature that makes a loud noise and thereby frightens the lion; others identify it as a skunk-like animal whose spray drives off even packs of lions. *Eimat yatush al ha-pil* — the fear of the mosquito upon the elephant, which is greatly distressed by mosquito bites and will go to considerable lengths to avoid them. *Eimat samamit al ha-akrav* — the fear of the spider upon the scorpion; the same creature mentioned above in its curative role is, in its living form, a predator that the scorpion fears, despite being physically smaller. *Ve-eimat senunit al ha-nesher* — the fear of the *senunit* (identified by Steinsaltz as a swallow) upon the *nesher* (which, as Yehuda Feliks and others have established, refers to a vulture rather than an eagle — a point worth noting given the regal biblical imagery associated with the *nesher*). Flocking birds can interfere with birds of prey. *Ve-eimat kilvis al Leviathan* — the fear of the *kilvis*, a small fish, upon the Leviathan, the great sea creature. Rashi explains that this small fish would enter the ear of the great fish, causing it particular distress.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *mai kra* — what is the scriptural basis for this? The verse *hamavlig shod al oz* — "one who spreads destruction upon the strong" — is cited, with Rashi reading *shod* as *shadud*, the downtrodden or diminished, who can nonetheless project fear onto the powerful.

## Animal Observations

### Goats & Sheep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אַשְׁכַּח לְרַב יְהוּדָה דַּהֲוָה קָאֵי אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי חֲמוּהּ, וְחַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה בְּדִיחָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ, וְאִי בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ כׇּל חֲלָלֵי עָלְמָא הֲוָה אָמַר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא עִיזֵּי מְסַגָּן בְּרֵישָׁא וַהֲדַר אִימְּרֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ כִּבְרִיָּיתוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דִּבְרֵישָׁא חֲשׁוֹכָא וַהֲדַר נְהוֹרָא. מַאי טַעְמָא הָנֵי מְכַסְּיָין וְהָנֵי מְגַלְּיָין? הָנֵי דְּמִכַּסִּינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ — מְכַסְּיָין, וְהָנֵי דְּלָא מִכַּסִּינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ — מְגַלְּיָין. מַאי טַעְמָא גַּמְלָא זוּטַר גְּנוּבְתֵּיהּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּאָכֵל כִּיסֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא תּוֹרָא אֲרִיכָא גְּנוּבְתֵּיהּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּדָיֵיר בְּאַגְמֵי, וּבָעֵי לְכַרְכּוֹשֵׁי בָּקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the nature of the world, the Gemara relates that **Rabbi Zeira found Rav Yehuda, who was standing at the entrance of his father-in-law's house, and observed that he was in an** especially **cheerful mood. And** Rabbi Zeira understood that **were he to ask** Rabbi Yehuda **about anything in the** entire **world, he would tell him** the answer. He therefore posed questions to him on a variety of topics unrelated to *halakha*. He asked: **Why do goats walk in front** of the flock **and then ewes** follow? **He said to him:** It **is** just **as** it was **in the creation of the world, which at first was dark and then light** followed. Goats, which are typically black, precede the ewes, which are typically white. And he asked: **Why are** the genitals of **these** ewes **covered** with a tail, **whereas these** goats **are exposed?** He answered: **These** ewes, whose wool **we** use to **cover ourselves,** are rewarded in that they are **covered; and those** goats, whose hair **we do not** use to **cover ourselves,** are **exposed.** And he asked: **Why is the camel's tail short?** He answered: **Because** a camel **eats thorns,** and a long tail would become entangled in the thorns. And he asked: **Why is the ox's tail long?** He answered: **Because** the ox **lives in a swamp and must fend off mosquitoes.**

The Gemara transitions to a charming narrative interlude. Rabbi Zeira found Rav Yehuda standing at the entrance of his father-in-law's home in an exceptionally good mood — *d'hava bedicha da'tei* — so good, indeed, that Rabbi Zeira felt this was the moment to ask him about all the wonders of the world: *ei ba'i minei kol chalala d'alma hava amar lei* — had he wished, he could have asked him anything about the open workings of the world, and Rav Yehuda would have answered him.

There is a real question about how to read this passage. One reading is that *bedicha* carries a tone of lightness and even humor: this was not a formal *beit midrash* exchange but a relaxed outdoor conversation, and the questions and answers that follow are playful rather than strictly scientific. Another reading takes the passage more earnestly: the Gemara chose to record these questions and their answers because curiosity about the natural world was considered a legitimate and even valued form of inquiry. This is consistent with the principle that one who can calculate the cycles of the heavens (*tekufot u-mazalot*) and does not do so has failed to appreciate *pe'ulat Hashem* — the work of God — and stands in a kind of negligence. On this reading, a *talmid chacham* of Rav Yehuda's stature was expected to have engaged with these questions precisely because they are part of understanding the divine creation.

Rabbi Zeira's first question: *mai ta'ama izi masgan b'reisha* — why do goats walk in front of sheep? Rav Yehuda answers: *k'veri'ato shel olam* — like the creation of the world, where first there was darkness (*chashocha*) and then light (*nehora*). Since goats tend toward dark coloring and sheep toward white, the order of the animals in the flock mirrors the sequence of creation.

### Sheep vs. Goats

The next question: *mai ta'ama hanei mikhasyan va-hanei migalyan* — why are sheep covered (i.e., their hindquarters concealed by their broad, fatty tails) while goats are exposed? The answer: *hanei de-mikhasyan minaihu mikhasyan* — those from whom we take material to cover ourselves (sheep, whose wool we use for clothing) have their own parts covered; *va-hanei de-lo mikhasyan minaihu, migalyan* — those from whom we do not take material to cover ourselves (goats) remain exposed. This is, as the questioner notes, a notably human-centered explanation — the covering of the animal mirrors our own use of it.

### Tails & Horns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא קַרְנָא דְקַמְצָא רַכִּיכָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּדָיְירָא בְּחִילְפֵי, וְאִי קַשְׁיָא — נָדְיָא וּמִתְעַוְּורָא. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי דְּלִיסַמְּיֵהּ לְקַמְצָא — לִשְׁלְפִינְהוּ לְקַרְנֵיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא הַאי תִּימְרָא דְתַרְנְגוֹלְתָּא מִדְּלֵי לְעֵילָּא? דְּדָיְירִי אַדַּפֵּי, וְאִי עָיֵיל קֻטְרָא — מִתְעַוְּורָא.
>
> **Translation:** He continued to ask: **Why is the grasshopper's antenna soft?** He answered: **Because** it **lives in the plains** among the grass, **and if** the antenna **were hard it would break and** the **grasshopper would go blind.** When the grasshopper loses its antenna it is unable to move, **as Shmuel said: One who seeks to blind a grasshopper should remove its antenna.** And he asked: **Why does the chicken's** lower **eyelid cover the upper one?** He answered: **Because** a chicken **lives on rafters** in houses, **and if the smoke** from the houses **were to enter** its eyes without the protection of the eyelid, it **would go blind.**

The questions continue: *mai ta'ama gamla zutra kinvaseh* — why does the camel have a short tail? Because it grazes among thornbushes (*kisei*), and a long tail would become entangled in the thorns. *Mai ta'ama tora arich kinvaseh* — why does an ox have a long tail? Because it grazes in swampy areas (*agmi*) and requires a long tail to swat away flies. *Mai ta'ama karna de-kamtza rekika* — why is the antenna (translated variously as "horn") of the locust soft and pliable rather than rigid? Because it lives among blades of grass (*chilfei*), and if the antenna were stiff, it would catch on the grasses and be torn out — which, the Gemara explains, would blind the locust.

### Antennae & Eyes

This leads to Shmuel's statement: one who wishes to blind a locust removes its antennae (*lishlifinu le-karnei*). Whether this means there is some magical connection between antennae and sight, or — more plausibly — that locusts navigate and locate themselves through their antennae, functioning somewhat like a cat's whiskers, so that their removal renders the creature effectively blind by depriving it of its sensory apparatus, the practical observation is the same.

The final question in this series: *mai ta'ama hai timra de-tarnagolta midli le-eila* — why does the chicken's eyelid move upward rather than downward? (Unlike humans, whose upper eyelid descends, a chicken's lower membrane rises.) The answer: *de-dayri adafi* — because chickens roost on beams and ledges high above the ground, and smoke rises. Since smoke and irritants ascend from below, having the lower eyelid move upward provides better protection for the eye against rising smoke. The adaptation is designed, on this account, precisely for the environment the creature inhabits.

## Aleph-Ayin & Aramaic (II)

### Aramaic Etymologies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דַשָּׁא״ — דֶּרֶךְ שָׁם. ״דַּרְגָּא״ — דֶּרֶךְ גַּג. ״מַתְכּוּלִיתָא״ — מָתַי תִּכְלֶה דָּא. ״בֵּיתָא״ — בֹּא וְאִיתֵּיב (בַּהּ). ״בִּיקְתָּא״ — בֵּי עָקְתָא.
>
> **Translation:** He asked further: Why is a door called ***dasha*** in Aramaic? He said to him: It is an acronym for ***derekh sham*,** which means through there. Why is a stair called ***darga*?** He said to him: It is an acronym for ***derekh gag*,** which means the way to the roof. Why are spices called ***mitkolita*?** He said to him: It is an acronym for ***matai tikhleh da*,** which means when will this be finished. Since spices are used sparingly, they last a long time. Why is a house called ***beita*?** He said to him: It is an acronym for ***bo ve'eitiv ba*,** which means come and I will sit in it. Why is a small house called ***bikta*?** He said to him: It is an acronym for ***bei akta*,** which means a narrow house.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כּוּפְתָּא״ — כּוּף וְתִיב. ״לִבְנֵי״ — לִבְנֵי בְּנֵי. ״הוּצָא״ — חֲצִיצָה. ״חַצְבָּא״ — שֶׁחוֹצֵב מַיִם מִן הַנָּהָר. ״כּוּזָה״ — כָּזֶה. ״שׁוּטִיתָא״ — שְׁטוּתָא. ״מְשִׁיכְלָא״ — מָאשֵׁי כּוֹלָּא. ״מְשִׁיכְלְתָא״ — מָשְׁיָא כַּלְּתָא. ״אֲסִיתָא״ — חַסִּירְתָּא. ״בּוּכְנָה״ — בּוֹא וְאַכֶּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** A barrel is called ***kufta*,** which is an acronym for ***kuf vetiv*,** which means turn it over and sit. Bricks are called ***livnei*,** which is an allusion to ***livnei benei*,** which means for the children of children. A fence made of thorns is called ***hutza*** and Rav Yehuda said that it is merely a ***ḥatzitza*,** which means partition, as opposed to a true fence. A jug used for drawing water is called a ***ḥatzba* because it hews [*ḥotzev*] water from the river.** A small jug is called ***kuza*,** alluding to ***kazeh*,** which means: Give me one like that. Myrtle branches with which people dance at a wedding are called ***shutita*,** an allusion to ***shetuta*,** which means madness, as the frenzied dancing appears to be madness. The large vessel used for washing is called ***meshikhla*,** which is an acronym for ***mashei kula*,** which means: It washes all. A small vessel used for washing is called ***mashkhilta*,** and Rav Yehuda explained that it is an acronym for ***mashya kalta*,** which means: It washes the bride. A mortar is called ***asita*,** alluding to ***ḥasirta*,** which means lacking, since it has a large cavity. The pestle that is inserted into the mortar for pounding its contents is called ***bukhna*,** which is an acronym for ***bo ve'akkena*,** which means: Come and I will strike it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְבוּשָׁה״ — לֹא בּוּשָׁה. ״גְּלִימָא״ — שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה בּוֹ כְּגֹלֶם. ״גּוּלְּתָא״ — גַּלִּי וְאִיתֵּיב. ״פּוּרְיָא״ — שֶׁפָּרִין וְרָבִין עָלֶיהָ. ״בּוֹר זִינְקָא״ — בּוֹר זֶה נָקִי. ״סוּדָרָא״ — סוֹד ה׳ לִירֵאָיו. ״אַפַּדְנָא״ — אַפִּיתְחָא דֵּין. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּזְקִינִין מוֹסִיפִין גְּבוּרָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: דָּג, וְנָחָשׁ, וַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** An outer garment is called ***levusha*,** which is an acronym for ***lo busha*,** which means no shame, as one who is clothed is no longer ashamed. A broad outer garment is called ***gelima*,** an allusion to the fact that, by wearing it, one **is transformed into** an amorphous **golem-like** figure, as his limbs are indistinguishable. A beautiful coat is termed ***golta*,** which is an acronym for ***geli ve'eitiv*,** which means reveal, remove the coat, and only then I will sit, so that the coat will not get dirty. A bed is called ***purya*,** an allusion to the fact that people ***parin veravin*,** which means procreate, **upon it.** An empty pit is called ***bor zinka*,** which is an acronym for ***bor ze naki*,** which means that this pit is empty of water. The head covering of Torah scholars is called ***sudara*,** an acronym for *sod yareh*, which is a reference to the verse: **"The counsel of the Lord is with them who fear Him [*sod Hashem lire'av*]"** (Psalms 25:14). A palace is called ***apadna*,** which is an acronym for ***apitḥa dein*,** meaning: To this entrance all come. The Gemara cites an additional *baraita* related to the nature of the creatures. There are **three** creatures **that grow mightier with age, and they are** the **fish, and** the **snake, and the pig.**

The Gemara now presents an extended series of Aramaic etymologies, tracing common Aramaic words back to Hebrew roots or phrases. The exercise reflects a view that Aramaic, however widely spoken, contains hidden layers of meaning derivable from the holy tongue.

*Dasha* (a door) derives from *derech sham* — the way of the path, the way through which one passes. *Darga* (a step or stair) derives from *derech gag* — the way to the roof. *Matkulisa* (a type of dip or condiment) derives from *matai tichleh da* — when will this be finished? — because dip is used so slowly and sparingly that it seems inexhaustible, lasting far longer than solid foods. *Beita* (a house) derives from *bo ve-isiv* — come and sit — expressing the fundamental hospitality embodied in a home. *Biksa* (a small house) derives from *be-aksa* — a narrow, confined space. *Kufta* (a basket, or perhaps a measuring vessel) derives from *kuf ve-tiv* — turn it over and sit on it — because these baskets were sometimes inverted and used as seats. *Livni* (bricks) derives from *livnei banei* — for your children's children — because a house built of brick will endure for generations.

*Hutza* (a divider between properties, made of weak materials like thorns and thistles) derives from *chatzitza* — a mere partition rather than a proper fence. *Chatzva* (a pail or jug used for drawing water) derives from *shotzev mayim min ha-nahar* — one who hews or draws water from the river. *Kuza* (a small flask or jug) derives from *ke-zeh* — just like this, a little of that — suggesting the casual borrowing or sharing of a small amount. *Shutisa* (a myrtle branch) derives from *shtuta* — foolishness — because the Gemara elsewhere records that great sages would dance with myrtle branches before the bride and groom at weddings, acting in an uncharacteristically loose and joyous manner in the spirit of *mesameach choson ve-kallah*.

*Mashichla* (a large bucket used for bathing) derives from *mashi kuleh* — it washes the entire body. *Mashchalta* (a smaller, finer bucket) derives from *mashi kallah* — it is used by a *kallah*, a bride, or more broadly by people of distinction, since it is the more elegant vessel. *Asita* (the mortar — the vessel that holds spices to be ground) derives from *chasirta* — something lacking or hollow, a cavity. *Buchna* (the pestle — the implement used to strike and grind) derives from *bo ve-akeh-nah* — come and let me strike it.

*Levusha* (a garment) derives from *lo busha* — no shame, for clothing removes nakedness and with it the experience of shame. *Glima* (a cloak, something like a long coat or mantle) derives from *she-na'aseh bo ke-golem* — one becomes shapeless when wearing it, as a heavy outer garment obscures the body's form. *Gulsa* (a finer cape or outer garment) derives from *gali ve-isiv* — reveal and sit — meaning one takes it off before sitting, lest the garment be spoiled. *Puraiya* (a bed) derives from *she-parin ve-ravin aleha* — that one is fruitful and multiplies upon it.

*Bor zinka* (an empty well) derives from *bor ze naki* — this well is empty. *Sudra* (a handkerchief or head-covering, particularly associated with *talmidei chachamim* who wore it as a distinctive mark) derives from *sod Hashem lire'av* — the secret of God is for those who fear Him — suggesting that this garment identified those initiated into the deeper dimensions of Torah. *Apadna* (a large mansion or palace) derives from *apitcha dein* — this is the entrance, the doorway — evoking the place where important matters, business dealings, or charitable requests were transacted.

## Growth & Strength

### Three Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּזְקִינִין מוֹסִיפִין גְּבוּרָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: דָּג, וְנָחָשׁ, וַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional *baraita* related to the nature of the creatures. There are **three** creatures **that grow mightier with age, and they are** the **fish, and** the **snake, and the pig.**

The Gemara teaches: *shlosha kol zman she-mazkinin mosifin gevurah* — three creatures, the older they grow, the more powerful they become. These are: *dag* (fish), *nachash* (snake), and *chazir* (pig). Fish simply keep growing larger and more powerful without reaching a stage of decline associated with old age. Snakes likewise increase in size and strength — as evidenced by the massive specimens found in places like the Bronx Zoo. And wild boars continue to grow in bulk and ferocity. The underlying observation is that these creatures do not experience senescence in the way other animals do; they grow continuously rather than plateauing and declining.

## Shiurim: Liquids (II)

### Oil: Small Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמֶן כְּדֵי לָסוּךְ אֵבֶר קָטָן. אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי שֶׁמֶן כְּדֵי לָסוּךְ אֵבֶר קָטָן שֶׁל קָטָן בֶּן יוֹמוֹ. מֵיתִיבִי: שֶׁמֶן כְּדֵי לָסוּךְ אֵבֶר קָטָן, וְקָטָן בֶּן יוֹמוֹ. מַאי לָאו: אֵבֶר קָטָן דְּגָדוֹל, וְאֵבֶר גָּדוֹל שֶׁל קָטָן בֶּן יוֹמוֹ! אָמְרִי לָךְ דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי — לָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: שֶׁמֶן כְּדֵי לָסוּךְ אֵבֶר קָטָן, שֶׁל קָטָן בֶּן יוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **oil** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to spread on a small limb. The school of Rabbi Yannai said** it should be explained as follows: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **oil** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to spread on** a **small limb of a day-old child.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **oil** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to spread on a small limb and a day-old child. Does this not** mean: **A small limb** of **an adult and a large limb of a day-old child?** The Gemara rejects this: **The school of Rabbi Yannai** could have **said to you: No, this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **oil** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to spread on a small limb,** and the small limb in question is that of a **day-old child.**

Returning to the *shiurim*: *shemen kedei lasuach eiver katan* — oil sufficient to anoint a small limb. The school of Rabbi Yannai specifies: *shemen kedei lasuach eiver katan shel katan ben yomo* — oil sufficient to anoint the smallest limb of a day-old infant. That is: take the tiniest person possible (a newborn) and the tiniest limb (a pinky, or even a segment of a pinky), and that is the measure. The resulting quantity is extraordinarily small.

The Gemara raises a challenge (*meitivei*): a *braisa* attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states *shemen kedei lasuach eiver katan ve-katan ben yomo* — oil sufficient to anoint a small limb *and* a day-old infant. The conjunction *vav* seems to suggest two separate measures: either a small limb of a full-grown adult, or alternatively a large limb of a day-old infant. If so, the *braisa* does not require both conditions simultaneously, and the resulting measure would be larger than the school of Rabbi Yannai's ruling.

The school of Rabbi Yannai pushes back: *hachi ka'amar* — this is how the *braisa* should be read. The *vav* is not conjunctive but explanatory: *shemen kedei lasuach eiver katan shel katan ben yomo* — a small limb of a day-old infant, with *katan ben yomo* specifying which *katan* is meant. Both conditions are required simultaneously, and the measure is indeed the minute amount they originally proposed.

The Gemara then asks whether this is a debate among *tanna'im*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, it now appears, holds that the *vav* is genuinely conjunctive and either condition alone suffices — a small limb of an adult, or a large limb of a day-old infant, with these treated as equivalent measures. Rabbi Natan, by contrast, says only *kedei lasuach eiver katan* — a small limb, full stop — which Abaye understands as referring to a small limb of an adult, with no reference to an infant.

The Gemara initially considers whether this represents a true dispute: does Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar hold that both criteria combined (small limb of a day-old infant) is the measure, while Rabbi Natan holds that only one condition at a time need be met? The Gemara rejects this framing: *de-kulei alma eiver katan de-katan ben yomo lo* — everyone agrees that a small limb of a day-old infant is too small a measure and does not constitute the threshold for *chiyuv*. The school of Rabbi Yannai is thus rejected by consensus. The actual debate is between Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who holds that a small limb of an adult and a large limb of a day-old infant are equivalent and interchangeable measures, and Rabbi Natan, who accepts only the small limb of an adult and does not allow the infant's limb as an alternative.

The Gemara concludes with a *braisa* in which Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar himself explicitly states *shemen kedei lasuach eiver katan shel katan ben yomo* — establishing the combined measure of small limb plus day-old infant as his definitive position. The *nafkah mina* for all of this, as noted, is the liability to bring a *korban chatat* for carrying oil on Shabbat.

### Water & Sh'chicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַיִם כְּדֵי לָשׁוּף בָּהֶן אֶת הַקִּילוֹר. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִכְּדֵי כׇּל מִילְּתָא דִּשְׁכִיחָא וְלָא שְׁכִיחָא, אֲזוּל רַבָּנַן בָּתַר דִּשְׁכִיחָא לְקוּלָּא. שְׁכִיחָא וּשְׁכִיחָא אֲזוּל רַבָּנַן בָּתַר דִּשְׁכִיחָא לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the *mishna*: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **water** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to rub** and spread on **an eye bandage. Abaye said: Now, since,** with regard to **any substance that is** utilized for both **common and uncommon** uses, **the Sages,** in their ruling, **followed the common** usage even **as a leniency,** i.e., one is liable only for carrying out the larger measure. However, when a substance has different uses and one is **common and** the other is **common** as well, **the Sages,** in their ruling, **followed the common** use that leads **to a stringency,** i.e., one is liable for carrying out even the smaller amount.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַיִן, שְׁתִיָּיתוֹ שְׁכִיחָא רְפוּאָתוֹ לָא שְׁכִיחָא — אֲזוּל רַבָּנַן בָּתַר שְׁתִיָּיתוֹ דִּשְׁכִיחָא לְקוּלָּא. חָלָב, אֲכִילָתוֹ שְׁכִיחָא רְפוּאָתוֹ לָא שְׁכִיחָא — אֲזוּל רַבָּנַן בָּתַר אֲכִילָתוֹ לְקוּלָּא. דְּבַשׁ, אֲכִילָתוֹ שְׁכִיחָא רְפוּאָתוֹ שְׁכִיחָא — אֲזוּל רַבָּנַן בָּתַר רְפוּאָתוֹ לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Proof for this principle can be seen in the following examples. **Wine, its** use for **drinking is common** and **its** use for **healing is uncommon. The Sages,** in establishing the measure that determines liability for carrying out wine, **followed its** use for **drinking, which** is **common,** which led **to a leniency.** The amount of wine that one typically drinks is greater than the amount of wine used for healing. **Milk, its consumption is common** and **its** use for **healing is uncommon. The Sages,** in establishing the measure that determines liability for carrying out milk, **followed its consumption, which** is **common, as a leniency. Honey, its consumption is common** and **its** use for **healing is** also **common. The Sages,** in establishing the measure that determines liability for carrying out honey, **followed its** use for **healing,** the smaller amount, **as a stringency.**

The Gemara turns to water: *mayim kedei lishofet et ha-kilor* — water in the amount needed to apply to the eye patch. This is a strikingly small measure, far less than the amount one would use for drinking. The Gemara now pivots to the conceptual question underlying this and the preceding *shiurim*: how does one determine which use of a substance governs the minimum measure?

## Sh'chicha Principle

### Abaye's Rule

Abaye articulates a general principle: *kol milta di-shchicha ve-lo shchicha, azlu rabbanan batar di-shchicha le-kula* — whenever a substance has two possible uses, one common (*shchicha*) and one uncommon, the Rabbis follow the more common use, and they do so even when that results in a leniency (a larger minimum measure, making it harder to be liable). Conversely, *shchicha u-shchicha, azlu rabbanan batar di-shchicha le-chumra* — when both uses are equally common, the Rabbis follow the common use and do so in the direction of stringency, adopting the smaller measure. The underlying logic is that a common use defines what the substance "is" for purposes of the *shiur*, and when two equally common uses compete, the stricter measure governs.

### Wine & Milk

Wine: *shtiyato shchiha, refuato lo shchiha* — drinking wine is common; using it medicinally is not. The medicinal use would correspond to a smaller measure; the drinking measure is larger. The Rabbis follow the more common use — drinking — *le-kula*, as a leniency, resulting in a higher threshold for liability. Milk: *achilato shchiha, refuato lo shchiha* — consuming milk is common; using it medicinally is not. Again, the Rabbis follow the eating measure *le-kula*, treating the larger, more common use as the governing measure.

### Honey: Equal Uses

Honey presents a different situation: *achilato shchiha ve-refuato shchiha* — both eating honey and using it medicinally are common uses. The question of whether a use is "common" in this context should not be understood as a frequency count of individual occasions. Rather, the relevant inquiry is whether someone who possesses honey would be equally likely to be using it for one purpose versus another — whether both purposes are among the primary identified uses of the substance. By that standard, honey is genuinely *shchicha* for both eating and healing. Accordingly, *azlu rabbanan batar refuato le-chumra* — the Rabbis follow the medicinal use *le-chumra*, adopting the smaller measure and thus a lower threshold for liability.

### Water Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַיִם, מִכְּדֵי שְׁתִיָּיתוֹ שְׁכִיחָא רְפוּאָתוֹ לָא שְׁכִיחָא, מַאי טַעְמָא אֲזוּל רַבָּנַן בָּתַר רְפוּאָתוֹ לְחוּמְרָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּגָלִילָא שָׁנוּ. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בִּשְׁאָר מְקוֹמוֹת, כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל שַׁקְיָינֵי מַסּוּ וּמְטַלְּלִי לְבַר מִמַּיָּא דְּמַסּוּ וְלָא מְטַלְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **However, water, since its drinking is common** and **its** use for **healing is uncommon, what is the reason** that **the Sages followed its** use for **healing as a stringency?** Based on the above principle, the Sages should have determined the measure based on its use for drinking. **Abaye said: They taught** this *halakha* **in the Galilee** where they typically drink wine. There, water is used as commonly for healing as it is for drinking (*Tosafot*). **Rava said: Even** if you **say** that this *halakha* applies **in the rest of the places** as well as in the Galilee, the use of water in treating the eye is common, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, as Shmuel said: All liquids** placed on the eye effect a **cure and cloud** the vision, **except for water which cures and does not cloud** the vision.

Water seems to fit the first pattern clearly: *shtiyato shchiha, refuato lo shchiha* — drinking water is overwhelmingly common; using it for a wound patch is not. The Rabbis should therefore follow the drinking measure *le-kula*, which is a much larger amount. Yet the Gemara rules the opposite: the measure for water is the small amount used for the eye patch. Why?

Abaye explains: *be-Galila shanu* — this was taught in the Galilee. In the Galilee, where people were relatively poor, water was the standard liquid used for wound dressings and eye patches, since other liquids like wine, oil, or honey were less accessible. In that regional context, using water medicinally was sufficiently common to elevate it to a primary use. This does not mean that water was used for medicinal purposes more often than for drinking in absolute terms; it means that within the list of primary recognized uses for water in that community, medicinal application appears near the top.

This explanation, however, raises an unsettling implication: does the *shiur* vary by region? If the measure for water as derived in the Galilee reflects Galilean practice, would the measure be different in Judea?

### Rava & Shmuel

Rava offers an alternative that sidesteps the regional specificity: *afilu teima bi-sh'ar mekomot, k'Shmuel* — even in other places, the same result follows, on the basis of a ruling by Shmuel. Shmuel stated: *kol shakyani massu u-metall'lei* — all liquids will heal a wound but will also cause clouding of the vision or some deleterious side effect. *Le-var mimaya* — with the exception of water. *D'massu ve-lo metall'lei* — water heals and does not cause blurring. Because water is uniquely effective for this purpose without side effects, it is preferred for use with the *kilor* eye patch wherever one might be. This makes the medicinal use of water sufficiently prominent — even in places where other liquids are plentiful — to elevate it to a primary recognized use.

### Regional Variation

Yet the question of regional variation does not fully dissolve. The possibility that *shiurim* could differ from place to place sits uncomfortably with the general conception of *shiurim* as fixed and formal legal categories. As the earlier analysis noted, once a *shiur* has been formalized, it tends to operate by its own internal rules, no longer directly beholden to the functional reasoning that originally generated it. The notion that a *shiur* might shift depending on local custom or economic conditions points in precisely the opposite direction — toward flexibility and responsiveness to context. The tension between these two tendencies — the formal fixity of *shiurim* on one hand, and their grounding in lived practice on the other — remains unresolved at the close of this passage, and is to be continued.
