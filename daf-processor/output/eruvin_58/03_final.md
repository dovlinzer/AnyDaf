# Eruvin 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fifty-Amah Rope

### Mishkan Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״אֹרֶךְ הֶחָצֵר מֵאָה בָאַמָּה וְרוֹחַב חֲמִשִּׁים בַּחֲמִשִּׁים״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: בְּחֶבֶל שֶׁל חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה מְדוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the Shabbat limit must be measured with a rope fifty cubits long, derived? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** They are derived from **that** which **the verse states: "The length of the courtyard shall be one hundred cubits, and the breadth fifty by fifty"** (Exodus 27:18). **The Torah states: Measure with a rope of fifty cubits,** i.e., the length and breadth of the courtyard must be measured "by fifty," with a rope fifty cubits long.

The *gemara* opens with the question *mina hani mili* — from where do we derive that the measuring rope used for *techumim* must be fifty *amot* in length? *Rav Yehuda* cites *Shmuel*, who points to the *pasuk* describing the courtyard of the *mishkan*: *orech hachatzer meah ba'amah v'rochav chamishim b'chamishim* — the length of the *chatzer* was a hundred *amot* and its width was fifty by fifty. The phrase "fifty by fifty" is admittedly unusual. The *gemara* elsewhere explains the simple meaning: the first fifty *amot* of the courtyard did not contain the *ohel mo'ed*, which was situated only in the rear fifty. But the peculiar formulation *b'chamishim* — with the *bet* prefix — invites further attention.

### Two Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִיטּוֹל חֲמִשִּׁים וּלְסַבֵּב חֲמִשִּׁים. אִם כֵּן לֵימָא קְרָא: ״חֲמִשִּׁים חֲמִשִּׁים״, מַאי ״חֲמִשִּׁים בַּחֲמִשִּׁים״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** repetitive usage of the word fifty **is necessary** to teach us something else, namely, that the area of a courtyard is equivalent to a square the size of the Tabernacle's courtyard. To this end, the Torah states: **Take** a square of **fifty** cubits by fifty cubits, **and surround** it with the remaining **fifty** cubits in order to form a square, each side of which is just over seventy cubits long. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse state: Fifty, fifty,** which would have sufficed to teach us the size and shape of a courtyard. **What is** the significance of the phrase: **Fifty by fifty? Conclude from this** that the verse comes to teach **two** things, both the matter of the square courtyard and that the length of the rope used to measure the Shabbat limit should be fifty cubits long.

The *gemara* asks: surely that phrase *b'chamishim* is already needed to teach something else — *litol chamishim v'lisaveiv chamishim*, to take fifty and surround fifty. That is, if one has a city plot of a hundred by fifty *amot*, one takes the second fifty and distributes it equally around the first fifty, effectively squaring the rectangle. The result is a square with a side equal to the square root of five thousand, which equals seventy and two-thirds *amot* — a figure that recurs throughout this tractate as the critical measurement for determining whether structures outside the city boundary are still considered connected to it. If so, does this use of the *pasuk* leave nothing over for deriving the fifty-*amah* rope?

The *gemara* responds: had the *Torah* intended only the squaring principle, it would have written simply *chamishim chamishim*. What does the additional *bet* — *b'chamishim* — teach? *Shema mina tarti* — you learn both. This is characteristic of how the *gemara* throughout this tractate treats *eruv techumim*: it consistently grounds these laws in *psukim*, even though the dominant view is that *eruv techumim* is *d'rabanan*. The tension between *drashot* of this kind and the rabbinic character of the law is itself notable.

### Rope Length Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא פָּחוֹת וְלֹא יוֹתֵר. תָּנָא: לֹא פָּחוֹת — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּרְבֶּה, וְלֹא יוֹתֵר — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: One may measure a Shabbat limit only with a rope fifty cubits long, **not less and not more,** as will be explained in the Gemara. **It was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **No less, because** a shorter rope improperly **increases** the Shabbat limit, as the rope is likely to be stretched. **And no more, because** a longer rope **reduces** the limit, as the rope is likely to sag due to its weight.

A *baraita* teaches: *lo phachot v'lo yoter* — not less and not more than fifty *amot*. Not less, because a shorter rope can be held very taut, causing the measurement to stretch beyond its true length. Not more, because a longer rope will sag, causing it to fall short of measuring the full distance. The practical logic underlying these reasons is worth noting: if the rule were truly derived from a *pasuk*, one would not expect functional justifications to be offered. This *baraita* thus lends the rule a pragmatic character that sits somewhat in tension with the scriptural derivation above.

### Rope Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: אֵין מוֹדְדִין אֶלָּא בְּחֶבֶל שֶׁל אַפְסְקִימָא. מַאי אַפְסְקִימָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: נַרְגִּילָא. מַאי נַרְגִּילָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב: דִּיקְלָא דְּחַד נְבָרָא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מַאי אַפְסְקִימָא? רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר: נַרְגִּילָא, רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: דִּיקְלָא דְּחַד נְבָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said: One may measure only with a rope of *afsakima*.** The Gemara asks: **What is *afsakima*? Rabbi Abba said:** It is the ***nargila*** plant. This name was also not widely known, and therefore the Gemara asks: **What is *nargila*? Rabbi Ya'akov said: A palm tree** that has only **one fibrous vine** wrapped around it. **Some say** a different version of the previous discussion, according to which the Gemara asked: **What is *afsakima*? Rabbi Abba said:** It is the ***nargila*** plant. **Rabbi Ya'akov** disagreed and **said:** It is **a palm tree** with **one fibrous vine.**

*Rabbi Assi* states: *ein modedin ela b'chevel shel afaskima* — you may only measure with a rope made of *afaskima*. The *gemara* then proceeds through two stages of translation. *Rav Abba* says *afaskima* is *nargila*. *Rav Yaakov* says *nargila* is *dikla de-chad navra* — a palm tree with a single vine wrapped around it. According to Steinsaltz, this refers to the coconut palm, and the relevant material is the fiber of the coconut husk, which is prized for making ropes that do not absorb water and therefore do not expand or contract with moisture. This property would allow the rope to maintain a consistent length regardless of conditions — exactly what is required for accurate measurement.

An alternative version of the tradition holds that *Rav Abba* and *Rav Yaakov* were not offering a chain of translation but two competing opinions: *Rav Abba* said *afaskima* is *nargila*, while *Rav Yaakov* said it is *dikla de-chad navra*, each being a distinct material, with the meaning of *nargila* itself left unclear. This second reading leaves even more uncertainty about what, precisely, one was supposed to use.

### Iron vs. Rope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָא: אֵין לְךָ שֶׁיָּפֶה לִמְדִידָה יוֹתֵר מִשַּׁלְשְׁלָאוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל, אֲבָל מַה נַּעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״וּבְיָדוֹ חֶבֶל מִדָּה״. וְהָכְתִיב: ״וּבְיַד הָאִישׁ קְנֵה הַמִּדָּה״? הָהוּא, לְתַרְעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya said: You have nothing better for measuring than iron chains,** as they do not stretch. **But what shall we do, as the Torah states:** "I lifted up my eyes again and looked, and behold a man **with a measuring rope in his hand"** (Zechariah 2:5), from which it is derived that measurements must be made with a rope. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it** also **written: "And in the man's hand a measuring reed** of six cubits long, of one cubit and a handbreadth each" (Ezekiel 40:5), which indicates that reeds may also be used for measuring? The Gemara answers: **That is** used **for** measuring **gates,** which are too narrow to be measured with lengthy ropes.

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya* states: *ein lecha she-yafeh l'medidah yoter mi-shalshela'ot shel barzel* — there is nothing better for measuring than iron chains. Iron chains do not sag and do not stretch, making them in principle the most reliable tool. Surveyors historically did indeed use iron — and later steel — chains for precisely this reason. But, he asks, *aval mah na'aseh* — what can we do? The *Torah* (or more precisely *Nach*, from *Zechariah*) says *u-vyado chevel midah* — in his hand was a rope of measurement — indicating that a rope is the prescribed instrument. The *gemara* then notes that *Yechezkel*, when describing the measurements of the *Mikdash Shelishi*, refers to *kneh ha-midah*, a measuring reed, which is also rigid. The *gemara* answers: that was for measuring the gates, which were short enough to be measured with a reed. For the longer distances involved in *techumim*, a rope is necessary since repeating many short reed-lengths would be impractical.

One might also ask: if a rope must be used, why hold it in the air rather than lay it on the ground? If the rope were truly a rigid chain, measuring along the ground might suffice. The answer, as will emerge from a later *gemara*, is that when the ground slopes at an angle, one measures the horizontal distance rather than the walking distance along the slope — and holding the rope at chest height facilitates taking the horizontal.

### Three Rope Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף, שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבָלִים הֵם: שֶׁל מָגָג, שֶׁל נֵצֶר, וְשֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן. שֶׁל מָגָג לְפָרָה, דִּתְנַן: כְּפָתוּהָ בְּחֶבֶל הַמָּגָג וּנְתָנוּהָ עַל גַּב מַעֲרַכְתָּהּ. שֶׁל נְצָרִים לְסוֹטָה, דִּתְנַן: וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵבִיא חֶבֶל הַמִּצְרִי וְקוֹשְׁרוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִדַּדֶּיהָ. שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן לִמְדִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef taught** that **there are three** kinds of **rope,** each required by *halakha* for a different purpose: A rope **of *magag*,** a kind of bulrush reed; a rope **of *netzer*,** made from fibrous palm vines; **and** a rope **of flax.** They are used for the following purposes: A rope **of *magag*** is utilized **for** the burning of **the** red **heifer, as we learned** in a mishna: **They would bind** the heifer **with a rope of *magag* and place it on its woodpile,** where it would be burned after it was slaughtered. A rope **of *netzer*** was required **for a *sota*,** a woman suspected of adultery, **as we learned** in a mishna: Before the *sota* is compelled to drink the bitter waters, her clothes are torn. **And after that** a priest **brings a *mitzri* rope,** i.e., a rope made of reeds [*netzarim*], **and binds it above her breasts,** so that her garments will not fall. A rope **of flax** is used **for measuring.**

*Rav Yosef* teaches that three types of rope appear in various *mishnayot*, each used for a different purpose. The rope of *megag* — identified by Rashi as a material not susceptible to *tumah* — was used for the *parah adumah*. As the *mishnah* in that tractate records, after the cow was slaughtered it was bound with a *chevel ha-megag* and placed on the wood pile to be burned. *Tosafot* notes that ropes generally are not *mekabel tumah* since they are twisted rather than woven; nevertheless, the preference was for a material that had never been susceptible to *tumah* at all, consistent with the general concern for purity in the *parah* procedure.

The rope of *netzer* — identified by Rashi as *sivei dedikla*, fibers of palm — was used for the *sotah*. The *mishnah* there records that after her garment was torn, a *chevel mitzri* (an Egyptian rope, understood here as this palm-fiber rope) was tied above her breasts to prevent her from standing exposed. Finally, *pishtan* — flax — was used for measurement. This stands in contrast to the earlier discussion that identified the measuring rope as being made from a type of palm. The basis for assigning flax to measurement is not explained; it is simply asserted. Flax, of course, is quite absorbent, which would seem to make it less ideal than a non-absorbent material, and the *gemara* does not address this tension.

## Valley Crossing

### Absorbing Valleys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מוֹדֵד וְהִגִּיעַ לְגַיְא אוֹ לְגָדֵר, מַבְלִיעוֹ, וְחוֹזֵר לְמִדָּתוֹ. הִגִּיעַ לְהַר, מַבְלִיעוֹ וְחוֹזֵר לְמִדָּתוֹ. וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יֵצֵא חוּץ לַתְּחוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If **one was measuring** the limit **and he reached a canyon or a fence,** the height of the fence and the depth of the canyon are not counted toward the two thousand cubits; rather, **he spans it and** then **resumes his measurement.** Two people hold the two ends of the rope straight across the canyon or the fence, and the distance is measured as though the area were completely flat. If **one reached a hill,** he does not measure its height, but rather **he spans** the hill as if it were not there **and** then **resumes his measurement,** **provided he does not** thereby **go out beyond the** city's Shabbat **limit,** as those watching the surveyor might mistakenly think the limit extends to that point.

The *gemara* now turns to a *baraita* that elaborates on the measuring procedure. *Haya moded vehigi'a hamidah lege* — if you were measuring and you reached a valley, the procedure depends on its width. If the valley is narrow enough — less than fifty *amot* — you can throw the rope across while two people stand on either side and hold it taut: *im yachol lehavli'o bechevel shel chamishim amah meval'o*, you absorb it within the fifty-*amah* rope. This is the simplest case.

*Rashi* explains that the phrase *chozer lemidato* — returning to one's measurement — which appears in the *mishnah*, implies the more complex case: when you cannot absorb it in a single rope-length because the valley is wider than fifty *amot*, you go to the side where the valley is narrow enough to cross, do your crossing there, and then sight back parallel to where you had been measuring: *vetzofeh keneged midato, vechozer lemidato* — you look to where you were and return to that line.

### Parallel Measurement

The requirement to return to the same measurement line raises an immediate practical difficulty: how does one ensure that the detour is made along a precisely parallel line and that one returns to exactly the correct spot? This is a genuine surveying problem. Any slight deviation from a true perpendicular during the detour will compound into an error in the final measurement. Moreover, if one walks due north for any significant distance, the curvature of the earth means that the distance along the ground from east to west actually varies, introducing small but real discrepancies. For the purposes of the *gemara*'s discussion, the goal of returning to one's line is simply assumed to be achievable, and no mechanism for verifying the parallel is specified.

### Bent Valleys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה גֵּיא מְעוּקָּם — מַקְדִּיר וְעוֹלֶה, מַקְדִּיר וְיוֹרֵד. הִגִּיעַ לְכוֹתֶל — אֵין אוֹמְרִים יִקּוֹב הַכּוֹתֶל, אֶלָּא אוֹמְדוֹ וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If the canyon was curved** so that it surrounds the city on more than one side, and it cannot be spanned on the side where he wishes to measure the limit, **he pierces and ascends, pierces and descends,** thereby measuring the canyon's width bit by bit. If **he reached a wall, we do not say that he should pierce the wall** so that it can be precisely measured; **rather, he estimates** its width **and** then **leaves** and continues on.

The *baraita* continues: *im haya gai me'ukam* — if the valley is bent, you do *makdir ve'oleh makdir veyored*, the piercing. *Rashi*'s simpler explanation of "bent" is that the valley curves in such a way that by the time you have gone far enough to the side to find a crossing point, you have already gone *chutz letchum* — outside the *techum* — in the lateral direction. If the surveyors are seen that far out, onlookers will mistakenly assume that the *techum* extends to where the surveyors are standing, when in fact it ends much closer. *Tosafot* adds a further consideration: even if the lateral displacement would not technically take the surveyors outside the *techum*, if the detour is so long that they cannot reliably find their way back to the same line, the method is no longer viable. In either case, when moving to the side is not an option, one must resort to the piercing technique.

## Piercing Technique

### Four-Amah Rope Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהַבְלִיעוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מְקַדְּרִין, תַּחְתּוֹן כְּנֶגֶד לִבּוֹ, עֶלְיוֹן כְּנֶגֶד מַרְגְּלוֹתָיו. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נְקִיטִינַן אֵין מְקַדְּרִין אֶלָּא בְּחֶבֶל שֶׁל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **If,** due to the width of the canyon or hill, **one cannot span it,** he may pierce it. **The Sages taught** a *baraita* which explains this procedure: **How does one** figuratively **pierce** a hill? Two people hold the two ends of a measuring rope. The one who is **lower** down on the hill holds the rope **at the level of his heart** while the one who is **higher** holds it **at the level of his feet,** and they proceed to measure in this fashion. **Abaye said:** Based on tradition, **we hold** that **one may pierce only with a rope of four cubits.**

The piercing technique — *makdir ve'oleh makdir veyored* — is a method for measuring horizontal distance when a valley is too wide to cross with a rope and too awkward or far to circumvent. The basic principle is this: instead of using a fifty-*amah* rope, one uses a rope of only four *amot*, as *Abaye* states: *naktinan ein mekadrin ela bechevel shel arba amot* — we have received the tradition that one only pierces with a rope of four *amot*. One person stands at a lower position and holds the rope at the level of his heart; the person above holds it at the level of his feet. This places the rope as close to horizontal as possible, minimizing the vertical component of the measurement. By repeating this process in successive steps up or down the slope, one accumulates a total that approximates the horizontal distance rather than the walking distance along the incline. The reason for using a short rope rather than the standard fifty-*amah* rope is precisely to reduce the angle: a longer rope held between two people at different heights would capture far more of the vertical than the horizontal.

This is, of course, a technological solution constrained by the instruments available. Since this procedure is not based on any *pasuk* and is simply a pragmatic approximation technique, it follows that if better methods were available — satellite imaging, for instance, or modern surveying equipment — one would presumably use them to obtain a more accurate horizontal measurement.

### Walls and Estimation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: מַבְלִיעוֹ וְחוֹזֵר לְמִדָּתוֹ? הָתָם, נִיחָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּא. הָכָא, לָא נִיחָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't we learn** in the mishna: If he reached a canyon or fence, **he spans it and** then **resumes his measurement?** Why is a precise measurement required there, whereas in the case of a wall, an estimate is sufficient? The Gemara explains: **There,** in the mishna, we are dealing with a place whose **use is convenient,** i.e., where the slope is relatively gentle so that the area can be crossed. Therefore, the area must actually be measured. However, **here,** in the *baraita*, the wall's **use is not convenient.** Since one cannot walk through the wall, an estimate of its width is sufficient.

The *gemara* then addresses a qualitatively different obstacle. *Higia le-kotel, ein omrim yikof ha-kotel* — if you arrive at a wall, we do not say you should pierce through it. *Tosafot* suggests this means one should not try to hold the rope up over the wall on poles, i.e., attempting to engineer a straight-line measurement over an impassable obstacle. Instead: *ela omdo ve-holech lo* — you estimate it and move on.

The *gemara* then asks: but we just said that in the valley case you absorb it and return to your measurement — why do we allow simple estimation here? The answer is *nicha tashmishtei* versus *lo nicha tashmishtei*: in the valley case, the terrain is usable, meaning accessible enough to do successive measurements; in the wall case, it is not. *Rashi* explains this distinction in terms of gradient. A relatively gradual slope or descent — one that can be traversed — permits either crossing with the rope directly, moving off to the side, or doing the successive measurements. A nearly vertical face — a steep mountain or a wall — cannot reasonably require the surveyors to climb it. In such a case, estimation is the appropriate response.

It should be noted that this asymmetry between valley and mountain treatment — that mountains admit estimation while valleys require more careful treatment — will persist as the *gemara* develops further rules. It is also worth noting the halakhic implication for the measurer's experience: the *techum* is measured horizontally, not by walking distance. Thus a person could in principle climb an enormous mountain on *Shabbat* without violating the *techum*, provided the horizontal distance does not exceed two thousand *amot*, since vertical climbing does not count against the *techum*.

### Plumb Line Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין חוּט הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת יוֹרֵד כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, אֲבָל חוּט הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת יוֹרֵד כְּנֶגְדּוֹ — מוֹדְדוֹ מְדִידָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: They taught** the method of piercing **only where a plumb line does not drop straight down,** i.e., where the canyon has a slope. **However,** if **a plumb line drops straight down,** i.e., if the canyon wall is very steep, **he measures** the width of the canyon **properly** at the bottom of the canyon, without taking its walls into account.

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel* introduces an important qualification regarding valleys. *Lo shanu ela she'ein chut hamishkolet yored kenegdo* — the rule that you do successive measurements applies only when the plumb line does not fall directly beneath your feet, i.e., when the valley wall is not purely vertical. *Aval chut hamishkolet yored kenegdo* — if the plumb line does fall straight down, indicating a sheer vertical drop — *modedo medida yafa*, you do a proper measurement. And what is that proper measurement? You go down to the bottom of the valley and measure the flat ground there.

The logic is as follows: if the drop is perfectly vertical, there is no horizontal component to the valley wall itself, and therefore the piercing technique (which measures horizontal distance in successive steps) is neither necessary nor applicable. The horizontal distance is simply the width of the valley floor, and one measures that directly. If however the plumb line does not fall straight down — meaning the valley is inclined at an angle — then measuring the floor would give you a shorter figure than the true horizontal span at the top, and you must use the successive measurements to capture that horizontal distance.

## Valley Depth Rules

### Hundred-Amah Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה עוֹמְקוֹ שֶׁל גֵּיא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אַלְפַּיִם. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: עָמוֹק מֵאָה וְרוֹחַב חֲמִשִּׁים — מַבְלִיעוֹ. וְאִם לָאו — אֵין מַבְלִיעוֹ? הוּא דְּאָמַר כַּאֲחֵרִים, דְּתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ עָמוֹק אַלְפַּיִם וְרוֹחַב חֲמִשִּׁים — מַבְלִיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the depth of a canyon** that may be spanned if it is not more than fifty cubits wide? **Rav Yosef said:** Up to **two thousand** cubits; but if it is deeper than that, the slope must be measured as well. **Abaye raised an objection** from the following *baraita*: If a canyon is up to **one hundred** cubits **deep and** up to **fifty** cubits **wide, one may span it; and if not, one may not span it.** How could Rav Yosef say that the canyon may be spanned if its depth is less than two thousand cubits? The Gemara answers: **He stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of ***Aḥerim*; as it was taught** in a *baraita*: ***Aḥerim* say: Even** if the canyon is **two thousand** cubits **deep and fifty** cubits **wide, one may span it.**

The *gemara* now turns to the question of how deep a valley may be before different rules apply. *Ve-chama omko shel gai* — how deep is the valley for which we permit you to absorb it? *Abaye* states: *omek kuf ve-rochav nun mavlio* — if it is a hundred *amot* deep and fifty *amot* wide, you can absorb it. If not — presumably meaning deeper than that — *ein mavlio*, you cannot absorb it in the simple sense, and you must use successive measurements. This is the position of the *tanna kama* as the *gemara* understands it.

### Two-Thousand-Amah Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲפִילּוּ יָתֵר מֵאַלְפַּיִם. כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא וְלָא כַּאֲחֵרִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternate version of the previous discussion. **Some say** that **Rav Yosef said: Even** if the canyon is **more than two thousand** cubits deep, it may be spanned. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Rav Yosef say this? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*, and** it is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **the *Aḥerim*.**

The *gemara* then cites an alternative view. *Acherim omrim*: even if the depth is two thousand *amot* and the width is fifty, you absorb it. This implies that beyond two thousand, one cannot absorb it. *Rav Yosef* goes further still, saying *afilu yatir me-alpayim* — even more than two thousand *amot* deep makes no difference in the absorbability. The *gemara* then asks: *keman* — who does *Rav Yosef* follow? *Lo k'tanna kama v'lo k'acherim* — it fits neither the *tanna kama* who limits it to a hundred *amot*, nor the view of *Acherim* who permit up to two thousand. *Rashi* explains the consequence of *Rav Yosef*'s view: even if the valley is deeper than two thousand *amot*, if you could otherwise absorb it within a single fifty-*amah* rope, you may still do so. Yet this explanation in *Rashi* is not explicit in the *gemara* itself.

One might ask: why should depth matter at all? The whole purpose of the measurement is to determine horizontal distance. Whether the valley is a hundred or two thousand *amot* deep, as long as it is narrow enough to throw the rope across or to circumvent, the technique seems the same. The *gemara* does not resolve this difficulty fully, and it remains genuinely puzzling — particularly since the practical scenario of someone walking through a two-thousand-*amah* chasm on *Shabbat* seems remote. Perhaps the intent is that when the valley is extraordinarily deep, doing the successive measurements involves so much effort — and covers so much distance — that it becomes effectively impossible, and the ruling adjusts accordingly.

### Plumb Line Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם — שֶׁאֵין חוּט הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת יוֹרֵד כְּנֶגְדּוֹ. הָכָא — בְּחוּט הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת יוֹרֵד כְּנֶגְדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** where the *tanna'im* disagree about the depth of a canyon that may be spanned, they refer to a case where **a plumb line does not drop straight down** and therefore there is reason to measure the slope. **Here,** however, where Rav Yosef says that the canyon may be spanned even if it is more than two thousand cubits deep, he is referring to a case where **a plumb line drops straight down.**

The *gemara* reconciles the seemingly conflicting standards — one hundred *amot* versus two thousand *amot* — by invoking the plumb line distinction introduced earlier. When the *tanna kama* and *Acherim* debate at what depth you can no longer absorb the valley, they are referring to a valley that is *she'ein chut hamishkoles yored kenegdo* — where the plumb does not fall straight down, i.e., the valley has inclined walls. In such a case it is possible to do the piercing and successive measurements, and the debate is about how deep a valley can be before we require you to do so rather than simply absorbing it with a rope. *Rav Yosef*, by contrast, is referring to the case *bechut hamishkoles yored kenegdo* — where the plumb falls straight down, meaning the walls are sheer. In that case, there is no way to perform the successive measurements on the wall at all, and so he says that no matter how deep it is, you simply go to the bottom and measure: depth is irrelevant to that calculus.

### Four-Amah Deviation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אֵין חוּט הַמִּשְׁקוֹלֶת יוֹרֵד כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר אֲבִימִי: אַרְבַּע. וְכֵן תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where a plumb line does not drop straight down, how much** must it extend from the top of the canyon in order for the wall of the canyon to be considered a slope rather than a vertical wall? **Avimi said: Four** cubits. If the bed of the canyon lies four cubits beyond the top edge of the canyon, the wall is sloped and must be included in the measurement. **And similarly, Rami bar Ezekiel taught,** based upon a *baraita*, that the maximum run is **four** cubits.

The *gemara* then asks: when we say the plumb line does not fall straight down, indicating the valley is inclined — *ad kama*, how much of a deviation constitutes that condition? *Avimi* states: four *amot*, and *Rami bar Yechezkel* teaches the same. That is, if when you drop a plumb line from the top of the valley wall, the weight lands four *amot* away from directly beneath you when it reaches the ground, that constitutes a non-vertical incline, and the successive-measurement procedure is required.

This raises a natural concern: four *amot* of deviation is a function of both the angle and the depth of the valley. Four *amot* of horizontal displacement over a very shallow valley would indicate a very gentle slope, while the same four *amot* over a deep valley would indicate a nearly vertical one. The *gemara* does not address this geometric ambiguity directly. The overall picture of valley measurement — when to absorb, when to pierce, when to go to the bottom, and what depth thresholds apply — is complex, and as *Tosafot* recognized, requires a systematic framework to understand in its totality.

## Mountain Slopes

### Ten-in-Four Slope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ לְהַר, מַבְלִיעוֹ וְחוֹזֵר לְמִדָּתוֹ. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּהַר הַמִּתְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂרָה מִתּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע, אֲבָל בְּהַר הַמִּתְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂרָה מִתּוֹךְ חָמֵשׁ — מוֹדְדוֹ מְדִידָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If **he reached a hill,** he does not measure its height, but rather **he spans** the hill as if it were not there **and** then **resumes his measurement. Rava said: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to a hill that has an incline of ten** handbreadths **within** a run of **four** cubits. **However, with regard to** a gentler **hill,** e.g., one **that has an incline of ten** handbreadths **within five** cubits, **one must measure** the hill **properly,** i.e., he must include the slope itself in his measurement.

*Rava* introduces the central qualification for mountains. *Lo shanu ela behar hamitlaket asara mitoch arba* — the rule that you move off to the side of a mountain applies only when the mountain rises ten *tefachim* within four *amot* of horizontal distance. An *amah* is six *tefachim*, so four *amot* equals twenty-four *tefachim*, and the slope is therefore ten over twenty-four, which works out to an angle of approximately 22.6 degrees. This is the standard threshold used in *halacha* to define a slope so steep that it is treated as a wall rather than mere inclined ground. It is roughly five times the maximum slope permitted under the Americans with Disabilities Act (one in twelve), which is itself already a fairly challenging incline. A slope that rises at this rate has the visual character and practical effect of a wall rather than a passable hillside.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן מַתְנֵי לְקוּלָּא. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּהַר הַמִּתְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂרָה מִתּוֹךְ חָמֵשׁ, אֲבָל בְּהַר הַמִּתְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂרָה מִתּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע — אוֹמְדוֹ וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, teaches a lenient** formulation of this *halakha*: **Rava said** that **they only taught** this *halakha* **with regard to a hill that has an incline of ten** handbreadths **within** a run of **five** cubits. **However, with regard to** a steeper **hill that has an incline of ten** handbreadths **within four** cubits, one need not take any precise measurements; instead, **he estimates** the length of the hill, **and** then **leaves** and continues measuring from the other side.

An alternative version of *Rava*'s statement is presented *lekula* — in the more lenient direction. *Aval behar hamitlaket asara mitoch chamesh* — but if the mountain rises ten *tefachim* within five *amot*, a more gradual slope, *modedo medida yafa* — you do a proper measurement. *Rashi* explains that *medida yafa* here means measuring as you walk: you simply treat the gradual slope as ordinary ground and measure along it, without trying to correct for the incline by moving off to the side or doing successive measurements.

Conversely, when the slope is sharper — ten in four — *omdo ve-holech lo*, you estimate and proceed. This is the statement *Rav Huna son of Rav Natan* taught in the lenient formulation. The overall logic is consistent with the general approach: when a mountain or slope is sufficiently gradual that walking it is straightforward, you measure the walking distance and treat it as ordinary terrain. When it is steep enough to be wall-like, you do not demand that the surveyors deal with it through precise measurement techniques; you simply estimate.

This represents a systematic leniency toward mountains relative to valleys. By valleys, elaborate measurement techniques — successive piercings, bottom measurements — are required in many scenarios. By mountains, once the slope reaches the threshold of a wall, the *gemara* simply says: estimate and move on.

### Techum Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יֵצֵא חוּץ לַתְּחוּם. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ מִדַּת תְּחוּמִין בָּאָה לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that one may measure a canyon or hill located within the Shabbat limit, **provided that one does not go out beyond the limit.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this restriction? **Rav Kahana said:** It is **a decree, lest** people **say: The measurement of the** Shabbat **limit comes to here.** Since people know that he set out to measure the Shabbat limit, if they see him measuring in a certain spot they will assume that the area is included in the Shabbat limit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהַבְלִיעוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מְקַדְּרִין, תַּחְתּוֹן כְּנֶגֶד לִבּוֹ, עֶלְיוֹן כְּנֶגֶד מַרְגְּלוֹתָיו. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נְקִיטִינַן אֵין מְקַדְּרִין אֶלָּא בְּחֶבֶל שֶׁל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **If,** due to the width of the canyon or hill, **one cannot span it,** he may pierce it. **The Sages taught** a *baraita* which explains this procedure: **How does one** figuratively **pierce** a hill? Two people hold the two ends of a measuring rope. The one who is **lower** down on the hill holds the rope **at the level of his heart** while the one who is **higher** holds it **at the level of his feet,** and they proceed to measure in this fashion. **Abaye said:** Based on tradition, **we hold** that **one may pierce only with a rope of four cubits.**

The rule that when moving to the side, the surveyors may not go *chutz latchum* in the other direction — *u-bilvad shelo yetzei chutz lat-techum* — is given its rationale by *Rav Kahana*: *gezeira shema yomeru midat techumin ba'ah lechan* — a rabbinic decree lest people say the *techum* boundary reaches that far. If surveyors are seen measuring in a particular location, observers will naturally assume that location falls within the *techum*, even if the surveyors are only there because they had to detour around an obstacle. To prevent that false impression, the surveyors must stay within the *techum* when making their lateral detour.

It follows that when the valley or obstacle is so broad that even moving off to the side would push the surveyors outside the *techum*, the only option is the piercing technique. And as *Abaye* states: *naktinan ein mekadrin ela bechevel shel arba amot* — the tradition is that this is done with a four-*amah* rope, the lower person holding it at his heart, the upper person at his feet. The short rope and the height differential work together to capture horizontal distance rather than the diagonal or vertical.

## Experts & Testimony

### Expert Measurers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מוֹדְדִין אֶלָּא מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One **may measure** the Shabbat limit **only with an expert** surveyor.

The *mishnah* states: *ein modedin ela min hamumche* — you may only measure from an expert. *Tosafot* offers a surprising reading: *mumche* here does not mean an expert measurer but rather the *middle point* of each side of the city, connecting the word to the expression *macha al ketef yam kinneret*. This is a minority reading. The straightforward understanding is that the *mishnah* requires trained, qualified individuals to perform the measurements.

### Conflicting Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִיבָּה לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד, וּמִיעֵט לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר — שׁוֹמְעִין לִמְקוֹם שֶׁרִיבָּה. רִיבָּה לְאֶחָד וּמִיעֵט לְאֶחָד — שׁוֹמְעִין לַמְרוּבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** If it is discovered that the surveyor **extended** the limit **in one place and reduced it in another place,** so that the line marking the Shabbat limit is not straight, **one accepts** the measurement of **the place where he extended** the limit and straightens the limit accordingly. Similarly, if the surveyor **extended** the limit **for one and reduced** it **for another, one accepts the extended** measurement.

*Riva bimakom echad u-mi'et bimakom acher* — if one measurement comes out longer and another shorter at different points along the same side of the city, *shomin limakom sheriva* — you accept the longer measurement. Since the entire institution of *techumim* is *d'rabanan*, we adopt the more lenient position, which in this context means the greater distance. The *gemara* notes a second formulation: *riva l'echad u-mi'et l'echad* — if different measurers produced different results, again you follow the more expansive result. The *gemara* will clarify the distinction between these two formulations, but the underlying principle — following the longer distance — is uniform.

### Slave Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ עֶבֶד אֲפִילּוּ שִׁפְחָה נֶאֱמָנִין לוֹמַר עַד כָּאן תְּחוּם שַׁבָּת, שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת הַדָּבָר לְהַחֲמִיר, אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **even** a gentile **slave** and **even** a gentile **maidservant,** whose testimonies are generally considered unreliable, **are trustworthy to say: The Shabbat limit** extended **until here; as the Sages did not state the matter,** the laws of Shabbat limits, **to be stringent, but** rather **to be lenient.** The prohibition to walk more than two thousand cubits is rabbinic in origin and is therefore interpreted leniently.

*Va'afilu eved va'afilu shifcha ne'emanim lomar ad kan techum Shabbat* — even a male or female slave is believed to testify as to where the *techum Shabbat* ends. The *mishnah* notably does not mention women separately, which is presumably because this is not formal *edut* at all — it is the kind of everyday informational statement governed by the principle of *eid echad ne'eman b'issurin*, under which even ordinarily disqualified witnesses are relied upon for practical matters of *issur*. Women are already trusted in such contexts; the novelty here is extending that trust to slaves, who might otherwise be considered less reliable. If a slave says that a marker has fallen but that the *techum* always ended at this particular spot, that testimony is accepted.

### Leniency Principle

The *mishnah* concludes: *shelo amru chachamim et hadavar l'hachmir ela l'hakel* — the Sages did not establish this matter in order to be strict but rather to be lenient. The principle of leniency pervades the laws of *techumim*: conflicting measurements resolve in favor of the greater distance, slave testimony is accepted to expand the *techum*, and — as has been noted at various earlier points in the tractate — when there is doubt in *eruv* matters, we generally rule *lekula*. This is the appropriate posture for a rabbinic ordinance: having established the framework, the Sages built into it a consistent tilt toward leniency rather than stringency.
