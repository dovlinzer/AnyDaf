# Bava Kamma 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tza'ar Introduction

### Mishna Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צַעַר – כְּווֹאוֹ בְּשַׁפּוּד אוֹ בְּמַסְמֵר, וַאֲפִילּוּ עַל צִפּוֹרְנוֹ מְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה חַבּוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** How is payment for **pain** assessed? If **he burned** another **with a skewer or with a** hot **nail,** or even if he burned him **on his fingernail,** which is **a place where he does not cause a bruise,** i.e., there was no personal injury, only pain, the court evaluates the monetary value of the pain.

The *gemara* on 85b opens with the category of *tza'ar* — pain and suffering — which the *mishnah* addresses as follows: if one burned another with a spit or a nail, even on the fingernail, which is a place that does not leave a *chavurah* (wound or bruise), we assess how much a person of similar standing would be willing to accept in order to undergo such suffering. The fingernail case is instructive: either the fingernail is more resistant to wounding and therefore leaves no lasting mark, or the area beneath the nail is particularly sensitive and therefore subject to acute pain even without permanent damage. Either way, the *mishnah* establishes that *tza'ar* can be assessed even in the absence of a lasting physical injury.

### No Bruise, Tza'ar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צַעַר שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק מִשְׁתַּלֵּם – מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רָבָא: בֶּן עַזַּאי הִיא – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״כְּוִיָּה״ נֶאֶמְרָה תְּחִילָּה. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: ״חַבּוּרָה״ נֶאֶמְרָה תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** This teaches that even when the **pain** is **not in a place** that causes **damage** to the injured party, the one who caused the injury must **pay** compensation. The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this statement? **Rava said: This is** the opinion of **ben Azzai, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the verse: "*Keviyya* for *keviyya*, a wound for a wound, *ḥabura* for *ḥabura*" (Exodus 21:25), **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: A burn [*keviyya*] is the first** term **stated** in the verse, and **ben Azzai says: A bruise [*ḥabura*] is the first** term **stated** in the verse.

This sets the stage for a fundamental question: can *tza'ar* be collected even when there is no *nezek* — no permanent, appreciable damage to the injured party? The phrase *makom she'eino oseh chavurah*, a place that does not leave a wound or bruise, becomes legally significant because a bruise or wound could potentially be something permanent. If, however, one can be liable for *tza'ar* even in a place that leaves no lasting mark, then *tza'ar shelo bemakom nezek mishtalem* — pain and suffering can be compensated even where there is no depreciating injury. We previously saw that other components of the five-fold damages were *mishtalem bemakom nezek*, payable only in conjunction with *nezek*. Here, *tza'ar* stands apart.

## Rabbi vs. Ben Azzai

### Tza'ar Shelo Nezek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי סָבַר: ״כְּוִיָּה״ – דְּלֵית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה מַשְׁמַע; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חַבּוּרָה״ לְגַלּוֹיֵי עֲלַהּ דִּכְוִיָּה – דְּאִית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה אִין, אִי לָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the *baraita*: Ben Azzai certainly agrees that the first term in the verse, "*keviyya*," means a burn. **With regard to what do they disagree? Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds that "*keviyya*" indicates** an injury **with no bruise,** and that one would be liable to pay for injuring another even in that manner; therefore, when **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: **"*Ḥabura*,"** it is **to reveal about** the meaning **of "*keviyya*"** that if the burn also **has a bruise,** then **yes,** the one who caused the injury must pay compensation for pain; but **if** the burn has **no** bruise, then **no,** the one who caused the injury does not pay compensation for pain alone.

The *gemara* asks: *man tana?* — which *tanna* holds that *tza'ar* is payable even without *nezek*? *Amar Rava: Ben Azzai hi.* Rava rules that this view belongs to Ben Azzai, and he derives this from a *baraita* that explicates the underlying *machloket*.

### Rabbi's Keviyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן עַזַּאי סָבַר: ״כְּוִיָּה״ – דְּאִית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה מַשְׁמַע; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חַבּוּרָה״ לְגַלּוֹיֵי עֲלַהּ דִּכְוִיָּה – דְּלֵית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And ben Azzai holds** that **"*keviyya*" indicates** a burn **that** also **has a bruise,** and that one would be liable to pay for injuring another only in that manner; therefore, when **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: **"*Ḥabura*,"** it is **to reveal about** the meaning **of "*keviyya*"** that even if it is a burn **with no bruise,** the one who caused the injury must pay compensation for pain alone, even though there is no damage.

The *baraita* states that Rabbi holds *keviyah ne'emrah techilah* — the word *keviyah* (a burn) is mentioned first in the relevant *pasuk*. Rava reads Rabbi's position as follows: Rabbi understands that *keviyah* naturally implies an injury that does *not* leave a lasting wound or bruise. Therefore, the *Torah* explicitly added the word *chavurah* to teach that only when there is also a *chavurah* — that is, only when there is also *nezek* — does the obligation to pay *tza'ar* apply. On this reading, Rabbi holds that *tza'ar* is payable only *bemakom nezek*.

### Ben Azzai's View

Ben Azzai, by contrast, holds *chavurah ne'emrah techilah* — that *chavurah* appears first in the *pasuk*. Rava reads Ben Azzai as understanding that *keviyah* naturally implies an injury that *does* leave a lasting wound. The *Torah* therefore added *chavurah* to teach that even a *keviyah* that does *not* create a lasting injury is included, and that *tza'ar* is therefore payable even *shelo bemakom nezek* — even in the absence of permanent damage. The two positions thus hinge on the default semantic implication of the word *keviyah*: does it suggest a permanent wound, or not?

### Klal U-Prat Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! רַבִּי אוֹמֵר ״כְּוִיָּה״ נֶאֶמְרָה תְּחִילָּה – סָבַר: ״כְּוִיָּה״ – דְּאִית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה מַשְׁמַע; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חַבּוּרָה״ לְגַלּוֹיֵי עֲלַהּ דִּכְוִיָּה – דְּלֵית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to** Rava's explanation of the *baraita*: **On the contrary, the opposite stands to reason,** as **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: A burn [*keviyya*] is the first** term **stated** in the verse, since **he holds** that **"*keviyya*" indicates** a burn **that** also **has a bruise,** and one would be liable to pay for injuring another only in that manner. Therefore, when **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: **"*Ḥabura*,"** it is **to reveal about** the meaning **of "*keviyya*"** that even if it is a burn **with no bruise,** the one who caused the injury must pay compensation for pain alone, even though there is no damage.

One might ask whether the disagreement is really a hermeneutic one of *klal u-frat*. In fact, the *gemara* concludes that the underlying *machloket* between Rabbi and Ben Azzai is precisely about *klal u-frat ham'ruchakim zeh mizeh* — the principle of *klal u-frat* when the general statement and the specific detail are not adjacent but separated by intervening words. The word *keviyah* (the general category of burn) and the word *chavurah* (the specific wound) are separated in the *pasuk* by the phrase *petza tachat petza* — wound for wound — so that *chavurah* does not appear immediately after *keviyah*. Rabbi holds that when the *klal* and the *prat* are distant from each other, one cannot apply the principle of *klal u-frat*, and therefore *chavurah* does not function as a limiting specification of *keviyah*. Consequently, Rabbi holds that *tza'ar* is payable even without *nezek*. Ben Azzai holds that even when the *klal* and *prat* are separated, the hermeneutic of *klal u-frat* still applies, and *chavurah* therefore limits *keviyah* to cases that also leave a lasting wound. The *gemara* also addresses what Rabbi does with the word *chavurah* if he cannot apply *klal u-frat*: it teaches *ledmamei mitematrin*, that when there is a *chavurah* one must also pay for *nezek*. This points to an important structural inversion: in the context of damages between human beings, *tza'ar* is in a sense primary — it is the most immediately personal form of harm — and the question of *nezek* arises as an additional layer when the injury leaves lasting consequences.

## Rav Papa's Reading

### Opposite Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר ״חַבּוּרָה״ נֶאֶמְרָה תְּחִילָּה – סָבַר: ״כְּוִיָּה״ – דְּלֵית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה מַשְׁמַע; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חַבּוּרָה״ לְגַלּוֹיֵי עֲלַהּ דִּכְוִיָּה – דְּאִית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה אִין, אִי לָא לָא. וְאַמַּסְּקָנָא קָיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa continues: And **ben Azzai says** that **a bruise [*ḥabura*] is the first** term **stated** in the verse, as **he holds** that **"*keviyya*" indicates** an injury **with no bruise,** and that one would be liable to pay for injuring another even in that manner. Therefore, when **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: **"*Ḥabura*,"** it is **to reveal about** the meaning **of "*keviyya*"** that if the burn also **has a bruise,** then **yes,** the one who caused the injury must pay compensation for pain; but **if** the burn has **no** bruise, then **no,** the one who caused the injury does not pay compensation for pain alone. **And** according to this explanation, the statements of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and ben Azzai with regard to the meaning of the terms in the verse are **based on** their **final understanding** of the verse. The term: The first term stated, is referring to the understanding of the first term after the second term has been written, not what the understanding of the first term would have been if not for the addition of the second term. According to the explanation of Rav Pappa, the mishna here is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

Rav Papa challenges Rava's reading of the *baraita* entirely. *Adarabba ipcha mistabra* — the opposite interpretation makes more sense. Rav Papa reads Rabbi as holding that *keviyah* naturally implies an injury that *does* create a lasting wound, and therefore the *Torah* added *chavurah* to include a *keviyah* that does *not* leave a lasting injury — extending *tza'ar* liability to cases without *nezek*. Ben Azzai, on Rav Papa's reading, understands *keviyah* as naturally implying an injury without a lasting wound, and the *Torah* added *chavurah* to specify that only when there is also a lasting injury is the obligation to pay *tza'ar* triggered, thus limiting *tza'ar* to *bemakom nezek*. Rav Papa thus inverts the positions of both *tanna'im* from Rava's reading.

### Keviyah Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא: ״כְּוִיָּה״ – בֵּין דְּאִית בָּהּ חַבּוּרָה בֵּין דְּלֵית בַּהּ חַבּוּרָה מַשְׁמַע; וְהָכָא בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט הַמְרוּחָקִים זֶה מִזֶּה קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and ben Azzai: **Alternatively, everyone** agrees that **"*keviyya*" indicates** an injury **whether there is a bruise or whether there is not a bruise. And here,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and ben Azzai **disagree** over whether the hermeneutical principle of a generalization and a detail is applied **with regard to a generalization and a detail that are distant from each other.**

The *gemara* then shifts to a broader reconciling formulation: perhaps all agree that the word *keviyah* encompasses both injuries that leave a lasting wound and those that do not — *bein d'it beih chavurah bein d'leit beih chavurah mashma*. The *machloket* between Rabbi and Ben Azzai then reduces entirely to the hermeneutic question of *klal u-frat ham'ruchakim zeh mizeh*, not to any disagreement about the semantic range of *keviyah* itself. Rabbi holds that separated *klal u-frat* cannot be applied, and therefore *chavurah* does not restrict *keviyah*; Ben Azzai holds that it can be applied even when terms are far apart, and therefore *chavurah* does restrict *keviyah* to cases with lasting injury.

## Tza'ar + Nezek

### Separation Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּזֶה רוֹצֶה לִיטּוֹל וְכוּ׳. צַעַר בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק, הֵיכִי שָׁיְימִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: How is the payment for pain assessed? The court **evaluates how much** money **a person** with **a similar** threshold for pain **as** the victim **is willing to take** in order to be made to suffer in this way. The one who burned the victim must then pay this amount. The Gemara asks: **How do we appraise pain in a case** where the injured party also suffered personal **injury?**

The *mishnah* stated that when assessing *tza'ar*, we evaluate how much a person of similar standing would be willing to accept — *kamah adam kayotzei bazeh rotzeh litol lihyot mista'er kach*. The *gemara* now raises a pointed question: *tza'ar bimkom nezek heichi sham'inan* — when there is both *tza'ar* and *nezek* present, how can one assess the *tza'ar* alone? The difficulty is that any realistic assessment of how much a person would accept to undergo a painful and injurious act inherently combines *tza'ar* and *nezek* — and several other components as well. If one asks someone how much they would accept to have their hand amputated, the answer will necessarily reflect not only the pain of the procedure but also the permanent loss of function, the embarrassment, and so forth. The components cannot be decoupled from the subjective assessment.

### Avuha D'Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיטּוֹל לִקְטוֹעַ לוֹ יָדוֹ. לִקְטוֹעַ לוֹ יָדוֹ?! לָא צַעַר לְחוֹדֵיהּ הוּא – הָא כּוּלְּהוּ חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים אִיכָּא! וְעוֹד, בְּשׁוּפְטָנֵי עָסְקִינַן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel's father says:** The court **evaluates how much** money **a person is willing to take** to allow someone **to sever his hand.** The Gemara objects: Do you really mean **to sever his hand?** Wouldn't this evaluation **be not just for pain, but** to **include all five types** of indemnity? **Moreover, are we dealing with fools,** who would accept money to have their hands severed?

Avuha d'Shmuel — Shmuel's father, who appears in several places in *shas* — suggests: we assess *omdim kama adam rotzeh litol liktoa lo yado*, how much a person would accept to have their hand cut off. But this suggestion is immediately rejected: *lo tza'ar lechudaihu* — that assessment does not isolate the *tza'ar* alone. *Hakol hu chamisha devarim nika* — it encompasses all five components. Moreover, there is an added problem of practical absurdity: *od beshoftei askinan* — what sane person would actually engage in such an assessment? No rational person would straightforwardly agree to negotiate a price to have their hand severed.

### Severed Limb Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִקְטוֹעַ אֶת יָדוֹ הַקְּטוּעָה. יָדוֹ הַקְּטוּעָה נָמֵי לָא צַעַר לְחוֹדֵיהּ אִיכָּא, הָא צַעַר וּבוֹשֶׁת אִיכָּא – דִּכְסִיפָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא לְמִשְׁקַל מִבְּשָׂרוֹ לְמִשְׁדְּיֵיהּ לִכְלָבִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the court evaluates how much money a person would accept to allow someone **to sever his** already **severed hand,** which is of no use. The Gemara objects: In the case of **his severed hand also, there is not pain alone, but there is** both **pain and humiliation, because it is an embarrassing matter for him** if someone were **to take** a part **of his flesh to throw it to dogs.**

A refinement is attempted: *liktoa lo yado haktu'ah* — what if the person's hand is already effectively lost, as in a case where a limb is gangrenous or has a degenerative condition and will be lost regardless? Even then the assessment fails, because *ika ha tza'ar uvoshet ika* — there is still both *tza'ar* and *boshet* present. By accelerating the point at which the limb is actually removed, one introduces pain and a form of embarrassment or indignity that would not have been present had the limb simply fallen away on its own.

### Boshet Components

The *gemara* adds a vivid dimension to the *boshet* component: *dechasifa lei milta lemishda bevissaro lachalvei* — it is deeply shameful that the amputated flesh is thrown to the dogs. This passing remark carries significant *halachic* weight. The *Shevut Yaakov* cites this as one of the rare sources in *shas* that appears to suggest that an amputated limb need not be buried — in contrast to the general rule that *ever min hachai* is subject to *tum'ah* and should be interred. However, the *Shevut Yaakov* concludes that *lehalacha* the normative practice is to bury such limbs, and the passage here should not be taken as a ruling to the contrary. The broader context of *ever min hachai* involves complex questions from *masechet Ohalot* concerning which limbs convey *tum'ah bemaga umasa* and which convey *tum'ah be'ohel*, what minimum of flesh is required together with bone, and the particular complications these raise for *kohanim* who wish to enter medical or surgical professions.

### Gov't Amputation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן לִקְטוֹעַ לוֹ יָדוֹ הַמּוּכְתֶּבֶת לַמַּלְכוּת, בֵּין סַיִיף לְסַם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the court **evaluates how much a person would give to** have someone **sever his hand, which is** already **condemned** by **a written** decree **from the government** to be severed, changing the decree **from** having it be severed by means of **a sword,** which is accompanied by pain, **to** having it be severed by means of **a drug,** which is not accompanied by pain.

The *gemara* ultimately arrives at a workable method for isolating *tza'ar*: *omdim kama adam rotzeh litein liktoa lo yado hamukhtav lamalchut bein besam lesayif* — we assess how much a person who is already sentenced by the government to have his hand cut off would be willing to pay in order to have the amputation performed with medicines (i.e., with something akin to anesthesia) rather than by the sword. In this framing, the *nezek* is already fixed — the person is going to lose the hand in any case — so the only variable is the manner of the procedure, isolating the pain component. A subsequent terminological question arises about whether the relevant verb is *litol* (to take) or *litein* (to give), since the monetary exchange can be described from either the payor's or payee's perspective; Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua resolves this by noting that *litol zeh mizeh* — to take from one another — captures either direction of the transaction.

## Ripuy & Shevet

### Mishna Complications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִפּוּי – הִכָּהוּ, חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ וְכוּ׳. [תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן:] עָלוּ בּוֹ צְמָחִים מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה, וְנִסְתְּרָה הַמַּכָּה – חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ, וְחַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ דְּמֵי שִׁבְתּוֹ. שֶׁלֹּא מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ דְּמֵי שִׁבְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: How is payment for **medical costs** assessed? If one **struck** another, then he is **liable to heal him** by paying for his medical costs. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:4): If **growths appeared on** the injured area **due to the injury, and the wound reopened,** then the one who caused the injury is again **liable to heal him** by covering his medical costs, **and is liable to give him the value of his loss of livelihood** while he recovers from the reopened wound. But if the growths that appeared **are not due to the injury, he is not liable to heal him** by covering his medical costs, **and he is not liable to give him the value of his loss of livelihood.**

The *mishnah* had addressed a further category of cases: *alu bo tzmachim machmat hamakah venistara hamakah* — if growths or scabs developed because of the wound, and the wound repeatedly reopened and healed, the injuring party remains liable for *ripuy* (medical expenses) and *shevet* (time off work), provided these complications stem from the original injury. If, however, the complications are *shelo machmat hamakah* — not caused by the injury — the injuring party is exempt from both.

### Agad Yetira Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה – חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ דְּמֵי שִׁבְתּוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שִׁבְתּוֹ – וּרְפוּאָתוֹ; כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּשֶׁבֶת – חַיָּיב בְּרִיפּוּי, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּשֶׁבֶת – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּרִיפּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **even** if the growths appeared **due to the injury, he is liable to heal him** by covering his medical costs, **but he is not liable to give him the** value **of his loss of livelihood. And the Rabbis say:** The one who caused the injury is not liable at all, because the Torah states the *halakhot* of compensation for **his loss of livelihood and his medical costs** together. This teaches that **anyone who is liable** to pay compensation **for loss of livelihood is** also **liable** to pay compensation **for medical costs, and** anyone **who is not liable** to pay compensation **for loss of livelihood is** also **not liable to** pay compensation **for medical costs.**

A *baraita* introduces a *machloket* between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that even when complications arise *machmat hamakah* (because of the injury), the injuring party must pay *ripuy* but need not pay *shevet* for the protracted recovery period. The *chachamim* hold that *ripuy* and *shevet* are linked: *kol shechayav beshevet chayav beripuy* — whoever is obligated in *shevet* is also obligated in *ripuy*, and conversely. Rava then explains the precise point of contention: *hacha bemakah nitna le'aged kamipalgi* — they are debating a wound that has been subjected to *agad yetira*, over-binding. In the medicine of the period, wrapping a wound too tightly could slow the healing process, whereas leaving it less bound might allow faster recovery. *Chachamim* hold that *makah nitna le'aged* — it is appropriate to bind the wound — and therefore even if healing takes longer as a result of this treatment, the entire extended recovery period is the injuring party's responsibility. Rabbi Yehuda holds that while basic binding is appropriate, *agad yetira* — excessive binding — is not medically necessary, and therefore the protracted recovery resulting from it is not attributable to the injuring party; hence, no *shevet* for the extended period.

This dispute has practical resonance whenever a doctor presents two treatment options — one slower and safer, one faster and riskier — and the question arises which approach the injuring party is obligated to fund, and for how long.

### Torah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? אָמַר רַבָּה: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן בְּבֵי רַב דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הָכָא בְּמַכָּה נִיתְּנָה לְאֶגֶד קָמִיפַּלְגִי – רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מַכָּה נִיתְּנָה לְאֶגֶד. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: מַכָּה לֹא נִיתְּנָה לְאֶגֶד; רִיפּוּי, דִּתְנָא בֵּיהּ קְרָא – מִיחַיַּיב; שֶׁבֶת, דְּלָא תְּנָא בֵּיהּ קְרָא – לָא מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree? Rabba said: I found the Sages in the study hall of Rav when they were sitting and were stating** an explanation: **Here,** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree about** whether **a wound may be** wrapped in a **bandage,** i.e., whether one is permitted to bandage his wound to lessen the pain, at the risk of causing damaging side effects. Rabba explains: **The Rabbis,** i.e., the first *tanna* in the *baraita*, **hold** that **a wound may be** wrapped **in a bandage.** Consequently, if the bandage causes side effects which lead the injured party to incur additional medical expenses, those expenses are considered to have resulted from the original injury. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **a wound may not be** wrapped **in a bandage.** Therefore, with regard to **medical costs,** about **which the verse repeated** the obligation to pay them, by stating: "And shall cause him to be thoroughly healed [*verappo yerappe*]" (Exodus 21:19), the one who caused the injury **is liable** to pay compensation. But for **loss of livelihood,** about **which the verse did not repeat** the obligation to pay it, the one who caused the injury **is not liable** to pay compensation.

The *gemara* then asks wherein lies the source for the obligation to pay *ripuy* and *shevet* at all. The *pasuk* states *rak shivto yiten verapo yerape* — he shall pay for his time off, and he shall surely heal him. The *chachamim* read *rak* as a limitation excluding complications that are *shelo machmat hamakah*, while the linkage between *shevet* and *ripuy* in the same *pasuk* serves to equate them. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, reads *rak* as limiting *shevet* even in cases of complications *machmat hamakah*: since the excessive binding is not halachically required, the injuring party is not liable for *shevet* during the period prolonged by that treatment. Rabbi Yehuda nonetheless requires *ripuy* because the *Torah* explicitly obligated it. The *chachamim* learn from the word *kol* — implicit in the *pasuk*'s phrasing — that *shevet* and *ripuy* rise and fall together.

The *gemara* also introduces a spectrum of opinions under the heading of *ikka de'amri* — some hold that even when complications arise *machmat hamakah*, the injuring party is entirely exempt; others hold that he is exempt from *shevet* but still liable for *ripuy*. The *rishonim* debate how to *pasken* from this sugya given the multiple *ikka de'amri* formulations.

### Doubled Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא״ – לִיתֵּן רְפוּאָה בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to compensation for medical costs in a case where one pays compensation for damage, **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava** that the **verse states: "He shall cause him to be thoroughly healed [*verappo yerappe*]"** (Exodus 21:19), and the doubled term teaches that the one who caused the injury is liable **to give** monetary compensation for **medical costs in a case of** compensation for **damage.**

The *gemara* raises an additional question about the doubled language *rappo yerappe*: since the *Torah* repeats the verb for healing, one might think this is entirely consumed by teaching the permission for doctors to heal. The *gemara* answers that the doubling is sufficient to teach both points simultaneously — *litein refuah bimkom nezek*, that medical expenses must be paid even when *nezek* is also being paid, and the permission for doctors to practice medicine. The *Torah* could have used a single form of the verb for one point, but the doubled language covers both.

### Doctor Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִכְדַאֲמַרַן, לְמִיתְנֵי בֵּיהּ קְרָא בְּרִיפּוּי! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא אוֹ ״רַפֹּא רַפֹּא״ אוֹ ״יְרַפֵּא יְרַפֵּא״, מַאי ״וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִיתֵּן רְפוּאָה בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** it is **still necessary for** the *halakhot* **that we stated** previously, i.e., it was necessary **for the verse to repeat** the language **of healing,** as the Gemara explained. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse say** one of the two words twice, **either: *Rappo rappo*, or: *Yerappe yerappe*. What** is taught by repeating the language with a change: **"*Verappo yerappe*"? Learn from** this wording that the one who caused the injury is liable **to give** monetary compensation for **medical costs in a case of damage.**

The *baraita* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from *verapo yerape* that *nitan reshut lerofei lerapot* — permission is granted to doctors to heal. The *Tosafot* here ask: why should a doctor need explicit permission? Their answer is that one might have thought that a doctor may treat only injuries caused by human beings, since those are what the *Torah* is discussing in context. One might have assumed that illnesses arising from natural causes — diseases decreed by God — should not be interfered with, because doing so appears to be *soter gzeirat hamelech*, undermining the divine decree. The *Torah* therefore teaches that this is permitted even for naturally arising illness.

This stands in contrast to the Rambam's position: the Rambam, himself a physician, does not cite this *drashah* granting permission. Instead, in his *peirush hamishna* to *masechet Nedarim*, he derives the obligation to provide medical care from *vehashevato lo* — the commandment of *hashavat aveidah*, returning a lost object. By *kal vachomer*: if one must return another's lost property, one must certainly act to restore another's health. The Rambam thus frames medical practice not as *reshut* (permission) but as *chovah* (obligation).

A further perspective is offered by the Ramban, also a physician, who in *parshat bechukotai* appears to suggest a hesitation about relying on doctors, preferring reliance on divine healing. The *Tzitz Eliezer* — the great *posek* of medical *halacha* — addresses this apparent tension and explains that the Ramban was speaking of an era of open miracles, in which divine intervention was manifest and one could reasonably expect divine healing. In our times, however, recourse to medicine is clearly obligatory.

## Medical Decisions

### Non-Injury Related

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מַאי ״שֶׁלֹּא מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה״ – כִּדְתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁעָבַר עַל דִּבְרֵי רוֹפֵא וְאָכַל דְּבַשׁ אוֹ כׇּל מִינֵי מְתִיקָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדְּבַשׁ וְכׇל מִינֵי מְתִיקָה קָשִׁים לַמַּכָּה; וְהֶעֱלָה מַכָּתוֹ גַּרְגּוּתְנִי; יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״רַק״. מַאי ״גַּרְגּוּתְנִי״? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָאתָא כְּרִיכְתָּא. מַאי אָסוּתֵיהּ? אַהֲלָא וְקִירָא וְקַלְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say: What** did the *baraita* mean when it said that the growths **were not due to the injury? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who violated** his **doctor's instructions and ate honey or any type of sweet** food, which the doctor had instructed him not to eat **because** of the fact **that honey or any type of sweet** foods **are detrimental to** the recovery from **the injury; and his injury developed *garguteni*;** one **might** have thought that the one who caused the injury **should be liable to heal him** by covering his medical costs. Therefore, **the verse states: "Only,"** to teach that he is exempt in this case. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***garguteni*? Abaye said: Necrosis. What is its remedy? Crystalline ice plant, and wax, and wine dregs.**

The *mishnah* had established that complications arising *shelo machmat hamakah* — not because of the injury — exempt the injuring party from *ripuy* and *shevet*. The *gemara* illustrates this with a concrete case: if the injured party violated the doctor's instructions, such as eating honey or other sweets — *kol minei metika kashim lamaka*, since sugar impedes healing — and as a result developed a *gargetani*, a kind of necrotic scab or dead-skin lesion, then the resulting complication is not the injuring party's responsibility. *Mah gargetani? Amar Abaye: nata krichta* — it is a bound-up dead-skin lesion, what Rashi describes as *basar met*, necrotic flesh. The *gemara* also notes a remedy: *ahala*, aloe, *kehiri*, wax, and *chalba*, a tree resin or pitch, are applied in sequence, followed by a medicine to restore the skin's color. The key point is that once a patient fails to comply with medical advice and thereby aggravates the condition, the injuring party bears no further liability for the resulting complications.

### Doctor Selection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אָסְיָיךְ אֲנָא״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״דָּמֵית עֲלַי כְּאַרְיָא אָרְבָא״. וְאִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״מַיְיתִינָא אָסְיָא דְּמַגָּן (בְּמַגָּן)״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אָסְיָא דְּמַגָּן (בְּמַגָּן) – מַגָּן שָׁוֵה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents several *halakhot* concerning compensation for medical costs: **And if** the one who caused the injury **says to** the injured party: **I will heal you** myself, as I am a doctor; the injured party may **say to him: You are to me as a predatory lion,** in that I fear you and do not trust you to heal me. **And if** the one who caused the injury **says to** the injured party: **I will bring a doctor who heals for no cost,** so that I will not need to pay for your medical costs; the injured party may **say to him: A doctor who heals for no cost** is **worth nothing.**

A series of practical legal questions follows regarding who controls the treatment process. If the injuring party proposes to treat the injured party himself — *amar lei asich ana* — the injured party may refuse, saying *damit li ke'arya arba*, you are like the lion who mauled me; I will not allow the one who harmed me to also be my doctor. The injured party is thus entitled to choose their own physician and cannot be compelled to accept treatment from the injuring party.

### Free vs. Paid

What if the injuring party offers to procure a doctor willing to treat the injured party *magan bemagan* — for free, pro bono? The injured party may refuse this as well, invoking the principle *asya demagan bemagan magan shaveh* — a doctor who works for free is worth nothing. The assumption embedded here is that monetary incentive is a meaningful driver of quality care, and a doctor who is not paid will not perform as well as one who is compensated. This point is also connected to the broader *drashah* of *verapo yerape*: according to the *Da'at Zekenim* from the *Ba'alei HaTosafot*, one reason the *Torah* must explicitly state the obligation to pay for medical care is precisely to establish that medicine is a compensated profession, since a paid doctor will treat more effectively than one working without remuneration.

### Remote Doctors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמַר: ״מַיְיתִינָא לָךְ אָסְיָא רַחִיקָא״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אָסְיָא רַחִיקָא – עֵינָא עָוַירא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the one who caused the injury **says to** the injured party: **I will bring you a doctor** from **a distant** place, the injured party may **say to him: A doctor** from **a distant** place **blinds the eye.** A local doctor has an incentive to ensure that the patient fully heals, while a doctor from a distant place has no incentive to monitor the patient once he has received his fee.

If the injuring party proposes to bring a physician from a distant location — *asya rechika* — the injured party may refuse, citing the principle *asya rechika eina avira*: a distant doctor's eye is a blind eye. A physician from far away either lacks understanding of local conditions, diet, and lifestyle, or — because he will eventually depart — has no long-term stake in the patient's outcome and will not provide adequate follow-up care. Continuity of care and familiarity with the patient's circumstances are thus recognized as legitimate considerations in selecting a physician.

### Fixed Price Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ הַיְאךְ: ״הַב לִי לְדִידִי, וַאֲנָא מַסֵּינָא נַפְשַׁאי״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פָּשְׁעַתְּ בְּנַפְשָׁךְ וְשָׁקְלַתְּ מִינַּאי טְפֵי״. וְאִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״קוּץ לִי מִקָּץ״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּפָשְׁעַתְּ בְּנַפְשָׁךְ, וְקָרוּ לִי: ״שׁוֹר הַמַּזִּיק״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if that one,** the injured party, **says to** the one who caused him injury: **Give** the money **to me and I will** take responsibility for **healing myself;** the one who caused him the injury may **say to him: You will be negligent with yourself and you will take more from me** than was originally necessary. **And if** the injured party **says to** the one who caused him injury: **Settle a fixed** sum **for me** to cover my medical costs, and I will take care of my own recovery; the one who caused him the injury may **say to him: All the more so** with this proposal is there a concern **that you will be negligent with yourself,** as you will wish to profit and you will not spend the money on your recovery, **and** people **will call me: An ox that causes damage,** because I caused an injury from which you did not recover.

What if the injuring party proposes to settle on a fixed price upfront for all medical expenses, so that the injured party need not worry about variability? The injured party may reject this as well, arguing *pashat b'nafshecha v'shaklat minai tafi* — with a fixed payment, you (the injured party) have an incentive to be negligent about your own recovery in order to extract more money than the treatment actually costs. This is the mirror image of the concern raised next.

### Shor Hamazik

Conversely, if the injured party proposes to receive a fixed sum directly and manage his own medical care — *hav li l'didi v'ana masina nafshai* — the injuring party may refuse on the grounds that the injured party will be negligent with his own recovery and then try to claim more. The injuring party adds that he worries about being compared to *shor hamazik*, a goring ox — the literary connection being that a *shor hamazik*, unlike a person, does not pay *ripuy*. If the recovery is managed poorly and results in lasting complications, onlookers will say the injured party healed as poorly as if it had been an ox that gored him, with no medical expenses covered. The injuring party thus has a reputational interest in ensuring proper medical care. The reference also underscores a structural distinction in *nezikin*: an *adam hamazik* is obligated in *ripuy*, while a *shor hamazik* is not, and the possibility of being assimilated to the latter category is itself a meaningful concern.

## Damages Concurrency

### Five Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וְכוּלָּן מִשְׁתַּלְּמִין בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״פֶּצַע תַּחַת פָּצַע״ – לִיתֵּן צַעַר בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to compensation for pain, medical costs, loss of livelihood, and humiliation, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **And all of these** types of indemnity **are paid in a case** where the one who caused injury must also pay compensation for **damage.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava: The verse states: "A wound for a wound"** (Exodus 21:25), to teach that the one who caused injury is liable **to give** compensation for **pain in a case of damage.**

A *baraita* establishes that all five categories of damages — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripuy*, *shevet*, and *boshet* — are payable together: *v'chulan mishtalmin bimkom nezek*, all of them are payable in the context of *nezek*. This does not mean *nezek* absorbs or exempts the others; rather, when *nezek* is owed, the other four are owed as well — each covering a distinct dimension of the injury.

### Petza Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹת שׁוֹגֵג כְּמֵזִיד וְאוֹנֶס כְּרָצוֹן! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״פֶּצַע בְּפָצַע״; מַאי ״פֶּצַע תַּחַת פָּצַע״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But isn't **this** verse **necessary** to teach an additional *halakha*: This serves **to include** in the category of those liable to pay compensation one who causes injury **unwittingly just as** one who causes injury **intentionally** is liable, **and** one who causes injury **due to circumstances beyond his control just as** one who causes injury **willingly.** The Gemara answers: **If so,** and the verse teaches only this, **let the verse write** the more succinct phrase: **A wound [*petza*] for a wound [*befatza*]; what** is taught by the expanded phrase: **"A wound [*petza*] for a wound [*taḥat patza*]"? Conclude two** conclusions **from** the verse, that the one who caused the injury is liable to pay compensation regardless of how he caused the injury, and that he must pay compensation for pain in addition to compensation for damage.

The *gemara* asks for the source. Rav Zevid in the name of Rava cites the *pasuk*: *petza tachat petza* — wound for wound — which teaches *leitein tza'ar bimkom nezek*, that *tza'ar* is paid alongside *nezek*. One might object that this *pasuk* is needed for a different *drashah* — to teach that a person must pay *shogeg kemezid ve'oneis keratzon*, that accidental injury is treated like intentional injury and compelled injury like willed injury. The *gemara* responds that had this been the *Torah*'s sole intent, it would have written *petza b'petza*. The additional formulation *petza tachat petza* — with the word *tachat* — yields *shema mina tarti*, two separate teachings simultaneously.

### Ripuy Without Nezek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא״ – לִיתֵּן רְפוּאָה בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק. הַאי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא״ – מִכָּאן שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה רְשׁוּת לָרוֹפֵא לְרַפּאוֹת! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״וְרוֹפֵא יְרַפֵּא״! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, לִיתֵּן רְפוּאָה בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק. וְאַכַּתִּי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִכְדַאֲמַרַן, לְמִיתְנֵי בֵּיהּ קְרָא בְּרִיפּוּי! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא אוֹ ״רַפֹּא רַפֹּא״ אוֹ ״יְרַפֵּא יְרַפֵּא״, מַאי ״וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִיתֵּן רְפוּאָה בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to compensation for medical costs in a case where one pays compensation for damage, **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava** that the **verse states: "He shall cause him to be thoroughly healed [*verappo yerappe*]"** (Exodus 21:19), and the doubled term teaches that the one who caused the injury is liable **to give** monetary compensation for **medical costs in a case of** compensation for **damage.** The Gemara challenges: **This** verse is **necessary for** teaching a different *halakha*, **that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught. As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** When the verse states: **"*Verappo yerappe*," from here** it is derived **that permission is granted to a doctor to heal.** The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse write: *Verofeh yerappe*,** meaning: And a doctor shall heal. **Learn from** the phrase employed that the one who caused the injury is liable **to give** monetary compensation for **medical costs in a case of damage.** The Gemara challenges: **But** it is **still necessary for** the *halakhot* **that we stated** previously, i.e., it was necessary **for the verse to repeat** the language **of healing,** as the Gemara explained. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse say** one of the two words twice, **either: *Rappo rappo*, or: *Yerappe yerappe*. What** is taught by repeating the language with a change: **"*Verappo yerappe*"? Learn from** this wording that the one who caused the injury is liable **to give** monetary compensation for **medical costs in a case of damage.**

Rav Pappa in the name of Rava derives the *ripuy* obligation from *verapo yerape*: *litein refuah bimkom nezek* — one must pay medical expenses even when *nezek* is also being paid. Again, one might object that this *pasuk* is needed for the teaching of *nitan reshut lerofei lerapot*. The *gemara* resolves this through analysis of the doubled verb: had the *Torah* used a consistent single form (*rapo rapo* or *yerape yerape*), one teaching would have been sufficient. The switch in form (*rapo yerape*) itself indicates an additional teaching — namely, *litein refuah bimkom nezek*. The redundancy accommodates both *drashot* simultaneously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְלָל דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק. שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that compensation for pain, medical costs, loss of livelihood, and humiliation are paid even where the one who caused the injury pays compensation for damage. The Gemara notes: **By inference,** it must be **that you find these** examples of cases where the one who caused an injury must pay compensation for these indemnities **that are not in a case of damage.** The Gemara asks: **How** would **you find** examples of **these** cases **that are not in a case of damage?**

The *gemara* then observes that if *ripuy* is paid *bimkom nezek*, this implies that *ripuy* can also apply *shelo bimkom nezek* — in cases without permanent damage.

### Tza'ar Ripuy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צַעַר – כִּדְקָתָנֵי: צַעַר – כְּווֹאוֹ בְּשַׁפּוּד אוֹ בְּמַסְמֵר, וַאֲפִילּוּ עַל צִפּוֹרְנוֹ מְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה חַבּוּרָה. רִיפּוּי – דַּהֲוָה כָּאֵיב לֵיהּ מִידֵּי, וּסְלֵיק וְאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ סַמָּא חֲרִיפָא, וְאַחְוְורֵיהּ לְבִישְׂרֵיהּ; דִּצְרִיךְ לְאוֹתֹבֵי לֵיהּ סַמָּא לְאַנְקוֹטֵיהּ גַּוְונָא דְּבִישְׂרֵיהּ. שֶׁבֶת – דְּהַדְּקֵיהּ בְּאִינְדְּרוֹנָא, וּבַטְּלֵיהּ. בּוֹשֶׁת – דְּרַק לֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: An example of a case where one must pay compensation for **pain** alone is **as that** which the mishna **teaches** explicitly: How is payment for **pain** assessed? If **he burned** another **with a skewer or with a** hot **nail,** or even if he burned him **on his fingernail,** which is **a place where he does not cause a bruise,** i.e., there was no personal injury, only pain, the court evaluates the monetary value of the pain. An example of a case where one must pay compensation for **medical costs** alone is **where some** body part **was hurting and** another **came and brought him a strong drug, and it whitened his flesh;** the *halakha* is **that** the second person **must give him** another **drug to restore the color to his flesh,** even though he caused no damage, i.e., loss of function. An example of a case where one must pay compensation for **loss of livelihood** alone is a case of one **who locked** another **in a room [*be'inderona*] and forced him to be idle,** but did not cause him any personal injury. An example of a case where one must pay compensation for **humiliation** alone is a case of one **who spit in the face of** another, without causing any personal injury.

The case of *tza'ar* without *nezek* is the paradigm: *kavo b'shipud u'bamasmer, afilu al tziporno, makom she'eino oseh chavurah* — one who burns another with a spit or nail even on the fingernail, in a place that does not create a lasting wound. Even here, *ripuy* is required — *d'hava ka ka'iv lei* — because the person is experiencing pain that requires treatment. Specifically, a sharp-acting ointment must be applied, which itself may bleach or discolor the skin, requiring a subsequent medicine to restore the skin's normal color. This sequence of treatments constitutes *ripuy* even though no *nezek* results.

Similarly, *shevet* can arise without any other component: *d'hadkeia b'adorna u'vtatlei* — if one locked another in a room and thereby caused that person to miss work, without any physical injury, that constitutes *shevet* in isolation. And *boshet* can arise independently as well: *d'rak lei b'apei* — if one spits in another's face, that is *boshet* without *tza'ar*, *nezek*, *shevet*, or *ripuy*.

## Shevet Standard

### Shomer Kishu'in

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבֶת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא שׁוֹמֵר קִישּׁוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: How is payment for **loss of livelihood** assessed? The court **views** the injured party **as though he were a watchman of cucumbers.**

When assessing *shevet* — compensation for time off work — the *gemara* establishes a specific benchmark: *shevet ro'in oto k'ilu hu shomer kishu'in* — we assess the injured party as if he were a watchman over a field of squash. This functions as a kind of halachic minimum wage for the *shevet* calculation.

### Low Standard Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שֶׁבֶת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא שׁוֹמֵר קִישּׁוּאִין. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: לָקְתָה מִדַּת הַדִּין; דְּכִי מִיתְּפַח הַאי גַּבְרָא, לָאו אַגְרָא דְּשׁוֹמֵר קִישּׁוּאִין הוּא שָׁקֵיל, אֶלָּא דָּלֵי דַּוְולָא וְשָׁקֵיל אַגְרָא; אִי נָמֵי אָזֵיל בִּשְׁלִיח[וּתָא] וְשָׁקֵיל אַגְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:2): How is payment for **loss of livelihood** assessed? The court **views** the injured party **as though he were a watchman of cucumbers. And if you say:** But **the standard of justice was compromised.** The Gemara interjects to clarify this statement of the *baraita*: It appears to be compromised, **as when this man will recover, he** will **not collect the wage of a watchman of cucumbers,** but **rather** he will **carry buckets and collect** a higher **wage, or** he will **travel as an agent and collect** an even higher **wage.** The assessment of loss of livelihood should also account for one of these types of labor.

This standard prompts an immediate challenge: *v'im tomar lakta midat hadin* — is this really fair? After all, *d'chi mifach hai gavra lav agar d'shomer kishu'in hu* — when this person recovers, he will not be going back to work as a squash watchman. He will be drawing water for wages, working as a messenger, or performing other labor at a higher rate. Why should such a minimal standard be applied?

### Nezek Covers Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדַּת הַדִּין לֹא לָקְתָה, שֶׁכְּבָר נָתַן לוֹ דְּמֵי יָדוֹ וּדְמֵי רַגְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to quoting the *baraita*: **The standard of justice was not compromised, because** the one who caused him injury **already gave him the value of his hand or the value of his leg,** and that compensation took into account the professional opportunities he lost due to the injury.

The *gemara* responds: *middat hadin lo lakta* — justice is not compromised here, *shekvar natan lo dmei yado udmei raglo* — because the injuring party has already paid for the hand or foot. The *nezek* payment reflects the diminution in the injured party's slave-market value — which itself is a function of the person's long-term earning capacity. The *nezek* compensation therefore already accounts for the damaged earning potential over the lifetime of the injury. The only remaining question for *shevet* is the bare minimum that a person could be earning during the recovery period as compared to nothing at all. Since a high threshold would amount to double-counting what has already been compensated through *nezek*, the *mishnah* appropriately sets the *shevet* standard at the lowest meaningful level of economic activity. This connects to the broader principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hareaya*: since *shevet* is extracted from another party, a high burden of proof and a conservative assessment are halachically appropriate.

### Injury Type Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קִטַּע אֶת יָדוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי יָדוֹ, וְשֶׁבֶת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא שׁוֹמֵר קִישּׁוּאִין. שִׁיבֵּר אֶת רַגְלוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי רַגְלוֹ, וְשֶׁבֶת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא שׁוֹמֵר אֶת הַפֶּתַח. סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי עֵינוֹ, וְשֶׁבֶת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא מַטְחִינוֹ בְּרֵיחַיִם. חֵירְשׁוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי כוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If one **severed the hand of** another, **he gives him the value of his hand** as compensation for damage, **and** for **loss of livelihood** the court **views** the injured party **as though he were a watchman of cucumbers.** If one **broke the leg of** another, **he gives him the value of his leg** as compensation for damage. **And** for **loss of livelihood** the court **views** the injured party **as though he were a watchman at the opening** to a courtyard, because he cannot work even as a watchman of cucumbers, as that requires walking around the cucumber field. Rava continues: If one **blinded the eye of** another, **he gives him the** value **of his eye** as compensation for damage; **and** as for **loss of livelihood,** the court **views** the injured party **as though** the one who caused the injury **caused him to grind with a mill,** as even a blind person can do this. If one **deafened** another, **he gives him his entire value** as compensation for damage, because one lacking the ability to hear is not fit for any form of employment.

Rava introduces a differentiated approach: the *shevet* benchmark is not uniform across all injuries but is calibrated to the type of incapacity produced by the specific injury. *Keta yado* — if one cut off another's hand — *shevet ro'in oto k'ilu hu shomer kishu'in*, assessed as a squash watchman: the person can still walk and function in a supervisory capacity, even if manual labor is limited. *Shiber et raglo* — if one broke another's foot — *shevet ro'in oto k'ilu hu shomer hapetach*, assessed as a doorkeeper or gate guardian: the person is limited to a sedentary, stationary role. *Sima et eino* — if one blinded another's eye — *shevet ro'in oto k'ilu hu matchino bereichayim*, assessed as a millstone grinder: a job that can be performed without sight.

### Cheresh Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵירְשׁוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי כוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If one **deafened** another, **he gives him his entire value** as compensation for damage, because one lacking the ability to hear is not fit for any form of employment.

The case of the *cheresh* — a deaf person — stands apart. *Noten lo dmei kulo* — the injuring party pays the injured party's full slave value. The rabbinic understanding of a person who is deaf assumed total incapacity: the *cheresh* was understood to have no mental competence and to be entirely unable to function in society, communicate, or work. Therefore the *nezek* reflects total incapacitation, and there is no *shevet* to assess separately, since the person has no working value against which to calculate a differential.

### Modern Cheresh

This understanding has been fundamentally revised in contemporary times. The development of sign language, the emergence of a rich textual and visual culture, and a deeper appreciation of cognitive capacity have demonstrated that deaf individuals possess full mental competence and can participate fully in society. The category of *cheresh* in the *gemara*'s sense — a person entirely unable to communicate or function — would today more accurately correspond to cases of severe traumatic brain injury or similar conditions causing total incapacitation. The *gemara*'s framework should thus be understood as addressing a functional category defined by total cognitive and social incapacity, not by deafness in the modern clinical sense.
