# Nazir 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maot Stumot Rule

### Intro: Money to Olah

The sugya on 26b opens with a ruling transmitted in the name of Rav Huna citing Rav: *lo shanu ela maot* — this principle applies only to money. The principle in question is the rule of *maot stumot*: if a *nazir* set aside an unspecified pile of money intended for his *chatat*, *olah*, and *shelamim*, and then died or had his *nezirut* annulled, that money is used entirely toward an *olah*. This is what we have been discussing. But Rav's ruling now introduces a critical limitation: *aval beavehemah, harei hi kimefureshet* — if instead of money it was animals, those animals are treated as if they were already explicitly designated.

### Animals vs Money

The question is: what exactly is the case of animals? If the animals in question were the precise three offerings a *nazir* is required to bring — a male lamb, a female lamb, and a ram — then the ruling would be self-evident. Each animal is inherently self-identifying, and of course each would be brought for its appropriate sacrifice. The case must therefore involve something less obvious.

*Tosafot* works out that the *gemara* is discussing a situation in which someone set aside three cows — animals that are within the category of valid sacrifices but are not themselves the required offerings for this particular *korban nazir*, which must come from sheep. The owner declares that these animals are sanctified for the purpose of his sacrifices, intending that they will be sold, the proceeds redeemed, and the money then used to purchase the required animals. The question is why this situation is treated differently from an unspecified pile of money.

### Tosafot: Cow Kedushah

*Tosafot*'s analysis turns on a careful distinction regarding *kedushah* and the ability to transfer it. To appreciate the problem, consider three scenarios. First: if one sanctifies a cow for *bedek habayit* — for the upkeep and operating expenses of the *Beit Hamikdash* — it may be sold immediately, just as one might donate a car or any object of value. Second: if one sanctifies a cow to be brought itself as an *olah*, then that *kedushah* cannot be transferred and the cow cannot be sold until it develops a *mum* — a blemish — since only the onset of a blemish can release it from the sanctity that had designated it as the animal itself to be offered.

The interesting middle case — the one *Tosafot* grapples with — is where one sanctifies a cow with the *kedushah* of an *olah*, but not because the cow itself will be brought as that *olah*. Rather, since the *nazir*'s *olah* must be a sheep, the cow is being sanctified solely as a funding mechanism: sell the cow, take the proceeds, buy the sheep. One might have thought that because the cow itself was never going to be brought, the *kedushah* could simply be transferred through a sale without waiting for a *mum*. But *Tosafot* concludes, drawing on the logic of this very *sugya*, that the answer is otherwise. Since the cow belongs to the world of valid *korbanot* — since in theory a cow could be brought as an *olah*, even though that is not the *kedushah* designated here — the *kedushah* clings to it with full force, and cannot be removed without a *mum*. By contrast, if one were to sanctify a chicken — which is not a valid candidate for an *olah* of an animal — that could simply be sold outright without waiting for any blemish, because the chicken does not inhabit the world of animal *korbanot* and therefore the *kedushah* does not attach to it in the same way.

This conclusion has direct implications for our *halakha*. The question of whether animals are treated as *mefureshet* or as *maot stumot* depends heavily on whether they can be freely liquidated, which in turn depends on whether a *mum* is required before sale.

### Rav Nachman: Tmimah

The *gemara* now provides the ruling of Rav Nachman, which clarifies the scope of Rav's statement: *ha de-amrinan be-behemah harei hi ke-mefureshet — lo shanu ela bitmimah*. When we say that animals are treated as explicitly designated, that applies only to an unblemished animal, a *tmimah*. Such animals, because of the *kedushah* problem just described, cannot be sold until they develop a *mum*, and this waiting period gives them a different legal character. *Aval ba'alat mum* — but if the animal already has a blemish — *harei hi kistumah* — it is treated like unspecified funds, like *maot stumot*.

This raises the obvious question: why should the presence or absence of a *mum* matter to our *halakha* about whether the funds ultimately go toward an *olah*? Even animals without a *mum* will eventually develop one, or be sold, and the proceeds will be divided among the required *korbanot*. Why should the ease or difficulty of liquidation determine whether the pile counts as specified or unspecified?

### Mezuman Definition

To answer this, the *gemara* offers two approaches. The first is formal: the *halakha* of *maot stumot* is a *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, and it was transmitted specifically regarding money — *maot* — not regarding other things. If one has non-cash items with *kedushah* mixed in — including potential *chatat* funds — and the case does not fall within the ambit of this *halakha*, then the proper resolution is to cast the money into the *Yam HaMelach*, because the *chatat* component cannot be used and the entire pile must be destroyed. On this formal approach, the *halakha* simply was not said about anything other than cash, and that is the end of the matter.

But then what is the line between cash and not cash? Here the concept of *mezuman* becomes operative. The Hebrew word for cash in modern parlance is *mezuman* — literally, "something ready." The underlying idea is that something readily and immediately convertible to money, with minimal delay or friction, shares the legal character of money. An animal with a *mum* has no *kedushah* impediment to its sale — one simply brings it to market and sells it. That is cash. An unblemished animal cannot be sold until it develops a blemish; it must sit, grazing, for an indeterminate period. That is not cash. Similarly, a *niska* — a bar of precious metal — was regarded by some as sufficiently difficult to sell at the right price that it too falls outside the *mezuman* category, while others, as Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak rules, disagree and consider even metal bars to be cash-equivalent. A *shura d'korei* — a pile of lumber or beams — is clearly not cash, since finding a buyer willing to inspect and purchase raw timber requires locating someone building a house and negotiating terms.

The second approach offers a logic beyond the formal rule. When one has three unblemished cows that must wait for a *mum* before they can be sold, one may, over time, mentally begin to pre-designate: "Bessie will go toward the *olah*, that other cow toward the *chatat*." Even if this mental designation was never verbalized, the extended waiting period creates the conditions for this kind of private assignment, and the pile accordingly begins to take on the character of *mefureshet* — as if it were already explicitly divided. This is, admittedly, a somewhat rabbinic-sounding concern, and *Tosafot* grapples with whether an unspoken mental designation can actually alter the legal status of the pile. But as a framework for understanding the distinction, it at least provides some logic, even if the cleaner approach remains the formal rule: the *halakha* was said only about *mezuman*, about things that are readily liquidated, and an animal without a *mum* simply does not qualify.

## Halakha Scope Debate

### Shimi: Metal & Wood

Rav Shimi bar Ashi poses a direct question to Rav Papa: *mai tama d'Rabbanan d'amri d'maot v'lo behema v'lo naska* — what is the reason that the Rabbis said this *halakha* applies to money and not to animals, not to metal bars, *v'lo shura* — and not to a pile of beams? He has just been told that the rule is formally limited to *maot* — cash or cash-like items — and he is challenging the basis of that limitation.

### Birds Case

But Rav Shimi presses further with what turns out to be the most interesting extension of this question: *maot v'lo ofot* — money, and not birds? To understand what he means, one must recall a distinct *halakha* involving *kinei yoledes* — the two birds that a woman who has given birth must bring, one as an *olah* and one as a *chatat*. There, the *halakha* is that she need not specify at the time of purchase which bird is which; the specification can be made either by the owner at the time of purchase or by the *kohen* at the time of bringing. What would happen, then, if such a woman purchased two birds without specifying, and then died before either stage of specification occurred?

One would naturally assume that since one bird is an *olah* and one is a *chatat*, the *kohen* should simply bring whichever one he designates as the *olah* and let the *chatat* die — since a *chatat* cannot be brought on behalf of someone who has died. But the question arises: should we treat those two unspecified birds the way we treat *maot stumot*? The birds have the same problem — there is a *chatat* and an *olah* mixed together in an unspecified pile, the owner has died, and perhaps we should simply bring both as an *olah*, just as we do with unspecified money. On the other hand, given that the *gemara* has been consistently narrowing the scope of the *maot stumot* rule — excluding animals, excluding metal bars, excluding lumber — perhaps birds too fall outside its ambit, and in that case both birds could not be brought as an *olah*.

This is the tension Rav Shimi is articulating. The initial assumption of the *gemara* is that these birds, having not yet been specified — neither by the owner at purchase nor by the *kohen* at the time of the offering — retain the status of *stumim*, unspecified. And if they are *stumim*, and the *halakha* of *maot stumot* applies broadly, then both should go toward an *olah*. But given how sharply the *gemara* has been limiting the rule, maybe not.

*Tosafot* here raises three possible readings of this passage, and the one that emerges as most coherent is the following: the *gemara* is not merely asking a hypothetical about whether the rule should apply to birds, but rather grounding its reasoning in the language Rav Chisda used — *mitparshot* — which will now become the pivot of the discussion.

### Rav Chisda: Birds

The *gemara* invokes the teaching of Rav Chisda: *ein hakinim mitparshot ela o belakichat bealim o ba'asiyat hakohen* — birds are not explicated, are not rendered *mefureshet*, except either at the moment the owner purchases them or at the moment the *kohen* performs the offering. The word Rav Chisda chose is significant. He did not say that the birds' identity is not *established* or not *fixed* — he said they are not *mitparshot*, not rendered explicit. And *mitparshot* is the precise opposite of *satum*. By using this language, Rav Chisda implicitly assigned the birds the status of *stumim* whenever neither the owner nor the *kohen* has yet acted. If the woman dies before either stage, the birds remain *satum*.

And if their status is *satum*, what follows? The *gemara* reads Rav Chisda's language as implying that such unspecified birds follow the *halakha* of *maot stumot* — if the owner dies, they are both brought as an *olah*, as a *nedava*. But then Rav Shimi's question bites: you have just been told the rule only applies to money, not to animals, not to metal, not to lumber. So why should birds be different? Why would the *halakha* of *maot stumot* apply here?

The *gemara* responds: *v'litameich* — according to your counter-reasoning, that we should not limit the rule to money at all and should apply it across the board to anything unspecified. If that is so, there is an immediate problem from the *Mishnah* cited by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *heivi shalosh behemot velo peireish* — if one brought three animals without specifying which is which, and those three animals happen to be the precise animals required — a male lamb, a female lamb, and a ram — then *harauya lechatas tikarev chatas, le-olah tikarev olah, ulishlamim tikarev shelamim* — each is brought for its appropriate sacrifice. The *kedushah* is apportioned correctly.

### Objection Logic

But if one wishes to apply the *maot stumot* rule to everything that is unspecified, one should be troubled by this ruling. The *gemara* says these animals were not specified — *behemah lav kimeforeshet dami* — animals are not like *mefureshet*. If animals truly share the status of *stumim*, then even these three animals — the exact right animals for the sacrifice — should follow the rule of *stumim* and we should bring them all as an *olah*. Why instead does each animal go to its appropriate sacrifice? This seems to directly contradict the position that *stumim* applies broadly to all unspecified items regardless of their form.

### Response: Gender

The response is sharp — almost dismissive. *Hacha nekeiva, hacha zachar* — here, the *chatat* is female and the *olah* is male. These animals are not unspecified in any meaningful sense. A male sheep and a female sheep are self-identifying. Of course they cannot have the status of *stumim* — it is perfectly clear which is which. The question was, frankly, absurd. *Stumim* describes a situation where it genuinely cannot be determined which item belongs to which category. If you have the exact right animals and their sex alone tells you which goes where, there is nothing unspecified about the situation. It is as though one had three piles of money with signs reading *olah*, *chatat*, and *shelamim* — that is not *stumim* at all.

So the broader debate remains: is the *maot stumot* rule formally limited to money and cash-equivalent items, or does it apply broadly to any genuinely unspecified constellation of *kodesh* items? The case of birds sharpens this question because birds present a genuine case of *stumim* — identical in appearance, capable of being brought as either *olah* or *chatat*, and unspecified until the act of the owner or *kohen*. If the rule is broad, both birds go toward an *olah* when the owner dies. If the rule is narrow and limited to money, the birds must be handled differently.

## Braita: Son & Father

### Setup: Father Dies

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* found in full on 30b, which the text here cites in abbreviated form. The *braita* poses the question: *keitzad amru ish megaleiach al nezirus aviv* — when does a person get to shave — meaning, when does a son get to use his deceased father's money to bring the *korbanot* for his own *nezirut*? The *braita* sets up the scenario: a father was a *nazir*, set aside unspecified money for his *nezirut*, and then died. Without the son's involvement, that money would simply go toward an *olah*, as we have established. The question is whether the son can step in and use that money for his own *korbanot* instead.

### Case 1: After Death

The first case: *v'amar hareini nazir al m'nas she'agaleach al ma'os abba* — the son declares himself a *nazir* on condition that he gets to use his father's money to bring his *korbanot*. This declaration was made after his father died. In this case, the son succeeds. The money was standing there, its original purpose no longer achievable since the father had died, and its ultimate destination had not yet been determined. The son stepped in at precisely the right moment, accepted *nezirut* explicitly tied to the use of that money, and is therefore entitled to use it. *Zehu she-megaleiach al ma'os aviv* — this is the case where the son actually does bring his *korbanot* with his father's money.

### Case 2: Simultaneous

The second case: *aval mi shehaya hu v'aviv nezirim* — he and his father were both *nezirim* at the same time, meaning the son had already accepted *nezirut* while his father was still alive. The father subsequently set aside money for his *nezirut* and then died. The son now says he wants to use that money for his own *korbanot*. *Harei elu yiplu l'nedavah* — in this case, he cannot do so. The money falls to *nedava*, following the standard rule of *maot stumot*. The son cannot claim it because he was already a *nazir* before his father died; there was no moment where the money was available and his *nezirut* was formed with the intent to use it.

### Specified Animals

The *braita* then addresses the parallel case involving animals. *Haysa lo behemah mefureshet* — if the father had specifically designated particular animals — *chatat tamus* — the *chatat* animal dies since it cannot be brought, *olah tikarev olah* — the *olah* is brought as an *olah*, *shelamim yikrevu shelamim* — the *shelamim* is brought as *shelamim*. Each specified animal follows its designated *kedushah*, and there is nothing unusual about this outcome.

## Mum Status

### Inference: Unspecified M…

The *gemara* now draws an inference from the *braita*. When the *braita* contrasts unspecified money — which goes toward an *olah* as *nedava* — with specified animals — where each goes to its designated sacrifice — the *gemara* asks: *mai lav afilu ba'alat mum* — does this not apply even to animals with a blemish? That is, the *braita* seems to be drawing a contrast between money and all forms of animals. If so, even animals with a *mum*, which we have suggested are cash-equivalent, would still fall on the "animal" side of the ledger and would be treated as *mefureshet* rather than as *maot stumot*. This would undermine what Rav Nachman had ruled earlier.

### Braita Scope

The *gemara* rejects this inference. *Lo, tmimah* — no, the *braita* is speaking of unblemished animals, or more precisely, it is speaking of clearly specified animals, where each animal's identity as *chatat*, *olah*, or *shelamim* was already made explicit by the owner. The *braita*'s contrast is not between money and all animals, but between unspecified money and explicitly designated animals. *Aval ba'alat mum k'stumah dami* — but an animal with a blemish retains the status of *stumim* and is treated like money.

This generates a further question from the *gemara*: if animals with a *mum* are truly cash-equivalent, then why does the *braita* not make this clear? Why contrast unspecified money with specified animals, when there is an intermediate category — unspecified blemished animals — that behaves just like money? Why not illustrate the *halakha* using that intermediate case?

### Mum as Money

The *gemara* responds: *hachi nami* — in fact, animals with a *mum* are simply included in the category of money. *Ba'alat mum l'mai kadeshah? Lidmei* — when an animal with a blemish is sanctified, what is its sanctity directed toward? To its monetary proceeds. It will be sold, and the *kedushah* will transfer to the money received. *D'mei haynu maot* — proceeds are money. When the *braita* says "money," it means anything whose *kedushah* operates through its monetary value, including blemished animals. The *braita* was not being incomplete; it was using "money" in a sense broad enough to encompass everything that is sold for its value and whose *kedushah* transfers to the proceeds.

### Final Principle

The *gemara* thus arrives at its final principle. The *halakha* of *maot stumot* — that an unspecified pile of funds intended for a *nazir*'s *korbanot* goes toward an *olah* when the owner dies — applies to anything whose *kedushah* operates through its monetary value: actual coins, blemished animals, and according to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, even metal bars. It does not apply to unblemished animals that cannot be sold until they develop a *mum*, and it does not apply to things like lumber that require significant effort to liquidate. The line is *mezuman* — readiness, liquidity, the practical ability to convert immediately into cash. Anything on the *mezuman* side of that line follows the *halakha* of *maot stumot*; anything that requires waiting or exceptional circumstances to convert falls outside it and is treated as *mefureshet*, as already designated, because the extended holding period creates the conditions for mental pre-designation or simply because the formal *halakha* was never transmitted for such cases in the first place.
