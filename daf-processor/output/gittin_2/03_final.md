# Gittin 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Gittin

### Masechet Overview

We begin a new *masechet*: Masechet Gittin. It is, in the fullest sense of the phrase, a classic *masechet* of the *gemara*. Unlike some of the more unusual tractates encountered elsewhere in the cycle — with non-standard language, absent *Rashi*, or unconventional structure — Gittin belongs to the heart of the *Shas* curriculum. Situated within *Nashim* and adjacent in spirit to *Nezikin*, it carries the full wealth of the *rishonim* and has been a staple of *yeshiva* education for centuries. And the reason it has remained so central is not merely because it is *halacha l'maaseh* — though of course the laws of delivering a *get* are of great practical consequence — but because it is extraordinarily rich in conceptual depth.

### Conceptual Themes

Masechet Gittin is packed with some of the deepest and most far-reaching themes in all of *Shas*. The laws of *shlichus* — agency — are explored here at length and in their most complex forms. So too are the laws of *shtarot*, legal documents, as well as questions of ownership, and the structure of conditions placed upon transactions. These are not parochial concerns; they are recurring themes throughout *Shas*, and Gittin becomes one of the primary sources to which the tradition returns again and again when working through them. It is a *masechet* with enormous conceptual reach.

### Divorce Context

There is, however, something almost melancholy about learning Masechet Gittin in the contemporary context. The *gemara*, like any legal document, focuses entirely on process: how does one write a *get*, how does one deliver it, what are the technical and conceptual requirements? And throughout, the social reality is simply taken for granted — of course a husband is giving a *get* to his wife. The complications are halachic, not social. Every page assumes that divorce is a natural and available option, and the legal analysis proceeds accordingly.

This stands in sharp contrast to the reality many Jewish women face today, where the process of obtaining a *get* is fraught with the possibility of extortion, refusal, and the tragedy of *iguna*. Part of what made the system more functional in Talmudic times was the absence of a parallel secular legal system: if you wanted to end a marriage, this was the only path. Just as today one cannot avoid obtaining a civil divorce if one wants legal dissolution, in that world a *get* was the sole mechanism. The tight-knit Jewish community, combined with a *beis din* that had real authority and the motivation to exercise it, created conditions in which the husband's cooperation was far more reliably obtained. That said, it is worth noting that in much of Western Europe and America, secular divorce was itself essentially unavailable until well into the twentieth century, owing in large part to Christian legal traditions. The contrast with the Talmudic system is therefore even more pointed.

### Perakim Structure

Masechet Gittin spans six *perakim* and does something characteristic of several *masechtot*: rather than beginning with the most fundamental questions — how does one write a *get*, what constitutes a valid *get* — it opens with what might appear to be a more peripheral topic. The first *perek* deals with *hameivi get mimedinat hayam*, the agent who delivers a *get* from overseas, and the testimony he must provide. The second *perek* continues this theme under the same heading, still concerned with the delivery of a *get* from afar — but midway through, it begins to address the specifics of how a *get* is written. We encounter *hakol kotvin*, the principle that one may write with all types of inks; the question of a *get* written by day and signed at night; what material the *get* may be written upon; and the rule that *ein kotvin bimchubar lakarka* — one may not write on something still attached to the ground.

It is also in this section that we encounter *lishmah* — the requirement that the *get* be written with the particular woman in mind. The third *perek* continues with questions relating both to the writing and delivery of the *get*, including the fascinating question of whether, while the *get* is being delivered, we may assume the husband who sent it is still alive. This opens into a broader digression: in general, when a *shaliach* has been dispatched to perform an act, what assumptions may he make about the one who sent him?

The fourth *perek*, called *ha-sholeach*, deals with a scenario of acute halachic danger: a husband who dispatches a *shaliach* to deliver a *get* to his wife, then subsequently cancels the agency — but does so not in the presence of the messenger. The messenger proceeds in good faith, the wife believes herself divorced, she remarries, and the children of that remarriage may be *mamzerim*. To prevent this catastrophe, the Rabbis intervened — and here we encounter one of the most celebrated concepts in the *masechet*: *tikkun haolam*. The Rabbis instituted certain enactments, and in some cases shaped or worked around biblical law, for the betterment of society. The fourth and fifth *perakim* are largely devoted to this theme — a wide-ranging survey, touching areas from *nashim* to *nezikin*, of things the Rabbis did *mipnei tikkun haolam*.

The sixth *perek* returns to the laws of giving a *get*, including the case of a *get* given conditionally — a topic that might sound like a technical curiosity, but which unfolds into a profound analysis of what constitutes a legally valid *tnai*. Toward the end of the *masechet* we reach the laws of how the woman receives the *get* and what constitutes valid receipt. And at the very end, in the final *mishnah*, we arrive at what might surprise a reader who has followed the tractate from the beginning: a discussion of under what circumstances a husband should divorce his wife in the first place. It is a rich and diverse *masechet* in every sense.

### Rambam's 10 Conditions

Before turning to the *daf* itself — which is notably short, with very few lines of *gemara* — it is worth pausing to appreciate a brilliant piece of systematic jurisprudence from the Rambam. His *Hilchos Gerushin*, particularly *perek* 1, offers a stunning overview of the entire subject. He opens by enumerating ten conditions, all *d'oraisa*, that must be met for a *get* to be valid. He then reveals the source: virtually all of them are derived from a single *pasuk* in the Torah, specifically from eight words.

The verse is from Devarim: *ve-kasav la sefer kerisus ve-nasan beyada ve-shilcha mibeiso* — "and he shall write for her a document of severance, and place it in her hand, and send her from his house." These eight words, combined with the general principle from *al pi shenayim eidim yakum davar*, underpin the entire architecture of *geirushin* from the Torah's perspective.

### Conditions Explained

The Rambam proceeds to derive each condition in turn. First, from *im lo timtza chen be'einav* — "if she does not find favor in his eyes" — we learn that the husband must act willingly: *melamed she-eino megaresh ela birtzono*. This distinguishes *gerushin* from ordinary transactions, where of course intent is always required; here the requirement of genuine consent carries particular weight. Importantly, the woman is divorced even against her will — *ha-isha mitgereshet birtzonah ve-she-lo birtzonah* — which is of course the pre-Rabbinic baseline, before the *cherem* of Rabbeinu Gershom.

Second, from *ve-kasav* — and he shall write — we learn that the *get* must be a written document: *eina mitgereshet ela bichtav*. Third, from *la* — for her — we derive *lishmah*: it must be written specifically with this woman in mind. A generic writ or one written without intention toward the particular woman does not suffice. Fourth, from *sefer kerisus* — a document of severance — we learn that the *get* must fully sever the relationship: *davar ha-koret beino u-veinah, she-lo yisha'er alav rashut*. If the husband appends an ongoing stipulation — some condition that leaves him with continued authority over her — the act is not one of complete severance. Fifth, from *ve-nasan beyada* — and he shall place it in her hand — we learn the obvious: the *get* must reach her, whether into her hand, into the hands of her *shaliach*, or into her property.

Sixth, from *ve-shilcha* — and he shall send her — the Rambam derives that the language of the *get* must be that *she* is being sent, not that *he* is leaving. The husband cannot formulate the *get* in terms of his own departure; the document must speak of her being divorced from him. Nor can the text be merely a declaration of freedom without reference to the act of divorce itself.

Seventh — and here the Rambam draws on the combination of *ve-kasav* and *ve-nasan* together — there must be no necessary intervening act between the writing and the giving. One may of course allow time to elapse, but there cannot be a situation in which some action is *required* between the writing and the delivery. The classic example is *mechubar lakarka*: one cannot write on a tree still attached to the ground, because the only way to give it would necessitate cutting the tree down first.

Eighth, from the combination of *sefer kerisus ve-natan beyada*, the Rambam derives that the *get* must be given explicitly as a *get*. One cannot hand a woman a piece of paper under false pretenses — "hold this for me" — and have that count as delivery of a *get*. It must be given as what it is.

The ninth condition — that there must be *eidim*, witnesses — is the one condition not derived from this *pasuk*, but rather from the general Torah principle of *al pi shenayim eidim yakum davar*. Whether this means witnesses must sign the document or witnesses must be present at the time of delivery is itself a dispute in the *gemara*. Tenth — and the Rambam closes the list here — the *get* must come from the husband himself, not from a court acting independently.

The elegance of this framework is remarkable: eight words of Torah, one additional *pasuk* about testimony, and the Rambam has mapped the entire *d'oraisa* architecture of *geirushin*. It is an ideal introduction to the *masechet*, and it is worth reading his *perek* 1 of *Hilchos Gerushin* in full before proceeding through the *daf yomi*.

## Hameivi Get

### Basic Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: An agent **who brings a bill of divorce [*get*]** from a husband to his wife **from a country overseas,** i.e., from outside of Eretz Yisrael to Eretz Yisrael, **is required to state** the following formula when he hands over the bill of divorce: This bill of divorce **was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence.**

The *mishnah* opens: *hamevi get mimedinat hayam* — one who brings a *get* from overseas. *Tosafos* immediately raises a point about this phrase. *Rashi* understands *medinat hayam* to mean simply *kol chutz la'aretz* — anywhere outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. *Tosafos* disagrees, arguing that if that were the intent, the *mishnah* would have said *hamevi get michutz la'aretz*. The use of the phrase *medinat hayam* suggests something more distant — a genuinely far-off place — and we will return to this distinction shortly.

The *mishnah* states: *tzarich she-yomar befanai nechtav uvfanai nechtam* — the agent delivering the *get* must declare, in front of witnesses or a *beis din*, "it was written in my presence and signed in my presence." The most natural question is: why? We have a *shaliach* standing before us, holding a *get*. We do not know who he is. We do not know who sent him. We have no way of verifying that the husband actually authorized this document, that the signatures on it are genuine, or that the *get* was written in accordance with *halacha*. The husband is not standing in front of us; the *shaliach* has not provided any prior affidavit of his agency. In that situation, the *shaliach* must testify — and his testimony that the *get* was written and signed in his presence serves as validation of the entire process. The *gemara* will go on to distinguish between the *nechtav* component and the *nechtam* component, attributing to each a different rationale. But at the *mishnah* level, both together constitute the required declaration.

### Geographic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמֵּבִיא מִן הָרְקָם וּמִן הַחֶגֶר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ מִכְּפַר לוּדִּים לְלוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel says: Even one who brings** a bill of divorce **from Rekem or from Ḥeger,** which are on the periphery of Eretz Yisrael, must make this declaration. **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** one who brings a bill of divorce **from the village of Ludim to Lod** must also make this declaration, despite the fact that these places are only a short distance apart. The reason is that the village of Ludim was not part of the main area settled by Jews in Eretz Yisrael.

The *mishnah* then records a series of positions among the *tannaim* about what geographic distance triggers this requirement. *Rabban Gamliel* says: *af hamevi mi-Rekem umehager* — even one who brings from Rekem and Hager must make this declaration. These appear to be places very close to the border of *Eretz Yisrael*. *Tosafos* devotes considerable effort to identifying them, suggesting they may be in the region of the land of the *Plishtim*, though acknowledges some ambiguity. *Rashi* associates them with the biblical Kadesh and Bered, the location to which Hagar fled when driven out by Sarah — again pointing to proximity to *Eretz Yisrael*. *Rabban Gamliel*'s position, then, is that even these nearby locations trigger the requirement; one need not travel to genuinely distant overseas territories.

*Rabbi Eliezer* goes further: *afilu mikfar Ludim le-Lud* — even one who brings from the village of Ludim to Lud. This generates two puzzles. First, Lud is not on the border of *Eretz Yisrael* in any straightforward sense; why would its immediate vicinity even be considered *chutz la'aretz*? Second, and more textually puzzling, *Tosafos* asks: why does *Rabbi Eliezer* state where he is *bringing it to*? The point of these *tannaitic* positions is to define the place of *origin* — it doesn't matter where it is being delivered. *Tosafos* offers two resolutions. One is that *kfar Ludim* must be understood as so closely adjacent to Lud, so much like its suburb, that one might have assumed the two localities share a legal identity for these purposes — *Rabbi Eliezer* teaches that even so, if it technically falls over the line, the requirement applies. The other answer *Tosafos* offers is that *Rabbi Eliezer* himself was from Lud, and therefore chose this as his illustrative example.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״, אֶלָּא הַמֵּבִיא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וְהַמּוֹלִיךְ. וְהַמֵּבִיא מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ מֵהֶגְמוֹנְיָא לְהֶגְמוֹנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say** that **one is required to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, only** if he **brings** a bill of divorce **from a country overseas** to Eretz Yisrael, **and** the same applies to **one who delivers** a bill of divorce from Eretz Yisrael to a country overseas. **And** likewise an agent **who brings** a bill of divorce **from** one **region to** another **region within** the **overseas countries is** also **required to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** This *halakha* applies not only to an agent who brings a bill of divorce from one country to another, but **even** to one who takes it **from** one **district [*hegmonya*] to** another **district** in the same country.

What all three positions — *Rabban Gamliel*, *Rabbi Eliezer*, and the following *chachamim* — share is the underlying claim: the *mishnah*'s phrase *medinat hayam* need not refer to genuinely distant overseas territory. Even proximity to the border triggers the requirement.

*Ve-chachamim omrim*: the *chachamim* disagree, saying *ela hameivi mimedinat hayam* — only one who brings from genuinely overseas must make the declaration. The phrase *medinat hayam* means what it says: a distant place across the sea.

### Eretz Yisrael Borders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵרְקָם לַמִּזְרָח, וּרְקָם – כַּמִּזְרָח; מֵאַשְׁקְלוֹן לַדָּרוֹם וְאַשְׁקְלוֹן – כַּדָּרוֹם; מֵעַכּוֹ לַצָּפוֹן, וְעַכּוֹ – כַּצָּפוֹן. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: עַכּוֹ – כְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְגִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to the borders of Eretz Yisrael, **from Rekem eastward** is considered to be part of the overseas country, **and Rekem** itself **is like east** of Eretz Yisrael, i.e., it is outside of Eretz Yisrael. **From Ashkelon southward** is outside of Eretz Yisrael, **and Ashkelon** itself **is like south** of Eretz Yisrael. Likewise, **from Akko northward** is outside of Eretz Yisrael, **and Akko** itself **is like north** of Eretz Yisrael. **Rabbi Meir says: Akko is like Eretz Yisrael with regard to** the *halakhot* of **bills of divorce.**

The *mishnah* continues with a separate question: how do we define the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* itself? This requires its own treatment, since knowing what is inside *Eretz Yisrael* is necessary to knowing when the requirement applies. *Rabbi Yehuda* provides the coordinates: from Rekem to the east — with Rekem counting as *outside* Israel; from Ashkelon to the south — with Ashkelon counting as outside; from Akko to the north — with Akko counting as outside Israel as well. This is immediately striking: Ashkelon is mentioned as one of the cities of Israel, and Akko is likewise associated with *Eretz Yisrael* in numerous other contexts.

*Rabbi Meir* takes a different position with respect to Akko: *Akko k'Eretz Yisrael legittin* — for purposes of the laws of *gittin*, Akko has the status of *Eretz Yisrael*. This raises a principle that will recur throughout the early *dappim* of the *masechet*: the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* may not be uniform across all areas of *halacha*. The borders relevant to *tumah* and *ma'asros* are generally those of the *Bayit Sheni* era. The borders associated with the *kedushah* of the land track the conquest of Yehoshua. But for the purposes of the *halacha* of *befanai nechtav uvfanai nechtam*, the relevant question may be more practical: is this a place where Jewish communities are established enough that *halachos* are reliably known, and where witnesses can be located if needed? If the concern driving the requirement is practical — that people far away do not know the laws of writing a *get*, or that one cannot find witnesses to validate signatures — then the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* for these purposes are drawn based on the cultural and communal realities of Jewish life, not necessarily on historical or sanctity-based demarcations.

### Within Eretz Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״. וְאִם יֵשׁ עָלָיו עוֹרְרִים, יִתְקַיֵּים בְּחוֹתְמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **One who brings a bill of divorce** from one place to another **within Eretz Yisrael is not required to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. And if there are those who contest it,** i.e., if the husband objects by saying that the bill of divorce is a forgery, **it should be ratified through its signatories.** The court must authenticate the signatures of the witnesses in order to ratify the document.

Finally, the *mishnah* closes: *hamevi get b'Eretz Yisrael eino tzarich lomar befanai nechtav uvfanai nechtam* — one who delivers a *get* within *Eretz Yisrael* does not need to make this declaration. When the entire transaction takes place internally within *Eretz Yisrael*, we have sufficient tools to verify everything, and the declaration is unnecessary. However, *v'im yesh alav orerim* — if a challenge is subsequently raised, if the husband appears and claims the *get* was a forgery — then *yitkayem be-chotmav*: the *get* is validated through its signatures. So within *Eretz Yisrael*, we do not validate proactively, but if and when a challenge arises, the mechanism of signature validation is available and sufficient.

### Mishnah Structure

This *mishnah* offers a remarkable illustration of a broader phenomenon in the structure of *mishnayos* — one that, once noticed, appears throughout the *Shas*. The *gemara* sometimes refers explicitly to a *mishnah rishona* and a *mishnah acharona*, cases where an earlier ruling was later superseded. But there is a more subtle and more common phenomenon: a later generation of *tannaim* received a simple, early *mishnah* containing basic terms, and then proceeded to debate what those terms meant. The apparent structure of *tanna kamma* versus *Rabbi Yehuda* versus *Rabbi Meir* often conceals what was really a simple original teaching, with the named opinions all being subsequent clarifications or debates about the scope of the original terms.

The phenomenon is visible even in the very first *mishnah* of Pesachim: *kol makom she-ein machnisin bo chametz ein tzarich bdikah* — wherever one does not bring *chametz*, one need not check. The *mishnah* then introduces the teaching about the two rows of the wine cellar (*shtei shurot ba-martef*), and *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* debate what "two rows" means. Clearly, there was an early, simple teaching, and the later *tannaim* argued about what the term encompassed.

Apply this lens to our *mishnah*. Strip away every named position, and what remains is a strikingly elegant, minimal teaching: *hamevi get mimedinat hayam* — one who brings a *get* from overseas must say *befanai nechtav uvfanai nechtam*; and if one brings it from one *medinah* to another in *medinat hayam*, or from *Eretz Yisrael* to *medinat hayam*; but *hamevi get b'Eretz Yisrael eino tzarich*. That's the whole *mishnah* in its original form. Crisp, simple, complete.

Then come the named *tannaim*, and what are they doing? They are debating the meaning of the term *medinat hayam*. Does it mean genuinely distant overseas territory, or does it mean any place outside *Eretz Yisrael* — even places right at the border? *Rabban Gamliel* says even Rekem and Hager. *Rabbi Eliezer* says even the vicinity of Lud. *Chachamim* say no, only genuinely distant places. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* takes the term *mimedina limedina* and says it applies even to *hegmonia le-hegmonia* — even two jurisdictions within a single city. And *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir* are not adding new positions but simply defining, from different angles, what the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* are.

This structural observation enriches the reading considerably. Rather than a cacophony of competing voices, we see a simple early ruling, and a later generation systematically working through every ambiguous term. As one participant in the *shiur* observed, this is in a sense the same process that the *amoraim* perform explicitly in the *gemara* — with the difference that in the *gemara* the process of clarification and debate is transparent, while here it is embedded in the *mishnah* itself.

### Get Convention

Two brief textual notes are worth recording here. First, the word *get*: *Tosafos* points out that it is a generic term for a *shtar* — a legal document — but that without further qualification, it almost always refers to a *get nashim*, a bill of divorce. Why the word *get* came to be so closely associated with divorce specifically is not entirely clear. Second, *Tosafos* notes a widespread *minhag* to write a *get* with twelve lines. The basis offered is the *gematria* of the word *get*: *gimel* equals 3, *tes* equals 9, totaling twelve. An alternative explanation *Tosafos* considers is that between each of the five books of the *Sefer Torah*, there is a gap of four lines; the three such gaps between the first four books amount to twelve dividing lines in total. To this day, a *get* is written so that the text fills exactly twelve lines.

### Border Cities

Returning to the question of Ashkelon and Akko: *Tosafos* acknowledges that both cities are, in various sources, counted as part of *Eretz Yisrael*, and that Ashkelon is in the region of *Eretz Pelishtim*. Yet, *Tosafos* argues, even if these cities are technically within the boundaries of *Eretz Yisrael*, they may sit at the periphery in a meaningful sense. If the underlying concern of *befanai nechtav uvfanai nechtam* is practical — that people in certain places do not know the *halachos* of *gittin* or that witnesses cannot be located — then cities on the extreme margins of Jewish settlement may not enjoy the presumption of halachic reliability that the interior of the land does. The determination of which side of the line they fall on for purposes of this *halacha* is therefore not identical with the question of whether they are technically within the borders of *Eretz Yisrael*.

This question — whether we use standard definitions of *Eretz Yisrael*'s borders, or whether we draw the map based on the specific concerns underlying the law of *befanai nechtav* — will continue to animate the discussion across the first several *dappim* of the *masechet*.

### No Forgery Defense

*Tosafos* notes in its commentary to *im yesh alav orer mitkayem be-chotmav* that as long as the husband has not come forward to challenge the *get* — in the case of a *get* delivered within *Eretz Yisrael* — the woman may remarry on the strength of the *get* alone. We do not, on her behalf, raise the concern that perhaps the *get* is forged. One might have thought the opposite: since the husband is absent from the proceedings, should the *beis din* not represent his interests and challenge the document's authenticity? *Tosafos* answers: *de-mishum iguna akilu bah rabanan* — because of the concern for *iguna*, the Rabbis were lenient and declined to mount the forgery defense on the husband's behalf. The woman cannot be left in a state of uncertainty indefinitely because of an unsubstantiated concern.

### Ramban Property

The *Ramban* takes a fundamentally different approach to the same question. He argues that the reason the *beis din* does not represent the husband's interests and challenge the *get* is not because of any *leniency* born of the concern for *iguna* — it is because the entire framing of "representing the absent party's interests" does not apply here in the first place. In monetary matters, if one party is attempting to seize another's property and the owner is absent, the court will defend the owner's interests. But the *Ramban* says: *ein ha-ishah nichsei ha-ba'al* — the woman is not the husband's property. The act of divorce is not the taking of something from the husband. It is the definition of a woman's legal status — a question that lies outside the framework of *dinei mamonos* entirely.

This is a striking conceptual point to encounter on the very first *daf* of the *masechet*. The entire tractate is, in some sense, structured around the husband's power — he writes, he delivers, he sends. And yet here, at the outset, the *Ramban* insists that however we frame the halachic mechanics, the woman is not to be understood as her husband's property, and that framing must be kept entirely separate from the language of monetary law. As one participant noted, this is in some ways consistent with *Tosafos*'s position as well: even *Tosafos*, who invokes *iguna* as the *ta'am*, still presupposes that the husband is seen as a party who could be "injured" by the loss of the *get* challenge — something the *Ramban* rejects at the conceptual root.

## Two Reasons

### Rabbah Lishmah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? רַבָּה אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one who brings a bill of divorce from a country overseas to Eretz Yisrael must say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this declaration? **Rabba says:** It is **because** the people who live overseas **are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake.** It is not sufficient for a bill of divorce to be written in a technically correct manner. It must also be written for the sake of the man and the woman who are divorcing. Therefore, when the witness comes before the court and says that it was written and signed in his presence, he is testifying that the writing and the signing of the bill of divorce were performed for the sake of the man and woman in question.

The *gemara* opens with a simple question: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for the requirement of *befanai nechtav uvfanai nechtam*? Two *amoraim* offer two fundamentally different answers, each focusing on a different word of the declaration.

*Rabbah* says: *lefi she-ein beki'in lishmah* — because people in *medinat hayam* are not expert in the requirement of *lishmah*, the rule that a *get* must be written specifically for the sake of this particular woman. Since we are concerned that the *get* was not written with proper *lishmah* in mind, the *shaliach* must testify: *befanai nechtav* — it was written in my presence. *Rabbah* is focused on the writing of the *get*, and his concern is about whether the *get* was produced in accordance with *halacha*.

*Tosafos* raises the obvious question: why single out *lishmah* specifically? There are many other requirements for writing a valid *get*. The answer *Tosafos* gives is that *lishmah* was the most commonly neglected requirement — perhaps because people in distant lands were not fully persuaded of its necessity, or perhaps because it was simply less well-known. But *Tosafos* does note other possible framings, including the possibility that in *medinat hayam* the problem was not ignorance so much as non-acceptance: people who knew the law of *lishmah* but did not reliably observe it.

The practical question then arises: how does the *shaliach*'s statement *befanai nechtav* — "it was written in my presence" — actually establish that *lishmah* was observed? All he is literally saying is that it was written in front of him. *Rashi* offers two possibilities. Either the declaration opens a line of inquiry — we ask him to elaborate on how the *get* was written, and through that inquiry we establish that *lishmah* was observed — or, more elegantly, the statement *befanai nechtav* functions as an implicit claim that everything was done in accordance with *halacha*, without any need for further questioning. The implicit logic is reinforced by the sociological observation: if people in *medinat hayam* know they will have to make this declaration before the *get* can be received in *Eretz Yisrael*, they will know that they are testifying to *halachic* compliance, and will take care accordingly.

### Rava Signatures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֵדִים מְצוּיִין לְקַיְּימוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** a different reason: It is **because there are no witnesses available to ratify it.** Since the bill of divorce was written in a distant place, it is possible that the husband, or someone else, might later claim that the bill of divorce is a forgery. For this reason the agent must say that the bill of divorce was written and signed in his presence, a declaration that bars any subsequent objection on the part of the husband.

*Rava* focuses on the other half of the declaration: *befanai nechtam* — it was signed in my presence. *Rava*'s concern is not about whether the *get* was written correctly, but about the practical problem of signature validation. We see at the end of the *mishnah* that a *get* delivered in *Eretz Yisrael* is subject to challenge: if the husband comes forward and claims the *get* is a forgery, the mechanism for resolving that challenge is *yitkayem be-chotmav* — signature validation, either through the witnesses themselves or through others who can identify their signatures.

Within *Eretz Yisrael*, this is manageable. One can locate witnesses, summon those who know the signatories, produce corroborating documents. But if the *get* was signed in a distant land and the challenge arises years later in *Eretz Yisrael*, how will one ever find witnesses to validate those signatures? They are back in *medinat hayam*, impossible to summon. *Rava* therefore says: we require upfront validation by the *shaliach* himself. He testifies at the point of delivery that the *get* was signed in his presence — and that testimony, even from a single witness as we will see, suffices to forestall the need for later signature validation.

### Nafka Mina Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאַתְיוּהוּ בֵּי תְרֵי. אִי נָמֵי, מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִי נָמֵי, בְּאוֹתָהּ מְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** these two explanations? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to a case **where two** people **brought** the bill of divorce. In this case, two witnesses are available to ratify the bill of divorce if someone objects to its validity. **Alternatively,** the difference concerns a case where the agent brings the bill of divorce **from** one **region to** another **region within Eretz Yisrael.** Here there is no concern that the bill of divorce might not have been written for her sake, as the residents of Eretz Yisrael are aware of this requirement. However, witnesses are not necessarily available to confirm the document. **Alternatively,** there is a difference between the two explanations in a case where the agent brings the bill of divorce **within that** same **region in a country overseas.** According to the opinion of Rabba, who says the concern is that the people there might not know that the document must be written for her sake, this problem is equally relevant in this case. However, according to the opinion of Rava, who says that the reason is because witnesses are not available, if the bill of divorce is brought in the same region then the witnesses will be available to ratify it.

The *gemara* then asks: *mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between these two reasons? And it proceeds to spell out several elegant *nafka minos*.

First: *de'asu bei trei* — two people brought the *get*. According to *Rava*, who is concerned about the ability to validate signatures at a later date, the presence of two witnesses delivering the *get* alleviates the concern. The two *messengers* together, by the very act of delivering the *get*, implicitly testify that it was legitimately dispatched by the husband; a subsequent forgery claim would be answerable through their testimony. But according to *Rabbah*, who is concerned about *lishmah*, the presence of two messengers solves nothing. The question of whether the *get* was written in accordance with *halacha* is entirely independent of how many people delivered it.

Second: *mimedina limedina b'Eretz Yisrael* — from one region to another within *Eretz Yisrael*, such as from *Yehuda* to *Galil*. According to *Rabbah*, everyone in *Eretz Yisrael* knows the requirement of *lishmah*, so there is no concern. But according to *Rava*, even within *Eretz Yisrael*, if the *get* was written in one region and delivered in another, the practicalities of locating witnesses for signature validation across those regions may still be difficult — one might need the declaration even there.

Third: *be-osah medinah b'medinat hayam* — within a single country in *medinat hayam*. According to *Rabbah*, who is concerned about *lishmah*, the problem exists even within one country abroad, since the concern is one of halachic knowledge. But according to *Rava*, if the *get* was written and delivered within the same country overseas, one will be able to locate witnesses for signature validation if needed — they are all in the same place.

These *nafka minos* are elegant because they isolate the exact axis of disagreement between the two *amoraim*: *Rabbah*'s concern is halachic competence, while *Rava*'s concern is logistical access.

### One Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבָּה דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ – לִיבְעֵי תְּרֵי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַכֹּל עֵדֻיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה! עֵד אֶחָד נֶאֱמָן בְּאִיסּוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabba, who said** that the reason is **because they are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake, let us require two** witnesses to testify about this, **just as is** the case **with regard to all testimonies in the Torah.** The Gemara answers: **One witness is deemed credible with regard to prohibitions.** In other words, if there is uncertainty as to whether a matter is prohibited or permitted, in the case of the heretofore married woman, the testimony of one witness is sufficient.

The *gemara* now asks: according to *Rabbah*, who says the concern is that people in *medinat hayam* do not know the laws of *lishmah*, why does one witness suffice? If we genuinely doubt whether the *get* was produced in accordance with *halacha* — if we are uncertain of its *kashrus* — surely we require two witnesses to resolve that doubt, as is the case with all testimony in the Torah.

The *gemara* answers: *ed echad ne'eman b'issurin* — a single witness is trusted in matters of *kashrus* status. When the question is not one of conflicting factual claims but of an object's halachic status, the tradition holds that one witness suffices. *Rashi* notes the breadth of this principle: the Torah trusted every individual Jew to testify reliably about the separation of *terumah*, about the *shechita* of an animal, about the removal of *gid ha-nasheh* and *chelev*. When you purchase meat from a butcher, you do not require two witnesses to confirm it was properly slaughtered; the butcher's own representation suffices. As *Tosafos* puts it: *heicha anu somchin al neshoteinu* — we rely on our wives when they serve us food they have prepared, when they tell us meat was properly salted, when they report that they have attended the *mikvah*.

### Challenge Issued

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמְרִינַן עֵד אֶחָד נֶאֱמָן בְּאִיסּוּרִין, כְּגוֹן חֲתִיכָה סָפֵק שֶׁל חֵלֶב סָפֵק שֶׁל שׁוּמָּן, דְּלָא אִיתַּחְזַק אִיסּוּרָא; אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִיתַּחְזַק אִיסּוּרָא דְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, הָוֵי דָּבָר שֶׁבָּעֶרְוָה; וְאֵין דָּבָר שֶׁבְּעֶרְוָה פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: One can **say that we say one witness is deemed credible with regard to prohibitions** in a case **such as** where there is **a piece** of fat, and it is **uncertain** if it is forbidden **fat** [***ḥelev***] and **uncertain** if it is permitted **fat.** In this situation the piece can be rendered permitted by a single witness, **as there is no presumption** that it is **forbidden.** Therefore, as there is an uncertainty, and one witness said it is permitted fat, he is deemed credible. **However, here, where there is a presumption** that this woman is **forbidden, as** she is **a married woman,** a status she retains until it is established that she has received a bill of divorce, if so, this **is a matter of forbidden sexual relations, and** the general principle is that **there is no matter** of testimony **for forbidden sexual relations** that can be attested to by **fewer than two** witnesses.

The *gemara* immediately raises a sharp challenge. When we say *ed echad ne'eman b'issurin*, in what circumstances does that apply? The typical case is like a piece of fat: there is a piece of meat before us, and we do not know whether it is *chelev* — forbidden fat — or *shuman* — permitted fat. There is no prior known status of the piece, and a single witness can establish which it is. But — and this is the challenge — *aval hacha de-it-chazak issura d'eshet ish*: here, the woman was known to be married. She entered the situation with an established status of *eshet ish*, a forbidden relationship. We are not in a situation of mere uncertainty; we are in a situation of *chazakas issur* — a presumption of prohibition. Can a single witness overturn that?

Moreover, *havei davar she-b'ervah*: this is a matter of *arayos* — sexual prohibitions — and for those, the Torah's standard is *ein davar she-b'ervah pachos mishnayim*, not fewer than two witnesses. The woman's marital status, and therefore her permissibility to others, is a question of *arayos*, and *arayos* categorically requires two witnesses.

So there are, in fact, two independent reasons why one witness should *not* suffice here: first, the *chazakas issur* of being a married woman; second, the inherent requirement of two witnesses for all matters of *arayos*. How, then, does the *mishnah* permit the single declaration of the *shaliach* to suffice?

### Beyado Concept

*Tosafos* introduces an important conceptual tool in response to the first problem. The reason one witness suffices to overturn a *chazakas issur* in ordinary *issurim* — the piece of fat, the unsalted meat, the question of *shechita* — is the principle of *beyado*: the matter lies within the power of the person testifying to change. A butcher *can* shecht the animal. A householder *can* salt his own meat. A person *can* separate *terumah*. Because it was within their power to have done the permissible thing, they are believed when they testify that they did so, even against a background presumption of prohibition.

By contrast, neither the woman nor the *shaliach* can bring about a divorce independently. No matter how much effort or money is applied, unless the husband willingly gives the *get*, the woman remains married. The divorce is not *beyado* — not within their power to accomplish. And according to *Tosafos*, when the matter is *lo beyado* — not within one's power to change — a single witness does not suffice to overcome a *chazakas issur*.

### Unresolved Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוֹב בְּקִיאִין הֵן. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא – סְתָם סָפְרֵי דְּדַיָּינֵי מִיגְמָר גְּמִירִי, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דְּאַצְרוּךְ; וְהָכָא מִשּׁוּם עִיגּוּנָא אַקִּילוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabba's concern is not equivalent to a case of uncertainty, as **most** Jewish people **are experts** in the requirement that a bill of divorce must be written for the woman's sake. **And** this is so **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who is** generally **concerned about a minority** in a matter of forbidden sexual relations. In this case Rabbi Meir concedes that one need not be concerned for the minority, as **ordinary judicial scribes,** who write bills of divorce, **are learned** in this *halakha*, and know that a bill of divorce must be written for the woman's sake. **And it is the Sages who required** testimony about this matter, as an extra precaution. **And here,** with regard to this testimony, **due to** the possibility of **desertion,** i.e., that she may become like a deserted wife, **the Sages were lenient with her** by saying that the agent who brought the bill of divorce is deemed credible, despite the fact that he is a single witness. This is to prevent women from becoming uncertain of their marital status as a result of the lack of testimony needed to ratify the bill of divorce.

This leaves us with a compounded difficulty. The *gemara*'s challenge runs on two tracks: *chazakas issur* combined with *lo beyado*, and the categorical requirement of two witnesses for *arayos*. Even for those authorities who hold that a single witness *can* overcome a *chazakas issur* even when the matter is *lo beyado*, the second problem remains: this is *ervah*, and *davar she-b'ervah* always requires two witnesses. The woman was a *eshet ish*, and her permissibility to others is a matter of *arayos* by definition.

So the question stands in full force, and the *gemara*'s answer will have to address both dimensions simultaneously. The answer is left as a *cliffhanger* — to be resolved at the opening of the next *daf*.
