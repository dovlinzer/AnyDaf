# Taanit 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yachid v'Tzibur Intro

### Opening & Dedication

We dedicate the learning of this *daf* for the well-being of the kidnapped teenagers and pray that they return safely to their families.

### Yachid/Tzibur Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַל יֹאמַר אָדָם תַּלְמִיד אֲנִי, אֵינִי רָאוּי לִהְיוֹת יָחִיד, אֶלָּא: כׇּל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים יְחִידִים. אֵיזֶהוּ יָחִיד וְאֵיזֶהוּ תַּלְמִיד? יָחִיד — כֹּל שֶׁרָאוּי לְמַנּוֹתוֹ פַּרְנָס עַל הַצִּבּוּר, תַּלְמִיד — כׇּל שֶׁשּׁוֹאֲלִין אוֹתוֹ דָּבָר הֲלָכָה בְּתַלְמוּדוֹ וְאוֹמֵר, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּמַסֶּכְתָּא דְכַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A person should not say: I am** only **a student,** and consequently **I am unworthy to be** considered **an individual** who fasts, as stated in the mishna. **Rather,** with regard to the fasts of individuals, **all Torah scholars are individuals.** The *baraita* elaborates: **Who is an individual and who is a student? An individual** is **anyone who is** learned in Torah **and worthy to be appointed leader** and teacher **over the community. A student** is **any** Torah scholar **who is asked a matter of *halakha* in his studies and he says** the correct answer, **and** this is the case **even** if he is familiar only **with** the **tractate of** the ***kalla*** month, i.e., the tractate the community studied that year.

Before turning to the material of 11a, it is worth drawing out one or two important themes from the previous day's learning, as they resurface with considerable force throughout the pages ahead. A major organizing theme of this *masechet* is the dynamic between *yachid* and *tzibbur* — the individual and the community. This tension appears already in the earlier *Mishnah*, where Rabban Gamliel holds that one does not begin praying for rain until the seventh of *Marheshvan* — not because the third of *Marheshvan* is necessarily too early, but rather *kedei sheyagia acharon shebeyisrael lenahar parat*, so that even the last stragglers among Israel can make it back to the Euphrates. Here the *tzibbur* waits on behalf of even a few *yachidim* — an inversion of the dynamic we might expect, where the needs of the many override those of the few. This sense of communal responsibility toward the individual is a defining motif.

### Who are the Yachidim

The *Gemara* then raises the question of who precisely constitutes the *yachidim* who begin fasting before the community reaches a state of crisis. The *Gemara*'s initial answer — that they are *rabbanan*, the Torah sages — is, on reflection, a little surprising. After all, the figure who most captures the imagination throughout this *masechet* is *Choni HaMa'agel*, who draws his circle and enjoys an almost uniquely intimate relationship with the Almighty. He is a charismatic *chasid*, not a Torah scholar — he is not "Rabbi Choni"; he is *Choni HaMa'agel*. The *Gemara* elsewhere contrasts the *yachidim* with the *talmidim* — Torah students who know Torah and may aspire to the role of *yachid*, and about whom the previous day's discussion considered whether doing so would constitute *yohara*, a kind of religious arrogance, particularly since fasting is genuinely demanding.

### Leadership Models

But the *Gemara*'s more definitive identification of the *yachidim* is striking: *kol hara'uyim limnot parnas al hatzibbur* — those who are fit to be appointed as *parnas*, a lay leader of the community. The word *parnas* derives from *leparnes*, to provide sustenance, and that etymology is meaningful. It is about a sense of responsibility for the well-being of the community. This is a very different model from Torah leadership. The *ra'uy limnot parnas al tzibbur* is not necessarily a great *talmid chacham*; rather, even before occupying any formal position, such a person is someone who assumes responsibility, has the capacity for it, and is constitutively oriented toward the welfare of the *tzibbur*. These, then, are the *yachidim* — people who see it as their duty to act for the community even before things have reached a crisis level.

It is worth pausing on the significance of this. The *Gemara* acknowledges that Torah scholars can aspire to this role, but the definition itself reflects an awareness that there are two parallel tracks of leadership — Torah leadership and lay leadership — and that the role of the *yachid* in this context is tied not to scholarship but to communal responsibility. The *parnas* model is about commitment to *tzibbur*, not primarily about learning. One might ask whether the category includes major donors or financial patrons of the community. The language of the *Gemara* — *ra'uy limnot parnas* — does not appear to carry that connotation; it is about the disposition and capacity to lead, not about wealth. This ongoing question of *yachid ve-tzibbur*, who bears responsibility to whom and in what direction, is a theme we will encounter repeatedly.

## V'ten Tal U'Matar Calend…

### Tekufot & Sixty Days

The *Gemara* states that in *Bavel* one begins reciting *v'ten tal u'matar* sixty days after the *tekufah*. The *tekufot* are the astronomical turning points of the year: the two equinoxes and the two solstices. *Tekufat Tishrei* corresponds to the autumnal equinox, *tekufat Tevet* to the winter solstice, *tekufat Nissan* to the vernal equinox, and *tekufat Tammuz* to the summer solstice — all falling approximately on the 21st of their respective secular months. Sixty days after September 21st brings us to approximately November 20th or 21st — and indeed, if one consults an older *siddur*, one will find that date recorded.

### Julian-Gregorian Shift

And yet in practice today, the insertion of *v'ten tal u'matar* in *Shemoneh Esrei* begins on the night of December 4th or 5th. The gap between November 20th and December 5th requires explanation.

The answer lies in the difference between the Julian and the Gregorian calendars. *Chazal* reckoned the *tekufot* according to the Julian calendar, which accounted for the roughly quarter-day surplus beyond 365 days by inserting a leap year every four years. The difficulty is that the solar year is actually slightly less than 365 and a quarter days. The Gregorian calendar corrected for this by eliminating three leap years every four hundred years — specifically, century years not divisible by four hundred do not receive a leap year under the Gregorian system, though they would under the Julian. This produces a drift of three days every four hundred years. Over enough centuries, the divergence between the two calendars becomes significant. By the time Pope Gregory introduced the reformed calendar, the accumulated discrepancy required adding eleven days, which means that November 20th on the Julian calendar falls on December 5th on the Gregorian.

What this means in practice is that we are not observing a Gregorian calendar date at all — we are observing the Julian date of November 20th, which happens to correspond in our era to December 5th Gregorian. Because the divergence continues to grow (except in years divisible by four hundred, which do not diverge — hence the start date remained December 5th across the year 2000), by the year 2100 the accumulated drift will shift the observance to December 6th.

This raises a genuine question that does not have a fully satisfying answer: why have we enshrined the secular date rather than the astronomical reality? The *tekufot* are phenomena of sun and season, not artifacts of any secular calendar system. The natural approach would be to calculate sixty days from the actual autumnal equinox and begin saying *v'ten tal u'matar* on approximately November 20th by the Gregorian reckoning — which is where the astronomical *tekufah* actually falls. The fact that we instead track the Julian date means we have, in effect, enshrined a historical calendar convention rather than the underlying astronomical fact.

### Rosh's Spain Case

This brings us to a famous *teshuvah* of the *Rosh*. When the *Rosh* arrived in Toledo, Spain, he encountered a striking scene: the community was engaged in special *tefillot* for rain and *ta'aniot* for rain — every practice described in this *masechet* — because it had not rained and rain was desperately needed. And yet they were not saying *v'ten tal u'matar*, because sixty days after the *tekufah* had not yet arrived. The *Rosh* expressed astonishment. This, he insisted, is not a dispute between the *Bavli* and the *Yerushalmi* in which we follow the *Bavli* as the more authoritative text. The very reason the *Gemara* fixes the date at sixty days after the *tekufah* is because that is when *Bavel* begins to need rain. In Spain, rain is needed at a much earlier point — closer to the schedule of *Eretz Yisrael* than of *Bavel* — and this is not an anomalous year but the regular pattern of the climate. The *Rosh* therefore ruled that in Spain, *v'ten tal u'matar* should be inserted whenever the season in that land typically requires rain, not sixty days after the *tekufah*.

His summary of the community's response is memorable: *tzovei'ach k'kruchiya velo ashgechu bi* — "I screeched like a crane and no one paid attention to me." This is, one might note, a certain comfort to any rabbi who has ever struggled to persuade a community of a straightforward halachic position in its own best interest. The *Rosh* — of his stature, on a matter of pure logic and clear halachic reasoning — was simply ignored. The community was not going to change what they had always done. *L'halacha*, regrettably or ironically, the community prevailed: we do not rule like the *Rosh*. The one echo of his position that does survive in *halacha* is the following: if one is living in a country that needs rain earlier than sixty days after the *tekufah* and one forgot and accidentally inserted *v'ten tal u'matar* earlier, one need not repeat *Shemoneh Esrei*. That is the degree to which we rule like the *Rosh*.

### Prayer to Liturgy Shift

This discussion points toward something deeper about how the character of *tefillah* has shifted over time. The *masechet* is built on a vision of rain as the expression of God's living relationship to the land and to the people — a relationship that is responsive and dynamic. The sequence of the *Mishnah* is almost urgent: begin praying on *zayin Marheshvan*; if rain has not come by *yud-zayin*, it is time to escalate. There is an implicit confidence in the efficaciousness of prayer, an assumption of divine responsiveness.

What the story of the *Rosh* in Toledo illustrates is that this vision has given way to something quite different: a fixed liturgy, a calendar date in the *siddur*, a practice defined by the *Mishnah Berurah*. The community that desperately needed rain and was fasting for it was nonetheless treating *v'ten tal u'matar* as a liturgical formula to be recited on its appointed date, disconnected from whether rain was actually needed. The shift from prayer as responsive communication with God toward fixed liturgy is one that this *masechet* makes us feel acutely. Part of what drives this shift, it should be noted, is geography and agriculture. *Bavel* was irrigated by rivers and was not rain-dependent in the same way as *Eretz Yisrael* or the Mediterranean lands of Spain and the Levant. In an irrigation-based agricultural system, rain is less existentially urgent, and that may be precisely where the practice of *v'ten tal u'matar* begins to feel more like liturgy and less like a genuine cry of need.

## Perishah Min Tzibur

### Mar'iv Atzmo Famine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמַּרְעִיב עַצְמוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי רְעָבוֹן, — נִיצָּל מִמִּיתָה מְשׁוּנָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּרָעָב פָּדְךָ מִמָּוֶת״. ״מֵרָעָב״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁמַּרְעִיב עַצְמוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי רְעָבוֹן — נִיצּוֹל מִמִּיתָה מְשׁוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Anyone who** has food for himself but nevertheless **starves himself in years of famine will be saved from an unusual death, as it is stated: "In famine, He will redeem you from death"** (Job 5:20). This is derived from the precise wording of the verse. According to its straightforward meaning, instead of "in famine," it **should have** said: **From famine,** as one is delivered from famine. **Rather, this is what** the verse **is saying: As a reward for starving himself in years of famine,** Job **will be saved from an unusual death.**

We now return to the *yachid* and *tzibbur* theme directly, picking up at the top of 11a. The *Gemara* cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *kol hamar'iv atzmo bishnei ra'avon, nitzal mimitah meshunah* — anyone who causes himself to go hungry in a time of famine is saved from an unnatural death. This does not mean one literally starves; it means that even if one has food and resources, one eats sparingly, identifying with the community's suffering rather than insulating oneself from it. The *Gemara* derives this from the verse *birav padekha mimavet* — in famine He has redeemed you from death. The *Gemara* reads the unusual formulation *birav* not as "from famine" but as containing the root *mar'iv*, hungering: in the merit of causing oneself to go hungry during the years of famine, one is saved from an unnatural death.

The theme is unmistakable: here is the *yachid* who could insulate himself from the community's suffering but who chooses instead to place himself within it — not merely emotionally but in concrete behavior.

### Tashmish Hamitah Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אָסוּר לְאָדָם לְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי רְעָבוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְיוֹסֵף יֻלַּד שְׁנֵי בָנִים בְּטֶרֶם תָּבוֹא שְׁנַת הָרָעָב״. תָּנָא: חֲסוּכֵי בָּנִים מְשַׁמְּשִׁין מִטּוֹתֵיהֶן בִּשְׁנֵי רְעָבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Reish Lakish said: It is prohibited for a person to have conjugal relations in years of famine,** so that children not be born during these difficult years. **As it is stated: "And to Joseph were born two sons before the year of famine came"** (Genesis 41:50). It was **taught** in a *baraita*: Nevertheless, **those without children may have marital relations in years of famine,** as they must strive to fulfill the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply.

*Reish Lakish* extends this principle beyond food: *assur l'adam leshamesh mitato bishnei ra'avon* — it is forbidden to engage in *tashmish hamitah* during years of famine. The verse adduced is the one describing Yosef's two sons: they were born before the period of famine arrived, but once the famine came, no more. There is a parallel here to the earlier material — just as there is *ta'anit* in the sense of *mar'iv atzmo*, so too there is the prohibition of *tashmish hamitah*, a further way of denying oneself pleasures in solidarity with communal suffering, and perhaps also an acknowledgment that bringing more mouths into the world at such a time reflects an indifference to what is happening around one.

However, a *braisa* qualifies this: *chasochei banim* — those who have not yet fulfilled the obligation of having children — are permitted *tashmish hamitah* even during famine years. This exception reflects the ongoing tension between individual need and communal obligation. The profound personal need of *peru u'revu* is not simply overridden by the imperative of identifying with the community's suffering. The balance between *yachid* and *tzibbur* here is not absolute; particular individual circumstances can and must be weighed.

### Heavenly Angels Declare

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל שְׁרוּיִים בְּצַעַר וּפֵירַשׁ אֶחָד מֵהֶן, בָּאִין שְׁנֵי מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת שֶׁמְּלַוִּין לוֹ לָאָדָם, וּמַנִּיחִין לוֹ יְדֵיהֶם עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, וְאוֹמְרִים: פְּלוֹנִי זֶה שֶׁפֵּירַשׁ מִן הַצִּבּוּר, אַל יִרְאֶה בְּנֶחָמַת צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **When the Jewish people is immersed in distress, and one of them separates himself** from the community and does not share their suffering, the **two ministering angels who accompany a person come and place their hands on his head,** as though he was an offering, **and say: This** man, **so-and-so, who has separated himself from the community, let him not see the consolation of the community.**

### Must Participate Tzaar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַצִּבּוּר שָׁרוּי בְּצַעַר, אַל יֹאמַר אָדָם: אֵלֵךְ לְבֵיתִי, וְאוֹכַל וְאֶשְׁתֶּה, וְשָׁלוֹם עָלַיִךְ נַפְשִׁי. וְאִם עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן — עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״וְהִנֵּה שָׂשׂוֹן וְשִׂמְחָה הָרֹג בָּקָר וְשָׁחֹט צֹאן אָכֹל בָּשָׂר וְשָׁתוֹת יָיִן אָכוֹל וְשָׁתוֹ כִּי מָחָר נָמוּת״, מָה כְּתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ — ״וְנִגְלָה בְאׇזְנָי ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אִם יְכֻפַּר הֶעָוֹן הַזֶּה לָכֶם עַד תְּמֻתוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** A similar idea **is taught in another** *baraita*: **When the community is immersed in suffering, a person may not say: I will go to my home and I will eat and drink, and peace be upon you, my soul. And if he does so, the verse says about him: "And behold joy and gladness, slaying oxen and killing sheep, eating flesh and drinking wine; let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we shall die"** (Isaiah 22:13). **And** the prophecy continues with **what is written afterward,** in the following verse: **"And the Lord of hosts revealed Himself in my ears: Surely this iniquity shall not be expiated by you until you die"** (Isaiah 22:14).

A second *braisa* shifts from pronouncement to imperative: *bizman she'hatzibbur sharui be'tza'ar, al yomar adam: eilech le'veisi ve'ochal ve'eshteh ve'shalom alayich nafshi* — when the community is in suffering, a person may not say "I will go home and eat and drink and be at peace." The verse invoked against one who does this — *ve'hineh sason ve'simcha, harog bakar ve'shachot tzon, achol basar u'shasos yayin, achol u'shaseh ki machar namus*, "and behold, joy and gladness, slaughtering cattle and killing sheep, eating meat and drinking wine: let us eat and drink for tomorrow we die" — is followed immediately by *ve'niglah be'oznei Hashem Tze-vaos, im yechupar he'avon hazeh lachem ad temusun*: this sin will not be atoned until you die. The severity is notable: *perishah min ha'tzibbur* through self-indulgence is placed among those sins for which one has no portion in *olam ha'ba*, the whole notion of *ein lo chelek le'olam ha'ba* rooted in the act of cutting oneself off from the community — which is, the *Gemara* notes, precisely what *kareis* means: *nichras me'amecha*, cut off from your people. The ultimate punishment is this very severance from the community.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן מִידַּת בֵּינוֹנִים. אֲבָל בְּמִדַּת רְשָׁעִים, מָה כְּתִיב? ״אֵתָיוּ אֶקְחָה יַיִן וְנִסְבְּאָה שֵׁכָר וְהָיָה כָזֶה יוֹם מָחָר״, מָה כְּתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״הַצַּדִּיק אָבָד וְאֵין אִישׁ שָׂם עַל לֵב כִּי מִפְּנֵי הָרָעָה נֶאֱסַף הַצַּדִּיק״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* comments: Up **to this point** is the **attribute of middling people,** who merely exclude themselves from the suffering of the community. **However,** with regard **to** the **attribute of wicked people, what is written** about those who hope for more of these days? **"Come, I will fetch wine, and we will fill ourselves with strong drink; and tomorrow shall be as this day,** and much more abundant" (Isaiah 56:12). **And what is written afterward? "The righteous perishes, and no man lays it to heart,** and godly men are taken away, none considering **that the righteous is taken away from the evil to come"** (Isaiah 57:1). This verse teaches that righteous people suffer early death to prevent them from witnessing the harm that will befall these evil people.

The *Gemara* then sharpens the distinction. What was just described — going home to eat and drink while others suffer — represents *middas beinonim*, the level of average sinners. The truly wicked, *middas resha'im*, are characterized differently: *esu ve'ekcha yayin venisbe'a shechar vehaya kaze yom machar* — "come, let us take wine and drink deeply of strong drink, and tomorrow will be like today." Rashi distinguishes the two: in the first case, the verse says *machar namus*, "tomorrow we die" — the person indulges out of genuine fear of death, a kind of desperate hedonism born of anxiety. In the second case, there is no such fear; the person simply wants to revel without any sense of mortality or urgency. That unqualified self-indulgence is what marks the *rasha*. The verse that follows — *hatzadik avad ve'ein ish sam al lev ki mipnei hara ne'esaf hatzadik* — tells us that the sins of such a person cause even the *tzadikim* in the community to die. Rashi reads this as meaning that the *tzadikim* who could have prayed on behalf of the community are taken away. And here again the *yachid* dimension reasserts itself: it is the *tzadik*, the *yachid*, who could come to the defense of the *tzibbur* — and the self-serving individual's sin undermines even that resource.

### Moshe's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, יְצַעֵר אָדָם עִם הַצִּבּוּר, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ שֶׁצִּיעֵר עַצְמוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וִידֵי מֹשֶׁה כְּבֵדִים וַיִּקְחוּ אֶבֶן וַיָּשִׂימוּ תַחְתָּיו וַיֵּשֶׁב עָלֶיהָ״, וְכִי לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה כַּר אֶחָד אוֹ כֶּסֶת אַחַת לֵישֵׁב עָלֶיהָ? אֶלָּא כָּךְ אָמַר מֹשֶׁה: הוֹאִיל וְיִשְׂרָאֵל שְׁרוּיִים בְּצַעַר — אַף אֲנִי אֶהְיֶה עִמָּהֶם בְּצַעַר. וְכׇל הַמְצַעֵר עַצְמוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר — זוֹכֶה וְרוֹאֶה בְּנֶחָמַת צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rather, a person should be distressed together with the community. As we found with Moses our teacher that he was distressed together with the community, as it is stated** during the war with Amalek: **"But Moses' hands were heavy; and they took a stone, and put it under him, and he sat upon it"** (Exodus 17:12). **But didn't Moses have one pillow or one cushion to sit upon;** why was he forced to sit on a rock? **Rather, Moses said as follows: Since the Jewish people are immersed in suffering, I too will be with them in suffering,** as much as I am able, although I am not participating in the fighting. The *baraita* adds: **And anyone who is distressed together with the community will merit seeing the consolation of the community.**

Rather than separation, says the *Gemara*: *yetzayer adam atzmo im ha'tzibbur* — a person must afflict himself together with the community, *shekein matzinu be'Moshe Rabbeinu*, as we find with Moshe. During the war against Amalek, they brought Moshe a stone to sit on. Did Moshe not have a cushion or a blanket? The *Gemara* explains: Moshe said, *ho'il ve'Yisrael shruyim be'tza'ar, af ani eheyeh imahem be'tza'ar* — since Israel is in pain, I too will be with them in their pain. One does not sit at home watching on television in comfortable detachment; one finds ways to genuinely participate in communal suffering.

### See Consolation

*Vechol hametzayer atzmo im ha'tzibbur, zocha vero'eh be'nechamas ha'tzibbur* — whoever afflicts himself together with the community will merit to see the community's consolation.

One might then ask: what if a person simply performs the public gestures of participation — fasting and praying with the community — while privately withdrawing? Who will testify to the inner reality?

### House Stones Testify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר אָדָם: מִי מֵעִיד בִּי? אַבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם וְקוֹרוֹת בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מְעִידִים בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי אֶבֶן מִקִּיר תִּזְעָק וְכָפִיס מֵעֵץ יַעֲנֶנָּה״. דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרִי: שְׁנֵי מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת הַמְלַוִּין לוֹ לָאָדָם, הֵן מְעִידִין עָלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי מַלְאָכָיו יְצַוֶּה לָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: **And lest a person say,** I have acted in secret; **who will testify against me** on the Day of Judgment? The *tanna* explains that the **stones of a person's house and** the **beams of a person's house** will **testify against him, as it is stated: "For a stone shall cry out from the wall, and a beam out of the timber shall answer it"** (Habakkuk 2:11). In **the school of Rabbi Sheila they say:** The **two ministering angels who accompany a person** will **testify against him, as it is stated: "For He will give His angels charge over you,** to keep you in all your ways" (Psalms 91:11).

The *Gemara* answers: *avnei beiso shel adam vekoros beiso shel adam me'idim bo* — the stones of a person's house and the beams of his house will testify against him. The verse cited is *ki even mikir tiz'ak, vechafis mei'etz ya'anena* — the stone will cry out from the wall, and the beam (*chafis*, which Rashi explains as the stones inserted between wood to provide stability) will answer it. Whatever one imagines is concealed within the privacy of the home will somehow be brought into the public sphere. The very structure of the house that enables the privacy of one's separation becomes the instrument of its exposure.

### Witnesses of Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִידְקָא אוֹמֵר: נִשְׁמָתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם הִיא מְעִידָה עָלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִשֹּׁכֶבֶת חֵיקֶךָ שְׁמֹר פִּתְחֵי פִיךָ״. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אֵבָרָיו שֶׁל אָדָם מְעִידִים בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַתֶּם עֵדַי נְאֻם ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥidka said: A person's soul** will **testify against him, as it is stated: "Keep the doors of your mouth from her who lies in your bosom"** (Micah 7:5). **And some say: A man's limbs** will **testify against him, as it is stated: "You are My witnesses, says the Lord"** (Isaiah 43:10).

The *Gemara* adds further witnesses. The school of Rabbi Shila teaches that the two heavenly angels who accompany a person throughout his life testify against him, as the verse says: *ki malachav yetzaveh lach* — God commands His angel concerning you. Rabbi Chalafta says it is a person's own soul that testifies against him: *mishocheves cheikecha shmor pischei ficha* — from what lies in your bosom, guard the openings of your mouth. And still another opinion holds that a person's own limbs testify against him: *atem eidai neum Hashem* — you are My witnesses, declares God.

The accumulation of witnesses is striking: the heavenly angels, the soul, the very limbs — all testify against private separation from communal suffering. There may be something deeper being said here. The point is not only that one's private behavior will eventually be made public by miraculous means, though the image of the house stones crying out gestures in that direction. Perhaps the stronger claim is that such separation creates a fracture within the person himself — a gap between the self one presents and the self one inhabits, between *neshamah* and action, between *evarim* and integrity. One cannot truly separate oneself from the community without it fracturing something within.

### El Emunah Theodicy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵל אֱמוּנָה וְאֵין עָוֶל״. ״אֵל אֱמוּנָה״, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנִּפְרָעִין מִן הָרְשָׁעִים לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא אֲפִילּוּ עַל עֲבֵירָה קַלָּה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין, כָּךְ נִפְרָעִין מִן הַצַּדִּיקִים בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה עַל עֲבֵירָה קַלָּה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* cites another verse that deals with judgment. **"A God of faithfulness and without iniquity,** He is just and righteous" (Deuteronomy 32:4). The *baraita* interprets **"a God of faithfulness"** to mean that **just as punishment is exacted from the wicked in the World-to-Come even for a light transgression that they commit, so too, punishment is exacted from the righteous in this world for a light transgression that they commit.** The righteous suffer their punishment in this world to purify them so they can enjoy the World-to-Come.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֵין עָוֶל״, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּשַׁלְּמִין שָׂכָר לַצַּדִּיקִים לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא אֲפִילּוּ עַל מִצְוָה קַלָּה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין, כָּךְ מְשַׁלְּמִין שָׂכָר לָרְשָׁעִים בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה אֲפִילּוּ עַל מִצְוָה קַלָּה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* turns to the second section of the verse: **"And without iniquity."** This teaches that **just as reward is paid to the righteous in the World-to-Come even for a minor mitzva that they fulfill, so too, reward is paid to the wicked in this world for even a minor mitzva that they fulfill,** to give the wicked all the reward they deserve for the performance of mitzvot in this world, and deprive them of any share in the World-to-Come.

The *Gemara* now touches on the theodicy theme that runs throughout *Masechet Taanit*. From *Ha'azinu*: *el emunah ve'ein avel* — a God of faithfulness, there is no perversion. The *Gemara* explains: just as the wicked are punished in *olam ha'ba* even for small sins, so too the righteous are punished in this world even for small sins. And just as the righteous receive reward in *olam ha'ba* even for small *mitzvot*, so too the wicked receive reward in this world even for small *mitzvot*. God is *tzadik veyashar* — righteous and upright.

This is *Chazal*'s approach to theodicy, and it is worth noting how it intersects with the themes of *Taanit*. The *masechet* is not primarily organized around the question of sin and *teshuvah*; its primary concern is the relationship between Israel and God, expressed through rain and prayer, through crying out and being heard. And yet theodicy cannot be avoided here, because the empirical world does not straightforwardly confirm the biblical promise that observance brings rain and sin brings drought. *Chazal*'s resolution is to push the full accounting to *olam ha'ba*. The *resha'im* prosper in this world because they are receiving their reward for small *mitzvot* — the ledger is being cleared so they can be fully accountable for their *aveirot* in *olam ha'ba*. The *tzadikim* suffer in this world because their minor *aveirot* are being paid off here so they can receive their full reward in *olam ha'ba*. That is *el emunah*: the account is always perfectly balanced, even if the settlement happens across two worlds rather than one.

### Deeds Enumerated Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צַדִּיק וְיָשָׁר הוּא״, אָמְרוּ: בִּשְׁעַת פְּטִירָתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם לְבֵית עוֹלָמוֹ כׇּל מַעֲשָׂיו נִפְרָטִין לְפָנָיו, וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: כָּךְ וְכָךְ עָשִׂיתָ בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: הֵן, וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: חֲתוֹם! וְחוֹתֵם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּיַד כׇּל אָדָם יַחְתּוֹם״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמַּצְדִּיק עָלָיו אֶת הַדִּין, וְאוֹמֵר לָהֶם: יָפֶה דַּנְתּוּנִי, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לְמַעַן תִּצְדַּק בְּדׇבְרֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the third section of the verse: **"He is just and righteous,"** the Sages **said: At the hour of a person's departure to his eternal home, all his deeds are enumerated before him** and are rendered visible to him once again, **and** the deeds themselves **say to him: You did such and such, in such and such a place, on such and such a day, and he says: Yes,** that is exactly what happened. **And they say to him: Sign** a statement that this is correct, **and he signs** it, **as it is stated: "He makes the hand of every man sign"** (Job 37:7). **And not only that,** but after one has been shown all his deeds, **he justifies the judgment upon himself, and says to them: You have judged me well.** This response serves **to fulfill that which is stated: "That You may be justified when You speak and be right when You judge"** (Psalms 51:6).

The passage concludes with a striking image: *besha'at ptirato shel adam le'beit olamo, kol ma'asav mifratim lefanav* — at the time of a person's death, all his deeds are enumerated before him. The angels say to him: *kach vekach asita, bimkom ploni, beyom ploni* — here is what you did, in this place, at this time. And he must say *hein* — yes. He must sign, as the verse says: *beyad kol adam yachtom* — in every person's hand it is sealed. And not only does he admit to everything, but *matzdik alav et hadin* — he accepts the justice of the judgment against him: *yafe dantuni*, you have judged me correctly, *lema'an tizdak bidvarecha* — that God may be found righteous in His word.

It is a sobering image. At the moment of death, every deed performed in private, every act of separation from the community, will be played back — and one will have to acknowledge it all and affirm the justice of what follows. In this way the image of death's accounting serves as the ultimate rejoinder to the person who asks *mi me'id bi* — who will testify against me? The answer is: you yourself, your soul, your limbs, the angels, the stones of your house — and ultimately, your own voice.

## Fasting as Sin

### Hayoshev B'Ta'anit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַיּוֹשֵׁב בְּתַעֲנִית נִקְרָא חוֹטֵא. סָבַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר בְּרַבִּי אוֹמֵר, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו מֵאֲשֶׁר חָטָא עַל הַנָּפֶשׁ״, וְכִי בְּאֵיזֶה נֶפֶשׁ חָטָא זֶה? אֶלָּא שֶׁצִּיעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִן הַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the primary topic of the tractate, the issue of fasts. **Shmuel said: Whoever sits in** observance of **a fast is called a sinner,** as it is inappropriate to take unnecessary suffering upon oneself. The Gemara comments: Shmuel **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the following *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar HaKappar the Great says: What** is the meaning when **the verse states,** with regard to a nazirite: **"And he will atone for him for that he sinned by the soul [*nefesh*]"** (Numbers 6:11). **But with what soul did this** nazirite **sin? Rather,** the nazirite sinned **by** the **distress** he caused **himself when he abstained from wine,** in accordance with the terms of his vow.

With the preceding discussion behind us — the dangers of separation from communal suffering, the imperative to identify with the community — the *Gemara* pivots to Shmuel's remarkable statement: *kol hayoshev be'ta'anit nikra choteh* — anyone who sits in a fast is called a sinner.

This is a striking formulation in a *masechet* devoted in large measure to the practice of fasting. The qualifier embedded in the word *hayoshev* — one who sits in a fast, habitually, regularly — is important. This is not a statement about the single communal fast undertaken in times of drought; it is a statement about the individual who makes fasting a regular personal practice.

### Asceticism Holy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה זֶה, שֶׁלֹּא צִיעֵר עַצְמוֹ אֶלָּא מִן הַיַּיִן — נִקְרָא חוֹטֵא, הַמְצַעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִכׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And are** these **matters not** inferred ***a fortiori*? And if this** nazirite, **who distressed himself** by abstaining **only from wine, is** nevertheless **called a sinner** and requires atonement, then with regard to **one who distresses himself** by abstaining **from each and every matter** of food and drink when he fasts, **all the more so** should he be considered a sinner.

Shmuel's view follows the opinion of Rabbi Elazar haKappar, who asks: what does the Torah mean when it says, with respect to the *nazir*, *vechiper alav me'asher chata al hanefesh* — he shall atone for him, for he sinned upon the soul? With which soul did the *nazir* sin? Rabbi Elazar haKappar answers: *shetzi'er atzmo min hayayin* — he sinned against his own soul by depriving himself of wine. And if this person who deprived himself only of wine is called a sinner, a *kal vachomer* follows: one who denies himself all things is all the more so a sinner.

A parenthetical observation is in order here. The *nazir*'s abstention from wine is often read through the lens of asceticism, and the *Gemara* uses this *kal vachomer* in precisely that spirit. But one might question whether the *nazir*'s relationship to wine is really about pleasure-denial at all. Wine intoxicates; it clouds the mind and undermines clarity of thought. The *nazir* denies himself wine, but he does not deny himself all physical pleasure — wine and meat (*basar ve'yayin*) are not the same, and the *nazir* abstains only from *yayin*. There is a question whether the *Gemara*'s reframing of the *nazir* as an ascetic properly represents the nature of the practice. Nevertheless, within the *Gemara*'s reading, the *nazir* is the paradigm case for self-denial, and it is on that basis that both Shmuel and the opposing view build their arguments.

In direct contrast, Rabbi Elazar holds that the *nazir* is called *kadosh* — holy — as the verse says: *kadosh yihyeh gadel pera se'ar rosho*, holy shall he be in the growth of the wild hair of his head. And if one who denied himself only one thing is called holy, *kal vachomer* one who afflicts himself from all things is all the more holy.

### Hair vs. Person Holy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: נִקְרָא קָדוֹשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קָדוֹשׁ יִהְיֶה גַּדֵּל פֶּרַע שְׂעַר רֹאשׁוֹ״. וּמָה זֶה, שֶׁלֹּא צִיעֵר עַצְמוֹ אֶלָּא מִדָּבָר אֶחָד — נִקְרָא קָדוֹשׁ, הַמְצַעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִכׇּל דָּבָר — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Rabbi Elazar says:** One who accepts a fast upon himself is **called sacred, as it is stated** with regard to the nazirite: **"He shall be sacred, he shall let the locks of the hair of his head grow long"** (Numbers 6:5). Here too, one can apply an *a fortiori* inference: **And if this** nazirite, **who distressed himself** by abstaining **from only one matter,** wine, is nevertheless **called sacred,** then with regard to **one who distresses himself** by abstaining from **every matter, all the more so** should he be considered sacred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל, הָא אִיקְּרִי קָדוֹשׁ! הָהוּא, אַגִּידּוּל פֶּרַע קָאֵי. וּלְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, הָא נִקְרָא חוֹטֵא! הָהוּא דְּסַאֵיב נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** the nazirite **is** indeed **called sacred,** as stated by Rabbi Elazar. The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is referring to** the sanctity of the **growth of the locks,** as the nazirite's hair does possess an element of sanctity, but it does not refer to the nazirite himself. The Gemara reverses the question: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** the nazirite **is called a sinner.** The Gemara answers: **That** verse **refers** specifically **to** a nazirite **who rendered himself ritually impure** by coming into contact with a dead body, an act that is prohibited for him. This particular nazirite must bring an offering to atone "for that he sinned by the soul."

How does Shmuel handle the verse that calls the *nazir* holy? The *Gemara* explains: *hahu agidul perah kai* — "holy" there refers to the hair, meaning the *nazir*'s hair is what is designated as holy; it does not imply that the *nazir* himself achieves holiness through his practice of abstention. And Rabbi Elazar, confronted with the verse that calls the *nazir* a sinner? *Hahu desaiyeb nafshieh* — that verse simply refers to the *nazir* who became *tamei* by contact with a corpse, the plain sense of the *pasuk*, not a condemnation of the *nazir*'s practice as such.

It is worth noting that this dispute has deep roots. The *Ramban*, reading the plain meaning of the *psukim*, argues that the *nazir* ideally should have remained a *nazir* his whole life, and the sin offering brought at the conclusion of the *nezirut* period atones for the sin of ending it. For the *Rambam*, by contrast — writing in *Shemonah Perakim* and drawing on the *Yerushalmi*'s even sharper formulation, *vekhi lo dayekha mah she-asrah Torah*, is it not enough for you what the Torah has forbidden that you go and add more prohibitions upon yourself? — asceticism as a religious practice represents a fundamental distortion, one he traces to Christian influence and one that is foreign to the Torah's vision of the *golden mean*, moderation rather than denial.

### Kadosh B'Kirbecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְעוֹלָם יָמוֹד אָדָם עַצְמוֹ
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Elazar** actually **say this,** that fasting is a virtuous act? **But didn't Rabbi Elazar say: A person should always consider himself**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּאִילּוּ קָדוֹשׁ שָׁרוּי בְּתוֹךְ מֵעָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּקִרְבְּךָ קָדוֹשׁ וְלֹא אָבוֹא בְּעִיר״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דְּמָצֵי לְצַעוֹרֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ, הָא — דְּלָא מָצֵי לְצַעוֹרֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **as though a sacred** object **is immersed in his bowels,** which he may not damage, **as it is stated: "The sacred is in your midst; and I will not come into the city"** (Hosea 11:9). This statement indicates that it is prohibited to take a fast upon oneself. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** first ruling, that one who fasts is sacred, is referring to a case **where he is able to distress himself** without causing bodily harm. **That** second ruling, that one may not overly burden his body, deals with a situation **when he is unable to distress himself** while avoiding all harm, and he proceeds to fast nevertheless.

The *Gemara* challenges Rabbi Elazar's position from within his own corpus. Did Rabbi Elazar not himself say: *le'olam yamod adam le'atzmo ke'ilu kadosh sharuy betoch me'av* — a person should always regard himself as if the Holy One dwells within his very innards? The verse cited is *be'kirbecha kadosh velo avo be'ir* — in your very midst is the Holy. If the Holy dwells within you, denying yourself food is not an act of holiness toward God; it is a failure of responsibility toward the divine presence within you. To feed oneself, to care for one's body, is itself a religious obligation — even more, it is caring for the vessel of the *Shechina*. The theology here is integrative rather than dualistic: the physical and spiritual are not competing; the body is not an obstacle to holiness but its container.

### Capacity Determines

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction within Rabbi Elazar's views with a practical distinction: *ha de'matzi letzaro'ei nafshieh, ha de'la matzi letzaro'ei nafshieh* — the one who is physically capable of enduring the affliction without damaging his body, for him it is holy; the one whose body cannot sustain it and who would be harmed by the practice, for him it is not. This resolution leans toward vindicating asceticism in cases where the body can manage it — though one may feel that this sits somewhat uneasily with the deeper theological point of *ke'ilu kadosh sharuy betoch me'av*, which seems to argue against self-denial on principle, not merely in cases where one's constitution cannot bear it.

### Gomel Nafsho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: נִקְרָא חָסִיד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גֹּמֵל נַפְשׁוֹ אִישׁ חָסֶד וְעֹכֵר שְׁאֵרוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַאי בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּיָתֵיב בְּתַעֲנִיתָא — לֵיכוֹל כַּלְבָּא לְשֵׁירוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said:** One who fasts is **called pious, as it is stated: "The pious man does good [*gomel*] to his own soul; but he who troubles his own flesh** is cruel" (Proverbs 11:17). The verb *gomel* can also mean weaning, or abstaining from unnecessary pleasure. Accordingly, Reish Lakish derives from this verse that one who abstains from food is called pious. Similarly, **Rav Sheshet said: This student of a Torah academy who sits in** observance of **a fast** has **let a dog eat his portion.** Since his fast weakens him and prevents him from studying Torah, it is considered as though a dog ate his meal, as the student derived no benefit from it.

*Reish Lakish* offers a further position, though the textual tradition here bifurcates. The standard text of the *Gemara* appears to cite *Reish Lakish* as saying the habitual faster is called a *chasid* — pious — from the verse *gomel nafsho ish chesed ve'ocher she'ero achzari*, one who is good to his soul is a man of kindness and one who afflicts his flesh is cruel. But *Rabbeinu Chananel* and *Tosafot* preserve an alternative *girsa* that reverses the reading entirely: *kol hamoneia atzmo min ha'ta'anit nikra chasid* — one who refrains from fasting is called pious. On this reading, the verse is understood straightforwardly: *gomel nafsho*, caring for one's soul and body, is the definition of *chesed* — of piety toward oneself — while self-denial is called cruelty. This *girsa* aligns directly with Shmuel's original position and represents the stronger anti-ascetic strand.

### Let Dog Eat

*Rav Sheshet* formulates this most bluntly: *amar Rav Sheshet, hai bar bei rav de'yatib be'ta'anita, leichol kalba sheiruteih* — a Torah student who sits in a fast: let a dog eat his meal. The force of the image is not merely rhetorical. The *Gemara* will make explicit what is implicit here: the *talmid chacham* who fasts is squandering something, wasting potential that belongs to a higher purpose.

### No Fast in Bavel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: אֵין תַּעֲנִית צִיבּוּר בְּבָבֶל אֶלָּא תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב בִּלְבַד. אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לֵישֵׁב בְּתַעֲנִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּמַעֵט בִּמְלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: There is no** completely stringent **communal fast in Babylonia, except for the Ninth of Av alone.** All other fasts, even those which are fixed and routine for the community, are treated as individual fasts, with regard to both the customs of the fast itself and the *halakhot* of who is obligated to fast. **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said** that **Reish Lakish said: A Torah scholar is not permitted to sit in** observance of **a fast, due to** the fact that his fasting **reduces** his strength for the **heavenly service** of Torah study and mitzvot.

At this point the *Gemara* inserts a statement of *Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba* that sits somewhat incongruously in this context: *ein ta'anit tzibbur be'Bavel ela Tisha B'Av bilvad* — there is no public fast in *Bavel* except for *Tisha B'Av* alone. The reason is that *Bavel* is not rain-dependent; it is irrigated by rivers. The entire structure of communal fasting described throughout this *masechet* — the escalating regimen in response to drought — simply does not apply in *Bavel*. The only practice that rises to the level of a full *ta'anit tzibbur* in *Bavel*, with all its attendant restrictions beginning from the night before, is *Tisha B'Av*. All of which is to say that the fasting discourse of this *masechet* has its natural home in the rain-dependent agricultural world of *Eretz Yisrael*; in *Bavel* it is largely theoretical.

### Talmid Chacham Can't Fast

The *Gemara* closes this sugya with *Reish Lakish*'s definitive statement: *ein talmid chacham rashai leishev be'ta'anit, mipnei shemema'et be'mlechet shamayim* — a *talmid chacham* is not permitted to sit in a fast because it diminishes *mlechet shamayim*, the work of Heaven. This is the explicit justification for Rav Sheshet's imagery about the dog. The *talmid chacham* who fasts is not simply failing himself physically; he is diminishing his capacity for Torah study, which is itself the *avodah* — the divine service — that defines his role. The fast is not intrinsically lacking in religious value; the problem is what it costs. And what it costs here is something more important.

This brings the discussion full circle. The *ta'anit yachid* undertaken as a private religious practice — not as part of a *ta'anit tzibbur*, not as part of the communal cry to God in times of drought, but as a self-standing act of self-denial — is viewed here with considerable skepticism, and in the case of a *talmid chacham*, with outright prohibition. The *yachidim* of this *masechet* who fast before the community reaches a crisis are fasting for the *tzibbur*, not for themselves. The fast that turns inward, that becomes a project of personal spiritual achievement separate from communal need, is the one that Shmuel calls sinful — the mirror image, in its way, of the earlier critique of the person who insulates himself from the community's suffering in order to indulge.
