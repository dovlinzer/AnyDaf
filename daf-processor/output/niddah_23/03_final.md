# Niddah 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Animal-like Fetus…

### Yoledet Status

The entirety of this *perek* is concerned with the question of when a woman acquires the *tumah* of a *yoledet* — one who has given birth. This *tumah* is of practical relevance even today, since it carries the same prohibition of conjugal relations as *tumah* of *niddah*. The discussion encompasses not only live births but also miscarriages: a child need not be viable to generate this status, but the fetus must meet certain halakhic criteria.

The *perek* has already examined at length whether the expulsion of matter from the uterus necessarily involves blood, and whether the manner in which blood exits the body affects its halakhic character. We now turn to the central question: what constitutes a fetus, *ולד*, in the first place — specifically, whether the *fetus* must bear human appearance in order for the woman to be defined as having given birth.

### Mishna: Animal Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה וְעוֹף — וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ מִצּוּרַת אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a woman who discharges tissue in the form of a type of domesticated animal, undomesticated animal, or bird, whether of a kosher or non-kosher species, if it is male, the woman observes the periods of impurity and purity for a woman who gives birth to a male. If it is female, the woman observes the periods of impurity and purity for a woman who gives birth to a female. And if its sex is unknown, the woman observes the strictures of a woman who gave birth to both a male and a female. This is the statement of **Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The son is not exempted from the requirement of redemption from a priest **unless** it follows the birth of a fetus **that takes the form of a person.**

The *mishna* presents the following case: *hamapelet kemin behemah* — a woman who miscarries something that resembles a *behemah*, a domesticated animal. Rabbi Meir holds that even this counts as a *vlad* and renders the woman *tamei* as a *yoledet*. The *chachamim* disagree. Notably, the *mishna* specifies "looks like" a *behemah* — the language of appearance, not of actual nature. For the moment, the most natural reading is that this is a human fetus that, for some reason, failed to take human form. The question is one of *tzurah*, appearance, not of *mahut*, essential nature — analogous to the *gemara*'s treatment of blood: even if something is biologically blood, if it lacks the requisite color, it may not carry the halakhic status of *niddah* blood. The *mishna* may be operating in precisely that vein: this is a human fetus, but its failure to appear human may exempt the woman from the laws of *yoledet*.

## R. Meir & Yetzirah

### Yetzira Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶמְרָה בּוֹ יְצִירָה כְּאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir,** that a woman who discharges a fetus in the form of an animal has the same status as one who discharges a fetus with human form or gives birth to a human? It is **since** the Torah uses a similar formulation in the two cases, as a term of **formation is stated with regard to** the creation of these types of animals, in the verse: "And out of the ground the Lord God formed every animal of the field, and every fowl of the air" (Genesis 2:19), **just as** it is stated with regard to the creation of **man:** "And the Lord God formed man" (Genesis 2:7).

The *gemara* opens by asking: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Meir* — what is Rabbi Meir's reason? The answer, cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, is a *gezerah shavah* based on the verb *yetzira* — divine fashioning. The *gemara* notes that when the Torah describes God's creation of animals, it uses the same verbal root as it uses for the creation of humans. This shared linguistic form, Rabbi Meir contends, places certain animals in a category sufficiently analogous to humanity that a fetus resembling such an animal retains the halakhic status of a human *vlad*. Fish, however, are excluded: the Torah's description of aquatic creation uses the verb *yishretzu* — "let the waters teem" — and never employs the root *yatzar*. Birds are included, as we will see, because the relevant verse in *Bereishit* chapter 2 uses *vayitzer* for them as well.

### Bereishit Verbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִלָּא מֵעַתָּה, הַמַּפֶּלֶת דְּמוּת תַּנִּין, תְּהֵא אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, הוֹאִיל (ונאמר) [וְנֶאֶמְרָה] בּוֹ יְצִירָה כְּאָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּבְרָא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הַתַּנִּינִים הַגְּדוֹלִים״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then with regard to a woman **who discharges** an item with **the form of a sea monster, its mother should be impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth, since** the concept of **formation is stated with regard to** its creation, **just as** it is stated with regard to the creation of **man. As it is stated: "And God created the great sea monsters"** (Genesis 1:21).

To understand the argument precisely, one must survey the relevant verses across both chapters of *Bereishit*. In chapter 1, the Torah uses different verbs for each category of creation: *yishretzu* for fish, *vaya'as* for land animals, and *vayivra* for humans. These are three distinct verbs for three distinct categories — no basis for linguistic linkage between humans and animals. But chapter 2 introduces a new description: *vayitzer Hashem Elokim min ha'adamah kol chayat hasadeh ve'et kol of hashamayim* — "God fashioned from the ground every animal of the field and every bird of the sky." This same root, *vayitzer*, appears earlier in that chapter in the account of the creation of the human being. Thus, in the second *perek*, the Torah uses *vayitzer* both for the human and for the land animals and birds — the very same verb, which Rabbi Meir reads as a halakhically meaningful link.

## Gezerah Shavah Rules

### Sea Monsters Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: דָּנִין יְצִירָה מִיצִירָה, וְאֵין דָּנִין בְּרִיאָה מִיצִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **One derives** *halakhot* of a matter with regard to which **formation** is stated by means of a verbal analogy **from** another matter with regard to which **formation** is stated, **but one does not derive** *halakhot* of a matter with regard to which **creation** is stated **from** a matter with regard to which **formation** is stated.

The *gemara* immediately presses: if the argument rests on a shared verb, why not extend the ruling to a case where a woman miscarries something resembling a *tannin*, a great sea creature? The verse in chapter 1 reads *vayivra Elokim et hatanninim hagedolim* — "God created the great sea monsters" — and the same root *bara* appears in the verse describing the creation of the human: *vayivra Elokim et ha'adam*. So there is a shared verb between sea monsters and humans as well. Why should the *gezerah shavah* not extend there?

The *gemara* answers: *danin yetzirah mi-yetzirah ve-ein danin briah mi-yetzirah* — we connect *yetzira* to *yetzira*, and we do not connect *briah* to *yetzira*. In other words, the preferred linkage is between two instances of the same verb in its most precise form. One might object: does it not say *vayivra* by humans as well? Indeed it does. The *gemara* therefore acknowledges that there are actually two verbs associated with humans in the Torah — *vayivra* in chapter 1 and *vayitzer* in chapter 2 — and similarly two verbs associated with sea monsters: *vayivra* in chapter 1 and, as the *gemara* initially suggests, possibly *vayitzer* via the verse mentioning *remes ha'adamah*. But the *gemara* refutes this: the *remes* mentioned in connection with *vayitzer* refers to land creatures, and sea monsters are not land animals. So the sea monsters have only one verb — *vayivra* — and that verb is not free.

### Mufneh Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? הָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשָׁב הַכֹּהֵן״ ״וּבָא הַכֹּהֵן״ — זוֹ הִיא שִׁיבָה זוֹ הִיא בִּיאָה! וְעוֹד, נִגְמַר בְּרִיאָה מִבְּרִיאָה, דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּבְרָא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הָאָדָם בְּצַלְמוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What difference is** there between formation and creation? A verbal analogy can be drawn between different words with similar meanings. For example, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a verbal analogy with regard to leprosy of houses. The verse states: **"And the priest shall return [*veshav*]** on the seventh day" (Leviticus 14:39), and another verse with regard to the priest's visit seven days later states: **"And the priest shall come [*uva*]** and look" (Leviticus 14:44). **This returning** and **this coming** have the same meaning, and one can therefore derive by verbal analogy that the *halakha* that applies if the leprosy had spread at the conclusion of the first week applies if it had spread again by the end of the following week. **And furthermore,** the *halakha* of a woman who discharges an item similar to a sea monster, with regard to which **creation** is stated, can be **derived** by a different verbal analogy **from** the *halakha* of human offspring, since here it also states **creation, as it is written: "And God created man in His own image"** (Genesis 1:27).

This brings the *gemara* to a general methodological discussion about the limits of *gezerah shavah*. The principle at stake is *mufneh* — whether the word used to establish the *gezerah shavah* is "free," i.e., not already fully occupied by its role in the verse. The *gemara* introduces three tiers:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: ״וַיִּבְרָא״ לְגוּפֵיהּ, ״וַיִּיצֶר״ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי, וְדָנִין יְצִירָה מִיצִירָה. אַדְּרַבָּה: ״וַיִּיצֶר״ לְגוּפֵיהּ, ״וַיִּבְרָא״ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי, וְדָנִין בְּרִיאָה מִבְּרִיאָה! אֶלָּא: ״וַיִּיצֶר״ מוּפְנֶה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין, מוּפְנֶה גַּבֵּי אָדָם וּמוּפְנֶה גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, ״וַיִּבְרָא״ גַּבֵּי אָדָם מוּפְנֶה, גַּבֵּי תַּנִּינִים אֵינוֹ מוּפְנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: The verse **"And** God **created** man" is necessary **to** teach the matter **itself,** i.e., the creation of man. By contrast, the term **"And** the Lord God **formed** man," serves **to render it free,** i.e., the mention of the formation of man is superfluous in its context and was stated for the purpose of establishing a verbal analogy. **And** therefore **one derives** the *halakhot* of animals, with regard to which **formation** is stated, **from** the *halakhot* of man, with regard to which **formation** is stated. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **On the contrary,** one can say that the verse **"And** the Lord God **formed** man" was stated **to** teach the matter **itself,** whereas the term **"And** God **created** man" serves **to render it free; and** therefore **one derives** the *halakhot* of sea monsters, with regard to which **creation** is stated, **from** the *halakhot* of man, with regard to which **creation** is stated. The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the reason it is animals and not sea monsters that are compared to man is that the term **"and…formed"** is **free on both sides,** i.e., **it is free with regard to man and it is free with regard to animals.** By contrast, the term **"and…created" is free with regard to man,** but **it is not free with regard to sea monsters.**

The strongest form is *mufneh mishnei tzedadim* — the word is free on both sides, meaning the linking term in each of the two source passages can be considered extra, since another word is doing the primary work of each verse. In such a case, one can say the shared word was placed there precisely to create a halakhic link.

The intermediate form is *mufneh mitzad echad* — one of the two instances of the linking term is extra, the other is not.

The weakest form is when neither instance is extra — both words are doing their ordinary work in their respective verses, and they simply happen to be the same word.

The *gemara* applies this framework here. The word *vayitzer* appears twice in connection with the cases under discussion: once by humans in chapter 2, and once by the animals and birds in chapter 2. Is either of those instances free? By humans: the verse in chapter 1 already has *vayivra*, so *vayitzer* in chapter 2 can be argued to be extra. By animals: the verse in chapter 1 already has *vaya'as*, so *vayitzer* in chapter 2 can be argued to be extra there as well. Both instances of *vayitzer* are therefore candidates to be considered free — *mufneh mishnei tzedadim*. By contrast, the word *vayivra* appears by the sea monsters in chapter 1 with no competing verb for that category, so it is not free — *eino mufneh*.

### Yishmael Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מוּפְנֶה גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב ״וַיַּעַשׂ אֱלֹהִים אֶת חַיַּת הָאָרֶץ״, וּכְתִיב ״וַיִּיצֶר [ה׳] אֱלֹהִים מִן הָאֲדָמָה כׇּל חַיַּת הַשָּׂדֶה״, גַּבֵּי תַּנִּין נָמֵי אִפְּנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֶה, דִּכְתִיב ״וְאֵת כׇּל רֶמֶשׂ הָאֲדָמָה״, וּכְתִיב ״וַיִּבְרָא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הַתַּנִּינִים הַגְּדוֹלִים״! ״רֶמֶשׂ״ דִּכְתִיב הָתָם, דְּיַבָּשָׁה הוּא. וּמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ בֵּין מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד לְמוּפְנֶה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason that the term "and…formed" that appears **with regard to animals** is considered **free? If we say** it is due to the fact **that it is written: "And God made the animals of the earth** after its kind, and the cattle after their kind, and everything that creeps on the ground after its kind" (Genesis 1:25), **and it is** similarly **written: "And out of the ground the Lord God formed every animal of the field"** (Genesis 2:19), and therefore this verse is superfluous, but **with regard to** the **sea monster as well,** the expression "and…created" is **free, as it is written:** "And God made…**and everything that creeps on the ground** after its kind" (Genesis 1:25), **and it is** also **written: "And God created the great sea monsters."** Consequently, the term "and…created" is also free on both sides of the verbal analogy. The Gemara answers: The **creeping animal that is written there is** referring to creeping animals **of the land,** not of the sea. Therefore, the term: "And…created," stated with regard to sea monsters is not superfluous. The Gemara asks: **But what difference is** there **between** a verbal analogy that is **free on one side and** a verbal analogy that is **free on both sides?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כׇּל גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מוּפְנָה כׇּל עִיקָּר — אֵין לְמֵדִין הֵימֶנָּה. מוּפְנָה מִצַּד אֶחָד, לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל — לְמֵדִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין, לְרַבָּנַן — לְמֵדִין וּמְשִׁיבִין. מוּפְנָה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל — לְמֵדִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The **difference** is with regard to that **which Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael,** with regard to the exegetical principle of verbal analogy: With regard to **any verbal analogy that is not free at all, one cannot derive** *halakhot* **from it.** If the verbal analogy is **free on one side, according to Rabbi Yishmael one can derive** *halakhot* from it, **and one cannot refute** it through logic, even if there are valid counterarguments. **According to the Rabbis, one can derive** *halakhot* from it, **but one can** also **refute** it logically if there are grounds to distinguish between the two cases. If a verbal analogy is **free on both sides, everyone agrees** that **one can derive** *halakhot* from it **and one cannot refute** it logically.

The *gemara* then cites a tradition transmitted by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, regarding the practical difference between these tiers. According to Rabbi Yishmael: if neither word is free (*eino mufneh kol ikar*), one cannot make the *gezerah shavah* at all. If one word is free (*mufneh mitzad echad*), one may derive from it and the derivation is not subject to refutation. The *Rabbanan* — presumably Rabbi Akiva's school — take a stricter position: even when one word is free, the *gezerah shavah* may be made but remains vulnerable to logical critique (*meshivin*). Only when both words are free do all opinions agree that the derivation is airtight and no challenge can overturn it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין מוּפְנָה מִצַּד אֶחָד לְמוּפְנָה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין? נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ, דְּהֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מוּפְנָה מִצַּד אֶחָד וּמוּפְנָה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין — שָׁבְקִינַן מוּפְנָה מִצַּד אֶחָד וְיָלְפִינַן מוּפְנָה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין. וּלְהָכִי אַפְנְיֵהּ רַחֲמָנָא לִבְהֵמָה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִגְמַר מִן מוּפְנָה מִצַּד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Yishmael, what** difference **is there between** a verbal analogy that is **free on one side and** a verbal analogy that is **free on both sides?** In both cases, he holds that one can derive *halakhot* from it and one cannot refute it. The Gemara answers: He holds that the **difference** is **that** in a case **where there** are two mutually exclusive verbal analogies, one that is **free on one side and** one that is **free on both sides, we disregard** the analogy that is **free on one side, and derive** the *halakha* from the one that is **free on both sides. And** it is **for this** reason that **the Merciful One rendered** the verbal analogy between **animal** and man **free on both sides, so that one would not derive** the *halakha* **from** the verbal analogy between sea monster and man, which is **free on** only **one side.**

A parallel tradition, attributed by Rav Acha the son of Rava to Rabbi Eliezer, presents what the *gemara* calls a *lenient* formulation — not lenient in the practical legal sense, but lenient in the sense of setting a lower threshold for making the *gezerah shavah* at all:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְקוּלָּא: כׇּל גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מוּפְנָה כׇּל עִיקָּר — לְמֵדִין וּמְשִׁיבִין, מוּפְנָה מִצַּד אֶחָד — לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לְמֵדִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין, לְרַבָּנַן לְמֵדִין וּמְשִׁיבִין, מוּפְנָה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לְמֵדִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, teaches in the name of Rabbi Elazar** a more **lenient** version of the aforementioned principle of exegesis of verbal analogy: With regard to **any verbal analogy that is not free at all, one can derive** *halakhot* from it, **but one can** also **refute** it logically. If the verbal analogy is **free on one side, according to Rabbi Yishmael one can derive** *halakhot* from it, **and one cannot refute** it. **According to the Rabbis, one can derive** *halakhot* from it, **but one can** also **refute** it. If the verbal analogy is **free on both sides, everyone agrees** that **one can derive** *halakhot* from it **and one cannot refute** it.

According to this version: even when neither word is free, one may still make the *gezerah shavah*, but it remains open to refutation. When one word is free, Rabbi Yishmael agrees it is airtight; the *Rabbanan* still permit critique. When both words are free, all agree it is watertight. The difference between the two traditions, then, concerns only the case where neither word is free: the first tradition forbids the *gezerah shavah* entirely; the second permits it, subject to challenge.

It is worth pausing over what this extended discussion reveals. The *gemara* clearly recognizes that *gezerah shavah* is among the most formally arbitrary of the thirteen hermeneutical principles: a word appears in one context, the same word appears in another, and a legal consequence is transferred between them. To restrain this arbitrariness, the *gemara* imposes a hierarchy of legitimacy. The Ramban elsewhere observes that the precise determination of which *gezerah shavah* is operative was never entirely fixed — the tradition was not that specific — and so the *gemara* engages in these comparative evaluations. But the underlying point stands: *gezerah shavah* requires some signal, beyond mere coincidence of vocabulary, that the Torah intended a link.

### Airtight Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי פִּירְכָא אִיכָּא? מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְאָדָם, שֶׁכֵּן מִטַּמֵּא מֵחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And here,** with regard to the verbal analogy between man and sea monster, which was rejected because it is free on only one side, **what** logical **refutation is there** on account of which this verbal analogy is rejected? The Gemara answers: The verbal analogy between man and sea monster is rejected **because it can be refuted** as follows: **What** is unique **about man?** Man is unique **in that** a person can **become impure while he is alive,** unlike an animal, which can become impure only after it dies, or a sea monster, which cannot become impure at all.

The *gemara* now concludes: *ve-lahachi afneih Rachmana le-vehema mishnei tzedadim* — the reason the Torah made the word *vayitzer* available at both ends, by the humans and by the animals, is precisely to indicate that the proper linkage is between those two categories, and not between humans and sea monsters via *vayivra*. When both words are free, the *gezerah shavah* is watertight; no logical objection may be raised against it.

What logical objection might otherwise have been raised? The *gemara* suggests: *mah le-adam shekein nitchayev be-mitzvot* — one might distinguish humans from animals on the grounds that humans are subject to *mitzvot*, and consequently to a broader regime of *tumah* — for a living animal cannot become *tamei*, while a living human can. Therefore, the laws of *tumah* associated with childbirth might not transfer to an animal-shaped fetus. But since the *gezerah shavah* is *mufneh mishnei tzedadim*, this critique is not admitted. The verse itself has signaled the link, and the link holds regardless of this potential distinction.

The *gemara* thus establishes Rabbi Meir's position: a woman who miscarries a fetus with the appearance of a mammal or bird is nonetheless a *yoledet* for purposes of *tumah*. Fish are excluded because the Torah never uses the root *yatzar* for them. Sea monsters are excluded because the only available verb for them, *vayivra*, is not free. The connection is built entirely on *vayitzer* — a word the Torah went out of its way to use in two categories, rendering it free in both, and thereby creating an airtight halakhic link.

## Facial Features Test

### Eyes Similarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר — הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶמְרָה בּוֹ ״יְצִירָה״ כְּאָדָם. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה הַמַּפֶּלֶת דְּמוּת הַר — אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כִּי הִנֵּה יוֹצֵר הָרִים וּבוֹרֵא רוּחַ״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַר מִי קָא מַפְּלָה? אֶבֶן הִיא דְּקָא מַפְּלָה, הָהוּא ״גּוּשׁ״ אִיקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir,** that a woman who discharges an item similar to a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird is impure: It is **because formation is stated with regard to** the creation of these animals, **just as** it is stated with regard to the creation of **man.** **Rabbi Ami said to him: If that is so,** then in the case of a woman **who discharges** an item that has **the shape of a mountain, its mother** should be **impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth, as it is stated** with regard to the creation of mountains: **"For He Who forms the mountains and creates the wind"** (Amos 4:13). Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: Does she discharge a mountain?** The discharged item cannot possibly be that large. **It is** an item with the form of **a stone that she discharges,** and **that is called a clod,** not a mountain.

A parallel tradition, cited by Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, offers a different rationale for Rabbi Meir's position. The reason that a fetus resembling an animal falls under the laws of *yoledet*, according to this account, is not primarily the shared verb in scripture but rather a physical fact: *eineihem domot ke-shel adam* — the eyes of such animals resemble those of a human being. Rashi understands this to mean specifically that the pupil is round, in contrast to fish eyes which have a different configuration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה הַמַּפֶּלֶת רוּחַ — תְּהֵא אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶמְרָה בּוֹ ״בְּרִיאָה״ כְּאָדָם, דִּכְתִיב ״וּבוֹרֵא רוּחַ״? וְכִי תֵימָא לֹא מוּפְנֶה, מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב ״יוֹצֵר הָרִים וְרוּחַ״, וּכְתִיב ״וּבוֹרֵא רוּחַ״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דָּנִין דִבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, וְאֵין דָּנִין דִבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִדִּבְרֵי קַבָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ami further inquired: **If that is so,** in the case of a woman **who discharges** an item having **an amorphous form [*ruaḥ*], its mother should be impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth, since creation is stated with regard to it, just as** it is stated with regard to **man, as it is written: "And creates the wind [*ruaḥ*]." And if you would say** that no verbal analogy can be drawn here, because the verse **is not free,** i.e., it is not superfluous, as it is necessary to recount the creation of the wind, that is not so. Rabbi Ami explains: **From** the fact **that** the verse **could have written: Who forms the mountains and the wind, and** instead **it is written:** "Who forms the mountains **and creates the wind," conclude from it** that the superfluous word "creates" serves **to render it free** for drawing a verbal analogy between *ruaḥ* and man. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to** Rabbi Ami: **One derives matters** that are stated in the **Torah from matters** that are stated in the **Torah,** i.e., from verses in the Torah, **but one does not derive matters** that are stated in the **Torah from the words of the tradition,** i.e., verses in the Prophets or the Writings, such as the verse in Amos.

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge from another direction. Rav Ami asks: if it resembles a *tannin*, would the mother be *tamei*? We have already dealt with that. The *gemara* then asks a stranger question: what if the fetus resembles a mountain? After all, the verse says *ki hinei yotzeir harim* — God fashions mountains — using the root *yatzar*. Ought we then derive that a mountain-shaped fetus renders the mother a *yoledet*? The response is direct: nothing that emerges from a woman's body would actually resemble a mountain; at best it might be described as a lump of earth. The category simply does not apply.

The *gemara* then asks: what if she aborts *ruach* — wind, or air? In context, this refers to the phenomenon where the embryonic sac appears filled with air rather than substance. The verse *uvorei ruach* — "and He creates wind" — uses the root *bara*, which might arguably be considered extra since the verse could have said *yotzeir harim ve-ruach* without a separate verb. Hence *borei ruach* could be free and available for a *gezerah shavah*. The *gemara* rejects this: *donin divrei Torah mi-divrei Torah ve-ein donin divrei Torah mi-divrei Kabbalah* — one derives *Torah* law from other books of *Torah*, meaning from the *Chumash*, and does not derive it from the later books of *Nevi'im* and *Ketuvim*. The verse about God fashioning mountains and creating wind is from the prophets, and cannot serve as the basis for a *gezerah shavah* in this context.

One might consider whether these questions — the mountain, the wind — were posed as genuine legal inquiries or as *reductio ad absurdum* challenges, designed to press the limits of a purely textual argument. If all that is required is a shared verbal root, the conclusions become strange indeed. The *gemara*, however, treats the objections seriously and answers them on their own terms. Nevertheless, the cumulative weight of these questions suggests that behind Rabbi Meir's position there may be an additional implicit premise: that the physical resemblance between certain animals and humans is not merely coincidental, but reflects a genuine proximity of form that makes the halakhic equation defensible. The verse serves as a peg, but the intuition about appearance does real work.

### Snake Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, הַמַּפֶּלֶת דְּמוּת נָחָשׁ תְּהֵא אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, הוֹאִיל וְגַלְגַּל עֵינוֹ עָגוֹל כְּשֶׁל אָדָם! וְכִי תֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, לִיתְנֵי נָחָשׁ! אִי תְּנָא נָחָשׁ, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּנָחָשׁ הוּא דִּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּלָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״יְצִירָה״, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה — לָא פְּלִיגִי, דִּכְתִיבָא (ביה) [בְּיהוּ] ״יְצִירָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** then in the case of a woman **who discharges** an item that has **the form of a snake, its mother should** likewise **be impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth, since the pupil of** a snake **is round, like** that **of a human. And if you would say** that **indeed,** this is the *halakha*, then **let** the mishna **teach** this case of a woman who discharges an item that has the form of **a snake** among the other cases where the woman discharges an item of an unusual form. The Gemara explains: **If** the mishna had **taught** the case of **a snake, I would say** that it **is** only **in** the case of a woman who discharges an item having the form of **a snake that the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Meir** and rule that the woman is not impure, **as** a term of **formation is not written with regard to** the creation of the snake. **But** with regard to a woman who discharges an item having the form of **a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal, they do not disagree** with Rabbi Meir, **as** the concept of **formation is written with regard to them.**

Returning to the physical-appearance rationale of Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba: if the critical feature is that the eyes resemble human eyes, one might ask why the *mishna* does not include the case of a fetus resembling a snake. After all, the *gemara* asserts that the pupil of a snake's eye is round, just like that of a human. The *gemara* answers this omission purposefully. Had the *mishna* mentioned snakes, one might have concluded that the *rabbanan* dispute Rabbi Meir's position *specifically* in the case of snakes — precisely because the Torah does not use the root *yatzar* for snakes — and that by *behemah* and *chayah*, where *yetzira* is written, even the *chachamim* might concede. By omitting the snake case, the *mishna* ensures that the reader understands the *rabbanan* dispute Rabbi Meir across the board: whenever the fetus lacks a human appearance, regardless of which animal it resembles, the *chachamim* do not consider it a *vlad*.

### Eye Anatomy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא גַּבֵּי מוּמִין קָתָנֵי לַהּ: אֶת שֶׁגַּלְגַּל עֵינוֹ עָגוֹל כְּשֶׁל אָדָם! לָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא בְּאוּכָּמָא, הָא בְּצִירְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But with regard to** the *halakhot* of **blemishes** that render the slaughter of a firstborn animal permitted, **it is taught** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 40a) that an animal **whose pupil is round like** that **of a human** is considered blemished. Evidently, the eyes of animals are dissimilar to those of humans. The Gemara answers that it is **not difficult; this** statement, that the eyes of animals are similar to those of humans, is referring **to** the **pupil,** and **that** statement, that the eyes of animals are not similar to those of humans, is referring **to** the entire eyeball in the **socket.**

A tension now arises from a source in *Bechorot*: if an animal's eye is round like a human eye, that is considered a *mum*, a blemish, in the animal. This implies that normally an animal's eye does not look like a human eye — which seems to contradict the claim that animal eyes generally resemble human eyes. The *gemara* resolves this: *ha be'uchma, ha be-tziraya* — one statement refers to the pupil (*the black*), the other to the socket or setting of the eye. The pupil of many animals does resemble a human pupil in being round; it is the overall shape and setting of the eye within the face that differs. The *mum* in *Bechorot* refers to an animal whose entire eye — the socket, the shape — looks human, which is abnormal. The claim about resemblance used by Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba concerns only the pupil.

### Eye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר — הוֹאִיל וְעֵינֵיהֶם הוֹלְכוֹת לִפְנֵיהֶם כְּשֶׁל אָדָם. וַהֲרֵי עוֹף, דְּאֵין עֵינָיו הוֹלְכוֹת לְפָנָיו, וְקָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּטָמֵא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּקַרְיָא וְקִיפוֹפָא, וּבִשְׁאָר עוֹפוֹת — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai said: This is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir:** It is **because the eyes of** these animals **are fixed in the front of** their heads **like** those **of a human,** unlike the eyes of birds and snakes, a woman who discharges an item of that kind is impure. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** there is the case of a woman who discharges an item similar to **a bird, whose eyes are not fixed in the front of** its head, **and** nevertheless **Rabbi Meir says that** the woman **is impure.** This apparently contradicts Rabbi Yannai's explanation. **Abaye said:** Rabbi Meir is referring **to** the **little owl [*bekarya*] and** the **great owl [*vekifofa*],** whose eyes are fixed in the front of their heads, **but in** the case of a woman who discharges any of the **other** species of **birds,** Rabbi Meir does **not** deem her impure.

Rabbi Yannai offers yet another formulation of Rabbi Meir's reasoning: *eineihem holchot le-faneihem ke-shel adam* — their eyes are directed forward, as with humans, rather than positioned on the sides of the head. This forward-facing orientation is characteristic of predators and certain mammals, and it creates a face that resembles the human face in a recognizable way. Fish, by contrast, have eyes on the sides of the head and a correspondingly different facial structure.

The *gemara* raises a difficulty with this rationale as applied to birds. If the criterion is that the eyes face forward, then most birds — whose eyes are typically set on the sides of their heads — would not qualify. Yet the *mishna* includes birds in the categories that Rabbi Meir subjects to his ruling. How can that be? The *gemara* clarifies: when Rabbi Meir includes *of*, birds, he does not mean all birds. He refers specifically to owls — the *bakiya* and *kipufa* — whose faces are strikingly human-like, with forward-facing eyes and rounded facial structures. Other birds would not qualify even according to Rabbi Meir.

### Compromise View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר בִּבְהֵמָה וְחַיָּה, וְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים בְּעוֹפוֹת. מַאי עוֹפוֹת? אִילֵּימָא בְּקַרְיָא וְקִיפוֹפָא, מַאי שְׁנָא בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה דְּעֵינֵיהֶן הוֹלְכוֹת לִפְנֵיהֶן כְּשֶׁל אָדָם? קַרְיָא וְקִיפוֹפָא נָמֵי! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּשְׁאָר עוֹפוֹת. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר פְּלִיג בִּשְׁאָר עוֹפוֹת! חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר — נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר בִּבְהֵמָה וְחַיָּה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְקַרְיָא וְקִיפוֹפָא, וְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים בִּשְׁאָר עוֹפוֹת, שֶׁאַף רַבִּי מֵאִיר לֹא נֶחְלַק עִמָּהֶם אֶלָּא בְּקַרְיָא וְקִיפוֹפָא, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר עוֹפוֹת — מוֹדֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this answer from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says: The statement of Rabbi Meir seems** correct **in** the case of a woman who discharges the form of **a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal, and the statement of the Rabbis** seems correct **in** the case of **birds.** The Gemara asks: To **what birds** is Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus referring? **If we say** he is referring **to** the **little owl and** the **great owl, what is the difference** between this case and the cases of **a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal,** with regard to which Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus accepts the opinion of Rabbi Meir? If the key factor is **that their eyes are fixed in the front of** their heads **like** those **of a human,** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus should accept the opinion of Rabbi Meir in the case of **a little owl or a great owl as well,** as their eyes are also fixed in the front of their heads. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that when Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says that he does not accept the opinion of Rabbi Meir, he is referring **to** the **other** species of **birds. From the fact** that it is necessary for Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus to reject Rabbi Meir's opinion in those cases, it may be concluded that **Rabbi Meir** himself **disagrees** with the Rabbis **with regard to** the **other** species of **birds** as well, despite the fact that their eyes are not fixed in the front of their heads. The Gemara explains that the *baraita* **is incomplete, and this** is what **it is teaching: Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says: The statement of Rabbi Meir seems** correct **in** the case of **a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal, and the same is true with regard to a little owl or a great owl. And the statement of the Rabbis** appears correct even to Rabbi Meir **with regard to** the **other** species of **birds.** The reason is **that even Rabbi Meir** agrees that if a woman discharges an item that has the form of one of the other species of birds, she is not impure, i.e., he **disagrees with them only with regard to a little owl or a great owl, but he concedes to** their opinion **with regard to** the **other** species of **birds.**

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel articulates a middle position. He says: *nirin divrei Rabbi Meir bi-vehemah ve-chayah* — the reasoning of Rabbi Meir appears compelling when it comes to land mammals; *ve-divrei Chachamim be-ofot* — while the position of the *chachamim* seems more persuasive when it comes to birds. The *gemara* asks: what does he mean by birds? If he means owls — creatures with notably human-like faces — why would he concede that case to the *chachamim*? Their faces are as human as those of many mammals. The *gemara* therefore concludes that Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel must be ruling against Rabbi Meir specifically in the case of *other* birds, those whose eyes are on the sides. From this it follows that Rabbi Meir himself must have applied his ruling even to non-owl birds — otherwise there would be no dispute to resolve in that category.

The *gemara* therefore emends the *braita*: *chasuri mechasra* — the text is incomplete. The correct reading is that Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel accepts Rabbi Meir's position for land mammals *and* for owls, and accepts the *chachamim*'s position for all other birds. And this also implies, the *gemara* concludes, that Rabbi Meir himself actually did not extend his ruling to non-owl birds — he agreed with the *chachamim* there — so the only genuine dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* was with respect to owls.

What is striking about this section is the way in which the two distinct rationales — the textual *gezerah shavah* based on *vayitzer*, and the physical rationale based on facial resemblance — lead to different practical outcomes. The textual argument groups all birds together with mammals under the shared verb. The physical argument introduces fine-grained distinctions: owls yes, other birds no; perhaps even the position of the eyes matters. The *gemara* does not force a single answer, but its ultimate resolution — that the actual halakhic dispute is limited to owls — reflects a strong pull toward the appearance-based rationale.

## Animal or Animal-Like?

### Braita Wording

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק: הַמַּפֶּלֶת מִין בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר — וָלָד, וּלְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים — אֵינוֹ וָלָד. בְּעוֹפוֹת — תִּיבָּדֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof for Abaye's claim that Rabbi Meir differentiates between an owl and other species of birds, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says:** In the case of a woman **who discharges** an item that has the form of **a type of domesticated animal or undomesticated animal, according to the statement of Rabbi Meir** it has the halakhic status of a full-fledged **offspring, and according to the statement of the Rabbis,** it does **not** have the status of a full-fledged **offspring. In** the case of a woman who discharges an item that has the form of **birds, it must be examined.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* that generates significant interpretive difficulty. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok states: *hamapelet min behemah va-chayah* — a woman who miscarries a *behemah* or a *chayah*. The language is troubling. Earlier, the *mishna* consistently said *kemin behemah* — "resembling a *behemah*." Here the *braita* drops the word *kemin* and says simply *min behemah* — a *behemah* itself. According to Rabbi Meir this constitutes a *vlad*; according to the *chachamim* it does not.

The *braita* continues: when it comes to birds, *tibbadek* — one must examine. This implies that Rabbi Meir's ruling applies to some birds and not others, precisely as the *gemara* concluded in the previous section: owls yes, others no. The *gemara* notes that this *tibbadek* is being evaluated according to Rabbi Meir — whether the particular bird-like form triggers his ruling — which aligns with the conclusion that Rabbi Meir distinguishes even within birds, accepting owls and rejecting others.

The more pressing question remains the language of the *braita* itself. Is it using *behemah* as shorthand for "something that looks like a *behemah*," or is it genuinely positing that a *behemah* — an actual animal — might be found in a woman's womb? One might argue that the *gemara* simply abbreviates, replacing the full *mishna* language with the shorter form. Yet the questions the *gemara* proceeds to ask make it increasingly difficult to sustain that reading.

### Kiddushin Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר בְּהֵמָה בִּמְעֵי אִשָּׁה וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, קִבֵּל בָּהּ אָבִיהָ קִידּוּשִׁין מַהוּ? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְאִיתְּסוֹרֵי בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya asked Rabbi Zeira: According to Rabbi Meir, who said** that **an animal in the womb of a woman is** considered **a full-fledged offspring, what is** the *halakha* in a case where it is a female, and **her father accepted betrothal for her,** i.e., he married her off by accepting betrothal money from a man, or a document of betrothal? Is such a betrothal valid? Rabbi Yirmeya elaborated: **What** practical **difference is** there whether it is valid? The difference is **with regard to** whether it is **prohibited** for the man **to** marry **her sister.** If the betrothal is valid, it is prohibited for the husband to marry her sister, as one may not marry his wife's sister.

Rav Yirmiyah poses a question to Rabbi Zeira: according to Rabbi Meir, who says that *behemah bi-mei isha vlad me'alya hu* — an animal in the mother's womb is a full-fledged *vlad* — if the father accepted *kiddushin* on behalf of this fetus, assuming the fetus is a daughter, would the *kiddushin* be valid? This question presupposes the general rule that a father may betroth his daughter before she is born if the fetus is viable. The question is whether this particular fetus — the *behemah*-like thing — qualifies.

The language *behemah bi-mei isha* — an animal in the mother's womb — is no longer easily read as mere shorthand. The *gemara* appears to be genuinely entertaining the possibility that the entity in question has some form of animal status, and the question about *kiddushin* is probing what its human or non-human status implies in practice.

### Viable by Species

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּחָיֵי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא אֲמָרָהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא הוֹאִיל וּבְמִינוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים! אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: עַד כָּאן הֱבִיאוֹ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא לִידֵי גִּיחוּךְ, וְלֹא גִּחֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** such an offspring **can live?** This factor is important, as a man is prohibited from marrying his wife's sister only during his wife's lifetime. **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: Rabbi Meir said** that a woman who discharges an item that has the form of an animal is impure **only since** there are other animals **of its type** that can **live,** i.e., there are animals similar to the discharged item that do survive, but not that creature itself. **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says: Rabbi Yirmeya tried this hard to cause Rabbi Zeira to laugh, but he did not laugh.** In other words, Rabbi Yirmeya was not asking his question seriously.

The *gemara* responds: *le-meimra de-chayei* — does this imply the thing will actually live? But Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *lo amar Rabbi Meir ela ho'il u-mino mitkayem* — Rabbi Meir only included this in the category of *vlad* because its *species* — the actual animal it resembles — is viable. The fetus itself, however, is never viable. It does not actually live. Therefore, the question of *kiddushin* is moot: betrothal can only apply to a fetus that is itself potentially viable. A fetus that was never capable of living independently cannot be the subject of a valid *kiddushin*, regardless of what it looks like.

Several *Rishonim* press this point further. Even if the thing will not live, are there not other halakhic implications — for instance, does the man who effected the *kiddushin* become forbidden to the mother? They respond: when the *gemara* says it is not viable, it means that *kiddushin* is simply inapplicable from the outset. The entire framework of a father accepting *kiddushin* on behalf of an unborn daughter presupposes a viable fetus. Something that is by nature never viable is categorically outside that framework.

### Absurd Question

The passage now turns to an important interpretive moment.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Yaakov remarks: *ad kan hivi Rabbi Yirmiyah le-Rabbi Zeira li-yadei gichuch* — Rabbi Yirmiyah almost brought Rabbi Zeira to laughter — *ve-lo gachich* — but he did not laugh. The question was apparently intended to be amusing, or at minimum to be a *reductio* of sorts. But in which direction? Was the absurdity meant to highlight the strangeness of treating this entity as an animal — and thereby deflating that implication — or was the absurdity meant to press the outer edge of treating it as a human subject of *kiddushin*? The *gemara* does not say, and Rabbi Zeira's refusal to laugh suggests he took the question seriously rather than as a joke.

### Tosafot: Bestiality

*Tosafot* make explicit what the *gemara* leaves implicit. They note that the *gemara* could have raised an even sharper version of the question. If at the moment the *gemara* was entertaining the possibility that this creature could be born viable, one could have asked: if someone had sexual intercourse with it, would they be liable for *sekila* — execution — as one is liable for the act of *bi'ah im behemah*, relations with an animal? *Tosafot* thus confirm that the underlying tension in this passage is precisely the question of human versus animal status. On one side of the ledger: is *kiddushin* binding, treating the fetus as a human? On the other side: would sexual contact with it constitute bestiality, treating it as an animal? These two halakhic questions bracket the same conceptual uncertainty.

The *Rosh* engages the question from yet another angle, asking whether the passage could have raised the issue of *tumat met* — corpse impurity. Would this entity, once miscarried, generate the *tumah* of a human corpse? The *Rosh* in his *Tosafot* answers that this was never a real question: since Rabbi Meir considers the mother to be a *yoledet*, the fetus necessarily has the status of a human *vlad* and is *tamei* as a *nefal*, a miscarried human fetus. Its human status for purposes of *tumah* is settled. The uncertainty that generates the *gemara*'s questions lies elsewhere — in those areas of halakha where the animal-like form of the entity creates genuine ambiguity about its classification.

## Viability & Inheritance

### Bechorot List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא אֲמָרָהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא הוֹאִיל וּבְמִינוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים. אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּפְתִּי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וְעוֹף — וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ מִצּוּרַת אָדָם. וְהַמַּפֶּלֶת סַנְדָּל, אוֹ שִׁלְיָא, אוֹ שָׁפִיר מְרוּקָּם, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מְחוּתָּךְ — הַבָּא אַחֲרָיו בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה, וְאֵינוֹ בְּכוֹר לְכֹהֵן. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ דְּחָיֵי, הַבָּא אַחֲרָיו בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה מִי הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Rabbi Meir said** that a woman who discharges an item that has the form of an animal is impure **only since** there are animals **of its type** that can **live. Rav Yirmeya of Difti says: We, too, learn** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 46a) that the fetus of a woman that has the form of an animal cannot survive: In the case of a woman **who** had previously **discharged a fetus** with the appearance **similar to** that **of a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or bird** before giving birth to any children, and subsequently she gives birth to a son, the son is considered a firstborn with regard to the *halakhot* of inheritance, but he does not require redemption, as the fetus is considered a full-fledged offspring in that regard. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The son is not exempted from the requirement of redemption from a priest **unless** it follows the birth of a fetus **that takes the form of a person;** otherwise, it is not considered the offspring that "opens the womb" (Exodus 13:2), and the son requires redemption as a firstborn. **And** in the case of a woman **who discharges** a fetus in the form of **a sandal fish, or** from whom **an afterbirth or a gestational sac** in which **tissue developed** emerged, **or** who delivered a fetus **that emerged in pieces,** the son **that follows them is** considered **a firstborn with regard to inheritance but is not a firstborn with regard to** redemption from **a priest.** The Gemara explains the proof: **And if it enters your mind that** a fetus that has the form of an animal **can survive, is the** son **that follows it a firstborn with regard to inheritance?**

The *gemara* now introduces a *mishna* from *Bechorot* that provides a list of types of miscarriages with the following halakhic consequence: the child born after them is a *bechor* for inheritance — receiving a double portion — but is not a *bechor* for *pidyon haben*. Understanding this requires clarifying the two distinct categories. For *pidyon haben*, the operative criterion is *peter rechem* — literally the first to breach the womb. Whether that first-born will live is irrelevant; it is a question of priority of birth. For the double portion in inheritance, however, the criterion is that the first-born was *viable* — a living child that survived. A miscarriage of something previously viable displaces the next child from receiving the double portion, since technically there was already a first-born. But a fetus that was *never* viable — that could never have lived — does not count as a prior birth for purposes of inheritance, and so the subsequent child receives the double portion.

On this framework, there is a category of birth that simultaneously generates both consequences: the subsequent child is *bechor le-nachalah* — receives the double portion — because the prior fetus was never viable, yet is *not bechor la-Kohen* — does not require *pidyon haben* — because the prior fetus did breach the womb. This is the category described in the *mishna* from *Bechorot*: the *sandal*, the *shilya*, the *shafir merukam*, and the dismembered fetus. These are forms that establish the act of prior birth but were never genuinely viable.

### Never Viable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם דְּחָיֵי, וְשָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״רֵאשִׁית אוֹנוֹ״ — מִי שֶׁלִּבּוֹ דָּוֶה עָלָיו, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין לִבּוֹ דָּוֶה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said,** in rejection of this proof: **Actually,** it is possible **that** a fetus shaped like an animal **can survive; but it is different there,** with regard to inheritance. The son that follows such a fetus has the status of a firstborn, **as the verse states** with regard to the inheritance of a firstborn: "By giving him a double portion of all that he has; for he is **the first fruits of his strength [*ono*];** the right of the firstborn is his" (Deuteronomy 21:17). It is derived from the verse that the status of a firstborn applies only to a son **over whose** death a father would **mourn.** The word *ono* is interpreted homiletically based on its similarity to the word *onen*, acute mourner. **This** offspring that has the form of an animal is therefore **excluded, as** its father **would not mourn over its** death.

The *gemara* applies this framework to the case of the animal-like fetus. Rabbi Meir's own position, as stated in this *braita*, is that the animal-shaped fetus falls into exactly this category. It is a *vlad* — a miscarriage that constitutes a birth — and therefore the subsequent child is not *bechor la-Kohen* for *pidyon haben*. Yet Rabbi Meir also holds that it was *never* viable, and therefore the subsequent child *is* the *bechor le-nachalah* and receives the double portion.

Rabbi Yirmiyah raises a challenge from this very ruling. The *gemara* notes: *va-afilu salka deitach de-chayei* — even if one were to entertain the possibility that such a fetus could be viable, the *mishna*'s ruling that the subsequent child receives the double portion would then be wrong. Since it is indeed ruled that the subsequent child receives the double portion, this proves that the animal-like fetus was never considered potentially viable. It is viable in name only — *mino mitkayem*, the species it resembles is viable — but the fetus itself has no potential for life.

The *chachamim* take the opposite position: since they do not consider the animal-shaped fetus a *vlad* at all, they would hold that the subsequent child is *bechor la-Kohen* as well, since no prior birth occurred.

### Father's Pain

Rava attempts to resist the proof. He argues: perhaps one could say the animal-like fetus *is* potentially viable, and yet the subsequent child still receives the double portion — not because the first was never viable, but because of a different *derashah*. The verse says *reishit ono* — "the first of his strength" — which the *gemara* reads as *mi she-libo daveih alav*, the child whose loss causes the father anguish. The implication is that the double portion applies only when the father genuinely mourns the loss of the first child. If the first "child" had an animal-like form, the father would not be pained by its loss — *zeh she-ein libo daveih alav* — and therefore the verse's criterion is not met, and the subsequent child receives the double portion, even if the first was technically viable.

### Miscarriage Status

The conclusion drawn by the *gemara* can now be stated. According to Rabbi Meir, the animal-like fetus carries *torat leida* — the legal character of a birth — and therefore the mother is a *yoledet*, and the subsequent child is not the *bechor* for *pidyon haben*. Yet this same fetus was never considered viable, and therefore the subsequent child receives the double portion in inheritance. The *chachamim* disagree at the foundational level: since the fetus did not have human form, it is not a *vlad* at all, and the woman is not a *yoledet*; the subsequent child is simultaneously the *bechor* for *pidyon haben* and eligible for the double portion.

Throughout this discussion, the *gemara*'s language — speaking of *behemah bi-mei isha* rather than *kemin behemah* — raises profound questions about how the fetus is being conceptualized. Whether the *gemara* ultimately resolves this as a matter of appearance and form alone, or whether it genuinely entertains the possibility of an entity of ambiguous human/animal status, the questions posed by *Tosafot* and the *Rosh* make clear that later authorities understood the passage to carry real halakhic weight along precisely these lines. At minimum, the *gemara* here is working through what it means for something to have human *tzurah* — human form — and whether form, rather than origin or biology, is the determinative criterion for acquiring the full range of halakhic statuses associated with human birth.
