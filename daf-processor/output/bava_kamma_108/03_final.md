# Bava Kamma 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shlach Yad & Kefel

### R. Chiya bar Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁלַח בּוֹ יָד. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״וְנִקְרַב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים, אִם לֹא שָׁלַח יָדוֹ בִּמְלֶאכֶת רֵעֵהוּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִי שָׁלַח בָּהּ יָד – מִיחַיַּיב; לְמֵימְרָא דִּבְשָׁלַח בָּהּ יָד עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef says:** With regard to an unpaid bailee **who** falsely **states the claim, with regard to a deposit,** that **a thief** stole it, and is discovered to have lied, **he is not liable** to pay double payment **unless he misappropriates it,** i.e., uses it for his own needs, before taking his oath. **What is the reason** for this? The verse states: "If the thief shall not be found, **the owner of the house shall approach the judges to determine if he misappropriated his neighbor's goods"** (Exodus 22:7), and the following verse concludes: "The one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor." **By inference,** one can learn **that if he misappropriated it, he is liable** for double payment; **that is to say that we are dealing** only **with** a case **where he misappropriates it.**

The daf opens with a statement of Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef that engages directly with the question of what conditions must be met for a *shomer* who claims *ta'anat ganav* — that the deposited object was stolen — to become liable to pay *kefel*, the double payment. Yesterday's discussion centered on a debate between Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef and Rabbi Chiya bar Abba concerning whether a *modeh b'miktzat* oath was needed alongside the *shvuat hashomer*. Here, Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef addresses a different question entirely: what is required to trigger *chiyuv kefel* in the first place?

His position is stated tersely: *hato'ein ta'anat ganav bepikadon eino chayav ad sheyishlach bo yad* — one who claims theft regarding a *pikadon* is not liable until he has performed *shlach yad*, that is, until he has actively misappropriated the object for his own use. It is insufficient that the *shomer* is lying and has taken a false *shvua*. Something more is required — an act of *shlichut yad*, of using the object for oneself.

### Scriptural Basis

The scriptural basis for this position is drawn from the *parsha* governing the oath of a *shomer*: *v'nikrav ba'al habayit el ha'Elokim im lo shalach yado bimlechet rei'eihu* — the owner shall approach the judges to swear that the *shomer* did not cast his hand against the friend's property. The implication, through *mikh'lal*, is that the entire framework of this *parsha* — leading to the obligation of *kefel* when one is proven to have lied — presupposes that there was *shlach yad*. The *kefel* payment described later in the passage applies specifically when the *shomer* was lying and had committed an act of *shlichut yad*. Without *shlichut yad*, the *chiyuv kefel* does not attach.

The precise moment that constitutes *shlichut yad* — whether it is physical handling, using the object, or spending money held in deposit — is a broader discussion belonging to Bava Metzia and is not resolved here.

### Rabbi Zeira's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּעוֹמֶדֶת עַל אֲבוּסָהּ שָׁנוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: דַּוְקָא בְּעוֹמֶדֶת עַל אֲבוּסָהּ קָאָמַר, אֲבָל שָׁלַח בָּהּ יָד – קָנָה, וּשְׁבוּעָה לָא מַהְנְיָא בֵּיהּ כְּלוּם; אוֹ דִלְמָא, אֲפִילּוּ עוֹמֶדֶת עַל אֲבוּסָהּ קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to** his students: **This** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They taught** this *halakha* that one who falsely claims that the deposit was stolen is liable for double payment **with regard to** an animal still **standing over its** feeding **trough,** i.e., the bailee is still safeguarding it for its owner. **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: Is he stating** his ruling **specifically with regard to** an animal **standing over its** feeding **trough, but if the** bailee **misappropriated it** before taking an oath he has **acquired** it, **and an oath is not effective for him at all,** not even to cause him to be liable to pay double payment? **Or perhaps he is stating** his ruling **even with regard to** an animal **standing over its** feeding **trough,** in addition to an animal that the bailee misappropriated?

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba challenged this position in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *be'omedet al avusa shonu* — the ruling about *kefel* was stated even when the animal was standing at its trough undisturbed, meaning even without any *shlichut yad* at all. Rabbi Zeira then posed a sharp question to Rabbi Chiya bar Abba: did Rabbi Yochanan mean that *shlichut yad* is not required to generate *kefel*, or perhaps he meant precisely the opposite — that *kefel* applies only when there was *no* shlichut yad, because if the *shomer* had already committed *shlichut yad*, the *shvua* he subsequently took is no longer the operative act of misappropriation?

The logic behind the second reading is this: the *chiyuv kefel* that the *psukim* describe arises specifically from the false *shvua* that triggers the *parsha* of the oath. But if the *shomer* had already performed *shlichut yad* before taking the oath, he had already become a *gazlan* at that earlier moment. His subsequent *shvua* is therefore not the act that turned him into a thief — he was already one. Paradoxically, then, being a greater *rasha* — having actually misappropriated the object — could technically exempt a person from paying *kefel* for the *shvua*, because the *shvua* was no longer the operative act of *gneiva*.

The counterintuitive nature of this reading was acknowledged: a person who has done more wrong is technically in a different legal category, not because his conduct was less culpable, but because the *shvua* does not independently generate *kefel* once he has already acquired the status of a *gazlan* through an antecedent act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי, כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ שָׁמַעְתִּי; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת אָבַד וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְחָזַר וְטָעַן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב וְנִשְׁבַּע, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּקָנָה בִּשְׁבוּעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: I did not hear this** ruling, so I cannot transmit Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion, but **I did hear** something **similar to it, as Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to an unpaid bailee **who** falsely **states the claim** that the deposit **was lost and takes an oath** to that effect, **and retracted and** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit **and takes an oath** to that effect, **and witnesses came** and testified that he lied, he is **exempt** from double payment for the second claim. **What is the reason; is it not due to** the fact **that** he already **acquired** the item **with** the **first oath,** and therefore the second oath is disregarded, which is why he is not liable for double payment for a false claim of theft? So too, one who misappropriates the deposit thereby acquires it and is no longer obligated to take an oath.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba replied that he had not heard specifically which version Rabbi Yochanan intended, but he had heard a parallel case. Rabbi Assi cited Rabbi Yochanan: if a *shomer* first swore *ta'anat avad* — that the object was lost — and then was asked again and swore *ta'anat ganav* — that it was stolen — and witnesses came to disprove the second *shvua*, he is *patur* from *kefel*. The reason is that the first *shvua* had already discharged the owner's ability to impose a further *shvua*, since *beit din* would never compel a second oath from someone who had already taken one. The second *shvua*, being one that *beit din* could not have legitimately imposed, does not carry the weight needed to generate *kefel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, הוֹאִיל וְיָצָא יְדֵי בְּעָלִים בִּשְׁבוּעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to him: No,** there is no proof from there, because one could argue that the reason is not because he acquired the item with the first oath; rather, **since it left the owner's possession with** the bailee taking the **first oath,** he is exempt from taking an additional oath with regard to the same claim, but his oath will be significant even after having misappropriated the item.

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן, כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁלַח בּוֹ יָד – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְנִקְרַב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים, אִם לֹא שָׁלַח יָדוֹ וְגוֹ׳״, הָא שָׁלַח יָדוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an *amora* who disputes the ruling of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef. **Rav Sheshet says:** With regard to an unpaid bailee **who** falsely **states the claim, with regard to a deposit,** that **a thief** stole it, and is discovered to have lied, **once he misappropriated it** before he took an oath he is **exempt. What is the reason** for this? **This is** what **the Merciful One is saying,** i.e., this is how the verse is to be understood: "If the thief shall not be found, **the owner of the house shall approach the judges if he has not misappropriated** his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:7), indicating that this verse speaks specifically with regard to a case where he did not misappropriate the deposit; **consequently,** if **he misappropriated it,** he is **exempt** from double payment.

The *gemara* thus identifies three possible positions. The first, that of Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef, holds that *shlichut yad* is a prerequisite for *kefel* — you need both *shlichut yad* and a false *shvua* to be liable. The second holds that *shlichut yad* is entirely irrelevant — a false *shvua* of *ta'anat ganav* generates *kefel* regardless of whether *shlichut yad* occurred. The third position — and this is where the question of Rabbi Zeira points — holds that *kefel* applies specifically in the absence of prior *shlichut yad*: once *shlichut yad* has occurred, the subsequent *shvua* is rendered inoperative because the *shomer* was already a *gazlan*, and the *shvua* was not the act that created his liability.

The parallel to the case of two successive oaths is instructive, but the *gemara* ultimately distinguishes between them. In the case of two oaths, the second is exempt from generating *kefel* because *beit din* could not legitimately impose it — the first oath had foreclosed that option. In the case of *shlichut yad*, the owner is ignorant of what the *shomer* did, and *beit din* could still impose an oath. Therefore, the case of prior *shlichut yad* remains an open question — the exemption may or may not apply.

Rav Sheshet then states explicitly: *hato'ein ta'anat ganav bepikadon, keivan sheshalach bo yad patur* — one who claims *ta'anat ganav* regarding a *pikadon*, if he had already committed *shlichut yad*, is *patur* from *kefel*. This is not the position of Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef, who required *shlichut yad* as a precondition for liability. Rather, Rav Sheshet's position is that *shlichut yad* actually exempts from *kefel*.

His reading of the *pasuk* is the reverse of the earlier one. Where the verse says *v'nikrav ba'al habayit el ha'Elokim im lo shalach yado* — the owner approaches the judges assuming he did not cast his hand against the object — Rav Sheshet reads the clause as establishing the entire *parsha*'s scope: all of this, including the *kefel* liability, applies only to the case where the *shomer* was *not* guilty of *shlichut yad*. That is the domain of this *parsha*. If the *shomer* had committed *shlichut yad*, the entire *parsha* does not apply, because from the moment of *shlichut yad* he was already a *gazlan*, and a different legal framework governs him. The *shvua* he subsequently takes is legally superfluous as a trigger for *kefel*.

Two readings of the same *pasuk* thus yield opposite results: one treats the *im lo shalach yado* clause as describing the *shomer*'s oath — he swears that he did *not* misappropriate, and if he is lying, he pays *kefel* — while the other treats it as establishing a precondition — the entire framework of liability applies only if *shlichut yad* was absent.

## Multiple Oaths

### Three Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן, וַהֲלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁבוּעוֹת מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא פָּשַׁעְתִּי בָּהּ״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא שָׁלַחְתִּי בָּהּ יָד״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתִי״. מַאי, לָאו ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא שָׁלַחְתִּי בָּהּ יָד״ – דֻּומְיָא דִּשְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתִי״? מָה ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתִי״, כִּי מִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ – חַיָּיב; ״אַף שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא שָׁלַחְתִּי בָּהּ יָד״, כִּי מִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא דִּשְׁלַח בָּהּ יָד – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to** Rav Sheshet: **But aren't three oaths administered** by the court **to** any unpaid bailee who claims that the deposit was stolen from him? The first is: **An oath that I was not negligent in** safeguarding **it,** thereby enabling it to be stolen; the second is: **An oath that I did not misappropriate it;** the third is: **An oath that it is not in my possession. What, is it not** so that: **An oath that I did not misappropriate it,** is **similar to: An oath that it is not in my possession,** in the following manner: **Just as** with regard to: **An oath that it is not in my possession, when the matter becomes revealed that it is in his possession,** he is **liable, so too,** with regard to: **An oath that I did not misappropriate it, when the matter becomes revealed that he did misappropriate it,** he should be **liable?**

Rav Nachman introduces the broader framework within which the *shlichut yad* discussion must be understood: a *shomer* is ordinarily required to take three separate oaths. The first is *shvua shelo pashati bah* — an oath that he was not negligent — because *pshiah* renders the *shomer* liable even for subsequent accidents. The second is *shvua shelo shalachti bah yad* — an oath that he did not misappropriate the object for his own use — because *shlichut yad*, regardless of intent, transforms the *shomer* into a *gazlan* and makes him liable even for unavoidable losses. The third is *shvua she'eina birshusi* — an oath that the object is no longer in his possession — because without it, one might suspect the *shomer* of simply retaining the object while falsely claiming it was lost or stolen.

These three oaths address three independent grounds of suspicion. The first two — negligence and *shlichut yad* — are about acts that change the *shomer*'s legal status regardless of what subsequently happened to the object. A *shomer* who was negligent, or who used the object for himself, remains liable even if the object is later destroyed by genuine *ones*. The third oath addresses the entirely separate possibility that nothing happened to the object at all and the *shomer* is simply holding onto it dishonestly.

### Shlach Yad vs. Negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא שָׁלַחְתִּי בָּהּ יָד״ – דּוּמְיָא דְּ״שֶׁלֹּא פָּשַׁעְתִּי בָּהּ״. מָה ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא פָּשַׁעְתִּי בָּהּ״ – כִּי מִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא דְּפָשַׁע בָּהּ, פָּטוּר מִכֶּפֶל; אַף ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא שָׁלַחְתִּי בָּהּ יָד״ – כִּי מִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא דִּשְׁלַח בָּהּ יָד, פָּטוּר מִכֶּפֶל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to him: No,** there is no proof from there, because one could say that the *halakha* of: **An oath that I did not misappropriate it,** is **similar to** the *halakha* of: An oath **that I was not negligent in** safeguarding **it,** in the following manner: **Just as** with regard to: **An oath that I was not negligent in** safeguarding **it, when the matter becomes revealed that he was negligent in** safeguarding **it,** he is **exempt from double payment, so too,** with regard to: **An oath that I did not misappropriate it, when the matter becomes revealed that he did misappropriate it,** he is **exempt from double payment.** Since the comparison can be made between different pairings of the oaths to suggest opposite conclusions, none of these comparisons are definitive.

The relationship between the oath of *shlichut yad* and the oath of negligence (*pshiah*) is clarified. These are independent conditions of liability: *pshiah* makes the *shomer* responsible for damages that flow from his carelessness, while *shlichut yad* immediately converts his relationship with the object from that of a *shomer* into that of a *gazlan*. The moment of *shlichut yad* — though its precise definition remains for Bava Metzia — is the moment from which the *shomer* becomes liable for *ones*, regardless of whether he intended to permanently appropriate the object.

### Parallel to Negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: מָמוֹן הַמְחַיְּיבוֹ כֶּפֶל פּוֹטְרוֹ מִן הַחוֹמֶשׁ, אוֹ דִלְמָא שְׁבוּעָה הַמְחַיַּיבְתּוֹ כֶּפֶל פּוֹטַרְתּוֹ מִן הַחוֹמֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* quoted earlier (65b) teaches that a bailee who would be liable for double payment if witnesses were to testify that he had in fact stolen a deposit about which he had taken an oath attesting to its theft does not pay the additional one-fifth payment normally imposed upon one who takes a false oath with regard to a monetary claim. The Gemara explores the reason for this *halakha*. **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** Is it the **monetary** obligation **that renders him liable** for **double payment** which **exempts him from the** additional **one-fifth** payment, **or perhaps** is it the taking of the **oath that renders him liable for double payment** which **exempts him from the** additional **one-fifth** payment?

The *gemara* now asks whether the oath of *shlichut yad* is analogous to the oath of negligence for purposes of *kefel* liability. The argument runs as follows: if it turns out that the *shomer* who swore *she'eina birshusi* — that the object is no longer in his possession — was lying, and the object is found in his possession, he is liable for *kefel*, since that false oath is the act that triggers the *parsha* of *kefel*. Should we say, then, that similarly, if the *shomer* swore *shelo shalachti bah yad* — that he did not misappropriate — and it is revealed that he did commit *shlichut yad*, he should also be liable for *kefel*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁטָּעַן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְחָזַר וְטָעַן טַעֲנַת אָבַד וְנִשְׁבַּע;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What are the circumstances** in which there is a practical difference between these possibilities? It is in a case **where he** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit **and takes an oath** to that effect, **and retracts and** falsely **states the claim** that it **was lost and takes an oath** to that effect,

The *gemara* rejects this analogy. The oath of *shlichut yad* is properly compared not to the oath of *she'eina birshusi*, but to the oath of negligence. And in the case of negligence — if it is revealed that the *shomer* was in fact negligent — he is *patur* from *kefel*. He is obligated for the principal, since his negligence caused the loss, but *kefel* does not apply because he did not steal anything in the operative legal sense. By analogy, if it is revealed that the *shomer* committed *shlichut yad*, he similarly is *patur mi'kefel* — he owes for the principal as a *gazlan*, but the *shvua* he took does not generate the additional penalty of *kefel*.

### Kefel vs. Chomesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָאוּ עֵדִים אַקַּמַּיְיתָא, וְהוֹדָה אַבָּתְרָיְיתָא. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **and witnesses come** and testify **with regard to** his **first** claim that at the time he took an oath that the deposit had been stolen, it was actually in his possession; **and he admits with regard to** his **second** claim that it had been a lie and that it had not actually been lost, **what** is the *halakha*: Is he obligated to pay the additional one-fifth payment?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָמוֹן הַמְחַיְּיבוֹ כֶּפֶל פּוֹטְרוֹ מִן הַחוֹמֶשׁ – וְהָא אִיחַיַּיב לֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא; אוֹ דִלְמָא שְׁבוּעָה הַמְחַיַּיבְתּוֹ כֶּפֶל פּוֹטַרְתּוֹ מִן הַחוֹמֶשׁ – וְהָא שְׁבוּעָה בָּתְרָיְיתָא, הוֹאִיל דְּלָא קָא מְחַיְּיבָא לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא, תְּחַיְּיבֵיהּ חוּמְשָׁא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two possibilities: Is it being obligated in **a monetary** obligation **that renders him liable** for **double payment** which **exempts him from the** additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** in this case **he is liable** for **the double payment for** the deposit due to the witness testimony and would therefore be exempt from the additional one-fifth payment? **Or perhaps** is it admitting to a false **oath that renders him liable** for **double payment** which **exempts him from the** additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** with regard to **this latter oath** that the item was lost, **since it does not render him liable** for **the double payment,** as he took an oath that it had been lost, not stolen, **will it render him liable** for **the** additional **one-fifth** payment?

Rami bar Chama poses a fundamental question: is it the money — the principal — that generates *kefel* which simultaneously exempts from paying the *chomesh*, or is it the *shvua* that generates *kefel* which exempts from the *chomesh*? The question matters practically, and the *gemara* constructs a scenario to illuminate the difference.

By way of background: if a *shomer* takes a false oath that the object was stolen and then admits it, he pays the principal, a *chomesh* (one-fifth of the principal added on), and brings a *korban*. If, however, witnesses come and expose the lie before he admits, he pays *kefel* and does not pay *chomesh*. The question is why *kefel* and *chomesh* are mutually exclusive. One possible answer is that the same *shvua* cannot generate two separate obligations — the *shvua* either generates *kefel* or *chomesh*, not both. The other possible answer is that the same *mammon* — the same money — cannot generate two types of additional payment beyond the principal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר לְאֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּק: ״הֵיכָן שׁוֹרִי שֶׁגָּנַבְתָּ?״ וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״. וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁגְּנָבוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. וְאִם הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a solution to this dilemma from a mishna (*Shevuot* 49b): In a scenario where one **said to someone from the marketplace: Where is my ox that you stole? And** the accused **says** in response: **I did not steal** it; whereupon the owner of the ox said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the accused **said: Amen,** accepting the oath; **and the witnesses testify about** the accused **that he stole it,** he must **pay double payment. And if he admitted of his own** accord that he stole it, then he must **pay the principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** bring **a guilt-offering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, עֵדִים הוּא דִּמְחַיְּיבִי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא; הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – אִין, אֲבָל הוֹדָה אַחַר עֵדִים – לָא;
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains the proof: **But here,** it is **witnesses who render him liable** for **the double payment,** as since the accused was not a bailee, the oath does not render him liable, and yet the mishna teaches that if **he admitted of his own** accord that he stole it, then **yes,** he must pay the additional one-fifth payment, **but** if **he admitted after witnesses** testified, then **no,** he is not required to pay the additional one-fifth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שְׁבוּעָה הַמְחַיַּיבְתּוֹ כֶּפֶל פּוֹטַרְתּוֹ מִן הַחוֹמֶשׁ, אַמַּאי הוֹדָה אַחַר עֵדִים לָא? מִכְּדִי הָא שְׁבוּעָה לָא קָא מְיחַיְּיבָא לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא, תְּחַיְּיבֵיהּ חוּמְשָׁא!
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **And if it enters your mind** to say that it is **an oath that renders him liable** for **double payment** which **exempts him from the** additional **one-fifth** payment, **why** does he **not** pay the additional one-fifth payment if **he admitted** that it was a false oath **after witnesses** testified? **After all, this oath does not render him liable** to pay **the double payment,** as the *halakha* that one who falsely takes an oath that the item was stolen pays double payment applies only in the case of a bailee; therefore, the oath **should render him liable** to pay **the** additional **one-fifth** payment, as does any false oath taken with regard to a monetary claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מָמוֹן הַמְחַיְּיבוֹ כֶּפֶל – פּוֹטְר(תּ)וֹ מִן הַחוֹמֶשׁ? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava completes the proof: **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** this mishna that it is the **monetary** obligation **that renders him liable** to pay the **double payment** which **exempts him from the** additional **one-fifth** payment? The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the mishna that the additional one-fifth payment is dependent upon the type of monetary obligation, and not an oath, resolving Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma.

The *nafkamina* is constructed as follows: suppose a *shomer* first swore *ta'anat ganav* — falsely — and then, when challenged again, swore *ta'anat avad* — also falsely. Witnesses then came to establish the first *shvua* was false, obligating *kefel*. The *shomer* then admits that the second *shvua* was also false. Since the first *shvua* made him liable for *kefel*, does the second — a different *shvua* — generate liability for *chomesh*? If it is the *shvua* that generates *kefel* which precludes *chomesh*, then only the first *shvua* is the problematic one, and the second, independent *shvua* could in principle generate a *chomesh*. But if it is the money — the same object — that cannot bear two types of payment, then neither *shvua* can generate *chomesh* as long as *kefel* is owed for that money.

Rava then draws a proof from a *baraita*. The *baraita* describes a case where someone accuses another of stealing his ox — and this is a case of actual *gneiva*, not a *shomer* scenario. The accused takes a false *shvua* that he did not steal it; witnesses come and prove otherwise; he pays *kefel*. If he admits on his own, he pays principal, *chomesh*, and brings an *asham*. The *gemara* clarifies that in the case of genuine *gneiva*, the *kefel* obligation is generated by the act of *gneiva* itself, substantiated by the *edim* — the *shvua* is not what makes him a thief. Yet even after witnesses come and *kefel* is established, if he admits, he does not pay *chomesh*. If the exemption from *chomesh* were tied only to the *shvua* that generated *kefel*, there would be no reason to exempt him here — the *shvua* he took is not the operative cause of *kefel*, so it should not block the *chomesh*. The fact that *chomesh* is nonetheless precluded demonstrates that it is the *mammon* — the money for which *kefel* is owed — that simultaneously exempts from the *chomesh*. *Shma mina*: it is money, not oath, that is the operative principle.

### Two Accomplices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבִינָא: חוֹמֶשׁ וּכְפֵילָא בִּתְרֵי גַבְרֵי, מַאי? הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁמָּסַר שׁוֹרוֹ לִשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְטָעֲנוּ בּוֹ טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב. חַד נִשְׁבַּע וְהוֹדָה, וְחַד נִשְׁבַּע וּבָאוּ עֵדִים. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the additional one-fifth payment, **Ravina raises a dilemma:** If the additional **one-fifth** payment **and the double payment** are split **among two men, what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara explains the dilemma: **What are the circumstances?** This is referring to a case **where** one **transferred his ox to two people** to safeguard, **and they stated the claim** that **a thief** stole **it** from them; **one** of them **took an oath** that it had been stolen **and** subsequently **admitted** of his own accord that he had lied, **and one** of them **took an oath** that it had been stolen **and** subsequently **witnesses came** and testified that he had lied. **What** is the *halakha* in this case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: בְּחַד גַּבְרָא קָפֵיד רַחֲמָנָא דְּלָא מְשַׁלֵּם חוּמְשָׁא וּכְפֵילָא; הַאי נְשַׁלֵּם כְּפֵילָא, וְהַאי נְשַׁלֵּם חוּמְשָׁא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, עִלָּוֵי חַד מָמוֹנָא קָפֵיד רַחֲמָנָא דְּלָא נְשַׁלֵּם עֲלֵהּ חוּמְשָׁא וּכְפֵילָא – וְהָכָא נָמֵי חַד מָמוֹנָא הוּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina clarifies the possibilities: **Do we say** that **the Merciful One is particular with regard to one man that he does not pay** both **the** additional **one-fifth** payment **and the double payment,** whereas in this case, **this one** about whom the witnesses testified **will pay the double payment and that one** who admitted of his own accord **will pay the** additional **one-fifth** payment? **Or perhaps the Merciful One is particular that the** additional **one-fifth** payment **and the double payment will not be paid for one monetary** obligation, **and here too, it is one monetary** obligation? The Gemara comments: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Ravina then raises a further question: if we have established that one money cannot generate both *kefel* and *chomesh*, does this restriction apply even when the two payments would fall on two different people? The case he constructs: two people were jointly entrusted with an animal, both claimed it was stolen, both were brought to court and made to swear. One subsequently admitted — generating liability for *chomesh* — while witnesses came against the other — generating *kefel*. Can one pay *kefel* and the other pay *chomesh* for the same underlying object?

Two framings are possible. Either the Torah restricts *one person* from incurring both *kefel* and *chomesh* for the same money — in which case two separate individuals paying separate penalties for the same money should be permitted — or the Torah restricts *one money* from generating both types of additional payment at all, regardless of how many people are involved. The question is left as *teiku*.

### Multiple Like Payments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: תְּרֵי חוּמְשֵׁי אוֹ תְּרֵי כְפֵילֵי בְּחַד גַּבְרָא, מַאי? הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? שֶׁטָּעַן טַעֲנַת אָבַד, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְהוֹדָה, וְחָזַר וְטָעַן טַעֲנַת אָבַד, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְהוֹדָה. אִי נָמֵי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁטָּעַן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים, וְחָזַר וְטָעַן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the additional one-fifth payment, **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** In a case of **two** additional **one-fifth** payments **or two double payments with regard to one man, what** is the *halakha*; must he pay twice for the same monetary obligation? The Gemara explains: **What are the circumstances?** This is referring to a case **where** one **stated the claim** that the deposit **was lost and took an oath and** then **admitted** that it was not lost, **and returned and** again **stated the claim** that the same deposit **was lost, and took** another **oath** supporting this claim **and** again **admitted** that it was not lost. **Alternatively,** this is referring to a case **where** one **stated the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit **and took an oath and** then **witnesses came** and testified that he had taken it, **and he returned and** again **stated the claim** that **a thief** stole the same deposit **and** again **took an oath and** then **witnesses came** and again testified that he had taken it; **what** is the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: תְּרֵי גַּוְונֵי מָמוֹנָא קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא דְּלָא נִשְׁתַּלְּמוּ עִילָּוֵי חַד מָמוֹנָא – וְהָכָא חַד גַּוְונָא הוּא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, תְּרֵי מָמוֹנֵי אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא דְּלָא לִשְׁתַּלְּמוּ עִילָּוֵי חַד מָמוֹנָא – וְהָכָא נָמֵי תְּרֵי מָמוֹנֵי נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa clarifies the possibilities: **Do we say** that **the Merciful One states** concerning **two types of monetary restitution,** e.g., double payment and the additional one-fifth payment, **that they will not be paid for one monetary** obligation, **but here, it is** only **one type,** merely doubled; **or perhaps the Merciful One states** concerning **two** payments of **monetary restitution that they will not be paid for one monetary** obligation, **and here too, they are two** payments of **monetary restitution,** whether in the case of two additional one-fifth payments or two double payments?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״וַחֲמִשִׁתָיו יֹסֵף עָלָיו״ – הַתּוֹרָה רִיבְּתָה חֲמִישִׁיּוֹת הַרְבֵּה לְקֶרֶן אַחַת! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a resolution to Rav Pappa's dilemma. **Come** and **hear, as Rava says:** The verse states: "Or anything about which he has sworn falsely, he shall restore it in full, **and shall add the fifth part [*ḥamishitav*] more thereto"** (Leviticus 5:24). By employing a plural term, *ḥamishitav*, and not the singular *ḥamishito*, **the Torah added many** additional **one-fifths for one principal,** meaning that one can become obligated in multiple one-fifth payments for the same principal. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** Rava's statement that this is the *halakha*.

Rav Pappa asks a related question: can one person pay *kefel* twice, or *chomesh* twice, for the same money? We have established that a single money cannot generate both *kefel* and *chomesh* for the same person. But does the prohibition concern two *different* types of payment, or any two payments beyond the principal?

The scenario: a *shomer* swore *ta'anat avad*, admitted it was false, then again swore *ta'anat avad* and admitted again — can each false admission generate a separate *chomesh*? Or: a *shomer* swore *ta'anat ganav*, witnesses came, and then he claimed it was stolen again and witnesses came again — can *kefel* be assessed twice?

Rava resolves this from the verse *v'chamishito yosef alav* — "he shall add its fifth part upon it" — from which the Torah derived that multiple fifths can be assessed for the same principal, provided each arises from a distinct act of false swearing followed by admission. *Shma mina*: the restriction is on two *different* types of payment for one money, not on multiple instances of the same type. Tosafot raises technical difficulties with the exact construction of the scenario — particularly regarding how the *shomer* could still be considered a *shomer* after having been proven a liar once — but the *gemara*'s conclusion stands.

## Kefel Distribution

### Mishna Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּבָעוּהוּ בְּעָלִים לַשּׁוֹמֵר, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְשִׁילֵּם, וְהוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב – כֶּפֶל לְמִי? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְבַעַל הַפִּקָּדוֹן, רָבָא אָמַר: לְמִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses an amoraic dispute concerning a similar case: If **an owner demanded of** an unpaid **bailee** that he return a deposit, and he claimed that it had been stolen from him **and took an oath** to that effect, **and** despite being exempt from payment, **he paid** of his own volition, **and** subsequently **the thief was identified, to whom** is **double payment** paid, the owner or the bailee? **Abaye says: To the owner of the deposit. Rava says: To the one in whose** possession **the deposit** was when it was stolen, i.e., the bailee.

The *gemara* now transitions into a discussion that properly belongs to Bava Metzia, addressing the *mishna* at the beginning of the third *perek* there. The *mishna* governs who receives the *kefel* when a genuine *ganav* is subsequently identified — the original owner, or the *shomer* who handled the claim.

The *mishna* presents two clean cases. First: if the *shomer* paid the owner without taking any oath — *shilem velo ratza lishava* — and the *ganav* is later found, the *ganav* pays *kefel* (or four and five in cases of slaughter and sale) to the *shomer*, who had reimbursed the owner. Second: if the *shomer* took an oath but did not pay — *nishba velo ratza leshalem* — then if the *ganav* is found, he pays *kefel* to the original owner. Critically, the *mishna* says nothing about the case where the *shomer* both swore *and* paid. Both disputants read the *mishna* through the lens of their own position, and the ambiguity is a genuine one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר לְבַעַל הַפִּקָּדוֹן – כֵּיוָן דְּאַטְרְחֵיהּ בִּשְׁבוּעָה, לָא מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא. רָבָא אָמַר לְמִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ – כֵּיוָן דְּשִׁילֵּם, מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ כְּפֵילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their opinions: **Abaye says** that the double payment is paid **to the owner of the deposit; since** the bailee **troubled him by** taking **an oath** instead of paying immediately, the owner is **not** willing to **grant acquisition of the double payment to** the bailee, so it is paid to the owner. **Rava says** that the double payment is paid **to the one in whose** possession **the deposit** was when it was stolen; **once he paid,** the owner **grants acquisition of the double payment to** the bailee.

Abaye reads the first clause of the *mishna* as the controlling principle. The *mishna* says he paid *and did not swear* — the key variable for the *shomer* receiving *kefel* is the absence of the oath. The reasoning is: by forcing the owner to put him through the oath process, the *shomer* has created an adversarial dynamic. The owner, having been compelled to drag the *shomer* to court for a *shvua*, would not reasonably be willing to transfer his right to *kefel* to such a *shomer*. Therefore, according to Abaye, the *shomer* receives *kefel* only if he paid *without ever taking an oath*. If any oath was taken — even if payment followed — the *kefel* reverts to the owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַבָּיֵי, קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא! אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: נִשְׁבַּע, וְלֹא רָצָה לְשַׁלֵּם קוֹדֶם הַשְּׁבוּעָה אֶלָּא לְאַחַר הַשְּׁבוּעָה, לְמִי מְשַׁלֵּם? לְבַעַל הַפִּקָּדוֹן. לְרָבָא, קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא! אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: שִׁילֵּם, וְלֹא רָצָה לַעֲמוֹד בִּשְׁבוּעָתוֹ אֶלָּא שִׁילֵּם, לְמִי מְשַׁלֵּם? לְמִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The inference from **the latter clause is difficult for** the opinion of **Abaye.** The Gemara explains. **Abaye** could have **said to you: This is what** the latter clause of the mishna **is teaching:** If the bailee **took an oath and did not want to pay** initially **before** taking **the oath,** but **rather** wanted to pay only **after** having taken **the oath,** in this case **to whom does** the thief **pay** double payment? **To the owner of the deposit.** Similarly, the Gemara notes: The inference from **the first clause is difficult for** the opinion of **Rava.** The Gemara explains. **Rava** could have **said to you: This is what** the first clause of the mishna **is teaching:** If he **paid,** meaning he had taken an oath **and did not want to stand by his oath, but** instead **paid** in order to nullify the oath he had taken, **to whom does** the thief **pay** double payment? **To the one in whose** possession **the deposit** was when it was lost.

Rava reads from the end of the *mishna*. The *mishna*'s second clause says the *shomer* swore *and did not pay*, and the *kefel* goes to the owner. The operative variable for Rava is the non-payment: the *shomer* receives *kefel* only when he ultimately paid the owner, regardless of whether he also took an oath along the way. Since the owner's fundamental interest is in being made whole, once the *shomer* actually reimburses him, the owner is content to cede the *kefel*. The oath is secondary.

The *gemara* then notes the challenge each position faces from the other clause of the *mishna*. For Abaye: the *mishna*'s second clause (*nishba velo ratza leshalem*) could imply that if he *did* pay even after swearing, he receives *kefel* — yet Abaye would deny this. Abaye resolves it by reading the clause as: the *shomer* swore and was not willing to pay *upfront* — meaning the refusal to pay *before* the oath is what deprives him of *kefel*. For Rava: the first clause (*shilem velo ratza lishava*) implies that swearing always removes the *shomer* from entitlement to *kefel*. Rava resolves this by reading *velo ratza lishava* not as "never took an oath" but as "did not insist on resting his case with only the oath" — meaning he went beyond the oath and ultimately paid. Both resolutions are somewhat forced, reflecting genuine ambiguity in the *mishna*'s language.

## Ganav's Admission

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּבָעוּהוּ בְּעָלִים לַשּׁוֹמֵר, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְהוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב; תְּבָעוֹ שׁוֹמֵר, וְהוֹדָה; תְּבָעוּהוּ בְּעָלִים, וְכָפַר, וְהֵבִיאוּ עֵדִים – מִי נִפְטַר הַגַּנָּב בְּהוֹדָאַת שׁוֹמֵר, אוֹ לֹא נִפְטַר הַגַּנָּב בְּהוֹדָאַת שׁוֹמֵר?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another dilemma concerning the status of a bailee and stolen deposits. If the **owner** of a deposit **demanded from a bailee** that he return their deposit, and he claimed that it had been stolen from him **and took an oath** to that effect; **and** then **the thief was recognized,** and **the bailee demanded of** the thief to pay **and he admitted** to having stolen it; and then the **owner demanded of** the thief to pay **and he denied** the claim, **and** the owner **brought witnesses** that he had stolen it, **did the thief become exempt** from double payment **through** his **admission** to the **bailee,** as is the *halakha* when one admits liability to a penalty? Does the bailee have standing vis-à-vis the thief despite having exempted himself by taking an oath to the owner, **or did the thief not become exempt through** his **admission** to the **bailee?**

The *gemara* turns to a new question: can the *ganav*'s admission to the *shomer* — rather than to the owner — exempt the *ganav* from *kefel*? The scenario: the owner's claim was resolved with an oath by the *shomer*, who swore truthfully that the object was stolen. The *ganav* is later identified; the *shomer* confronts the *ganav*, who admits the theft. Then the owner goes to the *ganav* and demands the object; the *ganav* denies it; witnesses are brought. Does the prior admission to the *shomer* count as a legitimate admission that would exempt the *ganav* from *kefel*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם בֶּאֱמֶת נִשְׁבַּע – נִפְטַר הַגַּנָּב בְּהוֹדָאַת שׁוֹמֵר. אִם בְּשֶׁקֶר נִשְׁבַּע – לֹא נִפְטַר הַגַּנָּב בְּהוֹדָאַת שׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: If** he **took an oath truthfully,** i.e., if it now becomes clear through the testimony of witnesses that the bailee's oath was true, he is assumed to be someone the owner would rely on to collect the stolen item for him, and he remains a bailee. Therefore, **the thief became exempt through** his **admission** to the **bailee. If** his **oath** was **taken falsely,** e.g., he took an oath that the animal given as a deposit died naturally and it is now clear that his oath was false, he is assumed to be someone whom the owner would not rely on to collect the stolen item for them, and he is no longer a bailee. In this case, **the thief did not become exempt through** his **admission** to the **bailee.**

Rava draws a distinction based on the nature of the *shomer*'s oath. If the *shomer* swore truthfully (*nishba be'emes*), then the *shomer* remains in a position of trust vis-à-vis the owner — his oath was honest, the owner's confidence in him is intact, and therefore the *shomer* can be understood to be acting on the owner's behalf in pursuing the *ganav*. In that case, the *ganav*'s admission to the *shomer* is a legally meaningful admission — *niftar haganav behoda'at shomer* — and he is exempt from *kefel*. But if the *shomer* swore falsely, he has severed his relationship with the owner through that falsehood. Even if the owner is not yet aware of it, the *shomer* no longer stands in for the owner in any meaningful sense. Therefore, *lo niftar haganav behoda'at shomer* — the *ganav*'s admission to him carries no weight.

Rashi's reading is the one followed here, though it is noted that an alternative *girsa* inverts the ruling: under that reading, it is specifically when the *shomer* swore falsely that he remains in the picture — because he is still technically liable to the owner, he continues to have a stake in recovering the object, making his confrontation with the *ganav* legally consequential.

### Intention Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: עָמַד לִישָּׁבַע בְּשֶׁקֶר וְלֹא הִנִּיחוּהוּ, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ. רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי; רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי מַתְנֵי – בָּעֵי רָבָא: נִשְׁבַּע לַשְּׁקֶר, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma** concerning the previous ruling: If a bailee **rose to take a false oath but** the owner **did not let him, what is** the *halakha*? Should he be considered as one who had taken a false oath, as that was his intention, and he is no longer a bailee; or, since he never actually took the false oath, he is still a bailee? The Gemara comments: The question **shall stand** unresolved. The Gemara notes that **Rav Kahana** would **teach** Rava's question **like this,** as quoted above. **Rav Tavyumei** would **teach** Rava's question differently: **Rava raises a dilemma:** If he actually **took a false oath, what is** the *halakha*? Is he still a bailee or not? The Gemara comments: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava raises a further question: what if the *shomer* had *intended* to swear falsely but was prevented from doing so by the owner — who told him not to swear yet? Does the mere *intention* to swear falsely, without the false oath having been taken, suffice to remove the *shomer* from his legal relationship with the owner? The question — *nishba besheker mahu* — is left as *teiku*.

## Owner's Admission

### Shomer's Kefel Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּבָעוּהוּ בְּעָלִים לַשּׁוֹמֵר – וְשִׁילֵּם, וְהוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב, תְּבָעוּהוּ בְּעָלִים – וְהוֹדָה, תְּבָעוֹ שׁוֹמֵר – וְכָפַר, וְהֵבִיא עֵדִים; נִפְטַר גַּנָּב בְּהוֹדָאַת בְּעָלִים, אוֹ לֹא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another dilemma concerning the status of a bailee and stolen deposits: If the **owners** of a deposit **demanded from a bailee** that he return their deposit **and** he **paid** them instead of taking an oath, **and** then **the thief was recognized,** and **the owners demanded of** the thief to pay **and he admitted** to having stolen it; and then the **bailee demanded of** the thief to pay **and he denied** the claim, **and** the bailee **brought witnesses** that he had stolen it, did **the thief become exempt** from double payment **through** his **admission** to the **owners,** as is the *halakha* when one admits liability to a penalty, **or not?**

The *gemara* now poses the mirror image of the previous question. If the *shomer* paid the owner — thereby acquiring the right to collect *kefel* from the *ganav* when found — and the *ganav* is then identified, but before the *shomer* can bring a claim, the original owner approaches the *ganav* and the *ganav* admits the theft to the owner, does that admission exempt the *ganav* from the *kefel* he owes to the *shomer*?

The *shomer* has fully reimbursed the owner and now holds the right to *kefel*. The owner technically has no further claim to *kefel*. Yet the *ganav* admitted to the owner. Is that a legally operative admission, given that the owner is no longer the rightful claimant? The *shomer* could reasonably say to the owner: you took your money, I paid you, this is no longer your affair — step aside.

### Moral Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר לִבְעָלִים: ״אַתּוּ, כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁקְלִיתוּ לְכוּ דְּמֵי – אִסְתְּלִיקְתּוּ לְכוּ מֵהָכָא״; אוֹ דִלְמָא, מָצֵי אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: ״כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאַתְּ עֲבַדְתְּ לַן מִילְּתָא, אֲנַן נָמֵי עָבְדִינַן לָךְ – טָרְחִינַן בָּתַר גַּנָּבָא; (שָׁקֵלְנָא) [שָׁקְלִינַן] אֲנַן דִּידַן, וּשְׁקוֹל אַתְּ דִּידָךְ״? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Do we say** that the **bailee can say to the owners: Once you took your money** from me **you removed yourselves from here** and have no further connection to this deposit. Therefore, I am now considered the owner of the stolen item and the right to the double payment is mine, and the thief's admission to you is meaningless. **Or perhaps** the owners **can say to him: Just as you performed a service for us** by paying us when you were not obligated, **we performed** a service **for you as well.** Instead of granting acquisition of the double payment to you, to enable you to be repaid **we took the trouble** to search **after the thief. We will take** what is **ours,** i.e., the stolen item, **and you take** what is **yours,** the money you paid us. The Gemara comments: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

On the other hand, the owner might say: you paid me even though you didn't have to, I feel a moral obligation to try to help you recover what you spent. I'll pursue the *ganav* on your behalf. In that sense, the owner still feels connected to the situation — he is acting out of a sense of moral responsibility born of the prior legal relationship. The question is whether this sense of moral obligation and the prior legal entanglement render the owner a sufficiently legitimate claimant that the *ganav*'s admission to him is meaningful, thereby exempting the *ganav* from *kefel* owed to the *shomer*. This too is left as *teiku*.

## After Ganav Found

### Abaye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: נִגְנְבָה בְּאוֹנֶס וְהוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִם שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם הוּא – רָצָה עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין, רָצָה נִשְׁבָּע. אִם שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר הוּא – עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין, וְאֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע. רָבָא אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה – עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין וְאֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated:** If an animal given as a deposit **was stolen in circumstances beyond the control** of the bailee, who is therefore exempt from liability regardless of whether he was paid, **and the thief was recognized, Abaye says: If he is an unpaid bailee,** then if **he** so **desires** he can choose to **enter** into **judgment with** the thief, i.e., the bailee will pay the owner and then get reimbursed by the thief; and if **he** so **desires** he can **take an oath** that he was not responsible and the owner can demand his money from the thief. **If he is a paid bailee he enters** into **judgment with** the thief **and does not** have the option to **take an oath** to become exempt, as the responsibility to collect payment from the thief is part of his duty as a paid bailee. **Rava says: Whether this** unpaid bailee **or that** paid bailee **enters** into **judgment with** the thief, **he does not** have the option to **take an oath.**

The *gemara* now turns to a distinct scenario: an object was stolen from a *shomer* by *ones* — unavoidable circumstances, such as armed bandits — and subsequently the *ganav* is identified. What are the *shomer*'s options?

Abaye distinguishes between a *shomer chinam* and a *shomer sachar*. A *shomer chinam* is exempt from liability for theft by *ones* and would ordinarily discharge his obligation to the owner by taking a *shvua*. But now that the *ganav* has been found and reimbursement is possible, Abaye holds that the *shomer chinam* has a choice: *ratza oseh imo din ratza nishba* — he may pay the owner directly and pursue the *ganav* himself, or he may take a *shvua* to the owner and let the owner pursue the *ganav*. A *shomer sachar*, however, whose level of responsibility is higher by virtue of being paid — *oseh imo din v'eino nishba* — must pay the owner and pursue the *ganav*; he does not have the option of simply swearing and stepping aside.

### Rava Position

Rava disagrees. In his view, both a *shomer chinam* and a *shomer sachar* must pay the owner and pursue the *ganav* — *echad zeh v'echad zeh oseh imo din v'eino nishba*. Neither has the option of swearing and stepping aside once the *ganav* has been found. Rava's position holds that when reimbursement from the *ganav* is actually available, the *shomer*'s obligation to make the owner whole is active and cannot be discharged by a *shvua* alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא פְּלִיגָא אַדְּרַב הוּנָא בַּר אָבִין? דִּשְׁלַח רַב הוּנָא בַּר אָבִין: נִגְנְבָה בְּאוֹנֶס וְהוּכַּר הַגַּנָּב, אִם שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם הוּא – רָצָה עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין, רָצָה נִשְׁבָּע; וְאִם שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר הוּא – עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין וְאֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that Rava **disagrees with** the ruling **of Rav Huna bar Avin? As Rav Huna bar Avin sent** this ruling: If an animal given as a deposit **was stolen in circumstances beyond the control** of the bailee **and the thief was recognized, if he is an unpaid bailee,** then if **he** so **desires** he can choose to **enter** into **judgment with** the thief; and if **he** so **desires** he can **take an oath** that he was not responsible, and the owner can demand his money from the thief. **And if he is a paid bailee,** then **he enters** into **judgment with** the thief **and does not** have the option to **take an oath.**

The *gemara* notes that Rav Huna bar Avin had sent a ruling that tracks exactly Abaye's position — *shomer chinam* has the choice, *shomer sachar* does not — and asks whether Rava is arguing with Rav Huna bar Avin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּדַם וְנִשְׁבַּע. וְהָא ״רָצָה עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין, רָצָה נִשְׁבָּע״ קָאָמַר! הָכִי קָאָמַר: רָצָה שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – עוֹמֵד בִּשְׁבוּעָתוֹ, רָצָה – עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rava** could have **said to you: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** it happened **that first** the bailee **took an oath,** before the thief was recognized. The Gemara questions this explanation: **But** Rav Huna bar Avin **says:** If **he** so **desires** he can choose to **enter** into **judgment with** the thief; and if **he** so **desires** he can **take an oath** that he was not responsible, indicating that the bailee had not yet taken an oath. The Gemara answers: Rather, **this is** what Rav Huna bar Avin **is saying:** Having first taken an oath before the thief was recognized, if **an unpaid bailee** so **desires** he can **remain with his oath** and not pay the owner, and if he so **desires** he can choose to **enter** into **judgment with** the thief.

Rava responds by attempting to reconcile his position with Rav Huna bar Avin's ruling. He suggests reading *ratza nishba* — "he may swear" — not as an original option available to the *shomer chinam*, but as a reference to a *shomer chinam* who had already jumped ahead and taken the oath before the *ganav* was found. In such a case, Rava allows, the *shomer* may elect to let the prior oath stand. But his original ruling remains: the *shomer*'s proper course, from the outset, is to pay the owner and pursue the *ganav*. He does not begin with the right to simply swear and exit.

The *gemara* presses back: the language of the ruling explicitly states *ratza oseh imo din ratza nishba* — "if he wants he may deal with the *ganav*, if he wants he may swear" — which grammatically presents a genuine initial choice, not merely a retrospective accommodation of a fait accompli. The forced nature of Rava's re-reading is acknowledged, and it is clear that Abaye and the position of Rav Huna bar Avin stand in genuine tension with Rava's view.

## Shomira After Theft

### Shomira Reinstatement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה זוּטֵי בָּעֵי לַהּ הָכִי: נִגְנְבָה בְּאוֹנֶס, וְהֶחְזִיר גַּנָּב בְּבֵית שׁוֹמֵר, וּמֵתָה בִּפְשִׁיעָה, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּנִגְנְבָה בְּאוֹנֶס – כָּלְיָא לֵיהּ שְׁמִירָתוֹ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּ[אַ]הְדְּרַהּ – הֲדַרָה לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba Zuti raises the dilemma like this:** If an animal given as a deposit **was stolen in circumstances beyond the control** of the bailee, **and** the **thief returned** the animal to the place from where he had stolen it, and it is now **in the house of the bailee, and it** then **died through** the bailee's **negligence, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **once it was stolen in circumstances beyond** the bailee's **control his guardianship is completed** and he has no more responsibility for the animal, even for subsequent negligence? **Or perhaps once it** was **returned, it returned to his guardianship,** and an unpaid bailee is liable for loss resulting from negligence? The Gemara comments: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava Zuti poses the final question of the daf. An object was stolen from a *shomer* by *ones* — clearly not the *shomer*'s fault — and then the *ganav* returned it, placing it back in the *shomer*'s premises. Subsequently, the *shomer* was negligent and the object was damaged. What is the *shomer*'s status?

The question is whether the *shomira* — the *shomer*'s custodial relationship with the object — was terminated at the moment of theft. Once it left his hands through no fault of his own, did his obligation as *shomer* come to an end? If so, when the *ganav* returned it without the owner's knowledge or instruction, the object simply arrived at the *shomer*'s home without reinstating the original custodial relationship. The *shomer* would therefore bear no liability for the subsequent negligence — he was no longer functioning as a *shomer* at that point, and the object should have been returned to the owner rather than to him.

Alternatively, once the object is returned to the *shomer*, the *shomira* is reinstated — *keivan d'hadra hadra l'shmirato* — and the *shomer* resumes his full obligations, including liability for *pshiah*. The question remains unresolved, and the daf concludes with *teiku*.
