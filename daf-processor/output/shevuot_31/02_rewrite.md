# Shevuot 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witnesses Standing

### Ameimar Seats Witnesses

Today's daf is 31. We pick up on the bottom of 30b, the second wide line, continuing to deal with *shvuat ha'edut*, but as a slight digression from that subject we are also examining how one comes to give testimony, how litigants stand, and how to balance *kavod* shown to rabbinic Torah authorities while still maintaining equal treatment during a court case.

The Gemara opens with a brief narrative. Rav Yemar knew testimony relevant to Mar Zutra and came before Ameimar, who was serving as judge. Ameimar seated all the witnesses — a departure from the standard procedure, in which witnesses stand and judges sit. Ameimar's concern was that having Rav Yemar stand before him would create a stark and disrespectful difference in power and status.

### Witnesses Must Stand

Rav Ashi raised an immediate objection. Had Ulla not ruled that the debate over whether litigants must stand pertains specifically to *ba'alei dinim* — the litigants themselves? But regarding witnesses, everyone agrees they must stand: *b'edim divrei hakol b'amida*. This is not merely a procedural preference; the verse *v'amdu shnei ha'anashim* establishes a positive obligation.

### Kavod Overrides Standing

Ameimar's response was that both obligations are in the category of *asseh*: the requirement for witnesses to stand is an *asseh* derived from *v'amdu shnei ha'anashim*, and the requirement to show *kavod* for a Torah authority is likewise an *asseh*. When two *asseh*s conflict, one must choose, and *asseh d'chevod hatorah adif* — the *asseh* of honoring the Torah takes precedence. Importantly, failing to have the witness stand would not invalidate the testimony, since the obligation operates at the level of an *asseh* and does not condition the validity of what is said. Furthermore, since Ameimar applied this ruling equally to all the witnesses, there was no concern of showing differential deference to one side.

## Judge Non-Defensive

### Midvar Sheker Tirchak

The Gemara now cites a *baraita*: *Minayin l'dayan shelo ya'aseh sanegor lidvarav* — how do we know that a judge should not become a defense attorney for his own words? The derivation is from *midvar sheker tirchak*, "distance yourself from words of falsehood."

### Sanegor Defense

The concern here is not about outright lying. Rashi explains that the problem arises when a judge takes a position, then begins to feel uncertain about it, and rather than honestly reassessing, moves into a defensive mode to protect what he said earlier. The English phrase "don't be so defensive" captures the idea precisely. A judge who has become defensive will not honestly evaluate the merits of the case; instead, he will marshal arguments to protect his prior position. Even though this does not involve saying a direct falsehood, channeling all one's energy into defending a stated position rather than remaining detached and open to revision leads inexorably to a false conclusion. Hence, *midvar sheker tirchak*.

### Perverting Truth

This opens a broader discussion of *midvar sheker tirchak*. Two features of this verse deserve emphasis. First, the word *tirchak* — "distance yourself" — indicates that the prohibition reaches beyond outright lying to encompass any influence or perversion of the truth, any process that makes an honest assessment less likely. Second, the verse appears in the context of the court and judges. The Gemara elsewhere permits altering facts for the sake of peace, *mutar lishanos mipnei hashalom*, citing the case of Yosef's brothers, who represented to Yosef that their father had asked him to forgive them — a claim the Gemara implies may not have been literally accurate. Similarly, the *Chasam Sofer* notes that there is a distinction between an outright lie and a technically accurate statement used to mislead, though the *Ramban* argues that the technical accuracy of one's words is irrelevant if the intent and effect are deceitful.

These discussions raise the broader question of whether there is an *issur* to lie in general — independent of the court context. The verse *midvar sheker tirchak* is primarily understood as operating in the setting of the *beit din*. So on the one hand it broadens the prohibition by using *tirchak* rather than a simple negative commandment against lying; on the other hand it is very much grounded in the judicial context specifically.

## Midvar Sheker Applicatio…

### Talmid Bur Exclusion

The *baraita* continues with a series of applications of *midvar sheker tirchak* to the courtroom. The first: *minayin l'dayan shelo yeishev talmid bur l'fanav* — how do we know that a judge should not have an ignorant student sitting before him? Rashi explains that *leshev l'fanav* includes bouncing ideas off of someone. If the judge's interlocutor lacks discernment, the conversation will lead him astray rather than toward the truth of the matter. Even without any intent to deceive, surrounding oneself with the wrong people creates conditions that undermine honest judgment. The derivation is again from *midvar sheker tirchak*.

### Gazlan Disqualification

The next case: *minayin l'dayan she-yodeia l'chaveiro she-hu gazlan, v'chein eid she-yodeia l'chaveiro she-hu gazlan* — how do we know that a judge who knows his fellow judge is a *gazlan*, or a witness who knows his fellow witness is a *gazlan*, should not join with him? The derivation is *midvar sheker tirchak*.

This requires some analysis. The *gazlan* is not necessarily lying; he may be testifying accurately about events he genuinely witnessed. He is simply *pasul* on technical grounds. One might therefore reason: as long as they are telling the truth, what is the problem with participating alongside a technically invalid witness? The answer, as Rashi points out, is that by going forward — by allowing the court to accept testimony that the technical laws of evidence would disqualify — one is participating in a procedural deception, even if the underlying facts happen to be accurate. That too is an element of *sheker* in the process.

This principle is illustrated by a question posed to the *Rashba*. A man claimed his adversary genuinely owed him money, but he lacked sufficient evidence to prove it in court. He asked whether, based on the principle of *adam dan dina l'nafsheih* — the right to take the law into one's own hands — he might be permitted to forge evidence and bring it to court, since he was confident the debt was real. The *Rashba* rejected this, explaining that the right to act in self-help does not extend to perverting the procedures of the court. Here too, the problem is not only in the final result but in the process itself.

### Suspect Corruption

A further application: *minayin l'dayan she-yodeia be-din she-hu merumeh* — how does a judge who has come to believe that a case before him is corrupt know that he may not simply go along with it? The scenario is one where the judge cannot identify the precise lie — perhaps he saw the witnesses conferring with a party outside, or some other circumstantial sign — but it is clear to him that the entire proceeding is being manipulated.

One might think the judge can reason: *ho'il v'eidim me'idim*, witnesses have testified, the technical requirements have been met, and *yei kolar talui b'tzavar eidim* — let whatever blame there is fall upon the witnesses; I will simply rule on the evidence before me. This is not permitted. *Midvar sheker tirchak*: a judge may not be party to a falsehood even without having committed a lie himself, even without knowing precisely where the lie is located.

What exactly the judge is supposed to do in such circumstances — whether he can recuse himself mid-case, what procedural options are available — the Gemara does not specify. But it is unambiguous that simply going along and issuing a ruling while knowing the proceeding is corrupt is not an option. Notably, the judge also cannot resolve the problem by ruling in favor of the party he suspects is being wronged without a legal basis to do so: he lacks the evidence to support a contrary ruling.

### Student Correction

Next: *minayin l'talmid she-yoshev lifnei rabbo v'ra'ah zechut l'ani v'chov l'ashir minayin shelo yishtok* — how do we know that a student sitting before his *rebbe* who perceives that a poor litigant has merit, and that a rich litigant actually has a debit in the case, may not remain silent? The student faces powerful social pressures: the *kavod* owed to his *rebbe*, the social weight of a wealthy and prominent opponent, and the fact that he is not even lying — he is merely withholding information. *Midvar sheker tirchak*: silence in the face of a miscarriage of justice is itself a form of distance one must keep from falsehood.

### No Strategic Silence

The final application in this series sharpens the point. *Minayin l'talmid she-ra'ah rabbo she-to'eh be-din* — how do we know that a student who perceives his *rebbe* is making an error in judgment may not say to himself: I will let my *rebbe* go down this wrong path, allow him to reach his erroneous conclusion, and then, in order to build myself up — *avastrenu v'avneno misheli k'dei she'yikra hadin al shemi* — demolish his position and reconstruct it properly, so that the ruling is named after me?

Here the student's motive is far from pure; he seeks to inflate himself at his *rebbe*'s expense. But the Gemara's concern goes beyond his bad intent. By waiting — allowing a misquoted source or a flawed argument to stand uncorrected for even an hour while the entire discussion proceeds on that false premise — the student is countenancing a perversion of the truth for the duration of his strategic silence. *Midvar sheker tirchak* requires him to speak up the moment he perceives the error, not when it becomes advantageous for him to do so.

## Witness Pressure Cas (II)

### Coerced Testimony

A further series of applications follows. *Linnoshe bachavero maneh shelo yomar lo* — regarding someone owed a hundred dollars by another, the *baraita* addresses a gambit: he goes to court claiming two hundred dollars, reasoning that the debtor — being an honest man — will admit to a hundred, thereby becoming a *modeh b'miktzat*, one who partially admits a debt. This obligates the debtor in an oath (*yitchayiv li shvua*), and once he must take one oath the creditor can leverage that to force additional oaths about other disputed debts — *v'agalgela alav shvua mimakom acher*. The creditor is not falsifying the facts of what is owed; he is making a deliberately inflated claim solely to manipulate the court process and compel oaths. The derivation forbidding this: *midvar sheker tirchak*.

It might seem surprising. Every pleading in modern civil litigation opens with an inflated demand for damages. The distinction, however, is between asking a court to award a particular amount in compensation — a claim about what one wants — and misrepresenting the underlying facts of the debt itself. The gambit the Gemara is prohibiting is the latter: asserting that someone owes two hundred when you know the sum is one hundred, purely to manufacture a procedural advantage.

The Gemara then poses the same question from the debtor's side. If the creditor has in fact claimed two hundred, the debtor faces the following temptation: deny the entire claim in *beit din* so as to avoid being a *modeh b'miktzat*, and then privately admit the hundred-dollar debt and pay it outside of court. This avoids the oath obligation entirely. The Gemara forbids this strategy as well, despite the fact that the creditor's original claim was itself a lie. The debtor may not respond to a lie in the proceeding by telling his own lie. *Midvar sheker tirchak*.

The Gemara then asks: the case that Jacob must not join the two of you as a false witness — is that really just *midvar sheker tirchak*? Is this not an outright lie, subject to the prohibition of *lo ta'aneh v'reacha ed sheker*? The Gemara clarifies: the truly outright case, where Jacob testifies to knowledge he does not have, is indeed a transgression of *lo ta'aneh v'reacha ed sheker*, bearing false witness. The *midvar sheker tirchak* case is the subtler one: suppose the creditor says to Jacob — I definitely have one witness, Chanan, who saw that Michael borrowed the money. Go stand with Chanan in court. Don't say anything. Just stand there. Michael will assume you are about to testify as well, and the pressure of believing two witnesses are present will induce him to admit the debt. *V'lo tima v'lo midi* — I'm not asking you to say a single false word. Your presence does the work.

Even this — standing silently in a way that communicates to Michael that you possess knowledge you do not possess — is forbidden by *midvar sheker tirchak*. The prohibition extends to any conduct that conveys a false impression, even without a spoken word, even when the underlying fact (that Michael owes the money) is perfectly true. The Ramban makes a parallel observation in the narrative of Avraham and Avimelech: it is irrelevant whether the words spoken were technically accurate if the intended and achieved effect was deception.

### Multiple Creditors

*Minayin lishelosha sheno'shin maneh b'echad* — how do we know that if three parties have each lent money to a fourth, they may not arrange themselves so that one plays the role of plaintiff while the other two pose as disinterested witnesses? They are all parties to the case — *ba'alei davar* — and therefore not qualified to testify for one another. Yet the facts they would be testifying to are true: the money was indeed borrowed. The problem is procedural deception: presenting themselves as witnesses when the technical laws of evidence disqualify them from that role. *Midvar sheker tirchak*.

## Dress Equality

### Litigant Dress

*Minayin lishnayim sheba'u ladin, echad lavush smartutin v'echad lavush itzalit bat meah maneh* — how do we know that when two litigants come to court, one dressed in rags and the other wearing a garment worth a hundred *maneh* (a lavish designer outfit, as it were), the court instructs the wealthy one to either dress down to a comparable level or to bring the other to his tailor and clothe him equivalently? *Midvar sheker tirchak*.

The reasoning is transparent: people are human and respond to status cues. A litigant who appears wealthy and well-dressed will have his words treated with greater weight and deference by the court, whether consciously or not. By appearing in dramatically superior dress, the wealthier party is, in effect, tilting the proceeding in his favor — creating a procedural falsehood that skews the weight given to each side's arguments. Even if the wealthy litigant genuinely believes he has truth on his side, that confidence does not entitle him to exploit the psychological dynamics of status. Responsibility here is not his alone; the court itself is presumably entitled and obligated to insist on comparability of dress — much as defendants in criminal proceedings are dressed in ordinary clothing rather than prison garb when they appear before a jury.

### Removing Fancy Shoes

The Gemara reports that when witnesses would come before Rava bar Rav Huna, he would say to them: *shulufu puzmukaichu v'nachatu ledina* — remove your fine shoes and come down to the judgment. The *puzmukeichu* were apparently an elaborate form of footwear. Even the difference between expensive and ordinary shoes was sufficient to create a disparity of status that Rava bar Rav Huna would not tolerate.

The word *v'nachatu* — "come down" — is also noteworthy. Shoes are *maamidim et ha'adam*; they lend a person stature and elevation. There is an element of humbling before the proceeding that Rava bar Rav Huna may have been cultivating — having all parties appear barefoot before the court. Whether the same concern about comparable dress would apply to witnesses as well as litigants, so that one side's witnesses cannot appear significantly more prestigious than the other side's witnesses, is an interesting question the Gemara leaves open.

## Witness Pressure Cas (II)

### Power of Attorney

The passage cites the verse from *Yechezkel*, *va'asher lo tov asa besoch amo*, describing one who does not do right in his nation. Rav interprets this as referring to a person who comes into a case with a *horsha'ah* — a power of attorney. *Tosafot* explains the concern precisely: the problem is not with all representatives, but specifically with one who comes because *hu alim u'va'al ta'anot* — he is imposing, forceful, and skilled in argumentation. What may have been a balanced dispute between two ordinary parties is suddenly transformed when one side introduces a powerful legal advocate. The other side cannot compete. This, says *Tosafot*, is the case of *lo tov asa*. By contrast, if Michael is traveling to Israel and simply asks a friend to collect money owed to him — a pure act of assistance — there is nothing improper about granting that power of attorney. The question of where the legitimate business arrangement ends and the unfair manipulation of the proceeding begins is one of degree and intent.

Shmuel offers a different referent for *lo tov asa*: one who purchases a field that has legal claims (*asikin*) against it. The buyer knows that the encumbrances have driven the price far below market value, and he has the legal muscle — financial resources, lawyers, connections — to defeat those claims. He acquires the property at a fraction of its worth, at the expense of the original holder who lacked the means to navigate the legal process effectively. Whether this constitutes simply seizing a good business opportunity or genuinely wronging the original claimant is a line that is not always clear.

## Both Parties Present

### Anchoring Bias

*Minalan d'dayan shelo yishma divrei ba'al din kodem sheyavo ba'al din chaveiro* — how do we know that a judge may not hear one litigant's presentation of his case before the other party has arrived? The derivation is *midvar sheker tirchak*.

Two concerns underlie this rule. First, the absent party is not present to correct misstatements or provide counter-evidence, which invites falsehood. Second — and more subtle — there is a well-recognized phenomenon (sometimes called "anchoring") by which the first framing of a situation shapes all subsequent evaluation. Once a judge has heard a case presented in a particular structure, it becomes very difficult to evaluate the opposing party's arguments on their own terms; the judge will almost inevitably process them through the lens of the initial presentation. Even if the first party is not intentionally deceiving anyone and is simply telling his story as he understands it, the effect is to pervert the judge's impartiality. The judge is not allowed to permit this, even when there is no deliberate manipulation.

### Persuasive Presentation

*Minalan l'ba'al din shelo yat'im devarav l'dayan kodem sheyavo ba'al din chaveiro* — and how do we know that the litigant himself may not present his case — giving it *ta'am*, making it attractive and palatable — before the other party arrives? *Midvar sheker tirchak*.

This is distinct from the general right of a litigant to advocate. The litigants are permitted — indeed expected — to present their narratives persuasively when both parties are before the court. The role of opening and closing statements, the right to tell one's story and not merely recite bare facts: all of this is legitimate *hataamah*. The court does not require litigants to speak in neutral, objective terms. The prohibition is specifically about doing so *before* the other party has arrived, where the persuasive presentation cannot be countered in real time. Under those conditions, even legitimate advocacy becomes an instrument of distortion.

Rav Kahana frames this point through an alternative derivation: *lo tisa shema shav* — do not carry a false report. By presenting one's case in the absence of the opposing party, one is, in effect, transmitting a false report into the mind of the judge — even if every word spoken was literally true.

## Shvuat Ha'Edut Scope

### Mina Debate

The Gemara now turns to a significant legal debate between Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan* regarding the scope of *shvuat ha'edut*. The issue: is a person liable for *shvuat ha'edut* when the denial and oath occur outside of *beit din*, or only when they occur in the presence of *beit din*?

The analytical framework involves two mechanisms of legal analogy. The verse governing *shvuat ha'edut* (*v'hu ed o ra'ah o yada im lo yagid*) establishes liability when the oath is imposed *mipi acheirim* — by others — and contextually locates this in a *beit din* setting. The verse governing *shvuat ha'pikadon* establishes liability for a self-imposed oath — *mipi atzmo* — and does not specify a *beit din* context; it refers to any oath taken falsely regarding a deposit. Each case has something the other lacks: *shvuat ha'edut* has the *beit din* context; *shvuat ha'pikadon* has the *mipi atzmo* mechanism.

Both Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan* agree that *shvuat ha'edut* generates liability when the oath is self-imposed (*mipi atzmo*), which they learn from *shvuat ha'pikadon*. Their dispute is about what happens once that *halacha* is imported. Rabbi Meir holds *don mina u'mina*: you learn the *halacha* from its source and you retain its original context. Since *shvuat ha'pikadon* is not limited to *beit din*, the *mipi atzmo* liability in *shvuat ha'edut* likewise extends outside *beit din*. The *Rabbanan* hold *don mina v'oki b'atrah*: you learn the *halacha* from its source but then transplant it into the context of the target law. Since *shvuat ha'edut* is fundamentally a *beit din*-based obligation, once *mipi atzmo* is imported, it takes on that context and is limited to *beit din*.

Expressed visually: Rabbi Meir imports *mipi atzmo* from the *pikadon* context (outside *beit din*) and carries that context with it into *shvuat ha'edut*. The *Rabbanan* import *mipi atzmo* but immediately situate it in the *edut* context (inside *beit din*), discarding the original *pikadon* context.

### Rav Papa Explanation

The *Rabbanan* of the *beit midrash* asked Rav Papa whether he could really say the debate is not about *don mina u'mina* versus *don mina v'oki b'atrah*. Rav Papa responded that if the *Rabbanan*'s learning of *mipi atzmo* were derived from *pikadon*, then everyone — even the *Rabbanan* — would hold *don mina u'mina* and concede that *mipi atzmo* applies even outside *beit din*. Rather, *ela hainu ta'ama d'Rabbanan*: the *Rabbanan* do not learn *mipi atzmo* from *pikadon* at all. They stay entirely within the world of *shvuat ha'edut* and derive *mipi atzmo* via *kal v'chomer*: if one is liable when someone else imposes the oath, certainly one is liable when one imposes it upon oneself. This derivation is self-contained within the *beit din* context of *shvuat ha'edut*, and by the rule of *dayo* — the sufficiency principle of *kal v'chomer* — the conclusion cannot exceed its premise. Since the premise (*mipi acherim*) is limited to *beit din*, the conclusion (*mipi atzmo*) is likewise limited to *beit din*.

The *Rabbanan* of the *beit midrash* then pressed Rav Papa further: surely you can prove the debate really is about *don mina u'mina*, because the positions reverse in the parallel case of *shvuat ha'pikadon*, and that reversal is explicable only on the basis of this exact methodological disagreement.

### Pikadon Mirror

The next *mishnah* establishes that *shvuat ha'pikadon* applies to men and women, relatives and non-relatives, qualified witnesses and disqualified ones, in the presence of *beit din* and outside *beit din* — but only *mipi atzmo*. Liability for *shvuat ha'pikadon* when the oath is imposed *mipi acherim* is not stated in the verse; it must be derived from *shvuat ha'edut*. This generates the mirror-image debate.

Rabbi Meir rules that by *pikadon*, one is liable *mipi acherim* only if the denial occurs in *beit din*. The *Rabbanan* rule that one is liable *mipi acherim* whether the denial occurs in *beit din* or outside *beit din*. This is the exact reversal of their positions in the *edut* case: Rabbi Meir was more expansive about *edut* (outside *beit din* included) but more limited about *pikadon* (*beit din* only); the *Rabbanan* were more limited about *edut* (*beit din* only) but more expansive about *pikadon* (anywhere).

The logic is perfectly symmetrical and confirms the methodological underpinning of the debate. Rabbi Meir holds *don mina u'mina*: you retain the original context of the source. When learning *mipi atzmo* for *shvuat ha'edut* from *pikadon*, the original context is *outside beit din*, so that becomes the operative context. When learning *mipi acherim* for *shvuat ha'pikadon* from *edut*, the original context is *inside beit din* — so that becomes the operative context. The *Rabbanan* hold *don mina v'oki b'atrah*: you always apply the context of the target law. When the target law is *shvuat ha'edut*, the context is *beit din*, so *mipi atzmo* is limited there. When the target law is *shvuat ha'pikadon*, the context is *outside beit din* (since *pikadon* has no *beit din* requirement), so *mipi acherim* extends there too. Whichever direction one goes, Rabbi Meir preserves the source context and the *Rabbanan* apply the target context — consistently, regardless of whether the result is more or less expansive.

The Gemara then asks: from where do the *Rabbanan* know that *mipi acherim* generates liability in *shvuat ha'pikadon*? It cannot be derived by *kal v'chomer*, since *mipi acherim* is, if anything, a weaker basis for liability than *mipi atzmo* — there is no logical inference from "you are liable when someone else imposes it" that could be *more* compelling than "you are liable when you impose it yourself." The only possible derivation is from *shvuat ha'edut*, which confirms that the *Rabbanan* do learn across these two domains when necessary — and that the methodological debate of *don mina u'mina* versus *don mina v'oki b'atrah* is precisely what drives the reversal of positions.

The Gemara concludes: *miha in* — yes, from the *pikadon mishnah* I concede that that is indeed the debate. The objection Rav Papa raised — that one could not prove the debate from the *edut* case alone, since there a *kal v'chomer* was available — is well-taken; but looking at the *pikadon* case, where no *kal v'chomer* exists, confirms unambiguously that the dispute concerns the methodology of *don mina u'mina* versus *don mina v'oki b'atrah*.

## Oath-Taker Status

### Mipi Atzmo Outside

Rabbi Meir's position — *don mina u'mina* — means that when *mipi atzmo* is imported from *shvuat ha'pikadon* into *shvuat ha'edut*, it carries with it the original context of *pikadon*, which is not limited to *beit din*. Consequently, Rabbi Meir holds that one who spontaneously takes a false oath denying knowledge of testimony is liable for *shvuat ha'edut* even if this occurs outside of *beit din*, provided that the oath is self-imposed.

### Mipi Atzmo Inside Only

The *Rabbanan*, applying *don mina v'oki b'atrah*, situate the imported *mipi atzmo* within the context of *shvuat ha'edut* — a law whose operative sphere is *beit din*. Consequently, they hold that *mipi atzmo* liability for *shvuat ha'edut* applies only within *beit din*, not outside it. Since the *Rabbanan* derive *mipi atzmo* from a self-contained *kal v'chomer* within the domain of *shvuat ha'edut*, they have no occasion to look to *pikadon* and no reason to absorb its context.

## Disqualified Witnesses

### King Afuki

The *mishnah* states that *shvuat ha'edut* applies only to someone who is qualified to testify. Rav Papa identifies one category the *mishnah* is excluding: the *melech*, the king. A king is not technically *pasul* — there is no formal disqualification — but he cannot be brought to court to testify; doing so would be incompatible with the honor of his position. Since the king is not *ra'uy l'ha'id* in practice, he falls outside the scope of *shvuat ha'edut*.

### Dice Player

Rav Acha the son of Rav Yaakov identifies a second category: the *mesachek b'kubiya*, the dice player. Unlike the king, the dice player is explicitly *pasul* — but only *d'Rabbanan*, on rabbinic grounds. The disqualification stems either from the *asmachta* problem (winnings from gambling may not constitute a valid acquisition) or from the fact that one who does not engage in a productive occupation lacks the social accountability that renders testimony reliable. But from the Torah's perspective, he is a fully valid witness.

### Rabbinic Effect

This generates a genuinely interesting question. From the biblical standpoint, the dice player can testify; his oath is therefore a valid *shvuat ha'edut*, and lying under it should obligate him in a *korban*. From the practical standpoint, any time he appeared in court to testify, the court would send him home — his testimony is not accepted. If the court would never accept his testimony regardless, does it make sense to hold him liable in a *korban* for *shvuat ha'edut*?

The argument for exempting him is intuitively compelling: since as a practical matter the court will reject him, the *shvuat ha'edut* framework — premised on the denial of testimony that could actually affect a case — never becomes operative for him. Whether the rabbinic disqualification is sufficient to override the biblical liability is the question, and it illustrates the broader problem of how a *d'Rabbanan* ruling intersects with and potentially displaces *d'Oraita* consequences.

## Liability Cases

### Meizid Liability

The *mishnah* states that one is liable for *shvuat ha'edut* even if one acts willfully — *meizid*. The Gemara derives this from a comparison with other *oleh v'yored* obligations (where the Torah explicitly uses the phrase *v'ne'elam*, "and it was hidden from him," indicating a requirement of inadvertence). In the laws of *shvuat ha'edut*, the verse does not include *v'ne'elam*. This omission is deliberate: it signals that one is liable *l'chayev al hameizid k'shogeg*, even for a willful violation, just as one would be for an inadvertent one.

### Shogeg Liability

The *mishnah* further states that one is liable for a *shogeg* of a particular type — *shigagato im zedonah ha'edut*. This formulation means that one is *shogeg* with respect to one element (the *korban* obligation) while being *meizid* with respect to another (the oath itself). One is not liable for a pure *shogeg* — *shigagatah greidah* — without any element of willfulness.

### Shogeg Definition

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav defines the actionable *shogeg* case precisely: *be'omer yodea ani sheshvua zo asura, aval eini yodea im chayavin aleha korban im lav* — I know this oath is forbidden, but I do not know whether one incurs a *korban* obligation for it. That is the case of *shogeg* in which one is liable. One knows one is lying; one knows taking a false oath is *assur*; but one is mistaken about whether the consequence is a *korban*. Liability follows because the *meizid* element — deliberate lying — is present, even if the *korban* obligation was unknown.

### Kahana and Assi

The Gemara then asks: is the *mishnah*'s exemption of a pure *shogeg* simply confirming what was already established by the famous case of Rav Kahana and Rav Assi? In that case, each took an oath as to what Rav had ruled on a certain matter, and one of them was mistaken. When they came before Rav and discovered the error, the one who had sworn falsely asked whether he had transgressed. Rav replied: *libcha anascha* — your heart compelled you; you genuinely believed what you said was true. Thinking one is telling the truth, even while factually mistaken, does not constitute a false oath.

Was that case not already implicit in the *mishnah*'s exemption of *shigagatah greidah*? The Gemara answers: *af al gav detanan, itzterich* — even though the *mishnah* states the exemption, the case of Rav Kahana and Rav Assi was still a *chiddush* that needed to be taught. The reasoning: one might have thought that since *shvuat ha'edut* does not use the phrase *v'ne'elam*, it requires an element analogous to *meizid* — and perhaps even believing one is telling the truth is not sufficient to remove liability entirely, because one ought to have verified the facts. In contexts where the Torah does write *v'ne'elam*, perhaps even pure inadvertence generates a *korban* for other oath types (*shvuat bitui*, for example), and perhaps the absence of *v'ne'elam* from *shvuat ha'edut* means something different altogether. The case of Rav Kahana and Rav Assi teaches unambiguously that genuinely believing one is telling the truth exempts completely — not only from the *korban* but from any transgression of false oath.

*Tosafot* adds a further point: the *mishnah*'s exemption of a pure *shogeg* may also encompass the case where one knows one is making a false statement but genuinely believes it is *muttar* to do so — that there is no prohibition against taking a false oath. Whether such a person has sufficient *meizid* consciousness to incur liability under *shvuat ha'edut* is a question *Tosafot* leaves open. The only *shogeg* explicitly identified by the Gemara as generating liability is the one defined by Rav: knowing one is lying and knowing it is forbidden, but being ignorant of the *korban* obligation.
