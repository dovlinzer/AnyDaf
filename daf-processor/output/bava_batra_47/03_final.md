# Bava Batra 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Amalek Siman - Four Cases

### Opening Remarks

The daf we take up today picks up from the bottom of 46, the second-to-last line, returning to the *siman* known as the *Amalek siman*. Some of the *acharonim* on the *Gemara* are interested in why *Chazal* chose specifically these four cases and organized them around a *siman* drawn from the name *Amalek* — a name we are simultaneously commanded to remember and to blot out. But whatever the reason, it serves as an effective mnemonic.

### Four Testimony Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (עֲמָלֵק סִימָן)
> תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עָרֵב מֵעִיד לַלֹּוֶה, וְהוּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אַרְעָא אַחֲרִיתִי. מַלְוֶה מֵעִיד לַלֹּוֶה, וְהוּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אַרְעָא אַחֲרִיתִי. לוֹקֵחַ רִאשׁוֹן מֵעִיד לְלוֹקֵחַ שֵׁנִי, וְהוּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אַרְעָא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents the word *Amalek* as **a mnemonic** for the cases discussed in the *baraita*. It stands for: ***Ayin*,** guarantor [*arev*]; ***mem*,** creditor [*malve*]; ***lamed*,** buyer [*loke'aḥ*]; ***kuf*,** unconditional guarantor [*kablan*]. **The Sages taught: A guarantor can testify for** the benefit of **the debtor** that the latter owns a particular parcel of land, **but that is** the *halakha* only **if** the debtor **has other land** from which the creditor can collect the debt. Otherwise, he is biased in his testimony, as the creditor could collect from him if the debtor were to lose ownership of this land. **A creditor can testify for** the benefit of **the debtor** that the latter owns a particular parcel of land, **but that is** the *halakha* only **if** the debtor **has other land** from which the creditor can collect. Otherwise, he is biased in his testimony, as this land is the only land available for collection. Similarly, if two people purchase land from one seller, the **first buyer can testify for** the benefit of the **second buyer** if someone else were to claim that the land was his, **but that is** the *halakha* only **if** the second buyer **has other land** that he purchased from the same seller either concurrent with or subsequent to the first buyer's purchase of the land in question. A creditor of the seller can collect a debt from the land that the seller sold most recently. Therefore, if the second buyer purchased only the land in question from the seller, the first buyer is biased in his testimony, as the second buyer's ownership of the land prevents the creditor from collecting a debt from the land from the first buyer.

The *Gemara* opens with a *tano rabbanan* listing four types of witnesses whose testimony might be suspected as self-interested, and the conditions under which their testimony is nevertheless valid. The organizing principle is the concept of *nogeia badavar* — being personally implicated in the outcome of one's testimony — and the question of when such an interest rises to the level that disqualifies the witness.

### Areiv Testimony

The first case involves an *areiv* — a cosigner on a loan. One might wonder why anyone would agree to cosign a loan, and indeed bankers sometimes refer to such individuals with colorful contempt. Yet in halachic life, serving as an *areiv* can function as a form of *tzedakah*, helping someone obtain a loan they could not otherwise secure.

The concern is that the *areiv* is *nogeia badavar*: if the *loveh* has no attachable property, the *malveh* may come to collect from the *areiv* directly. So if the *areiv* testifies that a particular property belongs to the *loveh*, he appears to be protecting his own interests by increasing the pool of assets available to satisfy the debt. The *tano rabbanan* rules that the *areiv* is not considered *nogeia badavar* to the extent of disqualification — provided that the *loveh* has other properties apart from the one being testified about. If the *loveh* already has *ara'a achriti*, other fields, then the creditor will not so readily turn to the *areiv*, and therefore the *areiv*'s testimony about this specific property carries no particular benefit to himself.

### Malveh Testimony

The second case concerns the *malveh* himself testifying on behalf of the *loveh* — asserting that a particular property belongs to the *loveh* — again *v'hu d'is leh ara'a achriti*, provided the *loveh* has other properties. Although the *malveh* has an even closer financial relationship to the outcome than the *areiv*, since he stands to gain directly from establishing that the *loveh* has assets, the same limiting condition applies: as long as the *loveh* possesses other properties, the *malveh*'s testimony about this particular field does not confer any specific advantage on him.

There is a further nuance worth noting here that the *Rashbam* addresses at the top of 47a: a *ba'al chov* collects from *beinonit*, medium-quality land, and so if a *loveh* holds only *ziburit*, low-quality land, the *malveh* might be especially interested in testifying that a *beinonit* field belongs to the *loveh*, since that would provide a more attractive collection target. This subtlety will become directly relevant in the *kablan* discussion that follows.

### Buyers' Testimony

The third case is the *loka'ach rishon* testifying on behalf of the *loka'ach sheini*. The rule in debt collection from sold properties is that one proceeds against the most recently sold property first. If a seller sold one field to a first buyer and subsequently sold another field to a second buyer, and a creditor now wishes to collect from encumbered property, the second buyer's field is targeted first. Thus the first buyer has an obvious interest in establishing that the second buyer owns the property: he is effectively using the second buyer as a buffer.

Nevertheless, the *Gemara* rules that the first buyer may testify — again *v'hu d'is leh ara'a achriti*, as long as the second buyer has other properties. In such a case, confirming that this particular field belongs to the second buyer does not materially enhance the first buyer's protection of his own purchase.

## Kablan Areiv

### Kablan Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבְּלָן – אָמְרִי לַהּ מֵעִיד, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֵינוֹ מֵעִיד. אָמְרִי לַהּ מֵעִיד – כְּעָרֵב דָּמֵי. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֵינוֹ מֵעִיד – דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלֶהֱווֹ בִּידֵיהּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ, דְּכִי אָתֵי בַּעַל חוֹב – מַאי דְּבָעֵי שָׁקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an unconditional guarantor [*kablan*],** from whom the creditor can collect even if the debtor is able to repay the loan, **some say** that he **can testify** on behalf of the debtor if the latter owns other land, **and some say** that he **cannot testify** even if the debtor owns other land. The Gemara explains: **Some say** that he **can testify** because he is **like a guarantor, and some say** that he **cannot testify,** as it is **preferable for him that both** fields **be in** the debtor's **possession,** so **that when a creditor comes** to collect the debt, he will **take what he wants,** and not collect from the unconditional guarantor.

We now reach the *kuf* of the *Amalek siman* — the *kablan*. The *Rashbam* clarifies that this is not the ordinary *kablan* in the sense of a hired worker, but specifically a *kablan areiv* — a special form of cosigner who has accepted more stringent terms than the ordinary *areiv*. Whereas with a regular *areiv*, the creditor must first attempt to collect from the *loveh* before turning to the *areiv*, the *kablan areiv* has agreed that the creditor may come to him immediately, without first pursuing the *loveh*.

### Kablan Testimony Dispute

Whether this *kablan* may testify is itself a *machloket*. *Amrei lah me'id* — there are those who say he may testify and is not considered *nogeia badavar*. *Ve'amrei lah eino me'id* — and there are those who say he may not.

### Nogeia B'Eidus Issue

The opinion that the *kablan* cannot testify reasons as follows. Since the creditor may collect from the *kablan* immediately, without first exhausting the *loveh*'s assets, the *kablan* has a concrete stake in whether the *loveh* holds *beinonit* or only *ziburit*. A *ba'al chov* is entitled to collect from *beinonit*, so if the *loveh* has no *beinonit* remaining — only *ziburit* — the creditor will be, as it were, pushed toward collecting from the *kablan*. By testifying that this property belongs to the *loveh*, the *kablan* would be increasing the pool of *beinonit* available from the *loveh*, thereby protecting himself. This is the basis for the view that he is *nogeia b'eidus* and may not testify.

### Kablan Can Testify

The opinion permitting the *kablan* to testify holds *k'areiv dami* — that his status is functionally equivalent to that of a regular *areiv*, and the benefit he derives from his testimony is sufficiently minimal that we do not disqualify him on that basis. The reasoning is that since the creditor can come to the *kablan* first regardless, the benefit the *kablan* gains by establishing the *loveh*'s ownership of this particular property is marginal.

### Kablan Benefit Analysis

The underlying *machloket* concerns how one understands the *kablan*'s relationship to the *loveh*'s assets. Does the *kablan*, precisely because he can be collected from immediately, care less about what the *loveh* owns — and is therefore less *nogeia badavar*? Or does he care more, because he wants the *loveh* to appear as an attractive target for collection so that the creditor will go there rather than to him directly? Both perspectives have internal logic, which is why the *Gemara* presents this as a genuine dispute.

### Ba'al Chov Collection

The *Gemara* notes that in both opinions — *tarvayhu* — when the *ba'al chov* comes to collect, *mai d'baei shakil*, he may take whatever he wants. That is the fundamental nature of the *kablan areiv* arrangement: the creditor retains full discretion over how and from whom to collect.

### Ri Megash vs Rashbam

There is a further difference of opinion between the *Ri Megash* and the *Rashbam* regarding the underlying nature of the *kablan* relationship. The question is how this arrangement came to be. Did the *kablan* himself borrow the money from the *malveh* and then lend it on to the *loveh* — in effect functioning as a direct lender in a two-stage transaction? In that case, his relationship to the *loveh* is simply that of a *malveh*. Alternatively, did he enter the arrangement as an *areiv* who simply accepted more demanding terms — agreeing to be collectible immediately? These two framings carry implications for how one analyzes related cases, though in the specific case at hand the practical ruling is agreed upon. There is considerably more to explore here in comparison to the *sugyot* of *Bava Metzia*.

## Craftsman & Thief Cases

### Uman & Aris Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוּמָּן אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, בֶּן אוּמָּן יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה. אָרִיס אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, בֶּן אָרִיס יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה. גַּזְלָן וּבֶן גַּזְלָן אֵין לָהֶן חֲזָקָה, בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל גַּזְלָן יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A craftsman does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership, but the **son of a craftsman has** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership. **A sharecropper does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership, but the **son of a sharecropper has** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership. Both **a robber and** the **son of a robber do not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership, but the **son of** the **son of a robber has** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership.

The *Gemara* now transitions to a teaching of *Rebbi Yochanan*. The *Mishnah* had already established that a craftsman — an *uman* — does not have a *chazakah* on objects in his possession, since we presume that an *uman* holding, say, a pair of shoes is holding them because he is repairing them for someone else. *Rebbi Yochanan* extends and refines this: *uman ein lo chazakah, ben uman yesh lo chazakah*. The craftsman himself has no *chazakah*, but the son of the craftsman does.

The scenario is one in which someone later comes and claims ownership of what is in the craftsman's household. The son — who is not himself a craftsman — can say: just because my father was a repairman does not mean I cannot own these items. Of course, the *chazakah* still requires *chazakah sheyesh imah ta'anah*, a credible accompanying claim, and the *Gemara* will elaborate on what that claim must look like.

Similarly: *aris ein lo chazakah, ben aris yesh lo chazakah*. A sharecropper working on someone else's land for three years does not thereby establish ownership, since the fact of his being an *aris* explains his presence. But his son — who is not himself an *aris* — can create a *chazakah* through three years of uncontested use, provided there is no *mecha'ah*. The principle is clear: it is not a *chazakah* on land generally but specifically on that land, and it applies only to the individual who held the agricultural relationship, not to one who inherited the land or came to use it in his own right.

### Gazlan Hierarchy

The rule extends further — but with a significant distinction. *Gazlan u'ven gazlan* — a thief and the son of a thief — neither of them can establish a *chazakah*. *Aval ben b'no shel gazlan, yesh lo chazakah* — but the grandson of a thief does have the ability to create a *chazakah*.

The immediate question is why the son of a thief is treated more harshly than the son of a craftsman or the son of a sharecropper. After all, the son of a thief is not himself a thief. The *Gemara* will probe this carefully.

### Gemara's Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי אָתוּ בְּטַעְנְתָא דַאֲבוּהוֹן – אֲפִילּוּ הָנָךְ נָמֵי לָא. אִי דְּלָא אָתוּ בְּטַעְנְתָא דַאֲבוּהוֹן – אֲפִילּוּ בֶּן גַּזְלָן נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** under which there is a distinction between the sons of the craftsman and the sharecropper and the son of the robber? **If** they **come** to court **with the claim** that the item in question **belonged to their fathers,** then **even these** sons of the craftsman and the sharecropper should **not** be able to establish the presumption of ownership, since their claims are based on ownership by those who cannot establish the presumption of ownership. **If** the case is **that** they **do not come** to court **with the claim** that the item in question **belonged to their fathers,** but that they own the item in their own right, then **even** the **son of a robber** should be able to establish the presumption of ownership.

The *Gemara* asks: *heichi dami?* What case is *Rebbi Yochanan* describing? If the children come *b'ta'anta d'avuhon* — with the same claim as their fathers — then obviously none of them should have a *chazakah*. If the *ben uman* says "my father received these shoes for repair," that is simply restating the father's relationship, not an independent claim. And *afilu ben gazlan nami* — on the other side, if all three categories of children come without their father's claim, then even the son of a thief should be believed.

So what is the intermediate case? The *Gemara* answers: *tzericha, d'ka'amri edim be-fanenu hodeh lo* — there are witnesses who say that in front of them, the original owner (*mara kamma*) admitted to the father that the property belongs to him. This is the fact pattern: the *mara kamma* acknowledged the father's ownership before witnesses, and now the son has inherited.

### Witness Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָא אָמְרִי עֵדִים: ״בְּפָנֵינוּ הוֹדָה לוֹ״. הָנָךְ – אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר קוּשְׁטָא קָא אָמְרִי. הַאי – אַף עַל גַּב דְּאוֹדִי נָמֵי לָא מְהֵימַן, כִּדְרַב כָּהֲנָא – דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: אִי לָאו דְּאוֹדִי לֵיהּ, הֲוָה מַמְטֵי לֵיהּ וּלְחַמְרֵיהּ לְשַׁחְווֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this distinction in a case **where** the **witnesses say:** The prior owner **admitted to** their father **in our presence** that the property was the father's and not stolen. The Gemara explains: With regard to **these,** the sons of the craftsman and sharecropper, it **can be said** that the sons **are saying the truth,** as their claim is substantiated by the testimony of the admission. But with regard to **that** one, the son of the robber, **even though** the prior owner **admitted** this, the son is **still not deemed credible, in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Kahana, as Rav Kahana said: If** the prior owner would **not** have **admitted** this **to** the robber, the robber **would have brought him and his donkey to** the **taskmaster [*leshaḥvar*],** meaning he would have caused him great difficulties. As a robber is assumed to be a ruffian, it is likely that the prior owner admitted this because he was intimidated, and not because the statement was true, so there is no evidence to support the claim of the robber's son.

This should resolve the matter. *Hoda'at ba'al din ke-mea edim* — an admission by the owner is as powerful as a hundred witnesses. Why, then, should the son of a thief not benefit from this admission? The *Gemara* explains the distinction: for the *ben uman* and the *ben aris*, we assume the witnesses are reporting a truthful admission — *kushta kamre*. But for the *ben gazlan* — *afal gav d'odei nami lo meheman* — even if the original owner admitted, we do not trust that admission, because it may have been coerced.

### Coerced Admission

This is the logic of Rav Kahana's statement, which the *Gemara* cites here: *i lav d'odei leh, havei mamti leh u-lechmariya le-shachvar* — "if you don't admit to me," the *gazlan* threatens, "I will hand it over to another strongman" — *shachvar*, a term the *Rashbam* traces to the *Targum* of the verse in *Bamidbar* 16, where Moshe says he took nothing from the people: *ve-lo chamra de-chad minhon shachrit*. The word connotes forcible seizure. So the *gazlan* is essentially extorting a confession: admit in front of witnesses that this property is rightfully mine, or I will make things worse for you. The *hoda'ah* is coerced, and therefore worthless.

This is why even a witnessed admission by the *mara kamma* does not help the *ben gazlan* — we assume the father extracted it under duress.

### RY vs RC Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל גַּזְלָן נָמֵי אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן דְּקָא אָתֵי בְּטַעְנְתָא דְּאַבָּא דַּאֲבוּהּ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי גַּזְלָן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּחְזַק עַל שָׂדֶה זוֹ בְּגַזְלָנוּתָא. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּבֵית פְּלוֹנִי, שֶׁהוֹרְגִין נְפָשׁוֹת עַל עִסְקֵי מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** There are **times when even** the **son of** the **son of** the **robber does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership. **What are the circumstances** under which this is so? This is so, for example, **in** a case **where** he **comes** to court **with the claim** that the item in question **belonged to his father's father.** Since his claim is based on its having belonged to one who did not have the ability to establish the presumption of ownership, he too is unable to establish the presumption of ownership. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** under which one is considered **a robber,** who does not have the ability to establish the presumption of ownership? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In a case **such as where it is established** that he is in possession **of this field through robbery. And Rav Ḥisda says:** Not only in a case where there is knowledge about this specific field, but even in a case **such as** one where he is a member **of** the **household of so-and-so,** a certain known criminal family at the time **who kill people over monetary matters.** Since people would be afraid to lodge a protest against them, members of this family cannot establish the presumption of ownership with regard to any land.

*Rava* introduces a further refinement: *pe'amim she'afilu ben beno shel gazlan nami ein lo chazakah* — there are even cases where the grandson of a *gazlan* will not have a *chazakah*, namely when he comes with the claim of his grandfather. What kind of *gazlan* are we discussing? *Rebbi Yochanan* says: *ke-gon she-huchzak al sadeh zu be-gazlanuta* — someone who is publicly known to have stolen specifically this field. *Rav Chisda* says: *ke-gon de-bei ploni she-horgin nefashot al iskei mamon* — a family known to kill people over monetary matters, like an organized crime family.

The *nafka mina* between their positions: according to *Rebbi Yochanan*, the *gazlan* is disqualified only with respect to this particular field — for which he is publicly known as a thief — but could establish a *chazakah* on any other field. According to *Rav Chisda*, the entire family is broadly disqualified. It may be relevant, as one might observe, that *Rav Chisda* lived in *Bavel* and *Rebbi Yochanan* in *Eretz Yisrael*, and perhaps the social realities of organized violence differed between those environments.

### Gazlan Teshuvah

The *Tur* in *siman* 139 — a very lengthy passage — quotes a *yesh omrim* to the effect that if a *gazlan* has done *teshuvah* and it is publicly known that his *teshuvah* was complete — *teshuvah gemurah* — then he would be trusted even regarding this field. The *Tur* himself is uncomfortable with this position. The question it raises is a profound one: there is a difference between *teshuvah* between a person and God, and the question of when a community is obligated — or even permitted — to treat a rehabilitated person as fully trustworthy again. Even if the *gazlan* has done *teshuvah* regarding the stolen field itself — presumably returning it or compensating for it — the question of whether the community's *chazakah* of distrust is lifted is a distinct matter. This connects to larger questions about communal treatment of those who have repented.

## Chazakah Qualifications

### Quit Profession Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוּמָּן – אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, יָרַד מֵאוּמָּנוּתוֹ – יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה. אָרִיס – אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, יָרַד מֵאֲרִיסוּתוֹ – יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה. בֵּן שֶׁחָלַק, וְאִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה – הֲרֵי הֵן כִּשְׁאָר כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: A craftsman does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership. If he **descended from his** position of **craftsmanship** and no longer works at that craft, then he **has** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership with regard to items related to his former craft. **A sharecropper does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership. If he **descended from his** position as **a sharecropper,** then he **has** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership with regard to land that he works and from which he profits for three years. A son does not have the ability to establish the presumption of ownership with regard to his father's property, nor a wife with regard to her husband's property. But with regard to **a son who separated** himself from his father's finances, **and a woman who became divorced, they are like other people** with regard to this property, and have the ability to establish the presumption of ownership.

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* that further specifies conditions under which *chazakah* can be established. *Uman ein lo chazakah* — we know this from the *Mishnah* — but if the *uman* quits his profession, *yarad me'umnato*, then subsequently he can create a *chazakah*. The same applies to the *aris*: while he is a sharecropper he has no *chazakah* on the land he works, but if he quits, *yarad me'arisuto*, he can thereafter create a valid *chazakah*.

### Adult Child Rule

The *baraita* also addresses the case of an adult child who has left home and is financially independent — *ben shechalak*. The halachic principle is that an adult still dependent on his parents, *gadol ha-somech al shulchan aviv nikra katan*, is treated in certain respects as a minor. But a financially independent adult child who has separated from the household has full independent standing, and his use of a field for three years without *mecha'ah* can establish a *chazakah* independent of any relationship to the parents' ownership.

### Divorced Woman

Similarly, *ve'isha shenitgarsha* — a divorced woman — *harei hen ke-shar kol adam*, is treated for purposes of *chazakah* like any other person, with full independent standing. Her ability to establish a *chazakah* is evaluated independently of her former husband.

### Safek Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּן שֶׁחָלַק – אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: אַחוֹלֵי אַחֵיל גַּבֵּיהּ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא. אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה – פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, בִּמְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, וְכִדְרַבִּי זֵירָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת – בַּעְלָהּ חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, it was necessary** to state that **a son** who **separated** himself establishes the presumption of ownership. If the *baraita* had not stated this, it would **enter your mind to say** that the father **forgave** the unauthorized use of his land **by** his son, and did not lodge a protest despite the fact that the land did not belong to the son. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us that** this is **not** so, and that the son does establish the presumption of ownership. **But** in the case of the **woman who became divorced,** it is **obvious** that she has no relationship with her ex-husband, so why is it necessary for the *baraita* to teach that she is able to establish the presumption of ownership? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach that she does not establish the presumption of ownership **in** a case where there is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Zeira. As Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says** that **Shmuel says: Wherever the Sages said** with regard to a woman that there is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced, her husband** is still **obligated with regard to her sustenance.** One might have thought that since she still has some right to her husband's property, insofar as he still has an obligation with regard to her sustenance he would not lodge a protest if she used his land without his authorization. It is therefore necessary to clarify that this is not so, and she has the ability to establish the presumption of ownership in her husband's property.

The *Gemara* notes that the *ben shechalak* case is not surprising — we need the ruling because one might have thought the parents simply forgive use of their property, and so there would be no valid *chazakah*, merely an implied gift. But the divorced woman's case seems obvious — *pshita*! Why state it?

The answer is: *lo tzricha — bim'gureshah v'eina m'gureshah* — the case where it is needed is one of uncertain divorce. In *Masechet Gittin*, there are numerous cases where the status of a woman's divorce is ambiguous — for instance, where the *get* was thrown and landed in a contested location, or other circumstances of uncertainty. In such cases, *Rebbi Zeira* transmits in the name of *Shmuel* via *Rebbi Yirmiyah bar Abba*: *kol makom she'amru chachamim m'gureshah v'eina m'gureshah, ba'al chayav bimzonoteha* — wherever the *chachamim* said the status is uncertain, the husband remains financially obligated to support her.

One might therefore think that if this woman attempts to establish a *chazakah* on her husband's property, the absence of his protest is not meaningful — perhaps he makes no *mecha'ah* simply because he is still financially entangled with her. The *memra* teaches us that this is not so: even a woman in the status of *safek m'gureshah* can establish an independent *chazakah* on property that belonged to her husband. Legally, we emphasize her *m'gureshah* status for purposes of property rights, even while her husband retains the *meizonot* obligation arising from the uncertain status.

### Proof Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר לִי הוּנָא: כּוּלָּן שֶׁהֵבִיאוּ רְאָיָה – רְאָיָיתָן רְאָיָה, וּמַעֲמִידִין שָׂדֶה בְּיָדָן. גַּזְלָן שֶׁהֵבִיא רְאָיָה – אֵין רְאָיָיתוֹ רְאָיָה, וְאֵין מַעֲמִידִין שָׂדֶה בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman said:** Rav **Huna said to me** that with regard to **all of** the types of people who do not have the ability to establish the presumption of ownership, **when they bring proof** by means of a document or witnesses that a field belongs to them, **their proof is** a valid **proof and** the court **places** the **field in their possession.** But if there is **a robber who brings proof** that a field is his, **his proof is not** a valid **proof, and** the court **does not place** the **field in his possession.** This is due to a concern that the proof was obtained through illegitimate means.

*Rav Nachman* quotes *Rav Huna*: *kulan she'hevi'u re'ayah, re'ayatan re'ayah* — for all of these individuals, if they bring actual proof — witnesses or a *shtar* — their proof is accepted and *ma'amidin sadeh b'yadan*, we establish the field as belonging to them.

### Gazlan Proof Rejected

The exception: *gazlan she'hevi re'ayah, ein re'ayato re'ayah* — if the *gazlan* himself brings proof, we do not accept it, and *ein ma'amidin sadeh b'yado*. The reason is that we assume a *gazlan* has strong-armed the witnesses into testifying on his behalf. This applies to the *gazlan* who has not done *teshuvah*; as the *yesh omrim* cited in the *Tur* would hold, a *gazlan* who has fully repented would presumably be believed even with testimony.

## Stolen Property Sale

### Sikrikon Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: לָקַח מִסִּיקָרִיקוֹן, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת – מִקָּחוֹ בָּטֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this **teaching us? We** already **learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 55b): If one **purchased** land **from a Sicarius [*Sikarikon*],** a violent gentile who had extorted the field from its owner with threats, **and** afterward one **returned and purchased** the same field **from the** prior **owner, his purchase is void,** as the owner of the field can say that he did not actually intend to sell the field. This teaches that a purchase following a robbery is invalid, despite the existence of documents or testimony, rendering the statement of Rav Huna superfluous.

The *Gemara* now connects this discussion to a case drawn from *Masechet Gittin* — the *sikrikon*. A *sikrikon* is a bandit or requisitioner who seizes people's land and then sells it. The *Rashbam* notes that this refers to a non-Jewish robber. The scenario: a *sikrikon* seizes a field from *Reuven* and sells it to *Shimon*. Subsequently, *Shimon*, wanting to secure his claim, goes and purchases the same field from *Reuven* as well. He has now paid twice — once to the *sikrikon* and once to *Reuven*.

The question is whether *Shimon*'s purchase is valid. The concern is that *Reuven*, still the rightful owner, sold the field not out of genuine willingness but because he felt powerless — the field was effectively gone, and he simply tried to make the best of a bad situation. If the sale from *Reuven* to *Shimon* is not genuine, *mekach hu batel* — the sale is cancelled. But which sale is cancelled — the one from the *sikrikon* to *Shimon*, or the one from *Reuven* to *Shimon*? And under what conditions might either sale be valid?

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַב – דְּאָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ חֲזֵק וּקְנִי״, אֲבָל בִּשְׁטָר – קָנָה;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Huna's statement serves **to exclude that which Rav** says, **as Rav says: They taught** that the purchase of a field from the prior owner after one purchased it from a Sicarius is void **only** when the prior owner **said to** the buyer at the time of the sale: **Go take possession and** thereby **acquire** the field, but did not write a document. **But** if the transaction was performed along **with a document** being given, the buyer **acquired** the field.

*Rav* says: *lo shanu ela de-amar leh lech chazek u-knei* — the *mekach hu batel* ruling applies only in a case where *Reuven* told *Shimon* to go perform a *kinyan chazakah* — to take possession by building a fence, locking a door, digging, and so forth. In that case, *Reuven* is essentially throwing up his hands and waving *Shimon* onto the land without full commitment. *Aval bi-shtar kanah* — but if *Reuven* sold it to *Shimon* by means of a *shtar*, that is a genuine sale and *Shimon* acquires it fully.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר: אַף בִּשְׁטָר נָמֵי לֹא קָנָה, עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, Rav Huna **teaches us** that he rules **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel, as** Shmuel **says: He does not acquire** the field **even** if the transaction was performed along **with a document** being given, **until** the owner of the field **writes a property guarantee,** i.e., a document that states that if the property is seized by the seller's creditor, the seller will reimburse the buyer for his loss. Writing such a document indicates that it is a sincere transaction.

*Shmuel* is more stringent: *af bi-shtar nami lo kanah ad she-yichtov achrayut nechasim* — even a sale by *shtar* is insufficient to establish *Shimon*'s acquisition unless the *shtar* includes *achrayut nechasim*.

### Achrayut Explained

*Achrayut nechasim* is the clause by which the seller accepts liability for any liens that may be extracted from the property after the sale. Ordinarily, if *Reuven* has outstanding debts, a creditor holding a *milveh b'shtar* against him could potentially collect from fields that *Reuven* sold after the loan — including the field *Shimon* bought. If *Reuven* sells to *Shimon* with *achrayut nechasim*, he is committing that if such a lien is ever collected from *Shimon*'s field, *Reuven* will compensate *Shimon*.

This clause benefits *Shimon* and is a disadvantage — a concession — on *Reuven*'s part. *Shmuel*'s reasoning is that the inclusion of this clause demonstrates *Reuven*'s genuine intent to sell: why would *Reuven* voluntarily assume this additional liability unless he truly intended *Shimon* to own the field? A seller acting under duress or out of a sense of hopelessness would not go so far as to take on obligations that benefit the buyer at his own expense. The *achrayut* clause is therefore the decisive indicator of authentic consent.

### Rav Bibi Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב בִּיבִי מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: קַרְקַע אֵין לוֹ, אֲבָל מָעוֹת יֵשׁ לוֹ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁאָמְרוּ עֵדִים: ״בְּפָנֵינוּ מָנָה לוֹ״, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ עֵדִים: ״בְּפָנֵינוּ הוֹדָה לוֹ״ – לֹא; כִּדְרַב כָּהֲנָא, דְּאָמַר: אִי לָאו דְּאוֹדִי לֵיהּ, הֲוָה מַמְטֵי לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ וְלַחֲמָרֵיהּ לְשַׁחְווֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Beivai concludes that** discussion of the statement of Rav Huna, that a robber does not retain possession of the field even if he brings proof of the transaction, with a comment **in the name of Rav Naḥman:** The robber **does not have** rights to the **land, but he does have** rights to the **money** that he paid for the land, and the owner has to reimburse him. **In what** case **is this statement** that the robber is reimbursed **said?** It is specifically **where** the **witnesses said:** The robber **counted** out the money **for** the owner and gave it to **him in our presence; but** if the **witnesses said:** The owner **admitted to** the robber **in our presence** that he received payment, then the robber is **not** reimbursed, as the admission may have been made under duress. This is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Kahana, who says: If** the owner would **not** have **admitted to** the robber that he received payment, the robber **would have brought him and his donkey to** the **taskmaster.**

*Rav Bibi* then elaborates in the name of *Rav Nachman*, clarifying that this entire dispute concerns *karka* — real estate. But in a case of *maos* — money — *yesh lo*, the right to the money is valid even without these additional conditions.

### Witness Specificity

There is a further qualification regarding testimony. If witnesses say *be-fanenu hodeh lo* — he admitted to him in front of us — without specifying an amount, that is insufficient. But if they say *be-fanenu maneh lo* — he counted out a specific sum to him in front of us — that carries legal weight. The distinction is between a vague, unspecified admission and a precise, quantified one. A general *hoda'ah stam*, without specifying the monetary amount, is too indeterminate to serve as the basis for a claim.

## Coerced Sales

### Gazlan Coercion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תַּלְיוּהוּ וְזַבֵּין – זְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל דִּמְזַבֵּין אִינִישׁ, אִי לָאו דַּאֲנִיס – לָא הֲוָה מְזַבֵּין, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי זְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי. וְדִילְמָא שָׁאנֵי אוּנְסָא דְנַפְשֵׁיהּ מֵאוּנְסָא דְאַחֲרִינֵי! אֶלָּא כִּדְתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos transactions performed under duress, the Gemara cites that which **Rav Huna says:** If one was **suspended,** e.g., from a tree, and thereby coerced to sell a certain item, **and he sold** it, **his sale is valid. What is the reason?** The Gemara suggests that it is because **whatever a person sells, were it not** for the fact **that** he is **compelled** by his need for money, he **would not sell** it, **and even so, his sale is valid.** This indicates that a transaction performed under duress is valid. The Gemara rejects this: **But perhaps duress** that results **from his own** needs, such as his need for money, **is different from duress** that results **from another,** as in this case. **Rather,** the basis for Rav Huna's ruling is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*:

*Rav Kahana*'s statement, which the *Gemara* cited in the context of the *gazlan* who coerces an admission, reappears here in its original context. The *gazlan* tells the original owner: if you do not admit that this property is rightfully mine in front of witnesses, I will hand it over to another strongman — *shachvar*. This threat explains why a witnessed admission from the *mara kamma* in favor of a *gazlan* is not trusted: it may simply be the product of extortion.

### Threatened Sale Valid

*Rav Huna* now states a broader and striking principle: *talyuhu ve-zavin, zivinei zivina* — if they hung him up and forced him to sell, his sale is nevertheless a valid sale. Coercion of this extreme kind does not invalidate the transaction.

### Ones Reasoning

The reasoning: *kol de-mazbin inish, ei lav de-anis, lo hava mazbin* — any person who sells something would not have done so absent some form of external pressure, some *ones*. This is a foundational halachic principle rooted in the Talmudic understanding that ownership of tangible assets — especially real estate — is always preferable to holding money. One never parts with property unless some external pressure — financial necessity, personal urgency, or even physical coercion — has intervened. Since all sales involve some form of *ones*, the fact of coercion per se does not distinguish a forced sale from any other sale in a way that would render it void.

### Types of Ones

One might raise an objection: is there not a meaningful distinction between *onsa de-nafshei* — pressure arising from one's own circumstances, such as needing money to move to *Eretz Yisrael* — and *onsa de-achrinei* — coercion imposed by another person, including physical threat? The *Gemara* acknowledges the potential distinction but presses forward.

### Forced Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יַקְרִיב אֹתוֹ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ. יָכוֹל בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לִרְצוֹנוֹ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who pledges to bring a burnt-offering, the verse states: "If his offering be a burnt-offering of the herd, **he shall offer it** a male without blemish; he shall bring it to the door of the Tent of Meeting, according to his will, before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:3). The seemingly superfluous phrase "he shall offer it" **teaches that they** can **coerce him** to bring the offering. One **might** have thought that it can be offered entirely **against his will,** by taking it from his possession and sacrificing it. Therefore, **the verse states: "According to his will"** (Leviticus 1:3). **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? **They coerce him** with various punishments **until he says: I want** to bring the offering. This seems to prove that consent resulting from coercion is considered to be valid consent. Perhaps this principle can apply to acquisition, as a source for Rav Huna's ruling.

The *Gemara* adduces a *braita* concerning the verse *yakriv oso* in *Vayikra* perek 1, regarding the bringing of a *korban olah*. The verse teaches *melamed she-kofin oso* — the *Beis Din* may coerce a person to bring the *korban*. Yet the same verse says *lirtzono* — it must be of his own will. *Ha keitzad?* How can these be reconciled? *Kofin oso ad she-yomar rotzeh ani* — they force him until he says "I want to." Once he says it, it is considered voluntary.

### Forced Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּתִיהְוֵי לֵיהּ כַּפָּרָה! וְאֶלָּא מִסֵּיפָא: וְכֵן אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגִיטֵּי נָשִׁים – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **But perhaps there it is different, since he is** in fact **amenable to** achieving **atonement,** despite his earlier statement to the contrary. **But rather,** prove Rav Huna's ruling **from the latter clause** of a mishna (*Arakhin* 21a): **And similarly you find** this *halakha* **with bills of divorce,** that when the court rules that he must divorce his wife, **they coerce him until he says: I want** to divorce my wife.

The *braita* continues: *ve-chein ata omer be-gitei nashim, kofin oso ad she-yomar rotzeh ani* — the same principle applies in the laws of *gittin*. When the *Beis Din* has determined that a husband is required to divorce his wife, they may coerce him until he says that he agrees to give the *get*. This is not considered a forced *get* in the sense that would invalidate it. The principle of *ein ones be-gittin* — which does appear in *Masechet Ketubot* in other contexts — refers to specific *tnai* arrangements, not to this general question of *Beis Din*-compelled divorce.

### Force & Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִצְוָה לִשְׁמוֹעַ דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים! אֶלָּא סְבָרָא הוּא – אַגַּב אוּנְסֵיהּ גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **But perhaps there it is different, because it is a mitzva to listen to the statement of the Sages.** The assumption is that when he is required by the court to divorce his wife, his real desire is to perform the mitzva of listening to the Sages, and therefore he actually wants to divorce her. This does not apply to the case of a transaction performed under duress. **Rather,** Rav Huna's ruling does not have a source in a mishna or *baraita*, but **is** based on **logical reasoning: By means of his being coerced,** the seller then willingly **decides** to sell the field **and transfers** it.

The *Gemara* considers whether these two cases of *korban* and *get* might be special — in the *korban* case because a person fundamentally wants *kapparah* for his sins, and in the *get* case because listening to *divrei chachamim* is itself a *mitzvah*. But it concludes: *sevara hu, agav onseih gamar u-makni* — it is a fundamental principle of human psychology and halachic reasoning that even under coercion, a person reaches a point where he makes a genuine internal decision to agree. That concluding commitment, however it was arrived at, constitutes a valid act of will. This is the principle underlying *Rav Huna*'s ruling that *talyuhu ve-zavin, zivinei zivina* — a sale concluded under duress is a valid sale.

It is worth noting that the prohibition of *lo tachmod* in the *Aseret ha-Dibrot* is understood to apply precisely to the kind of pressure that compels another person to sell something they do not wish to sell — the coercer may violate *lo tachmod*, yet the sale itself, once concluded with the seller's stated agreement, remains halachically valid.
