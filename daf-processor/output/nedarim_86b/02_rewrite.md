# Nedarim 86b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Specificity in Hafara

### Wife vs. Daughter

The *mishna* on 86b continues the discussion of *hafara* by a husband, shifting focus from the categories of *inui nefesh* and *devarim shebeino l'veinah* to the act of *hafara* itself. The *mishna* presents several cases of confusion: a man's wife took a vow but he thought it was his daughter, or his daughter took a vow but he thought it was his wife; a woman made a vow using *nazir* terminology but he thought she used *korban* language, or vice versa; she made a vow not to eat figs and he thought she made a vow not to eat grapes, or the reverse.

These cases span three distinct categories of confusion: the identity of the person whose vow is being acted upon, the object of the vow, and the type of vow made. The distinction between a *neder* made using *nazir* language and one using *korban* language is not merely terminological — these represent genuinely different types of *nedarim*, and the basis on which a husband might wish to annul one could differ substantially from his basis for annulling the other. Of course, one could argue the same for wife versus daughter: what he wishes to annul for his wife may not be what he wishes to annul for his daughter. But it seems the *mishna* is not primarily concerned with the substantive motivation in each case. The operative point is structural: you must know whom you are annulling for and what exactly you are annulling.

### Types of Vows

In all these cases of confusion, the *mishna* rules: *harei ze yachzor veyafer* — he must go back and be *mefer* if he wishes the annulment to take effect. One natural reading is that he attempted *hafara* while mistaken, and so he must try again. But the *Ran* suggests — and both readings may in fact be true simultaneously — that the *mishna* also contemplates the case where he was *mekayem* the vow under a mistaken impression. Since a *kiyum* made in error is not a binding *kiyum*, he retains the ability to go back and be *mefer*. Whether the act was *kiyum* or *hafara*, the *gemara* will make clear that one must know for whom the vow was made, on what object, and of what type.

### Reattempt Needed

This raises a timing question: does the confusion reset the clock, giving him until the end of a new day from when he realized his error? The answer appears to be no. The *mishna* seems to assume that the husband knew he was empowered to annul, knew he was dealing with a type of *neder* he could annul, and knew all the general circumstances — he simply had the details wrong. Because he did have that fundamental awareness when he first heard the vow, the operative day remains *beyom sham'o*, the day he heard it. The discovery of his error does not restart the twenty-four-hour window.

### How Husband Hears

A separate question arises: how did the husband confuse his wife for his daughter, or vice versa? One natural answer is that he heard through a door, the voices were similar, and he simply mistook one for the other. However, the *Ran* offers a surprising reading — that the case involves someone reporting the vow to him incorrectly: *sha'amru lo shebito nadra umitoch kach hefer o kiyem*, "they told him his daughter had vowed, and on that basis he annulled or affirmed." This is striking, because earlier in the tractate there was an extensive discussion establishing that the husband must personally hear the vow — he cannot appoint an agent to hear it on his behalf. The *Ran*'s formulation seems to imply that being told about a vow suffices for *hafara* to take effect, which sits in tension with that earlier position. This point is worth noting even if a full resolution is not readily at hand.

## Explicit Mislabeling

### Otah Davka

The *gemara* opens its analysis with the phrase *lema'imra di'otah davka*: the word *otah* — "her" — in the verse *yani otah* is precise and exclusive. He must know exactly which woman's vow he is annulling. Not only is there specificity required regarding the person, but the *gemara* draws the same inference from the phrase *v'heifer et nidrah* — "he shall annul her vow" — meaning he must know the specific *neder* he is annulling: *she-yakir ha-neder*. The *Rosh* here draws both requirements from the two components of the verse: the *otah* specifying the woman, and *nidrah* specifying the vow. Interestingly, *Tosafot* takes a different approach and understands the word *otah* by *yani otah* as referring not to the woman but to the vow itself. Either way, both *Rosh* and *Tosafot* arrive at the same practical conclusion: the husband must know both the person and the vow he is annulling.

### Keriah Analogy

The *gemara* then raises what appears to be a strong counterexample from the laws of *aveilus*. The verses regarding *keriah* use the formulation *al Shaul v'al Yehonatan beno* — explicitly naming for whom the rending is performed — suggesting that *keriah* must be done with a specific person in mind. Yet a *brayta* teaches: *amru lo meit aviv v'kara* — they told him his father died and he rent his garments — *v'achar kach nimtza b'no*, and it turned out it was actually his son who had died. Despite this, *yatza yedei kriya* — he fulfilled his obligation. On its face, this seems to contradict the principle that the act must be specifically directed, since the *al al* formulation in the *pesukim* appears at least as demanding as *otah davka*.

### Mind vs. Labeling

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ha b'stam v'ha b'meforesh* — the cases differ based on whether the misdirection was merely internal or was made explicit. What renders an act as being "for X" is not what is silently passing through one's mind, but rather explicit labeling. If a person performs *hafara* or *kiyum* — or *keriah* — while thinking of the wrong person, but without that mistake being verbally or explicitly declared, it does not disqualify the act. Only if the act is explicitly labeled as being for the wrong person does it fail.

Applied to the *brayta* about *keriah*: the case where *yatza* is ruled, the *Ran* explains, is not actually where they explicitly told him his *father* had died — for then it would be labeled as a *keriah* for the father. Rather, the case is where they said to him only that someone died (*amru lo meit*), he assumed it was his father (*v'savar aviv*), and then it emerged it was actually his son. Since his assumption was entirely internal and was never made explicit, the *keriah* succeeds.

### Keriah Efficacy

The *gemara* then brings a second *brayta* that distinguishes the two scenarios precisely. Where someone was explicitly told his father had died and rent his garments, and it later emerged it was his son — *lo yatza yedei kriya*, he did not fulfill the obligation. But where they told him only that someone had died (*amru lo meit lo meit*), he assumed it was his father, rent his garments, and it turned out it was his son — *yatza yedei keriah*, he fulfilled it.

This resolution is notable and somewhat counterintuitive. For *hafara* and *kiyum*, one might expect internal awareness of the correct person to be especially critical — after all, *kiyum* need not even be verbal; the *rishonim* earlier in the tractate established that *kiyum be-lev* suffices. An act of annulment or affirmation is, by its nature, an expression of approval or disapproval, and it seems to require knowing the actual circumstances, not merely performing the external act in the right general context. *Keriah*, by contrast, is a physical act that derives some of its meaning from the objective reality of rupture and mourning, regardless of what is precisely in one's head. Yet the *gemara* draws the analogy, and apparently *hafara* and *kiyum* similarly do not require specific internal intention toward the correct person — they require only that the act not be explicitly mislabeled for the wrong person.

## Toch Kedei Dibbur

### Rav Ashi's Rule

*Rav Ashi* goes further: *kan be-toch kedei dibbur, kan le-achar kedei dibbur* — the distinction between the two *braitot* is not about explicit versus implicit mislabeling at all. Rather, even explicit mislabeling can be corrected, provided the correction occurs within *toch kedei dibbur*. This is a significant extension. Normally, *toch kedei dibbur* is associated with acts of speech — the idea that within the immediately subsequent moment one can still define or redefine what one has said. It is more difficult to apply it to a physical act like *keriah* where no further speech follows, and where there is no continuing act being performed that one's subsequent thoughts might redirect.

### Death Later Case

According to *Rav Ashi*, then, the first *brayta* — where *yatza* is ruled — applies to a case where the discovery was made *toch kedei dibbur*, even if the act was explicitly labeled for the wrong person. The second *brayta* — where *lo yatza* is ruled — applies to a case where the discovery came only *le'achar kedei dibbur*, after the window had closed. A further *brayta* is adduced in support: someone had an ill person in his house who appeared to have died, and in that belief rent his garments, but it then emerged the person had actually died only a short time afterward. *Lo yatza yedei kriya* — he did not fulfill the obligation. To this, *Rav Shimon ben Pazi* in the name of *Rav Yehoshua ben Levi* in the name of *Bar Kappara* adds the qualification: *lo shanu ela shemet le'achar kedei dibbur* — this ruling applies only where the actual death followed the *keriah* by more than *kedei dibbur*. But *aval betoch kedei dibbur k'dibbur dami* — if the death occurred within *kedei dibbur* of the *keriah*, it is treated as simultaneous and the *keriah* counts.

### Discovery Timing

This last case is perhaps the most difficult in the entire discussion of *toch kedei dibbur*. It is one thing to say that an explicit mislabeling can be corrected within *toch kedei dibbur* — one could argue that within that window the act remains in a process of definition, and a corrective realization somehow relabels it. But in the case of the *brayta*, the problem is not mislabeling at all. The problem is that at the moment of *keriah*, the person had not yet died. The *keriah* was performed before the triggering circumstance even existed. For *toch kedei dibbur* to salvage this is not a question of relabeling — it seems to require treating *keriah* performed before a death as valid for a death that occurs afterward.

### Pre-Circumstance Acts

This raises a fundamental conceptual puzzle. *Toch kedei dibbur* is normally understood as an opportunity to correct or redefine an act while still in its immediate orbit. But one cannot straightforwardly correct an act done before the circumstances that would validate it even came into being. This seems categorically different from, say, correcting a mistaken *beracha* by immediately saying the right one. The person did *keriah* for someone not yet dead, then the person died within *kedei dibbur* — and the *gemara* says the *keriah* counts. How can one perform an act of mourning before there is anything to mourn?

### Simultaneity Concept

The most coherent way to understand this is that *toch kedei dibbur* is here being deployed as a concept of legal simultaneity rather than as an opportunity for relabeling or correction. *Halacha* does not measure time in seconds. It measures in *toch kedei dibbur* increments. Anything occurring within the same *toch kedei dibbur* window is treated as occupying the same legal moment — the same "tick" of the halakhic clock. On this understanding, the *keriah* and the death are legally simultaneous because they fall within the same *kedei dibbur* unit, and therefore the *keriah* validly marks the death. This is a striking and demanding application of the concept, and it does not obviously generalize. One would not say, for instance, that betrothing a woman takes effect retroactively because she received her *get* from a prior husband within *kedei dibbur* — yet the logic of simultaneity would seem to suggest that. Accordingly, it seems more precise to say that the function of *toch kedei dibbur* here is specifically about whether the act and the triggering event can be treated as occurring within the same legally indivisible unit of time, in a way that *halacha* demands but does not extend indiscriminately to all acts.

To summarize the structure of the *sugya*: first, specificity of intent is required — the act must not be explicitly labeled for the wrong person. Second, even if it is explicitly mislabeled, correction within *toch kedei dibbur* remedies this. And the same principles apply, by parallel, to *hafara* and *hakama*, where — despite the fact that *kiyum* is internal and one would expect much more is demanded by way of specific awareness — the same permissive framework operates.

## Exclusions to TKD

### Five Exclusions

The *gemara* concludes with a *hilchasa*, a ruling on the practical *halacha*: *toch kedei dibbur k'dibbur dami* — within *kedei dibbur* is treated as the statement itself. However, there are five categories excluded from this rule: *chutz mi-megadef v'oved avodas kochavim u-mekadesh u-megaresh* — blasphemy, idolatry, *kiddushin*, and *geirushin*. If someone uttered a blasphemy and immediately retracted, saying he did not intend God, or someone bowed in worship and then claimed he intended the *davening* posture, or someone betrothed a woman and *toch kedei dibbur* said he takes it back, or delivered a *get* and immediately claimed he did not mean it — in none of these cases does *toch kedei dibbur* provide any remedy.

Before examining the reasoning, an important structural point must be noted. All the *rishonim* observe that the framework of *toch kedei dibbur* being applied here — including to *hafara* and *hakama*, which are *d'oraisa* — is evidently a *d'oraisa* concept. This creates a serious difficulty for the position of *Rabeinu Tam*, who held that *toch kedei dibbur* is a rabbinic *takkanah* instituted to ease commerce, allowing parties to back out of a transaction within the first few moments. The application of *toch kedei dibbur* throughout this passage to biblical-level obligations makes *Rabeinu Tam*'s characterization very difficult to sustain, and all the *rishonim* raise this objection.

### Complete Intent

The *Ran* addresses the rationale for excluding the five cases. He writes: *u'l'chol milei d'lav chamiri kulei hai* — for ordinary matters that are not especially weighty, *k'she'adam oseh osam lo b'gmar da'ato hu oseh* — when a person does them, he never acts with fully resolved intention. There always remains a degree of tentativeness, an implicit reservation, a sense that within *kedei dibbur* he can still reconsider. Therefore *toch kedei dibbur* allows for retraction. But *hani keivan d'chamiri kulei hai ein adam oseh osam ella b'haskama gmura* — these five matters, being of such gravity, are never undertaken unless one's intention is completely unreserved and final. There is simply no subjective reservation being held. Therefore *chazara afilu toch kedei dibbur lo mahani* — even a retraction within *kedei dibbur* cannot undo them, because the *da'as* at the time of the act was already complete.

### Acts Speak

An alternative explanation does not focus on the internal state of the actor but on the objective weight of the act itself. Acts of such significance — idolatry, blasphemy, *kiddushin*, *geirushin* — are so weighty that *halacha* treats them as speaking for themselves and as inherently final. Even if one were to argue that subjectively the person maintained some reservation, the act itself is of a category that cannot be redefined by subsequent second thoughts. The act's weight forecloses the possibility of relabeling it through any later awareness or retraction, however immediate.

## Partial vs. Whole

### Mishna Position

The next *mishna* introduces a new scenario: *amrah konam te'einim va'anavim elu she'ani to'emes* — a woman vowed that these figs and grapes are forbidden to her in terms of her eating them. *Kayam l'te'enim kulo kayam* — if the husband affirmed the vow with respect to the figs, the entire *neder* is affirmed. *Heifer l'te'enim* — if however he annulled only the figs — *eino mufar ad sheyafer af l'anavim* — it is not annulled until he also annuls the grapes.

The straightforward reading of this *mishna* is that *hakama* and *hafara* operate on an all-or-nothing basis, but asymmetrically. Since the *neder* already exists and is a real, established thing, even a partial affirmation is enough to root and confirm it in its entirety. Annulment, however, must contend with the full *neder*: a partial *hafara* accomplishes nothing. To erase the vow, the husband must reject it in its entirety. An ounce of *kiyum* goes a long way; a partial *hafara* does nothing at all.

The *Ran* proposes an alternative reading of the *mishna* — that *eino mufar ad sheyafer af l'anavim* means the grapes are not annulled until he annuls them, but the figs *are* annulled by the partial *hafara*. On this reading, partial *hafara* works for the part it covers. This reading is, however, not the *pshat* of the *mishna*, and the *Ran* himself acknowledges as much. The plain sense remains: partial *kiyum* affirms everything; partial *hafara* accomplishes nothing.

### Separate Language

The *mishna* then introduces a limiting case: *amar konam te'enah she'ani to'emes v'anavah she'ani to'emes* — if she used the word *she'ani to'emes* separately for each item, *harei elu shenei nedarim* — these constitute two separate *nedarim*. Once the two items are treated as separate *nedarim*, the all-or-nothing framework applies independently to each one. Performing *kiyum* or *hafara* on one does not affect the other.

The natural question is what makes language sufficiently separating. If she had separately said *konam* before each item, they would clearly be two vows. Here she did not repeat the word *konam*, but she did repeat the verbal phrase *she'ani to'emes* for each item individually. Is that sufficient? The *mishna* says it is, though the *gemara* will examine how this connects to a broader debate about what constitutes the separation of *nedarim*.

### Rabbi Yishmael

The *gemara* asks: *mani matnitin* — whose view does our *mishna* represent? *Rabbi Yishmael*. This is established from a *brayta* that reproduces the *mishna*'s ruling almost verbatim: *ishah yekimenu v'ishah yeferenu* — her husband shall affirm it and her husband shall annul it. If he affirmed the figs, *kulo kayam* — the whole thing is affirmed. If he annulled the figs, it remains fully in force *ad sheyafer af l'anavim* — until he also annuls the grapes. *Divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — these are the words of *Rabbi Yishmael*. The *mishna* as we have it straightforwardly reflects *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position.

### Rabbi Akiva

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees. He begins from the same verse: *ishah yekimenu v'ishah yeferenu*. The linkage of *hakama* and *hafara* in a single verse implies they must operate with comparable efficacy. But what is the *drasha* that produces the rule that even a partial *hakama* works? *Rabbi Akiva* reads *yekimenu* as *yakim mimenu* — the *mem* in the word becomes the preposition *min*, yielding: he affirms even *from* it, meaning from a portion of it. A partial *hakama* of even one component affirms the entire vow. And since *hafara* is *hekesh*-ed to *hakama* by the same verse — *mah hakama mimenu af hafara mimenu* — partial *hafara* likewise works as a full *hafara*. On *Rabbi Akiva*'s view, both *hakama* and *hafara* operate symmetrically: partial works as complete for both.

*Rabbi Yishmael* would respond that the *drasha* of *mimenu* applies only to *yekimenu* — there is no such formulation by *yesirenu*. *Rabbi Akiva* would counter that the *hekesh* between them in the verse bridges that gap. The debate thus has a clear formal structure: *Rabbi Yishmael* reads partial *hakama* as complete by *drasha*, with no such extension to *hafara* — partial *hafara* is nothing. *Rabbi Akiva* uses the same *drasha* but extends it via *hekesh* — partial works as complete for both.

### Chachamim

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: those are the positions of *Rabbi Yishmael* and *Rabbi Akiva*. But the *chachamim* say: *makish hakama le-hafara* — they compare *hakama* to *hafara* in the opposite direction. *Mah hafara ma she-heifer heifer, af hakama ma she-kiyam kiyam* — just as *hafara* works on what is annulled, so *hakama* works on what is affirmed. The apparent meaning is that partial works for both — what is affirmed is affirmed, what is annulled is annulled — and neither sweeps the whole *neder* in its wake.

If we map out the logical possibilities, assuming an all-or-nothing framework, there are three: partial *hakama* is everything and partial *hafara* is nothing (*Rabbi Yishmael*); partial is everything for both (*Rabbi Akiva*); and partial is nothing for both. The *chachamim* as rendered here seem to represent a departure from all-or-nothing entirely, saying that partial works for the part it covers — a genuinely distinct fourth possibility.

### Ran's Girsa

The *Ran* reports a different *girsa* found in several manuscripts and attributed to *Rabbeinu Moshe bar Nachman* in his *hilchos*: *aval chachamim omrim mah hafara ma she-heifer lo hufar af hakama ma she-kiyam lo kiyam* — what he annulled is not annulled, and what he affirmed is not affirmed. On this *girsa*, the *chachamim* hold that partial *hafara* is nothing *and* partial *hakama* is nothing. This preserves a clean all-or-nothing structure across all three opinions: *Rabbi Yishmael* (partial *hakama* is everything, partial *hafara* is nothing), *Rabbi Akiva* (partial is everything for both), and *chachamim* (partial is nothing for both). On this reading, all three positions remain within the all-or-nothing framework, distributed across the logical possibilities.

### Rambam

The *Rambam* in *Hilchos Nedarim* 13 rules in accordance with our *girsa* of the *chachamim*, holding that partial works for both: if he affirmed the figs and annulled the grapes, *mah she-kiyam kiyam u'mah she-hufar mufar* — what he affirmed is affirmed and what he annulled is annulled. Each part is governed independently by whatever act was performed upon it.

## Oath Analogy

### Rava/Rabbi Shimon

On the question of what language is needed to separate a single compound vow into two distinct *nedarim*, *Rava* invokes *Rabbi Shimon*'s position from the laws of oaths. The case there involves someone who denies debts owed to multiple creditors with a single oath. If he says "I swear I owe none of you anything," that is one oath and incurs one *korban* if he lied. If he says "I swear I don't owe you, and I swear I don't owe you," addressing each creditor separately with the word *shvuah*, those are five separate oaths and incur five *korbanos*. The debate is what happens when he uses a form like "I swear I don't owe you or you or you" — one use of the oath-word covering multiple people. *Rabbi Shimon* holds that the oath-word must be repeated for each person individually — *ad sheyomar shvuah l'chol echad v'echad* — to produce separate oaths.

*Rava* says our *mishna* reflects this same position applied to *nedarim*. When the woman says *she'ani to'emes* separately for each item — figs and grapes — this is analogous to, though not perfectly identical with, reusing the oath-word. It is not as complete a separation as repeating *konam* before each item would be, but it is sufficient to constitute two *nedarim* according to the view that requires more explicit separation to break a compound declaration into its components.
