# Bekhorot 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bat Kohen/Levi & Pidyon

### Exemption Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: לְוִיָּיה שֶׁיָּלְדָה — בְּנָהּ פָּטוּר מֵחֲמֵשׁ סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** With regard to the daughter of **a Levite who gave birth** to a firstborn boy, **her son is exempt from** the obligation to give **five *sela*** coins to the priest for his redemption, as the child is considered to be the son of a Levite and Levites are exempt from this obligation.

The daf opens by returning to a discussion begun at the bottom of 47a: when the mother is a *bat levi* or *bat kohen* — referred to in the Talmudic text by the feminine titles *leviya* and *kohenet* — her child is exempt from *pidyon haben*. The Gemara has been probing the basis of this exemption.

### Two Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּאִיעַבְּרָה מִגּוֹי. וְלָא תֵּימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְזַהֲמִין אֶת הַוָּלָד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְזַהֲמִין אֶת הַוָּלָד, לֵוִי פָּסוּל מִיקְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** This is referring to the daughter of a Levite **who became pregnant from a gentile.** In such a case, the son's status is determined by that of the mother, and therefore the son is exempt from the redemption of the firstborn. **And do not say** this is the *halakha* only **according to the one who says** that in such a situation **one does not disqualify the child** at all due to his gentile father, and he is of fit lineage. **Rather, even according to the one who says** that **one does disqualify the child** due to the gentile father, nevertheless, one also follows the mother **and he is** therefore **called a Levite** of **flawed** lineage, and is exempt from redemption.

Two explanations have been offered. The first holds that the exemption applies only in the rare case where the father is not Jewish. Ordinarily, a child's tribal identity follows the father; but when the father is a non-Jew, the child's status is shaped to some degree by the mother. In such a case, the child would carry a quasi-*levi* status — not full *levi* standing, certainly not enough to receive *ma'aser*, but a residual connection sufficient to exempt from *pidyon haben*. On this view, the exemption is defined through the child's own quasi-status, however attenuated.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיעַבְּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, וְשָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״פֶּטֶר רֶחֶם״ — בְּפֶטֶר רֶחֶם תְּלָא רַחְמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Rav Yosef, says** a different explanation, **in the name of Rava: Actually,** Rav Adda bar Ahava is referring to a case **where** the daughter of a Levite **became pregnant from an Israelite. And** although the son's status is usually determined by that of the father, **there,** with regard to the redemption of the firstborn, **it is different, as the verse states:** "Whatever **opens the womb** among the children of Israel" (Exodus 13:2). This teaches that **the Merciful One renders** the obligation **dependent upon the opening of the womb,** and since this firstborn came from the womb of a daughter of a Levite, and not a Levite, the obligation of redemption does not apply.

The ruling opinion, cited in the name of Rava, rejects this narrow construction entirely. According to Rava, the mother's status as *kohenet* or *leviya* is independently sufficient to exempt the child from *pidyon haben*, regardless of whether the father is Jewish. The exemption is grounded not in the child's identity but in the mother's, because *pidyon haben* follows the principle of *petar rechem* — the opening of the womb — and the mother's status is halakhically operative in that context.

### Mixed-Up Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מִי שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים וְנָשָׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁלֹּא יָלָדָה, נִתְגַּיְּירָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, נִשְׁתַּחְרְרָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת. וְיָלְדָה הִיא וְכֹהֶנֶת הִיא, וּלְוִיָּה הִיא, וְאִשָּׁה שֶׁכְּבָר יָלָדָה. וְכֵן מִי שֶׁלֹּא שָׁהֲתָה אַחֲרֵי בַּעְלָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים וְנִשֵּׂאת וְיָלָדָה, וְאֵין יָדוּעַ אִם בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה לָרִאשׁוֹן אִם בֶּן שִׁבְעָה לָאַחֲרוֹן — בְּכוֹר לַכֹּהֵן וְאֵינוֹ בְּכוֹר לַנַּחֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty concerning the opinion of Rav Pappa: **We learned** in the mishna: **One who had sons and married a woman who had not given birth;** or a woman who **converted while** she was **pregnant,** or a Canaanite maidservant who **was emancipated while** she was **pregnant** and she gave birth to a son, he is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest but he is not a firstborn with regard to inheritance. **And** likewise, if an Israelite woman **and** the daughter or wife of **a priest,** neither of whom had given birth yet, or an Israelite woman **and** the daughter or wife of **a Levite,** or an Israelite woman **and a woman who had already given birth, gave birth,** and it is uncertain which son was born to which mother; **and likewise** a woman **who did not wait three months after** the death of **her husband and she married and gave birth, and it is unknown whether** the child was born after **nine months** and is **the son of the first** husband, or **whether** he was born after **seven months** and is **the son of the latter** husband, in all these cases the child is **a firstborn with regard to** redemption from **a priest but is not a firstborn with regard to inheritance.**

The Gemara now invokes a *mishna* to sharpen the question. The *mishna* presents several scenarios in which a child is obligated in *pidyon haben* but not in the double-portion inheritance (*bekhor l'nakhalah*), because paternity is uncertain. One such case: a woman's son, who is unambiguously obligated in *pidyon haben*, gets mixed up with the son of a *kohenet* or *leviya*, whose son is exempt. The father still performs *pidyon haben* — he simply does not know which of the two boys is his — but neither can be identified with certainty. The *mishna* also mentions a woman who remarried within three months of her first husband's death, bore a child of unknown paternity, and thus produced a child who is a *bekhor l'kohen* but not *bekhor l'nakhalah*. In all these cases, the upshot is: *bekhor l'kohen* — obligated in *pidyon haben*; *ein bekhor l'nakhalah* — not entitled to the double portion.

### Kohenet/Leviya Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל (דִּכְהוּנָּה) [דְּכֹהֶנֶת] וּלְוִיָּה פְּטוּרִין, דְּאִיעַבְּרָה מִמַּאן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: From the ruling in the case of one whose child became confused with that of a daughter of a priest or a Levite, **by inference** one can derive **that** the daughter of **a priest and** the daughter of **a Levite** are **exempt** from redemption. Now, **from whom did she become pregnant?**

The Gemara now asks: from whom does the exemption of the *kohenet*'s or *leviya*'s son derive? *Ha-av miman* — who is the father? If the father is a *kohen* or *levi*, then the exemption makes obvious sense — but then why does the *mishna* specify *kohenet* and *leviya*? A *bat yisrael* married to a *kohen* would produce the same result; there would be no need to mention the mother's identity at all. The very fact that the *mishna* uses the terms *kohenet* and *leviya* implies that the exemption derives from the mother's status specifically. This pushes toward the interpretation that we are speaking of a non-Jewish father — so that the child's quasi-*levi* or quasi-*kohen* status flows through the mother.

But this immediately creates a problem for the *kohenet* case, which leads to the next segment.

## Kohenet Status Loss

### Rav Pappa's Quiz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר דְּאִיעַבְּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, מוֹקֵי לַהּ בִּדְאִיעַבְּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, אֶלָּא לְרַב פָּפָּא בְּמַאי מוֹקֵי לָהּ? לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיעַבְּרָה מִכֹּהֵן, וְהִיא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ ״כֹּהֶנֶת״? דִּבְנָהּ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Mar, son of Rav Yosef,** citing **in the name of Rava, who says** that Rav Adda bar Ahava, who deems the son of a Levite woman exempt from the obligation of redemption, was referring to a case **where** she **became pregnant from an Israelite.** This is because he can **interpret** the mishna **as** referring **to** the daughter of a priest or the daughter of a Levite **who became pregnant from an Israelite. But according to** the opinion of **Rav Pappa,** who maintains that the son of a priest's or Levite's daughter who became pregnant from an Israelite is subject to the obligation of redemption from a priest, **with regard to what** case does he **interpret** the mishna? **Actually,** the mishna is not referring to the daughter of a priest but to a woman **who became pregnant from a priest.** Therefore, her son is exempt from redemption, **and** yet **she** herself is **an Israelite woman. And why does** the mishna **call her** the daughter of **a priest [*kohenet*]? Because her son is a priest.**

The Gemara reports that Rav Pappa told his students that Rava — some say Rabba — once tested them with the following question: if a *bat kohen* becomes pregnant from a non-Jew, what is the *din* regarding *pidyon haben*? Rav Pappa initially responded that this should be analogous to the ruling of Rav Adda bar Ahava regarding a *leviya* who bears a child from a non-Jew — that the child is exempt from the five *sela'im*. He assumed the cases were parallel.

### Bat Levi vs. Bat Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לִי: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! בִּשְׁלָמָא לְוִיָּה בִּקְדוּשְׁתַּהּ קָיְימָא, דְּתַנְיָא: לְוִיָּה שֶׁנִּשְׁבֵּית אוֹ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה בְּעִילַת זְנוּת — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְאוֹכֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa continues: **And** Rabba **said to me: How can** these cases **be compared? Granted,** if the daughter of **a Levite** has a child from a gentile, he is considered a Levite with regard to redemption since his mother **retains her sanctity. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a Levite woman who was captured,** leading to a concern that one of her captors might have engaged in sexual intercourse with her, **or** even **if** a Levite woman definitely **engaged in licentious sexual intercourse,** one nevertheless **gives her** first **tithe and she may eat** it.

Rava rejected this comparison. *Bishlama leviya bikdushata kayma* — a *bat levi* who has relations with a non-Jew, even illicitly, retains her *leviya* status undiminished. A *braita* is cited in support: a *leviya* who is taken captive — and captivity implies presumed rape — or who has illicit relations, nevertheless continues to receive *ma'aser* and eat it. Her *leviya* standing is entirely unaffected. This is also consistent with the broader principle that a *bat levi*, like a *bat kohen*, is directly entitled to the relevant priestly gifts — *ma'aser* in the case of *leviya*, *matanot kehuna* in the case of *kohenet* — in her own right, not merely derivatively through a husband.

### Why Status Compromised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כֹּהֶנֶת, כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵיל לַהּ, הָוְיָא זָרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to the daughter of **a priest, since if** a gentile **engages in intercourse with her she becomes** like **a non-priest** and may no longer partake of *teruma*, her son from a gentile should be considered like an Israelite and be obligated in the redemption of a firstborn. If so, in what case does the mishna exempt the son of a daughter of a priest or a daughter of a Levite from redemption?

A *kohenet* is different. Once she has relations with a non-Jew, *havya zara* — she becomes a *zara*, a stranger to the *kehuna*. She loses her status as *bat kohen* entirely. This is not merely a question of her being forbidden to marry a *kohen* — although that too is true. The very act of having relations with a non-Jew, since marriage with a non-Jew is halakhically impossible, constitutes an act of *zenut* in the technical sense employed by the Rambam: not prostitution literally, but an act of intimacy that could not have occurred within a valid marital framework. Even a *yisraelit* who has such relations becomes forbidden to a *kohen* for this reason; but for a *kohenet*, the same logic that would disqualify a *yisraelit* from marrying a *kohen* directly contradicts and undermines her own identity as *bat kohen*. Her *kehuna* status is therefore comprehensively forfeited.

*Tosafot* at the bottom of the page addresses an apparent escape route: since every *kohen* is also a *levi*, shouldn't the *kohenet* at least retain a *leviya* status even after losing her *kohenet* standing? *Tosafot* rejects this. The *kohenet*'s identity is a single unified package — she is not simultaneously a *kohenet* and a *leviya* as two separate statuses. Her entire standing is *kohenet*, and once that is compromised, it is compromised entirely.

### Gender Asymmetry

This leads to a striking asymmetry. A male *kohen* who has forbidden relations — with a non-Jew, with a *gerusha*, with a *chalala* — does not lose his *kehuna* status. His personal sanctity as a *kohen* remains intact. The children of those forbidden unions will be *chalalim*, lacking *kedushat kehuna*, but they are not worse off than an ordinary *yisrael*. The male *kohen* himself suffers no diminution of his own standing. The woman, however — whether a *yisraelit* rendered forbidden to a *kohen*, or a *kohenet* who forfeits her entire status — bears the full weight of the consequence. This asymmetry is inseparable from the broader halakhic structure in which the definition of forbidden relations, like the definition of adultery itself, is governed by the woman's status rather than the man's.

### Impact on Daughters

One might ask: if a *kohenet* loses her right to *teruma* after such an act, what does she actually eat? Her father, after all, may be a *kohen* with access only to *teruma*. The answer is that *teruma* can be sold for its monetary equivalent in *chulin*, even if the exchange rate is unfavorable. It is a hardship, but not an impossibility. The practical severity of her situation is real and significant.

## Kohen Father/Chalal Son

### Son Redeems Self

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: כֹּהֵן שֶׁמֵּת וְהִנִּיחַ בֵּן חָלָל — רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ, רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵין חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that in the case of **a priest who** married a divorcée and then **died and left a son** who was **a priest disqualified due to flawed lineage [*ḥalal*]** and has the status of a non-priest, **Rav Ḥisda says: The son is obligated to redeem himself. Rabba bar Rav Huna says: He is not obligated to redeem himself.**

The Gemara now turns to a new case. A *kohen* is married to, or has relations with, a *gerusha* — a divorced woman, forbidden to him — and the resulting son is a *chalal*: he lacks *kedushat kehuna*. The father is a *kohen*, the son is not. Now, because the mother is not a *bat kohen*, the exemption from *pidyon haben* cannot rest on the mother's status. And because the child is a *chalal* and not a *kohen*, the exemption cannot rest on the child's status either. Therefore, the father — as a *kohen* — is technically obligated in *pidyon haben* for this son.

Of course, while the father is alive, the obligation is peculiar: he would simply be moving the five *sela'im* from one pocket to another, giving it to himself. But the obligation is real. The case becomes interesting when the father dies. If the father is not around, the obligation of *pidyon haben* — when there is no halachic father present — reverts to the son himself, who must redeem himself upon reaching adulthood. This is unlike *brit mila*, where the obligation in the father's absence passes first (according to some *rishonim*) to the mother and then to the community. For *pidyon haben*, it reverts directly to the son.

### Rav Chisda Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי הֵיכָא דְּמֵת הָאָב בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ, דְּהָא לֹא זָכָה אָבִיו בְּפִדְיוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree when the father died within thirty days** of the birth of the son, before the obligation of redemption had taken effect. **Rav Ḥisda says the son is obligated to redeem himself, as his father did not acquire his redemption** money.

A dispute arises between Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna. The key variable is whether the father died before or after the son reached thirty days of age, since the obligation of *pidyon haben* does not take effect until day thirty.

### Rav Chisda's View

Rav Chisda holds: if the father died before day thirty arrived, *ben chayav lifdot et atzmo* — the son is obligated to redeem himself. The father never actually performed the *pidyon*, never reached the moment when the obligation came due while he was alive, and so no *pidyon* has ever taken place. The son must therefore perform it himself.

### Rabbah's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵין הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת עַצְמוֹ, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָתֵינָא מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא דְּלָא מָצֵית לְאִשְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says the son is not obligated to redeem himself, as** he can **say to** a priest who demands the redemption money from him: **I come due to the strength of a man with whom you cannot enter into judgment,** i.e., if my father were alive he would have separated five *sela* coins and taken the money for himself, and I inherit his claim.

Rabbah bar Rav Huna disagrees. He argues that the son can say to any *kohen* who demands the redemption money: *atina mikoach gavra d'lo matzit l'ishtuyei dina bahadei* — I come from a man you could never have brought to *beit din*. My father was a *kohen*; you could never have sued him for the *pidyon* money, because he could always have designated himself as the recipient. Since my obligation derives from my father's obligation, and since that underlying obligation was never enforceable against my father, it cannot now be enforced against me. The framing here is strikingly civil in character — the entire argument is cast in terms of whether a legal claim could be pressed in court, rather than in terms of the *mitzvah* dimension of *pidyon haben*.

### Final Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נִתְגַּיְּירָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — בְּכוֹר לַכֹּהֵן, וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אָתֵינָא מִכֹּחַ גַּבְרָא דְּלָא מָצֵית לְאִשְׁתַּעוֹיֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ! שָׁאנֵי גּוֹי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ חַיִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a challenge to the opinion of Rabba bar Rav Huna: **We learned** in the mishna: If one married a woman who **converted while** she was **pregnant,** having been impregnated by a gentile, the child is **a firstborn with regard to** redemption from **a priest** and must redeem himself. **But why** is this the *halakha*? **Let** this son **say to** the priest: **I come due to the strength of a man with whom you cannot enter into judgment,** as his father is a gentile, from whom the redemption money cannot be claimed. The Gemara responds: The case of **a gentile is different, as he has no lineage [*ḥayis*],** i.e., this man is not considered his father's son at all.

The Gemara attempts to resolve this dispute by appealing to a *mishna* about a woman who converted while pregnant: the child is *bekhor l'kohen* and obligated in *pidyon haben*, even though the biological father was a non-Jew who could never have been sued in *beit din* for the payment. Why doesn't the son invoke the same argument — you could never have collected from my father? The Gemara answers: *shani oved kokhavim d'leit lei chiyus* — a non-Jew is different because he has no halakhic family relationship to the son whatsoever. When there is no recognized halakhic father at all — whether because the father was a non-Jew, or perhaps in a case of artificial insemination — the obligation of *pidyon haben* originates with the son from the outset. It does not derive from any prior paternal obligation. But when there was a recognized halakhic father and the obligation did begin with him, the son's obligation is derivative, and the argument from non-enforceability has more force.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן יָאסִינְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כֹּהֵן שֶׁמֵּת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים וְהִנִּיחַ בֵּן חָלָל — הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא זָכָה הָאָב בְּפִדְיוֹנוֹ. מֵת לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — אֵין הַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁהֲרֵי זָכָה הָאָב בְּפִדְיוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** On this matter, the Gemara notes that **Rabbi Shimon Yasinia says** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says,** in accordance with the opinion of Rav Ḥisda: With regard to **a priest who died within thirty** days of the birth of his son **and left a son** who was **a *ḥalal*, the son is obligated to redeem himself, as the father** did **not acquire his redemption** money. If the father **died after thirty days** following the birth of the son, **the son is not obligated to redeem himself, as his father** already **acquired his redemption** money.

The *gemara* resolves the dispute through a ruling attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: if a *kohen* died within thirty days — *shelo zachah av befidyono*, the father never reached the moment when the *pidyon* would have been deemed effectively performed — the son is obligated to redeem himself. If the father died after thirty days — *sheharei zachah av befidyono*, at the moment day thirty arrived and the father was alive, it is as though the act of *pidyon* was automatically accomplished, the debt was dissolved — the son is not obligated. This is the consensus conclusion: the critical question is whether the father was alive on day thirty.

The overarching conceptual point is that the entire discussion treats *pidyon haben* as a matter of *mamonos* — civil obligation — rather than as a ritual act requiring an affirmative *ma'aseh mitzvah*. Whether that framing is correct, or whether the *mitzvah* dimension of the act introduces independent considerations, is a question the Gemara leaves open.

## Yakir & Inheritance

### Power of Attorney

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִכְתְּבוּ הַרְשָׁאָה לַהֲדָדֵי! וְכִי תֵּימָא: מַתְנִיתִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַרְשָׁאָה — הָא מוֹקְמִינַן לַהּ קַמַּן דִּבְהַרְשָׁאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let** the son with uncertain status and the son who follows him **write an authorization to one another,** by which each grants the other the right to claim his own share. In this manner they can claim the double portion together, as one of them is certainly the firstborn. **And if you would say** that **the mishna** is dealing with a case **where** there was **no authorization, we interpret it later on** (see 49a) as referring **to** a situation **where** there was **an authorization.**

The Gemara now considers the *mishna*'s case of a woman who remarried within three months and bore a child of unknown paternity. Such a child cannot inherit from either potential father's family, since each set of brothers will deny him. He also cannot receive the double portion of the *bekhor*, since his identity is uncertain. One might suggest a solution: this child of unknown paternity should give a *harsha'a* — a power of attorney — to the known firstborn son of one of the potential fathers. That known firstborn's claim to a double portion is itself under threat, because the brothers can argue that perhaps the unknown child is really their father's son and is therefore the true *bekhor*. So the two parties could give each other *harsha'a*: each empowers the other, so that whoever pursues a claim in court can say, "Either I am entitled to this portion in my own right, or I hold the power of attorney of the other claimant." This way, at least one *bekhor* portion could be secured between them.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: perhaps the *mishna* is simply not discussing such a case. But the Gemara notes that a later *mishna* — to be discussed on the next *daf* — is explicitly set in the context of *harsha'a*, so the option cannot be dismissed. The reason *harsha'a* does not work here must be principled.

### Yakir Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יַנַּאי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ — כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה, לֹא הוּכְּרוּ וּבְסוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ — אֵין כּוֹתְבִין הַרְשָׁאָה זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna, which indicates that there is no firstborn with regard to inheritance here, **supports** the opinion **of Rabbi Yannai, as Rabbi Yannai says:** In a case where two sons of two men became confused and it is unknown who is the father of whom, if the two sons **were** initially **recognized and were ultimately intermingled, each writes an authorization to the other,** and the other sons in each family cannot claim that these sons are not their brother. If **they were not** initially **recognized and were ultimately intermingled, each does not write an authorization to the other,** as even initially neither of them had any certain right to their father's inheritance. Likewise, in the case of the uncertain son and the one who follows him in the mishna, neither one was ever recognized as his father's firstborn. Therefore, writing an authorization is ineffective in claiming any inheritance.

The Gemara resolves this by citing Rabbi Yanai's rule. Rabbi Yanai distinguishes between two scenarios involving mixed-up babies. In the first — *hukru ul'vasof nit'arvu* — the identities of the children were known at some point and only later became confused. In that case, *kotvin harsha'a zeh la'zeh*: the children may write powers of attorney to one another, because at the moment of *yakir* — the moment of recognition — each child was formally identified as his father's son. The possibility of inheriting was, at that moment, actualized.

In the second scenario — *lo hukru ul'vasof nit'arvu* — there was never any moment of recognition. Rashi's example is a birth in a cave with no light, where even for an instant no one could see who belonged to whom. In such a case, *ein kotvin harsha'a zeh la'zeh*: they cannot give powers of attorney, because a child who was never recognized by his father as his son was never, even momentarily, established as an heir. The word *yakir* in the *Torah* — the verse concerning recognition of the firstborn — functions as a threshold requirement: without a moment of *yakir*, there is no claim to inheritance that can be assigned or transferred.

### No Yakir Moment

This principle explains why *harsha'a* cannot work in the case of the *mishna*. The woman who remarried within three months and gave birth to a child of uncertain paternity is precisely a case where there was never a *yakir* moment. Neither potential father ever recognized this child as definitively his son. Since the child's claim to inheritance never crystallized, there is nothing to assign by power of attorney, and the proposed solution fails. The concept of *yakir* as a prerequisite for inheritance rights is a major theme that recurs in *Bava Batra* as well.

## Bekor: Birth Types

### Miscarriage Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵי זֶהוּ בְּכוֹר לַנַּחֲלָה וְלַכֹּהֵן? הַמַּפֶּלֶת שַׁפִּיר מָלֵא מַיִם, מָלֵא דָּם, מְלֵא גְּנִינִין, הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין דָּגִים וַחֲגָבִים שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, וְהַמַּפֶּלֶת לְיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים — הַבָּא אַחֲרֵיהֶם בְּכוֹר לַנַּחֲלָה וּלְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Which is** the offspring **that is a firstborn** both **with regard to inheritance and with regard to** redemption from **a priest?** In the case of a woman **who miscarried a gestational sac full of water,** or one **full of blood,** or one **full of pieces of flesh;** or one **who miscarries** a mass **resembling a fish, or grasshoppers,** or **repugnant creatures, or creeping animals, or** one **who miscarries on the fortieth day** after conception, **the** son **who follows** any of **them is a firstborn with regard to inheritance and with regard to** redemption from **a priest.**

The *mishna* now turns to the question of what constitutes a *bekhor* for both inheritance and *pidyon haben* — specifically, what prior events affecting the mother do or do not compromise the status of a subsequently born child as *petar rechem*. The cases presented are miscarriages that halakhically do not affect the mother's status. These include: an embryonic sac filled with water; a sac filled with blood but with no formed fetus; a sac of various colors but nothing of human form; a miscarriage that resembles fish, grasshoppers, or other non-human creatures; and any miscarriage before day forty of gestation. The significance of day forty is well established across a range of halakhic contexts, including discussions of abortion: prior to day forty, the developing entity is not yet considered a formed human fetus. In all these cases, whatever was expelled from the womb does not count as *petar rechem*, and the child born afterward remains *bekhor l'nakhalah ul'kohen* — firstborn for both inheritance and *pidyon haben*.

### Chachamim: No C-Section

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, וְהַבָּא אַחֲרָיו — שְׁנֵיהֶן אֵינָן בְּכוֹר, לֹא לַנַּחֲלָה וְלֹא לַכֹּהֵן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹן לַנַּחֲלָה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a boy **born by caesarean section and the** son **who follows him, both of them are not firstborn, neither with regard to inheritance nor with regard to** redemption from **a priest. Rabbi Shimon says: The first** son is a firstborn **with regard to inheritance** if he is his father's first son, **and the second** son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest **for five *sela*** coins, because he is the first to emerge from the womb and he emerged in the usual way.

The *mishna* then turns to *yotzei dofen* — a child delivered by Caesarean section. The *chachamim* hold: *shneihem einam bekhor lo l'nakhalah velo l'kohen* — neither the child born by C-section nor the child born naturally afterward is a *bekhor* for either purpose. The C-section child is not *bekhor l'nakhalah* and not *bekhor* for *pidyon haben*; the naturally born child that follows is also not a *bekhor* for either.

### Rabbi Shimon: C-Section…

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *harishon l'nakhalah ve'hasheni l'chamisha sela'im* — the first child, born by Caesarean section, is the *bekhor* for inheritance and receives the double portion; the second child, the first to be born through natural vaginal birth, is the *bekhor* for *pidyon haben* and the five *sela'im*. On Rabbi Shimon's reading, both children have a *bekhor* status — each in a different domain — and neither is left without standing.

The position of Rabbi Shimon has considerable intuitive appeal. The C-section child is, after all, the father's firstborn — *reishit ono* — and the naturally born child is the first to open the womb as *petar rechem*. Why should either be denied his status? Nevertheless, the *halacha* follows the *chachamim*.

### Chachamim Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רִאשׁוֹן לַנַּחֲלָה לֹא — ״וְיָלְדוּ לוֹ״ בָּעֵינַן, לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים נָמֵי לָא — ״פָּטַר רֶחֶם״ בָּעֵינַן. שֵׁנִי לַנַּחֲלָה לֹא — ״רֵאשִׁית אוֹנוֹ״ בָּעֵינַן, לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים נָמֵי לָא — קָסָבַר: בְּכוֹר לְדָבָר אֶחָד לָא הָוֵי בְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that neither a son born by caesarean section nor the son who follows him are firstborn, neither with regard to inheritance nor with regard to redemption from a priest. The Gemara explains: The **first** son, born by caesarean section, is **not** considered the firstborn **with regard to inheritance,** as **we require** fulfillment of the verse: **"And they have borne him** children" (Deuteronomy 21:15), written in the context of inheritance, and a caesarean section is not called a birth. Likewise, **with regard to** the redemption with **five *sela*** coins, this son is **also not** considered the firstborn, as **we require** fulfillment of the verse: "Whatever **opens the womb"** (Exodus 13:2), and he did not open his mother's womb. Similarly, the **second** son who follows him is **not** considered the firstborn **with regard to inheritance,** as **we require** fulfillment of the verse: "For he is **the first of his strength,** the right of the firstborn is his" (Deuteronomy 21:17), and this son is not the first. In addition, he is **also not** considered the firstborn **with regard to** the redemption of **five *sela*** coins, as the *tanna* of the mishna **holds: A firstborn for** only **one matter,** i.e., he opened his mother's womb but is not her first child, **is not considered a firstborn** with regard to redemption.

The *chachamim*'s reasoning proceeds in two stages. First, why is the C-section child not *bekhor l'nakhalah*? The *Torah* uses the word *veyaldu lo* in the context of the firstborn inheritance. *Leida* — childbirth — in this context means natural, vaginal birth. The source for this definition is a *gezeira shava* traced by *Tosafot*: the *pasuk* in the context of *tum'at leida* — a woman's ritual impurity after childbirth — reads *isha ki tazria veyalda zachar*, and the *Gemara* reads that verse to require *ad sheteila mimakom shemazriat* — the birth must occur from the same place the seed enters. That limits *leida* for purposes of *tum'at leida* to vaginal birth, and by the same *gezeira shava*, the word *leida* for firstborn inheritance is understood identically.

Second, why does the naturally born second child not qualify for *pidyon haben*? The *Torah*'s term *petar rechem* explicitly means the natural opening of the womb. The C-section child did not emerge through *petar rechem*, so the womb remains unopened, and the second child is indeed the first to be *poter* — to open — the *rechem*. One might therefore expect him to qualify for *pidyon*. The *chachamim* respond: *bekhor ledavar echad lo havei bekhor*. One cannot be a *bekhor* in only one respect while lacking the other. To qualify for *pidyon haben*, a child must be both the first to be *petar rechem* and the first child of the mother. The second child satisfies the first condition but not the second — the mother's first child was the C-section child. Since both conditions must be met simultaneously, the second child does not qualify.

### Rabbi Shimon Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹן לַנַּחֲלָה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: ״תֵּלֵד״ לְרַבּוֹת יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן. וְהַשֵּׁנִי לְחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, קָסָבַר: בְּכוֹר לְדָבָר אֶחָד הָוֵי בְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says: The first** son is a firstborn **with regard to inheritance** if he is his father's first son, **and the second** son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest **for five *sela*** coins. The Gemara explains: The verse states: "And they have borne him children," and a caesarean section is not called a birth. **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** with regard to a verse that deals with the ritual impurity of a woman after childbirth: "But if **she bears** a girl" (Leviticus 12:5), that the apparently superfluous term "she bears" serves **to include** a child **born by caesarean section,** and the same applies with regard to a firstborn for inheritance. **And** Rabbi Shimon also rules that **the second** son is a firstborn with regard to redemption from a priest **for five *sela*** coins, as he **maintains: A firstborn for** only **one matter,** i.e., one who opened his mother's womb but is not her first child, **is** considered **a firstborn** with regard to the redemption of the firstborn, despite the fact that he is not his mother's first child.

Rabbi Shimon's position follows directly from his broader halakhic stance. In the context of *tum'at leida*, Rabbi Shimon holds that a woman becomes *tamei leida* even after a Caesarean section — his definition of *leida* is broad enough to include C-section birth. That same broad definition of *leida* applies to the inheritance *bekhor*, and therefore the first child, born by C-section, is fully considered *yalud* — born — and is *bekhor l'nakhalah*.

For *petar rechem*, however, Rabbi Shimon agrees with the narrow reading: *petar rechem* requires natural vaginal birth, the physical opening of the womb. The C-section child did not achieve *petar rechem*, and the second child, the first to emerge naturally, is the *petar rechem*.

As for the *chachamim*'s principle that *bekhor ledavar echad lo havei bekhor* — one cannot be a *bekhor* in only one dimension — Rabbi Shimon simply rejects that constraint: *bekhor ledavar echad havei bekhor*. It is entirely coherent for a child to be a *bekhor* in one domain without being a *bekhor* in the other. The first child is *bekhor* for inheritance; the second is *bekhor* for *pidyon haben*. Both statuses are real and operative independently.

The *halacha* follows the *chachamim*: a child born by Caesarean section is neither *bekhor l'nakhalah* nor *bekhor* for *pidyon haben*, and the naturally born child who follows is equally exempt from both statuses.
