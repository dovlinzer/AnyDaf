# Yevamot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Theme

### Opening

The daf under discussion is 24b, and it represents something of a thematic pivot. The immediately preceding *mishnah* had dealt with the complex case of two brothers who each married two women in a state of *safek*, and its conclusion established a general principle: *im kadmu v'kansu ein motzin mi-yadam* — if the brothers went ahead and performed *yibbum* in a manner that was technically impermissible, they are nonetheless allowed to remain married. The present *mishnah* picks up on that very theme: marriages that ought not to have occurred but that are permitted to stand after the fact.

### Theme Overview

This thematic thread — prohibited marriages that remain valid *bedi'avad* — will run through the entirety of the daf. The cases that follow explore what happens when a marriage has been contracted in violation of a rabbinic prohibition. In most such cases, the marriage stands; in one notable case it does not. Understanding what distinguishes the exceptional case from the general rule is the central analytical task of this daf.

### Yibbum Primacy

It is worth noting that this theme was somewhat interrupted by an intervening *mishnah* and its accompanying *gemara*, which addressed the question of which brother bears the primary obligation of *yibbum* — a discussion anchored in the *pasuk* *v'hayah habechor asher teled*, which led the *gemara* into a broader exploration of the *pesukim* governing *yibbum*: whether the verse concerns naming a son after the deceased brother, or whether it speaks to the *yavam*'s right to inherit his brother's estate. That extended discussion, however important in its own right, was something of an interruption. We now return to the theme of marriages contracted improperly and the question of whether they may stand.

---

## Mishnah: Affairs

### Slave/Non-Jew Case

The *mishnah* on 24b presents two distinct scenarios. The first: *ha-nit'an al ha-shifchah v'nishtachrerah* — a man who is suspected of having had a sexual relationship with a non-Jewish slave, who was subsequently freed, or *al ha-nachrit v'nitgayerah* — with a non-Jewish woman who subsequently converted. In both instances, the *mishnah* rules *harei zeh lo yikanes* — he should not go ahead and marry this woman. Even granting that the rumor may be entirely false, the act of marrying her would amount to a retroactive endorsement of the rumor. People will say: evidently it was true all along — she converted and married him.

### Rumor Concern

The *mishnah* continues: *v'im kanas, ein motzin mi-yado* — if he nevertheless went ahead and married her, we do not force a divorce. The prohibition is thus *lechatchila* only; once the marriage has occurred, it stands. The concern animating the *lechatchila* prohibition is entirely sociological: the marriage reinforces a damaging narrative. But once the marriage has been contracted, that concern does not rise to the level of requiring dissolution.

### Married Woman Case

The second scenario is categorically different. *Ha-nit'an al eshet ish v'hotzi'uha* — a man who was suspected of having an adulterous affair with a married woman who was subsequently divorced from her husband. Here, the ruling is *af al pi she-kanas yotzi* — even if he went ahead and married her *bedi'avad*, he must divorce her. The *mishnah*'s language *hotzi'uha* — literally, they removed her — suggests that the divorce from her first husband was compelled rather than voluntary, a point the *gemara* will revisit at length.

### Case Distinction

The asymmetry between the two cases invites explanation. One might initially think that forcing a *bedi'avad* divorce in the married woman case is counterproductive, since that forced divorce itself reinforces the very rumor it is meant to address: if the marriage has to be dissolved, people will say the affair must have been real. Yet the *mishnah* insists on the forced divorce all the same. Why?

### Biblical Prohibition

The answer lies in the severity of the underlying concern. In the case of the non-Jewish woman or the slave, even if the rumor were true, there is no *halacha* that would render her forbidden to him. A man who was involved with a non-Jewish woman before her conversion incurs no biblical prohibition against marrying her thereafter. The only objection is the reinforcement of the rumor. Accordingly, *bedi'avad* the marriage stands. In the adultery case, by contrast, if the rumor is true, there is a biblical prohibition at stake: a woman who commits adultery is forbidden *de'oraita* not only to her husband but to the man with whom she transgressed. Were the rumor true, he could not be married to her even biblically. This is the operative distinction — and it is why even *bedi'avad* we cannot permit them to remain married in the second case.

## Geirus Motivation

### Conversion Validity

The *gemara* now asks a sharper question about the *giyoret* in the first case. The *mishnah* implies that the conversion is valid — the objection is only to the marriage. But is this obvious? One might argue that a woman who converted precisely in order to marry a specific Jewish man — especially when there was already a rumor of a relationship between them — has converted with an ulterior motive. Does such a conversion qualify as genuine *geirus*?

### Ulterior Motives

The *gemara* raises a *braita* as a challenge: *echad ish shenitgayer leshum isha, ve'echad isha shenitgayerah leshum ish* — a man who converted for the sake of a woman, or a woman who converted for the sake of a man. The *braita* continues: *vechen mi shenitgayer leshum shulchan melachim* — someone who converted for the sake of, literally, the royal table; that is, because being Jewish offered social or economic advantage. An historical example would be the era of David and Solomon, when attaching oneself to the Jewish nation carried clear benefits. The *braita* also mentions *leshum avdei Shlomo* — converting to marry one of Solomon's servants, who were of non-Jewish origin. All of these are cases of conversion driven by calculation rather than sincere religious motivation.

### R. Nechemia Invalidity

*Einam gerim divrei Rabbi Nechemia* — none of these conversions are valid, according to Rabbi Nechemia. Rabbi Nechemia elaborates: whether one converted because of *girei arayot* — as the Samaritans did, out of fear of the lions sent against them, as recounted in *Tanach* at the end of the period of the First Temple — or because of a dream (*girei chalomot*), or because of the mass conversions inspired by fear during the Purim period, *girei Mordechai ve'Esther*, as the verse says *verabbim me'ammei ha'aretz mityahadim ki nafal pachad ha'yehudim aleihem*: in all these cases, fear rather than religious conviction was the motivating force. Rabbi Nechemia's ruling is sweeping: unless the conversion occurs *bazman hazeh* — at a time when there is neither great advantage nor great danger in being Jewish, and thus the only plausible motive is genuine religious commitment — the *geirus* is invalid.

### Rav Yitzchak Override

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction with the *mishnah* by citing a ruling: *amar Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta mishmei deRav: halacha kedivrei ha'omer kulam gerim heim* — the *halacha* follows the view that says they are all valid *gerim*. We do not rule like Rabbi Nechemia. What is notable is that no alternative *tanna* is even explicitly quoted; the *gemara* simply asserts the existence of an opposing voice — *ha'omer kulam gerim* — and rules accordingly. This is itself a striking feature of the passage. *Bedi'avad*, conversions with ulterior motives are valid, and the *giyoret* in our *mishnah* is fully Jewish.

### Marriage Allowance Q

This immediately raises a new question: *ei hachi lechatchila nami* — if the conversion is entirely valid, why should the man not be permitted to marry her outright? The *gemara* had initially assumed that the prohibition against marriage might be grounded in a doubt about the quality of the conversion itself; once that doubt is removed, what justification remains for prohibiting the marriage even *lechatchila*? Note that this question applies most naturally to the *giyoret* case, not to the freed slave, since the freeing of a slave involves no question of motivation — once freed, she is simply free.

### Rav Ashi Answer

The *gemara* answers by invoking the principle articulated by Rav Ashi: *haser mimcha ikshus peh v'lazus sefasayim* — remove from yourself the perversion of the mouth, avoid becoming the subject of rumor and disrepute. Even though the *giyoret* is fully Jewish and the marriage would be entirely valid, going ahead and marrying the woman about whom one's affair was the subject of public rumor reinforces that rumor, normalizes the behavior, and contributes to a corrosive social dynamic. The concern is not merely personal but societal: allowing the marriage to proceed *lechatchila* creates a visible pattern in which affairs are conducted, a conversion arranged, and a marriage follows — all apparently validated. *Chazal* sought to resist that pattern, even at the cost of restricting an otherwise valid marriage.

## Tosafot Pnuyot

### Unmarried Q

*Tosafot* raises a natural objection, precisely the question a careful reader would ask: what about a case where the suspected relationship was between two single people, a *pnuya*? If the prohibition is driven by the concern of *lazus sefasayim* — creating or reinforcing damaging rumor — should that concern not equally apply when an unmarried woman converts and then marries a Jewish man with whom she had been involved?

### Marriage Proper

*Tosafot* answers that in the case of two unmarried people, marriage is in fact the *proper* response. The Torah itself, in the context of *anusa*, prescribes marriage as the appropriate resolution to such a situation. When a man has been involved with an unmarried woman, the act of marrying her is not a concession to impropriety but rather the halakhically correct response — *adaraba mitzvah l'chonsah*.

### Behavioral Outcome

The distinction *Tosafot* draws is therefore one of behavioral outcome. The objection of *lazus sefasayim* applies only when marriage to the woman in question is itself improper — when the appropriate halakhic response to the prior relationship would have been to end it, not to formalize it. In the case of an unmarried woman, the prior relationship, while problematic, calls for marriage as its resolution. The rumor concern therefore falls away, and the marriage is not only permitted but indicated.

## Geirus Centrality

### Geirus Questions

This *gemara* is one of the most central in all of rabbinic literature for the contemporary questions surrounding *geirus*. There are two major issues that dominate the modern discussion: first, what constitutes adequate *kabbalat mitzvot* — the requisite acceptance of the commandments; and second, what role motivation plays in the validity of a conversion. These questions are often intertwined in practice, since someone who converts primarily for social or marital reasons may not be genuinely committed to *mitzvah* observance. But the *gemara* itself does not address them together.

### Motivation Focus

This *gemara* deals exclusively with the question of motivation, and it does so without any explicit reference to the depth of halakhic commitment. One might reasonably infer that Rabbi Nechemia's concern about ulterior motives is ultimately a concern about sincerity of commitment — someone who converts for the sake of marriage may not intend to observe *mitzvot* seriously. But Rabbi Nechemia himself does not say this. His stated objection is to the motivation itself, as if the act of conversion must be intrinsically and religiously motivated to be valid, regardless of what commitment it produces.

### Motivation Standard

The *halacha* follows the opposing view: motivation is a *lechatchila* concern, not a *bedi'avad* invalidation. A conversion performed with an ulterior motive is not ideal, but once performed, it stands. This is the clear ruling against Rabbi Nechemia.

### Modern Context

In the contemporary world, conversions for the sake of marriage occur routinely — including in contexts that touch on the broadly Orthodox community, particularly in cases where a couple has already intermarried and one partner subsequently wishes to convert. The classical halakhic position — that such conversions are *lechatchila* prohibited — has been subjected to significant reconsideration in modern *teshuvah* literature.

### Kluger Teshuvah

One important early treatment of this question is a *teshuvah* by Rav Shlomo Kluger. The argument runs as follows: historically, conversion for the sake of marriage served as the mechanism that made the marriage possible in the first place — without the conversion, the Jewish-non-Jewish marriage simply could not occur. In that context, the conversion was purely instrumental, a means to an otherwise unavailable end. But in modern societies where civil marriage across religious lines is entirely legal and socially accepted, a conversion no longer functions as a prerequisite for marriage. The couple could simply marry without any conversion. When a non-Jewish partner nonetheless chooses to convert, the motivation is therefore likely to be something more than merely removing an obstacle — it may involve a genuine desire to identify with the Jewish people, to be part of the spouse's family and community, or to raise Jewish children.

### Bedi'avad Reality

There is also a broader *bedi'avad* argument that has gained traction in contemporary *poskim*: we already inhabit a *bedi'avad* reality. The practical choice is not between a *lechatchila* conversion and no conversion, but between a conversion undertaken with mixed motives and the alternative of the couple marrying without any conversion whatsoever. Given that framing, a *bedi'avad* conversion represents a more halakhically constructive outcome than no conversion at all. The *cheshbon* has thus shifted significantly.

### Extreme Position

A more extreme position inverts this logic. Some authorities argue that in the specific case of our *mishnah* — where a woman converts and then intends to marry the man with whom she is suspected of having had an affair — the *geirus* should be considered invalid not merely *lechatchila* but even *bedi'avad*. The reasoning: conversion requires *kabbalat mitzvot*, and if the woman is planning from the outset to violate the halakhic prohibition against marrying this particular man, then she is actively excluding at least one *halacha* from her acceptance of *mitzvot* at the very moment of her conversion. A conversion that proceeds with the explicit intention of transgressing a rabbinic prohibition cannot be said to involve genuine *kabbalat mitzvot*. This is a demanding position, but it illustrates how the *gemara*'s ruling — that *bedi'avad* all these conversions are valid — does not necessarily resolve every case.

## Gerim David/Solomon

### Mashiach Era

The *gemara* now cites a *braita* that broadens the discussion of motivation-based restrictions on *geirus*: *ein mekabelim gerim lemot HaMashiach* — we do not accept converts in the era of *Mashiach*. The reasoning is plain: when the Jewish people are triumphant and the messianic reality is manifest, conversion would be driven by the obvious advantages of association with the victorious nation, not by genuine religious conviction.

### Historical Restriction

The *braita* extends this principle historically: *lo kibelu gerim lo bimei David velo bimei Shlomo* — converts were not accepted in the era of David and in the era of Solomon. These were periods of extraordinary Jewish flourishing, when conversion to Judaism carried real social and material benefit.

### Biblical Basis

The *gemara* cites the scriptural basis from a verse read homiletically: *hen gor yagor efes me'oti mi gar itach alayich yipol*. The phrase *efes me'oti* — without me, or apart from my benefit — is read as a condition: a *ger* who converts must do so without material or social benefit accruing to him. Only *mi gar itach* — one who joins when the Jewish people are in a less advantageous position — will have a genuine portion with them in the future.

### Tosafot Resolution

*Tosafot* raises an obvious difficulty: we know from the biblical record that conversions did occur in the time of David and Solomon. What of the wife of Uriah the Hittite, of *Ittai HaGitti*, or of Pharaoh's daughter whom Solomon married? *Tosafot* offers two answers. Regarding some — such as those wealthy individuals who attached themselves to Solomon's court — the economic motive argument does not apply since they were already prosperous. But the harder cases — the large-scale conversions recorded during David's reign — *Tosafot* explains as having occurred either spontaneously, *mei'aleihen*, without formal rabbinic reception, or as having been processed by a *beit din shel hedyotot*, a court of non-expert judges, or alternatively as cases of *gerim gerurim*, conversions that were driven along rather than genuinely embraced.

### Rambam Validity

The Rambam addresses this directly. He explains that even when conversions were not supposed to be accepted — because of the historical context that rendered all motivation suspect — any conversion actually performed by a functioning *beit din*, even an irregular one, is valid *bedi'avad*. This is consistent with the ruling against Rabbi Nechemia: *lechatchila* the courts should not have accepted these *gerim*, but having done so, the *geirus* stands. History has in fact produced many such *bedi'avad* realities, including perhaps the mass conversions of the *Chashmonaim* period and others.

## Adultery Evidence

### Divorce Q

The *gemara* now turns to the second case of the *mishnah* — the suspected affair with a married woman — and investigates what type of evidence is required to trigger the *bedi'avad* compelled divorce described there. If a woman whose husband divorced her on account of a suspected affair then goes ahead and marries the man she was suspected of having the affair with, must she divorce him?

### Rav: Witnesses

Rav's position is stringent in one sense and lenient in another: *ub'edim* — only if there are witnesses who actually observed the act of adultery will we compel a divorce from the second husband. A rumor, no matter how persistent or widespread, does not suffice. In the absence of formal testimony, even a woman who marries the man she was suspected of having an affair with need not be compelled to divorce him *bedi'avad*.

### Sheshet Challenge

Rav Sheshet objects forcefully. He says that Rav must have been falling asleep when he said that — *d'ki nayim v'shachiv Rav amrah l'shma'ata* — because a *braita* directly contradicts him.

### Braita Scenario

The *braita* presents the following case: a woman is suspected of having an affair with Reuven. She is divorced from her husband — *v'hotziuha* — and subsequently marries a different man, whom we will call Shimon, not the man she was suspected of having the affair with. She then divorces Shimon.

### Rumor Interrupt

The *braita* rules: *im kanas lo yotzi* — if Reuven then marries her, he need not divorce her. The reasoning is intuitive: the fact that she went and married a completely different man after the divorce effectively undermines any lingering suspicion that she and Reuven were involved. Had the affair been real, she presumably would have married Reuven immediately. Having married someone else first, the rumor has been interrupted and substantially discredited, and by the time she and Reuven marry, the narrative has sufficiently shifted.

### Forced Divorce

The *braita*'s logic, as the *gemara* reads it, cuts against Rav. If there were witnesses to the adultery, the *braita*'s scenario makes no sense — the interruption by a second marriage would be irrelevant, since we have objective proof she committed adultery and she is therefore forbidden to Reuven regardless. The scenario must therefore be one without witnesses. And the *braita*'s implication is clear: *ha lav hachi mafkinan* — had there been no marriage to a second man to interrupt the rumor, Reuven would have been compelled to divorce her even in the absence of witnesses. This appears to be a direct refutation of Rav.

---

## Rav/Braita Reconcile

### Rav Resolution

Rav responds to this challenge by proposing a reading of the *mishnah* and *braita* that preserves his position. Rav argues that the scenario involves two conditions simultaneously: the woman has children from her first husband, *and* there are witnesses to the adultery. On this reading, the case where Reuven is permitted to remain married to her after the second marriage interrupted the rumor is specifically because of the combination of these two factors, and the forced divorce in the other case is driven by the witnesses.

### Rav Position

Rav's actual halakhic position is thus: without witnesses, a divorce is never compelled, regardless of circumstances. With witnesses, a divorce is compelled even if the woman has children from her first husband. The reference to a second marriage interrupting the rumor, on this reading, is not about eliminating the need for witnesses but about limiting the *lechatchila* prohibition even in cases where witnesses are present.

### Braita Contradiction

The *gemara* presses further. The *braita* elsewhere seems to speak plainly: *keshe'ein lah banim* — when she has no children from her first husband — we do force a divorce, and this without any mention of witnesses. *Aval yesh lah banim, lo tetze* — if she has children from the first husband, we do not force a divorce, because to do so would reinforce the suspicion and potentially cast doubt on the legitimacy of those children, making people suspect they are *mamzerim*. And *ve'im ba'u edei tumah, afilu yesh lah kama banim tetze* — only if there are actual witnesses to the adultery do we force a divorce even when there are children. The implication is unambiguous: the baseline case of *ein lah banim* involves forcing a divorce even without witnesses. This is a compelling proof against Rav.

### Rava Intervention

Rava resolves the difficulty by focusing on the *mishnah*'s precise language. *Mah irya detani hotziuha* — why does the *mishnah* use the term *hotzi'uha*, meaning they removed her, rather than simply saying her husband divorced her? The only scenario in which a *beit din* would have compelled the divorce from the first husband is one in which there were witnesses. Courts do not force women to divorce their husbands on the basis of rumor alone.

### Rava Interpretation

On Rava's reading, *hotzi'uha* — *k'vo hotzi'uha d'beit din* — is the linguistic marker that the first divorce was court-compelled, which in turn requires witnesses. Rav is thus not making a novel halakhic claim; he is reading the *mishnah*'s specific language as presupposing witnesses. The underlying *halacha* — that without witnesses and without children a divorce would be forced, while without witnesses but with children it would not — is not disputed. What Rav maintains is only that the *mishnah*'s formulation implies witnesses were present. This removes any substantive *halakhic chiddush* from Rav's position, but the *gemara* subsequently makes clear that Rav's position is in fact halakhically serious in its own right, and that we will never force a *bedi'avad* divorce from the second husband without witnesses.

---

## Refined Halakha

### Compromise

The *gemara* now articulates a compromise between the positions of Rav and Rabbi. With witnesses, a *bedi'avad* divorce is forced, in accordance with Rav. But what about a *kala delo pasik* — a rumor that simply does not cease? In such a case, the *gemara* rules in accordance with Rabbi that a divorce may be forced even without direct testimony. This is a middle position: Rav himself would not have conceded this even for an unceasing rumor, and Rabbi might have gone further still in the direction of circumstantial evidence. But the compromise position adopted by the *gemara* is that the persistence of a rumor, by itself and without witnesses, creates a sufficient basis for compelling a divorce — provided other conditions are met.

## Rabbi Stringent

### Suspicious Conduct

The *gemara* now introduces the *braita* that establishes Rabbi's broader position on circumstantial evidence. *Rochel yotze v'ishah chogeres besinar* — the spice-seller, the traveling merchant, is leaving the house, and the husband's wife is fastening her girdle. The scene is, to put it delicately, strongly suggestive. There are no witnesses to any actual act, no testimony, no direct evidence. *Amar Rabbi hu mechurar hadavar, tetzei* — Rabbi says, because this is an unseemly thing, she must be divorced.

### Language Ambiguity

The phrase *hu mechurar hadavar* is itself ambiguous, and the ambiguity is significant. It could be read as an evidentiary claim: the circumstantial evidence is strong enough that we can reasonably assume something happened, and therefore she must divorce. Alternatively, it might be read as a propriety-based claim: regardless of whether anything actually happened, the situation is sufficiently improper that continuing the marriage in the shadow of such a scene is halakhically intolerable. The difference between *yesh raglayim ladavar* — there is good basis for assuming something occurred — and *mechurar hadavar* — the matter is unseemly — is meaningful. The latter formulation does not require that we conclude the act took place; it requires only that the appearance of impropriety has crossed a threshold we cannot ignore.

### Additional Cases

Rabbi applies the same standard to other suspicious circumstances. *Rok lemalah min hakilah* — if there is spit on the canopy above the bed, which *Rashi* takes literally. *Amar Rabbi hen l'mechurar davar tetzei*. *Minalayim hafuchim tachat hamitah* — shoes overturned beneath the bed, interpreted by the *gemara* as indicating that shoes had been placed there and the situation rearranged. In each of these cases, Rabbi rules *tetzei* — she must be divorced — on grounds of seemliness and the appearance of impropriety, without requiring direct testimony to the act of adultery itself.

## Final Ruling

### Contradiction

The *gemara* records two apparently contradictory rulings. *V'hilchata kevatei d'Rav* — we rule like Rav, requiring witnesses. *V'hilchata kevatei d'Rabbi* — we also rule like Rabbi, forcing a divorce based on circumstantial evidence. These two rulings seem irreconcilable.

### Resolution

The resolution is clean. *Lo kashya ha bekala depasik ha bekala delo pasik* — there is no difficulty. The two rulings apply to two different situations. Rav's requirement of witnesses applies to a *kala depasik* — a rumor that has ceased. Where the rumor has died down, we will not force a divorce without formal testimony. Rabbi's circumstantial evidence standard applies to a *kala delo pasik* — a rumor that has not ceased, that continues to circulate without interruption.

### Practical Application

The practical upshot: where the rumor is continuous and uninterrupted, it carries sufficient weight to force a *bedi'avad* divorce even in the absence of formal witnesses, in accordance with Rabbi. Where the rumor has stopped, only witnesses will suffice, in accordance with Rav. It is worth noting that Rav and Rabbi themselves would likely not have agreed to this precise compromise — Rav would not have conceded even a persistent rumor as grounds for compelled divorce, and Rabbi may have gone further in both directions. But this middle position is what the *gemara* adopts as the operative *halacha*.

## Refined Halakha…

### Rumor Duration

Having established that a *kala delo pasik* creates grounds for a compelled divorce, the *gemara* asks: *v'kala delo pasik ad kamma* — how long must a rumor persist to qualify? *Amar Abaye amrah li em* — Abaye said, my mother — who was in fact his foster mother — told me: a day and a half. This is a remarkably low threshold. A rumor sustained for a day and a half without interruption is sufficient to constitute a *kala delo pasik* and to create the legal basis for compelled divorce.

### Meaningful Interrupt

The *gemara* immediately qualifies this. *V'lo amrin ela d'lo pasik beinei u'beinei* — this applies only if the rumor was genuinely continuous without pause during that day and a half. If there was an interruption, the rumor is *pasik* and does not qualify. But the *gemara* adds a further qualification: *v'lo amrin ela d'lo pasik machmat yira* — even an apparent pause does not count as a genuine interruption if the people spreading the rumor stopped only because they were afraid of consequences. *Aval pasik machmat yira, machmat yira hu* — if the cessation was driven by fear rather than by the rumor having run its natural course, the interruption carries no weight. The rumor is still considered continuous.

### Enemies Caveat

Perhaps the most practically significant qualification is the last: *v'lo amrin ela d'leika oyvim* — all of these provisions apply only if the people involved have no enemies. *Aval ika oyvim, oyvim hu d'afki leih l'kala* — if there are enemies, they are the ones who generated and sustained the rumor, and a rumor driven by hostile parties carries no probative weight. This caveat provides a real avenue of defense: in a world where virtually anyone may have adversaries willing to spread damaging stories, the grounds for establishing a legally actionable *kala* are substantially narrowed.

## Tosafot Limitation

### Tosafot Challenge

*Tosafot* now raises a fundamental question about the scope of Rabbi's rulings in the suspicious-circumstance cases — the milkman leaving, the spit on the canopy, the shoes beneath the bed. Reading the *gemara* straightforwardly, one might conclude that Rabbi's standard for compelled divorce applies equally to the woman's divorce from her original husband: that these kinds of suspicious circumstances would be sufficient to compel her husband to divorce her.

### Second Husband

*Tosafot* emphatically limits Rabbi's rulings. *Kol ha'sugya airya b'nit'an* — the entire *sugya* is discussing whether to force a divorce from the man suspected of having had the affair, the second husband, not about compelling the dissolution of the original marriage. When Rabbi says *tetzei* in the milkman case, he means that if this woman subsequently marries the traveling merchant, she can be compelled to divorce him. He is not ruling that her original husband must divorce her on the basis of these suspicious circumstances.

### First Husband

For the divorce from the original husband, *Tosafot* insists on a far higher evidentiary standard. *De'ein isha nis'eseret al ba'alah ela al yedei kinui u'setira* — a woman is not rendered forbidden to her husband except through the formal *sotah* process: *edim* testifying to the husband's formal warning (*kinui*) followed by *edim* testifying that she secluded herself with the warned man (*setira*). Alternatively, direct observation of the sexual act — *o she'yiru k'derech hamenafim* — is required. These are the standards of Masechet Sotah, and they are the only grounds for compelling a woman's divorce from her husband.

### High Standard

*Tosafot*'s reasoning is clear. The stakes of compelling a woman to divorce her husband are of an entirely different order than the question of whether she may remain married to a second man she was not supposed to marry in the first place. The *gemara*'s discussion of persistent rumors and circumstantial evidence, which might have seemed to create a broadly applicable standard for compelled divorce in adultery cases, is therefore limited by *Tosafot* to the specific question of the second husband. For the original marriage, the evidentiary bar remains extremely high. Even a case where a private investigator produces significant circumstantial evidence — short of actual testimony to the sexual act — would not suffice, according to Rav's reading, to compel divorce from the original husband.

## Bad Rep/Vow

### Mishnah Rule

A further *mishnah*, cited from elsewhere, addresses two additional cases. *Hamotzi et ishto mishum shem ra, lo yachazir* — a man who divorces his wife because of a bad reputation, because of rumors of adultery, may not remarry her. *Mishum neder, lo yachazir* — a man who divorces his wife because she made a vow he found intolerable may likewise not remarry her. In both cases, the prohibition on remarriage is rabbinic.

### Vow Rationale

The rationale for the *neder* case is worth examining carefully. Suppose a man divorces his wife because of a vow she made. Later, she has the vow annulled — there are halakhic mechanisms for annulling vows through a *posek* who finds a *petach*, an opening, some basis on which the vow was made in error or can be retroactively undone. Or suppose the husband later comes to believe that the suspicion of adultery underlying *shem ra* was false. In either case, the husband might be tempted to argue: had I known that the vow could be annulled, or had I known the rumor was false, I never would have divorced her. The *get* was given on the assumption that these facts were as I believed them to be. Now that I discover they were otherwise, the *get* was given under a mistaken assumption and should be *batel*.

This concern is devastating: it means that the woman's status — and the status of any children she may have borne in a subsequent marriage — would always be in doubt, hostage to the first husband's later claims. The rabbinic response is to eliminate the possibility of remarriage entirely. If the man knows from the outset that there is no path back — that divorcing for *shem ra* or *neder* forecloses remarriage absolutely — then the very act of giving the *get* demonstrates that his reasons were not contingent. He would not have divorced her knowing he could never remarry her unless the reasons were real and final. The absence of any opportunity for remarriage thus guarantees the unconditional nature of the divorce.

### Unconditional

This is the logic: by foreclosing remarriage, the rabbis ensure that the *get* cannot subsequently be characterized as conditional or as given under a mistaken assumption. The man who divorces knowing he can never remarry is making a definitive statement. He cannot later plausibly claim he only intended to divorce on the condition that the facts were as he believed them.

### Post Facto Q

This leads naturally to the question that anchors the subsequent *gemara* discussion: if a man divorces his wife for *shem ra* or *neder* and then — in violation of the rabbinic prohibition — remarries her anyway, what is the ruling *bedi'avad*? Must he divorce her again? This question fits squarely within the daf's overarching theme: when a marriage that should not have occurred has nonetheless taken place, does it stand or must it be dissolved?

## Case Comparison

### Divorce Difference

The *gemara* explores whether the *bedi'avad* rule from the adultery case — *af al pi she-kanas yotzi* — can be applied to the *shem ra* and *neder* remarriage cases. An objection is raised: the two cases are not parallel. In the adultery case, the *gemara* noted that *hotzi'uha* implies the court forced the first divorce, which requires witnesses. The divorce from the original husband was externally compelled on the basis of objective evidence. In the *shem ra* and *neder* cases, the husband chose to divorce voluntarily. *Hasam hotziuha, hacha hotziah* — there the court removed her; here he removed her on his own initiative. The grounds for applying the same strict *bedi'avad* rule are therefore weaker.

### Parallel Structure

The counterargument is structural: in both cases, there is a rabbinic prohibition that was violated by the marriage. In both cases, the principle should be the same: *lo yikanes, v'im kanas, yotzi* — do not marry, and if you did, divorce. The violation of a rabbinic restriction should carry the same *bedi'avad* consequence regardless of the specific content of that restriction.

### Severity Difference

The *gemara* pushes back. The adultery case is categorically more severe, precisely because of the *kala* dynamic. When the suspected adulterer marries the woman he was suspected of having an affair with, the marriage itself reinforces the damaging rumor — it appears to confirm that the affair was real. This active reinforcement of a socially destructive narrative makes the *bedi'avad* marriage intolerable in a way that has no parallel in the *shem ra* and *neder* remarriage cases. In those cases, by contrast, a remarriage serves the opposite function: if a man divorces his wife claiming there was an affair and then remarries her, observers will conclude that the rumor was false — why else would he remarry her? The remarriage undermines rather than reinforces the rumor.

### Final Ruling

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *v'lo hi* — the comparison fails. *Hasam alumi alma l'kala* — in the adultery case, the marriage reinforces the rumor and cannot be permitted to stand. *Hacha amina kam bei b'kala u'leisa* — in the remarriage case, the act of remarrying demonstrates that the rumor must have been groundless. The *gemara* thus rules that in the *shem ra* and *neder* remarriage cases, *bedi'avad* the marriage stands and he need not divorce her. This ruling affirms the broader pattern of the *mishnah*: in the overwhelming majority of cases where a prohibited marriage has occurred, the marriage is permitted to endure. Only where the marriage itself compounds an ongoing *halachic* problem — as in the adultery case — does the *bedi'avad* dissolution become mandatory.
