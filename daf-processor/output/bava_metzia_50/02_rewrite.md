# Bava Metzia 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ona'ah Overview

### BM Structure

The fourth and fifth chapters of Bava Metzia represent a significant thematic shift. Whereas the first three chapters dealt with objects — finding them, being a *shomer* on them — the fourth and fifth turn their attention to money. The preceding chapter had already engaged with the question of what constitutes *kesef*, which party in a transaction holds the role of the money and which holds the role of the object. Now, building on that money-centered focus, the Gemara turns to the laws of *ona'ah* — overcharging and undercharging — and the following chapter will address *ribbis*, the prohibition of charging interest. These two chapters are, by any measure, among the more challenging in all of Shas. The Tosafot on today's *daf* is a case in point: its unusual length is not, as one might expect, an attempt to reconcile five different *Gemaras*. It is simply trying to give *pshat* in our own Gemara. When Tosafot of that scale is devoted purely to establishing the plain meaning of a single passage, one knows to proceed carefully.

### Torah Source

The *pasuk* in the Torah (Vayikra 25:14) reads: *vechi timkeru mimkar la'amitecha o kano miyad amitecha, al tonu ish et achiv* — "When you sell something to your fellow or buy from your fellow's hand, do not wrong one man his brother." The context makes clear that *al tonu* — "do not oppress" — refers to financial exploitation in the course of buying and selling, since the surrounding verses speak of calculating the price of land according to the number of years remaining until the *yovel*: the number of harvests determines what is being purchased, and the price should reflect that fairly.

Notably, the prohibition is bilateral. There is *ona'at loke'ach* — the prohibition against overcharging the buyer — but equally, there is *ona'at mocher*, the prohibition against undercharging the seller. One might have thought the seller bears sole responsibility for pricing his own wares; if he sets too low a price, that is his own failure. Nevertheless, the Torah prohibits exploiting a seller through underpayment just as it prohibits exploiting a buyer through overcharge.

### Exclusions

Here the Gemara introduces a striking irony: although the *pesukim* themselves are embedded in the context of land transactions — all of the surrounding discussion concerns *Shmita*, *yovel*, and real estate — *Chazal* teach that *ona'ah* does not apply to land (*karka*) at all. The prohibition governs only cattle and *metaltelin*, movable property. Whether this exclusion of land is formal — analogous to the rule that land cannot be stolen — or whether it reflects the reality that real estate values fluctuate so dramatically that a precise going rate is difficult to establish, remains somewhat open. Rabbeinu Tam holds that even for land there may be a form of *ona'ah* if the overcharge exceeds double the market value. But the baseline rule is that land is excluded, and the prohibition falls on movable property.

Similarly, *avadim* — servants — appear to be excluded from *ona'ah* in the same manner as land, though the precise reasoning warrants its own analysis.

### Ona'ah Definition

The prohibition of *ona'ah* is defined relative to the *sha'ar*, the going market rate. It is not about the amount of profit a seller makes in absolute terms; it is about charging a non-trivial amount more or less than what this item ordinarily costs. One might note that this raises interesting questions in scenarios where a product is genuinely novel and no established market rate yet exists, or where collective pricing among competitors produces a uniformly elevated price. *Chazal* did address egregious price manipulation in essential commodities — Shmuel taught that one should not profit excessively on staples like bread, wine, or oil — and communities historically employed social pressure and even rabbinic injunctions to counteract price-gouging on items like fish purchased before Shabbat. But the formal halachic definition of *ona'ah* is anchored to the concept of deviation from the market price, however that price is ultimately determined.

## Mishnah Standard

### 1/6 Standard

The Mishnah states: *ha'ona'ah arba'ah kesef me'esrim ve'arba'ah kesef lesela* — the measure of *ona'ah* is four *ma'ot* out of twenty-four *ma'ot* per *sela*. The arithmetic: six *dinarim* equal one *sela*, and four *ma'ot* equal one *dinar*, making a *sela* equivalent to twenty-four *ma'ot*. The four *ma'ot* overcharge therefore constitutes exactly one-sixth of the object's value — *shtus lemekach*, one-sixth of the purchase.

### Retraction Time

The Mishnah then asks: *ad matai mutar lehachzir* — until when may the aggrieved party retract the sale? The answer is: *ad kedei sheyera'eh letagar o lekrovo* — for the amount of time it takes to show the item to a merchant or a relative and receive an informed assessment of whether the price was fair. Tosafot notes that this is not a case-by-case determination, where we would need to assess how far away each person's relative lives or how accessible the nearest merchant is. Rather, we establish a standard fixed period — however long it normally takes for a person to obtain that kind of advice — and that period applies uniformly to everyone. Today one might scan the object and receive expert opinion in ten minutes; the principle, however, is clear.

### R. Tarfon

Rabbi Tarfon, ruling in Lod, takes a more lenient position for the seller: *ha'ona'ah shemonah kesef me'esrim ve'arba'ah kesef lesela, shlish lamikach* — *ona'ah* is only triggered by an overcharge of eight out of twenty-four *ma'ot*, that is, one-third. Up to a third above market price, the sale stands without consequence.

### Merchants' Choice

The merchants of Lod — *tigrei lod* — rejoiced at this ruling. And it is telling that it was the sellers, not the buyers, who were pleased. The sellers are typically the informed party; they know the market, they set the price, and they are the ones who classically exploit the transaction. A higher threshold for *ona'ah* means they can get away with a larger overcharge. However, when Rabbi Tarfon added the condition — *kol hayom mutar lachazor*, the buyer has the entire day to retract — they reversed course: *yanich lanu Rabbi Tarfon bimkomenu, vechazru ledivrei chachamim*. They preferred the Sages' position. Why? Because they would rather have a lower overcharge threshold but a shorter window for retraction, ensuring that once the brief period lapses, the sale is final. A smaller permissible overcharge combined with a limited return window is worth more to the seller than a larger permissible overcharge with an entire day of exposure.

### Three Stages

Before turning to the Gemara's analysis, it is important to anticipate the framework the Gemara will develop. The Mishnah seems to present a binary: either the overcharge is tolerable or it is *ona'ah* with a right of retraction. The Gemara, however, will analyze three distinct stages. First, an overcharge of less than one-sixth — below the threshold. Second, an overcharge of exactly one-sixth — the *ona'ah* category. Third, an overcharge of more than one-sixth. Within each category, the consequences differ, and there are two possible remedies for the aggrieved party: either reverse the sale entirely (*bitul mekach*) or retain the sale but recover the overcharge. The Gemara will map these remedies onto the three stages.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav's Position

*Itmar* — it was stated by the *Amoraim*: *Rav amar shtus mekach shaninu* — Rav holds that the one-sixth is calculated exclusively against the market value of the object. The object has a going rate; one-sixth of that rate defines the threshold of *ona'ah*.

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel disagrees: *shtus ma'os nami shaninu* — one-sixth must also account for the amount actually paid. This introduces a second reference point for calculating the sixth.

### Examples

The Gemara clarifies when the dispute is operative. If the object is worth six and was sold for five, or worth six and sold for seven — in either case, everyone agrees that the deviation is exactly one-sixth of the market value, and *ona'ah* applies. There is no dispute here.

The dispute arises in subtler cases. Consider: the object is worth five and was sold for six. If we measure only against the market value of the object, a sixth of five is five-sixths, and the overcharge of one exceeds that — meaning it crosses beyond *ona'ah* into *bitul mekach*, the gross overcharge that voids the sale entirely. But Shmuel says this case is still within the *ona'ah* category, because one is exactly one-sixth of the amount actually paid (six). Similarly, if the object is worth seven and was sold for six — a sixth of seven is one and one-seventh, meaning the underpayment of one is less than a sixth of the market value, and by Rav's calculation this falls below the threshold of *ona'ah* and constitutes *mechila*, a tolerable deviation. Shmuel again would classify it as *ona'ah*, since one is one-sixth of six, the amount paid.

### Shmuel Range

The conceptual move Shmuel is making is significant. Rather than having a single pinpoint — exactly one-sixth of the market value — that triggers *ona'ah*, Shmuel defines *ona'ah* as a range. The range extends from a sixth of the amount paid on one side to a sixth of the market value on the other. Everything within that band is *ona'ah*; only outside it does one enter either *mechila* or *bitul mekach*.

This is not merely a technical point. It is more coherent. If one insists on a single precise point as the dividing line, one ends up with a world where an overcharge of one precise amount produces one legal consequence, one cent more produces a qualitatively different and far graver consequence, and one cent less produces a completely different outcome on the other side. A range makes far better sense conceptually, which gives Shmuel's position intuitive force beyond its textual support.

### Chart Analysis

To see this concretely: if the market value of an object is six, Rav would say *ona'ah* occurs at exactly one point — when the transaction price is seven (overcharge) or five (undercharge). Anything beyond seven is *bitul mekach*, anything below five is *mechila*. For Shmuel, the range of *ona'ah* in the overcharge direction runs from just above five and one-seventh (where the amount paid is such that the overcharge is one-sixth of that amount) up through seven (where the overcharge is one-sixth of the market value). The range of *ona'ah* in the undercharge direction runs from just below seven and one-sixth down through five. Outside those bands — further below or above — one is in either *mechila* or *bitul mekach* territory. The specific arithmetic works out such that one-sixth of five and one-seventh equals six-sevenths, and one-sixth of seven and one-sixth equals one and one-sixth; the details confirm the range structure Shmuel articulates.

### Confirmation

The Gemara then brings a *Baraita* that supports Shmuel. *Mi shehotal alav yado al ha'elyonah* — the one who was cheated has the upper hand in choosing his remedy. *Keitzad*: if something worth five was sold for six, the buyer was cheated — *yad loke'ach al ha'elyonah*. He may say *ten li ma'otai* — return all my money, reversing the sale — or *ten li ma she'onitani* — keep the sale in place but return the overcharge. Conversely, if something worth six was sold for five, the seller was cheated — *yad mocher al ha'elyonah* — and he may demand either return of his object or payment of the shortfall.

Now, the first case — something worth five sold for six — is a sixth calculated from the amount paid, not from the market value of the object. By Rav's reckoning, that case would already be *bitul mekach*, not *ona'ah*. The very fact that the *Baraita* treats it as *ona'ah* — with the aggrieved party having a choice — confirms Shmuel's view that we also measure the sixth against the amount paid, producing the range Shmuel described. The second case, something worth six sold for five, is the classic one-sixth of the market value. Together, these two scenarios in the *Baraita* reflect precisely Shmuel's range theory, and the Gemara concludes accordingly.

## Three Consequences

### Categories

With Shmuel's framework established, the Gemara now makes explicit the three-tier structure that underlies the entire *ona'ah* discussion.

### Mechilah

When the deviation from market price falls below the *ona'ah* threshold — below the range Shmuel defines — the overpayment or underpayment is treated as *mechila*. The assumption is that a small discrepancy is tolerable; people understand that prices are not perfectly calibrated, and someone who pays slightly over market is presumed to have waived any claim to that marginal excess.

### Ona'ah

When the deviation falls within the range — one-sixth, measured by either reference point in Shmuel's formulation — the aggrieved party has a choice. The cheated buyer or seller is the one *yado al ha'elyonah*, who holds the upper hand: he may either demand return of the overcharge while preserving the sale, or retract the transaction entirely. This is the zone of *ona'ah* proper.

### Bitul Mekach

When the deviation exceeds the *ona'ah* range — crosses beyond a sixth of the market value — the transaction is *bitul mekach*: so grossly mispriced that the sale is void. Tosafot notes a certain irony here. One might expect that *bitul mekach*, being a more severe outcome, is worse for the seller — yet in a sense, *bitul mekach* is actually preferable to *ona'ah* from the seller's perspective, since in *ona'ah* the cheated party has a choice, which includes demanding the overcharge back while keeping the sale intact. In *bitul mekach*, the range of options is more constrained. Tosafot offers two resolutions: first, regardless of which outcome is more convenient for the seller, once the overcharge is so gross the transaction simply cannot be regarded as legitimate — that is not a matter of preference. Second, the practical picture is that in *bitul mekach*, it may be that the sale is only voided if the aggrieved party actively comes forward and demands it; absent such a claim, it is possible the sale stands.

### Baraita

The *Baraita* cited above makes explicit who holds the options and what they are. In each scenario — *ona'at loke'ach* or *ona'at mocher* — the cheated party is *yado al ha'elyonah* and may choose between recovering the overcharge or retracting the sale. This confirms the structure of the *ona'ah* category as a zone in which the aggrieved party retains meaningful agency.

## Mechilah Timing

### Question Posed

*Ibaia lehu* — the Gemara now poses the central question that will occupy the remainder of the *daf*: *pachos mishta lerabanan, le'altar havi mechila, o bikdei sheyireh letagar o likrovo*? When we say that a deviation below one-sixth is *mechila*, do we mean it is *automatically* tolerated — that the sale is final from the moment it occurs, with no right of recourse for the aggrieved party? Or do we mean that *mechila* is conditional on time passing — that if the cheated party comes back within the consultation period, before he has had the chance to show the item to a merchant or relative, he may still recover his money?

### Practical Difference

The Gemara anticipates an objection: if we say *mechila* is time-limited rather than automatic, what exactly distinguishes the *mechila* zone from the *ona'ah* zone? In both cases, the aggrieved party could come back within the consultation period and seek redress. The Gemara answers: the distinction lies in the nature of the redress available. In the *ona'ah* zone, the aggrieved party has a full choice — *ratzah chozer, ratzah koneh u'machzir ona'ah* — he may either reverse the sale entirely or keep the sale and recover only the overcharge. In the *mechila* zone, even if time-limited *mechila* applies and he can come back, his only option is to recover the overcharge; he cannot retract the sale. The transaction is definitively valid; the most he can claim is the refund of the excess.

### Merchants Logic

This question about whether *mechila* is automatic or time-limited turns out to be the key to understanding why the merchants of Lod ultimately abandoned Rabbi Tarfon. The Gemara now works through the logic of their choice.

---

## R. Tarfon Debate

### Parallel Assumption

The Gemara initially assumes that Rabbi Tarfon's position is simply a scalar extension of the Sages' framework: whatever the Sages say about the zone below one-sixth, Rabbi Tarfon says the same about the zone below one-third. That is, *pachos mishlish leRabbi Tarfon kipachos mishtus leRabanan* — the sub-threshold zone for Rabbi Tarfon (below a third) is equivalent to the sub-threshold zone for the Sages (below a sixth). Under this assumption, the Gemara reasons: if *mechila* is time-limited for the Sages — meaning even below a sixth the buyer can recover his overcharge within the consultation period — then for Rabbi Tarfon, below a third the buyer would have the entire day (*kol hayom*) to recover his overcharge. That is what initially attracted the merchants: they could overcharge more, and the buyer had all day to seek redress — a seemingly worse deal for the merchants. Yet they preferred the Sages' shorter window. The logic: they would rather have a small overcharge ceiling with only a brief window for recovery than a large overcharge ceiling with a full day. The short clock is worth more to the seller than the extra margin.

### Correction

But the Gemara corrects the initial assumption. It is not the case that *pachos mishlish leRabbi Tarfon* mirrors *pachos mishtus leRabanan*. Rather, *mishtus ad shlish leRabbi Tarfon keshtus atzmah leRabanan dami* — the zone between one-sixth and one-third for Rabbi Tarfon is equivalent to the *ona'ah* zone itself for the Sages. Rabbi Tarfon agrees that below a sixth is *mechila*; what he disputes is the classification of the space from one-sixth to one-third. The Sages treat that zone as *bitul mekach*. Rabbi Tarfon reclassifies it as *ona'ah*. So the merchants' preference for Rabbi Tarfon was not about extending *mechila* downward — it was about reclassifying a large zone from *bitul mekach* to *ona'ah*.

### Why Prefer

This immediately raises a problem. If the merchants were attracted to Rabbi Tarfon because he called that zone *ona'ah* rather than *bitul mekach*, why would that be preferable? *Ona'ah* actually gives the cheated buyer more options: he can choose to recover the overcharge or retract the sale. *Bitul mekach* would simply void the transaction. One might have thought the merchants would prefer *bitul mekach* precisely because it gives the buyer fewer options and constrains the outcome. The question is a pressing one, and it sets up the resolution that follows.

---

## Mechilah Timing (II)

### Clock Value

The key insight the Gemara now delivers resolves the merchants' seemingly paradoxical preference. It is not that *ona'ah* is substantively better for the seller than *bitul mekach*. The critical difference is temporal. *Ona'ah* comes with a clock: *ad sheyireh letagar o lekrovo*, the right to seek redress expires after the consultation period. *Bitul mekach* carries no such clock — *l'olam chozer*, the aggrieved party may retract forever. The merchants, once Rabbi Tarfon declared that zone to be *ona'ah*, understood immediately: there is a limited window. The exposure is finite. The sale, once that window closes, is unassailable. That is far more valuable to the seller than any consideration about the buyer's menu of options within the window.

---

## R. Tarfon Debate (II)

### Real Preference

With this in hand, the Gemara explains why the merchants nonetheless reversed themselves when Rabbi Tarfon added *kol hayom*. When he initially announced that the zone from one-sixth to one-third was *ona'ah*, they were pleased: *ona'ah* has a clock, and a short clock is gold for the seller. But when he then declared *kol hayom mutar lachazor* — the buyer has the entire day — the calculus shifted. The question becomes: is a full day so much longer than a few hours that it matters? Rashi's view is that in practice, the difference between a few hours and a full day is marginal — a buyer who will not act within a few hours will rarely act within a full day. Yet even granting that, the comparison is not between one day and a few hours; it is between one day and forever. *Bitul mekach*, as the Gemara establishes, carries no time limit — *l'olam chozer*. So one might still think the merchants should prefer Rabbi Tarfon's *ona'ah*-with-a-full-day over the Sages' *bitul mekach*-forever.

The Gemara's response is subtle. The merchants' preference for returning to the Sages may in fact be grounded in the nature of *bitul mekach* itself. When the buyer is in *bitul mekach* territory, he is not the only one with options: the seller, too, may have recourse, and the constraints on the buyer's ability to exploit the situation differ from those in *ona'ah*. In *ona'ah*, the cheated party is *yado al ha'elyonah* — it is entirely his choice how to proceed. In *bitul mekach*, the framing is different, and the seller is not simply at the buyer's mercy. Moreover, the Gemara notes *samchu bish'tus atzmah* — whatever ultimately drives their preference, the merchants concluded that returning to the Sages' framework, with its shorter retraction window on the genuine *ona'ah* zone and its different treatment of the larger overcharge, served their interests better than Rabbi Tarfon's extension of *kol hayom* across an enlarged *ona'ah* category.
