# Bava Metzia 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Renter & Borrower

### Setup & Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׂוֹכֵר פָּרָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְהִשְׁאִילָהּ לְאַחֵר, וּמֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ – יִשָּׁבַע הַשּׂוֹכֵר שֶׁמֵּתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל יְשַׁלֵּם לַשּׂוֹכֵר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כֵּיצַד הַלָּה עוֹשֶׂה סְחוֹרָה בְּפָרָתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ? אֶלָּא תַּחְזוֹר פָּרָה לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who rents a cow from another, and** this renter then **lends it to another** person, **and** the cow **dies in its** typical **manner,** i.e., of natural causes, in the possession of the borrower, **the renter takes an oath** to the owner of the cow **that** the cow **died in its** typical **manner, and the borrower pays the renter** for the cow that he borrowed. A renter is exempt in a case of damage due to circumstances beyond his control, including death, but a borrower is liable to compensate the owner even for damage due to circumstances beyond his control. **Rabbi Yosei said: How does the other** party, i.e., the renter, **do business with** and profit from **another's cow? Rather,** the value of the **cow should be returned to** the **owner.** The renter need not take an oath, but the borrower must compensate the owner of the cow.

The *mishnah* on 35b presents a layered scenario involving a *socher* (renter), a *shoel* (borrower), and the original owner. Reuven rents a cow from Shimon and then lends it to a third party, David. The cow dies of natural causes — *umesa kedarka* — which stands in contrast to *meisah machamas melachah*, death through normal use. The latter exempts all parties, including the *shoel*, on the grounds of assumed risk: when you lend someone an object for use, there is an implicit acceptance that the object may wear out through that use. But *umesa kedarka*, ordinary natural death, is a different matter. Natural death constitutes an *ones* — an unavoidable, uncontrollable accident — yet the *shoel* remains liable even in such cases. The *shoel* bears full liability for everything that befalls the borrowed object, since he receives all the benefit while paying nothing, the sole exceptions being *meisah machamas melachah* and the rule of *ba'alav imo* (the owner working for the borrower at the time).

The *mishnah* therefore rules: the *socher* takes an oath to Shimon — *yishava hasocher shemeisah kedarkah* — attesting that the cow died naturally, and is thereby exempt from liability, since a *socher* is not liable for an *ones*. David the *shoel*, meanwhile, pays the *socher*. The result is striking: the *socher* profits from an oath about someone else's cow.

A preliminary question lurks in the background: by what right did the *socher* lend the cow out in the first place? The *gemara* will address this, and the answer will be either that there is a general presumption permitting such sublending, or — more plausibly — that the original owner explicitly granted permission, much as a lease may or may not include a subletting clause. Either way, the *gemara* assumes that the *socher* did not violate the owner's wishes by lending it out.

### Rabbi Yosei's Objection

It is Rabbi Yosei who gives voice to the moral discomfort embedded in the *mishnah*'s ruling. *Keitzad halaz oseh sechorah beparato shel chaveiro* — how does this person profit from his friend's cow? The *socher* collects payment for the death of a cow that was never truly his. Rabbi Yosei therefore rules: *ela tachzor parah labaalim rishonim* — the cow, or rather its value, must return to the original owners. David the *shoel* is indeed liable, but since he ultimately borrowed Shimon's cow (the *socher* having only temporary use rights), his payment goes directly to Shimon rather than to the *socher*.

This ruling raises a conceptual question worth pausing on. The *Rambam* conceptualizes rental as a *kinyan haguf lizman lapeiros* — a form of ownership of the object for purposes of use, for a defined period. One might grant the *socher* a quasi-ownership for the duration of the rental. And yet even under that framework, the *socher*'s stake in a thousand-dollar cow may be worth only twenty dollars — his one month of use. How, then, does David owe the *socher* the full value of the cow? The *gemara* does not raise this question explicitly, but the logic appears to be that since the *socher* had control over the cow and conveyed it to David, David assumed an obligation to the *socher* commensurate with the object received — namely, the full cow. Whether this is entirely satisfying is another matter; but that is the operative framework.

### Kinyan at Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין לְאַבָּיֵי: מִכְּדֵי, שׂוֹכֵר בְּמַאי קָנֵי לְהַאי פָּרָה? בִּשְׁבוּעָה. וְנֵימָא לֵיהּ מַשְׂכִּיר לְשׂוֹכֵר: דַּל אַנְתְּ וְדַל שְׁבוּעֲתָךְ, וַאֲנָא מִשְׁתַּעֵינָא דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵי שׁוֹאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ, שׂוֹכֵר בִּשְׁבוּעָה הוּא דְּקָא קָנֵי לַהּ? מִשְּׁעַת מִיתָה הוּא דְּקָנֵי. וּשְׁבוּעָה כְּדֵי לְהָפִיס דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Idi bar Avin said to Abaye: After all,** with regard to **the renter, with what does he acquire this cow** to the extent that one who borrows the cow from him is liable to compensate him if it dies? He acquires it **with an oath** that he took to the owner of the cow that the cow died of natural causes. The Gemara asks: **But** since the acquisition is effected by the renter's oath, **let the one who rented** his animal for hire **say to the renter: Remove yourself and remove your oath.** I do not want to deal with you at all in this case, **and I will litigate with the borrower** to recover my cow. Abaye **said to** Rav Idi bar Avin: **Do you hold** that it **is with an oath** that the **renter acquires** the cow? That is not so, as **from the moment of** the cow's **death,** the renter **acquires** the cow. From the moment the cow dies in the possession of the borrower, the renter has the right to receive another cow in exchange. **And** this **oath** that the renter takes to the owner of the cow is not required by the *halakha*. Rather, he takes the oath **to alleviate the concerns of the owner,** so that the owner will not suspect him of negligence. Consequently, the owner of the cow cannot litigate with the borrower, and even if he waives his right to demand an oath from the renter, he is unable to receive a cow from the borrower.

Rabidi bar Avin poses a sharp question to Abaye. The *socher*, he says, acquires the cow through his oath (*bishvuah*) — meaning it is the act of swearing to the *maskir* (the owner-lessor) that removes the owner from the picture and entitles the *socher* to collect from the *shoel*. If so, why can't the *maskir* simply say: *dal at u'dal shvuatakh* — set aside yourself and your oath, I will deal directly with the *shoel*? By refusing the oath, the *maskir* would remain a party to the dispute and claim directly against David.

Abaye's response is incisive: *mi savrat sokher bishvuah hu dekakani lah?* Do you really think the *socher* acquires the cow — or more precisely, acquires his position in relation to the cow — only through the oath? No: *misheat mitah hu dekani* — from the very moment of death, the *socher*'s relationship with the *maskir* is severed. The oath serves only to appease the owner — *shvuah kedei lehafis da'ato shel ba'al habayit* — because the owner does not know the facts and deserves some assurance. But as a matter of legal reality, the moment the cow died through no fault of the *socher*, the *socher* had no monetary obligation to the *maskir*, and the *maskir* had no further claim. Even if the *maskir* waives the oath, he remains out of the picture, because his claim evaporated at the moment of death.

### Liability Ends

To be precise about the language: the *gemara* uses the phrase *misheat mitah hu dekani* — "from the moment of death, he acquires it" — which is puzzling. The *socher* does not take possession of the carcass; that returns to Shimon. The language of *kinyan* here seems almost metaphorical. What it really means is that from the moment of natural death, any obligation the *socher* might have owed the *maskir* was extinguished. Imagine a *beit din* convened at the instant of death: they would rule immediately that the *socher* owed nothing to the *maskir*, since it was a natural death and he bears no liability for *ones*. That severance is what is meant by the *socher* "acquiring" — it is not a property acquisition, but the termination of the *maskir*'s standing as a claimant. Since the *maskir* has no claim from that moment forward, he cannot re-enter the picture and deal directly with the *shoel*. The *socher*, now the only one with a claim against the *shoel*, collects.

## Multiple Liabilities

### Alternating Agreements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין כַּמָּה פָּרוֹת לַשּׂוֹכֵר. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אַגְרַהּ מִינֵּיהּ מְאָה יוֹמֵי, וַהֲדַר שַׁיְילַהּ מִינֵּיהּ תִּשְׁעִים יוֹמֵי, הֲדַר אַגְרַהּ מִינֵּיהּ תְּמָנַן יוֹמֵי, וַהֲדַר שַׁיְילַהּ מִינֵּיהּ שִׁבְעִים יוֹמֵי, וּמֵתָה בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי שְׁאֵלָתָהּ, דְּאַכֹּל שְׁאֵלָה וּשְׁאֵלָה מִיחַיַּיב חֲדָא פָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says:** According to the *halakha* in the mishna, there are **times when the owner pays several cows to the renter. What are the circumstances?** In a case **where** the renter **rented** a cow **from him** for **one hundred days, and the** owner of the cow **then borrowed** that cow **from** the renter for **ninety days, and** the renter **then rented** that cow **from** the owner for **eighty days, and** the latter **then borrowed** that cow from the renter for **seventy days, and** that cow **died within** the seventy-day **period of its borrowing,** then **for each and every** occasion of **borrowing** of the cow, the owner, who then became the borrower, **owes one cow.** Since there were two discrete acts of borrowing and two discrete acts of rental, the owner owes him four cows, two outright as compensation for the borrowed cows that died, and two cows for the renter to use for the duration of his rental periods.

Rabbi Zeira opens with a startling proposition: *pe'amim sheba'alim meshalmin kama paros lasocher* — there are circumstances under which the original owner winds up paying the *socher* the value of multiple cows for a single cow. The scenario he constructs involves a rapid alternation of rental and borrowing agreements between the same two parties.

The *socher* rents the cow from Shimon for one hundred days (*agrah minei me'ah yomei*). Shimon then asks to borrow it back for ninety days (*she'ilah minei tishim yomei*), to which the *socher* agrees. The *socher* then re-rents it from Shimon for eighty days (*hadar agrah minei shmonim yomei*). Shimon borrows it back again for seventy days (*vehadar she'ilah minei shivim yomei*). The net result is that the *socher* retains twenty days of rental use: ten from the first rental period (one hundred minus ninety) and ten from the second (eighty minus seventy). But crucially, there are now two distinct rental agreements and two distinct borrowing agreements in play.

Then the cow dies during one of the periods when Shimon holds it. Since Shimon borrowed the cow from the *socher* — even though it was originally his own cow — he is a *shoel* with respect to the *socher*, and as such he bears full liability when the cow dies. But since there were two separate borrowing agreements, each of which independently created its own obligation, Rabbi Zeira argues that each generates a full liability: *dechula she'ilah veshe'ilah mechayev chada parah* — every borrowing agreement obligates a full cow. And since Shimon had two such agreements, he owes the *socher* two cows. Moreover, he must provide two separate cows for the *socher* to use during the remaining twenty days of rental, rather than allowing one cow to satisfy both rental obligations.

The logic, strange as it sounds, is that one should not understand the *shoel*'s obligation as "returning the object" — if it were, one payment would obviously suffice. Rather, embedded within each borrowing transaction is an independent contractual obligation: if such-and-such occurs, I will pay you. Since there were two transactions, there are two obligations. One might compare this to taking out two insurance policies on the same object from the same insurer: the object is the same, but the contractual obligations are independent. The degree to which the *shomer*'s duty is one of object-restoration versus one of contractual liability is precisely what is at stake.

### One Cow Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מִכְּדִי חֲדָא פָּרָה הִיא, עַיְילַהּ וְאַפְּקַהּ, אַפְּקַהּ מִשְּׂכִירוּת וְעַיְּילַהּ לִשְׁאֵילָה, אַפְּקַהּ מִשְּׁאֵילָה וְעַיְּילַהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִי אִיתַהּ לְפָרָה בְּעֵינָא דְּנֵימָא לֵיהּ הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina** concerning this *halakha*: **After all, it is one cow, and he introduced it into** one legal status **and removed it** from another legal status. **He removed it from** the status of **rental and he introduced it into** the status of **borrowing; he removed it from** the status of **borrowing and introduced it into** the status of **rental.** How then does the owner pay multiple cows for one cow? Ravina **said to** Rav Aḥa: **And is** the **cow intact** so **that** the owner **could say this to** the renter? Since the borrower cannot return the cow to the creditor, he is liable to return that which he committed to return, and he committed to return two cows, not one.

Rav Aha mi-Diphti gives voice to the obvious objection: *chada para hi* — it is one cow! *Ayala v'apka* — he brought it in and took it out, alternating between rental and borrowing, but the object at the center of every transaction is the same animal. All the back-and-forth movement — *apka mis'chirut ve'ayala lish'eilah, apka mishe'eilah ve'ayala lisechirut* — is just reclassification of the same cow. How can one cow generate multiple full liabilities?

The argument operates on two levels. First, if one payment represents giving back the cow, then one payment is sufficient — you cannot claim to have lost multiple cows when only one cow died. Second, each subsequent agreement supplants its predecessor: once the cow was re-rented, the first borrowing agreement became moot; once re-borrowed, the rental became moot. There are no truly independent transactions; each one replaced the last.

### Two Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֵין לוֹ עֲלֵיהֶן אֶלָּא שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹת, חֲדָא דִּשְׁאֵלָה וַחֲדָא דִּשְׂכִירוּת. שׁוּם שְׁאֵלָה אַחַת הִיא, וְשׁוּם שְׂכִירוּת אַחַת הִיא. דִּשְׁאֵלָה קָנֵי לִגְמָרֵי, דִּשְׂכִירוּת – עָבֵד בַּהּ יְמֵי שְׂכִירוּתֵיהּ, וּמַיהְדַּר לַיהּ לְמָרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi said** a third opinion: The renter **has against** the owner **only** a claim of **two cows, one for** the **borrowing** done by the owner, **and one for** fulfillment of his **rental** agreement. This is because the **category of borrowing is one and** the **category of rental is one.** As for the cow that is repayment **for** the **borrowing,** the renter **acquires** it **completely.** And as for the one **for** the **rental, he works with it** for the duration of **its rental period and** then **he returns it to its owner.**

Mar Bar Rav Ashi provides the resolution. The *gemara* asks: *u'mi ita l'para b'eina?* — is the cow physically present? If the cow were physically available, the objection that there is "one cow" would be decisive — just hand it back once. But the cow is not present; what exists is a *chiyuv*, an obligation, a liability. And liabilities can be multiple even when the underlying object is single, just as multiple insurance contracts on one object create independent claims.

Mar Bar Rav Ashi therefore distinguishes: *chada deshe'eilah* — one cow is owed by virtue of the borrowing obligation, which the *shoel* owes outright; and *chada desechirut* — one cow is owed by virtue of the rental agreement, which requires Shimon to make available to the *socher* the twenty days of rental use he is owed. The *socher* keeps the first cow permanently (as payment for the death of his "loaned" cow) and uses the second cow for his twenty rental days before returning it. The *gemara* also advances the alternative position that all the *she'eilos* are considered one and all the *sechirus* arrangements are considered one — *mishum she'eilah achat hi umishum sechirut achat hi* — yielding a single borrowing obligation and a single rental obligation rather than multiplicative liabilities.

## Oaths & Offerings

### Oath Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם בְּחַטָּאת, פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם בְּאָשָׁם. פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַשּׂוֹכֵר בְּחַטָּאת וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל בְּאָשָׁם, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַשּׂוֹכֵר בְּאָשָׁם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל בְּחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the situations described in the mishna, **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** If the renter and the borrower each took a false oath and are liable to bring offerings for their false oaths, there are **times that both** are liable **to** bring **a sin-offering; there are** times **that both** are liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering;** there are **times that the renter** is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering and the borrower** is liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering;** there are **times that the renter** is liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering and the borrower** is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah now shifts the discussion to a different dimension of the same basic scenario — the *socher* and *shoel* each taking oaths, and what happens when those oaths are false. The organizing principle is as follows. A standard *shevuat sheker* — a false oath made without monetary gain — incurs a *chatat* (sin offering). A false oath that serves to deny a monetary obligation and thereby generate financial benefit — *kefiras mamon* — incurs an *asham* (guilt offering), which carries the additional obligation of a one-fifth surcharge. With this principle in hand, Rabbi Yirmiyah maps out four combinations.

### Both Chattat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? כְּפִירַת מָמוֹן – אָשָׁם. בִּיטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם – חַטָּאת. פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם בְּחַטָּאת – כְּגוֹן שֶׁמֵּתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְאָמְרוּ נֶאֶנְסָה. שׂוֹכֵר דְּבֵין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ מִיפְּטַר פָּטוּר בְּחַטָּאת, שׁוֹאֵל דְּבֵין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ חַיּוֹבֵי מִיחַיַּיב בְּחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **How so?** One who takes a false oath that involves the **denial of a monetary matter** is liable to bring **a guilt-offering.** One who takes a false oath on **an utterance of the lips** that involves no denial of a monetary debt is liable to bring **a sin-offering.** The Gemara elaborates: There are **times that both** are liable **to** bring **a sin-offering.** This is in a case **where** the cow **died in its** typical **manner and** the renter and the borrower both **said** that it died **due** to circumstances **beyond his control. A renter,** who **in any case is exempt** from paying whether it died of natural causes or due to circumstances beyond his control, is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering** if he took a false oath. **A borrower,** who **in any case is liable** to pay regardless of the circumstances of its death, is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering** if he took a false oath. In both cases, the oath involved no denial of monetary debt.

The first case: both the *socher* and *shoel* bring a *chatat*. The cow died of natural causes (*umesa kedarka*) but both parties falsely claim it was struck by lightning — some other form of *ones*. The lie concerns the facts, not the underlying liability. For the *socher*, it makes no difference: *bein kach uvein kach patur* — whether the death was natural or from lightning, the *socher* is exempt. His false oath generates no monetary gain. For the *shoel*, whatever the specific form of the *ones*, he remains liable to pay, so his false characterization of the accident likewise changes nothing. Both have lied without profit, and both bring a *chatat*.

### Both Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם בְּאָשָׁם – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּגְנְבָה, וְאָמְרוּ מֵתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה, דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ קָא כָפְרִי מָמוֹנָא, דְּהָא מִיחַיְּיבִי, וְקָא פָטְרִי נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** There are **times that both** are liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering.** This is in a case **where** the cow **was stolen** from a borrower, **and** the renter and the borrower both **said** that **it died due to** ordinary **labor.** That is a case **where both denied a monetary matter, as they are** both **liable** to pay in a case of theft, **and** both take an oath on a claim with which they seek to **exempt themselves.**

The second case: both bring an *asham*. The cow was stolen (*nignava*), but both claim it died through normal use (*meisah machamas melachah*). This matters because *meisah machamas melachah* exempts even the *shoel* — it is the one *ones*-like circumstance in which the *shoel* is not liable. The actual situation — theft — renders both the *socher* and *shoel* liable. By falsely claiming the cow died through work, both are denying their monetary obligations: *de-tavaihu ka-kafrei mamona*. Both bring an *asham*.

### Mixed: Renter Chattat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׂוֹכֵר בְּחַטָּאת וְשׁוֹאֵל בְּאָשָׁם – כְּגוֹן שֶׁמֵּתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְאָמְרוּ מֵתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה. שׂוֹכֵר דְּבֵין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ מִיפְּטַר פָּטוּר – חַיָּיב בְּחַטָּאת. שׁוֹאֵל דְּמִיחַיַּיב בְּמֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְקָא פָטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּמֵתָה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה – בְּאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** There are times when **a renter** is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering and a borrower** is liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering.** This is in a case **where** the cow **died in its** typical **manner and** the renter and the borrower both **said** that **it died due to** ordinary **labor. A renter, who in any case is exempt,** as he is exempt from paying in cases where the ox was damaged or died due to circumstances beyond his control, is **liable to** bring **a sin-offering,** as the false oath involved no denial of monetary debt. **A borrower, who is liable** to pay when the cow **died in its** typical **manner and** attempted **to exempt himself** with the claim that **it died due to** ordinary **labor,** is liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering.**

The third case: the *socher* brings a *chatat* and the *shoel* brings an *asham*. The cow died naturally, but both claim it died through work. For the *socher*, this makes no difference — he was exempt regardless, so his lie yields no gain: *bein kach uvein kach patur patur*, and he brings a *chatat*. For the *shoel*, however, the lie is consequential: had it truly died naturally (*meisah kedarkah*), he would be liable (*chayav*); by claiming it died through work, he exempts himself. His oath denies a real monetary obligation, and so he brings an *asham*.

### Mixed: Renter Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׂוֹכֵר בְּאָשָׁם וְשׁוֹאֵל בְּחַטָּאת – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּגְנְבָה וְאָמְרוּ מֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ. שׂוֹכֵר הוּא דְּמִיחַיַּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה, וְקָא פָטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּמֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ – בְּאָשָׁם. שׁוֹאֵל דְּבֵין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ חַיּוֹבֵי מִיחַיַּיב – בְּחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** There are times when **a renter** is liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering and a borrower** is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering.** This is in a case **where** the cow **was stolen** from the borrower, **and** the renter and the borrower both **said** that it **died in its** typical **manner. The renter, who is liable** to pay in cases of **theft and loss and** attempted **to exempt himself** with the claim that it **died in its** typical **manner,** is liable **to** bring **a guilt-offering. A borrower, who in any case is liable** to pay, is liable **to** bring **a sin-offering.**

The fourth case: the *socher* brings an *asham* and the *shoel* brings a *chatat*. The cow was stolen (*nignava*), but both claim it died naturally. Theft renders the *socher* liable — a *socher* is liable for theft and loss (*geneivah va'aveidah*). By claiming natural death, he exempts himself from that liability, so his oath constitutes a denial of a monetary obligation: he brings an *asham*. The *shoel*, meanwhile, is liable whether the cow was stolen or died naturally — *bein kach uvein kach havah mechayev* — so his false characterization does not change his actual obligation. He brings a *chatat*.

### Reject Rabbi Ami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁהַדַּיָּינִים מַשְׁבִּיעִים אוֹתָהּ – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אוֹ נֶפֶשׁ כִּי תִשָּׁבַע לְבַטֵּא בִשְׂפָתַיִם״ ״כִּי תִשָּׁבַע״ מֵעַצְמָהּ. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rabbi Yirmeya **teaching us** with this systematic presentation of these cases? They are merely details based on established halakhic principles. The Gemara answers: His statement serves **to exclude** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami, who says:** With regard to **any oath that the judges administer, one is not liable** to bring a sin-offering for taking a false **oath** on **an utterance, as it is stated** in the passage concerning the obligation to bring an offering for taking a false oath: **"Or if a soul takes an oath clearly to utter with lips"** (Leviticus 5:4). The Gemara infers: The liability to bring an offering for taking a false oath applies only to one **who takes an oath on his own** initiative, but not when the oath is administered by others. Rabbi Yirmeya **teaches us that** the *halakha* is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami,** as one is liable to bring a sin-offering for taking a false oath on an utterance even if it was administered by another.

One might ask what new information these four cases convey, given that the underlying principles about *chatat* and *asham* were already known. The *gemara* answers: the analysis comes *le'apukei mid'Rabbi Ami* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Ami. Rabbi Ami held that any oath imposed by a court (*shvuah shehadayanim mashbi'in otah*) never gives rise to a *chatat* for *shevuat bituy* (simple false speech). His reasoning was derived from the verse *o nefesh ki tishava levate bisfasayim* — "if a soul swears to enunciate with his lips" — the key word being that the person swears on his own initiative. A court-imposed oath, he argued, falls outside the domain of *shevuat bituy* entirely.

The cases of Rabbi Yirmiyah demonstrate otherwise: court-imposed oaths in this context can indeed generate a *chatat*, and the ruling rejects Rabbi Ami's position outright. *Ka mashma lan d'lo k'Rabbi Ami* — we do not rule like Rabbi Ami.

## Watchman to Watchman

### Rav vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: שׁוֹמֵר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר, רַב אָמַר: פָּטוּר, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to **a bailee who conveyed to** another **bailee** the deposit with which he was entrusted. **Rav says:** He is **exempt** from payment in the same cases in which he is exempt when the deposit is in his possession. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He is **liable** to pay even in cases of damage due to circumstances beyond his control.

The *gemara* now turns to one of the most conceptually rich disputes in the chapter: *shomer she'masar l'shomer* — a watchman who hands over the object in his care to another watchman. Has he violated his duty to the owner? *Rav amar patur, v'Rabbi Yochanan amar chayav* — Rav holds the watchman is exempt; Rabbi Yochanan holds him liable.

### Rav's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַב, לָא מִבַּעְיָא שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר דְּעַלּוֹיֵי עַלְּיַיהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, דְּגָרוֹעֵי גָּרְעַהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא מְסָרָהּ לְבֶן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** According **to Rav's** line of **reasoning, it is not necessary** to state his ruling in a case where he was initially **an unpaid bailee who conveyed** the deposit for safeguarding **to a paid bailee, as** in that case the unpaid bailee **enhanced** the level of **his safeguarding,** since a paid bailee is liable to pay in instances where an unpaid bailee is exempt. **But even** in the case of **a paid bailee who conveyed** the deposit for safeguarding **to an unpaid bailee, where** the paid bailee **diminished** the level of **his safeguarding,** he is **exempt. What is the reason?** He is exempt **because he conveyed** the deposit **to a mentally competent person,** thereby effectively safeguarded the deposit.

Rav's position is that handing the object to another party does not increase the watchman's liability. Whether the original *shomer* was a *shomer sachar* (paid watchman) or *shomer chinam* (unpaid watchman), giving the object to someone else to watch does not elevate or alter whatever level of liability already applied. The act of delegation, in Rav's view, is neutral with respect to the original custodial obligation.

### Yochanan's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לָא מִיבַּעְיָא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם דְּגָרוֹעֵי גָּרְעַהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, דְּעַלּוֹיֵי עַלְּיַיהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ – חַיָּיב. דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״אֵין רְצוֹנִי שֶׁיְּהֵא פִּקְדוֹנִי בְּיַד אַחֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** According **to Rabbi Yoḥanan's** line of **reasoning, it is not necessary** to state his ruling in a case where he was initially **a paid bailee who conveyed** the deposit for safeguarding **to an unpaid bailee, as** the paid bailee **diminished** the level of **his safeguarding,** since an unpaid bailee is exempt in instances where a paid bailee is liable to pay. **But even** in the case of **an unpaid bailee who conveyed** the deposit for safeguarding **to a paid bailee, where** the unpaid bailee **enhanced** the level of **his safeguarding,** he is **liable** to pay. What is the reason? He is liable **because** the owner of the deposit **said to him: It is not my desire that my deposit be in the possession of another** bailee.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees sharply: the very act of giving it to someone else constitutes a violation of the watchman's responsibility to the owner. And importantly, this is not merely about diminishing the quality of watchmanship — even if the replacement watcher is superior, the act is still a breach. Rabbi Yochanan's rationale is: *d'amar lei ant lo meimanat li bishvuata* — the owner can say to the original *shomer*, "I trust your oath about what happens to my object; I do not trust the oath of this other person." The owner never agreed to have anyone else hold the object, and it is precisely because he trusted the original *shomer* — and only that person — that the arrangement was made. By placing the object under someone else's care, the original *shomer* has deprived the owner of the ability to receive a credible oath from the person he trusted. That deprivation itself constitutes negligence, making the original *shomer* liable for any subsequent loss that can in any way be attributed to the transfer.

### Gardeners Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא דְּרַב לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִתְּמַר, אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא. דְּהָנְהוּ גִּינָּאֵי, דְּכֹל יוֹמָא הֲווֹ מַפְקְדִי מָרַיְיהוּ גַּבַּהּ דְּהַהִיא סָבְתָּא. יוֹמָא חַד אַפְקְדֻינְהוּ לְגַבֵּי חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, שְׁמַע קָלָא בֵּי הִלּוּלָא נְפַק אֲזַל. אַפְקְדִינְהוּ לְגַבַּהּ דְּהַהִיא סָבְתָּא, אַדַּאֲזַל וַאֲתָא אִגְּנוּב מָרַיְיהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב וּפַטְרֵיהּ, מַאן דַּחֲזָא סָבַר מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹמֵר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר פָּטוּר. וְלָא הִיא, שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּכֹל יוֹמָא נָמֵי אִינְהוּ גּוּפַיְיהוּ גַּבַּהּ דְּהַהִיא סָבְתָּא הֲווֹ מַפְקְדִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: This** statement **that** is attributed to **Rav was not stated explicitly. Rather,** it was **inferred** from another statement of his, **as** it is related: There were **these gardeners who each day would deposit their spades with a certain old woman. One day they deposited** their spades **with one of** gardeners. He **heard noise** from **a wedding hall** and set **out** and went there. **He deposited** the spades **with that old woman. In** the time **that he went and came** back from the wedding, **their spades were stolen.** The case **came before Rav, and** Rav **exempted** the gardener who deposited the spades with the old woman. **One who observed** Rav's ruling **thought** that Rav issued that ruling **due to** the fact that **a bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to** another **bailee is exempt. But** that **is not so. There,** in the case of the spades, it **is different, as** the gardeners **themselves would deposit** their spades **with that old woman.** Since the gardeners cannot claim that it is not their desire for their deposit to be in the possession of this old woman, the gardener who did so is exempt.

The *gemara* notes that Rav's position was not stated explicitly but inferred from a case. *De'hanu ginai* — certain gardeners would deposit their tools each day with a certain older woman (*savta*). One day, they all entrusted their hoes to one of their number who stayed behind while the others attended a celebration. That gardener, hearing the festivities, went and deposited the tools with the same woman they habitually used, then joined the party. The tools were stolen from her. When the case came before Rav, he exempted the gardener.

Observers assumed this meant Rav ruled that *shomer she'masar l'shomer* is *patur*. But the *gemara* corrects this: *shani hatam* — that case was different. Every day the gardeners themselves deposited their tools with that woman. The claim *ein retzoni she'yehei pikdoni beyad acher* — I don't want my object in another's hands — simply could not be made here, because they had implicitly consented to this very arrangement through their daily practice. Even Rabbi Yochanan would agree the gardener is exempt in that case. The case therefore cannot serve as proof of Rav's general position regarding *shomer she'masar l'shomer*.

### Money & Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הַמַּפְקִיד מָעוֹת אֵצֶל חֲבֵירוֹ צְרָרָן וְהִפְשִׁילָן לַאֲחוֹרָיו, מְסָרָן לִבְנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְנָעַל בִּפְנֵיהֶם שֶׁלֹּא כָּרָאוּי – חַיָּיב, שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמַר כְּדֶרֶךְ הַשּׁוֹמְרִים. טַעְמָא דִּקְטַנִּים, הָא גְּדוֹלִים – פָּטוּר. אַמַּאי? נֵימָא לֵיהּ: אֵין רְצוֹנִי שֶׁיְּהֵא פִּקְדוֹנִי בְּיַד אַחֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises an objection** from a mishna (42a): In the case of **one who deposited coins with another,** and that bailee **bound it** in a cloth **and slung it behind him,** or **conveyed them to his minor son or daughter** for safeguarding, **or locked** the door **before** the coins **in an inappropriate,** i.e., insufficient, manner to secure them, the bailee is **liable** to pay for the coins, **as he did not safeguard** the coins **in the manner** typical **of bailees.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** he is liable to pay is **that** he conveyed the coins to his **minor** children, **but** if he conveyed them to his **adult** son or daughter he is **exempt. Why? Let the** owner **say to him** as Rabbi Yochanan said: **It is not my desire that my deposit be in the possession of another** bailee, and therefore even if the children are adults the bailee should be liable to pay.

A related case is raised: one who deposits money with a watchman, and that watchman either binds it up and throws it behind his back, or gives it to his minor children to watch, or locks it away but not properly — he is *chayav*, because *shelo shamar kederech hashomrim*, he did not watch it in the manner that watchmen are expected to watch. From the fact that the case specifies *minor* children, one can infer that had the watchman given it to his *adult* children, he would be exempt — even though they are, technically, other people. The reason is articulated by Rava: *kol hamaphkid al da'at ishto uvanav hu demaphkid* — anyone who deposits money with someone understands implicitly that that person may rely on his wife and adult children. The owner's consent to the watchman's custody encompasses the watchman's household, much as the daily practice with the older woman constituted implicit consent. The case of strangers, by contrast, is different: whether adult or minor, a stranger falls under the prohibition of *ein retzoni she'yehei pikdoni beyad acher*, and the watchman would be *chayav* regardless. The *Nehardeans* reinforce this by noting that the specification of "children" (as opposed to simply "minors") confirms that only one's own children enjoy this dispensation — others do not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל הַמַּפְקִיד עַל דַּעַת אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבָנָיו הוּא מַפְקִיד. אָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי, דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי: אוֹ שֶׁמְּסָרָן לִבְנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים – חַיָּיב. הָא לִבְנוֹ וּלְבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים – פָּטוּר. מִכְלָל דְּלַאֲחֵרִים, לָא שְׁנָא גְּדוֹלִים וְלָא שְׁנָא קְטַנִּים – חַיָּיב, דְּאִם כֵּן, לִיתְנֵי קְטַנִּים סְתָמָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** No proof can be cited, as it is clear that in the case of **anyone who deposits** an item with another, it is **with the awareness** that at times the bailee's **wife and his children** will safeguard the item that **he deposits** it, as the bailee cannot be with the deposit at all times. The Sages **of Neharde'a say:** The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: Or** if he **conveyed** the coins **to his minor son or daughter** for safeguarding, he is **liable** to pay. **But** if he conveyed them to his **adult son and daughter,** he is **exempt. By inference,** one can conclude **that** with regard **to others,** it **is no different** if they are **adults and** it **is no different** if they are **minors.** Either way, the bailee is **liable** to pay, **as, if** there were a difference, **let** the *tanna* **teach:** If he conveyed the coins to **minors, without qualification.** The Gemara concludes: Since the *tanna* specifically addressed the case of one's minor children, **learn from** the wording of the mishna that the difference between minors and adults exists only with regard to one's children.

### Ruling: Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִלְכְתָא: שׁוֹמֵר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר – חַיָּיב, לָא מִבַּעְיָא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם דְּגָרוֹעֵי גָּרְעַהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – חַיָּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה, הַאיְךְ לָא מְהֵימַן לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: The *halakha*** is: **A bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to** another **bailee** is **liable** to pay. **It is not necessary** to say that this is the *halakha* if he was **a paid bailee who conveyed** the deposit **to an unpaid bailee, as** in that case the first bailee **diminished** the level of **his safeguarding,** as an unpaid bailee is exempt from paying in instances where a paid bailee is obligated to do so. **But even** if it was initially **an unpaid bailee who conveyed** the deposit for safeguarding **to a paid bailee,** the first bailee is **liable** to pay. **What is the reason** that he is liable in that case? He is liable, **as** the owner of the deposit can **say to him: You are trustworthy to me when** you take **an oath** that the item was stolen or lost. **That** person **is not trustworthy to me when** he takes **an oath.**

Rava issues the *hilcheta*: *shomer she'masar l'shomer chayav*. The original watchman is liable. And the ruling applies regardless of direction: *lo mibaya shomer sachar she'masar l'shomer chinam chayav* — not only is a paid watchman who gives to an unpaid one liable (having diminished the level of protection), but *afilu shomer chinam she'masar l'shomer sachar chayav* — even an unpaid watchman who gives to a paid one is liable, having apparently improved the protection. *Mai ta'ama?* — Rava grounds the ruling not in the language of negligence or diminished care, but in the formulation: *at meheimant li bishvua, hai lo meheiman li bishvua* — you are trustworthy to me by oath; this other person is not trustworthy to me by oath. By handing over the object, the original *shomer* has placed the owner in the position of being unable to rely on the oath of the one person he chose to trust. That is the essential violation, and it renders the *shomer* liable for any subsequent loss attributable to the transfer.

The *nafka mina* between Rava's reasoning and the prior formulations would emerge in a case where the original *shomer* directly witnessed what happened and can personally deliver the oath to the original owner. In such a case, the concern about oath-reliability would not apply. This question, along with the closely related principle of *techilato bifshi'ah vesofo b'ones* — beginning with negligence and ending with an unavoidable accident — will be taken up in the next session.
