# Zevachim 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah & Kiddush

### Kohen Becomes Tamei

The Gemara opens a new question within the broader framework of *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* — the hand and foot washing that *kohanim* must perform before the *avodah*. The *iba'ya lehu* asks: *tumah mahu she-to'il kiddush yadayim v'raglayim?* — if a *kohen* who has already washed his hands and feet becomes *tamei* in the course of the *avodah*, does the purification process he must undergo — immersing in a *mikvah* — count as a substitute for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, or must he wash his hands and feet again separately afterward?

The question is framed in a somewhat unusual way, which Rashi's explanation helps clarify. The Gemara presents two sides: *im timtzei lomar yetziah la pasla d'gavra chazi* — if leaving the *beis hamikdash* does not disqualify one's prior *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, this is because the *kohen* who has left is still a *gavra chazi*, a person who remains fit to perform the *avodah*. His departure does not strip him of his capacity. But in our case, where the *kohen* has become *tamei*, he is entirely removed from the ability to perform the *avodah*, and thus one might argue that he has experienced a heightened form of *hesach hadaat* — a loss of mindful focus — since he is no longer in a state of readiness to serve.

### Hesach Hadaat

The question therefore turns on what generates *hesach hadaat*. Is it the sheer duration of the interruption, or is it the qualitative character of the interruption — specifically, whether the person's circumstances are such that he is no longer oriented toward performing the *avodah*? The counter-argument is *odilma keivan d'hadar chazi daklei v'lo masech datei* — perhaps the very fact that he can go to the *mikvah* and become *tahor* again means that he never fully disengages, and so the immersion does not constitute a disqualifying interruption requiring a new *kiddush*.

The difficulty with the question, however, is that if a *kohen* becomes *tamei* in his entire body — as opposed to merely his hands — he normally requires not only immersion but also *he'erev shemesh*, waiting until sunset, before achieving full *taharah*. During that intervening period he is a *tevul yom*, an intermediate status that is not fully *tahor*. This extended waiting period would seem to compound the concern about *hesach hadaat*, and it is this objection that naturally arises against the question.

### Hands Alone Tamei

The Gemara addresses a simpler case first. The *braita* states: *kidesh yadav v'raglav v'nitma* — if a *kohen* washed his hands and feet and then his hands became *tamei* (the Rabbis recognize that hands can become *tamei* through contact with certain things that would not render the whole body impure), *matbilan v'ein tzarich lekadesh* — he may immerse his hands in a *mikvah* and does not need to wash them again from the *kiyor*. The Gemara immediately clarifies: *nitma'u yadav lo miba'ya lan; ki miba'ya lan nitma kol gufo* — the case of hands alone becoming *tamei* is not the genuine question, since it is readily resolved by simple immersion of the hands. That case does not require the prolonged waiting that would generate serious *hesach hadaat* concerns. The real question is *nitma kol gufo* — where the person's entire body has become *tamei*.

### Tumah Near Sunset

Once the question is restricted to whole-body *tumah*, the objection arises that the *kohen* will be required to wait for *he'erev shemesh*, which would seem to guarantee an extended interruption. The Gemara therefore narrows the question further: *kegon de-itmei samuch li-shki'at ha-chamah* — the case under discussion is specifically one where the *kohen* became *tamei* shortly before sunset, went quickly to the *mikvah*, and the sun then set, rendering him fully *tahor* — potentially within minutes. In such a scenario, does the brief interlude of *tumah* constitute sufficient *hesach hadaat* to require a renewed *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, or not? With the question properly framed, the Gemara has not yet provided an answer — it has only clarified the scope.

## Para Aduma

### Para Washing Location

The Gemara next attempts to resolve the open question by adducing a *braita* about *Para Aduma*, the red heifer. The *para aduma* represents a paradigmatic halachic borderline case: it bears many hallmarks of the *avodah* — it must be performed by a *kohen*, it requires *bigdei kehunah*, it demands *taharah* — yet it is performed outside the *beis hamikdash* and is not technically a *korban*. Its hybrid status means it is frequently cited as a comparison and just as frequently rejected as one, since its rules are so singular that lessons drawn from it are often inapplicable elsewhere.

*Rabbi Chiya berabbi Yosef* states that for the *para aduma*, a *kohen* may perform *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* using a *kli sharet* inside the *beis hamikdash* and then leave to perform the *para aduma* on *Har HaZeisim* — and the intervening time and distance are not considered a disqualifying interruption.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

*Rabbi Yochanan* goes further: *afilu bachutz* — one may even wash his hands outside the *beis hamikdash*, *va-afilu b'kli chol* — even using an ordinary vessel rather than a *kli sharet*, *va-afilu b'makeira shel cheres* — even a low-grade earthenware basin. This is because the *para aduma* is not truly an *avodah*, and the act of hand washing preparatory to it is correspondingly less formal in its requirements. The implication seems to address the timing concern as well: if one may wash in an entirely ordinary vessel outside the Temple, time elapsed between washing and performance would seem not to be the issue.

### Tzadukim Dispute

At this point a striking *mishnah* from *Masechet Para* is introduced — one that has become a flashpoint in historical and polemical discourse. *Metammim hayu ha-kohen ha-soref et ha-parah* — the Rabbis would deliberately render *tamei* the *kohen* who was about to burn the *para aduma*, and then immediately immerse him in a *mikvah*, *lehotzi milibam shel Tzadukim* — in order to counter the position of the *Tzadukim*. The *Tzadukim* held that the *para aduma* must be performed at the highest possible level of *taharah*, and that a *tevul yom* — one who has immersed but not yet waited for sunset — is insufficiently *tahor* to perform it. The *Perushim* disputed this: a *tevul yom* is *tahor* enough for *para aduma*. To demonstrate this publicly, the Rabbis would make the *kohen* *tamei*, take him to the *mikvah*, and have him perform the burning while he was still in the status of *tevul yom*.

This is itself a remarkable practice, given the extraordinary stringencies surrounding *para aduma* in other respects: the extension of the normal gradations of *tumah* to fifth and even further degrees of remove, the care taken with the children who drew the water — who were born and raised on special platforms in Jerusalem to ensure they had never contracted *tumah* — the requirement for a double bridge to Har HaZeisim. All of this stringency coexisted with the deliberate use of a *tevul yom* for the burning, and that is precisely the *Perushim*'s point: *tevul yom* is a categorically different status, and *para aduma* is categorically different from the regular *avodah*.

### Why Washing Needed

From the above *mishnah*, the Gemara attempts to derive that *tumah* is not an interruption for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*: *shema mina lo paslah bah tumah* — since the *kohen* was made *tamei* and then immersed, and yet performed the *para aduma* without a new *kiddush*, apparently the immersion sufficed and *tumah* did not invalidate the prior washing. But the Gemara immediately rejects this proof: *shani para ho'il ve-tevul yom lo pasil bah* — the *para aduma* is different, since a *tevul yom* is valid for it; one cannot draw ordinary conclusions from its rules. The retort is then raised: if *para aduma* is so anomalous, why is *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* required for it at all? The answer: *ke-ein avodah bayinan* — one must perform it in the manner of the *avodah*, even if it is not the *avodah* in the full sense. The *para aduma* walks the line — it resembles the *avodah* in some respects and not in others. The attempted proof from *para aduma* fails, and the original question remains unresolved.

## Kiyor Immersion

### Dipping Question

A new question is introduced: *mahu le-kadesh yadav ve-raglav be-kiyor* — may a *kohen* fulfill the requirement of *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* by dipping his hands and feet into the *kiyor* rather than having water poured from it over them? Rashi clarifies that without this precision, the question would not even arise. The Torah uses the expression *mimenu* in the context of *ve-rachatzu yadeihem ve-ragleihem* — "from it" — which may imply washing *from* the *kiyor* rather than immersing *into* it. The Gemara considers whether *velo betocho* — "and not inside it" — can be derived from *mimenu*, or whether *afilu betocho* — even immersing into the *kiyor* itself — might be permitted.

### Mei Me'arah Proof

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* attempts to resolve this by citing a *braita*: *o she-taval be-mei me'arah ve-avad avodato psulah* — if a *kohen* immersed in cave water (*mei me'arah*), essentially a *mikvah*, and then performed the *avodah*, the *avodah* is *pesulah*. Since the water of the *kiyor* resembles *mei me'arah* in being stationary — *ha-kiyor dumya de-mei me'arah* — perhaps immersing into it would similarly be invalid, and only having water poured from it would work. This would suggest that dipping hands and feet into the *kiyor* is prohibited.

### Exclusion Logic

The Gemara rejects this proof. The *braita* about *mei me'arah* was not written to exclude dipping in the *kiyor*; it was written to exclude something else entirely: *shelo tomar kol gufo tovel bahen yado ve-raglo lo kol sheken* — one might have thought that since a person immerses his whole body in a *mikvah* to achieve *taharah*, surely immersing only hands and feet would achieve *kiddush* for the *avodah*. The *braita* teaches that this is not so — immersion in a *mikvah*, even of the whole body, does not substitute for the formal *kiddush* from the *kiyor*, which must be performed with a *kli sharet*, in the *beis hamikdash*, with the proper intention. The *braita* therefore excludes dipping hands and feet into a *mikvah* — but it does not thereby settle the question of whether dipping hands and feet into the *kiyor* itself might be valid, since the *kiyor* is the designated vessel. The attempted proof of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak is not dispositive, and the question remains open.

## Kiyor Disqualification

### Rav Chiya's View

The Gemara turns to a related question: at what point does the water remaining in the *kiyor* become *pasul* for use the next day? The *kiyor* was equipped with a mechanism — a wooden crank or winch — that allowed it to be submerged into an underground cistern at night, keeping the water from becoming *pasul* through *lina* (overnight exposure). *Rav Chiya bar Yosef* offers a graduated rule: *mei kiyor nifsalin le-matirin ke-matirin, le-eivarim ke-eivarim*. The water becomes *pasul* in parallel with the *avodah* for which it is used. For *matirin* — the daytime *avodot* such as the *korbanot* that exempt a person from obligation — the water becomes *pasul* when those *avodot* become *pasul*, namely at sunset. For *eivarim* — the overnight burning of fats and limbs on the *mizbeach* — the water becomes *pasul* at sunrise. Rav Chiya bar Yosef thus has two distinct disqualification times: sunset for day-service, and sunrise for night-service.

### Rav Chisda's View

*Rav Chisda* disagrees: *af le-matirin ein nifsalin ela be-amud ha-shachar ke-eivarim* — even for the daytime *avodot*, the *kiyor* water is not disqualified until dawn. There is thus only one disqualification time for Rav Chisda: sunrise, regardless of which *avodah* the water is intended to facilitate.

### Rabbi Yochanan Reconciled

*Rabbi Yochanan* is cited with a statement that initially appears to posit a third, stricter position: *kiyor keivan she-shik'u shuv ein ma'alehu* — once the *kiyor* has been submerged in the evening, one may not raise it again until the next morning. This seems to imply that if the *kiyor* were raised during the night, the water would be disqualified — suggesting a disqualification time earlier than sunrise, and perhaps even applying to night-time *avodot*.

However, the Gemara then cites a second statement of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted by *Rabbi Assi* in the name of *Ilfa*: *kiyor she-lo shika'o me-etmol, mikadesh mimenu la-avodat laila* — if they did not submerge the *kiyor* on the previous evening, one may still wash from it during the night. *U-le-machar eino mikadesh* — but once it becomes day, it is no longer valid. This second version places disqualification at sunrise, apparently agreeing with Rav Chisda rather than introducing a stricter position.

To resolve the tension between Rabbi Yochanan's two statements, the Gemara considers whether his ruling about not raising the *kiyor* once it has been submerged refers specifically to *avodat yom* — the daytime service — rather than to *avodat laila*. If the *kiyor* was put away for the night and one seeks to raise it for a nighttime *avodah*, perhaps that is not permitted for the daytime service afterward, but it would still be usable for night-service. Under this reading, Rabbi Yochanan would coincide with *Rav Chiya bar Yosef*: different times for day and night *avodot*. But if so, what distinguishes Rabbi Yochanan from Rav Chiya bar Yosef?

### Gezeirat Shikua Debate

The Gemara resolves this: *ika beiniyahu gezeirat shikua* — the real disagreement between Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Chisda is not about the practical consequence of leaving the water out overnight, but about whether there was a positive mandate — *gezeirat shikua* — requiring the *kiyor* to be submerged each evening. Rabbi Yochanan holds that there was such a mandatory requirement, independently of whatever halachic consequence might follow from leaving it out. Rav Chisda holds that there was no such formal decree; if the *kiyor* was left out and the water remained valid until sunrise, no obligation was violated by not submerging it.

A further complication arises from Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that if one washed his hands in preparation for *trumat ha-deshen* — the removal of ash from the altar that takes place before dawn — he need not wash again later, *she-kvar kidesh mi-techilat avodah*, since his initial washing before the previous day's *avodah* carries forward. This fits well with the opinion of *Rava*, who holds the *halacha* like *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon* — that *ein layla ela be-kiddush*, nightfall alone does not invalidate a prior *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, and so a washing performed the previous afternoon remains in effect through the night. But it creates a difficulty for *Abaye*, who rules like *Rabbi* — that sunset *does* constitute an interruption, requiring a new *kiddush* at the start of the night. Under Abaye's view, how could one perform *trumat ha-deshen* on the basis of a *kiddush* from the prior afternoon? The Gemara acknowledges this difficulty — *kashya d'Rabbi ad-Rabbi* — and considers whether the cases can be distinguished based on whether the *kiyor* was submerged, removed, and submerged again. Ultimately, the Gemara concludes that the underlying debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Chisda reduces to the question of *mitzvat shiku'a* — whether the nightly submersion was a formal obligation or merely a practical precaution against forgetting — and is connected to the broader *machlokes* between Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi regarding whether nighttime serves as an interruption for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*.

## Kiyor Timing & Crank

### Crank Signal

The Gemara cites a *mishnah* from *Tamid* bearing on the timing of when the *kiyor* was raised and lowered. The *mishnah* states: *lo hayu ro'in oto ve-lo shom'im et kolo ad she-shom'im kol shel etz she-asa Ben Katin mochni la-kiyor* — they would not see it or hear it until they heard the sound of the wooden crank that *Ben Katin* had fashioned for the *kiyor*. Upon hearing that sound, the *kohanim* would say: the time has come for *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim min ha-kiyor*.

### Raising vs. Submerging

The Gemara asks: *mai lav de-maslek lei* — does this *mishnah* refer to the moment when they were raising the *kiyor* (taking it out of the water in the morning), or *de-mishkale* — when they were submerging it (lowering it into the water in the evening)? On the plain reading of the *mishnah*, it sounds like a morning event: people hear the crank sound and know it is time to come wash. But the Gemara attempts to read it as an evening event, in order to establish that the proper time for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* associated with the *kiyor* is at or around sunset.

The difficulty with reading it as submersion is: *mi mishtama kalei?* — if they are simply lowering the *kiyor*, why would the crank make a sound? Lowering would be aided by gravity, and could be accomplished without the noisy effort of the winch. The Gemara responds: *meshkale be-gilgala* — they would in fact lower it using the wheel, so it did produce sound. An alternative formulation clarifies: *demishkale be-chumratei ki heichi de-lishtama kalei* — they would lower it using the crank mechanism specifically so that the sound would be audible, and people would take note.

### Double Announcement

One might ask why such a sound signal was necessary at all, given that the *beis hamikdash* had *Gavini*, the official town crier, who would announce events and signal to the *kohanim* what time it was. The Gemara's answer is elegant: *bei terei hekeirei havu avdei* — they would make a double announcement. The crank sound and the human proclamation served as two independent signals so that no one would miss the notification — *de-shama me-hai atu, de-shama me-hai atu* — whoever heard one signal would come, and whoever heard the other would also come. The conclusion is that the *mishnah* in *Tamid* is understood as describing the evening submersion of the *kiyor*, and the crank sound together with the crier's announcement jointly marked the time of *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* for the night-service.
