# Zevachim 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah & Kiddush

### Kohen Becomes Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: טוּמְאָה, מַהוּ שֶׁתּוֹעִיל לְקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר יְצִיאָה לָא פָּסְלָה – דְּגַבְרָא חֲזֵי, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּגַבְרָא לָא חֲזֵי – אַסּוֹחֵי מַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דַּהֲדַר חֲזֵי – [דָּק לֵיהּ] וְלָא מַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to a priest who contracts **ritual impurity** during the service? Is this **effective to** disqualify the **sanctification of** his **hands and feet** such that he must sanctify them again when he becomes pure? **If you say** that **leaving** the Temple **does not disqualify** sanctification, perhaps this is **because the person** remains **fit** to serve; **but here, where the person is unfit** for service, perhaps **he becomes distracted. Or perhaps, since he** will become **fit again** when he becomes pure, **he takes care not to become distracted.**

### Hesach Hadaat

The question therefore turns on what generates *hesach hadaat*. Is it the sheer duration of the interruption, or is it the qualitative character of the interruption — specifically, whether the person's circumstances are such that he is no longer oriented toward performing the *avodah*? The counter-argument is *odilma keivan d'hadar chazi daklei v'lo masech datei* — perhaps the very fact that he can go to the *mikvah* and become *tahor* again means that he never fully disengages, and so the immersion does not constitute a disqualifying interruption requiring a new *kiddush*.

The difficulty with the question, however, is that if a *kohen* becomes *tamei* in his entire body — as opposed to merely his hands — he normally requires not only immersion but also *he'erev shemesh*, waiting until sunset, before achieving full *taharah*. During that intervening period he is a *tevul yom*, an intermediate status that is not fully *tahor*. This extended waiting period would seem to compound the concern about *hesach hadaat*, and it is this objection that naturally arises against the question.

### Hands Alone Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו וְנִטְמְאוּ – מַטְבִּילָן וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ. יָדוֹ לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן, כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן נִטְמָא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If a priest **sanctified his hands and feet and they became impure, he** may **immerse them, and he does not need to sanctify** them again. The Gemara responds: **We do not raise the dilemma** with regard to a case where only **his hands became impure. When we raise the dilemma** it is with regard to a case where **his entire body became impure.**

### Tumah Near Sunset

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל גּוּפוֹ?! תִּיפּוֹק לִי: כֵּיוָן דְּבָעֵי לְמֶעְבַּד הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ, אַסּוֹחֵי מַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ! כְּגוֹן דְּאִיטַּמִּי סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** But if **his entire body** contracted impurity, **let me derive** that the sanctification is disqualified **since he needs to allow the sun to set** in order to become pure after immersing, and **he** certainly **becomes distracted** in the interim. The Gemara responds: The dilemma concerns a case **where he became impure adjacent to sunset,** so that he does not need to wait long after immersing and will not become distracted.

Once the question is restricted to whole-body *tumah*, the objection arises that the *kohen* will be required to wait for *he'erev shemesh*, which would seem to guarantee an extended interruption. The Gemara therefore narrows the question further: *kegon de-itmei samuch li-shki'at ha-chamah* — the case under discussion is specifically one where the *kohen* became *tamei* shortly before sunset, went quickly to the *mikvah*, and the sun then set, rendering him fully *tahor* — potentially within minutes. In such a scenario, does the brief interlude of *tumah* constitute sufficient *hesach hadaat* to require a renewed *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, or not? With the question properly framed, the Gemara has not yet provided an answer — it has only clarified the scope.

## Para Aduma

### Para Washing Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פָּרָה – רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מְקַדֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בִּפְנִים, וְיוֹצֵא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּחוּץ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּכְלִי חוֹל, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקִידָּה שֶׁל חֶרֶס!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the following dispute: With regard to the priest who performs the rite of the red **heifer, Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rabbi Yosef, says:** He must **sanctify** his hands and feet **with a service vessel inside** the Temple, **and** afterward he **leaves** the Temple to perform the service. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He may sanctify his hands **even outside** of the Temple, **and even with a non-sacred vessel, and even with an earthenware bowl.**

The *para aduma* represents a paradigmatic halachic borderline case: it bears many hallmarks of the *avodah* — it must be performed by a *kohen*, it requires *bigdei kehunah*, it demands *taharah* — yet it is performed outside the *beis hamikdash* and is not technically a *korban*. Its hybrid status means it is frequently cited as a comparison and just as frequently rejected as one, since its rules are so singular that lessons drawn from it are often inapplicable elsewhere.

*Rabbi Chiya berabbi Yosef* states that for the *para aduma*, a *kohen* may perform *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* using a *kli sharet* inside the *beis hamikdash* and then leave to perform the *para aduma* on *Har HaZeisim* — and the intervening time and distance are not considered a disqualifying interruption.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

*Rabbi Yochanan* goes further: *afilu bachutz* — one may even wash his hands outside the *beis hamikdash*, *va-afilu b'kli chol* — even using an ordinary vessel rather than a *kli sharet*, *va-afilu b'makeira shel cheres* — even a low-grade earthenware basin. This is because the *para aduma* is not truly an *avodah*, and the act of hand washing preparatory to it is correspondingly less formal in its requirements. The implication seems to address the timing concern as well: if one may wash in an entirely ordinary vessel outside the Temple, time elapsed between washing and performance would seem not to be the issue.

### Tzadukim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא פָּרָה – דְּטַמּוֹיֵי מְטַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: מְטַמְּאִין הָיוּ הַכֹּהֵן הַשּׂוֹרֵף אֶת הַפָּרָה וּמַטְבִּילִין אוֹתוֹ, לְהוֹצִיא מִלִּבָּן שֶׁל צַדּוּקִין שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: בִּמְעוֹרְבֵי שֶׁמֶשׁ הָיְתָה נַעֲשֵׂית; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לָא פָּסְלָה בָּהּ (מַחְשֶׁבֶת) טוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** in preparation for the rite of the red **heifer, we** intentionally **impart impurity to** the priest after he has already sanctified his hands and feet, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Para* 3:7): **They would render the priest who burns the heifer ritually impure and immerse him** immediately, **to remove** a misconception **from the hearts of** the **Sadducees** by means of a public display of disregard for their ruling. **As** the Sadducees **would say:** Only **by those for whom the sun set was** the heifer ritual **performed.** The Sadducees believed that it is prohibited for an impure priest who immersed that day to burn the red heifer until sunset, when the purification process is completed. The Gemara concludes: Since this impurity does not disqualify the sanctification of his hands and feet, **learn from it** that **impurity does not disqualify** sanctification of the hands and feet in general.

At this point a striking *mishnah* from *Masechet Para* is introduced — one that has become a flashpoint in historical and polemical discourse. *Metammim hayu ha-kohen ha-soref et ha-parah* — the Rabbis would deliberately render *tamei* the *kohen* who was about to burn the *para aduma*, and then immediately immerse him in a *mikvah*, *lehotzi milibam shel Tzadukim* — in order to counter the position of the *Tzadukim*. The *Tzadukim* held that the *para aduma* must be performed at the highest possible level of *taharah*, and that a *tevul yom* — one who has immersed but not yet waited for sunset — is insufficiently *tahor* to perform it. The *Perushim* disputed this: a *tevul yom* is *tahor* enough for *para aduma*. To demonstrate this publicly, the Rabbis would make the *kohen* *tamei*, take him to the *mikvah*, and have him perform the burning while he was still in the status of *tevul yom*.

This is itself a remarkable practice, given the extraordinary stringencies surrounding *para aduma* in other respects: the extension of the normal gradations of *tumah* to fifth and even further degrees of remove, the care taken with the children who drew the water — who were born and raised on special platforms in Jerusalem to ensure they had never contracted *tumah* — the requirement for a double bridge to Har HaZeisim. All of this stringency coexisted with the deliberate use of a *tevul yom* for the burning, and that is precisely the *Perushim*'s point: *tevul yom* is a categorically different status, and *para aduma* is categorically different from the regular *avodah*.

### Why Washing Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, הוֹאִיל דִּטְבוּל יוֹם לָא פָּסֵיל בָּהּ. אִי הָכִי, לְמָה לִי דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ? כְּעֵין עֲבוֹדָה [בָּעֵינַן].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The service of the **heifer is different, as one who immersed that day is not unfit for** performing **it,** unlike any other rite. Likewise, impurity does not disqualify the sanctification of hands and feet preceding the service of the heifer. The Gemara asks: **If so, why do I** require **that** the priest **sanctify** his hands and feet at all for the service of the red heifer? The Gemara responds: **We require** that the service of the red heifer be **similar to** the **service** of offerings performed inside the Temple.

From the above *mishnah*, the Gemara attempts to derive that *tumah* is not an interruption for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*: *shema mina lo paslah bah tumah* — since the *kohen* was made *tamei* and then immersed, and yet performed the *para aduma* without a new *kiddush*, apparently the immersion sufficed and *tumah* did not invalidate the prior washing. But the Gemara immediately rejects this proof: *shani para ho'il ve-tevul yom lo pasil bah* — the *para aduma* is different, since a *tevul yom* is valid for it; one cannot draw ordinary conclusions from its rules. The retort is then raised: if *para aduma* is so anomalous, why is *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* required for it at all? The answer: *ke-ein avodah bayinan* — one must perform it in the manner of the *avodah*, even if it is not the *avodah* in the full sense. The *para aduma* walks the line — it resembles the *avodah* in some respects and not in others. The attempted proof from *para aduma* fails, and the original question remains unresolved.

## Kiyor Immersion

### Dipping Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לְקַדֵּשׁ יָדָיו [וְרַגְלָיו] בַּכִּיּוֹר? ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – וְלֹא בְּתוֹכוֹ; אוֹ דִלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ בְּתוֹכוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to a priest who wishes **to sanctify his hands and feet** by dipping them **in the Basin** rather than letting the water run from it onto them? Must one say that the priest may not do so, since **the Merciful One states:** "And Aaron and his sons shall wash their hands and their feet **from it"** (Exodus 30:19), **and not inside it? Or perhaps** the priest may **even** use water still **inside it.**

A new question is introduced: *mahu le-kadesh yadav ve-raglav be-kiyor* — may a *kohen* fulfill the requirement of *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* by dipping his hands and feet into the *kiyor* rather than having water poured from it over them? Rashi clarifies that without this precision, the question would not even arise. The Torah uses the expression *mimenu* in the context of *ve-rachatzu yadeihem ve-ragleihem* — "from it" — which may imply washing *from* the *kiyor* rather than immersing *into* it. The Gemara considers whether *velo betocho* — "and not inside it" — can be derived from *mimenu*, or whether *afilu betocho* — even immersing into the *kiyor* itself — might be permitted.

### Mei Me'arah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, תָּא שְׁמַע: אוֹ שֶׁטָּבַל בְּמֵי מְעָרָה וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. הָא מֵי כִיּוֹר דּוּמְיָא דְּמֵי מְעָרָה, וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **Or** if a priest **immersed in cave water and performed** the **service, his service is disqualified,** since this is not adequate sanctification of the hands and feet. From the *baraita* one infers: **But** if he used **water from** the **Basin** in a manner **similar to cave water,** i.e., by immersing his hands, **and performed** the **service, his service is valid.**

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* attempts to resolve this by citing a *braita*: *o she-taval be-mei me'arah ve-avad avodato psulah* — if a *kohen* immersed in cave water (*mei me'arah*), essentially a *mikvah*, and then performed the *avodah*, the *avodah* is *pesulah*. Since the water of the *kiyor* resembles *mei me'arah* in being stationary — *ha-kiyor dumya de-mei me'arah* — perhaps immersing into it would similarly be invalid, and only having water poured from it would work. This would suggest that dipping hands and feet into the *kiyor* is prohibited.

### Exclusion Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, מֵי מְעָרָה אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ; שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר: כׇּל גּוּפוֹ טוֹבֵל בָּהֶן – יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is possible that immersing one's hands in the Basin is insufficient as well. Nevertheless, it **was necessary for** the *baraita* to mention **cave water** so **that you shall not say:** If one **may immerse his entire body in** cave water to purify himself, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that he may sanctify **his hands and feet** by immersing them in cave water? The *baraita* therefore teaches that despite being fit for immersion of the entire body, cave water is unfit for sanctification of the hands and feet.

The Gemara rejects this proof. The *braita* about *mei me'arah* was not written to exclude dipping in the *kiyor*; it was written to exclude something else entirely: *shelo tomar kol gufo tovel bahen yado ve-raglo lo kol sheken* — one might have thought that since a person immerses his whole body in a *mikvah* to achieve *taharah*, surely immersing only hands and feet would achieve *kiddush* for the *avodah*. The *braita* teaches that this is not so — immersion in a *mikvah*, even of the whole body, does not substitute for the formal *kiddush* from the *kiyor*, which must be performed with a *kli sharet*, in the *beis hamikdash*, with the proper intention. The *braita* therefore excludes dipping hands and feet into a *mikvah* — but it does not thereby settle the question of whether dipping hands and feet into the *kiyor* itself might be valid, since the *kiyor* is the designated vessel. The attempted proof of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak is not dispositive, and the question remains open.

## Kiyor Disqualification

### Rav Chiya's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף: מֵי כִיּוֹר נִפְסָלִין לְמַתִּירִין כְּמַתִּירִין, לְאֵבָרִים כְּאֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef says: The water in** the **Basin is disqualified** by being left out, at different times of day for different rites. **For** rites that are **permitting factors** for an offering, i.e., that must be performed before the meat of the offering may be eaten, the water is disqualified at sunset, **like** the **permitting factors** themselves, which may be performed only during the day. For example, if a priest wishes to sprinkle an offering's blood on the altar, he may not sanctify his hands and feet from the Basin unless it was sunk into its pit at sunset on the previous day, preventing the water from being disqualified. Similarly, **for** the burning of the offering's **limbs** on the altar, which may be performed all night, the water in the Basin is disqualified at dawn, **like** the **limbs** themselves.

The Gemara turns to a related question: at what point does the water remaining in the *kiyor* become *pasul* for use the next day? The *kiyor* was equipped with a mechanism — a wooden crank or winch — that allowed it to be submerged into an underground cistern at night, keeping the water from becoming *pasul* through *lina* (overnight exposure). *Rav Chiya bar Yosef* offers a graduated rule: *mei kiyor nifsalin le-matirin ke-matirin, le-eivarim ke-eivarim*. The water becomes *pasul* in parallel with the *avodah* for which it is used. For *matirin* — the daytime *avodot* such as the *korbanot* that exempt a person from obligation — the water becomes *pasul* when those *avodot* become *pasul*, namely at sunset. For *eivarim* — the overnight burning of fats and limbs on the *mizbeach* — the water becomes *pasul* at sunrise. Rav Chiya bar Yosef thus has two distinct disqualification times: sunset for day-service, and sunrise for night-service.

### Rav Chisda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: אַף לְמַתִּירִין אֵין נִפְסָלִין אֶלָּא בְּעַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, כַּאֵבָרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Even for** the performance of **permitting factors,** the water **is disqualified only at dawn, as** it is for the burning of the **limbs.**

*Rav Chisda* disagrees: *af le-matirin ein nifsalin ela be-amud ha-shachar ke-eivarim* — even for the daytime *avodot*, the *kiyor* water is not disqualified until dawn. There is thus only one disqualification time for Rav Chisda: sunrise, regardless of which *avodah* the water is intended to facilitate.

### Rabbi Yochanan Reconciled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כִּיּוֹר כֵּיוָן שֶׁשִּׁקְּעוֹ – שׁוּב אֵין מַעֲלֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Once** one **has sunk** the Basin into its pit at the end of the day's service, reconnecting the water in the Basin to flowing water to prevent it from being disqualified, **he may not raise it up again** the entire night.

*Rabbi Yochanan* is cited with a statement that initially appears to posit a third, stricter position: *kiyor keivan she-shik'u shuv ein ma'alehu* — once the *kiyor* has been submerged in the evening, one may not raise it again until the next morning. This seems to imply that if the *kiyor* were raised during the night, the water would be disqualified — suggesting a disqualification time earlier than sunrise, and perhaps even applying to night-time *avodot*.

However, the Gemara then cites a second statement of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted by *Rabbi Assi* in the name of *Ilfa*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאִילְפָא: כִּיּוֹר שֶׁלֹּא שִׁקְּעוֹ מִבָּעֶרֶב – מְקַדֵּשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ לַעֲבוֹדַת לַיְלָה, וּלְמָחָר אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** if the Basin was not sunk before sunset the water inside is **unfit for** the **night's service as well? But doesn't Rabbi Asi say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Ilfa:** With regard to **a Basin that** the priests **did not sink** into its pit **at night,** a priest **sanctifies** his hands and feet **from it for the service of** that **night, and the next day he does not sanctify** from it? Apparently, for purposes of the night's service, the water in the Basin is not disqualified at sunset.

This second version places disqualification at sunrise, apparently agreeing with Rav Chisda rather than introducing a stricter position.

To resolve the tension between Rabbi Yochanan's two statements, the Gemara considers whether his ruling about not raising the *kiyor* once it has been submerged refers specifically to *avodat yom* — the daytime service — rather than to *avodat laila*. If the *kiyor* was put away for the night and one seeks to raise it for a nighttime *avodah*, perhaps that is not permitted for the daytime service afterward, but it would still be usable for night-service. Under this reading, Rabbi Yochanan would coincide with *Rav Chiya bar Yosef*: different times for day and night *avodot*. But if so, what distinguishes Rabbi Yochanan from Rav Chiya bar Yosef?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֵינוֹ מַעֲלֵהוּ״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר – לַעֲבוֹדַת יוֹם; אֲבָל לַעֲבוֹדַת לַיְלָה חֲזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **What** does it mean **when it says: He may not raise it up** again? It means that he may not raise it to sanctify his hands and feet in preparation **for** the **day's service; but** the water is **fit** for sanctification **for** the **night's service.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ גְּזֵירַת שִׁיקּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The difference **between them** is that according to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef, if the water is raised at any point throughout the night, it is disqualified for sanctification prior to the day's services. According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, the water is not disqualified unless the Basin is raised at the break of dawn, but the Sages issued a rabbinic **decree of sinking** the Basin into its pit all night *ab initio*, so that it would not be raised at dawn accidentally.

### Gezeirat Shikua Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו לִתְרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן – לְמָחָר אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ, שֶׁכְּבָר קִידֵּשׁ מִתְּחִילַּת עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does Rabbi Yoḥanan really hold that the Basin must be sunk all night? **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** If a priest **sanctified his hands** and feet in preparation **for the removal of the ashes** before dawn, then **the next day he does not need to sanctify** them again, **as he already sanctified** them **at the beginning of** the day's **service?** Evidently, the Basin is raised before dawn.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרָבָא, דְּמוֹקֵים לְהָהִיא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – הָא כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Granted, according to Rava, who interprets that** statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** that sanctification of the hands and feet is not disqualified overnight (see 20a), **this** statement that the Basin must be sunk all night is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that sanctification of the hands and feet is disqualified at dawn. Accordingly, he holds that the water in the Basin is disqualified as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי דְּמוֹקֵים לַהּ כְּרַבִּי – קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי אַדְּרַבִּי: מַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דִּמְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּלָא מְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Abaye, who interprets** that statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the first statement **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi is **difficult** to reconcile **with** this other statement **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **What is different there that** one must **sink** the Basin all night, **and what is different here,** with regard to sanctification prior to removal of the ashes, **that** one does **not** need to **sink it?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּמְסַלֵּק לֵיהּ וַהֲדַר מְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: There is no contradiction. Prior to removal of the ashes, one **raises** the Basin **and** then **sinks it again** so that it will not remain raised at dawn and render the water disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִצְוַת שִׁיקּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The difference **between them** is that according to Rav Ḥisda, there is no **mitzva of sinking** the Basin into its pit throughout the night, whereas Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that there is a mitzva of sinking the Basin into its pit all night *ab initio*.

The Gemara resolves this: *ika beiniyahu gezeirat shikua* — the real disagreement between Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Chisda is not about the practical consequence of leaving the water out overnight, but about whether there was a positive mandate — *gezeirat shikua* — requiring the *kiyor* to be submerged each evening. Rabbi Yochanan holds that there was such a mandatory requirement, independently of whatever halachic consequence might follow from leaving it out. Rav Chisda holds that there was no such formal decree; if the *kiyor* was left out and the water remained valid until sunrise, no obligation was violated by not submerging it.

A further complication arises from Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that if one washed his hands in preparation for *trumat ha-deshen* — the removal of ash from the altar that takes place before dawn — he need not wash again later, *she-kvar kidesh mi-techilat avodah*, since his initial washing before the previous day's *avodah* carries forward. This fits well with the opinion of *Rava*, who holds the *halacha* like *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon* — that *ein layla ella be-kiddush*, nightfall alone does not invalidate a prior *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, and so a washing performed the previous afternoon remains in effect through the night. But it creates a difficulty for *Abaye*, who rules like *Rabbi* — that sunset *does* constitute an interruption, requiring a new *kiddush* at the start of the night. Under Abaye's view, how could one perform *trumat ha-deshen* on the basis of a *kiddush* from the prior afternoon? The Gemara acknowledges this difficulty — *kashya d'Rabbi ad-Rabbi* — and considers whether the cases can be distinguished based on whether the *kiyor* was submerged, removed, and submerged again. Ultimately, the Gemara concludes that the underlying debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Chisda reduces to the question of *mitzvat shiku'a* — whether the nightly submersion was a formal obligation or merely a practical precaution against forgetting — and is connected to the broader *machlokes* between Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi regarding whether nighttime serves as an interruption for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*.

## Kiyor Timing & Crank

### Crank Signal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: לֹא הָיוּ רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ וְלֹא שׁוֹמְעִין אֶת קוֹלוֹ, עַד שֶׁשּׁוֹמְעִים קוֹל שֶׁל עֵץ שֶׁעָשָׂה בֶּן קָטִין מוּכְנִי לַכִּיּוֹר, וְהֵן אוֹמְרִים: הִגִּיעַ [עֵת] קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם מִן הַכִּיּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Ḥisda from a mishna (*Tamid* 28b): The priests **would not see** the priest performing the removal of the ashes, **nor would they hear his voice.** Rather, they were unaware of his actions **until they would hear the sound of the wood** with **which ben Katin made a mechanism for** sinking **the Basin** into water during the night. **And** when they heard the sound of the mechanism, **they would say** to each other: **The time for sanctifying hands and feet from the Basin has come.**

The Gemara cites a *mishnah* from *Tamid* bearing on the timing of when the *kiyor* was raised and lowered. The *mishnah* states: *lo hayu ro'in oto ve-lo shom'im et kolo ad she-shom'im kol shel etz she-asa Ben Katin mochni la-kiyor* — they would not see it or hear it until they heard the sound of the wooden crank that *Ben Katin* had fashioned for the *kiyor*. Upon hearing that sound, the *kohanim* would say: the time has come for *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim min ha-kiyor*.

### Raising vs. Submerging

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו דִּמְסַלֵּק לֵיהּ? אַלְמָא מְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What, is it not that** they heard the mechanism **raising** the Basin? **Apparently,** the mechanism would **sink it** during the night, in contradiction to the opinion of Rav Ḥisda, who holds that there was no need to sink it overnight.

The Gemara asks: *mai lav de-maslek lei* — does this *mishnah* refer to the moment when they were raising the *kiyor* (taking it out of the water in the morning), or *de-mishkale* — when they were submerging it (lowering it into the water in the evening)? On the plain reading of the *mishnah*, it sounds like a morning event: people hear the crank sound and know it is time to come wash. But the Gemara attempts to read it as an evening event, in order to establish that the proper time for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* associated with the *kiyor* is at or around sunset.

The difficulty with reading it as submersion is: *mi mishtama kalei?* — if they are simply lowering the *kiyor*, why would the crank make a sound? Lowering would be aided by gravity, and could be accomplished without the noisy effort of the winch. The Gemara responds: *meshkale be-gilgala* — they would in fact lower it using the wheel, so it did produce sound. An alternative formulation clarifies: *demishkale be-chumratei ki heichi de-lishtama kalei* — they would lower it using the crank mechanism specifically so that the sound would be audible, and people would take note.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דִּמְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ. אִי דִּמְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ – מִי מִשְׁתְּמַע קָלֵיהּ?! מְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ בְּגִילְגְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** they heard the mechanism **sinking** the Basin, which had been raised all night. The Gemara asks: **If** the mechanism were **sinking it, would its sound be audible?** No mechanism is necessary to lower the Basin; it can simply be lowered with a chain. The Gemara responds: The priest would still **sink it by** using **a wheel,** which would make a sound.

### Double Announcement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: דִּמְשַׁקַּע לֵיהּ בְּחוּמַרְתֵּיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִישְׁתְּמַע קָלֵיהּ וְלִשְׁמְעִי וְלֵיתֵי. וְהָא הֲוָה גְּבִינִי כָּרוֹז! (בי) [אַבֵּי] תְּרֵי הֶיכֵּרֵי הֲווֹ עָבְדִי; דְּשָׁמַע מֵהַאי אָתֵי, וְשָׁמַע מֵהַאי אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **another version** of the answer: The priest would deliberately **sink it by its wheel so that its sound would be audible, and** the other priests **would hear and come** to begin the service. The Gemara asks: **But wasn't there Gevini** the **crier,** who would notify the priests of the beginning of the service? The Gemara responds: **They would make two conspicuous markers** for the beginning of the service: The sound of the wheel and the crier. Anyone **who heard from this** marker **would come, and** anyone who **heard from that** marker **would come.**

One might ask why such a sound signal was necessary at all, given that the *beis hamikdash* had *Gavini*, the official town crier, who would announce events and signal to the *kohanim* what time it was. The Gemara's answer is elegant: *bei terei hekeirei havu avdei* — they would make a double announcement. The crank sound and the human proclamation served as two independent signals so that no one would miss the notification — *de-shama me-hai atu, de-shama me-hai atu* — whoever heard one signal would come, and whoever heard the other would also come. The conclusion is that the *mishnah* in *Tamid* is understood as describing the evening submersion of the *kiyor*, and the crank sound together with the crier's announcement jointly marked the time of *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* for the night-service.
