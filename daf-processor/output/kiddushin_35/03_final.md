# Kiddushin 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Exemption from Mitzvot

### Rule & Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת מְנָלַן? דְּיָלֵיף מִמּוֹרָא, מָה מוֹרָא נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת – אַף כׇּל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that **women are obligated in positive mitzvot that are not time bound?** The Gemara answers **that** one **derives** this **from** the mitzva of **fearing** one's mother and father: **Just as women are obligated** in the mitzva of **fear** (Leviticus 19:3), **so too, women are obligated in every positive mitzva that is not time bound.**

The daf begins where we left off at the bottom of 34b, dealing with the source for how we know that women are exempt from *mitzvot asei shehazman grama* (time-bound positive commandments) and obligated in *mitzvot asei shelo hazman grama* (non-time-bound positive commandments). The difficulty with this entire sugya is immediately apparent: the derivation is convoluted, there are as many exceptions as there are examples that fall under the rule, and the central question — why we treat certain cases as the paradigm and others as the exception rather than the reverse — is precisely what the *Gemara* itself struggles to answer. And when the *Gemara* does answer, the answer is not exactly overwhelming in its persuasiveness.

### Rambam's Masora

This observation leads naturally to what the Rambam explicitly states. Against the straightforward reading of the *Gemara*, which seems to treat the rule of *mitzvot asei shehazman grama* as genuinely operative — that it will tell us about cases where we don't know the status, absent something to teach us an exception — the Rambam says something fundamentally different. He maintains that none of this is actually known through derivation from a rule; everything is known through *masorah*, tradition. We know which *mitzvot* women are obligated in and which they are exempt from not because a rule tells us so, but because that is what has been transmitted. The link back to *pesukim* is secondary.

This reflects a broader question that *rishonim* confront throughout the Talmud: did *Chazal* have a tradition about certain matters and then went back to find how it was rooted in the *pesukim*, or did they genuinely begin without knowing and then derive the answer? The more convoluted the link to *pesukim* is, and the less clear it is why a particular path of derivation was chosen over an equally plausible alternative, the more likely *rishonim* are to say that what we are seeing is the second stage: the tradition came first, and the *pesukim* were then found to support it.

### Tefillin & Reaiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מִתַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה! – מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה וּפְרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכֹל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **derive** the opposite **from Torah study:** Just as women are exempt from Torah study, so too they should be exempt from all positive mitzvot that are not time bound. The Gemara answers: One cannot derive an exemption for women from their exemption from Torah study, **because Torah study and procreation are two verses that come as one,** as in both cases women are exempt, despite the fact that these are not time-bound mitzvot. **And any two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent.

To recall where we stand: the cases in the *Gemara* where women are exempt — and which we derive from explicit *pesukim* — are *tefillin*, *re'iyah* (*olat re'iyah*, the burnt-offering brought on pilgrimage), and *sukkah*. Notably, *tefillin* serves as the paradigm for the rule of *mitzvat asei shehazman grama*, even though its derivation comes by way of its link to *talmud Torah*, and *talmud Torah* itself is not in the same category. The cases where women are obligated — derived explicitly — are *hakhel*, *matzah*, and *simchah*. The irony, of course, is that *tefillin* serves as the rule-setting paradigm despite the awkwardness of its derivation.

### Rule vs Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that the earlier question remains difficult: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent. **But according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one **do teach** a precedent, **what can be said?** According to this opinion it can be derived that women are obligated in positive, time-bound mitzvot from *matza* and assembly, and that they are exempt from positive mitzvot that are not time bound, from Torah study and the redemption of the firstborn son.

The fundamental question is: which column is the rule and which is the exception? The *Gemara* argued that cases explicitly stated twice or more are the exception — the principle of *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad* — since if something were the rule, the *Torah* would only need to say it once. If it is stated more than once, the *Torah* is pointing out exceptions, not articulating a rule.

The *Gemara* initially argued that in the column of exemption (*patur*), the cases that are explicitly stated are superfluous, and therefore they constitute the *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad* — the exceptions — establishing the rule to be the other side. The *Gemara* explained why each of the obligation (*chiyuv*) cases needed to be explicitly stated: by *sukkah*, one might have thought *teishvu ke'ein taduru* (you shall dwell as you normally dwell) is a reason to obligate women; by one case, a *gezeirah shavah* to *hakhel* would have led to obligation; by *tefillin*, there was a different reason one might have thought obligation applies. Since each of those cases needed to be said explicitly, they are not *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad*; they are necessary specifications. By contrast, *hakhel* in the obligation column is superfluous — one could have known it from *matzah* — so the two obligation cases together are the *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad*, marking them as exceptions and the exemption side as the rule.

The corresponding question on the *shelo hazman grama* side: how do we know that women are obligated in non-time-bound *mitzvot*? The answer given is *mora* — from the verse concerning *yirat av va'em*, where the plural *tira'u* explicitly includes both men and women. And the challenge: why not derive from *talmud Torah* instead, which is also *shelo hazman grama* yet women are exempt? The answer is that *talmud Torah* and *piryah ve-rivyah* together constitute *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad* on the exemption side of *shelo hazman grama*, making them the exceptions, while *mora* is the rule.

### Peru Verevú Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא, דְּאָמַר: עַל שְׁנֵיהֶם הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְבָרֶךְ אֹתָם אֱלֹהִים פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ״ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה וּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכֹל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, who says** that **with regard to both of them,** men and women, the verse **states: "And God blessed them, and God said to them: Be fruitful and multiply, replenish the earth and conquer it"** (Genesis 1:28), **what can be said?** According to his opinion, women are exempt from only one positive mitzva that is not time bound, Torah study; why not derive other mitzvot from this case? The Gemara answers: The reason this is not a difficulty is **because Torah study and the redemption of the firstborn son,** from which women are also exempt, **are two verses that come as one, and any two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent.

At this point the *Gemara* introduces Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who holds that the verse *va-yivarech osam Elohim va-yomer lahem Elohim piru u-revu* ("and God blessed them and said to them: be fruitful and multiply") obligates women equally in *piryah ve-rivyah*. If so, *piryah ve-rivyah* moves to the obligation column, and there is now only one case — *talmud Torah* — on the exemption side of *shelo hazman grama*. One case cannot be *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad*; it could simply be the rule. Let *talmud Torah* be the rule, and let women be generally exempt from *shelo hazman grama*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא נָמֵי, נִיהְווֹ פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה וּמוֹרָא שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד וְאֵין מְלַמְּדִין! צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא מוֹרָא, וְלָא כְּתַב פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: ״וְכִבְשֻׁהָ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – אִישׁ דְּדַרְכּוֹ לְכַבֵּשׁ – אִין, אִשָּׁה דְּאֵין דַּרְכָּהּ לְכַבֵּשׁ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka as well, let procreation,** which he maintains applies to women, **and fear** of one's mother and father **be** considered **two verses that come as one and** they should **not teach** a precedent. The Gemara answers: Both cases **are necessary. As, if the Merciful One had written** only that women are obligated in **fear** of their parents, **and had not written** that they are obligated in **procreation, I would say** that as **the Merciful One states:** "Be fruitful and multiply, replenish the earth **and conquer it"** (Genesis 1:28), this leads to the conclusion that women are exempt from procreation, by the following reasoning: **As** it is **the manner of a man** to go to war and **to conquer, yes,** he is obligated in procreation, but **as it is not the manner of a woman to conquer,** she is **not** obligated in procreation.

The *Gemara* answers that even according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, you now have *piryah ve-rivyah* and *Pidyon HaBen* in the obligation column. *Pidyon HaBen* — the redemption of the firstborn son — is an obligation that, once the child reaches thirty days, continues without cessation, has no time-bound interruption, and is therefore *shelo hazman grama*. Women are nonetheless exempt. And we learn this from a *pasuk*. So again, two cases of *shelo hazman grama* where women are obligated — *piryah ve-rivyah* (according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka) and *mora* — and two cases where they are exempt — *talmud Torah* and *Pidyon HaBen*. The same structure holds. The cases that needed to be explicitly stated because they are necessary — and not superfluous — are the rule. The cases that were superfluous are the exception.

### Necessity Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה וְלֹא כְּתַב מוֹרָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אִישׁ דְּסִיפֵּק בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת – אִין, אִשָּׁה דְּאֵין סִיפֵּק בְּיָדָהּ לַעֲשׂוֹת – לָא, וְכֵיוָן דְּאֵין סִיפֵּק בְּיָדָהּ לַעֲשׂוֹת לֹא תִּתְחַיֵּיב כְּלָל, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Merciful One **had written** only that women are obligated in the mitzva of **procreation, and had not written** that they are obligated to **fear** their parents, **I would say:** With regard to **a man, as** it is **in his power to perform** this mitzva, **yes,** he is obligated to fear his mother and father, but with regard to **a woman, as** it is **not in her power to perform** this mitzva when she is married, since her obligations to her husband may prevent her from doing so, she is **not** obligated. **And as** it is **not in her power to perform** this mitzva when she is married, perhaps women **should not be obligated at all** and there should be no difference between a married and an unmarried woman. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the Torah to state that women are obligated in both procreation and the fear of parents, and these are not considered two verses that come as one.

But what makes a case necessary versus superfluous? The *Gemara* works this out carefully for the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka. The *Torah* had to explicitly state that *piryah ve-rivyah* applies to women because without that, the phrase *ve-chivshuha* — "and you shall subdue it" — in the context of the commandment would have suggested this was addressed only to men, since conquest and dominion are characteristically male activities (*ish darcho lichbosh ve-ein ishah darkah lichbosh*). Without an explicit statement, one would have restricted *piryah ve-rivyah* to men. Conversely, the *Torah* had to explicitly state that women are obligated in *yiras av va-em* because without that, one might have said that since a woman does not always have the latitude to fulfill the obligation — her primary responsibilities at home to husband and children may prevent her — she might be entirely exempt. Each case was therefore necessary, and the necessary case is the rule.

### Unresolved Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רָבָא: פַּפּוּנָאֵי יָדְעִי לַהּ לְטַעְמָא דְּהָא מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that the earlier question remains difficult: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent. **But according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one **do teach** a precedent, **what can be said?** According to this opinion it can be derived that women are obligated in positive, time-bound mitzvot from *matza* and assembly, and that they are exempt from positive mitzvot that are not time bound, from Torah study and the redemption of the firstborn son. **Rava said:** The Sages **of Pafunya know the reason for this matter.**

After all of this careful analysis, the *Gemara* returns to the earlier unresolved difficulty: all of the above works only according to those who maintain *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — that two cases explicitly stated make something an exception rather than a rule. But there is an opinion that *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad melamdin* — even with two cases explicitly stated, it can still be the rule. If so, the entire argument collapses. What makes the obligation cases exceptions rather than the rule? The same problem applies equally on the *zman grama* side and the *lo zman grama* side. How do we know which column is the rule and which is the exception?

### Tefillin Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַנּוּ – רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה לְךָ לְאוֹת עַל יָדְךָ וּלְזִכָּרוֹן בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ לְמַעַן תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת ה׳ בְּפִיךָ״ – הוּקְּשָׁה כָּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לִתְפִילִּין, מָה תְּפִילִּין מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, וְנָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת – אַף כׇּל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ – נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת. וּמִדְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ – נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת, מִכְּלָל דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ – נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And who is** the scholar called by the nickname: The Sages of Pafunya? It is **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov,** who said as follows: **The verse states** with regard to phylacteries: **"And it shall be a sign for you on your arm and for a memorial between your eyes, that the Torah of the Lord may be in your mouth"** (Exodus 13:9). In this manner **the entire Torah is juxtaposed to phylacteries: Just as** donning **phylacteries is a positive, time-bound mitzva and women are exempt** from it, **so too are women exempt** from **every positive, time-bound mitzva** in the Torah. **And from the fact** that **women are exempt from** every **positive, time-bound mitzva,** one can learn **by inference** that **women are obligated in** every **positive mitzva that is not time bound.**

Rava answers this in the name of the people of Papuna — a place whose location is noted by Rashi — whose tradition was transmitted by Rav Achai bar Yaakov. The answer, he says, is found in the verse: *vehayah lecha le'ot al yadcha ulezikaron bein einecha lema'an tihye Torat Hashem befecha* — "and it shall be for you a sign on your arm and a reminder between your eyes, so that the *Torah* of God shall be in your mouth." The conclusion of the verse — *lema'an tihye Torat Hashem befecha* — equates the entire *Torah* to *tefillin*: *hukshah kol ha-Torah kulah le-tefillin*. The entire *Torah* is measured against *tefillin* as its paradigm. Since *tefillin* is a *mitzvat asei shehazman grama* and women are exempt, the *pasuk* is identifying *tefillin* as the paradigm for all of *Torah*. And since the *Torah* chose a time-bound *mitzvah* as the model with respect to women's exemption, the implication is clear: it is specifically the time-bound characteristic that generates the exemption. Anything that is not time-bound, women would be obligated. The converse follows directly: *mi-klal de-mitzvat asei shehazman grama nashim pturot, michlal de-mitzvat asei shelo hazman grama nashim chayavot*.

This approach bypasses the entire complicated argument about which column has the right number of cases to be the exception. It is not about counting how many cases are in each column or whether they are superfluous. The *Torah* itself labels *tefillin* as the template for all of *Torah*, and that is where we get the rule — end of story.

### Tefillin Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּפִילִּין מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּפִילִּין מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר תְּפִילִּין מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְסָבַר לַהּ שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכֹל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that the mitzva of donning **phylacteries** is **a positive, time-bound mitzva. But according to the one who says** that donning **phylacteries** is **a positive mitzva that is not time bound,** as it is applicable the entire year, day and night, **what can be said?** The Gemara answers: **Who did you hear who said** that donning **phylacteries** is **a positive mitzva that is not time bound?** It is **Rabbi Meir, and he holds** that *matza* and assembly are **verses that come as one, and** he further maintains that **any two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: it works well according to those who hold that *tefillin* is indeed a *mitzvat asei shehazman grama* — that one is exempt on *Shabbat* and at night. But what about the opinion that *tefillin* is *mitzvat asei shelo hazman grama* — that it applies at all times, including nights and *Shabbat*? If *tefillin* is not time-bound, it cannot serve as the paradigm for the category of time-bound exemption. The *Gemara* identifies this position as that of Rabbi Meir.

The answer is that Rabbi Meir also holds *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — that two explicitly stated cases constitute an exception rather than a rule. Accordingly, Rabbi Meir does not need *tefillin* as the paradigm; he relies on the structure of multiple explicitly stated cases to identify which side is the exception. The two approaches are thus complementary alternatives: either *tefillin* is the paradigm (which works for those who say *tefillin* is time-bound), or the logic of *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad* identifies the exceptions (which is the approach of Rabbi Meir, who holds *tefillin* is not time-bound).

### Three Cases Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד מְלַמְּדִין, וּתְפִילִּין מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵאי מַצָּה, שִׂמְחָה, וְהַקְהֵל שְׁלֹשָׁה כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **two verses that come as one do teach** a precedent, **and** who also says that donning **phylacteries is a positive mitzva that is not time bound, what can be said?** The Gemara answers: It is not derived from here that women are obligated in positive, time-bound mitzvot **because** the verses that mention ***matza*, rejoicing, and assembly are three verses that come as one, and** everyone agrees **three verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent.

The *Gemara* then addresses Rabbi Yehuda, who presents the most demanding case: he holds both that *shnei ketuvim habaim ke'echad melamdin* (two cases can still be a rule) and that *tefillin* is *shelo hazman grama* (not time-bound). With neither path available, how does he establish which column is the exception?

The answer is *shlosheh ketuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — three explicitly stated cases do constitute an exception, even for Rabbi Yehuda. And here the *Gemara* reinstates *simchah* in the obligation column. Earlier, *simchah* had been somewhat set aside because the Talmud suggested the obligation of *simchah* on *Yom Tov* for a woman is fulfilled through her husband (*ba'alah mesamchah*). But according to Rabbi Yehuda, *simchah* is restored, giving three cases — *matzah*, *simchah*, and *hakhel* — on the obligation side, which is enough to label it as the exception.

This yields essentially two and a half distinct paths to the same conclusion: *tefillin* as the paradigm (for those who hold it is time-bound); or, if *tefillin* is not the paradigm, then the sheer number of explicitly stated exceptions — three against the rule on both sides — labels those explicit cases as exceptions and establishes the opposite column as the rule. The very fact that the *Torah* needed to state so many cases going against what would otherwise be the rule is itself the evidence that the rule runs the other way.

## Negative Mitzvot

### All Lav Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וְכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה כִּי יַעֲשׂוּ מִכׇּל חַטֹּאת הָאָדָם״ – הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ לְכׇל עוֹנָשִׁים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: **And** with regard to **all prohibitions,** whether or not they are time bound, both men and women are obligated to observe them. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, and likewise the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The verse states** with regard to a guilt-offering: **"When a man or woman shall commit any sin that a person commits"** (Numbers 5:6). **The verse equates a woman to a man with regard to all punishments in the Torah,** as a woman is also required to bring an offering for atonement.

Having established the framework for positive commandments, the *Gemara* turns to the *mishnah*'s next topic: *mitzvot lo ta'aseh*, negative commandments. The rule is that women are obligated in all negative commandments — *nashim chayavot* — with only a small number of exceptions, to be discussed in turn.

### Ish ve-Isha

Where does this rule come from? Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, and likewise the school of Rabbi Yishmael, derive it from the verse: *ish o ishah ki ya'asu mikol chatat ha'adam* — "a man or a woman who commits any of the sins of a person" (Bamidbar 5:6). The phrase *mikol chatat ha'adam* is generic and sweeping. The verse frames transgression as something that applies equally to *ish o ishah*. From this the *Gemara* derives: *hishvah ha-katuv ishah le-ish le-chol onashin sheba-Torah* — Scripture equates a woman to a man for all punishments in the *Torah*. Any transgression that makes one liable to punishment applies equally to men and women.

### Three Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר תָּנָא: אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר תָּשִׂים לִפְנֵיהֶם״ – הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ לְכׇל דִּינִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה תָּנָא: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהֵמִית אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה״ – הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ לְכׇל מִיתוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Eliezer taught** as follows. **The verse states:** "Now these are the ordinances **which you shall set before them"** (Exodus 21:1), stating "them" in the plural. This **verse equates a woman to a man** with regard **to all judgments in the Torah,** i.e., monetary cases and damages. **The school of Ḥizkiyya taught: The verse states,** with regard to the ransom one pays if his animal killed a person: **"And killed a man or woman"** (Exodus 21:29). Here too, **the verse equates a woman to a man,** with regard **to all deaths in the Torah,** i.e., the same *halakha* applies to an animal that kills either a man or a woman.

The *Gemara* then presents a broader package of three separate *limudim* that appear across *Shas*, each establishing women's equal legal standing in a different domain. First, from *ish o ishah ki ya'asu mikol chatat ha'adam*: equal obligation in transgressions and their associated punishments (*chatat ha'adam*). Second, from the school of Rabbi Eliezer, who derives from the verse *asher tasim lifneihem* — "which you shall place before them," the phrase introducing *Mishpatim* — that *hishvah ha-katuv ishah le-ish le-chol dinim sheba-Torah*: women are treated equally in all civil law across the *Torah*, all matters of financial litigation, damages, debts, and property. Third, from the school of Chizkiyah, who derives from the verse in *Mishpatim* — *ve-hemit ish o ishah* — concerning an ox that gores and kills a man or a woman and the owner must pay *kofer* (atonement payment) — that *hishvah ha-katuv ishah le-ish le-chol mitot sheba-Torah*: when a woman is the victim of a capital crime or a death caused by a negligent animal, the severity of the offense and the consequence are equal to those when the victim is a man.

### Why All Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא, מִשּׁוּם כַּפָּרָה חָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלַהּ, אֲבָל דִּינִין, אֵימָא: אִישׁ, דְּבַר מַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן – אִין, אִשָּׁה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to state all three of these verses. **As, if** the Torah **had taught us** only **this first** case, with regard to a woman's obligation to sacrifice guilt-offerings, I would say that **the Merciful One has pity on her due to atonement,** i.e., God gave her the possibility to atone for her sin through an offering. **But** with regard to monetary **judgments, I** would **say** that with regard to **a man, who** generally **conducts** business **negotiations, yes,** these *halakhot* apply to him, but in the case of **a woman,** who generally does not conduct business negotiations, **no,** the *halakhot* of monetary judgments do not apply to her.

The *Gemara* establishes that all three cases were necessary, as any two without the third would have left a gap. Had only the transgression case been stated — women are punished for their violations — one might have concluded that this is solely to provide *kaparah* (atonement) for women who transgress, not that they have equal standing in civil or capital contexts. Had only the civil law case been stated, one might have said this is necessary to allow women to function in civil society — without it, someone could steal from a woman with impunity, or she could steal and face no consequence, rendering her a pariah — but one could not have inferred equal treatment in the context of capital crimes. Had only the *kofer* case been stated, one might have attributed this simply to the weight of *ibud neshamah* — the loss of life is so severe that the *Torah* showed *rachamanut* and required the *kofer* payment regardless — but could not have inferred equal treatment in lesser civil matters.

### Status Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְועִינַן הָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּחַיּוּתַהּ הִיא, אֲבָל כּוֹפֶר אֵימָא: אִישׁ, דְּבַר מִצְווֹת – אִין, אִשָּׁה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly **if** the Torah **had taught us** only **this** case of monetary judgments, I would say that these judgments apply to a woman, **because** there are circumstances where engaging in business **is her livelihood. But** with regard to the **ransom** that is paid when one's animal killed someone, **I** would **say:** If the animal killed **a man, who is commanded in** all **mitzvot, yes,** its owner should have to pay the ransom, but if the animal killed **a woman,** who is obligated in only some mitzvot, **no,** he is exempt from the ransom.

The third case is particularly striking and worth pausing over. The *Gemara* suggests the *hava amina* — the rejected initial assumption — that one might have said: an ox must pay *kofer* when it kills a man because a man is *bar mitzvot* — obligated in commandments — but a woman, who has fewer *mitzvah* obligations, might not generate the same *kofer* liability. Tosafot completes this by adding *de-lav bat mitzvot*.

This is a deeply uncomfortable *Gemara*. The notion that a woman's life might be worth less in terms of the *kofer* obligation because she is obligated in fewer *mitzvot* is precisely the kind of reading that, misunderstood, would suggest the *Torah* regards women as having reduced fundamental status. As Rav Moshe Feinstein addressed in a responsum written decades ago in response to concerns raised during the early period of the feminist movement — that women in some communities felt the *Torah* viewed them as second-class — he rejected categorically any suggestion that exemption from certain *mitzvot* reflects diminished *kedushah* or status. The *hava amina* here, rejected by the *Gemara*, is precisely the kind of reasoning that the *Gemara* recognizes as a possible misreading and then refutes.

### Equal Citizens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא אִיבּוּד נְשָׁמָה חָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלַהּ, אֲבָל הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי – אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely: **If** the Torah had **taught us** that men and women are equated only in **this** case of the ransom, one might say that **because there is the loss of life the Merciful One has pity on her** and therefore the owner of the animal is always obligated to pay the ransom. **But** with regard to **those two** other categories, **I** might **say no,** a woman is not equated to a man. Therefore it was **necessary** to mention them all.

The practical irony is real: women bear tremendous *mitzvah* responsibility in sustaining the Jewish household — *kashrut*, *Shabbat*, raising children. And yet the formal structure of *mitzvah* obligation as enumerated tends to be weighted toward men, which can create a misleading impression of standing. The *Gemara*'s conclusion, however, is unequivocal.

### Three Exceptions

With the general rule of women's equal obligation in negative commandments established, the *mishnah* and *Gemara* turn to the exceptions: *tumat kohanim*, *bal takif*, and *bal tashchit* of the beard. Women are exempt from all three.

## Tumat Kohanim

### Benei Aharon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִ״בַּל תַּקִּיף״ וּ״בַל תַּשְׁחִית״ כּוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״בַּל תִּטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים״, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֱמֹר אֶל הַכֹּהֲנִים בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that women are obligated in all prohibitions **except for** the prohibitions of: **Do not round** the corners of one's head, **and: Do not destroy** the corners of your beard, and: Do not contract ritual impurity from a corpse. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** a woman of priestly lineage is not obligated in the mitzva of: **Do not contract ritual impurity from a corpse, as it is written: "Speak to the priests, the sons of Aaron,** and say to them: None shall become impure for the dead among his people" (Leviticus 21:1). This verse teaches that the prohibition applies to **the sons of Aaron, but not the daughters of Aaron.**

The exemption of women from *tumat kohanim* — the prohibition against a *kohen* becoming *tamei le-meitim* (ritually impure through contact with the dead) — is the least difficult of the three exceptions. The *Gemara* states simply: the *Torah* says *emor el ha-kohanim bnei Aharon* — "speak to the *kohanim*, the sons of Aharon." *Bnei Aharon* — the sons of Aharon, not the daughters. The daughters of a *kohen* are called *bat kohen*, and there is even some discussion in *Gemarot* elsewhere about whether the term *kohenet* applies and whether a *bat kohen* receives *matnot kehunah* such as *zeroa, lechayayim ve-keivah*. But fundamentally, only male offspring carry the status of *kohen*. The prohibition against *tumat meitim* therefore applies only to male *kohanim*.

## Bal Takif & Tashchit

### Zekanecha Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״בַּל תַּקִּיף״ וּ״בַל תַּשְׁחִית״ מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַקִּפוּ פְּאַת רֹאשְׁכֶם וְלֹא תַשְׁחִית אֵת פְּאַת זְקָנֶךָ״ – כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּהַשְׁחָתָה יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּהַקָּפָה. וְהָנֵי נְשֵׁי, הוֹאִיל וְלָא אִיתַנְהוּ בְּהַשְׁחָתָה, לֵיתַנְהוּ בְּהַקָּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this is **as it is written: "You shall not round the corners of your head and you shall not destroy the corners of your beard"** (Leviticus 19:27). The juxtaposition of the two prohibitions teaches that **anyone who is** included **in** the prohibition against **destroying** the beard **is** included **in** the prohibition against **rounding** the head. **And since these women are not** included **in** the prohibition against **destroying, they are** also **not** included **in** the prohibition against **rounding** the head.

The exemptions of women from *bal takif* (cutting the sidelocks of the head) and *bal tashchit* (destroying the corners of the beard) require more analysis. The source verse is: *lo takifu pe'at roshchem ve-lo tashchit et pe'at zekanecha*. The *Gemara* establishes the rule: *kol she-yeshno be-hashchatah yeshno be-hakafah* — whoever is subject to the prohibition of destroying the beard is also subject to the prohibition of cutting the sidelocks. Since women are not subject to *hashchatah* (the beard prohibition), they are not subject to *hakafah* (the sidelocks prohibition) either.

### Sevel Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּלָא אִיתַנְהוּ בְּהַשְׁחָתָה? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא: דְּהָא לָא אִית לְהוּ זָקָן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תַקִּפוּ פְּאַת רֹאשְׁכֶם וְלֹא תַשְׁחִית אֵת פְּאַת זְקָנֶךָ״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** women **are not** obligated **in** the prohibition against **destroying** the corners of one's beard? The Gemara answers: **If you wish, propose** a logical **reason, as** ordinarily women **do not have a beard. And if you wish, cite a verse** that teaches this point, **as the verse states: "You shall not round the corners of your head [*roshekhem*] and you shall not destroy the corners of your beard [*zekanekha*]"** (Leviticus 19:27).

But this raises the immediate question: how do we know women are not subject to *hashchatah* in the first place? Two answers are offered. The first is *sevara* — simple logic. The *Torah* is evidently addressing men, since the beard is a characteristic of men. Even if there are rare exceptions, the *Torah*'s presumed audience is those who naturally have beards. The second is textual: the verse says *pe'at zekanecha* — in the singular — rather than *pe'at zekhanchem* in the plural. The shift from the plural *roshchem* to the singular *zekanecha* is deliberate. *Zekanecha* — your beard, singular — means your beard and not your wife's beard (*ve-lo zikan ishtecha*). Since the verse itself limits the beard prohibition to a single male addressee, the entire prohibition is addressed to men alone.

### Braita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּשַׁנִּי קְרָא בְּדִיבּוּרֵיהּ, דְּאִם כֵּן נִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״פְּאַת זְקַנְכֶם״. מַאי ״זְקָנֶךָ״ – זְקָנֶךָ, וְלֹא זְקַן אִשְׁתְּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **From** the fact **that the verse changed its language,** as the term "your head [*roshekhem*]" is in the plural while "your beard [*zekanekha*]" is in the singular, it can be inferred that **if so,** if the prohibition against destroying one's beard applied to everyone, **let the Merciful One write:** And you shall not destroy the **corners of your beards [*zekanekhem*],** in the plural, so that the end of the verse parallels the beginning. **What** is indicated by the fact that the verse states: "And you shall not destroy the corners of **your beard [*zekanekha*],"** in the singular? This serves to teach: **Your beard** is included, **but not your wife's beard.**

The *Gemara* then introduces a challenge from a *braita*: *zikan ha-ishah ve-ha-saris she-he'elu sei'ar harei hen ke-zakan le-chol divreihem* — a beard grown by a woman or a eunuch (who would not normally grow facial hair) is treated as a beard for all their purposes. If so, this seems to contradict our *mishnah*, since the *braita* implies that the woman's beard carries full halachic status — presumably including the prohibition against shaving it.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: זְקַן אִשָּׁה, וְהַסָּרִיס שֶׁהֶעֱלוּ שֵׂעָר – הֲרֵי הֵן כְּזָקָן לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם. מַאי לָאו לְהַשְׁחָתָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is a woman **not** included in this prohibition? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Nega'im* 4:8): **The beard of a woman and** that of **a eunuch, if they grow** facial **hair, are considered like a beard for all matters. What, is it not** the case that this statement is referring **to** the prohibition against **destroying?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְהַשְׁחָתָה לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דְּיָלֵיף ״פְּאַת״ ״פְּאַת״ מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, מָה לְהַלָּן נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת – אַף כָּאן נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: You cannot say** that the *baraita* is referring **to destroying, as it is derived** that a woman is exempt through the verbal analogy of **"the corners** of your beard" (Leviticus 19:27) here and **"the corners** of their beard" (Leviticus 21:5) **from the sons of Aaron: Just as there,** in the case of priests, **women are** certainly **exempt** from the mitzva, as the verse is referring to the male descendants of Aaron who perform the Temple service and not to women, **so too here,** with regard to the prohibition against destroying one's beard, which is stated to all Jews, **women are exempt.** At this stage the Gemara assumes that the exclusion of women denoted by the verse: "Speak to the priests, the sons of Aaron" (Leviticus 21:1), which excludes women, is applied to all the mitzvot stated in that chapter, including destroying the corners of one's beard.

Abaye resolves this firmly: the *braita* cannot be referring to the prohibition of *hashchatah*. That women are exempt from *hashchatah* — from the beard-shaving prohibition — is beyond question. Abaye invokes an additional basis beyond the *sevara* and *zekanecha* analysis: a *gezeirah shavah* linking *pe'at* in the *Yisrael* verse to *pe'at* in the *kohanim* verse. By the *kohanim*, the *Torah* says *bnei Aharon* — only the sons, not the daughters — and the verse concerning the beard of the *kohanim* uses the same word *pe'at* as the verse concerning *Yisrael*. By the *gezeirah shavah*, what applies to *kohanim* — i.e., that the prohibition is limited to men — applies equally to *Yisrael*. Since the *kohanim* are unambiguously only men, and the beard prohibition for *Yisrael* is linked to it by *gezeirah shavah*, the *Yisrael* prohibition is likewise limited to men.

### Kal Vachomer Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּכִי כְּתִיב ״בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – אַכּוּלֵּיהּ עִנְיָנָא כְּתִיב, נִשְׁתּוֹק קְרָא מִינֵּיהּ וְתֵיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶם הַכָּתוּב מִצְווֹת יְתֵירוֹת – ״בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אַהֲרֹן, יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if we maintain that when** the Merciful One **writes: "The sons of Aaron"** (Leviticus 21:1), it **is written with regard to the entire manner** of that chapter, including the prohibition against destroying one's beard, **let the verse,** i.e., the Torah, **be silent** and not state **about** this prohibition concerning all Jews. **And** this *halakha* **could be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, as **I** could **say** the following: **And if** with regard to **priests, for whom the verse includes additional mitzvot,** this prohibition applies only to **the sons of Aaron and not the daughters of Aaron,** is it **not all the more so** the case with regard to **Israelites,** who have fewer mitzvot, that only men should be obligated and not women?

The *Gemara* interrupts Abaye's reasoning with a sharp question: if we accept that *bnei Aharon* applies throughout the entire section addressing *kohanim* — covering *tumah*, *korcha*, and *pe'at zekanam lo yigaleichu* — then why do we need the *gezeirah shavah* at all? We could simply derive by *kal va-chomer*: if *kohanim*, who are subject to greater *mitzvah* obligations and more stringent demands than an ordinary *Yisrael*, are limited to men (*bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon*) for the beard prohibition — then for *Yisrael*, who are subject to fewer obligations, certainly it should be limited to men as well.

### Gezerah Shavah Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָאו גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, the verbal analogy is necessary. **Were it not for** the **verbal analogy, I would say** that the *halakhot* of ritual impurity **concluded** discussion of **that matter.** In other words, the exclusion of women denoted by the phrase "the sons of Aaron" applies only to the *halakhot* of impurity, which appear immediately after that phrase. Conversely, the other *halakhot* mentioned in this chapter, including the prohibition against destroying the beard, apply to women as well.

Abaye's response is that without the *gezeirah shavah*, he would have said *hifsik ha-inyan* — the context was interrupted. That is, *bnei Aharon* at the beginning of the section applies only to the *tumah* prohibition immediately following; by the time the *Torah* reaches the beard prohibition later in the section, the limiting phrase no longer applies, and the beard prohibition would apply equally to men and women. If so, the *kal va-chomer* would also fail, since there would be nothing on the *kohanim* side limiting it to men with respect to *pe'at ha-zakan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי נֵימָא הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן! וְאִי מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״, יָכוֹל גִּילְּחוֹ בְּמִסְפָּרַיִים יִהְיֶה חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תַשְׁחִית״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **now too, let us say** that the *halakhot* of ritual impurity **concluded** discussion of **that matter,** and the daughters of Aaron are also prohibited to destroy their beards. **And if** you maintain that the reason the prohibition stated with regard to priests does not apply to women is **due to** the **verbal analogy** employing the term "the corners of," which serves to connect the *halakha* stated with regard to priests with the *halakha* stated with regard to all Jews, that verbal analogy **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to priests: **"Neither shall they shave off** the corners of their beard" (Leviticus 21:5). One **might** have thought that a priest **would be liable** even if **he shaved his** beard **with scissors.** Therefore **the verse states,** in a command issued to all Jews: **"And you shall not destroy** the corners of your beard" (Leviticus 19:27). This teaches that one is liable only for destroying the beard to the root, which is not achieved with scissors.

This is where the *gezeirah shavah* becomes essential. The very existence of the *gezeirah shavah* — the shared word *pe'at* linking the *Yisrael* and *kohanim* verses — signals that there is something to learn from one to the other. If the *kohanim* verse were also men and women (because *bnei Aharon* didn't extend that far), there would be nothing to learn. The fact that the *Torah* invites a *gezeirah shavah* forces us to conclude that there is a meaningful difference between the two cases, and the only meaningful difference is the restriction to men. Therefore, the *gezeirah shavah* itself tells us that *bnei Aharon* does extend through the entire section, including the beard prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לִקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? גִּילּוּחַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ הַשְׁחָתָה, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה תַּעַר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **if he extracted** his hairs **with tweezers,** which uproot hairs, **or** small **planes [*uvirhitni*], he should** likewise **be liable** for destroying his hair. **The verse** therefore **states: "Neither shall they shave off** the corners of their beard," to teach that shaving alone is prohibited and these actions are not considered shaving. **How** can both **these** requirements for the prohibition be met? The verse is referring to a type of **shaving that involves destruction. You must say** this **is** shaving with **a razor.** According to this *baraita*, the verbal analogy is necessary to define the action included in the prohibition against destroying, not to teach who is included in the prohibition.

The *Gemara* then asks: even granting that *hifsik ha-inyan* is a real concern, could the *gezeirah shavah* not be teaching something else entirely — not the restriction to men — while the beard prohibition by *kohanim* would still apply to women? And it answers: yes, because the *gezeirah shavah* is indeed needed for something else as well.

### Tashchit Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, נִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״אֶת שֶׁבִּזְקָנֶךָ״, מַאי ״פְּאַת זְקָנֶךָ״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If** it is **so** that the verbal analogy teaches only which action is included in the prohibition against destroying, **let the verse write: That which is of your beard. What** is added by the expression **"the corners of your beard"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** both the definition of the prohibition against shaving and the exemption of women.

The *gezeirah shavah* between *pe'at zekanam lo yigaleichu* (by *kohanim*) and *lo tashchit et pe'at zekanecha* (by *Yisrael*) is also needed to clarify the definition of what type of shaving is prohibited. *Tashchit* — destruction — implies getting to the root, cutting destructively at the surface; but it does not specify the instrument. *Yigaleichu* — shaving, cutting with a blade — implies a blade instrument but does not specify that it must destroy at the root. Each verse alone is incomplete: *tashchit* alone might include plucking with tweezers (*malket u-rahitani*), since that destroys the hair at the root. *Yigaleichu* alone might include scissors (*misparayim*), since scissors cut with a blade-like action.

The *Gemara* resolves this through the combination: *ha-keitzad, giluach she-yesh bo hashchatah* — what is the prohibited form? A shaving that both involves a blade and destroys at the root. *Havei omer zeh ta'ar* — that is a razor blade (*ta'ar*). Scissors do not destroy at the root; tweezers do not involve a blade-style shaving. Only a razor blade satisfies both conditions simultaneously. This is the halachic basis for the opinion that an electric shaver, which operates by a scissors-type action rather than a blade-against-skin action, might not violate *bal tashchit*.

So the *gezeirah shavah* serves a double purpose: it limits the prohibition to men (via the *bnei Aharon* extension), and it defines the nature of the prohibited act as razor-blade shaving only.

### Braita Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: זְקַן הָאִשָּׁה וְהַסָּרִיס שֶׁהֶעֱלוּ שֵׂעָר הֲרֵי הֵן כְּזָקָן לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: לְטוּמְאַת נְגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its question. **But that which is taught** in the *baraita*: **The beard of a woman and** that of **a eunuch, if they grew** facial **hair, are considered like a beard for all matters, with regard to what *halakha*** is this stated? **Mar Zutra says:** It is stated **with regard to ritual impurity from leprosy.** A leprous sore in the beard of a woman or a eunuch is treated like an affliction of the beard, not like an affliction on the skin. Different *halakhot* apply to leprous sores that develop on various parts of the body.

Returning now to the *braita* that stated a woman's beard is treated as a beard for all purposes: if it does not refer to *hashchatah*, what *halacha* does it convey? Mar Zutra initially suggests it refers to *tumat nega'im* — the impurity of *tzara'at* on the beard. The *Gemara* responds that *tumat nega'im* is explicit in a *pasuk*: *ve-ish o ishah ki yihyeh vo nega be-rosh o ve-zakan* — a man or a woman who has a spot on the head or beard. No *braita* is needed to teach something already in the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאַת נְגָעִים בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיבָא, ״וְאִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה כִּי יִהְיֶה בוֹ נָגַע בְּרֹאשׁ אוֹ בְזָקָן״! אֶלָּא אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: לְטׇהֳרַת נְגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: Concerning **ritual impurity from leprosy, it is written explicitly: "And when a man or woman has a plague upon the head or upon the beard"** (Leviticus 13:29). This indicates that there is no difference between a man and woman with regard to the beard in the case of leprosy. The *baraita* would not state a *halakha* that is explicit in the verse. **Rather, Mar Zutra says:** This *baraita* is referring **to ritual purification from leprosy,** i.e., women can also be purified from leprosy of the beard.

Mar Zutra then suggests the *braita* addresses *taharat nega'im* — the purification process. The *Gemara* dismisses this as well: once she is subject to *tumah* just like a man, she is obviously subject to the corresponding *taharah* process.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טׇהֳרַת נְגָעִים נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא, כֵּיוָן דְּבַת טוּמְאָה הִיא – בַּת טׇהֳרָה הִיא! אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא לִצְדָדִים כְּתִיב: ״אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה כִּי יִהְיֶה בוֹ נָגַע בְּרֹאשׁ״, ״אוֹ בְזָקָן״ – הֲדַר אֲתָאן לְאִישׁ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **ritual purification from leprosy it is also obvious: Since impurity applies to** a woman, **purity** likewise **applies to her.** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to state this with regard to the impurity of afflictions of the beard, as **it might enter your mind to say** that this verse **is written disjunctively,** i.e., that the phrase: **"And when a man or woman has a plague upon the head,"** applies to both a man or a woman; whereas when it states: **"Or upon the beard," we have come back to** the case of **a man** alone. Therefore the *baraita* **teaches us** that this phrase is not referring solely to a man, as there is no difference between a man and a woman with regard to leprosy.

The *Gemara* then settles on the correct reading: the *braita* is needed to clarify how to read the verse *ish o ishah ki yihyeh vo nega be-rosh o ve-zakan*. One might naturally read it as follows: *ish o ishah* — a man or a woman — *ki yihyeh vo nega be-rosh*, if there is a spot on the head; *o ve-zakan*, or — in the case of a man — on the beard. Under this reading, the reference to *zakan* reverts to *ish* only, and *isha* is mentioned only in connection with the head, not the beard. The *braita* comes to teach that this reading is incorrect: a woman's beard, if she has one, has the full status of a beard for purposes of *tzara'at* impurity — *harei hen ke-zakan le-chol divreihem* means for this purpose. The *braita* thus does not conflict with the *mishnah*'s ruling that women are exempt from *bal tashchit*; it addresses an entirely separate question regarding *nega'im*.

## Bal Yikrechu

### Isi's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי תָּנֵי: אַף ״בַּל יִקְרְחוּ״ – נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּאִיסִי דְּדָרֵישׁ הָכִי? ״בָּנִים אַתֶּם לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם לֹא תִתְגֹּדְדוּ וְלֹא תָשִׂימוּ קׇרְחָה בֵּין עֵינֵיכֶם לָמֵת. כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, בָּנִים וְלֹא בָּנוֹת – לְקׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Isi taught** in a *baraita*: **Women are also exempt** from the prohibition: **Do not make baldness** upon your heads, a prohibition against tearing out one's hair in grief over someone's death. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Isi?** The Gemara explains **that he teaches as follows:** The verse states: **"You are the sons of the Lord your God; you shall not cut yourselves, nor make any baldness between your eyes for the dead. For you are a holy people to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 14:1–2). This verse, which applies to **sons and not daughters,** is referring **to** causing **baldness,** and therefore this prohibition includes only men.

The *Gemara* now presents a teaching of Isi: *af bal yikrechu nashim pturot* — women are also exempt from the prohibition of *bal yikrechu korcha* — making a bald spot on the head as a sign of mourning (*lo tasimu korcha bein eineichem la-met*). This is a separate prohibition from the beard shaving, derived from a separate verse, and Isi teaches that it too does not apply to women.

### Banim Atem

The exegetical basis is the verse in Devarim: *banim atem la-Hashem Elokeichem, lo titgodedu ve-lo tasimu korcha bein eineichem la-met* — "you are children of the Lord your God; do not cut yourselves and do not make a bald spot between your eyes for the dead." Isi reads *banim* — sons — as limiting: *banim ve-lo banot le-korcha*. Sons, not daughters, are the subject of the *korcha* prohibition. This is a deliberate narrowing of the prohibition to men, derived from the masculine noun *banim*.

### Korcha vs Gedida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְקׇרְחָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִגְדִידָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ – הֲרֵי גְּדִידָה אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים בָּנִים וְלֹא בָּנוֹת – לְקׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Do **you say** that this is referring **to** causing **baldness, or is it** perhaps referring **only to** the prohibition against **cutting,** which appears first? The Gemara answers that **when it states: "For you are a holy people to the Lord your God,"** it **is stated** with regard to the prohibition against **cutting,** and this verse applies to both men and women, as they are all members of God's people. **How** then **do I realize** and explain the emphasis on **sons and not daughters?** This is referring **to** the prohibition against causing **baldness.**

However, the *Gemara* immediately asks whether this limitation also applies to *gedida* — lacerations in the flesh, which is also mentioned in the same verse (*lo titgodedu*). Perhaps *banim ve-lo banot* limits women's exemption to that prohibition as well?

The *Gemara* answers no, because of the continuation of the verse: *ki am kadosh atah la-Hashem Elokecha* — "for you are a holy people to the Lord your God." This phrase, *am kadosh*, encompasses everyone — *am* is the entire people, men and women alike — and it provides the basis for the prohibition of *gedida*. Therefore, the prohibition of lacerations in the flesh applies to women equally.

### Necessity Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגְּדִידָה וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַקׇּרְחָה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת הַגְּדִידָה, שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָהּ בִּמְקוֹם הַשֵּׂעָר וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם שֵׂעָר, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת הַקׇּרְחָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֵׂעָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see to include cutting and to exclude** causing **baldness?** Perhaps the opposite is true, and causing baldness applies to men and women whereas cutting applies only to men. The Gemara answers: **I include cutting,** whose prohibition is broader, **as it is** applicable both **in a place of hair and not in a place of hair; and I exclude** causing **baldness,** which is more limited, **as it** applies **only in a place of hair.**

The question then becomes: why do we include women in *gedida* from *am kadosh* but exclude them from *korcha* based on *banim*? The answer is the scope of each prohibition. *Gedida* — lacerations in the flesh — applies to any part of the body, whether in an area of hair or not (*she-yeshnah be-makom se'ar u-velo be-makom se'ar*). It is a broader prohibition. *Korcha* — a bald spot — applies only in an area of hair (*she-einah ela be-makom se'ar*). It is a more limited prohibition. Therefore, from *banim ve-lo banot* we exclude women from the narrower prohibition, and from *ki am kadosh* we include women in the broader one.
