# Sanhedrin 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Contradictory Testimony

### Witnesses Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּרַבָּנַן דִּפְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, וְהָכָא בְּאַקּוֹשֵׁי הַגָּדָה לִרְאִיָּה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: מַקְּשִׁינַן הַגָּדָה לִרְאִיָּה, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא מַקְּשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **and everyone,** both the first *tanna* and Rabbi Natan, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa,** and derive from this verse that it is necessary that the witnesses see the incident together, i.e., they were both present and observed the incident at the same time. **And here,** with regard to whether or not the witnesses must testify in court together, **they disagree with regard to** whether or not the **statement** of the witnesses, i.e., their testimony in court, **is compared to** their **observation** of the incident. One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **we compare** their **statement to** their **observation.** Therefore, just as they must see the incident together, so too, they must testify together in court. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Natan, **holds** that **we do not compare** their statement to their observation.

The daf opens by picking up where the previous discussion left off: the question of combining the testimony of two witnesses who each observed different aspects of a case. The paradigmatic issue is that of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who permits combining witnesses who testified about the same underlying obligation but observed it at different times — one seeing the loan on Monday, the other on Wednesday. The *gemara* now extends this inquiry into a related but distinct problem: what happens when two witnesses testify about the same case, but their accounts contradict each other on certain details?

### Bedikot vs. Chakirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, בֵּין בְּקַרְקָעוֹת בֵּין בְּמִטַּלְטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** As for the *halakha* in this matter, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** that it is unnecessary for the two witnesses to observe the incident together, **both with regard to** cases of **land** and **with regard to** cases of **movable property.**

Rav Yehuda establishes a foundational distinction. There are two categories of questions put to witnesses: *chakirot*, which are the foundational questions of time and place — when and where did the event occur — necessary to establish the possibility of rendering the witnesses *edim zommim*; and *bedikot*, which are questions about the particulars and circumstances of the case itself. Regarding *chakirot*, witnesses may not respond *eini yodeia* — "I do not know" — whereas regarding *bedikot* they may. The critical ruling Rav Yehuda now introduces is: *edut hamacheshet zo et zo b'dikot, k'sherah b'dinei mamonot* — testimony that contradicts itself in *bedikot* is valid in civil cases. If one witness says the borrower was wearing a red shirt and the other says black, that inconsistency does not invalidate the testimony in *dinei mamonot*. In *dinei nefashot*, however, such contradictions would be disqualifying.

### Trivial vs. Substantive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה בְּאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּאַרְנָקִי שְׁחוֹרָה״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּאַרְנָקִי לְבָנָה״. אֲבָל אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״מָנֶה שָׁחוֹר״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״מָנֶה לָבָן״ – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: The statement of Rav Yehuda is reasonable in** a case where **one** witness **says:** The money was **in a black purse [*be'arnaki*], and** the other **one says:** It was **in a white purse.** Since this is a secondary detail, they may not remember it accurately. **But** if **one says:** He lent him **a black coin,** i.e., the coin was dark, **and** the other **one says:** He lent him **a white coin,** the testimonies **are not combined.** Since they contradict each other with regard to a characteristic of the money itself, one is probably lying.

Rava clarifies and refines Rav Yehuda's principle. The distinction is not merely between *chakirot* and *bedikot*, but between contradictions in genuinely trivial peripheral detail versus contradictions that go to the substance of the transaction itself. Rava's example: if one witness says the borrower's wallet was black and the other says it was white, that is trivial — an *arnaki shechorah* versus an *arnaki levanah* — and the testimony stands in *dinei mamonot*. But if one says the coins lent were old and tarnished while the other says they were new and shiny — *mana shachor* versus *mana lavan* — while this does not change the amount of the loan, it touches on the substance of the transaction itself. In that case, Rava holds, the testimonies cannot combine: *ein mitztarfin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַרְנָקִי שְׁחוֹרָה בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת לָא? וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּסַיִיף הֲרָגוֹ״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בַּאֲרִירָן הֲרָגוֹ״ – אֵין זֶה ״נָכוֹן״; אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״כֵּלָיו שְׁחוֹרִים״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״כֵּלָיו לְבָנִים״ – הֲרֵי זֶה ״נָכוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges Rabbi Yehuda's ruling: **And** if witnesses contradict each other with regard to secondary details such as **a black purse in** cases of **capital law,** is it the case that their testimony is **not** accepted? **But doesn't Rav Ḥisda say:** In a case where **one** of the witnesses **says:** The murderer **killed** the victim **with a sword, and one** of the witnesses **says:** The murderer **killed** the victim **with an *ariran*,** another type of weapon, **this is not congruent** testimony, as this is a clear contradiction. But if **one** of the witnesses **says:** The murderer's **garments** were **black, and one** of the witnesses **says:** The murderer's **garments** were **white, this is congruent** testimony, as this is not a meaningful discrepancy.

The *gemara* then presses: does Rav Yehuda mean that even a trivial detail like the *arnaki shechorah* case would invalidate testimony in *dinei nefashot*? This seems harsh. The *gemara* raises the position of Rav Chisda, who states that one witness saying he was killed with a sword (*sayif*) and another saying with a spear are not in accord and thus inadmissible — *ein zeh nachon* — but if one says the killer was dressed in black and the other says in white, *harei zeh nachon*, that is acceptable even in *dinei nefashot*. So Rav Chisda himself does not invalidate trivial details in capital cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבְרָא אַגַּבְרָא קָא רָמֵית?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Are you setting** the statement of one **man against** the statement of another **man?** Rav Ḥisda holds that a contradiction with regard to secondary details does not disqualify the testimony even in capital law, and Rav Yehuda holds that it does disqualify the testimony. Neither Sage is bound by the statement of the other.

The *gemara* responds: *gavra gavra rami* — this is simply a debate between Rav Yehuda and Rav Chisda. 

### Overlap Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״מָנֶה שָׁחוֹר״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״מָנֶה לָבָן״ – מִצְטָרְפִים. כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה? אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה הֵיכָא דְּלָא מַכְחֲשִׁי אַהֲדָדֵי, הֵיכָא דְּמַכְחֲשִׁי אַהֲדָדֵי מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of Neharde'a say: Even** if **one says** that it was **a black coin and** the other **one says** that it was **a white coin** their testimonies **are combined. In accordance with whose** opinion is this? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa,** that as long as both witnesses testify that the defendant owes the plaintiff the same sum, the testimonies are combined? **Say that you heard Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** saying that two testimonies are combined in a case **where they do not contradict each other;** but in a case **where they contradict each other, did he say** that they are combined?

Nardea'ei then weigh in: even if witnesses contradict on the substantive details of the transaction itself in *dinei mamonot*, they may still combine. The *gemara* asks: is this then like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha? The answer is no — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's principle applies where witnesses are not contradicting each other at all, merely testifying to separate but similar events that can be abstracted together. Where witnesses genuinely contradict about the same event, even Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha would not combine them. The Nardea'ei must therefore be going on the basis of a different *tanna*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים, שֶׁאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת מָאתַיִם וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת מָנֶה, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בִּכְלַל מָאתַיִם מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Sages of Neharde'a **stated** their opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel do not disagree with regard to** a case of **two sets of witnesses, where one** set **says** that the plaintiff lent the defendant **two hundred** dinars, **and** the other **one says** that he lent him **one hundred dinars.** Both Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai agree that this is not considered a contradiction, and the testimony is accepted concerning the amount of one hundred dinars, **as one hundred dinars is** subsumed **within two hundred,** i.e., testimony concerning a large amount includes testimony concerning a smaller amount.

That *tanna* is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who states: *lo nechelku Beit Shammai u'Beit Hillel al shtei kitei edim she'achat omeret masayim ve'achat omeret maneh* — there was no dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel in the case of two *groups* of witnesses, one saying the borrower owes two hundred and the other saying one hundred. All agree we accept the lower figure, invoking the principle *yesh bichlal masayim maneh* — one hundred is subsumed within two hundred, so all witnesses agree at minimum to the lower amount.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ? עַל כַּת אַחַת, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נֶחְלְקָה עֵדוּתָן, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ בִּכְלַל מָאתַיִם מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** case **do they disagree?** They disagree **over** the case of **one set** of two witnesses, where one witness testifies that the defendant owes the plaintiff two hundred dinars, and the other witness says that he owes him one hundred. **As Beit Shammai say** that **their testimony is divided.** Since they are not testifying about the same amount, the entire testimony is disqualified. **And Beit Hillel say: One hundred dinars is** subsumed **within two hundred.** Apparently, according to Beit Hillel's opinion, as transmitted by Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, although the testimonies are not identical, since both attest to the defendant's liability to pay a certain amount of money, they are combined and accepted to that effect. This is the source for the opinion of the Sages of Neharde'a.

The debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar explains, concerned a single group of witnesses internally contradicting — one witness saying one hundred and another within the same group saying two hundred. Beit Shammai holds that since the group's testimony is divided, the entire testimony is voided: *nechelekah edutan*. Beit Hillel applies the principle of *yesh bichlal masayim maneh*: since two hundred contains one hundred, the group is unanimously testifying to at least one hundred, and that amount is accepted. This represents an even greater *chiddush* than Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's ruling: here witnesses are contradicting about the substantive terms of the very same case, and we nonetheless find the overlap and accept it.

### Application Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״חָבִית שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן״. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וְאָתֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. חַיְּיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא מִיגּוֹ חָבִיתָא דְמִשְׁחָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where **one** witness **says** that the plaintiff gave the defendant **a barrel of wine, and** the other **one says** that he gave him **a barrel of oil, there was** actually such **an incident, and it came before Rabbi Ami.** Since wine was cheaper than oil, **Rabbi Ami deemed** the defendant **liable to pay** the plaintiff only the value of **a barrel of wine out of** the value of **a barrel of oil,** an amount both witnesses agreed that he owed.

Two illustrative cases then appear. First: one witness says it was a barrel of wine (*chavit shel yayin*) and the other says a barrel of oil (*chavit shel shemen*). This incident came before Rav Ami, who ruled that the defendant must pay at least the value of wine — the lesser of the two — since all witnesses agree at minimum to that value.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר? אֵימַר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הֵיכָא דְּיֵשׁ בִּכְלַל מָאתַיִם מָנֶה, כִּי הַאי גַּוְונָא מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this ruling? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion transmitted by **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar? Say that Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said** that two contradicting testimonies can be combined in a case **where one hundred dinars is** subsumed **within two hundred;** perhaps one of the witnesses saw only half the loan, and the other one saw it all. But in **a case like this,** where the testimonies are about completely different items, **did he say** that they can be combined?

The *gemara* challenges: is this going like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar? The principle of *yesh bichlal masayim maneh* works because two hundred literally contains one hundred. But wine and oil are entirely different commodities; it cannot be said that oil "contains" wine in the way two hundred contains one hundred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, לִדְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this is not a case of a direct contradiction. This ruling is **necessary** only **with regard to monetary value,** i.e., where one witness says that the defendant owes the value of a barrel of wine, and the second one says that he owes the value of a barrel of oil. Therefore, it is comparable to a case of one hundred dinars and two hundred dinars.

The *gemara* retreats: the case must be reframed in terms of monetary value — one witness assessed the obligation at the value of wine, another at the value of oil — returning it to a standard high-low numerical dispute. The simpler reading of one commodity versus another is thus more difficult to sustain under the Beit Hillel principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בִּדְיוֹטָא הָעֶלְיוֹנָה״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בִּדְיוֹטָא הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, וְצֵירַף עֵדוּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where **one** witness **says** that the incident took place **on the upper floor [*badeyota*] and** the other **one says** that it occurred **on the lower floor, Rabbi Ḥanina says** that **an incident** like this **came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and he combined their testimonies.** This was in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa, that since they agree with regard to the matter itself, the secondary details are insignificant.

The second case: one witness says the transaction occurred on the upper balcony (*diyuta ha'elyona*) and another on the lower balcony (*diyuta hatachtona*). Rabbi Chanina reports that such a case came before Rabbi, who combined their testimony — a ruling that seems relatively unsurprising, since the location of the event within a building is as trivial a detail as one could imagine.

## Privacy & Disclosure

### Dissenting Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִין לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, מִנַּיִין לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא לֹא יֹאמַר: הֲרֵינִי מְזַכֶּה וַחֲבֵירַי מְחַיְּיבִין, אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁחֲבֵירַי רַבּוּ עָלַי? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא תֵלֵךְ רָכִיל בְּעַמֶּיךָ״, וְאוֹמֵר ״הוֹלֵךְ רָכִיל מְגַלֶּה סוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And from where** is it derived that **when** the judge **leaves** the courtroom, he should not say: I deemed you exempt and my colleagues deemed you liable, but what can I do, as my colleagues outnumbered me and consequently you were deemed liable? About this it is stated: "You shall not go as a talebearer among your people" (Leviticus 19:16), and it says: "One who goes about as a talebearer reveals secrets, but one who is of a faithful spirit conceals a matter" (Proverbs 11:13). **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that **when** the judge **leaves he should not say: I deemed** you **exempt and my colleagues deemed** you **liable, but what can I do, as my colleagues outnumbered me** and consequently you were deemed liable? **The verse states: "You shall not go as a talebearer among your people"** (Leviticus 19:16), **and it says: "One who goes about as a talebearer reveals secrets"** (Proverbs 11:13).

The *gemara* now turns to a section of the *Mishnah* addressing the prohibition on judges disclosing what transpired within the court. The *Mishnah* states: *minayin lechesheyetze lo yomar hareni mezakeh vachaverai mechayivin* — from where do we know that when a judge leaves court, he may not say "I voted to acquit but my colleagues outvoted me"? He may not go around cultivating sympathy outside the court, telling a litigant that he personally favored him. *U'mah a'aseh shechaverai rabu alai* — "what could I do, they were the majority against me?"

### Lo Telech Rachil

The biblical source is *lo telech rachil b'amecha* — do not go as a talebearer among your people. The *gemara* adds the verse *holech rachil megaleh sod* — one who goes and spreads tales reveals private matters. Judicial deliberations are precisely the kind of private matters this prohibition encompasses.

### Student Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא תַּלְמִידָא דִּנְפַק עֲלֵיהּ קָלָא דְּגַלִּי מִילְּתָא דְּאִיתְּמַר בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא בָּתַר עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין, אַפְּקֵיהּ רַב אַמֵּי מִבֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. אֲמַר: ״דֵּין גָּלֵי רָזַיָּא.״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain student, about whom a rumor emerged that he revealed a statement that was stated in the study hall** and should have been kept secret, and the rumor emerged **twenty-two years after** the time the statement was revealed. **Rav Ami removed him from the study hall** as a punishment. Rav Ami **said: This is a revealer of secrets** and he cannot be trusted.

A striking episode then follows. There were reports circulating that a certain student had revealed things that had been said in the *beit midrash* twenty-two years earlier — effectively writing a tell-all account of private discussions that had occurred decades prior. One might think that after so many years a kind of statute of limitations would apply. Nevertheless, Rav Ami expelled him from the *beit midrash*, declaring: *dein galya raza* — this man reveals secrets. The passage of twenty-two years provides no exemption from the prohibition of *galya raza*.

## Reopening Cases

### 30-Day Limit Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמֵּבִיא רְאָיָה – סוֹתֵר אֶת הַדִּין. אָמַר לוֹ: ״כׇּל רְאָיוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לְךָ הָבֵא מִיכָּן עַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״. מָצָא בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – סוֹתֵר, לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any time** one of the litigants **brings** additional **proof, he can overturn the verdict** that was decided according to previous proofs. If one litigant **said to** the other: **Bring all the proofs that you have from now until thirty days** from now, if **he found** additional proof **within thirty days, he can overturn** the verdict. If he found it **after thirty days, he cannot overturn** the verdict anymore.

The *Mishnah* teaches: *kol zman shemeivi raya soter et hadin* — any time a litigant produces new evidence, it can overturn the judgment. The court may attempt to place a limit on this: "bring whatever evidence you have within thirty days." If evidence is found within thirty days the case can be reopened; after thirty days it cannot. This is the position of the *Tanna Kamma*. The practical reasoning is clear: once a ruling has been issued and the prevailing party has acted on it — investing in property, developing land, entering into new arrangements — the other party cannot be permitted to unravel everything at any future moment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מָה יַעֲשֶׂה זֶה שֶׁלֹּא מָצָא בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים, וּמָצָא לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said:** He can still overturn the verdict, as **what should this** litigant, **who** sought and **did not find** additional proof **within thirty** days **but found** it **after thirty** days, **have done?**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *ma ya'aseh zeh shelo matza besoch shloshim umatza le'achar shloshim* — what is the person to do who simply did not find his evidence within thirty days but found it afterward? The court cannot impose such a cutoff. A person always retains the right to reopen a case with newly discovered evidence. This debate concerns *dinei mamonot*; in *dinei nefashot*, the door to reopening in favor of the accused is always kept open until the very last moment.

### Forfeiture Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא עֵדִים״, וְאָמַר: ״אֵין לִי עֵדִים״. אָמַר: ״הָבֵא רְאָיָה״, וְאָמַר: ״אֵין לִי רְאָיָה״. וּלְאַחַר זְמַן הֵבִיא רְאָיָה וּמָצָא עֵדִים – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where one litigant **said to** the other: **Bring witnesses, and** the latter **said: I have no witnesses,** and the former **said** to him: **Bring a proof, and he said: I have no proof, and he later brought a proof or found witnesses,** in this case, **this** proof or these witnesses are worth **nothing.** It is apparently a false proof or false testimony.

A distinct scenario: during the court proceedings themselves, the court asks the litigant to bring witnesses, and he says *ein li edim* — I have none. The court asks for documentary proof, and he says *ein li raya* — I have none. Subsequently he produces witnesses or evidence. Has he forfeited his right to bring them by explicitly declaring he had nothing? The *Tanna Kamma* says yes — by declaring before the court that he has no evidence, he has effectively testified to that fact and forfeited his ability to produce it later. *Harei zeh eino klum* — it is of no effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מָה יַעֲשֶׂה זֶה שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עֵדִים, וּמָצָא עֵדִים? לֹא הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ רְאָיָה, וּמָצָא רְאָיָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: What should this** litigant, **who did not know that he has witnesses and** ultimately **found witnesses,** or who **did not know that he has a proof and** ultimately **found proof, have done?** Therefore, he can still overturn the verdict.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *ma ya'aseh zeh shelo haya yodeia sheyeish lo eidim umatza eidim* — what is to be done for one who genuinely did not know he had witnesses, and then found them? He made an honest mistake. The practical lesson for the litigant under the *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling is clear: never state categorically before the court that you have no witnesses; always say "as far as I know I have none, but I cannot be certain without further inquiry."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאָה שֶׁמִּתְחַיֵּיב בַּדִּין, וְאָמַר: ״קָרְבוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וִיעִידוּנִי״, אוֹ שֶׁהוֹצִיא רְאָיָה מִתַּחַת פּוּנְדָּתוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If at the beginning of the discussion in the court one did not bring witnesses or other evidence for his claims, but then **he saw that he** was about to be **deemed liable** to pay **in the judgment, and said: Bring so-and-so and so-and-so, and they will testify on my behalf, or he pulled out a proof from under his belt [*pundato*],** even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds **that this is** worth **nothing.** If there was truth in the testimony of these witnesses or in this proof, he would not have hidden it until now.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel himself, however, acknowledges a limiting case: if the circumstances make it evident that something suspicious is occurring — the litigant said he had no evidence, then immediately as the ruling appeared to go against him, he produced witnesses from among those present (*kirvu ploni u'ploni veya'iduni*) or extracted a proof document from his own sack — then even he agrees the evidence cannot be accepted. When the duplicity is manifest, *harei zeh eino klum*.

### Halacha: First Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the first *halakha* in the mishna, **Rabba bar Rav Huna says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. And Rabba bar Rav Huna** also **says:** The ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis.**

Rabbah bar bar Chana states: *halacha keRabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the law follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the first case, namely that the court cannot impose a thirty-day limit on producing new evidence. One cannot simply infer this from the ruling in the first case, the *gemara* notes, because one might argue that *lechatchila* we advise courts not to impose such limits, but *bedi'avad*, if a court did impose a limit and then refused to hear new evidence, its ruling might stand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְכַתְּחִילָּה, אֲבָל דִּיעֲבַד שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאִי עָבֵיד – מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **this statement,** that the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, applies ***ab initio*, but after the fact,** even if the court ruled in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, the ruling is **valid,** as their opinion was not entirely rejected, Rabba bar Rav Huna therefore **teaches us that if** the court **acts** in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, **we send** the case **back** to court.

Therefore it is stated explicitly: *de'ahadori mehadreinan leih* — we reverse any such ruling. The right to produce new evidence and reopen a case is fully intact regardless of any court-imposed deadline.

### Halacha: Second Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הָבֵא עֵדִים כּוּ׳, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים. וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in a case where one litigant **said to** the other: **Bring witnesses,** and he admitted that he had none, and he subsequently found witnesses, **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said** that their testimony is valid. **Rabba bar Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis. And Rabba bar Rav Huna** also **says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

Rava bar Rav Huna said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha k'divrei chachamim* — the law follows the *Chachamim* in the second case. Once a litigant has declared before the court that he has no witnesses, he may not subsequently produce them. And symmetrically: *ein halacha k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* in this case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דִּבְהָהִיא אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל; הָא בְּכוּלְּהוּ – הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** statement of Rabba bar Rav Huna **teaches us that** specifically **with regard to that** *halakha*, the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel; but with regard to all** other statements of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the corpus of the Mishna, the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

The *gemara* notes the apparent redundancy of stating both sides, and resolves it: the emphasis on *ein halacha k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* here is deliberate, because in virtually every other instance in the *Mishnah* where Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears, the *halacha* follows him. This is the exceptional case, and it needed to be called out explicitly.

## Sealing Claims

### Three Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ, הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ, חוּץ מֵעָרֵב וְצַיְדָן וּרְאָיָה אַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **to the exclusion of that which Rabba bar bar Ḥanna says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Anywhere that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel taught** a ruling **in our Mishna,** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with his** opinion, **except for** the following three cases: The responsibility of the **guarantor, and** the incident that occurred in the city of **Tzaidan, and** the dispute with regard to **evidence** in the **final** disagreement. Whereas in the former dispute in the mishna here, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, in the latter dispute in the mishna here, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. Rabba bar Rav Huna, by contrast, maintains that in the case of a guarantor and in the case in Tzaidan, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

Rabbah bar bar Chana further cites Rabbi Yochanan's general rule: *kol makom sheshana Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel b'mishnatenu halacha k'moto* — wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears in our *Mishnah*, the *halacha* follows him, *chutz* — with three exceptions: *arev*, *Tzidan*, and *ra'aya achrona*.

*Arev* refers to the case at the end of Bava Batra concerning the guarantor — the co-signer — and the rules governing whether the creditor must first pursue the primary debtor or can go directly to the guarantor, depending on whether the guaranty was issued *b'sha'at matan ma'ot* or *achar matan ma'ot*. *Tzidan* refers to a case involving criticism of witnesses — a claim that witnesses were in a particular location — where the *halacha* departs from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. And *ra'aya achrona* — the "final evidence" — refers precisely to our case. It is called the final *ra'aya* because our *Mishnah* presents two scenarios involving a litigant's claim of having evidence. In the first scenario (the thirty-day limit), we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. In the second scenario (the litigant who declared he had no witnesses), we do not. *Ra'aya achrona* refers to that second scenario in our *Mishnah*.

## Child's Case

### Child Case Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יָנוֹקָא דְּתַבְעוּהּ לְדִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִית לְךָ סָהֲדֵי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא. אִית לָךְ רְאָיָה? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא. חַיְּיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain child who was taken to court before Rav Naḥman. They said to him: Do you have witnesses** on your behalf? The child **said to them: No.** They continued to ask: **Do you have evidence?** The child **said to them: No. Rav Naḥman deemed him liable,** in accordance with the claim of the other litigant.

A case: a child (*yanuka*) was brought before Rav Nachman for judgment. Rav Nachman asked him: *it lakh sa'adei?* — do you have witnesses? The child said no. *It lakh ra'aya?* — do you have evidence? Again, no. Rav Nachman ruled against him. The child left in tears. Certain bystanders who heard the child crying asked what had happened, and upon learning the circumstances, said: *anan yad'inan b'milei de-avukh* — we know about your father's business dealings; we have relevant evidence to offer.

### Children Unaware

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה קָא בָכֵי וְאָזֵיל. שַׁמְעוּהּ הָנָךְ אִינָשֵׁי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֲנַן יָדְעִינַן בְּמִילֵּי דַּאֲבוּךְ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בְּהָא אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ, דְּיָנוֹקָא בְּמִילֵּי דַּאֲבוּהּ לָא יָדַע.
>
> **Translation:** The child **was walking and crying. These people heard him,** and **said to him: We know about the** monetary **matters of your father** and can testify on your behalf. When he brought them before Rav Naḥman, **Rav Naḥman said: In** a case like **this, even the Rabbis concede** that the testimony is accepted, **as a child does not know about the** monetary **matters of his father.** Clearly, when he said that he has no witnesses or proof, he said so out of ignorance and was mistaken; there is no concern about artifice.

Rav Nachman rules: *b'ha afilu rabanan modu de-yanuka b'milei de-avuhu lo yada* — even the *Rabbanan* who rule against Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the *ra'aya achrona* case would concede here. The reason is not merely general circumstantial evidence suggesting the child was unaware; rather, it is that *as a matter of general principle* we do not assume that children know the details of their father's business affairs. When the child said he had no evidence, that statement cannot be treated as a categorical forfeiture, because it reflects a structural epistemic limitation inherent to the child's position, not a deliberate claim. The new evidence produced by the bystanders may therefore be accepted.

This raises the broader question of how far the principle extends. Is it limited to an *yoresh* — an heir who is not the direct *baal din* but is representing his father's estate — or would it apply in other circumstances where a litigant's unawareness of evidence is demonstrable? The *gemara* leaves this open.

## Receipt & Migo

### Woman & Receipt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דִּנְפַק שְׁטָרָא מִתּוּתֵי יְדַהּ, אֲמַרָה (לֵיהּ): יָדַעְנָא בְּהַאי שְׁטָרָא דִּפְרִיעַ הֲוָה. הֵימְנַהּ רַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman from whose possession** a promissory **note emerged,** i.e., she was appointed to hold it. **She said to** the judge: **I know that this** promissory **note was repaid.** The creditor should not use it to collect. **Rav Naḥman deemed her** testimony **credible** and did not allow the creditor to collect the debt.

A woman produced a *shetar* — a loan document — that was in her possession. She declared: *yada'ana b'hai shetara depari'a hava* — I know that this loan document has already been paid. In other words, the document evidences a debt that David ostensibly owes Michael, and the woman, who had been holding the document, claims the debt was in fact discharged, though there is no receipt.

### Rav Nachman Believes

Rav Nachman believes her declaration.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי, דְּאָמַר אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: In accordance with whose** opinion is your ruling? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who says** that **letters,** i.e., the content of a promissory note, **are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document? In other words, there is no need to write a deed for the transfer of a monetary document from one individual's ownership to another. By giving it to the recipient, he becomes the owner of the document. Therefore, since the promissory note is in this woman's possession, she is considered its legal owner, and her claim that it was repaid is consequently accepted.

Rava challenges Rav Nachman: *keman?* — on whose view are you relying? Is this going like Rabbi, who holds that *otiyot niknot b'mesirah* — a *shetar* can be transferred through physical delivery? If so, since the woman was holding the *shetar*, she could have claimed the debt as her own. But the *gemara* does not accept that reasoning.

### Validated in Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא – קְלָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** That is not the reason for my ruling; rather, **here it is different.** Here the woman's claim is accepted in any event, **as,** since the promissory note was in her possession, **if she had wanted** to, **she** could have **burned it.** Therefore, she is presumably telling the truth.

Rav Nachman explains his ruling differently: *shani hacha de-i ba'ya kaltah* — what is distinctive here is that she could have simply destroyed the document. Since she chose to produce it rather than burn it, we believe her claim that it was paid, on the basis of *migo* — had she wanted to conceal that the debt existed, she could have destroyed it, so her producing it and simultaneously claiming payment is credible. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִיתַּחְזַק בְּבֵי דִינָא, ״אִיבַּעְיָא קְלָתֵיהּ״ לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman answered him: **Since** the promissory note **was ratified in court, we do not say** that she is deemed credible to nullify its validity because **if she had wanted** to, **she** could have **burned it.** The validity of a ratified document is not nullified without evidence.

The *gemara* then presents an alternative version of the exchange. In this version, Rav Nachman does *not* believe her, and it is Rava who raises the *migo* argument: surely she could have destroyed it? Rav Nachman responds: *keivan de-itchazei b'bei dina i ba'ya kaltah lo amrinan* — once the *shetar* had been validated before a *beit din*, we no longer apply the *migo* of "she could have destroyed it." Rashi explains the reason: the principle is *edim hachatumim al hashetar kemi shenechkerah edutam b'beit din* — witnesses whose signatures appear on a document are treated as if their testimony had been formally examined in court. Accordingly, we have witnesses attesting that the debt exists; a *migo* is insufficient to override the testimony of witnesses. Tosafot questions this: even if the *shetar* was validated, she still *could* have destroyed it, and the *migo* argument should remain valid on its own terms. Tosafot therefore explains that *itchazei b'bei dina* means the *beit din* had previously seen the *shetar* in her possession, so it was already known to exist — burning it was no longer an option.

### Receipt Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: סִימְפּוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – יִתְקַיֵּים בְּחוֹתְמָיו. אֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, וְיָצָא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שָׁלִישׁ, אוֹ שֶׁיָּצָא אַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת – כָּשֵׁר. אַלְמָא שָׁלִישׁ מְהֵימַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to** the ruling of **Rav Naḥman** from a *baraita*: **A receipt [*simfon*]** of repayment of a debt **upon which witnesses** are signed **is ratified by** means of **its signatories.** The witnesses testify that these are their signatures, and it is thereby ratified. If **there are no witnesses** signed **on it, but** the receipt **emerged from the possession of a third party** serving as a trustee, **or** if **it emerged after the signing of the documents,** i.e., the receipt was written on the promissory note beneath the content of the note and the witnesses' signatures, it is **valid.** Rava states his objection: **Apparently,** the testimony of **a third party** serving as a trustee **is deemed credible,** as he can testify that the receipt is valid even if it was not signed by witnesses.

Rava then poses a challenge to Rav Nachman's position. Consider the case of a *simpon* — a receipt — that bears signatures: if an unsigned receipt is produced by the person holding the *shetar*, together with the *shetar* itself, the *shalish* — the trustee holding the documents — is believed. An unsigned receipt is normally worthless, yet since the person is producing both the *shetar* and the receipt, they are believed to say the *shetar* was paid. This apparently contradicts Rav Nachman's view that producing a document cannot lend credibility to an accompanying claim.

### Migo Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman is** indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The resolution: the act of producing the *shetar* itself creates a *migo*. By bringing the document forward, the holder demonstrates that if she had wanted to simply make the debt disappear she could have destroyed it. Therefore, when she produces it and simultaneously declares it paid, she is believed — even on the basis of an unsigned receipt. The *tiyuvta* against Rav Nachman's first position stands.

## Sealing Claims…

### Until Claims Sealed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם מֵבִיא רְאָיָה וְסוֹתֵר, עַד שֶׁיִּסְתַּתֵּם טַעֲנוֹתָיו וְיֹאמַר: קִרְבוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְהַעִידוּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** One **can always bring a proof** for his claim **and overturn** the previous verdict, **until his claims are stopped up,** i.e., until he has no more claims, **and he says: Come, so-and-so and so-and-so, and testify on my behalf,** in which case those witnesses are not allowed to testify.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he cited Rabbi Yochanan: *le'olam mevi ra'ayah vesoter* — one can always produce evidence and reopen a case, *ad she'yistatemu ta'anotav* — until his claims have been sealed off. The *gemara* initially puzzles over this formulation, because no external sealing mechanism has been identified in the *Mishnah*. The phrase *yistatemu ta'anotav* seems to refer to the litigant's own declaration — *ein li ra'ayah* — which is precisely what seals his claims and triggers the *Rabbanan*'s rule against further evidence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ ״יִסְתַּתֵּם טַעֲנוֹתָיו״ – אֲתָאן לְרַבָּנַן, וַהֲדַר אָמְרַתְּ ״קִרְבוּ אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְהַעִידוּנִי״ – אֲתָאן לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This matter itself** is **difficult.** When **you say:** Until **his claims are stopped up, we arrive at,** i.e., this represents, the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold in the mishna that once a litigant stops presenting his claims, he cannot present additional claims later. **And** when **you then say:** Until he says **come, so-and-so and so-and-so, and testify on my behalf, we arrive at** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** that it is only in that case that the testimony is not accepted.

The *gemara* then examines an apparent internal contradiction. On one reading, once claims are sealed — by saying *ein li edim*, *ein li ra'ayah* — the *Rabbanan*'s rule applies and no further evidence is admitted. But another formulation attributed to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel suggests evidence is inadmissible only when the circumstances are clearly suspicious — the moment he said he had nothing and immediately called for witnesses (*ploni u'ploni ye'iduni*). These are different standards. Could the entire *sugya* be read as following Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, with *yistatemu ta'anotav* interpreted narrowly to mean only the case of obvious manipulation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּי אֲתָא רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם מֵבִיא רְאָיָה וְסוֹתֵר, עַד שֶׁיִּסְתַּתֵּם טַעֲנוֹתָיו וְיֹאמְרוּ לוֹ: ״הָבֵא עֵדִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״אֵין לִי עֵדִים״. ״הָבֵא רְאָיָה״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״אֵין לִי רְאָיָה״. אֲבָל בָּאוּ עֵדִים מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, אוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה דִּיסַקַּיָּא שֶׁל אָבִיו מוּפְקֶדֶת בְּיַד אַחֵר – הֲרֵי זֶה מֵבִיא רְאָיָה וְסוֹתֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara presents another version of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement. **When Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One can always bring a proof and overturn** the previous verdict **until his claims are stopped up, and** the judges **say to him: Bring witnesses, and he says: I have no witnesses,** and they say to him: **Bring evidence, and he says: I have no evidence.** This is in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis in the mishna. **But** if **witnesses came from overseas, or** if **there was a saddlebag [*disakkaya*] of his father's** documents **deposited by another** individual, which are cases where he did not know of the evidence, **he can bring** this **evidence and overturn** the previous verdict, as there is no concern of artifice.

The *gemara* rejects this: Rabbi Yochanan had explicitly stated that the *halacha* follows the *Chachamim* and not Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the *ra'aya achrona* case. One cannot construe the ruling as being in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

The resolution comes when Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda cites Rabbi Yochanan with greater precision: *le'olam mevi ra'ayah vesoter ad she'yistatemu ta'anotav* — one may always produce evidence, until he seals his own claims by declaring *ein li edim, ein li ra'ayah*. At that point we rule like the *Rabbanan* and no further evidence is accepted. The phrase *u'veyomar kirvu ploni u'ploni ve'ha'iduni* describes the case where Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel himself would reject the evidence — when the suspicious circumstances are manifest — and this represents a separate ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, not the general standard.

### Exception Cases

Even after claims have been sealed by the declaration *ein li edim*, there remain certain exceptional circumstances in which evidence may still be admitted. First: *aval bau edim m'medinat hayam* — if witnesses arrived from overseas after the litigant had declared he had no witnesses, since there is a clear reason the litigant could not have known of their availability, their testimony may be admitted. Second: *o shehayah diskaya shel aviv mufkedet b'yad acher* — if the father's collection of documents and proofs was being held by a third party, and those documents came to light only later, that evidence may be brought. Importantly, this second case demonstrates that the exception is not limited to the specific case of a child and his father's affairs; it extends to any situation where there is demonstrable reason the litigant was unaware of available evidence, even in one's own affairs.

## Venue & Jurisdiction

### Court Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַתּוֹקֵף אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בַּדִּין, אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״נִדּוֹן כָּאן״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״נֵלֵךְ לִמְקוֹם הַוַּועַד״ – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ וְיֵלֵךְ לִמְקוֹם הַוַּועַד.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **one who attacks another in judgment,** i.e., tenaciously raises legal claims against another, and **one** of the litigants **says: Let us go to court here** in our locale, **and** the other **one says: Let us go to the place of the Assembly,** i.e., the Sanhedrin, or another High Court, the former litigant **is compelled to go to the place of the Assembly.**

When Rav Dimi came and cited Rabbi Yochanan, he also transmitted the following ruling: *hatokef et chavero b'din* — when one party seeks to bring another to court, and one says *nidon kan* — let us litigate here locally — while the other says *neilech l'makom hava'ad* — let us go to the major court — the rule is *kofin oto veyelech l'makom hava'ad*: the party who wants to go to the major court can compel the other to go there.

### Lender Dictates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: רַבִּי, מִי שֶׁנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, יוֹצִיא מָנֶה עַל מָנֶה? אֶלָּא, כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ וְדָן בְּעִירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said before him: My teacher,** if so, **must one who claims** a debt of **one hundred dinars from another spend one hundred dinars** of travel and lodging expenses **for** the **one hundred dinars** he wants to collect? **Rather,** one **is compelled to** appear and **be judged** in a court that presides **in his** own **city.**

Rabbi Elazar raises an objection before Rabbi: *mi she'nosheh b'chavero maneh yotzi maneh al maneh?* — if Michael is the lender and I the borrower, are you saying Michael must spend one hundred to recover one hundred, traveling at his own expense to a distant court because I insist on it? Rather, the *halacha* is: *kofin oto vedan b'iro* — the borrower can be forced to litigate in the lender's locale.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי: אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁנֵים שֶׁנִּתְעַצְּמוּ בַּדִּין, אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״נִדּוֹן כָּאן״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״נֵלֵךְ לִמְקוֹם הַוַּועַד״ – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ וְדָן בְּעִירוֹ. וְאִם הוּצְרַךְ דָּבָר לִשְׁאוֹל – כּוֹתְבִין וְשׁוֹלְחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that **Rav Safra says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **two who were struggling in judgment, one** of whom **says: Let us go to court here, and one** of whom **says: Let us go to the place of the Assembly,** the latter litigant **is compelled to** appear and **be judged** in a court that presides **in his** own **city. And if** the local court **needs to ask** a higher court about a certain **matter,** the judges **write** to the Assembly, **and** the higher court **sends** its response.

Rav Safra adds a complementary ruling: where two parties are being obstinate and disputing the venue, we rule that the case is heard locally, *d'im hutzrach davar lishol* — and if the local *beit din* needs to refer a question to the larger court, *kotvin vesholchin* — they write to them and receive their guidance. The mere existence of a major court does not require parties to travel there; the local court can access the major court's authority through written correspondence.

### Judgment Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר: ״כִּתְבוּ וּתְנוּ לִי מֵאֵיזֶה טַעַם דַּנְתּוּנִי״ – כּוֹתְבִין וְנוֹתְנִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** one of the litigants **says** to a court: **Write for what reason you judged me** in this manner **and give** it **to me,** as I do not trust your decision without explanation, the judges **write** it **and give** it **to him.**

In this same context, the *gemara* states: *ve-im amar kisvu usnu li me-eizeh ta'am dantuni, kosvin venosnin lo* — if the losing party requests that the court commit to writing the reasoning behind its judgment, the court writes it out and provides it to him. The structure of this ruling implies two things: first, the court does not routinely provide written reasoning absent a request; second, the right to request written reasoning appears to arise specifically in this context — where a party has been compelled to litigate before a local court over his objection.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot addresses this directly: *davka heicha denis-atzmu ladin vekafu ladun kan* — this provision applies specifically where the parties contended about venue and the party was compelled to litigate before the local court. In that circumstance alone does the losing party have the right to demand a written statement of reasons — *aval b'alma lo*, but not in the ordinary case. Tosafot notes a possible alternative reading, suggesting that perhaps in such a case the court must in any event notify the losing party of the grounds of the decision even without his asking, while in general cases written reasons are provided only upon request. The Tosafot's discussion also raises the implicit corollary: if the losing party in a local court obtains a written statement of reasons, could he then bring that document to the major court and argue that the local court erred? This connects to the broader framework of *ta'ah bidvar mishnah* and *ta'ah bidvar halacha* — categories of judicial error that are reversible — though the *gemara* does not spell this out.

## Venue & Jurisdiction…

### Yibum Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְּבָמָה, הוֹלֶכֶת אַחַר הַיָּבָם לְהַתִּירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Another *halakha* was stated with regard to the location of the judgment: **And** with regard to **a woman whose husband had a brother,** and he **died childless [*vehayevama*],** she **follows her brother-in-law [*hayavam*]** for him **to free her** of the levirate bond through *ḥalitza*. The *yavam* does not have to go to her.

The *gemara* introduces a case from *yibum*: *vehayevamah holeches achar yavam* — the *yevamah* (sister-in-law) must follow the *yavam* (brother-in-law) to his location in order to perform either *yibum* or *chalitzah*; she cannot demand that he come to her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֲפִילּוּ מִטְּבֶרְיָא לְצִפּוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How far** does she have to go? **Rabbi Ami says: Even from Tiberias to Tzippori.** This is the *halakha* even though the court in Tiberias is more prestigious than the one in Tzippori.

Rabbi Ami states: this applies even from Tiberias to Sepphoris. Rashi explains the significance: even though Tiberias is the larger city, the *yevamah* must travel to the smaller location of the *yavam* and cannot insist that he come to the larger city.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מַאי קְרָא? ״וְקָרְאוּ לוֹ זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ״, וְלֹא זִקְנֵי עִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: What is the verse** that alludes to this *halakha*? It is the verse that states: **"Then the elders of his city shall call him,** and speak to him, and if he stands and says: I do not wish to take her" (Deuteronomy 25:8). The fact that the elders of his city, **and not the elders of her city,** are mentioned indicates that *ḥalitza* is performed in his city and not in hers.

The biblical basis is derived by Rav Kahana: *veyikre'u lo ziknei iro* — the elders of *his* city are called, not the elders of *her* city. Accordingly, the proceedings take place in his city. This also means that if the *yavam* is away from his home, his city's court can summon him back — the *yevamah* is required to come to his city, not to wherever he happens to be at the moment.

### Lender-Borrower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הִילְכְתָא, כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ וְיֵלֵךְ לִמְקוֹם הַוַּועַד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ וְדָן בְּעִירוֹ! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ לֹוֶה לְמַלְוֶה, אֲבָל מַלְוֶה – ״עֶבֶד לֹוֶה לְאִישׁ מַלְוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says:** The ***halakha*** is that if one litigant wants to go to the place of the Assembly and the other does not, the latter **is compelled to go to the place of the Assembly. Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: But doesn't Rabbi Elazar say** that **he is compelled** to appear and **be judged** in a court that presides **in his** own **city?** Ameimar answered: **This statement** applies in a case **where the borrower said to the lender:** Let us go to the place of the Assembly; in that case, the debtor is compelled to be judged in his city. **But** if the **lender** requests to go to the place of the Assembly, his request is accepted, as it is stated: **"The borrower is servant to the lender"** (Proverbs 22:7).

Meimar rules: *halacha holeches, kofin oto veyelech l'makom hava'ad* — in the *yibum* case, the *yevamah* can compel the *yavam* to go to the major court. Rav Ashi challenges this on the basis of Rabbi Elazar's ruling that a party can be compelled to litigate locally. Rav Ashi resolves the apparent conflict: *hani mili heicha dekamar loveh le-malveh* — the principle that one goes locally applies when it is the borrower making demands of the lender. The underlying rationale is *malveh eved loveh le-ish malveh* — the borrower is in a position of subservience to the lender. It is therefore the lender who dictates the terms of where litigation occurs. If the lender wishes to remain local, the borrower cannot force him to the major court. If the lender wishes to bring the borrower to the major court, he may compel him to go. In any case, the lender's prerogative governs. The *yibum* case, involving different dynamics, is therefore distinguishable.

## Ukvin Case

### Letter to Mar Ukva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ לְמָר עוּקְבָא: לִדְזִיו לֵיהּ כְּבַר בִּתְיָה, שְׁלָם. עוּקְבָן הַבַּבְלִי קְבַל קֳדָמַנָא: יִרְמְיָה אָחִי הֶעֱבִיר עָלַי אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ. אִמְרוּ לוֹ: הַשִּׂיאוּהוּ וְיִרְאֶה פָּנֵינוּ בִּטְבֶרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **sent** a letter **to Mar Ukva,** the Exilarch in Babylonia, which stated: **To he who has light** upon him, **like** Moses, who is called **the son of Bithiah, Shalom.** The letter continued: **Ukvan the Babylonian complained before us** as follows: **Yirmeya, my brother, came past me,** i.e., did me a great evil. **And** therefore **tell** Yirmeya; **induce him [*hassiuhu*] to see our face in Tiberias,** where we will judge his case.

The *gemara* records that a letter was sent to Mar Ukva in highly poetic terms, referencing the luminosity associated with *Moshe Rabbeinu* — *karan or panav* — and applying the imagery to Mar Ukva as one who shines like the son of *Batya* (daughter of Pharaoh who raised Moshe). The letter conveyed that a certain *Ukvan HaBavli* had brought a complaint against someone — identified as *Yirmiyah achi* — claiming that he had wronged him: *he'evir alai et haderech*. The letter told Mar Ukva to judge the case, and instructed the defendant to appear before the court in Tiberias.

### Hagufa Kashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ ״אִמְרוּ לוֹ״, אַלְמָא דַּיְּינוּהּ אַתּוּן. ״הַשִּׂיאוּהוּ וְיִרְאֶה פָּנֵינוּ בִּטְבֶרְיָא״, אַלְמָא שַׁדְּרוּהּ הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** matter **itself** is **difficult** to understand. **You said: Tell him. Apparently** the intention was: **Judge him yourselves** in Babylonia. But then the letter states: **Induce him to see our face in Tiberias. Apparently** the intention was: **Send him here.**

The *gemara* notes an apparent internal contradiction in the letter: on one reading it says "you judge him," while on another it says "have him appear before us in Tiberias." *Hagufa kashya* — the passage itself is difficult.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אֶלָּא), הָכִי קָאָמְרִי: אֱמַרוּ לֵיהּ, דַּיְּינוּהּ אַתּוּן. אִי צָיֵית – צָיֵית, וְאִי לָא – הַשִּׂיאוּהוּ וְיִרְאֶה פָּנֵינוּ בִּטְבֶרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this** is what the Sages **were saying: Tell him,** i.e., **judge** him **yourselves. If he listens** to the court, **he listens,** and the issue is resolved. **But if** he does **not** listen, **induce him to see our face in Tiberias.**

The resolution: the message to Mar Ukva was structured as follows — *daynu atun*, we are asking you to judge him; *ei tzyat tzyat*, if you can get him to comply and submit to your judgment locally in Bavel, fine; *v'ei lo, hatzivu v'harei paneinu beTverya*, and if you cannot get him to submit, then have him appear before us here in Tiberias and we will hear it.

### Knas Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת הֲוָה, וּבְבָבֶל לָא דָּיְינִי דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת. וְהָא דִּשְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ הָכִי – כְּדֵי לַחֲלוֹק כָּבוֹד לְמָר עוּקְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** That is not the correct understanding of the letter. Rather, **it was** a case pertaining to **the *halakhot* of fines, and in Babylonia** the courts **do not adjudicate** in cases pertaining to **the *halakhot* of fines,** as there are no ordained judges there; such Sages are found only in Eretz Yisrael. Consequently, it was necessary to send the case to Eretz Yisrael. **And** the fact **that they sent** the letter to Mar Ukva using **this** wording, as though he were capable of adjudicating the case himself, was **in order to give honor to Mar Ukva,** who was a Sage and a leader. They hinted to him that he could not judge this case and that he therefore had to send the defendant to Eretz Yisrael.

Rav Ashi offers an alternative interpretation: *dinei knasot hava* — this was a case involving a *knas*, a penalty. In Babylonia, *dinei knasot* are not adjudicated — *u'vei b'Bavel lo dayni dinei knasot*. Therefore, the case was never going to be heard before Mar Ukva in Bavel at all; it was always going to be tried in Eretz Yisrael.

### Honoring Mar Ukva

If that is so, why was the letter sent to Mar Ukva at all? The *gemara* answers: *kedei lachlok kavod leMar Ukva* — to honor Mar Ukva. The Eretz Yisrael *beit din* reached out to the great Babylonian leader out of respect, informing him of the case and of their intention to hear it in Tiberias. The elaborate and poetic formulation of the letter was itself an expression of that honor.

## Court Costs

### No Expense Payment

Tosafot closes the daf with a significant observation arising from the earlier discussion of *mi she'nosheh b'chavero maneh yotzi maneh al maneh* — the concern that the lender would have to spend money to recover his money. One might ask: why not simply have the lender travel to the borrower's preferred venue and then require the guilty party to reimburse the lender's travel expenses? Tosafot answers: *mikan lemi shechayav badin d'lo m'shaleem l'idach yitziotav* — this very *gemara* is the source for the principle that even a party who is found liable in litigation does not pay the opposing party's expenses, *af al pi shemazkiko leilech ladun b'ir acheret*, even when the litigation required the other party to travel to a different city to pursue the case. The *gemara* never suggested that possibility as a solution, and that silence is itself the ruling.
