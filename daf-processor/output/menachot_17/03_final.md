# Menachot 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul & Shiyarim

### Sesame-Grain Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְטִיר שׁוּמְשׁוּם לֶאֱכוֹל שׁוּמְשׁוּם, עַד שֶׁכָּלָה קוֹמֶץ כּוּלּוֹ – רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: חַד אָמַר: פִּיגּוּל, וְחַד אָמַר: פָּסוּל, וְחַד אָמַר: כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** If one **burned** an amount the size of **a sesame** seed of the handful and frankincense with the intent **to consume** an amount the size of **a sesame** seed from the remainder the next day, and he repeated the same action with the accompanying intent **until** he burned **the entire** measure of the **handful** and frankincense, the *halakha* in this case is a matter of dispute between **Rav Ḥisda, Rav Hamnuna, and Rav Sheshet. One says** that the entire meal offering is ***piggul*, and one says** that the offering is **disqualified** but is not *piggul*, **and one says** that the offering remains **fit.**

The daf opens in the midst of the broader discussion of *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* — whether a *pigul* thought expressed during the performance of only one of two required acts (*chatzi matir*) is effective. The case under examination involves the *kometz* of the *minchah* and the *levona*: each is a half of the combined *matir* that permits the *shiyarim*, the remainder of the flour-offering, to the priests. The familiar debate between Rebbi Meir and the Rabbanan over *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* provides the theoretical backdrop.

The Gemara now introduces a far more intricate case. Rather than simply placing the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* with a *pigul* intent to eat a *kezayis* of *shiyarim* tomorrow — which would be a straightforward application of *pigul* — the scenario involves offering the *kometz* one sesame-grain (*shumshum*) at a time. With each individual grain placed upon the *mizbe'ach*, the *kohen* declares an intent to eat a correspondingly tiny amount of the *shiyarim* tomorrow. This continues grain by grain until the entirety of the *kometz* has been offered. In the aggregate, the accumulated intentions cover a *kezayis* or more of *shiyarim*, but no single act of *haktara* was accompanied by an intent involving a full *kezayis*, or indeed any halachically significant quantity, of *shiyarim*. The question is whether this incremental process generates *pigul*, renders the offering *pasul* without *pigul*, or leaves it entirely *kasher*.

### Chisda/Hamnuna/Sheshes

Three Amoraim — Rav Chisda, Rav Hamnuna, and Rav Sheshes — dispute the outcome. One holds the offering is *pigul*, subjecting one who eats the *shiyarim* to *kares*. A second holds it is *pasul* but not *pigul*. A third holds the *shiyarim* remain entirely *kasher*.

### Rebbi Meir Position

The Gemara attempts to align these three views with known Tannaitic positions. The opinion that the offering is *pigul* maps most naturally onto Rebbi Meir, who holds *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* — that even a *chatzi matir* suffices to generate *pigul*. In this reading, each individual grain of the *kometz* constitutes a *chatzi matir*, and the *pigul* intent accompanying each grain combines to create effective *pigul* over all the small portions of *shiyarim* envisioned.

It should be noted, however, that this is a somewhat unusual application of the concept of *chatzi matir*. The canonical cases of *chatzi matir* involve qualitatively discrete acts — the *kometz* and the *levona* as separate components, the two lambs of *shtei halechem*, perhaps the *kaneh* and *veshet* of animal slaughter. Each is a defined, independent act that together with another forms the complete *matir*. Here, by contrast, the division is purely quantitative: one has simply performed successive portions of the same act until the full *kometz* is consumed. Whether treating each such increment as a *chatzi matir* is conceptually apt is a question the Gemara will press. Nevertheless, this is how the Gemara frames the position at this stage.

### Rabbanan Position

The opinion that the offering is *pasul* — but not *pigul* — maps onto the Rabbanan, who reject *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*. Even so, the Rabbanan hold that an attempt to create *pigul* through a *chatzi matir* renders the *korban* at minimum *pasul* on a Rabbinic level. Thus, while the incremental *haktara* of the *kometz* with fragmented intent cannot achieve full *pigul*, it is sufficient to disqualify the offering.

### Rebbi Position

The opinion that the offering is entirely *kasher* maps onto Rebbi, who ruled earlier (on daf 14) that slaughtering one lamb with intent to eat a half-*kezayis* of one loaf, and then slaughtering the other lamb with intent to eat a half-*kezayis* of the other loaf, is entirely *muttar*. Rebbi holds that separate half-*kezayis* quantities directed at distinct acts do not combine. By analogy, each tiny grain of *kometz* offered with intent toward a tiny increment of *shiyarim* cannot aggregate into a disqualifying *machshava*, and the offering remains *kasher*.

### Conditional Analysis

The Gemara immediately challenges this neat alignment. In each case, one can construct a reading under which the Amora's position could equally well reflect a different Tannaitic view.

Consider Rebbi Meir first. His principle of *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* may apply specifically when the *machshava* accompanying the *chatzi matir* involves a full *kezayis* of *shiyarim* — that is, where the only deficiency is that the *matir* was only half performed, but the intended object was of full halachic significance. Here, however, each grain of *kometz* was paired with an intent concerning only a vanishingly small amount of *shiyarim*. Rebbi Meir might not extend his principle to a case where no single act of *haktara* was accompanied by an intent of sufficient magnitude.

Similarly for the Rabbanan: their rejection of *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* applies where only one of the two *matirim* was performed with *pigul* intent — say, the *kometz* but not the *levona*, or vice versa. Here, however, the *machshava* accompanied every single grain of the *kometz*, meaning the intent encompassed the entire *matir*. This is not a case of *chatzi matir* at all — the *matir* was performed in full, grain by grain. The Rabbanan's concern with *chatzi matir* is therefore irrelevant, and the question becomes a different one entirely: whether the atomized intent, fragmentary in its object rather than its subject, constitutes valid *pigul* intent.

And as for Rebbi: his ruling of *kasher* concerned two separate lambs, each associated with a half-*kezayis* from a *different* loaf. The crucial feature there is that the two half-*kezayis* amounts derived from distinct acts of *shechita* on distinct offerings, with distinct objects. Here, by contrast, it was the same *kometz* throughout, and all the fragmented intentions toward tiny amounts of *shiyarim* arose within a single act of *haktara*. Rebbi might hold that when all the intentions arise from one unified *avodah*, they aggregate, and the offering would be *pasul*.

### All Three Valid

Given that none of the three Amora positions can be pinned definitively to a single Tannaitic source, the Gemara concludes that each of the three outcomes — *pigul*, *pasul*, and *kasher* — could in principle be maintained according to all Tannaitic positions. Rav Adda bar Ahava's framework makes this explicit: whether the result is *pigul*, *pasul*, or *kasher* depends not on which Tanna one follows, but on which empirical and halachic premises one accepts about the nature of this particular act.

### Piggul Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר פִּיגּוּל דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, קָסָבַר: דֶּרֶךְ אֲכִילָה בְּכָךְ, וְדֶרֶךְ הַקְטָרָה בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The one who says** that **all agree** it is ***piggul* maintains** that **the manner of** its **consumption** is **in such** a manner, i.e., in small portions, **and** likewise **the manner of** its **burning is** also **in such** a manner.

The position that the outcome is *pigul* — *divrei hakol* — rests on two combined premises: first, that performing the *haktara* a tiny drop at a time constitutes a *derech haktara*, a valid manner of offering; and second, that intending to eat in corresponding tiny increments constitutes a *derech achila*, a cognizable manner of eating. If both premises hold, then one has performed a valid *haktara* accompanied by a genuine intent to eat the *shiyarim* at the wrong time — the classic formula for *pigul*. The further assumption, which the Gemara leaves implicit, is that all those tiny intentions toward tiny amounts of *shiyarim*, occurring within a single act of *haktara*, coalesce into a single intent of a *kezayis*, the threshold for *pigul*.

### Passul Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר פָּסוּל דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, קָסָבַר: אֵין דֶּרֶךְ אֲכִילָה בְּכָךְ, וְאֵין דֶּרֶךְ הַקְטָרָה בְּכָךְ, וְהָוְיָא לָהּ כְּמִנְחָה שֶׁלֹּא הוּקְטְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who says** that **all agree** it is **disqualified holds** that **the manner of** its **consumption** is **not in such** a manner. Accordingly, this type of *piggul* intent does not render the offering *piggul*. **And the manner of** its **burning** is also **not in such** a manner, **and** consequently **it is like a meal offering** whose handful **was not burned** properly, and is therefore disqualified.

The position that the outcome is *pasul* rests on accepting the first premise while rejecting the second: eating in tiny successive increments — a grain here, a grain there — does not constitute a *derech haktara* in the first place. If offering the *kometz* one sesame-grain at a time is not a valid *haktara* at all, the *korban* is *pasul* because it never underwent proper *haktara*, and no *pigul* can attach. The Gemara notes that such a *minchah* is comparable to one whose *haktara* was never performed. Interestingly, whether the *derech achila* criterion is met becomes irrelevant: once *haktara* fails, there is no valid *pigul* process to speak of.

### Kasher Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, קָסָבַר: דֶּרֶךְ הַקְטָרָה בְּכָךְ, וְאֵין דֶּרֶךְ אֲכִילָה בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** finally, **the one who says** that **all agree** it is **fit holds** that **the manner of** its **burning** is **in such** a manner, and therefore the burning was performed properly, **but the manner of** its **consumption** is **not in such** a manner, which means that the *piggul* intent is inconsequential.

The position that the outcome is *kasher* — again applicable according to all opinions — rests on accepting that doing the *haktara* incrementally is a valid *derech haktara*, while rejecting that incremental eating constitutes a *derech achila*. The *haktara* succeeds and the *korban* is properly offered. But the accompanying intent — to eat a grain here and a grain there in tiny successive amounts — does not meet the definition of *achila* required for *machsheves pigul*. Since the intent does not rise to the level of cognizable *achila*, no *pigul* thought has been formed, and the offering is entirely *kasher*. The question of *chatzi matir* is not germane here at all: the full *matir* was performed, and the thought simply did not constitute a valid *machshava* to eat.

## Haktara Debate

### Pumbedita Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲרִיפֵי דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא: הַקְטָרָה מְפַגֶּלֶת הַקְטָרָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי אֵין מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר – הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּחַשֵּׁב בֵּיהּ בְּשִׁירַיִם וּלְבוֹנָה בְּמִילְּתָא קָיְימָא, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּחַשֵּׁב לַהּ בִּלְבוֹנָה – כְּמָה דְּחַשֵּׁב בֵּיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ מַתִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **the sharp** people in the city **of Pumbedita: Burning renders burning *piggul*,** e.g., burning the handful with the intent to burn the frankincense the next day renders the meal offering *piggul*. **And** this is the *halakha* **even according to the Rabbis, who say** that **one does not render** an offering ***piggul* with** intent occurring during the sacrifice of **half of a permitting factor,** e.g., when sacrificing the handful alone with intent of *piggul*. The reason is that **this statement** applies only **where he had intent during** the sacrifice of the handful to consume **the remainder** the next day, **and** the **frankincense stands intact,** i.e., he had no intent with regard to it. **But here, when he had intent with regard to the frankincense** while burning the handful, **it is considered as though he had intent with regard to the entire permitting factor.**

The daf turns to a distinct but related question. The *charifei d'Pumbedita* — identified by Rashi in Sanhedrin as Eifa and Avimi, the sons of Rechava — maintain that *haktara mefagelet haktara*: that performing the *haktara* of the *kometz* with the intent to offer the *levona* tomorrow constitutes *pigul*.

This claim must contend with two distinct obstacles. The first is *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*: the *kometz* alone is only a *chatzi matir*, and even Rebbi Meir's position applies to *chatzi matir* situations; the Rabbanan reject the very possibility. The second, left implicit at this stage but surfacing shortly, is *ein matir mefagel matir* — a *matir* cannot generate *pigul* in relation to another *matir* — since both the *kometz* and the *levona* are themselves *matirim* for the *shiyarim*.

The *charifei d'Pumbedita* address the first obstacle as follows. The Rabbanan's principle of *ein mefaglin b'chatzi matir* applies when one's intent during the *kometz* concerns the *shiyarim* while the *levona* remains unaddressed — that is, when only half of the *matir* structure is engaged by the *machshava*, and the *levona* stands entirely apart. In that standard case, the intent is genuine only as to the *kometz*, and since the *kometz* is but half the *matir*, it is insufficient. Here, however, the intent accompanying the *haktara* of the *kometz* directly involves the *levona* — the very other half of the *matir*. True, the *levona* appears as the object of the thought rather than its subject. But because the *machshava* encompasses the *levona*, it is as though the entire *matir* has been drawn into the intent. The Gemara acknowledges that this is a *chiddush* — one would ordinarily require that the *machshava* bear upon the full act as its subject — but the *charifei* maintain that since the thought relates to the *haktara* of the *levona*, it implicates the complete *matir* structure and thereby achieves *pigul*.

### Mishna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַקּוֹמֵץ וְנוֹתֵן בִּכְלִי, וְהַמּוֹלִיךְ וְהַמַּקְטִיר, לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר, חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת, חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We learn** this *halakha* in a mishna, **as well** (12a): **This is the principle:** In the case of **anyone who removes the handful, or places** the handful **in the vessel,** or **who conveys** the vessel with the handful to the altar, or **who burns** the handful on the altar, with the intent **to partake of an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **partakes** of it, **or to burn an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **burns** it on the altar, **outside its** designated **area,** the meal offering is **unfit but there is no** liability for ***karet*.** If his intent was to do so **beyond its** designated **time,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* on account of it.**

Rava — or perhaps Abaye — adduces support from an earlier Mishnah. That Mishnah formulates the general principle governing *pigul*: anyone who performs *kmitza*, *netina b'kli*, *holacha*, or *haktara* with intent to eat something *she'darko le'echol* (normally eaten) at the wrong time, or to offer something *she'darko lehaktir* (normally offered) at the wrong time, generates *pigul* — *chutz lizmano pigul v'chayavin alav kares*. Rava argues that when the Mishnah lists *haktara* alongside the earlier three *avodot*, it implies that the intent of *haktara* during the *haktara* stage can encompass the same range as the other stages — including *lehaktir davar she'darko lehaktir*, that is, offering the *levona* at the wrong time. This would vindicate the *charifei d'Pumbedita* that an intent during the *haktara* of the *kometz* to offer the *levona* tomorrow creates *pigul*.

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו הַקְטָרָה דּוּמְיָא דְּהָנָךְ, מָה הָנָךְ – בֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל בֵּין לְהַקְטִיר, אַף הַקְטָרָה – בֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל בֵּין לְהַקְטִיר?
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **What, is it not** correct to say that the *halakha* with regard to **burning** is **similar to these,** i.e., the removal of the handful, its placement in a vessel, and the conveying? Accordingly, **just as** with regard to **these, whether** one's intent was **to partake** of the remainder the next day **or to burn** the frankincense the next day, the *halakha* is that the offering is *piggul*, **so too** with regard to **burning, whether** one's intent was **to partake** of the remainder **or to burn** the frankincense the next day, the offering is *piggul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָנָךְ – בֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל בֵּין לְהַקְטִיר; הַקְטָרָה – לֶאֱכוֹל אִין, לְהַקְטִיר לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: **No,** with regard to **these, whether** one's intent was **to partake** of the remainder **or to burn** the frankincense the next day, the offering is in fact *piggul*. But with regard to **burning,** if one's intent was **to partake** of the remainder the next day, **yes,** it is *piggul*, but if one's intent was **to burn** the frankincense the next day, it is **not** *piggul*.

The Gemara demurs. The Mishnah does not necessarily equate the *haktara* stage with the earlier stages regarding the range of valid *pigul* intentions. In the earlier stages — *kmitza*, *netina b'kli*, *holacha* — the thought could be either to eat the *shiyarim* at the wrong time or to offer the *kometz* at the wrong time, *bein le'echol bein lehaktir*. At the stage of actual *haktara*, however, one might distinguish: the intent to eat the *shiyarim* at the wrong time (*le'echol*), yes, can generate *pigul*; but the intent to offer the *levona* at the wrong time (*lehaktir*) may not, whether because of *ein mefaglin b'chatzi matir* or because of *ein matir mefagel matir*. The Mishnah's inclusion of *haktara* in the general formula does not, of itself, establish that the *haktara* stage can produce *pigul* through an intent directed at the other *matir*.

### Chisda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵב רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר גַּדָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, וְיָתֵב וְקָא אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: אֵין הַקְטָרָה מְפַגֶּלֶת הַקְטָרָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: ״מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר״ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Menashya bar Gadda sat before Abaye, and** while he was **sitting he said in the name of Rav Ḥisda: Burning does not render burning *piggul*,** e.g., burning the handful with the intent to burn the frankincense the next day does not render the offering *piggul*. **And** this is the *halakha* **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** in the mishna that one **renders** an offering ***piggul*** through intent **during** the sacrifice of **half** of **a permitting factor,** e.g., when sacrificing the handful but not the frankincense with intent of *piggul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּחַשֵּׁב בְּהוּ בְּשִׁירַיִם, דְּקוֹמֶץ מַתִּיר דִּידְהוּ, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּקוֹמֶץ לָאו מַתִּיר דִּלְבוֹנָה הוּא – לָא מָצֵי מְפַגֵּל בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Menashya bar Gadda explains that the reason is that **this statement** of Rabbi Meir applies only **where he had intent during** the sacrifice of the handful to consume **the remainder** the next day, **as the handful** is **their permitting factor. But here, as the handful is not a permitting factor of the frankincense,** the offering **cannot be rendered *piggul* through it.**

The opposing view is then stated explicitly. Rav Menashya bar Gadda, seated before Abaye, transmits in the name of Rav Chisda — and this transmission is subsequently confirmed as going back to Rav himself — the categorical ruling: *ein haktara mefagelet haktara*. The *haktara* of the *kometz* cannot generate *pigul* when the accompanying intent is to offer the *levona* tomorrow.

### Non-Relation Logic

The reasoning is compelling. Rav Chisda distinguishes between two types of intent during the *kometz's* *haktara*. When one intends to eat the *shiyarim* at the wrong time, the *pigul* thought is effective — even according to the Rabbanan — precisely because the *kometz* is a *matir* for the *shiyarim*. It stands in a constitutive relationship to the *shiyarim*: the *kometz* is what permits the *shiyarim*, even if only as half the combined *matir*. The *machshava* regarding the *shiyarim* thus flows directly from and attaches to the act of *haktara* of the *kometz*.

But the *kometz* is not a *matir* for the *levona*. Both the *kometz* and the *levona* are jointly *matirim* for the *shiyarim*, but they do not bear any constitutive relationship to one another. The *levona* can be offered before the *kometz*; the *kometz*'s *haktara* does not alter the *levona's* halachic state in any way. They operate in parallel, not in sequence. Since the *kometz* has no bearing on the *levona*, an intent formed during the *haktara* of the *kometz* concerning the *levona* is simply disconnected — the thought has no purchase on the *levona*, and therefore cannot generate *pigul* in relation to it.

### Two Lambs Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: עֲנִי מָרִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב: אֵין הַקְטָרָה מְפַגֶּלֶת הַקְטָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Menashya: **Answer me, my Master,** did Rav Ḥisda state this *halakha* **in the name of Rav?** Rav Menashya **said to** Abaye: **Yes.** The Gemara notes that it **was also stated** explicitly that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav says: Burning does not render burning *piggul*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, שָׁחַט אֶחָד מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ לְמָחָר – הוּא פִּיגּוּל וַחֲבֵירוֹ כָּשֵׁר, לֶאֱכוֹל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְמָחָר – שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּשֵׁרִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּכֵיוָן דְּלָאו מַתִּיר דִּידֵיהּ הוּא, לָא מָצֵי מְפַגֵּל בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ya'akov bar Idi said in the name of Abaye: We learn** in the mishna **as well** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rav: If **one slaughtered one of the lambs** with the intent **to partake of it the next day, that** lamb is ***piggul* and the other** lamb is **fit.** If he slaughtered one lamb with the intent **to partake of the other the next day, both** lambs **are fit. What is the reason?** Is it **not due to the fact that since** the first lamb **is not a permitting factor of** the second lamb, **it cannot render** the second lamb ***piggul*?** The same reasoning should apply to the case of the handful and frankincense.

Abaye finds support for Rav Chisda's position in a Mishnah. If one slaughters one of the two lambs of *shtei halechem* with intent to eat from that lamb tomorrow, that lamb is *pigul* while its companion remains *kasher*. But if one slaughters lamb one with intent to eat from lamb two tomorrow — the thought directed not at the lamb being slaughtered but at the companion — *shneihem kesherim*, both are entirely *kasher*. The reasoning, as Abaye understands it, is precisely Rav Chisda's logic: since lamb one is not a *matir* for lamb two, a *pigul* thought during the slaughter of lamb one that is directed at lamb two is entirely without effect. The two lambs, like the *kometz* and the *levona*, each serve as *matir* for the bread, but they have no constitutive relationship to one another.

### Vessel Unity Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא אִיקְּבַע בְּחַד מָנָא, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִיקְּבַע בְּחַד מָנָא – כִּי חַד דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No;** there is a difference between these cases. **It is** only **there,** in the mishna, that one lamb cannot render the other *piggul*, **as it was not fixed in one vessel** with the other lamb, and therefore each animal stands independent of the other. **But here, as** the handful and frankincense **were fixed in one vessel** for the purpose of offering them, **they are considered like one** item and one of them therefore renders the other *piggul*.

The Gemara, however, resists drawing this conclusion. The cases are distinguishable: the two lambs are never combined into a single vessel. They remain separate entities. In the case of the *minchah*, by contrast, the *kometz* and the *levona* are both placed together in the same *kli* — the vessel that holds the *minchah*. By virtue of this shared vessel, they may be regarded as *kechad damu*, constituting a unified whole. One could therefore argue that the *kometz* does bear a relationship to the *levona* mediated by their joint presence in the *kli*, even if that relationship is not one of *matir* to *matir*. If so, the *lambs* case does not successfully demonstrate that the *kometz* cannot implicate the *levona* through *pigul* intent. The proof from that Mishnah, while suggestive, is not conclusive.

### Hamnuna Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הָא מִילְּתָא אַבְלַע לִי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וּתְקִילָא לִי כְּכוּלֵּיהּ תַּלְמוּדַאי – הִקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ לְהַקְטִיר לְבוֹנָה, וּלְבוֹנָה לֶאֱכוֹל שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר – פִּגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna said: Rabbi Ḥanina helped me internalize this** following **matter, and to me it is equivalent to all** the rest of **my learning,** as it contains a significant novelty: If **one burned** the **handful** with the intent **to burn** the **frankincense** the next day, **and** burned the **frankincense** with the intent **to partake of** the **remainder the next day,** the meal offering is ***piggul*.**

Rav Hamnuna now introduces a teaching he received from Rabbi Chanina, which he treasures so deeply that he likens it to the weight of the entire Talmud. The formulation is this: *hiktir kometz lehaktir levona leechol shirayim lemachar* — one who offers up the *kometz* with the combined intent to offer the *levona* tomorrow and to eat the *shiyarim* tomorrow — the result is *pigul*.

Taken at face value, this ruling incorporates two distinct elements: the intent regarding the *levona* (*haktara mefagelet haktara*) and the intent regarding the *shiyarim* (*mefaglin b'chatzi matir*). Each element can be understood as a separate *chiddush*. The absence of the conjunctive *vav* before *leechol shirayim* suggests that these two intents are being bundled as a single, unified *machshava* rather than presented as two independent propositions.

### Text Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? אִי הַקְטָרָה מְפַגֶּלֶת הַקְטָרָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – לֵימָא: ״הִקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ לְהַקְטִיר לְבוֹנָה״! אִי מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – לֵימָא: ״הִקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ לֶאֱכוֹל שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר״! אִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אֲתָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – לֵימָא: ״הִקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ לְהַקְטִיר לְבוֹנָה וְלֶאֱכוֹל שִׁירַיִם לְמָחָר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is Rabbi Ḥanina **teaching us? If** he **is teaching us** that **burning renders burning *piggul*,** then **let him** simply **say:** If one **burned** the **handful** with the intent **to burn** the **frankincense,** the offering is *piggul*. **If** he **is teaching us** that one **renders** an offering ***piggul*** through intent **during** the sacrifice of **half of a permitting factor,** then **let him say:** If one **burned** the **handful** with the intent **to partake of** the **remainder the next day,** the offering is *piggul*. **If** he **is coming** to **teach us both of** these *halakhot*, **let him say:** If one **burned** the **handful** with the intent **to burn** the **frankincense or to partake of** the **remainder the next day,** the offering is *piggul*.

The Gemara presses the textual structure. If Rabbi Chanina meant to teach the *chiddush* of *haktara mefagelet haktara*, he should simply have stated: *hiktir kometz lehaktir levona lemachar* — the *kometz* was offered with intent to offer the *levona* tomorrow, and that is *pigul*. Why add the *leechol shirayim*? Conversely, if the *chiddush* is *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*, simply state *hiktir kometz leechol shirayim lemachar* — offering the *kometz* with intent to eat the *shiyarim* tomorrow generates *pigul* even though it is only a *chatzi matir*. Why bundle them? And if both *chiddushim* were intended, use the *vav* and state them as two separate principles: doing the *kometz* to be *maktir* the *levona* tomorrow is *pigul* (because of *haktara mefagelet haktara*), and doing the *kometz* for the *shiyarim* tomorrow is *pigul* (because of *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*). The unusual combined formulation, lacking the *vav*, demands explanation.

### Combined Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר אֵין הַקְטָרָה מְפַגֶּלֶת הַקְטָרָה, וְאֵין מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּפָשְׁטָא לַיהּ מַחְשָׁבָה בְּכוּלַּהּ מִנְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Actually,** Rabbi Ḥanina **holds** that **burning does not render burning *piggul*,** and therefore if one burned only the handful with the intent to burn the frankincense the next day, the offering is not *piggul*. **And** he also holds that one **does not render** an offering ***piggul*** through intent **during** the sacrifice of **half of a permitting factor,** and consequently if one burned only the handful with the intent to partake of the remainder the next day, the offering is not *piggul* either. **But** it **is different here, as intent** of *piggul* has **extended over the entire meal offering,** as he had intent of *piggul* during the burning of the handful with regard to the frankincense and during the burning of the frankincense with regard to the remainder.

Rav Adda bar Ahava provides the key. He reads Rabbi Chanina's teaching as making precisely the opposite point from what the *charifei d'Pumbedita* assumed. In truth, *ein haktara mefagelet haktara* — the intent during the *kometz* to offer the *levona* tomorrow does not, by itself, generate *pigul*. And *ein mefaglin b'chatzi matir* — the intent during the *kometz* to eat the *shiyarim* tomorrow does not, by itself, generate *pigul* either. Each principle, standing alone, fails to achieve *pigul*: the first because the *kometz* and *levona* do not stand in a *matir* relationship to one another; the second because one has performed only a *chatzi matir*.

The *chiddush* of Rabbi Chanina's formulation is that when both intents are present simultaneously — the intent to offer the *levona* tomorrow and the intent to eat the *shiyarim* tomorrow — they combine in a way that neither could achieve alone, and the result is *pigul*. The Gemara expresses this by saying that the *machshava* spreads throughout (*pashta lei machshava bekulei mincha*). The most coherent reading of this principle is that the combined intent effectively draws together both halves of the *matir*. The intent regarding the *shiyarim* overcomes the *chatzi matir* problem by being supplemented through the intent regarding the *levona* — since that intent brings the *levona* into view, the act of *haktara* of the *kometz* now relates, at least in the sphere of *machshava*, to the full *matir* structure, both halves. With the full *matir* thus engaged, the intent toward the *shiyarim* at the wrong time achieves *pigul*.

### Textual Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבָּא: הִקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ לֶאֱכוֹל שִׁירַיִם – לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל פִּגּוּל. וְהָא מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Yitzḥak bar Abba:** If **one burned** the **handful** with the intent **to partake of** the **remainder** the next day, **everyone agrees** that the meal offering is ***piggul*.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But don't** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree** in the mishna with regard to this very case? **Rather, say** that the *baraita* states: **Everyone agrees** that the offering is **disqualified,** as although the Rabbis hold that such intent does not render an offering *piggul*, they concede that it disqualifies the offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא: הֲרֵי זֶה פִּגּוּל, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא! תַּנָּא ״דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל״ אַתְנְיוּהּ, ״פִּיגּוּל״ בְּ״פָסוּל״ מִיחַלַּף לֵיהּ, ״הֲרֵי זֶה״ בְּ״דִבְרֵי הַכֹּל״ לָא מִיחַלַּף לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** if one must emend the *baraita*, **let him say** that the *baraita* states: **It is *piggul*, and** that **it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** The Gemara responds: It is reasonable that the ***tanna* taught** that **everyone agrees,** and that he accidentally **exchanged** the word ***piggul* for disqualified.** But **he would not confuse** the phrase: **This is** the opinion of Rabbi Meir, **with** the phrase: **Everyone agrees.**

The discussion concludes with a brief textual difficulty. A *tanna* recited before Rav Yitzchak bar Abba that offering the *kometz* with intent to eat the *shiyarim* tomorrow is *pigul divrei hakol*. The Gemara objects: this is precisely the debate of Rebbi Meir and the Chachamim. The formulation must be corrected to *pasul divrei hakol* — universally invalid — since all agree it is at minimum disqualified, even as the Rabbanan would stop short of full *pigul*. The Gemara further notes that one cannot simply substitute the phrase *harei zeh pigul* for *divrei hakol pigul*, preserving the term *pigul* while attributing it to one opinion. The textual corruption is intelligible in only one direction: a *tanna* who was originally taught *divrei hakol pasul* might plausibly have substituted the similar-sounding *pigul* for *pasul*, but one taught *harei zeh pigul* would not have garbled that phrase into *divrei hakol*. With that, the tractate's first chapter, *Ha-Kometz et ha-Mincha*, concludes.

## Wrong Category

### Rebbi: Kasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקּוֹמֵץ אֶת הַמִּנְחָה לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר – כָּשֵׁר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who removes a handful from the meal offering** with the intent **to consume**, beyond its designated time, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **not consume** it, i.e., the handful, **or to burn,** beyond its designated time, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **not burn** it on the altar, i.e., the remainder of the meal offering, the meal offering is **fit. Rabbi Eliezer deems** it **unfit,** although it is not *piggul*, and consuming it is therefore not punishable by excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*].

The new chapter opens with its own *Ha-Kometz et ha-Mincha*, presenting the following law: if one performs the *kmitza* of the *minchah* with the intent to eat something *she'ein darko le'echol* — something not normally eaten by a person in the context of this *korban* — or to offer something *she'ein darko lehaktir* — something not normally placed on the *mizbe'ach* — the offering is *kasher*.

The two examples are immediately apparent from the structure of the *minchah*. The *levona* is placed on the *mizbe'ach* but not eaten; the *shiyarim* are eaten by the priests but not offered on the altar. If one performs the *kmitza* intending to eat the *levona* tomorrow — a substance that the *korban* assigns to the *mizbe'ach*, not to human consumption — this intent, though misdirected, does not generate *pigul* and the *minchah* is *kasher*. Likewise, if one intends to offer the *shiyarim* on the *mizbe'ach* tomorrow — an act directly contrary to the *shiyarim*'s assigned role — that too does not constitute *machsheves pigul*, and the *minchah* is *kasher*. The operative principle is that *machsheves pigul* requires an intent to do at the wrong time precisely what is normally done: eat what is normally eaten, or offer what is normally offered. Crossing the categories — intending to eat what belongs on the *mizbe'ach*, or to offer what belongs to the priest — removes the thought from the domain of *pigul* entirely.

### Eliezer: Passul

Rebbi Eliezer disagrees. He holds that such category-crossing intent, while not generating *pigul* in the full sense, nonetheless renders the offering *pasul*.

### Sub-Kezayis Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת – כָּשֵׁר. לֶאֱכוֹל כַּחֲצִי זַיִת וּלְהַקְטִיר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁאֵין אֲכִילָה וְהַקְטָרָה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of one who removes a handful from the meal offering with the intent **to consume**, beyond its designated time, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **consume** it, **or to burn,** beyond its designated time, **an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one does **burn** it on the altar, but his intent was to consume or burn improperly **less than an olive-bulk,** the offering is **fit.** If his intent was both **to consume half an olive-bulk and to burn half an olive-bulk** beyond its designated time, the meal offering is nevertheless **fit, because eating and burning do not join together.**

Immediately, however, the Mishnah qualifies Rebbi Eliezer's ruling. If one intended to eat something *she'darko le'echol* or to offer something *she'darko lehaktir* — the normal categories, no crossing — but the quantity envisioned was less than a *kezayis*, the offering is *kasher* even according to Rebbi Eliezer. The *kezayis* threshold is sine qua non for disqualification.

Moreover, and distinct from the category-crossing cases: if one intended to eat a half-*kezayis* and to offer a half-*kezayis* — that is, combining an *achila* intent with a *haktara* intent, each below the threshold on its own — these two do not combine (*ein achila v'haktara mitztarfin*). The *achila* component and the *haktara* component are categorically distinct forms of consumption and cannot be aggregated to reach the *kezayis* required for disqualification.

Rebbi Eliezer's ruling thus applies to category-crossing with a full *kezayis*: intending to eat the *levona* — a *kezayis* of something that belongs on the *mizbe'ach* — or to offer a *kezayis* of the *shiyarim* — something normally eaten. Below a *kezayis*, and in cases of mixed-category combinations that fall short, Rebbi Eliezer agrees the offering is *kasher*.

### Pasuk Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אָמַר קְרָא ״וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אֶחָד אֲכִילַת אָדָם וְאֶחָד אֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ, לוֹמַר לָךְ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּחַשְּׁבִין בַּאֲכִילַת אָדָם, כָּךְ מְחַשְּׁבִין בַּאֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What is the reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer** that the intention to consume, beyond its designated time, an item that is not usually consumed renders the meal offering unfit? **The verse states** with regard to consuming an offering after its designated time: **"And if any of the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings is at all consumed [*he'akhol ye'akhel*]"** (Leviticus 7:18), repeating for emphasis the term for consumption. He derives from the repeated term that **the verse is speaking of two** types of **consumption: One** is **the consumption** of the offering **by a person,** e.g., by the priests or the owner of the offering, **and** the other **one is the consumption** of the sacrificial portions **by** their being burned on the **altar.** This serves **to tell you** that **just as** one's improper **intention with regard to the consumption of a person** renders the offering unfit, **so too,** one's improper **intention with regard to the consumption of** the **altar** renders the offering unfit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמְּחַשְּׁבִין מֵאֲכִילַת אָדָם לַאֲכִילַת אָדָם, וּמֵאֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ לַאֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ, כָּךְ מְחַשְּׁבִין מֵאֲכִילַת אָדָם לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וּמֵאֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ לָאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, this serves to tell you that **just as** one's improper **intention** that a portion of the offering designated for **consumption by a person** will be **consumed** on the following day **by a person** renders the offering unfit, **and** one's improper **intention** that a portion of the offering designated for **consumption of** the **altar** will be **consumed** on the following day **by** the **altar** renders the offering unfit, **so too,** one's improper **intention** that a portion of the offering designated for **consumption by a person** will be **consumed on** the **altar** renders the offering unfit, **and** one's improper intention that a portion of the offering designated for **consumption of** the **altar** will be **consumed** on the following day **by a person** renders the offering unfit.

Rabbi Assi, transmitting in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, addresses the exegetical foundation of Rebbi Eliezer's position. The *pasuk* that serves as the primary source for the laws of *pigul* reads: *v'im he'achol ye'achel mibesar zevach shelamav* — "and if it will surely be eaten from the flesh of his *shelamim*." The doubled expression — *he'achol ye'achel*, the infinitive absolute followed by the main verb — is the source from which the Gemara elsewhere derives the principle that intention (*machshava*) operates like action. The verse speaks of eating, but the Sages read it as applying to intent to eat at the wrong time.

The further question is what the doubled language teaches. The Gemara establishes that *bishtei achilot hakatuv medaber* — the verse speaks of two types of consumption: *achilat adam*, human eating, and *achilat mizbe'ach*, the altar's "consumption" through burning. The basic derivation is that just as *pigul* applies when one intends for a human to eat at the wrong time (*achilat adam*), so too *pigul* applies when one intends for the *mizbe'ach* to "eat" — i.e., to burn the *korban's* designated portions — at the wrong time (*achilat mizbe'ach*). This is uncontroversial; all agree to this derivation.

### Eliezer's Reading

Rebbi Eliezer goes further. He derives from the doubled language that the two categories of *achila* — *achilat adam* and *achilat mizbe'ach* — work across one another. Just as *pigul* applies to intra-category crossing (*me-achilat adam le-achilat adam*, *me-achilat mizbe'ach le-achilat mizbe'ach*), so too, Rebbi Eliezer reads, *pigul* — or at least some form of invalidity — applies to inter-category crossing: *me-achilat adam le-mizbe'ach* (intending to offer on the altar something normally eaten by a person) and *me-achilat mizbe'ach le-adam* (intending to feed to a person something normally burned on the altar). The doubled but slightly varied language (*he'achol ye'achel*) is, in Rebbi Eliezer's reading, the Torah's signal that a *chiddush* is embedded: the two types of *achila* can be crossed.

It must be noted that this exegetical move is counterintuitive. One might expect that a change in language signals distinction rather than equivalence — that had the verse used identical language (*he'achol he'achol*), one might conclude the categories are the same, whereas the variation implies they are separate domains. Yet this is a recognized Talmudic interpretive technique: the change of language is read as pointing to an additional *chiddush*, namely the cross-category application, rather than as marking a categorical boundary.

### Pasuk Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? מִדְּאַפְּקִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לְהַקְטָרָה בִּלְשׁוֹן אֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this derivation? It is derived **from** the fact **that the Merciful One expresses** the **burning** of the offering **using the language of consumption.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי דְּאַפְּקִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן אֲכִילָה, דְּלָא שְׁנָא כִּי מְחַשֵּׁב בִּלְשׁוֹן אֲכִילָה לְמִזְבֵּחַ, וְלָא שְׁנָא כִּי מְחַשֵּׁב בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקְטָרָה לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer, maintain the following: **This** fact **that the Merciful One expresses** the burning of the offering **using the language of consumption** demonstrates **that there is no difference if one expresses his intention using the language of: Consumption on** the **altar, and there is no difference if one expresses his intention using the language of: Burning on** the **altar.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, מָה אֲכִילָה בִּכְזַיִת – אַף הַקְטָרָה בִּכְזַיִת, וּלְעוֹלָם אֲכִילָה דְּאוֹרְחָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the doubled expression serves to teach that **just as** one renders the offering *piggul* only when one's intention involves the **consumption of an olive-bulk,** as this is the minimal measure for an act to be considered eating, **so too,** one renders the offering *piggul* only when one's intention involves the **burning of an olive-bulk. But actually,** the expression for **consumption** found in the verse **indicates** consuming it **in the usual manner,** and therefore an offering is rendered unfit only if one's improper intention involved consuming an item that is usually consumed, or burning an item that is usually burned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אִם הֵאָכֹל הֵאָכֹל״, אִי נָמֵי ״אִם יֵאָכֵל יֵאָכֵל״, מַאי ״הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what would **Rabbi Eliezer** respond? He would say that **if that were so,** that the verse intends to teach only that *halakha*, **let the Merciful One write** either: **If *he'akhol he'akhol*, or: If *ye'akhel ye'akhel*,** repeating the same form of the word twice. **What** is the reason that the verse states **"*he'akhol ye'akhel*,"** employing both repetition and variation? **Learn from** this formulation **two** *halakhot*. One, as the Rabbis explain, is that the offering is rendered unfit whether one uses an expression of consumption or an expression of burning, provided that one's intention is with regard to at least an olive-bulk. The second is that the offering is rendered unfit if one intends to burn on the altar an item that is usually consumed by a person, or to consume an item that is usually burned on the altar.

The Rabbanan read the same doubled language differently. For them, *dafkei rachmana lehaktara belashon achila* — the reason the Torah expresses *haktara* in the language of eating — is not to enable cross-category *pigul*, but to convey that the principles governing *achilat adam* apply equally to *achilat mizbe'ach*. Specifically: just as *achilat adam* requires a *kezayis* to generate *pigul*, so too *achilat mizbe'ach* requires a *kezayis*. Alternatively, the equivalence teaches that whether your intent is *I will have it eaten* or *I will have it burned* — both construed as forms of *achila* — either intent in the wrong time generates *pigul*. But the categories themselves remain distinct: *achilat adam* refers to what a human normally eats (*shiyarim*), and *achilat mizbe'ach* to what the *mizbe'ach* normally "eats" (*kometz*, *levona*). The doubled language does not sanction the import of one type of intent into the domain properly assigned to the other.

Rebbi Eliezer's counterargument is that if all the Torah wished to convey was that *achilat mizbe'ach* follows the rules of *achilat adam*, it could have written *im ye'achel ye'achel* or some equivalent uniform expression. The specific variation *he'achol ye'achel* must be generating an additional *chiddush*, which is the cross-category application.

The *machloket* between Rebbi Eliezer and the Rabbanan thus reflects a fundamental dispute over whether the verse's doubled language opens the categories to cross-application, or merely equates the operational rules governing each category when applied within its proper domain.

## Animal Sacrifice Cases

### D'Oraita vs Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַב אַסִּי: וְאִי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִשּׁוּם הָכִי הוּא, כָּרֵת נָמֵי לִיחַיַּיב? וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָא אַתְּ הוּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁאֵין עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rav Asi: But if the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer is due to that** derivation, and he understands that the verse equates the improper intent to consume an item that is usually consumed with the improper intent to consume an item that is usually burned, then **let one also be liable to** receive ***karet*** for consuming an offering brought with intention to consume, after its designated time, the part of the offering that is burned, or for intention to burn, after its designated time, an item that is usually consumed. Why does Rabbi Eliezer state only that the offering is rendered unfit? **And if you would say** that **indeed,** Rabbi Eliezer does hold that one who consumes such an offering is liable to receive *karet*, that is difficult: **But aren't you the one who said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Rabbi Eliezer concedes that** doing so is **not punishable by *karet*?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: פְּסוּלָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: פְּסוּלָה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi **said to him: It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im* as to** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer. There is one who says** that Rabbi Eliezer deems the offering to be **unfit by Torah law** and one is liable to receive *karet*. It was in accordance with this opinion that Rabbi Yoḥanan cited the proof from the verse. **And there is one who says** that Rabbi Eliezer deems the offering to be **unfit by rabbinic law,** and it was in accordance with this opinion that Rabbi Yoḥanan said that according to Rabbi Eliezer there is no punishment of *karet* for this transgression.

Having established Rebbi Eliezer's position and its *pasuk* basis, the Gemara notes — through a dialogue between Rabbi Zeira and Rav Assi — that there is a *machloket* among the *Tannaim* themselves regarding the nature of Rebbi Eliezer's ruling. Rabbi Yochanan had stated that Rebbi Eliezer concedes there is no *kares*. Yet the exegetical derivation from *he'achol ye'achel* implies a *d'Oraita* prohibition that would seem to yield *pigul* and hence *kares*. Rav Assi acknowledges the tension and resolves it: this very question — whether Rebbi Eliezer holds his ruling is *d'Oraita* (amounting to *pigul* with *kares*) or merely *d'Rabbanan* (a Rabbinic *pasul*) — is itself disputed among the *Tannaim*. The *braita* that follows is brought to illustrate this Tannaitic disagreement.

### Drink/Burn Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַזֶּבַח לִשְׁתּוֹת מִדָּמוֹ לְמָחָר, לְהַקְטִיר מִבְּשָׂרוֹ לְמָחָר, לֶאֱכוֹל מֵאֵימוּרָיו לְמָחָר – כָּשֵׁר, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל. לְהַנִּיחַ מִדָּמוֹ לְמָחָר – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַף בָּזוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who slaughters the offering** with the intention **to drink** some **of its blood,** which is designated to be presented on the altar, **on the next day,** or **to burn** some **of its meat,** which is meant to be eaten, **on the next day,** or **to eat** some **of its sacrificial portions,** which are designated to be burned on the altar, **on the next day,** the offering is **fit,** as his intention is either to eat an item that is usually sacrificed on the altar, or to burn on the altar an item that is usually eaten. **But Rabbi Eliezer deems** the offering **unfit.** If one slaughters the offering with the intention **to leave** some **of its blood for the next day,** but not to present it or consume it, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the offering **unfit. Rabbi Elazar said: Even in this** case **Rabbi Eliezer deems** the offering **unfit, and the Rabbis deem** it **fit.**

The *braita* examines analogous category-crossing cases in animal sacrifices. If one slaughtered a *korban* with intent to drink from its blood tomorrow — blood is placed on the *mizbe'ach*, so this is intending for a human to "eat" what belongs to the altar — or with intent to burn the flesh on the *mizbe'ach* tomorrow — flesh of a *shelamim* is eaten by people, so this is intending for the *mizbe'ach* to "eat" what belongs to humans — in both cases, the Chachamim hold the *korban kasher*. Category-crossing intent of this type has no *halachic* consequence in their view, consistent with the Mishnah's ruling.

Rebbi Eliezer disagrees and renders both cases *pasul*, consistent with his position in the *minchah* context. Whether this *pasul* is *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan* is precisely what is in dispute.

### Lehaniach Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן – הַשְׁתָּא, וּמָה הָתָם דְּקָא מְחַשֵּׁב בִּלְשׁוֹן אֲכִילָה מַכְשְׁרִי רַבָּנַן, הָכָא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **In accordance with whose** opinion is the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda** that the offering is unfit even if he intends only to leave the blood for the next day, but not present it or consume it? **If we say** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis, now** consider: **And if there, where** the priest **expresses his intention using the language of consumption, the Rabbis** nevertheless **deem** the offering **fit,** despite the fact that if he had used this expression with regard to the portion burned on the altar, the offering would be *piggul*, is it **not all the more so** the case that **here,** when he intends only to leave the blood until the next day, the offering should be fit?

The *braita* then introduces an additional case: intent to leave the blood over — *lehaniach midamo l'machar* — neither drinking it nor placing it on the *mizbe'ach*, but simply allowing it to remain and become *notar*. This is categorically distinct from the previous cases. *Pigul* is defined entirely in terms of eating or *haktara*, whether by a human or the altar. Intending to leave something over belongs to the domain of *notar*, not *pigul*. No Amora or Tanna would describe *lehaniach* as an instance of *machsheves pigul* in the classical sense.

### Yehuda's Distinction

Rabbi Yehuda holds that in the *lehaniach* case, the offering is *pasul*. Critically, however, he does not frame this as part of the debate between Rebbi Eliezer and the Chachamim. For Rabbi Yehuda, the Rebbi Eliezer / Chachamim debate concerns category-crossing *achila* — intent to eat what belongs to the altar, or to burn what belongs to the priest. That debate, Rabbi Yehuda implies, involves *pigul* or something very close to it; it is about the *d'Oraita* structure of *machsheves pigul* and whether category-crossing falls within it. The *lehaniach* case, by contrast, is a separate matter: it is simply a Rabbinic *pasul*, grounded not in the logic of *pigul* at all, but in the impropriety of forming any intent to mishandle the *korban's* components.

### Elazar's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַף בְּזוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that Rabbi Yehuda's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer. And** yet the *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Elazar said: Even in this** case **Rabbi Eliezer deems** the offering **unfit, and the Rabbis deem** it **fit.**

Rabbi Elazar disagrees. He states that the *lehaniach* case is equally governed by the dispute between Rebbi Eliezer and the Chachamim — *af b'zo Rabbi Eliezer posel v'Chachamim machshirin*. By assimilating *lehaniach* into the Rebbi Eliezer / Chachamim debate, Rabbi Elazar implies something significant about how he reads that debate. Since *lehaniach* is self-evidently not *pigul* in any traditional sense, and since Rabbi Elazar is saying it is governed by the same dispute, his reading must be that the entire dispute of Rebbi Eliezer and the Chachamim — including the category-crossing cases — is not about *d'Oraita* *pigul*, but about a Rabbinic *pasul*. If even *lehaniach* falls under the same debate, and *lehaniach* is at most a Rabbinic disqualification, then all of Rebbi Eliezer's rulings in this area must be Rabbinic in nature.

### Unresolved Status

The Gemara identifies the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar as reflecting precisely this underlying question. Rabbi Yehuda reads the Rebbi Eliezer / Chachamim debate as concerning *d'Oraita pigul* (or something approaching it) in category-crossing cases, with the *lehaniach* case being a separate, lesser Rabbinic disqualification. Rabbi Elazar reads the entire cluster — category-crossing and *lehaniach* alike — as involving nothing more than a Rabbinic *pasul*, and so the debate of Rebbi Eliezer and the Chachamim applies uniformly to all.

The Gemara signals that this reading of the *braita* is not the only possible one, and that the daf will continue to examine whether Rabbi Elazar's assimilation of *lehaniach* into the core debate necessarily implies a Rabbinic character for all of Rebbi Eliezer's rulings. That analysis will carry over into the following daf.
