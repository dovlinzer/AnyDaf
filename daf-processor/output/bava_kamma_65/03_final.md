# Bava Kamma 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kefel vs. Keren Payment

### Rav's Principle Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַב: קֶרֶן – כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב. תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself. Rav says:** When a thief makes his payments, the **principal** is paid **according to** the value as of the time **when he stole** it, whereas the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** are calculated **according to** the value at **the time of standing trial.**

The daf begins at the bottom of 65a with the statement of Rav, which will occupy much of today's discussion. The governing question is: when do we assess the value of the object for purposes of payment — at the time of the theft or at the time of the court case?

The distinction turns on the nature of the obligation. *Kefel* — the double payment — together with the four-and-five payments for slaughtering or selling a stolen ox or sheep, is a *knas*, a court-imposed financial penalty. It is not a natural debt that accrues automatically; it requires the court to impose it. Indeed, if the thief admits to the theft, he is exempt from the *knas* entirely. Rav therefore rules that the *kefel* and four-and-five payments are assessed based on the value of the object at the time the court adjudicates the case, whereas the *keren* — the principal — is assessed based on the time of the theft, since that is when the fundamental liability was incurred.

### Limitations on Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁמֵינָה וְהִכְחִישָׁהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב! הָתָם נָמֵי – מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ כּוּלַּהּ, מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ פַּלְגָא. כִּי קָאָמַר רַב – בְּיוּקְרָא וְזוּלָא הוּא דְּקָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Come** and **hear** another *baraita* that apparently contradicts Rav's ruling. If the animal was stolen when it was **fat and the thief caused it to become lean, he pays** the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment according to** the value of the animal as of the time **when he stole** it. The double and fourfold and fivefold payments are not paid in accordance with the value at the time of the trial, as claimed by Rav. The Gemara answers: **There too,** there is a reason for this exception, **because we say of** the thief: **What is it to me** if he **killed** the animal **entirely,** and **what is it to me** if **he killed it partially?** By causing the animal to become lean, the thief is considered to have started to put it to death. Consequently, when he ultimately kills the animal, he pays the fourfold or fivefold payment according to its value when he first began to weaken it, i.e., at the time of the theft. **When Rav says** his ruling, **he is saying** it only **with regard to** cases of **appreciation** in value **and depreciation** in value, not to changes in the animal's physical state.

The Gemara had already introduced certain qualifications on Rav's ruling. If the animal became fatter or thinner during the period of the theft, this does not shift the baseline — the thief neither benefits from an increase he contributed nor bears additional liability for a decrease in market value he did not cause. What Rav is addressing is a straightforward change in market value. If the thief slaughtered or sold the animal — both acts which independently trigger additional liability — then the principal payment for that subsequent act is assessed at the higher value, since a new debt is being incurred at that moment. But for a simple holding of the object without slaughter or sale, the split applies: the *keren* is assessed at the time of theft, and the *kefel* at the time of the court case, whether the value rose or fell in the interim.

## Rabbi Chanina's Baraita

### False Oath Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא לְסַיּוֹעֵיהּ לְרַב: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁטָּעַן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְהוֹדָה, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים – אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ עֵדִים הוֹדָה, מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם; וְאִם מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים הוֹדָה – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְאָשָׁם, וְחוּמְשׁוֹ עוֹלֶה לוֹ בִּכְפֵילוֹ; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina teaches** a *baraita* **in support of Rav:** In the case of **a homeowner** acting as a bailee, **who** falsely **stated the claim** that **a thief** stole the **deposit, and** subsequently **took an oath** as to the truth of his claim, **and** then **admitted** that he was lying and that he in fact took the item himself, **and** at some point **witnesses came** and testified that the bailee himself had the item, **if he admitted** his guilt **before the witnesses came** and testified, **he pays** the **principal and** an additional **one-fifth, and** he must also bring **a guilt-offering. But if he admitted** his guilt **after witnesses came** and testified, **he pays** the **double payment and** brings **a guilt-offering, and his** additional **one-fifth is covered by his double payment** that he pays; **this is the statement of Rabbi Ya'akov.**

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* of Rabbi Chanina brought specifically to support Rav's ruling. The case involves a *shomer* — a bailee — who, when asked about an object entrusted to his care, claims it was stolen (*ta'anat gannav*) and swears to that effect. This is the precise scenario that occupied the opening *dappim* of this chapter. Such a false oath creates two distinct and overlapping liabilities: on the one hand, if witnesses later establish that the object was not stolen but was kept by the *shomer*, he would owe *kefel*, since a *ta'anat gannav* with a false oath triggers the double-payment penalty when witnesses contradict it. On the other hand, the act of falsely swearing to keep another's property and then admitting to it triggers the obligation to bring an *asham gezeilot* — the special sin offering for theft through oath — and to pay back the principal plus a *chomesh*, a fifth.

The critical asymmetry is this: *kefel* is triggered by witnesses; the *korban* and *chomesh* are triggered by admission. The *baraita* now distinguishes three scenarios based on the timing of each.

### Admission Before Witnesses…

If the bailee admitted to his false oath before witnesses arrived, he is exempt from *kefel* — since admission to a *knas* exempts one entirely, and crucially, according to this *baraita*, this exemption persists even if witnesses subsequently arrive. He pays only the *keren*, brings the *asham*, and adds the *chomesh*. The *kefel* is simply off the table.

### Admission After Witnesses

If witnesses arrived first and the bailee admitted afterward, both liabilities are now active. The witnesses established the *kefel* before the admission could forestall it, and the subsequent admission — even if it cannot affect the *kefel* — still carries weight for purposes of the *asham* and *chomesh*. There is something striking here: one might have thought that an admission made only after witnesses have already exposed the wrongdoing should count for nothing. Yet the tradition recognizes that the act of admitting guilt retains moral and legal significance even at that stage, for the purposes of *kapparah* and *teshuvah*.

### Oath Then Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: ״בְּרֹאשׁוֹ וַחֲמִשִׁתָיו״; מָמוֹן הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּם בְּרֹאשׁ – מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, מָמוֹן שֶׁאֵין מִשְׁתַּלֵּם בְּרֹאשׁ – אֵין מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: אֵין חוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם מִשְׁתַּלֵּם בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ כֶּפֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** It is written concerning a bailee who falsely claims innocence with regard to a deposit entrusted to him: "He shall restore it **according to its principal, and** shall add **its fifth part** to it" (Leviticus 5:24). This verse teaches that with regard to **monetary restitution that is paid** precisely **according to the principal, one adds one-fifth,** but for **monetary restitution that is not paid** precisely **according to the principal,** as is the case here, where the bailee pays double the principal value, **one does not add one-fifth. Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** Neither the additional **one-fifth nor** the **guilt-offering is paid in a case where there is double payment.** This concludes the *baraita*.

The third case introduces a three-way dispute. Where witnesses came and the bailee later admitted, he pays *kefel* and brings the *asham*. The question is whether he also separately pays the *chomesh*. Rabbi Yaakov holds that the *chomesh* is subsumed within the *kefel* payment — conceptually, one counts toward the other. The *chachamim* disagree: their position, which they derive from the phrase *b'rosho v'chamishito* ("its principal and its fifth"), is that the *chomesh* obligation applies only when the base payment is the *keren* — the principal. When one's primary payment is *kefel*, no *chomesh* is added. The practical result for Rabbi Yaakov and the sages may be similar — no *chomesh* payment is made independently — but their reasoning differs: Rabbi Yaakov says it is conceptually absorbed into the *kefel*, while the sages say there is simply no obligation of *chomesh* in a *kefel* case. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai goes further still: not only is there no *chomesh* when the payment begins with *kefel* rather than *keren*, but there is no *asham korban* either.

## Chomesh in Kefel Debate

### Constant Value Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: חוּמְשׁוֹ עוֹלֶה לוֹ בִּכְפֵילוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה, חוּמְשׁוֹ עוֹלֶה לוֹ בִּכְפֵילוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina explains: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches: His** payment of the additional **one-fifth is covered by his double payment;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Ya'akov. What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that** the stolen item was **initially worth four** dinars **and ultimately,** at the time of trial, was still **worth four** dinars, how could he say: **His** payment of the additional **one-fifth is covered by his double payment?**

The Gemara now turns to interrogate Rabbi Yaakov's position. The notion that the *chomesh* can be said to be subsumed within the *kefel* presupposes that the two amounts are somehow equivalent or identifiable. But if the value of the object remained constant — say it was worth four throughout — then the *kefel* is four (the extra amount beyond the principal) and the *chomesh* is only one (a fifth of five, or equivalently a quarter of the principal). One cannot plausibly say that the one-*zuz chomesh* is subsumed within the four-*zuz kefel*, since they are manifestly distinct amounts.

### Value Decrease Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּפֵילָא אַרְבְּעָה, וְחוּמְשָׁא זוּזָא! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה, וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא, דִּכְפֵילָא זוּזָא וְחוּמְשֵׁיהּ זוּזָא?
>
> **Translation:** This statement is inaccurate in this case, as **the double payment,** i.e., the penalty included in the double payment, is **four** dinars, **and the** additional **one-fifth is one dinar.** When the Torah states "fifth" it means one-fifth of the total payment of the one-fifth and the principal together, i.e., one-quarter of the principal. For the additional one-fifth to be covered by the double payment, the two must be exactly equal, which is not the case here. **Rather, is it not** referring to a situation where **initially** it was **worth four** dinars **and at the end** it was **worth one dinar, as the** penalty component of the **double payment** is **one dinar and the** additional **one-fifth** is also **one dinar?**

The Gemara resolves this by proposing a case in which the value of the object decreased between the time of the theft and the time of the court case. If it was worth four when stolen but only one by the time of adjudication, then — on Rav's ruling that *kefel* is assessed at the time of the court case — the *kefel* would be only one *zuz*, while the *chomesh* is also one *zuz* (a fifth of the principal of four, since the *chomesh* is based on the principal). Now the two amounts are equal, and it is intelligible to say the *kefel* and the *chomesh* overlap. This, then, is simultaneously a demonstration of how Rabbi Yaakov's position works and a support for Rav: the only way to construct a case where *kefel* and *chomesh* are the same amount is if *kefel* is assessed at the later, lower value — exactly as Rav holds.

### Rava's Multiple Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קֶרֶן כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב, תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין! אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה וְהַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא: כְּפֵילָא אַרְבְּעָה וְחוּמְשֵׁיהּ זוּזָא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע וְחָזַר וְנִשְׁבַּע אַרְבַּע פְּעָמִים, וְהוֹדָה; וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה ״וַחֲמִשִׁתָיו״ –
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently,** the **principal,** as well as the additional one-fifth, is paid **according to** the value as of the time **when he stole,** whereas the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** is **according to** the value at **the time of standing trial,** as claimed by Rav. The Gemara refutes this analysis. **Rava said: Actually,** the *baraita* could be referring to a case in which **initially** it was **worth four** dinars **and now,** at the time of trial, it is **also worth four** dinars. **And** as for **what is difficult** with this case, namely that **the double payment** is **four** dinars **and the** additional **one-fifth** is **one dinar,** this problem can be resolved as follows: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where** the bailee **took an oath** that the deposit was stolen from him, **and he again took** the same **oath,** and so on **four times, and** after each oath **he admitted** afterward that he had lied. **And the Torah said: "And** shall add **its fifth part [*ḥamishitav*]** to it" (Leviticus 5:24).

Rava objects that this need not be a proof for Rav at all. He proposes that the case can be read even where the value remained at four throughout. How? If the bailee took four separate oaths — pressed to swear repeatedly by the owner — then upon admission he would owe a *chomesh* for each oath separately. The verse uses the plural *chamishisav* — "its fifths" — indicating that multiple *chomeshim* can accrue on a single principal. In such a case, four *chomeshim* of one *zuz* each would total four *zuzim*, equaling the *kefel* of four. Hence Rabbi Yaakov's position can be explained without any change in the object's value and without reference to Rav's principle. The apparent proof for Rav is therefore dissolved.

### Principal Triggers Chomesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַתּוֹרָה רִיבְּתָה חֲמִישִׁיּוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּקֶרֶן אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: The term *ḥamishitav* is in the plural, which indicates that **the Torah included** the possibility of **many** payments of an additional **one-fifth for a single principal.** In other words, each time the bailee takes a false oath he becomes obligated to pay an additional one-fifth, despite the fact that each oath concerns the same item. Since he did so four times, and the one-fifth is actually one-quarter of the principal, the total amount of payments of the additional one-fifth is equal to the principal, which is the same as the penalty component of the double payment.

The upshot of the exchange is that all opinions agree: the *chomesh* obligation is triggered only when the base payment is the *keren*, not the *kefel*. Whether the *kefel* "subsumes" the *chomesh* (Rabbi Yaakov) or the *chomesh* obligation simply does not arise in a *kefel* case (the *chachamim*), the practical result is that no additional *chomesh* is paid.

### Asham Korban Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: ״בְּרֹאשׁוֹ וַחֲמִשִׁתָיו״ – מָמוֹן הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּם בְּרֹאשׁ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, מָמוֹן שֶׁאֵין מִשְׁתַּלֵּם בְּרֹאשׁ אֵין מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ. אֲבָל אָשָׁם – מַיְיתֵי,
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **And the Sages say:** "He shall restore it **according to its principal, and** shall add **its fifth part** to it" (Leviticus 5:24). This verse teaches that for **monetary restitution that is paid** precisely **according to the principal, one adds one-fifth.** But for **monetary restitution that is not paid** precisely **according to the principal, one does not add one-fifth.** The Gemara infers: He does not add one-fifth, **but he** is obligated to **bring a guilt-offering.**

The debate between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai concerns the *asham korban*. Both hold that no *chomesh* is paid when the primary liability is *kefel*. The *chachamim* derive this from *b'rosho v'chamishito* — the *chomesh* is linked to paying the base principal. If, however, the *asham* is mentioned in the very next verse — *v'es ashamo yavi la'Hashem* — one might have thought the *asham* retains its independence even in a *kefel* case. The *chachamim* read the intervening text as severing the connection: the *asham* in the following verse stands on its own and is not similarly conditioned on the payment being a *keren*. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai disagrees, arguing that the word *v'et* reconnects the *asham* to the preceding conditions, so that just as the *chomesh* applies only when one begins with a base *keren* payment, so too the *asham* is brought only in such a case. The *chachamim* counter that *et* serves to demarcate rather than connect — the word separates the monetary payment owed to a person from the *korban* brought to Heaven, since it would be inappropriate to speak of both in a single breath. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, for his part, holds that *et* was necessary precisely to make that separation explicit, but that the substantive conditionality — beginning with *keren* — still governs both.

## Kinyanei Gneivah Models

### Two Models Explained

Having concluded the discussion of *kefel*, *chomesh*, and the *asham*, the Gemara now pivots to a topic that will occupy much of the remainder of the chapter: the various ways in which a thief acquires ownership of a stolen object. This framework of *kinyanei gneivah* is worth introducing with some conceptual clarity before examining the specific cases.

There are two distinct theoretical models for how a thief can take legal possession of something that was not given to him. The first model treats it as an affirmative act of *kinyan*: just as a normal buyer acquires through *meshichah* or *hagbahah*, a thief who performs some sufficiently transformative act on the object is understood to have forcibly taken possession of it, turning it from something belonging to the original owner into something that is now his. The act of physical change is itself the mechanism of acquisition.

The second model is grounded not in an act of taking but in the removal of an obligation to return. The Torah states *v'heshiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* — that one must return the stolen object as it was. If the object is no longer in its original state, the thief is no longer obligated to return the object itself; he need only pay its monetary value. According to this model, it is not that the thief actively acquires the object — rather, once his obligation to return it falls away, he naturally retains it, since he already had some measure of possession from the moment of the theft.

### Shinuy Ma'aseh

The clearest and most uncontested form of *kinyan* through transformation is *shinuy ma'aseh* — a physical act that changes the object. The thief steals wood and fashions it into a box; he steals wool and weaves it into a garment. This kind of transformation satisfies both conceptual models simultaneously: the thief has actively done something to make the object his own, and the object is now so different that the Torah's obligation to return "what was stolen" no longer applies to this new thing. *Shinuy ma'aseh* thus has the strongest claim to effect acquisition.

### Shinuy Hashem

*Shinuy hashem* — a change of name — refers to a natural transformation that the thief did not actively cause. A lamb grows into a ram; a calf becomes an ox. This differs from *shinuy ma'aseh* in a critical respect: no act of the thief produced the change. The object evolved on its own. One might argue that this still results in a new object with a new name — and therefore triggers acquisition — but one might equally argue that since nothing was done to it, it is simply the same thing at a later stage of its natural development. This is precisely the debate that will be explored below.

### Yeush Concept

*Yeiush* — the owner's despair of ever recovering the lost or stolen object — represents yet another model. When an owner gives up hope, his psychological and practical connection to the object is severed. If the thief continues to hold the object after *yeiush* occurs, there is a tradition that acquisition takes effect. This is more akin to the first model of an act of *kinyan* — not a transformation of the object, but a combination of the owner's abandonment and the thief's continued possession. The important question, however, is whether *yeiush* by itself is sufficient, or whether some additional element is required.

### Yeush & Shinuy Reshut

A final category combines *yeiush* with *shinuy reshut* — a change of domain. The thief steals an object, the owner despairs, and then the thief transfers the object to a third party. From that third party's perspective, the object came into his possession after *yeiush* had already occurred, and this combination — *yeiush* followed by a genuine change of hands — is widely accepted as effecting acquisition. The dynamics of each of these models will now be examined through the cases that follow.

## Calf-to-Ox Case

### Rabbi Ila's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: גָּנַב טָלֶה וְנַעֲשָׂה אַיִל, עֵגֶל וְנַעֲשָׂה שׁוֹר – נַעֲשָׂה שִׁינּוּי בְּיָדוֹ, וּקְנָאוֹ. טָבַח וּמָכַר – שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ, שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses other cases in which an object undergoes a change after it has been stolen. **Rabbi Ile'a says:** If one **stole a lamb and it** subsequently **became a ram,** or if he stole **a calf and it** subsequently **became a bull,** the stolen item **has undergone a change** while **in** the thief's **possession, and he has** therefore **acquired it** as his own property. Consequently, his obligation of restitution consists of monetary payment rather than giving back the stolen item itself. If he subsequently **slaughtered or sold** the animal, it is in effect **his own** animal that **he slaughters,** or it is **his own** animal that **he sells,** and he is not obligated in the fourfold or fivefold payment.

Rabbi Ila states: if one stole a lamb and it became a ram, or a calf and it became an ox, a *shinuy* has occurred in the thief's possession and he has thereby acquired it. The consequence is significant: if the thief then slaughters or sells the animal, he is doing so with something that already belongs to him. Therefore, he would not be liable to pay four or five, since the four-and-five penalty applies only to slaughtering or selling a *stolen* animal — one that still belongs to its original owner.

### Baraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא לְרַבִּי אִילְעָא: גָּנַב טָלֶה וְנַעֲשָׂה אַיִל, עֵגֶל וְנַעֲשָׂה שׁוֹר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קַנְיֵיהּ בְּשִׁינּוּי, אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם? שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ, שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina raised an objection to Rabbi Ile'a** from a *baraita*: If one **stole a lamb and it** subsequently **became a ram,** of if he stole **a calf and it** subsequently **became a bull, he pays** the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment according to** the animal's value as of the time **when he stole** it. **And if it enters your mind** that in a case of this kind the thief **has acquired** the animal as his own property **by** virtue of the physical **change** the animal underwent when it matured, **why does he pay** the fourfold or fivefold payment? After all, it is **his own** animal that **he slaughters,** or it is **his own** animal that **he sells.**

Rabbi Chanina challenges this from a *baraita*: if one stole a lamb and it became a ram, or a calf and it became an ox, one pays *kefel* and the four-and-five payment as of the time of the theft. This *baraita* poses a double difficulty. First, it contradicts Rav, since it assesses the payment at the time of theft rather than at the time of standing in court. Second, and more directly relevant here, it contradicts Rabbi Ila — for if the thief had already acquired the animal when it changed, why is he paying four or five at all? You cannot be penalized for slaughtering your own property.

### Rabbi Ila's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי הַשְׁתָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא מְשַׁלֵּם – מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״תּוֹרָא גְּנַבִי מִמָּךְ?״ ״דִּיכְרָא גְּנַבִי מִמְּךָ?״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַחֲמָנָא נַיצְּלַן מֵהַאי דַּעְתָּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה, רַחֲמָנָא נַיצְּלַן מִדַּעְתָּא דִּידָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: This is the reason that he does not pay in accordance with** the animal's value **now:** It is **because** the thief can **say to** the animal's owner: **Did I steal a bull from you,** or: **Did I steal a ram from you?** No; I stole only a calf or a lamb, and therefore I will pay you the value of the animal when I stole it. Rabbi Ile'a **said to** Rabbi Ḥanina: **May the Merciful One save us from this opinion** of yours! Rabbi Ḥanina **said** back **to him: On the contrary, may the Merciful One save us from your opinion!**

Rabbi Ila responds: even accepting the *baraita*, ask yourself — what does *shinuy hashem* accomplish in this case? He argues that the natural change is indeed *not* sufficient for the thief to acquire the object; it happened by itself, without any act on the thief's part, and therefore does not constitute the kind of transformation that effects acquisition. However — and this is Rabbi Ila's crucial move — the very same naturalness that prevents acquisition also prevents the *kefel* and four-and-five from being assessed based on the *current* value of the now-grown animal. For the thief can say: did I steal an ox from you? Did I steal a ram from you? No — I stole a calf, I stole a lamb. Just as the Gemara noted earlier that the thief does not pay *kefel* for the added value from fattening an animal (since he did not steal that added value), here too the thief pays *kefel* based on the value of what was actually stolen. The *kefel* is a double of the stolen object — and what was stolen was a calf, not a cow. Hence the *baraita* says you pay based on the time of the theft, meaning based on the object as it was when stolen, not based on the current transformed animal.

### Mutual Rejection

The exchange ends in a striking way. Rabbi Chanina said to Rabbi Ila: *Rachmana litzlan me'hai da'ata* — "Heaven save us from such an opinion." Rabbi Ila responded in kind: *addaraba, Rachmana litzlan mida'ata didach* — "On the contrary, Heaven save us from *your* opinion." This kind of direct and symmetrical rhetorical rejection is unusual. The underlying logic of each rejection, however, is intelligible. From one side: if you concede that the animal has become sufficiently different that you will not pay four or five based on its current form — acknowledging it as a distinct object — then you should equally concede that the thief has acquired it. From the other side: recognizing an object as different enough to anchor the calculation of the penalty does not mean the thief has performed a *kinyan* on it; a natural change he did not cause cannot simultaneously exempt him from returning the object and from the penalty. Both positions remain coherent, and the Gemara treats the impasse as genuine.

## Rabbi Zeira Challenge

### New Name Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: וְנִיקְנִינְהוּ בְּשִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira objects to this: But** even if an animal's natural growth is not considered a physical change, **let the thief acquire it through** its **change in name,** i.e., its change of classification, as the animal was originally called a calf or a lamb and now it is considered a bull or ram.

Rabbi Zeira now presses from a different angle. Granted that the transformation from calf to ox happened naturally — does it not at least constitute a *shinuy hashem*, a change of name sufficient to effect acquisition? Even if one argues that the thief's lack of active involvement prevents the change from functioning like *shinuy ma'aseh*, the simple fact that the animal is now called by a different name — no longer a *egel* but a *shor*, no longer a *taleh* but an *ayil* — might independently suffice.

### Rava's Name Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שׁוֹר בֶּן יוֹמוֹ קָרוּי ״שׁוֹר״, אַיִל בֶּן יוֹמוֹ קָרוּי ״אַיִל״. שׁוֹר בֶּן יוֹמוֹ קָרוּי ״שׁוֹר״ – דִּכְתִיב: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב אוֹ עֵז כִּי יִוָּלֵד״. אַיִל בֶּן יוֹמוֹ קָרוּי ״אַיִל״ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֵילֵי צֹאנְךָ לֹא אָכָלְתִּי״ – אֵילִים הוּא דְּלָא אֲכַל, כְּבָשִׂים אֲכַל?! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַיִל בֶּן יוֹמוֹ ״קָרוּי״ אַיִל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** There is in fact no change in name here, as even **a day-old bull is called a bull,** and even **a day-old ram is called a ram. A day-old bull is called a bull, as it is written: "When a bull, or a sheep, or a goat is born"** (Leviticus 22:27). **A day-old ram is called a ram,** as can be derived from Jacob's statement to Laban, **as it is written: "And the rams of your flock I have not eaten"** (Genesis 31:38). Now, did Jacob mean that **he did not eat** any of Laban's **rams,** but younger **lambs he did eat?** This is certainly not the meaning of this verse, as this would mean he was a thief. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** this verse that **a day-old ram is called a ram?**

Rava addresses this by challenging the premise that there has been a genuine change of name at all. He cites two prooftexts. First, the verse *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled* — "an ox, a sheep, or a goat when it is born" — indicates that an animal is called *shor* from the moment of birth. Second, from *ve'eilei tzonecha lo achalti* — Yaakov's statement to Lavan that he did not eat his *eilim* — Rava infers that *ayil* likewise applies from birth, for surely Yaakov did not mean he refrained from eating rams but helped himself to lambs. Thus, a one-day-old ox is already called an ox, and a one-day-old ram is already called a ram. The calf and the ox share the same categorical name from birth; the growth from young to mature does not introduce a genuinely new name. Without a new name, there is no *shinuy hashem*.

### Contradiction Remains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָא מַנִּי – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: שִׁינּוּי בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד(ת), וְלָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **In any case,** the *baraita* cited earlier by Rabbi Ḥanina **is difficult** for the opinion of Rabbi Ile'a, as it states that the thief must pay the fourfold or fivefold payment despite the fact that the slaughter or sale of the animal took place after it matured from a calf to a bull or from a lamb to a ram. To resolve the difficulty, **Rav Sheshet said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say:** An item, even if it has undergone a physical **change, remains in its place,** i.e., a stolen item remains in the possession of its owner, **and** a thief **does not acquire** it even if it undergoes a change.

Yet this answer, while resolving the *shinuy hashem* question in principle, does not dissolve the tension with the *baraita*. Even after Rava's response, the contradiction against Rabbi Ila remains: the *baraita* says one pays four or five even after the animal has grown, which — on Rabbi Ila's view that natural change effects acquisition — should never happen. The Gemara notes: *mikol makom kashya* — regardless of Rava's point about names, the contradiction to Rabbi Ila stands.

## Rav Sheshet Resolution

### Beit Shammai Position

Rav Sheshet resolves the difficulty by identifying the author of the *baraita*. The *baraita* that imposes four-and-five liability even after the animal changes reflects the position of Beit Shammai, who hold *shinuy bimkomo omedes* — a change stays in its place. Beit Shammai simply do not recognize transformation as creating a new object. For them, the changed object is still, legally and conceptually, the original object. Therefore, there is no acquisition through *shinuy*, and the animal remains the owner's property regardless of its growth, requiring the full payment.

### Prostitute Fee Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: נָתַן לָהּ בְּאֶתְנַנָּהּ חִיטִּין וַעֲשָׂאָן סוֹלֶת, זֵיתִים וַעֲשָׂאָן שֶׁמֶן, עֲנָבִים וַעֲשָׂאָן יַיִן; תָּנֵי חֲדָא אָסוּר, וְתָנֵי חֲדָא מוּתָּר. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּנֵי גּוּרְיוֹן דְּמֵאַסְפּוֹרַק: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹסְרִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **If one gave wheat** to a prostitute **for her payment,** i.e., the hire of her services, **and** she **made** the wheat into **flour;** or if he gave her **olives and** she **made them** into **oil;** or if he gave her **grapes and** she **made them** into **wine, it is taught** in **one** *baraita* that it is **prohibited** to bring these products as an offering in the Temple, in accordance with the verse: "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot…into the house of the Lord your God for any vow" (Deuteronomy 23:19). **And it is taught** in **one** *baraita* that these products are **permitted,** as the physical change renders them into new items. **And Rav Yosef says** that **Guryon of Asporak teaches** in a *baraita*: **Beit Shammai prohibit** these products **and Beit Hillel permit** them. If so, these two *baraitot* reflect a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.

Where do we find that Beit Shammai hold this principle? The Gemara turns to a *baraita* concerning *etnan zonah* — the fee given to a prostitute — which cannot be brought as a *korban*. The question is whether this prohibition applies to a transformed version of what was given. If one gave wheat as the fee and she ground it into flour, or gave olives and she made them into oil, or grapes made into wine — is the transformed product still forbidden? One *baraita* rules it forbidden, another permits it. Rav Yosef cites a tradition that this is precisely the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel: Beit Shammai forbid the transformed product; Beit Hillel permit it.

### Beit Shammai's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? דִּכְתִיב: ״גַּם״ – לְרַבּוֹת שִׁינּוּיֵיהֶם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל – ״הֵם״ וְלֹא שִׁינּוּיֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the source of these two opinions. **What is the reason of Beit Shammai** for prohibiting these products? **As it is written:** "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot, or the price of a dog, into the house of the Lord your God for any vow; for even both of these are an abomination to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19). The apparently superfluous word **"even"** serves **to include their changed** status. **And Beit Hillel,** who permit these items after they have undergone a physical change, maintain that the term **"these"** in the phrase "both of these" teaches that this prohibition applies only to the original items, **but not** to **their changed** form.

Beit Shammai derive their position from the word *gam* in the verse: *ki so'avat Hashem Elokecha gam sheneihem* — "for they are both an abomination before your God." The word *gam* serves to expand the prohibition and includes the transformed versions of these objects — *le-rabbos shinuiyyehem*, to encompass their changed forms. What was given retains its forbidden status through any transformation.

### Beit Hillel's Counter

Beit Hillel read the same verse differently. The operative word is *hem* — "they" — embedded in *sheneihem*: it is *they* that are an abomination, *velo shinuiyyehem* — but not their transformed versions. The changed object is a different object and is no longer included in the prohibition. Additionally, *hem velo veladoseihem* — the prohibition does not extend to offspring, since offspring too are a different category. Thus, Beit Hillel derives two exclusions from *hem*: transformed products are permitted, and offspring are permitted.

The word *gam*, which seems to favor Beit Shammai, poses a difficulty for Beit Hillel. The Gemara acknowledges: *gam le-Veit Hillel kashya* — that word is a problem for Beit Hillel's reading.

## Foundation Principles

### Changed Object Principle

Before the question of *kinyan gneivah* can even be addressed, there is a prior and more fundamental question: are we willing to look at a transformed object as a new object at all? This is the conceptual threshold that must be crossed before discussing whether a thief acquires through *shinuy*. If the answer is no — if we insist the object, even after transformation, is still the original object — then there is no basis on which to speak of a new entity that the thief might acquire.

### Recognition Framework

This is precisely the divide between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai, by refusing to recognize a transformed object as new, eliminate the entire discussion of *kinyan* through *shinuy* at the threshold. There is nothing to acquire; there is only the original object, still in its original legal identity, regardless of what has been done to it. Beit Hillel, by contrast, acknowledge the transformed object as a genuinely new entity. That recognition is the first step — once a transformation is acknowledged as producing something new, then one can proceed to ask whether a *ganav* has acquired it.

It is important to note, as one might fairly observe, that Beit Shammai's underlying position in the *etnan* debate need not have been intended as a universal principle about all transformations throughout *halacha*. The *baraita* is about a specific prohibition, and the *gam* could have been interpreted narrowly. It is the Gemara that reads from Beit Shammai's ruling a broader conceptual stance — namely, that they never recognize an object as genuinely new after transformation. Whether that is what Beit Shammai originally intended is a separate question, but it is the Gemara's reading, and it is what makes Rav Sheshet's resolution work: the *baraita* requiring payment of four and five even after the animal's growth is Beit Shammai's, who refuse to see the grown animal as anything other than the original stolen animal.

## Rava's Identity Answer

### Four-Word Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: קֶרֶן – כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב מְשַׁלֵּם, תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין? אָמַר רָבָא: טְלָאִים – כִּדְמֵעִיקָּרָא, דָּמִים – כְּשֶׁל עַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that this *baraita* constitutes **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav? As Rav says:** When a thief makes his payments, **he pays** the **principal according to** the value as of the time **when he stole** it, whereas the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** are calculated **according to** the value at **the time of standing trial. Rava said** in resolution of this difficulty: If one stole **lambs** that subsequently became rams or were fattened, he pays the double payment and the fourfold or fivefold payment according to the **initial** state of the animal, as claimed by both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. If there was a change in the animal's value due to fluctuation of the monetary **value** of animals, he pays **according to** the animal's worth **now,** at the time of standing trial.

The Gemara now returns to the direct challenge to Rav from the *baraita* that says *kefel* and four-and-five are paid *ke-sha'at hagneivah* — as of the time of the theft — which seems to contradict Rav's view that *kefel* is assessed at the time the case is adjudicated. Rava offers a terse resolution: *sheit kide'mei ikara, damim k'shel achshav* — the object is assessed according to its original identity, but the monetary value is assessed as of now.

### Tosafot's Explanation

Tosafot explains this answer by invoking the principle that already emerged in the exchange between Rabbi Ila and Rabbi Chanina. The *kefel* and four-and-five are always multiples of the *object that was stolen* — not of whatever object happens to be present at the time of the court case. If a calf was stolen and it has since grown into an ox, the four-and-five is four or five times the calf, not the ox. That is what *ke-sha'at hagneivah* means: you are paying based on the identity of the stolen object. However, once we have established that we are paying for a calf, we can still ask: at what price do we value a calf — the price when it was stolen or today's market price for calves? Here, Rav's principle applies: the monetary valuation of the *kefel* is assessed at the time of the court case. Thus, *ke-sha'at hagneivah* addresses the identity of the object — what unit are you multiplying — while *ke-sha'at amida badin* addresses the market price assigned to that unit.

### Rashi's Alternative

Rashi offers a somewhat different reading. Rather than a question about whether the object has changed, he frames the issue as a question about the units of payment. If the thief wishes to repay in kind — returning the same type of object he stole — then the payment is assessed at the original value. But if he wishes to pay in cash, the assessment is at today's market price. The conceptual foundation is similar — is one paying back an object or a monetary obligation? — though the application differs. Tosafot's explanation has the advantage of connecting most naturally to the reasoning already developed in the Gemara, and it makes the most intuitive sense given the cases examined: you owe four times the sheep you stole, not four times the ram now standing before the court.

## Shinuy Acquisition Proof

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: שִׁינּוּי קוֹנֶה – כְּתִיבָא וּתְנֵינָא. כְּתִיבָא – ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵלָה אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״? אִם כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּזַל – יַחֲזִיר, וְאִם לָאו – דָּמִים בְּעָלְמָא בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba said:** The principle that **a change** in a stolen item causes the thief to **acquire** it **is written** in the Torah, **and we learned** it in a mishna as well. **It is written** in the Torah: "Then it shall be, if he has sinned, and is guilty, **that he shall restore the item that he robbed"** (Leviticus 5:23). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** the seemingly superfluous phrase **"that he robbed"?** This serves to teach that **if** the item is the same **as** it was **when he stole** it, **he must return** the stolen item itself. **But if** it is **not** the same as it was then, **he is required** to **pay only money,** while the stolen item remains his to keep.

Rabbah now steps back to establish the very foundation of *kinyan* through *shinuy* — that it is supported both by a biblical source and by Tannaitic tradition. The Torah states *v'heshiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* — one must return the stolen object as it was stolen. The phrase *asher gazal* is interpreted to mean: you must return it *in the way in which you stole it*, i.e., in the same condition. If the object is *ke-ein shegazal* — in the same form — then you return the object itself. But if it is no longer in that form — because the thief has transformed it — then the obligation to return the object falls away, and the thief pays money but keeps the transformed object. The object has, in effect, become his.

### Mishnaic Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵינָא – הַגּוֹזֵל עֵצִים וַעֲשָׂאָן כֵּלִים, צֶמֶר וַעֲשָׂאָן בְּגָדִים – מְשַׁלֵּם כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** this *halakha* in a mishna as well, as it is taught (93b): In the case of **one who robs** another of **wood and fashions it** into **vessels,** or one who robs another of **wool and fashions it** into **garments, he pays** the robbery victim **according to** the value of the goods at the **time of the robbery,** and keeps the altered materials for himself.

The Talmudic tradition supports this as well. A later *mishnah* in the tractate states: if one stole wood and fashioned it into vessels, or stole wool and wove it into garments, he pays the value of what he stole — *ke-sha'at hagezeilah* — but he does not return the now-finished product. The owner receives the monetary value of the raw materials; the thief retains the box, the garment, the completed object. This is the practical expression of *kinyan* through *shinuy ma'aseh*.

### Dyeing Wool Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי – לֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיתְּנוֹ לוֹ עַד שֶׁצְּבָעוֹ, פָּטוּר. אַלְמָא שִׁינּוּי קוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** another mishna can serve as the source for this principle (*Ḥullin* 135a): **If one did not manage to give** the first of the sheared wool of his sheep to a priest **until** after **he dyed it** (see Deuteronomy 18:4), he is **exempt** from giving it to the priest. This mishna indicates that an item that has undergone a change is no longer considered the same item as it had been. **Apparently,** a physical **change** in a stolen item causes the thief to **acquire** it.

An additional source is drawn from a different area of *halacha* entirely. When one shears his flock, he is obligated to give *reishit hagez* — the first of the shearings — to a *kohen*. If one sheared the requisite amount and thereby incurred the obligation but then dyed the wool before transferring it, he is exempt from giving it to the *kohen* — *patur*. The dyed wool is no longer considered the same object. This is presented as further evidence that *shinuy* effects acquisition, or at minimum that transformation severs the object from its prior legal status. One might note that the degree of transformation here — dyeing and presumably processing raw wool into marketable colored threads — is not trivial, and likely encompasses more than a mere change of color.

## Yeush Acquisition Status

### Yeush Status Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵאוּשׁ – אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן דְּנִיקְנֵי, מִיהוּ לָא יָדְעִינַן אִי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אִי דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § Rabba continues to discuss the ways in which a thief acquires ownership of a stolen item: With regard to the owner's **despair** over ever recovering the stolen item, **the Sages say that** this too causes the thief to **acquire** the stolen item. **However, we do not know if** this *halakha* applies **by Torah law or by rabbinic law.**

Rabbah acknowledges that *yeiush* works — there is a rabbinic tradition to that effect — but he is uncertain whether its basis is biblical or rabbinic. The question matters greatly for the strength of the acquisition and its downstream consequences.

### Lost Object Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מִידֵּי דַּהֲוָה אַמּוֹצֵא אֲבֵידָה; מוֹצֵא אֲבֵידָה לָאו כֵּיוָן דְּמִיָּיאַשׁ מָרַהּ מִינַּהּ מִקַּמֵּי דְּתֵיתֵי לִידֵיהּ, קָנֵי לֵיהּ? הַאי נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּמְיָיאֵשׁ מָרַהּ, קָנֵי לֵיהּ. אַלְמָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba elaborates on the two possibilities he has just mentioned: **If** it applies **by Torah law,** this is **just as it is with regard to one who finds a lost item.** Is it **not** the case with regard to **one who finds a lost item** that **once** the **owner** of the item **despairs** of recovering it, **before** the item **came into** the finder's **possession,** the finder **acquires it** and may keep it for himself? The same principle applies to **this** thief **as well: Once the owner** of the item **despairs** of recovering it, the thief **acquires it** for himself and need no longer return it. **Apparently,** then, the despair of the owner causes the thief to **acquire** the stolen item.

The natural comparison is to *motzei aveidah* — finding a lost object. If an owner loses an object and despairs of recovering it, and a finder then picks it up, the finder is permitted to keep it. The reasoning is that the owner's *yeiush* severs whatever remained of his psychological and practical connection to the object. His ownership, already weakened by the object's having left his physical control, is finally dissolved when he mentally abandons it as well. At that point, the object is available for the finder to take into his possession.

### Key Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא דָּמְיָא לַאֲבֵידָה, אֲבֵידָה הוּא דְּכִי אֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ – בְּהֶתֵּירָא אֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ; אֲבָל הַאי, כֵּיוָן דִּבְאִיסּוּרָא אֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ – מִדְּרַבָּנַן הוּא;
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** the case of a stolen item is **not comparable to** the case **of a lost item,** as **it is** only with regard to **a lost item** that the owner's despair enables the finder to acquire the item, because the item **came into his possession in a permitted** manner. **But** in **this** case of the thief, **since** the item **came into his possession in a prohibited** manner, this case cannot be derived from the *halakha* of a lost item. If so, the ruling that despair effects acquisition in a case of theft applies not by Torah law but **by rabbinic law.**

The obvious difference between *aveidah* and *gneivah* is the timing of the *yeiush* relative to when the object entered the second party's possession. By a lost object, the *yeiush* must occur *before* the finder picks it up. Once a finder has taken the object with the intent to return it, he holds it on the owner's behalf — it is, as it were, back in the owner's domain through the finder's custodial role. *Yeiush* that occurs *after* this point does not help the finder, because the finder's holding is not adverse to the owner's interest; it is in service of it. The analogy offered is striking: if someone believed their wallet was lost on the street and gave up hope, yet all along it was sitting in their own backyard — and then someone walked into the backyard and picked it up, pointing to the *yeiush* — that would obviously not suffice, because the object was effectively within the owner's sphere all along.

### Yeush Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן נִיקְנֵי, מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַשָּׁבִים.
>
> **Translation:** Why would this *halakha* have been instituted by rabbinic law? **As, the Sages said that** a thief **should acquire** the stolen item in this manner, **due to an ordinance** instituted **for the penitent.** To encourage thieves to repent and repay their victims, the Sages instituted that they need not return the stolen item after the owner despairs of recovering it. Rather, they can reimburse the owner for the monetary value of the item.

The case of the thief is structurally different. From the moment the thief takes the object, every second of his holding is actively adverse to the owner's interest. He is not a custodian; he is an adversary. Therefore, when the owner subsequently has *yeiush* — gives up hope of recovering the object from this active adversary — the situation is not comparable to *yeiush* occurring while a finder holds it benevolently. In that sense, one might argue that *yeiush* by a thief is effective even though it occurs after the taking, because the thief's possession has always been a form of drawing the object away from the owner rather than preserving it for him.

Alternatively, the Gemara proposes, perhaps *yeiush* by a thief is only rabbinic — not because the logic of *yeiush* fails, but as a considered policy decision. The Rabbis allowed the thief to keep the object and merely pay its value, as a *takkanat hashavim*, an enactment to encourage those who have stolen to come forward and make restitution. If the law required returning the actual object no matter what, thieves would be reluctant to confess, since by doing so they would lose whatever they had made of the stolen goods. By allowing the thief to keep the transformed or long-held object and pay the original value, the Rabbis made the path of repentance easier.

## Rav Yosef's Position

### Yeush Alone Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: יֵאוּשׁ אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef says: Despair** on the part of the owner over the possibility of recovering his item **does not** cause the thief to **acquire** that item at all, **and** this is the *halakha* **even by rabbinic law.**

Rav Yosef disagrees with Rabbah's entire framing. In his view, *yeiush* by itself does not effect acquisition — not even rabbinically. The thief who has stolen an object and whose owner subsequently despairs must still return the original object if it remains intact; he has not acquired it through *yeiush* alone.

### Yeush With Transfer

Rav Yosef disagrees with Rabbah's entire framing. In his view, *yeiush* by itself does not effect acquisition — not even rabbinically. The thief who has stolen an object and whose owner subsequently despairs must still return the original object if it remains intact; he has not acquired it through *yeiush* alone.

What *does* work, according to Rav Yosef, is *yeiush v'shinuy reshut* — *yeiush* combined with a transfer to a third party. If the thief steals the object, the owner has *yeiush*, and then the thief sells or gives the object to someone else, *that* third party acquires it fully. From the third party's perspective, the object came into his hands after *yeiush* had already occurred — analogous to the finder who picks up a lost object after the owner has despaired. The acquisition is clean. This is the position accepted by most *rishonim* as the normative ruling.

### Thief's Prior Possession

The logic is that the thief's pre-*yeiush* possession prevents *yeiush* alone from severing the owner's claim. The thief came into possession of the object through an act of violation — *be'issura ata l'yadei* — and that tainted beginning is not cleansed simply by the owner's subsequent despair. The *yeiush* occurred too late, in the wrong sequence, to do the work it does by a lost object. Only when the object moves to a third party who received it after *yeiush* does the acquisition take hold.
