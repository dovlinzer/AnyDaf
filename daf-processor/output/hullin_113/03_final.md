# Hullin 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Salting & Taste Transfer

### Previous Case & Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ מִדִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מָלִיחַ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּרוֹתֵחַ, וְכָבוּשׁ הֲרֵי הוּא כִּמְבוּשָּׁל!
>
> **Translation:** **But let** Rava **say to** him the same ruling by instead citing the seemingly more relevant statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said: A salted** food imparts its flavor **like a boiling** food, **and a marinated** food **is** as absorbent **as a cooked** food. Clearly, the kosher meat absorbed flavor from the meat of the *tereifa* as it would have had they been cooked together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִדִּשְׁמוּאֵל, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי – דָּמָן, אֲבָל צִירָן וְרוֹטְבָן – לָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Had** Rava based his ruling only **on Shmuel's** statement, **I would say** in response: **This statement** applies only to the absorption of **the blood of** the meat, **but** kosher meat is **not** prohibited if it absorbs only **the juices and gravy of** the meat of the *tereifa*. Since in this case the meat is salted in a perforated vessel, the blood from each piece runs out and is not absorbed by the other, and one might think that the kosher meat remains permitted. Rava's interpretation of the verse in Leviticus **teaches us** that the juices and gravy of the meat of the *tereifa* must also be taken into account.

The Gemara on 113a opens by returning to the previous discussion concerning the salting of *kosher* and non-*kosher* meat together. By way of review: when *kosher* meat is salted, the blood runs off in a slippery fashion — *dam mishrak sharik* — and does not get absorbed into adjacent pieces of *kosher* meat being salted alongside it. However, when *kosher* meat is salted together with fish or with non-*kosher* meat, the concern is not blood per se but rather the juices (*tzir verotav*) of the non-*kosher* item, which can be absorbed into the *kosher* one.

The Gemara now asks: when the Gemara sought to prove that salting *kosher* and non-*kosher* meat together presents a problem, why did it not simply invoke Shmuel's well-known principle — *maliach harei hu kerose'ach*, that salted food is like roasted food, and *kavush harei hu kemevushal*, that pickled food is like cooked food? If salted is like roasted, presumably Shmuel's statement was addressing precisely such a scenario, in which salting two items together causes a transfer of taste. Why not cite Shmuel directly?

The answer the Gemara provides is that Shmuel's principle alone would be insufficient. *Ee mi-Shmuel, hava amina hani mili daman* — from Shmuel alone, one would have concluded that his statement refers only to blood. That is, Shmuel tells us that salting causes transfer of taste, but the paradigm case he addressed was blood — for instance, meat sitting in a vessel without a hole, saturated in its own blood, or blood being absorbed into fish. His principle establishes the *mechanism* of transfer (salting is like roasting) but says nothing about whether the *juices* of non-*kosher* meat are themselves forbidden. One might have thought that *aval tziran verotvan lo* — the juices and brine of non-*kosher* meat would not be halachically prohibited. It therefore required the independent verse — *hatme'im* — to establish that those juices are indeed forbidden. The two sources thus complement one another: Shmuel establishes that salting transfers taste, and the *braita* derived from *hatme'im* establishes that the juices of non-*kosher* meat are subject to the prohibition.

Tosafot address an implicit question: given that the *braita* is a *midrash halacha* and thus *de'oraisa*, why would one prefer Shmuel's teaching over it? Tosafot answer that each source contributes a distinct component of the equation. The *braita* tells us that the juices are forbidden but does not establish that salting is a mode of taste transfer. Shmuel establishes the transfer mechanism. The Gemara's point is therefore that the *braita* alone is not a self-sufficient proof — one needs both.

### Salting vs. Heat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: דָּג טָהוֹר שֶׁמְּלָחוֹ עִם דָּג טָמֵא – מוּתָּר. מַאי לָאו שֶׁהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן מְלוּחִין? לָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה טָהוֹר מָלִיחַ וְטָמֵא תָּפֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A kosher fish that one salted** together **with a non-kosher fish is permitted. What, is it not** referring to a case **where they were both salted** and expel juices? This indicates that the kosher fish is not prohibited by the juices of the non-kosher fish, contrary to Rava's statement. The Gemara responds: **No,** this is referring to a case **where** the **kosher** fish was **salted and** the **non-kosher** fish was **unsalted.** Since an unsalted fish does not emit juices, the kosher fish does not absorb the flavor of the non-kosher fish.

The Gemara proceeds to examine the precise mechanics of *maliach kerose'ach* and the ways in which salting differs from heat as a mode of taste transfer. When it comes to heat, it does not matter which of the two objects in contact is *kosher* or non-*kosher* — what matters at most is which object is on top and which is on the bottom, since heat rises. If the bottom object is hot, it will heat things up more effectively than if only the top object is hot. But the identity of the *kosher* versus *treif* item is irrelevant for heat-based transfer.

With salting, the analysis is fundamentally different, because the primary mechanism of salt is to draw out the juices of the object being salted. If one has salted *kosher* fish alongside non-*kosher* fish that has not been salted, there is no problem — the salt will only draw the juices out of the *kosher* fish; it will not pull anything out of the unsalted non-*kosher* fish. The crucial variable in salting, therefore, is specifically whether the *non-kosher* item has been salted. Only if the non-*kosher* item is salted will its juices be drawn out and potentially absorbed into the *kosher* item.

### Braita Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֲבָל אִם הָיָה טָהוֹר מָלִיחַ וְטָמֵא תָּפֵל, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא בְּשֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מְלוּחִין עָסְקִינַן! פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ: טָהוֹר שֶׁמְּלָחוֹ עִם דָּג טָמֵא – מוּתָּר, כֵּיצַד? שֶׁהָיָה טָהוֹר מָלִיחַ וְטָמֵא תָּפֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches: But if** the **kosher** fish **was salted and** the **non-kosher** fish was **unsalted** the kosher fish remains permitted, **it may be inferred that** in **the first clause we are dealing with** a case **where they are both salted.** The Gemara responds: The latter clause **is explaining** the *halakha* of the first clause. The *baraita* should be read as follows: **A kosher** fish **that one salted** together **with a non-kosher fish is permitted. How so?** This is the *halakha* **if** the **kosher** fish **was salted and** the **non-kosher** fish was **unsalted.**

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *braita*: *dag tahor shemalcho im dag tamei, mutar* — if one salted a *kosher* fish together with a non-*kosher* fish, it is permitted. The natural reading of this *braita* is that both fish were salted together, and yet the result is *mutar*. If so, this appears to contradict the position that salting both items together causes a forbidden transfer of taste.

The Gemara responds: *lo, kegon shehaya tahor maliach vetamei tafel* — no, the *braita* is describing the case where the *kosher* fish was salted and the non-*kosher* fish was *tafel*, meaning plain and unsalted. It is only *mutar* because the *treif* item was not salted and thus its juices were not drawn out. The case where both are salted would indeed be forbidden.

But the Gemara then complicates matters further. The *braita* continues: *haya tamei maliach vetahor tafel assur* — if the non-*kosher* fish was the one that was salted and the *kosher* fish was unsalted, it is forbidden. The implication of this formulation — *michlal dereisha shnayim meluchim askinan* — is that the first clause must have been discussing the case where both were salted, and even so it was *mutar*. This would undermine the position that salting both together is a problem.

### Non-Kosher Salted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן: בִּישְׂרָא דְּמַנַּח נַפְקוּתָא).
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for remembering the three *halakhot* stated by Shmuel below: The manner in which blood is expelled from **meat;** salted meat **that is placed** on a vessel; an animal whose neck is broken before its soul **departs.**

The Gemara responds that the second clause of the *braita* is not a separate ruling but an explanation of the first: *perushei kamfaresh* — it is explaining what the first clause means. The *braita* states that salting a *kosher* fish together with a non-*kosher* fish is *mutar* — and then immediately clarifies: under what circumstance? When the *kosher* fish was salted and the non-*kosher* fish was *tafel*. It is only in that specific case that the ruling of *mutar* applies.

### Both Salted Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ רֵישָׁא שְׁנֵיהֶם מְלוּחִים – הַשְׁתָּא שְׁנֵיהֶם מְלוּחִים שְׁרֵי, טָהוֹר מָלִיחַ וְטָמֵא תָּפֵל מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **So, too, it is reasonable** that this is the meaning of the *baraita*, **as if it enters your mind** that **the first clause** is referring to a case where **both of them are salted,** one can claim: **Now** that the *baraita* has stated that even if **both of them are salted** the kosher fish is **permitted, is** it **necessary** to state that the same applies in the less problematic case where the **kosher** fish was **salted and** the **non-kosher** fish was **unsalted?**

The Gemara acknowledges that if this reading is correct — that the first clause of the *braita* deals with the case where only the *kosher* fish was salted — then one might ask why the *braita* bothers to state the case at all. If both being salted were *mutar*, it would be unnecessary to state that only one being salted is also *mutar*. The Gemara therefore explains: *tana seifa legiluyei reisha* — the second clause was taught to clarify the first. Without the second clause, one might have read the first clause as referring to the case of both being salted. By specifying the case of *tahor maliach vetamei tafel*, the *braita* signals what it meant in the first clause. But this also means, the Gemara concludes, that one cannot derive from the *braita* an absolute ruling about what happens when both are salted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא לָא אִירְיָא, תְּנָא סֵיפָא לְגַלּוֹיֵי רֵישָׁא, דְּלָא תֵּימָא רֵישָׁא טָהוֹר מָלִיחַ וְטָמֵא תָּפֵל, אֲבָל שְׁנֵיהֶם מְלוּחִין אָסוּר, תְּנָא סֵיפָא טָהוֹר מָלִיחַ וְטָמֵא תָּפֵל, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא שְׁנֵיהֶן מְלוּחִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **If it is due to that** reason, **there is no** conclusive **argument.** It is possible that the first clause of the *baraita* is indeed referring to a case where both fish are salted, and the *tanna* of the *baraita* nevertheless **taught the latter clause to shed light on the first clause,** so **that you** should **not say: The first clause** is referring only to a case where the **kosher** fish was **salted and** the **non-kosher** fish was **unsalted, but** if they were **both salted,** then the kosher fish is **prohibited.** To rule this out, he **taught the latter clause,** which explicitly makes reference to a case where the **kosher** fish was **salted and** the **non-kosher** fish **unsalted,** which **by inference** indicates **that the first clause** is referring to a case where they are **both salted, and** teaches that **even so** the kosher fish is **permitted.**

The *sof davar* is that the *braita* cannot definitively prove whether two items being salted together is *mutar* or *assur*. The simple reading of the *braita* is that both being salted is *mutar*, but the Gemara demonstrates that it can be read otherwise. What does emerge clearly is that if only the non-*kosher* item is salted, it is forbidden — *tamei maliach vetahor tafel assur* — and if only the *kosher* item is salted, it is permitted. The case of both being salted remains unresolved by the *braita*, and thus the ruling established previously stands: a special principle applies to the *kashering* of meat with blood — *dam mishrak sharik* — but outside that specific context, even if both items are spewing out their juices simultaneously, the *kosher* item can still absorb from the non-*kosher* one, and two items salted together do present a problem.

The essential asymmetry between salting and heat thus remains in place: with heat, the *kosher* or *treif* identity of the hot object is irrelevant; with salt, the decisive question is specifically whether the *non-kosher* item is salted.

## Salting for Kashering

### Blood Release Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הַבָּשָׂר יוֹצֵא מִידֵי דָּמוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מוֹלְחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמְדִיחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: Meat cannot be rid of its blood unless one salts it thoroughly and rinses it thoroughly** in water.

The Gemara now transitions from the question of taste transfer through salting to the laws governing the *melichah* process itself — the salting of meat to remove blood before cooking. This discussion is anchored by a statement of Shmuel: *eina basar yotzei midei damo* — meat does not fully release all of its blood on its own. The implication is significant: unlike roasting, where whatever blood comes out comes out and whatever remains inside is not a problem (since it is not going anywhere once the meat is cooked *berotav*), the *melichah* process requires that *all* the blood be extracted. The reason is that after salting, the meat will be placed in a pot and cooked, and if any blood remains, it will flow out into the liquid and then be reabsorbed. Therefore, salting demands a thoroughness that roasting does not.

### Salting Practice

Shmuel accordingly rules: *ela im ken molcho yafeh yafeh umadicho yafeh yafeh* — one must salt the meat very well and rinse it very well. Admittedly, this formulation — "very well" — is not particularly precise, which points to the importance of a *mimetic* tradition in the transmission of *melichah* practice. How much salt, how long, at what angle the board should be tilted — these are matters better learned in practice from a parent or teacher than deduced from a textual description. In fact, one can find the basic rules of *koshering* printed on the side of packaged *kosher* salt in the supermarket: rinse the meat, salt it, leave it on a slanted surface for approximately 18 minutes, then rinse it again.

### Rinse-Salt Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מוֹלֵחַ וּמֵדִיחַ. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תְּנָא: מֵדִיחַ וּמוֹלֵחַ וּמֵדִיחַ. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי, הָא דְּחַלְלֵיהּ בֵּי טַבָּחָא, הָא דְּלָא חַלְלֵיהּ בֵּי טַבָּחָא.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated: Rav Huna says:** One must **salt and rinse** the meat in water. And it **was taught in a *baraita*:** One must **rinse** the meat, **and salt** it, **and** then **rinse** it again. The Gemara adds: **And** these two rulings **do not disagree. This** ruling of Rav Huna is referring to a case **where one** already **washed** the meat **in the slaughterhouse** before salting, whereas **that** *baraita* is referring to a case **where one did not wash** the meat **in the slaughterhouse.**

A *tannaitic* dispute is then cited regarding the order of rinsing and salting. Rav Huna rules *moleach umadiach* — one salts and then rinses. A *baraita*, however, teaches *madiach umoleach umadiach* — one rinses first, then salts, then rinses again. The Gemara reconciles the two positions: *hada de-chalei beit tavcha* — Rav Huna's ruling applies to a case where the meat was already rinsed in the butcher's shop. If it arrived pre-rinsed, one need only salt and rinse. If it was not rinsed at the butcher, the full three-step process of the *baraita* applies.

### Pre-Salting Rinse

Why must one rinse the meat *before* salting? Since the entire point is to extract the blood, why does it matter that there is surface blood present at the start? The *rishonim* offer approximately eight distinct reasons, which cluster around two underlying concerns. One category of answer holds that blood already on the surface, rather than being drawn out by the salt, may actually be driven inward by it, making the *kashering* counterproductive for those surface areas. Another category holds that rinsing first softens the meat, making it more receptive to the salt and thus making the salting process more effective. The pre-salting rinse should not use water that is too hot, as hot water would itself drive blood into the meat.

### Rav Dimi & Organs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא מָלַח לֵיהּ בְּמִילְחָא גְּלָלְנִיתָא, וּמְנַפֵּיץ לֵיהּ. אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: אֵין מַחֲזִיקִין דָּם בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים. תַּרְגְּמַאּ אַכַּרְכְּשָׁא וּמְעַיָּיא וְהַדְרָא דְּכַנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Dimi of Neharde'a would salt** meat **with coarse salt and** then **shake** the salt off the meat. **Rav Mesharshiyya says: One does not presume** that there is **blood in the intestines,** and therefore they are not prohibited if they have not been salted. The Gemara comments: The Sages **interpreted** this statement as referring **to the rectum, the intestines, and the spiral colon.**

The Gemara records that Rav Dimi Mi-Nehardea would salt with *milcha galinata* — thick salt, effectively what we would call *kosher* salt — and then *u-menafitz lei*, he would shake it off, scattering the excess salt from the surface before rinsing. Rav Mesharshaya then adds a separate ruling: *ein machazikim dam bifnei mei-ayim* — one need not be concerned about blood in the inner organs. The Gemara clarifies that this refers to the rectum, stomach, and large intestine. Whatever the physiological explanation, these organs are not halachically considered to contain *dam* in the relevant sense and do not require the usual blood-extraction treatment.

### Salt Mechanics

Rashi's comment on *u-menafitz lei* illuminates the reason for using thick salt in *kashering*. He explains that shaking off the salt is necessary *le-milcha le-achar zman* — after the salting period, before rinsing, so that the blood-saturated salt does not get reabsorbed into the meat. He further notes that thin salt (*melach dakka*) would not require shaking off, since it dissolves and washes away on its own. This might suggest thin salt is preferable. In practice, however, thick *kosher* salt is used because its larger, flatter crystals cover more surface area, draw out blood more effectively by remaining on the surface without penetrating the meat, and result in a less excessively salty final product.

### Vessel Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מַנִּיחִין בָּשָׂר מָלִיחַ אֶלָּא עַל גַּבֵּי כְּלִי מְנוּקָּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: One may place salted meat only on a perforated vessel,** so that the expelled blood can run out. But if the vessel is not perforated then the blood will pool and be reabsorbed by the meat.

Shmuel rules: *ein manichin basar maliach ela al gabi kli menukav* — one may not leave salted meat on anything other than a perforated vessel. This ensures that the blood draining from the meat does not pool beneath the meat and get reabsorbed. The same principle underlies the requirement to use a slanted board — the blood must run off and away from the meat, not collect beneath it.

### Individual vs. Multiple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מָלַח לֵיהּ גַּרְמָא גַּרְמָא. תְּרֵי מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּפָרֵישׁ מֵהַאי וּבָלַע הַאי? חַד נָמֵי פָּרֵישׁ מֵהַאי גִּיסָא וּבָלַע הַאי גִּיסָא! אֶלָּא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Sheshet would salt** meat **one bone,** i.e., one piece, **at a time.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that he would **not** salt **two** together? Could it be **because** the blood **leaves this** piece **and that** piece **absorbs** it? If so, with regard to **one** piece **as well,** one could claim that the blood **leaves this side** of the piece **and that side absorbs** it. **Rather,** there **is no difference** between one piece and two pieces, and one may salt even several pieces together.

Rav Sheshet would salt each piece of meat individually — *garma garma* — rather than salting multiple pieces together. The Gemara asks why he did not salt multiple pieces at once. The answer: he was concerned that blood running off one piece would be absorbed by an adjacent piece — *mishum de-parish mei-hai u-vala hai*. The Gemara, however, dismisses this concern. Even a single piece of meat would present the same problem — blood runs off the top surface and is absorbed by the bottom — yet we do not concern ourselves with that. Rather, the principle is *lo shna*: while a piece of meat is actively being salted and expelling blood, it does not simultaneously absorb blood from an adjacent piece. Tosafot works through the precise mechanics of this principle at length, noting that even if a lower layer of meat has finished salting before an upper layer has been added and is still expelling blood, the blood will simply slide off. The Gemara's conclusion is clear: one may salt multiple pieces together without concern.

### Neck-Breaking Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הַשּׁוֹבֵר מַפְרַקְתָּהּ שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה קוֹדֶם שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ – הֲרֵי זֶה מַכְבִּיד אֶת הַבָּשָׂר, וְגוֹזֵל אֶת הַבְּרִיּוֹת, וּמַבְלִיעַ דָּם בָּאֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya: One who breaks the neck of an animal** after it is slaughtered but **before its soul departs thereby makes the meat heavy.** The meat expels blood at the time of slaughter, but if one breaks the animal's neck, excess blood is trapped inside and weighs down the meat. **And** by this action **he robs people,** as **he causes blood to be absorbed in the** animal's **limbs,** and since he sells the meat by weight, people will pay extra to acquire the same amount of edible meat.

Shmuel then rules in the name of Rabbi Chiya: *ha-shover mafrakta she-beheima kodem she-teitzei nafsha, harei ze machbid et habasar vegozel et habriot umavlia dam baeivarim* — one who breaks the neck of an animal that has been *shechted* but before its life has fully departed is doing three things wrong. First, the meat becomes heavier — *machbid et habasar* — because blood that would have flowed out remains inside. Second, this constitutes *gezel*, a form of robbery — *gozel et habriot* — because if the butcher sells the meat by weight, the purchaser is receiving less actual meat and more blood per pound than anticipated. Third, and most serious for our purposes, *umavlia dam baeivarim* — the blood becomes embedded in the organs and limbs.

This third point is critical because of the difference between roasting and salting established above. When roasting, the blood can flow out as the meat cooks, and not all of it needs to come out beforehand. But when salting, all the blood must be extracted before the meat goes into the pot. If the neck-breaking maneuver has caused blood to become embedded and unable to exit through normal salting, the *kashering* itself is compromised.

### Kashering Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵיכִי קָאָמַר? מַכְבִּיד אֶת הַבָּשָׂר, וְגוֹזֵל אֶת הַבְּרִיּוּת מִשּׁוּם דְּמַבְלִיעַ דָּם בָּאֵבָרִים – הָא לְדִידֵיהּ שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא לְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי אֲסִיר? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **what** case **is he speaking?** Does Shmuel mean that there is only one problem with this practice, namely, that it **renders the meat heavy and robs people since he causes blood to be absorbed in the** animal's **limbs?** If so, it may be inferred that if one wishes to keep the meat **for himself,** one may **well** do so, since he is robbing no one. **Or perhaps** Shmuel is referring to two prohibitions, first, that the blood trapped in the meat renders it prohibited for consumption, and second, that of robbery. If so, then even if one wants to keep the meat **for himself,** it is **also prohibited.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara raises a *ibaia lehu* — an unresolved question. When Shmuel says that breaking the neck weighs down the meat and is a form of robbery, does he mean that *kashering* is still theoretically possible but the blood content makes the meat heavier and thus the seller is cheating the buyer? If so, it would be permitted to do this for one's own use — *ledidei shapir dami* — since there is no *gezel* involved, and the meat can still be *kashered*. Or does Shmuel mean that the blood becomes so embedded that *kashering* is genuinely ineffective? If so, even for one's own use it would be forbidden, since the meat cannot be rendered *kosher* through salting.

The Gemara concludes with *teiku* — the question remains unresolved. The practical ruling is therefore not to do it in any case.

## Meat & Cheese Table

### Poultry & Cheese

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּעֲלֶה אֶת הָעוֹף עִם הַגְּבִינָה עַל הַשֻּׁלְחָן – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who places** the meat of **birds with cheese on the table** upon which he eats **does not** thereby **violate** a Torah **prohibition.**

The *Mishnah* now states: *hamaleh et ha'of im hagevinah al hashulchan eino over belo ta'aseh* — one who places poultry and cheese on the same table does not transgress a biblical prohibition. This *Mishnah* is clarifying the rabbinic nature of the restriction against having meat and cheese at the same table simultaneously. The setting of the table together is, at most, a rabbinic prohibition — it is not a violation of the Torah's prohibition of *basar bechalav*.

### Poultry Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא אוֹכְלוֹ – עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּשַׂר עוֹף בְּחָלָב דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! אֵימָא: הַמַּעֲלֶה אֶת הָעוֹף עִם הַגְּבִינָה עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן – אֵינוֹ בָּא לִידֵי לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: Since the mishna mentions only that placing meat of birds and milk on one table does not violate a Torah prohibition, one may **consequently** infer that if one **eats** them together he does **violate** a Torah **prohibition.** If so, **learn from** the mishna that **meat of birds in milk** is prohibited **by Torah law,** contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who maintains that it applies by rabbinic law. The Gemara responds: **Say** that the mishna should be understood as follows: **One who places bird meat with cheese on the table will not** thereby **come to** violate a Torah **prohibition,** since eating the two together is a rabbinic prohibition, as Rabbi Akiva maintains.

The Gemara, however, attempts to read something more into the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* specifies that *ha'ochlo over belo ta'aseh* — one who *eats* them together transgresses a biblical prohibition. Might this imply that there is a *Tanna* who holds that eating poultry cooked with milk constitutes a biblical transgression? *Shema mina basar of bechalav de'oraita* — perhaps we can infer that chicken and milk is a biblical prohibition.

The Gemara rejects this reading: *eima hamaleh et of im gevinah al hashulchan eina ba lidei lo ta'aseh* — the *Mishnah* means only that having poultry and cheese on the table together can never even lead to a biblical prohibition, precisely because the biblical prohibition does not apply to poultry at all. As the subsequent *Mishnah* will make clear, the Torah says *lo tevashel gedi bachalav imo* — a kid in its *mother's* milk — and birds have no mother's milk. It is difficult to understand how birds could ever be included in the biblical prohibition. Nevertheless, the Gemara makes the attempt to read into the earlier *Mishnayot* the possibility of a *Tanna* who holds that birds are included — though this is never said explicitly by any named *Tanna*, and we do not rule that way.

## Meat & Milk Biblical

### Tanna Kamma Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּשַׂר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בַּחֲלֵב בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – אָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל וְאָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. בְּשַׂר בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בַּחֲלֵב בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה, בְּשַׂר בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה בַּחֲלֵב בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – מוּתָּר לְבַשֵּׁל וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is **prohibited to cook the meat of a kosher animal in the milk of** any **kosher animal,** not merely the milk of its mother, **and** deriving **benefit** from that mixture is **prohibited.** It is **permitted to cook the meat of a kosher animal in the milk of a non-kosher animal,** or **the meat of a non-kosher animal in the milk of a kosher animal, and** deriving **benefit** from that mixture is **permitted.**

The next *Mishnah* now sets out the scope of the biblical prohibition of *basar bechalav* directly. The *Tanna Kamma* states: *basar behemah tehorah bechalav behemah tehorah assur levashel ve'assur behana'ah* — the meat of a *kosher behemah* (a domesticated mammal such as a cow, sheep, or goat) cooked with the milk of such a *kosher behemah* is biblically forbidden to cook, and furthermore one may not derive any benefit (*hana'ah*) from it whatsoever. The prohibition thus extends beyond eating to all forms of benefit.

### Non-Kosher Species

The *Mishnah* continues: *basar behemah tehorah bechalav behemah teme'ah* — the meat of a *kosher* animal with the milk of a non-*kosher* animal, or *basar behemah teme'ah bechalav behemah tehorah* — the meat of a non-*kosher* animal with the milk of a *kosher* animal — *mutar levashal vemutar behana'ah*: it is permitted to cook them together and permitted to derive benefit from the result.

Of course, the meat or milk of a non-*kosher* species remains forbidden to eat for its own reasons — but it does not trigger the *basar bechalav* prohibition, not even rabbinically. The rationale is striking: once the non-*kosher* species is involved, the rabbis saw no need to add an additional prohibition of *basar bechalav* on top of an existing *issur*. The result is that one may even cook pig meat with cow's milk, or cow's meat with pig's milk, without violating *basar bechalav* — either biblically or rabbinically — and may derive benefit from the result.

It should be noted carefully, however, that this leniency applies specifically when the *species* is non-*kosher*. Non-*shechted* meat of a *kosher* species — *treif* cow's meat, for example — does not benefit from this leniency. Cooking non-*shechted* cow's meat with milk is still *assur bevishul ve'assur behana'ah*.

### Chicken Cooking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּה וָעוֹף אֵינָם מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים – פְּרָט לְחַיָּה, וּלְעוֹף, וּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** Cooking the meat of **an undomesticated animal or bird** in milk is **not** prohibited **by Torah law, as it is stated: "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk"** (Exodus 23:19, 34:26; Deuteronomy 14:21) **three times.** The repetition of the word "kid" three times **excludes an undomesticated animal, a bird, and a non-kosher animal.**

A separate question arises regarding poultry. Can chicken be cooked with milk? The *Rambam* derives from this *Mishnah* a broader principle: anything not biblically prohibited as *basar bechalav* may be cooked with milk and the result may be used for *hana'ah*. Other *rishonim* disagree, arguing that the *Mishnah*'s leniency with non-*kosher* species is a special case — it is because those items already carry their own independent *issur* that the rabbis did not pile on an additional *basar bechalav* prohibition. For something like chicken and milk, where the items are individually *kosher* but only *rabbinically* prohibited as *basar bechalav*, the rabbis would certainly have prohibited cooking them together.

Nevertheless, the ruling follows the *Rambam*: while one may not have chicken and cheese at the same table, one *may* cook chicken and milk together, provided one does not eat the result. One may cook the dish and give it to one's dog, give it to a non-Jew, or otherwise derive benefit from it.

This is admittedly counterintuitive and somewhat paradoxical. The biblical prohibition is stated in terms of *cooking* — *lo tevashel* — yet in practice the emphasis has shifted almost entirely to *eating*, such that we impose rabbinic restrictions around eating (including the table restriction) while not extending those restrictions to the act of cooking itself. Nonetheless, that is how the *Rambam* reads the *Mishnah* and how we rule.

### Benefit Prohibition

The prohibition of *hana'ah* from *basar bechalav* has significant practical implications that are not immediately obvious. A common contemporary example is dog food: much commercial dog food consists of meat cooked with milk-derived ingredients. If the meat is of a *kosher* species — even if it is not *shechted* — buying such dog food and feeding it to one's dog may constitute deriving *hana'ah* from cooked meat and milk.

One might think that since the meat is *neveilah* — not *shechted* — it would not be subject to the *basar bechalav* prohibition. But this raises the question of *issur chal al issur*: can the prohibition of *basar bechalav* take effect on meat that is already forbidden as *neveilah*? The question of *issur chal al issur* bears directly on whether such dog food becomes forbidden in *hana'ah* as well as in the act of cooking. This issue is somewhat debated, but the upshot is that one must exercise care when purchasing dog food or cat food that combines meat and milk ingredients.

## Chayah & Bird Debate

### Mother's Milk Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇל נְבֵלָה״, וְנֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״, אֶת שֶׁאָסוּר מִשּׁוּם נְבֵלָה אָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל בְּחָלָב. עוֹף שֶׁאָסוּר מִשּׁוּם נְבֵלָה, יָכוֹל יְהֵא אָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל בְּחָלָב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – יָצָא עוֹף שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֲלֵב אֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says** that **it is stated: "You shall not eat of any animal carcass"** (Deuteronomy 14:21), **and** in the same verse **it is stated: "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk."** This indicates that meat of an animal **that** is subject to be **prohibited due to** the prohibition of eating **an unslaughtered carcass** is **prohibited** for one **to cook in milk.** Consequently, with regard to meat of **birds, which** is subject to be **prohibited due to** the prohibition of eating **an unslaughtered carcass,** one **might** have thought that it **would be prohibited to cook** it **in milk.** Therefore, **the verse states: "In its mother's milk," excluding a bird, which has no mother's milk.**

Rabbi Akiva adds his voice to the *Mishnah*'s discussion: *chayah ve'of einam min haTorah* — wild animals (*chayah*) and birds are not included in the biblical prohibition. His derivation is based on the three occurrences of the verse *lo tevashel gedi bachalav imo* in the Torah. The starting assumption is that the prohibition is general — it refers to meat and milk broadly. The word *gedi* (kid) appears three times to *limit* the scope: only meat that is analogous to a *gedi* is included. The three exclusions are: *prat lechayah* — to exclude wild animals, *ule'of* — to exclude birds, and *uvehemah teme'ah* — to exclude non-*kosher* domesticated animals.

### Neveilah Inclusion

Rabbi Yossi HaGelili takes a different approach. His derivation is built on a juxtaposition within the Torah itself. The verse reads: *lo tochlu kol neveilah... lo tevashel gedi bachalav imo* — "do not eat *neveilah*... do not cook a kid in its mother's milk." From the juxtaposition, Rabbi Yossi HaGelili derives: *et she'assur mishum neveilah assur levashel bachalav* — anything that falls under the *neveilah* prohibition is also subject to the *basar bechalav* prohibition. This would include not only domesticated mammals (*behemah*) but also wild animals (*chayah*) and even birds, all of which are subject to *neveilah* if not properly *shechted*.

However, the same verse that includes them also limits them: *bachalav imo* — the mother's milk. A bird has no mother's milk. *Yatza of she'ein lo chalav em* — birds are excluded because the verse explicitly requires mother's milk, and birds produce none. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yossi HaGelili, then, concerns *chayah*: Rabbi Akiva excludes *chayah* from the *biblical* prohibition along with birds; Rabbi Yossi HaGelili includes *chayah*, since they do have mother's milk and are subject to *neveilah*.

## Shmuel's Gedi Drasha (II)

### Gedi Broadening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר קְרָא ״וַיִּשְׁלַח יְהוּדָה אֶת גְּדִי הָעִזִּים״, כָּאן גְּדִי עִזִּים, הָא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר גְּדִי סְתָם – אֲפִילּוּ פָּרָה וְרָחֵל בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Elazar said: The verse states: "And Judah sent the kid of the goats"** (Genesis 38:20). One may infer that **here,** since this verse specifies that it is referring to **a kid of the goats, consequently, anywhere** the word **"kid" is stated without specification, it means even a cow or a ewe.** Accordingly, the prohibition of meat cooked in milk applies to all kosher domesticated animals.

Before presenting Shmuel's expansive *drashas* on the word *gedi*, the Gemara first establishes that *gedi* is not limited to a goat. Rabbi Elazar cites the verse *Vayishlach Yehudah et gedi ha'izim* — Judah sent a kid *of the goats*. The fact that the Torah specifies *gedi ha'izim* — a kid *goat* — implies that when it wishes to speak of a goat specifically, it says so explicitly. When it simply says *gedi* without qualification, the term is broader and refers to any young animal. *Hacol makom shenemar gedi stam afilu parah urechelah bemashma* — whenever *gedi* appears without further specification, it encompasses any young animal, including a calf or a lamb.

One might counter: perhaps the paradigm of *gedi ha'izim* teaches the opposite — that *gedi* always means a goat, and only with the addition of *ha'izim* is it explicitly a goat? The Gemara answers that there is yet another verse — *ve'et orot gedayei ha'izim* (the skins of kids of the goats, from the story of Yaakov) — that also specifies *ha'izim* explicitly. The existence of two verses that feel the need to specify *ha'izim* means that these are not functioning as normative paradigms but as *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke'echad* — two verses arriving together to teach the same point, which paradoxically *cannot* serve as a normative paradigm according to one interpretive tradition. Rather, both are limiting the word in those verses to goats, which demonstrates that elsewhere *gedi* is more general.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵילַף מִינֵּיהּ? כְּתִיב קְרָא אַחֲרִינָא: ״וְאֵת עֹרֹת גְּדָיֵי הָעִזִּים״ – כָּאן ״גְּדָיֵי הָעִזִּים״, הָא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״גְּדִי״ סְתָם – אֲפִילּוּ פָּרָה וְרָחֵל בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **derive from** that verse that in general, every instance of the word "kid" is referring to a goat, including the prohibition of meat cooked in milk? The Gemara answers: This cannot be, as **another verse is written: "And the skins of the kids of the goats"** (Genesis 27:16). This indicates that only **here** they are **kids of the goats, but anywhere** the word **"kid" is stated without specification, it means even a cow or a ewe.**

### Inclusive Drashas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״גְּדִי״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַחֵלֶב, ״גְּדִי״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמֵּתָה, ״גְּדִי״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשְּׁלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel says:** Each of the Torah's three mentions of the prohibition of not cooking a kid in its mother's milk serves to include a different case. The first mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to include** liability for cooking in milk and eating forbidden **fats,** beyond the liability for eating forbidden fat per se. The second mention of the word **"kid"** likewise serves **to include** additional liability for cooking in milk and eating the meat of **a dead** animal carcass. Finally, the third mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to include** liability for cooking in milk and eating an animal **fetus.**

Shmuel derives three inclusive *drashas* from the three appearances of *gedi*. First, *gedi lerabot et hachelev* — the word *gedi* broadens the prohibition to include *chelev*, the forbidden fat of an animal. If one cooks *chelev* with milk, one transgresses the prohibition of *basar bechalav* in addition to the independent prohibition of eating *chelev* — fat is considered a form of meat for this purpose. Second, *gedi lerabot et hameta* — *gedi* includes an animal that died on its own (*neveilah*), even though it already carries its own prohibition. Third, *gedi lerabot et hashallil* — *gedi* includes the fetus of an animal.

### Exclusive Drashas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גְּדִי״ – לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַדָּם, ״גְּדִי״ – לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַשִּׁלְיָא, ״גְּדִי״ – לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַטְּמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Each mention excludes a case as well: The first mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to exclude** liability for cooking in milk and consuming **blood.** The second mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to exclude** liability for cooking in milk and eating **the placenta** of an animal. The third mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to exclude** liability for cooking in milk and eating the meat of **a non-kosher** animal.

Against these three expansive *drashas*, Shmuel also derives three narrowing ones. *Gedi lehotzi et hadam* — blood is excluded; cooking blood with milk does not constitute *basar bechalav*. *Gedi lehotzi et hashilya* — the placenta or afterbirth is excluded; it is not considered meat. *Gedi lehotzi et hatmeia* — a non-*kosher* animal is excluded, consistent with the *Mishnah*'s ruling and the derivations of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yossi HaGelili.

## Meat Definition

### Mother's Milk Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בַּחֲלֵב זָכָר, ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בַּחֲלֵב שְׁחוּטָה, ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בַּחֲלֵב טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, the first instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** indicates that one is **not** liable for cooking meat **in the milk of a male** animal, in the rare case that a male might produce milk. The second instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** indicates that one is **not** liable for cooking meat **in the milk of** an already **slaughtered** animal, since it is considered milk only if given while the animal is alive. The third instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** indicates that one is **not** liable for cooking meat **in the milk of a non-kosher** animal.

The Gemara adds a further set of exclusions derived not from the word *gedi* but from the phrase *bachalav imo* — in its *mother's* milk. This phrase yields three additional limits: *velo bechalav zachar* — the prohibition does not apply to milk that comes from a male animal (which, while physiologically unusual, can occasionally occur); *velo bechalav shechuta* — it does not apply to milk that is inside a slaughtered animal's udder, as in the case of *kachal*, which was discussed at length earlier in the tractate; and *velo bechalav teme'ah* — it does not apply to the milk of a non-*kosher* animal. In short, *bachalav imo* requires that the milk be from a living mother of a *kosher* species, produced naturally during her lifetime.

## Shmuel's Gedi Drasha (II)

### Drasha Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תְּלָתָא ״גְּדִי״ כְּתִיבִי, וַאֲנַן שִׁיתָּא דָּרְשִׁינַן! קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר, וְאִיסּוּר חֵלֶב וּמֵתָה מֵחַד קְרָא נָפְקִי. דָּם נָמֵי לָאו ״גְּדִי״ הוּא, וְשִׁלְיָא נָמֵי פִּירְשָׁא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תְּרֵי: חַד לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשְּׁלִיל, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The word **"kid" is written** only **three** times, **and** yet **we expound** it to teach **six** different *halakhot*. The Gemara responds: **Shmuel maintains** that **a prohibition takes effect** even where another **prohibition** already exists, **and** therefore the **prohibition of** forbidden **fat** in milk **and** the prohibition of **a dead** animal carcass in milk **are both derived from one verse,** as both are applications of the prohibition to an already prohibited item. The exclusion of **blood** from the prohibition **also** does not require its own verse, as blood **is not** considered **a kid** at all, **and likewise** there is no need for a verse to exclude **a placenta** from the prohibition, as it **is merely a secretion** of the animal, rather than a kind of meat. Therefore, **two** mentions of "kid" **are left; one** serves **to include a fetus, and one** serves **to exclude a non-kosher animal.**

Having established that Shmuel derives six *drashas* from the word *gedi* — three inclusive and three exclusive — the Gemara notes a difficulty: *aha tlata gedi ketivi veshisha darshinan* — but *gedi* only appears three times in the relevant verses. How can Shmuel extract six distinct *drashas* from three occurrences?

The Gemara's resolution is that three of those *drashas* were, strictly speaking, unnecessary. *De'issur chelev ve-neveilah mechada kra nafka* — the inclusion of *chelev* and *neveilah* can both be derived from a single verse, since both cases present the same underlying point: one *issur* takes hold upon another. Similarly, blood's exclusion from the category of *meat* does not require a verse — it is self-evident that blood is not meat. And the placenta's exclusion likewise needs no verse — it is simply refuse, not flesh. These three *drashas* were therefore not each consuming one of the three *gedi* occurrences in a meaningful way. The actual required *drashas* are: one for *chelev* and *neveilah* together (the principle of *issur chal al issur*), one to include the fetus, and one to exclude the non-*kosher* species.

### Issur Chal Al Issur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לְכֹהֵן טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּמִיתָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּמֵתוּ בוֹ כִּי יְחַלְּלֻהוּ״ – פְּרַט לָזוֹ שֶׁמְּחוּלֶּלֶת וְעוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Shmuel** really **maintain** that **a prohibition takes effect** where another **prohibition** already exists? **But didn't Shmuel say in the name of Rabbi Elazar: From where** is it derived that **an impure priest who partakes of impure *teruma*,** i.e., the portion of produce designated for the priest, **is not** punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven as he would had the *teruma* been ritually pure? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated,** with regard to the prohibition of an impure priest partaking of *teruma*: **"And die therein if they desecrate it"** (Leviticus 22:9), **to the exclusion of this** case of *teruma* that is impure, **which was already desecrated** before the priest ate it. Here, it seems, since impure *teruma* is already prohibited for consumption, the added prohibition of an impure priest partaking of *teruma* does not take effect.

Shmuel's position that *issur chal al issur* — one prohibition can take hold upon another already-existing prohibition — is the linchpin of why he needs no separate verse to include *chelev* and *neveilah* in the *basar bechalav* prohibition. Since he holds this principle as a general rule, it is already understood that cooking *chelev* with milk creates a double *issur*: the independent prohibition of *chelev* and the additional prohibition of *basar bechalav*. Similarly with *neveilah*: the meat of a *kosher* animal that died without *shechitah* is already forbidden as *neveilah*, yet Shmuel holds that cooking it with milk adds the *basar bechalav* prohibition on top of it.

This principle also has direct bearing on the dog food question raised earlier. When non-*shechted* cow's meat is cooked with milk, the question is whether the *basar bechalav* prohibition — which includes a prohibition of *hana'ah* — can take effect on meat already forbidden as *neveilah*. That is a specific application of *issur chal al issur* that remains the subject of debate and will be continued in the next *shiur*.

## Meat Definition (II)

### Fat Blood Placenta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּחָלָב זָכָר, ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בַּחֲלֵב שְׁחוּטָה, ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בַּחֲלֵב טְמֵאָה. אִתְּמַר: הַמְבַשֵּׁל בְּחֵלֶב בְּחָלָב, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – חַד אָמַר: לוֹקֶה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, the first instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** indicates that one is **not** liable for cooking meat **in the milk of a male** animal, in the rare case that a male might produce milk. The second instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** indicates that one is **not** liable for cooking meat **in the milk of** an already **slaughtered** animal, since it is considered milk only if given while the animal is alive. The third instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** indicates that one is **not** liable for cooking meat **in the milk of a non-kosher** animal. It **was stated:** With regard to **one who cooks** forbidden **fat in milk, Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** disagree as to the *halakha*. **One says** that he **is flogged** for violating the prohibition of meat cooked in milk, **and one says** that he **is not flogged.**

The Gemara closes with a clarification of the three-part distinction that Shmuel's analysis yields regarding what constitutes *meat* for the purposes of *basar bechalav*. *Chelev* — forbidden fat — is considered meat and is therefore included in the prohibition. *Dam* — blood — is not considered meat and is therefore excluded. *Shilya* — the placenta or afterbirth — is also not considered meat and is excluded. The fetus, by contrast, is included.

The underlying principle that emerges is that the category of *meat* for *basar bechalav* is not simply coextensive with everything that comes from an animal. Blood and placenta, despite being animal products, fall outside the definition. Fat, despite carrying its own independent prohibition, falls within it. These distinctions will continue to be developed in the subsequent discussion.
