# Sanhedrin 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zaken Mamre Overview

### Opening & Zaken Mamre

The daf begins on 88a, four lines from the top, at the word *divrei*. We are in the midst of discussing *zaken mamre*, and the focus is on how the *braisa* interprets the *pasuk* underlying this law. The verses — *divrei rivos bish'arecha bein dam l'dam bein din l'din divrei rivos bish'arecha vekamta ve'alisa* — are parsed phrase by phrase, with each element mapped onto a different halachic domain in which a potentially decisive debate might arise. The governing question is how these domains relate to the earlier position of Rabbi Meir, who requires that the underlying matter be one for which one would be *chayav kares* if done intentionally (*be-meizid*) or *chayav chatat* if done inadvertently (*beshogeg*).

### Divrei Rivos Pasuk

The *braisa* identifies the phrase *divrei rivos* — "words of contention" — as referring to *archin*, *charamin*, and *hekdeshos*: the laws of valuations, dedications (*cherem*), and sanctified items. These are domains in which halachic status is created through an act of speech, a *hafla'ah*, and in which disputes can arise that carry serious downstream consequences.

### Rebbi Meir's Position

The thread connecting these cases to Rabbi Meir's requirement — that the contested matter involve *kares* or *chatat* — runs through a common mechanism: if the halachic status of money or an item is in doubt, and that money or item is then used to effect *kiddushei isha*, the validity of the marriage hangs in the balance. If the *kiddushin* did not take effect and the woman subsequently had relations with another man, the question of adultery — which carries *kares* or worse — becomes directly implicated. This chain of reasoning is what allows each of the following categories to qualify under Rabbi Meir's standard.

---

## Archin Debate

### Archin, Charamin, Hekdes…

The *braisa* proceeds to specify what debate in each domain exemplifies the point. For *archin*, it is the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *rabbanan*, as recorded in a *braisa*: if someone says *erech ploni alai* — pledging the Torah-mandated *erech* valuation of a person — but the individual in question is an infant less than one month old, the Torah's *erech* schedule does not apply to such a child. What then?

### Erech Pachus Miben Chode…

Rabbi Meir rules *nosein damav* — the pledgor must pay the infant's market value as a slave, even though the term used was *erech* rather than *dmei*. His reasoning is the principle of *ein adam motzi dvarav l'vatala*: a person does not make a declaration without intending some practical consequence, so we interpret the pledge as if he had said *dmei ploni alai*. The *chachamim*, however, rule *lo amar klum* — he said nothing legally effective, since the formula he used (*erech*) has no applicability to an infant under one month. 

Now, if one follows Rabbi Meir's view, the money is *hekdesh* and belongs to the *Beis HaMikdash*. If one follows the *chachamim*, it is ordinary money the pledgor may keep. Should this money then be used for *kiddushei isha*, and the woman was unaware of its sanctified status, the *kiddushin* would be void according to one view but valid according to the other. If she subsequently cohabited with another man, the question of adultery — and thus *kares* — turns entirely on this underlying dispute about whether the money was *hekdesh*. This is how the *archin* debate connects to Rabbi Meir's criterion.

---

## Charamin Debate

### Stam Charamin Ruling

For *charamin*, the debate is between Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira and the *rabbanan*. If someone declares an item *cherem* without specifying to whom it goes, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira rules *stam charamin l'bedek habayis* — it goes to the Temple treasury — citing the verse *kol cherem kodesh kodashim hu l'Hashem*, which indicates that any *cherem* is consecrated to God. The *chachamim* rule *stam charamin l'kohein* — it goes to the *kohein* — citing the verse *k'sdeh hacherem l'kohein tihyeh achuzaso*, which assigns a field of *cherem* to the *kohein*'s possession. According to the *chachamim*, the earlier verse teaches only that a *cherem* declaration can take effect even upon items already sanctified — *al kadshei kodashim v'al kadshei kalim* — but does not indicate that *stam charamin* goes to the Temple.

The same practical consequence follows: if the item is *hekdesh* it cannot be used for *kiddushin*, while if it belongs to the *kohein* as ordinary property, someone in possession of it who attempts to use it to marry a woman creates a question about the validity of those *kiddushin* — and again, the chain leads to potential *kares* or worse.

---

## Hekdeshos Debate

### Tzinor Shel Hekdesh

For *hekdeshos*, the relevant dispute is between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *rabbanan*. The *braisa* records that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov rules: *afilu tzinor shel hekdesh tzarich asara bnei adam lifdoso* — even the smallest item of *hekdesh*, like a small implement, requires ten people to effect its redemption. The majority view holds that ten people — including a *kohein* — are required only for redeeming *karka* (land), while ordinary *hekdesh* requires only three, and even that requirement is essentially to ensure a proper valuation. If someone redeemed an item with only three people rather than ten, whether the item is still *hekdesh* or has been validly redeemed depends on this dispute.

Rashi notes the *kiddushin* mechanism again: if the redeemed item is still *hekdesh*, it cannot be used to betroth a woman. But Rashi also offers an independent path to *chiyuv kares*: if the item was genuinely *hekdesh* and someone who was *tamei* ate from its meat as a *korban me'ilah*, they would be *chayav kares* for eating *kodshim* in a state of *tum'ah*. Whether there was a *korban* at all, however, turns on whether the original object was truly *hekdesh* — which in turn depends on the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *rabbanan*.

## Four Rivos Cases

### Hashka'at Sotah

The word *rivos* — contentions — is next identified with three specific *mitzvos* that presuppose some act of conflict or suspicion: the drinking of the *sotah* waters (*hashka'at sotah*), the breaking of the *egla arufa*, and the purification of the *metzora*. Each of these is then matched to a specific *tannaic* dispute. The survey serves the dual purpose of illustrating the range of *divrei rivos* and demonstrating how each generates a case that can implicate *kares* or *chatat*.

For *hashka'at sotah*, the debate is between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, cited from the *Mishna*. When a husband performs *kinuy* — warning his wife not to enter into seclusion with a particular man — Rabbi Eliezer rules *mekana al pi shenayim u-mashka al pi ed echad*: the warning requires two witnesses, but her actual seclusion can be established by a single witness (or by the husband himself). Rabbi Yehoshua rules *mekana al pi shenayim u-mashka al pi shenayim*: both the warning and the seclusion require two witnesses.

The halachic consequence is as follows. If there were two witnesses to the *kinuy* and only one witness to the seclusion, Rabbi Eliezer would regard her as obligated to undergo the *sotah* ritual, and if she refused, she would lose her *ketubah* and be divorced without it. Rabbi Yehoshua would regard her as under no obligation and she would retain her *ketubah* rights. If she then sold her *ketubah* note and the buyer attempted to collect, the question of whether the money rightfully belongs to him depends on whether she had a valid *ketubah* claim. Money that may not legitimately belong to one party and is then used for *kiddushin* — validity uncertain — brings the matter once again into the domain of potential adultery and *kares*.

### Arifas Egla Arufa

The debate surrounding *arifas egla arufa* involves the question of from which point on the corpse the measurement to the nearest city is taken — since the city determined to be closest is the one obligated to bring the *egla*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that one measures from the navel (*tiburo*), viewing that as the bodily seat of vitality. Rabbi Akiva holds that one measures from the nose (*chotamo*), since breath is the defining sign of life. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that one measures from the neck (*tzavaro*) — the place where a person would normally be killed — regardless of where the actual wound was inflicted.

It may happen that two different cities each brought an *egla* in good faith, each believing itself to be the closest city according to one of these views. An *egla arufa* is forbidden in *hana'ah* — in deriving any benefit from it. If an item forbidden in *hana'ah* is used to betroth a woman, the *kiddushin* is invalid; if it is not forbidden because it is not the genuine *egla arufa*, the *kiddushin* is valid. The ramifications for adultery and *kares* follow in the same pattern as the cases above.

### Taharat Metzora

For the purification of the *metzora*, the dispute is between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon (with Rabbi Eliezer also mentioned). The Torah requires that the blood of the *asham* offering be placed on the *metzora*'s right thumb, right big toe, and right earlobe. The question is: what if the *metzora* lacks a thumb, big toe, or ear? The *tanna kamma* holds *ein lo taharah olamit* — there is simply no remedy; such a person can never be purified. Rabbi Eliezer holds that one places the blood where the thumb would have been. Rabbi Shimon holds *noten al smolo v'yotzei* — one may place it on the left thumb or toe.

Rashi's approach to connecting this to *kares* is that if the person is genuinely *tahor* after going through the process according to one of the lenient views, they may enter the *Beis HaMikdash* without incurring *kares*. If they are still *tamei*, entering the *Beis HaMikdash* would be *chayav kares*. Tosafot, however, objects that entering the *Beis HaMikdash* while *tamei* may be *chayav kares* but is not subject to a *chatat* when done inadvertently — and we also require the possibility of *chatat*. Tosafot therefore constructs a different scenario: the person touched *terumah*, and whether it was *terumah tme'ah* or *terumah tehorah* depends on whether the person was truly *tahor* after the disputed purification process. That *terumah* is then used to effect *kiddushin*, and if it was *terumah tme'ah* the *kiddushin* fails, bringing us back to the chain of implications for adultery and *kares*. The Gemara itself does not spell out these connections; it is Rashi and Tosafot who work through the details.

### Leket Shikcha Pe'ah

The final term, *bisharecha* — "in your gates" — is associated with *leket*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah*, the agricultural obligations to leave gleanings, forgotten sheaves, and the edge of the field for the poor. Each of these has its own threshold debate.

For *leket*: two stalks that fall during harvesting constitute *leket* and belong to the poor, but three do not, since three is already a substantial amount. For *shikcha*: two forgotten sheaves are considered *shikcha*, but three are not. On both of these, Beis Shammai rules that up to three constitute *leket* or *shikcha*, and four does not — thereby being more expansive in what belongs to the poor. If a poor person took what he believed was rightfully his *leket* and the question is whether it truly was his, and he uses that produce or its proceeds to betroth a woman, the familiar mechanism applies.

For *pe'ah*, the relevant dispute is in the name of Rabbi Yishmael versus the *rabbanan*. The primary *mitzvah* is to designate *pe'ah* while the grain is still standing — by simply not harvesting the edge of the field. If one failed to do so and already cut the grain, one may still designate *pe'ah* from the bundled sheaves. If one failed at that stage, one may designate it from the pile (*kari*) — but only before *mirach*, the smoothing over of the pile, which marks the point of *gmar melacha* at which the grain becomes obligated in *terumah* and *ma'aser*. Once *mirach* has occurred, the grain is *chayav* in *terumot u'ma'asrot*, and when the *pe'ah* portion is given to the poor person it must be given free of those obligations — meaning the owner must first separate *terumah u'ma'aser* and then give the remainder. In the name of Rabbi Yishmael, the authorities teach that even once one has ground the grain into flour and formed dough — *af mishiyina ha'isa* — if one has not yet given *pe'ah* one is still obligated to do so. The *chachamim* disagree. Whether the poor person has rights to a portion of that dough, whether the proceeds of its sale truly belong to him, and what happens if he uses that money to betroth a woman — all of this flows from this dispute about whether *pe'ah* can still be designated at the dough stage.

## Tradition vs. Logic

### Rav Kahana's Scenarios

Having established the categories of *divrei rivos* and their connection to *kares* and *chatat*, the Gemara turns to a fundamental question about the inner workings of the *zaken mamre* case itself: does it matter whether the sage's dissent is rooted in his own received tradition (*mipi hashemuah*) or in his own reasoning (*kach hu be'einai*)?

Rav Kahana presents a principled analysis structured around four possible combinations. First: *hu omer mipi hashemuah vehein omrim mipi hashemuah* — he says he has a tradition and they say they have a tradition. In that case, *eino neherag* — he is not executed. He genuinely holds a different received tradition; how can he be held morally accountable for not submitting to theirs? Second: *hu omer kach hu be'einai vehein omrim kach hu be'eineinu* — both sides argue from their own reasoning. Again, *eino neherag*. Even granting that the majority view prevails as *halacha*, he is entitled to his own logical conviction and is not simply compelled to abandon it. *Vekhol shekein* — the third case follows a fortiori: if he says he has a tradition and they respond with a logical argument, certainly he is not executed. His received tradition surely outweighs their inference.

The sole case in which execution applies, according to Rav Kahana, is the fourth and final combination: *hu omer kach hu be'einai vehein omrim mipi hashemuah* — he argues from his own logic and they present a received tradition. In this situation, he had no basis to maintain his position. His argument cannot overcome their tradition, and he should have submitted. The failure to do so, despite having no legitimate reason for holding firm, is what makes him *chayav misah*.

This reading of *zaken mamre* reflects a particular understanding of the institution's purpose: not primarily the blanket protection of rabbinic authority, but the requirement that a sage submit to superior authority when there is genuine reason to do so. If there is a legitimate basis for his disagreement — his own tradition, his own reasoning against their reasoning — he may personally maintain his view; he simply may not go out and instruct others to act on it.

### Rebbi Elazar's View

Rabbi Elazar disagrees sharply. He holds *neherag kedei shelo yirbu machlokes beYisrael* — he is executed in every case, in order that disputes not proliferate in Israel. According to Rabbi Elazar, the reasoning behind *zaken mamre* is not about the merit of the individual's position but about the institutional integrity of the *Beis Din*. The *halacha* is *acharei rabbim l'hatot*, and the *Beis Din*'s decision is the binding determination of *halacha* — even if one has an authentic competing tradition, and even if one is genuinely convinced the majority is wrong. The protection of the institution overrides the individual sage's epistemic standing.

### Akavya ben Mahalalel

As support for Rav Kahana's more limited position, the Gemara observes: *teda shelo hargu es Akavya ben Mahalalel* — note that Akavya ben Mahalalel was not executed. Akavya famously disputed four rulings with the *chachamim* and refused to retract, yet he was not put to death as a *zaken mamre*. Moreover, when he was dying, he told his son to follow the majority — explaining that he himself had held firm because he had received his tradition from a broader consensus, whereas his son was relying on a single authority (Akavya himself) and should therefore submit to the majority. This is precisely the scenario of competing traditions: Rav Kahana holds that in such a case, a sage is not executed.

One must note, however, as the Gemara itself will address, that being exempt from execution does not necessarily mean his conduct was entirely unproblematic. The Gemara will clarify: the reason Akavya was not executed was that *shelo hora halacha l'ma'aseh* — he did not actually issue a practical ruling for others to act upon. What makes one a *zaken mamre* is not the disagreement in principle, but setting oneself up as an opposing authority whose rulings are acted upon.

## Mishna Language Analysis

### Derashti vs. Lamadti

The Gemara now turns to the language of our *mishna*. The *mishna* describes the *zaken mamre* as saying: *kach darashti v'kach darshu chaverai* — "this is what I expounded, and this is what my colleagues expounded" — and also: *kach lamad'ti v'kach limdu chaverai* — "this is what I learned, and this is what they learned." The double formulation is striking: *darashti* implies derivation through one's own reasoning, while *lamad'ti* suggests received tradition. Since the *mishna* includes both formulations, it seems to imply that the *zaken mamre* applies regardless of which type of disagreement underlies the dispute — which would appear to contradict Rav Kahana, who limits liability to the single scenario in which logic confronts tradition.

### Gemara Reconciliation

The Gemara answers with a simple *lo* — no, you need not read the double language that way. The *mishna* can be reconciled with Rav Kahana's view by understanding the two phrases as describing the single qualifying scenario: *hu amar kach b'einai v'hein omrim mipi shmua* — he says "this is what I derived through my own reasoning" (*darashti*), and they say "this is what we received as tradition" (*lamad'ti*). Even though the *mishna* uses both terms across its two clauses, the combination maps onto Rav Kahana's lone case of liability. The Gemara admittedly does not elaborate at length on how precisely the double language of the *mishna* yields this reading, but it maintains the reading is available.

## Tosefta: Original Disput…

### Rebbi Yose on Original D…

The Gemara cites a *tosefta*, familiar from *Sotah*, *Chagigah*, and other places. Rabbi Yose states: *mi-techila lo hayu marbin machloket b'Yisrael* — originally, disputes did not proliferate in Israel. In the context of *Chagigah*, the *zugos* — the pairs of *Nasi* and *Av Beis Din* who preceded Hillel and Shammai — debated only one or two points, such as the question of *smicha* on *korbanos* on *Yom Tov*. The system worked because there was always a method to resolve any disagreement: when a question arose, it would travel up through successive levels of the court hierarchy until it reached an authoritative resolution, and the mechanism of that resolution was predominantly *shemuah* — received tradition.

### Three-Court System

The *tosefta* describes the structure in detail, corresponding to the picture painted in our *mishna*. The *Beis Din* of 71 sat in the *Lishkas HaGazis*. Two *batei dinim* of 23 sat respectively at the entrance to *Har HaBayis* and at the entrance to the *azarah*. And the many other *batei dinim* of 23 were spread throughout all the cities of Israel.

When a contested question arose, it was first brought to the local *Beis Din*. If they had a tradition (*im shamu*), they would answer. If not (*v'im lav*), the matter traveled to the regional court nearest the city. If that court had a tradition, they answered; if not, the question moved to the court at the entrance to *Har HaBayis*, then to the court at the entrance to the *azarah*, with the same inquiry at each level. It is striking that at no stage below the *Beis Din HaGadol* does the *tosefta* describe any court attempting to resolve the matter through its own reasoning. The sole question at each level is *im shamu* — do you have a tradition? If not, the matter moves up. The use of independent human analysis was the absolute last resort, reserved for the *Beis Din HaGadol* alone.

The *tosefta* notes that the *Beis Din HaGadol* would sit in the *Lishkas HaGazis* from the time of the morning *tamid* offering until the afternoon *tamid*, and on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* they would convene in the *Cheil* — the area surrounding the *Beis HaMikdash* within *Har HaBayis*. The *tosefta* specifies that on those days they sat in the *Beis HaMedrash* rather than the *Lishkas HaGazis*, since convening there might give the appearance of adjudicating capital cases, which is prohibited on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, even though they wished to continue learning *Torah*.

### Sanhedrin Vote System

When the question finally reached the *Beis Din HaGadol*, the same question was first posed: *im shamu*, did they have a tradition? If so, they answered. Only if there was no tradition did they proceed to analysis and a vote. *Rabu hamitame'im timu* — if the majority ruled *tamei*, it was *tamei*; *rabu hamitaharim tiharu* — if the majority ruled *tahor*, it was *tahor*. This is the foundational mechanism of *halacha acharei rabbim*. The implication for *zaken mamre* is clear: once the *Beis Din HaGadol* has issued a ruling — whether based on tradition or, as a last resort, on analysis and majority vote — the *zaken mamre* who continues to dissent and act on his opposing view is in defiance of the binding determination.

### Shammai & Hillel Effect

The *tosefta* then identifies the historical turning point: *mishe'rabu talmidei Shammai v'Hillel shelo shimshu kol tzorkan* — once the students of Shammai and Hillel, who had not devoted themselves sufficiently to their teachers' instruction, became numerous, *rabu machlokos b'Yisrael* — disputes proliferated throughout Israel, and *v'na'asis haTorah k'shtei Toros* — the *Torah* became as if it were two *Toros*.

It is worth pausing on what this means. If the system was designed to resolve every dispute, why did the proliferation of students who lacked complete training cause unresolvable debates? One might suggest: when the number of disputed issues is small, the court hierarchy can realistically handle every case that comes before it. When everything under the sun becomes disputed, it is practically impossible to bring each question all the way through the system to resolution.

But the deeper point, which the *tosefta* makes unmistakably clear, is about the primacy of *mesora*. The entire system — the repeated inquiry of *im shamu* at every level — frames *mesora* as the *ikar*. Human analysis was a concession of last resort, not the primary engine of *halacha*. The failure of Shammai and Hillel's students is described as a failure of transmission: they did not properly internalize all that they needed to. If *halacha* were primarily a product of human reasoning, the debate about how much time a student spent with his teacher would be beside the point — good reasoning is good reasoning regardless. The *tosefta* frames it otherwise. This reflects one strand of understanding *Torah she-ba'al peh* — that the *ikar* is the *mesora*, and that human analysis, however necessary, is a departure from the ideal.

## Judge Qualifications

### Judge Requirements

The *tosefta* continues by describing the qualifications required to ascend through the court system. To serve as a local judge, one must be *chacham u-shfal berech v'da'at ha-beriyot nocha heimenu* — wise, of humble bearing ("low of knee"), and someone who is pleasant in the eyes of people — meaning one who commands genuine public respect. If such a judge distinguished himself in his local capacity, he could be elevated: *ma'alin oto le-Har HaBayis*, then to the *azarah*, then to the *Lishkas HaGazis*. Advancement through the system was meritocratic, rooted in demonstrated wisdom, humility, and the confidence of the community.

The Gemara appends a related tradition. The question was asked: who is deserving of *olam ha-ba*? The answer given: one who is *anvatanuto u-shfal berech* — humble and lowly of bearing — who *shayeif ayil shayeif nafik*, bowing as he enters and bows as he exits, one who constantly bends himself for *Torah*; who *garis be-Oraita tadira* — studies *Torah* constantly; and who *lo machzik tivuta le-nafsheih* — never credits himself for his learning. The *rabbanan* looked at Rav Ulla bar Abba as one who exemplified these qualities.

## Halacha L'Ma'aseh Requir…

### Acting or Teaching Requir…

Returning now to the *mishna* — *chazar le-iro ve-shanah*, if the *zaken mamre* returns to his city and resumes teaching his ruling, he does not become *chayav* until he crosses the threshold of *halacha l'ma'aseh*: he must either act on his ruling himself or instruct others who then act upon it. A *braisa* records this explicitly: *eino chayav ad she-ya'aseh ke-horato* — he is not *chayav* until he acts according to his teaching — *o she-yoreh le-acherim ve-ya'asu ke-horato* — or until he rules for others and they act accordingly.

### Why Zaken Mamre Needed

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge to the second element of the *braisa* — the case where he teaches and others act — is straightforward: *me-ikara lav bar katla hu ve-hashta bar katla hu*. Before others acted, he was not yet *chayav misah*; once they act upon his ruling, he is. The transition is what makes him liable. But regarding the first element — the case where he himself acts on his ruling — a difficulty arises. If the underlying matter is something like *Shabbos* desecration, and he goes ahead and acts on his view that the practice is *mutar*, why do you need the *zaken mamre* framework at all? Even without the *zaken mamre* framing, if a person performs a *melacha d'Oraita* on *Shabbos* in violation of accepted *halacha*, he is *mechalel Shabbos*. What does the *zaken mamre* status add?

### Transition Eliminates Ha…

The Gemara's first answer is that the *braisa* is speaking of a case involving *chelev* and *dam* — violations that carry *kares* rather than *misa b'Beis Din*. In such a case, *me-ikara lav bar katla hu ve-hashta bar katla hu*: before the *zaken mamre* designation, eating *chelev* in violation of accepted *halacha* would make him *chayav kares*, not *chayav misa*. Once he has gone before the *Beis Din HaGadol*, received their ruling, returned, and acted on his opposing view, he is now *chayav misa*. The *zaken mamre* framing thus elevates the consequence from *kares* to execution.

But what about a case of *chayavei mitos Beis Din* — where the underlying violation itself already carries the death penalty? The Gemara then offers the key answer: *me-ikara ba'ei hatra'ah ve-hashta la ba'ei hatra'ah*. Before the *zaken mamre* framing, any execution for the underlying violation would require *hatra'ah* — a formal warning at the moment of commission. Now, however, because he has already stood before the *Beis Din HaGadol* and been told explicitly that he is wrong, and despite that he has returned to his city and acted on his view, the process of the *Beis Din*'s adjudication itself functions as the effective warning. He does not require a fresh *hatra'ah* at the moment of the act; what the *zaken mamre* framework accomplishes is to make him *chayav misah* for the sin of *zaken mamre* itself — without the need for *hatra'ah* — even though the underlying violation, taken in isolation, would have required it.

### Meisis & Avodah Zarah

The Gemara presses further. What if the case involves *avodah zarah* and he was acting as a *meisis* — one who entices others to idolatry — a category that already does not require *hatra'ah*? In that case, it seems, the *zaken mamre* framing adds nothing with respect to eliminating the *hatra'ah* requirement, since *meisis* dispenses with *hatra'ah* independently. Why then would we invoke *zaken mamre* at all?

The Gemara answers: *manah me'ikara i amar ta'ama mekablina minei, hashta i amar ta'ama lo mekablina minei*. In an ordinary *meisis* case, if the accused stood before the court and offered a reasoned explanation for why he believed his conduct was *mutar* under the circumstances, the court would be in a position to consider his argument and potentially exonerate him. Here, however, once he has gone through the *zaken mamre* process — brought his question to the *Beis Din HaGadol*, received their authoritative ruling, and defied it — no argument he now offers can be entertained. The sin is no longer simply *meisis*; it is the sin of *zaken mamre*. It is for that sin, and not the underlying violation, that he is executed. And for that sin, the entire prior proceedings serve in place of both *hatra'ah* and any opportunity to offer exculpatory reasoning.
