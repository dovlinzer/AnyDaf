# Hullin 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pharynx Status

### Anatomy Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: תּוּרְבַּץ הַוֶּושֶׁט – רַב אָמַר: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּרוּבּוֹ. רַב אָמַר בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ: מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר בְּרוּבּוֹ: לָאו מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if the gullet was perforated, the animal is a *tereifa*. **It was stated:** In a case where **the entrance [*turbatz*] of the gullet** from the pharynx was perforated, **Rav says:** The animal is a *tereifa* if **any** part of the gullet was perforated. **And Shmuel says:** It is a *tereifa* only if **its majority** was perforated. **Rav says:** If **any** part was perforated, because he holds that the entrance of the gullet **is a location** fit for **slaughter** and is therefore considered part of the gullet. **And Shmuel says:** If **its majority** was perforated, because he holds that **it is not a location** fit for **slaughter** and is not considered part of the gullet.

Our sugya begins mid-page on 43b, with the words *turbatz haVeshet* — the pharynx situated atop the esophagus. To appreciate the anatomical context: the *veshet* (esophagus) is the tube through which food passes, and at its very top sits the pharynx, a kind of expanded or elevated structure where the throat opens into the esophagus. This is distinct from the larynx and vocal cords, which sit atop the trachea. The *turbatz haveshet* is this pharyngeal region — a bump or flared area crowning the esophagus itself.

The halachic stakes are already familiar from earlier in the masechta: a hole of even the smallest size (*mashehu*) in the *veshet* renders an animal a *treifah*. The question now is whether the *turbatz haveshet* shares that status, or whether it occupies some different category. This question carries implications both for *hilchos treifah* and, as we will see, for *hilchos shechita* — since the validity of a *shechita* depends on where precisely the cut was made.

### Rav vs. Shmuel


The Gemara opens with a direct dispute: *rav amar bimashehu* — Rav holds that even the tiniest hole in the *turbatz haveshet* renders the animal a *treifah*, just as in the *veshet* itself. *shmuel amar berubo* — Shmuel holds that the *turbatz haveshet* has a different status; one would need a cut through the majority of it, analogous to the standard for the *gargeret* (trachea), not the hyper-stringent standard of the *veshet*.

The Gemara immediately ties these positions to *hilchos shechita*: *rav amar bimashehu, makom shechita hu* — Rav considers the pharyngeal area a valid location for *shechita*, and therefore it carries the full stringency of the *veshet* for purposes of *treifah*. *shmuel amar berubo, lav makom shechita hu* — Shmuel considers it outside the valid zone of *shechita*, and therefore it does not share the *veshet*'s stringent standard. The two dimensions — *makom shechita* and the *shiur* for *treifah* — are, in the Gemara's framing, logically linked.

### Definition Tests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי נִיהוּ תּוּרְבַּץ הַוֶּושֶׁט? אָמַר מָרִי בַּר מָר עוּקְבָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל שֶׁחוֹתְכוֹ וּמַרְחִיב – זֶה הוּא תּוּרְבַּץ הַוֶּושֶׁט, חוֹתְכוֹ וְעוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ – זֶהוּ וֶושֶׁט עַצְמוֹ. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב פַּפֵּי: מָר לָא אָמַר הָכִי, וּמַנּוּ? רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי. אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁחוֹתְכוֹ וְעוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ – זֶה תּוּרְבַּץ הַוֶּושֶׁט, אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ וֶושֶׁט עַצְמוֹ? כֹּל שֶׁחוֹתְכוֹ וְכָוֵיץ. יוֹנָה אָמַר זֵירָא: מַבְלַעְתָּא, וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב אַוְיָא: פָּחוּת מִשְּׂעָרְתָּא וַעֲדִיף מֵחִיטְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Which** area **is the entrance of the gullet? Mari bar Mar Ukva said** that **Shmuel said: All** of the area **that widens** outward **when cut** along its width, **this is the entrance of the gullet;** all the area that **remains in place when cut, this is the gullet itself. Rav Pappi said to them: Master did not say so. And who is** that Master? It is **Rav Beivai bar Abaye. Rather,** he said that **all** of the area **that remains in place when cut, this is the entrance of the gullet. But which** area, then, **is the gullet itself?** It is **all** of the area **that constricts when cut** and closes inward. The Sage **Yona says** in the name of Rabbi **Zeira:** The entrance of the gullet is only the area of the **throat immediately** bordering the gullet. **And how much** of the throat qualifies as the entrance of the gullet? **Rav Avya said: Less than** the length of **a barley** kernel **and more than** the length of **a wheat** kernel.

Having established the dispute, the Gemara asks: what precisely constitutes the *turbatz haveshet*? How do we identify which tissue is the *turbatz* and which is the *veshet* proper?

Mari bar Mar Ukva cites Shmuel: *kol shechotcho umarchiv zehu turbatz daveshet; chotcho ve'omed bimkomo zehu veshet atzmo* — any area where making an incision causes the wound to open and expand is the *turbatz daveshet*; any area where the cut remains as-is without expanding is the *veshet* itself. The logic reflects the differing physical properties of each region: the pharynx is more elastic and under greater tension, so incisions gape open, while the esophagus proper is more contained.

However, Rav Pappa then cites a different tradition in the name of Rav Bibi bar Abaye that reverses the identification: *kol shechotcho ve'omed bimkomo zehu turbatz daveshet* — it is specifically when the cut stays in place that we are in the *turbatz daveshet*; and *kol shechotcho vekavitz* — when the incision shrinks and contracts, that is the *veshet* itself. On this reading, the *veshet* is the more contracting, tighter structure, while the *turbatz* is the area where cuts remain static. Both traditions agree on the underlying physiology — that holes in the *turbatz* are larger relative to the *veshet* — but they disagree on the mechanism: does the *veshet* contract while the *turbatz* stays put, or does the *turbatz* expand while the *veshet* stays put?

### Swallowing Place


Yona cites Zeira with a strikingly different conceptual approach: *mivla'asa* — the *turbatz haveshet* is the place of swallowing. Rashi understands this to mean a region even higher up, closer to the actual opening of the throat where food is first received before descending into the esophagus. This is not merely a clarification of anatomy but a potentially distinct halachic identification, locating the *turbatz* in a more superior position than the other opinions.

How large is this zone? Rav Avya says: smaller than a barleycorn (*se'orah*) and larger than a wheat kernel (*chitah*) — a remarkably narrow band. That a barleycorn is larger than a wheat kernel is a reminder of how distant our daily experience is from the agricultural world the Gemara assumes. If the *turbatz haveshet* is this swallowing-zone, it is a very slender strip of tissue, and the practical consequences for both *shechita* and *treifah* would be confined accordingly.

## Mixed Shechita Case

### Shechita Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא תּוֹרָא דַּהֲוָה לִבְנֵי רַב עוּקְבָא, דְּאַתְחֵיל בֵּיהּ שְׁחִיטָה בְּתוּרְבַּץ הַוֶּושֶׁט, וּגְמַר בְּוֶושֶׁט. אָמַר רָבָא: רָמֵינָא עֲלֵיהּ חוּמְרֵי דְּרַב וְחוּמְרֵי דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, וְטָרֵיפְנָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident involving **a certain bull that belonged to the sons of Rav Ukva, where** its **slaughter began** with a small incision **in the entrance of the gullet and concluded** in its majority **in the gullet. Rava said: I impose upon it the stringencies of Rav and the stringencies of Shmuel, and deem it a *tereifa*.**

The Gemara now presents a practical case that crystallizes the entire dispute. There was an ox belonging to the sons of Rav Ukva: *de'atchayl bishchita beturbatz haveshet vegamar baveshet* — the slaughterer began the *shechita* in the *turbatz haveshet* area and completed it in the *veshet* itself. Rava was asked to rule.

The scenario is more complex than it first appears. The slaughterer did not cut the majority of the *turbatz haveshet* — he began there, at an angle, cutting only a small portion, and then continued through the *veshet*. The question is therefore twofold: was this a valid *shechita*, and does the partial cut in the *turbatz* render the animal a *treifah*?

### Rava's Hybrid Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּמְרֵי דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ. וְהָאָמַר רַב: מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא! כִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: לָאו מְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא. אִי שְׁמוּאֵל, הָאָמַר: בְּרוּבּוֹ! כְּרַב, דְּאָמַר: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** I impose the **stringencies of Rav, as Rav said:** The animal is a *tereifa* if **any** part of the entrance of the gullet was perforated before slaughter. Such is the case here, since the incision began in the entrance of the gullet. Perhaps one will ask: **But doesn't Rav say** that the entrance of the gullet **is a location** fit for **slaughter,** in which case the initial incision should be considered the beginning of the act of slaughter? To this I will respond: I hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, who says** that it is **not a location** fit for **slaughter.** If one asks: **If** I hold in accordance with the opinion of **Shmuel, doesn't he say:** It is a *tereifa* only if it was perforated **in its majority?** To this I will respond: I hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav, who says:** If **any** part was perforated. Consequently, I deem the animal a *tereifa*.

Rava ruled: *ramina lay chumray deRav vechumray deShmuel, vetarfana lay* — I apply to it the stringencies of both Rav's position and Shmuel's position, and we declare it a *treifah*. He then explained the reasoning: the stringency of Rav (*chumray deRav*) is that even a tiny hole (*bimashehu*) suffices to render the *turbatz* a *treifah*. Therefore, the partial cut at the start of the *shechita* is already a problem. As for the *shechita* itself, we apply Shmuel's position (*lav makom shechita hu*) to say it was not a valid location for *shechita*, so the *shechita* that followed in the *veshet* was also invalid.

The Gemara immediately presses: but according to Rav, the *turbatz* is a *makom shechita* — so the entire cut should have been valid *shechita*! Rava responds: when it comes to the question of whether it was a *makom shechita*, we rule like Shmuel. And when it comes to the question of what constitutes a *treifah*, we rule like Rav. The Gemara recognizes this for what it is: a deliberate grafting of stringencies from two mutually contradictory positions.

### Rabbi Abba's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיגַּלְגַּל מִילְּתָא, מְטַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: תּוֹרָא, בֵּין לְרַב בֵּין לִשְׁמוּאֵל שְׁרֵי. זִילוּ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא, דִּלְשַׁלֵּים דְּמֵי תוֹרָא לְמָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **the matter circulated,** and **it came before Rabbi Abba,** who **said to** his students: This **bull is permitted** for consumption, **both according to Rav,** who holds that the entrance of the gullet is a location fit for slaughter, **and according to Shmuel,** who holds that it is not a *tereifa* unless it is perforated in its majority. Therefore, **go tell the son of Rav Yosef bar Ḥama,** i.e., Rava, **that** he is **to pay the value of the bull to its owner,** since he improperly deemed it a *tereifa*.

The matter came before Rabbi Abba, who was unimpressed: *tora, bayn leRav bayn leShmuel shari* — this ox, whether you go to Rav or to Shmuel, is permitted. According to Rav, the *turbatz* is a valid *makom shechita*, so the *shechita* is good, and the cut there — being part of a valid *shechita* — poses no *treifah* problem. According to Shmuel, the *turbatz* is not the *veshet*, so a small cut there does not constitute a *treifah* by the stringent *mashehu* standard. Either way, the ox is permitted. To construct a hybrid ruling that makes it a *treifah* under neither consistent position is simply wrong.

Rabbi Abba's displeasure at Rava's ruling was evident. He referred to Rava not by name but as *brei deRav Yosef bar Chama* — the son of Rav Yosef bar Chama — a pointed rhetorical slight.

### Compensation Ruling


The consequence: *delishlam demay tora lemarey* — Rava must pay the owner the value of the ox. Since the animal was declared a *treifah* on the basis of an erroneous ruling, the owner presumably received far less than the animal's worth as kosher meat, or it was disposed of entirely. Having given a wrong *psak*, Rava bears financial responsibility for the loss.

The broader question of when a *posek* incurs liability for a mistaken ruling is treated in tractate Sanhedrin and involves factors such as the degree of error and the degree of active involvement in the loss. Here, the Gemara simply records the judgment: the ruling was not merely wrong, it was an absurd construction, and the *posek* is therefore accountable.

## Hillel-Shammai Braita

### No Hybrid Leniencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: מוֹתְבִינָא תְּיוּבְתָּא כְּלַפֵּי סָנְאֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, לְעוֹלָם הִלְכְתָא כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל, וְהָרוֹצֶה לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי – עוֹשֶׂה, כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל – עוֹשֶׂה. מִקּוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּמִקּוּלֵּי בֵּית הִלֵּל – רָשָׁע. מֵחוּמְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּמֵחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל – עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״הַכְּסִיל בַּחוֹשֶׁךְ הוֹלֵךְ״. אֶלָּא אִי כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי – כְּקוּלֵּיהֶן וּכְחוּמְרֵיהֶן, אִי כְּבֵית הִלֵּל – כְּקוּלֵּיהֶן וּכְחוּמְרֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Ravina, said: I offer a conclusive refutation to the enemies of Rava,** a euphemism for Rava himself, from a *baraita*: **The *halakha*** is **always in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel, but one who wishes to act in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai may do so,** and one who wishes to act **in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel may do so.** But if one wishes to adopt both **the leniencies of Beit Shammai and** also **the leniencies of Beit Hillel, he is a wicked person. And one who wishes to adopt both the stringencies of Beit Shammai and the stringencies of Beit Hillel, with regard to him the verse states: "The fool walks in darkness"** (Ecclesiastes 2:14). **Rather,** one should act **either in accordance with Beit Shammai,** following both **their leniencies and their stringencies, or in accordance with Beit Hillel,** following both **their leniencies and their stringencies.**

The Gemara does not stop at the case. Mar brei deRavina invokes a standing objection against Rava's approach — framed with the Talmudic euphemism that we direct the difficulty toward "the enemies of Rabbah," meaning Rava himself — from the famous *braita* found in multiple tractates including *sukkah* and *eduyot*:

*Le'olam halacha k'divrei Beis Hillel.* The ruling follows Beis Hillel. *v'ha-rotzeh la'asot k'divrei beis shammai, oseh* — if one wishes to rule like Beis Shammai, one may, and be consistent within that framework. But *mikulei Beis Shammai u'mikulei Beis Hillel* — if you cherry-pick the leniencies of both schools — *rasha*: you are considered wicked. The condemnation is strong: by always selecting the most permissive ruling regardless of which authority issues it, you reveal that you have already decided what you want the *halacha* to say, and you are simply shopping for validation.

What, then, of combining stringencies? *me'chumrei beis shammai u'me'chumrei beis hillel* — if you pick the stringencies of both schools — *alav hakatuv omer, haksil bachoshech holech*: regarding such a person, the verse says "the fool walks in darkness." The *braita* is unsparing in both directions: leniency-shopping is wicked; stringency-shopping is foolish. The mandate is to commit to a coherent position: *im k'Beis Shammai, k'kuleihem u'ke'chumreihem; im k'Beis Hillel, k'kuleihem u'ke'chumreihem* — either follow Beis Shammai in its entirety, or follow Beis Hillel in its entirety.

### Stringency Logic


A qualification emerges: not all combinations of stringencies are equally problematic. The Gemara notes elsewhere — and the issue is relevant here — that combining stringencies is most condemned when the stringencies are *logically dependent* and thus internally contradictory. An example from the previous daf illustrates the point: Beis Hillel and Beis Shammai dispute whether a skeleton missing a single vertebra causes *tumah b'ohel*. The same threshold that governs *tumah b'ohel* also governs *treifah*: if a missing vertebra renders a skeleton halachically incomplete for purposes of *tumah*, the same criterion renders a living animal a *treifah*. One cannot therefore be stringent like Beis Hillel for *tumah* (requiring two missing vertebrae) and simultaneously stringent like Beis Shammai for *treifah* (requiring only one). The positions are logically linked, and applying each stringently in its own domain produces a formal contradiction.

By contrast, if two unrelated disputes — say, about *sukkah* and about *pesach* — both involve a Beis Hillel–Beis Shammai disagreement, being stringent in both does not produce logical contradiction. One may simply be uncertain who to *pasken* like and defaults to stringency in each domain independently. This is not ideal — the preferred approach is genuine decision-making rather than reflexive stringency — but it does not carry the same condemnation as adopting contradictory positions to generate a combined stringency. The fool walking in darkness is specifically the one whose combined stringencies are logically self-defeating.

This is precisely what Rava did: he ruled that the *turbatz* is *not* a *makom shechita* (Shmuel's position) yet that a *mashehu* hole there constitutes a *treifah* (Rav's position) — two stringencies whose justifications, as the Gemara had articulated them, are logically dependent. Rav's *mashehu* derives directly from the *turbatz* being a *makom shechita*; the two cannot be separated without incoherence.

### Bat Kol Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גּוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ: לְעוֹלָם הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: וְהָרוֹצֶה לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי יַעֲשֶׂה! לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן – קוֹדֶם בַּת קוֹל, כָּאן – לְאַחַר בַּת קוֹל. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, אַף לְאַחַר בַּת קוֹל, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין מַשְׁגִּיחִין בְּבַת קוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to the wording of the *baraita*: **This** *baraita* **itself** is **difficult.** First **you say** that the ***halakha*** is **always in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel, and then** you **teach** that **one who wishes to act in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai may do so.** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. Here,** the statement that a person may act as he wishes was made **before the Divine Voice** emerged and announced that the *halakha* is always in accordance with Beit Hillel. **There,** the statement that the *halakha* is always in accordance with Beit Hillel was made **after the Divine Voice** issued this ruling. **And if you wish, say** instead that **even** the statement that a person may act as he wishes was made **after the Divine Voice** announced that the *halakha* is in accordance with Beit Hillel, **and** this statement **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who says: One disregards a Divine Voice** that attempts to intervene in matters of *halakha*. According to him, the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel has not yet been decided.

Before resolving the problem of Rava's ruling, the Gemara addresses an internal tension within the *braita* itself. The *braita* opens with *le'olam halacha k'divrei Beis Hillel* — the ruling always follows Beis Hillel — and then in the very next clause states that one may choose to rule like Beis Shammai if one wishes. How can both be true?

The Gemara answers: *kan kodem bas kol, kan le'achar bas kol* — one clause reflects the period before the *bat kol* and the other reflects the period after it. The famous *bat kol* was the heavenly voice that intervened in the ancient deadlock between Beis Hillel and Beis Shammai. The two schools disputed not merely individual laws but the meta-question of how to resolve their disputes — Beis Hillel argued from numerical majority, Beis Shammai from qualitative superiority. Each school naturally advocated the standard that would vindicate itself, producing a genuine logjam: the very rules for deciding the question were themselves in dispute.

Into this impasse, a *bat kol* declared: *eilu v'eilu divrei Elokim chayim* — both these and these are the words of the living God, both positions reflect legitimate divine intent — *ve'halacha k'divrei Beis Hillel*: yet in practice, the ruling follows Beis Hillel. The Gemara elsewhere notes that the declaration specified reasons: Beis Hillel distinguished themselves by their humility and by regularly acknowledging Beis Shammai's views alongside their own. The point is that the *bat kol* did not simply pick a side; it affirmed pluralism while establishing a practical norm for *psak*. Before that declaration, either school was a legitimate address for practical ruling, which is what the *braita*'s permissive clause reflects. After it, the ruling is definitively with Beis Hillel.

One might also note that the *yerushalmi* understands *bat kol* more broadly — not necessarily as a supernatural voice, but as something closer to an emergent consensus, a report of general opinion that crystallized over time. On that reading, the *braita* captures two historical stages: an earlier period of genuine open choice, and a later period once consensus had crystallized around Beis Hillel.

### Eliezer-Yehoshua


The Gemara then raises a second resolution of the *braita*'s internal tension: *v'iba'it eima, af achar bas kol, Rabbi Yehoshua de'amar ein mashgichin b'bas kol* — even after the *bat kol*, one could say the choice remains open, in the view of Rabbi Yehoshua who holds that we do not defer to a heavenly voice in halachic dispute.

This is the celebrated principle from the famous episode of the *tanur shel achnai*: Rabbi Eliezer invoked miraculous signs — walls bending, a river flowing backwards, and finally a voice from heaven — to support his position, and Rabbi Yehoshua rose and declared *lo bashamayim hi*: the Torah is not in heaven. God has no vote in halachic deliberation; we follow the rules of the earthly academy, which means majority decision-making. The Talmud famously adds that God laughed with delight, saying: "My children have defeated Me."

According to Rabbi Yehoshua's principle, the *bat kol* in the Hillel-Shammai dispute likewise carries no binding authority, leaving the question of which school to follow genuinely open — and thus the *braita*'s two clauses coexist without tension even in a single time-frame.

### Bat Kol Role


Tosafot observes a difficulty: we follow Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling that we do not heed the *bat kol* — but we also follow Beis Hillel, which was established by a *bat kol*. Does that not undercut the very basis of the ruling like Beis Hillel?

Tosafot's resolution is precise. In the case of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, the rules of decision-making were clear: Rabbi Eliezer was in the minority and had been outvoted by his colleagues. The *bat kol* was effectively trying to override a straightforward application of the majority rule that governs *psak*. In that situation, *ein mashgichin b'bas kol* — the heavenly voice has no standing to override an established procedural rule that produces a determinate outcome. By contrast, the Hillel-Shammai impasse arose precisely because the meta-rules for deciding the dispute were themselves in dispute. There was no procedure that could be applied without already presupposing an answer to the question at hand. Only an external intervention — the *bat kol* — could break the deadlock. There, we accept the *bat kol* not as a substantive halachic ruling but as a procedural resolution to an otherwise irresolvable logjam.

The Gemara then returns to the original difficulty: even after all this, how did Rava rule as he did? *amar rav tavos: kulei k'rava avda* — the answer is that Rava's ruling actually follows a coherent single position, because there is another tradition of Rav's view. Rami bar Yechezkel testified: do not follow what Yehuda my brother reported in Rav's name; here is what Rav actually said: *veshet natnu bo chachamim shiur* — the sages established a defined boundary for the *veshet*, beyond which it is no longer considered the *veshet* for *halacha*. That upper zone — the *turbatz* — is therefore not a *makom shechita*. But Rav never retracted his ruling that a *mashehu* hole in the *turbatz* constitutes a *treifah*. On this tradition, Rav himself held both positions simultaneously: the *turbatz* is not a *makom shechita*, and yet a tiny hole there renders the animal a *treifah*. There is no logical contradiction in holding both, because there are other organs for which a *mashehu* suffices for *treifah* even though they are not the *veshet*. So Rava was not grafting two contradictory authorities; he was faithfully transmitting a single coherent position of Rav.

The Gemara has thus resolved the practical ruling — but it is worth noting that it does not offer an alternative route of saying that Rava was entitled to combine the stringencies independently because they are not logically contradictory. The Gemara specifically requires Rav himself to have held both positions. This implies that the standard is not merely logical non-contradiction but consistency of authority: one must follow a *posek* consistently, not simply adopt whatever positions happen not to contradict each other.

## Esophagus Boundaries

### Upper Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעְלָה עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עַד כְּדֵי תְּפִיסַת יָד. לְמַטָּה עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: עַד כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּשְׂעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Since it was mentioned that the Sages gave a measure defining the portion of the gullet that is valid for slaughter, the Gemara asks: **How far up** the gullet is the upper boundary for valid slaughter? **Rav Naḥman said:** It is **until** the point that there remains only **sufficient** space for **a hand** to **grip** the gullet. The Gemara asks: **How far down** is the lower boundary? **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Until** the gullet **becomes hairy,** i.e., until the opening of the rumen, whose lining is hairy.

The Gemara now examines how far the *veshet* extends in each direction. *lema'la ad kama* — how high up does the *veshet* go? Rav Nachman answers: *ad kedei tfisas yad* — until the span of a hand-grip. Rashi suggests this means roughly three to four fingers' breadth, though another reading yields two fingers. The question being addressed here is not primarily the *makom shechita* — that has largely been covered — but rather the upper boundary for purposes of *treifah*: beyond a certain point upward, a hole no longer constitutes a *treifah* because the tissue is no longer considered *veshet*.

### Lower Boundary


*Lemata ad kama* — and how far downward? Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *ad kedei sheyis'ar* — until it becomes hairy. This refers to the anatomical distinction between the esophagus and the stomach (*keres*): the upper portion of the stomach near the esophageal junction is lined with a rougher, felt-like hairy texture, while the lower stomach is smooth. Where the tissue begins to exhibit that hairy character, the stomach has begun and the esophagus has ended. This is not merely an anatomical observation; it is a halachic boundary. Below that point, the rules governing the *keres* apply rather than those governing the *veshet*.

### Stomach Sections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רָבִינָא אָמַר גְּנִיבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: טֶפַח בַּוֶּושֶׁט סָמוּךְ לַכָּרֵס – זֶהוּ כָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִי. אַמַּאי? כִּי קָא שָׁחֵט בְּכָרֵס קָא שָׁחֵיט! אֵימָא: טֶפַח בְּכָרֵס סָמוּךְ לַוֶּושֶׁט – זֶהוּ כָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִי. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי קָאָמַר רַב – בְּתוֹרָא, דְּמַשְׂעַר טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Ravina say** that **Geneiva says in the name of Rav:** The lowermost **handbreadth in the gullet, adjacent to the rumen, this is the inner rumen?** If so, **why** does Rav Naḥman permit slaughter until the opening of the rumen? **When one slaughters** within the bottom handbreadth, **he is slaughtering in the rumen,** and his slaughter should be invalid. Rather, **say** that Rav's statement should be amended, as follows: The uppermost **handbreadth in the rumen, adjacent to the gullet, this is the inner rumen,** which is not a valid location for slaughter. By contrast, the lowermost handbreadth of the gullet is a valid location for slaughter. And **if you wish, say** instead that **when Rav says** that the lowermost handbreadth of the gullet is considered the rumen, he is referring specifically **to a bull, which is especially hairy,** and hairs appear even within the lowermost handbreadth of the gullet itself. By contrast, in other animals, the entire lower gullet is a valid location for slaughter.

The Gemara qualifies this with a statement of Ravina citing Kineva in Rav's name: *tefach ba-veshet samuch la-keres zehu keres ha-penimi* — a handbreadth of the esophagus right near the stomach is considered the inner stomach (*keres ha-penimi*). The Gemara immediately corrects the reading: it does not mean a handbreadth of esophagus is reclassified as stomach — that would be anatomically strange — but rather a handbreadth of *stomach* nearest the esophagus constitutes the inner *keres*. This is the hairy zone visible near the esophageal junction, which the Gemara identifies as the *keres ha-penimi* referenced in the Mishnah. The outer *keres* is the smooth portion of the stomach further from the esophageal inlet. The distinction matters because the Mishnah assigns different *shiurim* for the hole-size that constitutes *treifah* in each section.

What emerges is a layered anatomy: the *veshet* proper, the hairy band of *keres ha-penimi* immediately adjacent to the esophageal inlet, and then the smoother *keres ha-chitzon* beyond. The halachic boundary between *veshet* and *keres* is located where the hairy texture begins.

### Animal Variations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תּוּרְבַּץ הַוֶּושֶׁט שֶׁנִּיטַּל כּוּלּוֹ מִלֶּחִי – כָּשֵׁר, וּתְנָא תּוּנָא: נִיטַּל לֶחִי הַתַּחְתּוֹן – כָּשֵׁר. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: וְהָאִיכָּא עִיקּוּר סִימָנִים? וּלְרַב פָּפָּא קַשְׁיָא מַתְנִיתִין: נִיטַּל לֶחִי הַתַּחְתּוֹן – כָּשֵׁר. בִּשְׁלָמָא מַתְנִיתִין לְרַב פָּפָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאִיעֲקוּר אִיעֲקוֹרֵי, הָא דְּאִיגּוּם אִיגּוֹמֵי מֵעִילָּוֵי סִימָנִים, אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל קַשְׁיָא! לָא תֵּימָא כּוּלּוֹ, אֶלָּא אֵימָא רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** If **the entrance of the gullet was completely detached from the jaw,** the animal is **kosher. And the *tanna*** of the mishna also **taught** this later (54a): If **the lower jaw was detached** entirely, it is **kosher. Rav Pappa objects to this:** How can Shmuel say that if the entrance of the gullet is detached the animal is kosher? **But isn't there ripping of the *simanim*,** the gullet and the windpipe, from their place? This should render the animal a *tereifa*. The Gemara interjects: **But according to Rav Pappa, the mishna** is **difficult** as well, since it states: If **the lower jaw was detached,** it is **kosher.** If the lower jaw is detached, the windpipe and gullet will necessarily be detached as well, since they are attached to it. The Gemara responds: **Granted, the mishna** is **not difficult for Rav Pappa. This** *halakha*, that the ripping of the *simanim* renders the animal a *tereifa*, applies only **when** the *simanim* are **completely ripped** from the jaw. **That** statement of the mishna, that an animal whose jaw is detached is kosher, is referring to a case **where** the lower jaw **is severed above** its connection to the ***simanim*,** which themselves remain connected to the remaining flesh of the jaw. **But** the *halakha* that the ripping of the *simanim* renders the animal a *tereifa* is **difficult for Shmuel.** How can an animal remain kosher when the entrance of its gullet is removed, if this entails the ripping of the *simanim*? The Gemara responds: **Do not say** that Shmuel deems the animal kosher if the entrance of the gullet was **completely** detached. **Rather, say** that he deems it kosher only if it was **mostly** detached.

The Gemara notes a complication specific to one type of animal. *ki kama rav be-tura de-mas'ir tfeh* — Rav's statement about the hairy boundary applies to an ox (*tura*), which grows this hairy texture particularly well, beginning already at the very bottom of the esophagus or near it. In other animals such as goats and sheep, the corresponding texture does not appear at the base of the esophagus — there, the esophagus proper extends all the way to its terminus, and only the stomach itself exhibits the hairy characteristic. The practical implication is that in an ox, what might appear to be esophageal tissue near the juncture may already be classified as *keres ha-penimi*, while in smaller ruminants the same tissue would still be classified as *veshet*.

The Gemara additionally notes the related topic of *simanim she-nildedu* — displaced or dislocated *simanim* (trachea and esophagus) — which constitutes a *treifah* or at minimum invalidates a *shechita*. The cases of the *turbatz* having been removed from the jaw (*nital lechi ha-tachton kashar*) and the question of *ikur simanim* are distinguished: surgical removal in which the *simanim* remain embedded in surrounding tissue does not constitute *ikur simanim*, while a violent dislocation in which they are wrenched free does. Similarly, being "peeled" gently from the skin differs from being "yanked" away: the latter produces a *treifah* because it cannot heal, while the former, when limited to a majority rather than total separation, may be treated more leniently.

## Trachea Cut Standard

### Trachea Majority Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת. תָּנָא: כַּמָּה פְּסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת? בְּרוּבָּהּ. וְכַמָּה רוּבָּה? רַב אָמַר: רוֹב עוֹבְיָהּ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: רוֹב חֲלָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **Or** an animal with **a cut windpipe,** cut across its width, is a *tereifa*. With regard to this the Sages **taught: How much** must **the windpipe** be **cut** to render the animal a *tereifa*? **In its majority. And how much is its majority? Rav says: The majority of its width,** counting the width of the windpipe wall itself, the thickness of which is uneven. **And some say** that Rav says: The **majority of its space,** the inner area of the cross section of the windpipe, not counting the width of the windpipe wall.

The Gemara now turns to the *gargeret* (trachea). The Mishnah established that a *treifah* in the *gargeret* requires *pesikah* — a cut — of the majority. A hole alone does not suffice; there must be a significant severing. The Gemara asks: *kama psukat ha-gargeret? — b'ruba* — what constitutes "cut"? The majority. And how do we measure majority?

### Measurement Methods


Here the Gemara introduces a fundamental dispute about the unit of measurement. Rav says *rov ovya* — the majority of its thickness, meaning the majority of the material of the tracheal wall measured as solid matter. Others report Rav as saying *rov chalala* — the majority of its hollow, meaning one has cut through the majority of the interior space of the tube.

The difference is consequential because the trachea is not geometrically uniform: one side of the tracheal wall is substantially thicker than the other. If we measure by the total thickness of the solid material — *rov ovya* — we reach the majority threshold before we have cut through half of the actual interior space. This makes *rov ovya* the more stringent standard. If we measure by how much of the hollow has been traversed — *rov chalala* — we require more cutting before declaring a *treifah*, making that the more lenient standard.

### Rav's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא פְּסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, יָתֵיב וְקָא בָּדֵיק לַהּ בְּרוֹב עוֹבְיָהּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: לִימַּדְתָּנוּ רַבֵּינוּ בְּרוֹב חֲלָלָהּ! שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, בַּדְקַהּ בְּרוֹב חֲלָלָהּ וְאַכְשְׁרַהּ, וּזְבַן מִינַּהּ בִּתְלֵיסַר אִיסְתֵּירֵי פְּשִׁיטֵי בִּישְׂרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: There was **a certain** animal with **a cut windpipe that came before Rav,** i.e., it was brought for inspection to decide whether it was kosher. Rav **was sitting and checking it** to see if the windpipe had been cut **in the majority of its width. Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav:** Didn't **you teach us, our teacher,** that a cut windpipe is measured by the **majority of its space?** Rav **sent** the animal **before Rabba bar bar Ḥana,** who **checked it in the majority of its space and deemed it kosher, and purchased meat from it** at the price of **thirteen plain *istera* coins.**

A case came before Rav himself: *hahi psukat ha-gargeret de'atai lekamei de-Rav, yativ ve-kabdik lei b'rov ovya* — he sat and examined it using the *rov ovya* standard (majority of thickness), which is the more stringent measure. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi then told him: *rabbenu, you taught us* that the standard is *rov chalala* (majority of hollow). The existence of two versions of what Rav said may itself reflect this episode: Rav was inconsistent, or at least ambivalent, and his students had received different traditions. Faced with their reminder, Rav sent the case to Rabbah bar bar Chana for a further opinion.

### Rabbah's Ruling


Rabbah bar bar Chana examined the trachea using the *rov chalala* standard — the more lenient measure — and declared it *kasher*. Once it was declared *kasher*, he purchased meat from it for thirteen *istarei pshitei* — the value of thirteen coins of some denomination, essentially a regular purchase. This purchase then becomes the springboard for the Gemara's extended discussion of procedural *psak* in the following section.

## Overriding Rulings

### Non-Overriding Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: חָכָם שֶׁטִּימֵּא – אֵין חֲבֵירוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְטַהֵר, אָסַר – אֵין חֲבֵירוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְהַתִּיר! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּרַב לָא אֲסַר מֵיסָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how** could Rabba bar bar Ḥana **do this,** i.e., deem permitted an animal that Rav was going to deem prohibited? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If **a halakhic authority deemed** an item **impure, another** halakhic authority **is not allowed to deem** it **pure;** likewise, if he **prohibited** it, **another** authority **is not allowed to permit** it? The Gemara responds: It **is different here, since Rav did not** actually **prohibit** the animal. He merely considered doing so, but he sent it to Rabba bar bar Chana before issuing a formal ruling.

*V'heichi avid hachi* — how could he have done this? The Gemara cites the well-known rule: *chacham she-timah, ein chavero rashai le-taher; assar, ein chavero rashai le-hatir* — if one sage declared something *tamei*, a colleague may not declare it *tahor*; if one declared it forbidden, a colleague may not permit it. One *posek*'s ruling cannot be overridden by another.

At first glance, the question seems puzzling — Rav himself had not yet issued a final ruling but had simply begun applying a standard, and the case was then forwarded to Rabbah bar bar Chana. But the Gemara's answer clarifies the exact scope of the principle.

### No Ruling Exception


*Shani hacha de-Rav lo assar meissar* — the case of Rav is different because Rav never actually issued a ruling (*psak*) of forbidden. He was examining the animal using a measuring standard that would have led to a ruling of *treifah*, but he had not yet declared it so. Since no *psak* was formally issued, there was nothing for Rabbah bar bar Chana to override. The principle against overriding applies once a ruling has been given; before a ruling is issued, referring the matter to another authority is entirely legitimate.

This carries an important implication: even if a *posek* who has issued a *psak* wishes to have it reconsidered, he cannot simply invite a colleague to override him. He may reconsider and reverse himself, but a different authority cannot issue a contrary ruling — even at the original *posek*'s invitation.

### Benefit from Psak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דְּאוֹרִי בַּהּ חָכָם, הֵיכִי אֲכַל מִינַּהּ? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וָאֹמַר אֲהָהּ ה׳ אֱלֹהִים הִנֵּה נַפְשִׁי לֹא מְטֻמָּאָה וּנְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה לֹא אָכַלְתִּי מִנְּעוּרַי וְעַד עַתָּה וְלֹא בָא בְּפִי בְּשַׂר פִּגּוּל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And once a halakhic authority has ruled with regard to** the animal, even to permit it, **how** could Rabba bar bar Ḥana **eat from it? But isn't it written: "Then I said: Alas, Lord God, my soul has not become impure; and from my youth until now I have not eaten an unslaughtered carcass, or a *tereifa*; and no *piggul* flesh came into my mouth"** (Ezekiel 4:14).

The Gemara then raises a second question: even if Rabbah bar bar Chana's ruling was procedurally valid, how did he allow himself to benefit by purchasing the meat? *v'kivan de'orei bah chacham heichi achal minei* — once a sage had issued a *psak* on an animal, how may he eat from it?

### Yechezkel's Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִנֵּה נַפְשִׁי לֹא מְטֻמָּאָה״ – שֶׁלֹּא הִרְהַרְתִּי בַּיּוֹם לָבֹא לִידֵי טוּמְאָה בַּלַּיְלָה, ״וּנְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ – שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי בְּשַׂר ״כּוֹס כּוֹס״ מֵעוֹלָם, ״וְלֹא בָא בְּפִי בְּשַׂר פִּגּוּל״ – שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי מִבְּהֵמָה שֶׁהוֹרָה בָּהּ חָכָם. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נָתָן אָמְרוּ: שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי מִבְּהֵמָה שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ מַתְּנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages interpreted the verse as follows: **"My soul has not been become impure"** means **that I did not think** of sexual thoughts **during the day** so as **to come to** the **impurity** of a seminal emission **at night. "I have not eaten an unslaughtered carcass, or a *tereifa*"** means **that I never ate** the **flesh of** an animal that was in danger of imminent death, leading one to say: **Slaughter** it, **slaughter** it quickly, before it dies. **"And no *piggul* flesh came into my mouth,"** means **that I never ate from an animal** with regard to **which** there was uncertainty whether it is prohibited and **a Sage issued a ruling** to permit it. The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Natan:** The phrase means **that I never ate from an animal** from **which the gifts** of the priesthood, the foreleg, jaw, and abomasum, **were not separated.** The above acts are technically permitted but unseemly. How, then, could Rabba bar bar Ḥana consume the meat of this animal?

The Gemara invokes Yechezkel: *hinei nafshi lo metuma'ah, u'neveilah u'treifah lo achalti mine'urai, v'ad atah lo ba befi besar pigul* — Yechezkel declared that his soul was never defiled, that he never ate *neveilah* or *treifah*, and that no *pigul* ever entered his mouth. The Gemara parses this: the claim not to have eaten *treifah* cannot be taken at face value — of course Yechezkel did not eat *treifah*. Rather, *shelo achalti mibeheimah shehorah bah chacham* — he never ate from an animal that had required a rabbinic ruling. Any meat that had been a *shailah* — that needed a *posek* to determine its status — he would not eat.

The Gemara also records a parallel reading in the name of Rav Nasan: Yechezkel's declaration meant he never ate from an animal whose *matnos* (priestly gifts) had not been properly set aside. Both interpretations are preserved. The *yechezkel* standard is presented as an ideal — stringent and difficult to maintain practically — not as a binding norm.

### Judgment vs. Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי – מִילְּתָא דְתַלְיָא בִּסְבָרָא, רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אַגְּמָרֵיהּ סְמַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This statement,** that it is unseemly for a halakhic authority to rely on his own ruling to permit the meat, applies only to **a matter that depends on reasoning. Rabba bar bar Ḥana relied on his learning,** i.e., a received tradition. There is nothing unseemly about relying upon a received tradition.

*Hani milei milsa de-talya be-svara* — this scruple applies specifically when the ruling was a matter of judgment (*svara*), an assessment call where the *posek* weighed the evidence and reached a conclusion that another equally competent authority might have reached differently. There one can reasonably worry that the ruling was mistaken. But *rabbah bar bar chana agmarei samich* — Rabbah bar bar Chana's ruling was not a matter of his own judgment; it rested on a received tradition (*gemara*) that the standard is *rov chalala* rather than *rov ovya*. When a ruling is simply the application of a received tradition rather than a personal assessment call, the concern about error does not apply in the same way, and the *yechezkel* standard is not invoked.

### Suspicion Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא, דְּתַנְיָא: דָּן אֶת הַדִּין, זִיכָּה וְחַיָּיב, טִימֵּא וְטִיהֵר, אָסַר וְהִתִּיר, וְכֵן הָעֵדִים שֶׁהֵעִידוּ – כּוּלָּן רַשָּׁאִין לִיקַּח, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: הַרְחֵק מִן הַכִּיעוּר וּמִן הַדּוֹמֶה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** let one **derive** that Rabba bar bar Ḥana should not have purchased the meat **due to suspicion, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **issued a judgment, acquitted or convicted, deemed impure or pure, prohibited or permitted; or** if **witnesses testified** with regard to a case, in **all of** these instances the judges or witnesses **are allowed to purchase** the item that they deemed permitted. **But the Sages said: Distance** yourself **from unseemliness and from** things **similar to it.** If so, Rabba bar bar Ḥana should not have purchased the meat that he himself permitted.

A further concern is then raised: *v'sipok lei mishum chashada* — even apart from the *yechezkel* standard, should he not refrain from purchasing the meat to avoid the appearance of impropriety? The *braita* teaches: *dan et ha-din, zikey ve-chiyev, timei ve-tiher, assur ve-hitir... kullan resha'in likkach* — a judge who ruled on a property dispute, a sage who declared something *tamei* or *tahor*, *assur* or *muttar*, may technically engage in commerce with the party they favored. But *aval amru Chachamim, harchak min ha-kiyur u-min ha-domeh lo* — the sages say: distance yourself from what is ugly and from what resembles it. The concern is the appearance that the ruling was influenced by anticipated personal benefit — that the *posek* declared the animal *kasher* precisely because he wanted to buy the meat cheaply.

### Rava's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִידֵּי דִּמְזַבֵּין מִשּׁוּמָא, הָכָא מַתְקְלָא מוֹכַח, כִּי הָא דְּרַבָּה שְׁרָא טְרֵפְתָּא וּזְבַן מִינַּהּ בִּישְׂרָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ בַּת רַב חִסְדָּא: אַבָּא שָׁרֵי בּוּכְרָא וְלָא זָבֵן מִינֵּיהּ בִּישְׂרָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בּוּכְרָא דְּאַשּׁוּמָא מִזְדְּבַן, הָכָא מַתְקְלָא מוֹכַח, מַאי אִיכָּא – מִשּׁוּם אוּמְצָא מְעַלַּיְיתָא? כֹּל יוֹמָא אוּמְצָא מְעַלַּיְיתָא זַבִּנוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This statement** applies only to **an item that is sold** based on **an appraisal** of its value and not by standard measure. In such cases onlookers might suspect that the judge is receiving a favorable price in return for his judgment. But **here, the weight** of the meat **proves** that the judge is not receiving a discount, but is paying the standard price. This is **like that** incident **where Rabba permitted** a possible ***tereifa*** for consumption **and bought meat from it.** His wife, **the daughter of Rav Ḥisda, said to him: Father permitted a firstborn** animal, declaring that it possessed a blemish that renders it permitted for consumption, **but did not buy meat from it.** Why are you acting differently? Rabba **said to her: That matter** applies to **a firstborn, which is sold** based **on appraisal** of its value. **Here, the weight** of the meat **proves** that I am paying the standard price and not unfairly deriving benefit from my judgment. **What** suspicion **is there** in this case? Will people suspect me **because** I received **a superior piece of meat? Every day they sell me a superior piece of meat.**

The Gemara resolves: *hani mili be-midei de-mizben be-shuma* — this concern applies when the item is sold by estimated valuation, where it is plausible that the *posek* received a favorable estimate. *hacha be-matkla muchach* — in this case, the meat was sold by weight, a fully objective measure. No special deal could have been extended, and so there is no appearance of impropriety.

The Gemara then closes with a parallel story to illustrate the principle. Rava permitted an animal on a question of *treifah* and then purchased meat from it. His wife, the daughter of Rav Chisda, challenged him: her father, when he would permit a *bechor* by ruling that it had a *mum* (blemish) sufficient to permit it for ordinary consumption, would never purchase the meat from it. Why are you doing so?

Rava replied: *hani mili bechor de-vishuma mizdaben* — with a *bechor* that is sold by estimated valuation, there is genuine reason for concern that the ruling and the subsequent purchase look collusive. *hacha be-matkla muchach* — here, where it is sold by weight, it is demonstrable that no advantage was obtained. His wife pressed: even buying by weight, might he not have received a choice cut — *umtza malya* — a particularly desirable piece? Rava replied simply: *kol yoma umtza malya zavni li* — the butchers always give me the best cut because I am the rabbi. That raises other concerns about the relationship between rabbis and the marketplace, he acknowledged, but it does not raise concerns about the appearance of corrupt *psak*, which is the relevant issue here.

Tosafot notes a further nuance: the principle that a *posek* may technically benefit after issuing a ruling may be limited to contexts where multiple judges are involved, since collusion among several judges is less plausible than in a case of a single judge ruling alone. Whether a sole *posek* is even theoretically permitted to benefit from his ruling — or whether it is formally prohibited rather than merely discouraged — remains, in Tosafot's view, genuinely unresolved.
