# Makkot 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Review

### Opening Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אָכַל טֶבֶל שֶׁל מַעְשַׂר עָנִי – לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** If one **ate untithed produce** from which *teruma* and first tithe were separated and **poor man's tithe** was not separated, he is **flogged.**

Today's *daf yomi* covers Makkot 17, picking up at the bottom of 16b, approximately ten lines from the bottom. The preceding material involved a sustained discussion of *lav hanitak la'aseh* — a negative prohibition that is linked to a positive commandment — and the related principles of *bitlo v'lo bitlo* and *kiyum v'lo kiyum*: whether one has actively nullified or fulfilled the positive commandment that accompanies the prohibition. Classic cases included the get given to a divorcée one is forbidden to remarry, and whether actively remarrying her constitutes a nullification of the positive commandment.

### Multiple Aveiros & Bal T…

The preceding sugya also addressed the consumption of non-kosher food, including a characteristic *gemara* discussion about the multiple *aveiros* incurred when eating particular insects, and an important treatment of the prohibition of *bal teshaktzu* — the commandment not to render oneself abominable. The *gemara* focused this prohibition on eating repulsive substances, including, in a somewhat unexpected case, drinking from a vessel used in the process of bloodletting, where one ends up ingesting blood in the course of drinking. That digression, though brief, carries significant practical weight for the general concept of *bal teshaktzu*.

## Tevel & Ma'asrot

### Tevel Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן – כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הַטֶּבֶל שֶׁלֹּא הוּרַם מִמֶּנּוּ כׇּל עִיקָּר; הוּרַם מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה וְלֹא הוּרַם מִמֶּנּוּ מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן, מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Rav issue this ruling? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** One **might** have thought **that one is liable for** eating **only untithed produce from which no** gifts **were taken at all;** but if ***teruma gedola* was taken from** the produce, **but first tithe was not taken from it,** or if the **first tithe** was separated **but not second tithe, or even** if only **poor man's tithe** was not separated, **from where** is it derived that the halakhic status of the produce is that of untithed produce and one is liable for eating it?

The *gemara* now turns to the case in the *mishnah* dealing with the consumption of *tevel* — grain from which *terumos u'ma'asros* have not been properly separated — as well as the eating of *ma'aser sheni* outside of Jerusalem without redemption.

### Rav's Position

The *gemara* states: *achal tevel she-hu ma'aser ani, lokeh* — Rav rules that one who eats *tevel* from which only *ma'aser ani* has not yet been separated still incurs *malkos*.

### Hierarchy of Separations

This requires some context. Classic *tevel* is produce from which *terumah* has not been taken. Eating *terumah* is a prohibition that carries *chiyuv misah bidei shamayim*, and *tevel* that has not had *terumah* separated partakes of that severity. Similarly, if *ma'aser rishon* has not been taken, the *ma'aser rishon* within it contains *terumas ma'aser*, which is forbidden to non-*kohanim* — it too has an *issur* dimension. And *ma'aser sheni* that has not been separated is forbidden to eat outside Jerusalem.

### Ma'aser Ani Case

But what of *tevel* where the only thing not yet separated is *ma'aser ani*? In the years governed by *ma'aser ani* rather than *ma'aser sheni*, once *ma'aser ani* is separated it has no *issur* status — it simply belongs to the poor. So the question is whether consuming grain from which only *ma'aser ani* has not yet been separated rises to the level of *tevel* punishable by *malkos*, or whether it amounts to no more than a form of *gezel* — wrongfully withholding property owed to the poor — without the full gravity of *tevel*.

### Braitah: Rabbi Yosei

Rav's ruling is established as following a *tanna*, as it is taught in a *braitah*: Rabbi Yosei asks, might one think that *chiyuv* applies only to *tevel* from which nothing at all has been separated? Perhaps if *terumah gedolah* was taken but not *ma'aser rishon*, or *ma'aser rishon* but not *ma'aser sheni*, each of these partial states still involves something with genuine *issur* or *kedushah* status — but how do we know that one is liable even when only *ma'aser ani* has not been taken?

### Scriptural Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״ וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאָכְלוּ בִשְׁעָרֶיךָ וְשָׂבֵעוּ״, מָה לְהַלָּן מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, אַף כָּאן מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״לֹא תוּכַל״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: It is derived as **the verse states: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain or of your wine or of your oil" (Deuteronomy 12:17), **and there it states:** "And you shall give to the Levite, to the convert, to the orphan, and to the widow, **and they shall eat within your gates and be satisfied"** (Deuteronomy 26:12). **Just as there,** with regard to the phrase "and they shall eat within your gates," it is referring to **poor man's tithe, here too,** "you may not eat within your gates" is referring to produce in which there is **poor man's tithe,** as it has not yet been separated, **and the Merciful One states** a prohibition: **You may not** eat it.

The derivation proceeds from Deuteronomy: *lo suchal le'echol bish'arecha ma'aser degancha tiroshcha v'yitzharecha* and so on — a list of things forbidden to be consumed outside one's gates, i.e., outside Jerusalem. Later the verse says *ve'achlu bish'arecha ve'save'u*, referring to the poor eating *ma'aser ani* within one's gates. Through a *gezerah shavah* on the word *bish'arecha*, the prohibition of *lo suchal le'echol* is linked to *ma'aser ani*: just as there it refers to *ma'aser ani*, so here the *ma'aser* mentioned in the prohibition refers to *ma'aser ani*. There is no prohibition against eating *ma'aser ani* itself outside Jerusalem, so the only possible meaning is that grain from which *ma'aser ani* has not been separated retains the status of *tevel* and the prohibition applies. Even the weakest form of *tevel* — produce awaiting only the *ma'aser ani* separation — is included in the *mishnah*'s case.

## Demai Debate

### Demai Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּתַנָּאֵי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין צָרִיךְ לִקְרוֹת אֶת הַשֵּׁם עַל מַעְשַׂר עָנִי שֶׁל דְּמַאי, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: קוֹרֵא אֶת הַשֵּׁם, וְאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַפְרִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This matter is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. Rabbi Eliezer says: One need not** separate by means of **calling the name upon poor man's tithe of doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*].** With regard to produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz*, i.e., one who is unreliable with regard to tithes, there is a rabbinic ordinance requiring one to separate first and second tithe and *teruma* of the tithe from it. Nevertheless, one is not required to separate poor man's tithe from that produce, because poor man's tithe is a monetary debt owed to the poor, and in a case of uncertainty, the principle is: The burden of proof rests upon the claimant. Rabbi Eliezer holds that failure to separate poor man's tithe does not accord the produce the status of untithed produce. **And the Rabbis say: He designates a portion of the produce by means of calling the name upon poor man's tithe, but he is not required to** physically **separate** that portion and give it to the poor.

The *gemara* continues: *amar Rav Yosef, kitnai* — this matter is itself a dispute among *tannaim*. The *gemara* quotes: Rabbi Eliezer says one need not designate a portion of *demai* grain as *ma'aser ani*, while the Sages say one must designate it — though one need not physically separate it.

*Demai* refers to grain purchased from an *am ha'aretz* — the ordinary working farmer — concerning whom, as the tradition records in connection with Yochanan the High Priest, there was established uncertainty about whether *terumos u'ma'asros* had been properly taken. The one separation the *am ha'aretz* was presumed to take was *terumah gedolah*, because its minimum amount is virtually negligible — a single grain suffices for an entire storehouse — and because eating *terumah* as a non-*kohen* carries extreme severity. Everything beyond that was uncertain.

### Separating from Demai

Accordingly, the Sages required one who purchased grain from an *am ha'aretz* to take *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser sheni*, even without certainty that these had not already been taken. The reason is that this is an *issur* concern, not purely a monetary one, and the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof falls on the claimant — governs only monetary disputes. Once the issue becomes one of *issur*, one must act conservatively.

### Separation Process

To illustrate: suppose one purchases a hundred units of grain. One separates ten units as *ma'aser rishon*. Of those ten, one unit is designated as *terumas ma'aser* — which must genuinely be given to a *kohen*, as it has clear *issur* status. The remaining nine are *ma'aser rishon* proper. Now, a *levi* may claim those nine, but since one cannot be certain the grain was not already tithed, this becomes a monetary dispute, and one may retain and eat the nine units. For *ma'aser sheni*, one separates it out and redeems it. For *ma'aser ani*, one separates it in principle, but since the claim of the poor is also a monetary matter — Gloria, as it were, representing the poor, cannot prove that those units weren't already taken — one may consume the designated *ma'aser ani* oneself without physically removing it.

This is the position of the *chachamim*: one must at least verbally designate a portion as *ma'aser ani* — the process of naming it removes the grain from its *tevel* status — even though one need not physically set it aside.

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Chacha…

Rabbi Eliezer's position is that one need not even designate *ma'aser ani* in the case of *demai*. The *gemara* initially considers whether this means Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with the ruling of Rav entirely — that is, perhaps Rabbi Eliezer holds that even in a case of definite *tevel* from which only *ma'aser ani* remains unseparated, there is no *tevel* status, and one who eats it commits only *gezel*, not *tevel*. After all, how else can one explain his position that no designation is required? Before designation, the grain is *tevel*; if he says no designation is needed, he must believe it is not *tevel*.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: וַדַּאי – טוֹבְלוֹ. וּמָר סָבַר: וַדַּאי – אֵינוֹ טוֹבְלוֹ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, אַדְּמִיפַּלְגִי בִּסְפֵיקוֹ, לִיפַּלְגוּ בְּוַדַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef suggests: **What, is it not** that **they disagree with regard to this: That** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that produce from which poor man's tithe was **certainly** not separated **is rendered untithed produce,** and therefore, in a case where there is uncertainty whether poor man's tithe was separated, one is required to separate it; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds** that produce from which poor man's tithe was **certainly** not separated **is not rendered untithed produce,** and therefore, one need not even designate poor man's tithe by calling its name upon a portion of the produce? **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **If** it is **so,** that this is the point in dispute, **rather than disagreeing with regard to** a case of **uncertainty** whether poor man's tithe was separated, **let them disagree with regard to** a case of **certainty** that poor man's tithe was not separated.

Abaye challenges this reading: *ei hachi, adamiflagi b'sfeiku liflgu b'vadai* — if they genuinely dispute the *tevel* status of grain awaiting *ma'aser ani*, they should argue directly about a case of definite *tevel*. The fact that the debate is specifically framed around *demai* suggests something narrower is at stake.

### Debate Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא וַדַּאי – טוֹבְלוֹ. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: לֹא נֶחְשְׁדוּ עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ עַל מַעְשַׂר עָנִי שֶׁל דְּמַאי. כֵּיוָן דְּמָמוֹנָא הוּא – אַפְרוֹשֵׁי מַפְרֵישׁ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כֵּיוָן דִּטְרִיחָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא – לָא מַפְרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** contrary to the previous suggestion, say **that everyone agrees** that produce from which poor man's tithe was **certainly** not separated **is rendered untithed produce. And here,** it is **with regard to this** that **they disagree:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds: *Amei ha'aretz* are not suspected of** refraining from separating **poor man's tithe of *demai*. Since it is** merely a matter of **money,** and no sanctity is involved, **he separates** poor man's tithe, although he does not actually give it to the poor. **And the Rabbis hold: Since the matter** involves **exertion for him, he does not** even **separate** poor man's tithe.

The *gemara* concludes that the debate is not about *tevel* status at all — both Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages agree that definite *tevel* awaiting *ma'aser ani* is fully *tevel*. Rather, the debate concerns the behavior of the *am ha'aretz*: does he or does he not, in practice, separate *ma'aser ani*?

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *am ha'aretz* do separate *ma'aser ani*, even though they neglect other separations. The reasoning, as Rashi explains, is somewhat counterintuitive. It is not primarily out of sympathy for the poor. Rather: since *ma'aser ani* carries no *issur* obligation after separation — it is purely a monetary obligation to the poor — an *am ha'aretz* can separate it without incurring any new *issur* headaches. By contrast, separating *ma'aser rishon* obligates one to deal with *terumas ma'aser* and its attendant obligations, and separating *ma'aser sheni* requires eating in Jerusalem or redemption. The *am ha'aretz* finds these burdensome. But *ma'aser ani* merely creates a monetary obligation — one he can technically discharge while still consuming the rest. So he does it. *Keivan d'mamona hu, afrushei mafrish* — since it is a monetary matter, he separates it.

The *chachamim*, however, hold that *am ha'aretz* does not bother with *ma'aser ani* either — *keivan d'tarcha leih milsa, lo mafrish* — any burden, however minor, is enough for him to forgo. Accordingly, one who purchases grain from an *am ha'aretz* must at minimum verbally designate a portion as *ma'aser ani* before consuming it. Both sides agree, however, that in a case of definite *tevel* — where one knows with certainty that *ma'aser ani* has not been taken — the grain is *tevel*, and eating it is punishable by *malkos*.

## Quantity for Lashes

### Mishnah's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כַּמָּה יֹאכַל מִן הַטֶּבֶל וְכוּ׳: אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּחִטָּה, אֲבָל בְּקֶמַח – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל כְּזַיִת. וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **How much does one** need to **eat from untithed produce** and be liable to receive lashes? Rabbi Shimon says: Even if one ate any amount of untithed produce he is liable to receive lashes. And the Rabbis say: He is liable only if he eats an olive-bulk. **Rav Beivai says** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** Their **dispute** is **with regard to** one who eats one kernel of **wheat** of untithed produce. **But with regard to flour, everyone agrees** that one is liable only if he eats **an olive-bulk. And Rabbi Yirmeya** says that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** Just **as** there is **a dispute with regard to this** case of a kernel of wheat, **so too,** there is **a dispute with regard to that** case of flour.

The *mishnah* next asks: *kama yochal min ha-tevel* — how much *tevel* must one eat to incur *malkos*? This launches a significant dispute. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even the smallest amount — even a single kernel of grain — is sufficient.

### Ke'Briato Concept

The *mishnah* uses the phrase *kebriato* — in its natural, created state. One grain of wheat is a discrete, whole unit. The question is whether qualitative wholeness of a natural unit can substitute for the quantitative *shi'ur* of a *kezayit* that is normally required to incur *malkos* for eating forbidden food.

This raises a deeper conceptual question. The standard *shi'ur* of *kezayit* might reflect either a threshold of meaningfulness — one must eat enough for it to matter — or it might reflect a question of whether the act constitutes "eating" in the legal sense. Rabbi Eliezer seems to say that a whole unit, by virtue of being qualitatively complete, is legally significant even below a *kezayit*.

### Ant vs. Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִי אַתֶּם מוֹדִים לִי בְּאוֹכֵל נְמָלָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא כִּבְרִיָּיתָהּ. אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף חִטָּה אַחַת כִּבְרִיָּיתָהּ. חִטָּה – אִין, קֶמַח – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof from that which **we learned** in the mishna: **Rabbi Shimon said to them: Do you not concede to me with regard to one who eats an ant of any size that he is liable** to receive lashes? The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Shimon: He is flogged for eating an ant of any size **due to** the fact **that** it is an intact entity in the form **of its creation.** Rabbi Shimon **said to them: One** kernel of **wheat is** also in the form **of its creation.** The Gemara infers from Rabbi Shimon's response: For a kernel of **wheat, yes,** one is liable; for any amount of **flour, no,** one is not liable. Apparently, even Rabbi Shimon concedes that one is liable for eating only an olive-bulk of wheat flour, in accordance with the statement that Rav Beivai says that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says.

Rav Bibi cites Resh Lakish: the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages concerns specifically one grain of wheat. If one is eating flour, *divrei hakol kezayit* — everyone agrees that a *kezayit* is required, since flour is simply a quantity of an undifferentiated substance. Rabbi Yirmiyah citing Resh Lakish, however, reads the dispute more broadly: Rabbi Eliezer holds there is no minimum quantity required for *malkos* under any circumstances.

To press his argument, Rabbi Shimon (not Rabbi Eliezer — a clarification the *gemara* makes) challenges the Sages: *ee atem modim li be'ochel nemala she-kol she-hu she-chayav* — surely you agree that one who eats even a single ant incurs *malkos*? The Sages respond: *mipnei she-hu kebriato* — yes, because the ant is in its natural created state, a whole living creature. Rabbi Shimon replies: *af chitah achat kebriato* — one grain of wheat is equally in its natural state. The Sages' distinction: *biryas neshamah chashuvah, chitah lo chashuvah* — a creature that once possessed life is qualitatively significant in a way that an inanimate kernel is not.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִבְרֵיהֶם קָאָמַר לְהוּ: לְדִידִי – אֲפִילּוּ קֶמַח נָמֵי, אֶלָּא לְדִידְכוּ – אוֹדוֹ לִי מִיהַת דְּחִטָּה אַחַת כִּבְרִיָּיתָהּ. וְרַבָּנַן: בְּרִיַּית נְשָׁמָה – חֲשִׁובָה, חִטָּה – לָא חֲשִׁובָה. תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַכּוֹת, לֹא אָמְרוּ כְּזַיִס אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is in accordance **with** the **statement of** the Rabbis that Rabbi Shimon **spoke to them,** and he meant as follows: According **to my** opinion, **even** if one ate any amount of **flour** he is **also** liable. **But according to your** opinion, **concede to me at least that** one is liable if he eats **one** kernel of **wheat,** as it is in the form **of its creation. And the Rabbis** say in response: There is a difference; **an entity** with **a soul,** i.e., a living creature, **is significant,** and one is liable for eating an entity of any volume. A kernel of **wheat is not significant.** The Gemara notes: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yirmeya,** that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One who eats **any amount** of food that is forbidden is liable **to** receive **lashes;** the Sages **said** the measure of **an olive-bulk only with regard to** liability to bring a sin-**offering** for one who unwittingly ate forbidden food.

The *gemara* then cites a *braisa* that confirms Rabbi Yirmiyah's reading: *Rabbi Shimon omer kol she-hu le-malkos, lo amar kezayis ela le-inyan korban* — the smallest amount suffices for *malkos*. And then a striking ruling: the *kezayit* requirement was stated only with respect to bringing a *korban*.

This is genuinely surprising. One might have assumed the opposite: that for a *korban* — an internal, metaphysical act of atonement before God — perhaps even the smallest amount triggers guilt, while for society to actually impose physical punishment, a more substantial threshold is needed. Rabbi Shimon inverts this. For *malkos* — the public, judicial punishment — there is no minimum. For the *korban* — the individual's obligation to bring an atonement offering — the *kezayit* is required. The reasoning is not developed further here, but the inversion is itself remarkable.

### Tosafot's Logic

*Tosafot* offers an explanation for the underlying distinction that is more logically compelling than simply appealing to whether something once had life. The real difference between the ant and the grain of wheat is not biological but structural: it concerns how the Torah defines the prohibited object.

When the Torah says not to eat a *sheretz* — an insect — it names a discrete, defined entity. If one eats an ant, one has consumed precisely the object the Torah prohibited. The ant eaten is the prohibited thing itself, whole and complete. Similarly, if the Torah prohibits eating a *gid ha-nasheh*, eating the sinew in its entirety means one has consumed the defined prohibited object.

By contrast, *tevel* — like *chazir* — is not a defined discrete object. It is a category of substance, of which there can be more or less. Eating a small piece of *tevel* means one has eaten *some* of a prohibited substance, not the prohibited thing as a whole. In such cases, where the Torah's prohibition attaches to a substance rather than to a discrete object, a quantitative *shi'ur* is necessary. *Tosafot* puts it this way: a useful test for whether something qualifies as a *biryah* — a whole unit — is whether cutting it in half causes it to lose its name. Cut an ant in half: you no longer have an ant. Cut a kernel of wheat in half: you still have *tevel*, only less of it.

## Mishnah Cases

### Mishnah List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹכֵל בִּכּוּרִים עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרָא עֲלֵיהֶם, קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי חוּץ לַחוֹמָה. הַשּׁוֹבֵר אֶת הָעֶצֶם בַּפֶּסַח הַטָּהוֹר – הֲרֵי זֶה לוֹקֶה אַרְבָּעִים. אֲבָל הַמּוֹתִיר בַּטָּהוֹר וְהַשּׁוֹבֵר בַּטָּמֵא – אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה אַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a priest **who eats first fruits before** the one who brought the fruits to the Temple **recited over** those fruits the Torah verses that he is obligated to recite (see Deuteronomy 26:3–10); and one who ate **offerings of the most sacred order outside the curtains** surrounding the Tabernacle courtyard or outside the Temple courtyard; and one who ate **offerings of lesser sanctity or second-tithe** produce **outside the wall** of Jerusalem; and also **one who breaks the bone of a ritually pure Paschal offering;** in all these cases **he is flogged** with **forty** lashes. **But one who leaves** the flesh **of the ritually pure** Paschal offering until the morning of the fifteenth of Nisan, **and one who breaks** a bone **of a ritually impure** Paschal offering, is **not flogged** with **forty** lashes.

The *mishnah* enumerates additional cases that incur *malkos*. Among them: *ha'ochel bikurim ad shelo kara aleihem* — one who eats *bikurim* before the declaration has been made. Since *bikurim* may only be consumed by *kohanim*, this refers to a *kohen* who ate from the *bikurim* after they were brought to the Temple and handed over, but before the recitation of *Arami oved avi*. Also listed: *kodshei kodashim chutz le-kla'im* — eating the holiest sacrifices outside the Temple precincts. Even a *kohen* who is ordinarily permitted to eat a *chatat* may not do so outside the *azarah*, and doing so incurs *malkos*.

### Kodshim Kalim

The *mishnah* continues with *kodshim kalim* — the lighter sacrifices, such as *shelamim* — eaten outside Jerusalem, and *ma'aser sheni* eaten outside Jerusalem's walls. Both are permitted for consumption, but only in their proper location. Also mentioned: breaking a bone of a *pesach* offering that was *tahor* and fit to be eaten. The verse *v'chol etzem lo tishberu bo* prohibits this, and the *gemara* reads *bo* — specifically in the *tahor* case, not in the case of a *pesach* that became *tamei*. If the *pesach* was *tamei* and could not be eaten, breaking its bone does not incur *malkos*. Similarly, leaving over from a *korban* — *lo tosiru mimenu ad boker* — does not incur *malkos*, because it is a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh*: it is a passive violation. Alternatively, it may be because *notar* is subject to the positive commandment to burn it the next morning — making it a *nitak la'aseh* — or because of *hatra'as safeik*. Multiple reasons converge.

### Mother Bird Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹטֵל אֵם עַל הַבָּנִים, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּחַ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְשַׁלֵּחַ וְאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ קוּם עֲשֵׂה – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who takes the mother** bird **with** her **fledglings,** thereby violating the Torah prohibition: "You shall not take the mother with her fledglings; you shall send the mother, and the fledglings you may take for yourself" (Deuteronomy 22:6–7), **Rabbi Yehuda says: He is flogged** for taking the mother bird, **and does not send** the mother, **and the Rabbis say: He sends** the mother **and is not flogged,** as **this is the principle:** With regard to **any prohibition that entails** a command to **arise** and **perform** a mitzva, **he is not liable** to receive lashes **for its** violation.

The *mishnah* then presents the case of *shiluach ha-ken* — taking the mother bird together with her young. Rabbi Yehuda rules: *lokei v'eino meshaleach* — one incurs *malkos* and sending the mother bird away afterward does not exempt one. The obligation to send the mother bird away is not a *nitak la'aseh* that retroactively repairs the violation; it is a prior, independent obligation that one should have fulfilled before taking the mother. Once one has transgressed, subsequent sending does not undo the violation.

The *chachamim* disagree: *meshaleach v'eino lokeh* — one sends the bird away and does not receive *malkos*. They articulate the general principle: *kol mitzvas lo ta'aseh she-yesh bah kum asei, ein chayavin aleha* — any negative commandment accompanied by a positive commandment to "stand and do" — to affirmatively correct — does not incur *malkos*. This is, of course, the principle of *lav hanitak la'aseh* that dominated the preceding two days of learning.

## Rabbi Shimon Derashah

### Akiva vs. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְתִימְתָּאָה, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בִּכּוּרִים – הַנָּחָה מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן, קְרִיאָה אֵין מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן. וְלֵימָא זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְתִימְתָּאָה! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a priest who eats first fruits before the one who brought the fruits to the Temple recited the accompanying Torah verses is liable to receive lashes. With regard to this statement, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** whose statements are often cited in the mishna **unattributed. But the Rabbis say:** With regard to **first fruits,** the lack of **placement** alongside the altar **invalidates them,** and they may not be eaten; but the lack of **recitation** of the accompanying Torah verses **does not invalidate them,** and if one placed them and did not recite the accompanying Torah verses, the priest who eats them is not flogged. The Gemara suggests: **And let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **say: This** statement in the mishna is **the unattributed statement of Rabbi Shimon,** who stated this *halakha* explicitly, rather than attributing the statement to Rabbi Akiva, whose statement was not explicit. The Gemara answers: **This teaches us that** although he did not say so explicitly, **Rabbi Akiva holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states that the *stam mishna* — the anonymous formulation — follows Rabbi Akiva. The *chachamim* elsewhere hold that for *bikurim*, the act of *hanacha* — placing the basket before the altar — is the critical act that permits the *bikurim* to be eaten, while the *kria* — the declaration — does not prevent consumption if omitted. Rabbi Akiva, whose position this *mishnah* reflects, holds that it is specifically the *kria* that matters, not merely the placing down.

The *gemara* then asks: why attribute the *stam mishnah* to Rabbi Akiva — why not say it follows Rabbi Shimon? The answer: this is coming to inform us that Rabbi Akiva himself holds like Rabbi Shimon. Interestingly, Rabbi Shimon was a student of Rabbi Akiva — yet here, the formulation is that Rabbi Akiva's view aligns with Rabbi Shimon's. The *gemara*'s reason for focusing on Rabbi Shimon specifically is that the ensuing *braisa* attributed to Rabbi Shimon not only makes this point about *bikurim* — that the *kria* is what permits the *bikurim* to be eaten — but presents a comprehensive *derashah* establishing that a wide range of *korbanot* and sanctified items, if consumed in circumstances that render them forbidden, incur *malkos*. Since this *derashah* directly illuminates several cases in the *mishnah*, the *gemara* pivots to Rabbi Shimon's formulation.

### Devarim 12 Derashah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּתְרוּמַת יָדֶךָ״ – אֵלּוּ בִּכּוּרִים. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְאוֹכְלָן חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִמַּעֲשֵׂר הַקַּל: וּמָה מַעֲשֵׂר הַקַּל, אוֹכְלָן חוּץ לַחוֹמָה לוֹקֶה – בִּכּוּרִים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? הָא לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לָאוֹכֵל מִבִּכּוּרִים עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרָא עֲלֵיהֶם, שֶׁהוּא לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the statement of **Rabbi Shimon?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to food items that may not be eaten outside the walls of Jerusalem. It is written: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain, or of your wine, or of your oil, or the firstborn of your herd or of your flock, nor any of your vows that you vow, nor your gift offerings, nor the donation of your hand" (Deuteronomy 12:17). The Sages explain that with regard to the phrase **"nor the donation of [*terumat*] your hand," these are first fruits.** **Rabbi Shimon said: What does** this phrase **come to teach us? If** it is to teach the prohibition **to eat** the first fruits **outside the wall** of Jerusalem, there is no need for a verse, as it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from the lenient** case of second-**tithe** produce. **If** with regard to **the lenient** case of second-**tithe** produce, one who **eats them outside the wall is flogged,** then with regard to **first fruits, all the more so** is it **not** clear that he is flogged? Rather, **the verse comes to teach only with regard to** a priest **who partakes of first fruits before** the person who brought the fruits to the Temple **recited** the accompanying Torah verses **over them,** teaching **that he is flogged.**

The *gemara* presents the *braisa*: Rabbi Shimon offers a sustained *derashah* on a verse in Deuteronomy 12. The verse in question is: *lo suchal le'echol bish'arecha* — you shall not be able to eat within your gates — followed by a list of items: *ma'aser degancha, tiroshcha, v'yitzharecha, u-vechorot bekarcha v'tzonecha, v'chol nedarecha asher tidor, v'nidvosecha, u-serumas yadecha*.

The *peshat* of the verse is that all of these items must be eaten in Jerusalem — they may not be consumed in one's home cities. But Rabbi Shimon's *derashah* proceeds differently: he takes each term in the list and uses it to derive a specific *lav* — a distinct negative prohibition — that generates liability for *malkos* in a particular scenario, not necessarily about location but about the timing or status of consumption.

### Lo Tuchal Phrase

What grounds this reading is the observation that the same list appears earlier in the same chapter in a positive formulation: *ve-achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha ba-makom asher yivchar, ma'aser degancha, tiroshcha, v'yitzharecha, u-vechorot bekarcha v'tzonecha, v'chol nedarecha asher tidor, v'nidvosecha, u-serumas yadecha* — you shall eat all of these before the Lord your God in the chosen place. The negative formulation in *lo suchal le'echol bish'arecha* therefore could have simply said *lo suchal le'ochlem* — you shall not eat them — with a single pronoun encompassing everything. Instead, it repeats each item individually. The repetition is the key.

### Terumas Yadecha

*U-serumas yadecha* — this refers to *bikurim*, which is often called *terumah*. Rabbi Shimon asks: what is this coming to teach? If it means one may not eat *bikurim* outside Jerusalem, that is derivable from a *kal vachomer* from *ma'aser sheni*: if *ma'aser sheni* — the lighter case — incurs *malkos* when eaten outside Jerusalem, *bikurim* — which unlike *ma'aser sheni* is forbidden to non-*kohanim*, indicating greater sanctity — certainly incurs *malkos* for that. Since one could derive this through *kal vachomer*, the explicit mention of *bikurim* must be teaching something else. What, then, does it teach? *Le-echol bikurim ad shelo kara aleihem she-lokeh* — that eating *bikurim* before the declaration incurs *malkos*.

### Nedavot & Todah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנִדְבֹתֶיךָ״ – זוֹ תּוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְאוֹכְלָן חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִמַּעֲשֵׂר, הָא לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לָאוֹכֵל בְּתוֹדָה וּבִשְׁלָמִים לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, שֶׁהוּא לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **"Nor your gift offerings"; this is a thanks-offering and a peace-offering** that one donates voluntarily. **Rabbi Shimon says: What does** this phrase **come to teach us? If** it is to teach that it is prohibited **to eat** a thanks-offering and a peace-offering **outside the wall** of Jerusalem, there is no need for a verse, as it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of second-**tithe** produce, for whose consumption outside the wall one is flogged, despite the fact that it is not an offering. Rather, **the verse comes to teach only with regard to one who partakes of a thanks-offering or of a peace-offering before** the **sprinkling** of its blood on the altar, before the consumption of its flesh is permitted, **that he is flogged.**

*V'nidvosecha* — voluntary offerings — refers to *todah* and *shelamim*. Again, Rabbi Shimon asks: if this merely prohibits eating them outside Jerusalem, it is derivable from *ma'aser* by *kal vachomer*. So the explicit mention teaches something else: *le-echol be-todah u-shelamim lifnei zerikah she-lokeh* — one who eats from a *todah* or *shelamim* before *zerikat ha-dam* — before the blood has been sprinkled on the altar — incurs *malkos*. This prohibition is not about location but about time: eating before the act that renders the *korban* fit for consumption.

As Rashi notes, these prohibitions were already known as requirements from other sources; what was not previously established was that violating them constitutes a *lav* generating *malkos*. That is precisely what Rabbi Shimon derives here.

### Bechoros Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבְכֹרֹת״ – זֶה הַבְּכוֹר. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְאוֹכְלָן חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִמַּעֲשֵׂר, אִם לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִתּוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים! הָא לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לָאוֹכֵל מִן הַבְּכוֹר אֲפִילּוּ לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה, שֶׁהוּא לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **"Or the firstborn"; this is the firstborn. Rabbi Shimon says: What does** this verse **come to teach us? If** it is to teach that it is prohibited **to eat** a firstborn animal **outside the wall** of Jerusalem, there is no need for a verse, as it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of second-**tithe** produce. **If** it is to teach that it is prohibited to eat a firstborn animal **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a thanks-offering and a peace-offering,** which are offerings of lesser sanctity, as even non-priests may partake of their flesh. Rather, **the verse comes to teach only with regard to** a non-priest **who partakes** of the flesh **of a firstborn even after** the **sprinkling** of its blood, **that he is flogged.**

*U-vechorot* — *bechoros*, firstborn animals. Once the prohibition of eating *todah* and *shelamim* before *zerikah* is established, Rabbi Shimon argues through *kal vachomer* that the same applies to *bechor*. But the explicit mention of *bechor* is therefore teaching something beyond the *zerikah* issue: *le-echol min ha-bechor afilu le-achar zerikah she-lokeh* — one incurs *malkos* for eating from a *bechor* even after *zerikah*, referring to a non-*kohen*. The fact that a non-*kohen* may not eat a *bechor* was known; but that it constitutes a *lav* producing *malkos* is derived here.

### Bechoros Outside Temple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּקָרְךָ וְצֹאנֶךָ״ – זוֹ חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְאוֹכְלָן חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִמַּעֲשֵׂר, אִם לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִתּוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים, אִם לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבְּכוֹר! הָא לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לָאוֹכֵל מֵחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם אֲפִילּוּ לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים, שֶׁהוּא לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **"Of your herd or of your flock"; this is a sin-offering and a guilt-offering,** which are offerings of the most sacred order, which may be eaten only within the Temple courtyard. **Rabbi Shimon says: What does** this verse **come to teach us? If** it is to teach that it is prohibited **to eat** a sin-offering and a guilt-offering **outside the wall,** there is no need for a verse, as it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of second-**tithe** produce. **If** it is to teach that it is prohibited to eat a sin-offering and a guilt-offering **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a thanks-offering and a peace-offering,** which are offerings of lesser sanctity. **If** it is to teach that it is prohibited for a non-priest to eat a sin-offering and a guilt-offering **after** the **sprinkling** of its blood, it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a firstborn** animal. Rather, **the verse comes** to teach **only with regard to one who partakes** of the flesh **of a sin-offering or a guilt-offering even after** the **sprinkling** of its blood, which is the correct time to partake of it, but he partakes of it **outside the curtains** surrounding the Tabernacle courtyard or outside the Temple courtyard, **that he is flogged.**

*U-vchorot bekarcha v'tzonecha* — continuing through the list — along with the more general reference to animals — now encompasses *kodshei kodashim*, the holiest sacrifices such as *chatat* and *asham*. By layering the argument: what was established for *bechor* — a non-*kohen* may not eat it even after *zerikah* — extends by *kal vachomer* to *kodshei kodashim*. But the explicit mention of *chatat* and *asham* teaches something additional still: eating them *chutz la-kla'im* — outside the Temple precincts — incurs *malkos*.

### Olah Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נְדָרֶיךָ״ – זוֹ עוֹלָה. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְאוֹכְלָן חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִמַּעֲשֵׂר, אִם לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִתּוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים, אִם לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבְּכוֹר, אִם חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים – קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם! הָא לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לָאוֹכֵל מִן הָעוֹלָה לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה, אֲפִילּוּ בִּפְנִים, שֶׁהוּא לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **"Your vows"; this is the burnt-offering,** which is an offering of the most sacred order and is entirely consumed upon the altar, and is brought as a gift offering, not as an obligation. **Rabbi Shimon says: What does** this verse **come to teach us? If** it is to teach that it is prohibited **to eat** a burnt-offering **outside the wall** of Jerusalem, there is no need for a verse, as it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of second-**tithe** produce. **If** it is to teach that it is prohibited to eat a burnt-offering **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a thanks-offering and a peace-offering,** which are offerings of lesser sanctity. **If** it is to teach that it is prohibited for a non-priest to eat a sin-offering and a guilt-offering **after** the **sprinkling** of its blood, it may be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a firstborn** animal. **If** it is to teach that it is prohibited to eat a burnt-offering **outside the curtains** surrounding the Tabernacle courtyard or outside the Temple courtyard there is **an *a fortiori*** inference **from a sin-offering and a guilt-offering.** Rather, **the verse comes to teach only with regard to one who partakes** of the flesh **of a burnt-offering after** the **sprinkling, even inside** the courtyard, **that he is flogged.**

Finally, the term for animals in the verse encompasses the *olah* — the burnt offering. Once all prior categories have been addressed, the *olah* is mentioned to teach that no one — not even a *kohen*, not even after *zerikah*, not even inside the Temple — may eat an *olah* at all. *Le-echol min ha-olah le-achar zerikah afilu bi-fnim she-hu lokeh* — eating from an *olah* in any circumstance incurs *malkos*. This is the whole point of the *derashah*: not merely to establish that these things are *assur* — that was already known — but to establish that each of them constitutes an independent *lav* for which one incurs *malkos*.

## Critique of Arguments

### Rava's Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּיָלְידָא אִימֵּיהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – תֵּילִיד, וְאִי לָא – לָא תֵּילִיד. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית לְהוּ פִּירְכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** with regard to Rabbi Shimon's statement in the *baraita*: With regard to anyone **whose mother is bearing** a child who is **like Rabbi Shimon, she should bear** that child, **and if not,** it is preferable that **she does not bear** him at all. Rava was so impressed by Rabbi Shimon's statement that he praised him and characterized him as the model of a wise man. Rava added: **And** I say this **even though there is a refutation for** each of his conclusions. What are the refutations?

Rava responds to Rabbi Shimon's *derashah* with striking admiration: *de-yaldei imei ke-Rabbi Shimon teyalad* — whoever's mother gives birth to him should give birth to him like Rabbi Shimon. That is: one should be blessed to produce a mind like Rabbi Shimon's. The ingenuity lay in the layering — establishing each prohibition on the foundation of the prior one, working backwards through the list so that each successive item could be shown to be heavier than its predecessor, and therefore the explicit mention must be teaching something new.

### Kal Vachomer Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חוּמְרָא דְּבִכּוּרִים מִמַּעֲשֵׂר – שֶׁכֵּן אֲסוּרִים לְזָרִים. אַדְּרַבָּה, מַעֲשֵׂר חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן אָסוּר לְאוֹנֵן. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא דְּתוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים מִמַּעֲשֵׂר – שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ. אַדְּרַבָּה, מַעֲשֵׂר חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין כֶּסֶף צוּרָה. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא דִּבְכוֹר מִתּוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים – שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ מֵרֶחֶם. אַדְּרַבָּה: תּוֹדָה וּשְׁלָמִים חֲמוּרִים, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִים סְמִיכָה, וּנְסָכִים, וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא דְּחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מִבְּכוֹר – שֶׁכֵּן קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים. אַדְּרַבָּה, בְּכוֹר חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן קְדוּשָּׁתוֹ מֵרֶחֶם. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא דְּעוֹלָה מֵחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם – שֶׁכֵּן כָּלִיל. אַדְּרַבָּה, חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם חֲמִירִי, שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפְּרִי. וְכוּלְּהוּ חֲמִירִי מֵעוֹלָה, דְּאִית בְּהוּ שְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the **stringency of first fruits vis-à-vis** second-**tithe** produce? It is **that** first fruits **are forbidden to non-priests,** who are permitted to eat second-tithe produce. That can be refuted; **on the contrary,** second-**tithe** produce is more **stringent** than first fruits, in **that** it is **forbidden to an acute mourner,** i.e., one whose close relative died that day, which is not the case with regard to first fruits according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon (see *Yevamot* 73b). **And what** is the **stringency of a thanks-offering and a peace-offering vis-à-vis** second-**tithe** produce? It is **that** the thanks-offering and the peace-offering **require the placement of blood and sacrificial portions upon the altar.** That can be refuted; **on the contrary,** second-**tithe** produce is more **stringent** than the thanks-offering and the peace-offering, in **that** the redemption of second-tithe produce **requires money minted** into a coin. A disqualified thanks-offering and peace-offering, like other disqualified offerings, may be redeemed with any object of equal value. **And what** is the **stringency of a firstborn** offering **vis-à-vis a thanks-offering and a peace-offering?** It is **that** the **sanctity of** the firstborn takes effect **from the womb,** while the other offerings are consecrated by their owners. That can be refuted; **on the contrary, a thanks-offering and a peace-offering are more stringent,** in **that they require placing hands** on the head of the offering before its sacrifice, **and libations, and the waving of the breast and the thigh,** which is not the case with regard to a firstborn offering. **And what** is the **stringency of a sin-offering and a guilt-offering vis-à-vis a firstborn** offering? It is **that** a sin-offering and a guilt-offering are **offerings of the most sacred order.** That can be refuted; **on the contrary, a firstborn** offering **is more stringent,** in **that its sanctity** takes effect **from the womb,** not from consecration by its owner. **And what** is the **stringency of the burnt-offering vis-à-vis a sin-offering and a guilt-offering?** It is **that** it is burnt in its **entirety** upon the altar, and no part of it is given to the priests. That can be refuted; **on the contrary, the sin-offering and the guilt-offering are** more **stringent,** in **that they atone** for one's sins, and a burnt-offering is brought as a gift offering. **And all** of those matters listed in the *baraita* are more **stringent than the burnt-offering because there are two consumptions with regard to** each of these matters, as they are consumed upon the altar and consumed by their owner and the priests, whereas the burnt-offering is consumed in its entirety upon the altar.

But Rava immediately adds: *v'af al gav d'is lei pircha* — even though every single *kal vachomer* argument can be challenged. The praise is for the intellectual architecture, not for the logical inviolability of each step.

The challenges proceed systematically. Can one say *bikurim* is categorically weightier than *ma'aser sheni*, thereby making the *kal vachomer* from *ma'aser* to *bikurim* valid? *Shekein assurim le-zarim* — *bikurim* is forbidden to non-*kohanim*, while *ma'aser sheni* is not: this supports the *kal vachomer*. But *adaraba* — in the other direction — *ma'aser chamur shekein assur le-onein*: one who is in the immediate mourning state of *aninut* may not eat *ma'aser sheni* — *lo achalti be-oni mimenu* — whereas *bikurim* may actually be consumed by an *onein*. So *ma'aser* is weightier in that respect, and the *kal vachomer* is not absolute.

Similarly, the *kal vachomer* from *todah u'shelamim* to *bechor* can be challenged: *todah u'shelamim* require *semichah*, *nesachim*, *tenufah*, and the giving of *chazeh va-shok* to the *kohen* — a far more elaborate ritual structure — making them weightier in those respects than *bechor*, which is sanctified automatically from birth but involves a simpler procedure. And so on down the list: in each case, the supposedly "lighter" item can be shown to be weightier in some dimension, undermining the *kal vachomer*.

### Kal Vachomer Fails

The conclusion is that none of Rabbi Shimon's *kal vachomer* arguments is logically airtight. Yet Rava's admiration stands: the ingenuity of the structure — working backwards through the list, layering each prohibition to allow the next to be derived — remains impressive even if the individual *kal vachomer* steps cannot withstand scrutiny.

## Ain Mazirin Principle

### Ain Mazirin Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין? הָא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין, אֵין מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין! אִיסּוּרָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Rabbi Shimon, who derived prohibitions not written in the Torah by means of *a fortiori* inferences: **And does one** derive **a prohibition from** an *a fortiori* **inference? But even according to the one who says** that if there is a prohibition written explicitly in the Torah, **one administers punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference, one does not** derive **a prohibition from** an *a fortiori* **inference.** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon does not derive prohibitions for which one is flogged from those inferences; rather, he derives **a mere prohibition.**

But there is a more fundamental difficulty, beyond the question of whether each *kal vachomer* holds: *ve-chi mazhirin min ha-din?* — can one derive a prohibition itself from a *kal vachomer*? Even setting aside the debate about *ein onshin min ha-din* — whether a punishment can be imposed based on a *kal vachomer* — the principle that *ein mazhirin min ha-din* is universally accepted: one cannot establish the very *isur* of something through a *kal vachomer* alone. There must be an explicit verse. Rabbi Shimon's entire structure depends on deriving that these things are prohibited through *kal vachomer* from *ma'aser*. That cannot be the source of the prohibition.

### Yatira Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קְרָא יַתִּירָא הוּא, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב ״וְהֵבֵאתָ שָׁם וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בַּמָּקוֹם וְגוֹ׳״, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תוּכַל לְאוֹכְלָם״, מִיהְדָּר מְפָרֵשׁ בְּהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לְיַחוֹדֵי לְהוּ לָאוֵי לְכׇל חַד וְחַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the derivation is that the entire verse beginning: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain" (Deuteronomy 12:17) **is a superfluous verse. After all, it is** already **written: "And there you shall bring** your burnt-offerings, and your sacrifices, and your tithes, and the donation of your hand, and your vows, and your gift offerings, and the firstborn of your herd and of your flock, **and there you shall eat before the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 12:6–7); all the items that must be eaten within the walls of Jerusalem are enumerated. **Let the Merciful One write** simply: **You may not eat them,** in general terms, which would constitute a prohibition for which one would be liable to receive lashes for each of the cases enumerated. **Why do I** need **the Merciful One** to **again** specifically **enumerate** and **detail** each of **them?** **Rather,** this repetition serves **to designate** additional **prohibitions for each and every one** of the cases enumerated in the later verse (Deuteronomy 12:17). The prohibition is derived not by means of an *a fortiori* inference; rather, it is derived from the superfluous verse. Rabbi Shimon derives by means of the *a fortiori* inferences the additional prohibition that is in effect in each of these cases.

The *gemara*'s answer — and this is the punchline — is *kra yesera hu*: the verse is extra. This is Rabbi Shimon's actual mechanism, and it resolves everything. The prohibition of *lo suchal le'echol bish'arecha* could have simply said *lo tuchal le'ochlem* — you may not eat *them* — with a single pronoun encompassing all items previously listed. Instead, it repeats every single item individually.

### Scriptural Support

The redundancy is demonstrable: the immediately preceding verse in Deuteronomy lists these exact items in the positive: *ve-achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha ba-makom asher yivchar* — you shall eat before the Lord in the chosen place — and then enumerates: *ma'aser degancha, tiroshcha, v'yitzharecha, u-vechorot bekarcha v'tzonecha, v'chol nedarecha asher tidor, v'nidvosecha, u-serumas yadecha*. The negative verse then repeats the identical list. One word — *them* — would have sufficed. The Torah chose instead to enumerate each item.

### Word Repetition

Why does the Torah repeat each word individually? Rabbi Shimon's answer: to establish a distinct *lav* for each category. Each term in the negative formulation creates its own independent prohibition — not merely restating the location restriction that could have been inferred, but generating a new *lav* that applies to that category and to everything weightier than it.

### Separate Lavim

This is the resolution to *ein mazhirin min ha-din*. Rabbi Shimon is not deriving prohibitions through *kal vachomer* alone. He is deriving them from the *yatira* — the extra words in the verse. Each word, being superfluous as a mere repetition of the location prohibition, must therefore be teaching an independent *lav* applicable to that item and to the heavier categories above it. The *kal vachomer* serves only as the explanatory framework — demonstrating why the earlier prohibition does not need to be explicitly stated for the heavier items, and therefore why the explicit mention of each heavier item must be teaching something new. But the *lav* itself comes from the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: זָר שֶׁאָכַל מִן הָעוֹלָה לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה חוּץ לַחוֹמָה, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לוֹקֶה חָמֵשׁ! חֲמִשָּׁה אִיסּוּרִין הָווּ. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֵלּוּ הֵן הַלּוֹקִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rava say:** With regard to **a non-priest who ate** the flesh **of a burnt-offering before sprinkling** its blood, **outside the walls, according to Rabbi Shimon** he **is flogged** with **five** sets of lashes: One because he is a non-priest; one for eating a burnt-offering; one for eating the flesh of an offering before its blood was sprinkled; one for eating offerings of the most sacred order outside the Temple courtyard; and one for eating sacrificial food outside of Jerusalem. Apparently, Rabbi Shimon holds that all these are actual prohibitions for which one is flogged. The Gemara answers: It does not mean that one is actually flogged; rather, it means that **they are five prohibitions.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And these are** the people **who are flogged** by Torah law? Among them is a priest who eats first fruits before the recitation of the accompanying Torah verses, and the Gemara established that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

Rava accordingly rules: *zar she-achal min ha-olah lifnei zerikah chutz la-choma le-Rabbi Shimon lokei chamesh* — a non-*kohen* who eats from an *olah* before *zerikah* outside the walls incurs five sets of *malkos*, because he has violated the prohibition of eating an *olah* at all, the prohibition against eating outside the Temple precincts, the prohibition as a non-*kohen*, the prohibition of eating before *zerikah*, and the prohibition of eating outside Jerusalem. Each of the prohibitions layered through Rabbi Shimon's *derashah* applies, and each generates its own set of *malkos*. This is the whole point of the *derashah*: not merely to establish that these things are *assur* — that was already known — but to establish that each of them constitutes an independent *lav* for which one incurs *malkos*.
