# Gittin 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kitvu & Shlichim

### Two vs. Three People

The *daf* properly begins with the final line of 66a, where the *Mishnah* turns to a question of fundamental importance in the laws of *get*: when a husband appoints agents to write and deliver a *get*, must those specific individuals carry out the task themselves, or may they delegate to others?

The *Mishnah* draws a critical distinction based on both the number of people addressed and the precise language used. When a husband says to two people, *"tnu get l'ishti"* — give a *get* to my wife — they themselves must execute the task. Since giving a *get* necessarily implies writing it, they cannot hand that responsibility to anyone else. Similarly, if he says to three people, *"kitvu get u-tnu l'ishti"* — write a *get* and give it to my wife — the use of *kitvu* makes explicit that they must do it directly: *harei elu yichtevu v'yitnu*.

The more nuanced case arises when he says to three people simply *"tnu get l'ishti"* — without the word *kitvu*. Here the *Mishnah* reasons that three people constitute a *beis din*, and a *beis din* is understood as a supervisory body rather than a group expected to perform each action personally. Therefore, when *kitvu* is absent and the group is three, the husband may be understood as delegating oversight rather than direct execution, and those three may instruct others to write the *get*. This is *divrei Rabbi Meir*. The *Mishnah* further records a tradition transmitted by Rabbi Chanina ish Ono from prison — understood by *Rashi* as being in the name of Rabbi Akiva, who was then held under Roman imprisonment — confirming: *mekubalani b'omer lishloshah tnu get l'ishti she'yomru l'acherim v'yichtevu, mipnei she'asan Beis Din* — when three are told to give a *get*, they may delegate, because he constituted them as a *beis din*.

### Rabbi Yosi's Rule

Rabbi Yosi dissents sharply: *af anu mekubalin she-afilu amar l'Beis Din HaGadol she-bi-Yerushalayim tnu get l'ishti, harei elu yichtevu v'yitnu* — even if one said to the High Court in Jerusalem, "give a *get* to my wife," they themselves must write it and give it. Rabbi Yosi's position strips away any leniency based on the nature of the body addressed. Even the most eminent court in the world, if they received only a verbal instruction to give a *get*, must carry it out personally. If they do not know how to write a *get*, they must learn. They cannot delegate what was entrusted directly to them.

The *Mishnah* then shifts to a related but distinct case: when the husband addresses ten people. If he says *"kitvu get u-tnu l'ishti"* without the word *kulchem* — all of you — it means from among you: one writes and two sign. But if he adds *kulchem* — all of you — then all ten must be involved. The practical resolution, as the *Gemara* will clarify, is that one writes while all are present, analogous to the role of a *shaliach tzibur*, who acts on behalf of the entire assembled group. Crucially, *lifichach im meis echad meihem harei zeh get batel* — if one of them dies, the *get* is void — because the husband stipulated that everyone participate, and that condition was not met. This underscores a point worth pausing over: the husband's *da'at* and *ratzon* are so central to the nature of the *get* that failure to comply with his precise specification invalidates the document entirely.

### Signing vs. Scribing

The *Gemara* opens with a question sent from the academy of Rav to Shmuel — a remarkable fact in itself, given that Rav and Shmuel were contemporaries of roughly equal stature. The question: *amar l'shnayim kitvu u-tnu get l'ishti, amru l'sofer ve'chasav ve'chasmu hein mahu?* — if a husband told two people to write and give a *get*, and they instructed a *sofer* to scribe it while they themselves signed, what is the *halacha*?

The underlying question is what *kitvu* means when directed at a group. Does it refer to the physical scribing of the document, or does it refer to the signing — the affixing of witnesses' signatures? This distinction carries enormous practical weight. If *kitvu* means scribing, then having someone else write it violates the husband's instruction. If *kitvu* means signing, then having a *sofer* write the document is not a deviation at all — it was never their task to scribe it.

Shmuel's response was: *teitzei vehadavar tzarich talmud* — if she remarried on the basis of such a *get*, she must leave her second husband, because the *get* is uncertain; but the matter requires further investigation. The *Gemara* then explores what exactly requires investigation.

### Mili Lo Mimsaran

One possibility is that the question is about *mili*: can a verbal appointment be transferred to another agent? The *Gemara* immediately notes that this cannot be Shmuel's question, because Shmuel himself already rules *halacha keRabbi Yosi d'amar mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi that a verbal agency cannot be passed on to another agent. If that principle is settled, why is there any uncertainty?

The concept of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* requires some unpacking, as it is the central analytical thread running through this entire *daf*. When a husband appoints a *shaliach* to deliver a *get*, he hands over a physical document. That physical object, in some sense, embodies and concretizes the agent's authority, and it may be possible in certain configurations to transfer that authority along with the document. But when the appointment consists only of words — as when the husband tells two people to write a *get* before the document exists — there is nothing physical to convey. The agent holds only a verbal mandate. The question is whether such a verbal mandate is one the agent can pass on to someone else. Rabbi Yosi's view, accepted by Shmuel, is that it cannot. If I appoint you to perform this task verbally, you must do it yourself; you cannot re-appoint another in your place.

This principle, however, does not answer the question Shmuel actually intends to investigate. That question is narrower and more precise: *hai ktovu, ktav yadam hu o ktav ha-get hu?* — when the husband says *kitvu*, does he mean "sign it" or "scribe it"?

If *kitvu* means "sign it," then the husband never directed these two people to do the scribing at all. What he implicitly said was: find someone to write the *get* and sign it yourselves. Having a *sofer* write it is not delegating their task — it is executing the husband's implicit instruction that someone else handle the scribing. This falls under a distinct concept: *omer imru*, the husband directing his appointees to communicate to a third party. In that framework, the two witnesses are not passing on their own agency; they are simply informing a *sofer* that he has been appointed by the husband. The husband effectively appointed the *sofer* through them.

If, on the other hand, *kitvu* means "scribe it," then having someone else do the scribing is forbidden — not as a violation of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* (since that concerns re-delegation of one's own agency), but simply because the husband told them to scribe it and they did not.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve whether *kitvu* means scribing from the *Mishnah* itself: if it meant signing, why would the *Mishnah* say that members of the *Beis Din HaGadol* would have to *learn* to write the *get*? Surely they know how to sign their names. The *Gemara* deflects — perhaps it was a newly constituted court — but acknowledges the deflection is weak. The sugya thus arrives at two separate issues, which the *Gemara* now treats explicitly.

## Omer Imru Debate

### Omer Imru Basics

The concept of *omer imru* must be distinguished from *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*, though both concern verbal agency. *Mili lo mimsaran* addresses whether an agent who received a verbal appointment can pass that appointment on to a third party. *Omer imru* addresses a different question entirely: can the husband direct his appointed agents to *inform* others that they have been appointed directly by the husband?

The distinction is this: in *mili lo mimsaran*, I appoint Michael, and Michael tries to hand off his role to Jenna — something he has no authority to do. In *omer imru*, I tell Michael to inform Jenna that I am appointing her — the appointment comes from me, and Michael is merely the messenger conveying it. If *omer imru* is valid, then the husband can appoint a *sofer* and witnesses without speaking to them directly, so long as he instructs an intermediary to relay the appointments. If *omer imru* is invalid, then every person involved in the *get* must hear their appointment directly from the husband himself.

The *Gemara* now asks: does Rabbi Yosi actually agree that *omer imru* is permissible? And if so, what does that imply for the case of *chasam sofer ve'eid*?

### Chasam Sofer Concern

The *Gemara* raises the ruling of *chasam sofer ve'eid* — where the *sofer* who wrote the *get* is also one of the two signing witnesses. Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba clarifies that the *Mishnah* here means *chasam sofer shaninu*: the *sofer* signed as a witness. And Rav Chisda notes that this *Mishnah* follows the position of Rabbi Yosi, for whom *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*.

Now the *Gemara* presses: *ve'i salka da'atach modei Rabbi Yosi b'omer imru* — if Rabbi Yosi agrees that *omer imru* is valid, *nafka minah churbah*, disaster will follow. Here is the scenario: a husband says to two people, *"imru l'sofer veyichtov u-l'Reuven v'Shimon veyachtomu"* — go tell the *sofer* to write and tell Reuven and Shimon to sign. This is a legitimate use of *omer imru* if the concept is valid. But here is the problem: *mishum kisufa d'sofer* — out of embarrassment for the *sofer*. When the messenger approaches the *sofer* and says "write the *get*, but Reuven and Shimon are going to sign it," the *sofer* may feel slighted. "You trust me to write it but not to be a witness?" The messenger, anxious to avoid this embarrassment, decides to make the *sofer* one of the witnesses instead of Shimon. The result: the *get* is signed by witnesses whom the husband did not appoint, and *u'va'al lo amar hachi* — the husband never said this. The *get* is invalid, yet no one will know.

The *Gemara* attempts to deflect this concern by noting that *kasher v'lo ta'aseh kein b'Yisrael* has already been established — such appointments ought not be done in Israel, and the *sofer* should refuse to participate in this kind of arrangement. If no one is supposed to engage in it *lechatchila*, then we need not worry about the scenario. The *Gemara* then raises a second version of the same concern: even if the husband directly appoints the two witnesses but asks them to find a *sofer*, might the witnesses nonetheless rearrange the roles out of embarrassment — substituting the *sofer* for one of them? The same deflection applies: if witnesses who find their own *sofer* also fall under *lo ta'aseh kein b'Yisrael*, we are not concerned.

### Two Separate Issues

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a crucial clarification. The two concerns — *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* and *omer imru* — are conceptually separate, even though they may both bear on the validity of a *get*. Shmuel holds both principles, but distinguishes between them. *Mili lo mimsaran* means that an agent cannot re-delegate a verbal appointment he himself received. *Omer imru* is a distinct question about whether the husband can appoint someone *shelo befanav* — not in their presence — by having a messenger relay the appointment.

### Rabbi Yosi Both Ways

The *Gemara* concludes that the only way to fully account for the *Mishnah*'s treatment of *chasam sofer ve'eid* — without concern that the *sofer* was substituted for a witness out of embarrassment — is to say that Rabbi Yosi holds *both* prohibitions. *Rabbi Yosi tartei amar*: Rabbi Yosi says both that *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* and that *omer imru* is invalid. According to Rabbi Yosi, every participant in the *get* process must have heard their role directly from the husband. This resolves the *chasam sofer* concern entirely: if every appointment must be direct, there is no intermediary who could have rearranged things out of embarrassment.

This also explains Rabbi Yosi's position in the *Mishnah* far more coherently than it might first appear. When Rabbi Yosi says that even the *Beis Din HaGadol* in Jerusalem would have to write the *get* themselves if that is what was said, the implicit point is that no one may act as a conduit for an appointment. Even if the husband's practical intent was for the court to supervise and delegate, the *halacha* is that only those who heard the instruction directly from the husband may execute it. The reasoning of *mili lo mimsaran* and *omer imru* are conceptually distinct — the former concerns passing on one's own delegated authority; the latter concerns whether one can receive an appointment indirectly — but Rabbi Yosi holds that both are disqualifying. Together they constitute a comprehensive requirement that all agency in the writing and delivery of a *get* flow directly from the husband's own words to each individual actor.

## Shmuel's Psak

### Shmuel Splits

The *Gemara* now turns to the *gufa* of Shmuel's ruling: *ushmu'el savar lah keva'tei bechada u'falig alei bechada* — Shmuel agrees with Rabbi Yosi in one respect and disagrees in another. Shmuel accepts *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — a verbal agency cannot be transferred by the agent who holds it. He does not accept that *omer imru* is prohibited. According to Shmuel, the husband may appoint a *sofer* or a witness by relaying the appointment through an intermediary; *omer imru* is valid, and one may make a *shaliach shelo befanav*, appointing someone who is not present, so long as the message is conveyed.

This is precisely what clarifies the original question from Rav's academy. Shmuel's uncertainty about the case where two people had a *sofer* write the *get* while they signed was not a question about *mili* — that is settled. It was a question about *kitvu*: if *kitvu* means signing, then the two appointees were only ever asked to sign, and by implicitly directing them to find a *sofer*, the husband was effectively using the mechanism of *omer imru*. Since Shmuel holds *omer imru* is valid, such a *get* would be valid in principle — hence the need to determine what *kitvu* actually means in the husband's instruction.

### Nimuko Imo

The *Gemara* then records an exchange that illuminates the general standing of Rabbi Yosi's rulings. Shmuel transmitted this *halacha* in the name of *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi), prompting Rabbi Shimon, Rebbi's son, to object: given that Rabbi Meir and Chanina ish Ono — who brought the ruling from Rabbi Akiva in prison — both disagree with Rabbi Yosi, *mah ra'ah Rebbi lomar halacha keRabbi Yosi?* What motivated his father to rule like Rabbi Yosi against those other prominent opinions?

Rebbi's response is striking: *shtok beni shtok* — be silent, my son. Had you ever merited to see Rabbi Yosi in person, you would understand, because *nimuko imo* — his reasoning is with him. This is an ancient expression of confidence in Rabbi Yosi's intellectual authority, one that transcends the mere counting of opinions.

The *Gemara* then cites the famous passage from *Avot d'Rabbi Natan* in which Isi ben Yehudah catalogued the distinctive qualities of the great sages. Rabbi Meir: *chacham ve'sofer* — wise and a scribe (notably apt given the subject of this *daf*). Rabbi Yehudah: *chacham l'chesheyirtzeh* — wise when he chose to be, meaning when he took the time to reason things through carefully. Rabbi Tarfon: *gash shel egozim* — a heap of walnuts: ask him one thing and everything comes cascading out. Rabbi Yishmael: *chanut meyuzenet* — a well-stocked storehouse, anything one needed was available and organized. Rabbi Akiva: *otzar balum* — a sealed warehouse divided into compartments. The *Gemara* clarifies that this refers not to creativity — which one might naturally associate with Rabbi Akiva — but to his systematic organization: he would absorb traditions from his teachers in their mixed form (legal analysis, *midrash*, and *mishnah* all intertwined) and then sort them meticulously into their proper categories, a feat reflected in the work of his students who edited the *midrashei halacha*. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri: *kupat harochlim* — the basket of a traveling merchant, stocked with a variety of unusual wares. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah: *kupat shel besamim* — the spice merchant's basket, similarly varied if somewhat smaller in scope. *Mishnat Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*: *kav venaki* — a small measure, but clean: he taught little, but everything he taught was reliable and precise. And Rabbi Yosi: *nimuko imo* — his reasoning is with him.

The phrase is all the more striking because, as the *Gemara* itself notes, the reasoning is not always visible in the *Mishnah*. If anything, the surface logic might seem to favor Rabbi Meir and the tradition from Rabbi Akiva, who allow delegation when three people are addressed as a *beis din*. Yet the rule *halacha keRabbi Yosi she-nimuko imo* reflects a presumption that Rabbi Yosi's positions are grounded in deep, worked-out reasoning, even when that reasoning is not spelled out. The passage concludes with the description of Rabbi Shimon: *tochein harbeh u-motzi kima* — he grinds much and produces little. The *Gemara* clarifies that *motzi* here means "expels" rather than "produces": he forgets very little, and what he does forget was only the intellectual chaff.

## Two Appoint Sofer

### Kasher V'Lo Taaseh

The *Gemara* now examines the concrete case that had been hovering in the background. *Amar l'shnayim imru l'sofer veyichtov u-l'Ploni v'Ploni veyachtomu* — a husband says to two people: go tell the *sofer* to write, and go tell so-and-so and so-and-so to sign. Here the husband has appointed nobody directly; every appointment is conveyed through the intermediary of these two. Rav Huna citing Rav rules: *keshera v'lo ta'aseh zot b'Yisrael* — it is valid, but one should not do this in Israel.

### Hired Witnesses Fear

The reason for *lo ta'aseh*, when asked, is the concern *shema tiskor edim* — perhaps the woman has hired witnesses. In a case where the two appointed intermediaries are telling a *sofer* and witnesses what to do, and none of those actors are directly implicated by signing the document themselves, there is a real danger that the woman could find two people willing to falsely claim that the husband gave them this instruction. Unlike the case of forging signatures on a *get* — which the *Gemara* elsewhere says people will not do, since *ma'aseh lo avdi*, people will not commit an outright forgery by signing their names to a lie — here the two intermediaries are not signing anything. They are only transmitting a message. That lower threshold of personal implication makes bribery a credible concern.

### Dibura Kamari

The *Gemara* distinguishes this from the case of forged documents more precisely. By full document forgery, *ma'aseh lo avdi* — people will not place their own signatures on a fabricated document. But *dibura ka'amri* — mere speech is another matter. Relaying a false message, without signing one's name to any document, is exactly the kind of act that bribed witnesses might be willing to perform. This is the specific danger that makes *lo ta'aseh zot b'Yisrael* necessary even when the arrangement is technically valid.

## Witnesses & Sofer

### Rav Chisda Position

The *Gemara* then considers a variant: *amar l'shnayim imru l'sofer veyichtov v'atem chatomu* — what if the husband's two appointees are themselves the witnesses who will sign, and they only ask the *sofer* to write? In this case the hired-witness concern evaporates, since the witnesses are personally accountable — they are putting their own signatures on the *get*. Rav Chisda rules: *keshera v'lo ta'aseh* — valid, but should not be done. Here the only reason not to do it is prophylactic: if this is permitted, it may lead to the previous scenario being permitted as well.

### Amoraim Dispute

Several *Amoraim* then weigh in with differing positions. Rabba bar bar Chana holds the practice is *kasher ve-ta'aseh* — not only valid but permissible. Rav Nachman rules *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. Rav Sheshet rules *kasher ve-ta'aseh*. Rava rules *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. Rav Yosef rules *kasher ve-ta'aseh*. The *Gemara* adds that some authorities reversed the attributions of Rava and Rav Yosef. The practical upshot is that Shmuel's position — *halacha keRabbi Yosi* on *mili*, while accepting *omer imru* in principle — does not translate straightforwardly into permission *lechatchila*, since the *lo ta'aseh* concern about unverifiable messages applies even when *omer imru* is conceptually valid.

## Ten People Rule

### Without Kulchem

The *Gemara* turns to the rules governing a husband who addresses ten people. A *baraisa* teaches: *amar l'asara kitvu get u-tnu l'ishti, echad kotev al yedei kulam* — if he says to ten people to write a *get* and give it to his wife, one of them writes on behalf of all of them. The word *kitvu* without *kulchem* is interpreted as "from among you" — any one may scribe, any two may sign.

### With Kulchem

If he adds *kulchem* — *kulchem kitvu* — then *echad kotev bemaamad kulam*: one writes while all are present. The analogy to *shaliach tzibur* is instructive. Just as the prayer leader in the repetition of the *Shemoneh Esrei* represents the entire congregation — the entire *tzibur* prays as a single unit through one mouthpiece — so too here, when all ten are physically present and one writes on their behalf, it is as though all ten have written it. Similarly, *kulchem holichu* — all of you bring the *get* — means *echad molich bemaamad kulam*: one carries the *get* while all are present for the conveyance.

### Counting Question

The *Gemara* then raises a question: *mana otan mahu* — what if the husband counts them individually, naming each one? Does enumeration carry the force of *kulchem*, requiring all of them, or does it merely identify the pool from which some may act?

### Counting Debate

Rav Huna holds *mana eino kechulchem* — counting is not equivalent to saying *kulchem*; it means any one of them may act. Rav Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Elazar demin Romi holds the opposite: *mana harei hu kechulchem* — counting is equivalent to *kulchem*, and all of them are required.

### Rav Papa Resolves

Rav Papa resolves the apparent contradiction: *velo peligi* — they do not actually disagree. The distinction lies in whether the husband counted all of the people present or only some of them. *Ha demana kulhu* — if he counted everyone in the group — and *ha demana miktzathu* — if he counted only some. The *Gemara* reports that there is disagreement about which direction the rule runs: some hold that counting everyone is like *kulchem* (since he addressed each individually), while counting a subset means only "from among those I counted"; others hold the reverse. Either way, Rav Papa's point is that the two *Amoraim* were addressing different factual scenarios.

## Takana & Text

### Rav Yehuda Takana

The *Gemara* concludes with a practical ordinance. *Atkin Rav Yehuda be-gitta de-kulchem* — Rav Yehuda instituted a standardized formula for a *get* involving the *kulchem* scenario, designed to avoid the pitfalls just discussed. The formula he proposed: *ktovu o kulcham o kol chad minichon* — write it, whether all of you or any one of you; *chasomu shnayim o kulcham o kol trei minichon* — two of you should sign, whether all or any two; *holichu o kulcham o kol chad minichon* — bring it, whether all or any one of you. By explicitly including both alternatives, the husband's instruction encompasses all possible configurations and prevents invalidation if not everyone is ultimately present or involved.

### Rava Correction

Rava objects to this formulation and improves upon it. The problem with Rav Yehuda's text is structural: *zimnin de-gazlei diburei* — it sometimes happens that the end of the sentence gets cut off. If the husband says *"kulcham"* first and then fails to complete the phrase *"o kol chad minichon"*, the instruction defaults to requiring everyone, and the *get* becomes vulnerable to invalidity if even one person is absent or dies. Therefore, Rava proposes an alternative formulation: begin with the permissive language rather than the restrictive. Say *ktovu kol chad minichon* — write it, any one of you; *chasomu kol trei minichon* — sign it, any two of you; *holichu kol chad minichon* — bring it, any one of you. By establishing the permissive formula first — without ever invoking *kulchem* — the text eliminates the risk entirely. If the husband never says *kulchem*, there is nothing to satisfy and no way for accidental omission to generate a *pasul* *get*.
