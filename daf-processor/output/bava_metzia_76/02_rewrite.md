# Bava Metzia 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Craftsmen/Workers

### Umnin vs. Po'alim

The daf begins a new *perek* at the very bottom of 75b. The opening words are *hasocher et ha'umnin* — one who hires craftsmen. It is worth noting that the following *perek* is called *hasocher et hapo'alim*, and this immediately raises a terminological question. *Umnin* are, technically speaking, craftsmen, while *po'alim* is the more generic term for workers.

### Tosafot: Categories

Tosafot clarifies the distinction: *po'alim* refers more specifically to those paid a daily wage, whereas *umnin* functions as a broader umbrella category that can encompass both daily wage earners and a *kablan* — a contractor paid for completing a job rather than for time worked. This distinction between daily workers and job contractors will bear on the analysis throughout the *perek*.

## Deception/No Recourse

### Taromet Only

The *Mishna* opens: *hasocher et ha'umnin vehitu zeh et zeh, ein lahem zeh al zeh ela tar'omet* — one who hires workers, and they deceive one another, they have only a complaint (*tar'omet*) against one another, but no actual legal recourse for financial compensation. The nature of this deception — who deceived whom, and under what circumstances — is precisely what the *Gemara* will need to unpack. What is already striking is that the *Mishna* immediately proceeds to enumerate cases in which there *are* financial consequences, which raises the question of what the opening case of mere *tar'omet* actually looks like.

### Davar Avad Examples

The *Mishna* gives examples: if one hires a donkey driver or a wagon driver — *chamor* or *kadar* (or perhaps *karar*, as Tosafot notes the textual variant) — to transport materials for a wedding or a funeral, such as *priparin* (a kind of decorative wooden component, which Rashi understands as relating to constructing a *chuppah*, though it may equally refer to the bier used to carry the bride or the deceased) and *chalilim* (flutes, commonly used at funerals as a form of wailing), these are time-sensitive engagements. Similarly, if one hires *po'alim* to remove flax from soaking water at a precise moment — since leaving it too long will ruin it — that constitutes a *davar she'avad*, something that will be irretrievably lost if not attended to immediately.

### Flax Removal

The flax case is the clearest example of tangible loss: if the workers fail to act at the right moment, the flax is ruined and a concrete monetary loss follows. The wedding and funeral cases, however, require additional analysis.

### Tosafot: Davar Avad

Tosafot addresses the wedding and funeral scenarios directly, explaining that *davar ha'avud chashiv lei sha'atah zmano hu la'asot* — it counts as a loss because now is the time to act. If the *chuppah* is not built or the musicians do not arrive, the wedding proceeds without what was needed at the crucial moment. There is no diminution of one's bank account, no measurable net worth reduction — and yet the loss is real. The *davar avad* category, Tosafot makes clear, extends to time-sensitive services even when the harm is not reducible to a simple dollar figure.

## Davar Avad Exception

### Socher Aleihen

The *Mishna* now addresses the *davar avad* scenario where workers back out. If there are no other workers available to replace them — *makom she'ein sham adam* — then one may engage replacement workers even at exorbitant rates, and the original workers bear responsibility for the difference. This is called *socher aleihen*: the employer hires against them, at their expense.

### Deception as Remedy

Alternatively, the *Mishna* permits *mat'an* — deceiving the workers in order to ensure the work is completed. The employer tells the workers he will double their fee; they agree to stay; and after the job is done, he pays only the originally contracted amount. Since they had already reneged on a *davar avad*, the deception used to induce them to complete the work is legally sanctioned. These two remedies — *socher aleihen* and *mat'an* — establish that the opening statement of mere *tar'omet* cannot apply in the *davar avad* context.

## Yad Al HaTachtona

### Workers Back Out

The *Mishna* continues: if workers back out mid-job, *yadam al hatachtona* — they have the lower hand. And conversely, if the *ba'al habayit* backs out, *yado al hatachtona* — he has the lower hand. The expression *yado al hatachtona*, the lower hand as opposed to the upper hand, signals a disadvantaged legal position.

### Rashi on Deduction

Rashi explains the case where workers back out as follows. Suppose the employer contracted for a two-dollar job, the workers completed half, and now the going rate has risen so that finishing the job midday costs a dollar and a half. The workers have earned one dollar for their half, but the employer can withhold from that dollar the additional cost of completing the work, leaving the workers with only fifty cents, so that his total expenditure does not exceed the originally contracted two dollars. This deduction from earned wages is the operative meaning of *yad al hatachtona* when workers are the ones who retract. Of course, this mechanism only functions as long as the amount owed to the workers is sufficient to absorb the additional cost; if the gap exceeds what they have earned, the employer's recourse is more limited.

A natural question arises: if it is not a *davar avad*, must the employer wait until the next morning to hire replacements, or may he hire someone in the middle of the day? The *Mishna* does not make this explicit, and the precise limits of the employer's remedies in non-*davar avad* cases remain somewhat unclear.

### Employer Back Out

When the *ba'al habayit* backs out, the scenario Rashi presents is this: the workers were contracted for three dollars for the whole job, completed half, and then the employer retracted. By the time of the retraction, however, the going rate has fallen to two dollars for the whole job, meaning the workers' half-effort is objectively worth only one dollar at market rates. The ruling is nevertheless that the employer pays them a dollar and a half — the proportional share of the contracted price — rather than the one dollar their work is worth on the open market. The *chiddush* here is not enormous, since a contract was formed and the employer is the one who broke it, but it does illustrate the principle: whoever retracts is in the disadvantaged position.

### General Principle

The *Mishna* concludes by stating the governing principle: *kol hamishaneh yado al hatachtona* — whoever deviates from the agreed terms has the lower hand — and *kol hachozer bo yado al hatachtona* — whoever backs out has the lower hand. The former phrase refers to a broader category (as in the classic *Bava Kamma* case of dyeing wool the wrong color, where one pays the lesser of expenses or improvement to the wool), while the latter appears to be a direct summary of the cases developed in this *Mishna*.

With all of this in view, the original opening of the *Mishna* — the case of *tar'omet* only, with no financial recourse — remains unexplained. The *Gemara* now turns to clarify it.

## Foreman Deception Theory

### Workers Deceive Eachother

The *Gemara* observes: *chazru zeh bazeh lo katani* — the *Mishna* does not say they "backed out," but rather *hitu zeh et zeh*, they "deceived" one another. The *Gemara* takes this as a signal that the opening case is distinct from the subsequent scenarios of retraction. The question then becomes: what constitutes deception that does not involve backing out, and yet yields only *tar'omet*?

The *Gemara*'s initial reading is striking: *de'atu po'alim ahadadi* — the workers deceived one another. That is, *hitu zeh et zeh* does not describe employer deceiving employee, but workers deceiving each other.

### Foreman Pockets Difference

The mechanism the *Gemara* envisions is as follows. The *ba'al habayit* hires one man as a foreman and says to him: *zil ogar li po'alim* — go hire workers for me. The foreman is told to hire them at three dollars a day, but he goes and tells the workers they will receive four dollars a day, pocketing the difference himself. This is the deception: the workers deceive one another in the sense that the foreman, himself a kind of worker, defrauds the other workers he recruits.

One might immediately ask: if the going rate is three and the foreman promised four, why do the workers have any complaint at all? *Savru vekabilu* — they assessed the terms and accepted. Conversely, if the *ba'al habayit* authorized four and the foreman told them three, and then refused to pay more, the foreman has plainly undertaken a contractual obligation toward them.

### Sharchkem Al Ba'al Habayit

The critical case, the *Gemara* explains, is where the foreman tells the workers that their pay is *al ha-ba'al habayit* — the employer is responsible for their wages, not the foreman himself. In this configuration, the foreman has not personally assumed the obligation. He is merely the conduit. The workers' complaint, if any, runs only against the foreman — not against the *ba'al habayit*, who may not have authorized the higher figure, and not as a binding legal claim.

## Promise vs. Misrepresent…

### Foreman Assumes Obligation

A *baraita* clarifies the legal consequence when the foreman changes his posture. If the foreman says *sharchchem alai* — your wages are on me — he personally assumes the obligation. In that case, the workers collect the full promised amount from the foreman directly.

### Foreman Seeks Reimbursement

The foreman can then turn to the *ba'al habayit* and seek reimbursement *mah shenehneh* — for however much the employer actually benefited. This principle is illustrated by a *baraita*: if I hire Michael to mow my lawn and then show him my neighbor Gloria's lawn, telling him it is mine, I must pay Michael his full contracted fee. I may then seek reimbursement from Gloria — but only *mah shenehneh*, only for the benefit she actually received. If the going rate for mowing a lawn is ten dollars and I paid Michael twenty, I can recover ten from Gloria, not twenty.

This draws on the established principle of *yored lesoch sdei chaveiro shelo birshut* — one who enters another's field and improves it without permission. The owner of the field cannot be made to pay the worker's contracted fee, since she never entered into a contractual relationship, but she does owe for the concrete benefit she received. The *da'as* and willingness of the beneficiary matter: if Gloria prefers her lawn overgrown and would not have considered a trimmed lawn a benefit, the calculus becomes more complicated — but that question is set aside here.

Applied to the foreman case: if the foreman said *sharchchem alai*, he owes them four. He may then recover from the *ba'al habayit*, but almost certainly no more than three, assuming three was the authorized rate and presumably the going rate. In that scenario, there is no unresolved complaint — the workers collect from the foreman and the foreman settles with the *ba'al habayit*.

### Foreman Misrepresents Wage

The hard case is where the foreman said *sharchchem al ha-ba'al habayit* — placing the obligation on the employer — but misrepresented the wage. The workers worked for the *ba'al habayit*, trusting that the foreman accurately conveyed what the employer had authorized. Now the *Gemara* considers how much the *ba'al habayit* owes them.

The *Gemara* says: *venichzi po'alim heichi misgarei* — let us see what the going rate for workers actually is. If all workers are going for four, then even though the *ba'al habayit* only authorized three, he may well owe four — because that is the objective value of the labor he received, analogous to the *yored lesoch sdei chaveiro* principle. The *ba'al habayit* clearly needed the work done; the implicit understanding that he required the service may create a quasi-contractual obligation even absent an explicit agreement.

## Complaint Threshold

### Scenario 1: Alt Job Available

The interesting case arises when there is a split market — some workers available at four, some at three. The *Gemara* says: *de'amru lei — ei lav de'amrat lan be'arba, tarchinan u'misgarinan be'arba* — they say to the foreman: had you not told us four, we would have hustled and secured a four-dollar job elsewhere. This is a legitimate complaint. The misrepresentation caused a real opportunity cost. Yet the *ba'al habayit* is not legally liable for more than three, since he only intended to pay three and only received a benefit assessable at three. The foreman's workers cannot extract more than that from the employer, and so their complaint — while genuine — yields only *tar'omet*, a grievance without legal recourse.

This raises a practical question: if the *ba'al habayit* can always instruct his foreman to promise four when he is only willing to pay three, knowing that the workers will end up with three and have no recourse, what protection do the workers have? The honest answer is that, over time, reputation matters, and workers will learn to verify. But it remains a striking vulnerability in the system, and the *Gemara* does not fully resolve it.

### Scenario 2: Own Field

The second scenario is *ba'al habayit askinan* — not generic hired laborers, but someone who owns his own field and would normally have spent the day working his own land. He was induced to leave his own work by the promise of four dollars. His complaint is not that he could have found another four-dollar employer; it is that he would never have left his own work for the standard rate of three. The foreman's misrepresentation specifically caused him to forgo his own productivity. Again, however, since the *ba'al habayit* only intended to pay three and only benefited at the level of three, the worker collects three and registers his complaint.

### Scenario 3: Quality Work

The third scenario is that the workers, believing they were contracted for four, worked extra hard and did superior-quality work. Their complaint is: *kivan de'amrat lan be'arba, tarchinan ve'avidnan lach avidsa shapirta* — because you told us four, we exerted ourselves and did you an exceptional job. If they truly did work worth four dollars, the *ba'al habayit* would in principle owe them four — *mah shenehneh* — since that is the objective benefit he received. But the *Gemara* presents the case where verification is impossible: the job was digging a furrow, and by now it is *demali maya velo yediya* — filled with water, so there is no way to inspect and assess the quality of the work. Since *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — the burden of proof rests on the claimant — and the workers cannot prove they did a four-dollar job, they collect three and are left with only *tar'omet*.

### Benefit-Based Payment

All three scenarios share a common structure. The workers have a legitimate basis for complaint in each case: they could have waited for a four-dollar job, they would not have left their own land for three dollars, or they performed work of superior quality. These reasons satisfy the threshold for *tar'omet* — a genuine grievance, something like a well-founded complaint to a professional body. But the *ba'al habayit* is not liable beyond what he actually authorized and benefited. If there is no reason to have expected more than three — if, for instance, the entire market was going for three, the worker had no independent work to forgo, and there is no quality premium at issue — then even *tar'omet* falls away, because there is simply no basis for any complaint at all.

The *Gemara* also presents a fourth variation worth noting briefly: *le'olam de'amar lei ba'al habayit be'arba ve'azal ihu ve'amar lehu betlata* — the *ba'al habayit* authorized four and the foreman told the workers three. The workers accepted, saying *nelaseh kemo she'amar ba'al habayit*, "we'll do whatever the *ba'al habayit* says." Their complaint to the foreman rests on the principle *al timna tov mib'alav* — do not withhold good from its rightful owners; the employer was prepared to pay them well and the foreman underpaid them. Yet even here they cannot recover more than three from the *ba'al habayit*, since that is what was agreed.

## Trust/Authority Question

### Whose Word Standard

The *Gemara* now raises a fundamental question about what it means when the workers say *nelaseh kemo she'amar ba'al habayit* — we will act as the *ba'al habayit* has said. Does this mean they trust the foreman's representation, accepting his words as an accurate account of the employer's intent? Or does it mean they reserve judgment and will act according to what the *ba'al habayit* actually said, with his word taking precedence over the foreman's?

This question has real stakes. In the scenario where the *ba'al habayit* authorized four but the foreman told them three, and the workers said *kemo she'amar ba'al habayit*, the *Gemara* observes that it is *pshita* — obvious — that they meant the higher figure, since no one undersells themselves by invoking what the employer said when the employer said more. But the harder question is the reverse: where the *ba'al habayit* authorized three, the foreman told them four, and the workers said *kemo she'amar ba'al habayit* — did they mean "we trust you that this is what the employer said," thereby agreeing to four? Or did they mean "whatever the employer actually said, that is what governs" — thereby, had they known, agreeing to three?

### Gitin Analogy

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this by analogy to *gittin*. A woman says to a messenger: *hevei li gitti* — go bring me my *get*. She is designating him as a *shliach leholacha*, an agent to deliver the *get* from the husband to her. He goes to the husband and tells him: *ve'ishtecha amra, hitkabel li giti* — your wife told me to be a *shliach lekabbala*, an agent to receive it on her behalf, so that she is divorced the moment he accepts it. He has misrepresented what the wife said, elevating his own role (requiring less travel) instead of what she actually designated. The husband responds: *heilech kemah she'amra* — take it as she said.

The ruling: *afilu higi'a get leyada einah megoresheret* — she is not divorced even when the *get* arrives in her hands. Why not? Because the husband said *kemah she'amra*, trusting the messenger's representation of what the wife said. Since the wife did not designate him as *shliach lekabbala*, the husband's handing over the *get* on that basis accomplished nothing. And since the husband also did not designate him as *shliach leholacha*, delivery to the woman similarly accomplished nothing. The case implies that *kemah she'amra* means "as you represent her as having said" — trusting the messenger, not independently verifying the principal.

### Rav Ashi Distinction

Rav Ashi objects to the application of this case to the employment context. The *get* case, he argues, fails as a proof not because of any ambiguity in the meaning of *kemah she'amra*, but because of a prior structural problem: the messenger rejected his own *shlichus*. When the wife designated him *shliach leholacha* — obligating him to travel and deliver — and he instead declared himself *shliach lekabbala*, taking the easier role, he was in effect saying *shliach leholacha lo havina* — I refuse to serve as the delivery agent. He effectively terminated his *shlichus* before the *get* was even transmitted. The entire transaction therefore failed for a reason independent of whether the husband was relying on the messenger's words or on the wife's actual words.

### Hypothetical Test

Rav Ashi therefore specifies what would have constituted a proper test. Had the wife said *hitkabel li giti* — designating him as *shliach lekabbala* — and he then told the husband *ve'ishtecha amra, havi li giti* — representing her as having designated him *shliach leholacha*, a more onerous role — he would have been *accepting* greater responsibility rather than reducing it. In that scenario, one could ask: if the husband said *heilech kemah she'amra* and the *get* was delivered, is the woman divorced immediately (which would indicate the husband was relying on the wife's actual words, making him *shliach lekabbala*) or only upon delivery (indicating he was trusting the messenger's representation, treating him as *shliach leholacha*)? That would have been a clean test of the question. As reported by Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha citing Rav, in that hypothetical scenario she would be divorced *mishiga'a get leyada* — upon delivery — which would resolve the question in one direction. But since the *Gemara* does not actually have that case before it, the inference remains speculative.

### Unresolved Shlichus

The conclusion is that the *get* analogy does not resolve the employment question. Whether the workers' statement *kemo she'amar ba'al habayit* means they trusted the foreman's representation or reserved their reliance for the employer's actual words remains unanswered. The broader question of whether the foreman functions as a true *shliach* of the *ba'al habayit* — creating binding contractual obligations in the employer's name — is similarly left open. This is partly because *shlichus* in the classical sense typically attaches to concrete transactional acts (buying, selling, delivering a *get*), not to the act of entering into a labor arrangement. Something more like an *adrachta*, a power of attorney, might ordinarily be required for that kind of representational authority. The *da'as* of the *ba'al habayit* and the workers' implicit understanding of whom they are really contracting with may be the operative factors, but the *Gemara* does not resolve this fully.

## Baraita: Specific Cases

### Baraita Agrees

Returning now to the *pshat* of the *Mishna*, the *Gemara* suggests: *ei ba'it eima, hai tanna chazru nami hitu kari lei* — perhaps this *tanna* uses the word *hitu* (deceived) to describe what we would call backing out (*chazru*). The *Mishna* and the subsequent *baraita* may both be about the same employer-employee dynamic, and the word "deceived" is simply this *tanna*'s idiom for reneging on a commitment. A *baraita* is then quoted that opens with language parallel to the *Mishna*: *hasocher et ha'umnin vehitu et ha-ba'al habayit o ha-ba'al habayit hita otan* — the workers deceived the employer, or the employer deceived the workers — *ein lahem zeh al zeh ela tar'omet*, they have only complaints. This makes explicit what the *Mishna* left ambiguous: the deception is between the employer and the employees directly, not among the workers themselves.

### Before Work Begins

Critically, the *baraita* immediately qualifies its ruling: *bameh devarim amurim shelo halchu* — when is this true? When the workers have not yet arrived at the job site. Before the work begins, the arrangement has not yet ripened into a binding contract. It remains a plan, an agreement in principle. If either party backs out at this stage — even at the last moment, even after the other party has made preparations — the consequence is only *tar'omet*. No financial liability attaches. This is a striking rule: even if workers turned down other opportunities or made logistical preparations based on the arrangement, so long as they have not yet physically shown up to begin work, the other party can walk away with only a complaint lodged against them.

### Workers Arrive: Full Pay

Once the workers arrive, however, the calculus changes entirely. The *baraita* gives examples: the donkey drivers went to pick up grain and found none available (*velo matzu tvua*), or workers came to plow a field and found the soil too wet (*vematzu sade kesheehi lach*). In both cases, they showed up — and *noten lahem sechar mishalam*, the employer gives them their full wages. The concept of *poel batel* applies here: the employer does not pay for actual work done, since none was done, but rather the discounted amount a worker would accept to come and sit idle for the day rather than plow. The *Gemara* notes *eino domeh haba ta'un leba reikan* — it is not the same to come bearing a load as to come back with an empty wagon — and *oseh melacha leyoshev ubatel* — it is not the same to do work as to sit and do nothing. So the worker is paid the going rate minus what he would accept to stand idle: less than his full daily wage, but a real obligation nonetheless.

## Work Begun: Proportional Pay…

### Proportional Payment

The *baraita* continues: *elu hitchilu bemilacha, shamin lahem ma sheasu* — if they already began the work, one assesses what they have done and pays them accordingly.

### Grain Cutting Example

The illustration: workers contracted to cut an entire field of grain for two *sela'im*, cut half, and left. Or they contracted to weave a garment for two *sela'im* and completed half. *Shamin lahem et ma sheasu* — you assess what they did. The job is half done; pay them proportionally.

### Tanna Kamma View

What makes the *baraita* significant, however, is the dispute that emerges here. According to the Tanna Kamma, even if completing the remaining half will now cost more than what was originally allocated for it — say, one and a half *sela'im* instead of one — the workers who backed out still receive the full proportional wage for what they completed: one *sela* for the half they did. They are not penalized for the additional cost their departure imposes on the employer. The worker is always protected, even mid-job. This position, the *Gemara* implies, represents the Tanna Kamma view and actually differs from the position of our *Mishna*.

### Rabbi Dosa View

Rabbi Dosa disagrees: *shamin lahem ma she'atid le'hei'asos* — assess not what they did, but what it will cost to complete the remaining work. If finishing the other half will now cost one and a half *sela'im*, and the whole job was contracted for two, then the workers who completed the first half receive only half a *sela* — so that the employer's total outlay remains two. In other words, the additional cost of their departure is borne by the workers, deducted from what they have already earned. The *Gemara* concludes that our *Mishna* follows Rabbi Dosa on this point.

### Cost-Withholding Rule

The rules Rabbi Dosa and the Tanna Kamma give are symmetrical in one direction: if the remaining work is worth exactly one *sela*, then even Rabbi Dosa agrees the workers receive one *sela* — that is simply the proportional payment. The dispute concerns only the case where the remaining work costs *more* than the proportional share. Rabbi Dosa says that excess cost is absorbed by the departing workers; the Tanna Kamma says it is not. An alternative to deducting the extra cost: Rabbi Dosa allows the workers to simply finish the job and collect the full two *sela'im* — *o yigmeru melachtan veyitlu shnay sela'im* — rather than suffer the deduction. The choice between finishing and accepting the reduced payment is theirs to make.

## Davar Avad Remedy

### Replacement Availability

The *baraita* then returns to the *davar avad* situation. Even in a time-critical case, the remedies of *socher aleihen* and *mat'an* are not automatically available. The governing principle is *bameh devarim amurim bizman she'ein sham po'alim liskor* — these stronger remedies apply only when there are no readily available replacement workers in the market.

### Doubled Wage Deception

If *mat'an* is appropriate, the procedure is: *omer lahen, sela katzati lachem, bo'u utelu shtayim* — the employer tells them he will double their wage to keep them working, and then, once the job is complete, pays only the originally contracted amount. Since they had been in the process of abandoning a *davar avad*, the deception is legally sanctioned as a remedy.

### Cost Limit

As for *socher aleihen* — hiring replacements at elevated rates and holding the original workers responsible — the *baraita* says this extends *ad arba'im vechamishim zuz*, up to forty or fifty *zuz* of additional cost, a substantial sum, reflecting how seriously the law treats the harm of abandoning time-critical work.

### Market Availability

However, if there are workers readily available in the market, the employer cannot invoke these remedies even in a *davar avad* case. If the workers who backed out tell him to go hire others — *tze'u sechoru me'eilu* — and replacements are accessible, then *ein lo aleihen ella tar'omet*: the employer has only a complaint against them, no more. The accessibility of alternatives removes the emergency justification for the stronger remedies.

The *baraita* thus both reinforces and qualifies the *Mishna*. It confirms that the *tar'omet*-only rule applies when nothing has yet begun; it establishes that showing up for work converts the arrangement into a binding daily obligation; it records a dispute between the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Dosa about worker liability when work has already started; and it carefully circumscribes the *davar avad* remedies by market conditions. Tomorrow's shiur will take up Tosafot's discussion of the questions left open — particularly regarding lost opportunity costs and preparatory expenses incurred before the work begins.
