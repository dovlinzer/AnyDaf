# Bava Metzia 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Malbin Pnei Chaveiro

### Whitening Face

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ״ – בְּאוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּאוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹנָאַת מָמוֹן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְכִי תִמְכְּרוּ מִמְכָּר לַעֲמִיתֶךָ אוֹ קָנֹה מִיַּד עֲמִיתֶךָ״, הֲרֵי אוֹנָאַת מָמוֹן אָמוּר! הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״לֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ״? בְּאוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים. תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַמַּלְבִּין פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים, כְּאִילּוּ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** It is written: **"And you shall not mistreat [*tonu*] one man his colleague"** (Leviticus 25:17). **The verse is speaking with regard to verbal mistreatment.** The *baraita* proceeds: Do **you say** that it is speaking of **verbal mistreatment [*be'ona'at devarim*], or** perhaps **it is** speaking **only with regard to monetary exploitation [*be'ona'at mammon*]? When it says** in a previous verse: **"And if you sell to your colleague an item that is sold, or acquire from your colleague's hand,** you shall not exploit [*tonu*] his brother"** (Leviticus 25:14), **monetary exploitation is** explicitly **stated. How** then **do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"And you shall not mistreat one man his colleague"?** It is **with regard to verbal mistreatment.** The Gemara relates that **the *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: Anyone who humiliates another in public, it is as though he were spilling blood.**

The *daf* opens in the middle of a discussion about *ona'at devarim* — the prohibition against causing pain through speech. A *tanna* taught before Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *kol hamalbin pnei chaveiro b'rabim ke'ilu shofech damim* — anyone who whitens the face of his friend in public, causing him to blanch from embarrassment, is as though he has spilled blood. This is among the most widely recognized statements of the Gemara.

### Blood Rushing Away

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָא אָמְרַתְּ, דְּחָזֵינָא לֵיהּ דְּאָזֵיל סוּמָּקָא וְאָתֵי חִוּוֹרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **said to him: You have spoken well, as we see that** after the humiliated person blushes, **the red leaves** his face **and pallor comes** in its place, which is tantamount to spilling his blood.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responded to this teaching: *shapir ka'amart* — that is indeed a well-stated teaching, and here is why. *DeChazeina lei de'azil sumka v'ati chivra* — we can observe that when a person is publicly shamed, the redness drains from his face and is replaced by whiteness. The *malbin pnei chaveiro* thus carries a figurative resonance: you cause the blood to rush out of him, as if you have literally spilled it. The original force of the statement should not be lost, however. Public embarrassment does not merely cause momentary discomfort — it can devastate a person's standing in the community and leave him unsure whether he can ever face the public again. Something deep and destructive is at work. The metaphor of spilled blood reinforces and intensifies that intuition.

### Eretz Yisrael's Caution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב דִּימִי: בְּמַעְרְבָא בְּמַאי זְהִירִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאַחְווֹרֵי אַפֵּי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַכֹּל יוֹרְדִין לְגֵיהִנָּם, חוּץ מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Dimi: In the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **with regard to what** mitzva **are they** particularly **vigilant?** Rav Dimi **said to him:** They are vigilant **in** refraining from **humiliating** others, **as Rabbi Ḥanina says: Everyone descends to Gehenna except for three.**

Abaye asked Rav Dimi: *b'ma'arava b'mai zahiri* — in *Eretz Yisrael*, what are people particularly scrupulous about? The question is posed in the context of *bein adam l'chaveiro*, and the answer reflects that context. Rav Dimi replied: *b'achvuri appei* — whitening a person's face. The seriousness with which *Eretz Yisrael* regarded this prohibition is itself a significant moral teaching: when people ask what marks a truly *frum* person, the answer is not a stringency in ritual law but rather a sensitivity toward how words and actions affect another human being.

### Three Types to Gehinnom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כָּל הַיּוֹרְדִין לְגֵיהִנָּם עוֹלִים, חוּץ מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁיּוֹרְדִין וְאֵין עוֹלִין. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַבָּא עַל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְהַמַּלְבִּין פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים, וְהַמְכַנֶּה שֵׁם רַע לַחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does it enter your mind** that **everyone** descends to Gehenna? **Rather, say: Anyone who descends to Gehenna** ultimately **ascends, except for three who descend and do not ascend, and these are they: One who engages in intercourse with a married woman,** as this transgression is a serious offense against both God and a person; **and one who humiliates another in public; and one who calls** another **a derogatory name.**

This is grounded in a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan. The Gemara introduces his statement carefully. One might initially read *hakol yordim l'Gehinnom* as saying that everyone goes to *Gehinnom*, but the Gemara clarifies: the meaning is that all who descend to *Gehinnom* will ultimately ascend — *chutz mishelosha*, except for three. Those three go down and do not come back up. Rabbi Yochanan enumerates them: *haba al eshet ish* — one who commits adultery with a married woman; *hamalbin pnei chaveiro b'rabim* — one who publicly shames his fellow; and *hamachane shem l'chaveiro* — one who gives his friend a derogatory nickname.

### Three Unforgivable Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכַנֶּה הַיְינוּ מַלְבִּין? אַף עַל גַּב דְּדָשׁ בֵּיהּ בִּשְׁמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to **one who calls** another a derogatory name: **That is** identical to **one who shames** him; why are they listed separately? The Gemara answers: **Although** the victim **grew accustomed to** being called that name **in** place of **his name,** and he is no longer humiliated by being called that name, since the intent was to insult him, the perpetrator's punishment is severe.

The Gemara raises an apparent redundancy. Is giving a nickname not the same as *malbin pnei chaveiro*? The answer is *af al gav dedash bei bishmei* — even if the nickname has already become so common that everyone uses it, anyone who continues to employ it transgresses, because doing so perpetuates and reinforces a diminished perception of that person in society. The stakes here are striking: it is not only the originator of the nickname who bears culpability, but anyone who perpetuates it.

### Nicknames & Intent

This is admittedly a demanding ruling. A person who uses a name that has become universal — where even the bearer may claim not to mind — is apparently still included. Rashi, sensitive to this difficulty, limits the prohibition to a case where the speaker intends to shame: *mikol makom zeh l'hachlimo nitkaven*. Even if the name is in common usage, if the speaker deploys it with the intention of causing embarrassment, he is culpable. The Maharsha goes further, suggesting that perhaps the concern is akin to *motzi shem ra* — the spreading of a damaging reputation — even where the individual himself is not visibly shamed in the moment. He also notes that the Rambam does not restrict this to degrading nicknames but includes any nickname whatsoever. The range of opinions here reflects a genuine tension between the gravity of the principle and the expansiveness of its application. What remains constant across the authorities is the underlying moral priority: what people in *Eretz Yisrael* are most scrupulous about is not a ritual stringency but the way they speak about and to other human beings.

## David & Bathsheba

### Safek Eshet Ish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נוֹחַ לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיָּבוֹא עַל סְפֵק אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְאַל יַלְבִּין פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** **It is preferable for a person to engage in intercourse with a woman** whose **married** status is **uncertain and not humiliate another in public.**

Rabba bar Bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *noach lo le'adam sheyavo al safek eshet ish v'al yalbin pnei chaveiro b'rabim* — it is preferable for a person to have relations with a woman whose marital status is in doubt than to publicly shame his fellow. This is a remarkable statement: the sin of potential adultery is placed in second position to the sin of *malbin pnei chaveiro*. The *safek eshet ish* referred to here is a case where there is genuine uncertainty — for example, whether the woman's husband has died or is still alive. The basis for this comparison will be explored through the following Scriptural derivation.

### David's Psalm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לַן? מִדְּדָרֵשׁ רָבָא, דְּדָרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וּבְצַלְעִי שָׂמְחוּ וְנֶאֱסָפוּ... קָרְעוּ וְלֹא דָמּוּ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? The Gemara answers: It is **from that which Rava interpreted, as Rava interpreted: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And when I limped they rejoiced and gathered…they tore and did not cease [*damu*]"** (Psalms 35:15)? The term "*damu*" can also be understood as a reference to blood.

The Gemara asks: *mena lei* — from where does Rabbi Yochanan derive this? The answer is from what Rava explicated. Rava expounded on the verse in Tehillim: *uvatzali samachu ne'asfu, kar'u v'lo damu* — "in my stumbling they rejoiced and gathered; they tore me and it did not cease." The word *lo damu* is read not as "did not cease" but as *dam* — blood — yielding the meaning "and there was no blood." This reading sets the stage for David's statement to God.

### David Mocked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ, שֶׁאִם הָיוּ מְקָרְעִים בְּשָׂרִי, לֹא הָיָה דָּמִי שׁוֹתֵת לָאָרֶץ. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בִּנְגָעִים וְאֹהָלוֹת, אוֹמְרִים לִי: דָּוִד, הַבָּא עַל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, מִיתָתוֹ בַּמֶּה? וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר לָהֶם: מִיתָתוֹ בְּחֶנֶק, וְיֵשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning the fasting he undertook to atone for his sin with Bathsheba (see II Samuel, chapters 11–12), **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, it is revealed and known before You that if** my tormenters **were to tear my flesh, my blood [*dami*] would not flow to the ground,** due to excessive fasting. **And moreover,** they torment me to the extent that **even at the time** when **they are engaged** in the public study of the *halakhot* **of leprous sores and tents** in which there is a corpse, i.e., halakhic matters that have no connection to my sin, **they say to me: David, one who engages in intercourse with a married woman, his death** is effected **with what** form of execution? **And I say to them: One who engages in intercourse with a married woman** before witnesses and with forewarning, **his death is by strangulation, but he** still **has a share in the World-to-Come.**

The Gemara reads the verse as David's address to the Holy One. David says before God: *galui v'yadua lefanecha she'im hayu mekar'im b'sari lo hayah dami shotet la'aretz* — it is known to You that if people were to tear my flesh, my blood would not flow to the ground, because of all the fasting I have done in consequence of my sin involving Batsheva and the child. This personal diminishment is not the end of his suffering. Even when *talmidei chachamim* are engaged in the most demanding areas of *halacha* — *negaim* and *ohalot* — they stop their learning to mock him: *David, haba al eshet ish mitato b'meh* — David, one who commits adultery with a married woman, what is his punishment? They taunt him with his sin of Batsheva.

The Maharsha adds a pointed observation: the association with *negaim* is deliberate, since *tzaraat* is a consequence of *lashon hara*. The implicit message is sharp: these scholars who pride themselves on their *frumkeit*, defending the sanctity of the law, are themselves engaged in shaming another — which is the gravest of sins.

### David's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַמַּלְבִּין אֶת פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים, אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But one who humiliates another in public has no share in the World-to-Come.** The transgression of you, who humiliate me, is more severe than my transgression.

David's response is striking. He does not deny the sin and does not attempt to deflect the shame entirely. He answers: *mitato b'chenek, v'yesh lo chelek l'olam haba* — yes, the punishment for adultery is strangulation, but the sinner retains a share in the *olam haba*. By contrast, *hamalbin et pnei chaveiro b'rabim ein lo chelek l'olam haba* — one who publicly shames his fellow has no share in the world to come. David essentially turns the mockery back on his tormentors: however terrible my sin, the sin you are committing against me right now is categorically worse.

The earlier teaching about the *safek eshet ish* is also illuminated here. Based on another Gemara, there is a position that David may have arranged for Uriah to die in battle such that Batsheva retroactively was never fully married to him — which is the *safek eshet ish* of that discussion. David's own defense in the Psalm, however, does not even rely on the *safek*: his argument holds even in the case of clear adultery, which he acknowledges carried a real punishment. What he insists is that it did not forfeit his portion in *olam haba*, whereas *malbin pnei chaveiro* does. The parallel between his bloodless flesh and the draining of blood from a shamed face is also worth noting — though the Gemara does not spell it out directly, there is an implicit resonance between his self-description and the mechanism of *malbin pnei chaveiro*.

## Furnace Over Shame

### Fire Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא, וְאָמְרִי לָהּ: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: נוֹחַ לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיַּפִּיל עַצְמוֹ לְכִבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ וְאַל יַלְבִּין פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Mar Zutra bar Toviyya says** that **Rav says; and some say Rav Ḥana bar Bizna says** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida says; and some say Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: It is more comfortable for a person to cast himself into a fiery furnace,** than to **humiliate another in public** to avoid being cast into the furnace.

Mar Zutra bar Tuvya said in the name of Rav — and others attribute the statement to Rav Chana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida, or to Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *noach lo le'adam sheyapil atzmo l'kivshan ha'eish v'al yalbin pnei chaveiro b'rabim* — it is better for a person to throw himself into a fiery furnace than to publicly shame his fellow. The force of this statement extends beyond intentional shaming: even if one might inadvertently bring about another's public humiliation, one should be prepared to suffer extreme personal harm rather than allow it to occur. The comparison to loss of life recurs here as in earlier statements, underscoring the Gemara's consistent treatment of public embarrassment as tantamount to a mortal offense.

## Furnace Over Shame…

### Tamar's Discretion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לַן? מִתָּמָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״הִיא מוּצֵאת וְהִיא שָׁלְחָה אֶל חָמִיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive this? **From Tamar,** daughter-in-law of Judah. When she was taken out to be burned, she did not reveal that she was pregnant with Judah's child. Rather, she left the decision to him, to avoid humiliating him in public, **as it is written:** "And Judah said: Bring her forth, and let her be burnt. **When she was brought forth, she sent to her father-in-law,** saying: I am pregnant by the man to whom these belong. And she said: Examine these, whose are these, the signet, and the cords, and the staff?" (Genesis 38:24–25).

The Gemara derives this principle from the story of Tamar. *Minalan mi-Tamar* — from Tamar, as the verse states: *hi mutzet v'hi shalacha el chamia leimor* — she was being led out to be burned, and she sent a message to her father-in-law Judah with the evidence that he was the father of her child, rather than confronting him directly. She gave Judah the opportunity to recognize his own seal, cord, and staff — and to take responsibility himself — rather than shaming him publicly. She was prepared to be cast into the fire rather than expose him.

It is worth noting that on the level of *pshat*, Tamar's indirect approach may itself have been what made Judah capable of acknowledging the truth: direct confrontation might well have provoked denial. Nevertheless, the Gemara reads her conduct as a moral model — she valued his honor above her own life.

### Fire & Honor

The Maharsha adds a further dimension through wordplay. The word *hi mutzet* — she was being led out — can be read as related to *lehatzit*, to kindle a fire. In this reading, the fire was already being lit upon her, she was already in the face of the flames, and still she would not name him directly. The choice between her own destruction and his shame was made clearly in favor of his honor.

## David & Bathsheba…

### Tamar's Example

Tamar's precedent is invoked directly in connection with the earlier teaching about preferring to be cast into a furnace over publicly shaming another. Her willingness to be burned alive rather than expose Judah is the paradigmatic demonstration of that principle — the readiness to absorb the full weight of personal destruction rather than transfer shame onto another person.

## Ona'ah Conditions

### Torah Commitment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַח חִנָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְלֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ״? עַם שֶׁאִתְּךָ בְּתוֹרָה וּבַמִּצְווֹת, אַל תּוֹנֵיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rav Idi, says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And you shall not mistreat each man his colleague [*amito*]"** (Leviticus 25:17)? The word *amito* is interpreted as a contraction of *im ito*, meaning: One who is with him. **With** one who is **with you in** observance of **Torah and mitzvot, you shall not mistreat** him.

Rabbi Chanina, son of Rav Idi, asks: what is the meaning of the verse *velo tonu ish et amito* — do not afflict a man his fellow? Why does the verse use the word *amito* rather than *chaveiro*? He derives: *im she'itcha b'Torah uvemitzvot al tonehu* — only with one who is with you in Torah and *mitzvot* does this prohibition apply. This is a limiting principle: the prohibition of *ona'at devarim* in its fullest sense applies specifically to one who is committed to a life of Torah and *mitzvot*. By contrast, someone who has openly and defiantly abandoned that way of life — the *rasha* of that era, where such rejection represented a genuine and deliberate break — may appropriately be spoken about critically, in order to maintain distance from his influence and prevent others from following his path. It must be stressed that this limitation does not apply to monetary overreaching, which extends to everyone. The limitation applies specifically to verbal affliction.

### Wife's Sensitivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יְהֵא אָדָם זָהִיר בְּאוֹנָאַת אִשְׁתּוֹ, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁדִּמְעָתָהּ מְצוּיָה, אוֹנָאָתָהּ קְרוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: A person must always be careful about mistreatment of his wife. Since her tear is easily** elicited, punishment for **her mistreatment is immediate.**

Rav teaches: *leolam yehei adam zahir b'ona'at ishto* — a man should always be particularly careful that his words do not cause pain to his wife. Even presumably unintentional words require extra vigilance in this context.

### Women's Tears

The reason given is: *shemitoch shedimaatah metzuyah, ona'atah krova* — because her tears are close to the surface, the likelihood of *ona'ah* is much nearer. The Gemara is drawing on a generalization about emotional sensitivity. While one may set aside the gendered framing, the underlying moral counsel remains sound and practically important: words that would be absorbed without harm in some relationships can cause genuine pain in others, and the closer the relationship, the greater the care required. The particular sensitivity of a spouse — and the ease with which well-intentioned speech can cause harm — demands its own watchfulness.

## Gates of Heaven

### Prayer Gates Sealed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִיּוֹם שֶׁנֶּחְרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נִנְעֲלוּ שַׁעֲרֵי תְפִילָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּם כִּי אֶזְעַק וַאֲשַׁוֵּעַ שָׂתַם תְּפִילָּתִי״. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשַּׁעֲרֵי תְפִילָּה נִנְעֲלוּ, שַׁעֲרֵי דְמָעוֹת לֹא נִנְעֲלוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁמְעָה תְפִלָּתִי ה׳ וְשַׁוְעָתִי הַאֲזִינָה, אֶל דִּמְעָתִי אַל תֶּחֱרַשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Since the day the Temple was destroyed the gates of prayer were locked,** and prayer is not accepted as it once was, **as it is stated** in lament of the Temple's destruction: **"Though I plead and call out, He shuts out my prayer"** (Lamentations 3:8). Yet, **despite** the fact **that the gates of prayer were locked** with the destruction of the Temple, **the gates of tears were not locked,** and one who cries before God may rest assured that his prayers will be answered, **as it is stated: "Hear my prayer, Lord, and give ear to my pleading, keep not silence at my tears"** (Psalms 39:13).

Rabbi Elazar teaches: *miyom shenechrav Beit Hamikdash nin'alu sha'arei tefilla* — from the day the *Beit Hamikdash* was destroyed, the gates of prayer were sealed. The *Beit Hamikdash* served as the channel through which prayer ascended — *zeh sha'ar hashamayim* — and its destruction closed that direct access. The verse cited is: *gam ki ezak va'ashavea satam tefillati* — even when I cry out and beseech, my prayer is shut up.

Yet there is a qualification: *sha'arei dema'ot lo nin'alu* — the gates of tears were not sealed. The verse reads: *shim'ah tefillati Hashem, el dim'ati al techerash* — hear my prayer, God, do not deafen yourself to my tears. Rashi reads this not as a petition but as a declaration: God will not make Himself deaf to tears; He will certainly hear them. The implication for *ona'at devarim* is immediate: if the tears that flow from one who has been verbally afflicted reach God directly through ungated access, then the consequences of causing those tears are correspondingly severe.

## Household & Wife

### Husband-Wife Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַהוֹלֵךְ בַּעֲצַת אִשְׁתּוֹ נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רַק לֹא הָיָה כְאַחְאָב וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְהָא אָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי, אִיתְּתָךְ גּוּצָא – גְּחֵין וְתִלְחוֹשׁ לָהּ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּמִילֵּי דְעָלְמָא, וְהָא בְּמִילֵּי דְבֵיתָא. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: הָא בְּמִילֵּי דִשְׁמַיָּא, וְהָא בְּמִילֵּי דְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav says:** Nevertheless, **anyone who follows the counsel of his wife descends into Gehenna, as it is stated: "But there was none like Ahab,** who did give himself over to do that which was evil in the sight of the Lord, whom Jezebel his wife incited"** (I Kings 21:25). **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But don't people say** a popular proverb: If **your wife is short, stoop and whisper to her** and consult with her? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** statement of Rav instructs that one not follow her counsel **in general matters; and that** proverb instructs that one follow her counsel **in household matters.** The Gemara presents **another version** of this distinction: **This** statement of Rav maintains that one should not follow her counsel **in divine matters; and that** proverb maintains that one should follow her counsel **in general matters.**

Rav continues on the subject of a man's relationship with his wife, with a statement that at first appears to cut against everything that precedes it. Rav says: *kol haholech be'atzat ishto nofel b'Gehinnom* — anyone who follows his wife's counsel will ultimately fall into *Gehinnom*, invoking the verse: *rak lo hayah ke'Achav* — do not be like Achav, who followed the counsel of his wife Jezebel.

Rav Pappa raises the tension to Abaye: but do not people say — *ittech gutza, gachin vetilchosh lah* — even if your wife is short, bend down and have her whisper in your ear? That is, seek her counsel, value her wisdom. There are indeed precedents in the Gemara for great rabbis consulting their wives on practical matters.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya ha b'meili de'alma v'ha b'meili deveisa* — there is no difficulty; Rav's caution applies to worldly affairs, while the counsel about listening to one's wife refers to domestic matters. In questions of the household, she has direct knowledge and expertise, and her counsel should be followed. In broader worldly affairs, Rav counsels independence.

A second resolution is offered: *lishna acharina, ha b'meili dishmaya v'ha b'meili de'alma* — an alternative version distinguishes between spiritual and religious matters on the one hand, and practical worldly affairs on the other. On this reading, the wife's wisdom extends to all practical matters, including those beyond the household, and Rav's restriction applies only to religious determinations — a considerably narrower limitation, and one that grants women a substantially larger sphere of recognized wisdom.

## Gates of Heaven…

### Ona'ah Gate Open

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל הַשְּׁעָרִים נִנְעָלִים, חוּץ מִשַּׁעֲרֵי אוֹנָאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה ה׳ נִצָּב עַל חוֹמַת אֲנָךְ וּבְיָדוֹ אֲנָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: All the gates** of Heaven are apt to be **locked, except for the gates** of prayer for victims **of** verbal **mistreatment, as it is stated: "And behold, the Lord stood upon a wall built with a plumb line, and a plumb line in His hand"** (Amos 7:7). God stands with the scales of justice in His hand to determine if one has been subjected to injustice.

Rav Chisda returns to the theme of the gates: *kol hashe'arim nin'alu chutz mishe'arei ona'ah* — all the gates have been locked except for the gates of *ona'ah*. The verse cited is: *hineh Hashem nitzav al chomat anach uveyado anach* — God is not behind the wall, inaccessible; He is standing atop it, present and visible, ready to respond directly to those who have been afflicted through *ona'ah*.

### God's Direct Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַכֹּל נִפְרָע בִּידֵי שָׁלִיחַ, חוּץ מֵאוֹנָאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְיָדוֹ אֲנָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** In response to **all** transgressions, God **punishes** the perpetrator **by means of an agent, except for mistreatment [*ona'a*], as it is stated: "And a plumb line [*anakh*] in His hand."** The term for mistreatment and the term for plumb line are spelled in a similar manner, indicating that God Himself inflicts retribution.

Rav Elazar sharpens this further: *hakol nifra bidei shaliach chutz me'ona'ah* — all punishment comes through an intermediary except for *ona'ah*, in which case God Himself exacts retribution directly. The same verse provides the support: *uveyado anach* — God is holding the *ona'ah* in His own hand. This is a significant theological statement: not all divine punishment involves direct divine action. Most operates through intermediaries — natural processes, human agents, historical forces. But when a person has been devastated through *ona'ah*, God personally intervenes.

### Three Direct Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שְׁלֹשָׁה אֵין הַפַּרְגּוֹד נִנְעָל בִּפְנֵיהֶם: אוֹנָאָה, וְגָזֵל, וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אוֹנָאָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְיָדוֹ אֲנָךְ״. גָּזֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״חָמָס וָשֹׁד יִשָּׁמַע בָּהּ עַל פָּנַי תָּמִיד״. עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״הָעָם הַמַּכְעִיסִים אוֹתִי עַל פָּנַי תָּמִיד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says:** There are **three** sins **before** whose transgressors **the curtain [*hapargod*]** between the world and the Divine Presence **is not locked;** their sins reach the Divine Presence. They are: Verbal **mistreatment, robbery, and idol worship. Mistreatment, as it is stated: "And a plumb line in His hand"; robbery, as it is stated: "Violence and robbery are heard in her, they are before Me continually"** (Jeremiah 6:7); **idol worship, as it is stated: "A people that angers Me before Me continually;** that sacrifice in gardens, and burn incense upon bricks" (Isaiah 65:3).

Rabbi Abbahu adds a related teaching: *gimel ein hapargod ninal bifneihem* — there are three before whom the divine curtain is not locked: those who suffer from *ona'ah*, those victimized by *gezel* — theft — and those afflicted by *avodah zarah*. These three, unlike other transgressions, are direct affronts to God Himself. The *ona'ah* verse is *uveyado anach*; the verse for *gezel* is *chamas vashod yishama bah al panay tamid* — violence and plunder are constantly before My face; and for *avodah zarah*: *ha'am hamachisim oti al panay tamid* — the people who provoke Me to My face continually.

A theological coherence underlies this grouping. *Avodah zarah* is an obvious affront to God's face — the Torah itself says *lo yihiyeh lecha elohim acherim al panai*. *Gezel* desecrates the world that belongs ultimately to God — *laHashem ha'aretz umelo'ah*. And *ona'ah*, one might suggest, attacks the *tzelem Elokim* within the person — to crush the soul of one who bears the divine image is an affront to the face of God Himself.

## Household & Wife…

### Finances & Peace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְעוֹלָם יְהֵא אָדָם זָהִיר בִּתְבוּאָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁאֵין מְרִיבָה מְצוּיָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם אֶלָּא עַל עִסְקֵי תְבוּאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַשָּׂם גְּבוּלֵךְ שָׁלוֹם חֵלֶב חִטִּים יַשְׂבִּיעֵךְ״. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: כְּמִשְׁלַם שְׂעָרֵי מִכַּדָּא, נָקֵישׁ וְאָתֵי תִּגְרָא בְּבֵיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the topic of how a man should approach his household, **Rav Yehuda says: A person must always be careful about** ensuring that there is **grain inside his house, as discord is found in a person's house only over matters of grain, as it is stated: "He makes your borders peace; He gives you plenty with the finest wheat"** (Psalms 147:14). If there is the finest wheat in your house, there will be peace there. **Rav Pappa said: This** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: When the barley is emptied from the jug, quarrel knocks and enters the house.**

Rav Yehuda teaches: *leolam yehei adam zahir bitvuah betoch beito* — a person should always be scrupulous about finances and provisions within his household, because *ein merivah metzuyah betoch beito shel adam ela al iskei tvuah* — the quarrels that arise in a home almost invariably concern matters of sustenance and financial pressure. The verse supports this: *hasam gevulech shalom chelev chitim yasbiech* — He makes your borders peaceful, and sates you with the finest grain. Peace at home is tied directly to material sufficiency.

### Barley Proverb

Rav Papa cites a common saying that captures the same idea: *kemishlam se'arei mikada, nakish va'ati tigra* — when the barley runs out of the jug, contention comes knocking at the door. This folk wisdom confirms the principle: financial strain is one of the most reliable generators of household conflict.

An additional teaching in the same vein: *shelo nikreu Yisrael dalim ela al iskei tvuah* — Israel was never described as truly impoverished except in relation to insufficient grain. The citation is the verse in which Israel is described as very poor in consequence of Midianite raids that depleted their crops.

### Wife's Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: לְעוֹלָם יְהֵא אָדָם זָהִיר בִּכְבוֹד אִשְׁתּוֹ, שֶׁאֵין בְּרָכָה מְצוּיָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל אִשְׁתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְאַבְרָם הֵטִיב בַּעֲבוּרָהּ״. וְהַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא לִבְנֵי מָחוֹזָא: אוֹקִירוּ לִנְשַׁיְיכוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִתְעַתְּרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥelbo says: A person must always be careful about** sustaining **the honor of his wife, as blessing is found in a person's house only because of his wife, as it is stated** in allusion to this: **"And he dealt well with Abram for her sake,** and he had sheep and oxen" (Genesis 12:16). **And that is what Rava said to the residents of Meḥoza,** where he lived: **Honor your wives, so that you will become rich.**

Rabbi Chelbo teaches: *leolam yehei adam zahir bichvod ishto she'ain bracha metzuyah betoch beito shel adam ela bishvil ishto* — a man should always be careful to give his wife honor, because the blessing in a man's house exists only on her account. The verse is: *ule'Avram heitiv ba'avurah* — and to Abraham it was good for her sake, with the Maharsha reading *heitiv* midrashically as God's goodness to Abraham accruing through Sarah's merit.

Rava accordingly told the residents of his city Mechuza: *okiru le'neshaichu, di heichi d'tisatru* — honor your wives, so that you will become wealthy. After the digression about when and whether to follow a wife's counsel, and after the proverbs about household finances, the Gemara returns emphatically to this note: the blessing of the household flows through her, and her honor is inseparable from its prosperity.

## Oven of Achnai

### Oven Purity Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: חֲתָכוֹ חוּלְיוֹת, וְנָתַן חוֹל בֵּין חוּלְיָא לְחוּלְיָא – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna: If **one cut** an earthenware oven widthwise **into segments, and placed sand between each and every segment, Rabbi Eliezer deems it ritually pure.** Because of the sand, its legal status is not that of a complete vessel, and therefore it is not susceptible to ritual impurity. **And the Rabbis deem it ritually impure,** as it is functionally a complete oven.

At this point the Gemara introduces one of its most celebrated narratives, cited at this juncture because its conclusion involves *ona'ah* and the power of its consequences. The Gemara opens: *chatcho chulyot v'natan chol bein chulya l'chulya* — if an oven made of pottery sections was broken into segments with sand placed between them, what is its status with respect to *tumah*? A broken pottery vessel no longer has the capacity to become *tamei*. But here, with sand separating the pieces rather than plaster, the question is whether this reconstructed arrangement is considered a complete vessel again. Rabbi Eliezer held it to be *tahor*; the *Chachamim* held it to be *tamei*.

### Achnai Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה הוּא תַּנּוּר שֶׁל עַכְנַאי. מַאי עַכְנַאי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שֶׁהִקִּיפוּ דְּבָרִים כְּעַכְנָא זוֹ, וְטִמְּאוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** known as **the oven of *akhnai*.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the relevance of ***akhnai*,** a snake, in this context? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** It is characterized in that manner due to the fact **that** the Rabbis **surrounded** it **with** their **statements like this snake,** which often forms a coil when at rest, **and deemed it impure.**

This is the famous *tannur shel Achnai* — the oven of Achnai. Tosafot suggests that *Achnai* may be the name of a place, but the Gemara prefers a midrashic reading. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *shehikifu devarim k'achna zu* — they surrounded Rabbi Eliezer with arguments like a snake, circling him, and declared the oven *tamei*. The image of a snake encircling its prey captures the relentless argumentative pressure brought against him.

### Miracles Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם הֵשִׁיב רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כׇּל תְּשׁוּבוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם, וְלֹא קִיבְּלוּ הֵימֶנּוּ. אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתִי – חָרוּב זֶה יוֹכִיחַ. נֶעֱקַר חָרוּב מִמְּקוֹמוֹ מֵאָה אַמָּה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת אַמָּה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הֶחָרוּב. חָזַר וְאָמַר לָהֶם: אִם הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתִי – אַמַּת הַמַּיִם יוֹכִיחוּ. חָזְרוּ אַמַּת הַמַּיִם לַאֲחוֹרֵיהֶם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מֵאַמַּת הַמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught: On that day,** when they discussed this matter, **Rabbi Eliezer answered all** possible **answers in the world** to support his opinion, **but** the Rabbis **did not accept** his explanations **from him.** After failing to convince the Rabbis logically, Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: If** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with my** opinion, **this carob** tree **will prove** it. The **carob** tree **was uprooted from its place one hundred cubits, and some say four hundred cubits.** The Rabbis **said to him: One does not cite** halakhic **proof from the carob** tree. Rabbi Eliezer **then said to them: If** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with my** opinion, **the stream will prove** it. The water in **the stream turned backward** and began flowing in the opposite direction. **They said to him: One does not cite** halakhic **proof from a stream.**

The Gemara relates what occurred on that day. Rabbi Eliezer brought all the arguments in the world and was not accepted. Frustrated by the refusal of the *Chachamim* to yield to his reasoning, he resorted to miraculous appeals. He said: if the *halacha* is according to me, let this carob tree prove it — and the tree uprooted itself one hundred *amot* from its place, some say four hundred *amot*. The Rabbis responded: *ein mevi'in re'aya min hacharuv* — one does not bring proof from a carob tree. That is a nice miracle, but it does not constitute a logical argument in *halacha*. If you cannot persuade us through reasoning, a supernatural feat proves nothing.

He then said: if the *halacha* is according to me, let this stream of water prove it — and the stream began flowing backwards. Again: *ein mevi'in re'aya me'amat hamayim* — you do not bring proof from a water channel. The Maharsha suggests that the reversal of the stream was symbolically significant: the Gemara compares Torah to water, which always finds the low ground, a metaphor for humility. Rabbi Eliezer may have been suggesting that the *Chachamim*'s arrogance was preventing them from genuinely hearing his arguments. Regardless of the symbolic intent, the response was the same — this is not how *halacha* is determined.

He then said: if the *halacha* is according to me, let the walls of the *beit midrash* prove it — and the walls began to tilt and lean, threatening to fall. Rabbi Yehoshua rebuked the walls: *im talmidei chachamim menatzchim zeh et zeh b'halacha, atem mah tivachem* — if Torah scholars are contending against one another in *halacha*, what business is that of yours, O walls? The walls did not fall out of respect for Rabbi Yehoshua, but they did not return to upright out of respect for Rabbi Eliezer — and they remain inclined to this day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזַר וְאָמַר לָהֶם: אִם הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתִי – כּוֹתְלֵי בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ יוֹכִיחוּ. הִטּוּ כּוֹתְלֵי בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ לִיפּוֹל. גָּעַר בָּהֶם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מְנַצְּחִים זֶה אֶת זֶה בַּהֲלָכָה, אַתֶּם מָה טִיבְכֶם? לֹא נָפְלוּ מִפְּנֵי כְבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְלֹא זָקְפוּ מִפְּנֵי כְבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וַעֲדַיִן מַטִּין וְעוֹמְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **then said to them: If** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with my** opinion, **the walls of the study hall will prove** it. **The walls of the study hall leaned** inward and began **to fall. Rabbi Yehoshua scolded** the walls and **said to them: If Torah scholars are contending** with **each other in** matters of ***halakha*, what** is the **nature** of **your** involvement in this dispute? The Gemara relates: The walls **did not fall because of** the **deference** due **Rabbi Yehoshua, but they did not straighten because of** the **deference** due **Rabbi Eliezer, and they still remain leaning.**

There is something worth observing here about the character of the dispute. Rabbi Eliezer, finding that logical argument had failed, appealed to supernatural signs. The Gemara's framing suggests he was trying to communicate that the resistance to his arguments was itself unnatural — against the grain of the world. The *Chachamim*, for their part, consistently refused to allow miraculous demonstrations to substitute for the internal reasoning of *halacha*.

### Lo Bashamayim Hi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזַר וְאָמַר לָהֶם: אִם הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתִי – מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם יוֹכִיחוּ. יָצָאתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: מָה לָכֶם אֵצֶל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, שֶׁהֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. עָמַד רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: ״לֹא בַשָּׁמַיִם הִיא!״ מַאי ״לֹא בַּשָּׁמַיִם הִיא״? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְּנָה תּוֹרָה מֵהַר סִינַי, אֵין אָנוּ מַשְׁגִּיחִין בְּבַת קוֹל, שֶׁכְּבָר כָּתַבְתָּ בְּהַר סִינַי בַּתּוֹרָה ״אַחֲרֵי רַבִּים לְהַטֹּת״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **then said to them: If** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with my** opinion, **Heaven will prove** it. **A Divine Voice emerged** from Heaven **and said: Why are you** differing **with Rabbi Eliezer, as** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** his opinion **in every place** that he expresses an opinion? **Rabbi Yehoshua stood on his feet and said:** It is written: **"It is not in heaven"** (Deuteronomy 30:12). The Gemara asks: **What** is the relevance of the phrase **"It is not in heaven"** in this context? **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** Since **the Torah was already given at Mount Sinai, we do not regard a Divine Voice, as You already wrote at Mount Sinai, in the Torah: "After a majority to incline"** (Exodus 23:2).

Rabbi Eliezer then appealed directly to heaven: *im halacha kemoti min hashamayim yochi'chu* — let heaven prove that the *halacha* is according to me. A *bat kol* came forth and declared: *mah lachem eitzel Rabbi Eliezer shehalacha kemoto bechol makom* — why do you argue with Rabbi Eliezer? The *halacha* is always like him.

Rabbi Yehoshua rose to his feet and declared: *lo bashamayim hi* — it is not in heaven. This is the heart of the passage, and among the most audacious statements in all of rabbinic literature. Even a heavenly voice does not determine *halacha*. God gave the Torah at Sinai; the ongoing interpretation and application of that Torah is the province of human beings. Divine revelation has completed its role; human reasoning now governs.

The Gemara explicates: Rabbi Yirmiyah explains — *kvar nitna Torah me-Har Sinai, ein anu mashgichin b'vat kol* — the Torah was already given at Sinai; we do not heed a heavenly voice. The proof text is embedded in the Torah itself: *acharei rabbim le'hatos* — the rule that one follows the majority. God wrote into the Torah the very mechanism that now excludes His own direct intervention. The ruling of the *Chachamim*, arrived at through majority determination, is authoritative precisely because that is what the Torah requires.

### Elijah's Laughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַבִּי נָתָן לְאֵלִיָּהוּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי עָבֵיד קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא בְּהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קָא חָיֵיךְ וְאָמַר, ״נִצְּחוּנִי בָּנַי! נִצְּחוּנִי בָּנַי!״
>
> **Translation:** Years after, **Rabbi Natan encountered Elijah** the prophet and **said to him: What did the Holy One, Blessed be He, do at that time,** when Rabbi Yehoshua issued his declaration? Elijah **said to him:** The Holy One, Blessed be He, **smiled and said: My children have triumphed over Me; My children have triumphed over Me.**

The Gemara then inserts a passage in Aramaic — marking it as a later addition that confirms and closes the story. Rabbi Natan encountered Elijah the Prophet and asked: *mai avid Kudsha Brich Hu b'hahi sha'ata* — what was God's response at that moment? Elijah answered: *kachayich* — He was laughing, *v'amar: nitzchuni vanai, nitzchuni vanai* — and He said: my children have bested Me, my children have bested Me. The word *nitzchuni* echoes the earlier framing of the dispute: *talmidei chachamim menatzchim zeh et zeh b'halacha* — the scholars competing for victory. But now they have prevailed even over God, and God accepts and even delights in it.

This passage reassures the reader: if there was any doubt whether the ruling of the *Chachamim* was genuinely correct, God Himself confirms it. He wanted it to be resolved by humans, and He accepts that outcome even where it contradicts what a heavenly voice had said.

### Burning & Cherem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם הֵבִיאוּ כׇּל טְהָרוֹת שֶׁטִּיהֵר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וּשְׂרָפוּם בָּאֵשׁ. וְנִמְנוּ עָלָיו וּבֵרְכוּהוּ, וְאָמְרוּ: מִי יֵלֵךְ וְיוֹדִיעוֹ? אֲמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲנִי אֵלֵךְ, שֶׁמָּא יֵלֵךְ אָדָם שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן וְיוֹדִיעוֹ, וְנִמְצָא מַחְרִיב אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said:** On **that day,** the Sages **brought all the ritually pure** items **deemed pure by** the ruling of **Rabbi Eliezer** with regard to the oven **and burned them in fire, and** the Sages **reached a consensus in his regard and ostracized him. And** the Sages **said: Who will go and inform him** of his ostracism? **Rabbi Akiva,** his beloved disciple, **said to them: I will go, lest an unseemly person go** and inform him in a callous and offensive manner, **and he would thereby destroy the entire world.**

The Gemara then describes what followed. That same day, the Rabbis gathered all the foods that Rabbi Eliezer had declared *tahor* in connection with this oven and burned them in fire. This was not merely a quiet halachic correction. It was a public and emphatic statement — they made unmistakably clear where the authority in *halacha* rested. Furthermore, they voted and placed Rabbi Eliezer in a form of *cherem* — excommunication — which echoes the laws of *zaken mamre*, of a scholar who refuses to accept the ruling of the highest court. The authority of the *beit din* requires that those who disagree submit to its procedures. Rabbi Eliezer's refusal to do so — his insistence that his ruling was correct regardless of the procedural outcome — was seen as potentially destabilizing to the entire system of halachic authority, and the *Chachamim* felt they had no alternative.

## Eliezer's Suffering

### Akiva's Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? לָבַשׁ שְׁחוֹרִים וְנִתְעַטֵּף שְׁחוֹרִים, וְיָשַׁב לְפָנָיו בְּרִיחוּק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: עֲקִיבָא, מָה יוֹם מִיָּמִים? אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, כִּמְדוּמֶּה לִי שֶׁחֲבֵירִים בְּדֵילִים מִמֶּךָ. אַף הוּא קָרַע בְּגָדָיו וְחָלַץ מִנְעָלָיו, וְנִשְׁמַט וְיָשַׁב עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **What did Rabbi Akiva do? He wore black and wrapped himself in black,** as an expression of mourning and pain, **and sat before** Rabbi Eliezer **at a distance of four cubits,** which is the distance that one must maintain from an ostracized individual. **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: Akiva, what** is different about **today from other days,** that you comport yourself in this manner? Rabbi Akiva **said to him: My teacher, it appears to me that** your **colleagues are distancing** themselves **from you.** He employed euphemism, as actually they distanced Rabbi Eliezer from them. Rabbi Eliezer **too, rent his garments and removed his shoes,** as is the custom of an ostracized person, **and he dropped** from his seat **and sat upon the ground.**

The question of who would go and inform Rabbi Eliezer that he had been placed in *cherem* was itself a weighty one. He had to know, because the *cherem* required him to maintain four *amot* of distance from others. But informing him carelessly could trigger catastrophe — *nimtza machriv et kol ha'olam kulo* — as we have seen from his powers. Rabbi Akiva volunteered: *ani eilech* — I will go. His concern was that if an inappropriate person went, Rabbi Eliezer would feel dishonored by the messenger, and the resulting pain could be devastating.

Rabbi Akiva dressed himself entirely in black and sat four *amot* away from Rabbi Eliezer — in an openly visible act of mourning. He communicated the news through implication: *kemidumeh li shechaverim bdilim mimcha* — it appears to me that your colleagues are keeping their distance from you. He never used the word *cherem*. Rabbi Eliezer understood, and himself performed *keriah*, removed his shoes, and sat on the ground in the manner of one observing mourning.

### Tears' Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָלְגוּ עֵינָיו דְּמָעוֹת, לָקָה הָעוֹלָם: שְׁלִישׁ בְּזֵיתִים, וּשְׁלִישׁ בְּחִטִּים, וּשְׁלִישׁ בִּשְׂעוֹרִים. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף בָּצֵק שֶׁבִּידֵי אִשָּׁה טָפַח. תָּנָא: אַף גָּדוֹל הָיָה בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, שֶׁבְּכָל מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּתַן בּוֹ עֵינָיו רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – נִשְׂרַף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **His eyes shed tears,** and as a result **the** entire **world was afflicted: One-third of** its **olives** were afflicted, **and one-third of** its **wheat, and one-third of** its **barley. And some say** that **even dough** kneaded **in a woman's hands spoiled.** The Sages **taught: There was great anger on that day, as any place that Rabbi Eliezer fixed his gaze was burned.**

Then: *vazalgu einav dema'ot* — his eyes flowed with tears. And the consequences were immediate and cosmic. One third of the olives, one third of the wheat, and one third of the barley in the world were destroyed. Some versions add: even dough in a woman's hands would spoil. Rabbi Eliezer's gaze — whether in pain, grief, or anger — burned whatever it fell upon. *Af gadol hayah ba'oto hayom* — there was great wrath on that day.

The connection to the earlier teachings about the gates of tears is now explicit. The Gemara has just taught that *sha'arei dema'ot lo nin'alu* — the gates of tears are never sealed — and that *sha'arei ona'ah* are never locked. Here, the tears of a *talmid chacham* who has been devastated by the actions of the rabbinic court — however justified those actions may have been — produce visible, tangible destruction in the world.

### Gamliel's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָיָה בָּא בִּסְפִינָה. עָמַד עָלָיו נַחְשׁוֹל לְטַבְּעוֹ. אָמַר: כִּמְדוּמֶּה לִי שֶׁאֵין זֶה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן הוּרְקָנוֹס. עָמַד עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ שֶׁלֹּא לִכְבוֹדִי עָשִׂיתִי, וְלֹא לִכְבוֹד בֵּית אַבָּא עָשִׂיתִי, אֶלָּא לִכְבוֹדְךָ, שֶׁלֹּא יִרְבּוּ מַחְלוֹקוֹת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. נָח הַיָּם מִזַּעְפּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And even Rabban Gamliel,** the *Nasi* of the Sanhedrin at Yavne, the head of the Sages who were responsible for the decision to ostracize Rabbi Eliezer, **was coming on a boat** at the time, and **a large wave swelled over him** and threatened **to drown him.** Rabban Gamliel **said: It seems to me that this is only for the sake of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus,** as God punishes those who mistreat others. Rabban Gamliel **stood on his feet and said: Master of the Universe, it is revealed and known before You that neither was it for my honor** that **I acted** when ostracizing him, **nor was it for the honor of the house of my father** that **I acted; rather,** it was **for Your honor, so that disputes will not proliferate in Israel.** In response, **the sea calmed from its raging.**

Rabban Gamliel, as *nasi*, had authorized the *cherem*. At that moment he was at sea. A great wave arose, threatening to swallow the ship. He recognized the cause: *kimdumeh li she'ein zeh ela bishvil Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrkanos* — this can be for no other reason than what we have done to Rabbi Eliezer. He stood and addressed God: *galui v'yadua lefanecha she'lo lichvodi asiti v'lo lichvod beit abba asiti* — it is known to You that I did not act for my own honor or for my family's honor, *ela lichvodcha she'lo yirbu machloket b'Yisrael* — but for Your honor, that disagreement not proliferate in Israel. The sea calmed.

This is a nuanced moment. Rabban Gamliel does not recant. He does not say the *cherem* was wrong. He affirms that it was done for the right reasons — for the integrity of halachic authority and the prevention of destructive *machloket* in Israel. And yet the wave arose. The pain of Rabbi Eliezer was real and had consequences even when the decision causing that pain was principled and necessary.

### Ima Shalom Guards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִימָּא שָׁלוֹם, דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הֲוַאי. מֵהָהוּא מַעֲשֶׂה וְאֵילָךְ, לָא הֲוָה שָׁבְקָה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמִיפַּל עַל אַפֵּיהּ. הָהוּא יוֹמָא רֵישׁ יַרְחָא הֲוָה, וְאִיחַלַּף לַהּ בֵּין מָלֵא לְחָסֵר. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲתָא עַנְיָא וְקָאֵי אַבָּבָא, אַפִּיקָא לֵיהּ רִיפְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates: **Imma Shalom, the wife of Rabbi Eliezer, was the sister of Rabban Gamliel. From that incident forward, she would not allow Rabbi Eliezer to lower his head** and recite the *taḥanun* prayer, which includes supplication and entreaties. She feared that were her husband to bemoan his fate and pray at that moment, her brother would be punished. **A certain day was** around the day of **the New Moon, and she** inadvertently **substituted a full** thirty-day month **for a deficient** twenty-nine-day month, i.e., she thought that it was the New Moon, when one does not lower his head in supplication, but it was not. **Some say** that **a pauper came and stood at the door,** and **she took bread out to him.** The result was that she left her husband momentarily unsupervised.

Ima Shalom, the wife of Rabbi Eliezer and the sister of Rabban Gamliel, understood this completely. From the day of the *cherem* onward, she never permitted her husband to perform *nefilat appayim* — the *Tachanun* prayer — because she feared that if he allowed his grief and sense of affliction to pour out in prayer, the consequences would be catastrophic. *Tachanun* begins *tzar li me'od niplah na b'yad Hashem* — I am in great distress, let me fall into God's hand — language of acute suffering, precisely the kind of prayer that, coming from Rabbi Eliezer, could call down destruction.

One day she became confused about whether the preceding month had been a full month of thirty days or a deficient month of twenty-nine, and therefore miscalculated whether it was *Rosh Chodesh* — on which *Tachanun* is not recited. Thinking it was *Rosh Chodesh*, she did not stand guard. In another version, a poor man came to the door seeking *tzedakah*, and while she stepped away to bring him a loaf of bread, Rabbi Eliezer managed to perform *nefilat appayim*.

### Gamliel's Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ דְּנָפֵל עַל אַנְפֵּיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: קוּם, קְטַלְיתֵּהּ לְאָח. אַדְּהָכִי נְפַק שִׁיפּוּרָא מִבֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דִּשְׁכֵיב. אֲמַר לַהּ: מְנָא יְדַעְתְּ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִבֵּית אֲבִי אַבָּא: כׇּל הַשְּׁעָרִים נִנְעָלִים חוּץ מִשַּׁעֲרֵי אוֹנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** When she returned, **she found him** and saw **that he had lowered his head** in prayer. **She said to him: Arise, you** already **killed my brother. Meanwhile,** the sound of **a *shofar* emerged from the house of Rabban Gamliel** to announce **that** the *Nasi* **had died.** Rabbi Eliezer **said to her: From where did you know** that your brother would die? **She said to him: This is** the tradition that **I received from the house of the father of** my **father: All the gates** of Heaven are apt to be **locked, except for the gates** of prayer for victims **of** verbal **mistreatment.**

When she returned, she found him already prostrate in prayer. She cried out: *kataltia l'achi* — you have killed my brother. And indeed, shortly afterward came the announcement — the *shofar* was sounded from the house of Rabban Gamliel, signaling his death. Rabbi Eliezer asked her: *mina yadat* — how did you know that would happen? She replied: *kach mekublani mibeit avi abba* — this is the tradition I received from my father's household: *kol hashe'arim nin'alu chutz mishe'arei ona'ah* — all the gates are locked except for the gate of *ona'ah*.

This is the conclusion toward which the entire narrative of the *tannur shel Achnai* was driving. The story of the oven, the miraculous signs, *lo bashamayim hi*, the *cherem*, Rabbi Akiva's black garments, the burning crops, the storm at sea — all of it was a vehicle to demonstrate the power of *ona'ah*. The pain of Rabbi Eliezer, however it was caused — whether through words, actions, burning his *taharot*, or formal excommunication — passed through ungated access directly to God, and its consequences could not be contained.

It is worth pausing over what this story is and is not saying. The *Chachamim* made a principled decision. Rabban Gamliel acted for the right reasons. And yet the story does not redeem those actions from their consequences. Sometimes a necessary and correct decision still causes genuine harm to a real person, and that harm does not cease to be harm because the decision was justified. The story insists that we hold both truths simultaneously — the rightness of the ruling and the reality of the suffering it caused. The victory cry of *nitzchuni vanai* does not cancel out *vazalgu einav dema'ot*.

## Ona'ah & Converts

### Convert's Protections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְאַנֶּה אֶת הַגֵּר, עוֹבֵר בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה לָאוִין. וְהַלּוֹחֲצוֹ, עוֹבֵר בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה. מַאי שְׁנָא מְאַנֶּה, דִּכְתִיבִי שְׁלֹשָׁה לָאוִין: ״וְגֵר לֹא תוֹנֶה״, ״וְכִי יָגוּר אִתְּךָ גֵּר בְּאַרְצְכֶם לֹא תוֹנוּ אֹתוֹ״, ״וְלֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ״, וְגֵר בִּכְלַל עֲמִיתוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who** verbally **mistreats the convert violates three prohibitions, and one who oppresses him** in other ways **violates** three [correcting a previous reading of two]. **The three** *lavin* for verbal affliction are: **"And you shall neither mistreat a convert"** (Exodus 22:20); **"And when a convert lives in your land, you shall not mistreat him"** (Leviticus 19:33); **"And you shall not mistreat, each man his colleague"** (Leviticus 25:17), **and a convert is** included in the category of **colleague.**

The Gemara closes the tractate's treatment of *ona'at devarim* with a focus on the *ger* — the convert. A *baraisa* teaches: *hama'aneh et hager over bishelosha lavin* — one who verbally afflicts a convert transgresses three negative prohibitions. One who monetarily oppresses a convert (*lochatzo*) also transgresses three, correcting an initial version of the text that had read only two.

The three *lavin* for verbal affliction are: *ger lo toneh* (Shemot 22:20), *lo tonu oto* from the verse *ki yagur itcha ger b'artzechem* (Vayikra 19:33), and the general *velo tonu ish et amito* — with the Gemara confirming that a *ger* is included in the category of *amito*, one's neighbor. The three for monetary oppression are *velo tilchatzennu* (Shemot 22:20), *v'ger lo tilchatz* (Shemot 23:9), and *velo tihyeh lo k'nosheh* — do not be upon him as a creditor, which applies to the monetary dimension of affliction.

### Repetition of Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי מָה הִזְהִירָה תּוֹרָה בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה מְקוֹמוֹת, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: בְּאַרְבָּעִים וְשִׁשָּׁה מְקוֹמוֹת, בְּגֵר? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסּוֹרוֹ רַע.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says: For what reason did the Torah issue warnings in thirty-six places, and some say in forty-six places, with regard to** causing any distress to **a convert?** It is **due to** the fact **that** a convert's **inclination is evil,** i.e., he is prone to return to his previous way of living.

Rabbi Eliezer Hagadol asks: why did the Torah repeat the prohibition regarding the *ger* in thirty-six places — some say forty-six, depending on how one counts? His answer is: *mipnei shesoro ra* — because his turning is bad, meaning that a person who has come from outside and joined the community is vulnerable to being made to feel marginal, and there is a real risk that harsh treatment will drive him away. The repeated emphasis serves as a constant reminder that the Torah takes the *ger's* position seriously precisely because of that vulnerability.

### Personal Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְגֵר לֹא תוֹנֶה וְלֹא תִלְחָצֶנּוּ כִּי גֵרִים הֱיִיתֶם בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם״? תְּנֵינָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מוּם שֶׁבְּךָ אַל תֹּאמַר לַחֲבֵרֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And you shall not mistreat a convert nor oppress him, because you were strangers in the land of Egypt"** (Exodus 22:20)? **We learned** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: A defect that is in you, do not mention** it **in another.**

Rabbi Natan teaches: *mum she'bach al tomar l'chavercha* — a blemish that is in you, do not point out in your friend. This is a general principle about self-awareness before criticism.

### Glass Houses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: דִּזְקִיף לֵיהּ זְקִיפָא בִּדְיוּתְקֵיהּ, לָא נֵימָא לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ זְקֵיף בִּינִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** explains the adage **that people say: One who has** a person **hanged in his family [*bidyotkei*], does not say to another** member of his household: **Hang a fish for me,** as the mention of hanging is demeaning for that family.

The Gemara illustrates with a folk saying: *d'zakif lei zakifa b'dikutei, lo neima lei l'chavrei zakif binassei* — one whose family has had someone hanged should not say to his friend: would you hang up these fish for me? The word *zakif* — to hang — will inevitably call to mind the family disgrace, and what was meant as an innocent request will instead draw attention to a shameful history.

The application to the *ger* is instructive. The verse *ki gerim hayitem b'eretz mitzrayim* — ideally — calls on us to remember our own history of marginalization and to respond with empathy and solidarity. But for those who do not hear that call, Rabbi Natan's principle offers an alternative approach: before you point out that the *ger* comes from a different background, remember your own. The blemish you are about to expose in another may be found in yourself. Sometimes the road to ethical behavior runs through self-interest as much as through moral vision, and the Torah, in its wisdom, provides both paths.
