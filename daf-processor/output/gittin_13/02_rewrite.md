# Gittin 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Freedom as Benefit

### Shtar Shichrur & Get

The daf opens in the middle of a debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* concerning a case where someone instructs another to deliver either a *shtar shichrur* to his slave or a *get* to his wife. The question is whether the recipient of those instructions can be considered a *shaliach* acting on behalf of the slave or wife, such that the divorce or manumission takes effect automatically upon that person's acceptance of the document.

In the case of the wife, the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* — whereby one may act on another's behalf to confer a benefit, even without the other's knowledge — cannot apply, because there is a potential downside to being divorced: the husband is no longer legally obligated to provide for her. The case of the *eved* is more complex. The *chachamim* hold that freedom is an unambiguous benefit to the slave, and therefore *zachin* does apply. Rabbi Meir disagrees, arguing that there are real losses attendant upon manumission.

### Chachamim Position

The *chachamim* reason straightforwardly: to be freed from beneath one's master's hand and enter into liberty is an absolute good. Since it is purely beneficial, someone may represent the slave and act on his behalf to receive the *shtar shichrur*, and the slave is thereby automatically freed.

### Rabbi Meir: Terumah Loss

Rabbi Meir objects. The *baraita* records the exchange directly: the *chachamim* said to him, "Is it not a pure benefit to the slave to go out from under his master's hand to freedom?" Rabbi Meir replied, "*chov hu lo* — it is a detriment to him." His first contention was that the master might lose the practical incentive to feed the slave abundantly, since a master who owns a *kohen*'s slave can supply him with the inexpensive *terumah*, and freeing the slave removes that access. But more fundamentally, Rabbi Meir argues: *she-im haya eved kohen pasul min hatruma* — if he was the slave of a *kohen*, manumission causes him to lose the right to eat *terumah*. The wife of a *kohen* and the slave of a *kohen* both eat *terumah* — the wife because of the marital bond, the slave because he is the *kohen*'s property. Upon manumission, that privilege disappears. Rabbi Meir therefore holds that at minimum in the case of a *kohen*'s slave, there is a genuine loss, which disqualifies the application of *zachin*.

### No Feeding Obligation

The *chachamim* respond to the argument about the master's feeding obligation: *v'lo mai mafsed she-eino chayav le-zono u-le-farneso rashai* — what loss is there if the master has no legal obligation to feed the slave in the first place? If the master was never required to provide food, the slave loses nothing in that regard by being freed.

*Tosafot* explains that the *chachamim* understood Rabbi Meir's *terumah* argument as being about the cost of food — the *kohen* master could cheaply supply *terumah* food, making the slave's material situation better — and they responded that since there is no legal feeding obligation, the cheapness of *terumah* is irrelevant.

### Terumah Independent

Rabbi Meir, however, is not satisfied. He replies that the *terumah* point is entirely independent of any feeding obligation. The *baraita* states: *u-mah ilu eved kohen she-barach v'eshet kohen she-marda al ba'alah halo ochlin b'terumah* — even a slave of a *kohen* who has run away from his master, or a wife of a *kohen* who has rebelled against her husband, can still eat *terumah*. In other words, the access to *terumah* does not depend on the master or husband actually providing it — the slave can go independently to the threshing floors, to the grain stores, and acquire *terumah* on his own. This benefit belongs to him by virtue of his status as the *kohen*'s property, and it is entirely separate from the master's feeding obligation. Once he is freed — *v'zeh eino ochel* — he loses this right entirely.

The *baraita* makes the contrast sharp: *aval isha chov hu lah she-ken posla min hatruma u-mefasda min hamzonot* — by a woman, both losses are present upon divorce: she loses *terumah* rights and also the husband's legal obligation to feed her. For the slave, even though the feeding obligation doesn't apply, the *terumah* right is real and its loss is real.

The *gemara* then pauses to reconstruct the exact flow of argument: the *chachamim* had responded to the feeding point, and Rabbi Meir acknowledged it but pressed them on the *terumah* point which remained unaddressed. One might ask: could not the master simply throw the *get* within the slave's *arba amot* and free him against his will, rendering the question of representation moot? Rabbi Meir anticipates this: *shavik lei v'arak v'azil l'alma* — the slave can run away, and without the slave or his representative present, the master cannot remove the slave's *terumah* rights unilaterally. It is precisely because a *shaliach* is stepping in to represent the slave that the master gains the power to sever the slave's connection to *terumah*. Therefore, the act of representation is itself the vehicle of the slave's loss, and *zachin* cannot apply.

### Master Can Sell

The *gemara* asks: so the *chachamim*'s response was a good one — but then what is their rejoinder to Rabbi Meir's *terumah* argument? Rava explains: *hainu d'ka mahadrei lei b'matnisin mipnei she-hu kinyano* — the answer lies in the *mishnah*'s phrase "because he is his possession." Since the slave is the master's property, and since the reason the slave eats *terumah* in the first place is that he is the *kohen*'s possession, the master can eliminate this benefit at any time without the slave's involvement: *d'i ba'i shakil arba zuzei mi'Yisrael u'pasleh leh le-chol heicha d'iteih* — if the master wishes, he simply sells the slave to a non-*kohen* for a nominal sum, and wherever the slave is — even if he has fled to the ends of the earth — he is immediately barred from *terumah*. Since this benefit was always revocable by the master alone, the fact that a *shaliach* represents the slave and facilitates manumission does not constitute the source of any new loss. The *terumah* right was never secure to begin with.

### Sexual Freedom Loss

This disposes of Rabbi Meir's objection in the case of a *kohen*'s slave — but what about the slave of a *Yisrael*, an ordinary Israelite? There is no *terumah* to lose there. Where is the downside for such a slave? The *gemara* cites Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak: *mipnei she-mafsido mi-shifcha kena'anit* — because manumission deprives him of the ability to cohabit with a *shifcha kena'anit*, a non-Jewish female slave.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *adaraba*, on the contrary, he is now permitted to marry a Jewish woman! Why should the loss of access to a *shifcha* be considered a detriment when he gains access to a Jewish wife?

### Slave Preference

The *gemara*'s answer is striking: *avda b'hefkera nicha lei* — a slave prefers a life of *hefkera*, of unrestrained, unbounded existence. Upon manumission, the slave enters into the obligations and social constraints of Jewish law, including the norms governing marriage and sexual conduct. The passage elaborates, in Rashi's formulation: *zola hi b'einav u-mezalzel ba l-chol ta'avato* — the female slave is cheap in his eyes, he treats her with contempt and exploits her however he wishes. She is *shchicha lei*, perpetually available to him, and *pritza lei*, she too has no sexual restraints. The *gemara* describes this life of unchecked access as something the slave, reprehensibly, prefers to the constrained dignity of freedom. This entire picture is presented, of course, through the lens of the Talmudic sages imagining the slave's psychology — a perspective with its own troubling dimensions worth noting — but the legal point is clear: since there are scenarios in which a slave might genuinely not benefit from freedom, Rabbi Meir holds that one cannot automatically assume *zachin* applies.

## Mishnah: After Death

### Mishnah Cases

The *mishnah* before us presents a new set of cases: *ha-omer tenu get zeh l'ishti u-shtar shichrur zeh l'avdi u-meis lo yitnu l'achar misa* — if a man says "give this *get* to my wife" or "this *shtar shichrur* to my slave," and he then dies, the document may not be given after death. However, if a man says "give a hundred dollars to so-and-so" and then dies — *yitnu l'achar misa* — it is given.

There is a textual question about whether the word *zeh* ("this") appears in the first clause, suggesting a document already prepared and being handed over. Some read the *mishnah* without *zeh*, understanding the case as an instruction to write and then deliver — in which case the slave transfers to the heirs and the woman becomes an *almanah* upon the husband's death, and neither transaction can be completed. Either way, the *mishnah*'s logic is that the *get* and *shtar shichrur* cannot take effect after death. The puzzle is why the money *does* transfer.

### Rav Yitzchak: Conditions

Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta, citing Rav, states: *v'hu she-tzvurim u-munachim b'keren zavis* — the rule that the money transfers applies only when the money is piled together and set aside in a specific location, like a designated stack in the corner of a room. This condition of *tzburim u-munachim* — specific, concretely identifiable funds — is required for the transfer to take effect.

### Healthy vs. Deathbed

One might ask: under what circumstances does this case arise, and why does the specificity of *tzburim u-munachim* matter? The *gemara* poses the question directly: *b'mai askinan* — what are the circumstances? If we are dealing with a *bari*, a healthy person, then the mere fact that the money is piled in a corner does not help — *hala meshach*, the recipient never took possession. If no acquisition was made and the man later dies, his estate passes to the heirs; why should his instructions transfer the money?

We must therefore be discussing a *shchiv mei'ra*, a person on his deathbed. The *halacha* is that the words of a *shchiv mei'ra* take automatic legal effect — *divrei shchiv mei'ra k-chsuvim u-chmssurim damu*, the words of a dying person are regarded as if already written and delivered — because we are concerned that frustrating his final wishes would agitate him and hasten his death. But if that is the case, why does the money need to be in a pile at all? Even a general instruction to give a hundred dollars from his estate should suffice.

### Shchiv Mei'ra Effect

The *gemara* also considers why the *mishnah* does not explain the *get* and *shtar shichrur* as simply not transferring — rather than having actually taken effect — by resort to *mitzvah l'kayeim divrei ha-meis*, the obligation to fulfill the wishes of the deceased. *Tosafot* and Rashi discuss this; while the concept exists and is debated, the *gemara*'s assumption is that in the case of money, an actual transfer has occurred, not merely a posthumous wish to be honored. The question therefore remains: how and why does the money transfer, and why the requirement of *tzburim u-munachim*?

Rav Ashi answers: *le-olam bari u-kh'Rav Huna* — we can in fact be discussing a healthy person, consistent with Rav Huna's ruling. Rav Huna, citing Rav, had stated: *maneh li b'yadcha tenehu l'ploni b'maamad shloshtan kanah* — if Reuven says to Shimon "you owe me a hundred dollars; instead of giving it to me, give it to Levi," and all three parties are present at the time of this declaration, the transfer takes effect immediately. Shimon now owes Levi. This is the mechanism of *maamad shloshtan*.

On this reading, the *mishnah* is describing exactly this scenario: the money is piled in Shimon's possession, the man instructs Shimon to give it to a third party, all three are present, and the transfer takes effect by *maamad shloshtan*. This explains the requirement of *tzburim u-munachim* — it must be a concrete, specific object, because according to Rav, *maamad shloshtan* works only for a *pikadon*, something tangible, not for an abstract debt. Furthermore, as *Tosafot* notes, the advantage of *maamad shloshtan* over simple *zachin* is that *maamad shloshtan* can effect the transfer even if Shimon objects and does not wish to comply.

This also explains why the *get* and *shtar shichrur* cannot transfer: *maamad shloshtan* applies to money and property, not to documents whose legal effect depends on personal relationship.

### Rav Pappa Alternative

Rav Pappa offers an alternative reading: *le-olam b'shchiv mei'ra u-kh'idach d'Rav* — we are indeed discussing a *shchiv mei'ra*, and the relevant ruling of Rav is a different one: *shchiv mei'ra she-amar tenu maneh l'ploni mi-nechasai*, if a dying person says "give so-and-so a hundred dollars from my estate" — *maneh zeh nosnim*, if he specified which hundred dollars, it is given; *maneh stam ein nosnim*, if he said "a hundred dollars" without specification, it is not given. Why? *Chashinan shema maneh kavur ka-amar* — we are concerned that perhaps he meant a buried cache of money that no one can locate, and there is no identifiable sum to transfer.

On this reading, *tzburim u-munachim* means not the *pikadon* aspect for *maamad shloshtan*, but the specificity required so that the *shchiv mei'ra*'s instruction can be carried out without ambiguity. The *gemara* concludes: *v'hilchasa l'kavor lo chaishinan* — as a practical matter of *halacha*, we do not maintain the concern about buried money.

### Buried Money Issue

The logic of Rav Pappa's position, then, is that the dying person's words automatically take effect — *divrei shchiv mei'ra k-chsuvim u-chmssurim damu* — but only when the subject of those words is concretely identifiable. "A hundred dollars in that pile in the corner" works; "a hundred dollars from my estate" does not, because we cannot identify which funds he intended, and we fear he may have had buried money in mind. The *tzburim u-munachim* requirement functions here as a marker of sufficient specificity, not as the mechanism of transfer.

### Rav Zevid Objection

Rav Zevid resists Rav Pappa's reading. He argues: *lo misukma masnisin b'shchiv mei'ra* — the *mishnah* does not appear to be dealing with a *shchiv mei'ra* at all, and we should not introduce that reading. His evidence: the *mishnah* says that if the man said "give this *get* to my wife," then so long as he was alive, one would deliver it; the reason is that he said *tenu*, "give" — implying that had he merely said *kisvu*, "write," delivery would not follow. But by a *shchiv mei'ra*, the rule is *af al gav d'lo amar tenu nosnim* — even if he only said *kisvu*, "write," we assume he meant write it and deliver it. This principle is established in a *mishnah* elsewhere: a man being led out in chains to execution who said *kisvu get l'ishti* is understood to have meant "write it and give it." Similarly, one departing on a sea journey or caravan. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri adds even a person who is critically ill, i.e., a *shchiv mei'ra*.

Accordingly, if the *mishnah* were discussing a *shchiv mei'ra*, the distinction it draws between *tenu* and *kisvu* would not hold — a *shchiv mei'ra* who says *kisvu* would also have his instructions delivered. This undermines reading the *mishnah* as a *shchiv mei'ra* case.

### Write vs. Give

Rav Ashi challenges Rav Zevid: *u-mimai d'masnisin Rabbi Shimon Shezuri hee dilma rabbanan hee* — why must we assume the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's view? It may follow the *rabbanan*, who did not extend the *kisvu*-means-*tenu* principle to a *shchiv mei'ra*. Under the *rabbanan*'s view, a *shchiv mei'ra* who says only *kisvu* has his document written but not necessarily delivered. On that reading, the *mishnah*'s implication that *tenu* was necessary and *kisvu* would be insufficient is perfectly compatible with the case being a *shchiv mei'ra*.

Thus the *mishnah*'s distinction between *tenu* and *kisvu* in the context of *get* and *shtar shichrur* reflects a real legal boundary: "give" triggers delivery and therefore the full process of granting freedom or divorce, while "write" does not. The money case is categorically different — whether by *maamad shloshtan* for a healthy person or by *divrei shchiv mei'ra* for one on his deathbed — and therefore transfers even after death.

## Maamad Shloshtan

### Rav Huna Transfer

The *gemara* now sets aside its analysis of the *mishnah* and examines the principle of *maamad shloshtan* on its own terms. Rav Huna, citing Rav, states: *maneh li b'yadcha tenehu lo l'ploni b'maamad shloshtan kanah* — if Reuven says to Shimon "you owe me a hundred dollars; give it to Levi," and all three of them are present, Levi acquires it. The question before us is: what is the legal mechanism that makes this work, and what are its limits?

### Pikadon vs. Debt

Rava initially reasons that *maamad shloshtan* makes intuitive sense in the case of a *pikadon* — a concrete object held in trust. If Shimon is holding Reuven's laptop, and Reuven says in the presence of all three "give that laptop to Levi," one can identify a mechanism: Shimon's possession of the object, combined with his agreement, operates to transfer ownership to Levi. The object is specific; the relationship is tangible. Rava therefore says *mistabra milta d'Rav b'pikadon* — Rav's ruling is most naturally understood as applying to a *pikadon*.

But by a *milveh* — an abstract debt — there is no object changing hands. Shimon simply owes Reuven money. How does Reuven's declaration in the presence of all three suddenly convert that obligation into one running to Levi? Rava was initially inclined to say *aval b'milveh lo* — it does not work for a debt.

### Milveh Application

However, the *gemara* immediately corrects this: *v'ha lav hachi, amar Rava afilu b'milveh* — in fact, Rava himself said it applies even to a *milveh*. And this is supported by a parallel tradition: Shmuel, citing Levi, stated explicitly that *maneh li b'yadcha tenehu l'ploni b'maamad shloshtan kanah* applies even where what Shimon holds is a loan, not a tangible deposit. The consensus is therefore that *maamad shloshtan* works for *milveh* as well.

### Implicit Agreement

The *gemara* then asks *v'ta'ama mai* — what is the rationale? Amemar explains: *na'aseh k'omer lo b'sha'at matan ma'ot shibadna lach u-l'didach u-l'chol d'asu machmatchach* — it is as if, at the moment Shimon received the loan, he implicitly said to Reuven: "I obligate myself to you, and to you, and to anyone who comes on your account — any designee you name." When one borrows money, one implicitly undertakes to repay it not only to the lender but to whomever the lender might direct. This implicit undertaking is the legal mechanism behind *maamad shloshtan* for *milveh*.

### Future Designees

Rav Ashi raises a sharp difficulty with Amemar's explanation. The mechanism Amemar describes roots the obligation in what Shimon implicitly agreed to at the time he borrowed the money. But this only works if Levi was already alive at that time — Shimon could then be said to have implicitly obligated himself ten years ago to pay whoever Reuven might designate, including Levi. But what if Levi was born only after the loan was made?

Rav Ashi makes the point precise: *da'afilu le-Rabbi Meir de-amar adam makneh davar she-lo ba le-olam* — even according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that one may transfer ownership of something not yet in existence (e.g., selling future produce of a tree), that leniency concerns the *object* being transferred, not the *recipient*. *Aval le-davar she-eino ba'olam lo* — one cannot transfer to a person who does not yet exist. Shimon ten years ago could not have obligated himself to Levi who was not yet born, because one cannot incur obligations toward a non-existent person. Amemar's mechanism therefore breaks down in such cases.

### New Obligation

Rav Ashi therefore reformulates the mechanism: *be-hai hana'ah de-ka mishtanya lei bein milveh yeshanah le-milveh chadashah gamar u-meshabeid nafsheih* — at the moment of the *maamad shloshtan* transfer, Shimon benefits. An old debt is transformed into a new obligation running to Levi. Shimon, who was in arrears on a ten-year-old loan, now has a fresh obligation with Levi, giving him some breathing room. It is this benefit — received in real time at the moment of the transfer — that constitutes the consideration for Shimon's current self-obligation to Levi. This sidesteps the problem of retroactive obligation to someone not yet born, because the operative moment is now, not ten years ago.

### Undesirable Parties

Huna Mar, son of Rav Nechemya, challenges Rav Ashi's reformulated mechanism. If the logic is that Shimon benefits from the conversion of an old debt into a new one, then consider a case: *ke-gon hani de-bei Bar Elyashiv* — parties like those of the Bar Elyashiv household, *de-kaftei ve-shaklei le-altar* — who bind debtors and collect immediately. If Reuven transfers Shimon's debt to these people, Shimon is clearly worse off, not better. There is no benefit to him in this conversion whatsoever. Does *maamad shloshtan* therefore fail to work in such a case? Would we say *de-lo kanu* — no acquisition took place? And if we do say that, then we have created a mechanism that varies case by case, requiring us to always assess whether the specific transferee is one from whom Shimon genuinely benefits — *natata devarekha le-shiyurin*, you have made the law contingent on subjective assessment.

### Rabbinical Decree

Mar Zutra resolves the impasse by stepping back from the search for mechanism entirely: *hani telat milei shavinu rabanan ke-hilkheta belo ta'ama* — there are three things the Rabbis established as binding *halacha* without a clearly justifiable legal mechanism. *Maamad shloshtan* is one of them.

This is a striking conclusion. *Belo ta'ama* here does not mean without any motivation — presumably there was ample practical reason for the Rabbis to want such a transfer mechanism to exist. What it means is that there is no fully coherent underlying legal theory that explains *why* it works in all cases. In the case of *pikadon*, one can sketch a mechanism — Shimon's possession, combined with his consent, operates to transfer the object. But extending *maamad shloshtan* to *milveh* and to cases where the transferee was not yet alive at the time of the loan, and to cases where Shimon derives no benefit from the conversion — in all these extensions, the legal mechanism breaks down. The Rabbis nonetheless decreed that it works, and it stands as one of those rabbinic enactments grounded in practical wisdom rather than derivable doctrine.
