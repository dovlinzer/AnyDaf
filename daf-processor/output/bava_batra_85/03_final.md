# Bava Batra 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyan Kli Overview

### Kli as Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנֶה לוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, חוּץ מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel both say: A person's vessel effects acquisition for him in any place, except for the public domain.**

The sugya on daf 85a opens by continuing a discussion carried over from the final lines of 84b concerning *kinyan kli* — the act of acquisition effected by placing an object into the buyer's vessel. The underlying conceptual basis of this *kinyan* is not that the *kli* constitutes an independent mode of transfer, but rather that the buyer's vessel functions as a miniaturized *chatzer*: just as placing an object in someone's domain can effect acquisition, so too does placing it in his vessel, which stands as a surrogate for his personal domain. In the same way that placing goods on a buyer's donkey might effect acquisition, filling the buyer's bag or basket operates on an analogous principle.

### Kli in Reshut Hamocher

This framework immediately generates a sharp question. If the vessel functions as the buyer's *chatzer*, the mechanism depends on the vessel being meaningfully "his" domain at the relevant moment. This works cleanly when the vessel is located in a *simta* — a side alley — or in a *chatzer shel shneihem*, a space belonging to neither party exclusively, since in those settings the buyer has an uncontested right to have his vessel present. The problem intensifies when the vessel sits in the seller's own domain. The seller has, of course, permitted the buyer to set down his bag in the store while the goods are being loaded into it — so the buyer has some right to have the vessel there. Yet the store remains fundamentally the seller's domain, and the goods have not yet left the seller's sphere of control. Has a genuine transfer of possession occurred? This is the central question the *gemara* now pursues: does *kinyan kli* operate when the buyer's vessel is in *reshut hamocher*, the seller's domain?

## Kli in Reshut Harabim

### Rav and Shmuel

The *gemara* opens the formal discussion at the top of 85a with a paired ruling attributed to Rav and Shmuel, who state together: *kelo shel adam koneh lo b'chol makom* — a person's vessel acquires on his behalf anywhere — *chutz mirshut harabim*, except in the public domain. The reason for this exclusion is that in *reshut harabim* one has no right to leave one's vessels on the ground; absent that entitlement, the vessel cannot function as a surrogate domain. Importantly, this formulation of Rav and Shmuel implies that *kinyan kli* would work even in *reshut hamocher*, since the seller does permit the buyer to place his bags in the store — a question the *gemara* will press later.

### Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֲפִילּוּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish both say: Even in the public domain,** one's vessels effect acquisition of items placed in them.

Against this, Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — the preeminent *Eretz Yisrael* authorities — state that *kinyan kli* works *afilu birshut harabim*, even in the public domain. On its face, this creates a direct dispute: Rav and Shmuel say it fails in *reshut harabim*; Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish say it succeeds even there. One might speculate whether this *Bavel*–*Eretz Yisrael* divergence reflects genuine differences in how *reshut harabim* functioned practically in those respective societies, though the *gemara* itself does not pursue that line.

### Rav Papa Reconciles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא פְּלִיגִי; כָּאן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, כָּאן בְּסִימְטָא. וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לַהּ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים? שֶׁאֵין רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** These *amora'im* **do not disagree: Here,** when Rav and Shmuel state that one's vessel does not effect acquisition for him, they are speaking of a vessel placed **in the public domain; there,** when Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish state that his vessel effects acquisition on his behalf, they are referring to a vessel located **in an alleyway. And why do they call** an alleyway **the public domain?** The reason is **that** an alleyway is **not a private domain.**

Rav Papa attempts to dissolve the apparent conflict. He proposes that the two schools are not actually arguing: when Rav and Shmuel say *kinyan kli* does not work in *reshut harabim*, they mean a literal, full public thoroughfare. When Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish say it does work in *reshut harabim*, they do not mean it literally — they mean a *simta*, a semi-public alley, and they call it *reshut harabim* only in the loose sense of "not a private domain." On Rav Papa's reading, everyone agrees that in a true *reshut harabim* it does not work, and everyone agrees that in a *simta* it does.

### Gemara's Counter-Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קוֹנֶה לוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְהַנִּיחוֹ. יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת – אִין, אֵין לוֹ רְשׁוּת – לָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: **So, too, it is reasonable** to interpret Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement in this manner, **as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A person's vessel effects acquisition for him in any place where he has permission to keep them.** It can be inferred from here: In a location where **he has permission** to keep them, **yes,** his vessels effect acquisition for him. But in a place where **he does not have permission** to keep his vessels, they do **not** effect acquisition for him, and one has permission to keep his vessels in an alleyway but not in the public domain. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** this statement that when Rabbi Yoḥanan referred to the public domain he meant an alleyway.

The *gemara* questions whether this harmonization can be sustained. A *baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Abba reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kelo shel adam koneh lo b'chol makom sheyesh lo reshut lehanicho* — a person's vessel acquires for him wherever he has the right to place it. The *gemara* reasons from this formulation: if *kinyan kli* operates precisely where one has the *right* to leave one's vessel, then it should fail where one lacks that right — i.e., in *reshut harabim* — and this principle, stated in Rabbi Yochanan's own name, supports the Babylonian reading of his position rather than undermining it.

### No Kli in True RH

The *gemara* therefore concludes — *shema mina* — that *kinyan kli* does not work in a true *reshut harabim* because one lacks the right to deposit vessels there. The question of *hagbahah* is briefly noted as a separate matter: if a person is actually holding or carrying the bag rather than resting it on the ground, that operates as *hagbahah* — lifting — or perhaps as something the *acharonim* would call *kinyan yad*, since the object is directly on one's person. But a bag sitting on the ground in the public domain, unconnected to the buyer's body, cannot effect acquisition.

## Four Categories

### Measure Filling Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אַרְבַּע מִדּוֹת בַּמּוֹכְרִין: עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְמַלְּאָה מִדָּה – לַמּוֹכֵר, מִשֶּׁנִּתְמַלְּאָה מִדָּה – לַלּוֹקֵחַ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמִדָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם, אֲבָל אִם הָיְתָה מִדָּה שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן – רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן – קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a difficulty from a *baraita*: There are **four cases with regard to sellers,** i.e., four methods through which merchandise is acquired. When the seller measures merchandise for the buyer, **before the measuring** vessel **has been filled** the merchandise in the vessel still belongs **to the seller** and he can change his mind and cancel the sale. **Once the measuring** vessel **has been filled** the merchandise belongs **to the buyer. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said when the seller measures **with a measuring** vessel **that does not belong to either of them. But if the measuring** vessel **belonged to one of them,** the buyer **acquires** the items of sale **one** by **one** as they are placed in the measuring vessel.

The *gemara* now introduces a *Tosefta* — introduced with the formula *ta shema* — that enumerates four categories (*arba middos*) relevant to commercial transactions. The first category concerns the measuring process itself. Imagine a buyer purchasing three cups of flour from a seller, who fills a measuring vessel to the three-cup mark. The *Tosefta* states: *ad shelo nismaila hamiddah lamocher* — before the measure is filled to the purchased amount, the goods belong to the seller; *mishenismaila hamiddah lalokeach* — once the measure is filled to that amount, they belong to the buyer.

### Third Party Vessel

The *Tosefta* immediately qualifies this rule: *bameh devarim amurim bemiddah she'einah shel shneihem* — in what circumstances does this apply? When the measuring vessel belongs to neither party, i.e., it was borrowed from a third party. The *gemara* supplies the logic. Since neither the seller nor the buyer owns the vessel, the third party's loan operates sequentially: until the seller completes measuring out the purchased quantity, he is the current user of the borrowed vessel and it functions as his *kli*; once the measure is full, the buyer becomes the current user, and at that moment he acquires the goods by *kinyan kli*. This explains why the transfer occurs precisely at the moment the measure is filled.

### Rishon Rishon Kanah

The *Tosefta* continues: *aval im hayetah middah shel echad mehem — rishon rishon kanah*. But if the measuring vessel belongs to one of the parties, then *rishon rishon kanah* — each successive portion acquires immediately as it is poured in. The Rashbam clarifies the meaning of *kanah* here: it does not signal the completion of a transaction but describes the state of possession. If the vessel belongs to the seller, each increment poured remains in his possession — *kanah* means it stays his — and no *kinyan* has been effected on behalf of the buyer even once the measure is full, since the vessel throughout has been the seller's. If the vessel belongs to the buyer, then each successive portion poured in is immediately acquired by the buyer, since it is entering his vessel. Thus *rishon rishon kanah* captures the buyer's continuous, incremental acquisition as the goods flow in.

This generates a curious question: what is the practical difference if the vessel belongs to the seller versus a third party? If the vessel is the seller's, nothing transfers until the buyer physically removes the goods or performs *hagbahah*. If borrowed from a third party, the transfer occurs automatically at the moment the measure is filled, by *kinyan kli*. This discussion is all preliminary to the central spatial question: where exactly is this transaction taking place?

### Tosefta Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּבְחָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶן, אֲבָל בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיַּגְבִּיהֶנָּה אוֹ עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֶנָּה מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ. בִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁקִּבֵּל עָלָיו מוֹכֵר, קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ. בִּרְשׁוּת הַלָּה הַמּוּפְקָדִים אֶצְלוֹ – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל עָלָיו, אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר אֶת מְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said when the seller measures the items **in the public domain or in a courtyard that does not belong to either of them. But** if it happens **in the domain of** the **seller,** the buyer **does not acquire** the merchandise **until he lifts** the measuring vessel **or until he moves it out of the domain** of the seller. If it is **in the domain of** the **buyer, once** the **seller accepts upon himself** to sell, the **buyer acquires** it. If the merchandise is located **in the domain of this** individual **with whom it had been deposited,** the buyer **does not acquire** it **until** the bailee **accepts upon himself** to designate a place where the merchandise is to be stored for the buyer, **or until** the buyer **rents** from the bailee **the place** where the merchandise is situated.

The *Tosefta* now becomes directly relevant to the *gemara*'s central inquiry. It specifies the domains in which the above rules operate: *bameh devarim amurim — birshut harabim uvechatzer she'einah shel shneihem*. In what circumstances do these rules apply? In a *reshut harabim* and in a *chatzer* belonging to neither party. *Aval birshut mocher lo kanah* — but in the seller's domain, the buyer does not acquire — *ad she'yagbihennah*, until he lifts it, *o ad she'yotzienu mereshuto*, or takes it out of the seller's domain.

This is a striking *gemara*. The *Tosefta* takes a position that is almost the precise inverse of what the Babylonian *gemara* has been assuming. The Babylonian *gemara* treats *reshut harabim* as the paradigm case where *kinyan kli* fails — precisely because one has no right to leave vessels there. Yet the *Tosefta* says *kinyan kli* works in *reshut harabim*, while it is *reshut hamocher* where it fails. The *Tosefta*'s reasoning can be understood on its own terms: perhaps what matters is not the buyer's formal right to deposit the vessel but whether the goods have left the seller's sphere of control. Once the goods are in the buyer's vessel and no longer under the seller's hand, they are the buyer's — even if the buyer's vessel is technically sitting somewhere it should not be. Conversely, even if the buyer has permission to set his bag down in the seller's store, as long as they remain in the seller's domain they remain fundamentally under the seller's control.

### Reshut Lokeach

The *Tosefta* then addresses *reshut lokeach* — the buyer's own domain. Here, *keivan she'kibel alav mocher — kanah loke'ach*: once the seller has accepted the terms of the sale, the buyer acquires immediately. This is because the goods are already in the buyer's domain and *kinyan chatzer* operates automatically. One might find this somewhat counterintuitive: the usual value of a formal *kinyan* is to identify a concrete, unambiguous moment of transfer, and a simple statement of agreement seems less concrete than a physical act. Yet when the goods are already in the buyer's house — whether because the seller is a traveling merchant who has come to the buyer, or for some other reason — the act of agreement itself, *kibel alav*, is sufficient to trigger *kinyan chatzer*. The *chatzer* of the buyer is already in place; it only awaits the finalization of terms.

A related question arises whether this changes if the goods remain in the seller's own vessels within the buyer's domain. The *gemara* treats the two configurations — buyer's vessel in seller's domain, and seller's vessel in buyer's domain — as parallel and symmetrical. Whatever principle determines acquisition in one case must apply in the other.

### Third Party Domain

The fourth domain addressed in the *Tosefta* is that of a third party — for example, the seller's goods are being stored in David's warehouse, and the buyer wishes to acquire them while they remain there. The *Tosefta* rules: *lo kanah* — the buyer does not acquire — *ad she'kibel alav*, until David accepts upon himself to hold the goods on behalf of the buyer, *o ad she'yischar mekoman* — or until the buyer rents the space from David.

The underlying principle here is essential. If the goods are in the seller's own domain, the seller cannot simply declare "I will now hold these on your behalf" and thereby effect a transfer: one cannot simultaneously be the party relinquishing possession and the party receiving it on behalf of the other side. But if a neutral third party is holding the goods, David can declare that he now holds them for the buyer. This functions on the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* — one may act to confer benefit on another in his absence. David, as an independent third party, can use his *chatzer* or his *yad* to be *zocheh* on the buyer's behalf, effecting acquisition. This is precisely why *kibel alav* by a third party works, whereas the same declaration by the seller would be meaningless.

The question is raised whether David could, at the outset, make a blanket agreement to hold goods for whichever buyer the seller designates at the time of sale, without needing to know the buyer's identity in advance. This would resemble a form of *breira*, though not quite, since the *kinyan* would not operate retroactively — David would become the buyer's representative only at the moment the seller makes the designation. Such an arrangement might well be valid.

Tosafos and the Rashbam disagree about how exactly to count the "four categories" (*arba middos*). The Rashbam objects that the rules applicable in the seller's domain and in the third party's domain are in practice the same — *hagbahah* and removal work in both — and so they do not constitute genuinely distinct categories. Tosafos responds that there is indeed a difference: in the third party's domain, *kibel alav* by the third party suffices to effect acquisition without any rental or physical act, whereas in the seller's domain no such declaration by the seller can work. It is this difference that justifies treating them as four distinct categories.

### Gemara Reread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּבְחָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶן;
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** this *baraita* **teaches with regard to** a transaction in **the public domain or in a courtyard that does not belong to either of them** that if the measuring vessel belonged to one of them, the buyer acquires the items of sale one by one as they are placed in the vessel.

The *gemara* returns to what seems the most straightforward source: the *Tosefta* itself, which states explicitly — *birshut mocher lo kanah ad she'yagbihenu o ad she'yotzienu mereshuto* — that in the seller's domain the buyer does not acquire until the goods are lifted or removed from that domain. However, the *gemara* probes the *Tosefta* for internal consistency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מַמָּשׁ? לָא; סִימְטָא. וְהָא דּוּמְיָא דְּחָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **What,** is it **not** stating that a buyer's vessels effect acquisition of items on his behalf even **in the actual public domain,** which contradicts Rav Pappa's explanation? The Gemara answers: **No,** the *baraita* is referring to **an alleyway,** not the actual public domain. The Gemara asks: How can this be what the *baraita* means? **But** the *baraita* **teaches** that it is **similar to a courtyard that does not belong to either of them,** i.e., a location to which neither of them have any rights, whereas they both have some rights in an alleyway. Consequently, this must be referring to the actual public domain, not an alleyway.

The challenge arises from the *Tosefta*'s ruling on *reshut lokeach*: in the buyer's domain, once the seller accepts the terms (*kibel alav mocher*), the buyer acquires. If the *Tosefta* is uniformly discussing cases where the goods are in the seller's vessels, then why does *kinyan chatzer* of the buyer work in his domain — after all, the goods are still in the seller's bags? If the reasoning is that it is the seller's *kli* that matters and not the domain, then the buyer's *chatzer* should be irrelevant. If the reasoning is that the domain determines acquisition, then being in the seller's *kli* within the buyer's domain should not undermine *kinyan chatzer*. The *gemara* is pressing the point that the two principles — domain-based acquisition and vessel-based acquisition — cannot both be operative simultaneously without contradiction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״חָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם״ נָמֵי – דְּלָא דְּהַאי כּוּלָּהּ וְלָא דְּהַאי כּוּלָּהּ; אֶלָּא דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **A courtyard that does not belong to either of them?** It **also** refers to shared property, specifically a courtyard shared by partners, **which does not** belong to **this** one **entirely and does not** belong to **that** one **entirely, but rather** it is the property **of both of them.** Consequently, this courtyard is comparable to an alleyway.

### Goes by Reshut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּרֵישָׁא בְּכִלְיוֹ דְמוֹכֵר – סֵיפָא נָמֵי בְּכִלְיוֹ דְמוֹכֵר; אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו מוֹכֵר – קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ. וְאִי בְּכִלְיוֹ דְמוֹכֵר, אַמַּאי קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ? סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְכִלְיוֹ דְלוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But from** the fact **that the first clause** is referring **to the vessels of the seller,** as currently understood, **the latter clause** must **also** be referring **to the vessels of the seller. Say the latter clause:** If the merchandise was **in the domain of the buyer, once the seller accepts upon himself** to sell an item, **the buyer acquires** it. **And if** this is referring to merchandise **in the vessels of the seller,** as in the earlier clause, **why does the buyer acquire** it? The Gemara answers: In **the latter clause, we come to** a different scenario, which involves **the vessels of the buyer.**

The *gemara* resolves this by stepping back and concluding that the *Tosefta* is in fact entirely domain-based (*reshut*), not *kli*-based. The reason the *Tosefta* produces consistent results across all four cases is not that we are tracking whose *kli* the goods are in, but simply whose domain they are in. The *Tosefta* simply assumes, as a matter of practical reality, that goods in the seller's store are typically in the seller's vessels, and goods in the buyer's home are typically in the buyer's vessels — *stama demilta, bei mocher mani democher shechichei, bei loke'ach mani delokeach shechichei* — in the seller's premises, the available containers are the seller's; in the buyer's premises, the available containers are the buyer's. The whole rule goes by *reshut*, and the mention of *kelim* is incidental to the typical circumstance in each location. This is a notably forced reading, since the *Tosefta* nowhere says anything about whose *kli* it is — but the *gemara* maintains that the *Tosefta*'s principle is domain-based, and that the question of *kinyan kli* independent of domain remains open.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי פַּסְקָא? סְתָמָא דְמִילְּתָא, בֵּי מוֹכֵר – מָאנֵי דְמוֹכֵר שְׁכִיחִי, בֵּי לוֹקֵחַ – מָאנֵי דְלוֹקֵחַ שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the clauses of the *baraita* are addressing different cases, **why** was it stated **without qualification?** The Gemara answers: **The normal** way **of things** is that **in the house of the seller the vessels of the seller are** commonly **found, and in the house of the buyer the vessels of the buyer are** commonly **found.** In sum, the dilemma cannot be resolved from the *baraita*.

## Donkey Proof

### Pirkan Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: מָשַׁךְ חֲמָרָיו וּפוֹעֲלָיו, וְהִכְנִיסָן לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ; בֵּין פָּסַק עַד שֶׁלֹּא מָדַד, וּבֵין מָדַד עַד שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַק – שְׁנֵיהֶן יְכוֹלִין לַחֲזוֹר בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita*: If **one pulled his donkey drivers,** thereby dragging along with them the donkeys laden with goods, **and** likewise, if he pulled **his laborers,** who were carrying merchandise he wished to purchase, **and he brought them into his house, whether he fixed** a price **before he measured** the merchandise **or whether he measured before fixing** a price, **both** parties **can renege on** the sale, provided that the merchandise has not been unloaded from the laborers or the donkeys.

Rava introduces a new attempt to resolve the question, drawing on a *baraita* concerning a seller's donkeys loaded with goods. The case: the buyer leads the seller's donkey-drivers and their donkeys — laden with bags of apples — into the buyer's house. On its face, this might seem to constitute *meshichah* — the pulling or drawing that effects *kinyan* on movable goods — but the *gemara* notes that the buyer did not perform *meshichah* directly on the apples; he pulled the donkeys, not the apples themselves, and perhaps only guided the donkey-drivers rather than the donkeys. Moreover, even if one argued for *meshichah* on the donkeys, the goods on the donkeys' backs may not be affected. Similarly, while the goods have now entered the buyer's domain, they remain on the backs of the seller's donkeys — not resting in the buyer's space independently — and so *kinyan chatzer* may not apply either. The *baraita* then addresses the case of *pirkan* — where the donkeys have been unloaded and the bags now sit in the buyer's house.

### Pesak and Madad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּירְקָן וְהִכְנִיסָן לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ; פָּסַק עַד שֶׁלֹּא מָדַד – אֵין שְׁנֵיהֶן יְכוֹלִין לַחֲזוֹר בָּהֶן, מָדַד עַד שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַק – שְׁנֵיהֶן יְכוֹלִין לַחֲזוֹר בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** But in a case where **he unloaded** the merchandise from them **and brought it into his house,** if **he fixed** a price **before he measured** the merchandise, **both** parties **are no** longer **able to renege on** the sale. If **he measured** the merchandise **before fixing** a price, **both of them are able to renege on** the sale.

The *baraita* introduces a distinction based on *gemirut da'at* — finality of intent. If the price has been established (*pasak*) but the goods have not yet been measured (*madad*), or if the goods have been measured but no price established, then even after the donkeys are unloaded into the buyer's house, *shneihem yecholim lachzor bahen* — either party may retract, because there is no *gemirut da'at*. However, if both a price has been agreed upon and the goods have been brought into the buyer's domain via *pirkan*, then *ein shneihem yecholim lachzor bahen* — neither party may retract. The *kinyan chatzer* is complete once there is both *gemirut da'at* (established price) and physical entry into the buyer's domain. The measurement need not be complete for the *kinyan* to take effect; what matters is that both parties have committed to the transaction with sufficient finality.

### Chatzer Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּכִלְיוֹ דְמוֹכֵר בִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ – לֹא קָנָה, כִּלְיוֹ דְלוֹקֵחַ נָמֵי – בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר לֹא קָנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And from** the fact **that the vessels of the seller** when **in the domain of** the **buyer do not effect acquisition** of the merchandise for the seller, i.e., they do not prevent the buyer from acquiring the merchandise, one can derive that **the vessels of the buyer in the domain of** the **seller do not effect acquisition** of the merchandise on his behalf **as well.**

The crucial implication of the *pirkan* case is that once the goods have been unloaded into the buyer's house — even though they remain in the seller's bags — the sale becomes final. This appears to be a direct proof that *kinyan chatzer* operates according to the domain, not the vessel: the goods are in the seller's bags (*kelav delokeach* in the buyer's *reshut*), and yet the buyer acquires. If *kinyan kli* in the buyer's domain could be undermined by the fact that the goods remain in the seller's vessel, then the *baraita* would not rule that the buyer acquires at *pirkan*. The case thus appears to demonstrate symmetrically that it is the domain that controls, not the *kli*.

### Gemara Defenses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּשֶׁשְּׁפָכָן. אִיקְּפַד רָבָא – מִידֵּי ״שְׁפָכָן״ קָתָנֵי?! ״פֵּירְקָן״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: בִּמְתַאכְּלֵי דְתוּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The merchandise was not placed in the domain of the buyer in vessels belonging to the seller. Rather, the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he emptied** the vessels onto the ground. Conversely, if the merchandise remains in the seller's vessels, the buyer does not acquire it. **Rava became angry** with Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak at his rejection of Rava's proof, and retorted: **Does** the *baraita* **teach: He emptied them?** No; **it teaches: He unloaded them,** i.e., he kept the goods in vessels belonging to the seller. **Rather, Mar bar Rav Ashi says:** This proof can be rejected by means of a different interpretation, as the *halakha* of the *baraita* does not refer to vessels full of goods but is stated **with regard to bundles of garlic** that were tied together. Therefore, they are unloaded straight onto the floor in the buyer's domain.

The *gemara* refuses to accept this proof and offers two defensive readings. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes that *pirkan* — "he unloaded" — means the seller literally poured out the apples, emptying them directly onto the floor of the buyer's house, so there are no seller's bags involved at all. Rava objects sharply: the *baraita* uses the word *pirkan*, not *shafchan* — it says he unloaded, not that he dumped or poured out. *Pirkan* implies removing the bags from the donkeys while the goods remain inside them; the bags are still present. The *gemara* then offers a second defense, attributed to Mar bar Rav Ashi: the case involves *matachli detuma* — strings or braids of garlic, which were typically not packed in bags at all but carried loosely or braided together directly on the donkey's back. Since garlic was sold and transported in this form without bags, *pirkan* in this context means simply unloading the garlic strands onto the buyer's floor — and since there were never any bags, the proof says nothing about what happens when the seller's vessels are present. The question of *kinyan kli* in *reshut hamocher* thus remains unresolved, and the *gemara* leaves it open for the continuation of the sugya.

## Tosefta Evidence

### Tosefta Simple Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשׁוֹט לָהּ מֵהָא: בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיַּגְבִּיהֶנָּה אוֹ עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֶנָּה מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, resolve** the dilemma **from that** which was taught in a *baraita*: If the merchandise was **in the domain of a seller,** the buyer **does not acquire** the merchandise **until he lifts** it **or until he removes it from the domain** of the seller.

The *gemara* returns to what seems the most straightforward source: the *Tosefta* itself, which states explicitly — *birshut mocher lo kanah ad she'yagbihenu o ad she'yotzienu mereshuto* — that in the seller's domain the buyer does not acquire until the goods are lifted or removed from that domain. The simple reading of this *Tosefta* is transparent: it does not matter that the goods may be sitting in the buyer's vessel; as long as they remain in the seller's domain, the buyer has not acquired them. Only *hagbahah* or physical removal from the seller's domain suffices to complete the transaction. This apparently settles the question of *kinyan kli* in *reshut hamocher* — it simply does not work.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו בְּכִלְיוֹ דְלוֹקֵחַ? לֹא, בְּכִלְיוֹ דְמוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **What,** is it **not** referring to merchandise placed **in the vessels of the buyer,** which proves that the buyer's vessels do not effect acquisition of the merchandise on his behalf when they are in the seller's domain? The Gemara answers: **No,** this does not serve as proof, as it is referring to merchandise placed **in the vessels of the seller.** That is why the buyer must lift or pull the merchandise to acquire it.

But the *gemara* resists this conclusion and probes the *Tosefta* for internal consistency. The challenge arises from the *Tosefta*'s ruling on *reshut lokeach*: in the buyer's domain, once the seller accepts the terms (*kibel alav mocher*), the buyer acquires. If the *Tosefta* is uniformly discussing cases where the goods are in the seller's vessels, then why does *kinyan chatzer* of the buyer work in his domain — after all, the goods are still in the seller's bags? If the reasoning is that it is the seller's *kli* that matters and not the domain, then the buyer's *chatzer* should be irrelevant. If the reasoning is that the domain determines acquisition, then being in the seller's *kli* within the buyer's domain should not undermine *kinyan chatzer*. The *gemara* is pressing the point that the two principles — domain-based acquisition and vessel-based acquisition — cannot both be operative simultaneously without contradiction.
