# Gittin 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tikkun Olam Overview

### Shift to General Tikkun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין אַלְמָנָה נִפְרַעַת מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים, אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. נִמְנְעוּ מִלְּהַשְׁבִּיעָהּ; הִתְקִין רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן, שֶׁתְּהֵא נוֹדֶרֶת לַיְּתוֹמִים כֹּל מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצוּ, וְגוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִין עַל הַגֵּט, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם. וְהִלֵּל הִתְקִין פְּרוֹזְבּוּל, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A widow can collect** payment of her marriage contract **from the property of orphans only by** means of **an oath** that she did not receive any part of the payment of the marriage contract during her husband's lifetime. The mishna relates: The courts **refrained from administering an oath to her,** leaving the widow unable to collect payment of her marriage contract. **Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted that she should take, for** the benefit of **the orphans, any vow that** the orphans **wished** to administer to her, e.g., that all produce will become prohibited to her if she received any payment of her marriage contract, **and** after stating this vow, she **collects** payment of **her marriage contract. The witnesses sign** their names **on the bill of divorce,** even though the bill of divorce is valid without their signatures, **for the betterment of the world,** as the Gemara will explain. **And Hillel instituted a document that prevents the Sabbatical** Year **from abrogating an outstanding debt [*prosbol*] for the betterment of the world,** as the Gemara will explain.

The daf opens on Gittin 35b, and we now transition from *tikkun olam* rulings that were specifically connected to *gittin* to *tikkun olam* rulings of a more general character — improvements to the world that extend beyond the narrow context of divorce. The phrase *tikkun olam* has, of course, acquired broad connotations in other cultural contexts — universal social justice and the like — but as it has been used throughout this sugya, it carries a far more specific and halachic meaning: protecting people from legal abuse, or from situations where the halacha itself places them in untenable circumstances, particularly to protect women from having their *get* challenged improperly or from other related hardships.

### Defining Tikkun Olam

This raises a genuine conceptual question: who exactly is the *olam* being improved? In the cases we have seen, the *olam* is a fairly local one — primarily women who find themselves in precarious legal positions. The *tikkun* is about managing a specific societal reality and making the halachic system function in a way that doesn't leave people stranded.

### Tikkun Olam Examples

The *mishna* presents several examples. The first — which will be the focus of the *gemara* — is the rule that a widow cannot collect from the orphans' estate except by taking an oath. The second is the already-familiar rule that witnesses sign a *get* for the sake of *tikkun olam*, enabling the woman to remarry without producing the original witnesses — the position of Rabbi Eliezer that biblically no signatures were required, but rabbinically they were instituted to protect the woman. The third is Hillel's institution of the *pruzbul* for the sake of *tikkun olam*. Notably, the phrase *mipnei tikkun olam* does not explicitly appear alongside the widow's case — the *mishna* simply says *hitkin Rabban Gamliel* — while it appears explicitly by the witnesses and the *pruzbul*.

### Who Benefits from Tikkun

The question of who constitutes the *olam* being addressed is worth pausing over. In one case it is widows; in another it is women being divorced; in the third it is the broader economic reality of lending and borrowing. In the final case, the *olam* encompasses the entire Jewish community's financial life. But in all cases, the *olam* remains the Jewish *olam* — it is the internal world of halachic society that is being ordered and improved.

## Widow's Kesuba Collection

### General Debt Collection…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי אִירְיָא אַלְמָנָה, אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָמֵי; דְּהָא קַיְימָא לַן – הַבָּא לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִין, לֹא יִפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה! אַלְמָנָה אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: מִשּׁוּם חִינָּא אַקִּילוּ רַבָּנַן גַּבַּהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why** discuss **specifically a widow?** This *halakha* should apply to **everyone, as we maintain** that anyone **who comes to collect** payment **from the property of orphans can collect only by** means of **an oath.** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary for** the mishna to mention **a widow,** as it might **enter your mind to say: Due to** the increased **desirability** that this would bring her when trying to remarry, since this would ensure she would bring assets with her into a new marriage, **the Sages were lenient with her,** as the Sages issued several decrees in connection with the marriage contract in order to enable women to collect more easily. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that this is not the case.

The *gemara* immediately challenges the formulation of the *mishna*. Why does the *mishna* specify that an *almana* — a widow — cannot collect from the orphans without an oath? This rule ought to apply universally: anyone who comes to collect a debt from orphans after the father has died must take an oath, since the father is no longer alive to verify that the debt was never repaid. Perhaps he trusted the creditor and did not demand a receipt; perhaps he paid and the receipt was lost. The general rule is well established: *haba lipara minechasei yesomim lo yipara ela bishvua* — one who comes to collect from orphan heirs does not collect without an oath. So why single out the widow?

### Mishum China Doctrine

The *gemara* answers: *almana itztricha lei* — it was necessary to underscore that even the widow must take an oath, because one might have thought otherwise. The reasoning behind this assumption is the doctrine of *mishum china* — literally, finding favor. The idea is that because the *kesuba* was instituted to make marriage attractive and to protect the woman financially, one might have thought the rabbis would be lenient and not require her to take an oath, both to encourage women to marry with confidence that their financial interests would be protected, and to ensure the *kesuba* would genuinely function as a security. As Rashi explains: *mishum china sheyehei chen hanashim beeinei haanashim linasei lahem* — so that women would find favor in the eyes of men and be prepared to marry. Therefore, the *mishna* teaches us that notwithstanding this consideration, the oath requirement still applied to her — *ka mashma lan* — this leniency was not granted.

### Dinar-Bread Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְנְעוּ מִלְּהַשְׁבִּיעָהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא – דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא; וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת, שֶׁהִפְקִיד דִּינַר זָהָב אֵצֶל אַלְמָנָה; וְהִנִּיחַתּוּ בְּכַד שֶׁל קֶמַח, וַאֲפָאַתּוּ בְּפַת, וּנְתָנַתּוּ לְעָנִי; לְיָמִים בָּא בַּעַל הַדִּינָר, וַאֲמַר לַהּ: הָבִי לִי דִּינָרִי. אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: יְהַנֶּה סַם הַמָּוֶת בְּאֶחָד מִבָּנֶיהָ שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ אִשָּׁה, אִם נֶהֱנֵיתִי מִדִּינָרְךָ כְּלוּם! אָמְרוּ: לֹא הָיוּ יָמִים מוּעָטִין עַד שֶׁמֵּת אֶחָד מִבָּנֶיהָ; וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: מָה מִי שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע בֶּאֱמֶת – כָּךְ, הַנִּשְׁבָּע עַל שֶׁקֶר – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates: The courts **refrained from administering an oath to her.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that they refrained from administering oaths to widows? **If we say** that it is **because** of the statement **of Rav Kahana, as Rav Kahana says, and some say** that it was **Rav Yehuda** who **says** that **Rav says:** There was **an incident involving a person during years of famine who deposited a gold dinar with a widow, and she placed** the gold dinar **in a jug of flour and** unwittingly **baked it in** a loaf of **bread** along with the flour, **and** she **gave** the bread as charity **to a poor man. After a period of time, the owner of the dinar came and said to her: Give me my dinar. She said to him: May poison benefit,** i.e., take effect on, **one of the children of that woman,** i.e., my children, **if I derived any benefit from your dinar. It was said: Not** even **a few days** passed **until one of her children died, and when the Sages heard of** this **matter, they said: If one who takes an oath truthfully** is punished in **this** way for sin, **one who takes an oath falsely, all the more so.**

But then the situation changed. The *gemara* records that they stopped requiring the widow to take an oath — *nimne'u mil'hashbia* — because they became deeply concerned about the consequences of a false oath. The *gemara* introduces this concern through a famous story. Rav Kahana — and some say it was Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav — reports: there was an incident during years of famine in which a certain man deposited a gold *dinar* with a widow for safekeeping. She placed it in a pitcher of flour. She then forgot it was there, kneaded the dough, and baked it into bread. Given that it was a time of famine, she gave the loaf to a pauper. When the owner returned and asked for his coin, she could not produce it — she genuinely did not know where it had gone — and so she took what was intended as an oath: *yehaneh sam hamaves b'echad mibaneha shel osa isha im neheness midinaracha klum* — let poison take effect upon one of my children if I derived any benefit from your coin. A short time later, one of her children died.

When the sages heard of this, they said: if someone who swore truthfully — or at least believed she was swearing truthfully — suffered such consequences, how much more severe would be the punishment of one who swears falsely with intent. This incident crystallized the communal recoil from the oath and led to the reluctance to impose it upon widows.

### Benefit Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא אִיעֲנַשָׁה? דְּאִישְׁתָּרַשׁ לַהּ מְקוֹם דִּינָר. וּמַאי ״מִי שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע בֶּאֱמֶת״? כְּמִי שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע בֶּאֱמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first clarifies the details of the incident: **What is the reason** that **she was punished** if she in fact did not derive any benefit from the dinar? The Gemara answers: **Because she benefited** [***ishtarshi***] **from the place of the dinar,** as the dinar took up space in the bread, enabling her to use less flour. Therefore, she did derive some small benefit from the dinar. The Gemara asks: If she in fact did derive benefit from the dinar, then **what** is meant by the statement: **One who takes an oath truthfully?** Wasn't her oath actually false? The Gemara answers: It means that she was **like one who took an oath truthfully,** as her oath was truthful to the best of her knowledge. In any case, this woman was punished severely for a small mistake. The severity of taking a false oath, even inadvertently, is why the Sages ceased administering oaths to widows.

The *gemara* then probes: why was she punished at all, if she swore truthfully? The answer is *d'ishtareshi lah makom dinar* — she did in fact benefit from the coin, since the coin displaced a volume of flour in the bread, meaning she effectively gained that volume's worth of flour. But the irony compounds: she did not even eat the bread herself — she gave it to a pauper. What benefit did she derive? The *gemara* holds that her voluntary decision to give away the bread does not negate the fact that she used another person's property without authorization. The benefit was incidental, but it was real. And yet in her own mind, she was swearing entirely truthfully — she genuinely believed she had derived no benefit. The *gemara* addresses this by characterizing her as *k'mi shenishba be'emes* — as if she had sworn truthfully — meaning her subjective intention was entirely honest. And even so, the consequences were severe. Hence the sages' alarm: if this is what befalls one who swears honestly but is factually mistaken, what will befall one who swears with deliberate falsehood?

## Why Widows Uniquely at Risk

### Specificity Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא, מַאי אִירְיָא אַלְמָנָה? אֲפִילּוּ גְּרוּשָׁה נָמֵי! אַלְּמָה אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא אַלְמָנָה, אֲבָל גְּרוּשָׁה מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions if this could be the reason for the ordinance: **If** they refrained from administering oaths **due to this** reason, then **why** would this be limited **specifically** to **a widow? Even a divorcée** should not be allowed to take an oath to collect her marriage contract **as well. Why** then does **Rabbi Zeira say** that **Shmuel says: They taught** this **only** with regard to **a widow; however,** with regard to **a divorcée,** the court does **administer an oath to her?** Why would this concern not apply in the case of a divorcée as well?

The *gemara* now presses the obvious question: if the concern is about the weight of oaths and the danger of swearing falsely — even innocently — why is this uniquely a problem for the *almana*? Why do we continue to allow *gerushos* (divorced women) to take oaths, or indeed any creditor collecting from orphans? The concern about inadvertent false oaths should apply universally.

### Widow's Rationalization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה שָׁאנֵי; דִּבְהָהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא טָרְחָה קַמֵּי דְּיַתְמֵי, אָתְיָא לְאוֹרוֹיֵי הֶיתֵּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **A widow is different,** as she continues to live in the house with the orphans and performs many services for them in the running of the home. Therefore, there is a concern that **due to the benefit** they receive from her as a result of **the efforts** she exerts **for the orphans, she will rationalize and permit** herself to take an oath that she had not collected any of her marriage contract, when in fact she had received a part of it.

The *gemara* answers in the name of Rav Zeira citing Shmuel: *almana shani* — the widow is different. The reason is *debei hi na dekatarcha kmei dyasmei* — because she is there in the house, she exerts effort on behalf of the orphans. She is living in her late husband's home, cooking and cleaning, performing services for the orphans that no one is compensating her for. This creates the conditions for an almost unconscious rationalization: she may find a sum of money lying around and, having worked so hard without pay, tell herself she is entitled to take it as partial payment of her *kesuba*. She does not necessarily think of it as stealing — she genuinely believes she is owed this amount and that she is entitled to it. It is the kind of rationalization familiar from everyday life: the employee who takes home a notepad from the office because they have been working overtime without recognition.

### Divorced vs. Widow

The *gerusha*, by contrast, is not living in her former husband's home. She has no ongoing relationship with the estate, no daily proximity to the orphans, and no accumulating sense of uncompensated labor. There is therefore no analogous scenario in which she would take money and rationalize it as legitimate. Hence the concern is specific to the *almana*. The story of the *dinar* happened to involve a widow, but the widow's status was largely incidental to that narrative; the *ongoing* concern is structural and arises precisely from the widow's unique domestic situation.

### Tosafot on Negligence

Tosafot raises a pointed question based on a *gemara* in *Shevu'os* which states that an oath taken in good faith — believing oneself to be telling the truth — does not constitute a transgression, since the verse *lo yenakeh Hashem es asher yisa shemo lashav* applies only to those who take the name in vain, not to those who genuinely believe what they are saying. If so, why was the widow punished? Tosafot works through this at length and concludes: *veyesh lomar dehacha gabei pikadon haya lo lehizaher yoser leshomro heytev rauei lasukei adaitei shetavo lidei shvua* — here, since she was entrusted with the coin as a *pikadon* (a deposit), she bore a heightened responsibility to take adequate care of it. Because her error arose from negligence — she should have been more careful in tracking its location — the mistake cannot be considered entirely faultless, and therefore the normal exculpation for an unintentional false oath does not apply. The principle is significant: when one's false belief is itself the product of negligence, it is not treated with the same indulgence as a genuinely unavoidable error.

## Oath Types & Contexts

### Shift to Cherem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין, אֲבָל חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין – מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba said:** It is **Rav and Shmuel who both say** that **they taught only** that an oath is not administered to the widow **in court,** as the oath that one takes in court is a severe oath, which involves the mentioning of God's name and the holding of a sacred object. **However, outside of court,** where an oath is not taken in this manner, the judges **administer an oath to her.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But Rav does not collect** payment of **a marriage contract for a widow** because she has not taken an oath, which indicates that he also would not administer an oath to her outside of the court. The Gemara answers: This is **difficult,** as it contradicts the statement of Rav Yehuda.

Tosafot continues with a remark that opens onto a broader practice. He notes that in the present era, the practice has shifted further: *ulekach nimneu milehashbia bishvua meruba* — because the oath is so weighty, they have moved away from it, *ela behadara delo chamir kuli hai* — toward the *neder*, which is not as weighty. And then Tosafot adds: *vehashata bizman hazeh tiknu lekabel cherem bemakom shvua shelo yehei hamichshol kol kach* — in our times they have instituted taking a *cherem* (a conditional curse) in place of the *shvua*, so that the potential for catastrophic consequences is diminished while still maintaining a form of solemn attestation. This observation is important not only for the specific case of the widow but for the entire institution of testimony and sworn statements. The deep-seated recoil from taking a *shvua* — even a *shvuas emes*, a truthful oath — has been a feature of Jewish practice for centuries. The practical question of what a *beis din* actually administers today follows from this entire trajectory of concern.

### Shvua vs. Alah

There is also a subtle distinction worth noting regarding the widow's statement in the story itself. A *shvua* proper is an invocation of God's name to attest to the truth of one's statement, anchored in the verse *lo yenakeh Hashem es asher yisa shemo lashav*. An *alah*, by contrast, is a conditional curse — "let such-and-such happen to me if I am lying" — which calls down consequences rather than calling God as witness. The woman's declaration in the story — *yehaneh sam hamaves b'echad mibaneha* — is linguistically an *alah*, not a *shvua*. It is possible, of course, that a proper *shvua* preceded it and is simply not quoted. But the distinction is interesting: the *gemara* treats it as a *shvua*, even though the precise language cited is that of an *alah*.

### Oath Context Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי. בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי – אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין, אֲבָל חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין – מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ. וְרַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין נָמֵי אֵין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In** the city of **Sura they taught** the statement with regard to the opinions of Rav and Shmuel **like this,** as stated above. However, **in** the city of **Neharde'a they taught** the statement with regard to the opinions of Rav and Shmuel **like this: Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says** that **they taught only** that an oath is not administered to the widow **in court; however, outside of court** the judges **administer an oath to her. And Rav says: Even outside of court as well,** the judges **do not administer an oath to her.**

The *gemara* introduces a further distinction regarding the differential weight of oaths depending on context. Rav Yehuda cites Rav and Shmuel as saying: *lo shanu ela b'beis din* — the restriction on making the widow take an oath applies only within *beis din*; *aval chutz leveis din mashbinan lah* — outside of *beis din*, she can be made to take an oath. Rashi explains the difference: a *beis din* oath is a Torah-level obligation, administered while holding a *sefer Torah* or *tefillin*, invoking God's name or an appellation thereof — as we find by Avraham who said *sim na yadecha tachas yerechi vehishvacha baShem* — and its consequences are correspondingly severe. Outside *beis din*, the oath is rabbinic in character: there is no holy object held, the formulation is more in the nature of an *arur* (a curse), and the penalty, while still real, is not as severe.

## Rav's Stringent Position

### Rav's Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רַב לָא מַגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה לְאַרְמַלְתָּא! קַשְׁיָא. בְּסוּרָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי. בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי – אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין, אֲבָל חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין – מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ. וְרַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין נָמֵי אֵין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ. רַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ – דְּרַב לָא מַגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה לְאַרְמַלְתָּא. וְלִיאַדְּרַהּ וְלַיגְבְּיַיהּ! בִּשְׁנֵי דְּרַב קִילִי נִדְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that according to this version of their statements, **Rav** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rav does not collect** payment of **a marriage contract for a widow** in any case. The Gemara asks with regard to Rav's practice: **Let him administer a vow** to the widow, instead of an oath, **and collect** the marriage contract in accordance with the mishna, which states that a widow can take a vow in place of the oath. The Gemara answers: **In Rav's time vows were** treated **lightly,** and Rav was concerned that widows would not treat the prohibition created by the vow with appropriate severity. This would result in the orphans losing out on part of their inheritance, and the widows violating the prohibitions created by their vows.

The *gemara* now raises a difficulty with this framework. If outside *beis din* the widow can be made to take an oath, then why does the *gemara* report that Rav refused to collect the *kesuba* for a widow — *lo magbi kesuba l'armalta* — at all? If there exists a lower-stakes mechanism (the oath outside *beis din*) that could enable collection, why did Rav block collection entirely?

The *gemara* initially treats this as a *kashya* — a genuine difficulty — against the version in which Rav himself stated that outside *beis din* an oath can be administered. It then presents an alternative version from *Neharde'a*: in *Sura* the teaching attributed the outside-*beis din* leniency to Rav; in *Neharde'a*, it is attributed exclusively to Shmuel. Under the *Neharde'a* version, Rav held a far more stringent position: *afilu chutz l'beis din nami ein mashbi'in osah* — even outside *beis din*, we do not make her take an oath. And Rav is then internally consistent — *Rav l'ta'ameih* — since he refused to collect the *kesuba* for widows altogether.

### Rav's Concerns

But then the question shifts: if Rav held that a *shvua* — whether in or out of *beis din* — cannot be administered to the widow, why didn't he simply follow the *mishna*'s *takanah* of Rabban Gamliel and have her take a *neder* instead? The *gemara* answers: *bishnei d'Rav kili nidrei* — in the time of Rav, people treated *nedarim* lightly. A *neder* was no longer weighty enough to function as a reliable instrument of truth-telling. The *takanah* of Rabban Gamliel had depended on the *neder* occupying a middle ground — weighty enough to keep the woman honest, but not so weighty as to carry the catastrophic consequences of a *shvuas sheker*. In Rav's era, that balance had shifted: *nedarim* had lost their gravitas, and Rav was therefore unwilling to use them. This left the widow with no viable instrument for collection, which is precisely what the *mishna*'s *takanah* was designed to prevent. The irony is not lost: the authority of Rav was now functioning as an obstacle to the very *tikkun* that Rabban Gamliel had worked to achieve.

### Woman's Workaround

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לַהּ: מָה אֶעֱבֵיד לִיךְ, דְּרַב לָא מַגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה לְאַרְמַלְתָּא. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא – אֶלָּא דִּלְמָא נָקֵיטְנָא מִידֵּי מִכְּתוּבָּתִי, חַי ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אִם נֶהֱנֵיתִי מִכְּתוּבָּתִי כְּלוּם! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מוֹדֶה רַב בְּקוֹפֶצֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was an incident involving **a certain** widow **who came before Rav Huna** and attempted to collect payment of her marriage contract from the orphans. **He said to her: What can I do for you, as Rav does not collect** payment of **a marriage contract for a widow.** The widow **said to him: Isn't the reason** that I cannot collect payment **only** because of a concern that **perhaps I** already **took some** payment **of my marriage contract?** I swear **as the Lord of Hosts lives that I did not** derive **any benefit from my marriage contract. Rav Huna says:** Even though the court does not administer an oath to a widow, **Rav concedes with regard to one who leaps** and takes an oath of her own initiative that her oath is accepted, and she can collect payment of her marriage contract.

Against this background the *gemara* relates the story of a woman who came before Rav Huna. He told her he could not help her — *d'Rav lo magbi kesuba l'armalta* — citing Rav's authority. The woman, without waiting to be asked, declared: *chai Hashem Tzevaos im nehenesi miksuvasi klum* — by the life of the God of Hosts, I did not derive any benefit from my *kesuba*. She simply took the oath unilaterally.

Rav Huna responded: *modeh Rav b'kofetzes* — even Rav concedes in the case of one who "jumps in" and takes the oath voluntarily. The reluctance to administer the oath arose from rabbinic hesitation to impose it; but once the woman takes it on her own initiative, the oath is valid and she may collect. This creates a significant asymmetry: the court will not make her take the oath, but if she takes it herself, it is binding. Women needed to know this option existed.

### Widow Trapped

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לַהּ: מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד לִיךְ, דְּרַב לָא מַגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה לְאַרְמַלְתָּא, וְאַבָּא מָרִי לָא מַגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה לְאַרְמַלְתָּא. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: הַב לִי מְזוֹנֵי. אֲמַר לַהּ: מְזוֹנֵי נָמֵי לֵית לִיךְ; דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ בְּבֵית דִּין – אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אַפְכוּהּ לְכוּרְסְיֵהּ, כְּבֵי תְּרֵי עָבְדַתְּ לִי! הַפְכוּהּ לְכוּרְסְיֵהּ וְתָרְצוּהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא אִיפְּרַק מֵחוּלְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was an incident involving **a certain** widow **who came before Rabba bar Rav Huna** to collect payment of her marriage contract. **He said to her: What can I do for you, as Rav does not collect** payment of **a marriage contract for a widow, and my father, my master,** i.e., Rav Huna, **does not collect** payment of **a marriage contract for a widow?** **She said to him:** If I cannot collect payment of the marriage contract, then **provide sustenance** for **me** from my husband's property, to support me until I remarry. **He said to her: You also do not have** any right to **sustenance, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: One who demands** payment of **her marriage contract in court has no** right to receive **sustenance** any longer. The husband committed to provide for her sustenance only as long as she does not wish to remarry. Generally, once a widow demands payment of her marriage contract, she demonstrates that she wishes now to remarry and is no longer entitled to receive sustenance from her deceased husband's property. The widow became angry and **said to** Rabba bar Rav Huna: **May his chair be overturned,** i.e., he should fall from his position of power, as **he ruled for me in accordance with** the different opinions of **two** people. Since Rabba bar Rav Huna was concerned about her curse, **he overturned his chair** in order to fulfill the curse literally, **and** then **stood it** up, **and even so, he was not saved from** the **weakness** that resulted from her curse.

The second story is more troubling. A woman came before Rabbah bar Rav Huna seeking to collect her *kesuba*. He told her: *d'Rav lo magbi kesuba l'armalta* — and furthermore, *Abba Mari lo magbi kesuba l'armalta* — my father and teacher likewise did not collect a *kesuba* for a widow. The woman, finding this avenue closed, said: fine, then at minimum support me from my late husband's estate — *hav li mezoni*. He replied: *mezoni nami leis lach* — you cannot have that either. For Rav Yehuda had said in the name of Shmuel: *hatovasa kesuvashah b'veis din ein lah mezonos* — once a woman has gone to *beis din* to demand her *kesuba*, she has effectively declared her intention to sever her connection from her late husband's household, and she therefore forfeits the ongoing support that was premised on her continued identification with that household.

The woman was now caught between two rulings: she could neither collect her *kesuba* nor continue to receive support from the estate. She cursed Rabbah bar Rav Huna — *afchuah lekursai*, let your chair be turned upside down — and the *gemara* relates that they literally inverted his chair in an attempt to symbolically fulfill the curse and thereby neutralize it. Nevertheless, *va'afilu hachi lo ifrik mechulsha* — even so, he did not escape falling ill as a result of the curse. What the episode illustrates is how the invocation of Rav's authority, transmitted through generations of disciples without reassessment, had drifted entirely away from the animating concern of *tikkun olam* that motivated the original *takanah* of Rabban Gamliel.

## Rav Yehuda's Challenge

### Neder + Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב יִרְמְיָה בִּירָאָה: אַדְּרַהּ בְּבֵית דִּין, וְאַשְׁבְּעַהּ חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, וְלֵיתֵי קָלָא וְלִיפּוֹל בְּאוּדְנִי, דְּבָעֵינָא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאֶעֱבֵיד בָּהּ מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, the Gemara recounts: **Rav Yehuda,** the student of Shmuel, **said to Rav Yirmeya Bira'a:** If a widow comes to collect payment of her marriage contract, **administer a vow in court and administer an oath outside of court,** and **let the report be received in my ears** that you did so, **as I desire to perform an action,** i.e., to enable a widow to collect payment of her marriage contract, in contrast to the statements of Rav's students, who hold that a widow cannot collect payment of her marriage contract.

Rav Yehuda said to Rav Yirmiyah Bira'a: *adra b'veis din* — have her take a *neder* in *beis din*, *ve'ashbe'uha chutz leveit din* — and have her take a *shvua* outside of *beis din*. The proposal is to combine both instruments. Given the concern that neither alone is fully reliable — the *neder* because it had lost weight in Rav's era, and the *shvua* outside *beis din* because it may not be sufficiently weighty — perhaps the combination of both would satisfy the competing concerns. The *neder* in the formal setting of *beis din* and the *shvua* in the more lenient extra-judicial context would together constitute a sufficient basis for collection.

### Demand for Action

Rav Yehuda did not stop at the theoretical proposal. He demanded: *velesei kalla velipo be'udni* — let the announcement go out and let me hear with my own ears that you have actually acted on this — *debe'inah keheichi d'avid bah ma'aseh* — I want to know that you have carried this through in practice and that she has in fact collected her *kesuba*. The frustration is palpable: this is not a theoretical discussion. There is a woman who is owed money and cannot collect it, and the whole purpose of Rabban Gamliel's *takanah* was to ensure she could. The response of Rav Yehuda reflects a reassertion of the *tikkun olam* imperative against the paralysis that had set in through excessive deference to Rav's stringency.

### Widow vs. Divorced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא אַלְמָנָה, אֲבָל גְּרוּשָׁה – מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself.** The mishna taught that the court does not administer an oath to a widow in order to enable her to collect payment of her marriage contract. **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Shmuel says: They taught** this **only** with regard to **a widow; however,** with regard to **a divorcée,** the court does **administer an oath to her.**

At this point the *gemara* circles back to reinforce the earlier distinction. The leniency for a *gerusha* — who can still take the standard oath — rests on the absence of the rationalizing dynamic. Unlike the *almana*, the divorced woman has no ongoing entanglement with her former husband's estate. She is not living there, not working there, not accumulating a sense of uncompensated labor. The scenario in which she would unconsciously take funds and tell herself she was entitled to do so simply does not arise. Hence the concern that drove the abandonment of the oath for widows does not apply to her.

## Sarah bat Yaakov Case

### Case Facts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּגְרוּשָׁה דְּאַדְּרַהּ לָא?! וְהָא שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: ״אֵיךְ פְּלוֹנִיתָא בַּת פְּלוֹנִי קַבִּילַת גִּיטָּא מִן יְדָא דְּאַחָא בַּר הִידְיָא דְּמִתְקְרֵי ״אַיָּה מָרִי״; וּנְדַרַת וַאֲסַרַת פֵּירוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם עֲלַהּ דְּלָא קַבִּילַת מִכְּתוּבְּתַהּ אֶלָּא גְּלוּפְקְרָא אֶחָד, וְסֵפֶר תְּהִלִּים אֶחָד, וְסֵפֶר אִיּוֹב, וּמִמְשָׁלוֹת בְּלוֹאִים; וְשַׁמְנוּם בַּחֲמִשָּׁה מָנֶה; לִכְשֶׁתָּבֹא לְיֶדְכֶם הַגְבּוּהָ אֶת הַשְּׁאָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is that to say that if the court **administered a vow** to **a divorcée** and not an oath, then this is **not** sufficient to enable her to collect payment of her marriage contract? **But didn't they send from there,** from Eretz Yisrael, a document that states the following: **How so-and-so, the daughter of so-and-so, received a bill of divorce from the hand of Aḥa bar Hidya, who is called Ayya Mari, and she took a vow and prohibited the produce of the world to herself,** based on the truth of her statement **that she did not receive from her marriage contract anything other than one coat** [***gelofkera***], **and one book of Psalms, and a book of Job, and** a book of **Proverbs,** all of which were **worn out. And we appraised them,** and found that their value **is five hundred dinars. When she comes to you** with this document, **collect the rest** of the payment **for her** from her husband's property in Babylonia.

The *gemara* then presents a concrete case transmitted from *Eretz Yisrael* to *Bavel*. A message was sent: how should we handle the case of a certain woman — Sarah the daughter of Yaakov — who received her *get* from Akha bar Kida, also known as Ayah Mari, and who took a *neder* regarding produce — that all the fruit of the world would be forbidden to her — attesting that the only things she had received from her *kesuba* were certain specified items? The case is therefore a *gerusha* who took a *neder* rather than a *shvua*, and the question sent to *Bavel* was: proceed to collect for her the remainder of her *kesuba*.

### Kesuba Contents

The items she admitted to having already received as partial payment of her *kesuba* deserve a moment's attention. They were: a coat, a scroll of *Tehillim*, a scroll of *Iyov*, and a scroll of *Mishlei* — worn and valued at five *maneh*. That a woman's *kesuba* included *Tehillim*, *Iyov*, and *Mishlei* is a striking window into the reality of women's learning. *Tehillim* and *Mishlei* might be expected — *Tehillim* for prayer, *Mishlei* for its *mussar* character. But *Iyov* is something else — a text of profound theological and philosophical inquiry. That these scrolls formed part of a woman's valued personal property speaks for itself.

### Rav Ashi Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָהוּא, גֵּט יְבָמִין הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: That** bill of divorce **was a levirate bill of divorce** that she received from the brother of her deceased husband and not a standard bill of divorce. She therefore took a vow, and not an oath, in the manner of all widows, as she was demanding payment of her marriage contract from the property of her deceased husband.

Rav Ashi intervenes: *hahu get yavmim hava* — that *get* she received was actually a *get yavmim*, a *get* given by a brother-in-law in the context of *yibum*. What this means is that the woman's husband died and she became subject to *yibum*; the brother-in-law gave her a *get* to prevent the *yibum* from occurring, but the case is in reality one of an *almana*, not a *gerusha*, since it is her first husband who died and from whose estate she is collecting. Tosafot points out that *chalitza* — not a *get* from the brother-in-law — is technically what would finalize her status, and raises the question accordingly. Tosafot's preferred resolution is that the brother-in-law in fact performed *yibum* and then divorced her, making her technically divorced from the *yavam* (the brother-in-law), but since *yibum* is understood as a continuation of the first husband's bond, she collects from the first husband's estate. Either way, her status is ultimately that of an *almana* with respect to the original estate — and the case therefore does not provide the proof one might have thought for allowing a *gerusha* to take a *neder* in lieu of an oath.

## Neder Timing Rules

### Rav Huna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתְקִין רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן שֶׁתְּהֵא נוֹדֶרֶת כּוּ׳: אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁלֹּא נִיסֵּת, אֲבָל נִיסֵּת – אֵין מַדִּירִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted that she should take,** for the benefit of the orphans, **any vow** that the orphans wished to administer to her. **Rav Huna says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where **she did not marry** again; **however,** if **she married** again, **they do not administer a vow to her.**

The *gemara* now turns to the question of when, precisely, the widow may take the *neder* that enables her to collect. Rav Huna rules: *lo shanu ela b'shelo nises* — this applies only when she has not yet remarried. The *takanah* of Rabban Gamliel permitting her to take a *neder* and collect her *kesuba* is available only while she remains unmarried.

### Husband Annuls Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיסֵּת מַאי טַעְמָא – דְּמֵיפַר לַהּ בַּעַל; כִּי לֹא נִיסֵּת נָמֵי, לְכִי מִנַּסְבָא מֵיפַר לַהּ בַּעַל! אֵין הַבַּעַל מֵיפֵר בְּקוֹדְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **What is the reason** that a widow who **married** again may not take a vow in order to collect? It is because of a concern that perhaps she is lying and is not concerned about the vow that she took, as she relies on the fact **that her husband will nullify** her vow. If so, **when she is not married** one should **also** be concerned that she may rely on the fact **that when she will marry** again, **her husband will nullify** her vow. The Gemara answers: The *halakha* is that **the husband does not** have the ability **to nullify with regard to** vows his wife took **prior** to their marriage.

The reason is straightforward: once she has remarried, her husband acquires the right of *hafarah* — the power to annul her vows. If she takes a *neder* while already married to a second husband, he can simply annul it, and the *neder* provides no protection whatsoever. It cannot serve as the required instrument of attestation.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* challenges: but even if she takes the *neder* before remarrying, why does this help? The moment she remarries, her new husband will learn of the *neder* and annul it on the day of their marriage. So even a pre-marriage *neder* would be vulnerable.

### Pre-Existing Vows Safe

The *gemara* answers: *ein haba'al meifer bikodmin* — a husband cannot annul vows that predate the marriage. His power of *hafarah* operates only on vows made during the marriage, or at most on the day he first hears of them if they were made just before the marriage. Vows that were established prior to the marriage relationship are beyond his reach. Therefore, if she takes the *neder* while still single, it will remain binding even after she remarries, and the instrument works as intended.

## Release from Neder

### Chacham Annulment Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא אָזְלָה לְגַבֵּי חָכָם וְשָׁרֵי לַהּ! קָסָבַר צָרִיךְ לְפָרֵט אֶת הַנֶּדֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let us be concerned that perhaps** she in fact received payment of her marriage contract, and she relies on the fact that **she will go to a halakhic authority and he will dissolve** the vow **for her.** The Gemara answers: Rav Huna **holds** that one who wishes to have a vow dissolved **must detail the vow** before the halakhic authority who dissolves it. There is no concern that the halakhic authority, knowing that she vowed in order to collect the payment of the marriage contract, will dissolve it.

The *gemara* raises a further concern: even if remarriage is not a problem — since the husband cannot annul pre-existing vows — what prevents her from going to a *chacham* and obtaining a release from the *neder* through the mechanism of *hatarat nedarim*? Unlike a *shvua* regarding a past event, which either was true or was false at the moment it was taken and admits of no subsequent "release," a *neder* remains technically open to annulment by a *chacham* who finds a legitimate *petach* (opening) for release. If she can simply obtain an annulment of the *neder* after collecting her *kesuba*, the entire instrument is undermined.

### Detailing Requirement

The *gemara*'s answer: *kasavar tzarich lefareit es haneder* — we hold that one must detail the circumstances of the *neder* when seeking release. The requirement of *leprat es haneder* means that the woman must explain to the *chacham* not merely the formal content of the vow but the conditions and circumstances that gave rise to it. The standard *hatarat nedarim* formula recited on *Erev Rosh Hashanah* — where we acknowledge that the *halacha* requires detailing but waive that requirement because the vows are too numerous — reflects awareness of this principle.

### Chacham's Logic

Once the circumstances are detailed, the *chacham* will immediately understand that the *neder* was taken precisely to enable collection of the *kesuba* — it is the very instrument that secured her right to collect. He cannot release her from it without invalidating the basis on which she collected. The release is therefore unavailable. The reasoning depends, of course, on the woman being truthful with the *chacham*. If she lies about the circumstances and the *chacham* annuls based on false information, the annulment would presumably not be valid — a genuinely interesting question, though one the *gemara* does not explore here.

## Rav Nachman Leniency

### Neder B'Rabbim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נִיסֵּת. נִיסֵּת, וַדַּאי מֵיפַר לַהּ בַּעַל! דְּמַדְּרִינַן לַהּ בָּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman** disagreed with Rav Huna and **said: Even** if **she married** again, the orphans can have the court administer a vow to the widow. The Gemara asks: If **she married,** then her **husband will certainly nullify** this vow. The Gemara answers **that we,** the court, **administer the vow in public,** and therefore her husband cannot nullify the vow.

Rav Nachman takes a more lenient position: *afilu nises* — even if she has remarried, she can still take the *neder* and collect. The concern that her husband will annul the *neder* is addressed by ensuring that *demadrinan lah berabbim* — we have her take the *neder* publicly. Once taken in public, the *neder* can no longer be annulled.

### Berabbim Concept

The concept of *berabbim* rests on the principle that once a matter has become public, the private mechanisms of annulment no longer apply. A *neder* taken publicly is no longer a private affair susceptible to personal retraction; it belongs, in a sense, to the public record.

### Berabbim Types

The *gemara* distinguishes this from a related but distinct concept: *al da'as rabbim*. The *berabbim* principle holds that once a vow is publicly pronounced, the private mechanisms of annulment are structurally inapplicable — it is as if the vow has been "published." The *al da'as rabbim* principle is different: it means the vow is made not merely on the basis of the individual's own intent, but on the basis of what the community would desire for the conditions of the vow. This makes it resistant to annulment because the annulment would require addressing not just the individual's own circumstances but the communal understanding. These are two different tools, and the *gemara* will draw on both.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: נִיסֵּת – גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ אִם נָדְרָה. מַאי, לָאו נָדְרָה הַשְׁתָּא? לָא, דִּנְדַרָה מֵעִיקָּרָא. וְהָתַנְיָא: נִיסֵּת – נוֹדֶרֶת וְגוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: נֶדֶר שֶׁהוּדַּר בָּרַבִּים – יֵשׁ לוֹ הֲפָרָה; וְאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אֵין לוֹ הֲפָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Huna from a *baraita*: In a case where **she married** again, **she collects** payment of **her marriage contract if she has taken a vow. What, is it not** the case that **she takes a vow now,** after she has remarried? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is possible to explain that it is referring to **when she took a vow initially,** before remarrying. The Gemara raises another difficulty for Rav Huna: **But isn't it taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: If **she married** again, **she takes a vow and collects** payment of **her marriage contract.** Here, it is clear that she takes the vow after remarrying. The Gemara answers: **This is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as there is one who says: A vow that was taken in public has** the possibility of **nullification** by the husband, and therefore, even if the widow takes the vow in public, her husband can nullify it. As a result, she can collect payment of her marriage contract only if she takes a vow before she remarries. **And there is one who says:** A vow that was taken in public **does not have** the possibility of **nullification.** Therefore, even after the widow remarries, she is still able to take a vow and collect payment, as she takes the vow in public.

The *gemara* cites *baraisa* support for Rav Nachman's position. One *baraisa* states that if she has remarried — *nises govah kesuvashah im nadrah* — she collects her *kesuba* if she made a *neder*, which the *gemara* initially reads as meaning she made the vow after remarrying, and thus supporting Rav Nachman. A second *baraisa* is more explicit: *nises noderes v'govah kesuvashah* — she remarried, she takes a vow now, and she collects. Both *baraisas* clearly support the view that a post-marriage *neder* can work.

### Tannaitic Debate

The *gemara* resolves the tension between the two positions — Rav Huna's that she must be unmarried and Rav Nachman's that she can be married — by declaring *tannai hi*: this is a dispute among *tannaim*. The dispute hinges on a fundamental question in the laws of *nedarim*: does a *neder shehuddar berabbim yesh lo hafarah* — can a vow taken publicly be annulled — or *ein lo hafarah* — is it definitively beyond annulment? The opinion that she can take a *neder* even after remarrying must hold that a public *neder* cannot be annulled; the opinion that she cannot must hold that even public vows remain technically open to annulment, and the husband's power therefore remains a threat. The *beis din* context itself suffices to make the vow "public" — Rashi specifies *ba'asarah*, before ten, and in the *beis din* setting this is easily achieved.

## Detailing Debate

### Rav Nachman: No Detail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: צָרִיךְ לְפָרֵט אֶת הַנֶּדֶר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ; רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: צָרִיךְ. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ – דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ צָרִיךְ, זִימְנִין דְּגָיֵיז לֵיהּ לְדִיבּוּרֵיהּ, וְחָכָם מַאי דְּשָׁמַע מֵיפַר.
>
> **Translation:** § Since Rav Huna's statement included the fact that one who requests that a halakhic authority dissolve his vow must detail the vow, the Gemara mentions that **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does one who comes to a halakhic authority and requests that he dissolve his vow **need to detail the vow, or does he not need** to do so? **Rav Naḥman says: He does not need** to detail the vow. **Rav Pappa says: He needs** to detail the vow. The Gemara explains each one's reasoning: **Rav Naḥman says** that **he does not need** to detail the vow, **as if you say** that **he needs** to do so, **sometimes** the person who took the vow **will cut short his statement** and not provide all of the details of the vow, **and the halakhic authority dissolves** only **what he hears** and does not dissolve the vow in its entirety. Nevertheless, the one who took the vow will act as though the vow has been dissolved entirely. Therefore, it is preferable that he just report that he took a vow, and the halakhic authority will dissolve it entirely, whatever it is.

The *gemara* now presents a direct dispute between Rav Nachman and Rav Papa on whether *leprat es haneder* — detailing the circumstances of the vow — is required when seeking release from a *chacham*. Rav Nachman holds: *eino tzarich* — one does not need to detail the circumstances. His reasoning is given in the negative: if you required detailing, *zimnin degayiz lei lediburei* — sometimes a person will abbreviate and not give the full circumstances, *vechacham bemai deshama meifer* — and the *chacham* will annul based only on what he heard, which may be incomplete. If the *chacham* annuls based on a partial account, the annulment may be technically valid even though the full picture would have led him to refuse. By not requiring detail, you avoid the problem of partial disclosures generating flawed annulments.

### False Release Risk

But this raises an obvious concern: if no detailing is required, then a woman who took a *neder* to enable collection of her *kesuba* could subsequently go to a *chacham*, give him no specifics, and obtain a release without his knowing the *neder* was the instrument of her collection. This is precisely the scenario the *gemara* had earlier invoked as a reason to worry about the effectiveness of the *neder* mechanism. Under Rav Nachman, the answer to this concern is the *berabbim* solution — the vow is taken publicly and is therefore not open to annulment, regardless of whether details are given.

### Rav Papa: Detail Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר צָרִיךְ – מִשּׁוּם מִילְּתָא דְּאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says** that **he needs** to detail the vow, **because** the vow might concern **a matter that** is **prohibited,** such as the case of the mishna here where it is essential that the vow not be dissolved, as the purpose of the vow is to ensure that the widow will not lie. In such a case, if the halakhic authority is not aware of the circumstances that prompted the widow to take the vow, he could mistakenly dissolve it.

Rav Papa disagrees: *tzarich* — one must detail the circumstances, *mishum milta de'issura* — because without the details, the *chacham* may release someone from a vow that was serving as a safeguard against a transgression. The illustration is sharp: if a person took a *neder* not to walk down a certain street in order to avoid the temptation of a forbidden establishment, and then goes to a *chacham* and simply says "I have a *neder* not to walk down such-and-such street" without explaining why, the *chacham* may well annul it — thereby inadvertently removing the person's protection against sin. Without knowing the purpose of the vow, the *chacham* cannot properly evaluate whether annulment is appropriate. In the *kesuba* context, the same logic applies: without knowing that the *neder* was taken to enable collection of the *kesuba*, the *chacham* cannot know that annulment would allow the woman to retain money she does not deserve.

## Kohein's Neder Case

### Kohein's Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַנּוֹשֵׂא נָשִׁים בַּעֲבֵירָה – פָּסוּל, עַד שֶׁיִּדּוֹר הֲנָאָה. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: נוֹדֵר וְעוֹבֵד, יוֹרֵד וּמְגָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to bring a proof that one must detail the vow: **We learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 45b): A priest **who marries women in transgression** of a prohibition is **disqualified** from taking part in the Temple service **until he takes a vow** not to derive **benefit** from his wives, thereby requiring him to divorce them. **And it is taught with regard to this: He takes a vow and** immediately **serves** in the Temple. **He** then **descends** from the service **and divorces** his wives.

The *gemara* adduces a parallel case from the laws of *kehuna* to illuminate the same underlying tension. The *mishna* states: *hanosei nashim ba-aveira pasul* — a *kohen* who has married a woman he is forbidden to marry is disqualified from performing the *avoda* in the *Beis Hamikdash*. The mechanism to restore his eligibility is: *ad sheyidor hana'ah* — until he takes a *neder* forbidding himself any benefit from his wife, which will effectively compel him to divorce her since the marriage becomes untenable. Once he has taken that *neder*, a related *baraisa* states: *noder ve-oved* — he takes the vow and performs the *avoda*. He does his *avoda*, descends from the *mizbe'ach*, and then divorces her.

### Parallel Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְפָרֵט אֶת הַנֶּדֶר, לֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא אָזֵיל לְגַבֵּי חָכָם וְשָׁרֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **he does not need to detail the vow,** then **let us be concerned lest he go to a halakhic authority and** the halakhic authority **dissolve** the vow **for him.** He would then remain married to the women who are prohibited to him, and serve in the Temple despite being disqualified from doing so.

The same question arises here: if one does not need to detail the circumstances of a vow, what prevents this *kohen* from going to a *chacham*, obtaining release from his *neder* without disclosing that the whole point was to secure his divorce from a forbidden wife, and thereby escaping both the obligation to divorce and the disqualification from *avoda*? The mechanism by which the *neder* was supposed to guarantee his behavior becomes hollow if it can be quietly annulled.

### Same Solution Applied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמַדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ בְּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers **that we administer the vow** to the priest **in public,** and a public vow cannot be annulled.

The *gemara* gives the same answer: *demadrinan lah berabbim* — we ensure that the *neder* is taken publicly, and a public vow cannot be annulled. The parallel reinforces the point that the two mechanisms — *leprat es haneder* and *berabbim* — are the twin pillars on which the effectiveness of these vows depends.

## Neder Protection Methods

### Detail Method

The two mechanisms that protect a *neder* from being circumvented operate in different contexts. The first — *leprat es haneder*, requiring that the circumstances be detailed when seeking release from a *chacham* — ensures that the *chacham* has full information and can decline to annul when doing so would enable a transgression or undermine the purpose for which the vow was taken.

### Berabbim Method

The second — taking the *neder berabbim*, in public — removes the vow from the domain of private arrangements amenable to personal retraction. A vow made in public is no longer susceptible to annulment by a *chacham* (according to the view that *ein lo hafarah*) and certainly not by a husband.

### Context Application

The two mechanisms are not interchangeable in all contexts. Where the concern is a *chacham* annulling the vow, either mechanism can in principle address the problem: requiring detail will prevent a well-intentioned *chacham* from annulling without full information, and a *berabbim* vow forecloses the option entirely.

### Husband Annulment

But where the concern is the husband exercising his power of *hafarah*, the *leprat es haneder* mechanism is simply irrelevant. The husband does not need to be informed of the circumstances or reasons for the vow; his right of annulment is a marital prerogative that operates independently of his knowledge of the vow's purpose. In that context, only the *berabbim* solution works: by taking the *neder* publicly, the wife removes it from the reach of the husband's unilateral power of annulment. We will continue to explore what types of vows can or cannot be annulled as we proceed in the sugya.
