# Kiddushin 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Torah Sources

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֶבֶד עִבְרִי נִקְנֶה בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר, וְקוֹנֶה עַצְמוֹ בְּשָׁנִים, וּבַיּוֹבֵל, וּבְגִרְעוֹן כֶּסֶף. יְתֵירָה עָלָיו אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה שֶׁקּוֹנָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ בְּסִימָנִין. הַנִּרְצָע, נִקְנֶה בִּרְצִיעָה, וְקוֹנֶה אֶת עַצְמוֹ בַּיּוֹבֵל, וּבְמִיתַת הָאָדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A Hebrew slave** can be **acquired** by his master **through money or through a document, and** he can **acquire himself,** i.e., he is emancipated, **through years,** i.e., when he completes his six years of labor, **or through the** advent of the **Jubilee** Year, **or through the deduction of money.** The slave can redeem himself during the six years by paying for his remaining years of slavery. **A Hebrew maidservant** has one mode of emancipation **more than him, as she acquires herself through signs** indicating puberty. A slave **who is pierced** after serving six years is **acquired** as a slave for a longer period **through piercing** his ear with an awl, **and** he **acquires himself through the** advent of the **Jubilee** Year **or through** the **death of the master.**

The present daf picks up at the bottom of Kiddushin 14b, at the line *mai lav beha kamipalgi*. We have moved on from the *kinyan* of a woman and a *yevama* — topics bound up with marriage, though addressed in the context of *kinyanim* — and we now turn to the *kinyanim* of other persons. We are not yet dealing with the acquisition of a non-Jewish slave (*eved kna'ani*), where the person is treated more fully as property while retaining human status; that discussion lies ahead. Rather, we are concerned with the *eved ivri* and the *amah ivriyah* — the Hebrew slave — where ownership is not full ownership of the person as such, but ownership *lema'aseh yadav*, of the person's labor.

The *mishna* established that the way an *eved ivri* is acquired is through *kesef*. The *gemara* demonstrated that this applies both to the case of someone sold by the court and to someone who sells himself. Specifically: one can observe the *kesef* mechanism in the case of a daughter sold by her father (itself related to the case of *makhruhu beit din*, sold by the court, because the man stolen and sold to repay his theft), and one can transfer this to the case of *mokhher atzmo*, self-sale, through a *gezeirah shavah*. That background was established on the previous daf.

### Three Contexts

The Torah discusses the sale of Jewish slaves in essentially three locations. The opening of *Mishpatim* addresses the *amah ivriyah* — the minor girl sold by her father — and *hanimkar bignivato* — the man sold because he burgled and cannot repay. *Devarim* continues the discussion of the male slave who goes free after six years, and the various laws attending his release, including the ladening with gifts. Finally, in *Behar* at the end of *Vayikra*, within the broader framework of *shmita* and the jubilee year, the Torah discusses a person who sells himself — either to a fellow Jew or to a non-Jew.

### Categories & Link

These sources give rise to two principal categories: *makhruhu beit din*, the case in *Mishpatim* and *Devarim*, and *mokhher atzmo*, the case in *Behar*. The *gemara* asked: how do we know that the rules derived from *makhruhu beit din* — such as acquisition through *kesef* — carry over to the case of *mokhher atzmo*? The answer introduced was the *gezeirah shavah* of *sakhir sakhir*: both cases describe the slave as being like a hired hand, and this terminological link allows us to equate the two categories and transfer *halachot* between them. However, the *gemara* immediately complicated this by noting that if *sakhir sakhir* is disputed, we might need a different source. The *gemara* explored an alternative derivation based on the case of sale to a non-Jew. This is the background. The daf now focuses on the central question: do we in fact make this *sakhir sakhir* link between *mokhher atzmo* and *makhruhu beit din*?

## Tanna Kamma Distinctions

### Gezeirah Shavah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְנׇכְרִי, הוֹאִיל וְכׇל קִנְיָנוֹ בְּכֶסֶף, נִמְכָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמְּגָרַעַת פִּדְיוֹנָהּ, וְיוֹצְאָה. אַשְׁכְּחַן אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה, הוֹאִיל וּמִיקַּדְּשָׁה בְּכֶסֶף – מִיקַּנְיָא בְּכֶסֶף, עֶבֶד עִבְרִי מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִמָּכֵר לְךָ אָחִיךָ הָעִבְרִי אוֹ הָעִבְרִיָּה וַעֲבָדְךָ שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים״ – מַקִּישׁ עִבְרִי לְעִבְרִיָּה. אַשְׁכְּחַן מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין, הוֹאִיל וְנִמְכָּר בְּעַל כּוֹרְחוֹ, מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ מְנָלַן? יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Although **we found** this *halakha* with regard to **a Hebrew maidservant,** one cannot extrapolate from there to this case, as it is possible that it applies only to a female. The reason for this is that **since** she is ordinarily **betrothed through money,** she can also be **acquired** as a maidservant **through money. From where do we** derive that **a Hebrew slave** can likewise be acquired through money? The Gemara answers that the **verse states: "If your brother, a Hebrew man, or a Hebrew woman is sold to you, and he shall serve you six years"** (Deuteronomy 15:12). This verse **juxtaposes a Hebrew man to a Hebrew woman,** indicating that the modes through which they are acquired are the same. The Gemara further asks: **We found** a source in this verse for **a slave who is sold by the court.** This verse is referring to a thief who is unable to repay the value of his theft and is sold as a slave so that he can repay his debt. One can therefore argue that this case is unique, **since** he is **sold against his will. From where do we** derive that one who **sells himself** can be sold through money? **One derives** this through a verbal analogy between the terms **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker."**

The *gemara* frames the discussion as a potential dispute over the *gezeirah shavah* of *sakhir sakhir*. The *tanna kamma* of a *baraita* cited at the end of the previous daf enumerated numerous differences between *makhruhu beit din* and *mokhher atzmo* as they appear in the *pesukim*. The *gemara* now asks: is this dispute really about whether to make the *gezeirah shavah* at all — that is, does the *tanna kamma* reject the equation of the two categories root and branch, while Rabbi Eliezer accepts it?

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ נִמְכָּר לְשֵׁשׁ, וְיָתֵר עַל שֵׁשׁ, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר אֶלָּא לְשֵׁשׁ. הַמּוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – נִרְצָע. מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵין מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ. הַמּוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵין רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה אֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר אֶלָּא לְשֵׁשׁ, זֶה וָזֶה נִרְצָע, וָזֶה וָזֶה מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ, וְזֶה וָזֶה רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* teaches: One who **sells himself** as a slave **is sold for six** years, **and** if he wishes he can be sold for **more than six** years, whereas one who is **sold by the court is sold only for six** years, but no more. The *baraita* adds that there are additional differences between these two slaves: **One who sells himself may not be pierced** with an awl, whereas one who is **sold by the court may be pierced** with an awl. One who **sells himself is not granted** a severance gift by his master when he is emancipated, whereas one who is **sold by the court is granted** a severance gift. With regard to **one who sells himself, his master may not provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant** as a wife to produce slave children, whereas with regard to one **sold by the court, his master may provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant.** **Rabbi Elazar says** that there is no difference between these two types of slaves. Rather, both **this** slave **and that** slave may be **sold for only six** years; both **this and that** one may be **pierced** with an awl, both **this and that** one **are granted** a severance gift, and in both **this** case **and that** case **his master may provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant.**

The *baraita* sets out the position of the *tanna kamma* alongside that of Rabbi Eliezer. The *tanna kamma* holds that a series of *halachot* mentioned in the Torah in connection with *makhruhu beit din* do not apply to *mokhher atzmo*: the six-year limit on servitude, the rite of ear-piercing when the slave declares *ahavti et adoni*, the obligation to laden the slave with gifts upon his release, and the master's permission to give the slave a non-Jewish maidservant with whom to cohabit and produce children (the case associated with *ahavti et adoni et ishti ve'et banai*). According to the *tanna kamma*, none of these apply to *mokhher atzmo*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that they do apply, and the *gemara* initially reasons that Rabbi Eliezer must be relying on the *sakhir sakhir* equation to transfer these *halachot* from one category to the other, while the *tanna kamma* does not accept that equation.

## Sakhir Sakhir Debate

### Tavyumi on Abaye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יָלֵיף ״שְׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְהָכָא בְּהַאי קְרָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּאָמַר מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ נִמְכָּר לְשֵׁשׁ וְיָתֵר עַל שֵׁשׁ? מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין: ״וַעֲבָדְךָ שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים״ – זֶה, וְלֹא מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Tavyumei said in the name of Abaye:** That is not so; rather, **everyone derives** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," and here,** in the dispute in the *baraita*, **they disagree about this** following **verse. What is the reasoning** of **the first *tanna*, who says** that one who **sells himself is sold for six** years **and more than six** years? He maintains that with regard to one **sold by the court, the Merciful One excludes** a certain case by the verse: "If your brother, a Hebrew man, or a Hebrew woman, is sold to you, **and he shall serve you six years"** (Deuteronomy 15:12). That teaches that only **this** type of slave serves for six years and no more, but the same does **not** apply to one who **sells himself.**

The *gemara* now resists this framing. Rav Tavyumi cites Abaye: in fact, everyone may fundamentally hold of the *gezeirah shavah* of *sakhir sakhir*. The *sakhir sakhir* link may well be universally accepted — indeed, it was already used to establish that acquisition by *kesef* applies to *mokhher atzmo*, and perhaps other *halachot* are derived from it as well. The dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer need not be about whether to make the equation at all. Rather, it may be about how to read specific limiting words within each of the relevant *pesukim*.

## Tanna Kamma Distinctions (Continued)

### Six Year Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ: ״וַעֲבָדְךָ״ – לְךָ, וְלֹא לְיוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And the other,** Rabbi Elazar, learns a different *halakha* from this verse. **"And he shall serve you"** means that he works **for you and not for an heir** other than a son. If a master has no sons, his slave is not transferred as an inheritance to a daughter or to a brother like his other property. If he does have sons, they inherit a Hebrew slave.

The *gemara* proceeds to work through each of the four differences in turn. It begins: what is the *tanna kamma*'s reason for holding that *mokhher atzmo* is not sold for six years — that is, that the six-year limit on servitude applies only to *makhruhu beit din*? The answer: the Torah says regarding *makhruhu beit din*, *ve-avadcha sheish shanim* — "and he shall serve *you* six years." The word *avadcha* — "your slave," with the possessive suffix *cha* — functions as a limiting word (*miut*), restricting the law to this case and not extending it to *mokhher atzmo*.

## Sakhir Sakhir Debate (Continued)

### Reframing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר כְּשֶׁהוּא עֶבֶד.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Elazar, who does not derive this ruling in the same manner as the first *tanna*, **requires** this verse for a different *halakha*: A slave may declare that he wishes to be pierced **provided that he says** this **when he is** still **a slave.** After he has been emancipated, he can no longer say that he wishes to remain with his master.

This brings the *gemara* to its reframing of the entire dispute. The disagreement between *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer is not about whether the *sakhir sakhir gezeirah shavah* exists. Both may accept the fundamental equation. The question is instead how to interpret the limiting words embedded in the relevant *pesukim*. The *tanna kamma* reads each limiting word as excluding *mokhher atzmo*; Rabbi Eliezer reads each of those same words as conveying a different legal point while leaving *mokhher atzmo* included.

## Tanna Kamma Distinctions (Continued Again)

### Eliezer on Avadcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: לוֹ, וְלֹא לְיוֹרְשָׁיו. יוֹרְשָׁיו אַמַּאי לָא? ״שָׂכִיר״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, מָה שָׂכִיר פְּעוּלָּתוֹ לְיוֹרְשָׁיו – אַף הַאי פְּעוּלָּתוֹ לְיוֹרְשָׁיו! אֶלָּא: לוֹ, וְלֹא לְבַעַל חוֹבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **the other** Sage, the first *tanna*, derive from that verse? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** verse to teach the following: **"To him"** means that one grants a severance gift only to the slave himself **but not to his heirs.** The Gemara asks: **Why not** give this gift **to his heirs?** After all, **the Merciful One calls** a Hebrew slave **"a hired worker." Just as** payment for **the labor of a hired worker** is given **to his heirs** when he dies, **so too this** severance gift for **his labor** should be given **to his heirs** when he dies. **Rather,** this verse teaches **"to him," but not to** the slave's **creditor.** One to whom the slave owes money may not collect the slave's severance gift as repayment of the debt.

What does Rabbi Eliezer do with *avadcha*? He reads it not as excluding *mokhher atzmo* but as addressing the question of inheritance: *avadcha* — he serves *you*, not your heir. The *halacha* is that a slave does serve the master's son, since that is understood as a continuation of the father's ownership; but the slave would not be transferred to a brother, a father, or any other type of heir. This, Rabbi Eliezer argues, is the more natural reading of the word *avadcha* — it is restricting transmission to collateral heirs, not excluding the category of *mokhher atzmo*.

The *gemara* then addresses the fact that the word *avadcha* appears more than once in the relevant passage. The phrase *mishneh sechar sakhir avadcha sheish shanim* — "a double wage of a hired hand, he served you six years" — is invoked. The *tanna kamma* might argue this second *avadcha* is an additional limiting phrase. Rabbi Eliezer responds that this second *avadcha* is not generating a new *halacha* at all; rather, it is the Torah addressing the master, reminding him how much work the slave performed on his behalf so that the master should not begrudge giving the parting gifts. It is a moral exhortation to the master, not a legal restriction.

### Ear-Piercing Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ? הָהִיא – לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לִרְצִיעָה שֶׁהִיא בְּאֹזֶן יָמָנִית? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״אֹזֶן״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״אֹזֶן״. מָה לְהַלָּן יָמִין – אַף כָּאן יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Elazar, would say: **That** term, "his ear," **comes for a verbal analogy. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: From where** is it derived that the **piercing** of a Hebrew slave's ear with an awl is performed **on the right ear? It is stated: Ear, here,** with regard to a Hebrew slave, **and it is stated there,** with regard to the ritual purification of a leper: "The right **ear** of he who is to be purified" (Leviticus 14:14). **Just as there,** with regard to a leper, it specifies the **right** ear explicitly, **so too here,** the piercing of a slave must be performed on the **right** ear.

The *gemara* turns to the second difference: ear-piercing (*retzi'ah*). Why does the *tanna kamma* hold that *mokhher atzmo* cannot undergo the ear-piercing ceremony? The Torah says, in the context of *makhruhu beit din*: *ve-ratza adonav et ozno ba-martzei'a* — "his master shall pierce his ear with an awl." The word *ozno* — "his ear," with the possessive suffix — is the limiting word: *ozno shelo velo ozno shel mokhher atzmo*. It applies to this case and not to the case of self-sale. Thus the *tanna kamma* holds that *retzi'ah* is restricted to *makhruhu beit din*.

### Ozno Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ? אִם כֵּן לֵימָא קְרָא ״אֹזֶן״, מַאי ״אׇזְנוֹ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** Sage, the first *tanna*, derive that this piercing can be performed only on the right ear? The Gemara answers: He would argue that **if it is so** that the word "ear" is stated only for the sake of the verbal analogy, **let the verse say** merely: **Ear,** and one would learn the *halakha* through a verbal analogy from the case of the leper's ear. **What** is the reason that it states **"his ear"?** This serves to teach that one who sells himself may not be pierced.

Rabbi Eliezer, however, reads *ozno* not as a limiting word excluding *mokhher atzmo* but as the source for a *gezeirah shavah* — specifically, the link between the ear mentioned here and the ear mentioned in the context of the *metzora*, where the ritual is performed on the right ear. Rabbi Eliezer taught in a *baraita*: how do we know that the ear-piercing of the slave is performed on the right ear? Because it says here *ozen* and it says there (by the *metzora*) *ozen*, and just as there it is the right ear, so too here it is the right ear. This is why the Torah wrote *ozen* — to generate the *gezeirah shavah*.

This raises an obvious objection: if the *gezeirah shavah* is derived from the word *ozen* (without the suffix), then the limiting function was supposed to be carried by *ozno* (with the suffix *vav*). The *gemara* addresses this: if that were so, the Torah could simply have written *ozen* — why write *ozno*? The additional letter tells us something, and the *tanna kamma* says it excludes *mokhher atzmo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״לֹא תֵּצֵא כָּעֲבָדִים״, מַאי ״כְּצֵאת הָעֲבָדִים״? – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, the first *tanna*, from where does he derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: **"But if the slave shall say"** (Exodus 21:5). This indicates that piercing applies to **the slave but not** to **a maidservant.**

Rabbi Eliezer would reply that *ozno* is needed for a different exclusion: *ozno velo oznah* — his ear and not her ear, meaning the rite applies to the male *eved ivri* sold by *beit din* and not to the *amah ivriyah* sold by her father. The *tanna kamma* can extract the exclusion of the *amah* from elsewhere — from *v'im yomar ha'eved*, where the word *eved* (masculine) already excludes the female — and so the *vav* of *ozno* is free to exclude *mokhher atzmo*. Alternatively, the *tanna kamma* derives "while he is still a slave" from *eved*, and derives "not the *amah*" from *ha'eved* (the definite article), leaving *ozno* to exclude *mokhher atzmo*. The back-and-forth turns on precisely how many exclusions each word can carry and which readings of the text are considered operative.

### Gifting Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּאָמַר מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵין מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ? מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין: ״הַעֲנֵיק תַּעֲנִיק לוֹ״ – לוֹ, וְלֹא לְמוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies additional details with regard to this dispute: **What is the reasoning of the first *tanna*, who says** that **one does not grant** a severance gift **to one who sells himself? With regard to one sold by the court, the Merciful One excludes** a certain case by the verse: **"You shall give a severance gift to him"** (Deuteronomy 15:14). **"To him"** means to one who is sold by the court, **but not to one who sells himself.**

The *gemara* arrives at the third difference: the obligation to laden the slave with gifts (*ha'aneik ta'anik lo*) upon his release. What is the *tanna kamma*'s reason for holding that this does not apply to *mokhher atzmo*? The Torah says by *makhruhu beit din*: *ha'aneik ta'anik lo* — "you shall surely laden him." The word *lo* — "him" — is a limiting word: *lo velo le-mokhher atzmo*. It applies to this one and not to the person who sold himself.

This is a striking observation: the man sold by *beit din* was a *ganav*, a thief; yet he receives the parting gifts, while the person who chose to sell himself does not. The conceptual distinction behind this collection of differences is worth pausing on, though the *gemara* does not spell it out here.

### Wages Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: לוֹ, וְלֹא לְיוֹרְשָׁיו. יוֹרְשָׁיו אַמַּאי לָא? ״שָׂכִיר״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, מָה שָׂכִיר פְּעוּלָּתוֹ לְיוֹרְשָׁיו – אַף הַאי פְּעוּלָּתוֹ לְיוֹרְשָׁיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **the other** Sage, the first *tanna*, derive from that verse? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** verse to teach the following: **"To him"** means that one grants a severance gift only to the slave himself **but not to his heirs.** The Gemara asks: **Why not** give this gift **to his heirs?** After all, **the Merciful One calls** a Hebrew slave **"a hired worker." Just as** payment for **the labor of a hired worker** is given **to his heirs** when he dies, **so too this** severance gift for **his labor** should be given **to his heirs** when he dies.

The *gemara* then raises a complication with a different reading of *lo*. One might suggest that *lo* comes not to exclude *mokhher atzmo* but to exclude the heirs: *lo velo leyorshav* — "to him and not to his heirs." If the master dies before giving the gifts, his estate has no obligation; the gifts are personal to the slave. But the *gemara* challenges this: the Torah itself describes the slave as a *sakhir*, a hired hand — and wages owed to a hired hand do pass to his heirs. The *gemara* reasons: just as a worker's wages go to his heirs, so too here, *mah sakhir ploni leyorshav af hai ploni leyorshav*. The *gemara* is thus unwilling to read *lo* as excluding the heirs, since the Torah's own framing of the slave's work as hired labor would require the gifts to be treated as wages and to devolve to the heirs.

Interestingly, the Gra excises this entire passage. Rashi has it, Tosafot has it, but the Gra evidently holds that the gifts are not wages the slave has earned and to which he is entitled; they are a special Torah-mandated award. Therefore, they would not pass to the heirs. But our *gemara* insists: the Torah says the slave worked for you like a hired hand, he worked double, and therefore these gifts are treated as wages that would pass to the heirs.

### Lo Creditor Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לוֹ, וְלֹא לְבַעַל חוֹבוֹ. מִדִּסְבִירָא לַן בְּעָלְמָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן, דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן: מִנַּיִן לַנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵרוֹ מָנֶה, וַחֲבֵרוֹ בַּחֲבֵרוֹ – מִנַּיִן שֶׁמּוֹצִיאִין מִזֶּה וְנוֹתְנִין לָזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנָתַן לַאֲשֶׁר אָשַׁם לוֹ״. אֲתָא ״לוֹ״ לְאַפּוֹקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this verse teaches **"to him," but not to** the slave's **creditor.** One to whom the slave owes money may not collect the slave's severance gift as repayment of the debt. The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to emphasize that a creditor has no rights to the severance gift? That is necessary due to the fact **that we generally hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: From where** is it derived **that** in the case of **one who lends one hundred dinars to another, and the other** lends a similar sum **to** yet **another,** a third person, **from where** is it derived **that** the court **appropriates** the money **from this one,** the third person, **and gives** it **to that one,** the first creditor, without going through the second person, who owes money to the first and is owed that same amount by the third? **The verse states: "And he shall give it to him whom he has wronged"** (Numbers 5:7), which indicates that the loan should be repaid to the creditor to whom the money is ultimately owed. Therefore, the term **"to him" comes to exclude** this possibility, as the severance gift is given to the slave and not to his creditor.

The *gemara* therefore proposes a different understanding of *lo*: *lo velo le-ba'al chovo* — "to him and not to his creditor." Unsurprisingly, a man in this situation likely has other creditors. One might have thought — given that we generally hold like Rabbi Natan — that a creditor of the slave could approach the master directly and demand that the gifts be transferred straight to him, bypassing the slave. As the *braita* of Rabbi Natan teaches: if Reuven owes Shimon a hundred, and Shimon owes Levi a hundred, Levi can go directly to Reuven and demand payment — and the verse *venatan la'asher asham lo* ("and he shall give it to the one to whom he is liable") supports this. Tosafot has an extended discussion about whether we *pasken* that way.

The *gemara* says *lo* comes to tell us: in this case, the creditor cannot make that demand. The gifts must pass through the slave's own hands. There is a significance to the act of giving and receiving these gifts — *lo*, specifically to him — and the creditor cannot short-circuit that by seizing them from the master directly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? בְּעָלְמָא נָמֵי לָא סְבִירָא לַן כְּרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **the other** Sage, the first *tanna*, respond to this claim? He maintains that **we generally do not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan.** Consequently, this exposition is unnecessary.

Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, uses *lo* to address this creditor problem rather than to exclude *mokhher atzmo*. He thereby applies the parting gift obligation to *mokhher atzmo* as well. The *tanna kamma*, who uses *lo* to exclude *mokhher atzmo*, must explain differently why the creditor cannot intercept: he simply does not hold of Rabbi Natan's principle altogether — *be'alma nami lo sevira lan ke-Rabbi Natan* — and therefore needs no exclusion.

### Maidservant Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּאָמַר מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵין רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית? מַיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין: ״אִם אֲדֹנָיו יִתֶּן לוֹ אִשָּׁה״ – לוֹ, וְלֹא לְמוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **What is the reasoning of the first *tanna*, who says** that if **one sells himself his master does not provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant?** The Gemara answers: **With regard to one sold by the court, the Merciful One excludes** a certain case by the verse: **"If his master give to him a wife"** (Exodus 21:4). This serves to emphasize **"to him" but not to one who sells himself.**

The fourth and final difference: the master's ability to give the slave a non-Jewish maidservant (*shifcha kena'anit*) with whom to cohabit and produce children. The Torah says by *makhruhu beit din*: *im adonav yiten lo isha* — "if his master gives *him* a wife." Again the word *lo* is the limiting word: *lo velo le-mokhher atzmo*. The master can do this only in the case of *makhruhu beit din*.

### Double Wage & Force

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? לוֹ – בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ. וְאִידַּךְ? מִ״כִּי מִשְׁנֶה שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר״ נָפְקָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּי מִשְׁנֶה שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר עֲבָדְךָ״ – שָׂכִיר אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵד אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, עֶבֶד עִבְרִי עוֹבֵד בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Elazar, explains: **"To him"** means even **against his will.** **And** from where does **the other** Sage, the first *tanna*, derive that the slave must remain with this maidservant against his will? **He derives** it **from** the verse: **"For double of the hire of a hired worker** he has served you" (Deuteronomy 15:18). **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that this verse: **"For double of the hire of a hired worker he has served you,"** indicates that **a hired worker works only during the day,** whereas **a Hebrew slave works both during the day and at night.**

Rabbi Eliezer reads *lo* here differently: *lo be'al korcho* — even against his will. That is, *lo* is not excluding *mokhher atzmo*; it is telling us that the master can compel the slave to cohabit with the maidservant even if he is unwilling. This is obviously morally troubling from any contemporary vantage point, but the *gemara* proceeds with the analysis.

How does Rabbi Eliezer know the master can compel this? From a different *pasuk*, as a *braita* teaches: *ki mishneh sechar sakhir avadcha sheish shanim* — "for a double wage of a hired hand, he served you six years." What does "double wage" mean? A regular hired hand works only during the day; an *eved ivri* works both day and night, thus performing a double wage's worth of labor. Does this literally mean the slave works around the clock? The *gemara* itself rejects this: the Torah says *ki tov lo imach* — that it should be good for him in your household, including equal provisions and housing. Rabbi Yitzchak therefore explains: working "at night" means the slave works for the master's benefit at night through cohabiting with the maidservant to produce slave children for the master's household. That is the "night work" — and it is this *pasuk* that tells Rabbi Eliezer you may compel him (*be'al korcho*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תַעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁעֶבֶד עִבְרִי עוֹבֵד בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״ – עִמְּךָ בַּמַּאֲכָל, עִמְּךָ בַּמִּשְׁתֶּה. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִכָּאן שֶׁרַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And can it enter your mind that a Hebrew slave** actually **works both during the day and at night? But isn't it already stated: "Because he fares well with you"** (Deuteronomy 15:16), which teaches that he must be **with you in food** and **with you in drink?** All of the slave's needs must be fulfilled, and his living conditions must be equal to those of the master himself. If so, he cannot be forced to work under unreasonable conditions. **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says** in explanation of this *halakha*: **From here** it is derived **that his master may provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant** against his will to produce children for the master. This is the service he performs at night.

The *tanna kamma* responds: if that were the only source, I would have read *mishneh sechar sakhir* as establishing only that the cohabitation can occur — but perhaps only if the slave is willing. The word *lo* therefore comes to tell you that it can be done even against his will. And since *lo* carries that legal force, it cannot simultaneously serve to exclude *mokhher atzmo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ, אֲבָל בְּעַל כֻּרְחֵיהּ אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Elazar, would say that this is no proof, because **if** it is derived **from there** alone **I would say: This matter applies** only **with his consent; but** as for his master forcing him to live with a maidservant **against his will, I would say no,** he cannot do this. Therefore, the term "to him" **teaches us** that the master can even provide him with a Canaanite maidservant against his will.

Tosafot raises the question: if *lo* tells us it can be done against his will, what is *mishneh sechar sakhir* teaching? Tosafot answers — in parallel to what was said earlier about *hartza'at adon* — that it is not generating a new *halacha* but rather reminding the master of the slave's substantial work, so that the master does not begrudge giving the parting gifts. According to the *tanna kamma*, by contrast, *mishneh sechar sakhir* does establish the permissibility of compelled cohabitation, and *lo* restricts the entire provision to *makhruhu beit din*.

## Sakhir Sakhir Debate (Continued Again)

### Fundamental Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מַאן תַּנָּא דְּלָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״? הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁב אֶל מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״ אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: בַּמָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? אִי בְּמוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר!
>
> **Translation:** § According to the above explanation, both Rabbi Elazar and the first *tanna* accept the verbal analogy between "hired worker" and "hired worker." **Rather, who is the *tanna*** who **does not derive** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker"? It is this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* as follows with regard to the verse: "He shall labor with you until the Jubilee Year, then he shall go out from you, he and his children with him, **and shall return to his own family** and to the possession of his fathers he shall return" (Leviticus 25:40–41). **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: What is the verse speaking of** when it states: "And shall return to his own family"? **If** it is speaking **of one who sold himself** and the Jubilee Year arrived during his six years of slavery, this **is already stated:** "He shall labor with you until the Jubilee Year, then he shall go out from you" (Leviticus 25:40).

Having worked through all four differences, the *gemara* now states its conclusion explicitly: perhaps everyone accepts the *sakhir sakhir gezeirah shavah* as a matter of fundamental principle. The differences between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer are not about whether the two categories are linked, but about how to read the specific limiting words in each relevant *pasuk*. Each difference reflects a disagreement over whether a given word (*avadcha*, *ozno*, *lo*) is excluding *mokhher atzmo* or conveying some other legal or homiletical point.

If that is so, then the earlier framing of the dispute — that the *tanna kamma* does not make *sakhir sakhir* and Rabbi Eliezer does — is not sustained. The *gemara* therefore asks: who, then, is the *tanna* who fundamentally does not hold of the *sakhir sakhir gezeirah shavah*?

## Elazar ben Yaakov

### V'shav Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּנִרְצָע – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! הָא אֵין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בִּמְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים לִפְנֵי הַיּוֹבֵל, שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל מוֹצִיאוֹ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, לְמָה לִי? נֵילַף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״!
>
> **Translation:** **If** it is speaking **of a pierced** slave, it **is already stated,** as will be explained. It must be that **the verse is speaking of nothing other** than a man **sold by the court two or three years before the Jubilee** Year, and it teaches **that the Jubilee** Year **releases him** from slavery. The Gemara analyzes this opinion: **And if** it would **enter your mind** that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov **derives** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," why do I** need this verse? **Let him derive** that a man sold by the court is released in the Jubilee Year by the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker,"** from the case of one who sold himself.

The *gemara* answers: it is the *tanna* of the following *braita*. The verse *veshav el mishpachto ve-el achuzat avotav yashuv* — "and he shall return to his family and to his ancestral inheritance he shall return" — appears in *Vayikra* in the section dealing with *mokhher atzmo* and speaks of his going free at the *yovel*. Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov asks: to which case does this verse refer? If it refers to *mokhher atzmo*, that is already the subject of the entire *parsha* — no further verse is needed. If it refers to the *nirtza*, the slave who had his ear pierced and chose permanent servitude, that too is already covered elsewhere (as the *gemara* will explain). The verse must therefore be speaking of *makhruhu beit din* — a person sold by *beit din* who has served two or three years (not exactly two or three, but prior to completing his term) before the *yovel* arrives and frees him.

From this derivation — the fact that a separate verse is needed to include *makhruhu beit din* in the *yovel* freedom — the *gemara* infers that Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov does not hold of the *sakhir sakhir gezeirah shavah*. If he did, the fundamental equation of the two categories would have already implied that *makhruhu beit din* is freed at *yovel*, since *mokhher atzmo* is. The very need for an independent verse to achieve that inclusion shows that for Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, the categories are not fundamentally linked.

### Yovel Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ הוּא, דְּלָא עֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא, אֲבָל מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין, דַּעֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא, אֵימָא נִיקְנְסֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that this claim can be refuted: **Actually,** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov **derives** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker"** as well, **and** even so it **was necessary** for him to learn that *halakha* from a verse. The reason is that it might **enter your mind to say** that **one who sells himself, who did not commit a transgression,** is emancipated at the onset of the Jubilee Year even if six years have not passed, **but** with regard to **one sold by the court, who did commit a transgression,** as he was sold because he was unable to repay the value of his theft, one might **say** that **we should penalize him** and therefore he should not be emancipated in the Jubilee Year. Consequently, the verse **teaches us** that even this slave is emancipated in the Jubilee Year.

The *gemara* once again pushes back. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak says: one can accept *sakhir sakhir* and still require an additional verse for the *yovel* case. Even if we equate the categories in principle, one might have thought that *makhruhu beit din* — sold because he committed an *aveira*, because he was a *ganav* — should not benefit from the *yovel* release. The *yovel* provides a kind of extraordinary freedom that breaks the normal schedule of servitude; perhaps a person who landed in servitude through criminal behavior should be penalized and not receive this benefit. *Hu de-avid issura, eima nikansei.* Therefore, even with a *sakhir sakhir* link, an additional verse (*veshavtem ish el achuzato*) is needed to tell us that *makhruhu beit din* does indeed go free at *yovel*.

### Two V'shavtem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִי בְּנִרְצָע – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר. מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשַׁבְתֶּם אִישׁ אֶל אֲחֻזָּתוֹ וְאִישׁ אֶל מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״ בַּמָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? אִי בְּמוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** earlier, in the *baraita*: **If** it is speaking **about a pierced** slave, it **is already stated.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the verse that teaches that a pierced slave is released in the Jubilee Year? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: **"And you shall return, each man to his land, and** you shall return, **each man to his family"** (Leviticus 25:10). **What is the verse speaking of? If** it is speaking **of one who sells himself,** it **is already stated:** "He shall labor with you until the Jubilee Year" (Leviticus 25:40).

The *gemara* then traces the source for the *nirtza*'s inclusion in *yovel* freedom. A *baraita* is cited: *veshavtem ish el achuzato ve-ish el mishpachto tashuvu* — "and you shall return, each man to his inheritance, and each man to his family you shall return." This is a second *veshavtem* verse, distinct from the first. Applying the same logic as before: if it speaks of *mokhher atzmo*, that is already clear from the entire *parsha*. If it speaks of *makhruhu beit din*, that was already covered by the first *veshavtem* verse. Therefore this verse must be addressing the *nirtza* — the slave who chose to remain after his six years were up and had his ear pierced — specifying that even he goes free at the *yovel*, if the *yovel* arrives two or three years (loosely speaking) into his extended servitude.

### Necessity Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בִּמְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! הָא אֵין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בְּנִרְצַע שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים לִפְנֵי הַיּוֹבֵל, שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל מוֹצִיאוֹ. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִישׁ״, אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁנּוֹהֵג בָּאִישׁ וְאֵין נוֹהֵג בָּאִשָּׁה – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זוֹ רְצִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If** it is speaking **of one who was sold by the court,** it **is already stated:** "Then he shall go out from you, he and his children with him, and shall return to his own family" (Leviticus 25:41). It must be that **the verse is speaking of nothing other** than a slave **who is pierced two or three years before the Jubilee** Year, and **that the Jubilee** Year **releases him.** The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred** that the verse is specifically referring to a pierced slave? **Rava bar Sheila said** that **the verse states "man." What matter applies to a man and does not apply to a woman? You must say** that **this is piercing** a Hebrew slave's ear with an awl.

The word *ish* in this second verse is what signals the *nirtza*: *eizeh davar she-noheg be-ish ve-eino noheg be-isha* — what is a *halacha* that applies to a man and not to a woman in the context of slaves? It is *retzi'ah*, ear-piercing. We already established that *retzi'ah* applies only to the male. The verse therefore is addressing the *nirtza*, including him in *yovel* freedom.

The *gemara* establishes that both verses are necessary. It was necessary to write the inclusion of *makhruhu beit din* because one might have said: a person sold by *beit din* did not choose this situation and is presumably still seeking his freedom; he may not have had the opportunity to go free, and the *yovel* ought to release him. But perhaps the *nirtza* is different — he chose to remain, he declared *ahavti et adoni*, and perhaps he should not be freed at *yovel* as a kind of penalty for forgoing his earlier freedom. Therefore we needed a verse to include the *nirtza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב נִרְצָע. דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְטַאי זִמְנֵיהּ, אֲבָל נִרְצָע, דִּמְטַאי זִמְנֵיהּ, אֵימָא נִיקְנְסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it was necessary** for the Torah **to write** that **one sold by the court** leaves in the Jubilee Year, **and it was** also **necessary to write** the same with regard to **a pierced** slave, as neither case can be derived from the other. The Gemara elaborates: **As, if** the Torah had **informed us** only about **one who was sold by the court,** one might say that the Jubilee Year releases him **because his time had not come** to be freed. **But** with regard to **a pierced** slave, **whose time had come** but he did not wish to be freed, one might **say** that **we should penalize him** and he should remain a permanent slave.

Conversely, it was necessary to write the inclusion of the *nirtza* because one might have said: a person sold by *beit din* who has not yet completed even the basic six-year term has not "served out" his time. Perhaps it would be unfair to the master for *yovel* to free him before he has provided the full labor for which the master acquired him. Therefore we needed a verse to include *makhruhu beit din* as well. *Tzricha.*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן נִרְצָע, מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ שֵׁשׁ, אֲבָל מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין, דְּלָא עֲבַד לֵיהּ שֵׁשׁ, אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** the Torah had **informed us** only about a **pierced** slave, one might say that the Jubilee Year releases him **because he has served** the master **for six** years, as required, **but** with regard to **one who was sold by the court,** who **has not** yet **served** his master **for six** years, one might **say** that he should **not** be freed in the Jubilee Year. Therefore, **it was necessary** for the Torah to state this *halakha* with regard to both cases.

### Reconciliation

The two *veshavtem* verses thus divide their labor: one includes *makhruhu beit din*, one includes *nirtza*. Each is necessary precisely because there is an independent reason to have excluded the case it covers.

### L'olam Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״וְשַׁבְתֶּם״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״לְעוֹלָם״. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לְעוֹלָם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְעוֹלָם מַמָּשׁ, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְשַׁבְתֶּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise, **it was necessary** for the Torah **to write** with regard to the release of a pierced slave in the Jubilee Year: **"And you shall return,** each man to his land" (Leviticus 25:10), **and it was necessary to write** as well: "And he shall serve him **forever"** (Exodus 21:6), which is interpreted by the Sages as referring to until the Jubilee Year. **As, if the Merciful One** had **written** only **"forever," I would say** that this **actually** means **forever,** i.e., for his entire life. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "And you shall return."**

The *gemara* closes this section by addressing the apparent contradiction between *va-avado le'olam* — "he shall serve him forever" — and the *veshavtem* verse that grants the *nirtza* freedom at *yovel*. If we had only *le'olam*, we would understand it literally: permanent servitude, no *yovel* release. But *veshavtem* overrides this. Conversely, if we had only *veshavtem* telling us the *nirtza* goes free, without *le'olam*, we would not know the duration of his servitude between the piercing and the *yovel*. We might have thought: just as the initial servitude was six years, so too the post-piercing servitude would be six years, and at the end of that additional six years he would go free regardless of *yovel*. *Le'olam* therefore tells us: absent *yovel*, he works indefinitely — *olamo shel yovel*, "forever" in the *yovel* context means until the next *yovel*. Both verses are thus required: *le'olam* to tell us there is no fixed endpoint short of *yovel*, and *veshavtem* to tell us that *yovel* does in fact release him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְשַׁבְתֶּם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא עֲבַד שֵׁשׁ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דַּעֲבַד שֵׁשׁ לֹא יְהֵא סוֹפוֹ חָמוּר מִתְּחִלָּתוֹ, מָה תְּחִלָּתוֹ שֵׁשׁ – אַף סוֹפוֹ נָמֵי שֵׁשׁ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן: ״לְעוֹלָם״ – לְעוֹלָמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One** had **written** only **"and you shall return," I would say** that **this statement applies** only **where he did not serve six** years after being pierced; **but** in a case **where he did serve six** years after he was pierced, one might argue as follows: **His final** stage, after he is pierced, **should not be stricter than his initial** stage, when he was first sold: **Just as** after **his initial** stage he serves only **six** years, **so too** in **his final** stage he serves only **six** years and no longer. Therefore, the verse **teaches us "forever,"** which means **forever until the Jubilee** Year, even if the Jubilee Year arrives many years later. In any case, with regard to the issue at hand there is no proof that Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov does not derive the verbal analogy between "hired worker" and "hired worker."

## Rabbi Non-Jew Case

### Rabbi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאן תַּנָּא דְּלָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״? רַבִּי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאִם לֹא יִגָּאֵל בְּאֵלֶּה״ רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּאֵלֶּה הוּא נִגְאָל, וְאֵין נִגְאָל בְּשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, who is the *tanna*** who **does not derive** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker"? It is Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A Hebrew slave sold to a gentile can be redeemed by his relatives, as it is stated: **"And if he is not redeemed by any of these,** then he shall go out in the Jubilee Year" (Leviticus 25:54). **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: He can be redeemed** only **through these,** i.e., the assistance of his relatives, **and he is not redeemed after six** years of labor.

At the very end of 15a, the *gemara* finally identifies a *tanna* who clearly does not hold of the *sakhir sakhir* equation: it is Rabbi (Judah the Prince). A *braita* at the top of 15b cites the verse *ve-im lo yiga'el ba-eleh* — "and if he is not redeemed through these" — in the context of someone who has sold himself to a non-Jew. Rabbi states: *ba-eleh hu niga'el ve-eino niga'el be-sheish* — through these means (i.e., redemption by relatives) he may be redeemed, but his servitude to the non-Jew does not terminate automatically at the end of six years. This is surprising: one would think that a non-Jew should have *less* of a claim over a Hebrew slave than a Jewish master, not more. Yet Rabbi reads the verse as teaching that the six-year release that applies to self-sale to a Jew does not apply to self-sale to a non-Jew.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא, וּמָה מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל בְּאֵלֶּה – נִגְאָל בְּשֵׁשׁ, זֶה, שֶׁנִּגְאָל בְּאֵלֶּה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנִּגְאָל בְּשֵׁשׁ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאֵלֶּה״ – בְּאֵלֶּה הוּא נִגְאָל, וְאֵין נִגְאָל בְּשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi elaborates: **As I might have** argued that one sold to a gentile should be released after six years. Could this **not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference: If one who cannot be redeemed by these** relatives, i.e., a Hebrew slave who was sold to a Jew, as he cannot be redeemed by his relatives, nevertheless **is redeemed after six** years of labor, **is it not logical** that **this** Hebrew slave sold to a gentile, **who can be redeemed by these** relatives, **can** likewise **be redeemed after six** years of work? Therefore, **the verse states "by any of these,"** to emphasize that one sold to a gentile **can be redeemed** only **through these** relatives, **and he cannot be redeemed after six** years.

Rabbi supports this with a *kal va-chomer*. When a Jew sells himself to another Jew, there is no obligation on relatives to redeem him (*ge'ulat kerovim* does not apply), and yet his servitude ends after six years. By a *kal va-chomer*, when a Jew sells himself to a non-Jew — where *ge'ulat kerovim* does apply (the relatives have a *mitzvah* to redeem him and, provided the non-Jew is subject to Jewish authority, can compel the transaction), and where in addition the slave has the right *megara pidyono ve-yotzei*, to reduce his outstanding term and go free — should he not certainly be freed after six years? The argument is compelling: greater protections exist in the case of sale to a non-Jew, so surely the six-year rule should apply there too. Nevertheless, Rabbi says: *talmud lomar ba-eleh, ba-eleh hu niga'el ve-eino niga'el be-sheish* — the verse teaches that his only avenue for release is redemption through relatives, not the passage of six years.

### Rav Nachman Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, אַמַּאי קָאָמַר ״וּמָה מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל בְּאֵלֶּה״? נֵילַף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **And if it enters your mind that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **derives** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," why** does he **say: If one who cannot be redeemed by these,** with regard to one who is sold to a Jew? **Let him derive** that *halakha* from the case of one sold to a gentile, through the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker."** The term "hired worker" is used in reference to one sold to a gentile as well: "As a hired worker year by year he shall be with him" (Leviticus 25:53), and accordingly one can say that even one sold to a Jew can be redeemed by his relatives. The fact that he does not accept this claim indicates that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi rejects the verbal analogy between "hired worker" and "hired worker."

The *gemara* again applies the now-familiar move: Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak says that even Rabbi could in principle hold of the *sakhir sakhir gezeirah shavah* between *mokhher atzmo* and *makhruhu beit din*. The reason Rabbi does not make the *kal va-chomer* to extend the six-year release to sale-to-a-non-Jew is not that he rejects the fundamental equation of the two main categories — it is that a verse explicitly creates an exception for this specific case.

### Yigalenu Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״ – לָזֶה, וְלֹא לְאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This proof can be refuted, as one can say that **actually** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **does derive** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," and it is different here,** with regard to one sold to a gentile, **as the verse states:** "Either his uncle or his uncle's son **may redeem him"** (Leviticus 25:49). This emphasis of "him" teaches that redemption is an option only **for this** slave **and not for another** type of slave.

The *gemara* identifies that verse: *yigalenu*, "one shall redeem him." This word restricts the applicability of *ge'ulat kerovim* to the case of sale to a non-Jew and excludes it from the case of self-sale to a Jew. If *sakhir sakhir* fundamentally equates self-sale to a Jew and sale by *beit din*, and if *ge'ulat kerovim* applied to self-sale to a Jew just as it applies to sale to a non-Jew, then why did Rabbi argue in his *kal va-chomer* that there is no *ge'ulat kerovim* by sale to a Jew? Because *yigalenu* restricts the redemption-by-relatives provision specifically to the non-Jew case and excludes self-sale to a Jew. Rabbi thus uses *ba-eleh* to exclude the six-year release in the non-Jew case, and *yigalenu* to exclude *ge'ulat kerovim* from the Jew case — and he can hold *sakhir sakhir* throughout.

## Yossi & Akiva

### Braita Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יִגָּאֵל בְּאֵלֶּה״ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: ״בְּאֵלֶּה״ – לְשִׁחְרוּר, בִּשְׁאָר כׇּל אָדָם – לְשִׁעְבּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dispute itself the Gemara inquires: **And who is the *tanna*** who **disagrees with Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi with regard to this? It is **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva. As it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning the verse: "And if **he is not redeemed by any of these"** (Leviticus 25:54), that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** The expression **"by any of these"** indicates that redemption by one's relatives is **to freedom,** i.e., one becomes an entirely free man. But if a slave is redeemed **by anyone else** who pays the gentile master, it is **to slavery.** The redeemed slave becomes indentured to his redeemer until he repays the cost of his redemption through his work.

Having examined Rabbi's position, the *gemara* asks: who is the *tanna* who disagrees with Rabbi — that is, who holds that a person sold to a non-Jew *does* go free after six years? The answer: Rabbi Yossi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva. A *braita* is cited presenting their dispute.

### Yossi View

Rabbi Yossi HaGlili holds: *ba-eleh l'shichrur* — when it is the relatives who redeem the slave from the non-Jew, the slave goes free. *Beshar kol adam l'shibbud* — but when some other person (not a relative) redeems him, the slave does not go free. The stranger can force the non-Jewish master to sell the slave (compel the transaction, since the non-Jew is under Jewish authority), but the redeemed slave then must work for this stranger-redeemer — he has effectively been sold to a new Jewish master — until the six-year term or *yovel*. The value of this is still real: the slave is out of the hands of a non-Jew. But he does not immediately regain his freedom.

### Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״בְּאֵלֶּה״ – לְשִׁעְבּוּד, בִּשְׁאָר כׇּל אָדָם – לְשִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says** the opposite: **By these** relatives he is redeemed **to slavery,** whereas if he is redeemed **by anyone else,** it is **to freedom.**

Rabbi Akiva holds the mirror image: *ba-eleh l'shibbud* — when it is the relatives who redeem him, the slave must work for them. *Beshar kol adam l'shichrur* — but when a stranger redeems him, he goes free immediately. This is counterintuitive: relatives, who have a *mitzvah* to redeem and can compel the non-Jew, nevertheless end up holding the slave in servitude, while a stranger who redeems him out of pure goodwill grants him freedom? The logic will emerge from the *gemara*'s discussion of *svara*.

### Three Geulahs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִם לֹא יִגָּאֵל בְּאֵלֶּה״ – אֶלָּא בְּאַחֵר – ״וְיָצָא בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל״. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אִם לֹא יִגָּאֵל״ – אֶלָּא – ״בְּאֵלֶּה״, ״וְיָצָא בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? The verse states** concerning a redeemed slave who is emancipated: **"If he is not redeemed by any of these"** relatives; **but** if he is redeemed **by another, "then he shall go out in the Jubilee Year,"** not earlier, as he becomes enslaved to him. **And Rabbi Akiva says: "If he is not redeemed"** in any other manner but **only "by any of these," "then he shall go out in the Jubilee Year,"** i.e., if he is redeemed by relatives, he is emancipated only at the close of Yom Kippur of the Jubilee Year. By contrast, if he is redeemed by others he is emancipated immediately.

The *gemara* traces how the dispute arises from the text. The relevant passage contains three distinct categories of redemption: *o dodo o ven dodo yigalenu* — the uncle or cousin redeems him; this is *ge'ulat kerovim*. *O hisiga yado* — or his own hand reaches the means; this is *ge'ulat atzmo*, self-redemption. *Ve-nig'al* — and he shall be redeemed; this functions as a third category, *ge'ulat acherim*, redemption by others. The fact that this third grammatical form appears in the verse establishes that a stranger may compel the redemption and the non-Jew must accept it.

### Yossi Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר: מִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ לְפָנָיו, שְׁדִי גְּאוּלַּת קְרוֹבִים אַגְּאוּלַּת עַצְמוֹ, מָה גְּאוּלַּת עַצְמוֹ לְשִׁחְרוּר – אַף גְּאוּלַּת קְרוֹבִים לְשִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili maintains: A verse is interpreted homiletically** based on its juxtaposition to the verse immediately **preceding it.** Therefore, one should **cast,** i.e., compare, the case of **redemption** performed **by relatives to** the case of **redemption** performed **by himself: Just as redemption** performed **by himself** leads **to** complete **freedom, so too, redemption** performed **by relatives** leads **to freedom.**

With *ge'ulat atzmo* — self-redemption — positioned in the middle between the two, the question becomes: to which of the flanking categories is *ge'ulat atzmo* connected, and which connection determines who goes free? Rabbi Yossi HaGlili holds *mikra nidrash lefanav* — the verse is interpreted by reference to what precedes it. The earlier category, *ge'ulat kerovim*, is linked to *ge'ulat atzmo*. Just as when one redeems oneself one goes free, so too when relatives redeem: *mah ge'ulat atzmo le-shichrur af ge'ulat kerovim le-shichrur*. Others (*ge'ulat acherim*), not linked to *ge'ulat atzmo*, result in servitude.

### Akiva Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: מִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ לְאַחֲרָיו, שְׁדִי גְּאוּלַּת אֲחֵרִים אַגְּאוּלַּת עַצְמוֹ, מָה גְּאוּלַּת עַצְמוֹ לְשִׁחְרוּר – אַף גְּאוּלַּת אֲחֵרִים לְשִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva maintains** that **a verse is interpreted homiletically** based on its juxtaposition to the verse immediately **after it.** Therefore one should **cast** the case of **redemption** performed **by others to** the case of **redemption** performed **by himself: Just as redemption** performed **by himself is to freedom, so too, redemption** performed **by others is to freedom,** whereas if he is redeemed by his relatives, he becomes enslaved to them.

Rabbi Akiva holds *mikra nidrash le-acharav* — the verse is interpreted by reference to what follows it. The later category, *ge'ulat acherim*, is linked to *ge'ulat atzmo*. Just as self-redemption leads to freedom, so too redemption by others: *mah ge'ulat atzmo le-shichrur af ge'ulat acherim le-shichrur*. Relatives (*ge'ulat kerovim*), not linked to *ge'ulat atzmo*, result in servitude.

### Ba'eleh Limiting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״בְּאֵלֶּה״ לְמָה לִי? אִי לָאו ״בְּאֵלֶּה״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ בֵּין לְפָנָיו בֵּין לְאַחֲרָיו, וְהַכֹּל לְשִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that this is their dispute, **why do I** need the phrase **"by any of these,"** according to both Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva? The Gemara answers: **If** it were **not** for the phrase **"by any of these," I would say** that **a verse is interpreted homiletically** based on its juxtaposition to the verse immediately **preceding it as well as** to the verse immediately **after it, and** therefore in **every** manner in which he is redeemed it is **to freedom.** The phrase "by any of these" limits this freedom either to relatives or other people, according to the respective opinions of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva.

The *gemara* asks: if the entire debate is about which flanking category is linked to *ge'ulat atzmo*, why do they invoke the word *ba-eleh* at all? The *gemara* explains: without *ba-eleh*, one might say the verse is read in both directions — both the earlier and later categories are linked to *ge'ulat atzmo*. Then *all* forms of redemption would result in freedom. *Ba-eleh* tells us this is not so — only some of the categories enjoy the same status as *ge'ulat atzmo*. The question then is which ones, and the answer is not derivable from *ba-eleh* itself.

### Svara Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הֲדַר קוּשְׁיָא לְדוּכְתֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא, בִּסְבָרָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that the different opinions are based on the phrase "by any of these," then **the difficulty** with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, that the verse does not say: If he is not redeemed in any other manner but only by any of these, **has returned to its place,** i.e., it is still valid, as this phrase indicates that Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's interpretation of the verse is correct. **Rather,** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva **disagree with regard to reason.** Their dispute is a matter of logic and does not concern textual interpretation.

This leads to the *gemara*'s conclusion: the debate between Rabbi Yossi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva is ultimately grounded in *svara* — in reasoning and logic — rather than in a purely textual disagreement about the direction of interpretation. *Ba-eleh* establishes that not all categories are equal, and the reading direction (*lefanav* or *le-acharav*) may reflect the underlying *svara* rather than generating it.

### Yossi Svara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר: מִסְתַּבְּרָא גְּאוּלַּת אֲחֵרִים לְשִׁיעְבּוּד, דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ לְשִׁחְרוּר, הֲווֹ מִימַּנְעִי וְלָא פָּרְקִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili maintains: It stands to reason** that **redemption** performed **by others** is **to slavery, as, if you say** it is **to freedom, they will refrain and will not redeem him.** If the slave is required to serve them, and they incur no financial loss, they will redeem him.

Rabbi Yossi HaGlili's *svara*: it makes sense that redemption by others results in servitude. *De-i amrat le-shichrur havay memna ve-lo parki* — if you say that a stranger's redemption leads to freedom, then strangers will simply not bother redeeming the slave. Strangers have no *mitzvah* to redeem and no self-interest in doing so; offering them the "reward" of enslaving the redeemed person to themselves gives them an incentive to act and at least gets the slave out of non-Jewish hands. The relatives, who have an obligation, will redeem — and the slave goes free. Thus the system works: relatives are incentivized by obligation, others by self-interest, and the slave ultimately benefits.

### Akiva Svara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: מִסְתַּבְּרָא גְּאוּלַּת קְרוֹבִים לְשִׁיעְבּוּד, דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ לְשִׁחְרוּר, כׇּל יוֹמָא וְיוֹמָא אֲזַל וּמְזַבֵּין נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva maintains: It stands to reason** that **redemption** performed **by relatives** is **to slavery, as, if you say** it is **to freedom, each and every day he will go and sell himself** over and again, relying on his relatives to free him.

Rabbi Akiva's *svara*: it makes sense that redemption by relatives results in servitude. *De-i amrat le-shichrur kol yoma ve-yoma azil u-mezabin nafsheh* — if relatives' redemption leads to freedom, and relatives are obligated to redeem, then a penniless person with wealthy relatives would repeatedly sell himself to a non-Jew and pocket the sale price, knowing his relatives are obligated to free him at their expense. This is precisely the kind of abuse of system that must be prevented: like the famous example of offering bounties for pests and thereby incentivizing people to breed the very pests one seeks to eliminate. Servitude to relatives therefore deters the abuse. Strangers, by contrast, have no obligation — their redemption is wholly voluntary and unexpected — so if one chooses to act out of pure generosity and the slave goes free, there is no systemic incentive for abuse.

## Rabbi & Chachamim

### R. Chiya Quote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַכֹּל לְשִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva,** who hold that a slave is not invariably freed whenever he is redeemed from his gentile master. **But the Rabbis say** that in **every** case, when he is emancipated it is **to freedom.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba cites Rabbi Yochanan: the dispute described above — between one view that some redeemers free the slave and others subject him to servitude — is the position of Rabbi Yossi HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva. But there is a third view.

### Chachamim View

The Chachamim hold *hakol le-shichrur* — in all cases, whether relatives or strangers redeem the slave, the result is freedom. There is no distinction between categories of redeemer; every redemption from the non-Jew results in the slave going free.

### Rabbi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן חֲכָמִים? רַבִּי הִיא, דְּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְהַאי ״בְּאֵלֶּה״ לִדְרָשָׁה אַחֲרִינָא, וּמִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ בֵּין לְפָנָיו וּבֵין לְאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **Who** are these **Rabbis?** It is **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who derives another exposition from this** term **"by any of these,"** as stated above. **And** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that **a verse is interpreted homiletically** based on its juxtaposition to the verse immediately **preceding it as well as** to the verse immediately **after it.** Consequently, the Hebrew slave of a gentile is emancipated regardless of whether he is redeemed by relatives or other people.

The Chachamim in this context is Rabbi himself — not the same position he held in the earlier *braita* regarding the six-year release, but specifically on this question of who is *Chachamim* here. Rabbi does not use *ba-eleh* as a limiting word in the context of *ge'ulat kerovim* versus *ge'ulat acherim*. He uses *ba-eleh* entirely for his earlier teaching — that servitude to a non-Jew does not end at six years. Therefore the verse about all three categories (*ge'ulat kerovim*, *ge'ulat atzmo*, *ge'ulat acherim*) is read in both directions, both forward and backward, meaning *all* forms of redemption are equated with self-redemption — and all result in freedom. *Umikra nidrash bein lefanav bein le-acharav.*

### V'yatza Yovel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי, הַאי ״וְיָצָא בִּשְׁנַת הַיֹּבֵל״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וְיָצָא בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **what does he do with** the verse: "And if he is not redeemed by any of these means, **then he shall go out in the Jubilee Year"** (Leviticus 25:54)? The Gemara answers: **He requires** this verse **for that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"Then he shall go out in the Jubilee Year."** If the slave is not redeemed prior to this point in time he leaves his gentile master only in the Jubilee Year.

What does Rabbi then do with the verse *ve-yatza bi-shnat ha-yovel* — "he shall go free in the year of the jubilee"? This verse, which speaks of *yovel* freedom for the slave sold to a non-Jew, is applied by Rabbi to a specific circumstance: a non-Jew who is under Jewish control (*she-yeshno tachat yadecha*). Because such a non-Jew is subject to our authority, one might think we would exercise that authority to free the slave even before *yovel* — perhaps immediately. The verse therefore tells us: even in that case, we act within the law. We do not abuse our position of power; we wait for *yovel*. Alternatively, one might ask whether the *yovel* release applies even to a non-Jew *not* under our control — but that question answers itself: we cannot impose our laws on someone beyond our reach. The verse therefore addresses only the case where the non-Jew is within our authority, and its teaching is that we nonetheless act with restraint and do not deploy that authority to override the legal framework.
