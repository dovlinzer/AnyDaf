# Shabbat 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kesher Kayama & Definiti…

### Rashi: Permanence

The *daf* begins with a review of the foundational *machlokes* among the *rishonim* regarding the definition of a *kesher shel kayama* — the permanently-intended knot that constitutes a biblical transgression on *Shabbos*. Rashi's approach centers on a single criterion: permanence. A *kesher shel kayama* is one that is made to remain that way forever — as in the rope looped through the ring of a boat or the nose-ring of a camel, left indefinitely. That alone constitutes the biblical prohibition.

What is entirely *muttar* according to Rashi? Knots tied to things one regularly dons and removes — shoes, garments, and similar items. Rashi states explicitly at the end of his commentary on 111b: *dekeivan dechol yoma shari lei, lo dami midi lekesher shel kayama* — since it is undone every day, it bears no resemblance whatsoever to a *kesher shel kayama*. At the other extreme, something left for a week or longer falls into the intermediate category of *asur d'rabbanan*. Rashi's formulation on 112a reinforces this: *mipnei shepeamim shemaniach sham shavua o shavuayim* — sometimes it is left there for a week or two. The structure, then, is: permanent — biblical; daily — permitted; somewhere in the range of a week or more — rabbinically forbidden. The precise boundary between a day and a week remains undefined.

### Rambam: Two Criteria

The Rambam takes a different approach entirely. For him, biblical liability requires not one but two conditions: the knot must be a *kesher uman* — a craftsman's or professional-grade knot — and it must also be *kayama*, meaning intended to endure. How exactly one defines a *kesher uman* is itself a matter of debate among later authorities; some understand it to mean any unusually tight, durable knot, though it is not clear the Rambam himself would define it that broadly.

The interaction between these two criteria produces a framework that is neither simply more lenient nor more stringent than Rashi's. On one hand, by requiring the *kesher uman* criterion, the Rambam raises the threshold for biblical liability — an ordinary person making an ordinary knot does not transgress biblically even if the knot is quite durable. On the other hand, by adding this second criterion, the Rambam does not need *kayama* to mean "forever"; something considerably less than permanent can qualify as *kayama* under his framework, which makes the standard for *kayama* easier to reach.

### Summary of Approaches

The resulting halakhic map for the Rambam is as follows: a knot that is *muttar* completely is one that has neither characteristic — it is neither a professional knot nor *kayama* in any meaningful sense, as with ordinary daily-use knots on garments and footwear. A knot that has one of the two characteristics — either *kayama* without being a professional knot, or a professional knot without being particularly *kayama* — is *asur d'rabbanan*. Only when both criteria are present simultaneously is there a biblical violation. For Rashi, by contrast, the entire analysis turns on degree of permanence alone.

## Permitted: Garments & Da…

### Miftach Chaluka

The *Gemara* now turns to the category of knots that are *muttar* outright, beginning with the *miftach chaluka* — the opening of a garment. This refers to a garment tied with laces or crossed straps across the front or back. The *Gemara* asks: is this not self-evidently permitted? The answer is that the novelty lies in a case where the garment has two such straps. One might think that since it is possible to extricate oneself by undoing only one strap — a person being as prone to laziness then as now — perhaps the second strap would be left in place and the knot would take on a more permanent character. The *Gemara* teaches that we are not concerned about this; since garments of this type are regularly opened and closed, even occasional laziness does not transform the knot into anything approaching a *kesher shel kayama*.

### Hairnet Straps

Similarly, *chutei shvacha* — the straps cinching a hairnet — are obviously *muttar*. The novelty here concerns a hairnet that sits somewhat loosely on the head. One might suppose that rather than bothering to undo the strap, a woman would simply pull the hairnet off over her head, and since it is sometimes not untied, perhaps the knot takes on a more permanent character. The *Gemara* dismisses this: *d'itteta chasa a'saara* — a woman is solicitous of her hair and will undo the strap properly rather than risk disarranging it. Again, the conclusion is that since the strap is routinely undone, it poses no issue.

## Shoe/Sandal Straps

### Three Braitot

The *Gemara* now reaches the central and more complex discussion: *retzuot minal v'sandal* — the straps of a shoe or sandal. Three *braisos* are cited, each appearing to say something different. The first states that one who unties the straps of a shoe or sandal is *chayav chatat* — a full biblical violation (and correspondingly, tying them is likewise biblical). The second states that one is *patur aval assur* — exempt from biblical liability but rabbinically forbidden. The third states it is entirely *muttar l'chatchila*. These three positions apply symmetrically to both tying and untying. The *Gemara* immediately flags contradictions both internally and against the *mishnah*, and proceeds to resolve them by identifying different scenarios that correspond to each ruling.

### Shoe Scenarios

For regular shoes, the resolution is as follows. The ruling of *chayav chatat* applies to *ushkafei* — shoemakers — who tie the straps as part of the construction of the shoe itself. This is a permanent attachment, made by a craftsman, and it never comes undone in the ordinary course of use. This constitutes both a *kesher uman* and genuine *kayama*, satisfying the Rambam's criteria, and under Rashi's framework it is simply permanent. The ruling of *patur aval assur* applies to a different case, which Rashi describes as the manner in which rabbis wore their shoes: tied in a way that was slightly loose. Such a person might leave the shoes tied and simply kick them on and off without ever undoing the knot — keeping them tied for several days at a stretch, or even longer if they are infrequently worn. While the knot is not truly permanent — eventually one will need to tighten or loosen the straps, particularly in muddy conditions — it is also not undone on a daily basis, and therefore falls into the intermediate category. The ruling of *muttar l'chatchila* applies to the residents of *Mechuza*, who were scrupulous about their appearance and tied their shoes very tightly; such a person must undo the knots each night, and therefore there is no issue whatsoever.

This raises a practical question about *kavvanah* — intention. A knot can only be prohibited at the moment of tying; if one ties a knot intending to undo it that day but then changes one's mind, there is no retroactive violation. The more pressing concern is when a person is semi-aware at the moment of tying that he habitually does not undo the knot for extended periods. For example, one who receives *gelila* on *Shabbos mincha* and ties the *gartel* around the *Sefer Torah* is aware that it will not be untied until Monday — more than a day. This warrants *chumra*. Similarly, one whose *Shabbos* shoes habitually sit tied until the following *Shabbos* would do well to make a practice of untying them *motzei Shabbos*.

### Sandal Scenarios

The parallel resolution for sandals follows a similar structure. The *chayav chatat* ruling applies to *d'toi d'katri ushkhafi* — the sandals of nomadic travelers or merchants, where the shoemaker ties the straps onto the sandal base permanently. The wearers do not detach the straps from the base at all; they merely put the sandal on and off as a unit, with the strap attachment being fixed from the moment of manufacture. This satisfies the *kesher uman* criterion and is truly *kayama*. The *patur aval assur* ruling applies to ordinary people, *b'chumrsa d'katri inhu*, who attach their own straps to the sandal base themselves. While they do reconnect the strap to the base, they do so irregularly — leaving it for a week or a month at a time — and since the attachment is not made by a craftsman, it falls short of biblical liability but remains rabbinically problematic. The *muttar l'chatchila* ruling applies to a sandal shared by two people — not simultaneously, but on a rotating basis. Since two people of different foot sizes must regularly readjust the straps to fit each person, the knot is undone with sufficient frequency that it presents no concern.

### Shared Sandals

The *Gemara* illustrates this last point with a concrete example: *besandal denafki beih bi harei* — a sandal shared by two, worn alternately, requiring regular readjustment of the straps. This is precisely the *muttar* case.

## Rav Yehudah's Sandals

### Shared Use Resolution

The *Gemara* proceeds directly to a narrative involving Rav Yehudah, brother of Rav Salla Chasida, who owned a pair of sandals that he and his young child wore on an alternating basis — *zimnin nafik beih ihu, zimnin nafik beih yanukeih* — sometimes he wore them out, sometimes his child did. He came before Abaye to ask what the law was regarding these sandals on *Shabbos*. Abaye's initial response was startling: *chayav chatat* — a full biblical violation. Rav Yehudah was taken aback: he had come not even knowing whether it was rabbinically problematic, and here Abaye was telling him it was a biblical offense. Abaye then asked why he thought it should be permitted. Rav Yehudah explained that since every day, on a regular basis, sometimes he would wear them and sometimes his child would, the straps were constantly being readjusted. Abaye immediately reversed himself: *i hachi, muttar l'chatchila* — if that is the case, it is entirely permitted.

### Abaye's Challenge

Rashi explains why Abaye initially said *chayav chatat*: he had assumed, before hearing the full picture, that the sandal was of the permanently-attached type, perhaps tied with a craftsman's knot, and that there was no regular undoing. Once he understood that the shared use entailed routine readjustment, the ruling shifted completely. The governing principle, restated here clearly according to Rashi: permanent knots are biblical, daily-undone knots are permitted, and those left for a week or more are rabbinically prohibited. The space between a day and a week remains imprecise.

## Broken Straps & Vessels

### Yirmiyah/Abbahu

The *Gemara* now shifts to a different category of problem involving sandal straps: what happens when a strap breaks during *Shabbos*. Rav Yirmiyah was walking behind Rabbi Abbahu in a *charmelit* when his sandal strap broke. Tying it back would be at least *asur d'rabbanan*. The problem was that carrying the broken sandal was also rabbinically forbidden in a *charmelit*. Rabbi Abbahu's solution: find a moist reed or similar vegetation — something edible by animals, already available on the ground, and therefore not *muktzeh* — and use it to temporarily bind the sandal. Since it is entirely improvised and everyone understands it will be replaced *motzei Shabbos*, it has no character of permanence whatsoever.

### Abaye/Yosef

A parallel case involved Abaye, who was standing before Rav Yosef when his sandal strap broke — this time in a *chatzer*, a courtyard, so there was no carrying problem. Rav Yosef said: *shevukia* — just leave it. Since it is in a protected courtyard, we can retrieve it after *Shabbos*. Abaye pressed: why is this case different from the Rav Yirmiyah case, where a workaround was found? Rav Yosef's response implied that the sandal had become *muktzeh*. Abaye challenged this: *amana hu* — it is still a vessel, a *kli*, and should not be *muktzeh*. The *Gemara* notes an important distinction: in the Rav Yirmiyah case, the only problem was carrying in a *charmelit*; there was no claim that the sandal had lost its vessel status. Here, by contrast, the instruction to simply leave it implies that Rav Yosef viewed the sandal as having lost its *kli* status and become *muktzeh*.

### Reversing Sandal

Abaye proposed a resolution: *hafichna lah miyamina l'smola* — one can reverse the sandal from right to left. Rashi explains that sandals of this period had two straps: one on the instep (inner) and one on the outer edge. If the outer strap broke, the sandal was considered unusable for its own foot, because people would not bother to repair a broken outer strap — it was too visible and unsightly. Since no one would fix it, it was effectively discarded, making it *muktzeh*. However, Abaye argued that since sandals of the period were made on a relatively flat, non-contoured base — not shaped to a specific foot — one could switch the sandal from right to left. What had been the outer strap would now become the inner strap, and the sandal would remain functional. As long as this salvage was possible, the sandal retained its *kli* status and should not be *muktzeh*.

The *Gemara* notes that the broader question of *shivrei keilim* — the general *muktzeh* status of broken vessels — applies here, though the discussion focuses specifically on the sandal case rather than the general principle.

## Inner vs. Outer Strap

### Halakha Position

The *Gemara* now turns to the question of which position we follow. The context is set by a citation of Rabbi Yochanan, who will shortly weigh in. The statement that follows — *mideka matir Rabbi Yochanan aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda, shma mina halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — introduces a principle: since Rabbi Yochanan goes to the trouble of explaining and defending Rabbi Yehudah's position, we infer that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehudah. This has significant consequences for the *muktzeh* status of a sandal with a broken outer strap.

### Baraita: Sandal Damage

A *baraita* from *Keilim* is cited: *sandal shenipsaku shtei oznav o shtei tartsiyotav* — if a sandal's two ear-flaps or both strap-holders break, or if the entire base is removed, it becomes *tahor*, meaning it has lost its vessel status. If only one of the flaps or strap-connectors breaks, or only a majority of the base remains intact, it remains *tamei* — it is still a vessel. The underlying logic is familiar: *tumah* and vessel-status are coextensive. When a vessel completely loses its functional identity, the *tumah* departs and it becomes *tahor*. When enough integrity remains to still be considered a functional item, *tumah* persists.

### Rabbi Yehudah Position

Into this framework, Rabbi Yehudah introduces a sharper distinction: *nifska pnimis, tamei* — if the inner strap broke, the sandal remains *tamei*, because it is still usable and one would fix the inner strap. *Hachitzona, tahor* — if the outer strap broke, even though only one strap is affected, it is *tahor*, because one would not bother to repair an unsightly broken outer strap. The sandal has effectively been abandoned.

The Rabbis disagree: even a broken outer strap does not disqualify the sandal as a vessel. Only if both straps break simultaneously does it lose its status. One might reverse the sandal from right to left, so even a broken outer strap does not render it nonfunctional.

The halakhic stakes are immediate: if we follow Rabbi Yehudah, a sandal with a broken outer strap is not a *kli* and is therefore *muktzeh* on *Shabbos*. If we follow the Rabbis, it retains *kli* status and may be handled.

Rabbi Yochanan enters the picture with a sweeping statement: *kemachlokes le'inyan tumah, kach machlokes le'inyan Shabbos* — the same dispute about when the sandal loses vessel-status for *tumah* applies identically to *Shabbos*, determining when it becomes *muktzeh*. However — and this is striking — *aval lo le'inyan chalitzah*. When it comes to *chalitzah*, the dispute does not apply in the same way.

The *Gemara* probes this carefully. According to the Rabbis, a sandal with one broken strap (even outer) retains vessel status for *tumah* and *Shabbos*. Why, then, would they say it is disqualified for *chalitzah*? After all, we know from elsewhere that even a left shoe used on the right foot is valid for *chalitzah* — the standards for *chalitzah* are if anything lower, not higher. It would be incoherent for the Rabbis to be more lenient for *tumah* and *Shabbos* than for *chalitzah*.

The *Gemara* then examines the other side: according to Rabbi Yehudah, a sandal with a broken outer strap is not a vessel for *tumah* or *Shabbos*. But could Rabbi Yehudah nonetheless say it qualifies for *chalitzah*, invoking the principle that a left shoe on the right foot works? The *Gemara* rejects this: *heicha delemilsiah lav mana hu* — the principle of using a left shoe on the right foot applies only when the sandal is genuinely a complete shoe for its own purpose. Here, since Rabbi Yehudah holds that a sandal with a broken outer strap is not even a shoe for ordinary use on its proper foot, there is no basis to invoke the left-foot dispensation for *chalitzah*.

The *Gemara*'s resolution is therefore: Rabbi Yochanan's statement about *chalitzah* is not saying the parties converge there while diverging elsewhere. Rather, they dispute *chalitzah* in precisely the same way they dispute *tumah* and *Shabbos*. What his statement teaches is the following principle: the rule that a left shoe on the right foot is valid for *chalitzah* applies only when that shoe is a genuine, complete shoe for its own left foot. A shoe that has lost its vessel status entirely — as Rabbi Yehudah maintains when the outer strap breaks — is disqualified for *chalitzah* as well.

## Yochanan/Mishna Tension

### Yochanan Plain Mishna

Rabbi Yochanan's statement that *halacha* follows a *stam mishna* — an unattributed *mishnah* — creates a further difficulty. The inference that Rabbi Yochanan rules like Rabbi Yehudah rests on the fact that he elaborated and defended Rabbi Yehudah's position. Since Rabbi Yochanan also holds that we follow the *stam mishna* as a general principle, the *Gemara* asks whether there is a *stam mishna* that contradicts this.

### Problem Stated

There is. The *mishnah* in *Keilim* states: *sandal shenipseka achas me'aznav vetikna, tamei midras* — if one of the sandal's flaps breaks and is repaired, it remains *tamei midras*. The *Gemara* initially reads this as meaning that the sandal retains vessel status whenever only one strap breaks, regardless of whether it is the inner or outer one. If so, this *stam mishna* contradicts Rabbi Yehudah's view that a broken outer strap is sufficient to disqualify the sandal. The *Gemara* tries to read the *mishna* as referring only to the inner strap: *penimis davka* — only the inner strap case yields *tamei*. But then the objection arises: if that is the intent, why does the *mishna* not simply say so? Why draw the distinction at "one strap versus two straps" rather than at "inner versus outer"?

The *mishnah* continues: *nipseka hashniya vetikna, tahor min hamidras aval tamei maga midras* — if the second strap broke (and was repaired), the sandal is *tahor* from *midras* but remains *tamei maga midras*, as something that had contact with a *midras* object. This line requires careful unpacking. When the sandal bore only one broken strap (the inner one, on the Gemara's reading), it remained *tamei midras* — fully an *av hatumah*. Now both straps have broken and been repaired, and the *Gemara* asks: why is it now *tahor midras*? The sandal never ceased being a shoe — each strap was repaired immediately when it broke. Yet its *midras* status was lost.

Rashi offers two readings. In the first, the sandal genuinely stopped being a shoe when both straps were broken simultaneously enough to alter its identity — and since it briefly became a non-shoe (perhaps usable for another purpose, like a backscratcher), it could no longer function as a *midras* vessel. Yet because it was, at that transitional moment, in a sense "touching itself" as it shifted from one vessel-identity to another, it retains *tumah* as *maga midras* — the *tumah* of having had contact with a *midras* object. This is an unusual idea: an object that touched itself. *Tosafos* flags the conceptual difficulty involved. In Rashi's second reading, the sandal never actually stopped being a shoe — each strap was fixed before the next broke — but since by the end of the process both straps have been replaced entirely, it now has the identity of a *new* shoe. The new shoe never received *midras tumah* itself, but it was in contact with the old shoe that bore that *tumah*, and so it is *tamei maga midras*.

This second reading of Rashi introduces the critical concept: replacing all component parts, even sequentially, creates a new identity, even if there was never a moment at which the object ceased to function.

### Four-Strap Resolution

Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef offers a resolution to the contradiction between Rabbi Yochanan's apparent endorsement of Rabbi Yehudah's view and the *stam mishna*: *tehei mishnasenu b'sandal she'yesh lo arba oznayim v'arba tasiyoseihen* — interpret the *stam mishna* as dealing with a sandal that has four flaps and four strap-holders, two on each side. In such a case, breaking "one strap" means breaking one of the outer straps, leaving the second outer strap still intact. Since one outer strap remains, the sandal does not yet lose vessel status even according to Rabbi Yehudah. Breaking "the second strap" means breaking the remaining outer strap — at which point, under Rabbi Yehudah's view, the sandal becomes *tahor*. This reading harmonizes the *stam mishna* with Rabbi Yehudah's position and preserves Rabbi Yochanan's consistency.

The *Gemara* acknowledges that this is a strained resolution — it posits an unusual four-strap sandal that is not the standard case — but accepts it as preferable to allowing Rabbi Yochanan's positions to contradict one another: *shelo lishbor devarav shel Rabbi Yochanan*. It is worth noting the structure of the *Gemara*'s reasoning here. Rabbi Yochanan never explicitly stated he ruled like Rabbi Yehudah; the inference was drawn from the fact that he elaborated Rabbi Yehudah's position. Nor did Rabbi Yochanan ever explicitly invoke the *stam mishna* in this context — the *Gemara* applies his general principle. The resulting tension is therefore built on two layers of inference, yet the *Gemara* treats it as a genuine contradiction requiring resolution.

### Rabin Alternative

When Ravin came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he brought a different tradition: *amar Rav Chanina bar Abba amar Rav, halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — Rav ruled like Rabbi Yehudah. *Rabbi Yochanan*, however, did not. This resolves everything cleanly: Rabbi Yochanan does not rule like Rabbi Yehudah, and therefore his endorsement of the *stam mishna* — which holds that even a broken outer strap does not disqualify the sandal — is perfectly consistent. The earlier inference that Rabbi Yochanan ruled like Rabbi Yehudah was mistaken, or at least disputed. The *Gemara* responds that there is now a debate among *amoraim* about what Rabbi Yochanan's actual position was: *amorai ninhu ve'aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda* — there are two versions of how Rabbi Yochanan related to Rabbi Yehudah's view.

## Ship of Theseus

### Chizkiyah Question

The *Gemara* now presents a question posed by Chizkiyah that has a philosophical dimension reaching far beyond the immediate halakhic context. The *Gemara* had already established in another context that ordinary household vessels lose their *tumah* status only when they develop a hole large enough to allow a pomegranate to pass through — any smaller hole is not enough to render them nonfunctional. Chizkiyah asks: what if a vessel sustained a series of small holes, each no larger than an olive — below the threshold — and each was filled in (with putty or a similar material) before the next appeared, until the cumulative area of all the holes filled over time would, had they all been open simultaneously, equal a pomegranate-sized opening? At no single moment did the vessel have a disqualifying hole. Does it nonetheless lose its *tumah* status?

### New Faces/Identity

Rabbi Yochanan responds by invoking the sandal case: *rebbi, shnisah lanu* — you yourself taught us this. The *mishnah* states that if the first strap of a sandal broke and was repaired, the sandal remains *tamei midras*. If the second strap then broke and was also repaired, the sandal is *tahor midras* but *tamei maga midras*. The question that arises is why: the sandal never stopped being a shoe, so why does it lose its *midras* status? Rabbi Yochanan's answer, attributed here as something Chizkiyah himself had previously taught: *panim chadashos ba'u l'chan* — new faces have come here. Since every critical component has been replaced — even though sequentially — a new identity has been created. Although the object never ceased to exist as a functional vessel, the totality of replacement amounts to a new entity. The same principle answers Chizkiyah's question: if enough of the vessel's material has been removed and replaced that had all of it been gone at once the vessel would have lost its identity, then the sequential replacement constitutes the arrival of *panim chadashos*, a new identity.

### Both Straps New Shoe

The *Gemara* thus articulates a clear rule: a sandal in which both straps have been replaced, even one at a time, is no longer the original sandal. It is halakhically a new shoe. It does not carry the old *midras tumah*, though it is *tamei maga midras* because the new shoe was formed out of, or in contact with, the old one. The parallel for vessels: once enough material has been replaced that the original integrity has been wholly reconstituted with new components, the object has a new identity for purposes of *tumah*.

### Praise & Yeridat Drot

The response to Rabbi Yochanan's elegant resolution is extraordinary. Chizkiyah exclaims: *lais dain bar inash* — this is no ordinary human being, this is an angel. An alternate tradition says: *k'gon dain bar inash* — this is what it means to truly be a human being. From this praise, the *Gemara* transitions to a famous statement attributed to Rabbi Zeira in the name of Rava: *im rishonim bnei malachim, anu bnei anashim; v'im rishonim bnei anashim, anu kachamorim* — if the earlier ones were like angels, we are merely humans; if the earlier ones were human, we are like donkeys. And not like the distinguished donkeys of Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa or Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair, whose animals refused to eat food that was not theirs — but like ordinary donkeys.

### Yeridat HaDorot

This statement opens onto a much larger theological and intellectual question: the doctrine of *yeridat hadorot*, the decline of the generations. The idea holds that every successive generation stands on a lower spiritual and intellectual *madregah* than its predecessors, moving ever further from *Har Sinai*. There is no question that profound veneration of earlier *gedolim* is appropriate. The question is whether one must subscribe to a particular ideological commitment that later generations are simply nothing compared to earlier ones — and what the consequences of that commitment are.

A useful entry point into this debate is the book by Menachem Kellner, *Maimonides and the Descent of the Generations*, which argues that *yeridat hadorot* — though promoted by certain *Geonim* — was by no means a universally held or ideologically central doctrine. The Rambam, notably, never quotes it and in certain domains, particularly science, explicitly acknowledges that *Chazal* were limited by the knowledge available in their time and that later generations have made advances. This is not a blanket rejection of *Chazal*'s authority, but it is a recognition that the doctrine of generational decline does not have to be foundational.

The deeper question, as Kellner frames it, is: what is the basis of the authority of *Torah She-ba'al Peh*? If one holds that the authority of *Chazal* derives from the fact that they were the most brilliant and holy human beings who ever lived, then any challenge to their perfection becomes destabilizing — contradictions with science must be deflected, later authorities can never genuinely question earlier ones, and the entire enterprise risks becoming more like worship than scholarship. But there is another basis for the authority of *Torah She-ba'al Peh*: the Torah itself empowers *Beit Din*, and authority resides in the institution that is duly constituted and recognized. Just as the authority of a supreme court derives from the legitimacy of the institution rather than from the claim that its members were the greatest jurists who ever lived, so too the authority of the *Gemara* and of *Chazal* can rest on institutional empowerment rather than on the premise of irreversible generational decline. On this view, it is entirely possible to maintain full commitment to *Torah She-ba'al Peh* while engaging honestly with the historical and intellectual record, without requiring the past to be beyond scrutiny.

## More Permitted Knots

### Wine/Oil Flasks

The *mishnah* continues with additional examples of permitted knots. *Nodot yayin v'shemen* — leather flasks or pouches containing wine or oil — were typically cinched at the neck with a rope. The *Gemara* asks: is it not self-evident that this is permitted? The novelty, again, is a flask with two handles or ears. One might think that since it is possible to pour from the flask by loosening only one side of the cinching rope, a person might leave the other side tied and never undo the full knot. The *Gemara* teaches that we are not concerned about this; the general practice is to undo both sides, and occasional shortcuts do not transform the knot into a *kesher shel kayama*.

### Meat Pot

*Kdeirah shel basar* — a pot of meat — was sealed with a leather cover tied down. The *Gemara* again asks: obvious, is it not? The novelty involves a pot with a *shlucha* — some kind of spout. One might suppose that since one could pour the contents out through the spout without removing the cover, the tied cover might be left in place. The *Gemara* teaches that we are not concerned: ordinarily the cover is opened regularly, and the occasional possibility of using the spout does not change the character of the knot.

### Animal Pen Rope

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov states that one may tie a rope across the entrance of an animal pen. Since animals go in and out of the pen daily, the rope must be untied and retied constantly, and it could never take on a character of permanence. Again the *Gemara* asks: is this not obvious? The novelty is that there are two knots involved — either two separate ropes, one above and one below, or a single rope tied at both ends to the posts. One might think that at least one of the knots would be left in place — if there are two ropes, one might be left; if the rope is tied on both ends, one end might remain knotted. The *Gemara* teaches that we are not concerned: the routine of daily animal management ensures both knots are regularly undone.

### Halakha Rule

The *Gemara* then records: *amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, meaning that tying such a rope before an animal pen is permitted.

### Abaye/Yosef Debate

Abaye objects to Rav Yosef's formulation: *halacha miklal d'pligi?* — does saying "*halacha* follows him" not imply that there were others who disputed him? Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov was simply adding another example of a permitted knot, with no dispute involved. Rav Yosef replies: *mai nafka lach mina?* — what practical difference does it make? If the *halacha* is as he says, whether or not anyone disputed him is irrelevant. Abaye insists: *gemara gemar, zimereta hi?* — we have a tradition, do you want to reduce it to a mere tune? It matters to understand the full contours of the debate, what the underlying parameters were, and what considerations drove different positions. Even when the *halacha l'maaseh* is settled, understanding the intellectual structure of the disagreement provides insight that simple rule-recitation cannot.
