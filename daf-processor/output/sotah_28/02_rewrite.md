# Sotah 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Identity of Him

### Who is "Him"?

The *mishnah* with which we conclude the fifth *perek* opens with two parallel principles: just as the waters check her, they check him, and just as she is forbidden to the husband, she is forbidden to the adulterer. Before proceeding further, the *Gemara* pauses to ask a basic question: when the *mishnah* says the waters check *otoh* — him — who exactly is "him"? There are two candidates: the husband (*ba'al*) or the adulterer (*bo'el*).

### Waters Check Both

The *Gemara* raises the possibility that the waters check the husband. But what would the husband have done wrong? The *Gemara* considers that perhaps the husband himself sinned — for example, by sleeping with his wife after she had already acquired the status of a *sotah*, thereby rendering that act of intercourse forbidden. The *Gemara* immediately rejects this: if the husband is not free of sexual sin, the waters do not merely fail to check him — they fail to check *her* as well. The *baraita* is explicit: *venikah ish me'avon vehishah hahi tisa et avonah* — the man will be clean of sin and the woman will bear her sin. *Bizman she'ish menukah me'avon*, when he is clean of that specific sin, the waters are effective. If he is not, the waters are effective for neither of them. So the waters cannot be "checking him" in any sense that's useful — if he has sinned, nothing gets checked at all. The *otoh* must therefore refer to the *bo'el*, the man she is suspected of having had relations with.

### Parallel Language

This raises a stylistic difficulty. If the *mishnah* intends the *bo'el*, why not name him as such? Later in the very same *mishnah*, when it says she is forbidden to the husband just as she is forbidden to the adulterer — *keshem she'asurah laba'al kakh asurah labo'el* — the *mishnah* uses the explicit term *bo'el*. So why say *otoh* in the first clause instead of *bo'el*?

The *Gemara* resolves this elegantly: *reisha aidei detana otah tana otoh* — because the opening clause describes what happens to *her*, using the word *otah*, the natural parallel is *otoh*. The linguistic symmetry of *otah/otoh* dictates the phrasing. In the second clause, where the *mishnah* introduces the word *ba'al* for the husband, the natural parallel becomes *bo'el*. The parallel pairs are *otah/otoh* and *ba'al/bo'el* — and in both cases, the referent is the same man: the one she is suspected of having had relations with.

### Yerushalmi & Gematria

*Tosafot* on 27b quotes a *Yerushalmi* that develops this theme richly, and it is worth pausing over because the *Bavli* itself contains no parallel discussion. The *Yerushalmi* in the name of Rav Tanchuma observes that the *gematria* of *hamar'arim* — the cursed waters — equals 248 times two, corresponding to the 248 limbs in his body and the 248 limbs in her body: *minyan hamar'arim neged remach evarim sheyesh bo veneged remach evarim sheyesh bah*. The word *mar'arim* contains a *resh-mem-resh-mem* for 240, and the remaining letters bring the total to 480 — that is, 248 doubled. The waters thus symbolically destroy two complete bodies, his and hers.

The *Yerushalmi* then adds a further and striking observation: the waters check her for every act of forbidden intercourse she receives from her husband after she has slept with the *bo'el* — *keshem shemayim bodkin otah al kol biah shehi mekabeles miba'alah le'achar habo'el kakh heim bodkin otoh*. This is surprising on multiple levels. First, it implies that the waters' effect is not a single event but cumulative, potentially multiplying across repeated violations. Second, it implies that the *bo'el* is held responsible not only for his own act but for the cascade of forbidden acts that follow from it — her subsequent, now-prohibited intercourse with her husband. He is punished, as it were, for her sins that occur after his sin, because he is the root cause of all that follows.

This also challenges a common assumption — that the *sotah* waters simply kill the guilty woman. The *Yerushalmi* pictures something more complex: an ongoing, iterative consequence that unfolds over time.

The *Yerushalmi* then addresses the legal logic of why her status works through her: *lo eshet amisecha lo sitein shichavsecha lezara litam'ah bah* — you make yourself *tamei* through *her*. Her status is the operative one. *Im haysa mezidah asurah, shogeges muteres* — if she was fully intentional, she is forbidden; if she was unwitting, she may be permitted to her husband.

## Forbidden Status Effects

### Brother Restriction

The *Yerushalmi* adds another striking consequence: *ukhshem she'asurah le'achiv shel ba'al kakh asurah le'achiv shel bo'el* — just as she is forbidden to the *ba'al's* brother (in the case of *yibum*), so too she is forbidden to the *bo'el's* brother. The case of the husband's brother is understandable: the marriage has been compromised, and therefore the *mitzvah* of *yibum*, which depends on the legitimacy of that marriage, can no longer apply. But that she is forbidden to the *bo'el's* brother is far more difficult to understand. What is the mechanism? What logic connects her status to the family of the man she is suspected of having sinned with?

One might suggest — and this is an appealing idea — that the concern is a practical one: if she were permitted to marry the *bo'el's* brother and then that brother died, the result would be a *yibum* obligation with the *bo'el* himself, to whom she is forbidden. We thus preemptively prohibit the marriage to the brother in order to foreclose that possibility. That would at least offer a functional rationale, even if the prohibition seems unusual at first glance.

### Status Works Through Her

The underlying principle that emerges from the *Yerushalmi* is that her status is entirely operative through her, not through the man. A man who commits adultery with a married woman does not change his own personal status. So if the suspected man actually committed adultery not with her but with some other woman, the waters will not affect him when she drinks, because it all passes through her. The principle is: *bah hadavar talui* — it is all dependent on her.

## Asymmetrical Outcomes

### Him Affected Only

The *Gemara* now considers an unusual scenario: what if she drinks the waters and he is affected but she is not? The immediate explanation offered is *zechus toleh* — merit. She must have done it, since otherwise the *bo'el* would not have been affected. But her merit delayed or concealed the impact on her. This explains the phenomenon, but only according to the view that merit prevents the waters from showing any visible effect. If she is definitely guilty but her merit shields her temporarily, yet the *bo'el* had no such merit, then he is affected and she appears unaffected.

### Intentional/Unwitting

But this explanation fails according to the view that even when merit protects someone, the effect is nonetheless visible — the person begins to deteriorate, even if slowly. Under that view, if she showed no sign whatsoever, the *zechus* explanation is insufficient.

The *Gemara* then considers and rejects the possibility that he drank contaminated water (*mayim megulim*) independently — the effects of snake venom do not replicate the specific physiological signs of the *sotah* waters, namely the belly swelling and the thigh collapsing.

The *Gemara* ultimately resolves the case: *she'hayah hu mazid vehee shoggeget* — he was intentional, she was unwitting. He knew he was sleeping with a married woman; she, through some unusual circumstance, did not — perhaps she mistook him for her husband. According to *Rashi*, the paradigmatic case of *shogeg* for the *sotah* is exactly this: both parties thought they were sleeping with their own spouses. Because she was *shogegget*, the waters do not affect her. Because he was *mazid*, he is affected.

One must ask: what exactly constitutes *shogeg* in the context of adultery? The *Gemara* and *Rashi* define it narrowly — she must have genuinely believed the man was her husband. This is far more restrictive than simply believing the act was permitted. A famous responsum addresses a case where a woman was told by a man claiming to be Elijah the Prophet that sleeping with him would bring the *Mashiach*, and she believed him. The *posek* ruled she was not considered *shogegget*, because she knew she was transgressing against her husband — the only valid *shogeg* is genuinely mistaking the man for one's spouse.

### Her Affected Only

The *Gemara* then considers the reverse: she is affected by the waters but he is not. The immediate explanation is again *zechus* — his merit prevented the waters from affecting him. This works according to the view that merit shields someone entirely, but fails according to the view that merit still allows visible deterioration. Contaminated water is again raised as an alternative explanation and again rejected for the same reason.

The simplest explanation is that she committed adultery with a different man altogether — *ela ani omer im acher nistarah* — and the man we suspected is innocent. That accounts for why he was unaffected.

### Marriage Question

If he was truly unaffected because he was not the adulterer, might she now be permitted to him if she is divorced? The *Gemara* says no: *tifater she'hayah hu shogeg vehee mezidah* — we must entertain the possibility that he was the adulterer but was *shogeg* while she was *mezidah*. Since he was *shogeg*, the waters did not affect him. But since she was *mezidah*, she remains forbidden to him. We cannot read his non-reaction as proof of innocence.

### Four Cases Summary

The *Gemara* now systematically works through all four combinations of intentionality. If he was *mazid* and she was *shogegget*: the waters affect him, not her; she is permitted to continue living with her husband. If he was *shogeg* and she was *mezidah*: the waters affect her, not him; she is forbidden to her husband, and she cannot go and be with the *bo'el* — *efshar lomar yatza mitachas yado vehayeta le'ish acher*, could it be that she divorces from her husband on account of her adultery and then goes to live with the very man she committed adultery with? The answer is no. If he was *mazid* and she was *mezidah*: both are fully affected. If he was *shogeg* and she was *shogegget*: perhaps neither is affected, and theoretically those two might end up together — although this is a deeply unusual case.

The operative principle in all of this: she cannot become permitted to the *bo'el* as long as one of them was *mazid*. The only scenario in which they could perhaps eventually be together is if neither had any intentional culpability.

## Source from Verses

### Vav or Repetition

Returning to the *Bavli* proper, the *Gemara* at the top of 28a takes up the question of the *drashot* underlying the *mishnah's* opening clause. The waters are described in the *Torah* text using the word *ba'u* — they shall enter — and this word appears three times in the relevant *parsha*. Moreover, in two of those appearances, the word is prefaced by a *vav*, giving *uva'u*. The question: is the *drasha* that the waters affect the *bo'el* derived from the extra *vav* (i.e., *uva'u* rather than *ba'u*), or from the repetition of the entire word *ba'u* itself?

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the second clause of the *mishnah* — *nitme'ah venitme'ah* — where similarly a word appears with and without a *vav*. But the *Gemara* notes that the same question applies there as well, so it cannot resolve our question from that source.

The resolution comes from the explicit statement of *Rebbi* later in the *mishnah*, who says *shenei pe'amim* — twice, i.e., the derivation is from the repetition of the word itself, not from the extra *vav*. From this we infer by contrast that *Rabbi Akiva* — the one from whose position *Rebbi* is departing — does indeed derive *drashot* from the extra *vav*. This is consistent with Rabbi Akiva's well-known method of deriving meaning from every additional letter in the text, a method he received from Nachum Ish Gamzu.

### Six Instances Explained

According to Rabbi Akiva, then, there are effectively six textual units requiring explanation: the three appearances of *ba'u* and the three accompanying *vav*s — *shita krai ketivei*. Each pair of units — one for her and one for him — teaches something distinct about the manner in which the waters operate.

### Tzava, Asiyah, Yedia

The three categories are *tzava*, *asiyah*, and *yedia*. The *tzava* is where the *pasuk* issues a divine declaration, as it were, dictating what the waters will do — *uvau hamayim hamarerim litzvot beten* — this is the moment of commanding or establishing the reality of what the waters accomplish. The *asiyah* is where the *pasuk* describes what actually happens in practice — the water enters and her belly swells. The *yedia* is where the *kohen* informs her explicitly what she should expect.

The *yedia* carries a specific legal significance. There is an apparent contradiction in the *psukim* about the order in which the waters take effect. In one place, the *Torah* speaks of the thigh collapsing first, then the belly swelling — which corresponds to the anatomical sequence of the act of relations, progressing downward. But when the waters actually take effect, the belly is affected first, because that is the order in which the water physically enters the body. The *kohen* must inform her of this discrepancy explicitly, so that she does not think the waters failed to work because they did not proceed in the order she expected. The pair of *vav*s attached to each of these three categories indicates that the same framework — command, action, and informing — applies equally to the *bo'el*.

### Three Instances

*Rebbi* disagrees. He does not derive *drashot* from extra *vav*s — *tlata krai ketivei*, there are simply three instances of the word *ba'u*, one for each of the three categories: *tzava*, *asiyah*, and *yedia*. Under this approach, since every appearance of the word *ba'u* is already needed for those three contexts, how does *Rebbi* know that the waters affect the *bo'el* at all?

The *Gemara* answers: from the phrase *litzvot beten velanpil yarech* — to cause a swelling of the belly and a collapsing of the thigh — where the *Torah* does not use the feminine possessive. Elsewhere the *Torah* says *vetzavtah betnah venaphlah yerechah* — her belly and her thigh, with the female possessive. Since that verse already covers her belly and her thigh, the earlier, unpossessed phrase is redundant if applied to her. Its purpose must therefore be to indicate that his belly and his thigh will also collapse — *betno veyerecho shebo'el*.

Rabbi Akiva can then respond: according to him, the unpossessed phrase serves a different purpose — it is the *yedia*, the *kohen*'s informing her of the order of effects (belly first, then thigh). *Rebbi* counters: if that were all, the *Torah* would have simply written *betnah verechah*, with the female possessive. The deliberately generic phrasing signals that two things are being taught simultaneously: first, the order of effects for her, and second — by virtue of the absence of the feminine possessive — that the same effects apply to him.

## Three Consequences

### Rabbi Akiva's View

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* addressing the word *nitme'ah*, which appears three times in the *parsha*. According to Rabbi Akiva, each appearance teaches a distinct consequence of her *tum'ah* status. The first: she is forbidden to her husband. The second: she is forbidden to the *bo'el*. The third: *echad litrumah* — she is disqualified from eating *terumah*. If she is the wife of a *kohen*, or even a *bat kohen*, her status has been so fundamentally compromised that she may no longer partake of *terumah*. *Divrei Rabbi Akiva.*

### Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Yishmael responds with a *kal vachomer* that derives a fourth consequence. There exist women who are forbidden to marry a *kohen* — such as a *grushah* — and yet who may still eat *terumah* as daughters of *kohanim*. These women have a compromised marital status relative to *kehunah* but retain their *terumah* rights through their lineage. Now: if this *sotah* woman cannot even eat *terumah* — as Rabbi Akiva derives — *eino din she'asurah likhehunah*? How much more so must she be forbidden to marry a *kohen* altogether? This is not a disagreement with Rabbi Akiva but an extension: Rabbi Yishmael adds a fourth *halacha* using the existing three as a springboard.

The practical significance of this point is noteworthy. *Rashi* clarifies: *afilu hi bat kohen uba'alah kohen, nifselah min haterumah* — even if she is herself the daughter of a *kohen* and her husband is also a *kohen*, she is disqualified from *terumah*. This is not merely a consequence of the marital relationship being compromised; it affects her personal status as a *bat kohen*. So even if her husband dies and the marital basis for eating *terumah* is gone, she remains disqualified on the basis of her own defiled status. That is precisely what gives Rabbi Yishmael's *kal vachomer* its force: since even her personal status as a *bat kohen* has been overridden by her *sotah* status, certainly she cannot now marry a *kohen*.

## Presumed Guilty Status

### Why Repetition?

The *Gemara* then poses a sharp question: given that the *Torah* itself repeatedly acknowledges uncertainty — describing both the case where she became *nitme'ah* and the case where she did not — why does it use the word *nitme'ah* repeatedly as though her guilt were established? *Im nitme'ah lamah shoseh? Ve'im lo nitme'ah lamah mashkeh?* If she is definitely guilty, why go through the process? And if she is innocent, on what basis does the husband have the right to subject her to it?

### Definitely Forbidden

The answer is that the *Torah* is telling us something precise: *magid lecha hakatuv shehasafek asurah* — she is forbidden by virtue of the doubt alone. The repeated use of *nitme'ah* is not a declaration of certain guilt; it is a declaration that the *safek* itself confers a definite forbidden status. She is *vadai asurah* not because we know she sinned, but because the formal process of *kinui* and *stirah* — warning and seclusion — has created an objective status of *issur* that persists until resolved.

This is a remarkable *chiddush*. The normal approach to a *safek* in *halacha* is either to be stringent as a precautionary measure or to permit on the basis of a *chazakah*. What is unusual about the *sotah* is that the *safek* itself — independent of any probability assessment — creates a *vadai* prohibition. She knows whether she sinned or not. She could be entirely innocent. And yet, the moment she formally becomes a *sotah* through the required legal process, she is objectively and certainly forbidden to sleep with her husband. This is not merely "we are being cautious because we don't know." It is a real, positive *issur*.

### Issur Asei Nature

*Tosafot* develops this point carefully. The question is raised: why does the Torah need to establish this special status at all? A *safek* of adultery would presumably be *assur* anyway on general principles. The obvious answer is that she herself knows the truth — but *Tosafot* presses further.

*Tosafot* notes that the *issur* here is an *issur asei* — a prohibition derived from a positive formulation. The *Torah* does not say "do not sleep with your husband"; it describes her as *tamei*. The operative prohibition flows from that status: *venitme'ah* — she is *tamei*, and therefore sleeping with her husband violates the status that has been declared. This is structurally similar to other cases of *issur asei*, such as the derivation that consuming human flesh is prohibited through the positive command *kol beheima tehorah tocheilu* — since human flesh is not a *beheima tehorah*, consuming it violates that positive imperative implicitly.

The consequence is significant: *afilu hi tehorah ne'eneshes b'veis din shel ma'alah im ba'alah k'chayvei asei* — even if she is entirely innocent of adultery, if she sleeps with her husband while she formally holds the status of a *sotah*, she has violated an *issur asei*, and she will be held accountable before the heavenly court together with her husband. The waters clearing her do not retroactively permit what was done while she held the *sotah* status. The prohibition is real and present from the moment the status attaches.

## Safek Tumah Principle

### Comparison to Sheretz

From the principle that the *sotah's* doubtful status creates a definite *issur*, the *Gemara* extrapolates to general laws of *tum'ah*. *Mikhan attah dan lesheretz* — from here you can derive the rules governing *safek tum'ah* for ordinary *tumah* cases such as contact with a *sheretz*.

The *kal vachomer* proceeds as follows. By a *sotah*, we do not treat *shogeg* as equivalent to *mazid*, and we do not treat *ones* as equivalent to *ratzon* — that is, involuntary exposure does not make her a *sotah*. Yet despite this, *asah bah safek kevadai* — the doubt is treated as certainty. By a *sheretz*, in contrast, the law makes no distinction at all between *shogeg* and *mazid* or between *ones* and *ratzon* — any contact creates *tum'ah* regardless of intent. Therefore, how much more so should a *safek* contact with a *sheretz* be treated as *vadai tum'ah*?

This is the source for the principle of *safek tum'ah bireshut hayachid safeko tamei* — a doubtful *tum'ah* in a private domain is treated as *tamei*.

### Private Domain

The *Gemara* then applies the parameters of the *sotah* case to define when this rule operates. *Umah sotah reshut hayachid af sheretz reshut hayachid* — just as the *sotah* situation involves a *reshut hayachid*, the *safek tum'ah* rule applies only in a *reshut hayachid*, not in a *reshut harabbim*. One might immediately note that *seclusion* and *reshut hayachid* are not identical concepts. The definition of *reshut hayachid* for purposes of *safek tum'ah* is not the same as the Shabbat definition. It has more to do with the degree of privacy and the question of who was present and capable of observing what occurred.

### Kinui & Sitrah

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question about the comparison. By a *sotah*, the reason we treat the doubt as a definite *issur* is that there are *raglayim ladavar* — circumstantial grounds for suspicion: there was a *kinui* (warning) and a *stirah* (seclusion in defiance of that warning). By a simple *safek tum'ah* case — did the *sheretz* touch the food or not? — there are no such grounds. How can the comparison hold?

*Tosafot* answers by reframing the role of *kinui* and *stirah* in the *sotah* case. The *kinui* and *stirah* do not establish that it is *likely* she committed adultery — they merely elevate the situation from irrelevant seclusion to a genuine *safek*. Without *kinui*, a woman going into seclusion with a man would not constitute a *safek* at all, because we operate with a *chezkas kashrus* — a presumption of propriety. We do not assume that every private encounter involves adultery. The *kinui* strips away that presumption and converts the situation into a genuine 50-50 *safek*. Once it is a genuine 50-50 *safek*, it is comparable to a simple *safek* of whether the *sheretz* touched the food. The *raglayim ladavar* do not tip the balance toward guilt; they merely create the *safek* in the first place.

### Knowledge Requirement

The second parameter derived from *sotah*: *umah sotah davar sheyesh bo da'at lisha'el* — the *sotah* situation is one in which someone could in principle be asked what happened, namely the woman herself. Therefore, *af sheretz davar sheyesh bo da'at lisha'el* — the rule of *safek tum'ah bireshut hayachid* applies only where there is someone present who could have knowledge of what occurred.

Why should this matter? One approach is that the function of treating the *safek* as a *vadai issur* is to force the matter toward resolution. If someone is present who knows what happened, declaring the situation *tamei* creates pressure for that knowledge to surface. If no one could possibly know, the declaration of *tum'ah* serves no such clarifying function. This is also why *reshut harabbim* tends toward *tahor* — not simply because many people were present, but paradoxically because in a very public setting, no one specific individual can be said to have observed what occurred. It is the intermediate case — a few people present, one of whom could know — that triggers the rule.

*Tosafot* notes an application of this principle to the case of a woman with a doubtful *niddah* status when others were present: even if people were nearby, they could not observe anything about her internal status. The "someone who could be asked" must be someone with actual epistemic access to the relevant facts.

### Final Rule

The *Gemara* concludes with the fully articulated rule: *davar sheyesh bo da'at lisha'el bireshut hayachid safeko tamei* — if someone could be asked and the event occurred in a private domain, the *safek* is treated as *tamei*. *Bireshut harabbim safeko tahor* — if it occurred in a public domain, the *safek* is treated as *tahor*. *Veshein bo da'at lisha'el, bein bireshut hayachid uvein bireshut harabbim, safeko tahor* — and if there is no one who could be asked, whether in a private or public domain, the *safek* is *tahor*.
