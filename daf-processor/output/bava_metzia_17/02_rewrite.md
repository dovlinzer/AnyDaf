# Bava Metzia 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtar Hakna'ah

### Hakna'ah Overview

The *gemara* returns at this point to the broader topic of finding and returning *shtarot*, revisiting the central questions that have occupied the preceding discussion: are we concerned for *pira'on* — the possibility that a debt has already been paid — and are we concerned for *kenunya*, a collusive scheme? The particular document under consideration here is the *shtar hakna'ah*.

A *shtar hakna'ah* differs from an ordinary loan document in a fundamental way. In a standard loan document, Michael borrows a thousand dollars from Dov, and the liens on Michael's property become active only at the moment the money is actually lent. There is therefore a standing concern that the document was written in anticipation of a loan that was never ultimately made — what the *gemara* terms *kasav lilvos velo lava*. A *shtar hakna'ah*, by contrast, involves a *kinyan* performed at the time of writing, which immediately activates the lender's rights and liens over the borrower's property. From the very moment the document is executed, the obligation is in force — regardless of whether the money is ever actually transferred. The concern of *kasav lilvos velo lava* therefore does not apply.

The remaining question, however, is *pira'on*: perhaps the borrower subsequently paid off the debt, the lender returned the *shtar* to him, and the document then fell from his possession into the marketplace. Should that possibility prevent us from returning the found document to the lender?

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel rules: *hamotzi shtar hakna'ah bashuk* — one who finds a *shtar hakna'ah* in the marketplace — *yachziru lib'alim*, should return it to its owner, that is, to the *malveh* for whom it was written.

The reasoning proceeds in two steps. First, the concern of *kasav lilvos velo lava* is inapplicable by definition, since the *kinyan* made the obligation effective immediately. Second, and more significantly, we are not concerned for *pira'on* — *lo chaishinan lipira'on* — because *de'im isa dipar'ei, mikra hava kara leh*: if the borrower had in fact paid off the debt, the lender would have torn up the document on the spot. The assumption is that a paid debt is immediately accompanied by the destruction of its documentary evidence, and therefore a *shtar hakna'ah* found in the marketplace is presumed to still represent an outstanding obligation. This is the ruling of Shmuel, the same authority referenced several *dapim* earlier (14a) as the one who holds *lo chaish lipira'on*.

### Nachman's Testimony

Rav Nachman adds a striking personal recollection in support of this practice. He reports that his father was one of the scribes (*soferim*) in the court of Mar Shmuel, and that when Rav Nachman was approximately six or seven years old, he remembered that Shmuel's court would publicly announce: *hanei shtarei aknyeisa demishtakchei bishuka, nahadrinhu lemareihu* — those *shtarei kinyan* found in the marketplace should be returned to their owners, meaning to the *malveh* on whose behalf they were drawn.

### Court Documents Rule

Rav Amram invokes a forthcoming *mishnah* in support of the same principle: *kol ma'aseh beis din harei zeh yachzir* — any document issued by a court should be returned to its owner. The *gemara* observes that this *mishnah* is implicitly premised on the absence of concern for *kasav lilvos velo lava*, since court-issued documents are active from the moment of their writing — there is no question whether the underlying obligation came into being. The only remaining concern is *pira'on*, and since the *mishnah* instructs us to return such documents without reservation, it appears that *lo chaishinan lipira'on*: we are not concerned that the debt was paid. This apparently corroborates Shmuel's ruling.

### Chaltata Exception

Rav Zeira, however, proposes a limiting distinction. He suggests that the *mishnah*'s ruling concerns specifically *shtarei chaltasa v'adrachta* — court documents assigning specific property in payment of a debt (*chaltata*) or authorizing a creditor to locate and seize the debtor's property (*adrachta*). In such cases, the concern of *pira'on* is structurally inapplicable or at least greatly attenuated: either the creditor has taken possession of the designated property or he has not, and one can often verify the matter directly. *Rashi* explains *chaltata* as a document by which the court assessed a specific property in satisfaction of a debt. *Tosafot* raises a difficulty with respect to *adrachta*, noting that without specification it is less clear how one can determine whether the property has been seized, and appears to suggest either a different reading or that the term is absent in some textual versions.

The point of Rav Zeira's distinction is that the *mishnah* may be speaking precisely of these special categories of court writs where there is no meaningful concern for *pira'on* — and therefore it cannot serve as a proof for Shmuel's broader position that we are never concerned for *pira'on* in ordinary loan documents.

## Piraon Debate

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges Rav Zeira's claim that *shtarei chaltata* are categorically free of *pira'on* concerns. Rava cites the ruling of the Nehardeans: *shuma hadar atrei asar yarchai shata* — even after a piece of property has been formally assessed and awarded to a creditor in payment of a debt, the debtor retains the right to redeem it within twelve months by paying back the amount owed. If so, one cannot look at the physical state of the property and determine with certainty whether the debt has been resolved: the creditor may have taken the land, only for the debtor subsequently to have bought it back. The *shtar* in the creditor's hand would thus potentially represent a paid obligation, and the concern for *pira'on* resurfaces even for *shtarei chaltata*.

### Redemption Rights

The *gemara* goes further, citing Ameimar, who reports: *ana mi'Nehardea ana v'savira li d'shuma hadar l'olam* — I am from Nehardea and it is my view that the right of redemption is perpetual, not limited to twelve months. The logic, as the *gemara* develops it, is that since the creditor received the land only as a substitute for his monetary claim, and since the transaction was at root about money rather than land, the debtor retains an ongoing equitable right to recover his ancestral property by paying off what he owes.

Under either position — the Nehardean twelve-month rule or Ameimar's unlimited right — it becomes impossible to determine merely by inspecting the property whether the underlying debt has been satisfied. The debtor could be sitting on the land and yet the debt might have been paid; or the creditor might hold the land and the debt might have been redeemed. Rav Zeira's claim that *chaltata* documents are inherently free of *pira'on* concerns thus appears untenable.

### Rava's Rationale

Rava offers a different explanation for why we are not concerned for *pira'on* in *chaltata* cases, one that is specific to the structure of those transactions and does not extend to ordinary debt documents. The principle is: *ee hu de-afsid anafshei* — if the debtor in fact redeemed the property and failed to secure documentation, he has only himself to blame.

The argument unfolds as follows. At the moment the debtor paid back the money and reclaimed the land, he had two responsible options available to him: either to obtain the original *shtar chaltata* and tear it up (*de-be-idna de-para ibai lay le-mikrayeh le-shatara*), thereby destroying the evidence of the creditor's entitlement, or, alternatively, to obtain a new bill of sale from the creditor documenting that he was receiving the land back (*ee nami le-michtav shatara achrina ilvay*). His failure to do either is his own negligence, not an excusable oversight.

### Chaltata vs Debt

Why is the debtor held to a higher standard of diligence in *chaltata* cases than in ordinary loan transactions? The *gemara* explains through the doctrine of *shuma hadar* itself. The creditor's right to the land was, from a strict legal standpoint, absolute: *midina ara lo bay le-mihadar* — the land need not revert to the debtor at all. The land was taken in full and final payment of the debt; it belongs to the creditor. The debtor's right to redeem it exists only by virtue of rabbinic enactment grounded in the verse *ve-asisa ha-yashar ve-ha-tov be-einai Hashem* — "you shall do that which is right and good in the eyes of God." The Rabbis said to the creditor: since it was always about the money and not the land, do the right thing and give it back if he pays you.

What is remarkable, as one might note, is that *ve-asisa ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — ordinarily invoked as a general exhortation to go beyond the letter of the law — has here been institutionalized as a concrete *halacha*. The question of how far this institutionalization extends, and whether a creditor who has already improved the property can refuse to return it, or whether cases resembling *ribbis* arise, is left for another occasion.

The practical consequence is this: since legally the land is the creditor's, and the debtor is in reality executing what amounts to a new purchase when he pays the money and retrieves the property, he is acquiring Michael's land, and *ibai lay le-michtav shtar zvini* — he is obligated to obtain a bill of sale. A person purchasing land will always demand documentation. If the debtor failed to do so, his negligence disqualifies him from complaining that the *shtar chaltata* is still in the creditor's hand.

By contrast, in an ordinary loan transaction, the power dynamic is quite different. The debtor is under pressure to pay; the creditor can claim he does not have the document available today and will produce it tomorrow. Furthermore, the debtor may be obligated to reimburse the creditor for the costs of the original scribe (*apishitei de-safra zayer lay*), and until he does, the creditor may reasonably withhold the document. These realities make it understandable that the debtor might pay up without immediately obtaining the *shtar* to tear up. In such a case, we cannot say his failure to secure the document was irresponsible, and *pira'on* therefore remains a legitimate concern in ordinary *shtarei chov*.

The upshot of this extended analysis is as follows. The *mishnah* that instructs us to return *maaseh beis din* documents cannot serve as a proof for Shmuel's general position that we are not concerned for *pira'on*, because that *mishnah* may address only *shtarei chaltata*, where *pira'on* is not a concern for specific structural reasons unique to those documents. The broader debate between Shmuel — *lo chaishinan lipira'on* — and Rabbi Yochanan (as transmitted by Rabbi Abbahu), who holds *chaishinan lipira'on* even for validated documents, therefore remains unresolved by the *mishnah*.

## Validated Debt Rules

### Validated Docs

Rabbi Abbahu, citing Rabbi Yochanan, states: *hamotzi shtar chov bashuk, af al pi shekatuv bo hanpek, lo yachazir labaalim* — one who finds a debt document in the marketplace, even if it bears a court validation of its signatures (*hanpek*), should not return it to its owner. The ruling proceeds from less to more: *lo mibaya* that if there is no validation, the document should not be returned, since the concern of *kasav lilvos velo lava* applies — perhaps the money was never borrowed. But even where there is a *hanpek*, meaning the signatures have been authenticated by the court, which demonstrates that the creditor presented it and the loan was made — even then, *lo yachazir*, because *chaishinan lipira'on*: we are concerned that the debt was subsequently paid.

This stands in direct tension with Shmuel, who holds *lo chaishinan lipira'on* in all such cases.

### Mishnah Challenge

Rabbi Yirmiyah raises an objection to Rabbi Abbahu from the same *mishnah* cited earlier: *kol maaseh beis din harei zeh yachzir* — all court-issued documents should be returned. This appears to indicate that we are not concerned for *pira'on* even in cases where the underlying obligation is established, directly contradicting Rabbi Yochanan's ruling.

### Debtor Reliability

Rabbi Abbahu's father — or, more precisely, reading the text carefully, Rabbi Yirmiyah addresses Rabbi Abbahu as *beri*, "my son" — responds: *lav kol maaseh beis din shavin* — not all *maaseh beis din* documents are the same. And the distinction he draws is not, at this point, the distinction between *chaltata* and ordinary debt. Rather, he explains: *ella kegon shehuchzak kafran* — the *mishnah*'s instruction to return the document applies specifically where the debtor has been established as a known denier (*kafran*). In such a case, his claim of having paid is simply not believed, and therefore we have no reason to withhold the *shtar* from the creditor.

Under Rabbi Yochanan's general rule, *pira'on* is normally a sufficient concern to prevent us from returning a found debt document. But the *mishnah*, which instructs us to return court documents, is addressing a special circumstance: the debtor in question is someone already proven to be a liar, whose claim of payment carries no weight. The instruction to return the document is therefore not evidence against Rabbi Yochanan's general position — it is merely the application of a specific exception.

Rava immediately questions the scope of this principle. He asks: *mishum dehuchzak kafran chada zimna, tu lo para klal* — simply because this person was once established as a denier, does that mean his claim of payment can never be credited? The suggestion that a single proven lie permanently forecloses a person's credibility as a litigant in all subsequent dealings seems disproportionate and potentially unjust. A person might genuinely have paid a debt even if he previously lied about a different matter. Moreover, in the *ishtamutei* framework discussed earlier, even a debtor who denied a debt may not have been genuinely seeking to steal — he may have been merely trying to defer the obligation. Does such a person lose all standing to assert a claim of payment in his own defense?

These considerations lead Rava to abandon this resolution entirely. *Ella amar Rava masnisin b'shtar chaltaza v'adrachta uch'Rabbi Zeira* — Rava reverts to the distinction offered by Rav Zeira: the *mishnah* is discussing *shtarei chaltata and adrachta*, which, as established, do not raise *pira'on* concerns for the structural reasons described above. This leaves intact Rabbi Yochanan's general position that, for ordinary validated debt documents, one does not return them due to concern for *pira'on*.

## Huchzak Kafran

### Payment Claim Setup

Since the discussion has raised the concept of *huchzak kafran*, the *gemara* now develops this principle in its own right. The framework is introduced as follows. Rav Yosef bar Minyumi reports in the name of Rav Nachman: if a court issues an order to David — *amru lo tzei ten lo* — "go out and pay him," and David subsequently claims *parati*, "I have already paid," he is believed (*ne'eman*). Rashi adds that this belief is subject to David's taking an oath of *shvuat heset*.

This is a striking ruling. After a full judicial proceeding in which Michael has proven his case and the court has formally ordered David to pay, David is permitted to walk out and declare that he has already paid — and he is believed. The *rishonim* grapple with why this should be so, given that a court ruling carries the force of a *mitveh bishtar* and grants the creditor liens. The answer is that while the ruling creates public liens like a *mitveh bishtar*, it does not, unlike a written *shtar*, undermine the debtor's general credibility to claim payment. Just as a debtor who borrowed without a document is believed to say he has repaid, so too here David is believed — without documentation — to say he has paid pursuant to the court's order. The obvious potential for abuse is noted.

One practical consequence: if Michael comes to court afterward requesting that they write him a writ memorializing the debt — in order that David's future claim of *parati* should not be credited — the court does not issue one (*ba malveh lichtov ein kotvin venotnin lo*). The reasoning is that perhaps David has already paid in the interim, and we do not issue new documentation against a debt that may have been discharged.

### Order Types

There is a question, however, about whether the same rule applies when the court's formulation was not *tzei ten lo* — "go out and pay him" — but rather *chayav ata liten lo* — "you are obligated to pay him." The earlier tradition apparently distinguished between these two phrasings: *tzei ten lo* is a direct execution order with mandatory force, whereas *chayav ata liten lo* is more in the nature of a declaratory judgment. Under this view, only the former creates the conditions under which David would be believed to claim payment, and only in the former case would Michael be prevented from obtaining a subsequent writ.

### Zvid Harmonization

Rav Zvid, however, reporting in the name of Rav Nachman, rules that there is no operative distinction between the two formulations: *bein tzei ten lo bein chayav ata liten lo, ve'amar parati ne'eman* — in either case, if David claims he has paid, he is believed. Rav Zvid applies the same consequence to both: the *malveh* cannot subsequently obtain a writ, because in either case the debtor may have already paid.

Interestingly, the *Rosh* rules in accordance with Rav Zvid's formulation, and for an empirical reason: in his time and place, people do not meaningfully distinguish between *tzei ten lo* and *chayav ata liten lo* in practice. They treat both as mandatory court orders equally. The *Rosh*'s ruling is thus grounded partly in the realities of how court orders are received and understood by the litigating public.

### Witness Contradiction

Having established that David is believed to say *parati* in the wake of a court order, the *gemara* now asks: what if David claimed *parati*, but witnesses then appeared and testified that he had not in fact paid? For example, David says: "I paid him last Monday at 1:00 PM, in front of the bank." The witnesses respond: "That cannot be — we were with you apple picking all day Monday." In this scenario, David has been caught lying.

The *gemara* then considers: suppose David comes back and says, "Fine, I was lying about Monday, but I have now paid him." Is he still believed?

The answer depends on the nature of the original court order. If the court had issued a *tzei ten lo* — an outright execution order — and David then lied, claiming to have paid when witnesses establish he had not, then *huchzak kafran le'oto mamon*: for this specific debt, David has been established as a denier and his subsequent claim of *parati* is not credited.

### Denial vs Payment

If, however, the court's original formulation was *chayav ata liten lo* — the declaratory formulation — and David similarly lied about having paid, the *gemara* rules that he remains believed when he later claims he has paid: *lo huchzak kafran le'oto mamon*.

The rationale is *ishtamutei hu d'ka mishtamit minei*: David was not actually trying to escape his obligation permanently. Rather, he figured — *savar achti lo pasuku rabbanan l'dinei* — that since the court had not yet issued a final execution order, perhaps the judges would reconsider and change their ruling. He was buying time, stalling until the court's judgment would be finalized with a *tzei ten lo*. Under this reading, his lie about payment was not genuine theft — it was a tactical deferral, premised on the belief that the case was not yet fully adjudicated.

This rationale is admittedly difficult. The person did lie. He was not planning to steal, perhaps, but he was clearly making a false claim. Why, then, is his subsequent claim of *parati* credited? The *gemara* seems to be drawing a distinction between a moral failing that undermines fundamental credibility and a strategic evasion that, while dishonest, does not rise to the level of *kafran*. One might observe that the court is attempting to account for the realities of human behavior under the pressure of litigation — while still requiring justice to be done. Whether that rationale fully satisfies is, as one may note, a question worth sitting with.

## Kafran Applications

### Rabba Case

Rabba bar bar Chana reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan a case that directly illustrates *huchzak kafran*: "You owe me a hundred dinars." The defendant replies: "I owe you nothing." Witnesses then testify that the debt exists. The defendant, now cornered, says: "Fine — but I have already paid." In this scenario — where the person first made a *fundamental* denial of the entire claim — *huchzak kafran le'oto mamon*: his subsequent claim of *parati* is not believed, even though ordinarily a debtor's claim of payment is credited.

The distinction between this case and the *ishtamutei* scenario is important. The person who said *chayav ata liten lo* and then claimed he had paid was, on one interpretation, not truly trying to escape the obligation — he was misreading the procedural posture of the case. But a person who flatly denies that any debt exists, and is then proven by witnesses to be lying, has made a categorical misrepresentation. When he subsequently claims he has already paid, there is no procedural excuse to explain away the original lie, and so he cannot recover credibility on this particular claim.

### Shabtai Case

The *gemara* illustrates this principle with a concrete case: Shabtai the son of Rav Marinus wrote into his daughter-in-law's *kesubah* an obligation to provide her with a particular silk garment (*itzlita d'milta*), accepting personal responsibility for this obligation. When the *kesubah* was lost (*irkas ksubatah*), he denied that any such obligation had ever existed: *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — "it never happened." Witnesses then testified that he had indeed written the obligation. He then claimed: *l'sof amar lehu parati* — "In the end, I paid her." The matter came before Rabbi Chiya, who ruled: *huchzak kafran l'ota itzlita* — for the obligation regarding this garment, he is established as a denier, and his claim of payment is not credited.

This case differs slightly from the *tzei ten lo* scenario in that it was not a court proceeding but a direct debt dispute. The principle, however, is the same: where a person makes a categorical denial of an obligation, is proven false by witnesses, and then shifts to claiming payment, the *huchzak kafran* doctrine removes his credibility on that specific claim.

### Oath Extension

Rav Avin, citing Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, extends the *huchzak kafran* principle from monetary debts to *shvu'ot*. If a court obligated someone to take a *shvua* in a dispute — as in the classic case of *modeh b'miktzat*, where one admits to part of a claim and is obligated to swear regarding the remainder — and that person then claims: "I have already made the *shvua*, we went to Starbucks together and I swore there," and witnesses then testify that no such *shvua* was made, and the person subsequently says: "Fine, that was a lie, but I made the *shvua* yesterday" — then *huchzak kafran l'ota shvua*: his further claims of having fulfilled the oath are not believed.

This application is noteworthy for a reason beyond the *kafran* doctrine itself. It presupposes that a *shvua* mandated by a court can be validly discharged outside the court setting — that one may, in effect, "pay" the *shvua* to the other party informally, without the court administering it. The *shvua* is treated as a kind of obligation that can be satisfied privately, and the person who owes it is believed to say he has done so — just as he would be believed to say he has paid a money debt. That the *shvua* is treated in this way — as a transferable obligation rather than strictly a judicial instrument — is itself a remarkable premise of the *sugyah*.

### Oath Distinction

Rabbi Abbahu, when presented with this teaching, confirms and clarifies its scope. He says: *mistavera milta d'Rav Avin, shechiyvuh shvua b'beis din* — the rule makes sense precisely because the court obligated him to take the *shvua*. In that case, lying about the *shvua* is analogous to the *tzei ten lo* scenario: the court has issued what amounts to a binding directive, and evading it by falsely claiming it has been fulfilled renders one a *kafran* for that obligation.

But *aval chiyav atzmo shvua* — if the person voluntarily obligated himself to make a *shvua* without any court mandate, and then claimed he had made it, and was proven by witnesses to have lied — he remains believed when he subsequently claims to have fulfilled the oath. The reason is again *ishtamutei ka mishtamit lei*: since the *shvua* was self-imposed and not court-ordered, he did not regard himself as genuinely bound to it, and his initial lie was a casual evasion rather than a deliberate act of defiance of a legal obligation. He therefore does not lose credibility in the way one does when contradicting a *beis din*-mandated commitment.

Rabbi Avin himself later clarifies, when the matter is brought back to him, that this was precisely his intent from the outset: the rule of *huchzak kafran* applies to a *shvua* that the court obligated, but not to one that the person voluntarily assumed.

This is corroborated by a parallel tradition: Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Rav, and some say in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *haya chayav l'chavero shvua b'beis din, ve'amar nishbati, ve'eidim me'idim oso shelo nishba, huchzak kafran l'osa shvua* — if the court obligated him to swear, he claimed to have sworn, and witnesses proved otherwise, he is established as a *kafran* for that oath going forward.

## Document Dating

### Same-Day Docs

Rabbi Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, addresses a further scenario in the context of returning found debt documents: *hamotzi shtar chov bashuk, v'chasuv bo hanpek, v'chasuv bo zmano bo bayom, yachzir l'ba'alin* — if one finds a validated debt document dated to today, one may return it to the creditor. The reasoning follows the familiar two-part structure. The concern of *kasav lilvos velo lava* is neutralized by the *hanpek* — the court validation demonstrates the money was lent. And *pira bas yoma lo chaishinan* — we are not concerned that a debt would have been borrowed and repaid within the same day. Since the document is dated today, and same-day repayment is exceedingly unlikely, we infer that the debt is still outstanding, and the document may be returned to the *malveh*.

### Reuse Problem

Rabbi Zeira challenges this ruling. He reminds Rabbi Assi of his own reported teaching in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *shtar shelava bo u'para'o eino chozer veloveh bo* — if David borrowed money on a *shtar* and then paid off the debt, he cannot reuse that same document for a subsequent loan. The reason is that *shekvar nimchal shibudo* — the liens created by the document were discharged at the moment of repayment, and a new loan would require a new document with newly created liens. Using the old document for a new loan would be like using a burned match.

Now, Rabbi Zeira notes, when would such reuse even be a concern? If the original document was dated weeks or months ago and the debtor wants to reuse it for a new loan today, there is an independent problem: the document would be *mukdam*, predated, which invalidates it. *Shtarot hamukdamim pesulin* — predated documents are invalid. Therefore, the only scenario in which reuse is genuinely tempting and technically conceivable — the document has not yet become *mukdam* — is when the original loan and its repayment occurred on the same day as the new loan. In other words, the concern of document reuse exists precisely on the same day.

*Alma parei inshi b'yomei* — this shows that people do in fact pay off debts on the same day. Otherwise, there would be no reason to rule that a same-day document cannot be reused. This seems to undercut Rabbi Assi's assumption that same-day repayment is so rare that one need not worry about it when returning a found document.

### Reuse Resolution

Rabbi Assi responds: *mi kamina d'la para* — I never claimed that same-day repayment never happens. *Lo shechiach inshi l'mifrei b'yomei kamina* — I said it is uncommon. It is precisely because it is not the norm that one need not be concerned about it for the purpose of returning a found *shtar*. The ruling that one cannot reuse a document after repaying the loan is a general restriction that applies even to the rare case, and the ruling that one may return a same-day document is a probabilistic assessment that applies despite that rare case. There is no inconsistency: Rabbi Yochanan can simultaneously hold that same-day repayment does technically occur and that it is rare enough not to generate a halakhic concern in the context of returning found documents.

An alternative reading is offered by Rav Kahana: the ruling to return a same-day document applies specifically when the *loveh* himself admits he has not yet paid (*kshe'chayav modeh*). This reading avoids any tension with the reuse problem entirely, but raises its own questions.

### Creditor Cooperation

The *gemara* then addresses the concern that the debtor might be falsely claiming not to have paid — claiming the debt is still outstanding — specifically to engineer the return of the *shtar* so that it can be reused for a subsequent loan, thus saving the cost of engaging a new scribe. This scheme would require the debtor to say "I haven't paid yet" when in fact he has, in order to get the document back into the creditor's hands, after which they would reuse it for a new loan.

The *gemara* dismisses this concern by noting that the creditor would not cooperate: *de'im kein malveh gufei lo shavik* — if this is what the debtor is planning, the creditor himself would refuse to go along with it. Why? *Savar shami bei rabbanan u'mifsidi li* — the creditor would be afraid that the rabbis would find out and he would lose his loan entirely. The creditor knows that reusing a discharged *shtar* is legally impermissible, and he will not jeopardize his investment for the debtor's convenience.

### Early Dating

By contrast, the *gemara* notes that in the parallel case of *mukdam* documents, one might ask the same question: if the *malveh* would refuse to cooperate in any fraudulent scheme, why are we ever concerned about predated documents? The answer is that in the *mukdam* scenario, the creditor actually has a strong incentive to cooperate. An earlier date on the document gives the creditor earlier liens, allowing him to seize properties that were transferred by the debtor between the fabricated date and the actual loan date. The gain from earlier liens outweighs the risk of the document being voided. Therefore, *meinach nicha lei v'lo amar lei v'lo midi* — the creditor is content to accept the risk and say nothing. The concern for *mukdam* documents is therefore legitimate, whereas the concern for same-day reuse is not, because in the same-day scenario the creditor gains nothing from cooperating with the scheme.

The practical conclusion is: a found *shtar chov* bearing today's date and a *hanpek* may be returned to the creditor. The concern of *pira'on* is minimal given same-day repayment's rarity, and the concern of collusive reuse is neutralized because the creditor has no incentive to cooperate.

## Ketubah Principles

### Maaseh Beis Din

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, citing Rabbi Yochanan, introduces a further principle: *ha'toein achar maaseh beis din, lo amar klum* — one who asserts a claim against a *maaseh beis din*, his words are nothing. Specifically, in the context of the *kesubah*, which is an obligation that exists by force of rabbinic enactment even without a written document: if a woman's husband claims he has paid her *kesubah*, he is not believed on the basis of his bare assertion. *Kol maaseh beis din k'man d'nakit shtar b'yadei dami* — every *maaseh beis din* is treated as though the claimant is holding a *shtar*, and the burden falls on the one who claims to have discharged it to bring proof.

### Ketubah Proof

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba then asks Rabbi Yochanan whether this principle is not in fact already explicit in a *mishnah* in *Kesubos*: *hotziah get v'ein imah kesubah, govah kesubatah* — if a woman produces a *get* but has no accompanying *kesubah*, she nevertheless collects her *kesubah*. This implies that a woman without a physical *kesubah* document can still collect, meaning that the husband's claim of *parati* is not believed even in the absence of the written instrument. The *kesubah* obligation, being a *maaseh beis din*, stands without documentary proof.

Rabbi Yochanan responds with a statement that is itself an implicit claim to having illuminated the *mishnah* rather than simply derived from it: *had I not lifted the pottery shard, you would not have found the pearl beneath it.* That is, the *mishnah* alone, while consistent with the principle, could have been explained differently; it required Rabbi Yochanan's insight to clarify what it truly teaches.

### Documentation Requirement

Abaye takes up this challenge. He questions whether the *mishnah* truly proves the breadth of Rabbi Yochanan's principle, suggesting an alternative reading: *eima b'makom she'ein kosing kesuvah askinan, de'get hainu kesuvashah* — the *mishnah* may be addressing a place where *kesubot* are not customarily written, and in such a place the *get* itself serves as the functional equivalent of the *kesubah*. When she produces the *get*, she has a document; she is not collecting on the basis of a bare *maaseh beis din* alone.

Under this reading, in a place where *kesubot* are written, the rule would be: *ei nakta kesuvah gavya ve'ei lo lo gavya* — if she holds the *kesubah* she collects, if not she does not. The husband would be believed to claim payment if she produces no documentation. Abaye thus challenges whether the *mishnah* really establishes Rabbi Yochanan's sweeping principle.

### Widow Erusin

Abaye himself, however, immediately retracts this reading. He notes that if one follows his logic to its conclusion — that in a place where *kesubot* are written, a woman always needs a physical document to collect — then the case of an *almanah min ha'eirusin*, a widow from *eirusin*, becomes impossible. Such a woman is entitled to her *kesubah* from the time of the *erusin*, since the fundamental obligation arises then. But the *kesubah* document is typically not written until *nissuim*, and an *almanah* has no *get*. If documentary proof is always required, *b'mai gavya* — how would she ever collect? The heirs of the estate could simply say their father paid, and she would have no recourse.

*Im kein mah ho'ilu chachamim b'takanatam* — if this were the rule, what was the point of the Rabbis' enactment that a woman is entitled to a *kesubah*? The institution would be rendered worthless in precisely the case of the *almanah min ha'eirusin*, because anyone could defeat her claim with a bare assertion of payment.

Abaye therefore concedes that Rabbi Yochanan's principle is correct: the *kesubah* obligation is so firmly grounded as a *maaseh beis din* that the husband — or his heirs — is not believed to claim *parati* without proof. The practical consequence for the husband is that he must ensure he obtains the *kesubah* document itself from the woman upon payment, or a written receipt (*shovar*), and must retain that documentation. *Tosafot* notes that the standard presumption is *kotvin shovar* — the debtor may demand a receipt — and that expectation is the natural corollary of the stringent protection extended to the woman's claim.

### Braita Analysis

The *gemara* then raises a question about the foundational premise of Abaye's argument. Mar Kashisha the son of Rav Chisda asks Rav Ashi: the entire proof rests on the assumption that an *almanah min ha'eirusin* is entitled to a *kesubah* even without a written document. But how do we know this? The basic *kesubah* entitlement might be established only at *nissuim*, with various reciprocal obligations of support and the *tosefet* — the additional amount offered by the husband — all becoming operative only from *nissuim* onward.

The questioner cites a *mishnah*: *nis'es o nisgareshah, bein min ha'eirusin uvein min hanissuim, govah es hakol* — whether she married or was divorced, whether from *eirusin* or from *nissuim*, she collects the full amount. Does this not prove that the *kesubah* obligation exists from *erusin*?

The *gemara* demurs: perhaps this *mishnah* addresses only the case where a *kesubah* was actually written from the time of *erusin*. If so, the novelty of the ruling is not that the obligation exists without a document, but that it exists in full — including the *tosefet* — despite having been written at the stage of *erusin*. And the specific opposition it addresses is the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds: *shelo chasav lah ela al menas l'chonsah* — when a *kesubah* was written at *erusin*, the husband's intention in writing it, particularly with respect to the *tosefet*, was directed toward the eventual *chuppah*. If she is divorced before *nissuim*, she should not receive the *tosefet*, because that additional sum was contingent upon the marriage being consummated. The *rabbanan* reject this and say that once it is written, she collects everything — *govah es hakol* — even from *erusin*. The *mishnah*'s ruling thus addresses a debate about a case where a document exists, not a case where the obligation arises without any document.

The question of whether Abaye's proof — that an *almanah min ha'eirusin* is entitled to collect her *kesubah* without a written document — can be grounded in this *braita*, or requires some other source, remains open at the close of the *daf*. The full resolution is deferred to the next session.
