# Eruvin 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bittul & Joint Eruv

### Osrin & Me'arvin

The *gemara* here on 67a continues to examine the conditions under which *bittul* can operate effectively. The foundational principle, at least in *Shmuel*'s initial position, is that *bittul* requires two concurrent criteria: first, that the individual in question is *osrin* — that is, he creates a problem for the others by virtue of his presence — and second, that he is *me'arvin*, meaning he could in theory have joined the *eruv*. Both of these must be satisfied before *bittul* can function.

A further question then arises: what if both criteria are technically met, but the relationship between the parties is characterized as *mechatzar lechatzar* — one courtyard to another, rather than members of a single, unified domain? According to one version of *Shmuel*'s position, as represented by *Abaye*, even a *mechatzar lechatzar* configuration can suffice. According to another view associated with *Rava*, however, as long as the parties stand in a *mechatzar lechatzar* relationship, *bittul* cannot function between them. And there is also the position of *Rabbi Yochanan*, who rejects *Shmuel*'s framework entirely and holds that one can never perform *bittul me'reshut lereshut* — that is, *bittul* cannot operate across distinct domains at all.

### Eruv in A, Problem in B

Against this backdrop, the *gemara* presents a scenario in which the parameters converge in a specific and instructive way. Imagine two courtyards, designated here as A (the inner courtyard) and B (the outer courtyard). A joint *eruv* has been made, with the *eruv* physically placed in A. One of the residents of B then forgot to participate. In this case, A can simply disregard B: it has its *eruv*, B is the source of the problem, and A can function independently. As the *gemara* states at the very opening of 67a: *shachach echad min hachitzonah velo eirev* — if one of the outer courtyard's residents forgot and did not join the *eruv* — *vadai pnimis muteres* — the inner courtyard is certainly permitted. *De'achda dasha umishamsha* — it effectively closes its door and operates within its own domain.

The reason this works even for those who resist *bittul me'reshut lereshut* is that in this scenario, *bittul* is not really being used to bridge two distinct domains. What is happening, rather, is that B as a whole waives its participation in the *eruv*. So long as B maintains its participation, the presence of a problematic member in B contaminates A as well; but once all of B performs *bittul* — waives its share in the *eruv* — A is free to function on its own. *Vachitzonah asurah* — and B remains forbidden.

### B Waives Rights

If, however, the problem is located in A — that is, one of the members of A forgot — the situation is far more complicated. Since the *eruv* is housed in A, and since B is part of the *eruv* arrangement together with A, one cannot simply cut B off. For the *bittul* to be effective, the forgetful member of A would need to be *mevatel* not only to his fellow residents of A, but also to the members of B. And that is precisely what is prohibited according to those who hold *ein bittul me'reshut lereshut* — one cannot perform *bittul* across separate domains.

### Gal'u Dasha

This is the conclusion the *gemara* arrives at, and it encapsulates the most restrictive position on *bittul*: the mechanism functions reliably only when the parties constitute a single, coherent domain. The case where it works is effectively one in which B is simply excised — and what is labeled *bittul* is little more than a formal waiver that achieves the conceptual exclusion of the problematic party.

## Problem in Inner Court

### Huna's Challenge

*Amar leh Rav Huna brei deRav Yehoshua leRava* — Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua posed a challenge to Rava. He questioned the ruling that if a member of A forgot, both courtyards are forbidden. The *gemara* had explained that this is because the *eruv* encompasses B, and therefore *bittul* by the forgetful member of A would require him to be *mevatel* to B as well — which constitutes *bittul me'reshut lereshut* and is impermissible. But Rav Huna argued: why can't it work?

### Huna's Solution

His proposed solution: *livatel bar pnimis lipnimis* — let the member of A be *mevatel* only to his fellow residents of A, and *vetipoki chitzonah vetishtari bahadeihu* — let B automatically become permitted along with them. The logic is that once the forgetful member performs *bittul* to one member of his group, it takes effect for the entire group. Since the *eruv* is in A and the problem is in A, he directs his *bittul* to his own courtyard, and B gets drawn in automatically without requiring any direct *bittul* across domains.

### Eliezer vs Rabbanan

The response is: *ke'man* — according to whom are you saying this? *Ke'Rabbi Eliezer.* Rav Huna's proposed mechanism aligns with Rabbi Eliezer's view, *de'amar eino tzarich levatel reshus le'chol echad ve'echad* — that one need not be *mevatel* to every individual separately; a single act of *bittul* directed at one can be effective for all. But the *gemara* responds that when it was said that the case of A forgetting presents a problem, the ruling follows *lerabbanan* — *de'amri tzarich levatel le'chol echad ve'echad* — the Rabbis hold that one must be *mevatel* to each individual. Since B's members are residents of a separate domain, one would need to be directly *mevatel* to each of them, which is *bittul me'reshut lereshut* and cannot be done.

### Restrictive Position

This is therefore the most restrictive approach: *bittul* requires not merely *osrin u'me'arvin* — it demands that the parties be effectively one domain. The mechanism works only when B can simply be cut off, which is the case when the problem originates in B and B waives its participation. When the problem is in A, no clever workaround can circumvent the requirement, under the Rabbis' view, that each individual must be addressed directly.

One might ask what precisely *bittul* is, since it seems as though waiving one's rights to one member of an *eruv* group should carry over to the whole. The difficulty here is that at the moment *bittul* is needed, the *eruv* is not functioning — the outlier's presence has disrupted it. So the cohesion of the group cannot yet be assumed as a basis for the *bittul* to spread automatically. Furthermore, *bittul* is inherently weaker than an *eruv*: once one performs *bittul*, one cannot carry from one's own house into the courtyard, because doing so would reassert one's property rights and annul the *bittul*. This is why *bittul* is primarily a Shabbos emergency measure rather than a preferred arrangement, and why the parameters for its operation are so carefully delimited.

## Chisda & Sheshes

### Their Relationship

The *gemara* introduces a celebrated vignette: *Rav Chisda ve-Rav Sheshes, ki paguei bahadi hadadi* — when Rav Chisda and Rav Sheshes would encounter one another, *Rav Chisda martzin sifvosei mi-matneisa d'Rav Sheshes* — Rav Chisda's lips would tremble on account of Rav Sheshes' mastery of *beraisos*. Rav Sheshes was a consummate *baki*, a repository of *mishnayos* and *beraisos*, and whenever a halachic exchange was in the offing, Rav Chisda feared being outmaneuvered by a well-placed citation. On Rav Sheshes' side, *marta kulei gufei mi-pilpulei d'Rav Chisda* — his entire body would tremble from Rav Chisda's sharpness of analysis.

This is the classic *gemara* dichotomy between *Sinai* and *oker harim* — one who holds the tradition in its entirety and one whose analytic power can move mountains. In an era without books, where access to information depends entirely on what is stored in a scholar's memory, these two types represent complementary and mutually intimidating forms of mastery.

### Two Houses Setup

*Ba'ei minei Rav Chisda me-Rav Sheshes* — Rav Chisda, the analytically sharp one, posed a question to Rav Sheshes. *Shnei batim mishnei tzidei reshus harabbim* — two houses on opposite sides of a *reshus harabbim*, which can never be joined by an *eruv*. *Ubau nochrim vehikkifum mehitzah beshabbos* — non-Jews came on Shabbos and built a wall enclosing the *reshus harabbim*. A *mechitza* erected on Shabbos is halachically valid, so the enclosed space is now a *reshus hayachid*. However, no *eruv* was in place between the two houses when Shabbos began. The question: can *bittul* be performed now?

### Bittul Without Eruv

The question is posed specifically according to the view that *ein bittul reshus mechatzar l'chatzar* — *bittul* cannot operate from one courtyard to another unless there is sufficient unity between the parties. On that view, *lo tiba'i lach* — there is no question, it obviously cannot work. The argument runs as follows: if even in a case where the two courtyards *could* have joined together in an *eruv* — where *me'arvin* is satisfied — and yet we say *ain bittul mechatzar l'chatzar* because there is no *osrin*, then certainly in this case, where when Shabbos began the two houses had no possibility of making an *eruv* between them — the enclosed space not yet existing — there is no basis for unity and *bittul* cannot function.

The genuine question arises, Rav Chisda explains, *aliba d'man d'amar yesh bittul reshus mechatzar l'chatzar* — according to Rabbi Yochanan, who permits *bittul* even across separate domains. On Rabbi Yochanan's view, one might ask whether the reason *bittul* functions is because the parties had the potential of forming an *eruv* together (*me'arvin*), or whether Rabbi Yochanan holds that *bittul* is always available regardless of prior capacity. The former reading would mean that even Rabbi Yochanan could not allow *bittul* here, since there was no possibility of forming an *eruv* before Shabbos. The latter would mean Rabbi Yochanan allows *bittul* universally whenever it would be helpful. *O dilma lo shna* — or perhaps the distinction makes no difference.

This question is particularly resonant because Rabbi Yochanan was also the one who permitted *bittul* in a *churva* — a courtyard or ruin — a context with no relation to the conceptual unity question. This suggests Rabbi Yochanan's leniency on *bittul* may be categorical, not contingent on potential *eruv* formation. So the question is whether his position allows *bittul* even in this new and entirely unprecedented scenario.

### Sheshes Dismisses

*Amar lei ein m'vatlin* — Rav Sheshes responded simply: it does not work. Even according to Rabbi Yochanan, there is not enough here to constitute a unit, and *bittul* cannot function. A further question followed: what if the non-Jew who had appeared on Shabbos and disrupted the *eruv* then died on Shabbos? According to *Tosafos*, the scenario is that a non-Jew arrived on Shabbos, rendering the *eruv* inoperative and requiring both rental from him and *bittul*, and then died. Now that he has died, can *bittul* alone suffice? Rav Sheshes' response: *ani omer mevatlin, veHamnuna omer ein mevatlin* — I hold it works, and Rav Hamnuna holds it does not.

What is striking about this exchange is that it does not quite bear out the introduction's promise of a clash between analytic brilliance and encyclopedic tradition. Rav Sheshes does not deploy a *mishna* or *beraisa* in response; he simply gives bottom-line rulings. Perhaps this is itself the point. Rav Sheshes, knowing he cannot best Rav Chisda in a conceptual debate, declines to engage on that terrain. He offers no *shakla vetarya*, no discursive reasoning — only apodictic conclusions. Here is how I hold, here is how Rav Hamnuna holds, end of discussion. Avoiding the arena where Rav Chisda would have the advantage may be precisely what the story is meant to illustrate.

## Nochri Back Door

### Valley Opening OK

The *gemara* transitions to a series of cases involving a non-Jew whose presence in the courtyard creates a problem because he cannot participate in an *eruv*. The principle introduced is: *nochri sheyesh lo pesach arba'ah al arba'ah patuach lebik'ah* — a non-Jew who has a back door of four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* opening into a valley. Even if he uses his front door the entire day — *afilu machnis umotzi gemalim vekeronos kol hayom kulo derech mavoi* — bringing camels and wagons in and out through the front — *eino oser pnei mavoi* — he is not considered a problem for the *mavoi*. The reason: *bepsitcha demayachad lei bah nicha lei* — because of his private back door opening into a secluded space, we say that is his preferred domain.

This is, on its face, a remarkable legal fiction. The man is plainly using the front door all day long. Yet the halacha says that since he possesses a private opening to a private space, he effectively does not inhabit the courtyard in the legally relevant sense. His rights are considered to be exercised in his backyard, not the *chatzer*, and so he poses no problem there. The rationale is that a non-Jew whose legal footprint lies elsewhere — whose domain is conceptually elsewhere — does not assert rights over the courtyard, and therefore the *eruv* of the other residents remains intact.

### Karpef Question

*Ibaia lehu: patuach lekarpef mahu?* — the question was raised: what if the back door opens not into a wide valley but into a *karpef* — a walled enclosure, an open space that is bounded rather than unlimited? Does the same fiction apply? *Amar Rav Nachman bar Ami mishmei d'Ulla* — in the name of Ulla: *afilu patuach l'karpef* — even if it opens to a *karpef*, the principle holds.

The inquiry itself is revealing. The *gemara*'s willingness to question whether a *karpef* suffices suggests some underlying assumption that the fiction tracks something real: perhaps the idea is that a sufficiently large and private space genuinely draws the non-Jew's use away from the courtyard. When the space is smaller and more enclosed, the fiction becomes harder to sustain.

### Beit Sa'atayim Limit

*Rava v'Rav Yosef d'amri tarvaihu* — Rava and Rav Yosef jointly place a limit on this ruling: *beit satayim nochri beit satayim oser* — if the non-Jew's back enclosure is only a *beit satayim* or smaller, he does create a problem. If it is larger than a *beit satayim* — more than five thousand square *amos*, roughly a hundred feet by a hundred feet — then we maintain the fiction and say that is his domain, and he is not a problem.

The logic turns on the *beit satayim* threshold's halachic significance. A walled enclosure that is *yoter mibeit satayim* and was not enclosed for the purpose of habitation (*lo hukaf l'dirah*) is treated rabbinically as a *karmelit* — a domain in which carrying is prohibited. As a *karmelit*, it is not usable space for the non-Jew on Shabbos. His effective domain therefore remains the courtyard, and he is a problem there. If, however, the enclosure is *beit satayim* or smaller, it functions as a proper *reshus hayachid* — a genuinely private space — and we can say the non-Jew uses that space as his primary domain. *Tosafos* sharpens this: if the enclosure is *beit satayim* or smaller, it is halachically part of his house, which makes the fiction less sustainable in one direction but more coherent in another — it is all simply his private space.

### Jew Reverse Case

Interestingly, the reverse applies when the party with the back opening is a Jew who forgot to participate in the *eruv*. *Beit satayim eino oser yoter m'beit satayim oser* — a *beit satayim* enclosure does not make the Jew a problem; more than a *beit satayim* does. The inversion is striking. When the backyard is *yoter mibeit satayim* and is therefore a *karmelit* — prohibited for carrying — the Jew cannot use it on Shabbos. His only viable space is the courtyard, and so he genuinely asserts his presence there and becomes a problem. When the backyard is *beit satayim* or smaller and functions as a *reshus hayachid*, the Jew has a private space he can carry within, and we say he prefers it to the courtyard.

*Rashi* explains the asymmetry between the Jew and the non-Jew: for the Jew, Shabbos is Shabbos, and he wishes to carry in whatever private space is available to him; for the non-Jew, who does not observe Shabbos in the same sense, he requires more expansive space before we say it constitutes his primary domain. Whatever the precise rationale, the practical picture is that for the non-Jew the larger the space the better, while for the Jew the reverse is true — the fiction runs in the opposite direction.

## Reshus Status

### Yochanan's Teaching

*Amar Ulla amar Rabbi Yochanan* — the *gemara* now shifts into a more foundational discussion of *reshus* classification. The teaching: *karpaf yoter mibeit satayim shelo hukaf ledira* — a walled enclosure larger than a *beit satayim* that was not enclosed for purposes of habitation. The question of what constitutes *hukaf ledira* — enclosed for habitation — is itself a major issue with real-world implications for *eruv* construction; generally, any space that people regularly traverse or use is considered *ledira*, while spaces that go entirely unused, such as a large lake within the walls, raise genuine concerns.

Rabbi Yochanan's teaching continues: *ve'afilu kor ve'afilu korayim* — even if this enclosure is vast, the size of a *kor* or even greater, *hazorek letocho chayav* — one who throws an object into it is biblically liable. That is: biblically, no matter how large a walled enclosure is, it remains a *reshus hayachid*. Rabbinically it is treated as a *karmelit* and one may not carry within it — but that is only a rabbinic restriction. The biblical status is *reshus hayachid* in all cases where walls are present. *Mai ta'ama mechitza hi* — because it is genuinely walled. *Ela she-mechuseret diyurin* — it is simply that no one inhabits it, which is a rabbinic deficiency, not a biblical one.

### Rock in Sea

*Metiv Rav Huna bar Chinana* — Rav Huna bar Chinana challenged this position with a *beraisa*. The case: *sela she-bayam gavoah asara* — a rock in the sea that is ten *tefachim* tall and four by four *tefachim* wide. The sea is a *karmelis*. Given the rock's dimensions, it is a *reshus hayachid*, and accordingly: *ein metaltelin lo mitocho layam velo mehayam letocho* — one may not carry from the rock to the sea or from the sea to the rock, because this constitutes crossing from *reshus hayachid* to *karmelis*. *Pachus mikan metaltelin* — if the rock is smaller or lower, one may carry, because then it too is a *karmelis*, and crossing from one *karmelis* to another is rabbinically treated more leniently than crossing the *reshus hayachid* boundary.

Now, the *beraisa* then adds: *ad beit satayim* — this applies until a certain size. The question is on which clause does the *beit satayim* qualifier fall. *Ileima aseifa* — if it falls on the latter clause, the case where the rock is low and therefore a *karmelis*, that makes no sense: if the rock is a *karmelis* regardless of size, why would it matter whether it exceeds *beit satayim*? Moving from one *karmelis* to another is not a problem irrespective of the size of either. Therefore, *ella lav areisha* — it must apply to the first case, where the rock is tall enough to be a *reshus hayachid*. The *beraisa* is saying: the restriction on crossing the line from the rock to the sea applies only when the rock is *beit satayim* or smaller — that is, when it expresses itself as a genuine *reshus hayachid*. But once it is *yoter mibeit satayim*, one may carry across. The implication: at that point, it is no longer functioning as a *reshus hayachid* but as a *karmelis* — from which Rav Huna bar Chinana deduces a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan. If a space larger than *beit satayim* truly remains a biblical *reshus hayachid*, why does the prohibition on crossing the line dissolve?

### Rava Dismisses

*Amar Rava* — Rava responded sharply: *man d'lo yada litaratzei masnisa tiyuvta motiv lei* — a person who does not know how to properly explain a *beraisa* should not use it as a challenge. Learn the *beraisa* first, then deploy it. Rava's reading: the *beraisa* does indeed apply to the first clause, but the *beit satayim* reference does not mean that beyond that threshold one is permitted to cross the line. Rather, implicit in the prohibition on crossing the line is the permission to carry *on* the rock. The *beraisa* is noting that this permission to carry on the rock applies only up to *beit satayim*; beyond that, one may not carry on the rock. There is no implication that crossing the line becomes permitted — only that carrying within the larger space becomes prohibited as a *karmelis*. Whether this reading of the *beraisa* is fully persuasive is debatable; the text's plain structure does not obviously support it, and Rava's dismissive tone perhaps overreaches his interpretive case.

### Hen Amru Vehen

Rav Ashi offers a different and more compelling resolution. *Amar Rav Ashi le'olam areisha* — Rav Ashi agrees that the *beraisa* applies to the first case and that once the rock exceeds *beit satayim*, one is indeed permitted to cross the line. He does not dispute Rav Huna bar Chinana's reading. His answer is: *hein amru vehein amru* — they said, and they said. The Rabbis who enacted the rabbinic restrictions are the same Rabbis who defined their scope. The prohibition on crossing from *reshus hayachid* to *karmelis* is itself rabbinic. The classification of a *yoter mibeit satayim* enclosure as a *karmelis* is likewise rabbinic. When the Rabbis designated a large walled space as a *karmelis*, they simultaneously and consistently permitted crossing the line into it from the surrounding *karmelis*, because to do otherwise would be internally contradictory.

### Carrying Priority

The *gemara* presses further: *u'mai shna* — why do the Rabbis favor one restriction over the other? When facing a *yoter mibeit satayim* space, why do they maintain the prohibition on carrying within it while waiving the prohibition on crossing the line, rather than the reverse? The answer: *tocho shchi'ach* — carrying within the space is the more frequent concern, the more common scenario. *Mitocho leyam umiyam letocho lo shchi'ach* — crossing the line between the rock and the sea, in contrast, is uncommon; one can only go a distance of less than four *amos* in any case before being back over the line. The Rabbis' *gezeirah* targets what is most practically significant. If they had maintained the crossing-the-line prohibition while waiving the carrying restriction, the public would observe that one is permitted to carry in this large enclosed space and might conclude that it is a full *reshus hayachid*, leading to carrying throughout its entirety — *ve'asi letaltulei dechulei*. To prevent that inference, they prioritize the restriction on internal carrying. Keeping the crossing-the-line prohibition would paradoxically undermine their message, so they let it go in order to hold the more important line.

## Brit Milah Water

### Spilled Water

The *gemara* moves to a vivid practical episode involving *Rabba*. *Hahu yanuka de'ishtapich chamimei* — there was a child for whom a *bris milah* was to be performed, and the hot water prepared for post-operative care was spilled. It was Shabbos, and hot water was needed — held in one house, required in the courtyard or another location. *Tosafos* notes that this scenario pertains specifically to before the *bris milah* was performed; once the *bris* takes place, the situation becomes one of *pikuach nefesh* and the analysis changes entirely.

### Rabba's Plan

*Amar lei Rabba* — Rabba told those present: *naisu lei chamimei migov beisai* — go bring the hot water from my house; I have hot water we can use.

### Abaye Objects

*Amar lei Abaye: veha lo arvinnan* — but Abaye pointed out that no *eruv* had been made. This observation is itself remarkable on two levels. First, that Rabba apparently did not know they had failed to make an *eruv*. Second — and more puzzling — why had they not made an *eruv* at all? Establishing an *eruv chatzeros* requires minimal effort; it can be set up with something as simple as a *matzah* and left in place for an entire year. Why would a courtyard go without one?

### Rabba's Rejoinders

Rabba replied: *nismoch achituf* — we can rely on the *shituf mevo'os*. There is an opinion that a *shituf mevo'os* — the joining of the alleyway, done at the broader level encompassing multiple courtyards — can, *bedieved*, substitute for individual *eruv chatzeros*, permitting carrying within the individual courtyards. *Amar lei: veha lo shaftinnan* — Abaye informed him that the *shituf* had not been done either. The exchange has a certain wry humor: Rabba is spending his Shabbos absorbed in learning, entirely removed from the logistical realities of the courtyard.

Rabba then suggested: *namelei lenochri leiselei* — tell a non-Jew to bring the hot water. It was at this point that Abaye wished to raise a challenge, but *Rav Yosef* — who was present, and who was both Abaye's and Rabba's teacher — stopped him. *Veloch shavkan Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef would not let him raise the objection.

### D'Rabbanan vs Torah

*De'amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Kahana: ki havinnan bei Rav Yehuda, havi amar lan* — for Rav Yosef cited a principle he had received from Rav Kahana in the name of Rav Yehuda: when we were in the study house of Rav Yehuda, he taught us the following. *Bide'oraisa* — in a matter of biblical law, when there is a debate about permissibility, *mosvinnan tiyuvta vehadar avdinan ma'aseh* — we first raise all challenges before acting. A biblical prohibition demands that we exhaust our doubts before proceeding. *Biderabbanan* — in a matter of rabbinic law, where someone has articulated a position permitting a given action, *avdinan ma'aseh vehadar mosvinnan tiyufta* — we act first and raise challenges afterward. If a competent authority has stated a position and you have a question about it, hold your question; when the pressing need is rabbinic in nature, act on the ruling and debate it later.

This principle is less radical than it might first appear. It does not mean one presumes leniency in rabbinic matters without any basis. The scenario is that an authority has actually ruled, and a student wishes to challenge that ruling. Rav Yosef's point is: in a *d'rabbanan* context, do not let the challenge stand in the way of action.

### Hazah vs Amira

After the episode concluded, Rav Yosef turned to Abaye and invited him to share what he had wanted to ask. *Amar lei: detanya — hazah shevus, ve'amirah lenochri shevus* — from a *beraisa*: sprinkling (*hazah*) the ashes of a *parah adumah* is a *shevus* — a rabbinic prohibition — and instructing a non-Jew (*amira lenochri*) is also a *shevus*. The *beraisa* deals with a case where *erev Pesach* falls on Shabbos and a person is *tamei*. His only path to bringing the *korban Pesach* is through sprinkling the ashes of *parah adumah* on Shabbos. Yet we do not do so — we do not override the rabbinic prohibition of *hazah* even to enable the *korban Pesach*, a *mitzvah she'yeish bo kares*. If so, Abaye argued, by the same logic we should not override *amira lenochri* for the *bris milah*. Let the *bris* be delayed rather than violate a rabbinic prohibition.

The comparison carries force: both *bris milah* and *korban Pesach* are *mitzvos asei she'yeish bo kares*, positive commandments whose violation incurs *kares*. If we forego *korban Pesach* rather than perform a rabbinic violation, ought we not forego the timely *bris milah* rather than instruct a non-Jew? The *bris*, after all, can be performed the next day — it will be *shelo bizmano*, not at the appointed time, but it will be done. *Korban Pesach Sheini* is a makeup option as well, though perhaps a more significant deferral.

### Ma'aseh Distinction

*Amar lei Rav Yosef: velo shani lach bein shvus d'is beih ma'aseh l'shvus d'leis beih ma'aseh?* — Rav Yosef replied: do you not distinguish between a rabbinic prohibition that involves an *act* and one that does not? Sprinkling — *hazah* — is a *shvus d'is beih ma'aseh*: the person performing it is executing a physical action, a genuine act. *Amira lenochri* — instructing a non-Jew — is *shvus d'leis beih ma'aseh*: the speaker merely speaks; the physical act is performed entirely by the non-Jew. This removes the Jewish actor one significant step from any violation, making *amira lenochri* a categorically weaker rabbinic prohibition.

The *gemara* then adds a further clause: *d'ha'amar lo amar lenochri zil achim lei* — and he did not even tell the non-Jew to heat the water. Rabba instructed the non-Jew only to carry — and carrying is itself only a rabbinic prohibition in this context. Thus the *amira* was directed at a *d'rabbanan*, not a *d'oraisa*. This creates what is termed *shvus dishvus* — a double-layered rabbinic prohibition: the instruction itself (*amira lenochri*) is *d'rabbanan*, and the act being performed (carrying through an unenclosed space) is also *d'rabbanan*. This double remove from biblical prohibition is what permits the action, at least in a case of *tzorech mitzvah*.

This small response of Rav Yosef carries enormous halachic weight, and it is worth noting that its interpretation is contested. One reading is that Rav Yosef's primary point is the *d'leis beih ma'aseh* distinction — that *amira lenochri* is inherently weaker because it involves no act, and this lower threshold makes it permissible in circumstances of need. On this reading, other rabbinic prohibitions that do involve an act would require a higher threshold. The second reading focuses on the *shvus dishvus* element — that the specific permissibility here rests on the doubling of rabbinic removes, and that even *amira lenochri* would not be permitted if the act being commissioned were a *d'oraisa*.

These two readings undergird a wide range of practical *halachos*, including the parameters for *amira lenochri* for a *choleh she'ein bo sakanah* and the thresholds for *tzorech mitzvah* on Shabbos. The broad consensus follows the more lenient reading, under which *amira lenochri* is a sufficiently attenuated rabbinic act that it can be permitted across a meaningful range of pressing needs — a consequence that is, as the *gemara*'s own framing suggests, a considerable relief.

*Chag sameach* to all.
