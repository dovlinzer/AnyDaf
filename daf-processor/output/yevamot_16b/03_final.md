# Yevamot 16b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixed Marriages & Mamzer

### Chaggai & Tarmod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְקַבְּלִים גֵּרִים מִן הַקַּרְדּוֹיִים וְהַתַּרְמוֹדִים. אִינִי?! וְהָא תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: אֵין מְקַבְּלִים גֵּרִים מִן הַקַּרְדּוֹיִים!
>
> **Translation:** **And one accepts converts from the Karduyim and the Tarmodim.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rami bar Yeḥezkel teach** in a *baraita*: **One does not accept converts from the Karduyim?**

The Gemara on 16b continues from where the previous daf left off. Two testimonies were attributed to *Chaggai HaNavi*: first, that *tzarat habat* is *muteret*, in accordance with the position of *Beit Hillel*; and second, that *geirim* may be accepted from the inhabitants of Tarmod and Karduyim. That second point implies that those inhabitants are presumed to be non-Jews — as distinct from a concern that they might be Jews afflicted with *mamzerus*. The significance of that distinction is considerable: *mamzerus* cannot be resolved, whereas non-Jewish identity can be addressed through conversion. The Gemara had already noted a contradiction in the statements of Rabbi Yochanan regarding whether *geirim* could be accepted from Tarmod, and it is precisely that tension the Gemara now moves to resolve.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתַּרְמוֹד מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְסָבַיָּא, חַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם עַבְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם בְּנוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that the Sages do **not** accept converts **from Tarmod? Rabbi Yoḥanan and the Elders disagree** about **this** matter. Although they concur that converts from Tarmod are not accepted, they disagree with regard to the reason. **One** of them **said** that it is **due to the servants of** King **Solomon.** Solomon built a city in Tarmod (see I Kings 9:18), and his gentile servants, taking advantage of their status and power, married Jewish women unlawfully. Therefore, it is possible that the inhabitants of Tarmod and their descendants are *mamzerim*. **And** the other **one said** that it is **due to the daughters of Jerusalem,** who were taken captive and raped and gave birth to children among the gentiles.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the concern with Tarmod in the first place? *Pligi beih Rabbi Yochanan v'Savia* — Rabbi Yochanan and Savia, an exceedingly rare *Amora*, debate the matter. One holds the concern is *mishum avdei Shlomo* — because the non-Jewish servants of King Solomon had intermarried with Jewish women from that region. The other holds the concern is *mishum bnos Yerushalayim* — because of what was done to the young women of Jerusalem at the time of the *Churban Beit HaMikdash*. In both cases, the immediate question is: if there is a Jewish mother, what is the problem? If *yichus* follows the mother, these descendants should simply be Jewish. Why should *mamzerus* even enter the picture?

### Avdei Shlomo Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם עַבְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, קָסָבַר: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who said** that it is **due to the servants of Solomon,** this is logical, as **he holds** that in the case of **a gentile or a slave who had intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*.** Accordingly, as the servants of Solomon were slaves and they engaged in intercourse with Jewish women, their children are considered *mamzerim*.

The Gemara itself acknowledges the tension: *bishlama l'man d'amar mishum avdei Shlomo* — the concern about the servants of Solomon is intelligible if one holds the view that *kasavar oveid kochavim v'eved haba al bat Yisrael havlad mamzer* — that a non-Jew or a slave who has relations with a Jewish woman produces a *mamzer*. This is, to put it plainly, a striking position. The conventional understanding, which is also the normative ruling, is that *matrilineal descent* governs: the child of a Jewish mother is Jewish, full stop. Yet here is a view that the child is not merely Jewish, but specifically a *mamzer*.

The logical mechanism driving this view requires explanation, and *Tosafot* provides it in a careful and layered analysis.

### Tosafot on Mamzer

*Tosafot* in the *dibur hamatchil* of *kasavar* first proposes that this position follows Rabbi Akiva's opinion that a child born from a union prohibited by a *lav* — a simple negative commandment — carries the status of *mamzer*. This is the principle of *mamzer m'chiyuvei lavin*. That reasoning works more naturally for the case of an *eved*, where at least some *pasuk* can be read to establish a biblical *lav* against relations with a slave. But the case of a non-Jew is more complicated. Counterintuitive as it may seem given the profound sociological weight attached to intermarriage — parents historically sitting *shiva*, communities ostracizing such unions — the technical prohibition of relations with a non-Jew may in fact be only *d'rabanan*, at least where the non-Jew is not from the seven Canaanite nations. The famous principle of *kana'im pog'im bo*, applied in the episode of Pinchas, operates only when the act is committed publicly; in private, the biblical prohibition may not exist. So *Tosafot* asks: if the act itself is not biblically forbidden, why would the child be a *mamzer*?

*Tosafot* offers one resolution: perhaps the *mamzer* status here is only *mid'rabanan*. But then *Tosafot* advances a second and more conceptually significant answer. The relevant passage begins approximately a third of the way into the line starting with *al bat Yisrael havlad mamzer v'al korchacha Rabbi Akiva hi*, at the words *v'im tomar*. There, *Tosafot* writes: *bishlema me-eved havai mamzer d'v'eved ika lav ela me-oved kochavim amai havai mamzer* — granted that a slave produces a *mamzer* because there is a *lav*, but a non-Jew, why should that produce a *mamzer*? One answer the Gemara suggests is a rabbinic enactment — *Beis Din shel Shem gazru*. And then: *u'me-d'oraisa bias heter hi* — biblically, the act may actually be entirely permitted in private. Yet despite that, *Tosafot* continues: *v'yesh lomar*, one can say, even though the act is technically permitted *me-d'oraisa* in private, *keivan de-lo tafsi bah kiddushin havai mamzer k'mo b'eved* — since *kiddushin* cannot take effect in such a union, the child is a *mamzer*, just as in the case of a slave.

This is the crucial insight. The standard framework for *mamzerus* links it to the weight of transgression — *issurei kares* or *issurei lavin* according to Rabbi Akiva. What *Tosafot* is pointing to here is an entirely different axis: not the weight of the prohibition, but the impossibility of *kiddushin*. In the case of *issurei kares*, *kiddushin ein tofsin* — the betrothal does not take effect. The same is true, *Tosafot* argues, with a non-Jew, even absent any biblical prohibition: there is simply no capacity for the *Torah* to recognize *kiddushin* in such a union. The *mamzer* status, on this reading, is not a consequence of having violated a prohibition; it is a consequence of having produced a child from a union in which the marital bond is structurally impossible.

This reframing has a striking implication: a child born from relations with a *niddah* — which is unquestionably an *issur kares* — is not a *mamzer*, precisely because *kiddushin* is possible with a *niddah* and may even be present simultaneously. Conversely, a child born from a union with a non-Jew — where there may be no biblical prohibition at all — is a *mamzer* on this view, because *kiddushin* cannot take effect. The defining characteristic of *mamzerus*, on this approach, may have less to do with the severity of transgression and more to do with the structural incapacity for a recognized marital bond.

It bears emphasis that we do not rule in accordance with this position. The normative *halakha* is that the child of a Jewish mother and a non-Jewish father is simply Jewish, without any taint of *mamzerus*. At most, there may be questions regarding *pagum l'kehuna* — the fitness of such a child to marry a *kohen* — and that is a genuinely contested issue that arises in contemporary cases involving children of Jewish mothers and non-Jewish fathers. But the underlying concern of *mamzerus* itself is not applied.

### Bnos Yerushalayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ גּוֹיִם לַהֵיכָל, הַכֹּל נִפְנוּ עַל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב, וְהֵם נִפְנוּ עַל בְּנוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָשִׁים בְּצִיּוֹן עִנּוּ בְּתוּלוֹת בְּעָרֵי יְהוּדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **When the gentiles entered the Sanctuary** during the conquest of Jerusalem, they **all turned to** plunder the **silver and the gold** they saw there, **but** the warriors of Tarmod **turned to the daughters of Jerusalem, as it is stated: "They have ravished the women in Zion, the maidens in the cities of Judah"** (Lamentations 5:11). According to the opinion that children born of relations between gentiles and Jewish women are *mamzerim*, all the children born to these women are *mamzerim*.

The Gemara then turns to the second opinion — that the problem with Tarmod stems from *bnos Yerushalayim*. The *meimra* of Rav Yosef and the *rabbanan*, both in the name of Rabbah bar bar Channah, describes what occurred when the nations entered the *Heichal*. The debate between Rav Yosef and *rabbanan* is relatively minor — one says twelve thousand men and six thousand archers, the other says twelve thousand men among whom were six thousand archers, amounting to either twelve thousand or eighteen thousand total. What matters is the act they committed: *hakol nifnu al kesef vezahav* — everyone else rushed for the gold and the silver, *vehem nifnu al bnos Yerushalayim* — this group turned specifically to the young women of Jerusalem. The verse invoked is *nashim beTzion inu, betulot b'arei Yehuda* — the women of Zion were violated, the young women of Judah's cities. The plain meaning of *inu* here is rape.

The halachic consequence is structurally identical to the *avdei Shlomo* scenario: in both cases we are dealing with offspring of Jewish women and non-Jewish men. According to the view that such unions produce *mamzerim* — whether on the basis of Rabbi Akiva's principle regarding *chiyuvei lavin* or on the basis of *kiddushin ein tofsin* — the descendants in Tarmod would be *mamzerim* and could not simply convert. According to the normative view, which does not recognize *mamzerus* in such cases, the descendants would simply be Jewish and there would be no bar to their accepting *geirim* from there, since there would be no one who requires conversion at all.

### Sefer Torah Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״יָדוֹ פָּרַשׂ צָר עַל כׇּל מַחֲמַדֶּיהָ״ — זֶה עַמּוֹן וּמוֹאָב. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ גּוֹיִם לַהֵיכָל, הַכֹּל נִפְנוּ עַל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב, וְהֵם נִפְנוּ עַל סִפְרֵי תוֹרָה, אָמְרוּ: זֶה שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ ״לֹא יָבֹא עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי בִּקְהַל ה׳״ — יִשָּׂרֵף בָּאֵשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** further **said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The adversary has spread out his hand upon all her treasures;** for she has seen that the heathens have entered into her Temple, concerning which You commanded that they should not enter into Your congregation" (Lamentations 1:10)? **This** is referring to **Ammon and Moab.** How so? **When the gentiles entered the Sanctuary, all turned to** plunder **the silver and the gold, and** the soldiers from Ammon and Moab **turned to the scrolls of Torah** to destroy them. **They said:** Is **this** the scroll **in which it is written: "An Ammonite and a Moabite shall not enter into the assembly of the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 23:4)? **Let it be burnt by fire.**

The Gemara proceeds to a related *drasha* on the verse *yado parash tzar al kol machmadeha* — the enemy stretched out his hand over all her precious things. Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan identifies *tzar* here as Amon and Moav. When the non-Jews entered the *Heichal*, everyone else went for the gold and silver; Amon and Moav turned to the *Sefer Torah*. They declared: this is what it is written in — *lo yavo Amoni Moavi bi-kehal Hashem*, that we may not enter the congregation of God — and so they burned it. The *Maharsha* notes that the word *paras* read backwards yields *saras*, which is part of the wordplay operative in this passage. Additionally, the verse in *Eicha* states *asher tzivita lo yavo'u bi-kehalekha* — the enemies entered the Temple, the very enemies whom You commanded shall not enter Your congregation — and the *drasha* picks up on that resonance: those excluded from the *kahal* attacked the *Sefer Torah* that excluded them. The *machmada*, the most precious thing, was the *Sefer Torah* itself, and that is what Amon and Moav destroyed.

## Matrilineal Descent

### Rashi's Patrilineal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: גּוֹי שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְקִדּוּשִׁין, שֶׁמָּא מֵעֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁבָטִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said:** With regard to **a gentile who betrothed** a Jewish woman **nowadays, we are concerned that the betrothal** might be valid, despite the fact that a betrothal of a gentile is meaningless, **lest he be from the ten tribes** of Israel who intermingled with the gentiles.

The Gemara then introduces a statement that, if anything, is even more striking. Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Rav Assi: *oveid kochavim she-kideish bi-zman ha-zeh, chosheshin le-kiddushin* — a non-Jew who betroths a Jewish woman nowadays, we are concerned that the betrothal may be binding. Why? *Shema me-aseret ha-shevatim hu* — perhaps this non-Jew is actually a descendant of the ten tribes, and therefore may in fact be Jewish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כֹּל דְּפָרֵישׁ — מֵרוּבָּא פָּרֵישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **But** there is an important principle in *halakha* that **any** item **separated,** i.e., not fixed in its place, is presumed to have been **separated from the majority.** In this case, it can be assumed that any individual singled out from the gentiles belongs to the majority of gentiles and has no Jewish roots at all.

The Gemara immediately objects: *kol de-parish me-rubah parish* — the principle is that anyone who separates from a group is assumed to come from the majority. The majority of non-Jews are genuinely non-Jews. Why should we entertain this concern?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּקְבִיעִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: ״וַיַּנְחֵם בַּחְלַח וּבְחָבוֹר נְהַר גּוֹזָן וְעָרֵי מָדָי״, חֲלַח — זֶה חִלָּזוֹן, וְחָבוֹר —
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rav Yehuda means that there is a concern only with regard to those who came from **the permanent** dwelling **places** of the ten tribes. **As Rabbi Abba bar Kahana said** that the verse states about those exiled from Samaria: **"And he put them in Halah, and in Habor, on the river of Gozan, and in the cities of the Medes"** (II Kings 18:11). Rabbi Abba bar Kahana proceeded to identify these places. **Halah; this is** the place called **Ḥalzon. And Habor;**

The Gemara responds: *be-duchta de-kavia* — this applies specifically in a place where the ten tribes are known to have been settled. In such a location, one cannot rely on *rov*, because the very composition of the population is in question. The biblical source for the locations of the exile is cited: Rav Abba bar Kahana identifies the places of the Assyrian exile — *Chalach, Chavor, nehar Gozan, and arei Madai* — with the identifiable cities of *Chalazon, Chadyav, Ginzak, and Chemdan* respectively. A second tradition, cited as *ve-ika d'amri*, gives an alternative set of locations centered around *Nihar* and its surrounding cities, which Shmuel identifies as *Mushkhei, Chidki, and Dumkaya*.

Now one must pause and ask: in what way might the descendants of the ten tribes still be Jewish? The ten tribes were exiled centuries earlier, and obviously intermarriage with the surrounding non-Jewish populations would have occurred extensively. If we are concerned that some of these apparent non-Jews carry Jewish ancestry, we are implicitly concerned about a matrilineal chain — some great-great-grandmother, several generations back, who was Jewish. That is the straightforward reading, and it is the reading that *Tosafot* ultimately endorses.

But *Rashi* reads it differently, and his reading is remarkable. On the words *shema me-aseret ha-shevatim hu*, approximately six lines from the bottom of 16b, *Rashi* explains: *shenasu ovdot kochavim* — the Jewish men there married non-Jewish women. That is, the concern is not about a matrilineal Jewish grandmother but about a patrilineal Jewish grandfather. And *Rashi* continues: *ve-kasvar de-ovedet kochavim she-yaldah mi-Yisrael ha-velad mamzer ve-chosheshin le-kiddushei mamzer* — the opinion being operated with here is that a child born of a non-Jewish mother and a Jewish father is a *mamzer*, and therefore we are concerned that the *kiddushin* with this apparent non-Jewish man might be *kiddushei mamzer*.

This is an astonishing move. *Rashi* is reading the Gemara as operating with an assumption of patrilineal Jewish identity — the child of a Jewish father and a non-Jewish mother is, on this view, still Jewish, and indeed a *mamzer*. One might think this opens some door: there is a Talmudic opinion, apparently, that patrilineal descent confers Jewish identity. But as will become immediately clear, that is precisely the wrong conclusion to draw. If this view says the child is a *mamzer*, then patrilineal descent, on this reading, is worse than no descent at all — a *mamzer* cannot be remedied, whereas a non-Jew can convert.

### Tosafot Rejects Patrilineal Descent

*Tosafot* reacts with considerable force. In the *dibur hamatchil* beginning *oved kochav she-kidesh be-zman ha-zeh*, *Tosafot* writes: *peirush be-kuntres de-kasvar ovedet kochavim she-yaldah mi-Yisrael ha-velad mamzer ve-chosheshin le-kidushei mamzer* — so *Rashi* has explained it. But *Tosafot* objects: *u-teimah de-ve-kama mishnayot muchach de-ein ha-yachas ela achareha* — it is demonstrated from numerous *Mishnayot* that lineage follows the mother, not the father. When the father is Jewish and the mother is not Jewish, the child is not Jewish. The evidence from across *Shas* is overwhelming, and *Tosafot* marshals it.

*Tosafot* then attempts to defuse the difficulty: *ve-nireh de-lo kashya kol kach de-ha nami mishnah hi savira lei de-oved kochav ve-eved ha-ba al bat Yisrael ha-velad kasher* — the same position that says a non-Jewish man with a Jewish woman produces a *kasher* Jew would also say, in the mirror image, that a Jewish man with a non-Jewish woman produces a *kasher* child — just not Jewish. That is, if you accept the normative view that the Jewish-mother case produces a straightforward Jew with no *mamzer* issue, then logically you accept that the Jewish-father case produces a straightforward non-Jew with no *mamzer* issue either. *Ve-hu ha-din ipcha* — the same logic applies in reverse.

But *Tosafot* also explains what *Rashi's* reading would entail if followed through: *de-Rav Assi savar ke-Rabbi Akiva de-amar ein kiddushin tofsim be-chayvei lavin ve-ha-velad mamzer* — Rav Assi operates with Rabbi Akiva's position that *kiddushin* does not take effect with *chiyuvei lavin* and the child is a *mamzer*. If that were true in the case of a Jewish mother, *ve-hu ha-din* it would be true in the mirror image — the child of a Jewish father and non-Jewish mother would also be a *mamzer*. The two cases rise and fall together. Since we do not rule like this in the Jewish-mother case — the child is simply Jewish — we correspondingly do not apply it in the Jewish-father case — the child is simply non-Jewish. There is no *mamzer* in either direction.

*Tosafot's* final word on the matter is contained in the passage beginning with the word *Yehuda*: *ve-nireh de-tameih de-Rabbi Yehuda amar Rav Assi mishum banot* — the real reason for Rav Assi's concern is *mishum banot*, because of the Jewish women, that is, the concern is about a matrilineal chain through the Jewish mothers of the ten tribes, not through the Jewish fathers. This is the reading *Tosafot* ultimately endorses, and it is the reading consistent with the universal *halakhic* principle of matrilineal descent.

To summarize the two radical possibilities that have now emerged: one is that a Jewish mother combined with a non-Jewish father produces a *mamzer* — not on account of a biblical transgression, but because *kiddushin ein tofsin*. The other, following *Rashi's* reading, is that a Jewish father combined with a non-Jewish mother also produces a *mamzer* rather than a non-Jew. *Tosafot* shows that these two positions are interdependent: they stand or fall together, since they rest on the same underlying logic about the impossibility of *kiddushin*. The normative ruling rejects both: Jewish mother means Jewish child, Jewish father and non-Jewish mother means non-Jewish child, and *mamzerus* is not in play in either scenario.

### Shmuel's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לִי: בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִן יִשְׂרְאֵלִית קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, וְאֵין בִּנְךָ הַבָּא מִן הַגּוֹיָה קָרוּי בִּנְךָ, אֶלָּא בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda continued his recounting: **When I said this** *halakha*, that there is a concern about the betrothal of gentiles nowadays, **before Shmuel, he said to me:** One need not worry about this, as **your son from a Jewish woman is called your son,** i.e., he inherits his lineage from you, **and your son from a gentile woman is not called your son,** but **rather her son.** Consequently, all children born to Jews from gentile women are not considered Jews, as their lineage is determined by their gentile mothers.

The resolution is delivered by Shmuel himself. Rav Yehuda reports: when I stated this ruling before Shmuel, he said to me — *bincha haba min ha-Yisraelis kari bincha, v'ein bincha haba min ha-ovedet kochavim kari bincha ela bino* — your son who comes from a Jewish woman is called your son; the child who comes from a non-Jewish woman is not called your son, but her son.

This is the classical formulation of matrilineal descent in its most direct and unambiguous form. The child of a Jewish woman, regardless of who the father is, follows the mother and is Jewish. The child of a non-Jewish woman, regardless of who the father is, follows the mother and is not Jewish. The asymmetry is total and admits no qualification. This statement also retroactively explains why *Rashi* read the earlier Gemara the way he did: the Gemara was operating with an assumption that would be flatly denied by Shmuel, and Shmuel's ruling is precisely the rejection of that assumption.

One observation raised in discussion is worth noting here. It was pointed out that Shmuel's own daughters were reportedly kidnapped and violated, with the question of the status of their children being one that Shmuel himself would have faced with personal acuity. That biographical context lends an additional layer of significance to Shmuel's authoritative ruling on precisely this issue.

### Ki Yasir Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי יֵשׁוּ אַחִים יַחְדָּו, וַיְמִת אַחַד מֵהֶם וְלֹא־יְשׁ לוֹ בֵּן, וְלָקְחָה אֵשֶׁת־הַמֵּת אִשָּׁה זָרָה, לֹא תִהְיֶה אִשְׁתּוֹ לְאִישׁ מִחוּץ, יְבָמָהּ יָבוֹא עָלֶיהָ וִילַדָהּ ... בִּתְךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לְבְנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ לֹא תִקַּח לְבִנְךָ, כִּי יַסִּיר אֶת־בִּנְךָ מֵאַחֲרַי.
>
> **Translation:* The relevant verse is in *Devarim* 7, in the passage beginning *v'lo titchaten bam* — do not intermarry with them. The *pasuk* reads: *bitcha lo titein livno u-vito lo tikach l'vanecha* — do not give your daughter to his son, and do not take his daughter for your son. Note the structure: the daughter always follows the man she goes to. Now the reason given is *ki yasir et bincha me'acharai* — for he will turn your son away from Me.

Having established the ruling, the Gemara provides its biblical anchor. The relevant verse is in *Devarim* 7, in the passage beginning *v'lo titchaten bam* — do not intermarry with them. The *pasuk* reads: *bitcha lo titein livno u-vito lo tikach l'vanecha* — do not give your daughter to his son, and do not take his daughter for your son. Note the structure: the daughter always follows the man she goes to. Now the reason given is *ki yasir et bincha me'acharai* — for he will turn your son away from Me.

There is a grammatical puzzle here. *Ki yasir* is masculine — "he will turn." But who is the "he" who will turn "your son"? If the concern is about your daughter marrying a non-Jewish man, then *he* — the non-Jewish man — will take your daughter away. But it says *yasir et bincha*, "will turn your son." Why would the non-Jewish man turn your son away?

The Rabbinic reading resolves this by understanding *bincha* not as your immediate son but as your grandson. The structure is: do not give your daughter to his son — because the non-Jewish husband (*yasir*, masculine) will turn your grandson (*bincha*, meaning your daughter's child) away from God. And that child is *bincha*, is called *your* (grand)son — meaning he is Jewish — because the mother is Jewish. By contrast, the case of taking a non-Jewish woman for your son carries no such warning, because in that case your grandchild through your son is not Jewish to begin with. There is no grandson to lead astray.

This derivation hangs on the word *yasir* — masculine, referring to the non-Jewish man taking your daughter — and the object *bincha* — the product of that union who is nonetheless still your Jewish (grand)son. The child of a Jewish daughter and a non-Jewish man is Jewish; the child of a Jewish son and a non-Jewish woman is not Jewish. That is the halakha, and this is the verse from which it is derived. The whole edifice of matrilineal descent rests, as the Gemara understands it, on this single grammatical turn.

### Ravina's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא בָּנוֹת, וְאָמַר רָבִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בֶּן בִּתְּךָ הַבָּא מִן הַגּוֹי קָרוּי בִּנְךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Aren't there** Jewish **girls** who were captured by gentiles, whose children are considered to be Jews? **And Ravina said: Learn from this** that **the son of your daughter from a gentile is called your son.**

After Shmuel's ruling has established that descent from a non-Jewish mother renders a child non-Jewish regardless of paternal Jewish lineage, *Ravina* raises the obvious follow-up question. The Gemara's argument for dismissing concern about the inhabitants of the exiled-tribes' cities was that if Jewish fathers intermarried with non-Jewish women, the children would simply be non-Jewish — no cause for concern. But what about the reverse? What about the Jewish women of the ten tribes? Surely some of them also remained and had children. Those children, born of Jewish mothers, would be Jewish by matrilineal descent. And their descendants, several generations later, might still carry a matrilineal Jewish identity even while appearing fully assimilated into the surrounding non-Jewish population. Should we not be concerned that the apparent non-Jew in one of those cities is actually the matrilineal descendant of a Jewish woman from the ten tribes?

*Ravina* makes this point explicit: *shma mina, ben bitcha haba min ha-oved kochavim kari bincha* — we derive from the previous ruling that the son of your daughter, who had relations with a non-Jewish man, is called your son. That child is Jewish. So the concern about matrilineal descent from the women of the ten tribes is real and pressing.

### Women's Infertility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמִירִי דִּבְנָתָא דְּהָהוּא דָּרָא אִיצְטְרוֹיֵי אִצְטְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This is no concern, as it **is learned** as a tradition **that the girls** from the ten tribes **of that generation became barren** and did not give birth to any offspring, whereas some of the exiled men of the ten tribes married gentile women. Consequently, all of the children born there were gentiles.

The Gemara provides a definitive answer: *Gemiri, de-b'nesha de-hadora ishtaruei ishtaru* — we have a received tradition that the women of that generation — the women of the ten tribes who were exiled — experienced some form of rupture or infertility. *Rashi* understands this to mean that their wombs were somehow burst, rendering them unable to bear children. As a result, there are no matrilineal descendants of the ten tribes whatsoever. Any descendants that exist come only through the Jewish men, not the Jewish women — and since the Jewish men intermarried with non-Jewish women, those descendants are all non-Jewish. There is, therefore, no living person with a matrilineal claim to Jewish identity through the women of the ten tribes.

This is a striking and theologically loaded *gemara*. If taken at face value, it means that the ten tribes are genuinely and irrevocably lost — not merely assimilated, but without any surviving matrilineal line. Various eschatological traditions posit the return of the ten tribes at the end of days; this *gemara* would seem to complicate such a vision considerably. The identification of, for example, the Ethiopian Jewish community as descendants of *shevet Dan* by the *Radbaz* raises the question of how he navigated this tradition — whether he understood it narrowly, as applying only to those specific exiled populations, or in some other way.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם, עַד שֶׁעֲשָׂאוּם גּוֹיִם גְּמוּרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּה׳ בָּגָדוּ כִּי בָנִים זָרִים יָלָדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that Rav Yehuda actually related the following: **When I said this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said to me: They did not move from there,** the place where they deliberated on this matter, **until they rendered** all of **them,** including those who intermingled with the ten tribes in different locations, **full-fledged gentiles.** Consequently, there is no concern that their betrothals might be of any effect, **as it is stated: "They have dealt treacherously against the Lord, for they have begotten strange children"** (Hosea 5:7).

An alternative version of Shmuel's response to the same question is also cited: *lo zazu misham ad she-asam ovdei kochavim gemurim* — they did not leave there until they had been rendered completely non-Jewish. Whether this means a *halakhic* ruling was issued declaring them non-Jews, or whether it reflects the factual claim that all remaining descendants were patrilineal only, is not entirely clear. The verse cited in support is from *Hoshea*: *ba-Hashem bagadu ki banim zarim yaldu* — they betrayed God, for they bore foreign children. This could be read as describing deep assimilation destroying Jewish identity in a more diffuse sense, or as a restatement of the factual claim that all children born were non-Jewish because all were born of non-Jewish mothers.

## Cities of Defect

### Tarmod Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב יוֹסֵף אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְיָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: עֲתִידִין יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּעָבְדִי יוֹמָא טָבָא כִּי חָרְבָה תַּרְמוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rav Yosef sat behind Rav Kahana, and Rav Kahana sat before Rav Yehuda, and he sat and he said** this tradition: **In the future, the Jews will establish a Festival day when Tarmod is destroyed.**

The Gemara now shifts to a vivid scene of *amora'ic* discourse. Rav Yosef sat behind Rav Kahana, who sat in front of Rav Yehuda in the *beis midrash*. Rav Yehuda was teaching: *asidin Yisrael de-avdi yoma tava ki charvi Tarmod* — the Jewish people will celebrate a holiday when Tarmod is destroyed. The basis for this sentiment is the very material we have been studying: there was a persistent concern about the population of Tarmod, whether they were *mamzerim* or non-Jews, and according to *Rashi* they were regarded as *mamzerim* who were seeping into the Jewish community through intermarriage. The prospect of that city's elimination would be cause for celebration — though one might charitably understand "destroyed" to mean dispersed rather than anything more severe.

### Tarmod Persistent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חֲרִיב! הַהִיא תָּמוֹד הֲוַאי. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הַיְינוּ תַּרְמוֹד הַיְינוּ תָּמוֹד, אִכְּפוֹלֵי הוּא דְּמִכַּפְלָא: חֲרִיב מֵהַאי גִּיסָא — אִיתִּיב מֵהַאי גִּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But it has** already **been destroyed.** The Gemara answers: **That** place that was destroyed **was Tamud,** not Tarmod. **Rav Ashi said: Tarmod is** the same as **Tamud.** However, the city **is doubled.** In other words, when it **is destroyed from this side it is settled on that side.**

The Gemara responds with a practical challenge: *ha-charuv?* — but it has already been destroyed! Rav Yehuda distinguishes: the city that was destroyed was *Tamod*, not *Tarmod*; they are different cities. The Gemara, however, objects that we have said elsewhere these are in fact the same city. The answer is that the city was rebuilt: *ichpulei d'ichpal* — it redoubled. *Chariv m'hai gisa, yativ m'hai gisa* — if one side of the city is destroyed, the other side is built up, and vice versa. Tarmod is persistent in the way the *tzinim u-pachim* of the *Torah* are persistent — a recurring affliction that does not disappear. And so Rav Yehuda's hope for its eventual destruction remains very much aspirational.

### Harfanya Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּעוּלָּא וְקָא הָוֵי בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא, אָמַר: מָה גַּבְרָא וּמָה גַּבְרָא! אִי לָאו דְּהַרְפַּנְיָא מָאתֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Hamnuna sat before Ulla and was engaged in** the study of ***halakha*.** Ulla **said** about him: **What a man; what a man.** In other words: What a great man this Rav Hamnuna is. **If only Harpanya were not his city,** as the inhabitants of that place are all of flawed lineage, which indicates that the lineage of Rav Hamnuna is likewise impaired.

Rav Hamnuna sat before Ulla, engaged in *halacha*. Ulla was posing difficult questions, and Rav Hamnuna was struggling with them. Rav Hamnuna then remarked admiringly — though the remark was actually made about him, by Ulla: *mah gavra* — what a great person this is! *Mah gavra ila d'maharfanya mah avvad* — imagine how great he would be if he were not from Harfanya, if his lineage were not pulled down by his connection to that problematic city. Harfanya, like Tarmod, was a city with questions of *yichus*, though the concern there appears to be not *mamzerus* specifically but rather genealogical uncertainty more broadly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִכְּסִיף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּסַף גֻּלְגֻּלְתָּא, לְהֵיכָא יָהֲבַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְפוּם נַהֲרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, מִפּוּם נַהֲרָא אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hamnuna **was ashamed.** Ulla **said to him: Where do you give the money for** payment of the **poll** tax? **He said to him:** I pay it **to** the city of **Pum Nahara,** as my city is subject to taxation by that city. **He said to him: If so, you are from Pum Nahara,** not Harpanya, and your lineage is evidently not flawed.

Upon hearing this, Rav Hamnuna was embarrassed. Ulla sought to reassure him. He asked: *kesaf gulgalta l'heicha yahavat* — where do you pay your poll tax? Rav Hamnuna replied: to *Pum Nahara*. Just as an American living abroad might still file taxes with the United States, Rav Hamnuna, though residing in Harfanya, was registered as a subject of *Pum Nahara* — and we noted earlier that *Pum Nahara* was identified as a Jewish city. Ulla therefore concluded: *im ken mi-Pum Nahara at* — if so, you are really from *Pum Nahara*, not from Harfanya. His lineage is intact.

### Harfanya Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הַרְפַּנְיָא? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הַר שֶׁהַכֹּל פּוֹנִים בּוֹ. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: כׇּל שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ וְשִׁבְטוֹ נִפְנֶה לְשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the name **Harpanya? Rabbi Zeira said:** It means **the mountain to which all turn [*har shehakol ponin bo*].** In other words, it is the destination for all those who could not find wives anywhere else, as most of its population is of flawed lineage. **It is taught in a *baraita*: All those who do not know their family or tribe turn there.**

The Gemara then explains the name *Harfanya* through a form of *drash*. Rav Zeira says: *har she-hakol ponim bo* — a mountain toward which everyone turns, meaning everyone who has been excluded elsewhere, anyone with questionable parentage, converges there. The *Beraita* reinforces this: *kol she-ein makir mishpachto v'shivto nifne l'sham* — whoever cannot identify his family and tribal lineage goes to that city, because no one else will have him. This is a striking sociological observation embedded in the Talmudic text. Even setting aside the formal legal category of *mamzerus*, the community's insistence on *yichus* — knowing with certainty one's genealogical origins — was so acute that those who lacked such clarity were socially marginalized and effectively driven to settle among others in the same situation. This heightened concern with *yichus* will prove to be one of the central recurring themes of *Masechet Yevamot*.

### Worse Than Sheol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְהִיא עֲמוּקָּה מִשְּׁאוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִיַּד שְׁאוֹל אֶפְדֵּם מִמָּוֶת אֶגְאָלֵם״, וְאִילּוּ פְּסוּל דִּידְהוּ לֵית לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § Rava said: This ** type of flaw is appalling and **is deeper than the netherworld, as is stated: "Shall I ransom them from the power of the netherworld? Shall I redeem them from death?"** (Hosea 13:14). This verse indicates that it is possible to be ransomed and released from the netherworld, **whereas their disqualification cannot be rectified.**

Rava then delivers a sharp epigram: *v'hi amuka mi-she'ol* — Harfanya is deeper than *Sheol*, the netherworld. His source is the verse: *mi-yad she'ol efdem mi-mavet eg'alem* — from the hand of *Sheol* I will redeem them, from death I will ransom them. The logic is painful in its clarity: those in *Sheol* can be redeemed. Those afflicted with the *psulot* of Harfanya cannot be redeemed; their disqualification is more permanent than death itself.

### Defect Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּסוּלֵי דְהַרְפַּנְיָא מִשּׁוּם פְּסוּלֵי דְּמֵישׁוֹן, וּפְסוּלֵי דְּמֵישׁוֹן מִשּׁוּם פְּסוּלֵי דְתַרְמוֹד, פְּסוּלֵי דְתַרְמוֹד מִשּׁוּם עַבְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Those** who are **disqualified from Harpanya** are unfit **due to the disqualification** of the inhabitants **of** nearby **Meishon,** who were unfit and intermarried with the residents of Harpanya. **Those disqualified from Meishon** are unfit **due to those disqualified from Tarmod, and those disqualified from Tarmod** are disqualified **due to the servants of Solomon.**

The Gemara then traces a cascade of genealogical defect that flows from one city to the next. The *psulim* of Harfanya came from the *psulim* of Meshan. The *psulim* of Meshan came from the *psulim* of Tarmod. The *psulim* of Tarmod came from the servants of Solomon — *mishum avdei Shlomo* — the non-Jewish slaves who had intermarried with Jewish women at the beginning of this entire discussion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: קַבָּא רַבָּא וְקַבָּא זוּטָא, מִיגַּנְדַּר וְאָזֵיל לִשְׁאוֹל, וּמִשְּׁאוֹל לְתַרְמוֹד, וּמִתַּרְמוֹד לְמֵישָׁן, וּמִמֵּישָׁן לְהַרְפַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this** explains the folk saying **that people say** in this regard: **A large ephah and a small ephah,** which are both inaccurate utensils that may not be used for measuring, **roll onward to the netherworld, and from the netherworld to Tarmod, and from Tarmod to Meishon, and from Meishon to Harpanya.** The same idea expressed by the Sages with regard to the disqualification of lineage was also incorporated into a well-known adage among commoners.

And the folk saying captures this cascading descent: *kava rabba v'kava zuta, minder ve-azil li-she'ol, u-mi-she'ol le-Tarmod, u-mi-Tarmod le-Meshan, u-mi-Meshan le-Harfanya* — a large measure and a small measure rolling downhill, from *Sheol* to Tarmod to Meshan to Harfanya — each stage more irredeemable than the last.

One cannot help but sense, beneath this imagery, the human reality it describes: communities of people, generation after generation, for whom there was no return, no remedy, no reintegration into the broader Jewish community. *Chazal* were deeply aware of this, and their efforts to introduce leniencies — the principle of *safek mamzer mutar*, and the many *halakhic* mechanisms for dissolving *mamzerus* concerns — reflect their discomfort with this severity. But those leniencies did not erase the underlying social reality in which *yichus* functioned as a form of communal gatekeeping, and those without it were effectively exiled to cities of the rejected.

## Brother Not Alive

### Four-Stage Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ? שְׁנֵי אַחִים, וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְנוֹלַד לָהֶן אָח, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִיבֵּם הַשֵּׁנִי אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו, וּמֵת. הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה מִשּׁוּם צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Each of the women enumerated in the first chapter causes exemption from levirate marriage and *ḥalitza* for her rival wives. This is due to the close family relationship she has with her brother-in-law, making her forbidden to him. The single exception is the case explained in this mishna. **What** is the case of **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist?** For example: If there were **two brothers, and one of them died** childless, **and** subsequently **a brother of theirs was born, after which the second brother,** the elder, **took his** deceased **brother's wife in levirate marriage, and** then **died** as well. Consequently, two women require levirate marriage: The widow of the first brother who had been taken in levirate marriage by the second brother, and the widow of the second brother, the first widow's rival wife. **The first** widow, who had been the wife of the first brother to die, **goes out** without any obligation to be taken in levirate marriage by the youngest brother who was born later, **since** she is **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.** The first deceased brother never lived at the same time as the newly born brother. **The second** widow, who had been married to the second brother, is exempt **due to her rival wife.**

With the words *hadran alach chamisha asar nashim*, the *perek* concludes and the next one begins. The opening Mishnah asks: *keitzad eshet achiv shelo haya b'olamo* — how does the case of a brother's wife who was not alive at the same time as the deceased brother work? The scenario is built in four stages.

Stage one: Reuven and Shimon are alive. Reuven marries Rachel. Reuven then dies. Stage two: Levi is born. Because Levi was not alive when Reuven was alive — they never shared existence in this world — Levi has no *mitzva* of *yibum* with Rachel. For him, Rachel is simply his brother's wife, which is an *erva*. Stage three: Shimon, who was alive at the same time as Reuven, does *yibum* with Rachel. He is already married to Sarah. So now Shimon is married to both Sarah and Rachel. Stage four: Shimon dies. Now the question of Levi's obligations comes into full focus.

### Ervah & Tzarat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וּמֵת, שְׁנִיָּה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna discusses an additional situation: If the second brother had **performed** only **levirate betrothal with her,** meaning that he had not yet consummated the marriage, **and** then **died,** both the wife betrothed by a levirate betrothal to the second brother and the wife of the second brother fall before the youngest brother born after the death of the first brother. In that case, the first wife certainly goes out and is exempt from both *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage, since she is to him the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist. **The second,** however, was never effectively the rival wife of the first brother's wife, as the first brother's wife had only been betrothed by levirate betrothal and was not fully married to the second brother. Therefore, **she performs *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.**

The Mishnah's ruling is as follows. *Ha-rishona* — Rachel, the first woman — *yotzeis mishum eshet achiv shelo haya b'olamo* — is exempt from *yibum* with Levi because she is an *erva* to him, being the wife of a brother he never coexisted with. *Ve-ha-shniya mishum tzarasah* — Sarah, the second woman, is exempt because she is the *tzarat erva*: the co-wife of an *erva* is herself exempt from *yibum* just as the *erva* herself is.

### Maamar Case

The Mishnah continues with a further complication. Suppose Shimon did not actually perform *yibum* with Rachel but instead performed *maamar* — the rabbinic act of betrothal intended as a preliminary step toward *yibum*, which creates a quasi-*kiddushin* status for the *yevama* — and then he died. The halachic picture shifts. *De'oraita*, Shimon was fully and biblically married only to Sarah. Rachel was not biblically his wife; she was simply the *yevama* with whom he had performed a rabbinic betrothal. Biblically, the *yibum* obligation rests entirely with Sarah.

Yet because of the *maamar*, Rachel has a quasi-marital status vis-à-vis Shimon. That means that from Levi's perspective, Rachel is something of a quasi-*erva* — not a full biblical *erva*, since Shimon's marriage to her was only rabbinic, but sufficiently complicated to generate a concern resembling *tzarat erva*. Sarah, in turn, becomes a quasi-*tzarat erva*. The result: Levi cannot perform *yibum* with Sarah because it looks like a *tzarat erva* situation, but he also cannot simply walk away from his obligation to Sarah, because biblically Sarah is his true *yevama*. The resolution is *chalitza*: Levi must perform *chalitza* with Sarah, releasing her, without performing *yibum*. She is neither available for *yibum* nor free without *chalitza*.

A question was raised here: would not the *zika* — the quasi-betrothal bond that exists between a *yavam* and his *yevamot* even before any *maamar* is performed — create a similar concern even without the *maamar*? The answer is that in this case *zika* alone does not generate the same status as *maamar*, though the question of *zika* as quasi-*kiddushin* is a broader sugya that will be addressed as the *masechet* continues.

## Paternal Brotherhood

### Yachdov Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יֵשׁוּ אַחִים יַחְדָּו״ — יַחְדָּו שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהֶם יְשִׁיבָה אַחַת בָּעוֹלָם. פְּרַט לְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What is the source for the rule that a brother who was not alive at the same time as the deceased has no *mitzva* of *yibum*? **As it is written: "When brothers dwell together"** (Deuteronomy 25:5). The phrase **"together" teaches** that **they had one continuous existence in the world.** **This excludes the case of a brother** [to whom] **is the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.**

The Gemara now seeks the biblical source for the rule that a brother who was not alive at the same time as the deceased has no *mitzva* of *yibum* and his sister-in-law is an *erva* to him. Rav Yehuda identifies the verse: *ki yeshvu achim yachdov* — "when brothers dwell together." The word *yachdov* — "together" — teaches *she-hayta lahem yeshiva achat ba-olam* — that they must have shared simultaneous existence in the world. *Prat l'eshet achiv shelo haya b'olamo* — this excludes the case where a brother is born only after the other has already died. The word *yachdov* insists on contemporaneity.

### Paternal Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְיוּחָדִים בִּנְחָלָה. פְּרַט לְאָחִיו מִן־הַאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** Having established what *yachdov* teaches about temporal coexistence, the Gemara derives a second principle: **they must be united in inheritance. This excludes a maternal brother.**

Having established what *yachdov* teaches about temporal coexistence, the Gemara derives a second principle from the same word: *meyuchadim b'nachala* — they must be united in inheritance. *Prat l'achiv min ha-em* — this excludes a maternal brother. A maternal brother does not inherit together with his half-siblings through the father; inheritance flows through the paternal line. *Yachdov*, the togetherness required, is specifically the togetherness of those who share an inheritance — and that is a paternal, not a maternal, connection. Therefore, *yibum* applies only to paternal brothers, not to maternal ones.

### Yibum & Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְבוּם בִּנְחָלָה תַּלְיָא רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** ***yibum* is tied to inheritance. This is a striking claim. The surface rationale for *yibum* given by the Torah is *yakum al shem achiv ha-met* — the child born of the *yibum* will perpetuate the name of the deceased brother. But the Gemara understands that embedded within this rationale is the idea of inheritance: the *yavam* takes the deceased brother's *nachala* and passes it on through the child. *Yibum* is therefore structurally connected to inheritance law, and since inheritance flows through the father — *nachala min ha-av v'lo min ha-em* — maternal brothers, who do not share in the paternal inheritance, fall outside the scope of *yibum* altogether.**

But one might still ask: why would I have thought *yachdov* requires shared parentage on both sides? The Gemara's answer is fundamental:

*Yibum b'nachala talya rachamana* — the Torah has tied *yibum* to inheritance. This is a striking claim. The surface rationale for *yibum* given by the Torah is *yakum al shem achiv ha-met* — the child born of the *yibum* will perpetuate the name of the deceased brother. One might have thought to push in the opposite direction: since *yibum* involves overriding what would otherwise be an *erva* — a normally forbidden relationship — perhaps we should limit its application to only the closest possible fraternal bond, which would be brothers who share both mother and father. *Yibum* is a *chiddush*, an exception to the ordinary rules of *ervah*; perhaps we should minimize the exception and apply it only in the most intimate brotherly relationship. The Gemara rejects this through the dual derivation: *achim* from Yaakov's sons establishes that paternal brotherhood alone suffices, and *yachdov* rules out the extension to mere relatives. The result is the ruling as we know it: *yibum* applies to paternal brothers, not maternal ones, and only when both brothers were alive simultaneously.
