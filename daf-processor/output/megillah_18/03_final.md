# Megillah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oral vs. Written Reading

### Zachor Zachor Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָאָהּ עַל פֶּה לֹא יָצָא וְכוּ׳. מְנָלַן? אָמַר רָבָא: אָתְיָא זְכִירָה זְכִירָה, כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָאֵלֶּה נִזְכָּרִים״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״כְּתֹב זֹאת זִכָּרוֹן בַּסֵּפֶר״. מָה לְהַלָּן — בְּסֵפֶר, אַף כָּאן — בְּסֵפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** ** If one read the Megilla by heart he has not fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we derive this? Rava said:** This is **derived by means of a verbal analogy between one instance of the term remembrance and another instance of the term remembrance. It is written here,** with regard to the Megilla: **"That these days should be remembered"** (Esther 9:28), **and it is written elsewhere: "And the Lord said to Moses: Write this for a memorial in the book, and rehearse it in the ears of Joshua: That I will utterly blot out the remembrance of Amalek from under the heavens"** (Exodus 17:14). **Just as there,** with regard to Amalek, remembrance is referring specifically to something written **in a book,** as it is stated, "in the book," **so too here,** the Megilla remembrance is through being written **in a book.**

The daf opens with the ruling: *kara al peh lo yatza* — if one reads the Megillah orally, that is, from memory without reading from a scroll, one does not fulfill the obligation. The Gemara immediately asks *minalan* — what is the source? Rava derives it through a *gezeirah shavah* linking two appearances of the root *zachar*. The Megillah itself states *ve-hayamim ha-eleh nizkarim* — these days are remembered — and elsewhere, regarding Amalek, the Torah commands *ktov zot zikaron basefer* — write this as a remembrance in a book. The thematic connection to Amalek is not incidental: the story of Haman is understood as the culmination of the Amalekite thread running through the Torah. Since the earlier verse specifies *basefer* — in a book — the principle follows: *mah hallan basefer af kan basefer*, just as there it must be written in a book, so too here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּהַאי זְכִירָה קְרִיאָה הִיא, דִּלְמָא עִיּוּן בְּעָלְמָא! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ (דִּכְתִיב): ״זָכוֹר״ — יָכוֹל בַּלֵּב? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא תִּשְׁכָּח״, הֲרֵי שִׁכְחַת הַלֵּב אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״זָכוֹר״ — בַּפֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: **But from where do we know that this remembrance** that is stated with regard to Amalek and to the Megilla involves **reading it out loud from a book? Perhaps** it requires **merely looking into** the book, reading it silently. The Gemara answers: **It should not enter your mind** to say this, as it was taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Remember what Amalek did to you"** (Deuteronomy 25:17). One **might have thought that it suffices for one to remember this silently, in his heart.** But this cannot be, since **when it says** subsequently: **"You shall not forget"** (Deuteronomy 25:19), **it is already referring to forgetting from the heart. How,** then, **do I uphold** the meaning of **"remember"?** What does this command to remember add to the command to not forget? Therefore, it means that the remembrance must be expressed out loud, **with the mouth.**

One might ask: how do we know that *zechira* means active recitation rather than mere mental recall? Perhaps *nizkarim* simply means that the days should be kept in mind. The Gemara preempts this with a *beraisa* on the commandment of *zachor* regarding Amalek: *yachol balev* — perhaps it is fulfilled through internal remembrance? The Torah's parallel command *lo tishkach* — do not forget — already addresses the prohibition against forgetting in one's heart. The positive commandment of *zachor* must therefore add something beyond internal memory: it demands active oral remembrance, *bpeh*. Once that principle is established for *zachor* by Amalek, it carries over through the *gezeirah shavah* to the reading of the Megillah.

### Pirsumei Nisa & Ritual

This connection between memory and ritual act runs very deep in Jewish thought. Yosef Yerushalmi, in his aptly named book *Zachor*, draws a distinction between history and memory. The Jewish people have not historically produced historians in the modern sense, yet have been extraordinarily accomplished at memory — at creating communal and cultural frameworks through which the past is kept alive. The key instrument of that memory is ritual: all the commandments bound up with *yetzias mitzrayim*, *le-ma'an tizkor yom tzeischa me-eretz mitzrayim*, are about constructing memory through ritual acts. The Megillah reading belongs to this same tradition: it is not sufficient to remember the miracle of Purim inwardly; it must be enacted, performed, read aloud from a text.

### Hebrew Scroll/Reading

Having established that one must read from a scroll, the Gemara raises a question worth pausing over: is the requirement of Hebrew an insistence on the nature of the *reading* — that the act itself must be performed in Hebrew — or is it a requirement about the *scroll*, that only a Hebrew scroll constitutes a valid *megillah*? This distinction will play out in the subsequent discussion of reading in foreign languages, and it is not immediately obvious which way the analysis will resolve.

## Translation & Languages

### Two Translation Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָאָהּ תַּרְגּוּם לֹא יָצָא וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּכְתִיבָה מִקְרָא וְקָרֵי לַהּ תַּרְגּוּם, הַיְינוּ עַל פֶּה! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּכְתִיבָה תַּרְגּוּם וְקָרֵי לַהּ תַּרְגּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** ** If one read the Megilla in** Aramaic **translation he has not fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of this case? If we say that the Megilla was written in the original biblical text,** i.e., in Hebrew, **and he read it in Aramaic translation,** then **this is** the same as reading it **by heart,** as he is not reading the words written in the text, and the mishna has already stated that one does not fulfill his obligation by reading the Megilla by heart. The Gemara answers: **No, it is necessary** to teach this case as well, as it is referring to a case in which the Megilla **was written** not in the original Hebrew but **in Aramaic translation, and he read it** as written, **in Aramaic translation.**

The Gemara rules that *kara targum lo yatza* — if one read the Megillah in translation, one has not fulfilled the obligation. But the Gemara immediately asks *heichi dami* — what is the actual case? If the scroll is written in Hebrew but the reader recites a translation, that is simply reading from memory — *hainu al peh* — and the problem is just the one already addressed. The novel case must therefore be where the scroll itself is written in translation: *de-chsiva targum ve-karai lei targum* — written in a foreign language and read in that foreign language. That scroll-based reading is what fails to fulfill the obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קוֹרִין אוֹתָהּ לַלּוֹעֲזוֹת בְּלַעַז וְכוּ׳. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: קְרָאָהּ בְּכׇל לָשׁוֹן — לֹא יָצָא! רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בְּלַעַז יְווֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** ** However, for those who speak a foreign language, one may read the Megilla in that foreign language.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't you say in the mishna: If he read it in any** other **language he has not fulfilled his obligation? The Gemara cites the answer of Rav and Shmuel, who both say:** When the mishna says: A foreign language, it is referring specifically to **the Greek foreign language,** which has a unique status with regard to biblical translation.

Yet the very same passage then states: *aval korin oso le-loazim belaz* — one may read the Megillah for foreigners in a foreign language. The tension is immediate: you have just said reading in another language is invalid, and now you say it is valid?

### Rav & Shmuel Greek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּכְתִיבָה אַשּׁוּרִית וְקָרֵי לַהּ יְווֹנִית, הַיְינוּ עַל פֶּה! אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁכְּתוּבָה בְּלַעַז יְווֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of the case? If we say that the Megilla was written in *Ashurit,** i.e., in Hebrew, **and he read it in Greek, this is** the same as reading it **by heart,** and the mishna teaches that one does not fulfill his obligation by reading by heart. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Aḥa said** that **Rabbi Elazar said:** The mishna is dealing with a case in which the Megilla **was written in the Greek foreign language** and was also read in that language.

Rav and Shmuel both say *belaz Yevani* — Greek. Their approach is to resolve the contradiction by narrowing the valid exception to Greek specifically. They build on the earlier discussion in which a special position holds that while a *sefer Torah* may in principle be written in any language, one opinion permits only Greek as an alternative — a position associated with the tradition that Greek alone is an acceptable substitute for Hebrew in sacred scrolls. Rav and Shmuel apply this to the Megillah: the reason reading in another language is generally invalid is that a scroll in another language is not a *kasher* scroll; the single exception is Greek, which does constitute a valid scroll. Therefore, a Megillah written and read in Greek would satisfy the obligation.

But the Gemara must first sort out what scenario Rav and Shmuel are even addressing. If the scroll is written in Hebrew and the reader renders it in Greek, *hainu al peh* — that is again just oral recitation. Rav Acha citing Rabbi Elazar clarifies: the case must be *she-ktuva belaz Yevanis* — a Megillah actually written in Greek script.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁקְּרָאוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיַעֲקֹב ״אֵל״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא לוֹ אֵל אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לְמִזְבֵּחַ קְרָא לֵיהּ יַעֲקֹב ״אֵל״ — ״וַיִּקְרָא לוֹ יַעֲקֹב״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא ״וַיִּקְרָא לוֹ״, לְיַעֲקֹב, ״אֵל״. וּמִי קְרָאוֹ ״אֵל״ — ״אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos statements in this line of tradition, the Gemara adds: **And Rabbi Aḥa further said** that **Rabbi Elazar said: From where is it derived that the Holy One, Blessed be He, called Jacob El,** meaning God? **As it is stated:** "And he erected there an altar, **and he called it El, God of Israel"** (Genesis 33:20). It is also possible to translate this as: And He, i.e., the God of Israel, called him, Jacob, El. Indeed, it must be understood this way, **as if it enters your mind to say that the verse should be understood as saying that Jacob called the altar El, it should have** specified the subject of the verb and written: **And Jacob called it El.** **But since the verse is not written this way, the verse must be understood as follows: He called Jacob El; and who called him El? The God of Israel.**

At this point, the Gemara takes a digression. Since the statement was attributed to Rav Acha citing Rabbi Elazar, the Gemara chains on additional statements in that name — including a striking exegetical claim that God called Jacob "El": *vayikra lo El Elohei Yisrael*. The Gemara reasons that if Jacob were the subject calling the altar *El*, the verse should have said *vayikra lo Yaakov*; since it does not, *Elohei Yisrael* must be the subject, and it is God who calls Jacob by the divine name. This opens enormous theological terrain — Jacob's face on the *kise ha-kavod*, the kabbalistic identification of *Kneses Yisrael* with the tenth *sefirah* of *malchus* — but it must be bracketed here.

### Loazim Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: קְרָאָהּ גִּיפְּטִית, עִבְרִית, עֵילָמִית, מָדִית, יְווֹנִית — לֹא יָצָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discussing languages for reading the Megilla and **raises an objection against Rav and Shmuel, who said that one may read the Megilla in Greek but not in other foreign languages. It is taught in a *baraita*: If one read the Megilla in Coptic [*Giptit*], *Ivrit*, Elamite, Median, or Greek, he has not fulfilled his obligation,** indicating that one cannot fulfill his obligation by reading the Megilla in Greek.

Returning to the main discussion: the Gemara raises a challenge from a *beraisa*. *Kara Giftis, Ivris, Elamis, Madis, Yevanis lo yatza* — if one read the Megillah in Coptic, the language of the Hebrews, Elamite, Median, or Greek, one has not fulfilled the obligation. This appears to directly contradict Rav and Shmuel, since Greek is explicitly listed among the languages that do not work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְהָא: גִּיפְּטִית לְגִיפְּטִים, עִבְרִית לְעִבְרִים, עֵילָמִית לְעֵילָמִים, יְווֹנִית לִיווֹנִים — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The clause in the mishna that teaches that the Megilla may be read in a foreign language to one who speaks that foreign language **is comparable only to that which was taught in a different *baraita*: If one reads the Megilla in Coptic to Copts, in *Ivrit* to *Ivrim*, in Elamite to Elamites, or in Greek to Greeks, he has fulfilled his obligation.** The Megilla may be read in any language, provided the listener understands that language.

The Gemara then introduces a parallel *beraisa*: *Giftis le-Giftim, Ivris le-Ivrim, Elamis le-Elamim, Yevanis le-Yevanim — yatza*. One fulfills the obligation by reading each language *for the people of that language*. This is, the Gemara notes, simply the *pshat* of the Mishnah's own formulation: *le-loazim belaz* — for foreigners in their foreign tongue. The exception is not about Greek in particular; it is about tailoring the reading to its native speakers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל אַמַּאי מוֹקְמִי לַהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין בְּלַעַז יְווֹנִית? לוֹקְמוּהָ בְּכֹל לַעַז! [אֶלָּא, מַתְנִיתִין כְּבָרַיְיתָא.] וְכִי אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, בְּעָלְמָא אִיתְּמַר. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לַעַז יְווֹנִי לַכֹּל כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **if so,** that one who reads the Megilla in a foreign language that he speaks fulfills his obligation, **why did Rav and Shmuel establish the ruling of the mishna as referring specifically to Greek? Let them interpret it** as referring **to any foreign language that one speaks.** The Gemara explains: **Rather, the mishna is to be understood like the *baraita*,** that one who reads the Megilla in a language that he speaks fulfills his obligation; **and that which was stated in the name of Rav and Shmuel was said** as a **general** statement, not relating to the mishna but as an independent ruling, as follows: **Rav and Shmuel both say: The Greek language is acceptable for everyone,** i.e., anyone who reads the Megilla in Greek has fulfilled his obligation, even if he does not understand Greek.

This makes the prolonged effort to identify the exception as specifically Greek seem unnecessary, and the Gemara acknowledges it: given this understanding of the *beraisa*, why did Rav and Shmuel need to situate the entire case in Greek? Any language should work, as long as the reading is directed to native speakers of that language. The Gemara concludes: *ella masnisin ke-braisa* — Rav and Shmuel were simply mistaken about the Mishnah; the Mishnah follows the *beraisa* straightforwardly. *Le-loazim belaz* means: for people of that language, in their language.

### Greek Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָתָנֵי: יְווֹנִית לִיווֹנִים [לִיווֹנִים] — אִין, לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא — לָא! אִינְהוּ דַּאֲמוּר כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. דִּתְנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף סְפָרִים לֹא הִתִּירוּ שֶׁיִּכָּתְבוּ אֶלָּא יְווֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But doesn't the *baraita* cited above teach that if one reads the Megilla in Greek to Greeks he has fulfilled his obligation?** This implies that reading in Greek, **yes,** this is acceptable for Greeks, but **for everyone else, no,** it is not. The Gemara answers: Rav and Shmuel disagree with this statement of the *baraita*, because they **agree with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. As we learned in a mishna** (*Megilla* 8b): **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Even for books of the Bible,** the Sages **did not permit them to be written in any foreign language other than Greek,** indicating that Greek has a special status, and is treated like the original Hebrew.

Having set aside the question as it pertains to the Mishnah, the Gemara asks what Rav and Shmuel's point about Greek was intended to address. The answer is that they were making a broader claim: *laz Yevani le-chol kasher* — Greek is valid for *anyone*, not merely for Greek speakers. Just as a Megillah in Hebrew may be read to any person, even one who does not understand a word of Hebrew, so too a Megillah in Greek may be read to anyone. This rests on the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — *af sefarim lo hitiru she-yikasvu ella Yevanis* — that the sages permitted sacred books to be written only in Greek as an alternative to Hebrew. Since Greek functions as a kind of parallel to Hebrew for sacred writing, it shares Hebrew's universal applicability as a reading language.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימְרוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? אִי אָמְרִי הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי שְׁאָר סְפָרִים, אֲבָל מְגִילָּה דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״כִּכְתָבָם״ — אֵימָא לָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if this was the intention of Rav and Shmuel, **let them state explicitly: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** Why did Rav and Shmuel formulate their statement as if they were issuing a new ruling? The Gemara answers: **Had they said simply that the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, I would have said that this applies only to the other books of the Bible, but** with regard to **the Megilla, of which it is written: "According to their writing," I would say that one does not fulfill his obligation if he reads it in Greek.** Therefore they stated their own opinion to **teach us that even in the case of the Megilla one fulfills his obligation if he reads it in Greek.**

The Gemara then asks: why not simply state the ruling explicitly as *halachah ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*? The concern is that one might have thought Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position applied only to a *sefer Torah* and other sacred texts, but that the Megillah — which says *ke-chsavam*, as it is written — would require Hebrew specifically. The Gemara therefore teaches explicitly that Greek is valid for the Megillah as well.

It is worth noting that the word *ke-chsavam* appears in the Megillah in a context that cuts the other way entirely. *Ke-chsavam ve-chilshonam* — as each people's script and language — refers to Achashverosh's letters being sent to every nation in its own language and script. The very phrase used to insist on Hebrew has its origin in an embrace of universal linguistic diversity: to the Kushites in their script, to the Hebrews in theirs. There is something almost ironic in the fact that a phrase born of multicultural communication has been deployed as a limiting principle requiring Hebrew.

The practical upshot of Rav and Shmuel's position — though we do not rule this way — is that the same way anyone can fulfill the obligation by hearing a Megillah in Hebrew, even without understanding it, anyone could also fulfill it by hearing a Megillah in Greek. The *pshat* of the Mishnah, however, remains: a foreign-language scroll is valid only for native speakers of that language, meaning both that the scroll constitutes a sufficient *kasher* Megillah from the standpoint of the ritual object, and that the act of reading in that language counts as the *mitzvah* — but only under that condition.

### Understanding Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַלּוֹעֵז שֶׁשָּׁמַע אַשּׁוּרִית יָצָא וְכוּ׳. וְהָא לָא יָדַע מַאי קָאָמְרִי? מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַנָּשִׁים וְעַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** ** It was taught in the mishna: And one who speaks a foreign language who heard the Megilla being read in *Ashurit,** i.e., in Hebrew, **has fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it so that he does not understand what they are saying?** Since he does not understand Hebrew, how does he fulfill his obligation? The Gemara answers: **It is just as it is with women and uneducated people;** they too understand little Hebrew, but nevertheless they fulfill their obligation when they hear the Megilla read in that language.

This raises a natural question about the middle ground: what of someone who is a native speaker of another language but also understands Hebrew reasonably well, though not as fluently as their mother tongue? The analysis suggests that the native-language exception applies when Hebrew is genuinely inaccessible; once someone is sufficiently fluent in Hebrew, the accommodations afforded to *loazim* would no longer apply. The threshold is not sharply defined by the Gemara here, but the underlying principle seems to be: the tailoring to native language is for those to whom Hebrew is not a real option.

One observation worth making is that the tradition of the *torgeman* — the translator who rendered the Torah reading into Aramaic immediately after each verse — is conspicuously absent from this discussion. That may itself be telling. The *torgeman* system worked precisely because the listeners had some understanding of the Hebrew but needed the Aramaic clarification. Here the Gemara is dealing with people who may have no access to Hebrew at all. And that may be exactly why the Gemara in the case of the Mishnah concludes that the native-language Megillah is actually the *lechatchila* option for a *loaz*, with the Hebrew reading being the *bediavad* fallback — the language of *ve-lo shama sheyesh yatza*, "if one heard it in the original," sounds like a post-facto concession.

### Ravina Pirsumei Nisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: אַטּוּ אֲנַן ״הָאֲחַשְׁתְּרָנִים בְּנֵי הָרַמָּכִים״, מִי יָדְעִינַן? אֶלָּא מִצְוַת קְרִיאָה וּפַרְסוֹמֵי נִיסָּא, הָכָא נָמֵי — מִצְוַת קְרִיאָה וּפַרְסוֹמֵי נִיסָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina strongly objects to the premise of the question raised above,** i.e., that someone who does not understand the original, untranslated language of the Megilla cannot fulfill his obligation. **Is that to say that even we,** the Sages, who are very well acquainted with Hebrew, **know for certain the meaning of the obscure words ha'aḥashteranim benei haramakhim** (Esther 8:10), often translated as: "Used in the royal service, bred from the stud"? **But nonetheless, we fulfill the mitzva of reading the Megilla and publicizing the miracle of Purim** by reading these words as they appear in the original text. **Here too,** one who speaks a foreign language who hears the Megilla being read in Hebrew fulfills the **mitzva of reading the Megilla and publicizing the Purim miracle,** even if he does not understand the words themselves.

The Gemara presses: but if someone hears the Megillah in Hebrew without understanding a word of it — *ve-ha lo yadai mai kaamri* — how can they possibly be *yotzei*? The Gemara responds: *midai d'hava anashim* — it is no different from women and *amei ha-aretz* who similarly do not understand the Hebrew but fulfill the obligation by hearing it.

This answer only partially satisfies. Ravina responds with a rhetorical challenge of his own: *atu anan ha-achashteranim bnei ha-ramachim mi yadeinan?* — do we ourselves know what "the sons of the steeds" means? We, the rabbis, also recite phrases in the Megillah whose precise meaning eludes us. What then is the obligation? *Mitzvas kriya u-pirsumei nisa* — there is a commandment to read it, and it publicizes the miracle.

This is worth examining carefully. *Pirsumei nisa* would seem to require that the audience understand what is being said — if the miracle is to be publicized, surely comprehension is necessary. Yet Ravina uses *pirsumei nisa* to explain why even incomprehension does not defeat the obligation. Rashi's comment illuminates this: even those who do not understand the words they are hearing will ask those around them what this reading is and how the miracles came about — *sho'alim es ha-shom'im ve-omrim mah hi ha-kriyah ha-zo ve-eich ha-nissim modiyim lahem*. The public nature of the gathering creates the publicizing of the miracle, even without precise verbal comprehension. The ritual act of coming together, hearing the reading performed, and then talking about it — that is *pirsumei nisa*.

This explains why Ravina uses *pirsumei nisa* alongside *mitzvas kriya* rather than instead of it. The commandment to read has its own integrity as a ritual act; the *pirsumei nisa* dimension does not require precise understanding because it operates at the level of communal experience and subsequent conversation. The parallel to Chanukah is apt: on Chanukah there is no reading of a text at all — there is lighting, there is gathering, there is the public marking of the miracle. Here the reading is more specific, but the *pirsumei nisa* component functions similarly.

### Rare Hebrew Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָאָהּ סֵירוּגִין יָצָא וְכוּ׳. לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״סֵירוּגִין״, שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי דְּקָאָמְרָה לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דַּהֲווֹ עָיְילִי פִּסְקֵי פִּסְקֵי לְבֵי רַבִּי: עַד מָתַי אַתֶּם נִכְנָסִין סֵירוּגִין סֵירוּגִין?
>
> **Translation:** ** If one reads the Megilla at intervals [seirugin] he has fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara relates that **the Sages did not know what is meant by the word seirugin.** One day **they heard the maidservant in Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's house saying to the Sages who were entering the house intermittently rather than in a single group: How long are you going to enter seirugin seirugin?** As she lived in Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's house and certainly heard the most proper Hebrew being spoken, they understood from this that the word seirugin means at intervals.

The Gemara records a series of cases in which the rabbis encountered rare words — in Mishnaic texts, in Aramaic texts, and in Scriptural verses — whose meaning they did not know, and how they came to understand them through observation of everyday speech.

The first concerns the word *seirugin* itself, which appears in the Mishnah's ruling that one who reads the Megillah with breaks (*kara seirugin*) still fulfills the obligation. The rabbis did not know what the word meant. They heard the maidservant of Rabbi Judah the Patriarch's household (*amta d'vei Rabbi*) chide the scholars who were entering the house in disconnected groups rather than all together: *ad matay atem nichnasin seirugin seirugin* — how long will you keep entering in broken-up intervals? From this they understood: *seirugin* means breaks, intervals, a chopped-up pattern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״חֲלוֹגְלוֹגוֹת״. שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי דַּאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מְבַדַּר פַּרְפְּחִינֵי: עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מְפַזֵּר חֲלוֹגְלוֹגְךָ?
>
> **Translation:** It is similarly related that **the Sages did not know what is meant by the word ḥalogelogot,** which appears in various *mishnayot* and *baraitot*. One day **they heard the maidservant in Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's house saying to a certain man who was scattering purslane: How long will you go on scattering your ḥalogelogot?** And from this they understood that ḥalogelogot is purslane.

The second word was *haluglogot*, a vegetable mentioned in various *mishnayot*. Again, from the same household: the maidservant instructed a certain man to stop scattering his *parpachini* — another vegetable — and called it *haluglogekha*. The vegetable has been identified as parsley.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״סַלְסְלֶהָ וּתְרוֹמְמֶךָּ״. שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי דַּהֲווֹת אָמְרָה לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה מְהַפֵּךְ בְּמַזְיֵיהּ, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מְסַלְסֵל בִּשְׂעָרְךָ?
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **the Sages did not know what is meant by salseleha in the verse: "Get wisdom…salseleha and it will exalt you"** (Proverbs 4:7–8). One day **they heard the maidservant in Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's house talking to a certain man who was twirling his hair, saying to him: How long will you go on twirling [mesalsel] your hair?** And from this they understood that the verse is saying: Turn wisdom around and around, and it will exalt you.

The third case involved the scriptural phrase *salsela u-teromemeka* — delve into it and it will lift you up — from the book of Proverbs, in the context of Torah study. The rabbis did not understand *mesalsel*. The maidservant addressed a man who was constantly running his fingers through his hair: *ad matay ata mesalsel be-sa'arekha* — how long will you keep tussling your hair? The meaning: to run one's fingers through, to delve into and turn over — and thus, immerse yourself in Torah and it will elevate you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי: ״הַשְׁלֵךְ עַל ה׳ יְהָבְךָ״. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: זִימְנָא חֲדָא הֲוָה אָזֵילְנָא בַּהֲדֵי הָהוּא טַיָּיעָא, וְקָא דָרֵינָא טוּנָא וַאֲמַר לִי: שְׁקוֹל יַהְבָּיךְ וּשְׁדִי אַגַּמְלַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates additional examples: **The Sages did not know what is meant by the word yehav in the verse: "Cast upon the Lord your yehav"** (Psalms 55:23). **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: One time I was traveling with a certain Arab [Tayya'a] and I was carrying a load, and he said to me: Take your yehav and throw it on my camel,** and I understood that yehav means a load or burden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן מַאי ״וְטֵאטֵאתִיהָ בְּמַטְאֲטֵא הַשְׁמֵד״. שַׁמְעוּהָ לְאַמְּתָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי דַּהֲווֹת אָמְרָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ: שְׁקוּלִי טָאטִיתָא וְטַאטִי בֵּיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** And similarly, **the Sages did not know what is meant by the word matatei in the verse: "And I will tatei it with the matatei of destruction"** (Isaiah 14:23). One day **they heard the maidservant in Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's house saying to her friend: Take a tateita and tati the house,** from which they understood that a matatei is a broom, and the verb tati means to sweep.

What is striking in all three cases is that the maidservant of Rabbi's household speaks in Hebrew — the narrative framing is Aramaic, but her direct speech consistently uses the Hebrew terms that eluded the scholars. The vernacular of the time was Aramaic; Hebrew was the language of learning. That she was fluent in these rarer Hebrew usages likely reflects the linguistic environment of Rabbi's household, where Hebrew held a more prominent place. And there is a pointed irony here that one participant in the shiur rightly noted: just moments before, the Gemara had invoked women and *amei ha-aretz* as examples of people who do not understand Hebrew — and now the *amta d'vei Rabbi* is the one teaching the rabbis what Hebrew words mean. It is a notable counterpoint.

### Seirugin Breaks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קְרָאָהּ סֵירוּגִין — יָצָא, סֵירוּסִין — לֹא יָצָא. רַבִּי מוּנָא אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַף בְּסֵירוּגִין, אִם שָׁהָה כְּדֵי לִגְמוֹר אֶת כּוּלָּהּ — חוֹזֵר לָרֹאשׁ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** On the matter of reading the Megilla with interruptions, **the Sages taught the following *baraita*: If one reads the Megilla at intervals,** pausing and resuming at intervals, **he has fulfilled his obligation,** but if he reads it **out of order,** i.e., if he changes the order of the words or verses of the Megilla, **he has not fulfilled his obligation.** **Rabbi Mona said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda: Even** when he reads it **at intervals, if he pauses and interrupts his reading long enough for one to finish reading the whole Megilla during that time, he must go back to the beginning and start again. Rav Yosef said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona,** who **stated** his opinion **in the name of Rabbi Yehuda.**

Now that we understand *seirugin*, the Gemara returns to the ruling itself: *kara seirugin yatza* — one who reads with breaks still fulfills the obligation. The companion ruling is *serusin lo yatza* — reading out of order, or backwards, does not fulfill the obligation.

## Reading Continuity…

### Rav Mona Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: כְּדֵי לִגְמוֹר אֶת כּוּלָּהּ — מֵהֵיכָא דְּקָאֵי לְסֵיפָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא: מֵרֵישָׁא לְסֵיפָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵרֵישָׁא לְסֵיפָא, דְּאִם כֵּן, נָתַתָּ דְּבָרֶיךָ לְשִׁיעוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** When Rabbi Mona said: Long **enough for one to finish reading the whole Megilla, did he mean from** the verse **where he is now until the end? Or perhaps he meant long enough to read the entire Megilla from the beginning until the end. He said to him:** Rabbi Mona meant **from the beginning until the end,** as **if it were so that he meant from where he paused until the end of the Megilla, you would be subjecting your statement to the varying circumstances of each case.** There would be no standard principle to determine the length of a permitted pause; in each case, depending on where one stopped, it would take a different amount of time to finish the Megilla until the end. And the Sages did not institute measures that are not standardized.

The Gemara returns to the laws of *seirugin*. Rav Mona says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda: even regarding *seirugin*, if the pause was long enough — *im shahah kedei ligmor* — specifically, if in that interval one could have read the entire Megillah from beginning to end, then one must return to the beginning. At that scale, the continuity of the reading has been broken. Rav Yosef rules: *halachah ke-Rav Mona*.

Abaye then asks Rav Yosef to clarify: does "long enough to finish" mean long enough to read from the current point to the end of the Megillah, or long enough to read the entire Megillah from beginning to end? Rav Yosef answers: *mereisha le-sofah* — from beginning to end, the full measure. The reason is a fundamental principle of legal precision: *de-im ken natata devarecha le-shiurin* — if the threshold changes depending on where in the Megillah one is pausing, the measurement has no fixed value. A legal standard that varies at every moment is not a workable standard. The measure must be fixed, and the fixed measure is the full reading time of the Megillah.

### Rav vs. Shmuel Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא. בְּסוּרָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי. בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי: אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא. רַב בִּיבִי מַתְנִי אִיפְּכָא, רַב אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said that Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba said: Rav said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona, but Shmuel said that the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona.** The Gemara elaborates: **This is how they taught the opinions of the Sages in Sura. However, in Pumbedita they taught** it slightly differently, **like this: Rav Kahana said that Rav said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona, but Shmuel said that the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona.** **Rav Beivai taught the opposite: Rav said that the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona, but Shmuel said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Mona.**

The Gemara then presents the *halachic* dispute in two parallel formulations — one from Sura, one from Pumbedisa — but both arrive at the same substantive *machlokes*: Rav says we rule like Rav Mona, Shmuel says we do not. The only variation between the two versions is the identity of who transmitted Rav's position: in the Sura version it comes through Rav Abba citing Rav Yirmeya bar Abba, while in the Pumbedisa version it comes through Rav Kahana. Rav Bibi then reverses the attributions entirely: Rav says we do not rule like Rav Mona, and Shmuel says we do.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: נְקוֹט דְּרַב בִּיבִי בִּידָךְ, דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הוּא דְּחָיֵישׁ לִיחִידָאָה. דִּתְנַן: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ אָחִיו אֶת אֲחוֹתָהּ, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה אָמְרוּ, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הַמְתֵּן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה אָחִיךָ הַגָּדוֹל מַעֲשֶׂה. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Grasp the version of Rav Beivai in your hand,** i.e., accept it as the most authoritative one. It appears to be correct, as we know that **Shmuel takes into consideration even an individual dissenting opinion when it is more stringent than the majority opinion.** The Gemara proves its assertion about Shmuel: **As we learned in a mishna with regard to a different matter, the case of a widow whose husband died childless and who was waiting for one of his surviving brothers to perform the required levirate marriage with her or, alternatively, to release her with the *ḥalitza* ceremony:** In a case where **a woman was waiting for her brother-in-law and in the meantime one of her deceased husband's brothers betrothed this woman's sister,** they said **in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: We say to this brother: Wait until your older brother acts**, performing the levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*. The reason for this is that before levirate marriage or *ḥalitza* is performed, all the brothers are considered, by rabbinic decree, to have a quasi-marital connection with the widow. Consequently, just as one may not marry his wife's sister, he may not marry the sister of a woman who is waiting for him to perform levirate marriage. The Sages, however, disagree with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and maintain that only the oldest of the brothers is considered bound to the widow, as he is the primary candidate to perform these acts. Consequently, the widow has no connection at all with the other brothers. **And Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira.** This demonstrates that Shmuel takes into consideration the opinion of a single Sage against the majority when that minority opinion is more stringent than the majority opinion.

Rav Yosef instructs: *nekote de-Rav Bibi be-yadcha* — hold onto Rav Bibi's version. Why? *De-Shmuel hu de-chaish le-yachida'ah* — because it is characteristic of Shmuel to be concerned with individual minority opinions. This is demonstrated from a completely different context: a Mishnah dealing with *shomeres yavam*, in which a brother-in-law performs *kiddushin* with the sister of the woman awaiting *yibum* or *chalitzah*. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's minority opinion holds that one should wait until the elder brother has acted before consummating that marriage — since the elder brother's *zika* creates a quasi-marital connection to the first woman that must be resolved first. The consensus does not follow Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, yet *Shmuel amar halachah ke-Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* — Shmuel rules like the minority opinion. This is the one instance across all of *Shas* where Shmuel follows a *da'as yachid* against the consensus, and it establishes Shmuel's general tendency to take minority positions seriously.

Applied here: it makes sense that it is Shmuel who would be *choshesh* about the size of the break in the Megillah reading, even if the majority consensus is that no break — however large — invalidates the reading. Tosafot notes that since *halachah* generally follows Rav against Shmuel in disputes of this kind, the ruling is that we do not require concern about the size of a break. One may pause at length during the Megillah reading and still fulfill the obligation.

## Scroll Validity

### Missing Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִשְׁמִיט בָּהּ סוֹפֵר אוֹתִיּוֹת אוֹ פְּסוּקִין וּקְרָאָן הַקּוֹרֵא כִּמְתוּרְגְּמָן הַמְתַרְגֵּם — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** ** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: If the scribe who wrote the Megilla omitted letters or even complete verses when he wrote it, and the reader read these missing items as a translator would do when translating,** i.e., he recited the missing parts by heart, **he has fulfilled his obligation.** Missing material in a Megilla and reading words or verses by heart do not invalidate the reading.

The Gemara now turns from the act of reading to the physical scroll itself. A *beraisa* teaches: *hishmit bah ha-sofer osiyos o pesukim* — if the scribe omitted letters or verses — *ve-karan ha-koreh ke-meturgeman ha-metargem* — and the reader filled in those missing words as a translator would paraphrase, one has fulfilled the obligation. This ruling establishes two things simultaneously: first, a scroll missing some words or letters can still qualify as a *kasher* Megillah scroll; and second, even though the Megillah must generally be read *mitoch ha-sefer* — from the scroll — not every word needs to come from the written text.

The practical relevance is immediate. At a public Megillah reading, if a congregant was distracted and missed a *pasuk*, that person may read the missing verses independently — even without a *kasher* Megillah scroll in hand, even just from a printed text. The same flexibility that applies to the *ba'al koreh* who encounters an incomplete scroll applies to the listener who needs to fill a gap.

### Smudged Letters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיוּ בָּהּ אוֹתִיּוֹת מְטוּשְׁטָשׁוֹת אוֹ מְקוֹרָעוֹת, אִם רִשּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר — כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאִם לָאו — פְּסוּלָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּכוּלַּהּ, הָא בְּמִקְצָתַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection from another *baraita*: If a Megilla contains letters that are blurred or torn,** the following distinction applies: **If their imprint is still visible,** the Megilla **is fit for reading, but if not, it is unfit.** This *baraita* indicates that even the omission of several letters invalidates the Megilla. The Gemara resolves the contradiction between the two *baraitot*: **This is not difficult. This second *baraita*, which says that a Megilla with blurred or torn letters is unfit, is referring to a case where this is so throughout the whole of the Megilla; whereas this first *baraita*, which says that a Megilla is fit even if whole verses are missing, is referring to a case where the missing material is in only part of it.**

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *beraisa* that seems to impose stricter standards: if letters were *mitushtashot o mekuro'ot* — smudged or broken — the scroll is *kasher* only if *reshumanh nikar*, the form of the letter remains discernible. If not, it is *pasul*. This appears to suggest that even a few problem letters can invalidate the scroll. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya ha be-kulah ha be-miktzatah* — the stricter ruling addresses the case where most of the scroll is smudged, the more lenient ruling addresses partial deficiencies.

### Percentage Threshold

The operative principle is *rov* — majority. As long as more than half the scroll is properly written, it remains *kasher*; the reader may supply the missing portions from memory without invalidating the reading. However — and the Rama codifies this — even within the valid majority, a complete *inyan*, a discrete section, cannot be entirely absent, and the very beginning or very end of the Megillah cannot be missing even in a small amount. The threshold is approximately 51% properly written, with those qualifications.

The *Shulchan Aruch* formulation: *tzarich she-tehei ktuvah kulah lefanav le-chatchilah* — ideally the full text must be before the reader — *aval bediavad im hishmit ha-sofer be-emtza teiva afilu ad chatziyah ve-kara'o ha-korei al peh yatza* — but after the fact, even if up to nearly half the text is missing and the reader supplied it orally, the obligation is fulfilled. This is significant: one can potentially read up to fifty percent of the Megillah from memory and still be *yotzei bediavad*, provided the missing portions do not constitute whole sections and are not from the very beginning or end.

## Reading Continuity…

### Serussin Out of Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִשְׁמִיט בָּהּ הַקּוֹרֵא פָּסוּק אֶחָד, לֹא יֹאמַר: אֶקְרָא אֶת כּוּלָּהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ אֶקְרָא אוֹתוֹ פָּסוּק, אֶלָּא קוֹרֵא מֵאוֹתוֹ פָּסוּק וְאֵילָךְ. נִכְנַס לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וּמָצָא צִבּוּר שֶׁקָּרְאוּ חֶצְיָהּ, לֹא יֹאמַר: אֶקְרָא חֶצְיָהּ עִם הַצִּבּוּר וְאַחַר כָּךְ אֶקְרָא חֶצְיָהּ, אֶלָּא קוֹרֵא אוֹתָהּ מִתְּחִילָּתָהּ וְעַד סוֹפָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught in a *baraita*: If the reader of the Megilla omitted one verse, he may not say: I will continue to read the whole of the Megilla in order, and afterward I will go back and read that verse that I omitted. Rather, he must go back and read from that verse that he omitted and continue from there to the end of the Megilla.** Similarly, **if one enters a synagogue and encounters a congregation that has already read half of the Megilla, he may not say: I will read the second half of the Megilla with the congregation, and afterward I will read the first half. Rather, he must read it in its proper order from the beginning until the end.**

The *beraisa* teaches: *hishmit bah ha-korei pasuk echad* — if the reader skipped a *pasuk* — one may not continue to the end and then return to read the skipped verse. *Ela korei me-oto pasuk ve-eilach* — one must go back to that verse and read forward from there.

Similarly: if someone enters the synagogue and finds the congregation already halfway through the reading, one may not listen to the rest with the congregation and then go back to read the beginning. *Korei otah mi-techilatan ve-ad sofah* — the reading must proceed from beginning to end, in order. Reading out of sequence is *serusin*, which — as established — invalidates the fulfillment of the obligation.

## Intent & Consciousness

### Misnamnem Drowsy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתְנַמְנֵם יָצָא וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מִתְנַמְנֵם? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נִים וְלָא נִים, תִּיר וְלָא תִּיר, דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ וְעָנֵי וְלָא יָדַע לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי סְבָרָא, וְכִי מַדְכְּרוּ לֵיהּ מִידְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** ** It is taught in the mishna: If one read the Megilla while he is dozing off, he has fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of the case of dozing off? Rav Ashi said:** It is referring to a situation in which one is **asleep yet not fully asleep, awake yet not fully awake. If someone calls him he answers. And he is in a mental state in which he does not know how to provide an answer that requires logical reasoning, but when people remind him about something that has happened, he remembers it.**

The Mishnah rules *misnamnem yatza* — one who was dozing still fulfills the obligation — but if one was *nirdim*, fully asleep, one does not. The Gemara asks *heichi dami misnamnem* — what precisely is the state of *misnamnem*? Rav Ashi defines it: *nim ve-lo nim, tair ve-lo tair* — asleep and not asleep, awake and not awake. If called, one will respond; but if asked to reason through something, one cannot. Yet if reminded of something, one will remember. That liminal state — between wakefulness and sleep, responsive but not fully processing — is *misnamnem*, and within that state one is considered to have fulfilled the obligation.

### Copying Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה כּוֹתְבָהּ דּוֹרְשָׁהּ וּמַגִּיהָהּ אִם כִּוֵּן לִבּוֹ יָצָא וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקָא מְסַדַּר פְּסוּקָא פְּסוּקָא וְכָתֵב לַהּ, כִּי כִּוֵּן לִבּוֹ מַאי הָוֵי? עַל פֶּה הוּא! אֶלָּא דְּכָתֵב פְּסוּקָא פְּסוּקָא וְקָרֵי לֵיהּ,
>
> **Translation:** ** The mishna continues: If one was writing a Megilla, or expounding upon it, or correcting** it, and he read all its words as he was doing so, **if he had intent to fulfill his obligation with that reading he has fulfilled his obligation.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of this case? If he was articulating each verse of the Megilla and then writing it down, what of it that he intended to fulfill his obligation with that reading, since he recited those words by heart? Rather, it must be that he first wrote each verse in the Megilla and then read it out.**

The Mishnah continues: *haya kotevah, dorshah, u-magiha — im kiven libo yatza* — if one was copying the Megillah, expounding on it, or proofreading it, if one had intent, one fulfills the obligation. This introduces the question of *kavanah* — intent — in reading the Megillah. At minimum, intent to read the words is required; whether intent to fulfill the *mitzvah* is additionally necessary is a broader question, but the Gemara's focus here is on the act of reading itself.

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the scenario? If the person was composing the Megillah verse by verse from memory — reciting a verse and then writing it — the words being spoken preceded their being written, making the reading *al peh*, from memory. That does not help. The valid scenario, the Gemara concludes, is *de-manacha Megillah kameih ve-kary lah, ve-kary lah minah pesuka pesuka ve-khatev lah* — a complete Megillah lies before the copyist, and the copyist reads each verse from that existing scroll before transcribing it. Each word is read from a written source. The reading is therefore *mitoch ha-ksav* — from written text — even though the new scroll being produced is not yet complete.

### Complete Scroll

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי יָצָא? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״מֵאִישׁ יְהוּדִי״ — צְרִיכָה שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּתוּבָה כּוּלָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does one really fulfill his obligation in this way? Didn't Rabbi Ḥelbo say that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said that Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the statement of the one who says that the Megilla must be read in its entirety.** **And moreover, he said that even according to the one who said that one need not read the entire Megilla, but only from "There was a certain Jew"** (Esther 2:5) **and onward, the Megilla itself must nevertheless be written in its entirety.** How, then, can it be suggested that one who is reading each verse as he writes it can fulfill his obligation by reading from a Megilla that is not yet written to the end?

Rav Chama bar Guria citing Rav rules: *halachah ke-divrei omer kulah* — the *halachah* requires reading the entire Megillah. Various opinions exist about where the Megillah reading must begin — whether from the first verse (*vayehi bimei Achashverosh*) or from a later point (*ish Yehudi*) — but regardless, even according to the opinion that the reading need not start from the very beginning, the scroll itself must be complete: *tzrichah she-tehei ktuvah kulah* — the entire Megillah must be written in the scroll.

This addresses the copying scenario directly. One cannot fulfill the obligation by writing a word, reading it, writing the next word, reading it, and so on — because at each moment of reading, what lies before the reader is not a *kasher* Megillah. The scroll must pre-exist the reading as a complete object. What is read must be read from a legitimate scroll.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: דְּמַנְּחָה מְגִילָּה קַמֵּיהּ וְקָרֵי (לַהּ) מִינַּהּ פְּסוּקָא פְּסוּקָא וְכָתַב לָהּ. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָסוּר לִכְתּוֹב אוֹת אַחַת שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב. דִּלְמָא דְּאִתְרְמִי לֵיהּ אִתְרְמוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, this is a case in which a complete Megilla is lying before him and he is copying from it, and he was reading from that complete Megilla verse by verse and then writing each verse in his new copy.** The Gemara proposes: **Let us say that this supports the opinion of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, as Rabba bar bar Ḥana said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is prohibited to write even a single letter of the Bible when not copying from a written text.** Since it was necessary to explain the mishna as addressing a case in which one was copying a Megillah out of a written text lying before him, this supports Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling. The Gemara rejects this: This is not a proof, **as perhaps the mishna is merely dealing with a case where this is what happened to be what occurred,** that one happened to be copying the text from an existing Megillah, but it is not a requirement to do this.

The Gemara then raises a possible support from Rabbah Bar Bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *assur likhtov ot achat she-lo min ha-ksav* — it is forbidden to write even one letter of a *sefer Torah* without copying from an existing written text. If that is the rule, then the copying scenario can simply presuppose that one is working from a pre-existing Megillah. But the Gemara deflects: perhaps that ruling does not apply technically to the Megillah, and the case here is simply *de-istrami lei istramuyei* — it happened to involve copying from an existing scroll, without that being a requirement. The question of whether *she-lo min ha-ksav* is a technical prohibition or merely a safeguard remains open.

## Writing from Memory

### Rabbi Meir Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָסוּר לִכְתּוֹב אוֹת אַחַת שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב. מֵיתִיבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר שֶׁהָלַךְ לְעַבֵּר שָׁנָה בְּעַסְיָא, וְלֹא הָיָה שָׁם מְגִילָּה וּכְתָבָהּ מִלִּבּוֹ — וּקְרָאָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines Rabba bar bar Ḥana's statement. With regard to **the matter itself, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is prohibited to write even a single letter of the Bible when not copying from a written text.** The Gemara **raises an objection from a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said:** One Adar there was **an incident involving Rabbi Meir, who went to intercalate the year in Asia Minor,** as, owing to persecutory decrees, he could not do this in Eretz Yisrael. **And there was no Megilla there when Purim arrived, so he wrote a Megilla by heart and read from it.**

The Gemara examines the position of Rabbi Yochanan — *assur likhtov ot achat she-lo min ha-ksav* — and challenges it with a story: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar reports that Rabbi Meir once traveled to Asia to intercalate the year. No Megillah scroll was available; Rabbi Meir wrote the entire Megillah from memory and they read from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שָׁאנֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּמִיקַּיַּים בֵּיהּ ״וְעַפְעַפֶּיךָ יַיְשִׁירוּ נֶגְדְּךָ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִדִּפְתִּי: מַאי ״וְעַפְעַפֶּיךָ יַיְשִׁירוּ נֶגְדְּךָ״? אָמַר לוֹ: אֵלּוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: ״הֲתָעִיף עֵינֶיךָ בּוֹ וְאֵינֶנּוּ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי מְיוּשָּׁרִין הֵן אֵצֶל רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: Rabbi Meir is different, as in him is fulfilled the verse: "And let your eyelids look straight before you"** (Proverbs 4:25), and with regard to this verse, **Rami bar Ḥama said to Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti: What is the meaning of the phrase "and let your eyelids [afapekha]," from the root *a-p-p*, "look straight [yaishiru] before you"? He said to him: This is referring to the words of the Torah,** which are difficult to remember exactly, **and with regard to which it is written: "Will you glance upon it fleetingly [hata'if], from the root *a-p-p*, with your eyes? It is already gone"** (Proverbs 23:5), **but nevertheless they remain exact [meyusharin] in the memory of Rabbi Meir,** since he knows them all by heart.

Rabbi Abbahu explains this exception: *shani Rabbi Meir de-mikim beih ve-afapekha yashiru negdekha* — Rabbi Meir is different, because the verse "your eyelids are straight before you" applied to him. The image is of perfect clarity of sight — the entire text was, as it were, laid out before him with complete precision. He knew every letter exactly. The rule requiring one to copy from an existing text exists as a safeguard against error; for Rabbi Meir, who had the text with absolute fidelity, the safeguard was unnecessary.

Rami bar Chama then asks Rabbi Yirmiyah from Difti to explain the verse *ve-afapekha yashiru negdekha*. The explanation: it refers to words of Torah, about which the verse says *hata'if einekha bo ve-einennu* — avert your gaze from it and it disappears. Torah is so elusive in its precise details — especially in the written form, where every letter matters — that even a master can lose it. But *afilu hachi meyusharin hen etzel Rabbi Meir* — even so, for Rabbi Meir the text was perfectly clear and present. He could write it without error.

### She'at Hadchak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב חֲנַנְאֵל דַּהֲוָה כָּתֵב סְפָרִים שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רְאוּיָה כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לִיכָּתֵב עַל פִּיךְ, אֶלָּא כָּךְ אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אָסוּר לִכְתּוֹב אוֹת אַחַת שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב. מִדְּקָאָמַר: כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ רְאוּיָה שֶׁתִּיכָּתֵב עַל פִּיךְ, מִכְּלָל דִּמְיוּשָּׁרִין הֵן אֶצְלוֹ, וְהָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר כָּתַב? שְׁעַת הַדְּחָק שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **Rav Ḥisda once found Rav Ḥananel writing Torah scrolls, but he was not copying them from a written text,** as he knew it all by heart. **He said to him: It is fitting for the entire Torah to be written by your mouth,** i.e., relying on your memory, **but this is what the Sages said: It is prohibited to write even a single letter of the Bible when not copying from a written text.** The Gemara asks: **Since Rav Ḥisda said to him: The entire Torah is fitting to be written by your mouth,** it may be concluded **by inference that the words of the Torah were exact in his memory,** i.e., that Rav Ḥananel enjoyed total mastery of the text. **But didn't we say that Rabbi Meir wrote a Megilla without copying from a text due to similar proficiency?** The Gemara answers: **A time of exigent circumstances is different;** since there was no other option available, he was permitted to rely on his expertise, but otherwise this must not be done.

The Gemara then records Rav Chisda finding Rav Chananel writing *sefarim* — presumably *Sifrei Torah* — without copying from an existing text. Rav Chisda tells him: I have no doubt that *re'uyah kol ha-Torah kulah likatev al pikha* — the entire Torah could be written from your mouth; you know it perfectly. *Ella kach amru chachamim: assur likhtov ot achat she-lo min ha-ksav* — but the sages said: one may not write even one letter without copying from a written source. Whether this means the prohibition admits no exceptions even for those who know the text perfectly, or simply that we do not make individual exceptions because everyone imagines their knowledge to be perfect, the practical ruling is clear: one does not write *she-lo min ha-ksav*.

Yet by Rabbi Meir it was permitted. The Gemara resolves this: *she'at ha-dechak shani* — a time of pressing need is different. Rabbi Meir's situation — traveling in Asia, no Megillah available, the community unable to fulfill the *mitzvah* of reading — constituted a *she'at ha-dechak* that justified the exception. In ordinary circumstances, even someone who knows the text with complete accuracy does not write from memory. In an emergency, the normal safeguard gives way.

### Tefillin Mezuzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי שְׁרָא לִדְבֵי בַּר חָבוּ לְמִיכְתַּב תְּפִלִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב. כְּמַאן? כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ: תְּפִלִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת נִכְתָּבוֹת שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב, וְאֵין צְרִיכוֹת שִׂרְטוּט. וְהִלְכְתָא: תְּפִלִּין אֵין צְרִיכִין שִׂרְטוּט, מְזוּזוֹת צְרִיכִין שִׂרְטוּט. אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי נִכְתָּבוֹת שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַכְּתָב. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִיגְרָס גְּרִיסִין.
>
> **Translation:** It was further related that **Abaye permitted the scribes of the house of ben Ḥavu to write phylacteries and *mezuzot* when they were not copying from a pre-existing text.** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose opinion did he issue this allowance?** The Gemara explains: **In accordance with** the opinion of **the following *tanna*, as it is taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Yirmeya said in the name of our master,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **Phylacteries and *mezuzot* may be written when they are not copied from a written text, and they do not require scoring,** i.e., the parchment is not required to have lines etched in it. **The Gemara concludes: And the *halakha* is as follows: Phylacteries do not require scoring, whereas *mezuzot* require scoring. And both these and those, phylacteries and *mezuzot*, may be written when the scribe is not copying from a written text. What is the reason for this exception? These short texts are well known to all scribes,** and therefore it is permitted to write them by heart.

Finally, the Gemara records that Abaye permitted the household of Bar Chavu to write *tefillin* and *mezuzot* without copying from an existing text. The Gemara asks: like whom does this ruling go? It follows a *tanna* — Rabbi Yirmiyah citing Rabbeinu — who explicitly states that *tefillin u-mezuzot nikhtavim she-lo min ha-ksav* — *tefillin* and *mezuzot* may be written from memory, without copying. They also do not require *sirtut*, the scoring of lines, though the *halachah* distinguishes between them: *tefillin ein tzerikhin sirtut, mezuzah tzrichah sirtut* — *tefillin* do not require scoring but *mezuzot* do. Both, however, may be written *she-lo min ha-ksav*.

The reason given: *migras gariseynhu* — people know these texts thoroughly. *Shema* and the adjoining passages are recited twice daily; their texts are universally familiar. This is a striking conclusion. One might have expected *tefillin* and *mezuzot* — the most ritually charged of all scribal objects — to carry the strictest requirements regarding transcription. Yet here it emerges that *she-lo min ha-ksav* is not a technical legal requirement at all, but only a rabbinic safeguard against error. Where the text is so well-known that error is practically impossible, the safeguard has no application. The question of whether the scroll was *read* from a *tefillin* or *mezuzah* — an interesting question to raise — lies outside the scope of the discussion here, since these objects are not read from in the relevant halachic sense.
