# Bava Kamma 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Shmuel: Hevel

### Rav-Shmuel Intro

The daf opens by returning to the debate between Rav and Shmuel concerning whether *hevel* — the thick, pressurized air that accumulates within a *bor* — is a necessary condition for liability. To briefly recap the stakes of this dispute: Rav holds that *hevel* is the operative mechanism through which the owner of a pit is liable, while Shmuel holds that *chavata* — the blunt impact of striking the ground or wall — is an independent basis for liability. The practical difference emerges in a case such as a raised mound on which someone trips and strikes the ground: since no enclosed cavity is involved, there is no *hevel*, and Rav would therefore exempt the owner, while Shmuel would hold him liable based on the impact alone.

### Mishnah Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיוּ פְּחוּתִין מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וּמֵת – פָּטוּר. וְאִם הוּזַּק בּוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If any of the types of excavations **were less than ten handbreadths** deep, **and an ox or a donkey fell into** one of **them and died,** the digger of the excavation is **exempt. But if it was injured,** not killed, he is **liable** to pay damages.

The Gemara now turns to analyze the Mishnah in light of this debate. The Mishnah states: if the *bor* was less than ten *tefachim* deep and an ox or donkey fell into it and died, the owner is exempt — *patur*. However, if the animal was merely injured, the owner is liable — *chayav*. The Gemara initially presumes that the difference between a pit that can cause death and one that cannot is simply a function of the severity of the *chavata*: the shallower the pit, the less violent the impact. If so, the concept of *hevel* would seem irrelevant, since the Gemara assumes *hevel* does not vary with depth. But if *hevel* were sufficient on its own to create liability, the owner should be liable for death even in a shallow pit — since both Rav and Shmuel agree that *hevel* generates liability for death in a sufficiently deep pit.

### Hevel Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אֵין הַבְלָא לְמִיתָה, וְיֵשׁ הַבְלָא לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **answers: There are not** sufficient **fumes to** cause **death, but there are** sufficient **fumes to** cause **damage.**

The Gemara resolves this tension by rejecting the premise that *hevel* is all-or-nothing. Rather, the Gemara explains: *ein hevla le-mita ve-yesh hevla le-nezikin* — there is not enough *hevel* in a shallow pit to cause death, but there is enough to cause injury. The difference between death and damage, then, is not solely a function of impact force; it is also a function of the intensity of the *hevel*. Even according to Rav, who insists that *hevel* is the operative mechanism, the Mishnah's distinction between death and injury can be maintained: a pit shallower than ten *tefachim* generates insufficient *hevel* to be lethal, yet sufficient *hevel* to cause damage. This is a striking position, because it means that *hevel* is not binary but admits of degrees — a conceptually unusual claim that the Gemara treats as settled.

### Student Q&A

One might reasonably ask: how does a pit ten *tefachim* deep cause the death of an ox, given that the animal's head stands well above that height? The Gemara does not resolve this difficulty directly, and the precise physiological mechanism of *hevel* remains opaque throughout the sugya. One reading is that *hevel* is not merely a mysterious lethal vapor but rather a conceptual device that connects the person who dug the pit to all damage caused within it — that is, the *hevel* establishes causal ownership over the *chavata*. However, the Gemara does not seem to pursue this reading. Instead, the Gemara treats *hevel* as a genuinely distinct factor whose lethality depends on its own intensity within the cavity, independent of impact. This makes the discussion somewhat counterintuitive, but it is the framework the Gemara operates within.

## Ox in Canal Case

### Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא תּוֹרָא דִּנְפַל לַאֲרִיתָּא דְּדַלָּאֵי, שַׁחְטֵיהּ מָרֵיהּ. טַרְפֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain ox that fell into a water channel [*la'arita dedala'ei*]** whose depth was one cubit, i.e., six handbreadths. Because it suffered the impact of the fall, **its owner** assumed it would die and **slaughtered it** first, in order to eat the meat. **Rav Naḥman,** who was concerned that its organs were crushed by the fall, **deemed it an animal with a condition that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** which it is prohibited to consume.

The Gemara introduces a narrative: an ox fell into an *arista de-dilai* — an irrigation canal known to be a standard *ama* wide, which is six *tefachim* deep. The owner of the ox, seeing that his animal had fallen in, immediately performed *shechita* rather than waiting, presumably fearing it would die.

### Treifa Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִי שְׁקֵיל מָרֵיהּ דְּהַאי תּוֹרָא קַבָּא דְקִמְחָא, וַאֲזַל תְּנָא בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא: אִם שָׁהֲתָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת – כְּשֵׁירָה; לָא אַפְסְדֵיהּ לְתוֹרָא דְּשָׁוֵה כַּמָּה קַבֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **Rav Naḥman said: If the owner of this ox had taken a *kav* of flour** to bake into bread to eat instead of slaughtering his animal for its meat, **and gone and learned in the study hall** that although an ox that falls and hits the ground is considered *tereifa* if slaughtered immediately, **if** the animal **remained** alive **for twenty-four hours** and is then slaughtered, **it is fit to eat, he would not have lost** his **ox that was worth several *kav*** of flour.

Rav Nachman declared the animal a *treifa*. The basis for this ruling is the halachic concern of *risuk evarim* — a loosening or shattering of the internal organs caused by blunt impact. Even when no external wound is visible, an animal that has suffered a significant fall must be presumed to have sustained potentially fatal internal injuries and is considered a *treifa* unless it survives for twenty-four hours or demonstrates normal ambulatory behavior. Since the owner slaughtered the animal immediately, without allowing the requisite time to pass, the animal retained the status of *treifa*.

The Gemara records Rav Nachman's additional observation: had the owner of this ox expended a modest effort — taken a measure of flour's worth of coin to the *beis midrash* and studied the relevant *halacha* — he would have learned that *im shasta me-es le-es kesheira*: if the animal survives twenty-four hours, it is kosher. By acting in haste out of ignorance, the man forfeited the full value of his ox, which was worth far more than the cost of acquiring the knowledge that would have saved it. The Gemara's point is not merely moralistic; it illustrates the concrete, practical stakes of Torah study in the domain of everyday life.

### Challenge Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: הָיוּ פְּחוּתִין מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וּמֵת – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ חַבְטָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman** from the mishna: If any of the types of excavations **were less than ten handbreadths** deep, **and an ox or a donkey fell into** one of **them and died,** he is **exempt. What is the reason** for this? **Is it not because there is no** significant **impact** capable of causing death? If so, why is this animal deemed a *tereifa*?

The narrative raises a serious challenge to the framework established thus far. The canal was only six *tefachim* deep, yet Rav Nachman treated the impact as potentially fatal — sufficient to create a concern of *risuk evarim*. This appears to conflict directly with the Mishnah, which requires a pit to be ten *tefachim* deep before its impact can be considered lethal. Thus, *alma kasavar Rav Nachman yesh chavata be-pachos me-asara* — Rav Nachman appears to hold that an impact from a fall of less than ten *tefachim* can be fatal. The Gemara proceeds to press this tension.

## Execution Height

### Execution Site

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ שְׁתֵּי קוֹמוֹת. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: וְקוֹמָה שֶׁלּוֹ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שָׁלֹשׁ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ יֵשׁ חֲבָטָה בְּפָחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה, לְמָה לִי כּוּלֵּי הַאי?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **raised an objection to** the opinion of Rav Naḥman from a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 45a): **The structure** from which **the** punishment of **stoning** was implemented **was a height of two** people, **and it is taught in that regard** in a *baraita*: When the two-person height of the structure is combined with **his height,** i.e., the height of the one being stoned, **there is** a total height equal to that of **three** people. Rava explains his objection: **And if it enters your mind** that **there is an impact** capable of causing death **in** a pit **less than ten** handbreadths deep, **why do I** need **all of this** height?

Rava challenges Rav Nachman on the basis of this apparent position. The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *beis ha-sekila* — the platform from which a condemned person was pushed during the execution of *sekila* — was required to be two stories high, *ve-koma shelo*, plus the height of the condemned person himself. This yields a fall of effectively three human heights. Rashi translates *komos* as stories, though given the phrasing *koma shelo*, the likely intent is the equivalent of three persons' heights, amounting to something on the order of ten *amos* — a considerable distance, far exceeding ten *tefachim*. Rava's challenge: if Rav Nachman holds that a fall of less than ten *tefachim* can cause death, why does the law require such an extreme height for execution? Even a modest fall could produce death.

### Height Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, הֲרֵי קֵירַב הֶזֵּיקָא!
>
> **Translation:** On the contrary, by widening the opening of the pit, **he has brought the** potential for **damage closer** to an animal that might fall inside.

The Gemara itself deflates this challenge somewhat by noting that the question applies equally against either position. Even according to those who require ten *tefachim* as the threshold for lethality, the execution platform is vastly higher than ten *tefachim*, so the question of excessive height arises regardless. The Gemara's real answer is not about the minimum required for death but about the degree of certainty and the character of the death required.

### Merciful Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״ – בְּרוֹר לוֹ מִיתָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman answered him: **[Rav Naḥman says]** that **Rabba bar Avuh says** that the **verse states: "And you shall love your fellow as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), teaching that even with regard to a condemned prisoner, **select a good,** i.e., a compassionate, **death for him.**

The Gemara cites the principle taught by Rabbah bar Abbuha: *ve-ahavta le-re'acha kamocha* — love your neighbor as yourself — includes even the condemned. From this the Rabbis derive *beror lo mita yafa*: one is obligated to choose for the condemned person the most merciful available form of death. The condemned individual remains one's neighbor and friend even in the moment of execution. Therefore, the greater the height, the more instantaneous and painless the death — and the execution method must be calibrated accordingly to maximize mercy within the constraints of the law.

### Balance Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נַגְבַּהּ טְפֵי! מִשּׁוּם דְּמִינַּוַּול.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, let us raise** the structure even **more,** so that his death will be even less painful. The Gemara answers: This is not done, **because** if he fell from a greater height, his organs would be crushed and he would become completely **disfigured,** which is certainly not a way one would prefer to die.

However, the Gemara raises a counterpoint: if greater height yields more merciful death, why not make the platform arbitrarily high? The answer is *mishum deminuvel* — an excessive fall would disfigure and desecrate the body. There is thus a precise balance required: high enough to ensure swift, certain death; not so high as to cause grotesque disfigurement. The law's specification of two stories plus the person's height reflects this calibrated equilibrium.

## Bayit Definition

### Fence Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״כִּי יִפֹּל הַנֹּפֵל מִמֶּנּוּ״; ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ – וְלֹא בְּתוֹכוֹ. כֵּיצַד? הָיְתָה רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים גָּבוֹהַּ מִמֶּנּוּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל מִתּוֹכָהּ לְתוֹכוֹ – פָּטוּר. עֲמוּקָּה מִמֶּנּוּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְנָפַל מִתּוֹכוֹ לְתוֹכָהּ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rava again **raised an objection to** the opinion of Rav Naḥman: The Torah requires constructing a parapet on the roof of one's house to prevent anyone from falling to his death, as the verse states (Deuteronomy 22:8): "You shall not bring blood upon your house, **if any man falls from it."** The term **"from it"** teaches that liability exists only for falling from the roof of the house, **but not onto** the roof of a house. **How is this so?** If an area in the **public domain was ten handbreadths higher than** a private house and the owner did not construct a fence between his house and the public domain, **and** someone **fell from inside** the public domain **onto** the house, the owner of the house is **exempt.** By contrast, if the public domain was **ten handbreadths lower than** the house, and the owner did not construct a fence on his roof, **and** someone **fell from** the roof of the house **into** the public domain, he is **liable.**

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* concerning the obligation of *ma'akeh* — the Torah's requirement to build a parapet around one's roof. The *baraita* states: if the *reshut ha-rabim* is ten *tefachim* higher than your rooftop, such that a passerby in the public domain might fall *onto* your roof rather than *off* it, you are exempt from building a fence. Conversely, if the *reshut ha-rabim* is ten *tefachim* lower than your rooftop — meaning a person on your roof could fall down into the public domain — you are obligated to build a fence. The case of the overpass is illustrative: your building is below the level of the street, and someone walking above might fall down onto your roof. In that scenario, no fence on your roof would help anyway. The operative case is where your rooftop stands above the public space below, creating a potential fatal fall.

### Exterior vs. Interior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ יֵשׁ חֲבָטָה בְּפָחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה, לְמָה לִי עֲשָׂרָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי בַּיִת, דְּכׇל פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה – לָאו בַּיִת הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** that **there is** a sufficiently strong **impact** to cause death even at a height **of less than ten** handbreadths, **why do I** need the roof to be **ten** handbreadths high for there to be a requirement to construct a parapet? Rav Naḥman **said to him:** The *halakha* of the parapet for the roof of **a house is different, since any** structure **less than ten** handbreadths **is not** classified as **a house,** and only a house requires a parapet.

This *baraita* prompts a challenge to Rav Nachman's position. If it is true that a fall of less than ten *tefachim* can be lethal, why does the *ma'akeh* obligation only attach when the fall is ten *tefachim* or more? Presumably, the Torah only mandates the fence where the fall presents a genuine risk of death — and if even a shallow fall can be fatal, the threshold should be lower. Rav Nachman responds: the ten-*tefach* requirement here has nothing to do with the minimum height needed for a fatal fall. Rather, it is a definitional criterion: *kol pachot me-asara lav bayit hu* — anything whose interior height is less than ten *tefachim* does not attain the halachic status of a *bayit*. The *ma'akeh* obligation applies only to a *bayit*, and a structure lacking the requisite interior dimension simply does not qualify — even if its roof could theoretically produce a dangerous fall.

### Interior Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי דְּהָוֵי מֵאַבָּרַאי עֲשָׂרָה, דַּל מִינֵּיהּ תִּקְרָה וּמַעֲזִיבָה – מִגַּוַּאי לָא הָוֵי עֲשָׂרָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן דְּחַק מִגַּוַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava objects: **If that is so,** i.e., if the requirement to build a parapet is limited to a house that is ten handbreadths high, then **also now, where** the house **is ten** handbreadths higher than the public domain when measured **from the outside, remove** the height of **the ceiling and the plaster,** which is an additional layer on top of the ceiling. When measured **from the inside** in this manner, its height **is not ten.** According to you, then, it is not classified as a house. Rav Naḥman **said to him:** It is a case **where he hollowed out** an extra space **inside** in the floor of the house so that its height would be ten handbreadths.

The Gemara immediately pushes back: if the ten-*tefach* requirement is really about defining a *bayit* as a structure with sufficient interior living space, then the measurement must refer to the *internal* height of the cavity. Yet the *baraita* speaks of a ten-*tefach* fall, which is measured from the *exterior* — from the level of the rooftop to the ground below. Subtracting the thickness of the ceiling and its plaster (*tikra ve-aziva*), the interior height would necessarily be less than ten *tefachim*. So how can an exterior fall of ten *tefachim* satisfy an interior-based definition of *bayit*?

The Gemara resolves this by positing a specific architectural scenario: *kegon de-dachak migavai* — the house has been dug into the ground on the inside, so that the interior floor is below the exterior ground level. In such a structure, the interior height can be ten *tefachim* even while the exterior roofline stands at ten *tefachim* above the surrounding terrain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי לָא הָוֵי נָמֵי מֵאַבָּרַאי עֲשָׂרָה – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דְּהָוֵי מִגַּוַּאי עֲשָׂרָה כְּגוֹן דְּחַק בַּהּ טְפֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rava challenges him: **If so,** in a situation **where** the house **is not ten** handbreadths **from the outside as well, you** can **find** a scenario **where it is ten** handbreadths **from the inside, such as where he hollowed out extra** space **in** the floor, thereby transforming it into a house with the required height. Why, then, would a person be exempt in this case?

The Gemara then points out that this resolution cuts too far: if we are imagining a house where the interior is dug down, the exterior height of the rooftop could be *less* than ten *tefachim* while the interior still measures ten *tefachim* — *de-havei migavai asara kegon de-chachak bitfei*. This means that the *ma'akeh* obligation would apply even to a structure whose rooftop stands fewer than ten *tefachim* above the public domain. And if so, we are back to the conclusion that the real operative factor is not the *bayit* definition at all, but the height of the fall itself — and less than ten *tefachim* on the outside is not enough to be lethal. The *pshat* of the Mishnah is thus reinstated: ten *tefachim* is genuinely required for a lethal impact.

## Impact Calculation

### Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, סָבַר: מִכְּרֵיסָא דְתוֹרָא לְאַרְעָא כַּמָּה הָוֵי – אַרְבְּעָה; אֲרִיתָּא דְּדַלָּאֵי כַּמָּה הָוֵי – שִׁיתָּא; הָא עַשְׂרָה. אִישְׁתְּכַח דְּכִי קָא מִחֲבַט – מֵעֲשָׂרָה הוּא דְּקָא מִחֲבַט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the previous explanation must be entirely rejected, and **this is the reasoning of Rav Naḥman** when he ruled that one must suspect that the ox's organs were crushed upon falling into the water channel. He **maintains** the following argument: **How** far **is** the distance **from the stomach of the ox to the ground?** It is **four** handbreadths. **How** deep **is the water channel?** It is **six** handbreadths. **This** totals **ten** handbreadths. Therefore, it **transpires that when** the ox **hits** the ground, **it is from** a height of **ten** handbreadths **that it hits** the ground, since it landed on its stomach and not on its feet.

The Gemara now provides Rav Nachman's actual reasoning for the *treifa* ruling on the ox that fell into the six-*tefach* irrigation canal. It is not that Rav Nachman holds that a fall of less than ten *tefachim* is independently lethal. Rather, Rav Nachman's calculation is as follows: *michreisa de-tora le-ar'a kama havei* — from the underside of an ox's belly to the ground, what is the distance? The Gemara answers: four *tefachim*. And the depth of the irrigation canal was six *tefachim*.

### Total Impact

The total impact experienced by the falling ox is therefore four plus six — ten *tefachim*. The animal begins with its underside four *tefachim* above the ground; when it topples into a six-*tefach* canal, the effective fall traverses ten *tefachim* from its natural position to the floor of the canal. *Ishtakach de-chi ka-machbat me-asara ka-machbat* — it turns out that when it sustains the impact, it sustains an impact of ten *tefachim*. Rav Nachman's ruling is therefore entirely consistent with the Mishnah's threshold. He did not deviate from the principle that ten *tefachim* is required for a potentially fatal impact.

### Mishnah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: מָה בּוֹר שֶׁהוּא כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים; בְּשִׁיתָּא נָמֵי סַגְיָא! אָמְרִי: מַתְנִיתִין – דְּאִיגַּנְדַּר לְבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **the mishna, which teaches: Just as a pit that has sufficient** depth **to cause death** when falling into it is at least **ten handbreadths** deep, **so too, any** other excavations **that have sufficient** depth **to cause death** may be no less than **ten handbreadths** deep, why not say that a pit **with six** handbreadths would **also be sufficient?** The Sages **said** in reply: **The mishna** is referring to a case **where** the animal **rolled into the pit,** in which case the pit would be required to have a depth of ten handbreadths.

The Gemara draws a further distinction to close the apparent tension: the Mishnah's case is one of *igandar le-bor* — an animal that *rolled* into the pit from ground level. In that scenario, the animal was already lying on the ground before it rolled in, so the effective fall is simply the depth of the pit itself, and ten *tefachim* of pit depth is required. But in the case of an animal falling from a standing position, the height of the animal's underside above the ground must be added to the depth of the pit. In that situation, a pit of only six *tefachim* suffices to generate a ten-*tefach* impact. While this conclusion may seem to depart from a simple reading of the Mishnah, the Gemara treats it as the correct technical understanding.

## Two Partners Well

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בּוֹר שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין; עָבַר עָלָיו הָרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ – הַשֵּׁנִי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a pit belonging to two partners** was uncovered and **the first** partner **passed by it and did not cover it, and** then **the second** passed by it **and did not cover it, the second** is **liable** for any damage caused by means of the pit.

The Gemara now turns to a new Mishnah: *bor shel shnei shutafin* — a pit owned jointly by two partners. The Mishnah describes a case in which the first partner passed by without covering the pit, and the second partner also passed by without covering it. The Mishnah's ruling — at least according to the textual version familiar to Rashi — is that the second person is *chayav*. (The *girsa* of the Rif and Rambam has it the other way, holding the first partner liable — a point that will require examination, as it goes to the core ruling of the Mishnah.)

### Joint Ownership Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמְרִי: בּוֹר שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages **say: How can you find** such a case of **a pit belonging to two partners?**

Before reaching the substantive ruling, the Gemara asks a threshold question: *bor shel shnei shutafin heichi mishkachat lah* — how does one even arrive at a scenario of joint ownership of a pit for purposes of liability? This question is nontrivial because liability for a *bor* flows in different ways depending on one's theoretical framework.

### Liability Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא אִי סְבִירָא לַן כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ – חַיָּיב; מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּחָצֵר שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם וּבוֹר שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם, וְהִפְקִירוּ רְשׁוּתָן וְלֹא הִפְקִירוּ בּוֹרָן. אֶלָּא אִי סְבִירָא לַן בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ פָּטוּר; הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דְּחַיָּיב עֲלֵיהּ – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּבִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בּוֹר שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This works out well if we hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who says that one who digs a pit on his own property and then declares the property, but not the pit, ownerless, is liable for damage. According to him, **you find** such a case **where the courtyard belongs to both of them and the pit belongs to both of them, and they renounced ownership of their property but did not renounce ownership of their pit.** In that case, both are responsible for any damage caused. But if we hold that one who digs a pit on his property is exempt, then the only way to arrive at a scenario where someone is liable for a pit is in the public domain, and how can you find a case of a pit belonging to two partners in the public domain?

The Gemara notes that according to the view of Rabbi Akiva — that liability for a *bor* derives from ownership, not only from the act of digging — the case is easily constructed. Two partners could own a field jointly, with a pit situated within it; they then relinquish ownership of the field but retain joint title to the pit. In that scenario, both remain liable as owners. However, according to the competing view that ownership alone does not generate liability, and that liability flows only from the act of digging in the *reshut ha-rabim*, the question becomes more difficult. How can two people be jointly and equally responsible as diggers?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּשַׁוּוֹ שָׁלִיחַ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ, וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: ״זִיל כְּרִי לַן״, וַאֲזַל כְּרָה לְהוּ – אֵין שָׁלִיחַ לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה! וְאִי דִּכְרָה הַאי חַמְשָׁה וְהַאי חַמְשָׁה – נִסְתַּלְּקוּ לְהוּ מַעֲשֵׂה רִאשׁוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** **If they both** jointly **appointed an agent and said to him: Go dig** a pit **for us, and he went** and **dug** a pit **for** both of **them,** then neither is liable. This is because **there is no agency for transgression,** and the digger is solely responsible. **And if this** partner **dug** a pit **five** handbreadths deep **and this** partner dug **five** further handbreadths deep, **the** significance of the initial **act of** digging performed by **the first** partner **has been removed.**

One might propose that they both appointed an agent to dig on their behalf — *shavvu shliach tarvaihu*. The agent digs, and both principals are treated as having dug. The Gemara rejects this: *ein shliach le-dvar aveira* — one cannot appoint an agent for a transgression. The transgression attaches to the one who actually performs it, not to the one who commissioned it.

### Agency Principle

The Gemara's invocation of *ein shliach le-dvar aveira* here is notable. It raises the underlying question — left largely implicit — of whether digging a *bor* in the *reshut ha-rabim* constitutes an *aveira* in the first instance. A number of *Rishonim* identify the operative prohibition as *lifnei iver lo titein michshol*, understood in its literal sense of placing a stumbling block before others. However, regardless of the precise prohibition, the Gemara's point is applicable both in the domain of religious transgression and civil liability: the agent who chooses to dig the well is the responsible party. One cannot transfer civil liability through an agency instruction any more than one can transfer criminal culpability through an emissary. The company that orders a worker to dig is not, under this framework, the liable party — the worker who performs the act bears the responsibility.

The alternative scenario for joint liability is that both partners each dig a portion — Reuven digs five *tefachim*, Shimon digs five *tefachim*, together reaching ten. However, as the Gemara will explain, this generates complications in light of the principle of *nistalku maaseh rishon* — that when the second person transforms the pit into a full, lethal *bor*, his act effectively supersedes and displaces the first person's act. This principle, and its precise scope, is what the next section addresses.

## Rabbi vs. Rabbanan

### Baraita Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי וּמַאי רַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר תִּשְׁעָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהִשְׁלִימָהּ לַעֲשָׂרָה – הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַחַר אַחֲרוֹן לְמִיתָה, וְאַחַר שְׁנֵיהֶם לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis, the Gemara inquires: **What** is the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and what** is the opinion of **the Rabbis? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who digs a pit nine** handbreadths deep, **and another** person **came and completed** the digging **to** a depth of **ten** handbreadths, **the latter** individual alone is **liable** for an injury or death caused by the pit. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** One **follows the latter** for restitution **for death** caused by the pit. The latter is solely liable, since only a pit of ten handbreadths renders a person liable for an animal's death. **And** one **follows both of them** for restitution **for damage** caused by the pit.

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: one person digs a pit nine *tefachim* deep, and another comes and completes it to ten. *Ha-acharon chayav* — the last person is liable. This sets out the basic framework: the person who transforms a non-lethal pit into a lethal one bears primary responsibility for any death that occurs within it.

Rabbi qualifies this ruling. *Achar acharon le-mita* — the last person alone is liable for death. But *achar shneihem le-nezikin* — both are equally liable for damages. Rabbi's reasoning is that even the initial nine-*tefach* pit was a *bor* capable of causing injury, so the first digger retains liability for that dimension of harm. Only for death — which requires the full ten-*tefach* threshold — does the last person's transformative act take exclusive ownership.

### Rabbanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִפְתַּח״ וְ״כִּי יִכְרֶה״ – אִם עַל פְּתִיחָה חַיָּיב, עַל כְּרִיָּיה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֶלָּא לְהָבִיא כּוֹרֶה אַחַר כּוֹרֶה, שֶׁסִּילֵּק מַעֲשֵׂה רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis? As the verse states: "If** a man **shall open** a pit, **or if** a man **shall dig** a pit" (Exodus 21:33), it raises the question: **If** he is **liable for opening** a covered pit that has already been dug, then is it **not all the more so** that he should be liable **for digging** a new pit? What, then, does that latter phrase add? **Rather,** the verse serves **to include** the case of one person who begins to **dig** a pit and **after** he does so, a second person continues to **dig.** By subsequently stating: "If a man shall dig a pit," the Torah teaches **that** a new pit is effectively created by the second digger, whose digging **removed,** i.e., rendered irrelevant, **the action of the first** digger and who bears full responsibility for the pit.

The Rabbis disagree. In their view, once the second person completes the pit to ten *tefachim*, he becomes solely liable for both death *and* damages. The act of making the pit lethal is so qualitatively significant that it wholly displaces the first person's contribution. The first digger's nine *tefachim* are, in effect, subsumed into the second person's *bor*.

The Rabbis derive their position from the verse's phrasing: *ki yiftach ish bor, ki yichre ish bor* — whether a person *opens* or *digs* a pit. The inclusion of both terms might seem redundant — surely if one is liable for digging, all the more so for merely opening. The Rabbis read the apparent redundancy as a substantive teaching: *le-havi kore achar kore* — to include a digger who digs after a previous digger has already dug. This teaches that the subsequent digger can bear full and exclusive liability, *she-silak maaseh rishon* — because his act displaces the first act entirely. Additionally, the phrase *ki yichre ish bor* — singular *ish*, one man — teaches that there are situations where two people dig yet only one bears liability: specifically, the one who transforms the pit into something lethal.

### Redundancy Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אָמַר לָךְ: הָנְהוּ מִיצְרָךְ צְרִיכִי, כִּדְאָמְרִינַן. וְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי מִיצְרָךְ צְרִיכִי! אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן – אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִכְרֶה אִישׁ בּוֹר״ – אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם. וְרַבִּי – הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: ״כִּי יִכְרֶה אִישׁ בּוֹר״ – וְלֹא שׁוֹר בּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi could have **said to you: These** two clauses in the verse **are** both **necessary,** and the second clause cannot serve to teach the *halakha* of the sole liability of the second digger. The reason they are necessary is **as we said** earlier (50a), where interpretations were cited according to both Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. The Gemara asks: **And** don't **the Rabbis also** agree that they **are necessary** for those interpretations? If so, what is the source of their opinion that responsibility for the pit rests solely with the second partner? **Rather,** the previous explanation must be rejected, and **this is the reason of the Rabbis:** The **verse states: "If a man shall dig a pit,"** indicating that **one** person is liable, **but not two.** Therefore, if two people jointly created the pit, the second bears responsibility and not the first. The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi understand the phrase "if a man shall dig a pit"? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** phrase to teach another *halakha*. The phrase **"if a man shall dig a pit"** indicates that a person who digs a pit is liable, **but** one is **not** liable if his **ox** digs a **pit.**

Rabbi disputes this reading. He maintains that both phrases in the verse — *ki yiftach* and *ki yichre* — are genuinely necessary and not redundant, each addressing a distinct legal scenario: the first addresses liability for one who merely opens an existing pit without digging, and the second addresses one who digs. Since neither phrase is extraneous, no additional inference about *kore achar kore* can be drawn from them. Moreover, Rabbi reads *ish bor* differently: it serves to exclude a case where an ox, rather than a person, digs the pit — *ish bor ve-lo shor bor* — teaching that if an ox excavates a cavity, its owner is not liable for resulting injuries as a *bor* tortfeasor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן – תְּרֵי ״אִישׁ בּוֹר״ כְּתִיבִי. וְרַבִּי – אַיְּידֵי דִּכְתַב הַאי, כְּתַב הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where do **the Rabbis** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers that the combination of words: **Man** and **pit,** is **written twice:** "If a man shall open a pit, or if a man shall dig a pit." From one phrase, the *halakha* is derived that a person must do the digging, and not an animal. From the second phrase, the *halakha* is derived that one person bears responsibility for the damage, and not two people. The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi derive from this repetition? The Gemara answers: In his opinion, **since** the Torah **wrote this** first clause using this wording, it similarly **wrote this** second clause in the same fashion. The repetition is for stylistic reasons, and is not meant to teach a new *halakha*.

The Rabbis counter that the word *ish* appears *twice* in the relevant verses — once in *ki yiftach ish bor* and once in *ki yichre ish bor*. The first occurrence teaches the exclusion of an ox as digger; the second teaches that one person can be fully liable even when two have dug. Rabbi responds that the repetition of *ish* is not informative — *aidi de-katav ha katav ha*, once the Torah wrote it in one clause, it simply wrote it again in the parallel clause, without intending an additional teaching.

### Identified Digger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דִּלְחַיּוֹבֵי בָּתְרָא? דִּלְמָא לְחַיּוֹבֵי קַמָּא! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – הַהוּא דְּקָא עָבֵיד מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask about the opinion of the Rabbis: **And** even if they derive from the verse that only one person is liable for damage caused through the pit, **from where** do they know **that** the Torah intends **to render** specifically **the latter one liable** for the damage? **Perhaps** it is **to render the first one** who started the digging **liable** for the damage. The Gemara answers: It should **not enter your mind** to suggest this, **since the verse states: "And the carcass shall be for him"** (Exodus 21:34). This indicates that it is **the one who causes the death,** i.e., the second one, who is liable, and not the first. This is because it is the second one who completed digging the necessary depth for causing death, and not the first.

The Gemara then asks: even granting the Rabbis' position that the last digger bears sole liability, what identifies the *last* person as the liable party rather than the first? The verse *ve-ha-mes yihyeh lo* — the carcass shall be his — identifies the tortfeasor as the one whose act rendered the pit lethal: *hahu de-ka-avid mita*. The person who gave the pit its death-causing capacity is the one who owns it, and the carcass belongs to him as compensation for the liability he bears.

### Dual Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרָבָא – דְּאָמַר רָבָא: שׁוֹר פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין שֶׁנָּפַל לְבוֹר – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – בְּמִי שֶׁהַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ! אָמְרִי: וְלָאו מִמֵּילָא שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ, דִּבְהָהוּא דַּעֲבַד מִיתָה עָסְקִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't **this** clause: **"And the carcass shall be for him," necessary for that which Rava** states? **As Rava says:** With regard to **a disqualified consecrated ox that fell into a pit,** the pit's owner is **exempt, as it is stated: "And the carcass shall be for him."** Evidently, the verse is stated **with regard to** a case **where the carcass belongs** to him. This ox, which was consecrated and subsequently disqualified, does not fully belong to anybody, since its use is restricted. Since this verse serves to teach Rava's ruling, it cannot serve to teach about the liability of the second digger. The Sages **said** in response: **And can't you learn from** the words and emphasis of the verse **by itself that we are dealing with the one who caused** the animal's **death,** even if these words are also used to derive Rava's ruling? Therefore, the verse can serve as a source for both rulings.

The Gemara notes, however, that the same verse *ve-ha-mes yihyeh lo* is also needed for a separate teaching of Rava: that if the animal that fell into the pit was a *shor psulei ha-mukdashim* — an ox that had been consecrated as a *korban*, subsequently became blemished, and was redeemed — the owner of the pit is exempt from liability. The reason is that the carcass of such an animal cannot be used by its owner, since it was once sanctified property. The verse *ve-ha-mes yihyeh lo* — the carcass shall be *his* — implies liability only where the carcass is the property of the owner and has usable value. Where the carcass cannot benefit the owner, there is no compensable loss and therefore no liability. The Gemara resolves the double use of the verse by noting that both teachings can be derived from it: it establishes the principle that liability attaches to the one who made the pit lethal, and simultaneously teaches the exemption in the case of *psulei ha-mukdashim*.

## Plastering Case

### Baraita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר עֲשָׂרָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהִשְׁלִימָהּ לְעֶשְׂרִים, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהִשְׁלִימָהּ לִשְׁלשִׁים – כּוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין. וּרְמִינְהוּ: אֶחָד הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר עֲשָׂרָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְסִיֵּיד וְכִיֵּיד – הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar subject, **the Sages taught:** In a case of **one who digs a pit** to a depth of **ten** handbreadths, **and another comes and completes** the depth **to twenty** handbreadths by digging an additional ten handbreadths, **and another comes and completes it to thirty,** they are **all liable** for any damage caused by this pit. The Gemara comments: **And** one can **raise a contradiction** against this from a *baraita*: In a case of **one who digs a pit** to a depth of **ten** handbreadths, **and another came and plastered** it **and cemented [*vekhiyyeid*]** it, **the latter** is **liable** for damage, and not the first.

The Gemara cites a second *baraita*: one person digs a pit to ten *tefachim*, and another comes and plasters it (*siyado*) and engraves or carves it (*kiyado*). The *baraita* rules: *he-acharon chayav* — the last person is liable. This is striking. The first person already dug the pit to the full lethal depth of ten *tefachim*. Under the framework we have developed, once the first person creates a *bor* capable of causing death, his act should not be displaced. Yet the *baraita* gives sole liability to the one who merely plastered and carved. Why?

### Hevel Increase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: הָא וְהָא רַבָּנַן; עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן אַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב, אֶלָּא הֵיכָא דְּלָא עֲבַד קַמָּא שִׁיעוּר מִיתָה; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דַּעֲבַד קַמָּא שִׁיעוּר מִיתָה – אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ דְּכוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The way the first *baraita* accords with their opinion is that **the Rabbis say** that **the last one** is **liable only where the first** one **did not make the** pit of sufficient **measurement** to cause **death,** but dug less than ten handbreadths. **But where the first one made** a pit of sufficient **measurement** to cause **death,** such as in this case, where the first one dug ten handbreadths himself and it was subsequently deepened further by others, **even the Rabbis concede that they are all liable.**

The Gemara considers whether this *baraita* reflects the position of Rabbi alone (who distributes liability) or of the Rabbis alone (who concentrate it in the last person), or whether both opinions are represented across the two *baraitot*. The initial answer offered is that of Rav Zvid: *ha va-ha Rabbanan* — both *baraitot* reflect the position of the Rabbis, and there is no contradiction. The Rabbis say the last person is fully liable only when the first person had not yet created *shiur mita* — a pit meeting the threshold for lethality. When the first person *had* already created *shiur mita*, the Rabbis agree with Rabbi that all subsequent diggers remain jointly liable alongside the first.

### Width Hypothesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא סִיֵּיד וְכִיֵּיד, דְּקָא עֲבַד קַמָּא שִׁיעוּר מִיתָה, וְקָתָנֵי: אַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב! אָמְרִי: הָתָם – שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בּוֹ הֶבֶל לְמִיתָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהוֹסִיף בָּהּ הֶבֶל לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in the second *baraita*, where **he plastered and cemented** it, **the first made** a pit of sufficient **measurement** to cause **death, and** nevertheless, the *baraita* **teaches** that **the last person** is **liable. They said** in response: **There** it is referring to a case **where** the pit **did not have fumes** capable of leading **to death,** since it was very wide. **And** then **the other came, and** by narrowing it with plaster and cementing it, **he added to it** the characteristic of having **fumes** capable of leading **to death.**

The resolution of the *baraita* about plastering, then, must be that the first digger's ten-*tefach* pit did not in fact reach *shiur mita* — it lacked sufficient *hevel* to be lethal, even though it met the depth requirement. Rashi explains: *she-mitechila rechavo lo haya bo hevel kol kach ve-zeh siyado ve-kiyado* — at the outset, the width of the pit was such that it did not generate sufficient *hevel*; the second person, by plastering and narrowing the interior walls, increased the *hevel* to the level required for lethality.

### Alternative: Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי; הָךְ דְּקָתָנֵי: כּוּלָּן חַיָּיבִין – שַׁפִּיר; הָא דְּקָתָנֵי: אַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב – כְּגוֹן שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בּוֹ הֶבֶל לֹא לְמִיתָה וְלֹא לִנְזָקִין, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהוֹסִיף בּוֹ הֶבֶל בֵּין לְמִיתָה בֵּין לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that this is what **Rav Zevid said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **This** first *baraita*, **which teaches: They are all liable,** fits **well** with his opinion. **That** second *baraita*, **which teaches: The last one** alone is **liable,** is referring to a case **where** the pit **did not have** sufficient **fumes in it** that were capable of leading to **either death or damage, and** then **another** person **came and** by narrowing it with plaster and cementing it, **he added to it** the characteristic of having **fumes** capable of leading **to both death and damage.**

An alternative version (*ika de-amri*) has Rav Zvid saying instead: *ha va-ha Rabbi* — both *baraitot* reflect the position of Rabbi. In that case, the *baraita* where all are liable (the one who digs ten, the one who deepens to twenty, and so on) is straightforwardly Rabbi, who distributes liability broadly. The *baraita* where only the last person is liable — the plastering case — is also Rabbi, but in a scenario where the first digger's pit had no effective *hevel* at all, neither for death nor for injury. In that case even Rabbi agrees that the first person did nothing of legal significance, and the entire liability rests with the one who transformed it into a functional *bor*.

## Reversing Acts

### Filling Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִנִּיחַ אֶבֶן עַל פִּי הַבּוֹר, וְהִשְׁלִימָהּ לַעֲשָׂרָה – בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי וְרַבָּנַן. לְמַטָּה הוּא דְּהַבְלָא דִידֵיהּ קָא קָטֵיל לֵיהּ, אֲבָל לְמַעְלָה – דְּלָא הַבְלָא דִידֵיהּ קָא קָטֵיל, אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Elaborating on this subject, **Rava says:** With regard to one who **placed a stone at the opening of a pit** less than ten handbreadths deep **and brings** the pit **to** a depth of **ten** handbreadths by raising the ground next to the opening of the pit, **we have arrived at the dispute** between **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and the Rabbis** concerning whether only the second or both are responsible for damage caused. **Lest you say** that there is a distinction between a person deepening a pit **at the bottom,** in which case it **is his** lethal **fumes** that he caused **that kill** the animal, **but** if he added to the pit **at the top, where it is not his** lethal **fumes** that **kill** the animal, **say** that he would **not** be liable. Rava's statement **teaches us** that the last one is liable, and this case is subject to dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis as to whether only the last one or both are liable.

Rava poses a new question that emerges directly from the framework of the preceding discussion. Suppose the second person — who had dug the tenth *tefach* and thereby assumed ownership of the entire *bor* — subsequently filled in that tenth *tefach*, restoring the pit to nine *tefachim*. Alternatively, suppose the second person had placed a stone at the edge of the pit (to raise the effective fall height to ten *tefachim*) and then removed that stone. In both cases, the second person has reversed his own act. The pit now looks exactly as it did after the first person finished — a nine-*tefach* excavation. Whose *bor* is it?

### Reversal Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: טָם טֶפַח, וְסִילֵּק אֲבָנָיו, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: מַאי דַּעֲבַד – שַׁקְלֵיהּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, נִסְתַּלְּקוּ מַעֲשֵׂה רִאשׁוֹן, וְקָמָה לַיהּ כּוּלַּיהּ בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises** the following **dilemma:** If someone dug another handbreadth in a pit that was nine handbreadths deep, so that it reached a depth of ten handbreadths, and subsequently **closed up** that **handbreadth** inside the pit, **or** if **he removed his stones** that he had placed at the opening of the pit so that the depth of the pit was reduced to less than ten handbreadths, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say that what he did** to increase the depth of the pit to ten handbreadths, **he has** removed, and restored the initial situation that existed before he dug the extra handbreadth, causing responsibility for damage to revert solely to the original digger? **Or perhaps,** by closing it up, he **removed the action of** the **first** digger, since he has removed the ability of the pit to cause death? If so, it **moves entirely into his possession** and he now assumes full responsibility for the pit of nine handbreadths. The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

## Water Lethality

### Water Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר מָרְתָּא: בּוֹר שְׁמוֹנָה, וּמֵהֶן שְׁנֵי טְפָחִים מַיִם – חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל טֶפַח דְּמַיָּא – כִּתְרֵי דְיַבָּשָׁה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Shmuel bar Marta says:** With regard to **a pit** that is **eight** handbreadths deep and **two handbreadths of it** contain **water,** one is **liable** for it. **What is the reason? Each handbreadth of water is like two** handbreadths **of dry space.** Therefore, the pit is effectively ten handbreadths in depth and contains a sufficient quantity of lethal fumes to render its owner liable.

The Gemara introduces a teaching in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chanah citing Shmuel bar Marta: a *bor* that is eight *tefachim* deep, of which the bottom two *tefachim* are filled with water, renders the owner *chayav* for death — as though it were a full ten-*tefach* dry pit.

### Water Power

The underlying principle is: *kol tefach de-maya ke-trei de-yavsha dami* — each *tefach* of water is as injurious as two *tefachim* of empty space. Thus, an eight-*tefach* well with two *tefachim* of water at the bottom effectively functions as a ten-*tefach* pit: six *tefachim* of air cavity plus two *tefachim* of water, which count as four, yields the equivalent of ten. Rashi explains this through the lens of *hevel*, noting that water contributes additional *hevel* — whether understood as vapor, toxicity, or some quality of the air — beyond what a simple air-filled cavity of the same depth would produce. Whether one prefers to understand the water's additional danger in terms of drowning, asphyxiation, or the concentrated *hevel* of a damp enclosure, the halachic equivalence is two-to-one: water is twice as dangerous as an equivalent depth of dry air.

### Nine Plus One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בּוֹר תִּשְׁעָה וּמֵהֶן טֶפַח אֶחָד מַיִם, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּלָא נְפִישִׁי מַיָּא – לֵית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּעַמִּיק טְפֵי – אִית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **a pit** is **nine** handbreadths deep, **and from that** measurement, **one handbreadth** contains **water, what is** the *halakha*? Is this case equivalent to the previous one? **Do we say** that **since** there is **not** as **large** a quantity of **water** as in the case stated by Rabba bar bar Ḥana, the pit **does not have** a sufficient quantity of lethal **fumes** to render the digger liable? **Or perhaps** it can be argued that **since** the pit is **deeper** than the one in the previous case, **it does have** lethal **fumes,** despite containing less water?

The Gemara raises a series of unresolved questions about the limits of this principle. The first: what is the law concerning a pit that is nine *tefachim* deep with one *tefach* of water at the bottom? One *tefach* of water counting as two would bring the total to ten — making the owner liable. But perhaps there is a minimum threshold of water required before the two-to-one ratio applies: with only one *tefach* of water, there may be insufficient water to generate the enhanced *hevel*, and the pit should be treated as simply a nine-*tefach* dry pit, below the threshold for death. Alternatively, even one *tefach* of water counts as two, in which case the pit reaches the equivalent of ten. The further consideration is that a nine-*tefach* pit is deeper than an eight-*tefach* pit, and the greater depth might itself contribute to *hevel* in a way that supplements the water's contribution — *keivan de-amik tefei it beih havla*. The question is left unresolved.

### Seven Plus Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹר שִׁבְעָה, וּמֵהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים מַיִם, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דִּנְפִישִׁי מַיִם טְפֵי – אִית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא עַמִּקָא – לֵית בֵּיהּ הַבְלָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the following question can be raised: **What is** the *halakha* if **a pit** is **seven** handbreadths deep **and three of the handbreadths** contain **water? Do we say** that **since** there is a **much larger** quantity **of water** in this case, it is equivalent to a pit of ten handbreadths and therefore **has** lethal **fumes, or perhaps since it is not** as **deep, it does not have** a sufficient quantity of lethal **fumes** to render the digger liable? The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

The second unresolved question: what of a pit seven *tefachim* deep, of which three *tefachim* are water? The arithmetic is: four *tefachim* of air plus three *tefachim* of water (counting as six) yields the equivalent of ten. On this accounting, the owner should be *chayav*. But one might argue the opposite: because the air cavity begins only four *tefachim* down, the pit does not achieve sufficient depth to generate meaningful *hevel* regardless of the water — *keivan de-lo amik kuli hai leit beih havla*. Moreover, the ratio of water to air is now higher — three to four — but the overall structure is shallower. Whether the water's enhanced lethality can compensate for the reduced depth, or whether the reduced depth undermines the *hevel* framework entirely, remains unresolved.

## Well Expansion

### Expansion Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב שֵׁיזְבִי מֵרַבָּה: הִרְחִיבָהּ מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי מִיעֵט הַבְלָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה, הֲרֵי קֵירַב הֶזֵּיקָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi asked** the following question **of Rabba:** If one **widened** the opening of a pit that was already ten handbreadths deep, **what is** the *halakha*? Rabba **said to him:** By doing so, **he has** actually **reduced the** lethal **fumes** and has not worsened the situation. Therefore, he should not be liable. Rav Sheizevi **said to him: On the contrary,** by widening the opening of the pit, **he has brought the** potential for **damage closer** to an animal that might fall inside.

Rav Shizbi poses a question to Rava: *hirchiva mahu* — what is the law if the second person widened the pit? The first person dug a *bor*, and the second person came and expanded it — opening it wider without changing its depth. From one perspective, widening the pit appears to be a public service: it reduces the *hevel* within the cavity, since *hevel* is more concentrated in a narrower space. From another perspective, widening the pit increases the surface area exposed to the *reshut ha-rabim*, making it easier for people or animals to fall in — thereby increasing the hazard.

### Rav Ashi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן; אִי בְּהַבְלָא מִיית – הֲרֵי מִיעֵט הַבְלָא, אִי בְּחַבְטָה מִיית – הֲרֵי קֵירַב הֶזֵּיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said** concerning this question: **Let us see: If** the animal **died because of the** lethal **fumes,** the one who widened the pit should be exempt, since **he reduced the** lethal **fumes.** But **if** the animal **died because of the impact,** he should be liable, since **he has brought the** potential for **damage closer** to it.

Rava's initial response — that the widener reduced the *hevel* and should receive credit — is immediately challenged by the rejoinder that widening brings the danger closer (*kireiv heizeika*). Rav Ashi cuts through this impasse with a practical resolution: *nachzi anan* — let us look at the actual circumstances. The question is not purely theoretical; its answer depends on what actually occurs.

If the animal or person died as a result of *hevel* — some form of asphyxiation or toxic air — then the one who widened the pit is exempt, because his act reduced the *hevel* and thus diminished rather than increased that particular hazard. If the victim died by impact — *chavata* — then the one who widened the pit is liable, because his act increased exposure to the risk of falling.

### Location-Based

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן; אִי מֵהַהִיא גִּיסָא נְפַל – הֲרֵי קֵירַב הֶזֵּיקָא, וְאִי מֵאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא נְפַל – הֲרֵי מִיעֵט הַבְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rav Ashi said: Let us see: If** the animal **fell on that side** that he widened, then his action **has brought the** potential for **damage closer,** and so he should be liable. But **if** the animal **fell on the other side,** he should bear no responsibility, since **he reduced the** lethal **fumes.**

A second formulation of Rav Ashi's *nachzi anan* resolves the question by the physical location of the fall rather than the cause of death. If the victim fell into the portion of the pit that was expanded by the second person — the newly opened section — then the second person is liable, because the victim fell into territory he created. If the victim fell into the original portion of the pit dug by the first person, then the second person's widening actually reduced the *hevel* in that area, and he is exempt.

## Pit Proportions

### First Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: בּוֹר שֶׁעוֹמְקָהּ כְּרׇחְבָּהּ – רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, דְּאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מָנִי – חַד אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם יֵשׁ בָּהּ הֶבֶל, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא רׇחְבָּהּ יָתֵר עַל עוֹמְקָהּ;
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** With regard to **a pit whose depth is equal to its width, Rabba and Rav Yosef both say** a ruling **in the name of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, who says** it **in the name of Rabbi Mani,** but each present it differently. **One** of them **says:** A pit **always contains** a quantity of lethal **fumes** sufficient for liability, **unless its width is greater than its depth.**

The Gemara records a dispute transmitted in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chanah, citing Rabbi Mani, concerning the relationship between a pit's depth and its width and how this affects the presence of *hevel*. One opinion holds: *l'olam yesh bo hevel ad she-rachvo yater al omko* — there is always *hevel* in a pit as long as its width does not exceed its depth. Under this view, a pit whose width equals its depth still qualifies as having *hevel*; only when the width exceeds the depth does the *hevel* dissipate.

### Second Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אֵין בָּהּ הֶבֶל, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עוֹמְקָהּ יָתֵר עַל רׇחְבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And one says:** A pit **never contains** a quantity of lethal **fumes** sufficient for liability, **unless its depth is greater than its width.**

The opposing opinion holds: *l'olam ein bo hevel ad she-omko yater al rachvo* — there is no *hevel* in a pit until its depth exceeds its width. Under this view, a pit of equal depth and width lacks sufficient *hevel*; only when the depth strictly exceeds the width does the confined air generate *hevel* capable of creating liability. The case of exact equality is thus the point of contention between these two opinions — a structural question somewhat analogous to the Talmudic class of *i efshar le-tzamtzem* disputes, where a boundary condition admits of no easy resolution.

## Shared Well Mishnah

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בּוֹר שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין; עָבַר עָלָיו הָרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ – הַשֵּׁנִי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a pit belonging to two partners** was uncovered and **the first** partner **passed by it and did not cover it, and** then **the second** passed by it **and did not cover it, the second** is **liable** for any damage caused by means of the pit.

The Gemara now turns to the Mishnah's core ruling: the first person passed by the uncovered pit and did not cover it; the second person passed by and did not cover it. According to Rashi's textual version, the second person is *chayav*.

### Exemption Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבַר עָלָיו הָרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ. וְרִאשׁוֹן מֵאֵימַת מִיפְּטַר? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, דְּאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מָנִי – חַד אָמַר: מִשֶּׁמַּנִּיחוֹ מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשֶּׁיִּמְסוֹר לוֹ דׇּלְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **the first** partner **passed by** the pit **and did not cover it,** and then the second also passed by it and did not cover it, the second is liable. The Gemara asks: **And from what point is the first exempt** from responsibility for the pit? **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say** a ruling **in the name of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, who says** it **in the name of Rabbi Mani,** but each one presents it differently: **One says: From** the time **he leaves** the other partner **using it, and one says:** The first is absolved of responsibility only **when he conveys to him his bucket,** with which he will draw water from the pit.

The Gemara asks: *ve-rishon eimat miptar* — at what point does the first person become exempt? The question assumes that the first person was once liable — he passed an uncovered pit without covering it and bore some responsibility at that moment. Something subsequently occurred that shifted full liability to the second person. What was that event?

### Opinion One

One opinion holds: the first person is exempted from the moment the second person begins using the well — *mishemenicho mishtamesh*. Once the second person actively draws water or otherwise makes use of the pit, there is an implicit transfer of responsibility. The first person, seeing the second party take over, is reasonably understood to have been relieved of his obligation to cover. This requires no formal transaction; the practical assumption of control suffices.

### Opinion Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַמְדַלֶּה מַיִם מִן הַבּוֹר, וּבָא חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: הַנַּח לִי, וַאֲנִי אֲדַלֶּה מַיִם; כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִנִּיחוֹ מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ – פָּטוּר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיִּמְסוֹר לוֹ דׇּלְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This dispute is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to one **who draws water from a cistern, and his friend comes and says to him: Leave me and I will draw water, once** the first person **leaves** the second person while the second is **using** the cistern, the first is now **exempt** if he did not cover it. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The first person is not absolved of responsibility until the time **when he conveys his bucket to** the second person.

The second opinion is more demanding: the first person must physically hand over the cover — *misheyimsor lo dalyo* — or more precisely, the covering implement or bucket, as Rashi interprets *dalyo* to mean the *kisui ha-bor*. A mere assumption of use by the second party, without an explicit transfer, does not exempt the first. Until there is a concrete, tangible handover, the first person remains liable alongside the second.

## Water-Drawers

### Baraita Setup

The Gemara grounds this dispute in a *tannaic* debate. The *baraita* describes: one person is drawing water from a pit; a second person comes and says, *hanach li va-ani edleh* — leave it to me, I'll draw. *Keivan she-henicho mishtamesh patur* — once the first person leaves and the second takes over the drawing, the first is exempt.

### Stricter View

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees: *misheyimsor lo dalyo* — the first person is exempt only from the moment he physically hands over the bucket (or cover) to the second. A verbal declaration of assumed responsibility by the second party, followed by the first person's passive withdrawal, is not sufficient to transfer the obligation.

### Breira Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה – הַאי מִדִּידֵיהּ קָא מְמַלֵּא, וְהַאי מִדִּידֵיהּ קָא מְמַלֵּא. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין בְּרֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dispute: **With regard to what** principle **do** they **disagree? Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov maintains** that **there is** a legal concept of **designation.** According to this principle, a situation in which certain aspects were not initially clearly defined can become clarified retroactively after a certain action or event occurs. The *halakha* treats all the aspects as being clearly defined and identifiable from the outset. In this case, the water is considered divided between the partners even before they draw it. When they do draw it, it is treated as if **this one were filling from his** part, the location of which has now been determined retroactively, **and this one were filling from his** part. Therefore, as long as the first partner has not conveyed his bucket to the second, he, as an owner of a defined part of the water, has not absolved himself of responsibility for the cistern. **And** by contrast, **the Rabbis maintain** that **there is no designation** and they both have the right to draw water from the cistern. Therefore, as soon as the second partner is left alone to draw water, he is responsible for the cistern and all the water it contains, even without conveying the bucket.

The Gemara analyzes the underlying conceptual disagreement. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds *yesh breira* — that there is retroactive determination. Under the principle of *breira*, the division of responsibility between the two parties can be retroactively clarified based on what actually unfolds: *hai mideidieh ka-memalei ve-hai mideidieh ka-memalei* — each one is understood to have been drawing from his own portion, as it were. Because the two parties' responsibilities are conceptually separable and retroactively determinable, a verbal agreement to transfer is not enough; a concrete physical act of handover is required to mark the precise moment of transfer. The first opinion, which exempts the first person upon the mere assumption of use, presumably holds *ein breira* — that no such retroactive determination is possible, and therefore we must look to conduct and circumstances to determine when responsibility effectively passed. The precise working-out of this *breira* analysis is left for the following session.
