# Bava Kamma 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Geneivah & Reshut

### Ganav bi-Reshut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּנַב בִּרְשׁוּת הַבְּעָלִים, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר חוּץ מֵרְשׁוּתָם; אוֹ שֶׁגָּנַב חוּץ מֵרְשׁוּתָם, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר בִּרְשׁוּתָם; אוֹ שֶׁגָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר חוּץ מֵרְשׁוּתָם – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. אֲבָל גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר בִּרְשׁוּתָם – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **stole** an animal **in** its **owner's domain,** i.e., he took hold of it or established control over it but had not yet removed it from the owner's premises, **and** then **he slaughtered** it **or sold** it **outside** of the owner's **domain; or** if **he stole** an animal **outside** of the owner's **domain and slaughtered** it **or sold** it **in** the owner's **domain; or** if **he stole** an animal **and slaughtered** it **or sold** it, and all of this occurred **outside** the owner's **domain,** in all of these cases, **he must pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment. But** if **he stole** it **and slaughtered or sold** it, and all of this occurred **in** the owner's **domain,** he is **exempt** from any of the fines for theft, as it is not considered theft until the stolen object is actually removed from the owner's premises.

The new *mishnah* on 79a opens a discussion that, while still touching on *tevichah u-mechira*, is in many ways far more fundamental: it concerns the very act of *geneivah* itself. The *mishnah* states: *ganav bi-reshut ha-ba'alim ve-tavach u-machar chutz mei-reshutam* — he stole it while still within the owner's domain, yet by the time he slaughtered or sold the animal, he was outside their domain.

The scenario requires some imagination. One might wonder how a thief could be standing in someone's yard and still be considered a thief in any meaningful sense. But picture a farmer with acres upon acres of property — a thief could take an animal from the pen and wander a mile away while still remaining on the owner's land, camping somewhere in a forest on the estate. The critical point of the *mishnah* is that this does not yet constitute *geneivah*. To be a *ganav*, one must actually take the object out of the owner's *reshut*. The Gemara had cited this earlier in connection with the *pasuk*: *himatzei timatzei be-yado ha-geneivah — ein li ella yado, gag, chatzer u-kfifah minayin, talmud lomar himatzei timatzei*. For an act of *geneivah* to have occurred, the thief must have drawn the object into some sense of his own control, taking it away from the owner.

### Ma'aseh Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מוֹשְׁכוֹ וְיוֹצֵא, וּמֵת בִּרְשׁוּת הַבְּעָלִים – פָּטוּר. הִגְבִּיהוֹ אוֹ הוֹצִיאוֹ מֵרְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If the thief **was** in the process of **leading** the animal **and leaving** the owner's premises, **and it died** while it was still **in the owner's domain,** the thief is **exempt** from all fines. If **he lifted it up or led it out of** the **owner's domain and** then the animal **died,** he is **liable** for his theft. For an act to be considered theft, the thief must acquire the item by pulling it or moving it, which are ineffective forms of acquisition on the owner's premises; or by lifting it up, which is effective even when performed in the owner's domain.

The end of the *mishnah* makes clear that *hagbahah* — lifting — is treated as taking something out of the owner's *reshut*, because lifting it constitutes taking possession. One's feet may remain on the owner's property, but by raising the object one removes it from the owner's domain. The case where the thief is still considered to be *bi-reshutam* is therefore where he never performed *hagbahah* at all.

The deeper implication of the *mishnah*, as we will see discussed by Tosafot, is that one must perform a normal *ma'aseh kinyan* — the same type of act that demonstrates the transfer of ownership in any ordinary transaction, whether as a gift or a purchase. The valid acts are *hagbahah*, *meshikhah*, or bringing the object into one's own *reshut*. Importantly, *meshikhah* — dragging — does not function while the object remains in the seller's or original owner's domain; it only operates in some neutral space. As long as one remains on the owner's property, the only effective *kinyan* is *hagbahah*.

The *mishnah* thus proceeds to spell out its cases. *Ganav bi-reshut ha-ba'alim ve-tavach u-machar chutz mei-reshutam* — he was in their domain but slaughtered and sold outside. *O she-ganav chutz mei-reshutam* — or he performed the act of taking possession while the animal was in a neutral zone. *Ve-tavach u-machar bi-reshutam* — and then, for some reason, the transaction happened back on the owner's property. One can imagine someone's animal wandering back with the thief onto the estate, and the thief selling it to an unsuspecting buyer who assumes he is the owner. In all these cases, *tashlumei arba'ah va-chamishah* applies.

But *aval ganav ve-tavach u-machar bi-reshutam* — if at no stage did he ever take the animal out of the property or perform a *ma'aseh kinyan* — *patur*: he is exempt, not only from the fourfold and fivefold payments, but even from *kefel*. If he slaughtered the animal on the property, he is a *mazik* and liable for the damage, but he has not performed an act of *geneivah*.

Rashi raises an important question about how to read the *mishnah's* opening case. If the *mishnah* means that the *mekhi­rah* occurred outside the domain but the initial taking was never accompanied by a *ma'aseh kinyan*, at what point exactly did the act of *geneivah* take place? Rashi explains: *keshe-hotzi'o mei-reshut na'aseh alav ganav ve-kna'o* — the moment he took it out of the domain is when he became a *ganav* and acquired it. The *mishnah* is not saying that only the sale happened outside; rather, the act of taking it out of the *reshut* was itself the *ma'aseh geneivah*. The *mishnah* is somewhat imprecise in its formulation, but the principle is clear: at some stage, a *ma'aseh kinyan* — a *hagbahah* or an exit from the owner's *reshut* — must occur.

The *mishnah* continues: *hayah moshcho ve-yotze u-met bi-reshut ha-ba'alim — patur*. If one was dragging the animal out by way of *meshikhah* and it died just before crossing the property line, one is exempt, because *meshikhah* does not function as a *kinyan* within the owner's *reshut*. The only act that works within the *reshut ha-ba'alim* is *hagbahah*. The next line of the *mishnah* makes this explicit: *higbiho o hotzi'o mei-reshut ha-ba'alim u-met — chayav*. If he lifted it or walked it out across the property line, and it then died, he is liable, because he has now performed a valid act of *geneivah*.

### Tosafot: Simta vs Reshus

This leads to a crucial question raised by Tosafot. In the opening chapter of *Bava Metzia*, Rav Acha and Ravina dispute the following: one opinion holds that *meshikhah* out of the owner's domain requires that one bring the object into a *simta* — a side street or alley — while the other holds that bringing it directly into the *reshut ha-rabim* suffices. The question is conceptually rich: is the act of *geneivah* complete by the mere fact of removing the object from the owner's control, or must the thief also bring it under his own control?

### Meshikhah: Simta or Reshut Ha-Rabim

This is a striking question from a conceptual standpoint. For an ordinary *kinyan meshikhah*, it is universally assumed that one must drag the object in a *simta* — a semi-private space where the acquirer can exercise some degree of control. *Meshikhah* in a fully public domain does not work, because there is no demonstration of the new owner taking possession. But for a *ganav*, perhaps the analysis is different. The owner does not care whether the thief has established his own control over the object; what concerns him is that the thief has removed it from *his* control. So perhaps taking the object out of the owner's *reshut* into a *reshut ha-rabim* suffices — not because the thief took possession, but because the owner lost it.

This would be a meaningful conceptual distinction: half of a normal *ma'aseh kinyan* is taking away from the original owner, but does the *ganav's* act need to mirror the full *kinyan*, including the taking into one's own control? The debate between the *amora'im* appears to pivot on exactly this question.

Tosafot, however, deflates this somewhat. He argues that one should not draw a distinction between the *kinyan* of a *ganav* and ordinary *kinyan meshikhah* in general. He notes that Abaye and Rava in *Bava Basra* apparently agree that *meshikhah* only works in a *simta* and not in a *reshut ha-rabim*, and the dispute among the *amora'im* here reflects that broader disagreement. Moreover, Tosafot cites the *derashah* that established the *ganav's* liability: *ein li ella yado, gago, chatzeiro ve-kafiso minayin* — the verse emphasizes the thief's own domain and possession, implying that liability attaches only in a place where he is capable of acquiring. *Mashma de-lo mechayev ella be-makom she-ra'uy liknos* — it must be a place where acquisition can meaningfully occur.

Nevertheless, the underlying question remains genuinely provocative. One could argue that there is a difference even between *keren* and *kefel*: the *pasuk* of *himatzei timatzei be-yado ha-geneivah* — the source for *kefel* — emphasizes the thief's possession, and perhaps *kefel* requires that he have brought the object into his own domain, while liability for the *keren* (the principal) arises already from the moment the owner loses control. Regardless, Tosafot maintains that no such distinction should be drawn, and the act of *geneivah* requires the same conditions as any other *kinyan meshikhah*.

### Three Readings of Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוֹ לִבְכוֹרוֹת בְּנוֹ אוֹ לְבַעַל חוֹב, לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, לְשׁוֹאֵל, לְנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וּלְשׂוֹכֵר; וְהָיָה מוֹשְׁכוֹ, וּמֵת בִּרְשׁוּת הַבְּעָלִים – פָּטוּר. הִגְבִּיהוֹ אוֹ שֶׁהוֹצִיאוֹ מֵרְשׁוּת הַבְּעָלִים, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If the thief **gave** the animal as payment **for the redemption of his firstborn son, or** as payment **to a creditor,** or conveyed it for safeguarding **to an unpaid bailee,** or lent it **to a borrower,** or conveyed it for safeguarding **to a paid bailee, or** leased it **to a renter, and he was leading out** the animal **and it died in the owner's domain,** the thief is **exempt** from all fines. If that individual, following the thief's instructions, **lifted up** the animal **or led it out of the owner's domain, and it** subsequently **died,** the thief is **liable** for the theft. The thief is liable for instructing another to remove the animal for the purposes of payment of a debt, safekeeping, borrowing, or rental, as this is tantamount to the thief taking it with his own hands.

The *mishnah* continues with a line that has generated considerable interpretive difficulty: *netano li-bekhor beno, o le-va'al chovo, o le-shomer chinam, le-sho'el, le-nosei sakhar, ve-la-sokher — hayah moshcho ve-yotze u-met bi-reshut ha-ba'alim — patur; higbiho o hotzi'o mei-reshut ha-ba'alim u-met — chayav*.

Rashi and Tosafot identify three possible readings of this passage, ultimately rejecting the first. The first reading is that this is not about a *ganav* at all, but rather about the owner himself giving the animal to a *kohen* for *pidyon ha-ben*, or to a creditor in payment of a debt, or to any of these *shomrim* — and the question is at what moment the transaction is complete: when does the animal pass out of the owner's possession into that of the recipient? The *mishnah* would then be saying that *meshikhah* out of the *reshut* finalizes the transaction, and if the animal dies before that moment, the recipient has not yet taken possession.

Rashi and Tosafot reject this reading for two reasons. First, the word *patur* does not fit the case of *ba'al chov* and *pidyon ha-ben*: if the animal dies before leaving the domain, the debt has not been paid and the owner is still obligated — he is not *patur*, he is still *chayav*. The word *patur* is only meaningful in the *shomer* case, where it describes the *shomer's* freedom from liability before he takes possession. Second, if we are talking about a *shomer* and the animal simply died, a *shomer chinam* and most other *shomrim* would normally be exempt from a natural death in any case — the scenario would have to involve negligence, which is not the plain reading.

The reading embraced by Rashi and Tosafot is that we remain entirely within the scenario of the *ganav*. The *ganav*, having not yet performed a *ma'aseh kinyan*, gives the animal to a *kohen* for *pidyon ha-ben*, or to a creditor, or to a *shomer*, while still on the owner's property. The question then becomes: when does the *ganav* become liable? The answer invokes the principle of *shliach li-dvar aveirah* — the agent for a transgression — and this is where the *mishnah* achieves its most remarkable result.

## Shliach Lidvar Aveira

### Unwitting Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גָּנַב, וְנָתַן לְאַחֵר וְטָבַח; גָּנַב, וְנָתַן לְאַחֵר וּמָכַר;
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one **stole** an animal **and gave it to another and** that person **slaughtered it** for him, or if **he stole** an animal **and gave it to another and** that person **sold it** for him, the thief is liable to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, as performing these actions through an agent is equivalent to performing them by oneself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? אַשְׁמְעִינַן רֵישָׁא גָּנַב וְנָתַן לְאַחֵר וְטָבַח – דְּיֵשׁ שָׁלִיחַ לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה. אַף עַל גַּב דִּבְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ אֵין שָׁלִיחַ לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה, הָכָא יֵשׁ שָׁלִיחַ לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is this *baraita* **teaching us?** All of the *halakhot* it states are obvious. The Gemara answers: **The first clause teaches us,** through the case of one who **stole** an animal **and gave it to another and** that person **slaughtered** or sold it on his behalf, **that** in this case **there is agency for transgression. Even though in the entire Torah** there is a principle that **there is no agency for transgression, here there is agency for transgression.** The Torah's principle is that a transgression committed by an agent who was appointed by another person is not considered the act of the one who appointed the agent, but the independent act of the agent himself. The case discussed by this *baraita* is an exception to the rule, as here, the agent's slaughter or sale of the animal is legally considered the action of the thief.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? ״וּטְבָחוֹ״ וּ״מְכָרוֹ״; מָה מְכִירָה – דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָאו עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר, אַף טְבִיחָה עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר – מִחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is **the reason** that this case is an exception to the principle? It is because the verse states: **"And slaughters it or sells it"** (Exodus 21:37), which juxtaposes the two acts of slaughtering and selling. This teaches that **just as** one becomes liable for **selling, which** by definition **is impossible without another** party, i.e., the buyer, **so too one becomes liable** for **slaughtering** even when it is **by means of another** party, i.e., when the thief instructs another person to slaughter the animal on his behalf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַשְׁמְעִינַן סֵיפָא גָּנַב וְהִקְדִּישׁ – מָה לִי מְכָרוֹ לְהֶדְיוֹט, מָה לִי מְכָרוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And the latter clause** of the *baraita* **teaches us** a novelty in the case of one who **stole** an animal **and consecrated** it. The novelty is that this is considered a sale despite the fact that there is no purchaser. This is due to the following argument: **What** difference **is it to me** if **he sold** the animal **to an ordinary** person, and **what** difference **is it to me** if **he sold it to Heaven** by consecrating it? If the animal changes ownership, it is considered a sale, and it does not matter if the new owner is an ordinary person or the Temple treasury.

The scenario is this: the *ganav* has not yet performed a *ma'aseh kinyan* on the animal. He gives it to a *kohen* who believes it is the *ganav's* property, and this *kohen* proceeds to take it in payment of *pidyon ha-ben*, or to a creditor who believes he is collecting a legitimate debt, or to a *shomer* who believes he is simply watching someone's animal. These third parties are entirely oblivious to any wrongdoing. Each of them performs what, from their perspective, is a completely natural and legitimate act of taking possession — doing a *meshikhah*, lifting the animal, or walking it off the property. And at that moment, according to the *mishnah*, the *ganav* becomes liable as if he himself had performed the act of *geneivah*.

This is a far-reaching *chiddush*: the *ganav* uses an unwitting agent to accomplish the *ma'aseh geneivah*, and that agent's act is attributed to him. The question is how this can be, given the general principle of *ein shliach li-dvar aveirah* — that one cannot appoint an agent to do something forbidden, because the agent is himself an obligated person who should have known better: *divrei ha-rav ve-divrei ha-talmid, divrei mi shom'in* — when the instructions of a master and a student conflict, one follows the master.

Tosafot addresses this directly. *De-haneihu lo yad'ei de-ati le-yadeihu be-issura* — these individuals did not know they were doing anything improper; they believed the animal belonged to the *ganav*. The concept of *shliach li-dvar aveirah* in this context is not the same as ordinary agency, where one formally authorizes another to act on one's behalf. It is closer to a *grama* — a mode of indirect causation. The *ganav* has made the third party into his *keli*, his instrument, through which the act of *geneivah* is accomplished. The third party is not choosing to do something wrong; he is doing something entirely natural and expected. He is, in effect, the *ganav's* unwitting tool.

Tosafot elaborates this by reference to the two explanations of *ein shliach li-dvar aveirah* found in the first chapter of *Bava Metzia*. According to one view, the reason there is no agency for a transgression is that the agent is a legally responsible person who should refuse: *divrei ha-rav ve-divrei ha-talmid, divrei mi shom'in*. Where the agent does not know he is doing anything wrong, this rationale does not apply, and the unwitting agent is therefore attributed to the sender. According to the second view, the principle is that one cannot predict whether an agent will in fact carry out the instruction — *i ba'ei avid ve-i ba'ei lo avid* — and therefore the sender cannot be held responsible. But Tosafot notes that this reasoning also does not apply here: *yode'a hu she-yikach, me-acher de-chaser bi-shelo hu* — of course the creditor is going to take the animal, of course the *kohen* is going to accept payment. There is no doubt that the instruction will be followed. Therefore in this case, under both explanations, the *ganav* is responsible for the *ma'aseh geneivah* accomplished through the unwitting third party.

This principle raises an interesting further question: does it apply only in the realm of *bein adam la-chaveiro*, where the harm is the impact on another person and the *ganav* is effectively the cause of that harm? Or might it extend to ritual *aveiros* as well? In the domain of *bein adam la-chaveiro*, the focus is on the causal impact — the object has been removed from the owner — and that is why the *ganav* who brought about that removal through an unwitting agent bears responsibility. Whether this logic would apply, say, to a ritual act performed unwittingly on behalf of someone who arranged it, is a question that would require further analysis.

One further point that remains unresolved from the *mishnah* itself: we know the *ganav* is liable for the *keren* — the principal — when the unwitting agent carries out the act. But is he also liable for *kefel*? The *mishnah* establishes only that the act through the unwitting agent creates liability for the *ganav*; whether that liability extends to the fine of *kefel* or beyond remains an open question. As Rashi and Tosafot note, the *chiddush* of the *mishnah* — according to their reading — is that even *keren* is *chayav al yedei acher*, liability through another's action. That *tevichah u-mechira* can generate *arba'ah va-chamishah al yedei acher* had already been established; this *mishnah* adds that the principal itself is owed even when the act was accomplished through an unwitting intermediary.

### Meshikhah & Shomrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי אַמֵּימָר: תִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּשׁוֹמְרִים, אוֹ לֹא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ameimar raises a dilemma:** Did the Sages **institute** the requirement of **pulling** or leading an animal **with regard to bailees, or not?** When an animal or other item is purchased, the parties to the sale are not bound by the deal until the purchaser performs an act of acquisition, one of which is pulling the animal or leading it along. Did the Sages institute an ordinance that the same applies to bailees? In other words, does the bailee's obligation to safeguard the animal or item begin as soon as he consents to watch it, or only after he has taken the animal and moved it even minimally?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יֵימַר, תָּא שְׁמַע: נְתָנוֹ לִבְכוֹרוֹת בְּנוֹ אוֹ לְבַעַל חוֹבוֹ, לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וּלְשׁוֹאֵל, לְנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וּלְשׂוֹכֵר; הָיָה מוֹשְׁכוֹ וְיוֹצֵא, וּמֵת בִּרְשׁוּת הַבְּעָלִים – פָּטוּר. מַאי, לָאו שׁוֹמֵר – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּשׁוֹמְרִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar said: Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If the thief **gave** the animal as payment **for the redemption of his firstborn son, or** as payment **to a creditor,** or conveyed it for safeguarding **to an unpaid bailee,** or lent it **to a borrower,** or conveyed it for safeguarding **to a paid bailee, or** rented it **to a renter, and he was leading out** the animal **and it died in the owner's domain,** the thief is **exempt** from payment. **What, is it not** talking about the **bailee** leading out the animal, when it states that the thief is exempt? **And** if so, **conclude from** this mishna that the Sages **instituted** the requirement of **pulling with regard to bailees.** If the bailee's obligation begins immediately upon his consent to watch over the item, it would be considered stolen by the thief at that stage, even before the bailee moves it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, גַּנָּב.
>
> **Translation:** Ameimar **said to** Rav Yeimar: **No,** this is not necessarily the correct interpretation of the mishna. The mishna may mean that the owner gave the animal to his creditor or to a bailee, and subsequently a thief came to steal it from the house of the creditor or the bailee, and it was the **thief** who was leading the animal out of those premises when it died.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא! תְּנָא גַּנָּב שֶׁגָּנַב מִבֵּית הַבְּעָלִים, וּתְנָא גַּנָּב שֶׁגָּנַב מִבֵּית שׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yeimar asked: How can that clause of the mishna refer to an animal that died as the thief was leading it out? The mishna already **taught this** *halakha* in **the first clause.** Ameimar answered: Nevertheless, it is possible that the mishna **taught** this *halakha* concerning **a thief who stole** an animal **from its owner's house, and** then **taught** it again with regard to **a thief who stole** an animal **from a bailee's house.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא תְּדַחֲיֵיהּ; מָה לִי גַּנָּב שֶׁגָּנַב מִבֵּית שׁוֹמֵר, מָה לִי גַּנָּב שֶׁגָּנַב מִבֵּית בְּעָלִים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to** Ameimar: **Do not reject** Rav Yeimar's argument by means of this alternative interpretation of the mishna. It is not reasonable to assert that the mishna taught this *halakha* twice, as **what** difference **is** it **to me** if the **thief stole** it **from the bailee's house** and **what** difference **is** it **to me if** the **thief stole** it **from the owner's house?** There is no halakhic distinction between these two cases. Therefore, the mishna would not have discussed both of them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שׁוֹמֵר – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּשׁוֹמְרִין? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁתִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּלָקוֹחוֹת, כָּךְ תִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּשׁוֹמְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** more reasonable to say that the mishna should be interpreted as meaning that the **bailee** was leading the animal out of the owner's house at the behest of the thief, as Rav Yeimar claimed? **And** therefore, one should **conclude from** the mishna that the Sages **instituted** the requirement of **pulling with regard to bailees.** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the mishna that they did. **It was also stated** explicitly that **Rabbi Elazar says: Just as** the Sages **instituted** the requirement of **pulling with regard to purchasers, so too** they **instituted** the requirement of **pulling with regard to bailees.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁתִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּלָקוֹחוֹת, כָּךְ תִּיקְּנוּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּשׁוֹמְרִין. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁהַקַּרְקַע נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף, בִּשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה, כָּךְ שְׂכִירוּת נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף, בִּשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as** the Sages **instituted** the requirement of **pulling with regard to purchasers, so too** they **instituted** the requirement of **pulling with regard to bailees. And just as land is acquired by** the payment of **money, by** the writing of **a deed** of sale, **or by** the purchaser **taking possession** of the land through working it, **so too a rental** deal is finalized **by** the payment of **money, by** the writing of **a deed** of rent, **or by** the renter **taking possession** of the land. This concludes the *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂכִירוּת דְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא שְׂכִירוּת דְּמִטַּלְטְלִין, מִטַּלְטְלִי בְּנֵי שְׁטָרָא נִינְהוּ?! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שְׂכִירוּת דְּקַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: When the *baraita* speaks of rental, it is referring to **the rental of what** kind of property? **If we say it is speaking of the rental of movable property, is movable property** subject to the writing of **a deed?** Rather, **Rav Ḥisda said:** It is referring to **the rental of land.**

### Ganav Gives to Third

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: רָאוּהוּ שֶׁהִטְמִין בָּחוֹרָשִׁין וְטָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. אַמַּאי? הָא לֹא מָשַׁךְ! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שֶׁהִכִּישָׁהּ בְּמַקֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says:** If people **saw** a thief **who was hiding in the woods** near a herd of grazing animals, **and** that individual emerged and **slaughtered or sold** one of the animals from the herd, **he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** The Gemara asks: **Why** is the thief liable to the fourfold or fivefold payment? **But he did not pull** the animal. Rabbi Elazar is apparently describing a case where the thief emerged from the forest and slaughtered one of the animals without first moving it, and there is no fourfold or fivefold payment unless the thief first steals the animal. **Rav Ḥisda says:** This is referring to a case **where he hit** the animal **with a stick,** causing it to move.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וְכֵיוָן דְּרָאוּהוּ, גַּזְלָן הוּא! כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מִטַּמַּרי מִנַּיְיהוּ, גַּנָּב הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Rabbi Elazar's statement, the Sages **say: But since** people **saw him** doing all this, **he is a robber,** not a thief, and the fourfold or fivefold payment applies only to thieves. A thief is one who sneaks into a house or other private property; one who commits his act brazenly, in public, is classified as a robber. The Gemara answers: **Since he was hiding from them, he is** considered **a thief,** despite the fact that they saw him.

## Ganav vs Gazlan

### Hiding in Forest

The *gemara* now discusses when someone hiding in a forest is still considered a *ganav* despite being visible. The principle is that a *ganav* is distinguished from a *gazlan* not merely by whether people see him, but by his intent and posture — whether he is trying to conceal himself from public view and from legal consequences. This distinction becomes central to the Talmud's theological discussion about why the Torah penalizes theft more severely than robbery.

### Definition of Gazlan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא גַּזְלָן הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כְּגוֹן בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּגְזֹל אֶת הַחֲנִית מִיַּד הַמִּצְרִי, וַיַּהַרְגֵהוּ בַּחֲנִיתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if so, what are the circumstances** of a **robber? Rabbi Abbahu said:** Robbers are **like** the case of **Benaiah ben Jehoiada, as it is stated** concerning him: "He slew an Egyptian, a goodly man; and the Egyptian had a spear in his hand; but he went down to him with a staff, **and he robbed the spear out of the Egyptian's hand, and slew him with his own spear"** (II Samuel 23:21).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כְּגוֹן בַּעֲלֵי שְׁכֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּשִׂימוּ לוֹ בַעֲלֵי שְׁכֶם מְאָרְבִים עַל רָאשֵׁי הֶהָרִים, וַיִּגְזְלוּ [אֵת] כׇּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲבֹר עֲלֵיהֶם בַּדָּרֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** A different example of a robber is a case **like** that of **the men of Shechem, as it is stated: "And the men of Shechem set ambushers for him on the tops of the mountains, and they robbed all that came along that way by them"** (Judges 9:25).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מֵהַאי? אָמַר לָךְ: כֵּיוָן דְּמִטַּמְּרִי אִיטַּמּוֹרֵי, לָא גַּזְלָנֵי נִינְהוּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – הָא דְּקָא מִטַּמְּרִי, דְּלָא נִיחְזִינְהוּ אִינָשֵׁי וְנִיעְרְקוּ מִנַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rabbi Abbahu did not say** the example **from this** verse, which discusses the men of Shechem? He could have **said to you: Since** the men of Shechem **were hiding** in ambush, **they are not** considered **robbers** but thieves. **And** how would **Rabbi Yoḥanan** respond to this claim? **This** fact **that they were hiding** was not because they were hesitant to steal in view of the public. Rather, they acted in this manner **so that** the travelers **should not see them** in advance **and flee from them.**

### Motivation for Hiding

The *gemara* develops an important distinction: A *ganav* hides because he fears detection and punishment—he is afraid of being caught and brought to justice. A *gazlan* may strategically position himself so that his victims cannot escape or call for help, but he has no fear of being identified or prosecuted. The *gazlan* is willing to be seen and known; the *ganav* is fundamentally afraid of public exposure. This internal motivation becomes the key to the distinction.

### Theological Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה הֶחְמִירָה תּוֹרָה בְּגַנָּב יוֹתֵר מִגַּזְלָן? אָמַר לָהֶן: זֶה – הִשְׁוָה כְּבוֹד עֶבֶד לִכְבוֹד קוֹנוֹ, וְזֶה – לֹא הִשְׁוָה כְּבוֹד עֶבֶד לִכְבוֹד קוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara concludes its discussion of theft with several aggadic statements. **His students asked Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai: For what reason was the Torah stricter with a thief than with a robber?** Only a thief is required to pay the double, fourfold, or fivefold payment, not a robber. Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to them** in response: **This one,** the robber, **equated the honor of** the **servant to the honor of his Master, and that one,** the thief, **did not equate the honor of** the **servant to the honor of his Master.** The robber fears neither God nor people, as he is not afraid to rob in public. The thief does not fear God but he does fear other people, which demonstrates that he is more concerned about humans than God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּבְיָכוֹל עָשָׂה עַיִן שֶׁל מַטָּה כְּאִילּוּ אֵינָהּ רוֹאָה, וְאוֹזֶן שֶׁל מַטָּה כְּאִילּוּ אֵינָהּ שׁוֹמַעַת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי הַמַּעֲמִיקִים מֵה׳ לַסְתִּר עֵצָה, וְהָיָה בְמַחְשָׁךְ מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם וְגוֹ׳״; וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ לֹא יִרְאֶה יָּהּ וְלֹא יָבִין אֱלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״; וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי [אָמְרוּ] עָזַב ה׳ אֶת הָאָרֶץ, וְאֵין ה׳ רֹאֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it were,** the thief **establishes the eye below,** i.e., God's eye, **as though it does not see, and the ear below,** i.e., God's ear, **as though it does not hear.** The Gemara cites verses that describe people who imagine that God does not see their actions, **as it is stated: "Woe to them who seek deeply to hide their counsel from the Lord, and their works are in the dark,** and they say: Who sees us, and who knows us?" (Isaiah 29:15). **And it is written: "And they say: The Lord will not see, neither will the God of Jacob give heed"** (Psalms 94:7). **And it is written: "For they say: The Lord has forsaken the land, and the Lord does not see"** (Ezekiel 9:9).

This is a profound theological reading of the distinction between theft and robbery. The thief's sin is not merely taking someone else's property; it is a form of practical denial of God's omniscience. By committing his act in secret, the thief implicitly asserts that God does not see what he is doing—that divine oversight is limited to the public sphere or to acts committed openly. He respects human opinion and human authority, but he acts as though God's eye does not penetrate into the darkness.

The robber, by contrast, commits his act openly and publicly, without regard for human opinion. He is brazenly defiant, but his defiance is uniform. He acts as though neither God nor man can stop him. This is certainly worse in one sense—it shows open contempt for all authority—but it does not involve the specific heresy of denying divine omniscience. The thief's behavior, in subtle but powerful terms, declares that God is not watching.

### Rabban Gamliel's Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מָשְׁלוּ מָשָׁל מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לִשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהָיוּ בְּעִיר וְעָשׂוּ מִשְׁתֶּה, אֶחָד זִימֵּן אֶת בְּנֵי הָעִיר וְלֹא זִימֵּן אֶת בְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְאֶחָד לֹא זִימֵּן אֶת בְּנֵי הָעִיר וְלֹא זִימֵּן אֶת בְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ. אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן עוֹנְשׁוֹ מְרוּבֶּה? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה שֶׁזִּימֵּן אֶת בְּנֵי הָעִיר וְלֹא זִימֵּן אֶת בְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir said:** To illustrate the severity of a thief over a robber, as per Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai's explanation, **they stated a parable in the name of Rabban Gamliel. To what is this matter comparable? To two people who were** living **in the** same **city, and** both of them **prepared a feast. One** of them **invited the people of the city** to his feast **but he did not invite the king's sons. And** the other **did not invite the people of the city and** also **did not invite the king's sons. Which of them** deserves **a greater punishment? You must say** that it is **this one who invited the people of the city but did not invite the king's sons.** Likewise, both the thief and the robber show disdain for God, but the robber does not display more respect for people.

The parable is devastating in its simplicity. If you are hosting a feast and you invite the townspeople but exclude the king's family, you have made a statement: you value the approval of ordinary citizens more than the favor of the king. This is insulting not only to the king, but it reveals the nature of your priorities. The person who invites neither the townspeople nor the king's family is at least consistent in his contempt; he shows disdain for both equally. But the one who makes a distinction—honoring some and not others—has revealed that he understands hierarchy and chooses to invert it.

### Ox vs Sheep Fine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה גָּדוֹל כֹּחַ שֶׁל מְלָאכָה: שׁוֹר, שֶׁבִּיטְּלוֹ מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ – חֲמִשָּׁה; שֶׂה, שֶׁלֹּא בִּיטְּלוֹ מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ – אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses why there is a fourfold payment for a sheep but a fivefold payment for an ox. **Rabbi Meir said: Come and see how great the power of labor is.** The theft of **an ox, which** was forced **by** the thief **to cease its labor,** leads to **a fivefold** payment; whereas the theft of **a sheep, which** was **not** forced **by** the thief **to cease its labor,** as a sheep performs no labor, leads to only **a fourfold** payment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה גָּדוֹל כְּבוֹד הַבְּרִיּוֹת: שׁוֹר, שֶׁהָלַךְ בְּרַגְלָיו – חֲמִשָּׁה; שֶׂה, שֶׁהִרְכִּיבוֹ עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ – אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said: Come and see how great human dignity is.** The theft of **an ox, which walked on its own legs** as the thief stole it, leads to **a fivefold** payment, whereas the theft of **a sheep, which** the thief **carried on his shoulder** as he walked, thereby causing himself embarrassment, leads to only **a fourfold** payment.

The two explanations offer contrasting perspectives on why the fine differs. Rabbi Meir emphasizes the utility of the stolen object and the damage caused by its removal: an ox is a working animal, so stealing it deprives the owner of the means of production. A sheep produces wool, milk, and meat, but it is not a tool in the same way. The fine of five reflects the gravity of having taken away the owner's livelihood.

Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, by contrast, emphasizes human dignity—the dignity even of the thief himself. When someone steals an ox, they can lead it by its own legs; they walk behind it with some degree of dignity. But to steal a sheep, one must pick it up, place it on one's shoulder, and carry it. This is undignified. The thief has humiliated himself in the process of committing the crime. And the Torah, in its sensitivity to human dignity, recognizes this and reduces the fine from five to four. Even a thief deserves a measure of compassion if he has suffered loss of dignity in his own wrongdoing.
