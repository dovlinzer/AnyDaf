# Meilah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul & Nosar Tziruf

### Pigul/Nosar Non-Combinat…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפִּיגּוּל וְהַנּוֹתָר – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שְׁנֵי שֵׁמוֹת. הַשֶּׁרֶץ וְהַנְּבֵלָה, וְכֵן הַנְּבֵלָה וּבְשַׂר הַמֵּת – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה לְטַמֵּא, אֲפִילּוּ בַּקַּל שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Sacrificial meat that is ***piggul* and** sacrificial meat that is ***notar* do not join together** to constitute the requisite measure of an olive-bulk, **due to** the fact **that they** belong to **two** separate **categories** of prohibition. The flesh of the carcass of **the creeping animal and** the flesh of **the animal carcass, and likewise** the flesh of **the animal carcass and the flesh of the corpse, do not join together to transmit ritual impurity,** not **even for the** more **lenient of the two** impurities, i.e., the impurity that requires the greater measure.

The closing mishnah of the previous perek establishes that *pigul* and *nosar* do not combine with one another. Even if one possesses half a *kezayis* of *pigul* and half a *kezayis* of *nosar*, since they represent two distinct prohibitions — *mipnei shehein shnei sheimos* — neither threshold is met and one does not receive lashes for either.

### Tumah Category Distincti…

The mishnah then turns to the parallel question in the laws of *tumah*. Different fundamental categories of *tumah* do not combine: a *sheretz* and a *neveilah* do not combine, and likewise a *neveilah* and *basar ha-meis* do not combine — *ein mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — not even for the lighter of the two categories. The point is significant: even where two *tumos* share the same *shiur*, as a *neveilah* and a *meis* both require a *kezayis*, they remain distinct in character. A *neveilah* is an *av* while a *meis* is an *avi avot*, carrying a seven-day defilement and a host of additional stringencies. Possessing half a *kezayis* of *neveilah* and half a *kezayis* of *meis* does not allow one to say: the *meis* is stricter, so let it at least function as a *neveilah*. They are fundamentally different categories and do not combine.

### Shmuel on Hand Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְטוּמְאַת הַיָּדַיִם, דְּמִדְּרַבָּנַן הִיא. אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן אֲכִילָה – מִצְטָרְפִין. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל כִּי קֹדֶשׁ הוּא״ – כֹּל שֶׁבַּקֹּדֶשׁ פָּסוּל, בָּא הַכָּתוּב לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The mishna **taught** that *piggul* and *notar* do not join together **only with regard to** the ritual **impurity of the hands** if one touched them, which **is by rabbinic law. But with regard to** the **matter of eating, they do join together. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The verse states with regard to leftover sacrificial food from meat and bread: **"It shall not be eaten because it is sacred"** (Exodus 29:34). This teaches with regard to **anything sacred** that has been **disqualified** for whatever reason, that **the verse comes to apply a prohibition with regard to its consumption.**

The Gemara then introduces a teaching of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *lo shanu ela tumas hayadayim midrabbanan*. The mishnah's statement that *pigul* and *nosar* do not combine refers specifically to the rabbinic *tumah* of the hands. This opens a broader discussion. Beyond the prohibition of eating *pigul* and *nosar*, these substances also carry a *tumah* — but this *tumah* is only rabbinic in nature. The mark of its rabbinic character is precisely that it generates only *tumas yadayim*. Any biblical *tumah*, when contacted, renders one's entire body *tamei*; the rabbis, however, enacted certain *tumos* that affect only the hands. *Pigul* and *nosar* fall into this category.

### Kohanim Stringency

The Gemara's framing here points to a recognizable sociological reality: the *kohanim* were extraordinarily stringent regarding *tumah* in ways that sometimes exceeded their care for other serious prohibitions. Even *issurim* carrying *kares*, such as eating *pigul* or *nosar*, did not necessarily capture their attention as intensely as *tumah*-related concerns, which touched directly on their priestly role. *Chazal*, recognizing this dynamic, imposed the rabbinic *tumah* of the hands on *pigul* and *nosar* as a mechanism of control: by framing contact with these substances as generating *tumas yadayim*, they could rely on the *kohanim*'s existing stringency around *tumah* to keep them away from eating what is forbidden. The parallel with all forms of religious compartmentalization — great scrupulousness in ritual matters alongside relative leniency in ethical obligations such as *lo sisna* — is noted, and the dynamic here by the *kohanim* is of the same kind.

### Generic Eating Prohibiti…

Shmuel's teaching continues: *aval min ha'achila mitztarfin* — when it comes to eating, *pigul* and *nosar* do combine. A *baraisa* is cited in which Rabbi Eliezer derives from the verse *lo yochal ki kodesh heim* a generic negative prohibition applying to all items that are *pasul* by virtue of their *kedushah* status, of which *pigul* and *nosar* are the classic examples. Because this generic prohibition encompasses both, one can combine half a *kezayis* of *pigul* and half a *kezayis* of *nosar* to be liable for this *lav* — even though they do not combine for *kares*, since that requires each prohibition to be met independently on its own terms.

## Rishon/Sheni Derivative

### Av/Velad Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹכֶל שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּוְלַד הַטּוּמְאָה – מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה לְטַמֵּא בַּקַּל שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The food that became ritually impure** through contact **with a primary source of ritual impurity,** thereby assuming first-degree ritual impurity, **and** the food **that became ritually impure** through contact **with a secondary source of ritual impurity,** thereby assuming second-degree ritual impurity, **join together** to constitute the requisite measure of an egg-bulk **to transmit impurity in accordance with the** more **lenient of the two,** i.e., second-degree ritual impurity.

The next mishnah introduces a new dimension of *tziruf* in the laws of *tumah*: food that became *tamei* through an *av ha-tumah* and food that became *tamei* through a *velad ha-tumah* — a *rishon* and a *sheni* respectively — do combine: *mitztarfin zeh im zeh l'tamei b'kal shebishneihem*. They combine to convey *tumah* in accordance with the weaker of the two. If one has half a *kebeitzah* of food that is *rishon l'tumah* and half a *kebeitzah* of food that is *sheni l'tumah*, they combine for the *shiur* of a full *kebeitzah* — not to generate the effect of a *rishon*, but at least to generate the effect of a *sheni*.

### Derivative Genericity

This ruling seems at first to conflict with the principle established above: different categories of *tumah* do not combine. The resolution lies in understanding the nature of derivative *tumah*. Once *tumah* is no longer the original source but has been transmitted through contact, it loses something of its distinctive character. A *rishon l'tumah* is not the *tumah* itself — it is merely the first recipient of *tumah*. A *sheni* is still further removed. Both are derivative, and in becoming derivative they become, in a meaningful sense, generic. They no longer carry the specific identity of the originating *tumah*.

An illuminating parallel: a *zav* is prohibited from entering *Har HaBayis* — this is a stringency specific to *tumah* of *zav*. A *tevul yom* who was a *zav* may carry a residual question about that stringency because something of his original status lingers. But a person who merely touched a *zav* has entirely ordinary *tumah*; the quality of *tumas zav* does not extend to derivative contact. Once *tumah* is derivative, it becomes generic — it loses the character of the original *tumah* that produced it. Since both the *rishon* and the *sheni* are in this generic, derivative category, they can combine for the lesser of the two effects.

### R. Shimon Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה טַעַם? שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁיֵּעָשֶׂה רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: What is the reason** that food with first-degree ritual impurity joins together with food that has second-degree ritual impurity? The reason is **that it is possible for** the food with **second-**degree impurity **to render** another food impure with **first-**degree impurity.

The *tanna* Rabbi Shimon offers a rationale: *she'efshar l'sheni sheyehei rishon* — it is possible for a *sheni* to become a *rishon*. The Gemara immediately presses: how can a *sheni* become a *rishon*? That is plainly impossible on its own. The statement seems paradoxical.

### Rava's Reframing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי קָא עָבֵיד שֵׁנִי רִאשׁוֹן? הָא לָא אֶפְשָׁר! אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִי גָּרַם לַשֵּׁנִי? לָאו רִאשׁוֹן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But can** a food with **second-**degree impurity **render** another food impure with **first-**degree impurity? That is **not possible.** **Rava said** that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: What caused** that food to become impure with **second-**degree impurity? Is it **not** that it was touched by food with **first-**degree impurity?

Rava reframes Rabbi Shimon's intent: *mi garam l'sheni? lav rishon?* — what brought about the *sheni*'s status? Was it not a *rishon*? The point is that the *sheni* is conceptually — and not merely conceptually, but actually — a derivative of the *rishon*. Because the *rishon* is the source from which the *sheni* descends, the *rishon* certainly stands in sufficient proximity to combine with the *sheni*. This would not apply, however, to combining a *rishon* with an *av*. The *rishon* did become *rishon* because it touched an *av*, but the *av* is the originating *tumah* itself, not a derivative, and the generic, derivative character that permits *tziruf* here belongs only to the *rishon* and *sheni*, not to the *av*.

### Rav Ashi Category View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי, לְגַבֵּי שְׁלִישִׁי – בְּנֵי חֲדָא בִּיקְתָּא אִינּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi** similarly **said:** Food impure with **first-**degree impurity **and** food impure with **second-**degree impurity, **with regard to** food impure with **third-**degree impurity, **are** considered like **members of one house,** i.e., they both lead to third-degree impurity, either directly or indirectly, and for this reason they join together.

Rav Ashi frames the matter differently: *rishon v'sheni legabi shelishi benei chada bikta inun* — a *rishon* and a *sheni*, from the perspective of the *shelishi* they would render *tamei*, are of the same household. *Terumah* becomes *shelishi* upon contact with a *sheni*; from the *shelishi*'s point of view, a *rishon* and a *sheni* alike have the power to convey that effect and are not distinguished from one another. *Rabbeinu Gershom* notes that *bikta* here means something like "from the same house" — a way of expressing that they belong to the same category. As Rashi observes, this is not truly a different answer from Rava's but rather another formulation of the same underlying point: both *rishon* and *sheni* are derivative and therefore generic enough to combine for the lesser of their effects.

## Orlah/Kilayim Debate

### Orlah/Kilayim Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם – מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*]** (see Leviticus 19:23), **and diverse kinds,**i.e., grain sown **in a vineyard** (see Deuteronomy 22:9) **join together** to constitute the requisite measure to prohibit a mixture that they are mixed into. This applies when the volume of the permitted produce is less than two hundred times the prohibited produce. **Rabbi Shimon says: They do not join together.**

The mishnah then states that *orlah* and *kilay ha-kerem* combine with one another — *mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — while Rabbi Shimon holds they do not combine.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמִי צָרִיךְ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְצָרוֹפֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַכּוֹת? תְּנִי: אֵין צְרִיכִין לְצָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Shimon, do** the two half-measures **need to join** together, in order for one who eats them to be liable? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One who eats **any measure** of a prohibited food is liable **to** receive **lashes,** as the Sages stated the measure of an olive-bulk only with regard to the requirement to bring an offering? The Gemara answers: The wording of the mishna is not precise and must be emended, to **teach** that Rabbi Shimon says: **They do not need to join together,** as one is flogged even for less than the minimum measure.

The Gemara is immediately struck by a difficulty. Since when does Rabbi Shimon concern himself with *tziruf* at all? Rabbi Shimon holds *kol shehu l'malkot* — that even the smallest quantity suffices for liability to lashes, so no *shiur* is required. The question of combining quantities to reach a threshold is therefore irrelevant to him entirely. If so, what is Rabbi Shimon even disagreeing about? The Gemara resolves this by re-reading Rabbi Shimon's position: rather than *ein mitztarfin*, read *ein tzrichin l'tzaref* — they do not even need to be combined, because any quantity at all triggers liability. On Rabbi Shimon's view, one would receive lashes for a tiny amount of *orlah* and separately for a tiny amount of *kilay ha-kerem*, without any need to combine them.

This leaves a deeper question: why would the *tanna kamma* hold that *orlah* and *kilay ha-kerem* combine at all? They are distinct *issurim*, and the general rule is that different prohibitions do not combine. *Rabbeinu Gershom* reads the mishnah as speaking of combining for purposes of lashes — a striking position since they are different *lavin*. The *Or Sameach* cites the *Tosafot Yom Tov*, who draws on a Yerushalmi to explain that this reflects the view of Rabbi Meir, who holds that all biblically prohibited substances share a single underlying negative command and therefore combine.

### Bitul Interpretation

The *Rashash*, by contrast, follows what the *Rambam* also apparently holds: the combination discussed here is for purposes of *bitul* rather than lashes. The question is whether a small amount of *orlah* and a small amount of *kilay ha-kerem*, when mixed together into a larger permitted quantity, combine to prevent *bitul* of one another — that is, whether their prohibited volumes are aggregated when assessing whether the mixture is sufficiently diluted to be permitted. *Orlah* and *kilay ha-kerem* share similar *shiurim* and are linked in various other *halachot* of *bitul*, so their combination for this purpose, while still a *chiddush*, is less startling than Rabbeinu Gershom's reading. That the *Rambam* agrees with this interpretation is noted as confirming it as the plain sense of the passage.

## Material Tumah Sizes

### Cloth Size Combinations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבֶּגֶד וְהַשַּׂק, הַשַּׂק וְהָעוֹר, הָעוֹר וְהַמַּפָּץ – כּוּלָּן מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A garment must be at least three by three handbreadths in order to become a primary source of ritual impurity, by means of ritual impurity imparted by the treading of a *zav*. A sack made from goats' hair must be at least four by four handbreadths, while an animal hide must be five by five, and a mat six by six. **The garment and the sack, the sack and the hide,** and **the hide and the mat all join together** to constitute the requisite measure to become ritually impure in accordance with the material of the greater measure.

The mishnah then turns to the *tziruf* of different cloth materials. A *beged* (woven cloth), *sak* (sackcloth), *or* (leather), and *mapatz* (a woven reed mat) all combine with one another for purposes of *tumah* — *kulam mitztarfin zeh im zeh*. The underlying structure here is that different fabric materials require different minimum sizes to be considered significant enough to become *tamei*: woven cloth requires three fingerbreadths by three fingerbreadths; sackcloth requires four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* (roughly a foot by a foot); leather requires five *tefachim* by five *tefachim*; and a reed mat requires six *tefachim* by six *tefachim*. These are the minimum sizes for each material when present in isolation. When pieces of multiple materials are joined together, they combine — though not toward the smaller size, only toward the larger. A patchwork item containing pieces of cloth, sackcloth, leather, and reed mat may combine to meet the *shiur* of a *mapatz* at six by six *tefachim*.

### Conscious Cutting Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן רְאוּיִן לִיטָּמֵא בְּמוֹשָׁב. גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: קִיצַּע מִכּוּלָּן שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְעָשָׂה מֵהֶן בֶּגֶד לְמִשְׁכָּב – שְׁלֹשָׁה, לְמוֹשָׁב – טֶפַח, לַאֲחִיזָה – כׇּל שֶׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** that they join together, despite the fact that their requisite measures are not equal? **Because** all the component materials **are fit to become ritually impure** through the ritual impurity imparted **to a seat** upon which a *zav* sits, as they can each be used to patch a saddle or saddlecloth. Since the measure of all these materials is equal in the case of a *zav*, they join together for other forms of ritual impurity as well. GEMARA: A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where one **cut from each of** the materials listed in the mishna, i.e., the garment, the sack, the hide, and the mat, a piece of less than **three** by three handbreadths, **and** sewed them together, thereby **fashioning a garment from them;** if he formed the garment **for lying** upon it, then the minimum measure for it to become a primary source of ritual impurity is **three** handbreadths. If he intended it **for sitting,** the minimum measure is one **handbreadth.** If he intended to use it **for holding,** it becomes impure in **any measure.**

Rabbi Shimon explains the rationale: *mipnei sheru'in letamei bemoshav* — all of these materials share the capacity to receive *tumas moshav*, the *tumah* conveyed when a *zav* or *zavah* sits upon them. Because they all share this function, they are not conceptually distinct categories of *tumah*. They are generically "objects one sits upon," and therefore, parallel to the earlier discussion of derivative *tumah*, their generic shared character permits *tziruf* — again, only toward the more lenient threshold, not the more stringent one.

The Gemara introduces a significant refinement: *kata'ah mikulam shelosha*. If one intentionally cuts pieces of these materials — one consciously cuts a piece out of each — the required sizes change. The principle is that when someone deliberately cuts a piece to a specific size for a specific purpose, the subjective act of assigning significance to that piece dramatically reduces the objective minimum size required for *tumah* reception. Leftover scraps that remain after a garment is made carry *tumah* only if they meet the larger standard dimensions; but a piece consciously cut and set aside for a designated use acquires *tumah* significance at much smaller sizes.

### Four Use Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לַאֲחִיזָה? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: שֶׁכֵּן עוֹמֵד לְנַוְולָה. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְקוֹצְצֵי תְאֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason for the *halakha* in the case where one intended to use it **for holding?** Why is this cloth classified as a garment? **Reish Lakish says** that **Rabbi Yannai says:** It is considered a garment **because it stands** to be used as protection for the hands **when weaving [*lenavla*],** so that the weaver does not cut his fingers when straightening the threads. It **was taught in a *baraita*:** It is considered a garment **since it is suited for fig cutters** to place the material on their hands, to prevent them from getting dirty.

Four categories of use are enumerated, each with its own *shiur*. For *mishkav* — something to lie upon, such as a blanket or pillow — three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* suffices. For *moshav* — something to sit upon — only one *tefach* is required, since even a very small pad can serve as a seat. For *achiza* — something to grip or hold — *kol shehu*, the smallest amount, is sufficient. A further category, *machnica*, likewise requires only a minimal size.

### Thimble/String Uses

The term *achiza* is clarified by a teaching of Reish Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yannai: *sheken omeid lenavla*. The commentators, including *Tosafot*, explain this as deriving from the root meaning to weave — one who works a loom uses taut, sharp threads that can cut the fingers, and therefore wraps a small piece of cloth around a finger as a kind of thimble. For such a use, the smallest piece of cloth is entirely sufficient to receive *tumah*. Similarly, those who harvest figs wrap small pieces of cloth around their fingers to keep the sticky sap from their hands. In all such cases, since the piece has been consciously designated for a specific purposeful use, even a tiny size acquires the status necessary for *tumah* reception.

With this the *perek* of *kodshei mizbeiach* concludes — *hadran alach kodshei mizbeiach* — and attention turns to the new *perek*, which brings us at last to the central act of *meilah* itself.

## Meilah Core Concept

### Property to Act Shift

The preceding *dafim* of the tractate have been largely concerned with the objects of *kedushah* — what qualifies as *kodshei hashem*, what belongs to *kodshei kalim* as opposed to *kodshei kodashim*, what happens to *kodashim she-meitu* and *kodashim she-nifselu*. The new *perek* makes a decisive turn: from the properties of consecrated objects to the act of *meilah* itself. The discussion of *hana'ah* and *gzel* — of deriving benefit versus taking possession — which has run as a conceptual thread throughout, now comes to the fore directly.

### Benefit Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא פָּגַם – מָעַל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ פְּגָם – לֹא מָעַל, עַד שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם. וְשֶׁאֵין בּוֹ פְּגָם, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה – מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who derives benefit equal to** the value of **one *peruta* from a consecrated item, even though he did not damage** it, is **liable for** its **misuse;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say:** With regard to **any** consecrated **item that has** the potential to be **damaged,** one is **not liable for misuse until he** causes it one *peruta* of **damage; and** with regard to an item **that does not have** the potential to be **damaged, once he derives benefit** from it he is **liable for misuse.**

The mishnah opens: *ha-ne'eneh min ha-hekdesh shaveh peruta* — one who derives benefit from *hekdesh* to the value of a *peruta* has committed *meilah*. The minimum quantum is a *peruta*, and as established in earlier portions of the tractate, *kodshei mizbeiach* and *kodshei bedek ha-bayis* combine with one another to reach this threshold.

### Akiva/Chachamim Dispute

*Af al pi shelo pagam, ma'al* — even if the act caused no damage to the consecrated object, *meilah* has been committed. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva. The *Chachamim* distinguish: *kol davar she-yesh bo pegam* — anything whose normal mode of use is accompanied by wear and tear on the object, *lo ma'al ad she-yifgom* — there is no *meilah* until some actual diminishment of the object occurs. *U-ve-she-ein bo pegam, keivan she-ne'eneh, ma'al* — for objects whose use does not normally cause wear and tear, liability obtains as soon as any benefit is derived.

The *Chachamim* are thus not rejecting the centrality of *hana'ah*; the concept remains foundational. Rather, they hold that when *hana'ah* is of the kind that in its normal exercise is accompanied by *pegam*, that *pegam* must also occur before *meilah* is established. The Gemara will work to narrow the apparent dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*, ultimately reducing it to a technical disagreement about borderline cases.

### No-Damage Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? נָתְנָה קַטְלָא בְּצַוָּארָהּ, טַבַּעַת בְּיָדָהּ, שָׁתָה בְּכוֹס שֶׁל זָהָב, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה – מָעַל. לָבַשׁ בְּחָלוּק, כִּסָּה בְּטַלִּית, בִּיקַּע בְּקַרְדּוֹם – לֹא מָעַל עַד שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: **How so?** If a woman **placed** a consecrated gold **chain [*ketala*] around her neck,** or a gold **ring on her hand,** i.e., her finger, or if **one drank from** a consecrated **gold cup,** since they are not damaged through use, **once he derives benefit** equal to the value of one *peruta* from them, he is **liable for misuse.** If **one wore** a consecrated **robe, covered** himself **with** a consecrated **garment,** or **chopped** wood **with** a consecrated **ax,** he is **not liable for misuse until he** causes them one *peruta* of **damage.**

The mishnah provides clear illustrations of objects in each category. Where no wear and tear accompanies use: a woman took a gold necklace belonging to *hekdesh* and wore it around her neck; a ring and placed it on her finger; a golden cup from which she drank — *keivan shehenetat, ma'alah*. As soon as benefit is derived, *meilah* is established, because a gold necklace does not diminish from being worn, a gold cup does not diminish from being drunk from.

### Wear-Tear Examples

By contrast: *lavash bechaluk* — someone wore a garment belonging to *hekdesh*; *kissa be-tallit* — covered himself with a shawl; *bika be-kardom* — chopped wood with a hatchet of *hekdesh* — *lo ma'al ad she-yifgom*. Even if the person derived a full *peruta* of benefit, if no actual wear and tear occurred, *meilah* has not been committed. These are objects whose normal use wears them down — fabric frays, metal tools dull — and therefore the *pegam* must accompany the *hana'ah*.

### Animal Status Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הַחַטָּאת, כְּשֶׁהִיא חַיָּה – לֹא מָעַל, עַד שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם. כְּשֶׁהִיא מֵתָה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה – מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **One who derives benefit from a sin offering while it is alive** is **not liable for misuse until he** causes it one *peruta* of **damage. When it is dead, once he derives benefit** equal to the value of **one *peruta*** from it, he is **liable for misuse.**

The mishnah then introduces a distinction within a single category of object. *Ha-ne'heneh min ha-chattat keshehi chaya, lo ma'al ad she-yifgom* — one who derives benefit from a living *chattat* animal does not commit *meilah* until some *pegam* occurs, since riding an animal or otherwise using it normally involves some physical diminishment, even if temporary and recoverable. *Keshehi meita, keivan she-nehene, ma'al* — once the animal is dead, benefit alone suffices.

This raises an immediate difficulty: the beginning of the tractate discussed the principle that *kodashim she-meitu yatze'u mi-yedei me'ilah* — consecrated animals that died are removed from the laws of *meilah*. The answer, as the Gemara had already indicated at the very opening of the tractate, is that the *meilah* applicable to a dead consecrated animal is rabbinic — *meilah devar torah* does not apply, but a rabbinic form does.

The more conceptually interesting point is why the *meilah* of a dead *hekdesh* animal does not require *pegam*. One might think that since using a carcass normally involves *pegam*, *pegam* should be required here as well. The answer appears to be that *pegam* is not measured by the generic question of whether this type of object normally incurs diminishment through this type of use. Rather, *pegam* is measured from the perspective of *hekdesh* itself: does the act cause a loss to *hekdesh*? A dead consecrated animal is worthless to *hekdesh* — it has no function, no value it can yield. There is therefore no concept of *pegam* applicable to it, and consequently any act of *hana'ah* triggers liability immediately. This understanding — that *pegam* is assessed from *hekdesh*'s perspective, not generically — will bear further examination as the Gemara develops.

The deeper conceptual point underlying the *Chachamim*'s requirement of *pegam* is this: *meilah* is fundamentally about *hana'ah* — about taking the benefit that belongs to *hekdesh*. But in those cases where *hana'ah* is normally inseparable from wear and tear on the object, the question arises whether one has truly "taken" something from *hekdesh* if the object is returned intact. The analogy to *zeh nehene ve-zeh lo chaser* — deriving benefit from another's property without causing the owner any loss — is relevant here, though its applications are limited. When benefit is received without any accompanying loss, one might argue in certain contexts that nothing has truly been taken. The *pegam* requirement addresses precisely this: for objects that normally depreciate in use, *hekdesh* has not truly suffered a taking unless that depreciation occurs. For objects that do not depreciate — or whose depreciation is irrelevant, as with a carcass — the taking is complete with the benefit alone.

The question of how to measure the required *shiur* is itself instructive. The *Rambam* holds that *hana'ah* and *pegam* must each independently reach the value of a *peruta*. *Rabbeinu Gershom* appears to hold that a half-*peruta* of *hana'ah* and a half-*peruta* of *pegam* may combine. The plain sense of the mishnah seems to suggest that the *hana'ah* must reach a *peruta* while the *pegam* need only be *kol shehu* — any discernible diminishment — since it is the *hana'ah* that is the operative concept and the *pegam* serves merely as a qualifying condition establishing that a true taking occurred.

## Torah Source Derivation

### Shinui Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״נֶפֶשׁ״ – אֶחָד הַיָּחִיד, וְאֶחָד הַנָּשִׂיא, וְאֶחָד הַמָּשִׁיחַ. ״כִּי תִמְעֹל מַעַל״, אֵין ״מַעַל״ אֶלָּא שִׁנּוּי, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי תִשְׂטֶה אִשְׁתּוֹ וּמָעֲלָה בוֹ מַעַל״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּמְעֲלוּ בֵּאלֹהֵי אֲבֹתֵיהֶם וַיִּזְנוּ אַחֲרֵי הַבְּעָלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to misuse of consecrated property, the verse states: "If **anyone** commits a misuse, and sins through error, in the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). It is derived from the general term "anyone" that this *halakha* applies **whether** the guilty person is a common **individual, and whether** he is **the king, and whether** he is **the anointed** High Priest. Although in other contexts they have obligations to bring different offerings, these individuals have a uniform liability with regard to the sin of misuse. The *baraita* further interprets the verse: "If anyone **commits a misuse [*ma'al*],** and sins" (Leviticus 5:15). The term ***ma'al* means nothing other than deviation** from the norm. **And similarly,** the verse **states** with regard to a woman suspected of adultery: **"If any man's wife goes aside, and acts unfaithfully [*ma'al*] against him"** (Numbers 5:12). **And** it **states** with regard to idol worship: **"And they broke faith [*vayimalu*] with the God of their fathers, and went astray after** the gods of the peoples of the land, whom God destroyed before them" (I Chronicles 5:25).

The Gemara now derives the laws of *meilah* directly from their scriptural sources. The *baraisa* begins from the verse *nefesh ki sim'ol ma'al* — *echad yachid ve-echad nasi ve-echad mashiach*: the word *nefesh*, "a person," encompasses layperson, king, and *Kohen Gadol* alike. The various distinctions in *korban* obligations that apply to different classes of individuals — a *nasi*, a *kohen mashiach* — do not affect the laws of *meilah*; all are equally subject.

The key term is *ma'al*: *ein ma'al ela shinui* — *ma'al* means nothing other than diversion, a change of direction from the proper destination. The *baraisa* does not simply assert this; it demonstrates it from two other scriptural usages of the root.

### Sotah Pleasure Diversion

The first: *ish ish ki tisteh ishto uma'alah vo ma'al* — when a woman is *sotah*, the text uses the same root *ma'al*. What is the *ma'al* of a *sotah*? The pleasure — the *hana'ah* — that belonged rightfully to her husband has been diverted. It was redirected from its proper recipient to another. This is one conceptual model of *meilah* as *shinui*: diversion of *hana'ah* from its proper destination. *Tosafot* develops this at length, noting that this form of *shinui* is pleasure-based — it is the enjoyment itself that constitutes the diversion.

### Avodah Zarah Ownership

The second: *vayimalu beilohei avoteihem vayiznu acharei ha-ba'alim* — they committed *meilah* against the God of their fathers and went after the *ba'alim*. Here the *ma'al* is not about pleasure. It is about possession and belonging. The people of Israel were under God's dominion; in turning to *avodah zarah*, they were removed from His possession and transferred, as it were, to the domain of the *ba'alim*. This is a second conceptual model of *meilah* as *shinui*: diversion of ownership, of belonging — taking something from the domain of one and placing it in the domain of another.

### Two Models Explained

*Tosafot* identifies these two examples as reflecting two distinct models of *meilah*, both of which find expression in the laws of *meilah* from *hekdesh*. The first is *hana'ah*-based: the benefit that belongs to *hekdesh* — or that *hekdesh* would yield — is diverted and enjoyed by a private individual. The second is possession-based, closer to *gezeilah*: the object of *hekdesh* is removed from the domain of *hekdesh* and brought into private possession.

These two models are not merely theoretical; they shape the understanding of the entire tractate. The tension between them — is *meilah* about the *hana'ah* being diverted or about the object being taken? — maps onto the debates about *pegam*, about *shliach*, about what constitutes a qualifying act. Even in those cases where *hana'ah* is the operative measure, the *meilah* is not structured like the *issur hana'ah* of *avodah zarah*, where benefit is simply prohibited as taboo. Rather, the *hana'ah* is framed as taking something from *hekdesh* — diverting what belongs to *hekdesh* — and the obligation to repay the value reflects this. One pays back not because a prohibition was violated but because something was taken. The *hana'ah* model and the *gezeilah* model converge: even when the act is measured by benefit rather than by possession of the object, it is still conceptualized as a taking.

## Terumah Parallel

### Chet Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל פָּגַם וְלֹא נֶהֱנָה, אוֹ נֶהֱנָה וְלֹא פָּגַם, וּבִמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע, וּבְשָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְחָטְאָה״. נֶאֱמַר: ״חֵטְא״ בִּתְרוּמָה, וְנֶאֱמַר: ״חֵטְא״ בִּמְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that the comparison between misuse and idol worship teaches that one is liable for misuse even if he **damaged** the consecrated item **but did not derive benefit** from it, as that is the *halakha* with regard to idol worship, where one changes worship of God to idol worship without deriving benefit. **Or,** in light of the comparison between misuse and suspected adultery, it might have been thought that one would be liable for misuse if **he derived benefit** from the consecrated item **but did not damage** it, just as the adulteress derives benefit from the illicit act of intercourse without suffering any physical damage. **And** perhaps one should be liable for misuse **of** an item **attached to the ground. And in** the case of **an agent who** committed misuse while **he performed his** assigned **agency,** it might be thought that the agent himself, rather than the one who appointed him, should be liable for the misuse. **The *halakha* in these cases is derived from the fact that** the verse **states:** "If anyone commits a misuse, **and sins** through error, in the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). The word **"sin" is stated with regard to *teruma*,** in the verse: "And you shall bear no sin by reason of it, as you have set apart from it its best" (Numbers 18:32); **and "sin" is** also **stated with regard to misuse** of consecrated property. Several *halakhot* are derived from this verbal analogy.

The *baraisa* establishes a *gezerah shavah* between *meilah* and *terumah* on the shared word *chet* (sin). The Torah uses *chet* in the context of *meilah* — *ki sim'ol ma'al bishgaga mi-kodshei Hashem* — and uses *chet* in the context of *terumah* — *velo yisu alav chet*. This verbal analogy authorizes deriving the operational criteria of *meilah* from the model of *terumah*.

### Pegam/Hana'ah Coexistence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּתְרוּמָה, פּוֹגֵם וְנֶהֱנֶה, וּמִי שֶׁפָּגַם נֶהֱנֶה, וּבַדָּבָר שֶׁפּוֹגֵם בּוֹ נֶהֱנֶה, וּפְגִימָתוֹ וַהֲנָאָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד, וּבְתָלוּשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע, וּבְשָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – אַף ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּמְעִילָה, פּוֹגֵם וְנֶהֱנֶה, וּמִי שֶׁפָּגַם נֶהֱנֶה, וּבַדָּבָר שֶׁפּוֹגֵם בּוֹ נֶהֱנֶה, וּפְגִימָתוֹ וַהֲנָאָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד, וּבְתָלוּשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע, וּבְשָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* elaborates: **Just as** with regard to the word **"sin" stated with regard to *teruma*,** when someone sins by partaking of forbidden *teruma*, it is a circumstance in which one **damages and derives benefit, and** the **one who damaged** the *teruma* is the same person who **derived benefit; and** with regard to *teruma*, one is liable only if **he derives benefit from the same item that he damages; and** he is liable in the case of *teruma* only if **his damage and his benefit** occur **simultaneously; and** the status of *teruma* applies only **to** an item **detached from the ground; and** the status of *teruma* takes effect **in** the case of **an agent who performed his** assigned **agency** in designating an item *teruma*, the same applies to misuse. **So too,** the word **"sin" stated with regard to misuse** applies specifically when the individual both **damages and derives benefit** from consecrated items; **and** when the **one who damaged** also **derived benefit** from it himself; **and he derives benefit from the** same **item that he damages; and his damage and his deriving benefit** occur **simultaneously; and** misuse applies only **to** an item **detached from the ground; and** misuse is committed by the one appointing the agent **in** the case of **an agent who performed his** assigned **agency.**

What does the *terumah* model teach? By *terumah*, the *Torah* describes the scenario of eating *terumah* — an act that is quintessentially a *hana'ah* and that is inherently accompanied by *pegam*, since food is consumed and thereby diminished. The *terumah* model thus establishes that when *pegam* is relevant, *hana'ah* and *pegam* must coexist in the same act: *pgimato ve-hana'ato ke-echad* — the diminishment and the benefit must occur simultaneously.

### Five Criteria

From the *terumah* parallel, five criteria for *meilah* are derived. First, the same person must be both *pogem* and *nehene* — the one who causes the diminishment must be the one who derives the benefit. Second, the *pegam* must occur in the same object from which the *hana'ah* is derived — one cannot combine a *pegam* to one object of *hekdesh* with a *hana'ah* from another. Third, *pegam* and *hana'ah* must be simultaneous — it does not suffice for the *pegam* to precede or follow the *hana'ah* in a separate act. Fourth, the *meilah* applies only to *tolash min ha-karka* — to things already detached from the ground, just as *terumah* applies only to harvested produce. Fifth, there is a principle of *shliach* by *meilah*, derived from the fact that *terumah* also operates through the framework of agency.

### Multi-Actor Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אוֹכֵל, וְנֶהֱנֶה בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ פְּגָם, מִנַּיִן? אֲכִילָתוֹ וַאֲכִילַת חֲבֵרוֹ, הֲנָיָיתוֹ וַהֲנָיַית חֲבֵרוֹ, הֲנָאָתוֹ וַאֲכִילַת חֲבֵרוֹ, אֲכִילָתוֹ וַהֲנָיַית חֲבֵרוֹ שֶׁמִּצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה, אֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּמְעֹל מַעַל״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: From the verbal analogy **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for misuse if **he eats** consecrated items when he is not permitted to do so, similar to the case of *teruma*. **But** with regard to one who **derives benefit from an item that does not have** the potential to be **damaged, from where** do I derive that this also constitutes misuse? Similarly, if one eats from a consecrated item and gives to another person to eat, where there is **his consumption and another's consumption;** or if he derives benefit and gives to another to derive benefit, where there is **his** deriving **benefit and another's** deriving **benefit;** and likewise **his** deriving **benefit and another's consumption;** and **his consumption and another's** deriving **benefit;** the *halakha* is **that** in all these cases, benefit and consumption **combine with one another, even** over **a long** period of **time** on the same day, to amount to the value of one *peruta* that effects liability for misuse. **From where** is this *halakha* derived? **The verse states:** "If anyone **commits a misuse."** This general and inclusive clause teaches that one is liable for misusing one *peruta* worth of consecrated property **in any case,** i.e., even if he gave it to another and one of them ate while the other derived benefit, and even if it took some time on that day to use the value of one *peruta*.

The *baraisa* then asks: we know the model from eating — one person eats and derives benefit. How do we know that *achilato ve-achilat chavero*, *hana'ato ve-hana'at chavero* — one person's eating combined with another person's eating, or one person's benefit combined with another's benefit — can combine to reach the *shiur* of a *peruta*? And not only the same type of act: *hana'ah* of one person combining with *achilah* of another, or *achilah* of one with *hana'ah* of another? The answer comes from the verse: *ki sim'ol ma'al* — *mikol makom*, regardless, in any configuration. The combinations are not limited to the single-actor eating model.

One might ask: if Charlie derives the benefit while I arranged for it, why is the *meilah* attributed to me rather than to Charlie? The answer depends on the structure of the act. If I send Charlie as a *shaliach*, the principle of *shliach* in *meilah* — unlike the general rule of *ein shaliach li-dvar aveirah* — applies and the *meilah* falls on me. But there is another possibility: if I myself apply the oil to Charlie, or feed him directly, then I am the one performing the act of taking from *hekdesh*, even though Charlie is the recipient of the *hana'ah*. *Meilah* is about taking from *hekdesh*; one need not be the one who benefits — one must be the one who takes.

### Temporal Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּתְרוּמָה – לֹא צֵירַף שְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת כְּאֶחָד, אַף ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּמְעִילָה – לֹא צֵירַף שְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת כְּאַחַת. מִנַּיִן אָכַל הַיּוֹם וְאָכַל לְמָחָר, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּמְעֹל מַעַל״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: **If** there is a comparison between *teruma* and misuse, one can claim as follows: **Just as** in the case of the word **"sin" that is stated with regard to *teruma*,** the Torah does **not combine two** distinct acts of **consumption as one** prohibited act, **so too,** perhaps with regard to the word **"sin" that is stated with regard to misuse,** the Torah does **not combine two** acts of **consumption as one. From where** is it derived that if one **ate** half of the minimum amount of an olive-bulk **today and ate** another half **tomorrow, even if much time** has passed, the distinct acts of misuse combine and are considered one act, which means that the one who did so is liable for misuse? **The verse states:** "If anyone **commits a misuse."** The inclusive language of the verse teaches that he is liable **in any case.**

A further extension: *minayin akhal hayom ve-akhal lemachar ve-afilu zman merubbeh* — how do we know that two acts of eating or benefit, even separated by a long time, combine? Perhaps *meilah* should follow the model of ordinary *achilah*, which requires *kezayit bikhdei achilat pras* — a *kezayis* consumed within the time it takes to eat half a *pras*. The *baraisa* derives from *ki sim'ol ma'al — mikol makom*: even after many days, acts combine. *Achilato ve-achilat chavero, afilu mikan ve-ad shalosh shanim* — one person's act and another person's act combine even if separated by as long as three years.

### Taking Value Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּתְרוּמָה – עֵד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִן הַקֹּדֶשׁ לַחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If** it is derived from the verbal analogy that there are several *halakhot* common to both *teruma* and committing misuse, one can claim as follows: **Just as** in the case of the word **"sin" that is stated with regard to *teruma*,** a non-priest who partakes of *teruma* is not liable **until** the *teruma* **leaves from** the domain of **the sacred to** the domain of **the non-sacred,** as one who partakes of *teruma* becomes its owner, the same should apply to misuse.

The conceptual reason for this temporal combination is clarifying. *Meilah* is not a *ma'aseh achilah* — an act of eating — whose *shiur* depends on the physical parameters of a single eating event. It is a *ma'aseh lekichah* — a taking. And a taking is measured not by the act's temporal or physical unity but by how much value was removed from *hekdesh*. The operative question is always: was a *peruta*'s worth diverted from *hekdesh*? Because the measure is value, acts accumulate regardless of who performed them or when, as long as each act constituted a taking from *hekdesh*.

This returns to the foundational statement with which the Gemara opened its analysis: *ein me'ilah ela shinui* — *meilah* is nothing other than diversion. What is diverted may be the *hana'ah* itself, as in the model of the *sotah*. What is diverted may be the ownership and dominion, as in the model of *avodah zarah*. In either case, the act of *meilah* is defined by the removal of something from its proper place — from *hekdesh* — and its redirection elsewhere. That is the conceptual core of the entire tractate.
