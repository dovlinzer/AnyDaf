# Meilah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul & Nosar Tziruf (II)

### Pigul/Nosar Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפִּיגּוּל וְהַנּוֹתָר – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שְׁנֵי שֵׁמוֹת. הַשֶּׁרֶץ וְהַנְּבֵלָה, וְכֵן הַנְּבֵלָה וּבְשַׂר הַמֵּת – אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה לְטַמֵּא, אֲפִילּוּ בַּקַּל שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Sacrificial meat that is ***piggul* and** sacrificial meat that is ***notar* do not join together** to constitute the requisite measure of an olive-bulk, **due to** the fact **that they** belong to **two** separate **categories** of prohibition. The flesh of the carcass of **the creeping animal and** the flesh of **the animal carcass, and likewise** the flesh of **the animal carcass and the flesh of the corpse, do not join together to transmit ritual impurity,** not **even for the** more **lenient of the two** impurities, i.e., the impurity that requires the greater measure.

The final *mishnayot* of the preceding chapter conclude the extended discussion of *tziruf* — the combining of different items to reach a requisite *shiur* — before the tractate turns to the heart of the laws of *me'ilah* itself.

The *mishnah* states: *pigul ve-nosar ein mitztarfin* — *pigul* and *nosar* do not combine with one another. Although it has been established that different instances of *pigul* from different *korbanot*, or different portions of the same *korban*, may combine, and similarly for *nosar*, the two categories themselves are distinct prohibitions and do not combine. If one has half a *kezayit* of *pigul* and half a *kezayit* of *nosar*, one has not violated either prohibition in full and therefore does not incur lashes. *Mipnei shehein shnei sheimos* — they carry two different names, two distinct prohibitions.

The *mishnah* continues with *tumah* combinations. *Ha-sheretz ve-ha-neveilah* — a creeping creature and an animal carcass — and similarly *ha-neveilah u-vasar ha-meis* — a carcass and the flesh of a human corpse — do not combine with one another, *ein mitztarfin zeh im zeh*, even for the lighter of the two. Although *neveilah* and *meis* share the same *shiur* of a *kezayit*, they represent fundamentally different categories of *tumah*: a *neveilah* is an *av ha-tumah*, while a *meis* is an *avi avot ha-tumah* whose contact imparts seven-day *tumah* and other stringencies. They are not merely variations of degree but distinct ontological categories, and therefore even if one has half a *kezayit* of each, they cannot be combined and credited even toward the lesser.

### Tumas Yadayim Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְטוּמְאַת הַיָּדַיִם, דְּמִדְּרַבָּנַן הִיא. אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן אֲכִילָה – מִצְטָרְפִין. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל כִּי קֹדֶשׁ הוּא״ – כֹּל שֶׁבַּקֹּדֶשׁ פָּסוּל, בָּא הַכָּתוּב לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The mishna **taught** that *piggul* and *notar* do not join together **only with regard to** the ritual **impurity of the hands** if one touched them, which **is by rabbinic law. But with regard to** the **matter of eating, they do join together. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The verse states with regard to leftover sacrificial food from meat and bread: **"It shall not be eaten because it is sacred"** (Exodus 29:34). This teaches with regard to **anything sacred** that has been **disqualified** for whatever reason, that **the verse comes to apply a prohibition with regard to its consumption.**

The *Gemara* opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *lo shanu ela tumas ha-yadayim mi-de-rabbanan* — the rule that *pigul* and *nosar* do not combine applies specifically to the rabbinic *tumah* of hands. This requires some context.

*Pigul* and *nosar* carry not only the prohibition of eating but also a rabbinic *tumah*. Ordinarily, any contact with a biblically impure substance renders the entire body *tamei*. However, there are certain rabbinic enactments of *tumah* that are limited to the hands alone — *tumas yadayim*. The *tumah* of *pigul* and *nosar* is of this lesser, rabbinic variety. One might ask why the Rabbis chose this particular mechanism. The sociological reality, as the *Gemara* implies, was that the *kohanim* were extraordinarily scrupulous about *tumah* concerns in a way that outstripped their vigilance about other prohibitions, even those carrying the penalty of *kares*. By declaring that *pigul* and *nosar* render the hands *tamei*, the Rabbis ensured that the *kohanim*, in their intense concern about ritual purity, would refrain from handling — and thus from coming to eat — these disqualified sacred items. The irony is that people compartmentalize their religious rigor, often being meticulous about ritual-adjacent matters while treating graver ethical or biblical prohibitions with less urgency. The same dynamic operated here.

In any case, this rabbinic *tumah* — being of lesser character — is what the *mishnah* addresses when it says *pigul* and *nosar* do not combine.

### Achilah Prohibition

However, Shmuel adds: *aval min ha-achilah mitztarfin* — but with respect to the prohibition of eating, they do combine. A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Eliezer derives from the verse *lo yochal ki kodesh heim* — "it shall not be eaten for they are holy" — that there is a generic negative prohibition applicable to anything that has become *pasul* on account of its *kedushah* status, the classic examples being *pigul* and *nosar*. This generic prohibition of eating all *psulei ha-mukdashin* means that *pigul* and *nosar*, despite being distinct specific prohibitions, share a common *issur achilah* under which they can combine. The combination does not produce liability for *karet* — for that, one would need a *kezayit* of the specific item — but it does produce a transgression of this generic prohibition, for which one would receive *malkot*.

## Tumah Sources & Leve (II)

### Av vs. Velad HTumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹכֶל שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּוְלַד הַטּוּמְאָה – מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה לְטַמֵּא בַּקַּל שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם. כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִים מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה לִפְסוֹל הַגְּוִיָּיה כְּבַחֲצִי פְרָס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The food that became ritually impure** through contact **with a primary source of ritual impurity,** thereby assuming first-degree ritual impurity, **and** the food **that became ritually impure** through contact **with a secondary source of ritual impurity,** thereby assuming second-degree ritual impurity, **join together** to constitute the requisite measure of an egg-bulk **to transmit impurity in accordance with the** more **lenient of the two,** i.e., second-degree ritual impurity. **All the** ritually impure **foods join together** to constitute the requisite measure **to disqualify the body [*hageviyya*]** of one who eats **half of a half-loaf-bulk [*peras*]** of the impure foods from partaking of *teruma*.

A subsequent *mishnah* addresses a different combination: food that has become *tamei* through contact with an *av ha-tumah* and food that has become *tamei* through contact with a *velad ha-tumah* — a derivative source of impurity. *Mitztarfin zeh im zeh l'tamei b'kal she-bishneihem* — they combine, though only to produce the lesser of the two grades of *tumah*.

To illustrate: if food touches a *neveilah* directly, it becomes a *rishon l'tumah*. If different food touches a person who had touched a *neveilah*, it becomes a *sheni l'tumah*. Suppose one has only half a *kebeitzah* of each — the *shiur* for food *tumah* being a full *kebeitzah* — these two half-portions combine to constitute the requisite *shiur*, though only at the level of a *sheni*, not a *rishon*.

### Derivative Tumah Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה טַעַם? שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁיֵּעָשֶׂה רִאשׁוֹן. וּמִי קָא עָבֵיד שֵׁנִי רִאשׁוֹן? הָא לָא אֶפְשָׁר! אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִי גָּרַם לַשֵּׁנִי? לָאו רִאשׁוֹן?! רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי, לְגַבֵּי שְׁלִישִׁי – בְּנֵי חֲדָא בִּיקְתָּא אִינּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: What is the reason** that food with first-degree ritual impurity joins together with food that has second-degree ritual impurity? The reason is **that it is possible for** the food with **second-**degree impurity **to render** another food impure with **first-**degree impurity. The Gemara asks: **But can** a food with **second-**degree impurity **render** another food impure with **first-**degree impurity? That is **not possible.** If food impure with second-degree impurity touches other food, it renders that food impure with third-degree impurity, not first-degree impurity. **Rava said** that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: What caused** that food to become impure with **second-**degree impurity? Is it **not** that it was touched by food with **first-**degree impurity? Since they share a common source, they join together. **Rav Ashi** similarly **said:** Food impure with **first-**degree impurity **and** food impure with **second-**degree impurity, **with regard to** food impure with **third-**degree impurity, **are** considered like **members of one house,** i.e., they both lead to third-degree impurity, either directly or indirectly, and for this reason they join together.

This ruling seems at first to contradict the earlier principle that fundamentally different *tumot* do not combine. Two considerations explain the distinction. First, both a *rishon* and a *sheni* share the same *shiur* of a *kebeitzah* and produce broadly similar practical consequences — both are remedied by immersion without requiring nightfall. But more fundamentally, once *tumah* is derivative rather than originary, it loses something of its distinctive identity. A *rishon* and a *sheni* are not themselves sources of *tumah* the way a *neveilah* or *meis* is; they are recipients and transmitters of a *tumah* that originated elsewhere. This generic, abstracted quality is what makes combination possible. To illustrate the principle differently: a *zav* himself cannot enter *Har HaBayit*, and there is even a question whether a *tevul yom* of a *zav* may enter, given that some residue of his original *tumah* remains. But someone who merely touched a *zav* has only derivative *tumah* and may enter *Har HaBayit* freely, because that *tumah* has lost the character of *tumas zav* entirely.

The *Gemara* then asks the precise meaning of Rabbi Shimon's explanation: *she-efshar l'sheni she-yehei rishon* — that it is possible for the *sheni* to become a *rishon*. The *Gemara* challenges this: surely a *sheni* cannot on its own become a *rishon*. Rava reframes the teaching: *mi garam l'sheni? lav rishon?* — what caused the food to become a *sheni* if not the *rishon*? Because the *rishon* is the conceptual source of the *sheni's* status, the *rishon* naturally combines with it. The *sheni* is, in a real sense, a derivative of the *rishon*, and therefore the *rishon* is connected enough to the *sheni* to combine at the *sheni's* level.

Rav Ashi offers an additional formulation: *rishon v'sheni legabi shelishi benei chada bikta inun* — a *rishon* and a *sheni* are, from the perspective of a *shelishi*, members of the same household. The practical implication is that what a *sheni* typically seeks to render *tamei* is *terumah*, which it would render a *shelishi*. From the vantage point of the *terumah* being rendered *tamei*, both a *rishon* and a *sheni* occupy the same functional category — both have the capacity to defile it. As Rashi notes, this is not so much a different answer as another way of articulating the same underlying principle: that derivative *tumah* is generic enough in character that a *rishon* and a *sheni* can combine.

## Foods/Liquids Combination

### Food Shiurim Combos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִים מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה לִפְסוֹל הַגְּוִיָּיה כְּבַחֲצִי פְרָס. כְּבִמְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת לְעֵירוּב, וּכְבֵיצָה לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין, וְכִגְרוֹגֶרֶת לְהוֹצָאַת שַׁבָּת, וּכְכוֹתֶבֶת בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **All the** ritually impure **foods join together** to constitute the requisite measure **to disqualify the body [*hageviyya*]** of one who eats **half of a half-loaf-bulk [*peras*]** of the impure foods from partaking of *teruma*. Likewise, all foods join together to constitute the requisite measure **of food** sufficient for **two meals, to** establish **a joining of** Shabbat **boundaries; and** to form the requisite measure **of an egg-bulk, to render** an item **impure** with **the ritual impurity of food; and** to form the measure **of** a dried **fig-bulk,** which establishes liability **for carrying out** food on **Shabbat; and** to form the volume **of a large date,** which establishes liability for eating **on Yom Kippur.**

The *mishnah* proceeds to enumerate a series of *tziruf* rules for foods and liquids in various halachic contexts. *Kol ha-ochlin mitztarfin zeh im zeh lifsol et ha-giviyah k'chatzi pras* — all foods combine with one another to render the body *tamei*, provided one has consumed a total of half a *pras* of *tamei* food. This is a rabbinic decree: while food cannot render a person *tamei* at the biblical level through contact, the Rabbis established that consuming sufficient *tamei* food renders one *tamei*. The *shiur* is half a *pras*, which corresponds to approximately four or five *kezayitim*. All different types of *tamei* food — bread, fruit, whatever the case — combine toward this quantity.

The *mishnah* continues with related shiurim: *u-k'mazon shtei seudot l'eiruv* — for the purposes of *eiruv chatzeirot*, different types of food combine, provided their total equals two meals' worth. *U-k'beitzah l'tamei tumat ochlin* — for food to be susceptible to *tumah* or to transmit it, a *kebeitzah*-worth of different foods can combine. *U-k'grogeret l'hotza'at Shabbos* — the minimum quantity for the prohibition of carrying on *Shabbos* is the size of a pressed fig (*grogeret*), and different substances combine toward that *shiur*. *U-k'khotevet b'Yom Ha-Kippurim* — the quantity of food one must consume on *Yom Kippur* to incur liability is the volume of a large date (*khotevet*), and again different foods combine.

### Liquids Shiurim Combos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִין מִצְטָרְפִין זוֹ עִם זוֹ לִפְסוֹל אֶת הַגְּוִיָּיה בִּרְבִיעִית, וְכִמְלֹא לוּגְמָא בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **All the liquids join together** to constitute the requisite measure **to disqualify the body of** one who drinks **a quarter-*log*** of ritually impure liquid from partaking of *teruma*; **and** to constitute the measure **of a cheekful,** which establishes liability for drinking **on Yom Kippur.**

Similarly for liquids: *kol ha-mashkin mitztarfin zeh im zeh lifsol et ha-giviyah b'revi'it* — all *tamei* liquids combine to produce the rabbinic *tumah* of the body, if one has consumed a *revi'it* — approximately 3.2 ounces — of *tamei* liquid. Different types of liquids, whether wine, oil, water, or otherwise, all combine toward that *shiur*. And for *Yom Kippur*, all different types of liquid combine toward *melo lugmav* — the volume that fills one cheek — which is the *shiur* for transgressing the prohibition against drinking.

## Tumah Sources & Leve (II)

### Rishon/Sheni Combine

The *Gemara* returns to the question of *rishon* and *sheni* combining, providing a short discussion that has already been treated above. Rava's formulation — that the *rishon* caused the *sheni* and therefore certainly combines with it — and Rav Ashi's formulation — that *rishon* and *sheni* are *benei chada bikta*, members of the same category, from the perspective of the *shelishi* they seek to defile — both converge on the same point: once *tumah* is derivative, it becomes generic and abstract, losing the distinctive character of its originating source. For this reason, a *rishon* and a *sheni* can combine, though only at the level of the lesser, the *sheni*.

## Pigul & Nosar Tziruf (II)

### Orlah & Kilayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם – מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. גְּמָ׳ וּמִי צָרִיךְ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְצָרוֹפֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַכּוֹת? תְּנִי: אֵין צְרִיכִין לְצָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*]** (see Leviticus 19:23), **and diverse kinds,**i.e., grain sown **in a vineyard** (see Deuteronomy 22:9) **join together** to constitute the requisite measure to prohibit a mixture that they are mixed into. This applies when the volume of the permitted produce is less than two hundred times the prohibited produce. **Rabbi Shimon says: They do not join together.** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Shimon, do** the two half-measures **need to join** together, in order for one who eats them to be liable? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One who eats **any measure** of a prohibited food is liable **to** receive **lashes,** as the Sages stated the measure of an olive-bulk only with regard to the requirement to bring an offering? The Gemara answers: The wording of the mishna is not precise and must be emended, to **teach** that Rabbi Shimon says: **They do not need to join together,** as one is flogged even for less than the minimum measure.

The *mishnah* at the top of 17a states: *ha-orlah v'kilay ha-kerem mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — *orlah* and *kilay ha-kerem* combine with one another. Rabbi Shimon dissents, stating *ein mitztarfin*.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with Rabbi Shimon's position from a different angle: since Rabbi Shimon holds *kol she-hu l'malkot* — that even the smallest quantity is sufficient for *malkot* — why would the question of *tziruf* even arise for him? If no minimum *shiur* is required, there is no need to combine two partial amounts. The *Gemara* resolves this by rereadinh: instead of *ein mitztarfin* ("they do not combine"), read *ein tzrichin l'tzaref* — "they do not need to combine." According to Rabbi Shimon, *tziruf* is irrelevant because any amount, however small, already incurs *malkot* independently. On Rabbi Shimon's view, one would receive lashes for even a small amount of *orlah* and a small amount of *kilay ha-kerem* separately.

The more substantive question is how the *Tanna Kamma* permits combination of *orlah* and *kilay ha-kerem* at all, given the general rule that distinct prohibitions do not combine. Rabbeinu Gershom understands the combination here as applying to *malkot*, which would be a striking *chiddush* given that they are different *lavin*. The *Or Sameach* cites the *Tosafot Yom Tov* citing a *Yerushalmi* to explain that this reflects the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds that all biblically prohibited substances are governed by a single overarching negative commandment, which therefore enables them to combine. The *Rashash*, by contrast — and the *Rambam* agrees, as noted by ArtScroll — understands the combination here as applying not to *malkot* but to *bitul*: the question is whether a small amount of each, when mixed together into a larger permitted quantity, can join to prevent *bitul* of the whole. *Orlah* and *kilay ha-kerem* share similar *shiurim* and *halachot* of *bitul* in other contexts, making that combination less of a *chiddush* even if it still requires explanation. The position of Rabbeinu Gershom, that these combine for the purpose of lashes, remains a much more far-reaching ruling and one that stands in tension with the normative principle of *ein mitztarfin* for different prohibitions.

## Fabric Tumah Combos

### Fabric Types Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבֶּגֶד וְהַשַּׂק, הַשַּׂק וְהָעוֹר, הָעוֹר וְהַמַּפָּץ – כּוּלָּן מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן רְאוּיִן לִיטָּמֵא בְּמוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A garment must be at least three by three handbreadths in order to become a primary source of ritual impurity, by means of ritual impurity imparted by the treading of a *zav*. A sack made from goats' hair must be at least four by four handbreadths, while an animal hide must be five by five, and a mat six by six. **The garment and the sack, the sack and the hide,** and **the hide and the mat all join together** to constitute the requisite measure to become ritually impure in accordance with the material of the greater measure. **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** that they join together, despite the fact that their requisite measures are not equal? **Because** all the component materials **are fit to become ritually impure** through the ritual impurity imparted **to a seat** upon which a *zav* sits, as they can each be used to patch a saddle or saddlecloth. Since the measure of all these materials is equal in the case of a *zav*, they join together for other forms of ritual impurity as well.

The final *mishnah* of the chapter shifts back to *tumah* and addresses combinations of different fabric types. *Ha-beged v'ha-sak, ha-sak v'ha-or, ha-or v'ha-mapatz, kulam mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — cloth, sackcloth, leather, and a woven reed mat all combine with one another for purposes of *tumah*.

The context is the minimum size required for a material to be considered significant enough to become *tamei*. A *beged* — woven cloth — becomes *tamei* if it is at least three fingerbreadths by three fingerbreadths, a relatively small area. *Sak* — sackcloth — requires four *tefachim* by four *tefachim*, approximately a foot by a foot. Leather (*or*) requires five *tefachim* by five *tefachim*, and a *mapatz* — a mat woven from reeds — requires six *tefachim* by six *tefachim*. These ascending thresholds reflect the coarser and less refined nature of each successive material. The question addressed by the *mishnah* is: if one has a patchwork object composed of several of these materials, each portion being less than the required *shiur* for its own material, do they combine?

The *mishnah* rules that they do combine, though — consistent with the principle discussed earlier regarding derivative *tumah* — they combine only toward the most lenient *shiur* among them, i.e., the largest required *shiur*, that of the *mapatz*. Rabbi Shimon explains the rationale: *mipnei she-re'uyin l'tamei be-moshav* — because all of these materials are fit to receive *tumas moshav*, the impurity transmitted when a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah* sits upon them. Since all of them share this common susceptibility, they are not categorically distinct *tumot* but rather variations of a single category of cloth-like objects capable of serving as a *moshav*. Stripped of their individual identities, they are generic and therefore combinable — the same principle of genericization that permitted combining a *rishon* and a *sheni*.

### Intent in Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: קִיצַּע מִכּוּלָּן שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְעָשָׂה מֵהֶן בֶּגֶד לְמִשְׁכָּב – שְׁלֹשָׁה, לְמוֹשָׁב – טֶפַח, לַאֲחִיזָה – כׇּל שֶׁהוּ. מַאי לַאֲחִיזָה? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: שֶׁכֵּן עוֹמֵד לְנַוְולָה. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְקוֹצְצֵי תְאֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where one **cut from each of** the materials listed in the mishna, i.e., the garment, the sack, the hide, and the mat, a piece of less than **three** by three handbreadths, **and** sewed them together, thereby **fashioning a garment from them;** if he formed the garment **for lying** upon it, then the minimum measure for it to become a primary source of ritual impurity is **three** handbreadths. If he intended it **for sitting,** the minimum measure is one **handbreadth.** If he intended to use it **for holding,** it becomes impure in **any measure.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason for the *halakha* in the case where one intended to use it **for holding?** Why is this cloth classified as a garment? **Reish Lakish says** that **Rabbi Yannai says:** It is considered a garment **because it stands** to be used as protection for the hands **when weaving [*lenavla*],** so that the weaver does not cut his fingers when straightening the threads. It **was taught in a *baraita*:** It is considered a garment **since it is suited for fig cutters** to place the material on their hands, to prevent them from getting dirty.

The *Gemara* then introduces a related *tanna'itic* teaching that significantly qualifies the objective size requirements: *kata'ah mi-kulam sheloshah v'asah meihem beged le-mishkav* — if one deliberately cuts strips from all of these materials and fashions them into a cloth for lying upon, the required minimum size changes dramatically. When a person intentionally cuts a piece to a specific size for a definite purpose, that subjective intent confers significance upon the object, and the objective *shiur* can therefore be much smaller.

The *Gemara* elaborates: if the object is fashioned *le-mishkav* — for lying upon, such as a blanket or pillow — three *tefachim* suffices. *Le-moshav* — for sitting upon — requires only one *tefach*. *La-achizah* — for grasping, i.e., to use as a kind of thimble to protect the fingers — *kol she-hu*, even the smallest amount suffices. Reish Lakish citing Rabbi Yannai explains *achizah*: this refers to work on a loom, where the strong threads can lacerate the fingers and a small scrap of material wrapped around the finger serves as protection. The *Gemara* adds that fig harvesters similarly wrap their fingers to protect against the sticky sap of the figs. In both cases, one deliberately cuts a tiny piece of material for this specific purpose, and that intent grants the smallest scrap sufficient significance to become *tamei*.

The general principle is that the fixed objective *shiurim* apply to incidental or remnant pieces of material whose significance has not been deliberately established. When a person intentionally fashions something of a specific size for a defined use, that intentional use — however small the object — confers the significance required for susceptibility to *tumah*.

## Me'ilah Hana'ah/Pegam

### Peruta Hana'ah Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא פָּגַם – מָעַל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ פְּגָם – לֹא מָעַל, עַד שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם. וְשֶׁאֵין בּוֹ פְּגָם, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה – מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who derives benefit equal to** the value of **one *peruta* from a consecrated item, even though he did not damage** it, is **liable for** its **misuse;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say:** With regard to **any** consecrated **item that has** the potential to be **damaged,** one is **not liable for misuse until he** causes it one *peruta* of **damage; and** with regard to an item **that does not have** the potential to be **damaged, once he derives benefit** from it he is **liable for misuse.**

With the conclusion of the chapter on *tziruf*, the tractate turns to what is arguably its central topic: the act of *me'ilah* itself. The new *mishnah* opens: *ha-ne'eneh min ha-hekdesh shaveh perutah* — one who derives benefit from *hekdesh* to the value of a *perutah* has committed *me'ilah*. The monetary threshold is a *perutah*, and as established earlier, *kodshei mizbei'ach* and *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* combine toward that *shiur*. The *mishnah* then presents a dispute about what else is required beyond the *perutah* of *hana'ah*.

### R. Akiva vs. Chachamim

Rabbi Akiva holds: *af al pi she-lo pagam ma'al* — even if one has not caused any damage or wear to the object, one has committed *me'ilah*. For Rabbi Akiva, the act of *me'ilah* is constituted entirely by the *hana'ah* — the benefit derived from *hekdesh*. The *Chachamim* respond: *kol davar she-yeish bo pegam, lo ma'al ad she-yifgom* — for anything whose normal use entails some diminishment of value, there is no *me'ilah* until that actual diminishment occurs. Conversely, *u-ve-she-ein bo pegam, keivan she-ne'eneh ma'al* — for something whose use does not normally entail any loss of value, *me'ilah* is triggered as soon as one derives benefit.

The *Chachamim's* position thus introduces a distinction based on the nature of the object and the typical character of its use. *Hana'ah* remains the central concept — but when *hana'ah* ordinarily comes paired with *pegam*, that *pegam* must also be present.

### Me'ilah Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? נָתְנָה קַטְלָא בְּצַוָּארָהּ, טַבַּעַת בְּיָדָהּ, שָׁתָה בְּכוֹס שֶׁל זָהָב, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה – מָעַל. לָבַשׁ בְּחָלוּק, כִּסָּה בְּטַלִּית, בִּיקַּע בְּקַרְדּוֹם – לֹא מָעַל עַד שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: **How so?** If a woman **placed** a consecrated gold **chain [*ketala*] around her neck,** or a gold **ring on her hand,** i.e., her finger, or if **one drank from** a consecrated **gold cup,** since they are not damaged through use, **once he derives benefit** equal to the value of one *peruta* from them, he is **liable for misuse.** If **one wore** a consecrated **robe, covered** himself **with** a consecrated **garment,** or **chopped** wood **with** a consecrated **ax,** he is **not liable for misuse until he** causes them one *peruta* of **damage.**

The *mishnah* illustrates with concrete cases. On the side of things with no *pegam* — where *me'ilah* is triggered immediately upon *hana'ah*: *natna katla be-tzavara* — if a woman donned a gold necklace belonging to *hekdesh*; *taba'at be-yado* — or wore a ring; *shata be-khos shel zahav* — or drank from a golden cup. These are objects that sustain no wear or diminishment from such use. Gold does not scratch from being worn briefly; a cup does not deteriorate from a single use. Therefore, *keivan she-henetat ma'ala* — the moment she derived benefit, she has committed *me'ilah*.

On the other side — things where *me'ilah* requires actual *pegam*: if a man *lavash be-chaluk* — wore a garment — or *kissa be-tallit* — wrapped himself in a shawl — or *bika be-kardom* — chopped with a hatchet — all belonging to *hekdesh*, then *lo ma'al ad she-yifgom* — there is no *me'ilah* until some actual wear or diminishment occurs. Garments fray; hatchet blades dull. The *hana'ah* of using these items normally comes at a cost to the object itself, and therefore both elements must be present.

### Dead Sacrifices Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הַחַטָּאת, כְּשֶׁהִיא חַיָּה – לֹא מָעַל, עַד שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם. כְּשֶׁהִיא מֵתָה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנֶּהֱנָה – מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **One who derives benefit from a sin offering while it is alive** is **not liable for misuse until he** causes it one *peruta* of **damage. When it is dead, once he derives benefit** equal to the value of **one *peruta*** from it, he is **liable for misuse.**

The *mishnah* concludes with a case: *ha-ne'eneh min ha-chattat keshehi chayah lo ma'al ad she-yifgom* — one who derives benefit from a *chattat* while it is still alive has not committed *me'ilah* until he has caused some diminishment. This is because the use of a living animal — riding it, working with it — normally entails some exertion and temporary diminishment of the animal, even if the animal recovers afterward through food and rest. Therefore, actual *pegam* is required.

However, *keshehi metah, keivan she-ne'eneh ma'al* — once the animal is dead, *me'ilah* applies as soon as one derives any benefit. Now, this raises a sharp conceptual question: the beginning of the tractate cited a principle that *kodashim she-meitu yatze'u mi-yedei me'ilah* — consecrated animals that have died are no longer subject to *me'ilah*. The resolution, as Ulla stated at the outset, is that *me'ilah* for a dead *kodesh* is rabbinic rather than biblical. That resolves the formal contradiction.

But the conceptual question about *pegam* remains. Why is no *pegam* required when the animal is dead? After all, using the flesh of a carcass would normally entail some consumption or diminishment of the flesh. The answer appears to be that *pegam* is not measured by asking whether this type of use would ordinarily entail a diminishment of this type of object in a general sense. Rather, the question is whether the act causes a *pegam* to *hekdesh* specifically — whether *hekdesh* suffers any loss as a result. A dead *kodesh* is, from *hekdesh's* perspective, worthless — it has no further sacrificial purpose and no monetary value that *hekdesh* can realize. Accordingly, there is no *pegam* to be caused, *ein bo pegam*, and therefore the full trigger of *me'ilah* is *hana'ah* alone.

This points toward a conceptual understanding of why *pegam* is required at all. The underlying idea of *me'ilah* is about *hana'ah* — taking benefit from *hekdesh*. But when the benefit normally comes at the cost of diminishing the object, and in this particular instance no diminishment occurred, one might argue that nothing was truly taken from *hekdesh*. It is analogous to the concept in Bava Kamma of *zeh neheneh ve-zeh lo chaser* — one who derives benefit while the other party loses nothing. If I derive *hana'ah* from *hekdesh's* property without causing it any loss, then to some degree I have not taken something away from *hekdesh* in the full sense. Of course, even where no *pegam* occurs — as with the gold necklace — the *Chachamim* agree that *me'ilah* is committed; *hana'ah* itself, even without any loss, is still considered a form of taking. But where the *hana'ah* typically comes with loss and happens to occur without it, the argument that one has not truly diverted something from *hekdesh* carries more weight.

Regarding the *shiur* of *pegam* itself: the *Rambam* requires that one be *ne'eneh* a *perutah* and *pogem* a *perutah* — both independently. Rabbeinu Gershom, in the *Mefaresh*, appears to understand that a half-*perutah* of *hana'ah* and a half-*perutah* of *pegam* combine toward the full *shiur*. The *mishnah's* framing, however, suggests that *hana'ah* of a *perutah* is the primary threshold, while *pegam* of any minimal amount (*kol she-hu*) is what gives that *hana'ah* its qualifying character.

## Me'al = Shinui

### Sotah & AZ Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״נֶפֶשׁ״ – אֶחָד הַיָּחִיד, וְאֶחָד הַנָּשִׂיא, וְאֶחָד הַמָּשִׁיחַ. ״כִּי תִמְעֹל מַעַל״, אֵין ״מַעַל״ אֶלָּא שִׁנּוּי, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי תִשְׂטֶה אִשְׁתּוֹ וּמָעֲלָה בוֹ מַעַל״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּמְעֲלוּ בֵּאלֹהֵי אֲבֹתֵיהֶם וַיִּזְנוּ אַחֲרֵי הַבְּעָלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to misuse of consecrated property, the verse states: "If **anyone** commits a misuse, and sins through error, in the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). It is derived from the general term "anyone" that this *halakha* applies **whether** the guilty person is a common **individual, and whether** he is **the king, and whether** he is **the anointed** High Priest. Although in other contexts they have obligations to bring different offerings, these individuals have a uniform liability with regard to the sin of misuse. The *baraita* further interprets the verse: "If anyone **commits a misuse [*ma'al*],** and sins" (Leviticus 5:15). The term ***ma'al* means nothing other than deviation** from the norm. **And similarly,** the verse **states** with regard to a woman suspected of adultery: **"If any man's wife goes aside, and acts unfaithfully [*ma'al*] against him"** (Numbers 5:12). **And** it **states** with regard to idol worship: **"And they broke faith [*vayimalu*] with the God of their fathers, and went astray after** the gods of the peoples of the land, whom God destroyed before them" (I Chronicles 5:25).

A *baraita* addresses the core meaning of the word *me'al* itself. The verse states *nefesh ki sim'ol ma'al mi-kodshei Hashem* — if a soul commits *me'ilah* against the holy things of God. The *baraita* begins: *ein ma'al ela shinui* — *me'al* connotes only a *shinui*, a diversion or change. Two proof-texts are cited.

First: *ish ish ki tisteh ishto u-ma'alah vo ma'al* — any man whose wife strays and commits *ma'al* against him (Bamidbar 5:12). Second: *va-yimlu be-Elohei avoteihem va-yiznu acharei ha-ba'alim* — they were unfaithful to the God of their fathers and went after the *ba'alim* (Divrei HaYamim I 5:25).

Tosafot develops an important conceptual analysis from these two examples, observing that they illustrate two distinct models of *me'ilah* — two different forms of the *shinui* that constitutes *me'al*.

### Two Me'ilah Models

The first model, illustrated by the *sotah*, involves *hana'ah* — the diversion of pleasure or benefit. The adulterous act directed toward the wife a form of intimacy that belonged to her husband; it was *diverted* from its proper recipient. The pleasure that was the husband's right was enjoyed by another. This is *shinui* in the sense of redirection — taking what belongs to one domain and directing it elsewhere. On this model, *me'ilah* is about diverting *hana'ah* that belongs to *hekdesh* into private use.

The second model, illustrated by the *avodah zarah* example, involves not the diversion of pleasure but the transfer of possession and control. When Israel worshipped idols, they were not merely enjoying some benefit; they were transferring their allegiance — removing themselves from God's domain and placing themselves within the domain of the *ba'alim*. The question is not who is benefiting but who possesses, who owns, to whose *reshut* does the entity belong. On this model, *me'ilah* is about taking the object of *hekdesh* and transferring it — removing it from *hekdesh's* ownership — into private ownership, more akin in structure to *gezelah*.

These two models have significant practical implications for how *me'ilah* is conceptualized. Under the *hana'ah* model, it is the person receiving or directing benefit who is the actor; under the *gezelah/ownership* model, it is the one taking possession who is central. The tractate navigates between these two frameworks throughout, and both are reflected in the various *mishnayot* and *beraitot* that follow.

It should be noted that even on the *hana'ah* model, *me'ilah* is not purely analogous to the *issur hana'ah* of *avodah zarah*, where one simply may not derive benefit from the prohibited object and there is no obligation to repay. *Me'ilah* retains an element of *taking from hekdesh* — the person is answerable to restore the value to *hekdesh* and pay an additional *chomesh*. This indicates that even when framed through *hana'ah*, the conceptual structure of *me'ilah* is closer to the *gezelah* model than to a pure *issur hana'ah*: one is not merely doing something forbidden, one is taking something that belongs to another.

## Baraita Me'ilah Model

### Pegam vs. Hana'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל פָּגַם וְלֹא נֶהֱנָה, אוֹ נֶהֱנָה וְלֹא פָּגַם, וּבִמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע, וּבְשָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְחָטְאָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that the comparison between misuse and idol worship teaches that one is liable for misuse even if he **damaged** the consecrated item **but did not derive benefit** from it, as that is the *halakha* with regard to idol worship, where one changes worship of God to idol worship without deriving benefit. **Or,** in light of the comparison between misuse and suspected adultery, it might have been thought that one would be liable for misuse if **he derived benefit** from the consecrated item **but did not damage** it, just as the adulteress derives benefit from the illicit act of intercourse without suffering any physical damage. **And** perhaps one should be liable for misuse **of** an item **attached to the ground. And in** the case of **an agent who** committed misuse while **he performed his** assigned **agency,** it might be thought that the agent himself, rather than the one who appointed him, should be liable for the misuse. **The verse states:** "If anyone commits a misuse, **and sins** through error, in the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15). The word **"sin" is stated with regard to *teruma*,** in the verse: "And you shall bear no sin by reason of it, as you have set apart from it its best" (Numbers 18:32); **and "sin" is** also **stated with regard to misuse** of consecrated property. Several *halakhot* are derived from this verbal analogy.

A *baraita* now directly addresses the conceptual relationship between *pegam* and *hana'ah* in *me'ilah*. The *baraita* opens: *yakhol pegam belo ne'eneh o ne'eneh belo pegam* — might one think that *pegam* without *hana'ah*, or *hana'ah* without *pegam*, could constitute *me'ilah*? The *baraita* proceeds to resolve this through the *gezeirah shavah* linking *me'ilah* and *terumah*, both expressed with the word *kheit*.

### Terumah Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּתְרוּמָה, פּוֹגֵם וְנֶהֱנֶה, וּמִי שֶׁפָּגַם נֶהֱנֶה, וּבַדָּבָר שֶׁפּוֹגֵם בּוֹ נֶהֱנֶה, וּפְגִימָתוֹ וַהֲנָאָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד, וּבְתָלוּשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע, וּבְשָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – אַף ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּמְעִילָה, פּוֹגֵם וְנֶהֱנֶה, וּמִי שֶׁפָּגַם נֶהֱנֶה, וּבַדָּבָר שֶׁפּוֹגֵם בּוֹ נֶהֱנֶה, וּפְגִימָתוֹ וַהֲנָאָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד, וּבְתָלוּשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע, וּבְשָׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* elaborates: **Just as** with regard to the word **"sin" stated with regard to *teruma*,** when someone sins by partaking of forbidden *teruma*, it is a circumstance in which one **damages and derives benefit, and** the **one who damaged** the *teruma* is the same person who **derived benefit; and** with regard to *teruma*, one is liable only if **he derives benefit from the same item that he damages; and** he is liable in the case of *teruma* only if **his damage and his benefit** occur **simultaneously; and** the status of *teruma* applies only **to** an item **detached from the ground; and** the status of *teruma* takes effect **in** the case of **an agent who performed his** assigned **agency** in designating an item *teruma*, the same applies to misuse. **So too,** the word **"sin" stated with regard to misuse** applies specifically when the individual both **damages and derives benefit** from consecrated items; **and** when the **one who damaged** also **derived benefit** from it himself; **and he derives benefit from the** same **item that he damages; and his damage and his deriving benefit** occur **simultaneously; and** misuse applies only **to** an item **detached from the ground; and** misuse is committed by the one appointing the agent **in** the case of **an agent who performed his** assigned **agency.**

*Mah kheit ha-amur bi-terumah pogem ve-ne'eneh* — just as the *kheit* of *terumah* involves both *pegam* and *hana'ah*. The paradigm is eating *terumah* without authorization: eating is the quintessential act of *hana'ah* and it necessarily entails *pegam* — the food is consumed and gone. Since the Torah describes *me'ilah* with the same term *kheit* used for *terumah*, the structure of *me'ilah* must parallel the structure of unauthorized eating of *terumah*: *hana'ah* that comes through *pegam*.

### Same Person/Object/Time

*U-mi she-pagam nehene* — the one who caused the *pegam* must be the same person who received the *hana'ah*. If James caused the *pegam* and I received the *hana'ah*, that does not constitute *me'ilah*. *U-ve-davar she-pogem bo nehene* — the *pegam* must occur in the same object from which the *hana'ah* is derived. If one is *ne'eneh* from one object of *hekdesh* while causing *pegam* to a different object, the two do not combine to constitute *me'ilah*. *U-fgimato ve-hana'ato ke-echad* — the *pegam* and the *hana'ah* must be simultaneous, not sequential. It is not sufficient that one first caused diminishment and later derived benefit, or vice versa; they must occur in the same act, as in eating.

### Tolash & Shlichus

Two additional conditions are derived: *u-ve-tolash min ha-karka* — by *terumah*, produce must have been detached from the ground before it can become *terumah* and before unauthorized eating of it is relevant. Similarly, *me'ilah* does not apply to something still attached to the ground (*mukhubar*). And *u-vi-shliach she-asah shlichuto* — there is a concept of *shlichus* operative in *terumah* (as in other areas where a *shaliach* acts on one's behalf), and this same concept applies to *me'ilah*, as will be elaborated in subsequent *mishnayot*.

## Tziruf of Me'ilah Acts

### Different People Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אוֹכֵל, וְנֶהֱנֶה בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ פְּגָם, מִנַּיִן? אֲכִילָתוֹ וַאֲכִילַת חֲבֵרוֹ, הֲנָיָיתוֹ וַהֲנָיַית חֲבֵרוֹ, הֲנָאָתוֹ וַאֲכִילַת חֲבֵרוֹ, אֲכִילָתוֹ וַהֲנָיַית חֲבֵרוֹ שֶׁמִּצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה, אֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּמְעֹל מַעַל״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: From the verbal analogy **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for misuse if **he eats** consecrated items when he is not permitted to do so, similar to the case of *teruma*. **But** with regard to one who **derives benefit from an item that does not have** the potential to be **damaged, from where** do I derive that this also constitutes misuse? Similarly, if one eats from a consecrated item and gives to another person to eat, where there is **his consumption and another's consumption;** or if he derives benefit and gives to another to derive benefit, where there is **his** deriving **benefit and another's** deriving **benefit;** and likewise **his** deriving **benefit and another's consumption;** and **his consumption and another's** deriving **benefit;** the *halakha* is **that** in all these cases, benefit and consumption **combine with one another, even** over **a long** period of **time** on the same day, to amount to the value of one *peruta* that effects liability for misuse. **From where** is this *halakha* derived? **The verse states:** "If anyone **commits a misuse."** This general and inclusive clause teaches that one is liable for misusing one *peruta* worth of consecrated property **in any case,** i.e., even if he gave it to another and one of them ate while the other derived benefit, and even if it took some time on that day to use the value of one *peruta*.

The *baraita* then expands the analysis to cases of combination across persons and time. *Minayin achilato ve-achilat chavero, hana'ato ve-hana'at chavero, hana'ah ve-achilat chavero, achilato ve-hana'at chavero she-mitztarfin zeh la-zeh* — how do we know that one person's eating can combine with another's eating, or one's *hana'ah* with another's *hana'ah*, or one's eating with another's *hana'ah*, and so forth? If I derive half a *perutah* of *hana'ah* and Charlie derives half a *perutah*, do our two partial amounts combine to reach the *shiur* of *me'ilah*? And if so, who is *mo'el*?

This case is conceptually interesting precisely because *shlichus* cannot straightforwardly explain it in all scenarios. If I give Charlie the benefit — if I put oil on Charlie, or feed him — then perhaps I am the one *taking* the *hana'ah* from *hekdesh* even though Charlie is the one *receiving* it. The key distinction is between *taking* and *receiving*: the actor in *me'ilah* is the one who takes the *hana'ah* from *hekdesh*, not necessarily the one who ultimately enjoys it. The *baraita* derives from *ki sim'ol ma'al* — read *mikol makom*, from any case — that these combinations are valid regardless.

### Time Doesn't Break Tziruf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּתְרוּמָה – לֹא צֵירַף שְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת כְּאֶחָד, אַף ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּמְעִילָה – לֹא צֵירַף שְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת כְּאַחַד. מִנַּיִן אָכַל הַיּוֹם וְאָכַל לְמָחָר, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּמְעֹל מַעַל״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: **If** there is a comparison between *teruma* and misuse, one can claim as follows: **Just as** in the case of the word **"sin" that is stated with regard to *teruma*,** the Torah does **not combine two** distinct acts of **consumption as one** prohibited act, **so too,** perhaps with regard to the word **"sin" that is stated with regard to misuse,** the Torah does **not combine two** acts of **consumption as one. From where** is it derived that if one **ate** half of the minimum amount of an olive-bulk **today and ate** another half **tomorrow, even if much time** has passed, the distinct acts of misuse combine and are considered one act, which means that the one who did so is liable for misuse? **The verse states:** "If anyone **commits a misuse."** The inclusive language of the verse teaches that he is liable **in any case.**

The *baraita* further asks: *minayin akhal ha-yom ve-akhal le-machar ve-afilu zman merubbeh* — how do we know that if one derived half a *perutah* today and half a *perutah* tomorrow, or even after a much longer period, these two acts combine? In contrast to the laws of *terumah*, where eating must occur within the time of *achilat pras* for the acts to combine, might *me'ilah* similarly require temporal proximity?

The answer is that *me'ilah* does not: even after a very long time (*zman merubbeh*), partial acts of *me'ilah* combine. The *baraita* derives this again from the emphatic *ki sim'ol ma'ol* — *mikol makom*, without qualification as to time. And combining both variables: *minayin achilato ve-achilat chavero ve-afilu mikan ve-ad shalosh shanim* — how do we know that one person's partial *me'ilah* and another person's partial *me'ilah* combine even if the second act occurs three years after the first? Again, *talmud lomar: ki sim'ol ma'al mikol makom* — regardless.

### Taking from Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה ״חֵטְא״ הָאָמוּר בִּתְרוּמָה – עֵד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִן הַקֹּדֶשׁ לַחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If** it is derived from the verbal analogy that there are several *halakhot* common to both *teruma* and committing misuse, one can claim as follows: **Just as** in the case of the word **"sin" that is stated with regard to *teruma*,** a non-priest who partakes of *teruma* is not liable **until** the *teruma* **leaves from** the domain of **the sacred to** the domain of **the non-sacred,** as one who partakes of *teruma* becomes its owner, the same should apply to misuse.

The conceptual basis for this expansive rule of combination follows directly from the analysis developed throughout the *sugya*. *Me'ilah* is not structured like *ma'aseh achilah* — the act of eating — which has a defined *shiur* (*kezayit*) that must be consumed within a defined time frame (*kedei achilat pras*). Rather, *me'ilah* is structured around *taking from hekdesh*: how much value has been removed from *hekdesh's* domain? The threshold is a *perutah's* worth of *hana'ah* — a *perutah* of value taken away from *hekdesh*. Once the analysis is framed this way, there is no reason why the taking must occur in a single act, by a single person, or within any particular time window. Multiple partial takings, by multiple people, over any period of time, all aggregate toward the same *shiur* because they all represent incremental removals of value from *hekdesh*.

This also illuminates the *baraita's* framing of *me'al* as *shinui* — as diversion. Whether the model is the *hana'ah* of the *sotah* case or the possession-transfer of the *avodah zarah* case, what is common to both is the concept of diverting something from its proper domain. *Hekdesh* has a claim on both the object and on the *hana'ah* that object generates. *Me'ilah* is the act of diverting either the object or the *hana'ah* into private use — and however that diversion accumulates, across people and across time, the total diversion is what is measured.
