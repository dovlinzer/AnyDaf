# Hullin 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood of Slain vs. Shechita

### Rabbi Shimon & Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הוּכְשְׁרוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה. אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, שְׁחִיטָתוֹ מַכְשֶׁרֶת וְלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: They were rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **by means of** the **slaughter** itself. **Rav Asi said** that **Rabbi Shimon would say:** It is **its slaughter** that **renders it susceptible** to ritual impurity, **and not** the **blood** that emerges during the slaughter.

The daf begins at the very bottom of 35b, in the middle of a discussion of *rabbi shimon*'s position in the *mishna*: the act of *shechita* renders the meat *huchshar* — susceptible to *tumah* — without any need for blood contact. The *gemara* is now probing the further implications of this position: what is *rabbi shimon*'s view regarding the blood of *shechita* itself? Does that blood serve as a *machshir* for other foods, or does he deny it any such function?

The prior discussion established that *dam chalalim* — the blood of slain persons — is *machshir* according to *rabbi shimon*, with slight variations noted: the blood of someone who died naturally, or the blood of a wound that did not result in death, would not be *machshir*. Only the blood of someone actually slain qualifies. The status of *dam shechita* relative to that rule, however, remained unresolved. We now turn to the scriptural foundation for these distinctions.

### V'dam Chalalim Yishteh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא דַּם חֲלָלִים דְּמַכְשַׁיר? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְדַם חֲלָלִים יִשְׁתֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** with regard to **blood of** animals that **are killed that they render** food items **susceptible** to ritual impurity? It is due to the fact **that it is written:** "Behold, they are a people that rises up as a lioness, and as a lion he lifts himself up; he shall not lie down until he eats of the prey **and drinks blood of carcasses"** (Numbers 23:24). The fact that the blood of a carcass, which in the context of the verse is referring to an animal that was killed, is mentioned in the context of drinking, indicates that it is a liquid that renders food items susceptible to ritual impurity.

The *gemara* opens with a pointed question: *u'mai shna dam chalalim d'machshir* — why do we take it as given that the blood of a slain person is *machshir*? The answer is that the verse states *v'dam chalalim yishteh* — "and the blood of the slain he shall drink" — which refers to blood as something that is drunk, thereby treating it as a liquid with the same effect as water, namely, rendering food susceptible to *tumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּם שְׁחִיטָה נָמֵי כְּתִיב: ״עַל הָאָרֶץ תִּשְׁפְּכֶנּוּ כַּמָּיִם״! הָהוּא לְמִישְׁרֵי דָּמָן דִּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין בַּהֲנָאָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **blood of slaughter it is also written:** "Only, you shall not eat the blood; **you shall pour it upon the earth as water"** (Deuteronomy 12:16). The parallel to water ostensibly indicates that the blood of slaughter should also render food items susceptible to ritual impurity. The Gemara answers: **That** verse is not written with regard to susceptibility to impurity. The purpose for **which** it **comes** is **to permit benefit** from the **blood of disqualified consecrated** animals.

The *gemara* immediately presses further: but there is an equally explicit verse regarding *shechita*, which states *al ha'aretz tishpachenu kamayim* — "spill it on the ground like water." That should equally establish *dam shechita* as *machshir*. The *gemara* deflects: *hahu mishum dam d'psulei mukdashim behana'ah d'ata* — that verse is not teaching anything about susceptibility to *tumah*. Rather, it is teaching that when an animal that was consecrated as a sacrifice becomes disqualified through a blemish and is subsequently redeemed, the blood of its eventual *shechita* may be used for benefit. It is not treated like the blood of a valid sacrifice, from which no benefit may be derived. The verse is teaching permissibility of benefit, not the laws of *machshir*.

### Shechita Blood as Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וַאֲסִירִי בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה, דָּמָן לִבְעֵי קְבוּרָה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It could **enter your mind to say: Since** benefit from disqualified consecrated animals **is forbidden with regard to** their **fleece and labor,** perhaps benefit from **their blood** is also forbidden, and **let** it **require burial.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that benefit from their blood is permitted.

The comparison of *shechita* blood to water, then, is not establishing it as a *machshir* in the *tumah*-and-*tahara* sense. The *gemara*'s analysis makes clear that the verse's analogy to water in that context has a specifically delineated purpose — one that does not carry over into the laws of *hechsher*.

### Psulei Mukdashim

Picking up at the top of 36a, the *gemara* adds that one might have supposed the following: since certain restrictions still apply to *psulei mukdashim* even after redemption — specifically, they may not be shorn and may not be put to work — some remnant of their original sanctity remains. One might therefore have thought that their blood, too, retains a sacred character: if not used upon the altar, perhaps it should be buried and no benefit derived from it. The verse therefore comes to tell us — *kamashma lan* — that no, their blood is like water and benefit is permitted. Not that it is permitted to drink it, since the prohibition on blood remains, but that benefit may be derived in other ways.

### Restrictions & Blood Status

The conclusion, then, is that the verse about *shechita* blood cannot be used to establish it as a *machshir*. The *gemara* takes it as given that *dam chalalim* is *machshir* and that other categories of blood are not. The precise distinctions here may seem somewhat arbitrary, but that is the framework the *gemara* is working within. Having established what is and is not *machshir* in principle, the discussion now turns to the exact parameters of what kind of blood — even within the qualifying categories — actually functions as a *machshir*.

## Blood of Spurting

### Tana d'vei R. Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְדַם חֲלָלִים יִשְׁתֶּה״ – פְּרָט לְדַם קִילּוּחַ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכְשִׁיר אֶת הַזְּרָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that the verse: **"And drinks the blood of carcasses,"** from which it is derived that the blood of an animal that was killed renders food items susceptible to ritual impurity, serves to **exclude blood** that emerges in **a surge** due to arterial pressure at the moment of slaughter while the animal is still alive **that does not render seeds susceptible to** ritual impurity.

*Tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* — the school of *rabbi yishmael* taught: the verse *v'dam chalalim yishteh* — "the blood of the slain he shall drink" — *prat l'dam kiluach she'eino machshir et hazra'im* — excludes the blood of spurting, which is not *machshir*. This requires unpacking.

### Dam She'haneshama Yotzei

When a person or animal is killed or wounded, blood initially emerges in a trickle, then surges forth in a powerful spurt, and afterward subsides again. The *gemara* in *krisos* identifies this middle phase — the surging, spurting blood — as *dam she'haneshama yotzei bah*: the blood during which the soul departs. This is the blood identified with the life force itself, as the Torah says *ha'dam hu ha'nefesh* — "the blood is the life force." The surging blood is the very expression of that principle.

### Slain Person Timing

Reading this *sugya* in line with *tosafot*'s approach — which focuses on slain persons as the operative context when *shechita* is not explicitly mentioned — the rule runs as follows. We have already established that only the blood of someone actually slain, not someone who merely died or was wounded, is *machshir*. Now we add a further refinement: even when it is the blood of a slain person, the blood of the spurting phase is excluded. Counterintuitively, although that is the blood most intimately identified with the departing life force, the *gemara* says that the blood must come *after* the spurting — once the person is already considered dead. The blood during the process of dying does not qualify; only the blood that flows once death is complete.

### Shechita Blood Timing

For *dam shechita*, the rule is precisely the inverse. Based on another *gemara*, only the blood of the life force — the spurting phase — is considered *machshir*, not the blood that continues to flow after that stage has passed. The two categories form a perfect mirror image of one another: for a slain person, the blood must come after the spurting; for *shechita*, it must be the spurting itself.

## Blood on Gourd Debate

### Splattered Blood Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְהִתִּיז דָּם עַל הַדַּלַּעַת – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הוּכְשַׁר, רַבִּי חִיָּיא אוֹמֵר: תּוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who **slaughters** an animal **and splashed blood** of the slaughter **on a gourd** of *teruma*, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The gourd is **rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity. **Rabbi Ḥiyya says:** If the gourd came into contact with a source of impurity, **one places** the matter **in abeyance,** as there is uncertainty whether the blood rendered it susceptible to impurity.

*Tanu Rabanan*: if one is performing *shechita* and blood splatters onto a gourd, what is the status of that gourd? The question is whether this *shechita* blood functions as a *machshir*, rendering the gourd susceptible to *tumah*.

### Machshir vs. Tolun

*Rabbi* says: *huchshar* — it is rendered susceptible. *rabbi chiya* says: *tulin* — the matter is suspended. In practical terms, the significance of *rabbi chiya*'s ruling is that if the gourd is *terumah* and someone subsequently touches it, we cannot determine with certainty whether the *terumah* became *tamei*.

### R. Oshaya Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מֵאַחַר שֶׁרַבִּי אוֹמֵר הוּכְשַׁר, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא אוֹמֵר תּוֹלִין, אָנוּ עַל מִי נִסְמוֹךְ? בֹּאוּ וְנִסְמוֹךְ עַל דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁחִיטָה מַכְשֶׁרֶת וְלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya said: Since Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that the gourd is **rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **and Rabbi Ḥiyya says** that **one places** the matter **in abeyance, on whom shall we rely? Come and let us rely on the statement of Rabbi Shimon, as Rabbi Shimon would say:** It is **slaughter** that **renders** the animal **susceptible, and not blood.**

*Rabbi Oshaya* observed: given that *rabbi* says *huchshar* and *rabbi chiya* says *tulin*, *anu ammi nismoch* — whom do we rely upon? The intuitive answer might seem to be a weighted vote: *rabbi* says definitively that it is *huchshar*, while *rabbi chiya*'s *tulin* represents at most a half-measure of uncertainty. That would appear to give the *huchshar* position a decisive advantage.

### Voting Realignment

But *rabbi oshaya* redirects attention to *rabbi shimon* in our *mishna*, who holds that *shechita* itself — not the blood — is what renders the meat *huchshar*, and that the blood of *shechita* is not *machshir* at all. Factoring in *rabbi shimon*'s position, the alignment shifts: *rabbi* says *huchshar*; *rabbi shimon* says it is definitely not *huchshar*; *rabbi chiya* is uncertain. This creates a more complex three-way dynamic, and the *gemara* will now work through it carefully.

## Shechita Start & End

### Blood Remaining Throughout

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְדָם מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמַכְשַׁיר. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּנִתְקַנֵּחַ הַדָּם בֵּין סִימָן לְסִימָן, רַבִּי סָבַר: יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף, וְהַאי דַּם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** in explanation: **Everyone,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Ḥiyya, **agrees** that **where there is blood** on the gourd throughout the slaughter continuously **from beginning to end, everyone,** both Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, who says: Slaughter is defined only as the conclusion of its performance, and Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says: Slaughter is defined from the beginning to the end of its performance, **agrees that** the blood **renders** the gourd **susceptible** to impurity, in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, who disagree with Rabbi Shimon. **When they disagree** is in a case **where the blood is wiped** off the gourd **between** the cutting of one ***siman* and** the other ***siman*. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **slaughter is** defined **from** the **beginning to** the **end** of its performance, **and this blood** that splashed on the gourd **is blood of slaughter.**

*Rav Pappa* provides a clarifying framework. Setting aside *rabbi shimon*'s position for a moment and focusing on the debate between *rabbi* and *rabbi chiya*, he identifies the point of agreement and the point of dispute.

First, the key clarification: the scenario of "blood remaining from beginning to end" does not mean that blood was actively spurting throughout the entire *shechita*. Rather, it means that the blood that spurted at the start of the *shechita* landed on the gourd and remained there when the *shechita* was completed. In that scenario, *kulei alma lo pligi de-machshir* — everyone agrees it is *machshir*.

### Blood Wiped Off Mid-Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא סָבַר: אֵינָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה אֶלָּא בַּסּוֹף, וְהַאי דַּם מַכָּה הוּא, וּמַאי ״תּוֹלִין״? תּוֹלִין הַדָּבָר עַד גְּמַר שְׁחִיטָה – אִי אִיתֵיהּ לְדָם בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה מַכְשִׁיר, וְאִי לָא – לֹא מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya holds** that **slaughter is** defined **only** as **the conclusion** of its performance, **and this is blood of a wound,** which does not render food items susceptible to ritual impurity. **And what** is the meaning of the ruling of Rabbi Ḥiyya that **one places** the matter **in abeyance?** It means that **one places** the matter **in abeyance until** the **conclusion of** the **slaughter. If there is blood** remaining on the gourd **at the conclusion of the slaughter** the blood **renders** the gourd **susceptible** to ritual impurity, **and if not,** the blood **does not render** the gourd **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

The dispute arises in a different scenario: *b'nitkane'ach ha'dam bein siman l'siman* — the blood spurted onto the gourd, was quickly wiped off, and then the *shechita* was completed. This is the case about which *rabbi* and *rabbi chiya* disagree.

### Shechita from Start

*Rabbi* holds: *yesh lishchita mitichila v'ad sof* — *shechita* is a process that begins from the very first moment. Therefore, *v'hai dam shechita hu* — the blood that emerged with the cutting of the first *siman* is already blood of *shechita*, and it renders the gourd *huchshar* regardless of whether it was subsequently wiped away.

### Shechita Only at End

*Rabbi Chiya* holds: *ein lishchita ela b'sof* — *shechita* is only properly recognizable at the end. Until the process is complete, we cannot yet call what was happening a *shechita*. Therefore, the blood that emerged at the first *siman* is considered *dam maka* — blood of a wound — not blood of *shechita*, because at the moment of its emergence no *shechita* had yet been performed. However, if the blood is still present on the gourd when the *shechita* concludes, then at that moment — now that the *shechita* is complete — we can look back and call it blood of *shechita*, and it is *machshir*.

### Retroactive Shechita

This *sugya* yields an important clarification about the famous debate of *yesh lishchita mitichila v'ad sof*, which has been a recurring theme in the *gemara*. The question might seem puzzling: of course the process of *shechita* begins from the beginning, and of course it concludes at the end — what exactly is at stake?

Here the matter becomes clear. Both opinions agree that once the *shechita* is complete, we look back at the entire process and recognize it as a *shechita* process. The question is: *when* are we prepared to make that retrospective declaration? According to *rabbi chiya*, we can only do so once the process is finished — from that point forward, looking back, we say it was *shechita*. But this declaration operates only from the moment of completion onward; it does not retroactively alter the status of blood that is no longer present. Since the blood was wiped away, it matters not that we now call the process *shechita* — there is nothing left for that designation to act upon.

According to *rabbi* — who holds *yesh lishchita mitichila v'ad sof* in the fuller sense — even during the process itself, before completion, we treat what is happening as *shechita*. Therefore even blood that appears at the first cut and is subsequently wiped away is still blood of *shechita* at the moment of its emergence and renders the gourd *huchshar*.

This framing also illuminates why, even for the opinion that *shechita* is recognized only at the end, a pause in the middle of *shechita* that would constitute a *shehiya* would not turn the animal into a *treifa*: because once the *shechita* is ultimately completed, we retroactively call the entire process *shechita*.

### Vote Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״בֹּאוּ וְנִסְמוֹךְ עַל דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן״? לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֹא מַכְשִׁיר, לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא מַכְשִׁיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what** is the meaning of the statement of Rabbi Oshaya: **Come and let us rely on the statement of Rabbi Shimon?** According **to Rabbi Shimon** the blood of slaughter **does not render** food items **susceptible** to ritual impurity, while according **to Rabbi Ḥiyya** the blood of slaughter **renders** the gourd **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנִתְקַנֵּחַ מִיהוּ אַשְׁווֹ לַהֲדָדֵי, מָר לָא מַכְשִׁיר, וּמָר לָא מַכְשִׁיר, הֲוָה לֵיהּ רַבִּי חַד, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that in a case **where** the blood **is wiped** off the gourd prior to the conclusion of slaughter, **in any event,** the opinions of Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Ḥiyya **correspond to each other:** One **Sage** holds that the blood of slaughter **does not render** the gourd **susceptible** to ritual impurity **and** the other **Sage** holds that the blood of slaughter **does not render** the gourd **susceptible** to ritual impurity. The result is that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that the blood of slaughter renders the gourd susceptible to ritual impurity, **is one** Sage stating an individual opinion, **and the statement of one** Sage **has no** standing **in a place** where it is disputed by **two** Sages.

With this analysis in hand, the *gemara* can now explain the vote alignment. *u'mai bo v'nismach al divrei rabbi shimon* — what does it mean to rely on *rabbi shimon*? How is *rabbi shimon* relevant to the debate between *rabbi* and *rabbi chiya*?

*Rabbi Shimon* holds fundamentally that blood of *shechita* is not *machshir* at all. *rabbi chiya* holds that it is *machshir* — but only under the right conditions. Despite their entirely different rationales, in the case where the blood was wiped off mid-*shechita*, both reach the same practical conclusion: *b'nitkaneach mihu ashvu l'hadadi* — they agree the gourd is not *huchshar*. Like a Supreme Court decision where justices in a majority write separate opinions with different reasoning, what matters for the vote count is the outcome. *mar lo machshir u'mar lo machshir* — each says not *huchshar*, one as a categorical rejection of *shechita* blood's *machshir* status, the other because the blood was wiped off before the *shechita* concluded. Together they form a majority of two against *rabbi*'s single opinion of *huchshar*. *v'havei lei rabbi chad b'makom shnayim* — one does not prevail against two, even when the two arrive at their shared position through entirely different reasoning.

## Rav Ashi: Always Doubtful

### Tolun = Perpetual Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״תּוֹלִין״ לְעוֹלָם מַשְׁמַע, וְנִתְקַנֵּחַ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, אִי יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף, אוֹ אֵינָהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה אֶלָּא בַּסּוֹף, וּמַאי ״תּוֹלִין״? לֹא אוֹכְלִין וְלֹא שׂוֹרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that the term: **One places** the matter **in abeyance, indicates** that it remains in abeyance **forever.** It is a fundamental halakhic uncertainty for which there is no resolution. **And** in the case where the blood is **wiped** off the gourd prior to the conclusion of the slaughter, the *halakha* is unclear. This is because **Rabbi Ḥiyya is uncertain whether slaughter is** defined **from** the **beginning to** the **end** of its performance **or whether slaughter is** defined **only** as the **conclusion** of its performance. **And what** is the meaning of his ruling that **one places** the matter **in abeyance?** It means that if a source of impurity comes into contact with the gourd after the blood was wiped off the gourd, **one may neither eat** the gourd, as perhaps it is impure *teruma*, **nor may one burn** it, as perhaps it is pure.

*Rav Ashi* offers an alternative reading of the entire dispute. According to him, when *rabbi chiya* says *tulin* — suspended — this does not mean we wait to see whether the blood remains until the end of the *shechita*. Rather, *tolin l'olam mashma* — the term *tulin* indicates a perpetual state of doubt, one that is never resolved.

### R. Chiya's Uncertainty

Under *rav ashi*'s reading, *rabbi chiya* genuinely does not know how to rule — *sfukei mesafeka lei* — because he is uncertain about the foundational question: *ei yeshna l'shechitah mitchell v'ad sof o einah l'shechitah ela besof* — whether *shechita* is considered a process from the very beginning or whether it is only called *shechita* at its conclusion. He has a substantive unresolved doubt about the underlying law.

### Suspension Consequences

The practical consequence of *rabbi chiya*'s doubt — when the blood was wiped off mid-*shechita* — is therefore the classic response to doubtful *tumah* of *terumah*: *lo ochlin velo sorfin* — one neither eats it, since perhaps it is *tamei*, nor burns it, since perhaps it is *tahor*. One simply sets it aside to decay.

### Shimon's No-Burn Vote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״בֹּאוּ וְנִסְמוֹךְ עַל דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן״? לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֹא מַכְשִׁיר, לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא סְפֵיקָא, לְעִנְיַן שְׂרֵיפָה מִיהוּ שָׁווּ לַהֲדָדֵי: (ומר) [וּלְמָר] לָא שָׂרְפִינַן, (ומר) [וּלְמָר] לָא שָׂרְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to this explanation, **what** is the meaning of the statement of Rabbi Oshaya: **Come and let us rely on the statement of Rabbi Shimon?** According **to Rabbi Shimon** the blood of slaughter **does not render** food items **susceptible** to ritual impurity, while according **to Rabbi Ḥiyya,** there is **uncertainty** with regard to the status of the gourd. The Gemara answers that with regard **to the matter of burning, in any event,** the opinions of Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Ḥiyya **correspond to each other:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **does not burn** the gourd, because it was not rendered susceptible to impurity, **and** the other **Sage,** Rabbi Ḥiyya, **does not burn** the gourd, due to the uncertainty.

Now the *gemara* can re-examine the relevance of *rabbi shimon*. Under *rav ashi*'s reading, the phrase "let us rely on *rabbi shimon*" cannot mean relying on *rabbi shimon* to permit eating the gourd — that would pit *rabbi shimon* against both *rabbi* and *rabbi chiya*, since both of them hold there is reason for concern. Rather, the reliance on *rabbi shimon* is specifically on the question of burning: *l'inyan sreifah meyushvei l'hadadi* — on the question of burning, *rabbi shimon* and *rabbi chiya* agree. *rabbi shimon* says definitively not to burn it, because blood of *shechita* is not *machshir* at all. *rabbi chiya* says not to burn it, because in this case of wiped-off blood, he is uncertain. *mar lo sarfei u'mar lo sarfei* — both say not to burn. Against them, *rabbi* alone would say to burn it. *havei lei rabbi chad v'ein devarav shel echad bimakom shenayim* — a single opinion does not prevail against two.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵה לֵיהּ רַבִּי חַד, וְאֵין דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם שְׁנַיִם, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: כְּגוֹן זֹאת תּוֹלִין – לֹא אוֹכְלִין וְלֹא שׂוֹרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The result is that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that one burns the gourd since the blood of slaughter renders the gourd susceptible to ritual impurity and contact with a source of impurity renders it impure, **is one** Sage stating an individual opinion, **and the statement of one** Sage **has no** standing **in a place** where it is disputed by **two** Sages. **And this** is what Rabbi Ḥiyya **is saying:** In a case **such as this, one places** the matter **in abeyance; one may neither eat** the gourd **nor burn** it.

The result is thus precisely the position of *rabbi chiya*: *lo ochlin velo sorfin* — neither eating nor burning. One cannot eat because *rabbi* and *rabbi chiya* both give reason for concern. One cannot burn because *rabbi chiya* and *rabbi shimon* together form a majority against it. The vote comes out exactly one and a half against one and a half, yielding the suspended status that *rabbi chiya* himself articulated.

## Chibat Hakodesh Concept

### Chibat Hakodesh Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: צָרִיד שֶׁל מְנָחוֹת, מוֹנִין בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי, אוֹ אֵין מוֹנִין בּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי? כִּי מַהְנְיָא חִבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ לְאִפְּסוֹלֵי גּוּפֵיהּ, לְמִימְנֵא בֵּיהּ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי – לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish raises a dilemma:** With regard to a dry **portion** of consecrated flour that was not mixed with the oil **of meal offerings,** does **one count** the descending levels of impurity characteristic of other foods that come into contact with a primary source of impurity, i.e., that food assumes **first-degree** impurity, **and** food that comes into contact with that food assumes **second-degree** impurity; **or** does one **not count** the descending levels of **first-degree** impurity **and second-degree** impurity? The Gemara elaborates: **When regard for sanctity is effective** in rendering an item susceptible to impurity, is it effective only **to disqualify** that item **itself, but to count** the descending levels of **first-degree and second-degree** impurity it is **not** effective? **Or perhaps** once it is rendered susceptible to impurity there **is no difference** whether it is rendered susceptible by means of regard for sanctity or by means of contact with liquids.

*Rav Shimon ben Lakish* — *reish lakish* — posed the following question: what is the status of *tzarid shel menachot* — a clump of *minchah* flour that the oil did not reach? When oil is mixed into *minchah* flour, the oil renders the flour *huchshar* — susceptible to *tumah*. But if a clump of flour remains that the oil never touched, that flour is not *huchshar* in the ordinary sense. If a *tamei* person then touches it, does it become *tamei*?

On one hand, it has not been rendered susceptible through liquid contact. On the other hand, there is a principle — discussed several days ago and now returning to center stage — called *chibat hakodesh*: the dearness of sanctity. Because of the revered status of an object destined for the *mizbeach*, it is treated as susceptible to *tumah*. There is even a view, as we will see, that this is a biblical principle.

This concept bears a suggestive parallel to *rabbi shimon*'s position that *shechita* itself is *machshir*: in both cases, it is not a liquid but rather a transformative act or status — bringing something under human control, rendering it fit for offering, integrating it into the realm of human purposeful activity — that creates susceptibility. The *gemara* now asks: what exactly are the legal implications of *chibat hakodesh*, and how far does it extend?

### Liquid vs. Sanctity

*Reish Lakish*'s question is framed precisely: *monin bo rishon v'sheini o ein monin bo rishon v'sheini* — do we count within it the degrees of *rishon* and *sheini*, or do we not? If the flour were fully *huchshar* like food that had been in contact with liquid, then a *tamei* person who is a *rishon* who touches it would make it a *sheini*, which could in turn make something else a *shelishi*, and so on through the chain of *tumah* transmission. But perhaps *chibat hakodesh* operates on an entirely different track: perhaps it merely causes the object itself to become *pasul* — invalid — when touched by *tumah*, without actually conferring genuine *tumah* status that can be transmitted. *ki mehanu chibas hakodesh* — in what way does the dearness of *kodesh* function? Perhaps only *l'shaveyei gufa gufeih* — to invalidate the thing itself — but not to count *rishon* and *sheini* and create a real chain of *tumah* transmission.

### Water Exclusivity Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תָּא שְׁמַע, ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל וְגוֹ׳״ – אוֹכֶל הַבָּא בְּמַיִם – הוּכְשַׁר, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּא בְּמַיִם – לֹא הוּכְשַׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*. It is written: **"Of all food that may be eaten,** on which water comes, it shall be impure; and all drink that may be drunk it shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34). From that verse it is derived: **Food that comes into** contact with **water is rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity and to count the descending levels of impurity, but **food that does not come into** contact with **water is not rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity. Apparently, for the dry portion of flour that did not come into contact with a liquid but was rendered susceptible by regard for sanctity, one does not count the descending levels of impurity.

*Rabbi Elazar* offers a potential proof: the verse states *mi'kol ha'ochel asher ye'achel asher yavo alav mayim yitma* — "from all food that is eaten, if water comes upon it, it shall become *tamei*." This teaches that *ochel haba b'mayim huchshar* — food that comes into contact with water is susceptible — and seemingly implies that food not in contact with water is not susceptible. Perhaps this verse is meant to confine real *hechsher* to the track of liquid contact, leaving *chibat hakodesh* on a separate, lesser track.

### Further Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ לֵית לֵיהּ אוֹכֶל הַבָּא בְּמַיִם? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ הָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: חִבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ כְּאוֹכֶל הַבָּא בְּמַיִם דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish does not have** knowledge of the *halakha* that only **food that comes into** contact with **water** is susceptible to ritual impurity? The Gemara answers: **This is the dilemma** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish is raising:** Is the halakhic status of consecrated food that is subject to **regard for sanctity like** that of **food that comes into** contact with **water,** and one does not count the descending levels of impurity for items that come into contact with it, **or no,** its halakhic status is unique?

The *gemara* dismisses this immediately: surely *reish lakish* was fully aware of the principle of *ochel haba b'mayim*. That was precisely his question — whether *chibat hakodesh* functions equivalently to *ochel haba b'mayim* or operates differently.

### Food Susceptibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר נָמֵי מִיִּתּוּרֵי קְרָאֵי קָאָמַר, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב ״וְכִי יֻתַּן מַיִם עַל זֶרַע״, ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״ לְמָה לִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rabbi Elazar,** who cited the verse to resolve the dilemma of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, is not merely citing a verse that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish knows. Rather, he **too is stating** his proof **from the extraneous** formulation **of the verses. Since it is written: "But when water is placed upon the seed,** and any of their carcass falls upon it, it is impure for you" (Leviticus 11:38), **why do I** need the verse: **"Of all food which may be eaten,** on which water comes, shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34)?

*Rabbi Elazar*'s point is then clarified as deriving from a redundancy in the verse. The Torah already states elsewhere *v'chi yutan mayim al zera* — if water comes upon seeds they become susceptible. Why does it restate the principle? *lav l'asuyei chibas hakodesh* — is it not to teach us that the only genuine track of susceptibility is food with liquid, and that *chibat hakodesh* is inherently a different and lesser category?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי חִיבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ? לָא: חַד בְּטוּמְאַת מֵת וְחַד בְּטוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Is it **not to exclude** items rendered susceptible to impurity due to **regard for sanctity?** The Gemara rejects that proof: **No,** both verses teach that food becomes impure only after it is rendered susceptible to impurity by one of the seven liquids. **One** verse is referring to **impurity** imparted by a **corpse, and one** verse is referring to **impurity** imparted by the carcass of **a creeping animal.**

The *gemara* rejects this inference as well: the verse repeats the principle because it needs to address two distinct categories of *tumah* — *tumas meis* and *tumas sheretz* — each of which might otherwise have been thought to require different rules regarding *hechsher*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן טוּמְאַת מֵת – הָתָם הוּא דְּבָעֵי הֶכְשֵׁר, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, אֲבָל שֶׁרֶץ דִּמְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה – אֵימָא לָא לִיבְעֵי הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** both verses **are necessary, as had** the Torah **taught us** the requirement of being rendered susceptible to impurity only with regard to **impurity** imparted by **a corpse,** one would have concluded that **it is there** that food **requires** being **rendered susceptible** to impurity by one of the seven liquids in order to become impure, **due to** the fact **that** the impurity imparted by a corpse is less stringent, as a portion of a corpse the size of **a lentil-bulk does not render** people or vessels **impure. But** in the case of impurity imparted by the carcass of **a creeping animal,** which is more stringent, **as** the creeping animal **renders** people or vessels **impure** with a portion of it the size of **a lentil-bulk, say** that food **does not require** to be **rendered susceptible** by one of the seven liquids in order to become impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן שֶׁרֶץ, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, אֲבָל מֵת דִּמְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה – אֵימָא לָא לִיבְעֵי הֶכְשֵׁר, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the Torah **taught us** the requirement of being rendered susceptible to impurity only with regard to impurity imparted by the carcass of **a creeping animal,** one would have concluded that it is there that food must be rendered susceptible to impurity, **due to** the fact **that** the impurity imparted by a creeping animal is less stringent, as a creeping animal **does not render** people or vessels **impure** with **impurity** that lasts **seven** days. **But** with regard to **a corpse, which renders** people or vessels **impure** with **impurity** that lasts **seven** days, **say** that food **does not require** to be **rendered susceptible** by one of the seven liquids in order to become impure from it. Therefore, **it is necessary** for the Torah to teach both verses.

The argument runs as follows: had the Torah only addressed *tumas meis*, one might have thought that it is specifically with *meis*-impurity that the food must be *huchshar*, since the impurity of a corpse does not transfer in amounts as small as a lentil (*adasha*) — it requires at least an olive-size quantity — and so perhaps this more "limited" form of *tumah* requires the food to have been rendered susceptible. One might then have supposed that *tumas sheretz*, which transfers even from a lentil-sized piece, is more powerful in terms of the threshold needed and might make the *hechsher* requirement unnecessary. Conversely, had the Torah only addressed *tumas sheretz*, one might have thought that because sheretz-*tumah* is weaker in duration — it causes only a one-day impurity, unlike corpse-*tumah* which causes a seven-day impurity — perhaps the stronger, longer-lasting corpse-*tumah* renders the food susceptible even without *hechsher*. Both verses are therefore necessary, and neither is available to teach anything about *chibat hakodesh*. The question of *reish lakish* thus remains unanswered.

## Sacrifice Meat Source

### Vayikra Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא״, הַאי בָּשָׂר דְּאִתַּכְשַׁר, בְּמַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that it may be learned **by inference** that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish holds **that regard for sanctity** renders sacred items subject to ritual impurity **by Torah law,** as his dilemma was limited to whether one counts the descending levels of impurity from those susceptible items, i.e., first-degree impurity, second-degree impurity, not the impurity of the sacred item itself. **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? **If we say** that it is derived **from** that **which is written: "And the flesh that touches any impure** item shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 7:19), then it must be ascertained: **This flesh that was rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity, **by what means** was it rendered susceptible?

The *gemara* identifies the biblical anchor for *chibat hakodesh* in a verse from *vayikra*: *v'ha'basar asher yiga b'chol tamei* — "the meat that touches anything *tamei* may not be eaten." This verse presupposes that sacrificial meat is susceptible to *tumah* — it can become *tamei* when it touches something *tamei*. The question is: what rendered this meat susceptible in the first place?

### Machshir Puzzle

For food to become *tamei*, it must first have been *huchshar* — rendered susceptible through contact with a liquid. The verse thus implies that the sacrificial meat had been *huchshar* somehow. What was the source?

### No Sacrificial Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא דְּאִתַּכְשַׁר בְּדָם, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן לְדַם קָדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכְשִׁיר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ עַל הָאָרֶץ תִּשְׁפְּכֶנּוּ כַּמָּיִם״ – דָּם הַנִּשְׁפָּךְ כַּמַּיִם מַכְשִׁיר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִשְׁפָּךְ כַּמַּיִם אֵינוֹ מַכְשִׁיר!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say that it was rendered susceptible** to impurity **by means of** the **blood** of the animal, this is difficult. **But doesn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From where** is it derived **with regard to blood of sacrificial** animals **that it does not render** food **susceptible** to impurity? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "You shall not eat it; you shall pour it upon the earth like water"** (Deuteronomy 12:24). **Blood** of a non-sacred animal, **which is poured like water** when it is slaughtered, **renders** food **susceptible** to ritual impurity. By contrast, blood of a sacrificial animal, **which is not poured like water** but is presented on the altar, **does not render** food **susceptible** to impurity.

*Ileima de'itkashar b'dam* — perhaps the meat came into contact with blood, just as our *mishna* speaks of meat being rendered susceptible through blood contact? But this cannot apply to sacrificial blood. *rabbi chiya bar abba* said in the name of *rabbi yochanan*: the blood of *kodshim* — sacrificial blood — is not *machshir*. The source for this is that same verse about *shechita* blood: *lo tochelenu al ha'aretz tishpechenu kamayim* — "do not eat it, spill it on the ground like water." From this we derive: *dam ha'nishpach kamayim machshir* — blood that you may spill like water renders susceptible. *she'eino nishpach kamayim* — blood that you do not spill like water, i.e., sacrificial blood which must be caught and applied to the altar, *eino machshir* — does not render susceptible. Sacrificial blood, therefore, cannot be the source of *hechsher* for sacrificial meat.

### Temple Liquids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאִתַּכְשַׁר בְּמַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא, וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַשְׁקֵי בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא לֹא דַּיָּין שֶׁהֵן דְּכַן, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין מַכְשִׁירִין, וְכִי תֵּימָא תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַדָּם, וְהָא ״מַשְׁקֵי״ קָאָמַר! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאִתַּכְשַׁר בְּחִבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps say **that** the flesh **was rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **by means of the liquids of the** Temple **abattoir. But didn't Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, say:** With regard to **the liquids of the** Temple **abattoir, not only are they ritually pure, but they do not** even **render** food **susceptible** to impurity. **And if you would say** that one should **explain** the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, as referring exclusively **to blood,** and the other liquids render food susceptible, **but doesn't he say: Liquids,** in the plural? **Rather,** is it **not that** the flesh **was rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **via regard for sanctity?**

Perhaps the meat was rendered susceptible by the *mashkei beit mitbechaya* — the liquids of the slaughterhouse, a phrase the *gemara* uses in *shabbat* and elsewhere to refer to the liquids found in the *beit haMikdash*. *rabbi yose bar rabbi chanina* taught that these liquids are not only pure — *lo dayan she'hen dachein* — but beyond that, *ela she'ein machshirin* — they do not render food susceptible to *tumah*. This ruling was among the testimonies preserved from the period of *eduyot*, attributed to *yose ben yoezer*. The liquids of the Temple do not become *tamei* even when *tumah* touches them, and moreover they do not make other food susceptible.

*Tosafot* addresses why Temple liquids should uniquely lack this *machshir* capacity. *tosafot* explains that the underlying principle has little to do with the Temple setting as such. Rather, it concerns whether liquid is *mechubar* — attached to the ground, as in a lake or spring — or *talush* — detached, as in a vessel. According to this view, only liquid still connected to the ground is *machshir* at the biblical level; detached liquid is not *machshir* biblically. Since most liquids in the *beit haMikdash* are in vessels and thus detached from the ground, they are not *machshir*. *tosafot* notes that one could in principle construct a scenario in which liquid in the Temple did remain connected to the ground — such as if meat fell into the *kiyor* — but that is the exception, not the norm.

In any case, the *gemara* establishes that Temple liquids as a category do not render food susceptible. It further notes that the text uses the plural *mashkei* — liquids — indicating that this rule extends to all liquids in the Temple, not only blood. So blood of sacrifices cannot be the *machshir*, and other Temple liquids cannot be the *machshir* either.

### River Water Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ פָּרָה שֶׁל זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים, וְהֶעֱבִירָהּ בַּנַּחַל, שְׁחָטָהּ וַעֲדַיִין מַשְׁקֶה טוֹפֵחַ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And perhaps** the verse can be explained **in accordance with** the statement that **Rav Yehuda** says that **Shmuel says, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The verse is referring to a case **where one had a cow** that was to be sacrificed as **a peace offering, and** since the owner is entitled to the meat and the hide of the animal, in order to improve their quality **he conveyed it through the river** and **slaughtered** the animal **while** the **liquid was still upon it** and the animal was **damp.** That liquid rendered the meat susceptible to impurity.

The *gemara* entertains the possibility articulated by *rav yehuda* in the name of *shmuel*: perhaps the meat came into contact with an ordinary liquid outside the Temple. The example given is of someone who owned a cow designated as a *shelamim* sacrifice. Since the owner would benefit from the meat and hide, he had every interest in maximizing the animal's quality. Immediately before bringing it to the Temple, he passed it through a river — *v'he'evira benahar* — so that the animal would be wet when slaughtered, which makes it easier to flay the hide cleanly. *v'adayin mashka tofei'ach aleiha* — the liquid is still dripping on the animal when it is slaughtered. *rashi* notes that the liquid would be on the hide rather than the meat directly, but holds that it drips from the hide to the meat. *tosafot* takes a different approach, arguing that the hide is considered contiguous with the meat, so liquid on the hide counts as liquid on the meat. In any event, this scenario presents a case where sacrificial meat was rendered susceptible through ordinary river water — not through *chibat hakodesh* at all. The verse in *vayikra* might simply be addressing such a case.

## Kodesh Makes Non-Food

### Vehabasar Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסֵּיפָא: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר״, לְרַבּוֹת עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה. עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה בְּנֵי אֲכִילָה נִינְהוּ? אֶלָּא חִבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ מַכְשְׁרָא לְהוּ, וּמְשַׁוְּיָא לְהוּ אוֹכֶל. הָכָא נָמֵי, חִבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ מַכְשַׁרְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather** the proof is **from the latter** portion of that verse: "And the flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire. **And the flesh,** every pure person may eat flesh" (Leviticus 7:19). The Gemara explains: The second mention of the term "and the flesh" in the verse is superfluous and serves **to include** the *halakha* that with regard to sacred **wood and frankincense,** impurity disqualifies them from being burned on the altar. Are **wood and frankincense edible** and therefore included in the verse: "Of all food that may be eaten, on which water comes, shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34)? **Rather,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish derived from this that **regard for sanctity renders** them **susceptible** to ritual impurity **and renders their** status like that of **food. Here too,** in the case of a dry portion of flour that was not mixed with the oil of meal offerings, **regard for sanctity renders** it **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

The *gemara* answers that the source for *chibat hakodesh* as a biblical principle lies not in the first part of the verse but in what follows: *v'ha'basar* — "and the meat." This word is apparently redundant, since the verse could have simply stated *kol tahor yochal* — "any pure person shall eat." The extra word *v'ha'basar* — "and as for the meat" — is therefore expounded: *lerabbos eitzim ulevona* — to include items that are not meat at all, specifically wood and frankincense that are burned upon the altar.

### Wood & Frankincense

The *gemara* immediately raises the obvious difficulty: *eitzim ulevona bnei achila ninhu* — wood and frankincense are not foods. *tumah* applies to foods; it does not apply to wood or incense in their ordinary state. How then can these items become *tamei*?

### Kodesh Transforms

The answer: *ella chibas hakodesh machshar lehu u'mashvei lehu ochel* — the dearness of *kodesh* renders them *huchshar* and treats them as food. *chibat hakodesh* operates on two levels here simultaneously: it makes things susceptible to *tumah* (*machshir*), and it transforms even non-food items into objects that can receive *tumah* as if they were food. This is a striking double *chidush*. It is not merely that a qualifying liquid was applied to ordinary food — rather, the sanctity of the object's purpose elevates it, making wood and frankincense destined for the altar susceptible in the same way food is. *hacha nami chibas hakodesh machsharta* — here too, in the case of *minchah* flour untouched by oil, the dearness of *kodesh* is the *machshir*. This establishes *chibat hakodesh* as a genuine biblical principle.

## Chibat Hakodesh Levels

### Back to Reish Lakish


With *chibat hakodesh* established as a biblical principle, the *gemara* returns to the unresolved question of *reish lakish*. Yes, *chibat hakodesh* makes things susceptible at the biblical level — that much is now clear. But what is the precise scope of this biblical *machshir*?

### Two Possibilities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? כִּי מַהְנְיָא חִבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ לִפְסוּלָא דְּגוּפֵיהּ, אֲבָל לְמִימְנֵא בֵּיהּ רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי לָא, אוֹ דִּלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the resolution of the dilemma raised by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: **When regard for sanctity is effective** in rendering an item susceptible to impurity, is it only **to disqualify** that item **itself, but to count** the descending levels of **first-degree** impurity **and second-degree** impurity, it is **not** effective; **or perhaps** once it is rendered susceptible to impurity there **is no difference** whether it is rendered susceptible by means of regard for sanctity or by means of contact with liquids? The Gemara answers: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Ki mahanei chibas hakodesh lifsulei degufei* — perhaps *chibat hakodesh* operates only to invalidate the object itself: when *tamei* touches it, the sanctified item becomes *pasul* — unfit for use. *aval limmnei beih rishon v'sheini lo* — but it does not create real *tumah* with transmissible degrees. The item is invalidated, but *tamei* status does not flow from it to other objects in a *rishon*-*sheini* chain. Alternatively — *o dilma lo shena* — perhaps there is no difference at all between *chibat hakodesh* and ordinary liquid-based *hechsher*, and the item genuinely enters the full structure of *tumah* transmission.

### Teiku Resolution

The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the question remains unresolved. Regarding *chibat hakodesh*, we do not know whether it merely creates *pesul* or whether it generates full transmissible *tumah*.

### Parallel Uncertainty

And as the *gemara* makes explicit in closing, the same unresolved uncertainty applies directly to *rabbi shimon*'s position that *shechita* functions as a *machshir*. Is *rabbi shimon*'s ruling biblical or only Rabbinic? And even if it is biblical, does it create full *tumah* transmission, or only *pesul*? These questions remain open, forming a precise parallel to the unresolved *teiku* regarding *chibat hakodesh*.

With this, the *gemara* concludes its extended treatment of *tumah v'tahara* and *hechsher zera'im*, and the next *mishna* will return us to the central concerns of *shechita* and *chullin*.
