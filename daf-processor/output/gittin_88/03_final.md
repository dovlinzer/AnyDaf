# Gittin 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two-Column Get

### Two Columns, Signatures…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שִׁיֵּיר מִקְצָת הַגֵּט וּכְתָבוֹ בְּדַף הַשֵּׁנִי, וְהָעֵדִים מִלְּמַטָּה – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a scribe **left out part of the bill of divorce and wrote it in the second column,** i.e., the bill of divorce is written in two columns on one paper, **and the** signatures of the **witnesses are beneath** the second column, it is a **valid** bill of divorce.

The *mishna* before us — located on 88b, roughly in the middle of the page — opens with a case that is in many ways a continuation of the previous day's discussion, albeit the simpler version of it. The case is: *shi'er miktzat haget uchtavo bedaf hasheni vehayedim milmata kasher* — he wrote part of the *get* in one column and the remainder in a second column, and the witnesses signed at the bottom of the second column. Here *daf* carries the sense of *yeri'ah*, a column, as in a column of a Torah scroll. The ruling is *kasher*: the witnesses sign at the conclusion of the *get* after the entire text has been written across both columns, and that is sufficient.

### Signatures Top/Side

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָתְמוּ עֵדִים בְּרֹאשׁ הַדַּף, מִן הַצַּד, אוֹ מֵאַחֲרָיו; בְּגֵט פָּשׁוּט – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** If the **witnesses signed at the top of the column, on the side, or on the back of an ordinary,** non-folded **bill of divorce,** it is **invalid.**

The *mishna* then presents variations on signature placement. *Chatmu ha-eidim berosh ha-daf* — if the witnesses signed at the top of the second column rather than the bottom — or *min ha-tzad*, at the side, meaning in the margins of the document rather than at its conclusion — these are problematic. The intuition is straightforward: a contract is signed at its end. Signing in the margins or at the top does not follow the expected structure of a legally executed document.

### Signing on Back

A third case: *o me'achorav beget pashut pasul* — if they signed on the back of a plain *get*. This is distinguished from a *get mekushar*, the folded-style *get* treated in earlier passages where signing on particular external sections was the correct practice. For a standard *get*, signing on the back is *pasul*.

### Heads Touching Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקִּיף רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְהָעֵדִים בָּאֶמְצַע – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** If the scribe **placed the top of this** bill of divorce **next to the top of that** bill of divorce so that both are written in the same column but with the text in opposite directions, **and the witnesses** signed **in the middle,** between the bills of divorce, **both** bills of divorce **are invalid.**

The *mishna* then introduces cases requiring illustration, and those following along without a visual aid will need to hold the picture in mind. The first case: *hikif rosho shel zeh betzad rosho shel zeh veha-eidim ba-emtza sheneihem pesulin* — imagine two *gittin* placed so that the top (*rosh*) of one faces the top of the other, with the witnesses signing in the middle between them. Both *gittin* are invalid. The reason, as becomes clear, is that the witnesses appear at the head of one of the documents rather than at its conclusion.

### Tails Touching Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹפוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד סוֹפוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְהָעֵדִים בָּאֶמְצַע – אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים נִקְרִין עִמּוֹ, כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** If he placed **the end of this** bill of divorce **next to the end of that** bill of divorce, **and the witnesses** signed **in the middle** between them, the bill of divorce **with which the witnesses'** signatures **are read,** i.e., the bill that is written in the same direction as the signatures, **is valid.**

The next case: *sofo shel zeh betzad sofo shel zeh veha-eidim ba-emtza* — the tail (*sof*) of one *get* faces the tail of the other, with the witnesses between them. The ruling is *et she-nikra'im imo kasher* — the one that can be read together with the witnesses is valid. Visually: if *get* one is written normally and the witnesses appear at its bottom, and *get* two is written upside-down below the witnesses, then *get* one's witnesses are properly placed at its conclusion, and it is valid. The second *get*, read from the other direction, has the witnesses at its head — which is invalid.

## Two-Column Get (II)

### Head-Tail Adjacent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד סוֹפוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְהָעֵדִים בָּאֶמְצַע – אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים נִקְרִין בְּסוֹפוֹ, כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** If he placed **the top of this** bill of divorce **next to the end of that** bill of divorce, **and the witnesses** signed **in the middle,** the bill of divorce **at the end of which the witnesses are read,** i.e., the upper bill of divorce, **is valid.**

The final configuration: *rosho shel zeh betzad sofo shel zeh* — the head of one *get* is adjacent to the tail of another, with a single set of witnesses. Again, the ruling is *et she-nikra'im imo basof kasher* — the one for which the witnesses appear at the end is valid, and the other is not, for the same reason. In each of these cases, the governing principle is that witnesses must appear at the conclusion of the document they are signing.

## Hebrew-Greek Mix

### One Get, Two Languages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּט שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ עִבְרִית וְעֵדָיו יְווֹנִית; יְווֹנִית וְעֵדָיו עִבְרִית; עֵד אֶחָד עִבְרִי וְעֵד אֶחָד יְווֹנִי; כָּתַב סוֹפֵר וְעֵד – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a bill of divorce that was written in Hebrew and its witnesses** signed **in Greek,** or that was written **in Greek and its witnesses** signed **in Hebrew,** or in which **one witness** signed **in Hebrew and one witness** signed **in Greek,** or if a bill of divorce has **the writing of a scribe,** and the scribe identifies his handwriting, **and** one **witness** verifies his signature, it is **valid** as though two witnesses testified to ratify their signatures.

The *mishna* continues with a series of cases involving language. *Get she-ketavo Ivrit ve-chatamo Yevanit, o Yevanit ve-chatamo Ivrit, ed echad Ivri ve-ed echad Yevanit* — a single *get* written in Hebrew and signed in Greek, or the reverse, or one witness signing in Hebrew and the other in Greek — all of these are valid. This is the simple version of what was treated in more complex form in the previous day's material: so long as it is a single *get*, the mixing of languages and scripts presents no problem.

### Scribe + Witness

Then: *katav sofer ve-ed kasher* — the scribe wrote and signed, along with one additional witness. As the *gemara* clarifies, *katav* here really means *chatam* — the scribe himself signed the document in addition to writing it. This is not a case of only one witness; rather, the scribe's signature counts as one of the two required signatures.

The *gemara* identifies this ruling as the position of Rabbi Yossi: *ve-amar Rav Chisda man Rav Yossi hi*. The underlying logic relates to the rule that a *shliach* may not deviate from the husband's instructions. If a *shliach* could alter arrangements, one would worry that the scribe, having written the entire *get*, might be permitted to sign out of courtesy — even though the husband never designated him as a witness — and that a proper witness might then be displaced. But if a *shliach* cannot change what the husband specified, then the only way the scribe could be signing is if the husband himself, speaking directly, authorized it. Under those circumstances, the scribe's signature is entirely legitimate.

## Witness Name Variations

### Name Forms Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי, עֵד״ – כָּשֵׁר. ״בֶּן אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי, עֵד״ – כָּשֵׁר; ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי בֶּן אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי״, וְלֹא כָּתַב ״עֵד״ – כָּשֵׁר, וְכָךְ הָיוּ נְקִיֵּי הַדַּעַת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם עוֹשִׂין.
>
> **Translation:** As for the wording of the signature, if a witness signed: **So-and-so, witness,** without mentioning his father's name, it is **valid.** Similarly, if he did not write his name and instead wrote: **Son of so-and-so, witness,** it is **valid.** If he wrote: **So-and-so, son of so-and-so, but did not write** the word **witness,** it is **valid. And this is what the scrupulous people of Jerusalem would do,** i.e., they would sign without the word witness.

The *mishna* turns to different forms of witness identification. *Ish ploni ed kasher* — if a witness signed simply using his first name without his father's name, this is valid. *Ben ish ploni ed* — if he signed only as "son of so-and-so," giving his father's name but not his own first name, this too is valid. *Ish ploni ben ish ploni* without writing *ed* at all — simply his full name without the word "witness" — is also *kasher*, since it is self-evident why one would sign a *get* if not as a witness.

### Jerusalem Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתַב חֲנִיכָתוֹ וַחֲנִיכָתָהּ – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** As for the names of the husband and wife, if the scribe **wrote his surname [*ḥanikhato*]** or nickname **and her surname** or nickname, it is **valid.**

*V'kach hayu nikkei ha-da'at she-bi-Yerushalayim osim* — this was the practice of the scrupulous and refined people of Jerusalem. The connection to the next clause, *katav chanichato va-chanichato kasher* — if one wrote a nickname or family surname — may indicate that what the people of Jerusalem did was to sign using family names, emphasizing the family lineage rather than drawing attention to themselves individually. Either way, the ruling stands: all of these forms of identification are valid.

## Get Continuity Issues

### One or Two Gets?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא הָנֵי תְּרֵי גִּיטֵּי הֲווֹ; וְאִיתְרְמִי לֵיהּ זְמַן דְּקַמָּא, וְעֵדִים דְּבָתְרָא; וְגַזְיֵיהּ לִזְמַן דְּבָתְרָא וְעֵדִים דְּקַמָּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to a bill of divorce that is written in two columns, **let us be concerned** that **perhaps these were two** adjacent **bills of divorce,** the second one higher up on the page than the first, **and the date** written at the top **of the first** bill of divorce **and the** signatures of the **witnesses** written at the bottom **of the latter** bill of divorce **happened** to be written next to each other, **and** someone **cut out the date of the latter** bill of divorce **and** the signatures of **the witnesses of the first** so that it appears to be a single bill of divorce written in two columns.

With the basic cases of the two-column *get* established, the *gemara* raises a pointed question. How do we know that two columns constitute a single *get* rather than two separate *gittin*? The concern is concrete: perhaps someone cut part of one *get* and part of another and assembled them together, so that what appears to be a continuous document is actually a patchwork of two different instruments.

### Rava: Space Below

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: כְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ רֶיוַח מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Abba says** that **Rav says:** It is a case **where there is space** on the paper **below** the first bill of divorce, so it is clear that nothing was cut out.

*Amar Rava ke-she-yihye revach milmata* — Rava answers that there is a visible space at the bottom of the first column, indicating that the text continues and the column simply ran out of room. This space signals continuity — the document picks up where it left off.

### Cut and Paste Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא זְמַן דְּבָתְרָא מִיגָּז גַּזְיֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And perhaps he cut off the date of the latter** bill of divorce, making it look like a continuation of the first one, which was actually never finished?

The *gemara* immediately pushes back: *u-dilma zman d'batra migaz gayiz* — perhaps someone cut off the date from the second column. The space below is still there, but the authenticity of the connection between the two columns remains in doubt.

### Rav Papa: Space Above

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: כְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ רֶיוַח מִלְּמַטָּה; הָכָא נָמֵי – כְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ רֶיוַח מִלְּמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as Rabbi Abba says** that **Rav says** that it is a case **where there is space below** the first bill of divorce, **so too,** it is a case **where there is space above** the latter one, so it is clearly not a separate bill of divorce that was cut.

*Amar Rav Papa ke-she-yihye revach milmata, hacha nami ke-she-yihye revach mil'mala* — Rav Papa adds a parallel answer: just as there is a space below the first column, so too there is a space above the second column, indicating it begins mid-text. Both columns show visible evidence of continuation. For both spaces to be coincidentally present in two fraudulently combined documents would be extremely improbable.

### Dating Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא אִימְּלוֹכֵי אִימְּלִיךְ וּכְתַב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** although it is clearly one bill of divorce, **perhaps** the scribe wrote the first part of the bill of divorce and then the husband **changed his mind** about divorcing his wife, thereby canceling the bill of divorce, and afterward he changed his mind again **and** the scribe then **wrote** the rest of the bill of divorce in the second column, after the first part was already canceled.

Yet another concern is raised: *u-dilma imlochi imlach ve-katav* — perhaps the husband changed his mind mid-composition, and the *get* was written across two different days, completing the first column on one day and the second on another. This would create a problem of *get yashan* — an old *get* — where there is a meaningful gap between the date written on the *get* and when it was actually completed, with all the attendant difficulties: the wife might be considered divorced from the earlier date, potentially creating issues around her subsequent conduct, or questions about property rights. The *gemara* entertains this concern but then asks: if the text ended in the middle of a sentence — say, the first column ended with *harei at* and the second began with *muteret le-chol adam* — is one really to worry that the husband changed his mind mid-sentence and resumed writing months later? The *gemara* acknowledges that such a scenario would be far-fetched.

### Parchment Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ״ מִלְּמַטָּה, וּ״מוּתֶּרֶת״ מִלְּמַעְלָה. וְדִלְמָא אִיתְרְמִי לֵיהּ? כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is a case **where he wrote: You are hereby, on the bottom** of the first column, **and** continued: **Permitted** to marry any man, **at the top** of the second column. In this case there is obvious continuity between the columns, and he certainly did not change his mind in the meantime. The Gemara asks: **But perhaps it happened** that he stopped in the middle of the sentence? The Gemara answers: **We are not concerned to such a degree;** it is highly unlikely that he changed his mind in the middle of a sentence.

Rashi offers a more fundamental answer: *de-yada bei mi-tachtah de-megilta* — one can tell from the physical characteristics of the parchment itself. In antiquity, writing surfaces such as *klaf* or vellum were individually prepared, and each piece had its own distinctive texture and grain, much as one can tell from the grain of wood that a plank has not been joined from two separate pieces. If both columns share the same physical characteristics — the same coloration, the same grain, the same aging — it is evident that they constitute a single original document and are not a cut-and-paste fabrication. Rashi's answer thus addresses the foundational concern: the very materiality of the document testifies to its integrity.

## Signature Orientation

### Top/Side Signing Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רַב חָתֵים מִן הַצַּד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **Is that so? Wouldn't Rav sign on the side?**

Returning to the case of witnesses signing at the top of the page or to the side: the *mishna* ruled this invalid. The *gemara* expresses surprise — *eini*? Is that really so?

### Rav's Practice

*Ve-ha Rav chatam min ha-tzad* — but Rav himself used to sign from the side! If signing to the side were categorically invalid, how could Rav have done so?

### Letter Roof Orientation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם בְּשֶׁגַּגּוֹ כְּלַפֵּי כְתָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of Rav, he would sign **with the roof,** i.e., the top, **of** his signature **facing the text** of the document, so it was clear that he was signing the document.

The *gemara* resolves the question: *de-hatam bi-shgago kelapei ha-ktav* — the roof (*shigago*, meaning the top edge) of Rav's letters faced toward the text of the *get*. That is, Rav signed sideways, but oriented so that the heads of the letters pointed inward toward the document. The *mishna*'s invalidation applies to the opposite orientation — when the letters face away from the text. There is thus a meaningful distinction between a signature that is laterally placed but directed toward the document versus one that faces outward.

### Vertical vs Horizontal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּקָתָנֵי: הִקִּיף רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְהָעֵדִים בָּאֶמְצַע – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִין; וְלִיחְזֵי הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ כְּלַפֵּי כְתָב, וְלִיתַּכְשַׁר! הָתָם, דִּרְמֵי לֵיהּ כְּעִיבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** this contradicts **that which is taught** in the mishna: If the scribe **placed the top of this** bill of divorce **next to the top of that** bill of divorce, writing both bills of divorce in the same column in opposite directions, **and the witnesses** signed **in the middle** between them, **both** bills of divorce **are invalid.** According to the above answer, **let us see** with regard to **which** one **of them** the top of the witnesses' signatures **faces the text, and let it be rendered valid.** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the mishna, it is a case **where** the signatures are not parallel to the text. Rather, **they are placed like** a door **bolt,** perpendicular to the text, so they are not written in the same direction as either of the bills of divorce.

This resolution, however, raises a further question from a *baraita*: the case of *hikif rosho shel zeh betzad rosho shel zeh ve-ha-eidim ba-emtza sheneihem pesulin* — heads facing heads, witnesses in the middle, both invalid. In that case, the roof of the letters of the witnesses' signatures does face the text of one of the *gittin*. If facing inward is sufficient to validate the signature, why are both *gittin* invalid? The *gemara* answers: *hatam d'dami lei ke-ivura* — in the case of the *mishna*'s invalidation, the problem is not the orientation of the letters but that the signatures were written vertically, running up and down the page rather than across it horizontally. Written that way, the signature is ambiguous — one cannot determine which *get* it belongs to, since it is perpendicular to both. Hence: *lo adin nikarim imo* — neither document can clearly claim the witnesses as its own.

### Conclusion: Rav Not Signing Gets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי: רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד סוֹפוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְהָעֵדִים בָּאֶמְצַע – אֶת שֶׁהָעֵדִים נִיקְּרִין בְּסוֹפוֹ, כָּשֵׁר. וְאִי דִּרְמֵי כְּעִיבְרָא – לָאו בַּהֲדֵי הַאי מִיקְּרֵי, וְלָאו בַּהֲדֵי הַאי מִיקְּרֵי! אֶלָּא רַב בְּדִיסְקֵי הֲוָה חָתֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** consider **the latter clause** of the mishna, **which teaches** that if the scribe placed **the top of this** bill of divorce **next to the end of that** bill of divorce, **and the witnesses** signed **in the middle,** the bill of divorce **at the end of which the witnesses are read is valid. And if** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the signatures **are placed like a bolt,** perpendicular to the text, **they are neither read with this** bill of divorce, **nor are they read with that** one. **Rather,** a different answer must be offered: **Rav would sign** only **letters** and rulings on the side, where the location of his signature is inconsequential. By contrast, bills of divorce and monetary documents must be signed beneath the text.

Having exhausted attempts to harmonize Rav's practice with the cases involving multiple documents, the *gemara* concludes: *ela Rav be-duchti hava chatim* — Rav was signing in a different context entirely. He was signing court summonses or judicial orders, not *gittin*. His lateral signature was an authorization or approval on a court document, not the witnessing signature on a *get*. The *mishna*'s rules about signature placement apply to *gittin* specifically; Rav's practice was in an entirely separate domain and poses no challenge. Rashi notes that the term for such documents is something like a summons — an order confirming that a party may be summoned before the court — and that Rav's signature was simply his judicial approval, not a witnessing act governed by the laws of *get* signatures.

## Language Variants

### Language Variants OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּט שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ עִבְרִית וְכוּ׳ כָּתַב סוֹפֵר וְעֵד כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: ״חָתַם סוֹפֵר״ שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna: With regard to **a bill of divorce that was written in Hebrew** and its witnesses signed in Greek, or that was written in Greek and its witnesses signed in Hebrew, or in which one witness signed in Hebrew and one witness signed in Greek, or if a bill of divorce has **the writing of a scribe,** and the scribe identifies his handwriting, **and** one **witness** verifies his signature, it is **valid** as though two witnesses testified to ratify their signatures. **Rabbi Yirmeya says: We learned** that the mishna means that **the scribe signed** the bill of divorce as a witness; his handwriting in the text is not sufficient.

The *gemara* briefly revisits the *mishna*'s cases of language variation. A *get* written in one language and signed in another — Hebrew written, Greek signed, or the reverse — is valid in all its combinations. These rulings require little elaboration, as the principle is clear: for a single *get*, the language of composition and the language of signature are independent of one another.

### Scribe Signs

Then the scribe signed:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** that there is no concern that the scribe signed unlawfully.

The case of *katav sofer ve-ed kasher* — the scribe who also signed — is now taken up in the *gemara*'s own voice. The ruling is identified as the position of Rav Yossi.

### Direct Speech Requirement

The underlying principle is that the scribe is permitted to sign only if the husband spoke directly to him — directly commissioning the *get* and thereby implicitly authorizing the scribe's participation as a signatory. If the husband's instruction reached the scribe through intermediaries, the concern returns: perhaps the scribe was added as a signatory by those intermediaries without the husband's explicit authorization, which is impermissible under Rav Yossi's view that a *shliach* may not unilaterally alter the arrangements specified by the husband.

## Ketubah Recognition

### Recognizing One Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא כְּתוּבַּת חֲתָנִים דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דַּהֲווֹ יָדְעִי לֵיהּ לְטוּפְסָא, וְלַחֲתִימַת יְדָא דְּחַד סָהֲדָא. סְבַר לְאַכְשׁוֹרַהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: ״חָתַם סוֹפֵר״ שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain marriage contract that was brought before Rabbi Abbahu, which** people **recognized** and verified **its formula,** i.e., the scribe's handwriting, **and the signature of one witness.** Rabbi Abbahu **thought to deem it valid** based on the scribe's handwriting and the witness. **Rabbi Yirmeya said to him: We learned** that the mishna means that **the scribe signed** the bill of divorce; his handwriting alone is not sufficient.

A practical case is now introduced. A *ketubah* came before Rabbi Abbahu, and those present recognized the handwriting of the scribe who had written it and the signature of one of the witnesses. The question arose: since the scribe's handwriting and one witness's signature were both recognizable, could the *ketubah* be validated on that basis alone, without verifying the second witness's signature? Rabbi Abbahu was inclined to validate it — *savar Rabbi Abbahu le-achshurah*.

### Two Signatures Required

Rabbi Abba objected: *lav chatam sofer u-shnei eidim tnan* — did we not learn in the *mishna* that the operative case is the scribe's signature plus two additional witnesses? The *mishna*'s permission applies when the scribe himself is a signatory and there are two recognized witness signatures. You cannot substitute recognition of the scribe's handwriting as a *writer* for recognition of his signature as a *witness*. Two proper witness signatures are required; the handwriting of the scribe in his capacity as a scribe is not equivalent.

## Family Names Limits

### Nicknames Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲנִיכַת אָבוֹת בְּגִיטִּין – עַד עֲשָׂרָה דּוֹרוֹת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת – כָּשֵׁר, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 8:6): **A surname** from one's **forefathers** is valid **with regard to bills of divorce until ten generations** after the forefather. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** It is **valid** for only **three generations; from this** point **forward** it is **invalid.**

*Katav chanichato va-chanichato kasher* — if one recorded a nickname or family surname as the identifier of the parties, this is valid for purposes of a *get*.

### Generations Dispute

A *baraita* is cited on how far back one may reach for ancestral identification: *tanu rabbanan chanichat avot be-gittin ad yud dorot* — one may identify a party by an ancestral family name for up to ten generations. *Ve-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer: gimmel dorot kasher* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says three generations suffice and beyond that it is invalid. After ten or three generations — depending on which opinion one follows — the ancestral name may no longer serve as a legal identifier: *mi-kan ve-ilech pasul*.

The logic of the ten-generation position may be connected to a well-known phenomenon in Tanach: the genealogy of Shaul in Divrei HaYamim spans ten generations, even though the Torah in Parshat Lech Lecha telescopes four generations into what Divrei HaYamim shows are actually ten, skipping six intermediate generations because Shaul's more distant ancestor was sufficiently famous that intervening generations could be omitted. The ten-generation limit may reflect this principle — that ancestral identification retains legal currency for up to ten generations.

### Three Generations Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כָּתַב חֲנִיכַת אָבוֹת בְּגִיטִּין עַד שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת? כְּמַאן – כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי קְרָאָה? ״כִּי תוֹלִיד בָּנִים וּבְנֵי בָנִים וְנוֹשַׁנְתֶּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rabbi Ḥanina said,** that **the writing of a surname** from one's **forefathers** is valid **with regard to bills of divorce until three generations** after the forefather? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.** **Rav Huna said: What is the verse** from which this opinion is derived? It is the verse: **"When you will beget children, and children's children, and you will have been long [*venoshantem*]** in the land" (Deuteronomy 4:25). Since the word *venoshantem* stems from the word *yashan*, meaning old, it is derived that after one's grandchildren, or three generations, a matter is considered old and forgotten.

*Ka-man azla ha d'amar Rav Chanina: chatav chanichat avot be-gittin ad gimmel dorot?* — whose opinion does Rav Chanina follow when he rules that ancestral family names in *gittin* are valid for three generations? *Ke-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — he follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. *Amar Rav Huna: mai kra?* — Rav Huna asks what scriptural basis supports the three-generation position.

The *pasuk* cited is from Devarim: *ki tolid banim u-vnei vanim ve-noshantem* — when you have children and grandchildren and you have grown old in the land, and then you will sin and be driven out. The word *ve-noshantem* carries the sense of becoming ancient, of staleness — and Rav Huna reads the sequence as: you, your children, and your grandchildren; after that, the ancestral name has grown too old and too stale to serve as current identification. Beyond three generations it is *noshen* — antiquated — and may no longer be used. A scriptural proof for the ten-generation position is not offered in the *gemara* here, which is conspicuous.

## Seven Kingdoms Aggada

### Seven Kingdoms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לֹא חָרְבָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַד שֶׁעָבְדוּ בָּהּ שִׁבְעָה בָּתֵּי דִינִים עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: יָרׇבְעָם בֶּן נְבָט, וּבַעְשָׁא בֶּן אֲחִיָּה, וְאַחְאָב בֶּן עָמְרִי, וְיֵהוּא בֶּן נִמְשִׁי, וּפֶקַח בֶּן רְמַלְיָהוּ, וּמְנַחֵם בֶּן גָּדִי, וְהוֹשֵׁעַ בֶּן אֵלָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אוּמְלְלָה יוֹלֶדֶת הַשִּׁבְעָה, נָפְחָה נַפְשָׁהּ, בָּאָה שִׁמְשָׁהּ בְּעוֹד יוֹמָם, בּוֹשָׁה וְחָפֵרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another derivation from this verse. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** The first **destruction** of **Eretz Yisrael did not** occur **until seven** dynasties and their **courts committed idol worship in it, and they are these: Jeroboam, son of Nevat; and Baasa, son of Ahijah; and Ahab, son of Omri; and Jehu, son of Nimshi; and Pekah, son of Remaliah; and Menahem, son of Gadi; and Hoshea, son of Elah, as it is stated: "She who has given birth to seven languishes; her spirit droops; her sun is gone down while it was yet day, she is ashamed and confounded"** (Jeremiah 15:9).

At this point the *gemara* takes an *aggadic* turn. *Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: na'atzei de-aggadata* — here is a fine *aggadic* teaching. *Lo chavah Eretz Yisrael ad she-ba'u vah shiv'ah batei dinim shel avodah zarah* — the Land of Israel did not become desolate until seven successive dynasties of *avodah zarah* had arisen within it. The seven are: Yeravam ben Nevat, Acha ben Achiyah, Achav ben Omri, Yehu ben Nimshi, Pekach ben Remalyahu, Menachem ben Gadi, and Hoshea ben Elah. *Va-ilu hein* — these are the seven.

The scriptural basis: *umlala yoledet ha-shiv'ah* — the one who bore seven is desolate. The *gemara* reads *yoledet ha-shiv'ah* not as a mother of seven children but as the land that gave rise to seven royal dynasties of idolatry. *Nafcha nafshah, ba'ah shamshah be-od yomam, bosha va-chafrah* — she breathed her last, her sun set while it was still day, she was covered in shame and disgrace. After seven such dynasties, the Land is left empty of its inhabitants, driven into exile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַמֵּי: מַאי קְרָאָה? ״כִּי תוֹלִיד בָּנִים וּבְנֵי בָנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ammi said: What is the verse** from which it is derived that it was seven dynasties? **"When you will beget children, and children's children,** and you will have been long in the land, and will deal corruptly, and make a graven image, even the form of anything…you will soon utterly perish from off the land" (Deuteronomy 4:25–26). The phrase "when you will beget children and children's children" is interpreted homiletically as indicating seven generations. This is because the term "you will beget [*tolid*]," written in the singular form, indicates one generation, and the word "children," written in the plural form, indicates two generations. Since the word "children" is mentioned three times, seven generations are indicated in total.

*Amar Rav Ashi: mai kra?* — Rav Ashi asks how one derives the number seven from the same *pasuk* of *ki tolid banim u-vnei vanim* cited earlier. The derivation counts as follows: you yourself are one; *banim*, children, adds two; *bnei vanim*, grandchildren of grandchildren — following out the tree — adds four more. One plus two plus four equals seven. Tosafot notes that this arithmetic, while somewhat loose, follows the branching of a genealogical tree, with each generation doubling.

### Hoshea ben Elah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב, כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּהוֹשֵׁעַ בֶּן אֵלָה: ״וַיַּעַשׂ הָרַע בְּעֵינֵי ה׳, רַק לֹא כְּמַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וּכְתִיב: ״עָלָיו עָלָה שַׁלְמַנְאֶסֶר וְגוֹ׳״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav: It is written with regard to Hoshea ben Elah: "And he did that which was evil in the eyes of the Lord, yet not as the kings of Israel** that were before him" (II Kings 17:2). **But it is written** subsequently: **"Against him came up Shalmaneser,** king of Assyria" (II Kings 17:3) and exiled the people of Israel. If Hoshea performed less evil than the previous kings of Israel, why was his generation the one that was punished?

Rav Kahana raises a pointed question to Rav Ashi: it is written of Hoshea ben Elah that *va-ya'as ha-ra be-einei Hashem rak lo ke-malchei Yisrael* — he did evil in the eyes of God, but not like the kings of Israel before him. And yet it is also written that Shalmaneser came up against him and exiled his people. If Hoshea was less wicked than his predecessors, why was he the king under whom the final exile took place? Why did God not wait for a worse king?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: אוֹתָן פַּרְדְּסִיאוֹת שֶׁהוֹשִׁיב יָרׇבְעָם עַל הַדְּרָכִים כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָרֶגֶל, בָּא הוֹשֵׁעַ וּבִיטְּלָן, וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן לֹא עָלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָרֶגֶל. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אוֹתָן שָׁנִים שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָרֶגֶל – יֵלְכוּ בַּשֶּׁבִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to them: Hoshea came and canceled those guards [*pardesei'ot*] that Jeroboam placed on the roads in order to prevent** the subjects of the kingdom of **Israel from ascending to** Jerusalem **on the pilgrimage Festival, and even so** the subjects of the kingdom of **Israel did not ascend to** Jerusalem **on the pilgrimage Festival. The Holy One, Blessed be He,** therefore **said** that for **those years that** the subjects of the kingdom of **Israel did not ascend to** Jerusalem **on the pilgrimage Festival they will go into captivity.**

The answer: *otan prosda'ot she-hoshiv Yeravam ben Nevat she-lo ya'alu Yisrael la-regel, ba Hoshea u-vitlan* — those border checkpoints that Yeravam ben Nevat had established to prevent the people of Israel from going up to Jerusalem for the *regalim*, the pilgrimage festivals, were abolished by Hoshea. Hoshea ben Elah actually removed the blockade. In this respect he was genuinely less wicked: he gave the people back the ability to go to the *Beit HaMikdash*. So why was exile decreed specifically in his time?

*Afilu hachi lo alu Yisrael la-regel* — even so, the people did not go up. It was no longer the king who was stopping them; they simply no longer wanted to go. The sin had become internalized. God's response: *otan shanim she-lo alu Yisrael la-regel yelchu ba-shvi* — for all those years in which Israel chose not to ascend for the *regel*, they would now go into captivity. They had forfeited their connection to the Land, and so the Land would be taken from them. The blame ultimately traces back to Yeravam ben Nevat — *chata ve-hichti et ha-rabbim* — who sinned and caused the many to sin, as stated in Pirkei Avot, though the particular detail about the people's continued non-attendance after Hoshea lifted the restriction goes beyond what Pirkei Avot records.

### Divine Haste

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, דָּרֵשׁ מָרִימָר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁקוֹד ה׳ עַל הָרָעָה וַיְבִיאֶהָ עָלֵינוּ, כִּי צַדִּיק ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Mar Ukva said, and some say** that it was **Rav Ḥisda** who **said** that **Rabbi Yirmeya said,** that **Mareimar interpreted** a verse **homiletically** as follows: **What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And so the Lord has watched over the evil, and brought it upon us; for the Lord our God is righteous [*tzaddik*]"** (Daniel 9:14)? How can it be that **because the Lord, our God, is righteous, the Lord watched over the evil and brought it upon us?**

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Mar Ukva* — and some say *amar Rav Chisda amar Rav Yirmiyah darash Mareimar*: *mai de-chtiv vayishkod Hashem al ha-ra'ah vayevi'eha aleinu ki tzaddik Hashem Elokeinu?* — what does it mean that God was *vayishkod*, that He hastened the evil and brought it upon us, and that this demonstrates that God is righteous? The root *sh-k-d* is the same as in *shekedia*, the almond tree, the first to blossom — suggesting swiftness. But in what sense is it an act of righteousness to hasten punishment?

### Exile Preparation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא צְדָקָה עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁהִגְלָה גָּלוּת צִדְקִיָּהוּ – וַעֲדַיִן גָּלוּת יְכָנְיָה קַיֶּימֶת; דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּגָלוּת יְכָנְיָה: ״הֶחָרָשׁ וְהַמַּסְגֵּר אֶלֶף״;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be interpreted as follows: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, performed charity [*tzedaka*] with** the people of **Israel by exiling** them in **the exile of Zedekiah while the exile of Jeconiah still existed** in Babylonia, **as it is written with regard to the exile of Jeconiah: "And the craftsmen [*ḥarash*] and the smiths [*masger*] a thousand"** (II Kings 24:16). The great scholars, referred to in this verse as craftsmen and smiths, of the generation were exiled with Jeconiah, and they were therefore able to give guidance to those who were exiled in the time of Zedekiah when they came to Babylonia.

The answer involves the structure of the exile itself. The exile did not occur all at once. There was first the exile of Yechonyah — *im ha-golah asher hogleitah im Yechonyah melech Yehudah*, as recalled in Megillat Esther — and then afterward the exile of Tzidkiyahu. *She-dikdek HaKadosh Baruch Hu mi-Yisrael she-heglah galut Tzidkiyahu ve-adain galut Yechonyah kayemet* — God was precise: by the time the exile of Tzidkiyahu arrived, the earlier exiles of Yechonyah were already established in Babylon and had prepared the way.

As the verse records of the exile of Yechonyah: *hacharash ve-ha-masgeir elef* — a thousand *charash* and *masgeir*. Homiletically: *charash she-be-sha'ah she-posachin na'asu ha-kol ke-chereshin* — the *charash* are the *chachamim*, the great sages, so called because when they speak, everyone else becomes as if deaf to all else, silenced by the authority of their words. And *masgeir: keivan she-sogrin, shuv ein poschin* — the *masgeir* are those whose rulings, once closed, are not reopened. A thousand such figures were already in Babylon by the time the second wave arrived. The scholarly infrastructure — the *batei midrashos*, the *chachamim*, the capacity for Torah life — was already in place. The later exiles arrived not into a spiritual vacuum but into a functioning community. This is the righteousness of God: the hastening of the first exile allowed the second exile to survive with their identity intact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חָרָשׁ״ – שֶׁבְּשָׁעָה שֶׁפּוֹתְחִין, נַעֲשׂוּ הַכֹּל כְּחֵרְשִׁין. ״מַסְגֵּר״ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁסּוֹגְרִין, שׁוּב אֵינָן פּוֹתְחִין. וְכַמָּה – אֶלֶף.
>
> **Translation:** Why are the great scholars referred to as craftsmen [*ḥarash*] and smiths [*masger*]? ***Ḥarash*,** containing the same letters as *ḥeresh*, meaning deaf, is an allusion to the fact **that when they would introduce** a statement of Torah **everyone would become like deaf persons,** as they would not understand. ***Masger*,** which stems from the root *samekh*, *gimmel*, *reish*, meaning to close, alludes to the fact that **once they would close** a certain matter that they did not comprehend, **no** one **would introduce** it **again,** as no one was capable of solving such a problem. **And how many** scholars were there? There were **one thousand.**

### 850-Year Mercy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: שֶׁהִקְדִּים שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים לִ״וְנוֹשַׁנְתֶּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says** that God performed charity with the people of Israel **by advancing** their exile **by two years** relative to the numerical value of the word ***venoshantem*** that appears in the verse. Although the numerical value of the word is 852, God exiled the Jewish people from the land after only 850 years, so that the punishment mentioned subsequently, i.e., utter annihilation, would not be fulfilled either.

*Ulla amar she-hikdim shtei shanim le-ve-noshantem* — Ulla offers a different reading of God's righteousness. The *gematria* of *ve-noshantem* is 852. Yet God brought the exile after only 850 years — two years early. *Vayishkod Hashem al ha-ra'ah*: God hastened the evil by two years. Why is this righteous? Because the full text of the verse concludes with a warning of total annihilation: *ki avod toveidun* — you will utterly perish. Had God waited the full 852 years, the verse implies there would be nothing left of the Jewish people. By hastening the exile by two years, God cut short the countdown before the point of no return, ensuring that something of the Jewish people would survive. The *tzedakah* was in the timing: a slightly earlier, less complete punishment rather than a delayed, total destruction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, ״מְהֵרָה״ דְּמָרֵי עָלְמָא – תַּמְנֵי מְאָה וְחַמְשִׁים וְתַרְתֵּי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: Learn from** this numerical value that **soon [*mehera*] for the Master of the World is eight hundred and fifty-two** years, as it is stated in the verse in Deuteronomy: "You will soon [*maher*] utterly perish." Since the Jewish people dwelled in Eretz Yisrael for almost this amount of time, it is apparently considered soon.

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov: shema mina meheirah* — we learn from this what "quickly" means from God's perspective: 850 years. As the verse says elsewhere, *elef shanim be-einecha ke-yom etmol* — a thousand years in God's eyes are like yesterday. On a divine timescale, 850 years is barely a moment. The compassion embedded in the word *maher* — quickly — is not that God acted immediately, but that He acted before the situation became irreversible, giving centuries for *teshuvah* while still ensuring that when the judgment came, it came before the point of utter destruction.

## Coerced Get

### Jewish vs Non-Jewish Court…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גֵּט מְעוּשֶּׂה, בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל – כָּשֵׁר, וּבְגוֹיִם – פָּסוּל. וּבְגוֹיִם – חוֹבְטִין אוֹתוֹ וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״עֲשֵׂה מַה שֶּׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל אוֹמְרִים לָךְ״ (וְכָשֵׁר).
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a bill of divorce** that the husband **was compelled** by the court to write and give his wife, if he was compelled **by a Jewish** court it is **valid, but** if he was compelled **by gentiles** it is **invalid. But with regard to gentiles they may beat him** at the request of the Jewish court **and say to him: Do what the Jews are telling you, and it is a valid** divorce.

The *gemara* moves to an entirely new topic: the *get me'useh*, the coerced *get*. This subject gained notoriety in recent years when a group of individuals in New Jersey were prosecuted for using coercion to extract *gittin* from recalcitrant husbands — a reminder that these laws carry real contemporary stakes. Rashi's gloss on *me'useh* is a single word: *be-chezakah* — by force.

The *mishna* states: *get me'useh be-Yisrael kasher, uv'ovdei kochavim pasul* — a coerced *get* administered by a Jewish court is valid; one administered by a non-Jewish court is invalid. There is however a nuance: *ovdei kochavim chovtin oto ve-omrim lo: aseh mah she-Yisrael omrim lecha — kasher* — if the non-Jewish court beats the husband and says to him, "Do what the Jewish court is telling you to do," the resulting *get* is valid. The non-Jewish enforcement acts as the arm of Jewish judicial authority, not on independent grounds. Only when the non-Jewish court acts entirely on its own initiative, without reference to the Jewish court's ruling, is the *get* invalid.

### Acting on Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גֵּט הַמְעוּשֶּׂה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, כַּדִּין – כָּשֵׁר; שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין – פָּסוּל, וּפוֹסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **a bill of divorce** that the husband **was compelled by a Jewish** court to give his wife, if they did so **lawfully,** as the *halakha* obligated the husband to divorce his wife, it is **valid.** This is referring to cases where sexual intercourse is forbidden or specific cases where the Sages instituted that the husband is obligated to divorce his wife. If they did so **unlawfully,** the bill of divorce is **invalid, but** it is not considered entirely invalid, as it **disqualifies** the wife from marrying a priest after her husband's death.

This distinction is crucial. A non-Jewish court that serves as an enforcement mechanism for a legitimate Jewish *beit din* ruling is effectively an extension of that ruling. The *get* produced under such circumstances is as if it were produced under direct Jewish court compulsion. The invalidity arises only when the non-Jewish court operates on its own legal authority without grounding in Jewish law.

### According to Din

Rav Nachman, citing Shmuel, introduces a further grid of distinctions: *get ha-me'useh be-Yisrael ke-din kasher, she-lo ke-din pasul u-posel*. When a Jewish court coerces a *get ke-din* — according to law — the *get* is fully valid. When the coercion is *she-lo ke-din* — not in accordance with law — the *get* is *pasul u-posel*. This phrase requires careful parsing. *Pasul* means the *get* does not function to dissolve the marriage — she remains married. *Posel*, however, means that despite the *get*'s failure to actually divorce her, it renders her ineligible to marry a *kohen* — she is, so to speak, partially affected. The *get* has done something, just not the right thing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְגוֹיִם, כַּדִּין – פָּסוּל וּפוֹסֵל, שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין – אֲפִילּוּ רֵיחַ הַגֵּט אֵין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in a case where the husband was compelled **by gentiles,** if he was compelled **lawfully,** the bill of divorce is **invalid, but** it also **disqualifies** the wife from marrying a priest. But if he was compelled **unlawfully it does not have even the trace of a bill of divorce,** and the wife is not even disqualified from marrying a priest.

For a non-Jewish court: if it acted *ke-din*, the result is *pasul u-posel* — the same partial effect as a Jewish court acting improperly. If it acted *she-lo ke-din*, the *get* has absolutely no effect: *pircha ein bo* — it accomplishes nothing whatsoever. She remains fully and unambiguously married.

### Din Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי גּוֹיִם בְּנֵי עַשּׂוֹיֵי נִינְהוּ, אִיתַּכְשׁוֹרֵי נָמֵי לִיתַּכְשַׁר! אִי לָאו בְּנֵי עַשּׂוֹיֵי נִינְהוּ, מִיפְסָל לָא לִיפְסֹל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: With regard to the statement that if the husband was compelled by gentiles the divorce is invalid but it also disqualifies the wife from marrying a priest, **whichever way you look at it,** the statement is difficult. **If gentiles are** legally **capable of compulsion, it should be rendered valid** with regard to the woman's permission to remarry **as well. If they are not** legally **capable of compulsion, it should not disqualify** her either.

What does *ke-din* mean in the context of a coerced *get* by a Jewish court? Rashi specifies: *ke-gon hanach d'amrinan behu: yotzi ve-yiten ketubah, o she-hayetah asurah lo* — cases in which the *beit din* had already ruled *yotzi ve-yiten ketubah*, that he must divorce and pay the *ketubah*, or cases where the wife was forbidden to him in the first place, where the marriage itself was halachically impermissible. In other words, the paradigmatic case of a coerced *get ke-din* is not the common contemporary scenario of a husband who refuses to give a *get* to a wife seeking divorce in an otherwise permissible marriage. It is specifically a case where the marriage should not have continued — or perhaps should never have occurred — and the *beit din* had formally ruled that the husband was obligated to give a *get*, but he refused to comply with that ruling. The court's coercion in such a case is the enforcement of a pre-existing legal obligation, not the imposition of a new one.

Whether this framework can be extended by analogy to the modern problem of the recalcitrant husband in an otherwise valid marriage is a question the *gemara* raises implicitly but does not resolve in this passage, and one must be cautious about abstracting too freely from the specific cases the *rishonim* describe to broader contemporary applications.
