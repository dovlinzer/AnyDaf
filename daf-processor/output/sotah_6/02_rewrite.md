# Sotah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sotah Status & Yibum

### Sotah Suspected Status

We begin on 6b, approximately ten lines from the bottom, with the words *umet choletzet*. The subject is an interesting application of *yibum* law — a topic familiar to those who have studied Maseches Yevamos — arising from the case of the *sotah*.

To review the basic framework: a woman who has been warned (*kinui*) and subsequently secluded herself with the man against whom she was warned now carries the status of a *sotah*. Halakhically, she is a *safek* — a doubt — but she is treated as a *vaday*, a certainty, because the language of the *Torah* declares *venistarah vehi nitma'ah*. Based on the evidence accumulated at this stage, the *Torah* treats her as though *tumah* has been established, even though the ultimate clarification awaits the ritual at the *Beit HaMikdash*.

The practical consequence of this *safek*-treated-as-*vaday* status is that she is forbidden from having marital relations with her husband, and her husband is forbidden from having relations with her. Furthermore, if she is married to a *kohen*, she may not eat *terumah*. There is something deeper here than a mere technical prohibition: the bond of marriage has been, in some sense, weakened or compromised, which is why the *Torah*'s declaration of *v'nitma'ah* reaches beyond the bedroom and touches even her right to eat *terumah*. This will be developed further when we encounter the word *v'nitma'ah* appearing multiple times in the relevant *pesukim*.

### No Yibum Rule

Against this background, the *Mishnah* teaches that if the husband dies before the *sotah*'s status has been clarified — before she has had the opportunity to drink the bitter waters — the brother, in a case where the couple has no children, may not perform *yibum*. The reasoning is almost self-evident: *yibum* is an act of sexual intimacy, it establishes a sexual relationship with this woman, and it does so precisely because the brother steps into the shoes of the deceased husband. But the husband himself was forbidden from having relations with her. How, then, could the *Torah* mandate the brother to do what the husband could not?

At the same time, because the matter remains a *safek* — she has not been definitively proven guilty — she is not simply released. The brother must still perform *chalitzah* to sever the *zika*, the levirate bond that has transferred to him.

One might wonder: what if the man against whom she was warned and with whom she secluded herself was, in fact, the brother? In that case, the calculus would shift — but that is a separate discussion. The *Mishnah*'s case is the standard one, and the *Gemara* now asks the obvious opening question: *amay*? Why?

### Peshat Derivation

The *Gemara* first asks: why indeed can the brother not perform *yibum*? And then: if he cannot perform *yibum*, why must *chalitzah* be done at all?

Rav Yosef provides a scriptural basis: *v'yatz'ah mibeito v'halchah v'haytah l'ish acher* — "she shall leave his house and go to be with another man" (Devarim 24:2). This verse describes a woman who is divorced in a context of *matza bah ervat davar*, the discovery of some act of nakedness. The *drasha* extends the verse: we read it as applying not only to divorce but to any scenario in which a *sotah* is no longer with her first husband — whether through divorce or death — and the phrase *l'ish acher*, "to another man," is expounded as *ish acher v'lo yavam*: she may go to a completely different man, but not to the *yavam*, the brother-in-law.

This is admittedly a narrow and unusual reading of the *pesukim*. Rashi notes that the *drasha* treats *v'yatz'ah mibeito* as encompassing any manner by which she leaves the marriage — not just divorce — and then focuses on the next phrase: *v'halchah v'haytah l'ish acher*, her subsequent relationship must be with someone entirely unrelated to her first husband, not with someone who is essentially a continuation of that husband's legal persona. As a student rightly noted, this is clearly not the *peshat* of the verse. The verse describes a divorced woman, not a widow, and *yibum* applies in cases of death, not divorce. This is, in every sense, a *drasha* — a hermeneutical reading that reads particular significance into precise language.

## Yibum Prohibition Logic

### Abaye's Refutation

The *Gemara* immediately presses the internal logic. Abaye raises a sharp objection: *ella me'ata chalitzah nami lo tiba'i* — if the *sotah* is excluded from *yibum* by this *pasuk*, she should be excluded from *chalitzah* as well. If the verse categorically removes her from the domain of levirate law, why should the brother have any obligation toward her at all?

Rav Yosef responds: *ilu itei leva'al mi lo ba'ei get* — if the husband were still alive, would he not need to sever the bond with a *get*? The relationship does not dissolve on its own, even in cases of adultery or suspicion. The husband would still need to formally sever the marital bond through a *get*. Similarly, the *zika* that transfers to the brother upon the husband's death does not dissolve automatically. Even if *yibum* is unavailable, the bond must be formally severed, and that severance takes the form of *chalitzah*.

Tosafot, however, points out a problem with this logic. There are many situations in which a brother cannot perform *yibum* and yet is equally exempt from *chalitzah*, even though a husband in those same cases would still need to give a *get*. For example, if the widow is an *ervah* to the brother — a forbidden relative — the brother has no obligation of *chalitzah*, even though had the husband been alive, a *get* would have been necessary to dissolve the marriage. The principle that "the husband needs a *get*" does not automatically entail that "the brother needs *chalitzah*." So the argument does not quite hold.

### Voluntary vs Mandated

Rav Yosef offers a second version of the derivation. The *Torah* says *v'yatz'ah mibeito v'halchah v'haytah l'ish acher* — she goes to another man, to someone who, the *Gemara* explains, is *acher* in a particular sense: *acharei desani lah*, because she has become problematic for the household. The first husband did the right thing — he rid himself of a woman whose conduct had undermined the marriage. The language of *ish acher* carries a negative valence: the man who subsequently marries such a woman occupies a lower moral standing than the first husband who had the clarity to end the relationship. He was prepared to marry a woman suspected of adultery, described as a *rishah* in the context of *matza bah ervat davar*, and he *hichnis rishah l'toch beito* — he brought this problematic woman into his house.

The *Gemara* then asks pointedly: *v'at amrat tityabem nami yibumei?* — and you are proposing that the *Torah* would now mandate the brother to marry this woman under compulsion? The whole point of the verse was that removing her from this household was the right and proper response. How could the *Torah* turn around and require another man to take her on involuntarily?

Abaye pushes back: *ella me'ata le'acher lo tinasei* — if the logic is that she is the kind of woman no one should be entering into a relationship with, then she should be forbidden to everyone, not just to the brother. Every subsequent marriage should be prohibited. The *Gemara* responds: *mi ka ramina lah aleih be'al korchei?* — no one is forcing anyone to marry her. A third party who chooses to marry such a woman does so of his own free will; caveat emptor. But the *Torah* cannot mandate — cannot compel — a man to enter a relationship with a woman about whom these serious questions have been raised.

### Tosafot Resolution

As noted above, Tosafot acknowledges that neither of these derivations is fully satisfying as a formal logical proof. The real logic, Tosafot concludes, is more fundamental: if sexual relations are forbidden between this woman and her husband, then how can the *Torah* mandate *yibum*, which is a direct continuation of the husband's marital relationship — in effect, mandating the very act that was forbidden? That is the core of the argument. Because the suspicion is not definitive — it remains a *safek* — she cannot be fully excluded, and *chalitzah* is still required. But the mandate of *yibum* cannot stand where sexual relations were themselves prohibited with the man whose place the *yavam* is taking.

### House-Wrecker Logic

Tosafot here cites a beautiful elaboration from Rabbeinu Chananel. Where does the phrase *listore l'beito*, "to wreck the household," come from? Rabbeinu Chananel — and Tosafot says *hit'im devarav*, he sweetened the words, he gave them flavor — notes that it is the precise inverse of *asher lo yivneh et beit achiv*, "that he shall build his brother's house," which is the defining obligation of *yibum*. This woman, suspected of adultery, is in the category of a home-wrecker — *listore l'beito*. How could she be brought into the *mitzvah* of building a house? The very language of *yibum* contradicts the nature of what is being proposed.

### Remarriage to Others

The *Gemara* then tests the second version of Rav Yosef's argument — the *ish acher* reading — from a different angle. Abaye raises the following case: she was suspected of adultery while married to her first husband. Her first husband dies, or divorces her, and she subsequently marries a second man — an *ish acher*, in the language of the *pasuk*. They have a happy marriage, undisturbed by any suspicion of adultery. The second husband then dies without children, leaving his brothers obligated in *yibum*. Should the brothers of the second husband now be prohibited from performing *yibum* with this woman, on the grounds that the *pasuk* calls her second husband an *ish acher* — implying that marrying her was a poor choice, and no one should be mandated to do so?

The *Gemara* responds: *gavi d'hai mi b'shem tov hu ka'i* — but in the context of the second husband, she carries a good reputation. The successful second marriage has changed the picture. Whether it indicates she did *teshuva*, or that the problem lay with the first relationship rather than with her as a person, the point is that we now assess her relationship status through the lens of the second marriage. It is that relationship — the second husband's household — whose continuity the *yibum* obligation is meant to serve. The *ish acher* label applied to the second husband was appropriate in hindsight — he took a risk by marrying a woman with a questionable past — but by the time we reach the question of *yibum*, the relevant relationship is the second marriage, in which she was unimpeachable.

### Rava's Kal Vachomer

Rava offers a different approach: a *kal vachomer*. The act that caused her to become a *sotah* made her forbidden to her husband — the man she was *muttar* to, the man she was married to. If this compromised status is strong enough to forbid her to the person who had the most legitimate relationship with her, how much more so should it create a barrier to the person who, while her husband was alive, was *arayos* to her — her husband's brother, who was doubly forbidden to her as both a married woman's relative and her husband's brother. If the suspicion is potent enough to sever the permitted relationship, it should certainly be potent enough to prevent the creation of what had previously been a forbidden one.

### Kohen Gadol Example

The *Gemara* tests this *kal vachomer* with a counterexample. Consider a *kohen hedyot* — an ordinary priest — who dies, leaving his widow to the *yibum* obligation of his brother. But the brother in this case is a *kohen gadol*, and a *kohen gadol* is forbidden to marry a widow (*almanah*). Does the *kal vachomer* apply here? Could we say: since she is forbidden to the *kohen gadol* who was muttar to her — wait, no — he was never *muttar* to her. The *Gemara* dismisses this case: *ha asirah ve-kaima* — she was always forbidden to him. The *kal vachomer* requires a situation in which some new development turned a previously permitted relationship into a forbidden one. The *kohen gadol*'s prohibition against marrying a widow predated everything; it is not the result of any new status affecting her.

### Rape Case Analysis

The *Gemara* tries a better counterexample. A woman married to a *kohen* was raped. The rape, tragically, renders her forbidden to her husband — a *kohen* may not remain married to a woman who has been violated, even involuntarily. The husband dies, and the brother — who is a *challal*, the son of a *kohen* who married a *gerushah*, making the offspring a *challal* — is obligated in *yibum*. According to the *kal vachomer*, one might reason: the act of rape made her forbidden to the husband she was *muttar* to; how much more so should it make her forbidden to the brother! But this would prohibit the *challal* from performing *yibum*, which seems incorrect.

The *Gemara* responds: *ones be-yisrael mishra sharei* — rape by a non-*kohen* does not create any prohibition. The *challal* occupies the status of a non-*kohen* with respect to marriage restrictions. And *gavi d'hai leika issura* — there is no prohibition applicable to the brother in this scenario. The *kal vachomer* fails here because the prohibition created by rape is *kedushah*-specific: it is a restriction that applies only within the domain of *kehunah* and does not carry over into relationships where *kehunah* is not a factor. The *sotah*'s case is fundamentally different: the suspicion of adultery creates a prohibition that is relevant in any intimate relationship, not one limited to a particular domain. Therefore, it does transfer, so to speak, and the *kal vachomer* does hold: if it forecloses the permitted relationship with the husband, it certainly forecloses the continuation of that relationship through *yibum* with the brother. *Chalitzah* alone is what remains.

## Forbidden to Terumah

### Enumeration Cases

The *Mishnah* now turns to enumerate the women who, while carrying their unresolved *sotah* status, remain forbidden to eat *terumah*. The underlying structure of this list is important: each case represents a scenario in which the *sotah* process will never reach its completion through the actual drinking of the waters. The only way to lift the prohibition on *terumah* eating is to resolve the question — either by the ritual working or by some other definitive clarification. When that resolution becomes impossible, the prohibition remains in place. It is therefore somewhat pointed that the *Mishnah* frames these cases as *asurot milachol biterumah* — forbidden to eat *terumah* — since that framing emphasizes not just what happened, but what can never now be undone.

### Guilt & Witnesses

The *Mishnah* lists first: *ha'omeret temei'a ani lach* — if she herself admits that she has committed adultery, the process stops immediately. Similarly, if witnesses arrive and testify that she committed adultery — *v'shavu eidim she-hi temei'ah* — the process is halted. In both cases, the *sotah* ritual is superfluous. The matter has been resolved by direct evidence. These cases may seem obvious — if she is unquestionably guilty, of course she cannot eat *terumah* — but the *Mishnah* is making clear that the *terumah* prohibition, having attached, does not lift simply because the ritual is no longer going to take place. She must receive a *get* and the prohibition persists.

### Refusal Cases

The *Mishnah* continues: *ve'omeret eini shotah* — if she refuses to drink, the process ends. And: *o she-ha-ba'al eino rotzeh le-hashkotah* — if the husband refuses to go through with giving her the waters, the process equally ends. In all of these cases, the ritual will never take place. The *Mishnah* is teaching us what the practical consequence is when the process is interrupted at this stage: she gets divorced — without a *ketubah*, if it was her own refusal; possibly with a *ketubah*, if it was the husband who chose not to proceed — and she remains in her state of prohibition, since the situation will never be resolved.

### Husband Violation

Finally, the *Mishnah* lists: *ve-shavah ba'alah baderech* — if the husband had marital relations with her while she was in the process of being brought to undergo the ritual. By doing so, he violated the restrictions that the *sotah* status imposed on both of them. Once this violation occurs, one does not go through with the waters. Again the process is terminated, and again the prohibition on *terumah* eating remains unresolved.

A brief but important structural observation: the *Torah* grounds the entire *sotah* process in the husband's jealousy — *ruach kin'ah* — which is fundamentally subjective. Chazal sought to objectify the process, placing it under *beit din* supervision, requiring objective evidence of warning and seclusion, and so forth. But this created a tension: once the process is formalized and removed from the husband's unilateral control, a husband can no longer simply retract his suspicion and end the matter. The machinery, once set in motion, follows its own juridical path.

## Witnesses Overseas

### Rav Sheshet's Point

Rav Amram reports in the name of Rav Sheshet: *sotah she-yesh lah eidim bimdinat hayam ein ha-mayim bodkin otah* — if there is a *sotah* whose guilt is known to witnesses somewhere overseas (*medinas hayam*), in a distant place, the waters will not work on her. The scenario is this: those witnesses are unknown to the *beit din*; the woman and her husband proceed through the entire *sotah* ritual; the waters appear to have no effect; everyone concludes she is innocent. Rav Sheshet says: no. The reason the waters had no effect is not that she was innocent — it is that there existed objective evidence of her guilt elsewhere in the world, and the ritual is designed for the domain of unresolvable doubt. When concrete evidence exists — even unknown, even far away — the ritual is neutralized.

The *Gemara* immediately notes the epistemic problem this creates: how do we ever know *why* she came out appearing clean? The *Gemara* will address this.

Rav Sheshet adds: *ve-anhir lah einan mimatnisin* — and he illuminated his teaching from the *Mishnah* itself. The *chiddush* was not new; it was embedded in the *Mishnah*.

### Scriptural Basis

What is the scriptural basis for Rav Sheshet's ruling? The *Gemara* cites: *venistarah vehi nitma'ah ve-eid ein bah* — "and she was secluded and became *nitma'ah* and there is no witness" (Bamidbar 5:13). The phrase *ve-eid ein bah*, there is no witness who knows, is read precisely: *deleka deyadah bah*, there is nobody who knows of her guilt. *Le'apukei ha de-ika deyadah bah* — this excludes the case where someone does know, even from afar. In that case, the waters cannot function as a clarifying mechanism, because the truth is not hidden — it is merely inaccessible.

A question arises: does *ve-eid ein bah* require specifically two witnesses — *eidim* — or does even a single witness (*ed echad*) neutralize the ritual? This is a genuine question, since we have a principle that a single witness is believed after *kinui* and *stirah*, and can prevent the drinking from proceeding. Would a single distant witness similarly neutralize the waters? Tosafot is not certain. The question extends further: does a single witness who appears *after* the ritual has been performed have the same effect retroactively?

### Witnesses Post-Ritual

The *Mishnah* states that witnesses who arrive to testify she is *temei'ah* result in her not eating *terumah*, and the *Gemara* asks the diagnostic question: *de-atu eidim eimat* — when exactly do these witnesses arrive? If they arrive before she drinks, the case is trivial — she is proven guilty and of course does not eat *terumah*. The *Mishnah*'s formulation would then be unremarkable. The *Mishnah* must therefore be referring to a case where the witnesses arrive *after* she drank. Having drunk, and having appeared to emerge unharmed, she would seem to have been vindicated by the waters.

Yet the *Mishnah* says we still believe the witnesses. This, says the *Gemara*, makes perfect sense *bishlama* if we accept Rav Sheshet's principle — that when witnesses exist, the waters do not function as a truth-revealing mechanism. The waters' apparent silence proves nothing in that case; we now understand why the ritual seemed inconclusive. We therefore believe the witnesses, and her *terumah* prohibition remains.

But if, on the contrary, we maintain that the waters function even when witnesses are present elsewhere, then the waters' silence would constitute strong proof of her innocence — and the subsequent arrival of witnesses should retroactively render them liars. *Tiggalei milta le-mafrea de-sahadei shikrei ninhu* — it would become retrospectively clear that these witnesses were lying. Here the *Gemara* makes a remarkable implicit move: the *sotah* ritual, embedded within a juridical framework of *beit din*, objective evidence, and formal procedure, is treated as a category of valid evidentiary proof — strong enough, in principle, to override witness testimony. The fact that the *Mishnah* clearly trusts the witnesses even after the ritual therefore proves Rav Sheshet's point.

## Merit & Efficacy

### Rabbi's Merit View

Rav Yosef challenges Rav Sheshet's proof. *Le-olam eima lach mayim bodkin otah* — the waters do function even when overseas witnesses exist. How then do we explain the *Mishnah*'s ruling that we believe the witnesses who arrive after the ritual? *Ve-ha eimur zekhut tolah lah* — because there is another possible explanation for why the ritual had no apparent effect: the woman possessed *zechut*, merit, which suspended the waters' action. The witnesses are believed not because witnesses neutralize the ritual, but because there are other reasons the ritual might not have worked.

The *Gemara* then identifies what the two positions are debating: *be-minvella de-Rabbi* — the question of deterioration, as articulated by Rabbi in a *baraita*. Rabbi says: *zekhut tolah ba-mayim ha-marim* — merit suspends the effect of the bitter waters. But it does not suspend it indefinitely without consequence. She does not give birth normally, and she does not improve in her health: *ela minvella ve-holechet* — rather, she progressively deteriorates, until ultimately she dies in the manner the *Torah* described. It is a delayed reaction: the merit purchases time, but the end is the same.

### Rabbis' Position

The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi. According to the Rabbis, *zechut* is fully *tolah* — it fully suspends the effect of the waters, and she does not die at all. The *zechut* is complete. The question then becomes: do the Rabbis agree that she at least visibly deteriorates, even if she does not die? Or do they hold that there is no observable effect whatsoever — the waters simply do not work?

### Sheshet Interpretation

Rav Sheshet reads the debate as follows: *bein le-Rabbi bein le-Rabbanan hi misnavna* — whether according to Rabbi or according to the Rabbis, she deteriorates. The Rabbis may disagree with Rabbi about whether she ultimately dies, but they agree that some visible deterioration occurs. If that is correct, then the apparent complete absence of effect — the ritual seemingly working perfectly with no ill consequence — cannot be attributed to *zechut*. If *zechut* always produces at least observable deterioration, then when there is no deterioration at all, the only explanation is either that she is genuinely innocent, or that the waters were neutralized by the existence of overseas witnesses. And since the *Mishnah* credits the witnesses who arrive afterward, Rav Sheshet concludes that his position stands proven: the witnesses neutralize the waters.

### Yosef Counter

Rav Yosef reads the debate differently: *le-Rabbi hi misnavna, le-Rabbanan lo havya misnavna* — according to Rabbi, she deteriorates; according to the Rabbis, she does not deteriorate. Nor, for that matter, does she die — the Rabbis hold that with sufficient *zechut*, the waters have no effect whatsoever, and she lives her life undisturbed. If that is a possible outcome, then the waters' complete silence — no deterioration, no ill effect — could simply reflect that she had *zechut*. There is therefore no need to invoke the principle that witnesses neutralize the waters. The witnesses who arrive after the ritual are believed because they establish actual adultery; the ritual's silence is explained by *zechut*. The two explanations coexist, and neither requires the other.

## Shimon's Objection

### Policy Concern

Rabbi Shimon bar Ashi raises a fundamental objection to the entire framework of *zechut* suspending the waters' effect. *Ein zechut tolah ba-mayim ha-marim* — Rabbi Shimon categorically rejects the idea. His argument is one of policy and systemic legitimacy. If *zechut* can suspend or neutralize the waters' effect, then the entire ritual is undermined. *Madche ata et ha-mayim bifnei kol ha-nashim ha-shotot* — you have effectively discredited the waters in the eyes of all women who undergo this ritual. Anyone can point to a case where a woman who seems guilty emerged apparently unscathed, and attribute it to *zechut* rather than innocence. *Ve-ata motzi shem ra al ha-tehorot she-shatu* — you have cast aspersions on all the women who legitimately emerged innocent, because people will suspect that they too were actually guilty and simply benefited from their merit. The guilty will think they can get away with it; the innocent will be suspected of guilt. The entire mechanism is corrupted.

Rabbi Shimon's argument is striking precisely because it grounds a halakhic conclusion not in textual derivation but in policy reasoning. The logic of what a well-functioning institution would look like determines what the *halacha* must be.

### Parallel Logic

The *Gemara* then extends Rabbi Shimon's critique directly to Rav Sheshet's principle. *Ve-im ita yesh eidim bimdinat hayam nami ata motzi shem ra al ha-tehorot she-shatu* — if we accept that overseas witnesses neutralize the waters, then exactly the same systemic problem arises. Every woman who emerges apparently clean will be suspected of having gotten away with it because there are witnesses far away who know the truth. The same logic that Rabbi Shimon deploys against *zechut* applies with equal force to the principle of overseas witnesses.

### Shimon Rejects Both

How does the *Gemara* respond? *Rabbi Shimon ka-amrat?* — you are asking about Rabbi Shimon? *Rabbi Shimon midei de-zechut lo tali eidim nami lo talu* — Rabbi Shimon rejects both. He does not accept that *zechut* interferes, and he equally does not accept that witnesses interfere. But the *Gemara* is operating within the position that does accept these interferences, and within that position, both *zechut* and overseas witnesses play parallel roles. The argument is: if you accept one form of interference, you must accept that the same systemic concern applies to both, and yet both remain valid.

## Minchah Status

### Minchah Sequence

The *Gemara* now turns to the *sotah*'s *minchah* offering and what becomes of it in various interrupted scenarios. The sequence of the *minchah* process is key to understanding the analysis. The *minchah* is brought; it is placed in a *kli sharet* — a consecrated vessel — at which point it acquires *kedushat ha-guf*: the body of the offering is sanctified, and it can no longer be redeemed. The *kometz* — the prescribed handful — is then separated and offered on the *mizbe'ach*; the remainder (*shirayim*) would normally be eaten by the *kohanim*. The question is what happens to the *minchah* at each stage if the *sotah* process is interrupted.

### Rav's Argument

The *Gemara* connects this to the debate about overseas witnesses. The *Mishnah* taught that among the cases where the *minchah* is burned is the case where witnesses arrive to testify she is guilty — *ushev'u lah eidim she-hi temei'ah*. The *Gemara* asks the diagnostic question: when do the witnesses arrive? If before the *minchah* was placed in the *kli sharet*, it could simply be redeemed — *tifrok ve-teichulin*. The case in the *Mishnah* must therefore be where the witnesses arrive after the *minchah* was already placed in the *kli sharet*.

Now, says the *Gemara*, if we accept Rav Sheshet's position — that the waters function when there are no witnesses, but are neutralized when witnesses exist — then at the time of sanctification, there were no witnesses (or they were unknown), the process was valid, the *minchah* was properly sanctified, and its *kedushah* stands. When the witnesses subsequently arrive, the process is terminated, but the *minchah* retains its sanctity and must be burned. This is coherent.

But if we say the waters function even with overseas witnesses — meaning the waters are not neutralized by their existence — then we are forced to say that even had the witnesses not come, the process would have been ineffectual from the outset. The sanctification was therefore in error from the very beginning: *de-kadosh me-ikara be-ta'ut kadosh*. If so, the *minchah* was never truly sanctified, and rather than burning it, one should be able to take it out as ordinary *chulin* — *tipok le-chulin*. The fact that the *Mishnah* says it must be burned, and not simply redeemed or eaten as *chulin*, thus appears to support Rav Sheshet.

### Yehuda Limitation

Rav Yehuda mi-Diskarta offers a solution that limits the scope of the problem. He says: *kegon she-zinta ba-azara* — the scenario in the *Mishnah* is one where the witnesses testify not that she committed adultery a year ago in some distant city, but that she committed adultery *right now*, in the *azarah* itself, after the *minchah* was already placed in the *kli sharet*. In that case, at the time of sanctification, there were genuinely no witnesses — none existed anywhere. The sanctification was valid. The witnesses then come and testify about a brand new act of adultery, which independently terminates the process. The *minchah* was properly sanctified, but the process is now terminated, so it must be burned.

The *Gemara* playfully notes the practical difficulties with this scenario — she has an escort of young priests (*pirchei kehunah*). The objection is answered in several ways: she could have committed adultery with one of the escorting priests themselves, or she may have needed to use the bathroom and took advantage of that moment of relative privacy.

This answer, though technically valid, applies only to a narrow subset of cases. It does not resolve the general principle for the more common case of witnesses who arrive with testimony about prior adultery.

### Papa's Solution

Rav Papa offers the definitive resolution, one that operates without restricting the case. *Le-olam ke-da-amrinan me-ikara* — maintain the original scenario: witnesses arrive with testimony about prior adultery, long before and far away. Technically, one could argue the sanctification was retroactively in error and the *minchah* is simply *chulin*. But this cannot be permitted in practice. *Mi-de-rabbanan gezeira shema yomru motzi'in mi-kli sharet le-chol* — by rabbinic decree, we do not permit what appears to everyone to be a *kli sharet*-sanctified offering to be simply treated as ordinary food. The appearance of sanctity, once established in the public eye, cannot be undone without communal confusion and the undermining of *kedushah* as a category. Therefore, even if technically the sanctification was in error, we burn the *minchah* — not as a genuine *korban*, not on the *mizbe'ach*, but in the *beit ha-deshen*.

## Minchah Scenarios

### Pre-Sanctity Contamination

Rami bar Rav Mari enumerates the various scenarios systematically. If the *minchah* became *tamei* before it was placed in the *kli sharet*, it has not yet acquired *kedushat ha-guf*: *harei hi ke-chol ha-menachot ve-tipade* — it is treated like any ordinary *minchah* in that state, and may be redeemed.

### Death After Sanctity

If the *minchah* was already placed in the *kli sharet* and then became *tamei*, it has *kedushat ha-guf* and cannot be redeemed. The process is terminated, and: *harei hi ke-chol ha-menachot ve-tisaref* — it must be burned.

### Kometz Offered

If the *kometz* was already separated and offered on the *mizbe'ach* but the *shirayim* had not yet been eaten — and in the meantime either the husband or the wife died, terminating the process — the *kometz* offering was brought legitimately, at a time when everything was in proper order. In that case: *harei ke-chol ha-menachot ve-tei'achel* — the *shirayim* are eaten normally. The offering was brought in proper context.

### Doubtful Minchah

The case of *shel safeik ba'ah mitchila* — a *minchah* that from the very outset was brought in circumstances of doubt — is resolved by the principle of *kippurah s'fika v'halcha la*. Rashi clarifies that *kippurah* here does not mean *atonement* in its technical sense, but rather that the offering accomplished its purpose: it was brought as it was meant to be brought, in a context of genuine doubt, and that context was legitimate. The fact that retrospectively the doubt was never going to be resolved — because, say, one of the parties was going to die — is irrelevant, since at the time of bringing, everything was appropriate.

### Witnesses Post-Ritual

When witnesses arrive after the ritual: *minchasah tisaref* — the *minchah* is burned. As explained by Rav Papa, this is by rabbinic decree to preserve the appearance of *kedushah*. It is burned in the *beit ha-deshen*, not as an offering on the *mizbe'ach*.

### Zomemin Witnesses

Finally, if the original witnesses — those whose testimony established the *kinui* and *stirah* — were subsequently proven to be *eidim zomemin*, false witnesses contradicted by other witnesses, the entire basis for the *sotah* process collapses. *Minchasah chulin* — the *minchah* reverts to *chulin* and may simply be eaten. There is no obligation to burn it and no need to redeem it; it was never sanctified.

One might object: did we not just say that even when sanctification is retroactively in error, we burn for appearance's sake? The *Gemara* answers: *eidim zomemin kala is lehu* — the exposure of false witnesses is a public event. When witnesses are contradicted and proven to be liars by additional witnesses, the entire community becomes aware that the original testimony was fabricated. It becomes broadly understood that the *sotah* process was initiated on false grounds, and therefore the *minchah* was never sanctified. There is no danger of anyone thinking that a legitimately sanctified offering is being desecrated; the public knowledge of the *eidim zomemin* provides full transparency.

## Shimon Reconciliation

### Text vs Policy

Having established, in Rav Papa's framework, that the *minchah* in the witnesses-after-the-ritual case is burned by rabbinic decree, the *Gemara* returns to Rabbi Shimon's policy objection. Rabbi Shimon had argued that accepting any interference with the waters — whether from *zechut* or from overseas witnesses — systematically undermines the legitimacy of the entire *sotah* process. One might therefore think that Rabbi Shimon's objection strikes at the very source from which Rav Sheshet derived his principle.

The *Gemara* clarifies that this is not so. Rabbi Shimon's policy argument is powerful, but it cannot override a textual derivation (*drasha*). The question is: is there a textual basis that survives Rabbi Shimon's critique?

### Vetohorah Source

The *Gemara* cites the derivation from the word *vetohorah* — "and she will be pure" (Bamidbar 5:28). The verse states that if she is innocent, the waters will prove her pure and she will conceive. The word *vetohorah* is read as implying a limiting condition: the waters function as a mechanism of *taharah* only under specific conditions. *V'lo she-yesh lah eidim bimdinat hayam* — not when there are witnesses overseas who know she is guilty. And similarly, *v'lo she-tola lah zechus* — not when *zechut* is operative.

### Gossiping Peshat

The verse also yields a third case: *v'lo she-yisu v'yitnu bah mozeros b'levana* — not when she is the subject of widespread gossip among women knitting in the moonlight. This is a striking *drasha*: if her alleged adultery has become so publicly known that it is the talk of the community, the waters will not function as a revelation of hidden truth, because the matter is no longer truly hidden. The textual basis is the same word *vetohorah* — the waters are effective only when her status is genuinely unresolved and concealed; not when it has entered the domain of public knowledge, however informal.

### Rarity Mitigation

Rabbi Shimon's position is then reconciled with these derivations as follows. Rabbi Shimon's policy concern — that exceptions undermine legitimacy — is not without force, but it does not reach every possible case. The case of *zechut* was his primary target, and he rejects it entirely as too corrosive. The case of overseas witnesses is different. *Lo shchicha* — it is not common. Cases where witnesses exist in distant lands, unknown to the *beit din*, who could testify to her adultery, are genuinely rare. The textual derivation from *vetohorah* compels us to acknowledge this as a valid limiting condition, but its rarity means that it will not, in practice, generate the systemic suspicion that Rabbi Shimon feared. The waters' legitimacy is not meaningfully undermined by an exception that almost never arises. Rabbi Shimon can therefore concede the textual point while maintaining his broader objection to *zechut*, whose pervasive applicability would indeed corrode the entire institution.
