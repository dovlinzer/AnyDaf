# Avodah Zarah 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ethical Context

### Intro & Perek 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהִי: לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן בְּהֵמָה לְקׇרְבָּן, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לֹא מִשּׁוּם רוֹבֵעַ, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם נִרְבָּע, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם מוּקְצֶה, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם נֶעֱבָד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the assumption that gentiles are suspected of bestiality, the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:1): **One may purchase an animal from** gentiles **for** use as **an offering, and there is no concern** that it might be unfit **due to** it being **an animal that copulated with a person, or due to** is being **an animal that was the object of bestiality, or due to** it having been **set aside** for idol worship, **or due to** the animal itself having been **worshipped.**

Daf 23 marks our entry into the second *perek* of Avodah Zarah, which continues to elaborate on prohibitions governing interaction with the surrounding non-Jewish world, now moving explicitly into areas of a sexual and physical nature. We had already encountered a preliminary form of this concern on the previous daf, where the *mishnah* prohibited the sale of *behema daka* to non-Jews and the *gemara* explained that this was rooted in a concern over *riva'ah* — acts of bestiality. The current *perek* addresses these concerns more directly: an animal should not be left in a non-Jew's stable, a woman should not be in *yichud* with a non-Jew because of the concern of rape, and a man should not be in *yichud* because of the concern of murder.

### Prejudice & Fear

At this point in the learning, it is worth pausing to acknowledge the genuine discomfort these passages provoke. Concerns about commerce, about selling weapons, about building structures — even about land ownership — were all, however one interprets their ongoing applicability, at least rooted in a recognizable historical reality of persecution. But here, in the assumption that every non-Jew is searching for an opportunity to commit murder, rape, or bestiality, something qualitatively different is happening. The *yichud* concerns around rape and murder are, at least, somewhat understandable as a defensive posture born of real fear. People who see the outside world as hostile and their own community as safe will naturally be more guarded when venturing out. Rape and murder do occur, and a community that has experienced violence at the hands of outsiders may seek to minimize exposure.

### History vs. Today

The *riva'ah* concern, however — the assumption that non-Jews are constantly seeking opportunities to engage in acts of bestiality with animals, and specifically with Jewish-owned animals — is something else entirely. *Tosafot* himself will later note that he is not sure where the *gemara* draws this idea from. What makes this particularly troubling is not the historical emergence of the concern. One can understand, with a degree of sympathy, how a beleaguered community projects aberrant sexuality onto a threatening "other" — this is a well-documented human tendency. What is more troubling is the downstream effect: when such assumptions are absorbed into the *yeshiva* curriculum, learned year after year, they can quietly shape a generalized attitude toward non-Jews that outlasts whatever historical circumstances gave rise to the original fear. That is, the issue is less the historical genesis of the concern than its continuing internalization as received *halacha* and *hashkafa*. With that framing in mind, let us proceed to the *gemara* itself — where, as we shall see, the conclusion may turn out to be more measured than the premise.

## Ravina: Lechatchila/Bedi…

### Future vs. Past

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא לְכַתְּחִלָּה, הָא דִּיעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another resolution of the apparent contradiction between the mishna, which rules that gentiles are suspected of bestiality, and the *baraita*, which permits an animal purchased from gentiles to be sacrificed as an offering. **Ravina said** that it **is not difficult; this** mishna issues its ruling with regard to the *halakha **ab initio*****,** while **that** *baraita* is referring to the *halakha* **after the fact.**

The *gemara* on 23a opens with Ravina's answer to the apparent contradiction between the *mishnah*, which prohibits leaving an animal in a non-Jew's stable (out of concern for *riva'ah*), and the *baraita*, which permits purchasing animals from non-Jews for use as *korbanot*. *Lo kashya*, says Ravina — there is no difficulty. *Ha lechatchila, ha bedieved*: the distinction is between prospective concern and retrospective assessment. We are concerned about what might happen in the future, and therefore we do not leave the animal in the stable; but we do not assume that something already happened, and therefore we do not disqualify an animal purchased from a non-Jew for use as a sacrifice.

### Chezkat Chayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁאנֵי בֵּין לְכַתְּחִלָּה בֵּין לְדִיעֲבַד? דִּתְנַן: לֹא תִּתְיַיחֵד אִשָּׁה עִמָּהֶם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדִין עַל הָעֲרָיוֹת. וּרְמִינְהוּ: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנֶּחְבְּשָׁה בִּידֵי גּוֹיִם, עַל יְדֵי מָמוֹן — מוּתֶּרֶת לְבַעְלָהּ, עַל יְדֵי נְפָשׁוֹת — אֲסוּרָה לְבַעְלָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say that** there is **a difference** in this case **between *ab initio* and after the fact? As we learned** in the mishna: **A woman may not seclude herself with them because they are suspected of** engaging in **forbidden sexual intercourse. And** one can **raise a contradiction** from another mishna (*Ketubot* 26b): With regard to **a woman who was imprisoned by gentiles,** if she was imprisoned **due to monetary matters she is permitted to her husband** even if he is a priest, as there is no concern that she was raped. If she was imprisoned **due to a capital** offense **she is forbidden to her husband** if he is a priest, as the captors would not restrain themselves from raping her. The first clause of the mishna in *Ketubot* rules that a woman who was imprisoned in seclusion with gentiles is not assumed to have engaged in intercourse with them. This apparently contradicts the statement of the mishna here, which rules that a woman may not seclude herself with gentiles.

The question immediately arises: in what sense is this a genuine *lechatchila*/*bedieved* distinction? Ordinarily, *bedieved* leniency is justified by the concrete loss that would result from invalidating a *fait accompli*. But here, there is no significant loss — if one cannot buy a sacrificial animal from a non-Jew, one simply buys it from a Jew instead. So why should the *bedieved* framework apply?

The answer lies in a different dimension of the *lechatchila*/*bedieved* distinction, one that the *gemara* in Gittin makes explicit. There, the rule is *shema yamut chayyishinan, shema meis lo chayyishinan* — we are concerned that someone might die, but we do not assert that he is already dead. The classic application: if a husband instructs that a *get* be delivered to his wife, we deliver it without worrying that the husband has died in the interim; but if the *get* is conditional on taking effect a moment before his death, we cannot deliver it, since at any moment could be that fatal moment. The asymmetry is not about loss — it is about the nature of assertion. To worry about a future possibility is legitimate and prudent. To affirmatively assert that a person's status has changed, overturning his *chezkat chayim*, is a different legal act entirely. The same principle applies here: to say "we are concerned something might happen to this animal" is reasonable caution; to say "we will assume something already happened" is to make a positive claim that overturns the animal's presumptive status. That, Ravina holds, we do not do.

## Woman Seized Debate

### Yichud Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, שָׁאנֵי לַן בֵּין לְכַתְּחִלָּה לְדִיעֲבַד? מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: אֲפִילּוּ דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, דְּמִתְיָירֵא מִשּׁוּם הֶפְסֵד מָמוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** here that there is **a difference for us between *ab initio*,** as in the mishna here, **and after the fact,** as in the mishna in *Ketubot*? The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **From where** can this be proven? **Perhaps I** could **actually say to you:** Generally, **even after the fact,** one **may not** assume that a woman who was secluded with a gentile did not engage in intercourse with him, **and here,** in the mishna in *Ketubot*, **this is the reason** that she is permitted to her husband even after having been imprisoned: **Since** her husband might not agree to pay if his wife was raped, the gentile **is fearful** of raping her **due to the** potential **loss of his money.**

The *gemara* now attempts to derive a proof for Ravina's distinction from the *mishnah*'s prohibition on *yichud*. Our *mishnah* states: *lo tisyacheid isha imahem mipnei shechashudim al ha'arayot* — a woman may not be alone with non-Jews because they are suspected of sexual violations. Against this, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* presenting two distinct scenarios. In the first, a woman is seized by non-Jews as leverage to recover a debt owed by her husband (*al yedei mamon*): she is permitted to return to her husband, and — as *Tosafot* notes — this applies even if her husband is a *kohen*, meaning we do not assume she was raped. In the second scenario, she is seized because she herself has been condemned to death (*al yedei nefashos*, as Rashi glosses: *shenischayeva hariga*): in that case she is forbidden even to a husband who is a plain *Yisrael*. The reasoning in this second case is that, facing execution, she may have chosen to trade sexual compliance for her life — a situation the *gemara* regards as *ratzon* rather than *ones*, notwithstanding the profound coercive context of such a "choice."

### Challenge to Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּדַע, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: עַל יְדֵי נְפָשׁוֹת אֲסוּרָה לְבַעְלָהּ, וְתוּ לָא מִידִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Know** that this is the explanation, **as the latter clause** of that mishna **teaches:** If she was imprisoned **due to a capital** offense **she is forbidden to her husband.** Clearly, the difference is that in this case there is no incentive for the gentiles to leave her unharmed. The Gemara concludes: **And nothing more** needs discussion, as this is certainly the correct interpretation of that mishna.

The *gemara* challenges Ravina: if the *mishnah* prohibits *yichud* out of concern for rape, why — in the first scenario, where the woman was indisputably held in the captors' custody — do we not assume she was raped and forbid her to her *kohen* husband? This seems to support Ravina's principle: *lechatchila* we are concerned, but *bedieved* we are not. *Ela lav shema mina* — can we not infer from here that there is indeed a distinction between *lechatchila* and *bedieved*?

### Economics Protects

The *gemara* deflects this proof. *Dilma l'olam eima lach afilu bedieved nami* — perhaps even after the fact we would normally be concerned. The reason we are not concerned in the *mamon* case is not a general *lechatchila*/*bedieved* principle but rather a specific economic consideration: *d'mistapai mishum hefsed mamona*. The captor who is holding the woman to extract a debt from her husband has a financial incentive not to harm her. If he rapes her, the husband may reason that his wife has been "damaged" and refuse to pay the ransom. The captor's self-interest thus functions as a structural protection. By contrast, *Tosafot* notes that the *baraita* presents only two of three logically possible scenarios. A third case — a woman seized for no particular reason, neither *mamon* nor *nefashos* — is omitted, and in that case we would indeed be concerned after the fact, both for rape and for willing compliance. The *gemara* confirms this reading from the *seifa* of the *baraita* itself: the fact that she is forbidden when seized *al yedei nefashos* demonstrates that absent the protecting factor of economic self-interest, we do assume that sexual contact occurred. *V'su lo midi* — the *gemara* regards this as dispositive.

## Tosafot & Ashkenaz

### Ashkenazi Women

Tosafot, commenting on the phrase *v'su lo midi*, raises a concern of immediate practical urgency for the Ashkenazi world. If even a brief period of *yichud* with a non-Jew suffices, in principle, to raise a concern of rape after the fact — such that a woman would be forbidden to her *kohen* husband — then *Tosafot* poses a startling challenge: *im kein lo hinachta bas l'Avraham Avinu, eishes Kohen yosheves tachas ba'alah*. If we take this rule seriously, there is not a single wife of a *kohen* who could remain with her husband. Why? Because, writes *Tosafot*, *d'ee efshar shelo tisyached im shum oved kochavim sha'a achas* — it is simply impossible in their society that a woman will never find herself alone with a non-Jewish man, even for an hour.

This is a remarkable window into medieval Ashkenazi reality. The assumption underlying *Tosafot*'s objection is that women in Ashkenaz were actively engaged in the public sphere — not confined to the domestic world as was more characteristic of Sefardic society at the time. Women in Ashkenaz participated in commerce and business, which inevitably meant private meetings with non-Jewish men.

### Isha Hashuvah

This reality had already left its mark elsewhere in *Tosafot*'s rulings. The *gemara* states that a woman need not recline (*haseiba*) at the Pesach Seder because her posture of submission to her husband means that reclining would not express genuine freedom — unless she is an *isha hashuvah*, a woman of recognized social standing, in which case she does recline. *Tosafot* there applies this exception universally in Ashkenaz, stating that all Ashkenazi women are *nashim hashuvot*. The elevated social and economic status of women in Ashkenazi society was sufficiently acknowledged that *Tosafot* could make this claim categorically. The same reality surfaces here: women who conduct business with non-Jews will, on occasion, find themselves alone with them.

### Business vs. Captivity

*Tosafot*'s resolution is to distinguish between *yichud* in a business context and captivity. *D'davka nishbis shani* — it is specifically the case of being taken captive that is different, *lefi shehi mesurah b'yado*, because then the woman is entirely at the captor's mercy, removed from the normal social and commercial context, with no exit and no recourse. A private business meeting, by contrast, does not carry the same presumption. *Tosafot* holds that after the fact, in a business setting, we do not assume rape occurred. He does not, it should be noted, attempt to justify the *lechatchila* violation of the *mishnah*'s prohibition on *yichud*. He acknowledges that the *mishnah* prohibits it and does not argue otherwise — his concern is only with the *bedieved* consequence. The practical resolution, of course, is that *lechatchila* one takes whatever measures are available — an open door, a glass partition, a third party present — to avoid *yichud* in the first place.

## Rabbi Eliezer Dispute

### Bestiality Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי פְּדָת אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא רַבָּנַן. דִּתְנַן גַּבֵּי פָּרַת חַטָּאת: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ נִקַּחַת מִן הַגּוֹיִם, וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: חָיְישִׁינַן לִרְבִיעָה, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִרְבִיעָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Pedat said:** The contradiction between the mishna, which rules that gentiles are suspected of bestiality, and the *baraita*, which permits an animal purchased from gentiles to be sacrificed as an offering, is **not difficult; this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** while **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. As we learned** in a mishna (*Para* 2:1) **with regard to the** red **heifer of purification: Rabbi Eliezer says** that **it may not be purchased from gentiles, and the Rabbis permit** it to be purchased from gentiles. Rabbi Pedat explains: **What, is it not** correct to say that Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to this** issue, **that Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **we are concerned that** a person might have engaged in **bestiality** with the animal, **and the Rabbis hold** that **we are not concerned that** a person engaged in **bestiality** with the animal?

With the *lechatchila*/*bedieved* approach of Ravina having been effectively challenged, the *gemara* turns to a second answer, proposed by Rabbi Padat. *Lo kashya*, says Rabbi Padat — the apparent contradiction between the *mishnah* and the *baraita* is not a logical tension at all. *Ha Rabbi Eliezer v'ha Rabbanan* — the two sources simply reflect a *machloket* between Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* as to whether we are concerned about acts of *riva'ah* at all.

This is itself a striking move. Up to this point in the sugya, the concern over bestiality has been treated as an unquestioned premise of the *mishnah* — a given, to be resolved and worked around, but not itself contested. Now Rabbi Padat proposes that this concern is not universal rabbinic consensus but rather a *da'at yachid*, the position of Rabbi Eliezer alone, with the *chachamim* holding that we are not concerned.

At this juncture it is worth pausing on a terminological question. The text as it stands uses the term *ovdei kochavim*, which is a censored formulation introduced in the Middle Ages. The original *girsa* of the *gemara* was simply *goy*. The substitution might suggest that the concern was specifically with idolaters, which could open a conceptual door: perhaps the rabbis were not characterizing all non-Jews this way, but only the specific pagan populations of their era, whose immorality they believed they had observed. However, there is nothing in the *gemara* itself that links the *riva'ah* concern to the fact of idolatry as such — no suggestion that these acts were performed as pagan ritual or that the concern would dissolve for non-idolaters.

The more systematic resolution along these lines is offered by the *Meiri*, who employs the formulation *gedurim b'darkei hadatot vehanimusim* — nations that are restrained by the disciplines of religion and civil morality. The *Meiri*'s argument is that the ancient pagans about whom the *gemara* legislated were characterized by a generalized moral dissolution, and that the *gemara*'s concerns were grounded in that reality. When dealing with non-Jews who live by moral codes — Christians, Muslims, and others who accept civilized norms — the *Meiri* holds that these concerns simply do not apply. *Tosafot*'s answers, by contrast, tend to be more localized: specific reasons in specific cases for why a particular practice is not followed in his time. The *Meiri* offers the global framework.

### Parah Adumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִרְבִיעָה, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הִנִּיחַ (עֲלֵיהֶן) [עָלֶיהָ] עוּדָּה שֶׁל שַׂקִּין — פְּסָלָהּ, וּבְעֶגְלָה — עַד שֶׁתִּמְשׁוֹךְ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **From where** do you know that this is the case? **Perhaps everyone** agrees that **we are not concerned that** a person might have engaged in **bestiality** with the animal, **and here, this is the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer:** He holds **in accordance with** a statement **that Rabbi Yehuda** says that **Rav says. As Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If **one placed a bundle of sacks upon** a red heifer, **he has rendered it unfit** for purification, as a red heifer is fit only if it has not borne any burden, in accordance with the verse: "Upon which never came a yoke" (Numbers 19:2); **and in** the case of the **heifer** whose neck is broken, it is not rendered unfit **until you pull** a load **with it,** as the verse states: "And which has not drawn in the yoke" (Deuteronomy 21:3).

The evidence for Rabbi Padat's claim is drawn from a *baraita* concerning the *parah adumah*. Rabbi Eliezer says one may not purchase an animal for the *parah adumah* ritual from non-Jews, while the *chachamim* permit it. The *gemara* proposes: *mani lav beha kamiflegi* — is this not precisely the debate between them? *Rabbi Eliezer savar chayyishinan l'revia* — Rabbi Eliezer holds we are concerned about acts of bestiality; *rabbanan savri lo chayyishinan l'revia* — the *rabbanan* hold we are not.

## Final Resolution

### Tosafot's Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם הֲנָאָה פּוּרְתָּא לָא מַפְסֵיד טוּבָא! הָתָם, יִצְרוֹ תּוֹקְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **So too, let us say: Due to** the **slight pleasure** of engaging in bestiality with an animal, a gentile **will not forfeit a great deal** of money which he can otherwise obtain by selling the heifer. The Gemara responds: **There,** with regard to bestiality, **his inclination overcomes him,** and he is apt to engage in bestiality with the heifer despite the fact that he knows it is to his disadvantage to do so.

Before the *gemara* tests this proposed resolution, it is worth noting how *Tosafot* deploys it practically. On the previous *daf*, *Tosafot* confronted the question of why, in his time, Jews left their animals in the stables of non-Jews without concern — seemingly in violation of the *mishnah*. *Tosafot*'s ultimate answer was precisely this final resolution of the *gemara*: since the debate is between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* over whether to be concerned about *riva'ah* at all, and since the *halacha* follows the *chachamim*, we rule that there is no concern for such acts. For *Tosafot*, this is not an evasion but a clean *pesak*: the *mishnah*'s prohibition reflects Rabbi Eliezer's minority position, and the majority view of the *chachamim* — that we are not concerned — is the operative ruling.

## Burden Question

### Burden Hypothesis

The *gemara* now interrogates whether the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* is actually about *riva'ah* at all. *Mimai* — how do we know that is the point of contention? The *gemara* floats an alternative: *dilma d'kulei alma lo chayyishinan* — perhaps everyone agrees we have no concern about acts of bestiality, and the dispute is about something entirely different, namely, whether a non-Jew may have put a burden on the animal. As Rav Yehudah said in the name of Rav, even placing a bundle of sacks on the animal — without it pulling any load — invalidates it, since the verse states *asher lo ala aleha ol*, that no yoke has gone upon it. For the *egla arufa*, the verse says *asher lo mashcha be'ol*, which requires actually pulling a burden. Perhaps the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* is simply whether we are concerned that a non-Jew may have momentarily placed something on the animal, thereby disqualifying it.

### Burden Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, מִשּׁוּם נִיחָא פּוּרְתָּא לָא מַפְסֵיד טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It should **not enter your mind** that Rabbi Eliezer prohibits purchasing a red heifer from a gentile due to the concern that he might have placed sacks upon it, as **due to** the **slight convenience** of placing a bundle of sacks upon the heifer, the gentile will **not forfeit** the potential to earn **a great deal** of money which he can obtain by selling the heifer.

The *gemara* rejects this hypothesis with a straightforward argument: *mishum hana'a purta lo mafsid tuva* — no rational person would risk losing something worth tens or hundreds of thousands of *dinarim* for one moment's trivial use. If a non-Jew happens to possess a *parah adumah*, which could be sold for an extraordinary sum, he would never jeopardize that value by carelessly placing a burden on it. This concern, therefore, need not be entertained.

### Bestiality Only

The *gemara* then asks: why does the same logic not dismiss the *riva'ah* concern? Should we not equally say that a non-Jew would not risk such a valuable animal for a moment's gratification? The *gemara* answers: *hatam yitzro tokfo* — there, his *yetzer hara* overcomes him. Unlike placing a burden, which offers no compelling psychological pull, the sexual drive can override rational calculation. Thus the only coherent explanation for the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* remains whether we are concerned about *riva'ah*. The burden hypothesis is untenable; the *riva'ah* hypothesis stands.

## Everyone Not Concerned

### Verse Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִרְבִיעָה, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, כִּדְתָנֵי שֵׁילָא, דְּתָנֵי שֵׁילָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִקְחוּ אֵלֶיךָ״ — בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יִקְחוּ, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם יִקְחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And perhaps everyone** agrees that **we are not concerned that** a person might have engaged in **bestiality** with the animal, **and here, this is the reason of Rabbi Eliezer, in accordance with that** which **Sheila taught, as Sheila taught** in a *baraita*: **What is the reason of Rabbi Eliezer?** The verse states: **"Speak unto the children of Israel that they take to you** a red heifer" (Numbers 19:2). This teaches that **the children of Israel take** the red heifer, **but gentiles do not take** the red heifer.

The *gemara* makes a second attempt to argue that everyone agrees there is no concern for *riva'ah*, proposing instead that Rabbi Eliezer's reason for prohibiting purchase of the *parah adumah* from a non-Jew is textual. *Ki-detani Shila* — as Shila taught: the verse states *daber el benei Yisrael veyikchu elecha parah adumah temimah* — speak to the children of Israel and have them take for you an unblemished red heifer. The derivation: *benei Yisrael yikchu ve'ein ovdei kochavim yikchu* — it is the children of Israel who are to take it, not non-Jews. On this reading, Rabbi Eliezer's prohibition is a straightforward *derashah* on the verse, with no implication of any concern for bestiality.

### All Sacrifices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל בְּכׇל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת כּוּלָּן. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְתָנֵי שֵׁילָא, בִּשְׁלָמָא פָּרָה — כְּתִיב בָּהּ ״קִיחָה״, אֶלָּא כּוּלְּהוּ קׇרְבָּנוֹת — קִיחָה כְּתִיב בְּהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It should **not enter your mind** to say so, **as the latter clause** of that same *baraita* **teaches: And similarly, Rabbi Eliezer would disqualify** an animal purchased from a gentile **in** the case of **all offerings.** The Gemara elaborates: **And if** it should **enter your mind** that Rabbi Eliezer's reason is **in accordance with that** which **Sheila taught, granted, in** the case of the red **heifer** a term of **taking is written, but** is a term of **taking written with regard to all** other **offerings?**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *de-katani seifa, ve-chen haya Rabbi Eliezer posel be-chol ha-korbanot kulan* — a *seifa* of the *baraita* states that Rabbi Eliezer would disqualify all sacrificial animals purchased from non-Jews, not only the *parah adumah*. This is precisely the position that conflicts with the *baraita* cited earlier, which permits buying animals for *korbanot*.

### Bestiality General

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Since a term of taking does not appear in the context of other offerings, this cannot be Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning. The Gemara suggests: **And perhaps the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Eliezer**

If Rabbi Eliezer's reason were purely textual — anchored in the verse *daber el benei Yisrael* unique to the *parah adumah* — there would be no basis for extending his prohibition to other sacrifices, where no such verse exists. *Bishlama parah, ktiv bah kicha* — for the *parah adumah*, the verse does say "take" from *benei Yisrael*. *Ela kuleihu korbanot, kicha ktiv behu?* — but does any such verse exist for other sacrifices? It does not. Therefore, the extension of the prohibition to all *korbanot* cannot be grounded in a specific verse. It must reflect a general concern — and the only coherent general concern is *riva'ah*. The attempt to frame the dispute as purely exegetical collapses.

## Final Resolution (II)

### Only R. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּפָרָה, דְּדָמֶיהָ יְקָרִין, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר קׇרְבְּנוֹת מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **only with regard to** the red **heifer, as its price is exorbitant,** and the Rabbis maintain that the gentile would not risk forfeiting the profit for a temporary benefit. **But with regard to the rest** of the **offerings,** which are not exceptionally valuable, **they concede to** Rabbi Eliezer that animals purchased from gentiles may not be used for these offerings.

The *gemara* now arrives at its conclusion. The dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *rabbanan* is indeed over whether to be concerned about acts of *riva'ah*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that we are so concerned, and therefore prohibits both leaving animals in non-Jewish stables and purchasing animals from non-Jews for sacrificial use. The *rabbanan* hold that we are not so concerned, and therefore permit purchasing animals for *korbanot* — and by extension, the prohibition in the *mishnah* against leaving animals in non-Jewish stables represents Rabbi Eliezer's view. The practical *halacha*, as *Tosafot* concludes, follows the *chachamim*.

## Bestiality Invalidates

### Red Heifer Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, דְּחַטָּאת קַרְיַיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: Although it is classified as consecrated for Temple maintenance, the purification offering of the red **heifer is different, as the Merciful One labels it** with the term for **a sin-offering.** Accordingly, the red heifer is subject to the same *halakhot* as a sin-offering, which means it is disqualified if it is the object of bestiality.

With the primary dispute resolved, the *gemara* turns to a structural question: how can an act of *riva'ah* invalidate a *parah adumah* in the first place? We know from a classic *midrash halakha* that *riva'ah* and *avoda zarah* disqualify animals from use as *korbanot*, derived from the phrase *min hatzon korbano* — from the flock and not all the flock. But the *parah adumah* is not a *korban* in the conventional sense. It is slaughtered outside the Temple precincts; it is burned, not offered on the altar. What is its precise sanctity?

### Sacrifice Sanctity

The *gemara* considers the possibility that the *parah adumah* possesses *kedushat mizbeach* — sacrificial sanctity, the same category of holiness as an animal brought to the altar. If so, whatever invalidates a *korban* would naturally invalidate the *parah adumah* as well, including *riva'ah*.

### Temple Only

The *gemara* raises the counter-possibility: perhaps the *parah adumah* has only *kedushat bedek habayit* — the more general sanctity of objects consecrated for Temple use but not for the altar. An animal consecrated for non-sacrificial Temple purposes — say, for the *kohanim* to use for transportation — would not be invalidated by an act of *riva'ah*, since that disqualification pertains specifically to sacrificial holiness. If the *parah adumah* has only this lesser sanctity, *riva'ah* should not affect it.

### Called Chatat

The *gemara* resolves the question by pointing to the Torah's own language: *shani parah d'chatas karya rachmana* — the *parah adumah* is different, because the Torah itself calls it a *chatat*. The verse reads *l'mei niddah chatat hi* — it is a sin offering. The *gemara*'s position is nuanced: the *parah adumah* does not possess full *kedushat mizbeach* for all purposes, but because the Torah designates it a *chatat*, the invalidating conditions that apply to a *chatat* apply here as well. This is a functional rather than ontological equivalence — for the specific purpose of determining what disqualifies it, the Torah's terminology controls.

## C-Section Question

### C-Section Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תִּיפָּסֵל בְּיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: הִקְדִּישָׁהּ בְּיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן — פְּסוּלָה, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **If that is so,** then the red heifer **should be disqualified** if it was **born by caesarean section,** as this is the *halakha* concerning all other sin-offerings. The Gemara adds: **And if you would say** that **indeed, that is so,** then **why is it taught** in a *baraita*: If one **consecrated** a red heifer despite the fact that it was **born by caesarean section,** it is **disqualified** for use as a red heifer, **and Rabbi Shimon deems** the heifer **fit** for use in purification. If, as indicated by the verse, the *halakhot* of a sin-offering apply to the red heifer, how can Rabbi Shimon deem this animal fit?

The *gemara* now tests whether this reasoning is fully consistent. If the *parah adumah* shares the invalidating conditions of a *chatat*, it should also be invalidated by *yotzei dofen* — birth by Caesarean section. Ordinary sacrifices require natural vaginal birth, derived from the verse *ki shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled*, where *yivaled* is understood to mean birth from the place of conception, excluding C-section birth. Accordingly, if the *chatat* framework governs invalidation of the *parah adumah*, a *parah adumah* born by C-section should similarly be disqualified.

### C-Section Debate

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that addresses precisely this question. According to the *chachamim*, a *parah adumah* born by C-section is indeed invalid (*pesulah*). According to Rabbi Shimon, it is valid (*machshir*). The *chachamim*'s position coheres smoothly with the *chatat* framework: just as C-section birth invalidates a *korban*, it invalidates the *parah adumah*. Rabbi Shimon's validation requires explanation.

### R. Shimon General

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֶה הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְעִנְיַן קָדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And if you would say** that **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** (see *Nidda* 40a): A baby **born by caesarean section is** considered **a full-fledged offspring** and is no different from a baby born in a regular manner, that is difficult: **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that **Rabbi Shimon would concede with regard to sacrificial** animals **that** an animal born by caesarian section **is not consecrated?** If so, even Rabbi Shimon should agree that the heifer is disqualified.

The *gemara* initially proposes that Rabbi Shimon is simply consistent with his general position: *d'amar yotzei dofen velad maalya hu* — Rabbi Shimon holds that a C-section birth is a fully valid birth in all respects, and does not distinguish it from vaginal birth in any area of *halacha*. The classic illustration is the law of *yoledet*: the Torah says *isha ki tazria v'yalda zachar v'tam'a shivas yamim*, and the rabbis derive that the *tumah* of a *yoledet* applies only to vaginal birth — *ad sheyalda mimakom shemazraas*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees, holding that the *parsha* applies even to a C-section birth. Perhaps, then, Rabbi Shimon simply never treats C-section birth as categorically different.

### R. Yochanan

This solution is undermined, however, by a ruling of Rabbi Yochanan. *Modeh hayah Rabbi Shimon l'inyan kodashim she'eino kadosh* — Rabbi Yochanan states that Rabbi Shimon himself concedes that for the purposes of *kodashim*, a C-section animal is not sanctified as a sacrifice. Rabbi Shimon's general position does not extend to *korbanot*; even he agrees that for ordinary sacrificial animals, C-section birth is a disqualification. This creates a problem: if Rabbi Shimon agrees that C-section birth disqualifies normal sacrifices, and if the *parah adumah* is governed by the same invalidating conditions as a *chatat*, why does Rabbi Shimon validate a C-section *parah adumah*?

## Blemish Answer

### Physical & Ritual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, הוֹאִיל וּמוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ — דְּבַר עֶרְוָה וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה נָמֵי פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי מׇשְׁחָתָם בָּהֶם מוּם בָּם״, וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כָּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר הַשְׁחָתָה — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא דְּבַר עֶרְוָה וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather,** the red **heifer is different: Since a blemish disqualifies it, a matter of licentiousness and** a matter of **idol worship also disqualify it, as it is written:** "Neither from the hand of a foreigner shall you offer the bread of your God of any of these, **because their corruption is in them, there is a blemish in them"** (Leviticus 22:25). This verse indicates that corruption is considered a blemish, **and the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Anywhere that** the term **corruption is stated,** it **is** a reference to **nothing other than a matter of licentiousness and idol worship.**

The *gemara* resolves this through a reconceptualization of what invalidates the *parah adumah*. According to the *chachamim*, one might explain the *parah adumah* as possessing *kedushat mizbeach*, which would mean all sacrificial invalidities — including C-section — apply to it straightforwardly. Rabbi Shimon, however, does not treat the *parah adumah* as having sacrificial sanctity. For him, the operating invalidating principle is *mum* — physical blemish. The Torah requires the *parah adumah* to be *temimah*, unblemished. Since *mum* invalidates, and since Rabbi Shimon extends the concept of *mum* beyond physical defect to include what might be called ritual blemish, a broader but still bounded category of invalidities applies.

### Corruption Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבַר עֶרְוָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הִשְׁחִית כׇּל בָּשָׂר אֶת דַּרְכּוֹ עַל הָאָרֶץ״, וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״פֶּן תַּשְׁחִתוּן וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לָכֶם פֶּסֶל״; וְהָא פָּרָה נָמֵי, הוֹאִיל וּמוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ, דְּבַר עֶרְוָה וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה פָּסְלִי בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara supports this claim: Corruption is a reference to **a matter of licentiousness, as it is written** with regard to the generation of the flood: "And God saw the earth, and, behold, it was corrupt; **for all flesh had corrupted their way upon the earth"** (Genesis 6:12). **And** corruption also is a reference to **idol worship, as it is written: "Lest you deal corruptly, and make you a graven image"** (Deuteronomy 4:16). **And consequently,** with regard to the red **heifer also, since a blemish disqualifies it, a matter of licentiousness and idol worship** likewise **disqualify it.**

The textual basis for treating acts of *riva'ah* and *avoda zarah* as constituting a *mum* is the verse *ki moshchasam bahem mum bam* — their corruption is in them, their blemish is in them. The verse identifies *hashchasah* (corruption) with *mum* (blemish). The *gemara* then invokes a *midrash* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael to define what *hashchasah* means.

### R. Yishmael

*Vetana devei Rabbi Yishmael: kol makom shene'emar hashchasa, eino ela davar ervah ve'avoda zarah* — wherever the Torah uses the root *hashchasah*, it refers either to sexual perversion or to idolatry. Two prooftexts anchor this: for sexual perversion, *ki hishchis kol basar es darko al ha'aretz* — all flesh had corrupted its way upon the earth (the classic rabbinic reading of the generation of the Flood as having engaged in, among other perversions, acts of bestiality). For idolatry, *pen tashchisun va'asisem lachem pesel* — lest you act corruptly and make for yourselves an idol. The conclusion: acts of forbidden sexuality and acts of idolatrous worship both constitute a *hashchasah*, and by the equation of *hashchasah* with *mum*, they constitute a ritual blemish on the animal. It is this *mum* that, for Rabbi Shimon, disqualifies the *parah adumah* — not its sacrificial sanctity, but the specific requirement of *temimah* interpreted to include the absence of ritual corruption.

## Shila Verse

### Verse Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, תָּנֵי שֵׁילָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? דִּכְתִיב: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִקְחוּ״, בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יִקְחוּ, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם יִקְחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Earlier, Sheila provided a rationale for Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that a red heifer may not be purchased from gentiles. The Gemara examines **the** matter **itself. Sheila teaches** in a *baraita*: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer?** It is **as it is written: "Speak unto the children of Israel that they take to you** a red heifer" (Numbers 19:2). This indicates that **the children of Israel take** the red heifer, **but gentiles do not take** the red heifer. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then when the verse states with regard to the donations for the Tabernacle: **"Speak unto the children of Israel, that they take for Me an offering"** (Exodus 25:2), **so too** one can claim **that** only **the children of Israel take** an offering for God, **but gentiles do not take** an offering, and that no items for the Temple service may be purchased from gentiles.

The *gemara* now returns, in a section labeled *gufa*, to examine Shila's teaching in its own right. *Mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Eliezer?* — what is Rabbi Eliezer's actual reasoning for prohibiting purchase of the *parah adumah* from non-Jews? Shila teaches: *dechsiv daber el benei Yisrael veyikchu* — the Torah says speak to the children of Israel and have them take — *benei Yisrael yikchu ve'ein ovdei kochavim yikchu*, the children of Israel are to take it, and not non-Jews. On Shila's reading, Rabbi Eliezer's prohibition is a direct derivation from this verse, entirely independent of any concern over *riva'ah*.

### Terumah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִקְחוּ לִי תְּרוּמָה״, הָכִי נָמֵי דִּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יִקְחוּ, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם יִקְחוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then when the verse states with regard to the donations for the Tabernacle: **"Speak unto the children of Israel, that they take for Me an offering"** (Exodus 25:2), **so too** one can claim **that** only **the children of Israel take** an offering for God, **but gentiles do not take** an offering, and that no items for the Temple service may be purchased from gentiles.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *daber el benei Yisrael veyikchu li terumah* — the Torah also says speak to the children of Israel and have them take for Me a donation (*terumah*). Does this mean, by the same logic, that donations for the *Mishkan* may only be accepted from Jews, not from non-Jews? *Hachi nami de-benei Yisrael yikchu ve'ein ovdei kochavim yikchu?* Would Rabbi Eliezer hold that one cannot accept a non-Jew's contribution to the Temple building fund? The *gemara* considers the possibility — *v'chi teima hachi nami*, maybe so — but then raises a decisive counter-example.

## Dama ben Netina

### Filial Piety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: עַד הֵיכָן כִּיבּוּד אָב וָאֵם? אָמַר לָהֶם: צְאוּ וּרְאוּ מָה עָשָׂה גּוֹי אֶחָד לְאָבִיו בְּאַשְׁקְלוֹן, וְדָמָא בֶּן נְתִינָה שְׁמוֹ. פַּעַם אַחַת בִּקְּשׁוּ מִמֶּנּוּ אֲבָנִים לָאֵפוֹד
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **indeed, that is so,** this cannot be correct. **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel says:** The Sages **asked Rabbi Eliezer: To what extent** must one exert himself to fulfill the mitzva of **honoring one's father and mother?** Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: Go and see what a certain gentile did for his father in Ashkelon, and his name is Dama ben Netina. Once,** the Sages **sought** to purchase precious **stones from him for the ephod** of the High Priest

The *gemara* introduces the story of Dama ben Netina in the context of Rabbi Eliezer's own teaching. Asked how far the obligation of *kibud av va'em* extends, Rabbi Eliezer responds: *tze'u ur'u mah asah oved kochavim echad la'aviv be'Ashkelon* — go and see what a certain non-Jew in Ashkelon did for his father. The choice to cite a non-Jewish exemplar is itself notable. Rabbi Eliezer appears to be pointing to something universal in the moral architecture of filial piety — something so deeply embedded in the fabric of human ethics that even one not commanded in *kibud av va'em* will enact it at great personal cost.

### Dama's Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשִׁשִּׁים רִבּוֹא שָׂכָר, רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי בִּשְׁמוֹנִים רִבּוֹא, וְהָיוּ מַפְתְּחוֹת מוּנָּחוֹת תַּחַת מְרַאֲשׁוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָבִיו, וְלֹא צַּעֲרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **for six hundred thousand** gold dinars' **profit,** and **Rav Kahana teaches** that it was **eight hundred thousand** dinars' profit. **But the keys** to the chest holding the jewels **were placed under his father's head, and he would not disturb him.** It is evident from this story that the stones of the ephod may be purchased from gentiles. If so, Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning cannot be based on Numbers 19:2, as an identical phrase appears in Exodus 25:2, and yet contributions to the Tabernacle may be accepted from gentiles.

The non-Jew is identified as Dama ben Netina. The *chachamim* came to purchase precious stones for the *eifod* from him. They were prepared to pay 600,000 *dinarim* — and according to Rav Kahana's version of the story, 800,000. The keys to the warehouse, however, were under the head of Dama's sleeping father. Dama refused to disturb his father's sleep, and the transaction fell through at the cost of an enormous sum. The *poskim* note, with some wryness, that presumably Dama's father would have wished to be woken for such a deal — which is why these extreme stories of *kibud av va'em* are sometimes understood as illustrating an exceptional standard beyond strict obligation rather than a normative ruling. But for purposes of the *gemara*'s argument, what matters is the outcome: the *chachamim* intended to purchase the *avnei eifod* from this non-Jew.

### Ephod Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַבְנֵי שֹׁהַם״ הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן, וְהָא ״וְאַבְנֵי מִלּוּאִים״ כְּתִיב, דַּהֲדַר עָרְבֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the phrase **"onyx stones"** (Exodus 25:7) **concluded** discussion of that **matter.** In other words, almost all the items listed in Exodus, chapter 25, for the contributions to the Tabernacle, are joined by the conjunction "and." Before the verse mentions the stones for the ephod, it states "onyx stones," without the conjunction "and." This omission serves to disconnect these stones from the earlier phrase: "That they take" (Exodus 25:2), which means that the disqualification of items purchased from gentiles for use in the Temple derived from this phrase does not apply to the items listed after that point, including the stones of the ephod. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it** subsequently **written: "And stones to be set,"** by **which the verse goes back and combines** the list, so that the earlier verse applies once more? Accordingly, it should be prohibited to purchase any of the items listed in the verse from gentiles.

This creates a difficulty for Shila's reading of Rabbi Eliezer. The *avnei milu'im* — the *avnei eifod* — appear in the same passage as the *terumah*: *vezos haterumah asher tikchu me'itam*, this is the donation you are to take from them — followed by a long list of materials including, at the end, *avnei shoham ve'avnei milu'im*. If the verse *daber el benei Yisrael veyikchu* limits contributions to Jews alone, how could the *chachamim* have purchased the *avnei eifod* from Dama ben Netina? And since Rabbi Eliezer himself is the one who cites this story approvingly, he cannot simultaneously hold that such a purchase was impermissible.

## Unresolved

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: לְשָׁנָה אַחֶרֶת נוֹלְדָה לוֹ פָּרָה אֲדוּמָּה בְּעֶדְרוֹ, נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶם: יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי בָּכֶם שֶׁאִם אֲנִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִכֶּם כׇּל מָמוֹן שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם אַתֶּם נוֹתְנִין לִי, עַכְשָׁיו אֵינִי מְבַקֵּשׁ מִכֶּם אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן שֶׁהִפְסַדְתִּי בִּשְׁבִיל אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And furthermore, the latter clause teaches: In a subsequent year, a red heifer was born in** Dama's **herd** and **the Sages of Israel approached him,** seeking to purchase the heifer. Dama **said to them: I know concerning you that if I** were to **ask from you all the money in the world, you would give** it **to me. Now I am requesting from you only that** amount of **money which I lost by** refraining from waking **my father.**

The *gemara* thus leaves us with an unresolved tension: Rabbi Eliezer, on Shila's reading, derives a prohibition against purchasing the *parah adumah* from a non-Jew from the verse *daber el benei Yisrael veyikchu*. Yet the same linguistic logic, applied to the *terumah* passage, would prohibit accepting the *avnei eifod* from a non-Jew — and Rabbi Eliezer himself approvingly recounts the story of their purchase from Dama ben Netina. The *gemara* ends here, without resolution, leaving this as the cliffhanger that will open the next session.
