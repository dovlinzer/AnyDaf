# Shevuot 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## False Oath Impact

### Shevua vs. Other Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נִפְרָעִים מִמֶּנּוּ; וְכָאן מִמֶּנּוּ וּמִכׇּל הָעוֹלָם כֻּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **all transgressions that are in the Torah, only he himself is punished for them,** but here, concerning a false oath, **both he and the entire world are punished.**

Today's daf is formally *daf mem*, though we are picking up from the bottom of 39a, still unpacking the *baraita* that addresses the administration of an oath and the weight of a false one. That *baraita* echoes the warnings given to witnesses before testimony — how people's lives hang in the balance — except here the focus is how a false *shevua* can be destructive not only to the person who swears it, but to his family and the entire world.

The *gemara* picks up five lines from the bottom of the page: *v'chol aveirot shebatorah nifra'im mimenu* — for other sins, only the person himself is punished. But here, *v'kan mimenu umikol ha'olam kulo* — in the case of a false *shevua*, the entire world suffers.

### Collective Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחׇל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה מִכׇּל הָעוֹלָם לוֹ? וְהָא כְּתִיב: "וְכִי יִכָּשְׁלוּ אִישׁ בְּאָחִיו"; וּמְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲרֵבִים זֶה לָזֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** are **all transgressions that are in the Torah** also punished **from the entire world?** **But** the verse **states: "And when they stumble, one against the other,"** which **teaches that all Israel are guarantors for one another,** implying that the entire world is affected by the transgression of individuals.

This immediately raises an objection. The *gemara* asks: *v'chol aveirot shebatorah mikol ha'olam lo?* Are we saying that the rest of the world is entirely unaffected by other sins? But the verse states *vechi kashlu ish be'achiv* — a man will stumble through his brother's sin — and *melamed shekol Yisrael arevim zeh lazeh*, teaching that all of Israel are guarantors for one another. This suggests the world does suffer for others' sins!

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדָם לְמַחוֹת, וְלֹא מִיחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in that verse, the reference is to a case **where** the others **had the ability to protest** the transgression, **and** nevertheless, **they did not protest.** By contrast, when one takes a false oath, the entire world is punished, even those who were not able to protest.

The *gemara* resolves this: *hasam she'yesh b'yadam limchot v'lo michu* — that principle applies only where bystanders could have protested and failed to do so. They are passive enablers, and that degree of complicity accounts for their culpability. In the case of a false *shevua*, however, the metaphysical consequence extends even to those who bear no responsibility whatsoever.

### Punishment Gradations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רְשָׁעִים דְּמִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ לָרְשָׁעִים דְּעָלְמָא; בֵּין צַדִּיקִים דְּמִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ לְצַדִּיקִים דְּעָלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** the punishment of **the wicked** people **in** a transgressor's **family and** the punishment of the other **wicked** people **of the world,** and **between** the punishment of the **righteous** people **in his family and** the punishment of the other **righteous** people **of the world?**

The *gemara* further refines the distinctions. It asks: *mai ika bein rish'ei d'mishpacha l'rish'ei d'alma, bein tzadikei d'mishpacha l'tzadikei d'alma* — what is the difference between a *rasha* of the family and a *rasha* of the wider world, or between the *tzadikim* of the family and those of the world at large?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא בִּשְׁאָר עֲבֵירוֹת – בְּדִינֵיהּ; וּרְשָׁעִים דְּמִשְׁפָּחָה – בְּדִין חָמוּר; וּרְשָׁעִים דְּעָלְמָא – בְּדִין הַקַּל; צַדִּיקֵי דְּהָכָא וְהָכָא – פְּטִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With regard to other transgressions,** the transgressor **himself** is punished **with his** own **punishment,** i.e., that which is written in the Torah for that transgression. **And** the **wicked** people **of** his **family,** who covered for him, are punished **with** another **severe punishment, and** the **wicked** people of the rest **of the world,** who refrained from protesting his action, are punished **with a light punishment.** The **righteous** people both **here and here,** i.e., both his family members who did not cover for him, and others who were not able to protest his action, are **exempt** from punishment.

For ordinary sins, the perpetrator is punished with the full weight of his transgression — *b'sha'ar aveirot b'dinei*. Family members who enabled him, whether actively or passively, receive a weightier punishment — *v'rasha d'mishpacha b'din chamur*. Bystanders in the broader world who were passive enablers receive a lighter punishment — *v'rasha d'alma b'din hakal*. Crucially, those who are completely blameless, the *tzadikim*, are entirely exempt — *tzadikei d'hacha v'hacha peturei*.

This framework reveals two layers of distinction. First, culpability is required: one must be a *rasha* in some sense — either the perpetrator, an active enabler, or a passive one — before the punishment applies. The label *rasha* here is pointed: the person who simply did not protest is designated a *rasha* for that failure to act. Second, even among the culpable, punishment is graduated according to proximity to the wrong.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי שְׁבוּעָה, הוּא וּרְשָׁעִים דְּמִשְׁפָּחָה – כְּדִינֵיהּ; וּרְשָׁעִים דְּעָלְמָא – בְּדִין חָמוּר; וְצַדִּיקֵי דְּהָכָא וְהָכָא – בְּדִין הַקַּל.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to** one who takes a false **oath,** by contrast, **he and** the **wicked** people **of** his **family** are all punished **with his punishment,** i.e., they receive the same punishment he does. **And** the **wicked** people of the rest **of the world,** who refrained from protesting his action, are punished **with a severe punishment, and** the **righteous** people both **here,** in his family, **and here,** in the rest of the world, are punished **with a light punishment,** even though they did not behave inappropriately.

In the case of a *shevua*, the structure shifts. The perpetrator and his family enablers are punished with equal severity: *hu vchol mishpachto kidinei*. The broader world's passive enablers receive a weighty though lesser punishment: *rish'ei d'alma b'din chamur*.

### Universal Impact

Here is the major *chiddush*: *vetzadikei d'hacha d'hacha b'din kal* — even the entirely blameless, both of the family and of the world at large, receive some form of light punishment. This is the fundamental difference between a *shevua* and all other sins. For other transgressions, the *tzadikim* are exempt. For a false *shevua*, no one escapes entirely. The suffering of the world is not a function of guilt — it is a metaphysical reality set in motion by the oath itself. The *tzadikim* of the family may seem harder to envision, but there are always exceptions — infants, for instance, who could not possibly have been complicit.

## Oath Administration

### Refusal & Joint Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמַר ״אֵינִי נִשְׁבָּע״ – פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ מִיָּד, וְאִם אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נִשְׁבָּע״ – הָעוֹמְדִים שָׁם אוֹמְרִים זֶה לָזֶה: ״סוּרוּ נָא מֵעַל אׇהֳלֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים הָרְשָׁעִים הָאֵלֶּה״. בִּשְׁלָמָא הָהוּא דְּקָא מִשְׁתְּבַע – קָאֵי בְּאִיסּוּרָא; אֶלָּא הָהוּא דְּקָא מַשְׁבַּע לֵיהּ – אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* states: **If** the defendant **says: I will not take an oath,** the court **dismisses him immediately,** and rules him liable to pay. **And if he says: I will take an oath, the** people **standing there say to each other: "Depart, I pray you, from the tents of these wicked men."** The Gemara asks: **Granted that one who is taking the oath stands to** transgress **the prohibition** against taking a false oath; **but why is the one administering the oath to him,** i.e., the claimant, considered a wicked man?

The *baraita* continues: *im amar eini nishba potrin oto miyad* — if he says he will not swear, he is released immediately. But if he says *harei ani nishba* — he intends to go ahead with the oath — those present invoke the verse *suru na me'al ohalei ha'anashim harishaim ha'eileh*, calling on all to distance themselves from these wicked men. This designates both the one taking the oath and the one imposing it as a *rasha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״שְׁבֻעַת ה׳ תִּהְיֶה בֵּין שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁחָלָה שְׁבוּעָה עַל שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** designation is **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Tarfon says** that the verse: **"The oath of the Lord shall be between them both"** (Exodus 22:10), **teaches that** the punishment for a false **oath applies to both** the one who took the oath and the one who administered it to him.

The *gemara* asks: *bishlama hu deka mishtava kai be'issura* — understandably the one taking a false oath transgresses. But why is the one imposing the oath also called a *rasha*? The answer comes from a *braita* of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *shevuat Hashem tihyeh bein sheneihem* — the oath of God rests upon both of them. Both parties are, in some sense, participants in the act of swearing.

Rashi explains that both are punished because the one who imposed the oath failed to ensure proper documentary evidence or adequate witnesses, thereby allowing the situation to arrive at a *chillul Hashem*. This reasoning, though, sits uneasily in certain cases. In a situation involving *eid echad*, for instance, the claimant cannot always arrange for additional witnesses — a car accident has whatever witnesses it has. So the *gemara* may be gesturing at something more metaphysical: you set this in motion, and a false *shevua* resulted. Perhaps you could have waived your claim. The discussion here is subtly different from the earlier analysis of family and world — it is not simply about fault and guilt but about the raw reality of the *shevua* and who brought it about.

### Oath Language Standardization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁלֹּא עַל דַּעְתְּךָ וּכוּ׳. לְמָה לִי לְמֵימְרָא לֵיהּ הָכִי? מִשּׁוּם קַנְיָא דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* states: **And when** the judges **administer the oath to him, they say to him: Be aware that we administer an oath to you not according to your understanding,** but according to the understanding of the Omnipresent and according to the understanding of the court. The Gemara explains: **Why does** the court need **to say this to him?** It is **due to** deceptions like that **of the reed in Rava's** court, where the defendant secretly inserted into a hollow reed the money he owed and gave it to the claimant to hold for him, whereupon he took an oath that he had already given him the money. He then took back the reed, as the claimant was unaware of its contents. In this way, he was able to state an oath that was technically true, although he was guilty of deception. To prevent this, the court stipulates that the oath must be true according to the court's understanding.

The *baraita* continues: *uche'shemashbi'in oto omrim lo hevei yodeia shelo al da'atcha* — when administering the oath, the court informs the oath-taker that the oath is not interpreted according to his own understanding, but according to theirs.

### Hollowed Staff Case

The *gemara* asks: *lama li lemeimar lei hachi?* Why must the court say this? *Mishum kanya deRava* — because of the incident involving Rava's staff. A man who owed money hollowed out his staff, filled it with the coins he owed, and handed it to his creditor, asking him to hold it. He then swore that he had returned the money — technically true, since the creditor was at that moment holding the coins inside the staff. The debtor was thus playing semantic games, assigning private meaning to his oath. To prevent such manipulation, the court declares that the oath means only what they intend it to mean, not what the oath-taker privately has in mind.

This concludes the *baraita's* treatment of the weight and administration of a false *shevua* — the gravity of swearing falsely, the framing designed to deter the oath-taker, and the safeguard ensuring the oath means what the court says it means.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Mishnah Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַטַּעֲנָה שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף. אָמַר רַב: כְּפִירַת טַעֲנָה שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: טַעֲנָה עַצְמָהּ שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף; אֲפִילּוּ לֹא כָּפַר אֶלָּא בִּפְרוּטָה, וְלֹא הוֹדָה אֶלָּא בִּפְרוּטָה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the court administers an oath to one who admits to part of a claim only when **the claim is** for at least the value of **two silver** *ma'a*. Rav and Shmuel disagree with regard to the exact meaning of this statement. **Rav says** that **the denial of** the **claim** must be at least the value of **two silver** *ma'a*. **And Shmuel says** that the **claim itself** must be at least the value of **two silver** *ma'a*; i.e., **even** if the defendant **denied only one *peruta*** of the claim, **or** conversely, if **he admitted to only one *peruta*** of the two-*ma'a* claim, he is **liable** to take an oath.

The *gemara* now shifts to a more technical *halachic* discussion. The Mishnah states that *modeh bemiktzat* requires a *ta'anah* of *shtei kesef* — two *ma'ah*, which we will call twenty dollars for convenience — and an admission of at least a *perutah*, a penny. The question is an ambiguity in the Mishnah itself: does the requirement of *shtei kesef* apply to the *claim*, or to the *denial*?

### Rav's Position

*Amar Rav kefirat ta'ana shtei kesef* — Rav says the denial must be *shtei kesef*. It is not enough that twenty dollars was claimed; the amount actually denied must be at least twenty dollars.

### Shmuel's Position

*uShmuel amar ta'ana atzma shtei kesef* — Shmuel says the claim itself must be twenty dollars, but even if you admitted to nineteen dollars and ninety-nine cents and only denied a single penny, you are still *chayav*. Conversely, even if you admitted only to a penny and denied nineteen dollars and ninety-nine cents, you are still *chayav*. Once the original claim was large enough, the *shevua* attaches.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דַּיְקָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב, וּקְרָאֵי כְּוָתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. דַּיְקָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב – דְּקָתָנֵי הַטַּעֲנָה שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף וְהַהוֹדָאָה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְאִילּוּ כְּפִירַת טַעֲנָה פְּרוּטָה לָא קָתָנֵי. וּתְנַן נָמֵי הַהוֹדָאָה בִּפְרוּטָה, וְאִילּוּ כְּפִירָה בִּפְרוּטָה לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The language of **the mishna is precise in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav, and the** biblical **verses are** precise **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel.** The language of **the mishna is precise in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav, as it teaches: The claim** must be for at least the value of **two silver** *ma'a* **and the admission** must be at least **the value of one *peruta*, whereas it does not teach** that **the** minimum **denial of** the **claim** is **one *peruta*,** indicating that it must be more. **And also, we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 55a) that **the** minimum **admission** to part of a claim that renders one liable to take an oath is the value **of one *peruta*, whereas** that mishna **does not teach** that the minimum **denial** is the value **of one *peruta*.**

Rav himself acknowledges an internal tension: *dika matnitan kevasi d'Rav, vekrah kevasi de'Shmuel* — the Mishnah reads more naturally like Rav's position, but the *pesukim* read more naturally like Shmuel's.

The Mishnah's formulation supports Rav because it specifies a *ta'ana* of *shtei kesef* and an admission of a *perutah*, but never explicitly mentions the case where only a *perutah* is denied — which would be Shmuel's sharpest *chiddush*. Had Shmuel been right, the Mishnah should have highlighted the remarkable ruling that one takes an oath even when denying only a penny, yet that formulation is absent. The same observation applies to a parallel Mishnah in Bava Metzia.

### Kesef O Keilim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְרָאֵי כְּוָתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל – דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ כֶּסֶף אוֹ כֵלִים לִשְׁמוֹר״ – מָה ״כֵּלִים״ שְׁנַיִם, אַף ״כֶּסֶף״ שְׁנַיִם; מָה כֶּסֶף דָּבָר חָשׁוּב, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר חָשׁוּב; וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״כִּי הוּא זֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the verses are** precise **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel, as it is written** with regard to the case where an oath is administered due to admission to part of a claim: **"If a man delivers to his neighbor silver or vessels to safeguard"** (Exodus 22:6). It is derived from the juxtaposition of silver and vessels that **just as "vessels,"** in the plural, is referring to at least **two, so too, "silver"** is referring to at least **two** *ma'a* of silver. It is derived further that **just as silver is an item of substantial value, so too, any item of substantial value** is included. **And the Merciful One states** in the subsequent verses that an oath is administered in a case "where one says: **This is it"** (Exodus 22:8), i.e., where the defendant admits to only part of the claim of "silver or vessels" and denies the rest of the claim. Evidently, the denial can be for an amount less than two silver *ma'a*.

The *pesukim*, however, read more naturally like Shmuel. The verse in the section dealing with *shomrim* states *ki yiten ish l're'eihu kesef o keilim lishmor* — if a man gives his fellow silver or vessels to watch. The *gemara* learns from the plural *keilim* that *kesef* must also be plural — at minimum two — and the question becomes: two what? The tradition establishes it means two *ma'ah*, which we are calling twenty dollars.

The verse continues with an additional derivation: *ma kesef davar chashuv, af keilim davar chashuv* — just as silver must be significant, so too *keilim* must be significant. Here Tosafot's reading is preferable to Rashi's: *keilim* must be a *davar chashuv*, meaning each vessel must be worth at least a *perutah*, even if the pair together does not reach the monetary threshold of *shtei kesef*. A *kli* carries intrinsic significance beyond its market value — one needle can sew an entire garment — and so two needles worth a *perutah* each would be sufficient to trigger *modeh bemiktzat*, even though their combined value is far below twenty dollars. When dealing with *keilim*, you do not need the *shtei kesef* threshold at all.

The verse then says *ki hu zeh* — which the *gemara* uses to establish the basic requirement of *hoda'ah bemiktzat ha-ta'anah*, partial admission within the claim, and also the requirement that the admission be *minin ha-ta'anah*, of the same genre as what was claimed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב – הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְהוֹדָאָה בְּמִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל – כְּתִיב ״הוּא״ וּכְתִיב ״זֶה״, דְּאִי כְּפַר בְּמִקְצָת וְאוֹדִי בְּמִקְצָת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav** would respond **that** the phrase "this is it" **is necessary to** indicate **admission to a part of the claim,** but it does not indicate the value being denied, which must be at least two silver *ma'a*. The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Shmuel** derive both *halakhot* from the verse, namely, that admission to part of a claim is necessary for an oath to be administered, and that the denial can be less than the value of two *ma'a*? The Gemara answers: **It is written: "This is," and it is written: "It."** The repetition of these similar terms is homiletically interpreted to indicate **that if** the defendant **denied part** of the two-*ma'a* claim **and admitted to** the other **part,** he is **liable** to take an oath.

Shmuel reads the verse as a whole to indicate that the twenty dollars is what was claimed, not what was denied. The structure of the verse — *kesef o keilim* being what the person gave — points naturally to the claim. When the *gemara* presses that this seems to show the claim was *shtei kesef*, implicitly confirming Shmuel, Rav responds: *kesef ki asa me-ikara le-kfira hu de-asa* — the word *kesef* comes primarily to address the denial, not the claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר לְךָ רַב: ״כֶּסֶף״ כִּי אֲתָא מֵעִיקָּרָא – לִכְפִירָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא; דְּאִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ כֵּלִים לִשְׁמוֹר״ – וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: מָה כֵּלִים שְׁנַיִם, אַף כֹּל שְׁנַיִם; ״כֶּסֶף״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְטַעֲנָה, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִכְפִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav** could have **said to you** that **when** the word **"silver" came initially, it came** as a source **for** the value of the **denial,** not the claim; **as if** this were not **so,** if it were meant to refer to the value of the claim, **let the Merciful One write** in the verse: **If a man delivers to his neighbor vessels to safeguard,** without mentioning silver, **and I** would **say** that **just as vessels are** at least **two, so too,** the claim must be for at least **two** of **any** item, including coins of silver. Accordingly, **why do I** need the word **"silver" that the Merciful One wrote?** Rather, **if** it **is not** needed for the **matter of** the **claim, apply it** to the **matter of** the **denial,** and derive from it that the denial must be at least the value of two silver *ma'a*.

Rav's argument is as follows: if the Torah had only mentioned *keilim*, I would have derived *shtei kesef* on my own by analogy, knowing that monetary thresholds require a substantial amount. The additional word *kesef* must therefore be teaching something else, something not already derivable — namely, that the *denial* itself must reach the threshold of *shtei kesef*. *Im eino inyan le-ta'anah tnehu inyan le-kfira* — if it is not needed to teach the claim, apply it to the denial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר לָךְ: אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כֵּלִים״ וְלָא כְּתַב ״כֶּסֶף״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי כֵּלִים שְׁנַיִם, אַף כֹּל שְׁנַיִם – אֲבָל דָּבָר חָשׁוּב לָא בָּעֵינַן; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** could have **said to you** that **if the Merciful One had written** the word **"vessels" and** had **not written** the word **"silver" I would say** that **just as vessels are** at least **two, so too,** the claim must be for at least **two** of **any** item; **but we do not need** it to be **an item of substantial value.** Therefore, the word "silver" **teaches us** that it must be an item of substantial value, like silver.

Shmuel, for his part, maintains that without the word *kesef*, he might have known the quantity requirement but not the *davar chashuv* requirement for *keilim*. The word *kesef* is therefore necessary to teach that *keilim* must be significant objects. Both words together establish the claim threshold and the significance requirement for vessels.

The upshot is clear: the plain reading of the verses favors Shmuel, because the natural sense of *ki yiten ish el re'eihu kesef o keilim* is that this is what was entrusted — what was claimed — not what was denied. Rav's reading requires treating the word *kesef* as displaced from its plain meaning and redirected toward a different legal element. Rav himself concedes the Mishnah may read more like his position, but the verses read more like Shmuel's.

The subsequent discussion turns on how to read a series of Mishnaic cases — the case of claiming *shtei kesef* and the defendant admitting to a *perutah*, and the case of claiming *shtei kesef* plus a *perutah* with the defendant admitting only to a *perutah*. The *gemara* asks whether these cases involve disputes about monetary value or disputes about the physical coins themselves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא פְּרוּטָה״ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּחָסְרָה לָהּ טַעֲנָה – וּתְיוּבְתָּא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to prove Rav's opinion from the mishna. **We learned** in the mishna that if the claimant said to the defendant: **I have two silver** *ma'a* **in your possession,** and the latter responded: **You have only one** copper ***peruta* in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath. **What is the reason?** Is it **not because** the amount of the **claim** that was denied by the defendant **was lacking,** i.e., was less than, the minimum value of two *ma'a*? **And** accordingly, this is **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Shmuel.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ שְׁמוּאֵל: מִי סָבְרַתְּ שָׁוֶה קָתָנֵי?! דַּוְקָא קָתָנֵי – מַה שֶּׁטְּעָנוֹ לֹא הוֹדָה לוֹ, וּמַה שֶּׁהוֹדָה לוֹ לֹא טְעָנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel** could have **said to you: Do you maintain** that the case that **is taught** is one where the claim was for the **value** of two *ma'a*? The claim in the case that **is taught** was **specifically** for two silver *ma'a*. The defendant is exempt because the claim was for silver and he admitted to owing one *peruta* of copper; **that which he claimed from him, he did not admit to** at all, **and that which he admitted to, he had not claimed from him.**

Shmuel argues that the Mishnah is speaking of disputes about the coins as objects, not their value. Under this reading, when one says "you have two silver coins of mine," he means the actual coins, not their monetary equivalent. Thus when the defendant admits to only a *perutah* in the first case, it is not a *modeh bemiktzat* — what was claimed (silver coins) was not what was admitted (a copper penny). Rav, by contrast, holds that whenever monetary language is used without specification of the object itself, we are speaking of value, not physical coins.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף וּפְרוּטָה יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא פְּרוּטָה״ – חַיָּיב. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא שָׁוֶה – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי חַיָּיב; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דַּוְקָא – אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? מַה שֶּׁטְּעָנוֹ לֹא הוֹדָה לוֹ, וּמָה שֶׁהוֹדָה לוֹ לֹא טְעָנוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, say the latter clause:** If the claimant said: **I have two silver** *ma'a* **and one *peruta* in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only one *peruta* in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath. **Granted, if you say** that the claim was for the **value** of two *ma'a*, **due to that** reason he is **liable** in this case; unlike the previous case, here the defendant denied a debt of two full *ma'a*. **But if you say** the claim was **specifically** for two silver *ma'a* and one copper *peruta*, **why** is he **liable** to take an oath? In this case too, **that which he claimed from him, he did not admit to** at all, **and that which he admitted to, he had not claimed from him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא – אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל; הָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Isn't this discussion only according to Shmuel?** The proof was presented in order to attempt to refute the opinion of Shmuel, and **doesn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Shmuel says** that if one **claimed** that another owes **him** both **wheat and barley, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him one of them,** he is **liable** to take an oath? Here too, the claim was for two types of items, silver and copper, and the defendant admitted to owing one of the types, one *peruta* of copper. Therefore, according to Shmuel he is liable to take an oath.

A further case is raised: one claims a *litra zahav* — a pound of gold — and the defendant says he only received a *litra kesef* — a pound of silver. Here, because the word *litra* specifies a physical quantity of a substance, we are clearly talking about the object itself, not its monetary value. This is compatible with both positions. The subsequent case — where a gold *dinar* is claimed and a silver *dinar* admitted, with the ruling being *chayav* — is explained by Rabbi Elazar as a case where the claimant gave the coin for change (*b'shata'ano b'dinar matbeiot*). In that context, what matters is not the coin itself but the obligation to provide change, so the admission that one received a silver *dinar* worth of change is indeed a partial admission of the same claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דִּינַר זָהָב לִי בְּיָדֶךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא דִּינַר כֶּסֶף וּטְרֵיסִית וּפוּנְדְּיוֹן וּפְרוּטָה״ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהַכֹּל מִין מַטְבֵּעַ [אַחַת] הֵן. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא שָׁוֶה – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי חַיָּיב; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דַּוְקָא – אַמַּאי חַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the basis for Rav's explanation. **Know** that the mishna is referring to claims of monetary value, **as it teaches** in **the latter clause** that if the claimant said: **I have a gold dinar in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only a silver dinar, or a *tereisit*, or a *pundeyon*, or a *peruta* in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath, **as they are all** of **one type;** they are all **coins. Granted, if you say** that the claim was for the **value** of a gold dinar, it is **due to that** reason that he is **liable** to take an oath, as he admitted to a part of the claim. **But if you say** that the claim was **specifically** for a dinar of gold, **why** is he **liable?** The claim was for gold, and he admitted to owing silver or copper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּשֶׁטְּעָנוֹ בְּדִינָר מַטְבְּעוֹת, וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דִּפְרוּטָה בִּכְלַל מַטְבֵּעַ אִיתָא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: שֶׁהַכֹּל מִין מַטְבֵּעַ אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Rabbi Elazar says:** This mishna is referring **to** a case **where** one **claimed** that the defendant owes **him a dinar of coins,** i.e., coins worth a dinar. The claim was for specific coins, and not a monetary value. Since the claim concerned coins and not a specific weight of metal, and all coins are used for commercial transactions, all types of coins are considered of the same type. **And** it **teaches us that a *peruta* is included in** the category of **a coin.** According to this explanation, the language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches:** The defendant is liable to take an oath, **as** they are **all** of **one type [*min*];** they are all **coins.** In other words, even a *peruta* is a type of coin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב – שֶׁהַכֹּל דִּין מַטְבֵּעַ אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how would **Rav,** who holds that the claim in this case was for the value of a dinar, explain the fact that the mishna states: As they are all of one type; they are all coins? Why does it matter that they are all of one type? The Gemara answers that according to Rav, the reason the defendant is liable to take an oath is that **because** the claim is for the value of a dinar, **all** descriptions of monetary value based on different coins have **one** status under ***halakha* [*din*];** they are all **coins,** and it is of no significance that they are made of different materials.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – לֵימָא מִדְּסֵיפָא כִּשְׁמוּאֵל מְתָרֵץ, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי כִּשְׁמוּאֵל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** concerning the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, shall we say** that **since he explains the latter clause** in the mishna as referring to a specific item and not a value, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel,** then **also** with regard to **the former clause,** that states the claim must be for two silver *ma'a*, **he** apparently **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel,** i.e., that it is referring to two actual *ma'a*, and not the value of two *ma'a*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; סֵיפָא דַּוְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי ״שֶׁהַכֹּל מִין מַטְבֵּעַ אַחַת״; וְרֵישָׁא – כְּרַב אוֹ כִּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** Rabbi Elazar explains that **the latter clause** of the mishna is referring **specifically** to actual items in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel, **as it teaches** explicitly: **As** they are **all** of **one type;** they are all **coins. But the former clause** can be interpreted either **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav or in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel.**

According to Tosafot's reading, the case of *dinar zahov zahov* — where the claimant specifies that the coin is made of pure gold — establishes that only when one explicitly emphasizes the physical nature of the object are we dealing with the object as such. The standard formulation *dinar zahov*, absent such emphasis, refers to value, not the coin. This, says Rav Ashi, is how Shmuel would read it — *kol ha-omer dinar zahov ke-omer dinar zahov zahov dami*, treating every mention of a gold *dinar* as implicitly referring to the object. But this is acknowledged to be a forced reading. If every mention of a monetary sum were taken as a reference to the physical currency rather than the value, disputes about money would routinely become disputes about objects, releasing defendants from *shevua* far too easily. The simpler reading remains Rav's: monetary language denotes value, and the reason the defendant is *patur* in the Mishnah's first case is simply that the denial did not reach *shtei kesef*.

## Rabbi Chiyya Braita

### Sela Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְסַיּוֹעֵיהּ לְרַב: ״סֶלַע לִי בְּיָדֶךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא סֶלַע חָסֵר שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף״ – חַיָּיב, ״חָסֵר מָעָה״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** a *baraita* **in support of** the opinion of **Rav:** If the claimant said: **I have a *sela* in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only a *sela* minus two silver** *ma'a* **in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath. If the defendant responded: I owe you only a *sela* **minus one** silver ***ma'a*,** he is **exempt.** Clearly, the reason is that he denied less than two *ma'a* of the claim, in accordance with the opinion of Rav.

Since the Mishnah already reads more naturally like Rav, a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya explicitly supports him. *Sela li beyadecha* — you have a *sela* of mine, which is worth six *kesef*. The defendant says: *ein lecha beyadi ella sela chasar bet kesef* — you only gave me four *kesef*, a *sela* less two. In this case he is *chayav*, because the denial is two *kesef*, which satisfies Rav's threshold. But if the defendant says he only owes one *kesef* — *chasar ma'ah* — he is *patur*, because the denial is only one *kesef*, below the required two. This is a clear and direct statement: it is the denial, not the claim, that must be at least *shtei kesef*, just as Rav maintains. All the evidence points toward Rav's position, though the *gemara* does not formally resolve the dispute in favor of either side.

## Modeh vs. Eid Echad

### Shmuel's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּטַעֲנַת מַלְוֶה וְהוֹדָאַת לֹוֶה; אֲבָל טַעֲנַת מַלְוֶה וְהַעֲדָאַת עֵד אֶחָד, אֲפִילּוּ לֹא טְעָנוֹ אֶלָּא בִּפְרוּטָה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** that **Shmuel says:** The requirement that the claim be at least the value of two silver *ma'a* to render the defendant liable to take an oath was **taught only with regard to** a case where the oath is due to the **claim of** the **creditor and** the partial **admission of** the **debtor. But** in a case where the defendant's liability to take an oath is due to the **claim of** the **creditor and** the **testimony of one witness** in support of the claim, **even** if the claimant **claimed from him only one *peruta*,** the defendant is **liable** to take an oath.

The *gemara* moves to a new point. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak says in the name of Shmuel: *lo shanu ella be-ta'anat milveh ve-hoda'at loveh* — the requirement of *shtei kesef* applies only to the case of *modeh bemiktzat*, where there is a lender's claim and the borrower's partial admission. But *aval ta'anat milveh ve-ha'adat ed echad* — if instead of a partial admission there is a single witness corroborating the claim, then *afilu lo ta'ano ella be-frutah chayav* — even if the entire claim is only a *perutah*, the defendant takes a *shevua*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ לְכׇל עָוֹן וּלְכׇל חַטָּאת״ – לְכׇל עָוֹן וּלְכׇל חַטָּאת הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ קָם, אֲבָל קָם הוּא לִשְׁבוּעָה. וְתַנְיָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשְּׁנַיִם מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן, עֵד אֶחָד מְחַיְּיבוֹ שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this distinction? **As it is written: "One witness shall not rise up against a man for any iniquity, or for any sin"** (Deuteronomy 19:15). It is inferred from here that **it is for any iniquity or for any sin that he may not rise up,** as the testimony of one witness is not enough for this purpose, **but he may rise up to** render one liable to take **an oath. And it is taught** in a *baraita*: In **any place where two** witnesses **render** a defendant **liable** to pay **money,** the testimony of **one witness renders him liable** to take **an oath.** Therefore, since two witnesses render the defendant liable to pay if the claim is worth at least one *peruta*, so too, one witness renders him liable to take an oath with regard to a claim of this value.

Tosafot observes that this ruling tracks neatly with Shmuel's general position. Shmuel had already said that in *modeh bemiktzat*, as long as the initial claim was large enough, one takes an oath even if the amount actually denied is only a penny. That principle — that a *shevua* can attach to a very small disputed amount — naturally extends to *eid echad*: once a single witness corroborates the claim, an oath is triggered regardless of how small the sum. Rav, by contrast, required the denial itself to be *shtei kesef* in *modeh bemiktzat*, and Tosafot suggests he might similarly require a *shtei kesef* denial threshold in the *eid echad* case as well.

### Rav's Eid Echad View

This practical consequence matters for *halacha*. Tosafot rules directly: *leit hilchata ke'vateih de'Shmuel be'ha de'hacha* — we do not rule like Shmuel that *eid echad* triggers a *shevua* even on a *perutah*. Since this ruling about *eid echad* is a corollary of Shmuel's position in the earlier *modeh bemiktzat* dispute, and we rule like Rav in that dispute, it follows that we rule like Rav here as well. Tosafot cites Rabbi Chiyya's *baraita* and Rav Pappa's subsequent discussion as further grounds for ruling like Rav. Accordingly, just as *modeh bemiktzat* requires a denial of *shtei kesef*, so too *eid echad* may require that the amount in dispute reaches that same threshold before a *shevua* is imposed.

### Eid Echad Basis

What is the textual basis for the rule that a single witness can obligate a *shevua* at all? The verse states *lo yakum ed echad be'ish le'chol avon u'le'chol chatat* — one witness shall not stand against a man for any transgression or sin. *Le'chol avon u'le'chol chatat hu de'eino kam* — it is for the purpose of compelling monetary payment that one witness is insufficient. *Aval kam hu li'shvuah* — but one witness can obligate a *shevua*. A *braita* formalizes this: *kol makom she'shnayim mechayvin oto mamon, ed echad mechayvo shvuah* — wherever two witnesses would obligate payment, one witness obligates an oath. This is the principle underlying Shmuel's ruling, and it is why Shmuel extends it even to a claim of a single *perutah*.

## Unified Claim Problem

### Shmuel on Multiple Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין, וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** If one **claimed** that another owed **him wheat and barley, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him one of** these types, he is **liable** to take an oath, as he admitted to part of a claim.

The *gemara* introduces a new teaching: *Amar Rav Nachman amar Shmuel: ta'ano chitin u'se'orin v'hoda lo be'echad me'hen chayav* — if one claims that another owes him both wheat and barley, and the defendant admits to one of them while denying the other, he is *chayav*. Even though wheat and barley are two different *minim*, the claim is viewed as a unified *ta'anah*, and admitting to one of the two items constitutes *modeh bemiktzat* of the whole claim.

### Support for Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יִישַׁר, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִישְׁהָא הֲוָה שָׁהֵי לֵיהּ וְשָׁתֵיק לֵיהּ, אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: מִישְׁתָּא הֲוָה שָׁתֵי לֵיהּ וּשְׁתֵיק לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said to him:** You have spoken **well, and so** also **said Rabbi Yoḥanan.** The Gemara asks: **By inference,** does this mean **that Reish Lakish disagrees with** Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to this matter, since Rabbi Yitzḥak mentioned that Rabbi Yoḥanan agreed, and did not reference Reish Lakish, who often engages in disputes with Rabbi Yoḥanan? The Gemara answers: There is no record of Reish Lakish expressing his opinion at that time. **There are** those **who say** that this is because **he was tarrying and remaining silent,** waiting for Rabbi Yoḥanan to finish his statement, and Rabbi Yitzḥak did not find out whether or not he subsequently disagreed with him, and **there are** those **who say** that Reish Lakish **was drinking** at the time, **and he** therefore **remained silent** and did not respond to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement.

Rabbi Yitzchak responded with *yasher koach* — well said — and Rabbi Yochanan likewise stated the same principle. The *gemara* notes that since Rabbi Yitzchak acknowledged Rabbi Yochanan's agreement but did not mention Reish Lakish, one might infer that Reish Lakish disagreed. The *gemara* deflects this: according to some, Reish Lakish was still waiting to hear the statement concluded before chiming in; according to others, he happened to be in the middle of drinking and simply did not interject. Either way, no clear *masoret* emerged from Reish Lakish, which is why his position remained unknown to Rabbi Yitzchak.

### Wheat vs. Barley Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִין – פָּטוּר. וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מְחַיֵּיב. טַעְמָא דִּטְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִין, הָא חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** that the wording of the mishna **supports** Shmuel's opinion: If one **claimed** that another owes **him wheat, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him barley,** he is **exempt; and Rabban Gamliel deems** him **liable** to take an oath. The Gemara infers: **The reason** he is exempt according to the first *tanna* **is that he claimed** that he owes **him wheat and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him barley; but** if the claim was for both **wheat and barley, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him one of them,** it can be inferred he is **liable** to take an oath.

The *gemara* attempts to bring support from a Mishnah: *ta'ano chitin v'hoda lo bi'se'orin patur, v'Rabban Gamliel mechayev* — if one claims wheat and the defendant admits only to barley, he is *patur*; Rabban Gamliel says he is *chayav*. The *gemara* reasons: the only reason the *chachamim* rule *patur* is because the parties were talking entirely past one another, the claimant demanding wheat while the admission was barley. But if the original claim had been both wheat and barley — *chitin u'se'orin* — and the defendant admitted to one, surely he would be *chayav*, supporting Shmuel's teaching.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין נָמֵי פָּטוּר; וְהַאי דְּקָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּחִטִּין – לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** it is possible that **the same is true,** i.e., **that even** if the claim was for both **wheat and barley** the defendant is **exempt. And** the fact **that** the mishna states **that** the first *tanna* and Rabban Gamliel **disagree with regard to** a case where the claim was specifically for **wheat** and the defendant admitted to owing him barley is in order **to convey to you the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel,** that the defendant is liable to take an oath even if his admission is not at all of the same type as the claim.

The *gemara* rejects this inference. The *patur* ruling in the case of wheat versus barley could equally apply even if the original claim was *chitin u'se'orin* and only one was admitted. The Mishnah may have given the starker case — full cross-talk between wheat and barley — *le'hodiecha kocho de'Rabban Gamliel*, to reveal the remarkable extent of Rabban Gamliel's position: even where the parties seem to be speaking of entirely different things, Rabban Gamliel considers it *chayav*.

### Vessels & Land Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: טְעָנוֹ כֵּלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת; הוֹדָה בַּכֵּלִים וְכָפַר בַּקַּרְקָעוֹת, בְּקַרְקָעוֹת וְכָפַר בַּכֵּלִים – פָּטוּר. הוֹדָה בְּמִקְצָת קַרְקָעוֹת – פָּטוּר. בְּמִקְצָת כֵּלִים – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from the mishna: If one **claimed** that another owes **him vessels and land,** and the defendant **admitted to** owing him **vessels but denied** the claim of **land,** or conversely, if he admitted **to** owing him **land but denied** the claim of **vessels,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath. If **he admitted to part of** the claim about the **land,** he is **exempt.** If he admitted **to part** of the claim about the **vessels,** he is **liable** to take an oath.

A further challenge arrives: *ta'ano keilim v'karka'ot, hoda bi'keilim v'chafar bi'karka'ot, bi'karka'ot v'chafar bi'keilim patur* — if one claims both vessels and land, and the defendant admits to the vessels while denying the land, or admits to the land while denying the vessels, he is *patur*. The *gemara* proceeds to the case on 40b: *hoda be'miktzat karka patur* — if the admission consists entirely of some of the land, he is *patur*, since *karka* does not obligate a *shevua*. But *hoda be'miktzat keilim chayav* — if he admitted to some of the vessels while denying others, he is *chayav*, and the *shevua* on the vessels draws in the land as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּכֵלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת – דְּקַרְקַע לָאו בַּת שְׁבוּעָה הִיא; הָא כֵּלִים וְכֵלִים דּוּמְיָא דְּכֵלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** he is exempt in the first cases is **that** the claim was for **vessels and land, as** a claim with regard to **land is not subject to an oath; but** if the claim was for **vessels** of one type **and vessels** of another type, or for wheat and barley, **similar to** the case of a claim for **vessels and land** in that the defendant admitted to owing one type and denied owing the other type, he is **liable** to take an oath.

The *gemara* notes that the *diyuk* here seems strong: the reason for *patur* in the earlier cases involving land must be precisely that *karka lav bar shvua hi* — land is not subject to oath. If the only reason for *patur* is the involvement of land, then in a case of two types of vessels — analogous to *chitin u'se'orin* — admitting to one while denying the other should be *chayav*. This would directly support Shmuel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ כֵּלִים וְכֵלִים נָמֵי פָּטוּר; וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי כֵּלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּכִי הוֹדָה בְּמִקְצָת כֵּלִים, חַיָּיב אַף עַל הַקַּרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the inference: No, it is possible that **the same is true,** i.e., **that even** if the claim was for **vessels** of one type **and vessels** of another type, and the defendant admitted to owing one type and denied owing the other type, he is **exempt. And** the reason **that** the mishna **teaches** specifically the case where the claim is for **vessels and land** is that this **teaches us that** in a case **where** the defendant **admitted to a part of** the claim with regard to the **vessels,** he is **liable** to take an oath **concerning the land as well.**

The *gemara* resists the inference. The *patur* in the vessel-and-land cases could equally apply even in a case of two types of vessels, because the parties are addressing two distinct *minim*. The reason *karka* is specifically mentioned is not to identify the unique exemption for land, but to teach the *chiddush* at the end: when one is obligated by a *shevua* on the vessels, that *shevua* extends to cover the land as well. The *gemara* then asks whether this principle is not already derived from the Mishnah *zokkim et ha-nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut lishava aleihem* — that *metaltelin* on which an oath is taken drag in real estate. The *gemara* answers: *hai ikar hi agav grara nasveih* — here is the primary source for that ruling; what is stated in Kiddushin in the context of *kinyanim* is incidental. The *gemara* concedes that the *pshat* reading of the vessel-and-land case does seem to say one would be *chayav*, but presses the point that the *patur* there can be explained on grounds independent of the two-*minim* theory.

## Chitin Debate Resolution

### Unified Claims Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין, וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן – פָּטוּר. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יִישַׁר, וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן! אָמוֹרָאֵי נִינְהוּ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** disagrees with Shmuel and **says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **claimed** that another owes **him** both **wheat and barley, and** the latter **admitted to** owing **him one of them,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rabbi Yitzḥak say** to Rav Naḥman: You have spoken **well** in the name of Shmuel, i.e., in saying that the defendant is liable to take an oath in the aforementioned case; **and so** also **said Rabbi Yoḥanan?** If so, Rabbi Yoḥanan agrees with Shmuel, and not with Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba. The Gemara answers: **They are *amora'im*,** and they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

The *gemara* now directly confronts the competing versions of Rabbi Yochanan's position. One transmission, quoted by Rav Nachman in Shmuel's name, had Rabbi Yochanan agreeing that *chitin u'se'orin* with admission to one is *chayav*. But Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba reports in Rabbi Yochanan's name: *ta'ano chitin u'se'orin v'hoda lo be'echad me'hen patur* — you are *patur*.

The *gemara* acknowledges the contradiction and states: *amora'ei ninhu aliba de'Rebbe Yochanan* — it is a dispute among *Amoraim* about what Rabbi Yochanan actually held. The transmission was unclear, perhaps because Reish Lakish was quietly listening and others were not present to hear the definitive statement.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: טְעָנוֹ שׁוֹר וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּשֶׂה, שֶׂה וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּשׁוֹר – פָּטוּר. טְעָנוֹ בְּשׁוֹר וָשֶׂה וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** from a *baraita*: If one **claimed** that another owes **him an ox, and** the latter **admitted to** owing **him a sheep,** or conversely, if the claim was for **a sheep and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him an ox,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath. If one **claimed** that another owes **him an ox and a sheep, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him one of them,** he is **liable** to take an oath.

Rava presses harder with a cleaner source: *tanu shor v'hoda lo b'seh, seh v'hoda lo b'shor patur* — if you are claimed to have an ox and you admit only to the sheep, or vice versa, you are *patur*, because you are speaking of entirely different objects. But *tanu b'shor v'seh v'hoda lo b'echad mehem chayav* — if the original claim was for both an ox and a sheep, and you admit to one, you are *chayav*. This is the *chitin u'se'orin* scenario stated with perfect clarity, and it says *chayav*.

### Admon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא מַנִּי – רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא. אִי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אֲפִילּוּ רֵישָׁא נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him** in response: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel,** who deems the defendant liable to take an oath even if his admission was not of the same type of item as the claim. Rabbi Abba bar Memel responded: **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel,** the defendant should be liable to take an oath **even** in **the first clause** of the *baraita*, where the claim is for an ox and the admission is with regard to a sheep.

Rava anticipates the response: *ha mani Rabban Gamliel hi* — that is Rabban Gamliel's position. But the *gemara* immediately pushes back: if it were Rabban Gamliel, then even the first case of ox versus sheep should be *chayav*, since Rabban Gamliel rules *chayav* even when the parties are talking past one another. So it cannot be Rabban Gamliel. *Ela ha mani Admon hi* — this must be the position of Admon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא מַנִּי – אַדְמוֹן הִיא; וְלָא דַּחוֹיֵי מְדַחֵינָא לָךְ, אֶלָּא תַּלְמוּד עָרוּךְ הוּא בְּפִיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָא מַנִּי – אַדְמוֹן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba explained: **Rather,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Admon,** who holds that the admission must be of the same type as the claim, and maintains nevertheless that in a case where one claims that another owes him jugs of oil, and the latter admits that he owes him jugs, but not the oil, the defendant is liable to take an oath (see 38b). **And I am not dismissing your** objection insubstantially; **rather, it is a set tradition in the mouth of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who would say: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Admon.**

Here the *gemara* invokes the case of Admon from elsewhere: if one claims jars of olive oil and the defendant admits to having the jars but denies the oil, Admon rules *chayav*. Implicitly, that is exactly the *chitin u'se'orin* scenario — the claimant demands oil *and* the jars, the defendant admits only to the jars. Admon holds that admitting to one item within a unified claim is *modeh bemiktzat* of the whole. So whatever the *chachamim* hold in that dispute, Admon clearly rules *chayav* in the *chitin u'se'orin* type of case.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא דִּיחוֹדֵי מְדַחֵינָא לָךְ, אֶלָּא תַּלְמוּד עָרוּךְ הוּא בְּפִיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן ״אַדְמוֹן״ – הַחִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין עַל כַּחַד מֵהֶן הוּא דְּאַדְמוֹן חַיַּיב, אֲבָל הַחִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין עַל הַחִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין כִּדְרַבָּנַן, הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יִישַׁר.
>
> **Translation:** [The continuation resolves the dispute through careful analysis of positions] **I am not dismissing your** objection. Rather, this is an established teaching from **Rabbi Yoḥanan:** When he applies Admon's ruling, he applies it specifically to the case where admission is to one item out of a unified claim. By contrast, when the claim was for wheat and barley as a unified claim and the defendant admits to one of them — **this follows** the opinion of the *chachamim*, **and that is what Rabbi Yitzḥak meant when he said: "Well said."**

Rava concludes: The version that says *chayav* in the *chitin u'se'orin* case reflects Admon's position, which Rabbi Yochanan was perhaps citing or explaining rather than endorsing as normative. The version that says *patur* reflects Rabbi Yochanan's own ruling, following the *chachamim*. Rava establishes this as the definitive and settled teaching, and the daf concludes here.
