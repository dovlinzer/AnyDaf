# Shevuot 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## False Oath Impact

### Shevua vs. Other Sins

Today's daf is formally *daf mem*, though we are picking up from the bottom of 39a, still unpacking the *baraita* that addresses the administration of an oath and the weight of a false one. That *baraita* echoes the warnings given to witnesses before testimony — how people's lives hang in the balance — except here the focus is how a false *shevua* can be destructive not only to the person who swears it, but to his family and the entire world.

The *gemara* picks up five lines from the bottom of the page: *v'chol aveirot shebatorah nifra'im mimenu* — for other sins, only the person himself is punished. But here, *v'kan mimenu umikol ha'olam kulo* — in the case of a false *shevua*, the entire world suffers.

### Collective Responsibility

This immediately raises an objection. The *gemara* asks: *v'chol aveirot shebatorah mikol ha'olam lo?* Are we saying that the rest of the world is entirely unaffected by other sins? But the verse states *vechi kashlu ish be'achiv* — a man will stumble through his brother's sin — and *melamed shekol Yisrael arevim zeh lazeh*, teaching that all of Israel are guarantors for one another. This suggests the world does suffer for others' sins!

The *gemara* resolves this: *hasam she'yesh b'yadam limchot v'lo michu* — that principle applies only where bystanders could have protested and failed to do so. They are passive enablers, and that degree of complicity accounts for their culpability. In the case of a false *shevua*, however, the metaphysical consequence extends even to those who bear no responsibility whatsoever.

### Punishment Gradations

The *gemara* further refines the distinctions. It asks: *mai ika bein rish'ei d'mishpacha l'rish'ei d'alma, bein tzadikei d'mishpacha l'tzadikei d'alma* — what is the difference between a *rasha* of the family and a *rasha* of the wider world, or between the *tzadikim* of the family and those of the world at large?

For ordinary sins, the perpetrator is punished with the full weight of his transgression — *b'sha'ar aveirot b'dinei*. Family members who enabled him, whether actively or passively, receive a weightier punishment — *v'rasha d'mishpacha b'din chamur*. Bystanders in the broader world who were passive enablers receive a lighter punishment — *v'rasha d'alma b'din hakal*. Crucially, those who are completely blameless, the *tzadikim*, are entirely exempt — *tzadikei d'hacha v'hacha peturei*.

This framework reveals two layers of distinction. First, culpability is required: one must be a *rasha* in some sense — either the perpetrator, an active enabler, or a passive one — before the punishment applies. The label *rasha* here is pointed: the person who simply did not protest is designated a *rasha* for that failure to act. Second, even among the culpable, punishment is graduated according to proximity to the wrong.

In the case of a *shevua*, the structure shifts. The perpetrator and his family enablers are punished with equal severity: *hu vchol mishpachto kidinei*. The broader world's passive enablers receive a weighty though lesser punishment: *rish'ei d'alma b'din chamur*.

### Universal Impact

Here is the major *chiddush*: *vetzadikei d'hacha d'hacha b'din kal* — even the entirely blameless, both of the family and of the world at large, receive some form of light punishment. This is the fundamental difference between a *shevua* and all other sins. For other transgressions, the *tzadikim* are exempt. For a false *shevua*, no one escapes entirely. The suffering of the world is not a function of guilt — it is a metaphysical reality set in motion by the oath itself. The *tzadikim* of the family may seem harder to envision, but there are always exceptions — infants, for instance, who could not possibly have been complicit.

## Oath Administration

### Refusal & Joint Liability

The *baraita* continues: *im amar eini nishba potrin oto miyad* — if he says he will not swear, he is released immediately. But if he says *harei ani nishba* — he intends to go ahead with the oath — those present invoke the verse *suru na me'al ohalei ha'anashim harishaim ha'eileh*, calling on all to distance themselves from these wicked men. This designates both the one taking the oath and the one imposing it as a *rasha*.

The *gemara* asks: *bishlama hu deka mishtava kai be'issura* — understandably the one taking a false oath transgresses. But why is the one imposing the oath also called a *rasha*? The answer comes from a *braita* of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *shevuat Hashem tihyeh bein sheneihem* — the oath of God rests upon both of them. Both parties are, in some sense, participants in the act of swearing.

Rashi explains that both are punished because the one who imposed the oath failed to ensure proper documentary evidence or adequate witnesses, thereby allowing the situation to arrive at a *chillul Hashem*. This reasoning, though, sits uneasily in certain cases. In a situation involving *eid echad*, for instance, the claimant cannot always arrange for additional witnesses — a car accident has whatever witnesses it has. So the *gemara* may be gesturing at something more metaphysical: you set this in motion, and a false *shevua* resulted. Perhaps you could have waived your claim. The discussion here is subtly different from the earlier analysis of family and world — it is not simply about fault and guilt but about the raw reality of the *shevua* and who brought it about.

### Oath Language Standardiz…

The *baraita* continues: *uche'shemashbi'in oto omrim lo hevei yodeia shelo al da'atecha* — when administering the oath, the court informs the oath-taker that the oath is not interpreted according to his own understanding, but according to theirs.

### Hollowed Staff Case

The *gemara* asks: *lama li lemeimar lei hachi?* Why must the court say this? *Mishum kanya deRava* — because of the incident involving Rava's staff. A man who owed money hollowed out his staff, filled it with the coins he owed, and handed it to his creditor, asking him to hold it. He then swore that he had returned the money — technically true, since the creditor was at that moment holding the coins inside the staff. The debtor was thus playing semantic games, assigning private meaning to his oath. To prevent such manipulation, the court declares that the oath means only what they intend it to mean, not what the oath-taker privately has in mind.

This concludes the *baraita's* treatment of the weight and administration of a false *shevua* — the gravity of swearing falsely, the framing designed to deter the oath-taker, and the safeguard ensuring the oath means what the court says it means.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Mishnah Ambiguity

The *gemara* now shifts to a more technical *halachic* discussion. The Mishnah states that *modeh bemiktzat* requires a *ta'anah* of *shtei kesef* — two *ma'ah*, which we will call twenty dollars for convenience — and an admission of at least a *perutah*, a penny. The question is an ambiguity in the Mishnah itself: does the requirement of *shtei kesef* apply to the *claim*, or to the *denial*?

### Rav's Position

*Amar Rav kefirat ta'ana shtei kesef* — Rav says the denial must be *shtei kesef*. It is not enough that twenty dollars was claimed; the amount actually denied must be at least twenty dollars.

### Shmuel's Position

*uShmuel amar ta'ana atzma shtei kesef* — Shmuel says the claim itself must be twenty dollars, but even if you admitted to nineteen dollars and ninety-nine cents and only denied a single penny, you are still *chayav*. Conversely, even if you admitted only to a penny and denied nineteen dollars and ninety-nine cents, you are still *chayav*. Once the original claim was large enough, the *shevua* attaches.

Rav himself acknowledges an internal tension: *dika matnitan kevasi d'Rav, vekrah kevasi de'Shmuel* — the Mishnah reads more naturally like Rav's position, but the *pesukim* read more naturally like Shmuel's.

The Mishnah's formulation supports Rav because it specifies a *ta'ana* of *shtei kesef* and an admission of a *perutah*, but never explicitly mentions the case where only a *perutah* is denied — which would be Shmuel's sharpest *chiddush*. Had Shmuel been right, the Mishnah should have highlighted the remarkable ruling that one takes an oath even when denying only a penny, yet that formulation is absent. The same observation applies to a parallel Mishnah in Bava Metzia.

### Kesef O Keilim

The *pesukim*, however, read more naturally like Shmuel. The verse in the section dealing with *shomrim* states *ki yiten ish l're'eihu kesef o keilim lishmor* — if a man gives his fellow silver or vessels to watch. The *gemara* learns from the plural *keilim* that *kesef* must also be plural — at minimum two — and the question becomes: two what? The tradition establishes it means two *ma'ah*, which we are calling twenty dollars.

The verse continues with an additional derivation: *ma kesef davar chashuv, af keilim davar chashuv* — just as silver must be significant, so too *keilim* must be significant. Here Tosafot's reading is preferable to Rashi's: *keilim* must be a *davar chashuv*, meaning each vessel must be worth at least a *perutah*, even if the pair together does not reach the monetary threshold of *shtei kesef*. A *kli* carries intrinsic significance beyond its market value — one needle can sew an entire garment — and so two needles worth a *perutah* each would be sufficient to trigger *modeh bemiktzat*, even though their combined value is far below twenty dollars. When dealing with *keilim*, you do not need the *shtei kesef* threshold at all.

The verse then says *ki hu zeh* — which the *gemara* uses to establish the basic requirement of *hoda'ah bemiktzat ha-ta'anah*, partial admission within the claim, and also the requirement that the admission be *mimin ha-ta'anah*, of the same genre as what was claimed.

Shmuel reads the verse as a whole to indicate that the twenty dollars is what was claimed, not what was denied. The structure of the verse — *kesef o keilim* being what the person gave — points naturally to the claim. When the *gemara* presses that this seems to show the claim was *shtei kesef*, implicitly confirming Shmuel, Rav responds: *kesef ki asa me-ikara le-kfira hu de-asa* — the word *kesef* comes primarily to address the denial, not the claim.

Rav's argument is as follows: if the Torah had only mentioned *keilim*, I would have derived *shtei kesef* on my own by analogy, knowing that monetary thresholds require a substantial amount. The additional word *kesef* must therefore be teaching something else, something not already derivable — namely, that the *denial* itself must reach the threshold of *shtei kesef*. *Im eino inyan le-ta'anah tnehu inyan le-kfira* — if it is not needed to teach the claim, apply it to the denial.

Shmuel, for his part, maintains that without the word *kesef*, he might have known the quantity requirement but not the *davar chashuv* requirement for *keilim*. The word *kesef* is therefore necessary to teach that *keilim* must be significant objects. Both words together establish the claim threshold and the significance requirement for vessels.

The upshot is clear: the plain reading of the verses favors Shmuel, because the natural sense of *ki yiten ish el re'eihu kesef o keilim* is that this is what was entrusted — what was claimed — not what was denied. Rav's reading requires treating the word *kesef* as displaced from its plain meaning and redirected toward a different legal element. Rav himself concedes the Mishnah may read more like his position, but the verses read more like Shmuel's.

The subsequent discussion turns on how to read a series of Mishnaic cases — the case of claiming *shtei kesef* and the defendant admitting to a *perutah*, and the case of claiming *shtei kesef* plus a *perutah* with the defendant admitting only to a *perutah*. The *gemara* asks whether these cases involve disputes about monetary value or disputes about the physical coins themselves.

Shmuel argues that the Mishnah is speaking of disputes about the coins as objects, not their value. Under this reading, when one says "you have two silver coins of mine," he means the actual coins, not their monetary equivalent. Thus when the defendant admits to only a *perutah* in the first case, it is not a *modeh bemiktzat* — what was claimed (silver coins) was not what was admitted (a copper penny). Rav, by contrast, holds that whenever monetary language is used without specification of the object itself, we are speaking of value, not physical coins.

A further case is raised: one claims a *litra zahav* — a pound of gold — and the defendant says he only received a *litra kesef* — a pound of silver. Here, because the word *litra* specifies a physical quantity of a substance, we are clearly talking about the object itself, not its monetary value. This is compatible with both positions. The subsequent case — where a gold *dinar* is claimed and a silver *dinar* admitted, with the ruling being *chayav* — is explained by Rabbi Elazar as a case where the claimant gave the coin for change (*b'shata'ano b'dinar matbeiot*). In that context, what matters is not the coin itself but the obligation to provide change, so the admission that one received a silver *dinar* worth of change is indeed a partial admission of the same claim.

According to Tosafot's reading, the case of *dinar zahov zahov* — where the claimant specifies that the coin is made of pure gold — establishes that only when one explicitly emphasizes the physical nature of the object are we dealing with the object as such. The standard formulation *dinar zahov*, absent such emphasis, refers to value, not the coin. This, says Rav Ashi, is how Shmuel would read it — *kol ha-omer dinar zahov ke-omer dinar zahov zahov dami*, treating every mention of a gold *dinar* as implicitly referring to the object. But this is acknowledged to be a forced reading. If every mention of a monetary sum were taken as a reference to the physical currency rather than the value, disputes about money would routinely become disputes about objects, releasing defendants from *shevua* far too easily. The simpler reading remains Rav's: monetary language denotes value, and the reason the defendant is *patur* in the Mishnah's first case is simply that the denial did not reach *shtei kesef*.

## Rabbi Chiyya Braita

### Sela Case

Since the Mishnah already reads more naturally like Rav, a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya explicitly supports him. *Sela li beyadecha* — you have a *sela* of mine, which is worth six *kesef*. The defendant says: *ein lecha beyadi ella sela chasar bet kesef* — you only gave me four *kesef*, a *sela* less two. In this case he is *chayav*, because the denial is two *kesef*, which satisfies Rav's threshold. But if the defendant says he only owes one *kesef* — *chasar ma'ah* — he is *patur*, because the denial is only one *kesef*, below the required two. This is a clear and direct statement: it is the denial, not the claim, that must be at least *shtei kesef*, just as Rav maintains. All the evidence points toward Rav's position, though the *gemara* does not formally resolve the dispute in favor of either side.

## Modeh vs. Eid Echad

### Shmuel's Teaching

The *gemara* moves to a new point. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak says in the name of Shmuel: *lo shanu ella be-ta'anat milveh ve-hoda'at loveh* — the requirement of *shtei kesef* applies only to the case of *modeh bemiktzat*, where there is a lender's claim and the borrower's partial admission. But *aval ta'anat milveh ve-ha'adat ed echad* — if instead of a partial admission there is a single witness corroborating the claim, then *afilu lo ta'ano ella be-frutah chayav* — even if the entire claim is only a *perutah*, the defendant takes a *shevua*.

Tosafot observes that this ruling tracks neatly with Shmuel's general position. Shmuel had already said that in *modeh bemiktzat*, as long as the initial claim was large enough, one takes an oath even if the amount actually denied is only a penny. That principle — that a *shevua* can attach to a very small disputed amount — naturally extends to *eid echad*: once a single witness corroborates the claim, an oath is triggered regardless of how small the sum. Rav, by contrast, required the denial itself to be *shtei kesef* in *modeh bemiktzat*, and Tosafot suggests he might similarly require a *shtei kesef* denial threshold in the *eid echad* case as well.

### Rav's Eid Echad View

This practical consequence matters for *halacha*. Tosafot rules directly: *leit hilchata ke'vateih de'Shmuel be'ha de'hacha* — we do not rule like Shmuel that *eid echad* triggers a *shevua* even on a *perutah*. Since this ruling about *eid echad* is a corollary of Shmuel's position in the earlier *modeh bemiktzat* dispute, and we rule like Rav in that dispute, it follows that we rule like Rav here as well. Tosafot cites Rabbi Chiyya's *baraita* and Rav Pappa's subsequent discussion as further grounds for ruling like Rav. Accordingly, just as *modeh bemiktzat* requires a denial of *shtei kesef*, so too *eid echad* may require that the amount in dispute reaches that same threshold before a *shevua* is imposed.

### Eid Echad Basis

What is the textual basis for the rule that a single witness can obligate a *shevua* at all? The verse states *lo yakum ed echad be'ish le'chol avon u'le'chol chatat* — one witness shall not stand against a man for any transgression or sin. *Le'chol avon u'le'chol chatat hu de'eino kam* — it is for the purpose of compelling monetary payment that one witness is insufficient. *Aval kam hu li'shvuah* — but one witness can obligate a *shevua*. A *braita* formalizes this: *kol makom she'shnayim mechayvin oto mamon, ed echad mechayvo shvuah* — wherever two witnesses would obligate payment, one witness obligates an oath. This is the principle underlying Shmuel's ruling, and it is why Shmuel extends it even to a claim of a single *perutah*.

## Unified Claim Problem

### Shmuel on Multiple Items

The *gemara* introduces a new teaching: *Amar Rav Nachman amar Shmuel: ta'ano chitin u'se'orin v'hoda lo be'echad me'hen chayav* — if one claims that another owes him both wheat and barley, and the defendant admits to one of them while denying the other, he is *chayav*. Even though wheat and barley are two different *minim*, the claim is viewed as a unified *ta'anah*, and admitting to one of the two items constitutes *modeh bemiktzat* of the whole claim.

### Support for Shmuel

Rabbi Yitzchak responded with *yasher koach* — well said — and Rabbi Yochanan likewise stated the same principle. The *gemara* notes that since Rabbi Yitzchak acknowledged Rabbi Yochanan's agreement but did not mention Reish Lakish, one might infer that Reish Lakish disagreed. The *gemara* deflects this: according to some, Reish Lakish was still waiting to hear the statement concluded before chiming in; according to others, he happened to be in the middle of drinking and simply did not interject. Either way, no clear *masoret* emerged from Reish Lakish, which is why his position remained unknown to Rabbi Yitzchak.

### Wheat vs. Barley Alone

The *gemara* attempts to bring support from a Mishnah: *ta'ano chitin v'hoda lo bi'se'orin patur, v'Rabban Gamliel mechayev* — if one claims wheat and the defendant admits only to barley, he is *patur*; Rabban Gamliel says he is *chayav*. The *gemara* reasons: the only reason the *chachamim* rule *patur* is because the parties were talking entirely past one another, the claimant demanding wheat while the admission was barley. But if the original claim had been both wheat and barley — *chitin u'se'orin* — and the defendant admitted to one, surely he would be *chayav*, supporting Shmuel's teaching.

The *gemara* rejects this inference. The *patur* ruling in the case of wheat versus barley could equally apply even if the original claim was *chitin u'se'orin* and only one was admitted. The Mishnah may have given the starker case — full cross-talk between wheat and barley — *le'hodiecha kocho de'Rabban Gamliel*, to reveal the remarkable extent of Rabban Gamliel's position: even where the parties seem to be speaking of entirely different things, Rabban Gamliel considers it *chayav*.

### Vessels & Land Problem

A further challenge arrives: *ta'ano keilim v'karka'ot, hoda bi'keilim v'chafar bi'karka'ot, bi'karka'ot v'chafar bi'keilim patur* — if one claims both vessels and land, and the defendant admits to the vessels while denying the land, or admits to the land while denying the vessels, he is *patur*. The *gemara* proceeds to the case on 40b: *hoda be'miktzat karka patur* — if the admission consists entirely of some of the land, he is *patur*, since *karka* does not obligate a *shevua*. But *hoda be'miktzat keilim chayav* — if he admitted to some of the vessels while denying others, he is *chayav*, and the *shevua* on the vessels draws in the land as well.

The *gemara* notes that the *diyuk* here seems strong: the reason for *patur* in the earlier cases involving land must be precisely that *karka lav bar shvua hi* — land is not subject to oath. If the only reason for *patur* is the involvement of land, then in a case of two types of vessels — analogous to *chitin u'se'orin* — admitting to one while denying the other should be *chayav*. This would directly support Shmuel.

The *gemara* resists the inference. The *patur* in the vessel-and-land cases could equally apply even in a case of two types of vessels, because the parties are addressing two distinct *minim*. The reason *karka* is specifically mentioned is not to identify the unique exemption for land, but to teach the *chiddush* at the end: when one is obligated by a *shevua* on the vessels, that *shevua* extends to cover the land as well. The *gemara* then asks whether this principle is not already derived from the Mishnah *zokkim et ha-nechasim she'ein lahen achrayut lishava aleihem* — that *metaltelin* on which an oath is taken drag in real estate. The *gemara* answers: *hai ikar hi agav grara nasveih* — here is the primary source for that ruling; what is stated in Kiddushin in the context of *kinyanim* is incidental. The *gemara* concedes that the *pshat* reading of the vessel-and-land case does seem to say one would be *chayav*, but presses the point that the *patur* there can be explained on grounds independent of the two-*minim* theory.

## Chitin Debate Resolution

### Unified Claims Problem

The *gemara* now directly confronts the competing versions of Rabbi Yochanan's position. One transmission, quoted by Rav Nachman in Shmuel's name, had Rabbi Yochanan agreeing that *chitin u'se'orin* with admission to one is *chayav*. But Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba reports in Rabbi Yochanan's name: *ta'ano chitin u'se'orin v'hoda lo be'echad me'hen patur* — you are *patur*.

The *gemara* acknowledges the contradiction and states: *amora'ei ninhu aliba de'Rebbe Yochanan* — it is a dispute among *Amoraim* about what Rabbi Yochanan actually held. The transmission was unclear, perhaps because Reish Lakish was quietly listening and others were not present to hear the definitive statement.

The *gemara* returns to challenge the *patur* version of Rabbi Yochanan's position with the same sources raised before, now as contradictions rather than as support. The Mishnah states *ta'ano chitin v'hoda lo bi'se'orin patur, v'Rabban Gamliel mechayev*. The *diyuk*: the only reason for *patur* here is that the parties were talking past one another. If the claim was *chitin u'se'orin* and one was admitted, the implication is *chayav* — directly against this version of Rabbi Yochanan. The *gemara* gives the same answer as before: even in the *chitin u'se'orin* case you would be *patur*, and the Mishnah presents the cross-talk case only *le'hodiecha kocho de'Rabban Gamliel*. Similarly, the vessel-and-land case is re-examined and resolved identically to before.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava presses harder with a cleaner source: *tanu shor v'hoda lo b'seh, seh v'hoda lo b'shor patur* — if you are claimed to have an ox and you admit only to the sheep, or vice versa, you are *patur*, because you are speaking of entirely different objects. But *tanu b'shor v'seh v'hoda lo b'echad mehem chayav* — if the original claim was for both an ox and a sheep, and you admit to one, you are *chayav*. This is the *chitin u'se'orin* scenario stated with perfect clarity, and it says *chayav*.

### Admon's Position

Rava anticipates the response: *ha mani Rabban Gamliel hi* — that is Rabban Gamliel's position. But the *gemara* immediately pushes back: if it were Rabban Gamliel, then even the first case of ox versus sheep should be *chayav*, since Rabban Gamliel rules *chayav* even when the parties are talking past one another. So it cannot be Rabban Gamliel. *Ela ha mani Admon hi* — this must be the position of Admon.

Here the *gemara* invokes the case of Admon from elsewhere: if one claims jars of olive oil and the defendant admits to having the jars but denies the oil, Admon rules *chayav*. Implicitly, that is exactly the *chitin u'se'orin* scenario — the claimant demands oil *and* the jars, the defendant admits only to the jars. Admon holds that admitting to one item within a unified claim is *modeh bemiktzat* of the whole. So whatever the *chachamim* hold in that dispute, Admon clearly rules *chayav* in the *chitin u'se'orin* type of case.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava concludes: *v'lo dichuyi m'dachinan lach, ela Talmud aruchu b'piv shel Rabbi Yochanan* — I am not merely deflecting you. This is a well-established teaching in the mouth of Rabbi Yochanan himself. When Rabbi Yochanan rules *patur* in the *chitin u'se'orin* case, he is ruling in accordance with the *chachamim* who disagree with Admon, and when the *braita* about the ox and the sheep says *chayav*, *ha mani Admon hi* — that is Admon's position, not the standard *halacha*.

This resolves the apparent contradiction within Rabbi Yochanan's reported rulings. The version that says *chayav* in the *chitin u'se'orin* case reflects Admon's position, which Rabbi Yochanan was perhaps citing or explaining rather than endorsing as normative. The version that says *patur* reflects Rabbi Yochanan's own ruling, following the *chachamim*. Rava establishes this as the definitive and settled teaching, and the daf concludes here.
