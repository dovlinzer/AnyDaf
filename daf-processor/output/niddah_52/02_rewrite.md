# Niddah 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Dev & Maturity

### Daf Overview

Daf 52a marks a transitional moment in the masechet. We have been engaged, for some time, with an extended discussion of physical development and the halakhic implications of various stages of maturity in young men and women. This material is not yet *niddah* proper — that resumes tomorrow — but it brings to a close an important and detailed treatment of the ages and stages through which a boy or girl passes on the way to full halakhic adulthood.

### Girl w/ 2 Hairs

The *mishna* at the top of 52a opens with the case of *tinokes she'hevi'ah shtei sa'aros* — a young girl who has produced two pubic hairs. The unstated but well-established assumption, carried over from prior *mishnayos*, is that she must also be at least twelve years and one day old. Once she meets both conditions, she is considered an adult with all attendant halakhic consequences, and the *mishna* proceeds to enumerate them.

### Chalitzah/Yibbum

The first consequence cited is *chaletzet o mityabemes* — she may perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*. This choice of example is striking. For a boy becoming an adult, one might naturally point to *tefillin*, *tzitzis*, or eligibility as a *shliach tzibbur*. For a girl, however, those ritual markers are less straightforwardly applicable, and so the classic halakhic examples cluster around marital status. Marriage itself cannot serve as the example, since a minor girl can already be married off by her father. The distinguishing criterion, therefore, is *chalitzah*, which requires full adulthood in both parties.

The textual basis for this requirement derives from Devarim 25, where the Torah uses the word *ish* — man — in the context of the one who refuses *yibbum* and must perform *chalitzah*. The *chachamim* understand *ish* to mean an adult male, and from this the requirement of adulthood for *chalitzah* is derived for men. As we will see in the *gemara*, the parallel to the woman follows from a separate logical step. It is worth noting that the requirement of adulthood here is not grounded in the need for consent — if it were, one would expect *yibbum* to carry the same or a stricter requirement, since *yibbum* involves a far more intimate act. The requirement is purely textual.

### Bat Mitzvah Obligations

The *mishna* continues: *vechayeves bechol hamitzvos ha'amuros batorah* — she is obligated in all *mitzvos* enumerated in the Torah. A nuance is embedded here. The question of one's *obligations* under the law is not identical to the question of whether one's legal acts are *effective*. Secular law offers an instructive analogy: one may be prosecuted as an adult at eighteen, but may not purchase alcohol until twenty-one. The age of obligation and the age of legal capacity for certain transactions can differ. So too in *halacha* — her obligations as a *bat mitzvah* are one matter; her capacity to execute certain binding legal acts is another. *Chalitzah* and *yibbum* belong to the latter category.

### Boys & Ben Sorer

The *mishna* then turns to a parallel formulation for young boys: *vekhen tinok she'hevi shtei sa'aros chayav bechol mitzvos ha'amuros batorah*. Interestingly, the *mishna* does not here enumerate examples from marriage law for the boy, nor does it mention *chalitzah* explicitly. Instead, it moves directly to the obligation in all *mitzvos* and highlights one particular matter of personal status: *vera'uy lihyos ben sorer umoreh* — he is fit to be treated as a rebellious son.

The *gemara* in Sanhedrin establishes that *ben sorer umoreh* requires the boy to be an adult — he must be punishable — and yet also still young enough to be called *ben*, a son. Two pubic hairs mark the beginning of this window, but it has an upper limit as well, stated in the *mishna*: *ad sheyakif zakan hatachton* — until the lower beard is surrounded.

### Beard Development

The *mishna* then adds, *velo ha'elyon ela she'dibru chachamim bilshon nekiyah* — not the upper beard, but the rabbis are speaking in euphemistic language. In other words, not the beard on the face, but the pubic hair that surrounds the genital area. Once that region is surrounded — meaning the hair runs from one edge to the other of the pubic area — he is no longer considered a *ben* in the relevant sense.

The *gemara*'s reasoning is that at this later stage of development, he is capable of fathering children. The *gemara* assumes — whether or not this is biologically precise — that the capacity to impregnate is tied to this later stage of physical maturation. Once he can be a father, he can no longer be merely a *ben* in the sense required for *ben sorer umoreh*.

### Mi'un Right

The *mishna* returns to the girl with a distinct and practically important law: *einah yecholah lema'en* — once she has two pubic hairs, she can no longer perform *mi'un*.

*Mi'un* is the halakhic mechanism by which a minor girl, married off not by her father but by her mother or brothers, may refuse the marriage and walk away without requiring a *get*. The institution of allowing a mother or brothers to arrange a marriage was a rabbinic creation, driven by genuine socioeconomic necessity — the need to ensure that girls without fathers would be provided for. But since this marriage is only *miderabbanan*, it carries a corresponding leniency: the girl retains the right of refusal until she becomes an adult.

The *mishna* takes for granted that once she is an adult, the mother and brothers no longer have any authority over her at all. What it addresses is the narrower question of the prior rabbinic marriage: once she is an adult with two pubic hairs, she cannot undo even that rabbinic marriage through *mi'un*. She has crossed the threshold.

*Rabbi Yehuda* dissents: *ad sheyirbe hashachor* — she retains the right of *mi'un* even after two pubic hairs, until the black hair genuinely fills the pubic region. The distinction in language between *yakif* (surrounds, used for the boy) and *yirbe* (fills, used in Rabbi Yehuda's formulation for the girl) appears deliberate — *yakif* suggests a perimeter, while *yirbe* suggests a more substantial covering.

## Gemara: Chalitzah

### Michoel's Q

The *gemara* opens with a pointed question: once the *mishna* has stated *chayeves bechol hamitzvos ha'amuros batorah* — she is obligated in all *mitzvos* — why is it necessary to separately state that she performs *chalitzah* or *yibbum*? If she is an adult across the board, her eligibility for *chalitzah* should be implicit.

### Rabbi Yosi

The *gemara* answers: *le'appuki miDe'Rabbi Yosi* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi held that when the Torah writes *ve'im lo yachpotz ha'ish* in the *parasha* of *chalitzah* in Devarim 25, the word *ish* — man — is the only indicator of an adult requirement, and it appears only in reference to the man performing *chalitzah*. From this, Rabbi Yosi derived that only the *man* performing *chalitzah* must be an adult; the woman, however — *bein gedolah bein ketanah* — whether adult or minor, may perform *chalitzah*. The *mishna* therefore explicitly rejects this position.

### Woman = Man

The *mishna*'s counter-position rests on the principle of *ishah ke'ish* — the woman is parallel to the man in the act of *chalitzah*. Since the man must be an adult, so too must the woman. This parallel is logical given the bilateral nature of the *chalitzah* ceremony, even if the Torah's language does not make it explicit on the woman's side.

### Why Spell Out

A further question is raised: given that the *mishna* already said *vekhen hatinok* — the same applies to the boy — why does it then proceed to spell out that he is obligated in all *mitzvos*? If the *mishna* said "the same," the same should suffice. One might suggest the *mishna* does so in order to build toward the discussion of *ben sorer umoreh* and its upper time limit. But the *gemara* rejects that rationale.

### Already Taught

The *gemara* objects: *tanina hada zimna* — that law has already been taught. The *perek* of *ben sorer umoreh* in Sanhedrin contains a *mishna* that uses precisely the same language — *misheyavi shtei sa'aros ve'ad sheyakif zakan hatachton, velo ha'elyon ela she'dibru chachamim bilshon nekiyah* — word for word. The *mishna* here could simply have said "the same applies to the boy" without repeating anything. Why be so expansive?

The *gemara* concludes: *ein hakhi nami* — you are correct, it was not strictly necessary. *Ela aidi de'perish millei de'tinoket kama perish nami millei de'tinok* — since the *mishna* is spelling out all the details regarding the girl, it decided to spend some time elaborating on the boy as well, even if the material was not strictly necessary here. After all, the thrust of this entire *perek* and *masechet* is overwhelmingly concerned with the girl's stages of development; nevertheless, the *mishna* offers a parallel treatment of the boy, even if briefer and ultimately redundant.

## Mi'un & Sex

### Halakha: R. Yehuda

*Rabbi Abbahu* says in the name of *Rabbi Elazar*: *halacha keRabbi Yehuda* — the law follows Rabbi Yehuda. A girl married off by her mother or brothers retains the right of *mi'un* until the later stage of development, beyond two pubic hairs, when the pubic region becomes substantially filled with hair.

### Sex = Binding

However — and this is the important qualification — *umodeh Rabbi Yehuda she'miniv'alah le'achar shehevi'ah shtei sa'aros, shuv einah yecholah lema'en*. Even Rabbi Yehuda concedes that once the girl has had sexual relations with her husband *after* acquiring two pubic hairs, she can no longer perform *mi'un*. The reasoning is that once she is an adult — marked by two pubic hairs — and she has sex within what is nominally a marital context, that act of sex is interpreted as *kiddushei biah*. Sexual relations in a marital context, once she is an adult, constitute a new act of *kiddushin* effected through intercourse. And once that biblical *kiddushin* has been created, *mi'un* — which operates only against a rabbinic marriage — is no longer available.

### Minor vs Adult

This distinction between minor and adult is crucial. Before two pubic hairs, if the girl (who has no father) has sex with this man, no new marital status is created. The rabbinic marriage remains defined by the original act of the mother or brothers, and nothing about the sexual relations changes its nature. But once she is an adult, the legal calculus shifts: sex in a marital context by an adult woman creates *kiddushei biah*, upgrading the marriage from rabbinic to biblical.

### Sex Creates Marriage?

This analysis raises a broader and much-debated question: do we apply the principle of *kol habo'el al da'at kiddushin rishonim bo'el* — that anyone who has sex does so in the context and intent of the original *kiddushin*? This principle addresses cases where the initial act of *kiddushin* was somehow defective — the coin given was later discovered not to be worth a *perutah*, for instance, or a stipulation was not fulfilled. In such a case, the couple may have lived together for years, had children, and conducted themselves in every way as a married couple. The question is whether the ongoing sexual relations throughout that marriage constitute a new act of *kiddushei biah* that independently validates the marriage — or whether all of it remains framed entirely by the original, now-defective, act of *kiddushin*, such that no valid marriage was ever created.

This is not merely an abstract Talmudic debate. It is directly relevant to *agunah* cases today and to situations where a woman cannot obtain a *get* and a *posek* is examining whether the original marriage ceremony was halakhically valid.

### Agunah Cases

If the original ceremony was defective, one approach holds that the subsequent sexual relations — conducted in what the parties understood to be a marital relationship — themselves constitute *kiddushei biah* and create a biblically binding marriage. This was the strong position of Rav Yosef Eliyahu Henkin, who argued that cohabitation and sexual relations in a recognized marital context bind the couple regardless of defects in the original ceremony. A countervailing position holds that when the couple intended their relationship to be governed by the original ceremony, the sexual relations are always understood as occurring *al da'at* that original act — and if that act was void, nothing that follows validates the marriage.

One might try to distinguish between a case where there was *no ceremony at all* and one where there was a ceremony that turned out to be invalid. Where there was no ceremony, one might more readily infer that the parties intended their cohabitation itself to constitute marriage. Where there was a ceremony, however defective, the legal relationship might be understood as defined entirely by that ceremony. These distinctions are debated by the *rishonim* and *acharonim*, and *Tosafot* engages directly with the question.

The present *gemara* is operating in precisely this conceptual space: when a girl's marriage was arranged by her mother — a rabbinic act — and she subsequently has sex with this man as an adult, does that sex constitute a new, independent, biblically binding act of *kiddushei biah*? The *gemara*'s position here is that it does.

## R. Yishmael Story

### Chavruhei Ruling

The *gemara* reports that *chavruhei deRav Kahana* — the colleagues of Rav Kahana — *savru lema'avid uvda keRabbi Yehuda* — wished to rule in practice like Rabbi Yehuda in a case where the girl had already had sexual relations as an adult with the man to whom she had been married by her mother. They intended to permit her to perform *mi'un* regardless, taking the position that the sex did not constitute *kiddushei biah* and the marriage remained solely rabbinic.

### Child on Shoulders

Rav Kahana stopped them. *Lo kach haya ma'aseh bevito shel Rabbi Yishmael* — is this not exactly what happened in the case of the daughter of Rabbi Yishmael? Rabbi Yishmael had died, and his wife — the girl's mother — had married the daughter off. The girl came to the *beit midrash* to perform *mi'un* and leave her husband. But she arrived with her child on her shoulders — *uvna murchav al ketayfa*. She had already had a child with this man, which the *gemara* takes as indicating that she had sexual relations with him as an adult, since as a general rule girls do not give birth before reaching the age of *bat mitzvah*.

### Beit Midrash Weeps

That day, the statements of Rabbi Yishmael were quoted in the *beit midrash* — *huskeru devarav shel Rabbi Yishmael beBeit HaMidrash* — and the *beit midrash* erupted in weeping: *ravtsa bechiya gedola beBeit HaMidrash*. The language here is significant — *ravtsa* is not simply "she cried." It suggests a collective, overwhelming outpouring of grief. And they said: *davar she'amar oso hatzadik yikashel bo zaro* — shall the very ruling of that righteous man cause his own daughter to stumble?

To understand this, we must see what Rabbi Yishmael actually ruled.

### Kiddushei Taut

*De'amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Yishmael*: Rabbi Yishmael had taught a principle drawn from the *sota* passage — *vehi lo nitpasa*, "she was not seized," implying that a woman who committed adultery willingly is forbidden to her husband, while one who was raped (*nitpasa*) remains permitted. But Rabbi Yishmael added a further case: *yesh lach acheres she'af al pi she'lo nitpasa, muteres* — there is another category of woman who, even though she willingly had sex with a man who is not her husband, is nonetheless not forbidden.

### Ach'eres Case

What case is this? *She'kidusheha kidushei taut* — a case where the original marriage was a marriage in error. Perhaps the coin used for *kiddushin* was not worth a *perutah*; perhaps a stipulation was not met. In such a case, even if she had sex with another man — even willingly — and even *she'uvna murchav al ketayfa*, even if her child rides on her shoulders — *umeima'enes veholechet la*, she may still refuse the first "husband" and leave. Because the first marriage was never valid, her sexual relations with the other man did not constitute adultery. She needs no *get*, and the sex creates no binding status.

The *gemara* assumes that this principle applies equally to the case of *mi'un*: if the original marriage was arranged by the mother and is only rabbinic, then just as in a *kiddushei taut* the woman can walk away even after having sex and having a child, so too here, the girl can perform *mi'un* even after sex and even after having a child. According to Rabbi Yishmael's position, the daughter of Rabbi Yishmael should have been permitted to perform *mi'un*.

### Yavne Vote

But there was a problem. *Venimnu vegamru* — a vote was taken and it was concluded: *ad matai habat memanet, ad shetavi shtei sa'aros* — a girl may perform *mi'un* only until she produces two pubic hairs. This vote effectively overruled Rabbi Yishmael's position and established that once she is an adult — two pubic hairs — and has had sex in the marital context, she can no longer perform *mi'un*, regardless of whether the original marriage was *kiddushei taut* or a rabbinic marriage by the mother.

The weeping in the *beit midrash* is now intelligible. The daughter of Rabbi Yishmael had come seeking *mi'un* on the basis of her father's ruling — a ruling of a *tzadik* that should have permitted her to leave. Yet that ruling had been overruled. She stood there with her child on her shoulders, seeking release from a man she did not wish to remain with, and the *beit midrash* — relying on the majority vote — denied her. That a righteous man's ruling could leave his own daughter in this position was the source of the collective anguish.

There is an alternative reading of the episode, and it may actually fit the text more precisely. On that reading, the assembled sages were not lamenting that Rabbi Yishmael's ruling had been overruled. Rather, they felt it would be deeply wrong for the daughter to invoke her father's ruling in order to leave this man — the father of her child — and that it would therefore constitute a moral stumbling caused by the father's leniency. Under this reading, *venimnu vegamru* was not a prior vote being recalled to defeat her, but a vote taken in response to her appearance — a vote to overrule Rabbi Yishmael precisely because they found it unconscionable that his ruling would be used to separate a mother from the father of her child. This reading makes the sages less sympathetic to the girl — and arguably, as several in the room noted, less responsive to what may well have been a genuinely desperate situation.

What is not in doubt is that this happens — and happened — with some regularity. A young woman, perhaps trapped in an unwanted marriage, comes before a *beit din* with a valid legal argument, and is refused. The parallel to contemporary *agunah* cases requires little elaboration.

The *gemara* also raises the question of whether *kiddushei taut* and a rabbinic marriage by the mother are truly analogous. *Tosafot* addresses this and notes that the cases may be distinguishable. In a *kiddushei taut*, the original marriage was intended to be biblical but was defective from the outset; in the *mi'un* case, the marriage was always intentionally rabbinic. Whether the status of ongoing sexual relations is identical in both cases is a genuine question, and *Tosafot* discusses opinions that differentiate between them.

One deeper question runs through this entire discussion: is *kiddushei biah* created by the mere fact of sex in a marital context, or does it require a conscious *intent* to effect marriage through the act of sex? If intention is required, then ongoing marital relations — conducted without any deliberate thought of creating new *kiddushin* — may not constitute *kiddushei biah* at all, even when the parties consider themselves married. This consideration bears on how broadly the principle is applied in practice.

### Kahana Silenced

The *gemara* reports: *perash velo avad uvda* — Rav Kahana, having cited the story of Rabbi Yishmael's daughter, withdrew. He did not issue a ruling permitting *mi'un* after sexual relations as an adult. Whether he formally declared it prohibited or simply refrained from permitting it is not entirely clear, but he was effectively silenced by the precedent.

### Dissenting Voices

Nevertheless, *Rabbi Yitzchak vetalmidei Rabbi Chanina avdu uvda keRabbi Yehuda ve'af al gav d'niv'alah* — Rabbi Yitzchak and the students of Rabbi Chanina did in fact rule in practice like Rabbi Yehuda, permitting *mi'un* even after she had had sexual relations as an adult.

### Troops Deployed

*Ve'azal Rav Sheman bar Abba ve'amar kamei deRabbi Yochanan* — Rav Sheman bar Abba went and reported this before Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yochanan in turn brought it before Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah — the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the political head of the community. The response was swift and striking: *shadar bulshafi ve'apkinehu* — he dispatched officers, who removed the woman from the second husband to whom she had gone after performing *mi'un*. A ruling had been issued that permitted her to leave the first husband and remarry; she had done so; and yet the political authority sent troops to forcibly undo that remarriage. It does not state that she was returned to the first husband — only that she was taken from the second. The question of whether she could return to the first, given that she had since had relations with the second, touches on a separate discussion in Yevamot about women who remarry under the assumption their first husband is dead and later discover he is alive, and whether return is possible in such cases.

## Hair Criteria

### Two Long Hairs

Now that the volatile discussion of *mi'un* and its limits has concluded, the *gemara* turns to a more technical set of questions: what precisely constitutes the hair-related criteria for the various halakhic thresholds we have been discussing?

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Mar Ukva*: the phrase *yarba hashachor* does not require the entire pubic region to be physically covered with hair. *Lo she'yarba hashachor mamash* — it need not literally be filled. Rather, *kedei sheyihiyu shtei sa'arot shochvot venira'os kemi sheyarba hashachor al halavan* — two hairs suffice, provided they are long enough that if laid flat they would appear to cover the white of the skin beneath, creating the visual impression of black overwhelming white. This is a striking leniency in interpreting the requirement — though precisely how two hairs, however long, can visually fill an area remains somewhat unclear.

### Rava: Surrounding

*Rava amar shtei sa'arot hamakifot misafa lesafa* — Rava defines it as two hairs that surround from edge to edge. Whether this means hairs so long that they traverse the entire region, or a row of hairs that forms a band across the region which when laid flat would fill it, is not entirely clear from the text. It may not be substantially different from the position of Rav Chisda and Mar Ukva in practical terms.

### From Follicles

*Amar Rabbi Chelbo amar Rav Huna*: the two hairs referred to throughout — in the classic threshold for adulthood, not merely for the extended *mi'un* period — *tzarich sheyehei be'ikaran gumot*, must come from follicles. The hair must emerge from actual follicles. This seems obvious — how would a hair exist without a follicle? — but the *gemara* will shortly clarify why this requires statement.

### Follicles Alone

*Rav Malkio amar Rav Adda bar Ahava* takes this a significant step further: *gumot af al pi she'ein sa'arot* — follicles even without hairs are sufficient. The presence of follicles, even before hairs have actually grown from them, is enough to establish the halakhic threshold of adulthood. The question of whether follicles can exist before hair grows from them is left somewhat open, but the answer appears to be yes.

### Two Ravs

The *gemara* then pauses to clarify a point of attribution that has practical significance for *pesak*: the names Rav Malkio and Rav Malkia are easily confused, yet they are two distinct figures whose rulings should not be interchanged. *Rav Chanina berei deRav Ika* offers one sorting principle: *shipud, shefachot, vegumot Rav Malkio; belorit, efer mikleh, vegvinah Rav Malkia*. Three statements about a type of spit, maidservants (a *mishna* in Ketubot), and follicles belong to Rav Malkio; three others concerning a certain pagan haircut, the use of ashes, and cheese of non-Jews belong to Rav Malkia.

*Rav Papa* offers a cleaner mnemonic principle: *matnitin uMatnita Rav Malkio* — anything based on explaining a *mishna* or *baraita* was said by Rav Malkio; *shma'teta* — amoraic teachings — were Rav Malkia's. The mnemonic is *matnitin malchasa* — the *mishna* is the queen, and *malkia* (king/queen) helps you remember that it is Rav Malkia — wait, the logic runs in the other direction: *matnitin* is the queen, which calls to mind the word *malka*, which helps anchor Rav Malkio as the one associated with *mishnas* and *baraitas*. The practical difference this creates in the list concerns the case of *shefachot* — the maidservants ruling is a *mishna* in Ketubot and therefore belongs to Rav Malkio under Rav Papa's system, while *efer mikleh* (ashes), which is amoraic, is moved to Rav Malkia's column.

### Proof from Silence

*Rav Ashi* then reports: *Mar Zutra said to me* that *Rabbi Chanina mi-Sura* was troubled by the ruling that follicles alone constitute adulthood. His challenge was from silence: *lo ishtamit tana ve'ashmi'einan guma* — throughout all of *Shas*, wherever *tannaim* discuss the two-hair criterion, they always say "two hairs." Not once do they say "two follicles." If follicles without hairs were sufficient, surely at least one *tanna* would have mentioned it somewhere. The consistent silence must indicate that follicles alone are not sufficient.

### Two from One

*Rav Ashi* offers a defense. The *tannaim* always spoke about hairs rather than follicles not because follicles are insufficient, but because they wished to teach a further law: *de'afilu shtei sa'aros beguma achas* — that two hairs count toward the threshold even if both emerge from a single follicle. Had the *tannaim* taught about follicles, one might have assumed that you need two hairs *from two separate follicles*. By consistently saying "two hairs," they communicate that even two hairs from one follicle are sufficient — and implicitly preserve the ruling that follicles alone are also sufficient.

### Iyov Midrash

But this immediately prompts a question: *umi ika kai ha'gavna* — is it even possible to have two hairs from one follicle? The *gemara* cites the verse from Iyov: *asher bise'arah yeshufeni*, and notes that *Rava said: Iyov bi'se'arah cheiref* — Iyov used the whirlwind as the vehicle for his complaint. He blasphemed, essentially accusing God of having confused him (*Iyov*) for His enemy (*oyev*) — perhaps, he suggested, God's attention was distracted by a passing whirlwind. *Bise'arah heshivu* — and God answered him from the whirlwind.

The *gemara* presents God's response with remarkable specificity: *shoteh she-ba'olam, harbeh nimin barasi bero'sho shel adam, ulchol nima venima barasi lah guma bifnei atzmah* — you fool, I created many hairs on the human head, and for each and every hair I created its own individual follicle, so that two hairs should not share one follicle. *She'lo yehu shtayim yonkos miguma achas* — and had two hairs grown from one follicle, *makhish ma'or einav shel adam* — it would weaken the sight of a person. *Guma beguma lo nishalef li* — I ensure that not one follicle is confused with another. *Iyov be'oyev nishalef li?* — And you think I confused Iyov for oyev?

The wordplay on *se'arah* — both whirlwind and hair — runs through the entire exchange. God's point is that the perfection of the natural order, down to the unique assignment of each hair to its own follicle, testifies to a level of precision that makes any accusation of divine error absurd.

### Head vs Body

This *midrash*, however, creates a problem: if two hairs cannot share one follicle — as divine design ensures — then the entire reason Rav Ashi gave for why the *tannaim* speak of two hairs (to include the case of two hairs from one follicle) collapses. The *gemara* resolves this with the principle of *lo kashya*: *ha begufah ha bereisha* — the midrashic teaching about God ensuring each hair has its own follicle refers to the hairs of the head. On the body, it may be possible for two hairs to share a single follicle. Rav Ashi's defense therefore remains valid with respect to body hair, which is precisely what is at issue in the question of *shtei sa'aros* for halakhic maturity.

## Hair Location

### Different Locations

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: the two hairs need not be in the same location. For a young boy, even *achat al hakaf ve'achat al habetzim* — one in the region above the genitals and one at the testicles — the two hairs may be in different places and still combine.

### Girl: Varied Spots

A *baraita* teaches a parallel principle for a young girl: *afillu achat begavah ve'achat bechersa* — even one hair in the area below and one in the pubic area count together. Moreover, *achat al gabei kishrei etzbe'oteha shel yad ve'achat al gabei kishrei etzbe'oteha shel regel* — a hair at the knuckles of the hand and a hair at the knuckles of the foot may also count. The claim is striking: at the age of maturity, hair begins to grow not only in the pubic area but also on the hands and feet, and such hair itself constitutes evidence of — or perhaps even independently establishes — the halakhic threshold.

### R. Shimon's View

*Divrei Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda ish Kefar Akko she'amar mishum Rabbi Shimon*: this lenient position, allowing hair from the hands and feet to count and allowing hairs from different body locations to combine, is the view of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon.

### Same Place Only

The *rabbanan*, cited in the name of *Rav Chisda*, disagree: *ad sheyihyu shtei sa'arot bemakom echad* — the two hairs must be in the same place, and by implication they must be in the pubic area specifically. Hairs from the hands or feet do not count, and hairs cannot be combined across different regions of the body.

## Mi'un Duration

### Four Opinions

*Tanu rabbanan* presents a formal enumeration of the competing views on the duration of *mi'un* rights — that is, until what stage of development may a girl married off by her mother or brothers perform *mi'un* and leave without a *get*.

### Meir vs Yehuda

*Rabbi Meir* holds the strictest position: *ad shetavi shtei sa'arot* — only until she produces two pubic hairs. Once she is an adult, *mi'un* is closed. *Rabbi Yehuda*, as we have discussed at length, is considerably more lenient: *ad sheyirbeh shachor* — until the black pubic hair has substantially filled the area, allowing *mi'un* to continue well into what is technically adult status.

### Yosi Middle

*Rabbi Yosi* occupies a middle position: *ad shetakif ha'atarah* — until the crown is surrounded. This appears to mean a ring or perimeter of hair around the pubic region, more than Rabbi Meir's two hairs but less than Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of a full covering.

### Ben Shlakot

*Ben Shlakot* takes the most lenient position of all: *ad shetechalkel* — until *techalkel*, a term whose precise meaning is uncertain. Both Rashi and Tosafot point to a usage elsewhere in the *gemara* suggesting something like a large mop or mass of hair, more substantial even than Rabbi Yehuda's *yarba hashachor*, or at minimum equivalent to it. The exact boundary between the two remains unclear.

### Yavne Testimony

*Rabbi Shimon* reports that he encountered *Chananya ben Chakinai* in Tzidan, who asked him to relay a message to Rabbi Akiva: when you reach him, ask him until when a girl may perform *mi'un*. And if he tells you "until two hairs" — which Chananya presumed was Rabbi Akiva's position — tell him that Ben Shlakot testified before all of you in Yavne that it is until *techalkel*, and you said nothing in response. Silence, in the presence of the assembled sages at Yavne, implies consent. How can Rabbi Akiva now hold to the stricter position?

### Akiva Rejects

Rabbi Shimon reports that when he brought this message to Rabbi Akiva, the response was dismissive: *kalkal zeh eini yodea mahu* — I do not know what *kalkal* even means; *Ben Shlakot eini makir* — I do not recognize who Ben Shlakot is. Rabbi Akiva effectively refused to acknowledge the testimony — whether because he genuinely did not recall it, because he was in disagreement with it, or because he did not consider Ben Shlakot a credible witness is left unstated. His position remained: *ad shetavi shtei sa'arot* — until two pubic hairs only. This, of course, is the strictest ruling, cutting off the girl's right of *mi'un* at the earliest possible moment.

## Hair Length Standards

### Two Hairs Analogy

The *gemara* now turns to a different dimension of the two-hair criterion — not where the hairs must be, or how many follicles they require, but how long the hairs themselves must be. And this question, it emerges, is not unique to the context of maturity. *Shtei sa'arot ha'amuros beparah uvnega'im* — the "two hairs" mentioned in the context of the *parah adumah* and in the laws of *nega'im* are governed by the same length standard as the two hairs in the context of maturity. In *parah adumah*, two black hairs disqualify the animal from serving as the *parah adumah*; in *nega'im*, two white hairs within a leprous spot are halakhically significant. The question of minimum hair length thus applies across these domains.

### Bend to Root

*Rabbi Yishmael* establishes the baseline: *kedei lachof roshan le'ikaran* — the hair must be long enough to bend its tip over and touch its own root. This requires a hair of meaningful length — long enough to be folded back on itself.

### Fingernail Length

*Rabbi Eliezer* is more lenient: *kedei likrotz betziporen* — it suffices if the hair can be grasped and pulled out by a fingernail, which is presumably shorter than the length needed to fold back to the root.

### Tweezers Length

*Rabbi Akiva* specifies: *kedei she'yihiyu nitalos bezug* — long enough to be removed with tweezers. Rashi apparently understands this as *longer* than the fingernail standard — perhaps because tweezers require a bit more hair to grip than a fingernail — though this is somewhat counterintuitive and the precise reading is not obvious.

### Strict in All Ways

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Mar Ukva*: *halacha kedivarei kulam lehachmir* — the law follows all of the opinions, in the direction of stringency. We are strict in both directions. From the shortest possible length that satisfies any of the three opinions, we are concerned that the hair may already be sufficient — and therefore, in cases where the presence of sufficient hair is a leniency (such as *mi'un* being closed or *chalitzah* being valid), we take even the smallest qualifying hair seriously. Conversely, until the longest standard is met, we remain uncertain that the threshold has been definitively crossed — and therefore, in cases where the hair would serve as a stringency (such as requiring an additional *get* out of doubt), we hold out until the highest standard is satisfied.

### Back to Niddah

With this ruling, the extended treatment of physical development, maturity criteria, the laws of *mi'un*, and the technical dimensions of the two-hair standard comes to a close. Tomorrow the *masechet* returns to its primary subject: *niddah* proper.
