# Menachot 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shatnez in Tzitzit

### Tekelet in Linen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמָּא יִקָּרַע סְדִינוֹ בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ וְיִתְפְּרֶנּוּ. וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״תַּעֲשֶׂה״ – וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The reason for the rabbinic decree is **lest** one's **cloak rip within three** fingerbreadths of the edge of the corner of the garment, which is where the ritual fringes are placed, **and he sew it** with linen string and then use the excess string for ritual fringes. **And** in such a case the ritual fringes would be unfit because **the Torah states:** "You shall prepare yourself twisted cords" (Deuteronomy 22:12), which teaches: **Prepare** it, **and not from what has already been prepared.**

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained discussion about *shatnez* and *tzitzit*, and specifically the case of a linen garment: does one place *tzitzit* on it, and does one include *tekelet*? *Beit Shammai* says no; *Beit Hillel* says yes in principle. Yet even according to *Beit Hillel*, the *gemara* concluded that in practice the answer is also no. The reasoning comes down to two concerns: first, that people might use the wrong color and thereby fail to discharge the *mitzvah* of *tekelet*; and second, a concern that the garment might tear and be sewn back up in a way that would render the *tzitzit* invalid as *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* — and once the *mitzvah* is not properly discharged, the wearer is in fact violating *shatnez*. *Tosafot* reads that *sugya* as prohibiting not only *tekelet* but *tzitzit* entirely on such a garment, a point that will resurface later.

### Tearing Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁרָא רַבִּי זֵירָא לִסְדִינֵיהּ. רַב זֵירָא אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם כְּסוּת לַיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Because of this rabbinic decree, **Rabbi Zeira untied** the ritual fringes and removed them from **his** linen **cloak. Rav Zeira said:** The rabbinic **decree** prohibiting ritual fringes on a linen garment is **also due to** the concern that one might affix ritual fringes to **a nighttime garment.**

The *gemara* picks up with Rava's teaching — presented in his own name and corroborated by a parallel tradition from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Zeira — that when a garment tears within three fingerbreadths of the corner and is then sewn back up, the *tzitzit* that were already attached at that corner become invalid as *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*: the corner was reattached while the *tzitzit* were already on it, rather than the *tzitzit* being placed on an already-complete obligated garment. Because one is not discharging the *mitzvah*, the *shatnez* of the wool *tekelet* in a linen garment remains a violation.

### Night Garments

Since the concern about night garments has already been cited above, this section continues from that source:

Rav Zeira — who appears to be a different figure from Rabbi Zeira — offers an additional concern. Even setting aside the question of validity, there is a worry that a person will place *tzitzit* with *tekelet* on his daytime linen garment and then come to place them on a nighttime garment — pajamas, as it were — which is designated exclusively for night and is therefore not obligated in *tzitzit*. On such an exempt garment, the wool *tekelet* together with the linen would constitute *shatnez* without the protective exemption of *mitzvah* performance. Because of this concern, the Rabbis refrained from permitting it even on the obligated daytime garment.

### Garment vs. Corner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הָא מִילְּתָא אֲמַרִי, וְאִיתְּמַר בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא כְּוָותִי: הִיא שֶׁל בֶּגֶד וּכְנָפֶיהָ שֶׁל עוֹר – חַיֶּיבֶת, הִיא שֶׁל עוֹר וּכְנָפֶיהָ שֶׁל בֶּגֶד – פְּטוּרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? עִיקַּר בֶּגֶד בָּעֵינַן. רַב אַחַאי אָזֵיל בָּתַר כָּנָף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: This is a statement** that **I said, and it was stated in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **in the name of Rabbi Zeira in accordance with my** opinion: If a garment is made **from cloth and its corners** are made **from leather,** it is **required** to have ritual fringes. Conversely, if a cloak is made **from leather and its corners** are made **from cloth,** it is **exempt** from the mitzva of ritual fringes. **What is the reason** for this? **We require** that **the main** part of the **garment** be obligated, and a leather garment is not required to have ritual fringes. The Gemara notes: **Rav Aḥai** would **follow the corner** in determining whether the garment is required to have ritual fringes or not, because the Torah states: "On the corners of their garments" (Numbers 15:38).

Rava then echoes the teaching cited in *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Zeira: if the garment itself is of an obligated material but the corners are leather — leather being exempt from *tzitzit* — the garment is nonetheless *chayav*, since we follow the *ikkar beged*, the primary material of the garment, not the corner. Conversely, if the garment is leather but the corners are cloth, it is exempt for the same reason. Rav Achai takes the opposite position, holding that since the verse says *al ha-knaf petil tekelet*, the obligation and its conditions are determined by the nature of the *knaf*, the corner, not the garment body as a whole.

## Ta'aseh V'lo Min

### Three to Four Corners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב סְחוֹרָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הֵטִיל לְבַעֲלַת שָׁלֹשׁ, וְהִשְׁלִימָהּ לְאַרְבַּע – פְּסוּלָה, ״תַּעֲשֶׂה״ – וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **Rav Seḥora says** that **Rav Huna says:** In a case where one **affixed** ritual fringes **to** a garment **possessing** only **three** corners, which is not required to have ritual fringes, **and** then **completed** its **fourth** corner by sewing on additional material or cutting away some of the material, the ritual fringes he attached to the original three corners are **unfit.** This is due to the principle: **Prepare** it, **and not from what has already been prepared.**

Rava, citing Rav Sechora citing Rav Huna, rules: if one placed *tzitzit* on a three-cornered garment — meaning a garment that had only three corners and was not yet obligated — and then added a fourth corner to complete it, the *tzitzit* are invalid on account of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*. When the *tzitzit* were placed, the garment was exempt; when the garment became obligated, the *tzitzit* were already there without having been actively placed on an obligated garment. The principle parallels the requirement of *gedilim ta'aseh lecha* — you must actively make it — analogous to the requirement that a *sukkah* be actively constructed and not come about automatically.

### Chasidim Early Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָרְגוּ בָּהּ שָׁלֹשׁ, הָיוּ מְטִילִין לָהּ תְּכֵלֶת. אֵימָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁפָּצְעוּ בָּהּ שָׁלֹשׁ, הָיוּ מְטִילִין לָהּ תְּכֵלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: It is told of **the early** generations of **pious** men that **once they weaved three** fingerbreadths of the length of the garment, **they would affix** the white and **sky-blue** strings **to** the first two corners, even though the garment was not yet long enough to be obligated to have ritual fringes. The Gemara answers: **Say** that the *baraita* should read as follows: **Once they completed [*shepatzu*]** the garment until there were only **three** fingerbreadths left to weave, **they would affix** the white and **sky-blue** strings **to** the first two corners.

The *gemara* raises a challenge from the practice of the *chasidim ha-rishonim*: as soon as they had woven three fingerbreadths of their garment, they would place *tzitzit* on it — seemingly before the garment was fully obligated. This appears to contradict the concern for *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*. The *gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting the tradition: read not as "when they began the first three fingerbreadths," but rather "when they came within the last three fingerbreadths of finishing the second edge." At that point the garment had already reached its full obligated length, and the *tzitzit* were being placed on an already-obligated garment. There is a separate point that the *tzitzit* should not be placed right at the very edge — as we will see later — but *bedi'avad* that may not be disqualifying, and the *chasidim* placed their *tzitzit* within those final three fingerbreadths specifically so that when the last strip of weaving was complete, the *tzitzit* would sit within the valid zone from the edge. In any event, the tradition of the *chasidim* does not undermine the rule of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*.

### Extra Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמְרִינַן ״תַּעֲשֶׂה״ – וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי? (אִינִי), וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הֵטִיל לְמוּטֶּלֶת – כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר רָבָא: הַשְׁתָּא בְּ״בַל תּוֹסִיף״ קָאֵי, מַעֲשֶׂה לָא הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do we say: Prepare** it, **and not from what has already been prepared? Is that so** that this principle disqualifies ritual fringes that one affixed to a garment before he was required to do so? **But doesn't Rabbi Zeira say:** If one **affixed** ritual fringes **to** a garment that already had ritual fringes **affixed** to it and then removed the original strings, it is **fit,** despite the fact that when he attached the second set they were superfluous? This indicates that even if one attaches ritual fringes to a garment when there is no obligation to attach them, the ritual fringes are fit. **Rava said:** This does not present a difficulty, because **now** that he adds a second, unnecessary set of ritual fringes and **is** liable **for** violating the **prohibition of adding** to a mitzva (see Deuteronomy 13:1), **is** it **not** considered **an action?**

The *gemara* presses further: are we actually strict about *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* when it comes to *tzitzit*? Rav Zeira taught: if one placed an additional set of *tzitzit* — a fifth bundle — on a corner of a garment that already had *tzitzit*, and then removed the original *tzitzit*, the garment is *keshera*. Now this is precisely the situation of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* in reverse: you placed the new *tzitzit* while the garment already had *tzitzit* satisfying its obligation, and only after cutting off the originals did the new ones become the operative ones. That seems like they "arrived" automatically rather than being placed on an obligated garment at the moment of need.

Rava addresses this. Since when you placed that fifth bundle of *tzitzit* you were transgressing *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to the *mitzvot*, such as placing five *tzitzit* on a garment that requires only four — the act itself was as if no act occurred, *ma'ase lo havei*. Therefore, when you cut off the original *tzitzit*, that is in effect the moment of "making" — the act of placement — for the new *tzitzit*. This is not a case of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*.

It must be said that this reasoning is genuinely difficult. Rashi's formulation is that if you place *tzitzit* on an exempt garment, you have performed an act — just not a valid one — and when the garment subsequently becomes obligated, the act is already spent. Whereas when you transgress *bal tosif*, since the act was forbidden, it is as though no act was performed at all, and cutting the original *tzitzit* constitutes the act of making. Yet one might argue precisely the reverse: when a garment is not obligated, the act is entirely without weight, more akin to no act at all; but when a garment is obligated and one places an additional *tzitzit* in violation of *bal tosif*, the very fact that one is transgressing *bal tosif* demonstrates that the act carries real halachic weight — it just carries it in the wrong direction. Indeed, one might have argued that this is exactly a case of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* averted: the garment was obligated, and you actively placed *tzitzit* on it. And conversely, when a garment was exempt and subsequently became obligated, *that* is where the *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* problem genuinely lies. Rava's answer runs in the opposite direction, and it remains, at least on the surface, difficult to fully understand.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מִמַּאי דְּגַבְרָא לְאוֹסוֹפֵי קָא מִיכַּוֵּין? דִּלְמָא לְבַטּוֹלֵי קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, וּ״בַל תּוֹסִיף״ לֵיכָּא, מַעֲשֶׂה אִיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this: From where** is it known **that** Rabbi Zeira is discussing a case where the **person intended to add** to the original set of ritual fringes? **Perhaps** Rabbi Zeira is discussing a case where **he intended to nullify** the original strings, **and** therefore **there is no prohibition of adding** to a mitzva, and **there is an action.**

Rav Pappa challenges not the principle but its application: how can we be certain that the person placing the fifth *tzitzit* was intending to *add* to the existing *tzitzit* — which would make it *bal tosif* — rather than intending to *replace* the existing ones, i.e., he planned all along to remove the originals? If he was intending to negate the originals, then *bal tosif* does not apply, because in his mind the existing *tzitzit* were already as good as gone. But in that case, his act of placing the new *tzitzit* was a real act — just not yet a valid one. And when he cuts the originals, the new *tzitzit* automatically become operative without having been placed at the moment of obligation. That would be a *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* problem. The upshot is that Rav Zeira's ruling that such *tzitzit* are valid cannot be demonstrated as cleanly as Rava's argument suggested, and the underlying question is left without a fully satisfying resolution.

## Shmuel: No Shatnez

### Size Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תְּכֵלֶת אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם כִּלְאַיִם, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּטַלִּית פְּטוּרָה. מַאי טַלִּית פְּטוּרָה? אִילֵּימָא דְּלֵית בַּהּ שִׁיעוּרָא, וְהָתַנְיָא: טַלִּית שֶׁהַקָּטָן מִתְכַּסֶּה בּוֹ רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav Mattana says** that **Shmuel says:** White and **sky-blue** strings **are not subject to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if they are affixed **to a cloak** that is **exempt** from ritual fringes. The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by: **A cloak** that is **exempt** from ritual fringes? **If we say** that it is referring to a cloak **that is not of the size** necessary to require the affixing of ritual fringes, that is difficult: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a cloak that** is large enough for **a minor** to **cover his head and most of his** body **with** it,

Rabbi Zeira, citing Rav Masnah citing Shmuel, teaches: *tekelet* carries no *shatnez* problem — even on an exempt *tallis*. This is surprising, since everything discussed previously assumed that the exemption from *shatnez* applies only when the garment is obligated and one is properly discharging the *mitzvah*. On an exempt garment, the wool *tekelet* in a linen garment should remain a *shatnez* violation.

The *gemara* first asks: what does Shmuel mean by an "exempt *tallis*"? Perhaps he means one that is too small to be obligated. A *baraita* establishes the minimum size: a garment is obligated in *tzitzit* if it is large enough that a minor can cover the majority of his body with it — head and torso — in the manner of *atifat ha-rosh*, the head-draping style of wearing. If an adult can use it only temporarily, *derech arai*, it is also obligated. But if it is too small for even a minor to cover his majority, it is exempt — even if an adult might use it as a quick covering.

### Obligated vs. Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַגָּדוֹל יוֹצֵא בָּהּ דֶּרֶךְ עֲרַאי – חַיֶּיבֶת בְּצִיצִית, אֵין הַקָּטָן מִתְכַּסֶּה בּוֹ רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהַגָּדוֹל יוֹצֵא בָּהּ עֲרַאי – פְּטוּרָה, וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן כִּלְאַיִם. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: מַאי ״וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן כִּלְאַיִם״? אִילֵּימָא וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן אִיסּוּרָא דְכִלְאַיִם, וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֵין עֲרַאי בְּכִלְאַיִם! וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן סָדִין בְּצִיצִית. אֶלָּא מַאי פְּטוּרָה – הֵטִיל לַמּוּטֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **and an adult goes out** in public **on occasion** while wearing **it,** it is **required** to have **ritual fringes.** But if it is **not** large enough for **a minor** to **cover his head and most of his** body **with** it, then **even if an adult goes out** in public **on occasion** while wearing **it,** it is **exempt** from ritual fringes. **And so too with regard to diverse kinds,** i.e., the prohibition against wearing wool and linen together. **And we discussed it: What** is meant by: **And so too with regard to diverse kinds? If we say** that it means: **And so too with regard to the prohibition of diverse kinds,** that if a minor could not cover the majority of his head and body with it, the prohibition of diverse kinds does not apply, that is difficult: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kilayim* 9:2): **There is no** exemption **with regard to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds** for clothing that an adult would not wear even **occasionally** in public? **And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** in explanation: Rather, the *baraita* means: **And so too with regard to** whether a linen **cloak** is required **to** have **ritual fringes.** If the cloak is large enough for a minor to cover his head and most of his body with it, then it requires ritual fringes, and wearing the garment with the ritual fringes is not a violation of the prohibition of diverse kinds. But if the garment is smaller than that, it is prohibited to place ritual fringes on it, due to the prohibition of diverse kinds. Therefore, one cannot explain Shmuel's statement to mean that a cloak that is exempt from ritual fringes because it is too small is not subject to the prohibition of diverse kinds. **Rather, what** is meant by the statement that a cloak that is **exempt** from ritual fringes is not subject to the prohibition of diverse kinds? It is referring to where one **affixed** ritual fringes **to** a garment that already had ritual fringes **affixed** to it.

The *baraita* adds: *ve-chein l'inyan kilayim* — the same minimum size applies when it comes to *shatnez*. The *gemara* questions what this means. It cannot mean that one only violates the *shatnez* prohibition when wearing a garment of minimum size, since we have an established rule that even temporary use of *shatnez* is prohibited and there is no minimum size for the *issur* of *shatnez* as such. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak clarifies: *ve-chein l'inyan kilayim* refers specifically to *shatnez* in the context of *tzitzit*. If the linen garment is of the minimum obligated size and one places wool *tekelet* in it, there is no *shatnez* violation. But if the garment is below the minimum size and one places wool *tekelet* in it, there is a *shatnez* violation — because the garment is not obligated, and the *mitzvah* does not provide the exemption. This is precisely everything assumed throughout the previous discussion.

So what does Shmuel mean when he says there is no *shatnez* problem even on an exempt *tallis*? The *gemara* resolves this: Shmuel's "exempt *tallis*" refers to the case of *hitil le-muttelet* — placing a fifth set of *tzitzit* on a garment that already has *tzitzit*. In that situation, the garment is already obligated and already has wool *tzitzit* on it; adding another wool bundle does not constitute a new *shatnez* situation — it is already *shatnez* in the sense that wool and linen are combined, and that combination is already being sanctioned by the *mitzvah*. No additional *shatnez* concern arises from the extra bundle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַמְרַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא? חֲדָא מִכְּלָל דַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rabbi Zeira** already **say this one time** when he stated that if one attached ritual fringes to a garment that already had ritual fringes attached to it and he then removed the first set of strings, the garment is fit? The Gemara answers: **One was stated from the other by inference,** and Rabbi Zeira did not state both statements.

The *gemara* then asks: since Rav Zeira had already implied this once before — by ruling that placing *tzitzit* on an already-equipped garment and then removing the originals renders the new ones valid — was Shmuel's teaching not already redundant? The *gemara* answers: *chada miklal dechaverta* — he stated one of them explicitly, and the other was derived from it. The more explicit ruling appears to have been the *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* analysis, from which the absence of *shatnez* concern follows. Rashi and *Tosafot* debate which was stated and which was derived.

## Chovas Gavra/Mana

### Folded Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: טַלִּית כְּפוּלָה חַיֶּיבֶת בְּצִיצִית, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאִם כְּפָלָהּ וּתְפָרָהּ – שֶׁחַיֶּיבֶת. תְּפָרָהּ, פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּנַקְטַהּ בְּסִיכֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: A very long **cloak that is folded** in half is **required** to have **ritual fringes** at the fold. **And Rabbi Shimon deems** it **exempt** from ritual fringes at the fold because that is not where the corners of the garment are located. **And** Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis **agree that if he folded it and sewed it,** it is **required** to have ritual fringes at the fold. The Gemara challenges: If one **sewed it,** it is **obvious** that it is required to have ritual fringes at the fold. The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* because it is referring to a case **where he fastened it with pins** rather than sewing it in the conventional manner.

The *gemara* now presents the *mishnah*: a doubled-over *tallis* — one folded in half lengthwise so that what were the edges now become the current hem — is obligated in *tzitzit*, and the *tzitzit* are placed at the folded edges. Rabbi Shimon exempts such a garment, apparently because he does not view the folding as truly redefining the shape of the garment: neither the original natural edge nor the folded edge is unambiguously "the corner," and therefore neither location is clearly obligated. All agree, however, that if one folded and also sewed the fold, the garment is obligated at the folded edge. The *gemara* initially asks: sewing is obvious — of course sewing changes the shape. The *gemara* responds that the case requires specification: it refers not to sewing with thread but to securing the fold with fasteners, like pins, which is enough to redefine the garment's shape.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר הוּנָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רָבָא בַּר רַב נַחְמָן, חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה מִיכַּסֵּי טַלִּית כְּפוּלָה, וּרְמֵי לֵיהּ חוּטֵי עִילָּוֵי כְּפֵילָא. אִיפְּשִׁיטָא, וַאֲתָא חוּטָא וְקָם לַהֲדֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו הַיְינוּ כָּנָף דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! אֲתָא שַׁדְיַיהּ, אִיכַּסִּי גְּלִימָא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabba bar Huna arrived at the house of Rava bar Rav Naḥman,** and **he saw that** Rava bar Rav Naḥman **was wearing a cloak that was folded and** that he had **affixed strings to it on** the corners of **the fold.** The cloak **unfolded, and the string** that had been on the corner of the fold now **came and settled near his head,** i.e., in the middle of the cloak, as the two sides of the cloak were in the front and back of Rava bar Rav Naḥman. Rabba bar Huna **said to** Rava bar Rav Naḥman: **This is not** the **corner** of the garment **that the Merciful One writes** about **in the Torah.** Rava bar Rav Naḥman **went** and **threw it off,** and **he covered himself** with **a different cloak.**

Rabba bar Huna once visited the house of Rava bar Rav Nachman and observed that he was wearing a folded *tallis* with *tzitzit* placed at the fold, in the manner of the first opinion. The garment was worn like a tunic with both edges of the fold hanging down on each side of the body. Then Rava bar Rav Nachman unfolded it — and now the *tzitzit*, which had been at the fold, were sitting at his neck, not at the hem at all. Rabba bar Huna told him: that is not the *knaf* that the Torah meant; you now have the *tzitzit* in the middle of the garment, not at the edge. Rava bar Rav Nachman immediately removed the garment and put on a different one with properly placed *tzitzit*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ חוֹבַת גַּבְרָא הוּא? חוֹבַת טַלִּית הוּא, זִיל רְמִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Huna **said to** Rava bar Rav Naḥman: **Do you hold** that ritual fringes are **an obligation** incumbent upon **the man?** That is not so. Rather, **it is an obligation** that pertains to every **cloak** that one owns. Therefore, **go** and **affix** ritual fringes **to it** properly.

But Rabba bar Huna was not finished. *Mi savras chovas gavra hu? Chovas tallis hu.* Do you think this is an obligation on the person, dependent on whether you are wearing it? It is an obligation on the garment. Therefore the fact that you removed it changes nothing — you still have a four-cornered garment without *tzitzit* in the right place. Go put *tzitzit* on it.

### Angel's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָרְגוּ בָּהּ שָׁלֹשׁ – הָיוּ מְטִילִין בָּהּ תְּכֵלֶת. שָׁנֵי חֲסִידִים, דְּמַחְמְרִי אַנַּפְשַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that a *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rabba bar Huna: It is told of **the early** generations of **pious** men that **once they weaved three** fingerbreadths **of** the length of the garment, **they would affix** the white and **sky-blue** strings **to** the first two corners, even though the garment was not yet ready to be worn. This seems to prove that there is an obligation to affix ritual fringes to all the cloaks in one's possession, even if he is not currently wearing them. The Gemara rejects this proof: The **pious** men were **different, as they would act stringently with themselves.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּמַלְאֲכָא, דְּמַלְאֲכָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב קַטִּינָא דְּמִיכַּסֵּי סְדִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַטִּינָא, קַטִּינָא! סְדִינָא בְּקַיְיטָא וְסַרְבָּלָא בְּסִיתְוָא, צִיצִית שֶׁל תְּכֵלֶת מָה תְּהֵא עָלֶיהָ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this **disagrees with** what **an angel** said. **As an angel found Rav Ketina when he was wearing** a linen **cloak,** which is exempt from ritual fringes. The angel **said to him: Ketina, Ketina,** if you wear a linen **cloak in the summer and a coat [*sarbela*],** which has only two corners and is therefore also exempt from ritual fringes, **in the winter, what will become of** the **ritual fringes of sky-blue wool?** As a result, you will never fulfill the mitzva.

This raises the central question of *chovas gavra* versus *chovas mana* — is *tzitzit* a personal obligation triggered by wearing the garment, or is it a status obligation on the garment itself, analogous to *mezuzah* on a house? The *gemara* brings the famous story of Rav Katina. An angel found Rav Katina wearing a linen garment — *sadina b'kayta* — and wearing a round cloak without four corners in the winter. The angel challenged him: what is going to be with the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*? In the summer you wear linen and (per the Rabbinical concern about *shatnez*) do not place *tzitzit*; in the winter you wear a garment without four corners. You never fulfill *tzitzit*. Our text reads *tzitzit shel tekelet ma tehei aleha*, which appears to be a gloss clarifying that you never fully fulfill the *mitzvah* with *tekelet* either. *Tosafot* holds the original text was simply *tzitzit ma tehei aleha*, and for *Tosafot* the passage is evidence that the Rabbinic enactment forbade placing *tzitzit* at all on a linen garment, not merely *tekelet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עָנְשִׁיתוּ אַעֲשֵׂה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּזְמַן דְּאִיכָּא רִיתְחָא עָנְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ketina **said to him: Do you punish** us even **for** failing to fulfill **a positive mitzva?** The angel **said to him: At a time when there is** divine **anger** and judgment, **we punish** even for the failure to fulfill a positive mitzva.

Rav Katina responded: *anishat a'asei* — am I being punished for failing to perform a positive commandment? As *Tosafot* notes, this is not a claim that one can never be compelled to perform a *mitzvas asei* — courts do in fact compel the taking of *lulav* and sitting in a *sukkah*. Rather, his point is that he did not place himself in a situation of obligation. No one said he had to wear a four-cornered garment in order to be obligated in *tzitzit*. He was merely avoiding the circumstance, like someone who never eats bread in order to avoid the obligation of *birkat ha-mazon*. He was not transgressing anything directly. The angel replied: *be-idan de-ika ritcha anshinan* — when there is divine anger, yes, even the scheming to avoid obligation can bring consequences.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חוֹבַת גַּבְרָא הוּא – הַיְינוּ דְּמִחַיַּיב דְּלָא קָא רָמֵי, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ חוֹבַת טַלִּית הוּא – הָא לָא מִיחַיְּיבָא! אֶלָּא מַאי חוֹבַת גַּבְרָא הוּא? נְהִי דְּחַיְּיבֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא כִּי מִיכַּסֵּי טַלִּית דְּבַת חִיּוּבָא, כִּי מִיכַּסֵּי טַלִּית דְּלָאו בַּת חִיּוּבָא הִיא, מִי חַיְּיבֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: טַצְדְּקֵי לְמִיפְטַר נַפְשָׁךְ מִצִּיצִית?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to draw conclusions from the statement of the angel: **Granted, if you say** that the mitzva of ritual fringes **is an obligation** incumbent upon the **man, that is** why Rav Ketina would be **deemed liable** at a time of divine anger, **as he did not affix** ritual fringes to his cloak and thereby neglected the obligation incumbent upon him. **But if you say** that it **is an obligation** to attach them to every **cloak** that one owns, since Rav Ketina's cloaks were not required to have ritual fringes, **he was not obligated** to attach ritual fringes to them. Why should he be punished in a time of divine anger? **Rather, what** should one assume, that **it is an obligation** incumbent upon the **man?** Even so, **granted that the Merciful One rendered him obligated when he is wearing a cloak that** has four corners and is therefore **subject to the obligation** of ritual fringes, but **when he is wearing a cloak that is not subject to the obligation** of ritual fringes, **did the Merciful One deem him obligated?** **Rather, this** is what the angel **is saying to** Rav Ketina: Are you seeking **ploys [*tatzdeki*] to exempt yourself from** performing the mitzva of **ritual fringes?**

The *gemara* now integrates this narrative into the *chovas gavra / chovas mana* debate. *Im amrat bishlama chovas gavra hu* — if we say the obligation is personal, dependent on wearing the garment, then the angel's critique makes perfect sense: Rav Katina was never placing himself in a situation where he personally bore the obligation. He was held accountable for engineering his way out of it. But *ela im amrat chovas tallis hu* — if the obligation is on the garment itself, then Rav Katina never owned four-cornered garments, and the obligation simply never arose. One does not critique a person for not owning a house and therefore not having a *mezuzah*.

However, the *gemara* immediately complicates this. Even granting that it is *chovas gavra*, why does it matter whether you focus on wearing the garment or owning it? The Torah obligates you when you wear an obligated garment — *nahi de-chiyyvei rachmana ki michasei talis de-bar chiyuva*. But when you wear a garment that is not obligated, the Torah does not obligate you either. So the angel's critique would seem equally weak under *chovas gavra* as under *chovas mana*: whether the focus is on garments in your drawer or garments you are wearing, if Rav Katina was never in the circumstances of obligation, he was never violating anything.

The *gemara* therefore resolves that the angel was saying something simpler: *taztaki le-miftar nafshach mi-tzitzit* — are you scheming to exempt yourself from *tzitzit*? The point is not that he violated an obligation, but that he engineered his life to avoid ever being obligated at all, and for that calculated avoidance he was held to account. Whether the obligation is *chovas gavra* or *chovas mana*, the critique of scheming applies. There is nonetheless a view — debated between Rashi and *Tosafot* — as to whether the force of the angel's critique still resonates more powerfully under a *chovas gavra* framework, where the question of what you are actively wearing feels more immediate than the static status of what sits in your closet.

### Drawer Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּלֵי קוּפְסָא חַיָּיבִין בְּצִיצִית, וּמוֹדֶה שְׁמוּאֵל בְּזָקֵן שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ לִכְבוֹדוֹ שֶׁפְּטוּרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אֲשֶׁר תְּכַסֶּה בָּהּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, הַאי לָאו לְאִיכַּסּוֹיֵי עֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Tovi bar Kisna says** that **Shmuel says: Garments** that are not being worn but are stored in **a box are required** to have **ritual fringes,** because the mitzva pertains to the garment, not the man. **But Shmuel concedes in** the case of **an old man, where** the garment **was made** as a shroud **in his honor, that** the shroud is **exempt. What is the reason** for this? **The Merciful One states** in the Torah that one must place ritual fringes on the corners of garments **"with which you cover yourself"** (Deuteronomy 22:12). **This** shroud **is not made for** the purpose of **covering** oneself.

Rav Tivi bar Kisnai in the name of Shmuel rules explicitly: *klei kufsa chayavin be-tzitzit* — garments in your drawer are obligated in *tzitzit*. This is the clearest possible statement of *chovas mana*: the obligation is on the garment, not conditioned on the wearing of it.

### Shrouds Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא, וַדַּאי רָמֵינַן לֵיהּ, מִשּׁוּם ״לֹעֵג לָרָשׁ חֵרֵף עֹשֵׂהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **At that time,** i.e., a person's burial, **we certainly affix** ritual fringes **to** the shroud, **because** otherwise it would be a violation of: **"Whoever mocks the poor blasphemes his Maker"** (Proverbs 17:5).

Shmuel nonetheless agrees that if an elderly person prepared a garment for his honor — a euphemism for shrouds — it is exempt. The reason: the Torah says *asher techaseh bah*, that you cover yourself with it, and shrouds are not made for covering oneself in the normal living sense. The *gemara* then wonders: at the moment the shroud is actually placed on the deceased, surely then it is being used to cover him — why not place *tzitzit* at that point? The answer is *mishum lo'eg la-rash choreif osehu*: one who mocks a poor person blasphemes God. The deceased is "poor" in *mitzvot* — he has entered a world where he can no longer perform them. Sending a person to burial in a four-cornered garment without *tzitzit* would mock his new incapacity for *mitzvot*. Therefore, paradoxically, we do place *tzitzit* on the shroud — not because it is obligated, but to avoid this mockery. The irony is complete: the garment is exempt during the person's lifetime because it is designated for death; and *tzitzit* are placed on it at death not as an obligation but to honor the deceased.

*Tosafot* notes that the custom in *Ashkenaz* was in fact the reverse: to tear the *tzitzit* off the *tallis* in which a person was buried before lowering him into the ground. This likely reflects a different *minhag* that traveled from *Eretz Yisrael* to *Ashkenaz*, in contrast to the *Bavel* practice reflected in the *gemara*.

## Transfer & Tearing

### Sewing Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲבָה אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: טַלִּית שֶׁנִּקְרְעָה חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ – יִתְפּוֹר, תּוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ – לֹא יִתְפּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Raḥava says** that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In the case of **a cloak that became torn** at one of its corners, if it was torn **beyond three** fingerbreadths from the edge of the garment, one **may sew** it. But if it was torn **within three** fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment, then one **may not sew** it. There is a concern that he might use the thread with which he sewed the garment for the ritual fringes, in which case the strings would be unfit due to the principle: Prepare it, and not from what has already been prepared.

Rachava citing Rav Yehuda teaches: if a garment tore, the rule for sewing it depends on where the tear occurred. If it tore outside of three fingerbreadths from the corner, one may sew it up. If within three fingerbreadths, one may not. Following *Tosafot*'s explanation: if the corner with *tzitzit* tore off at a point within three fingerbreadths of the edge, the torn-off piece is too small to have independent garment-status; it is as though the *tzitzit* were removed from the garment entirely. If one then sews the corner back on, the *tzitzit* are now sitting on a complete garment without having been placed there — they were placed on what is now effectively no garment, and their presence on the restored garment comes about automatically. That is *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*. But if the tear occurred more than three fingerbreadths in, the corner piece is large enough to retain some independent garment-status; it is seen as still meaningfully connected to the fabric. Sewing it back is a restoration, not a fresh placement, and there is no *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* problem.

### Rabbi Meir Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: טַלִּית שֶׁנִּקְרְעָה, חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ – יִתְפּוֹר, תּוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִתְפּוֹר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יִתְפּוֹר. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא אֲפִילּוּ אַמָּה עַל אַמָּה מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר וּבָהּ תְּכֵלֶת, וְתוֹלֶה בָּהּ. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁמֵּבִיא תְּכֵלֶת מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר וְתוֹלֶה בָּהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a cloak that became torn** at one of its corners, if it was torn **beyond three** fingerbreadths from the edge of the garment, one **may sew** it. But if it was torn **within three** fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment, **Rabbi Meir says:** One **may not sew** it. **And the Rabbis say:** One **may sew** it. **And** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **agree that one may not bring** a piece of cloth, **even** if it is **a square cubit** in size, **from elsewhere, containing** white and **sky-blue** strings, **and attach** it **to** a cloak. **And** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis also **agree that one may bring** white or **sky-blue** strings **from elsewhere and attach** them **to** the garment, i.e., one may remove strings from one garment in order to attach them to another garment.

A *baraita* confirms this ruling with a nuance. If torn outside of three fingerbreadths, one may sew it back — that is consistent. If torn within three fingerbreadths: Rabbi Meir says one may not sew it up, while the *chachamim* say one may. The *chachamim* apparently hold that the act of sewing the corner back on is itself considered an act of *asiyah* — it is as though one is placing *tzitzit* by restoring the corner. This would mean the *chachamim* are not troubled by *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* in this case. Both Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* agree, however, on one thing: one may not take a corner from a completely different garment — even one amounting to a full cubit by cubit — that already has *tzitzit* on it, and sew it onto the edge of a different garment. That is *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*: the *tzitzit* on the new composite garment were not placed on that garment; they were placed on a different garment and are now there automatically. The allowance to restore applies only when the same garment is being reconstituted, not when pieces from entirely different garments are being combined.

### Remove & Replace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא מוּפְסֶקֶת. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ מַתִּירִין מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד? דִּילְמָא דְּאִי בְּלַאי.
>
> **Translation:** This is permitted **provided that** the strings **are not broken.** The Gemara asks: Should one **conclude from** this *baraita* that it is always permitted to **untie** ritual fringes **from** one **garment** in order to affix them **to** another **garment?** The Gemara rejects this inference: **Perhaps** the ruling of this *baraita* applies only **if** the first garment was **worn out** and no longer wearable.

Both parties agree, however, that one may take the *tzitzit* strings themselves — not the fabric — from one *tallis* and place them on another. The *gemara* draws from this: *shema mina matirin mi'beged le'beged* — one may remove *tzitzit* from one garment and place them on another. The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps this is only permitted when garment A has worn out and is no longer usable. Then salvaging the *tzitzit* and placing them on a functioning garment B is permitted. But it is not clear that one may remove *tzitzit* from an intact, usable garment A and transfer them to garment B.

### Blue Garment Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: טַלִּית שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ תְּכֵלֶת – כׇּל מִינֵי צִבְעוֹנִין פּוֹטְרִין בָּהּ, חוּץ מִקָּלָא אִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a cloak that** is made **entirely** of **sky-blue wool,** strings of **every type of color exempt it,** i.e., the ritual fringes that are not *tekhelet* may be any color **except for indigo,** a color that is indistinguishable from *tekhelet*. This indicates that if one attached strings dyed with indigo alongside the strings dyed with *tekhelet*, the ritual fringes are unfit.

Before resolving this, the *gemara* introduces a further discussion. *Tanu rabanan*: a *tallis* that is entirely blue — all *tekelet* colored — can use any color for the non-*tekelet* strings, the strings of the *knaf*, except for *kala ilan* (indigo), which is the same hue as *tekelet* but not valid *tekelet*. The reason: on a non-blue garment, the *tzitzit* of the *knaf* should match the garment color; but on an all-blue garment they cannot match, since matching would make them indistinguishable from *tekelet*. The restriction on *kala ilan* specifically is because it looks identical to *tekelet* and might be confused with it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: טַלִּית אֵין פּוֹטֵר בָּהּ אֶלָּא מִינָהּ. טַלִּית שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ תְּכֵלֶת – מֵבִיא תְּכֵלֶת וְדָבָר אַחֵר וְתוֹלֶה בָּהּ, וְקָלָא אִילָן לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּטַלִּית בַּת אַרְבָּעָה חוּטִין, כָּאן בְּטַלִּית בַּת שְׁמוֹנָה חוּטִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A cloak** is **exempted only by** strings of **its own type.** In the case of **a cloak that** is made **entirely** of **sky-blue wool,** one **brings sky-blue [*tekhelet*]** strings **and something else,** i.e., strings of a different color, **and attaches** them **to** the cloak. **And he may not bring** strings dyed with **indigo** along with the strings dyed with *tekhelet*. **But if he brought** strings dyed with indigo together with the strings dyed with *tekhelet*, the ritual fringes are **fit.** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This is **not difficult,** because **here,** in the *baraita* that holds that the ritual fringes are fit after the fact, it is referring **to a cloak that has** only **four strings,** two of *tekhelet* and two of indigo. **There,** in the *baraita* that holds that the ritual fringes are unfit after the fact, it is referring **to a cloak that has eight strings,** four of *tekhelet* and four of indigo. In this case, the Sages were concerned that one would take the four indigo strings from this garment and use them in another garment, thinking that they were *tekhelet*.

A *baraita* is cited that partially conflicts: it says that if one uses *kala ilan* on such a garment, *bedi'avad* it is valid — whereas the earlier ruling implied it was invalid even *bedi'avad*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: the invalidity applies when there are four strings and the wrong colors are used in the basic set. The leniency *bedi'avad* applies when the original four strings were done correctly — including proper *tekelet* and a legitimate other color — and one then added four additional strings of *kala ilan*. One should not do so *lechatchila*, both because of concerns related to *bal tosif* and because one might come to take those *kala ilan* strings and use them on another garment thinking them to be valid *tekelet*. But if done, the *tzitzit* are valid *bedi'avad* since the original four strings are proper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ מַתִּירִין מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד? דִּלְמָא דְּאִי עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Should **you conclude from** the fact that the Sages were concerned lest one take the indigo strings from this garment for use in another garment that in general **one may untie** ritual fringes **from** one **garment** in order **to** affix them to another **garment?** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps** their concern was **that if** one **did** transfer the strings, he might mistake indigo for *tekhelet*, but it is not permitted to transfer the strings *ab initio*.

The *gemara* then says: *shema mina* from this ruling — since the concern is that one might take these extra strings and transfer them to another garment, this implies that transferring *tzitzit* between garments is something people actually do and is in principle permitted. The *gemara* immediately qualifies: perhaps it is not permitted, and the concern is only that someone might do it improperly anyway — removing the *kala ilan* strings and using them on another garment while incorrectly believing that garment now has valid *tekelet*.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב אָמַר: אֵין מַתִּירִין מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַתִּירִין מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד. רַב אָמַר: אֵין מַדְלִיקִין מִנֵּר לְנֵר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַדְלִיקִין מִנֵּר לְנֵר. רַב אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּגְרִירָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּגְרִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is a dispute between *amora'im* with regard to this *halakha*. **Rav says: One may not untie** ritual fringes **from** one **garment** in order **to** affix them to another **garment. And Shmuel says: One may untie** them **from** one **garment** and affix them **to** another **garment.** The Gemara cites additional disputes between Rav and Shmuel: **Rav says: One may not light from** one Hanukkah **lamp to** another Hanukkah **lamp. And Shmuel says: One may light from** one Hanukkah **lamp to** another Hanukkah **lamp.** Rabbi Shimon rules that it is permitted to drag items on Shabbat despite the possibility that one might thereby create a furrow in the ground. Creating a furrow is a labor prohibited on Shabbat, but since he does not intend to create the furrow, and it is not certain that a furrow will be created, dragging the item is permitted. **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to dragging** items on Shabbat. **And Shmuel says** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to dragging** items on Shabbat.

The debate is now stated explicitly. Rav holds: *ein matirin mi'beged le'beged* — one may not remove *tzitzit* from one garment and place them on another. Shmuel holds: *matirin mi'beged le'beged* — one may. The *gemara* lists two additional disputes between Rav and Shmuel in the same context: whether one may light one *Chanukah* candle from another (*ner le-ner*), and whether the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon in the case of *gerirah* — dragging a bed or bench across a field on *Shabbat* without intending to make a furrow, which Rabbi Shimon permits on the grounds of *davar she'eino mitkavein*.

### Abaye Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כֹּל מִילֵּי דְּמָר עָבֵיד כְּרַב, לְבַר מֵהָנֵי תְּלָת דְּעָבֵיד כִּשְׁמוּאֵל: מַתִּירִין מִבֶּגֶד לְבֶגֶד, וּמַדְלִיקִין מִנֵּר לְנֵר, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּגְרִירָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: גּוֹרֵר אָדָם מִטָּה, כִּסֵּא וְסַפְסָל, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לַעֲשׂוֹת חָרִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In **all** halakhic **matters of the Master,** Rabba, **he conducted himself in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav, except these three where he conducted himself in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel.** He ruled that **one may untie** ritual fringes **from** one **garment** in order to affix them **to** another **garment, and one may light from** one Hanukkah **lamp to** another Hanukkah **lamp, and** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to dragging** items on Shabbat. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: A person may drag a bed, chair, or bench** on the ground on Shabbat, **provided that he does not intend to make a furrow** in the ground.

Abaye summarizes: in all matters, Rabba acted like Rav — except in these three, where he acted like Shmuel: *matirin mi'beged le'beged* (one may transfer *tzitzit* between garments); *madlikin mi'ner le'ner* (one may light one *Chanukah* candle from another); and *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon be-gerirah* (we rule like Rabbi Shimon regarding dragging). A supporting *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Shimon says a person may drag a bed, chair, or bench in a field, provided he does not intend to make a furrow — and this is the principle of *davar she'eino mitkavein*.

## Laundering Concern

### Guard Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה מָסַר לֵיהּ לְקַצָּרָא, רַב חֲנִינָא עָבֵיד לֵהּ סִיסָא, רָבִינָא חָיֵיט לְהוּ מֵיחָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Yehuda** would **give** his garments containing ritual fringes **to a laundryman** and was not concerned that the strings dyed with *tekhelet* might become detached and that the laundryman would replace them with strings dyed with indigo. **Rav Ḥanina** would **form a bundle** with his ritual fringes so that they would not become detached while they were being laundered. **Ravina** would tuck **them** into a pocket he formed on the garment and **sew** the cover of the pocket in order to protect the ritual fringes.

A brief but practical note: Rav Yehuda would hand his garment over to a launderer without concern for the *tzitzit* strings being damaged. Rav Chanina, by contrast, would bundle the strings up within the garment itself before laundering, in order to protect them from the wash.

## Strings & Ratio

### String Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כַּמָּה חוּטִין הוּא נוֹתֵן? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אַרְבָּעָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How many strings does one place** on a garment? **Beit Shammai say: Four** strings are inserted into the hole in the garment, so that there are eight strings hanging down altogether, **and Beit Hillel say: Three** strings are inserted into the garment.

*Tanu rabanan*: how many strings does one place? *Beit Shammai* says four — which is indeed the practice, four strings folded over to make eight. *Beit Hillel* says three.

### Binding Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה תְּהֵא מְשׁוּלֶּשֶׁת? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אַרְבַּע, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שָׁלֹשׁ. וְשָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁבֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים, אַחַת מֵאַרְבַּע בְּטֶפַח שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: טֶפַח דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אַרְבַּע בְּגוּדָל, שֵׁית בִּקְטַנָּה, חֲמֵשׁ בְּתִילְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And how much should be hanging [*meshulleshet*]** beyond the knots and windings? **Beit Shammai say: Four** fingerbreadths, **and Beit Hillel say: Three** fingerbreadths. **And** the **three** fingerbreadths **that Beit Hillel say** should be hanging are each **one-fourth of a handbreadth [*tefaḥ*] of any** average **person.** The Gemara notes that **Rav Pappa said:** The **handbreadth of the Torah** is **four** fingerbreadths if measured **by** the **thumb; six** fingerbreadths if measured **by** the **smallest** finger; and **five** if measured **by** the **third,** i.e., the middle, finger.

How long must the *meshuleshet* — the wrapped, knotted portion — be relative to the free-flowing strings? *Beit Shammai* says four fingerbreadths and *Beit Hillel* says three. *Tosafot* understands this to mean that the wrapped-and-knotted section is four fingerbreadths (*Beit Shammai*) or three fingerbreadths (*Beit Hillel*), with the free-flowing strings being double that length — eight or six fingerbreadths respectively — based on the principle that the *gedil* constitutes one-third and the free strings two-thirds of the total length. The *baraita* then specifies that the three fingerbreadths of *Beit Hillel* are measured as one-quarter of a *tefach*, i.e., three standard finger widths with four fingers to a *tefach*, of average adult size.

Rav Pappa clarifies the measurement of a *tefach*: by Torah standards, a *tefach* equals four thumb widths, or six pinky widths, or five index finger widths — all equivalent in actual measurement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אַרְבָּעָה בְּתוֹךְ אַרְבַּע, וּמְשׁוּלֶּשֶׁת אַרְבַּע, וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ, מְשׁוּלֶּשֶׁת שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says** that the *halakha* is: One must attach **four** strings **within four** fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment, **and** they should **hang** down **four** fingerbreadths beyond the knots and windings. **And Rav Yehuda says:** One must attach **three** strings **within three** fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment, and they should **hang** down **three** fingerbreadths beyond the knots and windings.

Rav Huna rules: four strings, within four fingerbreadths of the edge, with the knotted portion being four fingerbreadths. Rav Yehuda rules: three strings, within three fingerbreadths, with the knotted portion being three fingerbreadths.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הִלְכְתָא: אַרְבָּעָה בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ, מְשׁוּלֶּשֶׁת אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says** that **the *halakha*** is that one must attach **four** strings **within three** fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment, and they should **hang** down **four** fingerbreadths beyond the knots and windings.

Rav Pappa rules: the *halacha* is four strings, within three fingerbreadths of the edge, with the knotted portion being four fingerbreadths. This ruling — four strings within three fingerbreadths — connects to the three-fingerbreadth rule that has run throughout the entire discussion, from the tearing case to the placement rules.

### Max vs. Min

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּאִית לְהוּ שִׁיעוּרָא, וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״צִיצִית״ – אֵין צִיצִית אֶלָּא יוֹצֵא, וְאֵין צִיצִית אֶלָּא מַשֶּׁהוּ, וּכְבָר עָלוּ זִקְנֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְזִקְנֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל לַעֲלִיַּית יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּתִירָא, וְאָמְרוּ: צִיצִית אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר; כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, לוּלָב אֵין בּוֹ שִׁיעוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** ritual fringes **have** a required **measure? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this from a *baraita*: The verse states: "That they prepare for themselves **strings"** (Numbers 15:38). **Strings** are **nothing other than** what **emerges** from the corner of the garment, **and** the term **strings** indicates **only** that there must be strings of **any length. And** it **already** occurred that **the elders of Beit Shammai and the elders of Beit Hillel ascended to the attic of Yoḥanan ben Beteira, and** they discussed the matter and **said: Ritual fringes have no measure. Similarly, a *lulav* has no measure.**

The *gemara* then asks: does this imply there is a fixed *shiur* for how long the free-flowing strings must be? A *baraita* is cited: *ein tzitzit ela yotzei* — *tzitzit* means what flows out, like *tzitzit rosho*, the locks of the hair. And yet: *ein tzitzit ela mashehu* — even the smallest amount suffices. Furthermore, the elders of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* once gathered in the attic of Yochanan ben Beteira and declared: *tzitzit ein la shiur* — there is no minimum measure for *tzitzit*. Similarly, *lulav ein lo shiur* — there is no minimum measure for the *lulav*.

### No Maximum

The Sefaria text continues from the previous blockquote and provides the resolution in the following daf. Looking at Menachot 42a, the explanation continues:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר לְמַעְלָה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהּ שִׁיעוּר לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** the *baraita* means that ritual fringes **do not have a maximum measure,** i.e., the strings can be as long as one wants; **however, they do have a minimum measure.**

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *mai lav ein la shiur klal* does not mean there is no *shiur* at all. Rather, *ein la shiur le-mala* — there is no maximum length. But *yesh la shiur le-mata* — there is a minimum. The minimum is indeed the four-fingerbreadth requirement discussed throughout. The same applies to *lulav*: when the tradition says there is no *shiur* for *lulav*, it means no maximum — but there is indeed a minimum, as the *mishnah* establishes three *tefachim* as the minimum size for a valid *lulav*. What *afilu mashehu* means, per Rashi, is that if the strings were cut short after the fact and only a tiny remnant remains (*gardumim*), that remnant may still be valid *bedi'avad*.

## Tzitzit Meaning

### Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״צִיצִת״ – אֵין צִיצִית אֶלָּא עָנָף, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּקָּחֵנִי בְּצִיצִת רֹאשִׁי״, וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְצָרִיךְ לְפָרוֹדַהּ כִּי צוּצִיתָא דְּאַרְמָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "That they prepare for themselves **strings"** (Numbers 15:38). The term **strings [*tzitzit*]** means **nothing other than strings that hang down [*anaf*], and so it states** in the verse: **"I was taken by a lock [*betzitzit*] of my head"** (Ezekiel 8:3). **And Abaye says: And** one is **required to separate** the ritual fringes **like a gentile's lock** of hair, part of which is braided and the rest of which is allowed to hang loose.

*Tanu rabanan*: *ein tzitzit ela anaf* — *tzitzit* refers specifically to the free-flowing, branch-like strands. This is confirmed by the verse *va-yikacheini be-tzitzit roshi* (Ezekiel 8:3) — "He took me by the strands of my hair." The *gedil* is the wrapped, knotted portion; the *tzitzit* is the flowing part that hangs free.

### Strand Separation

The section on strand separation is part of the same baraita quoted above, so it continues without a separate blockquote:

Abaye adds: *ve-tzarich lefiroda ke-tzutzita de-Armaye* — the strands of the *tzitzit* must be kept separated from one another, not allowed to clump together. The model is the flowing, separated hair style familiar from non-Jewish practice — a style where a braided or bound section gives way to individually flowing strands. The *gedil* is the bound portion, and the *tzitzit* must emerge from it as clearly distinct, separated strands.

## Placement Rules

### Corner Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הֵטִיל עַל הַקֶּרֶן, אוֹ עַל הַגְּדִיל – כְּשֵׁירָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב (אוֹמֵר) פּוֹסֵל בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one **affixed** the ritual fringes **to** the tip of **the corner or to the border [*gadil*],** they are **fit. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov disqualifies** them **in both** cases.

*Tanu rabanan*: if one placed *tzitzit* right at the corner itself or right at the outer edge of the garment, it is valid. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says it is invalid in both cases — neither right at the outermost tip of the corner nor right at the edge.

### Dropping Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: צִיצִית צְרִיכָה שֶׁתְּהֵא נוֹטֶפֶת עַל הַקֶּרֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כַּנְפֵי בִגְדֵיהֶם״, כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: Ritual fringes must** be inserted into a hole above the corner and **hang down onto the corner** of the garment, **as it is stated: "On the corners of their garments"** (Numbers 15:38)? **In accordance with whose** opinion is this? The Gemara answers: It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.**

Rav Giddel in the name of Rav teaches: *tzitzit tzericha she-tehei notefet al ha-karn* — the *tzitzit* must drop from the corner. Not affixed right at the edge, but placed a bit in from the edge on both sides so that the strings drop downward from the corner, as is the familiar practice. The verse *al kanfei bigdeihem* is cited in support. This ruling aligns with the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov.

### Min Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּרְחִיק מְלֹא קֶשֶׁר גּוּדָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And** one **must distance** the hole through which the ritual fringes are inserted into the garment the length of **a full thumb joint** from the edge of the garment.

Rav Yaakov citing Rav Yochanan: the *tzitzit* must be placed no closer than *melo kesher godel* from the edge — the width of a thumb joint, measured from knuckle to fingertip. This is the minimum distance from the edge.

### Max Distance

The maximum distance has already been established in a previous blockquote as part of Rav Pappa's ruling. It is presented here for completeness in the essay context:

Rav Pappa's ruling — that the *tzitzit* must be within three fingerbreadths of the edge — establishes the maximum distance.

### Both Rules Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ דְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב, דְּאִי מִדְּרַב פָּפָּא הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: תּוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ דְּלָא לַירְחֵיק טְפֵי, וְכַמָּה דִּמְקָרַב מְעַלֵּי, אִיצְטְרִיךְ דְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. וְאִי מִדְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מְלֹא קֶשֶׁר גּוּדָל, דְּלָא לִיקְרַב טְפֵי, וְכַמָּה דְּרַחִיק מְעַלֵּי, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And it was necessary** to state the ruling **of Rav Pappa** (41b) that the ritual fringes must be inserted into a hole within three fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment, **and it was** also **necessary** to state the ruling **of Rabbi Ya'akov.** This is **because if** the location of the hole was taught only **from** the statement **of Rav Pappa, I would say** that his ruling that the hole must be **within three** fingerbreadths of the edge of the garment was to teach **that one may not distance** the hole from the edge of the garment by **more** than this amount, **but the closer** the hole is to the edge of the garment, **the better.** Consequently, **it was necessary** to include the statement **of Rabbi Ya'akov.** **And if** the location of the hole was taught only **from** the statement **of Rabbi Ya'akov, I would say** that his ruling that it must be **a full thumb joint** away from the edge of the garment was to teach **that one may not** situate the hole **closer** than that to the edge of the garment, **but the further** he places it, **the better.** Therefore, both statements were **necessary.**

The *gemara* explains why both rulings are necessary. Had we only Rav Pappa's teaching, we would know the maximum distance — within three fingerbreadths — but might have thought that the closer to the edge the better, and perhaps one should place it right at the edge. Rav Yaakov's teaching corrects this by establishing a minimum distance. Conversely, had we only Rav Yaakov's teaching, we would know that there must be at least a thumb-joint's width of distance from the edge — but we might have thought there is no maximum, and one could place the *tzitzit* anywhere far from the corner. Rav Pappa's ruling establishes the outer limit of three fingerbreadths. Both rulings are thus indispensable.

## Ravina's Garment

### Placement vs. Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא וְרַב סַמָּא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, חַזְיֵיהּ רַב סַמָּא לְקַרְנֵיהּ דִּגְלִימֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא דִּסְתַר וּבְצַר מִמְּלֹא קֶשֶׁר גּוּדָל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁעַת עֲשִׂיָּיה אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Ravina and Rav Samma were sitting before Rav Ashi. Rav Samma saw that the corner of Ravina's cloak was torn and** therefore the hole through which the ritual fringes were inserted was **less than** the **full** length of **a thumb joint** from the edge of the garment. Rav Samma **said to** Ravina: **Doesn't the Master hold in accordance with that** statement **of Rabbi Ya'akov** that the hole must be at least the length of a thumb joint from the edge of the garment? Ravina **said to** Rav Samma: It **was stated** that this distance is required **at the time** when the ritual fringes are **made.** If the corner tears later, causing the hole to be closer to the edge of the garment, the ritual fringes remain fit.

Ravina and Rav Sama were sitting before Rav Ashi. Rav Sama noticed that the corner of Ravina's garment was fraying — or, as Rashi suggests, the hole through which the *tzitzit* passed had enlarged — so that the *tzitzit* now sat within the thumb-joint width of the edge, violating the minimum distance requirement. Rav Sama asked: does the master not hold by the ruling of Rabbi Yaakov? Ravina replied: *bish'as asiyah itamar* — that requirement was stated for the moment of initial placement. If after the *tzitzit* were properly placed the garment changed — the hole grew larger, the edge frayed shorter — that is not a problem. Rav Sama was embarrassed, having thought he was correcting his colleague only to learn that he himself had misunderstood the ruling.

### Rav Ashi Consolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכְּסִיף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא תִּתְּקִיף לָךְ, חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ כִּתְרֵי מִינַּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Samma **became embarrassed** because he had asked his question based on a mistaken assumption. **Rav Ashi said to** Rav Samma: **Do not be upset** that Ravina is a greater scholar than you are; **one of them,** i.e., the Sages of Eretz Yisrael, **is like two of us,** i.e., the Sages of Babylonia.

Rav Ashi consoled him: *lo tatkif lach da* — do not take it so hard. *Chad minaihu ke-terei minan*: one scholar from *Eretz Yisrael* is the equivalent of two from *Bavel*. Of course Ravina's command of the *halacha* would exceed what Rav Sama expected. Whether or not that consolation fully eased the embarrassment, the daf ends on this note.
