# Menachot 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shatnez in Tzitzit

### Tekelet in Linen

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained discussion about *shatnez* and *tzitzit*, and specifically the case of a linen garment: does one place *tzitzit* on it, and does one include *tekelet*? *Beit Shammai* says no; *Beit Hillel* says yes in principle. Yet even according to *Beit Hillel*, the *gemara* concluded that in practice the answer is also no. The reasoning comes down to two concerns: first, that people might use the wrong color and thereby fail to discharge the *mitzvah* of *tekelet*; and second, a concern that the garment might tear and be sewn back up in a way that would render the *tzitzit* invalid as *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* — and once the *mitzvah* is not properly discharged, the wearer is in fact violating *shatnez*. *Tosafot* reads that *sugya* as prohibiting not only *tekelet* but *tzitzit* entirely on such a garment, a point that will resurface later.

### Tearing Risk

The *gemara* picks up with Rava's teaching — presented in his own name and corroborated by a parallel tradition from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Zeira — that when a garment tears within three fingerbreadths of the corner and is then sewn back up, the *tzitzit* that were already attached at that corner become invalid as *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*: the corner was reattached while the *tzitzit* were already on it, rather than the *tzitzit* being placed on an already-complete obligated garment. Because one is not discharging the *mitzvah*, the *shatnez* of the wool *tekelet* in a linen garment remains a violation.

### Night Garments

Rav Zeira — who appears to be a different figure from Rabbi Zeira — offers an additional concern. Even setting aside the question of validity, there is a worry that a person will place *tzitzit* with *tekelet* on his daytime linen garment and then come to place them on a nighttime garment — pajamas, as it were — which is designated exclusively for night and is therefore not obligated in *tzitzit*. On such an exempt garment, the wool *tekelet* together with the linen would constitute *shatnez* without the protective exemption of *mitzvah* performance. Because of this concern, the Rabbis refrained from permitting it even on the obligated daytime garment.

### Garment vs. Corner

Rava then echoes the teaching cited in *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Zeira: if the garment itself is of an obligated material but the corners are leather — leather being exempt from *tzitzit* — the garment is nonetheless *chayav*, since we follow the *ikkar beged*, the primary material of the garment, not the corner. Conversely, if the garment is leather but the corners are cloth, it is exempt for the same reason. Rav Achai takes the opposite position, holding that since the verse says *al ha-knaf petil tekelet*, the obligation and its conditions are determined by the nature of the *knaf*, the corner, not the garment body as a whole.

## Ta'aseh V'lo Min

### Three to Four Corners

Rava, citing Rav Sechora citing Rav Huna, rules: if one placed *tzitzit* on a three-cornered garment — meaning a garment that had only three corners and was not yet obligated — and then added a fourth corner to complete it, the *tzitzit* are invalid on account of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*. When the *tzitzit* were placed, the garment was exempt; when the garment became obligated, the *tzitzit* were already there without having been actively placed on an obligated garment. The principle parallels the requirement of *gedilim ta'aseh lecha* — you must actively make it — analogous to the requirement that a *sukkah* be actively constructed and not come about automatically.

### Chasidim Early Placement

The *gemara* raises a challenge from the practice of the *chasidim ha-rishonim*: as soon as they had woven three fingerbreadths of their garment, they would place *tzitzit* on it — seemingly before the garment was fully obligated. This appears to contradict the concern for *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*. The *gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting the tradition: read not as "when they began the first three fingerbreadths," but rather "when they came within the last three fingerbreadths of finishing the second edge." At that point the garment had already reached its full obligated length, and the *tzitzit* were being placed on an already-obligated garment. There is a separate point that the *tzitzit* should not be placed right at the very edge — as we will see later — but *bedi'avad* that may not be disqualifying, and the *chasidim* placed their *tzitzit* within those final three fingerbreadths specifically so that when the last strip of weaving was complete, the *tzitzit* would sit within the valid zone from the edge. In any event, the tradition of the *chasidim* does not undermine the rule of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*.

### Extra Tzitzit

The *gemara* presses further: are we actually strict about *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* when it comes to *tzitzit*? Rav Zeira taught: if one placed an additional set of *tzitzit* — a fifth bundle — on a corner of a garment that already had *tzitzit*, and then removed the original *tzitzit*, the garment is *keshera*. Now this is precisely the situation of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* in reverse: you placed the new *tzitzit* while the garment already had *tzitzit* satisfying its obligation, and only after cutting off the originals did the new ones become the operative ones. That seems like they "arrived" automatically rather than being placed on an obligated garment at the moment of need.

Rava addresses this. Since when you placed that fifth bundle of *tzitzit* you were transgressing *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to the *mitzvot*, such as placing five *tzitzit* on a garment that requires only four — the act itself was as if no act occurred, *ma'ase lo havei*. Therefore, when you cut off the original *tzitzit*, that is in effect the moment of "making" — the act of placement — for the new *tzitzit*. This is not a case of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*.

It must be said that this reasoning is genuinely difficult. Rashi's formulation is that if you place *tzitzit* on an exempt garment, you have performed an act — just not a valid one — and when the garment subsequently becomes obligated, the act is already spent. Whereas when you transgress *bal tosif*, since the act was forbidden, it is as though no act was performed at all, and cutting the original *tzitzit* constitutes the act of making. Yet one might argue precisely the reverse: when a garment is not obligated, the act is entirely without weight, more akin to no act at all; but when a garment is obligated and one places an additional *tzitzit* in violation of *bal tosif*, the very fact that one is transgressing *bal tosif* demonstrates that the act carries real halachic weight — it just carries it in the wrong direction. Indeed, one might have argued that this is exactly a case of *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* averted: the garment was obligated, and you actively placed *tzitzit* on it. And conversely, when a garment was exempt and subsequently became obligated, *that* is where the *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* problem genuinely lies. Rava's answer runs in the opposite direction, and it remains, at least on the surface, difficult to fully understand.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

Rav Pappa challenges not the principle but its application: how can we be certain that the person placing the fifth *tzitzit* was intending to *add* to the existing *tzitzit* — which would make it *bal tosif* — rather than intending to *replace* the existing ones, i.e., he planned all along to remove the originals? If he was intending to negate the originals, then *bal tosif* does not apply, because in his mind the existing *tzitzit* were already as good as gone. But in that case, his act of placing the new *tzitzit* was a real act — just not yet a valid one. And when he cuts the originals, the new *tzitzit* automatically become operative without having been placed at the moment of obligation. That would be a *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* problem. The upshot is that Rav Zeira's ruling that such *tzitzit* are valid cannot be demonstrated as cleanly as Rava's argument suggested, and the underlying question is left without a fully satisfying resolution.

## Shmuel: No Shatnez

### Size Requirement

Rabbi Zeira, citing Rav Masnah citing Shmuel, teaches: *tekelet* carries no *shatnez* problem — even on an exempt *tallis*. This is surprising, since everything discussed previously assumed that the exemption from *shatnez* applies only when the garment is obligated and one is properly discharging the *mitzvah*. On an exempt garment, the wool *tekelet* in a linen garment should remain a *shatnez* violation.

The *gemara* first asks: what does Shmuel mean by an "exempt *tallis*"? Perhaps he means one that is too small to be obligated. A *baraita* establishes the minimum size: a garment is obligated in *tzitzit* if it is large enough that a minor can cover the majority of his body with it — head and torso — in the manner of *atifat ha-rosh*, the head-draping style of wearing. If an adult can use it only temporarily, *derech arai*, it is also obligated. But if it is too small for even a minor to cover his majority, it is exempt — even if an adult might use it as a quick covering.

### Obligated vs. Exempt

The *baraita* adds: *ve-chein l'inyan kilayim* — the same minimum size applies when it comes to *shatnez*. The *gemara* questions what this means. It cannot mean that one only violates the *shatnez* prohibition when wearing a garment of minimum size, since we have an established rule that even temporary use of *shatnez* is prohibited and there is no minimum size for the *issur* of *shatnez* as such. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak clarifies: *ve-chein l'inyan kilayim* refers specifically to *shatnez* in the context of *tzitzit*. If the linen garment is of the minimum obligated size and one places wool *tekelet* in it, there is no *shatnez* violation. But if the garment is below the minimum size and one places wool *tekelet* in it, there is a *shatnez* violation — because the garment is not obligated, and the *mitzvah* does not provide the exemption. This is precisely everything assumed throughout the previous discussion.

So what does Shmuel mean when he says there is no *shatnez* problem even on an exempt *tallis*? The *gemara* resolves this: Shmuel's "exempt *tallis*" refers to the case of *hitil le-muttelet* — placing a fifth set of *tzitzit* on a garment that already has *tzitzit*. In that situation, the garment is already obligated and already has wool *tzitzit* on it; adding another wool bundle does not constitute a new *shatnez* situation — it is already *shatnez* in the sense that wool and linen are combined, and that combination is already being sanctioned by the *mitzvah*. No additional *shatnez* concern arises from the extra bundle.

The *gemara* then asks: since Rav Zeira had already implied this once before — by ruling that placing *tzitzit* on an already-equipped garment and then removing the originals renders the new ones valid — was Shmuel's teaching not already redundant? The *gemara* answers: *chada miklal dechaverta* — he stated one of them explicitly, and the other was derived from it. The more explicit ruling appears to have been the *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* analysis, from which the absence of *shatnez* concern follows. Rashi and *Tosafot* debate which was stated and which was derived.

## Chovas Gavra/Mana

### Folded Garment

The *gemara* now presents the *mishnah*: a doubled-over *tallis* — one folded in half lengthwise so that what were the edges now become the current hem — is obligated in *tzitzit*, and the *tzitzit* are placed at the folded edges. Rabbi Shimon exempts such a garment, apparently because he does not view the folding as truly redefining the shape of the garment: neither the original natural edge nor the folded edge is unambiguously "the corner," and therefore neither location is clearly obligated. All agree, however, that if one folded and also sewed the fold, the garment is obligated at the folded edge. The *gemara* initially asks: sewing is obvious — of course sewing changes the shape. The *gemara* responds that the case requires specification: it refers not to sewing with thread but to securing the fold with fasteners, like pins, which is enough to redefine the garment's shape.

Rabba bar Huna once visited the house of Rava bar Rav Nachman and observed that he was wearing a folded *tallis* with *tzitzit* placed at the fold, in the manner of the first opinion. The garment was worn like a tunic with both edges of the fold hanging down on each side of the body. Then Rava bar Rav Nachman unfolded it — and now the *tzitzit*, which had been at the fold, were sitting at his neck, not at the hem at all. Rabba bar Huna told him: that is not the *knaf* that the Torah meant; you now have the *tzitzit* in the middle of the garment, not at the edge. Rava bar Rav Nachman immediately removed the garment and put on a different one with properly placed *tzitzit*.

But Rabba bar Huna was not finished. *Mi savras chovas gavra hu? Chovas tallis hu.* Do you think this is an obligation on the person, dependent on whether you are wearing it? It is an obligation on the garment. Therefore the fact that you removed it changes nothing — you still have a four-cornered garment without *tzitzit* in the right place. Go put *tzitzit* on it.

### Angel's Critique

This raises the central question of *chovas gavra* versus *chovas mana* — is *tzitzit* a personal obligation triggered by wearing the garment, or is it a status obligation on the garment itself, analogous to *mezuzah* on a house? The *gemara* brings the famous story of Rav Katina. An angel found Rav Katina wearing a linen garment — *sadina b'kayta* — and wearing a round cloak without four corners in the winter. The angel challenged him: what is going to be with the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*? In the summer you wear linen and (per the Rabbinical concern about *shatnez*) do not place *tzitzit*; in the winter you wear a garment without four corners. You never fulfill *tzitzit*. Our text reads *tzitzit shel tekelet ma tehei aleha*, which appears to be a gloss clarifying that you never fully fulfill the *mitzvah* with *tekelet* either. *Tosafot* holds the original text was simply *tzitzit ma tehei aleha*, and for *Tosafot* the passage is evidence that the Rabbinic enactment forbade placing *tzitzit* at all on a linen garment, not merely *tekelet*.

Rav Katina responded: *anishat a'asei* — am I being punished for failing to perform a positive commandment? As *Tosafot* notes, this is not a claim that one can never be compelled to perform a *mitzvas asei* — courts do in fact compel the taking of *lulav* and sitting in a *sukkah*. Rather, his point is that he did not place himself in a situation of obligation. No one said he had to wear a four-cornered garment in order to be obligated in *tzitzit*. He was merely avoiding the circumstance, like someone who never eats bread in order to avoid the obligation of *birkat ha-mazon*. He was not transgressing anything directly. The angel replied: *be-idan de-ika ritcha anshinan* — when there is divine anger, yes, even the scheming to avoid obligation can bring consequences.

### Scheming Analysis

The *gemara* now integrates this narrative into the *chovas gavra / chovas mana* debate. *Im amrat bishlama chovas gavra hu* — if we say the obligation is personal, dependent on wearing the garment, then the angel's critique makes perfect sense: Rav Katina was never placing himself in a situation where he personally bore the obligation. He was held accountable for engineering his way out of it. But *ela im amrat chovas tallis hu* — if the obligation is on the garment itself, then Rav Katina never owned four-cornered garments, and the obligation simply never arose. One does not critique a person for not owning a house and therefore not having a *mezuzah*.

However, the *gemara* immediately complicates this. Even granting that it is *chovas gavra*, why does it matter whether you focus on wearing the garment or owning it? The Torah obligates you when you wear an obligated garment — *nahi de-chiyyvei rachmana ki michasei talis de-bar chiyuva*. But when you wear a garment that is not obligated, the Torah does not obligate you either. So the angel's critique would seem equally weak under *chovas gavra* as under *chovas mana*: whether the focus is on garments in your drawer or garments you are wearing, if Rav Katina was never in the circumstances of obligation, he was never violating anything.

The *gemara* therefore resolves that the angel was saying something simpler: *taztaki le-miftar nafshach mi-tzitzit* — are you scheming to exempt yourself from *tzitzit*? The point is not that he violated an obligation, but that he engineered his life to avoid ever being obligated at all, and for that calculated avoidance he was held to account. Whether the obligation is *chovas gavra* or *chovas mana*, the critique of scheming applies. There is nonetheless a view — debated between Rashi and *Tosafot* — as to whether the force of the angel's critique still resonates more powerfully under a *chovas gavra* framework, where the question of what you are actively wearing feels more immediate than the static status of what sits in your closet.

### Drawer Garments

Rav Tivi bar Kisnai in the name of Shmuel rules explicitly: *klei kufsa chayavin be-tzitzit* — garments in your drawer are obligated in *tzitzit*. This is the clearest possible statement of *chovas mana*: the obligation is on the garment, not conditioned on the wearing of it.

### Shrouds Exception

Shmuel nonetheless agrees that if an elderly person prepared a garment for his honor — a euphemism for shrouds — it is exempt. The reason: the Torah says *asher techaseh bah*, that you cover yourself with it, and shrouds are not made for covering oneself in the normal living sense. The *gemara* then wonders: at the moment the shroud is actually placed on the deceased, surely then it is being used to cover him — why not place *tzitzit* at that point? The answer is *mishum lo'eg la-rash choreif osehu*: one who mocks a poor person blasphemes God. The deceased is "poor" in *mitzvot* — he has entered a world where he can no longer perform them. Sending a person to burial in a four-cornered garment without *tzitzit* would mock his new incapacity for *mitzvot*. Therefore, paradoxically, we do place *tzitzit* on the shroud — not because it is obligated, but to avoid this mockery. The irony is complete: the garment is exempt during the person's lifetime because it is designated for death; and *tzitzit* are placed on it at death not as an obligation but to honor the deceased.

*Tosafot* notes that the custom in *Ashkenaz* was in fact the reverse: to tear the *tzitzit* off the *tallis* in which a person was buried before lowering him into the ground. This likely reflects a different *minhag* that traveled from *Eretz Yisrael* to *Ashkenaz*, in contrast to the *Bavel* practice reflected in the *gemara*.

## Transfer & Tearing

### Sewing Rules

Rachava citing Rav Yehuda teaches: if a garment tore, the rule for sewing it depends on where the tear occurred. If it tore outside of three fingerbreadths from the corner, one may sew it up. If within three fingerbreadths, one may not. Following *Tosafot*'s explanation: if the corner with *tzitzit* tore off at a point within three fingerbreadths of the edge, the torn-off piece is too small to have independent garment-status; it is as though the *tzitzit* were removed from the garment entirely. If one then sews the corner back on, the *tzitzit* are now sitting on a complete garment without having been placed there — they were placed on what is now effectively no garment, and their presence on the restored garment comes about automatically. That is *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*. But if the tear occurred more than three fingerbreadths in, the corner piece is large enough to retain some independent garment-status; it is seen as still meaningfully connected to the fabric. Sewing it back is a restoration, not a fresh placement, and there is no *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* problem.

### Rabbi Meir Dispute

A *baraita* confirms this ruling with a nuance. If torn outside of three fingerbreadths, one may sew it back — that is consistent. If torn within three fingerbreadths: Rabbi Meir says one may not sew it up, while the *chachamim* say one may. The *chachamim* apparently hold that the act of sewing the corner back on is itself considered an act of *asiyah* — it is as though one is placing *tzitzit* by restoring the corner. This would mean the *chachamim* are not troubled by *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui* in this case. Both Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* agree, however, on one thing: one may not take a corner from a completely different garment — even one amounting to a full cubit by cubit — that already has *tzitzit* on it, and sew it onto the edge of a different garment. That is *ta'aseh ve-lo min ha'asui*: the *tzitzit* on the new composite garment were not placed on that garment; they were placed on a different garment and are now there automatically. The allowance to restore applies only when the same garment is being reconstituted, not when pieces from entirely different garments are being combined.

### Remove & Replace

Both parties agree, however, that one may take the *tzitzit* strings themselves — not the fabric — from one *tallis* and place them on another. The *gemara* draws from this: *shema mina matirin mi'beged le'beged* — one may remove *tzitzit* from one garment and place them on another. The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps this is only permitted when garment A has worn out and is no longer usable. Then salvaging the *tzitzit* and placing them on a functioning garment B is permitted. But it is not clear that one may remove *tzitzit* from an intact, usable garment A and transfer them to garment B.

Before resolving this, the *gemara* introduces a further discussion. *Tanu rabanan*: a *tallis* that is entirely blue — all *tekelet* colored — can use any color for the non-*tekelet* strings, the strings of the *knaf*, except for *kala ilan* (indigo), which is the same hue as *tekelet* but not valid *tekelet*. The reason: on a non-blue garment, the *tzitzit* of the *knaf* should match the garment color; but on an all-blue garment they cannot match, since matching would make them indistinguishable from *tekelet*. The restriction on *kala ilan* specifically is because it looks identical to *tekelet* and might be confused with it.

A *baraita* is cited that partially conflicts: it says that if one uses *kala ilan* on such a garment, *bedi'avad* it is valid — whereas the earlier ruling implied it was invalid even *bedi'avad*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: the invalidity applies when there are four strings and the wrong colors are used in the basic set. The leniency *bedi'avad* applies when the original four strings were done correctly — including proper *tekelet* and a legitimate other color — and one then added four additional strings of *kala ilan*. One should not do so *lechatchila*, both because of concerns related to *bal tosif* and because one might come to take those *kala ilan* strings and use them on another garment thinking them to be valid *tekelet*. But if done, the *tzitzit* are valid *bedi'avad* since the original four strings are proper.

The *gemara* then says: *shema mina* from this ruling — since the concern is that one might take these extra strings and transfer them to another garment, this implies that transferring *tzitzit* between garments is something people actually do and is in principle permitted. The *gemara* immediately qualifies: perhaps it is not permitted, and the concern is only that someone might do it improperly anyway — removing the *kala ilan* strings and using them on another garment while incorrectly believing that garment now has valid *tekelet*.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The debate is now stated explicitly. Rav holds: *ein matirin mi'beged le'beged* — one may not remove *tzitzit* from one garment and place them on another. Shmuel holds: *matirin mi'beged le'beged* — one may. The *gemara* lists two additional disputes between Rav and Shmuel in the same context: whether one may light one *Chanukah* candle from another (*ner le-ner*), and whether the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon in the case of *gerirah* — dragging a bed or bench across a field on *Shabbat* without intending to make a furrow, which Rabbi Shimon permits on the grounds of *davar she'eino mitkavein*.

### Abaye Summary

Abaye summarizes: in all matters, Rabba acted like Rav — except in these three, where he acted like Shmuel: *matirin mi'beged le'beged* (one may transfer *tzitzit* between garments); *madlikin mi'ner le'ner* (one may light one *Chanukah* candle from another); and *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon be-gerirah* (we rule like Rabbi Shimon regarding dragging). A supporting *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Shimon says a person may drag a bed, chair, or bench in a field, provided he does not intend to make a furrow — and this is the principle of *davar she'eino mitkavein*.

## Laundering Concern

### Guard Methods

A brief but practical note: Rav Yehuda would hand his garment over to a launderer without concern for the *tzitzit* strings being damaged. Rav Chanina, by contrast, would bundle the strings up within the garment itself before laundering, in order to protect them from the wash.

## Strings & Ratio

### String Count

*Tanu rabanan*: how many strings does one place? *Beit Shammai* says four — which is indeed the practice, four strings folded over to make eight. *Beit Hillel* says three.

### Binding Measure

How long must the *meshuleshet* — the wrapped, knotted portion — be relative to the free-flowing strings? *Beit Shammai* says four fingerbreadths and *Beit Hillel* says three. *Tosafot* understands this to mean that the wrapped-and-knotted section is four fingerbreadths (*Beit Shammai*) or three fingerbreadths (*Beit Hillel*), with the free-flowing strings being double that length — eight or six fingerbreadths respectively — based on the principle that the *gedil* constitutes one-third and the free strings two-thirds of the total length. The *baraita* then specifies that the three fingerbreadths of *Beit Hillel* are measured as one-quarter of a *tefach*, i.e., three standard finger widths with four fingers to a *tefach*, of average adult size.

Rav Pappa clarifies the measurement of a *tefach*: by Torah standards, a *tefach* equals four thumb widths, or six pinky widths, or five index finger widths — all equivalent in actual measurement.

Rav Huna rules: four strings, within four fingerbreadths of the edge, with the knotted portion being four fingerbreadths. Rav Yehuda rules: three strings, within three fingerbreadths, with the knotted portion being three fingerbreadths.

### Max vs. Min

Rav Pappa rules: the *halacha* is four strings, within three fingerbreadths of the edge, with the knotted portion being four fingerbreadths. This ruling — four strings within three fingerbreadths — connects to the three-fingerbreadth rule that has run throughout the entire discussion, from the tearing case to the placement rules.

The *gemara* then asks: does this imply there is a fixed *shiur* for how long the free-flowing strings must be? A *baraita* is cited: *ein tzitzit ela yotzei* — *tzitzit* means what flows out, like *tzitzit rosho*, the locks of the hair. And yet: *ein tzitzit ela mashehu* — even the smallest amount suffices. Furthermore, the elders of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* once gathered in the attic of Yochanan ben Beteira and declared: *tzitzit ein la shiur* — there is no minimum measure for *tzitzit*. Similarly, *lulav ein lo shiur* — there is no minimum measure for the *lulav*.

### No Maximum

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *mai lav ein la shiur klal* does not mean there is no *shiur* at all. Rather, *ein la shiur le-mala* — there is no maximum length. But *yesh la shiur le-mata* — there is a minimum. The minimum is indeed the four-fingerbreadth requirement discussed throughout. The same applies to *lulav*: when the tradition says there is no *shiur* for *lulav*, it means no maximum — but there is indeed a minimum, as the *mishnah* establishes three *tefachim* as the minimum size for a valid *lulav*. What *afilu mashehu* means, per Rashi, is that if the strings were cut short after the fact and only a tiny remnant remains (*gardumim*), that remnant may still be valid *bedi'avad*.

## Tzitzit Meaning

### Etymology

*Tanu rabanan*: *ein tzitzit ela anaf* — *tzitzit* refers specifically to the free-flowing, branch-like strands. This is confirmed by the verse *va-yikacheini be-tzitzit roshi* (Ezekiel 8:3) — "He took me by the strands of my hair." The *gedil* is the wrapped, knotted portion; the *tzitzit* is the flowing part that hangs free.

### Strand Separation

Abaye adds: *ve-tzarich lefiroda ke-tzutzita de-Armaye* — the strands of the *tzitzit* must be kept separated from one another, not allowed to clump together. The model is the flowing, separated hair style familiar from non-Jewish practice — a style where a braided or bound section gives way to individually flowing strands. The *gedil* is the bound portion, and the *tzitzit* must emerge from it as clearly distinct, separated strands.

## Placement Rules

### Corner Placement

*Tanu rabanan*: if one placed *tzitzit* right at the corner itself or right at the outer edge of the garment, it is valid. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says it is invalid in both cases — neither right at the outermost tip of the corner nor right at the edge.

### Dropping Requirement

Rav Giddel in the name of Rav teaches: *tzitzit tzericha she-tehei notefet al ha-karn* — the *tzitzit* must drop from the corner. Not affixed right at the edge, but placed a bit in from the edge on both sides so that the strings drop downward from the corner, as is the familiar practice. The verse *al kanfei bigdeihem* is cited in support. This ruling aligns with the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov.

### Min Distance

Rav Yaakov citing Rav Yochanan: the *tzitzit* must be placed no closer than *melo kesher godel* from the edge — the width of a thumb joint, measured from knuckle to fingertip. This is the minimum distance from the edge.

### Max Distance

Rav Pappa's ruling — that the *tzitzit* must be within three fingerbreadths of the edge — establishes the maximum distance.

### Both Rules Needed

The *gemara* explains why both rulings are necessary. Had we only Rav Pappa's teaching, we would know the maximum distance — within three fingerbreadths — but might have thought that the closer to the edge the better, and perhaps one should place it right at the edge. Rav Yaakov's teaching corrects this by establishing a minimum distance. Conversely, had we only Rav Yaakov's teaching, we would know that there must be at least a thumb-joint's width of distance from the edge — but we might have thought there is no maximum, and one could place the *tzitzit* anywhere far from the corner. Rav Pappa's ruling establishes the outer limit of three fingerbreadths. Both rulings are thus indispensable.

## Ravina's Garment

### Placement vs. Change

Ravina and Rav Sama were sitting before Rav Ashi. Rav Sama noticed that the corner of Ravina's garment was fraying — or, as Rashi suggests, the hole through which the *tzitzit* passed had enlarged — so that the *tzitzit* now sat within the thumb-joint width of the edge, violating the minimum distance requirement. Rav Sama asked: does the master not hold by the ruling of Rabbi Yaakov? Ravina replied: *bish'as asiyah itamar* — that requirement was stated for the moment of initial placement. If after the *tzitzit* were properly placed the garment changed — the hole grew larger, the edge frayed shorter — that is not a problem. Rav Sama was embarrassed, having thought he was correcting his colleague only to learn that he himself had misunderstood the ruling.

### Rav Ashi Consolation

Rav Ashi consoled him: *lo tatkif lach da* — do not take it so hard. *Chad minaihu ke-terei minan*: one scholar from *Eretz Yisrael* is the equivalent of two from *Bavel*. Of course Ravina's command of the *halacha* would exceed what Rav Sama expected. Whether or not that consolation fully eased the embarrassment, the daf ends on this note.
