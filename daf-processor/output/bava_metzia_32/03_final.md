# Bava Metzia 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Aveida Parameters

### Three Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי דְּאִיתֵאּ לְמָרַהּ בַּהֲדַהּ, אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **but** in the case of **these two** mitzvot of unloading and loading, **where its owner is with it,** I might **say no,** there is no need to assist him. Therefore, it was **necessary** for the Torah to write both.

The daf officially begins at the top of 31b, where the Gemara is in the midst of developing the core parameters of *aveida*. The Gemara's *drasha* on the phrase *ve-hisalamta mei-hem* — the verse that commands one to "ignore" the lost object under certain conditions — yields three categories of exception. The first is dignity: just as one may prioritize one's own property over another's, one may prioritize one's own dignity over another's financial loss, and need not return an object when doing so would be beneath one's station. The second is the prohibition against violating another *halacha* in the course of returning a lost object — the paradigmatic case being a *kohen* who would have to enter a cemetery, which will appear explicitly in a later *mishna*. The third exception, which actually appears first in the *braisa* but arose first in the *mishna* as well, is that one need not return a lost object if doing so would cause oneself greater financial loss in the process.

### Aveidat Karka

A conceptually significant extension that emerged in the preceding Gemara is the application of *hashavat aveida* to *aveidat karka* — preventing damage to land. This represents a double abstraction: from movable property to immovable property, and from the act of returning an object to its owner to the more general obligation of preventing loss. That broader framing becomes critical when the Gemara moves to compare *aveida* with *prika u-teina*, since both seem, at the most abstract level, to be expressions of the same principle — protecting another person's property.

### Aveida & Prika U-Teina

This raises an obvious question: if both *aveida* and *prika u-teina* reduce to the same underlying concern of preventing property loss, why does the Torah need to legislate both separately? The Gemara addresses this by pointing out that the two *mitzvot* operate in genuinely distinct ways. In the case of unloading a struggling animal, the elevated obligation is driven partly by *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — the suffering of the animal — a factor absent in the ordinary *aveida* context. Conversely, in the *aveida* case, one dimension of the obligation is heightened by the fact that the owner is not present and cannot protect the object himself. The various *psukim* are thus teaching the scope of the obligation to protect another's property across a range of distinct scenarios, each with its own configuration of factors.

## Doubling Language

### Mos Yumat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מוֹת יוּמַת הַמַּכֶּה״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּמִיתָה הַכְּתוּבָה בּוֹ. מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לַהֲמִיתוֹ בְּמִיתָה הַכְּתוּבָה בּוֹ שֶׁאַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לַהֲמִיתוֹ בְּכׇל מִיתָה שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לַהֲמִיתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מוֹת יוּמַת״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional derivations from compound verb forms. "Or in enmity struck him with his hand, that he died; **he that struck him shall be put to death [*mot yumat*]"** (Numbers 35:21). **I have** derived **only** that the murderer is executed **with** the form of **death written with regard to him,** i.e., decapitation. **From where** is it derived **that if you are unable to execute him with** the form of **death written with regard to him, it is permitted for you to execute him with any death** with **which you are able to execute him? The verse states: "*Mot yumat*,"** to teach that you must execute him **in any case.**

The Gemara continues its survey of doubled verbal constructions in the Torah and what each instance comes to teach. The passage *mos yumat ha-makeh* — "the murderer shall surely be put to death" — cannot mean that the execution must be carried out multiple times, since one execution suffices. Instead, the Gemara reads the doubling as teaching: *ein li ela be-misah ha-ksuvah bo* — I know only that the murderer must be executed by the specific mode of execution prescribed for him, such as by the sword. But what if circumstances prevent implementation of that prescribed method — for instance, if the condemned man has escaped and the only way to stop him is by some other means? The double language — *mos yumat mi-kol makom* — authorizes execution by whatever means are available. This applies specifically after a *gmar din* has been issued, not during investigation or in the context of a pursuer trying to commit murder; those are governed by separate *halachot*.

### Hakei Takeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֵּה תַכֶּה״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּהַכָּאָה הַכְּתוּבָה בָּהֶן, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לַהֲמִיתָן בְּהַכָּאָה הַכְּתוּבָה בָּהֶן שֶׁאַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לְהַכּוֹתָן בְּכׇל הַכָּאָה שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַכֵּה תַכֶּה״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an idolatrous city, it is written: **"You shall strike [*hakeh takeh*]** the inhabitants of that city by sword, destroying it utterly" (Deuteronomy 13:16). **I have** derived **only** that the residents of the idolatrous city are executed **with** the form of **death written with regard to them,** i.e., decapitation. **From where** is it derived **that if you are unable to execute them with** the form of **death written with regard to them, it is permitted for you to execute them with any death** with **which you are able to execute them? The verse states: "*Hakeh takeh*,"** to teach that you must execute him **in any case.**

A parallel case: the verse commanding the smiting of the inhabitants of the *ir hanidachat* prescribes death by the sword. The doubling of *hakei takeh* teaches that if the prescribed mode of execution cannot be carried out, any other form of execution is permitted — *mi-kol makom*, under all circumstances.

### Hashev Tashiv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָשֵׁב תָּשִׁיב״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת בֵּית דִּין. מִשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת בֵּית דִּין, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הָשֵׁב תָּשִׁיב״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an item that a poor person needs, e.g., a blanket, that a lender took as collateral when lending him money, it is written: **"You shall restore [*hashev tashiv*]** to him the pledge when the sun goes down, that he may sleep in his garment, and bless you; and it shall be righteousness for you before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 24:13). **I have** derived **only** the obligation to return his garment each night in a case where the lender **took collateral with the sanction of the court. From where** do I derive the obligation to return his garment each night even in a case where the lender **took collateral without the sanction of the court? The verse states: "*Hashev tashiv*,"** to teach that he must return it **in any case.**

Returning now to a case directly relevant to *aveida*: the doubled form *hashev tashiv* in the context of returning collateral. The straightforward reading might suggest that the obligation applies only when the lender seized the collateral through a court officer, i.e., *birshut beit din*. The doubling — *hashev tashiv mi-kol makom* — extends the obligation to cover even cases where the collateral was seized through private agreement rather than by court order.

### Chavol Tachbol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חָבֹל תַּחְבֹּל״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת. מִשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חָבֹל תַּחְבֹּל״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְהָנֵי תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי לְמָה לִי? חַד לִכְסוּת יוֹם, וְחַד לִכְסוּת לַיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara brings another derivation from a compound verb written with regard to returning collateral: "If you **take as collateral [*ḥavol taḥbol*]** your neighbor's garment, you shall restore it to him until the sun sets" (Exodus 22:25). **I have** derived **only** the obligation to return his garment before sunset in a case where the lender **took collateral with the sanction of the court. From where** do I derive the obligation to return his garment each night even in a case where the lender **took collateral without the sanction of the court? The verse states: "*Ḥavol taḥbol*,"** to teach that he must return it **in any case.** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **these two verses, why do I** need both of them to teach the same *halakha*, that one must return to the debtor any garment that he needs? The Gemara answers: **One** is referring **to a garment** worn during the **day, and one** is referring **to a garment** worn during the **night** (see 114b).

The phrase *im chavol tachbol salmat re'akha* — "if you take your neighbor's garment as collateral" — also employs doubled language. One might initially ask why the Torah uses this construction here at all, and then a further question arises: why are there actually two separate *psukim* about collateral, one in Shemot 24 and one in Shemot 25? The answer is that one verse addresses *ksut yom* — a daytime garment that must be returned during the day — and the other addresses *ksut laila* — a nighttime garment that must be returned at night. The doubled language within each verse extends the obligation to cases where the collateral was seized without any authorization whatsoever — not merely without a court officer but even where the lender broke into the debtor's home and seized an item unilaterally. One might have thought that such an illegitimate seizure falls outside the Torah's protective framework, or conversely that the obligation to return might not apply since the original taking was improper. The doubled language — *chavol tachbol mi-kol makom* — establishes the obligation under all circumstances.

### Patoach Tiftach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פָּתֹחַ תִּפְתַּח״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לַעֲנִיֵּי עִירֶךָ. לַעֲנִיֵּי עִיר אַחֶרֶת, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״פָּתֹחַ תִּפְתַּח״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְ״נָתֹן תִּתֵּן״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מַתָּנָה מְרוּבָּה. מַתָּנָה מוּעֶטֶת, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נָתֹן תִּתֵּן״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the mitzva of giving charity and granting loans, it is written: "For the poor shall never cease out of the land; therefore I command you, saying: **You shall open [*patoaḥ tiftaḥ*]** your hand to your poor and needy brother in your land" (Deuteronomy 15:11). **I have** derived **only** the obligation to give charity **to the poor** residents **of your city. From where** is the obligation to give charity **to the poor** residents **of another city** derived? **The verse states: "*Patoaḥ tiftaḥ*,"** to teach that you must give charity to the poor **in any case.** With regard to the mitzva of giving charity it is written: "Beware…and your eye is stingy against your needy brother…**You shall give [*naton titten*]** him, and your heart shall not be grieved when you give unto him" (Deuteronomy 15:9–10). **I have** derived **only** the obligation to give **a large gift. From where** is the obligation to give even **a small gift** derived? **The verse states: "*Naton titten*,"** to teach that one must give gifts **in any case,** whether a large gift or a small one.

The phrase *patoach tiftach et yadekha lo* — "you shall surely open your hand to him" — in the context of giving to the poor. The Gemara first reads these *psukim* through the lens of *tzedaka*, noting that the verse also says *dei machsoro* — as much as he lacks. The doubling teaches, first, that the obligation extends beyond the poor of one's own city (*ani irkha*) to the poor of other cities as well. While those of one's own town take precedence, the obligation itself is not geographically limited. The doubling also addresses the question of partial giving: since the verse says to supply what the person lacks, one might think there is no *mitzvah* to give a small amount if one cannot provide everything that is needed. The doubled form *naton titein mi-kol makom* — give regardless — establishes that even a minimal gift fulfills the *mitzvah*.

### Ha'anek Ta'anik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַעֲנֵיק תַּעֲנִיק״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְבָּרֵךְ הַבַּיִת בִּגְלָלוֹ מַעֲנִיקִין. לֹא נִתְבָּרֵךְ הַבַּיִת בִּגְלָלוֹ, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַעֲנֵיק תַּעֲנִיק״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the release of a Hebrew slave it is written: **"You shall furnish [*ha'aneik ta'anik*]** him liberally from your flock, and from your threshing floor, and from your winepress; of that which the Lord your God has blessed you, you shall give unto him" (Deuteronomy 15:14). Based on the conclusion of the verse, **I have** derived **only** that **when the house is blessed because of him, one furnishes** the slave with gifts. **From where** have I derived the obligation to furnish him with gifts even when **the house is not blessed because of him? The verse states: "*Ha'aneik ta'anik*,"** to teach that one must furnish him with gifts **in any case.**

The doubled phrase *ha'aneik ta'anik lo* governs the *mitzvah* of loading up the freed slave with gifts when he goes free in the seventh year. The end of the verse credits the slave's service with bringing divine blessing to the household — *ki mishneh sechar sachir avadcha shesh shanim u-veirach'cha Hashem Elokecha*. One might therefore think that if the household was not in fact blessed — if the master's business suffered during the years of the slave's service — there is no obligation to provide these gifts. The doubled language — *ha'aneik ta'anik mi-kol makom* — establishes the obligation regardless of whether the household was blessed. The comparison to large bonuses paid even in years when a firm loses money is not inapt.

## Dibra Torah K'Lishon

### R. Elazar b. Azarya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, דְּאָמַר: נִתְבָּרֵךְ הַבַּיִת בִּגְלָלוֹ – מַעֲנִיקִין לוֹ, לֹא נִתְבָּרֵךְ הַבַּיִת בִּגְלָלוֹ – אֵין מַעֲנִיקִין, ״תַּעֲנִיק״ לְמָה לִי? דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who says:** If **the house is blessed because of him, one furnishes him** with gifts, and if **the house is not blessed because of him,** one need **not furnish** him with gifts, **why do I** need a compound verb, "*ha'aneik **ta'anik*****"?** The Gemara answers: **The Torah speaks in the language of people.** The compound verb is a common conversational style, and the Torah employs the same style. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya holds there is nothing extraordinary about it and therefore, nothing may be derived from it.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, however, holds that the obligation to give the departing slave gifts is indeed contingent on the household having been blessed by his service. If the household was not blessed, there is no obligation. This position must therefore explain what work the doubling of *ha'aneik ta'anik* performs in his reading.

### Doubling as Speech

The Gemara's answer for Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya is that the doubled form here teaches nothing additional: *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam* — the Torah sometimes speaks in the ordinary idiom of human language, using emphatic doubling as a stylistic feature rather than as a source of novel law. This principle opens a methodological question that Tosafot addresses directly: how does one determine when a doubled form is *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam* and when it carries halakhic content? Tosafot's answer is that the default assumption is that doubling expands the scope of the *mitzvah*, and only when no plausible expansion can be derived from context do we fall back on *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam*.

## Doubling Language…

### Ha'aveit Ta'avitenu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַעֲבֵט תַּעֲבִיטֶנּוּ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לוֹ וְאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לְהִתְפַּרְנֵס, אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: תֵּן לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ הַלְוָאָה. יֵשׁ לוֹ וְאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לְהִתְפַּרְנֵס, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תַּעֲבִיטֶנּוּ״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the mitzva of lending money to the poor it is written: "But you shall open your hand to him, and **you shall lend [*ha'avet ta'avitennu*]** him sufficient for his need that he is lacking" (Deuteronomy 15:8). **I have** derived **only** that in a case **where one does not have** resources **and does not want to be supported** with charity, **the Merciful One states: Provide** for **him by means of a loan.** In a case where **he has** resources **and he does not want to support himself** with his resources, **from where** is the obligation to lend him money derived? **The verse states: "*Ta'avitennu*,"** to teach that you must grant him a loan **in any case.**

The Gemara returns to the earlier *psukim* about giving to the poor, now focusing specifically on the doubled verb *ha'aveit ta'avitenu* — the root of which connotes collateral and lending — thereby indicating that the *pasuk* is also, or primarily, addressing the obligation to extend a loan. What does the doubling teach here?

The Gemara states: *ein li ela she-ein lo v'eino rotze l'hitparnes* — I know only that the obligation applies when the person has no means of his own and refuses to accept a gift, preferring instead to maintain his dignity by way of a loan. In such a case, the Torah says: give him the loan. This implies, interestingly, that when a person is willing to accept a gift, giving a gift is actually preferable to a loan — a debate addressed more fully in the Gemara in Ketubot 67b for those who wish to pursue it.

But the more pressing question is: what about a person who has money of his own but refuses to use it — *yesh lo v'eino rotze l'hitparnes mishalo ela mishalcha*? Rashi explains the case starkly: the person has assets but refuses to draw on them. One might imagine someone who is, as it were, asset-rich and cash-poor — perhaps unwilling to liquidate a prized possession — and who refuses to accept charity but is willing to accept a loan. Is one obligated to lend money to such a person, or can one say that his refusal to sell his assets is his own affair? The doubled form — *ta'avitenu mi-kol makom* — teaches that one is nonetheless obligated to extend the loan. People have their own particular circumstances and arrangements, and that does not dissolve one's obligation toward them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: יֵשׁ לוֹ וְאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לְהִתְפַּרְנֵס אֵין נִזְקָקִין לוֹ, ״תַּעֲבִיטֶנּוּ״ לְמָה לִי? דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Shimon, who says** that in a case where **he has** resources **and does not want to support himself** with his resources **one is not obligated to attend to his** needs, **why do I** need a doubled verb: "*Ha'avet **ta'avitennu*****"?** The Gemara answers: **The Torah speaks in the language of people** and nothing may be derived from it.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *yesh lo v'eino rotze l'hitparnes — ein nizkakin lo*. If a person actually has money, there is no obligation whatsoever. In his view, the doubled form of *ta'avitennu* teaches nothing, and the ruling is again *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam*.

## Poel Batel Wages

### Poel Batel Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה בָּטֵל מִן הַסֶּלַע, לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ ״תֵּן לִי סֶלַע״, אֶלָּא נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ כְּפוֹעֵל (בָּטֵל). תְּנַן: נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל. מַאי כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ מְלָאכָה, דִּבְטַל מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If in the course of tending to and returning the lost item, the finder **was idle from** labor that would have earned him **a *sela*, he shall not say to** the owner of the item: **Give me a *sela*** to compensate me for my lost income. **Rather,** the owner **gives him his wage as** if he were **a laborer.** The Gemara cites that **we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:11): The owner **gives him his wage as** if he were **an idle laborer.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **As** if he were **an idle laborer?** In fact, he is not idle, but engaged in return of a lost item. **Abaye said:** It means that he is paid **as a laborer** who is **idle from that** typical **labor** of his **from which he is** kept **idle.** In other words, he must receive the amount of money a person would be willing to accept to refrain from his current occupation and engage in returning a lost item. This calculation accounts for both the degree of difficulty of his steady employment and the amount of his remuneration.

The Gemara now turns to the question of reimbursement for returning a lost object. If a person loses time from work in order to return an *aveida*, the owner must compensate him — but not at his full wage rate. The *mishna* states he is paid *ke-schar poel batel* — like the wage of an idle worker. What does this mean?

Abaye explains: *ke-poel batel shel oto melacha de-batel mineih* — like a worker who is taking time off from the specific labor he would otherwise be performing. The key is not what he could earn at that labor, but what he would accept in order to take a break from it.

Rashi elaborates precisely. Consider a quarry worker earning twenty dollars an hour at hard physical labor. If asked how much he would accept in order to take an hour off from that labor and instead do something lighter — such as knocking on doors to find the owner of a lost object — he might well accept five or ten dollars, because the relief from hard labor has its own value. The *poel batel* rate is therefore that reduced figure: the amount a person would willingly accept to stop doing his regular work and do something easier instead. Critically, this rate is not fixed; as Rashi notes, it depends on the nature and difficulty of the original work and the level of compensation it commands.

This produces two asymmetric edge cases. A high-earning professional — say, someone billing at a substantial hourly rate — might find that returning a lost object is actually more demanding or at least no less demanding than his regular work, meaning his *poel batel* rate converges with his ordinary rate. Conversely, a laborer who desperately needs the income might theoretically accept a reduced rate but cannot actually afford to do so. These are the cases in which, as the *mishna* requires, one must stipulate the terms before a *beit din*.

### Beit Din Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם בֵּית דִּין – מַתְנֶה בִּפְנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If there are** three men **there** who can convene as **a court,** he **may stipulate before** the court that he will undertake to return the item provided that he receives full compensation for lost income.

The *mishna* states: *im yesh sham beit din matneh bifneihem* — if there is a court present, the finder stipulates the terms of reimbursement before them. The *beit din* in this context stands in for the interest of the owner of the lost object, approving the terms of compensation when the owner is not available to consent himself. The *beit din*'s precise role — whether it simply provides a formal stamp of approval or actually adjudicates what level of compensation is reasonable relative to the value of the object — is itself a matter for further analysis.

### Issur & Safra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר וְרַב סָפְרָא עֲבֻיד עִיסְקָא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. אֲזַל רַב סָפְרָא פְּלַג לֵיהּ בְּלָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּאִיסּוּר בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי תְרֵי. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי תְּלָתָא דִּפְלַגְתְּ קַמַּיְיהוּ, אִי נָמֵי תְּרֵי מִגּוֹ תְּלָתָא, וְאִי נָמֵי תְּרֵי סָהֲדִי דִּפְלַגְתְּ בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי תְלָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If there are** three men **there** who can convene as **a court,** he **may stipulate before** the court that he will undertake to return the item provided that he receives full compensation for lost income. The Gemara relates: **Issur and Rav Safra formed a joint venture with each other. Rav Safra went and dissolved** their partnership **without Issur's knowledge in the presence of two** witnesses. Rav Safra **came before Rabba bar Rav Huna** in order to ratify the dissolution of the partnership. Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to him: Go** and **bring me** the court of **three before whom you dissolved** your partnership. **Alternatively,** you may bring **two of** the **three** of them to testify that you dissolved the partnership before them. **Or alternatively,** bring **two witnesses** to testify **that you dissolved** the partnership **before** a court **of three.**

The Gemara introduces an illustrative case. Issur and Safra were business partners who had jointly purchased a stock of goods. Safra, while Issur was absent, unilaterally divided the partnership, taking his half of the merchandise — doing so in front of two witnesses to ensure complete transparency. When the matter came before Rabba bar Rav Huna, he told Safra: two witnesses are insufficient for a unilateral division of partnership assets; you need three, i.e., a *beit din*, or at minimum two witnesses who testify that they were part of a tribunal of three. Safra asked: from where do you derive that a *beit din* is required? Rabba bar Rav Huna replied: from the *mishna* — *im yesh sham beit din matneh bifneihem* — the same logic applies: when one party acts unilaterally in a way that affects another party's interests, a *beit din* must represent the absent party's interests and validate the arrangement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם בֵּית דִּין – מַתְנֶה בִּפְנֵיהֶם, אֵין שָׁם בֵּית דִּין – בִּפְנֵי מִי יַתְנֶה? שֶׁלּוֹ קוֹדֵם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Safra **said to** Rabba bar Rav Huna: **From where do you** know **this** *halakha*, that dissolution of the partnership may be accomplished only before a court? Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to him:** It is **as we learned** in the mishna: **If there are** three men **there** who can convene as **a court,** he **may stipulate before** the court that he will undertake to return the item provided that he receives full compensation for lost income. But **if there is no court there, before whom can he stipulate** his condition? Rather, in that case, **his** financial interests **take precedence,** and he need not return the lost item. Apparently, one stipulates binding conditions with regard to another's property only before a court.

Safra pushed back: how can you compare the two cases? In the *aveida* case, the finder is extracting money from the owner — creating an obligation the owner did not freely agree to — and therefore a *beit din* is needed to represent the owner. But here, Safra took only what was already his. In a partnership, he owns half; the division is merely a clarification of which specific items correspond to his half. This is *giluya milta b'alma* — a mere disclosure of what was already the case. Two witnesses should therefore suffice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם דְּמַפֵּיק מָמוֹנָא מֵהַאי וּמוֹתֵיב לְהַאי בָּעֵינַן בֵּית דִּין, אֲבָל הָכָא דִּידִיה שְׁקַלִי גִּילּוּי מִילְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא בִּתְרֵי סַגִּי לֵיהּ. תִּדַּע, דִּתְנַן: אַלְמָנָה מוֹכֶרֶת שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Safra **said to** Rabba bar Rav Huna: **Is** that case in the mishna **comparable** to this case? **There, where he is removing property from** the possession of **this** person **and giving** it **to that** person, **we require a court. But here,** referring to himself in the third person, **he is** merely **taking his own** property, and not the property of any other person. There is no transaction effected here. It is **mere disclosure** of **the matter** that he divided the joint property equitably, and **two** witnesses **are sufficient for him** to disclose that fact. Rav Safra cites proof. **Know** that this is so, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 97a) that **a widow** owed sustenance from her husband's estate **sells** the property of the estate when **not before a court.** Apparently, one need not involve the court when reclaiming property that belongs to him.

The Gemara marshals support: a widow selling property from an estate to collect her *ketuva* may do so without a *beit din*, because she has a pre-existing lien on the property. Abaye, however, notes a refinement: Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said in the name of Rav Nachman that the widow does not need a *beit din shel mumchin* — a court of ordained experts — but she does need a *beit din shel hedyotos* — a court of lay members. The implication is that even when one is taking what one fundamentally has a right to, some form of communal oversight is required to protect the interests of others who might be affected, even if it falls short of a full expert tribunal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְלָאו מִי אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ ״אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַלְמָנָה אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מוּמְחִין, אֲבָל צְרִיכָה בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹטוֹת״?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But wasn't it stated with regard to** that mishna that **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** The court before which **a widow** sells the property of the estate **need not** be **a court of experts, but is required** to be at least **a court of laymen.** Therefore, as in the parallel case of the widow, even when disclosing that one took property belonging to him, two witnesses are not sufficient and a court is required.

## Finding Objects

### Matza B'Refet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מְצָאָהּ בָּרֶפֶת – אֵין חַיָּיב בָּהּ. בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – חַיָּיב בָּהּ. וְאִם הָיְתָה בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת – לֹא יִטַּמֵּא לָהּ. אִם אָמַר לוֹ אָבִיו ״הִיטַּמֵּא״, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ ״אַל תַּחְזִיר״ – לֹא יִשְׁמַע לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one found** an animal **in a stable** belonging to its owner, he is **not obligated** to return **it** to its owner. If he found it **in a public area,** he is **obligated** to return **it. And if** the animal **was** lost **in a graveyard** and a priest found it, **he may not become impure to** return **it. If his father said to him: Become impure; or** in a case **where** one was obligated to return the animal and his father **said to him: Do not return** it, **he may not listen to** his father, as one may not violate Torah law to honor his father.

The *mishna* opens with a straightforward rule: *matza b'refet ein chayav bah* — if one finds an animal in a barn, one is not obligated to return it. In the public domain, *birshut ha-rabim*, one is obligated. An additional exception is introduced: if the animal is in a cemetery and one is a *kohen*, one may not become *tamei* in order to return it — the prohibition against *tum'at kohanim* supersedes the obligation of *hashavat aveida*. This represents the third of the exceptions derived from the *drasha* of *ve-hisalamta mei-hem*, now appearing explicitly in the *mishna*.

## Prika U-Teina Intro

### Multiple Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרַק וְטָעַן, פָּרַק וְטָעַן, אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִישָׁה פְּעָמִים – חַיָּיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״עָזֹב תַּעֲזֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** If **one unloaded** a burden from an animal collapsing under its weight **and** then later **loaded** it onto the animal, and later **unloaded and loaded** it again, **even** if this scenario repeats itself **four or five times,** he is **obligated** to continue unloading and loading, **as it is stated:** "If you see the donkey of him that hates you collapsed under its burden, you shall forgo passing him by; **you shall release it [*azov ta'azov*]** with him" (Exodus 23:5). It is derived from the verse that one is obligated to perform the action as needed, even several times.

The *mishna* moves immediately to *prika u-teina* — the obligation to unload and reload a burdened animal — without first describing the general parameters of the *mitzvah*. It opens instead with the question of repetition: if one has unloaded and reloaded the animal, and the animal collapsed again, must one repeat the process? The *mishna* answers: *afilu arba'ah v'chamisha pe'amim chayav* — even four or five times, one remains obligated. The verse *azov ta'azov* — "you shall surely help" — grounds this in the doubled language, which the *mishna* reads as indicating multiple obligatory occurrences. It is worth noting that in the previous *mishna* regarding *hashev teshivem*, Rava had argued that the first word of a doubled form carries the meaning of multiple occurrences, while the second word expands the scope of the obligation. Here the *mishna* again uses the doubling to indicate repetition.

### Owner Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלַךְ וְיָשַׁב לוֹ, וְאָמַר: ״הוֹאִיל וְעָלֶיךָ מִצְוָה, אִם רְצוֹנְךָ לִפְרוֹק – פְּרוֹק״ – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עִמּוֹ״. אִם הָיָה זָקֵן אוֹ חוֹלֶה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner **went, and sat, and said** to a passerby: **Since** there is **a mitzva** incumbent **upon you** to unload the burden, **if it is your wish to unload** the burden, **unload** it, in such a case the passerby is **exempt, as it is stated:** "You shall release it **with him,"** with the owner of the animal. **If** the failure of the owner to participate in unloading the burden was due to the fact **he was old or infirm,** the passerby is **obligated** to unload the burden alone.

If the owner is present, able-bodied, and refuses to assist — saying, in effect, since you have a *mitzvah* to help, you go ahead and I will wait — the finder is exempt. The verse states *imo* — "with him" — indicating that the obligation is conditioned on the owner's participation. If the owner is present and capable but unwilling to assist, the obligation lapses.

If, however, the owner is elderly or ill and genuinely unable to assist, one remains obligated even though the owner cannot participate. The *mitzvah* of *prika u-teina* is not voided by the owner's incapacity; it is voided only by the owner's willful refusal to help when capable of doing so.

## Reload Obligation

### Mishnah Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה לִפְרוֹק אֲבָל לֹא לִטְעוֹן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף לִטְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** There is **a mitzva by Torah law to unload** a burden, **but** there is **no** mitzva **to load** it. **Rabbi Shimon says:** There is **even** a mitzva **to load** the burden.

The *mishna* states: *mitzvah min ha-Torah lifrok aval lo litoan* — the Torah obligation is to unload the animal, but not to reload it. The Gemara will press immediately on this formulation.

### R. Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *af litoan* — there is a *mitzvah* to reload as well, and it is an equal Torah obligation. The Gemara will develop the reasoning behind both positions and then use the disagreement as the springboard for examining the principle of *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*.

## Finding Objects…

### Refet Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: רֶפֶת שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵינָהּ מַתְעָה וְאֵינָהּ מְשַׁמֶּרֶת. אֵינָהּ מַתְעָה, מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בָּהּ״. וְאֵינָהּ מְשַׁמֶּרֶת, מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיתְנֵי ״אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בָּהּ״. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מְשַׁמֶּרֶת, הַשְׁתָּא מַשְׁכַּח לָהּ אַבָּרַאי מְעַיֵּיל לַהּ לְגַוַּאי, מַשְׁכַּח לַהּ מִגַּוַּאי מִבַּעְיָא? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֵינָהּ מְשַׁמֶּרֶת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one found an animal in a stable, he need not return it to its owner. **Rava said:** The **stable that** the Sages **mentioned** in the mishna is one that **neither encourages** the animal **to stray nor secures** the animal so it will not flee. The Gemara explains Rava's statement. That it **does not encourage** the animal **to stray** is learned **from** the fact **that** the *tanna* **teaches: He is not obligated in its** return. The fact that **it does not secure** the animal is learned **from** the fact that it **was necessary** for the *tanna* **to teach: He is not obligated in its** return.

The Gemara asks what kind of barn the *mishna* has in mind when it exempts the finder from obligation. Rava establishes the parameters: *refet she-amru einah mateh v'einah meshameret* — the barn referred to is one that neither misleads the animal into wandering nor provides it full protection. Each characteristic is inferred from the *mishna* itself.

That the barn is not misleading — not actively encouraging the animal to leave, as a fire or wide-open gates might — is inferred from the fact that the *mishna* exempts the finder. If the barn were the kind of place from which an animal was likely to wander, the situation would constitute *aveidah mi-da'at* on the owner's part: a careless exposure of the animal to likely loss. In that case, one would be obligated to close the gate or take action, since the anticipated departure of the animal is one's responsibility to prevent. Since the *mishna* exempts the finder, the barn must not be creating that kind of situation.

That the barn is nonetheless not fully protective is inferred from the fact that the *mishna* needed to address the case at all. If the barn were completely secure, finding an animal inside it would so obviously not constitute an *aveida* that there would be no need to state the exemption. The *mishna*'s need to specify it tells us the barn provides less than complete protection — perhaps there is a small breach through which the animal might, under unusual circumstances, escape — but the risk is not sufficiently imminent to obligate the passerby.

The practical line the Gemara is drawing is between *anticipated* loss, which creates an obligation to prevent it, and *possible but remote* loss, which does not. One who walks by a barn with a wide-open gate while horses are about to realize they can walk out has an obligation to close the gate. One who walks by a barn with a small breach through which an animal might, in some unlikely circumstance, eventually find its way out does not.

### Two Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצָאָהּ בָּרֶפֶת אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: וְהוּא שֶׁעוֹמֶדֶת תּוֹךְ לַתְּחוּם. מִכְּלָל, דְּבִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם – נָמֵי חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **one found** an animal **in a stable** belonging to its owner, **he is not obligated** to return it. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: And that** is the *halakha* only in a case **where** the animal **is standing within the** city **limits.** The Gemara concludes **by inference that** if the animal was **found in a public area** he is obligated to return it, **and even** if it was **within the** city **limits,** he is **also obligated** to return it.

A further question concerns the relationship between the two conditions mentioned in the *mishna* — the animal being in a barn versus in the public domain — and whether one condition alone suffices to determine obligation. Two readings are presented.

### Yitzchak's View

Yitzchak interprets the *mishna* as follows: *vehu she'omedet toch la-tchum* — the exemption for an animal found in a barn applies only when the barn is within the city boundaries. If the barn is outside the city limits, the combination of reduced protection and distance from the owner creates sufficient risk that one is obligated even though the animal is in a barn.

Under the first reading of Yitzchak's position, the result is an expansive standard of obligation: an animal in the public domain — *birshut ha-rabim* — generates an obligation even within city boundaries, and an animal in a barn generates an obligation if it is outside the city boundaries. Either condition alone may be sufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא: בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים חַיָּיב בָּהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: וְהוּא שֶׁעוֹמֶדֶת חוּץ לַתְּחוּם. מִכְּלַל, דְּבָרֶפֶת אֲפִילּוּ עוֹמֶדֶת חוּץ לַתְּחוּם – נָמֵי אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are those who teach** this statement **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna: If he found it **in a public area,** he is **obligated** to return **it. Rabbi Yitzḥak says: And that** is the *halakha* only in a case **where** the animal **is standing beyond the** city **limits.** The Gemara concludes **by inference that** in a case where the animal was **found in the stable, even** if the animal **is standing beyond the** city **limits, he is also not obligated in its** return.

Under the second reading — attributed to those who place Yitzchak's statement at the *seifa* of the *mishna* rather than the *reisha* — the standard is more restrictive: one is obligated only when both conditions obtain simultaneously: the animal must be in the public domain *and* outside the city limits. A barn outside city limits alone would not suffice; the public domain alone within city limits would not suffice. Both factors together are required.

## Aveida Exceptions

### Cemetery Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת לֹא יִטַּמֵּא לָהּ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם אָמַר לוֹ אָבִיו ״הִיטַּמֵּא״, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ ״אַל תַּחְזִיר״ שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁמַע לוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ אִמּוֹ וְאָבִיו תִּירָאוּ וְאֶת שַׁבְּתוֹתַי תִּשְׁמֹרוּ אֲנִי ה׳״, כּוּלְּכֶם חַיָּיבִין בִּכְבוֹדִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And if** the animal **was** lost **in a graveyard** and was found by a priest, **he may not become impure to** return **it.** In a case where a priest's father said to him: Become impure, or in a case where one was obligated to return the animal and his father said to him: Do not return it, he may not listen to his father. The Gemara cites a *baraita* in which **the Sages taught: From where** is it derived **that if** a priest's **father said to him: Become impure, or that** if one's father **said to him: Do not return** a lost item that you found; **he should not listen to him?** It is derived from the verse, **as it is stated: "Every man shall fear his mother and his father, and you shall observe My *Shabbatot*; I am the Lord"** (Leviticus 19:3). From the fact that the verse concludes: "I am the Lord," it is derived that: **You are all,** parent and child alike, **obligated in My honor.** Therefore, if a parent commands his child to refrain from observing a mitzva, he must not obey the command.

The *mishna* states: *be-veit ha-kevarot lo yitama lah* — a *kohen* should not become *tamei* in order to return a lost animal found in a cemetery. The Gemara moves past this ruling relatively quickly, having addressed the underlying principle earlier: a positive commandment (*aseh*) cannot override a negative commandment combined with a positive commandment (*lo ta'aseh* and *aseh*). The *kohen*'s prohibition against becoming *tamei* — *le-nefesh lo yitama* as the *lo ta'aseh* and *kedoshim tihyu* as the *aseh* — cannot be overridden by the *aseh* of returning a lost object.

### Paternal Commands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֶת שַׁבְּתוֹתַי תִּשְׁמֹרוּ״ הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא (צָיְיתָא) [נֵיצִית] לֵיהּ? וְאַמַּאי? הַאי עֲשֵׂה, וְהַאי לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה – וְלָא אָתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְדָחֵי אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** that a priest must not obey his father's command to become impure is **because the Merciful One writes: "You shall observe My *Shabbatot*;** I am the Lord"; **but** if **it were not so, I would say** that the child **must obey him.** The Gemara asks: **But why? This** obligation to obey a parent is **a positive mitzva,** as it is written: "Honor your father and your mother" (Exodus 20:12), **and that** obligation of a priest to refrain from becoming impure is both **a prohibition:** "To the dead among his people he shall not defile himself" (Leviticus 21:1), **and a positive mitzva:** "You shall be holy" (Leviticus 19:2); **and** the principle is that **a positive mitzva does not come and override a prohibition and a positive mitzva.**

The mishna then introduces a related case that has nothing to do with *tum'ah*: *im amar lo aviv hitama* — if one's father tells him to become *tamei*, or *al tachzir* — to not return a lost object — *lo yishma lo* — he should not obey. Here, two *mitzvot* are in tension: honoring or fearing one's father on one side, and a Torah prohibition (or obligation) on the other.

The scriptural source for the ruling is: *ish imo ve'aviv tira'u ve'et shabtotai tishmoru, ani Hashem* — fear your mother and father, and observe My Sabbaths; I am God. The verse places both commandments in proximity and concludes with *kulchem chayavin bi-chevodi* — all of you are ultimately answerable to Me. The message is that honoring parents is a genuine obligation, but it operates within a framework in which divine authority is sovereign. When a father commands something that violates a divine obligation, the father's instruction falls outside the scope of legitimate parental authority.

This is a point worth dwelling on. One might have reasoned: returning an *aveida* is a monetary obligation, while honoring one's father is a ritual-spiritual obligation; the latter should therefore take precedence over the former. The *pasuk* forecloses that reasoning — not by saying that monetary obligations are equal in weight to ritual ones, but by establishing that the scope of *kibbud av va'em* does not extend to commanding the violation of divine law.

### Kibbud Av V'Em

A deeper question runs beneath the surface: is obeying one's father actually part of the Torah obligation of *kibbud av va'em* at all? Many *Rishonim* and *Poskim* hold that the core obligations of *kavod* (honor) and *yirah* (fear/awe) toward parents are behavioral rather than volitional — feeding and caring for them, not contradicting them publicly, not occupying their seat, and so on — but that simple obedience to their instructions is not, strictly speaking, a *d'oraita* obligation. Not contradicting parents in a disrespectful manner is different from being required to do whatever they say.

If that is correct, then the *mishna*'s case requires some explanation: why would there even be a question of obeying a father who tells one not to return an *aveida*? Several *Rishonim* therefore read the scenario as one where the father is not simply issuing an arbitrary command but is invoking his *kibbud*-related needs. For example, if the father says: I need you here at home attending to my care — don't go off returning someone else's lost object — then the *mitzvah* of honoring the father by meeting his needs does come into tension with the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveida*. Similarly, if the father needs something retrieved from the cemetery and asks his *kohen* son to go in.

This reading makes the ruling more striking, not less. The *mishna* is not saying: ignore your father's whims in favor of a Torah obligation. It is saying: even when your father is invoking a legitimate dimension of *kibbud av va'em* — his actual needs — the divine obligation takes precedence and you do not obey. This sits in interesting tension with other cases the Gemara recognizes: one may refrain from returning an *aveida* to protect one's own financial interests, and one may refrain because returning it would compromise one's dignity. Why should protecting one's father's legitimate needs be any different? The question has no simple resolution, and the *Rishonim* are sensitive to it.

## Reload Obligation…

### Why Both?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא תְּנֵיהּ? אָמְרוּ: לוֹמַר לָךְ: פְּרִיקָה בְּחִנָּם, טְעִינָה בְּשָׂכָר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: זוֹ וָזוֹ בְּחִנָּם. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא טְעִינָה וְלָא בָּעֵי פְּרִיקָה. וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה טְעִינָה דְּלֵית בַּהּ צַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים וְלֵיכָּא חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס – חַיָּיב, פְּרִיקָה דְּאִית בָּהּ צַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים וְחֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! אֶלָּא לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא? לוֹמַר לָךְ: פְּרִיקָה בְּחִנָּם, טְעִינָה בְּשָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis** that there is a distinction between unloading and loading with regard to remuneration? The reason is **that if it enters your mind** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, let the Merciful One write** only the mitzva of **loading, and** then He would **need not** write the mitzva of **unloading, and I would say: Just as** with regard to **loading, where there is no** potential **suffering of animals and there is no** potential **monetary loss** for the owner, one is **obligated** to load the burden, with regard to **unloading, where there is** potential **suffering of animals and there is** potential **monetary loss** for the owner, is it **not all the more so** clear that one is required to unload the burden? **Rather, with regard to what *halakha*** did **the Merciful One write** the mitzva of unloading? It is **to tell you:** The mitzva of **unloading** the burden is performed **for free, but** the mitzva of **loading** is performed **with remuneration.**

The Gemara now returns to the *machloket* between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon regarding the obligation to reload. The Gemara's question: if both unloading (*prika*) and reloading (*teina*) are Torah obligations — as Rabbi Shimon maintains — why does the Torah mention both? If only reloading were stated, one could derive unloading by *kal va-chomer*; if only unloading were stated, one could derive reloading similarly. The need for two separate *psukim* requires explanation.

The *Rabbanan*'s answer is that the two *psukim* serve to differentiate the two obligations: unloading must be done for free, while reloading may be done for payment. This differentiation cannot be derived by inference alone and requires explicit textual basis. The reasoning is that unloading involves both *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — the immediate suffering of the overburdened animal — and *chisron kis* — the risk of the animal collapsing and dying, constituting immediate financial loss. These dual factors obligate assistance without any right to compensation. Reloading, by contrast, involves neither the animal's immediate suffering nor the same immediacy of financial loss; it is still a *mitzvah*, but one for which the helper may receive payment.

The practical question this raises is striking. One is obligated to help load a person's animal, yet may request payment for doing so. How does one actually handle that in practice — does one approach the person and negotiate? The answer seems to be that the obligation exists, the right to payment exists alongside it, and the two can be harmonized through a straightforward conversation. More interesting is the question of when exactly the obligation to reload attaches: does one have a *mitzvah* only upon encountering an animal that has already collapsed, or even when one encounters a person in the midst of loading who simply needs assistance? These questions await further *sugya*.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְסַיְּימִי קְרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that even loading is performed for free, **what is the reason** that the Torah writes the mitzva of unloading? The Gemara answers: It is **because the verses are not** clearly **defined,** and it is unclear which of the verses refers to loading and which refers to unloading. Had the Torah written one verse, it would have been interpreted with regard to unloading, and there would be no source that one needs to load an animal.

Rav Huna takes a position against the majority: *zu va-zu be-chinam* — both unloading and reloading should be performed without payment.

The *Rabbanan* respond to Rav Huna's position by articulating why the two obligations must be asymmetric. Their argument is structural: if even reloading — the lesser obligation — carries with it no right to payment, then certainly unloading would carry no right to payment. But the whole point of having two separate *psukim* must be to differentiate the two cases, and therefore the Torah explicitly states the obligation to reload precisely in order to indicate that unloading is the more stringent of the two.

### Rabbis' Reason

The Gemara makes the *kal va-chomer* structure explicit: *uma te'ina de-leit bah tza'ar ba'alei chayim chayav, prika de-it bah tza'ar ba'alei chayim lo kol she-ken?* — if reloading, which does not involve animal suffering, nonetheless carries an obligation, then unloading, which does involve animal suffering, certainly carries one. This *kal va-chomer* is the argument the *Rabbanan* say could have been used to derive unloading from reloading, making it unnecessary for the Torah to have stated both — unless the second *pasuk* is coming to differentiate the two cases in terms of payment.

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אַמַּאי לָא מְסַיְּימִי קְרָאֵי? הָכָא כְּתִיב ״רוֹבֵץ תַּחַת מַשָּׂאוֹ״, הָתָם כְּתִיב ״נוֹפְלִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ״, דִּרְמוּ אִינְהוּ וּטְעוּנַיְיהוּ בְּאוֹרְחָא מַשְׁמַע. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״נוֹפְלִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ״ אִינְהוּ וּטְעוּנַיְיהוּ עִלָּוַיְיהוּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** could ask: **Why** does Rabbi Shimon say that **the verses are not** clearly **defined? Here it is written: "Collapsed under its burden"** (Exodus 23:5), clearly referring to the case of a burden that needs unloading, and **there it is written: "Fallen down by the way"** (Deuteronomy 22:4), **indicating** that both the animals **and their burdens are** lying **on the way** and are in need of loading. **And Rabbi Shimon** explains that the verses are not defined because the phrase **"fallen down by the way"** could be understood as **indicating** that the animals are fallen **with their burdens upon them,** and referring to unloading.

## Tzaar Baalei Chayim

### Both Acknowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִדִּבְרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם נִלְמַד צַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא קָאָמַר, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְסַיְּימִי קְרָאֵי. אֲבָל מְסַיְּימִי קְרָאֵי דָּרְשִׁינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר, מִשּׁוּם מַאי? לָאו מִשּׁוּום צַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים דָּרְשִׁינַן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** **From the statements of both of** these *tanna'im* it can be **learned** that the requirement to prevent **suffering to animals** is **by Torah law. As even Rabbi Shimon says** that he disagreed with the opinion of the Rabbis **only because the verses are not** clearly **defined; but** had the **verses** been clearly **defined, we** would have **learned** the same ***a fortiori*** inference. **Due to what** factor can that inference be learned? **What, is it not due to** the matter of **suffering of animals,** which is a factor in unloading and not a factor in loading, that **we** would have **learned** the *a fortiori* inference?

Rava now derives a broader principle from the *machloket* between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon: *mi-divrei sheneihem nilmad* — from both positions together we can infer that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* — concern for animal suffering is a Torah principle. The inference runs as follows. The *Rabbanan* explicitly ground the elevated obligation of unloading in *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. And even Rabbi Shimon's position does not actually reject that principle: he holds that the two *psukim* are of equal obligation only because the *psukim* themselves are ambiguous — *de-lo mesaymi kra'ei* — and it is not clear textually which one addresses unloading and which addresses reloading. But if the *psukim* were explicit — *aval mesaymi kra'ei* — he would agree to the Rabbis' *kal va-chomer* logic, and therefore he implicitly accepts that unloading, involving animal suffering, is a weightier obligation. The agreement in principle between the two positions establishes that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* is a Torah value, even if the precise halakhic implications of that value are worked out differently.

A caveat is important here: invoking *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* in this context does not necessarily mean there is a free-standing independent obligation to alleviate animal suffering whenever one encounters it. What the Gemara is establishing here is that concern for animal suffering is a recognized Torah value that factors into the weight and scope of other obligations — specifically the obligation of *prika*.

### Alternative: Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: וּמָה טְעִינָה דְּלֵית בַּהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס – חַיָּיב, פְּרִיקָה דְּאִית בַּהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof. **Perhaps** the *a fortiori* inference is **due to** the fact **that there is** the factor of **monetary loss** in unloading but not in loading, **and this is what** the Rabbis **are saying: If** in the case of **loading, where** if one fails to assist the owner **there is no** potential **monetary loss,** one is **obligated** to help load the animal, in the case of **unloading, where** if one fails to assist the owner **there is** potential **monetary loss,** is it **not all the more so** clear that one is required to unload the burden?

The Gemara does not let the inference stand unchallenged. Perhaps, the Gemara suggests, the difference between unloading and reloading has nothing to do with the animal's suffering at all. Perhaps the relevant factor is purely financial: unloading is more urgent because the animal, if left burdened, might collapse and die — representing a direct property loss to the owner (*chisron kis*) — whereas reloading does not carry the same immediacy of financial loss. On this reading, the entire structure of the *machloket* is about the owner's property interests, not about any concern for the animal per se.

### Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּטְעִינָה אֵין בָּהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס? מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּאַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי בָּטֵיל מִשּׁוּקֵיהּ, אִי נָמֵי אָתוּ גַנָּבֵי וְשָׁקְלִי כֹּל מָה דְּאִיכָּא בַּהֲדֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is there no** potential **monetary loss in loading? Are we not** also **dealing** with a case **where in the meanwhile,** while the owner waits for assistance, he will be **prevented from** bringing **his** merchandise to the **marketplace** in time to sell it; **alternatively, thieves** might **come and take all** the merchandise **that is there with him?** Therefore, no *a fortiori* inference can be learned on the basis of monetary loss, and the inference must be based on the matter of the suffering of animals.

The Gemara refutes this alternative. If the only factor were *chisron kis*, then one could equally describe scenarios in which reloading also involves significant financial loss. A merchant trying to bring goods to market who cannot reload his animal will miss the market entirely — *batil mi-shukei* — losing the commercial opportunity. Or, more dramatically, he will be stranded on the road, exposed to bandits who may rob him of both his goods and the animal — *atu ganvei ve-shakli kol mah de-ika ba-hadeih*. Financial loss is a real risk in the reloading scenario as well.

The counter-argument that unloading involves *immediate* loss while reloading involves only *possible future* loss has some merit, but the Gemara does not find it decisive. Both unloading and reloading can plausibly involve financial loss to the owner of the animal; the genuine asymmetry between them must therefore lie elsewhere — and that elsewhere is *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. The animal suffers under the burden when it needs to be unloaded; it does not suffer in the same way when it simply needs to be reloaded. That is the real difference.

### R. Yossi H-G

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּדַע דְּצַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה עָלָיו יָתֵר עַל מַשָּׂאוֹ – אֵין זָקוּק לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״תַּחַת מַשָּׂאוֹ״, מַשּׂאוֹי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בּוֹ. לָאו מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר זָקוּק לוֹ? מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּום דְּצַעַר בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional proof: **Know that** the requirement to prevent **suffering to animals** is **by Torah law, as it is taught** in **the latter clause** of the mishna: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: If there was** a burden **upon** the animal **greater than** its typical **burden, one need not attend to it, as it is stated: "Under its burden"** (Exodus 23:5). Rabbi Yosei holds that the obligation to unload an animal is with regard to **a burden that** the animal **can bear;** does this **not** indicate **by inference that the first *tanna* holds** that **he must attend to it** to unload a burden that is greater than its typical burden? **What is the reason** for this ruling; is it **not due to** the fact **that** the requirement to prevent **suffering to animals** is **by Torah law?**

The Gemara uses the contrast between Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili and the *Tanna Kamma* to sharpen the question. Since Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili holds one may ignore even an overburdened, suffering animal when the owner is at fault, from his ruling alone one cannot prove that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* is the operative principle. But the *Tanna Kamma* — who holds one is *zakuk lo* even in the case of an overburdened animal — seems to demand explanation. If the owner caused the loss himself, one would normally apply the logic of *aveidah mi-da'at* and have no obligation. The only compelling reason to nevertheless require assistance would be concern for the animal's suffering, independent of the owner's culpability. That would suggest that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* is indeed the operative factor for the *Tanna Kamma*.

### Final Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא ״בְּתַחַת מַשָּׂאוֹ״ פְּלִיגִי, דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: דָּרְשִׁינַן ״תַּחַת מַשָּׂאוֹ״, מַשּׂאוֹי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בּוֹ, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא דָּרְשִׁינַן ״תַּחַת מַשָּׂאוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof: **Perhaps** it is with regard to the meaning of the phrase **"under its burden"** that they **disagree, as Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili **holds that we interpret** the phrase **"under its burden"** to mean: **A burden that** the animal **can bear. And the Rabbis hold** that **we do not interpret** the phrase **"under its burden"** in this manner.

The Gemara does not close the matter here. It notes the alternative: perhaps the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili are simply in a technical dispute about how to read the word *masa'o* in the *pasuk*, not a substantive dispute about *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. And even the *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling that one must help in the overloading case may be explicable not as concern for the animal but as concern for the owner — the Torah, one might say, sometimes obligates us to help even those who brought trouble upon themselves, not because we are indifferent to fault but because human beings sometimes do foolish things and deserve assistance nonetheless. Whether *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* can be definitively established as a Torah principle from this *sugya* remains the open question with which the daf concludes.
