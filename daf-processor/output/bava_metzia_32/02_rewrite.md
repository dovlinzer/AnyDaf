# Bava Metzia 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Aveida Parameters

### Three Exceptions

The daf officially begins at the top of 31b, where the Gemara is in the midst of developing the core parameters of *aveida*. The Gemara's *drasha* on the phrase *ve-hisalamta mei-hem* — the verse that commands one to "ignore" the lost object under certain conditions — yields three categories of exception. The first is dignity: just as one may prioritize one's own property over another's, one may prioritize one's own dignity over another's financial loss, and need not return an object when doing so would be beneath one's station. The second is the prohibition against violating another *halacha* in the course of returning a lost object — the paradigmatic case being a *kohen* who would have to enter a cemetery, which will appear explicitly in a later *mishna*. The third exception, which actually appears first in the *braisa* but arose first in the *mishna* as well, is that one need not return a lost object if doing so would cause oneself greater financial loss in the process.

### Aveidat Karka

A conceptually significant extension that emerged in the preceding Gemara is the application of *hashavat aveida* to *aveidat karka* — preventing damage to land. This represents a double abstraction: from movable property to immovable property, and from the act of returning an object to its owner to the more general obligation of preventing loss. That broader framing becomes critical when the Gemara moves to compare *aveida* with *prika u-teina*, since both seem, at the most abstract level, to be expressions of the same principle — protecting another person's property.

### Aveida & Prika U-Teina

This raises an obvious question: if both *aveida* and *prika u-teina* reduce to the same underlying concern of preventing property loss, why does the Torah need to legislate both separately? The Gemara addresses this by pointing out that the two *mitzvot* operate in genuinely distinct ways. In the case of unloading a struggling animal, the elevated obligation is driven partly by *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — the suffering of the animal — a factor absent in the ordinary *aveida* context. Conversely, in the *aveida* case, one dimension of the obligation is heightened by the fact that the owner is not present and cannot protect the object himself. The various *psukim* are thus teaching the scope of the obligation to protect another's property across a range of distinct scenarios, each with its own configuration of factors.

## Doubling Language

### Mos Yumat

The Gemara continues its survey of doubled verbal constructions in the Torah and what each instance comes to teach. The passage *mos yumat ha-makeh* — "the murderer shall surely be put to death" — cannot mean that the execution must be carried out multiple times, since one execution suffices. Instead, the Gemara reads the doubling as teaching: *ein li ela be-misah ha-ksuvah bo* — I know only that the murderer must be executed by the specific mode of execution prescribed for him, such as by the sword. But what if circumstances prevent implementation of that prescribed method — for instance, if the condemned man has escaped and the only way to stop him is by some other means? The double language — *mos yumat mi-kol makom* — authorizes execution by whatever means are available. This applies specifically after a *gmar din* has been issued, not during investigation or in the context of a pursuer trying to commit murder; those are governed by separate *halachot*.

### Hakei Takeh

A parallel case: the verse commanding the smiting of the inhabitants of the *ir hanidachat* prescribes death by the sword. The doubling of *hakei takeh* teaches that if the prescribed mode of execution cannot be carried out, any other form of execution is permitted — *mi-kol makom*, under all circumstances.

### Hashev Tashiv

Returning now to a case directly relevant to *aveida*: the doubled form *hashev tashiv* in the context of returning collateral. The straightforward reading might suggest that the obligation applies only when the lender seized the collateral through a court officer, i.e., *birshut beit din*. The doubling — *hashev tashiv mi-kol makom* — extends the obligation to cover even cases where the collateral was seized through private agreement rather than by court order.

### Chavol Tachbol

The phrase *im chavol tachbol salmat re'akha* — "if you take your neighbor's garment as collateral" — also employs doubled language. One might initially ask why the Torah uses this construction here at all, and then a further question arises: why are there actually two separate *psukim* about collateral, one in Shemot 24 and one in Shemot 25? The answer is that one verse addresses *ksut yom* — a daytime garment that must be returned during the day — and the other addresses *ksut laila* — a nighttime garment that must be returned at night. The doubled language within each verse extends the obligation to cases where the collateral was seized without any authorization whatsoever — not merely without a court officer but even where the lender broke into the debtor's home and seized an item unilaterally. One might have thought that such an illegitimate seizure falls outside the Torah's protective framework, or conversely that the obligation to return might not apply since the original taking was improper. The doubled language — *chavol tachbol mi-kol makom* — establishes the obligation under all circumstances.

### Patoach Tiftach

The phrase *patoach tiftach et yadekha lo* — "you shall surely open your hand to him" — in the context of giving to the poor. The Gemara first reads these *psukim* through the lens of *tzedaka*, noting that the verse also says *dei machsoro* — as much as he lacks. The doubling teaches, first, that the obligation extends beyond the poor of one's own city (*ani irkha*) to the poor of other cities as well. While those of one's own town take precedence, the obligation itself is not geographically limited. The doubling also addresses the question of partial giving: since the verse says to supply what the person lacks, one might think there is no *mitzvah* to give a small amount if one cannot provide everything that is needed. The doubled form *naton titein mi-kol makom* — give regardless — establishes that even a minimal gift fulfills the *mitzvah*.

### Ha'anek Ta'anik

The doubled phrase *ha'aneik ta'anik lo* governs the *mitzvah* of loading up the freed slave with gifts when he goes free in the seventh year. The end of the verse credits the slave's service with bringing divine blessing to the household — *ki mishneh sechar sachir avadcha shesh shanim u-veirach'cha Hashem Elokecha*. One might therefore think that if the household was not in fact blessed — if the master's business suffered during the years of the slave's service — there is no obligation to provide these gifts. The doubled language — *ha'aneik ta'anik mi-kol makom* — establishes the obligation regardless of whether the household was blessed. The comparison to large bonuses paid even in years when a firm loses money is not inapt.

---

## Dibra Torah K'Lishon

### R. Elazar b. Azarya

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, however, holds that the obligation to give the departing slave gifts is indeed contingent on the household having been blessed by his service. If the household was not blessed, there is no obligation. This position must therefore explain what work the doubling of *ha'aneik ta'anik* performs in his reading.

### Doubling as Speech

The Gemara's answer for Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya is that the doubled form here teaches nothing additional: *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam* — the Torah sometimes speaks in the ordinary idiom of human language, using emphatic doubling as a stylistic feature rather than as a source of novel law. This principle opens a methodological question that Tosafot addresses directly: how does one determine when a doubled form is *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam* and when it carries halakhic content? Tosafot's answer is that the default assumption is that doubling expands the scope of the *mitzvah*, and only when no plausible expansion can be derived from context do we fall back on *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam*.

## Doubling Language…

### Ha'aveit Ta'avitenu

The Gemara returns to the earlier *psukim* about giving to the poor, now focusing specifically on the doubled verb *ha'aveit ta'avitenu* — the root of which connotes collateral and lending — thereby indicating that the *pasuk* is also, or primarily, addressing the obligation to extend a loan. What does the doubling teach here?

The Gemara states: *ein li ela she-ein lo v'eino rotze l'hitparnes* — I know only that the obligation applies when the person has no means of his own and refuses to accept a gift, preferring instead to maintain his dignity by way of a loan. In such a case, the Torah says: give him the loan. This implies, interestingly, that when a person is willing to accept a gift, giving a gift is actually preferable to a loan — a debate addressed more fully in the Gemara in Ketubot 67b for those who wish to pursue it.

But the more pressing question is: what about a person who has money of his own but refuses to use it — *yesh lo v'eino rotze l'hitparnes mishalo ela mishalcha*? Rashi explains the case starkly: the person has assets but refuses to draw on them. One might imagine someone who is, as it were, asset-rich and cash-poor — perhaps unwilling to liquidate a prized possession — and who refuses to accept charity but is willing to accept a loan. Is one obligated to lend money to such a person, or can one say that his refusal to sell his assets is his own affair? The doubled form — *ta'avitenu mi-kol makom* — teaches that one is nonetheless obligated to extend the loan. People have their own particular circumstances and arrangements, and that does not dissolve one's obligation toward them.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *yesh lo v'eino rotze l'hitparnes — ein nizkakin lo*. If a person actually has money, there is no obligation whatsoever. In his view, the doubled form of *ta'avitenu* teaches nothing, and the ruling is again *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam*.

## Poel Batel Wages

### Poel Batel Definition

The Gemara now turns to the question of reimbursement for returning a lost object. If a person loses time from work in order to return an *aveida*, the owner must compensate him — but not at his full wage rate. The *mishna* states he is paid *ke-schar poel batel* — like the wage of an idle worker. What does this mean?

Abaye explains: *ke-poel batel shel oto melacha de-batel mineih* — like a worker who is taking time off from the specific labor he would otherwise be performing. The key is not what he could earn at that labor, but what he would accept in order to take a break from it.

Rashi elaborates precisely. Consider a quarry worker earning twenty dollars an hour at hard physical labor. If asked how much he would accept in order to take an hour off from that labor and instead do something lighter — such as knocking on doors to find the owner of a lost object — he might well accept five or ten dollars, because the relief from hard labor has its own value. The *poel batel* rate is therefore that reduced figure: the amount a person would willingly accept to stop doing his regular work and do something easier instead. Critically, this rate is not fixed; as Rashi notes, it depends on the nature and difficulty of the original work and the level of compensation it commands.

This produces two asymmetric edge cases. A high-earning professional — say, someone billing at a substantial hourly rate — might find that returning a lost object is actually more demanding or at least no less demanding than his regular work, meaning his *poel batel* rate converges with his ordinary rate. Conversely, a laborer who desperately needs the income might theoretically accept a reduced rate but cannot actually afford to do so. These are the cases in which, as the *mishna* requires, one must stipulate the terms before a *beit din*.

### Beit Din Role

The *mishna* states: *im yesh sham beit din matneh bifneihem* — if there is a court present, the finder stipulates the terms of reimbursement before them. The *beit din* in this context stands in for the interest of the owner of the lost object, approving the terms of compensation when the owner is not available to consent himself. The *beit din*'s precise role — whether it simply provides a formal stamp of approval or actually adjudicates what level of compensation is reasonable relative to the value of the object — is itself a matter for further analysis.

### Issur & Safra

The Gemara introduces an illustrative case. Issur and Safra were business partners who had jointly purchased a stock of goods. Safra, while Issur was absent, unilaterally divided the partnership, taking his half of the merchandise — doing so in front of two witnesses to ensure complete transparency. When the matter came before Rabba bar Rav Huna, he told Safra: two witnesses are insufficient for a unilateral division of partnership assets; you need three, i.e., a *beit din*, or at minimum two witnesses who testify that they were part of a tribunal of three. Safra asked: from where do you derive that a *beit din* is required? Rabba bar Rav Huna replied: from the *mishna* — *im yesh sham beit din matneh bifneihem* — the same logic applies: when one party acts unilaterally in a way that affects another party's interests, a *beit din* must represent the absent party's interests and validate the arrangement.

Safra pushed back: how can you compare the two cases? In the *aveida* case, the finder is extracting money from the owner — creating an obligation the owner did not freely agree to — and therefore a *beit din* is needed to represent the owner. But here, Safra took only what was already his. In a partnership, he owns half; the division is merely a clarification of which specific items correspond to his half. This is *giluya milta b'alma* — a mere disclosure of what was already the case. Two witnesses should therefore suffice.

The Gemara marshals support: a widow selling property from an estate to collect her *ketuva* may do so without a *beit din*, because she has a pre-existing lien on the property. Abaye, however, notes a refinement: Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said in the name of Rav Nachman that the widow does not need a *beit din shel mumchin* — a court of ordained experts — but she does need a *beit din shel hedyotos* — a court of lay members. The implication is that even when one is taking what one fundamentally has a right to, some form of communal oversight is required to protect the interests of others who might be affected, even if it falls short of a full expert tribunal.

## Finding Objects

### Matza B'Refet

The *mishna* opens with a straightforward rule: *matza b'refet ein chayav bah* — if one finds an animal in a barn, one is not obligated to return it. In the public domain, *birshut ha-rabim*, one is obligated. An additional exception is introduced: if the animal is in a cemetery and one is a *kohen*, one may not become *tamei* in order to return it — the prohibition against *tum'at kohanim* supersedes the obligation of *hashavat aveida*. This represents the third of the exceptions derived from the *drasha* of *ve-hisalamta mei-hem*, now appearing explicitly in the *mishna*.

---

## Prika U-Teina Intro

### Multiple Times

The *mishna* moves immediately to *prika u-teina* — the obligation to unload and reload a burdened animal — without first describing the general parameters of the *mitzvah*. It opens instead with the question of repetition: if one has unloaded and reloaded the animal, and the animal collapsed again, must one repeat the process? The *mishna* answers: *afilu arba'ah v'chamisha pe'amim chayav* — even four or five times, one remains obligated. The verse *azov ta'azov* — "you shall surely help" — grounds this in the doubled language, which the *mishna* reads as indicating multiple obligatory occurrences. It is worth noting that in the previous *mishna* regarding *hashev teshivem*, Rava had argued that the first word of a doubled form carries the meaning of multiple occurrences, while the second word expands the scope of the obligation. Here the *mishna* again uses the doubling to indicate repetition.

### Owner Present

If the owner is present, able-bodied, and refuses to assist — saying, in effect, since you have a *mitzvah* to help, you go ahead and I will wait — the finder is exempt. The verse states *imo* — "with him" — indicating that the obligation is conditioned on the owner's participation. If the owner is present and capable but unwilling to assist, the obligation lapses.

### Owner Infirm

If, however, the owner is elderly or ill and genuinely unable to assist, one remains obligated even though the owner cannot participate. The *mitzvah* of *prika u-teina* is not voided by the owner's incapacity; it is voided only by the owner's willful refusal to help when capable of doing so.

## Reload Obligation

### Mishnah Position

The *mishna* states: *mitzvah min ha-Torah lifrok aval lo litoan* — the Torah obligation is to unload the animal, but not to reload it. The Gemara will press immediately on this formulation.

### R. Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *af litoan* — there is a *mitzvah* to reload as well, and it is an equal Torah obligation. The Gemara will develop the reasoning behind both positions and then use the disagreement as the springboard for examining the principle of *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*.

---

## Finding Objects…

### Refet Definition

The Gemara asks what kind of barn the *mishna* has in mind when it exempts the finder from obligation. Rava establishes the parameters: *refet she-amru einah mateh v'einah meshameret* — the barn referred to is one that neither misleads the animal into wandering nor provides it full protection. Each characteristic is inferred from the *mishna* itself.

That the barn is not misleading — not actively encouraging the animal to leave, as a fire or wide-open gates might — is inferred from the fact that the *mishna* exempts the finder. If the barn were the kind of place from which an animal was likely to wander, the situation would constitute *aveidah mi-da'at* on the owner's part: a careless exposure of the animal to likely loss. In that case, one would be obligated to close the gate or take action, since the anticipated departure of the animal is one's responsibility to prevent. Since the *mishna* exempts the finder, the barn must not be creating that kind of situation.

That the barn is nonetheless not fully protective is inferred from the fact that the *mishna* needed to address the case at all. If the barn were completely secure, finding an animal inside it would so obviously not constitute an *aveida* that there would be no need to state the exemption. The *mishna*'s need to specify it tells us the barn provides less than complete protection — perhaps there is a small breach through which the animal might, under unusual circumstances, escape — but the risk is not sufficiently imminent to obligate the passerby.

The practical line the Gemara is drawing is between *anticipated* loss, which creates an obligation to prevent it, and *possible but remote* loss, which does not. One who walks by a barn with a wide-open gate while horses are about to realize they can walk out has an obligation to close the gate. One who walks by a barn with a small breach through which an animal might, in some unlikely circumstance, eventually find its way out does not.

### Two Readings

A further question concerns the relationship between the two conditions mentioned in the *mishna* — the animal being in a barn versus in the public domain — and whether one condition alone suffices to determine obligation. Two readings are presented.

### Yitzchak's View

Yitzchak interprets the *mishna* as follows: *vehu she'omedet toch la-tchum* — the exemption for an animal found in a barn applies only when the barn is within the city boundaries. If the barn is outside the city limits, the combination of reduced protection and distance from the owner creates sufficient risk that one is obligated even though the animal is in a barn.

Under the first reading of Yitzchak's position, the result is an expansive standard of obligation: an animal in the public domain — *birshut ha-rabim* — generates an obligation even within city boundaries, and an animal in a barn generates an obligation if it is outside the city boundaries. Either condition alone may be sufficient.

Under the second reading — attributed to those who place Yitzchak's statement at the *seifa* of the *mishna* rather than the *reisha* — the standard is more restrictive: one is obligated only when both conditions obtain simultaneously: the animal must be in the public domain *and* outside the city limits. A barn outside city limits alone would not suffice; the public domain alone within city limits would not suffice. Both factors together are required.

## Aveida Exceptions

### Cemetery Exception

The *mishna* states: *be-veit ha-kevarot lo yitama lah* — a *kohen* should not become *tamei* in order to return a lost animal found in a cemetery. The Gemara moves past this ruling relatively quickly, having addressed the underlying principle earlier: a positive commandment (*aseh*) cannot override a negative commandment combined with a positive commandment (*lo ta'aseh* and *aseh*). The *kohen*'s prohibition against becoming *tamei* — *le-nefesh lo yitama* as the *lo ta'aseh* and *kedoshim tihyu* as the *aseh* — cannot be overridden by the *aseh* of returning a lost object.

### Paternal Commands

The *mishna* then introduces a related case that has nothing to do with *tum'ah*: *im amar lo aviv hitama* — if one's father tells him to become *tamei*, or *al tachzir* — to not return a lost object — *lo yishma lo* — he should not obey. Here, two *mitzvot* are in tension: honoring or fearing one's father on one side, and a Torah prohibition (or obligation) on the other.

### Scripture Basis

The scriptural source for the ruling is: *ish imo ve'aviv tira'u ve'et shabtotai tishmoru, ani Hashem* — fear your mother and father, and observe My Sabbaths; I am God. The verse places both commandments in proximity and concludes with *kulchem chayavin bi-chevodi* — all of you are ultimately answerable to Me. The message is that honoring parents is a genuine obligation, but it operates within a framework in which divine authority is sovereign. When a father commands something that violates a divine obligation, the father's instruction falls outside the scope of legitimate parental authority.

This is a point worth dwelling on. One might have reasoned: returning an *aveida* is a monetary obligation, while honoring one's father is a ritual-spiritual obligation; the latter should therefore take precedence over the former. The *pasuk* forecloses that reasoning — not by saying that monetary obligations are equal in weight to ritual ones, but by establishing that the scope of *kibbud av va'em* does not extend to commanding the violation of divine law.

### Kibbud Av V'Em

A deeper question runs beneath the surface: is obeying one's father actually part of the Torah obligation of *kibbud av va'em* at all? Many *Rishonim* and *Poskim* hold that the core obligations of *kavod* (honor) and *yirah* (fear/awe) toward parents are behavioral rather than volitional — feeding and caring for them, not contradicting them publicly, not occupying their seat, and so on — but that simple obedience to their instructions is not, strictly speaking, a *d'oraita* obligation. Not contradicting parents in a disrespectful manner is different from being required to do whatever they say.

If that is correct, then the *mishna*'s case requires some explanation: why would there even be a question of obeying a father who tells one not to return an *aveida*? Several *Rishonim* therefore read the scenario as one where the father is not simply issuing an arbitrary command but is invoking his *kibbud*-related needs. For example, if the father says: I need you here at home attending to my care — don't go off returning someone else's lost object — then the *mitzvah* of honoring the father by meeting his needs does come into tension with the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveida*. Similarly, if the father needs something retrieved from the cemetery and asks his *kohen* son to go in.

This reading makes the ruling more striking, not less. The *mishna* is not saying: ignore your father's whims in favor of a Torah obligation. It is saying: even when your father is invoking a legitimate dimension of *kibbud av va'em* — his actual needs — the divine obligation takes precedence and you do not obey. This sits in interesting tension with other cases the Gemara recognizes: one may refrain from returning an *aveida* to protect one's own financial interests, and one may refrain because returning it would compromise one's dignity. Why should protecting one's father's legitimate needs be any different? The question has no simple resolution, and the *Rishonim* are sensitive to it.

## Reload Obligation…

### Why Both?

The Gemara now returns to the *machloket* between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon regarding the obligation to reload. The Gemara's question: if both unloading (*prika*) and reloading (*teina*) are Torah obligations — as Rabbi Shimon maintains — why does the Torah mention both? If only reloading were stated, one could derive unloading by *kal va-chomer*; if only unloading were stated, one could derive reloading similarly. The need for two separate *psukim* requires explanation.

The *Rabbanan*'s answer is that the two *psukim* serve to differentiate the two obligations: unloading must be done for free, while reloading may be done for payment. This differentiation cannot be derived by inference alone and requires explicit textual basis. The reasoning is that unloading involves both *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — the immediate suffering of the overburdened animal — and *chisron kis* — the risk of the animal collapsing and dying, constituting immediate financial loss. These dual factors obligate assistance without any right to compensation. Reloading, by contrast, involves neither the animal's immediate suffering nor the same immediacy of financial loss; it is still a *mitzvah*, but one for which the helper may receive payment.

The practical question this raises is striking. One is obligated to help load a person's animal, yet may request payment for doing so. How does one actually handle that in practice — does one approach the person and negotiate? The answer seems to be that the obligation exists, the right to payment exists alongside it, and the two can be harmonized through a straightforward conversation. More interesting is the question of when exactly the obligation to reload attaches: does one have a *mitzvah* only upon encountering an animal that has already collapsed, or even when one encounters a person in the midst of loading who simply needs assistance? These questions await further *sugya*.

### Rav Huna

Rav Huna takes a position against the majority: *zu va-zu be-chinam* — both unloading and reloading should be performed without payment.

### Rabbis' Reason

The *Rabbanan* respond to Rav Huna's position by articulating why the two obligations must be asymmetric. Their argument is structural: if even reloading — the lesser obligation — carries with it no right to payment, then certainly unloading would carry no right to payment. But the whole point of having two separate *psukim* must be to differentiate the two cases, and therefore the Torah explicitly states the obligation to reload precisely in order to indicate that unloading is the more stringent of the two.

### Kal V'Chomer

The Gemara makes the *kal va-chomer* structure explicit: *uma te'ina de-leit bah tza'ar ba'alei chayim chayav, prika de-it bah tza'ar ba'alei chayim lo kol she-ken?* — if reloading, which does not involve animal suffering, nonetheless carries an obligation, then unloading, which does involve animal suffering, certainly carries one. This *kal va-chomer* is the argument the *Rabbanan* say could have been used to derive unloading from reloading, making it unnecessary for the Torah to have stated both — unless the second *pasuk* is coming to differentiate the two cases in terms of payment.

---

## Tzaar Baalei Chayim

### Both Acknowledge

Rava now derives a broader principle from the *machloket* between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon: *mi-divrei sheneihem nilmad* — from both positions together we can infer that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* — concern for animal suffering is a Torah principle. The inference runs as follows. The *Rabbanan* explicitly ground the elevated obligation of unloading in *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. And even Rabbi Shimon's position does not actually reject that principle: he holds that the two *psukim* are of equal obligation only because the *psukim* themselves are ambiguous — *de-lo mesaymi kra'ei* — and it is not clear textually which one addresses unloading and which addresses reloading. But if the *psukim* were explicit — *aval mesaymi kra'ei* — he would agree to the Rabbis' *kal va-chomer* logic, and therefore he implicitly accepts that unloading, involving animal suffering, is a weightier obligation. The agreement in principle between the two positions establishes that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* is a Torah value, even if the precise halakhic implications of that value are worked out differently.

A caveat is important here: invoking *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* in this context does not necessarily mean there is a free-standing independent obligation to alleviate animal suffering whenever one encounters it. What the Gemara is establishing here is that concern for animal suffering is a recognized Torah value that factors into the weight and scope of other obligations — specifically the obligation of *prika*.

### Alternative: Loss

The Gemara does not let the inference stand unchallenged. Perhaps, the Gemara suggests, the difference between unloading and reloading has nothing to do with the animal's suffering at all. Perhaps the relevant factor is purely financial: unloading is more urgent because the animal, if left burdened, might collapse and die — representing a direct property loss to the owner (*chisron kis*) — whereas reloading does not carry the same immediacy of financial loss. On this reading, the entire structure of the *machloket* is about the owner's property interests, not about any concern for the animal per se.

### Refutation

The Gemara refutes this alternative. If the only factor were *chisron kis*, then one could equally describe scenarios in which reloading also involves significant financial loss. A merchant trying to bring goods to market who cannot reload his animal will miss the market entirely — *batil mi-shukei* — losing the commercial opportunity. Or, more dramatically, he will be stranded on the road, exposed to bandits who may rob him of both his goods and the animal — *atu ganvei ve-shakli kol mah de-ika ba-hadeih*. Financial loss is a real risk in the reloading scenario as well.

The counter-argument that unloading involves *immediate* loss while reloading involves only *possible future* loss has some merit, but the Gemara does not find it decisive. Both unloading and reloading can plausibly involve financial loss to the owner of the animal; the genuine asymmetry between them must therefore lie elsewhere — and that elsewhere is *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. The animal suffers under the burden when it needs to be unloaded; it does not suffer in the same way when it simply needs to be reloaded. That is the real difference.

### R. Yossi H-G

The Gemara cites a further proof from the position of Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili in the *mishna*: *im hayah alav yoter al masa'o ein zakuk lo* — if the animal is overloaded beyond what it can bear, and the overloading was the owner's fault, one has no obligation to help. The verse says *tachat masa'o* — under its burden — which Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili reads as *masa'o ha-raui she-yachol la'amod bo* — a burden it can normally sustain. An animal overloaded beyond that threshold is outside the *pasuk*'s scope.

This position is notable. If the obligation of *prika* were grounded in *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — concern for the animal's suffering — then Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili's position would be difficult to understand. An overburdened animal is suffering, regardless of who is responsible. Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili's willingness to walk away from the suffering animal strongly suggests that in his view, the obligation is entirely about the owner's property interests, not the animal's welfare. Since the owner caused the problem himself — this is a form of *aveidah mi-da'at* — the owner bears the consequences and the passerby has no obligation.

### Final Question

The Gemara uses the contrast between Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili and the *Tanna Kamma* to sharpen the question. Since Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili holds one may ignore even an overburdened, suffering animal when the owner is at fault, from his ruling alone one cannot prove that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* is the operative principle. But the *Tanna Kamma* — who holds one is *zakuk lo* even in the case of an overburdened animal — seems to demand explanation. If the owner caused the loss himself, one would normally apply the logic of *aveidah mi-da'at* and have no obligation. The only compelling reason to nevertheless require assistance would be concern for the animal's suffering, independent of the owner's culpability. That would suggest that *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* is indeed the operative factor for the *Tanna Kamma*.

The Gemara does not close the matter here. It notes the alternative: perhaps the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili are simply in a technical dispute about how to read the word *masa'o* in the *pasuk*, not a substantive dispute about *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. And even the *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling that one must help in the overloading case may be explicable not as concern for the animal but as concern for the owner — the Torah, one might say, sometimes obligates us to help even those who brought trouble upon themselves, not because we are indifferent to fault but because human beings sometimes do foolish things and deserve assistance nonetheless. Whether *tza'ar ba'alei chayim de-oraita* can be definitively established as a Torah principle from this *sugya* remains the open question with which the daf concludes.
