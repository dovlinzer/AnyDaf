# Menachot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav & Ben Batera

### Rav's Zar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: זָר שֶׁקָּמַץ – יַחְזִיר, וְהָא אֲנַן ״פָּסַל״ תְּנַן? מַאי ״פָּסַל״? פָּסַל עַד שֶׁיַּחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** In the case of **a non-priest who removed a handful, he should return** the handful to the meal offering. The Gemara challenges: **But we learned** in the mishna that a non-priest **disqualified** the meal offering by removing a handful from it. The Gemara responds: **What** does the mishna mean when it says: **Disqualified?** It means that the non-priest has **disqualified** the meal offering **until** such time as **he returns** the handful to the meal offering, whereupon a priest fit for the Temple service should again remove a handful from the meal offering and sacrifice it.

The *mishnah* at the opening of our sugya established that performing the *kmitzah* — the priestly handful — with the left hand renders the *minchah* invalid, and it recorded the dissenting view of Ben Batera, who ruled that if the *kmitzah* was taken with the left hand, it should be returned to the *kli* and the *kmitzah* taken again properly with the right. The *gemara* now extends this discussion by citing a statement of Rav: *zar shekamatz yachzir* — if a non-*kohen* performed the *kmitzah*, the handful should be returned and the act repeated. Rav thus takes the principle underlying Ben Batera's ruling and extends it even to the case of a *zar*.

### Gemara Questions Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ בֶּן בְּתִירָא? אִי דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְקוֹמֶץ בְּעֵינֵיהּ – לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּבֶן בְּתִירָא, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – דַּחֲסַר קוֹמֶץ. רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא יָבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנּוּ, בֶּן בְּתִירָא סָבַר: יָבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then **this is** identical to the opinion of **ben Beteira** in the mishna, who says that if a priest removed a handful with his left hand it is returned to the meal offering whereupon the priest removes a handful from the offering with his right hand. What difference is there between the two opinions in the mishna? The Gemara explains: **If the handful** that was removed by one unfit for Temple service **is in its unadulterated form,** then **the Rabbis do not disagree with** the opinion of **ben Beteira,** and the handful is returned to the meal offering. **They disagree when** the **handful is lacking. The Rabbis hold: One does not bring** flour **from within his house and refill** the vessel containing the handful, whereas **ben Beteira holds** that **one brings** flour **from within his house and refills it.**

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty. The *mishnah* itself declares such performances *pasul*, and we initially assume that Rav does not follow Ben Batera's position at all. So on the one hand, Rav appears to be expanding Ben Batera; on the other hand, he is following Ben Batera. The *gemara* therefore asks: *mai pasul pasul ad sheyachzir*? Perhaps the *mishnah*'s use of the word *pasul* does not mean the act is irremediably void but rather that it is invalid only until corrected — a temporary disqualification that permits a do-over.

### Psul Ad Sheyachzir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: יַחְזִיר וְיַחֲזוֹר וְיִקְמוֹץ בְּיָמִין?! בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: יַחְזִיר וְיָבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנּוּ, וְיַחֲזוֹר וְיִקְמוֹץ בְּיָמִין, מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if the mishna is referring to a handful that is lacking, then the statement of the mishna: **Ben Beteira says he must return** the handful to the vessel with the meal offering **and again remove a handful with** his **right** hand, is imprecise, as the mishna **should have** taught: **Ben Beteira says he must return** the handful to the vessel with the meal offering **and bring** flour **from within his house and refill** the missing amount, **and again remove a handful with** his **right** hand.

The *gemara* immediately objects to this reading: *ee hachi, haynu Ben Batera* — if that is what the *mishnah* means, then the *mishnah* is simply agreeing with Ben Batera, which cannot be right, since Ben Batera's view is presented as a minority dissent. The attempt to read the Rabbis as more lenient than Ben Batera — as permitting do-overs in a wider range of cases while Ben Batera merely adds one more — collapses under this pressure.

### Lacking Handful Debate

The *gemara* then proposes a different resolution: perhaps the Rabbis and Ben Batera do not argue at all when the original *kometz* is still intact and available. *Ee de'itay lekometz be'einay, lo pligi Rabbanan alei deBen Batera* — if the same handful can simply be returned as-is, perhaps everyone agrees that a do-over is permitted. The disagreement, on this reading, is limited to *dechasar kometz* — a case where the *kometz* is no longer available and must be replenished. There, the Rabbis hold *lo yavi mitoch beito vayamleinu* — one may not bring fresh flour from home to refill the handful — while Ben Batera holds *yavi mitoch beito vayamleinu*, that one may indeed do so.

### Refilling from Home

This reconstruction radically rewrites the *mishnah*, locating the entire dispute at the point where the original *kometz* has been lost and must be reconstituted from home. But the *gemara* finds this unacceptable. The language of the *mishnah* itself undermines the reading: Ben Batera says *yachzir veyachzor veyikmotz biyemino* — he should return it and take the handful again with his right hand. If the dispute were about refilling from home, Ben Batera should have said *yachzir veyavi mitoch beito vayamleinu veyachzor veyikmotz biyemino*. The plain language of the *mishnah* is clear: Ben Batera is speaking of returning the original *kometz*, and the Rabbis reject do-overs even in that case.

### Rav per Ben Batera

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא אָמַר רַב לְבֶן בְּתִירָא, פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: עַד כָּאן לָא קָא מַכְשַׁר בֶּן בְּתִירָא אֶלָּא בִּשְׂמֹאל, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר פְּסוּלִין לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore suggests a different answer: **When Rav said** that the handful is returned to the meal offering, he said this only **according to** the opinion of **ben Beteira.** The Gemara asks: But isn't it **obvious** that this is the opinion of ben Beteira? The Gemara responds: Rav's statement is necessary **lest you say** that **ben Beteira deems** the meal offering **fit only** when the handful was removed **with** one's **left** hand, **but with regard to other disqualifications,** he does **not** deem it fit. Therefore, Rav **teaches us** that with regard to all of the disqualifications cited in the mishna, ben Beteira holds that the handful is returned to the meal offering whereupon a new handful is removed from it and sacrificed upon the altar.

We are thus returned to the original question: what is the force of Rav's statement that a *zar* who performed the *kmitzah* may redo it? The *gemara* answers: *ki ka'amar Rav, leBen Batera* — Rav was speaking in accordance with Ben Batera's position, not the Rabbis'. The Rabbis clearly reject do-overs altogether. Rav's statement is situated within the framework of Ben Batera, extending it to a case beyond the left hand.

## Left Hand Analysis

### Left Hand Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא שְׂמֹאל, דְּאַשְׁכְּחַן לֵהּ הֶכְשֵׁירָא בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים? זָר נָמֵי אַשְׁכְּחַן לֵהּ הֶכְשֵׁירָא בִּשְׁחִיטָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in the case of a handful removed with a priest's **left** hand that one might have thought that ben Beteira's opinion applies only in this case? Perhaps the reason is **that we find** that the Temple service performed with the priest's left hand is **fit on Yom Kippur,** i.e., when the High Priest would enter the Holy of Holies holding the vessel containing the incense in his left hand. But with regard to **a non-priest as well, we find** that the Temple service is **fit with regard to slaughter,** as an offering may be slaughtered by a non-priest. Why then is it necessary for Rav to teach that the opinion of ben Beteira applies to the case of a non-priest as well?

Having established that Rav follows Ben Batera and extends the do-over principle beyond the left hand, the *gemara* now probes whether Rav's extension was necessary. The *gemara* reasons that one might well have thought Ben Batera's leniency was *specific* to the left hand, and therefore Rav had to tell us it applies more broadly. The question is: what is the logical basis for singling out the left hand as a distinctive case? The *gemara* answers: *mai shna smol, dashkechan lehachshara b'Yom Kippurim smol* — the left hand has a context in which it is entirely valid, namely on *Yom Kippurim*, when the *Kohen Gadol* carries the incense pan in his left hand as he enters the *Kodesh Kodashim*. Since the left hand is not categorically excluded from valid service, one might reason that it is less severely disqualified than other invalidities, and that Ben Batera's permission to redo applies specifically there.

### Zar in Shechita

The *gemara* then asks whether the same logic could apply to a *zar*: is there not a context in which a non-*kohen* performs a valid Temple act? *Ashkechan lehachshara b'shechitah* — *shechitah* can always be performed by a non-*kohen*, even in the *mikdash*. This might suggest that a *zar* is not categorically excluded from Temple service either.

### Shechita Not Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחִיטָה לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Slaughter is not** considered a sacrificial **rite,** and it is for that reason that a non-priest may slaughter an offering. Accordingly, without the statement of Rav one would not have concluded that ben Beteira holds that a handful removed by a non-priest may be returned, as the Temple service is never fit when performed by a non-priest.

The *gemara* rejects this parallel: *shechitah lav avodah hi* — *shechitah* is not an *avodah* in the technical sense. Therefore, the fact that a *zar* may perform *shechitah* does not demonstrate that we ever allow a *zar* to perform an actual priestly *avodah*. Rashi often explains this by saying that *shechitah* is permitted to a *zar* and therefore we know it is not an *avodah* — but Tosafot objects that this would make the *gemara*'s reasoning circular. Tosafot instead offers two independent grounds: first, that *shechitah* does not require the performer to stand within the *azarah* proper, whereas any genuine *avodah* requires standing within the *Beit HaMikdash*; and second, that *shechitah* is not a distinctively Temple act — it is performed for all animals whenever one wishes to eat meat — and therefore even when it occurs within the *mikdash*, it retains its generic character and does not become a Temple *avodah*.

### Parah Aduma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: שְׁחִיטַת פָּרָה בְּזָר פְּסוּלָה, וְאָמַר רַב עֲלַהּ: ״אֶלְעָזָר״ וְ״חוּקָּה״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ. שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, דְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is slaughter really **not** considered a sacrificial rite? **But doesn't Rabbi Zeira say** that **Rav says: The slaughter of** a red **heifer by a non-priest** is **not valid? And Rav said with regard to this** *halakha*: This is because both the term: **"Elazar** the priest" (Numbers 19:3), **and** the term: **"Statute"** (Numbers 19:2), **are written in** the Torah's description of the slaughter of the red heifer. The term "statute" indicates that if one deviates from any of the details of the service as delineated in the verses, the service is not valid. The Gemara answers: The *halakhot* of the red **heifer are different, as** the heifer **is** considered **sanctified for** the purpose of **Temple maintenance,** not for sacrifice upon the altar. Accordingly, one cannot derive from the case of the red heifer that the slaughter of an offering is considered a sacrificial rite.

The *gemara* then raises a complication: did not Rabbi Zeira say in the name of Rav, *shechitat parah b'zar psulah* — that if a *zar* slaughters the *parah adumah*, it is invalid? And Rav himself explained: *Elazar v'chukah ktiv bah* — the Torah mentions Elazar and uses the word *chok*, indicating that a *kohen* is required. So we see that even for *shechitah* — which we just said is not an *avodah* — a *kohen* is necessary in this case, and a *zar* is excluded. This seems to undercut the argument that *shechitah* proves a *zar* is sometimes valid.

### Parah Kedushá

The *gemara* responds: *shani parah, d'kodshei bedek habayit hi* — the *parah adumah* is different, because its *kedushah* is not *kedushat mizbe'ach*. Since the *parah adumah* is slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, it cannot be a *korban* in the full sense. The *gemara* describes it as having *kedushat bedek habayit*, which in this context means any sanctity that does not fall under *kedushat mizbe'ach*. This is a somewhat imprecise use of the term, since *bedek habayit* ordinarily refers to building materials and *klei sharet*, but the *gemara*'s intent is that the *parah adumah* represents a sui generis form of *kedushah* that cannot serve as a model for standard Temple *avodot*.

### Tzara'at Negai'm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו כָּל דְּכֵן הוּא? קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת בָּעוּ כְּהוּנָּה, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לָא בָּעוּ כְּהוּנָּה?! אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמַּרְאוֹת נְגָעִים, דְּלָאו עֲבוֹדָה נִינְהוּ וּבָעֵי כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is it not all the more so?** If items **sanctified for** the purpose of **Temple maintenance require** that the performance of rites relevant to them be performed by a member of the **priesthood,** then with regard to items **sanctified for** sacrifice upon the **altar, shouldn't they** certainly **require** that their rites be performed by a member of the **priesthood? Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said** in response: The requirement that the red heifer be slaughtered by a priest is not proof that its slaughter is a sacrificial rite. Rather, that requirement is **just as it is in** the case of examination of the **shades of** leprous **marks, which is** obviously **not** considered a sacrificial **rite, and** yet the Torah **requires** that these marks be examined by a member of the **priesthood.**

But the *gemara* then proposes a *kal vachomer*: if something that is not a full *korban* requires a *kohen*, should not a *korban* itself require a *kohen* all the more? This would mean that when a *zar* performs *shechitah* on a *korban*, it is not a true exception but rather an anomaly — and therefore, just as with the left hand, one might argue that Ben Batera's do-over principle should apply here too. The *gemara* rejects this *kal vachomer* by citing Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi: there are obligations that require *kohanim* even though they have nothing to do with Temple *avodot*, such as the declaration of *tamei* or *tahor* in cases of *tzara'at*. A *talmid chacham* may examine the blemish and instruct the *kohen*, but only the *kohen* may pronounce the words. Since *kohanim* can be required outside the context of *avodah*, the fact that *parah adumah* requires a *kohen* proves nothing about whether *shechitah* of *korbanot* in principle requires one.

### Shechita Logic

The conclusion is thus that *shechitat korbanot* in principle does not require a *kohen*, it is not an *avodah*, and the permission for a *zar* to perform it is not an exception at all. The *gemara* therefore maintains the original logical structure: the left hand has a genuine parallel where it is used in valid service (*Yom Kippurim*), giving a plausible reason to think Ben Batera's do-over was limited to that case. There is no comparable parallel for a *zar*. This establishes why Rav needed to teach that Ben Batera's principle extends beyond the left hand — there was a strong reason to have thought otherwise. It is worth noting an interesting textual curiosity along the way: the *gemara* reports that *Rabbi Zeira amar Rav* stated that the *shechitah* of the *parah* by a *zar* is invalid, and then records *v'amar Rav alah* — Rav commenting on his own statement as transmitted by Rabbi Zeira. The best explanation is that by the time the *gemara* was crystallized in its current form, Rabbi Zeira was the one who had originally published and circulated Rav's ruling; it was remembered as having come through his mouth, while Rav's subsequent clarification was recorded separately.

## Baraita & Ben Batera

### Baraita Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן רַב: קָמַץ וַאֲפִילּוּ קִידֵּשׁ, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹסֵי בֶּן יָאסְיָין וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנַּחְתּוֹם אָמְרוּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁקָּמַץ וְלֹא קִידֵּשׁ, אֲבָל קִידֵּשׁ – פָּסַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore suggests an alternative explanation: **Rather, this** is what **Rav teaches us:** Ben Beteira holds that if one unfit for Temple service **removed a handful** it may be returned to the meal offering **even** if **he sanctified** the handful by placing it in a service vessel. **And** the statement of Rav serves **to exclude** the opinion **of these *tanna'im*: As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei ben Yosei ben Yasiyyan and Rabbi Yehuda the baker said: In what** case **is this statement** of ben Beteira **said,** i.e., in which case does he rule that the handful may be returned to the meal offering? It is in a case **where** the unfit individual **removed a handful and did not sanctify** it by placing it inside a service vessel. **But** if **he sanctified** it then **he** has **disqualified** it, even according to ben Beteira, and it may not be returned to the meal offering.

Having established that Rav had good reason to extend Ben Batera's principle beyond the left hand, the *gemara* raises a further challenge. It cites a *baraita*: *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon omrim, machzir haya Ben Batera b'chol ha'psulim kulam* — these two *tanna'im* explicitly state that Ben Batera permitted a do-over for all invalidities, not just the left hand. If such a *baraita* exists and makes the extension explicit, who needs Rav? Rav's statement appears redundant.

One might respond that not everyone had access to this *baraita*, and Rav was teaching the same principle for those who did not know it. But the more precise analysis is that there is a genuine *machloket tanna'im* here. The *mishnah*, as incorporated by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, appears to reflect the view that Ben Batera spoke only of the left hand — for the logical reason we articulated, that the left hand has a valid application on *Yom Kippurim*. These two *tanna'im* in the *baraita* held that Ben Batera extended the principle to all invalidities. Rav, on this reading, was siding with the *baraita* against the implicit position of the *mishnah*. The *gemara*, however, does not frame it this way; instead, it treats the *baraita* as straightforwardly clarifying Ben Batera, and therefore presses: given the *baraita*, what remains for Rav to add?

### Komatz Misham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: ״וְקָמַץ מִשָּׁם״ – מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁרַגְלֵי הַזָּר עוֹמְדוֹת. בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם קָמַץ בִּשְׂמֹאל שֶׁיַּחֲזִיר וְיַחֲזוֹר וְיִקְמוֹץ בְּיָמִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקָמַץ מִשָּׁם״, מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁקָּמַץ כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Ben Beteira says** that the verse should be interpreted as follows: **From where** is it derived **that if** one **removed a handful with** his **left** hand, **that he must return** the handful to the vessel with the meal offering **and again remove the handful with** his **right** hand? It is derived from that which **the verse states: "And he shall remove from there,"** indicating that the handful is removed **from the place where he already removed** it, i.e., the handful is returned to the meal offering and thereupon removed from the same meal offering with the right hand. This concludes the *baraita*.

The *baraita* derives its ruling from a *pasuk*. The verse states *v'kamatz misham* — the *kohen* shall take the handful from there. The *tanna kamma* understands "from there" to mean from the place where the *zar* stands, establishing that the *kmitzah* may be taken anywhere in the *azarah*, including the area normally reserved for non-*kohanim*. Ben Batera, however, learns a different lesson: *minayin she'im kamatz b'smol she'yachazir v'yachzor v'yikmotz b'yamin? Talmud lomar v'kamatz misham* — the verse teaches that the *kmitzah* should be taken from the place from which it was already taken, i.e., it may be returned and taken again. This is the *pasuk*ic basis for Ben Batera's do-over principle.

### Stam Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דִּקְרָא סְתָמָא כְּתִיב בַּהּ, מָה לִי שְׂמֹאל, וּמָה לִי שְׁאָר הַפְּסוּלִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty from the *baraita*: **And since the verse is written** in an **unspecified** manner, i.e., it does not mention which handfuls are returned, **what** difference is it **to me** if the handful was removed with the **left** hand, **and what** difference is it **to me** if it was removed by means of one of **the other disqualifications?**

Since the *pasuk* is written *stam* — without qualification — specifying neither the left hand nor any other particular invalidity, the *gemara* concludes that it gives no reason to limit Ben Batera's ruling to the left hand. *Mah li smol, mah li sha'ar psulim* — the same reasoning applies to any disqualification. Ben Batera's position, properly understood from its textual basis, extends to all invalidities.

### Rav Redundancy

This brings the *gemara* back to its pointed question: if the *baraita* makes all of this explicit, what is Rav's *chiddush*?

## Kamatz v'Afilu Kidesh

### Tannai'm on Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: קָמַץ – אִין, קִידֵּשׁ – לָא. כְּמַאן? כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִתַּנָּא קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that Rav teaches the opposite, that if an unfit person merely **removed a handful, yes,** ben Beteira permits such a handful to be returned to the meal offering from which it was removed, but if he already **sanctified** the handful by placing it inside a service vessel, it may **not** be returned. **In accordance with whose** opinion is this explanation? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **these *tanna'im*,** i.e., Rabbi Yosei ben Yosei ben Yasiyyan and Rabbi Yehuda the baker, **and** it is **to exclude** the opinion **of the first *tanna*** in that *baraita*, who disagrees with the ruling of Rabbi Yosei ben Yosei ben Yasiyyan and Rabbi Yehuda the baker and maintains that a handful may be returned even after it was sanctified by a service vessel.

The *gemara* now answers by identifying a different dimension of Rav's teaching. The question is not simply whether Ben Batera's principle applies beyond the left hand, but rather: *until when* does the possibility of a do-over remain open? Is there a point in the process after which reversal is no longer permitted? The *gemara* presents the issue with the phrase *kamatz va'afilu kidesh* — Rav's teaching addresses the case where the *kohen* not only performed the *kmitzah* but also placed the *kometz* into the second *kli sharet*, thereby sanctifying it at the next stage. Rav holds that even at that point, the do-over remains available.

This is a meaningful *chiddush* because one might have assumed that placing the *kometz* into the second *kli sharet* completes the act and forecloses reversal — just as in chess, once you release the piece, you cannot take the move back. Once the *kometz* enters the vessel, the first act is done and the process has moved to its next stage.

### HaNachtom Limit

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi Yosi ben Yosi ben Yassian and Rabbi Yehuda HaNachtom (the baker — the *gemara* notes this epithet with evident interest, suggesting his expertise in flour lent him particular authority in *menachot* discussions) rule: *bameh devarim amurim shekamatz v'lo kidesh* — Ben Batera's do-over applies only if the *kometz* was taken but not yet placed into the second vessel. *Aval kidesh, kidesh pasal* — once it has been placed in the second *kli sharet*, it is invalidated and no do-over is possible. There is also a version of the ruling that simply states *kamatz in, kidesh lo* — if only the *kmitzah* has been done, yes to a do-over; once it has been placed in the vessel, no.

On this reading, Rav's statement would not be extending Ben Batera but rather agreeing with the *tanna kamma* who opposes these two *tanna'im* — and rejecting the view of Rabbi Yehuda HaNachtom and his colleague. Rav's *chiddush* is to say that even *kamatz va'afilu kidesh*, even after the placement in the second *kli sharet*, a do-over remains available.

### Rav Nachman Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: מַאי קָא סָבְרִי הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי? אִי קְמִיצַת פְּסוּלִים עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא עָבֵיד לֵיהּ מַתַּן כְּלִי! אִי קְמִיצַת פְּסוּלִים לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, כִּי עֲבַד לֵהּ מַתַּן כְּלִי מַאי הֲוָה? הֲדַר אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: לְעוֹלָם עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, וְלָא גָּמְרָה עֲבוֹדָתָהּ עַד דְּעָבֵיד לֵהּ מַתַּן כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman objects to this: What do these *tanna'im*** of the *baraita* **hold? If** they hold that **the removal of a handful by** one **unfit** for Temple service **is** considered the performance of a sacrificial **rite** to the extent that it disqualifies the offering, then the meal offering should be disqualified **even though he did not perform** the stage of the **placement** of the handful **in a vessel.** And **if** they hold that **the removal of a handful by an unfit** individual **is not** considered the performance of a **rite,** then **when he performed** the stage of **its placement in a vessel, what** significance **was** there to this action? He can still return the handful to the meal offering. **Rav Naḥman** reconsidered and **then said: Actually,** those *tanna'im* hold that the removal of a handful by one unfit for Temple service **is** considered the performance of a sacrificial **rite, but the rite of** the handful **is not complete until he performs** the stage of **its placement in a vessel.** Consequently, the meal offering is disqualified only after a person unfit for Temple service places the handful inside a service vessel.

Rav Nachman then poses a sharp analytical question: *mai kasvari hanei tanna'im* — what exactly are these *tanna'im* holding? There are two possible logical frameworks. If a *kmitzah* performed by an invalid person is itself considered a valid *avodah* — albeit a flawed one — then from the moment the *kmitzah* is taken it has the status of a *pasul avodah*, and even before the second vessel no do-over should be available. Conversely, if a *kmitzah* performed by an invalid person is so defective that it is considered as though no *avodah* occurred at all, then placing it in the second vessel should make no difference — there would never be any reason to foreclose the do-over.

Rav Nachman then resolves his own difficulty: *le'olam avodah hi* — it is indeed an *avodah*, but a flawed one, and here is the key: *dela gamrah avodatah ad de'avdieh lamatten kli* — the *avodah* is not complete until the act of placing in the second vessel. While the *avodah* remains in progress, correction is still possible. Once the *avodah* is fully executed and becomes past history, it can no longer be undone. This analysis is elegant and makes considerable sense: it is not that the *kmitzah* was nothing, but that it was an ongoing *avodah* that was still capable of being remedied until its completion. When it is complete, it is fixed — for better or worse.

One might ask what those who allow do-overs even after the second vessel would say. They may hold the alternative position Rav Nachman considered and then set aside — that an invalid *kmitzah* is not considered an *avodah* at all, and therefore the process can always be restarted, conceivably even after *holacha*.

## Kli Sharet Midaat

### Reish Lakish Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא מִדַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this challenge, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That is to say** that **service vessels sanctify** items placed in them **only** when they are placed there **with** specific **intent** that they be sanctified by that vessel. Since the priest does not return the handful to the vessel containing the meal offering with such intent, the handful is not disqualified, because the rite was not completed.

The *gemara* now shifts its focus from Ben Batera's do-over principle to a related but distinct question: how does the *kli sharet* function in the *minchah* process? This becomes necessary because of a difficulty the *gemara* raises: if we grant that a do-over is possible, then when the *kohen* returns the *kometz* to the original *kli sharet* in order to perform the *kmitzah* again, why doesn't that act of returning itself count as the second stage? The original *kli sharet* would now be receiving the *kometz* for the second time, and nothing in principle requires it to be a *different* vessel. Tosafot notes that the presence of the remaining flour does not create a *chatzitzah* problem, since *min b'mino lo chotzetz*.

This question opens a broader inquiry into the mechanics of *kli sharet*. Unlike in *korbanot* involving animals, where the *kli sharet* appears primarily in the catching of the blood, the *minchah* involves numerous vessels at multiple stages — the flour is brought in a vessel, measured, mixed, the *kmitzah* is taken and placed in a vessel, and so forth. There is thus far more occasion in Menachot to examine precisely how *kli sharet* operates.

Against this backdrop, Reish Lakish poses a striking question to Rabbi Yochanan: *klei sharet ma sheyakdishu pasul l'chatchilah* — can a *kli sharet* take something that is invalid and render it fit to be offered? The question sounds radical, and it is. The underlying intuition is something like the principle of *hamizbe'ach mekadesh* — that the altar can sanctify objects placed upon it. Perhaps the *kedushah* of the *kli sharet*, when intentionally invoked, can similarly transform the status of a disqualified *kometz* and make it presentable on the *mizbe'ach*. Tosafot asks the obvious: if so, why could we not eliminate all *psulei zevachim* by placing the blood in a *kli sharet* with the intent to sanctify? Tosafot's response is that perhaps the *hava amina* was more limited — not that the *kli sharet* makes the *korban* fully valid and allows one to be *yotzei*, but rather that being in a *kli sharet* is analogous to being on the *mizbe'ach*, and therefore the physical material might at least be burned rather than destroyed. Even so, it is a remarkable question.

### Yochanan Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין לִיקְרַב, אֲבָל מִקַּדְּשִׁין לִיפָּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** This is what Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: They do not sanctify** the disqualified items that are placed inside them **to** the extent that they may be **sacrificed, but they** do **sanctify** them **to** the extent **that they** **are disqualified.**

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *ein mekadesh* — the *kli sharet* cannot accomplish this. Even with full intent, it cannot take something invalid and make it fit to be offered. This answer is decisive and closes the *hava amina*.

### Midaat Requirement

But the *gemara* now refines the picture through a crucial distinction. Rabbi Yochanan's negative answer — *ein mekadesh* — does not mean the *kli sharet* has no effect at all. The *gemara* formulates it thus: *ein mekadesh l'kareiv, aval mekadesh l'pasel* — the *kli sharet* cannot render an invalid *kometz* fit to be offered, but it can designate it as having reached the second stage, which forecloses the do-over. This is not a full *kedushah* sufficient for the *mizbe'ach*, but it is enough to mark the process as having advanced. This distinction is coherent: the *kli sharet* draws the object into the next stage of the procedure even if it cannot repair its underlying defect.

This leads to a further principle, stated explicitly: *klei sharet ein mekadesh ela mida'as* — a *kli sharet* does not sanctify unless done with intent. When the *kohen* returns the invalid *kometz* to the original vessel as part of a do-over, he is not intending to advance the process to stage two — he is attempting to reverse it. Since the *kli sharet* requires *da'as*, and the *kohen*'s *da'as* here is directed toward reversal rather than sanctification, the vessel does not function to mark completion of the first stage. Therefore, the return of the *kometz* to the original vessel does not foreclose the do-over.

This principle — *klei sharet ein mekadesh ela mida'as* — is one of the important conceptual contributions of Menachot to the laws of *kli sharet*. It reveals that *kedushah* in this domain is not automatic and not mechanical; it depends on the intent of the one performing the act. Just as *piggul* and related concepts demonstrate the centrality of thought in *avodah*, here too the sanctifying power of the vessel is contingent on conscious direction. A student raises a sharp observation: the altar operates by the principle of *hamizbe'ach mekadesh*, which seems almost automatic — placing something on the *mizbe'ach* creates a connection that allows it to remain. Yet by *kli sharet* we do not apply that logic. The *gemara*'s implicit answer is that *kli sharet* and the *mizbe'ach* operate by different principles, and the centrality of *kavanah* is especially pronounced in the *kli sharet* domain.

## Vessel on Ground

### Overflowing Handful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ לְבִיסָא גְּדוּשָׁה. וּמִקְמָץ הֵיכִי קְמַץ? אֶלָּא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ לְבִיסָא טְפוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram says:** Even if service vessels sanctify items without specific intent, it is possible to return the handful to the meal offering without the vessel sanctifying the handful, **such as** in a case **where he returned it to a heaped bowl [*levisa*],** i.e., he placed the handful upon the heap of flour in such a manner that the handful did not enter the airspace of the vessel containing the meal offering. Consequently, the handful is not sanctified by the vessel. The Gemara asks: **But** if the meal offering was heaped, **how** was he initially able to **remove a handful** from it? The handful must initially be removed from within a vessel. **Rather,** it is possible to return the handful without sanctifying it in a case **where he returned it to a full [*tefufa*] bowl,** i.e., it was full to the brim but not heaped. When the priest initially removes a handful from such a vessel, he removes it from inside the vessel, but when it is returned, it does not enter the airspace of the vessel.

The *gemara* now presents a related scenario via a statement of Rav Amram: *kegon shehechiziru l'visa gedushah* — the case where the do-over fails is one in which the *kometz* was returned to a vessel that was already overflowing. When the *minchah* fills the vessel to overflowing and the *kometz* is placed on top of the mound of flour rather than inside the vessel itself, the *kli sharet* cannot function, because only what is contained within the walls of the vessel is sanctified by it — not what spills above the rim.

### Same Vessel Return

The *gemara* asks: how was the *kmitzah* performed in the first place on such a *minchah*? A vessel overflowing with flour would itself seem to be problematic for the initial *kmitzah*. The *gemara* clarifies: *hacha shehechiziru l'visa tefufa* — here the *minchah* was returned to a vessel that was flattened down to the lip of the vessel, packed level.

### Lip vs. Inside

The problem, then, is whether placing the *kometz* on the flat, level surface at the rim counts as placing it inside the vessel. The *gemara* treats the rim and the interior as meaningfully distinct: only what is genuinely inside the walls of the vessel is subject to its *kedushah*.

### Monkey Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דִּקְמַץ לֵיהּ עֲבַד לֵיהּ גּוּמָּא, כִּי מַהְדַּר לְגַוֵּויהּ דְּמָנָא קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ! מִכִּי מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, מַנַּח לֵיהּ אַדֻּפְנָא דְּמָנָא, וּמְנִיד לֵיהּ וְנָפֵל מִמֵּילָא, דְּנַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ הַקּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But once he removed a handful, he formed a furrow** in the surface of the meal offering, and therefore **when he returns** the handful to its previous place inside the vessel, **he is** in fact **returning it to** a spot **within the vessel,** i.e., the furrow. If so, the handful should be sanctified to the extent that the vessel disqualifies it. The Gemara responds: **When he returns it** to the vessel containing the meal offering, he does not place it directly in the furrow. Rather, he **lays it on the wall of the vessel and moves** the vessel, **and** the handful **falls by itself** into the furrow. In this manner, **it is as though a monkey** rather than a person **returned** the handful to the furrow, and the handful is therefore not sanctified.

But then the *gemara* raises a further subtlety: when the *kohen* made the original *kmitzah*, he created a hollow — an indentation — in the flour. When he returns the *kometz*, it goes back into that hollow and thereby falls into the vessel. Should this not count as returning it to the interior of the vessel? The *gemara* answers: *miki mahadar lei, manach lei adafna demana* — when he returns it, he places it at the wall of the vessel, and *venafal mimeila* — it falls in on its own. *Dena'aseh kemi shehechiziro ha'kof* — it is as though the monkey returned it. The image of the monkey is invoked in the *gemara* as a paradigm for an act that occurs without genuine human agency. The *kohen* did not actively place the *kometz* inside the vessel; his movement caused it to slide in passively. Since the *kli sharet* requires a conscious act of placement — even if it does not require specific *kavanah* to sanctify — the passive sliding does not count. There is no act, and therefore no sanctification.

This teaches a second important principle about *kli sharet*: even if we set aside the question of *da'as*, the vessel requires an act. A passive or inadvertent entry into the vessel is insufficient. The *kohen* must actively place the *kometz* within.

### Vessel Holding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: וְלוֹקְמַהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ לִכְלִי הַמּוּנָּח עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע! אֶלָּא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קוֹמְצִין מִכְּלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the opinion of ben Beteira. **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira: And let** one **interpret** ben Beteira's ruling as speaking of a case in which the handful is not sanctified by the vessel containing the meal offering, **such as where he returned it to a vessel that is resting upon** the **ground. Rather,** the fact that this was not suggested indicates that service vessels sanctify items placed inside them even while resting on the ground. Is it correct to **conclude from** here that **one may remove a handful** of a meal offering **from** a service **vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground?**

With the analysis of the monkey act complete, Rabbi Yirmiya poses the question to Rabbi Zeira: *velokma kegon shehechezira likli hamunach al gabei karka* — why did you not simply say the reason the return fails is that the *kohen* placed the *kometz* into a *kli* that was resting on the ground, without a *kohen* holding it? This raises the question of whether the *kli sharet* must be held by a *kohen* in order to function. One might reason that just as the *kohen* must stand on the ground — grounded human agency creates the sacred context — so too the vessel must be held and connected to that human presence. Without it, the *kli sharet* is inert.

Rabbi Yirmiya notes: since Rabbi Zeira did not offer this explanation — a vessel on the ground — the implication is that such an explanation is not available. *Shema mina, komtzi mikli she'al gabei karka* — learn from this that one may indeed perform the *kmitzah* from a *kli* resting on the ground. Holding is not required.

### Rav Avimi Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קָא נָגְעַתְּ בְּבַעְיָא דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לַן, דְּרַבִּי אֲבִימִי תָּנֵי מְנָחוֹת בֵּי רַב חִסְדָּא. וַאֲבִימִי בֵּי רַב חִסְדָּא תָּנֵי? וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: קוּלְפֵי טָאבֵי בְּלַעִי מֵאֲבִימִי עֲלַהּ דְּהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, בָּא לְהַכְרִיז רְצוּפִין – שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, שֵׁנִי וַחֲמִישִׁי וְשֵׁנִי – שִׁשִּׁים יוֹם. אֲבִימִי מַסֶּכְתָּא אִיתְעֲקַרָא לֵיהּ, וַאֲתָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְאִדְּכוֹרֵי גְּמָרֵיהּ. וְלִישְׁלַח לֵיהּ וְלֵיתֵי לְגַבֵּיהּ? סְבַר: הָכִי מִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִילְּתָא טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to him: You have touched upon a dilemma that was** already **raised before us, when Rabbi Avimi** was **learning** tractate ***Menaḥot*** in the **study hall of Rav Ḥisda.** The Gemara interrupts this statement with a question: **And** did Rabbi **Avimi** really **learn** in the **study hall of Rav Ḥisda? But didn't Rav Ḥisda say: I absorbed many blows [*kulfei*] from Avimi** as a result **of that *halakha*,** i.e., Avimi would mock me when I questioned his statements with regard to the sale of orphans' property by the courts, which were contradictory to the ruling of a particular *baraita*. Avimi explained to me that if the court **comes to announce** such a sale on **consecutive** days, then it is announced for **thirty days,** in accordance with that *baraita*. But if it will be announced only on **Monday, Thursday, and Monday,** then it is announced over the course of **sixty days.** If so, Rav Ḥisda was in fact the pupil while Rabbi Avimi was his teacher. The Gemara answers: **Avimi** was in fact the teacher, but **tractate** *Menaḥot* **was uprooted for him,** i.e., he forgot it, **and** Avimi **came before** his student **Rav Ḥisda to** help him **recall his learning.** The Gemara asks: If Rav Ḥisda was in fact Avimi's student, **let** Avimi **send for him and** Rav Ḥisda **come to** Avimi. The Gemara responds: Avimi **thought** that **this** would be **more helpful** in this **matter,** i.e., that by exerting the effort to travel to his pupil in order to learn from him, he would better retain his studies.

Rabbi Yirmiya adds that this was precisely the question being asked in the *beit midrash*. He relates an episode involving Rav Avimi, who had taught *menachot* in the academy of Rav Chisda. The *gemara* notes the apparent oddity: Rav Avimi was Rav Chisda's own *rebbe*, so why would he be learning *menachot* in Rav Chisda's house? The *gemara* explains: *masechta it'akra it'aker lei* — the *masechet* had been uprooted from him. Rav Avimi had lost his command of the text of *menachot* — the oral tradition he had memorized — and he needed to recover it. In a culture of oral transmission, mastery of the text was the essential prerequisite for all further analysis. Even a scholar of Rav Avimi's stature could find himself needing to relearn the words from a younger colleague who held them in memory. He chose to go to Rav Chisda himself rather than summon Rav Chisda to him — *savar hachi mistaya milta tfei* — reasoning that he would receive greater *siyata dishmaya*, divine assistance, by humbling himself and going as a student rather than demanding that his former student come to him.

The *gemara* mentions a related tradition about Rav Avimi's character: Rav Chisda once said he had received many *kufi tabi* — strikes — from Rav Avimi over the teaching that when a court auctions property for orphans and makes announcements daily, thirty days suffice, but when announcements are made only on Mondays and Thursdays (market days), sixty days are required, though spanning a longer calendar period. Rav Chisda praised this teaching and Rav Avimi apparently reprimanded him for circulating the praise — consistent with Rav Avimi's evident humility.

### Nachman Avimi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּגַע בֵּיהּ רַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֵּיצַד קוֹמְצִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִכְּלִי זֶה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי קוֹמְצִין מִכְּלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּמַגְבַּה לֵיהּ כֹּהֵן. כֵּיצַד מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַמְּנָחוֹת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נוֹתְנָהּ לִכְלִי זֶה. וְכִי מְקַדְּשִׁין בִּכְלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּמַגְבַּה לֵיהּ כֹּהֵן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, הוּצְרַכְתָּה שְׁלֹשָׁה כֹּהֲנִים! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּתְהֵא צְרִיכָה שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר, כַּתָּמִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman encountered** Avimi upon his return from the study hall of Rav Ḥisda. Rav Naḥman **said to him: How** does one properly **remove a handful** from a meal offering? Avimi pointed to a vessel that was resting on the ground and **said to him: From this vessel** one may properly remove a handful. Rav Naḥman **said to him: But may one remove a handful from a vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground?** Avimi **said to him:** When I said that such a vessel may be used, I meant **that** one **priest** would first **raise it** from the ground and then another priest would remove a handful from it. Rav Naḥman proceeded to ask Avimi another question: **How** does one properly **sanctify the meal offerings?** Avimi pointed to a vessel that was resting on the ground and **said to him:** The priest **places it into this vessel.** Rav Naḥman again said to him: **But can one sanctify** a meal offering **in a vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground?** Avimi **said to him:** When I said that such a vessel may be used, I meant **that** another **priest** would initially **raise it** from the ground, and only then would the meal offering be placed inside it. Rav Naḥman **said to** Avimi: **If so,** then **you require** the involvement of **three priests,** i.e., one to raise the vessel, one to perform the *kmitzah* with his right hand, and one to hold the second vessel. Avimi **said to him: And let it require** even **thirteen** priests, **just as** the service of the **daily** burnt **offering** required the involvement of thirteen priests. The need for several priests presents no difficulty.

The story continues. Rav Nachman encountered Rav Avimi — presumably while Rav Avimi was at Rav Chisda's house — and asked him: *keitzad komtzin* — how does one perform the *kmitzah*? Rav Avimi pointed to a vessel near at hand, on the ground, and said: from a *kli* like this one. Rav Nachman challenged him: can you really perform *kmitzah* from a vessel that is on the ground? Rav Avimi immediately backed away: *d'magbah lei kohen* — I meant while a *kohen* is holding it. Rav Nachman then asked: *keitzad mekadshin et haminchos* — how does one sanctify the *menachot* into the next vessel? Again Rav Avimi pointed to a vessel and said: you place it into such a vessel. And again Rav Nachman challenged: can one sanctify into a vessel on the ground? Again Rav Avimi retreated: I meant that a *kohen* was holding it.

Rav Nachman then drew out the implication: *im kein, hutzrachta gimmel kohanim* — if a *kohen* must hold each vessel, you need three *kohanim* for the process: one to hold the original vessel, one to perform the *kmitzah* with his right hand, and one to hold the second vessel. Rav Avimi responded: *v'tei tlisha-sar k'tamid* — so what? We know the *tamid shel shachar* involved thirteen *kohanim* carrying limbs to the *mizbe'ach*. Having three *kohanim* is not inherently problematic.

### Sheshes Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוּ מִינֵּיהּ מִדְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַהוּ לִקְמוֹץ מִכְּלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פּוֹק חֲזִי מָה עָבְדִין לְגָאו.
>
> **Translation:** On the same topic, the Sages **raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: What is** the *halakha* with regard to the permissibility of **removing a handful from a vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground?** Is this removal valid? Rav Sheshet **said to** one of the Sages who raised the dilemma: **Go out** and **see what they do within** the Sanctuary when they remove the bowls containing the frankincense that were placed upon the Table of the shewbread in order to burn the frankincense upon the altar.

Still, the question of whether a vessel must be held remained unresolved. The *gemara* then reports: *ba'u minei me'Rav Sheshes, mahoo likmotz mikli she'al gabei karka* — they asked Rav Sheshes the same question, whether *kmitzah* may be performed from a vessel resting on the ground. Rav Sheshes replied: *puk chazi mai avdin legav* — go look at what they did inside.

### Showbread Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבָּעָה כֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין, שְׁנַיִם בְּיָדָם שְׁנֵי סְדָרִים, וּשְׁנַיִם בְּיָדָם שְׁנֵי בָּזִיכִין, וְאַרְבָּעָה מְקַדְּמִין לִפְנֵיהֶם, שְׁנַיִם לִיטּוֹל שְׁנֵי סְדָרִים, וּשְׁנַיִם לִיטּוֹל שְׁנֵי בָּזִיכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna (99b) states: When the priests would replace the shewbread every Shabbat, **four priests** would **enter** the Sanctuary, **two** with the **two arrangements** of the new shewbread **in their hands and two** with the **two bowls** of frankincense **in their hands. And four** priests would **precede** them and enter the Sanctuary **before them, two to remove** the **two arrangements** of the old shewbread **and two to remove** the **two bowls** of frankincense.

Rav Sheshes directs attention to the procedure for the *lechem hapanim* — the showbread — on *Shabbat*. The *mishnah* describes it thus: four *kohanim* enter the *heichal*, two holding the two new columns of bread and two holding the two *bezichin* — the small spoons of frankincense. And four *kohanim* precede them to remove the old bread: two to take the two columns of bread being removed, and two to take out the two spoons of incense. The incense was then burned and the *kohanim* would eat the bread — the spoons and frankincense serving the role of the *kometz*, and the bread serving the role of the remainder of the *minchah*.

### Table Not Held

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ מַגְבִּיהַּ אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָן לָא קָתָנֵי. לָאו אָמְרַתְּ הָתָם, סֵדֶר עֲבוֹדוֹת נָקֵט? הָכָא נָמֵי סֵדֶר עֲבוֹדוֹת נָקֵט. מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא נָחֵית לְמִנְיָינָא דְּכֹהֲנִים, הָכָא נָחֵית לְמִנְיָינָא דְּכֹהֲנִים. אִם אִיתָא – לִיתְנֵי מַגְבִּיהַּ! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: קוֹמְצִין מִכְּלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet notes: The entire process of the replacement of the shewbread is taught in the mishna, **and yet** the statement: A priest **raises the Table** above the ground so that the bowls of frankincense can be properly removed from them, **is not taught.** One can therefore conclude from the mishna that just as the bowls of frankincense are removed from a vessel that is resting upon the ground, i.e., the Table, so too, one may remove a handful of a meal offering from a vessel that is resting upon the ground. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Didn't you** already **say there,** with regard to the mishna that discusses improper intentions expressed during the service of a meal offering (12a), that the *tanna* **cited** only **the order of** sacrificial **rites? Here too,** the *tanna* **cited** only **the order of** sacrificial **rites.** Therefore, one cannot prove from here that there is no requirement to raise the Table. The Gemara asks: **Are** these *mishnayot* **comparable? There,** on 12a, the *tanna* **did not delve into the number of priests** involved in the service of a meal offering. **Here,** on 99b, the *tanna* **does** in fact **delve into the number of priests** involved in the service of the shewbread. Therefore, **if it is so,** i.e., if the Table must be raised before the bowls of frankincense are removed, **let** the *tanna* **teach** that another priest **raises** the Table. **Rather, conclude from** the mishna that **one may remove a handful** of a meal offering **from a vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground.** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** here that this is so.

The critical observation: *v'ilu magbi'ah et hashulchan lo katani* — the *mishnah* does not mention any *kohen* whose function was to hold up the *shulchan*, the table. The table rested on the ground. And the bread was taken off the table and the new bread placed on it — all without anyone holding the table.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps the *mishnah* simply did not mention the one holding the table because it was only describing the *avodot* relevant for *piggul*, not all participants. This was the response given earlier when a similar question was raised from a different *mishnah*. But Rav Sheshes's proof is different in a crucial respect: *hacha nachit leminyana dechahanim* — this *mishnah* explicitly enumerates the number of *kohanim*. Four go in with the new items, four come out with the old. If anyone were holding the table, the *mishnah* would have counted him. The enumeration is precise and deliberate. The absence of a table-holder from the count demonstrates that no one held the table — and the service over the table was valid.

### Kometz Proof

The *gemara* draws the conclusion: *ela shema mina, kometz mikli al gabei karkah, shema mina* — learn from this that one may indeed perform the *kmitzah* from a *kli* resting on the ground. The vessel need not be held by a *kohen* for the *kli sharet* to function. The proof from the *shulchan*, while perhaps unexpected, is treated by the *gemara* as decisive. A table is of course naturally a floor-resting object, and one might have thought it different from a vessel that one would normally hold. But the *gemara* does not draw that distinction. Halachically, all *klei sharet* are treated uniformly, and if the *shulchan* — itself a *kli sharet* — functioned without being held, then any *kli sharet* may function without being held.

The practical implication is significant: only one *kohen* is required for the *kmitzah*, not three. The *ba'alim* — the owner — brings the *minchah*; the *kohen* takes the *kmitzah* and may either hand it to another *kohen* holding the second vessel or place it in a vessel resting on the ground.

This extended discussion of *kli sharet* yields three distinct principles. First, there is a debate regarding whether *kli sharet* requires *da'as* — explicit intent — to have its sanctifying effect; Rabbi Yochanan rules that it does. Second, even setting aside the question of intent, the *kli sharet* requires an actual conscious act of placement; passive entry — as in the monkey case — is insufficient. Third, the *kli sharet* can function effectively even when resting on the ground of the *mikdash*, without being held by a *kohen*.
