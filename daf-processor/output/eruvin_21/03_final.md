# Eruvin 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Passei Biraot Rules

### Olei Regalim Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַדָּא: לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לְעוֹלֵי רְגָלִים בִּלְבַד. וְהָתַנְיָא: לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לְגַבֵּי בְהֵמָה בִּלְבַד! מַאי בְּהֵמָה — בֶּהֱמַת עוֹלֵי רְגָלִים. אֲבָל אָדָם
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Adda said:** Upright **boards surrounding wells were only permitted to Festival pilgrims.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **boards surrounding wells were permitted only for cattle?** The Gemara answers: **What is** the **cattle** mentioned here? It means **the cattle of festival pilgrims. However, a person**

The Gemara at the very bottom of 21b opens with a statement by Rav Yitzchak bar Adda: *lo hutru passei bira'ot ella le'olei regalim bilvad* — the special leniency of *passei bira'ot* was permitted only for those ascending to Jerusalem for the *regel*, the pilgrimage festival, and not for ordinary individuals. The *passei bira'ot* is a remarkable rabbinic construction that allows the creation of a halachically enclosed space even when the majority of its area remains uncovered — a leniency whose unusual breadth is itself the subject of these restrictions. Notably, the occasion for drawing water under this arrangement was *Shabbat* specifically; *Yom Tov* presented no difficulty in this regard and required no special dispensation.

### Animals Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לְגַבֵּי בְהֵמָה בִּלְבַד, אֲבָל אָדָם מְטַפֵּס וְעוֹלֶה מְטַפֵּס וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** Upright **boards surrounding wells were permitted only for cattle, but a person must climb up** and **climb down** into the well and drink there.

A *baraita* then adds a further restriction: *lo hutru passei bira'ot ella legabei behema bilvad* — the arrangement was permitted only for the sake of animals, not for human drinking. The Gemara synthesizes these two statements: *behemet olei regalim*, the animals of those ascending for the *regel*. The cumulative rule is thus doubly limited: the *passei bira'ot* applies only to the animals of *olei regalim*, not to ordinary animals, and not to human beings even among the pilgrims themselves.

### Human Access

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לִבְאֵר מַיִם חַיִּים בִּלְבַד. וְאִי לִבְהֵמָה, מָה לִי חַיִּים מָה לִי מְכוּנָּסִין? בָּעִינַן מִידֵּי דַּחֲזֵי לְאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Is that so?** Is the allowance of upright boards for animals alone? **Didn't Rav Yitzḥak say** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** Upright **boards surrounding wells were permitted only** where the **wells** contain potable, running **spring water? If** the allowance is only **for animals, what is** the difference **to me** if it is **spring** water **and what is** the difference **to me** if it is **collected** water? Granted, collected water is inferior to spring water, but it is still suitable for animals to drink. The Gemara answers: **We require something that is fit for humans.**

If humans are excluded from drawing water through this arrangement, how then do they drink? The Gemara answers: *aval adam mitapes ve'oleh u'mitapes ve'yored* — a person can scale down the wall into the well and scale back up. Notably, the Gemara phrases this with going up mentioned before going down, which is something of an odd reversal. The answer is striking in its austerity: despite the entire elaborate construction of *passei bira'ot*, a human being must climb down the wall of the well to draw his own water.

This leads to a further question. The Gemara invokes an earlier ruling in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *lo hutru passei bira'ot ella leve'er mayim chayim bilvad* — the *passei bira'ot* was only permitted for a well of live, flowing water, not for collected rainwater. The Gemara presses: if the entire arrangement is for the sake of animals, why should the source of the water matter? An animal has no preference between *mayim chayim* and *mayim mechumasim*. The Gemara answers: *be'inan midi dechazi le'adam* — we require water that is fit for human consumption. Rashi explains that the water itself lends dignity and legal weight to the *mechitza*, and therefore *mayim chashuvim*, water of importance, is required.

### Tosafot Leniencies

At this point Tosafot intervenes to soften the severity of the position considerably. The earlier Gemara had permitted feeding the animal grain within the enclosure, despite grain not being water; Tosafot notes that this is also presumably something *dechazi le'adam*, fit for human use. More significantly, Tosafot poses the obvious question directly: if the *passei bira'ot* is permitted only *letzorech behema*, for the sake of animals, why does it matter whether the water is fit for humans?

Tosafot offers two leniencies in response. The first: *she'im dela letzorech behema ve'nishtayer sham, shari le'adam* — if one drew water for the animal and water remained, that leftover water is permitted for human consumption. The second, and more far-reaching leniency: once the *passei bira'ot* has been constructed and put in place *letzorech behema*, it is thereafter permitted *lechatchila* to draw water even explicitly for a human being. The restriction, according to Tosafot, applies only to the original intent when establishing the arrangement; once legitimately erected for animals, humans may freely draw from it as well. This makes the position far more intelligible and practical.

### Wide Troughs Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לִבְהֵמָה בִּלְבַד, אֲבָל אָדָם מְטַפֵּס וְעוֹלֶה מְטַפֵּס וְיוֹרֵד. וְאִם הָיוּ רְחָבִין, אֲפִילּוּ לְאָדָם נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines the *baraita* cited in the course of the previous discussion. Returning to **the matter itself,** the statement quoted above: Upright **boards surrounding wells were permitted only for cattle, but a person must climb up** and **climb down** into the well and drink there. **But if** the wells **were** too **wide** for him to climb, **they are** permitted **for a person as well.**

The Gemara then returns to the core ruling: *lo hutru passei bira'ot ella legabei behema bilvad, aval adam mitapes ve'oleh mitapes ve'yored* — a person scales the wall. The Gemara then introduces an exception: *ve'im hayu rechavim, afilu adam nami* — if the walls of the well are too wide to scale, then even a human being may draw water through the *passei bira'ot* arrangement.

Tosafot explains the precise meaning of this exception in light of his two earlier leniencies. In the general case, a person benefits from the arrangement only through leftover water or through drawing *lechatchila* once the setup was made for animals. But where scaling is physically impossible, even those secondary steps are unnecessary: one may *lechatchila* draw water through the *passei bira'ot* directly for a human being, without any prior drawing for the animal's benefit.

## Vessel Holding Rules

### Direct Holding Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יְמַלֵּא אָדָם מַיִם וְיִתֵּן לִפְנֵי בְהֶמְתּוֹ, אֲבָל מְמַלֵּא הוּא וְשׁוֹפֵךְ לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה וְשׁוֹתָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **A person may not fill** a bucket with **water and hold** it **before his animal on Shabbat, but he may fill** it **and pour** it **out** before the animal into a trough, **and** the animal **drinks of its own accord.**

The Gemara now revisits a ruling encountered earlier: *velo yemalei adam mayim ve'yiten lifnei behemato* — a person may not fill a vessel and hold it before his animal so the animal can drink from it directly. The permitted alternative is: *aval memale hu ve'shofech lifnei behema ve'shota me'eleha* — one may pour the water into the trough and the animal drinks from it on its own. The concern, as discussed previously, is that holding the vessel before the animal creates a risk that one might follow the animal out of the *passei bira'ot* enclosure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב עָנָן: אִם כֵּן, מָה הוֹעִילוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת? מָה הוֹעִילוּ?! לְמַלּאוֹת מֵהֶן! אֶלָּא: מָה הוֹעִיל רֹאשָׁהּ וְרוּבָּהּ שֶׁל פָּרָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Anan strongly objects to this** explanation: **If so, what purpose do the boards surrounding a well serve?** The Gemara immediately expresses its surprise: How can he ask **what purpose do they serve?** They allow people **to draw** water **from** the wells, which would otherwise be prohibited. **Rather,** Rav Anan's question should be understood as follows: **What purpose is served** in requiring that the enclosed area be large enough for **the cow's head and most of its** body, if in any case the cow may not be given to drink straight from the bucket?

The Gemara challenges: if one may not hold a vessel before the animal anyway, what purpose does the *passei bira'ot* serve? The response is that it allows one to fill the trough. But then a sharper question follows: if the vessel cannot be held in front of the animal regardless, why did the earlier ruling limit the arrangement to the case where *rosh ve'rubo*, the majority of the animal, is within the enclosure? Even if the animal were to step outside, since one is not holding the vessel there should be no concern.

### Abaye's Trough Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּאֵיבוּס הָעוֹמֵד בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וְרוֹחַב אַרְבָּעָה וְרֹאשׁוֹ נִכְנָס לְבֵין הַפַּסִּין וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In fact, it is permitted to give the animal to drink in any manner in the area enclosed by the boards surrounding the well. **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a special case, **with a manger** or trough **that stands in the public domain,** and is **ten** handbreadths **high and four** handbreadths **wide,** i.e., it constitutes a private domain, **and** one **end of it interposes into** the area **between the** upright **boards** surrounding a well.

It is this challenge that motivates Abaye's clarifying statement: *hacha be'mai askinan* — what is the case we are discussing here? *Be'evus ha'omed be'reshut harabim, gavo'ah asarah ve'rochav arba, ve'rosho nichnas bein hapasim* — a trough standing in the public domain, ten *tefachim* tall and four *tefachim* wide, whose inner end extends into the space between the boards. In this configuration, the trough itself constitutes a *reshut hayachid*, and the concern is that if one is holding the vessel over the trough and sees it is cracked or broken, one might take the vessel into and out of different domains. It is in this case specifically that holding the vessel is permitted, and therefore the requirement of *rosh ve'rubo* — that the majority of the animal be within the enclosure — remains necessary to prevent the person from inadvertently following the animal outside.

The upshot is that the general rule *does* permit holding the vessel before the animal. The prohibition discussed here is confined to this specific configuration of the trough as a *reshut hayachid*, and the *rosh ve'rubo* requirement is the mechanism by which the person is kept within bounds.

## Burganin & Geography

### Rav Yirmiyah Bar Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: אֵין בּוּרְגָּנִין בְּבָבֶל, וְלֹא פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** It is prohibited to walk more than two thousand cubits from a city on Shabbat. However, if there are small watchmen's huts [*burganin*] outside the city that are relatively close together, they are considered part of the city, and consequently the two thousand cubit limit is measured from the last such hut. **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said** that **Rav said:** The law with regard to these **huts [*burganin*] does not apply in Babylonia, nor does** the allowance with regard to upright **boards surrounding a well apply outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוּרְגָּנִין בְּבָבֶל לָא — דִּשְׁכִיחִי בִּידְקֵי, פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ לָא — דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי מְתִיבָתָא. אֲבָל אִיפְּכָא עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The law with regard to **huts does not** apply **in Babylonia because floods are common** there; and since the huts are liable to be swept away by the floodwaters, they are not regarded as dwellings. The allowance with regard to upright **boards surrounding a well does not** apply **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **because *yeshivot* are not common** there, and the allowance was only granted to those traveling for the sake of a mitzva such as Torah study. **But we do** say **the opposite,** i.e., we apply the law of huts outside of Eretz Yisrael and we apply the allowance of upright boards surrounding a well in Babylonia.

*Burganin* are small way-stations or huts erected outside a city for watchmen guarding the fields. Halachically, these structures extend the *techum* of the city, provided they are within seventy and two-thirds *amot* of the city boundary — a distance small enough that they are considered attached to the city.

The Gemara explains why *burganin* do not function in Bavel: *deshchichi bidkei* — because flooding is common there, and structures are frequently washed away. Since such huts cannot be relied upon to remain standing, they cannot serve as legitimate extensions of the city boundary. As for *passei bira'ot* in *chutz la'aretz*: *delo shchichi mesivsa* — because there are no *yeshivot* there.

This reasoning reveals something quietly profound. The original justification for *passei bira'ot* was *olei regalim*, the pilgrimage traffic converging on Jerusalem. After the *churban*, the Gemara assumes, the institution would have continued to apply wherever similar conditions of significant travel existed — namely, Bavel, where people streamed to the great academies of Torah learning. In its very assumption, the Gemara has articulated a theological and sociological proposition: the *yeshiva* replaces the *Beit Hamikdash* as the center of Jewish spiritual gravity. The people traveling to Neharde'a and Pumbedisa stand in for the *olei regalim* ascending to Jerusalem. *Chutz la'aretz*, lacking such centers of Torah, has no analogous circumstance and therefore cannot avail itself of the *passei bira'ot*.

### Alternative View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: אֵין בּוּרְגָּנִין וּפַסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת, לֹא בְּבָבֶל וְלֹא בַּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ. בּוּרְגָּנִין בְּבָבֶל לֹא — דִּשְׁכִיחִי בִּידְקֵי, בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ נָמֵי לָא — דִּשְׁכִיחִי גַּנָּבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternative version of the previous discussion. **Some say** that **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said** that **Rav said:** The laws with regard to **huts and** upright **boards surrounding a well** apply **neither in Babylonia** specifically, **nor outside of Eretz** Yisrael generally. The Gemara explains: The law with regard to **huts does not apply in Babylonia, because floods are common** there. It **also does not** apply **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **because thieves** who steal from such huts **are common** there; therefore, people do not regard the huts as dwellings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת בְּבָבֶל לָא — דִּשְׁכִיחִי מַיָּא. בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ נָמֵי לָא — דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי מְתִיבָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The allowance with regard to upright **boards surrounding a well** does **not** apply **in Babylonia, because water is common** there. Babylonia has many rivers and canals, and therefore wells are not essential there. **Outside of Eretz** Yisrael in general it **also does not** apply, **because *yeshivot* are not common** there.

The Gemara then presents an alternative formulation: *ika d'amri*, some say that Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba taught that neither institution applies in either location. In this version, *burganin* do not function in Bavel because of flooding (*deshchichi bidkei*), and they do not function in *chutz la'aretz* because of theft (*deshchichi ganavei*) — thieves will simply take the huts, rendering them unreliable as permanent structures. As for *passei bira'ot*: they do not apply in Bavel because water is abundantly available (*deshchichi maya*), and there is therefore no pressing need for the special institution; and they do not apply in *chutz la'aretz* because the necessary concentration of travelers does not exist there.

### Three Parsaot Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְמָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: אָמְרִי אָתֵיתוּ מִבַּרְנִשׁ לְבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא דְּדָנִיֵּאל דַּהֲוָה תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי בְּשַׁבְּתָא, אַמַּאי סָמְכִיתוּ — אַבּוּרְגָּנִין? הָא אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אֵין בּוּרְגָּנִין בְּבָבֶל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to Marei, son of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yirmeya bar Abba:** People **say** that **you walk from** the city of **Barnish to Daniel's synagogue, which is** a distance of **three parasangs [*parsei*], on Shabbat. Upon what do you rely?** Do you rely **on the huts** located at the city's outskirts that extend the Shabbat boundary toward the synagogue? **Didn't your father's father say in the name of Rav:** The law of **huts does not** apply **in Babylonia?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפֵק וְאַחְוִי לֵיהּ הָנְהוּ מָתָווֹתָא, דְּמִבַּלְעָן בְּשִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Marei then **went out and showed** Rav Ḥisda **certain** ruins of **towns that were subsumed within** a distance of **seventy cubits and a remainder,** two-thirds of a cubit, of each other.

The Gemara, having just cited Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba, introduces a connected story. Rav Chisda said to Mari, the son of Rav Huna who was the son of Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba: I have heard that on *Shabbat* you traveled from Barnash to the *beit knesset* of Daniel — a distance of three *parsaot*. Now a *parsa* is four *mil*, and the ordinary *techum Shabbat* permits travel of 2,000 *amot* beyond the city limits. Three *parsaot* is twelve times that ordinary distance. How did you manage this? Rav Chisda challenges: surely you relied on *burganin* — but your own grandfather taught in the name of Rav that *burganin* do not function in Bavel.

In response, the grandson went outside and pointed to a series of *masvata* — Rashi understands these as destroyed cities, each one situated within seventy and two-thirds *amot* of the next, so that each ruined settlement served as a connector to the following one. Once one reaches a city, the entire length of that city counts toward one's *techum*, and then another connector can bridge the gap to the next settlement. The arithmetic is staggering: three *parsaot* is 24,000 *amot*, and if each connector spans only seventy and some *shirayim* *amot*, the number of such connectors required is enormous. One must assume that the ruined settlements themselves provided substantial length, with the gaps between them bridged by these small connectors.

Tosafot raises an interesting philological point here. He initially considers whether *masvata* refers to small villages rather than destroyed cities, and notes that in the first chapter of Gittin the word *masvata* appears to refer to large cities. This bears practical significance for the writing of a *get*: since the standard formula designates the location with the word *masa ploni*, one must ask whether that term applies to a town or only to a city. Tosafot ultimately concedes that Rashi's reading — destroyed cities — is correct and that the evidence from this Gemara therefore does not resolve the *get* question. What is clear in any case is that even a destroyed city is halakhically sufficient to extend the *techum* and connect the chain of settlements.

## Torah She'be'al Peh

### Boundless Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, דָּרֵישׁ מָרִי בַּר מָר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לְכׇל תִּכְלָה רָאִיתִי קֵץ רְחָבָה מִצְוָתְךָ מְאֹד״, דָּבָר זֶה אֲמָרוֹ דָּוִד וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוֹ, אֲמָרוֹ אִיּוֹב וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוֹ, אֲמָרוֹ יְחֶזְקֵאל וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוֹ, עַד שֶׁבָּא זְכַרְיָה בֶּן עִדּוֹ וּפֵּירְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Mari bar Mar interpreted homiletically: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "I have seen a limit to every purpose; but Your commandment is exceedingly broad"** (Psalms 119:96)? **This** idea with regard to the breadth of the Torah **was stated by David, but he did not explain it;** it was **stated by Job, but he** too **did not explain it;** it was **stated by Ezekiel, but he** also **did not explain it, until Zechariah,** son of Berechiah, **son of Iddo, came and explained it.**

The Gemara now embarks on an extended *aggadic* digression that, as we will see, ultimately circles back to the theme of rabbinic legislation. Rav Chisda relates that Mari bar Mar expounded the verse: *l'chol tichla ra'iti ketz, rechava mitzvatecha me'od* — for all things I have seen an end, but Your commandments are exceedingly wide. David said this and did not explain it. Job said it and did not explain it. Yechezkel said it and did not explain it. And it was not until Zechariah came and explained it.

### Zechariah Scroll

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמָרוֹ דָּוִד וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְכׇל תִּכְלָה רָאִיתִי קֵץ רְחָבָה מִצְוָתְךָ מְאֹד״. אֲמָרוֹ אִיּוֹב וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲרוּכָּה מֵאֶרֶץ מִדָּהּ וּרְחָבָה מִנִּי יָם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda explains: This idea was **stated by David, but he did not explain it, as it is written: "I have seen a limit to every purpose; but Your commandment is exceedingly broad,"** i.e., he stated that the Torah is exceedingly broad, but he did not explain how broad. And likewise this idea was **stated by Job, but he** too **did not explain it, as it is written: "Its measure is longer than the earth and broader than the sea"** (Job 11:9).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמָרוֹ יְחֶזְקֵאל וְלֹא פֵּירְשׁוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפְרֹשׂ אוֹתָהּ לְפָנַי וְהִיא כְתוּבָה פָּנִים וְאָחוֹר וְכָתוּב אֵלֶיהָ קִינִים וָהֶגֶה וָהִי״.
>
> **Translation:** And similarly, it was **stated by Ezekiel, but he** also **did not explain it, as it is written: "And He spread it,"** the scroll, **"before me, and it was written inside and outside; and in it was written lamentations, and melody [*hegeh*], and woe [*vahi*]"** (Ezekiel 2:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קִינִים״ — זוֹ פּוּרְעָנוּתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״קִינָה הִיא וְקוֹנְנוּהָ״. ״וָהֶגֶה״ — זוֹ מַתַּן שְׂכָרָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עֲלֵי הִגָּיוֹן בְּכִנּוֹר״. ״וָהִי״ — זוֹ הִיא פּוּרְעָנוּתָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הֹוָה עַל הֹוָה תָּבֹא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **"Lamentations," this** refers to **the punishment of the righteous in this world, and so it is stated: "It is a lamentation and they shall make lament with it"** (Ezekiel 32:16). **"And melody [*hegeh*]," this** refers to **the reward of the righteous in the World-to-Come, and** the proof that this word is an expression of joy is the verse that **states:** "Upon an instrument of ten strings, and upon the harp, **to the melody [*higayon*] of a lyre"** (Psalms 92:4). **And "woe [*vahi*]," this is the punishment of the wicked in the World-to-Come, and so it states: "Calamity [*hova*] shall follow upon calamity"** (Ezekiel 7:26).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁבָּא זְכַרְיָה בֶּן עִדּוֹ וּפֵּירְשׁוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי מָה אַתָּה רוֹאֶה וָאוֹמַר אֲנִי רוֹאֶה מְגִילָּה עָפָה אׇרְכָּהּ עֶשְׂרִים בָּאַמָּה וְרׇחְבָּהּ עֶשֶׂר בָּאַמָּה״. וְכִי פָּשְׁטַתְּ לַהּ, הָוְיָא לַהּ עֶשְׂרִין בְּעֶשְׂרִין. וּכְתִיב: ״הִיא כְתוּבָה פָּנִים וְאָחוֹר״, וְכִי קָלְפַתְּ לַהּ, כַּמָּה הָוְיָא לַהּ — אַרְבְּעִין בְּעֶשְׂרִין.
>
> **Translation:** But nonetheless, Ezekiel did not explain the extent of the Torah, **until Zechariah,** son of Berechiah, **son of Iddo, came and explained it, as it is written: "And he said to me: What do you see? And I said: I see a flying [*afa*] scroll; the length of it is twenty cubits, and the breadth of it is ten cubits"** (Zechariah 5:2). Since the scroll was flying, the implication is that it had two equal sides, **so that when you open it, it is twenty by twenty** cubits. **And it is written: "And it was written inside and outside,"** i.e., on both sides. **And when you peel them apart** and separate the two sides, **how much is it?** Its entire area **amounts to forty by twenty** cubits, or eight hundred of God's cubits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״מִי מָדַד בְּשׇׁעֳלוֹ מַיִם וְשָׁמַיִם בַּזֶּרֶת תִּכֵּן וְגוֹ׳״ — נִמְצָא כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ אֶחָד מִשְּׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים וּמָאתַיִם בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In order to determine the measure of God's cubit, the Gemara cites a verse that describes the size of the span between God's thumb and little finger, in a manner of speaking. **And it is written: "Who has measured the waters in the hollow of His hand, and meted out heaven with the span,** and comprehended the dust of the earth in a measure" (Isaiah 40:12). If the entire world measures one square span, which is a quarter of one square cubit, **we find** according to this calculation that **the entire world is one** part **in three thousand and two hundred of the Torah.**

Each figure is cited in turn. David proclaimed *rechava mitzvatecha me'od* without specifying the dimension. Job declared *arucha me'eretz midah, u'rechava mini yam* — greater in length than the earth, and wider than the sea — but gave no precise measure. Yechezkel described a scroll spread out before him, written on both sides — *panim ve'achor* — containing *kinim*, *vahege*, and *vahi*: the sufferings of the righteous in this world, the reward of the righteous in the world to come, and the punishment of the wicked in the world to come. Yet Yechezkel too gave no precise dimensions for the scroll.

It is Zechariah who finally provides the measurement: *ani ro'eh megilla afa, aruka esrim b'amma, ve'rachva eser b'amma* — a flying scroll, twenty *amot* in length and ten in width. These, the Gemara specifies, are divine *amot*, whose precise length is beyond ordinary human reckoning. The Gemara then unpacks the calculation. The scroll is folded, so when unfolded it is twenty by twenty — 400 square divine *amot*. It is written on both sides; if one were to separate and lay those two sides flat side by side, the total would be forty by twenty — 800 square divine *amot*.

By comparison, the verse in Isaiah states *mi madad besh'alo mayim, ve'shamayim ba'zeret tiken* — God measured the heavens with a *zeret*, the span from thumb to little finger, which equals half a divine *amah*. The heavens in both dimensions are thus half a divine *amah* by half a divine *amah* — a quarter of a square divine *amah*. The ratio of 800 to one-quarter is 3,200. Thus, *nimtza kol olam kulo echad mishloshei alafim u'matayim ba'Torah* — the entire world is only one thirty-two-hundredth of the Torah. The scope of *Torah she-be'al peh* — which is what this midrash is understood to concern — dwarfs the entire physical universe.

The Maharsha notes a dimension of this image: the scroll written *panim ve'achor*, front and back, alludes to *pshat, remez, drash*, and *sod* — the layers of meaning that lie folded within the text, some visible on the surface and some requiring the text to be opened and its inner face revealed.

### Two Pots Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, דָּרֵישׁ מָרִי בַּר מָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִנֵּה שְׁנֵי דּוּדָאֵי תְאֵנִים מוּעָדִים לִפְנֵי הֵיכַל ה׳. הַדּוּד הָאֶחָד תְּאֵנִים טוֹבוֹת מְאֹד כִּתְאֵנֵי
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda** further **said: Mari bar Mar interpreted homiletically: What is** the meaning of that **which is written:** "The Lord showed me, **and behold two baskets of figs were set before the temple of the Lord,** after Nebuchadrezzar king of Babylon had carried away captive Jeconiah, son of Jehoiakim, the king of Judah, and the princes of Judah with the craftsmen and the smiths, from Jerusalem, and had brought them to Babylon. **One basket [*dud*] had very good figs, like the figs**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבַּכּוּרוֹת וְהַדּוּד הָאֶחָד תְּאֵנִים רָעוֹת מְאֹד אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵאָכַלְנָה מֵרוֹעַ״ —
>
> **Translation:** **that are first ripe, and the other basket [*dud*] had very bad figs, so bad they could not be eaten"** (Jeremiah 24:1–2).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּאֵנִים הַטּוֹבוֹת — אֵלּוּ צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים. תְּאֵנִים הָרָעוֹת — אֵלּוּ רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים. וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר אָבַד סִבְרָם וּבָטַל סִיכּוּיָם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַדּוּדָאִים נָתְנוּ רֵיחַ״ — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ עֲתִידִין שֶׁיִּתְּנוּ רֵיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Good figs, these are** the **full-fledged righteous** people; **bad figs, these are** the **full-fledged wicked** people. **And lest you say that the hope** of the wicked **is lost and their prospect is void, the verse states,** interpreting the word *duda'im* homiletically: **"The baskets [*duda'im*] yield a fragrance"** (Song of Songs 7:14), meaning that **both of them,** the righteous and the wicked, **will eventually yield a fragrance.**

The Gemara returns to Mari bar Mar's exposition, now on the verse: *ve'hineih shenei dudaim te'enim mu'adim lifnei heichal Hashem* — two baskets of figs set before the sanctuary of God. One basket contained figs that were *tovot me'od*, exceedingly good, like the finest first-ripened figs; the other contained figs that were *ra'ot me'od*, exceedingly bad, utterly inedible. The good figs, the Gemara explicates, represent *tzaddikim gemurim*, the fully righteous; the bad figs represent *resha'im gemurim*, the wholly wicked.

One might conclude that the *resha'im* are a lost cause, that their hope is entirely annulled. But the verse from *Shir Hashirim* — playing on the shared root of *dud* and *duda'im* — intervenes: *haduda'im natnu rei'ach*, the mandrakes gave forth their fragrance. *Elu va'elu atidim sheyitnu rei'ach* — both groups, even the thoroughly wicked, will in the future give forth their goodness. The *resha'im* possess *mitzvot* as well, and ultimately their hidden worth will be revealed in the time to come.

### Rava's Figs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״הַדּוּדָאִים נָתְנוּ רֵיחַ״ — אֵלּוּ בַּחוּרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא טָעֲמוּ טַעַם חֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava interpreted** the verse cited above **homiletically** as follows: **What is** the meaning of that **which is written:** "The mandrakes [*duda'im*] yield a fragrance, and at our doors are all manner of choice fruits, new and old, which I have laid up for you, O my beloved" (Song of Songs 7:14)? **"The mandrakes [*duda'im*] yield a fragrance," these are the young men of Israel who have never tasted the taste of sin.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְעַל פְּתָחֵינוּ כׇּל מְגָדִים״ — אֵלּוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁמַּגִּידוֹת פִּתְחֵיהֶן לְבַעֲלֵיהֶן. לָשׁוֹן אַחֵר: שֶׁאוֹגְדוֹת פִּתְחֵיהֶן לְבַעֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **"And at our doors [*petaḥeinu*] are all manner of choice fruits [*megadim*]," these are the daughters of Israel who inform [*maggidot*] their husbands about their passageway [*pit'ḥeihen*],** i.e., they tell them when they are menstruating. **Another version** of this interpretation is: **They bind [*ogedot*] their passageway** and save it **for their husbands,** and do not have relations with others.

Rava offers a different reading of *haduda'im natnu rei'ach*. Rather than both baskets, the *duda'im* here refers to something else entirely: *elu bachurei Yisrael shelo ta'amu ta'am chet* — these are the young men of Israel who have not yet experienced sin. Like fresh young shoots that have not yet matured, they have not yet acquired the taste of transgression. The Maharsha adds that mandrake plants carry little flavor, which makes the wordplay *lo ta'amu ta'am chet* all the more apt.

Rava continues with the next phrase: *ve'al petacheinu kol megadim* — by our doorways are all manner of delicacies. This refers to *benot Yisrael shemagidot pitcheihen leva'aleihen* — the women of Israel who faithfully report their status as *niddot* to their husbands. An alternate version reads *she'ogdot pitcheihen leva'aleihen* — they bind up their openings, scrupulously observing the laws of *niddah* and refraining from relations during the forbidden period.

### Mandrakes Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חֲדָשִׁים גַּם יְשָׁנִים דּוֹדִי צָפַנְתִּי לָךְ״ — אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הַרְבֵּה גְּזֵירוֹת גָּזַרְתִּי עַל עַצְמִי יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁגָּזַרְתָּ עָלַי, וְקִיַּימְתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **"New and old, which I have laid up for you, O my beloved," the Congregation of Israel said before the Holy One, Blessed be He,** and continued: **Master of the Universe, I have decreed many decrees upon myself** through the enactments and ordinances of the Sages, **more than what You decreed upon me** in the Torah, **and I have fulfilled them.** These are the new laws which were added to the old ones stated in the Torah.

*Chadashim gam yeshanim, dodi tzafanti lach* — new things and old things, my beloved I have stored up for you. The Gemara reads this as the voice of the community of Israel speaking before God: *Ribbono shel Olam, harbeh gezeirot gazarti al atzmi yoter mima shegazarta alai* — Master of the Universe, I have imposed upon myself many more prohibitions than You have imposed upon me. The Maharsha connects this specifically to the *chumra* of Rabbi Zeira and other stringencies in the laws of *niddah*, and it resonates broadly with the world of *chumrot* that Jewish communities have layered upon themselves — from *kitniyot* on Pesach to *Yom Tov sheni* — and I have fulfilled all of them. *Chadashim gam yeshanim*: the new ones, the rabbinic additions, alongside the old ones, the biblical commandments.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְהָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן דַּהֲוָה קָא מְסַדַּר אַגָּדָתָא קַמֵּיהּ: מִי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ ״חֲדָשִׁים גַּם יְשָׁנִים״ מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵלּוּ מִצְוֹת קַלּוֹת וְאֵלּוּ מִצְוֹת חֲמוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **Rav Ḥisda said to one of the Sages who would arrange the** traditions of the ***aggada* before him: Did you hear what** the meaning of: **New and old** is? **He said to him: These,** the new, **are the** more **lenient mitzvot, and these,** the old, **are the** more **stringent mitzvot.**

Rav Chisda then tests a student who was organizing *aggadot* before him, asking: have you heard a good interpretation of *chadashim gam yeshanim*? The student replied: these are the light *mitzvot* and the heavy *mitzvot*. Rav Chisda objected: what does that have to do with old and new? The Torah was not given in two installments, the lighter ones later than the heavier ones. Rather, the correct reading is: *hallalu midivrei Torah ve'hallalu midivrei soferim* — some are from the Torah itself, those are the *yeshanim*, the old; and some are from the sages, those are the *chadashim*, the new. The student's confusion apparently arose from equating the rabbinic, and therefore ostensibly lighter, laws with the *chadashim* — a reasonable but mistaken conflation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי תּוֹרָה פְּעָמִים פְּעָמִים נִיתְּנָה? אֶלָּא — הַלָּלוּ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, וְהַלָּלוּ מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him:** This cannot be so, **for was the Torah given on two** separate **occasions,** i.e., were the more lenient and more stringent mitzvot given separately? **Rather, these,** the old, **are** mitzvot **from the Torah, and these,** the new, **are from the Sages.**

This also illuminates why, in the *drasha* to follow, the *chadashim* — the rabbinic laws — are mentioned first. As Rava will immediately demonstrate, the rabbinic laws demand a heightened vigilance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיוֹתֵר מֵהֵמָּה בְּנִי הִזָּהֵר עֲשׂוֹת סְפָרִים הַרְבֵּה וְגוֹ׳״ — בְּנִי, הִזָּהֵר בְּדִבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים יוֹתֵר מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. שֶׁדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה יֵשׁ בָּהֶן עֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. וְדִבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים — כׇּל הָעוֹבֵר עַל דִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים חַיָּיב מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava expounded** another verse in similar fashion: **What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And more than these, my son, be careful: of making many books [*sefarim*]** there is no end; and much study is a weariness of the flesh" (Ecclesiastes 12:12)? **My son, be careful** to fulfill **the words of the Sages [*soferim*]** even **more than the words of the Torah. For the words of the Torah include positive and negative** commandments, and even with regard to the negative commandments, the violation of many of them is punishable only by lashes. **Whereas** with respect to **the words of the Sages, anyone who transgresses the words of the Sages is liable to** receive the **death** penalty, as it is stated: "And whoever breaches through a hedge, a snake shall bite him" (Ecclesiastes 10:8), taking hedges to refer metaphorically to decrees.

Rava expounds the verse from Kohelet: *veyoter mehema beni hizaher asot sefarim harbe* — and more than all this, my son, be careful of the making of many books. *Beni hizaher bedivrei sofrim yoter midivrei Torah* — be more careful about rabbinic law than about biblical law. The explanation offered is that biblical law carries positive and negative commandments, whereas *kol ha'over al divrei sofrim chayav misa* — one who transgresses rabbinic enactments is, as it were, deserving of death. Rashi quotes the verse *u'foretz geder yishchenu nachash* — one who breaks through the fence will be bitten by a snake — framing the rabbinic law precisely as *geder*, a protective fence around the biblical law. To breach it is to expose oneself to the gravest consequence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר: אִם יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מַמָּשׁ, מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נִכְתְּבוּ?! אָמַר קְרָא: ״עֲשׂוֹת סְפָרִים הַרְבֵּה אֵין קֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Lest you say: If** the words of the Sages **are of substance** and have such great importance, **why were they not written** in the Torah, therefore, **the verse states: "Of making many books there is no end,"** meaning that it is impossible to fully commit the Oral Torah to writing, as it is boundless.

One must, however, understand this in context. The Maharsha notes that this cannot be taken in its simple, absolute sense — halachically, rabbinic law is generally treated more leniently than biblical law, and the ordinary rules of *safeik*, doubt, and other principles reflect this. The Maharsha therefore suggests the statement refers specifically to rabbinic laws that protect against biblical violations carrying the death penalty. Even if one accepts a broader reading, it remains rhetorical exaggeration aimed at instilling vigilance, not a strict halachic equation of rabbinic and biblical severity. The statement *chadashim gam yeshanim* now reads with added force: the *chadashim*, the rabbinic enactments, are listed first because it is they that demand this particular vigilance.

Lest one wonder why the *divrei sofrim* were not simply incorporated into the written Torah from the beginning, the verse itself addresses this: *asot sefarim harbe ein ketz* — the making of many books would have been endless. The *Torah she-be'al peh* is genuinely boundless — as the entire calculation from Zechariah's scroll demonstrated, it exceeds the physical universe thirty-two hundredfold — and no written document could have contained it. The written Torah must be finite and manageable; the oral tradition, by its nature, can expand without limit while remaining responsive to the particular needs of each generation and each era.

### Rabbinic Severity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלַהַג הַרְבֵּה יְגִיעַת בָּשָׂר״ — אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בַּר אַדָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בַּר עוּלָּא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל הַמַּלְעִיג עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, נִידּוֹן בְּצוֹאָה רוֹתַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** What is the meaning of the words: **"And much study [*lahag*] is a weariness of the flesh"? Rav Pappa, son of Rav Aḥa bar Adda, said in the name of Rav Aḥa bar Ulla: This teaches that whoever mocks [*malig*] the words of the Sages will be sentenced to boiling excrement,** which results from the weariness of the flesh of man.

The Gemara concludes this sequence with a comment on the final phrase of the verse: *velahag harbe yegi'at basar* — and much engagement brings weariness of flesh. Rav Papa the son of Rav Acha bar Adda in the name of Rav Acha bar Ulla teaches: *shekol hamal'ig al divrei chachamim nidon betzoa rotachat* — anyone who mocks the words of the sages will be judged in boiling excrement. The word *lahag* is read as *mal'ig*, to mock, and *yegi'at basar* as the excretion of the flesh. But the same phrase carries an alternate reading: if one instead puts in genuine effort — *yegi'ah* — and engages seriously, then *ta'am basar*, it is like the taste of meat, *geshmak*, deeply satisfying. What appears from the outside to be foolishness reveals itself, under proper engagement, to be richly substantive.

The arc of the entire *aggadic* passage is now clear. The *Torah she-be'al peh* is incomprehensibly vast, extending far beyond anything the physical world can contain. It was given this way deliberately, because no written text could hold it. It demands more vigilance, not less, than the written Torah. It encompasses not only the rabbinic *gezeirot* and *takanot* but even the *chumrot* that the Jewish people have taken upon themselves. And to mock or dismiss it brings the gravest consequence, while to engage with it seriously is among the deepest pleasures available to a human being.

## Rabbi Akiva Story

### Prison Hand-Washing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְהָיָה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הַגַּרְסִי מְשָׁרְתוֹ. בְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם הָיוּ מַכְנִיסִין לוֹ מַיִם בְּמִדָּה. יוֹם אֶחָד מְצָאוֹ שׁוֹמֵר בֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, אָמַר לוֹ: הַיּוֹם מֵימֶךָ מְרוּבִּין, שֶׁמָּא לַחְתּוֹר בֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין אַתָּה צָרִיךְ? שָׁפַךְ חֶצְיָין וְנָתַן לוֹ חֶצְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning the significance of observing the words of the Sages, the Gemara relates: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **It once happened that Rabbi Akiva was incarcerated in a prison, and Rabbi Yehoshua HaGarsi would** come to the prison to **attend to his** needs. **Every day** his disciples **would bring him water in a measured** quantity. **One day the prison guard met** Rabbi Yehoshua HaGarsi and **said to him:** The amount of **your water today is more** than usual; **perhaps you need** it **in order to** soften the walls and thus **undermine the prison. He** then **poured out half** the water, **and gave him** the other **half** to take in to Rabbi Akiva.

The Gemara now presents a narrative that grounds the foregoing abstract discussion in vivid, almost unbearable, human terms. *Ma'aseh be'Rabbi Akiva shehaya chavush be'veit ha'asurin* — it happened that Rabbi Akiva was imprisoned by the Roman government. His attendant was Rabbi Yehoshua Ha-Garsi — Rashi suggests this may be either a place name or a reference to one who ground bran, *grisin*. Each day, Rabbi Yehoshua would bring Rabbi Akiva a measured, fixed quantity of water, the maximum the authorities would permit.

One day the jailer intercepted Rabbi Yehoshua and declared that he was carrying too much water — perhaps, the jailer suggested, intending to use it to soften the ground and dig an escape tunnel. He poured off half and gave Rabbi Yehoshua only half. When Rabbi Yehoshua arrived, Rabbi Akiva said to him: Yehoshua, do you not know that I am old and my life depends on you and what you bring me each day? Rabbi Yehoshua recounted the entire incident. Rabbi Akiva replied: give me the water so that I may wash my hands before eating bread. Rabbi Yehoshua was stunned: there is not enough water here even for you to drink, and you wish to use what little remains for *netilat yadayim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁבָּא אֵצֶל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָמַר לוֹ: יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אֵין אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁזָּקֵן אֲנִי וְחַיַּי תְּלוּיִין בְּחַיֶּיךָ?
>
> **Translation:** **When** Rabbi Yehoshua **came to Rabbi Akiva,** and the latter saw the small amount of water he had brought, **he said to him: Yehoshua, do you not know that I am old, and my life depends on your life?** No one else brings me water, so if you bring me less than I need, my life is endangered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָח לוֹ כׇּל אוֹתוֹ הַמְאוֹרָע. אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי מַיִם שֶׁאֶטּוֹל יָדַי, אָמַר לוֹ: לִשְׁתּוֹת אֵין מַגִּיעִין, לִיטּוֹל יָדֶיךָ מַגִּיעִין?! אָמַר לוֹ: מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁחַיָּיבִים עֲלֵיהֶן מִיתָה? מוּטָב אָמוּת מִיתַת עַצְמִי, וְלֹא אֶעֱבוֹר עַל דַּעַת חֲבֵירַי!
>
> **Translation:** After Rabbi Yehoshua **related to him the entire incident,** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: Give me water so that I may wash my hands.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him:** The water that I brought **will not suffice for drinking;** how **will it suffice for washing your hands? He said to him: What can I do; for** transgressing the words of the Sages and eating without first washing hands **one is liable to** receive the **death** penalty. And if so, it is **better that I should die my own death** by thirst, **rather than transgress the opinion of my colleagues** who enacted that one must wash hands before eating.

Rabbi Akiva's response is striking in its simplicity: *mah e'eseh, shechayavin aleiha mita* — what can I do? One who transgresses this rabbinic enactment is deserving of death. *Mutav amut mitat atzmi ve'lo e'evor al da'at chaverai* — better that I die my own natural death than that I transgress the view of my colleagues and become deserving of a heavenly death. He refused to eat until they brought him sufficient water for *netilat yadayim*.

The word *chaverai*, colleagues, is itself charged with meaning. On one hand, it might seem to reduce the weight of the obligation — these are his own contemporaries who made this rule, not towering earlier generations. And yet it heightens another dimension: the obligation not to betray the integrity of the enterprise one is part of, the commitment to one's colleagues and to the shared *halachic* project they have built together. The two readings amplify rather than cancel each other.

### Sages' Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ: לֹא טָעַם כְּלוּם עַד שֶׁהֵבִיא לוֹ מַיִם וְנָטַל יָדָיו, כְּשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: מָה בְּזִקְנוּתוֹ כָּךְ, בְּיַלְדוּתוֹ עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. וּמָה בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין כָּךְ, שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **They said** that **he would not taste anything until** Rabbi Yehoshua **brought him water and he washed his hands. When the Sages heard about this, they said: If in his old age** and weakened state he is still **so** meticulous in his observance of the mitzvot, **how much more so** must he have been **in his youth.** And **if in prison** he is **so** scrupulous in his behavior, **how much more so** must he have been **when not in prison.**

When the sages heard of this they said: *mah bizknuto kach* — if he had this inner strength in his old age and in prison, *beyalduto al achat kama vechama* — how much more so in his youth; and *uma beveit ha'asurim kach, shelo beveit ha'asurim al achat kama vechama* — if he was capable of this in prison, how much more in freedom.

Tosafot notes an important qualification: the Gemara elsewhere, in the first chapter of *Eruvin*, states that four things are exempt in the camp, and *netilat yadayim* is among them — and all the more so when there is genuine danger. Rabbi Akiva was thus not halachically required to go to such extremes; *machmir al atzmo haya*, he was being stringent upon himself. This actually reinforces the larger point: the story is not primarily about the technical obligation but about Rabbi Akiva's extraordinary commitment to *divrei chaverav*, the enactments of the sages, even beyond what the law demanded of him.

## Shlomo Eruvin

### Bat Kol Rejoicing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁתִּיקֵּן שְׁלֹמֹה עֵירוּבִין וּנְטִילַת יָדַיִם, יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״בְּנִי אִם חָכַם לִבֶּךָ יִשְׂמַח לִבִּי גַּם אָנִי״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״חֲכַם בְּנִי וְשַׂמַּח לִבִּי וְאָשִׁיבָה חֹרְפִי דָבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** It is prohibited to walk more than two thousand cubits from a city on Shabbat. However, if there are small watchmen's huts [*burganin*] outside the city that are relatively close together, they are considered part of the city, and consequently the two thousand cubit limit is measured from the last such hut. Rav Yehuda said that Shmuel said: At the time that** King **Solomon instituted** the ordinances of ***eiruv*** of courtyards **and** of **washing hands** to purify them from their impurity, which are added safeguards to the words of the Torah, **a Divine Voice emerged and said** in his praise: **"My son, if your heart is wise, My heart will be glad, even Mine"** (Proverbs 23:15). **And it states** with regard to him: **"My son, be wise and make My heart glad, that I may respond to he who taunts Me"** (Proverbs 27:11).

The entire foregoing *aggadic* passage — the boundlessness of *Torah she-be'al peh*, the severity of transgressing rabbinic law, the story of Rabbi Akiva's self-sacrifice for *netilat yadayim* — now converges on the theme of the tractate itself. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *beshe'a shetikan Shlomo eiruvin u'netilat yadayim, yatzta bat kol ve'amra* — at the hour when Shlomo enacted *eiruvin* and *netilat yadayim*, a heavenly voice went forth and proclaimed: *beni im chacham libcha yismach libi gam ani* — my son, if your heart is wise, my heart too will rejoice. And further: *chacham beni ve'samach libi va'ashiva chorfi davar* — be wise, my son, and gladden my heart, that I may respond to those who taunt me with a word.

The attribution of *eiruvin* and *netilat yadayim* to Shlomo, accompanied by divine rejoicing expressed through a *bat kol*, grants these institutions a weight that transcends their technical rabbinic classification. Heaven itself celebrated their enactment.

### Field Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לְכָה דוֹדִי נֵצֵא הַשָּׂדֶה נָלִינָה בַּכְּפָרִים נַשְׁכִּימָה לַכְּרָמִים נִרְאֶה אִם פָּרְחָה הַגֶּפֶן פִּתַּח הַסְּמָדַר הֵנֵצוּ הָרִמּוֹנִים שָׁם אֶתֵּן אֶת דּוֹדַי לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional teachings that **Rava interpreted homiletically: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Come, my beloved, let us go forth into the field; let us lodge in the villages. Let us get up early to the vineyards; let us see if the vine has flowered, if the grape blossoms have opened, if the pomegranates are in flower; there will I give you my loves"** (Song of Songs 7:12–13)?

Rava expounds the verse: *lecha dodi neitzei hasadeh, nalina bakfarim, nashkima lachramim, nireh im porchah hagefen, pitach hasmadar, henetzu harimonim, sham etein et dodai lach* — Come, my beloved, let us go out to the field, lie in the villages, rise early to the vineyards, see if the vine has flowered, if the unripe grape has opened, if the pomegranates have blossomed; there I will give my love to you.

The community of Israel speaks to God: do not judge me as You judge those who dwell in cities — where stealing, sexual sin, and false oaths abound, driven by commerce and desire. *Neitzei hasadeh* — come to the field, and I will show You Torah scholars who labor in Torah from a place of deprivation, free from all distraction. *Nalina bakfarim* — do not read *bakfarim*, villages, but *bakofrim*, the rebels — come and see those upon whom You have bestowed goodness, and yet they have denied and rejected You. This is a stark contrast between those who study Torah in poverty and those who, having received divine blessing, turn away from God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְכָה דוֹדִי נֵצֵא הַשָּׂדֶה״ — אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אַל תְּדִינֵנִי כְּיוֹשְׁבֵי כְרַכִּים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן גָּזֵל וַעֲרָיוֹת וּשְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא וּשְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר, ״נֵצֵא הַשָּׂדֶה״ — בֹּא וְאַרְאֲךָ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה מִתּוֹךְ הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the words: **"Come, my beloved, let us go forth into the field," the Congregation of Israel said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, do not judge me like those who reside in large cities where there is robbery and licentiousness, and vain oaths and false oaths,** but rather: **"Let us go forth into the field," come and I will show You Torah scholars who** work the land but nonetheless **engage in Torah** study, **in** poverty and in **distress.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נָלִינָה בַּכְּפָרִים״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי בַּכְּפָרִים אֶלָּא בַּכּוֹפְרִים: בֹּא וְאַרְאֲךָ אוֹתָם שֶׁהִשְׁפַּעְתָּ לָהֶן טוֹבָה וְהֵן כָּפְרוּ בְּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the words, **"Let us lodge in the villages," do not read** the phrase as: **In the villages [*bakefarim*], but rather** as: **By the deniers [*bakoferim*],** meaning, **come and I will show You** the nations of the world, **whom You showered with good, but** yet **they have denied You.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נַשְׁכִּימָה לַכְּרָמִים״ — אֵלּוּ בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת. ״נִרְאֶה אִם פָּרְחָה הַגֶּפֶן״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מִקְרָא. ״פִּתַּח הַסְּמָדַר״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מִשְׁנָה. ״הֵנֵצוּ הָרִמּוֹנִים״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי גְמָרָא. ״שָׁם אֶתֵּן אֶת דּוֹדַי לָךְ״ — אַרְאֲךָ כְּבוֹדִי וְגוֹדְלִי שֶׁבַח בָּנַי וּבְנוֹתַי.
>
> **Translation:** **"Let us get up early to the vineyards," these are the synagogues and houses of study. "Let us see if the vine has flowered," these are the masters of Bible,** who are proficient in the first stage of Torah study. **"If the grape blossoms have opened," these are the masters of Mishna. "If the pomegranates are in flower," these are the masters of Gemara. "There will I give you my loves,"** means **I will show You my glory and my greatness, the praise of my sons and daughters,** how they adhere to sanctity.

This is a striking *drasha* given its source: it comes from Rava, who lived and taught in Mechuza, a prosperous, cosmopolitan city. The historian Yaakov Elman has written extensively on Rava's fraught relationship with the urban culture of Mechuza, its wealthy merchants, and their resistance to rabbinic authority. The strong condemnation of city life that appears here — the cities of the wealthy who deny God despite His largesse — fits precisely into Rava's social context and his ongoing struggle to instill Torah values in an affluent, materially comfortable community.

The *drasha* continues: *nashkima lachramim*, let us rise early to the vineyards — these are the *batei knesiot* and *batei midrashot*. The rows of a vineyard, as elsewhere in rabbinic literature, evoke the rows of the study house, the *kerem b'Yavneh*. *Nireh im porchah hagefen* — let us see if the vine has flowered — these are the *ba'alei mikra*, those who know the written Torah. *Pitach hasmadar*, the unripe grape has opened — these are the *ba'alei Mishna*, whose learning is more developed. *Henetzu harimonim* — the pomegranates, far larger and more developed than the grape, have blossomed — these are the *ba'alei Gemara*, who represent the apex of Torah learning. *Sham etein et dodai lach* — there I will give my love to you; *arecha kevodi u'godli, shevach banai u'vnotai* — I will show You my honor and my greatness, the praise of my sons and daughters and all the *Torah she-be'al peh* they have developed.

### Handles Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְדַבֵּר שְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים מָשָׁל וַיְהִי שִׁירוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה וָאָלֶף״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָמַר שְׁלֹמֹה עַל כׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר שֶׁל תּוֹרָה שְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים מָשָׁל, עַל כׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר שֶׁל סוֹפְרִים חֲמִשָּׁה וְאֶלֶף טְעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expounds further concerning King Solomon. **Rav Hamnuna said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And he spoke three thousand proverbs, and his poems were a thousand and five"** (I Kings 5:12)? This **teaches that Solomon pronounced three thousand proverbs for each and every word of the Torah,** and **one thousand and five reasons for each and every word of the Scribes.**

The Gemara then cites: *vayedaber shloshet alfei mashal, vayehi shiro chamisha va'alef* — Shlomo spoke three thousand parables, and his song was a thousand and five. This is interpreted to mean that on every matter of Torah, Shlomo gave three thousand explanatory parables, and on every matter of rabbinic teaching, he offered a thousand and five reasons. The point is the same as before: even the *divrei sofrim*, the words of the sages, are not arbitrary or thin — they carry thousands of deep explanations that are not immediately visible to the surface reader. Rava then expounds the verse from Kohelet: *veyoter shehaya Kohelet chacham, od limad da'at et ha'am, ve'izen ve'chiker, tiken meshalim harbe* — more than being wise himself, Kohelet taught knowledge to the people; he listened, investigated, and arranged many parables. Rashi explains: *d'agmarah b'simanei teamim, kava lah mesoret ve'simanim bein b'teivot hamikra bein b'girsa shel Mishna* — he taught through signs of cantillation, establishing a *mesora* and markers both for the words of Scripture and for the formulation of the Mishnah. In other words, Shlomo established not merely specific enactments but the foundational apparatus of the *masora* — the system of transmission — for *Torah she-be'al peh* itself, just as the written Torah has its own *masorah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיוֹתֵר שֶׁהָיָה קֹהֶלֶת חָכָם עוֹד לִימַּד דַּעַת אֶת הָעָם [וְ]אִיזֵּן וְחִקֵּר תִּיקֵּן מְשָׁלִים הַרְבֵּה״. ״לִימַּד דַּעַת אֶת הָעָם״ — דְּאַגְמְרַיהּ בְּסִימָנֵי טְעָמִים, וְאַסְבְּרַהּ בְּמַאי דְּדָמֵי לַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** also **taught: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And besides being wise, Koheleth also taught the people knowledge; and he weighed, and sought out, and set in order many proverbs"** (Ecclesiastes 12:9). Rava interpreted homiletically: **He taught the people knowledge,** meaning **he taught it with the accentuation marks** in the Torah, **and** he **explained** each matter **by means of** something **similar to it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״[וְ]אִיזֵּן וְחִקֵּר תִּיקֵּן מְשָׁלִים הַרְבֵּה״ — אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: בַּתְּחִילָּה הָיְתָה תּוֹרָה דּוֹמָה לִכְפִיפָה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ אׇזְנַיִם, עַד שֶׁבָּא שְׁלֹמֹה וְעָשָׂה לָהּ אׇזְנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to: **"And he weighed [*izzen*], and sought out, and set in order many proverbs," Ulla said** that **Rabbi Eliezer said: At first the Torah was like a basket without handles [*oznayim*], until Solomon came and made handles for it.** By means of his explanations and proverbs he enabled each person to understand and take hold of the Torah, fulfill its mitzvot, and distance himself from transgressions.

### Handles Creation

This image is deliberately distinct from the familiar image of a *geder*, a fence. A fence protects against transgression by keeping people away from the prohibited. Handles do something different: they allow you to grasp, to carry, to hold on. Rashi connects this explicitly back to the earlier statement — the *oznayim* refer to the enactments of *eiruvin* and *netilat yadayim*, and also to Shlomo's *gezeira* on *shniyot l'arayot*, the secondary forbidden relationships elaborated in Yevamot. These are not merely prohibitions; they are positive frameworks that enable people to *hold onto* the *mitzvot* and live within them. *Ochzim Yisrael ba'mitzvot sheyitrachaku min ha'aveira* — through them, Israel grasps the commandments and thus distances itself from transgression.

The distinction is worth dwelling on. *Eiruvin* and *netilat yadayim* are not *gezeirot* in the standard prohibitory sense. *Eiruvin* is a constructive act — one builds, one establishes, one makes a *bracha*. *Netilat yadayim* similarly involves a positive ritual with its own *bracha*. These are *mitzvot* in the fuller sense, rabbinic *mitzvot* that give people something to do, something to perform, a handle by which to grasp the larger fabric of *halacha*. The Beit HaG, it is worth noting, includes some of these — *eiruvin*, *mikra megilla*, *chanukah* — within his enumeration of the 613 commandments, reflecting in his own way this sense that these rabbinic enactments have achieved a status that approaches the constitutional.

### Hair Locks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קְווּצּוֹתָיו תַּלְתַּלִּים״, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִדְרוֹשׁ עַל כׇּל קוֹץ וָקוֹץ תִּילֵּי תִּילִּים שֶׁל הֲלָכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse, "His head is as the most fine gold, **his locks [*kevutzotav*] are wavy [*taltalim*],** and black as a raven" (Song of Songs 5:11), **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Mar Ukva said:** This **teaches that it is possible to expound from each and every stroke [*kotz*]** of the letters in the Torah **mounds upon mounds [*tilei tilim*] of laws.**

Rav Chisda in the name of Mar Ukva states: *melameid sheyeish lidrosh al kol kotz ve'kotz tilei tilim shel halachot* — on every little thorn, every tiny stroke of a letter in the Torah, one can derive piles upon piles of halachot. This is the capstone of the entire passage on the breadth of *Torah she-be'al peh*: not only is it thirty-two hundred times the size of the physical universe in its totality, but every single letter of the written Torah, every minimal graphical element, contains within it an inexhaustible depth of oral tradition.

## Torah Cost

### Raven Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁחוֹרוֹת כָּעוֹרֵב״: בְּמִי אַתָּה מוֹצְאָן — בְּמִי שֶׁמַּשְׁכִּים וּמַעֲרִיב עֲלֵיהֶן לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. רַבָּה אָמַר: בְּמִי שֶׁמַּשְׁחִיר פָּנָיו עֲלֵיהֶן כְּעוֹרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Black [*sheḥorot*] as a raven [*orev*]** means: **In whom do you find** the words of Torah? **In him who, for** the Torah's **sake, gets up early** in the morning [*shaḥar*] **and stays late** in the evening [*erev*] **in the study hall. Rabba said: In him who, for** the Torah's **sake, blackens his face like a raven,** i.e., who fasts and deprives himself for the sake of Torah study.

The Gemara continues with a verse from *Shir Hashirim*: *shachorot ka'orev* — black as a raven. In whom does one find all this Torah? *Bemi shemashkim u'ma'ariv aleihem le'veit hamidrash* — in one who rises early and stays late in the *beit hamidrash*, *shachorot* evoking *shachar*, dawn, and *erev*, dusk. Torah is not acquired casually; it demands the full arc of the day.

### Cruelty to Family

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בְּמִי שֶׁמֵּשִׂים עַצְמוֹ אַכְזָרִי עַל בָּנָיו וְעַל בְּנֵי בֵּיתוֹ כְּעוֹרֵב. כִּי הָא דְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל לְבֵי רַב, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ: יָנוֹקֵי דִידָךְ מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד לְהוּ? אֲמַר לַהּ: מִי שְׁלִימוּ קוּרָמֵי בְּאַגְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: In him who makes himself cruel to his sons and** other **members of his household like a raven** for the sake of Torah. This **was the case with Rav Adda bar Mattana,** who **was** about to **go to the study hall** to learn Torah, and **his wife said to him: What shall I do for your children?** How shall I feed them in your absence? **He said to her: Are all the rushes [*kurmei*] in the marsh** already **gone?** If there is no other bread, let them eat food prepared from rushes.

Rava reads the image with even greater harshness: *bemi shemashchir panav aleihem ka'orev* — in one who darkens his face over them like a raven, who denies himself sunlight and all ordinary comfort. And further: *bemi shemeisim atzmo achzori al banav ve'al benei beito ka'orev* — in one who makes himself cruel to his children and members of his household like a raven, completely abandoning their needs in total devotion to Torah study.

This is a striking statement and it is not without tension within the broader rabbinic tradition. The Gemara in Ketubot takes a far more critical view of abandoning one's family for Torah study. Yet here the abandonment is presented in positive terms, almost as a virtue. The *Vilna Gaon* in his commentary on *Mishlei* cites this very passage at length to support the ideal of complete self-abnegation for Torah, including the abandonment of worldly and domestic obligations.

### Ada Bar Matna

The Gemara illustrates this with the story of Rav Ada bar Matna, who went to the house of Rav to study. His wife asked him: what will become of your children, who will provide for them? He replied: *mi shelimu kurmei be'agma* — are there no longer reeds in the swamp? Meaning: let them go find reeds and subsist on whatever the earth provides. It is an extraordinarily unfeeling response, and the Gemara presents it without apparent criticism.

The tension this creates is real and worth acknowledging. The weight of rabbinic literature, including other passages in the Talmud, generally disapproves of this degree of abandonment. That this passage praises it — implicitly, through inclusion and framing — reflects a genuine diversity within the Talmudic tradition on the question of what the pursuit of Torah demands. And as noted above, it is Rava who bears the weight of this position, a man who struggled throughout his career with a community that he felt was insufficiently devoted to Torah and too seduced by the comforts and corruptions of urban wealth. In that context, perhaps, the rhetoric of total sacrifice becomes comprehensible, even if it cannot be universalized.
