# Bava Batra 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gag Overlooking Chatzer

### Four Amot Wall

Today's daf picks up in the middle of 6b, where the *gemara* has been discussing who pays for a shared wall. The *mishnah* established that the first four *amot* of wall construction is shared; beyond that, one cannot compel a neighbor to contribute unless that neighbor demonstrates his own desire for the wall by building a parallel structure. But before moving forward, the *gemara* returns to examine some of the foundational questions surrounding *hezek reiyah* — the damage caused by overlooking — and when one can be compelled to build even a basic privacy wall.

Rav Nachman quotes Shmuel: *gag hasamuch lachatzar chaveiro, ose lo maakeh gavoha arba amot* — if one has a roof that overlooks a neighbor's yard, one must erect a wall around the roof four *amot* high. The logic is clear: a wall on the neighbor's side does no good, since the overlooking party can still see in. It is therefore the owner of the roof who must build a *maakeh* for privacy purposes, setting aside entirely the separate *mitzvah* of *maakeh* for safety.

### Adjacent Rooftops

The *gemara* continues: *aval bein gag legag lo* — but between two adjacent rooftops, no such obligation exists. One can imagine the Old City of Jerusalem, where one can walk across the tops of buildings as if traversing a continuous concrete expanse, with the boundary between individual rooftops invisible from below. Even in such a case, no four-*amot* wall is required.

Why the difference? Presumably, Shmuel's position is that rooftops are used only occasionally, and therefore the *hezek reiyah* concern is diminished. Unlike a yard, where one expects the neighbor to emerge from his house at any moment, a rooftop sees far rarer and more irregular use. Whatever private activities one wishes to conduct there, both parties can readily observe whether the other is present. This does not rise to the level of a serious *hezek*.

Rav Nachman himself, however, goes further: *amar eino zakuk l'arba amot aval zakuk limchitzah asarah* — one is not required to build a four-*amot* wall, but one is required to erect a partition of ten *tefachim*, approximately two and a half feet. The *gemara* immediately asks: *lemai* — for what purpose? If the concern is *hezek reiyah*, ten *tefachim* is plainly insufficient; four *amot* would be needed. And if Shmuel maintains there is no *hezek reiyah* concern at all between adjacent rooftops, then even ten *tefachim* seems superfluous.

### Trespass Concern

The *gemara* works through the alternatives. *Iy l'hezek reiyah, arba amot beinan* — if for privacy, four *amot* are required. *Iy lenitzolei alav keganav* — if the concern is preventing trespass, so that no one inadvertently wanders onto a neighbor's rooftop, a simple property marker would suffice. The *gemara* indeed suggests *bemitzra b'alma sagi* — small pegs or boundary markers are enough.

What about *iy legidayim u'rtushin* — to prevent goats and young animals from crossing over? This may seem puzzling in a contemporary context, but if one recalls that rooftops in ancient Mesopotamia and the Land of Israel were regularly used for sleeping, drying fruit, and various agricultural purposes, the presence of animals is not inconceivable. Even so, for that purpose *bechdi shelo yizdaker bevat rosh sagi* — it is sufficient to have a barrier high enough that an animal cannot put its head through, which is only a few *tefachim*, not ten.

The *gemara* concludes: *leolam lo nitfas alav k'ganav* — the function of the ten-*tefachim* partition is indeed trespass prevention. The reason a mere marker is insufficient is that one can evade it with an excuse: *dematzei matzei mishtamit lei* — one could claim to have been pressed against the edge without noticing the small pegs, or as the text puts it, *amrei mitzurei ka mitzureina*, one was simply jostled across. But *mechitzas asarah lo matzei mishtamit lei* — with a genuine partition of ten *tefachim*, no such excuse is available. The partition makes the property boundary undeniable.

This introduces a striking principle: the same way neighbors can compel one another to share in the cost of a wall addressing *hezek reiyah*, one can apparently compel a neighbor to share in building a trespass-prevention barrier. Neighboring relationships and shared spaces generate mutual obligations — not only for the common good, as in the case of a town wall, but also to prevent the specific, interpersonal damage of trespass. The trespass concern is treated as a form of potential damage that both parties share responsibility to prevent.

### Higher Yard Exemption

The *gemara* raises a challenge: *im haya chatzero lemala migago shel chaveiro, ein nizkakin lo* — if one's yard is situated higher than a neighbor's rooftop, no wall is required. Here the person in the yard is constantly and directly overlooking the roof below, which would seem to present a far more serious *hezek reiyah* — the presence is more constant and direct than the adjacent-rooftop case. Yet no wall is demanded.

The explanation is that compromising the use of a *gag* — a rooftop — is simply not treated as a weighty *hezek reiyah* concern. Because the rooftop sees irregular use, the ongoing sight line from above does not constitute the kind of *hezek* that compels remedy. *Ein lo esek imo* — one cannot make any demands.

The *gemara* asks whether this means one cannot demand anything at all, not even the ten-*tefachim* trespass partition. It resolves: *lo ein lo esek imo b'arba amot* — one cannot demand a four-*amot* privacy wall, since the underlying *hezek reiyah* claim regarding a rooftop is insufficient, *aval esek yesh lo b'mechitzah shel asarah* — but one can still demand the ten-*tefachim* trespass barrier.

One might ask why a trespass barrier is needed at all once the two properties are at different elevations. The answer appears to be that if the difference in height is gradual enough, or less than ten *tefachim*, one could still wander from one level to the other without realizing it — particularly if the terrain slopes rather than drops sharply. Only once the step between levels is itself at least ten *tefachim* does the geography itself serve as a sufficient divider. As Rashi notes, without such a barrier, trespass could more plausibly be characterized as intentional, lending it the character of *nitpas alav k'ganav* — being caught as a thief — which is more culpable than mere accident.

## Yards at Different Levels

### Rav Huna Opinion

Having established the framework for rooftop privacy, the *gemara* turns to a related and more complex scenario: *shtei chatzerot zo l'malah mi'zo* — two courtyards, one situated higher than the other. Here *hezek reiyah* is clearly operative: the person at the upper level has a continuous and unobstructed view into the lower yard, which sees regular use. The lower resident has a genuine and serious complaint.

Rav Huna rules: *tachton boneh miknegdo v'oleh, v'elyon boneh miknegdo v'oleh* — the lower party builds up from his own level and the upper party builds up from his. The picture is one of a property line running between the two levels, where the wall that will ultimately block the sight line must be constructed jointly, with each party responsible for the portion corresponding to his own position. Exactly how to visualize this is not immediately obvious. It may be that the slope between the two properties is gradual, with the property line running at some intermediate elevation, and each party is responsible for raising the wall from that line to his own level.

### Rav Chisda Opinion

Rav Chisda disagrees: *elyon mesayeya milimata uboneh* — the upper party must also contribute to the construction of the lower portion of the wall. The reasoning is that in order for the wall to reach the height necessary to protect the upper yard, the foundation of the lower portion must first be built. Since the upper party requires that lower construction to achieve his own protection, he must share in its cost. The *gemara* confirms: *tanyah kevata de-Rav Chisda* — a *braita* is cited in support of Rav Chisda's position. That *braita* also incorporates the earlier rule: *v'im hayetah chatzeir l'malah migago shel chaveiro, eino zakuk lo* — if the yard is higher than a neighbor's roof, there is no obligation to build, consistent with what was established above.

## Shared Housing Structure

### Upper Tenant Refusal

The *gemara* shifts to a scenario involving two tenants sharing a single structure, one occupying the lower level and one the upper. *Chad hava dayar elai v'chad hava dayar tata'ei* — one lived above, one below. Then the lower structure partially collapsed or sank — Rashi explains that the wall sank into the ground, causing the floor of the upper level to drop, and with it the ceiling of the lower space. The upper tenant remains entirely comfortable; the lower tenant now cannot stand upright.

The lower tenant asks the upper one to cooperate in rebuilding. The upper tenant refuses: *ana shappir kadiyerna* — I am fine, why should I rebuild? The lower tenant then offers to pay for the entire reconstruction himself. The upper tenant refuses again: he does not wish to be displaced from his home during construction, even temporarily. The lower tenant offers to rent him alternative accommodations. The upper tenant declines: *lo tarchna* — I do not want the trouble of moving.

At this point the lower tenant resorts to bitter humor: *shufa kreichach v'aeul, v'shufa kreichach v'afok* — squirm in on your belly and squirm out again. That is not my problem.

Rav Chama rules: *b'dina kamiaikeiv* — the upper tenant's refusal is entirely legitimate. One cannot be compelled to vacate one's home in order to accommodate a neighbor's need to rebuild, even at the neighbor's expense.

### Ten Tefachim Threshold

Rav Chama qualifies this ruling: *v'hanei milei d'lo matu kashurei l'mata m'asarah* — this applies only so long as the ceiling beams have not dropped to within ten *tefachim* of the floor. Once the ceiling descends to within ten *tefachim* — approximately thirty to thirty-six inches, the same minimum height that defines a valid *sukkah* — the analysis changes. *Aval matu kashurei l'mata m'asarah, matzi amar lei l'mata m'asarah reshusi didi hu* — the lower tenant can then say: below ten *tefachim* is my domain, and you have no right to encroach upon it.

This is a striking formulation. The *gemara* does not frame the issue merely as uninhabitability — that the space has become too low to live in — but as a genuine property claim. Ten *tefachim* is recognized as the basic airspace belonging to ground level: the height of a *reshut ha-rabim* is ten *tefachim*, and a *reshut ha-yachid* extends upward from there. Within ten *tefachim*, one is, legally speaking, occupying the lower tenant's space. The upper tenant is not merely causing inconvenience; he is effectively residing within his neighbor's domain. What precise share of the reconstruction costs the upper tenant can be compelled to bear in this situation remains an open question.

### Stipulation & Heikhal

All of the above applies when the two tenants — who are presumed to have been brothers dividing an inherited house — made no prior stipulations. *Aval asnu bahadi hadadi, v'ki asnu bahadi hadadi satro u'vanu* — if they stipulated that should the structure ever require rebuilding both parties would cooperate, then rebuilding may proceed. This is not a remarkable *chiddush* on its face.

The more interesting question is: if they stipulated in general terms but did not specify what threshold of deterioration triggers the obligation to rebuild, how far must the ceiling drop before the stipulation is invoked? Scholars reported to Rava in the name of Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman, who cited Rav Nachman: *k'osa sheshaninu, rumo k'chatzi archo v'chatzi rachvo* — like that which we have taught, that the standard height is half the combined length and width. This is derived from the dimensions of the *Mikdash*, which was forty *amot* long, twenty *amot* wide, and thirty *amot* high. Forty plus twenty is sixty; half of sixty is thirty, matching the actual height. On this view, if you have a large space with great width and length, the appropriate ceiling height scales proportionally — a grand hall demands a grand height. This same principle, it was claimed, would apply here: if the height drops below half the combined length and width of the space, the stipulation is triggered.

### Normal Usage Standards

Rava rejected this attribution sharply: *lav amina l'chu lo titlu bei bukei srikei b'Rav Nachman* — did I not tell you not to attribute such empty things to Rav Nachman? He could not believe Rav Nachman would say something so contrived. The attribution came from Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman, citing his own father, which Rava apparently found implausible.

What Rav Nachman actually said, Rava insists, is *k'd'daieri inshi* — as normal people dwell. The relevant standard is not a geometric formula derived from the Temple's proportions but simply the local norm for habitable living space. The *gemara* then asks: and how much is that? Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua answers with a practical image: *k'heichi d'ayeli istura d'machuza v'hadar* — like someone carrying a long bundle of sticks into a house in the city of Machuza and being able to turn around. The image is evocative even if not mathematically precise: the space must be sufficient for ordinary movement while carrying common loads.

An interesting possibility raised in the course of this discussion is that the "turning around" image may itself encode a measurement — take the length of the bundle that allows one to just complete a circle within the space, then stand that dimension upright, and you have the required ceiling height. This would yield something close to half the shorter dimension of the room, a modest variation from the *Mikdash* formula. Regardless, the governing principle is the norm of habitation in that time and place, not an arbitrary geometrical ratio.

## Building Near Windows

### Window Solutions

The *gemara* introduces a vivid dispute: *hahu gavra d'avei banya ashista achorei kivei d'chavrei* — someone began building a structure directly facing his neighbor's windows. The neighbor protested: *ka ma'afeilat alai* — you are darkening my home.

What followed was an extended negotiation, with each proposed solution generating a new objection. The builder first offered to seal the existing windows — now blocked by his construction anyway — and cut new windows higher up in the neighbor's wall, above the height of his own structure. The neighbor objected that cutting new holes would damage the integrity of the existing wall. The builder then offered to demolish the top portion of the neighbor's wall down to where the windows would go and rebuild it with windows already incorporated. The neighbor replied that a wall that is old at the bottom and new at the top is structurally unsound. The builder then offered to demolish the neighbor's wall entirely and rebuild it from scratch, with windows properly placed above the new construction. The neighbor objected that one new wall in an otherwise old house would also be structurally unsound. The builder finally offered to tear down and rebuild the entire house. The neighbor said: then I will have nowhere to live. The builder offered to rent him a place during construction. The neighbor refused: *lo tarachna* — I do not want the trouble of moving my belongings.

### Rav Chama Ruling

Rav Chama again rules: *bedin ka-me'akeiv* — the neighbor is within his rights. The person who built a structure that violated the prohibition against blocking another's light is responsible for fixing the problem; the aggrieved party cannot be compelled to cooperate, absorb inconvenience, or vacate his home to facilitate the remedy. Whatever the cost or complexity, it falls entirely on the one who created the violation.

The *gemara* asks: is this case not essentially identical to the previous one about the upper and lower tenants? *Hainu hach* — is this not the same point? It answers: *mahu d'teima af al gav d'lo mishtamesh ela l'tivna v'tzivei d'alma* — one might have thought that if the building was used only as a storehouse for fodder and straw, not as a residence, the owner could not make such strong demands about light. The second case teaches that even for a storehouse, the claim stands. That is itself a significant *chiddush* — one would not have expected the right to natural light to extend to agricultural storage.

A point worth noting: the word *bedin* used by Rav Chama throughout these rulings is not functioning as a reference to a body of law called *din*, but as an adjective confirming that the claims being made are legally valid. The parties are making demands that might seem extreme, and Rav Chama is confirming that those demands are entirely justified.

### Brothers' Division

The *gemara* introduces a related case. Two brothers divided their father's estate. One received the *asplida* — according to Rashi, a mansion or fine building — and the other received the *tarbitza*, the open yard in front of it. The one who received the yard then began building a wall right in front of the mansion's entrance. The mansion owner complained: *ka mafchat alai* — you are darkening my property.

The builder responded: *bedidi kavenina* — I am building on my own land. And Rav Chama again rules: *bedin ka-amar lei* — that response is legally correct. Rashi explains the underlying reason: at the time of the division, neither brother had yet established a *chezkat orah* — a presumptive right to a particular quality of light. That right crystallizes only after three years of undisturbed use. Since the division just occurred and construction began immediately, no such right has been established, and the builder is acting entirely within his rights to use his own property.

## Estate Division Rights

### Vineyards Precedent

Ravina challenges Rav Chama's ruling from a *braita*: *shnei achim she-chalaku, echad mehem natal sdeh karem v'echad natal sdeh lavan* — two brothers divided an estate, one taking a vineyard and one taking a wheat field. The *braita* rules: *yesh lo l'baal ha-karem arba amot b'sdeh lavan* — the owner of the vineyard retains rights to four *amot* within the wheat field, since that is the standard space required to tend the vines along the border.

The principle embedded here is significant: when an estate is divided, the division is presumed to preserve the normal operational rights associated with each portion. One does not receive a vineyard while simultaneously losing the ability to tend it. So too, one might argue, a person who receives a mansion in a division retains the right to the light the mansion normally enjoys.

Why does this *braita* not refute Rav Chama? The *gemara* explains: *hatam de-alu le-hadadi* — in that case, the parties made up the difference in value with money. Since a vineyard is worth more than an equal acreage of wheat field, the one who received the vineyard paid the other to equalize. Once monetary compensation is exchanged to balance the division, it is reasonable to assume the compensation reflects and preserves all normal usage rights, including the four *amot* for tending the vines.

### Air Space Question

The *gemara* then presses: in the case of the mansion and the yard, surely there was also monetary compensation. One brother took a fine building worth far more than a bare plot of land; clearly money changed hands to equalize. If so, by the same logic, all the mansion's normal usage rights — including light — should be preserved.

The *gemara* responds: *nehi de-alu le-hadadi, d'mivne kishorei v'hudrei* — yes, money was paid, but that money covered the cost of the building materials, the beams, the construction itself. *Da-havu d'mei avira lo halu ah-dadai* — but the payment did not cover the air space. The one who received the yard retained his own right to build on his own plot. When he accepted cash compensation for giving up the mansion, he did not give up the right to use his land. He received the equivalent of the mansion's construction value, but never sold the right to keep his plot of land free of construction. Accordingly, he may build.

One might ask: can the mansion owner not say that by accepting the division he implicitly relied on the assumption that the mansion would continue to be used as it always was, with its light and openness? The *gemara* anticipates this: *matzi amar lei ada'ata d'pag d'diurna beh ka-de-hava hatam* — in an estate division among brothers, one is presumed to intend that what was received continue to be used as it was during the father's lifetime. This is not a straightforward commercial sale of a labeled commodity; it is a joint family settlement with embedded expectations.

### Name & Identity

The *gemara* raises the possibility that the builder could simply insist that a mansion is still a mansion even if it is darkened — *shma b'alma palgei leh*, he gave him what is called a mansion, and it is still called that. A parallel is drawn from a *braita*: *ha-omer beis kor afar ani mocher lach* — if someone says "I am selling you a *kor*-measure plot" and it turns out to hold only half that amount, the sale may still stand if that smaller plot has always carried the name of *kor*. The sale is of the name — *shelo machar lo ela shem* — as long as the buyer receives fair market value for what was actually sold. Similarly: a *pardes* with no pomegranates is still a *pardes*; a *kerem* with no vines remaining is still a *kerem*, provided it still bears that name and the buyer receives his money's worth.

Tosafot flags the peculiarity of this rule sharply: it means one can sell a tiny plot for a fraction of its nominal price, invoke an old place-name, and have the sale stand. Iceland and Greenland, as it were. The one protection the buyer has is monetary: he must receive fair value. But he cannot demand the actual acreage or the actual vines.

The *gemara* distinguishes this from the brothers' case: *hacha matzi amar leh ada'ata d'pag d'diurna beh ka-de-hava hatam* — in a commercial sale, the seller says "I am selling you X" and if it is still called X, the sale stands. But in an estate division, both brothers understood what they were taking. The one receiving the mansion expected to continue living in it as it had always been lived in — with light and normal habitability. It is not about labels; it is about the shared understanding of what was being divided.

### Shmuel Position

Mar Yanuqa and Mar Kashisha, sons of Rav Chisda, explain to Rav Ashi why Rav Chama consistently rules in favor of the builder: *Naharda'ei ta'amayu* — his rulings follow the school of Neharde'a. Shmuel is from Neharde'a, and Rav Nachman transmits his position: *ha-achim she-chilku ein lahem lo derech zeh al zeh v'lo chalonot zeh al zeh* — brothers who divided an estate have no rights of passage over each other's portions and no rights to windows or light through each other's property.

The principle is broad and uncompromising. If Shimon took the back portion of a field, he cannot claim a right of way through Reuven's front portion, even if that leaves him with no access to the public thoroughfare. Similarly, if dividing a building leaves one party's windows facing the other's new portion, the other may build freely. *V'lo sulamot zeh al zeh* — no right to lean a ladder; *v'lo amat ha-mayim zeh al zeh* — no right to access an irrigation canal through another's property.

The *gemara* adds: *v'hizharu bahen she-halachot kevu'ot hen* — be careful about these laws, for they are fixed and binding. The word *v'hizharu* likely serves as a practical warning: know these rules before dividing an estate, so that you stipulate explicitly whatever rights you wish to retain. One who accepts a portion without securing those stipulations loses them.

Rava dissents: *yesh lahem* — the divided parties do retain some of those normal usage rights. This is the position Rav Chama opposes. The *gemara* thus frames Rav Chama's repeated rulings as grounded in Shmuel's Neharde'an school, which holds that estate division creates a clean break, with each party limited to exactly what was explicitly allocated.

## Orphans' Receipts

### Keep Original Shtar

The *gemara* presents a final case attributed to Rav Chama. Orphans held a *shtar* — a document establishing that someone owed their late father a sum of money. When they sought to collect, the debtor produced a *tavra* — a receipt indicating the debt had been paid. Rav Chama rules: *agvuyei lo magvinan lei* — we do not collect on the *shtar*, since a receipt has been produced against it. *U'mikra lo karinan lei* — but we also do not tear up the *shtar*.

Why preserve a document that apparently reflects a paid debt? *D'chi gadli yasmi, dilma maisi ra'aya u'marlei* — because when the orphans come of age and are in a position to investigate their father's affairs themselves, they may find evidence that the receipt was forged. Minors cannot adequately represent their own interests; their appointed guardian cannot do so perfectly on their behalf. The *shtar* is preserved so that the orphans, upon reaching adulthood, retain the option to challenge the receipt's authenticity.

### Rav Chama Summary

Looking back over the daf, the *gemara* notes that Rav Chama is the authority behind a series of significant rulings: that the upper tenant cannot be compelled to vacate his home; that a brother who received the yard may build in front of the mansion even though it darkens it; and that the orphans' *shtar* is preserved. Rav Acha son of Rav asks Ravina: *hilchasa mai* — what is the practical ruling?

### Final Psak Decision

The *gemara* rules: *bekulu hilchasa k'Rav Chama, l'bar mi'tafra* — we rule like Rav Chama in all these matters except for the *tavra*, the receipt case. In the other rulings we follow Rav Chama. But regarding the receipt: *d'sahdi b'shikri lo machzinan* — we do not presume that the witnesses who signed the receipt committed perjury. A duly signed and witnessed document cannot simply be treated as suspect on the possibility that orphans might someday disprove it. We tear up the *shtar*.

Mar Zutra son of Rav Mari disagrees: *bahana nami hilchasa k'Rav Chama* — even in the receipt case we rule like Rav Chama and preserve the *shtar*. His reason: *d'im issa d'tavra ma'alya hu, ibayei lei l'afukei b'chayei avuhon* — if the receipt were genuine, it should have been produced while the father was still alive, when he could have contested or responded to it. The fact that it surfaces only after his death, and only when the orphans attempt to collect, is itself grounds for suspicion. *U'med'lo afka, shma mina ziufei zeifin* — since it was not produced earlier, there is reason to conclude that it was forged. On this view, Rav Chama's ruling to preserve the *shtar* is followed even here.
