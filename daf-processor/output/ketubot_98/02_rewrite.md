# Ketubot 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketubah Payment & Mezonot

### Mishnah Overview

The daf opens with a *mishnah* found at the bottom of 97b. The *mishnah* addresses the situation of a widow whose husband's estate is obligated both to pay her *ketubah* and to provide her with *mezonot* — sustenance — for as long as she remains a widow. The underlying structure of this arrangement is that the widow is entitled to be fed from the estate during the period of her widowhood, and her collection of the *ketubah* effectively terminates that entitlement. The *mishnah* further concerns her ability to sell estate property in order to realize payment of what is owed to her, and whether doing so requires the involvement of a *beit din*.

The *mishnah* states: *makhra kesuvatah miktzatah* — if she sold her *ketubah* or even part of it; *mashkanah kesuvatah miktzatah* — if she collateralized her *ketubah* or a part of it; *nasanah kesuvatah le-acher o miktzatah* — or if she gave it as a gift, in whole or in part; then *lo timkor hash'ar ela beveit din* — she may not sell the remainder except through a court.

The *mishnah* here proceeds on the assumptions associated with Rabbi Shimon's position: first, that the only circumstance in which the widow may sell estate property without a *beit din* is when she is doing so to fund her *mezonot*, not to collect the base *ketubah* payment; and second, that once she begins to receive any payout toward the *ketubah* itself — even a partial payout — she loses her entitlement to *mezonot* entirely. With those two premises in place, the *mishnah*'s ruling follows logically: once she has begun collecting on the *ketubah*, any further sale is necessarily for the purpose of the *ketubah*, and that requires a *beit din*.

The *mishnah* may seem imprecise, because even the initial sale for the *ketubah* itself requires a *beit din* under Rabbi Shimon's view. What the *mishnah* is describing is a widow who has been selling property over time for her *mezonot*, and who has now crossed into the territory of receiving *ketubah* payment. From that point forward, two consequences follow: she no longer receives *mezonot*, and any further sale must proceed through the court.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

The *mishnah* then cites what appears to be a continuation of Rabbi Shimon's position. A *baraita* makes explicit what Rabbi Shimon holds: even if she only sold or collateralized half of her *ketubah* — *makhra v'lo mishkan kesuvatah ela machtzitah* — she has nonetheless *avdah mezonoteha*, lost her *mezonot* entirely. This is the essence of Rabbi Shimon's position regarding the relationship between partial collection of the *ketubah* and the ongoing right to *mezonot*.

### Chachamim's View

The *chachamim* reject both of Rabbi Shimon's positions. Their view is stated in the *mishnah* as follows: *mocheret hi afilu arbaah vachamishah peamim* — she may sell even four or five times. This reflects their ruling that even when collecting the *ketubah* itself, piecemeal and repeatedly, she does not require a *beit din*. Furthermore, *umocheret mezonot shelo beveit din* — she may continue to sell for *mezonot* without a *beit din* as well. The crucial addition of the *chachamim*'s position is that even after partial *ketubah* payment has begun, as long as the *ketubah* has not been fully paid out, she retains her right to *mezonot* and may continue selling for that purpose. She writes on the transaction record, as discussed in the previous daf, that the sale was for *mezonot* — *ha-kosevet limezonot machrati* — a formality whose purpose was analyzed there at length.

### Gerushah Rules

The *mishnah* concludes with a separate rule: *u-gerusha lo timkor ela beveit din* — a divorced woman may only sell through a *beit din*. The rationale is that the dispensation allowing the widow to sell without *beit din* — even according to the *chachamim* — is rooted in the concern that the husband would not have wished his widow to be subjected to the indignity of repeated court proceedings. That consideration applies to his widow, not to his divorced wife. Accordingly, the *gerushah* must transact through the *beit din*, and she does so only for collection of the base *ketubah*, as *mezonot* does not apply to a divorced woman.

## Miktzat Kesef Analysis

### Attribution & Baraita

The *gemara* opens by identifying the *mishnah*: *masnisin mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Shimon — as demonstrated by the *baraita* which articulates the views of the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon respectively. According to the *tanna kamma* (which may reflect the view of Rabbi Meir), once the entire *ketubah* is effectively collected — whether by sale, collateralization, or making it an *apotiki* — the widow loses her *mezonot*. Rabbi Shimon goes further: *af al pi she-lo machar v'lo mishkan kesuvatah ela machtzitah* — even if she only sold or collateralized half of it, *avdah mezonoteha* — she has lost her *mezonot*.

### Partial Payment Status

The *gemara* then draws out the conceptual underpinning of the dispute. It states: *le-meimra de-Rabbi Shimon savar delo amrinan miktzat kesef ke-chol kesef* — this seems to say that Rabbi Shimon holds we do not apply the principle that partial money is like all the money. This phrasing is initially counterintuitive, since Rabbi Shimon's ruling seems to equate partial receipt with full receipt. The resolution is that the phrase refers to what remains outstanding: *miktzat kesef ke-chol kesef* means that as long as some portion of the *ketubah* remains unpaid, it is as though the entire debt is still outstanding. Rabbi Shimon rejects this principle — once some of the *ketubah* has been received, the outstanding balance does not preserve her *mezonot* entitlement. The *chachamim*, by contrast, affirm the principle: *amrinan miktzat kesef ke-chol kesef* — as long as part of the debt remains, the full weight of the outstanding obligation continues, and she retains *mezonot*.

### Virginity Analogy

The *gemara* then raises an apparent contradiction from a different context. It notes: *ha-ipcha shaminun lehu* — we have heard them hold the exact opposite positions. The proof comes from a *baraita* concerning the verse *ve-hu ishah bi-vsuleha yikach* — the *kohen gadol* must marry a woman in her virginity. The *baraita* records a dispute: *prat le-voggeret she-kalu visuleha divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir excludes a woman who has passed the age of majority, whose hymen has thinned with puberty. *Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbi Shimon makhshirin be-vogeret* — Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon permit the *kohen gadol* to marry her. This is a genuinely striking *gemara*, because the positions seem reversed: here it appears that Rabbi Meir holds that partial diminishment of the hymen is like total absence of it, while Rabbi Shimon holds that as long as some remains, it is considered fully present — the very opposite of their apparent positions in our *sugya* regarding the *ketubah*.

### Virginity Dispute

The details of the dispute are elaborated further in the *baraita*. Rabbi Meir interprets the verse *bi-vsuleha* — with its possessive and expanded form — to require that *kol bisuleha* be present, the full and complete hymen. Under his reading, *ke-darkah ein she-lo ke-darkah lo* — even if she had only non-vaginal relations, as long as the hymen itself has thinned due to puberty, she is disqualified. His focus is entirely on the physical intactness of the hymen, not on the question of sexual experience. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon interpret *besulah* in its base form to imply a complete virgin, while *bi-vsuleha* with its expanded construction means *afilu miktzat besulim* — even partial presence of the hymen suffices. Their interpretation shifts the disqualification to sexual activity itself: *bein ke-darkah bein she-lo ke-darkah* — whether vaginal or not, if she has had sexual relations she is disqualified regardless of puberty-related changes to the hymen.

### Why Analogy Works

The *gemara*'s introduction of this analogy may seem bizarre at first, but there is a genuine conceptual connection worth noting. The standard *ketubah* amount of two hundred *zuz* is traditionally associated with the *mohar* — the bride price for a *betulah*. The *ketubah* has its roots in the biblical institution of the bride price tied to virginity. There is thus an inherent connection between the intactness of the hymen and the amount of the *ketubah*: partial virginity maps, in a real sense, onto partial *ketubah*. The *gemara* may be reminding us of this deep structural echo — the transformation of an upfront bride price into a deferred insurance mechanism preserved the underlying logic that the *ketubah* reflects the status of *betulah* at the time of marriage.

### Clarifying the Analogy

Despite the apparent elegance of the analogy, the *gemara* ultimately rejects the cross-inference: *hasham bikra palgi* — there, in the case of *besulah*, the dispute is not about a general jurisprudential principle of whether partial presence equals full presence. It is a narrowly textual dispute about how to read the verse. According to Rabbi Meir, the base form *besulah* would have included even a partial *besulah*, and the expanded form *bi-vsuleha* signals that the full hymen is required. According to Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, *besulah* in its base form implies a complete virgin, and *bi-vsuleha* expands eligibility to include partial intactness. The disagreement is entirely about the exegesis of the verse, and cannot be extrapolated into a general principle governing whether partial payment functions as full payment in other contexts. The positions of Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim* in the *ketubah* dispute therefore stand independently of what may appear to be their inverse positions in the *besulah* context.

## Silver Cup Case

### The Story

The *gemara* now introduces a narrative. There was a certain woman — *hahi iteta* — who seized a cup of silver as partial payment toward her *ketubah*. This was not a *mezonot* transaction; she was beginning to collect the base *ketubah* itself. Having received this partial payment, she then came before the court seeking continued *mezonot*.

### Rava's Ruling

The case came before Rava. He ruled: *zilu havu lah mezonot* — go, continue to pay her *mezonot*. The partial receipt of *ketubah* does not sever her entitlement to ongoing sustenance.

### Halakhic Basis

Rava grounded his ruling explicitly: *leit dechash lah le-deRabbi Shimon* — nobody is concerned for the position of Rabbi Shimon. We rule in accordance with the *chachamim*: *amrinan miktzat kesef ke-chol kesef* — as long as a portion of the *ketubah* remains outstanding, the full debt is treated as outstanding, and *mezonot* continues. One might add that if the heirs wished to stop paying *mezonot*, the practical remedy was to pay out the full remaining *ketubah* balance — a choice they apparently preferred not to make.

## Shevuah Requirement

### The Question

The *gemara* records that Rava's son sent a question to Rav Yosef: *mocheret shelo be-veit din tzerikhah shevuah o einah tzerikhah shevuah* — when a widow sells estate property without going through the *beit din*, as she is entitled to do — certainly for *mezonot*, and possibly even for the base *ketubah* depending on which authority one follows — must she take an oath? The concern would be that she swears the debt has not already been paid, ensuring that she is not collecting twice. As *Tosafot* immediately notes, the requirement for a widow to take an oath before collecting from the orphans' estate is itself already established in an earlier *mishnah* in *perek ha-kones*: *ein almanah nifra'at mi-nechasei yetomim ela bi-shevuah* — a widow may not collect from the orphans' estate except by oath. The question here must therefore concern something beyond that basic oath — as *Tosafot* develops at length.

### Hachrazah Concern

The *gemara* challenges the framing of the question: *ve-tiba'ei leih hachrazah* — why not ask first about the requirement of public announcement? When property is sold under court supervision, a process of *hachrazah* — public advertisement and auction — is conducted to ensure the property fetches a fair price and protects the interests of the orphans. If the widow is selling without *beit din*, does she still need to conduct such a public process?

The questioner responds: *hachrazah lo kamiba'ya li* — I have no question about *hachrazah*. The reason is established by Rabbi Zeira in the name of Rav Nachman: *almanah she-shamah le-atzmah lo asata ve-lo kelum* — if a widow assessed the property for her own benefit, meaning she appraised it and then purchased it herself, the transaction is entirely void. The *gemara* asks: what are the circumstances? *Ei de'achriz* — if a proper process of public announcement had taken place, why should it matter that she was the winning bidder? If the auction was fair and open and she had the highest bid, who is harmed? The answer must therefore be that we are discussing a case where *no* public process occurred — *ela lav de-lo achriz*. And in that case, the rule is: *ha-la'acher mai de'avad avad* — if she sold to a third party without *hachrazah*, the sale stands. But she cannot sell to herself — *maan shamlich* — who was it that sold it to you? You cannot be both seller and buyer. *Lo yatzah karka me-reshut yetomim* — the land never left the orphans' domain. There must be two parties to a transaction.

The *gemara* is thus reading the law as follows: *hachrazah* is indeed required in the context of a court-supervised sale, but the widow selling on her own authority does not require it — and if she did require it, she could then legitimately participate as a bidder. The law that her self-sale is void is therefore on different grounds: the inherent structural requirement of a two-sided transaction. This is explicitly stated by Rashi: *mimi kabalt mechirah zo? Lo mi-veit din, ve-lo mi-yasmei* — from whom did you receive this transaction? Not from the court, and not from the orphans.

### Cannot Sell to Self

This raises a striking observation: the widow, selling without *beit din*, has enormous latitude. She may select which portion of the estate to sell, she may conduct a private transaction without public announcement, and she may sell for whatever price she negotiates. The only firm prohibition is self-purchase. This understandably prompts concern about the orphans' interests. If she is ignorant of the property's value, or indifferent to the orphans' welfare — for instance if they are not her own children — the estate could suffer significant loss without any obvious remedy. At the same time, the *sugya* suggests the law may be structured this way precisely to incentivize the heirs to take proactive steps: going to *beit din* preemptively and establishing a structured arrangement, perhaps setting aside funds in escrow, rather than leaving the widow with unrestricted access to the estate's *karka*.

### Self-Assessment Story

The *gemara* illustrates the prohibition on self-assessment with a narrative. A certain man was entrusted with an item belonging to orphans — whether a flask, some coral, or fodder, the precise nature being uncertain. He was serving in an *apotropus*-like capacity as estate administrator. He assessed the item himself at 400 *zuz* and purchased it for that amount, directing the proceeds to the estate. The value subsequently rose to 600 *zuz*. The case came before Rabbi Ami, who ruled: *maan shamlich* — who sold this to you? The property never legitimately left the orphans' domain. The transaction was void regardless of whether the price he paid was fair, because a person cannot serve simultaneously as both parties to a sale. The item belonged to the orphans.

### Tosafot's Analysis

*Tosafot* addresses the nature of the *shevuah* at stake. The *gemara*'s conclusion is *tzrikhah shevuah* — she must take an oath. *Tosafot* points out that one reading would be that the oath concerns whether she has already been partially paid — ensuring she is not collecting twice in the course of selling off the estate piecemeal. She might be required to swear that the *ketubah* has not already been collected to a greater degree than she claims.

However, *Tosafot* in the name of *Rabbi* (the *Ri*) argues that the *shevuah* is not about overpayment at all — *lo ke-ferush ha-kuntreis shelo gavtah yoter* — it is not the oath that she has not collected more than she states. Rather, *ela miba'ei lei im mashbi'in otah shelo zilzelah ba-nechasim* — the question is whether we require her to swear that she did not deal carelessly or irresponsibly with the estate property. This reading is supported by the structure of the *gemara*'s challenge about *hachrazah*: the connection between an oath and a public announcement process is not about prior payment but about the quality of the transaction itself. The *shevuah* and the *hachrazah* are both mechanisms for protecting the orphans against negligent or inadequate sale prices. The *gemara* concludes that *hachrazah* is not required, but an oath of responsible dealing is. Even so, the practical protection this affords the orphans is limited — an oath that one acted to the best of one's knowledge does not guarantee a competent or well-advised transaction.

## Oversell/Undersell Rules

### Undersell Case

The next *mishnah* addresses two scenarios of improperly calibrated sales. The first: *almanah she-hayetah ketuvatah matayim u-mocheret shaveh maneh be-matayim* — a widow whose *ketubah* was 200 sold a piece of estate property worth 100 for 200. In this case, *nigveta ketuvatah* — her *ketubah* is considered fully paid. She got 200; she was owed 200. The fact that the property was worth only half what she received is irrelevant — the debt is discharged.

The reverse is then stated: *o shavim matayim be-maneh* — or she sold property worth 200 for only 100. Again, *nigveta ketuvatah* — her *ketubah* is considered fully paid. Even though she undersold and received less cash than the property was worth, she is done. She sold a piece of the estate valued at the full amount of her *ketubah*, and the estate has discharged its obligation in the form of the property transferred, even if she made a bad deal in converting it to cash.

### Oversell Case

The second scenario is different in kind: *hayetah ketuvatah maneh ve-makhra shaveh maneh ve-dinar be-maneh* — her *ketubah* was 100, and she sold a piece of the estate worth 125 for 100. Here, *mekherah batel* — the sale is entirely void. She sold property worth more than her outstanding debt. She was authorized to sell in order to collect what was owed to her, not to sell estate property of greater value. And even if she offers to return the excess — *afilu hi omeret akhzir et ha-dinar la-yorshim* — to give back the 25 to the heirs, whether in cash or by purchasing replacement property for them, the *mishnah* still rules the sale void. She sold property she was not authorized to sell, and that invalidity is not cured by a subsequent offer of restitution.

### Rabban Shimon's View

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies this last ruling: *le-olam mekhra kayam ad she-tehe sham kedei she-tishtayer ba-sadeh bat tish'ah kabbin* — the sale is always valid unless the overage constitutes a separate and independently viable parcel of land — specifically, a field of nine *kav* (or, for a garden, a half *kav*, and per Rabbi Akiva, a quarter *kav*). His logic is that absolute precision in the size of property sold is not always possible. A seller cannot realistically draw a line through the middle of a field to sell exactly the square footage corresponding to her debt. Therefore, modest overage is acceptable — the law does not require impossible precision.

However, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel draws the line at overage large enough to constitute an independently subdivisible parcel. If the extra portion is sufficiently large that it *could* have been sold as a separate piece of land, then the seller had no excuse for not subdividing and should have done so. The criterion is not monetary value but physical acreage, for the reason that what determines whether a parcel is independently viable is its size, not its per-unit price. Notably, the principle regarding the quality of land (*ziburit*) is not at issue in this context.

### Multiple Sales

The *mishnah* then extends the analysis to a case involving sequential sales: *hayetah ketuvatah arba me'ah zuz u-machru la-zeh be-maneh, ula-zeh be-maneh, ula-acharon yafeh maneh ve-dinar be-maneh* — her *ketubah* was 400 *zuz*, and she sold to one buyer a field worth 100, to a second a field worth 100, to a third a field worth 100, and to a fourth, a field worth 125 for 100. The ruling is *acharon batel ve-shel kulan kayam* — only the last sale is void; all the prior sales are valid. She had a 400 *zuz* debt, she had collected 300 *zuz* worth of legitimate sales, and the final sale exceeded her remaining entitlement of 100 by 25. The invalidity attaches only to the transaction that overstepped her authority, while the preceding sales, which were within her rights, stand.

## Undersell/Oversell Analy…

### At Hifsadet Principle

The *gemara* analyzes the rulings of the *mishnah*. The first question is why, when she sells property worth 200 for only 100, her *ketubah* is considered discharged. The answer: *de-amri la at hifsadet* — they say to her, you suffered the loss. The estate delivered to her a piece of property valued at her full *ketubah*; whatever she received in cash for it is her business. She chose to undersell, and the estate has done its part by having property of the requisite value available for her to collect. This also provides a practical incentive: the widow has every reason to ensure she is not underselling. While she may not be motivated to drive the price higher than necessary, she certainly has reason not to be cheated.

### Reverse Question

The *gemara* then asks why the reverse does not apply symmetrically. When she sells property worth 100 for 200 — a genuinely good deal — why can she not argue *ana arvachna* — I only took a piece of the estate worth 100, and the extra 100 I received was the product of my own business acumen? If the standard for underselling is based on the size of the property transferred, why not apply the same logic to overselling, treating the surplus as a personal gain separate from the *ketubah* payment?

### Hakol Leba'al Hama'ot

Rav Nachman citing Rav bar Avuha answers: *kan shana Rabbi, hakol le-ba'al hama'ot* — here the rule is that everything goes to the one with the money. The principle is that when a transaction is conducted on someone else's behalf — when the money in question belongs to the person who sent you to the market — any surplus generated through that transaction accrues to the owner of the money, not to the one conducting the transaction. The widow is not selling her own property; she is selling estate property, collecting against a debt. The extra money she received above the property's value is part of the price realized for the estate's asset, and it goes toward payment of her *ketubah* debt. She cannot claim it as a personal windfall.

### Baraita: Fixed Amounts

A *baraita* is cited that complicates this picture. The case concerns an agent sent to purchase goods: *hosifu lo achat yeteirah* — if the seller added an extra item. Rabbi Yehuda says it belongs to the agent — *hakol la-shaliach* — it was a gift to him personally. Rabbi Yossi says they split it — *cholkin*. But a separate *baraita* records Rabbi Yossi as saying *hakol le-ba'al hama'ot* — all of it goes to the one who provided the money.

### Rami's Resolution

Rami bar Chama resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — kan be-davar she-yesh lo kitzva, kan be-davar she-ein lo kitzva* — there is no difficulty. The cases differ. When there is a fixed, standard price for the item — a fixed quantity at a fixed rate — and the seller throws in an extra unit, that extra is a gift. It is not part of the agreed transaction; it is a bonus extended to the agent personally, and the agent may keep it or share it. But when the amount is not fixed — when the quantity or price is negotiable — then the extra is not a gift but part of the negotiated transaction, and it accrues to the person whose money funded the purchase.

### Land Sales Application

The application to the widow's case is then made explicit. The sale of land is always *ein lo kitzva* — there is no fixed price. Land is always subject to negotiation; there is no standard per-unit price at which real estate trades. Therefore, any surplus price she receives above the assessed value of the property is not a personal bonus to her — it is part of the negotiated price for that piece of land. The money all goes toward discharge of the *ketubah* debt. Accordingly, when she sells property worth 100 for 200, the entire 200 is treated as having been received by the estate against her *ketubah* claim, and her *ketubah* is discharged. Rav Pappa states the *halakha*: for items with a fixed price, they split the bonus between the agent and the owner; for items without a fixed price, *hakol le-ba'al hama'ot* — it all goes to the party who provided the money.

## Agent Excess Case

### Agent Excess Q

The *gemara* now poses a question that circles back to the *mishnah*'s case of the widow who oversold. The scenario: *amri lei zavin li litkha* — you instruct your agent to go purchase for you a *litkha*, half a *kor* of grain. *Ve-azil ve-zavin leh kura* — and he goes and purchases a full *kor*. What is the legal status of this transaction?

Two possibilities are presented: *mosif al devarav havei* — perhaps he is considered to have merely added to his instructions. On this reading, the first half of the purchase — the *litkha* — was conducted as a valid agent acting within his authority, and that half is a binding transaction. The second half he exceeded his mandate, and on that portion he acted on his own account. Alternatively, *ma'avir al devarav havei* — perhaps by buying more than he was told, he has completely disregarded his instructions, and is therefore considered to have acted entirely on his own initiative for the *entire* purchase. On this reading, even the first *litkha* is not a valid agency transaction, and *ve-litkha nami lo kani* — he has not even validly acquired the half he was authorized to acquire.

### Me'ilah Principle

To resolve this question, the *gemara* turns to the laws of *me'ilah* — misappropriation of consecrated property. The principle of *me'ilah* applies only *be-shogeg*, unwittingly; one who knowingly benefits from *hekdesh* property does not bring a *korban me'ilah* though other penalties may apply. One of the distinctive features of *me'ilah* is that it is among the few areas in which we apply *yeish shliach le-davar aveirah* — an agent's unwitting transgression on behalf of an equally unwitting principal is attributed to the principal. If both the one who sent the agent and the agent are *shogeg*, the *me'ilah* is treated as though committed by the sender.

### Meat Story

The relevant case from *me'ilah* law: *amar ba'al ha-bayit li-shlucho* — a homeowner says to his agent, *ten lahem chaticha la-orchim* — give each guest one piece of meat. The meat is *kodashim*. The agent instead says to the guests: *telu shtayim* — each take two. The guests then take three pieces each. The ruling: *kulam ma'alu* — all of them have committed *me'ilah*. The homeowner is liable for the first piece, since that was within his agency. The agent is liable for the second piece, since that was on his own initiative. The guests are liable for the third piece, since that exceeded even what the agent authorized.

### Attribution Distinction

The *gemara* then explains precisely when this apportionment applies. The case assumes that the agent explicitly distinguished between the two pieces: *telu achat mi-da'ato shel ba'al ha-bayit ve-achat mi-da'ati* — take one because the owner authorizes it, and take another because I say so. That explicit attribution is what creates the clean division of liability. If, by contrast, the agent simply said *telu shtayim* without any such distinction — presenting the double allocation as the owner's instruction — he would be treated as having completely overridden his agency, and the homeowner would bear no *me'ilah* liability at all, even for the first piece. The agent, by claiming to act for the owner while wildly exceeding his mandate, is considered to have been acting entirely on his own initiative. The *gemara* thus establishes: when an agent straightforwardly adds to what he was told, the first part remains valid agency and the excess is on his own account. But when the agent presents his excessive action as authorized — *ma'avir al devarav* — he has abandoned his agency entirely and acts solely for himself.

### Proof from Mishnah

The *gemara* now attempts to bring proof from our *mishnah* to resolve the original question. The case is: *hayetah ketuvatah maneh ve-makhra shaveh maneh ve-dinar be-maneh* — her *ketubah* was 100 and she sold property worth 125 for 100. The *mishnah* rules *mekherah batel* — the entire sale is void. The *gemara* reads this as: she actually sold the property worth 125 for 125 — the phrase *be-maneh* means she sold it in exchange for the discharge of her 100 *maneh* debt — and the question is why the sale is entirely void rather than valid for the first 100 worth of property.

The *gemara* suggests: *mai lav de-zavin shaveh maneh ve-dinar be-maneh ve-dinar* — is this not a case where she sold a property worth 125 for 125, and the only issue is that she sold 25 more than she was authorized to? If the principle were *mosif al devarav* — exceeding one's authority only invalidates the excess — then the sale of the first 100 should remain valid. Yet the *mishnah* rules the entire sale void. This would appear to prove that exceeding one's authority — *ma'avir al devarav* — invalidates the entire transaction, including the portion that was within the agent's authority.

### Rav Huna's Correction

Rav Huna son of Rav Natan rejects this proof. The *mishnah* is not discussing a case where she sold the property at fair value and simply took an oversized parcel. Rather, *be-doshaya* — the case in the *mishnah* is one of underselling: the property was worth 125 and she sold it for only 100. The reason the sale is void is not that she overreached her agency by taking too large a parcel at a fair price, but rather that she undersold a piece of property she was not fully authorized to sell, and the orphans are left worse off. The voiding of the sale is therefore a remedy for the loss caused to the orphans by the inadequate price, not a ruling about agency doctrine. Consequently, our *mishnah* provides no proof one way or the other about the question of whether *mosif al devarav* leaves the authorized portion intact.

### Unresolved Q

The *gemara* thus leaves the original question unresolved. If the widow sold a piece of property of appropriate size at a fair price, but the parcel happened to be slightly larger than the precise value of her outstanding *ketubah* — in a manner that exceeds her authorized mandate but does not prejudice the estate through underselling — it remains uncertain whether the portion she was authorized to sell is valid while only the overage is void, or whether the entire transaction is invalidated because she exceeded her authority from the outset.
