# Bava Metzia 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Elu Chayav Lehachriz

### Parallel to Elu Metziot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז: מָצָא פֵּירוֹת בִּכְלִי, אוֹ כְּלִי כְּמוֹת שֶׁהוּא. מָעוֹת בְּכִיס, אוֹ כִּיס כְּמוֹת שֶׁהוּא. צִבּוּרֵי פֵּירוֹת, צִבּוּרֵי מָעוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** **And** for **these** found items, one is **obligated to proclaim** his find: If **one found produce inside a vessel, or a vessel by itself; coins inside a pouch, or a pouch by itself; piles of produce; piles of coins,**

The daf begins at the bottom of 24b with a *mishna* that stands as a direct structural parallel to the opening *mishna* of *Elu Metziot*. Where that first *mishna* enumerated the objects a finder is permitted to keep — *elu metziot shelo*, these are the found objects that are his — the present *mishna* presents the inverse: *elu chayav lehachriz*, these are the objects a finder is obligated to announce and return to their owner. The underlying logic of both *mishnayot* is identical: the presence or absence of identifiable *simanim*. In the earlier *mishna*, the objects lacked any *siman* and were therefore deemed ownerless upon being lost. Here, by contrast, the objects carry *simanim* — identifying characteristics — that allow the original owner to stake a claim.

### Simanim and Identification

One of the striking features of studying *mishna* as an independent body of law, distinct from its embedding within the Talmud, is that the *mishnayot* address the laws themselves without articulating the underlying principles that drive them. Neither this *mishna* nor the parallel earlier one explicitly mentions the term *simanim*. The concept, along with its entire associated *machloket* — including the debates over whether *simanim de-oraita* or *simanim de-rabbanan* — emerges entirely from the *Amoraim*'s analysis. The principle of *simanim* is implicit throughout the *mishna*; the *mishna* records outcomes, while the *gemara* excavates the framework beneath them.

### Mishna Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה מַטְבְּעוֹת זֶה עַל גַּב זֶה, כְּרִיכוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וְכִכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר הַלְּקוּחִין מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן, כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** **three coins** stacked **one atop another; bundles** of grain **in a secluded area; loaves of a homeowner,** as each shapes his loaves in his own unique manner; **wool fleeces that are taken from the house of a craftsman,** as each craftsman processes the wool in his own unique manner; **jugs of wine; or jugs of oil.** If one finds any of **these,** he is **obligated to proclaim** his find.

The *mishna* opens: *ve-elu chayav lehachriz* — these are the objects one must announce. The cases are as follows. *Matza peirot bekli* — if one found fruit in a vessel. Notably, this is the direct parallel to *peirot mefuzarot* in the previous *mishna*: scattered fruit lacked any *siman* and could be kept, but fruit placed inside a *kli* takes on the vessel as its *siman*. The *kli* itself, moreover, carries its own *simanim*, since virtually every vessel differs from every other in some identifiable way. The *mishna* adds *o kli kemoto* — or the vessel alone, even without its contents — since the vessel independently carries identifying characteristics. Similarly, *ma'ot bekis* — coins found in a wallet — and *o kis kemoto* — the wallet alone, even without coins. The reasoning mirrors the fruit case: the wallet provides a *siman* for the coins, and the wallet itself is independently identifiable.

Beyond these, the *mishna* enumerates several additional categories. *Tziburei peirot* and *tziburei ma'ot* — piles of fruit and piles of coins — not scattered, but gathered into defined heaps. *Krichot birshut hayachid* — bundles of straw found in a private domain, contrasted with the earlier *mishna*'s case of bundles in *reshut harabim*, where the *siman* would have been presumed destroyed by foot traffic. *Veikrot shel ba'al habayit* — the loaves of a homeowner, as opposed to a baker's standardized loaves, since a homeowner's bread reflects individual variation in braiding, shape, or form; the homeowner is personally familiar with each loaf. *Vegizei tzemer halekuchim mibeit ha'uman* — shearings of wool taken from the artisan's house, meaning wool that has already undergone some processing and thus carries a degree of *siman* that raw unprocessed wool from the countryside does not. And finally, *kadei yayin ukadei shemen* — jars of wine and jars of oil. All of these, the *mishna* concludes, *chayav lehachriz* — require the finder to make an announcement.

## Fruits Next to Container

### Reasoning Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּמָצָא פֵּירוֹת בִּכְלִי וּמָעוֹת בְּכִיס. הָא כְּלִי וּלְפָנָיו פֵּירוֹת, כִּיס וּלְפָנָיו מָעוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. תְּנֵינָא לְהָא, דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָצָא כְּלִי וּלְפָנָיו פֵּירוֹת, כִּיס וּלְפָנָיו מָעוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. מִקְצָתָן בַּכְּלִי וּמִקְצָתָן עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, מִקְצָתָן בַּכִּיס וּמִקְצָתָן עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the mishna: **The reason** one is obligated to proclaim his find is **that he found produce inside the vessel or coins inside the pouch; but** if he found **a vessel and produce** was **before it,** or if he found **a pouch and coins** were **before it, those,** the produce and coins, belong **to him.** The Gemara comments: **We learn** from this mishna by inference **that** which **the Sages taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: If one found **a vessel and produce** was **before it,** or if he found **a pouch and coins** were **before it, those,** the produce and coins, belong **to him.** If **some of** the produce is **in the vessel and some of** the produce is **on the ground,** or if **some of** the coins are **inside the pouch and some of them** are **on the ground,** one is **obligated to proclaim** his find.

The *gemara* opens with a sharp inferential question. *Ta'ama dematza peirot bikli* — the reason you must announce and return the found object is that you found the fruit inside the vessel, *ume'ot bekis* — and the coins inside the wallet. The implicit *diyuk* from the *mishna* is: *ha kli ulfanav peirot, kis ulfanav me'ot — harei elu shelo* — but if the fruits were merely *next to* the container, or the coins *next to* the wallet, those would be yours to keep. The *gemara* thus identifies a gray zone between *peirot mefuzarot* — which are entirely scattered and clearly ownerless — and fruit inside a vessel — which clearly carries a *siman*. Money lying next to a wallet is neither scattered nor contained, and the question of how to treat such intermediate cases has real halachic stakes: there is a positive commandment to return lost objects and a negative commandment, *lo tuchal lehitalem*, prohibiting one from ignoring them. The boundary cannot be left undefined.

### First Braita Case

The *gemara* notes that this *diyuk* finds support in a *braita*: *matza kli ulfanav peirot* — if you found a vessel with fruit in front of it, *o kis ulfanav me'ot* — or a wallet with coins beside it — *harei elu shelo*, they are yours. The *mishna* is thus the operative *halacha*: it is only when the fruit or coins are actually *inside* the container that the finder is obligated to announce and return. The *braita* continues, however, with a more nuanced clause: *miktzatan bikli umiktzatan al gabei karka* — if some of the fruit are inside the vessel and some on the ground, or *miktzatan bekis umiktzatan al gabei karka* — some of the coins inside the wallet and some outside — *chayav lehachriz*, one must announce everything. The reasoning is intuitive: if a bucket of apples has some apples still inside it and a pile of apples beside it, one can reasonably presume those outside fell out of the vessel. The partial containment reveals a connection between the vessel and the adjacent items.

### Second Braita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: מָצָא דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סִימָן בְּצַד דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סִימָן – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. בָּא בַּעַל סִימָן וְנָטַל אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ – זָכָה הַלָּה בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סִימָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: If one **found an item on which there is no distinguishing mark alongside an item on which there is a distinguishing mark,** he is **obligated to proclaim** that he found both. If the **owner of** the item with the **distinguishing mark came and took his** item but did not claim ownership of the other item, **the other** person, who found the items, **acquires the item on which there is no distinguishing mark.** This *halakha* should also apply when one finds a vessel on which there is a distinguishing mark and produce on which there is no distinguishing mark.

A second *braita* is then cited as a challenge. This *braita* states: *matza davar she'ein bo siman b'tzad davar she'yesh bo siman* — if one found something without a *siman* alongside something with a *siman* — *chayav lehachriz*, one must announce. So far this appears consistent with the first *braita*. But the *braita*'s *seifa* creates an apparent contradiction: *ba ba'al siman v'natal et shelo, zacha haleh b'davar she'ein bo siman* — if the owner of the object *with* a *siman* comes and retrieves what is his, the finder acquires the object *without* a *siman*. This seems to imply that the fruits — which have no independent *siman* — can ultimately be retained by the finder even in this mixed-case scenario, in tension with the first *braita*'s ruling of *chayav lehachriz* on the whole.

*Rashi* reads the *gemara* with a distinctive approach: *chayav lehachriz al hakol* — one must announce everything found, including both the vessel and the adjacent fruit. The owner of the vessel is given the opportunity to claim the fruit as well. But if he takes his vessel and explicitly disclaims any connection to the fruit — *ein hama'ot sheli*, the coins are not mine — then the finder may keep them. This reading integrates the two *braitot*: the initial obligation is to announce everything, but the ultimate disposition of the *siman*-less items depends on whether the vessel's owner asserts a claim over them.

### Rav Zevid's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּכוּבָּא וְכִיתָּנָא, הָא בְּצַנָּא וּפֵירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites several possible resolutions to this contradiction. **Rav Zevid said** that this is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, **where** the finder is obligated to proclaim his finding of both the vessel and the produce, is referring **to a container and flax.** Since the flax fibers are intertwined, when part of the flax falls out of the container, all of the flax would fall out. Therefore, the fact that the flax is completely outside the container is not an indication that it was never in the container. **That** mishna, from which it is inferred that produce found outside the vessel belongs to the finder, is referring **to a basket and produce.** Had the produce fallen out of the basket, presumably some produce would remain in the basket, because the individual units of produce are not connected. Therefore, the fact that no produce was found in the basket indicates that the produce did not fall out of the basket.

*Amar Rav Zevid, la kashya* — Rav Zevid declares that the two *braitot* do not in fact contradict one another; they address different materials. *Ha bekuba dechitna* — the first case concerns a box of flax; *ha betzina upeirei* — the second concerns a barrel of fruit. The distinction hinges on how the respective materials behave when a container tips over or is disturbed.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* disagree on the mechanics. According to *Rashi*, flax — being long, bunched, and tangled — does not spill out cleanly; some will always remain caught inside the container even if it tips. Fruit, by contrast, being round, will roll freely and scatter all at once. Rav Zevid's resolution thus follows: if a container has some flax inside and some flax lying beside it, one can reasonably presume the external flax spilled from the container — some spilled out, some remained, consistent with how flax behaves. Therefore, *chayav lehachriz* on everything. But if it is a barrel of fruit with fruit inside and fruit beside it, and fruit would be expected to roll entirely out if the barrel had tipped, then the presence of fruit still inside the barrel *disproves* the theory that the adjacent fruit spilled out. The external fruit must therefore have a separate origin, and the finder need not include them in the announcement.

*Tosafot* inverts the mechanical assumption: flax, being tangled, would all fall out as one cohesive mass if the container tipped, leaving nothing inside. But the underlying principle of Rav Zevid's resolution is clear regardless of which view one accepts: the question is whether the physical properties of the spilled material allow one to infer a connection between the items inside and outside the container.

### Rav Pappa Alternatives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא בְּצַנָּא וּפֵירֵי, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִשְׁתְּיַיר בַּהּ מִידֵּי, הָא דְּלָא אִשְׁתְּיַיר בַּהּ מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Both **this** ruling **and that** ruling are referring to **a basket and produce,** and nevertheless it is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, **where** the finder is obligated to proclaim his finding of the produce found outside the  vessel, is referring to a case **where some** produce **remains in** the basket. **That** mishna, from which it is inferred that produce found outside the vessel belongs to the finder, is referring to a case **where no** produce **remains in** the basket.

Rav Pappa offers two alternative resolutions. His first: *ha de-ishtayer ba midi, ha de-lo ishtayer ba midi* — the distinction has nothing to do with the type of material (flax versus fruit), but simply whether anything remained inside the container. If some did remain, one presumes the external items fell out; if nothing remained, no such presumption arises. His second alternative (*ve'ibeit eima*): even if both cases involve an entirely empty container with fruit alongside it, the determinative factor is the orientation of the container's opening. *Ha de-mahadrei apei legabei pirei* — if the mouth of the container faces the pile of fruit, one can presume the fruit rolled out in that direction. If the mouth faces away, no such inference is available.

### Container Design Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּלָא אִשְׁתְּיַיר בַּהּ מִידֵּי, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּמְהַדְּרִי אַפֵּיהּ לְגַבֵּי פֵּירֵי, הָא דְּלָא מְהַדְּרִי אַפֵּיהּ לְגַבֵּי פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Both **this** ruling **and that** ruling are referring to a case **where no** produce **remains in** the basket, **and** nevertheless, it is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, where the finder is obligated to proclaim his finding of the produce found outside the empty vessel, is referring to a case **where** the **mouth** of the basket **is facing the produce,** indicating that the produce fell from it. **That** mishna, from which it is inferred that produce found outside the vessel belongs to the finder, is referring to a case **where** the **mouth** of the basket **is not facing the produce.**

A final resolution (*ve'ibeit eima*) addresses the case where, in both scenarios, the container's mouth faces the adjacent fruit. Here the distinction turns on whether the container has a lip or lid — *de-it lei ognin letzana* versus *de-leit lei ognin letzana*. If the container has a lip designed to retain its contents, one cannot presume the adjacent fruit spilled out of it, since the structural design would have prevented spillage. If there is no such lip or lid, the inference of spillage remains available.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דִּמְהַדְּרִי אַפֵּיהּ לְגַבֵּי פֵּירֵי, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִית לֵהּ אוּגְנִין לְצַנָּא, הָא דְּלֵית לֵהּ אוּגְנִין לְצַנָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Both **this** ruling **and that** ruling are referring to a case **where** the **mouth** of the basket **is facing the produce,** and nevertheless, it is **not difficult: That** mishna, from which it is inferred that produce found outside the vessel belongs to the finder, is referring to a case **where** the empty **basket has a rim.** Had the produce fallen out of the basket, the rim would have prevented some of the produce from falling. **This** *baraita*, **where** the finder is obligated to proclaim the produce found outside the empty vessel, is referring to a case **where the basket has no rim** and therefore the produce in its entirety could have fallen from the basket.

## Piles of Fruit/Coins

### Number vs. Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִבּוּרֵי פֵּירוֹת וְצִבּוּרֵי מָעוֹת. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ מִנְיָן הָוֵי סִימָן, תְּנִי: ״צִבּוּר פֵּירוֹת״. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ מָקוֹם הָוֵי סִימָן, תְּנִי: ״צִבּוּרֵי פֵּירוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: And for these found items, one is obligated to proclaim his find: **Piles of produce and piles of coins. Conclude from it** that **number is a distinguishing mark,** and one reclaims his produce or coins by correctly declaring the number of piles. The Gemara rejects that proof. Perhaps one should **teach** the mishna as stating: **A pile of produce.** It is not the number of piles but their location that serves as a determining mark. Based on that emendation, **conclude from it** that **location is a distinguishing mark.** The Gemara rejects that proof as well. Perhaps one should **teach** the mishna as stating: **Piles of produce.** Since the authoritative version of the mishna is unclear, no proof can be cited from it.

The *mishna*'s formulation *tziburei peirot u-tziburei ma'ot* — piles of fruit and piles of coins — prompts a *gemara* question about what precisely qualifies as a *siman* in this context. The *gemara* suggests: *minyan havei siman* — perhaps the *number* of piles serves as an identifying characteristic, since the *mishna* uses the plural form *tziburei* rather than the singular *tzibur*. A claimant would have to specify how many piles he left, and that count would function as the *siman*.

But the *gemara* counters: *shma mina makom havei siman* — perhaps the relevant *siman* is *location*, not quantity. The *gemara* then notes that the *mishna*'s doubled formulation — both *tziburei peirot* and *tziburei ma'ot* — may be designed to convey both: that the number of piles and the location of those piles each independently function as *simanim*. This stands in contrast to *mefuzarot*, where the items are too scattered to constitute any defined pile — no number and no location are identifiable. It is worth noting that while *minyan* as a *siman* works naturally when discussing the number of discrete piles, it would be far less applicable as a count of individual pieces when dealing with grain, where the number of kernels is practically unknowable. The *diyuk* from the *mishna* thus remains somewhat open, pointing to both possibilities without definitively resolving which one the *mishna* itself intends.

## Three Coins Stacked

### Migdala's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה מַטְבְּעוֹת זֶה עַל גַּב זֶה. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק מַגְדְּלָאָה: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִין. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מָצָא מָעוֹת מְפוּזָּרוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, עֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִים – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן עֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִים? שְׁלֹשָׁה מַטְבְּעִין זֶה עַל גַּב זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: And for these found items, one is obligated to proclaim his find: **Three coins** stacked **one atop another. Rabbi Yitzḥak from Migdal says: And** one **is** obligated to proclaim the find in a case **where** the coins **are arranged in** well-ordered **towers. This is also taught** in a *baraita*: If one **found scattered coins, these** belong **to him.** If the coins **are arranged in** well-ordered **towers,** he is **obligated to proclaim** his find. The *baraita* elaborates: **And these** coins **are arranged in towers: Three coins** stacked **one atop another.**

The *mishna* states *shelosha matbe'ot zeh al gav zeh* — three coins one on top of the other — as a configuration requiring *chayav lehachriz*. What exactly does this mean visually? The *gemara* cites *Amar Rabbi Yitzchak Migdala* — Rabbi Yitzchak of Migdala — who says: *kemigdalin*, like a tower. This is a charming instance of a tanna or *amora* teaching a *halacha* that appears to echo his own name or place of origin, a phenomenon found in numerous places throughout *Shas*.

### Tower vs. Scattered

A *braita* provides parallel support: *matza ma'ot mefuzarot, harei elu shelo* — scattered coins are yours — while *asuyin kemigdalin, chayav lehachriz* — if they are arranged like towers, one must announce. The *braita* then defines the tower form: *shelosha matbe'ot zeh al gav zeh* — three coins stacked one atop another. This brings the discussion back to where it started, since the definition offered remains imprecise. Does it mean three coins in a straight vertical stack? A triangle? Some other configuration?

### Tanna's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גּוּפַהּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ מָצָא מָעוֹת מְפוּזָּרוֹת הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, הָא מְשַׁלְחֲפִי שַׁלְחוֹפֵי חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז! אֵימָא סֵיפָא: עֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִין חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז, הָא מְשַׁלְחֲפִי שַׁלְחוֹפֵי הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ! תָּנָא כֹּל שֶׁאֵין עֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִין – מְפוּזָּרוֹת קָרֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes an apparent contradiction in the *baraita*. **This** *baraita* **itself is difficult.** In the first clause of the *baraita*, **you said:** If one **found scattered coins, these** belong **to him,** from which it can be inferred that **if** the coins **partially overlap [*meshalḥefei shalḥufei*],** he is **obligated to proclaim** his find. **Say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: If the coins **are arranged in** well-ordered **towers,** he is **obligated to proclaim** his find, from which it can be inferred that **if** the coins **partially overlap, those** coins **belong to him.** The Gemara answers: The ***tanna*** of the *baraita* **calls any** pile of coins **that is not arranged in** well-ordered **towers: Scattered.**

The *gemara* notes that the *braita*'s formulation creates a potential internal tension. If the rule is *matza ma'ot mefuzarot harei elu shelo* — scattered coins are yours — then coins that are *somewhat* stacked but not perfectly aligned (*mishalchafei*) fall into a gray zone: they are not perfectly scattered, yet not in a tower. The implication might be that they require *chayav lehachriz*. But the *seifa* — that only *kemigdalin* requires announcing — implies the opposite: anything less than a tower counts as scattered and may be kept. The *tanna*'s resolution: *kol she'einan asuyin kemigdalin mefuzarot kari lehu* — anything that does not constitute a proper tower formation is classified as *mefuzarot*. The criterion is binary: either it is a *migdal* or it is considered scattered, with no intermediate category. The logic is that a defined stack of coins signals deliberate placement — the owner intends to return for them — whereas a lopsided or casual arrangement lacks that intentional quality.

### Rabbi Chanina's Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים, אֲבָל שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דַּעֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִין – אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד נָמֵי, וְאִי דְּאֵין עֲשׂוּיִין כְּמִגְדָּלִין – אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁל שְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The Sages **taught** that one must proclaim his find **only** when he finds coins minted **by three** different **kings, but** if all the coins were minted **by one king, one is not obligated to proclaim** his find. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** the coins **are arranged in** well-ordered **towers,** then **even** if all the coins were minted **by one king,** the finder should **also** be obligated to proclaim his find. **And if** the coins **are not arranged in** well-ordered **towers,** then **even** if the **coins** were minted **by three kings,** the finder should **also not** be obligated to proclaim his find.

*Amar Rabbi Chanina: lo shanu ela shelosha melachim* — Rabbi Chanina limits the tower rule to three coins bearing *different* kings, meaning three different denominations or coin types. *Aval shel melech echad eino chayav lehachriz* — if all three bear the same king's image (i.e., are identical coins), one need not announce. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: if the coins are arranged in tower form, why should the identity of the king matter? And if they are *not* in tower form, then even three different kings would not require announcement. What work is Rabbi Chanina's distinction actually doing?

### Chanina Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר, הָכִי אִתְּמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד כְּעֵין שְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים, אֲבָל שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי דַּעֲשׂוּיִים כְּמִגְדָּלִים? רְוִיחָא תַּתָּאָה וּמְצִיעָא עִילָּוֵיהּ וְזוּטָא עִילָּוֵיהּ מְצִיעָא. דְּאָמְרִינַן: אַנּוֹחֵי אַנְחִינְהוּ. אֲבָל שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד, דְּכוּלְּהוּ כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ אַף עַל גַּב דְּמַנְּחִי אַהֲדָדֵי – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, אֵימַר: אִתְרְמוֹיֵי אִתְרְמִי וּבַהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי נְפוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** Rabbi Ḥanina's ruling **was stated, this** is how **it was stated:** The Sages **taught** that one must proclaim his find **only** when he finds coins of different sizes minted **by one king,** which are **similar to** coins minted by **three kings. But** if they are coins of the same size minted **by one king, he is not obligated to proclaim** his find. The Gemara elaborates: According to this interpretation, **what are the circumstances** of coins **that are arranged in** well-ordered **towers** and which one must proclaim? It is when **the bottom** coin is **broadest, and the intermediate-sized** coin is **atop it and the smallest** coin is **atop the intermediate** one, **as we say: They were placed** there and are not lost at all. **But** if one finds coins minted **by one king, each of them sized like the other, even if each is placed upon the other, those coins belong to** the finder. The reason is that it is possible to **say** that **it** is **happenstance and they fell together,** so their arrangement is not a distinguishing mark.

The *gemara* resolves this by reinterpreting Rabbi Chanina's statement: *lo shanu ela shel melech echad ke'ein shelosha melachim* — Rabbi Chanina was discussing coins of one king but arranged *in the style* of three different kings, meaning three coins of different sizes stacked in descending order. *De'asuyin kemigdalin: revicha tata'ah* — the widest coin on the bottom, *umetzaya iluyeh* — the medium-sized coin placed above it, *vezuta ilui metzaya* — and the smallest coin on top of that. This is the operational definition of *kemigdalin* according to Rabbi Chanina: a graded, pyramidal stack — like a ziggurat or stepped pyramid — where each successive coin is smaller than the one below. This configuration, Rabbi Chanina holds, constitutes a meaningful *siman* because the ordered gradation is not accidental; it signals intentional placement. But if all three coins are identical in size and denomination, one might reasonably suppose they simply fell that way — *itramuyei itrami u-be-hadei hadadi nefil*, they happened to tumble and land stacked together — and so the configuration does not rise to the level of a reliable *siman*.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד נָמֵי מַכְרֵיז. מַאי מַכְרֵיז? מִנְיָן. מַאי אִירְיָא תְּלָתָא? אֲפִילּוּ תְּרֵין נָמֵי. אָמַר רָבִינָא: טִבְעָא מַכְרֵיז.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** if the coins were minted **by one king,** one is **also** obligated to **proclaim** his find. The Gemara asks: **What** does one **proclaim** in order to invite the owner to describe his item? The Gemara answers: He proclaims that he found coins and the owner specifies the **number** of coins. The Gemara asks: If so, **why** does the mishna **specifically** teach a case where one found **three** coins when **even** if one found **two** coins they could be identified by their number? **Ravina said:** Since the finder **proclaims** that he found **coins,** using the plural term, indicating that there were at least two coins, if the owner claims that he lost two coins, the default of the plural term, he is not providing a distinguishing mark. Therefore, the mishna teaches a case of three coins.

*Rabbi Yochanan amar: afilu shel melech echad nami machriz* — Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with Rabbi Chanina and holds that even three identical coins in tower formation require announcement. The tower itself, regardless of denomination, is a sufficient *siman*. This prompts a counter-question: *mai irya tlata? afilu terei* — why specifically three? Even two coins stacked atop one another would form a "tower" of sorts.

### Ravina's Quantity Limit

*Amar Ravina: tiba machriz* — Ravina responds that one must be able to make a meaningful announcement. He explains, based on *Rashi*, that the announcement is *matbe'ot matzati*, "I found coins." Two coins are simply too small a quantity to constitute a useful announcement — *demi'ut matbe'ot*, it is a negligible number of coins. A threshold of significance is required before the finder is obligated to announce, and three is that threshold. Two does not suffice.

### Rav Yirmiyah's Shapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּשֵׁיר, מַהוּ? כְּשׁוּרָה, מַהוּ? כַּחֲצוּבָה, מַהוּ? כְּסוּלָּם, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If one found coins configured **like** a round **bracelet, what is** the *halakha*? If they were configured **like** a straight **line, what is** the *halakha*? If they were configured **like a triangle, what is** the *halakha*? If they were configured **like** a **ladder,** one partially upon the other and partially protruding, **what is** the *halakha*?

*Ba'ei Rav Yirmiyah* — Rav Yirmiyah then poses a series of questions about alternative configurations. *Kashir mai* — what if the coins are arranged in a circle, like a caravan looping around? *Keshura mai* — what if they are in a straight horizontal line? *Ke-chatzuva mai* — what if in a triangular arrangement? *Ke-sulam mai* — what if in a ladder-like pattern, apparently a staggered or overlapping arrangement (based on *Rashi*'s gloss, which associates *sulam* with the Old French *escalone*, meaning the rungs of a ladder, understood as an overlapping staircase formation)? For all of these, Rav Yirmiyah asks simply: *mai*? What is the law?

### Rav Nachman Criterion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מֵהָא חֲדָא, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ מַכְנִיס לָהֶ[ן] קֵיסָם בֵּינֵיהֶן וְנוֹטְלָם בְּבַת אַחַת, חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Resolve at least one** of these dilemmas, **as Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** For **any** arrangement of coins such that **if one** were to **introduce a wood chip between** the coins he could thereby **lift them all at once** with that wood chip, he is **obligated to proclaim** his find. Based on that criterion, one can conclude that if one finds coins configured like a ladder, he is obligated to proclaim his find.

*Pashut me-ha de-amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha* — the answer is resolved from a principle taught by Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *kol she-ilu machnis la keisam beineihen ve-notlan be-vat achat, chayav lehachriz* — any configuration in which one could insert a splinter of wood beneath the coins and lift them all together as a single unit requires announcement. The criterion is structural cohesion: does the arrangement hold together as a unified formation that can be transported intact? If so, it bears the mark of intentional placement. The practical implications for each of Rav Yirmiyah's shapes — circle, line, triangle, ladder — would depend on whether those configurations satisfy this test.

### Rav Ashi Imagery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּאַבְנֵי בֵּית קוּלִיס, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** If they were configured **like the stones of the house** of worship dedicated to the Roman deity **Mercury, what is** the *halakha*?

*Rav Ashi* describes the relevant configuration as *ke-avnei beis Kulus* — like the stones of the house of Kulus. *Rashi* identifies Kulus with *Markulis*, the Roman god Mercurius, whose worship involved throwing stones at a statue. The arrangement of those stones — with one stone on each side and one placed on top (*achas mikan ve-achas mikan ve-achas al gabeihen*) — forms a distinctive configuration. *Tosafot* raises the question of how the name *Markulis* is permitted to be invoked in *halachic* discussion, given the prohibition of *lo yizacher al picha*, which forbids mentioning the names of *avodah zarah*. *Tosafot* suggests that the true name of the idol is *Kulus*, and *Markulis* is a different designation, though this leaves a *kashya* on the *sugya* since the *gemara* itself uses the *beis Kulus* formulation.

### Beis Kulus Configuration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: מָצָא מָעוֹת מְפוּזָּרוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, כְּאַבְנֵי בֵּית קוּלִיס – חַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיז. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן אַבְנֵי בֵּית קוּלִיס: אַחַת מִכָּאן וְאַחַת מִכָּאן וְאַחַת עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of the dilemma. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **found scattered coins, these belong to him.** If they were configured **like the stones of the house** of worship dedicated to **Mercury,** he is **obligated to proclaim** his find. The Gemara explains: **And these are** coins that were configured **like the stones of the house** of worship dedicated to **Mercury: One** was situated **here** on one side, **and one** was situated **there** alongside it, **and one** was situated **atop** the two of **them.**

A *braita* confirms this as a distinct recognized configuration: *matza ma'ot mefuzarot harei elu shelo; ke-avnei beis Kulus chayav lehachriz* — scattered coins are yours; coins arranged like the stones of *beis Kulus* require announcement. And the *braita* defines the form explicitly: *achas mikan ve-achas mikan ve-achas al gabeihen* — one coin here, one coin here, and one coin placed on top spanning them both. This is a third distinct *migdal*-type configuration, alongside the vertical stack and the graded pyramid.

## Coins Fungibility

### Coin Claims Not Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹצֵא סֶלַע בְּשׁוּק, וּמְצָאוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר: שֶׁלִּי הִיא, חֲדָשָׁה הִיא, נִירוֹנִית הִיא, שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ פְּלוֹנִי הִיא – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שְׁמוֹ כָּתוּב עָלֶיהָ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. לְפִי שֶׁאֵין סִימָן לְמַטְבֵּעַ. דְּאָמַר: דִּלְמָא אַפּוֹקֵי אַפְּקַהּ וּמֵאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא נְפַל.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who finds a *sela*** coin **in the marketplace and another** person **finds him and says: It is mine,** and the distinguishing mark is that **it is new,** or that it **is** a coin minted **by** the emperor **Nero,** or that it is minted **by king so-and-so, he has not said anything** and the finder need not give him the *sela*. **Moreover, even** if **his name is written on** the *sela* **he has not said anything, due to** the fact **that there is no distinguishing mark for a coin** that is effective in its recovery, **as** the finder **says: Perhaps he spent** the coin **and it fell from another person.**

A *braita* addresses what kinds of claims are *not* valid *simanim* for coins. If one finds a coin in the marketplace and someone comes claiming it is his, citing the following identifiers: *chadasha hi* — it is brand new; *nironis hi* — it bears Nero's face; *shel melech ploni hi* — it belongs to a particular king — *ve-lo amar klum*, none of these constitute a valid claim. The *braita* thus rules that these descriptions, however specific they may seem, are legally insufficient as *simanim* for coins.

### Fungibility Reason

The reason is stated plainly: *lefi she'ein siman la-matbe'ah* — there is simply no such thing as a valid *siman* for a coin. Unlike a *kli* — a vessel — which each person tends to hold onto over time and with which they develop familiarity, coins change hands constantly. Describing a coin as new, or as bearing a particular ruler's image, identifies a category that encompasses potentially thousands or millions of identical coins. The *siman* offered is no *siman* at all, because it fails to distinguish this coin from any other of its type.

### Spent Coin Presumption

The *gemara* adds a further structural argument: *de-amar dilma afokei afkeih* — one can always say that the claimant already spent the coin, *u-me'enish achrina nafal* — and it fell from a different person entirely. The chain of custody for a coin is by its nature untraceable. The person claiming the coin may well have once possessed a coin matching that description, but coins circulate — they pass from hand to wallet to merchant to bank to the next person in a roll of change. No claim based on a coin's appearance can overcome this presumption of circulation. This is fundamentally different from a *kli*, where one presumes that if a person can correctly identify the *simanim* of a vessel, they have not sold or otherwise transferred it in the interim, and their familiarity with it constitutes a genuine identifying bond.

## Birds & Trash Piles

### Tied Birds Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצָא אַחַר הַגָּפָה אוֹ אַחַר הַגָּדֵר גּוֹזָלוֹת מְקוּשָּׁרִים, אוֹ בִּשְׁבִילִין שֶׁבַּשָּׂדוֹת – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִגַּע בָּהֶן. מָצָא כְּלִי בָּאַשְׁפָּה, אִם מְכוּסֶּה – לֹא יִגַּע בּוֹ, אִם מְגוּלֶּה – נוֹטֵל וּמַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **found, behind** a wooden **fence or behind** a stone **fence, bound fledglings, or** if he found them **in the paths that** run **through fields, he may not touch them,** as they were certainly placed there intentionally. In a case where **one found a vessel in a garbage dump, if** it is **concealed, he may not touch it,** as a person certainly concealed it there. **If** it is **exposed,** the finder **takes** the item **and proclaims** his find.

The *mishna* now introduces a third category of ruling — distinct from both *harei elu shelo* and *harei elu chayav lehachriz* — with the instruction *lo yigah bahen*, do not touch them. *Matza achar hagafah o achar hageder, gozalot mekusharim* — if one found small birds tied together behind a fence or behind a wall, *o bishvilin shebasadeh* — or in the paths running through a field — *harei zeh lo yigah bahen*. This is a striking ruling. The Torah obligates one not to ignore a lost object — *lo tuchal lehitalem* — yet here the *mishna* instructs the finder to do exactly that: leave them alone.

### Location Siman Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמְרִינַן: הָנֵי, אִינָשׁ אַצְנְעִינְהוּ, וְאִי שָׁקֵיל לְהוּ – לֵית לְהוּ לְמָרַיְיהוּ סִימָנָא בְּגַוַּויְיהוּ, הִלְכָּךְ לִשְׁבְּקִינְהוּ עַד דְּאָתֵי מָרַיְיהוּ וְשָׁקֵיל לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the reason** that one may not touch the fledglings? The Gemara answers: The reason is **that we say** with regard to **these** birds: **A person concealed them, and if one takes them, their owner has no distinguishing mark on them** that would enable him to reclaim them. **Therefore, let** the finder **leave** the birds in place **until their owner comes and takes them.**

The *gemara* explains: *mai ta'ama de-amrinan hanei inish atzninehu* — the presumption is that a person went and hid them there. These birds were placed behind the fence or wall deliberately, as a form of storage or concealment, and the location itself is the *siman*. *Ve-ee shakil lehu, leit lehu lemareihu simana begaveihu* — if you take them, you destroy the only *siman* the owner has. Since the birds themselves have no intrinsic *siman* — one tied pigeon looks like any other tied pigeon — the *makom*, the location, is the owner's sole means of identifying and reclaiming them. Removing them from that location eliminates the *siman* and makes return impossible. Therefore, *hilchach leshavkinehu* — leave them in place until the owner comes.

### Knot Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי לֶיהֱוֵי קֶשֶׁר סִימָנָא! אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: בִּמְקוּשָּׁרִין בְּכַנְפֵיהֶן, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הָכִי מְקַטְּרִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why? Let** the **knot** binding them serve as their **distinguishing mark. Rabbi Abba bar Zavda said** that **Rav said:** This is a case **where** the birds were **bound at their wings. Since everyone binds them** in **that** manner, the knot binding the birds is not a distinguishing mark.

A *gemara* challenge: *ve-amai lehavei kesher simana* — why isn't the knot in the rope binding the birds together a *siman* in itself? Each person might tie their birds differently, and the particular knot could serve as an identifying mark.

### Rav's Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֶהֱוֵי מָקוֹם סִימָן! אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: בְּמִדַּדִּין. אִי בְּמִדַּדִּין – מֵעָלְמָא אָתוּ וּמוּתָּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** their **location** serve as their **distinguishing mark. Rav Ukva bar Ḥama said:** This is a case **where** the birds **hop** and do not remain in place. The Gemara asks: **If** it is a case **where** the birds **hop,** perhaps the birds **came** to that location **from elsewhere and** it **is permitted** for the finder to keep them.

Two answers are offered. *Amar Rabbi Abba bar Zavda amar Rav: bimkusharim bichenfeihem* — the birds are tied by their wings, *dekulei alma hachi mitkasher lehu* — and everyone ties birds by the wings in essentially the same standard way. There is therefore no distinctive knot that could function as a *siman*. The second difficulty then arises: *velehavei makom siman* — very well, but then let the location itself be a *siman* and require the finder to announce. *Amar Rav Ukva bar Chama: bimdadim* — the birds are still able to move and wander slightly, meaning their precise location is not fixed. And if they can wander, one might say they arrived there on their own rather than having been placed there — *ika lemeimar me'alma atu*. At the same time, one cannot rule out that someone placed them there — *ve-ika lemeimar inish atzninehu*. This irreducible uncertainty is *safek hinuach* — doubt as to whether the object was intentionally placed — and *safek makom siman*, uncertainty whether location constitutes a *siman* in this instance.

### Safek Hinuach Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מֵעָלְמָא אֲתוֹ, וְאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר אִינָשׁ אַצְנְעִינְהוּ, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ סְפֵק הִינּוּחַ, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: כֹּל סְפֵק הִינּוּחַ – לְכַתְּחִילָּה לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם נָטַל לֹא יַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It can be said that** the birds **came from elsewhere and it can be said** that **a person concealed them, and** the result **is uncertainty** with regard to whether the **placement** of the birds was deliberate, i.e., whether or not they are lost items. **And Rabbi Abba bar Zavda says** that **Rav says:** In **any** case of **uncertainty** as to whether the **placement** of an item was deliberate, one **may not take** it ***ab initio*. And if he took** it, he need **not return** it.

*Ve-amar Rav Abba bar Zavda amar Rav: kol safek hinuach le-chat'chila lo yitol, ve-im natal lo yachzir* — for any case of *safek hinuach*, one should not take the object in the first instance; but if one has already taken it, one does not return it. The latter clause requires explanation. *Rashi* reads *lo yachzir* to mean that the owner cannot compel the finder to return the object, because the owner has no *siman* by which to make a claim — having removed the object, the finder has inadvertently eliminated the owner's sole means of identification, and the owner is now in no better position than any other claimant. The *Rambam* appears to agree with this reading. The *Ra'avad* and *Tosafot*, however, both argue that *lo yachzir* means nearly the opposite: the finder does not get to keep the object for himself, but neither can he give it back to any particular claimant. He is instead obligated to hold it as a *shomer* indefinitely — he has the *mitzvah* to return it but lacks the ability to do so, since no owner can produce a valid *siman* to claim it.

### Vessel in Trash Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא כְּלִי בָּאַשְׁפָּה, מְכוּסֶּה – לֹא יִגַּע בּוֹ, מְגוּלֶּה – נוֹטֵל וּמַכְרִיז. וּרְמִינְהוּ: מָצָא כְּלִי טָמוּן בָּאַשְׁפָּה – נוֹטֵל וּמַכְרִיז, שֶׁכֵּן דֶּרֶךְ אַשְׁפָּה לִפַּנּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In a case where **one found a vessel in a garbage dump, if** it is **concealed, he may not touch it,** as a person certainly concealed it there. **If** it is **exposed,** the finder **takes** the item **and proclaims** his find. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: If one **found a vessel concealed in a garbage dump,** the finder **takes** the item **and proclaims** his find, **because** it is **routine** for **a garbage dump to be cleared.** Therefore, presumably it was not placed there; rather, it is a lost item and one is obligated to proclaim his find.

The *mishna* continues with an analogous case involving vessels in a trash pile. *Matza kli be-ashpah: im mechuseh, lo yigah bo* — if you found a vessel in a trash pile and it was covered, do not touch it. *Im megulah, notel u-machriz* — if it was uncovered, take it and announce. The parallel to the birds case is clear: a covered vessel in a trash pile was presumably placed there deliberately as a hiding spot, so the location (and the act of concealment) is the *siman*; removing it destroys that *siman*. A vessel that is partially exposed, by contrast, appears to have been discarded rather than hidden, and is treated as a normal found object requiring announcement.

### Trash Braita Contradiction

A *braita* is cited as a challenge: *matza kli tamun be-ashpah, notel u-machriz* — if you found a vessel hidden in a trash pile, you take it and announce it — *shaken derech ashpah lifnot* — because the trash pile is regularly cleaned out. This appears to contradict the *mishna*, which said a covered vessel in the trash should not be touched.

### Zevid Vessel Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּכוּבֵּי וְכָסֵי, הָא בְּסַכִּינֵי וְהֶמְנֵיק. בְּכוּבֵּי וְכָסֵי – לֹא יִגַּע, בְּסַכִּינֵי וְהֶמְנֵיק – נוֹטֵל וּמַכְרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said** that this is **not difficult: This** mishna is referring **to containers or cups. That** *baraita* is referring **to knives or a fork [*vehamnik*].** The Gemara elaborates: **In** the case of **containers or cups,** which are large, it is inconceivable that they fell there inadvertently, so **he may not touch** them. **In** the case of **knives or forks,** which are small, there is room for uncertainty as to whether it was placed there or whether it fell, so the finder **takes** the item **and proclaims** his find.

*Amar Rav Zevid: lo kashya* — Rav Zevid resolves the apparent contradiction through a distinction based on the type of vessel. *Ha bekuba u-khese* — one ruling addresses large containers such as barrels and platters; *ha be-sakinei ve-himnak* — the other addresses small objects such as knives and forks. Large vessels, even if hidden in a trash pile, are unlikely to be accidentally discarded; their size makes deliberate concealment the far more plausible explanation. Small objects, however, might easily have been accidentally mixed into the trash and are presumed lost rather than hidden. Therefore, for small objects, *chayav lehachriz*; for large ones, *lo yigah bo*, since the act of hiding is presumed deliberate and the location is the *siman*.

### Trash Cleaning Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא בְּכוּבֵּי וְכָסֵי, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּאַשְׁפָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה לִפַּנּוֹת, כָּאן בְּאַשְׁפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לִפַּנּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** mishna are referring **to containers and cups, and** nevertheless, it is **not difficult: Here,** the *baraita* is referring **to a garbage dump that is designed to be cleared;** therefore, he must take the vessel and proclaim his find to prevent it from being cleared with the garbage. **There,** the mishna is referring **to a garbage dump that is not designed to be cleared;** as it is possible that the owner placed it there, the finder may not touch it.

An alternative resolution (*ve-ibeit eima*, here attributed to the *mar bar Rav Pappa* position in the *gemara*): the distinction in both rulings — for large and small vessels alike — depends on the nature of the trash pile. *Kan be-ashpah ha-asuya lifnot* — one case involves a trash pile that is regularly cleared out; *kan be-ashpah she-eina asuya lifnot* — the other involves a pile that is not regularly cleared. If the pile is regularly emptied, hiding anything there makes no practical sense as a long-term storage strategy. The further scenario involves a trash pile not normally cleared that the owner of the courtyard then decided to empty — *ve-nimlach aleha lifnotah* — which accounts for why objects might be found even within a heap that had been used as a hiding place.

### Yeush Mida'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁפָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה לִפַּנּוֹת אֲבֵידָה מִדַּעַת הִיא! אֶלָּא בְּאַשְׁפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לִפַּנּוֹת, וְנִמְלַךְ עָלֶיהָ לְפַנּוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How could one be obligated to proclaim his find of a vessel in a **garbage dump that is designed to be cleared?** Even if the owner of the vessel concealed it there, it is **a deliberate loss** and the owner renounced ownership of the vessel. The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the *baraita* is referring to **a garbage dump that is not designed to be cleared, and** the owner of the land **reconsidered** and decided **to clear it.**

The *gemara* introduces the category of *aveida mida'at* in this context. If a person hides something in a trash pile that is regularly cleaned out, that itself constitutes a form of *yeush mida'at* — knowing abandonment. The owner effectively forfeited his object by placing it somewhere it was certain to be discarded. In such a case, the finder may take it outright, without any obligation to announce. The cases requiring *chayav lehachriz* or *lo yigah bahen* are precisely those where the trash pile is *not* regularly cleared, so that deliberate concealment there was reasonable, and the owner retains a genuine expectation of recovery. What neither *amora* ultimately invokes is the simple criterion the *mishna* seemed to state — whether the vessel was covered or uncovered by trash — suggesting that both Rav Zevid and the alternative resolution effectively replace that criterion with their own, more analytically precise distinctions.

## Objects in Walls

### Wall Ownership Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצָא בְּגַל וּבְכוֹתֶל יָשָׁן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. מָצָא בְּכוֹתֶל חָדָשׁ, מֵחֶצְיוֹ וְלַחוּץ – שֶׁלּוֹ, מֵחֶצְיוֹ וְלִפְנִים – שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. אִם הָיָה מַשְׂכִּירוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – אֲפִילּוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **found** lost items **in a heap** of stone rubble **or in an old wall, these** belong **to him.** If **one found** lost items **in a new wall from its midpoint and outward,** they belong **to him.** If he found the items **from its midpoint and inward,** they belong **to the homeowner. If** the homeowner **would rent** the house **to others** on a regular basis and there was a steady turnover of residents, **even** if one found lost items **inside the house, these** belong **to him.** Since the owner of the lost items cannot be identified based on location, he will certainly despair of recovering his lost items.

The *mishna* turns to objects found within structures. *Matza be-gal* — if one found something in a pile of rocks — *o be-kotel yashan* — or in an old, crumbling wall — *harei elu shelo*, one may keep it. The reasoning is that such finds are of indeterminate origin; the wall or rockpile is ancient enough that any connection to a current owner has long been severed. By contrast, *matza be-kotel chadash* — if one found something in a new wall — the calculus changes, because new walls were commonly built with deliberate *hidey-holes* (as we know from the *mishna* in *Pesachim* and related sources) into which homeowners would place valuables for safekeeping.

### Midpoint Division

In the case of a new wall, the *mishna* applies a midpoint rule. *Mechetzo lachutz, shelo* — from the midpoint of the wall outward, the object belongs to the finder, because a homeowner would not store valuables in the outer half of his wall. *Mechetzo u-lefanim, shel ba'al habayit* — from the midpoint inward, the object belongs to the householder, because the inner wall is where one would naturally create and use a hiding place.

### Rental Exception

*Im haya maskiro le-acheirim, afilu be-toch habayit, harei elu shelo* — if the property was rented to others, even objects found entirely inside the house belong to the finder. The reason is that in a rental property, the identity of prior occupants is unknown, and any tenant who left something behind has presumably abandoned it, without the ability to return and stake a claim. No current occupant can be presumed to be the owner, unlike in the case of an owner-occupied dwelling where the *ba'al habayit* is the obvious presumptive owner of any hidden item.

### Canaanite Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לוֹ לוֹמַר לוֹ שֶׁל אֲמוֹרִיִּים הֵן. אַטּוּ אֱמוֹרִים מַצְנְעִי, יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא מַצְנְעִי? לָא צְרִיכָא דִּשְׁתִיךְ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one found a lost item in a heap of rubble or in an old wall it belongs to him. The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: It is his **due to** the fact **that** when the owner of the heap or wall claims the property, the finder **can say to him: They** belong **to the Amorites,** who lived in Eretz Yisrael before it was conquered by the Jews. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say** that **Amorites conceal** items but **Jews do not conceal** items? Perhaps it was the homeowner who placed the item in the wall or the heap. The Gemara answers: **No,** the *baraita* **is necessary** only in the specific case **where** the item is **extremely rusted,** indicating that it had been left there for a long time.

The *gemara* asks: when we say a find in an old wall or rockpile may be kept because we assume it belongs to the *Emori'im* — the ancient Canaanite inhabitants — is that assumption reasonable? *Atu Emori'im matnyei, Yisrael lo matnyei* — do only Canaanites hide things in walls, and not Israelites? Surely Israelites also hide valuables; why then do we attribute the find to Canaanites and permit it?

### Deep Burial Requirement

*Lo tzericha: de-shaich tfei* — the case must involve a find that is deeply embedded or particularly well concealed, or *halud haluda*, thoroughly encrusted or sealed. Alternatively, it must have been placed inside some container within the wall. The point is that ordinary storage of items in a wall would not result in the kind of burial or containment that characterizes these finds. When an object is found truly embedded within an old wall — sealed inside, corroded into the structure, or held within an ancient container — one can no longer realistically attribute it to any Israelite who might have stored it there in living memory. Only then does the presumption of ancient Canaanite origin become reasonable, and the finder may keep it.
