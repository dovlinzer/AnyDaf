# Menachot 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kometz Sunset

### Kometz Before Sunset

The sugya opens at the bottom of 26b, where the *gemara* resolves a tension regarding the validity of the *mincha* offering. The question is whether the *kometz* must be substantially consumed by fire — that is, whether the fire must seize the majority of it — in order for the *mincha* to be considered valid. How, then, can we infer from an earlier *beraita* that it suffices to place the *kometz* on the fire immediately before sunset, allowing it to continue burning into the night?

The *gemara*'s answer is that this arrangement is entirely valid: the *mincha* does not achieve its valid status until the fire has seized the majority of the *kometz*, yet so long as the human act of the *avodah* — the placement on the fire — was performed during the day, the burning may continue and complete itself after nightfall. The point at which the fire seizes the majority, even if that moment occurs at night, is when the *mincha* becomes valid.

### Action & Process

This resolution reflects a broader principle found elsewhere in *halacha*: where a human act must be performed at a specific time, the subsequent process of completion — which unfolds on its own — may extend beyond that window. A parallel example is placing an item on a flame before *Shabbat* begins: since the act of placement was performed before *Shabbat*, the cooking that continues over *Shabbat* is attributed to that initial act. The *gemara* understands the *kometz* similarly: the act of the *avodah* defines the legal moment; the process of combustion carries forward from there.

### Rabbi Elazar Pikuin

Rabbi Elazar offers an alternative reading of the *beraita*. He does not require us to invoke the principle of the ongoing process at all. Instead, he reads the *beraita* as referring to *pikuin* — items that popped off the *mizbe'ach*. In such a case, the *kometz* had originally been placed on the altar during the day; it subsequently fell off; and it may then be restored even after nightfall. According to this reading, we are not permitting a nighttime *avodah* in any primary sense, but merely allowing restoration of something that was properly placed during the day and accidentally dislodged.

This principle is corroborated by Rav Dimi, who reported in the name of Rabbi Yanai: the *beraita* indeed concerns *pikuin*.

### Inner vs Outer Altar

A question immediately arises, however: does Rabbi Yanai himself actually hold that items which fell off the *mizbe'ach* may be restored after nightfall? For Rabbi Yanai himself taught that *ketoret* which flew off the *mizbe'ach* — even the tiniest fragment — may not be restored. This appears to contradict the principle of *pikuin* entirely.

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between the inner and outer *mizbe'ach*. Rabbi Chanina bar Minyumi teaches in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: the source for restoring fallen items is the verse *asher tochal ha'esh et ha'olah al hamizbe'ach* — the fire consumes the *olah* on the altar. The verse specifies things burned like the *olah*, and on the *mizbe'ach* in question: that is, on the outer *mizbe'ach*. From this we derive that items which fall off the outer *mizbe'ach* may be restored — but items belonging to the inner *mizbe'ach*, such as the *ketoret* burned on the golden altar, are not subject to this principle at all. The *gemara* therefore concludes: remove *ketoret* from this discussion. The *kometz* and the *levonah* — which belong to the outer *mizbe'ach* — may be restored after nightfall if they fell off. The *ketoret* of the inner *mizbe'ach* may not.

The logic here is precise: we are speaking of items that must *lechatchila* be placed during the day — not items like the limbs and fats of the *olah*, which may be placed *lechatchila* at night as well. If such daytime-required items were placed properly and then fell off, the question is whether they may be restored at night. The answer is yes for the outer *mizbe'ach*, no for the inner.

## Kometz Burning Method

### Kometz Under Wood

Having resolved the temporal questions surrounding the *kometz*, the *gemara* turns to a different set of concerns: the precise manner in which the *kometz* must be burned on the *mizbe'ach*. These are distinct from the earlier questions and involve the physical arrangement of the offering relative to the firewood.

Rav Assi reports that when Rabbi Elazar was studying Menachot, he posed the following question: if one arranged the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* and then placed the firewood on top of it — rather than placing the *kometz* on the wood — what is the *halacha*? Is this considered *derech haktara*, the normal manner of burning an offering? Or is it not *derech haktara*, and therefore invalid?

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

One might understand this question as bearing on the formal act of placing the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* — whether there is a required manner of deposition, as there is with blood. Alternatively, the concern may be more functional: when the *kometz* is beneath the wood rather than atop it, the smoke may not rise as effectively. The question thus may implicate not only the legal act but the physical result of the burning.

### Olah Under Wood

Chizkiya posed a closely parallel question regarding the limbs of an *olah*: if one arranged the limbs on the *mizbe'ach* and then placed the firewood on top of them, what is the *halacha*? Here the question carries an additional dimension, because the Torah explicitly states *al ha'etzim* — on the wood — implying that the limbs must rest upon the firewood. Does *al ha'etzim* mean specifically that, and only that arrangement? Or does the verse *asher tochal ha'esh et ha'olah al hamizbe'ach* — that the fire consumes the *olah* on the altar — suggest that any configuration which results in burning on the altar suffices, and either arrangement is acceptable?

The *gemara* again answers: *teiku*.

What is significant here is the nature of the comparison. The *kometz*, in this discussion, is being treated not as something analogous to blood — which requires a formal act of application — but as something analogous to the fats and limbs of an animal, which are simply placed on the *mizbe'ach* to burn. Unlike those items, the *kometz* is a *matir*, the component that renders the *korban* valid. Yet the *gemara*'s framing suggests that the legal concern is less about a formal act of placement and more about the factual result: is it burning in a legitimate configuration?

### Limbs Beside Wood

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha posed a third question: what if one arranged the limbs of an *olah* not on top of the firewood, but alongside it — adjacent to the *ma'aracha* rather than upon it? The fire will still reach the limbs; they will still burn. So what is the *halacha*?

The *gemara* frames this question around a linguistic debate about the word *al*. If *al* means literally "on," then placing the limbs beside the wood rather than on it clearly fails the requirement of *al ha'etzim*. But if *al* can sometimes mean "near" — as in *al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafona*, which means near the side of the *mizbe'ach* rather than literally on its flank — then perhaps *al ha'etzim* could be satisfied even when the limbs are merely adjacent. Against this, one could argue that the phrase *al ha'etzim* parallels *al hamizbe'ach*, and just as the latter means literally on the altar and not merely near it, so too the former must mean literally on the wood.

The *gemara* again concludes: *teiku*.

The cumulative effect of these three unresolved questions reinforces the earlier observation: the *gemara* is treating the burning of the *kometz* as more akin to the burning of animal parts than to the act of applying blood. The concern is less the formal legal act and more the physical manner and arrangement of combustion.

## Me'akev Components

### Individual Requirements

With the beginning of daf 27a, the *gemara* enters a major new *mishna*, one that will generate extended discussion spanning numerous topics across all of *halacha*. The *mishna* introduces the principle of components that are *me'akev* — that is, that hold one another back, such that if one is missing the entire offering is invalid.

The *mishna* opens with individual quantitative requirements: even the smallest portion of the *kometz*, if lacking, invalidates the rest — *mi'uto me'akev et rubo*, the minority holds back the majority. Similarly, the *isaron* of flour that constitutes the *mincha* must be complete; even the smallest deficiency invalidates the *isaron* as a whole. The same applies to wine and to oil: whatever amount the Torah prescribes for a given *korban*, the full amount is required, and any shortfall renders the remainder invalid.

### Flour & Oil

The *mishna* then moves beyond individual measurements to address the interdependence of distinct components. This is a more significant step: it is no longer merely a question of how much of a single ingredient is required, but of whether entirely different components of a *korban* are bound up with one another.

*Hasolet v'hashemen me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the flour and the oil of a *mincha* are mutually interdependent. A *mincha* without the proper amount of flour is not valid, but equally, a *mincha* without the proper amount of oil is not valid. The relationship is not one-directional; each requires the presence of the other.

### Kometz & Levona

Similarly, the *kometz* and the *levona* — the handful of the *mincha* and the frankincense placed upon it — are mutually interdependent. Both must be present for the *mincha* to be valid. The absence of either one prevents the other from functioning.

### Textual Sources

The *gemara* then methodically traces the textual basis for each of these requirements.

For the *kometz*: the Torah uses the phrase *melo kumzo* — the fill of his handful — twice, which the *gemara* reads as emphatic: the full *kometz* is required, and any deficiency invalidates.

For the *isaron* of flour: the verse uses the possessive form *misolta* — its *solet* — conveying that it must be exactly its proper amount. Any deficiency renders it invalid.

For the wine accompanying offerings: the verse states *kacha ye'aseh lashor ha'echad o la'ayil ha'echad* — "so shall you do for each ox or each ram" — using the word *kacha*, meaning "thusly" or "exactly so." This formulation implies that any deviation from the prescribed amount invalidates.

For the oil: by *minchat nesachim*, the verse likewise uses *kacha*; by the free-will *mincha*, the verse says *u'mishamnah* — "from its oil" — the possessive again conveying that the exact amount is required.

Here a general principle of *korbanot* deserves notice. Unlike most areas of *halacha*, where a single statement in the Torah is presumed to carry the full weight of a requirement, by *korbanot* the principle is *shana alav hakatuv le'akev*: the Torah must repeat or reinforce a detail in order for us to conclude that its absence invalidates. This seems counterintuitive — one might think that the dense and elaborate ritual of the *avodah* would be precisely the domain where every detail is indispensable. Yet *Chazal* reasoned in the opposite direction: the sheer profusion of details in the sacrificial laws makes it implausible that every one is strictly *me'akev*. A repetition or emphasis in the verse is therefore needed to establish that a particular requirement is truly indispensable.

For the interdependence of flour and oil: the Torah says *misolta u'mishamnah* — "from its flour and from its oil" — linking them together. A second verse then repeats *migrasah u'mishamnah* — "from its meal and from its oil" — and the repetition establishes the interdependence.

For the *kometz* and *levona*: one verse says *al kol levonatah* — "upon all of its frankincense" — and another says *ve'et kol halevona* — "and all of the frankincense" — specifically in the context of offering it *asher al hamincha*. The repetition establishes that neither can be offered validly without the other.

## Multi-Component Systems

### Yom Kippur Goats

The *mishna* now proceeds through an extended catalog of cases organized on a numerical principle, moving from two-component systems to three, four, and seven.

*Shnei se'irei Yom Hakippurim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two goats of Yom Kippur hold one another back. One goat is sent to *Azazel*; the other is slaughtered and its blood is sprinkled inside the *heichal*. Their processes are quite distinct from one another, yet both must be present and both must be valid for either to be meaningful. This is worth pausing over: unlike the *kometz* and *levona*, which are offered together in a unified act, the two Yom Kippur goats are processed very differently. And yet the *mishna* teaches that they are interdependent — if something happens to one before the other is completed, neither may proceed. The extensive treatment of this problem in the *mishnayot* of Yoma reflects just how live and consequential this interdependence is.

The textual basis: the verse at the conclusion of the Yom Kippur *avodah* in *Acharei Mot* states *v'hayta zot lachem l'chukat olam* — this shall be a perpetual statute for you. The word *chukkah* is understood to require that the *avodah* be performed exactly as specified; and that *chukkah* encompasses the interdependence of the components within it.

### Shavuot Lambs Bread

*Shnei kivsei Atzeret me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two lambs of Shavuot hold one another back. The textual basis is *solet tihyena* — "fine flour they shall be" — and the word *tihyena* conveys "it shall be exactly thus." Though this verse speaks directly of the *shtei halechem*, the *gemara* applies the force of *havayah* — the word "shall be" — to the lambs as well.

*Shtei chalot me'akvot zu et zu* — the two loaves of the *shtei halechem* hold one another back. Likewise from *havayah*.

*Shnei sedarim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two columns of *lechem hapanim* hold one another back. The verse states *me'ishei Hashem chok olam* — an everlasting statute of God's fire-offerings — and the word *chok* requires exactness.

*Shnei bezichin me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two cups of frankincense that accompany the *shulchan* hold one another back. The same verse containing *chok* applies.

*Hasedarim vehabezichin me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the columns of bread and the cups of frankincense are mutually interdependent as well. You cannot burn the frankincense without the bread being present, and you cannot eat the bread without having burned the frankincense.

It is worth noting that while the word *chukkah* appears repeatedly and carries the work of establishing these interdependencies, it does not by itself define the scope of what is grouped together. For the *shulchan* and its components, the *gemara* identifies everything as required. For Yom Kippur, we group certain elements together but do not say, for instance, that the inability to bring the *par* of the *kohen gadol* prevents the *se'ir* from being offered. The application of *chok* to establish *me'akev* relationships therefore always involves a measure of conceptual grouping that the *gemara* takes as understood.

### Nazir Parah Todah

*Shnei minim shebanazir me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two types of bread that accompany the *shlamim* of a *nazir* hold one another back. The textual basis is *ken ya'aseh al torat nizro* — "so shall be done according to his *nazirite* law" — the word *ken*, meaning "thus," conveying exactness.

*Gimel shebeparah me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the three components of the *parah adumah* purification, the *agudat ezov*, the *eitz erez*, and the *shni tola'at*, all hold one another back. The textual basis is *zot chukat hatorah* — this is the statute of the Torah — where both *zot* (this, exactly this) and *chukkah* reinforce the requirement.

*Arba shebetodah me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the four types of bread of the *todah* offering hold one another back. All forty loaves — ten of each type — must be present; if any type is missing, none of the loaves is valid, and none may be eaten. The textual basis is a linkage: the verse by the *todah* says *al zevach todah shlamav* — upon the slaughtering of his *todah* of his *shelamim* — and there is a teaching that the word *shlamav* serves to link the *shalmei nazir* to the *todah*. Since the interdependence of the bread types of the *nazir* is established by *ken ya'aseh*, and the *todah* is linked to the *nazir*, the same rule applies to the *todah*.

### Lulav Metzora

*Arba shebalulav me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the four species of the *lulav* hold one another back. The textual basis is the drasha of *lekicha tama*: the verse says *u'lekachtem*, and the *gemara* reads this as *lekicha* that is *tama* — a whole, complete taking — meaning all components must be present.

*Arba shebemetzora me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the four components of the *metzora* purification hold one another back. These are the *agudat ezov*, the *shni tola'at*, the *eitz erez*, and the birds — whose blood is used for the sprinkling. The textual basis is *zot tihyeh torat hametzora* — this shall be the law of the *metzora* — where *tihyeh* conveys that it must be exactly this way.

The organizational principle of this section of the *mishna* is explicitly numerical: it proceeds through two, three, four, and will continue to seven. This explains what might otherwise appear to be arbitrary jumps between topics — from bread offerings to the *parah adumah* to the *lulav* — as the *mishna* is simply following the count.

### Seven Sprinklings

*Sheva hazayot shebeparah me'akvot zu et zu* — the seven sprinklings of the *parah adumah* toward the *beit hamikdash* hold one another back. If any one of the seven is missing, the purification process is invalid. The textual basis is *chukkah*.

*Sheva hazayot shebein habadim ve'al parochet ve'al mizbe'ach hazahav me'akvot zu et zu* — the seven sprinklings of the inner *chata'ot*, whether performed between the staves of the *aron* on Yom Kippur, or upon the *parochet*, or upon the golden altar, all hold one another back. This applies to Yom Kippur, to the *par kohen hamashiach*, to the *par he'elam davar*, and to the goats brought for communal *avodah zarah*, all of which are inner *chata'ot*.

The textual basis for the inner *chata'ot*: the verse by *par he'elam davar* states *v'asa lapar ka'asher asa lapar harishon* — he shall do for the *par* as he did for the first *par* — meaning the procedure of the *kohen gadol*'s *par* is the template. But why does the Torah need to state this? It is to double and reinforce the *haza'ot*, teaching that every individual sprinkling is required, such that if even one is missing, nothing was accomplished: *she'im chisar achat m'kol hamatanot lo asa klum*.

## Lulav Unity Lesson

### Fruit Bearers

Having enumerated the four species in the context of *me'akev*, the *gemara* now elaborates on the *lulav* specifically, introducing a *beraita* that contains both a legal and a moral dimension.

Rav Chana bar Rava teaches: the rule that the four species are mutually interdependent (*me'akvin zeh et zeh*) applies only when one does not possess all four. If one has all four species available, *ein me'akvin* — they do not hold one another back. What this means will be developed shortly, but the basic point is that the *me'akev* relationship is triggered by actual absence, not by the choice to take them separately when one owns all of them.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a *beraita*: the four species of the *lulav* — two of which bear fruit (the *lulav*, which yields dates, and the *etrog*) and two of which do not (the *hadas* and *arava*) — are mutually dependent in both directions. The fruit-bearers need the non-fruit-bearers, and the non-fruit-bearers need the fruit-bearers. And the *beraita* states: *ein adam yotzei yedei chovato bahen ad she'kulam b'agudah achat* — a person does not fulfill his obligation unless all of them are in a single bundle.

### Divine Bundle

The *beraita* then draws its moral: *v'chen Yisrael b'hartza'ah* — so too with Israel when they come seeking divine favor. They must all be gathered together as one. The proof-text is *haboneh bashamayim ma'alotav va'agudato al eretz yesadah* — He who builds His chambers in the heavens, His bundle is established upon the earth. When the Jewish people are unified — forming God's *agudah* on earth — the divine presence is properly established above and God responds to their prayers.

The moral deserves attention. The *beraita* is careful to make the dependency mutual: it is not only that those who do not bear fruit depend upon those who do, but equally that the fruit-bearers depend upon the non-fruit-bearers. One reading is that we need the non-fruit-bearers for the sake of unity — that we must not exclude any Jew. But the language suggests something more: that those who do not practice *mitzvot* in the conventional sense nonetheless contribute qualities and strengths to the Jewish people that those who do are not providing. The building of *Eretz Yisrael*, for instance, was carried forward by those who were not engaged in *Torah* study, and that contribution is, in its own way, indispensable.

### Bundle Debate

The *beraita*'s statement that all four species must be *b'agudah achat* — in one bundle — points toward a debate among the *tanna'im* that the *gemara* now introduces: must the *lulav* be physically bound? According to the *rabbanan*, it is valid whether bound or not. According to Rabbi Yehuda, if it is bound it is valid; if not bound it is invalid. Rabbi Yehuda derives this from a *gezeirah shavah* of *kicha kicha*: the Torah says *u'lekachtem lachem* using the root *lekicha*, and elsewhere it says *agudat ezov* — a bundle of hyssop — also in the context of *kicha*. Just as there a bundle is required, so too here. The *rabbanan* do not accept this *gezeirah shavah*.

A further *beraita* teaches: it is a *mitzvah* to bind the *lulav*, but if one did not bind it, it is still valid. The *gemara* asks: whose position is this? If it is Rabbi Yehuda's, why is it valid when not bound? And if it is the *rabbanan*'s, why would binding be a *mitzvah* at all? The *gemara* resolves: it follows the *rabbanan*, and the *mitzvah* of binding is derived from *zeh keili v'anveihu* — the principle of enhancing a *mitzvah* through beauty. Binding makes the *lulav* look more beautiful, and so it is praiseworthy though not required for validity.

However, this explanation may be incomplete. If the *rabbanan* agree at the conceptual level that the four species constitute a single act of taking — which the *gemara* elsewhere implies — then the *mitzvah* of binding may not be merely aesthetic. Binding them together physically expresses and reinforces their unity as a single *mitzvah*. On this reading, the *mitzvah l'ogdo* is about making the act more fully what it is meant to be — a single, unified taking — not merely about appearance.

It is also worth noting that even Rabbi Yehuda does not require the *etrog* to be literally bundled together with the others; the verse *u'lekachtem lachem bayom harishon pri etz hadar kapot temarim* does not link the *etrog* to the *lulav* with a *vav*, leaving the *etrog* somewhat apart even according to his view. This suggests that even Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of *agud* is more conceptual than physical at the level of the *etrog*, though one holds them together in practice.

### Lekicha Tama

Returning to Rav Chana bar Rava's ruling — that if one possesses all four species they are not *me'akev* — the *gemara* specifies what this means practically. It does not mean one can take three and omit one. Rather, it means they need not be bound in a single *agudah*. The taking of each one separately, when all are present and owned, is not disqualified by the absence of a bundle.

*Tosafot* cites *Halachot Gedolot* and notes that the *Rambam* goes even further: if all four species are available (*yesh lo*), one may take them in four separate sequential acts rather than simultaneously, and still be *yotzei*. The *Rambam* apparently reasons that since one could have taken them all at once, taking them sequentially is viewed as if they were taken together.

The logical question this raises is profound: the verse says *u'lekachtem* — a single verb, a single act. By *tefillin*, the Torah uses two verbs — *ukshartem l'os al yadecha* and *v'hayu l'totafot bein einecha* — and it is easier to understand why they might constitute two distinct *mitzvot* that happen to be interdependent. For the *lulav*, there is one verb and one act; how can we permit four separate acts? The answer given by *Halachot Gedolot* and the *Rambam* seems to be that since one owned all four and could have performed a single act, any sequential performance is retrospectively viewed as equivalent to the single act one could have performed.

The *Rambam*'s ruling goes still further: he holds that if one does take them separately, separate *brachot* are recited over each — one for the *etrog*, one for the *lulav*, one for each of the other species. This is a striking ruling, suggesting that at some level they are conceived of as distinct *mitzvot*. Yet this sits in tension with the single verb of *u'lekachtem*. This tension — between whether interdependent components constitute a single unified *mitzvah* or distinct *mitzvot* that are merely bound together — is precisely the conceptual question that will occupy the *gemara* for many dafim to come.

## Sprinklings Intent

### Parah Adumah

The *gemara* now takes up a *baraita* addressing the seven sprinklings of the *parah adumah* toward the *ohel mo'ed*, and two conditions under which they may be invalidated.

The first condition is *shelo lishmo* — performing the *haza'ot* without the proper intent. The Torah refers to the *parah adumah* as a *chata't*, and the general rule established in the first *mishna* of Zevachim is that a *chata't* performed *shelo lishmo* is *pasul*. Accordingly, if the sprinklings of the *parah adumah* are performed without the proper intent, they are invalid.

The second condition is *shelo m'chuvanot* — performing the sprinklings without proper direction. The verse specifies *el nochach pnei ohel mo'ed* — directly facing the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. If the *kohen* sprinkled in the wrong direction, entirely missing the orientation toward the *beit hamikdash*, this too is *pasul*.

### Inner Chata'ot

The same *baraita* addresses the sprinklings of the inner *chata'ot* — those performed inside the *heichal*, between the staves of the *aron*, upon the *parochet*, and upon the golden altar — as well as the sprinklings associated with the purification of a *metzora*.

For both the inner *chata'ot* and the *metzora*, performing the sprinklings *shelo lishman* — without proper intent — renders them *pasul*. For the inner *chata'ot*, this follows from the rule that a *chata't shelo lishmo* is invalid. For the *metzora*, it requires additional discussion, since the sprinkling in that context is part of an *asham* rather than a *chata't*; the *gemara* will need to explain what *shelo lishmo* means in that context.

### Direction Contradiction

Here a striking contradiction emerges. The *baraita* stated that for the *parah adumah*, sprinklings that are *shelo m'chuvanot* — not directed correctly — are *pasul*. Yet the same *baraita* then says that for the inner *chata'ot* and the *metzora*, sprinklings that are *shelo m'chuvanot* are *kasherot* — valid even if not precisely directed. How can the same condition (improper direction) invalidate one set of sprinklings but leave another set valid?

This is a genuine contradiction within the *baraita*, and the *gemara* treats it as such.

### Facing Debate

Rav Chisda resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. One ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda and the other follows the *rabbanan*. The two positions are rooted in a larger debate about the meaning of the Torah's description of the *avodah* on Yom Kippur. The verse in *Acharei Mot* states *v'al yavo b'chol et el hakodesh mibeit laparochet el pnei hakaporet* — no one may enter the inner sanctum, from within the *parochet* facing the *kaporet*. The phrase *el pnei hakaporet* — toward the face of the *kaporet* — triggers a debate: are one's obligations (and prohibitions) defined by being in the *kodesh kodashim* at all (*mibeit laparochet*), or only when one is directly facing the *aron* (*el pnei hakaporet*)?

This debate about the meaning of "facing" in the context of Yom Kippur entry carries over into the laws of sprinkling. Whether the requirement to sprinkle toward the *kodesh kodashim* means precisely facing it, or merely facing its general direction, depends on the same underlying dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *rabbanan*. The *parah adumah* ruling reflects one position on this, and the inner *chata'ot* ruling reflects the other.

The full elaboration of this debate — with its nuances and halachic implications — will occupy the beginning of the next shiur.
