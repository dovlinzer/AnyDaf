# Menachot 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kometz Sunset

### Kometz Before Sunset

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא עִם בֹּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ – לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ שֶׁתַּצִּית הָאוּר בְּרוּבּוֹ. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לִקְלוֹט, כָּאן לְהַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the handful is brought up and burned concurrent **with the setting of the sun, you do not find that the majority of it is consumed by the fire** before sunset. How does this *baraita* accord with Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that the majority of the handful must be consumed by the fire in order to render permitted the consumption of the remainder by the priests? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** where the *baraita* does not require the consumption by fire of the majority of the handful, it is referring only to that which is required in order for the altar **to receive** the handful, so that it is considered the food of the altar and may continue to burn all night long. **There,** Rabbi Yoḥanan states that in order **to render permitted** the consumption of the remainder by the priests, the majority of the handful must be consumed by the fire.

The sugya opens at the bottom of 26b, where the *gemara* resolves a tension regarding the validity of the *mincha* offering. The question is whether the *kometz* must be substantially consumed by fire — that is, whether the fire must seize the majority of it — in order for the *mincha* to be considered valid. How, then, can we infer from an earlier *beraita* that it suffices to place the *kometz* on the fire immediately before sunset, allowing it to continue burning into the night?

The *gemara*'s answer is that this arrangement is entirely valid: the *mincha* does not achieve its valid status until the fire has seized the majority of the *kometz*, yet so long as the human act of the *avodah* — the placement on the fire — was performed during the day, the burning may continue and complete itself after nightfall. The point at which the fire seizes the majority, even if that moment occurs at night, is when the *mincha* becomes valid.

### Action & Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַתְנֵי לַהּ ״מִבּוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״, וּמוֹקֵים לַהּ בְּפוֹקְעִין, וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: בְּפוֹקְעִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Rabbi Elazar teaches** the *baraita* the way it was initially presented, as asking how it is known that items that are usually sacrificed during the day may be burned **after sunset. And he interprets** the *baraita* as referring **to** parts of the offering that were **dislodged** from the fire after sunset, which may be returned to the fire throughout the night. **And similarly, when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he said that **Rabbi Yannai said** the *baraita* is referring **to** parts that were **dislodged** from the fire after sunset.

This resolution reflects a broader principle found elsewhere in *halacha*: where a human act must be performed at a specific time, the subsequent process of completion — which unfolds on its own — may extend beyond that window. A parallel example is placing an item on a flame before *Shabbat* begins: since the act of placement was performed before *Shabbat*, the cooking that continues over *Shabbat* is attributed to that initial act. The *gemara* understands the *kometz* similarly: the act of the *avodah* defines the legal moment; the process of combustion carries forward from there.

### Rabbi Elazar Pikuin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: קְטֹרֶת שֶׁפָּקְעָה מֵעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אֲפִילּוּ קְרָטִין שֶׁבָּהּ אֵין מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתָן! וְתָנֵי רַב חֲנִינָא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי בִּדְבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: ״אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל הָאֵשׁ אֶת הָעֹלָה עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ – עִיכּוּלֵי עוֹלָה אַתָּה מַחְזִיר, וְאִי אַתָּה מַחְזִיר עִיכּוּלֵי קְטֹרֶת! סְמִי מִיכָּן קְטֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Yannai** in fact **say this? But doesn't Rabbi Yannai say:** In the case of **incense that was dislodged from on top of the altar,** the priests **may not return even small lumps of it** to the fire? **And** similarly, **Rav Ḥanina bar Minyumi from the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"That which the fire will consume of the burnt offering on the altar"** (Leviticus 6:3). This teaches that if parts **of a burnt offering** that were partially **consumed** were dislodged from the external altar **you shall return** them, **but you do not return incense** that was partially **consumed** and was dislodged from the internal altar. The Gemara answers: **Remove from** the *baraita* **here** the word **incense,** so that it is not included in the list of items that may be burned throughout the night.

Rabbi Elazar offers an alternative reading of the *beraita*. He does not require us to invoke the principle of the ongoing process at all. Instead, he reads the *beraita* as referring to *pikuin* — items that popped off the *mizbe'ach*. In such a case, the *kometz* had originally been placed on the altar during the day; it subsequently fell off; and it may then be restored even after nightfall. According to this reading, we are not permitting a nighttime *avodah* in any primary sense, but merely allowing restoration of something that was properly placed during the day and accidentally dislodged.

This principle is corroborated by Rav Dimi, who reported in the name of Rabbi Yanai: the *beraita* indeed concerns *pikuin*.

### Inner vs Outer Altar

A question immediately arises, however: does Rabbi Yanai himself actually hold that items which fell off the *mizbe'ach* may be restored after nightfall? For Rabbi Yanai himself taught that *ketoret* which flew off the *mizbe'ach* — even the tiniest fragment — may not be restored. This appears to contradict the principle of *pikuin* entirely.

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between the inner and outer *mizbe'ach*. Rabbi Chanina bar Minyumi teaches in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: the source for restoring fallen items is the verse *asher tochal ha'esh et ha'olah al hamizbe'ach* — the fire consumes the *olah* on the altar. The verse specifies things burned like the *olah*, and on the *mizbe'ach* in question: that is, on the outer *mizbe'ach*. From this we derive that items which fall off the outer *mizbe'ach* may be restored — but items belonging to the inner *mizbe'ach*, such as the *ketoret* burned on the golden altar, are not subject to this principle at all. The *gemara* therefore concludes: remove *ketoret* from this discussion. The *kometz* and the *levonah* — which belong to the outer *mizbe'ach* — may be restored after nightfall if they fell off. The *ketoret* of the inner *mizbe'ach* may not.

The logic here is precise: we are speaking of items that must *lechatchila* be placed during the day — not items like the limbs and fats of the *olah*, which may be placed *lechatchila* at night as well. If such daytime-required items were placed properly and then fell off, the question is whether they may be restored at night. The answer is yes for the outer *mizbe'ach*, no for the inner.

## Kometz Burning Method

### Kometz Under Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: כִּי פָּשֵׁיט רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בִּמְנָחוֹת, בָּעֵי הָכִי – בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: קוֹמֶץ שֶׁסִּידְּרוֹ, וְסִידֵּר עָלָיו אֶת הַמַּעֲרָכָה, מַהוּ? דֶּרֶךְ הַקְטָרָה בְּכָךְ, אוֹ אֵין דֶּרֶךְ הַקְטָרָה בְּכָךְ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said: When Rabbi Elazar would explain** the *halakhot* **of** the **meal offerings,** he would **raise** this **dilemma: Rabbi Elazar raises a dilemma:** With regard to **a handful that** a priest **arranged** on the altar, **and he arranged the arrangement** of wood on the altar **on top of it, what is** the *halakha*? Is **this** considered a proper **manner of burning, or** is **this not** considered a proper **manner of burning,** since the handful is not arranged on top of the wood? The Gemara comments: No answer was found, and the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Having resolved the temporal questions surrounding the *kometz*, the *gemara* turns to a different set of concerns: the precise manner in which the *kometz* must be burned on the *mizbe'ach*. These are distinct from the earlier questions and involve the physical arrangement of the offering relative to the firewood.

Rav Assi reports that when Rabbi Elazar was studying Menachot, he posed the following question: if one arranged the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* and then placed the firewood on top of it — rather than placing the *kometz* on the wood — what is the *halacha*? Is this considered *derech haktara*, the normal manner of burning an offering? Or is it not *derech haktara*, and therefore invalid?

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

One might understand this question as bearing on the formal act of placing the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* — whether there is a required manner of deposition, as there is with blood. Alternatively, the concern may be more functional: when the *kometz* is beneath the wood rather than atop it, the smoke may not rise as effectively. The question thus may implicate not only the legal act but the physical result of the burning.

### Olah Under Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי חִזְקִיָּה: אֵבָרִין שֶׁסִּידְרָן וְסִידֵּר עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַמַּעֲרָכָה, מַהוּ? ״עַל הָעֵצִים״ אָמַר רַחְמָנָא דַּוְקָא עַל הָעֵצִים, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב קְרָא אַחֲרִינָא: ״אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל הָאֵשׁ אֶת הָעֹלָה עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, אִי בָּעֵי הָכִי עָבֵיד, אִי בָּעֵי הָכִי עָבֵיד? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya raises a dilemma:** With regard to the **limbs** of the burnt offering **that** a priest **arranged** on the altar **and arranged the arrangement** of wood on the altar **on top of them, what is** the *halakha*? Do we say that **the Merciful One states:** "And Aaron's sons, the priests, shall lay the pieces and the head, and the fat, in order **upon the wood** that is on the fire upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:8), teaching that they must be placed **specifically upon the wood? Or perhaps, since it is written** in **another verse: "That which the fire will consume of the burnt offering on the altar"** (Leviticus 6:3), indicating that the burnt offering may be arranged directly on the altar, **if** the priest **desires** to arrange the limbs in **this** manner he may **do** so, **and if** he **desires** to arrange them in **that** manner he may also **do** so. The Gemara comments: No answer was found, and the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Chizkiya posed a closely parallel question regarding the limbs of an *olah*: if one arranged the limbs on the *mizbe'ach* and then placed the firewood on top of them, what is the *halacha*? Here the question carries an additional dimension, because the Torah explicitly states *al ha'etzim* — on the wood — implying that the limbs must rest upon the firewood. Does *al ha'etzim* mean specifically that, and only that arrangement? Or does the verse *asher tochal ha'esh et ha'olah al hamizbe'ach* — that the fire consumes the *olah* on the altar — suggest that any configuration which results in burning on the altar suffices, and either arrangement is acceptable?

The *gemara* again answers: *teiku*.

What is significant here is the nature of the comparison. The *kometz*, in this discussion, is being treated not as something analogous to blood — which requires a formal act of application — but as something analogous to the fats and limbs of an animal, which are simply placed on the *mizbe'ach* to burn. Unlike those items, the *kometz* is a *matir*, the component that renders the *korban* valid. Yet the *gemara*'s framing suggests that the legal concern is less about a formal act of placement and more about the factual result: is it burning in a legitimate configuration?

### Limbs Beside Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא: אֵבָרִין שֶׁסִּידְּרָן בְּצִידֵּי הַמַּעֲרָכָה, מַהוּ? אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״עַל״ מַמָּשׁ – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּעַל הָעֵצִים כְּתִיב. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, מַאי? הָכָא נָמֵי ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, אוֹ דִלְמָא ״עַל הָעֵצִים״ דּוּמְיָא דְּ״עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ – מָה הָתָם עַל מַמָּשׁ, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי עַל מַמָּשׁ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa raises a dilemma:** With regard to the **limbs** of an offering **that** a priest **arranged adjacent to the arrangement** of wood on the altar, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara explains: **Do not raise the dilemma according to** the opinion **of the one who says** that the phrase **"upon [*al*]** the wood" is meant **literally,** as **"upon [*al*]** the wood" **is written,** and not: Next to the wood. **When should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion **of the one who says** in the mishna (96a) that the term **"upon [*al*]"** (see Numbers 2:20) means **adjacent to.** According to that *tanna*, **what** is the *halakha* in this case? Is it explained that **here, too,** the phrase **"upon [*al*]** the wood" can mean **adjacent to** the wood? **Or perhaps,** the phrase "upon [*al*] the wood that is on the fire upon the altar" teaches that **"upon the wood"** is to be understood as **similar to "upon the altar": Just as there "upon** the altar" is meant **literally, so too here,** the phrase **"upon** the wood" is meant **literally.** The Gemara comments: No answer was found, and the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha posed a third question: what if one arranged the limbs of an *olah* not on top of the firewood, but alongside it — adjacent to the *ma'aracha* rather than upon it? The fire will still reach the limbs; they will still burn. So what is the *halacha*?

The *gemara* frames this question around a linguistic debate about the word *al*. If *al* means literally "on," then placing the limbs beside the wood rather than on it clearly fails the requirement of *al ha'etzim*. But if *al* can sometimes mean "near" — as in *al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafona*, which means near the side of the *mizbe'ach* rather than literally on its flank — then perhaps *al ha'etzim* could be satisfied even when the limbs are merely adjacent. Against this, one could argue that the phrase *al ha'etzim* parallels *al hamizbe'ach*, and just as the latter means literally on the altar and not merely near it, so too the former must mean literally on the wood.

The *gemara* again concludes: *teiku*.

The cumulative effect of these three unresolved questions reinforces the earlier observation: the *gemara* is treating the burning of the *kometz* as more akin to the burning of animal parts than to the act of applying blood. The concern is less the formal legal act and more the physical manner and arrangement of combustion.

## Me'akev Components

### Individual Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקּוֹמֶץ מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ, עִשָּׂרוֹן מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ, הַיַּיִן מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ, הַשֶּׁמֶן מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the handful,** failure to sacrifice **the minority of it prevents the majority of it,** which was sacrificed, from rendering it permitted for the priests to consume the remainder of the meal offering. With regard to **a tenth** of an ephah of flour brought as a meal offering, failure to sacrifice **the minority of it prevents the majority of it,** which was sacrificed, from qualifying as a proper meal offering. With regard to **the wine** poured as a libation, failure to pour **the minority of it prevents the majority of it,** which was poured, from qualifying as a proper libation. With regard to **the** *log* of **oil** that is required for the meal offering, failure to add **the minority of it prevents the majority of it,** which was added, from being a sufficient measure of oil.

With the beginning of daf 27a, the *gemara* enters a major new *mishna*, one that will generate extended discussion spanning numerous topics across all of *halacha*. The *mishna* introduces the principle of components that are *me'akev* — that is, that hold one another back, such that if one is missing the entire offering is invalid.

The *mishna* opens with individual quantitative requirements: even the smallest portion of the *kometz*, if lacking, invalidates the rest — *mi'uto me'akev et rubo*, the minority holds back the majority. Similarly, the *isaron* of flour that constitutes the *mincha* must be complete; even the smallest deficiency invalidates the *isaron* as a whole. The same applies to wine and to oil: whatever amount the Torah prescribes for a given *korban*, the full amount is required, and any shortfall renders the remainder invalid.

### Flour & Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַסּוֹלֶת וְהַשֶּׁמֶן מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, הַקּוֹמֶץ וְהַלְּבוֹנָה מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the fine flour and the oil,** failure to bring **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to **the handful and the frankincense,** failure to burn **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.**

The *mishna* then moves beyond individual measurements to address the interdependence of distinct components. This is a more significant step: it is no longer merely a question of how much of a single ingredient is required, but of whether entirely different components of a *korban* are bound up with one another.

*Hasolet v'hashemen me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the flour and the oil of a *mincha* are mutually interdependent. A *mincha* without the proper amount of flour is not valid, but equally, a *mincha* without the proper amount of oil is not valid. The relationship is not one-directional; each requires the presence of the other.

### Kometz & Levona

Similarly, the *kometz* and the *levona* — the handful of the *mincha* and the frankincense placed upon it — are mutually interdependent. Both must be present for the *mincha* to be valid. The absence of either one prevents the other from functioning.

### Textual Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא ״מְלֹא קֻמְצוֹ״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי. עִשָּׂרוֹן מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִסׇּלְתָּהּ״, שֶׁאִם חָסְרָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – פְּסוּלָה. הַיַּיִן מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ, ״כָּכָה״. הַשֶּׁמֶן מִיעוּטוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת רוּבּוֹ, דְּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים – ״כָּכָה״, וּמִנְחַת נְדָבָה – אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּמִשַּׁמְנָהּ״, שֶׁאִם חָסַר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – פְּסוּלָה. הַשֶּׁמֶן וְהַסּוֹלֶת מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, ״מִסׇּלְתָּהּ וּמִשַּׁמְנָהּ״, ״מִגִּרְשָׂהּ וּמִשַּׁמְנָהּ״. הַקּוֹמֶץ וְהַלְּבוֹנָה מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, ״עַל כׇּל לְבוֹנָתָהּ״, ״וְאֵת כׇּל הַלְּבוֹנָה אֲשֶׁר עַל הַמִּנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the reason** that the failure to sacrifice the minority of the handful disqualifies the entire offering? This is derived from the fact that **the verse states "his handful" twice,** once with regard to the voluntary meal offering (Leviticus 2:2) and once with regard to the meal offering of a sinner (Leviticus 5:12), and any *halakha* repeated in the verses is deemed indispensable. The mishna teaches: With regard to **a tenth** of an ephah of flour brought as a meal offering, failure to sacrifice **the minority of it prevents the majority of it** from qualifying as a proper meal offering. **What is the reason? The verse states:** "The priest shall remove of it a handful of its fine flour" (Leviticus 2:2). The usage of the term "of its fine flour" instead of: Of the fine flour, teaches **that if any amount** of its flour **was missing,** it is **not valid.** The mishna teaches: With regard to **the wine** poured as a libation, failure to pour **the minority of it prevents the majority of it** from qualifying as a proper libation. What is the reason? The verse states concerning the libations: **"So** shall it be done" (Numbers 15:11). The term "so" indicates that the libations must be sacrificed exactly in the manner described, without any deviation. The mishna teaches: With regard to **the** *log* of **oil** that is required for the meal offering, failure to add **the minority of it prevents the majority of it** from being a sufficient measure of oil. In the case of the oil **of the meal offering** that accompanies the **libations,** this *halakha* is learned from the term: **"So"** (Numbers 15:11), stated with regard to the libations. **And** in the case of the *log* of oil that accompanies **a voluntary meal offering, the verse states: "And of its oil"** (Leviticus 2:2), demonstrating **that if any amount** of its oil was **missing,** it is **not valid.** The mishna teaches: With regard to **the fine flour and the oil,** failure to bring **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** The *halakha* that each is indispensable is derived from the fact that the two are juxtaposed in the verse: "The priest shall remove of it a handful of its fine flour and of its oil" (Leviticus 2:2), and the fact that this requirement is repeated in the verse: **"Of its groats, and of its oil"** (Leviticus 2:16), teaches that each is indispensable. The mishna teaches: With regard to **the handful and the frankincense,** failure to burn **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** The *halakha* that each is indispensable is derived from the repetition of the mention of the two together in the verse, as it is written: "The priest shall remove of it a handful of its fine flour and of its oil, **as well as all of its frankincense"** (Leviticus 2:2), and again with regard to the meal offering of a sinner it is stated: **"And all the frankincense which is upon the meal offering"** (Leviticus 6:8).

The *gemara* then methodically traces the textual basis for each of these requirements.

For the *kometz*: the Torah uses the phrase *melo kumzo* — the fill of his handful — twice, which the *gemara* reads as emphatic: the full *kometz* is required, and any deficiency invalidates.

For the *isaron* of flour: the verse uses the possessive form *misolta* — its *solet* — conveying that it must be exactly its proper amount. Any deficiency renders it invalid.

For the wine accompanying offerings: the verse states *kacha ye'aseh lashor ha'echad o la'ayil ha'echad* — "so shall you do for each ox or each ram" — using the word *kacha*, meaning "thusly" or "exactly so." This formulation implies that any deviation from the prescribed amount invalidates.

For the oil: by *minchat nesachim*, the verse likewise uses *kacha*; by the free-will *mincha*, the verse says *u'mishamnah* — "from its oil" — the possessive again conveying that the exact amount is required.

Here a general principle of *korbanot* deserves notice. Unlike most areas of *halacha*, where a single statement in the Torah is presumed to carry the full weight of a requirement, by *korbanot* the principle is *shana alav hakatuv le'akev*: the Torah must repeat or reinforce a detail in order for us to conclude that its absence invalidates. This seems counterintuitive — one might think that the dense and elaborate ritual of the *avodah* would be precisely the domain where every detail is indispensable. Yet *Chazal* reasoned in the opposite direction: the sheer profusion of details in the sacrificial laws makes it implausible that every one is strictly *me'akev*. A repetition or emphasis in the verse is therefore needed to establish that a particular requirement is truly indispensable.

For the interdependence of flour and oil: the Torah says *misolta u'mishamnah* — "from its flour and from its oil" — linking them together. A second verse then repeats *migrasah u'mishamnah* — "from its meal and from its oil" — and the repetition establishes the interdependence.

For the *kometz* and *levona*: one verse says *al kol levonatah* — "upon all of its frankincense" — and another says *ve'et kol halevona* — "and all of the frankincense" — specifically in the context of offering it *asher al hamincha*. The repetition establishes that neither can be offered validly without the other.

## Multi-Component Systems

### Yom Kippur Goats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנֵי שְׂעִירֵי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, שְׁנֵי כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, שְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ. שְׁנֵי סְדָרִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. שְׁנֵי בָּזִיכִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. הַסְּדָרִין וְהַבָּזִיכִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. שְׁנֵי מִינִים שֶׁבַּנָּזִיר, שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁבַּפָּרָה, וְאַרְבָּעָה שֶׁבַּתּוֹדָה, וְאַרְבָּעָה שֶׁבַּלּוּלָב, (וארבע) [וְאַרְבָּעָה] שֶׁבַּמְּצוֹרָע – מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. שֶׁבַע הַזָּאוֹת שֶׁבַּפָּרָה מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, שֶׁבַע הַזָּיוֹת שֶׁעַל בֵּין הַבַּדִּים, שֶׁעַל הַפָּרֹכֶת, שֶׁעַל מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב – מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ. גְּמָ׳ שְׁנֵי שְׂעִירֵי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה – חוּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the two goats of Yom Kippur,** the absence of **each** goat **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to **the two sheep** brought together with the meal offering of the two loaves on ***Shavuot*,** failure to bring **each** of the sheep **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to the **two loaves** brought on *Shavuot*, failure to bring **each** of the loaves **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to the **two arrangements** of the shewbread, failure to place **each** of the arrangements **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to the **two bowls** of frankincense that accompany the shewbread, failure to place **each** of the bowls **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to **the arrangements** of the shewbread **and the bowls** of frankincense, failure to bring **each** of them **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** With regard to the **two types** of loaves **that** accompany the offerings **of a nazirite:** The bread and wafers (see Numbers 6:15); the **three** species **that** are part of the rite **of the** red **heifer:** The cedar, hyssop, and scarlet wool (see Numbers 19:6); **and** the **four** types of loaves **that** accompany **the thanks offering:** The loaves, wafers, loaves soaked in hot water, and leavened bread (see Leviticus 7:12); **and** the **four** species **of the *lulav*:** The *lulav*, *etrog*, myrtle, and willow (see Leviticus 23:40); **and** the **four** species **that are** used **in** the purification process of **the leper:** The cedar, hyssop, scarlet wool, and birds (see Leviticus 14:4), failure to bring **each** of the components **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others.** With regard to the **seven sprinklings** of the blood **of the** red **heifer** that the priest sprinkles opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary (see Numbers 19:4), failure to sprinkle **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others.** With regard to the **seven sprinklings** of the blood of the bull and goat of Yom Kippur **that** are sprinkled **on** the Ark **between the staves** (see Leviticus 16:14–15), the seven sprinklings **that** are sprinkled **on the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, and the sprinklings **that** are sprinkled **on the golden altar** on Yom Kippur, and from all other inner sin offerings, failure to sprinkle **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others.** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: With regard to **the two goats of Yom Kippur,** the absence of **each** goat **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** This is derived from the verse that states with regard to the Yom Kippur service: "And it shall be **a statute** forever" (Leviticus 16:29), since wherever the term "statute" appears concerning a sacrificial rite, it signifies that the rite is an indispensable requirement.

The *mishna* now proceeds through an extended catalog of cases organized on a numerical principle, moving from two-component systems to three, four, and seven.

*Shnei se'irei Yom Hakippurim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two goats of Yom Kippur hold one another back. One goat is sent to *Azazel*; the other is slaughtered and its blood is sprinkled inside the *heichal*. Their processes are quite distinct from one another, yet both must be present and both must be valid for either to be meaningful. This is worth pausing over: unlike the *kometz* and *levona*, which are offered together in a unified act, the two Yom Kippur goats are processed very differently. And yet the *mishna* teaches that they are interdependent — if something happens to one before the other is completed, neither may proceed. The extensive treatment of this problem in the *mishnayot* of Yoma reflects just how live and consequential this interdependence is.

The textual basis: the verse at the conclusion of the Yom Kippur *avodah* in *Acharei Mot* states *v'hayta zot lachem l'chukat olam* — this shall be a perpetual statute for you. The word *chukkah* is understood to require that the *avodah* be performed exactly as specified; and that *chukkah* encompasses the interdependence of the components within it.

### Shavuot Lambs Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה – הֲוָיָה, שְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת – הֲוָיָה. שְׁנֵי סְדָרִין – חוּקָּה, שְׁנֵי בָּזִיכִין – חוּקָּה, הַסְּדָרִין וְהַבָּזִיכִין – חוּקָּה. שְׁנֵי מִינִים שֶׁבַּנָּזִיר, דִּכְתִיב: ״כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה״, שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁבַּפָּרָה – חוּקָּה. אַרְבָּעָה שֶׁבַּתּוֹדָה, דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְנָזִיר, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל זֶבַח תּוֹדַת שְׁלָמָיו״, וְאָמַר מָר: ״שְׁלָמָיו״ – לְרַבּוֹת שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר. וְאַרְבָּעָה שֶׁבִּמְצוֹרָע, דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת הַמְּצֹרָע״, וְאַרְבָּעָה שֶׁבַּלּוּלָב – ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם״, לְקִיחָה תַּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: With regard to **the two sheep** brought together with the meal offering of the two loaves on ***Shavuot*,** failure to bring **each** of the sheep **prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.** This is derived from the verse: "They shall be holy" (Leviticus 23:20), since the employment of a term of **being** indicates an indispensable requirement. Similarly, with regard to the **two loaves** brought on *Shavuot*, the reason failure to bring each of the loaves prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other is that the verse states: "They shall be of fine flour" (Leviticus 23:17), employing a term of **being.** With regard to the **two arrangements** of the shewbread, the reason failure to place each of the arrangements prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other is that the verse employs the term **statute** concerning them (see Leviticus 24:9). With regard to the **two bowls** of frankincense that accompany the shewbread, the reason failure to place each of the bowls prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other is that the verse employs the term **statute** concerning them (see Leviticus 24:9). With regard to **the arrangements** of the shewbread **and the bowls** of frankincense, the reason failure to bring each of them prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the other is that the verse employs the term **statute** concerning them, as that verse addresses each of these two components. With regard to the **two types** of loaves **that** accompany the offerings **of a nazirite,** each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, **as it is written** with regard to the nazirite: **"So he must do** after the law of his naziriteship" (Numbers 6:21), demonstrating that he must bring his offerings precisely as detailed in the verse. With regard to the **three** species **that** are part of the rite **of the** red **heifer,** each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, since the term **statute** is written about them: "This is the statute of the law" (Numbers 19:2). With regard to the **four** types of loaves **that** accompany **the thanks offering,** each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, **since** the thanks offering **is juxtaposed to** the offerings of **a nazirite, as it is written** with regard to the thanks offering: **"With the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanksgiving"** (Leviticus 7:13). **And the Master said:** The term **"his peace offerings"** serves **to include** the loaves of **the peace offering of a nazirite,** and it has already been demonstrated that with regard to the loaves that accompany the offerings of a nazirite, each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others. **And** with regard to the **four** species **that are in** the purification process of **the leper,** each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, **as it is written: "This shall be the law of the leper"** (Leviticus 14:2), and the term "shall be" indicates an indispensable requirement. **And** with regard to the **four** species **of the *lulav*,** each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, as the verse states: **"And you shall take" [*ulkaḥtem*]"** (Leviticus 23:40), which alludes to: **A complete taking [*lekiḥa tamma*],** comprising all four species.

*Shnei kivsei Atzeret me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two lambs of Shavuot hold one another back. The textual basis is *solet tihyena* — "fine flour they shall be" — and the word *tihyena* conveys "it shall be exactly thus." Though this verse speaks directly of the *shtei halechem*, the *gemara* applies the force of *havayah* — the word "shall be" — to the lambs as well.

*Shtei chalot me'akvot zu et zu* — the two loaves of the *shtei halechem* hold one another back. Likewise from *havayah*.

*Shnei sedarim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two columns of *lechem hapanim* hold one another back. The verse states *me'ishei Hashem chok olam* — an everlasting statute of God's fire-offerings — and the word *chok* requires exactness.

*Shnei bezichin me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two cups of frankincense that accompany the *shulchan* hold one another back. The same verse containing *chok* applies.

*Hasedarim vehabezichin me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the columns of bread and the cups of frankincense are mutually interdependent as well. You cannot burn the frankincense without the bread being present, and you cannot eat the bread without having burned the frankincense.

It is worth noting that while the word *chukkah* appears repeatedly and carries the work of establishing these interdependencies, it does not by itself define the scope of what is grouped together. For the *shulchan* and its components, the *gemara* identifies everything as required. For Yom Kippur, we group certain elements together but do not say, for instance, that the inability to bring the *par* of the *kohen gadol* prevents the *se'ir* from being offered. The application of *chok* to establish *me'akev* relationships therefore always involves a measure of conceptual grouping that the *gemara* takes as understood.

### Nazir Parah Todah

*Shnei minim shebanazir me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the two types of bread that accompany the *shlamim* of a *nazir* hold one another back. The textual basis is *ken ya'aseh al torat nizro* — "so shall be done according to his *nazirite* law" — the word *ken*, meaning "thus," conveying exactness.

*Gimel shebeparah me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the three components of the *parah adumah* purification, the *agudat ezov*, the *eitz erez*, and the *shni tola'at*, all hold one another back. The textual basis is *zot chukat hatorah* — this is the statute of the Torah — where both *zot* (this, exactly this) and *chukkah* reinforce the requirement.

*Arba shebetodah me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the four types of bread of the *todah* offering hold one another back. All forty loaves — ten of each type — must be present; if any type is missing, none of the loaves is valid, and none may be eaten. The textual basis is a linkage: the verse by the *todah* says *al zevach todah shlamav* — upon the slaughtering of his *todah* of his *shelamim* — and there is a teaching that the word *shlamav* serves to link the *shalmei nazir* to the *todah*. Since the interdependence of the bread types of the *nazir* is established by *ken ya'aseh*, and the *todah* is linked to the *nazir*, the same rule applies to the *todah*.

### Lulav Metzora

*Arba shebalulav me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the four species of the *lulav* hold one another back. The textual basis is the drasha of *lekicha tama*: the verse says *u'lekachtem*, and the *gemara* reads this as *lekicha* that is *tama* — a whole, complete taking — meaning all components must be present.

*Arba shebemetzora me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the four components of the *metzora* purification hold one another back. These are the *agudat ezov*, the *shni tola'at*, the *eitz erez*, and the birds — whose blood is used for the sprinkling. The textual basis is *zot tihyeh torat hametzora* — this shall be the law of the *metzora* — where *tihyeh* conveys that it must be exactly this way.

The organizational principle of this section of the *mishna* is explicitly numerical: it proceeds through two, three, four, and will continue to seven. This explains what might otherwise appear to be arbitrary jumps between topics — from bread offerings to the *parah adumah* to the *lulav* — as the *mishna* is simply following the count.

### Seven Sprinklings

*Sheva hazayot shebeparah me'akvot zu et zu* — the seven sprinklings of the *parah adumah* toward the *beit hamikdash* hold one another back. If any one of the seven is missing, the purification process is invalid. The textual basis is *chukkah*.

*Sheva hazayot shebein habadim ve'al parochet ve'al mizbe'ach hazahav me'akvot zu et zu* — the seven sprinklings of the inner *chata'ot*, whether performed between the staves of the *aron* on Yom Kippur, or upon the *parochet*, or upon the golden altar, all hold one another back. This applies to Yom Kippur, to the *par kohen hamashiach*, to the *par he'elam davar*, and to the goats brought for communal *avodah zarah*, all of which are inner *chata'ot*.

The textual basis for the inner *chata'ot*: the verse by *par he'elam davar* states *v'asa lapar ka'asher asa lapar harishon* — he shall do for the *par* as he did for the first *par* — meaning the procedure of the *kohen gadol*'s *par* is the template. But why does the Torah need to state this? It is to double and reinforce the *haza'ot*, teaching that every individual sprinkling is required, such that if even one is missing, nothing was accomplished: *she'im chisar achat m'kol hamatanot lo asa klum*.

## Lulav Unity Lesson

### Fruit Bearers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לוֹ, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ – אֵין מְעַכְּבִין. מֵיתִיבִי: אַרְבָּעָה מִינִין שֶׁבַּלּוּלָב, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶן עוֹשִׂין פֵּירוֹת, וּשְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם אֵין עוֹשִׂין פֵּירוֹת; הָעוֹשִׂין פֵּירוֹת יִהְיוּ זְקוּקִין לְשֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין, וְשֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין פֵּירוֹת יִהְיוּ זְקוּקִין לְעוֹשִׂין פֵּירוֹת, וְאֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּן בַּאֲגוּדָּה אֶחָת. וְכֵן יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּהַרְצָאָה, עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּן בַּאֲגוּדָּה אֶחָת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַבּוֹנֶה בַשָּׁמַיִם מַעֲלוֹתָיו וַאֲגֻדָּתוֹ עַל אֶרֶץ יְסָדָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava says:** The mishna **taught** that the four species of the *lulav* are necessary for the fulfillment of the mitzva **only** in a case **where one did not have** all four species; **but** if **one has** all four species, failure to take each of the components **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, and he fulfills the mitzva by taking each species individually. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to the **four species of the *lulav*, two of them,** the *lulav* and *etrog*, **produce fruit, and two of them,** the myrtle and willow, **do not produce fruit. Those that produce fruit have a bond with those that do not produce fruit, and those that do not produce fruit have a bond with those that produce fruit. And a person does not fulfill his obligation** of taking the *lulav* **until they are all** bound together **in a single bundle.** **And so too,** when **the Jewish people** fast and pray **for acceptance** of their repentance, this is not accomplished **until they are all** bound together **in a single bundle, as it is stated: "It is He that builds His upper chambers in the Heaven, and has established His bundle upon the earth"** (Amos 9:6), which is interpreted as stating that only when the Jewish people are bound together are they established upon the earth. This *baraita* contradicts Rav Ḥanan bar Rava's statement, since it teaches that the four species of the *lulav* must be taken together in order for one to fulfill his obligation of taking the *lulav*.

Having enumerated the four species in the context of *me'akev*, the *gemara* now elaborates on the *lulav* specifically, introducing a *beraita* that contains both a legal and a moral dimension.

Rav Chana bar Rava teaches: the rule that the four species are mutually interdependent (*me'akvin zeh et zeh*) applies only when one does not possess all four. If one has all four species available, *ein me'akvin* — they do not hold one another back. What this means will be developed shortly, but the basic point is that the *me'akev* relationship is triggered by actual absence, not by the choice to take them separately when one owns all of them.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a *beraita*: the four species of the *lulav* — two of which bear fruit (the *lulav*, which yields dates, and the *etrog*) and two of which do not (the *hadas* and *arava*) — are mutually dependent in both directions. The fruit-bearers need the non-fruit-bearers, and the non-fruit-bearers need the fruit-bearers. And the *beraita* states: *ein adam yotzei yedei chovato bahen ad she'kulam b'agudah achat* — a person does not fulfill his obligation unless all of them are in a single bundle.

### Divine Bundle

The *beraita* then draws its moral: *v'chen Yisrael b'hartza'ah* — so too with Israel when they come seeking divine favor. They must all be gathered together as one. The proof-text is *haboneh bashamayim ma'alotav va'agudato al eretz yesadah* — He who builds His chambers in the heavens, His bundle is established upon the earth. When the Jewish people are unified — forming God's *agudah* on earth — the divine presence is properly established above and God responds to their prayers.

The moral deserves attention. The *beraita* is careful to make the dependency mutual: it is not only that those who do not bear fruit depend upon those who do, but equally that the fruit-bearers depend upon the non-fruit-bearers. One reading is that we need the non-fruit-bearers for the sake of unity — that we must not exclude any Jew. But the language suggests something more: that those who do not practice *mitzvot* in the conventional sense nonetheless contribute qualities and strengths to the Jewish people that those who do are not providing. The building of *Eretz Yisrael*, for instance, was carried forward by those who were not engaged in *Torah* study, and that contribution is, in its own way, indispensable.

### Bundle Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: לוּלָב בֵּין אָגוּד בֵּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ אָגוּד – כָּשֵׁר; רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אָגוּד – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אָגוּד – פָּסוּל. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? גָּמַר קִיחָה קִיחָה מֵ״אֲגוּדַּת אֵזוֹב״, מָה לְהַלָּן בַּאֲגוּדָּה – אַף כָּאן בַּאֲגוּדָּה. וְרַבָּנַן, לָא גָּמְרִי ״קִיחָה קִיחָה״. כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: לוּלָב מִצְוָה לְאוֹגְדוֹ, וְאִם לֹא אֲגָדוֹ – כָּשֵׁר? כְּמַאן? אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לֹא אֲגָדוֹ אַמַּאי כָּשֵׁר? אִי רַבָּנַן, מַאי מִצְוָה? לְעוֹלָם רַבָּנַן, וּמַאי מִצְוָה? מִשּׁוּם ״זֶה אֵלִי וְאַנְוֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Whether the different species must be taken together **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*; as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A *lulav*, whether** it is **bound** with the myrtle and willow or **whether** it is **not bound,** is **fit. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If it is **bound,** it is **fit;** if it is **not bound,** it is **unfit.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara answers: By means of a verbal analogy, he **derives** the term **taking,** written with regard to the four species, **from** the term **taking** written with regard to **the bundle of hyssop.** It is written there, in the context of the sacrifice of the Paschal offering in Egypt: "Take a bundle of hyssop" (Exodus 12:22), and it is written here, with regard to the four species: "And you shall take for you on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of a date palm, boughs of dense-leaved trees, and willows of the brook" (Leviticus 23:40). **Just as there,** with regard to the Paschal offering, the mitzva to take the hyssop is specifically **in a bundle, so too here,** the mitzva to take the four species is specifically **in a bundle.** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning of **the Rabbis?** The Gemara answers: **They do not derive** the meaning of the term **taking** from the meaning of the term **taking** by means of the verbal analogy. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which is taught** in a *baraita*: There is **a mitzva to bind** the myrtle and the willow together with the ***lulav*, but if one did not bind it,** it is **fit? In accordance with whose** opinion is the *baraita*? **If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** if **one did not bind it, why** is it **fit? If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, what mitzva** is one fulfilling by binding it? The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. And what mitzva** is one fulfilling? The mitzva is **due to** the fact that it is stated: **"This is my God and I will beautify Him"** (Exodus 15:2), which is interpreted to mean that one should beautify himself before God in the performance of the mitzvot. The Rabbis agree that although failure to bind the three species does not render them unfit for performing the mitzva, the performance of the mitzva is more beautiful when the *lulav* is bound.

The *beraita*'s statement that all four species must be *b'agudah achat* — in one bundle — points toward a debate among the *tanna'im* that the *gemara* now introduces: must the *lulav* be physically bound? According to the *rabbanan*, it is valid whether bound or not. According to Rabbi Yehuda, if it is bound it is valid; if not bound it is invalid. Rabbi Yehuda derives this from a *gezeirah shavah* of *kicha kicha*: the Torah says *u'lekachtem lachem* using the root *lekicha*, and elsewhere it says *agudat ezov* — a bundle of hyssop — also in the context of *kicha*. Just as there a bundle is required, so too here. The *rabbanan* do not accept this *gezeirah shavah*.

A further *beraita* teaches: it is a *mitzvah* to bind the *lulav*, but if one did not bind it, it is still valid. The *gemara* asks: whose position is this? If it is Rabbi Yehuda's, why is it valid when not bound? And if it is the *rabbanan*'s, why would binding be a *mitzvah* at all? The *gemara* resolves: it follows the *rabbanan*, and the *mitzvah* of binding is derived from *zeh keili v'anveihu* — the principle of enhancing a *mitzvah* through beauty. Binding makes the *lulav* look more beautiful, and so it is praiseworthy though not required for validity.

However, this explanation may be incomplete. If the *rabbanan* agree at the conceptual level that the four species constitute a single act of taking — which the *gemara* elsewhere implies — then the *mitzvah* of binding may not be merely aesthetic. Binding them together physically expresses and reinforces their unity as a single *mitzvah*. On this reading, the *mitzvah l'ogdo* is about making the act more fully what it is meant to be — a single, unified taking — not merely about appearance.

It is also worth noting that even Rabbi Yehuda does not require the *etrog* to be literally bundled together with the others; the verse *u'lekachtem lachem bayom harishon pri etz hadar kapot temarim* does not link the *etrog* to the *lulav* with a *vav*, leaving the *etrog* somewhat apart even according to his view. This suggests that even Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of *agud* is more conceptual than physical at the level of the *etrog*, though one holds them together in practice.

### Lekicha Tama

Returning to Rav Chana bar Rava's ruling — that if one possesses all four species they are not *me'akev* — the *gemara* specifies what this means practically. It does not mean one can take three and omit one. Rather, it means they need not be bound in a single *agudah*. The taking of each one separately, when all are present and owned, is not disqualified by the absence of a bundle.

*Tosafot* cites *Halachot Gedolot* and notes that the *Rambam* goes even further: if all four species are available (*yesh lo*), one may take them in four separate sequential acts rather than simultaneously, and still be *yotzei*. The *Rambam* apparently reasons that since one could have taken them all at once, taking them sequentially is viewed as if they were taken together.

The logical question this raises is profound: the verse says *u'lekachtem* — a single verb, a single act. By *tefillin*, the Torah uses two verbs — *ukshartem l'os al yadcha* and *v'hayu l'totafot bein einecha* — and it is easier to understand why they might constitute two distinct *mitzvot* that happen to be interdependent. For the *lulav*, there is one verb and one act; how can we permit four separate acts? The answer given by *Halachot Gedolot* and the *Rambam* seems to be that since one owned all four and could have performed a single act, any sequential performance is retrospectively viewed as equivalent to the single act one could have performed.

The *Rambam*'s ruling goes still further: he holds that if one does take them separately, separate *brachot* are recited over each — one for the *etrog*, one for the *lulav*, one for each of the other species. This is a striking ruling, suggesting that at some level they are conceived of as distinct *mitzvot*. Yet this sits in tension with the single verb of *u'lekachtem*. This tension — between whether interdependent components constitute a single unified *mitzvah* or distinct *mitzvot* that are merely bound together — is precisely the conceptual question that will occupy the *gemara* for many dafim to come.

## Sprinklings Intent

### Parah Adumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שֶׁבַע הַזָּאוֹת שֶׁבַּפָּרָה, שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן – בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא מְכוָּּונוֹת אֶל נֹכַח פְּנֵי אוֹהֶל מוֹעֵד – פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest **performed the seven sprinklings of** the blood of **the** red **heifer** improperly, **either** by performing them **not for their** own **sake or** performing them **not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting** of the Tabernacle (Numbers 19:4), which corresponds to the Sanctuary in the Temple, **they are not valid.**

The *gemara* now takes up a *baraita* addressing the seven sprinklings of the *parah adumah* toward the *ohel mo'ed*, and two conditions under which they may be invalidated.

The first condition is *shelo lishmo* — performing the *haza'ot* without the proper intent. The Torah refers to the *parah adumah* as a *chata't*, and the general rule established in the first *mishna* of Zevachim is that a *chata't* performed *shelo lishmo* is *pasul*. Accordingly, if the sprinklings of the *parah adumah* are performed without the proper intent, they are invalid.

The second condition is *shelo m'chuvanot* — performing the sprinklings without proper direction. The verse specifies *el nochach pnei ohel mo'ed* — directly facing the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. If the *kohen* sprinkled in the wrong direction, entirely missing the orientation toward the *beit hamikdash*, this too is *pasul*.

### Inner Chata'ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁבִּפְנִים, וְשֶׁבִּמְצוֹרָע – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, פְּסוּלוֹת; שֶׁלֹּא מְכוָּּונוֹת, כְּשֵׁרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to the sprinkling of the blood **that** takes place **inside** the Sanctuary, of inner sin offerings, the blood of the bull and goat of Yom Kippur, the blood of the bull of the anointed priest, the blood of the bull for an unwitting communal sin, and the blood of the goats of idol worship, which are to be sprinkled "before the Lord, in front of the Curtain of the Sanctuary" (Leviticus 4:6), **and** the sprinkling of the oil **that** takes place during the purification **of the leper,** which is done "seven times before the Lord" (Leviticus 14:16), if these are performed **not for their** own **sake,** then **they are not valid.** But if they are performed **not precisely** toward the direction where they should be sprinkled, **they are valid.**

The same *baraita* addresses the sprinklings of the inner *chata'ot* — those performed inside the *heichal*, between the staves of the *aron*, upon the *parochet*, and upon the golden altar — as well as the sprinklings associated with the purification of a *metzora*.

For both the inner *chata'ot* and the *metzora*, performing the sprinklings *shelo lishman* — without proper intent — renders them *pasul*. For the inner *chata'ot*, this follows from the rule that a *chata't shelo lishmo* is invalid. For the *metzora*, it requires additional discussion, since the sprinkling in that context is part of an *asham* rather than a *chata't*; the *gemara* will need to explain what *shelo lishmo* means in that context.

### Direction Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא גַּבֵּי פָּרָה: שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – פְּסוּלוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא מְכוָּּונוֹת – כְּשֵׁרוֹת. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* **concerning** the sprinklings of the blood of the red **heifer** that if they were performed **not for their** own **sake, they are not valid,** but if they were performed **not precisely** toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting or Sanctuary, **they are valid? Rav Ḥisda said:** This is **not difficult; this** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** whereas **that** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Here a striking contradiction emerges. The *baraita* stated that for the *parah adumah*, sprinklings that are *shelo m'chuvanot* — not directed correctly — are *pasul*. Yet the same *baraita* then says that for the inner *chata'ot* and the *metzora*, sprinklings that are *shelo m'chuvanot* are *kasherot* — valid even if not precisely directed. How can the same condition (improper direction) invalidate one set of sprinklings but leave another set valid?

This is a genuine contradiction within the *baraita*, and the *gemara* treats it as such.

### Facing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מְחוּסְּרֵי כַפָּרָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לָעֲזָרָה בְּשׁוֹגֵג – חַיָּיב חַטָּאת, בְּמֵזִיד – עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר טְבוּל יוֹם וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַטְּמֵאִים. וּטְהוֹרִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לִפְנִים מִמְּחִיצָתָן, לַהֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ – בְּאַרְבָּעִים, מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת – בְּמִיתָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ וּמִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת – בְּאַרְבָּעִים, וְאֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת – בְּמִיתָה. בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? בְּהַאי קְרָא: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן אָחִיךָ וְאַל יָבוֹא בְכׇל עֵת אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת אֲשֶׁר עַל הָאָרוֹן וְלֹא יָמוּת״. רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ – בְּ״לֹא יָבֹא״, ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״ וְ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ – בְּ״לֹא יָמוּת״. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וּ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״ – בְּ״לֹא יָבֹא״, וְ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ – בְּ״לֹא יָמוּת״. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וְ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״, וְלָא בָּעֵי ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: הֵיכָל מִיחַיַּיב, מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת מִבַּעְיָא? ״מִבֵּית הַפָּרֹכֶת״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בְּמִיתָה. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וְלָא כְּתַב ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי קוֹדֶשׁ – מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת, אֲבָל הֵיכָל לָאו נָמֵי לָא. וְרַבָּנַן: הָהוּא לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דְּהֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ אִיקְּרִי ״קוֹדֶשׁ״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִבְדִּילָה הַפָּרֹכֶת לָכֶם בֵּין הַקֹּדֶשׁ וּבֵין קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים״. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָא אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֶל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ״ וּ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״, וְלָא בָּעֵי ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרוֹכֶת״ בְּמִיתָה, ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ מִיבַּעְיָא? ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ בְּמִיתָה, ״מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת״ בְּאַזְהָרָה. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר ״אֶל פְּנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת״ דַּוְקָא, וְ״הִזָּה אֶל נֹכַח״ נָמֵי דַּוְקָא. וְרַבָּנַן: מִדְּהָתָם לָאו דַּוְקָא, הָכָא נָמֵי לָאו דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Kelim* 1:10): With regard to **those who have not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete the purification process, and therefore are not permitted to enter the Temple or partake of sacrificial meat, **who entered the** Temple **courtyard unwittingly,** they are **liable** to bring **a sin offering.** If they entered **intentionally,** then this is **punishable by *karet*. And needless to say,** the same applies to **one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **and all the others who are ritually impure** and have not yet immersed. **And** with regard to those who are **pure who entered beyond their boundaries,** i.e., beyond where it is permitted for them to enter, such as a priest who enters the Sanctuary for a purpose other than performing the Temple service, if one entered **any** part **of the Sanctuary,** he is liable **to** receive **forty** lashes. If he entered **within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, i.e., into the Holy of Holies, or he entered the Holy of Holies all the way until he was **before the Ark Cover,** he is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he entered **any** part **of the Sanctuary or within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies, he is liable **to** receive **forty** lashes; **but** if he entered the Holy of Holies all the way until he was **before the Ark Cover,** he is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. **With regard to what** issue **do** the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** the proper understanding of **this verse: "And the Lord said to Moses: Speak to Aaron your brother, that he not come at all times into the holy place, within the Curtain, before the Ark Cover which is upon the Ark, that he not die"** (Leviticus 16:2). **The Rabbis hold** that entering **into the holy place,** i.e., the Sanctuary, is **subject to** the prohibition of: **He shall not come,** and one who violates it is punished with lashes, whereas entering **within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies **and before the Ark Cover** is **subject to** the warning of: **He shall not die,** and entering there is punished by death at the hand of Heaven. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that entering **into the holy place,** i.e., the Sanctuary, **and within the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies is **subject to** the prohibition of: **He shall not come,** and one who violates it is punished with lashes, whereas entering **before the Ark Cover** is **subject to** the warning of: **He shall not die,** and entering there is punished by death at the hand of Heaven. **What is the reason** for the interpretation **of the Rabbis?** **If** it should **enter your mind** to explain the verse **as Rabbi Yehuda says,** that entering the Holy of Holies is punishable by death at the hand of Heaven, **let the Merciful One write:** That he not come at all times **into the holy place and before the Ark Cover** that he not die, **and there is no need** to write **"within the Curtain," and I would say:** If one **becomes liable** to receive lashes for even entering the **Sanctuary, is** it **necessary** to teach that one incurs this punishment for entering **within the Curtain? Why do I** need the phrase **"within the Curtain" that the Merciful One wrote? Learn from** that seemingly extraneous term that entering the Holy of Holies is punishable **by death** at the hand of Heaven. **And Rabbi Yehuda** understands: **If the Merciful One had written** only that it is prohibited to come **"into the holy place" and did not write "within the Curtain," I would say: What** is the **holy place?** It is **within the Curtain,** i.e., the Holy of Holies, and one who enters it violates a prohibition, **but** if one enters the **Sanctuary** he does **not even** violate **a prohibition. And the Rabbis** respond to this claim: **You cannot say that, as the entire Sanctuary is called** "the **holy place," as it is stated: "And the Curtain shall divide for you between the holy place and the Holy of Holies"** (Exodus 26:33). **And what is the reason** for the interpretation **of Rabbi Yehuda?** Why does he hold that one who enters the Holy of Holies violates a prohibition but is not punished with death at the hand of Heaven? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda holds that **if** it should **enter your mind** to explain **as the Rabbis say,** that entering the Holy of Holies is punishable by death at the hand of Heaven, **let the Merciful One write:** That he not come at all times **into the holy place and within the Curtain** that he not die, **and there is no need** to write **"before the Ark Cover." And I would say:** If entering **within the Curtain,** i.e., the Holy of Holies, is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **is** it **necessary** to teach that one incurs this punishment for entering **before the Ark Cover? Why do I** need the phrase **"before the Ark Cover" that the Merciful One wrote? Learn from** that seemingly extraneous term that entering **before the Ark Cover** is punishable **by death** at the hand of Heaven, but entering **within the Curtain** merely violates **a prohibition.** **And the Rabbis?** **Indeed,** it is so **that** in order to teach the punishment of death at the hand of Heaven it **is not necessary** for the verse to also state "before the Ark Cover." **And** the reason **that the Merciful One wrote "before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover"** was in order **to exclude** one who entered the Holy of Holies through **a roundabout path,** as one who did not enter facing the Ark Cover, i.e., from the east, is not punished with death at the hand of Heaven. This is **as the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov taught:** With regard to the verse: "And he shall sprinkle it with his finger **before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover to the east"** (Leviticus 16:14), **this established a paradigm** that **any place** in the Torah **where it is stated: "Before [*penei*],"** it is referring to **nothing other than before** the **eastern** side. The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to this, as it is clear that the term "before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover" is necessary to exclude one who entered the Holy of Holies through a roundabout path? The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Yehuda, if the purpose was for that reason, **let the verse say: Before [*penei*]** the Ark Cover. **What** is the purpose of the word ***el*? Learn from** that seemingly extraneous term that one is punished with death at the hand of Heaven **specifically** if he entered directly **before** the Ark, but not if he merely entered the Holy of Holies. **And the Rabbis** hold that the term **"*el*"** does **not** mean **specifically** one who enters directly before the Ark Cover. The Gemara now returns to its suggestion that the contradiction between the two *baraitot* with regard to whether the sprinklings of the red heifer are valid or not when performed not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting can be resolved by explaining that one *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda and the other is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that the expression **"before [*el penei*] the Ark Cover"** teaches that the punishment is limited to one who **specifically** entered directly before the Ark Cover, holds that the expression: **"And sprinkle** of its blood **toward [*el*] the front"** (Numbers 19:4), **also** means that the sprinklings must be performed **specifically** toward the front of the Sanctuary. **And the Rabbis** are of the opinion that **from** the fact **that there** the term *el* does **not** mean **specifically** that one is liable to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven only if he enters directly before the Ark Cover, **here too** they hold that it is **not** meant **specifically,** and therefore the sprinklings are valid even when performed not precisely toward the entrance of the Tent of Meeting.

Rav Chisda resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. One ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda and the other follows the *rabbanan*. The two positions are rooted in a larger debate about the meaning of the Torah's description of the *avodah* on Yom Kippur. The verse in *Acharei Mot* states *v'al yavo b'chol et el hakodesh mibeit laparochet el pnei hakaporet* — no one may enter the inner sanctum, from within the *parochet* facing the *kaporet*. The phrase *el pnei hakaporet* — toward the face of the *kaporet* — triggers a debate: are one's obligations (and prohibitions) defined by being in the *kodesh kodashim* at all (*mibeit laparochet*), or only when one is directly facing the *aron* (*el pnei hakaporet*)?

This debate about the meaning of "facing" in the context of Yom Kippur entry carries over into the laws of sprinkling. Whether the requirement to sprinkle toward the *kodesh kodashim* means precisely facing it, or merely facing its general direction, depends on the same underlying dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *rabbanan*. The *parah adumah* ruling reflects one position on this, and the inner *chata'ot* ruling reflects the other.

The full elaboration of this debate — with its nuances and halachic implications — will occupy the beginning of the next shiur.
