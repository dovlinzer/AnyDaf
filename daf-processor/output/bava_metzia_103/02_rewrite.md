# Bava Metzia 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tenant Payment Disputes

### Burden of Proof Setup

The daf opens by doubling back nine lines from the bottom of 102b. The *gemara* presents a question posed to Rav Yannai: *socheir amar nasati, maskir amar lo natalti* — the tenant claims to have already paid the rent, while the landlord claims he never received it. Who bears the burden of proof?

### Hamotzi Mechavero Princi…

At first glance, the answer seems straightforward. We normally apply the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — whoever seeks to extract money from another bears the burden of proof. This is a classic case of *kofer hakol*: the tenant admits there was a rental agreement and presumably a debt, but claims to have paid it. By the standard rule, the landlord is the one making a demand, and the tenant, as *kofer hakol*, should be believed — he need not even take a *shvua de'oraisa*, and at most would be subject to a rabbinic *shvuas heses*. So why would this case be any different?

### Temporal Presumptions

The *gemara* resolves the question by distinguishing *eimas* — when, precisely, is the dispute occurring? If it is *be-soch zemano*, in the middle of the rental period before the rent is due, the *gemara* says *tanina* — we already learned this in a *mishna*, and the clear implication is that the landlord is believed: people simply do not prepay debts before they are obligated. Conversely, if the dispute arises *le-achar zemano*, after the payment date has passed, the *gemara* again says *tanina*, and the implication now runs the other way — once the obligation has come due, there is a presumption that payment was made.

### Pidyon Haben Analogy

The source the *gemara* invokes for this principle of temporal presumption is a *mishna* dealing with *pidyon haben*. The obligation to redeem a firstborn son falls on the thirtieth day after birth. If the father died *be-soch sheloshim yom*, within the thirty days, the son is in a *chezkat she-lo nifda* — a presumption that the redemption was never performed — and must assume he remains obligated unless he can bring evidence that the father acted early. But if the father died *le-achar sheloshim yom*, after the thirty days, the son operates under a presumption that the father did indeed perform the *mitzvah* once obligated, unless neighbors or others come and tell him explicitly that the father did not get around to it.

This is a striking proof, and it is worth pausing on why it is somewhat unusual. The *pidyon haben* context involves a personal ritual obligation — no particular *kohen* is suing the son, and no court proceeding is underway. One might argue that the presumptions operating there are about religious caution and piety rather than evidentiary weight in a monetary dispute. If I want to fulfill the *mitzvah* properly, I should perhaps assume before thirty days that it has not been done and act accordingly. But that is not necessarily the same as what the rules of *al mi le-havi re'aya* would dictate if a *kohen* were actually to make a monetary claim. The *gemara*, however, clearly treats the analogy as directly applicable, which itself is noteworthy.

### Last Day Payment Practice

The *gemara* then presses further: *tzericha beyomei de-mashlim zemanei* — what about the very last day of the rental period, when the obligation is technically not yet due but is coming due that same day or night? The question is *bei avid inish de-para beyomei de-mashlim zemanei o lo* — do people sometimes pay on that final day before the technical deadline arrives? Rabbi Yochanan responds *tenitua* — we have also learned this from a *mishna*, which he proceeds to cite.

### Worker Oath Exception

The *mishna* in tractate *Shvuos* that Rabbi Yochanan invokes deals with *sakhir bizmano nishba venotel* — a hired day laborer who, on the very day his wages are due, may take a *shvua* and collect. This is one of the special *takanos* enacted by the Sages departing from the standard *kol hanishba'in nishba'in ve'ein meshalmin* framework, whereby ordinarily it is the defending party who takes the *shvua* and goes free. In the case of a worker, since an employer running a large field with many laborers is likely to lose track of who was paid and who was not, *chazal* ruled that the worker — who has only one wage to track — is in the stronger epistemic position. The *gemara* explains: *sakhir hu deramu rabanan shvua aleia mishum deba'al habayis tarid befo'alav* — in the case of a day laborer, they imposed the *shvua* on the employer and allowed the worker to collect because the *ba'al habayis* is distracted by managing his many hired hands. The day on which wages are due is precisely the one day where the *ba'al habayis* cannot be expected to have kept careful track, because payment was due and things were hectic. This is why that single day receives a unique ruling.

The parallel to the rental dispute is instructive by contrast. There, the tenant has only one landlord; he is not distracted and has every reason to track payment. The *gemara* concludes that without the distraction rationale, there is no basis to depart from the underlying principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*. The tenant as *kofer hakol* would take the standard *shvuas heses* and be exempt. What is fascinating, however, is that the *gemara* implicitly accepts the strong version of the earlier presumption: in the middle of the month, the *chazaka* that people do not prepay actually *overrides* the normal default, and one who claimed mid-month to have prepaid would not be believed even as a *kofer hakol* — he would need to bring affirmative proof.

## House Rental Disputes

### Ten-Year Rental Case

The *gemara* turns to a ruling of Rav Nachman, four lines from the top of the new daf. Suppose someone rented a house to his friend for ten years, and a *shetar* was written documenting the rental, but no start date was recorded. The landlord later claims the tenant has already used five years and has only five left; the tenant insists he has only been there a shorter time and claims more years remain. Rav Nachman rules that the tenant is believed.

### Shetar Purpose Analysis

Rav Acha of Difti challenges Ravina with an obvious objection: surely this should be analogous to the case of a loan. If one borrows a hundred *zuz* and a *shetar* documents the full debt, and the borrower later claims he paid half, he is not believed — the *shetar* in the lender's hand proves the full amount is owed, and the borrower would have needed to obtain a written acknowledgment of partial payment, a *shover*, to rebut that evidence. Why is the rental *shetar* not functioning the same way? The tenant holds a document saying he has ten years; the landlord says five have elapsed; the tenant should be able to wave the *shetar* and demand seven or eight more years the same way a borrower might demand that his document be honored in full.

The response distinguishes the two cases at the level of purpose. *Hatam shtar le-guvaina ka'ei* — there, a loan document exists specifically to serve as the instrument of collection. Everyone involved understands that the document will be used to extract the debt, and accordingly both parties know that if any payment occurs, it must be documented in a counter-instrument. *Im ita de-para ibayei leh limchtav agabei* — if the borrower actually paid fifty, he would insist on having "fifty paid" written on the face of the *shetar*, or he would demand a separate *shover*, precisely because he knows the *shetar* is the evidentiary weapon that will be used against him.

### Shetar Non-Extractive Pu…

In the rental context, the *shetar* does not function as an instrument of extraction. *Hai de-chtav lach shtara ki heichi de-lo tachzik alei* — the document is written so that the tenant cannot later claim *chazaka*, i.e., so that after occupying the property for years the tenant cannot argue he purchased it or that some transfer of ownership occurred. This connects directly to the laws of *chazaka* as developed in Bava Batra: a tenant occupying land for an extended period might otherwise generate a presumption of ownership, and the *shetar* forestalls that by clarifying the nature of the relationship.

Because the *shetar* is not an instrument designed to prove how many years of rent remain, it does not need to be rebutted with counter-documentation each time a year of use elapses. It does not carry that evidentiary function. The number of years elapsed is therefore simply unknown from the document, and we fall back on the basic principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: the *karka* remains in the possession of its owner, and it is the tenant who is making the claim of additional usage rights. The burden falls on the tenant.

## Loan Language Interpreta…

### Betuvo Indefinite Rights

Rav Nachman introduces a principle: *shoel adam betuvo le'olam* — if one says to a friend "lend me [your tool] *betuvo*," meaning "while it is still good" or "in its good condition," then the borrower acquires the right to use it for as long as it remains functional. The word *betuvo* is being read as a temporal parameter: lend it to me for however long it retains its functional integrity.

### Kinyan Sudar for Ongoing…

Rav Mari the son of Rav Shmuel adds a qualification: *vehu dekani minei* — this unlimited usage right applies only if a *kinyan sudar* was performed. Rashi explains that the distinction concerns the moment the object has been returned. So long as the borrower is still holding the object, his right to continued use is uncontested — the lending is ongoing. But once the object has been returned, if the borrower later wishes to assert a right to borrow it again by invoking the *betuvo* language, that ongoing right requires something more than the original verbal agreement; it requires a *kinyan sudar* to demonstrate a binding commitment extending beyond the first transaction.

### Return in Broken Conditi…

Rav Mari the son of Rav Ashi adds a further corollary: *umehadar lei katei* — when the borrower does eventually return the object under the *betuvo* arrangement, he returns it in its broken form. That is, he uses it until it breaks, and the moment of breakage is the moment of return. This follows directly from the logic of *betuvo*: the loan extends through the full functional life of the object, so there is nothing to return until that functionality is exhausted.

## Shovel Lending Specifici…

### Orchard Scope Limits

Rava introduces a parallel set of cases about scope of a lending agreement. If one says to a friend *oshlan mara lemirpak bah bei pardaisa* — "lend me a shovel to dig in *this* orchard" — then the shovel may only be used for that specific orchard. Specifying "this orchard" restricts the permission. If one says simply *pardaisa*, "an orchard," without specifying which one, then *rapik bei kol pardaisa deba'ei* — the borrower may use it for any orchard he wishes, without restriction as to which one, though only for a single orchard at a time. If one says *pardaisai*, "my orchards" in the plural possessive, then *rapik ve'azil kol pardaisa dait lei* — the borrower may dig in all the orchards the lender owns, even if they are located in different regions. The guiding logic is that singularity can be either restricting ("this specific one") or open-ended ("any one"), while the plural possessive expands the permission to cover everything the lender owns.

### Meita Machmat Melacha

In all these cases, the principle of *meta machmat melacha* applies: the borrower uses the shovel until it breaks from the work itself, at which point the obligation of the lending is fulfilled and the broken implement is returned.

## Well Lending Collapse

### Named vs. Abstract Well

Rav Papa addresses an analogous situation involving a well — specifically a *garguta*, a well fed by living water rather than a cistern. If one says *oshlan hai garguta* — "lend me *this* well" — and the well subsequently collapses, the borrower has no right to rebuild it. The specific named well was what was lent; once it no longer exists in that form, rebuilding would produce a new well, not a restoration of the original. But if one says simply *garguta*, "a well," without specifying which one, and it collapses, then *bani lei* — the borrower may rebuild it. The lender agreed to make available a well, not a specific one; the commitment remains in force, and rebuilding is within the scope of what was agreed.

### Area of Wells Rights

A third formulation is *bei garguta*, "this area of wells." In that case, *kari va'azil kama garguta be'ara ad demitrami lei* — the borrower may keep digging within the designated area until he finds water, even if the first well collapses and a second must be dug. The parameter is not the specific well nor even a single well in the abstract, but the geographic zone.

### Kinyan for Non-Possessed…

However, in all these cases where the lending right concerns something not yet physically in the borrower's possession — as in the area-of-wells scenario where the borrower might be digging a future well — *vetzarich lemikni minei*, a *kinyan sudar* is required to make the commitment legally binding. Without physical delivery of an existing object, a mere verbal agreement to allow access to a future or reconstructed well is only a promise, and a promise may be retracted. A *kinyan sudar* transforms the commitment into an enforceable right.

## Fallen House Rental

### Specific vs. Generic Hou…

The *gemara* now takes up the last *mishna* of the perek. The *mishna* states: if one rents a house to another and it falls down, the landlord is obligated to rebuild or replace it — *chayav le'ha'amid lo bayis*. Replacement must preserve the parameters of the original: if it was small it may not be made large; if large it may not be made small; one room may not become two, nor two become one; windows may not be reduced or added without mutual agreement.

The *gemara* asks: under what circumstances does the obligation to replace arise in the first place? If the landlord said *bayis zeh* — "this house" — and it fell, the contractual subject has ceased to exist. There is nothing left; the specific object of the agreement is gone. But if the landlord said *bayis stam* — simply "a house," without further specification — then the abstract commitment to provide a house survives the collapse of any particular building. This explains the obligation to replace. But the question then becomes: what constrains the *specifications* of the replacement? If all he said was "a house," why cannot the landlord fulfill the obligation by providing any house at all, perhaps larger or smaller than the original?

### House Like This Standard

Reish Lakish resolves this by explaining the operative language: *de'amar lei bayis she'ani maskir lecha mido kach ve'kach* — the landlord said "the house I am renting you has such-and-such dimensions," specifying the square footage. This specification, combined with the generic *bayis stam* formulation that creates the obligation to replace, produces the *mishna*'s ruling: you must provide a house of that square footage, no more and no less. The *gemara* initially objects that if the square footage was explicitly stated, it is self-evident that the replacement must match it. What new information is the *mishna* providing?

The *gemara* then offers a revised version from Ravin in the name of Reish Lakish: the operative language is *bayis kazeh ani maskir lecha* — "I am renting you a house *like this* one." The word *kazeh*, "like this," means the specific building is not the subject of the contract — hence the obligation survives its collapse — but the "like this" language binds the landlord to match its parameters. Again the *gemara* objects: if you say "like this," the dimensions are obviously implied. What is the novelty?

### River View Plus Footage

The *gemara* answers: *lo tzricha d'kaya guda d'nahara* — the case concerns a house situated on a riverbank, with a scenic view. The novelty concerns the interplay between the river view and the square footage. Rashi understands the *gemara*'s point as follows: one might have thought that replacing with a river-view property alone satisfies the *kazeh* obligation, without regard to square footage. The *mishna* therefore teaches that the river view is not sufficient by itself — matching square footage is also required. Both the view and the dimensions are part of what *kazeh* encompasses, and the landlord must provide a replacement that satisfies both parameters.

## Agricultural Tenancy

### Harvest vs. Uproot Pract…

With the beginning of the new perek, the *mishna* addresses the case of one who takes on a field from another — *ha-mekabel sadeh me-chavero*. Two arrangements are possible: a *chocher*, who pays a fixed annual rent and keeps all the produce, or an *arisa*, a sharecropper, who covers the working expenses and splits the harvest with the owner, the classic proportion being a quarter to the owner. The *mishna* rules that in a place where the practice is to harvest with a sickle — *liktzor* — leaving the stubble in the ground, the worker must do so; where the practice is to uproot entirely — *la'akor* — pulling out the roots, that must be done instead. Everything follows *minhag hamedinah*.

The accompanying *baraita* elaborates: *u'shneihem me'akvin zeh al zeh* — and both parties may object to a deviation from the local practice. Initially this might seem redundant — if you have already stated that one is *eino rashai* to change, it is obvious that the other party can object. The *gemara* clarifies that the double formulation is *ma ta'am ka'amar* — it is explanatory. The reason deviation is forbidden is precisely because *shneihem me'akvin zeh al zeh* — each side has a legitimate interest that runs in opposite directions.

If the practice is to harvest and the landowner wants to insist on uprooting, he can say *beina d'titban li ara'ai* — I want the stubble left on the ground to feed my animals. The worker resists uprooting because it is more labor-intensive. Conversely, if the practice is to uproot and the worker pushes to merely harvest, the landowner can say *beina d'tinakeir ara'ai* — I want my land fully cleared. And the worker, as sharecropper or renter who is entitled to the straw, can say *beina tivna* — I want the uprooting precisely because it yields me the straw that I am entitled to. Both parties have genuine competing interests, and therefore neither may unilaterally override the *minhag*.

### Plowing After Harvest

The *mishna* further states that if the local practice is to plow after the harvest, one must do so. This seems self-evident given the general principle of following *minhag hamedinah*, and the *gemara* notes the objection: *pshita*. The answer is that the case is not obvious. It addresses a location where the standard practice is *not* to weed by hand throughout the growing season but only to plow afterward. Yet the worker in this case went ahead and weeded by hand throughout, putting in considerable extra effort. He then argues: the whole reason I weeded by hand during the season was so that I would not have to plow at the end. The *gemara* rules against this. If the worker wanted to substitute his hand-weeding for the customary final plowing, he needed to state this explicitly at the outset as a condition of the arrangement. *De'ibei lei lefarushei lei* — he was required to stipulate it clearly. One cannot unilaterally substitute an alternative practice and then retroactively claim it was meant to discharge the standard obligation. Standard practice governs unless explicitly modified in advance.

### Tree Rights Allocation

The *mishna*'s phrase *hakol ke-minhag hamedinah*, "everything follows local custom," is understood by the *gemara* as a broad inclusive formulation, bringing in an additional *baraisa*: if the local practice in a region is that trees growing in a field go along with the rental or sharecropping agreement — meaning the person working the land gets the benefit of the fruit — then that applies. If the practice is that trees are excluded and the owner retains the fruit, then that applies instead.

### Explicit Stipulation Ove…

What is not self-evident is when one party has deviated from the standard split in the percentage arrangement, and argues that the deviation implicitly modified the tree rights. In the first scenario: the norm is that the owner takes a third as *arisa* (sharecropper's percentage to the owner), but the owner agreed to take only a quarter. Can the owner later argue that the reduction in his percentage was *implicitly conditional* on receiving the tree fruit — i.e., that since the practice *includes* tree rights for the worker, his reduced percentage was compensation for giving that up? The *gemara* rules *de'ibei lei lefarushei lei* — if that was the owner's intent, he needed to say so explicitly. He cannot retroactively argue that his below-standard percentage was conditioned on retaining the trees. The *minhag* controls and the worker gets the trees unless explicitly excluded.

The reverse case is equally instructive: the norm is the owner takes a quarter, but the sharecropper agreed to give the owner a third — more than standard. The sharecropper argues that his above-standard payment was made on the assumption he would receive the tree fruit, and since the local practice *excludes* tree fruit from the worker's share, he implicitly negotiated extra compensation for that loss. Again the *gemara* rules that without explicit stipulation, the argument fails. *Minhag hamedinah* simply prevails, and if any deviation was intended as compensation for something else, it had to be spelled out.

### Straw Division Practice

Returning to the *mishna*: just as the grain is divided between owner and sharecropper, so too the straw and stubble, since these also have value as animal fodder. Rav Yosef reports that in Babylonia, *nahug dela yahvi tivna le'arisa* — the accepted practice was that the sharecropper does *not* receive the straw. This is not a change in *halacha*, since the *mishna* itself establishes that everything follows *minhag hamedinah*, and Babylonian practice differed.

The *gemara* asks why this needs to be stated — we already know that local custom governs, so we would investigate Babylonian practice whenever relevant. The answer is that occasionally one finds individuals who act with unusual generosity and give the straw to the sharecropper even in Babylon. A sharecropper might look at his neighbor's arrangement and argue that since it is done elsewhere, perhaps he too is entitled. Rav Yosef's statement makes clear that such generosity is an *ayin yafah* — a generous gesture outside the norm — and does not reflect the sharecropper's legal entitlement. The baseline Babylonian *minhag* is that the straw stays with the owner.

### Field Protection Division

Rav Yosef proceeds to delineate the division of protective structures around the field. He lists *buchra*, a first mound of earth; *tifsa*, a second mound built adjacent to it; *arkavata*, a third mound; and *kanei de-hizra*, the stakes used for a fence and the strings woven around them — all of these are the obligation of the *ba'al habayis*, the landowner. The landowner is responsible for the basic protective infrastructure of the field itself.

However, *hizra gufeh*, the actual thorns woven into the fence strings to create something analogous to barbed wire, providing the enhanced protective deterrent beyond the basic structure, is the obligation of the *arisa*, the sharecropper. The underlying principle is: *kol ikar balma de-ba'al ha-bayit, netiruta yetirata de'arisa* — the foundational protection of the land is on the owner; extra measures that go beyond basic protection and serve also to protect the crops are on the sharecropper, who benefits directly from those crops.

Rav Yosef continues: a hoe, a manure shovel, a water-drawing bucket, and a water flask are all the responsibility of the *ba'al habayis* — the owner provides the equipment. The sharecropper, however, is responsible for the labor of digging the irrigation canals from the main river to the field itself. The general pattern emerges: the owner provides the physical infrastructure and equipment; the sharecropper provides the agricultural labor and enhanced materials that benefit the crops directly.

### Vine Trellis Sharing

The *mishna* states that just as owner and sharecropper divide the wine from the vineyard, they also divide the *zimorot*, the vine prunings, and the *kanim*. A *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yannai explains that *kanim* here refers to the split rods used to construct the trellises on which the vines grow — *kanim ha-mechulakim she-bahen ma'amidim et ha-gefanim*. The *mishna* further states *u'shneihem maspikin et ha-kanim* — both parties jointly supply the wood for the trellises.

The *gemara* asks why this needs to be stated separately; both the supply rule and the division rule seem self-evident. The answer is that the two clauses explain each other. The reason both parties divide the wood of the trellises at the end of the season is *mishum de-shneihem maspikin et ha-kanim* — because both parties supplied it during the season. It is the joint investment that generates the joint division, and the *mishna* is making that causal relationship explicit.

## Well and Tree Drying

### Unnamed Features No Redu…

The final *mishna* of the daf addresses a sharecropper or renter who takes on a field that contains a well or trees — features that benefit the person working the land. The well reduces the need to bring water from afar; the trees yield produce the worker is entitled to without having to cultivate them. The *mishna* rules: if the well dries up or the tree dies, *eino menake lo min chikuro* — the renter may not demand a reduction in rent. Taking on the field with these features was his good fortune; losing them is his bad luck. The original rental figure stands.

### Named Features Allow Red…

However, if the renter specifically named these features in the agreement — *im amar lo chor li sadeh beit ha-shlachin zu*, "rent me *this* field, the one that has a well," or *sadeh beit ha-ilan zu*, "this one, the one with the tree" — then the situation changes. By naming these specific features, he demonstrated that they were material to his decision to pay the agreed-upon rent or accept the agreed-upon sharecropping percentage. Their presence was a condition, not merely incidental. Therefore, if the well dries up or the tree dies, *menake lo min chikuro* — he is entitled to a reduction in what he owes. The explicit identification of the feature in the agreement converts it from a background circumstance into a term of the contract, and its disappearance entitles the injured party to renegotiate the price.
