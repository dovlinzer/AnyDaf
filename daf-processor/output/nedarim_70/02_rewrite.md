# Nedarim 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father & Groom Partnersh…

### Father's Death

The present *mishna* returns to the fundamental dynamic of the partnership between a father and an *arus* in annulling vows, after the preceding discussion of *hafarah* and *hakamah* performed in sequence. The *mishna* states: *meit av, lo nisroknah reshut l'baal* — if the father dies, his authority does not transfer to the husband. The underlying principle is that the father is the anchor of the entire annulment process. Even if the father has already performed his portion of the *hafarah*, the *arus* cannot complete it on his own, because the *hafarah* is fundamentally grounded in the father's presence and participation. Without the father, the process cannot be concluded.

### Groom's Death

The converse, however, obtains in the opposite direction: *meis habaal, nisroknah reshut l'av* — if the *arus* dies, his authority transfers back to the father. Whether the *arus* had already annulled his portion or had not yet done so, the father may now either complete the annulment or perform it in its entirety. The one exception is if the *arus* had performed *hakamah* before dying — in that case, the vow is confirmed and nothing further can be done. The asymmetry is telling: the father can absorb the *arus*'s role, but the *arus* cannot absorb the father's, because at bottom it is the father who is the principal actor.

### Power Comparison

The *mishna* then draws the comparison explicitly: *b'zeh yafeh koach ha'av mikoach habaal* — in this respect, the power of the father exceeds that of the *arus*. But then the *mishna* turns in a surprising direction: *b'davar acher yafeh koach habaal mikoach ha'av* — in another matter, the power of the *arus* exceeds that of the father. In what respect? *Shehabaal meifer b'bogeres, v'av eino meifer b'bogeres* — the *arus* can annul the vow of a *bogeres* (a young woman who has passed twelve and a half years of age), whereas the father cannot. The father's inability is readily understandable: once she becomes a *bogeres*, she is no longer *bi'neureiha beis aviha*, no longer subject to her father's household authority. But why does the *arus* have this power? We have been emphasizing throughout that the *arus* has no independent authority of his own — his power derives entirely from piggybacking on the father. If there is no father in the picture, either because she is a *bogeres* or because she is a *yesomah*, the *arus* should have no standing to annul. This question will be addressed momentarily.

### Verse Derivation

The *gemara* begins by asking the source for the rule that when the father dies, the *arus* cannot complete the annulment. The answer is the verse *bi'neureiha beis aviha* — in her youth, in her father's house. This phrase establishes that she is fundamentally located in her father's domain; the *arus* is an adjunct to that arrangement, not its foundation. Consequently, even if the father performed his part and then died, his absence renders the entire process incomplete.

On the other side, the *gemara* asks: from where do we know that when the *arus* dies, the father may take over even the *arus*'s share — not merely complete his own portion, but absorb the *arus*'s role entirely? Rabba answers from the verse *ve'im hayo tihyeh l'ish u'nedareiha aleiha* — "and if she shall come to be married to a man, and her vows are upon her." The doubling of *hayo tihyeh* — "having been" and "shall be" — is read as encompassing the case of successive marriages: she was betrothed to a first man, he died, and now she is to be betrothed to a second. The *gemara* derives from this that the father's rights before the second betrothal (*kodem havayah sheniyah*) are equivalent to his rights before the first betrothal (*kodem havayah rishonah*). Before groom number one appeared, the father had complete and sole authority to annul. So too, in the interval between groom number one's death and groom number two's arrival, the father's authority is once again complete.

### Rights Transfer

The *gemara* immediately presses this derivation with an objection. The analogy holds cleanly for vows that were never under the *arus*'s auspices — vows made after groom number one died, or vows he simply never heard of. Before groom number one entered the picture, the father's authority was unlimited and unencumbered. If the parallel is to vows equally free of prior entanglement, then of course the father's authority is complete in that parallel period. But what of vows that groom number one actually heard? In that situation, some residual dimension of the *arus*'s authority had attached to those vows. How do we know that, upon his death, it is specifically the father who inherits that dimension of authority?

The *gemara* resolves this elegantly. If the *pasuk* were needed only to tell us that vows the *arus* never heard revert to the father, it would be unnecessary — that ruling already follows from *bi'neureiha beis aviha* alone. A betrothed girl whose *arus* dies without having heard the vow remains *beis aviha* in every relevant sense, and the father's authority is self-evident. The very fact that the *pasuk* is needed to teach something implies it is teaching something non-obvious: namely, that even vows the *arus* had heard and over which he had authority revert to the father upon his death. The father is empowered to annul them in full.

## Groom Heard Vow

### Analogy Challenge

With the principle now established that the father's authority is restored upon the *arus*'s death even regarding vows the *arus* had heard, the *gemara* examines the analogy underpinning this ruling more carefully. The *hayo tihyeh* derivation equates the father's position before the second betrothal with his position before the first. But this analogy is most naturally read as applying to vows that were never under the first *arus*'s authority — since before the first betrothal, no *arus* had ever been in the picture at all. Does the analogy genuinely extend to vows that passed through the first *arus*'s *reshut*?

### Case Distinction

The *gemara* therefore entertains the possibility that the analogy should be read narrowly: *eima hanei milei bi-nedarim she-lo nir'u la-arus* — perhaps the father's restored authority applies only to vows that the first *arus* never heard. In those cases, there is a clean structural parallel: before groom number one, the father had full authority over vows made without any *arus* involvement; before groom number two, he has full authority over vows equally unrelated to groom number one. The cases are genuinely analogous.

But what of vows that the first *arus* did hear, over which he had some degree of control? One might argue that those vows carry a residual character different from vows that were always purely within the father's domain, and that the death of the first *arus* does not straightforwardly restore the father's authority over that peculiar category.

### Groom's Residual Rights

The *gemara* dismisses this concern, and its reasoning is important. If the *pasuk* were only needed to cover vows that the *arus* never heard, it would be superfluous — that ruling is already derivable from *bi'neureiha beis aviha* without any additional verse. A girl whose *arus* dies and whose vows he never heard about is simply back to being fully in her father's house; no new derivation is required. The *pasuk* must therefore be addressing a case not already covered — namely, vows the *arus* had heard and over which he had exercised some authority. The ruling is that even for those vows, the father's authority is restored in full upon the *arus*'s death.

This reinforces the conceptual framework that has been building throughout: the *arus*'s participation in the annulment process is not a free-standing right but a conditional one, wholly dependent on the father's continued presence and authority. When the *arus* dies, there is no independent *reshut* of his that persists or passes to another party independently — it simply dissolves back into the father's comprehensive authority.

## Bogeres Anomaly

### Groom > Father

We turn now to the second half of the *mishna*'s comparison: the case in which the *arus* has greater power than the father. The *mishna* states that the *arus* can annul the vow of a *bogeres* while the father cannot. This is genuinely puzzling. We have established that the *arus* has no independent *reshut* — his power is entirely derivative of the father's. How, then, can he have any authority in a case where the father has none?

### No Father Present

The *gemara* first considers the possibility that the case involves a woman who was betrothed while already a *bogeres* — that is, the father was never in the picture to begin with. If there was no father present at the time of the *erusin*, perhaps the *arus*'s authority does not need to piggyback on anything and stands on its own.

### Groom's Lack of Power

The *gemara* rejects this attempted resolution, and it should, because the logic does not hold. The absence of the father from the picture does not generate any new authority in the *arus*. We have established that even in cases where the father is gone — whether because he died or because she was always a *yesomah* — the *arus* as such has no power to annul vows. His authority arises only in conjunction with the father's. Simply removing the father does not fill the vacuum with *arus* authority; it leaves a vacuum. So the question remains: what is the source of the *arus*'s power over a *bogeres*?

## Later Mishna Reference

### 73b Mishna

To resolve the *bogeres* anomaly, the *gemara* points to a later *mishna* on 73b. The principle embedded in the current *mishna* is, in effect, borrowed from that later context, and understanding the later *mishna* is necessary to make sense of what appears here.

### R. Eliezer's View

The *mishna* on 73b addresses the case of a *bogeres* who waited twelve months after betrothal, or a widow who waited thirty days. Rabbi Eliezer rules: *hoil u'baal chayav bi'mezonoseha, yafeir* — since the husband is already obligated to provide for her sustenance, he may annul her vows. Rabbi Eliezer's position is that the arrival of the time designated for *nisuin* — the point at which the husband becomes financially responsible for her even though the marriage has not yet technically been consummated — is sufficient to grant him the annulment authority of a husband.

### Financial Obligation

The reasoning underlying Rabbi Eliezer's position, as the *gemara* explains it, is that once the husband has begun bearing the financial obligations of marriage, and the expected time of *nisuin* has arrived, the woman's vows are implicitly made with an understanding that her husband's acceptance is a condition of their binding force. He is now in a relationship of financial responsibility toward her, she knows she is about to enter *nisuin*, and in that context her vows carry an implicit *tnai* of spousal consent. Rabbi Eliezer is therefore prepared to treat her as effectively *nesuah* for purposes of *hafarah* from the moment those conditions obtain.

### Chakhamim Disagree

The *chakhamim* reject this position: *ein habaal meifer ad shetikanes lireshuto* — the husband does not have annulment power until she has actually entered into his domain through *nisuin*. Financial obligation alone, however significant, does not substitute for the formal entry into the marital relationship. On this view, the *arus* has no authority as *arus* regardless of how long the betrothal has persisted, and the arrival of the expected time of *nisuin* changes nothing legally.

## Mishna's Purpose

### Teaching Location

This raises the question: why does our *mishna* on 70a mention the *bogeres* case at all, given that the fuller treatment — including the time periods, the case of the *almana*, and the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chakhamim* — belongs to the *mishna* on 73b? The *gemara* notes the apparent redundancy: *hasnina chada zimna* — we have taught this once already (or will teach it in its proper place), so why mention it here?

Before answering, the *gemara* first refines what the *bogeres* case in the later *mishna* actually means. The later *mishna* mentions a *bogeres* who waited twelve months. But the *gemara* objects: twelve months is the waiting period applicable to a *katana* or *na'arah* — a girl under twelve and a half. By a *bogeres*, the time of *nisuin* is expected to arrive much sooner, within thirty days. Why, then, does the later *mishna* attach the twelve-month figure to the *bogeres*? The *gemara* resolves this by reading the two conditions disjunctively: either she is a *bogeres* — in which case thirty days suffice — or she is a *katana* or *na'arah* who has waited the twelve months. The *mishna* on 73b is listing two parallel paths to the same legal threshold, not applying twelve months to the *bogeres* specifically.

### Parallel Structure

The *gemara* then offers two possible answers to the question of why the *bogeres* is mentioned in our *mishna* here rather than only in its natural location. The first answer is *ee ba'is eima hacha davka*: here in our *mishna* the mention of the *bogeres* is in fact the primary teaching, offered in a compressed form, and the later *mishna* on 73b then elaborates with the full details — the time periods, the *almana*, the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chakhamim* — that clarify precisely when this rule applies and according to whom. The second answer inverts this: *ve-ee ba'is eima*, the real and proper location for teaching the *bogeres* law is the later *mishna* on 73b, where the full discussion naturally belongs. Our *mishna* mentions it only incidentally — *ve-aydi de-nasiv reisha bazeh nasiv nami seifa bazeh* — because our *mishna* was structured around the phrase *bazeh yafeh koach ha'av mikoach habaal*, and the redactor wanted a parallel clause in the form *bazeh yafeh koach habaal mikoach ha'av*. To supply that parallel, our *mishna* borrowed the *bogeres* case from the later *mishna*, which is its true home.

### Conceptual Summary

The conceptual upshot must not be lost in these structural observations. The *arus* by himself has no independent power whatsoever. Even if the father is dead, even if she is a *bogeres*, even if he is the only man in her life — as *arus*, he has no *reshut* to annul her vows. His authority exists only as a piggyback on the father's authority. The single exception — and it is Rabbi Eliezer's position, disputed by the *chakhamim* — is when the time of *nisuin* has arrived. At that point, Rabbi Eliezer is prepared to treat her as effectively *nesuah* and to grant the husband full annulment power. A question that will require attention when the *mishna* on 73b is reached directly: if a *katana* reaches the time of *nisuin* according to Rabbi Eliezer, does the father still participate? When it is a *bogeres* with no father in the picture, the husband's full power at the time of *nisuin* is clean and unencumbered. But where the father is still legally present — because she has not reached *bagrus* — does Rabbi Eliezer's grant of husband-like power to the *arus* operate alongside or in place of the father's role? That question remains open.

## Successive Betrothals

### Multiple Divorces

The *gemara* now introduces a new scenario that pushes the logic of the father's authority further. A woman makes a *neder*, is betrothed (*arusa*), then divorced and re-betrothed — and this happens multiple times, even up to a hundred times in a single day (*bo bayom*). The stipulation that everything occurs on the same day is not incidental. Someone — either the father or the first *arus* — has heard the vow, the twenty-four-hour window has begun, and all the subsequent betrothals and divorces occur within that same window before the clock expires on whoever has not yet performed the annulment.

### Last Groom Powers

The *halakha* stated in the *mishna* is: *afilu l'meiah, aviha u'va'alah ha'acharon mefirin nedareha* — even if there were a hundred such betrothals and divorces, it is the father and the last *arus* together who annul her vows. The last *arus* in the chain is the one who participates in the annulment process, not any of the intermediate ones. And crucially, the last *arus* participates in annulling vows over which he was never directly a party — vows that groom number one may well have heard, over which groom number one had some authority, and which passed through potentially dozens of subsequent betrothals before reaching him.

### Father-Focused Logic

This result is, on reflection, powerful evidence that the entire framework is father-centered. What does groom number ten have to do with vows she made under groom number one? Nothing, in any direct sense. But viewed through the lens we have been developing, the answer becomes clear. Those vows were never really under groom number one's authority in any primary sense — they were always fundamentally under the father's authority. The *arus* at any stage is simply the individual who happens to be betrothed to her at the time of the annulment and who therefore joins the father in the process. Whether it is groom number one or groom number ten is irrelevant to the structure, because neither groom is the foundation; the father is the foundation. Groom number ten joins the father in the annulment the same way groom number one would have — not because he has any special connection to those vows, but because he is the current *arus* piggybacking on the father's authority.

### Continuous Control

The *mishna* then articulates the governing principle: *zeh haklal, kol she-lo yatz'ah li-reshut atzmah sha'ah achat* — the rule is that as long as she has never attained independent status for even a moment, the father retains continuous control. What would constitute independence for such a moment? Entry into *nisuin* followed by divorce or the husband's death; the death of the father himself; or the attainment of *bagrus*. If none of these has occurred — if she has passed through an unbroken chain of betrothal, divorce, and re-betrothal without ever exiting the father's domain — then *aviha u'va'al acharon mefirin nedareha*: the father and the current *arus* together annul her vows, regardless of how many intermediate *arusim* there have been.

## Verse Derivation

### Derivation Question

Having established the *halakha*, the *gemara* asks the obvious question: what is the textual source for the rule that the last *arus* can annul vows that the first *arus* heard? There is a significant distinction to draw here. We know from the earlier discussion that after groom number one dies and the father's authority is restored, the father can annul even vows groom number one had heard — that was derived from *bi'neureiha beis aviha* and the *pasuk*'s implicit teaching about the non-obvious case. But how do we know that a subsequent *arus* — not the father, but a new *arus* — gains the power to participate in annulling vows specifically heard by the first *arus*? The word *nir'u* ("were seen," i.e., came under the authority of) is used deliberately: once vows were presented to and heard by the first *arus*, they carry a distinct character. How does the second *arus* acquire authority over them?

### Shmuel's Derivation

Shmuel answers from the verse: *ve'im hayo tihyeh l'ish u'nedareiha aleiha* — "and if she shall have been married to a man, and her vows are upon her." The phrase *nedareiha aleiha* — "her vows upon her" — refers to vows she already carries with her. The *hayo tihyeh* construction, as established earlier, encompasses the case of successive marriages. Shmuel reads *nedareiha aleiha* in conjunction with *tihyeh*: the vows that were upon her from the first marriage (*hayo*) follow her into the second (*tihyeh*), and the second husband acquires authority over them.

### Word 'Aleha'

The *gemara* raises the objection that perhaps this derivation applies only to vows that were never under the first *arus*'s auspices — that is, the *hayo tihyeh* teaching addresses the transfer of vows the father controlled, not vows that had passed through the first *arus*'s *reshut*. The *gemara* responds: *aleha kra yeseira* — the word *aleha* is superfluous and carries additional legal weight. *Nedareiha aleiha* means "her vows, whatever was upon her" — in whatever state they existed, regardless of whether the first *arus* had heard them or not. She carries those vows into the new marriage, and the new *arus* acquires full authority over them as part of the joint annulment process with the father.

## Timing Questions

### Father's Clock Start

The *gemara* now identifies several unresolved timing questions that the foregoing analysis leaves open. The first concerns the father's twenty-four-hour window. When groom number one hears the vow, his twenty-four-hour clock begins. If he then dies and the father's authority is restored — including over the vows groom number one heard — does the father's own clock begin from the moment groom number one first heard the vow, or does it begin only from the moment the father himself becomes aware of the vow, which may be some hours after the *arus*'s death?

### Later Groom's Clock

A parallel question arises for the later *arus*. When groom number two or groom number ten enters the picture, from what point does his twenty-four-hour window run? Does it begin from when groom number one originally heard the vow — since the *hayo tihyeh* derivation in effect equates the second *arus*'s position with the first's — or does it begin only from when the later *arus* himself first hears about the vow?

### 24-Hour Definition

There is also the prior definitional question of what exactly the *mishna* means by the annulment window. The verse speaks of *miyom el yom* — "from day to day" — which implies a twenty-four-hour period. But the precise parameters of this window, and whether "day" means a calendrical day ending at nightfall or a full twenty-four-hour unit measured from the time of hearing, remains to be defined as the *gemara* continues. These questions are left open for subsequent discussion.
