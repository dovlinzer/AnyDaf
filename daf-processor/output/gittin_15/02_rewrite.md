# Gittin 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Holech Status Review

### Debt vs Gift Distinction

Two important points require clarification from the previous shiur. The first concerns the distinction between the two *sugyot* on the *daf*. On *amud aleph*, the *gemara* assumed that *holech* is *k'zachi* — that when a messenger conveys money on behalf of the sender, the recipient effectively acquires it at the moment it is dispatched. The scenario there involved paying back a debt or returning a *pikadon*, and the question was whether the concept of *zachin* — acquiring on behalf of another — operated sufficiently to prevent the sender from retracting, even if not yet fully discharging his obligation.

The *sugya* now under discussion, however, deals with a case of a one-sided gift, and here the *gemara* pushes firmly in the opposite direction: *holech* is *lav k'zachi*. Unless the giver is a *shchiv mera* — someone on his deathbed — the messenger's conveyance does not immediately transfer ownership to the recipient, and if the recipient is found to have died, the money reverts to the sender.

### Tosafos on Debt/Gift

This apparent tension — that *holech* is *k'zachi* in one *sugya* and *lav k'zachi* in the next — is addressed by *Tosafos* several *daf* earlier. *Tosafos* explains that the distinction tracks the nature of the transaction. When the sender owes money to the recipient, there is a stronger sense of legal and moral commitment to ensuring the money reaches its destination. Whether because psychologically the sender is more deeply invested in delivering what is owed, or because the legal framework treats money that is genuinely owed as already conceptually belonging to the other side, we interpret *holech* as *k'zachi* in the debt context. In the case of a pure gift, however, where the other side has no preexisting claim on the money, we interpret *holech* with more reservation, as *lav k'zachi*, and the money does not transfer until actually received.

### Shchiv Mera Conditions

The second point requiring clarification involves *shchiv mera* cases. The *sugya* assumes that even where the giver is a *shchiv mera* — which would ordinarily cause the gift to take effect — the recipient must have been alive at the moment the gift was given. The question this raises is pointed: the words of a *shchiv mera* take effect only at the moment of his death, not when he speaks them. So even if the recipient was alive when the gift was declared, if he died before the *shchiv mera* himself died, the gift should already be nullified, since it had not yet legally taken effect.

*Tosafos* on 14b raises this question explicitly. The *Ri* answers that what matters is the intention of the *shchiv mera* at the moment of giving: *dedaas nosen hu d'keivan sheyeshno l'mekabel b'shas masnos* — since the giver's intent was conditioned on the recipient's existence at the time of the gift declaration, the *shchiv mera* meant his bequest to reach either the recipient or his heirs: *sheyizke mekabel achar misaso o hu o yarshav*. The *Rosh*, however, leaves the question as a *tzarich iyun*, and the answer remains less than fully satisfying. The point is flagged not because it is resolved, but because it is a genuine difficulty in the *sugya* that the *rishonim* grapple with and that should not pass without acknowledgment.

## Gift-Giving Tannaic Deba…

### Baraita Setup

The *gemara* now asks *leima holech k'zachi tanna'ei* — perhaps the question of whether *holech* is *k'zachi* is itself a *tannaic* debate. This is essentially a *chazarah* of the ground covered in the previous discussion, but applied now to the more contested domain of gift-giving.

The *gemara* introduces a *baraita*: if a sender says *holech mane l'ploni* — convey this hundred dollars to so-and-so as a gift — and the messenger goes and cannot locate the recipient because he has died, the question becomes: what is done with the money?

### Competing Positions

The *baraita* presents multiple opinions. *Tanna kamma* says *yachzeru l'mishaleach* — return it to the sender. If the sender himself has since died, *Rabbi Natan* and *Rabbi Yaakov* say it goes to the heirs of the sender, which is functionally the same ruling: the recipient never acquired it. *Yesh omrim*, by contrast, says it goes to the heirs of the recipient — *l'yorshei mi shenishtalach lo* — which implies that the recipient had in fact acquired the money when it was dispatched.

Mapping these positions onto the *holech* question: *tanna kamma*, *Rabbi Natan*, and *Rabbi Yaakov* all hold *holech lav k'zachi* — the recipient does not acquire at the moment of dispatch — and so the money remains in the sender's domain and reverts accordingly. *Yesh omrim*, by saying it goes to the recipient's heirs, implicitly holds *holech k'zachi* — the recipient acquired the moment the messenger set off.

*Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* then adds a further position, citing *Rabbi Yaakov* in the name of *Rabbi Meir*: *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet* — there is an obligation to fulfill the wishes of the deceased. Importantly, this formulation presupposes *holech lav k'zachi* — the other side does not automatically own the money — but once the sender has died, a separate principle creates a moral-legal obligation to honor what he wished. The outcome resembles that of *yesh omrim* but differs fundamentally in mechanism: it is not that the recipient owns the money by right, but that the fulfillment of the deceased's wishes creates an imperative to deliver it.

*Tosafos* notes that *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet* does not apply universally whenever someone expresses a wish before dying. It applies particularly in situations like this, where money was already placed in the hands of an agent for a specific purpose and the person who designated it subsequently died. It would not apply, for instance, to money still sitting in the deceased's account with no specific instruction attached. Additionally, *mitzvah l'kayem* differs from *shchiv mera* in a further respect: *shchiv mera* operates automatically and takes legal effect at death; *mitzvah l'kayem* creates an obligation to act, but does not effect a legal transfer by itself.

*Chachamim omrim yachloku* — the *chachamim* say to divide the money — reflecting their uncertainty about which rule governs. A further opinion, attributed to *Ben Batera*, says *kol mah sheyirtzeh shaliach ya'aseh* — let the messenger do as he sees fit.

The question of what *shuda* means in this context is itself debated. *Rashi* explains that *shuda d'dayni* means the *dayan* should exercise his best judgment — assessing what the sender would have wished, based on his knowledge of the relevant relationships and circumstances. *Rabbeinu Tam* in *Tosafos* objects that where there is genuinely nothing to go on, such an assessment is impossible. *Tosafos* therefore reads the phrase more broadly: *ela yehe hadayan l'mi sheyirtze* — the *dayan* may simply decide as he wishes, perhaps in favor of the needier party, or on some other equitable basis. This opens a genuinely interesting range of possibilities: does decision-making in a legal vacuum revert to the sender's intent, to policy, or to something essentially arbitrary?

One further structural question arises here. When the *shaliach* holds the money and we do not know whose domain it belongs to, there is no obvious *chezkat mamon* to fall back on in the usual sense. *Tosafos* notes that the general rule — *kol teiku shebaShas*, the *Gemara*'s unresolved questions in monetary matters, is resolved by splitting — applies only where the *gemara* explicitly states *yachloku*. Otherwise, the default rule of *haMotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* would apply. Here, since we genuinely cannot determine which party the *shaliach* is holding for, splitting is eminently sensible.

### Precedent Case

*Rabbi Shimon ben Nansi* then reports a practical precedent: *oy bi hayah ma'aseh* — I had a case just like this, and the ruling came down in favor of the heirs of the sender, consistent with the first position.

The *gemara* asks whether this precedent case is being cited to indicate that *Rabbi Shimon ben Nansi* holds *holech lav k'zachi* — with the money reverting to the sender — or whether it simply constitutes a legal story without *halachic* weight. This opens a broader question: *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* — is this figure a *nasi*, or is the attribution *Rabbi Shimon* who says it in the name of *the nasi*? *Tosafos* notes that there is no known *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* in the tradition, making the attribution puzzling. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

The deeper issue is whether all the positions in this *baraita* represent a genuine *tannaitic* debate about *holech k'zachi* versus *lav k'zachi*.

### Healthy Giver Rule

The *gemara* answers: *lo* — this is not a debate about *holech k'zachi*. *B'bari d'kulei alma lo peligi* — if the giver was healthy, all opinions agree: *holech lav k'zachi*, and the money reverts to the sender. The *gemara* resolves the apparent plurality of positions by relocating the entire debate to a different axis.

### Shchiv Mera Exception

The *baraita* is speaking throughout of a *shchiv mera* case — the evidence being that in the scenario, the sender dies. *Yesh omrim*, which said the recipient's heirs receive the money, was not asserting *holech k'zachi*; rather, it was applying the rule that the words of a *shchiv mera* take automatic legal effect. The positions that deny the recipient his money are not denying *holech k'zachi* either — they are denying that *divrei shchiv mera* are effective at all, essentially holding like *Rabbi Eliezer* in the dispute cited from elsewhere: that a *shchiv mera* must still perform a formal *kinyan* for his gift to take effect, and mere words are insufficient.

This resolution is frankly convoluted, but its purpose is clear: the *gemara* is deeply committed to avoiding any acknowledgment that a *tanna* holds *holech k'zachi* by a gift. The elaborateness of the resolution reflects how strongly the *gemara* resists that conclusion.

*Tanna kamma*, then, holds like *Rabbi Eliezer* — *holech lav k'zachi* and *shchiv mera* is ineffective without a formal *kinyan*. *Yesh omrim* holds like the *rabbanan* — *shchiv mera*'s words are automatically effective. *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi*, citing *Rabbi Meir*, holds like *Rabbi Eliezer* on the *shchiv mera* question, but introduces *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet* once the sender has died. *Chachamim* are uncertain. The position allowing the *shaliach* to decide represents a *safek* between splitting and *shuda d'dayni*.

The practical bottom line is this: the *gemara*'s determined effort to neutralize every reading that would support *holech k'zachi* by a gift strongly implies that the *halachic* consensus runs against it. By a *matanah*, *holech lav k'zachi*.

## Amoraic Refinements

### Rav Yosef's Ruling

The *gemara* states: *gufa amar Rav Yosef halacha Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* — the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi*. *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* ruled that the money reverts to the sender, which aligns with *holech lav k'zachi*. This is straightforward as far as a healthy giver is concerned.

### Deceased Giver Obligation

The complication arises immediately: *v'ka'imalan divrei shchiv mera kechisuvin u'kmsurin damu* — we also hold that the words of a *shchiv mera* are treated as though written and delivered. If that is the case, how can we rule that the money reverts to the sender when the giver was a *shchiv mera*? *Rav Yosef* therefore interprets the ruling of *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* as applying to a *bari* — a healthy person — not to a *shchiv mera*. This limits the ruling's scope but preserves its coherence.

### Reconciling Principles

A further tension remains: *v'ka'imalan mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet* — we also hold that there is an obligation to fulfill the wishes of the deceased. So when *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* rules that the money returns to the sender, does that mean even if the sender subsequently dies, his heirs may keep it and ignore his expressed wish to give the money away?

The *gemara* resolves this by limiting the ruling: *tani yachziru l'meshaleach* — when *Rabbi Shimon HaNasi* says it returns to the sender, he means to the sender while alive. Once the sender dies, the principle of *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet* takes effect, and the money should be delivered to the recipient or the recipient's heirs. The *pesak* thus integrates both principles: *holech lav k'zachi* in the ownership sense, so that the money never automatically passes to the recipient; but *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet* applies once the sender dies, creating an imperative — though not a legal transfer — to honor his wishes.

This means that as long as the *shaliach* holds the money, it remains entirely within the *meshaleach*'s legal domain. If the recipient dies, the money returns to the sender, for *holech lav k'zachi* and no ownership ever transferred. If the sender dies while the money is in transit, his heirs inherit it — but should, by dint of *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet*, deliver it to the recipient or the recipient's heirs. In neither case does ownership vest in the recipient prior to actual receipt. The broader conceptual richness of this *sugya* — touching on *zachin l'adam shelo b'fanav*, *divrei shchiv mera*, *mitzvah l'kayem divrei hamet*, and *holech k'zachi* — illustrates why *Gittin* has long been a tractate beloved in the *yeshivos* for precisely these kinds of expansive conceptual discussions.

## B'Fanai Nichtav/Nechtam

### Rava's Framework

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah* governing the *shaliach* who brings a *get* from overseas. The *mishnah* requires the *shaliach* to declare *b'fanai nichtav u'v'fanai nechtam* — it was written in front of me and it was signed in front of me. This declaration is a *takkanah d'rabbanan* — a rabbinic enactment — and it is striking how much of the opening *prakim* of *Gittin* is devoted to this relatively secondary enactment. Nevertheless, it touches on core issues of *chasimat haget* and document validation, and the *gemara*'s analysis is accordingly detailed.

The foundational framework, following *Rava*, is that *b'fanai nichtav* is a special *takkanah* that is categorically distinct from normal *kiyum shtarot* — the ordinary process of validating a document by having witnesses recognize the signatures. Normal *kiyum shtarot* requires two witnesses. The *takkanah* here allows a single *shaliach* to validate the document, but it does so through a distinct mechanism: the *shaliach* demonstrates that he was personally present at both the writing and the signing, thereby signaling that this is not ordinary *kiyum shtarot* but a *takkanat chachamim*. The declaration of *b'fanai nichtav* is precisely what marks it as belonging to a different category.

Several variants of the declaration are listed in the *mishnah* as invalid. If the *shaliach* says *b'fanai nichtav aval lo b'fanai nechtam* — it was written in front of me but not signed — the *get* is *pasul*. Similarly, *b'fanai nechtam aval lo b'fanai nichtav* — signed in front of me but not written — is *pasul*. One might have thought the signing testimony alone should suffice, since it is the signatures that *kiyum shtarot* validates, but the *takkanah* requires both.

### Full Attestation Require…

More significantly, the *mishnah* invalidates partial testimony across the two requirements. Even if the *shaliach* says *b'fanai nichtav kulo u'v'fanai nechtam chetzyo* — it was all written in front of me, and I saw both signatures — the *get* is still *pasul* if only one signature was witnessed. Conversely, *b'fanai nichtav chetzyo u'v'fanai nechtam kulo* — where the *shaliach* witnessed the full signing but only part of the writing — is equally *pasul*. The *takkanah* must be executed in its entirety; any deviation renders the *get* invalid.

### Half Writing Invalid

The case of partial writing testimony — *b'fanai nichtav chetzyo* — might have seemed acceptable given that the purpose of mentioning the writing is simply to mark this as a distinct mode from normal *kiyum shtarot*. If half the writing was witnessed, one might argue that purpose is served. The *mishnah* rejects this: the *takkanah* must be performed completely.

### Rav Ashi Clarification

The *gemara* presses further: which half of the writing matters? *Rav Ashi* clarifies that if the *shaliach* witnessed only the final portion of the writing, the *get* remains *pasul*, since his testimony does not cover the beginning. The implicit rule established by *Rabbi Elazar* — that witnessing even a single line of writing at the outset suffices, since we assume the rest followed suit — explains why only the *first* half of the writing would have any potential validity. Witnessing the *last* half cannot retroactively validate what the *shaliach* did not see. The *mishnah*'s ruling of *pasul* for *b'fanai nichtav chetzyo*, then, is understood to refer specifically to witnessing the later portion without the beginning.

The *mishnah* then introduces a case with two *shluchim*: one says *b'fanai nichtav* and the other says *b'fanai nechtam*. Despite having testimony covering both the writing and the signing, this too is *pasul*. The *chiddush* here is that the *shaliach* who declares *b'fanai nichtav* must himself also testify to the signing. The whole point of the declaration is that one person can do what normally requires two, precisely because he was present for the entirety of the process. Splitting the testimony between two people defeats the mechanism. *Rabbi Yehuda*, however, considers the *get* valid in such cases — and the *gemara* indicates that his leniency extends to the earlier cases as well, wherever the combination of testimonies covers writing and signing even if not by the same individual.

Finally, if two witnesses together say *b'fanenu nichtav* and a single separate individual says *b'fanai nechtam*, the *get* is *pasul*. But if one says *b'fanai nichtav* and two witnesses say *b'fanenu nechtam*, the *get* is valid — for the obvious reason that two witnesses testifying to the signatures constitutes ordinary *kiyum shtarot*, which is always available as an independent mode of validation.

## Witness Testimony Modes

### Rav Chisda Position

*Rav Chisda* articulates a principle that runs through several cases on this *amud*: *o kulo b'kiyum haget o kulo b'takkanat chachamim* — one must either rely entirely on the ordinary process of *kiyum shtarot* or entirely on the *takkanat chachamim* of *b'fanai nichtav*. Mixing the two modes, even when the combination would seem to provide adequate validation, is *pasul*.

The specific case *Rav Chisda* addresses is where the *shaliach* says *b'fanai nechtam* for the first signature — which works through the *takkanah* — and then two other witnesses from the *shuk* validate the second signature through normal *kiyum shtarot*. Naively, this seems stronger than using the *takkanah* alone. Yet *Rav Chisda* rules it *pasul*: one must choose one mode and apply it throughout. There is no compelling *sevara* that explains why mixing modes is categorically prohibited, but *Rav Chisda*'s position is that the *takkanah* is designed as a self-contained mechanism, and one does not blend it with regular document validation.

### Rava's Three-Quarter Iss…

*Rava* challenges *Rav Chisda*: is there really a rule that having *more* witnesses makes a document *less* valid? If the *shaliach* alone could say *b'fanai nechtam* on both signatures and achieve *kashrus*, how can adding two fully qualified witnesses for the second signature create *pesul*? *Mi ika midi d'ilu amar chad kasher v'chi amri terei pasul* — is there truly a scenario where one witness suffices but two invalidate?

*Rava* therefore reframes the *chiddush*. The problematic case, in his view, is where the *shaliach* himself — together with one other witness from the *shuk* — validates the second signature. Here *Rava* provides an actual *sevara* for the *pesul*: *atei l'achlufei b'kiyum shtarot d'alma* — this arrangement creates a dangerous resemblance to an invalid mode of regular *kiyum shtarot*. In ordinary *kiyum shtarot*, it is impermissible for one witness to contribute disproportionately to the validation of a document. If the same person validates one full signature on his own and then provides half the testimony for the second signature, three-quarters of the document's validation rests on a single witness — *v'kanafik nechi riv'a d'mamona apuma d'chad sahada*. That is structurally defective by the rules of *kiyum shtarot*.

To illustrate: if a document bears two signatures and the *shaliach* validates the first entirely by saying *b'fanai nechtam*, and then the *shaliach* and *Reuven* together validate the second through signature recognition, then one witness has validated half the document outright and half of the remaining half — three-quarters in total. This is exactly the sort of lopsided validation that *kiyum shtarot* prohibits. And because this arrangement so closely resembles that prohibited structure, one who witnesses it might mistakenly conclude that such testimony is acceptable in ordinary *kiyum shtarot* as well.

*Rav Ashi* raises a pointed challenge against *Rava*: in the case just described, had the *shaliach* been present for the signing of the second signature, he could have validated the entire document himself simply by saying *b'fanai nechtam* on both. If one person can validate the whole document through the *takkanah*, why should validating three-quarters of it be problematic? *Rav Ashi* argues that *Rava*'s concern about the appearance of three-quarters validation does not translate cleanly, since the baseline capacity here is full single-person validation. *Rava*, however, maintains his position on the grounds of *atei l'achlufei* — the concern is not theoretical capacity but practical confusion with the rules of *kiyum shtarot*.

### Rav Ashi Solution

*Rav Ashi* offers his own formulation of the *pesul* case. He agrees that the cases *Rav Chisda* and *Rava* addressed are not *pasul*. But a case that *is* invalid, according to *Rav Ashi*, is where the *shaliach* says *b'fanai nechtam* for the first signature — relying on the *takkanah* — and then testifies to his own signature for the second, relying on the standard rule that a signatory may always authenticate his own signature. The result is that the *get* is validated partly through *takkanat chachamim* and partly through the ordinary mode of self-authentication — two different validation mechanisms, each legitimate on its own, but improperly combined. As *Rav Ashi* puts it, echoing *Rav Chisda*'s principle: *o kulo b'kiyum haget o kulo b'takkanat chachamim* — choose one mode and stay in it.

The three *amoraic* positions thus present a spectrum. *Rav Chisda* holds that even completely valid *kiyum shtarot* for the second signature cannot be combined with the *takkanah* for the first — pick one box entirely. *Rava* holds that two strangers validating the second signature is fine, but the *shaliach* himself contributing to validating the second creates a problematic resemblance to defective *kiyum shtarot*. *Rav Ashi* holds that even the two-strangers case and the *shaliach*-plus-one case are both fine, but the *shaliach* who mixes *b'fanai nechtam* with self-authentication of his own signature cannot combine the two.

## Mishna Hermeneutics

### Implicit vs Explicit

Having laid out the positions of *Rav Chisda*, *Rava*, and *Rav Ashi*, the *gemara* raises a methodological challenge. If the *mishnah* — by stating *b'fanai nechtam chetzyo pasul* — is meant to convey a genuine *chiddush* about partial signing testimony being invalid, the case it implies must be one where *something* was done to validate the second signature, not merely nothing. After all, if no attestation whatsoever was provided for the second signature, the *pesul* would be entirely trivial. Any meaningful reading of the *mishnah* requires us to assume that some attempt at validation was made for the second signature — and that it still failed.

But if so, the *mishnah* must be alluding to one of the specific cases debated by the *amoraim* — either *Rav Chisda*'s case, *Rava*'s, or *Rav Ashi*'s. The *gemara* suggests that the *mishnah* is therefore: *o k'Rava o k'Rav Ashi* — oriented toward one of those latter two positions. And this implies: *ule'apuki miderav Chisda* — to exclude *Rav Chisda*'s ruling. The argument is that if *Rav Chisda*'s case — where two complete, independent, qualified witnesses validate the second signature — were the *chiddush* of the *mishnah*, the *mishnah* would have stated it explicitly. Such a dramatic *chiddush* — that more witnesses can make things worse — would not be left as a mere implication of oblique phrasing.

### Rav Chisda Defense

*Rav Chisda* responds with a hermeneutical counter-principle. The *gemara* itself is employing the assumption that every case in the *mishnah* must convey a new *chiddush*. But this is not always the case. *Rav Chisda* points to the *mishnah*'s earlier line — *b'fanai nichtav aval lo b'fanai nechtam* — which states that if only the writing but not the signing was declared, the *get* is *pasul*. That is obviously *pasul*. There is no *chiddush* there at all. Yet the *mishnah* lists it.

The *mishnah* follows the rhetorical pattern of *lo zu af zu* — not only this, but also that. It lists cases in order, not because each individual case is a *chiddush*, but to build systematically through a range of scenarios, some obvious and some not. Accordingly, the *mishnah*'s mention of partial signing testimony being invalid does not necessarily imply that the second signature was validated by some additional means. It may simply be listing an obvious scenario as part of a larger progressive enumeration. *Rav Chisda*'s ruling — that even full, two-witness *kiyum* for the second signature is *pasul* when combined with the *takkanah* for the first — is therefore not excluded by the *mishnah*'s silence. The *mishnah* simply was not making a point of that specific case; it was following the *lo zu af zu* structure.

This is a characteristically striking exchange. Usually when the *gemara* argues that a *mishnah* does not make a *chiddush*, the purpose is to *deny* that something special is being communicated. Here, paradoxically, *Rav Chisda* uses the same argument to *defend* the largest *chiddush* of the three — by maintaining that the *mishnah* need not communicate it at all.

## Gidud/Mechitza Combinati…

### Rav Chisda Position

The *gemara* now applies the *o kulo... o kulo* — all-or-nothing — principle to an analogous case in the laws of *Shabbos*. *Rav Chisda* states: *gidud chamisha u'mechitza chamisha ein mitztarfin* — a ditch five *tefachim* deep combined with a fence five *tefachim* high does not constitute a *reshut hayachid*. To qualify as a *reshut hayachid*, an enclosure needs walls or barriers of ten *tefachim*. One might think that a five-*tefach* ditch and a five-*tefach* wall placed on its edge could combine — after all, from the inside of the ditch, the effective barrier height is ten *tefachim*. *Rav Chisda* says no: either the full ten *tefachim* must come from the ditch alone, or from the wall alone. Combining two different physical mechanisms, even when the arithmetic works out, does not suffice. *Ad d'ika o kulo b'mechitza o kulo b'gidud.*

### Mareimar & Halacha

*Mareimar* disagrees: *gidud chamisha u'mechitza chamisha mitztarfin* — they do combine. And the *halacha* is ruled in accordance with *Mareimar*: *v'hilchata mitztarfin*.

### Tosafos Distinction

*Tosafos* raises an interesting point here. *Tosafos* considers the case as *Rav Chisda* framed it — ditch plus fence on its edge — to be obvious: from inside the ditch, the entire surrounding barrier is ten *tefachim*, and of course this constitutes a *reshut hayachid*. For *Tosafos*, the less obvious case, and thus the one actually under discussion, involves a different configuration: a mound that is five *tefachim* high, with a five-*tefach* wall built atop it. In that scenario, someone standing at ground level outside the mound sees only a five-*tefach* wall; someone inside, atop the mound, also faces only a five-*tefach* drop on the outside. The combined barrier of ten *tefachim* is not experienced uniformly from any single vantage point, which is what makes the case genuinely debatable. Either way, the *halacha* is that they combine.

## Lashon Bnei Adam

### Lav Yaseira

The *mishnah* presents a case involving vow language. *Konam she'ani to'em* — may what I taste be forbidden like a *korban* — is a straightforward prohibition against tasting. But the *mishnah* then addresses *konam she'ani lo to'em* — *konam* that I do not taste — and rules that this formulation is *equally* a prohibition against tasting. This is surprising, since *she'ani lo to'em* would grammatically mean "if I do not taste," which should constitute a vow to taste rather than a vow not to.

The *gemara* explains: *lashon bnei adam* — we follow colloquial speech. People commonly insert an extra *lo* in their speech without intending a logical negation. Someone who says *she'ani lo to'em* colloquially means exactly the same thing as someone who says *she'ani to'em* — they are not eating. The additional *lo* is a *lav yaseira*, a superfluous negative, a speech pattern of ordinary people that does not reverse the meaning. Both formulations therefore express the same prohibition.

### Nedarim vs Shavuot

This raises a natural question: why should *nedarim* be treated differently from *shavuot*? In *shavuot*, precision of language is essential. *Shavuah she'ochal* — an oath that I will eat — obligates eating. *Shavuah she'lo ochal* — an oath that I will not eat — prohibits eating. The *lo* makes all the difference; its logical force is fully operative.

The *gemara* explains that *nedarim* and *shavuot* function on different linguistic bases. *Nedarim* follow *lashon bnei adam* — the way ordinary people actually speak. Since ordinary people speak loosely and sometimes insert extra negatives without intending to reverse meaning, the laws of *nedarim* accommodate that colloquial reality. *Shavuot*, by contrast, are governed by strict legal precision of language; the common speech patterns of *lashon bnei adam* do not override the logical content of the words used. The same phrase therefore produces opposite results depending on whether it appears in the domain of *nedarim* or *shavuot*.
