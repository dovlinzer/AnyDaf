# Avodah Zarah 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ger Toshav Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה שַׁדַּר לֵיהּ קוּרְבָּנָא לַאֲבִידַרְנָא בְּיוֹם אֵידָם, אָמַר: יָדַעְנָא בֵּיהּ דְּלָא פָּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב? כֹּל שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵרִים שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda sent a gift to** a gentile named **Avidarna on their festival day.** Rav Yehuda **said: I know of him that he does not worship idols,** so he is not considered a gentile with regard to the prohibition against giving a gift to a gentile on their festival. **Rav Yosef said to him: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Who is a *ger toshav*?** It is **anyone who has accepted upon himself before three *ḥaverim* to not worship idols.** Avidarna had not accepted this upon himself before three Jews. Rav Yehuda replied to him: **When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is with regard to the mitzva **to sustain him.**

### Opening Context

Today's *daf* is 65, and we pick up at the last line of 64b. The *Gemara* has been in the middle of a larger discussion — something of a return to unfinished business from the previous *perek* — concerning whether a *nokhri* can be *mevatel* *avodah zarah* that he himself does not worship. The *Gemara* had established that a person can be *mevatel* the *avodah zarah* of a god he does not personally follow; a Zeus-worshipper, for instance, can be *mevatel* Aphrodite's idol. But what of someone who is not an *oved avodah zarah* altogether? That question led into a broader discussion about who qualifies as a *ger toshav* — and what the relevant threshold is.

The categories under consideration represent a spectrum of standards. At one end is the minimal standard of simply not being an *oved avodah zarah*; at another is accepting the *sheva mitzvot bnei noakh*; and at yet another, more demanding end, is a position requiring observance of virtually all *mitzvot* save *nevelah*. The practical stakes of this classification are not merely theoretical. One central question is the obligation of *mitzvah le-hayoto* — the positive communal obligation to sustain and care for such a person. Another, immediately relevant to this *masekhet*, is whether such a person renders wine forbidden as *stam yeinam* by touching it.

### Formal Status Question

The *Gemara* poses the question sharply: must a non-Jew formally belong to the category of *ger toshav* in order for certain *halakhot* of *avodah zarah* to apply — or not apply — to him, or is it sufficient that he simply does not, in practice, engage in *avodah zarah*? This is the question the *Gemara* proceeds to address through a pair of stories.

It is worth recalling how *Tosafot* enters this discussion. *Tosafot* notes that there are Christians who are not *ovdei avodah zarah* — whatever *Tosafot* understood about the nature of Christian practice, or at minimum that they do not use wine in their *avodah zarah* worship outside of the specific act of communion and transubstantiation. On this basis, *Tosafot* reasons that their wine would not be *assur be-hana'ah* in the manner of true *yayin nesekh*, since it lacks the idolatrous dimension. It would, however, remain *assur be-shtiah* due to concerns of *benotayhem* — the intermarriage-related prohibitions. As the *Gemara* concluded at the end of the previous *daf*, their wine is comparable to their oil: the prohibition on oil, bread, and similar foods was never about *avodah zarah* but about social barriers against intermarriage. Accordingly, once someone falls into the *ger toshav* category, the *avodah zarah* concerns about wine are removed, but the intermarriage-based concern remains, so one may derive *hana'ah* from the wine but may not drink it.

### Avidrana Gift Story

With this framework in mind, the *Gemara* now presents the last line of 64b. Rav Yehuda sent a gift — the word used is *korbana*, a term with strongly sacrificial, religious overtones, which makes its deployment here particularly striking — to Avidrana on his pagan holiday. This raises an immediate question from the very first *mishna* of *Avodah Zarah*: is one not forbidden to conduct business with an *oved avodah zarah* on his *yom eida*?

The answer implied by the story is telling. Rav Yehuda apparently knew that Avidrana personally did not participate in the religious dimension of the holiday — he was not a genuine *oved avodah zarah*. The word *korbana* for gift is therefore suggestive: everyone else may have been sending gifts in a religious context, as a kind of cultic offering associated with the holiday, yet it was still permitted here because this specific individual was not participating religiously. Two aspects of this are worth emphasizing. First, it is striking that Rav Yehuda permitted this even on the day itself, when the surrounding cultural activity had a religious character. Second, and crucially, it is not stated that Avidrana was a *ger toshav* in any formal sense — he had not formally accepted the *sheva mitzvot bnei noakh* before a *beit din*. It was enough, for this purpose, that *lema'aseh* he did not worship *avodah zarah*.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב? כֹּל שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲבֵרִים שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said to him: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Who is a *ger toshav*?** It is **anyone who has accepted upon himself before three *ḥaverim* to not worship idols.**

Rav Yosef challenges this reasoning. *Ve-hatanya*: who is a *ger toshav*? *Kol she-kibel alav bifnei gimel chaverim shelo la'avod avodah zarah* — anyone who formally accepted, before three *chaverim*, not to worship *avodah zarah*. Even if someone does not *lema'aseh* worship *avodah zarah*, and even if he observes the *sheva mitzvot*, the *halakhic* status requires a formal act of acceptance. Personal practice, in Rav Yosef's view, is insufficient.

### Status vs. Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is with regard to the mitzva **to sustain him.**

The *Gemara* resolves this tension with a crucial distinction: *ki tanya hi le-hayoto*. The requirement of formal *ger toshav* status — the acceptance before three *chaverim* — applies specifically to the obligation of *le-hayoto*, the positive communal duty to sustain and care for the person. But for the *de-rabbanan* concerns of *avodah zarah* — such as the question of *yom eidam* — there is no requirement of formal status change. For those purposes, it is sufficient to assess the person's actual practice on a case-by-case basis.

This is a genuinely important distinction that goes to the heart of how *halakha* categorizes people. Not every *halakhic* consequence requires a formal personal status transformation. It may be enough to know that this individual does not worship *avodah zarah* in order to do business with him on his holiday. But for the more substantial social-communal implications — the positive obligation of care — a formal change of status is required.

### Religious Communities

Where does wine fall along this spectrum? The previous *daf* situated the question of wine squarely in the *ger toshav* discussion, suggesting that wine requires formal status change rather than individual practice. But *Tosafot* was working with a different model: not an individual's personal practice, but the practice of an entire religious community. All Christians — in *Tosafot*'s understanding — do not use wine in *avodah zarah* worship. That communal-level fact may occupy a middle position between individual practice (relevant for *yom eidam* questions) and formal individual acceptance of *ger toshav* status (required for *le-hayoto*).

A similar analysis applies to Muslims. The *Rambam* states that Muslims are like *gerei toshav* — since Islam entirely rejects *avodah zarah*, including all images, Muslims fall into the *ger toshav* category with respect to wine: one may derive *hana'ah* from their wine, though one may not drink it due to intermarriage concerns. The *Rambam* does not go so far as to say Muslims are formal *gerei toshav* for purposes of *le-hayoto*, but for the specific issue of wine, the entire religious community's non-idolatrous character is sufficient.

### Three Categories

This analysis yields three distinct categories. First, there is the individual who personally refrains from *avodah zarah* even while surrounded by a pagan community — a case with limited *halakhic* applicability, perhaps sufficient only for the *yom eidam* question. Second, there is the person who formally accepts *ger toshav* status before a *beit din* — this is sufficient for everything, including *le-hayoto*. Third, there is an entire religious people or community that, as a collective, is not *oved avodah zarah* — this intermediate category may be sufficient for questions like wine even without formal individual acceptance. Different *halakhot* shift depending on where a person or group is situated along this continuum.

## Lehachayoto Obligation

### Circumcision Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב שֶׁעָבְרוּ עָלָיו שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא מָל, הֲרֵי הוּא כְּמִין שֶׁבָּאוּמּוֹת! הָתָם כְּגוֹן שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו לָמוּל וְלֹא מָל.
>
> **Translation:** **But doesn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A *ger toshav*** for **whom twelve months passed without** him **circumcising** himself **is considered as a heretic of the gentiles,** and certainly this Avidarna is not circumcised. Rav Yehuda replied: **There,** Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to a case **where** the gentile who desired to become a *ger toshav* **took it upon himself to become circumcised, but** he recanted and **did not circumcise** himself, and in such a case it is assumed that he did not circumcise himself due to apostasy.

The *Gemara* now cites a statement of Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a *ger toshav* who did not undergo *bris milah* within twelve months is treated as if he were a non-Jewish apostate — a remarkably harsh formulation that does not appear elsewhere in the *halakhic* literature and is not quoted as authoritative in the *poskim*. The implication of this statement is striking: it seems to suggest that *ger toshav* status is inherently transitional, a way-station between leaving *avodah zarah* and becoming a full Jew. If he has not converted within a year, his *ger toshav* standing is effectively forfeit.

### Conversion Intent

The *Gemara* immediately limits this ruling. *Hasam kegon she-kibel alav lamul ve-lo mal* — this applies specifically to a case where the person had explicitly committed to undergoing *milah* and then failed to follow through. In that case, if after a year he still has not done it, one gives up on him. But someone who inhabits the *ger toshav* status as a permanent condition — without ever having expressed an intention to convert — is not subject to this harsh characterization. He can comfortably remain a *ger toshav* indefinitely without any implication of blame for not completing conversion to Judaism.

## Rava & Bar Sheishach

### Gift on Holiday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אַמְטִי לֵיהּ קוּרְבָּנָא לְבַר שֵׁישַׁךְ בְּיוֹם אֵידָם. אֲמַר: יָדַעְנָא בֵּיהּ דְּלָא פָּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava brought a gift to** a minister named **bar Sheshakh on their festival day.** Rava **said: I know of him that he does not worship idols.**

The *Gemara* now presents: *Rava amti lei korbana le-Bar Sheishach beyom eida*. Rava presented a gift — again, *korbana*, the same suggestive term — to Bar Sheishach on a pagan holiday. Rava's justification: *yadana beih d'la palach l'avodat kochavim* — I know personally that he does not worship *avodah zarah*. This story thus reinforces the previous discussion: one need not establish formal *ger toshav* status in order for certain *avodah zarah* concerns to be set aside. Personal knowledge of this individual's non-idolatrous practice is sufficient for the purpose of giving a gift on *yom eidam*.

One might observe that the *Gemara* may also intend this story to make a further point: the fact that a person is morally problematic in other respects does not affect the narrow *halakhic* question. The approval of a non-Jew for purposes of *yom eidam* is not a general moral endorsement. This distinction becomes vivid immediately.

### Bar Sheishach Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּיָתֵיב עַד צַוְּארֵיהּ בְּוַורְדָּא, וְקָיְימָן זוֹנוֹת עֲרוּמּוֹת קַמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִית לְכוּ כְּהַאי גַּוְונָא לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **went** to him and **found him sitting up to his neck in rose** water, **and naked prostitutes were standing before him.** Bar Sheshakh **said to him: Do you have** anything as fine **as this in the World-to-Come?**

When Rava subsequently came upon Bar Sheishach, he found him *dyatib atzvarei be-varda* — seated amid rose petals — *ve-kaiman zonot arumot kameyh* — with naked prostitutes arranged before him. Bar Sheishach turned to Rava and asked: *it l'chu ki hai gavna l'alma d'atei*? Do you have anything like this in your *Olam Ha-ba*?

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּידַן עֲדִיפָא טְפֵי מֵהַאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טְפֵי מֵהַאי מִי הָוֵה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתּוּן אִיכָּא עֲלַיְיכוּ אֵימְתָא דְּמַלְכוּתָא, אֲנַן לָא תֶּיהְוֵי עֲלַן אֵימְתָא דְמַלְכוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Ours is better than this.** Bar Sheshakh **said to him: Is there** anything **finer than this?** Rava **said to him: You have the fear of the government upon you; we will not have the fear of the government upon us** in the World-to-Come.

Rava replied: *didan adifa tfei mehai* — ours is better than this. Bar Sheshakh pressed: *tfei mehai mi hava*? What could possibly surpass this? Rava answered: *atun ika l'chu eimta d'malchuta* — you live under subjugation to your king. *Anan lo havya lan eimta d'malchuta* — in our *Olam Ha-ba* we will not be subject to any human sovereign; we will stand directly before God. Of course, one might note that this response does not directly challenge the premise that *Olam Ha-ba* consists of physical sensory pleasure — it sidesteps that premise rather than refuting it, perhaps because Bar Sheishach could not have appreciated a response framed in purely spiritual terms.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא מִיהָא מַאי אֵימְתָא דְּמַלְכוּתָא אִיכָּא עֲלַי?
>
> **Translation:** Bar Sheshakh **said to him:** As for **me, in any event, what fear of the government is there upon me?**

Bar Sheishach then retorted: *ana mi it li eimta d'malchuta*? — I am so wealthy and powerful that I have nothing to fear from the king.

### King's Messenger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד דְּיָתְבִי, אֲתָא הָהוּא פְּרִיסְתָּקָא דְּמַלְכָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם, דְּקָבָעֵי לָךְ מַלְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **While they were sitting, a certain royal officer [*peristaka*] came** and **said to** bar Sheshakh: **Rise, as the king requires you** to appear before him.

*Ad yatib* — while they were still sitting together — *ata hu pristaka d'malka* — a messenger arrived from the king. *Kum k'ba'i lach malka* — arise, the king requires you. The timing was perfect: Rava's point was proven on the spot.

### Blessing & Blinded Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָפֵיק וְאָזֵיל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עֵינָא דְּבָעֵי לְמִיחְזֵי לְכוּ בִּישׁוּתָא תִּיפְקַע. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אָמֵן. פְּקַע עֵינֵיהּ דְּבַר שֵׁישַׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **As he was going out, he said to** Rava: May any **eye that wishes to see evil upon you burst,** as it is clear that you were correct. **Rava said to him: Amen.** And then **bar Sheshakh's eye burst.**

As Bar Sheishach was rushing out, he said to Rava: *eina d'ba'i l'michzei l'chu bishuta tipaka* — the eye of anyone who seeks to look evilly upon you Jews should pop out. You Jews — clearly whatever you say, God makes come true. Rava responded: *Amen*. And the eye of Bar Sheishach popped out — presumably because, beneath the apparent blessing, his words carried an edge of envy.

### Alternative Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי: אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵימְרָא לֵיהּ מֵהַאי קְרָא ״בְּנוֹת מְלָכִים בְּיִקְּרוֹתֶיךָ, נִצְּבָה שֵׁגַל לִימִינְךָ בְּכֶתֶם אוֹפִיר״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵימְרָא לֵיהּ מֵהָכָא ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ יַעֲשֶׂה לִמְחַכֵּה לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappi said:** Rava **should have said** a response **to him from this verse: "Kings' daughters are among your favorites; at your right hand stands the queen in gold of Ophir"** (Psalms 45:10), indicating that in the World-to-Come, the daughters of kings will serve the Jewish people. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: He should have said** a response **to him from here:** The reward of the Jewish people will be such that **"no eye has seen it, God, aside from You, Who will do for those who await Him"** (Isaiah 64:3).

Rav Pappa then suggests that rather than making the argument about subjugation to a human king, Rava should have quoted a verse from *Tehillim*: *bnot melachim be-yikoroteicha, nitzva shegal liminecha be-chetem Ofir* — daughters of kings are among your honored ones, the queen stands at your right hand adorned with the gold of Ofir. This verse, interestingly, seems to play directly into Bar Sheishach's vision of *Olam Ha-ba* as sensory and physical — here they would be women of status and royalty rather than *zonot*, but it is the same basic fantasy, just elevated. Rashi reads *yikoroteicha* as connoting honor, while *Tosafot* offers a different reading.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then proposes yet another verse: *ayin lo ra'ata Elohim zulatecha ya'aseh le-mechakeh lo* — no eye has seen, O God, other than You, what You will do for those who long for You. This verse entirely reframes the conversation: the nature of *Olam Ha-ba* is beyond all human imagination and sensory comprehension. No analogy to physical pleasure can capture it.

### Ultimate Principle

This concludes the *aggadata*, though its thematic connection to the *masekhet* as a whole is direct: when does a non-Jew exit the category of *oved avodah zarah*, and for which *halakhot*? The high *halakha* of *le-hayoto* requires formal status. The *de-rabbanan* concerns of *yom eidam* require only knowledge of personal practice. The intermediate issue of *yayin nesekh* appears to be more status-based. And as this story vividly illustrates, the narrow *halakhic* determination that one may give a gift to this person on his holiday says nothing whatsoever about general moral approval of the person's behavior.

## Earning from Wine

### Hiring Workers Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂכָרוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ מְלָאכָה אַחֶרֶת. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְעִיתּוֹתֵי עֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **If the gentile hired him to do other work with him,** even if he said to him while he was working with him: Transport the barrel of wine used for a libation for me from this place to that place, his wage is permitted, i.e., it is permitted for the Jew to derive benefit from the money. The Gemara notes that this formulation of the mishna indicates that this wage is permitted **even if** the gentile **did not say to him** to transport the barrel of wine **toward evening,** i.e., toward the end of his workday.

The rule under discussion is: if one hires a worker for general labor and in the course of that broader engagement the worker also handles *stam yeinam* or *yayin nesekh*, this is permitted. The general hire was for general work; the incidental contact with wine is folded into that broader arrangement. This is what the *Mishnah* claims.

### Nightfall Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל, וּלְעִיתּוֹתֵי עֶרֶב אָמַר לוֹ: ״הַעֲבֵר חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן נֶסֶךְ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם״ — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר. טַעְמָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְעִיתּוֹתֵי עֶרֶב — אִין, כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who hires a laborer, and toward evening** the employer **said to him: Transport** this **barrel of wine** used for **a libation from** this **place to** that **place, his wage** is **permitted.** By inference, **the reason** for this ruling is **that** the employer **said** it **to him toward evening,** and therefore **yes,** it is permitted, as it is clear that he has completed the labor for which he is being paid and the wage is not for moving the barrel. But if this happened during **the entire day,** not toward evening, this would **not** be permitted.

A *Tosefta* appears to complicate this, however. *Ha-socher et ha-poel, l'tutei erev, amar lo, hevei chavis shel yayin nesekh mi-makom le-makom* — if one hired a worker and then, toward nightfall, asked him to bring a barrel of *yayin nesekh* from one place to another, *secharo mutar* — his wages are permitted. The implication of the *Tosefta* is that this leniency applies specifically when the request comes at nightfall — after the worker has already effectively earned his day's wages. But during the workday itself, such a request would not be permissible on the worker's wages. This creates tension with the *Mishnah*, which appears to permit it without any such temporal qualification.

### Lump Sum Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי תְּנַן נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין, דְּאָמַר לְעִיתּוֹתֵי עֶרֶב תְּנַן. רָבָא אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַעֲבֵר לִי מֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת בְּמֵאָה פְּרוּטוֹת״, הָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַעֲבֵר לִי חָבִית חָבִית בִּפְרוּטָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: When we learned** this in **the mishna as well, we learned** this with regard to the case **where** the employer **said** this **toward evening. Rava said:** This is **not difficult. This** case, where the wage is forbidden if it was not toward evening, is a case **where** the employer **said to him: Transport one hundred barrels for me for one hundred *perutot*,** in which case moving all of the barrels is considered a single task, and if one of the barrels was wine used for a libation, the entire wage is forbidden. **That** case, in which the wage is permitted in any event, is a case **where** the employer **said to him: Transport each barrel for me for one *peruta*,** such that each barrel is its own task.

Abaye resolves the tension by saying that the *Mishnah* too is speaking of the nightfall case — though this is not a natural reading of the text. Rava offers a cleaner resolution. *Lo kashya*: it depends on the payment structure. *Ha d'amar lei ha'aver li me'ah chavis be-me'ah prutos* — if the employer said, "move a hundred barrels for a hundred dollars" as a lump sum — and among those hundred barrels one turned out to be *yayin nesekh* — then *secharo assur*: none of the pay is permissible. Since the worker could not earn any of the lump sum without completing all hundred barrels, the single barrel of *yayin nesekh* is inextricably bound up with the entire payment. Every dollar he earned is, in a sense, connected to the *yayin nesekh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״הַעֲבֵר לִי מֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת בְּמֵאָה פְּרוּטוֹת״, וְנִמְצֵאת חָבִית שֶׁל יֵין נֶסֶךְ בֵּינֵיהֶן — שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר. חָבִית חָבִית בִּפְרוּטָה, וְנִמְצֵאת חָבִית שֶׁל יֵין נֶסֶךְ בֵּינֵיהֶן — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** so **it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who hires a laborer and said to him: Transport one hundred barrels for me for one hundred *perutot*, and a barrel of wine** used for **a libation was found among them, his wage** is **forbidden.** But if the employer said to him: Transport **each barrel for one *peruta*, and there was a barrel of wine** used for **a libation among them, his wage** is **permitted.**

*Ha d'amar lei ha'aver li chavis chavis be-pruta* — if, however, the pay was one dollar per barrel — then *secharo mutar*: he keeps ninety-nine dollars and simply declines payment for the one barrel of *yayin nesekh*, saying in effect, "I'll move that one for free." A *beraita* confirms this distinction.

Accordingly, the *Mishnah*'s case of permitting mid-workday involvement with wine is when the worker is being paid per piece — he simply forgoes the unit of pay for the wine barrel. The *Tosefta*'s nightfall case applies to the lump-sum scenario, where the only way to permit it is if it occurs after the day's wages have effectively been earned. Yet even this is not entirely clean: if the pay is a lump sum tied to completing all the barrels, how does moving the wine barrel after hours change matters? *Tosafot* struggles with this and does not fully resolve it, and the *Gemara*'s treatment leaves the *l'tutei erev* question with limited scope.

### Animal Rental

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר לְהָבִיא עָלֶיהָ יֵין נֶסֶךְ — שְׂכָרוֹ אָסוּר. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא! סֵיפָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ: שְׂכָרָהּ לֵישֵׁב עָלֶיהָ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהִנִּיחַ גּוֹי לְגִינוֹ עָלֶיהָ — שְׂכָרוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to a gentile who rents** a Jew's **donkey to carry wine** used for **a libation on it, its** rental **fee** is **forbidden.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this additional** clause? **This is** the same as **the first clause,** as the principle is the same; only the example is different. The Gemara answers: **It was necessary to** teach **the latter clause** in order to teach that if the gentile **rented** the donkey **to sit upon it, even if the gentile placed his jug** of wine used for a libation **on it, its** rental **fee** is **permitted.**

The *Mishnah* continues: *ha-socher et ha-chamor le-havi'o aleha yayin nesekh, secharo assur* — if one rents out his donkey specifically to transport *yayin nesekh*, the rental income is forbidden, even though it is not the owner's personal labor that is being performed. The *Gemara* initially asks whether this is not already obvious from the first clause of the *Mishnah* — why repeat it for the case of the donkey?

In fact, one might have thought the donkey case is a genuine *chiddush*. The first clause involves the owner's personal labor. Here, the owner stays home; he is simply collecting a rental fee while someone else uses his animal. The connection seems far more attenuated. One might have thought that since the owner is not personally involved, and the renter does whatever he wishes with the donkey, the fact that the renter chose to use it for wine should be irrelevant to the owner. Yet the *Mishnah* rules it forbidden. The *Gemara*, however, says this is not actually the surprising case — the end of the *Mishnah* is what requires statement.

It also emerges that the standard model of donkey rental in this period is not like renting a car and staying home. The donkey owner typically comes along as the donkey driver — it is more like a limousine with a driver. This changes the intuition considerably: the owner is personally schlepping the *stam yeinam*, which makes the prohibition far less surprising.

### Donkey Driver Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא, דְּלָגִין לָאו דִּינָא הוּא לְאוֹתוֹבֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that it is not the legal right** of the renter **to place a jug** upon the donkey, and therefore placing the jug on the donkey was not included in the rental?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר — שׂוֹכֵר מַנִּיחַ עָלֶיהָ כְּסוּתוֹ וּלְגִינָתוֹ וּמְזוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ הַדֶּרֶךְ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ חַמָּר מְעַכֵּב עָלָיו. חַמָּר מַנִּיחַ עָלֶיהָ שְׂעוֹרִים וְתֶבֶן וּמְזוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ שׂוֹכֵר מְעַכֵּב עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who rents a donkey** to ride on, the **renter may place on it his garment, his water jug, and his food for that journey. Beyond those** items, the **donkey driver,** who would take the renter on the journey, **may prevent him** from placing anything on the animal by saying that he does not wish to further burden the donkey. The **donkey driver may place on it barley and hay** for the donkey **and his food for that** first **day** alone. **Beyond those** items, the **renter may prevent him** from placing anything on the animal, on the grounds that it will inhibit its progress.

The *Gemara* then asks about the end of the *Mishnah*: *sechara le-yeshev aleha* — if one rents out the donkey for the renter to ride on, as personal transportation, *af al pi she-hinniach oved kochavim legino aleha, secharo mutar* — even if the renter incidentally places his wine flask on the donkey, the owner's fee is permitted. The reason the *Gemara* initially suggests is that putting a wine flask on the donkey was not part of the rental agreement — the renter only paid for the right to ride, not to transport wine. Therefore, the owner is not being paid for anything connected to the wine.

The *Gemara* challenges this by pointing to a rule in *halakha*: one who rents a donkey for the day has the right to place on it his clothes, his wine flask, and his provisions for the trip. This seems to contradict the premise that the flask is outside the rental agreement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נְהִי דְּלָגִין דִּינָא הוּא לְאוֹתוֹבֵי, מִיהָא אִי לָא מוֹתֵיב לֵיהּ, מִי אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: נַכִּי לֵיהּ אַגְרָא דִּלְגִינָתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Granted that it is the legal right** of the renter **to place a jug** upon the donkey, but **in any event, if he does not place it** on the donkey, **can we say to** the driver: **Deduct the fee of his jug** from the rental? Since the donkey driver will not deduct any amount from the rental fee if he does not place the jug on the donkey, therefore, even if he does place it, the rental fee is not forbidden.

Abaye resolves this: *nehi de-login dinei hu le-ozfei* — yes, it is true that the renter has a right to place his wine flask there — *mi ka amar lei mosif li, mi amar lei, nekhit li agra de-laginato* — but if he does not bring his flask, he gets no reduction in his fare. He does not pay separately for the flask; it is simply an implicit entitlement bundled into the ride. The owner is therefore not being paid for the flask at all. Just as a taxi passenger is not entitled to a discount for not placing luggage in the trunk, even if it would have been permitted — the price is the price. Accordingly, even if the renter does use the flask, the owner's fee is unconnected to it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחַ לְמִזְבַּן — חַמָּר נָמֵי לְעַכֵּב, וְאִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ לְמִזְבַּן — שׂוֹכֵר נָמֵי לָא לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the main point of the *baraita*, the Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** locations in which one is able **to purchase** provisions on the way **are common,** the **donkey driver can also prevent** the renter from putting provisions on the donkey for the entire journey, **and if** locations in which one is able **to purchase** provisions along the way **are uncommon,** the **renter cannot prevent** the driver from taking his provisions for the journey **either.**

The *Gemara* then examines the rule distinguishing between the renter's right to carry a week's worth of provisions and the donkey driver's right to carry only a day's worth. If provisions are available for purchase along the route, the donkey driver could also be expected to buy as he goes, so the restriction to a day's provisions is justified. If provisions are not available, why should the renter have more right than the driver?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא צְרִיכָא דִּשְׁכִיחַ לְמִיטְרַח וּלְמִזְבַּן מֵאַוָּנָא לְאַוָּנָא, חַמָּר דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְמִיטְרַח וּלְמִזְבַּן, שׂוֹכֵר לָאו דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְמִיטְרַח וּלְמִזְבַּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* with regard to a case **where** locations in which one is able **to go to the trouble of purchasing** provisions **are common between station and station,** i.e., one can find locations to purchase provisions along the way, but only with difficulty. **A donkey driver is accustomed to troubling himself to purchase** provisions along the way, so it is not permitted for him to pack provisions for the entire journey on the donkey. **A renter is not accustomed to troubling himself to purchase** provisions along the way, so he is allowed to pack all of his provisions.

Rav Pappa resolves this: in practice one can make the effort to seek out provisions at stops along the way, but it is burdensome. The traveler, who is the paying customer, is entitled to avoid that burden and bring everything in advance. The donkey driver, whose job is to handle logistics, is expected to manage his own provisioning along the way.

### Personal vs. Incidental

Abaye's ruling establishes the principle: the wages are for the personal transportation, not for the incidental use of the flask. Even if the flask is *yayin nesekh*, the owner is not earning money from it in any meaningful sense, because his pay would be identical whether or not the flask was there.

### Wine Pouring Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא הֲוָה שָׁפֵיךְ לְהוּ חַמְרָא לְגוֹיִם, וְאָזֵיל מְעַבַּר לְהוּ מַעְבָּרָא, וְיָהֲבוּ לֵיהּ גּוּלְפֵי בְּאַגְרָא. אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְאַבָּיֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: כִּי קָא טָרַח — בְּהֶתֵּירָא קָא טָרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **The father of Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, would pour wine** which he sold **to gentiles** into their wineskins **and go** and **ferry them over the crossing, and they would give him** the **jugs** in which the wine had been stored **as payment.** The jugs that the wine had been stored in were included in the sale, and the gentiles would pay him by returning the jugs. People **came** and **told Abaye** that Rav Ika was accepting a wage from the wine of gentiles. Abaye **said to them: When he labored, he labored with permitted** wine, since he was pouring kosher wine into the wineskins, and only subsequently the wine was rendered forbidden by being in the possession of the gentiles.

The *Gemara* now presents a story. The father of the sons of Rav Acha son of Rav Ika — *avuh d'vnei Rav Acha brei d'Rav Ika* — would pour wine into the flasks of non-Jews who came to purchase it, and would then help them cross the river, receiving an empty barrel as his payment for that service. The practice was: the non-Jews would arrive, he would pour the wine from his barrel into their various flasks. Once the wine entered their flasks, it became forbidden — either by contact with residual *yayin nesekh* in the flasks, or simply by virtue of passing into the non-Jew's possession, a question *Tosafot* notes without fully resolving. The barrel was now empty. He would then help them transport the flasks across the river and keep the barrel as his fee.

When people brought this to Abaye's attention, he said: *ki katrach be-heteira katrach* — when he exerted himself, he did so with something that was still *heter*. The act of pouring the wine occurred while the wine was still permitted.

### Heteira & Rotzeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ, דְּלָא נִצְטְרוֹ זִיקֵי! דְּמַתְנֵי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ? אִי נָמֵי, דְּמַיְיתוּ (פְּרִיסְדְּקֵי) [פַּרְדִּיסְקֵי] בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't he desire the preservation of** the wine in the wineskins in **that the wineskins should not be torn,** as he would then need to return the jugs he received in payment? The Gemara answers: It is a case **where he stipulated with** the gentile purchasers that even if the wineskins were to tear it would be at their expense and not his, and he would keep the jugs in any event. **Alternatively,** it is a case **where** the gentile purchasers **brought barrels [*perisdakei*] with them,** so that they could pour the wine into them should the wineskins be torn.

The *Gemara* then raises two additional concerns. First, *rotzeh be-kiyumo*: while helping the flasks cross the river, he does not want them to break, since he is responsible for them — and this desire for the *yayin nesekh* to remain intact is an independent problem, analogous to *mekayeim kil'ayim*. The *Gemara* offers two resolutions: either he explicitly stipulated at the outset that if the flasks break in the crossing he bears no responsibility — so he is not *rotzeh be-kiyumo* — or he had spare jugs along, so that if the flasks broke the wine could be transferred, meaning he had no stake in the flasks' integrity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא מְעַבַּר לְהוּ מַעְבָּרָא, דְּקָא טָרַח בְּאִיסּוּרָא! דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְמַבָּרוֹיָא מֵעִיקָּרָא, אִי נָמֵי, דִּנְקִיטִי בֵּיהּ קִיטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't he ferry them over the crossing, which is laboring with forbidden** wine? The Gemara answers: This is not a case where he was ferrying the wine over the crossing himself, **as he** made an arrangement with the ferrymen and **said to the ferryman initially** that the latter would ferry the buyers and the barrels without payment. **Alternatively,** it was a case **where he held** special signal **knots in** collusion with the ferryman that he would ferry these people without payment. In any event, he was not laboring for them himself.

The *Gemara* then raises the concern that he is *mistaker be-issurei hana'ah* by crossing the wine over the river and receiving payment for doing so. To this the *Gemara* says: *d'amar lei le-mavrumi me'ikara* — he had arranged payment with the ferryman in advance, so he himself was not personally ferrying the wine across; or, alternatively, *d'nakit pukitri* — he gave the ferryman a pass or voucher, so his own personal labor was not directly applied to transporting the wine.

The impression left by this passage is significant, and notably unremarked upon by *Rashi*, *Tosafot*, the *Rif*, and the *Rosh*: it appears that the problems of *rotzeh be-kiyumo* and *mistaker be-issurei hana'ah* are substantially limited when one is not personally and directly involved in the handling of the wine. The case of renting out a vehicle one does not personally operate may well fall outside the scope of these prohibitions for precisely that reason, though the *Gemara* does not articulate this explicitly.

## Wine Mixtures

### Wine on Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יֵין נֶסֶךְ שֶׁנָּפַל עַל גַּבֵּי עֲנָבִים — יַדִּיחֵן וְהֵן מוּתָּרוֹת, וְאִם הָיוּ מְבוּקָּעוֹת — אֲסוּרוֹת. נָפַל עַל גַּבֵּי תְּאֵנִים אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי תְּמָרִים, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהֶן בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **wine** used for **a libation that fell on grapes, one rinses them and they are permitted. But if** the grapes **were cracked, they are forbidden.** In a case where the wine **fell on figs or on dates, if there is** sufficient wine **in them to impart flavor, they are forbidden.**

The *Mishnah* now turns to cases of mixture. *Yayin nesekh she-nafal al gabei anavim* — *yayin nesekh* fell on grapes. Since this is cold, there is normally no absorption. *Yedicham ve-hen mutarot* — rinse them off and they are permitted. *Ve-im hayu mevuka'ot* — if the grapes were cracked — *asurot* — they are forbidden, since cracked produce is porous enough to absorb even without heat.

### Min Bemino Rule

One might ask why the cracked-grape case requires rinse-and-permit at all for the uncracked grapes, rather than simply ruling it forbidden in any amount. The answer touches on a fundamental distinction in *halakha*: *min be-mino* — the same type mixed with itself — is forbidden in any amount, *be-khol she-hu*, at least by rabbinic enactment in the case of wine. But *eino mino* — wine mixed with something not of its kind — follows the standard rule of *nosein ta'am*, perceptible taste. Wine falling on uncracked grapes may be *min be-mino* in some sense, yet since the grapes have not absorbed and the wine is rinsed off, the *Mishnah* permits it. The cracked-grape case becomes *assur* because absorption has occurred.

### Veitos Ben Zonam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּבַיְתוֹס בֶּן זוּנֵן שֶׁהֵבִיא גְּרוֹגְרוֹת בִּסְפִינָה, וְנִשְׁתַּבְּרָה חָבִית שֶׁל יֵין נֶסֶךְ וְנָפַל עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן, וְשָׁאַל לַחֲכָמִים וְהִתִּירוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there was **an incident involving Boethus ben Zunen, who transported dried figs in a ship, and a barrel of wine** used for **a libation broke and fell on them, and he asked the Sages** as to the *halakha*, **and** the Sages **deemed** the figs **permitted.**

*Nafal al gabei te'einim o al gabei temarim* — if it fell on figs or dates, which are more porous: *im yesh bahen be-nosein ta'am, assur* — if there is enough to impart taste, it is forbidden. The *Mishnah* then cites a story: *u-ma'aseh be-Veitos ben Zonam* — a case involving Veitos ben Zonam, who was transporting dried figs by sea when a barrel of *yayin nesekh* broke and spilled on them. He brought the question to the sages, who permitted it. This seems puzzling: wine on figs would ordinarily seem to be a good taste and therefore forbidden. The *Gemara* concludes that the *Mishnah* is missing something — *chisore mekhsara ve-hachi katani* — and the story was meant to be preceded by the concluding principle.

### Good vs. Bad Taste

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁבַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — אָסוּר, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — מוּתָּר, כְּגוֹן חוֹמֶץ שֶׁנָּפַל עַל גַּבֵּי גְּרִיסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle: Anything that benefits** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it, i.e., the forbidden item contributes a positive taste to it, is **forbidden,** and **anything that does not benefit** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it is **permitted, such as** forbidden **vinegar that fell onto** split **beans,** as the flavor imparted by the vinegar does not enhance the taste of the beans.

*Nosein ta'am li-fgam mutar* — if the mixture imparts a bad taste, it is permitted. This is the rule that was implicitly presupposed by the Veitos ben Zonam story: wine that broke open in a ship's hold and fell onto dried figs is not wine improving those figs — it is spoiled, off-putting, a negative taste. Accordingly it is *mutar*. The rule is thus two-layered: there must be perceptible taste, and that taste must be *li-shevach*, a positive improvement, for the mixture to be forbidden.

### Wheat Sale Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָהוּא (כרי) [כַּרְיָא] דְּחִיטֵּי דִּנְפַל עֲלֵיהּ חָבִיתָא דְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ, שַׁרְיֵיהּ רָבָא לְזַבּוֹנֵיהּ לְגוֹיִם. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר לֵיוַאי לְרָבָא: בֶּגֶד שֶׁאָבַד בּוֹ כִּלְאַיִם, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִמְכְּרֶנָּה לְגוֹי, וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂנָּה מַרְדַּעַת לַחֲמוֹר, אֲבָל עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ תַּכְרִיכִין לְמֵת מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara relates: There was an incident involving **a certain heap of wheat upon which a barrel of wine** used for **a libation fell. Rava permitted selling it to gentiles,** as deriving benefit from it is not prohibited. **Rabba bar Livai raised an objection to Rava** from a *baraita*: With regard to **a garment in which diverse kinds,** a prohibited mixture of wool and linen, **have been lost,** i.e., it is known that linen fibers became mixed into a woolen garment but they cannot be detected and removed, **one may not sell** the garment **to a gentile, nor fashion it** into **a saddlecloth for a donkey, but one may make it** into **shrouds for a corpse with no one to bury it [*met mitzva*],** as a corpse is not obligated in the observance of mitzvot.

The *Gemara* presents a case: a pile of wheat onto which a barrel of *yayin nesekh* broke and spilled. Rava permitted selling the wheat to a non-Jew. Rabbah bar Liva'i challenged him: even if the sale price excludes any value attributable to the wine, there remains the concern that the non-Jew will turn around and sell the wheat to a Jew — just as one may not sell a *kil'ayim* garment to a non-Jew lest he sell it back to a Jew. Rava conceded the point.

### Bread Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְגוֹי מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְזַבּוֹנֵיהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, הָכָא נָמֵי אָתֵי לְזַבּוֹנֵיהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל! הֲדַר שְׁרָא לְמִיטְּחִינְהוּ וְלִמְפִינְהוּ וּלְזַבּוֹנִינְהוּ לְגוֹיִם, שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Livai asked: **What is the reason** that it is **not** permitted to sell it **to a gentile? Perhaps he will come to sell it to a Jew,** who will not know that it is forbidden. **Here too,** with regard to the wheat, the gentile purchasers **may come to sell it to a Jew,** who is prohibited from consuming it. Rava then **retracted** his decision and **permitted grinding** the wheat **and baking** bread with **it and selling it to gentiles not in the presence of Jews.** In this manner, Jews will not be likely to buy bread from the gentiles, as the bread of gentiles is forbidden to Jews.

Rava then revised his ruling: *hadar sharya le-midchino, le-mifyeinu ve-lizvineihu le-ovdei kochavim, shelo bifnei Yisrael* — he permitted grinding the wheat into flour and baking it into bread, then selling the bread to non-Jews, but not in the presence of Jews. Why not in their presence? Because if a Jew were to see the transaction, he might assume it was bread baked by a Jew and purchase it, not knowing it had been contaminated. Outside Jewish view, it will be recognizable as bread of a non-Jew, which Jews will not purchase due to the prohibition of *pat akum* — unless one follows the *heter* of *pat palter* — so the concern of the wheat ultimately reaching a Jewish consumer is effectively eliminated.

### Wheat Kernel Grooves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: יֵין נֶסֶךְ שֶׁנָּפַל עַל גַּבֵּי עֲנָבִים — יַדִּיחֵן וְהֵן מוּתָּרוֹת, וְאִם הָיוּ מְבוּקָּעוֹת — אֲסוּרוֹת. מְבוּקָּעוֹת — אִין, שֶׁאֵין מְבוּקָּעוֹת — לָא. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שָׁאנֵי חִיטֵּי, הוֹאִיל וְאַגַּב צִירַיְיהוּ כִּמְבוּקָּעוֹת דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: In the case of **wine** used for **a libation that fell on grapes, one rinses them and they are permitted. But if** the grapes **were cracked, they are forbidden.** The Gemara infers: If the grapes are **cracked,** they **are** forbidden, but grapes **that are not cracked** are **not** forbidden. If so, what is the cause for concern in the case where wine spilled on the wheat? It should be sufficient to rinse the wheat. **Rav Pappa said: Wheat is different, since, because of its slits,** its status is **similar to** that of grapes that are **cracked.**

A final question arises from the *Mishnah*: the *Mishnah* required only rinsing for uncracked grapes, implying that without cracks there is no absorption in cold. Why then was the wheat a problem? The *Gemara* answers: *amar Rav Papa, shani chiti, agav tzirayu ke-mevuka'ot dami* — wheat is different, because its kernels have a groove or nick in them. That groove functions like a crack, making wheat kernels porous in a manner analogous to *mevuka'ot* grapes. Even without heat, the wine seeps into the wheat through that groove. And since the taste imparted was *li-shevach* rather than *li-fgam*, the wheat became forbidden, requiring the indirect remedy of grinding and baking before selling it in a way that would not reach Jewish consumers.
