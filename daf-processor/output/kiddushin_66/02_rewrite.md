# Kiddushin 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witnesses in Kiddushin

### Central Question

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained inquiry into one of the most fundamental questions in the laws of *kiddushin*: are witnesses merely required to *prove* that a marriage took place, or are they constitutive of the act itself — such that without witnesses, no *kiddushin* occurs at all? This distinction carries enormous practical weight. If witnesses serve only a probative function, then one might argue that where both parties openly acknowledge the marriage, their mutual admission substitutes for testimony. But if witnesses are constitutive — if their presence is what makes the act of *kiddushin* happen — then no degree of mutual consent can compensate for their absence.

The Gemara had been testing this question by examining various cases: a husband claiming marriage while his wife denies it (where are the witnesses?), and the dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel regarding a divorced couple discovered together in an inn (where witnesses to the seclusion raise the question of whether they stand in for witnesses to the act itself). Throughout, the thrust of the discussion has been that witnesses are not optional — without them, the *kiddushin* did not take place.

### Rav's Position

The Gemara now presents a direct ruling, transmitted by Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta in the name of Rav: *ha-mekadesh be-ed echad ein chosheshin le-kiddushav* — one who performs *kiddushin* with only a single witness present need not be concerned; no *kiddushin* has occurred. Critically, the ruling adds *ve-afilu shneihem modim* — even if both parties, the man and the woman, mutually agree and acknowledge that the marriage took place, it makes no difference. Without two witnesses, nothing happened.

### Rabbah bar Rav Huna

Rabbah bar Rav Huna then offers a parallel formulation: one who performs *kiddushin* with only one witness — *Beidina Rabba amrei ein chosheshin le-kiddushav* — the great *beis din* rules there is no concern. The Gemara asks: who is this *Beidina Rabba*, this great *beis din*? The answer: Rav. That is, the earlier authority, Rav himself, is being identified as the source of this ruling, lending it still greater weight.

### Textual Variants

The Gemara then records an alternative version of the transmission: *ve-ika de-amrei*, some say the chain runs differently — Rabbah bar Rav Huna said in the name of Rav that the great *beis din* rules there is no concern. In this reading, Rav is explicitly named, and the *Beidina Rabba* he references is identified not as Rav but as Rebbi — the Nasi, Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi. Either way, the ruling emerges with formidable authority: whether it is Rav himself or Rebbi standing behind it, the dominant voice in the Gemara is unambiguous that *kiddushin* performed with only one witness is of no legal effect whatsoever.

## Two Men, One Woman

### The Scenario

The Gemara now introduces a counter-challenge from Rav Achdevai bar Ami. Two men arrive from *medinas ha-yam* — from overseas — accompanied by a woman and a bundle of property. The first man claims: "This is my wife, this is my slave, and this is my luggage." The second man claims: "This is my wife, that is my slave, and that is my bundle." And the woman herself declares: "Both of you are my slaves, and the bundle is mine." It is a remarkable scenario, made all the more pointed by the fact that no one simply says "three people arrived" — two people come *with* a woman, and she is listed alongside the luggage.

### The Ruling

The *halacha* in this case is: *tzerichah shnei gittin* — she requires a *get* from each of them — *ve-gomer kesuvahsah min ha-chavilah* — and she may collect the value of her *kesuvah* from the bundle. Each man's claim that she is his wife creates at least a conditional obligation: if he is to give her a *get*, he must pay her *kesuvah*, and *mima nafshach* she is entitled to collect that amount from the disputed bundle, since each man acknowledges the luggage came with the woman he claims to have married.

### Case Clarification

The Gemara immediately asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If each man has witnesses establishing that the woman is his wife, how can her claim that both are merely her slaves have any standing at all? The presence of witnesses to *kiddushin* should overwhelm her counterclaim. The Gemara considers the possibility that each man has one witness — but then the witnesses neutralize one another, and we are back to square one. There is no objective basis for requiring her to obtain a *get* at all, since a man's bare claim that a woman is his wife does not, by itself, impose upon her the need for a *get*. Indeed, the Gemara confirms: *ela lemishrei le'alma dechulei alma lo peligi desharya* — she requires no *get* to remarry, as there is no objective halachic basis for such a requirement.

What then is the case actually saying? The scenario is best understood as one in which the claims are all a matter of he-said-she-said, with no standing witnesses available at the time of judgment. In such a case, no one can compel anyone to do anything. The only practical resolution is: if one of the men voluntarily gives her a *get* — why he would, given that he is not halachically required to, remains unclear, but if he does — only then would she be entitled to collect her *kesuvah* from the bundle.

It is worth noting the contemporary weight of this discussion. The Gemara is extremely reluctant to say a woman requires a *get* without clear halachic basis, precisely because of the consequences for the woman: she would be *asurah le-kohen*, he would be *asur bi-krovah*, and the specter of *agunah* looms over any unnecessary imposition of *get* requirements.

### Rabbi Meir's Chiddush

If no *get* is actually required and the entire scenario is contingent on voluntary action, what is the *chiddush*? The Gemara explains: the novel ruling here follows Rabbi Meir, *de-amar metalteli mishta'abdi le-kesuvah* — Rabbi Meir holds that movable property is subject to the lien of a *kesuvah*. The innovation is not about the *get* requirement or the witness question per se, but about the eligibility of the bundle — *metaltelin*, chattel — to serve as collateral for collecting a *kesuvah*. According to Rabbi Meir, one need not hold real estate to pay a *kesuvah*; movable goods suffice.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Kahana & Papa

The Gemara now asks: *mai hava alah* — what is the practical upshot of all this discussion? Despite the weight of amora'ic opinion that two witnesses are required, it has proven difficult to adduce clear Tannaitic support for the rule. Rav Kahana therefore rules: *ein chosheshin le-kiddushin* — no concern whatsoever; *kiddushin* with one witness simply did not occur. Rav Papa, however, says: *chosheshin le-kiddushin* — he remains somewhat concerned. Even if the *kiddushin* may not be fully valid, Rav Papa is unwilling to declare it entirely without effect.

### Gezeirah Shavah

Rav Ashi then challenges Rav Kahana directly: what is the source for your position? You must have derived it through a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy — between the word *davar* appearing in *al pi shnayim edim yakum davar* (the requirement for two witnesses in monetary cases) and the word *davar* in *ki matza bah ervat davar* (pertaining to personal status and *davar she'be'ervah*). The analogy imports the two-witness requirement from monetary law into the domain of personal status.

But this raises a powerful objection: in monetary matters, *hoda'as ba'al din ke-me'ah edim dami* — a litigant's own admission is equivalent to the testimony of a hundred witnesses. If I acknowledge that I owe someone money, the debt stands even without any witnesses to the original transaction. Witnesses in monetary law are *edei beirur* — their function is purely probative, to prevent lying and establish facts in court. If the parties are honest and admit the reality, no witnesses are needed. If this is the model from which the *kiddushin* requirement is derived, why should not the same logic apply there? If husband and wife both acknowledge the marriage, why should that admission not suffice?

### Beirur vs. Kiyum

The Gemara's response is sharp: *lo k'chayev l'achrinei hacha k'chayev l'achrinei* — there, in monetary law, when I admit a debt I am only harming myself. Here, when a man and woman admit to a marriage, they are imposing consequences on others. The woman's admission restricts her to this man; other men are now forbidden to her, and relations with them would constitute adultery. His admission restricts him from her relatives. The marriage, unlike a private debt, is not a transaction between two parties only — it ramifies throughout society.

This reasoning, however, only explains why personal admission is insufficient as a *standard of proof*. It does not yet prove that witnesses are *constitutive* — that without them the *kiddushin* literally did not occur. This gap is noted by the *acharonim* as something the Gemara never fully closes: we begin from monetary law where witnesses serve only to prove that something happened, and we arrive at the conclusion that in *kiddushin* and *gittin* the act does not even occur without witnesses — which is a far more radical claim.

There is indeed a significant debate among *acharonim* about how to conceptualize this. One approach holds that even in *kiddushin* the witnesses are fundamentally *edei beirur* — their role is probative — but because the act *chav le-achrinei*, because it impacts all of society, an act that cannot be proven in the requisite manner is treated as if it did not happen. Another approach holds that witnesses are genuinely constitutive of the act: *kiddushin* is so weighty a transaction that it requires the formal presence of witnesses to give it legal reality, in the same way that some acts require a justice of the peace.

A practical *nafka mina* between these two approaches concerns the case of witnesses who were hidden — watching from concealment — without the bride and groom's knowledge. If witnesses are merely probative, it should not matter whether the parties knew they were present; the fact can be proven. But if witnesses are constitutive — if they formalize and give weight to the act — perhaps the parties must be aware of their presence. This question is in fact debated among the *rishonim*, and the *Ketzos ha-Choshen* in *Choshen Mishpat* is among the primary places where the competing views are laid out, alongside a *teshuvah* of the Rashba.

A practical corollary: the Rema in *Even HaEzer* notes that since the Gemara did not entirely reject the possibility that *kiddushin* with one witness and mutual admission might carry some weight, one should *lechatchila* take it seriously if the situation arises — particularly in cases involving potential *agunah* concerns. Rav Moshe's responsa address precisely this scenario (a case of a teenager who said *harei at mekudeshet li* before only one witness), and the answer is nuanced: we are somewhat concerned *lechatchila*, but not to the point of treating the woman as requiring a *get* in the face of serious hardship.

## Witnesses & Monetary Law

### Asset Division

The Gemara now addresses a parallel case that further develops the conceptual framework. Mar Zutra and Rav Ada Saba, sons of Rav Mari bar Issur, had divided their assets — presumably a partnership or an inheritance — without any witnesses present. They came before Rav Ashi with a fundamental question: when the Torah says *al pi shnayim edim*, does that mean witnesses are necessary even to effectuate a valid division of assets? That is, if nobody wishes to back out and both parties acknowledge what was done, must the division be repeated with witnesses? Or were witnesses created only to prevent subsequent denial?

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye answers with one of the most important and oft-cited principles in halachic literature: *lo ivru sahadei ela le-shakrei* — witnesses were created only for liars. The sole purpose of witnesses in monetary matters is to protect against dishonest denial. In a world of universal honesty, witnesses would be unnecessary. Since neither party here is interested in reneging, the division stands entirely without witnesses.

### Framework

This ruling crystallizes the distinction that underlies the entire sugya. In monetary law, witnesses are *edei beirur* — probative instruments designed to establish facts against potential denial. The act itself — the loan, the division, the sale — exists independently of whether witnesses were present. Hoda'as ba'al din works precisely because the underlying obligation is real regardless; witnesses merely allow us to prove it in court. *Edei kiyum*, by contrast, are constitutive: without them, the act itself has no legal existence.

This framework also raises fascinating parallels with capital law, where formal witnesses and *hatra'ah* — forewarning — are equally required. Are those requirements merely evidentiary — to establish that the act was truly willful — or are they part of what defines the gravity of the act itself? The question of whether witnesses and *hatra'ah* make an act *deserving* of punishment by constituting its character, or merely allow us to *prove* it, maps precisely onto the debate about *edei beirur* versus *edei kiyum* in the domain of *kiddushin*.

## Single Witness Cases

### Contaminated Terumah

The Gemara now presents a series of cases in which Abaye rules that a single unopposed witness is believed. A single witness tells someone *nitme'u taharosecha* — your pure items, your *terumah* grain, have become *tamei* — and the owner is silent. Abaye rules: *ne'eman* — the witness is believed, and the grain may not be eaten. A supporting *baraisa* confirms: if the owner actively contradicts the witness — saying *lo nitme'u* — the grain is *tahor*. The reason the grain is *tahor* in the contradicted case is that he said no; the implication is that had he been silent, the witness would have been believed.

### Korban Obligation

Abaye offers a second case: a single witness tells someone *achalta chelev* — you ate forbidden fat — *be-shogeg*, unintentionally, and the owner is silent. The witness is believed and the person must bring a *korban*. Here too the *baraisa* confirms: if the person says *lo achalti* — I did not eat — he is exempt. The implication is that silence would have obligated him.

### Animal Status

A third case: a single witness tells someone *shorcha nirva* — your ox was used in an act of bestiality — and the owner is silent. The witness is believed, and the ox is disqualified from use as a *korban*. A *baraisa* teaches that animals disqualified through sin — whether through bestiality (*nirva*) or through having killed a person (*shehemit*) — are disqualified *al pi ed echad* or *al pi baalim*, on the basis of a single witness or the owner's own admission. The Gemara notes: if the owner is admitting, that is the second category (*al pi baalim*). The case of a single witness must therefore be the case where the owner is simply silent — and silence suffices.

### Why Three Cases

The Gemara explains why all three cases are necessary: *tzricha*. Each case presents a scenario where one might have thought the silence is not a meaningful admission. In the *korban* case, silence seems clearly an admission — the owner would never remain silent if he did not know he had eaten *chelev*, because bringing an unnecessary *korban* would constitute *chullin ba-azarah*, which no one would risk. Hence his silence is unmistakably an acknowledgment. But one might have thought that in the *terumah* case, silence is not meaningful, since the owner might reason: even if the grain is *tamei*, I can eat it during my own days of *tum'ah* — no real loss. The Gemara counters: he does lose the ability to eat it while *tahor*. And one might think the ox case requires no admission at all, since the owner may not have been planning to bring this particular animal as a *korban* anyway — why would he care if it is disqualified? All three cases are needed to establish that in each context, the silence does constitute a meaningful admission.

### Tosafos Analysis

Tosafos draws from this *tzrichusa* a significant principle: what the Gemara is really crediting in these cases is not the weight of the single witness per se, but rather the owner's silence read as an admission. If the Gemara were simply recognizing the witness's credibility as a matter of *ed echad ne'eman be-issurin*, no *tzrichusa* would have been necessary. The reason we need all three cases is that each requires us to carefully establish that silence in that specific context is indeed an admission.

This yields an important *nafka mina*: if the owner says *I don't know* rather than remaining silent, the witness is not believed — because the admission that underlies the ruling is absent. Similarly, if the witness testifies about something that occurred while the owner was elsewhere and had no way of knowing about it, silence cannot be read as admission, and the witness's testimony alone does not suffice.

This analysis also intersects with the question of why *ed echad ne'eman be-issurin* does not simply operate here on its own. In these cases, the witness is telling someone something about their own property, effectively imposing a financial consequence — the grain must be discarded, the animal cannot be sold as a *korban*. That is more akin to a monetary imposition than to a simple status ruling, and it is not obvious that the ordinary rule of *ed echad ne'eman be-issurin* applies where the consequence is a direct loss to the owner. Tosafos introduces additional factors here as well, such as whether the person was in a position to have done something about the outcome — as in the case of an animal given to be slaughtered, where the *shochet* who declares it *treif* is more readily believed because he could have handled the *shechita* differently.

## Wife & Adultery

### The Case

The Gemara now introduces the central case of the broader discussion: *ishto zinsa be-ed echad ve-shotek* — a single witness testifies to a man that his wife committed adultery, and the man is silent. What is the law?

Before addressing the ruling, it is worth situating this within the broader framework. If a man himself declares that his wife committed adultery, the halachic consequence is not that the court objectively establishes adultery — his unilateral declaration cannot do that, as it is *chav le-achrinei*. Rather, he is bound by his own words and must divorce his wife, though he remains obligated to pay her *kesuvah* since there is no objective proof. By contrast, a single witness to adultery would not ordinarily be credited in matters of personal status — *davar she'be'ervah* requires two witnesses even to prove it. The question here combines these issues: a single witness speaks, the husband says nothing. What now?

### Abaye vs. Rava

Abaye says: *ne'eman* — the witness is believed, and the husband must divorce his wife. Rava says: *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed. *Havei davar she'be'ervah ve-ein davar she'be'ervah pachos mi-shnayim* — this is a matter of *ervah*, and no matter of *ervah* can be established with fewer than two witnesses.

Tosafos offers an elegant framework for understanding what is actually being disputed. Abaye and Rava agree that personal status matters require two witnesses for objective halachic effect. They disagree about what this case actually is. Abaye reads the husband's silence as an admission: he is not contesting the witness, which is tantamount to saying he himself acknowledges his wife committed adultery — and just as a man's own statement about his wife's adultery binds him to divorce her, so too here. Rava, by contrast, does not read silence as admission in this context. This case is about the credibility of an unopposed single witness in a matter of *davar she'be'ervah* — and such a witness simply cannot carry the requisite halachic weight, regardless of whether anyone contradicts him.

### Reframing

The distinction is sharp and consequential. For Abaye, the operative mechanism is *shtika ke-hoda'ah* — silence as admission — and the admission of a party binds him to consequences he would bear from his own declaration. For Rava, the operative question is the inherent credibility of the witness's testimony in a *davar she'be'ervah* matter, and a single witness — even unchallenged — cannot establish *davar she'be'ervah*.

## Blind Man Story

### The Setup

Abaye now attempts to adduce a source for his position. He introduces a story: there was a blind scholar — a *tanna*, one who had memorized Tannaitic material and served as a teaching assistant in the *yeshiva* — who would regularly arrange *mishnayos* before Mar Shmuel. One day he was running late. Shmuel sent a messenger to fetch him. The messenger and the scholar took different paths and missed each other; the scholar arrived at the *yeshiva* and the *shiur* proceeded. When the messenger returned, he reported what he had seen: *ishta zanya* — the scholar's wife had been with another man.

### Shmuel's Ruling

The scholar came before Shmuel and reported what the messenger had said. Shmuel told him: *im meiman lach, zil afkeih* — if you trust this person, go and divorce her. *Ve-i lo, lo tappeik* — and if not, do not divorce her.

### Abaye's Proof

Abaye uses this story to prove his position. Note that this case cannot be explained by silence as admission: the husband was away, the witness reports what he saw, and the husband's lack of response is obviously not an admission — he had no way of knowing what happened. Yet Shmuel appears to rule that an unopposed single witness can obligate the husband to divorce his wife. This is precisely what Abaye holds: an unopposed *ed echad* carries sufficient weight in *davar she'be'ervah* to require divorce, even without any admission from the husband.

### Rava's Reading

Rava reads the story differently. When Shmuel said *im meiman lach* — if you trust him — he did not mean simply "if you know him to be an honest person and not a *gazlan*." He meant: *im meiman lach ke-vei trei* — if you trust him to the degree that you would trust two witnesses — if you have such absolute, ironclad personal confidence in this individual that his word is equivalent to formal testimony. Only in that extraordinary case should you act on his report. This is not a general ruling about unopposed single witnesses; it is a ruling about a case of such unusual personal certainty that it approaches the weight of two witnesses. And even then, Rava would likely maintain, the husband would still need to pay the *kesuvah*, since there remains no objective testimony sufficient to establish adultery in court.

## Yannai Story

### The Feast

Abaye's broader proof is derived from a remarkable historical *baraisa* — one with a clear literary quality unusual in Tannaitic texts, and with a known parallel in Josephus. The story concerns King Yannai, one of the Hasmonean monarchs. He sent forces to *Kochalit* in the wilderness and conquered sixty cities there — an achievement he celebrated with great joy upon returning. He summoned all the sages of Israel and told them: our ancestors, in the days when they were preoccupied with building the *Beis HaMikdash*, ate salted fish — the food of poverty. Let us eat salted fish in their memory. Rashi understands this as a gesture of *me-shibbud li-ge'ulah*: a recognition of how far they had come, from grinding poverty to this moment of military triumph, with the food of the past served on tables of gold — *meluchim al shulchanot shel zahav* — a striking and deliberate contrast.

### The Insult

At this feast there was, as the *baraisa* describes in almost scriptural language, *ish echad latz lev ra u-vliya'al* — one person, a mocker, evil-hearted and worthless — *ve-Elazar ben Po'irah sh'mo* — and his name was Elazar ben Poira. He said to King Yannai: the hearts of the Pharisees — the *chachamim* — are against you; they do not support you. Yannai asked: what can I do to test this? Elazar told him: wear the *tzitz* — the golden headplate of the *Kohen Gadol* — and make them stand before you. (There is a dispute among *rishonim* here: Rashi permits wearing *bigdei kehuna* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* based on the principle that the Torah was not given to ministering angels — one cannot demand instant removal of sacred garments after completing the *avodah*. Tosafos restricts this leniency and suggests the *tzitz* may be an exception, as the verse states *ve-hayah al mitzcho tamid* — it shall be upon his forehead always.)

One elder at the feast, Yehudah ben Gedidya, then spoke — unwisely. He said to King Yannai: *rav lecha keter malchut* — the crown of kingship is enough for you; leave the crown of *kehuna* to the descendants of Aharon. His basis was a rumor: *imo nishbis be-Modi'in* — that Yannai's mother had been taken captive in Modi'in, which, if true, would have rendered her unfit as the wife of a *kohen* and Yannai himself a *chalal*, disqualified from priestly service and from the *kehuna gedolah*. The problem was that *vi-yuvakash ha-davar ve-lo nimtza* — the matter had been investigated and nothing had been found to substantiate the claim. Yehudah ben Gedidya's remark was thus not only politically reckless but factually without support.

### The Rumor

The sages at the feast — *vayivadlu chachmei Yisrael be-za'am* — were driven out under the king's displeasure. Rashi explains this as their being expelled from the feast under Yannai's anger; the suggestion is also that their collective silence, their failure to defend Yannai or rebuke the offending speaker more forcefully, made them appear complicit in the insult. From Josephus's parallel account, we know that the sages in fact imposed lashes on the offender for *motzi shem ra* — defamation — but Elazar then argued to Yannai that the punishment was inadequate given the magnitude of the insult to a king and high priest.

### The Conspiracy

Elazar ben Poira then said to Yannai: *im atah shome'a le-atzati* — if you follow my counsel — destroy the sages, wipe them out. Yannai asked: *ha-Torah mah te'ei aleiha* — but what will become of the Torah? Elazar replied: *harei krucha u-munachat be-keren zavis* — it is rolled up and sitting in the corner; *kol ha-rotzeh yilmod yavo ve-yilmod* — whoever wants to learn may come and learn. The written Torah remains; why do you need the sages?

### Heresy & Restoration

The Gemara records: Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak said that immediately an *apikorsus* — a heresy — entered into Yannai. What was his sin? *De-hava leih le-meimar* — he should have responded: *tenach Torah she-bichtav* — granted, the written Torah remains, *Torah she-be-al peh mah* — but what of the Oral Torah? Who will transmit it? By remaining silent in the face of Elazar's dismissal of the sages, Yannai tacitly accepted the premise that the written Torah alone suffices. Note that *apikorsus* in the Gemara — especially as it appears in Sanhedrin — specifically denotes *mevazeh talmidei chachamim*, contempt for Torah scholars. The term here is thus precise: Yannai's silence was a form of dismissing the indispensable role of the *chachamim*.

The *baraisa* continues: *u-tutzatz ha-ra'ah al yedei Elazar ben Po'irah* — the evil blossomed forth through Elazar ben Poira — *ve-yarequ kol chachmei Yisrael* — and all the sages of Israel were killed. *Ve-hayah ha-olam mishtomem* — the world was desolate. This continued *ad she-ba Shimon ben Shetach* — until Shimon ben Shetach came — who had been hidden away, reportedly under the protection of Yannai's own wife, who was his sister. *Ve-hechzir es ha-Torah le-yoshnah* — and he returned the Torah to its former glory. Some connect Shimon ben Shetach to the establishment of primary education for children throughout the land — an interesting counterpoint to this very story, in that the response to the near-annihilation of Torah scholarship was to ensure that Torah would never again be concentrated only in the hands of a small group of sages who could be wiped out in a single night.

## Analysis of Story

### The Question

The Gemara now turns to ask what this story proves. Abaye had invoked it as a source for his position that an unopposed single witness can be credited in matters of personal status. The question is: what was the nature of the evidence about Yannai's mother's captivity, and how exactly was it refuted?

### Evidence Structure

The Gemara considers the possibilities. If two witnesses had testified that she was taken captive, and two others testified she was not, then we have *bei trei u-bei trei* — two against two. What would that produce? In monetary matters, we say they neutralize one another and return to the status quo. But in personal status matters, the analysis is more complex.

### Rashi's View

Rashi's reading is that even if the two sets of witnesses neutralize each other in some sense, the child — Yannai — would remain in *safek*. The mother herself has a *chazkas kashrus* — a presumption of fitness — and so the conflicting testimony leaves her status intact. But Yannai as a child never had an established *chazakah* of priestly fitness in this particular context. Since the case before the court is about the *child's* status, and the child lacks an independent *chazakah* that could resolve the doubt, the result is that Yannai's status as a *kohen* would remain *be-safek*. The *baraisa* says the matter was investigated and nothing was found — Rashi takes this to mean that even in the two-against-two scenario there would have remained a real question, which is why the story is halachically meaningful.

### Gemara's Inference

From here the Gemara draws its inference for Abaye's position: *ela be-echad* — the initial testimony must have been by a single witness. And if the sole reason the case was dismissed is that one or two witnesses contradicted that single witness — *te'ima de-kamachshei lei bei trei* — then in the absence of that contradiction, *meheiman*, the single witness would have been believed. This is Abaye's proof: even in a *davar she'be'ervah* matter — the personal status of the king's mother, which carries enormous ramifications — an unopposed single witness would have been sufficient.

### Rava's Counter

Rava responds: *le-olam trei ve-trei* — in fact, it was two against two from the start. And the reason the case was completely dismissed — rather than leaving Yannai in *safek* — is because the second pair of witnesses employed *edei hazamah*, the mechanism of impeachment. Rather than contradicting the content of the first testimony, they said to the first witnesses: you could not have seen what you claim to have seen — you were elsewhere at that time. *Hazamah* does not merely neutralize; it entirely discredits and removes the first testimony from consideration. Rav Acha bar Minyumi is cited in a parallel context for precisely this principle. Thus the first testimony was not neutralized by equal counter-testimony; it was thrown out entirely, leaving nothing. This is why the *baraisa* says the matter was found to be without basis.

### Alternative Explanation

The Gemara then offers a further alternative: *ve-i ba'it eima, ki de-Rabbi Yitzchak* — if you prefer, explain it like Rabbi Yitzchak, who says *shifcha hichnisu tachteha* — a maidservant was substituted in her place. On this reading, the two sets of witnesses do not contradict each other at all. The first pair testified that Yannai's mother was taken captive — and they were correct. The second pair testified to the rest of the story: yes, she was initially taken, but she was then rescued, and a maidservant was substituted so that the captors would not notice. The second witnesses thus complete and supplement the first testimony rather than contradict it, and the combined account establishes that Yannai's mother had ultimately not been harmed in her personal status.

The daf concludes with the debate between Abaye and Rava unresolved in the text at this point — though of course, as the Gemara will make clear, the *halacha* follows Rava: in all matters of *davar she'be'ervah*, whether to constitute the act or to prove it, two witnesses are required, and even an unopposed single witness does not suffice. For Abaye to maintain that an unopposed *ed echad* is believed in such cases — with consequences as severe as compelling a man to divorce his wife — is, as the Gemara acknowledges, a striking and counterintuitive position, and its implications underscore just how much rides on the foundational distinction between *edei beirur* and *edei kiyum*.
