# Gittin 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kol: Substantiation

### Kol Requires Beit Din

We pick up on 89b in Gittin, in the middle of a discussion about the *kol* — the rumor or report — that a woman has contracted a marriage. The *mishna* established that when such a *kol* circulates, we must be concerned about it and not permit the woman to marry someone else without a *get* from the man she was rumored to have married. The *gemara* has been probing exactly when that concern applies: how much substantiation a *kol* requires, whether we are *mevatlinan kala* — whether we proactively negate the *kol* once we discover it was unfounded — and what the practical consequences of a *kol* are once it has been established.

We now pick up with the ruling of Rav Ashi. The *gemara* states: *kol kol d'lo it-chazek b'vei dina lav kola hu* — any rumor that the *beis din* has not investigated and formally established as a matter of concern is not a *kol* to be reckoned with. This reinforces the earlier principle that substantiating circumstances alone are not sufficient; the *beis din* must actively examine the matter and declare it worthy of concern. Rashi explains: *lo chashinan lei ela im ken yadu beis din ha-ir she-kach yatza kol ploni me-hekhan shama ve-gazru she-yeichushu le-kol zeh* — we are not concerned about the *kol* unless the *beis din* of the city investigated the rumor, determined its source, and formally ruled that it must be attended to.

### Kol Achar Nisuin

Rav Ashi adds a second ruling: *kol kol d'vasar nisuin lo chashinan lei* — any *kol* that arises after the woman is already married is not a *kol* we attend to. Once she is already living with her husband, a rumor about her prior status — that she had been married to someone else, or had committed some act that would render her forbidden to a *kohen* — is disregarded, because acting on it would impact the man she is currently married to.

The *gemara* then qualifies: *d'vasar erusin chashinan lei* — but if the *kol* arose after *erusin* but before *nisuin*, when there is a formal bond but the couple has not yet begun living together, we would still be concerned. Rav Chaviva, however, goes further: *afilu d'vasar erusin nami lo chashinan lei* — even a *kol* arising after *erusin* is disregarded, since even at that stage there is already an existing relationship being impacted by the rumor. The *halacha* follows Rav Chaviva: once *erusin* has taken effect, a *kol* that emerges thereafter is not acted upon.

## Kol + Kiddushin

### Case Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a case sent from the academy of Rav to Shmuel: *yatza lah kol me-rishon u-va acher ve-kiddeshah kiddushei Torah mahu* — a *kol* went out that this woman had been married to Reuven, and then Shimon came and gave her *kiddushin* in a full and public manner — *kiddushei Torah*, witnessed *kiddushin* performed openly — in disregard of the existing *kol*. What should be done?

This is a case in which the *kol* was presumably of the serious, substantiated variety that would ordinarily require concern, and yet Shimon proceeded to marry her anyway. The question is how we now navigate the tangle of her status.

### Tetzei Response

Shmuel's reply is: *teitzei* — she must leave the second man. And then: *ve-ha-amidu ha-davar al buryo* — clarify the underlying facts. But what precisely does this instruction to "clarify" entail?

### Investigation: Eidus

A first reading: perhaps it means we investigate and discover that the first marriage was in fact not valid. There was, say, a strong *kol* supported by circumstantial evidence — everyone was carrying candles, everyone celebrated what looked like a wedding — but no formal *eidus*. Upon investigation we find, say, that the ring was not worth a *shaveh prutah* and the *kiddushin* was defective. In that case, *mevatlinan kala*, we would set aside the *kol* and allow the situation to resolve itself in Shimon's favor.

But this reading is rejected: *v'ha Neharde'a atreih de-Shmuel hu, v'lo mevatlinan kala* — Neharde'a was Shmuel's own city, and the practice of Neharde'a is that we are *not mevatlinan kala*. This is a striking ruling. Even when *beis din* determines that the *kol* was factually false, the practice in Neharde'a is still not to proactively negate it. The reason touches on a fundamental principle: the only difference between reality and perception is that it is far easier to change reality than perception. Even if *beis din* knows the truth, they cannot rule in a way that openly contradicts the public's settled understanding of the situation. Therefore, even in such a case, Shimon would still be required to give a *get*, since everyone believes the woman is married to someone else.

The correct reading of "clarify the matter" must therefore be the opposite: we investigate and find witnesses who can testify that the first marriage to Reuven was, in fact, genuine and valid. In that case, *lo tzricha get mi-sheni* — she does not need a *get* from Shimon at all. If the first marriage is proven by *eidus*, Shimon's *kiddushin* was simply void, and she returns to Reuven without any further action.

To summarize the ruling: the *teitzei* is absolute — she cannot remain with Shimon regardless. The only question is whether she needs a *get* from Shimon. If there is only a *kol* and no firm evidence of her marriage to Reuven, she needs a *get* from Shimon before returning. If the marriage to Reuven is established by testimony, she needs no *get* from Shimon whatsoever.

## Kol + Kiddushin…

### Perception Issue

This ruling is noted to be in conflict with the position of Rav Huna. Rav Huna holds, based on a principle of Rav Hamnuna, that if a married woman reaches out and accepts *kiddushin* from another man, she is considered at least *safek mekudeshes* to the second man — because a woman who does such a thing is, in effect, declaring to her husband that he has divorced her. Rav Hamnuna's principle is: *ha-ishah she-amrah le-ba'alah geirashtani ne'emenet, chazakah ein ishah me'izah paneha bifnei ba'alah* — a woman who tells her husband directly to his face "you have divorced me" is believed, since we presume no woman would be so brazen if it were untrue. Accepting *kiddushin* from Shimon is an act tantamount to making that declaration.

If we were to rule according to Rav Huna, then even in a case where we know for a fact that the woman is married to Reuven, the act of accepting *kiddushin* from Shimon would minimally require a *get* from Shimon. Yet Shmuel's ruling is that in such a case she does not need a *get* from Shimon at all.

The *gemara* resolves the tension: Rav Hamnuna's principle applies only when the act of accepting *kiddushin* — or making the declaration — occurs in her husband's presence, *be-fanav*. It is specifically because the husband is present and does not protest that we infer the divorce must be real. In our case, the woman accepted *kiddushin* from Shimon *she-lo be-fanav* — not in Reuven's presence — and therefore the act carries no such inferential weight. If we know as fact she is married to Reuven, Shimon's *kiddushin* is meaningless, no *get* is required from Shimon, and she simply returns to being married to Reuven. Tosafot notes that this point — that the principle of Rav Hamnuna requires the husband's presence — was never actually in dispute; the discussion here merely clarifies that even absent that concern, no *get* is needed from Shimon when the first marriage is established by evidence.

### Rav Huna View

Having addressed the confirmed-marriage scenario, the *gemara* turns to the unresolved case: *lo matza davar barur ve-ma hu* — investigation was conducted but no definitive conclusion was reached. The *kol* remains as it was, neither confirmed nor refuted, and Shimon has already given her *kiddushin*. What now?

Rav Huna rules: *megaresh rishon ve-nosei sheni* — the first man, Reuven, must divorce her, and then she may marry the second man, Shimon. This is how Rav Huna understands the instruction *teitzei*: she leaves Reuven (through a *get* from him), and that resolves the *kol*.

### Machzir Grushaso

But Rav Huna also rules: *u-megaresh sheni ve-nosei rishon lo* — we would not permit the reverse arrangement, whereby Shimon divorces her and she returns to marry Reuven. Why not? *Asi le-meimar machzir grushaso min ha-erusin* — people will say that Reuven had married her, then divorced her, then Shimon married her, then Shimon divorced her, and now Reuven is taking her back — which creates the appearance of *machzir grushaso*, a prohibited remarriage of a divorced wife. Even though there is no actual prohibition in this case, the appearance of impropriety forecloses this option according to Rav Huna. It is, to put it plainly, unfortunate that we must so constrain our practical options in order to manage public perception. Yet that is Rav Huna's position.

### Rav Idi Disagrees

The son of Rav Idi — identified in the discussion of competing rumors as Ashmeimar — disagrees: *af megaresh sheni ve-nosei rishon* — we are not concerned about the appearance of *machzir grushaso*. His reasoning: *meimar amri inei rabbanan be-kiddushei ve-kiddushei ta'us hava* — people will say the rabbis looked into the matter and determined that Shimon's *kiddushin* was an error, that it was never valid. Since there was already a *kol* that she was married to Reuven, and the investigation could not refute it, the public perception will naturally be that Shimon's marriage was mistaken from the outset — not that Reuven remarried a woman he had previously divorced. According to Tosafot, the *halacha* follows the son of Rav Idi on this point.

## Kol: Substantiation…

### Lo Mevatlinan Kala

This section of the discussion crystallizes the position of Neharde'a regarding *mevatlinan kala*. The Neharde'a position is that we do not negate a *kol* — *lo mevatlinan kala* — even after *beis din* has determined that the underlying facts do not support it. As noted above, this reflects the principle that perception, once formed, cannot simply be undone by legal decree. If the *kol* had the substantiating features that made it credible in the community's eyes, *beis din* cannot retroactively announce that nothing happened and expect that announcement to undo the public's settled understanding. The consequence is that even a woman who is in fact free of any prior marriage must still contend with the *kol* and all that it requires — including, in certain scenarios, a *get* from a man she was never truly married to.

## Competing Rumors

### Two Rumors Setup

The *gemara* now poses a further case: *yatza lah kol mi-zeh u-mi-zeh mahu* — suppose there were not a confirmed *kiddushin* by Shimon but rather two competing *kolot*. On Monday, a strong *kol* emerged that she had married Reuven; on Tuesday, a strong *kol* emerged that she had married Shimon. Both *kolot* are of the variety that would ordinarily demand concern. We were unable to clarify either one. What are her options?

### Rav Papa View

Rav Papa holds that even in this case of two competing rumors, she remains constrained in the same manner as the single-*kol* case under the approach of Rav Huna: *af zu megaresh rishon ve-nosei sheni* — even here, the *kol* tied to Reuven must be addressed by Reuven giving a *get*, and she may then remain with Shimon. The concern for *machzir grushaso* still limits the options to that one arrangement.

### Ashmeimar View

Ashmeimar disagrees: *muteres li-shneihem* — she is permitted in relation to both. When both marriages are based purely on rumors and neither is established by evidence, we cannot treat either *kol* as so definitive that we become worried about the appearance of *machzir grushaso*. Since it is enough for only one of the rumors to have been false in order for her to remain with Reuven without any taint of a prohibited remarriage, we do not impose that constraint here. Both competing *kolot* weaken each other, and the strict perception-management that drove Rav Huna's concern is simply inapplicable when everything is rumor.

### Halacha Ruling

*Ve-hilkheta muteres li-shneihem* — the *halacha* follows Ashmeimar: when there are competing *kolot* with no established facts, she retains full flexibility and is not restricted to any particular outcome. We must still attend to the *kolot* — they do not simply vanish — but we will not give both of them such absolute weight as to generate a concern of *machzir grushaso*. The understanding is that the public, seeing her return to Reuven, will naturally assume that the marriage to Shimon was never established. This remains, in any case, a less than ideal situation, particularly for those who rule *lo mevatlinan kala* — that even known falsehood must be accommodated as perception. That we are required to manage the appearance of reality even after knowing the truth is, one must acknowledge, genuinely unfortunate.

## Grounds for Divorce

### Beit Shammai View

Having concluded an entire tractate devoted to the technical legal act of divorce and the writing of the *get*, we arrive at the final *mishna* of Gittin, which addresses the circumstances that actually warrant a divorce. It is worth appreciating the framing: the *Torah* touches on divorce almost in passing, within the broader prohibition of *machzir grushaso*. The verse that frames the entire institution — *ve-hayah im lo timtza chen be-einav ki matza bah ervat davar*, "it shall be if she does not find favor in his eyes because he found in her *ervat davar*" — arises primarily to set the context for why the first husband divorced her, before the *Torah* goes on to forbid the first husband from remarrying her.

Beit Shammai reads: *lo yegaresh adam et ishto ella im ken matza bah devar ervah, shene'emar ki matza bah ervat davar* — a man should not divorce his wife unless he has found actual *ervat davar* in her, a matter of sexual sin, as it is written that "he found in her *ervat davar*." The language of *matza bah* — "he found in her" — suggests actual discovery, something known to have occurred. Beit Shammai therefore grounds the warrant for divorce in genuine adultery, or at the very least demonstrated sexual impropriety.

It is important to note what this discussion is and is not about. The question of whether a *get* is *valid* — whether it effects a divorce — is a separate matter entirely. The *get* is not like a court-issued civil divorce that requires fault; it is an act performed between husband and wife, and it does not require the approval or certification of *beis din*. The *mishna* is addressing what is *appropriate*, what is *warranted*, what the *Torah* is saying a man should do — not whether the *get* will take effect if he acts outside those parameters. The entire discussion is framed in terms of guidelines for proper conduct, not legal preconditions for validity.

### Beit Hillel View

Beit Hillel holds: *afilu hikdicha tavshilo* — even if she burned his soup, that is sufficient grounds for divorce. Rashi understands this broadly — too many spices, or simply something done to annoy him. The textual support: *shene'emar ki matza bah ervat davar* — the same verse, but Beit Hillel focuses not on *ervah* but on *davar*, "any matter," any small thing that offended him. One might note, and the *gemara* will address this, that Beit Hillel's position is lenient in the particular sense of expanding human freedom and reducing legal restrictions — though whether it is beneficial to the wife is another question entirely, depending on whose interests one has in view.

### Rabbi Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva goes furthest: *afilu matza acheret na'ah mimennah, shene'emar ve-hayah im lo timtza chen be-einav* — even if he simply found someone more beautiful or more pleasing than she is, that is grounds for divorce. The wife need not have done anything wrong whatsoever; she may be the perfect wife in every respect. Yet if the husband is attracted to another woman, the verse says "if she does not find favor in his eyes" — and that subjective failure of attraction is, according to Rabbi Akiva, sufficient.

This is a position that invites real reflection. Rabbi Akiva is, of course, the *tanna* whose love for his wife is among the most celebrated in all of rabbinic literature — the man whose wife Rachel sacrificed everything for him and whom he revered and adored in return. Some have tried to read his position here in that light: perhaps he is a romantic who believes one must always be passionately in love, and therefore if that feeling is gone, the marriage should end. But the position does create real difficulties in terms of protecting the interests of the wife, and it also connects to Rabbi Akiva's rulings elsewhere — regarding certain practices during *niddah* and on *Tisha B'Av* — where he emphasizes that a wife must take care not to become displeasing to her husband, *she-lo titganeh al ba'alah*. If a husband is understood to have the right to leave whenever he finds another woman more attractive, that places an enormous and asymmetric burden on the wife to always present herself favorably. This is something to genuinely reckon with in light of contemporary sensibilities.

## Gemara Analysis

### Davar vs. Ervah

The *gemara* now records a *baraita* in which Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai engage each other directly. Beit Hillel says to Beit Shammai: does it not say *davar* — "any matter" — which proves that even minor things suffice? Beit Shammai replies: does it not say *ervat* — sexual impropriety — which proves it must be something serious? Beit Hillel then explains why both words are necessary: had the verse said only *ervat* and not *davar*, I might have thought a woman can be divorced for *ervah* but not for some lesser *davar*. Had it said only *davar* and not *ervat*, I might have thought that if she remarries after being divorced for a *davar*, she is still permitted to marry anyone — but that if divorced for adultery, she is forbidden to marry anyone else. Since the verse goes on to say *ve-halkhah ve-haytah le-ish acher* — "she went and became the wife of another man" — we needed *ervat* precisely to teach that even in the case of adultery she may remarry. The double language thus serves two purposes: *davar* extends the grounds for divorce, and *ervat* teaches that even an adulteress may remarry (anyone other than the adulterer, as *halacha* specifies).

What then does Beit Shammai do with the word *davar*? They apply the *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar kan davar ve-ne'emar le-ilan davar al pi shnayim eidim o al pi shloshah eidim yakum davar* — the word *davar* appears here and also in the verse requiring two or three witnesses to establish "a matter." Just as there, a matter is established through testimony, so here, the *ervah* must be established through witnesses. Beit Shammai thus uses *davar* not to expand the grounds for divorce but to impose an evidentiary standard: adultery must be proven through formal testimony before it constitutes grounds for divorce.

### Interpretation of Pasuk

The *gemara* then asks: what is the underlying dispute? Reish Lakish had established that the word *ki* in the *Torah* carries four meanings: *im* (if), *dilma* (lest), *ela* (rather), and *de-ha* (because). Rashi illustrates these with several examples; notably, both *ela* and *de-ha* appear in proximity in the exchange between Sarah and Avraham — Sarah saying *ki yareti* (because she was afraid) and Avraham responding *lo ki tzachakt* (rather, you did laugh). The dispute between the positions turns on how one reads the word *ki* in this verse.

### Beit Shammai Reading

Beit Shammai reads the entire clause as a single, unified statement: *ve-hayah im lo timtza chen be-einav* describes the lack of favor, and *ki matza bah ervat davar* explains why she does not find favor — "because" (*de-ha*) he found *ervat davar* in her. On this reading, the *Torah* presents one scenario: she has committed adultery, and that is why he no longer finds favor with her. The lack of favor is not an independent ground; it is the consequence of the adultery. There is thus only one condition that justifies divorce.

### Rabbi Akiva Reading

Rabbi Akiva reads the verse as containing two entirely separate statements: *im lo timtza chen be-einav* — if she does not find favor — stands alone as an independent ground for divorce, and *ki matza bah ervat davar* — if he found *ervat davar* — is a second, separate ground. The *ki* here means *im*, "if." On this reading, the verse presents two distinct cases, either of which independently suffices. Beit Hillel's reading, it should be noted, is closer to Beit Shammai's in structure — they too do not read *lo timtza chen* as an independent clause — and it is only Rabbi Akiva who fully separates the two conditions into two freestanding grounds for divorce.

## Get Mechanics

### Get Validity

Rav Pappa asks Rava: if a man divorces his wife with no justification at all — no *ervah*, no *davar* even in Beit Hillel's broad sense — does the *get* take effect? The question itself confirms what was said earlier: the grounds for divorce discussed in the *mishna* are matters of propriety and guideline, not legal preconditions. Rava's answer, however, engages the question from a different angle.

### Rapist Cannot Divorce

Rava says: *degali Rachmana gabei ones* — the Torah made a specific disclosure regarding the rapist (*ones*), stating *lo yukhal leshalchah kol yamav* — "he may not send her away all his days." The *gemara* understands this as *bekum ve-hachzeir*: if a man who raped a woman and was compelled to marry her then divorces her, he must take her back. That is, the Torah explicitly imposes on the rapist not just the prohibition of divorcing but an obligation to re-marry if he does. The implication: *hatam hu de-gali Rachmana* — it was *there*, in the case of the rapist, that the Torah made explicit that the *get* does not stand; *aval hacha mai de-avad avad* — here, in the ordinary case of a divorce without grounds, whatever was done was done. The *get* takes effect, even if the motivation was improper.

### Hidden Intent

Rav Mesharshiya poses a different question to Rava: if a man has inwardly decided to divorce his wife but conceals this intention, allowing her to continue living in his home, serving him, and being intimate with him — what is the ruling on this? Rava does not answer in legal terms. Instead, he cites a verse: *al tacharosh al re'akha ra'ah ve-hu yoshev lavetach itakh* — "do not plot evil against your friend while he dwells securely beside you." The language *re'im ha-ahuvim* — beloved companions — captures the essence of the marriage bond, and to scheme a betrayal while one's spouse lives in trusting intimacy is precisely what this verse forbids. It is an act of profound treachery that the verse characterizes as plotting evil against one who reposes complete trust in you.

## Fastidious Man

### Rabbi Meir Parable

A *baraita* records: Rabbi Meir would say — *keshem she-ha-de'ot ba-ma'akhal kach de'ot ba-nashim* — just as people differ in their attitudes toward food, so they differ in their attitudes toward wives. The analogy, troubling as it may be in certain respects, is developed through three character types.

### Overly Fastidious

The first type: there is a person who, when a fly falls into his cup of wine, discards the entire cup — so fastidious that the slightest contamination renders the wine undrinkable. *Ve-zu hi middat Pappos ben Yehuda* — this is the character of Pappos ben Yehuda, who, when leaving his house, would lock his wife inside, keeping her completely secluded from the outside world. This is presented as extreme, and Rashi remarks directly on how counterproductive and harmful this is: *u-midah she-einah hogenet hi zo she-mi-tokh kakh eivah nikhneset beineihem u-mezanah tachtav* — it is an improper character trait, because it breeds terrible enmity between husband and wife and may lead, of all ironies, to the very infidelity he feared. The controlling dynamic of locking one's wife away does not produce fidelity; it produces hatred.

### Average Man

The second type: the ordinary person, when a fly falls into his cup, removes the fly and drinks the cup. *Ve-zo hi midat kol adam* — that is how most people behave. Correspondingly, most husbands allow their wives to speak with brothers and relatives and other familiar contacts, and do not become suspicious or jealous over normal social interaction.

### Least Fastidious

The third type: there is a person into whose stew a fly falls, who wrings out every drop of flavor from the fly and then eats the stew. *Zo hi midat adam ra* — this is the character of a bad person. Such a man sees his wife go out with her hair uncovered, spinning in the marketplace with her arms exposed, wearing clothing slit up the sides — and does not react. He tolerates everything.

## Women's Conduct

### Immodest Behavior

The *baraita* now enumerates the categories of women's public conduct that fall under the rubric of *ervat davar* in its broader sense — not necessarily adultery, but behavior that constitutes *dat Yehudit*, the norms of modest Jewish conduct within marriage. These include: going out with her hair uncovered, spinning in the marketplace in a manner that exposes her arms, and wearing clothing that is open at the sides.

### Public Bathing

The final and most striking example: *ve-rochetzet im bnei adam* — and bathes with men. The *gemara* objects immediately: literally with men? *Im bnei adam salka datcha* — can that really be meant? The clarification: *ela bemakom she-bnei adam rochtzin* — in a location where men bathe, with sufficient proximity to constitute impropriety even if not literal simultaneous bathing.

In such a case: *zo mitzva min ha-Torah le-garshah* — it is a *mitzvah* to divorce her. The *gemara* invokes *ki matza bah ervat davar* not in the narrow sense of proven adultery but in the broader sense of conduct that is sexually inappropriate and transgresses the norms of modest conduct in marriage. The discussion in *Ketubot* on *dat Moshe* and *dat Yehudit* addresses this at length. The *gemara* is careful to note that the verse then says *ve-halkhah ve-haytah le-ish acher* — she went and became the wife of another man — and the second man who takes such a woman, *hakatuv kera'o acher* — the verse calls him "different" — suggesting he is not of the same character as the first. The first man, *hotzi rasha mi-beito*, rid his house of a wicked woman. The second, *hichnis rasha le-tokh beito*, brought her into his. If the second man merits it, *shelakh* — he too will divorce her. If not, *kevertahu* — she will bury him. *Kedai hu ba-mitah* — death is fitting for him who should have known better.

## Ki Sane Shalakh

### Rabbi Yehuda

The *parasha* contains the phrase *ki sane shalakh* — which can be read as "if he hates, send away." Rabbi Yehuda understands this as a directive: *im sane'tah shalakh* — if you hate her, send her away. Rashi reads this as consonant with the position of Rabbi Akiva, though one might equally associate it with Beit Hillel's broader reading. The common thread is that subjective disaffection — the loss of positive feeling — constitutes grounds for ending the marriage.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan reads it entirely differently: *sanui ha-mishalei'ach* — the one who sends her away is the one who is to be despised. On this reading, the verse is not a permission but a condemnation: the man who divorces his wife is hateful. This aligns with a stricter, Beit Shammai–like sensibility that divorce without serious cause is an act of which one ought to be ashamed.

### Bezug Rishon/Sheni

The *gemara* says: *ve-lo peligi* — in fact they do not disagree. *Ha be-zug rishon ha be-zug sheni* — one statement applies to a first marriage, the other to a second. The context determines which is meant, as we will see in what follows.

## Altar Cries

### First Wife Tragedy

Rabbi Elazar states: *kol ha-megaresh ishto rishonah, afilu mizbe'ach morid alav dema'ot* — anyone who divorces his first wife — the *mizbe'ach*, the altar, sheds tears on his account. The verse is brought from the prophet: *ve-zot shenit ta'asu, kessut dim'ah et mizbe'ach Hashem be-bekhi ve-anakah* — "you cover the altar of God with tears and weeping and sighing." God no longer turns to the *minchah* and no longer wishes to receive the offering from your hands. And when you ask why — *al ki Hashem he'id beinekha u-vein eshet ne'urekha* — because God has testified between you and the wife of your youth, *asher atah bagadta vah* — that you have betrayed her.

### Chavratcha

The verse concludes with words of singular beauty: *ve-hi chavratekha ve-eshet beritecha* — she is your companion and the woman of your covenant. The word *chavratekha* — your friend, your partner — conjures an image of the marriage relationship that is strikingly egalitarian, especially in a context where so much of the tractate has dealt with the husband's unilateral power in matters of divorce. The word *berit* — covenant — suggests a sacred, binding relationship entered into by two parties with equal standing.

### Balance Issues

This brings us back to the tension between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yochanan, resolved by the distinction between a first and second marriage. It is the divorce of the first wife — the wife of one's youth, the foundational relationship — that causes the altar to cry. Applying a Beit Shammai–like stringency to that relationship, insisting that divorce not be undertaken lightly and certainly not for trivial reasons, captures something profound and important. At the same time, it is worth acknowledging that this framework, applied rigidly and without sensitivity, can cause enormous harm. A woman who has carefully and thoughtfully determined that a marriage must end, after long deliberation and many steps in the process, should not be made to feel that she is doing something monstrous simply by appearing before a *beis din*. Sometimes divorce is the right decision. The challenge is to honor the genuine tragedy of the dissolution of a first marriage without weaponizing that recognition to obstruct, shame, or manipulate people who are trying to move forward with their lives.

### Closing: Partnership

It is perhaps fitting to close the tractate on the note of *chavratekha ve-eshet beritecha*. Amid all the discussion of the mechanics of *gittin*, the rules of agency, the conditions of the document, and the grounds for divorce, these words from the prophets remind us what marriage is at its deepest level: a partnership between two people bound by covenant. It is striking that in *ketubot* discovered in the Cairo *Geniza*, this very language is used to describe the marriage relationship — *chaverti ve-eshet briti*, my companion and the woman of my covenant. That the language of friendship and covenantal partnership found its way into those documents of daily life speaks to how central this vision of marriage was — and remains. On that note of *chavratekha ve-eshet beritecha*, we conclude *Masekhta Gittin*.
