# Shevuot 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gezel Karka Debate

### R. Yochanan vs. R. Eleazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קַרְקַע – פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; חַד אָמַר חַיָּיב, וְחַד אָמַר פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated:** In a case where one **administers an oath to witnesses** who deny knowing information with regard to ownership **of land** and they deny knowledge of the matter, **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar disagree: One says** that the witnesses are **liable** to bring a sin-offering for a false oath of testimony, **and one says** that they are **exempt.**

The daf opens by picking up approximately two-thirds of the way through 37b, with Rav Yirmiyah addressing Rabbi Abahu. The topic at hand is whether a *shevuah* may be taken when the underlying obligation involves *shibud karkos* — a lien on land. The Gemara had already established that this question was the subject of a dispute between two Amoraim: Rabbi Yochanan, who held that one is *patur* from a *shevuah* in such a case because *karka* is categorically excluded from the laws of *shevuah* and its associated *korban*, and Rabbi Eleazar, who held one is *chayav*. With this framework in place, the Gemara now asks whether these two Amoraic positions can be mapped directly onto an underlying Tannaitic dispute.

### R. Eliezer vs. Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבָּנַן קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? דִּתְנַן: הַגּוֹזֵל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וּשְׁטָפָהּ נָהָר – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ שָׂדֶה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Abbahu: Shall we say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute of Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis? As we learned** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who robbed another of a field and** then **a river flooded it,** he is **liable to provide** the field's owner with **a different field,** since the value of the flooded field was significantly decreased and the robber must return the value of that which he stole; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. And the Rabbis say:** He is exempt from doing so, as **he** can **say to** the owner: **That which is yours is before you.** The robber may return the flooded field to its owner without reimbursing him for the loss in its value, since according to the Rabbis, land cannot be stolen. Consequently, the field is considered to be in the possession of its owner, and the thief is not obligated in the mitzva of returning a stolen item.

The Gemara proposes: *leima Rabbi Yochanan ve-Rabbi Elazar be-plugta de-Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbanan kamiflegi* — let us say that Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Eleazar are simply following the Tannaitic debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages.

### Stolen Field Flooded

The relevant Tannaitic dispute concerns the case of *ha-gozel sadeh me-chavero ve-shatafah nahar* — one who steals a field from his fellow and then that field is flooded by a river. Rabbi Eliezer rules that the thief is *chayav le-ha'amid lo sadeh* — he must provide a replacement field — because the act of stealing the field created a *gezeilah* obligation, and just as one who steals a chattel and it is destroyed must pay, so too here. The Sages, however, say *omer lo harei shelcha lefanecha* — he may say, "Here is what is yours before you." According to the Sages, *karka* is categorically excluded from *gezeilah*. The boundary-crosser never truly "stole" anything in the legally actionable sense, since the land was never removed or possessed in the manner that movable property can be. Therefore, when the flood came, it was the claimant's own field that was destroyed, and the wrongdoer bears no obligation to replace it.

### Ribui u-Mi'ut Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דָּרֵישׁ רִבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי, וְרַבָּנַן דָּרְשִׁי כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya continues: **And we say: With regard to what do they disagree? Rabbi Eliezer interprets** the verses that discuss an oath on a deposit and the mitzva to return stolen items according to the hermeneutical principle of **amplifications and restrictions, and the Rabbis interpret** them according to the hermeneutical principle of **generalizations and details.**

The Gemara explains that this Tannaitic dispute flows from a methodological disagreement in *derashah*: Rabbi Eliezer employs *ribui u-mi'ut*, while the Rabbanan employ *klal u-prat*. It is worth pausing on this distinction, as it drives much of the analysis here.

The relevant verses include the phrase *ve-chichesh ba-amito* — denying one's fellow — combined with *ve-heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal*, the obligation to return what was stolen. The first expansive term (*ribui*) includes everything; the middle specification (*mi'ut*) narrows, offering examples such as *pikadon*, *tzumat yad*, and *gezel*; and the concluding expansive term (*o mi-kol asher yishava alav la-sheker*, "or from all upon which he swears falsely") broadens again. Under *ribui u-mi'ut ve-ribui*, the middle term is not a limiting list but a narrowing gesture. The final *ribui* then expands to include everything similar to the middle examples — excluding only what is maximally dissimilar.

Under *klal u-prat u-klal*, by contrast, the middle term functions as a limiting list, and the final broadening yields only *ke-ein ha-prat* — things that share the defining characteristics of the specific examples. The Rabbanan abstract from the *prat*: the specific items mentioned are *davar ha-mitaltel ve-gufo mamon* — movable objects with intrinsic value. This yields three exclusions: *karka'ot* are excluded because they are not movable; *avadim* are excluded because slaves were conceptually assimilated to land; and *shtarot* are excluded because, although a promissory note can be moved, it has no intrinsic value of its own.

### Shtar Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה – רִיבָּה הַכֹּל. מַאי רִיבָּה? רִיבָּה כֹּל מִילֵּי; וּמַאי מִיעֵט? מִיעֵט שְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, as the Torah **amplified and** then **restricted and** then **amplified** again, it has **amplified** the *halakha* to include **everything** except for the specific matter excluded by the restriction. **What** is included due to the fact that the verse **has amplified** the *halakha*? The verse **has amplified** the *halakha* to include **everything** that one steals. **And what** is excluded due to the fact that the verse **restricted** the *halakha*? **It restricted** the *halakha* to exclude financial **documents,** which are dissimilar to a deposit in that their value is not intrinsic but rather due to their function. Consequently, according to Rabbi Eliezer, land that was stolen is included in the *halakhot* stated in these verses, and one who steals land must reimburse the field's owner.

Under Rabbi Eliezer's *ribui u-mi'ut*, the question of what is excluded by the *mi'ut* receives a specific answer: *mi'et shtarot*. The only category excluded from *gezeilah* obligations is *shtarot*, since a *shtar* — a promissory note — has no intrinsic worth. The underlying loan is not the piece of paper, and destroying the paper does not destroy the debt. Land, by contrast, does have intrinsic value, and Rabbi Eliezer therefore includes it. The Rabbanan would add the further requirement that the object be movable, which land by definition is not.

### Amoraic Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דִּמְחַיֵּיב – כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וּמַאן דְּפָטַר – כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya concludes: Shall we say that the **one who deems** the witnesses **liable** in a case of an oath of testimony concerning land, i.e., Rabbi Elazar, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** that land is included in the mitzva of returning stolen property and in the *halakhot* of an oath on a deposit, and by extension, in the *halakhot* of an oath of testimony; **and the one who deems** them **exempt,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** that land is excluded from these *halakhot*?

The mapping now seems neat: Rabbi Eleazar the Amora, who holds one is *chayav* for a *shevuah* relating to *karka*, would be following Rabbi Eliezer, who holds *karka* is subject to *gezeilah*; Rabbi Yochanan, who exempts, would be following the Rabbanan, who exclude *karka* from *gezeilah*. The Gemara notes that the relevant *pasuk*, *ve-chi yecheta be-amito*, appears in the context of *shevuah*, so it is being used to derive both the laws of *gezeilah* and the laws of *shevuah* simultaneously.

However, the Gemara pushes back: *amar lei lo*. The parallel is not forced. For Rabbi Eleazar, who obligates a *shevuah* regarding *karka*, he indeed must be within Rabbi Eliezer's framework — if *karka* is not subject to *gezeilah*, it certainly cannot be subject to a *shevuah* built on that framework. But Rabbi Yochanan's exemption from *shevuah* does not require him to follow the Rabbanan on *gezeilah*. He could hold like Rabbi Eliezer on *gezeilah* — that *karka* is subject to *gezeilah* — and yet still exempt from *shevuah*, because the verse governing *shevuah* says *mi-kol asher yishava alav la-sheker*, and *mi-kol* — "from all" — is a narrower formulation than *kol*, "all." Even Rabbi Eliezer might agree that *shevuah* applies to a more restricted category than *gezeilah*.

### Mishnah Arguments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״גָּנַבְתָּ אֶת שׁוֹרִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״ – חַיָּיב. וְאִילּוּ ״גָּנַבְתָּ אֶת עַבְדִּי״ – לָא קָתָנֵי; מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּעֶבֶד אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְקַרְקָעוֹת, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין קׇרְבָּן עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava: The mishna is also precisely** formulated, **as it teaches:** In a case where one accuses another: **You stole my ox, and** the defendant **says: I did not steal** your ox, if the claimant replied: **I administer an oath to you, and** the defendant **said: Amen,** he is **liable.** The mishna discusses a claim of a stolen ox, **whereas** it **does not teach** a claim of: **You stole my** Canaanite **slave. What is the reason? Is it not due to the fact that** a Canaanite **slave is compared to land, and one is not liable to bring an offering for a denial** in a matter of **a lien on land?**

The Gemara then attempts to derive textual support from arguments of silence in the Mishnah. Rav Pappa in the name of Rava observes that our Mishnah gives the example of *ganavta et shori* — you stole my ox — as the paradigm case. Why does it not give the more novel example of *ganavta et avdi*, you stole my slave? Since slaves are legally assimilated to *karka*, if slaves were subject to *shevuah*, that would have been a more striking *chiddush* to cite. The omission suggests that slaves and, by extension, land are excluded. However, Rav Papi in the name of Rava counters by noting the concluding *klal* of the Mishnah: *kol ha-meshalem al pi atzmo chayav* — whatever you would pay upon your own admission obligates you. This general formulation seems to include slaves. The two arguments from silence neutralize each other, and the Gemara concludes: *ela me-ha lika le-mishma mina* — from either of these, nothing can be proven.

## Specification Debate

### Klal vs. Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן כֵּיצַד? ״תֵּן לִי פִּקָּדוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּלָל – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. פְּרָט – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״שְׁבוּעָה לֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לָךְ״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **What is the case of an oath on a deposit?** It is where the claimant **said to** the defendant: **Give me my deposit, which is in your possession,** and the defendant replied: On my oath nothing of yours is in my possession, or the defendant said to him: Nothing of yours is in my possession; the claimant responded: I administer an oath to you, and the defendant said: Amen. In either case this defendant is liable to bring a guilt-offering if he lied. The mishna then discusses a case where five people sued him and he took an oath denying all of their claims. With regard to this case, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If he **included** all the denials in one oath, **he is liable** for **only one** false oath; if he **specified** them, **he is liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** that if he said: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is in my possession, **and nothing** of **yours, and nothing** of **yours,** he is **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim.

The Gemara moves on to address the next portion of the Mishnah. The Mishnah presents two contrasting situations: if five claimants all demand the return of a *pikadon* and he responds with a single denial — *shvua she'ein lachem be-yadi* — he is *chayav* only one *shevuah*. But if he says *she'ein lecha be-yadi ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha*, addressing each one individually, he is *chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — one *shevuah* for each person. Rabbi Eleazar adds that one must say *shevuah* at the end for each denial to count as separate. Rabbi Shimon goes further, requiring *shevuah* to be stated explicitly before each individual denial.

### R. Meir Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לְךָ, שְׁבוּעָה״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר שְׁבוּעָה לְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Eliezer says** that only if he said: **Nothing** of **yours** is in my possession, **and nothing** of **yours, and nothing** of **yours,** on my **oath,** i.e., he said the word oath at the end, is he **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim. **Rabbi Shimon says:** He is not liable for his oath concerning each individual claim **unless he says:** On my **oath, to each and every** claimant.

A *baraita* introduces a dispute among Tannaim. The *baraita* states: *klal* — a general denial — obligates only one *shevuah*; *prat* — specification — obligates one *shevuah* for each. This is *divrei Rabbi Meir*.

### R. Yehuda Vav Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר – פְּרָטוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה; כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – פְּרָטוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Shmuel and Rabbi Yoḥanan disagree with regard to the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The phrasing where one uses the conjunction: And, between denials is considered **by Rabbi Meir** to be **a general** denial and is considered **by Rabbi Yehuda** to be **a specific** denial; and the phrasing where one refrains from using the conjunction: And, is considered **by Rabbi Yehuda** to be **a general** denial and is considered **by Rabbi Meir** to be **a specific** denial.

Rabbi Yehuda then states: *shvua lo lecha ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — if one says "not you, and not you, and not you," with the *vav* connective between each denial, he is *chayav* for each. This formulation sounds identical to what Rabbi Meir calls *prat*, raising the immediate question: what exactly is the dispute?

### Shmuel Inference

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel reads the *baraita* as follows: *klalo shel Rabbi Meir prato shel Rabbi Yehuda* — what Rabbi Meir calls a *klal* is what Rabbi Yehuda calls a *prat*, and conversely, *klalo shel Rabbi Yehuda* is *prato shel Rabbi Meir*. In other words, Shmuel reads the two authorities as strict inverses. For Rabbi Yehuda, when you say *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha* — with *vav* connectives — those are separate *shevuot*, what he calls *prat*. What Rabbi Yehuda calls *klal* would be the case without *vav*: *lo lecha, lo lecha, lo lecha*. For Rabbi Meir, this is reversed: with the *vav* is *klal*, and without the *vav* is *prat*.

This inversion — that the absence of a *vav* could make a statement more of a *klal* than the presence of one — is genuinely difficult to understand intuitively. One might speculate that the *vav* extending the word *shevuah* across multiple denials unifies them into one statement, whereas without the *vav* each stands alone. But the lecturer frankly acknowledges that this reasoning is elusive, and invites the reader to sit with that difficulty.

### R. Yochanan Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּ״וְלֹא לָךְ״ – שֶׁהוּא פְּרָט; לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּ״לֹא לָךְ״ – שֶׁרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר פְּרָט, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר כְּלָל. וְאֵיזֶהוּ כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר? ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם בְּיָדִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All concede in** a case where the defendant says: **And nothing** of **yours, that it is** considered **specific** and that he is liable for his oath concerning each claim, even Rabbi Meir. **They disagree only with regard to** a case where the defendant said: **Nothing of yours,** without the conjunctive: And. **As Rabbi Meir says:** It is considered **specific, and Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is considered **general. And what is** the case of **a general** denial according **to Rabbi Meir,** where one is liable for only one oath? It is the case where the defendant says, in the plural: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession.**

Rabbi Yochanan reads the situation differently. He maintains that everyone agrees the formulation with *vav* — *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha* — constitutes a *prat*, separate *shevuot*. The debate, Rabbi Yochanan holds, concerns only the formulation without *vav*: *lo lecha, lo lecha, lo lecha*. Rabbi Meir considers that a *prat* as well; Rabbi Yehuda considers it a *klal*.

The textual foundation of Shmuel's reading: since Rabbi Yehuda specifically states that the *vav* formulation creates separate *shevuot*, the implication (*miklal*) is that in Rabbi Meir's view that formulation creates a *klal*. This is a straightforward *diyuk* — Rabbi Yehuda is declaring "this is my position," implying that he is correcting Rabbi Meir, who held the opposite. Shmuel uses this *diyuk* from the *baraita* to ground his inversion.

Rabbi Yochanan objects: the *diyuk* is not compelling. Shmuel adds that when debating, one typically states the position one *disagrees* with, not the one one *agrees* with. But Rabbi Yochanan reads Rabbi Yehuda as saying: here is the case where I agree with you — the *vav* formulation is separate — but I disagree with you on the case without *vav*, where you call it *prat* and I call it *klal*. Shmuel's rejoinder: that is not how disputants present their positions. You state the disagreement, not the agreement. The *diyuk* from the *baraita* therefore remains a strong textual support for Shmuel's reading.

### Textual Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? שְׁמוּאֵל דָּיֵיק מִבָּרַיְיתָא, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דָּיֵיק מִמַּתְנִיתִין. שְׁמוּאֵל דָּיֵיק מִבָּרַיְיתָא: מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה ״וְלֹא לָךְ״ פְּרָטָא הָוֵי – מִכְּלָל דְּשַׁמְעֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר כְּלָלָא הָוֵי, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פְּרָטָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree** that they explain the opinion of Rabbi Meir differently? The Gemara responds: **Shmuel inferred** his explanation **from the *baraita*, and Rabbi Yoḥanan inferred** his explanation **from the mishna.** The Gemara explains: **Shmuel inferred** his explanation **from the *baraita*** as follows: **From** the fact **that Rabbi Yehuda says** that the phrase: **And nothing** of **yours, is** considered **a specific** denial, for which one is liable for his oath concerning each and every claim, one may conclude **by inference that** Rabbi Yehuda **understood that Rabbi Meir said** that **it is** considered **a general** denial, **and** therefore, **Rabbi Yehuda** disagreed and **said to him:** No, **it is** considered **a specific** denial.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: תַּרְוַיְיהוּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר פְּרָטָא הָוֵי; וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בִּ״וְלֹא לָךְ״ מוֹדֵינָא לָךְ, בְּ״לֹא לָךְ״ פְּלִיגְנָא עֲלָךְ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל – עַד דְּאוֹדִי לֵיהּ אוֹדוֹיֵי, לִפְלוֹג עֲלֵיהּ אִיפְּלוֹגֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in response to this inference that the *baraita* can be explained differently: **Both** phrases: Nothing of yours, and: And nothing of yours, **are** deemed **specific** denials **by Rabbi Meir; and Rabbi Yehuda said to him: With regard to: And nothing** of **yours, I concede to you** that it is considered specific. But **with regard to: Nothing** of **yours, I disagree with you** and consider it a general denial. **And Shmuel** would respond: If that is so, then why does Rabbi Yehuda state in the *baraita* the instance in which he concedes to Rabbi Meir? **Instead of conceding, let him disagree** and state the instance in which they differ.

Rabbi Yochanan supports his reading from the Mishnah: since the Mishnah itself — which we presume reflects Rabbi Meir's position as *stam* — describes *ein lachem be-yadi* as the *klal* case, then any formulation involving individual *lo lecha* — with or without *vav* — must be a *prat* for Rabbi Meir. If the *vav* formulation were itself a *klal* for Rabbi Meir, the Mishnah would have taught that case instead of, or in addition to, *ein lachem be-yadi*. From the Mishnah's silence on the *vav* case as a *klal*, Rabbi Yochanan infers that for Rabbi Meir it is a *prat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דָּיֵיק מִמַּתְנִיתִין – מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם בְּיָדִי״ – כְּלָלָא הָוֵי, מִכְּלָל דִּ״וְלֹא לָךְ״ – פְּרָטָא הָוֵי; דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״וְלֹא לָךְ״ כְּלָלָא הָוֵי – אַדְּמַשְׁמַע לַן ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם בְּיָדִי״, נַשְׁמְעִינַן ״שְׁבוּעָה לֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ״, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם בְּיָדִי״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to explain the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan inferred** his explanation **from the mishna** as follows: **From** the fact **that Rabbi Meir,** who is the *tanna* associated with unattributed statements in the Mishna, **says:** If he addressed all of the claimants and said: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession, it is** deemed **a general** denial, one may conclude **by inference that** a denial phrased: **And nothing** of **yours, is** deemed **specific. As if it would enter your mind** that Rabbi Meir also deems: **And nothing** of **yours,** to be **a general** denial, then **instead of teaching us** that when one states in the plural: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** it is deemed general, **let him teach us** that when one states: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is in my possession, **and nothing** of **yours, and nothing** of **yours,** it is general, and **all the more so** it would be clear that when one states in the plural: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** it is considered general.

Shmuel could respond: *kol omer ve-lo lecha k'omer shevuah she'ein lachem be-yadi dami* — anyone who says *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha* is effectively saying the same as *ein lachem*, the *vav* binding them into one category, so the Mishnah need not spell it out separately.

The Gemara then attempts to resolve the dispute by examining the wording of the Mishnah more carefully. The Mishnah's various cases — *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha*, the *pikadon ve-tzumat yad ve-gezel ve-aveida* cases — are all presented with *vav*s, and the Mishnah treats them as cases of *prat*. Each time Shmuel's position would require the Gemara to propose emending the Mishnah to remove the *vav*, the Gemara is forced to say *tani* — "read it without the *vav*." Eventually, the Gemara grows exasperated: *ve-hai tana kol hachi shabish tani ve-azil?* — is this *tanna* so confused that he gets it wrong in every single case? If the *vav* is a *klal* for Rabbi Meir, why would the Mishnah consistently write *vav* in contexts that are meant to be *prat*?

### Attribution Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״וְלֹא לָךְ״, כְּאוֹמֵר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם בְּיָדִי״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says** that one may interpret the statement of Rabbi Meir as follows: **Anyone who says: And nothing** of **yours, is** considered **as though he says** in the plural: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״לֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ״! תְּנִי: ״לֹא לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to bring a proof for the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **We learned** in the mishna that if the defendant said: On my oath **nothing of yours** is in my possession, **and nothing** of **yours, and nothing** of **yours,** he is liable for his oath concerning each and every claim that he falsely denied. Evidently, Rabbi Meir deems: And nothing of yours, to be specific. The Gemara rejects the proof: Emend the language of the mishna and **teach: Nothing** of **yours,** nothing of yours, nothing of yours.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״תֵּן לִי פִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד וְגָזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה״! תְּנִי: ״תְּשׂוּמֶת יָד גָּזֵל אֲבֵידָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from that which is taught in the mishna: In a case where the claimant says: **Give me** back **my deposit, and pledge, and stolen item, and lost item** that are in your possession, and the defendant responds: On my oath I do not have in my possession your deposit, or pledge, or stolen item, or lost item, he is liable for his oath concerning each and every claim. Evidently, by using the conjunction: Or, Rabbi Meir deems the denials specific, counter to the explanation of Shmuel. The Gemara rejects the proof: Emend the language of the mishna and **teach:** On my oath you do not have a deposit, **a pledge, a stolen item, a lost item,** in my possession.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״תֵּן לִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״! תְּנִי: ״שְׂעוֹרִין כּוּסְּמִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from the mishna: In a case where the claimant said: **Give me** back my **wheat, and barley, and spelt** that are in your possession, if the defendant responds: On my oath nothing of yours is in my possession, he is liable for only one false oath. But if he responds: On my oath I do not have in my possession your wheat, or barley, or spelt, he is liable for his oath concerning each and every claim. The mishna is therefore counter to the explanation of Shmuel. The Gemara rejects the proof: Again, emend the language of the mishna and **teach:** Wheat, **barley, spelt,** without the conjunction: Or.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֲפִילּוּ״? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: אֲפִילּוּ חִטָּה בִּכְלַל חִטִּין, וּשְׂעוֹרָה בִּכְלַל שְׂעוֹרִין, וְכוּסֶּמֶת בִּכְלַל כּוּסְּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** novelty is there in a case where one takes an oath in this manner, that Rabbi Meir says: **Even? Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said: Even** the singular form for **wheat includes** much **wheat, and** the singular form for **barley includes** much **barley, and** the singular form for **spelt includes** much **spelt,** i.e., although the defendant refers to the grains in the singular, his denial is referring to all wheat, all barley, and all spelt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא כֹּל הָכִי שָׁבֵישׁ תָּנֵי וְאָזֵיל?! אֶלָּא הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָא שְׁנָא ״כְּזַיִת, כְּזַיִת״, וְלָא שְׁנָא ״כְּזַיִת וּכְזַיִת״ – פְּרָטָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** could it be that **this *tanna* errs so much as he teaches** the mishna? The Gemara offers an alternative explanation: According to Shmuel, the mishna is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir. **Rather,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who says:** There **is no difference** if one says: **An olive-bulk an olive-bulk,** and there **is no difference** if one says: **An olive-bulk and an olive-bulk;** both **are** considered **specific** formulations.

The Gemara concedes the point about the Mishnah's text but resists attributing it to Rabbi Meir. Instead: *ella mani Rebbi hi* — the Mishnah follows *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi). There exists a position of *Rebbi*, expressed in the context of *piggul* thoughts during *shechita*, that whether you said *k'zayis k'zayis* or *k'zayis u-kzayis* — with or without a *vav* — they are treated as separate statements. Rebbi holds that any formulation of separate items, regardless of the connective, constitutes distinct, separable statements. On this reading, our Mishnah's consistent use of the *vav* formulation as *prat* reflects Rebbi's position — not Rabbi Meir's — leaving Shmuel's reading of Rabbi Meir potentially intact.

## Multiple Categories

### Afilu Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּלָל – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. פְּרָט – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״שְׁבוּעָה לֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לָךְ״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If he **included** all the denials in one oath, **he is liable** for **only one** false oath; if he **specified** them, **he is liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** that if he said: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is in my possession, **and nothing** of **yours, and nothing** of **yours,** he is **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim.

The Mishnah introduces Rabbi Meir's statement: *afilu chita u-seora ve-kusemes, chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — even if the items demanded were wheat, barley, and spelt, he is *chayav* for each. The word *afilu* — "even" — signals that this is a surprising or non-obvious ruling. Rav Acha son of Rav Ika explains: *afilu chita bichlal chitin* — even if the claimant said *chita* (singular), we interpret it as *chitin* (the generic category), and similarly for the others. The *taanah* is always interpreted generously enough to constitute a *taanah* of at least *shaveh perutah*; we do not read *chita* as a single grain of wheat.

### Combining Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תֵּן לִי פִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד גָּזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״ כּוּ׳. ״תֵּן לִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פְּרוּטָה מִכּוּלָּם מִצְטָרֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Give me** back **my deposit, and pledge, stolen item, and lost item that are in your possession, etc.** If the claimant said: **Give me** back my **wheat and barley,** and spelt, and the defendant responds: On my oath I do not have in my possession your wheat, or barley, or spelt, he is liable for his oath concerning each and every claim. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If **all of the** wheat, barley, and spelt were collectively worth at least one ***peruta*,** then even if each type of grain was worth less than one *peruta*, their total value **combines** to render him liable.

Rabbi Yochanan rules that if someone is owed *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin*, and none of the individual categories amounts to a *perutah* on its own, *perutah mi-kula mitztarefes* — a *perutah* may be assembled across all categories combined. The question then becomes: in which case does this statement apply — the case of *prat* (where each category is individually denied) or the case of *klal* (where everything is denied together in one statement)?

### Generic Oath Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא – חַד אָמַר: אַפְּרָטֵי מִיחַיַּיב, אַכְּלָלֵי לָא מִיחַיַּיב; וְחַד אָמַר: אַכְּלָלֵי נָמֵי מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree with regard to** the ruling of the mishna. **One says** that when the mishna teaches that one is liable for his oath concerning each and every claim, it means that **he is liable for** the three **specific** denials of wheat, barley, and spelt, and he must bring three separate offerings; but **he is not liable for** the **general** oath taken at the beginning of his denial, i.e., when he said: On my oath I do not have in my possession. Accordingly, the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan was said with regard to the previous case in the mishna, where the defendant said: On my oath I do not have in my possession. **And one says** that **he is liable also for the general** oath taken at the beginning of his denial, so that the defendant is liable to bring a total of four offerings. Accordingly, even if the grains were worth only one *peruta* collectively and the defendant is not liable for any of the specific oaths, the defendant remains liable to bring an offering for the general oath according to Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The question depends on a dispute between two opinions regarding the case of *prat*. One opinion holds that when the Mishnah says he is *chayav al kol achat ve-achat*, he is *chayav* only for those individual specific *shevuot* — there is no additional generic *shevuah* bundling them all. The other opinion holds that in a *prat* case, in addition to the individual *shevuot*, there is also one general *shevuah* that encompasses all of them together, since before enumerating each item the denier effectively made a blanket denial of everything. If the second view is correct, then Rabbi Yochanan's statement — that fractions combine to a *perutah* — applies even within the *prat* case, because the general *shevuah* ties all the categories together. If the first view is correct, Rabbi Yochanan's statement applies only to the *klal* case, where everything was denied as one.

### R. Chiya Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֲרֵי כָּאן חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת; וְאִם אִיתָא – עֶשְׂרִים הָוְיָין! הַאי תַּנָּא – דִּפְרָטֵי קָא חָשֵׁיב, דִּכְלָלֵי לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the second opinion: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** in a *baraita*: If five people claimed from one defendant wheat, barley, and spelt, and the defendant took an oath denying each claim of each claimant, **there are** then **fifteen sin-offerings here** that the defendant is liable to bring? **And if it is so** that the defendant is liable for the general oath as well, **there would be** a total of **twenty** sin-offerings that he is liable to bring. The Gemara responds: **That *tanna* calculated** the defendant's liability for **the specific** oaths; **he did not calculate** the defendant's liability for **the general** oaths.

A *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya states: *harei kan chamish esrei chataot* — here we have fifteen *chataot*. The scenario is five claimants each demanding *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin*, each category denied individually. Five times three yields fifteen. The Gemara challenges: *ve-i ita esrim havyun* — if there were also a general *shevuah* for each claimant encompassing all three categories, the count should be twenty. The Gemara deflects: perhaps Rabbi Chiya was only counting the specific *shevuot*, not denying the existence of the general ones.

This deflection is reinforced by a second *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya that says *harei kan esrim chataot* — twenty. Rather than treating this as a contradiction, the two *baraitot* are reconciled: fifteen reflects the case of *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin* — three specific categories — counted for five claimants; twenty reflects the case of *pikadon*, *tzumat yad*, *gezel*, and *aveida* — four categories — counted for five claimants. Neither version requires positing a general *shevuah* alongside the specific ones; the different totals simply reflect different underlying case structures.

### Carry-Over Specification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: ״הֲרֵי כָּאן עֶשְׂרִים חַטָּאוֹת״! הָהִיא אַפִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד וְגָזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now questions the first opinion: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** in a different *baraita*: **There are twenty sin-offerings here?** Evidently, Rabbi Ḥiyya does calculate the general oaths. The Gemara responds: **That** *baraita* does not count the general oaths either; rather, it is referring **to** a different case entirely, where each of the five claimants claimed from the defendant **a deposit and a pledge and a stolen item and a lost item.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: הָיוּ חֲמִשָּׁה תּוֹבְעִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״תֵּן לָנוּ פִּקָּדוֹן תְּשׂוּמֶת יָד וְגָזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָנוּ בְּיָדְךָ״; אָמַר לְאֶחָד מֵהֶן: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי פִּקָּדוֹן תְּשׂוּמֶת יָד גָּזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה, וְלֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ וְלֹא לָךְ״ – מַהוּ? אַחֲדָא מִיחַיַּיב,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman:** In a case where **five** people **were suing** another **and they said to him: Give us back our deposit, pledge, and stolen item, and lost item that are in your possession,** and the defendant **said to one of them:** On my **oath your deposit, pledge, stolen item, and lost item** are **not in my possession, and neither** are **yours, and neither** are **yours, and neither** are **yours, and neither** are **yours, what is** the *halakha*? Is **he liable for** only **one** oath for each of the four claimants to whom he said: And neither are yours, since these are considered general oaths?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא אַכׇּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא מִיחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps he is liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim made by each claimant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֲרֵי כָּאן עֶשְׂרִים חַטָּאוֹת. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּפָרֵישׁ – רַבִּי חִיָּיא מִנְיָנָא אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא פָּרֵישׁ – וְשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ פְּרָטָא הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from **that** which **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** in a *baraita*: **There are twenty sin-offerings here** that the defendant must bring. The Gemara elaborates: **What are the circumstances** of the *baraita*? **If** the defendant **specified** each claim of each of the claimants in his oath, does **Rabbi Ḥiyya come to teach us a number?** Obviously, the defendant is liable to bring twenty offerings. **Rather, is it not** that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he did not specify** an oath to each claimant, but he specified it to the first claimant and said: And neither are yours, to each of the remaining claimants? **And** therefore, **conclude from** the *baraita* that such a denial **is** considered **specific.**

Rava poses a question to Rav Nachman: what if five claimants demanded *pikadon*, *tzumat yad*, *gezel*, and *aveida*, and the denier, addressing the first claimant, said *shevuah she'ein lecha be-yadi pikadon ve-tzumat yad ve-gezel va-aveida* — and then for each of the remaining four claimants said simply *ve-lo lecha, ve-lo lecha, ve-lo lecha, ve-lo lecha*, without repeating the enumeration? Does the initial specification of four categories carry over to each subsequent *ve-lo lecha*, making each of those four as well, yielding a total of twenty *shevuot*? Or does specifying for the first claimant not carry over, so that the remaining claimants each incur only one generic *shevuah* — yielding eight total?

The Gemara resolves this from the very *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya: *harei kan esrim chataot*. If he spelled out the four categories for every single claimant, that would be trivial arithmetic, and Rabbi Chiya would not need to announce the sum. The only reason Rabbi Chiya's number is informative is if the case involves *not* repeating the specification — that the initial enumeration carries over automatically to the subsequent *ve-lo lecha*s. The Gemara concludes: *ela lav de-lo parish ushma mina prata hava* — the *chiddush* is that even without repeating the specification, the *prat* carries forward, and the total is indeed twenty.

## Ones ve-Pittuy

### Ones Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָנַסְתָּ וּפִתִּיתָ אֶת בִּתִּי כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? הוֹאִיל וְעִיקַּר קְנָס הוּא תּוֹבֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one accuses another: **You raped or you seduced my daughter,** and the other says: I did not rape and I did not seduce your daughter, to which the father replied: I administer an oath to you, and the defendant said: Amen, the defendant is liable to bring a guilt-offering if it is a false oath, and Rabbi Shimon deems him exempt from liability for a false oath on a deposit. The reason is that the payment for rape or seduction is a fine, and one does not pay a fine based on his own admission; therefore, he is also exempt from bringing a guilt-offering for having taken a false oath. The Rabbis hold that he is liable since he would have been liable to pay compensation for humiliation and degradation resulting from her being raped or seduced, which are monetary claims. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon?** Rabbi Shimon holds that **since** the father **is primarily claiming the fine** and his claim to the other payments is secondary, the defendant is exempt from liability.

The Mishnah presents the case of *anasta u-fissita et biti* — you raped or seduced my daughter. The dispute: *Chachamim* hold the denier is *chayav* a *shevuah* because the claim encompasses not only *knas* — the fixed fifty *kesef* penalty — but also *nezek*, *pegam*, and *boshet*, which are genuine *mamon* obligations. Rabbi Shimon exempts, reasoning that the dominant focus of the claim is the *knas*.

### Ikar Concept

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan explains Rabbi Shimon's rationale: *ho'il ve-ikar knas hu toveia* — since the primary focus (*ikar*) of the claim is the fifty *kesef* penalty, and one does not take a *shevuah* on a *knas*, the *shevuah* obligation falls away even though subsidiary *mamon* components accompany the claim.

### Rava Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁאָמַר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״תֵּן לִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, אָמַר לוֹ: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי חִטִּין״; וְאִשְׁתְּכַח דְּחִטִּין הוּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, הָא שְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין אִית לֵיהּ – דְּפָטוּר; דְּכִי אִשְׁתְּבַע אַחִטִּין – אַקּוּשְׁטָא מִשְׁתְּבַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: To what** case is this explanation of the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon compared?** It is compared **to** a case **where a person said to another: Give me** back **my wheat and barley and spelt that are in your possession,** and the other **said to him:** On my **oath your wheat** is **not in my possession; and it was** then **discovered that it** was only **wheat that he did not have, but he did have** the claimant's **barley and spelt.** In that case, the *halakha* is **that he is exempt** from liability for an oath on a deposit, **as when he took an oath with regard to the wheat, he took a truthful oath.**

Rava attempts to illustrate Rabbi Shimon's reasoning with an analogy: if someone demands *chitin, se'orin, u-khusmin* from you and you deny only the *chitin* — and it turns out you genuinely do not owe him *chitin* — you are *patur*, because the *shevuah* you took (*she'ein lecha be-yadi chitin*) was truthful. You never actually addressed the *se'orin* and *kusmin*, so no false *shevuah* was taken regarding them. Similarly, Rabbi Shimon might say: the denier took a *shevuah* on the *knas* component, which he is entitled to be exempt from, and thus incurs no *chiyuv shevuah*.

### Abaye Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם – בְּחִטִּין קָא כָפַר לֵיהּ, בִּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין לָא קָא כָפַר לֵיהּ. הָכָא – בְּכוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא הוּא דְּקָא כָפַר לֵיהּ! הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״תֵּן לִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי כְּלָל״; וְאִשְׁתְּכַח חִטִּין הוּא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, הָא שְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין אִית לֵיהּ – דְּמִיחַיַּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **Are** the two cases **comparable? There,** in the case of the grain, the defendant **denies** only the other's claim of **wheat,** but he **does not deny his** claim of **barley and spelt. Here,** the defendant **denies the entire matter,** as he claims that he never raped or seduced the man's daughter. Rather, **this** explanation **is comparable only to** a case where one **said to another: Give me** back **my wheat and barley and spelt that are in your possession,** and the defendant said to him: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession at all, and it was** then **discovered** that it was only **wheat that he did not have, but he did have** the claimant's **barley and spelt.** In that case, the *halakha* is **that he is liable** for an oath on a deposit. This, then, cannot be the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon, as he exempts the defendant from liability.

Abaye rejects the analogy: *mi dami?* — the cases are not comparable. In Rava's hypothetical, the denier explicitly denied only the *chitin* and made no statement at all about the *se'orin* and *kusmin*. Hence no false *shevuah* was taken regarding the latter. In the *ones* case, however, the accused denies everything — *"I did not rape your daughter"* — a blanket denial that implicitly encompasses *nezek*, *pegam*, and *boshet* along with the *knas*.

Abaye offers a corrected analogy: if someone demanded *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin* and the denier responded *shevuah she'ein lecha be-yadi klum* — a total denial — and it later emerged that he did not owe the *chitin* but did owe the *se'orin* and *kusmin*, that person is transparently *chayav*, since he took a false *shevuah* that denied *se'orin* and *kusmin* which he genuinely owed, even if the *chitin* was not the *ikar* of the claim.

### Ikar Limits

The Gemara acknowledges that Abaye's corrected analogy makes clear why the *ikar* concept does not exempt in a case of total denial. Taking a false *shevuah* on any part of a legitimate *mamon* claim renders one *chayav*, regardless of whether that part was the primary focus.

### Knas vs. Mammon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְדִבְרֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן – קְנָס הוּא תּוֹבֵעַ, וְלֹא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם; לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים – אַף בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם הוּא תּוֹבֵעַ. בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: לָא שָׁבֵיק אִינִישׁ מִידֵּי דְּקִיץ, וְתָבַע מִידֵּי דְּלָא קִיץ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא שָׁבֵיק מִידֵּי דְּכִי מוֹדֵה בֵּיהּ לָא מִיפְּטַר, וְתָבַע מִידֵּי דְּכִי מוֹדֵה בֵּיהּ מִיפְּטַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** According **to the statement of Rabban Shimon,** the father **is claiming** only the **fine and not** the compensation for **humiliation and degradation** resulting from his daughter's having been raped or seduced. According **to the statement of the Rabbis, he is also claiming** payment for **humiliation and degradation** resulting from her having been raped or seduced. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree? Rav Pappa said: Rabbi Shimon holds: A person does not forgo** a claim to **something with a fixed** value, such as a fine, **and claim something whose** value is **not fixed** and requires an appraisal, such as humiliation and degradation resulting from being raped or seduced. **And the Rabbis hold** that one does **not forgo** a claim to **something that** the defendant **is not exempt** from paying **if he admits** his guilt, **and claim something that** the defendant **is exempt** from paying **if he admits** his guilt, such as a fine.

When Rabbi Yochanan's teaching is transmitted via Ravin, the formulation is more precise: *le-divrei Rabbi Shimon, knas hu toveia ve-lo boshet u-pegam* — according to Rabbi Shimon, the claimant in the *ones* case is demanding only the *knas*, not the *boshet* and *pegam*. This is the crux: Rabbi Shimon reads the claim as focused exclusively on the fixed penalty. *Le-divrei Chachamim*, the Sages read the claimant as seeking all components — *knas* and *mamon* alike.

Rav Pappa articulates the underlying disagreement: *Rabbi Shimon savar lo shevik inish midi de-kayitz u-vatir midi de-lo kayitz* — Rabbi Shimon holds that a person does not abandon a well-defined, fixed sum in pursuit of an uncertain or less-defined one. Since the fifty *kesef knas* is a precisely defined amount, when someone brings such a claim, his legal focus is entirely on that fixed sum; the *boshet* and *pegam* are a separate matter not operative in this proceeding. The Sages disagree: *lo shevik inish midi de-ei modi bei lo miftar u-vatir midi de-ei modi bei miftar* — a person would never ignore a component of the claim for which admission would create inescapable liability (*mamon*), attending only to the *knas* component from which admission would discharge him. Therefore, the *mamon* aspects are always implicitly part of the demand, rendering the blanket denial a false *shevuah* on those components as well.

## Shevuat HaDayanim

### Modeh be-Miktzat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁבוּעַת הַדַּיָּינִין – הַטַּעֲנָה שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף, וְהַהוֹדָאָה בְּשָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. וְאִם אֵין הַהוֹדָאָה מִמִּין הַטַּעֲנָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: By Torah law, the **oath** imposed **by the judges** upon one who admits to part of a claim is administered only when **the claim is** for the value of at least **two silver** *ma'a*, **and the** defendant's **admission is** for **the value of** at least **one *peruta*. And** furthermore, **if the admission is not of** the same **type** as **the claim,** i.e., the defendant admitted to a debt that the claimant did not claim, the defendant is **exempt** from taking an oath.

The new chapter addresses *shevuat ha-dayanim* — a *shevuah* imposed by the *beit din* rather than taken voluntarily or demanded by one party. The paradigmatic case is *modeh be-miktzat*: Reuven claims Shimon owes him money; Shimon partially admits and partially denies, and the court imposes a *shevuah* on the disputed remainder.

### Minimum Amounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? ״שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא פְּרוּטָה״ – פָּטוּר. ״שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף וּפְרוּטָה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא פְּרוּטָה״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If the claimant said to the defendant: **I have two silver** *ma'a* **in your possession,** and the latter responded: **You have only one *peruta*,** a coin made from copper, **in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath (see 39b). But if the claimant said: **I have two silver** *ma'a* **and one *peruta* in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only one *peruta* in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath.

The Mishnah establishes threshold requirements. The *taanah* — the claim — must be at least *shtei chesef*, two units of silver. The *hoda'ah* — the admission — must be worth at least a *perutah*. The case: if the claim was exactly *shtei kesef* and the defendant admitted only to a *perutah* (denying the balance), he is *patur*. Why?

Two readings emerge. On the simpler reading, the Mishnah means that the portion *denied* must be at least *shtei kesef*. When the claim is only *shtei kesef* and the defendant admits to a *perutah*, the denial amounts to less than *shtei kesef*, insufficient for *modeh be-miktzat*. On the alternative reading, there is no minimum denial; the issue is *mimain ha-taanah* — the admission must come from the same category as the claim. The Gemara will address both.

The Mishnah then gives the case of *shtei kesef u-perutah*: the claim is two *kesef* plus a *perutah*, the defendant admits only to the *perutah* and denies the rest — here he is *chayav*, because he denied a full *shtei kesef*. The straightforward reading follows: the denial of a full *shtei kesef* creates *modeh be-miktzat*.

### Mimain ha-Tana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ – פָּטוּר. ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא חֲמִשִּׁים דִּינָר״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If the claimant said: **I have one hundred dinars in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **Nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** he is **exempt,** as he denies the entire claim. But if the claimant said: **I have one hundred dinars in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only fifty dinars in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath, as he admitted to a part of the claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מָנָה לְאַבָּא בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא חֲמִישִּׁים דִּינָר״ – פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the claimant said: My late **father had one hundred dinars in your possession,** and I am now claiming them, and the defendant responded: **You have only fifty dinars in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath, **as he is like one returning a lost item,** since he could have easily denied the entire claim.

The Mishnah addresses the requirement that admission be *mi-main ha-taanah* — from the same category as what was claimed. The case of *maneh la-abba be-yadcha* — if the claimant says "my father deposited a hundred *dinar* with you" and the defendant admits to fifty — the defendant is *patur*, because this is *ke-meishiv aveidah*: since the claimant has no direct personal relationship with the loan and no certain knowledge, the defendant, if he wished to lie, could simply deny everything without it being implausible. Accordingly, his partial admission does not generate the compulsion that creates *chiyuv shevuah*.

### Denial & Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, אָמַר לוֹ ״הֵן״; לְמָחָר אָמַר לוֹ: ״תְּנֵהוּ לִי״, ״נְתַתִּיו לָךְ״ – פָּטוּר. ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna discusses other cases where the defendant denies an entire claim. In a case where one said to another: **I have one hundred dinars in your possession,** and the latter **said to him: Yes,** I acknowledge that claim; and **the next day** the claimant **said to him: Give** the money **to me,** and the defendant responded: **I** already **gave it to you,** he is **exempt.** But if he responded: **Nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** i.e., he denies that a debt ever existed, he is **liable** to pay, as he already admitted that he owed him the amount.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, אָמַר לוֹ ״הֵן״; ״אַל תִּתְּנֵהוּ לִי אֶלָּא בְּעֵדִים״, לְמָחָר אָמַר לוֹ ״תְּנֵהוּ לִי״, ״נְתַתִּיו לָךְ״ – חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לִיתְּנוֹ בְּעֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the claimant said: **I have one hundred dinars in your possession,** and the defendant **said to him: Yes,** to which the claimant responded: **Give** the money **to me only in** the presence of **witnesses,** then if **the next day** the claimant **said to him: Give** the money **to me,** and the defendant responded: **I** already **gave it to you,** he is **liable** to pay, **as** he is **required to give it** to him **in** the presence of **witnesses,** and he cannot prove that he did so.

The Mishnah continues. *Maneh li be-yadcha* — if the claim is a hundred *dinar* and the defendant says *ein lecha be-yadi ela chamishim dinar*, he is *chayav*. If he says *ein lecha be-yadi klum* — he is *patur*, since *kofer ha-kol* does not generate a court-imposed *shevuah*. The *migo*-based exemption from *shevuah* when admitting partially — why can't the admitter say "I could just as easily have denied everything"? — is addressed by Rava's principle elsewhere: one is not so brazen as to deny money owed to a benefactor who lent it to you.

### Object Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לִיטְרָא זָהָב יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא לִיטְרָא כֶּסֶף״ – פָּטוּר. ״דִּינַר זָהָב יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא דִּינָר כֶּסֶף וּטְרֵיסִית וּפוּנְדָּיוֹת וּפְרוּטָה״ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהַכֹּל מִין מַטְבֵּעַ אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna resumes discussion of the oath imposed by the court in a case where the defendant admits to a part of a claim. If the claimant said: **I have a *litra*,** i.e., a specific weight, **of gold in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only a *litra* of silver in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath, as his admission relates to a different item than that which the claim relates to. But if the claimant said: **I have a gold dinar in your possession,** and the defendant responded: **You have only a silver dinar, or a *tereisit*, or a *pundeyon*, or a *peruta* in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath, **as** they are **all** of **one type;** they are all **coins.** Since the claim concerns money, the difference between the different types of coins is disregarded, as the claim is essentially referring to the monetary value, not to a specific type of coin.

The Mishnah distinguishes between categories of objects. *Litra zahav yesh li be-yadcha — ein lecha be-yadi ela litra kesef*: claiming a pound of gold and admitting to a pound of silver — *patur*, because these are categorically different objects. Admitting to silver when gold was claimed is not an admission within the claim but rather an admission to a different thing entirely.

### Cash Unity

*Dinar zahav yesh li be-yadcha — ein lecha be-yadi ela dinar kesef, trasiyot, pundaiyot, o perutah*: claiming a gold *dinar* and admitting to a silver *dinar*, or other types of coins — *chayav*, because *she-ha-kol min matbeah achat* — all coins belong to one category. When money of any denomination is claimed, an admission to money of lesser denomination is an admission within the same category. Unlike gold and silver as commodities, coins as currencies all constitute *mamon*, and the admission is genuinely *mi-main ha-taanah*.

### Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין, וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִין – פָּטוּר. וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If one **claimed** that another owes **him wheat, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing **him barley,** he is **exempt; and Rabban Gamliel deems** him **liable** to take an oath. According to Rabban Gamliel, one who admits to a part of the claim is liable to take an oath even if the admission is not of the same type as the claim.

The Mishnah records: *tano chittin ve-hodu lo bi-se'orin — patur*. If wheat was claimed and barley admitted, he is exempt — the two are different categories. *Rabban Gamliel mechayev* — Rabban Gamliel holds him liable. This is a striking ruling, especially since all the preceding cases followed the stricter view. The *Gemara* will need to reconcile Rabban Gamliel's position with the Mishnah's general framework.

### Barrels & Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַטּוֹעֵן לַחֲבֵירוֹ בְּכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן, וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בְּקַנְקַנִּים – אַדְמוֹן אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְהוֹדָה לוֹ מִקְצָת מִמִּין הַטַּעֲנָה, יִשָּׁבַע. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הַהוֹדָאָה מִמִּין הַטַּעֲנָה. אָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: רוֹאֶה אֲנִי אֶת דִּבְרֵי אַדְמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who claims** that **another** owes him **jugs of oil, and** the latter then **admitted** that he owes **him pitchers,** i.e., the jugs themselves, but not the oil, **Admon says: Since he admitted to him** with regard to **a part** of the claim, and his admission was **of the** same **type** as **the claim,** i.e., the claim included both containers and oil and he admitted to owing him containers, **he must take an oath. And the Rabbis say: The** partial **admission** in this case **is not of** the same **type** as **the claim,** as he completely denied owing him oil. **Rabban Gamliel said: I see the statement of Admon** as correct.

*Ha-tone le-chavero be-kad shemen ve-hodu lo be-kankanim*: claiming a barrel of oil, and the defendant admits to the barrel but not to the oil. *Admon* says this counts as *modeh be-miktzat* because the barrel is part of what was claimed. The Sages disagree: *ein hoda'ah mi-main ha-taanah* — the real claim is about the oil; the barrel is merely its container, not the object of the claim. Rabban Gamliel endorses Admon's view: *ro'eh ani et divrei Admon*, expressing the opinion that admitting to the barrel is a meaningful acknowledgment within the overall claim.

### Land Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְעָנוֹ כֵּלִים וְקַרְקָעוֹת; וְהוֹדָה בַּכֵּלִים וְכָפַר בַּקַּרְקָעוֹת, בַּקַּרְקָעוֹת וְכָפַר בַּכֵּלִים – פָּטוּר. הוֹדָה בְּמִקְצָת הַקַּרְקָעוֹת – פָּטוּר. בְּמִקְצָת הַכֵּלִים – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהַנְּכָסִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן אַחְרָיוּת זוֹקְקִים אֶת הַנְּכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן אַחְרָיוּת לִישָּׁבַע עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If one **claimed** that another owes **him vessels and land, and** the defendant **admitted to** owing him **vessels but denied** the claim of **land,** or conversely, he admitted **to** owing him **land but denied** the claim of **vessels,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath, as oaths are not taken concerning claims involving land. If **he admitted to part** of the claim about **the land,** he is **exempt.** If he admitted **to part** of the claim about **the vessels,** he is **liable** to take an oath concerning the entire claim, **as property that does not serve as a guarantee,** i.e., movable property, **binds the property that serves as a guarantee,** i.e., the land, so that the oath about the movable property can be extended to require him **to take an oath concerning** the land as well.

The Mishnah addresses the intersection of *karka* with *modeh be-miktzat*. *Tano keilim ve-karka'ot ve-hodu be-keilim ve-kafar be-karka'ot, be-karka'ot ve-kafar be-keilim — patur* in both variants. The exemption applies whether land is the admitted or the denied component: land cannot serve as the basis for generating a *shevuah*. Even *hoda'ah be-miktzat al karka'ot* — a partial admission regarding land alone — yields *patur*, since land cannot trigger a court-imposed *shevuah*.

### Zokek Principle

However, there is an important qualification. *Be-miktzas ha-keilim chayav*: if the claim included both *keilim* and *karka*, and the defendant admitted to part of the *keilim* while denying the rest of the *keilim* and all of the *karka*, he is *chayav* a *shevuah* — including on the *karka*. This is the *zokek* principle: *she-nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut zokkein et ha-nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut le-hishava aleihem* — movable property (*nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut*) that is admitted to and denied can pull land (*nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut*) into the *shevuah* along with it. As long as the admission and denial each include *keilim*, the court can impose a *shevuah* encompassing the land as well.

### Eligibility Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל טַעֲנַת חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, וְאֵין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אֶת הַקָּטָן; אֲבָל נִשְׁבָּעִין לַקָּטָן וְלַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **One does not take an oath concerning the claim of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor. And** the court **does not administer an oath to a minor. But one does take an oath to a minor, or to** a representative of **the Temple** treasury with regard to consecrated property.

Finally, the Mishnah establishes rules of eligibility. *Ein nishvaim al taanas cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* — one does not take a *shevuah* in response to a claim brought by a *cheresh*, a *shoteh*, or a *katan*, since their claims lack the legal standing of a competent adult's *taanah*. *Ve-ein mashbiin et ha-katan* — a *katan* cannot be required to take a *shevuah*. However: *aval nishvaim le-katan u-le-hekdesh* — one does take a *shevuah* in certain contexts involving a *katan* or *hekdesh* (consecrated property). The apparent tension — earlier excluding the *katan* as a claimant, here including him as one to whom a *shevuah* is taken — will be the subject of the *Gemara*'s analysis in subsequent discussion.
