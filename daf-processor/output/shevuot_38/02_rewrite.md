# Shevuot 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gezel Karka Debate

### R. Yochanan vs. R. Eleaz…

The daf opens by picking up approximately two-thirds of the way through 37b, with Rav Yirmiyah addressing Rabbi Abahu. The topic at hand is whether a *shevuah* may be taken when the underlying obligation involves *shibud karkos* — a lien on land. The Gemara had already established that this question was the subject of a dispute between two Amoraim: Rabbi Yochanan, who held that one is *patur* from a *shevuah* in such a case because *karka* is categorically excluded from the laws of *shevuah* and its associated *korban*, and Rabbi Eleazar, who held one is *chayav*. With this framework in place, the Gemara now asks whether these two Amoraic positions can be mapped directly onto an underlying Tannaitic dispute.

### R. Eliezer vs. Sages

The Gemara proposes: *leima Rabbi Yochanan ve-Rabbi Elazar be-plugta de-Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbanan kamiflegi* — let us say that Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Eleazar are simply following the Tannaitic debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages.

### Stolen Field Flooded

The relevant Tannaitic dispute concerns the case of *ha-gozel sadeh me-chavero ve-shatafah nahar* — one who steals a field from his fellow and then that field is flooded by a river. Rabbi Eliezer rules that the thief is *chayav le-ha'amid lo sadeh* — he must provide a replacement field — because the act of stealing the field created a *gezeilah* obligation, and just as one who steals a chattel and it is destroyed must pay, so too here. The Sages, however, say *omer lo harei shelcha lefanecha* — he may say, "Here is what is yours before you." According to the Sages, *karka* is categorically excluded from *gezeilah*. The boundary-crosser never truly "stole" anything in the legally actionable sense, since the land was never removed or possessed in the manner that movable property can be. Therefore, when the flood came, it was the claimant's own field that was destroyed, and the wrongdoer bears no obligation to replace it.

### Ribui u-Mi'ut Analysis

The Gemara explains that this Tannaitic dispute flows from a methodological disagreement in *derashah*: Rabbi Eliezer employs *ribui u-mi'ut*, while the Rabbanan employ *klal u-prat*. It is worth pausing on this distinction, as it drives much of the analysis here.

The relevant verses include the phrase *ve-chichesh ba-amito* — denying one's fellow — combined with *ve-heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal*, the obligation to return what was stolen. The first expansive term (*ribui*) includes everything; the middle specification (*mi'ut*) narrows, offering examples such as *pikadon*, *tzumat yad*, and *gezel*; and the concluding expansive term (*o mi-kol asher yishava alav la-sheker*, "or from all upon which he swears falsely") broadens again. Under *ribui u-mi'ut ve-ribui*, the middle term is not a limiting list but a narrowing gesture. The final *ribui* then expands to include everything similar to the middle examples — excluding only what is maximally dissimilar.

Under *klal u-prat u-klal*, by contrast, the middle term functions as a limiting list, and the final broadening yields only *ke-ein ha-prat* — things that share the defining characteristics of the specific examples. The Rabbanan abstract from the *prat*: the specific items mentioned are *davar ha-mitaltel ve-gufo mamon* — movable objects with intrinsic value. This yields three exclusions: *karka'ot* are excluded because they are not movable; *avadim* are excluded because slaves were conceptually assimilated to land; and *shtarot* are excluded because, although a promissory note can be moved, it has no intrinsic value of its own.

### Shtar Exclusion

Under Rabbi Eliezer's *ribui u-mi'ut*, the question of what is excluded by the *mi'ut* receives a specific answer: *mi'et shtarot*. The only category excluded from *gezeilah* obligations is *shtarot*, since a *shtar* — a promissory note — has no intrinsic worth. The underlying loan is not the piece of paper, and destroying the paper does not destroy the debt. Land, by contrast, does have intrinsic value, and Rabbi Eliezer therefore includes it. The Rabbanan would add the further requirement that the object be movable, which land by definition is not.

### Amoraic Application

The mapping now seems neat: Rabbi Eleazar the Amora, who holds one is *chayav* for a *shevuah* relating to *karka*, would be following Rabbi Eliezer, who holds *karka* is subject to *gezeilah*; Rabbi Yochanan, who exempts, would be following the Rabbanan, who exclude *karka* from *gezeilah*. The Gemara notes that the relevant *pasuk*, *ve-chi yecheta be-amito*, appears in the context of *shevuah*, so it is being used to derive both the laws of *gezeilah* and the laws of *shevuah* simultaneously.

However, the Gemara pushes back: *amar lei lo*. The parallel is not forced. For Rabbi Eleazar, who obligates a *shevuah* regarding *karka*, he indeed must be within Rabbi Eliezer's framework — if *karka* is not subject to *gezeilah*, it certainly cannot be subject to a *shevuah* built on that framework. But Rabbi Yochanan's exemption from *shevuah* does not require him to follow the Rabbanan on *gezeilah*. He could hold like Rabbi Eliezer on *gezeilah* — that *karka* is subject to *gezeilah* — and yet still exempt from *shevuah*, because the verse governing *shevuah* says *mi-kol asher yishava alav la-sheker*, and *mi-kol* — "from all" — is a narrower formulation than *kol*, "all." Even Rabbi Eliezer might agree that *shevuah* applies to a more restricted category than *gezeilah*.

### Mishnah Arguments

The Gemara then attempts to derive textual support from arguments of silence in the Mishnah. Rav Pappa in the name of Rava observes that our Mishnah gives the example of *ganavta et shori* — you stole my ox — as the paradigm case. Why does it not give the more novel example of *ganavta et avdi*, you stole my slave? Since slaves are legally assimilated to *karka*, if slaves were subject to *shevuah*, that would have been a more striking *chiddush* to cite. The omission suggests that slaves and, by extension, land are excluded. However, Rav Papi in the name of Rava counters by noting the concluding *klal* of the Mishnah: *kol ha-meshalem al pi atzmo chayav* — whatever you would pay upon your own admission obligates you. This general formulation seems to include slaves. The two arguments from silence neutralize each other, and the Gemara concludes: *ela me-ha lika le-mishma mina* — from either of these, nothing can be proven.

## Specification Debate

### Klal vs. Prat

The Gemara moves on to address the next portion of the Mishnah. The Mishnah presents two contrasting situations: if five claimants all demand the return of a *pikadon* and he responds with a single denial — *shvua she'ein lachem be-yadi* — he is *chayav* only one *shevuah*. But if he says *she'ein lecha be-yadi ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha*, addressing each one individually, he is *chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — one *shevuah* for each person. Rabbi Eleazar adds that one must say *shevuah* at the end for each denial to count as separate. Rabbi Shimon goes further, requiring *shevuah* to be stated explicitly before each individual denial.

### R. Meir Framework

A *baraita* introduces a dispute among Tannaim. The *baraita* states: *klal* — a general denial — obligates only one *shevuah*; *prat* — specification — obligates one *shevuah* for each. This is *divrei Rabbi Meir*.

### R. Yehuda Vav Rule

Rabbi Yehuda then states: *shvua lo lecha ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — if one says "not you, and not you, and not you," with the *vav* connective between each denial, he is *chayav* for each. This formulation sounds identical to what Rabbi Meir calls *prat*, raising the immediate question: what exactly is the dispute?

### Shmuel Inference

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel reads the *baraita* as follows: *klalo shel Rabbi Meir prato shel Rabbi Yehuda* — what Rabbi Meir calls a *klal* is what Rabbi Yehuda calls a *prat*, and conversely, *klalo shel Rabbi Yehuda* is *prato shel Rabbi Meir*. In other words, Shmuel reads the two authorities as strict inverses. For Rabbi Yehuda, when you say *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha* — with *vav* connectives — those are separate *shevuot*, what he calls *prat*. What Rabbi Yehuda calls *klal* would be the case without *vav*: *lo lecha, lo lecha, lo lecha*. For Rabbi Meir, this is reversed: with the *vav* is *klal*, and without the *vav* is *prat*.

This inversion — that the absence of a *vav* could make a statement more of a *klal* than the presence of one — is genuinely difficult to understand intuitively. One might speculate that the *vav* extending the word *shevuah* across multiple denials unifies them into one statement, whereas without the *vav* each stands alone. But the lecturer frankly acknowledges that this reasoning is elusive, and invites the reader to sit with that difficulty.

### R. Yochanan Counter

Rabbi Yochanan reads the situation differently. He maintains that everyone agrees the formulation with *vav* — *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha* — constitutes a *prat*, separate *shevuot*. The debate, Rabbi Yochanan holds, concerns only the formulation without *vav*: *lo lecha, lo lecha, lo lecha*. Rabbi Meir considers that a *prat* as well; Rabbi Yehuda considers it a *klal*.

The textual foundation of Shmuel's reading: since Rabbi Yehuda specifically states that the *vav* formulation creates separate *shevuot*, the implication (*miklal*) is that in Rabbi Meir's view that formulation creates a *klal*. This is a straightforward *diyuk* — Rabbi Yehuda is declaring "this is my position," implying that he is correcting Rabbi Meir, who held the opposite. Shmuel uses this *diyuk* from the *baraita* to ground his inversion.

Rabbi Yochanan objects: the *diyuk* is not compelling. Shmuel adds that when debating, one typically states the position one *disagrees* with, not the one one *agrees* with. But Rabbi Yochanan reads Rabbi Yehuda as saying: here is the case where I agree with you — the *vav* formulation is separate — but I disagree with you on the case without *vav*, where you call it *prat* and I call it *klal*. Shmuel's rejoinder: that is not how disputants present their positions. You state the disagreement, not the agreement. The *diyuk* from the *baraita* therefore remains a strong textual support for Shmuel's reading.

### Textual Variants

Rabbi Yochanan supports his reading from the Mishnah: since the Mishnah itself — which we presume reflects Rabbi Meir's position as *stam* — describes *ein lachem be-yadi* as the *klal* case, then any formulation involving individual *lo lecha* — with or without *vav* — must be a *prat* for Rabbi Meir. If the *vav* formulation were itself a *klal* for Rabbi Meir, the Mishnah would have taught that case instead of, or in addition to, *ein lachem be-yadi*. From the Mishnah's silence on the *vav* case as a *klal*, Rabbi Yochanan infers that for Rabbi Meir it is a *prat*.

Shmuel could respond: *kol omer ve-lo lecha k'omer shevuah she'ein lachem be-yadi dami* — anyone who says *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha* is effectively saying the same as *ein lachem*, the *vav* binding them into one category, so the Mishnah need not spell it out separately.

The Gemara then attempts to resolve the dispute by examining the wording of the Mishnah more carefully. The Mishnah's various cases — *ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha ve-lo lecha*, the *pikadon ve-tzumat yad ve-gezel ve-aveida* cases — are all presented with *vav*s, and the Mishnah treats them as cases of *prat*. Each time Shmuel's position would require the Gemara to propose emending the Mishnah to remove the *vav*, the Gemara is forced to say *tani* — "read it without the *vav*." Eventually, the Gemara grows exasperated: *ve-hai tana kol hachi shabish tani ve-azil?* — is this *tanna* so confused that he gets it wrong in every single case? If the *vav* is a *klal* for Rabbi Meir, why would the Mishnah consistently write *vav* in contexts that are meant to be *prat*?

### Attribution Issue

The Gemara concedes the point about the Mishnah's text but resists attributing it to Rabbi Meir. Instead: *ella mani Rebbi hi* — the Mishnah follows *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi). There exists a position of *Rebbi*, expressed in the context of *piggul* thoughts during *shechita*, that whether you said *k'zayis k'zayis* or *k'zayis u-kzayis* — with or without a *vav* — they are treated as separate statements. Rebbi holds that any formulation of separate items, regardless of the connective, constitutes distinct, separable statements. On this reading, our Mishnah's consistent use of the *vav* formulation as *prat* reflects Rebbi's position — not Rabbi Meir's — leaving Shmuel's reading of Rabbi Meir potentially intact.

## Multiple Categories

### Afilu Meaning

The Mishnah introduces Rabbi Meir's statement: *afilu chita u-seora ve-kusemes, chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — even if the items demanded were wheat, barley, and spelt, he is *chayav* for each. The word *afilu* — "even" — signals that this is a surprising or non-obvious ruling. Rav Acha son of Rav Ika explains: *afilu chita bichlal chitin* — even if the claimant said *chita* (singular), we interpret it as *chitin* (the generic category), and similarly for the others. The *taanah* is always interpreted generously enough to constitute a *taanah* of at least *shaveh perutah*; we do not read *chita* as a single grain of wheat.

### Combining Categories

Rabbi Yochanan rules that if someone is owed *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin*, and none of the individual categories amounts to a *perutah* on its own, *perutah mi-kula mitztarefes* — a *perutah* may be assembled across all categories combined. The question then becomes: in which case does this statement apply — the case of *prat* (where each category is individually denied) or the case of *klal* (where everything is denied together in one statement)?

### Generic Oath Debate

The question depends on a dispute between two opinions regarding the case of *prat*. One opinion holds that when the Mishnah says he is *chayav al kol achat ve-achat*, he is *chayav* only for those individual specific *shevuot* — there is no additional generic *shevuah* bundling them all. The other opinion holds that in a *prat* case, in addition to the individual *shevuot*, there is also one general *shevuah* that encompasses all of them together, since before enumerating each item the denier effectively made a blanket denial of everything. If the second view is correct, then Rabbi Yochanan's statement — that fractions combine to a *perutah* — applies even within the *prat* case, because the general *shevuah* ties all the categories together. If the first view is correct, Rabbi Yochanan's statement applies only to the *klal* case, where everything was denied as one.

### R. Chiya Counts

A *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya states: *harei kan chamish esrei chataot* — here we have fifteen *chataot*. The scenario is five claimants each demanding *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin*, each category denied individually. Five times three yields fifteen. The Gemara challenges: *ve-i ita esrim havyun* — if there were also a general *shevuah* for each claimant encompassing all three categories, the count should be twenty. The Gemara deflects: perhaps Rabbi Chiya was only counting the specific *shevuot*, not denying the existence of the general ones.

This deflection is reinforced by a second *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya that says *harei kan esrim chataot* — twenty. Rather than treating this as a contradiction, the two *baraitot* are reconciled: fifteen reflects the case of *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin* — three specific categories — counted for five claimants; twenty reflects the case of *pikadon*, *tzumat yad*, *gezel*, and *aveida* — four categories — counted for five claimants. Neither version requires positing a general *shevuah* alongside the specific ones; the different totals simply reflect different underlying case structures.

### Carry-Over Specification

Rava poses a question to Rav Nachman: what if five claimants demanded *pikadon*, *tzumat yad*, *gezel*, and *aveida*, and the denier, addressing the first claimant, said *shevuah she'ein lecha be-yadi pikadon ve-tzumat yad ve-gezel va-aveida* — and then for each of the remaining four claimants said simply *ve-lo lecha, ve-lo lecha, ve-lo lecha, ve-lo lecha*, without repeating the enumeration? Does the initial specification of four categories carry over to each subsequent *ve-lo lecha*, making each of those four as well, yielding a total of twenty *shevuot*? Or does specifying for the first claimant not carry over, so that the remaining claimants each incur only one generic *shevuah* — yielding eight total?

The Gemara resolves this from the very *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya: *harei kan esrim chataot*. If he spelled out the four categories for every single claimant, that would be trivial arithmetic, and Rabbi Chiya would not need to announce the sum. The only reason Rabbi Chiya's number is informative is if the case involves *not* repeating the specification — that the initial enumeration carries over automatically to the subsequent *ve-lo lecha*s. The Gemara concludes: *ela lav de-lo parish ushma mina prata hava* — the *chiddush* is that even without repeating the specification, the *prat* carries forward, and the total is indeed twenty.

## Ones ve-Pittuy

### Ones Dispute

The Mishnah presents the case of *anasta u-fissita et biti* — you raped or seduced my daughter. The dispute: *Chachamim* hold the denier is *chayav* a *shevuah* because the claim encompasses not only *knas* — the fixed fifty *kesef* penalty — but also *nezek*, *pegam*, and *boshet*, which are genuine *mamon* obligations. Rabbi Shimon exempts, reasoning that the dominant focus of the claim is the *knas*.

### Ikar Concept

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan explains Rabbi Shimon's rationale: *ho'il ve-ikar knas hu toveia* — since the primary focus (*ikar*) of the claim is the fifty *kesef* penalty, and one does not take a *shevuah* on a *knas*, the *shevuah* obligation falls away even though subsidiary *mamon* components accompany the claim.

### Rava Analogy

Rava attempts to illustrate Rabbi Shimon's reasoning with an analogy: if someone demands *chitin, se'orin, u-khusmin* from you and you deny only the *chitin* — and it turns out you genuinely do not owe him *chitin* — you are *patur*, because the *shevuah* you took (*she'ein lecha be-yadi chitin*) was truthful. You never actually addressed the *se'orin* and *kusmin*, so no false *shevuah* was taken regarding them. Similarly, Rabbi Shimon might say: the denier took a *shevuah* on the *knas* component, which he is entitled to be exempt from, and thus incurs no *chiyuv shevuah*.

### Abaye Objection

Abaye rejects the analogy: *mi dami?* — the cases are not comparable. In Rava's hypothetical, the denier explicitly denied only the *chitin* and made no statement at all about the *se'orin* and *kusmin*. Hence no false *shevuah* was taken regarding the latter. In the *ones* case, however, the accused denies everything — *"I did not rape your daughter"* — a blanket denial that implicitly encompasses *nezek*, *pegam*, and *boshet* along with the *knas*.

Abaye offers a corrected analogy: if someone demanded *chitin*, *se'orin*, and *kusmin* and the denier responded *shevuah she'ein lecha be-yadi klum* — a total denial — and it later emerged that he did not owe the *chitin* but did owe the *se'orin* and *kusmin*, that person is transparently *chayav*, since he took a false *shevuah* that denied *se'orin* and *kusmin* which he genuinely owed, even if the *chitin* was not the *ikar* of the claim.

### Ikar Limits

The Gemara acknowledges that Abaye's corrected analogy makes clear why the *ikar* concept does not exempt in a case of total denial. Taking a false *shevuah* on any part of a legitimate *mamon* claim renders one *chayav*, regardless of whether that part was the primary focus.

### Knas vs. Mammon

When Rabbi Yochanan's teaching is transmitted via Ravin, the formulation is more precise: *le-divrei Rabbi Shimon, knas hu toveia ve-lo boshet u-pegam* — according to Rabbi Shimon, the claimant in the *ones* case is demanding only the *knas*, not the *boshet* and *pegam*. This is the crux: Rabbi Shimon reads the claim as focused exclusively on the fixed penalty. *Le-divrei Chachamim*, the Sages read the claimant as seeking all components — *knas* and *mamon* alike.

Rav Pappa articulates the underlying disagreement: *Rabbi Shimon savar lo shevik inish midi de-kayitz u-vatir midi de-lo kayitz* — Rabbi Shimon holds that a person does not abandon a well-defined, fixed sum in pursuit of an uncertain or less-defined one. Since the fifty *kesef knas* is a precisely defined amount, when someone brings such a claim, his legal focus is entirely on that fixed sum; the *boshet* and *pegam* are a separate matter not operative in this proceeding. The Sages disagree: *lo shevik inish midi de-ei modi bei lo miftar u-vatir midi de-ei modi bei miftar* — a person would never ignore a component of the claim for which admission would create inescapable liability (*mamon*), attending only to the *knas* component from which admission would discharge him. Therefore, the *mamon* aspects are always implicitly part of the demand, rendering the blanket denial a false *shevuah* on those components as well.

With *hadran alach shevuat ha-pikadon*, the chapter of *shevuat ha-pikadon* concludes and the Mishnah of *shevuat ha-dayanim* begins.

## Shevuat HaDayanim

### Modeh be-Miktzat

The new chapter addresses *shevuat ha-dayanim* — a *shevuah* imposed by the *beit din* rather than taken voluntarily or demanded by one party. The paradigmatic case is *modeh be-miktzat*: Reuven claims Shimon owes him money; Shimon partially admits and partially denies, and the court imposes a *shevuah* on the disputed remainder.

### Minimum Amounts

The Mishnah establishes threshold requirements. The *taanah* — the claim — must be at least *shtei chesef*, two units of silver. The *hoda'ah* — the admission — must be worth at least a *perutah*. The case: if the claim was exactly *shtei kesef* and the defendant admitted only to a *perutah* (denying the balance), he is *patur*. Why?

Two readings emerge. On the simpler reading, the Mishnah means that the portion *denied* must be at least *shtei kesef*. When the claim is only *shtei kesef* and the defendant admits to a *perutah*, the denial amounts to less than *shtei kesef*, insufficient for *modeh be-miktzat*. On the alternative reading, there is no minimum denial; the issue is *mimain ha-taanah* — the admission must come from the same category as the claim. The Gemara will address both.

The Mishnah then gives the case of *shtei kesef u-perutah*: the claim is two *kesef* plus a *perutah*, the defendant admits only to the *perutah* and denies the rest — here he is *chayav*, because he denied a full *shtei kesef*. The straightforward reading follows: the denial of a full *shtei kesef* creates *modeh be-miktzat*.

Further cases are given. *Maneh li be-yadcha* — if the claim is a hundred *dinar* and the defendant says *ein lecha be-yadi ela chamishim dinar*, he is *chayav*. If he says *ein lecha be-yadi klum* — he is *patur*, since *kofer ha-kol* does not generate a court-imposed *shevuah*. The *migo*-based exemption from *shevuah* when admitting partially — why can't the admitter say "I could just as easily have denied everything"? — is addressed by Rava's principle elsewhere: one is not so brazen as to deny money owed to a benefactor who lent it to you.

### Mimain ha-Tana

The Mishnah addresses the requirement that admission be *mi-main ha-taanah* — from the same category as what was claimed. The case of *maneh la-abba be-yadcha* — if the claimant says "my father deposited a hundred *dinar* with you" and the defendant admits to fifty — the defendant is *patur*, because this is *ke-meishiv aveidah*: since the claimant has no direct personal relationship with the loan and no certain knowledge, the defendant, if he wished to lie, could simply deny everything without it being implausible. Accordingly, his partial admission does not generate the compulsion that creates *chiyuv shevuah*.

### Denial & Payment

The Mishnah continues. *Maneh li be-yadcha — amar lo hen*: if the defendant admits to the full claim, and the next day the claimant says "give it to me," and the defendant responds *netativ lach* — I already gave it to you — he is *patur*, since once the admission was made he was entitled to repay and may be believed that he did. But if he now says *ein lecha be-yadi*, effectively retracting his earlier admission — he is *chayav*.

The Mishnah then addresses a case where the claimant specified: *al titnehu li ela be-eidim* — "don't give it to me except in the presence of witnesses." The next day the defendant claims *netativ lach*: here he is *chayav*, because the condition was that payment could only be made with witnesses present, and without witnesses his claim of payment is not credible.

### Object Distinctions

The Mishnah distinguishes between categories of objects. *Litra zahav yesh li be-yadcha — ein lecha be-yadi ela litra kesef*: claiming a pound of gold and admitting to a pound of silver — *patur*, because these are categorically different objects. Admitting to silver when gold was claimed is not an admission within the claim but rather an admission to a different thing entirely.

### Cash Unity

*Dinar zahav yesh li be-yadcha — ein lecha be-yadi ela dinar kesef, trasiyot, pundaiyot, o perutah*: claiming a gold *dinar* and admitting to a silver *dinar*, or other types of coins — *chayav*, because *she-ha-kol min matbeah achat* — all coins belong to one category. When money of any denomination is claimed, an admission to money of lesser denomination is an admission within the same category. Unlike gold and silver as commodities, coins as currencies all constitute *mamon*, and the admission is genuinely *mi-main ha-taanah*.

### Rabban Gamliel

The Mishnah records: *tano chittin ve-hodu lo bi-se'orin — patur*. If wheat was claimed and barley admitted, he is exempt — the two are different categories. *Rabban Gamliel mechayev* — Rabban Gamliel holds him liable. This is a striking ruling, especially since all the preceding cases followed the stricter view. The *Gemara* will need to reconcile Rabban Gamliel's position with the Mishnah's general framework.

### Barrels & Oil

*Ha-tone le-chavero be-kad shemen ve-hodu lo be-kankanim*: claiming a barrel of oil, and the defendant admits to the barrel but not to the oil. *Admon* says this counts as *modeh be-miktzat* because the barrel is part of what was claimed. The Sages disagree: *ein hoda'ah mi-main ha-taanah* — the real claim is about the oil; the barrel is merely its container, not the object of the claim. Rabban Gamliel endorses Admon's view: *ro'eh ani et divrei Admon*, expressing the opinion that admitting to the barrel is a meaningful acknowledgment within the overall claim.

### Land Exemption

The Mishnah addresses the intersection of *karka* with *modeh be-miktzat*. *Tano keilim ve-karka'ot ve-hodu be-keilim ve-kafar be-karka'ot, be-karka'ot ve-kafar be-keilim — patur* in both variants. The exemption applies whether land is the admitted or the denied component: land cannot serve as the basis for generating a *shevuah*. Even *hoda'ah be-miktzat al karka'ot* — a partial admission regarding land alone — yields *patur*, since land cannot trigger a court-imposed *shevuah*.

### Zokek Principle

However, there is an important qualification. *Be-miktzas ha-keilim chayav*: if the claim included both *keilim* and *karka*, and the defendant admitted to part of the *keilim* while denying the rest of the *keilim* and all of the *karka*, he is *chayav* a *shevuah* — including on the *karka*. This is the *zokek* principle: *she-nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut zokkein et ha-nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut le-hishava aleihem* — movable property (*nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut*) that is admitted to and denied can pull land (*nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut*) into the *shevuah* along with it. As long as the admission and denial each include *keilim*, the court can impose a *shevuah* encompassing the land as well.

### Eligibility Rules

Finally, the Mishnah establishes rules of eligibility. *Ein nishvaim al taanas cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* — one does not take a *shevuah* in response to a claim brought by a *cheresh*, a *shoteh*, or a *katan*, since their claims lack the legal standing of a competent adult's *taanah*. *Ve-ein mashbiin et ha-katan* — a *katan* cannot be required to take a *shevuah*. However: *aval nishvaim le-katan u-le-hekdesh* — one does take a *shevuah* in certain contexts involving a *katan* or *hekdesh* (consecrated property). The apparent tension — earlier excluding the *katan* as a claimant, here including him as one to whom a *shevuah* is taken — will be the subject of the *Gemara*'s analysis in subsequent discussion.
