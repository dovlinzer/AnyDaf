# Zevachim 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah: Dechuyah Review

### Metzora & Tevul Yom

The daf opens with a brief recapitulation of yesterday's discussion. The *gemara* had been wrestling with the case of the *metzora* who inserts his hands into the *azarah* at the Gate of Nicanor in order to perform *smicha* on his *asham*. The complication arose when the *metzora* was simultaneously a *tevul yom* — one who had immersed in a *mikvah* after a seminal emission (*ba'al keri*) but whose purification would not be complete until nightfall. The *gemara* had offered multiple approaches: perhaps *bia'ah b'miktzat lo shemah bia'ah* — that entering only partially, with one's hands, does not constitute a halachically cognizable entry — or alternatively, that since the Torah had already overridden the prohibition (*hutra*) to permit the *metzora* to insert his hands as part of his purification ritual, that override extended as well to his status as a *ba'al keri*.

### Hutra vs. Dechuyah

The *gemara* then extended that question to the more general issue of *tumah* in the context of communal sacrifices. When a majority of the *tzibur* is *tamei meis* — impure from contact with the dead — and simultaneously *zavim*, would the same logic apply? This led to a broader discussion of whether the status of the *metzora* is one of *hutra* — meaning there was never any prohibition for him in the first place — or whether *tumah* in general is merely *dechuyah*, overridable under pressing circumstances but still intrinsically prohibited. The *gemara*'s concluding formulation is: *michlal d'travayhu svira lehu tumah dechuyah hi b'tzibur* — it can be inferred that both positions in the debate agree that *tumah* is *dechuyah*, not *hutra*, in the context of communal sacrifices. The practical consequence of this distinction is significant: if *tumah* is *dechuyah*, one must minimize the extent of the override and cannot treat the dispensation more expansively than necessary, even though a mechanism for override does exist.

## Bia'ah B'Miktzat at Gate

### Smicha/Shechita Verse

With that summary concluded, the *gemara* turns to the central theme of the day: whether *bia'ah b'miktzat* constitutes a genuine violation. The *gemara* introduces a potential proof: *kol ha-smichot she-hayu sham korei ani aleihem teichef lismicha shechita* — all the acts of *smicha* performed in the *Beis HaMikdash* are governed by the juxtaposition of *smicha* and *shechita* in the verse, which places the two acts in immediate sequence. This juxtaposition obtains for every *korban* — *chutz mizo*, except for the *asham* of the *metzora*, which was performed at the Gate of Nicanor. Because the *metzora* cannot fully enter the *azarah* to have his *smicha* juxtaposed to the *shechita*, the verse's requirement of *teichef lismicha shechita* cannot be fulfilled in his case.

### Gate Geography

To appreciate this fully, the geography of the *Beis HaMikdash* must be kept in mind. The Gate of Nicanor stood precisely at the threshold between the *Ezrat Nashim* — the women's courtyard — and the *azarah* proper, which is the *Ezrat Yisrael* leading into the *Ezrat Kohanim*. Crossing that gate constituted entry into the sanctified precincts of the *azarah*. The distinction between who was permitted where is important: a *mechusar kippurim* — one who has undergone immersion but has not yet brought his purification offering — is permitted in the *Ezrat Nashim* but is forbidden to enter the *azarah* itself. Mid'oraita, a *tamei meis* could likewise proceed as far as the *Ezrat Nashim*, though the rabbis restricted this as well. It is thus on the outer side of the Gate of Nicanor that the *metzora* stands.

### Metzora Restrictions

The verse mandates that the *metzora* cannot enter until the blood of his *chatat* and *asham* have been processed — *ad she-mazin alav midam chatato midam ashamo*. Rashi notes that this formulation is slightly imprecise: the blood of the *chatat* is not literally sprinkled upon him, but the verse is indicating that both the *chatat* and the *asham* must be brought first, and the blood of the *asham* is used in the purification ritual applied to his thumbs, big toes, and right ear. Until this process is complete, the *metzora* is not sufficiently *tahor* to enter.

The natural question presents itself: why not simply have the *metzora* stand at the gate, thrust his hands inside, perform *smicha* on the animal standing within the *azarah*, and then immediately proceed to *shechita*? The fact that the *gemara* does not entertain this solution — and instead indicates that the *smicha* cannot be juxtaposed to the *shechita* — implies that inserting one's hands constitutes a prohibited entry. This cuts against what had been assumed yesterday, when the *gemara* proceeded on the assumption that the *metzora* was permitted by the Torah to insert his hands as part of the ritual. Today's discussion entertains the alternative: that sticking one's hands in is a genuine problem, even for the *metzora*, and therefore the solution must be to bring the animal out to him into the *Ezrat Nashim* so that he can perform *smicha* there. This is itself a remarkable *chiddush*, since it implicitly assumes that *smicha* need not be performed *lifnei Hashem* — with the animal standing inside the *azarah*.

A student correctly noted that this cannot be the complete picture, because the application of the *asham*'s blood to the *metzora*'s thumbs and toes cannot be accomplished by bringing the blood out of the *azarah* — blood of a *korban* may not be taken outside. Therefore, for the blood-application ritual, there is no alternative: the *metzora*'s thumbs must go in, and the Torah implicitly granted that dispensation. The *smicha*, however, presents a different situation — there, we can bring the animal out to him, which is the approach the *gemara* pursues, provided the animal does not need to be physically within the *azarah* for *smicha* to be valid.

### Juxtaposition Problem

The implicit assumption that *bia'ah b'miktzat* is a problem — demonstrated by the fact that we do not simply have the *metzora* extend his hands inside to perform *smicha* — thus serves as a possible proof that *bia'ah b'miktzat shemah bia'ah*. The *gemara* states this directly: *v'im amarta bia'ah b'miktzat lo shemah bia'ah*, let him insert his hands and perform the *smicha* immediately adjacent to the *shechita*. The fact that the *halacha* does not permit this suggests that partial entry is itself a violation.

## Three Solutions

### Merchak Tzafon Answer

The *gemara* answers: *amar Rav Yosef*, this entire passage reflects the position of Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *merchak tzafon* — that the valid northern zone for *kodshei kodashim* is not the broad area generally north of the altar, but rather the area immediately opposite the altar wall itself, not at a distance from it. According to this view, the Gate of Nicanor, which sits roughly in the center of the complex, is too far from the immediate north of the altar. The *metzora* could theoretically insert his hands to perform *smicha* — since *bia'ah b'miktzat* is not the problem — but the animal would then need to be walked to the directly opposite northern zone of the altar for *shechita*, during which time the *metzora* cannot accompany it further into the *azarah*. Hence the *smicha* and *shechita* cannot be juxtaposed. This is not a statement about *bia'ah b'miktzat* at all.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *metzora* can insert his hands and the only obstacle is that the animal needs to be moved north before *shechita*, why not simply cut a small doorway (*pishpash*) at the northern wall of the *azarah*, allowing the *metzora* to stand there and insert his hands directly adjacent to the proper northern zone, thereby achieving *teichef lismicha shechita*? The *gemara* answers in the name of Abaye and Rava that the structure of the *Mikdash* cannot be arbitrarily altered: *ha-kol bi-chtav mi-yad Hashem alai hiskil* — everything was divinely prescribed. If such a doorway cannot be created, then the *mechak tzafon* problem remains even if *bia'ah b'miktzat* is permitted, and the proof fails.

### Kol Kocho Answer

A second version of Rav Yosef's statement offers an entirely different resolution. According to this version, *bia'ah b'miktzat lo shemah bia'ah* is perfectly valid — the *metzora* could in principle insert his hands without transgressing. The problem is a different one: *kol ha-somech rosho ve-rubo machnees* — one who performs *smicha* must lean in with his head and the majority of his body, because *smicha* requires *kol kocho*, one's full bodily weight and force. A mere gentle placement of hands from outside the threshold is insufficient; the person must genuinely press down, putting his back into it, which necessarily means that the greater part of his body crosses the line. And that constitutes a full *bia'ah*, not a *bia'ah b'miktzat* at all. Therefore it is not a question of partial entry — his entry would necessarily be complete, and that is what is forbidden.

### Rabbinic Override

Rav Adda bar Matnah raises a deeper challenge. If *smicha* for the *asham metzora* is biblically required (*d'oraita*), and *teichef lismicha shechita* is likewise a *d'oraita* obligation, then the very fact that the Torah requires both of these for the *metzora* implicitly licenses him to fulfill them — just as the Torah implicitly licenses the insertion of the thumbs for the blood-application ritual. If the Torah truly demands *smicha* followed immediately by *shechita* from this *metzora*, then presumably it also provides the means to do so. Why then is there any problem?

Rav Adda bar Matnah answers: in truth, *d'oraita* the *metzora* does have a license — either to enter with his full body to the north of the altar, or to perform *bia'ah b'miktzat*, or to lean in with *kol kocho*, depending on which obstacle one accepts. The rabbis, however, introduced a rabbinic override restricting this, out of concern for *gezeirah shema yarbeh bifsi'ot* — lest he take more steps than necessary into the forbidden zone. For a *metzora* to whom entry is otherwise categorically prohibited, even one extra step inside is a serious matter, and the rabbis restricted the license to its absolute minimum.

Yet even accepting this rabbinic restriction, a further question remains: if we resolve the problem by bringing the animal out to the *metzora* in the *Ezrat Nashim*, how has the obligation of *smicha* been fulfilled? The animal is outside the *azarah* — is that a valid *smicha* at all?

## Smicha Location

### Tahorim Derasha

The *gemara* now confronts this question directly by examining the scriptural basis for *smicha* requirements. The verse states *vesamach veshachat* — he shall lean and he shall slaughter — and from this juxtaposition the *gemara* derives: *mah smicha b'tehorim af shechita b'tehorim* — just as *smicha* must be performed in a state of *tahara*, so too *shechita* must be performed in a state of *tahara*. The premise of this *derasha* — that *smicha* requires *tahara* — itself presupposes that *smicha* must be performed inside the *azarah*, where only those who are *tahor* may stand. If the animal could simply be brought outside to an impure person for *smicha*, there would be no basis for requiring *tahara* as a precondition for *smicha*.

### Implications

The *gemara* draws out the full implication: if one were to say that *teichef lismicha shechita* is not *d'oraita*, then *smicha* could also be performed *b'temei'im* — with impure persons — by bringing the animal out to them in the *Ezrat Nashim*. But the *derasha* clearly presupposes both that *smicha* is performed inside the *azarah* (*lifnei Hashem*) and that it is immediately followed by *shechita*. Hence both the location requirement and *teichef lismicha shechita* are *d'oraita*. The solution of bringing the animal out to the *metzora* in the *Ezrat Nashim* is therefore in direct tension with these established requirements.

### Non-Obligatory Smicha

The *gemara*'s resolution is to reverse the assumptions entirely: *semikhat asham metzora lav d'oraita* — the *smicha* performed as part of the *asham metzora* ritual is not a biblical obligation at all. *Teichef lismicha shechita* is indeed *d'oraita*, but since *smicha* itself is not required for the *asham metzora*, the entire framework collapses. There is no biblical mandate being bypassed, and therefore no implicit license is created by the Torah.

Why is *smicha* not required here? Rashi explains that the *asham metzora* is unusual among sacrifices in that its entire purpose is instrumental — it exists to generate the blood that will be applied to the *metzora*'s thumbs, toes, and ear as part of his purification. Unlike an ordinary *chatat* or *asham*, where the owner brings the *korban* as an act of spiritual identification and atonement, the *asham metzora* is not truly being offered as an expression of the owner's religious act; it is a means to produce the purifying blood. Tosafot raises difficulties with this reasoning, but that is the *gemara*'s position.

The full picture can now be stated coherently. The *metzora* cannot insert his hands for *smicha* — whether because of *bia'ah b'miktzat*, or because *kol kocho* would make it a full *bia'ah*, or because of the *merchak tzafon* issue. He has no implicit license to enter for *smicha* because *smicha* is not actually required of him. The solution of bringing the animal out to him in the *Ezrat Nashim* is therefore not a bypass of a *d'oraita* obligation; since the *smicha* is not biblically required anyway, bringing the animal to him constitutes a rabbinic pseudo-*smicha*, performed in the spirit of the ritual even though it does not satisfy any biblical standard. The parallel is the well-known *gemara* about women performing *smicha* — the *tanna* records that the animal was brought into the *Ezrat Nashim* and women laid their hands upon it, *lo she-hasemicha banashim ella k'dei la'asot nachas ruach lanashim* — not because *smicha* is obligatory for women, but as a meaningful gesture. Similarly here, the *metzora* performs a symbolic *smicha* in the *Ezrat Nashim*, which suffices precisely because no biblical *smicha* is demanded of him. The thumbs-in ritual for applying the blood remains in a separate category: there, entry is unavoidable, the Torah implicitly licensed it, and the rabbis left that license intact.

## Ravina's Solution

### Lashes for Touching

The *gemara* now returns to the original question from yesterday's discussion with a fresh approach, attributed to Ravina. Recall the problem: even granting that the *metzora* has a general override permitting his hands to enter the *azarah* for purposes of his purification, why can he insert his thumbs when he is simultaneously a *ba'al keri* — a status that independently prohibits entry? Yesterday's answer was that the *ba'al keri* status is subsumed within the general *metzora* override. Ravina offers an entirely different answer.

The statement that *bia'ah b'miktzat* carries lashes — *le'inyan malkot itmar* — was specifically said in the context of touching a *korban* while impure. The question of *bia'ah b'miktzat* and lashes was never about spatial entry into the *azarah* as such; it was about the act of a *tamei* person making physical contact with a sacred object. When one extends a finger and touches a *korban*, that contact is the violation, and lashes follow.

### Malkot vs. Karet

The crucial implication of Ravina's reading is this: if *bia'ah b'miktzat* carries only *malkot* and not *karet*, then the *metzora* who is a *ba'al keri* and inserts his thumbs for the blood ritual incurs at most a prohibition punishable by lashes — not *karet*. A prohibition of *malkot* alone is capable of being overridden by the positive obligation to bring a *korban pesach*, which is an *asei she-yesh bo karet* — a positive commandment whose violation incurs *karet*. Therefore, the need to complete the *metzora*'s purification so that he can bring his *korban pesach* provides sufficient grounds to override the *malkot*-level prohibition associated with the *ba'al keri* status. Tosafot is troubled by this reasoning — since when does *korban pesach* override a *lav* that carries *malkot*? — but that is the structure of Ravina's answer.

## Ravin's Rejection

### Bia'ah Not Topic

When Ravin arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a tradition in the name of Rabbi Abba that fundamentally reframes the entire discussion: *le'inyan tamei she-nagah b'kodesh itmar* — the original statement about lashes was never about *bia'ah b'miktzat* at all. There was never a ruling that partial entry into the *azarah* with a limb constitutes a violation. The statement about *malkot* for using only part of one's body was always about something else entirely: a *tamei* person who physically touches a *korban*. That is the violation — *bia'ah b'miktzat* as an independent category does not exist.

### Reish Lakish Ruling

With this clarification, the *gemara* pivots to the underlying dispute. *D'itmar tamei she-nagah b'kodesh* — it was stated regarding a *tamei* person who touches a sacred object: *Reish Lakish amar lokeh* — Reish Lakish rules that one receives *malkot*. The verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga* — "you shall not touch any sacred thing" — explicitly prohibits a *tamei* person from touching a *korban*, and Reish Lakish takes this prohibition at face value as generating *malkot*.

### Yochanan Disputes

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *eino lokeh* — one does not receive *malkot* for touching a *korban* while *tamei*. In Rabbi Yochanan's reading, the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga* is not referring to *korbanot* at all — *hu b'trumah ksiv*, it is written in the context of *terumah*. And moreover, Rabbi Yochanan maintains that even within that context, the verse's primary referent is eating *terumah* in a state of *tumah*, not mere touching. One might attempt a *kal va-chomer* from *terumah* to *korbanot*, but the principle *ein onshin min ha-din* — one does not derive punishments by logical inference — would preclude deriving *malkot* for touching *korbanot* from a verse about *terumah*.

## Touching Korban

### Touching & Eating

The *gemara* now elaborates the full positions of Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan. Reish Lakish, who reads *bechol kodesh lo tiga* as referring to *korbanot*, derives two distinct prohibitions from this single verse — a prohibition against *touching* a *korban* while *tamei*, and a prohibition against *eating* a *korban* while *tamei*. The verse's use of the language of *negiah* (touching) establishes a *lav* for the act of touching; simultaneously, the verse is also understood to generate a *lav* for eating. This requires explanation, since a single phrase cannot straightforwardly yield two separate prohibitions.

### Terumah Argument

Rabbi Yochanan's counterargument proceeds in two stages. First, the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga* is not about *korbanot* at all — it is about *terumah*. Second, even regarding *terumah*, what the verse prohibits is eating while *tamei*, not mere touching. The prohibition against eating *terumah* in a state of *tumah* is indeed subject to *malkot*, but it is derived differently. Rabbi Yochanan does not generate his prohibition for eating *korbanot* while *tamei* from this verse; he derives it through a *gezeirah shavah* linking the phrase *v'tumaso alav* that appears in the context of *ochel kodesh* to the phrase *o tumaso bo* that appears in the context of *bia'at mikdash*. Just as entering the *Mikdash* while *tamei* incurs *karet* and is subject to a negative prohibition (derived from *v'lo yitamu es machneichem*), so too eating *kodesh* while *tamei* incurs *karet* and is subject to a negative prohibition — not from the *tiga* verse, but from this derivation. According to Rabbi Yochanan, then, one never receives *malkot* for merely touching a *korban*; that prohibition simply does not exist.

### Both Inferred

The *gemara* presses Reish Lakish: can he truly derive both touching and eating from this single phrase? He needs the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga* to establish the *azhara* for *ochel basar kodesh* — the prohibition against eating sanctified meat while *tamei*. If the verse is generating a prohibition for eating, is it also simultaneously available to generate a prohibition for touching? The *gemara* answers on behalf of Reish Lakish: yes, it can do both. The verse prohibits touching (*tiga*), which gives a *lav* for *negiah*. But the verse is also implicitly about eating — and this is known not from the language of touching, but from the structural context of the verse.

### Karet Connection

The reasoning is as follows. The verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el ha-mikdash lo tavo* juxtaposes touching sacred things with entering the *Mikdash*. We know that entering the *Mikdash* while *tamei* carries *karet*. We also know independently that eating *korbanot* while *tamei* carries *karet* — whereas touching does not. Therefore, when the verse says *lo tiga* in the same breath as *v'el ha-mikdash lo tavo*, the implicit referent of the prohibition must include eating, because that is the act associated with *karet*. Touching alone does not trigger *karet*, so the *karet*-bearing element of the verse must be pointing at eating. But since the verse uses the language of *negiah*, it is simultaneously generating a *lav* for touching as well. In this way, Reish Lakish extracts from a single verse a prohibition on touching (derived from the literal language) and a prohibition on eating (derived from the *karet* connection to *bia'at mikdash*).

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, denies that this verse speaks of *korbanot* at all and therefore never concedes a *lav* for touching *korbanot* while *tamei*. His *lav* for eating *korbanot* while *tamei* comes from the *gezeirah shavah*, and the *karet* for eating is independently established by *v'tumaso alav venichratah*. The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that supports Reish Lakish's position: *bechol kodesh lo tiga asro le'ochal* — this verse prohibits eating, and the *baraita* then derives this through the very mechanism Reish Lakish employs, comparing *kodesh* to *mikdash* on the basis of the *karet* penalty and the juxtaposition of the two clauses.

The *sugya* thus concludes with the following structure. According to Ravin citing Rabbi Abba, the original statement attributed to Reish Lakish was never about *bia'ah b'miktzat* as spatial entry; it was about the *lav* for a *tamei* person touching a *korban*. Reish Lakish holds that such a *lav* exists and is derived from *bechol kodesh lo tiga*, and he further derives from the same verse a *lav* for eating. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the verse speaks only of *terumah*, that no *lav* exists for touching *korbanot*, and that the *lav* for eating *korbanot* while *tamei* is derived through the *gezeirah shavah* of *tumaso-tumaso* connecting *ochel kodesh* to *bia'at mikdash*.
