# Bava Metzia 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtar Chov Overview

### Finding Shtar Chov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצָא שִׁטְרֵי חוֹב, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – לֹא יַחְזִיר, שֶׁבֵּית דִּין נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן. אֵין בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – יַחְזִיר, שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to one who **found promissory notes, if they include a property guarantee** for the loan **he may not return** them to the creditor, **as,** if he were to return them, **the court** would then **use them to collect repayment** of the debts from land that belonged to the debtor at the time of the loan, even if that land was subsequently sold to others. If **they do not include a property guarantee, he returns** them to the creditor, **as** in this case **the court** will **not use them to collect repayment** of the debt from purchasers of the debtor's land. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *daf* opens by extending the *masechta*'s ongoing discussion of *metziah* — found objects — into a domain we have touched on previously: the finding of a *shtar chov*, a document of debt. When one finds a document stating that Shimeon borrowed a thousand dollars from Reuven, the immediate question is to whom it should be returned. The difficulty is obvious: we cannot simply hand it back to the *malveh* (creditor, Reuven) without knowing whether the debt has already been repaid.

### Liens Present/Absent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ לֹא יַחְזִיר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבֵּית דִּין נִפְרָעִין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** In **both this** case **and that** case **he should not return** the promissory notes to the creditor, **as,** if he were to return them, **the court** would in any event **use them to collect repayment** of the loan from purchasers of the debtor's land.

The *Mishnah* introduces a crucial distinction based on whether the document contains *achrayut nechasim* — liens on property. The presence or absence of liens determines whether *beit din* can use the document to seize property that the *loveh* (debtor, Shimeon) has sold to third parties.

### Rabbi Meir's View

Rabbi Meir's position is as follows: *im yesh bahen achrayut nechasim lo yachzir* — if the document contains liens, do not return it to Reuven, because *beit din* would then extract encumbered property from purchasers (*lakochot*) who acquired land from Shimeon. *Ein bahen achrayut nechasim yachzir* — if there are no liens, return it, since *beit din* cannot collect from encumbered property and the purchasers are therefore unaffected. This position is, at first glance, striking: it seems entirely indifferent to Shimeon's own claims about the document.

### Chachamim View

The *chachamim* take a more sweeping stance: *bein kach uvein kach lo yachzir* — whether there are liens or not, never return the document to Reuven, since *beit din* will use it to collect regardless. The *Mishnah* thus leaves open a critical question: what exactly is Shimeon saying in this case? Is he admitting the debt or denying it? And why does the presence or absence of written liens determine the *halachah*?

## Shimeon's Claim?

### Bemai Askinan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, כִּי יֵשׁ בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים אַמַּאי לֹא יַחְזִיר? הָא מוֹדֶה! וְאִי כְּשֶׁאֵין חַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, כִּי אֵין בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים אַמַּאי יַחְזִיר? נְהִי דְּלָא גָּבֵי מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדֵי – מִבְּנֵי חָרֵי מִגְבָּא גָּבֵי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **With what** case **are we dealing? If we say** it is a case **when** the **liable party,** i.e., the debtor, **admits** that he wrote the promissory notes and that the debts have not yet been repaid, then **when** the promissory notes **include a property guarantee, why should** the finder **not return** them to the creditor? **Doesn't** the debtor **admit** to the debts? **And if** it is in a case **when** the **debtor does not admit** to the debts, claiming that he dropped the promissory notes after he repaid his debts, then even **when** the promissory notes **do not include a property guarantee, why should** the finder **return** them to the creditor? **Granted,** the creditor **cannot collect** these debts **from liened** property that has been sold, but **he can collect from unsold** property. The returning of the promissory note would be disadvantageous to the borrower, who claims that he repaid the loan.

The *Gemara* opens with its characteristic probing question: *bemai askinan* — what precisely is the case of the *Mishnah*? Two possibilities present themselves. If Shimeon *admits* he owes the money (*keshechayav modeh*), then when there are liens, why not return the document? He is admitting the debt, so what is the problem? Conversely, if Shimeon *denies* the debt (*kshe'ein chayav modeh*), then even when there are no liens the document could still be used to collect from Shimeon's own unencumbered property (*benei chorin*) — his cash, his movable assets — and why would Rabbi Meir permit returning it?

### Shimeon Admits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: דְּחָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִלְוֹת בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא לָוָה עַד תִּשְׁרִי, וְאָתֵי לְמִטְרַף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **when** the **liable party admits** to the debts, **and here, this is the reason** that the finder may not return the promissory notes: It is **that we are concerned** that **perhaps** the debtor **wrote** in the promissory note that he would **borrow** the money **in Nisan but he did not** actually **borrow** the money **until Tishrei,** and between Nisan and Tishrei he sold land. These lands are not liened to the debt, as the liability to repay the loan took effect only when he actually borrowed the money. **And** the creditor **will come to repossess** the land that was sold between Nisan and Tishrei from the **purchasers, unlawfully.**

The *Gemara*'s first answer is *le'olam kshechayav modeh*: the *Mishnah* is indeed discussing the case where Shimeon admits the debt. The reason not to return the document even in that case, says the *Gemara*, is concern for the *lakochot* — those who purchased property from Shimeon. The admission by Shimeon resolves the personal dispute between the parties, but the document's date creates an independent problem for third parties.

### Predated Liens Problem

The specific concern articulated is: *dechashinan shema katav lilvot beNisan velo lava ad Tishrei* — perhaps the document was written in Nisan (the new year for documents, from which dating begins) but the money was not actually borrowed until Tishrei. If that is so, the document falsely grants the *malveh* six months of liens he does not legitimately possess, and the *lakochot* — people who purchased Shimeon's property during those six months — would have their property unjustly seized (*ve'atu vatarfu lakochot shelo kedin*). This is the most natural reading of the *Mishnah*, which seems entirely preoccupied with the lien issue and makes no mention of Shimeon's personal claims at all: presumably because he has admitted the debt, there is nothing to adjudicate between the two primary parties. The entire concern is the downstream harm to innocent purchasers.

## Document Suspicion

### Challenge to Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כֹּל שְׁטָרֵי דְּאָתוּ לְקַמַּן נֵיחוּשׁ לְהוּ הָכִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the court must be concerned that the date on a promissory note predates the actual loan, **we should likewise be concerned that all** promissory **notes that come before us** to the court are perhaps predated.

The *Gemara* immediately raises an objection. If the concern is that a document may have been written before the money was borrowed — and that doing so would give the *malveh* illegitimate liens — then this concern applies to *every* loan document. Why single out a found document?

### Rashi's Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שְׁטָרֵי לָא רִיעֵי, הָנֵי רִיעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The credibility of **all** promissory **notes** in general has **not** been **compromised,** as they remain in the creditor's possession, which is the correct location in the case of an outstanding loan. The credibility of **these** promissory notes has been **compromised** by the fact that they were lost.

Rashi explains the logic as follows: if this were a fully legitimate document, the *malveh* would have been more careful with it. The fact that it was lost suggests something fishy — perhaps the document encodes liens that are a few months ahead of the actual loan, and the holder was therefore less invested in protecting it carefully. This rationale, however, seems strained. If a document represents a claim of ten thousand dollars, the holder would hardly be cavalier with it merely because the lien date is a few months early. The idea that sloppiness proves dishonesty is difficult to sustain.

### Lamudic Approach

A more conceptually rigorous explanation — developed more fully by later *acharonim* — runs as follows. A signed document functions halachically as testimony before *beit din*: *kemi shenekhkra eidutun beveit din*. But a document lying on the street is not yet testimony — it is a piece of paper with words on it. The act of returning it to the *malveh* transforms it from a neutral object into operative *edus* (testimony). To effect that transformation — to take a document with uncertain provenance and empower the *malveh* to use it in litigation — requires a high degree of certainty. Any residual concern, however slight, justifies withholding that transformation. The phrase *isra lei* (suspicion has fallen upon it) thus means not that there is a logical inference of fraud from the act of dropping, but that a document found on the street does not yet possess the status of testimony, and we will not confer that status upon it in the face of any plausible concern.

## Pre-Borrowing Docs

### Signing Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: כּוֹתְבִין שְׁטָר לַלֹּוֶה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַלְוֶה עִמּוֹ. לְכַתְּחִילָּה הֵיכִי כָּתְבִינַן? נֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִלְוֹת בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא לָוָה עַד תִּשְׁרִי, וְאָתֵי לְמִטְרַף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 167b): **One may write** a promissory **note for a borrower even if** the **lender is not with him** because it is the borrower who assumes liability based on the note, the question arises: **How can** one **write** this promissory note ***ab initio*? Let us be concerned** that **perhaps** the borrower wants to **write** the note as he intends **to borrow** money **in Nisan, but** will ultimately **not borrow** the money **until Tishrei, and** the lender might then **come to repossess** the land that the borrower sells between Nisan and Tishrei from the **purchasers, unlawfully.**

At this stage the *Gemara* raises an important general question that the specific discussion of predated liens calls to mind. We have an established rule that *kotvin shtar le'loveh af al pi she'ein malveh imo* — one may write a loan document for the borrower even if the lender is not present at the time of writing, since the document disadvantages only the borrower, who has requested it. But this immediately raises a problem: if the borrower goes to his two friends and asks them to sign a document in Nisan, and then does not actually secure the loan until Tishrei, the document will give the lender liens six months earlier than he is legitimately entitled to them, and the *lakochot* will be harmed. How, then, can we permit this practice at all?

### Rav Assi: Kinyan Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: בִּשְׁטָרֵי הַקְנָאָה, דְּהָא שַׁעְבֵּיד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said:** This mishna is referring not to one who finds an ordinary promissory note but **to** one who finds **deeds of transfer.** This refers to a promissory note that establishes a lien on the debtor's property from the date the note is written, regardless of when he borrows the money. **Because** the debtor **obligated himself** from that date, the creditor has the legal right to repossess his land from any subsequent purchasers.

Rav Assi answers: the only case in which witnesses may legitimately sign a loan document without the lender present is a *shtar hakna'ah* — a document incorporating a formal *kinyan* (act of legal encumbrance). *Beshtar hakna'ah hu, dashiv beneifshei* — because in such a document the borrower encumbers himself as of today.

### Shtar Hakna'ah

A *shtar hakna'ah* works as follows. The borrower goes to his witnesses, performs a *kinyan* — taking a handkerchief and formally transferring something in the context of the act — and the document records that he has granted the lender liens on his property as of this very day. This is not predating a loan; it is an outright, contemporaneous act of encumbrance. From that moment, the liens legitimately attach to the borrower's property, regardless of when the money eventually changes hands. Indeed, the *shtar hakna'ah* raises a further question: if the lender never actually extends the loan, does the borrower remain encumbered? There is a distinction between two questions — when the liens take effect, and whether they depend on the money eventually being lent. A *shtar hakna'ah* may grant liens even if the money is never borrowed at all. This is not predating; it is a genuine legal act performed today. In any case, because this *kinyan* is performed openly and contemporaneously, the *lakochot* are on notice that the encumbrance exists. Witnesses may therefore write the document without the lender present only in this specific case. Without a *shtar hakna'ah*, they may not write it, because doing so risks creating liens prior to the actual loan.

## Apply Kinyan Rule

### Return w/ Kinyan?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: "אִם יֵשׁ בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים לֹא יַחֲזִיר", וְאוֹקֵימְנָא כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, וּמִשּׁוּם שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִלְוֹת בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא לָוָה עַד תִּשְׁרֵי, וְאָתֵי לְמִטְרַף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין, אַמַּאי לֹא יַחֲזִיר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** the following difficulty arises: How will one account for the ruling of **the mishna** here, **which teaches** that **if** the promissory notes **include a property guarantee,** the finder **should not return** them to the creditor; **and we established** that the reference is to a case **when** the **debtor admits** that he still owes the debt **and** that the promissory note should not be returned **due to** suspicion that **perhaps** the debtor **wrote** it with the intention **to borrow** the money **in Nisan but did not** actually **borrow** it **until Tishrei, and** therefore, if the promissory note is returned to the creditor **he will come to repossess** the land from the **purchasers unlawfully.** If Rav Asi's explanation is correct, **why shouldn't** the finder **return** the document?

Having established Rav Assi's principle, the *Gemara* returns to the *Mishnah* with a sharp challenge. If a found *shtar* contains a *kinyan* — a *shtar hakna'ah* — then its liens were legitimately created on the date it was written, regardless of when the money was borrowed. If so, why not return it to the *malveh*?

### No Kinyan Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְזֵי אִי בִּשְׁטַר הַקְנָאָה – הָא שַׁעְבֵּיד לֵיהּ נַפְשֵׁיהּ! אִי בִּשְׁטָר דְּלָא הַקְנָאָה – לֵיכָּא לְמֵיחַשׁ, דְּהָא אָמְרַתְּ כִּי לֵיכָּא מַלְוֶה בַּהֲדֵיהּ לָא כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Let us see** what the possibilities are. **If** the reference is **to a deed of transfer, didn't** the debtor **obligate himself** that his property can be collected for payment of the loan from the date that the deed of transfer was written? Conversely, **if** the reference is **to** a promissory **note that is not** a deed of **transfer, there is no** room for **concern, as you said** that in such a case, **when the lender is not** present **together with** the borrower, **we do not write** such a document.

And if the document does not contain a *kinyan*, the concern about predating should never arise in the first place. For Rav Assi has just ruled that witnesses may only sign without the lender present in a *shtar hakna'ah* case. If it is not a *shtar hakna'ah*, then there is no legitimate scenario in which it would have been signed before the money was borrowed — and therefore the concern the *Gemara* raised should never materialize. The *Gemara* states this precisely: *ki leika malveh bahadeih lo kasvinan* — if the lender is not present and the money is not being lent then and there, witnesses simply should not have written the document. There is accordingly no reason to suspect predating, and the document should always be returnable.

### Protocol Violation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב אַסִּי: אַף עַל גַּב דִּשְׁטָרֵי דְּלָאו הַקְנָאָה, כִּי לֵיכָּא מַלְוֶה בַּהֲדֵיהּ לָא כָּתְבִינַן, מַתְנִיתִין כֵּיוָן דִּנְפַל אִתְּרַע לֵיהּ, וְחָיְישִׁינַן דִּלְמָא אִקְּרִי וּכְתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Asi** could have **said to you: Although we do not write** promissory **notes that are not** deeds of **transfer when the lender is not** present **together with** the borrower, with regard to the case in **the mishna** it can be explained that **since** the promissory note **was dropped,** its credibility **was compromised, and** consequently **we are concerned** that **perhaps it happened to have been written** in the absence of the lender, deviating from the standard procedure.

Rav Assi responds: *keivan de'nafal isra lei* — once the document has fallen, suspicion attaches. *Ve'chaishinan dilma ikrei ve'kasav* — we are concerned that perhaps the witnesses deviated from protocol. A borrower eager to secure a loan might have gone to his two friends and implored them to sign the document immediately, before the lender arrived: "He promised he will lend me the money, I'm running to catch him, please, just sign now." The witnesses, moved by this appeal, obliged — breaking the rules. We are therefore concerned that this document was signed before the money was actually lent, conferring illegitimate liens. 

This explanation may seem far-fetched: the probability that the witnesses violated protocol is not high, and the fact that a document was dropped does not in itself prove malfeasance. This is precisely why, as noted above, the more satisfying conceptual explanation is that a found document is simply not yet operative *edus*, and we will not confer that status upon it in the presence of any possible concern, however remote. When the *Gemara* says *isra lei*, it means the document's status as testimony has been suspended, and any plausible irregularity — including the theoretical possibility of a protocol violation by the witnesses — suffices to prevent us from re-establishing it.

## Abaye's Approach

### Edim Bachasiman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: עֵדָיו בַּחֲתוּמָיו זָכִין לוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁטָרֵי דְּלָאו הַקְנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye stated** an alternative explanation of the mishna that allows one to write a promissory note for a borrower in the absence of the lender: The document's **witnesses, with their signatures, acquire** the lender's lien on the borrower's land **on** the lender's **behalf,** despite the fact that the loan did not occur yet. **And** this applies **even** with regard to promissory **notes that are not** deeds of **transfer.**

Abaye now enters the discussion with a fundamentally different principle. Abaye's point is not yet an explanation of the *Mishnah*; it is rather an alternative answer to the initial question of how witnesses may ever sign a document when the money has not yet been borrowed. Abaye says: *edei hachasumah zachin lo* — the witnesses, through their signatures alone, award the lender the liens as of the date of signing. That is to say: the act of signing the document is itself a legal mechanism that encumbers the borrower's property from that moment forward. Every document, in Abaye's view, is effectively a *shtar hakna'ah*. There is therefore never a problem with predated liens, because the liens legitimately exist from the moment of signing, regardless of when the money is actually lent.

### Abaye vs. Kinyan

A distinction must nonetheless be maintained even within Abaye's framework. A *shtar hakna'ah* may encumber the borrower's property even if the money is *never* borrowed — the lien is an outright grant, not contingent on a loan. A regular *shtar* under Abaye's principle is different: the retroactive effectiveness of the signing is conditional on the money eventually being lent and the document eventually being delivered. The lender does not acquire absolute rights by the signature alone; rather, when he eventually receives the document after the loan has been made, the liens are deemed to have existed from the date of signing. This remains a meaningful difference between a *shtar hakna'ah* and a regular document even in Abaye's view.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: מָצָא גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים, דְּיָיתֵיקֵי, מַתָּנָה וְשׁוֹבָרִים – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יַחְזִיר, שֶׁמָּא כְּתוּבִים הָיוּ וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶם שֶׁלֹּא לִיתְּנָם. וְכִי נִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶם מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: עֵדָיו בַּחֲתוּמָיו זָכִין לוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** how can Abaye's opinion be reconciled with **that which we learned** in a mishna (18a): If one **found bills of divorce, or** bills **of manumission of slaves,** or **wills [*deyaitiki*],** or deeds of **gift, or receipts, he may not return** them to the people who are presumed to have lost them. The reason is that **perhaps they were** only **written and** not delivered, because the one who wrote them subsequently **reconsidered about them** and decided **not to deliver them.** The Gemara asks: **If he reconsidered** and decided not to deliver **them, what of it? Didn't you say** that a document's **witnesses, with their signatures, acquire** it **on behalf of** the recipient? If so, why shouldn't it be returned to him?

The *Gemara* challenges Abaye's position from the *Mishnah* that follows, which discusses found documents of various kinds — bills of divorce, writs of manumission, deathbed bequests (*dyatiki*), gift documents, and receipts. The ruling is not to return these to the intended recipients, since *shektuvim hayu ve'nimlach aleihem she'lo litnam* — they may have been written but then the issuer changed his mind. The *Gemara* presses: *vchi nimlach aleihem mai havay ve'ha amarta eidim bechasiman zachin lo* — if you changed your mind, what difference does it make? You said, Abaye, that the signatures themselves effect the legal change. Why would a change of heart matter if the act of signing has already taken effect?

## Conditional Effect

### Delivery Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקָא מָטוּ לִידֵיהּ. אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא מָטוּ לִידֵיהּ לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This statement,** that a creditor acquires the lien on the debtor's land immediately when the witnesses sign the document, applies only in a case **where** the document **came into** the creditor's **possession; but** in a case **where** the document **did not come into his possession,** as it was never given to him, **we do not say** that.

The *Gemara* resolves this by clarifying the precise scope of Abaye's principle. *Hani mili heicha de'matu l'yadei* — this applies only when the document is eventually delivered. The signature does not effect the change in isolation; rather, when the document is given over, the signatures cause the legal change to take effect *retroactively* as of the date of signing. But if the document is never delivered — *heicha de'la matu l'yadei lo amrinan* — the signature alone accomplishes nothing. A change of heart that prevents delivery therefore neutralizes the signatures entirely.

### vs. Hakna'ah

This distinction explains the continued relevance of *shtar hakna'ah* even within Abaye's system. A *shtar hakna'ah* operates independently of delivery — the borrower has performed a formal act of encumbrance by *kinyan*, and that act may not require the document to be handed over or even the money to be lent. By contrast, a regular *shtar* signed by witnesses creates liens only retroactively, and only upon delivery.

### Retroactive Liens

To illustrate the full implication of Abaye's principle: if a *shtar* is signed today and delivered one year from now, the liens are deemed to have existed since the day of signing. This potentially has significant consequences in the realm of gifts (*shtar matana*) as well. If one executes a gift document today, has witnesses sign it, and delivers it to the recipient a year later, the recipient may be deemed to have owned the property retroactively from the date of signing — since the principle of *zachin lo* (we may effect a benefit on someone's behalf) could apply, in monetary matters, from the moment the signatures are affixed, subject to eventual delivery.

## Return w/o Liens

### Both Admit Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: מָצָא שִׁטְרֵי חוֹב, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהֶם אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – לֹא יַחְזִיר. וְאוֹקִימְנָא כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, וּמִשּׁוּם שֶׁמָּא כָּתַב לִלְוֹת בְּנִיסָן וְלֹא לָוָה עַד תִּשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather,** how can **the mishna** be reconciled with Abaye's opinion? **As it teaches:** With regard to one who **found promissory notes, if they include a property guarantee, he may not return** them to the creditor. **And we established** that the mishna is referring to a case **when** the **liable party,** i.e., the debtor, **admits** to the debts, **and** nevertheless the finder may not return the note **due to** the suspicion that **perhaps he wrote** the promissory note with the intention **to borrow** the money **in Nisan but he did not** actually **borrow** it **until Tishrei.**

Abaye's principle now creates a difficulty for the *Mishnah*. If every signature retroactively creates liens from the date of signing — rendering predating a non-issue — and if both parties admit the loan, why does the *Mishnah* say not to return the document? The liens are always legitimate; Shimeon admits the debt; there seems to be nothing to protect against.

### Fraud Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי, מַתְנִיתִין הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: דְּחָיְישִׁ[ינַן] לְפֵרָעוֹן וְלִקְנוּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Abaye** could have **said to you** that **this is the reason** for the ruling in **the mishna:** It is **that** the *tanna* **suspects** that there was **repayment and collusion.**

Abaye therefore offers a different explanation for the *Mishnah*'s ruling, one that does not rely on the concern for predating. *Hainu ta'ama dechaya shel pirayon u'knunya* — the reason we do not return the document is the concern for *pirayon* (a paid-off debt) and *knunya* (a scheme).

### Scheme Details

The scheme works as follows. Shimeon paid his thousand-dollar debt to Reuven and received the *shtar* back from him. Subsequently, the two conspire. Shimeon previously sold a piece of property to Charlie, and Charlie was unwise enough to purchase it without taking out *achrayut* (a guarantee) from Shimeon that if creditors seized the property, Shimeon would reimburse him. Reuven and Shimeon now agree to pretend the debt was never paid. Reuven demands the money, Shimeon claims to have none, and Reuven uses the *shtar* to seize the property from Charlie. Charlie cannot recover from Shimeon because he took no guarantee, and Reuven and Shimeon divide the proceeds.

It is true that such a scheme could theoretically be executed at any time by any creditor. Under normal circumstances, the *Gemara* does not suspect such collusion. But here the document has been found on the ground — it is no longer operative *edus* — and to convert this piece of paper back into testimony capable of destroying Charlie's property rights, any possible concern suffices to make us hesitate. Indeed, there is even a modest logical connection to the act of dropping: if the debt were genuinely unpaid and the *shtar* were in Reuven's active possession, it is unlikely he would have been so careless with a document worth ten thousand dollars. The fact that it was found on the ground is at least weak evidence that the document may have already been returned to the borrower after payment.

## Shmuel's View

### No Fraud Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לְפֵרָעוֹן וְלִקְנוּנְיָא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָנִיחָא אִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב אַסִּי דְּאָמַר בִּשְׁטָרֵי הַקְנָאָה – מוֹקֵי מַתְנִיתִין בִּשְׁטָרֵי דְּלָאו הַקְנָאָה. אֶלָּא אִי סָבַר כְּאַבָּיֵי דְּאָמַר עֵדָיו בַּחֲתוּמָיו זָכִין לוֹ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to Shmuel, who says** that **we do not suspect repayment and collusion, what is there to say?** How can the mishna be explained? **This works out well if** Shmuel **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Asi, who says** that only **in** the case of **deeds of transfer** is it permitted to write a promissory note for a borrower in the absence of the lender. Accordingly, Shmuel **can establish** the mishna as referring **to** promissory **notes that are not** deeds of **transfer. But if** Shmuel **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Abaye, who says** that a document's **witnesses, with their signatures, acquire** the lien **on** the creditor's **behalf, what is there to say?**

Shmuel takes the most permissive position in this entire discussion: *lo chaishinan le'pirayon u'knunya* — we entertain no concern for a paid-off debt or a scheme. Shmuel reasons that if a debt had been paid, the document would have been torn up. The fact that the document is intact is sufficient evidence that the debt has not been paid. Accordingly, Shmuel would always treat a *shtar* as legitimate and return it to the *malveh*.

### Reinterpret Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל מוֹקֵי לְמַתְנִיתִין כְּשֶׁאֵין חַיָּיב מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel can establish the mishna** as referring to a case **when the** purported **liable party does not admit** to the debt, and therefore the finder may not return the promissory notes to the creditor.

This position forces Shmuel to reread the *Mishnah* entirely. Since he cannot explain the *Mishnah*'s ruling of non-return through either predating (which Abaye has shown is never a legitimate concern) or schemes (which Shmuel himself denies), Shmuel must locate the concern elsewhere. The *Gemara* therefore says: *Shmuel moki l'matnisin k'she'ein chayav modeh* — Shmuel reads the *Mishnah* as discussing the case where the *loveh* does *not* admit the debt. Shimeon is denying he ever borrowed the money, claiming the document is a forgery.

### Debtor Denies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי אֵין בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים אַמַּאי יַחְזִיר? נְהִי דְּלָא גָּבֵי מִן מְשַׁעְבְּדֵי, מִבְּנֵי חָרֵי מִגְבֵּי גָּבֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** in a case **when** the promissory notes **do not include a property guarantee, why must** the finder **return** them to the purported creditor? **Granted,** the creditor **cannot collect** the debt **from liened** property that had been sold, but **he can collect** it **from** the debtor's **unsold** property, even though the debtor claims to be exempt.

When Shimeon denies the loan, the concern is no longer about protecting *lakochot* — it is about protecting Shimeon himself. Even if the document could not collect from encumbered property, it could still be used to collect from Shimeon's unencumbered assets (*benei chorin*), and since Shimeon denies the loan, there is reason to withhold the document from Reuven. This reading shifts the entire orientation of the *Mishnah* away from protecting third-party purchasers and toward protecting the debtor from a possibly fraudulent document.

## With/Without Liens

### Meir: Worthless

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שְׁטַר חוֹב שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – אֵין גּוֹבֶה לָא מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי וְלָא מִבְּנֵי חָרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Shmuel says** that **Rabbi Meir would say:** In the case of **a promissory note that does not include a property guarantee,** the creditor **collects neither from liened** property that has been sold **nor from unsold** property. Therefore, there is no harm in the finder returning the promissory note to the creditor.

Within Shmuel's reading of the *Mishnah* — where Shimeon denies the debt — Rabbi Meir's permission to return the document when there are no liens written in requires explanation. If Shimeon is denying the debt, why would we hand the document back to the *malveh* under any circumstances? The answer lies in Rabbi Meir's view of what a *shtar* without *achrayut* is worth: according to Rabbi Meir, such a document is completely worthless — *eino goveh lo mi'meshubadim ve'lo mi'benei chorin*. It cannot be used to collect from encumbered property, and it cannot even be used to collect from unencumbered property. It is, for all halachic purposes, an inoperative piece of parchment.

### Bottle Stopper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אַמַּאי יַחְזִיר? אָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לָצוֹר עַל פִּי צְלוֹחִיתוֹ שֶׁל מַלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But since** the creditor **cannot collect** the debt, **why should** the finder **return** the promissory note? For what purpose can the creditor use it? **Rabbi Natan bar Oshaya says:** The **creditor** can use it **to cover the opening of his flask.** Its only value is as a piece of paper.

If so, why return it to the *malveh* at all? The *Gemara* cites Rabbi Natan bar Oshaya: *latzor pi tzluchiso* — to use as a bottle stopper. Here, uniquely, the phrase is not ironic but entirely literal. The document has no legal value whatsoever according to Rabbi Meir in this scenario, so returning it does no harm — the *malveh* might as well have it for its physical use.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַהְדְּרֵיהּ (לְהוּ) לְלֹוֶה לָצוֹר עַל פִּי צְלוֹחִיתוֹ שֶׁל לֹוֶה! לֹוֶה הוּא דְּאָמַר לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the document has only the value of the paper, **let** the finder **return it to the debtor, to cover the opening of** the **debtor's flask.** The Gemara answers: **The debtor is** the one **who says** that these **matters,** the loan, **never happened** and that the promissory note is forged. Therefore, he has no claim to the paper on which the promissory note is written.

The *Gemara* then asks: if it is only worth using as a bottle stopper, why give it to the *malveh*? Give it to Shimeon the debtor — he could use it as a bottle stopper too. The *Gemara* answers: because Shimeon is denying that the document exists at all (*lo hayu devarim me'olam*). Since he denies it ever arose, he has no claim to it. So Rabbi Meir says it may be returned to the *malveh*, not as a functioning legal instrument, but as a scrap of material.

## Two Readings

### Elazar Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁאֵין חַיָּיב מוֹדֶה. דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: שְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לָא מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי וְלָא מִבְּנֵי חָרֵי. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי הוּא דְּלָא גָּבֵי, מִבְּנֵי חָרֵי – מִגְבָּא גָּבֵי. אֲבָל כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יַחְזִיר, וְלָא חָיְישִׁינַן לְפֵרָעוֹן וְלִקְנוּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says:** The **dispute** in the mishna between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is **in** a case **when** the purported **liable party does not admit** to the debt. **As, Rabbi Meir holds** that **with** a promissory **note that does not include a property guarantee,** one **can collect** a debt **neither from liened** property that has been sold **nor from unsold** property. **And the Rabbis hold** that **it is** only **from liened** property **that one cannot collect** a debt using this promissory note but that one **does collect** a debt **from unsold** property. **But** in a case **when** the **liable party admits** to the debt, **everyone agrees** that the finder **must return** the promissory note, **and we do not suspect** the creditor and the debtor of engaging in **repayment and collusion [*veliknuneya*]** to the detriment of one who purchased land from the debtor.

Rabbi Elazar now articulates the reading of the *Mishnah* that Shmuel's approach implies: *machlokes b'she'ein chayav modeh* — the debate in the *Mishnah* between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* concerns the case where Shimeon does not admit the loan. Rabbi Meir permits returning the document when there are no liens because he holds such a document is utterly worthless. The *chachamim* forbid returning it in any case — with or without liens — because they hold that even a document without written liens can collect, and therefore it remains a dangerous instrument that could be used against Shimeon.

## With/Without Liens (II)

### Chachamim View

The *chachamim*'s position is that *achrayut* (liens) are always presumed to exist in a loan document, even when not explicitly written — the principle known as *achrayut ta'us sofer* (a scribal omission of liens is still legally operative). According to this view, a document without written liens still encumbers the borrower's property, and therefore the *malveh* could use it to seize property from *lakochot* as well as from the borrower himself. Since the document retains full legal force, even when Shimeon denies the loan, one must never return it: *bein kach uvein kach lo yachzir*.

---

## Two Readings (II)

### Yochanan Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: שְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי הוּא דְּלָא גָּבֵי, אֲבָל מִבְּנֵי חָרֵי – מִגְבָּא גָּבֵי. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדֵי נָמֵי גָּבֵי. אֲבָל כְּשֶׁאֵין חַיָּיב מוֹדֶה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא יַחְזִיר, דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לְפֵרָעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** is in a case **when** the **liable party admits** to the debt. **As, Rabbi Meir holds** that **it is** only **from liened** property **that one cannot collect** a debt using a promissory **note that does not include a property guarantee, but** one **does collect** a debt **from unsold** property. **And the Rabbis hold** that one **collects** a debt **from liened** property **too. But** in a case **when the liable party does not admit** to the debt, **everyone agrees** that the finder **may not return** the promissory note, **as we suspect** that perhaps there was **repayment.**

Rabbi Yochanan takes the diametrically opposite position on how to read the *Mishnah*: *machlokes keshechayav modeh* — the debate concerns the case where Shimeon *admits* the loan. On Rabbi Yochanan's reading, Rabbi Meir does not say a *shtar* without *achrayut* is completely worthless. Rather, Rabbi Meir holds that a document without liens may still collect from *benei chorin* (unencumbered property in the borrower's possession), but it does not create *achrayut* — it cannot reach encumbered property that Shimeon has sold to others. Since the only person affected by such a document is Shimeon himself, and Shimeon admits the debt, Rabbi Meir permits returning it to the *malveh*.

### Protection Target

The pivot in Rabbi Yochanan's reading is the identity of who is being protected. In Shmuel and Rabbi Elazar's reading, the *Mishnah* is about protecting the *loveh* — Shimeon, who denies the debt. In Rabbi Yochanan's reading, the *Mishnah* is about protecting the *lakochot* — third-party purchasers, who stand to be harmed by a *shtar* with liens. The *chachamim*, on Rabbi Yochanan's reading, say that *achrayut* is always implied, and therefore the document always threatens *lakochot*, and therefore one must never return it even if Shimeon agrees. As for the case where Shimeon denies the loan, Rabbi Yochanan would say *divrei hakol lo yachzir* — all agree not to return it, and the reason is concern for *pirayon* — that the debt may have been paid and a scheme is being perpetrated against the purchasers.

## Baraisa Proof

### Both Admit Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּחֲדָא, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דִשְׁמוּאֵל בְּתַרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, and** from it there is also **a conclusive refutation of one** element of the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar and a conclusive refutation of two** elements of the opinion **of Shmuel.**

A *Baraisa* now provides explicit support for Rabbi Yochanan's reading.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא שִׁטְרֵי חוֹב וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶם אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה. אֵין בָּהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַלֹּוֶה מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לַמַּלְוֶה, אֵין הַלֹּוֶה מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: In a case where one **found promissory notes and they include a property guarantee, even if both** the creditor and the debtor **agree** about the existence of the debt, the finder **should not return** it **to this** creditor **or to that** debtor. If **they do not include a property guarantee,** then in a case **when the debtor admits** to the debt, **one should return** the promissory note to the creditor. But if **the debtor does not admit** to the debt, **one should not return** it **to this** creditor **or to that** debtor. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *Baraisa* states: *matza shitrei chov sheyesh bahen achrayut nechasim* — if one finds loan documents containing liens — *af al pi she'shneihem modim lo yachzir lo l'zeh v'lo l'zeh* — even if both parties admit the loan, do not return the document to either party. This could not be clearer: both admission and denial of the loan leave the document unreturnable when there are liens.

### Purchaser Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: שְׁטָרי שֶׁיֵּשׁ (בָּהֶם) [בּוֹ] אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. וְשֶׁאֵין (בָּהֶם) [בּוֹ] אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **As Rabbi Meir would say:** With promissory **notes that include a property guarantee, one can collect** the debt **from liened property; but** with those **that do not include a property guarantee,** one **collects** the debt only **from unsold property. And the Rabbis say:** With **both this** type **and that** type of promissory note, **one can collect** the debt **from liened property.**

Why do we not return the document even when both parties admit? The only plausible answer is that we are concerned for the *lakochot* — and specifically, that there may be a scheme (*knunya*) in play, in which a paid-up debt is being fraudulently resurrected to seize property from unsuspecting purchasers. This is a direct proof for Rabbi Yochanan's framework.

### Implicit Liens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּחֲדָא, דְּאָמַר: לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר שְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים וְלֹא מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. וְקָאָמַר: בֵּין לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין לְרַבָּנַן לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִקְנוּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **a conclusive refutation** of **one** element **of** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, who says** that **according to Rabbi Meir, with** a promissory **note that does not include a property guarantee** one **can collect** a debt **neither from liened** property that has been sold **nor from unsold** property. **And** Rabbi Elazar also **says** that **according to both Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis, we do not suspect** that there is **collusion** between the debtor and the creditor.

The *Baraisa* continues: when there are no liens written in the document, if the *loveh* admits, return it to the *malveh*; if the *loveh* does not admit, do not return it to either party — *lo yachzir lo l'zeh v'lo l'zeh*. The entire debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* therefore turns on one question: whether liens are presumed to exist even when not explicitly written. That is the sole issue in the *Mishnah*.

## Contra Elazar

### Still Collects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָרָיְיתָא קָתָנֵי: שְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי הוּא דְּלָא גָּבֵי, הָא מִבְּנֵי חוֹרִין מִגְבָּא גָּבֵי. וְקָתָנֵי: בֵּין לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין לְרַבָּנַן, חָיְישִׁינַן לִקְנוּנְיָא. דְּקָתָנֵי: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה. אַלְמָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִקְנוּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the ***baraita* teaches** that with a promissory **note that does not include a property guarantee** the creditor **cannot collect** a debt **from liened** property, **but he can collect** it **from unsold** property. **And** the *baraita* also **teaches** that **according to** the opinions of **both Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis, we suspect** that there is **collusion** between the debtor and the creditor, **as it is taught** that if one found promissory notes that include a property guarantee, **even if both** the creditor and the debtor **agree** about the existence of the debt, the finder **should not return** it **to this** creditor **or to that** debtor. **Apparently, we suspect collusion.** This refutes Rabbi Elazar's opinion that there is no suspicion of collusion.

The *Baraisa* directly contradicts Rabbi Elazar's reading in two respects. Rabbi Elazar had asserted that according to Rabbi Meir, a *shtar* without *achrayut* is completely worthless — it cannot collect from encumbered or unencumbered property alike. But the *Baraisa* explicitly says the opposite: *m'shubdei hu d'lo gabi benei chorin miha gabi* — without written liens, the document cannot collect from encumbered property, but it *can* collect from unencumbered property. The document is not worthless; it retains practical legal force.

### Meir Refuted

This means Rabbi Elazar's explanation of Rabbi Meir — that the document would be returnable because it is legally inert — cannot stand. The real reason Rabbi Meir permits returning the document (when there are no liens and the *loveh* admits) is not because it is worthless, but because without *achrayut* the document poses no threat to *lakochot*: it can only be used against the borrower himself, who has already conceded the debt. And the real reason the *chachamim* prohibit returning it even then is that they hold liens are implicit in every loan document and therefore the *lakochot* remain at risk.

### Schemes Confirmed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But aren't these two** elements of Rabbi Elazar's statement that are refuted by the *baraita*? Why was it stated above that only one element is refuted?

The *Baraisa*'s ruling that even when both parties admit the document should not be returned with liens confirms the second point as well: we *are* concerned about schemes (*chaishinan l'knunya*). This contradicts Rabbi Elazar's second assertion — that there is no concern for *pirayon* or *knunya*. The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Elazar's two positions are intertwined: because he read the *Mishnah* as concerning the *loveh*'s denial, he was compelled to explain Rabbi Meir's permission as resting on the document's worthlessness; and because of that explanation, he had no room for a concern about schemes. The *Baraisa* refutes both positions as a unified whole.

## Unified Reading

### Core Issue

According to Rabbi Yochanan's reading, confirmed by the *Baraisa*, the core issue of the *Mishnah* is the presence or absence of *achrayut* — liens — not whether the *loveh* admits or denies the loan. The *Mishnah* operates in the background assumption that both parties agree the loan exists; the question is about the document's impact on third parties.

### Purchaser Risk

The animating concern throughout Rabbi Yochanan's reading is the protection of *lakochot* from *pirayon u'knunya* — the scheme in which a paid-off debt is fraudulently used to seize property from innocent purchasers. This concern is real enough that even mutual admission by both debtor and creditor does not suffice to permit returning a document that carries liens.

### Implicit Liens Q

The debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* thus resolves into a single halachic dispute: does *achrayut* exist implicitly in every loan document, even when not recorded by the scribe (*achrayut ta'us sofer*)? Rabbi Meir says no — without written liens, there are no liens, and the document's reach is limited to the borrower's own unencumbered property. The *chachamim* say yes — liens are always implied in a loan document, and therefore the *malveh* can always use it to collect from property the *loveh* has sold.

### Never Return

The *Mishnah*'s bottom line, as clarified by the *Baraisa* and vindicated by Rabbi Yochanan, is this: whenever *achrayut* exists — whether explicitly written or implied by the *chachamim*'s rule — never return the document to the *malveh*, because doing so would endanger *lakochot* who may be victimized by a scheme. The debate in the *Mishnah* is confined to the case where *achrayut* is not written in: does it nonetheless exist? That question — *achrayut ta'us sofer* — is what divides Rabbi Meir from the *chachamim*, and it is that question, and only that question, that the *Mishnah* puts before us.
