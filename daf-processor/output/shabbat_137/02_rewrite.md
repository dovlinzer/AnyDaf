# Shabbat 137 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixed-Up Infants

### Case Setup

The *mishna* on 137a opens with a famous debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning a *mohel* who found himself in an unusual predicament. He had two infants to circumcise: one whose *brit milah* was scheduled for Sunday, and one whose *brit milah* was scheduled for *Shabbat*. He forgot which was which.

### Sunday on Shabbat

In the first scenario, the *mohel* proceeded on *Shabbat* believing he was circumcising the *Shabbat* infant, but in fact circumcised the Sunday infant. Not only did he violate *Shabbat* without proper license for this particular child, but the circumcision itself accomplished nothing halachically — a seventh-day *milah* is of no consequence. The *mishna* rules that he is *chayav* a *korban chatat*: he was *shogeg*, not *meizid*, but he violated *Shabbat* without justification for the child he actually operated on.

### Friday on Shabbat

The second scenario introduces a subtler case. One infant was to be circumcised on Friday and the other on *Shabbat*. The *mohel* failed to perform the Friday circumcision on Friday — the most natural reading of the *mishna* is that he mixed up the infants, circumcised the *Shabbat* infant on Friday, and then on *Shabbat* itself mistakenly circumcised the Friday infant. This is meaningfully different from the first case: although the ninth-day *milah* should not have overridden *Shabbat*, it was at least a valid *mitzvah* of *brit milah*. A seventh-day *milah* is nothing at all; a ninth-day *milah* is still a *mitzvah*, merely one that does not warrant desecrating *Shabbat*.

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

In this second scenario, Rabbi Eliezer rules that the *mohel* is still *chayav* a *chatat*, while Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him. It is worth noting the apparent tension in Rabbi Eliezer's overall position: the same Rabbi Eliezer who ruled, just days earlier in our learning, that one may fashion a knife and carry it through *reshut ha-rabbim* to perform a *brit milah* for the infant one has a *mitzvah* to circumcise — that same Rabbi Eliezer rules stringently when the *mohel* mistakes one infant for another.

### Ta'a Bidvar Mitzvah

The *gemara* articulates the principle underlying Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption: *ta'a bidvar mitzvah ve-asa mitzvah patur* — one who erred in the course of performing a *mitzvah* and, through that very error, still accomplished a *mitzvah*, is exempt from a *chatat*. Nobody maintains that such a person did not violate *Shabbat*, and certainly it is not *mutar*; the exemption is purely from the obligation to bring a sin offering. The *gemara* offers a parallel: one who picks up a *lulav* on *Shabbat* and inadvertently carries it into *reshut ha-rabbim* — even there, where there was no license to violate *Shabbat* for the *lulav* at all, the fact that the violation occurred in the context of performing a *mitzvah* and actually succeeded in performing one renders the person *patur* according to Rabbi Yehoshua.

## Resolving Dispute

### Rav Huna Version

The *gemara* now confronts a textual tension. *Rav Huna masni chayav* — Rav Huna's version of the *mishna* holds that in the first case (the Sunday infant circumcised on *Shabbat*) everyone agrees the *mohel* is *chayav*. The dispute concerns only the second case (the Friday infant circumcised on *Shabbat*). A *baraita* cited by Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar supports this reading explicitly: Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua did not disagree when the Sunday infant was circumcised on *Shabbat*, because that constitutes *ta'a bidvar mitzvah velo asa mitzvah* — an error in the performance of a *mitzvah* that did not result in any *mitzvah* being accomplished. On that everyone agrees the *mohel* is *chayav*. Their dispute concerns the Friday infant circumcised on *Shabbat*, where *ta'a bidvar mitzvah ve-asa mitzvah* fully applies.

The *baraita* adds a striking note: both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua derive their respective positions from *avodah kochavim*. One would not have expected this. The explanation is that the *parasha* in *Shelach* dealing with *chatat* — *v'chi tishgu v'lo ta'asu et kol ha-mitzvot ha-eileh* — is understood by *chazal* as referring paradigmatically to *avodah zarah*, and therefore that case serves as the template for determining when a *chatat* is incurred. Rabbi Eliezer reasons: just as by *avodah zarah* the *Torah* says do not do this, and if you do it you are *mechayev*, so too here, no distinction. Rabbi Yehoshua counters: *hasam lo tarid mitzvah* — there, the sin occurred with no connection to performing a *mitzvah*; *hacha tarid mitzvah* — here the person was involved in a *mitzvah* and succeeded in performing one, and that changes the analysis. In practice, of course, what is being derived is not so much from the specifics of *avodah zarah* as from the general proposition of when a *chatat* is incurred — the contrast each *amora* draws is simply whether being engaged in a *mitzvah* context alters that standard.

### Rabbi Meir Version

*Rabbi Yehuda masni patur* — Rabbi Yehuda's version, supported by a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Meir, reverses the consensus case entirely. According to Rabbi Meir, everyone agrees that the Friday infant circumcised on *Shabbat* is *patur*, because a *mitzvah* was in fact accomplished. The debate concerns the Sunday infant circumcised on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Eliezer says *chayav*; Rabbi Yehoshua says *patur*. But here Rabbi Yehoshua's reasoning shifts: *hasam lo tarid mitzvah* — and Rabbi Yehoshua responds *hacha tarid mitzvah*, meaning that even though the *mohel* did not ultimately perform any *mitzvah* (the seventh-day circumcision is nothing), he was nonetheless in the thick of performing a *mitzvah*. Being immersed in the activity of *brit milah* is itself enough to exempt according to Rabbi Yehoshua, even without having actually fulfilled a *mitzvah*. The same arguments from *avodah kochavim* apply, with the same structure.

Yet a third *baraita*, attributed to Rabbi Chiya in the name of Rabbi Meir, introduces yet another permutation that seems to reverse the intuitively "better" case: when the Friday infant is circumcised on *Shabbat*, everyone agrees the *mohel* is *chayav* — despite a *mitzvah* having been performed — whereas when the Sunday infant is circumcised on *Shabbat*, that is precisely where Rabbi Yehoshua exempts. This appears paradoxical: how can Rabbi Yehoshua exempt in the case where no *mitzvah* was accomplished but hold liable in the case where a *mitzvah* was?

### License to Override

The school of Rabbi Yannai resolves this apparent reversal with an insight that reframes the entire discussion. The more common scenario when someone circumcises the Friday infant on *Shabbat* is that the infants were mixed up: the *Shabbat* infant was circumcised on Friday, and the Friday infant on *Shabbat*. When *Shabbat* arrives under those circumstances, there is no longer any infant present for whom *Shabbat* may be overridden — the *Shabbat* infant was already circumcised yesterday. *De-lo nitnah Shabbat le-hidachos*: *Shabbat* no longer carries a right to be overridden, because the one infant who could have justified it has already been circumcised. The fact that a *mitzvah* was incidentally accomplished does not save the *mohel* from liability, because he had no license to be violating *Shabbat* at all.

By contrast, in the scenario where the Sunday and *Shabbat* infants were confused and the Sunday infant was circumcised on *Shabbat*, the *Shabbat* infant has not yet been circumcised. *Seifa nitnah Shabbat le-hidachos*: because the *Shabbat* infant is still waiting, there remains a valid license to override *Shabbat*. The *mohel* simply confused which baby was which. This reframes the debate entirely: the critical variable is not only whether a *mitzvah* was accomplished, but whether the *mohel* possessed an active license to override *Shabbat* in the first place.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

Rav Ashi raises a sharp objection to Rav Kahana: even if the *Shabbat* infant has already been circumcised, surely there are other eight-day-old infants somewhere in the world for whom *Shabbat* may be overridden. The *mohel* standing before the Friday infant believed it to be a *Shabbat* infant, and in principle such infants exist — should that general fact not constitute sufficient license?

### Specific License

The *gemara* rejects this. *Le-hai gavra miha lo ishtari* — for this particular *mohel* in this particular situation, there is no license. The license to override *Shabbat* is not abstract and theoretical; it must be grounded in a specific circumstance facing this specific person. The existence of eight-day infants elsewhere in the world, for whom he has no role or assignment, does not grant him personal license to violate *Shabbat* for the infant standing before him.

## Timing & Health

### Normal Eighth Day

The next *mishna* opens with a deceptively simple enumeration: an infant may be circumcised on the eighth, ninth, tenth, eleventh, or twelfth day — not earlier, not later under normal circumstances. *K'darko lishmonah*: the ordinary case is the eighth day.

### Bein Hashmashos

When an infant is born during *bein hashmashos* — the twilight period between two days — there is genuine uncertainty about which day he entered the world. If born at *bein hashmashos* between Monday and Tuesday, the *brit milah* is performed on nine days counting from Monday, since perhaps he was born on Tuesday and the eighth day counting from Tuesday coincides with nine days from Monday.

### Holiday Obstacles

The *mishna* catalogs how layered holiday complications can push the *brit milah* further out. If a *bein hashmashos* birth occurs between Friday and *Shabbat*, the *brit* cannot be performed the following Friday (because the infant might be a *Shabbat* infant, making it the seventh day) and cannot be performed on *Shabbat* (because if the infant is a Friday infant, this is the ninth day, and a ninth-day *brit* does not override *Shabbat*). The *brit* therefore falls on Sunday — the tenth day counting from Friday. If the following Sunday is *Yom Tov*, it is pushed to Monday, the eleventh day. If Sunday and Monday together are *Rosh Hashanah* — a configuration our calendar does not permit today, but the *mishna* addresses — neither day is available, and the *brit* is performed on Tuesday, the twelfth day from Friday.

### Sickness Delay

If the infant is ill, the *brit milah* is postponed until he recovers: *katan ha-choleh ein molin oso ad she-yivarei*. This is straightforwardly a matter of preserving the child's life and health.

### Healing as Rebirth

Shmuel rules that if an infant's fever has broken — *chaltzaso chamah* — one does not rush to perform the *brit milah* immediately. Seven days are granted to ensure full recovery. The *gemara* raises the question of whether *me'et le-et* is required: must seven full twenty-four-hour periods elapse, or is it sufficient to count into the seventh calendar day?

A *tanna* from Lod teaches: *yom havraso ke-yom hivaldo* — the day of recovery is treated as the day of birth. Just as from birth the *brit milah* is performed on the eighth day, so too from the day of recovery. The *gemara* then considers whether this analogy works symmetrically in both directions. From birth, *me'et le-et* is not required — a child born at the end of Sunday may have his *brit* first thing the following Sunday morning. But perhaps by illness the standard is stricter, since the concern is genuinely medical rather than halachic: perhaps full seven twenty-four-hour days are necessary to confirm genuine recovery. The *gemara* leaves this as a live question. It should be noted that the *gemara* here addresses only the health concern itself; there are other contexts in halachic literature where the question arises whether the eight-day count from birth is suspended during a period of illness — that question is not resolved here, and the *gemara* speaks only to the recovery period.

## Valid Brit Standards

### Corona Visibility

The *mishna* identifies which pieces of residual skin (*tzitzin*) invalidate the *brit milah*: *basar ha-chofeh et rov ha-atarah* — flesh that covers the majority of the *atarah*, the corona. One who has such flesh remaining is still considered an *arel*, and a *kohen* in that state may not eat *terumah*, as the verse states *kol arel lo yochal bo*. The *gemara* notes this applies even to a hemophiliac who cannot undergo circumcision — the status follows regardless of fault.

Rav, in a tradition transmitted through Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba and Rabbi Avin, clarifies the precise meaning of *rov ha-atarah*. The *mishna* does not mean that flesh must cover the majority of the *circumference* of the corona (which is what one might naturally assume). Rather, it means flesh that covers the majority of the *height* of the corona — even in a single location. Accordingly, the entire circumference must be clean: even one small piece of skin anywhere along the circumference that covers the full height of the corona renders the *brit milah* invalid. This is a more demanding standard than a simple reading of *rov ha-atarah* would suggest.

### Obese Infant Case

The *mishna* adds a separate rule: *ve-im haya ba'al basar* — if the infant is very heavy and fleshy, one corrects the appearance *mipnei marit ha-ayin*, out of concern for appearances. This is quite striking. The *brit milah* may have been performed completely and correctly, yet extra flesh around the area could make it appear that circumcision was not done. The context is a society with a prominent public bathhouse culture. There is additionally a historical dimension: during periods of Hellenistic influence, some Jews were not undergoing or were actively reversing their *brit milah*, which would heighten communal sensitivity to appearances in this area.

### Shmuel's Method

Shmuel addresses the practical question of how to assess the obese infant: *ro'in oto* — one inspects him. *Kol zeman she-mitkasheh ve-nir'eh mahul* — whenever he has an erection and appears circumcised at that point, *eino tzarich lamol* — no additional surgery is required, because the flesh stretches and the proper result is visible. *Ve-im lav, tzarich lamul* — if even then he appears uncircumcised, surgery is required. The reasoning is that when the flesh stretches under arousal, the true state of the circumcision becomes apparent. Shmuel's standard: if it *looks* circumcised under those conditions, the infant is fine.

### Rabban Gamliel

A *baraita* records the same rule in the name of Rabban Gamliel, but with a subtle difference in formulation: *kol zeman she-mitkasheh ve-eino nir'eh mahul, tzarich lamul* — whenever he has an erection and appears *uncircumcised*, surgery is required. *Ve-im lav, eino tzarich lamul* — if that is not the case, no surgery is needed.

### Appearance Standards

The *gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu* — what practical difference exists between Shmuel's formulation and Rabban Gamliel's? *Ika beinaihu nir'eh ve-eino nir'eh* — the difference is a middle state where the appearance is ambiguous: not clearly circumcised, not clearly uncircumcised. Shmuel's standard requires that the infant *look circumcised* in order to be exempt from further surgery — anything short of clearly circumcised requires intervention. Rabban Gamliel's standard requires only that the infant not *look uncircumcised* — so in that ambiguous middle zone, Rabban Gamliel requires no further surgery while Shmuel would. Shmuel thus applies the stricter standard.

### Missing Periah

The *mishna* concludes: *mal velo para et ha-milah ke-ilu lo mal* — one who circumcised without performing *periah*, the tearing away of the membrane beneath the foreskin, is as if he did not circumcise at all. The *gemara* understands *periah* to be part of *halachah le-Moshe mi-Sinai* for *brit milah*, and it likewise overrides *Shabbat*. The *gemara* also notes that *periah* made it practically impossible to reverse the circumcision — a consideration not irrelevant to its historical significance.

## Brachot & Blessings

### Mohel's Beracha

The *gemara* turns to the *brachot* of *brit milah*. It is notable that these *brachot* do not appear in the *mishnayot* themselves — they are introduced here from a *tosefta* or *baraita*. The *mohel* who performs the circumcision recites: *asher kidishanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu al ha-milah* — the standard formulation of a *birchat ha-mitzvah* over the act itself.

### Father's Beracha

The *avi ha-ben* — the father of the infant — recites: *asher kidishanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu lehachniso bivrito shel Avraham Avinu* — You have commanded us to bring him into the covenant of our patriarch Abraham. The distinction between the *mohel*'s blessing and the father's is carefully calibrated. The *mohel* performed the physical act, so his *beracha* is *al ha-milah*. The father's *mitzvah* is not to personally perform the circumcision but to ensure that his son is brought into the covenant — and his blessing reflects precisely that obligation: *lehachniso*, to bring him in. This framing simultaneously reflects the father's halachic role and elevates the act beyond a surgical procedure into something covenantal. The father is not merely complying with a positive commandment in the way one takes a *lulav*; he is initiating his son into the eternal *brit* of *Avraham Avinu*.

### Assembly's Response

Those standing present respond: *keshem shenichnas la-brit ken yikanes le-Torah u-le-chupah u-le-ma'asim tovim* — just as he entered the covenant, so may he enter into Torah, marriage, and good deeds. This formulation closely parallels the *mishna* in Kiddushin enumerating the father's obligations toward his son: to circumcise him, to marry him off, and to teach him a trade. The three elements of the assembly's response — Torah, *chupah*, and *ma'asim tovim* — map precisely onto those three obligations.

### Ashkenazic Revision

The formulation just described — *keshem shenichnas* — is, however, not the original *girsa* of the *gemara*. The *Bach* establishes, and there is little reason to doubt, that the original text read: *keshem she-hicknasto la-brit ken tachniseyhu le-Torah u-le-chupah u-le-ma'asim tovim* — just as *you* brought him into the covenant, so may you bring him into Torah, marriage, and good deeds. This version is addressed to the father, building directly on his *beracha*: having declared that God commanded him *lehachniso bivrito shel Avraham Avinu*, the assembled guests respond by expressing the hope that he will similarly fulfill his remaining obligations. The blessing is for the father, not for the infant.

The shift to *keshem shenichnas* — refocusing the blessing on the infant rather than the father — was an Ashkenazic emendation rooted in medieval historical circumstance. In Ashkenazic communities of the Middle Ages, the father was very often not present at the ceremony, whether because of persecution, early death, or other hardships. When the father was absent, one could not say *keshem she-hicknasto*, addressed to someone who was not there. The formulation was therefore adjusted to *keshem shenichnas*, which centers on the infant and does not presuppose a living father. Once adopted for cases of a deceased or absent father, it was eventually standardized across all cases, so that today most people are unaware that the original *beracha* was directed at the father entirely. The change in meaning is real and significant: the original assembly's response was a charge and blessing to the father regarding his ongoing obligations; the current version is a prayer for the infant's future.

### Mevarech's Beracha

The *mevarech* — an appointed individual, distinct from the *mohel* and the father, perhaps serving a function analogous to a rabbi or officiant — recites a longer blessing:

*Asher kiddash yedid mi-beten, chok bi-sh'eiro sam, ve-tze'etza'av chatam be-ot berit kodesh. Al ken, bischar zot, El chai chelkenu, tzivah le-hatzil yedidut she'erenu mi-shachat, lema'an brito asher sam bi-sh'erenu. Baruch Atah Hashem, koreit ha-berit.*

"Who sanctified the beloved one from the womb, placed a statute in his flesh, and sealed his descendants with the sign of the holy covenant. Therefore, in the merit of this, the living God who is our portion commanded to save our beloved ones from destruction, for the sake of His covenant that He placed in our flesh. Blessed are You, God, who makes the covenant."

There is a textual question noted by the *Ya'avetz*, Rabbi Yaakov Emden, about whether the correct *girsa* is *tzivah* (He commanded, past tense) or *tzaveh* (command, an imperative addressed to God) — whether we are affirming what God has done or petitioning God to act.

The blessing is notable and striking. Its opening is theologically rich — tracing the covenant in the flesh of the infant back through his future descendants, embedding the *brit* in the very fabric of Jewish continuity. But its ending takes an unexpected turn: the covenant is invoked as protection, as the means by which the beloved ones are saved *mi-shachat*, from destruction. What does this mean? Rashi interprets *mi-shachat* as *mi-gehennom*, from Gehenna — citing the verse *gam at be-dam britech shilachti asirayich mi-bor ein mayim bo* (Zechariah 9:11). On Rashi's reading, the blessing is fully consistent with the covenantal theme: the *brit milah* is what grants Israel its Jewish identity and, through that, ultimate salvation. The notion that *brit milah* protects from *gehennom* is a prominent idea in medieval thought, less so in the *gemara* itself, but that is how Rashi renders the conclusion of the blessing.

One could also read *mi-shachat* more literally — as a reference to infant mortality — and see in this closing phrase a reflection of ancient concern about infant survival, with the *brit milah* understood as a divine sign of protection. Either reading, the blessing closes with *koreit ha-berit*, acknowledging God as the maker of the covenant, which frames the entire ceremony as an act of divine initiation rather than human ritual.

### Geirim Brachot

When the *milah* is performed on a *ger* — a convert — the *mohel* recites the same standard *beracha*: *asher kidishanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu al ha-milah*. The *mevarech* for a *ger* says: *asher kidishanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu lamol et ha-geirim u-lehatif mehem dam berit* — You commanded us to circumcise converts and to draw the blood of the covenant from them. The emphasis on *dam berit*, the blood of the covenant, is significant: converts very frequently require only *hatafat dam berit* rather than full circumcision, and the *beracha* is constructed to cover that case prominently.

The closing of the *mevarech*'s *beracha* for *geirim* invokes the cosmic significance of *dam berit*: *she-ilmalei dam berit lo nitkaymu shamayim va-aretz, she-nemar im lo briti yomam va-lailah chukot shamayim va-aretz lo samti* — were it not for the blood of the covenant, heaven and earth would not endure, as it is written: if not for My covenant by day and by night, I would not have established the laws of heaven and earth (Jeremiah 33:25). The closing is the same: *Baruch Atah Hashem, koreit ha-berit*.

This formulation raises an interesting observation. One might have expected the *beracha* for a *ger* to emphasize what is specifically happening to him — being brought into the *brit shel Avraham Avinu* — parallel to the father's *beracha* for his son. Instead, the significance invoked is generic: the cosmic importance of *dam berit* for the maintenance of the world. While the *beracha* does close with *koreit ha-berit*, acknowledging that a covenant is being made, the specific act of bringing this individual into the covenant of Abraham is not foregrounded. Whether one reads this as a deliberate theological choice or simply a formulation that was standardized for a different reason, it is curious.

### Slaves Brachot

When *milah* is performed on a *Canaanite slave* (*eved*), the formulation is identical in all respects to that used for *geirim*: the same *beracha* over the act, the same *mevarech* blessing invoking *dam berit*, the same closing *pasuk*, and the same *Baruch Atah Hashem koreit ha-berit*. No distinction is drawn between the *ger* and the *eved* in the blessing's text, despite the fact that their entry into the covenant carries different halachic weight. The *eved* has a partial *chiyuv mitzvot* and occupies a unique intermediate status — not a full Jew, not an outsider. Yet the *beracha* says *koreit ha-berit* for him as well, acknowledging that even the circumcision of a slave is a covenantal act. One might have expected some textual distinction to register this difference. The *gemara* records none.

## Tollin & Yom Tov

### Eliezer Allows

With the conclusion of the *perek* on *brit milah* — *hadran alach Rabbi Eliezer de-milah* — the *gemara* opens a new *perek*, again associated with a ruling of Rabbi Eliezer. The opening *mishna* shifts entirely to the laws of *Yom Tov*. *Rabbi Eliezer omer: tolin et ha-mashmeret be-Yom Tov* — Rabbi Eliezer permits suspending the *mashmeret*, a sieve or strainer, over a vessel on *Yom Tov*. The practice is to place this filter over a jar and pour wine through it, allowing the wine to pass through while trapping the lees and grape remnants above. Rabbi Eliezer permits setting up this apparatus on *Yom Tov* itself.

### Sages Stringent

The *chakhamim* disagree. *Ein tolin et ha-mashmeret be-Yom Tov* — the Sages prohibit setting it up on *Yom Tov*, even though food preparation is generally permitted on *Yom Tov*. Their reasoning is that this could have been done the day before, and we do not want acts to be performed on *Yom Tov* that could have been completed in advance. However, *aval nosnin li-keli be-Yom Tov* — if the strainer was already suspended prior to *Yom Tov*, one may pour wine through it on *Yom Tov*, since the food-related use itself is permitted. On *Shabbat*, even using a pre-set strainer is prohibited, because it resembles *borer*.

### The Problem

The *gemara* raises a sharp question against Rabbi Eliezer's leniency. If Rabbi Eliezer permits setting up the *mashmeret* on *Yom Tov*, he is apparently sanctioning the construction of an *ohel* — a covered structure — *le-chatchilah* on *Yom Tov*. The large cover placed over the vessel functions halachically as a kind of roof. But we know from another *sugya* that Rabbi Eliezer does not even permit adding a temporary addition to an existing *ohel arai* on *Yom Tov*, let alone creating a new *ohel arai* from scratch.

The source for that position is a *mishna* discussing *pkak ha-chalon* — a stopper for a window or skylight. Rabbi Eliezer permits using it only if it was already tied and suspended before *Shabbat*; otherwise, sealing the window would constitute adding to an enclosure. Rabban Gamliel and the Sages permit it either way. Rabbi Yochanan further clarifies, in a statement attributed to the school of Rabbah bar bar Chanah, that all opinions agree one may not construct a new *ohel arai* on *Yom Tov* — this goes without saying for *Shabbat*. The only dispute is about *adding* to an existing *ohel arai*: Rabbi Eliezer prohibits even an addition on *Yom Tov*; the Sages permit additions even on *Shabbat*. How, then, can Rabbi Eliezer allow the creation of a new *ohel* over the wine vessel on *Yom Tov*?

### Eliezer=Yehuda

The *gemara* resolves this by identifying Rabbi Eliezer's position with that of Rabbi Yehuda. *Rabbi Eliezer savar lei ke-Rabbi Yehuda*. A *baraita* teaches that the distinction between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* is that on *Yom Tov* one may perform acts directly related to *ochel nefesh*, food preparation. Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *matir af machshirei ochel nefesh* — he permits not only the direct food preparation but even the preparatory acts that facilitate food preparation. Rabbi Eliezer, the *gemara* proposes, holds in accordance with this more lenient position: since constructing the sieve apparatus is preparatory to the food act of straining wine, it is *machshirei ochel nefesh* and thus permitted on *Yom Tov*.

### Milah Parallel

The *gemara* immediately notes the elegant parallel: just as Rabbi Eliezer permitted *machshirei milah* — the preparatory acts enabling *brit milah* — to override *Shabbat*, so too here he permits *machshirei ochel nefesh* to be performed on *Yom Tov*.

The *gemara* presses further, however. Rabbi Yehuda permits *machshirei ochel nefesh* only when those preparatory acts *could not have been performed before Yom Tov*. Things that could have been set up the day before *Yom Tov* are not permitted on *Yom Tov* even according to Rabbi Yehuda. And the sieve, presumably, could have been suspended before *Yom Tov*. So the identification of Rabbi Eliezer with Rabbi Yehuda does not fully resolve the problem.

The *gemara* concludes that Rabbi Eliezer goes one step beyond Rabbi Yehuda. The same pattern obtains by *milah*: Rabbi Eliezer permitted *machshirei milah* even when they could theoretically have been done before *Shabbat*. He takes the same expansive position regarding *machshirei ochel nefesh* on *Yom Tov* — permitting preparatory food-related acts even when they could have been done in advance. The parallel between his rulings on *milah* and his rulings on *Yom Tov* is thus precise and consistent. The remainder of this *mishna*'s discussion will continue in the next *daf*.
