# Eruvin 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv vs. Shittuf

### Asymmetry Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַנִּי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בָּעִינַן עֵירוּב וּבָעִינַן שִׁיתּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: We require an *eiruv* and we** also **require a merging** of the courtyards in an alleyway, and one is not sufficient without the other.

This daf, 74a, picks up at the very bottom of 73b. To recapitulate: the *mishna* had raised the question of whether a *shittuf* can substitute for an *eruv* among courtyards, and conversely, whether an *eruv* can substitute for a *shittuf* in a *maboi*. The *mishna* seemed to reflect an asymmetry — a *shittuf* can work in place of an *eruv*, but not an *eruv* in place of a *shittuf*. There is a certain logic to this, since the *eruv* of courtyards does not directly incorporate the *maboi*. Nevertheless, the *gemara* was unwilling to accept the asymmetry at face value and worked to resolve it, whether according to Rabbi Meir or according to the *Rabbanan*.

### Rav's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: עֵירְבוּ בַּחֲצֵירוֹת וְנִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בַּמָּבוֹי, וְשָׁכַח אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חָצֵר וְלֹא עֵירַב — מוּתָּרִים כָּאן וְכָאן. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא בַּטֵּיל, אַמַּאי מוּתָּרִים? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּבַטֵּיל. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שָׁכַח אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי מָבוֹי וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ — מוּתָּרִין בַּחֲצֵירוֹת וַאֲסוּרִין בַּמָּבוֹי, וְאִי דְּבַטֵּיל — אַמַּאי אֲסוּרִין בַּמָּבוֹי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: If **they established an *eiruv* in the courtyards and** also **merged** the courtyards **in the alleyway, and one of the residents of the courtyard forgot and did not** contribute to the ***eiruv*** in his courtyard but did participate in the merging of the alleyway, **they are permitted** to carry both **here and there. What are the circumstances? If** the person who forgot **did not renounce** his rights to the courtyard in favor of the others, **why are they permitted** to carry? **Rather,** it is **obvious that he did renounce** those rights. But if so, **say the last clause** of the mishna: If **one of the members of the alleyway forgot and did not** participate in the **merging** of the alleyway, **they are permitted** to carry **in the courtyards and prohibited** from carrying **in the alleyway. But if he renounced** his rights, **why are they prohibited** from carrying **in the alleyway?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּלָא תָּנֵי פְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ. קָסָבַר כׇּל שִׁיתּוּף שֶׁאֵין מַכְנִיסוֹ וּמוֹצִיאוֹ דֶּרֶךְ פְּתָחִים בַּמָּבוֹי — לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ שִׁיתּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: What is the reason he did not teach** the mishna as stating that the five courtyards **open into one another?** Rav Yosef replied: Because **he holds** that **any merging** of alleyways **that is not brought in and taken out by way of the entrances** that open **into the alleyway,** i.e., which is not brought from each courtyard into the alleyway and then taken from the alleyway into the courtyard where it will be deposited, **is not considered** a valid **merging** of the alleyway.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַב, דְּקָא סָבַר: אֵין מָבוֹי נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת פְּתוּחִים לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we must retract the previous explanation and say that **the reason Rav** did not teach the mishna as stating that the courtyards opened into one another is **that he holds** that **an alleyway cannot** be **rendered permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam unless there are houses and courtyards opening into it.**

The *gemara* then turned to Rav's statement that the *mishna* was speaking of a case where the courtyards did not open into one another — they were separate from each other. This observation may originally have been intended to resolve the asymmetry problem, but the *gemara* redirected it toward a different issue entirely: the question of when it suffices to use only a *lechi* or *kora* — a vertical or horizontal beam — for the fourth wall of a *maboi*. Ordinarily, a *maboi* requires four walls, but the Rabbis were lenient and permitted a *lechi* or *kora* — provided the alleyway has the formal status of a *maboi*, which is normally defined as having two *chatzerot*, each with at least two houses. Rav's point was that if the courtyards open into one another and could theoretically all form a single unified *eruv*, they are considered one *chatzer* rather than two, and the leniency of the *lechi* or *kora* is therefore unavailable.

## Maboi Requirements

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב: אֵין מָבוֹי נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת פְּתוּחִין לְתוֹכוֹ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּיִת אֶחָד וְחָצֵר אַחַת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חוּרְבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now examines **the matter itself** cited in the previous discussion. **Rav said: An alleyway cannot** become **permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam,** **unless there are houses and courtyards opening into it.** This formulation implies that there must be at least two courtyards, each of which contains at least two houses. In the absence of these conditions, however, it is not considered an alleyway that can be permitted by means of a side post or a cross beam. **And Shmuel said: Even one house** without a courtyard **and one courtyard** with just one house is enough. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even a ruin** and a courtyard with a house suffice for a side post or a cross beam to render carrying in an alleyway permitted.

With that background established, the *gemara* now takes up the relevant passage directly — *gufa* — beginning at the top of 74a. Rav states: *ein maboi nitar b'lechi v'kora ad she-yihiyu batim v'chatzerot petuchim l'tocho* — a *maboi* cannot be rendered permissible with a *lechi* or *kora* unless it has houses and courtyards opening into it, meaning at least two houses in each *chatzer* and two *chatzerot* opening into it. That is the threshold that qualifies it as a *maboi* for which the Rabbis granted the leniency.

Shmuel disagrees: *afilu bayit echad v'chatzer achat* — even one house and one courtyard suffice. Rashi explains this carefully. Shmuel does not mean a single house standing alone or a bare yard. Rather, his picture is that one house opens directly into the *maboi* and one *chatzer* opens into the *maboi* as well — and for a space to qualify as a *chatzer*, it must of course have at least one house attached to it. So whereas according to Rav, each of the two *chatzerot* requires two houses, according to Shmuel, a single house opening directly into the *maboi* combined with a *chatzer* containing at least one house is sufficient. There is much that Shmuel's brief formulation leaves unstated; the explanation just offered is Rashi's reading of the passage.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁל כְּרָמִים? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֶלָּא בְּחוּרְבָּה, דַּחֲזֵי לְדִירָה. אֲבָל שְׁבִיל שֶׁל כְּרָמִים, דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְדִירָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** Did **Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that **even a vineyard path** and a courtyard with a house suffice to allow a side post or a cross beam to render carrying in the alleyway permitted? **He said to him: Rabbi Yoḥanan said** his ruling **only** in the case of **a ruin, which is fit** to serve **as a residence. However, a vineyard path, which is not fit** to serve **as a residence,** is **not** sufficient.

Rabbi Yochanan goes even further: *afilu horva* — even a ruin or desolate unbuilt lot qualifies. Not even a house is required on the second side. Rashi, however, supplies an important assumption that is not explicit in the text: everyone agrees that at least one *chatzer* must be present. What is being debated is only what can stand on the other side. According to Rashi, Rabbi Yochanan holds that the second element need not be a house at all — even a *horva* will do. But all authorities agree that one *chatzer* is required as a baseline.

### Horva vs. Path

The *gemara* raises a further question. Abaye asks Rav Yosef: does Rabbi Yochanan's leniency extend even to a *bishya shel keramim* — a path running through a vineyard? If almost nothing suffices, perhaps even a footpath through a forest or vineyard would do. The *gemara* answers: No. Rabbi Yochanan limited his ruling to a *horva* — a ruin or desolate lot — *d'chazi l'dira*, something that is fit in principle to be built up and inhabited. A *shevil shel kramim*, a path through a vineyard that is not fit for habitation and cannot be built into a dwelling, does not qualify.

### Rav Huna's Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא: וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן (אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן): אֶחָד גַּגּוֹת וְאֶחָד קַרְפֵּיפוֹת וְאֶחָד חֲצֵרוֹת — רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ לְתוֹכָן, וְלֹא לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana said: And Rabbi Yoḥanan followed his** regular line of **reasoning** in this regard, **as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Shimon said: Roofs, enclosures, and courtyards are all** considered **one domain with regard to vessels that rested inside them** at the beginning of Shabbat. Therefore, it is permitted to carry vessels that were in one of these areas at the beginning of Shabbat to any of the other areas. However, they are **not** considered the same domain **with regard to vessels that rested inside the house** at the beginning of Shabbat.

Rav Huna bar Chinana then observes that Rabbi Yochanan is being consistent with his own broader reasoning: *v'azda Rabbi Yochanan l'ta'amei*. The *gemara* connects this to a *mishna* in which Rabbi Shimon articulates a far-reaching principle — and it is to that principle that we now turn.

## Rabbi Shimon

### Unified Domains

Rabbi Shimon says: roofs, enclosed lots, and courtyards are all one domain with respect to vessels that began Shabbat within them — but not with respect to vessels that began Shabbat inside a house.

### Vessel Identity

To understand this, it is worth pausing to think carefully about what an *eruv* actually accomplishes and what the underlying problem is when no *eruv* is made. When residents of a courtyard have not made an *eruv* together, each house and the courtyard itself are all *reshuyot hayachid* — private domains. The prohibition is not simply carrying in general but specifically the act of moving an object from one type of domain into another. A vessel that begins Shabbat inside a particular house may not be taken from that house into the courtyard. Conversely, a vessel that began Shabbat in the courtyard may be carried within the courtyard — because the problem is not carrying per se, but crossing from one domain-type into a distinct domain-type. When the residents make an *eruv*, these separate units merge into one collective domain, and free movement among them becomes permitted.

### Carrying Between Domains

Rabbi Shimon now applies this logic across a broader set of spaces. Suppose there are multiple courtyards — call them A1, A2, A3 — that open into one another but whose residents have not made a joint *eruv*. The standard assumption would be that without an *eruv* among them, one cannot carry from the courtyard of A1 into the courtyard of A3, because they are distinct *reshuyot hayachid*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He holds that courtyards, rooftops, and enclosed lots all belong to the same fundamental category of *reshut hayachid* — they are all spaces that are not actual places of habitation. Because they share this character, a vessel that begins Shabbat in one of them may be carried into any other, without an *eruv*. One still cannot take a vessel from inside a house of A1 into the courtyard of A3, because the house is a qualitatively different type of domain. But among courtyards, rooftops, and enclosed lots, Rabbi Shimon sees one unified domain.

This is a striking position, and it is worth reiterating because it is essential to everything that follows. The Rabbis' requirement of an *eruv* to carry from house to courtyard reflects the fact that these are distinct domain-types, even though both are technically *reshut hayachid*. Rabbi Shimon says the same logic applies across courtyards: since a vessel that started in a courtyard carries the identity of that courtyard, and since all courtyards share the same essential character, no special unification is needed.

### Rashi's Expansion

Rashi actually goes even further with Rabbi Shimon's principle, noting that according to Rabbi Shimon, one may carry not only from one courtyard to another but also from a rooftop in A1 into the courtyard of A2 and from there into an enclosed lot — because all three categories (*chatzer*, *gag*, and *karpaf*) are treated as one single abstract domain. They are all spaces that are not actual homes. One can therefore traverse from one to another freely, provided a vessel began Shabbat within this category. This is not immediately apparent from the text itself, and the *gemara* does explore it later — there is indeed a debate between those who hold that each subcategory constitutes its own unit and those who, like Rashi's reading of Rabbi Shimon, treat them all as one large unified domain. The questions of adjacency also apply: one cannot cross a *reshut ha-rabbim* to get from one of these spaces to another; the discussion assumes they are all adjacent to one another.

## Gezeirah Concern

### Rav's Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְהוּא שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ. אֲבָל עֵירְבוּ, גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָאנֵי דְבָתִּים לְחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav said: The halakha is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And this is** only in a case **where** the residents of the courtyards **did not establish an *eiruv*** for each courtyard, so that they may only carry the vessels left in the courtyards, but they may not take out vessels from their houses into their courtyards. **However, if they established an *eiruv*** for each courtyard, **we decree** against carrying even vessels that were in the courtyard when Shabbat began, **lest they come to take out objects** from their **houses to the courtyard.** This would lead to the mistake of carrying those objects from one courtyard to another, which is prohibited.

Given Rabbi Shimon's principle, the *gemara* now reports: *amar Rav halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Shimon. But then Rav adds a critical qualification: *v'hu she-lo eirvu* — this leniency applies only if the courtyard residents did not make an *eruv*. The irony is striking. Rav says: if A1 made an *eruv* internally, so that its residents can carry freely between their houses and their courtyard, then the rule of Rabbi Shimon — that a vessel from A1's courtyard may be carried into A3's courtyard — no longer applies.

Why? Because as soon as A1 makes an *eruv*, it becomes permissible to take a vessel from inside a house of A1 into the courtyard of A1. Once that happens, the *gemara* explains, we are concerned — *gazrinan d'atei l'apukei manei d'vatim l'chatzer* — that people will come to take vessels that began Shabbat in a house of A1 into A1's courtyard and from there carry them into A3's courtyard. The vessel that made it from the house to the courtyard has now been carried in from a qualitatively different domain-type, and yet it continues traveling into other courtyards. This would be a prohibited carrying, and Rav imposes a *gezeirah*: when an *eruv* has been made, the leniency of Rabbi Shimon is suspended entirely, lest people fall into this error.

### Shmuel Rejects Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בֵּין עֵירְבוּ וּבֵין לֹא עֵירְבוּ. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, בֵּין עֵירְבוּ וּבֵין לֹא עֵירְבוּ. אַלְמָא: לָא גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָאנֵי דְבָתִּים לְחָצֵר, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָאנֵי דְחָצֵר לְחוּרְבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **whether** the residents of the courtyards **established an *eiruv*** for each courtyard **or whether they did not establish an *eiruv*** for each courtyard. **And so too, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, whether they established an *eiruv*** for each courtyard **or whether they did not establish an *eiruv*** for each courtyard. **Apparently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that **we do not decree** against carrying vessels that began Shabbat in the courtyard **lest they come to take out objects from** their **houses to the courtyard. Here too,** with regard to an alleyway that contains a ruin, **we do not decree** against carrying in the alleyway **lest they come to take out objects from the courtyard to the ruin** by carrying it through the alleyway.

Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan both reject this *gezeirah*. Shmuel rules: *bein eirvu bein lo eirvu* — whether or not the residents made an *eruv*, the principle of Rabbi Shimon stands and one may carry a vessel from A1's courtyard into A3's courtyard. Rabbi Yochanan states the same: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon bein eirvu bein lo eirvu*. Neither of them is concerned that permitting cross-courtyard carrying will lead people to mistakenly take vessels that originated in a house.

### True Concern

At this point the *gemara* shifts course, and it is worth pausing to appreciate that this is genuinely a change of direction. Until now, the dispute between Rav, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan about the minimum requirements for a *maboi* seemed to be a purely formalistic question: how many *chatzerot* does a *maboi* need, and how many houses does a *chatzer* need? Now the *gemara* introduces a different concern entirely. The concern is not only formal but functional: even if we recognize a *maboi* as valid, the fact that a *horva* has no residents means that no one has made an *eruv* or *shittuf* incorporating it. It therefore remains a conceptually separate zone — a distinct space that has not been unified with the rest. If we permit carrying in the *maboi*, people may come to carry from the *maboi* into the *horva*: *lo gazrinan dilma atei l'apukei manei d'chatzer l'chorva*.

The *gemara* concludes that Rabbi Yochanan is consistent with his broader reasoning precisely here. Since Rabbi Yochanan does not make such a *gezeirah* in the context of Rabbi Shimon's principle — he is untroubled by the concern that permitting something will lead people to extend it improperly — so too here he is not troubled by the fear that permitting carrying in the *maboi* will lead people to carry into the *horva*. That is why Rabbi Yochanan *azda l'ta'amei* — his ruling about the *maboi* flows naturally from his general refusal to impose *gezeirot* of this type.

One might note an additional irony: since nobody inhabits the *horva*, no *shittuf* is needed to cover it — there is only one party in this entire alleyway. But since the *horva* is not part of any *eruv* and remains a conceptually distinct type of space, one cannot actually carry into it. According to Rabbi Yochanan, however, this residual concern does not prevent us from recognizing the *maboi* as valid for the purpose of the *lechi* and *kora* leniency.

## Eivu Bar Ichi Case

### Rav Bruna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב בְּרוֹנָא וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר בֵּי רַב: אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַחְוִי לִי אוּשְׁפִּיזֵיהּ, אַחְוִי לֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲמַר מָר הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beruna sat and recited this *halakha*** stated by Shmuel, that an alleyway containing one house and one courtyard can be rendered permitted for carrying by means of a side post or a cross beam. **Rabbi Elazar, a student of a Torah academy, said to him: Did Shmuel** really **say this?** Rav Beruna **said to him: Yes,** he did. **He said to him: Show me his lodging** and I will go and ask him myself, and **he showed him.** Rabbi Elazar **came before Shmuel** and **said to him:** Did **the Master** actually **say this?** Shmuel **said to him: Yes,** I did.

The *gemara* now returns to the earlier dispute about the minimum requirements for a *maboi*. Rav Bruna was sitting and relating Shmuel's lenient ruling — that even one house and one courtyard suffice — when Rabbi Elazar bar Bei Rav challenged him: did Shmuel really say this? Rav Bruna confirmed that he did. Rabbi Elazar bar Bei Rav was not satisfied and insisted on going to Shmuel himself to verify the report.

When Rabbi Elazar bar Bei Rav came before Shmuel and asked whether he had truly ruled this way, Shmuel confirmed it. Rabbi Elazar then pressed Shmuel with a challenge: *ein lanu b'eiruvin ela k'lashon mishnateinu* — we follow exactly the language of the *mishna*, and nothing more. Which *mishna* is he invoking? The *mishna* that states: *she-ha-maboi la-chatzerot k'chatzer la-batim* — the *maboi* is to the courtyards as the *chatzer* is to the houses. This is an analogy: just as units of houses constitute a *chatzer*, so units of *chatzerot* constitute a *maboi*. And since the *mishna* uses the plural — *chatzerot* for the *maboi* and *batim* for the *chatzer* — a *maboi* requires two *chatzerot* and a *chatzer* requires two houses. How then can Shmuel permit a *maboi* with only one house and one courtyard?

### Mishna's Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר: אֵין לָנוּ בְּעֵירוּבִין אֶלָּא כִּלְשׁוֹן מִשְׁנָתֵינוּ, שֶׁהַמָּבוֹי לַחֲצֵירוֹת כֶּחָצֵר לַבָּתִּים. אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar raised the following objection: **Wasn't it the Master** himself who **said** concerning a different issue: **With regard to** the *halakhot* of ***eiruv*, we have only the wording of our mishna.** The mishna states **that an alleyway is to** its **courtyards like a courtyard is to** its **houses,** which indicates that an alleyway must have at least two courtyards in order to be considered an alleyway and be rendered permitted for carrying through a side post or cross beam. Shmuel **was silent** and did not answer him.

In the face of this challenge, the *gemara* reports: *ishtik* — Shmuel was silent. The *gemara* then asks: did he accept the refutation and reverse his ruling, or did he not? *Kiblah minei o lo kiblah minei?*

### Chazan Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אוֹ לָא קַבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּהָהוּא מְבוֹאָה דַּהֲוָה דָּיַיר בֵּיהּ אַיְבוּת בַּר אִיהִי, עֲבַד לֵיהּ לִחְיָיא, וּשְׁרָא לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Did Shmuel's silence indicate that he **accepted** Rabbi Elazar's objection and retracted his statement, **or did he not accept it from him?** The Gemara attempts to bring a proof from the following incident. **Come** and **hear:** There was **a certain alleyway that Ivut bar Ihi lived in,** which contained only one house and one courtyard. **He erected a side post for it, and Shmuel permitted him** to carry in it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא רַב עָנָן שַׁדְיֵהּ. אָמַר: מְבוֹאָה דְּדָיַירְנָא בֵּיהּ וְאָתֵינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל, נֵיתֵי רַב עָנָן בַּר רַב נִישְׁדְּיֵיהּ מִן?! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Following Shmuel's death, **Rav Anan came and threw** the side post down, thus indicating to Ivut bar Ihi that it is prohibited to carry in the alleyway, as a side post is effective only for an alleyway that has at least two courtyards containing at least two houses each. Ivut bar Ihi **said** with resentment: **The alleyway in which I have been living and walking** based on a ruling **in the name of Master Shmuel, shall Rav Anan bar Rav come** now and **throw** its side post away **from** me? The Gemara comments: **Learn from** the fact that this side post remained intact throughout Shmuel's lifetime that he **did not accept** Rabbi Elazar's objection.

To resolve this, the *gemara* cites a case. There was a certain *maboi* in which a person named Eivu bar Ichi was living. He placed a *lechi* and Shmuel permitted it. The assumption is that this *maboi* had only one *chatzer* and one house — which would be the minimum Shmuel originally permitted. Rabana then came and threw down the *lechi*, declaring that the arrangement was invalid because the *maboi* lacked two *chatzerot*.

Eivu bar Ichi protested: this is the *maboi* in which he lives, and he has been carrying in it in the name of Shmuel — is Rabana bar Rav now going to throw down his *lechi*? The *gemara* infers from the fact that Shmuel maintained his original ruling despite Rabana's challenge that Shmuel had not reversed his position in the face of the earlier *kashya* about the *mishna*'s language.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּהּ, וְהָכָא חַזָּנָא הוּא דַּהֲוָה אָכֵיל נַהֲמָא בְּבֵיתֵיהּ וְאָתֵי בָּיֵית בְּבֵי כְנִישְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof. **Actually, you can say** that Shmuel **accepted** Rabbi Elazar's objection and retracted his opinion, **and here there was** a synagogue **attendant [*ḥazzana*] who would eat bread in his** own **house** that was located elsewhere, **but** would **come and sleep in the synagogue,** which was open to the alleyway.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַיְבוּת בַּר אִיהִי סָבַר: מְקוֹם פִּיתָּא גָּרֵים. וּשְׁמוּאֵל לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: מָקוֹם לִינָה גָּרֵים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ivut bar Ihi holds** that **the place** where a person eats his **bread determines** his place of residence. Therefore, he did not consider the synagogue a residence, as the attendant would eat elsewhere, and Ivut bar Ihi thought that Shmuel had permitted him to set up a side post for his alleyway even though he lived there by himself. In fact, however, this was not the case, as **Shmuel followed his** regular line of **reasoning, as he said: The place** where a person **sleeps determines** his place of residence. Since the attendant would sleep in the synagogue, it was considered a residence. Consequently, the alleyway contained two houses and courtyards, and could be made permitted for carrying by means of a side post or a cross beam.

But the *gemara* immediately offers an alternative reading. Perhaps Shmuel did accept the refutation and did reverse his position. In that case, what was actually happening in the case of Eivu bar Ichi? The *gemara* explains: *v'hacha chazana hu d'hava achil nahma b'veiteih v'ati bayit b'veit kenishta* — there was a *chazan*, a synagogue sexton, who ate his meals in his own home but slept in the synagogue, and the synagogue was located in this *maboi* and courtyard.

Now the question becomes: does the synagogue count as a second house for purposes of the *eruv*? This depends on a broader debate about what constitutes a person's primary residence. Eivu bar Ichi thought that Shmuel was permitting the *lechi* because he held that where a person eats determines his dwelling place — *makom pita goreim* — and since the *chazan* ate elsewhere, he was not considered a resident of the *maboi*, and Shmuel was permitting it even with only one actual dwelling. But in fact, Eivu bar Ichi had misunderstood Shmuel's ruling. Shmuel's actual view is *makom linah goreim* — the place where a person sleeps determines his residence — and since the *chazan* slept in the synagogue, he was deemed a resident of the *maboi*. The synagogue thus counted as a second house, giving the *maboi* the two houses and two *chatzerot* it needed.

This reading allows the *gemara* to bring the entire discussion full circle: even Shmuel, in the end, may have reversed himself and agreed with the requirement of two houses and two *chatzerot*, and the case of Eivu bar Ichi turns not on a lenient minimum threshold but on the question of what determines a person's legal place of habitation.

## Non-Jew Maboi

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מָבוֹי שֶׁצִּידּוֹ אֶחָד גּוֹי וְצִידּוֹ אֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵין מְעָרְבִין אוֹתוֹ דֶּרֶךְ חַלּוֹנוֹת לְהַתִּירוֹ דֶּרֶךְ פְּתָחִים לַמָּבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said:** With regard to **an alleyway, one side of which** was occupied by **a gentile and one side of which** was occupied by **a Jew,** and the house of the Jew was connected to the houses of other Jews via windows but not via doors, and those other houses open directly into the public domain, the residents of the houses on the side of the alleyway where the Jews live **may not establish an *eiruv* through the windows** in order **to render** it **permitted** for the residents of the other houses to carry **through the doors** of the house leading **to the alleyway.**

The *gemara* continues with a new ruling transmitted in the name of Rav: *mavoi she-tzido echad oved kochavim v'tzido echad Yisrael* — a *maboi* in which one side is inhabited by non-Jews and the other side by Jews. Rashi provides a helpful diagram of the arrangement: the *maboi* is the alleyway, one *chatzer* on one side is occupied by non-Jews, and the *chatzer* on the other side has Jewish residents.

### Rav's Prohibition

Rav rules: *ein me'arvin oto derech chalonot l'hatiro derech petachim la-maboi* — one may not make an *eruv* through windows between the houses in order to permit carrying through the doorways into the *maboi*. The Jews cannot solve the problem of the non-Jewish presence by making an internal *eruv* among themselves through windows connecting their houses, and then use that to enable carrying into the *maboi*.

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: אָמַר רַב אֲפִילּוּ בְּחָצֵר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, דְּאִי לָא אָמַר מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** Did **Rav say** this **even with regard to a courtyard,** one side of which was occupied by a gentile and the other side of which was occupied by a Jew whose house was connected through windows to the houses of other Jews? **He said to him: Yes, as** even **if he did not say** so, **what** would be the difference? It is the exact same principle.

Abaye asked Rav Yosef: would Rav say the same even if instead of a *maboi* between them, it was a *chatzer*? That is, does this prohibition apply equally when the obstruction is a courtyard rather than a *maboi*?

### Non-Jew Domain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה אָמֵינָא טַעְמָא דְּרַב מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר: אֵין מָבוֹי נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת פְּתוּחִין לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye responded: **I would have said** that **the rationale for** the opinion of **Rav is because he holds** that **an alleyway cannot be rendered permitted** for carrying within it **with a side post and** a cross **beam unless there are houses and courtyards opening into it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי! צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי מֵהַהִיא —
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef said: If that were the reason, **why** would **I need two** rulings regarding the same issue? Rav already stated that an alleyway can be rendered permitted for carrying within it only if it has houses and courtyards opening into it. Abaye explained that both rulings are **necessary. As, if** Rav had taught this *halakha* only **from that** general ruling,

Rav Yosef replied in the affirmative: yes, Rav would say the same even by a *chatzer*. And this response is necessary, because without it one might have misunderstood the entire force of Rav's ruling. If Rav had spoken only about a *maboi*, one would have said that his concern is simply the formalistic one: you do not have two valid *chatzerot* here, because the non-Jew's *chatzer* does not count as a proper *chatzer*. That would merely restate his earlier requirement of two *chatzerot* with two houses, with the added note that a non-Jew's dwelling does not qualify. But then there would have been no need to articulate this ruling separately at all — it would have been fully subsumed by the earlier statement. The fact that Rav applies this ruling even to a *chatzer* — where the issue of the *lechi* and *kora* is irrelevant — shows that there is something more at stake.

## Yachid Issue

### Unified Household

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דִּירַת גּוֹי שְׁמָהּ דִּירָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּדִירַת גּוֹי לֹא שְׁמָהּ דִּירָה. וְאִי מֵהָכָא — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לָא יָדַעְנָא בָּתִּים כַּמָּה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן בָּתִּים תְּרֵין.
>
> **Translation:** **I would have said** that **the residence of a gentile is considered a residence** with regard to defining an area as an alleyway. Therefore, he **teaches us that the** legal **status of the residence of a gentile is not considered a** full-fledged **residence** in this regard. **And if** Rav had taught this *halakha* only **from** the ruling **here,** with regard to gentiles, **I would have said** that **I do not know how many houses there** are. Therefore, he **teaches us** that there must be at least **two houses** and two courtyards.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב אֲפִילּוּ חָצֵר, טַעְמָא דְּרַב דְּקָא סָבַר אָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת יָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם גּוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **Now that Rav** has **said** that this *halakha* applies **even** to **a courtyard,** this implies that **the reason for** the opinion of **Rav is that he holds: It is prohibited for an individual to establish** his home **in the place** where **a gentile** resides. Consequently, he is prohibited from establishing an *eiruv*, so that the difficulties of living there will force him to move.

What, then, is the real concern? The *gemara* explains Rav's reasoning: *ta'ama d'Rav d'kasavar asur la'asot yachid bimkom ovdei kochavim* — Rav holds that it is prohibited to constitute oneself as a single unified household in the presence of non-Jews. The Jews in this courtyard were attempting to solve the problem by making an *eruv* among themselves through the windows connecting their houses — essentially merging into one household without going through the courtyard at all. The intention was to bypass the non-Jewish presence entirely. But Rav rules that this very maneuver — creating a *yachid* in the presence of a *nochri* — is itself prohibited.

### Rav Yosef Understanding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי טַבְלָא דְּאָמַר: ״גּוֹי גּוֹי״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: If so, this is** why **I heard Rabbi Tavla say: A gentile, a gentile, two times** while teaching this subject, even though **I did not understand** then **what he** meant to **say.** Now I realize that he was speaking about both an alleyway and a courtyard.

Rav Yosef then adds a striking observation. Now, he says, I understand something that had previously puzzled him. Rav Tavla, when transmitting this teaching of Rav, repeated the phrase *ovdei kochavim* — non-Jews — twice in the course of a single short ruling: *mavoi she-tzido echad ovdei kochavim, tzido echad Yisrael, ein me'arvin oto derech chalonot l'tzorech derech petachim la-maboi*. Rav Yosef had not understood why Rav Tavla emphasized the non-Jewish element twice — *ovdei kochavim, ovdei kochavim, trei zimnei*. It seemed redundant.

### Yachid Summary

But now it is clear. The repeated emphasis on the non-Jew is not stylistic redundancy but the very heart of the ruling. The reason the *eruv* through windows is prohibited is not a formalistic concern about the count of *chatzerot* — it is because what the Jews are trying to create is a *yachid bimkom nochri*, a single unified household in the presence of a non-Jew, and that is independently forbidden. By stressing the non-Jewish presence twice, Rav Tavla was flagging that the non-Jew is not incidental background detail but the operative legal factor. The entire ruling turns on it.

To summarize the arc of this final section: what at first appears to be a ruling about the technical structure of a *maboi* with a mixed neighborhood reveals itself, upon closer examination, to be driven by the principle of *yachid bimkom nochri*. The attempted solution — forming one household through windows while ignoring the non-Jewish courtyard — is precisely what Rav prohibits. And the need to state this ruling both for the *maboi* and for the *chatzer* was necessary in order to make clear that the concern is not about the count of *chatzerot* required by the *lechi* and *kora* leniency, but about the independent prohibition of constituting a *yachid* in the presence of a non-Jew.
