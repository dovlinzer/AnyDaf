# Yoma 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wife Separation

### Miluim/Har Sinai Recap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַפְרִישִׁין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וְכוּ׳. לָמָּה מַפְרִישִׁין? לָמָּה מַפְרִישִׁין?! כִּדְקָאָמְרִינַן, אִי לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ, אִי לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ כִּדְאִית לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** § After a long digression in which many peripheral issues were addressed, the Gemara returns to interpreting the mishna. It was taught in the mishna: The Sages would **remove the High Priest** from his house to the Chamber of *Parhedrin*. The Gemara asks: **Why do** the Sages **remove him?** The Gemara asks in astonishment: **Why do** the Sages **remove him?** It is **as we stated above: Whether** it is according **to Rabbi Yoḥanan, as per his** opinion: Sequestering of the High Priest is derived from the sequestering prior to the inauguration; **or whether it is** according **to Reish Lakish, as per his** opinion: Sequestering of the High Priest is derived from sequestering at Sinai, the answer is clear. What is the point of the Gemara's question?

The daf opens, approximately eight lines from the top of *vav amud aleph*, with the *gemara* revisiting the discussion that occupied the preceding four days. The *mishna* states *mafrishin kohen gadol mibeto* — the *kohen gadol* is separated from his house for seven days prior to the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur*, during which time he would reside in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* within the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *gemara* asks: *lama mafrishin?* — why do we separate him? Its own reply is almost impatient: *kideka'amarnan* — as we have already said. Either according to Rabbi Yochanan, who derived the seven-day separation from the model of the *miluim*, or according to Reish Lakish, who derived it from *Har Sinai* — the reasoning has already been established over the preceding discussion.

### Beto = Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִבֵּיתוֹ לָמָּה פֵּירַשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This is** what the Gemara **is saying. Why did he withdraw from his house,** i.e., his wife?

Having dispatched the general question, the *gemara* turns to the specific word *mibeto*. The word *bayit* throughout *Shas* commonly refers not merely to a physical dwelling but to a man's wife — the Aramaic word for wife, *devataiyu*, itself reflects this usage — and the *gemara* takes for granted that the *mishna*'s phrasing means he was separated from his wife. This is consistent with the *pasuk* of *Yom Kippur*, *vechiper ba'ado uve'ad beto*, which the *gemara* elsewhere reads as *beto zu ishto*, his wife, and which is why the *kohen gadol* is required to be married on *Yom Kippur*. The *gemara* now asks: *mibeto lama pirash?* — specifically why was he separated from his wife? Why couldn't his wife join him in the *Lishkat Parhedrin*?

### Lishkat Parhedrin

The *gemara* frames this as a genuine question and proceeds to seek the reason. Before it offers an answer, the question itself implicitly presupposes that the *Lishkat Parhedrin* was not possessed of the full *kedushah* of the *azarah* — otherwise the question would be self-answering, since one may not even sit in the *azarah*, let alone sleep there or engage in marital relations. The nature of the *lishkah*'s sanctity is itself a matter that the *gemara* will address in greater detail.

## Wife Separation…

### Tumah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁמָּא תִּמָּצֵא אִשְׁתּוֹ סָפֵק נִדָּה וְיָבֹא עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** It is due to the concern **lest his wife be found** to be in a situation of **uncertainty** as to whether or not she has the halakhic status of **a menstruating woman, and** he will **have relations with her** and become impure.

The *gemara* presents a *baraita*: *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira omer, shema timatze ishto safek nida veyavo aleha* — lest his wife turn out to be a *safek niddah* and he come upon her. The reason for separation is not, as one might have supposed, rooted in the *miluim* model or the *Har Sinai* model — it is a *tumah* concern. The *kohen gadol* must be *tahor* on *Yom Kippur*, and if his wife is present and they are living as husband and wife, there is a risk that he will have relations with her while she is in a state of *niddah*, thereby contracting a seven-day *tumah*.

## Safek Niddah

### R. Yehuda ben Beteira

A man who has relations with a woman who is a *niddah* acquires, in addition to the grave prohibition entailing *karet*, the *tumah* of the *niddah* herself — a seven-day *tumah*. The *baraita* of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira thus grounds the entire institution of separation in a concern for *tumah* rather than in any conceptual framework of spiritual preparation. This is itself a striking feature of the *gemara*'s approach, to which we will return.

### Atu Bershei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ בְּרַשִּׁיעֵי עָסְקִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks in astonishment: **Is that to say** that **we are dealing with wicked people?**

The *gemara* immediately raises an objection: *atu bersha'ei askinan?* — are we dealing with wicked people? He would never knowingly have relations with his wife when she is definitely a *niddah*, or even when she is a *safek niddah*. The prohibition itself would restrain him, quite apart from any *tumah* concern.

### Timing Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: שֶׁמָּא יָבֹא עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ וְתִמָּצֵא סָפֵק נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** rephrase the statement: It is due to the concern **lest he have relations with his wife and** then **she be found** to be in a situation of **uncertainty** as to whether or not she has the halakhic status of **a menstruating woman.** In a case where blood is found on the sheets after the couple engaged in relations, and there is uncertainty as to whether or not the High Priest had relations with his wife while she had the halakhic status of a menstruating woman, the status of the High Priest is one of uncertain impurity.

The *gemara* therefore refines the scenario: *ela shema yavo al ishto vetimsa safek nida* — rather, the concern is that he will have relations with his wife permissibly, and only afterward will she discover blood, creating a retroactive *safek* as to whether she was a *niddah* at the time of relations. The act is done entirely in good faith; the *safek* arises after the fact. While the *gemara* could have stated the concern in terms of her definitely becoming a *niddah* mid-act, it frames it as a *safek* in order to describe a scenario that is both more realistic and more common — they have relations with no indication of any problem, and then some time later a bloodstain is discovered. The question is then whether that retroactive *safek* is sufficient to render the husband *tamei*.

## Rav Chisda Position

### R. Akiva/Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה אֶת בּוֹעֲלָהּ. דְּאִי רַבָּנַן, הָא אָמְרִי: אֵין נִדָּה מְטַמְּאָה אֶת בּוֹעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages stated** the following assumption **before Rav Ḥisda: In accordance with whose** opinion is that a reason for concern? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said: A menstruating woman** who found blood on the sheets within twenty-fours after having relations, creating uncertainty with regard to her status when she engaged in relations, **renders the man who had with relations with her** retroactively **impure. As, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** it is difficult: **Didn't they say** that a woman with regard to whom there is uncertainty as to whether or not she has the halakhic status of **a menstruating woman does not render the man who had with relations with her** retroactively **impure.** Therefore, the High Priest need not leave his wife during the week prior to Yom Kippur.

The rabbis brought this concern before Rav Chisda. His response — *keman?* — cuts to the issue: this would only be a legitimate concern according to Rabbi Akiva, *d'amar niddah metama bo'alah*, who holds that the *niddah* renders her husband *tamei* retroactively. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold *ein niddah metama bo'alah* — the *niddah* does not render her husband *tamei* in this way.

### 24-Hour Retroactive

This requires explanation. The *rabbanan* do not deny that a man who has relations with a definite *niddah* is *tamei*. The question concerns the mechanism of *tumah* when the woman's status as *niddah* is established only retroactively. The first *mishna* in *Niddah* establishes that when a woman finds blood — whether through an internal examination or on her garments — she is *temeiah* retroactively for the preceding twenty-four hours (absent a more recent internal check that would shorten the window). This retroactivity governs objects she handled: anything she touched in the prior twenty-four hours is retroactively *tamei*. The debate is whether this retroactive *tumah* extends to her husband as well. The *rabbanan* hold that it does not — *psychologically*, as it were, the concern that one's wife might retroactively turn out to have been a *niddah* during any prior act of relations would make marital life untenable, and the sages did not extend the retroactivity that far. Rabbi Akiva disagrees and holds that even the husband is rendered *tamei* retroactively. Rav Chisda's point is therefore that if this is the governing concern behind the separation, it would only be operative according to Rabbi Akiva.

## Rav Chisda Position…

### Three Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימְרוּ רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶלָּא בְּאַחַר אַחַר, אֲבָל בְּחַד אַחַר — מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to** the Sages: **Even if you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of the **Rabbis, the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Akiva** with regard to retroactive impurity **only** in a case where blood was discovered on the sheets **long afterward,** after there was time for the woman to leave the bed and bathe and only then discover the blood. Due to the time that elapsed, the Rabbis hold that there is no way to prove a connection between when the woman menstruated and when they engaged in relations. **However,** if the blood was found merely **afterward,** the Rabbis **concede to** Rabbi Akiva that she renders the man retroactively impure.

Rav Chisda, however, does not leave it there. He responds to the rabbis: *afilu teima rabanan* — even granting the position of the *rabbanan*, the concern remains. The *rabbanan* only dispute Rabbi Akiva regarding *achar achar* — the "after of the after," a time period beyond approximately thirty seconds following relations. The *gemara* identifies three distinct temporal scenarios.

The first is immediate discovery: she checks the moment they separate and finds she is bleeding. In that case, there is a presumption that she was already a *niddah* during relations, with implications even for the obligation to bring a *korban*. The second scenario is discovery within approximately thirty seconds — enough time to rise from the bed and perform a rinse. In that window, the *safek* is genuine and real at a biblical level, and everyone agrees that the husband would be *tamei*, since it is a true *safek* whether she was a *niddah* during the act itself. The third scenario is discovery after that thirty-second window. It is here that the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *rabbanan* applies: only Rabbi Akiva would extend retroactive *tumah* to the husband in that case; the *rabbanan* would not. Rav Chisda's point is that even according to the *rabbanan*, there is a real window — the first thirty seconds — during which everyone agrees the husband would be *tamei* at a biblical level. The concern is not merely theoretical.

## Safek Niddah…

### Time Periods

So the *gemara*'s description of the concern is precise: the scenario is not that she was bleeding while they were having relations, but that a period of time elapsed afterward — corresponding to the middle window — during which discovery would create a genuine, biblically-grounded *safek* as to the husband's *tumah* status. It is precisely that realistic scenario which motivates the separation.

## Conceptual Questions

### Spiritual Model Q

At this point, a deeper question presents itself. Why does the *gemara* ground the separation entirely in this *tumah* concern — a secondary, practical matter — rather than in a more primary conceptual framework? One might have supposed that the very models the *gemara* invoked earlier — *miluim* and *Har Sinai* — themselves supply a compelling reason for the *kohen gadol* to be separated from his wife. Why is *tumah* offered as the explanation rather than the concept of spiritual preparation or elevated consecration implicit in those models?

### Miluim Immersion

On reflection, the *miluim* model may not carry the weight one expects here. The verse *mipetach ohel moed lo teitzu shivat yamim pen tamutu* — "you shall not depart from the entrance of the tent of meeting for seven days lest you die" — describes an immersion into the life of the *kehunah* and the *avodah* of the *Mishkan*. The *miluim* was about initiating the *kohanim* into their role, inaugurating the space, and conferring upon them the status of *kehunah*. As has been discussed elsewhere, the Rav understood the *Yom Kippur* process in terms of imparting the *kohen gadol* with a special status. But the *miluim* model is not essentially about shedding the material self or transcending the physical world — it is about full immersion into a new institutional role. One could therefore argue that even within the *miluim* model, having one's wife present while fully inhabiting the priestly role is not obviously contradicted.

### Har Sinai Emphasis

The *Har Sinai* model, however, is considerably more difficult to dismiss. The forty days Moshe spent on the mountain were characterized by complete abstinence: *lachem lo achalti umayim lo shatiti* — he ate no bread and drank no water. The *gemara* notes that perhaps the first days were to purge him of food. And the three days prior to *matan Torah*, the verse states *al tigshu el isha* — the separation from women was explicit. Moshe's own permanent separation from his wife, *Tzipporah*, alluded to in *chazal*'s reading of various passages, underscores that in the *Har Sinai* model the separation from one's wife is intrinsic and self-evident. It is therefore genuinely puzzling why, according to Reish Lakish who derives the seven-day separation from *Har Sinai*, the *gemara* should need any additional explanation grounded in *tumah* concern. According to that model, the reason for the separation from the wife seems entirely self-evident from the paradigm itself. This question does not receive a clear answer in the *gemara*.

## Lishkat Status

### Rashi

A second question concerns what Rashi calls the *havaminah* of the question itself. When the *gemara* asks why the *kohen gadol* could not have his wife present in the *Lishkat Parhedrin*, it implicitly assumes she could be there and they could live as husband and wife. But how is that conceivable? Is the *Lishkat Parhedrin* not within the *Beit HaMikdash*? Rashi addresses this directly. He writes: *tavo ishto imo sham* — let his wife come with him there, *sheharei al korchacha Lishkat Parhedrin lo haysa kedushah* — because by necessity the *Lishkat Parhedrin* could not have had the *kedushah* of the *azarah*. Rashi's reasoning: *she-ein yeshiva ba-azarah ela le-malchei beis David kol she-kein shechivah* — one may not even sit in the *azarah* other than kings of the Davidic line, and certainly not lie down. The *kohen gadol* must sleep during his seven days, and therefore the *lishkah* in which he slept cannot have possessed the sanctity of the *azarah* proper. Since it lacks *azarah* sanctity, the question then arises why his wife could not be there with him.

### Tosafot Q

Tosafot, however, presses further. Granting that the *Lishkat Parhedrin* lacked the *kedushah* of the *azarah*, it still would have had at minimum the *kedushah* of *Har Habayit*. As a *baraita* in *perek Bachatzot* states, *kol Har Habayit machaneh Leviyah hu* — the entire Temple Mount has the status of *machaneh Leviyah*, and a *ba'al keri* — one who has had a seminal emission — may not enter *machaneh Leviyah*. If the *kohen gadol* were to have marital relations in the *Lishkat Parhedrin*, he would thereby become a *ba'al keri*, who is forbidden even on *Har Habayit*. So even before the *tumah* of *boel niddah* becomes relevant, the very act of relations would render him a *ba'al keri* and disqualify him from remaining in that space. Why then is *ba'al keri* not itself the stated concern?

### Underground Tunnel

Tosafot resolves this by reframing the *havaminah* entirely. The question is not why his wife cannot join him in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* as it actually existed, but rather: why was the separation not conducted in a *mechilah* — an underground tunnel beneath *Har Habayit*? The *gemara* in *perek Ketzad Tzolin* establishes that underground chambers were not sanctified with the *kedushah* of the temple precincts above them, *de-mechilot lo nitkadshu*. Had the *kohen gadol* resided in such a tunnel, neither *azarah* sanctity nor *Har Habayit* sanctity would apply, and presumably marital relations could have taken place there. It is against that *havaminah* — residence in an underground space devoid of *kedushah* — that the *tumah* concern of *boel niddah* serves as the answer.

This has an interesting implication. If the *havaminah* is that the *kohen gadol* might have been situated in an underground tunnel, then the concern about fulfilling the models of *miluim* or *Har Sinai* — particularly Reish Lakish's model in which the *kohen gadol* must be in some sense *nichtav be'anan*, enveloped in the divine cloud — would seem to require his presence in or near the *Mishkan* or *Beit HaMikdash* itself, not merely in some subterranean space beneath it. This is, of course, only the *havaminah*. In the event, since his wife does not accompany him, the *kohen gadol* is in the *Lishkat Parhedrin* itself, located on *Har Habayit* grounds — whether with the sanctity of *Har Habayit* or even of the *azarah*, depending on the question of which space its entrance opens onto, a matter the *gemara* will address separately.

## Ba'al Niddah Mikvah

### Mikvah Timing Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה אֵינוֹ כְּנִדָּה, וְטוֹבֵל בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Learn from it** that one who is impure because **he had relations with a menstruating woman is unlike a menstruating woman** in terms of the time of immersion. Unlike the woman, who immerses after nightfall following the seventh day after her menstruation ceased, such a man may **immerse** on the seventh **day** and need not wait for nightfall. Therefore, if a High Priest has relations with his wife just before he is sequestered and there is uncertainty as to whether she has the halakhic status of a menstruating woman, the seventh day of his impurity occurs on Yom Kippur eve. He immerses himself that day and completes the purification process at nightfall. This allows him to enter the Temple to perform the Yom Kippur service.

The *gemara* now presents an inference by Rav Zeira: *shema mina ba ve-nidal eina ke-nida ve-tovel be-yom* — from this discussion one can infer that the *tumah* of a *ba'al niddah* — a man who has had relations with a *niddah* — is not identical to the *tumah* of the *niddah* herself, and specifically that he may immerse in the *mikvah* during the daytime of the seventh day, not waiting for the following night.

The reasoning requires unpacking. A *niddah* counts seven days from the beginning of her menstrual flow and goes to the *mikvah* at nightfall — the conclusion of the seventh day, not during the day. This is because the Torah states *shivat yamim tihyeh ve-nidatah*, and the full seven days must elapse. Many *meforshim* explain that since a menstrual period can last up to seven days, there is a Torah-level concern that bleeding might continue through the seventh day itself, and therefore immersion must wait until nightfall. The verse *ve-tehi tumatah alav* — "her *tumah* shall be upon him" — indicates that the husband acquires her *tumah*, but the question is whether he acquires it in precisely the same form she has it, including the requirement to wait until nightfall on the seventh day.

### Timeline Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה כְּנִדָּה, אֵימַת טָבֵיל — בְּלֵילְיָא. לִמְחַר הֵיכִי עָבֵיד עֲבוֹדָה? וְהָא בָּעֵי הֶעֱרֵב הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה אֵינוֹ כְּנִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **As, if you say that** one who is impure because **he had relations with a menstruating woman is like a menstruating woman** in terms of the time of immersion, **when does he immerse?** He may immerse only **at night** after seven complete days. Since that night is Yom Kippur, **how can he perform** the Yom Kippur **service the next day? Isn't he required** to wait for **sunset** following his immersion to complete the purification process? Until then his status is that of one who immersed that day, who may not serve in the Temple until the nightfall following his immersion. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that one who is impure because **he had relations with a menstruating woman is unlike a menstruating woman** in terms of the time of immersion? He immerses on the seventh day, Yom Kippur eve, and at nightfall he may serve in the Temple.

Rav Zeira argues that the *tumah* concern driving the separation reveals the answer. Consider the scenario: the *kohen gadol* comes to the *Beit HaMikdash* to begin his seven-day separation. The concern is that he might have had relations with his wife immediately before arriving — perhaps at eleven in the morning, before coming at noon. If that act rendered him a *ba'al niddah*, when does his seventh day occur? If he arrived at noon on the first day, the seventh day would be *Yom Kippur* itself. If he were like a *niddah* and could only immerse at nightfall of the seventh day, he would go to the *mikvah* the night of *Yom Kippur* — and then, since *tvilah* requires *haarev shemesh* (the setting of the sun following immersion) for full *taharah*, he would not be completely *tahor* until *motzaei Yom Kippur*, after the service is long over.

### Day Seven Mikvah

The point, as Michael correctly observed in the course of the discussion, is that he did not come at noon on day one — we must worry about the worst-case scenario, namely that he had relations a moment before his arrival. So day seven lands precisely on *Yom Kippur*. If he could go to the *mikvah* during the day of the seventh — as is the standard rule for ordinary seven-day *tumot* — then by nightfall of *Yom Kippur*, which is simultaneously the *haarev shemesh* following his daytime immersion, he would be completely *tahor* and eligible to perform the *avodah*. The fact that the *gemara* frames the separation as a solution to the *tumah* concern therefore proves that the *ba'al niddah* goes to the *mikvah* during the daytime of the seventh day. *Ella lav shema mina boel niddah eino ke-niddah* — this demonstrates that he is not like the *niddah* in this respect.

The conceptual logic is elegant. The reason a *niddah* waits until nightfall is connected to the possibility that menstruation may continue throughout the seventh day. The *ba'al niddah* has the *tumah* without the menstruation — and as a *tumah* of seven days, it follows the normal pattern of seven-day *tumot*, which permit daytime immersion on the seventh day. A woman who is a *zavah* (with non-menstrual flow) goes to the *mikvah* on the day of the seventh; one who becomes *tamei* through contact with a *meit* completes the sprinkling of ashes on the seventh day. The *ba'al niddah*, as a seven-day *tumah* without the specific menstrual dimension, belongs to that category and goes to the *mikvah* during the day.

## Textual Support

### Niddah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי טְבִילוֹת טְבִילָתָן בַּיּוֹם, נִדָּה וְיוֹלֶדֶת טְבִילָתָן בַּלַּיְלָה. נִדָּה אִין, בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion that one who is impure because he had relations with a menstruating woman immerses at night: With regard to **all** those **obligated in immersions, their immersion is during the day.** The exceptions are **a menstruating woman and a woman after childbirth, whose immersion is at night.** It can be learned by inference: With regard to **a menstruating woman, yes,** she immerses at night; with regard to one who is impure because **he had relations with a menstruating woman, no,** he does not immerse at night.

The *gemara* now tests Rav Zeira's position against textual sources. *Meitvei* — an objection is raised. A *mishna* states: *kol chayavei tvilos tvilosan ba-yom* — all those obligated in *tvilah* immerse during the daytime. The only exceptions are a *niddah* and a *yoledet* (a woman who has given birth), whose *tvilah* is at night: *tvilosan ba-laylah*. The *mishna* lists only *niddah* and *yoledet* as exceptions. Since *ba'al niddah* is not listed among the exceptions, it would appear he follows the standard rule and goes during the day — consistent with Rav Zeira, and a challenge to anyone who would dispute him.

### Category Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִדָּה וְכׇל דְּאָתֵי מֵרִבּוּיָיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof because the term **menstruating woman** in that *baraita* includes the woman **and everyone whose inclusion** in the impurity **is derived** from her status. The understanding is that a man who has relations with a menstruating woman assumes her impurity, and therefore his immersion would be identical to hers.

The *gemara* deflects: *niddah ve-chol de-asi mi-ribui* — "*niddah*" in the *mishna* refers to a *niddah* and everyone who falls within the chapter of *niddah tumah*. Since the *ba'al niddah*'s *tumah* derives from the *niddah*, he is subsumed within the term *niddah* as used there. Therefore no inference can be drawn from his absence in the list of exceptions — he may well be included among those who wait for nightfall.

### Dead Body Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בַּעַל קֶרִי כְּמַגַּע שֶׁרֶץ, בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה כִּטְמֵא מֵת. מַאי לָאו לִטְבִילָה? לֹא, לְטוּמְאָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises** another **objection:** The halakhic status of **one** who is ritually impure due to **a seminal emission** is **like** that of one who came into **contact with a creeping animal,** whereas the legal status of **one** who is ritually impure because he **had relations with a menstruating woman is like** that of one who became **impure** with ritual impurity imparted by **a corpse.** The Gemara asks: **What,** does the *baraita* **not** mean that one who had relations with a menstruating woman is like one impure with impurity imparted by a corpse in terms of **immersion,** which he may perform during the day? The Gemara rejects that conclusion. **No,** the *baraita* is merely comparing the duration of **their impurity.** One who experiences a seminal emission is impure for one day, like one who came into contact with a creeping animal; one who has relations with a menstruating woman is impure for seven days, like one impure with impurity imparted by a corpse.

A second challenge is mounted. A *mishna* in *Zavim* states: *ba'al keri ke-maga sheretz* — one who has had a seminal emission is like one who touched a *sheretz* (a dead creeping creature). The *mishna* thereby groups these *tumot* together, and separately groups the *ba'al niddah* with *tamei meit* — one who became *tamei* through contact with a corpse — as both being seven-day *tumot* of the status of *av ha-tumah*. Contact with a *meit* is in fact the most severe form of *tumah* — an *avi avot ha-tumah* — yielding the status of *av* to the one who touches it. A *ba'al niddah* similarly acquires an *av ha-tumah* status, for seven days. The *mishna* appears to equate them, and since *tamei meit* goes to the *mikvah* during the day of the seventh (via the sprinkling of ashes), the same should apply to *ba'al niddah* — supporting Rav Zeira.

### Duration Not Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם לְטוּמְאָתָן, סֵיפָא אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא שֶׁחָמוּר מִמֶּנּוּ בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה, שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב בְּטוּמְאָה קַלָּה, לְטַמֵּא אוֹכָלִין וּמַשְׁקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that conclusion. **No; actually,** the *baraita* is referring **to** the duration of **their impurity.** Although there is nothing novel in that equation, it **was necessary for** the *tanna* to teach **the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **However,** in one sense, the case of **one** who is impure because he **had relations with a menstruating woman** is **more severe than** one impure with impurity imparted by a corpse. One who is impure because he had relations with a menstruating woman **transmits impurity through** lying on **a bed or** sitting on **a chair,** even if he never came into direct contact with the chair or the bed. He renders the bed or chair impure **with a mild** form of **impurity.** He confers upon them first-degree ritual impurity status, sufficient only to **render foods and liquids impure.** One impure with impurity imparted by a corpse transmits impurity by means of direct contact.

The *gemara* responds: *lo letumatan* — the *mishna* is not making this comparison for the purpose of *tvilah* timing. It is comparing them only with respect to the duration of *tumah* — both are seven-day *tumot* rather than one-day *tumot*. Since the Torah states explicitly that both are seven-day *tumot*, the *mishna* does not need to teach that; it therefore must be teaching something else.

### Mishkav/Moshav

That something else is found in the *seifa* of the *mishna*: *ella shechamur mimenhu boel niddah* — in one respect, the *ba'al niddah* is more stringent than the person who contacted a corpse. That stringency is *shemetamei mishkav umoshav betumah kallah letamei ochlin umashkin* — he renders the seat or bed upon which he rests *tamei*, with a *tumah kallah* that goes on to make food and liquids *tamei*.

This requires some elaboration. Among the special categories of *tumah* enumerated in *Tazria-Metzora*, a *zav*, *zavah*, *niddah*, and related persons generate a particular type of *tumah* for anything they sit or lie upon — even without direct contact, merely by having their weight supported. A seat or bed that bears the weight of a *zav* or *zavah* acquires the status of *mishkav* or *moshav* and becomes an *av ha-tumah* in its own right, not merely a *rishon*. This is the distinctive *tumah* of objects that bear the weight of those whose *tumah* derives from a bodily condition. A person who contacts a corpse, despite the severity of that *tumah* in other respects, does not generate this *mishkav/moshav* effect.

The *ba'al niddah* occupies a middle position. He acquires enough of the *niddah*'s *tumah* to generate *mishkav* and *moshav* — he too renders what he sits upon *tamei* even without contact. But his *mishkav* and *moshav* are not an *av ha-tumah*; they are only a *rishon le-tumah*. The *rishon* level means he can render food and liquids *tamei* but cannot render persons or vessels *tamei* — that requires the level of *av*. The *mishna* is teaching this unique intermediate status of the *ba'al niddah*, and it is for this purpose — not for *tvilah* timing — that it groups him with *tamei meit*. No inference about daytime immersion can therefore be drawn from the *mishna*.

## R. Chiyya Baraita

### Seven-Day List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הַזָּב וְהַזָּבָה וְהַמְּצוֹרָע וְהַמְצוֹרַעַת וּבוֹעֵל נִדָּה וּטְמֵא מֵת טְבִילָתָן בַּיּוֹם, נִדָּה וְיוֹלֶדֶת טְבִילָתָן בַּלַּיְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** an additional proof **that Rabbi Ḥiyya taught:** For **the *zav* and the *zava* and the leper and the female leper, and one who had relations with a menstruating woman, and one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse, their immersion is during the day.** For **a menstruating woman and a woman after childbirth, their immersion is at night.**

The *gemara* now brings a decisive source. *Tanya devei Chiyya* — a *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Chiyya is taught. It lists the various seven-day *tumot*: *ha-zav ve-ha-zavah ve-ha-metzora ve-ha-metzora'at* — a man or woman with these flows, or a man or woman with *tzara'at*.

### Ba'al Niddah Listed

The *baraita* continues: *u-voel niddah u-tmei meit* — and a *ba'al niddah*, and one who became *tamei* through contact with a corpse. All of these are seven-day *tumot*, and *tevilatan ba-yom* — they all immerse during the daytime. The *baraita* then states the lone exception: *niddah ve-yoledet tevilatan ba-laylah*. The *ba'al niddah* is listed explicitly and unambiguously among those who immerse during the day.

### Rav Zeira Victory

*Dayka* — this is a direct and explicit contradiction to the challengers, and it vindicates Rav Zeira completely. His position is definitively established: *boel niddah eino ke-niddah* in this respect — the *ba'al niddah* goes to the *mikvah* during the day of the seventh, not at nightfall. Consequently, the entire structure of the separation works: the *kohen gadol* arrives on day one, even if the very worst-case scenario obtained (relations immediately before arrival), day seven is *Yom Kippur*, he immerses during the day, nightfall brings *haarev shemesh*, and he is completely *tahor* for the *avodah*.

## Death Tumah Q

### Logical Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד שֶׁאַתָּה מַפְרִישׁוֹ מִטּוּמְאַת בֵּיתוֹ, הַפְרִישֵׁהוּ מִטּוּמְאַת הַמֵּת!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the sequestering of the High Priest, the Gemara asks: **And before you remove him from the** potential of **impurity of his house, remove him from the** potential of the more severe **impurity** imparted by **a corpse.**

The *gemara* now turns to what initially seems like a logical parallel concern. If the reason for separating the *kohen gadol* from his wife is to prevent the *tumah* of *boel niddah*, one might equally ask: why is there no comparable concern about *tumat meit*? Any human being in his proximity might die, and death-contact *tumah* is also a seven-day *tumah*. Once the *gemara* has established that *tumah* concern is the driving rationale, the question follows naturally: *tefrisho mitumat meit* — you should also separate him from any contact with other human beings, lest someone die near him during the seven days and render him *tamei meit*.

### Hutrah Baitzibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא (בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא) מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: טוּמְאַת הַמֵּת הוּתְּרָה הִיא בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥlifa, father of Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa, said in the name of Rava: That is to say** that **impurity** imparted by a **corpse is permitted in** cases involving **the public.** In cases where the public is involved, impurity imparted by a corpse does not prevent the Temple service from being conducted. Since the service of the High Priest on Yom Kippur is a service involving communal offerings, impurity imparted by a corpse does not invalidate the service.

Rav Tachlifa, the father of Rav Huna, in the name of Rava, responds: *zot omeret* — this teaches us something. The fact that no one suggests such a separation indicates that *tumat meit hutrah hi be-tzibbur* — *tumah* from contact with a corpse is not merely overridden for the sake of a *korban tzibbur*, it is fully *hutrah*, completely permissible, as if there were no prohibition at all. The principle learned in *Pesachim* is that if the *kohanim* are *teme'im* and there is a *korban tzibbur* — especially one with a fixed time that must be brought today — the *korban* is brought even in a state of *tumah*. The question, however, is whether this represents *hutrah* — no violation whatsoever — or *dchuya* — a prohibition that is overridden under duress but remains a violation. Rav Tachlifa argues that since no effort is made to isolate the *kohen gadol* from potential *tumat meit*, it must be that *tumat meit* is *hutrah* entirely, and therefore there is nothing to avoid.

### Status vs Contact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא טוּמְאַת הַמֵּת דְּחוּיָה הִיא בְּצִבּוּר. טוּמְאַת הַמֵּת לָא שְׁכִיחָא, טוּמְאַת בֵּיתוֹ שְׁכִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Even** if **you say that impurity** imparted by **a corpse is** merely **overridden in public** and not completely permitted, the idea that the High Priest is not removed from the potential of impurity imparted by a corpse can be understood. **Impurity** imparted by **a corpse is uncommon.** The likelihood that one visiting the High Priest will suddenly die is minimal. In contrast, **impurity of his house is common,** as uncertainty with regard to his wife's status as a menstruating woman could arise at any moment.

Why then must he be separated from his wife? Because the category of *tumah* that is *hutrah be-tzibbur* is specifically contact-based *tumah* — *tumat meit*, touching a *sheretz*, and similar externally contracted *tumot*. A *tumah* that derives from a personal bodily status, such as *niddah*, *zav*, *zavah*, or *boel niddah*, is not *hutrah be-tzibbur*. The *kohen gadol*'s becoming a *boel niddah* is not the kind of *tumah* that is overridden for a *korban tzibbur*, and therefore it remains a genuine obstacle to his service on *Yom Kippur* — which is precisely why the separation from his wife is necessary.

## Ravina Refinement

### Dchuya Status

Ravina offers an alternative framework. Even granting that *tumat meit* is only *dchuya be-tzibbur* — overridden rather than fully permissible — one still would not require the *kohen gadol* to be isolated from all other human beings. Ravina's position is that even under *dchuya*, one would work to avoid *tumah* where possible, but the concern is tempered by a different consideration.

### Frequency Argument

*Tumat meit lo shechicha* — contact with a corpse simply does not happen with sufficient frequency to warrant preemptive isolation. Someone dying in the presence of the *kohen gadol* during a seven-day period is a statistically remote possibility. By contrast, the possibility that his wife will find herself in a state of *niddah* — even unexpectedly, even outside her anticipated *veset* — is considerably more common. The *gemara* does not dismiss *tumah* concerns entirely; it calibrates the level of preventive action to the realistic probability of the *tumah* occurring. Contact *tumah* from a corpse is rare enough that no special measures are required, even under *dchuya*. *Niddah* status is common enough that it does warrant the precaution of separation.

It is worth noting in passing that the *gemara* will need to revisit this framework when it addresses the practice of sprinkling the ashes of the *parah adumah* on the *kohen gadol* throughout the seven days — a practice that is itself entirely concerned with *tumat meit*. That discussion will require further examination of the interplay between *hutrah* and *dchuya*.

## Hutrah vs Dchuya

### All Tamei Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, טוּמְאַת הַמֵּת, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: הוּתְּרָה הִיא בְּצִיבּוּר, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: דְּחוּיָה הִיא בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** There is an amoraic dispute with regard to the effect of impurity imparted by a corpse on the conduct of the Temple service. **It was stated** with regard to **impurity** imparted by **a corpse** that **Rav Naḥman said: It is permitted in** cases involving **the public;** e.g., when a majority of the Jewish people is impure, the service of a ritually pure priest is not preferable to that of an impure priest. The Temple service proceeds as though there was no impurity at all. **And Rav Sheshet said:** Impurity imparted by a corpse is merely **overridden in** cases involving **the public,** and service performed by a ritually pure priest is preferable.

The *gemara* now examines the practical difference between *hutrah* and *dchuya* in a concrete scenario. Setting aside the specific context of *Yom Kippur*, consider a Monday when the *beit av* — the subgroup of *kohanim* assigned to serve that day — are all *teme'im*. A *korban tzibbur*, specifically the *tamid*, must be brought. The *kohanim* of the *mishmar* who are not assigned to Monday are elsewhere in Jerusalem, having come for the week's service. It would take minimal effort to summon a few *tehorim* from among those available to perform the *avodah* instead.

### Nafka Mina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא טְמֵאִין וּטְהוֹרִין בְּהָהוּא בֵּית אָב — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּטְהוֹרִין עָבְדִי, טְמֵאִין לָא עָבְדִי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי וּלְאֵתוֹיֵי טְהוֹרִין מִבֵּית אָב אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara restricts the scope of the dispute. In a case **where there are** both **ritually impure and pure** priests in **that patrilineal family** tasked with serving in the Temple on that day, **everyone,** even Rav Naḥman, **agrees that the pure** priests **serve** and **the impure** priests **do not serve. When they disagree,** it is with regard to a case where the entire patrilineal family is impure. Is it necessary **to seek out and bring pure** priests **from a different patrilineal family** belonging to the same priestly watch, who are tasked with serving in the Temple on a different day that week?

The *nafka mina* between *hutrah* and *dchuya* is precisely this: under *dchuya*, since *tumah* is still a problem that we merely override when we have no alternative, we would make the effort to find *tahor kohanim* if they are accessible. Under *hutrah*, since there is no prohibition at all in bringing a *korban tzibbur* with *temei'im*, there is no reason to make even the minimal effort to find alternatives.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: הֶיתֵּר הִיא בְּצִיבּוּר, וְלָא מַהְדְּרִינַן. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: דְּחוּיָה הִיא בְּצִיבּוּר, וּמַהְדְּרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** The prohibition against performing the Temple service in a state of impurity imparted by a corpse **is permitted** in cases involving **the public, and we do not** need to **seek out** other priests. Since the Torah permitted the performance of the Temple service by priests impure with impurity imparted by a corpse, it is completely permitted and it is as though the service is performed in purity. **Rav Sheshet said:** The prohibition of impurity imparted by a corpse **is overridden** in cases involving **the public,** and wherever possible **we seek out** ritually pure priests.

Rav Nachman holds *hutrah hi be-tzibbur* — it is completely permissible. Accordingly, *lo mahadrinan* — we do not go to the effort of summoning *tehorim* from another *beit av*. The *teme'im* may serve; there is nothing to remedy.

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet holds *dchuya hi be-tzibbur* — it is overridden rather than permitted. Accordingly, *u-mahadrinan* — we would make the effort to find *tahor kohanim* who could perform the *avodah*, since the *tumah* remains problematic even if ultimately permitted under duress.

## Extreme Hutrah

### Tame'im With Tahorim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא טְהוֹרִין וּטְמֵאִין בְּהָהוּא בֵּית אָב פְּלִיג רַב נַחְמָן, וְאָמַר: עָבְדִי נָמֵי טְמֵאִין, דְּכֹל טוּמְאַת מֵת בְּצִיבּוּר רַחֲמָנָא שַׁרְיַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that the dispute is slightly different: **Even** in a case **where there are** both **ritually pure and ritually impure** priests in **that patrilineal family, Rav Naḥman disagreed** with Rav Sheshet **and said** that the priests serve **even** when they are **impure,** **as** in **all** situations **of impurity** imparted **by corpses** in cases involving **the public, the Merciful One permits** those who are impure to perform the Temple service.

An alternative and more extreme version of the debate is reported. Some say the dispute between Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet applies even in a case where the *beit av* of that day contains both *teme'im* and *tehorim* — that is, where there are *tahor kohanim* readily available within the very same group assigned to serve. Even in that case, Rav Nachman would say the *teme'im* may serve alongside or instead of the *tehorim*.

### Contact Tumah Hutrah

The basis for this extreme position: *de-chol tumat meit be-tzibbur rachmana sharya* — any *tumat meit* in the context of a *korban tzibbur* is simply permitted by the Torah, without qualification. The prohibitions in the Torah against a *tamei kohen* performing the *avodah* were never stated with respect to a *korban tzibbur*. There is no violation — and therefore no preference for the *tahor kohen* over the *tamei* one when it comes to bringing the *korban tzibbur*. This is an admittedly radical position, but it flows directly from an uncompromising application of *hutrah*.

## Korban Categories

### Pesach Status

Tosafot introduces an important distinction that clarifies the scope of the entire debate. The *korban pesach* presents a borderline case. On one hand, it overrides *Shabbat* and *tumah* and has a fixed time, features associated with *korban tzibbur*. On the other hand, it is not truly a *korban tzibbur* in the full sense: it is not brought by *Klal Yisrael* as a corporate entity through a single offering, but rather each household brings its own. It comes *be-khnufya* — en masse, as a large group — but it is not brought in *shutfus*, in partnership, as one collective *korban*. Tosafot notes: *chashiv ke-korban tzibbur* in some respects, but *keivan de-lav kulhu meisi chad pesach be-shutfus*, since they do not all bring a single shared *korban*, it is not a fully constituted *korban tzibbur*.

### True Tzibbur Only

Tosafot's framework is consequently as follows. The extreme position of *hutrah* — the position that even with *tehorim* available, *teme'im* may serve without any preference for the *tahor* — applies only to a genuine *korban tzibbur*, a single offering brought on behalf of all of *Klal Yisrael* as a collective. That is the context in which the Torah's prohibition against *tamei* service was never stated in the first place.

### Yachid Consensus

For a *korban yachid* — an individual offering — even one with a fixed time, everyone agrees that we operate under *dchuya* at most. Accordingly, everyone agrees that we would seek *tehorim* to bring it if they are available. The *korban pesach*, being a *korban yachid* brought *be-khnufya*, falls into this category: even according to the view that holds *hutrah* for a true *korban tzibbur*, everyone would agree to seek *tahorim* for the *korban pesach*.

### Kohen Gadol Korban

The same logic applies to the *kohen gadol*'s personal *korban* on *Yom Kippur*. Even though it has a fixed time and cannot be postponed — and even though in some respects it functions similarly to a *korban tzibbur* — it is at the end of the day a *korban yachid*: *keivan de-korban yachid hu, mahadrinan atahorin le-chulei alma* — since it is a personal offering, everyone agrees we would try to ensure the *kohen gadol* is *tahor*. That is the overarching reason the seven-day separation is taken seriously: the *kohen gadol*'s personal offerings on *Yom Kippur* demand *taharah*, because even those who hold *hutrah* for a true *korban tzibbur* would insist on *tehorim* for a *korban yachid*. With that, Tosafot's framework neatly ties together the entire discussion of *hutrah* and *dchuya* and its application to the *kohen gadol*'s service.
