# Hullin 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father's Role Intro

### Father-Child Inclusion

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion concerning the prohibition of *oso v'es beno*—the biblical prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day. The question at hand is whether this prohibition applies exclusively to the mother and child, or whether it extends to the father and child as well. The verse itself is written in the masculine, which might initially suggest that the father is included. Yet the Gemara's natural inclination is to read the prohibition as applying to the mother, because in the animal world the family unit is understood primarily as the bond between mother and offspring. The biological connection between mother and child is self-evident, whereas the father's social role in the animal kingdom lacks the kind of structured familial presence it carries in human society.

### Mother Focus Reasoning

This asymmetry drives the Gemara's assumption: the family unit relevant to *oso v'es beno* is the mother-child relationship, and it is an open question whether the father-child relationship rises to a comparable level of halakhic significance. Despite the masculine formulation of the verse, the Gemara pushes back against reading it as an inclusion of the father, precisely because the entire orientation of animal family life centers on the mother.

### Chosheim L'Zera Ha-Av

This question is framed in the Gemara's language as the issue of *chosheim l'zera ha-av*—whether we concern ourselves with the seed of the father. It is important not to misread *chosheim* here as a question of paternal identification, as if we were asking merely whether we know who the father is. It is taken for granted that we know. The question is rather whether it matters. In the animal world, the father's role can be understood as that of a seed-donor—he contributes the *zera* and departs, without ongoing connection to the child. The question, then, is whether that contribution is halakhically significant, and specifically whether the father and child are treated as a meaningful family unit under the prohibition of *oso v'es beno*.

---

## Chananya & Shmuel

### Halacha K'Chananya

The Gemara now turns to a ruling. Three lines from the top of 79a, we read: *amar Rav Huna bar Chiya amar Shmuel, halacha k'Chananya*—Rav Huna bar Chiyya said in the name of Shmuel: the halacha follows Chananya. Chananya is the authority who holds that the prohibition of *oso v'es beno* applies to the father and child as well, not only to the mother and child. The father, according to this ruling, does possess halakhic significance.

### Shmuel's Reasoning

The Gemara then adds: *ve-azal Shmuel li-tameih*—Shmuel follows his own consistent line of reasoning. This ruling is not isolated; it connects to Shmuel's broader view on *chosheim l'zera ha-av*, the significance of the father's contribution. This observation is what propels the Gemara into what will occupy the remainder of the daf—a wide-ranging discussion of *chosheim l'zera ha-av* extending well beyond the specific prohibition of *oso v'es beno*.

---

## Kilayim & Mules

### Mishnah: Mules

The Gemara now cites a *Mishnah* bearing on this general question of *chosheim l'zera ha-av*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, ha-nolad min ha-sus, af al pi she-aviv chamor, mutarim zeh ba-zeh*—Rabbi Yehuda says that mules born of a horse-mother, even though their father is a donkey, may freely interbreed with one another. The subject is mules: a horse and a donkey can be crossbred in either direction, with either species serving as the mother. The resulting offspring—whether the mother was a horse or a donkey—is what we call a mule, though as we will see, the species identity of that offspring turns out to be precisely the question.

The issue in the laws of *kilayim* is whether crossbreeding two mules with one another constitutes a violation. One might think that all mules, as mules, are their own species, and that mating mule with mule is therefore entirely permitted. But Rabbi Yehuda's position challenges that assumption. Under the principle of *ein chosheim l'zera ha-av*—ignoring the father—the mule's identity is entirely that of its mother. A mule born of a horse-mother is, for halakhic purposes, a horse; a mule born of a donkey-mother is, for halakhic purposes, a donkey. It would then follow that mating a horse-mule with a donkey-mule is a violation of *kilayim*, since one is effectively a horse and the other a donkey. Moreover, having two such mules draw a carriage together would violate the prohibition against *lo tarbia*—harnessing two animals of different species together. Even if mules are largely infertile, the attempt to mate them, or the act of hitching them together under a yoke, constitutes the biblical violation regardless of whether offspring result.

The *Mishnah* continues: *aval ha-nolad min ha-chamor im ha-nolad min ha-sus asurin*—but mules whose mother is a donkey may not interbreed with mules whose mother is a horse, because one is effectively a horse and the other effectively a donkey. This is the position of the *Tanna Kamma*: the father is ignored, and the mule's species is entirely that of the mother.

### R. Yehuda: No Father

Rav Yehuda then reported in the name of Shmuel: *zu divrei Rabbi Yehuda, de-amar ein chosheim l'zera ha-av*—this is the individual opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that we do not concern ourselves with the father's seed, and therefore the mule is classified entirely according to its mother's species.

### Chachamim: Father Matters

The Gemara then introduces the opposing view: *aval Chachamim omrim, kol minei peradot achat hen*—but the Sages say that all varieties of mules are considered a single species. According to the *Chachamim*, the father's contribution is weighed equally alongside the mother's, and therefore all mules—regardless of whether the horse was the mother or the father—are hybrid creatures of an identical type. Since all mules are equal hybrids of horse and donkey, they constitute one halakhic species, and any two mules may freely interbreed. The Gemara then identifies these *Chachamim* as aligning with the position of Chananya: *Chananya hu, de-amar chosheim l'zera ha-av*. Chananya is the one who holds that the father's contribution is significant, and the *Chachamim* here mirror that view.

### Two Different Questions

At this point it is essential to note a distinction that Tosafot and other *Rishonim* flag carefully: the various *sugyot* across *Shas* do not always reach the same conclusion on *chosheim l'zera ha-av*, and part of the reason is that there are actually two distinct questions being asked under that heading.

The first question, which was the subject of yesterday's discussion, is a question about the family unit: does the father-child relationship in the animal kingdom rise to the level of a family bond that the Torah recognizes in the prohibition of *oso v'es beno*? This is essentially a social-structural question about what constitutes a family.

The second question, which today's Gemara raises in the context of *kilayim* and mules, is a question about genetic identity: does the offspring inherit a halakhic species-identity from the father, even when the father plays no ongoing familial role? These are meaningfully different questions. It is entirely coherent to say that the father is not part of the family unit—and therefore not included in *oso v'es beno*—while still acknowledging that the offspring is a hybrid of both parents in terms of its species identity. Conversely, it is a significant further claim to say that the father's genetic contribution is so irrelevant that this mule is, for all halakhic purposes, simply a horse. The Gemara itself notes how striking this second position is. It is one thing to exclude the father from a family-based prohibition; it is quite another to declare that his biological contribution to the offspring counts for nothing at all, such that a mule born of a horse-mother is to be treated as a pure horse. These *sugyot* are thus not always asking the same question, and the rulings that emerge from each need not be identical.

## Testing R. Yehuda

### Certain or Uncertain?

The Gemara now pursues a further question regarding Rabbi Yehuda's position: *mi-pshat peshita lei le-Rabbi Yehuda de-ein chosheim l'zera ha-av, o dilma sipuki mesafeka lei*—is it certain in Rabbi Yehuda's view that we do not concern ourselves with the father's seed, or is he perhaps merely uncertain about the matter?

### Mule with Horse

At first glance this seems to make no practical difference. Whether Rabbi Yehuda definitively discounts the father, or whether he is simply unsure, the result is the same: a mule born of a horse-mother cannot interbreed with a mule born of a donkey-mother, either because the father is definitely irrelevant and species is determined entirely by the mother, or because the father's relevance is uncertain and therefore we must be stringent in both directions. Either way, the two types of mules cannot interbreed.

The question, however, matters enormously for a separate case: *le-mishrei pri im ha-eim*—may the offspring interbreed with a pure member of the mother's own species? If this mule's mother was a horse, may it interbreed with a horse? According to Rabbi Yehuda, is this mule simply a horse, such that mating it with a horse raises no concern? Or does Rabbi Yehuda hold, perhaps, that the father's relevance is uncertain—not that the father is definitely irrelevant, but that we cannot rule it out—and therefore this mule may be partly donkey, which would make mating it with a horse a possible violation of *kilayim*?

If it is certain in Rabbi Yehuda's view that the father is irrelevant, then the mule is a horse, and interbreeding it with a horse is not merely permitted but expected. If, however, Rabbi Yehuda is uncertain, then he must be strict in both directions: he cannot breed it with a horse, because maybe the father is relevant and this creature is part donkey; but he also cannot breed it with a donkey-mule, because maybe the father is irrelevant and both of these are effectively horses. And he cannot freely breed it with any horse-mule either, unless that mule has precisely the same parentage—because if the father is relevant, then two horse-mules with different fathers might be different creatures, and if the father is irrelevant, then they are both horses and may freely interbreed. In short, Rabbi Yehuda's uncertainty forces a complete halt: strict in every direction, safe nowhere.

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question from a *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* says: *kol ha-nolad min ha-sus, af al pi she-avihem chamor, mutarim zeh ba-zeh*—all mules born of horse-mothers, even though their fathers are donkeys, may interbreed with one another. The Gemara first asks whether this tells us anything new. Is the *Mishnah* saying you may mate a horse-mule with a horse-mule—but that seems obvious, since both share the same type of mother? The Gemara therefore considers reading the *Mishnah* as going further: *d'avuh d'hai sus v'avuh d'hai chamor*—the case is where one has a horse-father and a horse-mother, while the other has a donkey-father and a horse-mother. The *Mishnah* is telling us that since both share a horse-mother, they may interbreed. This would imply that Rabbi Yehuda definitively discounts the father: the mule of a horse-mother is simply a horse, regardless of the father, and may therefore interbreed not only with other horse-mules but potentially even with horses.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading: *le-olam d'avuh d'hai chamor v'avuh d'hai chamor*—the *Mishnah* is only speaking of the case where both have donkey-fathers, and is simply mating a horse-mule with another horse-mule. And if this seems trivially obvious, the Gemara offers a reason it was necessary to state: one might have thought that since a mule is not a naturally occurring species and cannot propagate on its own, there is no halakhic category of "mule" as such, and every mule should therefore be viewed as merely an amalgam of horse and donkey. Under that view, mating one horse-donkey amalgam with another horse-donkey amalgam might itself be *kilayim*, because on some dimension each animal contains characteristics of both species, and the two animals might be interbreeding their respective "horse-parts" with their respective "donkey-parts." Therefore, the *Mishnah* tells us: no, once you identify both as belonging to the same mother-type, you may treat them as the same species and interbreed them freely. But this reading does not require Rabbi Yehuda to have definitively discounted the father; it only establishes that mules of the same mother-type may interbreed.

A *baraita* then appears to press the matter further: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, pirdah she-tav'ah, ein marvi'in aleha lo sus ve-lo chamor ela minah*—a female mule in heat may be mated only with its own kind, not with a horse and not with a donkey. This is a striking statement. If Rabbi Yehuda definitively discounts the father and therefore considers a horse-mule to be, for all purposes, a pure horse, why can it not mate with a horse? The explicit prohibition in the *baraita* against mating it with a horse seems to imply that Rabbi Yehuda does not completely discount the father. He must be at least uncertain about the father's relevance, and therefore he prohibits mating the horse-mule with a horse.

The Gemara, however, offers a reinterpretation: *de-lo yad'inan mina de-ima mai nihu*—the case is one where we do not know the species of the mother. When you encounter a mule in the field and do not know whether its mother was a horse or a donkey, you naturally cannot mate it with a horse (because maybe the mother was a donkey, and this is really a donkey-mule). But neither can you mate it with another mule of unknown parentage (because maybe the two mules have different mothers). And neither can you mate it with a donkey. In effect, when the parentage is unknown, no mating is permitted. What Rabbi Yehuda's *baraita* is saying is simply: do not mate this mule with any animal until you determine who the mother is.

The Gemara then raises an immediate objection: but the *baraita* says you may mate it *ela minah*—only with its own kind, implying there is some mating that is permitted. If you do not know the parentage at all, how can there be any permitted option? So the Gemara reads *ela minah* not as permitting something but as part of the same prohibition: you may not mate it with a horse, not with a donkey, and indeed not with any other mule of uncertain parentage—meaning, none of the above until the mother's species is established.

### Female Mule In Heat

This re-reading points toward a practical question: how do you determine the mother's species? The Gemara responds that physical characteristics are available to examine, and it is to this that the discussion of *simanim* now turns.

### Unknown Parentage

Before reaching those characteristics, the Gemara acknowledges that the simple reading of the *baraita* would indicate that Rabbi Yehuda does not completely discount the father—otherwise there would be no reason to prohibit mating a horse-mule with a horse. The Gemara has worked hard to re-read the *baraita* as a case of unknown parentage, precisely to preserve the possibility that Rabbi Yehuda does definitively discount the father. The question remains formally unresolved: *mai havi alah*—what was the final conclusion of this question? No definitive resolution emerges.

## Physical Traits

### Abaye: Voice

The Gemara now addresses how one determines a mule's maternal lineage from observable characteristics. Abaye teaches: *avi kalei bar chamra, snif kalei bar susaya*—a deep-voiced mule is the offspring of a donkey-mother, and a whinnying, higher-pitched voice indicates the mother was a horse. The voice, according to Abaye, follows the mother.

### Rav Pappa: Ears & Tail

Rav Pappa adds further *simanim*: *ravarvin udnei ve-zutra gnuvatei, bar chamra; zutrin udnei ve-raba gnuvatei, bar susaya*—large ears and a short tail indicate a donkey-mother, while small ears and a long tail indicate a horse-mother. These are the characteristic features of a donkey and a horse respectively, and they manifest in the offspring in accordance with the mother's species.

### Mute & Mutilated

The Gemara notes a practical limitation on these characteristic-based determinations: *hacha be-mai askinan? be-ilemet ve-gidemet*—the case in the *baraita* of unknown parentage must be one involving a mule that is mute and has had its ears and tail removed. With the voice unavailable and the external markers destroyed, none of the characteristic indicators can be employed. This is admittedly an extreme edge case, but it is the scenario the Gemara invokes to defend its reading of the *baraita* as pertaining to unknown parentage. From here we also learn a broader principle: *simanim de-oraisa*—physical identifying characteristics may be relied upon even for biblical-level determinations. Rav Abba's instruction to his servant, to select mules of similar appearance before harnessing them together, demonstrates this principle in practice: one may use observable markers to establish species identity even where the halakhic consequences are of biblical weight.

---

## Consensus: Pri Im Ha-Em

### Rav Huna: All Agree

Although the Gemara has not resolved whether Rabbi Yehuda definitively discounts the father, Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua clarifies what all parties do agree upon: *hakol modim be-pri im ha-em she-asur*—everyone agrees that mating the offspring with a pure member of the mother's own species is forbidden. Even Rabbi Yehuda, who discounts the father and treats the horse-mule as essentially a horse, either does so uncertainly or does so only partially. Even if the father is largely irrelevant, he is not so completely irrelevant that this mule can simply be mated freely with a horse. There remains some element of the father's contribution—whether definite or uncertain—that prevents the horse-mule from being treated as a pure horse.

This actually yields a very coherent position. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the mother's species dominates: a horse-mule and a donkey-mule are different creatures, and a horse-mule is very different from a donkey-mule. But the father is not so wholly irrelevant that the horse-mule becomes a pure horse. Therefore the horse-mule may not mate with a horse (it is not entirely a horse), and it may not mate with a donkey-mule (it is a different species of mule). It may mate only with another horse-mule of similar parentage—a mule of the same mother-type—because in that pairing, whatever role the father plays or does not play, it plays the same role on both sides.

### Selecting Mules

Rav Abba's instruction to his servant encapsulates the practical consequence: *ayein le-hanech de-damyan le-hadadi ve-ayeil li*—select mules that look similar to one another and only then bring them together to draw the carriage. This reflects the view of *ein chosheim l'zera ha-av* in the sense that Rav Abba does not treat all mules as equivalent; he insists on matching mules of similar maternal lineage. He does not treat every mule as an identical hybrid regardless of parentage—which would be the position of the *Chachamim* who say *kol minei peradot achat hen*. Rather, he aligns with the view that the mother's identity is what primarily determines species, and therefore mules of similar apparent maternal origin may be harnessed together, while mules of different apparent lineages may not.

## Oso V'es Beno & Koy

### Baraita on Kilayim

A *baraita* is now cited: *oso v'es beno noheg be-kilayim u-ve-koy*—the prohibition of *oso v'es beno* applies both to *kilayim* (crossbred domestic animals) and to *koy*. The first case—*kilayim* of domestic animals—was already established yesterday. A crossbreed of a sheep and a goat, for instance, remains within the world of *behema*, and *oso v'es beno* applies to it. The introduction of the *koy*, however, is striking.

A *koy* is a hybrid between a *behema* (a domestic animal) and a *chaya* (a wild animal)—perhaps a goat and a deer, or some analogous combination. As the Torah limits *oso v'es beno* to *behemot* and excludes *chayas*, one might have thought that a *koy*, being at least partly *chaya*, would be exempt. The *baraita* asserts otherwise, and the Gemara will now explore under what conditions and from whose perspective this ruling holds.

### R. Eliezer: Domestic Only

Rabbi Eliezer dissents: *kilayim ha-ba min ha-ez u-min ha-rachel, oso v'es beno noheg bo*—a crossbreed of goat and sheep, which is entirely within the *behema* category, does fall under *oso v'es beno*. But as for the *koy*, which is a part-*chaya* creature, Rabbi Eliezer holds *eino oso v'es beno noheg bo*—the prohibition does not apply.

---

## Oso V'es Beno & Koy…

### Which Koy?

Rav Chisda then frames the essential question: *eizehu koy she-nechelku bo Rabbi Eliezer ve-Chachamim?*—which type of *koy* is at the center of their debate? He answers: *zeh ha-ba min ha-tayish u-min ha-tzviyah ha-yaldah*—a *koy* that is the offspring of a male goat and a female *tzviyah* (some form of wild animal, perhaps a deer or a wild variety of goat). The critical variable is the identity of the mother.

---

## First Gen Koy

### Mother Chaya Exempt

The Gemara first considers the possibility that the case involves a *tzviyah* as the mother and a male goat as the father, with the *koy* itself being שחטed together with its offspring. But this is immediately dismissed: *hakol modim be-tzvi u-vnah tayish she-patur*—everyone agrees that when the mother is a *chaya* and the child is a *behema*-type, there is no violation of *oso v'es beno*. Why? *Seh u-vno amar Rachmana ve-lo tzvi u-vno*—the Torah speaks of a *seh* or *shor* (a sheep or ox) with its offspring. The mother must be a *behema*. If the mother is a *chaya*, it does not matter what the offspring looks like; you are not violating *oso v'es beno* because the prohibition is anchored in the mother's identity as a *behema*. Since the mother here is a *chaya*, it is exempt regardless of the father's species or the child's appearance.

### Father's Role in Koy

The Gemara therefore redirects: *ella be-tzvi ha-ba al ha-teishah ve-yaldah*—the case must be where the *tzviyah* (the male wild animal) bred with a female goat (the *behema*), and the offspring—a *koy*—is now the mother in the *oso v'es beno* scenario, slaughtered together with its own child. Now we ask: what is the halakhic identity of this *koy*? Is it a *behema* for purposes of *oso v'es beno*?

The Gemara notes that Rav Chisda himself had already established: *hakol modim be-hitiya seh u-vnat tzvi she-chayav*—everyone agrees that if the mother is a goat (a *behema*) and the child is a *chaya*-type offspring, you are nonetheless *chayav* for *oso v'es beno*. Why? *Seh amar Rachmana u-vno kol dehu*—the Torah says a *seh* with its child, *whatever* that child may be. The Torah requires only that the mother be a *behema*; the child's species is irrelevant to the violation. The prohibition attaches to the mother's identity, not the offspring's.

Given this, the debate over the *koy* cannot concern the identity of the *koy*-as-child; the child's species is always irrelevant. Rather, the debate must concern the identity of the *koy* as the mother in the *oso v'es beno* prohibition—specifically, when this *koy* is slaughtered together with its own offspring.

### Chachamim: Father Counts

The *Rabbanan* hold *chosheim l'zera ha-av*: the father's contribution matters. The father of this *koy* was a *behema* (the male goat), and therefore the *koy* is part *behema*. Moreover, the *Rabbanan* further extend the principle: *seh afilu miktzat*—even being only partly a *behema* suffices for the prohibition of *oso v'es beno* to apply. This *koy* is a partial *behema*, and partial *behema*-status is enough. Therefore, according to the *Chachamim*, you transgress *oso v'es beno* by slaughtering this *koy* with its offspring.

### R. Eliezer: Father N/A

Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein chosheim l'zera ha-av*: the father's contribution is irrelevant. This *koy*'s mother was a *chaya*, and since we ignore the father entirely, this *koy* is one hundred percent *chaya*. There is no *behema* in it whatsoever. *Ve-seh afilu miktzat lo amrinan*—the argument that even partial *behema* suffices cannot help here, because this animal has zero *behema* in it. The prohibition of *oso v'es beno* therefore does not apply.

---

## Two Debates Distinguished

### Koy Debate Purpose

The Gemara now asks why the *Tannaim* needed to frame this debate specifically around the *koy*. If all they were disputing is whether *chosheim l'zera ha-av*, they could have simply debated the original case: is a father and child included in *oso v'es beno* alongside a mother and child? Why introduce the *koy* at all?

The Gemara answers: *ai peligi be-hee, hava amina, beha afilu Rabbanan modeh*—had the debate been framed only around the father-child question in *oso v'es beno*, one might have thought even the *Rabbanan* would concede in the *koy* case. The reasoning would be: yes, we care about the father; but caring about the father means we recognize his contribution to the animal's identity, not necessarily that a partial *behema*-status on one side is enough to trigger the prohibition. To know that the *Rabbanan* hold *seh afilu miktzat*—that even partial *behema* suffices—they had to frame the debate explicitly around the *koy*, where the *behema* element comes entirely and only from the father's contribution. The *koy* debate therefore carries an additional ruling beyond the baseline of *chosheim l'zera ha-av*: not only does the father's contribution count, but even a fractional *behema* identity, when derived from the father, is sufficient to trigger *oso v'es beno*.

---

## Koy on Yom Tov

### Don't Slaughter Koy

A *Mishnah* is now cited: *ein shochtin oto be-yom tov*—one may not slaughter a *koy* on *Yom Tov*. The reason is that the *koy*'s status is uncertain: it may be a *chaya*, in which case its blood must be covered after slaughter; or it may be a *behema*, in which case covering the blood is not required. On *Yom Tov*, covering blood—a *melachah*—is only permitted if obligatory. If there is doubt whether it is obligatory, one may not perform it, lest one transgress *Yom Tov* with an unnecessary *melachah*. And since the *koy* generates precisely this uncertainty, the safest course is to avoid the entire situation by not slaughtering it on *Yom Tov* at all.

### Don't Cover Blood

If, however, one did slaughter a *koy* on *Yom Tov*, the ruling is *ein mechasin et damo*—do not cover the blood. Better to commit a possible sin of omission (failing to cover if obligated) than a definite sin of commission (performing an unnecessary *melachah*). The blood may be covered after *Yom Tov*.

### Safek Scenario

The Gemara now asks: what type of *koy* creates this *safek*? If the mother was a *chaya* and the father was a *behema*, even without resolving *chosheim l'zera ha-av*, the animal is at minimum part *chaya* through the mother alone. And since we established that even partial *chaya*-status triggers the obligation to cover blood, this *koy* should definitely require covering—there is no *safek*.

The *koy* that generates genuine uncertainty must therefore be the reverse: the mother was a *behema* and the father was a *chaya*. In that case, the question is whether the father's *chaya* contribution is significant. If *chosheim l'zera ha-av*, then the father's *chaya*-element matters, and this *koy* is part *chaya*, obligating the covering of blood. If *ein chosheim l'zera ha-av*, the father is irrelevant, and this *koy* is pure *behema*, with no obligation to cover blood.

### Chachamim's Uncertainty

Here the Gemara delivers a striking conclusion: *le-Rabbanan, be-emet sipuki mesafka lehu ei chosheim l'zera ha-av o ein chosheim*—the *Rabbanan* themselves are uncertain. Even the Sages who ruled that the father's contribution is significant and that *oso v'es beno* applies to a *koy* whose father is a *behema* do not hold this with certainty. Their ruling in the context of *oso v'es beno* reflects a stringency born of doubt, not a definitive position that *chosheim l'zera ha-av* is the law. Since they are uncertain whether we follow the father, they must be stringent wherever the *safek* creates a possible *issur*—and they cannot use that same *safek* to create a *heter*.

This is the principle of *safeka de-oraita le-chumra* applied at the level of the very question itself. The *safek* is not "maybe this particular animal's father was a *chaya*"—the parentage is known. The *safek* is "maybe the halacha is that we follow the father." If we are certain we do not follow the father, there is no *safek* at all and no *chumra* is required. But because the *Rabbanan* are uncertain whether *chosheim l'zera ha-av* is the rule, and because that uncertainty might result in an *issur*, they must be strict. The *Rabbanan*'s own uncertainty about *chosheim l'zera ha-av* is thus what generates the *safek* status of the *koy* on *Yom Tov*—and with this, the daf concludes.
