# Hullin 129 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meat from Dangling Limb

### Ever vs. Basar Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַחוֹתֵךְ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר מֵאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, חֲתָכוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ חִישֵּׁב עָלָיו – טָהוֹר. חִישֵּׁב עָלָיו, וְאַחַר כָּךְ חֲתָכוֹ – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who severs an olive-bulk of flesh from a limb** severed **from the living,** the flesh does not impart the impurity of a carcass but it does impart impurity as food if one designated it as food before it came into contact with a source of impurity. Therefore, if **one severed** the flesh from the limb **and afterward intended it** to be used for the consumption of a gentile, the flesh remains **pure** because at the time he designated the flesh as food it was not in contact with a source of impurity. But if **he intended it** to be used for the consumption of a gentile **and afterward severed** the flesh, the flesh is **impure,** because it came into contact with a source of impurity, i.e., the limb, after it was designated as food.

Daf 129 is the final *daf* of this *perek*, which has dealt with the laws of dangling limbs, skin, meat, *tumah*, and *taharah*. We pick up at the bottom of 128b, where a *Tanu Rabanan* begins roughly four lines from the bottom.

The *braisa* returns us to the fundamental framework governing dangling limbs. When an animal dies, any limb that was dangling is treated as though it separated during the animal's lifetime, rendering it *ever min hachai*. As a whole limb from a living animal, it is a *source* of *tumah*. However, *basar min hachai* — mere flesh, as opposed to a complete limb — is not a source of *tumah*. The meat can, however, *receive* *tumah* as food, provided one has designated it as food through *chishev alav*, i.e., consciously planned to eat it. This designation is necessary because *ever min hachai* is ordinarily not eaten — neither by Jews nor, halachically, by non-Jews — and therefore without such intent it does not qualify as food.

### Cut Then Designate

The *braisa* presents the following case: one cuts a *kezayis* of meat from an *ever min hachai*. Rashi notes that for purposes of becoming *tamei* as food the relevant measure is actually a *kabeitzah*, but the core ruling stands regardless. The ever min hachai as a whole is a source of *tumah*; the question is whether the piece of meat cut away from it can itself *receive* *tumah*.

The ruling turns on the sequence of actions. If one first cuts the meat away and only afterward designates it as food — *chatcho ve'ach kach chishev alav* — it is *tahor*. The meat, once separated, is no longer part of a *tumah* source, and it has not yet been defined as food. When it is subsequently defined as food, it is no longer in contact with the limb and so receives no *tumah* from it.

If, however, one first designated the meat as food while it was still part of the limb, and only afterward cut it away — *chishev alav ve'ach kach chatcho* — it is *tamei*. The logic is as follows: at the moment of designation, the meat was still physically part of the *ever min hachai*, which is itself a *tumah* source. Once defined as food in that state, it was in contact with — indeed, was part of — the very source of *tumah*. When it is subsequently severed, it retains the *tumah* it absorbed while connected. It is no longer a *source* of *tumah*, but it carries the *tumah* it received.

### Assi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַסִּי לָא עָל לְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי זֵירָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֲמוּר בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי קַשְׁיָא לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דְּקָתָנֵי: חִישֵּׁב וְאַחַר כָּךְ חֲתָכוֹ טָמֵא. טוּמְאַת בֵּית הַסְּתָרִים הִיא, וְטוּמְאַת בֵּית הַסְּתָרִים לָא מְטַמְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** One day **Rabbi Asi did not go to the study hall. He found Rabbi Zeira,** and **said to him: What was said** today **in the study hall?** Rabbi Zeira **said to him: What** matter is **difficult for you** that you think may have been discussed in the study hall? Rabbi Asi **said to him:** I find difficult that **which is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one intended** flesh from a limb that was severed from a living animal to be used for the consumption of a gentile, **and afterward he severed** the flesh from the limb, the flesh is **impure.** Why should the flesh be impure? Since the source of its impurity is the limb, and the location of the contact between the limb and the flesh is hidden and not visible, **it** constitutes contact with a source of **impurity** in **a concealed part** of the body, **and** the principle is that contact with a source of **impurity** in **a concealed part** of the body **does not render** an item **impure.**

Rabbi Assi had not been present in the *beis medrash* on the day this was discussed. He encountered Rabbi Zeira and asked what had been said, sensing from the context of the *sugya* that something in it was troubling. He cited the *braisa* — *chishev ve'ach kach chatcho tamei* — and raised the following difficulty, which we encounter at the opening of 129a: the contact through which the meat absorbed *tumah* from the limb was contact at an unexposed surface — *tumas beis hastarim*. And *tumas beis hastarim lo metamei* — contact at a hidden, non-exposed part of one's body does not transfer *tumah*. When a piece of meat is being cut away from a limb, it is in contact with the limb precisely at the point of severance, which is not yet exposed until the cutting is complete. Once the cut is made and the surface is exposed, the contact has ended. How, then, did the *tumah* transfer?

### Zeira's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף לְדִידִי קַשְׁיָא לִי, וּשְׁאֵילְתֵּיהּ לְרַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל, וְאָמַר לִי: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: טוּמְאַת בֵּית הַסְּתָרִים מְטַמְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rabbi Asi: This matter is **difficult for me as well, and I asked Rabbi Abba bar Memel, and he said to me:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said** that contact with a source of **impurity** in **a concealed part** of the body **renders** an item **impure.**

Rabbi Zeira responded that this very question had bothered him the previous day in the *beis medrash*, and he had posed it to Rabbi Abba bar Mamal. Rabbi Abba bar Mamal answered: *hani Rabbi Meir hi* — this *braisa* follows the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds that *tumas beis hastarim* does transfer *tumah*. The reference is to a case discussed roughly sixty pages earlier, involving a fetus that extended its limb outside the womb. If the animal was then slaughtered, that extended limb is considered a *neveila* rather than something rendered permissible by *shechita*, and it thus becomes a source of *tumah*. The question arose whether the fetus inside became *tamei* through contact with its own dangling limb — contact that occurred only at an unexposed surface. Rabbi Meir held that it did, and it is that position that underpins our *braisa*.

### Assi's Second Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָאו זִימְנִין סַגִּיאִין אַמְרַהּ קַמַּאי, וַאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין טוּמְאָה דְּבָעֲיָא הֶכְשֵׁר וּבֵין טוּמְאָה דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Asi **said to** Rabbi Zeira: **But hasn't** Rabbi Abba bar Memel **said** this answer **in my presence many times? And I said to him** that this answer does not explain the ruling of the *baraita*. The reason is that with regard to a concealed part of the body imparting impurity, **Rabbi Meir differentiates between** a type of **impurity that requires** an item to be **rendered susceptible** in order to take effect **and** a type of **impurity that does not require** an item to be **rendered susceptible.** The case in the *baraita* is one where the flesh was not yet rendered susceptible to impurity when it was severed from the limb, and Rabbi Meir concedes that in such a case impurity should not apply to a concealed part of the body.

Rabbi Assi, however, was not satisfied. He reported to Rabbi Zeira that Rabbi Abba bar Mamal had offered him this same answer on previous occasions, and that he had consistently rejected it. The cases are not comparable. In the fetus case, the *shechita* of the animal rendered the fetus itself susceptible to *tumah* without any need for prior *hechsher* — contact with liquid — because *dam shechita*, the blood of ritual slaughter, serves as *machshir*. In our case, by contrast, the meat was cut from an *ever min hachai*, an animal whose limb fell away without *shechita*. That meat never came into contact with any liquid that would render it susceptible to *tumah*. The concern is not only about the unexposed surface — that concern may indeed be resolved by invoking Rabbi Meir's position — but there remains an entirely separate problem: the piece of meat never underwent *hechsher*, and therefore how could it have become susceptible to *tumah* in the first place? The question stands.

One might wonder whether the blood produced during the cutting process could serve as *machshir*. The answer is no: only *dam shechita* renders food susceptible. Ordinary blood from a wound — *dam makah* — does not qualify as *machshir*, and so this avenue is closed.

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא בְּשֶׁהוּכְשַׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** was surprised by Rabbi Asi's statement and **said: But what is the difficulty? Perhaps** the *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the flesh **was rendered** susceptible to impurity before it was severed from the limb.

Rava responded to this second difficulty. Why is the absence of *hechsher* a problem? *De-neima de-huchshar* — one can simply posit that the meat did come into contact with liquid at some point. The circumstances of the *braisa* can be read to include that the meat was rendered susceptible before being cut away — that it was first designated as food, then came into contact with liquid, and then was severed. This is the sequence that produces the ruling of *tamei*.

### Alternative Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן לְרָבָא: לְמָה לִי הֶכְשֵׁר, הֲרֵי מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה אַגַּב אָבִיו?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan said to Rava: Why do I** need the flesh severed from the limb to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity? Flesh that is upon a limb from a living animal **imparts a severe** form of **impurity due to its original** limb, as it is considered part of the limb that was severed from a living animal, which imparts the impurity of a carcass, a severe form of impurity that is transmitted even to people and vessels. Therefore, it is not necessary to render this flesh susceptible to impurity after its separation from the limb, because the *halakha* is that any food item that will eventually impart a severe form of impurity does not require contact with liquid in order to be rendered susceptible to imparting a lesser form of impurity.

Rava bar Rav Chanan, however, offered a more fundamental answer. Why assume any *hechsher* is necessary at all? *Harei metamei tumah chamurah agav aviv* — this piece of meat, when it was part of the limb, was itself part of a weighty source of *tumah*. There is a principle that something which will become — or, even more powerfully, something that already *was* — a source of heightened *tumah* does not require prior contact with liquid to become susceptible. This meat began its existence as part of an *ever min hachai*, an *av ha-tumah*. That prior status as a *tumah* source should exempt it from any *hechsher* requirement; it is already so thoroughly embedded in the world of *tumah* that the liquid-contact requirement becomes superfluous.

## Ma'aseh Etz Doctrine

### Identity Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּשֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, מַעֲשֵׂה עֵץ שִׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan: This principle applies only when the more severe and more lenient forms of impurity are both impurities of food. But in the case of the *baraita*, the flesh needs to be rendered susceptible to impurity after it is severed from the limb, because **when it** initially **served** as part of the limb, **it performed the role of wood,** i.e., it had the status of flesh of the limb, which is necessary to give the limb the status of a limb severed from the living (see 128b), but it was not impure due to its status as food.

Rava, however, objected to Rava bar Rav Chanan's principle. *Keshe-shimesh ma'aseh etz shimesh* — when this piece of meat served the limb, it was functioning as wood. That is, its identity then was not as *food*. The reason an *ever min hachai* is a source of *tumah* has nothing to do with its classification as food — as Rashi and Tosafot both note, an *ever* requires bone, sinew, and flesh together; what defines it as an *ever* is its identity as a *limb*, not its identity as something edible. When the piece of meat was part of the limb, it served in the capacity of a limb. That is a categorically different identity, to which Rashi applies the phrase *panim chadashot ba'u lechan* — a new face, a new identity, has arrived. As food, this meat never was a source of *tumah*. Its prior status as part of a *tumah* source was under a different identity entirely, and therefore it cannot exempt the meat — in its current identity as food — from the *hechsher* requirement.

This principle — *ma'aseh etz kimei shehayah*, that something functioning as wood, i.e., in a non-food identity, carries no relevance to its food-identity status — becomes the organizing principle for a series of cases the *Gemara* now works through, each asking why a particular food still requires *hechsher* if it once was or could become a weighty *tumah* source, and each receiving the same response.

### Hardened Dough Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ כּוֹפֶת שְׂאוֹר שֶׁיִּחֲדָהּ לִישִׁיבָה – בָּטְלָה. טוּמְאָתָהּ לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מָצִינוּ לָאֳכָלִין שֶׁמְּטַמְּאִין טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, כְּשֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ – מַעֲשֵׂה עֵץ שִׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** §Rava said that if a food item serves a function other than food, the principle that if it will eventually contract a severe form of impurity it does not require contact with liquid in order to be rendered susceptible to a lesser form of impurity does not apply. The Gemara now relates a number of matters that are explained with the same reasoning. **Abaye said:** The Sages **said** in a *baraita*: **A mass of** hardened **leaven that one designated for** the purpose of **sitting** upon it, not for consumption, **is nullified.** The item is no longer considered food and one may possess it in his house during Passover. But the item is now considered a chair, and it is subject to ritual impurity imparted by treading. It therefore is rendered impure if a *zav* sits on it. Abaye explained: Although the forbidden leaven that is hardened and designated as a seat imparts impurity, **its impurity is clearly not by Torah law** but by rabbinic law; **as if it enters your mind** that it is impure **by Torah law,** then **we have found** that **food can become susceptible to a severe** type of **impurity.** This cannot be true, because the category of food that requires contact with liquid to be susceptible to impurity is food that will not eventually impart a more severe type of impurity. Based on Rava's reasoning, the Gemara responds: The seat imparts impurity by Torah law. **When** the leaven **served** as a chair it was not considered food, as **it performed the role of wood.**

Abaye introduces the first case. The ruling states: a block of hardened leaven (*kupat se'or*) that was designated to be sat upon — *sheyichdah liyeshivah* — its *tumah* as food is negated (*batlah tumato*). It is no longer considered food and therefore is not subject to the *chametz* prohibition; simultaneously, since it now functions as a seat, a *zav* who sits upon it renders it *tamei* as a *midras* vessel.

### Abaye's Question

Abaye raises the question: if that *tumah* — the capacity of the hardened dough, now functioning as a seat upon which a *zav* sits, to become a weighty *tumah* source — were biblical, we would have established that any food can potentially become a source of *tumah chamurah*, since any food could in principle be hardened and repurposed as a seat. If that were sufficient to waive the *hechsher* requirement, food would never require *hechsher* at all. Since we know that conclusion is false, the status of this repurposed block as a *tumah* source must be only rabbinic.

The Gemara implicitly grants that there are two possible objections to Abaye's reasoning: certain foods are more naturally suited to becoming seats than others, and additionally, the principle waiving *hechsher* may apply only when becoming a *tumah* source is inevitable rather than merely theoretical. But Abaye assumes the theoretical possibility is sufficient, and on that assumption the conclusion follows.

### Ma'aseh Etz Response

The *Gemara*'s response is the same formula it will deploy throughout this series: *pirshu mishum ma'aseh etz*. Even if food is repurposed as a seat and thereby becomes a *tumah* source, in that role it is functioning as wood — as furniture, not as food. Its identity has changed entirely. The *panim chadashot* principle applies: as food it will never be a *tumah* source, and therefore it requires *hechsher* in its food identity. If it later becomes a seat, that is a new identity altogether, irrelevant to its current status. Tosafot raises the question of how it could then be permitted to own such an object on *Pesach* if its *tumah* status as a vessel is only rabbinic — but one answer may be that there is a middle category in which the object is sufficiently hardened and repurposed that it loses its *chametz* classification, without necessarily acquiring full biblical vessel status.

### Avodah Zarah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ תִּקְרוֹבֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל אֳכָלִין מְטַמְּאִין בְּאוֹהֶל, טוּמְאָתָהּ לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מָצִינוּ לָאֳכָלִין שֶׁמְּטַמְּאִין טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה! כְּשֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ – מַעֲשֵׂה עֵץ שִׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Abaye said:** The Sages **said** in a *baraita*: **An idolatrous offering of food imparts impurity in a tent.** Abaye explained: **Its impurity is not by Torah law** but by rabbinic law; **as if it enters your mind** that it is impure **by Torah law,** then **we have found** that **food can become susceptible to a severe** type of **impurity.** The Gemara responds: The idolatrous offering imparts impurity by Torah law. **When** the food **served** as an idolatrous offering it was not considered food, as **it performed the role of wood.**

Abaye presents a second case. The ruling states that food offerings to *avodah zarah* (*tikrovet avodah zarah shel ochlin*) cause *tumah* under a covered space — *metamein be'ohel* — a form of *tumah* as severe as that of a corpse. If this were biblical law, then since any food could theoretically be offered to an idol and thereby become a weighty *tumah* source, food would never require *hechsher*. Abaye concludes that this *tumah* must be rabbinic.

The *Gemara*'s answer is again *tishmesh matzao ve-asao tishmush* — when offered to an idol, the food functions in the capacity of a gift or tribute, not as food per se. One offers jewelry to an idol; one offers vessels; the fact that food is offered does not mean its identity as food is what gives the offering its significance. It is its identity as a dedicatory gift that matters. Therefore, whatever *tumah* status it carries in that role has nothing to do with its identity as food, and the *hechsher* requirement for food remains intact. It should be noted that the *Gemara* in *Shabbos* independently reaches the conclusion that *tumah* of *avodah zarah* offerings is rabbinic; what our *Gemara* adds is that in any case the identity-change argument would also preclude deriving any implications for the food-*tumah* system.

### Food in Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ חִבּוּרֵי אֳכָלִין כְּכֵלִים דָּמוּ, טוּמְאָתָן לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מָצִינוּ לְאוֹכֶל שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה! כְּשֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ – מַעֲשֵׂה עֵץ שִׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Abaye said:** The Sages **said** in a *baraita*: **Foods that are connected** to vessels are considered **like the vessels.** For example, if dough is attached to a kneading bowl and the owner wishes for the dough to remain there, the dough is considered part of the bowl. Therefore, if an olive-bulk of a corpse touches that dough, it becomes impure with the more severe impurity of a vessel, which imparts impurity to people and other vessels. Abaye explained: **Their impurity is not by Torah law** but by rabbinic law; **as if it enters your mind** that it is impure **by Torah law,** then **we have found** that **food can become susceptible to a severe** type of **impurity.** The Gemara responds: Food connected to vessels imparts impurity by Torah law. **When** the food **served** as a connection to the vessel it was not considered food, as **it performed the role of wood.**

Abaye presents a third case. The ruling states: *chiburei ochlin ke-keilim damu* — food that adheres to or is integrated into vessels takes on the status of the vessel itself. Rashi's example is food lodged in the cracks of a vessel, serving structurally as a kind of sealant. In such a case, the food is no longer independent; it is one with the vessel. Now: if such a vessel touches a *meis*, the vessel itself becomes an *av ha-tumah*. Food alone can never become an *av ha-tumah*. But food that has become integrated into the vessel can, through the vessel's contact with a corpse, attain that status. Were this biblical, any food could potentially be integrated into a vessel and thereby become an *av ha-tumah*, eliminating the *hechsher* requirement for all food. Hence this principle must be only rabbinic.

The answer is once again the same: when the food is integrated into the vessel, it is functioning as putty, as a structural filler — *ma'aseh etz*, serving in the identity of wood rather than food. That role is categorically distinct from its food identity, and no implications follow for the food-*tumah* laws.

### Fat/Kidney Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: הָא דְּתַנְיָא, חֵלֶב נְבֵלָה בַּכְּפָרִים צָרִיךְ מַחְשָׁבָה וְהֶכְשֵׁר, טוּמְאָתוֹ אַגַּב כּוּלְיָא לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מָצִינוּ לְאוֹכֶל שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה! כְּשֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ – מַעֲשֵׂה עֵץ שִׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Pappa said to Rava** in explanation of **that which is taught** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:3): **Fat** forbidden in consumption for a Jew from **an animal carcass in the villages, requires designation** as food in order for it to become susceptible to contract impurity as food, **and** it must be **rendered susceptible** via contact with liquid. Rav Pappa explained: Although the forbidden fat that covers the kidney of a carcass imparts the impurity of a carcass, **its impurity due to** the impurity of the **kidney is not by Torah law** but by rabbinic law. **As if it enters your mind** that it is impure **by Torah law,** then **we have found** that **food can impart a severe** type of **impurity** and subsequently become susceptible to impurity as food. The Gemara responds: The fat of a carcass imparts impurity by Torah law. **When** the fat **served** the kidney as protection and imparted the impurity of a carcass it did not serve as food, as **it performed the role of wood.**

Rav Papa addresses a fourth case. The ruling concerning fat (*chelev*) of a *neveila* notes that in villages, where such fat is not normally eaten, one requires both *machshava* (designation as food) and *hechsher* (contact with liquid) before the fat becomes susceptible to *tumah*. Rav Papa points out that the classic location of *chelev* is surrounding the kidneys. A kidney embedded in a mass of *chelev* is itself a *neveila* — since this is an animal that was not *shechted* — and is therefore a *tumah* source. The principle of *shomer* applies: the *chelev* that surrounds and protects the kidney functions as a *shomer* for it, meaning that touching the *chelev* is halachically equivalent to touching the kidney itself. The *chelev*, as *shomer*, thus effectively renders one *tamei* as though one had touched the *neveila* directly. This means the *chelev*, while connected to the kidney, was itself a source of *tumah* — not a derived or secondary one but one effective through the *shomer* principle. Why, then, should it require *hechsher* once separated and designated as food? The answer is the same: *keshe-shimesh ma'aseh etz shimesh* — when it served as *shomer* to the kidney, it was functioning in a structural, protective capacity, not as food. A *shomer* is conceptually analogous to a shell or husk — it is "wood." That identity is entirely distinct from its identity as food, and the *hechsher* requirement stands.

This entire series of cases establishes a principle while simultaneously demonstrating its limits. In every case where food might have been thought to be exempt from *hechsher* because it once was or could become a *tumah* source, the exemption fails because the *tumah* source role was not in the food's capacity *as food*. The one genuine application of the exemption is *neveilas of tahor* — the carcass of a *kosher* bird — which, when swallowed, renders one *tamei* from within. There the food *is* a *tumah* source in its capacity as food, and accordingly it does not require *hechsher*.

### Seeds/Roof Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ בַּיִת שֶׁסִּכְּכוֹ בִּזְרָעִים – טָהֵרוּ. טוּמְאָתוֹ לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – מָצִינוּ לִזְרָעִים שֶׁמְּטַמְּאִין טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה! כְּשֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ – מַעֲשֵׂה עֵץ שִׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Mattana said:** The Sages **said** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a house that one roofed with seeds,** i.e., vegetation, if those seeds were impure, they are rendered **pure** when they are used as the roof of the house. The seeds are no longer considered food but rather part of the house. Therefore, if the house becomes leprous, the entire house becomes impure. Rav Mattana explained: **Its impurity is not by Torah law** but by rabbinic law; **as if it enters your mind** that it is impure **by Torah law,** then **we have found** that **seeds can become susceptible to a severe** type of **impurity.** The Gemara responds: The seeds used for the roof of the house impart impurity by Torah law. **When** the seeds **served** as the roof of the house they were not considered food, but rather **performed the role of wood.**

Rav Masna adds a fifth case. The ruling states: *beis she-tikho b'zraim taharu* — a house whose interior was covered with seeds (more precisely, with grain still in its stalks) and those stalks were designated for use as the roof, those seeds/stalks become *tahor*, losing their prior status as food, since they are now treated as attached to the structure and part of the ground. But as part of the house, if the house contracts *tzara'at*, the stalks become part of a *tumah* source — indeed, an *av ha-tumah*.

Were this biblical, any grain could eventually be placed on a roof, become part of a house with *tzara'at*, and thereby become an *av ha-tumah*, eliminating the *hechsher* requirement. Hence this must be rabbinic. The answer: *keshe-shimesh me'eitz shimesh* — when they became part of the roof, they were functioning as wood, as building material. Their *tumah* in that context is in their identity as structural components, not as food. The *hechsher* requirement for food is therefore unaffected.

Rashi notes, with evident candor, that in this last case he does not know the source from which the *Gemara* is drawing the ruling. In all the previous cases the *Gemara* cited identifiable *Mishnas* or *Braisos*, but here the cross-reference is absent, and Rashi acknowledges that the source of this accepted ruling is unclear to him.

## Shimon on Living

### Shimon's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי מִיתָה עוֹשָׂה נִיפּוּל – לִיטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, אִי אֵין מִיתָה עוֹשָׂה נִיפּוּל – לִיטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַנְּבֵלָה! רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי: הָאֵבֶר וְהַבָּשָׂר הַמְדוּלְדָּלִין בִּבְהֵמָה מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין בִּמְקוֹמָן, וּצְרִיכִין הֶכְשֵׁר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna teaches with regard to a hanging limb or flesh of a living animal that if the animal died, the hanging flesh needs to be rendered susceptible to impurity, as its halakhic status is that of flesh severed from a living animal, which is ritually pure. Rabbi Meir then states that the hanging limb imparts impurity as a limb severed from a living animal, but not as an unslaughtered carcass. And **Rabbi Shimon deems** the limb **pure.** The Gemara asks: **Whichever** way **you** look at it, the ruling of Rabbi Shimon is difficult. **If death renders** a hanging limb one that has **fallen off,** i.e., if when the animal died of its own accord the hanging limb is considered to have fallen off its body beforehand, the limb **should become impure as a limb from a living** animal. **If death does not render** a hanging limb one that has **fallen off,** the limb **should become impure as a limb from a carcass.** How is it possible for Rabbi Shimon to deem the limb pure? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon is not responding to Rabbi Meir's statement in the final case of the mishna. Rather, **Rabbi Shimon is referring to the first clause** of the mishna, which teaches: **The limb and the flesh** of an animal that were partially severed and remain **hanging from the animal impart impurity as food** although they remain **in their place** attached to the animal. **But** in order for them to become impure, **they need** to be **rendered susceptible** through contact with a liquid. **And Rabbi Shimon deems** them **not susceptible to impurity** at all.

With the *ma'aseh etz* doctrine established, the *Gemara* turns to the concluding statement of the *Mishnah* — Rabbi Shimon's ruling that the dangling limb is *tahor*. The context of the *Mishnah* involves three cases: the dangling limb while the animal is alive; the dangling limb after *shechita*; and the dangling limb after the animal dies naturally. Rabbi Shimon says *tahor*.

The *Gemara* opens with the striking observation: *man d'shach* — either way Rabbi Shimon's ruling is difficult. If death renders the limb as though it fell off during life (*misa osah nipul*), it should be *tamei* as *ever min hachai*. If death does not render it as though it fell off (*ein misa osah nipul*), the limb died with the animal and should be *tamei* as part of a *neveilah*. How can it be *tahor* under either reading?

The *Gemara* answers: *Rabbi Shimon areisha kai* — Rabbi Shimon is not referring to the final case of the *Mishnah* (the animal's death); he is going back to the first case — *ha'ever vehabasar hameduldalin bibeheimah* — the dangling limb while the animal is alive. The *Mishnah* rules that such a limb is susceptible to *tumah* as food and requires *hechsher*. It is to this ruling that Rabbi Shimon says *tahor* — this dangling limb, while the animal is alive, is not considered food.

### Yochanan's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר קְרָא ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – קָרוּי אוֹכֶל, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – אֵין קָרוּי אוֹכֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon? The verse states** with regard to impurity as food: **"From all food which may be eaten,** that on which water comes shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34). The repetitive phrase "food which may be eaten" indicates that only **food that you are able to feed to others,** including gentiles, **is called food** with regard to being susceptible to impurity as food, but **food that you are not able to feed to others,** such as the limb and flesh from a living animal, which are forbidden even to gentiles, **is not called food.**

Rav Assi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: what is Rabbi Shimon's reason? The verse says *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel* — from all food that will be eaten. *Ochel she'atah yachol lehachilo l'acherim karui ochel* — only something that you can feed to others, something that either a Jew or a non-Jew could eat and from which benefit can be derived, is called *ochel*. *Ochel she'ee atah yachol lehachilo l'acherim ein karui ochel* — something that cannot be fed to anyone is not called food. The dangling limb while the animal is alive is *ever min hachai*: it is forbidden to Jews, forbidden to non-Jews, and one may not derive any benefit from it whatsoever. Since it can be fed to no one and yields no permissible benefit, it cannot be called *ochel*, and therefore it is not susceptible to *tumah* as food.

### Zeira's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אַסִּי: דִּילְמָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָתָם, הוֹאִיל וּמְעוֹרֶה – מְעוֹרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira** questioned the explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan and **said to Rabbi Asi:** If Rabbi Shimon is discussing the first clause in the mishna, claiming that a hanging limb or flesh is pure during the lifetime of the animal, the reason for his statement is not necessarily that food that is forbidden to all people is not considered food. **Perhaps the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon there** is that **since** the flesh or limb is still **attached** to the animal, it is considered **attached.**

Rabbi Zeira challenges this. Why invoke such a sophisticated explanation? Perhaps Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is far simpler: *dilma ta'ama d'Rabbi Shimon hasham mishum me'ura me'ura* — perhaps the reason is simply that the limb is still physically intertwined with the animal, still one unit with it. To classify a dangling limb of a living animal as *food* and therefore susceptible to *tumah* is already a conceptual stretch; Rabbi Shimon might simply be saying that physical attachment, however tenuous, renders it still part of the animal. Why is this more elaborate explanation needed?

### Tree Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: יִחוּר שֶׁל תְּאֵנָה שֶׁנִּפְשַׁח וּמְעוֹרֶה בַּקְּלִיפָּה – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִם יָכוֹל לִחְיוֹת – טָהוֹר, וְאִם לָאו – טָמֵא. וְאָמְרִינַן לָךְ: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? וְאָמְרַתְּ לַן: הוֹאִיל וּמְעוֹרֶה – מְעוֹרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:8): With regard to **a branch of a fig** tree **that was detached** from the tree **and** remains **attached** only **to the bark** of the tree, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the figs on the branch **not susceptible to impurity,** as they are considered attached to the tree. **And the Rabbis say: If** it is possible to reattach the branch to the tree and the branch **can** continue to **live** and produce fruit, then it is considered attached to the tree, and the fruit **is not susceptible to impurity. But if not,** the fruit is **susceptible to impurity. And we said to you,** Rabbi Asi: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda? And you said to us: Since** the branch is still **attached** to the bark of the tree, it is considered **attached.** Therefore, the same logic applies to the statement of Rabbi Shimon.

Rabbi Zeira supports this from a *braisa*: the case of a fig-tree limb that has cracked off but remains intertwined through its bark (*yichur shel te'enah she-nifshach u-me'ura b'klipatah*). Rabbi Yehuda rules that the fruit on such a limb is *tahor* — it is still considered attached to the tree. The Sages say: if the limb is capable of surviving and reconnecting (*im yachol lichyot, tahor*); if not, the fruit is *tamei*. And when the *Gemara* asks for Rabbi Yehuda's reason, the answer is *ho'il u-me'ura me'ura* — since it is still physically intertwined, it is considered one with the tree. If that principle applies to a cracked tree limb, the same *me'ura be-me'ura* reasoning should explain Rabbi Shimon's ruling about the dangling animal limb without needing to invoke the *ochel she'ee atah yachol lehachilo l'acherim* formula.

The *Gemara* concludes from this that Rabbi Yochanan's explanation — which invokes the food-definition argument — cannot have been offered for the first case of the *Mishnah* (the living animal), since the simpler *me'ura be-me'ura* explanation suffices there. This implies that Rabbi Yochanan's explanation was offered for a different case in the *Mishnah*, as will emerge.

## Shimon on Shechita

### Shechita Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, אַמְּצִיעֲתָא: נִשְׁחֲטָה הַבְּהֵמָה, הוּכְשְׁרָה בְּדָמֶיהָ – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר; רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ. (אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי) אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר קְרָא ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״ – אוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – קָרוּי אוֹכֶל, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאֵין אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – אֵין קָרוּי אוֹכֶל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Asi **said to** Rabbi Zeira: Rabbi Yoḥanan is explaining the reasoning for Rabbi Shimon's opinion in **the middle clause** of the mishna, which teaches: If **the animal was slaughtered,** the limb and the flesh **were rendered** susceptible to impurity **with the blood of** the slaughtered animal; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Shimon says: They were not rendered** susceptible with the blood of the slaughtered animal. **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon?** The reason is that **the verse states** with regard to impurity as food: **"From all food which may be eaten,** that on which water comes shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34). The phrase "food which may be eaten" indicates that only **food that you can feed to others,** including gentiles, **is called food** in this regard, but **food that you cannot feed to others,** such as the limb and flesh from a living animal, **is not called food.**

The *Gemara* now moves to the middle case of the *Mishnah*: the animal has been slaughtered (*nishchata ha-behemah*). The *Mishnah* states that according to Rabbi Meir, the blood of *shechita* renders the dangling limb susceptible — *huchshara b'dameha*. Rabbi Shimon says the *shechita* blood did not render it susceptible (*lo huchshara*). Since the simpler *me'ura be-me'ura* explanation resolves Rabbi Shimon's position regarding the living animal, the *Gemara* proposes that the food-definition argument (*ochel she'ee atah yachol lehachilo l'acherim*) belongs here, in the middle case — where Rabbi Shimon denies *hechsher* by the *dam shechita*.

### Non-Consumable

Rabbi Yochanan's explanation in that context would be: the blood of *shechita* does not render this dangling limb susceptible because it is not *ochel*. Since it cannot be fed to anyone, it is not food, and the *hechsher* mechanism does not apply to it.

### Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּהָהִיא אִי כִּדְרָבָא, אִי כִּדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira questioned this explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan as well, and said to Rabbi Asi: If it is with regard to Rabbi Shimon's opinion in the middle clause of the mishna, **perhaps the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon in that** clause is not that food that is forbidden to all is not called food. Rather, it is **either in accordance with** the explanation **of Rava or in accordance with** the explanation **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** cited earlier (127b–128a). According to both explanations of Rabbi Shimon's opinion, the mishna is discussing a case where only the body of the animal, but not the partially severed limb, came into contact with the blood of slaughter. According to Rava, the reason for Rabbi Shimon's opinion is that the body of the animal serves the partially severed limb as a handle, and he holds that a handle of a food item transmits impurity to the attached food, but a handle that comes into contact with liquid does not render the attached food susceptible to impurity. And according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, Rabbi Shimon holds that if one grasps a small part of a large item such that the large part does not ascend with the small part, the small part is not considered part of the item with regard to impurity (see 127b).

The *Gemara*, however, rejects this placement as well. *Dilma ta'ama d'Rabbi Shimon be-hai ika ke-Rabbi ika d'Rabbi Yochanan* — for the middle case there is already a full set of explanations debated earlier about why the *shechita* blood does or does not apply to a dangling limb; there is no need to invoke this entirely new food-definition argument. Positioning Rabbi Yochanan's explanation at the middle case is therefore also untenable.

## Shimon on Dead

### Dangling Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם אַסֵּיפָא, וְלָאו אַאֵבֶר, אֶלָּא אַבָּשָׂר. מֵתָה הַבְּהֵמָה – הַבָּשָׂר צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר קְרָא ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – קָרוּי אוֹכֶל, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים – אֵין קָרוּי אוֹכֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually,** one must explain the statement of Rabbi Shimon as it was explained originally, that he is referring **to the latter clause** of the mishna. **And** he is **not** referring **to** the case of a partially severed **limb, but rather to** the case of partially severed **flesh.** Therefore, the latter clause of the mishna teaches: If **the animal died** without slaughter, Rabbi Meir holds that **the** hanging **flesh needs** to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity in order to impart impurity as food, **and Rabbi Shimon deems** the limb **not susceptible to impurity** even if it came into contact with liquid. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon?** The reason is that **the verse states** with regard to impurity as food: **"From all food which may be eaten,** that on which water comes shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34). The phrase "food which may be eaten" indicates that only **food that you can feed to others,** including gentiles, **is called food** with regard to being susceptible to impurity as food, but **food that you cannot feed to others,** such as flesh from a living animal, which is forbidden even to gentiles, **is not called food.**

Having rejected both the first and middle cases as the home of Rabbi Yochanan's explanation, the *Gemara* concludes: *ela le'olam ha'seifa* — return to the simple reading, that Rabbi Shimon is addressing the final case, the animal's death. But the *Gemara* now refines which aspect of that final case is at issue. Rabbi Shimon cannot be arguing about a whole dangling *limb* when the animal dies — a whole limb presents the intractable *either-way* problem raised at the outset. Rather, he is arguing about dangling *meat* (*basar*). The case is thus: *lav a'ever ela a'basar* — not the limb but the flesh.

### Hechsher Problem

When an animal dies with dangling meat, the meat is *basar min hachai*: it is not a *tumah* source. As food, it could in principle become *tamei* if it had *hechsher*. The *Mishnah* teaches *meisa ha-behemah, ha-basar tzarich hechsher* — if the animal died, the dangling meat requires *hechsher* to become susceptible. On this ruling, Rabbi Shimon says *tahor* — even with *hechsher*, this dangling meat will never become *tamei*.

### Shimon's Answer

Rabbi Yochanan explains Rabbi Shimon's position: *amar kra, mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel* — the verse again. *Ochel she'atah yachol lehachilo l'acherim karui ochel; ochel she'ee atah yachol lehachilo l'acherim ein karui ochel.* The dangling meat when the animal died is *basar min hachai*. Nobody can eat it — it is forbidden to Jews, forbidden to non-Jews, and yields no permissible benefit. Since it cannot be fed to anyone, it is not *ochel*, and therefore even with *hechsher* it cannot become *tamei*.

### Chachamim's View

The *Chachamim* disagree. Their position is that the definition of food for purposes of *tumah* is determined by the physical capacity to be eaten, not by the halachic permissibility of eating. *Basar min hachai* is physically fit to be consumed — it is objectively edible as a physical matter, even if halachically forbidden. That physical capacity suffices to classify it as food for *tumah* purposes, and therefore it can become *tamei* upon *hechsher*.

This dispute echoes the fundamental question that has been present throughout this *perek*: is food defined by objective physical edibility or by halachic accessibility? Rabbi Shimon holds the latter; the *Chachamim* hold the former.

## Human Dangling Limbs

### Human Dangling Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאֵבֶר וְהַבָּשָׂר הַמְדוּלְדָּלִין בְּאָדָם – טְהוֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The limb and the flesh** of a person that were partially severed and remain **hanging from a person are ritually pure,** although there is no potential for healing.

The *Mishnah* closes the *perek* by extending these laws to humans. *Ha'ever vehabasar hameduldalim b'adam tehorim* — a dangling limb or dangling meat of a human is *tahor*. This requires no elaborate explanation: a human being is not subject to *shechita*, the food-status framework does not apply to human limbs, and there is no basis on which to classify a dangling limb of a living person as *ochel*. While the person is alive, the dangling limb has no halachic status whatsoever.

### After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת הָאָדָם – הַבָּשָׂר טָהוֹר. הָאֵבֶר מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If **the person died, the** hanging **flesh is ritually pure,** as its halakhic status is that of flesh severed from a living person. **The** hanging **limb imparts impurity as a limb** severed **from the living and does not impart impurity as a limb from a corpse;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Shimon deems** the flesh and the limb **ritually pure.**

When the person dies, the *Mishnah* distinguishes between the two cases. *Ha-basar tahor* — the dangling meat remains *tahor*, because it is treated as having separated during the person's lifetime and is thus *basar min hachai* of a human, which is not a *tumah* source. *Ha'ever metamei mishum ever min hachai ve'eino metamei mishum ever min hameis* — the dangling limb, however, is *tamei* as an *ever min hachai*, since death causes it to be treated as though it separated during life, but it is not considered a limb of a corpse (*ever min hameis*).

The practical significance of this distinction is substantial. An *ever min hachai* requires the complete limb to be a *tumah* source; pieces that separate from it do not independently cause *tumah*. An *ever min hameis*, by contrast — as a component of a corpse — would cause *tumah* even through smaller pieces: a *kezayis* of flesh, or a bone the size of a barleycorn, can independently be *tumah* sources when they derive from a *meis*. By classifying the dangling limb as *ever min hachai* rather than *ever min hameis*, the *Mishnah* limits the *tumah* to the whole limb alone.

## Human Dangling Limbs…

### Logic Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי מִיתָה עוֹשָׂה נִיפּוּל – לִיטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, וְאִי אֵין מִיתָה עוֹשָׂה נִיפּוּל – לִיטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara challenges the opinion of Rabbi Shimon in the latter clause of the mishna: **Whichever** way **you** look at it, the ruling of **Rabbi Shimon** is difficult. **If death renders** a hanging limb **fallen off,** i.e., if after the person dies the hanging limb is considered to have fallen off his body beforehand, the limb **should impart impurity as a limb** severed **from the living. And if death does not render** a hanging limb **fallen off,** and the limb is considered attached to the body at the time of death, then the limb **should impart impurity as a limb from a corpse.**

The *Gemara* is troubled. If the dangling limb of a human who has died is *tamei* as *ever min hachai*, then what is the content of Rabbi Shimon's ruling that it is *tahor*? The same *either-way* problem arises: if death causes the limb to be treated as having fallen off (*misa osah nipul*), it should be *tamei* as *ever min hachai*; if not, it should be *tamei* as part of the corpse.

### Shimon's Real Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּעָלְמָא קָאֵי, דְּקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא: הָאֵבֶר מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי וְאֵין מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַנְּבֵלָה, אַלְמָא אֵבֶר הַמֵּת בְּעָלְמָא מְטַמֵּא, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵבֶר הַמֵּת בְּעָלְמָא לֹא מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This statement of Rabbi Shimon is not referring directly to the case in the mishna. Rather, the statement of **Rabbi Shimon is referring** to the matter of a limb that separates from a corpse **in general.** Rabbi Shimon inferred from **that which the first *tanna*,** Rabbi Meir, **said: The** hanging **limb imparts impurity as a limb** severed **from the living and does not impart impurity as a limb from a corpse,** that **evidently, in general the limb of a corpse imparts impurity. And** in reference to this **Rabbi Shimon said to him: In general, the limb of a corpse does not impart impurity,** if it does not contain an olive-bulk of flesh.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *Rabbi Shimon be'alma kai* — Rabbi Shimon is not disputing whether the dangling limb itself is *tamei*. His *tahor* ruling is a response to the *tanna kamma*'s implicit position, not a direct negation of *ever min hachai* status. The *tanna kamma* states that the limb is *tamei* as *ever min hachai* and *not* as *ever min hameis* — implicitly establishing that an *ever min hameis* would *normally* be *tamei*. It is to that implicit premise that Rabbi Shimon objects. Rabbi Shimon holds: *ever min hachai* — agreed, *tamei*; but *ever min hameis be'alma lo metamei* — a limb from a corpse, when it lacks sufficient flesh or other components to cause *tumah* on its own, is *tahor*.

### Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי מְטַמֵּא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִן הַחַי וְלֹא מִן הַמֵּת? וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר – וּמָה חַי שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר, אֵבֶר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ טָמֵא; מֵת שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Another pair of *tanna'im* had the same dispute as Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: I heard that a limb** severed **from the living imparts impurity. Rabbi Yehoshua said to him:** May one infer from this statement that a limb **from a living** person imparts impurity **but** a limb **from a corpse does not?** But it can be inferred ***a fortiori*** that a limb from a corpse imparts impurity: **If** with regard to **a living** person, **who is pure** and does not impart impurity, nevertheless **a limb that separates from him is impure,** then with regard to **a corpse, which is impure, all the more so** is it **not** clear that the limb that separates from it is impure?

The *Gemara* now brings a *braisa* that aligns Rabbi Shimon with Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Eliezer states: *shamati she'ever min hachai metamei* — I have received the tradition that a limb from a living person causes *tumah*. Implicitly, his tradition specifies *min hachai* — from a living person — and not *min hameis*, meaning that a limb from a corpse that lacks sufficient independent components would not cause *tumah*.

### Yehoshua's Logic

Rabbi Yehoshua challenges this with a *kal vachomer*: *ma chai she-hu tahor, ever ha-poresh mimenu tamei* — a living person is fundamentally *tahor*, yet a limb that separates from him causes *tumah*. *Meis she-hu tamei, lo kol sheken* — a corpse is *tamei* in its entirety; how much more so should a limb from a corpse, even without sufficient independent flesh, be *tamei*. Rabbi Yehoshua reinforces his argument by analogy to *Megillas Taanis*, which records that *Pesach Sheni* is a day on which one does not deliver a eulogy. One should not read this as implying that *Pesach Rishon* permits eulogies — it means even *Pesach Sheni*, the minor *Pesach*, carries this restriction. Similarly, Rabbi Eliezer's tradition that *ever min hachai* is *tamei* should not be read as excluding *ever min hameis*; it means even *ever min hachai* is *tamei*.

### Tradition Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב בִּמְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית: ״פִּסְחָא זְעִירָא דְּלָא לְמִסְפַּד״, הָא רַבָּה לְמִסְפַּד?! אֶלָּא כָּל דְּכֵן! הָכָא נָמֵי, כׇּל דְּכֵן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, Rabbi Yehoshua adds that **it is written in *Megillat Ta'anit*:** On **Minor Passover,** i.e., the fourteenth of Iyyar, **one does not eulogize.** Should one infer from here **that** on **Major** Passover, i.e., the fourteenth of Nisan, it is permitted **to eulogize?** Clearly that is not the case. **Rather,** if one may not eulogize on the fourteenth of Iyyar, **all the more so** one may not eulogize on the fourteenth of Nisan. **Here too,** if a limb from a living person is impure, **all the more so** a limb from a corpse is impure. Rabbi Eliezer **said to him:** Despite this reasoning, **such** is the ruling **I heard** from my teachers.

Rabbi Eliezer's response is simply: *kach shamati* — this is what I received as tradition. The tradition specifies that a limb from a living person causes *tumah*, but a limb from a corpse — absent sufficient flesh — does not. The *kal vachomer* cannot override a received tradition.

## Practical Distinctions

### Whole vs. Pieces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי לְאֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת? כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר וְעֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מֵאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. דִּתְנַן: כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מֵאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַמֵּא, וְרַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מְטַהֲרִין. עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מֵאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי – רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא מְטַמֵּא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מְטַהֲרִין.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna teaches that Rabbi Meir holds that with regard to a partially severed limb of a person, after the person dies the limb imparts impurity as a limb from a living person but not as a limb from a corpse. The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** the impurity of **a limb from a living** person **and** the impurity of **a limb from a corpse?** The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is with regard to the case of **an olive-bulk of flesh, or a bone** the volume of **a barley grain, that separates from** the severed **limb of a living** person. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 6:3): In the case of **an olive-bulk of flesh that separates from a limb** severed **from a living** person, **Rabbi Eliezer deems** it **impure, and Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana and Rabbi Yehoshua deem** it **pure.** In the case of **a bone** the volume of **a barley-grain that separates from a limb** severed **from a living** person, **Rabbi Neḥunya deems** it **impure, and Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua deem** it **pure.**

The *Gemara* asks: once we have established that the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon disagree, what is the practical difference between *ever min hachai* and *ever min hameis* as the *Mishnah* frames it? The answer is *kezayis basar ve'etzem kesiorah ha-poresh me'ever min hachai ika beinaihu* — the difference concerns pieces that separate from the limb. For an *ever min hachai*, *tumah* attaches only to the complete limb; pieces that separate from it — a *kezayis* of flesh, a barleycorn-sized bone — are not *tumah* sources. For an *ever min hameis*, by contrast, considered part of the corpse, a *kezayis* of flesh or a barleycorn-sized bone is independently *tamei*. This distinction has real halachic consequences.

### Flesh Dispute

The *Gemara* cites a *Mishnah*: *kezayis basar ha-poresh me'ever min hachai* — a *kezayis* of flesh separated from an *ever min hachai* of a human. Rabbi Eliezer holds it *tamei*; Rav Nechunya ben HaKana and Rabbi Yehoshua hold it *tahor*. This is notable: by an animal, *basar min hachai* is simply not a *tumah* source. Here, some opinions hold that flesh from an *ever min hachai* of a human is *tamei*, indicating that the rules for human *ever min hachai* are stricter in certain respects than for animals.

### Bone Dispute

Regarding a bone: *etzem kesiorah ha-poresh me'ever min hachai* — a barleycorn-sized bone separated from a human *ever min hachai*. Here the positions shift: Rav Nechunya ben HaKana holds it *tamei*, while Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua hold it *tahor*. Again, regardless of the individual positions, the overarching point is that by an *ever min hachai*, even of a human, the pieces that separate from it are a matter of dispute — and at least according to some opinions, they are *tahor* — whereas by an *ever min hameis*, these quantities would be straightforwardly *tamei* as components of a corpse.

### Final Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, בֵּין תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נָמֵי, כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר וְעֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הָעוֹר וְהָרוֹטֶב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Now that you have arrived at this** dispute between *tanna'im*, it is possible to say that the difference **between the first *tanna*** in the mishna, i.e., Rabbi Meir, **and Rabbi Shimon** is **also** with regard to the cases of **an olive-bulk of flesh and a bone** the size of **a barley grain.** Rabbi Meir states that the partially severed limb of a person imparts the impurity of a limb from a living person but not the impurity of a limb from a corpse. The difference between these two types of impurity is with regard to a case where either an olive-bulk of flesh or a bone the size of a barley grain was separated from the severed limb; Rabbi Neḥunya holds that flesh that separated from a limb of a living person is pure, but a bone that separated from a limb of a living person is impure, and Rabbi Eliezer holds vice versa. Rabbi Meir consequently holds in accordance with one of these two opinions. Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua that both a bone and flesh that separated from a limb of a living person are pure.

The *Gemara* concludes: *hasha de'asis le'hachi* — now that we have arrived at this framework of disputes about pieces separating from an *ever min hachai*, the scope of Rabbi Shimon's *tahor* ruling can be understood more narrowly. When Rabbi Shimon says the dangling limb is *tahor*, he need not be asserting the extreme position that an *ever min hameis* is entirely *tahor*. He may instead be disputing the *tanna kamma* regarding pieces that separate from an *ever min hachai* — the *tanna kamma* holding that at least flesh or bone pieces cause *tumah*, and Rabbi Shimon holding that only the whole *ever* causes *tumah* as *ever min hachai*, while any piece separated from it is *tahor*. Either way, the scope of Rabbi Shimon's *tahor* is limited: it applies to specific pieces or specific identity-classifications, not to a sweeping exemption of all dangling limbs.
