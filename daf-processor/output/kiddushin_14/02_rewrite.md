# Kiddushin 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Context

### Opening Remarks

Dan Margulis is filling in for Rabbi Linzer. By way of introduction, he notes that it is *shoshon purim*, the fifteenth of Adar, which is, by the well-known principle, *shloshim yom kodem hachag* — thirty days before Pesach — the time when we begin *sho'alin vedorshin b'hilchot hapesach*. Though the tractate under study is *Kiddushin*, the spirit of Pesach is present: the essential act of Pesach is asking questions, and that spirit animates the entire enterprise of Talmud study. The sugya before us today is on 13b.

### Eternal Marriage Polemic

The sugya at hand addresses how we know that a husband's death permits his widow to remarry. This is not merely a technical halakhic question; it carries polemical weight against theological traditions — certain of them still present today — that maintain the concept of an eternal marriage bond persisting even after one spouse's death. The Gemara's sustained attention to deriving this permission from scriptural sources reflects a serious halakhic and philosophical commitment to establishing that death fully dissolves the marital bond.

## Widow Remarriage

### Kohen Gadol Source

The Gemara proposes that we may derive the widow's permission to remarry from the special prohibition applying to the *kohen gadol*: *ela mide'amar rachmana almanah le'kohen gadol asura ha le'kohen hedyot sharya* — the Torah specifically prohibits the *kohen gadol* from marrying an *almanah*, which implies that a regular *kohen* may do so, and therefore an *almanah* is in general permitted to remarry. The only one absolutely prohibited from marrying a widow would be the *kohen gadol*.

### Mitzvat Asei Proposal

The Gemara immediately challenges this derivation. Perhaps the prohibition for the *kohen gadol* is a *lav*, while for all others — *Yisraelim*, regular *kohanim*, and everyone else — there remains an *issur asei* derived from the verse *vedavak be'ishto*, the idea that one is bound by a positive commandment to be joined with one's wife. The *Ri* in *Tosafot* raises precisely this possibility: perhaps even after the husband's death, some residual *mitzvat asei* prohibits another man from marrying the widow. This is framed as the Gemara's *hava amina* — a position that will ultimately be rejected, but whose very consideration underscores how counterintuitive it would be to require explicit derivation for something as seemingly obvious as a widow's right to remarry. As the Gemara notes, the entire *shakla vetarya* here is designed to push against that intuition and demonstrate that the permission is in fact well-grounded in *psukim*.

### Lav vs Asei

The Gemara probes what this hypothetical *mitzvat asei* would actually accomplish. *Hai asei mai avid lei* — what does this *asei* do? Either *mitat habaal* completely releases her (*tishari legamri*), or it does not release her at all and she remains in her prior status (*tukma be'milta kamaisa*). The problem is that a *lav* and an *asei* operating together cannot produce a clean intermediate result. *Rashi* explains that if such an *issur asei* existed for everyone, there would be no reason for the *kohen gadol*'s prohibition to be expressed as the more severe *lav*.

The Gemara then entertains a further refinement: perhaps *mitat habaal* removes her from the category of capital crime (*chiyuv mitat beit din*) but leaves intact an *issur asei*. The analogy offered is *psulei hamukdashim* — animals consecrated for sacrifice that subsequently became disqualified by a permanent blemish. Before the blemish, there is a prohibition of *me'ilah* (using consecrated property) as well as prohibitions against shearing or using the animal for labor. Once an act of *me'ilah* occurs under certain circumstances, the *kedushah* is removed and *me'ilah* no longer applies — yet the prohibitions of shearing and labor (*giza va'avoda*) remain. Similarly, one might think that the husband's death removes the *issur* punishable by *chenek*, but an *issur asei* persists.

This analogy is ultimately rejected, and the Gemara arrives at a real *pasuk*: *pen yamut bamilchama ve'ish acher yikachena* — from the section of those exempt from military service, the Torah anticipates that a soldier recently betrothed might die in battle, and another man will marry her. The word *yikach* is recognized as language of marriage (*ki yikach ish isha*), proving incidentally that another man may marry her after the first husband's death. This *drasha*, however, applies only to *erusin*, not *nisuin*, which will require separate derivation.

### Rav Sheshet's Objection

*Matkif lah Rav Sheshet breih de'Rav Idi*: perhaps the verse does not prove that she may remarry any man she chooses; perhaps the only man permitted to marry her would be the *yavam* — her deceased husband's brother — to whom she has an existing obligation. This is a pointed challenge and one that proves highly relevant to deeper questions about the nature of *yibum*.

### Yavam Not Acher

*Amar Rav Ashi: shtei teshuvot badavar* — there are two answers. First, *yavam lo ikrei acher* — the *yavam* is not called "another" man. This is a significant statement about the nature of *yibum*: the *yavam* is, in some sense, a continuation of or stand-in for his deceased brother, not a new and unrelated third party. The *Rambam*, notably, highlights in the opening *halachot* of *Hilchot Yibum VeChalitzah* that the *ketubah* in *yibum* is paid *al nikhsei ba'ala harishon* — from the estate of the first husband — underscoring the continuity of the original relationship. Whether *yibum* constitutes a new relationship or the continuation of the prior one is a rich question in the *rishonim*.

### Death Like Divorce

The second answer: *ve'od kativ ve'sane'ah ha'ish ha'acharon* — the verse discussing *machzir grushato* (the prohibition against a man remarrying his divorced wife after she has been married to and divorced or widowed from another man) refers to the second husband as *ha'ish ha'acharon*. The verse contemplates that the second husband either divorces her or dies. If he dies, she is then permitted to remarry a third man — proof that death, like divorce, severs the marital bond entirely and permits remarriage. *Ve'hikish mita l'gerushin* — the Torah's juxtaposition of death and divorce in this verse establishes that just as *gerushin* completely severs the relationship and allows her to remarry (*ma gerushin sharya ve'gomar, af mita sharya ve'gomar*), so too death accomplishes the same full severance.

An aside of contemporary halakhic interest: the *rishonim* discuss the case of *Eliyahu HaNavi*, who ascended to heaven in a chariot and did not die in the ordinary sense. What is the status of *Eliyahu*'s wife? This serves as a theoretical construct for exploring the case of someone who has neither died nor is still present among the living. Related questions arise regarding persons who died and returned to life — whether through miraculous revival as recorded in *Tanakh*, or through modern resuscitation such as CPR. The *Pitchei Teshuva* addresses this, and the *Ben Ish Chai* has a *teshuva* distinguishing between *techiyat hameitim al yedei nes* — miraculous revival that might characterize modern medicine — and *techiyat hameitim* in the messianic era, which is categorically different. Whether cardiac death followed by resuscitation would in principle dissolve a marriage is thus at least a *hava amina* in the *poskim*, even if practical ruling consistently forecloses that conclusion.

## Yibbum Mechanism

### Yibbum Not Kesef

The *mishnah* taught: *hayevamah nikneit be'viah* — a *yevamah* is acquired through *biah* alone, and she acquires her freedom through *chalitzah* or the *yavam*'s death. The Gemara now asks: *be'viah minalan?* The question, as the *rishonim* clarify, is not merely how we know *biah* works, but specifically how we know that *kesef* and *shtar* do not work. The only effective mechanism for *yibum* is *biah*, and this requires derivation.

### Yavo Aleha Source

*Amar kra: yevamah yavo aleha ulekachah lo le'ishah* — the verse in *Ki Tetze* states that her brother-in-law will come upon her and take her as his wife. The expression *yavo aleha* is the Torah's euphemism for a sexual encounter. One might have thought that since the verse concludes *ulekachah lo le'ishah*, comparing her to any other wife, all the mechanisms of standard *kiddushin* — *kesef*, *shtar*, and *biah* — would apply. *Rashi* makes this explicit: *dilma ke'ishah, mah ishah kesef ushtar ke'viah, af yevamah kesef ushtar ke'viah* — just as a wife can be acquired through *kesef* and *shtar* in addition to *biah*, perhaps the same is true of the *yevamah* precisely because the verse says *le'ishah*. This is why an explicit exclusionary *drasha* is necessary.

### Veyibmah Redundancy

A *braita* is cited: one might have thought that *kesef* and *shtar* complete the acquisition of the *yevamah* just as *biah* does. *Talmud lomar: veyibmah* — the verse contains an apparently redundant word: after stating *yavo aleha ulekachah lo le'ishah*, it adds *veyibmah*, "and perform *yibum* with her." This redundancy teaches: *biah gomeret bah ve'ein kesef ushtar gomerin bah* — only *biah* completes the acquisition; *kesef* and *shtar* do not.

This reflects a fundamental difference between *yibum* and ordinary *kiddushin*. The mechanisms differ, and more profoundly, the *chalos* — the legal status created — differs. *Yibum* is predicated on a pre-existing relationship; it is a very different kind of bond from *kiddushin*, which brings together two people with no prior legal connection.

### Implicit Consent

The Gemara then asks: perhaps the word *veyibmah* is needed to teach not the exclusion of *kesef* and *shtar*, but rather that *yibum* may be performed *baal karkhah* — without the explicit present consent of the woman? The Gemara resolves this by noting that the verse could have said *veyabem* (masculine verb); the fact that it says *veyibmah* (with the feminine suffix, creating an additional textual redundancy) allows us to derive both principles simultaneously: *shema mina tartei* — we learn two things from the single word. *Biah* is the exclusive mechanism, and *yibum* may proceed *baal karkhah*.

The halakhic understanding of *baal karkhah* here is nuanced. The woman's *da'at* — her consent — at the time of her initial marriage is understood to carry with it an implicit legal acceptance of *yibum* should her husband predecease her without children. She entered marriage knowing the *halacha*; her consent to the marriage therefore encompasses implicit consent to the potential *yibum* obligation. This raises profound questions, discussed at length in the *rishonim* and *acharonim*, about how much voice the woman genuinely has in choosing between *yibum* and *chalitzah*, particularly given the question — debated in the first *perek* of *Yevamot* and in *Bechorot* — of whether we practice *yibum* today at all.

## Chalitzah Release

### Chalitzah Permits Remarr…

The *mishnah* also taught that the *yevamah* acquires her freedom through *chalitzah*. The Gemara asks: *minalan d'chalutzah muteret lehinnase?* — how do we know that a woman who has undergone *chalitzah* is permitted to remarry? One might think that after *chalitzah* she remains unable to remarry anyone: she has been rejected by the *yavam* (or has rejected him), but that alone does not necessarily permit her to marry a stranger. The death of her first husband left strings attached; her only options were *yibum* or *chalitzah*, and it is not self-evident that performing *chalitzah* entirely dissolves the bond and opens the door to any other marriage.

### Shoe Removal Source

The Gemara derives the permission from the verse: *ve'nikra shemo be'Yisrael beit chalutz hana'al* — the *yavam* who submitted to *chalitzah* will be known in Israel as "the house of him whose shoe was removed." From the phrase *shemo be'Yisrael*, the Gemara infers: *keivan shechalaẓ bah na'al, hutrah le'chol Yisrael* — once she removes his shoe, she is permitted to marry any member of *Klal Yisrael*. This does not, of course, include a *kohen*, since a *chalutzah* is prohibited to a *kohen* — though the *rishonim* largely hold that the prohibition of *chalutzah le'kohen* is only *d'rabbanan*, since *chalitzah* resembles but is not identical to *gerushin*.

### Beth Din Jurisdiction

The Gemara immediately challenges this derivation: is the word *Yisrael* in this verse really needed to teach her permission to remarry, or does *Rav Shmuel bar Yehudah* need it to teach something else? *Rav Shmuel bar Yehudah* holds that the word *Yisrael* teaches that *chalitzah* must be performed before a *beit din shel Yisrael* — a Jewish court — and not before a non-Jewish court.

The alternative text discussed, following the *Hagahot HaBach*, reads *beit din shel geirim* rather than *beit din shel ovdei kochavim*. The question of whether converts may serve as *dayanim* for *chalitzah* is significant: it touches on the nature of *chalitzah* as a proceeding — is it a formal legal act requiring a fully qualified *beit din*, or is the *beit din* present primarily as witnesses to a ceremony? The *hava amina* that *chalitzah* might be valid before a non-Jewish court is striking and points to a genuine ambiguity about what kind of authority the *dayanim* present at *chalitzah* are exercising.

This line of reasoning connects to broader questions about civil law in *agunah* cases. Rav Kook, in a *teshuva* dealing with an *agunah* case, made the radical suggestion that at the *d'Oraisa* level, a civil divorce might qualify as a *get pasul*, employing this as one *snif le'hakeil* in a broader *diyun*. Similarly, questions arise about how much weight to give civil marriage or common-law marriage in Jewish legal contexts.

### Multiple Yisrael Uses

The resolution: *trei be'Yisrael kesivi* — the word *Yisrael* appears twice in the *parsha* of *chalitzah*, and therefore we may derive multiple *halachot* from the multiple occurrences.

### Rabbi Tarfon Procedure

A *braita* quoted by *Rabbi Yehuda* teaches that he and others once sat before *Rabbi Tarfon* when a *yevamah* came to perform *chalitzah*. *Rabbi Tarfon* instructed all those present to call out *chalutz hana'al, chalutz hana'al* — "the one whose shoe was removed" — immediately upon the completion of the ceremony. This raises the question of how literally to understand the verse *ve'nikra shemo be'Yisrael beit chalutz hana'al*: is the formal public calling of this name a required act, to be performed right then by those present, or is it merely a figurative description of how he will be known going forward?

### Calling Him by Name

The Gemara resolves this: *hahu mi've'nikra shemo nafka* — that particular *halacha* (the public calling of his name) is derived from the phrase *ve'nikra shemo*, not from the word *Yisrael*. Therefore, all three *halachot* can be derived independently: (1) the *beit din* must be a Jewish court; (2) the *chalutzah* is permitted to remarry any *Yisrael*; and (3) upon completion of the ceremony, those present must call out *chalutz hana'al*.

## Yavam's Death

### Kal Vachomer Logic

The *mishnah* further states that the *yevamah* acquires her freedom through the death of the *yavam*. The Gemara asks: *minalan?* And proposes a *kal vachomer* from *eishet ish*: if an *eishet ish* — a fully married woman whose violation carries the penalty of *chenek* — is released by the death of her husband, then a *yevamah* whose prohibition carries only the lesser penalty of a *lav* (punishable by lashes) should certainly be released by the death of her *yavam*.

### Get vs Chalitzah

The *kal vachomer* is challenged: *mah le'eishet ish she'ken yotzah be'get* — the *eishet ish* is different in that she can be released through a *get*, which is not available to the *yevamah*, who can only be released through *chalitzah*. This asymmetry may undermine the comparison. The response is: *ha nami yotza be'chalitzah* — the *yevamah* too has a release mechanism, namely *chalitzah*, and perhaps *chalitzah* and *get* are sufficiently analogous to preserve the *kal vachomer*.

### Yavam's Dual Role

A deeper challenge: *mah le'eishet ish she'ken oserah matirah* — in the case of *eishet ish*, the one who created the prohibition (the husband) is the same one who releases her (through death or *get*). Can this be compared to the *yevamah*, where at first glance it seems the deceased husband created the prohibition while the *yavam* releases her through *chalitzah*?

*Amar Rav Ashi: ha nami osrah u'matirah* — Rav Ashi resolves this by pointing out that in the case of *yibum* as well, the same party both creates and dissolves the prohibition. The *yavam* himself is the one who creates the *issur*: if the *yavam* did not exist — if there were no brothers — the woman would be permitted to remarry immediately upon her husband's death. It is the very existence of the *yavam* that creates the prohibition of *yevamah lashuk*. Therefore, it is the *yavam* who both creates the *issur* and, through *chalitzah* or *yibum*, dissolves it.

### Reverse Kal Vachomer

The Gemara now explores the mirror image: can we run the *kal vachomer* in reverse? *Vetehei eishet ish yotza be'chalitzah mikal vachomer* — perhaps an *eishet ish* could also be released through *chalitzah*? The reasoning would be: if a *yevamah*, who cannot be released by a *get*, can be released by *chalitzah*, then surely an *eishet ish*, who can be released by a *get*, should also be releasable by *chalitzah*. *Amar kra: sefer kortah ve'ein davar acher kortah* — the Torah specified that only a written document severs the marriage of an *eishet ish*; nothing else can serve that function.

Similarly, one might argue in the other direction: *vetehei yevamah yotza be'get mikal vachomer* — if an *eishet ish*, who cannot be released by *chalitzah*, can be released by a *get*, surely a *yevamah* who can be released by *chalitzah* should also be releasable by a *get*?

### Kacha & La

The Gemara invokes two separate scriptural limitations. By the laws of *chalitzah*, the Torah used the word *kacha* — *kacha ye'aseh la'ish asher lo yivneh et beit achiv* — and *kacha ikuva*: the word *kacha* creates a restriction, limiting the procedure to precisely this form and no other. This would seem to preclude learning from a *kal vachomer* that a *get* could work for a *yevamah*. But can *kacha* truly block a *kal vachomer*?

### Yom Kippur Example

The Gemara addresses whether a term of restriction (*chukkah* or *kacha*) can entirely block a *kal vachomer* inference, and brings the example of *Yom Kippur* and the two goats. The Torah states that only the *goral* — the lottery — designates which goat becomes the *korban chatat* and which is sent to *Azazel*; *kriyat shem* (verbal declaration) does not work for this designation. Yet a *kal vachomer* argument is conceivable: in other cases where a *goral* does not work, *kriyat shem* does work; so where *goral* does work (on *Yom Kippur*), surely *kriyat shem* should work as well. The Torah needed a specific verse — *ve'asahu chatat, hagoral oseh chatat ve'ein hashem oseh chatat* — to exclude this. This proves that even when the Torah employs a limiting term like *chukkah*, the *hava amina* of a *kal vachomer* still arises and must be defeated by an explicit verse. Therefore, *kacha* alone is not sufficient to block the *kal vachomer* that a *get* might work for a *yevamah*; an explicit verse is needed.

That verse is *la* — *ve'chatav lah*, "and he shall write it for her." *La v'lo le'yevamah*: the Torah's use of *lah* specifies that the *get* is written for his wife, not for a *yevamah*. This is the explicit scriptural exclusion that prevents a *get* from working for a *yevamah*.

A student raises the question: is not the word *lah* needed for the *drasha* that a *get* must be written *lishmah* — for the specific woman for whom it is intended? *Trei lah ketivi* — the word appears twice, and therefore both *drashot* can be sustained. A further question: does the second *lah* teach *la v'lo le'yevamah* or *la v'lo lah ve'lachavertah* — that a husband cannot divorce two wives with a single *get*? The Gemara resolves this by turning to the *parsha* of *chalitzah*: *naal, naal — naal ein midi achrina lo* — just as there is a *drasha* that only a written document severs an ordinary marriage (*sefer kortah ve'ein davar acher kortah*), so too in *chalitzah* the Torah specifies the shoe, teaching that only the shoe effects *chalitzah* and nothing else can substitute for it.

The underlying theme of this entire *sugya*, as the discussion makes clear, is the *chachamim*'s insistence on the primacy of *psukim* over *kal vachomer*. *Kal vachomer* is a powerful tool for deriving *halacha*, yet the Gemara works strenuously to ground each rule in explicit scriptural derivation rather than purely logical inference. The reason is that *yibum*, *gerushin*, *eishet ish*, and *amah ivriyah* are not simply variations on a single theme; each is a unique and distinct legal institution, and the Torah's precise language in each context reflects those distinctions. Logical analogy alone cannot safely be used to import rules from one domain into another, because the apparently similar categories are in fact genuinely different.

## Chalitzah Shoe

### Whose Shoe Works

The word *naal* in the *chalitzah* verse implies the *yavam*'s own shoe — *na'alo*. But the *drasha* of *naal* (unmodified) teaches expansively: *naal shel kol adam* — any person's shoe may be used. The *yavam* need not own the shoe; it may be borrowed or provided by the *beit din*.

### Shoe Exclusions

The *braita* provides three exclusions. *Prat le'gadol she'ein yachol lehalech bo* — a shoe so large that the *yavam* cannot walk in it (the image of clown shoes makes the point) is invalid. *Prat le'katan she'eino chofeh rov raglo* — a shoe so small that it does not cover the majority of his foot is likewise invalid. The legal principle of *rubo ke'kulo* (the majority counts as the whole) is operative, though practically speaking a shoe that covers only the majority of the foot would still be uncomfortable; the criterion is a legal one. *Prat le'mesulyah she'ein lo akeiv* — certain sandal types lacking a proper heel cup (effectively, flip-flops) are also excluded.

### Proper Fit

The general principle is *na'alo hara'uy lo* — the shoe must be appropriately fit for him. A flip-flop, lacking the structural cup to hold the heel, is not considered a proper shoe in the relevant halakhic sense.

### Veyibmah Teaches All

*Im kein nechtov kra naal — mai hanaal? Shema mina tartei*: had the Torah written only *naal*, the word *hanaal* (with the definite article, creating apparent redundancy) would be unnecessary. From this redundancy we derive two things: first, that *chalitzah* can only be effected through the shoe and no other mechanism; and second, all the specifications regarding the shoe's fitness and physical requirements are also derived. The detailed laws governing the *chalitzah* shoe are treated at length in *Masechet Yevamot*.

## Eved Ivri Intro

### Three Sources

The *mishnah* now shifts to a new subject: *eved ivri*, the Jewish indentured servant. The laws of *eved ivri* appear three times in the Torah: in *Parshat Mishpatim*, in *Parshat Behar*, and in *Parshat Re'eh* in *Sefer Devarim*. Each passage has a different emphasis and focus. Biblical critics have argued that these three sections reflect different literary sources. Professor Aaron Koller of Yeshiva University has argued persuasively, drawing on both traditional sources and Ancient Near Eastern parallels, that the three sections are entirely compatible, mutually complementary parts of a unified legal system. A recurring failure of modern biblical scholarship, on this view, is to identify apparent tensions and attribute them to source pluralism without adequately examining analogous legal structures in the ancient world, which demonstrate that such complexity is entirely expected within a coherent legal code.

### Two Paths

There are two paths by which a Jew could become an *eved ivri*. The first is *mocher atzmo* — a man in financial distress voluntarily sells himself into indentured servitude. The second is *mecharu beit din* — a *beit din* sells a person as a servant when he has stolen and is too poor to repay the principal (*keren*) of what he stole. The thief can be sold to cover the *keren* but not the *keifel* (the double payment), since the *keifel* is a punitive fine rather than a restitutionary obligation, and one cannot be sold into servitude to pay a penal surcharge.

### Mishnah Overview

The *mishnah* states: *eved ivri nikne be'chesef uve'shtar* — an *eved ivri* is acquired through money or a document — *ve'koneh atzmo be'shanim* — and acquires his freedom through the passage of years (specifically six years, as the verse states *ve'avadcha sheish shanim*), *uve'yovel* — or through the arrival of the Jubilee year, which releases him even mid-term — *uve'giraon kesef* — or through a proportional monetary buyout of the remaining term.

### Amah Ivriyah

The *mishnah* notes that the *amah ha'ivriyah* has a special status. She is a minor girl indentured by her father. (An adult woman apparently has no mechanism to indenture herself; similarly, minor boys have no such track. Only adult men and minor girls have defined paths into *eved ivri* status.) The *amah ha'ivriyah* acquires her freedom also upon the appearance of *simanim* — physical signs of puberty. She has an additional path not available to the adult male: the institution of *yi'ud*, whereby the *adon* or his son may marry her, transforming the indentured arrangement into a marital one. *Tosafot* return to this point repeatedly in comparing the acquisition of an *amah ha'ivriyah* to the mechanisms of marriage, and the comparison proves illuminating throughout the earlier part of the *perek*. From the father's perspective, the arrangement provides an opportunity for his daughter to marry into a prosperous family — a context that must be understood against the backdrop of its time, even as it engages obvious tensions with contemporary sensibilities regarding child labor and child marriage.

### Eved Nirtza

The *mishnah* further mentions the *eved nirtza*, the pierced-ear servant. After six years of service, if the servant declares that he loves his master and does not wish to go free, the *adon* brings him to the doorpost and pierces his ear with an awl. The servant then acquires the status of *eved nirtza*, which is distinct from ordinary *eved ivri* in that it does not expire after six years; instead, the *eved nirtza* is released only at the *yovel* year or upon the death of the *adon*. He cannot buy his freedom out early, and this status can only be acquired by a *mecharu beit din*, not a *mocher atzmo* — a distinction that will be explored further. The *midrash* notes that Rabbi Hertz, in his *Chumash* commentary, observes that the final law in Hammurabi's Code addresses what to do when a slave runs away, while the first law in *Parshat Mishpatim* addresses what to do when a slave wishes to remain — a juxtaposition that reflects the Torah's aspiration that the master's household be so humane and generous that the servant might genuinely prefer to stay.

## Eved Ivri Kinyan

### Non-Jew Case

The Gemara asks: *eved ivri nikna be'chesef — minalan?* How do we know that an *eved ivri* can be acquired with money? The derivation begins, perhaps surprisingly, with the case of a Jew sold to a non-Jew. The *pasuk* in *Parshat Behar* discusses a situation where a Jew has been purchased by a non-Jewish resident (*ger toshav*) and his relatives wish to redeem him: *mikesef miknato* — from the money of his purchase price, implying he was acquired through money in the first place. For a non-Jew, this is not remarkable, since all acquisitions by non-Jews work through *kesef*; the non-Jewish system of *kinyanim* operates primarily on a financial-monetary basis. But this still leaves the primary case — a Jew indentured to a fellow Jew — underived.

### Amah Extension

The Gemara turns to *amah ha'ivriyah*: *ve'hifdah* — a verse in the *parsha* of *amah ha'ivriyah* speaks of redeeming her freedom proportionally (*migra'at pidyonah*), implying she was originally acquired for money. The connection to *kesef* is thus established for the *amah ha'ivriyah*. This is bolstered by the analogy to marriage: just as she may be acquired (*mekadshah be'chesef*) through money for the purpose of *kiddushin*, so too she is acquired (*maknya be'chesef*) through money for the purpose of indenture. But this still does not directly establish the rule for the adult male *eved ivri*.

### Primary Source

For the primary case of an adult male *eved ivri* sold to a fellow Jew, the Gemara turns to *Devarim*: *ki yimacher lecha achicha ha'Ivri o ha'Ivriyah* — when your Jewish brother or sister is sold to you. The verse then says *ve'avadcha sheish shanim* — he shall serve you six years. *Makish eved Ivri le'Ivriyah* — the adult male is explicitly connected in this verse to the *amah ha'ivriyah*, and the rules derived for her (including acquisition by *kesef*) are extended to him. This establishes the *mecharu beit din* case.

### Mocher Atzmo

The *mocher atzmo* case requires separate derivation. The Gemara invokes a *gezeirah shavah*: *yalif sachir sachir* — the word *sachir* (hired worker) appears in the *eved ivri* context both in *Vayikra* (where *Parshat Behar* describes *mocher atzmo*) and in *Devarim* (where the *mecharu beit din* case is described). This verbal analogy equates the two cases, extending the rules of acquisition by *kesef* to *mocher atzmo* as well.

For the view that does not employ the *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir sachir*, the Gemara offers an alternative: *amar kra ve'chi tassig* — a verse in *Parshat Behar* beginning with "if his hand reaches" adds an additional gloss to the prior discussion. *Mosif al inyan rishon, veyilmad elyon mitachton* — the upper passage (the case of a Jew sold to a non-Jew) and the lower passage (the case of a Jew sold to a fellow Jew) appear in the same broader literary unit of *Parshat Behar*, and through contextual proximity — a principle the Gemara describes as *zeh ke'ein semichut parshiyot* — one learns from the other. This is a literary rather than strictly logical principle: two adjacent paragraphs about the same subject illuminate each other. It is looser than a true *heikesh* but functions similarly, and reflects a recognition that the Torah's arrangement of material is itself an interpretive tool.

## Two Types Compared

### Tanna Kamma View

The Gemara presents a *braita* that enumerates the distinctions between *mocher atzmo* and *mecharu beit din* according to the *tanna kamma*. These distinctions are largely rooted in a careful reading of the *pshat* of the relevant *psukim*, and reflect a view that the two categories of *eved ivri* are genuinely different legal institutions, not merely two paths into the same status.

### Six Year Limit

*Hamocher atzmo nimkar le'shesh ve'yater al shesh* — a person who sells himself into indentured servitude may agree to a term longer than six years. Since the arrangement is essentially a voluntary contractual business deal between two parties, the Torah's six-year limit does not apply to him; he may agree to whatever terms he chooses. *Mecharu beit din eino nimkar ela le'shesh* — but a person sold by the *beit din* against his will for theft can only be sold for a maximum of six years. This is a Torah-imposed limit on an involuntary punitive transaction; the Torah specifically circumscribes it with *ve'avadcha sheish shanim*.

### Nirtza Only

*Hamocher atzmo eino nirtza* — a person who voluntarily sold himself cannot have his ear pierced to become an *eved nirtza*. The institution of *eved nirtza* — and the entire emotional and social scenario of the slave who has genuinely found a home in his master's household — applies only to the *mecharu beit din* case, where the servant was thrust into the arrangement against his will and may, over six years, come genuinely to prefer it.

### Severance Gifts

*Mocher atzmo ein ma'anikim lo* — upon release from self-imposed servitude, no severance gifts are provided. *Mecharu beit din ma'anikim lo* — upon the completion of a *beit din*-imposed term, the *adon* is obligated to provide the freed servant with provisions: produce, food, and money (*ha'anek ta'anik lo*). The rationale is clear: the *mecharu beit din* servant was someone who was so poor that he could not even repay stolen goods. At the end of his term, his debt is discharged but he has nothing. Without this endowment, he faces an immediate return to destitution with no resources to restart his life — and perhaps temptation to steal again. The Torah's mandatory severance gift addresses this directly.

### Shfachah Rights

*Mocher atzmo — ein rabo moser lo shifcha kna'anit* — if a man sold himself voluntarily, his *adon* cannot provide him with a *shifcha kna'anit* to marry and produce children who would themselves become *avadim*. *Mecharu beit din — rabo moser lo shifcha kna'anit* — in the *beit din*-imposed case, the *adon* may provide him with a *shifcha kna'anit*, and the children born of that union would be *eved kna'ani*. This is the institution of *yelid bayit* — slaves born in the household — which the Torah explicitly addresses.

The distinction reflects the different legal characters of the two arrangements: *mecharu beit din*, being closer to a form of court-imposed servitude for criminal restitution, carries the fuller range of the Torah's slave laws, including the ability of the *adon* to arrange the servant's domestic life. *Mocher atzmo*, being closer to an employment agreement entered voluntarily, does not carry those features.

### Rabbi Elazar View

*Rabbi Elazar* disagrees with all of these distinctions: *zeh ve'zeh eino nimkar ela le'shesh, zeh ve'zeh nirtza, ve'zeh ve'zeh ma'anikim lo, ve'zeh ve'zeh rabo moser lo shifcha kna'anit* — both categories are subject to a maximum six-year term, both can become *eved nirtza*, both receive severance gifts, and both may be provided with a *shifcha kna'anit*. *Rabbi Elazar* collapses the distinction between *mocher atzmo* and *mecharu beit din*, treating *eved ivri* as a single unified institution.

### Amoraim Resolution

The *Amoraim* initially assumed that the *machloket* between the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Elazar* reflects a disagreement about whether to apply the *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir sachir*. On this reading, the *tanna kamma* does not apply the *gezeirah shavah* and therefore sees the two *psukim* — in *Vayikra* and *Devarim* — as addressing genuinely different cases (*mocher atzmo* and *mecharu beit din* respectively), each with its own distinct rules. *Rabbi Elazar*, who does apply the *gezeirah shavah*, equates the two and sees a unified system.

*Amar Rav Tavyumi mishmeih de'Abaye*: the *Amoraim* reject this framework. In fact, both *tannaim* agree that the *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir sachir* is operative. Their dispute lies elsewhere — in the interpretation of specific verses. According to the *tanna kamma*, the verse *ve'avadcha sheish shanim* limits only *mecharu beit din*, not *mocher atzmo* (*zeh ve'lo mocher atzmo*). *Rabbi Elazar* reads *ve'avadcha lecha ve'lo le'yoresh* — the verse teaches that the *eved ivri*'s obligation runs to the specific *adon* and does not transfer to his heirs — rather than imposing the six-year limit exclusively on *mecharu beit din*.

Additional distinctions in the *braita* — such as *eved ivri* service not being inherited by the *adon*'s son — will continue to be worked through in the following session, with the Gemara tracing how each *tanna* derives or does not derive each particular rule.
