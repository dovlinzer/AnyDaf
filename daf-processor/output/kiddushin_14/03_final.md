# Kiddushin 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Context

### Opening Remarks

Dan Margulis is filling in for Rabbi Linzer. By way of introduction, he notes that it is *shoshon purim*, the fifteenth of Adar, which is, by the well-known principle, *shloshim yom kodem hachag* — thirty days before Pesach — the time when we begin *sho'alin vedorshin b'hilchot hapesach*. Though the tractate under study is *Kiddushin*, the spirit of Pesach is present: the essential act of Pesach is asking questions, and that spirit animates the entire enterprise of Talmud study. The sugya before us today is on 13b.

### Eternal Marriage Polemic

The sugya at hand addresses how we know that a husband's death permits his widow to remarry. This is not merely a technical halakhic question; it carries polemical weight against theological traditions — certain of them still present today — that maintain the concept of an eternal marriage bond persisting even after one spouse's death. The Gemara's sustained attention to deriving this permission from scriptural sources reflects a serious halakhic and philosophical commitment to establishing that death fully dissolves the marital bond.

## Widow Remarriage

### Kohen Gadol Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל – אֲסוּרָה, הָא לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט שַׁרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states** that **a widow is forbidden to a High Priest.** This indicates that it is **permitted** for her **to** marry **a common priest,** and the same certainly applies to a non-priest.

The Gemara proposes that we may derive the widow's permission to remarry from the special prohibition applying to the *kohen gadol*: *ela mide'amar rachmana almanah le'kohen gadol asura ha le'kohen hedyot sharya* — the Torah specifically prohibits the *kohen gadol* from marrying an *almanah*, which implies that a regular *kohen* may do so, and therefore an *almanah* is in general permitted to remarry. The only one absolutely prohibited from marrying a widow would be the *kohen gadol*.

### Mitzvat Asei Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּלָאו, לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בַּעֲשֵׂה? הַאי עֲשֵׂה מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? אִי דְּאַהַנְיָא מִיתַת הַבַּעַל – תִּישְׁתְּרֵי לִגְמָרֵי, אִי דְּלָא אַהַנְיָא מִיתַת הַבַּעַל – תּוֹקְמַהּ בְּמִילְּתָא קַמַּיְיתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But perhaps** one can say that she is forbidden **to a High Priest by a prohibition,** whereas she is forbidden **to everyone else** only **by** dint of **a positive mitzva.** The Gemara asks: **This positive mitzva, what is its purpose? If the death of the husband affects** her status, **she should be entirely permitted,** and **if the death of the husband does not affect** her status **she remains in her initial state** when her husband was alive, and therefore those who engage in sexual intercourse with her would be liable to receive the death penalty.

The Gemara immediately challenges this derivation. Perhaps the prohibition for the *kohen gadol* is a *lav*, while for all others — *Yisraelim*, regular *kohanim*, and everyone else — there remains an *issur asei* derived from the verse *vedavak be'ishto*, the idea that one is bound by a positive commandment to be joined with one's wife. The *Ri* in *Tosafot* raises precisely this possibility: perhaps even after the husband's death, some residual *mitzvat asei* prohibits another man from marrying the widow. This is framed as the Gemara's *hava amina* — a position that will ultimately be rejected, but whose very consideration underscores how counterintuitive it would be to require explicit derivation for something as seemingly obvious as a widow's right to remarry. As the Gemara notes, the entire *shakla vetarya* here is designed to push against that intuition and demonstrate that the permission is in fact well-grounded in *psukim*.

### Lav vs Asei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה לָא? אַפִּיקְתַּהּ מִמִּיתָה וְאוֹקֵימְתַּהּ עַל עֲשֵׂה, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַפְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁים, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא אִית בְּהוּ מְעִילָה, וַאֲסִירִי בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה. פַּרְקִינְהוּ – מְעִילָה לֵית בְּהוּ, בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה אֲסִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** And **why** is this suggestion **not** considered reasonable? One could argue that the death of the husband **removed her from** the class of people with whom one is punished with **death** for engaging in intercourse, **and placed her in** the class of people with whom one is prohibited to engage in intercourse due to **a positive mitzva, just as it is with consecrated** animals **that have been disqualified.** This is referring to animals that were designated as offerings but became invalid due to some blemish. The *halakha* with regard to such animals is **that initially,** when they are unblemished, **they are subject** to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** i.e., if one benefited from them he has misused consecrated items. **And** likewise **shearing** them and performing **labor** with them **is prohibited.** Once these animals have developed a blemish and **one has redeemed them,** the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated items **does not apply to them,** but **shearing** them and performing **labor** with them remains **prohibited.**

The Gemara probes what this hypothetical *mitzvat asei* would actually accomplish. *Hai asei mai avid lei* — what does this *asei* do? Either *mitat habaal* completely releases her (*tishari legamri*), or it does not release her at all and she remains in her prior status (*tukma be'milta kamaisa*). The problem is that a *lav* and an *asei* operating together cannot produce a clean intermediate result. *Rashi* explains that if such an *issur asei* existed for everyone, there would be no reason for the *kohen gadol*'s prohibition to be expressed as the more severe *lav*.

### Death Like Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״פֶּן יָמוּת בַּמִּלְחָמָה וְאִישׁ אַחֵר יִקָּחֶנָּה״. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: אֵימָא, מַאן אַחֵר – יָבָם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the source that a widow may remarry is **from that** which **the verse states: "Lest he die in battle and another man take her"** (Deuteronomy 20:7). This indicates that a man may marry a widow. **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, objects to this:** One can **say: Who is** this **other** man mentioned by the verse? It is **a *yavam*.**

The Gemara then entertains a further refinement: perhaps *mitat habaal* removes her from the category of capital crime (*chiyuv mitat beit din*) but leaves intact an *issur asei*. The analogy offered is *psulei hamukdashim* — animals consecrated for sacrifice that subsequently became disqualified by a permanent blemish. Before the blemish, there is a prohibition of *me'ilah* (using consecrated property) as well as prohibitions against shearing or using the animal for labor. Once an act of *me'ilah* occurs under certain circumstances, the *kedushah* is removed and *me'ilah* no longer applies — yet the prohibitions of shearing and labor (*giza va'avoda*) remain. Similarly, one might think that the husband's death removes the *issur* punishable by *chenek*, but an *issur asei* persists.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁתֵּי תְּשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר: חֲדָא, דְּיָבָם לָא אִיקְּרִי אַחֵר. וְעוֹד: כְּתִיב: ״וּשְׂנֵאָהּ הָאִישׁ הָאַחֲרוֹן וְכָתַב לָהּ סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת״ ״אוֹ כִי יָמוּת הָאִישׁ הָאַחֲרוֹן״, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ מִיתָה לְגֵירוּשִׁין – מָה גֵּירוּשִׁין שַׁרְיָא וְגוֹמֶרֶת, אַף מִיתָה שַׁרְיָא וְגוֹמֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that there are **two refutations of** this **statement: First, a *yavam* is not called "another,"** as his relationship with her is not entirely separate from that of the first husband. **And furthermore, it is written: "And the latter husband hates her, and writes her a scroll of severance and gives it in her hand and sends her out of his house, or if the latter husband dies"** (Deuteronomy 24:3). In this manner the verse **juxtaposes death to divorce: Just as divorce permits** a woman to everyone **and completes** the severing of a husband from his wife, thereby allowing her to marry another man, **so too, death permits** her **and completes** the severing, allowing her to marry another man.

This analogy is ultimately rejected, and the Gemara arrives at a real *pasuk*: *pen yamut bamilchama ve'ish acher yikachena* — from the section of those exempt from military service, the Torah anticipates that a soldier recently betrothed might die in battle, and another man will marry her. The word *yikach* is recognized as language of marriage (*ki yikach ish isha*), proving incidentally that another man may marry her after the first husband's death. This *drasha*, however, applies only to *erusin*, not *nisuin*, which will require separate derivation.

### Rav Sheshet's Objection

*Matkif lah Rav Sheshet breih de'Rav Idi*: perhaps the verse does not prove that she may remarry any man she chooses; perhaps the only man permitted to marry her would be the *yavam* — her deceased husband's brother — to whom she has an existing obligation. This is a pointed challenge and one that proves highly relevant to deeper questions about the nature of *yibum*.

### Yavam Not Acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁתֵּי תְּשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר: חֲדָא, דְּיָבָם לָא אִיקְּרִי אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that there are **two refutations of** this **statement: First, a *yavam* is not called "another,"** as his relationship with her is not entirely separate from that of the first husband.

*Amar Rav Ashi: shtei teshuvot badavar* — there are two answers. First, *yavam lo ikrei acher* — the *yavam* is not called "another" man. This is a significant statement about the nature of *yibum*: the *yavam* is, in some sense, a continuation of or stand-in for his deceased brother, not a new and unrelated third party. The *Rambam*, notably, highlights in the opening *halachot* of *Hilchot Yibum VeChalitzah* that the *ketubah* in *yibum* is paid *al nikhsei ba'ala harishon* — from the estate of the first husband — underscoring the continuity of the original relationship. Whether *yibum* constitutes a new relationship or the continuation of the prior one is a rich question in the *rishonim*.

### Death Like Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: כְּתִיב: ״וּשְׂנֵאָהּ הָאִישׁ הָאַחֲרוֹן וְכָתַב לָהּ סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת״ ״אוֹ כִי יָמוּת הָאִישׁ הָאַחֲרוֹן״, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ מִיתָה לְגֵירוּשִׁין – מָה גֵּירוּשִׁין שַׁרְיָא וְגוֹמֶרֶת, אַף מִיתָה שַׁרְיָא וְגוֹמֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, it is written: "And the latter husband hates her, and writes her a scroll of severance and gives it in her hand and sends her out of his house, or if the latter husband dies"** (Deuteronomy 24:3). In this manner the verse **juxtaposes death to divorce: Just as divorce permits** a woman to everyone **and completes** the severing of a husband from his wife, thereby allowing her to marry another man, **so too, death permits** her **and completes** the severing, allowing her to marry another man.

The second answer: *ve'od kativ ve'sane'ah ha'ish ha'acharon* — the verse discussing *machzir grushato* (the prohibition against a man remarrying his divorced wife after she has been married to and divorced or widowed from another man) refers to the second husband as *ha'ish ha'acharon*. The verse contemplates that the second husband either divorces her or dies. If he dies, she is then permitted to remarry a third man — proof that death, like divorce, severs the marital bond entirely and permits remarriage. *Ve'hikesh mita l'gerushin* — the Torah's juxtaposition of death and divorce in this verse establishes that just as *gerushin* completely severs the relationship and allows her to remarry (*ma gerushin sharya ve'gomar, af mita sharya ve'gomar*), so too death accomplishes the same full severance.

An aside of contemporary halakhic interest: the *rishonim* discuss the case of *Eliyahu HaNavi*, who ascended to heaven in a chariot and did not die in the ordinary sense. What is the status of *Eliyahu*'s wife? This serves as a theoretical construct for exploring the case of someone who has neither died nor is still present among the living. Related questions arise regarding persons who died and returned to life — whether through miraculous revival as recorded in *Tanakh*, or through modern resuscitation such as CPR. The *Pitchei Teshuva* addresses this, and the *Ben Ish Chai* has a *teshuva* distinguishing between *techiyat hameitim al yedei nes* — miraculous revival that might characterize modern medicine — and *techiyat hameitim* in the messianic era, which is categorically different. Whether cardiac death followed by resuscitation would in principle dissolve a marriage is thus at least a *hava amina* in the *poskim*, even if practical ruling consistently forecloses that conclusion.

## Yibbum Mechanism

### Yibbum Not Kesef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְּבָמָה נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה כּוּ׳. בְּבִיאָה מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״יְבָמָהּ יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ וּלְקָחָהּ לוֹ לְאִשָּׁה״. וְאֵימָא, לְכוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא כְּאִשָּׁה! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהוּ כֶּסֶף וּשְׁטָר גּוֹמְרִים בָּהּ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַבִּיאָה גּוֹמֶרֶת בָּהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״ – בִּיאָה גּוֹמֶרֶת בָּהּ וְאֵין כֶּסֶף וּשְׁטָר גּוֹמְרִים בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And a *yevama*** can be **acquired** by the deceased husband's brother, the *yavam*, only **through intercourse.** And she acquires herself through *ḥalitza* or through the death of the *yavam*. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that she can be acquired **through intercourse? The verse states: "Her brother-in-law will engage in sexual intercourse with her and take her to him as a wife,** and perform levirate marriage with her"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), which indicates that intercourse renders her his wife. The Gemara asks: **But** one can **say** that **with regard to all matters** she is **like a wife,** and therefore she can also be acquired with money or a document like any other woman. The Gemara answers: It could **not enter your mind** to say this, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **money and a document can complete** the acquisition of a *yevama* and cause her to be married, **in the manner that intercourse completes** her acquisition. Therefore, **the verse states: "And perform levirate marriage with her"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), which indicates that only **intercourse completes her** acquisition, **but money or a document do not complete her** acquisition.

The *mishnah* taught: *hayevamah nikneit be'viah* — a *yevamah* is acquired through *biah* alone, and she acquires her freedom through *chalitzah* or the *yavam*'s death. The Gemara now asks: *be'viah minalan?* The question, as the *rishonim* clarify, is not merely how we know *biah* works, but specifically how we know that *kesef* and *shtar* do not work. The only effective mechanism for *yibum* is *biah*, and this requires derivation.

### Yavo Aleha Source

One might have thought that since the verse concludes *ulekachah lo le'ishah*, comparing her to any other wife, all the mechanisms of standard *kiddushin* — *kesef*, *shtar*, and *biah* — would apply. *Rashi* makes this explicit: *dilma ke'ishah, mah ishah kesef ushtar ke'viah, af yevamah kesef ushtar ke'viah* — just as a wife can be acquired through *kesef* and *shtar* in addition to *biah*, perhaps the same is true of the *yevamah* precisely because the verse says *le'ishah*. This is why an explicit exclusionary *drasha* is necessary.

### Veyibmah Redundancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: מַאי ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״? דִּבְעַל כֻּרְחַהּ מְיַבֵּם. אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״וְיִבֵּם״, מַאי ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one can **say: What** is the meaning of: **"And perform levirate marriage with her"?** It teaches **that he can perform levirate marriage without her consent,** as unlike an ordinary marriage, a levirate marriage does not require the woman's agreement. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse say: And perform levirate marriage,** and one would understand that the matter depends entirely on him. **What is** the meaning of: **"And perform levirate marriage with her"? Learn two** conclusions **from this:** First, that a levirate marriage can be performed against her will, and second, that the only way to acquire a *yevama* is through sexual intercourse.

A *braita* is cited: one might have thought that *kesef* and *shtar* complete the acquisition of the *yevamah* just as *biah* does. *Talmud lomar: veyibmah* — the verse contains an apparently redundant word: after stating *yavo aleha ulekachah lo le'ishah*, it adds *veyibmah*, "and perform *yibum* with her." This redundancy teaches: *biah gomeret bah ve'ein kesef ushtar gomerin bah* — only *biah* completes the acquisition; *kesef* and *shtar* do not.

This reflects a fundamental difference between *yibum* and ordinary *kiddushin*. The mechanisms differ, and more profoundly, the *chalos* — the legal status created — differs. *Yibum* is predicated on a pre-existing relationship; it is a very different kind of bond from *kiddushin*, which brings together two people with no prior legal connection.

### Implicit Consent

The Gemara then asks: perhaps the word *veyibmah* is needed to teach not the exclusion of *kesef* and *shtar*, but rather that *yibum* may be performed *baal karkhah* — without the explicit present consent of the woman? The Gemara resolves this by noting that the verse could have said *veyabem* (masculine verb); the fact that it says *veyibmah* (with the feminine suffix, creating an additional textual redundancy) allows us to derive both principles simultaneously: *shema mina tartei* — we learn two things from the single word. *Biah* is the exclusive mechanism, and *yibum* may proceed *baal karkhah*.

The halakhic understanding of *baal karkhah* here is nuanced. The woman's *da'at* — her consent — at the time of her initial marriage is understood to carry with it an implicit legal acceptance of *yibum* should her husband predecease her without children. She entered marriage knowing the *halacha*; her consent to the marriage therefore encompasses implicit consent to the potential *yibum* obligation. This raises profound questions, discussed at length in the *rishonim* and *acharonim*, about how much voice the woman genuinely has in choosing between *yibum* and *chalitzah*, particularly given the question — debated in the first *perek* of *Yevamot* and in *Bechorot* — of whether we practice *yibum* today at all.

## Chalitzah Release

### Chalitzah Permits Remarriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲלִיצָה. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּית חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁחָלַץ בָּהּ נַעַל, הוּתְּרָה לְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the *yevama* acquires herself **through *ḥalitza*.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **As it is written** with regard to levirate marriage, after the *yavam* has performed *ḥalitza*: **"And his name shall be called in Israel: The house of him who had his shoe removed"** (Deuteronomy 25:10). This indicates: **Once** his **shoe is removed by her, she is permitted to all of Israel.**

The *mishnah* also taught that the *yevamah* acquires her freedom through *chalitzah*. The Gemara asks: *minalan d'chalutzah muteret lehinnase?* — how do we know that a woman who has undergone *chalitzah* is permitted to remarry? One might think that after *chalitzah* she remains unable to remarry anyone: she has been rejected by the *yavam* (or has rejected him), but that alone does not necessarily permit her to marry a stranger. The death of her first husband left strings attached; her only options were *yibum* or *chalitzah*, and it is not self-evident that performing *chalitzah* entirely dissolves the bond and opens the door to any other marriage.

### Shoe Removal Source

The Gemara derives the permission from the verse: *ve'nikra shemo be'Yisrael beit chalutz hana'al* — the *yavam* who submitted to *chalitzah* will be known in Israel as "the house of him whose shoe was removed." From the phrase *shemo be'Yisrael*, the Gemara infers: *keivan shechalaẓ bah na'al, hutrah le'chol Yisrael* — once she removes his shoe, she is permitted to marry any member of *Klal Yisrael*. This does not, of course, include a *kohen*, since a *chalutzah* is prohibited to a *kohen* — though the *rishonim* largely hold that the prohibition of *chalutzah le'kohen* is only *d'rabbanan*, since *chalitzah* resembles but is not identical to *gerushin*.

### Beth Din Jurisdiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״יִשְׂרָאֵל״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה: ״בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״ – בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלֹא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל נׇכְרִים! תְּרֵי ״בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״ כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** term **"Israel," does it come** to teach **this** *halakha*? **It is required for that which is taught** by **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda:** The verse says **"in Israel"** to teach that *ḥalitza* may be performed only **in a Jewish court, but not in a gentile court.** The Gemara answers that the phrase **"in Israel" is written twice** in that chapter: "And his name shall be called in Israel" (Deuteronomy 25:10), and: "To establish a name for his brother in Israel" (Deuteronomy 25:7). A different *halakha* is derived from each of these instances.

The Gemara immediately challenges this derivation: is the word *Yisrael* in this verse really needed to teach her permission to remarry, or does *Rav Shmuel bar Yehudah* need it to teach something else? *Rav Shmuel bar Yehudah* holds that the word *Yisrael* teaches that *chalitzah* must be performed before a *beit din shel Yisrael* — a Jewish court — and not before a non-Jewish court.

The alternative text discussed, following the *Hagahot HaBach*, reads *beit din shel geirim* rather than *beit din shel ovdei kochavim*. The question of whether converts may serve as *dayanim* for *chalitzah* is significant: it touches on the nature of *chalitzah* as a proceeding — is it a formal legal act requiring a fully qualified *beit din*, or is the *beit din* present primarily as witnesses to a ceremony? The *hava amina* that *chalitzah* might be valid before a non-Jewish court is striking and points to a genuine ambiguity about what kind of authority the *dayanim* present at *chalitzah* are exercising.

This line of reasoning connects to broader questions about civil law in *agunah* cases. Rav Kook, in a *teshuva* dealing with an *agunah* case, made the radical suggestion that at the *d'Oraisa* level, a civil divorce might qualify as a *get pasul*, employing this as one *snif le'hakeil* in a broader *diyun*. Similarly, questions arise about how much weight to give civil marriage or common-law marriage in Jewish legal contexts.

### Multiple Yisrael Uses

The resolution: *trei be'Yisrael kesivi* — the word *Yisrael* appears twice in the *parsha* of *chalitzah*, and therefore we may derive multiple *halachot* from the multiple occurrences.

### Rabbi Tarfon Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִינוּ יוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, וּבָאתָה יְבָמָה לַחְלוֹץ, וְאָמַר לָנוּ: עֲנוּ כּוּלְּכֶם וְאִמְרוּ: ״חֲלוּץ הַנַּעַל, חֲלוּץ הַנַּעַל״. הָהוּא, מִ״וְּנִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still, we** also **require** the additional term "in Israel" **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*, that **Rabbi Yehuda said: Once we were sitting before Rabbi Tarfon and a *yevama* came to perform *ḥalitza*. And** Rabbi Tarfon **said to us** after the *ḥalitza*: **All of you** should **answer and say: The one whose shoe was removed, the one whose shoe was removed.** This indicates that the phrase: "And his name shall be called in Israel" (Deuteronomy 25:10), teaches that the matter must be announced and publicized throughout the nation. The Gemara answers: **That** *halakha* is not derived from the phrase "in Israel." Rather, **it is derived from: "And his name shall be called,"** which indicates that it must be announced that this is his name.

A *braita* quoted by *Rabbi Yehuda* teaches that he and others once sat before *Rabbi Tarfon* when a *yevamah* came to perform *chalitzah*. *Rabbi Tarfon* instructed all those present to call out *chalutz hana'al, chalutz hana'al* — "the one whose shoe was removed" — immediately upon the completion of the ceremony. This raises the question of how literally to understand the verse *ve'nikra shemo be'Yisrael beit chalutz hana'al*: is the formal public calling of this name a required act, to be performed right then by those present, or is it merely a figurative description of how he will be known going forward?

### Calling Him by Name

The Gemara resolves this: *hahu mi've'nikra shemo nafka* — that particular *halacha* (the public calling of his name) is derived from the phrase *ve'nikra shemo*, not from the word *Yisrael*. Therefore, all three *halachot* can be derived independently: (1) the *beit din* must be a Jewish court; (2) the *chalutzah* is permitted to remarry any *Yisrael*; and (3) upon completion of the ceremony, those present must call out *chalutz hana'al*.

## Yavam's Death

### Kal Vachomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמִיתַת הַיָּבָם. מְנָלַן? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ שֶׁהִיא בְּחֶנֶק – מִיתַת הַבַּעַל מַתִּירָתָהּ, יְבָמָהּ שֶׁהִיא בְּלָאו – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches that a *yevama* is released from levirate marriage and may remarry **through the death of the *yavam*.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers that it is **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If,** with regard to **a married woman, who is** forbidden to other men by a prohibition punished **with strangulation,** and yet **the death of her husband renders her permitted,** is **it not all the more so** that with regard to **a *yevama*, who is** forbidden to other men while the *yavam* is alive **by** only a mere **prohibition,** that the death of the *yavam* should render her permitted?

The *mishnah* further states that the *yevamah* acquires her freedom through the death of the *yavam*. The Gemara asks: *minalan?* And proposes a *kal vachomer* from *eishet ish*: if an *eishet ish* — a fully married woman whose violation carries the penalty of *chenek* — is released by the death of her husband, then a *yevamah* whose prohibition carries only the lesser penalty of a *lav* (punishable by lashes) should certainly be released by the death of her *yavam*.

### Get vs Chalitzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, שֶׁכֵּן יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט, תֹּאמַר בְּזוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט! הָא נָמֵי יוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this *a fortiori* inference: **What** is unique **about a married woman** is **that she can leave through a bill of divorce** as well. Will **you say** the same **with regard to this** *yevama*, **who does not leave through a bill of divorce?** The Gemara answers: This is an insufficient refutation of the *a fortiori* inference, as **this** *yevama* **also leaves through *ḥalitza*,** which serves the same function as a bill of divorce.

The *kal vachomer* is challenged: *mah le'eishet ish she'ken yotzah be'get* — the *eishet ish* is different in that she can be released through a *get*, which is not available to the *yevamah*, who can only be released through *chalitzah*. This asymmetry may undermine the comparison. The response is: *ha nami yotza be'chalitzah* — the *yevamah* too has a release mechanism, namely *chalitzah*, and perhaps *chalitzah* and *get* are sufficiently analogous to preserve the *kal vachomer*.

### Yavam's Dual Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מָה לְאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ שֶׁכֵּן אוֹסְרָהּ מַתִּירָהּ! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָא נָמֵי אוֹסְרָהּ מַתִּירָהּ – יָבָם אוֹסְרָהּ, יָבָם שָׁרֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one can challenge this *a fortiori* inference in the following manner. **What** is unique **about a married woman** is **that** the one who **renders her forbidden** to everyone else, i.e., her husband, also **renders her permitted** when he dies. This is not so with regard to a *yevama*, as she is rendered forbidden upon the death of her husband. **Rav Ashi said:** With regard to **this** *yevama* **as well,** he who **renders her forbidden** is also the one who **renders her permitted:** The ***yavam* renders her forbidden,** because if he was not alive she would automatically be permitted to other men. Likewise, the ***yavam* renders her permitted** through *ḥalitza*.

A deeper challenge: *mah le'eishet ish she'ken oserah matirah* — in the case of *eishet ish*, the one who created the prohibition (the husband) is the same one who releases her (through death or *get*). Can this be compared to the *yevamah*, where at first glance it seems the deceased husband created the prohibition while the *yavam* releases her through *chalitzah*?

*Amar Rav Ashi: ha nami osrah u'matirah* — Rav Ashi resolves this by pointing out that in the case of *yibum* as well, the same party both creates and dissolves the prohibition. The *yavam* himself is the one who creates the *issur*: if the *yavam* did not exist — if there were no brothers — the woman would be permitted to remarry immediately upon her husband's death. It is the very existence of the *yavam* that creates the prohibition of *yevamah lashuk*. Therefore, it is the *yavam* who both creates the *issur* and, through *chalitzah* or *yibum*, dissolves it.

### Reverse Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְהֵא אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ יוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה יְבָמָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט – יוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה, זוֹ, שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת״ – סֵפֶר כּוֹרְתָהּ, וְאֵין דָּבָר אַחֵר כּוֹרְתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let a married woman leave** a marriage **through *ḥalitza*,** as derived **by an *a fortiori*** inference: **If a *yevama*, who cannot leave through a bill of divorce, can leave through *ḥalitza*, is it not logical** that **this** married woman, **who can leave through a bill of divorce, can** likewise **leave through *ḥalitza*?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to a married woman: **"A scroll of severance"** (Deuteronomy 24:3), which indicates that **a scroll,** i.e., a written document, **severs her** from her husband **and nothing else severs her** from him. While the husband is alive only a bill of divorce can dissolve a marriage.

The Gemara now explores the mirror image: can we run the *kal vachomer* in reverse? *Vetehei eishet ish yotza be'chalitzah mikal vachomer* — perhaps an *eishet ish* could also be released through *chalitzah*? The reasoning would be: if a *yevamah*, who cannot be released by a *get*, can be released by *chalitzah*, then surely an *eishet ish*, who can be released by a *get*, should also be releasable by *chalitzah*. *Amar kra: sefer kortah ve'ein davar acher kortah* — the Torah specified that only a written document severs the marriage of an *eishet ish*; nothing else can serve that function.

Similarly, one might argue in the other direction: *vetehei yevamah yotza be'get mikal vachomer* — if an *eishet ish*, who cannot be released by *chalitzah*, can be released by a *get*, surely a *yevamah* who can be released by *chalitzah* should also be releasable by a *get*?

### Kacha & La

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְהֵא יְבָמָה יוֹצֵאת בְּגֵט מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ שֶׁאֵין יוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה – יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט, זוֹ שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה – אֵין דִּין שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כָּכָה״, וְ״כָכָה״ עִיכּוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And let a *yevama* leave through a bill of divorce,** as derived **by an *a fortiori*** inference: **If a married woman, who cannot leave through *ḥalitza*, can leave through a bill of divorce, is it not logical** that **this** *yevama*, **who can leave through *ḥalitza*, can** also **leave through a bill of divorce?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "So** shall it be done to the man" (Deuteronomy 25:9), with regard to the *ḥalitza* of a *yevama*, **and "so" precludes** another option.

The Gemara invokes two separate scriptural limitations. By the laws of *chalitzah*, the Torah used the word *kacha* — *kacha ye'aseh la'ish asher lo yivneh et beit achiv* — and *kacha ikuva*: the word *kacha* creates a restriction, limiting the procedure to precisely this form and no other. This would seem to preclude learning from a *kal vachomer* that a *get* could work for a *yevamah*. But can *kacha* truly block a *kal vachomer*?

### Yom Kippur Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא עִיכּוּבָא לָא דָּרְשִׁי קַל וָחוֹמֶר? וְהָא יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים דִּכְתִיב: ״גּוֹרָל״, וְ״חוּקָּה״, וְתַנְיָא: ״וְעָשָׂהוּ חַטָּאת״ – הַגּוֹרָל עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת, וְאֵין הַשֵּׁם עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it true that **anywhere that there is** a term that **precludes** another option **one cannot learn an *a fortiori*** inference? **But there is** the case of the designation of the goats on **Yom Kippur, as it is written:** "The goat upon which the **lot** came up for the Lord" (Leviticus 16:9), **and:** "This shall be an everlasting **statute** to you" (Leviticus 16:34), which indicates that everything stated with regard to the mitzvot of Yom Kippur is critical to the performance of the service of the day. **And it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: "Aaron shall bring forward the goat upon which the lot came up for the Lord, **and he shall offer it for a sin-offering"** (Leviticus 16:9). The verse indicates that **the lottery renders** it **a sin-offering,** but **a verbal designation** of the goat with the status of a sin-offering **does not render** it **a sin-offering.**

The Gemara addresses whether a term of restriction (*chukkah* or *kacha*) can entirely block a *kal vachomer* inference, and brings the example of *Yom Kippur* and the two goats. The Torah states that only the *goral* — the lottery — designates which goat becomes the *korban chatat* and which is sent to *Azazel*; *kriyat shem* (verbal declaration) does not work for this designation. Yet a *kal vachomer* argument is conceivable: in other cases where a *goral* does not work, *kriyat shem* does work; so where *goral* does work (on *Yom Kippur*), surely *kriyat shem* should work as well. The Torah needed a specific verse — *ve'asahu chatat, hagoral oseh chatat ve'ein hashem oseh chatat* — to exclude this. This proves that even when the Torah employs a limiting term like *chukkah*, the *hava amina* of a *kal vachomer* still arises and must be defeated by an explicit verse. Therefore, *kacha* alone is not sufficient to block the *kal vachomer* that a *get* might work for a *yevamah*; an explicit verse is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא קִידֵּשׁ הַגּוֹרָל – קִידֵּשׁ הַשֵּׁם, מְקוֹם שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ הַגּוֹרָל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּקַדֵּשׁ הַשֵּׁם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָשָׂהוּ חַטָּאת״ – הַגּוֹרָל עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת וְאֵין הַשֵּׁם עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת. וְטַעְמָא דְּמַעֲטֵיהּ קְרָא, הָא לָאו הָכִי דָּרְשִׁינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר, אַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״חוּקָּה״!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: A verse is necessary to teach this *halakha*, **as** one **might** have thought that the opposite conclusion is correct: Could this **not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference,** as follows: **Just as in a case in which** the use of **a lottery does not effect the consecration** of the animals with a specific designation, e.g., with regard to a woman who has given birth and must bring two birds, one as a sin-offering and one as a burnt-offering, and nevertheless **a verbal designation** of the animals with the required status **does effect the consecration** of them, so too, in **a case in which** the use of **a lottery does effect the consecration** of the animals, **is it not** logically **right that a verbal designation** of the animals with the required status **should effect the consecration** of them? To counter this reasoning, **the verse states** with regard to the goat: **"He shall offer it for a sin-offering"** (Leviticus 16:9), to indicate that **the lottery renders** it **a sin-offering, but a verbal designation** of the goat with the status of a sin-offering **does not render** it **a sin-offering. And the reason** that a verbal designation is ineffective **is that the verse excluded** this possibility, from which it may be inferred **that if it were not so, one would learn an *a fortiori*** inference, **even though it is written with regard to it "statute."** The same logic should apply in the case of *ḥalitza*, i.e., the term "so" should not prevent one from learning an *a fortiori* inference, and therefore it might be argued that a *yevama* can be released through a bill of divorce.

That verse is *la* — *ve'chatav lah*, "and he shall write it for her." *La v'lo le'yevamah*: the Torah's use of *lah* specifies that the *get* is written for his wife, not for a *yevamah*. This is the explicit scriptural exclusion that prevents a *get* from working for a *yevamah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״לָהּ״, ״לָהּ״ – וְלֹא לִיבָמָהּ. וְאֵימָא ״לָהּ״ לִשְׁמָהּ! תְּרֵי ״לָהּ״ כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, the Gemara explains that here too, there is a limitation derived from a verse. **The verse states** with regard to a bill of divorce: "And write **for her** a scroll of severance" (Deuteronomy 24:3). **"For her"** is a limitation, which teaches: A bill of divorce is effective for a regular woman **but not for a *yevama*.** The Gemara asks: **But** one can **say** that the phrase **"for her"** indicates that the bill of divorce must be written **for her sake.** The Gemara answers: **"For her" is written twice** (Deuteronomy 24:1, 3), and therefore both *halakhot* can be derived.

A student raises the question: is not the word *lah* needed for the *drasha* that a *get* must be written *lishmah* — for the specific woman for whom it is intended? *Trei lah ketivi* — the word appears twice, and therefore both *drashot* can be sustained. A further question: does the second *lah* teach *la v'lo le'yevamah* or *la v'lo lah ve'lachavertah* — that a husband cannot divorce two wives with a single *get*? The Gemara resolves this by turning to the *parsha* of *chalitzah*: *naal, naal — naal ein midi achrina lo* — just as there is a *drasha* that only a written document severs an ordinary marriage (*sefer kortah ve'ein davar acher kortah*), so too in *chalitzah* the Torah specifies the shoe, teaching that only the shoe effects *chalitzah* and nothing else can substitute for it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״נָעַל״ – נַעַל אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara suggests a different explanation as to why a bill of divorce is ineffective for a *yevama*. With regard to a *yevama* **the verse states:** "The house of him who had his **shoe** removed" (Deuteronomy 25:10), which teaches: If she is released by the *ḥalitza* of the **shoe, yes,** she is released from the *yavam*, but if she was released by means of **something else, no,** she is not released.

The underlying theme of this entire *sugya*, as the discussion makes clear, is the *chachamim*'s insistence on the primacy of *psukim* over *kal vachomer*. *Kal vachomer* is a powerful tool for deriving *halacha*, yet the Gemara works strenuously to ground each rule in explicit scriptural derivation rather than purely logical inference. The reason is that *yibum*, *gerushin*, *eishet ish*, and *amah ivriyah* are not simply variations on a single theme; each is a unique and distinct legal institution, and the Torah's precise language in each context reflects those distinctions. Logical analogy alone cannot safely be used to import rules from one domain into another, because the apparently similar categories are in fact genuinely different.

## Chalitzah Shoe

### Whose Shoe Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״נָעַל״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״נַעֲלוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נַעֲלוֹ, נַעַל שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם מְנָא לֵיהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נַעַל״ ״נַעַל״ – רִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But this** term **"shoe," does it come** to teach **this? It is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse said: "And remove **his shoe"** (Deuteronomy 25:9). **I have** derived **only** that **his shoe** may be used. **From where** do I derive that the **shoe of any person** may also be used for *ḥalitza*? **The verse states "shoe,"** and states again in the next verse **"shoe,"** which **includes** any other shoe.

The word *naal* in the *chalitzah* verse implies the *yavam*'s own shoe — *na'alo*. But the *drasha* of *naal* (unmodified) teaches expansively: *naal shel kol adam* — any person's shoe may be used. The *yavam* need not own the shoe; it may be borrowed or provided by the *beit din*.

### Shoe Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נַעֲלוֹ״? – נַעֲלוֹ הָרָאוּי לוֹ, פְּרָט לְגָדוֹל שֶׁאֵין יָכוֹל לְהַלֵּךְ בּוֹ, פְּרָט לְקָטָן שֶׁאֵין חוֹפֶה אֶת רוֹב רַגְלוֹ, פְּרָט לִמְסוּלְיָם שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עָקֵב! אִם כֵּן נִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״נָעַל״, מַאי ״הַנָּעַל״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states "his shoe,"** which is apparently referring to the shoe of that particular man? This teaches that it must be **his shoe that is fit for him, excluding** a shoe so **large that he cannot walk in it,** and **excluding** a shoe so **small that it does not cover most of his foot,** and **excluding sandals [*mesulayim*] that have no heels,** which do not qualify as shoes. The Gemara answers: **If so,** that the term is stated only for that purpose, **let the verse write "shoe." What** is the meaning of **"the shoe"? Learn** the **two** previously stated conclusions **from it.**

The *braita* provides three exclusions. *Prat le'gadol she'ein yachol lehalech bo* — a shoe so large that the *yavam* cannot walk in it (the image of clown shoes makes the point) is invalid. *Prat le'katan she'eino chofeh rov raglo* — a shoe so small that it does not cover the majority of his foot is likewise invalid. The legal principle of *rubo ke'kulo* (the majority counts as the whole) is operative, though practically speaking a shoe that covers only the majority of the foot would still be uncomfortable; the criterion is a legal one. *Prat le'mesulyah she'ein lo akeiv* — certain sandal types lacking a proper heel cup (effectively, flip-flops) are also excluded.

### Proper Fit

The general principle is *na'alo hara'uy lo* — the shoe must be appropriately fit for him. A flip-flop, lacking the structural cup to hold the heel, is not considered a proper shoe in the relevant halakhic sense.

### Veyibmah Teaches All

The *braita* provides three exclusions, and the Gemara explains how they are derived from the redundant terminology in the verse. *Im kein nechtov kra naal — mai hanaal? Shema mina tartei*: had the Torah written only *naal*, the word *hanaal* (with the definite article, creating apparent redundancy) would be unnecessary. From this redundancy we derive two things: first, that *chalitzah* can only be effected through the shoe and no other mechanism; and second, all the specifications regarding the shoe's fitness and physical requirements are also derived. The detailed laws governing the *chalitzah* shoe are treated at length in *Masechet Yevamot*.

## Eved Ivri Intro

### Three Sources

The *mishnah* now shifts to a new subject: *eved ivri*, the Jewish indentured servant. The laws of *eved ivri* appear three times in the Torah: in *Parshat Mishpatim*, in *Parshat Behar*, and in *Parshat Re'eh* in *Sefer Devarim*. Each passage has a different emphasis and focus. Biblical critics have argued that these three sections reflect different literary sources. Professor Aaron Koller of Yeshiva University has argued persuasively, drawing on both traditional sources and Ancient Near Eastern parallels, that the three sections are entirely compatible, mutually complementary parts of a unified legal system. A recurring failure of modern biblical scholarship, on this view, is to identify apparent tensions and attribute them to source pluralism without adequately examining analogous legal structures in the ancient world, which demonstrate that such complexity is entirely expected within a coherent legal code.

### Two Paths

There are two paths by which a Jew could become an *eved ivri*. The first is *mocher atzmo* — a man in financial distress voluntarily sells himself into indentured servitude. The second is *mecharu beit din* — a *beit din* sells a person as a servant when he has stolen and is too poor to repay the principal (*keren*) of what he stole. The thief can be sold to cover the *keren* but not the *keifel* (the double payment), since the *keifel* is a punitive fine rather than a restitutionary obligation, and one cannot be sold into servitude to pay a penal surcharge.

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֶבֶד עִבְרִי נִקְנֶה בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר, וְקוֹנֶה עַצְמוֹ בְּשָׁנִים, וּבַיּוֹבֵל, וּבְגִרְעוֹן כֶּסֶף. יְתֵירָה עָלָיו אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה שֶׁקּוֹנָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ בְּסִימָנִין. הַנִּרְצָע, נִקְנֶה בִּרְצִיעָה, וְקוֹנֶה אֶת עַצְמוֹ בַּיּוֹבֵל, וּבְמִיתַת הָאָדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A Hebrew slave** can be **acquired** by his master **through money or through a document, and** he can **acquire himself,** i.e., he is emancipated, **through years,** i.e., when he completes his six years of labor, **or through the** advent of the **Jubilee** Year, **or through the deduction of money.** The slave can redeem himself during the six years by paying for his remaining years of slavery. **A Hebrew maidservant** has one mode of emancipation **more than him, as she acquires herself through signs** indicating puberty. A slave **who is pierced** after serving six years is **acquired** as a slave for a longer period **through piercing** his ear with an awl, **and** he **acquires himself through the** advent of the **Jubilee** Year **or through** the **death of the master.**

The *mishnah* states: *eved ivri nikne be'chesef uve'shtar* — an *eved ivri* is acquired through money or a document — *ve'koneh atzmo be'shanim* — and acquires his freedom through the passage of years (specifically six years, as the verse states *ve'avadcha sheish shanim*), *uve'yovel* — or through the arrival of the Jubilee year, which releases him even mid-term — *uve'giraon kesef* — or through a proportional monetary buyout of the remaining term.

### Amah Ivriyah

The *mishnah* notes that the *amah ha'ivriyah* has a special status. She is a minor girl indentured by her father. (An adult woman apparently has no mechanism to indenture herself; similarly, minor boys have no such track. Only adult men and minor girls have defined paths into *eved ivri* status.) The *amah ha'ivriyah* acquires her freedom also upon the appearance of *simanim* — physical signs of puberty. She has an additional path not available to the adult male: the institution of *yi'ud*, whereby the *adon* or his son may marry her, transforming the indentured arrangement into a marital one. *Tosafot* return to this point repeatedly in comparing the acquisition of an *amah ha'ivriyah* to the mechanisms of marriage, and the comparison proves illuminating throughout the earlier part of the *perek*. From the father's perspective, the arrangement provides an opportunity for his daughter to marry into a prosperous family — a context that must be understood against the backdrop of its time, even as it engages obvious tensions with contemporary sensibilities regarding child labor and child marriage.

### Eved Nirtza

The *mishnah* further mentions the *eved nirtza*, the pierced-ear servant. After six years of service, if the servant declares that he loves his master and does not wish to go free, the *adon* brings him to the doorpost and pierces his ear with an awl. The servant then acquires the status of *eved nirtza*, which is distinct from ordinary *eved ivri* in that it does not expire after six years; instead, the *eved nirtza* is released only at the *yovel* year or upon the death of the *adon*. He cannot buy his freedom out early, and this status can only be acquired by a *mecharu beit din*, not a *mocher atzmo* — a distinction that will be explored further. The *midrash* notes that Rabbi Hertz, in his *Chumash* commentary, observes that the final law in Hammurabi's Code addresses what to do when a slave runs away, while the first law in *Parshat Mishpatim* addresses what to do when a slave wishes to remain — a juxtaposition that reflects the Torah's aspiration that the master's household be so humane and generous that the servant might genuinely prefer to stay.

## Eved Ivri Kinyan

### Non-Jew Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ עֶבֶד עִבְרִי נִקְנֶה בְּכֶסֶף. מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּקְנֶה בְּכֶסֶף. אַשְׁכְּחַן עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְנׇכְרִי, הוֹאִיל וְכׇל קִנְיָנוֹ בְּכֶסֶף,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that **a Hebrew slave** can be **acquired through money.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers that the **verse states: "Out of the money that he was bought for"** (Leviticus 25:51), which **teaches** that he can be **acquired through money.** The Gemara asks: **We found** that **a Hebrew slave who is sold to a gentile** is acquired with money, which is the case discussed in that verse, but this proves nothing with regard to a Jew sold to a Jew. One could argue that **since all of the acquisitions** of a gentile are performed only **with money,** he can likewise purchase a Hebrew slave with money.

The Gemara asks: *eved ivri nikna be'chesef — minalan?* How do we know that an *eved ivri* can be acquired with money? The derivation begins, perhaps surprisingly, with the case of a Jew sold to a non-Jew. The *pasuk* in *Parshat Behar* discusses a situation where a Jew has been purchased by a non-Jewish resident (*ger toshav*) and his relatives wish to redeem him: *mikesef miknato* — from the money of his purchase price, implying he was acquired through money in the first place. For a non-Jew, this is not remarkable, since all acquisitions by non-Jews work through *kesef*; the non-Jewish system of *kinyanim* operates primarily on a financial-monetary basis. But this still leaves the primary case — a Jew indentured to a fellow Jew — underived.

### Amah Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְכָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהׇפְדֵּה״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמְּגָרַעַת פִּדְיוֹנָהּ, וְיוֹצְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** But **from where do we** derive that a Hebrew slave can be **sold to a Jew** with money? The Gemara explains that with regard to a Hebrew maidservant, the **verse states:** "Then he shall let her **be redeemed"** (Exodus 21:8), which **teaches** that if she acquires money and wishes to be emancipated before her time is complete, she **deducts** a sum **from her redemption.** The maidservant can deduct the value of time served from her purchase price and pay the remaining amount, and she then **is emancipated.**

The Gemara turns to *amah ha'ivriyah*: *ve'hifdah* — a verse in the *parsha* of *amah ha'ivriyah* speaks of redeeming her freedom proportionally (*migra'at pidyonah*), implying she was originally acquired for money. The connection to *kesef* is thus established for the *amah ha'ivriyah*. This is bolstered by the analogy to marriage: just as she may be acquired (*mekadshah be'chesef*) through money for the purpose of *kiddushin*, so too she is acquired (*maknya be'chesef*) through money for the purpose of indenture. But this still does not directly establish the rule for the adult male *eved ivri*.

### Primary Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה, הוֹאִיל וּמִיקַּדְּשָׁה בְּכֶסֶף – מִיקַּנְיָא בְּכֶסֶף, עֶבֶד עִבְרִי מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִמָּכֵר לְךָ אָחִיךָ הָעִבְרִי אוֹ הָעִבְרִיָּה וַעֲבָדְךָ שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים״ – מַקִּישׁ עִבְרִי לְעִבְרִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Although **we found** this *halakha* with regard to **a Hebrew maidservant,** one cannot extrapolate from there to this case, as it is possible that it applies only to a female. The reason for this is that **since** she is ordinarily **betrothed through money,** she can also be **acquired** as a maidservant **through money. From where do we** derive that **a Hebrew slave** can likewise be acquired through money? The Gemara answers that the **verse states: "If your brother, a Hebrew man, or a Hebrew woman is sold to you, and he shall serve you six years"** (Deuteronomy 15:12). This verse **juxtaposes a Hebrew man to a Hebrew woman,** indicating that the modes through which they are acquired are the same.

For the primary case of an adult male *eved ivri* sold to a fellow Jew, the Gemara turns to *Devarim*: *ki yimacher lecha achicha ha'Ivri o ha'Ivriyah* — when your Jewish brother or sister is sold to you. The verse then says *ve'avadcha sheish shanim* — he shall serve you six years. *Makish eved Ivri le'Ivriyah* — the adult male is explicitly connected in this verse to the *amah ha'ivriyah*, and the rules derived for her (including acquisition by *kesef*) are extended to him. This establishes the *mecharu beit din* case.

### Mocher Atzmo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין, הוֹאִיל וְנִמְכָּר בְּעַל כּוֹרְחוֹ, מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ מְנָלַן? יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **We found** a source in this verse for **a slave who is sold by the court.** This verse is referring to a thief who is unable to repay the value of his theft and is sold as a slave so that he can repay his debt. One can therefore argue that this case is unique, **since** he is **sold against his will. From where do we** derive that one who **sells himself** can be sold through money? The Gemara answers: One **derives** this through a verbal analogy between the terms **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker."**

The *mocher atzmo* case requires separate derivation. The Gemara invokes a *gezeirah shavah*: *yalif sachir sachir* — the word *sachir* (hired worker) appears in the *eved ivri* context both in *Vayikra* (where *Parshat Behar* describes *mocher atzmo*) and in *Devarim* (where the *mecharu beit din* case is described). This verbal analogy equates the two cases, extending the rules of acquisition by *kesef* to *mocher atzmo* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּיָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּלָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִי תַשִּׂיג״ – מוּסָף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן, וְיִלְמַד עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who** derives this verbal analogy between the terms **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker." But according to the one who does not derive** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," what can be said?** From where does he derive that one who sells himself may be purchased with money? The Gemara answers: The **verse states** at the start of the passage dealing with one sold to a gentile: **"And if** a stranger who is a settler with you **becomes rich"** (Leviticus 25:47). The conjunction "and" serves to **add to the first matter,** i.e., the passage discussing one who sells himself to a Jew, as it joins the two issues. And therefore **let the above case** of one who sells himself to a Jew **be derived from the case below** of one who is sold to a gentile: Just as one who is sold to a gentile can be acquired with money, so too, one who sells himself to a Jew can be acquired with money.

For the view that does not employ the *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir sachir*, the Gemara offers an alternative: *amar kra ve'chi tassig* — a verse in *Parshat Behar* beginning with "if his hand reaches" adds an additional gloss to the prior discussion. *Mosif al inyan rishon, veyilmad elyon mitachton* — the upper passage (the case of a Jew sold to a non-Jew) and the lower passage (the case of a Jew sold to a fellow Jew) appear in the same broader literary unit of *Parshat Behar*, and through contextual proximity — a principle the Gemara describes as *zeh ke'ein semichut parshiyot* — one learns from the other. This is a literary rather than strictly logical principle: two adjacent paragraphs about the same subject illuminate each other. It is looser than a true *heikesh* but functions similarly, and reflects a recognition that the Torah's arrangement of material is itself an interpretive tool.

## Two Types Compared

### Tanna Kamma View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמַאן תַּנָּא דְּלָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״? הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ נִמְכַּר לְשֵׁשׁ, וְיָתֵר עַל שֵׁשׁ, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר אֶלָּא לְשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And who** is the *tanna* **who does not derive** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker"? It is this *tanna*, as it is taught in** a *baraita*: One who **sells himself** as a slave **is sold for six** years, **and** if he wishes he can be sold for **more than six** years, whereas one who is **sold by the court is sold only for six** years, but no more.

The Gemara presents a *braita* that enumerates the distinctions between *mocher atzmo* and *mecharu beit din* according to the *tanna kamma*. These distinctions are largely rooted in a careful reading of the *pshat* of the relevant *psukim*, and reflect a view that the two categories of *eved ivri* are genuinely different legal institutions, not merely two paths into the same status.

### Six Year Limit

The *braita* teaches that one who sells himself may be sold for longer than six years, while one sold by the court can only be sold for six years.

### Nirtza Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הַמּוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – נִרְצָע. מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵין מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ. הַמּוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ אֵין רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית, מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds that there are additional differences between these two slaves: **One who sells himself may not be pierced** with an awl, whereas one who is **sold by the court may be pierced** with an awl. One who **sells himself is not granted** a severance gift by his master when he is emancipated, whereas one who is **sold by the court is granted** a severance gift. With regard to **one who sells himself, his master may not provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant** as a wife to produce slave children, whereas with regard to one **sold by the court, his master may provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant.**

A person who voluntarily sold himself cannot have his ear pierced to become an *eved nirtza*. The institution of *eved nirtza* — and the entire emotional and social scenario of the slave who has genuinely found a home in his master's household — applies only to the *mecharu beit din* case, where the servant was thrust into the arrangement against his will and may, over six years, come genuinely to prefer it.

### Severance Gifts

Upon release from self-imposed servitude, no severance gifts are provided. Upon the completion of a *beit din*-imposed term, the *adon* is obligated to provide the freed servant with provisions: produce, food, and money (*ha'anek ta'anik lo*). The rationale is clear: the *mecharu beit din* servant was someone who was so poor that he could not even repay stolen goods. At the end of his term, his debt is discharged but he has nothing. Without this endowment, he faces an immediate return to destitution with no resources to restart his life — and perhaps temptation to steal again. The Torah's mandatory severance gift addresses this directly.

### Shfachah Rights

If a man sold himself voluntarily, his *adon* cannot provide him with a *shifcha kna'anit* to marry and produce children who would themselves become *avadim*. In the *beit din*-imposed case, the *adon* may provide him with a *shifcha kna'anit*, and the children born of that union would be *eved kna'ani*. This is the institution of *yelid bayit* — slaves born in the household — which the Torah explicitly addresses.

The distinction reflects the different legal characters of the two arrangements: *mecharu beit din*, being closer to a form of court-imposed servitude for criminal restitution, carries the fuller range of the Torah's slave laws, including the ability of the *adon* to arrange the servant's domestic life. *Mocher atzmo*, being closer to an employment agreement entered voluntarily, does not carry those features.

### Rabbi Elazar View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה אֵינוֹ נִמְכָּר אֶלָּא לְשֵׁשׁ, זֶה וָזֶה נִרְצָע, וָזֶה וָזֶה מַעֲנִיקִים לוֹ, וְזֶה וָזֶה רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא לָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** that there is no difference between these two types of slaves. Rather, both **this** slave **and that** slave may be **sold for only six** years; both **this and that** one may be **pierced** with an awl, both **this and that** one **are granted** a severance gift, and in both **this** case **and that** case **his master may provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant. What, is it not** the case that **they disagree with regard to this, that the first *tanna* does not derive** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," and Rabbi Elazar derives** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker"?**

*Rabbi Elazar* disagrees with all of these distinctions: *zeh ve'zeh eino nimkar ela le'shesh, zeh ve'zeh nirtza, ve'zeh ve'zeh ma'anikim lo, ve'zeh ve'zeh rabo moser lo shifcha kna'anit* — both categories are subject to a maximum six-year term, both can become *eved nirtza*, both receive severance gifts, and both may be provided with a *shifcha kna'anit*. *Rabbi Elazar* collapses the distinction between *mocher atzmo* and *mecharu beit din*, treating *eved ivri* as a single unified institution.

### Amoraim Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יָלֵיף ״שְׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְהָכָא בְּהַאי קְרָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּאָמַר מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ נִמְכַּר לְשֵׁשׁ וְיָתֵר עַל שֵׁשׁ? מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מְכָרוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין: ״וַעֲבָדְךָ שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים״ – זֶה, וְלֹא מוֹכֵר עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Tavyumei said in the name of Abaye:** That is not so; rather, **everyone derives** the verbal analogy between **"hired worker"** and **"hired worker," and here,** in the dispute in the *baraita*, **they disagree about this** following **verse. What is the reasoning** of **the first *tanna*, who says** that one who **sells himself is sold for six** years **and more than six** years? He maintains that with regard to one **sold by the court, the Merciful One excludes** a certain case by the verse: "If your brother, a Hebrew man, or a Hebrew woman, is sold to you, **and he shall serve you six years"** (Deuteronomy 15:12). That teaches that only **this** type of slave serves for six years and no more, but the same does **not** apply to one who **sells himself.** If he so stipulates, he may serve for more than six years.

The *Amoraim* initially assumed that the *machloket* between the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Elazar* reflects a disagreement about whether to apply the *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir sachir*. On this reading, the *tanna kamma* does not apply the *gezeirah shavah* and therefore sees the two *psukim* — in *Vayikra* and *Devarim* — as addressing genuinely different cases (*mocher atzmo* and *mecharu beit din* respectively), each with its own distinct rules. *Rabbi Elazar*, who does apply the *gezeirah shavah*, equates the two and sees a unified system.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִידָּךְ: ״וַעֲבָדְךָ״ – לְךָ, וְלֹא לְיוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And the other,** Rabbi Elazar, learns a different *halakha* from this verse. **"And he shall serve you"** means that he works **for you and not for an heir** other than a son.

*Amar Rav Tavyumi mishmeih de'Abaye*: the *Amoraim* reject this framework. In fact, both *tannaim* agree that the *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir sachir* is operative. Their dispute lies elsewhere — in the interpretation of specific verses. According to the *tanna kamma*, the verse *ve'avadcha sheish shanim* limits only *mecharu beit din*, not *mocher atzmo* (*zeh ve'lo mocher atzmo*). *Rabbi Elazar* reads *ve'avadcha lecha ve'lo le'yoresh* — the verse teaches that the *eved ivri*'s obligation runs to the specific *adon* and does not transfer to his heirs — rather than imposing the six-year limit exclusively on *mecharu beit din*.

Additional distinctions in the *braita* — such as *eved ivri* service not being inherited by the *adon*'s son — will continue to be worked through in the following session, with the Gemara tracing how each *tanna* derives or does not derive each particular rule.
