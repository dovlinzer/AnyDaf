# Rosh Hashanah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Creation to Year Categor…

### Constellations & Creation

The discussion of creation-era chronology — when the constellations were set in place, when the waters rose — reaches its natural conclusion here. The debate over the *girsa* turns on a question of seasonal plausibility: the rising of waters and the melting of snows fit the spring far better than the fall, as does the ascending of Aries. Rashi's formulation is that the *tekufot* represent the moment we imagine the constellations and the sun to have been placed — which occurred on the fourth day of creation, when the luminaries were created — and the computation of that moment proceeds from Nisan. Both the *chachmei umot ha'olam* and the *chachmei Yisrael* appear to converge on Nisan as the basis for this calculation.

### Nisan vs. Tishrei Tension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מוֹנִין לַמַּבּוּל כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְלַתְּקוּפָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. חַכְמֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מוֹנִין אַף לַמַּבּוּל כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The Jewish Sages count** the years **from** Creation and **the flood in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** from Tishrei, and they calculate **the cycles** of the sun and the moon **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** from Nisan. **The sages of the** gentile **nations of the world,** on the other hand, **count both** the years **from** Creation and **the flood in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** from Nisan.

This consensus will prove startling when the *Gemara* later encounters a discussion in which our very liturgy reflects the position that the world was created in Tishrei. The tension between the computational tradition anchoring creation in Nisan and the liturgical tradition anchoring it in Tishrei remains unresolved. One might be tempted to resolve it by positing two planes — *alah b'machshava*, the world arose in thought in one month, and came into being in another — but the *Gemara* itself leaves this open.

### Vegetables & Ma'aser Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַיְּרָקוֹת. תָּנָא: לַיְּרָקוֹת וְלַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת וְלַנְּדָרִים. לַיְּרָקוֹת מַאי נִינְהוּ? מַעֲשֵׂר יָרָק.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **And** the first of Tishrei is the new year **for vegetables.** It **is taught** in a *baraita*: The first of Tishrei is the new year **for vegetables, and for tithes, and for vows.** The Gemara asks: **What is** meant by the term: **For vegetables?** It means **for** the **vegetable tithe,** i.e., one may not take *teruma* or tithes from vegetables picked before Rosh HaShana in order to fulfill the obligation for vegetables picked after Rosh HaShana.

The *Gemara* now returns from its extended excursus on the *pesukim* of Noach and the *mabul*, and resumes the list in the *mishnah* of domains for which the first of Tishrei constitutes the *rosh hashanah*. The next item on that list is *u'leyerakot* — for vegetables. The operative concern is *ma'aser yerek*: one may not tithe from one year onto the next. If, for example, a farmer has twenty bushels from this year and ten from the following year and attempts to designate a tithe covering all thirty, the portion taken on behalf of the ten units of the second year's produce is legally ineffective — and this is true even *bedi'avad*. Beyond the question of cross-year tithing, the year-designation for *ma'asrot* also determines which *ma'aser* applies within the seven-year cycle: *ma'aser ani* in years three and six, and *ma'aser sheni* in years one, two, four, and five.

## Vegetables Cross-Year Mi…

### Year Boundary Rules

Two conceptually distinct questions arise whenever the year-boundary for *terumot u'ma'asrot* is at stake. The first is the calendar date: when does the new year begin? For vegetables, the answer is the first of Tishrei. The second is the operative event: what must have occurred by that date to assign the produce to one year or the other? By *ma'aser behemah*, the relevant event is the birth of the animal — whether it was born before or after *rosh chodesh Elul*, or before or after the first of Tishrei, depending on how one rules in that debate. For vegetables, as the *Gemara* will demonstrate, the operative event is the moment of picking.

### Calendar Date vs. Event

These two questions — the fixed calendar date and the determining event — operate in tandem. The date answers *when* the year turns; the event answers *what* must have happened to place the produce on one side of that boundary or the other. Neither question is trivial, and the *Gemara* treats them as analytically independent.

## Braita Analysis

### Three Categories Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ מַעַשְׂרוֹת? תְּנָא דְּרַבָּנַן וְקָתָנֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **this is the same as** the meaning of the term **for tithes,** and yet the *baraita* distinguishes between them. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **taught** first about a tithe that is **by rabbinic** law, i.e., the vegetable tithe, that its new year is the first of Tishrei, **and** then **it teaches** about a tithe that is **by Torah** law, i.e., the tithe of grain, wine, and oil, that its new year is also the first of Tishrei.

The *Gemara* cites a *braita*: *u'leyerakot u'lema'asrot u'lenedarim* — not just vegetables, but also *ma'asrot* generally, encompassing fruit and grain, and also vows. The *Gemara* immediately asks: why does the *braita* mention both *yerakot* and *ma'asrot*? If *yerakot* means the *ma'aser* obligation as it pertains to vegetables, is that not simply a subset of *ma'asrot*?

### Rabbinic vs. Biblical Ma…

The answer is that the two categories are legally distinct: *tana d'rabbanan v'tana d'oraita*. The obligation of *ma'aser* on vegetables is rabbinic — *ma'aser yerek* is *derabbanan* — while *ma'asrot* in the second clause refers to the biblical obligations on grain, olives, and grapes. This raises a significant question: what precisely falls within the biblical obligation? Reading the *Gemara* plainly, it sounds as though anything other than vegetables would be biblical, which would include all fruit. Whether *ma'aser* on fruit is indeed *d'oraita* is a major dispute among the *rishonim*. Rashi, throughout *Shas*, maintains that the Torah's obligation of *terumot u'ma'asrot* applies only to *degancha tiroshcha ve'yitzharecha* — the five grains, oil from olives, and wine from grapes — and that everything else, including fruit, is rabbinic.

### Fruit Tithes Debate

Tosafot here raises a lengthy counter-argument rooted in the *Yerushalmi*, which seems to indicate that all fruit carries a biblical *ma'aser* obligation, with vegetables alone being the rabbinic exception. Tosafot marshals this tradition at length, arriving at the formulation *mishma d'she'ar kol peyros d'oraita* — that all other fruit is biblical. Yet Tosafot ultimately reverses course. Several lines later, Tosafot concludes with the words *aval i efshar lomar kein* — "but it is impossible to say this" — because in numerous places throughout the *Gemara*, the rabbis treat fruit *ma'aser* as rabbinic. Tosafot thus ultimately aligns with Rashi's position: the biblical obligation applies only to grain, olives, and grapes.

### Ordering Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּרֵישָׁא! אַיְּידֵי דַּחֲבִיבָא לֵיהּ — אַקְדְּמַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **let** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **teach** that which is **by Torah** law **in the first clause.** The Gemara explains: **Since** the *halakha* with regard to vegetable tithes **was dear to him, he mentioned it first.** He taught first about the tithe that is by rabbinic decree, as it involves a greater novelty, and afterward he taught about the tithe that is by Torah law.

A further question presses: why does the *braita* list the rabbinic category (*yerakot*) before the biblical one (*ma'asrot*)? The *Gemara* answers with a principle invoked in multiple places: *aydi d'chaviva lei akdamah* — the *tanna* is partial to rabbinic enactments and places them first in order to give them weight, so that people will not treat them lightly.

### Mishna Word Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דִּידַן? תְּנָא דְּרַבָּנַן, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And** with regard to **the *tanna*** of **our** mishna, who mentioned only vegetables but not tithes, what is his reasoning? The Gemara explains: **He taught** vegetable tithes, which are **by rabbinic decree, and** from which one may infer **all the more so** that the first of Tishrei is the new year for the tithe of grain, wine, and oil, which is by Torah law.

The *Gemara* then turns to the *tanna didan* — the author of our *mishnah* — who mentions only *yerakot*, without using the broader term *ma'asrot*. The explanation offered is *tana d'rabbanan v'chol shekein d'oraita*: by specifying the rabbinic category, one conveys the obligation for the biblical category *a fortiori*. Rashi understands the *kal va'chomer* as follows: if there is a fixed date for the rabbinic *ma'aser* that governs year-boundaries, then certainly there is a fixed date for the biblical obligation. Alternatively, the *kal va'chomer* runs in the other direction: if the rabbinic category — which was modeled on the biblical one — operates by the first of Tishrei, then certainly the biblical category, which served as the model, operates the same way.

### Singular vs. Plural

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי ״מַעֲשֵׂר״! אֶחָד מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן. וְלִיתְנֵי ״יָרָק״! תְּרֵי גַּוְונֵי יָרָק, דִּתְנַן: יָרָק הַנֶּאֱגָד — מִשֶּׁיֵּאָגֵד, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱגָד — מִשֶּׁיְּמַלֵּא אֶת הַכְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question about the *baraita*: **But let** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **teach: Tithe,** in the singular. Why teach tithes in the plural? The Gemara answers: He uses this formulation to include **both** the **animal tithe and** the **grain tithe.** The Gemara asks further: **But let him teach: Vegetable,** in the singular. Why teach: Vegetables, in the plural? The Gemara answers: He means to include **two categories of vegetables, as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **a** type of **vegetable that is** usually **made into bundles** before being sold, the time of tithing is **from when it is bundled; and** with regard to a type of vegetable **that is not** usually **made into** such **bundles,** the time of tithing is **from when one fills a vessel** with it.

The *Gemara* then asks why the *mishnah* did not simply say *ma'aser*, which would have generically included both rabbinic and biblical obligations. Tosafot explains that had the *mishnah* used the generic term *ma'aser*, it might have been construed as encompassing *ma'aser behemah* as well — and whether the first of Tishrei governs *ma'aser behemah* is itself a matter of dispute. By saying *yerakot* instead, the *tanna* avoids any implication about *ma'aser behemah*. Tosafot further notes that the term *ma'asrot* in the *braita* does not carry the same connotation of *ma'aser behemah*, since across Tannaitic literature *ma'asrot* refers to the multiple tithes separated from produce — *ma'aser rishon*, *ma'aser sheni*, *ma'aser ani* — and not to the animal tithe.

### Two Vegetable Types

The plural *yerakot* thus signals that while all vegetables share the same rule for year-assignment, they differ with respect to this separate question of *gmar melacha*. For vegetables that are sold in bundles — asparagus, broccoli, and similar produce that is typically bound together for market — the *gmar melacha* occurs when they are bundled, at which point they are fully processed and ready for sale. For vegetables that are not bundled — such as loose tomatoes or lettuce — the *gmar melacha* occurs when the vessel is filled: *mi'she'yimalei et ha'keli*. If the vessel is never filled, then the *gmar melacha* is determined by the end of the harvest. It is critical to note that this distinction governs the point at which casual eating becomes prohibited — it does not govern year-assignment for *ma'asrot*, which is always determined by the moment of picking. The plural *yerakot* thus signals that while all vegetables share the same rule for year-assignment, they differ with respect to this separate question of *gmar melacha*.

## Picking & Day Boundary

### Picking Erev & After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לִיקֵּט יָרָק עֶרֶב רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה עַד שֶׁלֹּא תָּבֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, וְחָזַר וְלִיקֵּט מִשֶּׁתָּבֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ — אֵין תּוֹרְמִין וּמְעַשְּׂרִין מִזֶּה עַל זֶה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין תּוֹרְמִין וּמְעַשְּׂרִין, לֹא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ עַל הַיָּשָׁן, וְלֹא מִן הַיָּשָׁן עַל הֶחָדָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one picked vegetables on the eve of Rosh HaShana before the sun had set,** so that they belong to the previous year, **and** then **he returned and he picked** more vegetables **after sunset,** so that they belong to the new year, **one may not set aside *teruma* and tithe from the one to the other, as one may not set aside *teruma* and tithe from the new** crop **for the old nor from the old** crop **for the new.**

A *braita* presents the following case: a person was picking vegetables in his garden on *erev Rosh Hashanah* before sunset and continued picking after sunset. Given that picking after sunset would constitute *melachah* on *Yom Tov*, the *Yerushalmi* resolves this by positing a non-Jewish worker who, on his own initiative — *adaata d'nafshei* — continued the harvest after dark.

### Yom Tov & Obligation

Even granting that the post-sunset picking may have involved a *Yom Tov* violation, the *braita* rules that both batches remain *chayav b'terumot u'ma'asrot*. The *Yom Tov* concern does not vitiate the *ma'aser* obligation.

### Picking Moment Decisive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיְתָה שְׁנִיָּה נִכְנֶסֶת לִשְׁלִישִׁית — שְׁנִיָּה מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, שְׁלִישִׁית — מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וּמַעְשַׂר עָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** If it was the second** year of the Sabbatical cycle **going into the third** year, the *halakha* is: From what he picked in **the second** year he must set aside **first tithe,** which he gives to a Levite, **and second tithe,** which he eats in Jerusalem; from what he picked in **the third** year, he must set aside **first tithe and poor man's tithe,** which he gives to one who is needy.

More importantly for the conceptual framework, the *braita* rules that *ein tormin u'ma'asrin mi'zeh al zeh* — one may not take *terumah* or *ma'aser* from the pre-sunset batch on the post-sunset batch, nor vice versa. This establishes not merely the date — the first of Tishrei — but the operative event: the moment the produce is picked determines which year it belongs to. Produce picked five minutes before sunset and produce picked five minutes after sunset cannot be tithed against one another, because they belong to different years. The rule is stated explicitly: *lo min ha'chadash al ha'yashan velo min ha'yashan al ha'chadash*.

### Year Cycle Application

The *braita* then spells out the consequence for the seven-year cycle. If it is the second year of the cycle transitioning into the third: produce picked before sunset — belonging to year two — requires *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser sheni*, the latter being eaten in Jerusalem. Produce picked after sunset — belonging to year three — requires *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser ani*, the tithe designated for the poor. Four *nafka minot* thus emerge in sequence: (A) the calendar date is the first of Tishrei; (B) the operative event is the moment of picking; (C) produce from one year may not be tithed against produce from another year; and (D) the applicable *ma'aser* within the cycle is determined by the year of picking.

## Ma'aser Ani Years 3 & 6

### Ma'aser Ani Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״כִּי תְכַלֶּה לַעְשֵׂר אֶת כׇּל מַעְשַׂר תְּבוּאָתְךָ בַּשָּׁנָה הַשְּׁלִישִׁית שְׁנַת הַמַּעֲשֵׂר״, שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂר אֶחָד. הָא כֵּיצַד? מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וּמַעְשַׂר עָנִי, וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי יִבְטַל.
>
> **Translation:** **From where are these matters** derived that during the third year one must set aside poor man's tithe and not second tithe? **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** The verse states: **"When you have made an end of tithing all the tithes of your produce in the third year, which is the year of the tithe"** (Deuteronomy 26:12). This is referring to **a year when there is only one** of the two **tithes** that had been given in the previous years. **How so?** One sets aside **first tithe and poor man's tithe,** which is explicitly mentioned in that section, **and second tithe is nullified** that year.

The *Gemara* now digresses briefly to establish the biblical source for *ma'aser ani* in years three and six. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derives it from the verse: *ki techaleh la'aser et kol ma'aser tevuatcha ba'shanah ha'shlishit shenat ha'ma'aser* — "when you finish tithing all the tithe of your produce in the third year, the year of the tithe" — followed by *ve'natata la'Levi la'ger la'yatom ve'la'almana*. Ordinarily, *ma'aser rishon* goes to the Levi and *ma'aser sheni* is eaten in Jerusalem by its owner. The inclusion of *ger*, *yatom*, and *almana* as recipients marks this as the year of *ma'aser ani*.

### Only One Other Tithe

The phrase *shenat ha'ma'aser* — the year of *the* tithe — is interpreted to mean that in this year there is only one other *ma'aser* besides *ma'aser ani*. The inference is that *ma'aser sheni* is suspended: only *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser ani* apply.

### Ma'aser Rishon Continuity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אַף מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי יִבְטַל — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶל הַלְוִיִּם תְּדַבֵּר וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵיהֶם כִּי תִקְחוּ מֵאֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַמַּעֲשֵׂר אֲשֶׁר נָתַתִּי לָכֶם מֵאִתָּם בְּנַחֲלַתְכֶם״, הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לְנַחֲלָה: מָה נַחֲלָה אֵין לָהּ הֶפְסֵק — אַף מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן אֵין לוֹ הֶפְסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps **it is not** like this, **but** in fact **even first tithe is nullified** during the third year and only one tithe is set aside, i.e., the poor man's tithe. Therefore, **the verse states: "Thus speak to the Levites, and say to them: When you take of the children of Israel the tithes which I have given you from them for your inheritance"** (Numbers 18:26). **The verse juxtaposes** the first tithe that is given to the Levites **to an inheritance** of land: **Just as** with regard to **an inheritance there is no interruption,** as by Torah law a landed inheritance cannot be sold in perpetuity, but rather it passes from one generation to the next without interruption, **so too,** with regard to the **first tithe** that is given to the Levites **there is no interruption,** but rather it is taken every year.

The *Gemara* raises an objection: perhaps *shenat ha'ma'aser* means that even *ma'aser rishon* is suspended, leaving only *ma'aser ani* altogether? This is resolved by the verse: *v'el ha'Levi'im tedaber v'amarta aleihem ki tikchu mei'et b'nei Yisrael et ha'ma'aser asher natati lachem mei'itam b'nachalatchem* — "and to the Levites you shall speak... the tithe that I have given you from them as your inheritance." The verse links *ma'aser rishon* to an inheritance: just as an inheritance has no interruption — *ma nachala ein lah hefsek* — so too *ma'aser rishon* has no interruption. It follows that *shenat ha'ma'aser* signifies the suspension of *ma'aser sheni* only, not of *ma'aser rishon*.

### Second Braita Confirmati…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״כִּי תְכַלֶּה לַעְשֵׂר וְגוֹ׳״, שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂר אֶחָד. הָא כֵּיצַד? מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וּמַעְשַׂר עָנִי, וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי יִבְטַל. יָכוֹל אַף מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי יִבְטַל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבָא הַלֵּוִי״, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁבָּא — תֵּן לוֹ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The same *halakha* **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states: **"When you have made an end of tithing** all the tithes of your produce in the third year, which is the year of the tithe." This is referring to **the year when there is only one** of the two **tithes** that had been given in the previous years. **How so?** One sets aside **first tithe and poor man's tithe, and second tithe is canceled.** One **might** have thought **that even first tithe is canceled** during the third year. Therefore, **the verse states: "And the Levite,** because he has no part or inheritance with you, and the stranger, and the fatherless, and the widow, who are within your gates, **shall come,** and shall eat and be satisfied" (Deuteronomy 14:29). **Whenever** the Levite **comes, give him;** every year you must give the Levite his tithe. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

A second *braita* confirms this reading independently. The same verse — *ki techaleh la'aser... shenat ha'ma'aser* — is quoted, and the same conclusion is reached: only *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser ani*, while *ma'aser sheni* is suspended. The *braita* then asks whether *ma'aser rishon* might also be suspended, and resolves this from a different verse: *u'va ha'Levi* — "and the Levi shall come" — which appears in the context of the third year. The verse *u'va ha'Levi ki ein lo chelek v'nachala imach v'ha'ger v'ha'yatom v'ha'almana* singles out the Levi distinctly, alongside the *ger*, *yatom*, and *almana*, demonstrating that even in the year of *ma'aser ani*, the Levi continues to receive his tithe. The ruling is thus: *u'va ha'Levi kol zman sheba ten lo* — whenever the Levi comes, give to him — even during the third year.

### R. Eliezer ben Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶל הַלְוִיִּם תְּדַבֵּר וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵיהֶם כִּי תִקְחוּ מֵאֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַמַּעֲשֵׂר אֲשֶׁר נָתַתִּי לָכֶם מֵאִתָּם בְּנַחֲלַתְכֶם״, הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לְנַחֲלָה: מָה נַחֲלָה אֵין לָהּ הֶפְסֵק — אַף מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן אֵין לוֹ הֶפְסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: One need not** learn this from here but from another source, as **it says: "Thus speak to the Levites, and say to them: When you take of the children of Israel the tithes which I have given you from them for your inheritance." The verse juxtaposes** the first tithe **to an inheritance** of land: **Just as** with regard to a landed **inheritance there is no interruption, so too,** with regard to **first tithe there is no interruption.**

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov maintains that the verse *u'va ha'Levi* is unnecessary for this derivation. The earlier verse — *v'el ha'Levi'im tedaber... asher natati lachem b'nachalatchem* — linking *ma'aser rishon* to an inheritance already suffices: *ma nachala ein lah hefsek, af ma'aser rishon ein lo hefsek*. Just as an inheritance admits no interruption, *ma'aser rishon* admits no interruption, and the continuity of the Levi's entitlement is established without need of the additional verse.

## Vows & Year Term

### Vow Duration & Expiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַנְּדָרִים וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְשָׁנָה — מוֹנֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם. וְאִם אָמַר לְשָׁנָה זוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ לֹא עָמַד אֶלָּא בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְתִשְׁעָה בֶּאֱלוּל, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ יוֹם אֶחָד בְּתִשְׁרִי — עָלְתָה לוֹ שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the *baraita* cited above: **And** the first of Tishrei is the new year **for vows. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One** who is **prohibited by a vow** from **deriving benefit from another for a year counts twelve months from day to day,** from the day that the vow was taken. This applies whether one took a vow not to derive any benefit from another for a year, or he was the subject of someone else's vow prohibiting him from deriving any benefit from that individual's property for a year. **But if,** when he took the vow, **he said: For this year,** then **even if he took** the vow **only on the twenty-ninth of Elul, once the first day of Tishrei,** the following month, **has arrived, it is counted as a year,** and he is permitted to derive benefit from the other.

The *braita* on vows rules as follows: one who vows not to derive benefit from another person "for a year" counts twelve months from day to day — that is, returning to the same date on the calendar, accounting for leap years. However, if the vow was formulated as "for this year" — *lishana zo* — the vow expires when the first of Tishrei arrives, since that marks the end of the current year.

### One-Day Vow Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יוֹם אֶחָד בַּשָּׁנָה אֵינוֹ חָשׁוּב שָׁנָה, לְצַעוֹרֵיהּ נַפְשֵׁיהּ קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ — וְהָא אִצְטַעַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This is true **even according to the one who said** that **one day in a year is not considered a year,** since in the case of a vow the person **accepted upon himself to suffer affliction, and he has** already achieved his goal and **suffered affliction,** and so he has fulfilled his vow.

Even if the vow was taken on the twenty-ninth of Elul — *b'esrim v'tisha b'Elul* — once the first of Tishrei arrives, *altah lo shanah*: the year is complete and the vow expires, even though the vow was in force for only a single day.

### Self-Affliction Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא נִיסָן? בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But say** that a year with regard to vows ends in **Nisan.** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* is that **with regard to vows, follow the** ordinary **language of people.** The meaning of a vow is understood in accordance with the way that the words are used in common speech, and when people speak of a year, they ordinarily count it from the beginning of Tishrei.

The *Gemara* notes that this outcome holds even according to those who hold that one day in a year is ordinarily insufficient to be counted as a full year — as debated extensively regarding trees and rental agreements, where one camp required the new year to arrive only after at least a month's residence. In the case of vows, everyone agrees that a single day suffices. The reason is *letzaarei nafshia kavilah alei* — in making a vow, a person takes upon himself an act of self-affliction, and that self-affliction is achieved immediately, from the very first moment. Unlike a rental arrangement in which the practical benefit of the lease accumulates over time, the purpose of a vow of self-denial is accomplished from the instant the restriction takes effect. One need not endure the vow for a prolonged period for it to have been meaningful; even a single day of deprivation constitutes a genuine act of *inui*, and thus the most minimal reading of "this year" — even a single day — is legally sufficient.

## Stage-Based Designation

### Mishnah Stage Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַתִּלְתָּן — מִשֶּׁתִּצְמַח, הַתְּבוּאָה וְהַזֵּיתִים — מִשֶּׁיָּבִיאוּ שְׁלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Ma'asrot* 1:3): From when is produce obligated in tithes? **Fenugreek** is obligated **from** the time **when it sprouts. Grain and olives** are obligated **from** the time **when they have reached one-third** of their growth.

The *Gemara* now cites a *mishnah* from tractate *Ma'asrot*: *ha'taltan mishetitzmach* — fenugreek is governed from the time it sprouts; *ha'tvuah v'ha'zeitim misheyavi'u shlish* — grain and olives are governed from the time they have reached a third of their ripeness. This *mishnah* introduces a layer of complexity: while the calendar date (the first of Tishrei) is uniform across all categories, the operative event — the stage of development that assigns a given crop to a given year — differs by type of produce. Grain and olives are assigned by *shelish* (a third ripening); fenugreek by sprouting. Fruit, not mentioned here, will be assigned by blossoming. The *Gemara* has not yet explained why these different stages apply to different plants, but the question is noted.

### Shelish from Shmita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּמָטוּ בָּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִקֵּץ שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים בְּמוֹעֵד שְׁנַת הַשְּׁמִטָּה בְּחַג הַסּוּכּוֹת״, שְׁנַת הַשְּׁמִטָּה מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ בְּחַג הַסּוּכּוֹת? שְׁמִינִית הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, that the years of produce follow the first third of their growth? **Rav Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said, and** some **determined** that this statement was said **in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: The verse states: "At the end of every seven years, in the time of the year of release, in the festival of *Sukkot*"** (Deuteronomy 31:10). **What is the purpose of the Sabbatical Year** being mentioned **with the festival of *Sukkot*?** The festival of *Sukkot* is already **the eighth** year.

The *Gemara* focuses first on the rule of *shelish* for grain. Rav Assi cites Rabbi Yochanan — with some transmitters attributing it to Rabbi Yose HaGlili — who derives this from the verse concerning *hakhel*: *miketz sheva shanim b'mo'ed shnat ha'shmitah b'chag ha'sukkot* — "at the end of seven years, at the appointed time of the year of *shmita*, during *Sukkot*." The verse presents an apparent difficulty: if it is *at the end* of seven years, then *Sukkot* must already be in the eighth year. Why does the Torah call it *shnat ha'shmitah*? The answer is: *kol tvuah shehevia shelish bi'shvi'it lifnei Rosh Hashana, atah noheg bah minhag shevi'it bi'shminit* — any grain that reached a third of its ripeness during the seventh year before *Rosh Hashanah* retains the sanctity of the *shmita* year even when harvested in the eighth year. The verse calls it *shnat ha'shmitah* because there is still grain being harvested in that eighth year that carries *kedushat shevi'it*, having crossed the *Rosh Hashanah* threshold while already a third ripe.

### Challenge to Shelish Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לוֹמַר לָךְ: כׇּל תְּבוּאָה שֶׁהֵבִיאָה שְׁלִישׁ בַּשְּׁבִיעִית לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה — אַתָּה נוֹהֵג בּוֹ מִנְהַג שְׁבִיעִית בַּשְּׁמִינִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it comes **to tell you** that the *halakhot* of the Sabbatical Year continue to apply on *Sukkot* of the following year, as **you must treat all produce that reached one-third** of its growth **in the Sabbatical Year before Rosh HaShana with the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year,** even if it fully grows and is able to be used only **in the eighth** year.

Rav Zeira raises two challenges to this derivation. The first: perhaps the verse does not speak of the sanctity of fruit at all, but rather of a continuation of *shmita* practices — an extension of *shmita* law until *Sukkot*, analogous to the principle of *mosifin me'chol al ha'kodesh*, adding from the ordinary into the sacred. The *Gemara* dismisses this by reading the word *asif* — used in the verse *v'chag ha'asif b'tzet ha'shanah* — as referring to active harvesting, not to the general practices of *shmita*. If *Sukkot* is called the time of *asif*, it means people are harvesting then; one does not harvest during *shmita*, so the verse must be addressing grain that is being harvested, grain that grew during *shmita* and is now being cut in year eight.

The second challenge, pressed by Rav Zeira (or Rabbi Yirmiyah), is more pointed: granted that some grain harvested at *Sukkot* has *kedushat shevi'it*, how do you derive that the operative stage is precisely *shelish*? Even if we accept that grain harvested on *Sukkot* grew during *shmita*, how do we know it was *specifically* a third ripe before *Rosh Hashanah*, as opposed to some lesser stage? The *Gemara* answers: the rabbis knew — *v'kayam lehu l'rabbanan* — that any grain ready to be harvested on *Sukkot* was, by definition, a third ripe before *Rosh Hashanah*. The temporal relationship between the stage of *shelish* and the point of harvestability is established knowledge.

### Precision of Law Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַב אַסִּי: וְדִלְמָא לָא עָיֵיל כְּלָל, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא תְּשַׁמֵּט וְתֵיזִיל עַד חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rav Asi:** **But perhaps** the verse is referring to produce that **did not grow at all** during the seventh year, and **nevertheless, the Merciful One states** in the Torah that all the *halakhot* of the Sabbatical Year **continue to apply until the festival of *Sukkot*** of the eighth year.

At this point a deeper objection surfaces: even granting that *shelish* is the operative standard, how can a legal line be drawn at such a biologically imprecise threshold? What difference does a hair's breadth make — a grain that is just barely at *shelish* versus one that is barely less? The response is definitive: *kol midat chachamim ken hi* — this is the very nature of rabbinic legal measurement. The law draws lines and those lines hold without exception. The *mikvah* requires *arba'im se'ah* of water; *arba'im se'ah* minus a *kurtav* — a thumbnail's volume — and one cannot immerse: *b'arba'im se'ah chaser kurtav eino yachol litbol bahen*. A piece of food the size of an egg (*k'beitza*) imparts *tumah* of foodstuffs; a *k'beitza* minus a single sesame seed (*shum-shum*) does not. A piece of cloth three by three handbreadths (*sheloshah al sheloshah*) is susceptible to *midras* tumah from a *zav*; remove a single thread and it is not. Law requires defined thresholds; the question of whether one can always detect the precise threshold with certainty is beside the point. All measurements — including *beitza* and *mikvah* — are calibrated according to the aggregate norm and applied as consistently as humanly possible. The imprecision inherent in biological development no more undermines the *shelish* standard than the variability of individual throats undermines the egg-size standard for *tumah* of foodstuffs.

### Shelish Applications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְחַג הָאָסִיף בְּצֵאת הַשָּׁנָה״, מַאי ״אָסִיף״? אִילֵּימָא: חַג הַבָּא בִּזְמַן אֲסִיפָה — הָכְתִיב: ״בְּאׇסְפְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It should not enter your mind** to say this, **as it is written: "And the festival of gathering, which is at the end of the year,** when you have gathered in your labors out of the field" (Exodus 23:16). **What is** the meaning of **"gathering"? If we say** that it means: **A Festival that comes at the time of gathering** the crops, **isn't it** already **written: "When you have gathered** in your labors"? There is no need to repeat this a second time.

Tosafot draws a unifying conclusion from this discussion: the various stage-markers that appear throughout *Ma'asrot* — sprouting (*mishetitzmach*) for fenugreek, blossoming for fruit, third-ripening for grain and olives — are all, in fact, applications of the *shelish* principle. Each marker identifies the moment at which the produce in question reaches what is functionally equivalent to a third of its full development. The reason the *mishnah* does not simply say *misheyavi'u shlish* for all categories is that by most produce there are identifiable, visible signs that correspond to this stage and can be described concretely, whereas for grain and olives no such convenient external sign exists, so the quantitative measure of *shelish* must be stated explicitly.

### Taltan & Seed Planting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי ״אָסִיף״ — קָצִיר. וְקִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּכׇל תְּבוּאָה שֶׁנִּקְצְרָה בֶּחָג, בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהֵבִיאָה שְׁלִישׁ לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, וְקָא קָרֵי לַהּ ״בְּצֵאת הַשָּׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is** meant here by **"gathering"?** It means **harvesting. And the Sages have** an accepted **tradition that any grain that** reaches full growth so that it **is harvested on the festival** of *Sukkot* **is known** to **have reached one-third** of its growth **before Rosh HaShana, and** the Torah **calls** that period of the year until *Sukkot* **"at the end of the year,"** thereby indicating that it is still subject to the *halakhot* governing the previous year.

The *Yerushalmi* offers a further confirmation of this framework specifically regarding fenugreek. The word *mishetitzmach* — from the time it sprouts — is interpreted by Rabbi Zeira in the *Yerushalmi* through a *derashah* on the verse *aser te'aser et kol tevuat zarecha*: a thing that is planted and sprouts. What is excluded? That which has not yet reached *shelish*, because less than *shelish* ripe, the seeds are not yet fit for replanting. Once the seeds of the fenugreek are fit to be planted, *mishetitzmach* applies — and this is precisely the *shelish* threshold expressed in a form appropriate to fenugreek. The convergence of the *Bavli* and *Yerushalmi* on this point, as Tosafot notes, confirms that the diverse stage-markers throughout *Ma'asrot* are all expressions of a single underlying principle: the moment a crop's seeds are viable for replanting marks its legal transition into the category of produce subject to *ma'aser* year-assignment.
