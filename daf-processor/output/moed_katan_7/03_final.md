# Moed Katan 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trapping Pests

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִשְּׂדֵה הָאִילָן כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וּמִשְּׂדֵה הַלָּבָן שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: In an orchard** he may trap them **in his** usual **manner, but in a field of grain,** where there is no danger of substantial financial loss, he may only trap them in a way that is **not his** usual **manner.**

We open daf 7a by continuing the Mishnah's discussion of what may be done on *Chol HaMoed* in relation to fields — specifically, whether a given activity constitutes a *davar ha-aved* (a preventable loss that would justify the labor) or not. The Mishnah records the position of Rabbi Yehuda: in an orchard (*sdei ha-ilan*), one may lay traps for moles, weasels, and rats in the normal fashion (*kedarko*), whereas in a wheat field (*sdei ha-lavan*), one may only do so in an irregular fashion (*shelo kedarko*), since the potential loss in a wheat field is presumably not as severe.

It is worth pausing to note the dual context in which this rule applies. On *shevi'it*, the sabbatical year, trapping subterranean pests is difficult to categorize as a violation, since one is not actually working the land. On *Chol HaMoed*, however, the concern is labor itself — framed by the Gemara less in terms of the thirty-nine *melachos* and more broadly in terms of *tircha*, the expenditure of burdensome effort. The question of *tircha* turns out to be far wider in scope than the formal categories of prohibited labor.

### Trapping Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד כְּדַרְכּוֹ? חוֹפֵר גּוּמָּא וְתוֹלֶה בָּהּ מְצוּדָה. כֵּיצַד שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכּוֹ? נוֹעֵץ שַׁפּוּד וּמַכֶּה בְּקוּרְדּוֹם וּמְרַדֶּה הָאֲדָמָה מִתַּחְתֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **How** does one trap **in his** usual **manner? He digs a hole** in the ground **and hangs a trap in it. How** does one trap in a way that is **not his** usual **manner? He inserts a spit** into the ground where the rodents are suspected of hiding, **strikes** it **with a spade, and removes the earth from beneath it** until he finds and kills the rodents.

The Gemara now asks: what exactly constitutes a normal method (*keitzad kedarko*) of trapping these animals, and what is the irregular method? A *beraita* teaches: the normal method is *chofer guma vetoleh ba metzuda* — one digs a pit and places a trap within it. The abnormal method is *no'etz shipud umakkeh bekurdam* — one drives in a spike and strikes it with a hammer — and then either pounds down the earth beneath it (*umeraded ha-adamah mitachteiha*) or causes tremors in the surrounding soil that frighten and dislodge the subterranean pests.

### Field Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כְּשֶׁאָמְרוּ מִשְּׂדֵה לָבָן שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכּוֹ, לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׂדֵה לָבָן הַסְּמוּכָה לָעִיר, אֲבָל בִּשְׂדֵה לָבָן הַסְּמוּכָה לִשְׂדֵה הָאִילָן — אֲפִילּוּ כְּדַרְכּוֹ, שֶׁמָּא יֵצְאוּ מִשְּׂדֵה הַלָּבָן וְיַחְרִיבוּ אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: When they said** that one may trap moles and mice **in a field of grain** on the intermediate days of a Festival only in a way that is **not his** usual **manner, they spoke only with regard to a field of grain** that is **adjacent to the city,** where the damage is limited to that field and is not extensive. **But in a field of grain** that is **adjacent to an orchard,** one may trap **even in his** usual **manner, lest** the moles and mice **leave the field of grain and destroy the trees** in the adjacent orchard, causing great damage.

A further *beraita* is cited in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov (or Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar): the rule that in a wheat field one must use irregular methods applies only to a wheat field that is close to a city (*hasemucha le-ir*), where presumably such pests are less of a threat and there is no spillover danger. But if the wheat field is adjacent to an orchard (*hasemucha lesdei ilan*), even in the wheat field one may use the normal method of trapping. The reason is captured in the verb *veyachrivu* — the animals might escape from the wheat field and devastate the trees. The threat to an orchard is qualitatively more severe, and therefore the adjacent wheat field is treated with the same urgency.

## Wall Repairs

### Temporary Repairs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְקָרִין אֶת הַפִּירְצָה בַּמּוֹעֵד. כֵּיצַד מְקָרִין? רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בְּהוּצָא וְדַפְנָא. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: צָר בִּצְרוֹר, וְאֵינוֹ טָח בְּטִיט.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may seal a breach** in the wall of his garden **on** the intermediate days of **a Festival.** The Gemara asks: **How does one seal** such a breach? **Rav Yosef said: With palm branches [*hutza*] and** the branches of a **bay tree [*dafna*],** which do not create a significant partition, but simply a temporary barrier. **It was taught in a *baraita*: One fills** in the breach **with stone, but he does not plaster** the stones **with clay.** One may pack in stones (*tzar betzror*) but not plaster them over (*ve'eino tach batit*). One may make the repair sturdier, but not render it permanent. In context, the obvious purpose is to prevent animals — rabbits and the like — from entering and trampling or consuming the produce.

### Garden vs. Yard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כּוֹתֶל הַגִּינָה, אֲבָל כּוֹתֶל הֶחָצֵר — בּוֹנֶה כְּדַרְכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: They taught** that he may seal a breach but not build a wall in his usual manner **only** with regard to **the wall of a garden,** as no significant loss will be suffered if he delays building until after the Festival. **However,** with regard to **the wall of a courtyard,** which prevents the entry of strangers who are likely to steal from him, **he may build** a wall **in his** usual **manner** even on the intermediate days of a Festival.

The Gemara then records an important distinction introduced by Rav Chisda. He rules: *lo shanu ela kotel haginah* — the limitations just described apply only to the wall of a garden, a vegetable garden that one wants to protect from animals. But *kotel hachatzer* — the wall of one's yard or courtyard — may be built in the fully normal fashion on *Chol HaMoed*. Rabbeinu Gershom explains the rationale: the principal loss associated with a yard wall is protection of the home from potential robbers (*ganavim*), and that concern is sufficient to permit normal construction.

### Danger Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: כּוֹתֶל הַגּוֹחֶה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — סוֹתֵר וּבוֹנֶה כְּדַרְכּוֹ מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** the following *baraita* **supports** Rav Ḥisda's statement: With regard to **a wall that is leaning [*goḥeh*] toward the public domain** and is likely to fall, **one may demolish and rebuild** it **in his** usual **manner** on the intermediate days of a Festival, **due to the danger** that it poses to passersby.

The Gemara attempts to bring a proof for Rav Chisda's position from a *Tosefta*: if one has a wall leaning dangerously into the public domain (*reshut harabim*), one may knock it down and rebuild it entirely in the normal way — *mipnei hasakana*, on account of the danger to passersby. The Gemara reasons that since we are discussing a wall adjacent to the public thoroughfare, it must be the wall of a yard, not an agricultural garden — and yet full rebuilding is permitted, lending support to Rav Chisda.

### Sakana Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא — מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **There, the reason is as** the *baraita* explicitly **teaches,** i.e., it is **due to the danger** that the wall poses to passersby, and not due to the protection that it affords the courtyard.

The Gemara rejects this inference. The permission to rebuild fully, it responds, is not independent evidence for Rav Chisda's position. Rather, *hatam katani taama mipnei hasakana* — there, the reason is explicitly the danger. Since the *sakana* compels us to permit the knocking down (*soter*), the rebuilding necessarily follows. Without the *sakana*, however, even a yard wall could not be rebuilt in the normal way — a direct challenge to Rav Chisda.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב חִסְדָּא: הָתָם סוֹתֵר וּבוֹנֶה, הָכָא בּוֹנֶה וְלֹא סוֹתֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda** could have **said to you: There,** in the case where the existing wall poses a danger, **he is** even permitted to **demolish** the wall **and build** it from scratch. **Here,** in the case of an ordinary wall enclosing a courtyard, **he is permitted to build** the breached wall in the usual manner, **but not to demolish** it.

Rav Chisda responds: *hatam soter uboneh, hacha boneh velo soter*. The *sakana* is invoked there only to justify the demolition, which would otherwise be impermissible on *Chol HaMoed*. The rebuilding after the demolition follows naturally. But when there is simply a breach in the wall — no demolition involved, just filling — then building normally is permitted in a yard regardless of *sakana*, precisely as Rav Chisda holds. The Gemara then adds a practical observation: if one were told to knock down the dangerous wall but forbidden from immediately rebuilding, nobody would comply with the order to knock it down. Hence the permission to rebuild is bundled with the obligation to demolish, but this tells us nothing about independent rebuilding of a yard wall.

### Rav Ashi's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית בּוֹנֶה כְּדַרְכּוֹ. דְּהֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּחָצֵר, צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא לָאו, דְּגִינָּה. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּמַאן דְּעָבֵיד נְטִירוּתָא לְפֵירֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The wording of **the mishna is also precise** and indicates that it is referring to the wall of a garden, as understood by Rav Chisda, **as it teaches: During the Sabbatical Year one may** even **build in his** usual **manner. Where** is the wall to which the mishna refers? **If we say** that the mishna is referring to the wall **of a courtyard, need it be said** that it may be built during the Sabbatical Year? Only agricultural labors are prohibited during the Sabbatical Year, but construction is permitted. **Rather, is it not** referring to the wall of **a garden,** and it was necessary to state that this wall may be built during the Sabbatical Year to indicate that **even though he appears as one who is building a protection for his produce,** he is nevertheless permitted to do so. This proves that the mishna's discussion pertains to building the wall of a garden. **Learn from this** that Rav Chisda's interpretation is correct.

Rav Ashi offers a further linguistic argument from the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah states that on *shevi'it* one may build the wall in the normal way. Rav Ashi asks: if the Mishnah were discussing a yard wall, why would it need to say this? Repairing the wall of one's front yard has nothing whatsoever to do with the prohibitions of *shemitta*, which concern agricultural activity. The very fact that the Mishnah finds it necessary to permit such wall-building on *shevi'it* demonstrates that it is discussing an agricultural garden wall — for only in that context would the *shemitta* question be relevant. A yard wall requires no special dispensation under *shemitta* law. Thus the Mishnah throughout is addressing garden walls, and the question of yard walls is left to Rav Chisda's ruling.

### Netiruta Issues

This brings us to the interesting question of what the *chiddush* is in permitting garden wall repair on *shevi'it* at all. After all, on *shemitta* one is not working the land — one is merely erecting a fence around it. The Gemara addresses this through the concept of *netiruta l'peira* — the act of guarding or protecting the fruit. The concern is not simply agricultural labor; there is also the separate *shemitta* obligation to allow the fruit to be *hefker*, freely available to all. Putting up a wall that appears to demonstrate exclusive proprietary control over the produce could implicate that principle, even without any actual field work. The Mishnah therefore teaches that building the wall is nevertheless permitted, because one's purpose is not to assert ownership but rather to prevent trampling by animals and casual trespass — analogous to the laying of traps, which also protects the produce without constituting actual agricultural labor.

The distinction is a subtle but important one: there are forms of protecting produce on *shemitta* that shade into a prohibited assertion of ownership, and forms that are merely protective in the ordinary sense. The Mishnah permits the latter.

### Privacy vs. Robbery

Returning to the question of yard walls, the *Tosafot* here cites a ruling in the name of Rabbeinu Peretz: the *halacha* follows Rav Chisda, and the wall of a courtyard may be built normally on *Chol HaMoed*. However, Rabbeinu Peretz carefully limits this dispensation. It applies specifically to a wall situated adjacent to the public domain, where there is a genuine concern about robbers (*ganavim*). But a wall between adjoining courtyards — where the exposure is only to one's neighbors — may not be built in the normal way. This is a striking ruling, given how seriously the Gemara elsewhere treats the right to privacy. Rabbeinu Peretz is explicitly ruling that the basis for the leniency is protection from robbery, not the interest in privacy — and therefore the leniency does not extend to walls erected solely for privacy between neighbors.

## Tzara'at Inspection

### Rabbi Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִים אֶת הַנְּגָעִים בַּתְּחִילָּה לְהָקֵל, אֲבָל לֹ לְהַחְמִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא לְהָקֵל וְלֹא לְהַחְמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **MISHNA: Rabbi Meir says:** A priest **may initially examine** an individual showing **symptoms of leprosy on the intermediate days of a Festival** in order **to be lenient,** i.e., he may pronounce the individual to be free of leprosy, **but not** in order **to be stringent;** he may not pronounce the individual to be impure. **And the Rabbis say:** The priest may **not** examine the symptoms in order **to be lenient or** in order **to be stringent.**

We now move from matters of fields and walls to a topic of much broader significance on *Chol HaMoed*: the inspection of *tzara'at* marks. The Mishnah states, in the name of Rabbi Meir: *ro'in et hanega'im lehakel velo lehachmir* — one may examine bodily spots on *Chol HaMoed* in order to declare the person *tahor* (pure), but not in order to declare them *tamei* (impure). In other words, if upon examination the spot warrants a lenient ruling, the *kohein* may issue it. If the ruling would be stringent — if the person would need to be declared *tamei* — the *kohein* withholds judgment until after the festival.

The analogy that comes to mind is a medical professional who is permitted to tell a patient that a test result is clear, but defers to the physician to communicate concerning findings. Rabbi Meir's position creates precisely that asymmetry: good news may be delivered, bad news is held.

### Sages' Position

The Sages (*Chachamim*) take a stricter view: *lo lehakel velo lehachmir* — do not examine the spots at all on *Chol HaMoed*, neither to be lenient nor to be strict. The instinct might be to explain this as follows: if you look at the spot and say nothing, the person will understand from the silence that the news is bad, and you have effectively communicated *tamei* status without formally declaring it, causing anguish during the festival. But as we shall see, the Gemara's actual reasoning is more nuanced.

### Rabbi Yossi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִים אֶת הַנְּגָעִים לְהָקֵל, אֲבָל לֹא לְהַחְמִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא לְהָקֵל וְלֹא לְהַחְמִיר. שֶׁאִם אַתָּה נִזְקָק לוֹ לְהָקֵל — [אַתָּה] נִזְקָק לוֹ אַף לְהַחְמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir says:** A priest **may examine** an individual showing **symptoms of leprosy on the intermediate days of a Festival** in order **to be lenient, but not** in order **to be stringent. Rabbi Yosei says:** The priest **may not** examine the symptoms **to be lenient or to be stringent.** The reasoning behind Rabbi Yosei's opinion is **that if you attend to** the individual with the symptoms of leprosy **to be lenient, you must attend to him even to be stringent.** If the priest sees that the symptom is in fact leprosy, he must declare the affected person ritually impure rather than remain silent. Consequently, in order to avoid declaring that he has leprosy on the Festival, the priest should not examine him at all.

A *beraita* is cited that presents a parallel dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi. Rabbi Meir's position is reproduced: one may look at the spot to be lenient but not to be strict. Rabbi Yossi says: neither to be lenient nor to be strict. But Rabbi Yossi's reason is specific: *she'im ata nizkak lo lehakel, nizkak lo af lehachmir* — once you are *nizkak lo*, once you have committed yourself to addressing the case and examining the person, you are obligated to give a ruling one way or the other. You cannot selectively deliver only the favorable result. Therefore, Rabbi Yossi's position is not that you are prohibited from examining; it is that examination entails an obligation to rule completely, and since you may be forced to rule stringently, you should not begin the examination at all.

## Musgar/Muchlat

### Status Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמוּסְגָּר, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּמוּחְלָט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The statement of Rabbi Meir appears** to be correct **with regard** to the case of **a quarantined** leper. **And the statement of Rabbi Yosei appears** to be correct **with regard to** the case of **a confirmed** leper.

Rebbi (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) issues a ruling that attempts to reconcile the positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi by applying each to a different category of *metzora*. He says: *nirin divrei Rabbi Meir bemusgar, vedivrei Rabbi Yossi bemuchlat* — the reasoning of Rabbi Meir seems applicable when the person is a *musgar*, and the reasoning of Rabbi Yossi when the person is a *muchlat*.

These two statuses require careful definition, as they represent distinct stages in the *tzara'at* examination process. When a person presents a suspicious spot to a *kohein*, the *kohein* has several possible responses. He may declare the person *tahor* outright. Or, if the status is uncertain, he may quarantine the individual for a week — a status called *musgar* (*hisgiro hakohen*, the *kohein* has locked him in). The *musgar* is fully *tamei* but in a preliminary, provisional state. After one week, the *kohein* examines again. Three outcomes are possible: the spot has resolved entirely, in which case the person is declared *tahor*; the spot has grown, in which case the person becomes a *muchlat* — a full, confirmed *metzora*; or the spot is unchanged, in which case the quarantine continues for a second week, at the end of which the person will be either *muchlat* or *tahor*.

The Gemara in *Megillah* notes: *ein bein Metzora Musgar leMetzora Muchlat ella peria u'frima* — the essential difference between the *musgar* and the *muchlat* is the latter's obligation to let his hair grow wild and to rend his garments, and to call out *tamei tamei*. In terms of underlying *tumah* status and physical separation from the community, both are largely equivalent — both are *chutz lamachaneh*, outside the camp. But the social and visible dimension of ostracization is far more pronounced for the *muchlat*.

The language *nirin divrei Rabbi Meir* is somewhat puzzling, as Rebbi is not actually ruling like Rabbi Meir in the sense of permitting examination with selective disclosure. Rebbi operates under the assumption of Rabbi Yossi — that examination commits one to full disclosure. What he is saying, then, is that in the *musgar* case we will proceed with examination (because the downside is manageable), and in the *muchlat* case we will not.

### Consensus Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּטָהוֹר כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא חָזוּ לֵיהּ. בְּהֶסְגֵּר רִאשׁוֹן דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּחָזֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: With regard to** an individual with symptoms of leprosy who is still **ritually pure,** i.e., who has not yet been examined by a priest, **everyone agrees that** the priest **does not examine him,** as his status can only worsen due to the examination. **With regard to** a suspected leper who is in his **first** week of **quarantine, everyone agrees that** the priest **examines him,** as the priest may declare him pure if his symptoms have subsided, and even if his symptoms remained as they were, he will simply be quarantined for another week.

Rava clarifies the contours of the dispute. Two cases are uncontested. First: a person who is entirely *tahor* and has not yet appeared before a *kohein* — everyone agrees one should not examine him on *Chol HaMoed*. If you look at him, you may be compelled to rule him *tamei*, transforming someone currently unencumbered into a person with *tzara'at* status. The risk is entirely downward; there is no benefit. Second: a person who is in the *hesger rishon*, the first week of quarantine — everyone agrees one should examine him. Why? Because whatever happens, his status cannot be meaningfully worsened. He is already *tamei* and *musgar*. Examination can only either release him (*tahor*) or extend his quarantine for another week — a continuation of the status quo. There is no catastrophic downward movement, and the possibility of improvement justifies looking.

### Second Stage Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי, בְּהֶסְגֵּר שֵׁנִי. מָר סָבַר: בְּכֹהֵן תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא. אִי טָהוֹר — אָמַר לֵיהּ "טָהוֹר", וְאִי טָמֵא — שָׁתֵיק. וּמָר סָבַר: "לְטַהֲרוֹ אוֹ לְטַמְּאוֹ" כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree,** it is with regard to **a suspected leper who is already in his second** week of **quarantine. One Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **the matter depends upon** the discretion of **the priest; if he is** found ritually **pure, the priest declares him pure, and if he is** found **ritually impure, the priest** can **remain silent.** **And one Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that since **it is written:** "This is the law of the plague of leprosy…**to pronounce it pure or to pronounce it impure"** (Leviticus 13:59), the priest is not permitted to be silent; just as he is obligated to declare him pure when that is the case, so too, he is bound to declare him impure when his symptoms indicate impurity.

The dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi, Rava explains, is specifically over *hesger sheni* — the second stage of quarantine. This is where the stakes are real: the spot could resolve (*tahor*), remain (*musgar* continues, leading eventually to *tahor* or *muchlat*), or worsen (*muchlat*). Rabbi Meir holds that the *kohein* has discretion: *bei'kohein talya milta* — it depends on what the *kohein* says. If the ruling is favorable, declare *tahor*; if the ruling would be stringent, withhold it. The person does not become *muchlat* until the *kohein* utters the ruling. Rabbi Yossi counters with a verse: *le-taharo o le-tame'o ketiv* — Scripture indicates that examination entails a binary obligation to either purify or declare impure. Once you examine, you must rule one way or the other.

### Worsening Status

This brings us to a counterintuitive result in Rebbi's initial formulation. He says we apply Rabbi Yossi's principle — i.e., we do not examine — to the *muchlat*, and we do examine the *musgar*. Intuitively, one would expect the opposite: surely it is safer to examine the *muchlat*, whose status can only improve. Why would we withhold examination from the one person for whom examination could only help?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי. מָר סָבַר: צַוְותָּא דְעָלְמָא עֲדִיף לֵיהּ. וּמָר סָבַר: צַוְותָּא דְאִשְׁתּוֹ עֲדִיפָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is** a dispute between ***tanna'im* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **One Sage,** the author of the latter *baraita*, **holds that the company of the world** at large **is preferable to** the leper. **And one Sage,** the author of the *baraita* (7a), **holds that the company of his wife is preferable to** the leper.

The Gemara's answer introduces a remarkable consideration: *tannai hi aliba d'Rebbe* — there is actually a dispute among *tannaim* about Rebbi's ruling, reflecting two different assessments of what is beneficial for the *metzora*. The question turns on whether the person prefers *tzavta d'alma* — the company of the broader community — or *tzavta d'ishto* — the company of his wife.

## Marriage & Tzara'at

### Wife vs. Community

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּמוּחְלָט מוּתָּר בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה? אִין, וְהָתַנְיָא: "וְיָשַׁב מִחוּץ לְאׇהֳלוֹ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים", שֶׁיְּהֵא אָסוּר בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה. וְאֵין "אׇהֳלוֹ" אֶלָּא אִשְׁתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לֵךְ אֱמוֹר לָהֶם שׁוּבוּ לָכֶם לְאׇהֳלֵיכֶם". רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: "שִׁבְעַת יָמִים יִסְפְּרוּ לוֹ", יְמֵי סְפִירוֹ וְלֹא יְמֵי חִלּוּטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Is this to say that a confirmed** leper **is permitted** to engage **in conjugal relations** with his wife? **Yes, and** so too **it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a leper who is counting his seven days, it is written: **"But he shall remain outside his tent seven days"** (Leviticus 14:8). This verse teaches **that** the leper **is prohibited** from engaging **in conjugal relations,** as the words **his tent** refer **only** to **his wife, as it is stated: "Return again to your tents"** (Deuteronomy 5:27). **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The verse states: "And after he is cleansed, **they shall count for him seven days"**, indicating that he is prohibited from having conjugal relations during **the days of his counting, but not** during **the days of his confirmed** leprosy.

Both the *musgar* and the *muchlat* are *chutz lamachaneh* — outside the camp, separated from the community. But the two statuses differ in one crucial respect when it comes to marital relations. The verse says *v'yashav michutz le-ohalo shivat yamim* — he shall dwell outside his tent for seven days. The word *ohalo*, his tent, is understood by the Gemara as a reference to his wife — as we know from the parallel usage at *Matan Torah*, where *shuvu lachem le-oholeichem* meant returning to one's wife, while Moshe alone was told *ve-ata po amod imadi*. The verse in *tzara'at* is thus understood to prohibit marital relations during the seven-day *tahara* purification period, after the *metzora* has been declared *tahor* and is in the process of becoming fully purified.

The question is therefore: during the *musgar* and *muchlat* stages themselves, before the *tahara* period begins, is the *metzora* permitted or forbidden to his wife?

### Intimacy Rules

This question is the subject of a *tannaitic* dispute. Rabbi Yehuda rules: *yemei sipuro velo yemei chaluto* — the prohibition on marital relations applies during the days of counting (the *tahara* period), but not during the days of *chalut* (the full *muchlat* status). During the *chalut* period, despite being fully *tamei* and *chutz lamachaneh*, the *metzora* is still permitted to his wife. She may come to him outside the camp.

### Tannaitic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה יְמֵי סְפִירוֹ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לִימֵי חִלּוּטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** Since the verse indicates that the prohibition to engage in conjugal relations applies during **the seven days of his counting** before becoming ritually pure, it follows based on an ***a fortiori*** inference that the prohibition should also apply during **the days of his confirmed** leprosy, when his impurity is more severe.

Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, arguing by means of a *kal vachomer*: if even during the process of becoming *tahor* — the *tahara* period — the Torah prohibits marital relations, then certainly during the *chalut* period, when the person is in the most severe *tamei* state, marital relations should be forbidden. This is the logical reading: if even the cleaner state is restricted, how much more the more impure one?

One might challenge: can one simply reject a *kal vachomer* by claiming *mai degali gali u'mai dela gali la gali* — the Torah said what it said, and did not apply the restriction elsewhere? The *Tosafot* addresses this, noting that such an argument could undermine any *kal vachomer*. The *Tosafot* suggests that Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda may hold that *tzara'at* is a *chiddush* — an anomalous set of laws that do not follow regular halachic logic — and therefore logical extensions cannot be assumed.

### Uziyahu Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא, דַּנְתִּי לִפְנֵי רַבִּי: לִימַּדְתָּנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, יוֹתָם לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לְעוּזִּיָּהוּ אֶלָּא בִּימֵי חִלּוּטוֹ, אָמַר לוֹ: אַף אֲנִי כָּךְ אָמַרְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya said: I deliberated** this matter **before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and said: **Have you** not **taught us, our teacher,** that King **Jotham was only** born **to Uzziah,** the king of Judah, **during the days of his confirmed** leprosy? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: I too said this;** I am also of the opinion that a confirmed leper is permitted to engage in conjugal relations.

Rabbi Chiyya reports that he discussed this question before Rebbi, who responded: *limadtanu rabbenu, Yotam lo haya lo le-Uziyahu ela bi-yemei chaluto* — our master taught us: Yotam was born to Uziyahu only during his *chalut* period. Since Uziyahu was a *metzora* in full *muchlat* status, yet his wife conceived during that time, we have historical precedent — derived from the biblical verses as analyzed by the *Tosafot* — that a *muchlat* is permitted to his wife. Rebbi confirmed: *af ani kach amarti* — I have always said the same.

### Alternative Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בִּימֵי סְפִירוֹ וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בִּימֵי חִלּוּטוֹ. וּמָר סָבַר: מַאי דְּגַלִּי — גַּלִּי, וּמַאי דְּלָא גַּלִּי — לָא גַּלִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do **they disagree?** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds: The Merciful One revealed** the prohibition of conjugal relations with one's wife **during the days of his counting; and all the more so** the prohibition applies **during his days of confirmed** leprosy. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **maintains: That which** the verse **revealed, it revealed, but that which it did not reveal, it did not reveal;** the prohibition is not interpreted in a way that adds an extra stringency beyond what is stated explicitly in the Torah.

With this clarification, we can now understand the two versions of Rebbi's ruling. One version says: do not examine the *muchlat* on *Chol HaMoed*, because if you tell him he is *tahor* and initiates the *tahara* process, he will be separated from his wife — a genuine worsening of his personal situation even as his halachic status improves. He may well prefer to remain *muchlat* and retain marital relations rather than begin the path to *tahara* and be separated from his wife, even temporarily. Therefore, examine the *musgar* (where this specific concern does not apply in the same way) and withhold examination from the *muchlat*. The alternative version follows the more intuitive logic: examine the *muchlat* because his status can only improve, and withhold examination from the *musgar* because examination there risks pushing him into the *muchlat* category — a genuine and visible worsening, marked by rent garments, wild hair, and the cry of *tamei tamei yikra*.

The *Tosafot* also touches on the psychological dimension: in the case of the *musgar*, the person has reason to hope that the week will resolve favorably; to be told at that stage that he is confirmed *tamei* is not only a change in status but a blow to his sense of imminent recovery. These considerations, while not made explicit in the Gemara itself, are noted by the *Tosafot* as legitimate.

## Kohen Discretion

### Tamei Determination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּבְכֹהֵן תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא? אִין, וְהָתַנְיָא: "וּבְיוֹם הֵרָאוֹת בּוֹ". יֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ יוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Is this to say that the matter depends upon** the discretion of **the priest,** i.e., the priest can decide whether to declare the affected person ritually pure or impure or whether to examine the leprous symptoms or not? **Yes, and** so too **it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"But on the day it appears in him"** (Leviticus 13:14), from which it may be inferred that **there is a day when you examine** the symptoms found in **him and there is a day when you do not examine** those symptoms.

The Gemara now addresses directly the foundational principle that has been operative throughout this discussion: *bei'kohein talya milta* — the *tamei* status of the *metzora* depends on the *kohein*'s utterance, not on some pre-existing objective reality. Until the *kohein* declares the person *tamei*, the halachic status has not taken effect. This is why the question of whether and when to examine has any relevance at all: withholding examination is not merely a formality, it genuinely postpones the imposition of *tamei* status.

A *beraita* establishes this from a verse: *uv'yom hera'ot bo* — on the day that the spot is presented to him. The Gemara derives from this: *yeish yom she'ata ro'eh, ve'yeish yom she'ei ata ro'eh bo* — there are days that you examine and days that you do not. The very phrasing implies that the *kohein* exercises discretion about when to look, and that during periods of non-examination, no status change occurs.

### Days of Examination

The derivation establishes a principle of discretionary timing: the *kohein* is not compelled to examine on any particular day. The legal status of the *metzora* is thus genuinely contingent on the *kohein*'s action, not merely announced by it.

### Special Exemptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: חָתָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד בּוֹ נֶגַע — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שִׁבְעָה יְמֵי הַמִּשְׁתֶּה. לוֹ וּלְבֵיתוֹ וְלִכְסוּתוֹ. וְכֵן בָּרֶגֶל — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הָרֶגֶל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From here they stated:** With regard to **a bridegroom upon whom leprous symptoms came into being, we give him the seven days of the** wedding **feast** before the examination that determines ritual purity or impurity. This ruling applies whether the leprous symptoms appeared **upon him, upon his house, or upon his clothing. Similarly,** if the symptoms of leprosy appeared upon an individual **during a pilgrimage Festival, we give him the seven days of the Festival** in order to avoid causing him distress during that time; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

From this principle, the *beraita* draws practical applications: *mikan amru, chatan shenolad bo nega* — from here the rabbis derived that if a groom develops a spot, he is given the full seven days of the wedding celebration (*shivat yemei hamishte*). This applies not only to a spot on his body but also to spots on his garments or his house. Similarly, during a *regel* — a pilgrimage festival — one is given the entirety of the festival days without examination. This is the view of Rabbi Yehuda. In his formulation, the exemption from examination during these periods is not merely about emotional consideration; it reflects the *kohein*'s formal authority to postpone examination, grounded in the verse.

### House Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: "וְצִוָּה הַכֹּהֵן וּפִנּוּ אֶת הַבַּיִת". אִם מַמְתִּינִים לוֹ לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת — כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן לִדְבַר מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The ruling is correct, but **there is no need** to prove it from this verse, as a much simpler proof can be brought from a different source. **It says: "Then the priest shall command that they empty the house** before the priest goes into it to see the plague, so that all that is in the house be not made unclean"** (Leviticus 14:36). **If we delay** the priest's examination of the house in order to give the owner time to remove his utensils and prevent them from contracting ritual impurity, which is merely **an optional matter, all the more so** should we delay his examination **for a matter of mitzva,** e.g., so as not to detract from the bridegroom's joy or from the joy of a Festival.

Rebbi disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda — not about the practical ruling, but about its derivation. Rebbi says: *eino tzarich* — one need not derive the *kohein*'s discretion from *uv'yom hera'ot bo*. There is another verse: *v'tzivah hakohein u'pinu et habayit* — when a house shows signs of *tzara'at*, the *kohein* commands that the house be cleared before he enters to examine it, so that nothing inside should become *tamei* as a result of his declaration. The verse thus explicitly builds in a waiting period — the clearing of the house — before the *kohein* issues his ruling. From this we learn that even for a matter of mere convenience (*davar hareshut*), not involving a *mitzvah*, one may postpone the examination. And if we postpone for something as incidental as clearing furniture, how much more so do we postpone for *simchat haregel* or *simchat hanisuin* — the joy of the festival or of a wedding.

One student made the astute observation that this verse itself demonstrates that *tumah* is created by the *kohein*'s utterance rather than existing independently — for if the *tumah* were already present, it would make no difference whether the *kohein* issued his ruling before or after the house was cleared. The very fact that the clearing is meant to prevent the objects inside from becoming *tamei* shows that the objects become *tamei* only upon the *kohein*'s declaration, and by delaying that declaration, one genuinely prevents their contamination.

The practical implication, according to Rebbi, is broader than Rabbi Yehuda's ruling: if we can postpone examination even for a *davar hareshut* — a mere inconvenience with no *mitzvah* dimension — then the *kohein* has wide general discretion about when to examine. The person need not even be engaged in a *mitzvah* such as a wedding or *regel* for the *kohein* to defer inspection.

### Chiddush Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — מֵהָתָם לָא גָּמְרִינַן, דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא, דְּהָא עֵצִים וַאֲבָנִים בְּעָלְמָא לָא מִטַּמְּאוּ, וְהָכָא מִטַּמְּאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** why is it not permitted to delay the priest's examination of leprous symptoms on an individual's body for an optional matter, just as with regard to leprous symptoms on one's house? **We do not learn** a halakhic principle **from there because** the *halakha* of leprosy of houses **is** itself **a novelty,** a unique biblical law from which one cannot extrapolate to other cases. The Gemara explains that the *halakha* of leprous symptoms on a house constitutes a novelty, **as** by Torah law, **wood and stones are generally not susceptible to ritual impurity, yet here** in the case of house leprosy **they are susceptible to ritual impurity.**

Rabbi Yehuda objects to Rebbi's use of the house case as a source for discretionary authority over body examinations. His argument: *de-chiddush hu* — the *tzara'at* of a house is itself an anomaly, a *chiddush*. Normally, raw materials such as wood and stone cannot become *tamei*; *tumah* requires a vessel. Furthermore, built structures attached to the ground (*mechubar l'karka*) are not subject to *tumah*. And the stones of a *metzora*'s house, even after demolition, retain their *tumah* — something unparalleled in normal halachic categories. Because the entire institution of house *tzara'at* is a *chiddush* that defies ordinary halachic logic, any special rules embedded within it — including the *kohein*'s discretion about when to examine — cannot be exported to other domains. You cannot derive from an anomalous exception a general principle applicable to the normal category of bodily *tumah*.

### Rabbi's Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אָמַר: אִצְטְרִיךְ, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "וּבְיוֹם הֵרָאוֹת בּוֹ", הָוֵה אָמֵינָא לִדְבַר מִצְוָה — אִין, לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת — לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "וְצִוָּה הַכֹּהֵן". וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "וְצִוָּה הַכֹּהֵן", הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי — אִין, דְּלָאו טוּמְאָה דְגוּפֵיהּ, אֲבָל טוּמְאָה דְגוּפֵיהּ — אֵימָא מִיחְזָא חַזְיָא לֵיהּ. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: It was necessary** for the Torah to state both verses: "But on the day it appears in him" (Leviticus 13:14), as well as: "Then the priest shall command that they empty the house" (Leviticus 14:36). **As, if the Merciful One had written** only: **"But on the day it appears in him," I would have said** that **for a matter of mitzva, yes,** one may delay the priest's examination of the leprous symptoms, but **for an optional matter, no,** one may not, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. Therefore **the Merciful One wrote: "Then the priest shall command,"** in order to teach that one may delay his examination even for an optional matter. **And** conversely, **if the Merciful One had written** only: **"Then the priest shall command," I would have said** that **for these** utensils that are found in the house, **yes,** one may delay the priest's examination, as this is **not** a case of **impurity of the** individual's **body** but only that of his possessions; **but** in a case of **impurity of** the individual's **body, I** would **say** that the priest **must examine it** immediately. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the Torah to state that even in this case, one may delay his examination.

Rebbi responds by explaining why both verses are necessary. Had the Torah only given us *uv'yom hera'ot bo* — suggesting that there are days when one examines and days when one does not — one might have concluded that the exception applies only to *mitzvah* occasions such as a *regel* or wedding, but not to a *davar hareshut*. Had the Torah only given us *v'tzivah hakohein u'pinu et habayit* — establishing that even a *davar hareshut* can justify postponement — one might have thought that such broad discretion applies only to house *tzara'at*, which is not the person's own body and is a *chiddush* category, but not to bodily *tumah*, which is more weighty and about which one might expect a stricter rule. By giving us both verses, the Torah establishes: from the house case, we learn that discretion extends even to *davar hareshut*; and from the body verse, we learn that this discretionary principle applies even to bodily *tumah*. Together, they yield Rebbi's position: the *kohein* has broad discretion to postpone examination of a *metzora*'s body even in circumstances that do not involve a formal *mitzvah*.

The *Tosafot* clarifies the precise contours of this dispute: according to Rabbi Yehuda, the *kohein*'s discretion over body examinations is limited to cases of *mitzvah* — festival and wedding — while his discretion over house examinations is broader, extending to *davar hareshut*. According to Rebbi, both verses together yield broad discretion for body examinations as well, matching the standard for house examinations. We will continue from here in the next shiur.
