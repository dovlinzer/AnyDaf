# Bava Kamma 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Second Theft & Yeush

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין הַגּוֹנֵב אַחַר הַגַּנָּב מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, אֲבָל לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ – קְנָאוֹ גַּנָּב רִאשׁוֹן, וְגַנָּב שֵׁנִי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל לְגַנָּב רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** One who steals an item after a thief has already stolen it, i.e., one who steals a stolen item, **does not pay** the **double payment** to the thief or to the prior owner. Rather, he pays the principal amount alone. **Rav says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where the second thief stole from the first thief **before** the owner's **despair** of recovering his item. **But** if the second thief stole it **after** the owner's **despair, the first thief had acquired** the stolen item for himself as a result of the owner's despair, **and the second thief pays** the **double payment to the first thief,** who at the time of the second theft was its legal owner.

Daf 68 opens by returning to the *mishna* that began the seventh *perek*. The *mishna* establishes that a second thief who steals from a first thief (*ganav*) does not pay *tashlumei kefel* — the double payment — because the first *ganav* did not own the stolen item. Similarly, if the second thief slaughters the animal, he does not pay the fourfold or fivefold penalty (*tashlumei arba'ah v'chamisha*) to the first *ganav*, again because ownership had not transferred to the first *ganav*.

### Rav's Position

*Amar Rav* — Rav qualifies the *mishna*'s ruling: *lo shanu ela lifnei ye'ush* — the exemption of the second *ganav* applies only when the theft occurred before *ye'ush*, the owner's despair of recovering the stolen item. But *aval l'achar ye'ush kano ganav rishon* — after *ye'ush*, the first *ganav* acquires ownership of the stolen item. This reflects the position that *ye'ush lechudei kanei* — *ye'ush* alone is sufficient to transfer ownership — and consequently the second *ganav* would owe *tashlumei kefel* to the first *ganav*, who is now the legal owner.

### Rav Sheshet Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אָמֵינָא כִּי נָיֵים וְשָׁכֵיב רַב אָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה טָבַח וּמָכַר מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּשְׁתָּרֵשׁ בַּחֵטְא. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: I say** that **when Rav was sleepy and lying down** to rest **he said this *halakha*,** i.e., he did not give it enough thought. This ruling is incorrect, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva said: For what reason did the Torah say that** if a thief **slaughtered or sold** a stolen ox or sheep **he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment?** It is **because** by selling or slaughtering the animal the thief **becomes** more **deeply entrenched in sin.** Rav Sheshet analyzes Rabbi Akiva's statement: **When** did this sale of the stolen animal take place? **If we say** it occurred **before** the owner's **despair** of recovering his property,

Rav Sheshet objects sharply, introducing a *beraita* that appears to contradict this position. The *beraita* asks: why did the Torah say that one who slaughters or sells a stolen animal pays four or five? Rabbi Akiva answers: *mipnei shenishtares b'cheit* — because the thief has driven his sin deeper, setting further roots into the transgression. The act of slaughter or sale deepens the original sin of theft.

## Nishtaresh B'Cheit

### R. Akiva

Rabbi Akiva's principle of *nishtaresh b'cheit* — deepening or entrenching the sin — serves as the rationale for the obligation of *tashlumei arba'ah v'chamisha*. By slaughtering or selling the stolen animal, the thief goes beyond the original act of theft and compounds his wrongdoing.

### Timing Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אִיכָּא נִשְׁתָּרֵשׁ? אֶלָּא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יֵאוּשׁ קָנֵי, אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה? שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ, שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר!
>
> **Translation:** is it possible to say that **there** is any **entrenchment** in sin? The sale does not take effect, as it occurred before the owner despaired, which means that the thief did not in fact deepen his sin by selling the animal. **Rather,** it must be that the sale occurred **after** the owner's **despair. And if it enters your mind** that the owner's **despair** causes the thief to **acquire** the animal, **why does he pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment?** Since the animal belongs to him after the owner's despair, in effect **he slaughters his own** animal or **he sells his own** animal.

The *Gemara* then presses on the timing: when, precisely, does this deepening of sin occur? If we say it refers to a sale before *ye'ush* — *iyleima lifnei ye'ush* — the question arises: *mi ika nishtares*? In what sense has the sin deepened? The animal has merely been transferred to another person, but it has not been taken any further from the owner's sphere; the owner has not yet despaired of recovering it. The sin has not truly gone deeper in any meaningful sense.

Therefore, the *Gemara* concludes, it must be referring to a sale *after* *ye'ush* — *ela l'achar ye'ush*. At that point, *ye'ush* alone has not yet transferred ownership, but now the *ganav* sells it, creating *ye'ush v'shinui reshus* — despair combined with a change of domain — which genuinely removes the object from the owner's legal ownership. That is when the sin has truly been entrenched.

The critical inference follows: *v'ei salka da'atach ye'ush lechudei kanei* — if *ye'ush* alone were sufficient to transfer ownership to the first *ganav*, then when he subsequently sold the animal after *ye'ush*, he would be selling something already his. *Shelo tovach v'shelo mocher* — he would be slaughtering or selling his own property, for which there can be no *chiyuv* of four and five. The fact that the Torah obligates him for the sale therefore demonstrates that *ye'ush* alone is not *koneh* — it does not transfer ownership.

### Rava's Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי, כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשָּׁנָה בַּחֵטְא; הָכָא נָמֵי, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשָּׁנָה בַּחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say:** It is possible to explain the term: Entrenched in sin, **in accordance with** what **Rava said:** The thief is liable **because he repeated** his **sin,** i.e., he sinned a second time by going through the motions of the sale, regardless of the fact the sale was not legally binding. **Here too,** one can say that the thief is liable **because he repeated** his **sin.**

The *Gemara* responds on behalf of Rav's position: *k'd'amar Rava* — as Rava explained, the phrase *nishtaresh b'cheit* need not be interpreted as "deepening" the sin. Rava reads it as *shana b'cheit* — the thief has repeated his sin. Even if the sale did not push the stolen object further from the owner's legal grasp, the thief has once again demonstrated his brazen disregard for the owner's ownership by treating the animal as his own to dispose of as he wishes. This repetition of the original transgression is sufficient grounds for the obligation, even before *ye'ush*.

Under Rava's reading, the obligation for *mechira* falls *before* *ye'ush*, consistent with Rav's position that *ye'ush* is *koneh*. Under the alternative reading of *nishtaresh b'cheit* — genuine deepening of the sin — the obligation falls *after* *ye'ush*, which simultaneously demonstrates that *ye'ush* alone is not *koneh*.

## First Beraita

### Slaughter/Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וּטְבָחוֹ אוֹ מְכָרוֹ״; מַה טְבִיחָה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, אַף מְכִירָה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, אַמַּאי אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת? אֶלָּא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קַנְיֵיהּ, אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה? שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ, שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: It is written: "If a man steal an ox or a sheep, **and slaughter it or sell it"** (Exodus 21:37). **Just as slaughter** is an act **that cannot be undone, so too** the **selling** referred to here is an act **that cannot be undone.** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: Now, **when** is a sale irreversible? **If we say** this sale occurred **before** the owner's **despair** of recovering his stolen animal, **why can it not be undone?** Since the thief's sale of the animal before the owner's despair is ineffective, the sale is easily undone. **Rather,** the *baraita* must be speaking about a case where the thief sold the animal **after** the owner's **despair. And if it enters your mind** that a thief **acquires** a stolen item after the owner's despair, **why does he pay the fourfold or fivefold payment** for slaughtering or selling the animal at this point? **He slaughters his own** animal or **he sells his own** animal. Apparently, a thief does not acquire the stolen item after the owner's despair.

The *Gemara* now brings a *beraita* as further evidence. The *beraita* states: *u'tevacho u'mecharo* — if one slaughters or sells the animal — *mah tevicha she'eina chozeres af mechira she'eina chozeres* — just as slaughter cannot be undone and the object cannot be restored to its original state, so too the *mechira* must be one that cannot be undone and restored. The *Gemara* asks: when does this apply? If before *ye'ush* — *iyleima lifnei ye'ush* — why would the sale be irreversible? The owner can simply recover the object from whoever holds it; it has not truly changed status. It must therefore be that the *beraita* is speaking of a sale *after* *ye'ush* — *ela l'achar ye'ush* — at which point the purchaser legally owns it, and the matter cannot be undone. This parallels slaughter, which likewise cannot be reversed.

The inference follows immediately: if *ye'ush* alone transferred ownership to the *ganav*, he would be selling his own property and could not be obligated for four and five. The *beraita*'s requirement of *she'eina chozeres* — irreversibility — implies that the *mechira* occurs after *ye'ush* while the *ganav* still does not yet own the object, proving *ye'ush* is not *koneh*.

### Rav Nachman's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: פְּרָט לְשֶׁהִקְנָה לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם, הָכָא נָמֵי, פְּרָט לְשֶׁהִקְנָה לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: The explanation for this *baraita* is **as Rav Naḥman said** in reference to a different *baraita*. Rav Naḥman said that when that *baraita* speaks of a sale that cannot be undone, this **excludes one who transferred** ownership of the animal to another in a temporary manner, e.g., **for thirty days. Here too,** one can answer in the same way, that it **excludes one who transferred** ownership of the animal **for thirty days.** Accordingly, if one sold the animal permanently, even if the sale was performed before the owner despaired and is therefore not legally binding, he is nevertheless required to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

The *Gemara* deflects this proof by invoking Rav Nachman's interpretation. Rav Nachman explains *she'eina chozeres* as referring not to whether the object physically returns to the original owner, but to the *intention* with which the sale is framed: *prat l'shehikna lishloshim yom* — excluding a sale made explicitly for thirty days. The Torah's obligation of four and five applies only when the *ganav* sells the animal in the framework of a permanent transaction. If Reuven the *ganav* sells to Shimon saying "I sell this to you for thirty days," that is not the kind of *mechira* for which the Torah imposes liability. *She'eina chozeres* thus characterizes the nature of the sale's intent, not its actual legal effect. Under this reading, the *beraita* offers no proof about *ye'ush* at all, and the challenge is neutralized.

## Sequential Theft Cases

### Simple Theft Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: גָּנַב וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא קֶרֶן בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from another *baraita*: If one **stole** an animal, **and another** person subsequently **came and stole it** from him, **the first** thief **pays double payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays only the principal** to the first thief. This is because one who steals from a thief does not pay the double payment, as the mishna teaches.

The *Gemara* advances to a second *beraita* containing a series of sequential theft scenarios. The first case: *ganav uva acher ugnavo* — Reuven stole an animal, and Shimon came and stole it from Reuven. The *beraita* rules that Reuven pays *tashlumei kefel* to the original owner, while Shimon pays only *keren* — the principal — to Reuven, and nothing more. Shimon does not pay *kefel* because Reuven does not own the animal; one cannot incur the double payment penalty by stealing from someone who is not the true owner.

### Theft-Sale-Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּנַב וּמָכַר, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. גָּנַב וְטָבַח, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, אֶלָּא קֶרֶן בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** If one **stole** an animal **and sold** it, **and another** individual **came and stole it** from the purchaser, **the first** thief **pays the fourfold or fivefold payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays** the **double payment** to the purchaser, who has become the new owner of the animal. If one **stole** an animal **and slaughtered it, and another** person **came and stole** the slaughtered animal, **the first** thief **pays the fourfold or fivefold payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **does not pay** the **double payment but only the principal,** in accordance with the *halakha* that one who steals from a thief does not pay the double payment.

The second case introduces a sale: *ganav u'machar uva acher ugnavo* — Reuven stole the animal, sold it to Shimon, and then Levi came and stole it from Shimon. The *beraita* rules that Reuven pays *tashlumei arba'ah v'chamisha* — four and five — and Levi pays *tashlumei kefel* to Shimon. Reuven is liable for four and five because he stole and sold; Levi is liable for double because he stole from Shimon, who legitimately owns the animal.

The third case: *ganav v'tavach uva acher ugnavo* — Reuven stole and slaughtered the animal, and then Levi came and stole it from Reuven. Reuven pays four and five, but Levi pays Reuven only *keren*, not *kefel*.

### Middle Case Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא מְצִיעֲתָא: גָּנַב וּמָכַר, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, שֵׁנִי אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל? שִׁינּוּי רְשׁוּת בְּלֹא יֵאוּשׁ מִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דְּקָנֵי? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יֵאוּשׁ קוֹנֶה, אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה? דִּידֵיהּ הוּא דְּזַבֵּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** in **the middle clause:** If one **stole** an animal **and sold** it, **and another came and stole it** from the purchaser, **the first** thief **pays the fourfold or fivefold payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays** the **double payment** to the purchaser. **When** did this sale occur? **If we say** it happened **before** the owner's **despair, why does** the second thief **pay** the **double payment** to the purchaser? **Is there anyone who says that a change in possession** of a stolen item, i.e., when it leaves the possession of the thief and enters the possession of another, **without** the additional factor of the owner's **despair,** causes the one who has it to **acquire** the item? There is no such opinion. Therefore, the so-called purchaser of the animal does not become its owner, as the sale is invalid. This should be considered as a case of one who steals from a thief, and consequently there should be no double payment. **Rather, it is obvious** that the sale occurred **after** the owner's **despair. And if it enters your mind** that a thief **acquires** a stolen item after the owner's **despair, why** should the first thief **pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment?** After all, **it is his own** animal **that he sold,** having acquired it immediately upon the owner's despair.

The *Gemara* focuses on the middle case — *katni meihas metzi'ata* — and brackets the problematic third case for the moment. Why does Levi pay *kefel* to Shimon in the sale scenario? *Iyleima lifnei ye'ush* — if there was no *ye'ush* — how does Shimon own the animal such that Levi's theft from him triggers *kefel*? *Shinui reshus belo ye'ush mi ika man d'amar dekani* — there is no opinion holding that a mere change of domain without *ye'ush* confers ownership. Therefore, *ela pishita l'achar ye'ush* — it must be that *ye'ush* occurred before the sale to Shimon, so that Shimon owns the animal through *ye'ush v'shinui reshus*, and Levi's theft from Shimon triggers *kefel*.

But then, *v'i salka da'atach ye'ush koneh* — if *ye'ush* alone transferred ownership to Reuven — why would Reuven be obligated for four and five for selling to Shimon? *Didei hu d'zavin* — he would be selling his own property. The inescapable conclusion: *shema mina ye'ush lo konei* — *ye'ush* alone is not *koneh*. This creates a contradiction for Rava's position as well: the first case of the *beraita* — simple theft from a *ganav* — establishes that the second thief pays only *keren*, not *kefel*. Yet we have now established that *ye'ush* must be present for the sale case to make sense. If *ye'ush* occurred in the hands of the first *ganav*, why does the second thief pay only *keren* in the simple theft scenario? The answer again: *ye'ush* alone is not *koneh*, and consequently the first *ganav* does not own the animal merely because of *ye'ush*.

### Theft-Slaughter-Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, דְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: גָּנַב, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – רִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אֵין מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** consider **that** which the *baraita* **teaches** in **the first clause:** If one **stole** an animal, **and another** subsequently **came and stole it** from him, **the first** thief **pays** the **double payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays only the principal** to the first thief.

The third case presents a genuine difficulty. Reuven slaughtered the animal and then Levi stole it. Reuven pays four and five, but Levi pays only *keren* to Reuven. This is puzzling: slaughter is a *shinui ma'aseh* — a physical transformation of the object — which should confer ownership on Reuven. If Reuven now owns the animal carcass, Levi who steals it ought to pay Reuven *kefel*. Why does Levi pay only *keren*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ קָיְימִינַן; וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יֵאוּשׁ קוֹנֶה, שֵׁנִי אַמַּאי אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא קֶרֶן? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יֵאוּשׁ לָא קָנֵי, וְקַשְׁיָא לְרַב?
>
> **Translation:** **Now,** in this *baraita* **we are dealing with** a situation **after** the owner's **despair,** as the Gemara just established. **And if it enters your mind** that the owner's **despair** causes the thief to **acquire** the item, **why does** the **second** thief **pay only the principal?** After the owner has despaired of recovering his item, the thief becomes the owner of the item, and the second thief should have to pay him double payment for stealing his property. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** the *baraita* that the owner's **despair does not** cause the thief to **acquire** the item? **And** this presents **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rav.**

Rava points out that there is no reading of the *beraita* that makes all three cases internally consistent. The problematic third case cannot simply be set aside.

### Textual Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְתִסְבְּרָא הָא מְתָרַצְתָּא הִיא?! אֶלָּא דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: גָּנַב וְטָבַח, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – רִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְשֵׁנִי אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא קֶרֶן; וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שִׁינּוּי מַעֲשֶׂה לָא קָנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And** can **you understand** that the text of **this** *baraita* **is accurate? But** consider **that** which **it teaches** in **the last clause:** If one **stole** an animal **and slaughtered** it, **and another came and stole** the slaughtered animal, the **first** thief **pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays only the principal** to the first thief. **And is there anyone who says that** a physical **change** brought about by **an action does not** cause the thief to **acquire** the item? There is no such opinion; the thief certainly becomes the owner of the animal after slaughtering it. Consequently, the second thief should have to pay the double payment to the thief for stealing his slaughtered animal. It must therefore be concluded that the text of the *baraita* has been corrupted and must be reformulated.

One resolution: *v'eipoich seifa l'mitziata u'mitziata l'seifa* — the second and third cases in the *beraita* have been transposed due to a scribal error, and the correct reading is the reverse of what appears. In the corrected version, the slaughter case is the one where the subsequent thief pays *kefel* — because *shinui ma'aseh* gives Reuven ownership, and Levi steals from an owner — and the sale case is the one where the subsequent thief pays only *keren* — because the sale was before *ye'ush* and Shimon therefore does not own the animal. Under this reading, the entire *beraita* discusses cases *lifnei ye'ush*, and the apparent difficulty dissolves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם כּוּלַּהּ לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, וְאֵיפוֹךְ סֵיפָא לִמְצִיעֲתָא וּמְצִיעֲתָא לְסֵיפָא, וְאֵימָא הָכִי: גָּנַב וּמָכַר, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא קֶרֶן – דְּשִׁינּוּי רְשׁוּת בְּלֹא יֵאוּשׁ לָא קָנֵי. גָּנַב וְטָבַח, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְנָבוֹ – הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל; דְּקַנְיֵיהּ בְּשִׁינּוּי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually the entire** *baraita* is referring to the stage **before** the owner's **despair. And** you must **reverse** the *halakha* stated in **the last clause with** that stated in **the middle clause, and** reverse the *halakha* stated in **the middle clause with** that stated in **the last clause, and say like this:** If one **stole** an animal **and sold** it, **and another came and stole it** from the purchaser, **the first** thief **pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays only the principal** to the purchaser, **as a change in possession without** the additional factor of owner's **despair does not** cause one to **acquire** the item. If one **stole** an animal **and slaughtered** it, **and another came and stole** the slaughtered animal, **the first** thief **pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** to the owner, **and the second** thief **pays** the **double payment** to the first thief, **as** the first thief **acquired** it through the physical **change** brought about by **an action,** i.e., slaughter.

Rav Pappa offers an alternative that avoids reversing the *beraita*'s text: *seifa Beit Shammai hi d'amar shinuy bimkomo omed* — the third case, in which Reuven slaughtered the animal yet Levi pays only *keren*, reflects the position of Beit Shammai. Beit Shammai holds that even a *shinui* — a physical transformation — does not confer ownership on the thief; the transformation remains in its place legally. Hence Reuven does not acquire the carcass through slaughter, and Levi's theft from him triggers no *kefel*. The middle case then reflects the standard view — *ye'ush* plus *shinui reshus* transfers ownership to Shimon — and the first case involves no *ye'ush* at all. Whether this creates a difficulty for Rav's position remains to be explored.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ; סֵיפָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: שִׁינּוּי בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Actually, do not reverse** the middle and last clauses of the *baraita*. With regard to the difficult **last clause, it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say:** Even if an item has undergone a physical **change,** it **remains in its place,** i.e., a stolen item remains the property of the owner. A thief never attains ownership of it, even if it undergoes a physical change.

## Rav Zvid's Resolution

### Yeush Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: לְעוֹלָם כּוּלַּהּ לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – שֶׁנִּתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים בְּלוֹקֵחַ וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ בְּגַנָּב, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יֵאוּשׁ וְשִׁינּוּי רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said: Actually, the entire** *baraita* is dealing with the stage **before** the owner's **despair. And with what are we dealing here,** in the middle clause of the *baraita*? We are dealing with a case **where the owners despaired** of recovering the item when it was already **in** the possession of the **purchaser, but they had not** yet **despaired** when the item was **in** the possession of the **thief.** The purchaser is considered the owner **because** after the despair occurred, **there were** two factors in play, both the owner's **despair and a change in possession.** The combination of these two factors effects acquisition of the item on behalf of the purchaser.

Rav Zvid offers a comprehensive reconciliation of the *beraita* without reversing any cases and without invoking Beit Shammai. He proposes that all three scenarios in the *beraita* are set *lifnei ye'ush* as far as the first *ganav* is concerned, but that *ye'ush* occurred after the animal passed into the purchaser's hands — not while it was still in the possession of the first *ganav*.

### Both Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא תֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵינַן יֵאוּשׁ וְשִׁינּוּי רְשׁוּת, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ בְּיֵאוּשׁ לְחוֹדֵיהּ נָמֵי קָנֵי גַּבֵּי גַנָּב;
>
> **Translation:** Rav Zevid continues: **And do not say** that in the middle clause the reason the owner's item is acquired by another is only **because we require** two factors, i.e., the owner's **despair and change of possession. Rather, even** in a case **of** the owner's **despair by itself,** the item **is acquired by the thief,** in accordance with Rav's opinion that despair alone effects acquisition on behalf of the thief.

This resolves both obligations simultaneously. Since *ye'ush* had not yet occurred while the animal was in Reuven's possession, Reuven never acquired ownership through *ye'ush*. That is why Levi, who stole from Reuven in the simple theft scenario, pays only *keren* — Reuven is not the true owner. And that is why Reuven, who sold the animal to Shimon, is still obligated for four and five — he was selling something not yet his. As for Levi who stole from Shimon: *ye'ush* occurred after the animal reached Shimon's hands, so Shimon acquired it through *ye'ush* combined with *shinui reshus*, and Levi's theft from Shimon triggers *kefel*.

### Yeush Alone Koneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּלָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ דִּמְשַׁלְּמִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ גַּנָּב רִאשׁוֹן וְגַנָּב שֵׁנִי, אֶלָּא בְּהָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Nevertheless,** the *baraita* chose a case in which both of these factors are present **because you will not find a case in which both of them, the first thief and the second thief, pay** a penalty **except in such** a case. The first thief pays the fourfold or fivefold payment, and the second thief pays the double payment. Had the owner despaired before the sale, the first thief would have been considered the owner and would therefore be exempt from the additional payment.

One might ask: if we needed *ye'ush* plus *shinui reshus* to give Shimon ownership, why not simply have *ye'ush* occur while the animal was still with Reuven? Then Reuven would be the owner and the second thief would owe him *kefel*. The answer: *afilu b'ye'ush lechudei nami kani gabei ganav* — *ye'ush* alone would indeed be sufficient to transfer ownership to Reuven. However, the *beraita* specifically constructs the scenario to yield *both* obligations — Reuven's four and five *and* Levi's *kefel* — simultaneously. *D'lo meshkachat d'mishalem tarvaihu ganav rishon v'ganav sheini ela b'hachi* — you cannot get both obligations at once except in precisely this configuration, where *ye'ush* occurs in the purchaser's hands rather than in the first *ganav*'s hands. The scenario is designed this way not because *ye'ush* alone is insufficient, but because it is the only arrangement that produces both liabilities at once.

## Three Positions

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ – רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to a thief **who sells** a stolen animal **before** the owner's **despair, Rav Naḥman says:** He is **liable** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, despite the fact that the sale is invalid. **Rav Sheshet says:** He is **exempt** from this payment.

Having traced the debate through the *beraita*, the *Gemara* now articulates three distinct positions on when the obligation for *mechira* applies. *Itmar* — it was stated: Rav Nachman rules *chayav* — obligated — for a sale made before *ye'ush*. His reasoning: *u'mcharo amar Rachmana v'azvin* — the Torah says "and he sold it"; it does not require that the sale actually effect a transfer of ownership. The act of sale itself suffices. Therefore *lo shena lifnei ye'ush v'lo shena l'achar ye'ush* — it makes no difference whether the sale occurs before or after *ye'ush*. Rav Nachman holds that *ye'ush* is not *koneh*, so a post-*ye'ush* sale does give rise to *ye'ush v'shinui reshus*; but he equally maintains liability before *ye'ush*, where the act of sale, even without effect, is sufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר חַיָּיב – ״וּמְכָרוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא זַבֵּין; לָא שְׁנָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, לָא שְׁנָא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר פָּטוּר – חִיּוּבֵיהּ לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ הוּא, דְּאַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשָׂיו; אֲבָל לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ דְּלָא אַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשָׂיו – לָא מִיחַיַּיב; דֻּומְיָא דִּטְבִיחָה בָּעֵינַן, דְּאַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשָׂיו.
>
> **Translation:** The two *amora'im* explain the reasons for their respective rulings. **Rav Naḥman says:** He is **liable,** because **the Merciful One states** with regard to the fourfold or fivefold payment: "And slaughter it **or sell it"** (Exodus 21:37), **and he has sold it.** There **is no difference** if the sale occurs **before despair,** and there **is no difference** if it takes place **after despair. Rav Sheshet says:** He is **exempt,** as **his liability is** in effect only if he sells the animal **after** the owner's **despair, when his action,** i.e., the sale, **is effective. But** if he sells it **before** the owner's **despair, when his action is not effective,** he **is not liable,** as **we require** that the sale be **similar to the slaughter,** as they are mentioned together, and **when** he slaughters the animal **his action is effective.**

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet disagrees: you are *patur* — exempt — if you sold before *ye'ush*. *Chiyuva l'achar ye'ush hu* — the obligation falls only after *ye'ush*, *de'ahani maasav* — because only then does the act of sale actually accomplish something legally, actually transferring ownership through *ye'ush v'shinui reshus*. Before *ye'ush*, the sale has no legal effect; *lo ahani maasav* — the thief's act accomplished nothing. The comparison to slaughter is instructive: just as slaughter produces a genuine, irreversible physical change — *dumya d'tevichah d'ahani maasav* — so too the *mechira* must produce a genuine legal change. That genuine change occurs only after *ye'ush*.

### Missing Position

The *Gemara* has now identified a logical gap. If *ye'ush* is *koneh*, the obligation can only arise *before* *ye'ush* — since after *ye'ush* the *ganav* is selling his own property. If *ye'ush* is not *koneh*, there is a further debate between Rav Nachman, who obligates both before and after *ye'ush*, and Rav Sheshet, who obligates only after *ye'ush* when *ahani maasav*. All three logical possibilities have now been mapped.

## Rabbi Akiva Revisited

### Proof Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לָהּ? דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה טָבַח וּמָכַר מְשַׁלֵּם אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּשְׁתָּרֵשׁ בַּחֵטְא. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, מִי אִיכָּא נִשְׁתָּרֵשׁ? אֶלָּא לָאו לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ הוּא? אָמַר רָבָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשָּׁנָה בַּחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: From where do I say** he is exempt? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva said: For what** reason **did the Torah say that** a thief **who slaughtered or sold** a stolen animal **pays the fourfold or fivefold payment?** It is **because he has become entrenched in sin** by slaughtering or selling the animal he stole. Rav Sheshet analyzes the *baraita*: **When** did this sale, referred to by Rabbi Akiva, occur? **If we say** that it happened **before** the owner's **despair, is** it possible that **there** is any **entrenchment** in sin here? A sale before the owner's despair is invalid, which means there is no deepening of his earlier sin. **Rather, is it not** the case that this **is** referring to a sale **after** the owner's **despair? Rava said:** This is not a proof, as one can explain that the thief is entrenched in sin **because he repeated** his **sin,** i.e. sinned a second time, by his act of sale, regardless of the fact that the sale is invalid.

The *Gemara* returns to Rav Sheshet's position in greater detail. *Mena amina lah* — where does Rav Sheshet derive his ruling? From the *beraita* of Rabbi Akiva: *mipnei mah amra Torah tavach u'machar meshallem arba'ah v'chamisha mipnei shenishtaresh b'cheit* — the Torah mandated four and five because the thief entrenched himself in sin. As established earlier, this entrenchment — *nishtaresh b'cheit* — cannot refer to a sale before *ye'ush*, where the sin has not genuinely deepened; it must refer to a sale after *ye'ush*, where the sale genuinely removes the stolen object from the owner's legal ownership. Therefore, *chiyuva l'achar ye'ush hu*.

The *Gemara* then rehearses Rava's counter: *mipnei sheshanah b'cheit* — Rabbi Akiva may mean only that the thief repeated his sin, which would support an obligation before *ye'ush* as well. And again the *beraita* on *she'eina chozeres* is cited — *u'tevacho u'mecharo mah tevichah she'einah chozeres af mechira she'einah chozeres* — and the response attributed to Rav Nachman's interpretation, that the requirement of permanence refers to the framing of the sale as a permanent transaction, not to an actual change in legal ownership. These arguments track what was established above and lead into the position of Rabbi Eliezer.

### Counter Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וּטְבָחוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ״; מָה טְבִיחָה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, אַף מְכִירָה – שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, אַמַּאי אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת? אֶלָּא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חִיּוּבָא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ הוּא! תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: פְּרָט לְשֶׁהִקְנָה לוֹ לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof for the opinion of Rav Sheshet from a different *baraita*: It is written: **"And slaughter it or sell it"** (Exodus 21:37). **Just as slaughter** is an act **that cannot be undone, so too** the **selling** referred to here is an act **that cannot be undone.** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **When** did this sale take place? **If we say** that it happened **before** the owner's **despair** of recovering his stolen item, **why can it not be undone?** The thief's sale of the animal is invalid before the owner's despair, and therefore the sale is easily undone. **Rather,** it must be referring to a sale that took place **after** the owner's **despair. And** one can **conclude from** the *baraita* that there is **liability** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment only if the animal is sold **after** the owner's **despair. Rav Naḥman** rejected this proof, as he **interpreted** that *baraita* as serving to **exclude one who transferred** ownership of the animal temporarily, e.g., **for thirty days.**

At this point a natural question arises: even granting that the obligation for *mechira* requires *ye'ush*, how do we know that *ye'ush* is always present in a standard theft case? Perhaps in any given instance there was no *ye'ush*, and the *ganav* who sells cannot be held liable. Why should the Torah's formulation of four and five be assumed to apply to every case of theft?

### Slaughter Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבַר חִיּוּבֵיהּ לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ הוּא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And Rabbi Elazar also holds** that **the liability** of a thief to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment applies if he sells it **after** the owner's **despair** of recovering his stolen animal, **as Rabbi Elazar says:**

The *Gemara* responds by pressing the comparison to slaughter: *dumya d'tevichah mah tevichah d'ahani maasav af mechira d'ahani maasav* — just as slaughter has a genuine irreversible effect, so too *mechira* must actually accomplish something. The Torah does not attach a special precondition to slaughter; it obligates immediately upon the act. Since the obligation for *mechira* is placed on the same footing as slaughter — *u'tevacho u'mecharo*, as an immediate obligation — we cannot require that *ye'ush* be separately demonstrated in each case. It must be built into the standard assumption.

### Yeush Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּדַע שֶׁסְּתָם גְּנֵיבָה יֵאוּשׁ בְּעָלִים הוּא – שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. וְדִלְמָא לָא אִיָּיאַשׁ! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: סְתָם גְּנֵיבָה – יֵאוּשׁ בְּעָלִים הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Know that an ordinary** case of **theft is** assumed to result in **the owner's despair** of recovering the stolen item. In other words, in the absence of evidence to the contrary, one may assume that the victim of theft has despaired of recovering his item. The reason is **that the Torah stated** that if a thief **slaughtered or sold** the animal he had stolen, **he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment. But** why is this so? **Perhaps** the owner **did not** yet **despair** at the time the thief sold the animal, in which case the sale is invalid, and there should be no fourfold or fivefold payment. **Rather, is it not because we say that an ordinary** case of **theft is** assumed to result in **the owner's despair** of recovering the stolen item? This concludes the statement of Rabbi Elazar, which clearly indicates that there is no fourfold or fivefold payment if the animal is sold before the owner's despair.

This leads Rabbi Eliezer to his explicit ruling at the top of 68b: *teda she-stam gneiva ye'ush be'alim hu* — you should know that a standard case of theft carries with it the assumption that the owners have despaired. The proof: the Torah says one who slaughters or sells pays four and five — *she-harei amra Torah tavach u'machar meshallem tashlumei arba'ah v'chamisha*. Since the obligation for *mechira* requires *ye'ush* (as Rav Sheshet and Rabbi Eliezer assume), and the Torah speaks of this obligation without requiring any additional showing of *ye'ush*, the Torah must be assuming that *ye'ush* accompanies every standard theft.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אִיָּיאַשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to Rabbi Elazar's assumption: **But perhaps** the Torah obligates the thief to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment **even if** the owner has **not** yet **despaired** at the time of the sale, despite the fact that the sale is invalid, as Rav Naḥman stated.

One might still ask: perhaps the Torah is simply telling us the law in a case where we happen to know *ye'ush* occurred. Rabbi Eliezer responds with the temporal logic: *dumya d'tevichah mah tevichah l'alter af mechira l'alter* — just as one who slaughters is obligated immediately, so too one who sells is obligated immediately. The Torah treats both as arising from the act alone, with no additional required condition. This can only be the case if *stam gneiva ye'ush be'alim* — standard theft is accompanied by the owners' despair.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דּוּמְיָא דִטְבִיחָה – מָה טְבִיחָה דְּאַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשָׂיו, אַף מְכִירָה דְּאַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשָׂיו. וְאִי לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, מַאי אַהֲנוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** In response to this question the Sages **say:** This **cannot enter your mind,** as Rabbi Elazar maintains that the juxtaposition of slaughtering and selling in the verse that states: "And slaughter it or sell it," teaches that the thief's sale of the animal is **similar to** his **slaughter** of it. **Just as slaughter** is a matter in **which his action was effective, so too,** the case of **selling** the animal is one in **which his action was effective,** i.e., the sale is valid. **And if** the sale occurred **before** the owner's **despair,** in **what** sense were his actions **effective?**

*Tosafot* notes that *l'alter* cannot be taken literally as absolutely instantaneous, since the rule of *ye'ush shelo m'da'at lo havai ye'ush* — despair without the owner's knowledge does not constitute valid *ye'ush* — means the owner must actually become aware of the theft before *ye'ush* can occur. *Tosafot* therefore reads *l'alter* as "shortly thereafter" — by the time the *ganav* has brought the animal home and is ready to slaughter it, it can be assumed the owner is already aware of the theft and has despaired. The Torah requires no separately articulated condition of *ye'ush* because the assumption is that it will be present in virtually every case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא דְּשַׁמְעִינֵיהּ דְּאִיָּיאַשׁ! אָמְרִי: לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דּוּמְיָא דִטְבִיחָה – מָה טְבִיחָה לְאַלְתַּר, אַף מְכִירָה לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question with regard to Rabbi Elazar's reasoning: **But perhaps** the Torah requires the fourfold or fivefold payment only in the specific case **where** the animal is sold after **we heard that** the owner **despaired** of its recovery. The Sages respond to this and **say:** This **cannot enter your mind,** since, as explained above, the thief's sale of the animal must be **similar to** his **slaughter** of it. Therefore, **just as** the thief is liable to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment for **slaughtering** the animal even if he does so **immediately, so too,** the *halakha* that he pays the fourfold or fivefold payment for **selling** it applies even if he does so **immediately,** before it is known that the owner has despaired of recovering the animal.

## Gneivat Nefesh

### No Yeush Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גְּנֵיבָה בְּנֶפֶשׁ תּוֹכִיחַ – שֶׁאֵין יֵאוּשׁ בְּעָלִים, וְחַיָּיב! מִכְּלָל דְּסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Rabbi Elazar: The case of **stealing a human being,** i.e., kidnapping, **proves** that your reasoning is incorrect. The Torah states: "And he who steals a man and sells him or if he is found in his hand, he shall be put to death" (Exodus 21:16). In this case **there is no owner's despair,** as no one ever despairs of his own freedom. Consequently, the thief's sale of the person he kidnapped is invalid, **and** yet the Torah states that he is **liable** to receive the death penalty for selling him. One can learn **by inference** from this question **that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that if the thief sells the stolen animal **before** the owner's **despair** he is **liable** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

Rav Yochanan now challenges the view that the obligation for *mechira* falls only after *ye'ush*. His challenge comes from a striking case: *gneiva b'nefesh tochi'ach* — the case of kidnapping proves the point. As Rashi notes explicitly — particularly poignant in our own times — *she'ein adam mitya'esh al atzmo*: a person never despairs of his own redemption. *Ye'ush* by the owner is simply impossible in a case of *gneivat nefesh*, yet the Torah obligates the kidnapper who sells his victim. This demonstrates that an obligation for *mechira* exists even without *ye'ush*.

### Torah Obligation

The structure of the argument is transparent: the Torah writes *gonev ish u'mecharo* — one who kidnaps a person and sells him — and the *chiyuv* is clear. Since there can be no *ye'ush be'alim* in such a case — a person is always his own *be'alim* and never gives up hope of being freed — the Torah's obligation must operate *lifnei ye'ush*. This implies that the obligation for *mechira* can arise even before *ye'ush*.

### Yochanan-Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ מַאי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: If the thief sells the animal **after** the owner's **despair, what** is the *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He is **liable. And Reish Lakish says:** He is **exempt.**

The *Gemara* draws out the further implications. From Rav Yochanan's argument it is clear that he holds *lifnei ye'ush chayav* — one is obligated before *ye'ush*. What is the status *after* *ye'ush*? The positions diverge: *Rav Yochanan amar chayav* — Rav Yochanan holds one is obligated both before and after *ye'ush*, since *ye'ush* is not *koneh*; there is therefore always a basis for liability. *Resh Lakish amar patur* — Reish Lakish says one is *patur* after *ye'ush*, because for Reish Lakish *ye'ush* is *koneh* — the thief acquires the stolen item through *ye'ush*, and any subsequent slaughter or sale is of his own property: *shelo hu tovei'ach shelo hu mocher*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר חַיָּיב – חִיּוּבֵיהּ בֵּין לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, בֵּין לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר פָּטוּר – חִיּוּבֵיהּ לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ הוּא, אֲבָל לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ – קָנָה, וְשֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ וְשֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He is **liable,** as the thief's **liability** applies **whether** the animal is sold **before** the owner's **despair or after** his **despair. Reish Lakish says:** He is **exempt,** as the thief's **liability is** in effect only if he sells the animal **before** the owner's **despair, but after** the owner's **despair** there is no liability, because the thief has **acquired** the animal by virtue of the owner's despair, **and** therefore it is **his own** animal that **he slaughters or his own** animal that **he sells.**

## Sanctification Proof

### Sanctification Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: גָּנַב וְהִקְדִּישׁ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ טָבַח – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: If one **stole** an animal **and consecrated** it, **and subsequently slaughtered** it, **he pays** the **double payment** to the owner of the animal, **but he does not pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** The Torah states: "The one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8). The word "neighbor" excludes the case of one who steals a consecrated item from the Temple treasury. Since there is no double payment to the Temple treasury there can be no fourfold or fivefold payment either, as the fourfold or fivefold payment is considered an addition to, not a replacement of, the double payment (see 75a). Therefore, a thief does not pay the fourfold or fivefold payment to the Temple treasury for slaughtering one of its animals.

Rav Yochanan now presses his challenge to Reish Lakish's position directly. A *beraita* states: if someone stole an animal, sanctified it (*hikdish*), and then slaughtered it — *meshalem tashlumei kefel v'eino meshalem tashlumei arba'ah v'chamisha* — he pays *kefel* but not four and five. The reason for the exemption from four and five is that once the animal is *hekdesh*, it no longer belongs to the *ganav*'s victim in the relevant sense — *re'eihu v'lo hekdesh* — and slaughtering *hekdesh* does not trigger the four and five penalty.

### Cannot Sanctify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִלֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, מִי קָדוֹשׁ?! ״אִישׁ כִּי יַקְדִּשׁ אֶת בֵּיתוֹ קֹדֶשׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – מָה בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **When** did the events described in the *baraita* occur? **If we say** they happened **before** the owner's **despair, is** the animal **consecrated** at all? A thief cannot consecrate a stolen item before the owner despairs of recovering it, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: "And **when a man shall sanctify his house to be holy unto the Lord"** (Leviticus 27:14), from which it is derived: **Just as one's house belongs to him, so too, anything** that one consecrates must **belong to him,** and therefore a thief cannot consecrate a stolen item.

The question is: when did the *ganav* sanctify the animal? *Iyleima lifnei ye'ush* — *mi kadosh*? Can the *ganav* sanctify before *ye'ush*? The *Gemara* invokes the rule: *ish ki yakdish et beiso kodesh amar Rachmana mah beiso shelo af kol shelo* — the Torah says a person may sanctify his house; just as his house is his own, so whatever he sanctifies must be his own. A *ganav* who has not yet acquired ownership cannot sanctify. Therefore the sanctification must have occurred *l'achar ye'ush*. This means *ye'ush* plus the sanctification — which constitutes a *shinui reshus* or *shinui hashem* — together confer ownership upon the *ganav*, enabling the sanctification to take effect. Accordingly, when the *ganav* slaughters the animal, *d'ki katavach d'hekdesh katavach* — he is slaughtering something that belongs to *hekdesh*, and therefore he does not pay four and five.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ; וְטַעְמָא דְּהִקְדִּישׁ הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, דְּכִי קָא טָבַח – דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ קָא טָבַח, אֲבָל לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ – טָבַח מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יֵאוּשׁ קוֹנֶה, אַמַּאי מְשַׁלֵּם? שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ, שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is dealing with events that occurred **after** the owner's **despair. And** yet **the** only **reason that he does not pay the fourfold or fivefold payment is that he consecrated** the animal, on the grounds **that when he slaughtered** it **he slaughtered consecrated** property. **But** if **he did not consecrate** the animal before **he slaughtered** it **he would pay the fourfold or fivefold payment. And if it enters your mind that** the owner's **despair** serves to **acquire** the animal for the thief, **why** would he have to **pay?** At that point it is **his own** animal that **he slaughters or his own** animal that **he sells.**

The critical inference follows: *v'i salka da'atach ye'ush kanah* — if Reish Lakish is correct that *ye'ush* alone is *koneh* — why would the *ganav* owe anything at all? Once *ye'ush* occurred, the animal was already his before the sanctification; he would be slaughtering his own property and would be exempt without any need for *hekdesh* to intervene. The fact that the *beraita* requires the sanctification to explain the exemption from four and five demonstrates that *ye'ush* alone is not *koneh*. That is Rav Yochanan's proof.

### Owners Sanctify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁוּהוּ בְּעָלִים בְּיַד גַּנָּב.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **With what are we dealing here?** The *baraita* is referring to a case that occurred before the owner's despair, and it is not the thief who consecrated the animal, as he cannot do so. Rather, we are dealing with a case **where the owner consecrated the animal** while it was **in the thief's possession.**

Reish Lakish responds: *hacha b'mai askinan k'gon shehikdishu be'alim beyad ganav* — the case is not one in which the *ganav* sanctified the animal; rather, the original owners sanctified it while it was still in the *ganav*'s possession, before *ye'ush*. Since the owners sanctified it, when the *ganav* slaughters it he is slaughtering *hekdesh* and therefore does not pay four and five. The *ganav* himself never needed to be the one who sanctified; the scenario does not implicate *ye'ush* at all.

## Reish Lakish Defense

### Pidyon Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי קָדוֹשׁ?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּזַל וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – שְׁנֵיהֶם אֵינָן יְכוֹלִים לְהַקְדִּישׁ, זֶה לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְזֶה לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ! אָמְרִי: הוּא דְּאָמַר – כִּצְנוּעִין, דִּתְנַן: הַצְּנוּעִין מַנִּיחִין אֶת הַמָּעוֹת, וְאוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַנִּלְקָט מִזֶּה, יְהֵא מְחוּלָּל עַל הַמָּעוֹת הָאֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** the animal **consecrated** in this situation? **But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** If one **stole** an item **and the owner has not** yet **despaired** of recovering it, **neither of them is able to consecrate** it. **This** one, the thief, cannot consecrate the item, **because it does not belong to him; and that** one, the owner, cannot consecrate it, **because it is not in his possession.** The Sages **state** an answer on behalf of Reish Lakish: **He stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the pious ones [*tzenuin*],** who acted not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 5:1): **The pious ones would set aside** some **coins and say: Anything that was picked from this** vine by passersby **shall be desacralized onto these coins.** This mishna is referring to a grapevine in its fourth year after planting. The grapes on this vine must be either eaten in Jerusalem or redeemed with money that is then taken to Jerusalem and spent on food. The fruit may not be eaten outside Jerusalem without being redeemed. The pious ones were concerned that a passerby might help himself to some of their grapes, thereby transgressing a prohibition. Therefore, they would redeem any of their grapes that might be picked and eaten by passersby. Evidently, these pious ones were of the opinion that the owner of a stolen item can redeem it despite the fact that it is no longer in his possession. Likewise, they would say, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, that an owner of a stolen item can consecrate it even though it is no longer in his possession.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges Reish Lakish's alternative reading: *u'mi kadosh* — can the original owners actually sanctify something that is no longer under their control? And Rav Yochanan himself is on record stating: *gezel v'lo nityashu habe'alim shneihem einam yecholim l'hakdish* — if something was stolen and the owners have not yet despaired, *neither party* can sanctify it. The *ganav* cannot sanctify because *it is not his*; the owner cannot sanctify because *it is not in his domain*. How, then, can Reish Lakish propose that the owners sanctified it?

Reish Lakish responds by pointing to the ruling of the *tznuin* — the scrupulously pious — as a source of analogy. The *mishna* teaches that those who were careful about transgressions would, when their orchard was in its *neta revai* year, set aside coins and declare: *kol hanilkat mizeh yehei mechulal al hamaos ha'elu* — whatever anyone takes from this tree shall have its sanctity transferred onto this money. Since passersby would take fruit without permission — effectively in an act of *gezilah* — the owner could not restore the fruit to his possession, yet he was nevertheless able to effect a transfer of *kedushah* through this *pidyon*. This demonstrates, argues Reish Lakish, that an owner retains some ability to act legally with respect to stolen property even when it is out of his domain.

*Tosafot* notes that the analogy is imperfect. *Pidyon* — redemption — is fundamentally different from *hakdash*. When one redeems something, the *kedushah* is already present and one is merely transferring it to a different object; moreover, the *tznuin* who perform the *pidyon* are acting to benefit the unwitting sinners who take the fruit (*zochin l'adam shelo b'fanav*), so the ability to perform *pidyon* here is explained by that consideration. Sanctifying something that is already stolen — taking an object out of the domain of its rightful owner and dedicating it to *hekdesh* — is an altogether different act and arguably cannot be derived from the *pidyon* case. Nonetheless, the *Gemara* accepts that Reish Lakish would press this analogy in defense of his position.

### Beit Din Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי חָזְרָה קֶרֶן לַבְּעָלִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to Reish Lakish's explanation of the *baraita*, that it is discussing a case where the owner of the animal consecrated it after it was stolen: **But the principal** amount, i.e., the stolen animal itself, **has returned to the owner.** By consecrating the animal, the owner has exercised his ownership of it, and it is therefore considered to have been returned to him at that point. The thief should therefore not be required to pay the double payment, as a thief is liable for double payment only when the stolen item is in his possession at the time of the trial, as it states: "The theft shall be found in his hand" (Exodus 22:3).

The *Gemara* continues to press Reish Lakish: even if the owners could sanctify the stolen animal, if the owners sanctified it, doesn't that mean they reasserted control over their property? The *ganav* could argue that when he comes before *beit din*, the sanctification by the owners is tantamount to their having retrieved the animal — *chazra keren l'be'alim* — the principal has effectively returned. And if the principal returned before *beit din* issued its ruling, the *ganav* would not owe *kefel*. Why then does the *beraita* say he pays *kefel*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the thief **stood in judgment** and was found guilty of theft before the owner consecrated the animal. Consequently, the liability to pay the double payment preceded the owner's consecration of his property.

Reish Lakish answers: *k'amad b'din* — the case is one where the *ganav* had already stood before *beit din*, and *beit din* had already obligated him in *kefel*, before the owners sanctified the animal. Only thereafter did the *ganav* slaughter it. Since the *kefel* obligation was established before the sanctification, it remains in force; and since the owners sanctified it before the slaughter, the slaughter is of *hekdesh* and the *ganav* is exempt from four and five.

### Obligation vs. Command

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאָמְרִי ״צֵא תֵּן לוֹ״ – מַאי אִירְיָא הִקְדִּישׁ? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ נָמֵי לָא לִיחַיְּיבֵיהּ! דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״צֵא תֵּן לוֹ״, טָבַח וּמָכַר – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּפַסְקוּהָ לְמִילְּתֵיהּ וְטָבַח וּמָכַר – הָוֵה לֵיהּ גַּזְלָן, וְגַזְלָן אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer: **What are the circumstances** of this trial of the thief? **If** it refers to a situation **where** the members of the court **say** to him: **Go out** and **give** the animal back **to** its owner, **why** does the *baraita* say that the thief is exempt from the fourfold or fivefold payment **specifically** because the owner **consecrated** the animal before it was slaughtered? **Even if he did not consecrate it** the thief **should also not be liable** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment. **As Rava says:** If the court tells a thief: **Go out** and **give** the stolen animal back **to** the owner and instead the thief **slaughtered or sold** it, he is **exempt** from the fourfold or fivefold payment. **What is the reason** for this? **Since** the court **has issued a definitive ruling in** this **matter, and he** later **slaughtered or sold** the animal, **he is considered a robber, and a robber does not pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** The difference between a thief, concerning whom the Torah prescribes the double payment and the fourfold or fivefold payment, and a robber, who does not incur these obligations, is that a thief acts stealthily, whereas a robber brazenly uses force to take an item from its owner. Once a court has obligated a thief to make restitution and he has defied this sentence, he is considered a robber, and therefore the fourfold or fivefold payment and its atonement are not applicable to him.

The *Gemara* refines the scenario further by invoking Rava's important distinction. If *beit din* issued an actual command — *tzei ten lo*, "go and pay him" — then, as Rava rules, *tavach u'machar patur*: if the *ganav* subsequently slaughtered or sold the animal, he would be exempt even without the *hekdesh* intervening, because *keivan d'paski l'milta* — the matter has been concluded. The *ganav* is no longer operating within the framework of the original *geneivah*; he has been ordered to pay and his continued withholding constitutes something closer to *gezilah*. Any subsequent slaughter is therefore not a continuation of the original theft and does not trigger four and five. Since even without *hekdesh* the *ganav* would be exempt in such a case, the *hekdesh* would be doing no work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: ״חַיָּיב אַתָּה לִיתֵּן לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this is not a challenge to the ruling of Reish Lakish. It is **necessary** for the *baraita* to state the *halakha* in a case **where they say to him** only: **You are obligated to give** the stolen animal back **to** its owner. Consequently, he remains categorized as a thief.

Rava therefore distinguishes: the relevant case is *chayav atah litten lo* — where *beit din* declared the *ganav obligated* but had not yet issued the operative command to go and pay. In that intermediate state, *keivan d'lo paski l'milta* — the matter has not been fully concluded — and *achti ganav hu* — the *ganav* remains within the original framework of his theft. If he slaughters in that state, he would normally pay four and five. It is only the sanctification by the owners that exempts him. This, concludes the *Gemara*, is the precise scenario Reish Lakish is describing: *beit din* has declared the obligation (*chayav litten lo*), the owners have then sanctified the animal, and the *ganav* subsequently slaughters it — obligated for *kefel* by virtue of the *beit din*'s prior declaration, yet exempt from four and five by virtue of the *hekdesh*. With this clarification, Reish Lakish's account of the *beraita* is fully resolved.
