# Makkot 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bikurim Permissibil (III)ity

### Bikurim Timing Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאֵימָתַי (מ)חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם – מִשֶּׁיִּרְאוּ פְּנֵי הַבַּיִת. כְּמַאן – כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּכּוּרִים מִקְצָתָן בַּחוּץ וּמִקְצָתָן בִּפְנִים, שֶׁבַּחוּץ – הֲרֵי הֵן כְּחוּלִּין לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, שֶׁבִּפְנִים – הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **from when is** a non-priest who eats first fruits **liable** to receive death at the hand of Heaven **for their** consumption? One is liable **from when** the fruits **will enter inside the Temple.** The Gemara notes: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this *halakha* stated? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to **first fruits, some of which** are **outside and some of which** are **inside** the Temple, the halakhic status of those **that are outside** the Temple is **like** that of **non-sacred** produce **for all matters concerning them,** and the halakhic status of those **that are inside** the Temple is **like** that of **consecrated** produce **for all matters concerning them.**

The daf opens in the middle of an ongoing discussion regarding when *bikurim* become permissible for a *kohen* to eat. This question has a natural flip side: before the decisive moment — whether that moment is the *hanachah* (placement before the altar) or the *kriyah* (the declaration) — the *bikurim* are forbidden to everyone, much like a *korban* before the blood is sprinkled. After that moment, a *kohen* may eat them, while a non-*kohen* who eats them violates *zarus*, the prohibition against a non-*kohen* eating priestly portions.

### Rav Yehudah Opinion

Rav Yehudah opens with the following formulation: *Bikurim — eimasai mechayevin aleihem?* When is a non-*kohen* liable for eating them as a *zar*, in a manner that would render him *chayav misah bidei shamayim*? *Misheyiru pnei habayis* — from the moment they have been brought within the *Beis Hamikdash*. This is neither the *hanachah* nor the *kriyah*, but rather the physical entry into the Temple. This position reads the word *hanachah* in the relevant verse as referring to waving rather than to placement, and therefore emphasizes the act of bringing the *bikurim* inside the *Beis Hamikdash* as the determinative moment.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

The Gemara asks: *keman* — whose view does Rav Yehudah follow? It answers that this corresponds to the position of Rabbi Eliezer in a *baraisa*: if one has a basket of *bikurim* and half of it is inside the boundary of the *azarah* while half is outside, the portion outside remains like *chulin* in all respects — meaning it has not yet acquired the status of *hekdesh* and does not implicate *zarus* — while the portion inside the *azarah* is fully sanctified in all respects. Once the *bikurim* cross the line into the *azarah*, they acquire full *kedushah* and can only be eaten by a *kohen*, with a non-*kohen* incurring the violation of *zarus*.

### Rav Sheshes Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בִּכּוּרִים, הַנָּחָה – מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן, קְרִיָּיה – אֵין מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Sheshet says:** With regard to **first fruits,** the lack of **placement** alongside the altar **invalidates them;** while the lack of **recitation** of the accompanying Torah verses **does not invalidate them.**

Rav Sheshes takes a different position: *bikurim — hanachah me'akeves bahen; keriyah ein me'akeves bahen.* It is the placing down before the altar that is essential, not the declaration. This stands against the plain reading of the *mishnah*, which seems to indicate that it is the *kriyah* that matters. The Gemara asks whose view this follows, and answers that it corresponds to the position recorded in a *baraisa* cited by Rabbi Yose, who transmitted three rulings in the name of three elders. The first of these — cited in the name of Rabbi Ishmael — forms the transition into a new and closely related topic.

## Ma'aser Sheni Statu (III)

### Rabbi Ishmael Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן – כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מִשּׁוּם שְׁלֹשָׁה זְקֵנִים, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֶה אָדָם מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְיֹאכְלֶנּוּ? וְדִין הוּא: בְּכוֹר טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם, וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי טָעוּן הֲבָאַת מָקוֹם, מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת – אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this *halakha* stated? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*,** Rabbi Yishmael, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says three statements in the name of three elders,** and one of those statements is that which **Rabbi Yishmael says:** One **might** have thought that **a person would bring second-tithe** produce **up to Jerusalem in the present,** after the destruction of the Temple, **and eat it. And** ostensibly, **it** could be derived by means of **a logical inference** that one may not do so: **A firstborn** offering **requires bringing** it **to** the **place,** to Jerusalem, and eating it there, **and** second**-tithe** produce **requires bringing** it **to** the **place; just as** the **firstborn** offering may be eaten there **only in the presence of the Temple, so too,** second**-tithe** produce may be eaten there **only in the presence of the Temple.**

Rabbi Yose transmits the following question in the name of Rabbi Ishmael: *Yachol ya'aleh adam ma'aser sheni bazman hazeh biYerushalayim v'yochlenu* — might a person bring *ma'aser sheni* in the present day to Jerusalem and eat it there? This is a genuine and interesting question. *Ma'aser sheni* must be eaten in Jerusalem. So too must *kodshim kalim*, *bechor*, and *bikurim*. But there is an important distinction between *ma'aser sheni* and the others with respect to the *Beis Hamikdash*.

### Korbanot vs Non-Korbanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִבְכוֹר שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ! בִּכּוּרִים יוֹכִיחוּ. מָה לְבִכּוּרִים, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִים הַנָּחָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael continues and counters: **What** is notable **about a firstborn?** Bringing the firstborn to Jerusalem is required only in the presence of the Temple, because it is notable in **that it requires placement of** its **blood and** its **sacrificial portions upon the altar;** will you say the same with regard to second-tithe produce, which requires only that it be consumed in Jerusalem? He then suggests: **First fruits will prove** that placement of blood upon the altar is not a factor, as they do not require placement of blood upon the altar, and yet they are brought to Jerusalem only in the presence of the Temple. Rabbi Yishmael counters: **What** is notable **about first fruits?** They are notable **in that they require placement** alongside the altar. Perhaps, since second-tithe produce does not require placement at all, even in the present one must bring it to Jerusalem and eat it there.

*Bechor* and *kodshim kalim* require concrete rituals performed at the *Beis Hamikdash*: the blood of *korbanos* must be dashed on the altar, and *bikurim* must be waved and placed *lifnei mizbach Hashem Elokecha* — before the altar of God. By contrast, *ma'aser sheni* requires only that one eat it *lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — in the presence of God, meaning in Jerusalem. There is no ritual act tied to the altar itself. This gives rise to a reasonable suggestion: even without a *Beis Hamikdash*, perhaps one should be able — or even be obligated — to bring *ma'aser sheni* to Jerusalem and eat it there. In practice today, *ma'aser sheni* is redeemed and its monetary value is set aside, but the question is whether the eating in Jerusalem should still apply.

### Logical Distinction

The *baraisa* sets up a logical argument for why *ma'aser sheni* should indeed require the *Beis Hamikdash*: just as *bechor* requires being brought to Jerusalem and cannot be eaten nowadays, the same should apply to *ma'aser sheni* which also requires being brought to Jerusalem. But this argument is immediately challenged: *mah l'bechor shekein te'un dam ve'eimurim legabei mizbe'ach* — the reason *bechor* cannot be eaten nowadays is that it requires the blood to be dashed on the altar and the fats to be burned. *Ma'aser sheni* has no such requirement. One might therefore argue that *ma'aser sheni* can indeed be eaten in Jerusalem today.

## Bikurim Permissibil (III)ity…

### Bikurim as Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ [שָּׁם] לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״ – מַקִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂר לִבְכוֹר, מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי הַבַּיִת – אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי הַבַּיִת. וְאִם אִיתָא, לִיפְרוֹךְ: מָה לְבִכּוּרִים – שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין קְרִיָּיה וְהַנָּחָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael concludes: Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God…**the tithe of your grain…and the firstborn of your herd and your flock" (Deuteronomy 14:23); the Torah **juxtaposes** second-**tithe** produce **to** the **firstborn. Just as** the **firstborn** may be eaten there **only in the presence of the Temple, so too,** second**-tithe** produce may be eaten there **only in the presence of the Temple.** The Gemara explains: the proof of Rav Sheshes's opinion from the *baraita* is: **And if it is so** that the lack of recitation of the Torah verses invalidates the ritual of first fruits, **let** the *baraita* **refute** the derivation by saying: **What** is notable **about first fruits?** They are notable **in that they require recitation** of the Torah verses **and placement** alongside the altar.

The *baraisa* responds by invoking *bikurim* as a counter-proof. *Bikurim* are not a sacrifice and do not require blood dashed on the altar, yet it is taken as given that *bikurim* cannot be brought to Jerusalem and eaten there nowadays. This demonstrates that the inability to eat in Jerusalem is not solely a function of the altar-based sacrifice requirement. Something about *bikurim* also prevents it. What is that something? *Mah l'bikurim shekein te'unim hanachah* — *bikurim* must be placed before the altar. So while *bikurim* are not a *korban*, they still have a concrete link to the altar through the act of *hanachah*. This is precisely the reason the *baraisa* mentions *hanachah* as the distinguishing feature of *bikurim* rather than *kriyah* — and that is the side-point relevant to our earlier discussion. The *baraisa* uses *hanachah* as the key feature of *bikurim*, which suggests that *hanachah* is what makes *bikurim* permissible, not the *kriyah*.

## Ma'aser Sheni Statu (III)

### Textual Link Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לִיפְרוֹךְ: מָה לְבִכּוּרִים – שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין קְרִיָּיה וְהַנָּחָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: the proof of Rav Sheshes's opinion from the *baraita* is: **And if it is so** that the lack of recitation of the Torah verses invalidates the ritual of first fruits, **let** the *baraita* **refute** the derivation by saying: **What** is notable **about first fruits?** They are notable **in that they require recitation** of the Torah verses **and placement** alongside the altar.

Since both *bechor* and *bikurim* have altar-based requirements that *ma'aser sheni* lacks, one might ask: why not derive the requirement for *ma'aser sheni* from the common denominator of *bechor* and *bikurim* — that both require Jerusalem and the *Beis Hamikdash* — and apply that to *ma'aser sheni*? The Gemara responds: *mishum de'ika l'mifrach* — there is a valid rebuttal, namely that the common feature of *bechor* and *bikurim* is *shekein yeish bahen tzad mizbe'ach* — both are concretely connected to the altar in some way. From that shared feature one cannot derive a ruling for *ma'aser sheni*, which has no altar connection whatsoever.

The *baraisa* therefore turns to a textual source. The verse states: *ve'achalta sham lifnei Hashem Elokecha ma'aser degancha tiroshcha ve'yitzharecha uvechoros bekarcha ve'tzoncha* — "And you shall eat there before the Lord your God your tithe of grain, wine, and oil, and the firstlings of your herd and flock." This verse juxtaposes *ma'aser* and *bechor* in a single context of eating before God in Jerusalem. The *baraisa* derives: *makish ma'aser l'bechor* — just as *bechor* can only be eaten when there is a *Beis Hamikdash*, so too *ma'aser*. The linkage in this verse is in fact close to the *peshat* of the *pesukim*: both items are treated as belonging to the same category, eaten together before God in Jerusalem. The same Jerusalem that is required for *bechor* — one with a standing *Beis Hamikdash* — is the same Jerusalem required for *ma'aser*: *af ma'aser eino ela lifnei habayis*.

## Bikurim Permissibil (III)ity…

### Rav Ashi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נְהִי דְּעִיכּוּבָא לֵיכָּא, מִצְוָה מִי לֵיכָּא? וְלֵימָא מִצְוָה וְלִיפְרוֹךְ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בִּכּוּרֵי הַגֵּר, דְּבָעֵי לְמֵימַר אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּע [ה׳] לַאֲבֹתֵינוּ״, וְלָא מָצֵי אָמַר – לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** There is no proof from the fact that recitation is not mentioned. **Although** the lack of recitation **does not invalidate** the first fruits, **is there no mitzva?** Everyone agrees that there is a mitzva to recite the Torah verses. **And** therefore **let** the *tanna* **say** that there is **a mitzva** to recite the portion; **and refute** the proof from first fruits in that manner, as in the case of second tithe there is no mitzva to recite Torah verses. **Rather, Rav Ashi said** that there is a different reason that recitation was omitted from the refutation: It is **that there is** the case of **the first fruits of a convert, who needs to recite:** "I have come to the land **that the Lord swore unto our fathers"** (Deuteronomy 26:3), **and** since **he cannot say** it, as the Lord did not swear to give the land to the ancestors of the convert, he brings the first fruits but does not read the portion. Therefore, the obligation to recite the Torah verses **is not clear-cut for** the *tanna* and he did not mention it.

Having established, as a side-point, that the *baraisa* emphasizes *hanachah* rather than *kriyah* as the distinctive feature of *bikurim* connecting them to the altar, the Gemara returns to the original debate and subjects this inference to scrutiny. Rav Ashi challenges the proof: *nehi de'ikuva leika* — granted that according to Rav Sheshes, *kriyah* is not absolutely required to render the *bikurim* permissible, nevertheless, *mitzva mi leika?* Is there no *mitzva* to recite the declaration? Surely everyone agrees there is a *mitzva* of *kriyah* by *bikurim*. If that is the case, when the *baraisa* lists what is distinctive about *bikurim* and what connects them to the altar, it should have mentioned *kriyah* as well — even if it is not strictly *me'akev*, it is still a feature that connects *bikurim* to the altar. The failure to mention *kriyah* therefore cannot be taken as proof that *kriyah* is not essential.

Rav Ashi then supplies his own explanation for why the *baraisa* omitted *kriyah*: *kivan de'ika bikurei hager* — since a *ger* who brings *bikurim* cannot recite the declaration, because he cannot say *asher nishba Hashem la'avoteinu latet lanu* — "which God swore to our forefathers to give us." This prompts a brief but important digression. There is in fact a *tannaitic* dispute about this very point: one *tanna* holds that upon converting, the *ger* adopts not only the religion but the history of the Jewish people, and therefore can recite *la'avoteinu latet lanu*. The *Rambam*, in his celebrated letter to Ovadya HaGer, rules decisively that a *ger* does say *Elokeinu ve'Elokei avoteinu* and that Avraham Avinu is the *ger*'s father as well, making the *ger* fully part of the people and its history.

In any event, the operative point is that since there exist cases where *kriyah* is not performed by a *ger* — or similarly, when *bikurim* are brought after *Sukkot* — the *baraisa* could not cite *kriyah* as a universal feature of *bikurim*. It therefore cited only *hanachah*, which always applies. The logical conclusion is: *lo psika lei* — it was not a categorical rule applicable in all cases. The absence of *kriyah* from the *baraisa*'s list therefore says nothing about whether *kriyah* is or is not *me'akev*. The only thing that can be inferred is that *hanachah* is the universally applicable feature. The debate between *hanachah* and *kriyah* as the decisive moment remains formally unresolved.

## Ma'aser Sheni Statu (III)s…

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַקִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂר לִבְכוֹר, מָה בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי הַבַּיִת – אַף מַעֲשֵׂר אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **And he compares** the status of second-**tithe** produce **to** the status of **a firstborn** offering, and derives that one may partake of second-tithe produce in Jerusalem only when the Temple is standing.

Returning to the central question of *ma'aser sheni*, the Gemara now asks why the common denominator argument — deriving the *Beis Hamikdash* requirement for *ma'aser sheni* from both *bechor* and *bikurim* together — fails. The answer, as noted, is that the shared characteristic of *bechor* and *bikurim* is their connection to the altar. The *baraisa* therefore relies instead on the textual juxtaposition in the verse linking *ma'aser* and *bechor* — a *hekesh* — to establish that just as *bechor* requires the *Beis Hamikdash*, so does *ma'aser sheni*: *af ma'aser eino ela lifnei habayis*.

## Kedushah Status

### Kedushah Rishonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִדְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִדְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא – בְּכוֹר נָמֵי, אִי קָסָבַר קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִדְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְלֹא קִדְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא – אֲפִילּוּ בְּכוֹר נָמֵי תִּבְּעֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what** opinion **does** Rabbi Yishmael **hold** that led to his initial assumption that one is obligated to bring a firstborn animal to Jerusalem only when the Temple is standing? **If** he **maintains** in general that **the initial consecration** of the Temple **sanctified** Jerusalem **for its time and sanctified** Jerusalem **forever,** and the location of the Temple remains sacred even after the Temple was destroyed, then one should **also** be obligated to bring **a firstborn** animal to the place of the Temple and sacrifice it on an altar and eat it. **If he maintains** that **the initial consecration** of the Temple **sanctified** Jerusalem **for its time but did not sanctify** Jerusalem **forever,** then he should **raise a dilemma even** with regard to **a firstborn,** whether it may be eaten in Jerusalem.

Having established through the *hekesh* that *ma'aser sheni* requires the *Beis Hamikdash*, the Gemara probes a deeper dimension of the question. The inquiry becomes: what does one hold about the *kedushah* of Eretz Yisrael and Jerusalem? *Ei kasavar kedushah rishonah kidshah l'sha'atah vekidshah l'asid lavo* — if one holds that the original *kedushah* of the *Beis Hamikdash*, as established in the time of Yehoshua or Shlomo, persisted even after the destruction of the first *Beis Hamikdash* and continues to apply even now after the destruction of the second, then everything retains its full *kedushah* today. If so, the question arises: why is it obvious that one cannot bring *bechor* nowadays? The answer is that according to the principle *makrivin af al pi she'ein bayis* — one may bring sacrifices even without a standing *Beis Hamikdash* — in theory one could bring *bechor* today, provided one constructs a *mizbe'ach* on the site of the *azarah*. This is the very principle cited in the context of those who wish to bring a *korban Pesach* on Har Habayis.

Conversely, *va'ei kasavar kedushah rishonah kidshah l'sha'atah velo kidshah l'asid lavo* — if one holds that the original *kedushah* did not survive the destruction — then the *kedushah* of Jerusalem no longer exists at all. In that case, why would anyone think one could bring *ma'aser sheni* to Jerusalem and eat it there? There is no *kedushah* of Jerusalem remaining to render the eating permissible, and the question of Rabbi Ishmael would be moot. The Gemara's point is that without a distinction between types of *kedushah*, the analysis leads to an all-or-nothing conclusion: either both *bechor* and *ma'aser sheni* can be brought to Jerusalem (if *kedushah* remains), or neither can (if it does not).

### Kedushah Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר קִדְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְלֹא קִדְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְהָכָא בִּבְכוֹר שֶׁנִּזְרַק דָּמוֹ קוֹדֶם חוּרְבַּן הַבַּיִת, וְחָרַב הַבַּיִת וַעֲדַיִן בְּשָׂרוֹ קַיָּים. וּמַקְּשִׁינַן בְּשָׂרוֹ לְדָמוֹ, מָה דָּמוֹ בַּמִּזְבֵּחַ – אַף בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּמִּזְבֵּחַ. וּמַקִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂר לִבְכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Actually,** Rabbi Yishmael **maintains** that the initial consecration of the Temple **sanctified** Jerusalem **for its time but did not sanctify** Jerusalem **forever; and** why is it obvious to him that the firstborn is not eaten? It is because **here,** he is stating the *halakha* **with regard to** the case of **a firstborn whose blood was sprinkled before the destruction of the Temple, and the Temple was** then **destroyed, and its flesh is still intact. And** based on a juxtaposition: "You shall sprinkle their blood upon the altar…and their flesh shall be for you" (Numbers 18:17–18), **we compare** the status of **its flesh to** the status of **its blood; just as its blood** must be sprinkled **at** a time when the **altar** is standing, **so too its flesh** may be eaten only **at** a time when **the altar** is standing. **And he compares** the status of second-**tithe** produce **to** the status of **a firstborn** offering, and derives that one may partake of second-tithe produce in Jerusalem only when the Temple is standing.

This is where *Tosafot*'s reading of the Gemara opens a particularly illuminating conceptual dimension. *Tosafot* maintains a different *girsa* than the standard text, and following *Tosafot*'s reading, the answer offered by Ravina draws on a crucial distinction between different categories of *kedushah*. There is the *kedushah* of Eretz Yisrael as a whole, and there is the distinct *kedushah* of Jerusalem and the site of the *Beis Hamikdash*. These are not the same.

The *kedushah* of Eretz Yisrael — grounded in the divine promise to Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and described by the verse *einei Hashem Elokecha bah mereshit hashanah ve'ad acharis shanah* — is not contingent on the physical structure of the *Beis Hamikdash*. It can therefore persist even after the Temple's destruction. Grain growing in Eretz Yisrael today is genuinely obligated in *ma'aser sheni* on a Torah level, because the *kedushah* of the land endures.

By contrast, the unique *kedushah* of Jerusalem — particularly the *kedushat hamikdash* on the Temple Mount itself — may well be linked to the physical structure. When that structure no longer stands, the question of whether Jerusalem retains its special status is genuinely open. This yields a precise and elegant formulation of Rabbi Ishmael's question: *ma'aser sheni* originates in the *kedushah* of Eretz Yisrael, which endures. It requires no altar ritual. One might therefore argue that the eating of *ma'aser sheni* in Jerusalem is tied to a different dimension of Jerusalem's holiness than the eating of *bechor* or *bikurim* — one tied to the capital of the land rather than to the altar of the Temple. If so, perhaps *ma'aser sheni* can be eaten in Jerusalem even today, when the *kedushat hamikdash* may no longer apply.

### Ravina Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶקֵּשׁ? מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן חוּלִּין הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does a matter derived via juxtaposition then teach** another matter **via juxtaposition?** The principle with regard to the *halakhot* of consecrated matters is that a *halakha* derived via one of the hermeneutical principles cannot serve as the basis for derivation of another *halakha*; each *halakha* requires its own source. The Gemara answers: Second **tithe of grain is non-sacred** produce, and *halakhot* of non-sacred matters derived via hermeneutical principles may serve as the basis for deriving other *halakhot* using hermeneutical principles.

Ravina's answer, according to *Tosafot*'s reading, is: *le'olam kasvar kidshah l'sha'atah vekidshah l'asid lavo* — one holds that the *kedushah* does in fact persist. The *kedushah* of the Temple site and of Jerusalem endures. Yet the reason one cannot eat *bechor* in Jerusalem today is not that the *kedushah* is absent, but that there is no physical *mizbe'ach*. The question then becomes: does *ma'aser sheni*, which never required the altar for its eating in the first place, also require a physical *mizbe'ach*?

### Blood/Flesh Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּם וּבָשָׂר חֲדָא מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is not a matter derived from a matter derived from a juxtaposition, as the status of the firstborn offering is not derived from the status of blood; **blood and flesh are one matter.** There is only one derivation in this case, which is that the status of second-tithe produce is derived from the status of the blood and the flesh of the firstborn.

To sharpen this question, the Gemara introduces a hypothetical case: *bechor she'nizrak damo kodem churban habayis* — a *bechor* whose blood was properly dashed on the altar just before the Temple was destroyed, and whose flesh remains uneaten after the destruction. The blood-sprinkling was completed; the altar was present for the bringing. But now there is no *mizbe'ach*. Can the flesh be eaten without one? This scenario is introduced not because it commonly occurs, but because it conceptually isolates the act of eating from the act of bringing, allowing one to ask: is the presence of a physical altar required for the eating of *bechor* as well, even once it has been properly brought? And if it is, that has implications for *ma'aser sheni*, which also requires no altar-based bringing — only the eating in Jerusalem.

### Flesh-Blood Linkage

The Gemara answers by linking the flesh to the blood: *umakshinan besaro ledamo — mah damo bemizbe'ach af besaro bemizbe'ach*. Rashi cites the verse: *es damam tizrok al hamizbe'ach uvasaram yihyeh lach* — "their blood you shall dash on the altar, and their flesh shall be yours." The juxtaposition of blood and flesh in this verse creates an *hekesh*: just as the blood requires the altar, so the flesh requires the presence of an altar for its permissible consumption. A *bechor* whose blood was dashed before the destruction still cannot be eaten once the *mizbe'ach* is gone, because flesh and blood are treated as a single integrated *halachah* — *dam u'vasar chada milta hi* — and not as two independent applications that could be separately derived.

This in turn resolves the case of *ma'aser sheni*. Through the *hekesh* linking *ma'aser* to *bechor*, we know: just as *bechor* requires a physical *mizbe'ach* for its permissible eating — even when it has already been properly brought — so too *ma'aser sheni* requires the presence of a physical *mizbe'ach*. The *kedushah* of Jerusalem and the Temple site may well persist, but without a standing physical altar, neither *bechor* nor *ma'aser sheni* may be eaten. The Gemara also addresses a potential challenge regarding double-derived *limudim* (*davar halamad behekesh chozer umelamed behekesh*), but notes that since *ma'aser dagan chullin hu* — *ma'aser* of grain is, relative to *korbanos*, considered non-sacred — the restriction against chained *hekeshim* does not apply. And since blood and flesh are ultimately one *halachah*, the derivation is properly a single link: *ma'aser* is connected to *bechor*, and what applies to *bechor* applies to *ma'aser*.

## Ma'aser Tumah Violation

### Redundancy Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדּוּ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: סֵיפָא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי טָהוֹר וְגַבְרָא טָהוֹר, דְּקָא אָכֵיל חוּץ לַחוֹמָה. רֵישָׁא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי טָמֵא וְגַבְרָא טָמֵא, וְקָא אָכֵיל לֵיהּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who ate **offerings of the most sacred order** outside the Temple courtyard and one who ate second-tithe produce outside the wall of Jerusalem is flogged with forty lashes. The Gemara asks: Didn't **we** already **learn** this **one time** in the previous mishna (13a), that one who ate **second-tithe** produce **or sacrificial** food **that was not redeemed** is flogged? Why does the *tanna* repeat the *halakha* of second-tithe produce in this mishna? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said:** These are different cases; **the latter clause,** this mishna, is **in** the case of **ritually pure second-tithe** produce **and a ritually pure person who eats** it **outside the wall** of Jerusalem. **The first clause,** the previous mishna, is **in** the case of **ritually impure second-tithe** produce **and a ritually impure person who eats** it **within Jerusalem.**

Having concluded the discussion of *ma'aser sheni*'s dependence on the *Beis Hamikdash*, the Gemara turns to a new topic within the same subject matter. The *mishnah* in the present chapter states that one of the violations for which lashes are incurred is eating *kodshim kalim* or *ma'aser sheni chutz lachomah* — outside the walls of Jerusalem. But the first *mishnah* of the chapter already taught that one of the cases warranting lashes is eating *ma'aser sheni ve'hekdesh she'lo nifdah* — *ma'aser sheni* or consecrated property that has not been redeemed. The Gemara raises the question: is this not the same violation? Eating *ma'aser sheni* outside of Jerusalem is a problem precisely because it has not been redeemed. Why does one *mishnah* frame the violation as "unredeemed *ma'aser sheni*" while the other frames it as "eating outside Jerusalem"?

### Resolution Offered

The first *mishnah* — *reisha* — by contrast, is speaking of a case where either the person or the *ma'aser sheni* itself is *tamei*. In such a case, the violation is not simply that one ate outside Jerusalem; rather, even inside Jerusalem one would be forbidden to eat it. If one is *tamei* or the *ma'aser sheni* is *tamei*, the problem is that it has not been redeemed, because the proper response to *tumah* — even within Jerusalem — is redemption. The two *mishnayos* therefore describe two distinct violations: eating unredeemed *ma'aser sheni chutz lachomah* when both are *tahor*, and eating unredeemed *ma'aser sheni be'tumah* even within Jerusalem.

### Rabbi Shimon Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּמִחַיַּיב עֲלֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא בִּעַרְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ בְּטָמֵא״ – בֵּין שֶׁאֲנִי טָמֵא וְהוּא טָהוֹר, בֵּין שֶׁאֲנִי טָהוֹר וְהוּא טָמֵא. וְהֵיכָן מוּזְהָר עַל אֲכִילָה אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that one is liable** to receive lashes **due to impurity?** It is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says** that the verse in the portion of the declaration of tithes: **"I have not put any of it away when impure"** (Deuteronomy 26:14), is a general formulation that is interpreted to mean: **Whether I am impure and** the second-tithe produce **is ritually pure,** or **whether I am ritually pure and** the second-tithe produce **is impure.** Rabbi Shimon adds: **And I do not know where** it is that **one is warned,** i.e., where is there a prohibition, **with regard to eating.** Although it is clear from the verse cited that it is prohibited for one to partake of second-tithe produce while impure, the source for this prohibition is unclear.

This raises a further question: how do we know that eating *ma'aser sheni* while *tamei* incurs lashes at all? The *baraisa* quotes the declaration recited upon the separation of *ma'aser*: *lo vi'arti mimenu b'tamei* — "I did not consume from it in a state of *tumah*" — *bein she'ani tamei v'hu tahor, bein she'ani tahor v'hu tamei* — whether I was *tamei* and it was *tahor*, or I was *tahor* and it was *tamei*. This establishes that there is indeed a prohibition, but for lashes one needs an explicit negative commandment, not merely an implicit inference from a positive declaration. The *baraisa* therefore asks: *v'heichan muzhar al ha'achilah?* — where is the explicit prohibition against eating?

### Pasuk Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאַת הַגּוּף – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב: ״נֶפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תִּגַּע בּוֹ וְטָמְאָה עַד הָעָרֶב וְלֹא יֹאכַל מִן הַקֳּדָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״ אֶלָּא: טוּמְאַת עַצְמוֹ מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** Before citing the source of the prohibition, the Gemara asks: With regard to one with **impurity of the body** who partakes of second-tithe produce, it is **explicitly written: "A soul that touches it shall be impure until the evening and shall not eat of the consecrated food"** (Leviticus 22:6), which the Sages interpret to include second-tithe produce. This is a prohibition with regard to a ritually impure person partaking of second-tithe produce. **But** when Rabbi Shimon says: I do not know where it is that one is warned with regard to eating, he is stating: With regard to **the impurity of** the second-tithe produce **itself, from where** is the warning derived?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״. וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ הַטָּמֵא וְהַטָּהוֹר״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is derived **as it is written** with regard to second-tithe produce: **"You may not eat within your gates** the tithe of your grain or of your wine or of your oil" (Deuteronomy 12:17), **and later,** with regard to a blemished firstborn animal, the verse **states: "Within your gates you may eat it, the impure and the pure** may eat it alike" (Deuteronomy 15:22).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֲפִילּוּ טָמֵא וְטָהוֹר אוֹכְלִין בִּקְעָרָה אַחַת וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: הַאיְךְ טָמֵא דִּשְׁרַי לָךְ גַּבֵּי טָהוֹר הָתָם – הָכָא לָא תֵּיכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael: Even a ritually pure** person **and a ritually impure person may eat** the flesh of a blemished firstborn animal **in one dish and** need **not be concerned,** as there is no prohibition for one to eat it while he is impure or while it is impure. **And the Merciful One says:** It is **that impure** flesh of the blemished firstborn **that is permitted for you** to eat **with a ritually pure** person within your gates **there,** whereas **here,** in the case of second-tithe produce in Jerusalem, **you may not eat** it within your gates in the manner that one eats the flesh of the firstborn.

The Gemara interjects before the *baraisa* reaches its answer: *tumat haguf behadya ketiv* — the prohibition against eating holy items while one's body is impure is stated explicitly in the verse: *nefesh asher tigah bo v'tamah ad ha'erev velo yochal min hakodashim* — "a person who touches it shall be impure until evening and shall not eat from the holy things." This includes *ma'aser sheni*. What remains to be established is the prohibition on eating *ma'aser sheni* when it itself is *tamei* even if the person is *tahor*. For that the Gemara cites: *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* — "you cannot eat within your gates" — referring to *ma'aser*. The word *bish'arecha* — "in your gates," i.e., outside Jerusalem — is compared to another verse (*bish'arecha tochelenu*) in which *tamei* and *tahor* persons may eat together from an animal redeemed from *korban* status due to a blemish. From that context, eating "in your gates" carries the connotation of eating in any manner, including in a state of *tumah*. The Torah therefore says: *lo tuchal* — you cannot eat *ma'aser sheni* in that manner. Do not eat *ma'aser sheni be'tumah*, whether you are *tamei* or whether it is *tamei*.

## Ma'aser Redemption

### Redemption When Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּבַר פְּדִיָּיה הוּא? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא שֶׁפּוֹדִין אוֹתוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי לֹא תוּכַל שְׂאֵתוֹ.״ – וְאֵין ״שְׂאֵת״ אֶלָּא אֲכִילָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיִּשָּׂא מַשְׂאֹת מֵאֵת פָּנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** It is **as Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived **with regard to second-tithe** produce **that became impure that** one **may redeem it even in Jerusalem?** It is derived as **the verse states: "Because you are unable to carry it [*se'eto*],** as the place is too far from you…and you shall turn it into money" (Deuteronomy 14:24–25). **And *se'et*** means **nothing other than eating, as it is stated: "And portions [*masot*] were taken to them from before him"** (Genesis 43:34), referring to gifts of food. Therefore, the phrase: You are unable *se'eto*, means: You cannot eat it, referring to a case where it is impure. The Torah states that in that case one may redeem the produce even if it is in Jerusalem, not at a distance.

The *mishnah*'s implication that when *ma'aser sheni* becomes *tamei* it should be redeemed raises a further question: from where do we know that *ma'aser sheni* that has become *tamei* can be redeemed even while one is inside Jerusalem? Ordinarily, redemption of *ma'aser sheni* is performed before arriving in Jerusalem. Rabbi Elazar derives the permissibility of redemption within Jerusalem in cases of *tumah*: *minayin l'ma'aser sheni she'nitma she'podin oto afilu biYerushalayim?* — from the verse: *ki lo tuchal se'eto* — "for you are unable to carry it [to Jerusalem]."

### Se'eto Interpretation

The word *se'eto* is interpreted not as "carry" but as "eat." The derivation is supported by the verse regarding Yosef's gifting of food portions to his brothers: *vayisa masa'ot me'et panav* — where *masa'ot* refers to food portions, showing that the root *s-a-t* can denote a food allotment. The verse therefore means: because you cannot eat it in a state of permissibility, you are permitted to redeem it even within Jerusalem. One can thus redeem *ma'aser sheni* that has become *tamei* even inside Jerusalem rather than having to bury it.

### One Footstep Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מִנַּיִן לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי טָהוֹר שֶׁפּוֹדִין אוֹתוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בִּפְסִיעָה אַחַת חוּץ לַחוֹמָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא תוּכַל שְׂאֵתוֹ״. הַאי מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another, related interpretation. **Rav Beivai says** that **Rav Asi says: From where** is it derived with regard to **ritually pure second-tithe** produce **that** one **may redeem it even** if it is **one stride outside the wall** of Jerusalem, contrary to the plain understanding of the verse: "As the place is too far from you"? It is derived **as it is stated: "Because you are unable to carry it [*se'eto*],"** indicating that if there is any reason that one is unable to carry the produce and bring it into Jerusalem, he may redeem it regardless of its distance from Jerusalem. The Gemara objects: **That** verse **is necessary to** derive the *halakha* **of Rabbi Elazar** that one may redeem impure second-tithe produce even in Jerusalem.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״לֹא תוּכַל לְאוֹכְלוֹ״, מַאי ״שְׂאֵתוֹ״? וְאֵימָא כּוּלּוֹ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״לֹא תוּכַל לִיטְּלוֹ״, מַאי ״שְׂאֵתוֹ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so,** that the verse is required to derive only the *halakha* of Rabbi Elazar, then **let the verse state** explicitly: **You are unable to eat it. What** is the reason that the verse employs the term **"*se'eto*"?** The Gemara objects: **And say** that the term **comes entirely for this** purpose, to teach that one may redeem the produce even if it is not far from the city, and nothing is derived with regard to impure produce. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse state: You are unable to take it. Why** does the verse employ the term **"*se'eto*"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it:** One may redeem ritually pure second-tithe produce even if it is just outside the walls of Jerusalem, and one may redeem ritually impure second-tithe produce even in Jerusalem.

Having established that *tumah* permits redemption within Jerusalem, Rabbi Bibi in the name of Rabbi Assi extends this to the case of *ma'aser sheni* that is *tahor*: *minayin l'ma'aser sheni tahor she'podin oto afilu bipsi'ah achat chutz lachomah?* — from where do we know that even *tahor ma'aser sheni* can be redeemed when one is even a single footstep outside the walls of Jerusalem? The source verse *ki yirchak mimcha hamakom* — "when the place is far from you" — might suggest a minimum distance. But since the Torah also says *lo tuchal se'eto* — you cannot even lift it — the emphasis is on any displacement at all: if there is any movement required to bring the *ma'aser sheni* into Jerusalem, that is sufficient to permit redemption. The principle of *natata devarecha lish'iurin* — that the Torah does not leave its measures undefined — supports this: since no minimum distance is specified, any distance outside the wall suffices.

### Partial Entry Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב חֲנִינָא וְרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא, וְקָא מִבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אַפִּיתְחָא דִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, מַהוּ? פְּשִׁיטָא, הוּא בַּחוּץ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ בִּפְנִים – קְלָטוּהוּ מְחִיצוֹת, הוּא בִּפְנִים וּמַשָּׂאוֹ בַּחוּץ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** In connection to the discussion of where one may redeem second-tithe produce, the Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥanina and Rav Hoshaya sat, and a dilemma was raised before them:** If the second-tithe produce is at **the entrance of Jerusalem, what is** the *halakha*; can one redeem it there? The Gemara elaborates: It is **obvious** in a case where **one is outside** Jerusalem **and his burden** of second-tithe produce **is inside** Jerusalem; the tithe is **admitted** by **the walls** of Jerusalem and its status is that of produce that entered the city. But in a case where **one is inside** Jerusalem **and his burden** of second-tithe produce **is outside** Jerusalem, **what is** the *halakha*?

The Gemara now turns to the Gemara's further inquiry, raised by Rabbi Chanina and Rav Oshaia sitting together: what is the rule regarding the *afpiskei* — the thresholds or gates — of Jerusalem? A preliminary case is ruled obvious: if the person is outside and his load is inside, *kaltuhu mechitzot* — the walls have absorbed it, and it is considered inside Jerusalem. It cannot be redeemed (unless it is *tamei*). The more genuinely uncertain scenario is the reverse: the person is inside Jerusalem but his load — his *ma'aser sheni* — is still outside the gates, in his pack on his back with the pack extending past the threshold.

### Fullness Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא לְהוּ הָהוּא סָבָא בִּדְבֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: ״כִּי יִרְחַק מִמְּךָ הַמָּקוֹם״ – מִמִּילּוּאֲךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elder taught them** *a baraita* **of the school of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai.** It is written: **"As the place is too far from you [*mimmekha*]"** (Deuteronomy 14:24); and the term "*mimmekha*" is interpreted as: **From your fullness [*mimmilluakha*],** indicating the person and all his appurtenances, including the burden he bears. If any part of him or his appurtenances is inside the city, it is as though he is entirely inside the city, and therefore he may not redeem the second-tithe produce.

An elder from the school of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai teaches from a *baraisa*: *ki yirchak mimcha hamakom — mimlu'acha* — "when it is far from your fullness," meaning the entirety of you. Rashi explains that when one carries something on one's back, that person and the pack are a single unit. Since part of that unit — the person — is already inside Jerusalem, the whole unit is considered inside. Once any part of you has entered, you have entered fully, and redemption is no longer possible for *tahor ma'aser sheni*.

### Rav Pappa Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: נָקֵיט לֵיהּ בְּקַנְיָא, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises** a related **dilemma:** If **one is holding** the second-tithe produce **on a reed,** and it is suspended behind him, **what** is the *halakha*? Since he is carrying the burden, does it fall into the category of his fullness, and it is no different from a burden that he bears on his person, or does its status differ because it is not actually resting on his body? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Pappa presses the boundary of this principle with a final question: *nakti leh b'kanya mai?* — what if the food is not in a backpack but is being held at a distance on a stick, in the manner of a traveler carrying his pack on a pole? The person is inside Jerusalem; the stick extends outward and the *ma'aser sheni* bag hangs at the far end, outside the walls. Is the *ma'aser sheni* considered to be "with" the person who is inside, or does the separation afforded by the length of the stick make a legal difference? The Gemara concludes: *teyku*. The question stands unresolved, and the discussion of what constitutes "entry" into Jerusalem — and at what point redemption of *ma'aser sheni* becomes impossible — continues to the next daf.

A final note of significance: at this point in the commentary of Rashi on Maseches *Makkot*, the following parenthetical remark appears, five lines from the top of the narrow column: *Rabbeinu — gufo tahor v'yatza nishmato b'tahara* — a reference to the tradition that Rashi departed this world while pronouncing the word *tahor*, his soul departing in purity. From this point forward, the commentary is no longer Rashi's own but that of his student Rabbeinu Yehuda bar Nosson, known as the *Rivan*. *Mikan va'eilach l'shon talmido Rabbeinu Yehuda Bar Nosson.*
