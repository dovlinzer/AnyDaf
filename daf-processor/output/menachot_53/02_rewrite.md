# Menachot 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## All Menachot - Matzah

### Chapter Overview

We begin at the bottom of 52b with a new *perek* called *Hamenachot*. The previous two *prakim*, though we spent a wonderful time dealing with *tallis*, *tzitzis*, and *tefillin*, were structured around the theme of *ma'akvin zeh et zeh* — things that prevent one another from being *yotzei*. The preceding *perek* concluded with discussions about the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, the daily *korban* that the *Kohen Gadol* would bring — half in the morning, half in the afternoon — and also discussions about which *korbanot* are *ma'akev zeh et zeh*.

Now there is a major shift. We are no longer discussing *ma'akvin zeh et zeh*. In a manner similar to Zevachim, where after working through five or six *prakim* one finally arrives at the beginning of the conversation about how to actually bring a *korban*, so too here we now begin discussing the rules governing how a *mincha* is actually made. Having worked through all the things that can invalidate and all the *ma'akvin*, we arrive at what might have been the beginning of the entire *masechta*.

### Matzah Rule & Exceptions

The *Mishnah* opens: *kol hamenachot ba'ot matzah* — all *menachot* must come as *matzah*. The *Torah* explicitly states *lo te'afeh chametz*, you may not make them *chametz*; you must bring them *matzah*, and this is stated openly in the *pesukim*. The only exceptions are the forty loaves accompanying a *todah* — ten of which are *chametz* — and the *shtei halechem*, the two loaves brought with the lambs of *Atzeret* on *Shavuot*, about which the *Torah* states that they come *chametz*.

Notably, the bread accompanying the *Nazir*'s sacrifice — some of which is also *chametz* — is not mentioned here, though it arose in the previous *perek*.

### Exceptions - Not Burned

Despite being *chametz*, those loaves that come as exceptions are never placed on the *mizbe'ach*. The *Torah* states clearly: *ki kol se'or vechol devash lo taktiru mimenu isheh l'Hashem* — any leaven or honey shall not be offered as a fire-offering to God — and thus *al hamizbe'ach lo ya'alu l'rei'ach nichoach*. In the case of the *todah*, one slaughters it and sprinkles its blood; in the case of the *shtei halechem*, the two lambs are slaughtered and their blood is sprinkled. No *kmitzah* is ever performed on the loaves themselves to place anything on the *mizbe'ach*. In this way these loaves differ from a standard *mincha*, where either the *kometz* — for a normal *mincha* — or the entire offering — for a *minchat kohanim* — is burned.

*Tosafot* is bothered by the inclusion of the *lachmei todah* in this *Mishnah*, since the *Torah* does not itself call those loaves a *mincha*. The *Torah* does apply the term *mincha* to the *shtei halechem* and to the *minchat nesachim*, so the mere fact of accompanying another offering would not alone be a reason to exclude it from the *mincha* category. Yet the *lachmei todah* are not directly called *mincha*. *Tosafot* is left somewhat troubled by this, and there is no straightforward resolution offered as to why the loaves of the *Nazir* similarly go unmentioned.

---

## Making Chametz - R. Meir

Having established that certain loaves must come *chametz*, the *Mishnah* now discusses how one actually produces *chametz* for those loaves. It is worth pausing to appreciate the novelty: we spend an entire holiday figuring out how to prevent things from becoming *chametz*, and here we discuss how to deliberately make *chametz*. Notably, no opinion suggests simply waiting for the dough to rise on its own; all assume that some form of *se'or* — sourdough — is required to produce the fermentation.

*Rebbe Meir* says: *hase'or boded lahem mitochen* — you take *se'or* from the middle of what you are making. One has the flour, adds water to make the *isah*, takes a piece of it from the middle, allows it to ferment and become sourdough in a damp environment, and then returns it to the mixture. Since this process takes time, one would naturally not do this in *klei shares*, which would render the offering *pasul* due to *linah*.

*Rabbi Yehuda* objects: *af hi einah min hamuvchar* — that method is not ideal. Good sourdough is sourdough that has been fermenting for a long time, handed down and continuously maintained, not quickly produced from the middle of one's own dough. *Rabbi Yehuda* therefore says: *meivi es hase'or* — bring sourdough from home, *venosno lesoch hamidah* — place it in the measuring vessel, and then *umemale es hamidah* — fill the remainder of the vessel with flour to the required measure.

---

## Measuring Issues

The *Chachamim* respond to *Rabbi Yehuda*: *af hi haysah chaseirah o yeseirah* — even this method will result in an inaccurate measure of flour; it will either be too much or too little.

The reason, as Rashi explains, is that sourdough is denser than flour. Sourdough is flour plus water, and when flour is mixed with water and fermented, it can become either more or less dense than plain flour. Consider the case where the sourdough is denser than flour: the quantity of flour that went into producing that sourdough would, had it remained flour, occupy more volume. When the dense sourdough is placed in the measuring vessel and the rest is filled with plain flour, one ends up with more flour than intended, because the flour that went into making the sourdough is taking up less space than it would have occupied as loose flour. Conversely, if the sourdough is less dense than flour, the result would be too little flour.

---

## Flour Measurement Basis

All of this rests on an important principle that the *Gemara* states explicitly: the measurement of flour for a *mincha* is determined by the amount of flour that went into it, not by the current volume of whatever one has in front of one. When the *Torah* says *issaron soles*, it means the quantity of flour that went into the preparation, not the current measure once it has been combined with water and made into dough.

This leads to broader and interesting discussions about how *shiurim* are determined generally — especially in the context of *chametz* on *Pesach* and *matzah*. The question of how to measure a *kezayis* of something like a cookie or *challah* is well known: does one measure the item as it is, with all its air pockets, or does one determine the flour that went into it? Or does one compress it to remove the air pockets?

---

## Tosafot - Measuring

Before turning to the *Gemara*, attention should be directed to *Tosafot* on the words *chaseirah o yeseirah*, the third *Tosafot* in the section. *Tosafot* cites *Rabbeinu Chaim*, who derives from here a rule about how flour is measured when there is a need to determine *shiurim* on *Pesach* — either for the purpose of *challah*, that is, determining whether one has sufficient dough to be obligated in the separation of *challah*, or for the purpose of *matzah*. *Tosafot* rules: *ein lehachbid al hasolet* — one does not compress the flour.

The reasoning follows directly from the logic of the *Mishnah*: if sourdough can be denser than flour, it must be the case that flour is measured as it naturally settles, without compression. For if flour were compressed when measured — eliminating all air pockets — it would be impossible for sourdough to be denser than the flour, since sourdough contains additional ingredients beyond plain flour. The fact that the *Gemara* entertains the possibility that sourdough is denser than flour proves that flour must be measured in its natural, uncompressed state.

*Rabbeinu Chaim* applies this to *challah* — that is, determining the *shiur* obligating one to separate *challah* — and to *matzah*. With respect to *matzah*, he appears to be referring to the *minhag* of using a measure equivalent to an *issaron* or similar *korban*-based measure to bake the three *matzos* used for the *mitzvah* at the Seder, which would directly parallel what was done for a *mincha*. In any case, already in these early sources we encounter the question of whether *shiurim* are measured by volume of flour, by current volume, or by compressed volume — a question with profound practical implications for how much *matzah* one must eat and for *beracha* obligations.

---

## Source for Matzah

The *Gemara* now opens with a question from *Rav Preida* to *Rav Ami*: *minalan de'chol menachot ba'ot matzah* — from where do we know that all *menachot* must come as *matzah*?

*Rav Ami* responds that the answer seems simple: for some *menachot*, the *Torah* explicitly states that they must be *matzah*. For those where the *Torah* does not explicitly state this, the *Torah* states at the opening of *Tzav* — *Zot torat hamincha* — this is the law of the *mincha*, followed by the verse that the remainder *matzot te'achel*, shall be eaten as *matzah*. Since it cannot be eaten as *matzah* if it was not baked as *matzah*, and since this is the stated law of the *mincha*, we derive that all *menachot* must be *matzah*.

*Rav Preida* replies: *mitzvah lo ka'mivaya li* — that it is a *mitzvah* to do it is not my question; I know those *pesukim* too. *Ki ka'mivaya li l'akev* — what I want to know is how we derive that if a *mincha* is not made as *matzah*, it is invalid. The general principle by *kodashim* is that the *Torah* must repeat something to indicate that failure to observe it invalidates the offering. One might have thought this principle should not apply here — after all, a *mincha* is a manufactured object. When the *Torah* specifies that it must be *matzah*, it seems to be defining the object itself, not merely prescribing a ritual detail. Just as the requirement that an *olah* be male defines what kind of animal we are speaking about, so too the *matzah* requirement might be understood as defining the type of object that constitutes a valid *mincha*, such that a *chametz* version simply is not the object. Yet the *Gemara* treats it as requiring a repeated source, as it does for other requirements.

*Rav Ami* responds: *va-akev nami ketiv* — you also have a *pasuk* that tells you failure renders it invalid. The *Torah* says *lo te'afeh chametz* — do not bake it as *chametz*. Paired with *matzot te'achel*, you have both a positive and a negative commandment repeating the same requirement, which suffices to establish *ikuv*.

---

## Chametz Nuksheh

*Rav Chisda* challenges this answer: *ve'eima lo te'afeh chametz ella se'or* — the conclusion does not follow. The *Torah* says do not make it *chametz*, but *chametz* and *matzah* are not exhaustive opposites. There is a middle space between them.

*Se'or* — which in this context the *Gemara* identifies with what is called *chametz nuksheh* elsewhere — is a substance that has begun to ferment but has not fully fermented. It is neither *chametz* nor *matzah*. How do we know that bringing a *mincha* in this quasi-fermented state is invalid? I may be violating the requirement to bring it as *matzah*, but that was stated only once. And I am not violating the prohibition against bringing *chametz*, because this is not real *chametz*. Therefore, since neither *pasuk* addresses this middle category twice, perhaps *bedi'avad* such a *mincha* is valid.

---

## R. Meir vs. R. Yehuda

The *Gemara* then asks: whose *se'or* are we discussing — *Rabbi Meir*'s or *Rabbi Yehuda*'s? This refers to a well-known dispute in *Pesachim* regarding when dough reaches the problematic intermediate stage during *Pesach*.

When dough is left alone, fermentation begins. The external surface first develops a hardened crust — *hichsifu paneha*. At a later stage, cracks begin appearing in that crust, described by the *Gemara* as resembling a spider's web or the horns of a locust.

*Rabbi Meir* is strict: once the crust forms, the dough has already entered the intermediate category and is *chametz nuksheh*. There is a special *pasuk* — *kol machmetzet lo tochelu* — that includes this quasi-*chametz*, and one who eats it on *Pesach* would receive *malkut*. Once the cracks appear, it is full *chametz* and one would receive *karet*. *Rabbi Meir* thus holds that the middle category of *chametz nuksheh* exists and begins at the crusting stage.

*Rabbi Yehuda* is lenient in both directions. He holds that the only relevant stage is the cracking, not the crusting. Moreover, even at that stage, he is not certain there is a *Torah* problem — one would not even receive *malkut*. *Rabbi Yehuda* may thus effectively deny the existence of any *Torah*-level middle category.

The *Gemara* works through the permutations: if the *se'or* in question is *Rabbi Meir*'s stage — the crusting — then from *Rabbi Yehuda*'s perspective it is *matzah me'alya*, perfectly good *matzah*, so there is no middle category to worry about. If it is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s stage — the cracking — then from *Rabbi Meir*'s perspective it is *chametz me'alya*, full *chametz*, and similarly presents no middle category. The only scenario in which a genuine middle ground exists is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s stage from *Rabbi Yehuda*'s perspective: the cracks have appeared, it is *assur*, yet one does not receive *malkut*. The *Gemara* assumes this means it is neither *chametz* nor *matzah* at the *Torah* level — a genuine intermediate state.

*Tosafot* notes a potential difficulty: if *Rabbi Meir* says one receives *malkut* for eating the crusted dough on *Pesach*, that might actually indicate it is full *chametz* — after all, for full *chametz* one receives *karet*, while *malkut* without *karet* suggests it is treated as a lesser category. The *Gemara* initially assumes that lashes indicate full *chametz*, but *Tosafot* points out this is not obvious, and indeed the *Gemara* itself will later clarify this.

---

## Boiled Dough

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* raises yet another challenge: *ve'eima lo te'afeh chametz ella chalut* — what if the *mincha* is not baked as *chametz*, but is instead boiled? Throwing the dough into boiling water before it has a chance to ferment stops the fermentation process, yet it also does not produce *matzah*, since *matzah* is unfermented bread — and boiled dough, though unfermented, is not bread in the conventional sense.

The underlying assumption of the *Gemara* is that *matzah* and *chametz* are not merely categories defined by the presence or absence of fermentation in any cooked product. They must also qualify as *lechem*, as bread. Dough cooked by boiling would seem to lack this quality: it is flour and water that has been cooked, but in a manner that does not produce bread. One might argue that if *matzah* simply means flour and water that has not fermented, then boiled dough qualifies. But the *Gemara* appears to understand that *matzah* means unfermented bread specifically, and boiling produces something categorically different.

This parallels a broader principle that the *Rambam* seems to articulate: for something to be *chametz* in the full *Torah* sense requiring *karet*, and for something to be *matzah* in the *mitzvah* sense, it must also be describable as *lechem*. There are cases in the *Rambam* where even fermented items do not carry the full punishment of *karet* seemingly because they fall short of qualifying as *lechem*.

So *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* asks: perhaps boiled dough occupies the same middle ground as *chametz nuksheh* — neither *chametz* nor *matzah* — and perhaps a *mincha* brought in this form is valid *bedi'avad*, since it violates neither the prohibition on *chametz* nor the requirement for *matzah* in a fully repeated way?

---

## Mincha Revucha

The *Gemara* responds to this challenge: *chalut mai nihu — revucha* — what is this boiling? It is *revucha* — a *mincha* that is boiled in oil. That is an established category of *mincha*. If one is actually bringing the *mincha revucha* that is supposed to be brought that way, then there is no question — one has done precisely what was required.

But the *Gemara* presses further: *ve'ha lo ketiv bah revucha* — what about a *mincha* for which the *Torah* does not say *revucha*? Can one bring it as *revucha bedi'avad* and still be valid? The *Gemara* entertains the thought that where the *Torah* specifies *revucha*, the *mitzvah* is to do it that way, but perhaps where it does not, one has the option to bring it either as *revucha* or as *matzah*. In other words, maybe the specification of *matzah* for those *menachot* is not *me'akev*, and one could have validly brought them boiled. If so, the *Torah*'s description of a *mincha* as a *machavat* or some other type is simply describing one permitted mode, not an exclusive requirement, and *bedi'avad* bringing it boiled would be acceptable.

---

## Prohibition vs. Validity

*Ravina* raises a third challenge to the original proof. The *Gemara* had argued that *lo te'afeh chametz* — do not bake it as *chametz* — combined with *matzot te'achel* constitutes a double statement establishing both the positive obligation and the *ikuv*. But *Ravina* objects: *ve'eima lo te'afeh chametz lameikam gavra belav b'alma* — perhaps the *Torah* is not establishing *ikuv* at all. Perhaps it is telling you that you, the person, will have transgressed a *lav*, but *ufesulei lo mifsal* — the actual *korban* will not be rendered invalid.

This is a striking point. Most of the procedural details of the *beit hamikdash* are stated descriptively: here is how you perform *shechitah*, here is where you sprinkle the blood. The *Torah* does not typically say "you may not do it wrong" as a prohibition — it simply describes the correct process, and violation invalidates. Here, by contrast, the *Torah* frames the matter as a personal prohibition: *lo te'afeh chametz*. *Ravina* argues that this framing suggests something different: the *Torah* is imposing a personal obligation not to do it, much as the *Rambam* suggests this prohibition may be rooted in the need to distance the *mikdash* from associations with spring fertility rites involving fermented grain. The *Torah* emphasizes: *you* may not do this. But perhaps the *korban* itself remains *kasher* if one did.

This gives us three separate challenges to the simple proof: first, there is the middle category of *chametz nuksheh* not addressed by either *pasuk*; second, there is boiled dough, similarly a middle category; and third, even for real *chametz*, perhaps the prohibition addresses personal culpability without invalidating the *korban*.

---

## Baraita Resolution

After all these challenges, the *Gemara* concludes: *ella minalan — k'detanya* — from where do we derive the rule? From a *baraita*. The *baraita* cites the *pasuk* by the *mincha al hamachavat* (Vayikra 2:5): *u'mincha al hamachavat korbancha soles belulah bashemen matzah tihyeh* — your meal offering on the flat pan shall be of fine flour with oil; *matzah tihyeh*. The *baraita* asks: *yachol mitzvah* — perhaps this is only a *mitzvah*, not obligatory? *Talmud lomar tihyeh* — the word *tihyeh* teaches that it shall be. *Hakasuv keva'ah chovah* — the *Torah* makes it an absolute obligation.

What is the mechanism? Had the *Torah* wished only to establish *ikuv*, it would have written *matzah hi* — it is *matzah*. The use of *tihyeh* — it shall be — implies something more: the *Torah* makes it an ongoing, obligatory state. Now of course this *pasuk* is by one specific *mincha*, the *machavat*. How does one generalize to all *menachot*? *Tosafot* explains that the detail was already known for this *mincha*, and therefore the *Torah*'s act of repeating it is itself the generalization — teaching a rule applicable to all *menachot*. The derivation is somewhat anti-climactic: after all the sophisticated arguments about middle categories, personal prohibitions, and the structure of *pasuk* pairs, the answer rests on the word *tihyeh* in one specific context being generalized to the whole.

---

## Lukewarm Water & Vigilan…

*Rav Preida* now poses a further question to *Rav Ami*: *minayin l'chol hamenachot sheniloshos befoshrin umishmartan shelo yachmitzu* — from where do we know that all *menachot* must be kneaded with lukewarm water, and that one must be vigilant to ensure they do not become *chametz*? A later *Mishnah* establishes that lukewarm water is used when making a *mincha* — hot water accelerates fermentation — and that one must be constantly vigilant, working the dough and taking care throughout the process. But where is the source for a positive obligation to be vigilant?

One might suggest: *nilmadenah miPesach* — let us learn it from *Pesach*, where the *Torah* says *u'shmartem es hamatzos* — you shall watch over the *matzot*. Perhaps the same concept of watchfulness applies to *menachot*.

*Rav Ami* responds: there is no need to derive it from *Pesach*, for *begufah ksiv* — it is written in the *mincha* itself. The same *pasuk* — *matzah tihyeh* — teaches this. 

---

## Matzah Tihiyeh Derash

The *Gemara* now explains the derivation. If the *Torah* wished only to establish *ikuv*, it would have sufficed to write *matzah hi*. The use of *tihyeh* yields an additional teaching: *shema mina tarti* — derive from it two things. First, failure to make it *matzah* renders the *mincha* invalid. Second, the word *tihyeh* carries a sense of *techayeh* — give it life, bring it to life as *matzah* — communicating an obligation of active vigilance to ensure it remains *matzah* and does not become *chametz*.

The connection to *Pesach* is telling: the idea of vigilance — *shmirah* — is central to *Pesach*, where the prohibition of *chametz* is framed as much as a mental and attitudinal stance as a physical one. Here, too, the *Torah* is not merely prohibiting the wrong product; it is mandating a posture of active watchfulness throughout the preparation. There is an interesting parallel to how the *Gemara* at the beginning of this *masechta* assumed the use of water — a detail never explicitly mentioned in the *Torah* — yet the whole framework of making a *mincha* without fermenting it presupposes water used carefully and deliberately.

There is also an interesting interplay between the *hei* and the *chet* in this *derash* — reading *tihyeh* as containing a sense of *techayeh*. This same linguistic dynamic will arise again later in the *sugya*.

---

## Rav Preida & Rabbi Ezra

The *Gemara* now transitions to an aggadic passage. The *rabanan* — the students in the *beit medrash* — said to *Rav Preida*: *Rabbi Ezra bar bereih d'Rabbi Avtulas* — Rabbi Ezra, grandson of Rabbi Avtulas, *de'hu asiri l'Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* — who is a tenth-generation descendant of *Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah*, *de'hu asiri l'Ezra* — who was himself a tenth-generation descendant of *Ezra Hasofer* — *kai abava* — is standing at the door, wishing to enter the *beit medrash*.

*Rav Preida* responded: *mai kulei hai* — what is all this introduction about his lineage? *Ei bar oryan ihu, ya'ei* — if he is a *ben Torah*, that is nice. *Ei bar oryan u'var avohan, ya'ei v'ya'ei* — if he is a *ben Torah* and also has fine *yichus*, that is doubly nice. But *ei bar avohan velo bar oryan* — if he has *yichus* but is not a *ben Torah* — *esh techeilneih* — let fire consume him. I am unimpressed with *yichus* alone; this is a *yeshiva*.

The students confirmed: *bar oryan hu* — he is indeed a *ben Torah*. *Rav Preida* immediately said: *leiyel, v'leisei* — let him come in. So *Rabbi Ezra* entered the *beit medrash*, and the *Gemara* reports that *Rav Preida* observed that he was in a foul or darkened mood — perhaps, one may reasonably infer, because his proud *yichus* had just been pointedly dismissed as insufficient grounds for entry on its own.

---

## God's Gratitude

*Rav Preida* decided to open with an *aggadeta* to address the mood in the room. The *pasuk* he chose was from Tehillim: *amarta l'Hashem Adonai atah, tovasi bal alecha* — you said to God, "You are my God; my goodness is not upon You." This is a striking *pasuk*: why would the Congregation of Israel say that their goodness is not upon God?

The *Gemara* explains: *Knesses Yisrael* said to God — *Ribbono Shel Olam, hachzeik li tova*, grant me credit. Why? *De-hodia'ticha ba-olam* — I have made Your fame known in the world; I have spread recognition of Your existence and Godhood. Give me credit for that.

God responds — *amarta, tovasi bal alecha*: my gratitude is not for you, *ein ani machzik tova* — I have no debt of gratitude toward you. My gratitude is *ela l'Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov*, for it was they who first made My name known in the world — *she-hodia'uni techilla ba-olam*. The next *pasuk* continues: *likdoshim asher ba-aretz heimah, v'adirei kol cheftzi bam* — to the holy ones in the land, the mighty ones in whom all My desire resides. The *adirim* — the mighty ones — are associated here with the *Avot*.

One of the *mefarshim* suggested that *Rav Preida* was indirectly saying to *Rabbi Ezra*: it is your grandparents to whom credit belongs, not to you yourself. Another *mefaresh* suggested *Rav Preida* was saying that the students deserved credit for vouching for him and spreading his name. But the more compelling reading is that *Rav Preida* was explaining his own position: do not take offense. *Knesses Yisrael* says "look at all we have accomplished," and God says the credit belongs to the ancestors. Children do not inherit the merit of what their ancestors did. You don't get credit for your *yichus*. Whether this was meant to console *Rabbi Ezra* or further explain *Rav Preida*'s position is not entirely clear — it might not have been immediately comforting to someone proud of his lineage.

---

## Drashot on Affinity

Upon hearing the word *adir* in *Rav Preida*'s exposition, *Rabbi Ezra* recalled a *drasha* on that same concept, and opened with a series of closely related *drashot* on the theme of affinity between similar things.

*Yavo adir veyipara la'adirim me'adirim be'adirim* — let the *adir* come and pay back the *adirim* from the *adirim* in the *adirim*. Who is each? *Yavo adir* — the mighty one — *zeh Hakadosh Baruch Hu*, God, who is *adir bamarom*. *Veyipara la'adirim* — and let Him pay back the *adirim* — *elu Yisrael* — this is Israel — *de'ketiv v'adirei kol cheftzi bam*. Crucially, *Rabbi Ezra* reads this *pasuk* differently from *Rav Preida*: where *Rav Preida* applied *adirei kol cheftzi bam* to the *Avot*, *Rabbi Ezra* reads it as referring to Israel itself. *Me'adirim* — from the mighty ones — *elu haMitzrim*, the Egyptians — *de'ketiv tzalelu ka'oferet bemayim adirim*, they sank like lead in the mighty waters. *Be'adirim* — in the mighty ones — *elu hamayim* — the water — *de'ketiv mikolot mayim rabim adirim mishberei yam*. Thus: God, the mighty one, paid back Israel from and through the Egyptians in the sea.

The second *drasha*: *yavo yedid ben yedid, veyivneh yedid l'yedid bechelko shel yedid, veyitkapru bo yedidim* — let the beloved son of the beloved come and build the beloved thing for the beloved one in the portion of the beloved one, and let the beloved people be atoned through it. *Yavo yedid* — *zeh Shlomo HaMelech*, who was called *Yedidyah* — *ba'avur ki Hashem aheivo*. *Ben yedid* — the son of the beloved — *zeh Avraham*, not David, interestingly — *de'ketiv mah liyedidi beveisi*, a *pasuk* from *Yirmiyah* which will be expounded shortly. *Veyivneh yedid* — and let the beloved build — *zeh Beis HaMikdash* — *de'ketiv mah yedidos mishkenosecha*. *Leyedid* — for the beloved — *zeh Hakadosh Baruch Hu*. *Bechelko shel yedid* — in the portion of the beloved — *zeh Binyamin*, for the *Beis HaMikdash* was located in the portion of *Binyamin* — *de'ketiv l'Vinyamin amar yedid Hashem yishkon lavetach alav*. *Veyitkapru bo yedidim* — and let the beloved ones be atoned through it — *elu Yisrael* — *de'ketiv nasati es yedidus nafshi bechaph oyveha*.

The third *drasha*: *yavo tov veyekabel tov mitov l'tovim* — let the good come and receive the good from the good one for the good. *Yavo tov* — *zeh Moshe* — *de'ketiv vateira oso ki tov hu*. *Veyekabel tov* — and receive the good — *zu Torah* — *de'ketiv ki lekach tov nasati lachem*. *Mitov* — from the good one — *zeh Hakadosh Baruch Hu*, for God is good to all. *Letovim* — for the good — *elu Yisrael* — *de'ketiv heitivah Hashem letovim*.

The fourth *drasha* strikes one as the most abstract: *yavo zeh veyekabel zot mizeh le'am zu* — let this one come and receive this from this one for this people. *Yavo zeh* — *zeh Moshe* — *de'ketiv ki zeh Moshe ha'ish*. *Veyekabel zot* — *zot Torah* — *de'ketiv vezot haTorah*. *Mizeh* — from this — *zeh Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — *de'ketiv zeh Keli va'anvehu*. *Le'am zu* — *elu Yisrael* — *de'ketiv am zu kanita*.

## Lineage & Torah

What is the point of this series of *drashot*? The theme running through all of them is affinity: *adir* connects to *adirim*, *yedid* builds for *yedid*, *tov* gives to *tovim*, *zeh* gives *zot* to *am zu*. When entities share the same essential character, they naturally work together and belong together.

*Rabbi Ezra* is implicitly making a response to *Rav Preida*. He is not saying that *yichus* alone entitles one to anything. He heard *Rav Preida*'s point. But he is saying something more nuanced: when *yichus* and *Torah* combine, there is a special power in that combination. He is himself the bearer of both — he is a *ben Torah*, as the students confirmed, and he descends from *Ezra Hasofer*. The poetry of *yavo yedid ben yedid* speaks precisely to this: let Ezra come for Ezra, for Ezra — there is something poetically and substantively real about a family of *Torah* carrying *Torah* forward. The affinity is not merely decorative.

It is worth noting that *Rav Preida*'s own *girsa* says simply *ya'ei v'ya'ei* — it is good and it is good — when someone has both *Torah* and *yichus*. *Rabbi Ezra* seems to be pressing that the combination is not merely additive but constitutes something of its own. The historical record bears this out: one need only observe that the vast bulk of the marginal commentary on the *Gemara* — *Rashi* on one side, *Tosafot* on the other — was produced by a single family. *Rashi*'s grandsons were *Rabbeinu Tam* and the *Rashbam*, and the *Ri* was a nephew. One family, across generations, produced the foundational *rishonim* on virtually the entire *Shas*. When *Torah* and *yichus* align, they can reinforce each other in ways that are historically consequential.

The *Gemara* also invokes the principle of *machzik ma'asei avotav beyado* — when one follows in the ways of one's ancestors, the reward or consequence of those ancestors is reinforced and renewed in the current generation. That dynamic, for good or for ill, is what *Rabbi Ezra* is gesturing toward. He is not asking for unearned credit. He is pointing to the way in which *yichus*, when paired with genuine *Torah* learning, carries a particular resonance — and the poetry of *adir* paying back *adirim*, of *yedid* building for *yedid*, captures something real about how the world of *Torah* works across generations.

The *aggadeta* is brought here in the context of *Rav Preida*, who was the *Amora* featured in the previous legal discussion. The *drashot* at the end of this section touch on *pesukim* that in their fuller context relate to the *Churban Habayis*, giving the passage a broader resonance with the general themes of *Kodshim* that will be taken up in the shiur to follow.
