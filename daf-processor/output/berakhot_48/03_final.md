# Berakhot 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zimmun Composition

### Minyan Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תְּנַן: נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים וּקְטַנִּים אֵין מְזַמְּנִין עֲלֵיהֶם! הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ קָטָן הַמּוּטָּל בַּעֲרִיסָה אֵין מְזַמְּנִין עָלָיו, אֲבָל עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ סְנִיף לַעֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't we learn** in the mishna **that women, slaves, and minors are not included in a *zimmun*?** **He** stated his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Although a minor lying in a cradle is not included in a *zimmun*, one may make him an adjunct to** complete an assembly of **ten** people, enabling them to invoke God's name in a *zimmun*.

The daf opens on 48a with a statement of Rav Yehuda, picking up from the preceding discussion about who may count toward the quorum for *zimmun*. Yesterday's sugya raised pivotal questions about the *minyan* of ten — whether a minor could serve as the tenth, the debates found in Tosafot about whether the same logic might extend to a woman, the device of a minor holding a *Sefer Torah*, and the broader question of whether there are different standards for the person who completes the group of ten versus the group of three. These issues have had profound practical relevance in communities historically struggling to assemble a *minyan*. The Gemara now turns from those questions to the equally daily and practical question of what constitutes a valid *zimmun*.

### Tisha Achlu Dagan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: תִּשְׁעָה אָכְלוּ דָּגָן וְאֶחָד אָכַל יָרָק — מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat, said in the name of Rav:** If **nine ate grain and one ate vegetables, they join** and form a *zimmun* of ten.

Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat, states in the name of Rav: *tisha achlu dagan ve-echad achal yerak* — if nine people ate grain and one person ate only vegetables, the tenth nevertheless joins to form a *zimmun* of ten, allowing the leader to say *Eloheinu*. The parallel to the previous discussion is instructive: there, the Gemara entertained the idea of a minor counting as the tenth; here the person is certainly an adult, but has not eaten bread. The minimum threshold is that he ate something.

### Majority Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּעַאי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁמֹנָה מַהוּ? שִׁבְעָה מַהוּ? אָמַר לִי: לָא שְׁנָא. שִׁשָּׁה וַדַּאי לָא מִבַּעְיָא לִי. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ דְּלָא אִיבַּעְיָא לְךָ, הָתָם טַעְמָא מַאי — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא רוּבָּא, הָכָא נָמֵי אִיכָּא רוּבָּא. וְאִיהוּ סָבַר רוּבָּא דְמִינְּכַר בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: I raised a dilemma before Rav Yehuda: What is** the ruling if **eight** ate grain and two ate vegetables? May they join together? **What is** the ruling if **seven** ate grain? **He said to me: There is no difference. I certainly had no dilemma with regard to six,** as it was clear to me that six are insufficient to warrant a *zimmun*. **Rabbi Yirmeya said to him: You did well that you had no dilemma** with regard to six, but for the opposite reason. **There,** in the case of seven or eight, **what is the reason** that they form a *zimmun* of ten? **Because there is a majority** of those dining who ate grain. **Here, too, there is a majority.** Rabbi Zeira, however, **held: We require an obvious majority.** Therefore, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Yirmeya, it was clear to him that six who ate grain are insufficient to form a *zimmun*.

Rav Zeira then reports that he posed a series of questions to Rav Yehuda: *shemona mahu, shiva mahu* — what about eight, what about seven? Rav Yehuda replied that it makes no difference — the same rule applies. Rav Zeira then lamented that he had not thought to ask about six, since he was uncertain whether that would suffice. Rav Yirmiya responds that it was well that he did not ask, since the ruling in the case of nine, eight, and seven rests on the principle that there exists a *ruba* — a majority — who ate bread, and six would equally constitute a majority. Rav Zeira's position, however, is that he requires *ruba de-minchar* — a discernible, obvious majority — and six, being barely a majority, may not meet that standard.

The practical *halacha* follows this latter view: if seven of ten ate bread, one may say *Eloheinu*; if only six ate bread and four did not, one may not.

### Vegetable & Wine Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ טִבֵּל עִמָּהֶם אֶלָּא בְּצִיר, וְלֹא אָכַל עִמָּהֶם אֶלָּא גְּרוֹגֶרֶת אַחַת — מִצְטָרֵף. וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָם — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת דָּגָן. אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הִלְכְתָא, אָכַל עֲלֵה יָרָק וְשָׁתָה כּוֹס שֶׁל יַיִן — מִצְטָרֵף. לְהוֹצִיא — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, this *halakha* **was also stated: Rav Ḥana bar Yehuda said in the name of Rava: Even if he only** **dipped with them** a small bit of food **in brine and ate with them only a single dry fig, he joins them. And to satisfy the obligation of the many, he does not satisfy** their obligation **until he eats an olive-bulk of grain. Rabbi Ḥana bar Yehuda said in the name of Rava** that **the *halakha* is:** If one **ate a vegetable leaf and drank a cup of wine, he joins** the diners. However, **to satisfy** the obligation of others, **he does not satisfy** their obligation **until he eats an olive-bulk of grain.**

The Gemara then records the rule: *ve-hacha hilchata* — the practical ruling is that even one who ate a vegetable or drank a cup of wine *mitztareif*, he joins the group. The question of wine is addressed by the principle that drinking is included within the category of eating. The ruling is that such a person may be counted toward the quorum, and even the word *Eloheinu* may be said, since *shitiya bichlal achila* — drinking is subsumed within the act of eating.

### Three-Person Zimmun

The Gemara does not explicitly address the parallel question for the three-person *zimmun* — whether all three must eat bread. This gap is filled by the *rishonim*. Tosafot records two opinions without resolving the matter. The *Shulchan Aruch* brings three positions: one requiring all three to have eaten bread; a second permitting the third to have eaten only *mezonot*; and a third permitting even *shehakol* or vegetables. This is a genuine three-way dispute among the authorities.

### Modern Practice

The *Shulchan Aruch* ideally prescribes that if a third person joins who has not eaten bread, one should proactively encourage him to eat bread — or at least *mezonot* — so as to participate fully in the *zimmun*. In practice, if the third person has eaten only vegetables or some other food, the *zimmun* may nonetheless proceed. The person leading the *zimmun*, however, should certainly be one who has eaten bread.

There is, however, an important qualification shaping modern practice: in the time of the Gemara, the leader of the benching was *motzi* the others — he discharged their obligation entirely. Today, everyone recites *Birkat Hamazon* for themselves; the leader says only the introductory formulas. This shift has led to a degree of practical leniency on these questions, since the stakes of the leader's eating status are considerably lower when he is not discharging anyone else's obligation.

## Yannai & Shimon Story

### Yannai's Purge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַנַּאי מַלְכָּא וּמַלְכְּתָא כְּרִיכוּ רִיפְתָּא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וּמִדִּקְטַל לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן לָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ אִינִישׁ לְבָרוֹכֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **King Yannai and the queen ate bread together. And since** Yannai **executed the Sages, there was no one to recite the** Grace after Meals **blessing on their behalf.**

The Gemara now presents a famous narrative: *Yannai Malka u-malchata kerichu rifta bahadi hadadei* — King Yannai and the queen were eating bread together. The story is set against the backdrop of a well-known historical episode in which Yannai had killed all the rabbis. As a result, *lo hava lei inish levoruchei lehu* — there was no one left who could lead the benching for them. The absence of qualified individuals to lead *Birkat Hamazon* is treated here with the same gravity as the destruction of the rabbinic leadership itself — a striking juxtaposition.

### Shimon Brought In

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לַהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ: מַאן יָהֵיב לַן גַּבְרָא דִּמְבָרֵךְ לַן? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִשְׁתְּבַע לִי דְּאִי מַיְיתֵינָא לָךְ גַּבְרָא דְּלָא מְצַעֲרַתְּ לֵיהּ. אִשְׁתְּבַע לַהּ. אַיְיתִיתֵיהּ לְשִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח אֲחוּהָ. אוֹתְבֵיהּ בֵּין דִּידֵיהּ לְדִידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to his wife: Who will provide us** with **a man to recite the blessing on our behalf? She said to him: Swear to me that if I bring you** such **a man, you will not harass him. He swore,** and **she brought her brother, Shimon ben Shataḥ. She sat him between** the King's throne **and hers.**

Yannai said to his wife: *man yahav lan gavra demevarech lan* — who can find us someone to lead the benching? She replied: swear to me that if I bring someone, you will not harm him. He swore. She then produced her brother, Shimon ben Shetach, whom she had concealed from the purge of the sages. They seated him between the king and the queen. The *Yerushalmi* provides additional context: Yannai had been embarrassed in front of Persian visitors who had previously known Shimon ben Shetach as a great sage and inquired about him, making it pragmatically necessary for Yannai to rehabilitate him. The queen, *Shlomtzion Hamalka*, had hidden her brother precisely to make this possible.

### Shimon's Reply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חָזֵית כַּמָּה יְקָרָא עָבֵידְנָא לָךְ. אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו אַתְּ קָא מוֹקְרַתְּ לִי אֶלָּא אוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא דְּמוֹקְרָא לִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״סַלְסְלֶהָ וּתְרוֹמְמֶךָּ תְּכַבֵּדְךָ כִּי תְחַבְּקֶנָּה״. אֲמַר לַהּ: קָא חָזֵית דְּלָא מְקַבֵּל מָרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The King **said to him: Do you see how much honor I am according you? He responded:** It is **not you who honors me; rather, the Torah honors me, as it is written: "Extol her and she will exalt you; she will bring you to honor when you embrace her"** (Proverbs 4:8). Yannai **said to** his wife: **You see that he does not accept authority.**

Yannai said to Shimon: *chazi kama yekara avdinan lach* — see how much honor we are giving you. Shimon responded without hesitation: *lav at mokrat li ela oraita hi demokra li* — it is not you who honors me; it is the Torah that confers my honor, as it is written, *salsaleha uteromemeka, techabedeka ki techabkenah* — honor the Torah and it will exalt you; embrace it and it will honor you. Yannai turned to his wife and said: you see — this man does not accept my authority. One can only wonder at Shimon ben Shetach's sense of self-preservation in this exchange, though perhaps his point was precisely that no royal oath could substitute for that relationship.

### Not Eaten Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ כָּסָא לְבָרוֹכֵי. אֲמַר: הֵיכִי אֲבָרֵיךְ — ״בָּרוּךְ שֶׁאָכַל יַנַּאי וַחֲבֵירָיו מִשֶּׁלּוֹ״? שַׁתְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא כָּסָא, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ כָּסָא אַחֲרִינָא וּבָרֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **They gave** Shimon ben Shataḥ **a cup** of wine **over which to recite** Grace after Meals. **He said: How shall I recite the blessing?** Shall I say: **Blessed is He from Whom Yannai and his companions have eaten?** I have not eaten anything. **He drank that cup** of wine. **They gave him another cup, and he recited the** Grace after Meals **blessing.** By drinking the first cup he joined the other diners and was therefore eligible to recite Grace after Meals on their behalf.

They gave Shimon ben Shetach a cup to lead the benching. He said: *heichi evarech?* — how can I make a blessing? I have not eaten with them. He proposed saying *Baruch she-achal Yannai ve-chaverav* — blessed is the One from whose food Yannai and his companions ate — since he could not truthfully say *nevareich*, which requires the leader to have partaken. Instead, he drank the cup they had given him, effectively joining the meal through drinking. They then refilled the cup, and he led the benching. The Gemara immediately points out, through Rabbi Abba son of Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, that *Shimon ben Shetach de-avad legarmei hu de-avad* — what Shimon did, he did as a personal ruling for himself, not as normative *halacha* for others.

### Kezayit Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח דַּעֲבַד — לְגַרְמֵיהּ הוּא דַּעֲבַד, דְּהָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this story, **Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, said** (that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said): That which Shimon ben Shataḥ did,** reciting Grace after Meals on their behalf, **he did on his own,** and not in accordance with the accepted *halakha*, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: One** who recites Grace after Meals **cannot fulfill the obligation of others** to recite it **until he eats an olive-bulk of grain.**

The normative rule, stated by Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is: *le-olam eino motzi et ha-rabbim yedei chovatan ad she-yochal kezayit dagan* — one can never discharge the public from their obligation until one has eaten a *kezayit* of grain. The leader of the benching, who in that era was genuinely *motzi* the others, had to have eaten bread. The reason Shimon ben Shetach's personal arrangement involved drinking rather than eating is that he found himself in an extraordinary circumstance and ruled leniently for himself — and even that ruling is not accepted as binding precedent.

## Kezayit vs. Satiation

### Rashi/Tosafot Q

This ruling generates a sharp analytical question raised by both Rashi and Tosafot: the Gemara implies that eating a *kezayit* of bread suffices to enable one to lead the benching and discharge others. But eating only a *kezayit* creates only a *derabbanan* obligation to bench — the *de-oraita* obligation of *Birkat Hamazon* is triggered only by *sevi'ah*, by actual satiation. Yannai, the queen, and the rest of the company had certainly eaten their fill. How, then, could Shimon ben Shetach, had he eaten only a *kezayit*, have discharged a *de-oraita* obligation through a *derabbanan* performance?

### Tosafot Solution

Rashi's answer is that in certain circumstances, a *derabbanan* obligation can indeed discharge a *de-oraita* one — a position Tosafot finds entirely unacceptable. Tosafot offers a striking and counterintuitive alternative: in principle, one can be *motzi* another person in *Birkat Hamazon* even without having eaten at all. The reasoning is drawn from an analogy to other *mitzvot*: if one has already fulfilled an obligation — already heard *shofar*, already read *Megillah* — one can still perform the act again to discharge the obligation of another who has not yet fulfilled it. The mechanism of *motzi* is not dependent on one's own current state of obligation, but on the category of the *mitzvah* itself. The sole reason the Gemara requires the leader to have eaten is so that he can truthfully say *she-achalnu mi-shelo* — that "we ate from His bounty." It is the verbal declaration, not the mechanism of discharge, that requires prior eating.

This is a remarkable position with far-reaching implications. If the only reason the leader must eat is to truthfully say *she-achalnu mi-shelo*, then in a context where that phrase need not be said, one might be *motzi* another even without having eaten at all. Some *Poskim* accept this inference; others find it untenable. The question has practical ramifications: if two people are eating together and one, for whatever reason, cannot bench — would the other be able to bench for him even without himself having eaten? The debate among *Poskim* on this question flows directly from Tosafot's position here.

### Behag View

The *Behag* (*Halachot Gedolot*) offers an entirely different resolution, one that many find more intuitive: when the Gemara says that a *kezayit* is sufficient for one to discharge others, it means that the others have likewise eaten only a *kezayit*. But if others have eaten to satiation and incurred a *de-oraita* obligation, someone who has eaten only a *kezayit* — and bears only a *derabbanan* obligation — cannot discharge them. The *de-oraita* can only be discharged by someone equally obligated at the *de-oraita* level.

The *Behag*'s answer is conceptually comfortable, but it does not fully fit the circumstance of the Yannai story, where it seems clear that the assembled guests had eaten to satiation. Perhaps the Gemara's statement that Shimon acted for himself carries a deeper implication — that even he would not have been prepared to rule this way in ordinary circumstances.

### Modern Leniency

As a practical summary: the *halacha* requires a majority of seven out of ten to have eaten bread for *Eloheinu* to be said; for the group of three, the ideal is that all three have bread, with *mezonot* being a widely accepted fallback, and even vegetables or *shehakol* being permissible after the fact. The person who leads should ideally have eaten to satiation, or at minimum a *kezayit* of bread. In contemporary practice, however, where the *zimmun* consists only of the opening formulas and everyone benches individually, these standards are applied with somewhat less stringency, since the leader is not in fact discharging anyone else's obligation.

## Rabban Shimon Rule

### Dipping & Fig

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְיִתִיבִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: עָלָה וְהֵסֵיב עִמָּהֶם אֲפִילּוּ לֹא טִבֵּל עִמָּהֶם אֶלָּא בְּצִיר, וְלֹא אָכַל עִמָּהֶם אֶלָּא גְּרוֹגֶרֶת אַחַת — מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on what was taught in a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One who entered and reclined together** with those who were dining, **even if he only dipped with them** a small bit of food **in** spicy **brine** that was before them **and ate with them only a single dry fig, he joins them.** This *baraita* demonstrates that one need not necessarily eat grain to recite Grace after Meals on their behalf.

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that if someone came and reclined at the table, even if he dipped only in *tzir* — brine or vinegar — and ate no more than a single *grogeret*, a pressed fig, he *mitztareif* — he joins the group. At first glance, this seems to contradict the ruling requiring a *kezayit* of bread to lead the benching, since a fig is not bread.

### Mitztareif Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרוֹפֵי מִצְטָרֵף אֲבָל לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן — עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Indeed, **he joins them, but** he cannot **satisfy the obligation of the many unless he has eaten an olive-bulk of grain.**

The Gemara resolves this cleanly: *mitztareif mitztareif* — the word *mitztareif* means he may join the group for the count of three or ten, but *le-hotzi et ha-rabbim yedei chovatam ad she-yochal kezayit dagan* — to actually lead and discharge others, he must have eaten a *kezayit* of grain. These are two separate questions: what qualifies one to be counted as part of the group, and what qualifies one to lead the benching. The answer to the first is lenient; the answer to the second is not.

### Leaf & Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הִלְכְתָא, אָכַל עֲלֵה יָרָק וְשָׁתָה כּוֹס שֶׁל יַיִן — מִצְטָרֵף. לְהוֹצִיא — אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא עַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת דָּגָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥana bar Yehuda said in the name of Rava** that **the *halakha* is:** If one **ate a vegetable leaf and drank a cup of wine, he joins** the diners. However, **to satisfy** the obligation of others, **he does not satisfy** their obligation **until he eats an olive-bulk of grain.**

A further ruling is then cited: Rav Chana bar Yehuda in the name of Rava states the *halacha* explicitly — even if one ate only a leaf of vegetable or drank a cup of wine, he may join (*mitztareif*), but to discharge others he must have eaten a *kezayit* of bread. This is the final practical ruling of this section: *achal aleh yerek ve-shatah kos shel yayin mitztareif; le-hotzi eino motzi ad she-yochal kezayit dagan*.

## Sources of Benching

### Moshe & Manna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מֹשֶׁה תִּקֵּן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּרְכַּת ״הַזָּן״ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיָּרַד לָהֶם מָן. יְהוֹשֻׁעַ תִּקֵּן לָהֶם בִּרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לָאָרֶץ. דָּוִד וּשְׁלֹמֹה תִּקְּנוּ ״בּוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the origins of the four blessings of Grace after Meals, **Rav Naḥman said:**  **Moses instituted for Israel the** first **blessing of: Who feeds** all, **when the manna descended for them** and they needed to thank God. **Joshua instituted the blessing of the land when they entered Eretz Yisrael.** **David and Solomon instituted the** third blessing: **Who builds Jerusalem,** in the following manner:

The Gemara now turns from the composition of the *zimmun* to the institution of *Birkat Hamazon* itself, tracing each blessing to its historical founder. Rav Nachman states: *Moshe tikken le-Yisrael birkat ha-zan* — Moshe instituted the blessing of *ha-zan et ha-kol* at the time the *man* fell in the wilderness. This makes sense: the *de-oraita* verse, *ve-achalta ve-savata u-veirachta*, commands blessing for food, but the blessing *al ha-aretz ve-al ha-mazon* could not yet be said — they had not yet entered the land. The blessing for sustenance could be said over the *man*, even miraculous sustenance, because the *man* makes us aware that all food — natural and miraculous alike — ultimately comes from God. This is the conceptual foundation of *ha-zan et ha-kol*.

A note on the Sefardic *nusach*: some versions of the first blessing include the phrase *ka-katuv poseach et yadecha u-mashbia le-chol chai ratzon*, a verse from *Tehillim*. The Rashba notes in a *teshuvah* that this is difficult to reconcile with the view that Moshe instituted this blessing, since that verse had not yet been written in Moshe's time. This textual difficulty remains unresolved.

### Yehoshua & Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּוִד תִּקֵּן ״עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל עַמֶּךָ וְעַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם עִירֶךָ״, וּשְׁלֹמֹה תִּקֵּן ״עַל הַבַּיִת הַגָּדוֹל וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **David instituted "…on Israel Your people and on Jerusalem Your city…"** as he conquered the city,  **and Solomon instituted "…on the great and Holy Temple…"** as he was the one who built the Temple.

*Yehoshua tikken lahem birchat ha-aretz* — Yehoshua instituted the blessing of *al ha-aretz ve-al ha-mazon* when the people entered the land of Israel. Only upon entering the land could one thank God specifically for the good land. One can only imagine what that moment must have meant to Yehoshua — the one thing Moshe could not do, Yehoshua was now able to fulfill. A *mitzvah* that had been latent since Sinai was finally able to be performed in its fullness.

### David/Shlomo

This is encompassed in the passage above.

*David u-Shlomo tiknu boneh Yerushalayim* — David and Shlomo together instituted the blessing of *boneh Yerushalayim*. David established the portion mentioning Israel and Jerusalem — *al Yisrael amcha ve-al Yerushalayim irecha* — since he was the one who established Jerusalem as the capital. Shlomo added the reference to *ha-bayit ha-gadol ve-ha-kadosh* — the great and holy house — since it was Shlomo who built the Temple.

### Boneh De'oraita

This raises an immediate question: does *boneh Yerushalayim* not imply a destroyed Jerusalem, as though it were instituted only after the *churban*? The Gemara understands otherwise. Just as *birkat ha-aretz* was a *de-oraita* obligation that could not be performed until there was a land to speak of, so too *boneh Yerushalayim* is a *de-oraita* obligation that could not be expressed until there was a Jerusalem to praise. The verb *boneh* — building — reflects God's ongoing creative involvement in the city and the Temple. Even when Jerusalem stands fully built, God is understood as the one who built it and sustains it, so the blessing is not inherently retrospective or lamenting. It praises God as the builder of Jerusalem in an active, present sense.

### Yavne & Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״ בְּיַבְנֶה תִּקְּנוּהָ כְּנֶגֶד הֲרוּגֵי בֵּיתָר. דְּאָמַר רַב מַתְנָא: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁנִּיתְּנוּ הֲרוּגֵי בֵּיתָר לִקְבוּרָה תִּקְנוּ בְּיַבְנֶה ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״. ״הַטּוֹב״ — שֶׁלֹּא הִסְרִיחוּ, ״וְהַמֵּטִיב״ — שֶׁנִּיתְּנוּ לִקְבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They instituted** the blessing: **Who is good and does good, at Yavne in reference to the slain** Jews **of** the city of **Beitar** at the culmination of the bar Kokheva rebellion. They were ultimately brought to burial after a period during which Hadrian refused to permit their burial. **As Rav Mattana said: On the same day that the slain of Beitar were brought to burial, they instituted** the blessing: **Who is good and does good, at Yavne. Who is good,** thanking God that the corpses **did not decompose** while awaiting burial, **and does good,** thanking God **that they were** ultimately **brought to burial.**

*Ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* was instituted in Yavne, in connection with the victims of Beitar following the Bar Kokhba revolt. Rav Matna states: on the day the slain of Beitar were permitted burial, the sages in Yavne instituted the blessing of *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*. *Ha-tov* — God is good — refers to the fact that the bodies did not decompose; *ha-meitiv* — God does good for others — refers to the granting of burial. In its original form, the blessing was brief: *Baruch Hashem ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*; it was expanded into a full paragraph over time.

### Exile Context

The theological significance of *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* in the context of *Birkat Hamazon* is considerable. Halachically, this blessing is of greater weight than *shehecheyanu* — the latter marks something good for oneself, while the former marks something good for others as well. More broadly, its placement after *boneh Yerushalayim* creates a striking juxtaposition: the benching as a whole thanks God for the good land and for Jerusalem — precisely what the Jewish people in exile do not have. Into this structure of longing and loss, *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* inserts a declaration that even in exile, even when the land is gone and the Temple is destroyed, even when people are being killed, one can still find cause to thank God. The bodies of Beitar's victims were preserved and eventually buried — and from that, a blessing of joy was composed. This is somewhat analogous to *Kaddish*: in the depths of mourning, one proclaims God's greatness. But the orientation here is slightly different — not *tzidduk ha-din*, accepting God's judgment, but rather the active discovery of goodness within a context of exile and loss.

## Benching Order

### Four Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, סֵדֶר בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן כָּךְ הִיא: בְּרָכָה רִאשׁוֹנָה — בִּרְכַּת ״הַזָּן״, שְׁנִיָּה — בִּרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ, שְׁלִישִׁית — ״בּוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״, רְבִיעִית — ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״, וּבְשַׁבָּת — מַתְחִיל בְּנֶחָמָה וּמְסַיֵּים בְּנֶחָמָה, וְאוֹמֵר קְדוּשַּׁת הַיּוֹם בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the blessings of Grace after Meals, the Gemara adds that **the Sages taught** in a *baraita* that **the order of Grace after Meals is as follows: The first blessing is the blessing of: Who feeds** all; **the second is the blessing of the land; the third is: Who builds Jerusalem; and the fourth is: Who is good and does good. On Shabbat one begins** the third blessing **with consolation and ends with consolation and mentions the sanctity of the day** with mention of Shabbat **in the middle.**

The Gemara now presents the formal order of *Birkat Hamazon*: the first blessing is *birkat ha-zan*; the second is *birkat ha-aretz*, i.e., *al ha-aretz ve-al ha-mazon*; the third is *boneh Yerushalayim*; and the fourth is *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*. This is the basic four-part structure of the benching.

### Shabbat Insertion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: רָצָה לְאוֹמְרָהּ בַּנֶּחָמָה — אוֹמְרָהּ, בְּבִרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ — אוֹמְרָהּ, בִּבְרָכָה שֶׁתִּקְּנוּ חֲכָמִים בְּיַבְנֶה — אוֹמְרָהּ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ אוֹמְרָהּ אֶלָּא בַּנֶּחָמָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: If one wishes to recite** the supplement for the sanctity of Shabbat in **the blessing of consolation:** Who builds Jerusalem, **he recites it** there; **in the blessing of the land, he recites it** there; **in the blessing instituted by the Sages at Yavne,** Who is good and does good, **he recites it** there. **And the Rabbis say: He may only recite** the mention of the sanctity of Shabbat **in** the context of **the blessing of consolation.**

On Shabbat, one begins and ends the third blessing with *nechama* and mentions *kedushat ha-yom* — the holiness of Shabbat — in the middle: *matchil be-nechama u-mesayem be-nechama ve-omer kedushat ha-yom be-emtza*. Rashi understands this to mean that the standard text of *boneh Yerushalayim* is retained, with the Shabbat insertion placed within it. The blessing opens with the normal *nusach* — which Rashi identifies as beginning with *Rachem* — and concludes with the standard closing of *boneh Yerushalayim*, with the Shabbat material inserted in the middle.

### Tosafot Variant

Tosafot records an alternative tradition according to which the *nusach* of the third blessing is actually changed on Shabbat. The reading *matchil be-nachamenu ve-chotmin be-nechamat Tziyon ircha u-vinyan Yerushalayim* suggests a different opening formula — *nachamenu*, console us — on the grounds that *Rachem*, in its plain sense, is a petition, and one does not offer petitions on Shabbat. Tosafot ultimately rejects this alternative, noting that *nachem* is equally a request and therefore equally problematic by that logic. But the existence of this tradition attests to genuine diversity in the liturgical *nusach* of the third blessing on Shabbat.

### R. Eliezer Flexibility

Rabbi Eliezer takes a broader view: one may insert the Shabbat mention in any of the later three blessings — in *boneh Yerushalayim*, in *birkat ha-aretz*, or in the blessing the sages of Yavne instituted, i.e., *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*. Notably, he does not permit inserting it in the first blessing, *ha-zan*. This parallels the principle in *Shemoneh Esrei* that modifications and insertions are not made in the first three blessings — which form a fixed, foundational unit — but rather in the middle or later blessings. Even where Rabbi Eliezer permits flexibility, he treats the first blessing as inviolate.

Why is Shabbat mentioned specifically in *boneh Yerushalayim* in the standard practice? One possibility is the thematic connection to the *korbanot* — the *musaf* sacrifices — which are associated with both Jerusalem and the special days of the calendar. But the precise connection remains somewhat open, which may itself explain why Rabbi Eliezer was willing to permit the mention in any of the three later blessings.

The related question of *me'akev* — whether omitting these insertions requires one to repeat the benching — follows a nuanced rule: in *Shemoneh Esrei*, most special insertions are *me'akev*, with the notable exception of *Al ha-Nissim* (though *Ya'aleh ve-Yavo* is debated). In benching, the insertion is generally *me'akev* only when the day itself obligates a meal — as on Shabbat and Yom Tov — which gives the Shabbat insertion its special connection to the meal being blessed.

## Scriptural Sources

### Ha-Zan Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לְבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן מִן הַתּוֹרָה? — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ וְשָׂבָעְתָּ וּבֵרַכְתָּ״ — זוֹ בִּרְכַּת ״הַזָּן״, ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ — זוֹ בִּרְכַּת הַזִּמּוּן, ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ — זוֹ בִּרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ, ״הַטּוֹבָה״ — זוֹ ״בּוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״הָהָר הַטּוֹב הַזֶּה וְהַלְּבָנוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **From where** is it derived that **Grace after Meals is from the Torah? As it is stated: "And you shall eat and be satisfied, and you shall bless** the Lord, your God, for the good land that He has given you" (Deuteronomy 8:10). The Gemara explains: **And you shall bless, that is the blessing of: Who feeds** all. **The Lord, your God, that is the *zimmun* blessing** in which God's name is invoked. **For the land, that is the blessing of the land; good, that is** the blessing: **Who builds Jerusalem, and it also says: "This good mountain and Lebanon"** (Deuteronomy 3:25), which is interpreted homiletically as referring to Jerusalem and the Temple.

The Gemara now proceeds to derive each of the four blessings from Scripture. The *baraita* asks: from where do we know *Birkat Hamazon* from the Torah? The verse is *ve-achalta ve-savata u-veirachta* — you shall eat, be satisfied, and bless. The word *ve-achalta* — eating — corresponds to *birkat ha-zan*. *Et Hashem Elokecha* — God your Lord — corresponds to *birkat ha-zimmun*, the introductory call to bless, since it is in the declaration of God's name that we call others to join in praise, as in *ki shem Hashem ekra havu godel l'Elokeinu*.

It is worth noting the *peshat* emphasis here: the Torah's primary concern, as expressed throughout *Sefer Devarim*, is not simply that one ate and should be grateful for the food, but that one is being brought to a great land — *eretz harim u-vekaot*, a land of hills and valleys — filled with all good things, and should therefore bless God *al ha-aretz ha-tovah*. The thanksgiving for the food is inseparable from the thanksgiving for the land.

### Ha-Aretz Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לָךְ״ — זוֹ ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְאַחֲרָיו, לְפָנָיו מִנַּיִן? — אָמְרַתְּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר: כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׂבֵעַ מְבָרֵךְ, כְּשֶׁהוּא רָעֵב — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **That He gave you, that is: Who is good and does good.** However, **I only have** a Torah source for blessings **after** eating, i.e., Grace after Meals. **From where** is it derived that one is obligated to recite blessings **before** eating? **You said** that it can be derived through **an *a fortiori* inference: When one is satisfied, he** is obligated to **recite a blessing** and thank God for food; **when he is hungry, all the more so** that he should recite a blessing to offer thanks for the food he will eat.

*Al ha-aretz* — on the land — gives rise to *birkat ha-aretz*. This is essentially *peshat*: the Torah itself frames the obligation to bless in terms of gratitude for the land, not merely for the food. The *de-oraita* character of *birkat ha-aretz* is therefore well anchored in the plain meaning of the verse.

### Boneh Source

*Ha-tovah* — the good — gives rise to *boneh Yerushalayim*. What makes the land good? Not only its agricultural bounty, but the presence of the *Shekhinah*, the existence of Jerusalem, and the *Beit ha-Mikdash*. The verse *ha-har ha-tov ha-zeh ve-ha-Levanon* is cited — "this good mountain and the Lebanon" — and the Gemara understands "this good mountain" as a reference to Jerusalem. Thus, thanking God for the goodness of the land necessarily encompasses thanking God for Jerusalem as the locus of divine presence. This is what makes *boneh Yerushalayim* a *de-oraita* obligation — not the loss of Jerusalem, but the recognition that it constitutes the fullness of what makes the land "good."

### Tov Source

The phrase *asher natan lach* — which God has given you — gives rise to *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*. The principle is that for everything God gives us, we acknowledge that God acts with goodness toward us and toward others. One opinion in the Gemara had argued that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is itself *de-oraita* on the basis of this verse, though this view is largely rejected — the consensus of the *rishonim* is that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is *derabbanan*, instituted in Yavne, as established earlier.

This verse, *asher natan lach*, appears in this sugya with considerable versatility: it is used as the source for *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*, for the blessing before eating, and as we shall see, for *Birkat ha-Torah* as well. This breadth reflects a general hermeneutical principle: the act of God's giving — *natan* — implies an obligation of acknowledgment on our part for whatever is given.

The question is sometimes raised whether all this emphasis on the land means that *Birkat Hamazon* might not be *de-oraita* outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. The Gemara, written in Bavel, does not entertain this suggestion. The obligation to bless is treated as universal, and the mention of Jerusalem in the benching is understood as directed toward a future in which Jerusalem will be rebuilt, not as a precondition for the obligation. Just as one acknowledges the land God has given even without living on it, one acknowledges Jerusalem even while in exile.

## De'oraita Debate

### Rabbi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר [אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ]: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ וְשָׂבָעְתָּ וּבֵרַכְתָּ״ — זוֹ בִּרְכַּת הַזָּן, אֲבָל בִּרְכַּת הַזִּמּוּן מִ״גַּדְּלוּ לַה׳ אִתִּי״ נָפְקָא. ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ — זוֹ בִּרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ, ״הַטּוֹבָה״ — זוֹ ״בּוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״הָהָר הַטּוֹב הַזֶּה וְהַלְּבָנוֹן״. ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״ בְּיַבְנֶה תִּקְּנוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is not necessary** to interpret the verse this way; rather, it should be understood in a slightly different manner, as follows: **"And you shall eat and be satisfied, and you shall bless," that is the blessing of: Who feeds** all; **however, the *zimmun* blessing is derived** from the verse: **"Praise God with me** and we will exalt His name together" (Psalms 34:3). He continues: **For the land, that is the blessing of the land. Good, that is** the blessing: **Who builds Jerusalem, and it also says: This good mountain and Lebanon. They instituted** the blessing: **Who is good and does good, at Yavne** and, as such, it has no biblical source.

The Gemara now turns to a *Tannaitic* dispute about which elements of *Birkat Hamazon* are *de-oraita* and which are *derabbanan*. The *baraita* first states the majority position from which *birkat ha-zimmun* and the three blessings are derived together. Rabbi (Rebbi) argues that *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv* is *derabbanan* — instituted in Yavne — and not *de-oraita*, even according to those who tried to anchor it in *asher natan lach*. This is the view accepted by virtually all *rishonim*. Rabbi further contends that *birkat ha-zimmun* should be derived from a different verse — *gadlu l'Hashem iti u-neromemah shemo yachdav* — rather than from *Et Hashem Elokecha* as in the earlier derivation. This may reflect Rabbi's view that the *zimmun* is not itself *de-oraita* — a position that is overwhelmingly the consensus of the *rishonim*, even if certain sources seem to suggest otherwise.

### Before Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא לְאַחֲרָיו, לְפָנָיו מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לָךְ״ — מִשֶּׁנָּתַן לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** However, **I only have** a Torah source for blessings **after** eating, i.e., Grace after Meals. **From where** is it derived that one is obligated to recite blessings **before** eating? **The verse states: That he gave you.** A blessing must be recited over food **from** the moment that God **gave it to you,** not only afterward.

The *baraita* then asks: from the verse *ve-achalta ve-savata u-veirachta*, I know only the obligation to bless *after* eating. What is the source for a blessing *before* eating? The *baraita* proposes a *kal va-chomer*: if one blesses when satisfied, how much more so should one bless when hungry, since the hunger makes one more acutely aware of one's dependence on and appreciation for God's bounty. One might ask whether this *kal va-chomer* is truly persuasive — the experience of satiation is itself the occasion that triggers the gratitude expressed in benching, whereas the blessing before eating is a response to anticipated benefit. But the *baraita* takes the *kal va-chomer* seriously.

Whether this argument establishes the blessing *before* eating as *de-oraita* is debated. The conclusion at the opening of the sixth *perek* of this tractate assumed it to be *derabbanan*. Yet the *Tannaim* here seem genuinely invested in establishing its biblical basis. There may be sources that treat the pre-eating blessing as *de-oraita* and not merely an *asmachta*, even if the weight of the *sugya* moves in the other direction.

### R. Yitzchak Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וּבֵרַךְ אֶת לַחְמְךָ וְאֶת מֵימֶיךָ״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״וּבֵרַךְ״ אֶלָּא ״וּבָרֵךְ״. וְאֵימָתַי קָרוּי ״לֶחֶם״ — קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֹּאכְלֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** That source for the obligation to recite a blessing beforehand **is not necessary, as it says: "And He will bless your bread and your water"** (Exodus 23:25); **do not read: And He will bless [*uveirakh*], rather: And you will bless [*uvareikh*]. And when is it called bread? Before it is eaten.**

Rabbi Yitzchak says one need not resort to a *kal va-chomer*. There is a verse that directly supports a pre-eating blessing: *u-veirach et lachmecha ve-et meimecha* — "and He shall bless your bread and your water." Reading the verse not as a divine promise but as a human obligation — *al tikrei u-veirach* God will bless, *ela u-vareich* you should bless — yields a direct *de-oraita* source for blessing before eating. Furthermore, the word *lechem* applies specifically to bread *before* it is eaten; once consumed, it is no longer called *lechem*. Therefore, the blessing over *lechem* must come before the eating.

### R. Nasan Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״כְּבֹאֲכֶם הָעִיר כֵּן תִּמְצְאוּן אוֹתוֹ בְּטֶרֶם יַעֲלֶה הַבָּמָתָה לֶאֱכוֹל כִּי לֹא יֹאכַל הָעָם עַד בֹּאוֹ כִּי הוּא יְבָרֵךְ הַזֶּבַח אַחֲרֵי כֵן יֹאכְלוּ הַקְּרֻאִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan says:** That source for the obligation to recite a blessing beforehand **is not necessary, as it says** when the maidens told Saul: **"As soon you come into the city, find him right away, before he goes up to the high place to eat; for the people will not eat until he comes, because he will bless the sacrifice; and afterwards all those who are invited will eat;** now go, for you shall find him at this time of day" (I Samuel 9:13). A blessing recited prior to eating is explicitly mentioned in that verse.

Rabbi Nasan goes further afield. He derives the blessing before eating from a verse in the narrative of Shmuel: when young women direct Saul to the city, they mention that *ki hu yevarech ha-zevach acharei chen yochlu ha-kru'im* — Shmuel will bless the sacrifice and only afterward will the invited guests eat. The Gemara understands "bless the sacrifice" as referring to a blessing over the food before eating, not a sacrificial blessing. This derivation shifts the source away from the Five Books entirely, which tends to push it out of the *de-oraita* category — a blessing learned from a narrative in *Nevi'im* is more naturally understood as a *derabbanan* institution with scriptural support, or at most an *asmachta*.

## Women Digression

### Reasons Debated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? לְפִי שֶׁהַנָּשִׁים דַּבְּרָנִיּוֹת הֵן. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: כְּדֵי לְהִסְתַּכֵּל בְּיׇפְיוֹ שֶׁל שָׁאוּל, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִשִּׁכְמוֹ וָמַעְלָה גָּבֹהַּ מִכׇּל הָעָם״. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַלְכוּת נוֹגַעַת בַּחֲבֶרְתָּהּ אֲפִילּוּ כִּמְלֹא נִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** Tangentially, the Gemara asks: **Why** did these maidens go on **so expansively** while speaking to Saul? **It is because women are chatterers. And Shmuel said** a different reason: They spoke expansively **in order to gaze upon Saul's beauty** longer, **as it is written** about him: "An excellent young man; no one among the Israelites was better than he; **he was taller than the people from the shoulders up"** (I Samuel 9:2). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that their expansiveness was initiated by God, because **one sovereignty does not overlap with its counterpart, even** one **hairbreadth.** Saul's coronation was delayed so that Samuel's leadership would not be curtailed.

The mention of the women who spoke to Saul at such length prompts the Gemara to ask: *ve-chol kach lamah* — why did they speak so extensively? Three answers are offered. The first is that *nashim davraniyot hen* — women are chatterers by nature. The Gemara does not leave it there. Shmuel offers a second reason: *ke-dei le-histakel be-yofyo shel Shaul* — they wanted to keep looking at Saul, who was famously handsome. As the verse says, *mi-shichmo va-mala gavoah mi-kol ha-am* — from his shoulders and above he was taller than all the people. So strikingly attractive was Saul that they prolonged the conversation simply for the opportunity to gaze at him.

### R. Yochanan View

Rabbi Yochanan offers a completely different explanation that exonerates the women entirely: *lefi she-ein malchut nogaat be-chavertah afilu ke-melo nima* — one reign cannot overlap with another even by a hairsbreadth. Saul's moment had not yet arrived; Shmuel's authority had not yet concluded. Any meeting between them would have constituted the inauguration of Saul's kingship, and that could not happen before the precise moment ordained for it. The women's extended speech served, whether they knew it or not, to delay the encounter until the appointed time.

## Birkat Ha-Torah

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן. בִּרְכַּת הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, עַל חַיֵּי שָׁעָה מְבָרֵךְ, עַל חַיֵּי עוֹלָם הַבָּא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר נַחְמָנִי תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״עַל הָאָרֶץ הַטּוֹבָה אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לָךְ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶתְּנָה לָךְ אֶת לֻחֹת הָאֶבֶן וְהַתּוֹרָה וְהַמִּצְוָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* with regard to Grace after Meals continues: **I only have** a Torah source for **Grace after Meals. From where** is the obligation to recite **the blessing of the Torah** derived? Several answers are offered: **Rabbi Yishmael said:** It is derived through **an *a fortiori* inference** from Grace after Meals: **Over** food, which is an aspect of **temporal life, one recites a blessing, all the more so** one recites a blessing over the Torah, which is **eternal life. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Naḥmani,** the **student of Rabbi Yishmael, says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: This** *a fortiori* inference **is not necessary,** as this *halakha* can be derived from the same verse from which Grace after Meals is derived, **as it states: "For the good land that He gave [*natan*] you," and below,** with regard to the Torah, **it says: "And I will give [*ve'etna*] you the stone tablets, and the Torah and the mitzva,** which I have written, that you may teach them" (Exodus 24:12). Here, just as giving with regard to the good land requires a blessing, giving with regard to the Torah requires a blessing.

The Gemara transitions from the sources of *Birkat Hamazon* to a related question: *Birkat ha-Torah minayin* — from where do we derive the obligation of *Birkat ha-Torah*? This question belongs naturally in this discussion for two reasons. First, *Birkat ha-Torah* is understood by many *rishonim* to be genuinely *de-oraita* — unlike the blessing before food, which the Gemara ultimately treats as *derabbanan* — making it the other major *de-oraita* blessing alongside *Birkat Hamazon*. Second, the two obligations are intertwined in an earlier sugya at the beginning of the tractate, where the Gemara proposed a *kal va-chomer* in both directions: just as *Birkat ha-Torah* is said before Torah study, so too one should bless before eating; and just as *Birkat Hamazon* is said after eating, so too one should bless after Torah (at public readings). The convergence of these two sets of obligations explains why the Gemara addresses *Birkat ha-Torah* here in the same breath.

### Rav Shmuel KVC

Rav Shmuel argues for a *kal va-chomer*: *ha-chayei sha'ah mevarech, ha-chayei olam ha-ba lo kol she-ken* — if one blesses for sustenance that nourishes only in this world, how much more so should one bless for Torah, which gives one sustenance for the world to come. From an intellectual standpoint, the argument is compelling. From a purely experiential standpoint it is more complex — the immediate sensory pleasure of food is different in kind from the spiritual benefit of Torah. One might also note that this *kal va-chomer*, modeled on *Birkat Hamazon*, would more naturally suggest a blessing *after* learning — since *Birkat Hamazon* itself comes after eating.

### R. Chiya Analogy

Rabbi Chiyya bar Nachmani, a student of Rabbi Yishmael, offers a different derivation in Rabbi Yishmael's name: *al ha-aretz ha-tovah asher natan lach* — God gave you the good land. And elsewhere it says: *va-etna lach et luchot ha-even ha-Torah ve-ha-mitzvah* — I have also given you the tablets of stone, the Torah, and the commandment. Just as we thank God for the land God has given us, so we must thank God for the Torah God has given us. The parallel is drawn not through a *kal va-chomer* but through the shared language of *natan* — giving. This derivation, like the one from *asher natan lach* for *ha-tov ve-ha-meitiv*, works through the principle that any divine gift warrants acknowledgment and blessing. Conceptually it suggests a blessing of thanksgiving — one that might come after the fact — though it is used here as a source for the blessing before Torah study as well.

### R. Meir Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּבָרֵךְ עַל הַטּוֹבָה כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הָרָעָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לְךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, דַּיָּינְךָ, בְּכָל דִּין שֶׁדָּנְךָ, בֵּין מִדָּה טוֹבָה וּבֵין מִדַּת פּוּרְעָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this verse, **Rabbi Meir says: From where** is it derived that **just as one recites a blessing over the good** that befalls him **he recites a blessing over the bad? The verse states: "That the Lord, your God gave you."** "Your God" is a reference to the attribute of divine justice; **your Judge, in whatever judgment that He judges you, whether it is a positive measure of goodness or a measure of calamity.**

Rabbi Meir derives the source from the verse already cited earlier in this tractate in connection with *zimmun*: *ki shem Hashem ekra havu godel l'Elokeinu* — when I proclaim the name of God, ascribe greatness to our God. The proclamation of Torah — which declares God's revelation — demands that those present respond by ascribing greatness to God. This understanding frames *Birkat ha-Torah* primarily as an act of praise, parallel to the praise-dimension of *zimmun*. There is a subtle but important distinction: *Birkat Hamazon* is primarily gratitude — *nodeh lecha* — while the *ki shem Hashem ekra* model is primarily praise — *havu godel*. Both dimensions are present in *Birkat ha-Torah* as we know it, and the *rishonim* generally understand the blessing to be *de-oraita* in the mode of gratitude and praise rather than as a *birkat ha-mitzvah*.

This is supported by a striking liturgical fact: the blessing recited at a public *keriat ha-Torah* does not include *asher kidshanu be-mitzvotav ve-tzivanu* — the *birkat ha-mitzvah* formulation. The public reading blessing is purely a blessing of thanks and praise. This aligns with the conceptualization drawn from *ki shem Hashem ekra* and *asher natan lach* rather than from the *mitzvah*-obligation framework.

## Bad & Good

### Bad Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּבָרֵךְ עַל הַטּוֹבָה כָּךְ מְבָרֵךְ עַל הָרָעָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לְךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, דַּיָּינְךָ, בְּכָל דִּין שֶׁדָּנְךָ, בֵּין מִדָּה טוֹבָה וּבֵין מִדַּת פּוּרְעָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this verse, **Rabbi Meir says: From where** is it derived that **just as one recites a blessing over the good** that befalls him **he recites a blessing over the bad? The verse states: "That the Lord, your God gave you."** "Your God" is a reference to the attribute of divine justice; **your Judge, in whatever judgment that He judges you, whether it is a positive measure of goodness or a measure of calamity.**

The Gemara now shifts to a broader theological principle: *ke-shem she-mevarechin al ha-tovah kach mevarechin al ha-ra* — just as one blesses God for the good, so one blesses God for the bad. Rabbi Nehorai states this principle and asks for its scriptural basis.

### Giving Source

The source is again *asher natan lach Hashem Elokecha* — whatever God has given you. The Gemara reads this expansively: *dayanecha be-chol din she-dancha* — accept God's judgment in whatever way God judges you, *bein midah tovah bein midat puranut* — whether in the mode of goodness or the mode of affliction. Whatever God gives, it is all recognized as coming from God and as part of God's plan.

### Din & Rachamim

This principle finds a beautiful parallel in a teaching of the *Sfat Emet* on *Rosh Hashanah*. He addresses the apparent tension between *Yom ha-Din* — the Day of Judgment — and *middat ha-rachamim* — divine mercy. His insight is that *din* becomes *rachamim* precisely when we present ourselves willingly before God's judgment, when we accept that being in this relationship with a God who cares enough to judge us is itself an expression of love. When one approaches *Yom ha-Din* not with dread but with the recognition that God's attention and care are themselves the expression of a loving relationship, the very act of judgment is transformed into mercy. The parallel to *mevarech al ha-ra* is apparent: seeing even what seems bad as part of God's giving, as *asher natan lach*, is what transforms judgment into relationship, and affliction into something that can itself be blessed.

## Required Themes

### R. Eliezer Reqs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁלֹּא אָמַר ״אֶרֶץ חֶמְדָּה טוֹבָה וּרְחָבָה״ בְּבִרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ, וּמַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד בְּ״בוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״ — לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the formula of Grace after Meals, the Gemara continues: **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: Anyone who did not say: A desirable, good, and spacious land in the blessing of the land, and** who did not mention **the royal house of David in** the blessing: **Who builds Jerusalem, did not fulfill his obligation.**

The Gemara now turns to the required thematic content of each blessing. Rabbi Eliezer states: *kol she-lo amar eretz chemdah tovah u-rechavah be-birkat ha-aretz* — whoever did not say in *birkat ha-aretz* that the land is "beloved, good, and spacious," *u-malchut Beit David be-boneh Yerushalayim* — and whoever did not mention the kingship of the house of David in *boneh Yerushalayim* — *lo yatza yedei chovato*, has not fulfilled his obligation.

This is a striking ruling. The *de-oraita* source — *al ha-aretz ha-tovah* — requires only the mention of the land and its goodness. Yet Rabbi Eliezer insists that the fullness of that goodness must be expressed: the land is *chemdah tovah u-rechavah*, and its ultimate significance includes not merely the Temple but the Davidic kingdom that sanctified Jerusalem. As Rashi notes, it was through David that Jerusalem became holy — *she-al yado niskadsha Yerushalayim* — so the Davidic monarchy is integral to what *boneh Yerushalayim* is praising. The obligation is not merely to mention Jerusalem, but to recognize the full sacred character of what Jerusalem represents, which includes Davidic sovereignty.

### Brit Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַחוּם הַזָּקֵן אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּזְכּוֹר בָּהּ בְּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Naḥum the Elder says: One must mention the covenant** of circumcision **in** the blessing of the land.

Nachum ha-Zaken adds: *she-tzarich she-yizkor bah brit* — one must mention the covenant in *birkat ha-aretz*. The connection between covenant and land is explicit in the Torah: leading up to the commandment of *brit milah*, the verse says *ve-nasati lecha u-le-zaracha acharecha et eretz megurecha et kol eretz Kena'an* — I will give to you and to your descendants all the land of Canaan. The possession of the land is grounded in a *brit*, and that covenantal foundation must be acknowledged when thanking God for the land.

### Torah Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּזְכּוֹר בָּהּ תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: One must mention the Torah in** the blessing of the land.

Rabbi Yosi adds: *tzarich she-yizkor bah Torah* — one must also mention Torah in *birkat ha-aretz*. His reasoning, as elaborated by Rashi, reflects the recurring motif of *Sefer Devarim*: the land is possessed and retained through the observance of *Torah u-mitzvot*. As the verse says, *le-ma'an tichyu u-rivitem u-vastem vi-riashtem et ha-aretz* — so that you may live and increase and come and inherit the land. The *mitzvot* are not peripheral to the land; they are the condition of inhabiting it. Acknowledging the land without acknowledging the Torah would be to miss the essential covenantal structure that makes the land meaningful.

### Brit Milah Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלֵימוֹ אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּקְדִּים בְּרִית לְתוֹרָה, שֶׁזּוֹ נִתְּנָה בְּשָׁלֹשׁ בְּרִיתוֹת, וְזוֹ נִתְּנָה בִּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה בְּרִיתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Pelimu,** one of the last *tanna'im*, **says: He must** make mention of the **covenant** of circumcision **preceding** mention of the **Torah, as this,** the Torah, **was given** to the Jewish people **with three covenants,** **and that,** the covenant of circumcision, **was given with thirteen covenants,** as the word *brit*, covenant, appears thirteen times in the portion dealing with the circumcision of Abraham (Genesis 17:1–14).

Plimo adds a further precision: *tzarich she-yakdim brit le-Torah* — the mention of *brit* must precede the mention of Torah, because the *brit* was given in three covenants, whereas — and here Plimo shifts to *brit milah* specifically — the covenant of circumcision was mentioned thirteen times in the *parasha* of *brit milah*. The repetition of *brit* thirteen times in that *parasha* underscores its primacy; it is therefore essential that *brit* — now understood as *brit milah* — be mentioned before Torah. This observation opens the question, which will become explicit shortly, about how women relate to this required mention of *brit milah* in a blessing they also recite.

### Hodaya Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַבָּא אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר בָּהּ הוֹדָאָה תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba says: One must mention thanks** in the blessing of thanksgiving in Grace after Meals **at the beginning and the end** of the blessing.

Rabbi Abba concludes the list of required themes: *tzarich she-yomar bo hoda'ah techila ve-sof* — one must open and close *birkat ha-aretz* with thanksgiving. This frames the entire blessing as an act of gratitude: *nodeh lecha* at the opening, and *ve-al kulam Hashem Elokeinu anachnu modim lach* at the close. Whatever else the blessing contains — mention of land, covenant, Torah, kingship — it must be held within a structure of thanksgiving from beginning to end.

## Improper Forms

### Min Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַפּוֹחֵת לֹא יִפְחוֹת מֵאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And one who decreases** the number of expressions of thanksgiving **may not decrease** their number **to fewer than one.**

The Gemara establishes a minimum standard: *ve-ha-pocheit lo yifchat me-achat* — if one reduces the content of praise, one must not fall below one positive mention. It is not entirely clear what this "one" refers to — whether to *eretz chemdah tovah u-rechavah* as a unit, or to some other required element. Whatever the referent, the principle is clear: there is a floor below which the content of *birkat ha-aretz* cannot fall.

### Wrong Closing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הַחוֹתֵם ״מַנְחִיל אֲרָצוֹת״ בְּבִרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ, וּ״מוֹשִׁיעַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל״ בְּ״בוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלָיִם״ — הֲרֵי זֶה בּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara added that the conclusions of the blessing of the land and the blessing: Who builds Jerusalem, may also not be changed. **Anyone who concludes the blessing of the land: Who bequeaths lands and** concludes **the blessing: Who builds Jerusalem,** with the formula: **Who redeems Israel, is an ignoramus,** as he thereby corrupts the intention of the blessing.

A serious error in the closing formula (*chatima*) of the blessing is then identified. If in *birkat ha-aretz* one concludes with *manchil aratzot* — He who bequeaths lands (in the plural) — instead of the standard closing referring to *al ha-aretz ve-al ha-mazon*, or if in *boneh Yerushalayim* one concludes with *moshia Yisrael* — He who saves Israel — instead of *boneh Yerushalayim*, this is condemned. The Gemara calls such a person a *bur* — an ignoramus. It is worth noting that the Gemara uses such strong language precisely because people were actually using these formulations — otherwise there would be nothing to censure.

### Exile Variants

These alternative closings can be understood as responses to the difficulty of the *galut* situation. How does one say *al ha-aretz ve-al ha-mazon* while living outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, eating food not grown in the holy land? The formulation *manchil aratzot* — God who distributes lands to all peoples — attempts to universalize the blessing, abstracting away from the specific gift of *Eretz Yisrael* and toward a more general theological claim that all land ultimately comes from God. This is not without grounding: the *Ramban*'s comment at the opening of *Bereishit* about God's ownership of all land and the right to give and take it from nations is a related idea. Similarly, *moshia Yisrael* in place of *boneh Yerushalayim* might reflect a *galut* sensibility that shifts the focus from building Jerusalem — which currently lies in ruins — to the hope of redemption from exile.

The Gemara, however, does not accept either substitution. The specific language of *birkat ha-aretz* and *boneh Yerushalayim* must retain its *Eretz Yisrael* focus and its Jerusalem-centered orientation. Even in exile, the blessing must speak of the specific land and the specific city as they appear in the *pesukim*. To abstract or universalize the closing formula is not a permissible accommodation to exile — it is an erasure of the essential theological content of the blessing.

### R. Elazar Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר בְּרִית וְתוֹרָה בְּבִרְכַּת הָאָרֶץ וּמַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד בְּ״בוֹנֵה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״ — לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And anyone who does not mention covenant and Torah in the blessing of the land and the royal house of David in** the blessing: **Who builds Jerusalem, did not fulfill his obligation.**

The Gemara concludes this section with a *tanna* of great stringency. A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Elazar, in the name of Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha in the name of *Rabbeinu* (likely Rebbe), states: *kol she-lo amar brit ve-Torah be-birkat ha-aretz u-malchut Beit David be-voneh Yerushalayim lo yatza yedei chovato* — whoever did not mention covenant, Torah, and the kingship of the House of David in their respective blessings has not fulfilled their obligation. This formulation consolidates all the required themes — *brit*, *Torah*, and *malchut Beit David* — and declares that their omission constitutes a failure to discharge one's obligation. Whether this refers to a biblical or rabbinic obligation, and what the practical *halacha* is when someone has benched without these elements, will require further examination.
