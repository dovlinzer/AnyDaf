# Menachot 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reish Lakish: Tablets

### Negation as Foundation

The daf begins at the final line of 99a, picking up from the preceding *aggadeta* concerning the *aron*, which taught that the broken *luchot* and the whole *luchot* were carried together — establishing that a *talmid chacham* who forgets his Torah through no fault of his own is not to be treated with disrespect. From this point, Reish Lakish offers a series of related teachings.

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *pe'amim shebitulah shel Torah zo hi yesodah* — there are times when the negation of Torah is precisely the laying of its foundation. The proof text is *asher shibarta* — the verse in which God says to Moshe, "which you broke." The Gemara reads God's response as an expression of approval: *yashar kochacha sheshibarta* — well done that you broke the *luchot*. There was a moment when breaking the *luchot* actually allowed the relationship between God and *Bnei Yisrael* to be preserved and ultimately healed.

### Rashi's Explanation

What exactly does Reish Lakish mean when he says *bitulah zo hi yesodah* — that negating Torah is its foundation? Rashi explains: *shebitulah shel Torah kegon shemevatel Torah lehotza'at hamet ulehachnasat kallah* — one who suspends Torah study in order to perform a *mitzvah* that the entire community should participate in, such as accompanying the dead or bringing a bride to the *chupah*. And this, says Rashi, *zehu yesodah* — is its very foundation, because Torah must ultimately lead to action. One receives credit *ke'ilu yoshev umeyasdah* — as though one were sitting and laying its foundation. The point runs deeper than simply not losing out for *bitul Torah*: in some sense, the very doing of *mitzvot* constitutes a form of *osek baTorah*.

The textual basis in the *pasuk* lies in the tone of *asher shibarta* — God mentions the breaking of the *luchot* matter-of-factly, without anger or rebuke: *shelo amar lei belashon ka'as*. From this one derives that God consented, that the *shechina* agreed with Moshe's action, since he acted for the good — *kivan deniskaven letovah*.

### Broader Interpretations

It is worth noting that the phrase is frequently quoted not as *bitulah zo hi yesodah* but as *bitulah zo hi kiyumah* — its negation is its fulfillment. As a formulation, *kiyumah* works more naturally as an antonym: the opposite of *bitul* is *lekayem*, to fulfill. In the *yeshiva* world the phrase is often invoked to justify stepping away from the *beit midrash* — taking time for rest, exercise, or a short break — on the grounds that *bitulah zo hi kiyumah*: the brief respite allows one to return with renewed energy and thereby sustain the learning.

There is also a more radical reading, one that Rashi is certainly not advancing and which Reish Lakish likely does not intend either: that sometimes actually violating something is necessary for its *kiyum* — the concept the Gemara elsewhere calls *aveirah lishmah*. This is a genuinely dangerous idea and is treated as such, but it does surface in other contexts in the Gemara.

## Fallen Scholars & Forget…

### Honoring Fallen Scholars

Reish Lakish continues with a second teaching in the same spirit as *shivrei luchot*. *Ve'amar Reish Lakish*: *talmid chacham shesarach ein mevazin oto befarhesia* — if a *talmid chacham* has gone off course — *sarach*, like meat that has gone rancid — one does not shame him publicly. The proof text is *vechashalta hayom vechashal gam navi imcha layla* — if you stumble today, and even the *navi*, the person of great religious standing, stumbles at night, then conceal it as the night conceals. The teaching speaks to protecting the dignity of someone who was once a person of great Torah stature. The concern is real, of course, that such a principle can be misused to cover up genuine wrongdoing — *chas veshalom* — but absent matters of *bein adam lechavero*, the principle of protecting people's dignity stands.

### Forgetting Torah

*Ve'amar Reish Lakish*: *kol hamishakeach davar echad mitalmudo over belav* — whoever intentionally causes himself to forget even one thing from his learning violates a negative prohibition. This is distinct from the *talmid chacham* discussed at the end of 99a who loses his Torah *machmat onso* — through no fault of his own, because he could not retain everything. Here we are speaking of one who goes out of his way to forget what he learned.

The proof text is *hishamer lecha ushmor nafshecha me'od pen tishkach et hadevarim* — guard yourself and watch your soul greatly, lest you forget the things you witnessed. In context, the verse refers to the experience of *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, but Reish Lakish reads it as an admonition to retain all of Torah received at that revelation. Intentional forgetting thus constitutes a violation.

### Number of Violations

The Gemara then records a dispute about how many negative prohibitions are violated. The background principle is that of Rav Avin in the name of Rabbi Ila: *kol makom shene'emar hishamer pen ve'al, eino ela lo ta'aseh* — wherever the Torah uses the language "guard yourself," "lest," or "do not," it is the language of a negative prohibition.

Ravina counts two violations: *hishamer lecha* and *pen tishkach* — two distinct negative formulations in the verse. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak goes further and counts three *lavin*, pointing to the three verbs of admonition in the full verse — *hishamer lecha ushmor nafshecha me'od pen tishkach*. The Torah's repetition of warning here is notably emphatic.

### Intent vs. Circumstance

The Gemara asks whether this prohibition might apply even to one who forgets *machmat onso* — through unavoidable circumstances, simply because one cannot retain everything. The verse itself answers: *ufen yasuru milevavecha* — "lest they turn away from your heart" — implies an active removal, a deliberate turning away. *Bimesira milev hakatuv medaber* — the verse speaks of an intentional distancing from the heart.

Rabbi Dostai beRabbi Yannai raises a further case: what if *takfa alav mishnato* — the material proved too difficult for him to master? Rashi explains this as *shelo yachol le'amid girsato* — he simply could not hold the learning, whether due to difficulty or to the sheer volume he was trying to retain. The verse again provides the limit: *rak* — the limiting word — narrows the prohibition to cases of genuine intention to forget. Both the case of one who forgot because he could not remember everything and the case of one for whom the material was beyond his capacity are exempted from the prohibition.

## Forty Days Teaching

### Torah & Soul Formation

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar together state: *Torah nitna be'arba'im veneshama notzra be'arba'im* — the Torah was given over forty days, referring to *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, and the soul takes form over forty days. This reflects the well-known teaching of *Chazal* that for the first forty days of gestation a fetus is regarded as *maya be'alma* — like water — and only on day forty does it begin to take the shape of a human being, *tzurat havlad*, and this is also when *yetzirat haneshama* — the formation of the soul — occurs. The parallel between Torah and soul, both formed over forty days, sets up what follows.

### Protecting Torah & Soul

*Kol hameshamer toraso, nishmaso mishtameret* — whoever protects his Torah, his soul is protected. The act of *shemirat Torah* — actively guarding what one has learned, through review, through mnemonics, through disciplined effort — is placed in direct parallel with the protection of one's soul. And conversely: *ve'chol she'eino meshamer et hatorah, ein nishmaso mishtameret* — one who does not guard his Torah does not guard his soul. There is clearly also a deeper sense here: the more diligent one is in Torah, the more one protects one's religious wellbeing, and neglect exposes one to spiritual danger.

### Bird Parable

The school of Rabbi Yishmael offers a *mashal*: *mashal le'adam shemasar tzippor dror le'avdo* — it is like a person who gave a wild bird to his servant. The master says to the servant: if you lose it, do not think you will merely pay the price of a bird — *nishmascha ani notel mimcha* — I will take your life. The image is apt: a wild bird that wants to fly free, that you must actively hold and restrain. Torah is like that bird — it strains to escape the mind and must be actively protected. Lose it, and you pay not with the cost of a pet but with something far more fundamental.

## Two Tables Setup

### Marble & Gold Tables

The next *mishnah* opens: *shnei shulchanos hayu ba'ulam* — there were two tables in the *ulam*, the antechamber that preceded the *heichal* and then the *Kodesh Kodashim*. Unlike the *Mishkan*, which had no *ulam*, the *Beis Hamikdash* had a full structure of *ulam*, *heichal*, and *dvir*. The *heichal* was where the *shulchan*, *menorah*, and *mizbe'ach hazahav* stood; the *ulam* was the outer chamber.

The two tables were *mibifnim al pesach habayis* — on the inner side, at the rear of the *ulam* near the opening to the *heichal*: *echad shel shayish ve'echad shel zahav* — one of marble and one of gold. Tosafot notes that in many manuscripts the reading is *echad shel kesef* — one of silver — and attempts to reconcile the two versions, while Rashi suggests that marble and silver have a similar appearance. The arrangement was as follows: on the marble table they placed the *lechem hapanim* upon its entry — when the priests brought the new bread in on *Shabbat* to place it in the *heichal*. On the gold table they placed the bread upon its exit — when the old bread was removed and set aside in the *ulam* while the *levonah* was being burned on the outer *mizbe'ach* before the *kohanim* would eat it.

### Ascending Holiness

The principle governing this arrangement is *ma'alin bakodesh velo moridin* — one ascends in sanctity and does not descend. Rashi notes that there was no strict halakhic requirement to set the bread on the marble table on the way in, but it was done in order to embody this principle: by passing through an intermediate stage of marble or silver before reaching the gold *shulchan* in the *heichal*, the entire process was given more layers of ritual and reverence. And on the way out, since the bread was already associated with the gold *shulchan* inside, it could not be placed on something of lesser sanctity — yet it also could not go higher than gold. The solution: it remains on a gold table. One does not descend; one may stay level.

### Gold Table & Showbread

*Ve'echad shel zahav mibifnim* — and then of course there was the gold table inside the *heichal* itself, *she'alav lechem hapanim tamid* — upon which the *lechem hapanim* rested constantly. This is the central *shulchan* of the Temple service.

## Showbread Ceremony

### Four Priests Enter

*Ve'arba'ah kohanim nichnasim* — four *kohanim* would enter: *shnayim beyadam shnei sdarim* — two of them holding the two columns of bread, six loaves stacked upon each other in a column, and *shnayim beyadam shnei bazichin* — two holding the two dishes of *levonah*. In terms of the workload, those carrying the bread carried something considerably bulkier and more difficult to manage; those carrying the *bazichin* had a far simpler task.

### Four Priests Precede

*Ve'arba makdimim lifnehem* — four additional *kohanim* would go in ahead of them, whose role was to remove the existing bread and *levonah* as the new contingent placed the new ones. Two were assigned to take out the two columns of bread and two to take out the two dishes of *levonah*.

### Positioning & Coordinati…

The spatial choreography was precise. *Hamachnisim omdim batzafon upnehem ladarom* — those bringing the new bread stood in the north, facing south. Since the *shulchan* was itself positioned in the north, these four were positioned between the northern wall and the *shulchan*, facing inward toward the table. *Hamotzim omdim badarom upnehem batzafon* — those removing the old bread stood to the south, in the more open space of the *heichal*, facing toward the *shulchan*.

*Elu moshchin ve'elu manichim* — the ones on the outside pull away the old bread while those between the table and the wall place the new bread into the cleared spaces. *Vetfacho shel ze keneged tfacho shel ze* — one *tefach* is directly opposite the other *tefach*, meaning the movements are so closely coordinated that the hands of the two groups are at virtually the same distance from the table at every moment.

### Continuous Presence Deba…

This choreography is driven by the requirement of *lefanai tamid* — constantly before Me. For the *Tanna Kamma*, *tamid* means there cannot be even a single moment when the table holds no bread. The simultaneous pulling-away and placing-on, with hands within a *tefach* of one another, ensures that at no instant is the *shulchan* left bare.

*Rabbi Yose omer*: even if the old bread is removed before the new is placed — *afilu elu notlin ve'elu manichim* — it still constitutes *tamid*. The Gemara will elaborate on how far Rabbi Yose extends this position.

### Transfer & Division

After the ceremony, *yatzu venatnu al shulchan hazahav* — those who removed the old bread carried it out to the *ulam* and placed it on the gold table mentioned at the outset of the *mishnah*. Then *hiktiru habezichim* — the two *kohanim* who had taken the *bazichin* burned the *levonah* on the outer *mizbe'ach*. Only once the *levonah* had been offered were the *chalot* divided among the *kohanim*.

## Yom Kippur on Shabbat

### Timing Issues

*Chal Yom HaKippurim lihyot beShabbat* — if Yom Kippur fell on *Shabbat*, the priests could not divide and eat the bread on *Shabbat* itself. *Hachalot mitchalkot la'erev* — the *chalot* would be divided and eaten *Motzei Shabbat*, which is also *Motzei Yom Kippur*. This does not constitute *linah* — overnight disqualification — since *linah* does not apply until the following morning.

If Yom Kippur fell on *erev Shabbat*, a further complexity arose: *se'iro shel Yom HaKippurim ne'echal la'erev* — the goat offered as *chatat* on the outer *mizbe'ach* on Yom Kippur — not the *se'ir la'azazel* sent to the wilderness, nor the goat whose blood was sprinkled inside, but the ordinary external *chatat* whose meat was to be eaten by the *kohanim* — would need to be eaten that night, *Motzei Yom Kippur*, i.e., Friday night, to avoid it becoming *notar*. But it went directly from Yom Kippur into *Shabbat* with no opportunity to roast or cook it.

### Babylonian Priests

*Vehavliyim ochlim oto keshehu chai* — and the Babylonian priests would eat it raw. *Mipnei sheda'atan yafa* — because, the *mishnah* says euphemistically, "they have a refined temperament." This is clearly ironic: they were willing to eat raw meat, which is in fact an expression of coarseness. The Gemara will shortly identify who these "Babylonians" actually were.

## Rabbi Yose's View

### Morning Removal/Evening…

The Gemara now examines Rabbi Yose's position. A *baraita* states: *Rabbi Yose omer afilu silek et hayashan shacharit vesider et hachadasha aravit, ein bakach klum* — even if the old bread was removed in the morning and the new bread was not arranged until the evening, it is not a problem at all — not even *bedi'avad*. For Rabbi Yose, the requirement of *tamid* is not violated by a daytime gap.

---

## Rabbi Yose's View (II)

### Reinterpretation of Tamid

*Ela mah ani mekayem lefanai tamid* — how then does Rabbi Yose interpret the word *tamid* — "constantly"? *Shelo yalin shulchan belo lachem* — the table should not pass the night without bread. What is prohibited is an overnight gap, not a momentary or even a lengthy daytime absence. The word *tamid*, for Rabbi Yose, means something like continuity across days — no night should pass without bread having been on the table — rather than an unbroken moment-by-moment presence.

## Rabbi Ami Derivation

### One Chapter AM/PM

From Rabbi Yose's position, Rabbi Ami draws a halakhic lesson for Torah study. *Afilu shana adam ela perek echad shacharit u'perek echad aravit* — even if a person studied only one chapter of Torah in the morning and one chapter in the evening, *kiyam mitzvat lo yamush sefer haTorah hazeh mipicha* — he has fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *lo yamush sefer haTorah hazeh mipicha*, the commandment that this *sefer Torah* shall not depart from your mouth. The verse continues *vehagita bo yomam valailah* — you shall meditate in it day and night. Just as Rabbi Yose holds that *tamid* is satisfied by continuity without an overnight gap rather than by every-moment presence, so too *yomam valailah* is satisfied by some learning in the day and some in the night. There is always something of one's day that is Torah — and that continuous thread is enough.

## Kriyas Shema Fulfillment

### Shema AM/PM

Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai goes a step further: *afilu lo kara adam ela kriyat shema shacharit ve'arvit, kiyam lo yamush* — even if a person only recited *kriyat shema* morning and evening, he has fulfilled *lo yamush*. This is not as surprising as it might initially appear. The *psukim* of *shema* are themselves *psukim* of Torah; the *bracha* of *ahavat olam* that precedes *shema* functions as *birchat haTorah*; and the text of *shema* itself — *velimadetem otam et beneichem vedibarta bam beshivtecha beveitecha*, *beshachbecha uvekumecha* — speaks directly about the constant engagement with Torah, about *talmud Torah* as a way of life, from lying down to rising up. When one recites *kriyat shema* morning and evening, fulfilling *beshachbecha uvekumecha*, one is also fulfilling a sense of *yomam valailah* — there is Torah at the start of one's day and Torah at the close.

### Yochanan vs. Rava

However, Rabbi Yochanan adds: *vedavar zeh assur l'omro bifnei amei ha'aretz* — this teaching should not be said in front of the *amei ha'aretz*. If an *am ha'aretz* — someone not oriented toward serious Torah study — hears that *kriyat shema* alone fulfills *lo yamush*, he will simply conclude that there is nothing more required of him and will use it as an easy exemption. The teaching is meaningful only for *talmidei chachamim* who already learn extensively and for whom it is merely an interesting theoretical point; for those who would be affected by it in practice, it is better left unsaid.

Rava disagrees: *mitzvah l'omro bifnei amei ha'aretz* — it is a *mitzvah* to say it to the *amei ha'aretz*. Hearing this, they will feel valued, feel that their Torah matters, and may well be inspired to do more. Rashi elaborates: if even *kriyat shema* earns such extraordinary reward — the verse concludes *ki az tatzliach et derachecha*, then you will be greatly successful — how much more so if one were to learn all day? The generosity of the reward should serve as an enticement toward greater learning, not as a ceiling. This is a genuine pedagogical dispute: does it serve people better to tell them what they have already accomplished, or to communicate the full weight of what remains?

## Greek Wisdom Prohibition

### Ben Dama's Question

Ben Dama, the nephew of Rabbi Yishmael, posed a question to his uncle: *kegon ani shelamadti kol haTorah kullah, mahu lilmod chochma yevanit* — someone like me, who has learned the entire Torah — can he now learn Greek wisdom?

### No Time for Other Studies

Rabbi Yishmael responded by citing the very verse under discussion: *lo yamush sefer haTorah hazeh mipicha vehagita bo yomam valailah* — this sefer Torah shall not depart from your mouth, and you shall study it day and night. *Tze u'vedok sha'ah she'eina lo min hayom velo min halaylah u'lemad bah chochmat Yevanit* — go find a time that is neither day nor night, and in that time you may study Greek wisdom. For Rabbi Yishmael, the verse addressed to a *talmid chacham* cannot be read in the lenient manner of Rabbi Ami or Rabbi Yochanan. For someone at Ben Dama's level, *yomam valailah* means what it says: every moment of day and every moment of night belongs to Torah. If you can identify a moment that is neither, that is when you may learn something else. Since no such moment exists, there is simply no room.

This raises the question of what *chochmat Yevanit* actually means. The attractive reading is Greek philosophy or secular studies more broadly. There is, however, a narrower and more historically specific interpretation: the Gemara elsewhere records a *gezeirah* that one may not teach one's son *chochmat Yevanit*, and in that context it may refer to something more specifically dangerous — perhaps a form of secret code language or a set of ideas particularly associated with Hellenistic religious subversion — rather than general secular learning. The *mishnah* in context probably points to that more specific danger, though the verse Rabbi Yishmael cites has been applied broadly across the tradition.

## Mitzvah or Blessing?

### Blessing Not Obligation

*U'fliga d'Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* — this entire discussion of whether *lo yamush* imposes a strict obligation or can be satisfied minimally is challenged by Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani, who says in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *pasuk zeh eino lo chovah velo mitzvah ela brachah* — this verse is neither a *chovah* nor a *mitzvah* but a blessing. This is probably the *pshat* of the verse. The setting is the beginning of *Sefer Yehoshua*: after Moshe's death, God speaks to Yehoshua. Rather than imposing an obligation, God is blessing him.

### Divine Enticement

Why? *Ra'ah haKadosh Baruch Hu et Yehoshua shedivrei Torah chavivin alav beyoter* — God saw that Torah was extraordinarily dear to Yehoshua. The proof: *umesharto Yehoshua bin Nun naar lo yamish mitoch ha'ohel* — his servant Yehoshua son of Nun, a young man, never departed from the tent, understood as the tent of *talmud Torah*. *Amar lo haKadosh Baruch Hu*: Is Torah so beloved to you, that you are always reaching for it — *kol kach chavivin alecha divrei Torah*? Then I bless you: *lo yamush sefer haTorah hazeh mipicha* — may this Torah never depart from your mouth.

### Not a Burden

The school of Rabbi Yishmael offers a formulation that attempts to hold both dimensions in tension: *divrei Torah lo yihiyu alecha chovah, ve'ee atah rasha'ee liftor atzmecha mehen* — the words of Torah should not be an obligation upon you, yet you are not permitted to exempt yourself from them.

### Torah & Worldly Life

Rashi reads this as a single coherent statement: don't treat Torah like a debt — the attitude of *matai efra'enu ve'epater*, "when can I discharge this and be done?" — because you cannot discharge it and be done. You are never exempt; the study must always continue. For Rashi, both clauses say the same thing from different angles: don't treat it as a burden you can satisfy.

Tosafot reads the two clauses differently. *Lo yihiyu alecha chovah shelo ta'asok ela bahen* — do not make it so all-encompassing an obligation that you do nothing but Torah. *Ve'ee atah rasha'ee liftor atzmecha mehen* — but you cannot exempt yourself from them entirely either, *shelo ta'asok bahen klal*. The conclusion: *ela yafeh talmud Torah im derech eretz* — Torah study combined with worldly occupation is the ideal. Tosafot also offers an additional reading: *lo alecha hamelacha ligmor* — you are not obligated to complete all of the Torah — echoing the teaching in Avot — but *ee atah rasha'ee liftor atzmecha*, you cannot opt out. Do what you can, but you cannot choose nothing.

The reading perhaps most aligned with the spirit of the formulation is: do not approach Torah as a burden. It is an obligation, yes, but the attitude should not be one of discharging a debt. Rather, treat it as an opportunity — an approach reminiscent of what Rav Soloveitchik articulated about *tefillah*: *tefillah* is indeed *keva*, it is fixed and obligatory, yet one must not *approach* it as *keva* — as a rote obligation to discharge. One should approach it as *tachanunim lifnei hamakom*, as pouring out the heart before God. The same reframing applies here: Torah study is obligatory, but the disposition toward it should be one of opportunity, not burden.

## Gehinnom Midrash

### Human vs. Divine

Rav Chizkiya expounds the verse *ve'af hesitcha mipi tzar rachav lo mutzak tachteiha* — "He has enticed you from a narrow place, wide and unconfined beneath." *Shelo kemidat haKadosh Baruch Hu midat basar vadam* — the ways of God are not like the ways of flesh and blood. *Adam mesit et chaveiro midarchei chayim ledarchei mita* — when a human being seduces his fellow, he leads him from the path of life toward the path of death, away from the straight and narrow toward destruction. *Vehakadosh Baruch Hu mesit et ha'adam midarchei mita ledarchei chayim* — but when God seduces and entices a person, He draws him away from the path of death toward the path of life. *Ve'af hesitcha mipi tzar* — God entices you from the narrow place, which is *Gehenom*, *shepiha tzar* — whose opening is narrow.

### Gehinnom Description

The image of *Gehenom* presented here is striking: its entrance is narrow but its interior is deep and wide. *She'ashana tzavur betocha* — its smoke is all contained within it, which is why the narrow opening gives no sense of the vastness inside. The Gemara anticipates the objection: perhaps since the opening is narrow, the interior too is small and constrained — *keshem shepiha tzar kach kula tzara*. The verse answers: *he'emik hirchiv* — it is made deep and wide. The opening is deceiving; within, there is vast space for fire and punishment.

### Gehinnom Assurances

The *midrash* continues with a series of reassurances — or warnings, depending on how one reads them. *Veshema tomar lemelech lo hukan* — perhaps you will say it is not prepared for a king, meaning a *talmid chacham* (as Rashi explains) — perhaps someone of Torah stature is exempt. The verse answers: *gam hi lemelech hukan* — it is prepared even for a king. *Veshema tomar ein ba eitzim* — perhaps you will say there is no wood there, that the fire is not sustained. The verse answers: *medurata esh ve'eitzim harbeh* — its conflagration is fire and abundant wood. And lest one think the entire reward of Torah is merely escape from *Gehenom*: *venachat shulchancha male dhashen* — your table will be set full of richness. The ultimate reward of Torah is not merely the avoidance of punishment but the abundance of life and blessing.

## Babylonians vs. Alexandr…

### True Identity Revealed

Returning to the *mishnah*'s reference to the "Babylonian priests" who ate the *se'ir* raw, the Gemara provides a clarification. *Amar Rabbah bar Bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *lo Bavliyim hem, ella Alexandriiyim hem* — they were not actually Babylonians but Alexandrians, Greeks from Alexandria. *Umitoch shesonim et haBavliyim korin otam al shem Bavliyim* — and because the sages of Israel had such antipathy toward "the Babylonians," when they wanted to describe someone as coarse and barbaric in their eating habits, they reached for the term "Babylonian" as a slur. A *baraita* confirms this: Rabbi Yose states the same — they were Alexandrians, called Babylonians as a term of derision.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Relief

*Amar lo Rabbi Yehuda: tanuach da'atcha shenachata et da'ati* — Rabbi Yehuda said to him: may your mind be at ease, for you have set my mind at ease. The reason, as Rashi explains, is that Rabbi Yehuda's own family was from Babylonia — *mishpachat Rabbi Yehuda miBavel hayeta* — and he was therefore relieved to learn that this attribution of coarse behavior to "Babylonians" was not an actual slur against Babylonian Jews but rather the use of "Babylonian" as a byword for something else entirely.

It is worth noting the somewhat ironic undertone: that the sages of *Eretz Yisrael* used "Babylonian" as a curse word reflects a degree of inter-communal tension. One possible reading that softens this is that *haBavliyim* referred not to the Jewish community of Bavel but to native Babylonians — the gentile population — and that the *kohanim* of Alexandria were being compared, unfavorably, to them. That reading would make the dynamic less internally contentious, though the usage remains a pointed one.
