# Bava Metzia 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Price Protection

### Forward Purchase Rules

The daf opens mid-discussion of the *mishnah*'s ruling on *posek imo k'sha'ar hagavo'ah* — the practice of forward purchasing. When someone pays money upfront for produce to be delivered later in the year, at a time when prices are typically higher, the seller must either own the produce or it must be readily available in the market. The *mishnah* permits the purchaser to stipulate price protection: if, say, a hundred bushels are bought at ten dollars per bushel and the price later drops, delivery can be made at the lower price. This is the position of the *tanna kamma*. Rabbi Yehuda goes further, and Rabbi Yossi holds that even without an explicit stipulation the purchaser retains this right.

### Rav Papa's Decision

The *gemara* opens with a case: a certain man advanced money to purchase jewelry (*nidunya*) on behalf of his father-in-law's household, and by the time delivery was due the price had fallen — meaning he could have received more for his money. The matter came before Rav Papa, who ruled: if you stipulated at the time of payment that you would always receive the benefit of the lowest price, then take delivery at the current lower price. But if you made no such stipulation, you take delivery at the originally agreed price. This follows the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*. Critically, throughout this discussion we are dealing with a situation where money was paid but no *meshichah* — no act of formal acquisition — was performed. Since *meshichah* had not been done, the sale is not yet final, which is the entire framework within which these stipulations operate.

### Backing Out Rights

The rabbinical students challenged Rav Papa: if no stipulation was made, why must the purchaser accept the higher price? Since money alone does not finalize a sale (*ma'os lo kanu*), could the purchaser simply back out, demand his money returned, and repurchase at the now-lower price? Rav Papa replied that this question misses the point of his ruling. Of course the purchaser may back out — but doing so without a stipulation exposes him to a *mi shepara*, the formal curse for reneging on an agreement. His ruling was precisely about how to avoid that curse: if the seller stipulated price protection and the seller subsequently wishes to back out, the seller receives the *mi shepara*; if the purchaser made no such stipulation and now wishes to back out because the price has dropped, it is the purchaser who receives the *mi shepara*. The options exist, but they carry consequences.

### Ravina's Position

Ravina then raised a broader theoretical challenge, attempting to reframe the entire structure of the *mishnah*. He argued that the strict position in the *mishnah* — requiring an explicit stipulation — might in fact represent the view of Rabbi Shimon, while the more lenient position of Rabbi Yehuda might actually represent the *chachamim* who disagree with Rabbi Shimon.

To understand this argument, one must grasp the underlying debate. Rabbi Shimon holds that the purchaser generally cannot back out, even to accept a *mi shepara*. The *chachamim* hold that either party may back out and receive a *mi shepara*. Ravina's proposal: the position requiring explicit stipulation is Rabbi Shimon's, since in his view the purchaser normally has no right to back out at all — and therefore the only mechanism by which the purchaser may exit when prices change is through an explicit prior stipulation. For the *chachamim*, however, who routinely permit the purchaser to back out (albeit with a *mi shepara*), a change in market price represents a natural and obvious reason to exit — so obvious, Ravina argues, that the purchaser's intent to have done so should be presumed (*da'atya d'inish a'tara zeila*, people always assume the lesser value). On this reading, the *chachamim* would hold like Rabbi Yehuda of the *mishnah*: when prices change, the purchaser may withdraw without any *mi shepara*, even absent an explicit stipulation. This would make Rabbi Yehuda's position not a lone minority view but the mainstream halachic position of the *chachamim*.

### Rav Papa Responds

Rav Papa rejected this construction. His response: even Rabbi Shimon would permit the purchaser to back out when the market price has changed, though with a *mi shepara*. His proof: the relevant *baraita* articulating Rabbi Shimon's position explicitly invokes the *mi shepara* curse, and the language there — *mikol makom*, nevertheless — includes both seller and purchaser. This can only make sense if Rabbi Shimon envisions a scenario where the purchaser does back out and receives a *mi shepara*. Were Rabbi Shimon's position an absolute bar on the purchaser backing out under any circumstances, there would be no occasion to speak of the purchaser receiving a *mi shepara* at all. The *gemara* attempts to deflect this by suggesting that perhaps for Rabbi Shimon no such scenario ever exists for the purchaser, but Rav Papa insists the *baraita*'s language forecloses that reading.

The conclusion is therefore: Rabbi Shimon allows the purchaser to back out when prices change, but only with a *mi shepara* — and the requirement to stipulate explicitly arises because even in a changed-price scenario, one still incurs a *mi shepara* without such a stipulation. Rav Papa then extends this logic to the *chachamim*: even though they always permit the purchaser to back out, even they would agree that a price change does not automatically exempt the purchaser from a *mi shepara* unless there was an explicit stipulation. He therefore maintains that one must pasken like the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*, requiring an explicit stipulation, and does not accept Ravina's attempted reconciliation.

### Shlichut Argument

A final challenge came from Rav Acha the son of Rava, addressed to Rav Ashi. Setting aside the entire *mi shepara* discussion, he raised an agency (*shlichut*) argument: the father-in-law had appointed his son-in-law as his agent (*shaliach*) to make the purchase. Since the son-in-law failed to stipulate price protection — acting imprudently — could the father-in-law simply declare the entire transaction void on the grounds that he appointed an agent to benefit him, not to harm him, and that a *shaliach* who exceeds or betrays his mandate is not acting as a valid agent at all? Rav Ashi replied: the case involves a merchant who buys and sells on his own account (*tagra d'zavin u'mazvin*) — the son-in-law was purchasing from the sellers with his own money and then reselling to his father-in-law, so the *shlichut* framework cannot be invoked to nullify the transaction. The upshot of the entire passage is that we pasken like the *chachamim* of the *mishnah*, requiring explicit stipulation.

## Sharecropper Loans

### Seed vs. Grain

The next *mishnah* addresses lending seed to sharecroppers (*malveh arisin*). A landowner may lend his sharecroppers grain for planting (*lezera*) and later receive back an equal quantity at harvest time. However, he may not lend them grain for consumption (*le'echol*) to be repaid in kind later, because that would constitute *se'ah be-se'ah* — lending a measure of food to be returned measure for measure — which is a form of rabbinic *ribbit*. The concern is that by the time of repayment the grain may have risen in value, so the borrower would effectively be returning something worth more than what he received.

### Rabban Gamliel

The *mishnah* notes that Rabban Gamliel would lend his sharecroppers seed for planting, and if the price changed in either direction by the time of repayment, he always accepted the lesser value. If the price rose, he accepted only enough grain to equal the original value; if the price fell, he accepted the lower-value grain even though it represented a loss. The *mishnah* clarifies that this was not because *halacha* required it — lending seed and expecting back equal quantity is in fact permitted — but because Rabban Gamliel chose to be strict upon himself. The point of the *mishnah* is precisely that one should not derive a normative ruling from Rabban Gamliel's personal stringency.

### Before/After Work

A *baraita* introduces a distinction absent from the *mishnah*: lending seed and expecting repayment in kind is permitted only before the sharecropper has gone down and begun working the field (*shelo yarad*), but once he has commenced work (*aval yarad*), it is forbidden. The *gemara* asks: why does the *mishnah* draw no such distinction, while the *baraita* does?

### Renegotiation Logic

Rav Idi explained the discrepancy to Rava as follows. In the locale of the *mishnah*'s author, the practice was for the sharecropper to supply the seed. Since the sharecropper bears responsibility for the seed but lacks it, the landowner retains the right to eject him from the arrangement at any time — whether he has begun working or not — simply on the grounds that the sharecropper has failed to fulfill his obligation. This right of ejection gives the landowner perpetual leverage to renegotiate the terms. When the landowner provides seed under these conditions, it is not a loan but a renegotiated term of the sharecropping agreement: the sharecropper will provide his customary share of the harvest plus one additional measure of seed in lieu of what he owed. This is not *se'ah be-se'ah* because the seed is part of the compensation structure, not a loan.

In the locale of the *baraita*, by contrast, it is the landowner who is obligated to provide the seed. Before work begins, the landowner can still rescind the arrangement and offer revised terms — the provision of extra seed becomes a condition of the new agreement rather than a loan. But once the sharecropper has begun working, the landowner loses the right of ejection. At that point, if the landowner provides additional seed and demands its return at harvest, that is a genuine loan of food repayable in kind, and it does constitute prohibited *se'ah be-se'ah*.

## Se'ah Be-Se'ah

### Price Fixing

The *mishnah* teaches that one may say to a friend: lend me a measure of wheat and we will fix the price at the current rate. The mechanism is straightforward: instead of creating an obligation to return grain — which may be worth more at repayment — the obligation is converted into a monetary debt. You lend me a bushel of wheat worth ten dollars; I owe you ten dollars.

### Price Mechanics

The *gemara* raises a difficulty: if the price was fixed and then later falls, why should the borrower be permitted to repay in grain rather than in the fixed monetary amount? Rav Sheshet clarifies that the *baraita* is not addressing the case where a price was fixed. Rather, it addresses the case where no price was fixed at all. If someone says simply "lend me a bag of wheat and I will repay a bag of wheat" — which is technically *se'ah be-se'ah* — how does one avoid the *ribbit* problem at the moment of repayment? The answer: if prices have fallen or remained the same, repaying in kind is permissible because the returned grain is worth no more than what was borrowed; if prices have risen, one must repay the monetary value the grain held at the time of the loan, not a full bag of grain.

### Harvest Return Ban

The *mishnah* further rules that one may not say: lend me a measure of wheat and I will repay you after the threshing season. This too is *se'ah be-se'ah*, since by harvest time the grain may have risen in value. However, the *mishnah* permits one to say: lend me this wheat and as soon as my son returns and opens the storehouse — or as soon as I find the key — I will repay you immediately. This is analogous to the rule on forward purchases: if the item is in one's possession or readily available, the transaction is permissible even if literal repayment occurs slightly later.

### Hillel's Strictness

Hillel, however, would forbid even this. He also extends the concern to the domestic sphere: a woman should not lend her neighbor even a loaf of bread or a small domestic quantity without first fixing a price, lest the price of flour rise and a seemingly neighborly exchange become a form of rabbinic *ribbit*. Hillel's stringency thus operates on two axes: he disallows even deferred repayment when the item is merely accessible but not immediately at hand, and he extends *se'ah be-se'ah* concerns to small domestic loans. It is worth noting the irony that Hillel — normally the lenient authority — adopts the stricter position here, even as he was also the author of the *prosbul*, another institution touching on the law of loans.

### Ownership Requirement

The *mishnah*'s permission to borrow when one already possesses the item raises the question of how much one must own. Rav Huna holds: one may borrow only up to the quantity one possesses. If you have one bag stored away, you may borrow one bag; if you have two, you may borrow two; but you may not borrow more than what you currently own.

Rabbi Yitzchak disagrees: even if you own only a single measure, you may borrow as many large quantities as you wish. His logic is that since *se'ah be-se'ah* is in any case rabbinic (*ribbit derabanan*), the requirement of ownership operates pro forma — a token amount suffices. Conceptually, one can also break the borrowing into serial transactions: you own one measure, you borrow one more (permissible), now you own two, you borrow two more, and so on. Though admittedly a fiction, it provides the halachic basis for the leniency. A *baraita* of Rav Chiya is cited in support of Rabbi Yitzchak: the *baraita* rules that one may not borrow wine if one has not a drop of wine, nor oil if one has not a drop of oil — the implication being that if one possesses even a single drop, one may borrow as much as one wishes.

### Chachamim's Leniency

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: this is Hillel's position, but the *chachamim* say one may simply borrow and repay without such formalities (*lovin stam u'pormin stam*). In practice, this leniency applies to domestic contexts where the exchanges are not commercial in character, parties are not exacting about precise quantities, and there is an implicit mutual tolerance for slight variations. Because no one is truly *makpid* — strictly insistent — the exchange lacks the character of a commercial loan, and the rabbinic *ribbit* concern does not apply.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel adds a further warning: members of a *chavurah* who take turns hosting one another but are very precise in accounting for every cost and every dish will transgress the prohibitions against measuring on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, and also the prohibition against lending and borrowing on *Yom Tov* — since their exacting accounting turns hospitality into a transaction. From Hillel's perspective, such a group also transgresses *ribbit*, since the precision of their accounting triggers even domestic *se'ah be-se'ah* concerns. Notably, the *gemara* implies that even the *chachamim* might not be concerned about *se'ah be-se'ah* for a group that is *makpid*, which is striking.

## Scholar Loans

### Scholar Gifts

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel teaches: *talmidei chachamim* are permitted to lend to one another and to return a little extra. The rationale offered is that they know *ribbit* is forbidden, so any extra given is understood to be a gift rather than interest. This is a striking *gemara*: after so many pages developing protections against *ribbit*, it appears to create a sweeping exemption for scholars. Tosafos is clearly troubled and explains that the permission applies only to a small, token extra that one would have given the person anyway even without the loan — and only among *talmidei chachamim*, where the social context makes the gift's independent character plausible. The difficulty remains: if one knows *ribbit* is forbidden, why does that knowledge transform a payment made in the loan context into something other than *ribbit*?

### Pepper Loan

Shmuel demonstrates the principle in practice, saying to the father of bar Ihi: lend me a hundred peppers and I will return a hundred and twenty — and all will be fine (*v'orach*). Rashi understands this as an illustration of the gift principle discussed above. The most coherent reading is that this operates within an informal domestic register — not a commercial transaction but a personal exchange where a slight overreturn is no different from giving back a full box of cornflake crumbs when only half a box was borrowed, because restoring the exact original quantity would be impractical or socially awkward. The extra is not paid *on account* of the loan; it arises from the natural dynamics of informal exchange.

### Teaching Interest

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav rules that a person is permitted to lend his own children with interest, in order to give them a taste (*ta'am*) of what *ribbit* feels like, so that they will understand the weight of the prohibition. The analogy is to allowing a child to try something harmful so that the experience itself serves as the lesson. The *gemara* immediately rejects this: *v'lav milta hi* — it is not a sound practice — because it may backfire (*d'asi l'misrach*). The child who is made to pay interest may conclude not that *ribbit* is dangerous but that it is profitable. The deeper question — why this pedagogical purpose would suspend an apparent *issur d'oraisa* — is left genuinely open by the *gemara* and is a notable difficulty.

## Labor Exchange

### Equal Labor

The *mishnah* teaches that a person may say to his friend: weed with me today and I will weed with you tomorrow (*nakesh immi v'anakesh immach*), or plow for me today and I will plow for you tomorrow (*ador immi v'ador immach*). Such reciprocal exchanges of equal labor are permitted. Agricultural communities routinely coordinated labor this way, pooling workers across fields, and the *mishnah* treats this as an ordinary arrangement. All days of the dry season (*gerid*) are treated as equivalent to one another, and all days of the rainy season (*revi'ah*) are similarly treated as equivalent — so exchanging a day of work in the dry season for another day of work in the same season raises no concern.

### Unequal Labor

However, the *mishnah* prohibits unequal exchanges: one may not say, "You weed for me and I will plow for you" — since weeding and plowing may be valued differently — nor may one exchange labor across seasons, such as: "Plow for me in the dry season and I will plow for you in the rainy season." Work in one season may be harder than in the other. Both of these cases raise a *ribbit*-like concern analogous to *se'ah be-se'ah*: if the service rendered in return is worth more than the service received, it functions as a premium on what was effectively a deferred obligation.

## Usury Types

### Prepaid Usury

Rabban Gamliel teaches that there are two additional categories of *ribbit* that operate even without an explicit stipulation in the loan agreement, provided the extra benefit is given on account of the loan. The first is *ribbit mukdemes* — prepaid *ribbit*. If a would-be borrower sends a gift to a potential lender with the explicit statement "in order that you lend me the money," this constitutes rabbinic *ribbit*, even though the loan has not yet occurred. While the classic structure of *ribbit* envisions compensation for the lender's waiting (*natar agar*), even a payment made before the loan — as an inducement — falls within the rabbinic prohibition.

### Delayed Usury

The second category is *ribbit me'ucheres* — delayed or posthumous *ribbit*. If after repaying the loan in full the borrower sends a gift to the lender with the explanation "this is because your money was idle with me," that is *ribbit me'ucheres*. Here the *natar agar* logic applies precisely: the gift is openly described as payment for the lender's forbearance during the loan period. Since it was not stipulated, it is rabbinic rather than biblical *ribbit*, but it is prohibited nonetheless.

### Usury of Words

Rabbi Shimon adds a further category: *ribbit devarim* — *ribbit* of words. A borrower should not say to the lender, "Know that so-and-so has arrived from such-and-such a place," conveying insider information as a form of repayment for the loan. A *baraita* expands this: one who has lent money to another, and the borrower was not in the habit of greeting the lender warmly, should not now go out of his way to do so on account of the loan — for even a greeting, a word of warmth extended because of the debt, constitutes a form of *ribbit*. The source is *neshech kol davar asher yishach* — the word *kol davar* (any matter) includes even speech. This raises interesting practical questions about what constitutes ordinary *menschlichkeit* and what crosses into *ribbit devarim* — questions the later authorities (*poskim*) engage with at length.

## Usury Transgressors

### Lender Violations

The final *mishnah* of the chapter enumerates who transgresses and under what prohibitions. Abaye teaches that the lender transgresses all of them: *lo sitten* — the prohibition on giving money at interest — when he hands over the loan; *al tikach* — the prohibition on taking interest — when he receives it back; *lo tihyeh lo kenosheh* — the prohibition on bearing down on the borrower like a creditor — through the entire relationship of demanding repayment; *lo tasimun alav neshech* — the prohibition on placing the bite upon him — through the creation of the debt and its documentation; and *lifnei iver lo sitten michshol* — the prohibition on placing a stumbling block before the blind — by causing the borrower to transgress the borrower's own prohibitions.

### Other Violators

The borrower transgresses *lo sashich le'achicha* — the prohibition on being bitten — and *lifnei iver* against the lender, since the transaction requires two parties and the borrower enables the lender to sin. The guarantor (*arev*) and the witnesses transgress *lo tasimun alav neshech* — by making the loan happen they participate in the imposition of the debt. Tosafos adds that in cases where the loan would not have occurred without the guarantor or witnesses, they additionally transgress *lifnei iver*, since their involvement was a necessary condition of the transgression. If the loan would have occurred without them, their participation is still improper, but the *lifnei iver* analysis does not apply. The *chachamim* add the scribe (*sofer*) to the list of transgressors, for the same reason.

### Financial Loss

Rabbi Shimon closes with a sobering observation: those who lend with *ribbit* lose more than they gain. Ultimately their properties are lost. Beyond the financial consequences, he adds that they implicitly disparage Moshe Rabbeinu and the Torah — since by ignoring the prohibition on *ribbit* they effectively declare that the Torah's law makes no economic sense. The *gemara* gives voice to this implicit attitude: "If Moshe Rabbeinu had known there was profit to be made in this, he would not have written the prohibition." In other words, lending with *ribbit* is an act of defiance against the wisdom of the Torah, particularly pointed in the commercial context where the economic rationale for the prohibition is less intuitively obvious than in the agricultural world for which it was originally articulated.

## Loans Without Witnesses

### Avoiding Humiliation

Rav Dimi teaches: how do we know that if one has lent money to someone and knows that person currently lacks the funds to repay, one must not walk past him or allow oneself to be seen by him? From the verse *lo tihyeh lo kenosheh* — do not be over him like a creditor. This prohibition does not require a *ribbit* context; it applies to any loan. The power dynamic of the creditor-debtor relationship, when made visible and felt through the lender's presence, creates a profound and forbidden humiliation. Rav Ami and Rav Asi add: one who does this is as if one has inflicted two punishments upon the borrower, citing the verse *hirkavta enosh leroshinu banu va'eish uvamayim* — You put a person above our heads; we came through fire and water.

### Witness Requirement

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav rules: if one has money and lends it without witnesses, one transgresses *lifnei iver lo sitten michshol* — placing a stumbling block before the blind. The mechanism is indirect but real: without a record or witnesses, the borrower may forget the loan, or over time convince himself he does not owe it, and will fail to repay — not even necessarily in bad faith. The lender, by creating a situation in which the borrower can easily fall into non-repayment, has caused him to sin. Resh Lakish adds a further consequence: the lender will be cursed, because when he comes to collect and the borrower denies owing, the lender appears as a liar who is making false claims against an upright person — *te'almanah siftei sheker hadovrot al tzaddik atak*.

### Ravina's Example

The Rabbis told Rav Ashi that Ravina was scrupulous in fulfilling every ruling of the Rabbis in this matter. Just before *Erev Shabbos*, someone sent Ravina a message asking to borrow ten *zuz* — a hundred dollars' worth — to purchase a parcel of land, a *metziah* that had just become available. Ravina replied: certainly, but first bring witnesses and we will write a document. The borrower responded: "Even me? You don't trust me? I am a Torah scholar!" Ravina replied: "Precisely you — you are so absorbed in your learning and your *girsa* that you are likely to forget you borrowed the money at all, and then when I come to collect you will deny it in good faith, and since you are such a *tzaddik* people will assume I am lying." This vignette exemplifies the exact harm the rabbis sought to prevent, and Ravina's insistence on witnesses — even with a close colleague, even on *Erev Shabbos*, even with someone who considered the request an affront — illustrates how seriously this teaching was taken.

### Unanswered Prayers

The *gemara* closes the chapter with a *baraita*: three people cry out and are not answered. First, one who has money and lends it without witnesses — he has caused his own loss and has no legitimate claim on heaven's sympathy. Second, one who acquires a master over himself (*hakoneh adon le'atzmo*): some interpret this as one who falsely claims his property belongs to a *nochri* to conceal his wealth, only to find the *nochri* eventually asserting ownership; others interpret it as one who signs over his property to his children while still alive — like King Lear — thereby inverting the natural power dynamic; still others interpret it as one who has a bad reputation in his city and simply does nothing to change it by moving elsewhere. Third, a man who allows his wife to have dominion over him. With this teaching the chapter of *ribbit* concludes.

*Hadran alach eizehu neshech.*
