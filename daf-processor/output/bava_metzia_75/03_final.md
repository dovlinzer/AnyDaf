# Bava Metzia 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Price Protection

### Forward Purchase Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפוֹסֵק עִמּוֹ כְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One may set** a price **at the highest rate,** meaning he may set a price for the future delivery of produce and stipulate that if the market rate falls below the agreed-upon price, he will purchase the product according to the lowest price in effect in the market at any point during the year, which is the price that will provide the highest amount of merchandise for the amount he agreed to pay.

The daf opens mid-discussion of the *mishnah*'s ruling on *posek imo k'sha'ar hagavo'ah* — the practice of forward purchasing. When someone pays money upfront for produce to be delivered later in the year, at a time when prices are typically higher, the seller must either own the produce or it must be readily available in the market. The *mishnah* permits the purchaser to stipulate price protection: if, say, a hundred bushels are bought at ten dollars per bushel and the price later drops, delivery can be made at the lower price. This is the position of the *tanna kamma*. Rabbi Yehuda goes further, and Rabbi Yossi holds that even without an explicit stipulation the purchaser retains this right.

### Rav Papa's Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּיהַיב זוּזֵי לִנְדוּנְיָא דְּבֵי חֲמוּהּ, לְסוֹף זַל נְדוּנְיָא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי פְּסַקְתְּ (עִמּוֹ) כְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ – שְׁקֹל כִּהַשְׁתָּא. וְאִי לָא – שְׁקֹל כִּי מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who gave money** to sellers to buy jewelry **for** his betrothed's **dowry [*linduneya*]** on behalf **of his father-in-law,** as his intended father-in-law made him an agent to buy the jewelry for part of the dowry. The betrothed man stipulated with the sellers that they would provide the jewelry in time for the wedding. **Ultimately, the** jewelry for the **dowry became less expensive,** as the price fell. The betrothed man wanted to retract his commitment to buy the jewelry at the higher price. The parties **came before Rav Pappa** for a ruling. Rav Pappa **said to** the betrothed man: **If you set** a price **with** the seller to buy the jewelry **at the highest rate,** i.e., the largest amount of jewelry for the price you are willing to pay, then **take** the jewelry **at the present** price. **But if not, take** it **at** the price that you set **initially.**

The *gemara* opens with a case: a certain man advanced money to purchase jewelry (*nidunya*) on behalf of his father-in-law's household, and by the time delivery was due the price had fallen — meaning he could have received more for his money. The matter came before Rav Papa, who ruled: if you stipulated at the time of payment that you would always receive the benefit of the lowest price, then take delivery at the current lower price. But if you made no such stipulation, you take delivery at the originally agreed price. This follows the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*. Critically, throughout this discussion we are dealing with a situation where money was paid but no *meshichah* — no act of formal acquisition — was performed. Since *meshichah* had not been done, the sale is not yet final, which is the entire framework within which these stipulations operate.

### Backing Out Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב פָּפָּא: וְאִי לָא פְּסַק – שָׁקֵיל כְּמֵעִיקָּרָא? מָעוֹת נִינְהוּ, וּמָעוֹת לָא קָנוּ! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא נָמֵי לְקַבּוֹלֵי עֲלֵיהּ ״מִי שֶׁפָּרַע״ קָא אָמֵינָא. אִי פְּסַק כְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ, מוֹכֵר קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ, מְקַבֵּל עֲלֵיהּ מוֹכֵר ״מִי שֶׁפָּרַע״. אִי לָא פְּסַק – לוֹקֵחַ קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ, מְקַבֵּל עֲלֵיהּ לוֹקֵחַ ״מִי שֶׁפָּרַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rav Pappa: And if he did not set** a price at the highest rate, **must he take** the merchandise **at** the price he set **initially? This is** a case where he paid **money, and** giving **money** alone **does not effect acquisition.** Rav Pappa **said to them:** I did not mean that it was an actual acquisition; rather, **I also** agree that giving money does not effect acquisition. What **I said** was **with regard to accepting upon himself** the curse: **He Who exacted payment. If** the betrothed man **set** a price **at the highest rate** the buyer is in the right, and if so, **the seller** is the one who **retracted,** and therefore **the seller accepts upon himself** the curse: **He Who exacted payment.** But **if** the betrothed man **did not set** a price at the highest rate, then the betrothed man, i.e., the **buyer,** is the one who **retracted,** and therefore **the buyer accepts upon himself** the curse: **He Who exacted payment.**

The rabbinical students challenged Rav Papa: if no stipulation was made, why must the purchaser accept the higher price? Since money alone does not finalize a sale (*ma'os lo kanu*), could the purchaser simply back out, demand his money returned, and repurchase at the now-lower price? Rav Papa replied that this question misses the point of his ruling. Of course the purchaser may back out — but doing so without a stipulation exposes him to a *mi shepara*, the formal curse for reneging on an agreement. His ruling was precisely about how to avoid that curse: if the seller stipulated price protection and the seller subsequently wishes to back out, the seller receives the *mi shepara*; if the purchaser made no such stipulation and now wishes to back out because the price has dropped, it is the purchaser who receives the *mi shepara*. The options exist, but they carry consequences.

### Ravina's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב פָּפָּא: וּמִמַּאי דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא דִּפְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמְרִי מָעוֹת לָא קָנוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי: אִי פְּסַק כְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ – שָׁקֵיל כִּדְהַשְׁתָּא, אִי לָא פְּסַק – שָׁקֵיל כִּדְמֵעִיקָּרָא, דִּלְמָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת: וְכִי פְּסַק כְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ – שָׁקֵיל כִּי הַשְׁתָּא, אִי לָא פְּסַק – שָׁקֵיל כְּמֵעִיקָּרָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָנֵי לְהוּ זוּזֵי. אֲבָל לְרַבָּנַן, בֵּין פְּסַק בֵּין לָא פְּסַק – שָׁקֵיל כִּי הַשְׁתָּא, דְּדַעְתֵּיהּ דְּאִינִישׁ אַתַּרְעָא זִילָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Pappa: From where** do you know **that** the ruling of the mishna, i.e., that if he did not set a price according to the highest rate he must acquire the merchandise at the price he set initially, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Shimon and** who **say** that giving **money does not effect acquisition? And even so,** they hold that **if he set** a price **at the highest rate, he takes** it **at the current** price, and **if he did not set** a price at the highest rate, **he takes** it **at** the price he set **initially.** **Perhaps** the ruling of the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that giving **money effects acquisition, and** therefore **if he set** a price **at the highest rate, he takes** it **at the current** price, and **if he did not set** a price at the highest rate, **he takes** it **at** the price he set **initially, since** giving **money effects acquisition. But according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, whether he set** a price at the highest rate **or did not set** a price at the highest rate, **he takes** it **at the current** price, **because a person's intention** is always to acquire merchandise **at the least expensive price.**

Ravina then raised a broader theoretical challenge, attempting to reframe the entire structure of the *mishnah*. He argued that the strict position in the *mishnah* — requiring an explicit stipulation — might in fact represent the view of Rabbi Shimon, while the more lenient position of Rabbi Yehuda might actually represent the *chachamim* who disagree with Rabbi Shimon.

To understand this argument, one must grasp the underlying debate. Rabbi Shimon holds that the purchaser generally cannot back out, even to accept a *mi shepara*. The *chachamim* hold that either party may back out and receive a *mi shepara*. Ravina's proposal: the position requiring explicit stipulation is Rabbi Shimon's, since in his view the purchaser normally has no right to back out at all — and therefore the only mechanism by which the purchaser may exit when prices change is through an explicit prior stipulation. For the *chachamim*, however, who routinely permit the purchaser to back out (albeit with a *mi shepara*), a change in market price represents a natural and obvious reason to exit — so obvious, Ravina argues, that the purchaser's intent to have done so should be presumed (*da'atya d'inish a'tara zeila*, people always assume the lesser value). On this reading, the *chachamim* would hold like Rabbi Yehuda of the *mishnah*: when prices change, the purchaser may withdraw without any *mi shepara*, even absent an explicit stipulation. This would make Rabbi Yehuda's position not a lone minority view but the mainstream halachic position of the *chachamim*.

### Rav Papa Responds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּחַד תַּרְעָא, בִּתְרֵי תַּרְעֵי מִי אָמַר? דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, ״מִי שֶׁפָּרַע״ בְּלוֹקֵחַ – לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa **said to him: Say that Rabbi Shimon said** his ruling that giving money effects acquisition in a case **where** there was **one price,** i.e., the price did not change in the meantime. **Did he say** his ruling **where** there were **two prices?** Certainly Rabbi Shimon will concede that the buyer can withdraw from the sale if the market price changes. **As, if you do not say so,** then the curse: **He Who exacted payment, will not** apply **to the buyer** under any circumstances **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא: מִכׇּל מָקוֹם כָּךְ הֲלָכָה, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים ״מִי שֶׁפָּרַע״ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Indeed,** the curse: He Who exacted payment, never applies to a buyer according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon says: Even though the Sages said that when one party takes possession of a garment, the other party acquires a gold dinar, but when one party takes possession of a gold dinar, the other party does not acquire a garment, but when one party performs an act of acquisition by pulling the gold dinar into his possession, the other party acquires the garment, **in any case, that is** what the ***halakha*** would be. **But** the Sages **said** with regard to one who withdraws from a transaction where one party performed an act of acquisition by pulling the gold dinar into his possession: **He Who exacted payment** from the people of the generation of the flood, and from the people of the generation of the dispersion, and from the inhabitants of Sodom and Gomorrah, and from the Egyptians in the Red Sea, will in the future exact payment from whoever does not stand by his statement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מִכׇּל מָקוֹם? לָאו דְּלָא שְׁנָא לוֹקֵחַ וְלָא שְׁנָא מוֹכֵר מְקַבֵּל עֲלֵיהּ ״מִי שֶׁפָּרַע״? אֶלָּא כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בְּחַד תַּרְעָא, בִּתְרֵי תַּרְעֵי לָא אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa clarifies: **What** is the meaning of: **In any case?** Does it **not** mean that **there is no difference** whether it is **the buyer and there is no difference** whether it is **the seller** who withdraws from the sale, that either way he **accepts upon himself** the curse: **He Who exacted payment? Rather,** it must be that **when Rabbi Shimon is saying** that giving money effects acquisition, he is referring to a case **where** there was **one price,** but in a case **where** there were **two prices he did not say** it.

Rav Papa rejected this construction. His response: even Rabbi Shimon would permit the purchaser to back out when the market price has changed, though with a *mi shepara*. His proof: the relevant *baraita* articulating Rabbi Shimon's position explicitly invokes the *mi shepara* curse, and the language there — *mikol makom*, nevertheless — includes both seller and purchaser. This can only make sense if Rabbi Shimon envisions a scenario where the purchaser does back out and receives a *mi shepara*. Were Rabbi Shimon's position an absolute bar on the purchaser backing out under any circumstances, there would be no occasion to speak of the purchaser receiving a *mi shepara* at all. The *gemara* attempts to deflect this by suggesting that perhaps for Rabbi Shimon no such scenario ever exists for the purchaser, but Rav Papa insists the *baraita*'s language forecloses that reading.

The conclusion is therefore: Rabbi Shimon allows the purchaser to back out when prices change, but only with a *mi shepara* — and the requirement to stipulate explicitly arises because even in a changed-price scenario, one still incurs a *mi shepara* without such a stipulation. Rav Papa then extends this logic to the *chachamim*: even though they always permit the purchaser to back out, even they would agree that a price change does not automatically exempt the purchaser from a *mi shepara* unless there was an explicit stipulation. He therefore maintains that one must pasken like the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*, requiring an explicit stipulation, and does not accept Ravina's attempted reconciliation.

### Shlichut Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּשָׁלִיחַ שַׁוְּיֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּתַגָּרָא דְּזָבֵין וּמְזַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: But let** Rav Pappa **derive** this *halakha* in the case of the dowry employing a more straightforward reasoning: The father-in-law **initially made** the betrothed man **an agent,** and since he was an agent, the father-in-law could say to him: I sent you to act for my benefit, not to my detriment. Purchasing the jewels at a more expensive price is to the detriment of the father-in-law, and therefore the agency and the sale itself are nullified. Rav Ashi **said to him:** It is speaking here about a case where the father-in-law did not actually make him an agent. Rather, the betrothed man was **a merchant who buys and sells** merchandise. The father-in-law understands that he engages in commerce and that he will not always profit from his trading.

A final challenge came from Rav Acha the son of Rava, addressed to Rav Ashi. Setting aside the entire *mi shepara* discussion, he raised an agency (*shlichut*) argument: the father-in-law had appointed his son-in-law as his agent (*shaliach*) to make the purchase. Since the son-in-law failed to stipulate price protection — acting imprudently — could the father-in-law simply declare the entire transaction void on the grounds that he appointed an agent to benefit him, not to harm him, and that a *shaliach* who exceeds or betrays his mandate is not acting as a valid agent at all? Rav Ashi replied: the case involves a merchant who buys and sells on his own account (*tagra d'zavin u'mazvin*) — the son-in-law was purchasing from the sellers with his own money and then reselling to his father-in-law, so the *shlichut* framework cannot be invoked to nullify the transaction. The upshot of the entire passage is that we pasken like the *chachamim* of the *mishnah*, requiring explicit stipulation.

## Sharecropper Loans

### Seed vs. Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַלְוֶה אָדָם אֶת אֲרִיסָיו חִטִּין בְּחִטִּין לְזֶרַע, אֲבָל לֹא לֶאֱכוֹל. שֶׁהָיָה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מַלְוֶה אֶת אֲרִיסָיו חִטִּין בְּחִטִּין לְזֶרַע, בְּיוֹקֶר וְהוּזְלוּ, אוֹ בְּזוֹל וְהוּקְרוּ – נוֹטֵל מֵהֶן כְּשַׁעַר הַזּוֹל. וְלֹא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֲלָכָה כֵּן, אֶלָּא שֶׁרָצָה לְהַחְמִיר עַל עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may lend wheat to his sharecroppers** in exchange **for wheat,** for the purpose **of seeding,** meaning that he may lend them a quantity of wheat with which to seed the field, and at harvest time the sharecropper will add the amount of grain that he borrowed to the landowner's portion of the yield. **But he may not** lend wheat for the sharecroppers **to eat** and be paid back with an equivalent quantity because this creates a concern about interest, as the price of wheat may rise. **As Rabban Gamliel would lend wheat to his sharecroppers** in exchange **for wheat,** for purposes **of seeding,** and if he lent it **at a high price and the price** then **fell, or** if he lent it **at an inexpensive price and the price** subsequently **rose,** in all cases **he would take** it back **from them at the inexpensive price. But** this was **not because this is** the ***halakha*; rather, he wanted to be stringent with himself.**

The next *mishnah* addresses lending seed to sharecroppers (*malveh arisin*). A landowner may lend his sharecroppers grain for planting (*lezera*) and later receive back an equal quantity at harvest time. However, he may not lend them grain for consumption (*le'echol*) to be repaid in kind later, because that would constitute *se'ah be-se'ah* — lending a measure of food to be returned measure for measure — which is a form of rabbinic *ribbit*. The concern is that by the time of repayment the grain may have risen in value, so the borrower would effectively be returning something worth more than what he received.

The *mishnah* notes that Rabban Gamliel would lend his sharecroppers seed for planting, and if the price changed in either direction by the time of repayment, he always accepted the lesser value. If the price rose, he accepted only enough grain to equal the original value; if the price fell, he accepted the lower-value grain even though it represented a loss. The *mishnah* clarifies that this was not because *halacha* required it — lending seed and expecting back equal quantity is in fact permitted — but because Rabban Gamliel chose to be strict upon himself. The point of the *mishnah* is precisely that one should not derive a normative ruling from Rabban Gamliel's personal stringency.

### Before/After Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַלְוֶה אָדָם אֶת אֲרִיסָיו חִטִּים בְּחִטִּים לְזֶרַע. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁלֹּא יָרַד, אֲבָל יָרַד אָסוּר. מַאי שְׁנָא תַּנָּא דִּידַן דְּלָא קָא מְפַלֵּיג בֵּין יָרַד וּבֵין לֹא יָרַד, וּמַאי שְׁנָא תַּנָּא בָּרָא דְּקָא מְפַלֵּיג בֵּין יָרַד וּבֵין לֹא יָרַד?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may lend wheat to his sharecroppers** in exchange **for wheat,** for the purpose **of seeding. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **when** the sharecropper **has not** yet **gone down** into the field to begin to work, **but** if he had already **gone down** into the field to begin to work, lending him wheat under these terms is **prohibited.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the *tanna* of our** mishna, **who does not differentiate between** whether the sharecropper **went down or did not go down, and what is different** about **the *tanna* of the *baraita*, who does differentiate between** whether **he went down or he did not go down?**

A *baraita* introduces a distinction absent from the *mishnah*: lending seed and expecting repayment in kind is permitted only before the sharecropper has gone down and begun working the field (*shelo yarad*), but once he has commenced work (*aval yarad*), it is forbidden. The *gemara* asks: why does the *mishnah* draw no such distinction, while the *baraita* does?

### Renegotiation Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, רַבִּי אִידִי אַסְבְּרַהּ נִיהֲלִי: בְּאַתְרָא דְּתַנָּא דִּידַן, אֲרִיסָא יָהֵיב בִּיזְרָא, בֵּין יָרַד וּבֵין לֹא יָרַד – כַּמָּה דְּלָא יָהֵיב בִּיזְרָא מָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ, וְכִי קָא נָחֵית – לִבְצִיר מֵהָכִי קָא נָחֵית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Idi explained** the matter **to me: In the locale of the *tanna* of our** mishna, the local custom was that **the sharecropper** would **provide the seeds,** and therefore, **whether he went down or did not go down, as long as** the sharecropper **has not put the seeds** into the field the landowner **can remove him** from the field. Consequently, in a case where the landowner gives the sharecropper the seeds, he sets the terms of the sharecropping tenancy, **and when** the sharecropper **goes down** into the field, **he goes down** into the field **for less than this,** under the agreement that he will reduce his share of the crop in order to return the seed to the landowner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאַתְרָא דְּתַנָּא בָּרָא, מָרֵי אַרְעָא יָהֵיב בִּיזְרָא, אִי לֹא יָרַד דְּמָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ, כִּי קָא נָחֵית – לִבְצִיר מֵהָכִי קָא נָחֵית, אִי יָרַד דְּלָא מָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **in the locale of the *tanna* of the *baraita*, the landowner** is the one who would **provide the seeds,** so **if he** has **not** yet **gone down** into the field the landowner **can remove him,** and therefore, **when he goes down** into the field, **he goes down for less than this.** But **if he went down,** and therefore the landowner **can no longer remove him,** lending him wheat under these terms is **prohibited,** because he took upon himself in advance to work the field without receiving seeds from the owner of the field. Consequently, these seeds that he then receives are like a loan and the prohibition of interest applies.

Rav Idi explained the discrepancy to Rava as follows. In the locale of the *mishnah*'s author, the practice was for the sharecropper to supply the seed. Since the sharecropper bears responsibility for the seed but lacks it, the landowner retains the right to eject him from the arrangement at any time — whether he has begun working or not — simply on the grounds that the sharecropper has failed to fulfill his obligation. This right of ejection gives the landowner perpetual leverage to renegotiate the terms. When the landowner provides seed under these conditions, it is not a loan but a renegotiated term of the sharecropping agreement: the sharecropper will provide his customary share of the harvest plus one additional measure of seed in lieu of what he owed. This is not *se'ah be-se'ah* because the seed is part of the compensation structure, not a loan.

In the locale of the *baraita*, by contrast, it is the landowner who is obligated to provide the seed. Before work begins, the landowner can still rescind the arrangement and offer revised terms — the provision of extra seed becomes a condition of the new agreement rather than a loan. But once the sharecropper has begun working, the landowner loses the right of ejection. At that point, if the landowner provides additional seed and demands its return at harvest, that is a genuine loan of food repayable in kind, and it does constitute prohibited *se'ah be-se'ah*.

## Se'ah Be-Se'ah

### Price Fixing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, אוֹמֵר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ: הַלְוֵינִי כּוֹר חִטִּין וְקוֹצֵץ לוֹ דָּמִים, הוּזְלוּ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ חִטִּים, הוּקְרוּ – נוֹתֵן דְּמֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: A person may say to another:** **Lend me a *kor* of wheat, and** the lender may **set a price for him,** stating that the borrower must repay the wheat in the future according to the value of wheat at the time of the loan. If, by the time the borrower must repay the loan, the wheat **depreciates** in value, he **gives** the lender a quantity of **wheat** equivalent to what he borrowed, and if it **appreciates,** he **gives** the **value of** the wheat he borrowed as per the market rate when he borrowed it, as agreed, but no more.

The *mishnah* teaches that one may say to a friend: lend me a measure of wheat and we will fix the price at the current rate. The mechanism is straightforward: instead of creating an obligation to return grain — which may be worth more at repayment — the obligation is converted into a monetary debt. You lend me a bushel of wheat worth ten dollars; I owe you ten dollars.

### Price Mechanics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא קָצַץ! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכִי קָאָמַר. אִם לֹא קָצַץ – הוּזְלוּ – נוֹטֵל חִטָּיו. הוּקְרוּ – נוֹתֵן דְּמֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this ruling: If the price of wheat depreciates, why should it be permitted for the borrower to pay him with wheat worth less than the value of the amount he borrowed? **But he fixed** a price at the time of the loan, and therefore the borrower owes him this amount of money. **Rav Sheshet said: This** is what the *tanna* **is saying: If** the lender **did not set** a price but merely lent him wheat, and it **depreciates** in value, the lender **takes his wheat,** as they did not agree that the borrower must repay the wheat according to its value at the time that the loan was taken out. But if it **appreciates** in value, the borrower **gives** the **value of** the wheat he borrowed as per the market rate when he borrowed it, in order to avoid the payment of interest.

The *gemara* raises a difficulty: if the price was fixed and then later falls, why should the borrower be permitted to repay in grain rather than in the fixed monetary amount? Rav Sheshet clarifies that the *baraita* is not addressing the case where a price was fixed. Rather, it addresses the case where no price was fixed at all. If someone says simply "lend me a bag of wheat and I will repay a bag of wheat" — which is technically *se'ah be-se'ah* — how does one avoid the *ribbit* problem at the moment of repayment? The answer: if prices have fallen or remained the same, repaying in kind is permissible because the returned grain is worth no more than what was borrowed; if prices have risen, one must repay the monetary value the grain held at the time of the loan, not a full bag of grain.

### Harvest Return Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יֹאמַר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ: הַלְוֵינִי כּוֹר חִטִּין וַאֲנִי אֶתֵּן לָךְ לַגּוֹרֶן. אֲבָל אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הַלְוֵינִי עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא בְּנִי אוֹ עַד שֶׁאֶמְצָא מַפְתֵּחַ. וְהִלֵּל אוֹסֵר. וְכֵן הָיָה הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: לֹא תַּלְוֶה אִשָּׁה כִּכָּר לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ עַד שֶׁתַּעֲשֶׂיהָ דָּמִים, שֶׁמָּא יוּקְרוּ חִטִּין, וְנִמְצְאוּ בָּאוֹת לִידֵי רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may not say to another: Lend me a *kor* of wheat and I will give** it back **to you at** the time the wheat is brought to **the granary,** as the wheat may increase in value, which would mean that when he gives him back a *kor* of wheat at the time the wheat is brought to the granary it is worth more than the value of the loan, and he therefore will have paid interest. **But he may say to him: Lend me** a *kor* of wheat for a short period of time, e.g., **until my son comes or until I find** the **key,** as there is no concern about a change in price during such a short interval of time. **And Hillel prohibits** the practice even in this case. **And Hillel would similarly say: A woman may not lend a loaf** of bread **to another unless she establishes its monetary** value, **lest the wheat appreciate** in value before she returns it, **and they will** therefore **have come to** transgress the prohibition of **interest.**

The *mishnah* further rules that one may not say: lend me a measure of wheat and I will repay you after the threshing season. This too is *se'ah be-se'ah*, since by harvest time the grain may have risen in value. However, the *mishnah* permits one to say: lend me this wheat and as soon as my son returns and opens the storehouse — or as soon as I find the key — I will repay you immediately. This is analogous to the rule on forward purchases: if the item is in one's possession or readily available, the transaction is permissible even if literal repayment occurs slightly later.

Hillel, however, would forbid even this. He also extends the concern to the domestic sphere: a woman should not lend her neighbor even a loaf of bread or a small domestic quantity without first fixing a price, lest the price of flour rise and a seemingly neighborly exchange become a form of rabbinic *ribbit*. Hillel's stringency thus operates on two axes: he disallows even deferred repayment when the item is merely accessible but not immediately at hand, and he extends *se'ah be-se'ah* concerns to small domestic loans. It is worth noting the irony that Hillel — normally the lenient authority — adopts the stricter position here, even as he was also the author of the *prosbul*, another institution touching on the law of loans.

### Ownership Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יֵשׁ לוֹ סְאָה – לֹוֶה סְאָה. סָאתַיִם – לֹוֶה סָאתַיִם. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ יֵשׁ לוֹ סְאָה – לֹוֶה עָלֶיהָ כַּמָּה כּוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna said:** One who **has a *se'a*** of an item in his house **may borrow a *se'a*** of that item. Since he has available a *se'a* that he could give back right away, he may borrow one *se'a*, and similarly, if he has **two *se'a*** available he **may borrow two *se'a*. Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Even if he has** only one ***se'a*,** he **may borrow several *kor*** in reliance **upon it.** Since he can repay part of the loan immediately, and as the market value has yet to change there is only a concern about future interest, this concern is mitigated when it does not apply to the entire loan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְסַיּוֹעֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק: טִיפַּת יַיִן אֵין לוֹ, טִיפַּת שֶׁמֶן אֵין לוֹ. הָא יֵשׁ לוֹ – לֹוֶה עָלֶיהָ כַּמָּה טִיפִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** a *baraita* **in support of Rabbi Yitzḥak's** ruling: If one **does not have a drop of wine** or if **he does not have a drop of oil,** he may not borrow wine or oil. **Consequently,** by inference it can be derived: If **he does have** a drop of wine or oil, he **may borrow many drops** in reliance **upon it,** as the *tanna* is certainly not referring to a case where he borrows just a few meager drops.

The *mishnah*'s permission to borrow when one already possesses the item raises the question of how much one must own. Rav Huna holds: one may borrow only up to the quantity one possesses. If you have one bag stored away, you may borrow one bag; if you have two, you may borrow two; but you may not borrow more than what you currently own.

Rabbi Yitzchak disagrees: even if you own only a single measure, you may borrow as many large quantities as you wish. His logic is that since *se'ah be-se'ah* is in any case rabbinic (*ribbit derabanan*), the requirement of ownership operates pro forma — a token amount suffices. Conceptually, one can also break the borrowing into serial transactions: you own one measure, you borrow one more (permissible), now you own two, you borrow two more, and so on. Though admittedly a fiction, it provides the halachic basis for the leniency. A *baraita* of Rav Chiya is cited in support of Rabbi Yitzchak: the *baraita* rules that one may not borrow wine if one has not a drop of wine, nor oil if one has not a drop of oil — the implication being that if one possesses even a single drop, one may borrow as much as one wishes.

### Chachamim's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלֵּל אוֹסֵר. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הִלֵּל. וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ. וְכֵן הָיָה הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: לֹא תַּלְוֶה אִשָּׁה [וְכוּ׳]. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי הִלֵּל, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹוִים סְתָם וּפוֹרְעִים סְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And Hillel prohibits** this practice. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of Hillel.** The Gemara comments: **But the *halakha* is not,** in fact, **in accordance with** the ruling of Shmuel. § The mishna further teaches: **And Hillel would similarly say: A woman may not lend** even a loaf of bread due to concern that she will violate the prohibition of interest. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: This** is **the statement of Hillel, but the Rabbis say** that **one may borrow** various types of foods **without specification and repay** them **without specification.** If neighbors are not particular with one another about these items, there is no concern about interest, in contrast to Hillel's opinon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה הַמַּקְפִּידִין זֶה עַל זֶה, עוֹבְרִין מִשּׁוּם מִדָּה וּמִשּׁוּם מִשְׁקָל וּמִשּׁוּם מִנְיָן, וּמִשּׁוּם לֹוִין וּפוֹרְעִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וּכְדִבְרֵי הִלֵּל אַף מִשּׁוּם רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to the **members of a group** of people that eat together **who are particular with each other** and insist that each pay for precisely what he ate, if they dine together on Shabbat, **they transgress** a prohibition **with regard to** the strictures of **measure, and with regard to** the strictures of **weight, and with regard to** the strictures **of counting,** all of which are calculations that are forbidden on Shabbat. **And they transgress** a prohibition **with regard to lending and repaying on a Festival, and according to the statement of Hillel,** they **also** transgress the prohibition **with regard to interest.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: this is Hillel's position, but the *chachamim* say one may simply borrow and repay without such formalities (*lovin stam u'pormin stam*). In practice, this leniency applies to domestic contexts where the exchanges are not commercial in character, parties are not exacting about precise quantities, and there is an implicit mutual tolerance for slight variations. Because no one is truly *makpid* — strictly insistent — the exchange lacks the character of a commercial loan, and the rabbinic *ribbit* concern does not apply.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel adds a further warning: members of a *chavurah* who take turns hosting one another but are very precise in accounting for every cost and every dish will transgress the prohibitions against measuring on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, and also the prohibition against lending and borrowing on *Yom Tov* — since their exacting accounting turns hospitality into a transaction. From Hillel's perspective, such a group also transgresses *ribbit*, since the precision of their accounting triggers even domestic *se'ah be-se'ah* concerns. Notably, the *gemara* implies that even the *chachamim* might not be concerned about *se'ah be-se'ah* for a group that is *makpid*, which is striking.

## Scholar Loans

### Scholar Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מוּתָּרִים לִלְווֹת זֶה מִזֶּה בְּרִבִּית. מַאי טַעְמָא – מִידָּע יָדְעִי דְּרִבִּית אֲסוּרָה, וּמַתָּנָה הוּא דְּיָהֲבִי אַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It is **permitted** for **Torah scholars to borrow from one another with interest.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? It is because **they are** fully **aware that interest is prohibited,** and therefore they do not intend the loan to be a formal business transaction. They willingly forgo the additional payments to each other at the outset, **and** the extra payment **is a gift that they give one another.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel teaches: *talmidei chachamim* are permitted to lend to one another and to return a little extra. The rationale offered is that they know *ribbit* is forbidden, so any extra given is understood to be a gift rather than interest. This is a striking *gemara*: after so many pages developing protections against *ribbit*, it appears to create a sweeping exemption for scholars. Tosafos is clearly troubled and explains that the permission applies only to a small, token extra that one would have given the person anyway even without the loan — and only among *talmidei chachamim*, where the social context makes the gift's independent character plausible. The difficulty remains: if one knows *ribbit* is forbidden, why does that knowledge transform a payment made in the loan context into something other than *ribbit*?

### Pepper Loan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לַאֲבוּהּ בַּר אִיהִי: הַלְוֵינִי מֵאָה פִּלְפְּלִין בְּמֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִין פִּלְפְּלִין, וַאֲרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel said to Avuh bar Ihi: Lend me one hundred peppers** in exchange **for 120 peppers** that I will give you at a later date. **And** you should know that this matter is **fitting** and appropriate, as I intend that the additional twenty peppers be a gift.

Shmuel demonstrates the principle in practice, saying to the father of bar Ihi: lend me a hundred peppers and I will return a hundred and twenty — and all will be fine (*v'orach*). Rashi understands this as an illustration of the gift principle discussed above. The most coherent reading is that this operates within an informal domestic register — not a commercial transaction but a personal exchange where a slight overreturn is no different from giving back a full box of cornflake crumbs when only half a box was borrowed, because restoring the exact original quantity would be impractical or socially awkward. The extra is not paid *on account* of the loan; it arises from the natural dynamics of informal exchange.

### Teaching Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מוּתָּר לוֹ לָאָדָם לְהַלְווֹת בָּנָיו וּבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ בְּרִבִּית, כְּדֵי לְהַטְעִימָן טַעַם רִבִּית. וְלָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתֵי לְמִיסְרַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: It is permitted for a person to lend to his sons and the members of his household with interest, in order to have them taste the taste of interest** so that they will understand how interest increases and how hard it is to repay it, which will discourage them from ever borrowing with interest again. The Gemara comments: **But** this **is not correct, because** the members of his household **may become corrupted** by doing so and act similarly with others in cases when there is no justification for such behavior.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav rules that a person is permitted to lend his own children with interest, in order to give them a taste (*ta'am*) of what *ribbit* feels like, so that they will understand the weight of the prohibition. The analogy is to allowing a child to try something harmful so that the experience itself serves as the lesson. The *gemara* immediately rejects this: *v'lav milta hi* — it is not a sound practice — because it may backfire (*d'asi l'misrach*). The child who is made to pay interest may conclude not that *ribbit* is dangerous but that it is profitable. The deeper question — why this pedagogical purpose would suspend an apparent *issur d'oraisa* — is left genuinely open by the *gemara* and is a notable difficulty.

## Labor Exchange

### Equal Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אוֹמֵר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ: נַכֵּשׁ עִמִּי וַאֲנַכֵּשׁ עִמָּךְ, עֲדוֹר עִמִּי וְאֶעֱדוֹר עִמָּךְ. וְלֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: נַכֵּשׁ עִמִּי וְאֶעֱדוֹר עִמָּךְ, עֲדוֹר עִמִּי וַאֲנַכֵּשׁ עִמָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may say to another: Weed** the wild growths from my field **with me** now, **and I will weed** your field **with you** at a later stage, or: **Till** my field **with me** today **and I will till with you** on a different day. **But he may not say to him: Weed with me** today **and I will till with you** a different day, or: **Till with me** today **and I will weed with you,** as due to the different nature of the tasks it is possible that one of them will have to work harder than the other did, which is a type of interest, since he repaid him with additional labor.

The *mishnah* teaches that a person may say to his friend: weed with me today and I will weed with you tomorrow (*nakesh immi v'anakesh immach*), or plow for me today and I will plow for you tomorrow (*ador immi v'ador immach*). Such reciprocal exchanges of equal labor are permitted. Agricultural communities routinely coordinated labor this way, pooling workers across fields, and the *mishnah* treats this as an ordinary arrangement. All days of the dry season (*gerid*) are treated as equivalent to one another, and all days of the rainy season (*revi'ah*) are similarly treated as equivalent — so exchanging a day of work in the dry season for another day of work in the same season raises no concern.

### Unequal Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל יְמֵי גְּרִיד – אֶחָד. כׇּל יְמֵי רְבִיעָה – אַחַת. לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: חֲרוֹשׁ עִמִּי בִּגְרִיד וַאֲנִי אֶחְרוֹשׁ עִמְּךָ בָּרְבִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **All** the **dry days** during the summer, when it does not rain, are viewed as **one** period, meaning that if they each agreed to work one day, the dry days are viewed as though they were all exactly equal in length, despite the slight differences between them. Similarly, **all** the **rainy days** are treated as **one** period. But **he may not say to him: Plow with me in the dry** season **and I will plow with you in the rainy** season.

However, the *mishnah* prohibits unequal exchanges: one may not say, "You weed for me and I will plow for you" — since weeding and plowing may be valued differently — nor may one exchange labor across seasons, such as: "Plow for me in the dry season and I will plow for you in the rainy season." Work in one season may be harder than in the other. Both of these cases raise a *ribbit*-like concern analogous to *se'ah be-se'ah*: if the service rendered in return is worth more than the service received, it functions as a premium on what was effectively a deferred obligation.

## Usury Types

### Prepaid Usury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ רִבִּית מוּקְדֶּמֶת וְיֵשׁ רִבִּית מְאוּחֶרֶת. כֵּיצַד? נָתַן עֵינָיו לִלְווֹת הֵימֶנּוּ, וְהוּא מְשַׁלֵּחַ לוֹ וְאוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתַּלְוֵנִי – זוֹ הִיא רִבִּית מוּקְדֶּמֶת. לָוָה הֵימֶנּוּ וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ אֶת מְעוֹתָיו, וְהוּא מְשַׁלֵּחַ לוֹ וְאוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁבִיל מְעוֹתֶיךָ שֶׁהָיוּ בְּטֵילוֹת אֶצְלִי – זוֹ הִיא רִבִּית מְאוּחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel says: There is** a case of **pre**-paid **interest, and there is** also a case of **interest** paid **later,** both of which are prohibited. **How so?** If he **had hopes of borrowing** money **from him** in the future, **and he sends him** money or a gift **and says:** I am sending you this gift **in order that you will lend to me, this is pre**-paid **interest.** Similarly, if he **borrowed** money **from him and** subsequently **returned his money, and he** later **sends** a gift **to him and says:** I am sending you this gift in order to repay you **for your money, which was idle with me,** preventing you from earning a profit from it, **this is interest** paid **later.**

Rabban Gamliel teaches that there are two additional categories of *ribbit* that operate even without an explicit stipulation in the loan agreement, provided the extra benefit is given on account of the loan. The first is *ribbit mukdemes* — prepaid *ribbit*. If a would-be borrower sends a gift to a potential lender with the explicit statement "in order that you lend me the money," this constitutes rabbinic *ribbit*, even though the loan has not yet occurred. While the classic structure of *ribbit* envisions compensation for the lender's waiting (*natar agar*), even a payment made before the loan — as an inducement — falls within the rabbinic prohibition.

The second category is *ribbit me'ucheres* — delayed or posthumous *ribbit*. If after repaying the loan in full the borrower sends a gift to the lender with the explanation "this is because your money was idle with me," that is *ribbit me'ucheres*. Here the *natar agar* logic applies precisely: the gift is openly described as payment for the lender's forbearance during the loan period. Since it was not stipulated, it is rabbinic rather than biblical *ribbit*, but it is prohibited nonetheless.

### Usury of Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ רִבִּית דְּבָרִים. לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ ״דַּע כִּי בָּא אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Not only is there interest consisting of payment of money or items, but **there is** also **verbal interest.** For example, the borrower **may not say to** the lender: You should **know that so-and-so has come from such and such a place,** when he is aware that this information is of significance to his creditor. Since his intention is to provide a benefit to the lender, he has effectively paid him an extra sum for the money he lent him, which constitutes interest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיתַּנְיָא, שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה רָגִיל לְהַקְדִּים לוֹ שָׁלוֹם, שֶׁאָסוּר לְהַקְדִּים לוֹ שָׁלוֹם...
>
> **Translation:** And it was taught: One who has lent money to another and the borrower was not in the habit of greeting the lender warmly, should not now go out of his way to do so on account of the loan — for even a greeting, a word of warmth extended because of the debt, constitutes a form of *ribbit*. The source is *neshech kol davar asher yishach* — the word *kol davar* (any matter) includes even speech. This raises interesting practical questions about what constitutes ordinary *menschlichkeit* and what crosses into *ribbit devarim* — questions the later authorities (*poskim*) engage with at length.

Rabbi Shimon adds a further category: *ribbit devarim* — *ribbit* of words. A borrower should not say to the lender, "Know that so-and-so has arrived from such-and-such a place," conveying insider information as a form of repayment for the loan. A *baraita* expands this: one who has lent money to another, and the borrower was not in the habit of greeting the lender warmly, should not now go out of his way to do so on account of the loan — for even a greeting, a word of warmth extended because of the debt, constitutes a form of *ribbit*. The source is *neshech kol davar asher yishach* — the word *kol davar* (any matter) includes even speech. This raises interesting practical questions about what constitutes ordinary *menschlichkeit* and what crosses into *ribbit devarim* — questions the later authorities (*poskim*) engage with at length.

## Usury Transgressors

### Lender Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ עוֹבְרִין בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה: הַמַּלְוֶה וְהַלֹּוֶה וְהֶעָרֵב וְהָעֵדִים. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַסּוֹפֵר. עוֹבְרִים מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תִתֵּן״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״אַל תִּקַּח מֵאִתּוֹ״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תִהְיֶה לוֹ כְּנוֹשֶׁה״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תְשִׂימוּן עָלָיו נֶשֶׁךְ״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשׁוֹל וְיָרֵאתָ מֵּאֱלֹהֶיךָ אֲנִי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** people **violate a prohibition** of interest: **The lender, and the borrower, and the guarantor, and the witnesses. And the Rabbis say: Also the scribe** who writes the promissory note violates this prohibition. These parties to the transaction **violate** different prohibtions. Some are in violation of: **"You shall not give** him your money with interest" (Leviticus 25:37), **and of: "Do not take from him** interest or increase" (Leviticus 25:36), **and of: "Do not be to him as a creditor"** (Exodus 22:24), **and of "Do not place interest upon him"** (Exodus 22:24), **and of: "And you shall not place a stumbling block before the blind, and you shall fear your God; I am the Lord"** (Leviticus 19:14).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לַנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְאֵינוֹ רָגִיל לְהַקְדִּים לוֹ שָׁלוֹם, שֶׁאָסוּר לְהַקְדִּים לוֹ שָׁלוֹם – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶשֶׁךְ כׇּל דָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁךְ״ – אֲפִילּוּ דִּיבּוּר אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: From where** is it derived **with regard to one who is owed one hundred dinars by another, and** the borrower **is not accustomed to greeting** that lender, **that it is prohibited to** start **greeting him** after being granted the loan? **The verse states: "Interest of any matter [*davar*] that is lent with interest"** (Deuteronomy 23:20), which can also be read as indicating that **even speech [*dibbur*]** can be **prohibited** as interest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ עוֹבְרִין, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַלְוֶה עוֹבֵר בְּכוּלָּן. לֹוֶה עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תַשִּׁיךְ לְאָחִיךָ״. ״וּלְאָחִיךָ לֹא תַשִּׁיךְ״, ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשׁוֹל״. עָרֵב וְהָעֵדִים אֵין עוֹבְרִים אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תְשִׂימוּן עָלָיו נֶשֶׁךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And these** people **violate** the prohibition of interest. **Abaye says:** The **lender violates all of them,** meaning all of the prohibitions listed in the mishna. The **borrower violates** the prohibition **of: "You shall not lend to your brother with interest"** (Deuteronomy 23:20), as he enables his brother to lend with interest. And they also violate the prohibition: "You may lend to a gentile with interest, but **to your brother you shall not lend with interest"** (Deuteronomy 23:21), as well as: **"And you shall not place a stumbling block before the blind"** (Leviticus 19:14). The **guarantor and the witness violate only: "Do not place interest upon him"** (Exodus 22:24).

The final *mishnah* of the chapter enumerates who transgresses and under what prohibitions. Abaye teaches that the lender transgresses all of them: *lo sitten* — the prohibition on giving money at interest — when he hands over the loan; *al tikach* — the prohibition on taking interest — when he receives it back; *lo tihyeh lo kenosheh* — the prohibition on bearing down on the borrower like a creditor — through the entire relationship of demanding repayment; *lo tasimun alav neshech* — the prohibition on placing the bite upon him — through the creation of the debt and its documentation; and *lifnei iver lo sitten michshol* — the prohibition on placing a stumbling block before the blind — by causing the borrower to transgress the borrower's own prohibitions.

The borrower transgresses *lo sashich le'achicha* — the prohibition on being bitten — and *lifnei iver* against the lender, since the transaction requires two parties and the borrower enables the lender to sin. The guarantor (*arev*) and the witnesses transgress *lo tasimun alav neshech* — by making the loan happen they participate in the imposition of the debt. Tosafos adds that in cases where the loan would not have occurred without the guarantor or witnesses, they additionally transgress *lifnei iver*, since their involvement was a necessary condition of the transgression. If the loan would have occurred without them, their participation is still improper, but the *lifnei iver* analysis does not apply. The *chachamim* add the scribe (*sofer*) to the list of transgressors, for the same reason.

### Financial Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מַלְוֵי רִבִּית יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁמַּרְוִיחִים – מַפְסִידִים. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמְּשִׂימִים מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ חָכָם וְתוֹרָתוֹ אֱמֶת. וְאוֹמְרִין: אִילּוּ הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ שֶׁיִּהְיֶה רֶיוַח בַּדָּבָר לֹא הָיָה כּוֹתְבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: Those who lend** with **interest lose more than they gain,** as they will eventually be punished by God. **Moreover,** a loan of this kind desecrates the name of Heaven, **as they cause** it to seem that **Moses our teacher** is **a scholar and his Torah** is **true.** This is a euphemism; Rabbi Shimon means that their actions make a mockery of Moses and his Torah. **And** this is because **they say: Had Moses our teacher known that there was a profit** involved **in the matter, he would not have written it** as a prohibition. Not only do they violate a mitzva but they also belittle the Torah.

Rabbi Shimon closes with a sobering observation: those who lend with *ribbit* lose more than they gain. Ultimately their properties are lost. Beyond the financial consequences, he adds that they implicitly disparage Moshe Rabbeinu and the Torah — since by ignoring the prohibition on *ribbit* they effectively declare that the Torah's law makes no economic sense. The *gemara* gives voice to this implicit attitude: "If Moshe Rabbeinu had known there was profit to be made in this, he would not have written the prohibition." In other words, lending with *ribbit* is an act of defiance against the wisdom of the Torah, particularly pointed in the commercial context where the economic rationale for the prohibition is less intuitively obvious than in the agricultural world for which it was originally articulated.

## Loans Without Witnesses

### Avoiding Humiliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: מִנַּיִן לַנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְיוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין לוֹ, שֶׁאָסוּר לַעֲבוֹר לְפָנָיו – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תִהְיֶה לוֹ כְּנֹשֶׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites further statements with regard to loans in general. **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he **said: From where** is it derived that with regard to **one who is owed one hundred dinars by another and knows that** the borrower **does not have** the funds to repay him, **that** it is **prohibited** for him **to pass before** the borrower, so as not to embarrass the borrower and cause him discomfort? **The verse states: "Do not be to him as a creditor"** (Exodus 22:24). Even if he does not claim the debt from the borrower, his presence reminds the latter of the debt, which distresses him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ כְּאִילּוּ דָּנוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי דִּינִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִרְכַּבְתָּ אֱנוֹשׁ לְרֹאשֵׁנוּ בָּאנוּ בָאֵשׁ וּבַמַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi both say** that if one upsets another in this way, it is **as though he sentences him to two** types **of punishments, as it is stated: "You have caused men to ride over our heads; we went through fire and through water"** (Psalms 66:12). As the one in control, a creditor is regarded as though he had brought the debtor through fire and water.

Rav Dimi teaches: how do we know that if one has lent money to someone and knows that person currently lacks the funds to repay, one must not walk past him or allow oneself to be seen by him? From the verse *lo tihyeh lo kenosheh* — do not be over him like a creditor. This prohibition does not require a *ribbit* context; it applies to any loan. The power dynamic of the creditor-debtor relationship, when made visible and felt through the lender's presence, creates a profound and forbidden humiliation. Rav Ami and Rav Asi add: one who does this is as if one has inflicted two punishments upon the borrower, citing the verse *hirkavta enosh leroshinu banu va'esh uvamayim* — You put a person above our heads; we came through fire and water.

### Witness Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כָּל מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מָעוֹת וּמַלְוֶה אוֹתָן שֶׁלֹּא בְּעֵדִים – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: גּוֹרֵם קְלָלָה לְעַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תֵּאָלַמְנָה שִׂפְתֵי שָׁקֶר הַדּוֹבְרוֹת עַל צַדִּיק עָתָק״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Whoever has money and lends it not in** the presence of **witnesses violates** the prohibition of: **"And you shall not place a stumbling block before the blind"** (Leviticus 19:14), as this tempts the borrower not to repay his debt. **And Reish Lakish says: He brings a curse upon himself, as it is stated: "Let the lying lips be dumb, which speak arrogantly against the righteous,** with pride and contempt" (Psalms 31:19), as when the lender comes to claim his money without any proof, people will think he is falsely accusing the borrower, and they will end up cursing him.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav rules: if one has money and lends it without witnesses, one transgresses *lifnei iver lo sitten michshol* — placing a stumbling block before the blind. The mechanism is indirect but real: without a record or witnesses, the borrower may forget the loan, or over time convince himself he does not owe it, and will fail to repay — not even necessarily in bad faith. The lender, by creating a situation in which the borrower can easily fall into non-repayment, has caused him to sin. Resh Lakish adds a further consequence: the lender will be cursed, because when he comes to collect and the borrower denies owing, the lender appears as a liar who is making false claims against an upright person — *te'almanah siftei sheker hadovrot al tzaddik atak*.

### Ravina's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: קָא מְקַיֵּים רָבִינָא כֹּל מָה דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵי פַּנְיָא דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא: לִישַׁדַּר לִי מָר עַשְׂרָה זוּזֵי דְּאִתְרְמִי לִי קַטִּינָא דְּאַרְעָא לְמִזְבַּן. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: נַיְתֵי מָר סָהֲדֵי וְנִכְתֹּב כְּתָבָא. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ אֲנָא נָמֵי? שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן מָר דִּטְרִיד בְּגִירְסֵיהּ מִשְׁתְּלֵי וְגוֹרֵם קְלָלָה לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related incident: **The Sages said to Rav Ashi: Ravina fulfills all** of the directives **that the Sages say.** Seeking to test him, Rav Ashi **sent** a messenger **to him close to sunset on the eve of Shabbat,** at the busiest time of the week, with the following request: **Let the Master send me ten dinars** as a loan, **as I have happened upon a small piece of land for an acquisition** and I need the money. Ravina **sent** a message **to him: Let the Master bring witnesses and we will write a written** document for this loan. Rav Ashi **sent** a message **to him: Even I, as well?** Do you suspect even me of shirking payment? Ravina **sent** a message **to him: All the more so** it is necessary to document a loan to **the Master, who is occupied with his studies** and therefore very likely **to forget,** and I will thereby **bring a curse upon myself.**

The Rabbis told Rav Ashi that Ravina was scrupulous in fulfilling every ruling of the Rabbis in this matter. Just before *Erev Shabbos*, someone sent Ravina a message asking to borrow ten *zuz* — a hundred dollars' worth — to purchase a parcel of land, a *metziah* that had just become available. Ravina replied: certainly, but first bring witnesses and we will write a document. The borrower responded: "Even me? You don't trust me? I am a Torah scholar!" Ravina replied: "Precisely you — you are so absorbed in your learning and your *girsa* that you are likely to forget you borrowed the money at all, and then when I come to collect you will deny it in good faith, and since you are such a *tzaddik* people will assume I am lying." This vignette exemplifies the exact harm the rabbis sought to prevent, and Ravina's insistence on witnesses — even with a close colleague, even on *Erev Shabbos*, even with someone who considered the request an affront — illustrates how seriously this teaching was taken.

### Unanswered Prayers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה צוֹעֲקִין וְאֵינָן נַעֲנִין, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מָעוֹת וּמַלְוֶה אוֹתָן שֶׁלֹּא בְּעֵדִים, וְהַקּוֹנֶה אָדוֹן לְעַצְמוֹ, וּמִי שֶׁאִשְׁתּוֹ מוֹשֶׁלֶת עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There are **three** who **cry** out **and are not answered,** as they are responsible for their own troubles. **And they are: One who has money and lends it not in** the presence of **witnesses, and one who acquires a master for himself, and one whose wife rules over him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹנָה אָדוֹן לְעַצְמוֹ מַאי הִיא? אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: תּוֹלֶה נְכָסָיו בְּגוֹי. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לְבָנָיו בְּחַיָּיו. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דְּבִישׁ לֵיהּ בְּהָא מָתָא וְלָא אָזֵיל לְמָתָא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: One who **acquires a master for himself, what is it? There are** those **who say** that it is referring to one who **attributes his property to a gentile.** He falsely claims that his possessions belong to a gentile in order to evade his obligations, thereby inviting the gentile to take advantage of this declaration. And **there are** those **who say** that it is referring to one **who writes** a document bequeathing **his property** as a gift **to his children in his lifetime,** as he becomes financially dependent on them. And **there are** those **who say that** it is referring to one **who has bad** fortune **in this town but does not go to a different town.** He is consequently responsible for his own misfortunes.

The *gemara* closes the chapter with a *baraita*: three people cry out and are not answered. First, one who has money and lends it without witnesses — he has caused his own loss and has no legitimate claim on heaven's sympathy. Second, one who acquires a master over himself (*hakoneh adon le'atzmo*): some interpret this as one who falsely claims his property belongs to a *nochri* to conceal his wealth, only to find the *nochri* eventually asserting ownership; others interpret it as one who signs over his property to his children while still alive — like King Lear — thereby inverting the natural power dynamic; still others interpret it as one who has a bad reputation in his city and simply does nothing to change it by moving elsewhere. Third, a man who allows his wife to have dominion over him. With this teaching the chapter of *ribbit* concludes.

*Hadran alach eizehu neshech.*
