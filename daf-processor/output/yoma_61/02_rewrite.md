# Yoma 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yehuda vs. Nechemia

### Debate Overview

The sugya begins at the very bottom of 61b with the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemia concerning where the order of the *Yom Kippur avodah* is *me'akev* — that is, where performing actions out of sequence invalidates what was done. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the order is *me'akev* only with respect to what is performed in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the realm of *lavan bifnim*. According to Rabbi Nechemia, the order is *me'akev* for everything done *bebigdei Lavan*, whether *bifnim* — inside the Holy of Holies — or *bachutz* — outside, including the blood sprinkled on the *parochet* and on the *mizbeach*. Rabbi Nechemia's position thus encompasses the entire sequence performed in white garments: the *hagrala*, the *shechitas hapar*, the *shechitas hase'ir*, the *chafinas ketoret*, and everything else performed *bebigdei Lavan bachutz*.

### Shirayim Question

One question left open in this debate concerns the *shirayim* — the pouring of the remnants of the blood — which is performed afterward. Ordinarily, the *shirayim* is not *me'akev*, but on *Yom Kippur* there may be a special consideration. The Torah says *vechilah mechaper* — that atonement is achieved only upon completing the entire service — which raises the possibility that the *shirayim* on *Yom Kippur* is *me'akev*. This generates two sub-questions: first, whether failing to perform the *shirayim* renders one *yotzei* at all; and second, if it is indeed *me'akev*, it would naturally be included within the scope of wherever order is mandated. That remains the outstanding question, joined by another external act yet to be discussed — the sending of the *se'ir la'azazel*, which is also performed *bebigdei Lavan bachutz* — but that is deferred for later.

### R. Chanina/Ketoret

The Gemara then cites a statement of Rabbi Chanina: if one performed the *chafinas ketoret* — the scooping of the incense — before the *shechitas hapar*, *lo asah v'lo klum*, it counts for nothing. To review the order: the *shechitas hapar* comes first, followed by the *hagrala*, then the *chafinas ketoret*, then bringing the *ketoret* inside, then taking the blood of the *par* and performing the blood service, then slaughtering the goat, and so on. If one performed the *ketoret* before slaughtering the *par*, that is out of order and the *ketoret* does not count. This appears straightforwardly to follow Rabbi Nechemia's view, since the *chafinas ketoret* is performed outside — *bebigdei Lavan bachutz* — and Rabbi Nechemia is the one who holds that order matters even for external acts.

### Disqualification Logic

A brief clarification is in order: when something is performed out of sequence, what is disqualified is the act done prematurely. So if one performed the *chafina* before the *shechitas hapar*, the *chafina* itself was invalid — *lo asah v'lo klum*. There may be a ripple effect if subsequent actions then follow in an order that presupposes the invalid act, but the fundamental principle is that the premature act itself does not count.

### Tzorech Pnim Question

The Gemara asks: *k'man* — whose view does Rabbi Chanina's statement follow? Apparently not Rabbi Yehuda's, since Rabbi Yehuda holds that order matters only for actions performed inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and the *chafinas ketoret* is an external act.

### Tzorech Pnim Resolution

The Gemara responds: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehuda* — one can read the statement as consistent with Rabbi Yehuda as well. The principle invoked is *tzorech pnim kepnim dami* — that which is done outside for the sake of the inside is treated as if it were inside. This significantly narrows the scope of the dispute. Rabbi Yehuda's position is not merely that order matters inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, but that it also matters for everything performed outside that is necessary for what takes place inside: the *shechitas hapar*, the *chafinas ketoret*, the *shechitas hase'ir* — all of it is *tzorech pnim* and therefore subject to the same order requirement as the inner service itself. The genuine debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemia, then, concerns only the blood on the *parochet*, the blood on the *mizbeach*, and perhaps the *shirayim* — the external acts that are not preparatory to the inner service but constitute an independent outer service.

## Blood Spills

### Mishna: Spilled Blood

The Gemara now tests this expanded understanding of Rabbi Yehuda's view against the Mishna. The *seifa* of the Mishna addresses not the question of order per se, but the question of what to do when blood spills mid-process: does one restart from the beginning of the entire unit, or simply from the point of interruption? The Mishna states: *im ad shelo gamar matnos shebifnim nishpach hadam yavi dam acher veyachzor veyachel betchila mibifnim* — if before completing the inner *matanot* in the *Kodesh Kodashim* the blood spilled, one brings fresh blood and restarts from the beginning of the inner service.

### Ketoret Problem

This generates a serious difficulty. If the blood spilled during the inner service of the *par* — say, in the middle of step four — and one must go back to bring new blood, that means *shechting* the animal again. But once one must go back to *shechitas hapar*, if we now hold — as Rabbi Yehuda does, thanks to the principle of *tzorech pnim* — that the order of the external preparatory acts is also *me'akev*, then the sequence following the new *shechita* must observe that entire order as well. That means performing the *chafinas ketoret* again, bringing the *ketoret* inside again, and only then obtaining the new blood and entering the *Kodesh Kodashim*. Yet the Mishna says simply: bring new blood and restart from the beginning of the inner service. It says nothing about repeating the *ketoret*. How can the Mishna remain silent on that?

### Ketoret Solution

The Gemara answers: *beketoret lo kamairi* — the Mishna is simply not discussing the *ketoret*. It does not mean that one skips the *ketoret*; it means the Mishna is focusing exclusively on the blood service. As Rashi makes explicit: *ומיהו ודאי צריך להקטיר קטורת אחרת אחר שחיטת פר זה* — although the Mishna mentions only the blood, it is certainly true that one must also perform another *haktarat ketoret* after the new *shechita* of the *par*. The Mishna's brevity on this point does not imply otherwise; it simply leaves those steps implicit.

## Yehuda vs. Nechemia…

### Ulla/Se'ir

A parallel statement from Ulla now follows: *se'ir sheshachato kodem matan damo shel par lo asa velo klum* — if one slaughtered the *se'ir* before performing the blood service of the *par*, it counts for nothing. This presumably holds even according to Rabbi Yehuda, for the same reason: the *shechitas hase'ir* is *tzorech pnim*, done for the sake of the inner service, and therefore subject to the same order requirement. This is a statement in the same vein as Rabbi Chanina's: order matters even for these external acts that serve the inner service.

---

## Blood Spills…

### Se'ir Problem

Ulla's statement creates a challenge that parallels the one raised against the Mishna above. The Mishna states that if one applied the blood of the *se'ir* before the blood of the *par* — *hikdim damah shel se'ir ledam hapar* — one simply holds the blood of the *se'ir* and, after completing the blood service of the *par*, performs the *se'ir*'s blood service in its proper place. The Mishna says *yachzor veyaze midam hase'ir achar dam hapar* — go back and sprinkle the blood of the *se'ir* after the blood of the *par*. But this is problematic. If one sprinkled the blood of the *se'ir* early, it means one had already *shechted* the *se'ir* before the blood service of the *par* — since to have blood of the *se'ir* available, one must have already slaughtered it. And since the *shechitas hase'ir* is *me'akev* as Ulla states, the Mishna should not say simply "do the blood of the *se'ir* afterward." It should say: complete the blood of the *par*, then slaughter a new *se'ir*, and only then do its blood service. The Mishna implies one simply continues with the existing blood, which seems to violate the very principle Ulla articulated.

### Ulla/Location Fix

The Gemara resolves this by restricting the scope of the Mishna: *targuma Ulla bematanot shebaheichal* — Ulla himself explained that the Mishna is referring to the blood applications performed in the *Heichal*, not those in the *Kodesh Kodashim*. Rav Afis concurs: *vechein amar Rav Afis bematanot shebaheichal*. The distinction is crucial. In the scenario where one performs the blood of the *se'ir* before the blood of the *par* while both are already slaughtered — because everything has already been slaughtered and one is now only at the stage of applying blood in the *Heichal* — no new *shechita* is implicated. Doing step seven before step six, when both animals are already slaughtered, means one simply holds off and performs the *par*'s blood in the *Heichal* first, then performs the *se'ir*'s blood afterward. There is no need to slaughter another *se'ir*. But if one were to *shecht* the *se'ir* out of order — before completing the blood of the *par* — then the entire complex of *tzorech pnim* requirements would have been violated, and a new *se'ir* would need to be slaughtered. The Mishna's case avoids that problem by being situated entirely within the *Heichal* blood applications, where all slaughtering has already occurred.

## Separate Units

### Baraita: Units

A *baraita* is now introduced that articulates the foundational principle governing all of these cases: each location — the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the *Heichal*, and the *mizbeach* — constitutes a separate unit of *kapara*, an independent domain of atonement. The source for this will be examined through the *pesukim* momentarily, but the structural point is clear: each location is self-contained.

### Intra-Unit Spill

Within any given unit, if the blood spills in the middle, one must bring new blood and restart from the beginning of that unit. If blood spills during the *Kodesh Kodashim* service, one returns to the beginning of the *Kodesh Kodashim* service. If it spills during the *Heichal* service, one returns to the beginning of the *Heichal* service. If it spills during the *mizbeach* service, one returns to the beginning of the *mizbeach* service. This is the view of the *Tana Kamma*; Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon dispute this and hold instead that one picks up precisely where one left off, a debate to be addressed below.

### Inter-Unit Spill

If the blood spills *between* units — after one unit is fully completed but before the next has begun — there is no question: one brings new blood and simply begins the next unit. One is never required to go back and repeat a unit that has already been completed. The separateness of the units provides clear stopping points.

## Pesukim Analysis

### Pesuk Structure

Before examining the debate between the *Tana Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon in greater depth, the Gemara first turns to the *pesukim* of *Acharei Mot* to ground the theological meaning of the entire *avodah*. The *pesukim* contain multiple statements of *vechipper* — atonement — directed at distinct locations and groups. After the *Kodesh Kodashim* service, the Torah says *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot bnei Yisrael umipisheihem lechol chatotam*. Then, *vechein ya'aseh le'ohel moed hashochen itam betoch tumatam* — referring to the *parochet*. Then *veyatza el hamizbeach vechipper alav*, and *vechilah mechaper et hakodesh ve'et ohel moed ve'et hamizbeach*. After the sending of the *se'ir la'azazel*, at the very end of the *avodah*, the Torah says *vechipper et mikdash hakodesh ve'et ohel moed ve'et hamizbeach yechapper ve'al hakohanim ve'al kol am hakahal yechapper*.

### Gemara Interprets

The Gemara works through this closing *pasuk* systematically. *Vechipper et mikdash hakodesh* — *zeh lifnai velifnim*, this refers to the *Kodesh Kodashim*. *Ohel moed* — *zeh heichal*, this refers to the *Heichal*, since the earlier *pasuk* had already interpreted *vechein ya'aseh le'ohel moed* as referring to the blood applied on the *parochet*, which is for the sake of the *Heichal*. *Mizbeach kemashma'o* — the golden inner altar, taken straightforwardly. *Yechapper* — *elu azarot*, this encompasses the outer courtyard where the outer *mizbeach* stands. *Hakohanim kemashma'o* — the *kohanim*, as the word implies. *Am hakahal* — *elu Yisrael*, the congregation of Israel. And the additional *yechapper* — *elu haLeviyim*, deriving a separate category for the *Leviyim*, who, once the *kohanim* are singled out, would require their own mention rather than being subsumed under *am hakahal*.

### Peshat vs. Chazal

This is a striking Gemara, because the *peshat* of the *pesukim* points in a markedly different direction from the interpretation adopted by *Chazal*. In *peshat*, the entire *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is oriented around the purification — not the personal atonement, but the metaphysical cleansing — of the *mikdash* so that God can continue to dwell therein. The language *vechipper al hakodesh mitumot bnei Yisrael* is about cleansing the sanctuary from the accumulated defilement caused by Israel's sins. Sin pollutes; it adheres, as it were, to the walls of the sacred space. Just as *velo titame'u et ha'aretz* — sin defiles the land — so too sin defiles the *mikdash*, and the ritual of *Yom Kippur* is a national, cosmic cleansing of that defilement so that the *Shechina* can continue to abide there. This is not about you and your relationship with God as an individual; it is about the *mikdash* and the conditions for divine presence.

*Chazal*, however, have shifted this entire frame. They interpret *vechipper et mikdash hakodesh* not as a cleansing of the sanctuary but as atonement for the *sin* of entering the *mikdash* in a state of *tumah*. Every location — the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the *Heichal*, the *mizbeach* — atones not for the defilement of that place but for the personal sin of whoever entered that location in an impure state. And this is quite a narrow category: it applies specifically to the case of *yedi'ah bit'chilah* — awareness at the outset — without *yedi'ah basof* — awareness at the conclusion — such that the person never came to know they had sinned, and therefore never had the opportunity to bring a *korban oleh veyored*. Rashi makes this explicit: this entire elaborate *Yom Kippur* service functions to protect those individuals from punishment *ad sheyivada lo veyavi b'olah veyored* — until they become aware and can bring their own individual atonement-offering.

This reframing is historically and theologically significant. The enormous collective ritual of *Yom Kippur* is, in *Chazal*'s reading, occasioned by one particular sin — *tumat mikdash* — under very specific circumstances. The shift from the *peshat*'s grand national and metaphysical cleansing to this narrowly defined personal transgression reflects a broader movement in *Chazal*'s thinking away from the *mikdash*-centric theology of divine presence and toward an emphasis on personal sin, personal responsibility, and personal atonement.

### Par vs. Se'ir

Within this framework, it is worth attending to the distinct roles of the *par* and the *se'ir la'azazel*. The *par* comes with a *viduy* — *vechipper ba'ado uvead beiso* — and this *viduy* relates to him and his household, understood by *Chazal* to include the *kohanim*. The *shechitas hase'ir* in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, by contrast, does not carry its own *viduy*. The *se'ir la'azazel*, on the other hand, bears the grand confession: *vehitvadah alav et kol avonot bnei Yisrael ve'et kol pisheihem lechol chatotam*. In *peshat*, the most natural reading is that the *par* and the inner *se'ir* are oriented toward the cleansing of the *mikdash* and the *kohanim* who serve in it — the *kohanim* being, in this reading, an extension of the *mikdash* itself, part of its *kelim*, sanctified alongside the sanctuary just as they were at the original *chanukas hamishkan* — while the *se'ir la'azazel* is directed at the sins of the people. The final *pasuk* *ve'al hakohanim ve'al kol am hakahal yechapper* would then refer to the *kohanim* being cleansed through the *par* process and the congregation through the *se'ir la'azazel*'s *viduy*.

### R. Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda reads the concluding *pasuk* — *ve'al hakohanim ve'al kol am hakahal yechapper* — as equating *kohanim* and *Yisrael* under a single shared *kapara*. The unifying force of this *yechapper* points, for Rabbi Yehuda, to the *se'ir hamishtaleach* as the single vehicle of atonement for *sha'ar aveirot* — the other transgressions — for both *kohanim* and *Yisrael* alike. The *par* and the inner *se'ir* handle *tumat mikdash vekodashav* specifically. The *se'ir la'azazel* handles everything else — all remaining transgressions for both groups equally.

This reading represents the thoroughgoing *Chazalic* shift: no longer a metaphysical cleansing of the sacred space, but a comprehensive atonement for personal transgression. One sin, *tumat mikdash*, receives special treatment; all other 612 are handled through the *se'ir hamishtaleach*. The strangeness of this reading, however, is notable: a single enormous ritual complex becomes, in effect, dedicated primarily to one out of 613 transgressions under narrow conditions, while the *se'ir hamishtaleach* absorbs everything else.

### R. Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He does not accept the equation implied by the final *yechapper* as mandating a single shared vehicle. Instead, he reads the structure symmetrically: just as the blood of the *se'ir* performed inside atones for *Yisrael* with respect to *tumat mikdash vekodashav*, so too the blood of the *par* atones for the *kohanim* with respect to *tumat mikdash vekodashav* — *keshem shedam hase'ir hana'asah bifnim mechaper al Yisrael betumat mikdash vekodashav, kach dam hapar mechaper al hakohanim betumat mikdash vekodashav*. And just as the *viduy* of the *se'ir hamishtaleach* atones for *Yisrael* with respect to *sha'ar aveirot*, so too the *viduy* of the *par* atones for the *kohanim* with respect to their other transgressions — *kach viduy shel par mechaper lakohanim beshear aveirot*.

Rabbi Shimon's position is fascinating on two counts. First, it establishes that even for personal sin — *sha'ar aveirot* — the *kohanim* have a separate and distinct mechanism of atonement from the rest of *Yisrael*. They do not share in the *se'ir hamishtaleach*'s atonement for other transgressions; their atonement for other transgressions comes through the *viduy* of the *par*. Why the *kohanim* would require a categorically different ritual of personal atonement than the rest of Israel is a question in itself, though it may be connected to their special status as active participants in the *avodah*.

### Blood to Viduy Shift

The second and perhaps more profound point in Rabbi Shimon's view is the radical reallocation of weight within the *avodah*. Notice what is doing the heavy lifting of atonement in Rabbi Shimon's reading: not the blood, not the elaborate ritual of the *matanot* in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, but the *viduy* — the act of confession. The blood of the *par* and the blood of the inner *se'ir* handle *tumat mikdash vekodashav*; everything else — the vast remainder of human transgression — is handled by *viduy*. The *viduy* on the *par* covers the *kohanim* for their other sins. The *viduy* on the *se'ir hamishtaleach* covers *Yisrael* for their other sins. It is not the sending of the *se'ir la'azazel* per se, not the ritual act of dispatching it to the wilderness, but the *viduy* pronounced upon it that achieves the atonement.

This is a complete inversion of emphasis. The *peshat* reading centered the ritual — the blood, the sprinkling, the cleansing of the sacred space. Rabbi Shimon has now placed the *viduy* at the center. The blood is for *tumat mikdash*; the *viduy* is for everything else. And a *viduy*-centered theology of atonement is precisely the theology that can survive the destruction of the *mikdash*, that can function without a *kohen gadol*, without blood, without an inner sanctum. The shift from *dam* to *viduy* is, as Rabbi Shimon articulates it, the conceptual bridge from the *Beit HaMikdash* to *teshuvah*.

## Separate Units…

### Eliezer/Shimon Debate

Returning to the practical law, a *baraita* now articulates the full structure of the debate between the *Tana Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon. The *Tana Kamma* holds that if blood spills in the middle of a unit, one returns to the beginning of that unit. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon hold: *eino matchil ela mimakom shepasak* — one picks up exactly where one left off. This applies within the *Kodesh Kodashim*, within the *Heichal*, and within the *mizbeach* service, as the *baraita* makes clear by rehearsing each scenario in turn.

### Shirayim Not Me'akev

The one point on which everyone agrees is that the pouring of the *shirayim* — the remnants — at the base of the outer altar is not *me'akev*. Even on *Yom Kippur*, even given the possibility that *vechilah mechaper* imposes a completeness requirement, the *halakha* as stated here is *divrei hakol lo meakev*: the pouring of the remnants does not invalidate the atonement if it is not performed or if the blood spills before it is done. This directly addresses the question raised at the outset of the sugya about the *shirayim* and its *me'akev* status.

## Restart Debate

### Single Pesuk

Rabbi Yochanan now offers a striking observation: both sides of the dispute — those who say one must restart at the beginning of the unit and those who say one picks up where one left off — derive their positions from a single *pasuk*. The verse is *midam chatat hakippurim achat bashana* — "from the blood of the sin-offering of atonement once a year." The word *achat* is the hinge; it can be read in two ways, yielding two incompatible conclusions.

### R. Meir Position

Rabbi Meir understands *achat* as modifying *chatat*: *chatat achat amarti lecha velo shtei chatatot* — I told you one sin-offering, not two. Each unit of the *avodah* may draw only on the blood of a single animal. If blood spills mid-unit and one begins again from the start, one acquires fresh blood from a new *shechita*, and the entire unit is performed with blood from one animal — no mixing of blood from two different animals. This is workable: each unit is a self-contained atonement, and within that unit the blood must come from a single offering. To pick up mid-unit where one left off, however, would mean using blood from one animal for the first portion and blood from another animal for the remainder — that constitutes two sin-offerings, which the *pasuk* prohibits.

### R. Eliezer/Shimon

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon understand *achat* differently: *chitu echad amarti lecha velo shtei chituyim* — I told you one process of sprinkling, not two. One may not duplicate the process; if one performs, say, four of the seven sprinklings inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* and then restarts and performs all seven again, one has created two overlapping processes of *chitu* — a repetition that the *pasuk* forbids. Therefore one must pick up where one left off: complete the remaining sprinklings from where the interruption occurred, ensuring there is only one continuous process. What does not concern them is whether the blood of the second segment comes from the same animal as the first; the prohibition is against duplicating the *process*, not against using blood of a second animal.

### Log Oil Case

An analogous debate is now introduced by Rabbi, citing what he received from Rabbi Yaakov, concerning the *log shemen* of the *metzora*. The purification process of the *metzora* involves a *log* of oil: the *kohen* sprinkles oil in the *Heichal*, then applies oil to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and toe, and finally pours the remaining oil on the *metzora*'s head. If the oil spills mid-process, does one restart at the beginning of the unit or continue from where one left off? Rabbi reports that Rabbi Yaakov taught him a ruling specific to the *log*, though the question arises immediately whether the oil case differs from the blood case.

### Metzora Oil

A *baraita* demonstrates that the same dispute applies to the *log shemen*: the *Tana Kamma* requires restarting from the beginning of the unit in which the oil spilled, while Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon say *mimakom shepasak hu matchil* — one picks up from the point of interruption. Across both units — the sprinkling in the *Heichal* and the application to the *behonot* (ear, thumb, toe) — the debate runs identically to the blood debate. The *baraita* thus demonstrates that what Rabbi Yaakov taught was not a distinction between the *log* case and the blood case — as if the *log* case were undisputed — but rather that Rabbi Yaakov *taught* the dispute itself in the context of the *log*. The Gemara accordingly emends: Rabbi did not mean that Rabbi Yaakov told him the *log* case is categorically different; he meant that Rabbi Yaakov transmitted the same dispute as applied to the *log*.

### Head Not Me'akev

The one point of universal agreement in the *metzora* case is that the pouring of oil on the *metzora*'s head does not *meakev*. The *pasuk* provides two distinct references: when mandating the application to the ear and thumb, it says *umiyeter hashemen asher al kapo* — "from what remains of the oil on his palm." Then, after those applications, it says *vehaNotar bashemen... yiten al rosh hamitaher* — "and what is left over of the oil shall be placed on the head of the one being purified." Rashi formulates this precisely: the applications to the *behonot* are themselves called *shirayim* — leftovers — since the verse describes them as *miyeter hashemen*. The pouring on the head is thus *shirei shirayim* — the leftovers of the leftovers. Just as *shirayim* at the base of the *mizbeach* are not *me'akev*, so too *shirei shirayim* — what is left after the *shirayim* themselves — are certainly not *me'akev*. The parallel to the blood service on *Yom Kippur* is precise: pouring the *shirayim* at the *yesod hamizbeach* is the counterpart to pouring the remaining oil on the *metzora*'s head, and neither is *me'akev*.

## Asham Paradox

### Asham Scenario

The Gemara now introduces a case that places the two positions in irreconcilable tension with each other. The *asham* of the *metzora* occupies a unique structural position in the purification ritual: the blood of the *asham* is applied both to the *mizbeach* — as with all *korbanot* — and then directly to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and toe, as a separate ritual act. Suppose the blood of the *asham* spills after being applied to the *mizbeach* but before the application to the *behonot*. One is in the middle of a process — analogous to all the cases discussed above — and the blood is gone.

### Meir: New Asham

According to Rabbi Meir's principle — *chatat achat*, focus on one sin-offering, and restart at the beginning of the unit if blood spills — the solution here is to bring another *asham*, *shecht* it, and start the process again from the beginning. Rabbi Meir's framework allows this: the requirement is that within any given process, one works with the blood of one animal; but one may obtain a new animal and begin anew.

### Eliezer/Shimon Stuck

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon are caught in a paradox. Their principle says *mimakom shepasak hu matchil* — one picks up precisely where one left off — and that one may not duplicate the process. Here, that would mean: bring another *asham*, take its blood, and proceed directly to the *behonot*, skipping the *mizbeach* application since that was already done. But this runs directly into the problem of *oto* — the Torah says *vehikriv oto la'asham*, implying one specific animal, *oto*, the very same one. One cannot use a second animal to complete what was begun with the first. And yet if one cannot use a second animal, and one also cannot go back to the beginning because that would mean two full processes of application, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon are left with *ein lo takana* — there is no remedy. The *metzora* is stuck in an incomplete state from which there is no halakhic exit under their framework.

### Chisda Challenge

Rav Chisda raises a sharp challenge: if the *pasuk* says *oto* — implying this very *asham* — then even Rabbi Meir should be constrained. How can Rabbi Meir say one brings another *asham* and starts again if the *pasuk* limits the process to *oto*, the specific animal already designated? The Gemara acknowledges the difficulty: *kashya*.

### Becomes Olah

A *baraita* is then cited that provides the resolution. If the *asham metzora* was slaughtered in a manner that disqualifies it for its designated purpose — or, in the *girsa* of Rabbeinu Chananel adopted by *Tosafot*, if the blood spilled before the application to the *behonot* — *harei ze oleh lagabei mizbeach vetatun nesachim*: the animal is brought as an *olah* on the *mizbeach* and requires the accompanying libations. The animal's blood has already been placed on the *mizbeach*, and the animal itself is consumed there as an *olah*. But *vetzarich asham acher lehachshiro* — a separate *asham* is still required to complete the *metzora*'s purification process. The force of *oto* thus applies to one specific aspect of the ritual; but the overall process of *taharah* can still be completed through a subsequent *asham*, resolving the apparent conflict with Rabbi Meir's position — though the precise mechanics carry us into territory that must be deferred to the next session.
