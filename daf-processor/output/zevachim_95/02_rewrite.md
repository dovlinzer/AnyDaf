# Zevachim 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood-Stained Garment

### Blood Left Azarah

The daf begins at the bottom of 94b, completing the discussion of the blood of the *chatas* and its laundering, before transitioning to the related laws governing vessels used for cooking *kodashim*. The opening Mishnah presents a straightforward case: a garment that absorbed the blood of a *chatas* must, as we learned in the previous Mishnah, be laundered *bimkom kadosh* — in the sacred precincts of the *Azarah*. If such a garment left the *Azarah*, the solution is simple: bring it back in and launder it there, as the Mishnah states, *nichnas umekabbeso bimkom kadosh*.

### Tearing Garment

The Mishnah then introduces a more complex scenario: *nitma chutz lekla'im* — the garment became *tamei* outside the *Azarah*. Now a genuine tension arises. On one hand, there is a *mitzvah* to bring the garment into the *Azarah* for laundering; on the other hand, one may not bring a *tamei* object into the *Azarah*. The Mishnah's solution is *koro'o* — tear it. By tearing the garment, it ceases to be a *beged* in the halachic sense, the *tumah* dissipates, and it may then be brought in and laundered in the holy place.

The immediate difficulty, however, is glaring: if tearing the garment causes it to cease being a *beged*, then what exactly is being laundered? The entire *mitzvah* of *lekabbes bimkom kadosh* presupposes an object that is *ra'ui lekabbel tumah* — capable of contracting *tumah*. A mere scrap of cloth that cannot serve as a garment is not subject to *tumah* and arguably not subject to this laundering requirement either. This question — which the Gemara will take up momentarily — is the crux of the ensuing analysis.

It is also worth noting a second puzzle that arises here: why does the Mishnah not simply suggest immersing the garment in a *mikveh* to remove the *tumah*, rather than tearing it? The later authorities address this: immersing the garment in a *mikveh* outside the *Azarah* might itself remove some of the blood, thereby constituting an act of laundering performed outside the holy precincts — precisely what the *halacha* prohibits. This explanation, cited in the name of the *Chok Nassan* and *Sefas Emes*, resolves the garment case, though as we will see, the analogous question about the metal vessel requires a separate treatment.

## Cooking Vessels

### Pottery Vessels

The Mishnah continues with the laws of vessels used for cooking. The parallel case to the blood-stained garment involves a *kli cheres* — a pottery vessel — in which a *chatas* was cooked. The Torah's requirement is that such a vessel be shattered in a holy place. If the vessel was taken outside the *Azarah* and then became *tamei* there, one cannot bring a *tamei* vessel back in. The Mishnah's solution: *nokvo* — make a hole in it. Creating a hole removes the vessel's status as a *kli*, thereby eliminating its *tumah*, after which it may be brought in and the shattering completed in the proper location, as the verse requires.

### Metal Vessels

For a *kli nechoshet* — a copper or metal vessel — the Torah's requirement is *merika ushtifa*, scouring and rinsing. If such a vessel left the *Azarah*, it is brought back and the procedure performed there. If it became *tamei* outside, the Mishnah prescribes *pocho'o* — making an opening or crack in it — which removes its status as a vessel, eliminates the *tumah*, and allows it to be brought in for *merika ushtifa* in the holy place.

Here too one might ask: why not simply immerse the metal vessel in a *mikveh* to remove the *tumah*? The *Chazon Ish* explains that immersion might wash away material on the vessel's exterior and thereby constitute a partial *shtifah* — part of the very cleansing process that must be performed inside the *Azarah*. The *mikveh* option is thus foreclosed for essentially the same structural reason as with the garment.

There is a substantial *Tosafos* here, and it is one of the reasons why this *daf* is relatively compact: *Tosafos* grapples with a deep question about what happens when a *tamei* object loses its status by being torn or broken. In general, when something *tamei* is rendered into a form that is no longer susceptible to *tumah*, the *tumah* departs. But there is a counterintuitive principle that an object can in some sense "touch itself" in the moment of transition, potentially acquiring a new, derivative degree of *tumah* even as it sheds the first. This complex analysis, while not the immediate focus of the *daf*, underlies the Gemara's ensuing discussion.

## Blood-Stained Garment…

### Challenge to Tearing

The Gemara now turns to challenge the Mishnah's solution of tearing the garment. *Ravina* raises the objection directly: *kero'o — beged amar Rachmana velav beged hu* — the Torah spoke of a *beged*, yet once you tear it, it is no longer a *beged*. The tearing solves one problem — the *tumah* — only to create another: there is no longer any *beged* upon which the *mitzvah* of laundering can operate. The Gemara's initial answer is *demashayer bah kedei ma'ofores* — you do not tear the garment completely in two; you leave enough material at the point of connection that the remaining strip could be used, for instance, as a small cloth for wrapping something. The garment is thus partially torn but not wholly dismembered.

### Middle State Solution

This answer introduces the key conceptual move of the sugya: a kind of middle state in which the garment has sufficiently lost its original identity for the *tumah* to depart, while retaining enough of the character of a *beged* that the laundering *mitzvah* still applies. The logic, following Rashi, is that since the majority of the garment has been rent, it is no longer the original *tallis* or garment that contracted the *tumah* — it has been transformed into something else, perhaps a kind of large cloth — and therefore the *tumah* of the original garment dissipates. Yet because the strip left at the point of connection is itself substantial enough to be used as a small cloth in its own right, the object still retains the status of a *beged* for purposes of the laundering requirement.

This is a striking conceptual position: the *tumah* goes away precisely because the object is no longer the original *beged*, yet the *mitzvah* still applies because it has not ceased to be *a* beged altogether. It is worth noting that this middle state is also what gives rise to the large *Tosafos* mentioned earlier: if it remains a *beged* — just a different one — why does the new *beged* not now acquire a derivative *tumah* from having touched the original one in the moment of transformation?

### Rav Huna Position

The Gemara then raises a further complication. *Rav Huna* is quoted as ruling that the tearing only eliminates the *tumah* when one does *not* leave behind a strip *kedei ma'ofores* — large enough to be usable as a small cloth. If one *does* leave such a strip, *chibur hu* — it is considered still connected, and the *tumah* remains.

At first glance this contradicts the Mishnah's solution entirely. The Gemara resolves the tension by distinguishing between biblical and rabbinic *tumah*: *miderabbanan hu*. Rav Huna's ruling that a remaining strip *kedei ma'ofores* preserves the *tumah* is only a rabbinic-level enactment — a *gezeira* lest people come to tear only a small amount and think the *tumah* is removed. Biblically, as Rashi explains, once the majority of the garment is torn, the *tumah* is gone, even if a functional strip remains. And since the prohibition against bringing *tamei* objects into the *Beis Hamikdash* operates at the biblical level, bringing in this partially torn garment — which is only rabbinically *tamei* — does not violate that prohibition. Meanwhile, the functional strip that remains ensures the object still qualifies as a *beged* for the purposes of the laundering *mitzvah*.

The conceptual elegance here is notable: the partial tear creates a zone where the object is biblically no longer the original *beged* (so the *tumah* dissolves), yet it is still halachically a *beged* of some kind (so the *mitzvah* remains operative).

## Cooking Vessels…

### Vessel Identity

The Gemara now runs a parallel analysis for the pottery vessel. The challenge is the same in structure: *kli amar Rachmana velo kli hu* — once you make a hole in the pottery vessel, it is no longer a *kli*, and so there is no longer a *kli* upon which to perform the *mitzvah* of shattering. The resolution mirrors the garment case: *shenikav beshoresh katan* — one makes only a small hole, the size of a small root. In the laws of *keilim*, different degrees of damage to a vessel affect its usability for different purposes. A small hole may disqualify the vessel for holding liquids while still permitting it to hold dry goods such as fruit. This partial diminishment is enough to strip the vessel of its original identity — so the *tumah* departs — while leaving it enough of a functional *kli* that the *mitzvah* of shattering still applies.

### Refashioned Vessels

For the metal vessel, the Gemara offers a different and arguably stronger answer: *deratzif mirtzaf* — after making the opening or crack, one goes ahead and hammers it closed again, refashioning it into a vessel. The *tumah* departed when the vessel lost its status, and now that it has been refashioned into a functioning *kli*, the *mitzvah* of *merika ushtifa* can be performed upon it.

This answer raises an interesting question that the Gemara itself does not fully address: if the metal vessel has been completely refashioned into a new vessel, is this truly the same *kli* in which the *chatas* was cooked? One might prefer the earlier logic — where the object retains some residual identity connecting it to its original form — over the logic of total destruction and re-creation. If a vessel is completely remade, it is in some sense a brand new *kli*, and one might argue that the *mitzvah* no longer applies to it in the same way that it applies to the vessel that actually absorbed the *kodashim*. The garment's "middle state" solution avoids this problem entirely; the metal vessel's solution, at least on its face, does not.

## Me'il Kohen Gadol

### No Tearing Me'il

The Gemara now raises the case of the *me'il* of the *kohen gadol*. Reish Lakish presents the question: what happens when the *me'il* absorbs blood and must be laundered, but then becomes *tamei* outside the *Azarah*? The solution used for ordinary garments — tearing — is unavailable here, because the Torah explicitly prohibits tearing the *me'il*, as the verse states: *lo yikarei*. So how is the *me'il* to be brought in for laundering?

The Gemara's answer is: *machnisa befachos mishalosh al shalosh umechabsa* — one inserts only a tiny portion of the garment at a time, less than three finger-widths by three finger-widths, launders that section, and then moves on to the next section. It is worth recalling from the earlier Mishnah that laundering in this context does not require washing the entire garment — only the spot where the blood is found. So the process involves working only on the stained section, inserting it into the *Azarah* in small portions.

### Partial Entry

This raises an immediate conceptual difficulty. The standard concern about bringing a *tamei* object into the *Azarah* is not obviously resolved merely by bringing in a small piece of it. After all, if a person is *tamei*, even inserting a finger into the *Azarah* constitutes a violation — we do not say "had the finger been severed, would it be *tamei*?" The finger is part of a *tamei* person and its partial entry is a problem. Why should the case of the *me'il* be any different?

*Tosafos* addresses this directly, citing the principle of *bi'ah bemiktzat* — partial entry — and whether it counts as full entry. *Tosafos* distinguishes garments from persons: because a garment is an inanimate object that *could* be divided, one may conceptually view the small section being inserted as if it had been separated from the rest of the garment and brought in as an independent piece. Once viewed as a separated piece measuring less than three finger-widths by three finger-widths, it would not carry *tumah* on its own, since such a small piece does not halachically function as a garment at all. Therefore, bringing in this tiny section is not considered bringing a *tamei* object into the *Azarah*.

This conceptual framework — treating a portion of a connected object as if it had been physically separated — echoes earlier discussions in the tractate about how one analyzes the parts of a *korban* that is partially on the *kevesh*. The principle is specific to inanimate objects that are capable of being divided.

### Conceptual Framework

To summarize the conceptual framework at work here: one cannot tear the *me'il*, so tearing is not an option. Instead, one inserts each tiny section — less than *shalosh al shalosh* — into the *Azarah*, and this small section is treated as if it had been detached. As a detached piece smaller than three finger-widths by three finger-widths, it would not be *tamei* at all, and so its entry does not violate the prohibition. The laundering of that section can then be performed in the holy place. One proceeds section by section until the stain is removed.

It may be noted that this process addresses only the *mitzvah* of laundering in a holy place; the *me'il* itself presumably remains *tamei* throughout this procedure. Only after the laundering is complete would the *me'il* need to be immersed in a *mikveh* to restore its *taharah* so that the *kohen gadol* could wear it again.

### Thick Garments

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* challenges this ruling. He cites a principle regarding garments that are particularly thick or soft — *ha'avin veharachin* — that the standard measure of *shalosh al shalosh* (three finger-widths by three finger-widths) does not apply to them. Because a thick or soft piece of cloth is not normally used as a patch or functional cloth, even a piece measuring three fingers by three fingers is insignificant; for such material, a significant piece requires a full *shlosha tfachim* by *shlosha tfachim* — three hand-breadths by three hand-breadths. The challenge to our case is therefore: since the *me'il* of the *kohen gadol* is a thick, finely woven garment, why must one work in such tiny increments of three finger-widths? One should be able to insert larger sections at a time, since for thick garments the threshold of significance is *tfachim*, not fingers.

### Connected Garment

The Gemara's response is *agav avihen chashivi* — because the small section remains connected to the larger garment, it is evaluated differently than if it had truly been detached. Had the section actually been cut off, one would correctly apply the *shlosha tfachim* measure for thick garments, and the detached piece would not be *tamei*. But since the section is still attached to the whole *me'il*, its significance is derived from the significance of the larger garment to which it belongs. Even a tiny three-finger-width portion of a significant, *tamei* garment partakes of that garment's significance and *tumah*. Therefore, one must limit each insertion to less than three finger-widths — using the most stringent measure — so that the conceptual framework of "treating it as if detached" can render it non-*tamei* even by the standard of thin garments, which is the more lenient measure.

The ruling thus rests on a subtle asymmetry: the smallness of the section is argued in one direction (it is small enough that, were it detached, it would not be *tamei*), while the connection to the larger garment is argued in the other direction (it is significant enough, by virtue of its connection, that we apply the smallest threshold). The resolution is coherent but strikes one as genuinely counterintuitive.

## Seven Agents

### Seven Cleansing

Having discussed *how* the *me'il* can be brought in for laundering, the Gemara asks a further question: *veha'ba'i shiva simmanim* — doesn't the laundering require seven cleansing agents? The halachic tradition, as stated by Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha, is that the blood of a *chatas* — and likewise the examination of *nega'im* — requires *shiva simmanim*, seven cleansing agents applied sequentially, each with vigorous rubbing. The Gemara cites the requirement that these agents be applied individually and in order: if they are applied out of sequence or all at once, the procedure is ineffective — *lo asa velo klum*.

The practical question underlying this discussion is broader than the *me'il* alone. Rashi understands the question as applying to the Mishnah's laundering requirement generally: what exactly is the mandated procedure? *Tosafos*, however, explains that the question becomes acute specifically for the *me'il* because there is an alternative cleansing agent — a kind of bleach or strong solvent — that can effectively remove blood stains without resorting to the seven agents. Ordinarily one might use this bleach, and the halacha of *techabes bimkom kadosh* would be satisfied with whatever effective method of stain removal is employed. The *me'il* is different: using a bleaching agent would remove its color along with the stain, which is impermissible. Therefore, the seven agents — which are more delicate and preserve the color of the fabric — become necessary for the *me'il*. And this is what makes the following problem acute.

### Urine Problem

One of the seven cleansing agents is urine (*mei raglayim*). And urine may not be brought into the *Azarah*. The Gemara already knows this from a separate context: the *braita* that lists things that may not be brought into the *Mikdash* includes urine, notwithstanding the fact that urine would have improved the aromatic quality of the *ketores*. So the same prohibition applies here. Urine is required for effective laundering but cannot be brought into the sacred precincts. This creates a real practical problem.

### Spit Solution

The Gemara considers and rejects several proposed workarounds. Perhaps mix the urine together with all the other agents before bringing them in? The Gemara rejects this: mixing all seven together in advance renders the procedure ineffective, since the *braita* requires each agent to be applied separately with individual rubbing. Perhaps mix the urine with just one of the other agents? This too fails, since each agent must be applied individually and the vigorous rubbing — *kiskus* — performed three times with each one.

The Gemara's resolution is: *ella demavlei lehu berok tafel* — add a small amount of spit to the urine. This admixture does not impair the effectiveness of the urine as a cleansing agent, but it sufficiently alters its character that it is no longer considered straight urine and may be brought into the *Azarah*.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish adds that this is in fact the standard procedure, not merely an ad hoc accommodation: *rok tafel tzarich shehei kol echad ve'echad* — a small amount of tasteless spit should accompany each of the seven cleansing agents as a matter of course. Thus the addition of spit is not merely a workaround for the urine problem; it is part of the normal method of applying each of the seven agents.

### Rambam Rejects

It is worth noting that the *Rambam* disagrees with this entire approach and rejects the use of urine in the laundering process altogether, holding that all the other agents are used but urine is excluded. This requires further examination, as the *Rambam*'s position appears to stand against the plain reading of the Gemara.

## Cooking Kodashim

### Cooking vs. Pouring

The Gemara now shifts from the blood on the garment to the cooking of *kodashim* in vessels. The Mishnah presents its next rule: *echad shebishal bo ve'echad she'irah letocho rotei'ach* — whether one actually cooked in the vessel or whether one poured boiling hot [liquid] into it, the requirement of *merika ushtifa* applies in both cases. This is a significant ruling: even if the vessel itself was never placed over a fire, if boiling hot *kodashim* were poured into it, that vessel is subject to the same cleansing requirement as one in which the *kodashim* were actually cooked.

### Kodashim Kalim

The Mishnah further extends the requirement beyond the *chatas*: *ve'echad kodshei kodashim ve'echad kodashim kalim te'unim merika ushtifa* — whether the *korban* cooked in the vessel was *kodshei kodashim* (such as an *olah* or *asham*) or even *kodashim kalim* (such as a *shelamim*), the vessel requires cleansing. Rabbi Shimon dissents: *kodashim kalim ein te'unim merika ushtifa* — the requirement applies only to *kodshei kodashim*, not to *kodashim kalim*. The Gemara will develop the basis for this dispute.

### Even Pouring

The *Tanna'im* in the *braita* derive the case of pouring from the verse itself. The Torah says *asher tevushal bo* — in which it was cooked. From the plain reading, one might have thought only actual cooking in the vessel triggers the requirement. The *braita* asks: *eira letocho rotei'ach minayin* — how do we know the rule applies when boiling liquid is merely poured in? The derivation comes from the additional phrase in the verse — *va'asher tevushal bo*, according to one reading, or *bo yishaver* according to Rashi's *girsa* — which broadens the application beyond literal cooking to include any scenario in which the *kodashim* were present in a hot state within the vessel. The Gemara will proceed to explore what this implies about the relationship between *bishul* (cooking) and *blia* (absorption) as independent halachic triggers.

## Irui Framework

### Oven Spit

*Rami bar Chama* poses a pointed question: *tala'o be'avir tanur mah* — what is the law if the *chatas* was suspended in the air-space of an oven on a spit, without touching the oven's walls? The oven is itself a type of *kli*. The meat is indisputably being cooked — *bishul* is occurring — but the taste of the *chatas* is not being absorbed into the walls of the oven, since nothing is in physical contact with them. Does the oven require *merika ushtifa* or, if it is a pottery oven, must it be shattered?

### Bishul vs. Blia

This question draws out a fundamental distinction between *bishul* (cooking) and *blia* (absorption). The case of *irui* — pouring boiling liquid into a vessel — presents the mirror image: the liquid was not cooked *in* that vessel (it was cooked elsewhere), but the vessel absorbs the taste of the *kodashim* as the hot liquid is poured in. So *irui* gives us *blia* without *bishul*. The oven-spit case gives us *bishul* without *blia*. Does either one alone suffice to trigger the Torah's requirement, or do both need to be present?

### Rava's Proof

*Rava* addresses the question by drawing on the *irui* case already established in the Mishnah. The Mishnah explicitly rules that pouring boiling hot liquid into a vessel requires *merika ushtifa*, even though no cooking occurred within that vessel. This demonstrates that *blia* without *bishul* is sufficient to trigger the requirement. What Rami bar Chama wants to know is the mirror image: is *bishul* without *blia* likewise sufficient? Rava notes that the *irui* case answers the half of the question that was not in doubt — *blia* alone suffices — but leaves open whether *bishul* alone, without absorption, also suffices.

### Metal Oven

The Gemara then cites a tradition of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *tanur shel mikdash shel matechet hayah* — the oven of the *Mikdash* was made of metal. The implication is that this was specifically to avoid the problem of having to destroy a pottery oven every time it was used for *kodashim*. A metal oven can be scoured and rinsed; a pottery oven would have to be shattered.

If *bishul* without *blia* were not a halachic concern, one could simply have used a pottery oven and placed the *chatas* on a spit without touching the walls — no absorption, no requirement. The fact that they deliberately constructed a metal oven suggests that *bishul* alone does suffice. However, the Gemara deflects this inference: *keivan d'ika sheirei menachos d'afiyasun betanur* — because the remnants of *menachos* were baked directly in the oven, making contact with its walls. For those items, the oven had both *bishul* and *blia*. Since the metal oven was necessary in any case for the baking of *menachos* — which involves direct contact — one cannot prove from the choice of metal that *bishul* without *blia* would have been a concern. The question therefore remains unresolved.

The Gemara's analysis carries significant practical implications for other areas of *halacha*. Most directly: if *bishul* without *blia* does not trigger the requirement — if what matters is absorption into the vessel's walls — then the widespread concern about sequential use of non-kosher ovens is at least halachically debatable when nothing physically touches the oven's walls. There are indeed significant *halachic* positions that hold that absorbed flavor in oven walls, without any physical residue, does not constitute a halachic problem when nothing is simultaneously present. The simple reading of this Gemara supports such a position.

The *irui* discussion also sheds light on the broader debate among the *Rishonim* — most prominently the dispute between *Rashbam* and *Rabbeinu Tam* — over whether pouring from a *kli rishon* constitutes *bishul*. The Gemara here appears to treat *irui* as a case of *blia* explicitly *without bishul*, which would suggest that pouring does not constitute cooking in the halachic sense. The vessel receiving the pour is not the instrument of cooking; the cooking occurred in the original vessel. Even granting that hot liquid might cook what it contacts at a surface level, the Gemara's framing is that *irui* represents absorption rather than cooking — and that absorption alone is the operative mechanism. This is, in any case, the straightforward reading, and it carries implications for *basar bechalav*, *bishul Shabbos*, and related areas.

## Fleishig Oven

### Permanent Fleishig

The Gemara introduces a new case. There was an oven that had been smeared with animal fat (*alya*, the fat tail of a sheep). *Rabba bar Ashi* ruled that this oven could never be used for baking bread. The reasoning: any bread baked in this oven will be permanently *fleishig*, and since bread is a dietary staple, *Chazal* prohibited *fleishig* or *milchig* bread categorically. The concern is not about any single meal but about the structure of eating habits: bread is eaten with everything, and if bread is *fleishig*, it will inevitably be consumed alongside dairy products.

### Hergel Aveira

The term for this concern is *hergel aveira* — habituating oneself to transgression. Even if one intended to eat the *fleishig* bread only with meat or alone, the fact that bread functions as a universal accompaniment means that inevitably, over time, one will come to eat it with dairy. Therefore *Chazal* did not permit *milchig* or *fleishig* bread at all, with certain limited exceptions where the bread's appearance or shape makes it obvious that it is not ordinary bread, or where a distinctive marking is placed upon it.

### Burning Out

The Gemara then cites a *braita* that appears to undercut *Rabba bar Ashi*'s ruling of permanent *fleishig* status: *ad sheyasik et hatanur* — until one burns out the oven. The *braita* itself prohibits smearing an oven with a fat tail and prohibits kneading dough with milk, declaring all resulting bread forbidden. But it adds this qualification: the bread remains forbidden only until the oven has been burned out from the inside. This implies that a pottery oven *can* be koshered — the prohibition is lifted once the oven has been subjected to sufficient internal heat.

### Oven vs. Pot

The Gemara now draws a critical distinction. *Ravina* asks *Rav Ashi*: if Rava bar Ilai's position has been refuted and we now see that pottery can be koshered by burning, why does the ruling of *Rav* require pottery pots (*kederot*) used for *chametz* on *Pesach* to be shattered? Why not simply kosher them by applying heat from within? The response draws the structural distinction: *ze hiseiko mibifnim veze hiseiko mibachutz* — when an oven is koshered, the fire is applied from the inside; when a pot is placed in an oven or exposed to external heat, the fire comes from the outside. Internal fire applied to pottery is intense enough to effectively kosher it; external fire surrounding pottery is not.

### Pottery Protection

The Gemara then raises the obvious counter: why not simply place coals or fire *inside* a pottery pot, just as one does with an oven? The answer is *chayas aleihu dilma pak'ei* — one is protective of pottery vessels and fears that they will crack or shatter. Therefore one will not apply the fire intensely enough from the inside to achieve genuine koshering. In practice, the owner's concern for the vessel will cause him to underheat it, rendering the procedure ineffective.

The ruling is therefore as follows: pottery ovens can in principle be koshered because the fire is naturally on the inside, and there is no structural obstacle to applying sufficient heat. Pottery pots cannot be effectively koshered in practice, even though in theory placing fire inside them would work, because the owner's protectiveness will compromise the procedure. Metal vessels, by contrast, can be koshered because metal conducts heat differently and is not subject to the same cracking concern.

The Gemara also briefly addresses a question that arises from the *fleishig* oven case: why does the principle of *nat bar nat* — *nosein ta'am bar nosein ta'am* — not resolve the problem? The fat goes from the oven wall into the bread; that is two degrees of transfer, which ordinarily suffices to render the bread *pareve*. *Tosafos* raises this question and offers two responses: first, the principle of *nat bar nat* may not be absolute, particularly in cases where there is a direct heating element involved; second, and more specifically to the oven case, the physical nature of pottery is such that actual residue of fat — not merely absorbed flavor — remains on the oven's interior surface. Where there is a physical film of fat remaining on the vessel walls, the bread is not merely receiving a twice-removed flavor transfer but is coming into contact with the fat itself, which removes the case from the ambit of *nat bar nat* entirely.

## Kasher vs. Destroy

### Contradiction

The Gemara closes on a question that will be taken up on the following *daf*: given everything that has been established about the possibility of koshering pottery ovens through internal fire, why does the Torah require the pottery vessels of the *chatas* to be *shattered* rather than koshered? We have now seen that pottery is not altogether beyond the reach of *kashering* — it is possible, at least in certain configurations. So why is shattering the mandated response for the vessel of the *chatas*, rather than some form of internal burning or heating? This question is left open, and the sugya will resume with it on the next *daf*.
