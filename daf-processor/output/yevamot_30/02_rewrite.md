# Yevamot 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Setup

### Three Brothers Setup

The Mishnah with which we begin deals with a scenario involving three brothers and two sisters. The Hebrew runs: *shlosha achim shnayim mehem nesuim shtei achayot ve-echad nasui achrit* — three brothers, two of whom are married to two sisters, and one who is married to an unrelated woman. For purposes of clarity, we will name the brothers Reuven, Shimon, and Levi. Reuven is married to Rachel, Shimon is married to Leah, and Levi is married to Devorah. Rachel and Leah are sisters, while Devorah is entirely unrelated.

### Three-Stage Sequence

The Mishnah then traces a three-stage sequence of events. Stage one: *gerash echad mibaalei ha-achayot et ishto* — one of the husbands of the sisters divorces his wife. Specifically, Shimon divorces Leah; a *get* is given and Leah exits the picture. Stage two: *u-met nasui achrit* — Levi, the brother who was married to Devorah, dies. At this point, Devorah falls before the surviving brothers for *yibbum*. Stage three: *u-kinesa ha-migaresh* — the one who had divorced, meaning Shimon, goes ahead and performs *yibbum* with Devorah, marrying her. Thereafter, *u-met* — Shimon himself dies.

### Devorah Permitted

The question now is what Reuven's status is with respect to Devorah. When Levi died, both Reuven and Shimon became potential *yevamim* for Devorah, and Shimon acted on that obligation and married her. Now that Shimon has died, Devorah once again falls before the remaining brother, Reuven. The Mishnah rules that Devorah is permitted to Reuven. The logic is straightforward: the potential complication would have been Leah, who is Rachel's sister and therefore an *ervah* to Reuven. Had Leah been present as Shimon's wife when Devorah entered the picture, Devorah could have become a *tzarat ervah*. But Leah was already gone — divorced by Shimon even before Levi died — so she was out of the picture from the very outset. With Leah removed, Devorah enters free of any taint, and when Shimon subsequently dies, Reuven may freely perform *yibbum* with Devorah.

The Mishnah's closing formulation, *zu hi she-amru ve-khulan she-metu o nisgarshu tzaroteihen mutarot*, captures the principle: when the *ervot* have died or been divorced, their co-wives are permitted. This is presented as an extreme case — the easiest possible scenario — because Leah was removed from the picture entirely before any of the subsequent complications even arose.

## Zika Debate

### Extreme Case

The Gemara immediately focuses on the fact that the Mishnah chose to present this particular, most straightforward scenario. The Gemara says: *ta'ama de-geiraish ve-achar kakh met* — the reason this works is that Shimon first divorced Leah and only then did Levi die. Leah was out of the picture before Devorah even became relevant to the discussion. That is why everything proceeds smoothly. This is indeed an extreme case — Leah was removed at the first step before anything else had occurred.

### Middle Case

But the Gemara now asks about a more complicated middle case that the Mishnah did not address: *aval met ve-achar kakh geiraish* — what if Levi died first, and only afterward was Leah divorced? In that scenario, when Levi died, Devorah fell before the brothers for *yibbum*. At that moment, Leah had not yet been divorced. What is the legal status of that situation?

The issue is *zika*. Even before *yibbum* is performed, the death of Levi creates a bond — a *zika* — between Devorah and each of the surviving brothers. If *yesh zika*, if that bond has legal force, then at the moment Levi dies, Devorah becomes, in some sense, linked to both Reuven and Shimon. Since Leah is still present and is an *ervah* to Reuven, Devorah would immediately become a *tzarat ervah* by virtue of the *zika* alone. This would potentially problematize Devorah permanently for Reuven, even if Leah were subsequently divorced and Shimon later died.

The Gemara thus reads the Mishnah's choice to present the extreme case as implying that had the order been reversed — Levi dying before Leah's divorce — there would indeed have been a problem. This implication favors *yesh zika*.

### Rav Ashi vs Rav Nachman

From this reading of the Mishnah, Rav Ashi draws an explicit conclusion: *zot omeret, yesh zika afilu bitrei achai* — from here we infer that there is *zika* even in a case of two brothers. The significance of specifying "two brothers" is that the force of *zika* might be thought weaker when there are multiple potential *yevamim*, since no single brother is obviously designated to act. Yet Rav Ashi maintains that even so, the *zika* is operative the moment the *yavam* dies, and it is strong enough to render Devorah a *tzarat ervah* with respect to Reuven.

However, the Gemara immediately raises the difficulty from the contrary position: *u-le-Rav Ashi kashya de-Rav Nachman*. Rav Nachman had maintained *ein zika afilu be-chad* — that there is no operative *zika* even with a single brother. This was derived from a different Mishnah.

### Nachman's Source

To reconstruct Rav Nachman's source, the Gemara recalls an earlier Mishnah in which Reuven and Shimon are both married to sisters Rachel and Leah, while Levi is married to Devorah. Reuven dies, and Levi — now the relevant *yavam* for Rachel — does a *ma'amar* with Rachel. Levi then dies. The Mishnah states that Shimon cannot perform *yibbum* with Devorah, because Rachel has been drawn into the picture through the *ma'amar*.

Rav Nachman's question is sharp: why does it take a *ma'amar* to create the problem? If *zika* alone were operative, the moment Reuven died and Levi had a *zika* to Rachel, that *zika* would already have made Rachel a kind of *ervah* — and Devorah, as her co-wife, would already have been problematized for Shimon. The fact that the Mishnah required a *ma'amar* to bring Rachel into the equation implies that *zika* alone accomplishes nothing. Hence: *ein zika*.

### Ashi's Response

Rav Ashi responds: *amar lekha Rav Ashi, hu ha-din de-af al gav de-lo avid ma'amar* — you are correct that even without *ma'amar*, the *zika* alone would have created a problem. Even without the *ma'amar*, Rachel was already pulled into the equation by the *zika*, and Shimon would therefore have been unable to perform *yibbum* with Devorah. The *ma'amar* was not mentioned because it was necessary to create the prohibition; it was mentioned for a different reason entirely.

Why then did the Mishnah specify *ma'amar*? *Le-afukei mi-Beit Shammai de-amri ma'amar koneh kinyan gamur* — to exclude the view of Beit Shammai, who held that a *ma'amar* effects a complete acquisition of the woman, equivalent to full marriage. According to Beit Shammai, if Levi performed a *ma'amar* with Rachel, Rachel might be considered fully his wife. In that case, Leah's status as a *tzarat ervah* might be considered complete and unambiguous, and she might even be able to walk free without *chalitza*. The Mishnah therefore emphasizes *ma'amar* to tell us not that *ma'amar* is necessary to create a problem — the *zika* alone already did that — but that *ma'amar* does not function as a full *kinyan* in the manner Beit Shammai would suggest. Even with a *ma'amar*, Devorah still requires *chalitza* rather than being freed automatically.

This is, as one might note, a striking feature of this tractate: the opinions of Beit Shammai carry more weight in the context of *yibbum* than they tend to elsewhere, and the Mishnah explicitly legislates against their view in a way that presupposes their position as a real halakhic possibility.

## Mishnah Exclusions

### Nachman's Exclusion

Having established the two opposing positions — Rav Ashi maintaining *yesh zika* and Rav Nachman maintaining *ein zika* — the Gemara now asks how each position reads the Mishnah. Specifically, the Mishnah's formulation sounds as though it is permitting a specific case while implicitly excluding other cases. The Gemara asks: *zu le-ma'utei mai* — what is the Mishnah coming to exclude?

There are, upon analysis, three possible sequences of events to consider in this scenario. Case one is what the Mishnah actually presents: Leah is divorced first, then Levi dies, then Shimon performs *yibbum* with Devorah, then Shimon dies. Case two is: Levi dies first, creating a *zika*, and then Leah is divorced, and only after that Shimon performs *yibbum* with Devorah. Case three is: Levi dies, Shimon performs *yibbum* with Devorah so that he is now married simultaneously to both Leah and Devorah, and only then is Leah divorced — before Shimon himself dies.

According to Rav Nachman, who holds *ein zika*, the *zika* of case two is legally meaningless. Whether Levi died before or after Leah's divorce makes no difference, because the *zika* does not draw Devorah into any problematic status. Therefore, both case one and case two are equally permissible. What the Mishnah must be coming to exclude is case three — the scenario in which Shimon was simultaneously married to both Devorah and Leah, and only divorced Leah thereafter. That case remains problematic. But why?

### Nishuin/Mita Debate

This brings us to an important underlying debate: *nishuin rishonim mappilin* versus *mita mappelet*. The question is how we determine whether a *tzarat ervah* situation exists. Is it determined at the time of marriage — *nishuin rishonim mappilin* — meaning we look at who was married to whom simultaneously and that determines status? Or is it determined at the moment of death — *mita mappelet* — meaning we look at who is in the picture at the time the *yavam* dies, and that determines whether the co-wife is forbidden?

If we say *mita mappelet*, then what matters is only who is present at the moment Shimon dies. If Leah was divorced before Shimon died, she is out of the picture at the critical moment and Devorah is permitted. Under this view, case three would also be permissible, since Leah was gone before Shimon's death — and there would be nothing left for the Mishnah to exclude, since every scenario involving any divorce before death would be acceptable.

On the other hand, if we say *nishuin rishonim mappilin*, then the fact that Shimon was married to both Devorah and Leah simultaneously — even briefly — is what counts. In case three, they were co-wives under the same husband at the same time, and that status, once established at the time of marriage, cannot be undone by a subsequent divorce. Case three thus remains problematic, and that is precisely what the Mishnah is coming to exclude.

The Gemara therefore concludes that Rav Nachman, who holds *ein zika* and thus cannot distinguish between cases one and two, must hold *nishuin rishonim mappilin* in order to have something left to exclude — namely, case three. As the Gemara puts it: *al korchach ke-Rabbi Yirmiyah sevira leh* — he must hold like Rabbi Yirmiyah, who maintained that the two Mishnayot reflect different opinions, one holding *nishuin rishonim mappilin* and one holding *mita mappelet*.

### Rava's Exclusion

The Gemara then turns to Rava, who maintained that it is all one *tanna* and that we always go by *mita mappelet*. Under Rava's position, any divorce before death resolves the situation — and under *mita mappelet*, cases one, two, and three are all potentially permissible. So the Gemara asks: *zu le-ma'utei mai* — according to Rava, what does the Mishnah exclude?

The answer depends on whether Rava aligns with Rav Ashi (*yesh zika*) or Rav Nachman (*ein zika*). If Rava holds *yesh zika* like Rav Ashi, there is one scenario that remains problematic even under *mita mappelet*: the case in which Levi dies, creating a *zika* between Devorah and the surviving brothers, and Shimon then dies without ever divorcing Leah at all. In that case — *meis belo geiraish* — the *zika* alone was present, and since Leah was never divorced at any point before Shimon's death, the *zika* made Devorah a *tzarat ervah* and she is forbidden. This is the case the Mishnah excludes.

Paradoxically, this is the case where Shimon's connection to Devorah was at its weakest — a mere *zika*, not actual marriage — yet it is precisely this weak connection that creates the problem, because Leah was never removed from the picture at all.

### Rava's Position

The Gemara makes the conclusion explicit: *al korchach ke-Rav Ashi sevira lei* — Rava must hold like Rav Ashi, maintaining *yesh zika*. If Rava were to hold *ein zika* like Rav Nachman, there would be nothing left to exclude. Under *mita mappelet*, case three is permissible because the divorce occurred before death. Under *ein zika*, cases one and two are both permissible. There is literally no scenario involving a divorce at any point before death that would remain prohibited. The Mishnah would be excluding nothing, which is impossible.

Therefore, Rava must hold both *yesh zika* and *nishuin rishonim* — wait, not *nishuin rishonim*, but *mita mappelet* with *yesh zika*. It is the combination of *mita mappelet* and *yesh zika* that leaves exactly one case to be excluded: *meis belo geiraish*, dying without having divorced Leah, where the *zika* alone was operative and Leah was never removed from the picture.

## Safek Cases

### Safek Principle

The Mishnah now shifts to a different type of problem. Rather than cases where the *ervah* is definitively present or definitively absent, we encounter cases where her status is uncertain. The Mishnah introduces the category of *safek kiddushin* and *safek gerushin* — cases where we do not know whether the *ervah* was ever fully married or whether she was ever validly divorced. The Mishnah rules that in all such cases of uncertainty, the co-wife performs *chalitza* and does not do *yibbum*: *choltzot ve-lo misyabmot*. This is because perhaps the *ervah* is in the picture, in which case *yibbum* would involve an *issur ervah*, while if she is not in the picture, the co-wife still requires *chalitza* or *yibbum* to be released from the *zika*. Since we cannot know, we require *chalitza* without *yibbum*.

As for the *ervah* herself — Rachel in our scenario — she requires no action in any case. If she was never truly married, she has no obligation; if she is genuinely married and therefore an *ervah*, she is forbidden to the *yavam* entirely.

### Safek Kiddushin

The Mishnah illustrates *safek kiddushin* with the following case: *zaraka kiddushin, safek karov lo safek karov lah* — he threw the ring of *kiddushin*, and we do not know whether it landed closer to him or closer to her. The operative principle of acquisition here is the four-cubit radius of personal domain (*arba amot*). Rashi describes the scenario where the couple stands exactly eight *amot* apart, so the precise midpoint between them is where their respective four-cubit domains meet. The ring fell at or near that midpoint, and it is unknown whether it came to rest within her four *amot* — effecting a valid *kinyan* — or within his. This constitutes a genuine *safek kiddushin*.

### Safek Gerushin

For *safek gerushin*, the Mishnah takes a somewhat different approach. Rather than offering a parallel case of uncertainty about whether the *get* reached her — which would be the simple structural parallel to the *kiddushin* case — the Mishnah instead enumerates three types of *gittin* that are rabbinically invalid but Biblically valid. The Mishnah calls these *safek gerushin*, though in truth they represent a distinct category.

The three cases are: first, a *get* written in the husband's own handwriting but bearing no witnesses' signatures (*katav bi-khtav yado ve-ein alav eidim*); second, a *get* bearing witnesses but lacking a date (*yesh alav eidim ve-ein bo zman*); and third, a *get* bearing a date but signed by only one witness rather than two (*yesh bo zman ve-ein bo ella ed echad*). The Mishnah's formulation is notably crafted for mnemonic continuity: each clause picks up where the previous left off — "it has witnesses but no date," "it has a date but only one witness" — carrying the reader from one case to the next in a fluid chain.

Each of these *gittin* is Biblically valid as a *get* but rabbinically disqualified. The woman is therefore in a quasi-divorced state, treated as half-and-half, which is why the Mishnah classifies this as *safek gerushin* and requires *chalitza* rather than *yibbum* for the co-wife.

### Missing Parallel

The Gemara notes a conspicuous asymmetry: *ve-ilu be-gerushin safek karov lo safek karov lah lo katani* — the Mishnah did not include the parallel case for *gerushin* of a *get* thrown and of uncertain receipt, analogous to the thrown ring in *kiddushin*. The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason?

The answer, developed in detail in the next section, relates to the principle of *chazakah* — status quo. A *safek get* of the thrown variety would not alter the woman's existing status. Since she was definitely married before the uncertain act occurred, her status quo as a married woman remains intact. The uncertainty about whether the *get* reached her does not disturb that status quo. The Mishnah therefore had no reason to include this case, because Rava holds that we simply leave her with her prior status. The cases the Mishnah does bring — the three rabbinically defective *gittin* — are categorically different and warranted inclusion for their own reasons.

## Chazakah/Status Quo

### Rava's Logic

Rava now articulates the general principle underlying the Mishnah's selectivity. The principle is *chazakah*: a person's legal status before the uncertain event occurred remains her status after it, absent proof of change. One does not alter legal status on the basis of doubt.

### Get Status Quo

Applied to the case at hand: if Shimon was definitely married to Rachel and threw her a *get* that may or may not have reached her, Rachel's *chazakah* is that of a married woman — *bechezkat eshet ish*. We do not change that status based on doubt. Rachel remains presumed married. Correspondingly, Devorah's *chazakah* before the uncertain *get* was that she was a *tzarat ervah* — forbidden to the *yavam*. We do not change that status either. Rava formulates it as: *ishah zo bechezkat heter lashuk omedet u-misafek attah ba le-osrah al te'asrennah misafek* — this woman stands in a status of being permitted, and you are coming to forbid her on the basis of doubt; do not forbid her from doubt. Everyone retains their prior status.

### Challenge to Logic

The Gemara immediately presses this reasoning: *i hachi be-kiddushin nami neima* — if so, apply the same logic to *safek kiddushin*. Before the ring was thrown, Devorah's status was that she was permitted to the *yavam* for *yibbum*. The question of whether Rachel was married is now a *safek*. Why not say: Devorah stands in her prior status of being permitted to do *yibbum*, and we should not disturb that status based on doubt?

### Chalitza Required

The Gemara answers: *hatam le-chumra* — there it is a case of being stringent. The possibility that *yibbum* would be a violation of *issur ervah* — a prohibition carrying *karet* — is sufficiently serious that we do not allow *yibbum* to proceed even though in principle the status quo would permit it. We require *chalitza* instead. This is a *chumra de-rabbanan*: go ahead and do *chalitza* and forgo *yibbum* as a matter of caution.

## Chumra Costs

### No-Cost Chumra

The Gemara now engages in a sophisticated cost-benefit analysis of rabbinic stringencies, and the discussion is genuinely striking. The question is not only whether a *chumra* is warranted, but whether it comes at a cost — specifically, whether it sends a misleading message that will lead to real halakhic harm.

In the case of *safek kiddushin*, requiring *chalitza* rather than *yibbum* is a no-cost *chumra*. Here is why. The true status, based on *chazakah*, is that Rachel was not married to Shimon — the *safek kiddushin* did not alter her prior status as an unmarried woman. Devorah's true status is therefore that she is permitted to do *yibbum*. We are being stringent and not allowing *yibbum*, but we do require *chalitza*. What message does *chalitza* send? It sends the message that Rachel is out of the picture — not married to Shimon. That is precisely the true state of affairs according to *chazakah*. The act of *chalitza* is fully consistent with the correct legal reality. 

Moreover, if someone were to observe the *chalitza* and draw the conclusion that *yibbum* could have been performed — and were to go ahead and perform *yibbum* — even that mistake would not be a serious halakhic violation, because Rachel really was not married to Shimon in the first place. The *chumra* of requiring *chalitza* instead of permitting *yibbum* is thus entirely costless: it is consistent with the true status, it sends the right message, and even if the message is misread and leads to *yibbum*, the *yibbum* would in fact have been permissible. As the Gemara puts it: *v'keivan de-kamatzreikh chalitza, midei yada de-chumra be-alma hu* — since *chalitza* is demanded, it is understood to be a mere stringency, and the right message is communicated.

Furthermore, the concern about the absence of *yibbum* sending a wrong message is misplaced, because people do not observe the absence of *yibbum* — they observe the presence of *chalitza*. And *chalitza* here says exactly the right thing.

### Why Not Get Chumra

The Gemara then asks the natural question: why not apply the same reasoning to *safek get*? If a *get* of uncertain delivery was given, why not require *chalitza* as a *chumra* there as well? After all, perhaps Rachel was divorced and Devorah therefore needs to be freed from her obligation.

### Wrong Message

The answer is that in the case of *safek get*, *chalitza* sends exactly the wrong message. The true status based on *chazakah* is that Rachel remains married to Shimon. The *safek get* did not change that. Devorah's status as a *tzarat ervah* therefore remains. Now, if *chalitza* is performed, observers will reason: why is *chalitza* being done for Devorah? The only reason *chalitza* would be performed is that Rachel is out of the picture. But Rachel is not out of the picture — she is presumed married. *Chalitza* here therefore communicates that Rachel was successfully divorced, which is false. And that false message has serious consequences: people may conclude that Rachel is now free to remarry, when in fact she remains a married woman.

### Status Quo Solution

The solution, therefore, is to go by the status quo without requiring any *chalitza* at all. Devorah retains her status as a *tzarat ervah* — forbidden to the *yavam* — and no *chalitza* is performed. Not because we are being lenient about the *safek*, but because requiring *chalitza* here would actively mislead people into thinking Rachel was divorced when *chazakah* says otherwise. The cost of the *chumra* is too high: it would generate a false message that leads to precisely the wrong behavior. As Rava's logic is developed through the Gemara, the rule emerges: *chumrot* are appropriate only when they do not themselves produce a *kula* — a leniency in the wrong direction. A *chumra de-atya lidei kula* is not a genuine stringency; it is a counterproductive measure that halakha rejects.

## Building Collapse

### Setup

Abaye now challenges Rava's principle of *chazakah* with a case from a different Mishnah. The new case: *nafal ha-bayit alav ve-al bat achiv ve-ein yaduah eizeh meihem meis rishon* — a building fell on a man and on his niece, and we do not know which of them died first. Let us reconstruct the case. Shimon is married to Rachel, who is the daughter of Reuven — meaning Rachel is Reuven's daughter and therefore an *ervah* to Reuven. Shimon is also married to Devorah. The building then collapses on both Shimon and Rachel.

### Uncertainty

The uncertainty about the order of death creates a halakhic dilemma. If Rachel died first, then when Shimon subsequently died, Devorah was the only wife in the picture — no *ervah* was present — and Devorah is therefore permitted to Reuven for *yibbum*. But if Shimon died first, then at the moment of his death Rachel was still alive and present as his wife, making Devorah a *tzarat ervah*, forbidden to Reuven. We have a genuine *safek* about the order of death and therefore about Devorah's status.

### Mishnah Solution

The Mishnah rules: *tzarasah choletzet ve-lo misyabbemet* — in a case of *safek*, Devorah performs *chalitza* but does not do *yibbum*. This is the standard response to uncertainty about whether *tzarat ervah* applies.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye now applies Rava's own reasoning. Before the building collapsed, what was Devorah's status? She was *muteret la-shuk* — free of any *yibbum* obligation that would preclude her from the world. Her *chazakah* was that of a woman who had no issue of *tzarat ervah*. Why, asks Abaye, do we not apply Rava's principle? Let Devorah retain her prior status. The *safek* should not change it. She should be free to walk away without *chalitza*.

### No Wrong Message

One might try to answer Abaye's challenge the way the earlier cases were resolved: perhaps *chalitza* is required here because it is a no-cost *chumra*. After all, in the building-collapse case, Rachel is not visibly present in the way she would be in a divorce scenario. People are not walking around saying "but what about Rachel?" So *chalitza* does not send the same kind of wrong message here that it would in the *safek get* scenario. Performing *chalitza* in this case does not make people think Rachel was divorced; it merely suggests that the Rabbis took precautions about an uncertain situation.

### Frequency Principle

The Gemara's resolution, however, operates on a different principle altogether: *gerushin de-shkhikhei gazru bohu rabbanan; mapoles de-lo shkhikhei lo gazru bohu rabbanan* — cases of divorce are common, and the Rabbis therefore legislated concerning them; cases of a building collapse are rare, and the Rabbis did not legislate for them.

The import of this distinction is as follows. The reason the Rabbis were concerned about requiring *chalitza* in the *safek get* case was not merely the wrong message, but the cost that flows from that wrong message in a commonly recurring context. If the rule were that *chalitza* is required whenever a *get* might have been given, the message sent by *chalitza* — that Rachel is divorced and Devorah can potentially do *yibbum* — would propagate widely, generating real harm across many cases. The frequency of divorce as a social reality means that such a policy would lead to systematic violations: people would perform *yibbum* with genuine *ervot* based on the misleading implication of the *chalitza*.

In contrast, a building collapsing on a husband and his wife simultaneously is exceedingly rare. The scenario practically never arises. Therefore, even if requiring *chalitza* in this case sends a somewhat ambiguous message, the Rabbis did not need to create a general policy of leniency to address it. The risk of mass confusion and systematic violation simply does not exist. The Rabbis can afford to be stringent — requiring *chalitza* — in a case that is so unusual that it will generate no meaningful pattern of misleading behavior.

The Gemara adds a further nuance. In the *safek get* case, people seeing a *chalitza* being performed will ask: why is *chalitza* being done for Devorah? Rachel is still around. This actively prompts them to reason backward to the conclusion that Rachel must have been divorced — and that conclusion will then lead to harmful behavior. In the building-collapse case, by contrast, Rachel's absence from the scene (she is deceased, one way or another) means that no one is asking pointed questions that would lead them to draw dangerous conclusions. The Rabbis therefore determined that the calculus differs: *gerushin* is common and the wrong message spreads dangerously; *mapoles* is rare and the wrong message, to the extent it is even sent, causes no systemic harm.
