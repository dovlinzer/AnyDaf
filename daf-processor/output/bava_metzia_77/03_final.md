# Bava Metzia 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Employment Scenarios

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא הָלְכוּ. אֲבָל הָלְכוּ חַמָּרִים וְלֹא מָצְאוּ תְּבוּאָה, פּוֹעֲלִין וּמָצְאוּ שָׂדֶה כְּשֶׁהִיא לַחָה – נוֹתֵן לָהֶן שְׂכָרָן מִשָּׁלֵם, אֲבָל אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה הַבָּא טָעוּן לַבָּא רֵיקָן, עוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה לְיוֹשֵׁב וּבָטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In what** case **is this statement said? When they did not go** to the workplace, i.e., the employer reneged immediately. **But** if **donkey drivers went and could not find** any **produce** to carry, or **laborers** went off to work **and found that the field was** too **moist** for tilling, the employer **must give them their full wages** to which they are entitled. **But** he does not give them the entire stipulated amount, as a donkey driver **who comes** back **loaded cannot be compared to one who comes** back **empty,** nor can a laborer **who performs work** be compared **to one who sits idle.** The employer deducts a sum from the laborers' wages, paying them the amount they are willing to receive given that they do not actually have to perform the work.

The seventh chapter of Bava Metzia, *Hasokher et Ha-umnin*, is concerned with the consequences that follow when either an employer or employee backs out of a work arrangement. The *baraita* at the center of this discussion covers three broad scenarios. First, if an employer cancels before the workers have arrived in the morning, the workers have a *taro'met* — a grievance — but no financial recourse. Second, if the workers arrive and find no work available, or are told upon arrival that their services are not needed, the employer must pay them for the full day, albeit at the reduced rate appropriate for idleness rather than labor. Third, if workers actually begin work and then leave mid-job, a *tannaitic* dispute governs whether they are paid pro-rated with no further consequence, or whether the employer may dock their wages to cover any additional replacement costs. Finally, in cases of *davar ha-aved* — an irretrievable or unrecoverable loss — the employer may even engage in a degree of deception, promising higher wages to secure replacement workers and then recovering that cost from the departing workers' pay.

### Three Scenarios

These three scenarios map cleanly onto distinct legal regimes. The pre-arrival cancellation, while morally objectionable, generates no monetary liability. The failure to provide work once the workers have arrived imposes full liability on the employer, subject to important qualifications about fault and foreseeability that the *gemara* will shortly develop. The mid-job departure triggers a *tannaitic* debate about whether the worker's right to leave is unconditional or whether it comes at a cost. And the *davar ha-aved* category introduces an exceptional level of employer protection precisely because the stakes of non-completion are so high.

### Tosafot on Garmi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הָאוּמָּנִין וְהִטְּעוּ אֶת בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, אוֹ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת הִטָּאוֹ אוֹתָן – אֵין לָהֶם זֶה עַל זֶה אֶלָּא תַּרְעוֹמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who hires artisans** or laborers, **and they deceived the employer, or the employer deceived them, they have nothing but a grievance against one another,** and no monetary claim.

Before the *gemara* proceeds to analyze these scenarios in detail, Tosafot raises a significant question regarding the pre-arrival cancellation. If I have agreed to work for you and, relying on that commitment, turned down other employment opportunities, have I not suffered a real financial loss when you fire me the night before or early in the morning? The case of *garmi* — a heightened form of indirect causation that generates legal liability under the principle attributed to Rabbi Meir — would seem to support a claim for damages. As Tosafot frames it: *im ken, amai lo yiten lahem ke-fo'el batel* — if there is genuine causation, why should the employer not at least pay the workers the value of their lost day, the rate applicable to a worker sitting idle?

Tosafot's answer is that the *baraita* is speaking of a scenario in which workers, even after being told not to come in, can still find alternative employment. *De-mairi keshe-chozer bo od yimtze'u l'histakar* — we are dealing with a situation where, if the employer withdraws before the day begins, the workers can still secure other work. There is therefore no actual loss from which a *garmi* claim could arise. And yet the complaint, the *taro'met*, remains — because *lo yimtze'u ela al yedei torach*, they will only find that replacement work through significantly greater effort and difficulty. Lost ease of opportunity, even without lost income, is sufficient to sustain a moral grievance, though not a legal one. Tosafot's broader principle is striking and important: where there genuinely is a real, concrete loss resulting from the employer's withdrawal, *garmi* liability would apply and recovery would be available. The no-recourse rule of the *baraita* is therefore narrowly construed — it applies only when the workers can realistically find other work in time.

## Land Inspection

### Rav Nasan Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: נוֹתֵן לָהֶם שְׂכָרָן מִשָּׁלֵם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲבִיבִי אָמַר, אִילּוּ אֲנָא הֲוַאי – לָא הֲוָה יָהֵיבְנָא לָהֶן אֶלָּא כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ נוֹתֵן לָהֶם שְׂכָרָן מִשָּׁלֵם!
>
> **Translation:** **A *tanna* taught** that *baraita* **before Rav:** The employer **must give them their full wages.** Rav **said to him: My uncle [*ḥavivi*],** Rabbi Ḥiyya, **said: If I were** ruling on this case, **I would give them only** the wages **of an idle laborer,** but no more, **and** yet **you said** that **he gives them their full wages?**

The *gemara* records that a *tanna* taught before Rav Nasan: *nosnim lahem s'charan mushlam* — when workers arrive and find no work available, you give them their full wage. Rav Nasan transmitted this in the name of his beloved, *chavivi*, referring to Rabbi Hiyya — the figure traditionally associated with the editing of *baraitot* and the *Tosefta* — and expressed astonishment at the ruling: had I been present, he says, *lo havi yahivna lehu ela ke-fo'el batel* — I would not pay them their full wage but only the lesser rate appropriate for a day of idleness.

### Rashi vs. Rambam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּסַיְּירַהּ לְאַרְעֵיהּ מִדְּאוּרְתָּא, הָא דְּלָא סַיְּירַהּ לְאַרְעֵיהּ מֵאוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It is **not difficult,** as **this** case, where Rabbi Ḥiyya would rule that the laborers are not paid at all, is referring to one **who surveyed his land the night before,** observed that it was fit to be tilled, and hired laborers on the basis of this examination. It is their misfortune that something occurred in the meantime to prevent them from carrying out the task. Conversely, **that** case, where the *baraita* rules that they are given some payment, is referring to a landowner **who did not survey his land the night before.** Since he failed to check his own field, he must bear the responsibility.

The *gemara* initially presents two explanations for Rav's apparent objection. The first is *lo simua kamei* — the *baraita* was not finished in front of him; he reacted before the clarification was read, not realizing that the *baraita* itself specifies that the payment is for idleness, not for labor. The second account, *ika d'amri*, proposes a more radical version: Rav heard the *baraita* in full and nonetheless insisted that in his view the workers should receive nothing at all — not even the *ke-fo'el batel* rate.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *baraita*'s ruling and Rav's stricter position by introducing a crucial factual distinction: *ha d'sayrei l'ara me-ortya, ha d'lo sayrei l'ara me-ortya* — did the employer inspect the land the night before or not? Here Rashi and Rambam diverge in a significant and consequential way. According to Rambam, the inspection refers to what the *employer* did. If the employer examined the field the prior evening and it appeared ready to be worked, and then an unforeseen event — a flood, for example — rendered it unworkable overnight, the employer has fulfilled his obligation of due diligence. He is blameless, and the workers must absorb the loss. If the employer failed to inspect the field the night before and therefore hired workers without verifying that work would be available, he bears a degree of negligence, and the workers must be compensated at the *ke-fo'el batel* rate.

Rashi, by contrast, reads the inspection as something the workers themselves are expected to perform. If the workers were brought to the field and had an opportunity to examine it but failed to notice that it was unworkable, that negligence is theirs and they walk away with nothing. If, however, they had no such opportunity, they are without fault and must be paid.

The gap between these two positions is significant. Under Rambam, the determining variable is employer negligence. Under Rashi, it is worker negligence. This means there exists a middle case — where neither party is at fault — that each authority would resolve differently. Where Rambam would find for the workers (since the employer did not inspect and is therefore negligent), Rashi might find for the employer (since the workers also had no opportunity to inspect and thus bear no fault either). The underlying principle is consistent across both opinions: liability tracks negligence, and the party whose fault caused the loss must bear it.

### Rava Irrigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּאֲגַר אֲגִירֵי לְרִפְקָא וַאֲתָא מִטְרָא וּמַלְיַיהּ מַיָּא, אִי סַיְּירַהּ לְאַרְעֵיהּ מֵאוּרְתָּא – פְּסֵידָא דְפוֹעֲלִים, לָא סַיְּירַהּ לְאַרְעֵיהּ מֵאוּרְתָּא – פְּסֵידָא דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת וְיָהֵיב לְהוּ כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** This **is like that which Rava said:** With regard to **one who hires laborers to till, and rain fell and filled** his land with **water,** preventing the laborers from performing the work, **if he surveyed his land the night before** and did all he could, this is **the laborers' loss,** as it is a consequence of their misfortune. But if **he did not survey his land the night before,** it is **the employer's loss, and he gives them** the wages **of an idle laborer.**

Rava develops the negligence framework through a series of concrete cases involving irrigation work. In the first, *hai man d'agar agirei le-rafka* — one who hires workers to deepen an irrigation ditch — and then rain falls overnight and fills the ditch with water, rendering the work impossible: if the owner inspected the field the night before, *paseida d'po'alim*, the loss falls on the workers. If the owner did not inspect it the night before, *paseida d'va'al ha-bayit*, the employer must pay them the *ke-fo'el batel* rate for the day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּאוֹגַיר אֲגִירֵי לְדַוְולָא, וַאֲתָא מִטְרָא – פְּסֵידָא דְפוֹעֲלִים. אֲתָא נַהֲרָא – פְּסֵידָא דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, וְיָהֵיב לְהוּ כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** further **said:** With regard to **this one who hires laborers to draw water** from a river or a trench to irrigate his field, **and rain fell,** so that he no longer needs laborers, this is **the laborers' loss.** The employer does not need to pay them, as he could not have known ahead of time that this would happen. But if **the river comes** up and irrigates the field, this is **the employer's loss,** as he should have taken this possibility into consideration. **And** therefore **he gives them** the wages **of an idle laborer.**

Rava's second case involves workers hired *le-davla*, to irrigate the field, when rain then does the irrigation for them: *paseida d'po'alim* — the loss is the workers' and the employer need not pay. However, if the rain came through the field's own irrigation ditch — *ata nahra* — the loss shifts to the employer. Tosafot explains the logic: a field equipped with an irrigation ditch will be effectively watered by even a modest rainfall. An owner who knows this should have anticipated the possibility that even a small amount of rain would eliminate the need for hired irrigation workers. The decision to hire workers under those conditions carries a degree of foreseeable risk for which the employer is responsible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּאוֹגַיר אֲגִירֵי לְדַוְולָא, וּפְסַק נַהֲרָא בְּפַלְגָא דְיוֹמָא, אִי לָא עֲבִיד דְּפָסֵיק – פְּסֵידָא דְפוֹעֲלִים. עֲבִיד דְּפָסֵיק – אִי בְּנֵי מָתָא, פְּסֵידָא דְפוֹעֲלִים. לָאו בְּנֵי מָתָא – פְּסֵידָא דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** With regard to **this one who hires laborers to draw water** from a river or a trench to irrigate his field, **and** the flow of the part of **the river** used to irrigate the field **stopped midday,** the *halakha* depends on the circumstance. **If it is not prone to stopping,** this is **the laborers' loss,** a consequence of their misfortune. If **it is prone to stopping,** then one acts in accordance with this consideration: **If** the workers are residents **of that city** and know that this might happen, it is **the laborers' loss;** if the laborers are **not** residents **of that city** and are not aware that this is a likely occurrence, it is **the employer's loss.**

The third case of this series involves workers hired to irrigate, where the irrigation ditch dries up halfway through the day. If this is not a recurring occurrence — *lo avid d'fasik* — the employer could not have been expected to anticipate it, and *paseida d'po'alim*, the workers bear the loss for the remainder of the day. But if the ditch commonly dries up mid-day — *avid d'fasik* — the employer should have foreseen it. Even then, if the workers are local residents — *b'nei mata* — they too are presumed aware of this local feature of the irrigation system and thus share the responsibility. In that case, *paseida d'po'alim*: the workers bear the loss. If, however, the workers were brought in from elsewhere and could not have known about this characteristic of the local infrastructure — *lav b'nei mata* — then *paseida d'va'al ha-bayit*, the employer must pay them for the lost portion of the day.

The governing principle throughout is foreseeability and the assignment of fault. The more the employer could have anticipated that work would not be available, the more he bears responsibility for the workers who arrived to find nothing to do.

### Half-Day Completion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דַּאֲגַר אֲגִירֵי לַעֲבִידְתָּא, וּשְׁלִים עֲבִידְתָּא בְּפַלְגָא דְיוֹמָא, אִי אִית לֵיהּ עֲבִידְתָּא דְּנִיחָא מִינַּהּ – יָהֵיב לְהוּ, אִי נָמֵי דִּכְוָתַהּ – מְפַקֵּד לְהוּ, דְּקַשְׁיָא מִינַּהּ – לָא מְפַקֵּד לְהוּ, וְנוֹתֵן לָהֶם שְׂכָרָן מִשָּׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** With regard to **this one who hires laborers to** perform a specific **task and the task is completed by midday, if he has** another **task that is easier than** the first one, **he** may **give it to them. Alternatively,** if he has other work that is **similar to** the first one in difficulty, **he** may **assign it to them.** But if he has other work that is **more difficult than it, he may not assign** it **to them, and he gives them their full wages.**

Rava adds another scenario: *hai man d'agar agurei le-avidta u-shlim avidta b'palga d'yoma* — one who hires workers for a job that is then completed by midday. If the employer has other work that is equally easy or equivalent in difficulty, *d'nicha minei* or *d'chvatah*, he may assign it to them for the remainder of the day, *yahiv lehu* or *mafkid lehu*. The worker cannot insist that he was hired only for the specific original task and refuse all other assignments, nor can the employer simply discharge him mid-day and pay only for half the work. The arrangement cuts in both directions: the employer has paid for the day and is entitled to direct the worker's labor for its duration, but only to tasks of commensurate difficulty. If the only available remaining work is harder — *d'kashya minei* — the employer may not compel it, and must instead pay the worker the full day's wage, *yahiv lehu s'char mushlam*.

The *gemara* then questions whether that full payment is truly appropriate in such a case — shouldn't the worker receive only the *ke-fo'el batel* rate for the idle portion of the day? — and answers:

### Achlushei Mechuza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רָבָא, בְּאַכְלוֹשֵׁי דְמָחוֹזָא, דְּאִי לָא עָבְדִי – חָלְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When Rava said** his ruling in this case, he was referring **to workers [*be'akhlushei*] of Meḥoza, who become weak if they do not work.** These laborers were accustomed to steady, strenuous work, and therefore sitting idle was difficult, not enjoyable, for them.

The full-pay rule applies specifically in the case of the *achlushei de-Mechuza* — the heavy field laborers of Mechuza. Rambam explains that for this class of worker, *de-i lo avdi chalshi*: if they do not continue working, their muscles atrophy and idleness is actually physically detrimental to them. Because being idle carries no benefit — and may even carry a cost — the ordinary discount applied to *ke-fo'el batel* does not apply. For such workers, the rate for sitting idle and the rate for working are effectively the same, and so they receive their full *s'char mushlam* for the remainder of the day. More broadly, the principle extends to any scenario in which a worker derives no benefit from resting — whether because of physical need, professional preference, or any other reason — in which case *fo'el batel* becomes equivalent to full wages.

## Mid-Job Departure

### Tanna Kamma View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: שָׁמִין לָהֶם אֶת מַה שֶּׁעָשׂוּ, כֵּיצַד? הָיָה יָפֶה שִׁשָּׁה דִּינָרִים – נוֹתֵן לָהֶם סֶלַע. קָא סָבְרִי רַבָּנַן: יַד פּוֹעֵל עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita*: The court **appraises for them that which they have done. How so? If** the current wage for the part of the task they have done **was** now **worth six dinars,** a *sela* and a half, as the price for this assignment increased, there are two possibilities: One is that **he gives them a *sela*,** as originally agreed upon, since they do not forfeit their stipulated wages. The Gemara explains: **The Rabbis hold** that **the laborer is at an advantage,** and therefore even if the laborer reneges on the assignment, he does not lose everything.

The *baraita* now addresses the case in which workers voluntarily leave in the middle of the job. *Amar Mar: shayminan lahem mah she-asu* — we assess what they have done. If they were hired for two *sela'im* for the whole job, and the remaining work would cost a dollar and a half — *hayu yafeh shishah dinarim* — the first opinion, the *Tanna Kamma*, holds that the workers are nonetheless entitled to their pro-rated dollar for the half-job already completed. *Kasavar rabanan yad po'el al ha-elyona* — the *Tanna Kamma* holds that the worker retains the upper hand. Even if his departure causes the employer additional expense because prices have risen since the original hiring, the worker bears no consequence.

### Worker Persuasion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ יִגְמְרוּ מְלַאכְתָּן וְיִטְּלוּ שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא: דְּאִיַּיקַּר עֲבִידְתָּא וְאִימְּרוּ פּוֹעֲלִים וַאֲזַל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וּפַיְּיסִינְהוּ. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצוּ אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: כִּי מְפַיְּיסִינַן – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּטָפֵית לַן אַאַגְרָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאָמַר לְהוּ: אַדַּעְתָּא דְּטָרַחְנָא לְכוּ בַּאֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states the second possibility: **Or they finish their work and take two *sela*.** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that this is the case? That is the sum they agreed on at the outset. The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where the price of labor increased** during the day **and the laborers rebelled** and did not want to work anymore, **and the employer went and appeased them** and they agreed to finish their task. **Lest you say** that **they can say to him: When we were appeased,** it was **with the intent that you would increase our wage,** the *baraita* **teaches us that** the employer can **say to them:** I appeased you **with the intent that I would trouble myself for you** by providing you **with** superior **food and drink,** not that I would increase your wages.

The *gemara* next considers a scenario in which prices rise mid-job and the workers, aware that they could earn more elsewhere, begin to signal that they may leave. *Ve-amru po'alim* — they back off, not yet quitting outright, but creating the impression that they might. The employer, needing them to stay, goes and persuades them — *ve-azal ba'al ha-bayit u-fayasinhu* — without making any explicit promise of higher wages.

### Employer Promise

When the workers agree to remain after being persuaded, the question becomes how to interpret the employer's inducement. One might think — *mahu d'teima* — that the workers can argue: *ki mefaysinan adaita de-tafit lan agra*, we assumed when you convinced us to stay that you were agreeing to raise our wages to the current market rate. The *gemara* teaches, however, that the employer can respond: *adaita de-tarichna lechu be-achilah u-ve-shtiyah* — I meant only that I would treat you better, provide nicer food or drink. I never promised more money. Accordingly, absent an explicit renegotiation of the contract, the workers continue to work under the terms of the original agreement and are not entitled to additional compensation simply because the market rate has risen while they were on the job.

This raises a further question about the case where the workers were hired at above-market rates to begin with. If the going rate for the whole job was a dollar and a half and the employer offered two dollars — *d'zal avida me-ikar v'agran b'trei zuzei* — and the going rate subsequently rises to two dollars, can the workers now demand two and a half, claiming the employer had promised to pay them a premium over market? The *gemara* says no. The employer can respond: *ki amri lach trei zuzei, de-lo hava kim l'chu* — when I promised two dollars, it was because I recognized that your work was genuinely worth two dollars, even though the market had not yet reflected that value. I was not committing to a perpetual half-dollar premium above the going rate; I was paying you what I believed your work was worth. Now that the market has caught up to that valuation, two dollars is exactly what we agreed upon, and that is what you will receive.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֶלַע נוֹתֵן לָהֶם סֶלַע. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא – דְּזַל עֲבִידְתָּא מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְאַגְרִינְהוּ בִּטְפֵי זוּזָא, וּלְסוֹף אִיַּיקַּר עֲבִידְתָּא וְקָם בִּטְפֵי זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches that if they performed work worth **a *sela*, he gives them a *sela*.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** the price of **labor was inexpensive at the outset and he hired them for one dinar more** than accepted, **and ultimately the price of labor increased and** the going wage now **stands at** that rate of **one more dinar.**

### Rabbi Dosa View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: שָׁמִין לָהֶן אֶת מַה שֶּׁעָתִיד לְהֵיעָשׂוֹת, הָיָה יָפֶה שִׁשָּׁה דִּינָרִים נוֹתֵן לָהֶם שֶׁקֶל. קָסָבַר יַד פּוֹעֵל עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches that **Rabbi Dosa says:** The court **appraises for them that which must** still **be done.** If the current wage for the part of the task they had not done **was worth six dinars,** i.e., he can find laborers who will complete it only for six dinars, which is equivalent to one and a half *sela*, there are two possibilities: One is that **he gives** the first laborers **a shekel,** which is equivalent to half a *sela*. The Gemara explains that Rabbi Dosa **holds** that the **laborer is at a disadvantage,** in accordance with the principle that whoever reneges is at a disadvantage.

Rabbi Dosa's position, which reflects the view of the *Mishna*, takes a more protective stance toward employers. *Shamim lahen mah she-atid la'asos* — we assess what remains to be done. If the workers leave mid-job and the remaining work would now cost a dollar and a half, the workers are entitled only to the difference: fifty cents for the work they have already done, since the employer must expend a dollar and a half to complete the job, and he had agreed to pay only two dollars total. *Kasavar yad po'el al ha-tachtona* — Rabbi Dosa holds that when the worker backs out, the worker bears the consequences. Any additional cost the employer incurs to complete the job is deducted from the departing workers' wages.

### Price Decline Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ יִגְמְרוּ מְלַאכְתָּן וְיִטְּלוּ שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּזַל עֲבִידְתָּא וְאִימַּר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וַאֲזוּל פּוֹעֲלִים וּפַיְּיסוּהּ. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַדַּעְתָּא דְּבָצְרִיתוּ לִי מֵאַגְרַיי. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אַדַּעְתָּא דְּעָבְדִינַן לָךְ עֲבִידְתָּא שַׁפִּירְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* states the second possibility: **Or they finish their work and take two *sela*.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** the price of **labor decreased** midday **and the employer rebelled,** seeking to cancel the agreement, **and** the **laborers went and appeased him** so that he would let them continue their work. **Lest you say** that the employer **can say to them:** When I was appeased, that was **with the intent that you would decrease your wages for me,** therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us that** the laborers can **say to him:** When we spoke it was **with the intent that we will do improved work for you.**

The *baraita* now introduces the flip side of the price-rise scenario, this time from Rabbi Dosa's more employer-protective perspective. Suppose prices have fallen mid-job, so that the employer could now hire replacements to complete the remaining work for less than the pro-rated contract price. The employer, tempted to let the workers go and hire cheaper replacements, signals that he might dismiss them. The workers, needing the income, come and plead with him to keep them on — *v'azu po'alim le-fayuso*.

When the employer relents, the question is what exactly the workers agreed to when they said they would "make it right" for him. One might think — *mahu d'teima* — that the employer can say: *ada'ata d'vatzirtu li mei-agrei*, I assumed you were agreeing to accept the new, lower market rate. But the workers respond: *ada'ata d'avdinan lakh avida shapira* — we meant only that we would do exceptionally fine work for you. Unless there was an explicit renegotiation, the parties remain bound by the terms of the original contract. The principle is symmetrical: neither side can unilaterally re-read an ambiguous expression of goodwill as a change in the agreed-upon rate. Explicit renegotiation is required, and absent that, the original contract governs.

The scenario also raises, at least implicitly, a question that the *baraita* never directly resolves: under Rabbi Dosa's framework, is the employer permitted to fire workers mid-job without consequence? Until now the *gemara* has been clear that an employer who turns away workers after they have already shown up must pay them for the day. But the structure of this scenario — in which the employer contemplates dismissing workers because prices have fallen — suggests that the answer may be less obvious under Rabbi Dosa's employer-protective approach. The *baraita* leaves this question open.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֶלַע נוֹתֵן לָהֶם סֶלַע.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches that Rabbi Dosa said: And if the current wage for the part of the task they had not done was worth **a *sela*, he gives them a *sela*.** 

Where the remaining work is worth the same dollar that it always was — the total job is two dollars, the workers did half, and the remaining half is still worth one dollar — even Rabbi Dosa agrees that the workers are paid their pro-rated dollar. They caused no loss, so there is nothing to dock.

### Rav Huna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן: לָא צְרִיכָא דְּאוֹזִילוּ אִינְהוּ גַּבֵּיהּ זוּזָא מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּלְסוֹף זַל עֲבִידְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where they reduced for him** the accepted price **by a dinar at the outset, and ultimately** the price of **labor decreased,** so that the standard wage became equal to the price they had agreed on.

Rav Huna son of Rav Natan identifies a further sub-case that arises specifically within Rabbi Dosa's framework. Suppose the workers themselves had originally offered a discount: the going rate for the whole job was two and a half, but they needed the work and agreed to do it for two dollars. *De-azulei inhu gabei zuzei me-ikara* — they went to the employer and offered reduced wages. The going rate then falls to two dollars. Can the employer now argue, from Rabbi Dosa's protective stance, that since the workers agreed to charge a half-dollar less than the going rate, they should now do the work for a dollar and a half, maintaining that same discount against the new market price?

The *gemara* says no. The workers can respond: *ki amrinan l'chu b'batzer zuza* — when we agreed to do the job for less, it was because *de-hava kim l'chu*, you were unaware that the work was worth two dollars. We knew the true value, we needed the income, and we agreed to accept two dollars because that was the fair price. We were not agreeing to give you a half-dollar discount in perpetuity. *Hashta kim l'chu* — now that the going rate has confirmed that two dollars is the correct value, that is what we are owed and what we contracted for.

The broader lesson is that protecting one party — worker or employer — does not mean interpreting every ambiguity in that party's favor. Both Rabbi Dosa's employer-protective position and the *Tanna Kamma*'s worker-protective position operate as context-sensitive frameworks, not as blanket rules of construction. The outcome in each case depends on what the parties actually agreed to and what their words, fairly understood, meant at the time.

## Rav's Ruling on Mid-Job Departure

### Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Dosa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי דּוֹסָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that same dispute in the *baraita*, **Rav said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa.**

With the *baraita* analyzed, the *gemara* turns to a striking ruling: *amar Rav halacha ke-Rabbi Dosa* — Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Dosa, establishing that when workers leave mid-job, their pay may be docked to cover the employer's resulting losses.

## Rav's Ruling

### At-Will Employment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב: פּוֹעֵל יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בַּחֲצִי הַיּוֹם! וְכִי תֵּימָא שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֵּין שְׂכִירוּת לְקַבְּלָנוּת, וּמִי שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rav** really **say that? But doesn't Rav say** that **a laborer can renege from** his commitment **even at midday? And if you would say** that **there is a difference for Rabbi Dosa between hired** work **and contracted** work, as a hired laborer can renege but a contracted laborer cannot, **is there** really **a difference for him?**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this ruling. Rav himself states elsewhere: *poel yachol lachzor bo afilu b'chatzi ha-yom* — a worker may withdraw even in the middle of the day. This principle is grounded in a *pasuk* from the Torah: *ki li bnei Yisrael avadim, avadai hem* — the Jewish people are servants to God, not *avadim la-avadim*, servants to other servants. The Torah's prohibition against reducing a person to slavery means that a worker must retain the freedom to leave employment at any time. If leaving mid-day will result in having one's wages docked or incurring financial liability, that freedom is illusory rather than real. Rav's ruling that workers may withdraw without consequence therefore seems to align with the *Tanna Kamma*, not with Rabbi Dosa.

### Sachir vs. Kablan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל, וְלַחֲצִי הַיּוֹם שָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּת לוֹ מֵת, אוֹ שֶׁאֲחָזַתּוּ חַמָּה, אִם שָׂכִיר הוּא – נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ, אִם קַבְּלָן הוּא – נוֹתֵן לוֹ קַבְּלָנוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who hires a laborer, and at midday** the laborer **heard that** a relative **of his died** and he has to tend to the burial, **or if** the laborer **was gripped with fever** and could not continue to work, **if he is a hired laborer,** **he gives him his wage; if he is a contractor, he gives him his contracted** payment?

One possible resolution is that Rabbi Dosa distinguishes between a *sachir* — a day laborer hired for a fixed period — and a *kablan* — a contractor hired to deliver a completed job. Rashi explains the conceptual distinction elegantly: a *sachir* is hired for a block of time during which he is, in a real sense, under the employer's authority and direction. The power dynamic inherent in that relationship is precisely what the Torah's anti-slavery concern is designed to address, and so the *sachir* must retain the unqualified right to leave. A *kablan*, by contrast, works on his own terms, delivers a completed product, and operates more like a party to an exchange than like a subordinate. He is already, in a meaningful sense, free — not subject to the employer's moment-to-moment control — and so the anti-slavery concern applies with less force. If a *kablan* abandons a job mid-completion, there is no ethical difficulty in holding him responsible for the resulting loss.

Under this reading, Rav could rule like Rabbi Dosa as applied to contractors, while simultaneously holding that day laborers may leave without consequence — and these positions would not be in conflict.

### Emergency Cases

The *gemara* tests this proposed distinction against a *baraita*: *ha-socher et ha-po'el, u'lechatzi ha-yom shama she-met lo met* — one who hires a worker, and at midday the worker hears of a death in the family, *o she-achazato chamah*, or he is seized by fever. *Im sachir hu, noten lo s'charo* — if he is a day laborer, the employer pays him his wages for the work already done. *Im kablan hu, noten lo kablanuto* — and if he is a contractor, the employer similarly pays him his pro-rated fee for the work already completed.

The point is not that the employer pays for work that was never done, but that the worker who leaves mid-job due to an *ones* — an unforeseeable emergency beyond his control — suffers no deduction from his wages. He is paid for what he did, and no more is withheld.

### Emergency Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִילֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, מַאי אִירְיָא שָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּת לוֹ מֵת אוֹ שֶׁאֲחָזַתּוּ חַמָּה דַּאֲנִיס, כִּי לָא אֲנִיס נָמֵי – הָא אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן יַד פּוֹעֵל עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי דּוֹסָא הִיא, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֵּין שְׂכִירוּת לְקַבְּלָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Whose** opinion does this *baraita* follow? **If we say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, why** does the *baraita* rule that he receives his full payment **specifically** in a case when the laborer **heard that** a relative **of his died, or if** he **was gripped with fever, where he was** unable to work due to **circumstances beyond his control? When** he is **not** compelled by **circumstances beyond his control** to stop working, this should **also** be the *halakha*. After all, **the Rabbis said** that **the laborer is at an advantage. Rather, is it not** correct to say that this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa? And** one can **learn from it** that **Rabbi Dosa** does **not differentiate between hired** work **and contracted** work in this regard.

The *gemara* asks: whose opinion does this *baraita* represent? If it follows the *Tanna Kamma* — who holds that workers may always leave without consequence and that *yad po'el al ha-elyona* — then the specification of death and illness would be superfluous. The *Tanna Kamma* would protect workers who leave for any reason, and there would be no need to single out emergency cases. The fact that the *baraita* specifically invokes cases of *ones* implies that ordinarily there would be consequences for leaving — and thus this must reflect Rabbi Dosa's position. Rabbi Dosa normally docks the pay of workers who leave mid-job, but carves out an exception when the departure is compelled by an unforeseeable emergency.

And since Rabbi Dosa's exception applies equally to the *sachir* and the *kablan*, this seems to demonstrate that Rabbi Dosa himself makes no distinction between the two categories. If he did, there would be no reason to state the rule for both.

This creates the difficulty: Rav rules like Rabbi Dosa, yet Rav also says a *sachir* may leave without consequence. If Rabbi Dosa himself makes no distinction between *sachir* and *kablan*, how can Rav invoke that very distinction to reconcile his two positions?

### Alternative Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּדָבָר הָאָבוּד, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The ruling of this *baraita* is stated **with regard to a matter that involves financial loss** if the work is not completed. Consequently, the employer is at an advantage, unless the laborer is compelled to stop working due to circumstances beyond his control, in which case **everyone agrees** that he receives his full wages.

The *gemara* offers an alternative explanation: *amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, be-davar ha-aved ve-divrei ha-kol* — this *baraita* is not within Rabbi Dosa's opinion at all. It is a case of *davar ha-aved*, an irretrievable loss, and under those circumstances all opinions agree that there would normally be consequences for departure — yet even then, the *ones* of a death in the family or sudden illness suspends those consequences entirely. Rabbi Dosa may well retain his distinction between *sachir* and *kablan* in ordinary cases.

## Land Sale

### General Retraction Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כׇּל הַמְשַׁנֶּה יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, וְכׇּל הַחוֹזֵר בּוֹ יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא כׇּל הַמְשַׁנֶּה יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, דִּסְתַם לַן תַּנָּא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אֶלָּא כׇּל הַחוֹזֵר בּוֹ יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לָאו לְאֵתוֹיֵי פּוֹעֵל וּכְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא?
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **Whoever changes** the terms accepted by both parties **is at a disadvantage, and whoever reneges** on an agreement **is at a disadvantage.** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** with regard to the statement: **Whoever changes is at a disadvantage,** one can understand this, **as the *tanna* taught us an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** indicating that this is the *halakha*. **But** concerning the clause: **Whoever reneges is at a disadvantage, what** does it serve **to add?** Does it **not** serve **to add** the *halakha* of a **laborer, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa,** who holds that workers may not renege?

The *gemara* turns to the *Mishna*'s general rule: *kol ha-meshaneh yado al ha-tachtona, ve-chol ha-chozer bo yado al ha-tachtona* — whoever changes the terms of an agreement has the lower hand, and whoever retracts has the lower hand. The first clause — regarding one who changes the terms — follows Rabbi Yehuda's position as discussed in Bava Kamma and need not be elaborated here. The second clause — that whoever retracts has the lower hand — has already been illustrated in the context of workers who leave mid-job and suffer deduction from their wages. Why, then, does the *Mishna* need to state it again as a general principle?

The *gemara* suggests that *kol ha-chozer bo yado al ha-tachtona* is extending the rule to an additional case: the sale of land. A field was sold for a thousand *zuz*, and the purchaser paid two hundred of them. *Bizman she-ha-mocher chozer bo, yad ha-lokeach al ha-elyona* — if the seller retracts, the buyer has the upper hand. And *yad po'el al ha-tachtona* refers here to the fact that the retracting party — in this case the seller — always has the lower hand.

## Rav's Position Reconciled

### Rav's Split Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי דּוֹסָא תַּרְתֵּי קָאָמַר, וְרַב סָבַר לַהּ כְּווֹתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא וּפָלֵיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Evidently, Rav's ruling does not accord with the opinion of Rabbi Dosa. **Rather, Rabbi Dosa is saying two** *halakhot*, **and Rav holds in accordance with his** opinion **in one** matter **and disagrees with his** opinion **in one** matter. Rav does not agree with Rabbi Dosa's ruling that laborers are at a disadvantage, but he does agree with him with regard to the manner of calculating wages.

Returning to the reconciliation of Rav's positions: the *gemara* ultimately resolves the tension by concluding that *Rav savar lah kavatei be-chada u-falig alei be-chada* — Rav agrees with Rabbi Dosa in one respect and disagrees in another. Rav rules like Rabbi Dosa with respect to the *kablan*: a contractor who abandons a job mid-completion has his pay docked to cover the employer's resulting losses. But Rav disagrees with Rabbi Dosa regarding the *sachir*: a day laborer retains the unconditional right to leave mid-day without having his wages reduced, in keeping with the Torah's principle that the Jewish people are servants of God and not of one another.

Alternatively, the *gemara* proposes *iba'is eima*: perhaps Rabbi Dosa himself does in fact distinguish between *sachir* and *kablan*, and the *Mishna*'s phrase *kol ha-chozer bo yado al ha-tachtona* is not directed at establishing that Rabbi Dosa applies his rule uniformly to both. Rather, it introduces the entirely separate case of the land sale, to which we now return.

## Land Sale

### Seller Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: ״כָּל הַחוֹזֵר בּוֹ יָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה״, לְכִדְתַנְיָא: כׇּל הַחוֹזֵר בּוֹ כֵּיצַד? הֲרֵי שֶׁמָּכַר שָׂדֶה לַחֲבֵירוֹ בְּאֶלֶף זוּז, וְנָתַן לוֹ מָעוֹת מֵהֶן מָאתַיִם זוּז. בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַמּוֹכֵר חוֹזֵר בּוֹ – יַד לוֹקֵחַ עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** a different interpretation of the mishna. The phrase: **Whoever reneges is at a disadvantage,** is not discussing employment arrangements, but is referring **to that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Whoever reneges** is at a disadvantage; **how so? If one sold a field to another for one thousand dinars, and** the buyer **gave him two hundred dinars** as a down payment, and then one of them reneged, **when the seller reneges on** his commitment, **the buyer is at an advantage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָצָה – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״תֵּן לִי מְעוֹתַי״, אוֹ: ״תֵּן לִי קַרְקַע כְּנֶגֶד מְעוֹתַי״. מֵהֵיכָן מַגְבֵּיהוּ? מִן הָעִידִּית. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁלּוֹקֵחַ חוֹזֵר בּוֹ – יַד מוֹכֵר עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה, רָצָה אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֵילָךְ מְעוֹתֶיךָ״, רָצָה אוֹמֵר ״הֵילָךְ קַרְקַע כְּנֶגֶד מְעוֹתֶיךָ״. מֵהֵיכָן מַגְבֵּיהוּ? מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, if the buyer **desires, he** may **say to him: Give me** back **my money** that I gave you as a down payment, **or give me land corresponding to** the value of **my money.** If you will not give me all the land as per our agreement, I should at least receive land in proportion to the money I already paid you. **From which** type of land **does** the seller **give** the buyer? **From superior-quality** land. **And when the buyer reneges, the seller is at an advantage:** If **he desires,** the seller **says to him: Take your money,** and if **he desires, he says to him: Take land corresponding to** the value of **your money** that you already paid. **From which** type of land **does** the seller **give** the buyer? Even **from inferior-quality** land.

When a field is sold for a thousand *zuz* and the buyer has paid two hundred as a partial payment, neither party has yet concluded the transaction fully. Either side may still retract. If the *seller* retracts, *yad ha-lokeach al ha-elyona* — the buyer has the upper hand and the right to choose the form of remedy. *Ratza omer lo: ten li ma'osai* — he may demand his two hundred *zuz* back, or *ten li karka kineged ma'osai* — he may demand two hundred dollars' worth of land instead. Furthermore, when choosing land, *me-heichan magbihu? Min ha-idis* — he takes from the best-quality land, since the wronged party is entitled to the most favorable selection.

If the *buyer* retracts, *yad ha-mocher al ha-elyona* — the seller has the upper hand. *Ratza omer lo: heilach ma'osecha* — he may return the two hundred *zuz*, effectively throwing the money back, or *heilach karka kineged ma'osecha* — he may offer two hundred dollars' worth of land. In the latter case, *me-heichan magbihu? Min ha-ziborit* — he gives from the poorest-quality land. In both directions, then, the party who was wronged — who kept to the deal while the other retracted — gets to choose both the form of settlement and, if land is involved, the quality of that land. The retracting party has the lower hand in every dimension.

### Rabban Shimon Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מְלַמְּדִין אוֹתָן שֶׁלֹּא יַחְזְרוּ. כֵּיצַד? כּוֹתֵב לוֹ: ״אֲנִי פְּלוֹנִי בֶּן פְּלוֹנִי מָכַרְתִּי שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית לִפְלוֹנִי בְּאֶלֶף זוּז, וְנָתַן לִי מֵהֶם מָאתַיִם זוּז, וַהֲרֵינִי נוֹשֶׁה בּוֹ שְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת זוּז״, קָנָה וּמַחֲזִיר לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁאָר, אֲפִילּוּ לְאַחַר כַּמָּה שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: We teach them from the outset not to renege,** so that the agreement will not be canceled and end in conflict. **How so?** The seller **writes** for **him** a bill of sale that states: **I, so-and-so, son of so-and-so, sold such and such a field to so-and-so for one thousand dinars, and of them he gave me two hundred dinars. And** therefore, **now he owes me eight hundred dinars.** In this manner, the buyer **acquires** the entire field, **and** the buyer **returns the remaining** eight hundred dinars **to** the seller **even after several years.** The remainder of the payment for the field has been transformed into a standard written loan.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers a practical solution to avoid this entire regime of retractions and their attendant complications: *melamdin otan shelo yachzoru* — one should structure transactions in such a way that neither party can retract at all. *Keitzad?* — How? When the two hundred *zuz* are handed over, a *shtar* is written immediately: "I, Ploni ben Ploni, have sold such-and-such a field to Ploni for one thousand *zuz*; he has paid me two hundred of them, *ve-hareini nosheh bo shmoneh me'ot zuz* — and I hold a debt against him for the remaining eight hundred." By treating the remaining eight hundred as a loan owed to the seller, rather than as an unpaid balance that leaves the sale incomplete, the transaction is rendered legally final from the moment of the partial payment. Neither party may thereafter retract. *U-machzir et ha-she'ar afilu achar kamah shanim* — the buyer then repays the eight hundred even if it takes many years, but the sale itself is irrevocable from the moment the *shtar* is executed.
