# Avodah Zarah 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nosein Ta'am Lifgam Deba…

### R. Shimon vs. R. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם וְאֶחָד נוֹתֵן טַעַם לְשֶׁבַח — אָסוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְשֶׁבַח — אָסוּר, וְלִפְגָם — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: **Both** in a case where the forbidden food **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the flavor of the permitted food, **and** in a case where it **imparts flavor that enhances** the permitted food, the mixture is **forbidden;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Shimon says:** If it **enhances** the flavor it is **forbidden, but** if it causes it **detriment** it is **permitted.**

The daf opens eight lines from the top of 68b, at the Gemara's question *hani telasa bavei dekattani lamma li* — why were all three sections of the *braisa* necessary? To understand this question, we must first recall the parameters of the debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir concerning *nosein ta'am lifgam*, the principle that a forbidden substance which imparts a bad taste does not render the food into which it falls forbidden. Our *mishnah* records that Rabbi Shimon holds *nosein ta'am lifgam* to be *mutar*, which is the source of many practical leniencies — particularly in cases where something is more than a day old. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, holds *nosein ta'am lifgam* to be forbidden.

### Pagum Me'ikara Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּגַם, אֲבָל פָּגַם מֵעִיקָּרָא — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says:** This **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon is with regard to a forbidden food **that enhanced** the flavor of a dish when it first fell into it **and subsequently detracted** from its flavor. In this case Rabbi Meir deems the dish forbidden, since the forbidden food enhanced its flavor at the outset. **But** in a case of forbidden food that **detracted** from the flavor of the dish **at the outset, everyone agrees** that it is **permitted.**

The Gemara has just established, based on a *braisa* supporting the position of Ulla, that the debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir turns on a case of *pagum me'ikara* — where the forbidden substance gives a bad taste from the very outset. This is the case where Rabbi Shimon holds it to be permissible and Rabbi Meir holds it to be forbidden even so. However, if the substance was *hishbiach u-levasof pagam* — initially its taste was good and only later turned bad — then everyone would agree it is forbidden, since the forbidden status of the food was established at the moment the substance initially improved it.

### Two Cases Compared

This understanding aligns with the earlier discussion in the *mishnah* about vinegar that falls on cold lentils and is subsequently heated: since initially the vinegar's contribution was beneficial and only afterward became detrimental, the food remains forbidden. The critical distinction, then, is between *pagum me'ikara*, where the bad taste is present from the start and Rabbi Shimon permits, and *hishbiach u-levasof pagam*, where the taste was initially good and later deteriorated, which all agree renders the food forbidden.

### Braisa Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׂאוֹר שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה וְשֶׁל חוּלִּין שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לְתוֹךְ הָעִיסָּה, בְּזֶה כְּדֵי לְהַחְמִיץ וּבְזֶה כְּדֵי לְהַחְמִיץ וְחִימְּצוּ — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. נָפַל שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה תְּחִלָּה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר. נָפַל שֶׁל חוּלִּין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָפַל שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה אוֹ שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another source for the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Come** and **hear** that which is taught in a *baraita*: In the case of **leaven of *teruma* and non-sacred** leaven **that fell into the dough,** if **this one** was **sufficient to cause** the dough **to become leavened and that one** was **sufficient to cause** the dough **to become leavened, and they** both **cause** the dough **to become leavened,** it is **forbidden. Rabbi Shimon deems** it **permitted.** If the leaven **of *teruma* fell** in **first, everyone agrees** that it is **forbidden.** If the **non-sacred** leaven **fell** in first **and subsequently** the leaven **of *teruma* or of diverse kinds** that were planted **in a vineyard fell** in, this is also **forbidden; but Rabbi Shimon deems** it **permitted.**

The *braisa* in question contains three sections. The final section deals with forbidden wine or vinegar that fell on lentils, demonstrating that the debate of Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir applies to *pagum me'ikara* — even when the taste is bad from the beginning, Rabbi Meir holds it forbidden. The middle section deals with *se'or* — yeast — of *terumah* falling into dough, followed by additional yeast: since the *terumah* yeast initially improved the dough, its status was established at that moment, and even Rabbi Shimon agrees it is forbidden. Together, these two sections provide the full picture: *pagum me'ikara* is the disputed case, and *hishbiach u-levasof pagam* is agreed by all to be forbidden.

### Yeast Two-Stage Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָה, דִּפְגַם מֵעִיקָּרָא הוּא, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Amram comments: **But here it is** a case **where** the forbidden substance **detracted** from the flavor of the dough **at the outset, and** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon **disagree,** as a mishna that is not attributed explicitly to a *tanna* represents the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

Given that, the Gemara asks: *ella reisha lamma li* — why was the first section of the *braisa* needed at all? The first section addresses a case where *se'or* of *terumah* and *se'or* of *chullin* fell into the dough simultaneously, resulting in over-fermentation from the outset. That appears to be simply another case of *pagum me'ikara*. The Gemara, however, notes an important nuance: over-yeasted dough, while not ideal for bread, could be used as sourdough starter. So this is not a pure case of *pagum me'ikara* with nothing but bad taste — there is also an element of benefit. The Gemara then presses the point further: if in the final section, where there is no benefit at all, Rabbi Meir already holds it to be forbidden, then obviously Rabbi Meir would hold the over-yeast case — where there is at least some benefit — to be forbidden as well. And by the same token, since that case is bad from the beginning (*pagum me'ikara*), why would Rabbi Shimon's lenient ruling here not follow automatically from what we already know? What does the first section add?

### Reisha Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רֵישָׁא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִצְטְרִיךְ, וְהָכִי קָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: עִיסָּה זוֹ רְאוּיָה לְהַחְמִיץ בִּשְׁתֵּי שָׁעוֹת, מִי גָּרַם לָהּ שֶׁתַּחֲמִיץ בְּשָׁעָה אַחַת? אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: The first clause is necessary to** teach that **Rabbi Shimon** deems it permitted. **And this** is what **the Rabbis said to Rabbi Shimon: This dough was fit to become leavened in two hours** if the permitted leaven had fallen into it alone. **What caused it to become leavened in one hour?** The **forbidden** leaven. Therefore, the dough is forbidden.

The Gemara answers: *reisha amar Abaye reisha le-Rebbi Shimon itztreech* — the first section was needed for Rabbi Shimon's ruling. It might not have been obvious that Rabbi Shimon would permit this case, and one might have thought he would agree with Rabbi Meir here. It is to forestall that possibility that the first section was required.

## Abaye's Explanation

### Initial Benefit Frame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? כְּשֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחוּ, שְׁנֵיהֶם הִשְׁבִּיחוּ; כְּשֶׁפָּגְמוּ, שְׁנֵיהֶם פָּגְמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon** could respond that **when** the two types of leaven **enhanced** the flavor of the dough, **they both enhanced** it, not only the forbidden leaven; and **when they** subsequently **detracted** from it, **they both detracted** from it. Therefore, it is permitted.

Abaye frames the matter as follows: the case of the over-yeasted dough is one where there is an argument that the forbidden *se'or* contributed something positive before ultimately becoming detrimental — a possible instance of *hishbiach u-levasof pagam*.

### Fermentation Speed

The argument that Rabbi Meir would press against Rabbi Shimon runs as follows: *issa zu reuyah lehachmitz b'shtei sha'os mi garam lah shetachmitz b'sha'ah achas issur* — this dough would have fermented in two hours on its own, but the forbidden yeast caused it to ferment in one. The effect of the *issur* was therefore initially an improvement — it sped up the fermentation — and only later, when the dough became over-fermented, did the harmful effect manifest. On this analysis, the forbidden yeast was initially *mashbiach*, and the case should be treated as *hishbiach u-levasof pagam*, making it forbidden according to everyone, including Rabbi Shimon.

### Shimon's Combo Reply

Rabbi Shimon's response, as framed by Abaye, is: *keshehishbichu sheneihem hishbichu, keshepigu sheneihem pagmu* — when it improved, both of them improved together; when it deteriorated, both of them deteriorated together. The *issur* did not make it better on its own; it made it better only in combination with the *hetter*. Since the forbidden yeast was not acting alone in producing the initial improvement, it cannot be characterized as the *issur* making it *hishbiach* — and therefore the case cannot be framed as *hishbiach u-levasof pagam*. The status remains one of *pagum me'ikara*, and Rabbi Shimon permits it.

It must be acknowledged that this answer is not entirely clear. If we are already conceding that there were two stages — an initial period of improvement followed by deterioration — it is difficult to see why the fact that the *issur* did not act alone suffices to strip away the character of *hishbiach u-levasof pagam*. Rashi explains *she'ne-hem hispichu ve-lo ha'issur levado hispicho* — both improved and not the *issur* alone — but this alone does not resolve the difficulty: even if the *issur* contributed to the improvement jointly, the food still tasted good at first and only bad later, which would seem to trigger the *hishbiach u-levasof pagam* rule. A more satisfying answer may be that from the very outset, the dough had too much yeast, and the mixture was never fully good — it always had the character of excess and was always going to be problematic. In any event, the conclusion stands: the debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir applies to *pagum me'ikara*, and we rule accordingly that *nosein ta'am lifgam* is *mutar*.

## Tziruf & Combination

### Combination Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לִיצְטָרֵף הֶיתֵּר וְאִיסּוּר בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי וְלִיתְּסַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Even **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, let** the **permitted** leaven **and** the **forbidden** leaven **combine together and render** the dough **forbidden,** since Rabbi Shimon concedes that if the permitted food was initially enhanced by the forbidden substance it is forbidden.

Having explained Rabbi Shimon's position, the Gemara raises a further challenge. If we accept Abaye's framing — that there were two phases, an initial improvement and a subsequent deterioration — then why can we not say that even according to Rabbi Shimon, the permitted and forbidden yeast should combine to constitute a meaningful *hishbiach*? The Gemara asks: *Rebbi Shimon litzterof hetter ve'issur bahadei hadadi* — why should Rabbi Shimon not hold that the *hetter* and *issur* combine to produce a status of *hishbiach*?

### Shimon's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אִיסּוּר וְאִיסּוּר נָמֵי לָא מִיצְטָרְפִי, דִּתְנַן: הָעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם מִצְטָרְפִין, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** that **even a forbidden** substance **and** another **forbidden** substance **do not combine** to render a mixture forbidden. Accordingly, a forbidden substance and a permitted substance certainly do not. The Gemara comments: This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 18a): If ***orla* and diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard** fell into a permitted substance, and neither one is sufficient in its own quantity to render the mixture forbidden, **they combine** to render it forbidden if together they are of a sufficient quantity. **Rabbi Shimon says: They do not combine,** and each forbidden substance is treated individually. Here too, since when the forbidden leaven enhanced the dough, it was not sufficient to enhance it by itself, the permitted leaven does not combine with it to render the dough forbidden.

The Gemara answers that Rabbi Shimon goes according to his well-known principle: *afilu issur ve'issur lo mitstarfe* — even two separate forbidden items do not combine with each other if each one individually is insufficient. If Rabbi Shimon holds that even two items of *issur* do not combine for this purpose, then certainly a forbidden item and a permitted item would not combine.

### Orlah & Kilai

The source for this principle is a *mishnah*: *ha-orlah ve-khilai ha-kerem mitstarfin, Rebbi Shimon omer ein mitstarfin*. The case is where one has half a *kezayit* of *orlah* — grapes from a vine in its first three years — and half a *kezayit* of *kilai ha-kerem* — grapes that had grown in a vineyard planted with mixed species. The *tanna kamma* holds that these two half-*kezaytim* combine to constitute a full *kezayit* of forbidden grapes, such that sixty *kezaytim* of permissible food would be needed to render them *batel*. With only fifty-nine, the mixture retains the taste of forbidden grapes and remains forbidden. Rabbi Shimon disagrees: each of the two forbidden items operates independently, and each is *batel* on its own. From this ruling it follows that Rabbi Shimon does not recognize combination between two separate *issurim*, let alone between an *issur* and a *hetter*. The first section of the *braisa* was therefore necessary to teach us that even Rabbi Shimon permits the over-yeasted dough, since it is his view that combination between the permitted and forbidden yeast does not suffice to create a status of *hishbiach*.

### Tosafot Alternative

Tosafot raises an important conceptual observation. Rather than framing the question in terms of *tziruf* — combination — one might have framed it as a question of *zeh ve-zeh gorem*, mutual causation: when two contributing factors each play a role, does each one's involvement count as halachically significant even though neither acts alone? Tosafot responds with a key distinction: the question of *zeh ve-zeh gorem* properly arises when each factor is individually insufficient — when each one is necessary but not independently sufficient, and together they bring about the result. In that circumstance one could argue either that no single cause is meaningful, or that they operate as genuine partners. That is the classic *zeh ve-zeh gorem* question. Here, however, since either piece of yeast — the *terumah* or the *chullin* — would on its own have been capable of leavening the dough, the case does not present a true *zeh ve-zeh gorem* situation. Tosafot concludes that when each factor is sufficient on its own, there is actually stronger reason to regard the *issur* as significant — you could remove the other and the *issur* alone would have accomplished the result. Nevertheless, the Gemara chooses to frame the question through the lens of *tziruf* rather than *zeh ve-zeh gorem*.

## Rat in Beer Case

### Rav Forbids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא עַכְבְּרָא דִּנְפַל לְחָבִיתָא דְּשִׁיכְרָא, אַסְרֵיהּ רַב לְהָהוּא שִׁיכְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara recounts an incident involving **a certain mouse that fell into a barrel of beer. Rav deemed that** barrel of **beer forbidden.**

The Gemara now introduces a new case: *hahu akhbra de-nafal le-chavita de-shikhra* — a rat that fell into a barrel of beer (or mead). Rav ruled that the beer is forbidden. Tosafot raises the preliminary question of how the taste of the rat could have transferred to the cold beer, and suggests either that it sat in the liquid long enough for the taste to seep out — implicating the principle of *kavush ke-mevushal*, the debate among the *rishonim* about whether extended immersion in a cold liquid suffices to transfer taste — or perhaps the incident occurred while the beer was being processed and the liquid was still warm.

### Questioning Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: נֵימָא קָסָבַר נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said before Rav Sheshet: Shall we say** that Rav **maintains** that even in a case where the forbidden substance **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **forbidden?** Presumably, the mouse imparted flavor to the detriment of the beer.

Upon hearing Rav's ruling, the Rabbis posed the question to Rav Sheshes: *neima kasavar nosein ta'am lifgam assur* — does this mean Rav holds that *nosein ta'am lifgam* is forbidden? A rat, after all, is naturally disgusting, and its taste in the beer would be a bad one. If Rav forbids even this, it would seem to indicate that he rules like Rabbi Meir against Rabbi Shimon on the fundamental question.

### Rat Disgust Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בְּעָלְמָא סָבַר רַב נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם מוּתָּר, וְהָכָא חִידּוּשׁ הוּא, דְּהָא מִימְאָס מְאִיס וּבְדִילִי אִינָשֵׁי מִינֵּיהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי אַסְרֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא. הִלְכָּךְ נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם נָמֵי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said to them: Rav generally maintains** that in a case where the forbidden substance **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **permitted. But here,** in the case of a mouse, **it is a novelty** that the Torah prohibits the flavor from a mouse at all, **as it is repulsive and people distance themselves from** consuming **it, and even so the Merciful One prohibits it. Therefore,** although it **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **still forbidden.**

Rav Sheshes rejected this interpretation. He said: *be-alma savar Rav nosein ta'am lifgam mutar* — as a general rule, Rav holds that *nosein ta'am lifgam* is permitted. The rat case, however, is a *chiddush* — a novel and distinct situation. The reason: *damimas ma'is u-vdili inish minei* — rats are genuinely disgusting and people naturally recoil from them.

### Torah Ignores Taste

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם — הִלְכָּךְ, אִיסּוּר וּנוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם קְרוּי אִיסּוּר מַאי טַעַם? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן...
>
> **Translation:** [This passage discusses the Torah's treatment of the mouse despite its undesirability.]

Rav Sheshes continues: *ve-afilu hachi asrei Rachmana* — and despite being disgusting, the Torah forbade the rat anyway. *Hilkhakh nosein ta'am lifgam nami assur* — therefore, when it comes to rats, *nosein ta'am lifgam* should also be forbidden. The argument, as understood by the *rishonim*, is as follows: ordinarily, the Torah forbids things that taste good, and therefore when something imparts only a bad taste, the principle of *nosein ta'am lifgam* exempts it. But the Torah's prohibition of rats teaches us that in this domain, we do not operate through the lens of taste at all — the rat is forbidden regardless of any consideration of palatability. This argument works especially well if one assumes a rat to be under normal circumstances entirely inedible: the Torah is essentially saying that even something which no person would want to eat is still forbidden to place in one's mouth. If that is the Torah's stance *be-ein* — regarding the rat itself — then the same logic applies to its taste: even *nosein ta'am lifgam* does not exempt it, because the Torah, when it comes to rats, is not operating through ordinary standards of taste.

### Yavesh Sheratzim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לִיטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ, אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: מְטַמְּאִין לַחִים וְאֵין מְטַמְּאִין יְבֵשִׁים?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rav Sheshet: If that is so,** that the *halakha* with regard to a mouse is considered a novelty and is therefore understood to be more stringent than the norm, then a dead mouse **should impart ritual impurity** whether it is **moist or dried out. Why did we learn** in a mishna (*Nidda* 54b) that carcasses of creeping animals **impart impurity** when they are **moist but do not impart impurity** when they are **dried out?**

The Rabbis press a counterargument against Rav Sheshes: *ela mei'ata letamei lach ve-yavesh* — if your reasoning is correct, that we set aside ordinary standards of edibility and palatability when it comes to rats and *sheratzim*, then a rat should convey *tumah* even when dried out and desiccated. Yet the *mishnah* rules explicitly: *metamin lachin ve-ein metamin yeveshin* — *sheratzim* convey *tumah* when moist but not when dried out. The logic of the objection, as Tosafot explains, is that if we are prepared to say that ordinary measures of fitness for eating are irrelevant for rats and *sheratzim* — that they are forbidden even when inedible, disgusting, or not in any normal sense "food" — then by the same token we should not require that they be moist (i.e., in a food-like state) to convey *tumah*. The dried-out state should be no different.

### Lach Specification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע תְּטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ, אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: מְטַמְּאִין לַחִים וְאֵין מְטַמְּאִין יְבֵשִׁים? אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר? ״שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, בִּרְאוּיָה לְהַזְרִיעַ. הָכָא נָמֵי, ״בְּמֹתָם״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, כְּעֵין מוֹתָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet responded: **And according to your reasoning,** in which you compare the *halakhot* of ritual impurity to forbidden foods, then with regard to **semen,** which is also repulsive, it **should impart impurity** whether **moist or dried out. Why did we learn** in a mishna (*Nidda* 54b) that semen **imparts impurity** when it is **moist but it does not impart impurity** when it is **dried out?** **Rather, what have you to say?** With regard to the ritual impurity of semen, **the Merciful One states: "The flow of seed"** (Leviticus 15:16), meaning that the reference is to semen that is **fit to fertilize. Here too,** with regard to the ritual impurity of a mouse, the verse states: **"When they have died"** (Leviticus 11:32). **The Merciful One states** that the carcasses of creeping animals impart impurity only when they are **similar to** their state at time of **their death,** i.e., when they are still moist. Therefore, there is no contradiction to the claim that the prohibition against eating a mouse is a novelty and consequently applies even when it detracted from the flavor of the food into which it fell.

Rav Sheshes responds with a counter-analogy. He turns the Rabbis' logic back on them: *shichvas zera tetamei lach ve-yavesh?* — by your reasoning, semen should also convey *tumah* whether moist or dried out, since semen is obviously not in the category of food and we do not apply ordinary standards of edibility to it. Yet we also learn *metamin lachin ve-ein metamin yeveshin* by semen. The Gemara resolves both cases through scriptural specification: for semen, the Torah writes *shichvas zera*, "the laying of seed," which the Gemara reads as requiring a state in which conception remains possible — *bera'uya lehazria* — thereby excluding dried semen. For *sheratzim*, the verse *be-motam* — "in their death" — is read as *ke-ein motam*, meaning in a state resembling how they were at death, i.e., moist, not desiccated. Both limitations are thus derived directly from the text of the Torah, and their application to *tumah* law does not undermine Rav Sheshes's argument about taste and prohibition.

## Rava's Conclusion

### Halacha Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם מוּתָּר, וְעַכְבְּרָא בְּשִׁיכְרָא לָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב, אִי מִשּׁוּם דְקָסָבַר נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם אָסוּר, וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ, אִי מִשּׁוּם דְקָסָבַר נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם מוּתָּר, וְעַכְבְּרָא בְּשִׁיכְרָא אַשְׁבּוֹחֵי מַשְׁבַּח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The *halakha*** is that if the forbidden substance **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **permitted. But** with regard to **a mouse** that fell **into** a barrel of **beer, I do not know what the reason was that Rav** deemed it forbidden. I do not know **whether** it was **because he maintains** that if the forbidden substance **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture it is **forbidden, and** if so, **the *halakha*** is **not in accordance with his** opinion, or **whether** it was **because** although **he maintains** that if the forbidden substance **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture it is **permitted, a mouse** that falls **into beer enhances** its flavor.

Rava states the normative conclusion: *hilkheta nosein ta'am lifgam mutar* — the *halacha* is that *nosein ta'am lifgam* is permitted. This is of considerable practical significance, as it undergirds a broad range of leniencies.

### Rav's Reasoning Unclear

As for the case of the rat in beer, Rava states: *lo yadana mai ta'ama de-Rav* — I do not know Rav's reason for forbidding it. There are two possibilities. Either Rav holds that *nosein ta'am lifgam* is *assur* as a general rule — in which case we do not rule like him — or Rav actually holds that *nosein ta'am lifgam* is *mutar*, but he believed that in this specific case *achbara be-shichra ashvuchi meshvach* — a rat in beer actually improves the taste of the beer, making it a case of *nosein ta'am le-shevach* rather than *lifgam*. Tosafot notes this latter possibility with some puzzlement, suggesting that perhaps the rat lent a certain pungency or strengthened the beer in some way. In any event, Rava's fundamental ruling is clear: the principle of *nosein ta'am lifgam* applies even to naturally disgusting substances, and even a rat's taste, if it is a bad taste, would in principle fall within the *hetter* of *nosein ta'am lifgam*.

### Be'ein vs. Ta'am

This leads to an important conceptual distinction made by the *rishonim*. One might ask: if the Torah forbids a rat even though it is disgusting and people recoil from it, should not *nosein ta'am lifgam* also be irrelevant in this case? The *rishonim* answer by drawing a sharp distinction between *be-ein* and *ta'am*. When one eats the actual rat itself — *be-ein* — the Torah prohibits it as long as it is edible, regardless of whether it tastes good or bad. One is eating the forbidden object itself. But when it comes to *ta'am*, the Torah prohibits something that qualifies as *ta'am* — taste — in the halachic sense. The principle derived from *neveilah* teaches that *ta'am lifgam* is not halachically considered *ta'am* at all. Something that is not normally desired as a taste does not constitute the kind of *ta'am* the Torah forbids. The object itself, consumed *be-ein*, is forbidden because one is eating the rat regardless of taste; but the imparted taste, if it is a bad taste, does not rise to the level of halachically significant *ta'am*, and the food into which it falls remains permitted. This distinction is what allows us to say that even the naturally repugnant rat, whose very existence in a food product one finds disgusting, does not render the food forbidden when it operates only through *nosein ta'am lifgam*. The practical ruling is that even a cockroach that falls into scrambled eggs would be *nosein ta'am lifgam* and the food would be *mutar*, notwithstanding the natural revulsion one feels toward the food — since that sense of disgust is itself what qualifies the taste as *lifgam*.

## Rat in Vinegar

### Vinegar Pungency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַל לְגוֹ חַלָּא, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הִילֵּל לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא, וַאֲסַר רַב כָּהֲנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא אִימַּרְטוּטֵי אִימַּרְטַט.
>
> **Translation:** If a mouse **fell into vinegar, what** is the *halakha*? Does it enhance its flavor? **Rav Hillel said to Rav Ashi: There was** such **an incident** in **the study hall of Rav Kahana, and Rav Kahana deemed** the vinegar **forbidden.** This indicates that it enhances the flavor. Rav Ashi **said to him:** This is not a proof. **That** mouse **was dismembered,** and Rav Kahana deemed the vinegar forbidden due to concern that one might consume a substantive piece of the mouse in the vinegar, which is prohibited regardless of the taste.

The Gemara poses a new question at the top of 69a: what is the law if the rat fell not into beer but into vinegar? As Rashi explains, the question is whether the extreme pungency of vinegar might prevent the rat from imparting any detectable taste at all — *hachometz ta'amo kasheh ve-ein ha-sheretz mafigo litet bo ta'am* — since the vinegar's sharp flavor might dominate and neutralize whatever the rat would otherwise contribute.

### Rav Kahana Case

Rav Hillel reports to Rav Ashi that a case of this nature arose in the academy of Rav Kahana, and Rav Kahana forbade the vinegar. The precise reasoning is not entirely clear — whether it is because the rat did not give a bad taste but rather enhanced or neutralized the taste in a way that made it *nosein ta'am le-shevach* or simply imparted no taste at all, with the assumption that a neutral imparting of taste is also problematic.

### Pieces Problem

Rav Ashi, however, clarifies that Rav Kahana's case was not a question of taste at all: *ha'u imrutay imratay* — there were actual small pieces of the rat that remained in the vinegar, making the issue one of *be-ein* rather than *ta'am*. Since it was the actual substance of the rat present in the vinegar, the question of *nosein ta'am lifgam* did not arise.

### Honey Bee Legs

This clarification about *be-ein* prompts Tosafot to raise a striking practical question: *heikhan anu okhlim devash dvorim* — how is it that we eat bee honey? Bee honey regularly contains fragments of bee legs, which are *be-ein* of a forbidden *sheretz*. The principle of *nosein ta'am lifgam* would not help, since the problem is the actual physical presence of the forbidden item. Tosafot answers in the name of Rabbeinu Tam: *hanach raglei dvorim kivan de-atzmot be-alma ninhu mutarin* — bee legs are essentially bones with no meat, and the bones of a forbidden creature without any attached meat are not forbidden. This is a fascinating exchange that reflects how careful the *rishonim* were to reconcile theoretical halachic principles with the everyday foods they actually consumed.

## Shiurei Bittul

### Ravina 101 Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא סְבַר לְשַׁעוֹרֵי בִּמְאָה וְחַד, אָמַר: לָא גָּרַע מִתְּרוּמָה, דִּתְנַן: תְּרוּמָה עוֹלָה בְּאֶחָד וּמֵאָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר גִּיזָּא לְרָבִינָא: דִּלְמָא כְּתַבְלִין שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה בִּקְדֵירָה דָּמֵי, דְּלָא בָּטֵיל טַעְמַיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ravina thought** that the quantity of vinegar necessary for nullifying the flavor of the mouse should be **calculated at 101** times the volume of the mouse. **He said:** It should **not** be rendered **worse,** i.e., more stringent, **than *teruma*,** which is nullified by 101 times its volume in a mixture. This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 4:7): ***Teruma* is nullified** in a mixture **by 101** times its volume of permitted food. **Rav Taḥlifa bar Giza said to Ravina: Perhaps** this case is **similar to spice of *teruma* in a pot, whose flavor is not nullified** even by 101 times its volume of permitted food, as the flavor imparted by spice is exceptionally strong.

The Gemara now turns to the question of *shiurei bittul* — the measures of nullification required for a rat that fell into beer. Ravina was inclined to require a ratio of a hundred and one, analogous to the nullification of *terumah*, which requires sixty plus one, i.e., a hundred and one parts of permitted food against one of *terumah* — *truma olah be-echad u-meah*. The thinking seems to be that even if the rat's taste in beer is arguably *lifgam*, one might want to treat it more strictly than ordinary *issurim*, requiring a more substantial majority for *bittul*.

### Spices Exception

Rav Tachlipa bar Gizla challenged Ravina: perhaps the rat in beer should be compared not to standard *terumah* but to spices of *terumah* — *tavlin shel trumah* — or similar substances that are *avida le-ta'ama*, made to impart taste, and which therefore cannot be *batel* at the standard ratio. Even a minute quantity of such spices renders its presence detectable, and accordingly a far greater ratio of permitted food may be needed before the taste is truly undetectable. Tosafot is careful to note that *avida le-ta'ama* items are not categorically incapable of *bittul* — it is not that they belong to a more severe class of *issur*. Rather, the practical reality is that they impart taste even in minute quantities, and so one must determine just how much permitted food is needed before the taste is genuinely neutralized — which could be a thousand parts or two thousand, but eventually *bittul* does occur.

### Acha: Fifty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחַאי שַׁיעַר בְּחַלָּא בְּחַמְשִׁין, רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא שַׁיעַר בְּשִׁיכְרָא בְּשִׁיתִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥai calculated** the amount **of vinegar** necessary to nullify the flavor of the mouse **at fifty** times its volume. Although forbidden food in a mixture usually requires the presence of sixty times its volume of permitted food to be nullified, vinegar has a sharp enough flavor that it nullifies the mouse with less. **Rav Shmuel, son of Rav Ika, calculated** the amount **of beer** necessary for nullifying the mouse **at sixty** times the volume of the mouse.

Rav Acha offered a different measure: when the rat fell into vinegar, fifty parts of vinegar are sufficient to render it *batel*. The implication seems to be that vinegar's sharp pungency reduces the rat's flavor impact, requiring a smaller ratio for nullification.

### Shmuel: Sixty

Rav Shmuel the son of Rav Ika measured differently: for beer, sixty parts are sufficient for *bittul*.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּשִׁיתִּין, וְכֵן כׇּל אִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that **this and that,** both vinegar and beer, nullify the mouse **with sixty** times its volume, **and so** is the ruling for **all prohibitions in the Torah.**

The Gemara rules: *ve-hilkheta adidi va-adidi be-shishin, ve-khen kol issurim she-ba-Torah* — the *halacha* is that whether the forbidden item fell into vinegar or into beer, sixty is the required ratio, and the same standard of sixty applies to all *issurim* in the Torah. This is the foundational rule of *bittul be-shishim*, sixty-to-one nullification, here derived and codified from this discussion.

### Rashi Bittul Types

The ruling opens a discussion in Rashi about the distinction between *min be-mino* — a forbidden item that falls into the same type of food — and *min be-she'eino mino* — a forbidden item that falls into a different type. Rashi argues that *min be-mino* follows the standard of sixty, while *min be-she'eino mino* would require *be-mashu* — any detectable quantity would render the mixture forbidden. This is a position we do not rule in accordance with; the accepted *halacha* is that *min be-mino* also goes by sixty. The ruling of *shishim* here, it should be noted, applies to cases where the taste of the forbidden substance is genuine — *nosein ta'am le-shevach*. If the substance imparts only a bad taste — *nosein ta'am lifgam* — then as Rava has already ruled, the mixture is entirely *mutar*, and the question of the sixty-to-one ratio does not arise at all.
