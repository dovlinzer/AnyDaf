# Berakhot 54b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Crossings & Wadis

### Baraita Overview

The Gemara resumes near the bottom of 53b and continues onto 54b, where the *mishnah* has been discussing *brachot* made upon witnessing a place where a miracle occurred for the many — and, separately, a miracle that occurred to an individual himself. Now the Gemara transitions to a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan*, our Rabbis taught.

The *baraita* lists specific locations and objects upon seeing which one must offer praise to God: the fords of the sea (*ma'avarot hayam*), the fords of the Jordan River (*ma'avarot hayarden*), the wadis of Arnon (*ma'avarot nachlei Arnon*), the hailstones of Elgavish at the descent of Beit Choron, the stone that Og King of Bashan sought to hurl at Israel, the stone upon which Moses sat when Joshua fought against Amalek, the pillar of salt that was Lot's wife, and the wall of Jericho that was absorbed in its place. Upon all of these one must give thanks and praise before God.

It is worth noting that the *baraita* does not specify a particular *nusach ha-bracha*, as the *mishnah* had. It also moves fairly quickly from place-centered miracles to objects — the stones, the pillar of salt — indicating that the focus here is somewhat broader than geography alone.

### Red Sea Crossing

The Gemara first asks what the scriptural basis is for each of these. The crossing of the Red Sea is understood from the verse *vayavo'u Bnei Yisrael betoch hayam bayabasha* — the Children of Israel entered into the midst of the sea on dry land. That this location constitutes a place of miracle requires no elaboration.

### Jordan Crossing

Similarly, the crossing of the Jordan River at the time of Joshua is anchored in the verse describing how the *kohanim* bearing the Ark stood fixed in the dry riverbed while all of Israel crossed on dry land: *vaya'amdu hakohanim nosei aron berit Hashem becharava betoch hayarden hachen, vechol Yisrael overim becharava*. The Jordan crossing differed somewhat from the Red Sea in that the water was not split in the same manner — a wall formed at one point while the lower waters drained away — but it was nonetheless a manifest miracle, and the location of the crossing is accordingly a place upon which one recites a *bracha*.

### Wadis of Arnon

The miracle of the wadis of Arnon requires more elaboration, since it is far less familiar. The Gemara derives it from a cryptic verse in *Bamidbar*: *al ken ye'amar besefer milchamot Hashem et vahev besufa ve'et hanechalim Arnon* — "therefore it shall be said in the Book of the Wars of God: Vahev in Suphah and the wadis of Arnon."

The Gemara expounds: *et vahev besufa* is read as *be-sof*, referring to those at the rear of the Israelite camp. At the time when Israel was about to enter Canaan, two *metzora'im* were walking at the back of the camp — as required, since a *metzora* must dwell outside the encampment. The Amorites, aware that Israel would be passing through a narrow mountain passage, had carved out hidden caves in the walls of the surrounding mountains. Their plan was to wait until the Israelites passed below and then attack them from above — a form of guerrilla ambush.

What the Amorites did not account for was the Ark of the Covenant, which traveled in advance of the Israelite camp and would level the terrain before them. As the Ark passed through, the mountains were caused to press toward one another. The protrusions of each mountain entered into the caves of the opposing mountain, crushing all the Amorites who had hidden there. The blood poured out and flowed into the wadi, eventually reaching the place called Vahev. The *metzora'im*, traveling at the back of the procession, witnessed this aftermath — the blood streaming from between the mountains — which the main body of Israel had not seen. They came and reported it to Israel, and Israel gave song.

This is the meaning of the verse: *ve-eshed hanechalim asher nata leshevet Ar ve-nish'an ligvul Moav* — the pouring of the wadis that inclined toward the inhabitants of Ar and leaned toward the border of Moab. The mountains inclined toward one another, and the blood flowed out accordingly. It is upon passing through this wadi that one recites a *bracha*.

It is worth pausing here to observe a broader point. We have now moved, subtly, from miracles that are directly rooted in explicit *pesukim* into territory closer to *midrash* — specifically a *midrash* that interprets cryptic verses. The subsequent entries in the *baraita* will move further still in this direction. The fact that the *baraita* obligates one to recite an actual *bracha* upon encountering these sites suggests that *Chazal* understood these accounts as genuine historical events, not merely rhetorical or homiletical constructs. This question becomes all the more acute when we reach the episode of Og.

## Hailstones Elgavish

### Stones Suspended

The Gemara next asks about the *avnei elgavish* — the hailstones of Elgavish. A *tanna* teaches: these are stones that stood suspended above one person and fell upon another person — two separate events, connected by the same stones.

They stood above one person: this refers to Moses, *ha-ish Moshe anav me'od* — for Moses is called *ish*. When the plague of hail was struck in Egypt, Moses prayed and the hail ceased — but the verse states *va-yachdelu hakolot ve-habarad u-matar lo nitach artzah*, the thunder and hail stopped and the rain did not descend to the ground. The understanding is that the hailstones remained suspended in mid-air after Moses called upon them to cease. They never actually fell. They were thus *al gav ish* — suspended above the person of Moses.

### Beit Choron Context

They then descended upon another person: this is Joshua, of whom the verse says *kach lecha et Yehoshua bin Nun ish asher ruach bo* — Joshua is also called *ish*. And the verse recounts: *va-yehi benosam mipnei Bnei Yisrael hem be-morad Beit Choron va-Hashem hishlich aleihem avanim gedolot* — when the enemies fled before the Children of Israel at the descent of Beit Choron, God hurled great stones upon them. These are the very hailstones that had been suspended since the time of Moses and now came down upon Israel's enemies at Beit Choron. Thus the site of the descent of Beit Choron is a place where one recites a *bracha* — and at least that second location is a definite geographic site.

## Og's Mountain

### Og Plans Attack

The *baraita* next mentions the stone that Og King of Bashan sought to hurl at Israel. The Gemara states plainly: *gemara gemiri lah* — this is a transmitted tradition, not derived from a *pasuk*. What follows is entirely *aggadic*.

Og calculated the size of the Israelite camp: three *parsa'ot*, roughly twelve kilometers across. He reasoned: if I can uproot a mountain the size of the entire encampment and hurl it down upon them, I can destroy them all at once. He went and uprooted such a mountain and carried it above his head, intending to cast it upon Israel.

### Mountain Destroyed

God then sent grasshoppers — or some creature capable of rapid destruction — to gnaw holes through the mountain as Og held it aloft. The mountain cracked and split, and rather than being hurled forward, it collapsed down over Og's own head, settling around his neck. He attempted to pull it off, *hava bei le-mishlafeh*, but could not.

### Og's Teeth

As Og struggled to free himself, his teeth extended outward in each direction — *mashchei shinei le-hai gisa u-le-hai gisa* — and hooked into the holes in the mountain, making it impossible to remove. This is the fulfillment of the verse *shinei rasha'im shibarta* — You have broken the teeth of the wicked. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish teaches: do not read *shibarta* (you broke) but *shirvavta* (you extended). The verse describes not breaking but a grotesque elongation that trapped Og by his own teeth.

### Moses Defeats Og

Moses then came to finish the battle. How was this possible, given Og's stature? The Gemara establishes that Moses was ten *amot* tall — derived from elsewhere in the Gemara, on the grounds that Moses could spread the rooftop tapestries over the *Ohel Moed*, which was itself ten *amot* high, and he could only have done so without a ladder if he were that tall himself. Moses took an axe that was also ten *amot* long, then jumped ten *amot* upward — placing himself thirty *amot* off the ground, roughly forty-five feet in the air — and struck Og at the ankle, *u-machyei be-karsulei*, and killed him.

The story is, of course, fantastical in the extreme — a theater of the absurd, one might say: a mountain cracking over a giant's head, his teeth caught in the rubble, while Moses leaps thirty *amot* to deliver the coup de grâce at the ankle. Yet the *baraita* treats it with full seriousness, requiring a *bracha* upon the site. One might speculate that the practical likelihood of ever encountering this stone is vanishingly small, which perhaps renders the obligation theoretical — but nonetheless the Gemara does not distance itself from the account. The *midrash* about the stone, incidentally, is more modest than the full narrative the Gemara constructs around it; the *baraita* mentions only a stone, while the Gemara elaborates into something considerably more elaborate.

## Stone Moses Sat

### Stone at Amalek War

The *baraita* also includes the stone upon which Moses sat during Joshua's war against Amalek. What is noteworthy here is that the natural expectation might have been to mark the location of the battle itself as the place of the miracle. Instead, the *baraita* focuses on the specific stone where Moses sat — the place where Moses held his hands aloft and thereby determined the outcome of the war. The emphasis is on the object and Moses' role rather than the battlefield itself.

## Lot & Jericho

### Lot's Wife

The *baraita* continues with the pillar of salt that was Lot's wife — derived from the verse *va-tabet ishto me-acharav va-tehi netziv melach*, his wife looked behind her and became a pillar of salt — and the wall of Jericho that was absorbed in its place.

### Punishment vs Salvation

The Gemara raises a sharp challenge. It is understandable that all the others are miracles, *bishlama kulu nisa* — but the fate of Lot's wife was a punishment, *puranuta hi*. Even granting that it occurred through miraculous means, one might have thought it makes no difference whether the miracle involved salvation or punishment. Yet the Gemara's reasoning here is that a *bracha* of thanks is appropriate specifically for a miracle of salvation, not for one of punishment.

### Specific Blessings

The resolution is to modify the text of the *baraita*. For Lot and his wife, one recites two separate *brachot*: upon seeing the pillar of salt that was Lot's wife, one says *Baruch Dayan HaEmet*; upon seeing the place associated with Lot's salvation, one says *Baruch Zocher HaTzadikim*.

This is a striking passage for several reasons. First, if we follow those *rishonim* who hold that these *brachot* require *shem u-malchut*, then we are continually encountering new formulations of blessings — not simply the generic *she-asa nes* formula of the *mishnah*, but novel texts. Second, this is also the first miracle in the entire list that is not a national Jewish experience but rather a family matter of Abraham's relative, and one that occurred long before Sinai. Yet the Gemara creates a specific *bracha* for it. Third, the notion of reciting *Baruch Dayan HaEmet* upon seeing something that evokes divine judgment — even without it being a recent death or a fresh tragedy — is familiar from the practice of reciting it upon visiting a cemetery. Here too, the sight evokes the appropriate response regardless of when the event occurred.

### God Remembers Righteous

Rabbi Yochanan draws out a theological lesson from the *Baruch Zocher HaTzadikim* formulation: even at the moment of God's anger, He remembers the righteous. The verse states, *va-yehi be-shachet Elohim et arei ha-kikar va-yizkor Elohim et Avraham va-yeshalach et Lot mi-toch ha-hafecha* — when God was destroying the cities of the plain, He remembered Abraham and sent Lot out from the upheaval. The deeper point here engages with other *midrashim* that suggest that at moments of wholesale destruction, the destructive force does not discriminate between righteous and wicked — as in the command on the night of *Pesach* that Israel remain indoors, *ve-lo yiten ha-mashchit lavo el bateichem linigof*. Rabbi Yochanan counters: no, God does remember the righteous even in such moments. Lot, who was arguably not himself fully righteous, was saved for the sake of Abraham.

### Jericho Wall

Returning to the wall of Jericho, the Gemara asks: the *baraita* says the wall was *absorbed* in its place — but the verse says it *fell*, *va-tipol ha-choma tachtecha*, the wall fell beneath itself. How are these reconciled?

The Gemara's answer involves geometry. The wall of Jericho was as wide as it was tall — *potya ve-ruma ki hadadi ninhu*, its width and its height were equal. Therefore if the wall had simply tipped over and fallen to the ground, the resulting pile would be as high as the wall was wide — which is the same as its original height. It would not have truly "fallen" in any meaningful sense of being brought low. For the wall to genuinely collapse, it had to be absorbed downward into the ground. Hence the *baraita* correctly describes the miracle as one of absorption.

## Four Thanksgivings

### Four Categories

The Gemara transitions from these historical miracle sites to a more universally applicable *halacha*. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: four categories of people are obligated to give thanks — *arbaah tzrichim lehodot*. All four are rooted in Psalm 107, a *perek* of *Tehillim* devoted entirely to the theme of divine deliverance and gratitude. The Gemara also notes the connection to the *Korban Todah*, the thanksgiving offering described in the Torah, which the Torah does not explicitly associate with particular triggering circumstances. These four categories supply those circumstances.

### Seafarers

The first category is *yordei hayam* — those who descend to the sea in ships. The Gemara quotes the relevant *pesukim* from Psalm 107: they saw the works of God and His wonders in the deep; He spoke and raised a stormy wind that lifted the waves to the heavens and plunged them to the depths; they reeled like drunkards; they cried out to God in their distress; He stilled the storm to a calm; they rejoiced; *yodu la-Hashem chasdo* — they gave thanks to God for His kindness and His wonders to humankind. The theme throughout is crisis, prayer, deliverance, and the obligation to offer public gratitude.

### Desert Travelers

The second category is *holchei midbarot* — those who travel in the wilderness or desert. The *pasuk*: they wandered in the desert in desolate places, found no inhabited city, cried out to God, and were led on a straight path. Again the concluding injunction: *yodu la-Hashem chasdo*.

### Healed from Sickness

The third category is those who were sick and recovered. What is notable here is that the *pesukim* explicitly frame this as a consequence of sin: *evilim mi-derech pish'am u-me-avonoteihem yitanu* — the fools, through their sinful path, are afflicted because of their iniquities. Their appetite fails; they cry out to God; He sends His word and heals them. Unlike the seafarer or the desert traveler, whose danger is circumstantial, the sick person's affliction is portrayed as arising from moral failure. The obligation of thanksgiving nonetheless applies equally.

### Released Prisoners

The fourth category is those who were imprisoned and freed. Here again, sin is implicated — *ki himru imrei el*, because they rebelled against God's word. They dwelt in darkness and the shadow of death; God humbled their hearts through their affliction; they cried out; He brought them out of darkness. *Yodu la-Hashem chasdo*. The recurring structure across all four categories is striking: crisis, divine abandonment or distance, cry to God, deliverance, and mandated gratitude.

## Blessing Formula

### Gomel Blessing

Having established the four categories, the Gemara asks: what precisely does one recite? Rav Yehuda states: *Baruch gomel chasadim tovim* — Blessed is He who bestows good kindnesses. This is the basis of the familiar *birkat ha-gomel*, though our standard *nusach* — *Baruch Atah Hashem ha-gomel le-chayavim tovot she-gemalani kol tov* — is somewhat more elaborate.

### Ten Witnesses

Abaye adds a procedural requirement: one must recite this *bracha* before ten people, *tzarich le-oduyei kammai asara*. The source is the verse from Psalm 107: *ve-yeromemuhu bi-kehal am* — they shall exalt Him in the congregation of the people. A *kahal* constitutes a quorum of ten.

### Two Sages Required

Mar Zutra adds a further requirement: two of those ten must be Torah sages, *rabanan*. His source is the continuation of the verse: *u-ve-moshav zekenim yehaleluhu* — and in the assembly of elders they shall praise Him. *Zekenim* here refers to sages, and since the plural implies at least two, two of the ten must be learned figures.

Rav Ashi challenges this: perhaps all ten should be sages, since the verse says *zekenim*? Mar Zutra's position is that the primary requirement is a *kahal am* — a general congregation of people — and the *zekenim* are an additional specification. But then Rav Ashi raises a further difficulty: perhaps one needs ten regular people *plus* two sages separately, not two sages included within the ten? The Gemara concludes *kashya* — this remains a problem with no definitive resolution. In practice, the requirement of two sages has not been maintained with great precision; what is observed is the *minyan* of ten.

## Rav Yehuda's Recovery

### Rav Yehuda Heals

The Gemara now recounts a related incident. Rav Yehuda became ill and recovered. Rav Chana Bagdata'ah came to visit him together with the rabbis — presumably his students — and they said to him: *brich rachmana di-yavach n'halan ve-lo yavach le-afra* — Blessed is God who has given you back to us and has not given you to the dust. A gracious and touching formulation.

### Exemption Claim

Rav Yehuda then said to them: *patartun yati me-l'oduyei* — you have exempted me from giving thanks. By praising God on his behalf, he claimed, they had fulfilled his obligation for him.

### Amen as Equivalent

The Gemara objects: but Abaye ruled that the thanksgiving must be recited before ten — implying that the one who experienced the deliverance must be the one who gives thanks. The Gemara resolves this: there were in fact ten people present. But then the Gemara presses further: Rav Yehuda himself did not give thanks — it was others who praised God. The resolution: *lo tzarich de-ani batraihu amen* — he had answered *amen* after them, and that is sufficient.

This is a remarkable principle in context. Throughout the tractate, the question of whether *shome'a ke-oneh* — hearing as equivalent to reciting — applies in various settings has been a recurring theme in discussions of *tefillah*, *birchot ha-nehenin*, and *birkat ha-mazon*. Here we see an even bolder application: the power of *amen* is such that when another person recites a *bracha* on your behalf and you answer *amen*, it is treated as equivalent to your own recitation, at least for the purposes of *birkat ha-gomel*.

Also worth noting: the blessing as recited here had no *shem u-malchut* — no formal divine name and kingship formula. Tosafot addresses this, noting that Rav Yehuda was a student of Rav, and Rav holds that *shem u-malchut* is not required for a *bracha*. But the question is broader: does this reflect a not-yet-fully-crystallized *nusach*, or does it reflect a deliberate halakhic position? The answer has implications for how one understands the entire institution of *birkat ha-gomel*. A suggestive parallel is the passage in the *Leil Seder* Haggadah, where one of the *amora'im* asked a slave what he would do if freed, and the slave responded with effusive gratitude; the *amora* then said, "You have exempted us from reciting *Ma Nishtana*." The principle that another person's expression of the relevant sentiment, offered in your presence, can fulfill your own obligation appears in both contexts, with appropriate differences.

## Three Need Protection

### Vulnerable Categories

Rav Yehuda taught: *shlosha tzrichim shimur* — three categories of people require special protection, apparently from *mazikin*, harmful spirits, who are drawn to or jealous of those in particularly vulnerable states. These three are: a sick person (*choleh*), a bride and groom (*chatan ve-kallah*), and — implicit in the formulation — others to be added by the *baraita*. This is the source for the practice of having *shomrim*, guardians, accompany the *chatan ve-kallah*. Rashi explains that it is because the *mazikin* are jealous of the joy of the *chatan ve-kallah*.

### Extended Categories

The *baraita* adds further categories: a woman who has recently given birth (*chaya*) — for she is physically vulnerable in her weakened state — and, according to some, even a mourner (*avel*), apparently on the grounds that misfortune may attract further misfortune, or some similar vulnerability. And some say: even Torah scholars at night (*talmidei chachamim ba-lailah*), though the precise rationale here is not entirely clear from the text, possibly related to jealousy or to their spiritual intensity drawing unwanted attention. With this, the Gemara closes its discussion on 54b.
