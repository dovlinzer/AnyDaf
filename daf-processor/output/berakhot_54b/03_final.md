# Berakhot 54b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Crossings & Wadis

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה מַעְבְּרוֹת הַיָּם, וּמַעְבְּרוֹת הַיַּרְדֵּן, מַעְבְּרוֹת נַחֲלֵי אַרְנוֹן, אַבְנֵי אֶלְגָּבִישׁ בְּמוֹרַד בֵּית חוֹרוֹן, וְאֶבֶן שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לִזְרוֹק עוֹג מֶלֶךְ הַבָּשָׁן עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֶבֶן שֶׁיָּשַׁב עָלֶיהָ מֹשֶׁה בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָשָׂה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִלְחָמָה בַּעֲמָלֵק, וְאִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל לוֹט, וְחוֹמַת יְרִיחוֹ שֶׁנִּבְלְעָה בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — עַל כּוּלָּן צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּתֵּן הוֹדָאָה וָשֶׁבַח לִפְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* a list of places where one is required to recite a blessing due to miracles that were performed there: **One who sees the crossings of the** Red **Sea,** where Israel crossed; **and the crossings of the Jordan; and the crossings of the streams of Arnon; the hailstones of Elgavish on the descent of Beit Ḥoron; the rock that Og, King of Bashan, sought to hurl upon Israel; and the rock upon which Moses sat when Joshua waged war against Amalek; and Lot's wife; and the wall of Jericho that was swallowed up in its place. On all of these** miracles **one must give thanks and** offer **praise before God.**

The *baraita* lists specific locations and objects upon seeing which one must offer praise to God: the fords of the sea (*ma'avarot hayam*), the fords of the Jordan River (*ma'avarot hayarden*), the wadis of Arnon (*ma'avarot nachlei Arnon*), the hailstones of Elgavish at the descent of Beit Choron, the stone that Og King of Bashan sought to hurl at Israel, the stone upon which Moses sat when Joshua fought against Amalek, the pillar of salt that was Lot's wife, and the wall of Jericho that was absorbed in its place. Upon all of these one must give thanks and praise before God.

It is worth noting that the *baraita* does not specify a particular *nusach ha-bracha*, as the *mishnah* had. It also moves fairly quickly from place-centered miracles to objects — the stones, the pillar of salt — indicating that the focus here is somewhat broader than geography alone.

### Red Sea Crossing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מַעְבְּרוֹת הַיָּם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּבֹאוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּתוֹךְ הַיָּם בַּיַּבָּשָׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Granted,** the miracles at **the crossings of the sea** are recorded explicitly in the Torah, **as it is stated: "And the Israelites went into the sea on dry ground** and the water was a wall for them on their right and on their left" (Exodus 14:22).

The crossing of the Red Sea is understood from the verse *vayavo'u Bnei Yisrael betoch hayam bayabasha* — the Children of Israel entered into the midst of the sea on dry land. That this location constitutes a place of miracle requires no elaboration.

### Jordan Crossing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעְבְּרוֹת הַיַּרְדֵּן, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַמְדוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים נֹשְׂאֵי הָאָרוֹן בְּרִית ה׳ בֶּחָרָבָה בְּתוֹךְ הַיַּרְדֵּן הָכֵן וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עֹבְרִים בֶּחָרָבָה עַד אֲשֶׁר תַּמּוּ כׇּל הַגּוֹי לַעֲבוֹר אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן״.
>
> **Translation:** So too, the miracle at **the crossings of the Jordan, as it is stated: "The priests who bore the ark of God's covenant stood on dry land within the Jordan, while all Israel crossed on dry land until the entire nation finished crossing the Jordan"** (Joshua 3:17).

Similarly, the crossing of the Jordan River at the time of Joshua is anchored in the verse describing how the *kohanim* bearing the Ark stood fixed in the dry riverbed while all of Israel crossed on dry land: *vaya'amdu hakohanim nosei aron berit Hashem becharava betoch hayarden hachen, vechol Yisrael overim becharava*. The Jordan crossing differed somewhat from the Red Sea in that the water was not split in the same manner — a wall formed at one point while the lower waters drained away — but it was nonetheless a manifest miracle, and the location of the crossing is accordingly a place upon which one recites a *bracha*.

### Wadis of Arnon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַעְבְּרוֹת נַחֲלֵי אַרְנוֹן מְנָלַן? — דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל כֵּן יֵאָמַר בְּסֵפֶר מִלְחֲמֹת ה׳ אֶת וָהֵב בְּסוּפָה וְגוֹ׳״. תָּנָא: ״אֶת וָהֵב בְּסוּפָה״ — שְׁנֵי מְצוֹרָעִים הָיוּ, דַּהֲווֹ מְהַלְּכִין בְּסוֹף מַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל. כִּי הֲווֹ קָא חָלְפִי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲתוֹ אֱמוֹרָאֵי עָבְדִי לְהוֹן נְקִירָתָא וּטְשׁוֹ בְּהוֹן. אָמְרִי: כִּי חָלְפִי יִשְׂרָאֵל הָכָא — נִקְטְלִינּוּן, וְלָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי דְּאָרוֹן הֲוָה מְסַגֵּי קַמַּיְיהוּ דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וַהֲוָה מַמֵּיךְ לְהוּ טוּרֵי מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ. כֵּיוָן דַּאֲתָא אָרוֹן, אִדְּבַקוּ טוּרֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וְקַטְלִינּוּן, וּנְחַת דְּמַיְיהוּ לְנַחֲלֵי אַרְנוֹן. כִּי אֲתוֹ אֶת וָהֵב, חֲזוֹ דְּמָא דְּקָא נָפֵיק מִבֵּינֵי טוּרֵי. אֲתוֹ וְאָמְרִי לְהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל וַאֲמַרוּ שִׁירָה. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶשֶׁד הַנְּחָלִים אֲשֶׁר נָטָה לְשֶׁבֶת עָר וְנִשְׁעַן לִגְבוּל מוֹאָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **However, from where do we** derive the miracle that occurred at **the crossing of the streams of Arnon? As it is stated: "Wherefore it is said in the Book of the Wars of the Lord: *Vahev* in *Sufa***, and the valleys of Arnon. And the slope of the valleys that incline toward the seat of Ar, and lean upon the border of Moab" (Numbers 21:14–15). It was **taught: *"Vahev in Sufa";* there were two lepers,** one named Et and the second named Hev, **who were walking at the rear of the camp of Israel. As Israel passed, the Emorites came** and **prepared caves for themselves and they hid in them. They said: When Israel passes here we will kill them. And they did not know** that the **Ark** of the Covenant **preceded** the children of **Israel and would flatten mountains before them. When** the **Ark came, the mountains adhered one to another and killed them; and their blood** flowed **down to the streams of Arnon. When Et and Hev,** the lepers, **arrived, they saw the blood that was emerging from between the mountains, and they came and told Israel** what had happened. Israel **recited** a **song** of praise, **as it is stated: "And at the cascade of the brooks that goes down to the dwelling of Ar, and lies upon the border of Moab"** (Numbers 21:15). This refers to the cascade of the brooks where the mountain, which had once been a valley, spread out in the direction of the mountain in Ar, in Moab.

The miracle of the wadis of Arnon requires more elaboration, since it is far less familiar. The Gemara derives it from a cryptic verse in *Bamidbar*: *al ken ye'amar besefer milchamot Hashem et vahev besufa ve'et hanechalim Arnon* — "therefore it shall be said in the Book of the Wars of God: Vahev in Suphah and the wadis of Arnon."

The Gemara expounds: *et vahev besufa* is read as *be-sof*, referring to those at the rear of the Israelite camp. At the time when Israel was about to enter Canaan, two *metzora'im* were walking at the back of the camp — as required, since a *metzora* must dwell outside the encampment. The Amorites, aware that Israel would be passing through a narrow mountain passage, had carved out hidden caves in the walls of the surrounding mountains. Their plan was to wait until the Israelites passed below and then attack them from above — a form of guerrilla ambush.

What the Amorites did not account for was the Ark of the Covenant, which traveled in advance of the Israelite camp and would level the terrain before them. As the Ark passed through, the mountains were caused to press toward one another. The protrusions of each mountain entered into the caves of the opposing mountain, crushing all the Amorites who had hidden there. The blood poured out and flowed into the wadi, eventually reaching the place called Vahev. The *metzora'im*, traveling at the back of the procession, witnessed this aftermath — the blood streaming from between the mountains — which the main body of Israel had not seen. They came and reported it to Israel, and Israel gave song.

This is the meaning of the verse: *ve-eshed hanechalim asher nata leshevet Ar ve-nish'an ligvul Moav* — the pouring of the wadis that inclined toward the inhabitants of Ar and leaned toward the border of Moab. The mountains inclined toward one another, and the blood flowed out accordingly. It is upon passing through this wadi that one recites a *bracha*.

It is worth pausing here to observe a broader point. We have now moved, subtly, from miracles that are directly rooted in explicit *pesukim* into territory closer to *midrash* — specifically a *midrash* that interprets cryptic verses. The subsequent entries in the *baraita* will move further still in this direction. The fact that the *baraita* obligates one to recite an actual *bracha* upon encountering these sites suggests that *Chazal* understood these accounts as genuine historical events, not merely rhetorical or homiletical constructs. This question becomes all the more acute when we reach the episode of Og.

## Hailstones Elgavish

### Stones Suspended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבְנֵי אֶלְגָּבִישׁ. מַאי אַבְנֵי אֶלְגָּבִישׁ? תָּנָא: אֲבָנִים שֶׁעָמְדוּ עַל גַּב אִישׁ, וְיָרְדוּ עַל גַּב אִישׁ. עָמְדוּ עַל גַּב אִישׁ — זֶה מֹשֶׁה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָאִישׁ מֹשֶׁה עָנָו מְאֹד״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּחְדְּלוּ הַקֹּלוֹת וְהַבָּרָד וּמָטָר לֹא נִתַּךְ אָרְצָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Among the sites enumerated in the *baraita* where one is obligated to recite a blessing in recognition of the miracles that occurred there, was the site of **the hailstones of Elgavish.** The Gemara asks: **What** are the **hailstones of Elgavish? It is taught** in the midrash: They are the **stones that** remained **suspended** in the air and did not fall **because of [*al gav*] a man [*ish*] and they fell down because of [*al gav*] a man [*ish*].** The Gemara explains: **They** remained **suspended because of a man; that is Moses,** whom the verse refers to as a man, **as it is written: "And the man Moses was very modest"** (Numbers 12:3), **and it is written:** "And Moses went out of the city from Pharaoh, and spread forth his hands unto the Lord; **the thunders and hail ceased, and the rain was not poured upon the earth"** (Exodus 9:33). Moses' hailstones remained suspended.

The Gemara next asks about the *avnei elgavish* — the hailstones of Elgavish. A *tanna* teaches: these are stones that stood suspended above one person and fell upon another person — two separate events, connected by the same stones.

They stood above one person: this refers to Moses, *ha-ish Moshe anav me'od* — for Moses is called *ish*. When the plague of hail was struck in Egypt, Moses prayed and the hail ceased — but the verse states *va-yachdelu hakolot ve-habarad u-matar lo nitach artzah*, the thunder and hail stopped and the rain did not descend to the ground. The understanding is that the hailstones remained suspended in mid-air after Moses called upon them to cease. They never actually fell. They were thus *al gav ish* — suspended above the person of Moses.

### Beit Choron Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָרְדוּ עַל גַּב אִישׁ — זֶה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דִּכְתִיב: ״קַח לְךָ אֶת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר רוּחַ בּוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בְּנוּסָם מִפְּנֵי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל הֵם בְּמוֹרַד בֵּית חוֹרֹן וַה׳ הִשְׁלִיךְ עֲלֵיהֶם אֲבָנִים גְּדֹלוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** And the stones **descended because of a man; that is Joshua,** who was also called man, **as it is written: "Take Joshua the son of Nun, a man in whom is spirit"** (Numbers 27:18). **And it is written** that when Joshua and his people waged war against the army of the Emorite kings, God told him not to fear them because God would deliver them into his hands; and indeed, they died by means of these stones: **"As they fled from before Israel, while they were at the descent of Beit Ḥoron, that the Lord cast down great stones** from heaven upon them unto Azeka, and they died; they were more who died with the hailstones than they whom the children of Israel slew by sword" (Joshua 10:11).

They then descended upon another person: this is Joshua, of whom the verse says *kach lecha et Yehoshua bin Nun ish asher ruach bo* — Joshua is also called *ish*. And the verse recounts: *va-yehi benosam mipnei Bnei Yisrael hem be-morad Beit Choron va-Hashem hishlich aleihem avanim gedolot* — when the enemies fled before the Children of Israel at the descent of Beit Choron, God hurled great stones upon them. These are the very hailstones that had been suspended since the time of Moses and now came down upon Israel's enemies at Beit Choron. Thus the site of the descent of Beit Choron is a place where one recites a *bracha* — and at least that second location is a definite geographic site.

## Og's Mountain

### Og Plans Attack

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶבֶן שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ עוֹג מֶלֶךְ הַבָּשָׁן לִזְרוֹק עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ. אֲמַר מַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל כַּמָּה הָוֵי — תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי, אֵיזֵיל וְאֶיעֱקַר טוּרָא בַּר תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי וְאִישְׁדֵּי עֲלַיְיהוּ, וְאִיקְטְלִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the **rock that Og, King of Bashan, sought to throw upon Israel,** there is no biblical reference, but rather **a tradition was transmitted.** The Gemara relates that Og **said: How** large **is the camp of Israel?** It is **three parasangs. I will go and uproot a mountain three parasangs** long **and I will hurl it upon them and kill them. He went, uprooted a mountain three parasangs** long, **and brought it on his head.**

The *baraita* next mentions the stone that Og King of Bashan sought to hurl at Israel. The Gemara states plainly: *gemara gemiri lah* — this is a transmitted tradition, not derived from a *pasuk*. What follows is entirely *aggadic*.

Og calculated the size of the Israelite camp: three *parsa'ot*, roughly twelve kilometers across. He reasoned: if I can uproot a mountain the size of the entire encampment and hurl it down upon them, I can destroy them all at once. He went and uprooted such a mountain and carried it above his head, intending to cast it upon Israel.

### Mountain Destroyed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל עֲקַר טוּרָא בַּר תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי וְאַיְיתִי עַל רֵישֵׁיהּ, וְאַיְיתִי קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא עֲלֵיהּ קַמְצֵי וְנַקְבוּהּ, וּנְחֵית בְּצַוְּארֵיהּ. הֲוָה בָּעֵי לְמִשְׁלְפֵהּ, מָשְׁכִי שִׁינֵּיהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא וּלְהַאי גִּיסָא וְלָא מָצֵי לְמִשְׁלְפֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** God then sent grasshoppers — or some creature capable of rapid destruction — to gnaw holes through the mountain as Og held it aloft. The mountain cracked and split, and rather than being hurled forward, it collapsed down over Og's own head, settling around his neck. He attempted to pull it off, *hava bei le-mishlafeh*, but could not.

### Og's Teeth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׁנֵּי רְשָׁעִים שִׁבַּרְתָּ״. וְכִדְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״שִׁנֵּי רְשָׁעִים שִׁבַּרְתָּ״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי ״שִׁבַּרְתָּ״ אֶלָּא ״שִׁרְבַּבְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is what is written: "You break the teeth of the wicked"** (Psalms 3:8). **And** this is **in accordance with** the homiletic interpretation of **Rabbi Shimon Ben Lakish, as Rabbi Shimon Ben Lakish said: What** is the meaning of **that which is written: "You break the teeth of the wicked"? Do not read** it as: **You break [*shibarta*], but rather** as: **You lengthened [*shirbavta*].**

As Og struggled to free himself, his teeth extended outward in each direction — *mashchei shinei le-hai gisa u-le-hai gisa* — and hooked into the holes in the mountain, making it impossible to remove. This is the fulfillment of the verse *shinei rasha'im shibarta* — You have broken the teeth of the wicked. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish teaches: do not read *shibarta* (you broke) but *shirvavta* (you extended). The verse describes not breaking but a grotesque elongation that trapped Og by his own teeth.

### Moses Defeats Og

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֹשֶׁה כַּמָּה הֲוָה — עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, שְׁקֵיל נַרְגָּא בַּר עֲשַׂר אַמִּין, שְׁוַור עֲשַׂר אַמִּין, וּמַחְיֵיהּ בְּקַרְסוּלֵּיהּ וְקַטְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The story concludes: **How** tall **was Moses?** He was **ten cubits** tall. **He took an axe ten cubits** long, **jumped** up **ten cubits, and struck** Og **in the ankle and killed him.**

Moses then came to finish the battle. How was this possible, given Og's stature? The Gemara establishes that Moses was ten *amot* tall — derived from elsewhere in the Gemara, on the grounds that Moses could spread the rooftop tapestries over the *Ohel Moed*, which was itself ten *amot* high, and he could only have done so without a ladder if he were that tall himself. Moses took an axe that was also ten *amot* long, then jumped ten *amot* upward — placing himself thirty *amot* off the ground, roughly forty-five feet in the air — and struck Og at the ankle, *u-machyei be-karsulei*, and killed him.

The story is, of course, fantastical in the extreme — a theater of the absurd, one might say: a mountain cracking over a giant's head, his teeth caught in the rubble, while Moses leaps thirty *amot* to deliver the coup de grâce at the ankle. Yet the *baraita* treats it with full seriousness, requiring a *bracha* upon the site. One might speculate that the practical likelihood of ever encountering this stone is vanishingly small, which perhaps renders the obligation theoretical — but nonetheless the Gemara does not distance itself from the account. The *midrash* about the stone, incidentally, is more modest than the full narrative the Gemara constructs around it; the *baraita* mentions only a stone, while the Gemara elaborates into something considerably more elaborate.

## Stone Moses Sat

### Stone at Amalek War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶבֶן שֶׁיָּשַׁב עָלֶיהָ מֹשֶׁה — דִּכְתִיב: ״וִידֵי מֹשֶׁה כְּבֵדִים וַיִּקְחוּ אֶבֶן וַיָּשִׂימוּ תַחְתָּיו וַיֵּשֶׁב עָלֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** One must recite a blessing when he sees the **rock upon which Moses sat, as it is written: "But Moses' hands were heavy; and they took a stone and put it under him, and he sat thereon"** (Exodus 17:12).

The *baraita* also includes the stone upon which Moses sat during Joshua's war against Amalek. What is noteworthy here is that the natural expectation might have been to mark the location of the battle itself as the place of the miracle. Instead, the *baraita* focuses on the specific stone where Moses sat — the place where Moses held his hands aloft and thereby determined the outcome of the war. The emphasis is on the object and Moses' role rather than the battlefield itself.

## Lot & Jericho

### Lot's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל לוֹט — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתַּבֵּט אִשְׁתּוֹ מֵאַחֲרָיו וַתְּהִי נְצִיב מֶלַח״. וְחוֹמַת יְרִיחוֹ שֶׁנִּבְלְעָה — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתִּפֹּל הַחוֹמָה תַּחְתֶּיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one must recite a blessing upon seeing **Lot's wife, as it is stated: "But his wife looked back from behind him, and she became a pillar of salt"** (Genesis 19:26). **And the wall of Jericho that was swallowed, as it is written: "And the wall fell down flat"** (Joshua 6:20).

The *baraita* continues with the pillar of salt that was Lot's wife — derived from the verse *va-tabet ishto me-acharav va-tehi netziv melach*, his wife looked behind her and became a pillar of salt — and the wall of Jericho that was absorbed in its place.

### Punishment vs Salvation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא כּוּלְּהוּ — נִיסָּא, אֶלָּא אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל לוֹט פּוּרְעָנוּתָא הוּא! — דְּאָמַר ״בָּרוּךְ … דַּיַּין הָאֱמֶת״. וְהָא ״הוֹדָאָה וָשֶׁבַח״ קָתָנֵי! תְּנִי: עַל לוֹט וְעַל אִשְׁתּוֹ מְבָרְכִים שְׁתַּיִם. עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ אוֹמֵר ״בָּרוּךְ … דַּיַּין הָאֱמֶת״, וְעַל לוֹט אוֹמֵר ״בָּרוּךְ … זוֹכֵר אֶת הַצַּדִּיקִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** that one recites a blessing on **all of these.** They **are miracles; however, Lot's wife is a tragedy.** Why recite a blessing on a tragedy? The Gemara answers: One who sees that place **recites: Blessed…the true Judge. But** the *baraita* teaches that for all of these one must give **thanks and** offer **praise?** The Gemara answers: The language of the *baraita* should be emended **and teach: Over Lot and his wife one recites two blessings. Over his wife he recites: Blessed…the true Judge, and on Lot he recites: Blessed…Who remembers the righteous.**

The Gemara raises a sharp challenge. It is understandable that all the others are miracles, *bishlama kulu nisa* — but the fate of Lot's wife was a punishment, *puranuta hi*. Even granting that it occurred through miraculous means, one might have thought it makes no difference whether the miracle involved salvation or punishment. Yet the Gemara's reasoning here is that a *bracha* of thanks is appropriate specifically for a miracle of salvation, not for one of punishment.

### Specific Blessings

This is a striking passage for several reasons. First, if we follow those *rishonim* who hold that these *brachot* require *shem u-malchut*, then we are continually encountering new formulations of blessings — not simply the generic *she-asa nes* formula of the *mishnah*, but novel texts. Second, this is also the first miracle in the entire list that is not a national Jewish experience but rather a family matter of Abraham's relative, and one that occurred long before Sinai. Yet the Gemara creates a specific *bracha* for it. Third, the notion of reciting *Baruch Dayan HaEmet* upon seeing something that evokes divine judgment — even without it being a recent death or a fresh tragedy — is familiar from the practice of reciting it upon visiting a cemetery. Here too, the sight evokes the appropriate response regardless of when the event occurred.

### God Remembers Righteous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת כַּעֲסוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, זוֹכֵר אֶת הַצַּדִּיקִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בְּשַׁחֵת אֱלֹהִים אֶת עָרֵי הַכִּכָּר וַיִּזְכֹּר אֱלֹהִים אֶת אַבְרָהָם וַיְשַׁלַּח אֶת לוֹט מִתּוֹךְ הַהֲפֵכָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** As **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** From the story of Lot it is possible to learn that **even during a time of wrath of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** He **remembers the righteous, as it is stated: "And it came to pass, when God destroyed the cities of the plain, that God remembered Abraham and sent Lot out of the midst of the overthrow, when He overthrew the cities in which Lot dwelt"** (Genesis 19:29).

Rabbi Yochanan draws out a theological lesson from the *Baruch Zocher HaTzadikim* formulation: even at the moment of God's anger, He remembers the righteous. The verse states, *va-yehi be-shachet Elohim et arei ha-kikar va-yizkor Elohim et Avraham va-yeshalach et Lot mi-toch ha-hafecha* — when God was destroying the cities of the plain, He remembered Abraham and sent Lot out from the upheaval. The deeper point here engages with other *midrashim* that suggest that at moments of wholesale destruction, the destructive force does not discriminate between righteous and wicked — as in the command on the night of *Pesach* that Israel remain indoors, *ve-lo yiten ha-mashchit lavo el bateichem linigof*. Rabbi Yochanan counters: no, God does remember the righteous even in such moments. Lot, who was arguably not himself fully righteous, was saved for the sake of Abraham.

### Jericho Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוֹמַת יְרִיחוֹ שֶׁנִּבְלְעָה. וְחוֹמַת יְרִיחוֹ נִבְלְעָה? וְהָא נָפְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי כִשְׁמֹעַ הָעָם אֶת קוֹל הַשּׁוֹפָר וַיָּרִיעוּ הָעָם תְּרוּעָה גְדוֹלָה וַתִּפֹּל הַחוֹמָה תַּחְתֶּיהָ״! — כֵּיוָן דְּפוּתְיַהּ וְרוּמַהּ כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אִבַּלְעָה בְּלוֹעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also taught that we recite a blessing for **the wall of Jericho that was swallowed** up in its place. The Gemara asks: **Were the walls of Jericho swallowed up** into the ground? **Didn't they fall, as it is stated: "And it came to pass, when the people heard the sound of the *shofar*, that the people shouted with a great shout, and the wall fell down flat"** (Joshua 6:20)? The Gemara explains: **Since the width and height** of the walls **were equal to one another, therefore, they were swallowed.** Had they merely fallen it would have had no effect, as their width was equal to their height.

Returning to the wall of Jericho, the Gemara asks: the *baraita* says the wall was *absorbed* in its place — but the verse says it *fell*, *va-tipol ha-choma tachtecha*, the wall fell beneath itself. How are these reconciled?

The Gemara's answer involves geometry. The wall of Jericho was as wide as it was tall — *potya ve-ruma ki hadadi ninhu*, its width and its height were equal. Therefore if the wall had simply tipped over and fallen to the ground, the resulting pile would be as high as the wall was wide — which is the same as its original height. It would not have truly "fallen" in any meaningful sense of being brought low. For the wall to genuinely collapse, it had to be absorbed downward into the ground. Hence the *baraita* correctly describes the miracle as one of absorption.

## Four Thanksgivings

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַרְבָּעָה צְרִיכִין לְהוֹדוֹת: יוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם, הוֹלְכֵי מִדְבָּרוֹת, וּמִי שֶׁהָיָה חוֹלֶה וְנִתְרַפֵּא, וּמִי שֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים וְיָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Four must** offer **thanks** to God with a thanks-offering and a special blessing. They are: **Seafarers, those who walk in the desert, and one who was ill and recovered, and one who was incarcerated in prison and went out.** All of these appear in the verses of a psalm (Psalms 107).

The Gemara transitions from these historical miracle sites to a more universally applicable *halacha*. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: four categories of people are obligated to give thanks — *arbaah tzrichim lehodot*. All four are rooted in Psalm 107, a *perek* of *Tehillim* devoted entirely to the theme of divine deliverance and gratitude. The Gemara also notes the connection to the *Korban Todah*, the thanksgiving offering described in the Torah, which the Torah does not explicitly associate with particular triggering circumstances. These four categories supply those circumstances.

### Seafarers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם מְנָלַן? — דִּכְתִיב: ״יוֹרְדֵי הַיָּם בָּאֳנִיּוֹת וְגוֹ׳ הֵמָּה רָאוּ מַעֲשֵׂי ה׳״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיַּעֲמֵד רוּחַ סְעָרָה יַעֲלוּ שָׁמַיִם יֵרְדוּ תְהוֹמוֹת״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יָחוֹגּוּ וְיָנוּעוּ כַּשִּׁכּוֹר״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּצְעֲקוּ אֶל ה׳ בַּצַּר לָהֶם וּמִמְּצוּקֹתֵיהֶם יוֹצִיאֵם״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יָקֵם סְעָרָה לִדְמָמָה״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּשְׂמְחוּ כִי יִשְׁתֹּקוּ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יוֹדוּ לַה׳ חַסְדּוֹ וְנִפְלְאוֹתָיו לִבְנֵי אָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **From where do we** derive that **seafarers** are required to thank God?  **As it is written: "They who go down to the sea in ships,** who do business in great waters; **they see the works of the Lord"** (Psalms 107:23–24).  **And it says: "For He commands and raises the stormy wind** which lifts up the waves thereof.  **They mount up to the heaven, they go down again to the depths:** their soul is melted because of trouble" (Psalms 107:25–26).  **And it says: "They reel to and fro, and stagger like a drunken man,** and are at their wits' end."  **And it says** immediately thereafter: **"Then they cry unto the Lord in their trouble, and He brings them out of their distress"** (Psalms 107:28). **And it says: "He makes the storm calm,** so the waves thereof are still" (Psalms 107:29),  **and it says: "Then are they glad because they be quiet;** so He brings them unto their desired haven" (Psalms 107:30),  **and it says: "They are grateful to God for His loving-kindness and His wonders for mankind"** (Psalms 107:31).

The first category is *yordei hayam* — those who descend to the sea in ships. The Gemara quotes the relevant *pesukim* from Psalm 107: they saw the works of God and His wonders in the deep; He spoke and raised a stormy wind that lifted the waves to the heavens and plunged them to the depths; they reeled like drunkards; they cried out to God in their distress; He stilled the storm to a calm; they rejoiced; *yodu la-Hashem chasdo* — they gave thanks to God for His kindness and His wonders to humankind. The theme throughout is crisis, prayer, deliverance, and the obligation to offer public gratitude.

### Desert Travelers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹלְכֵי מִדְבָּרוֹת מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״תָּעוּ בַמִּדְבָּר בִּישִׁימוֹן דָּרֶךְ עִיר מוֹשָׁב לֹא מָצָאוּ … וַיִּצְעֲקוּ אֶל ה׳ … וַיַּדְרִיכֵם בְּדֶרֶךְ יְשָׁרָה … יוֹדוּ לַה׳ חַסְדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **those who walk in the desert** are required to thank God? The Gemara answers:  **As it is written** in the same psalm:  **"They wandered in the wilderness in a solitary way; they found no city in which to dwell"** (Psalms 107:4), **"And then they cried unto the Lord** in their trouble, and He delivered them out of their distresses.  **And He led them forth by the right way"** (Psalms 107:6–7). After God guides them on the right way, it is said: **"They are grateful to God for His goodness"** (Psalms 107:8).

The second category is *holchei midbarot* — those who travel in the wilderness or desert. The *pasuk*: they wandered in the desert in desolate places, found no inhabited city, cried out to God, and were led on a straight path. Again the concluding injunction: *yodu la-Hashem chasdo*.

### Healed from Sickness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁחָלָה וְנִתְרַפֵּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֱוִילִים מִדֶּרֶךְ פִּשְׁעָם וּמֵעֲוֹנֹתֵיהֶם יִתְעַנּוּ. כׇּל אֹכֶל תְּתַעֵב נַפְשָׁם וְגוֹ׳ וַיִּזְעֲקוּ אֶל ה׳ בַּצַּר לָהֶם וְגוֹ׳ יִשְׁלַח דְּבָרוֹ וְיִרְפָּאֵם וְגוֹ׳ יוֹדוּ לַה׳ חַסְדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** That **one who was ill and recovered** must offer thanks is derived, **as it is written:**  **"Fools, because of their transgression and because of their iniquities, are afflicted.** **Their soul abhors all manner of food** and they draw near unto the gates of death" (Psalms 107:17–18),  and: **"Then they cry unto the Lord in their trouble,** and He saves them from their distress" (Psalms 107:19),  and then: **"He sent His word and healed them,** and delivered them from their destructions" (Psalms 107:20).  After they are healed: **"They are grateful to God for His goodness"** (Psalms 107:21).

The third category is those who were sick and recovered. What is notable here is that the *pesukim* explicitly frame this as a consequence of sin: *evilim mi-derech pish'am u-me-avonoteihem yitanu* — the fools, through their sinful path, are afflicted because of their iniquities. Their appetite fails; they cry out to God; He sends His word and heals them. Unlike the seafarer or the desert traveler, whose danger is circumstantial, the sick person's affliction is portrayed as arising from moral failure. The obligation of thanksgiving nonetheless applies equally.

### Released Prisoners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין מְנָלַן? — דִּכְתִיב: ״יֹשְׁבֵי חֹשֶׁךְ וְצַלְמָוֶת וְגוֹ׳ כִּי הִמְרוּ אִמְרֵי אֵל וְגוֹ׳״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיַּכְנַע בֶּעָמָל לִבָּם וְגוֹ׳״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּזְעֲקוּ אֶל ה׳ בַּצַּר לָהֶם״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יוֹצִיאֵם מֵחֹשֶׁךְ וְצַלְמָוֶת וְגוֹ׳״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יוֹדוּ לַה׳ חַסְדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** do we derive that **one who was incarcerated in prison** must offer thanks? **As it is written: "Such as sit in darkness and in the shadow of death,** bound in affliction and iron.  **Because they rebelled against the words of God** and scorned the counsel of the most High" (Psalms 107:10–11).  **And it says: "Therefore He brought down their heart with labor;** they fell down, and there was none to help" (Psalms 107:12),  **and it says: "Then they cried unto the Lord in their trouble,** and He saved them from their distresses" (Psalms 107:13),  **and it says: "He brought them out of darkness and the shadow of death,**  and broke their shackles" (Psalms 107:14).  **And** after God takes them out from that darkness and shadow of death, **it says: "They are grateful to God for His goodness."**

The fourth category is those who were imprisoned and freed. Here again, sin is implicated — *ki himru imrei el*, because they rebelled against God's word. They dwelt in darkness and the shadow of death; God humbled their hearts through their affliction; they cried out; He brought them out of darkness. *Yodu la-Hashem chasdo*. The recurring structure across all four categories is striking: crisis, divine abandonment or distance, cry to God, deliverance, and mandated gratitude.

## Blessing Formula

### Gomel Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״בָּרוּךְ גּוֹמֵל חֲסָדִים טוֹבִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What blessing** does he **recite? Rav Yehuda said: Blessed is…Who bestows acts of loving-kindness.**

Having established the four categories, the Gemara asks: what precisely does one recite? Rav Yehuda states: *Baruch gomel chasadim tovim* — Blessed is He who bestows good kindnesses. This is the basis of the familiar *birkat ha-gomel*, though our standard *nusach* — *Baruch Atah Hashem ha-gomel le-chayavim tovot she-gemalani kol tov* — is somewhat more elaborate.

### Ten Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: וְצָרִיךְ לְאוֹדוֹיֵי קַמֵּי עַשְׂרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וִירוֹמְמוּהוּ בִּקְהַל עָם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: And he must** offer **thanks before ten** people, **as it is written** in the same chapter: **"Let them exalt Him also in the congregation of the people** and praise Him in the assembly of the elders" (Psalms 107:32), and congregation indicates a group of at least ten.

Abaye adds a procedural requirement: one must recite this *bracha* before ten people, *tzarich le-oduyei kammai asara*. The source is the verse from Psalm 107: *ve-yeromemuhu bi-kehal am* — they shall exalt Him in the congregation of the people. A *kahal* constitutes a quorum of ten.

### Two Sages Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: וּתְרֵין מִינַּיְיהוּ רַבָּנַן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְמוֹשַׁב זְקֵנִים יְהַלְלוּהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said: Two of them** must be **Sages, as it is stated** there: **"And praise Him in the assembly of elders."** These elders are the Sages, and the use of the plural indicates a minimum of two.

Mar Zutra adds a further requirement: two of those ten must be Torah sages, *rabanan*. His source is the continuation of the verse: *u-ve-moshav zekenim yehaleluhu* — and in the assembly of elders they shall praise Him. *Zekenim* here refers to sages, and since the plural implies at least two, two of the ten must be learned figures.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: וְאֵימָא כּוּלְּהוּ רַבָּנַן?! — מִי כְּתִיב ״בִּקְהַל זְקֵנִים״? ״בִּקְהַל עָם״ כְּתִיב. וְאֵימָא בֵּי עַשְׂרָה שְׁאָר עַמָּא, וּתְרֵי רַבָּנַן! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi strongly objects to this: Say** that **all of them** must be **Sages.** The Gemara rejects this: **Is it written: In the congregation of elders? In the congregation of the people is written;** and the Sages are among them. Yet there is still room to object: **Say** that **ten** are from **the rest of the people,** and in addition there must be **two Sages.** No satisfactory answer was found, and the question remains **difficult,** although the *halakha* was not rejected.

Rav Ashi challenges this: perhaps all ten should be sages, since the verse says *zekenim*? Mar Zutra's position is that the primary requirement is a *kahal am* — a general congregation of people — and the *zekenim* are an additional specification. But then Rav Ashi raises a further difficulty: perhaps one needs ten regular people *plus* two sages separately, not two sages included within the ten? The Gemara concludes *kashya* — this remains a problem with no definitive resolution. In practice, the requirement of two sages has not been maintained with great precision; what is observed is the *minyan* of ten.

## Rav Yehuda's Recovery

### Rav Yehuda Heals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה חֲלַשׁ וְאִתְּפַח. עָל לְגַבֵּיהּ רַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה וְרַבָּנַן. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: ״בְּרִיךְ רַחֲמָנָא דְּיַהֲבָךְ נִיהֲלַן וְלָא יַהֲבָךְ לְעַפְרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Yehuda fell sick and recovered, Rav Ḥana of Baghdad and the Sages entered to** visit **him. They said to him: Blessed is God Who gave you to us and did not give you to the dust.**

The Gemara now recounts a related incident. Rav Yehuda became ill and recovered. Rav Chana Bagdata'ah came to visit him together with the rabbis — presumably his students — and they said to him: *brich rachmana di-yavach n'halan ve-lo yavach le-afra* — Blessed is God who has given you back to us and has not given you to the dust. A gracious and touching formulation.

### Exemption Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: פְּטַרְתּוּן יָתִי מִלְּאוֹדוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** He **said to them: You have exempted me from** offering **thanks,** as your statement fulfilled his obligation for him.

Rav Yehuda then said to them: *patartun yati me-l'oduyei* — you have exempted me from giving thanks. By praising God on his behalf, he claimed, they had fulfilled his obligation for him.

### Amen as Equivalent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי בָּעֵי אוֹדוֹיֵי בְּאַפֵּי עַשְׂרָה! — דַּהֲווֹ בֵּי עַשְׂרָה. וְהָא אִיהוּ לָא קָא מוֹדֵה! — לָא צְרִיךְ, דְּעָנֵי בָּתְרַיְיהוּ ״אָמֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Abaye say** that **one must offer thanks before ten?** The Gemara answers: **There were ten** people there when the Sages blessed God in Rav Yehuda's presence. The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But** Rav Yehuda **did not offer thanks** himself; others offered thanks on his behalf. The Gemara answers: **He did not need** to recite it himself **as he answered amen after their** blessing. Answering amen after a blessing is tantamount to reciting the blessing himself.

The Gemara objects: but Abaye ruled that the thanksgiving must be recited before ten — implying that the one who experienced the deliverance must be the one who gives thanks. The Gemara resolves this: there were in fact ten people present. But then the Gemara presses further: Rav Yehuda himself did not give thanks — it was others who praised God. The resolution: *lo tzarich de-ani batraihu amen* — he had answered *amen* after them, and that is sufficient.

This is a remarkable principle in context. Throughout the tractate, the question of whether *shome'a ke-oneh* — hearing as equivalent to reciting — applies in various settings has been a recurring theme in discussions of *tefillah*, *birchot ha-nehenin*, and *birkat ha-mazon*. Here we see an even bolder application: the power of *amen* is such that when another person recites a *bracha* on your behalf and you answer *amen*, it is treated as equivalent to your own recitation, at least for the purposes of *birkat ha-gomel*.

Also worth noting: the blessing as recited here had no *shem u-malchut* — no formal divine name and kingship formula. Tosafot addresses this, noting that Rav Yehuda was a student of Rav, and Rav holds that *shem u-malchut* is not required for a *bracha*. But the question is broader: does this reflect a not-yet-fully-crystallized *nusach*, or does it reflect a deliberate halakhic position? The answer has implications for how one understands the entire institution of *birkat ha-gomel*. A suggestive parallel is the passage in the *Leil Seder* Haggadah, where one of the *amora'im* asked a slave what he would do if freed, and the slave responded with effusive gratitude; the *amora* then said, "You have exempted us from reciting *Ma Nishtana*." The principle that another person's expression of the relevant sentiment, offered in your presence, can fulfill your own obligation appears in both contexts, with appropriate differences.

## Three Need Protection

### Vulnerable Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁלֹשָׁה צְרִיכִין שִׁימּוּר, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן — חוֹלֶה, חָתָן, וְכַלָּה. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: חוֹלֶה, חַיָּה, חָתָן, וְכַלָּה. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף אָבֵל. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Incidental to Rav Yehuda's earlier statement, which organized several cases into a single category, the Gemara cites similar statements of his. **Rav Yehuda said: Three require protection** from harm: **A sick person, a bridegroom, and a bride. It was taught in a *baraita*: A sick person, a woman in childbirth, a bridegroom, and a bride** require protection from harm. **And some say: Even a mourner. And some say: Even Torah scholars at night.** Those whose thoughts are focused elsewhere or are in a weakened physical state require protection.

Rav Yehuda taught: *shlosha tzrichim shimur* — three categories of people require special protection, apparently from *mazikin*, harmful spirits, who are drawn to or jealous of those in particularly vulnerable states. These three are: a sick person (*choleh*), a bride and groom (*chatan ve-kallah*), and — implicit in the formulation — others to be added by the *baraita*. This is the source for the practice of having *shomrim*, guardians, accompany the *chatan ve-kallah*. Rashi explains that it is because the *mazikin* are jealous of the joy of the *chatan ve-kallah*.

### Extended Categories

The *baraita* adds further categories: a woman who has recently given birth (*chaya*) — for she is physically vulnerable in her weakened state — and, according to some, even a mourner (*avel*), apparently on the grounds that misfortune may attract further misfortune, or some similar vulnerability. And some say: even Torah scholars at night (*talmidei chachamim ba-lailah*), though the precise rationale here is not entirely clear from the text, possibly related to jealousy or to their spiritual intensity drawing unwanted attention. With this, the Gemara closes its discussion on 54b.
