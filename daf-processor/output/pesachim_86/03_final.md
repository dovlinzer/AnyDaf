# Pesachim 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Windows & Wall Status

### Intro: Doorways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הָאֲגַף וְלִפְנִים — כְּלִפְנִים, מִן הָאֲגַף וּלְחוּץ — כְּלַחוּץ. הַחַלּוֹנוֹת וְעוֹבִי הַחוֹמָה — כְּלִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** How does one determine the outer boundaries of a particular location? Anything that is located **from the** inside of the **doorway inward is** considered **as though** it is **inside,** and anything that is located **from the doorway outward is** considered **as though** it is **outside. And the windows** in the wall **and the thickness of the wall are** considered **as though** they are **inside,** such that an offering is considered to have exited the premises only if it is taken outside the wall.

The *mishna* with which our *daf* opens addresses what happens when a limb from the *korban pesach* exits its designated zone — the zone being *Yerushalayim*, though we had already learned that the prohibition also encompasses leaving the *chabura*, the group of participants eating the offering. The *mishna* further specified that the disqualified portion must be removed, a practically complicated matter given that the meat is still on the bone.

The final clause of the *mishna*, which is our immediate focus, concerns the status of the doorway: is the space within a doorway considered inside the city or outside? And what of the space where the door itself closes? The *mishna* rules that windows and the thickness of the wall (*ovi ha-choma*) are treated as though they are on the inside of the city. This produces a striking asymmetry: once one crosses to the outer side of the door, one is definitively outside — yet that same wall thickness, when approached via a window or from above, is considered inside. The determining factor is not the physical location within the wall but precisely where one stands in relation to it.

### Rambam: House Focus

*Rashi* understands this *mishna* to be discussing the walls encircling *Yerushalayim* and the question of when one has exited the city — since taking any *korban*, any *kodshim kalim*, out of *Yerushalayim* renders it invalid. *Rambam*, however, reads the *mishna* in a notably different way. He understands it to be discussing specifically the *korban pesach* and the physical *bayit* in which it is consumed, drawing on the verse *lo totzi min ha-basar min ha-bayit chutza* — one may not take the meat outside the house. What constitutes "outside the house"? What if one is eating in a window embrasure? What if one is at the top of a wall without a roof? What if one is in the doorway itself?

The *Rambam*'s reading connects to an issue raised earlier in the *masechta*: whether the prohibition is exclusively about leaving the *chabura*, or whether the physical house also matters as an independent boundary. For *Rambam*, it is clear that the physical house matters — and thus the *mishna* is defining what counts as "the house" for purposes of the *korban pesach*. As we will see, however, the *gemara* reads the *mishna* in terms of *Yerushalayim* and *kedusha*, which is precisely where *Rashi*'s reading is grounded.

### Rooftops Unsanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחַלּוֹנוֹת וָעוֹבִי הַחוֹמָה וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: גַּגִּין וַעֲלִיּוֹת לֹא נִתְקַדְּשׁוּ. אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא ״כְּזֵיתָא פִּסְחָא, וְהַלֵּילָא פָּקַע אִיגָּרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that **the windows and the thickness of the wall** are considered to be on the inside. **Rav said: Roofs and upper stories were not sanctified.** Therefore, the roofs of the chambers in the Temple courtyard did not have the sanctity of the courtyard, and the roofs of buildings in Jerusalem did not have the sanctity of Jerusalem. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rav say** the following aphorism **in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya: The Paschal lamb is** the size of **an olive-bulk,** as it was common for so many people to register for a single Paschal lamb that each one would receive only an olive-bulk of its meat. **And the *hallel*** that is said during its consumption **breaks the roof;** there were so many people who said *hallel* together, it seemed as though the roofs were breaking due to all the commotion and noise.

The *gemara* opens with a statement of *Rav*: *gagin va'aliyot lo nitkadshu* — rooftops and upper stories were not sanctified with the *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim*. This immediately raises a conceptual difficulty: all of *Yerushalayim* is an area in which *kodshim kalim* may be eaten. Does that *kedusha* extend only to ground level? Can one eat such offerings on a rooftop?

### Kedusha Concepts

The question, though initially strange, admits of a principled resolution. The *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim* can be understood in two distinct ways. One way is to see it as an extension of the *beit ha-mikdash* itself — a more distant zone of sanctity continuous with the inner sanctity of the Temple. From this perspective, the relevant analogy is the *kedusha* of the *beit ha-mikdash* floor, where the *kohanim* walk barefoot and the sanctity is specifically bound to the ground surface. We encountered this principle earlier in discussing *kohanim* walking through blood — the very floor of the *mikdash* is sanctified, and contact with it is structurally significant. If *Yerushalayim* is understood as an extension of that sanctity, one might argue that only ground-level space participates in it, just as the *mikdash* itself is conceptually anchored to the floor.

The second way to understand the *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim* is not as an extended *beit ha-mikdash* but as proximity — a kind of sacred neighborhood or suburb of the *mikdash*, where one's relationship to the city itself, rather than to a specific floor, defines the sanctity. Under this second conception, vertical position might be irrelevant. These two frameworks produce different conclusions about whether rooftops and upper stories share the *kedusha* of the city.

A further illustration of the first framework emerges from the *mitzvah* of *lulav*. The verse *vesamachtem lifnei Hashem Elokeichem shivat yamim* — rejoicing before God for seven days — was understood by *chazal* to require taking the *lulav* in *Yerushalayim* for all seven days as a *mitzvah d'Oraisa*. Yet no one suggests this applies only to ground level. That is because in this context, *Yerushalayim* functions as a place of proximity, not as an extension of the *mikdash* floor. By contrast, when it comes to eating *korbanot*, where the analogy to the *beit ha-mikdash* is most operative, the question of ground-level sanctity becomes real.

A related point arises regarding the ramp (*kevesh*) leading up to the *mizbeach*: the *kohen* walking up that ramp is not at ground level, yet the ramp is clearly part of the sanctified structure. The reason is that the ramp is a permanent, integral element of the *mikdash*'s architecture — there is no space beneath it — and as such it is treated as part of the ground. This distinction between permanent structural features and the more ambiguous status of rooftops and upper stories is conceptually useful, even if not fully determinative.

### Hallel Rooftop Image

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לָאו, דְּאָכְלִי בְּאִיגָּרָא וְאָמְרִי בְּאִיגָּרָא! לָא: דְּאָכְלִי בְּאַרְעָא וְאָמְרִי בְּאִיגָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** the case **that they eat** the Paschal lamb **on the roof and say** *hallel* **on the roof,** which would mean that roofs have the sanctity of Jerusalem? The Gemara responds: **No,** there is no proof from here, as it is possible **that they eat on the ground and say** *hallel* **on the roof.**

The *gemara* then challenges *Rav*'s ruling with a poetic statement transmitted by *Rav* in the name of *Rabbi Chiya*: *k'zayita Pischa v'Hallel paka igra* — when each person receives only a *k'zayit* of the *korban pesach* it means there is a great crowd, and their *Hallel* bursts through the rooftops. It is a vivid image: the house packed with people, each receiving just a *k'zayit*, and the power of their song overflowing. The phrase also rhymes in Aramaic, giving it an almost musical quality.

The challenge is: does this not imply that people were eating and saying *Hallel* on the rooftop itself — *d'achli b'igra v'amri b'igra*? The *gemara* rejects this reading: *d'achli b'ara v'amri b'igra* — they ate at ground level and then, after finishing, ascended to the rooftop to continue with *Hallel*. But then a further problem arises.

### Afikoman & Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָתְנַן: אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַפֶּסַח אֲפִיקוֹמָן, וְאָמַר רַב: שֶׁלֹּא יֵעָקְרוּ מֵחֲבוּרָה לַחֲבוּרָה. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בִּשְׁעַת אֲכִילָה, כָּאן — שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת אֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't we learn** in a mishna: **We do not conclude after** eating **the Paschal lamb with *afikoman*, and Rav said** that this means that after eating the Paschal lamb one may not say: *Afikoman*, which refers to removing the utensils in order to go eat somewhere else, because **they may not uproot** themselves **from** one **group to** another **group.** This would indicate that one must complete the entire Passover seder in one place. The Gemara answers: **It is not difficult. Here,** where it says that one may not change places, it is **during the time of eating. There,** where it says they would say *hallel* on the roof, it is **not during the time of eating.**

We have a *mishna* that rules *ein maftirin achar ha-pesach afikoman*. The word *afikoman* is Greek. The Babylonian *Talmud* did not work from its Greek etymology — the *chachamim* therefore offered various interpretations of the word. *Rav* explains it to mean that after the *korban pesach* has been eaten, one must not uproot oneself from one *chabura* and join another *chabura*. We already learned that one cannot physically transfer the *pesach* from one group to another; *Rav* adds that even after eating is complete, one should not go and say *Hallel* together with a different group. The imagery evoked is that of a livelier gathering next door and the temptation to join their celebration. In fact, the word *afikoman* in Greek apparently refers to precisely this phenomenon — going from party to party after a meal — which *chazal* prohibited. Given this ruling, how could the people in the *gemara*'s scenario move up to the rooftop to say *Hallel*, leaving the location where they ate?

### Post-Meal Movement

The *gemara* resolves this with a distinction: *lo kashya — kan b'sha'at achila, kan shelo b'sha'at achila*. The prohibition against leaving one's group applies during the time of eating — even if one has personally finished, leaving at that moment creates the appearance of joining another group's eating. But once the time of eating has concluded for everyone, there is greater latitude of movement. It is then permitted to move — for instance, ascending to the rooftop — because one is not joining another group's meal but simply relocating after the meal is over.

One might have thought the distinction would turn on whether one joins another *chabura* or merely moves within one's own group. But the *gemara*'s principle is temporal rather than spatial: what matters is whether the time of eating has passed.

### Upper Chamber Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: עֲלִיַּית בֵּית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים חֲמוּרָה מִבֵּית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים. שֶׁבֵּית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים — כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל נִכְנָס לוֹ פַּעַם אַחַת בַּשָּׁנָה, וַעֲלִיַּית בֵּית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים — אֵין נִכְנָסִין לָהּ אֶלָּא פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשָׁבוּעַ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: פַּעֲמַיִם בְּשָׁבוּעַ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: פַּעַם אַחַת בְּיוֹבֵל, לֵידַע מָה הִיא צְרִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to bring another proof with regard to this issue. **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this issue based upon the following *baraita*: **Abba Shaul says: The upper story of the chamber of the Holy of Holies is more stringent** with regard to the prohibition to enter it **than the chamber of the Holy of Holies** itself. How so? With regard to **the chamber of the Holy of Holies, the High Priest would enter it once a year** on Yom Kippur in order to offer the incense and sprinkle the blood between the staves of the ark, whereas **the upper story of the chamber of the Holy of Holies is not entered except** for **once in seven years; and some say twice in seven years, and some say** just **one time in a Jubilee,** i.e., once in fifty years, **to see what it needs** in case there are renovations that must be done. This indicates that the sanctity of the upper story was even greater than that of the Sanctuary itself.

The *gemara* now brings a further challenge from a *baraita* attributed to *Abba Shaul*: *Aliyat Beit Kodesh Kadashim chamura mi-Beit Kodesh Kadashim* — the upper chamber above the *Kodesh Kadashim* is treated with even greater stringency than the *Kodesh Kadashim* itself. This is noteworthy given that today's *daf* would have ideally been studied on *Yom Kippur*, when the *Kodesh Kadashim* itself is so central.

The distinction is this: *Shebeit Kodesh Kadashim Kohen Gadol nichnas lo pa'am achas ba-shana* — the *Kohen Gadol* entered the *Kodesh Kadashim* once a year, on *Yom Kippur*. The upper chamber, however, was entered only once every seven years, or according to some opinions twice every seven years, and according to yet others only once every fifty years — *leda ma hi tzericha*, to inspect whether it required repairs. In one sense, this makes the upper chamber less significant: it was visited only for functional, structural purposes and had no ongoing ritual role. Yet its extreme infrequency of access was understood as reflecting a kind of heightened *chamur* quality.

The *Maharsha* connects this to a famous *midrash* regarding *sukkah*: the divine presence (*Shechina*) never descended below ten *tefachim*, and no human ever ascended within ten *tefachim* of the heavens — the impossibility of actual contact between the human and the divine, like the famous image of Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel ceiling where God and Adam's fingers do not quite touch. By this reading, since the *Shechina* resides in the *Kodesh Kadashim* but cannot descend fully below a certain threshold, the chamber *above* the *Kodesh Kadashim* is in some sense even closer to the divine presence than the room below it.

Whatever the theological rationale, the practical import of *Abba Shaul*'s ruling for our *gemara* is this: minimally, it implies that the chamber above the *Kodesh Kadashim* possessed the *kedusha* of the *mikdash* — otherwise, why speak of it in terms of greater *chamur* than the *Kodesh Kadashim*? And if an upper story has *kedusha*, it would seem to undercut *Rav*'s ruling that rooftops and upper stories were not sanctified.

### Access Defines Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מֵהֵיכָל נֵיקוּם וְנִיתֵּיב אִינִישׁ? שָׁנֵי הֵיכָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתֵּן דָּוִד לִשְׁלֹמֹה בְנוֹ אֶת תַּבְנִית הָאוּלָם וְאֶת בָּתָּיו וְאֶת גַּנְזַכָּיו וַעֲלִיֹּתָיו וַחֲדָרָיו הַפְּנִימִים וּבֵית הַכַּפּוֹרֶת״, וּכְתִיב: ״הַכֹּל בִּכְתָב מִיַּד ה׳ עָלַי הִשְׂכִּיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Can a person get up and ask** based on the **Sanctuary? The Sanctuary is different, as it is written: "Then David gave to Solomon his son the pattern of the Entrance Hall of the Temple, and of its houses, and of its treasuries, and of its upper rooms, and of its inner chambers, and of the place of the ark cover"** (I Chronicles 28:11), **and it is written** with regard to the plans for the construction of the Temple: **"All this is put in writing by the hand of the Lord, Who has instructed me"** (I Chronicles 28:19). Therefore, the general rules of sanctity of roofs do not apply to the Sanctuary, as the details of its construction were determined through divine inspiration.

*Rav Yosef* pushes back: can one really derive a proof from the *Heichal*? By the *Heichal*, the verse states explicitly: *Vayiten David li-Shlomo b'no et tavnit ha-ulam* — David gave Shlomo the plans for the *ulam*, its chambers, its treasury rooms, *ve'aliyotav* — and its upper stories, *ve-Beit ha-Kapporet* — and even the *Kodesh Kadashim* where the *aron* stood. And the verse continues: *Hakol bi-chtav mi-yad Hashem alai hiskil* — everything came from God, God gave me the blueprints directly. Since the *Heichal* and all its upper stories were part of the divinely transmitted plan, it is unsurprising that those second-story chambers were sanctified. That tells us nothing about *Yerushalayim* generally or about the status of rooftops there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַלְּשָׁכוֹת הַבְּנוּיוֹת בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ וּפְתוּחוֹת לַחוֹל — תּוֹכָן חוֹל, וְגַגּוֹתֵיהֶן קוֹדֶשׁ! תַּרְגְּמָא רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּשֶׁגַּגּוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁוִין לְקַרְקַע עֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another possible proof based on the following mishna: With regard to **the chambers** in the Temple **that were built in the sanctified** area **and open to the unsanctified** area of the Temple Mount, **their interiors are unsanctified and their roofs are sanctified.** This states explicitly that the roofs are sanctified. The Gemara answers: **Rav Ḥisda interpreted it** as referring to chambers that were built under the Temple courtyard, and **their roofs were level with the ground of the courtyard.** In this case, the reason their roofs were sanctified is because they were part of the courtyard itself.

The *gemara* then attempts a proof from elsewhere in the *mikdash* itself — not from the *Heichal* specifically: *ha-lishkot ha-bnuyot ba-kodesh u-ptuchot la-chol, tochan chol, ve-gaggoteihem kodesh*. Chambers that were physically built within the sacred zone but whose doors opened to the non-sacred area — their interior is *chol* and their rooftops are *kodesh*.

To understand this ruling, one must picture the layout. Imagine a chamber built into the outer wall of the *mikdash* complex, physically embedded within the sacred zone, but with its door opening outward toward *Har ha-Bayit* — not into the *Azara* itself. In such a case, the ground-floor interior of the chamber is functionally associated with the outside space to which it opens; those entering and using it come from the outside. Its floor is therefore treated as *chol*. But its roof, physically located within the sanctified zone, is *kodesh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בְּנוּיוֹת בַּחוֹל וּפְתוּחוֹת לַקּוֹדֶשׁ — תּוֹכֶן קוֹדֶשׁ וְגַגּוֹתֵיהֶן חוֹל. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בְּשֶׁגַּגּוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁוִין לְקַרְקַע עֲזָרָה, הָוְיָא לַהּ מְחִילּוֹת, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְחִילּוֹת לֹא נִתְקַדְּשׁוּ! כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — בִּפְתוּחוֹת לְהַר הַבַּיִת. כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בִּפְתוּחוֹת לָעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, say the latter clause** of the mishna as follows: If the chambers were **built in the unsanctified** area **and open to the sanctified** area, **their interiors are sanctified and their roofs are unsanctified. And if it should enter your mind** to say that this is referring to chambers **whose roofs were level with the ground of the courtyard,** such chambers **would be tunnels, and Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **the tunnels were not sanctified.** The Gemara answers: **When Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that, he was referring **to** tunnels that were **open to the Temple Mount; when that *baraita*,** which says that the interiors were sanctified **was taught,** it was referring **to** rooms that **open to the** Temple **courtyard** itself.

The inverse ruling follows: *benuyot ba-chol u-ftuchot la-kodesh, tochan kodesh ve-gaggoteihem chol* — chambers built in the non-sacred zone but whose doors opened into the *kodesh* have their interiors treated as *kodesh* (because they are accessed and used by those in the sacred space), while their rooftops are *chol*. The governing principle is that access defines status: the interior of a space follows the direction from which it is entered and used. The rooftop, being equally accessible from neither side in the simple sense, follows the physical location of the wall in which the chamber is embedded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְחִילּוֹת מִתַּחַת הַהֵיכָל חוֹל! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — שֶׁפְּתוּחוֹת לַחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: The tunnels under the Sanctuary are unsanctified?** The Gemara answers: **When that *baraita* was taught,** it was pertaining to tunnels **that open to the unsanctified** area, which are considered unsanctified, as stated previously.

*Rav Chisda* then redirects the analysis. The *gemara* had been using this *mishna* as evidence that rooftops can be *kodesh*, but *Rav Chisda* explains: *targuma Rav Chisda be-she-gaggoteihen shavin le-karka Azara* — we are actually speaking of a chamber that is underground, recessed into the terrain, such that its roof is flush with the floor of the *Azara*. In that case, the "rooftop" is not elevated above ground at all — it is level with the *Azara* floor. The "roof" of the chamber is in fact the floor of the *Azara*, and its *kedusha* follows from that ground-level status. There is no proof here for elevated rooftops having *kedusha*.

The *gemara* then presses on the *seifa* of that same *mishna*. If *Rav Chisda*'s interpretation holds — that we are always talking about underground chambers — then when the *mishna* rules *tochan kodesh ve-gaggoteihem chol* for chambers built in the *chol* but opening into the *kodesh*, those "rooftops" would have to be flush with the non-sacred ground outside. But wait: if we are describing underground chambers throughout, we are talking about *mechilot* — tunnels. And *Rabbi Yochanan* taught explicitly: *mechilot lo nitkadshu* — the tunnels were not sanctified. How, then, can the *gemara* say *tochan kodesh*?

### Tunnels & Kedusha

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between two classes of tunnels. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling that tunnels are not sanctified applies to tunnels beneath *Har ha-Bayit* — those were deliberately left unsanctified to allow *tamei* individuals to exit the area without violating the *kedusha* of *Har ha-Bayit* above them. This is exactly the logic of the *mechilot ha-Kotel* tours available today. By contrast, tunnels or underground chambers whose openings are into the *Azara* — the sanctified precinct itself — do bear *kedusha*, because their access and use is from the sanctified zone. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s statement that tunnels beneath the *Heichal* are *chol* refers specifically to those whose opening is to the *chol* side. Tunnels beneath the *Azara* that open into the *kodesh* are themselves *kodesh*. The determining principle is consistently: where does the entrance face?

To summarize what emerges from the foregoing: the *kedusha* of underground spaces follows from their point of access. Tunnels beneath *Har ha-Bayit* were kept unsanctified as a matter of deliberate policy — to allow *tamei* persons to depart without being in a sanctified zone. Tunnels that open into the *Azara* proper, however, carry *kedusha*. The same logic that defines the chamber interiors defines the tunnels: access determines status.

### Measuring Sticks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְגַגּוֹ קוֹדֶשׁ! וְתִסְבְּרָא?! וְהָא קָתָנֵי: גַּגִּין הַלָּלוּ אֵין אוֹכְלִין שָׁם קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְאֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין שָׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים! וְאֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא גַּגּוֹ קֹדֶשׁ! אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא: לְאוֹתָן שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts another proof: **Come** and **hear** another proof from the same source cited above: It says in the *baraita* that **the roof** of the Sanctuary **was sanctified,** and the roof of the Sanctuary is not mentioned in the plans for the Temple laid out in the book of Chronicles. Therefore, the answer mentioned earlier, that everything in those plans was based upon divine inspiration, is not relevant. In that case, one should be able to derive that all the roofs were sanctified. The Gemara responds: **And** how **can you understand** it that way? **Isn't it teaching:** In the case of **these roofs** of the courtyard and the chambers located there, **one may not eat offerings of the most sacred order and one may not slaughter offerings of lesser sanctity there** because they do not have the sanctity of the courtyard? **However, that is difficult.** It contradicts the previous statement that **the roof is sanctified. Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said:** The roof of the Sanctuary was considered sanctified only because **those two** measuring rods used to measure **cubits** were stored there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת הָיוּ בְּשׁוּשַׁן הַבִּירָה, אַחַת עַל קֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית וְאַחַת עַל קֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית דְּרוֹמִית. זוֹ שֶׁעַל קֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית הָיְתָה יְתֵירָה עַל שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה חֲצִי אֶצְבַּע, וְזוֹ שֶׁעַל קֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית דְּרוֹמִית הָיְתָה יְתֵירָה עָלֶיהָ חֲצִי אֶצְבַּע, נִמְצֵאת יְתֵירָה עַל שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה אֶצְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: **There were two** rods for measuring **cubits in** the chamber called **Shushan the Capital, one in the northeast corner and one in the southeast corner. The one in the northeast corner was longer than** the cubit mentioned in the Torah from the time **of Moses,** which was six handbreadths, by **half a fingerbreadth, and the one in the southeast corner was longer than that by** another **half a fingerbreadth. Consequently,** the second measuring rod was **longer than Moses'** cubit **by** a full **fingerbreadth.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָמָה הָיוּ אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה וְאַחַת קְטַנָּה? שֶׁיִּהְיוּ הָאוּמָּנִין נוֹטְלִין בַּקְּטַנָּה וּמַחְזִירִין בַּגְּדוֹלָה, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָבוֹאוּ לִידֵי מְעִילָה. וְתַרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? אַחַת — לְכַסְפָּא וְדַהֲבָא, וְאַחַת — לְבִנְיָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And why** did they construct two measures for cubits, **one large and one small?** It was **so that the artisans** who were working in the Temple **would take** payment according the amount of work they did, as measured **by the small** cubit, **and return** it to the Temple through their work, as measured **by the large** cubit, **so they would not come to misuse consecrated property.** If they would accept any payment that they did not deserve, they would be misusing consecrated property. Therefore, this system of measuring was instituted in order to slightly reduce their payment and prevent accidental misuse of Temple funds. The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **two** large cubits? The Gemara answers: **One,** the shorter of the two, was **for silver and gold,** so that the artisans would not lose too much, **and one was for construction.**

The *gemara* makes one more attempt to address the status of rooftops, this time from a *baraita* stating that the roof of the *Heichal* is *kodesh* — *gago shel Heichal kodesh*. But the *gemara* immediately counters with another teaching: *gagim halalu einu ochlim sham kodshei kodashim ve-ein shochtim sham kodashim kalim* — one cannot eat *kodshei kodashim* on those rooftops and one cannot perform *shechita* of *kodashim kalim* there. These are the defining acts of the *Azara*, the sanctified courtyard. Since neither can be done on the roof of the *Heichal*, that rooftop clearly does not have the *kedusha* of the *Azara*. If so, in what sense is it *kodesh*?

*Rav Chama bar Guria* answers: *l'otan shtei amot* — the reference to the roof being *kodesh* is connected to the two *amot*, i.e., the two measuring sticks (*amot* here meaning not the cubits themselves but the measuring rods). The *mishna* teaches: *Shtei amot hayu be-Shushan ha-Bira* — they kept two measuring sticks in the chamber called *Shushan ha-Bira* (a special room, named for the image of the city of Shushan carved above the eastern gate). One was kept at the northeast corner and one at the southeast corner. The northeastern stick was a half-finger larger than the official *amah* as transmitted from *Moshe Rabbeinu* — roughly 2% larger. The southeastern stick was a half-finger larger still — 4% larger than the *amah* of *Moshe*.

Why maintain measuring sticks of different sizes? *She-hayu umnim notlin bi-ketana u-machzirin be-gedola kedei she-lo yavo'u lidei me'ila* — craftsmen hired to work on the *mikdash* would receive their raw materials measured out by the smaller stick, and return the finished goods or unused materials measured by the larger stick. This ensured that they could not pocket any sacred materials: whatever they received was measured conservatively, and whatever they returned was measured generously, meaning they had to account for every last bit. Two different sizes were needed because *achat le-kesef ve-zahav ve-achat le-vinyana* — one for precious metals (where a 2% discrepancy is substantial) and one for building materials like stone and wood (where the margin could be larger). The point was not to make it impossible for the craftsmen to profit, but to incentivize extreme care with *hekdesh* property so as to guard against *me'ila*.

Returning to the main question: what does this have to do with the roof of the *Heichal* being called *kodesh*? *Tosafot* clarifies that the measuring sticks were not actually stored on the rooftop. *Shushan ha-Bira* was a depiction of the city of Shushan carved above the eastern gate of the *mikdash*, and that gate was one of the gates of the *Azara* itself. *Tosafot* therefore says: just as those eastern gates were sanctified enough to hold sacred vessels, so too was the roof of the *Heichal* sanctified in that same sense — *le-hatzni'a sham klei kodesh*, to store sacred implements there. But *Tosafot* immediately notes that *klei kodesh* do not actually require a sanctified location to be stored; you do not need *Azara*-level *kedusha* to house them. So the statement that the roof is *kodesh* means no more than that it was used for this purpose — it does not establish that the roof had the full *kedusha* of the *Azara*. The use of the word *kodesh* there is *lav davka*.

### Windows & Ground Level

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַחַלּוֹנוֹת וְעוֹבִי הַחוֹמָה — כְּלִפְנִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא הַחַלּוֹנוֹת, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דְּשָׁוְיָה לְקַרְקַע עֲזָרָה. אֶלָּא עוֹבִי הַחוֹמָה, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **The** space within the **windows and the thickness of the wall were** considered to be **inside.** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted,** with regard to the windows, **you will find it** to be sanctified with the sanctity of the Temple courtyard **when it is level with the ground of the courtyard. But** with regard to **the thickness of the wall,** which must have been significantly above the ground, under **what circumstances** can **you find** it to be level with the ground of the courtyard? Even the roofs of chambers in the courtyard were not sanctified; therefore, if the thickness of the wall was not level with the floor of the courtyard, it would certainly not have been sanctified.

The *gemara* now turns back to the *mishna* itself: *ha-chalonot ve-ovi ha-choma ki-fenim* — windows and wall thickness are like the inside. The *gemara* attempts to clarify how to account for these physically. For windows, it proposes that we understand them as *shaveh le-karka* — flush with the ground, as in a case where a building is constructed into the slope of *Yerushalayim*'s terrain, such that a window is at the same level as the city floor. Someone standing in that window embrasure is therefore at ground level.

*Rashi* notes that the *gemara*'s references to *Azara* in this context are not precisely meant — we are really discussing *Yerushalayim* as a whole. For the wall thickness (*ovi ha-choma*), though, the geometrical challenge is more acute: the whole purpose of a city wall is that it rises higher than the ground it surrounds, so how can the top of the wall be flush with the interior ground level?

### Secondary Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּבַר שׁוּרָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּאֲבֶל חֵל וְחוֹמָה״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שׁוּרָא וּבַר שׁוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You find it in** the case of **the low wall** on the Temple Mount, which was the same height as the floor of the courtyard. This low wall is mentioned in the Bible, **as it is written: "He has made the rampart and wall mourn"** (Lamentations 2:8), **and Rabbi Aḥa said, and some say** it in the name of **Rabbi Ḥanina,** that this double language of both rampart and wall in the verse is referring to **a wall and a low wall** that were next to each other. This low wall was the same height as the floor of the courtyard.

The *gemara* answers: *mishkachat lah be-var shura* — you find this in the case of a secondary wall. The verse in *Eichah* speaks of *cheil ve-choma* being mourned in the destruction of *Yerushalayim*, and *Rabbi Acha* (or *Rav Chanina*) explains these as *shura u-var shura* — the main wall and the secondary wall. *Rashi* describes an arrangement in which the outer wall of the city rises to full height, while inside, a lower inner or retaining wall runs parallel to it. The top of this secondary, lower wall can be flush with the interior floor of the city — just as the top of the *Kotel* today is roughly flush with the level of *Har ha-Bayit* and the *Kippat ha-Sela*. In that way, "the thickness of the wall" that is treated as being on the inside refers to the space associated with this inner retaining wall, the top of which is at ground level with the inner city.

The *gemara* does not reach a fully explicit resolution regarding the general status of rooftops in *Yerushalayim*. What emerges is a consistent effort to translate all the evidence into cases where surfaces are flush with the ground — the strong implication being that when a surface is genuinely elevated, it may not carry the *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim*, though this remains without a definitive ruling. The discussion throughout has also presented a challenge to the *Rambam*'s reading of the *mishna* as pertaining to the physical house of the *korban pesach*, since the *gemara* consistently frames the analysis in terms of *kedusha*, the sanctification of space, rather than merely the legal definition of a house for purposes of the offering.

## Dividing Groups

### Two Groups Separated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי חֲבוּרוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹכְלִין בְּבַיִת אֶחָד, אֵלּוּ הוֹפְכִין אֶת פְּנֵיהֶם הֵילָךְ וְאוֹכְלִין, וְאֵלּוּ הוֹפְכִין אֶת פְּנֵיהֶם הֵילָךְ וְאוֹכְלִין, וְהַמֵּיחַם בָּאֶמְצַע. כְּשֶׁהַשַּׁמָּשׁ עוֹמֵד לִמְזוֹג, קוֹפֵץ אֶת פִּיו וּמַחֲזִיר אֶת פָּנָיו, עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ אֵצֶל חֲבוּרָתוֹ וְאוֹכֵל. וְהַכַּלָּה הוֹפֶכֶת אֶת פָּנֶיהָ וְאוֹכֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Two groups that were eating** one Paschal lamb **in one house** need not be concerned that they will appear to be one group. Rather, **these turn their faces this way and eat, and these turn their faces that way and eat. And** it is permissible for them to have **the boiler** from which they pour hot water **in the middle,** so that the waiter can easily serve both groups. **When the attendant** who is serving both groups **gets up to pour** for the group of which he is not a member, **he must close his mouth and turn his face** so that he does not accidentally eat with the other group, **until he reaches his group** again **and eats** with it. **And the bride,** who is embarrassed to eat in the presence of men she does not know, **turns her face** away from her group **and eats,** although this may make it seem as though she is part of a different group.

*Rashi* reads this *mishna* as establishing that it is *permitted* to divide one *korban pesach* between two groups — a matter that will be debated in the *gemara*. The *mishna* thus tells us that two groups may eat one *pesach* in one house, each turning away from the other. *Rambam*, however, reads the *mishna* as stating a *requirement*: once you have committed to two groups eating in one house — whether from one or two *korbanot* — the boundary lines between them must be maintained visibly and clearly. The act of each group turning away from the other is not merely a permission but an obligation to ensure that the two groups do not become confused. The heating vessel (*micham*) may be placed between them without undermining this clarity, since it is a shared utilitarian object.

### Baraita: Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״עַל הַבָּתִּים אֲשֶׁר יֹאכְלוּ אֹתוֹ בָּהֶם״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַפֶּסַח נֶאֱכָל בִּשְׁתֵּי חֲבוּרוֹת. יָכוֹל יְהֵא הָאוֹכֵל אוֹכֵל בִּשְׁתֵּי מְקוֹמוֹת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּבַיִת אֶחָד יֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Who is** the *tanna* of **the mishna?** The Gemara answers: I**t is Rabbi Yehuda. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Upon the houses wherein they shall eat it"** (Exodus 12:7). This **teaches that** one **Paschal lamb may be eaten in two** separate **groups,** even if the groups eat it in separate houses. I **might** have thought that one person **who eats** from it **may eat** it **in two** separate **places; therefore, the Torah states: "In one house shall it be eaten [*ye'akhel*]"** (Exodus 12:46).

The *gemara* establishes whose view the *mishna* reflects: *matnisin mani? Rebbi Yehuda hi* — the *mishna* follows *Rebbi Yehuda*. The source is a *baraita* on the verse *al ha-batim asher yochlu oto bahem* — the *pesach* shall be eaten in the houses in which you will eat it. The word *batim* is plural. One could read this as: every group eats in its own house, but a single *pesach* is eaten in multiple houses — i.e., divided between two groups. *Melamed she-ha-pesach ne'echal bi-shtei chaburot* — it teaches that a *pesach* may be eaten by two *chaburot*.

One might then have thought that just as the *pesach* itself can be divided, an individual can eat in two locations — starting with one group and then joining the other half of his *pesach* elsewhere. The *baraita* therefore continues: *talmud lomar be-bayit echad ye'achel* — "in one house it shall be eaten." The *gemara* reads this as *yochal* (the subject being the eater), meaning the *individual* must eat in one location, even if the *pesach* is distributed across two groups.

### Attendant Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַשַּׁמָּשׁ שֶׁאָכַל כְּזַיִת בְּצַד הַתַּנּוּר, אִי פִּקֵּחַ הוּא מְמַלֵּא כְּרֵיסוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ, וְאִם רָצוּ בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה לַעֲשׂוֹת עִמּוֹ טוֹבָה — בָּאִין וְיוֹשְׁבִין בְּצִדּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From here they stated** that if **an attendant** who was registered for a Paschal lamb **ate an olive-bulk** of it **next to the oven** in which it is being roasted, and thereby made himself into an independent group at that location, **if he is prudent he will fill his stomach with it** because when the Paschal lamb is moved to a different location he will no longer be able to eat it. **And if the members of the group wanted to do him a favor** so that he may continue eating, **they would come and sit at his side.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

From this the *baraita* derives a practical ruling: *ha-shamash she-achal ke-zayit be-tzad ha-tanur* — if the attendant (*shamash*) was tending the oven while the *korban pesach* was roasting and he tasted a *ke-zayit* of it there, he has established his eating location as the small oven room. Since a *chabura* can consist of even one individual, and one must eat in only one place, he cannot now go to the main room to continue eating. *Im pikei'ach hu, memaleh karsav mimenu* — if he is wise, he eats his full share right there at the oven. And if the members of the main *chabura* wish to do him a kindness, *ba'in ve-yoshvin tzido* — they come and sit next to him. That is *Rebbi Yehuda*'s position.

### Rabbi Yehuda: One Place

*Rebbi Yehuda*'s ruling is thus that a person can eat in only one location. The logic of this is tight: wherever one first eats establishes one's *makom*, one's designated location. Even if that location is not where the rest of the *chabura* is eating, one is fixed there. The *chabura* is defined not only by group membership but by spatial commitment.

### Rabbi Shimon: Multiple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״עַל הַבָּתִּים אֲשֶׁר יֹאכְלוּ אֹתוֹ בָּהֶם״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָאוֹכֵל אוֹכֵל בִּשְׁתֵּי מְקוֹמוֹת. יָכוֹל יְהֵא נֶאֱכָל בִּשְׁתֵּי חֲבוּרוֹת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּבַיִת אֶחָד יֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** that the verses should be understood in the opposite manner: **"Upon the houses wherein they shall eat it" teaches that** one person **who eats** the Paschal lamb **may eat** it **in two places. I might have thought that a single Paschal lamb may be eaten in two separate groups; therefore, the verse states: "In one house shall it be eaten."**

*Rebbi Shimon* reads the verse differently. *Al ha-batim asher yochlu oto bahem* — the plural *batim* teaches *melamed she-ochel ochel bi-shtei mekomot*: an individual *may* eat in two locations. He is not locked into his first eating spot. On the other hand, *Rebbi Shimon* reads *be-bayit echad ye'achel* more literally — the *pesach* must be eaten in one house, one group. One cannot divide a single *korban pesach* between two *chaburot*. *Rebbi Shimon* and *Rebbi Yehuda* thus arrive at opposing conclusions from the same verses, each one treating one clause as the restriction and deriving freedom from the other.

### Debate Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yehuda holds that the consonantal text of the Torah is authoritative;** meaning, the primary understanding of the verse is according to the way it is spelled. If this is the case, the verse may be rendered: In one house shall he eat it [*yokhal*], referring to the person eating the Paschal lamb. This would indicate that one eating from the Paschal lamb must eat in one location, but the verse does not prohibit dividing the offering between multiple groups. **And Rabbi Shimon holds that the vocalized text of the Torah is authoritative.** Since the word is pronounced *ye'akhel*, it is clear that it is referring to the Paschal lamb itself, and the verse requires that the offering be consumed by a single group of people (Rabbeinu Ḥananel).

The *gemara* explains: *Rebbi Yehuda savar yesh em la-masoret* — *Rebbi Yehuda* holds that we follow the *written* tradition, i.e., the *ketiv*. The word *ye'achel* as written can be read as *yochal* — the *person* must eat in one place. *Rebbi Shimon savar yesh em la-mikra* — *Rebbi Shimon* holds we follow the *pronunciation*, the *kri*. The word is read as *ye'achel* — the *pesach* shall be eaten in one house. Hence their disagreement over which clause restricts movement of the person and which restricts division of the offering.

*Rashi* clarifies *Rebbi Shimon*'s position: the requirement of *be-bayit echad* means that the primary eating of the *pesach* must take place with one *chabura* — it may not be divided from the outset. But as for an individual's movement: once the group has started eating together, a person may relocate within the house or even to other spaces, as long as he is not joining a different *chabura* eating a different *pesach*.

### Partition Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין וְנִפְרְסָה מְחִיצָה בֵּינֵיהֶם, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר פֶּסַח נֶאֱכָל בִּשְׁתֵּי חֲבוּרוֹת — אוֹכְלִין, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֵין הַפֶּסַח נֶאֱכָל בִּשְׁתֵּי חֲבוּרוֹת — אֵין אוֹכְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to clarify this *halakha*: If members of a group **were sitting** and eating the Paschal lamb, **and a partition was spread between them** such that they now constitute two separate groups, **according to** Rabbi Yehuda, **who says a Paschal lamb may be eaten in two groups, they may** continue to **eat; according to** Rabbi Shimon, **who says a Paschal lamb may not be eaten in two groups, they may not** continue to **eat** because they now constitute two groups.

The *gemara* then presents a series of scenarios testing these principles. In the first: one group was eating one *korban pesach* together in one space, and then a *mechitza* was erected between two halves of the group. According to the view that a *pesach* may be eaten by two *chaburot* (*Rebbi Yehuda*'s position), they may continue eating — the *mechitza* does not create a problem. According to the view that a *pesach* may not be eaten by two *chaburot* (*Rebbi Shimon*'s position), the erection of the *mechitza* effectively divides what was one group into two, and they may no longer continue eating.

### Partition Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין וְנִסְתַּלְּקָה מְחִיצָה בֵּינֵיהֶן, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר הָאוֹכֵל אוֹכֵל בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת — אוֹכְלִין, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֵין הָאוֹכֵל אוֹכֵל בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת — אֵין אוֹכְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** On the other hand, if **they were sitting** in two separate groups separated by a partition, **and the partition between them was removed, according to** Rabbi Shimon, **who says that one who eats** the Paschal lamb **may eat** it **in two places, they may eat; according to** Rabbi Yehuda, **who says that one who eats** the Paschal lamb **may not eat** it **in two places, they may not eat.** The removal of the partition defines a new place and it is as though they are eating in a new location.

The second scenario involves two separate groups, each eating its own *korban pesach*, separated from the start by a *mechitza*. The *mechitza* is then removed. Does the new spatial configuration — now larger than the original space of either group — create a problem?

According to the view that a person may eat in two locations (*Rebbi Shimon*), it is fine: even if the expanded space constitutes a new location, that is permitted. According to the view that a person must eat in only one location (*Rebbi Yehuda*), there is a problem: although no one has moved, the space in which one now finds oneself is arguably a *new* and expanded space — and eating in a second location is prohibited. Note the asymmetry between the two cases: when a *mechitza* is erected to create a subset of the original space, the smaller space is contained within the original and does not constitute a truly new location. But when a *mechitza* is removed to create a *larger* space that was previously divided, that new, expanded space may be considered a different location altogether.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא קָא פָּשֵׁיט לֵיהּ מִפְשָׁט. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וְתִיבְּעֵי לָךְ אִיבַּעְיָא: סִילּוּק מְחִיצָה וַעֲשִׂיַּית מְחִיצָה מִי הָוֵי כִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת, וְכִשְׁתֵּי חֲבוּרוֹת דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Kahana sat and** taught this lesson **simply,** as though it were absolutely clear that erecting a partition divides a group into two distinct groups and removing a partition causes the area to be regarded as a new location. **Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana: You should raise this dilemma as a question: Does removal of a partition or the construction of a partition** in one location during the eating of the Paschal lamb render the place **comparable to two locations and** cause the people to be considered **two groups, or not?** In fact, there is no clear answer to this dilemma, and the Gemara concludes: **Let** the question **stand** unresolved.

*Rav Ashi* challenged *Rav Kahana*, who was presenting these cases as obvious settled rulings. *Rav Ashi* argued: *ibayei lach ibaya* — these should be posed as open questions, not taken for granted. The question of whether erecting or removing a *mechitza* constitutes a meaningful change — *siluk mechitza va-asiyat mechitza, ni havei k'vei mekomot u-k'vei chaburot dami o lav* — whether placing or removing a partition genuinely converts one space into two or two spaces into one for purposes of these *halachot* — remains genuinely unresolved, and *Rav Kahana* should have framed it as a *she'ela* rather than a *peshat*.

### Bride's Modesty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכַּלָּה הוֹפֶכֶת אֶת פָּנֶיהָ וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא בּוֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that **a bride turns her face.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** she turns her face? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is because she is embarrassed** by other members of the group looking at her.

The final clause of the *mishna* states: *ve-ha-kallah hofechet panei-ha ve-ochelet* — the bride turns her face away and eats. *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* explains: *mishum she-hi bosha* — because she is embarrassed. As a newly married woman, she is self-conscious about eating in front of strangers — particularly in a room with men she does not know well. *Rashi* indicates that everyone's attention was naturally drawn to her as the new bride. The *chiddush* of the *mishna*, then, is not that she is required to turn away, but that she is *permitted* to do so without it appearing that she has separated herself into a different *chabura* or relocated to a different space. Her turning away is a matter of modesty, not a statement about group membership.

## Etiquette & Customs

### Rav Huna Visit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מָה שְׁמָךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: רַב הוּנָא. אֲמַרוּ: נִיתֵּיב מָר אַפּוּרְיָא! יְתֵיב, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ כָּסָא, קַבְּלֵיהּ בְּחַד זִימְנָא וְשַׁתְיֵיהּ בִּתְרֵי זִימְנֵי, וְלָא אַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In this regard, the Gemara relates that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, happened to come to the house of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak. They said to him: What is your name? He said to them: Rav Huna,** even though using his title appeared to show conceit. **They said: Our master may sit on the bed** due to his great stature. **He sat** immediately, despite the fact that common etiquette dictated that he initially refuse. **They gave him a cup** of wine that **he accepted the first time,** without initially refusing it. **And he drank it in two** sips **and did not turn his face** from the rest of the people who were present.

The *gemara* now introduces a series of narratives about proper conduct at a host's table, which connect back to the *mishna*'s mention of the bride turning her face. *Rav Huna brei d'Rav Nasan ikla l'vei Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* — *Rav Huna son of Rav Nasan* came to visit the house of *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak*. They asked him his name. He told them: *Rav Huna*. Everything that followed was, from the perspective of the household, a series of social missteps — each of which *Rav Huna* then proceeded to justify on the basis of principle.

### Couch Protocol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא קָרֵית לְךָ רַב הוּנָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ בַּעַל הַשֵּׁם אֲנִי. מַאי טַעְמָא כִּי אֲמַרוּ לָךְ נִיתֵּיב אַפּוּרְיָא יְתֵבְתְּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: כׇּל מַה שֶׁיֹּאמַר לְךָ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת עֲשֵׂה (חוּץ מִצֵּא).
>
> **Translation:** These actions all appeared to be departures from the common etiquette and surprised his hosts, who **said to him: What is the reason you call yourself Rav Huna? He said to them: I am** known by **that name** since my youth, and therefore referring to myself with that title does not indicate conceit. They asked him: **What is the reason** that **when they told you to sit on the bed, you sat** immediately and did not initially refuse? **He said to them:** We have learned that **anything the master of the house says to you,** you should **do, except for** an inappropriate request, such as if he says to **leave.**

When they told him to sit on the *puraya* — the couch, the honored reclining place rather than an ordinary chair — he accepted immediately and sat. This was contrary to local etiquette, where a guest was expected to initially decline such an honor. *Rav Huna* justified himself: *kol ma she-yomar lecha ba'al ha-bayit asei* — whatever the *ba'al ha-bayit* tells you to do, you should do. There is a well-known principle of deferring to one's host. He added the qualifier *chutz mi-tzei* — except for "leave." Once the host tells you to leave, he is no longer functioning as your *ba'al ha-bayit*, and in any case you must go. The *Maharsha* adds a nuance: once told to leave, the relationship of *ba'al ha-bayit* and guest has dissolved, and there is no further obligation to follow his instructions.

### Wine Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא כִּי יָהֲבִי לָךְ כָּסָא קַבֵּלְתְּ בְּחַד זִימְנָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: מְסָרְבִין לַקָּטָן, וְאֵין מְסָרְבִין לַגָּדוֹל. מַאי טַעְמָא אִשְׁתֵּיתֵיהּ בִּתְרֵי זִימְנֵי? אָמַר לְהוּ: דְּתַנְיָא, הַשּׁוֹתֶה כּוֹסוֹ בְּבַת אַחַת הֲרֵי זֶה גַּרְגְּרָן, שְׁנַיִם — דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, שְׁלֹשָׁה — מִגַּסֵּי הָרוּחַ. מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אַהְדַּרְתְּ אַפָּךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״כַּלָּה הוֹפֶכֶת פָּנֶיהָ״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** They continued to question his conduct: **What is the reason that when they gave you the cup, you accepted it the first time** and did not politely demur? **He said to them: One may refuse a lesser person, but one may not refuse a great person;** when an important person makes a request, it is respectful to comply immediately. They persisted in their questioning: **What is the reason you drank it in two** sips? **He said to them: As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One who drinks his cup at one time is a guzzler;** drinking it in **two** sips is **proper manners;** one who drinks his cup in **three** sips **is haughty,** as he thereby demonstrates that he is pampered and indulgent. They continued to question his conduct: **What is the reason you did not turn your face** in accordance with the common etiquette? **He said to them: We learned in the mishna that a bride turns her face;** however, there is no reason for anyone else to turn his face.

Similarly, when they handed him a cup of wine, he accepted it at once without the customary show of reluctance. Again, local practice apparently called for an initial refusal. *Rav Huna* explained: *mesarvin mi-katan ve-ein mesarvin mi-gadol* — one pushes off and initially refuses when a person of lesser status makes an offer, but when someone of importance offers, one accepts immediately. By accepting their offer without hesitation, he was expressing that he regarded them as people of importance — his acceptance was itself a form of respect.

### Drinking Etiquette

When he then drank the cup in two sips (*terei zimnei*), they again noted this. The *baraita*: *ha-shoseh koso be-vas achas, harei zeh gargaran* — one who drinks a cup all in one gulp is considered a glutton. *Shnayim derech eretz* — two gulps is the proper, accepted manner. *Shlosha mi-gasei ha-ruach* — drinking in three small, slow sips conveys arrogance and excessive delicacy. *Rav Huna* had therefore drunk in two gulps precisely because that is *derech eretz*, the correct middle path.

### Face Turning

Finally, they observed that when he drank he did not turn his face away. *Rav Huna* replied: *Kallah hofeches panei-ha* — it is the bride who must turn her face; the *mishna* specifies only her. This is the reason the narrative about *Rav Huna*'s visit is introduced in the context of the *mishna*'s ruling about the bride: the *kallah hofeches panei-ha* is brought here to teach that turning one's face away while eating or drinking is not a general requirement of *derech eretz* but a specific dispensation — or rather accommodation — for the newly married woman who is too embarrassed to eat facing others. In ordinary circumstances, a man is not required to, and indeed may not need to, turn away.

### Yishmael Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן לָקוֹנְיָא. יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ כָּסָא, קַבְּלֵיהּ בְּחַד זִימְנָא וְשַׁתְיֵיהּ בְּחַד זִימְנָא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר הַשּׁוֹתֶה כּוֹסוֹ בְּבַת אַחַת הֲרֵי זֶה גַּרְגְּרָן? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא אָמְרִי בְּכוֹסְךָ קָטָן וְיֵינְךָ מָתוֹק וּכְרֵיסִי רְחָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident that is somewhat similar to the one just quoted: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, happened to come to the house of Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yosei ben Lakonya. They gave him a cup** of wine to drink. **He accepted it the first time** it was offered **and drank it at one time. They said to him: Does our master not hold** of the *halakha* that **one who drinks his cup at one time is a guzzler? He said to them: They did not say** this rule **with regard to your small cup, and your sweet wine, and my wide stomach.**

The *gemara* then records a parallel episode. *Rabbi Yishmael be-Rabbi Yosi ikla le-vei Rabbi Shimon be-Rabbi Yosi ben Lakunya* — *Rabbi Yishmael* came to visit *Rabbi Shimon*'s house. They gave him a cup of wine; he accepted it immediately and drank it all in one gulp (*be-vas achas*). They challenged him: does the master not hold the principle that one who drinks a cup all at once is considered a glutton? He replied: *lo amru be-hai* — they did not say this ruling applies in this case, for three reasons. First, *koscha katan* — your cup is small. Second, *ve-yeincha matok* — your wine is sweet, making it easy and natural to drink quickly. Third, *ve-charsi rechava* — my stomach is large. When all three factors combine, drinking the whole cup at once is not gluttony but simply normal consumption. The principle is not absolute; the circumstances of the cup, the wine, and the person matter.

### Servant Protocol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה נִכְנָסִין בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וְיוֹצְאִין אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶחָד. אָמַר רַבָּה: וְהוּא דְּעָיֵיל בְּעִידָּנָא דִּרְגִילִי לְמֵיעַל, וְהוּא דִּרְגַשׁ בְּהוּ דַּיָּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Members of a group enter with three,** meaning that when there are three members present the waiter must start serving them, **and they may leave even with one,** meaning that they may leave one at a time, and the waiter must continue serving those who remain until they are finished. **Rabba said: And that is** true only **when** the last member of the group **entered at a time when it is common to enter** the meal and not unusually early or late, **and it is** true only **when the waiter [*dayyala*] knew about them,** i.e., he knew that the members of this group leave one by one as they finish their meals and do not eat the entire meal together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: וְנוֹתְנִין שְׂכַר דָּמִים, וְצָרִיךְ הָאַחֲרוֹן לְהוֹסִיף דָּמִים. וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: And** the people who stayed late and extended their meal **must give** the waiter extra **money** for the **wages** he earned during the extra time that he served them, **and the last** one **must add money** for the time the waiter stayed to serve him individually. The Gemara notes: **And the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion** of Ravina. Rather, the waiter must serve until the last member of the group has completed his meal without additional compensation.

The *gemara* closes with a brief teaching of *Rav Huna*: *bnei chabura nichnasim bi-shlosha ve-yotzin afilu be-echad* — a group that has hired a *shamash* (attendant) to serve them requires a minimum of three members before the attendant is obligated to serve them. He cannot be made to attend to just one or two people. When they leave, however, *ve-yotzin afilu be-echad* — he must continue attending to them even if only one remains, as they break up one by one.

*Rava* adds two conditions: *ve-hu de-ayeil be-idna de-ragil le-me'al* — this applies only if the group assembled at the normal time, within the attendant's normal hours of service. And *ve-hu de-ragash bei dayala* — it applies only if the *dayala* (the attendant) is alert to the possibility that the group may break up and prepared to serve them individually. *Ravina* adds: *ve-notel sechar damim* — if the attendant is in fact required to serve individuals after the main group has broken up, he is entitled to additional compensation. *Ve-tzarich achron le-hosif damim* — the last member of the group remaining, who causes the attendant the most extra service, must contribute additionally from his own pocket. *Ve-livsof yachloku ze im ze* — and ultimately the additional payment is divided among all the members of the group.

*Tosafot* notes that according to another reading, this passage is not about general *derech eretz* regarding hired attendants but speaks specifically in the context of a *chabura* assembled to eat the *korban pesach* — addressing the question of how many members must be present before one is obligated to wait for the others before beginning to eat.

*Hadran alach kefei tzon.*
