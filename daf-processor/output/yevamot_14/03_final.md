# Yevamot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Uniformity vs. Pluralism

### Lo Titgodedu Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד כָּאן לֹא שָׁנִיתָ מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְלָאכָה בְּעַרְבֵי פְּסָחִים עַד חֲצוֹת — עוֹשִׂין, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲשׂוֹת — אֵין עוֹשִׂין!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** Have **you not taught until now:** In **a place where** the people were **accustomed to perform labor on Passover eve until midday, one may do** so on that day; in **a place where** the people were **accustomed not to perform** labor, **one may not do** so? This shows that different places can have different customs without violating the prohibition against dividing into factions.

The discussion picks up from a broader question that was raised by Rav Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning consistency of practice — specifically, the concern of *lo titgodedu agudot agudot*, the prohibition against creating factions through divergent communal practice. This prohibition has a particular relevance to situations where *halacha* itself seems to produce differing outcomes for different people. The Gemara's inquiry, however, has evolved from the narrow question of *lo titgodedu* into something considerably wider: whether we demand uniformity of *pesak* across the community, or whether we accommodate — and perhaps even welcome — a pluralism of halachic practice.

The Gemara's working answer has been that *lo titgodedu* applies only when different people within the same geographic location are acting inconsistently. If they belong to distinct communities in distinct places, that is permitted. The irony, noted previously, is that geographic separation may actually *reinforce* factionalism by entrenching the identity of each locale around its particular practice — yet at least within any single place, there is coherence. Nowadays, when communities are no longer defined by geography but rather by affiliation and tradition, the concept of a "virtual community" has taken on increasing relevance, though the Gemara's framework remains primarily geographic.

### Rava's Radical Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל כִּשְׁתֵּי בָּתֵּי דִינִים בְּעִיר אַחַת דָּמֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כִּי אָמְרִינַן ״לֹא תִתְגּוֹדְדוּ״ — כְּגוֹן בֵּית דִּין בְּעִיר אַחַת, פְּלַג מוֹרִין כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, וּפְלַג מוֹרִין כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. אֲבָל שְׁתֵּי בָּתֵּי דִינִין בְּעִיר אַחַת — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: But** the dispute between **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel is considered like** a case of **two courts in one city,** as these two schools of thought were found everywhere, not in any specific place. **Rather, Rava said: When we say** that the prohibition: **"You shall not cut yourselves"** applies, we are referring to a case **where** there is **a court in one city, a section** of which **rules in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai and** another **section rules in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel. However,** with regard to **two courts** located **in one city, we have no** problem **with it.**

Rava went considerably further, asserting that even within a single geographic location, differences in *pesak* do not constitute a violation of *lo titgodedu*. In his view, the prohibition applies only when a *beit din* has failed to arrive at a ruling and the result is a kind of halachic anarchy — everyone doing as they please without any principled basis. But where there are genuinely different *psakim* held by different authorities, and different people follow different *psakim*, that does not violate *lo titgodedu*. This is, admittedly, a fairly radical position, and in practice something between Abaye and Rava — allowing for community-based distinctions even without strict geographic separation — seems more workable. Nevertheless, the Gemara has effectively reduced *lo titgodedu* to a quite narrow concern.

### Mishna Challenge

The remaining question — the one that drives the discussion forward — is what actually happened *lema'aseh* with Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Did Beit Shammai ultimately follow Beit Hillel's *psak*, or did they maintain their own? And beyond the question of *lo titgodedu*, the issue is sharpened by the challenge posed by the Mishna itself: the Mishna states that despite their conflicting positions, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel intermarried with one another. This is a startling statement. If a woman from Beit Hillel is, according to Beit Shammai's *psak*, forbidden — or if her children would be problematic — how could they intermarry freely? If one answers that Beit Shammai simply adopted Beit Hillel's practice, then the Mishna's statement is almost trivially true and loses its force as a *chiddush*. The tension between the straightforward meaning of the Mishna and the halachic implications of genuine pluralism of practice is precisely what the Gemara must now resolve.

## Geographic Locations

### Rabbi Eliezer Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָיוּ כּוֹרְתִים עֵצִים לַעֲשׂוֹת פֶּחָמִים בְּשַׁבָּת לַעֲשׂוֹת בַּרְזֶל. בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הָיוּ אוֹכְלִים בְּשַׂר עוֹף בְּחָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites other relevant sources. **Come** and **hear: In the locale of Rabbi Eliezer,** where his ruling was followed, **they would cut down trees** on Shabbat **to prepare charcoal** from them **to fashion iron** tools with which to circumcise a child **on Shabbat.** In Rabbi Eliezer's opinion, not only does the mitzva of circumcision override Shabbat, but also any action required for the preparation of the tools necessary for the circumcision likewise overrides Shabbat. The *baraita* adds: **In the locale of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili they would eat poultry meat in milk,** as Rabbi Yosei HaGelili held that the prohibition of meat in milk does not include poultry.

The Gemara introduces a *ta shema* from a source dealing with the place of Rabbi Eliezer. In the place of Rabbi Eliezer, they would actually cut down trees on Shabbat in order to make charcoal, in order to forge iron implements, in order to fashion a knife for the *milah* — because Rabbi Eliezer held that not only the act of *milah* itself overrides Shabbat on the eighth day, but so do all of its preparatory implements, *machshirei milah*. Tosafot explains that in the place of Rabbi Eliezer, they would intentionally perform all these preparatory acts on Shabbat itself, out of a desire to demonstrate the supreme importance of *bris milah* — its capacity to override even the full range of Shabbat restrictions. This, of course, is not how we rule; the accepted *pesak* is that only the act of *milah* itself overrides Shabbat, not its preparations.

### Rabbi Yosi HaGelili

Similarly, in the place of Rabbi Yosi HaGelili, they would eat chicken cooked in milk. Rabbi Yosi HaGelili maintained that the prohibition of *basar b'chalav* applies only to the flesh of an animal in its mother's milk, and since fowl has no mother's milk, the prohibition does not apply even rabbinically. The accepted ruling disagrees — we hold that poultry with milk is rabbinically forbidden — but Rabbi Yosi HaGelili maintained his position, and the people of his community maintained it with him. Here, then, is a clear instance of at least two distinct geographic locations following different halachic positions, and indeed the connection between a locale and its rabbi's *psak* was not random — it was a *mesorah*, a tradition following the ruling of the *rav* of that place.

### Eliezer vs Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — אִין, בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — לָא. דְּתַנְיָא, כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לַעֲשׂוֹתָהּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — אֵין דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **In the locale of Rabbi Eliezer, yes,** they would act in this manner, whereas **in the locale of Rabbi Akiva,** for instance, **no,** they would not do so, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **a principle was stated by Rabbi Akiva: Any prohibited labor that can be performed on Shabbat eve does not override Shabbat** even if it involves a mitzva. A mitzva whose proper time is on Shabbat overrides Shabbat only if its performance was impossible earlier, e.g., the act of circumcision itself, which cannot be performed earlier.

The Gemara notes a further distinction: in the place of Rabbi Eliezer, they would cut trees on Shabbat for the purpose of *milah* preparations, but in the place of Rabbi Akiva they would not. The reason is a general principle articulated by Rabbi Akiva: any *melachah* that could have been performed before Shabbat does not override Shabbat. The preparations could have been done on *erev Shabbos*, and therefore they cannot be done on Shabbat itself. This is in fact the ruling we follow. The upshot is that even in this area — where Rabbi Akiva's position is the normative *psak* — in the specific locale of Rabbi Eliezer, they followed Rabbi Eliezer.

### Shabbat Gravity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא מִשּׁוּם חוּמְרָא דְשַׁבָּת — כְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It might **enter your mind to say** that **due to the severity of Shabbat, it,** i.e., the world, **is considered like a single locale.** In other words, one might have thought that the permission to tolerate diverse customs in different places applies only to other prohibitions, whereas the prohibition of Shabbat is so severe that it is unacceptable to allow different customs, as this might lead people to disrespect Shabbat. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that even in the case of Shabbat there can be different customs in various locales.

The Gemara asks why this case is even posed as a challenge. The answer was already given: *mikomos mikomos shani* — different locations can follow different *psakim*, and that is not a problem. The Gemara responds that one might have thought that because of the particular gravity of Shabbat, even geographically distinct communities should maintain a uniform practice. The concern is that *mishum chumrah d'Shabbos k'chad makom dami* — the weight of Shabbat is so great that perhaps all communities should be treated as a single place for purposes of Shabbat practice. The Gemara rejects this: even in matters as weighty as Shabbat, different communities in different places may follow different practices.

One area where the Gemara does insist on absolute uniformity is the calendar — when *Rosh Chodesh* falls and consequently when the holidays are observed. The calendar must be shared. Divergence on when Shabbat or Yom Tov is observed is precisely the kind of break that sunders a community into fundamentally different peoples, as was the case with the sect of Jubilees and the *Tzadukim*. Indeed, the dispute between the *Geonim* of Bavel and the *Geonim* of Eretz Yisrael over calendar reckoning was ultimately resolved in favor of Bavel, and that resolution effectively marked the transfer of halachic authority to the Babylonian academies. Beyond calendar, however, the Gemara is prepared to allow divergent practice even in the most serious areas of Shabbat law.

## Rabbi Abba Case

### Lamp at Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ כִּי אִיקְּלַע לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הֲוָה מְטַלְטֵל שְׁרָגָא. וְכִי אִיקְּלַע לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לָא הֲוָה מְטַלְטֵל שְׁרָגָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another relevant case involving Shabbat: **Come** and **hear that Rabbi Abbahu, when he happened** to come **to the place of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, would move** an oil **lamp [*sheraga*]** after the flame that had been lit for that Shabbat had burned out, as Rabbi Yehoshua accepted the ruling that it is permitted to carry items of this sort that had been set aside. **But when he happened** to come **to the place of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who prohibited carrying items of this kind that had been set aside on Shabbat, **he would not move** an oil **lamp.** This indicates that divergent customs are followed in different places.

The Gemara brings another *ta shema*. When Rabbi Abba would come to the place of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, he would carry an extinguished lamp — that is, a lamp that had been burning on Shabbat and was then extinguished. The question is whether such a lamp is *muktzeh* or not. In the place of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, Rabbi Abba would carry it.

### Lamp at Yochanan

But when Rabbi Abba came to the place of Rabbi Yochanan, he would not carry the lamp. Rabbi Yochanan did not permit carrying such objects.

### Deference Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וְכִי מִקְּלַע לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא הֲוָה מְטַלְטֵל מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וְהָאִיכָּא שַׁמָּעָא! דְּמוֹדַע לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Abbahu holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** that it is permitted to carry this item. **And when he happened** to come **to the place of Rabbi Yoḥanan he would not carry it, in deference to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** so as not to act contrary to his ruling in the place where he was the authority. The Gemara asks: **But there was a servant** accompanying Rabbi Abbahu who would see him carrying these types of articles that had been set aside. Wasn't Rabbi Abbahu concerned that the servant might carry them in Rabbi Yoḥanan's locale? The Gemara explains **that he would inform the servant** and explain to him the reason for his change in behavior.

The Gemara asks how this is to be understood. Different places may follow different *psakim* — that much has been established — but Rabbi Abba is a single individual. How could one person practice differently in two different places? The answer is that Rabbi Abba personally ruled like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, the lenient position. But when he was in the place of Rabbi Yochanan, he refrained from carrying the lamp out of deference — *mishum kvod de-Rabbi Yochanan*. He was not changing his *psak*; he simply did not act on it in Rabbi Yochanan's presence, as a gesture of respect.

The Gemara then asks: but what about his attendant? His attendant had seen Rabbi Abba carry the lamp in one place, and might assume the practice applies in Rabbi Yochanan's place as well. The answer: Rabbi Abba informed his attendant ahead of time. He would tell him: when we are in the place of Rabbi Yochanan, be careful not to carry the lamp.

This episode — the fact that Rabbi Abba took care to inform his attendant of the change in practice — actually anticipates the Gemara's resolution of the broader Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel question, as will become clear.

## Beit Shammai Question

### Mishna Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵלּוּ אוֹסְרִים וְאֵלּוּ מַתִּירִים — לֹא נִמְנְעוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מִלִּישָּׂא נָשִׁים מִבֵּית הִלֵּל, וְלֹא בֵּית הִלֵּל מִבֵּית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the question of whether Beit Shammai followed their own rulings. **Come** and **hear** that which is taught in the mishna: **Although** Beit Hillel **prohibit and** Beit Shammai **permit,** and these disqualify the women and those deem them fit, **Beit Shammai did not refrain from marrying women from Beit Hillel, nor did Beit Hillel** refrain from marrying women **from Beit Shammai.**

Returning now to the central question, the Gemara cites the statement of the Mishna: *af al pi she'elu ossrin ve'elu mattirin* — even though these forbid and these permit — *lo nimne'u Beit Shammai milisa nashim miBeit Hillel vilo Beit Hillel miBeit Shammai* — Beit Shammai did not refrain from marrying women of Beit Hillel, nor did Beit Hillel from women of Beit Shammai.

### Two Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לֹא עָשׂוּ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לֹא נִמְנְעוּ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ עָשׂוּ, אַמַּאי לֹא נִמְנְעוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that Beit Shammai **did not act** in accordance with their opinion, it is **due to that** reason that **they did not** have to **refrain** from marrying women from Beit Hillel. **However, if you say** that **they did act** in accordance with their opinion, **why didn't they refrain** from marrying one another?

The Gemara now revisits the two possible ways of reading this Mishna. If one says that Beit Shammai did not keep to their own practices — that is, that they followed Beit Hillel — then of course there would be no impediment to intermarriage: *mishum hachi lo nimne'u*, that is precisely why they could intermarry. But if one says that Beit Shammai maintained their own practices, then the Mishna's statement becomes deeply puzzling: *amai lo nimne'u* — how could they not hold themselves back from intermarrying?

## Tzarot Practices

### Asymmetric Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מִבֵּית הִלֵּל לֹא נִמְנְעוּ, דִּבְנֵי חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין נִינְהוּ. אֶלָּא בֵּית הִלֵּל מִבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אַמַּאי לֹא נִמְנְעוּ? בְּנֵי חַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת — מַמְזֵרִים נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Granted, Beit Shammai did not refrain** from marrying into **Beit Hillel,** as even if Beit Shammai maintain in a certain case that a rival wife required levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*, if she went ahead and married another man their **children are** born to a union whose partners are **liable by** a regular **prohibition:** "The wife of the dead man shall not be married outside" (Deuteronomy 25:5). Since this transgression does not entail *karet*, the children of this relationship are not *mamzerim*. **However, why did Beit Hillel not refrain** from marrying into **Beit Shammai?** In the opinion of Beit Hillel the **children** of these rival wives who entered into levirate marriage are born of a union whose partners are **liable to** receive ***karet*,** as the prohibition of a brother's wife was never nullified in this case, which means that the children **are *mamzerim*.** If so, how could Beit Hillel allow these marriages?

There is an asymmetry in the concern here that requires examination. As the Gemara will work out, the concern is not equal in both directions. Beit Shammai would have less reason to fear marrying into Beit Hillel, while Beit Hillel would have very strong reason to fear marrying into Beit Shammai. Understanding why requires working through the specifics of the *tzara* dispute.

## Beit Shammai Question…

### Lav vs. Kares

The Gemara spells out why Beit Shammai would have relatively little to fear in marrying into Beit Hillel. According to Beit Hillel's position, the *tzara* is exempt from *yibbum* and goes free. According to Beit Shammai, she is obligated in either *yibbum* or *chalitzah*. If she proceeds to marry another man without *chalitzah*, that is a violation of a *lav* from Beit Shammai's perspective — but the child of such a union is not a *mamzer*, because a child born of a violation of a *lav* alone is not rendered a *mamzer*. So the worst outcome for Beit Shammai when it comes to children from Beit Hillel women is that there has been a *lav* violation — significant, but not producing *mamzerius*.

### Mamzerim Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא קָסָבְרִי בֵּית הִלֵּל דְּאֵין מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת — וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנֶּחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּצָרוֹת, מוֹדִים שֶׁאֵין מַמְזֵר אֶלָּא מִמִּי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה וְעָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **Beit Hillel maintain that** the child of a union whose partners are **liable to** receive ***karet* is not a *mamzer*,** as a *mamzer* is only one whose parents violated a prohibition that entails the death penalty, **didn't Rabbi Elazar say: Although Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagreed** with regard **to rival wives, they concede that a *mamzer* is only from** a union **whose prohibition** is **a prohibition of forbidden relations punishable by *karet*?** Consequently, the children of rival wives are *mamzerim* according to Beit Hillel.

The situation is entirely different for Beit Hillel in relation to Beit Shammai. According to Beit Hillel, the *tzara* is completely exempt from *yibbum* — she is the *tzaras ervah*, and the prohibition of *eishet ach* applies. If Beit Shammai performs *yibbum* with the *tzara* — as they maintain is not only permitted but obligatory — then from Beit Hillel's perspective, this is an act of *issur kares*. The children of such a union are *mamzerim*. Beit Hillel could not countenance marrying into families where they believed *mamzeirim* had been produced. The asymmetry is therefore stark: Beit Shammai has relatively little to lose, while Beit Hillel has everything to lose.

The Gemara therefore presses: one might answer that Beit Hillel holds that *mamzeirus* does not result from *chayvei kerisot*, only from *chayvei misos*. But this is foreclosed by Rabbi Elazar's ruling, which we will examine shortly, that both houses agree there is no *mamzer* except from one whose prohibition carries a penalty of *kares*. Since Beit Hillel holds the *yibbum* done by Beit Shammai on the *tzara* is an *issur kares*, the child is indeed a *mamzer* according to Beit Hillel.

## Informing Solution

### Shammai Kept Stance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם עָשׂוּ, דְּמוֹדְעִי לְהוּ וּפָרְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **No; actually,** Beit Shammai **did act** in accordance with their opinion. As for the problem with these marriages, the answer is **that they would inform** Beit Hillel **and** Beit Hillel would **withdraw** from the match. When those who acted in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel would come to marry women from those who followed the rulings of Beit Shammai, they would be notified that certain children were born of rival wives and that those people were considered *mamzerim* in the opinion of Beit Hillel, who therefore declared them forbidden in marriage.

The Gemara concludes that Beit Shammai did in fact maintain their own practices — *lo le'olam assu* is rejected; *le'olam assu* is affirmed. They did not abandon their *pesak*.

## Informing Solution…

### Demodiim Lehu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: כָּל הַטְּהָרוֹת וְכׇל הַטְּמָאוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ אֵלּוּ מְטַהֲרִין וְאֵלּוּ מְטַמְּאִין — לֹא נִמְנְעוּ עוֹשִׂים טְהָרוֹת אֵלּוּ עַל גַּבֵּי אֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And so too, it is reasonable** that this is the case, **as** the mishna **teaches** in **the latter clause:** With regard to **all of the** disputes concerning the *halakhot* of **ritual purity and impurity, where those,** Beit Hillel, rule an article **ritually pure and these,** Beit Shammai, rule it **ritually impure, they did not refrain from handling ritually pure** objects **each with the other.**

How then did they intermarry? The Gemara answers: *demodei'ei lehu ufarshi* — they were understanding and sympathetic enough to one another, such that they would proactively inform each other of relevant family status. If someone from Beit Hillel sought to marry into a Beit Shammai family, Beit Shammai would say: look, you should know that this woman's status involves a *tzara* situation — here is the background. They shared the information because they genuinely respected each other's halachic position and were concerned for one another's well-being. This did not mean there was no concern — of course there was — but the *ahava* and *rei'ut*, the love and friendship between the houses, motivated proactive disclosure. The concern about *mamzerius* would then be neutralized not by ignoring it but by supplying the information needed to navigate it.

This dynamic is not unfamiliar. Sometimes a host will say, when inviting guests of different halachic standards: here is exactly what I use, here is my practice, so you can make an informed decision. The *chiddush* here is that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel operated with that degree of mutual transparency.

### Tumat Tahara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּמוֹדְעִי לְהוּ — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לֹא נִמְנְעוּ, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּלָא מוֹדְעִי לְהוּ, בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מִבֵּית הִלֵּל לֹא נִמְנְעוּ — דִּטְמָאוֹת דְּבֵית הִלֵּל, לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי טְהָרוֹת נִינְהוּ. אֶלָּא בֵּית הִלֵּל מִבֵּית שַׁמַּאי לָמָּה לֹא נִמְנְעוּ? טְהָרוֹת דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, לְבֵית הִלֵּל טְמָאוֹת נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּמוֹדְעִי לְהוּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say that they notified them,** it is **due to that** reason that **they did not** need to **refrain** from using their objects. **However, if you say** that **they did not notify them, granted,** it is logical that **Beit Shammai did not refrain from** handling items belonging to **Beit Hillel, as ritually impure** objects **for Beit Hillel are ritually pure for Beit Shammai,** and therefore no special care is necessary. **However, why didn't Beit Hillel refrain from** touching articles that belonged to **Beit Shammai?** After all, **ritually pure** objects **for Beit Shammai are ritually impure for Beit Hillel. Rather, is it not** the case that Beit Shammai **notified** Beit Hillel that these items were ritually pure only in their own opinion, and Beit Hillel separated themselves from them? The Gemara summarizes the discussion: **Conclude from** here that this is the correct interpretation.

The Gemara then offers a corroborating proof from the *seipha* of the same source: *kol ha-teharos ve-kol ha-temeios she-hayu eileh metaharim ve-eileh metameim lo nimnu osim teharos elu al gabei elu* — all the matters of purity and impurity, where one house declared pure and the other declared impure, they did not refrain from using each other's vessels for *taharos*. The Gemara asks: how was this possible? If one holds the other's vessel is *tamei*, how can the *machmir* borrow from the *meikel*? What the *meikel* considers *tahor*, the *machmir* considers *tamei*. The only way to explain this is that they informed each other — *ela lav demodei lehu*. They would disclose the status of their vessels so the other house could make an appropriate judgment. The Gemara accepts this as *shema mina* — a valid proof.

The Gemara then asks why we need both proofs — the intermarriage case and the *tumah ve-tahara* case. The answer: one might have thought that in the case of the *tzara*, the matter was well-known — *tzara kala it lei* — and they did not need to actively inform each other because the information was public. But from the case of *tumah ve-tahara*, where there was no particular reason the status would be publicly known, we learn that the houses went out of their way to inform one another even in cases that were not self-evident.

### Love and Friendship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁחִיבָּה וְרֵיעוּת נוֹהֲגִים זֶה בָּזֶה, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָאֱמֶת וְהַשָּׁלוֹם אֱהָבוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** This serves **to teach you that** they **practiced affection and camaraderie between them, to fulfill that which is stated: "Love truth and peace"** (Zechariah 8:19).

The *braita* draws its conclusion with a verse: *ha'emet ve'hashalom ahavu* — love both truth and peace. The Maharsha observes beautifully that this is not *emes le'amito* — not the claim that there is one absolute exclusive truth — but rather the *emes* of *eilu ve'eilu divrei Elokim chayim*, a pluralistic truth. The houses were able to maintain both truth and peace precisely because they could embrace the notion that the other's position, though not their own, was a legitimate expression of the living word of God. That is what made the combination of *emet ve'shalom* possible: not compromise, but genuine respect for legitimate pluralism.

## Tzarot Practices…

### Information Exchange

The Gemara's conclusion regarding the *tzarot* case is therefore that Beit Shammai gave Beit Hillel the relevant information about family status. They did not abandon their practices, and they did not pretend the differences did not exist — but they cared enough about one another to ensure that no one was unwittingly walking into a halachic problem according to the other's *psak*.

## Yerushalmi Position

### Yerushalmi Challenge

The Bavli's resolution — that the houses informed each other and thus intermarriage was possible without compromising either *pesak* — is notably not the approach of the Yerushalmi. The Yerushalmi in Yevamot poses the same question: *mamzerius beintayim ve'at amarta hachin?* — there are *mamzeirim* in this situation and you are saying they married one another? How could that have happened?

One answer in the Yerushalmi, attributed to Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is that Beit Shammai conceded to Beit Hillel on the stringent side — a position that parallels certain Bavli discussions. Then Rabbi Tanchum in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds: *elu ve'elu k'halacha hayu osim* — each house followed their own *halacha* properly. And this leads again to the question: if each followed their own *halacha*, how did intermarriage work?

### Divine Providence

The Yerushalmi's ultimate answer is: *hamakom mishamer ve'lo ira maaseh le'olam* — God watched over them and it never actually came to pass. That is to say, it never actually happened that someone from Beit Hillel married a person who according to their own *psak* was a *mamzer* from a Beit Shammai union. Divine Providence ensured it.

### Legal Fictions

This answer is striking in that it actually accepts the Mishna's literal meaning at face value — yes, they simply intermarried, each following their own *halacha* — and resolves the problem not by harmonizing the practices but by invoking a divine guarantee that the collision of *psakim* would never actually produce harm. The Bavli is unwilling to rest on that, and instead constructs the answer of mutual notification. But the Yerushalmi is apparently more comfortable with the notion that the system will be protected from its own consequences.

This connects to a broader characteristic of the Yerushalmi: a greater readiness to sustain legal fictions even in the face of countervailing evidence. A case in point, which will arise much later in this tractate, involves a woman whose husband was testified to be dead, who then remarried, and whose original husband subsequently reappears. The Yerushalmi's approach in such cases is to insist that the legal conclusion stands — clearly he is not her husband, because the witnesses testified he died — even when the facts on the ground seem to say otherwise. The capacity to maintain a halachic determination against empirical counter-evidence is something the Yerushalmi is more prepared to deploy than the Bavli.

A further parallel from the Yerushalmi, striking in its own right: the Yerushalmi rules that if a girl below age three was violated, the hymen regenerates. But above age three, it does not. The Yerushalmi then says that if such an event occurred when she had just turned three — but the court subsequently declared a leap year, inserting a second Adar — then her birthday technically had not yet occurred, and the hymen *will* regenerate. This is not a physical claim so much as an assertion that *beit din*'s legal determinations carry metaphysical force, or perhaps that halachic categorization can be sustained even in tension with what the physical facts seem to show. The Yerushalmi's answer of *hamakom mishamer* fits within this same orientation: we are prepared to say that the system works, that divine providence undergirds the halachic world, rather than that we must preemptively restructure practice to ensure no collision occurs.

The Bavli, by contrast, wants to work within what is knowable and manageable — and so it gives us the answer of mutual disclosure.

## Mamzer Analysis

### Elazar Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנֶּחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּצָרוֹת, מוֹדִים שֶׁאֵין מַמְזֵר אֶלָּא מִמִּי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה וְעָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Since the statement of Rabbi Elazar was mentioned, the Gemara turns to discuss **the** matter **itself. Rabbi Elazar said: Although Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagreed** with regard **to rival wives, they concede that a *mamzer* is only from** a union **whose prohibition** is **a prohibition of forbidden relations punishable by *karet*.**

The Gemara now takes up the specific legal statement of Rabbi Elazar: *af al pi shenechleku Beit Shammai uBeit Hillel betzaros, modin she'ein mamzer ela mimi she'issuro issur erva ve'anush kares* — even though Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagreed about the status of *tzaros*, they agree that there is no *mamzer* except from one whose prohibition is *issur ervah* with a penalty of *kares*.

### Concession Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן מוֹדִים? אִילֵּימָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵּית הִלֵּל — פְּשִׁיטָא! בְּנֵי חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין, כְּשֵׁרִים נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who concedes** to whom? **If we say** that **Beit Shammai** concede **to Beit Hillel,** this is **obvious,** since if they acted in accordance with their own opinion that rival wives who married others without *ḥalitza* **are liable** only **for** violating a regular **prohibition,** their **children are** therefore **fit** and are not *mamzerim* at all.

The Gemara asks: who is conceding to whom in this statement of *modin*? Is it Beit Shammai conceding to Beit Hillel, or Beit Hillel conceding to Beit Shammai?

### Shammai Concedes

The Gemara first considers: if it is Beit Shammai conceding to Beit Hillel, then the concession would be that children born in Beit Hillel's world — where a *tzara* marries another man without *chalitzah*, which Beit Shammai regards as a violation of a *lav* — are not *mamzerim*. But this is obvious: *pshita bnei chayavei lavin ninhu* — they are children of a mere *lav* violation, and nobody has ever suggested that a *lav* violation alone produces *mamzerius*. There is no *chiddush* there for Beit Shammai to make.

### Akiva Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵּית הִלֵּל, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: יֵשׁ מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאֵין מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Actually, Beit Shammai** concede **to Beit Hillel,** and this is not a novelty with regard to their basic dispute. Instead, the statement comes **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, who said:** The offspring **from** forbidden relations for which one is **liable for** violating **a prohibition is a *mamzer*.** Rabbi Elazar **teaches us** that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that the offspring **from** forbidden relations for which one is **liable for** violating **a prohibition is not a *mamzer*;** rather, one is a *mamzer* only if he is born of a union punishable by *karet*.

So the Gemara concludes: *ella Beit Hillel le'Beit Shammai* — it is Beit Hillel conceding to Beit Shammai. Beit Hillel acknowledges that where Beit Shammai performs *yibbum* with the *tzara*, even though Beit Hillel regards this as an *issur kares* and the child would be a *mamzer*, the *mamzer* status flows from *issur kares* — and that is exactly what *mamzerius* requires according to both houses.

The Gemara then clarifies what the *chiddush* is: *le'afukei mid'Rabbi Akiva* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that there is a *mamzer* even from *chayvei lavin*. The statement that both houses agree there is no *mamzer* except from *chayvei kerisot* is directed against Rabbi Akiva's position, establishing that neither Beit Shammai nor Beit Hillel follows him in this regard. As Tosafot notes, the question of whether even Rabbi Akiva would apply this rule to the specific case of *yevamah le'shuk* is debated, but the principal point stands: both houses align against the broader application of Rabbi Akiva's position.

## Tzarot Practices……

### Two Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: הֵיאַךְ הֲלָכָה זוֹ רוֹוַחַת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל? נַעֲשֶׂה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר לְדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל, נַעֲשֶׂה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל — הַוָּלָד פָּגוּם לְדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. בּוֹאוּ וּנְתַקֵּן לָהֶן לַצָּרוֹת, שֶׁיְּהוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara offers an alternative resolution: **Come** and **hear, as Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: How should** one act with regard to **this *halakha*** of rival wives? In what form should it **become widespread among the Jewish people?** If **we act in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai** and permit a rival wife in levirate marriage, **the offspring** will be **a *mamzer* according to the statement of Beit Hillel.** If **we act in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel** and grant full exemption to the rival wives, **the offspring will** be of **flawed** lineage **according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** as he is the child of a woman prohibited in marriage by a regular prohibition. Although he is not a *mamzer*, his lineage is nevertheless defective. Instead, **let us enact** a general decree **for rival wives that they should perform *ḥalitza* and not enter into levirate marriage.**

The Gemara now poses a dilemma from another *braita* that discusses how Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did or did not avoid intermarriage. The *braita* lists a range of disputes between the houses — *tzaros*, sisters, *get yashan*, *safek eishet ish*, a case of a woman divorced and then spending the night with her former husband at an inn, and the question of the value of the *kesef* for *kiddushin*. In the *kiddushin* case, the asymmetry is particularly sharp: Beit Shammai requires a *dinar* and Beit Hillel requires only a *prutah*. If Reuven betrothed a woman with a *prutah* and Shimon subsequently betrothed her with a *dinar*, then according to Beit Hillel she is married to Reuven and according to Beit Shammai she is married to Shimon. Whichever man sleeps with her, he has committed adultery according to the other house, and the child is a *mamzer*. This is not a case of *meikel* and *machmir* — it is a case where each *psak* creates a genuine prohibition for the other, and there is no hierarchy.

Despite all of this, the *braita* states: *lo nimne'u Beit Shammai milisa nashim miBeit Hillel vilo Beit Hillel miBeit Shammai*. And the reason given is *le'kayem mah shene'emar ha'emet ve'hashalom ahavu* — to fulfill the verse, "love truth and peace" — and *she'chibah ve're'ut nohagim zeh bazeh*, that love and friendship governed their relationship with one another.

If Beit Shammai maintained their own practices throughout, one understands why Rabbi Shimon later says they did hold themselves back in cases of *vadai* — but then the first part of the *braita* needs explanation. And if Beit Shammai followed Beit Hillel's practices, why does any of this controversy matter at all?

## Definite vs. Uncertain

### Shimon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: נִמְנְעוּ הֵן מִן הַוַּדַּאי, וְלֹא נִמְנְעוּ מִן הַסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: They did refrain in the certain** cases, **but they did not refrain in the uncertain** cases. In other words, Beit Hillel were not worried that any ordinary woman from Beit Shammai might be one of those of uncertain status.

Rabbi Shimon enters with a clarification: *nimne'u hen min ha'vadai ve'lo nimne'u min ha'safek* — they held themselves back from intermarrying in cases of *vadai* prohibition, but they did not hold themselves back in cases of *safek*. The idea is that where a woman was definitely forbidden according to one house's *psak*, that house would not marry her. But where the issue was one of doubt — as in the *safek eishet ish* case where a husband and wife spent a night together at an inn after divorce, and it is uncertain whether they effectively remarried — then one could be more accommodating of the other house's lenient ruling.

### Asymmetry Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי נָמֵי דְּעָשׂוּ, בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית הִלֵּל — נִמְנְעוּ מִבֵּית שַׁמַּאי, דְּחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת נִינְהוּ, וּמַמְזֵרִים הֵם לְבֵית הִלֵּל. אֶלָּא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — אַמַּאי נִמְנְעוּ מִבֵּית הִלֵּל? בְּנֵי חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין נִינְהוּ, וּכְשֵׁרִים נִינְהוּ! כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לַצָּרָה עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **Even if they acted** in accordance with their opinion, **granted** that **Beit Hillel refrained from** marrying into **Beit Shammai,** as **those** rival wives who entered into levirate marriage **are liable to** receive ***karet* and** their children **are *mamzerim* according to** the opinion of **Beit Hillel. However, why did Beit Shammai refrain from** marrying the offspring of rival wives of **Beit Hillel?** The parents are **liable for** violating a regular **prohibition, and** therefore their children **are fit. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This amendment was **necessary,** not for the benefit of the children of rival wives, but **only for the rival wife herself.** If these rival wives married others, in the opinion of Beit Shammai their marriages were flawed, as they did not perform *ḥalitza*.

The Gemara then asks: accepting that Beit Shammai maintained their own practices, one understands why Beit Hillel would hold themselves back from Beit Shammai — the children are *mamzerim* according to Beit Hillel. But why would Beit Shammai hold themselves back from Beit Hillel, even in a case of *vadai*? The children from Beit Hillel women are *bnei chayvei lavin* — they are not *mamzerim* at all. What is the concern that would cause Beit Shammai to refuse marriage in a clear case? The Gemara answers by invoking the principle stated later by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *lo nitzrecha ela le'tzara atzma* — the issue is not the children but the *tzara* herself. Marrying the *tzara* directly would be a violation of a *lav* according to Beit Shammai, and Beit Shammai would not want to be *over* on that *issur lav*. So Beit Shammai would hold themselves back not because of the children's status but because of the woman's own status.

### Stam Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִן הַוַּדַּאי — דְּאִיסּוּרָא הוּא, סָפֵק נָמֵי אִיסּוּרָא הוּא! לָא תֵּימָא: מִן הַסָּפֵק, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מִן הַסְּתָם, דְּמוֹדְעִי לְהוּ וּפָרְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And in what** way are uncertain cases **different from the certain** cases, such as forbidden rival wives? If you say **that** certain cases are undoubtedly **prohibited** in the opinion of Beit Hillel, the cases involving **uncertain** circumstances, e.g., one who divorced his wife and then stayed with her in the same inn, **are also prohibited.** Even if Beit Hillel render these cases prohibited only due to some uncertainty, the ruling is the same. The Gemara answers: **Do not say: In the uncertain** cases, i.e., that the *halakha* involved doubtful circumstances. **Rather, say: From the unspecified** case. In other words, barring clear knowledge of an uncertain betrothal within a specific family, they would take women from that family in marriage. The reason is **that** Beit Shammai **would notify** Beit Hillel of the prohibition according to their opinion, **and** they would **refrain** from the marriage. If there was no notification, this was a clear sign that no doubt was involved in this case at all.

The Gemara then catches a problem: Rabbi Shimon said they would not hold themselves back in cases of *safek*. But *safek issur d'Oraita le'chumra* — a doubtful Torah-level prohibition should be treated stringently. Why should a *safek* be more permissive? The Gemara answers: *lo teima min ha'safek ela eima min ha'stam* — do not say the category is *safek* (doubt); rather say the category is *stam* — cases where the status of the woman was simply not known, not cases of active uncertainty. The resolution is that in a *stam* case, one could rely on the assumption that if there were a problem, one would have been informed. *Demodeu lehu parshi* — the proactive disclosure between the houses was reliable enough that a person could marry any woman whose status was simply unknown, trusting that had there been an issue, someone would have said something. The *stam* intermarriage was thus not a gamble on a *safek* but rather an act of confidence in the system of mutual transparency.

### Mutual Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאַהֲבָה וְרֵיעוּת נוֹהֲגִים זֶה בָּזֶה? הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּכוּלַּהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **what** does **this** come to **teach us?** Is it **that** they had **relations of affection and camaraderie between them,** i.e., that each trusted that the other side would never cause them to err with regard to something they held to be prohibited? **This is** the same as the **first clause** of the *baraita*. What is Rabbi Shimon adding by his statement? The Gemara answers: This comes to **teach us that the entire** *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** This is not a dispute between two Sages. Rather, Rabbi Shimon's opinion elucidates the earlier statement.

This is a significant *chiddush*. The beginning of the *braita* says that they intermarried freely — *lo nimne'u*. According to this reading, that refers to *stam* cases: any woman about whom nothing particular was known. The confidence to marry her rested not on ignoring the other house's concerns but on the assurance that the mutual love and friendship between the houses guaranteed proactive notification when necessary. As Rashi frames it: I know that in your community there are women who according to my *psak* are *mamzerot*. But I can go ahead and seek a wife there because I know that if there is a problem, you will tell me. And you can assume the same from me. The *chiddush* of the *braita* — *she'chibah ve're'ut nohagim zeh bazeh* — is precisely this: that the love and friendship between the houses was reliable enough to serve as a halachic foundation for trust.

### Shimon Authorship

The Gemara concludes: *ha ka'mashma lan d'kula Rabbi Shimon hi* — the entire *braita* is to be attributed to Rabbi Shimon. This is a significant move: the beginning of the *braita*, which appeared to say simply that they did not hold themselves back — suggesting perhaps a radical tolerance of the other house's *psak* even in cases of *vadai* — is now read through the lens of Rabbi Shimon's clarification. Rabbi Shimon is not simply disagreeing with the beginning of the *braita*; he is its author and is explaining what it means. They held back in *vadai* cases, they did not hold back in *stam* cases — and the stated reason of love and friendship is the explanation of how *stam* cases could be navigated with confidence: through the reliable practice of mutual disclosure.

## Nuri's Proposal

### Nuri's Question

The Gemara now introduces the proposal of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri: *heicha halacha revacha be'Yisrael?* — how should the *halacha* be propagated broadly in Israel regarding the dispute of *tzaras ervah*? Should we follow Beit Shammai or Beit Hillel? This framing suggests an attempt to establish a consensus *pesak* — a single ruling that would apply throughout.

### Mutual Problems

The difficulty is that each position creates a problem from the other's perspective. If we rule like Beit Shammai — that *yibbum* may be performed with the *tzara* — then according to Beit Hillel, the children of that *yibbum* are *mamzerim*. If we rule like Beit Hillel — that the *tzara* is completely free and requires neither *yibbum* nor *chalitzah* — then according to Beit Shammai the child born from her subsequent marriage is *pagum*, flawed in status, as the child of a *lav* violation. And leaving aside the children, the woman herself is in a problematic status according to one house or the other. There is no single ruling that satisfies both houses' concerns simultaneously.

### Chalitzah Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִסְפִּיקוּ לִגְמוֹר אֶת הַדָּבָר עַד שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה הַשָּׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They were unable to finalize the matter** according to the proposition raised by Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **before times of trouble** arrived. Due to the outbreak of war they were unable to gather together to vote and establish an accepted halakhic ruling.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's proposed solution is elegant: *bo'u ve'nesaken lahem le'tzaros she'choltzot ve'lo misyabmot* — establish as the requirement that every *tzara* perform *chalitzah* and not *yibbum*. Beit Hillel is satisfied because the woman is freed without *yibbum* having been performed. Beit Shammai is satisfied because *chalitzah* was performed — even if they believe *yibbum* would have been preferable, their requirement has been met. The cost is that *chalitzah* renders a woman forbidden to marry a *kohen*, but at least it resolves the question in a way that neither house can object to in principle.

The parallel to contemporary discussions about *geirus* standards is noteworthy: some have proposed that everyone simply undergo a new conversion *le'chumra* to satisfy all positions — a *yotzei kol hadeos* approach. But as the Gemara's subsequent discussion makes clear, such an approach is not without serious costs.

Ultimately, this proposal was never brought to completion: *lo hispiku ligmor et ha'davar* — they did not manage to conclude the matter before the opportunity was lost. Something disrupted the process, and the consensus was never formalized.

## Gamliel's Objection

### Earlier Tzarot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מָה נַעֲשֶׂה לָהֶם לַצָּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת מֵעַתָּה?
>
> **Translation:** Sometime later, when they returned to discuss the issue, **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said to** the other Sages: **What shall we do with those earlier rival wives from now** onward? Since in the meantime some rival wives had entered into levirate marriage, if we issue a collective ruling that rival wives may not do so, their children will be declared *mamzerim*. Therefore, it is better not to establish this *halakha* at all.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel raised an objection to the proposal: *mah na'aseh le'tzara rishona mei'ata?* — what are we to do about the earlier *tzaros*, the women who were already in this situation? If we now establish that *chalitzah* is required, what does that imply about all the previous cases?

### No Problem If Followed

The Gemara considers: if Beit Shammai had been following Beit Hillel all along — that is, if the resolution were that *lo assu*, they did not maintain their own practices — then this objection would not apply. Everyone had been following Beit Hillel, no *yibbum* had been done, and there are no prior cases to be concerned about. But since the Gemara has concluded that *le'olam assu* — Beit Shammai maintained its own practices — there are indeed earlier *tzaros* who may have done *yibbum* or whose status may be affected.

### Tzara Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא עָשׂוּ — הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר מָה נַעֲשֶׂה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לֹא עָשׂוּ, מַאי מָה נַעֲשֶׂה? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְצָרָה עַצְמָהּ, ״וּמָה נַעֲשֶׂה״ הָכִי קָאָמַר: הָנָךְ צָרוֹת דְּבֵית הִלֵּל, לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this episode in relation to the matter at hand. **Granted, if you say** that Beit Shammai **did act** in accordance with their own opinion, **this is** the meaning of **what** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **said: What shall we do?** Since these rival wives had already entered into levirate marriage, it was impossible to change the status of their children retroactively. **However, if you say** that **they did not act** in accordance with their opinion, **what is** the meaning of: **What shall we do?** If Beit Shammai did not act upon their ruling, they never actually permitted a *yavam* to take a rival wife in levirate marriage. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This amendment was **necessary,** not for the benefit of the children of rival wives, as Beit Shammai never in fact acted on their ruling; **rather,** it was necessary **only for the rival wife herself.** If these rival wives married others, in the opinion of Beit Shammai their marriages were flawed, as they did not perform *ḥalitza*. And as for the question: **What shall we do, this is what** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **is saying:** With regard to **those rival wives** who were entirely exempt according **to Beit Hillel, what** should **we do with them** according **to Beit Shammai,** as these rival wives had already married others without performing *ḥalitza*?

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak sharpens the concern: *lo nitzrecha ela le'tzara atzma* — the real issue is not the children but the *tzara* herself. The scenario is this: under the working arrangement where everyone has been following Beit Hillel, the *tzara* has been treated as completely free — she has married another man and built her life. Now, if we formally require *chalitzah* going forward, we implicitly say that there *was* a *zika*, an obligation of *yibbum* or *chalitzah*, hanging over her. That retroactively casts a shadow over her status — she has been living with a man without *chalitzah* having been performed, and now we are suggesting that perhaps she should have had *chalitzah* first.

### Human Cost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיחְלְצוּ — מִימַּאֲסִי אַגַּבְרַיְיהוּ. וְכִי תֵּימָא לִימַּאֲסָן — ״דְּרָכֶיהָ דַרְכֵי נוֹעַם וְכׇל נְתִיבוֹתֶיהָ שָׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: If you say **let them perform *ḥalitza*, they will be repulsive to their husbands,** as it will seem to the husband that the woman he has been living with for some time suddenly requires a permit for marriage. **And if you would say, let them be repulsive** to their husbands, as that is not our concern, this is not the case, as the Torah says: **"Her ways are ways of pleasantness, and all her paths are peace"** (Proverbs 3:17).

The specific human cost is captured in the phrase: *mi masya gavreihu* — her husband will find her disgusting. The meaning is that he will feel retroactively troubled, knowing that he had married a woman who, according to at least one major *psak*, still had an unresolved *zika* to her deceased husband's brother when she came to him. Even if one says the Beit Hillel *psak* was correct and there was no actual prohibition, the retroactive implication is damaging. The suggestion that she now do *chalitzah* sends a message about her prior status that is destructive to the marriage and to her dignity.

### Pleasantness

The Gemara invokes the principle: *darcheha darchei noam ve'chol nesivoseha shalom* — the Torah's ways are ways of pleasantness and all its paths are peace. Playing it safe by imposing *chalitzah* on everyone sounds responsible, but it carries enormous human costs — costs that the Torah does not countenance. This principle is equally relevant to modern analogies involving *geirus*: when rabbinic authorities suggest that a convert simply do a *mikvah* again *le'chumra*, or that a couple perform a new *kiddushin* to satisfy all opinions, they often underestimate the profound message that sends about the person's prior status, their family's standing, and their own sense of religious authenticity. The Gemara here is explicit that those costs are real halachic considerations — not sentimentalities to be overridden in the name of safety. *Pasken* a position and follow it. The attempt to cover all bases at human expense is not a *chumra*; it is a violation of *darchei noam*.
