# Shabbat 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Women's Ornaments

### Intro & Nose Rings

Today's *daf* is 60. We begin at the bottom of 59b, five lines down in the wide lines, with the words *velo be-katla*. The *mishnah* is enumerating various items that a woman may not wear outdoors on *Shabbat*, due to a concern that she will come to remove them and carry them — although, interestingly, the *mishnah* itself never states this rationale explicitly; it is the *Gemara* that supplies it.

What is a *katla*? The *Gemara* explains it is a *minkata de-parei*, a kind of small bib or breastplate suspended from the neck, with respect to which there is presumably a concern it will be removed. *Nezamim* — rings — here refers specifically to nose rings, *nizmei ha-af*, not earrings. Rashi and Tosafot both explain that earrings would be too complicated to remove, and therefore we are not concerned they will be taken off. Nose rings, one might suppose, are at least as difficult to remove as earrings, but that is the ruling as stated.

The *mishnah* then says *velo be-taba'at she'ein aleha chotam* — a woman may not go out with a non-signet ring. The underlying logic, as the *Gemara* will explain, is that a non-signet ring is still considered jewelry and its prohibition is therefore only *de-rabbanan* — we are concerned she may remove it. A signet ring, by contrast, is not fundamentally jewelry but rather a functional tool for carrying around one's seal, and therefore the concern there is biblical: wearing it constitutes carrying.

### Signet Ring Debate

The *Gemara* spells this out directly. *Ha-yesh aleha chotam, chayyevet* — if the ring bears a seal, the woman is liable, because it is not considered a *takhshit*. The *Gemara* then raises a difficulty: *ve-raminhu* — we have a contradiction from a *mishnah* in *Kelim* (*Mishnah Kelim*) which lists women's ornaments — *takhshitei nashim* — that are susceptible to *tum'ah* because they are considered a form of *kli*. That list includes *katla'ot*, *nezamim*, *taba'ot* — and then, regarding finger rings specifically, the *mishnah* states *bein she-yesh aleha chotam bein she-ein aleha chotam*: whether it has a seal or not, it is considered jewelry and can become *tamei*. The list continues with *nizmei ha-af*, nose rings.

So we have two apparent inconsistencies. The first, which the *Gemara* passes over, concerns earrings: Rashi and Tosafot explain that earrings are jewelry and we permit wearing them because we are not afraid a woman will remove them — our *mishnah*'s list covers only items we fear will be taken off, and for whatever reason, earrings are not among them. The more serious problem is with signet rings. The *mishnah* in *Kelim* seems to treat a signet ring as a woman's jewelry, while a later *mishnah* in our tractate will state explicitly that a woman wearing a signet ring is engaged in biblical carrying. Is it jewelry or not?

### Zeira's Answer

The *Gemara* introduces Rabbi Zeira's resolution with the phrase *ve-amar Rabbi Zeira* — notable for the introductory *vav* — and he says: *lo kashya*. The two *mishnayot* reflect a dispute between Rabbi Nechemiah and the *rabbanan*, as we learn from a *baraita*.

### Mixed Materials

The *baraita* discusses a ring made of mixed materials — for instance, *hi she-metakhet ve-chotamah shel almog*: the ring itself is metal and the seal is cedar wood. Since a ring has no *beit kibbul* (receptacle), its susceptibility to *tum'ah* depends on whether it is classified as a metal vessel or a wood vessel. Metal vessels become *tamei* even without a *beit kibbul*; wood vessels require one.

The first opinion in the *baraita* holds that a ring made of metal with a wooden seal is *tamei*, because the ring part is what defines it — it is fundamentally a metal vessel. Conversely, a ring made of wood with a metal seal is *tahor*, because the wood of the ring is primary. Rabbi Nechemiah disagrees: *be-taba'at holech achar chotamah* — when it comes to a signet ring, you go by the seal. The seal defines the essential character of the vessel. Tosafot adds that Rabbi Nechemiah's principle would apply symmetrically: if the ring is metal and the seal is wood, he would say the ring is *tahor*, because the seal always determines the nature of the object.

The *baraita* then applies the same analytical principle to a series of other composite vessels. With a *beol* — a yoke — one goes by the vertical bars (*simlonah*) that press against the animal's neck and hold it in place, not by the crossbar. Rashi explains that in the relevant period, yokes were not U-shaped as in modern depictions but rather consisted of a horizontal bar across the neck of the animal with two vertical posts on either side. With a *kolav* — a frame from which a shopkeeper would hang wares — one goes by the nails from which things are hung, *holech achar masmerotav*, since the function of the entire structure is to make those hanging points available. With a *sulam*, a ladder, one goes by the rungs, *holech achar shlibotav*, which is again intuitively understandable since the rungs are what one actually uses. With an *arsi* — a type of scales, depicted in the Steinsaltz edition as a balance with chains and two weighing pans — one goes by the chains, *holech achar shalshilotav*. This last case is somewhat harder to parse conceptually, since the chains are not obviously the most central component, yet that is the ruling. The *chachamim* offer their own principle: *hakol holech achar ha-maamid* — one goes by whatever gives the vessel its structure and form. By a ladder, this might mean the side rails; by some of the other examples, the identification of the *maamid* is less obvious.

The relevance of all this to our question is as follows. According to the *rabbanan*, who follow the ring part of a signet ring, even a ring with a seal is fundamentally a ring — a piece of jewelry — and therefore the *mishnah* in *Kelim* rightly lists it among women's ornaments that are susceptible to *tum'ah*. According to Rabbi Nechemiah, who follows the seal, a signet ring is not primarily a ring but a device for carrying a seal, and therefore it is not jewelry. The *mishnah* in our tractate that treats a woman's signet ring as biblical carrying reflects Rabbi Nechemiah's position.

One might note the analogy to a contemporary question: whether a wristwatch is fundamentally jewelry and clothing that happens to tell the time, or merely a convenient means of carrying a miniature clock. According to Rabbi Nechemiah's framework, if the functional instrument defines the object, the ring is not jewelry but a carrying vessel — and wearing it constitutes *masa*.

### Rava's Gender Norms

The *Gemara* then presents a second answer. Rava says: *letzdadin kitani* — the *mishnah* in *Kelim* should not be read as declaring all rings to be women's jewelry. Rather: *yesh aleha chotam — takhshit ish*, a ring with a seal is a man's jewelry; *ein aleha chotam — takhshit isha*, a ring without a seal is a woman's jewelry. The social norm was that signet rings were associated with men — presumably because men were more involved in commercial dealings requiring a seal — while plain decorative rings were associated with women. This perfectly accounts for the later *mishnah* in our tractate, which will state that a woman wearing a signet ring is engaged in biblical carrying, while a man wearing one is not, because for a man it falls within the norm of jewelry.

This point connects to a striking example discussed in the preceding *daf*: the case of Rabbi Meir and the *ir shel zahav*, a gold tiara. Even if an individual woman wears something with ornamental intent, once the Rabbis determine that such an item falls entirely outside accepted norms, it is classified as a burden regardless of her subjective purpose. The classification follows social convention, not personal intention.

### Tum'ah vs Shabbat

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a third answer, introduced by the elegant phrase *tum'ah ve-Shabbat karamit?* — are you comparing *tum'ah* and *Shabbat*? This is a striking methodological observation. The *Gemara* frequently assumes that a definition from one halachic domain transfers seamlessly into another, as if legal categories were universal. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak insists that *tum'ah* and *Shabbat* operate with fundamentally different concerns and therefore generate different definitions.

For *tum'ah*, the Torah says *kli ma'aseh* — any vessel can become *tamei*. The category is broad: a toothpick, a fork, any functional object qualifies. For *Shabbat*, the concern is *mishum masa* — carrying — and the exemption applies only to clothing or jewelry. Anything else is a burden. Therefore: *ein aleha chotam — takhshit*, without a seal the ring is jewelry and wearing it on *Shabbat* is at most a rabbinic prohibition; *yesh aleha chotam — masa*, with a seal, the ring is not jewelry but a burden, and wearing it is a biblical violation. When the *mishnah* in *Kelim* said that rings — whether with a seal or without — are susceptible to *tum'ah*, it was not classifying both as jewelry. The plain ring is jewelry; the signet ring is a vessel in the broader *tum'ah* sense, usable for impressing wax seals. Both can become *tamei*, but for different reasons.

Taken together, the three answers are largely consistent with the practical halacha that will emerge from the later *mishnah*: a woman wearing a signet ring is engaged in biblical carrying; a man wearing one is wearing jewelry. For *tum'ah*, the signet ring can become *tamei* either because it is men's jewelry (Rava's answer) or because it qualifies as a vessel even when not classified as jewelry (Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's answer). The critical methodological point — that the definitions of jewelry for *Shabbat* are far narrower than the broad vessel category governing *tum'ah* — deserves emphasis. One cannot simply import categorical determinations across halachic domains.

## Needles & Hairpins

### Needle Purpose

The *Gemara* continues: *velo be-machat she'eina nekuva* — a woman may not go out with a needle that has no hole. If the needle has a hole, it is considered an implement for sewing and therefore carrying it is not even a rabbinic issue in the same category — but if it has no hole, it is considered a form of clothing accessory rather than a tool, and the concern is rabbinic: we are afraid she will remove it.

The *Gemara* asks: *le-mai chazya?* — what would a woman ordinarily use this for? Rav Yosef answers: *ho'il ve-isha ogeret bah se'arah* — because a woman uses it to gather her hair. Rashi understands this as something like a long knitting needle around which she winds the hair emerging from her cap. Tosafot, however, explains that it is a pin used to secure a hairnet so it will not slip off — and Tosafot's reading fits better with the subsequent *Gemara*. On this understanding, the needle functions as a hatpin and is therefore treated as part of the garment itself, holding the clothing in place. It is thus not a biblical carrying concern, only a rabbinic one.

### Birit Clasp

Abaye challenges this: if the needle is there only to keep the hat in place, why should there even be a rabbinic concern? *Ve-tehavi ke-virit tehora ve-tishari* — let it be like the *birit*, which is *tehorah* and permitted. The *birit* is a clasp used to keep leggings in place. It cannot become *tamei* because it is not an ornament; as the principle holds, a vessel that serves another vessel is not considered a vessel directly used by a person and therefore is not susceptible to *tum'ah*. The implication, as Tosafot understands Abaye's argument, is that since the *birit*'s *toharah* status demonstrates it is not an ornament, we are not afraid a woman will remove it to show her friend — we fear only that a woman will show off an attractive piece of jewelry. A functional clasp, or similarly a hairpin keeping a hat in place, does not raise this concern.

Rashi reads the *birit* case somewhat differently: by both the *birit* and the hairpin, there is a *tzniut* consideration that would prevent a woman from removing them in public. Undoing a legging clasp would not be *tzniut*, and removing a hairpin would expose hair, which is equally immodest. Either way, the *Gemara* agrees: if the needle serves only as a functional fastener like the *birit*, there should be no rabbinic prohibition.

### Dual Function

The *Gemara* responds by citing Rav Adda of Narshaa, who explained to Rav Yosef that the needle described here is used on weekdays to split the hair — as a sort of comb or parting device. The *Gemara* then points out: *ve-tana be-Shabbat le-mai chazya?* — very well, but what is the woman doing wearing it on *Shabbat*? If it is fundamentally a comb, what justifies wearing it even as a rabbinic matter? If she were to carry a comb outdoors it would be straightforward carrying, so what makes it only a rabbinic issue?

### Ornamental Pin

Rava resolves this: *tas shel zahav yesh al rosha* — it has a gold plate at one end. On weekdays she carries it in her pocket and uses it to part her hair. On *Shabbat*, *menihata ke-neged padahta* — she attaches it to her hat and wears it as a kind of forehead ornament. It thus functions as jewelry on *Shabbat*. The best way to understand this, perhaps, is by analogy to decorative combs — those made of fine materials like ivory or porcelain — which can be worn in the hair as ornaments while also functioning as combs. The rabbinic concern, then, is the familiar one: since it is ornamental, she may come to remove it to show a friend. At that point, the immodesty concern arises as well, since pulling it out may involve revealing her hair.

This then concludes the opening section of the *daf*, having clarified the list of items that carry a rabbinic but not biblical prohibition on *Shabbat*. The central distinction established by the *Gemara* — that the definitions of jewelry for *Shabbat* are far narrower than the vessel categories governing *tum'ah* — provides the framework for the analysis that follows.

## Nailed Sandal Events

### Cave Incident

The next *mishnah* introduces the prohibition of *lo yetze ha'ish be-sandal ha-mesumar* — a man may not go out with a nailed sandal on *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* asks: *sandal ha-mesumar mai ta'ama?* — what is the reason for the prohibition? Shmuel answers: *shulfei ha-gezeirah hayu* — it was connected to a persecution decree, when people tried to continue observing *mitzvot* while in hiding.

The story is as follows. A group of Jews concealed themselves in a cave. They established a rule: anyone may enter the cave, but no one may leave. The logic was that entering safely could be done by checking whether one was followed, but exiting a concealed entrance exposed both the person and the hiding place. During this period, *nehepach sandalo shel echad meihem* — one of them accidentally put his sandal on backwards. The resulting footprint looked as though someone had walked out of the cave rather than in. Seeing the print, they thought one of their number had left the cave and that the enemy now knew their location. In the ensuing panic, *dachaku zeh ba-zeh ve-hargu zeh et zeh* — they pressed against one another and killed each other, more than the enemies themselves had ever killed among them.

### Alternate Account

Rabbi Ilai offers a second version of the cave incident. He agrees that people were hiding in a cave — *be-me'ara hayu yoshvin* — but says the cause of the panic was different: *ve-shamu kol me'al gabei ha-me'ara* — they heard a sound coming from above the cave. Thinking the enemy had arrived — *kasvorin hayu she-ba'u aleihem oyvim* — they again pressed against one another and killed each other, more than the enemies did. In this version, the sandal itself does not cause the panic. Tosafot suggests that the sound heard above the cave was specifically the sound of a nailed sandal — *kol shel sandal ha-mesumar* — which would explain why the sandal remains central to the narrative. The later *Gemara* makes clear that the nails were not exposed as cleats — they were used to join the layers of the sole — so the causal link to the deaths was not direct physical harm but rather the terror that the sound or sight of the sandal provoked, ultimately causing the panic.

### Synagogue Incident

Rami bar Yechezkel offers a third version: *be-veis ha-knesses hayu yoshvin* — they were hiding in a synagogue. They heard a sound from behind the synagogue, again presumably the sound of nailed sandals approaching. Believing the enemy was upon them, the same tragedy unfolded. At that moment, they declared: *al yeitzei adam be-sandal ha-mesumar* — no person should go out with a nailed sandal.

### Why Shabbat

The *Gemara* immediately raises the obvious question: *ei hachi*, if that is the reason, *be-chol nami lissar* — why not forbid the nailed sandal on weekdays as well? The *Gemara* answers: *ma'aseh she-hayah be-Shabbos hayah* — the incident happened on *Shabbat*. A parallel is instructive: Rav Oshaya was once asked whether recorded music is permitted during the Three Weeks. He answered that it is *assur* — and when pressed about the distinction from live music, he said, look, in his view it is problematic the entire year, but people do not listen to him the rest of the year. On *Shabbat*, when a vast array of objects and activities are already restricted and a long list of garments cannot be worn, adding this prohibition fits an established pattern and people will accept it.

The *Gemara* then asks: *be-yom tov lishari?* — should it at least be permitted on *Yom Tov*? The answer comes from a *mishnah* in *Beitzah*: one may send various items as gifts on *Yom Tov*, provided they could in principle be used during the rest of the week — *bein tforin bein she'einan tforin* — whether sewn or not. But *aval lo sandal ha-mesumar* — one may not send a nailed sandal. This implies the sandal has no permitted use on *Yom Tov* either.

The *Gemara* explains: *de'ika kinufia* — on *Shabbat* people gather together, and one does not want to re-enact circumstances reminiscent of the original tragedy. The same applies to *Yom Tov — beyom tov nami ika kinufia* — crowds gather then as well. But then the *Gemara* asks: what about *ta'anis tzibbur*? On *Tisha B'Av* or a communal fast, the entire community assembles — why not prohibit it then too? The *Gemara* ultimately concedes that the gatherings must be combined with the prohibition on *melachah*. The decree was issued in a context that was both a *kinufia de-issura* — a gathering under conditions of prohibited labor — and *Shabbat*. A fast day is *kinufia de-heteira*; the *melachot* are not prohibited. *Yom Tov*, which shares the essential structure of *Shabbat* in this regard, is therefore included in the prohibition.

The deeper principle seems to be that anchoring the commemoration to *Shabbat* — and by extension *Yom Tov* — embeds it within an existing framework of prohibitions that people observe and respect. A prohibition limited to a specific day of communal assembly would have no obvious framework to sustain it.

### Red Heifer Ashes

The *Gemara* then observes: *ve-afilu le-Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya*, who said *lo asru ela be-Yarden u've-sfinah ke-ma'aseh she-hayah* — even Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya would agree to extend this prohibition from *Shabbat* to *Yom Tov*.

The reference is to a precedent in which ashes of a *parah adumah* were being transported by boat, and at the end of the crossing a corpse was discovered at the bottom of the boat, rendering the ashes *tamei*. The Rabbis responded by forbidding the transport of *parah adumah* ashes over any body of water by any vessel. Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya maintained a strict literalism: one should prohibit only the exact circumstances of the original incident — a boat, specifically on the Jordan River. One should not extrapolate to other rivers or other watercraft. Note that there at least one can identify an ongoing concern: crossing water creates ongoing risk of exposure to *tum'ah*, so limiting the prohibition to the original circumstances is a real substantive position.

Yet even Rabbi Chananya ben Akavya agrees that the sandal prohibition extends from *Shabbat* to *Yom Tov*. Why is this different? *Hani mili Yarden de-shani mi-she'ar neharos* — the Jordan River is genuinely different from other rivers, in length, depth, and character, so restricting to the Jordan alone makes sense there. *Aval Yom Tov ve-Shabbos ke-hadadei ninhu* — but *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* are essentially parallel: *ein bein Yom Tov le-Shabbos ela ochel nefesh bilvad* — the only practical difference is that cooking is permitted on *Yom Tov*. So extending from one to the other is not an extrapolation to a genuinely different context; it is applying the rule to essentially identical circumstances.

## Nail Purpose Test

### Strength vs Ornament

Having established the historical basis and scope of the prohibition, the *Gemara* proceeds to delineate which sandals are actually prohibited. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel says: *lo shanu le-chazek* — the prohibition applies only to nails used to strengthen the sandal, to hold its parts together in the manner of the original nailed sandals. *Aval le-noy* — but if nails are placed purely for ornament, as decorative studs, *mutar* — they are permitted. Such a sandal does not resemble the original object of the decree.

### Five vs Seven

How many ornamental nails remain within the *le-noy* category? Rabbi Yochanan says: *chamesh be-zeh ve-chamesh be-zeh* — five on each sandal. Rabbi Chanina says: *sheva be-zeh ve-sheva be-zeh* — seven on each.

### Positioning Detail

Rabbi Yochanan explained the positioning to Shemen bar Abba: *shtayim mikan u-shtayim mikan ve-achat be-tarsiyosav* — two on the instep, two on the outer part, and one on the strap. That symmetrical arrangement reads as ornamental. Rabbi Chanina's count is *gimmel mikan u-gimmel mikan ve-achat be-tarsiyosav* — three on the instep, three on the outside, and one on the strap — seven total, still considered ornamental rather than structural.

## Permitted Exceptions

### Imbalanced Sandals

A *baraita* challenges this: *sandal ha-noteh* — an imbalanced sandal, one whose sole has worn unevenly and requires correction — *oseh lo sheva*, one may put in seven nails to restore balance. This is the position of Rabbi Noson. *Rebbi matir be-13* — Rebbi permits up to thirteen. So now we have seven and thirteen, alongside the earlier five and seven. The *Gemara* asks: according to Rabbi Chanina who said seven is permissible for an ornamental sandal, he may be following Rabbi Noson's seven. But Rabbi Yochanan, who said five, is apparently going like whom? The *Gemara* answers: *hu de-amar ke-Rav Nehorai* — he follows Rav Nehorai, who taught explicitly in a *baraita*: *heh mutar ve-zayin assur* — five is permitted, seven is forbidden.

The resolution to the apparent contradiction between five and seven in the earlier discussion and seven and thirteen here is: *noteh shani* — an imbalanced sandal is a distinct case. When additional nails are needed to correct a structural imbalance, more are permitted than would be allowed when nails are placed purely for ornament on an otherwise intact sandal. This distinction carries through to Rabbi Yochanan's position as well: when he said five, he was speaking of ornamental nails on a balanced sandal; the numbers seven and thirteen refer to the separate case of correcting an imbalanced sandal.

### Modifications

A shoemaker asked Rabbi Ammi: *tephalo mi-bifnim mahu?* — what is the law if one sewed a leather lining into the inside of the sandal, effectively enclosing the foot and transforming the sandal's character? Rabbi Ammi replied: *mutar* — permitted — though he added *ve-lo yadana mai tama*, he did not know the reason. Rav Ashi later supplied the reasoning: *keivan de-tephalo mi-bifnim, havah lei minal* — once a lining has been sewn inside, it becomes a shoe, not a sandal. *Be-sandal gazru bei rabbanan, be-minal lo gazru bei rabbanan* — the Rabbis issued their decree against the sandal, not against a shoe. Even with the same number of nails, the structural transformation changes its halachic category.

Similarly, Rabbi Abba bar Zavda asked Rabbi Abba bar Avina: *asao ke-min kalbos mai?* — what if the nails are not straight nails but hook-shaped, like staples with two prongs? The answer: *mutar* — permitted. The *gezeirah* targeted the original nailed sandal with its specific type of nail; a sandal with staple-shaped fasteners is sufficiently different in appearance from the original object of the decree to fall outside it. The difference would have been visually apparent, since the connecting bar of the staple would be visible on the surface.

An independent statement of Rabbi Yosi be-Rabbi Chanina confirms: *asao ke-min kalbos, mutar*.

### Cleated Sandals

Rav Sheshet rules: *chipo kulo be-masmarot kedei shelo tichleh ara* — if the entire base of the sandal is covered with nails so that the ground does not eat away the leather, serving as cleats, *mutar* — this is permitted. This is perhaps the most counterintuitive ruling: a sandal bristling with metal nails protruding from the bottom to grip the ground is apparently not subject to the decree, because the nails are not there to join structural layers of the sandal but to serve as traction. A *tanna'itic* source confirms this ruling. The *Gemara* elsewhere notes such a sandal raises its own *Shabbat* questions regarding interaction with the ground — potentially a *psik reisha* issue — but those questions are not addressed in this context.

### Non-Wearing Uses

The *Gemara* makes clear that the prohibition on the nailed sandal is not a carrying concern but a commemorative decree: *velo yetayel mi-beis le-beis, afilu mi-mittah le-mittah* — one should not walk with it even from one house to the next, or even from bed to bed within one's own home. It is completely off-limits for wearing.

However: *aval metaltelin oso le-chasos bo et ha-keli u-lismoch bo karei ha-mittah* — one may move it in a non-wearing capacity, such as to cover a vessel or support a bed leg. Tosafot invokes the category of *kli she-melachto le-issur* — a tool whose primary use on *Shabbat* is forbidden, like a hammer. Such a tool may be used for a permissible secondary function. Here, the forbidden function is wearing the sandal; the permitted secondary use is covering a vessel or supporting a bed.

Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon disagrees and forbids even these secondary uses. He apparently holds that since the decree was specifically commemorative, the sandal should be treated as a complete *Shabbat* taboo — worse than a standard *kli she-melachto le-issur* — and may not be moved for any purpose. The practical halacha, as we will see, does not follow his position.

## Nail Quantities

### Worn Down Nails

The *Gemara* addresses a case where most of the nails have fallen out: *nashru rov masmerosav, ve-nishtayer bo arba o chamesh, mutar* — if four or five remain, it is permitted. Rabbi Meir says seven remain is still acceptable. But there is an internal contradiction: the *baraita* first said that even if most have fallen out it is still permitted — implying that even if many remain it could be fine — and then immediately specifies only four or five.

### Size Difference

Rav Sheshet resolves this: *kan she-nigmemu, kan she-ne'ekru* — the distinction depends on whether the nails wore down gradually or were completely pulled out. If they wore down, the worn remnants are still visible and onlookers understand the sandal had more nails originally; seeing a minority of nails remaining, they understand it is a worn sandal, not a newly nailed one, and it is permitted. If nails were completely removed, people will assume the sandal was made this way from the outset and will not understand it as a remnant of the original prohibited form; therefore only four or five is acceptable.

Rav Chisda then clarifies: *arba mi-sandal katan ve-chamesh mi-sandal gadol* — four remaining nails is the threshold for a small sandal, and five for a large sandal. Rebbi permits up to seven in the worn-down case.

### Context Variation

The apparent contradiction between Rebbi saying seven in the worn-down case and thirteen in the imbalanced-sandal case is resolved by *noteh shani* — the imbalanced sandal is a separate category where more nails are needed to restore structural balance, and in that context thirteen is permitted.

### Final Halacha

The *Gemara* records: *ein halacha ke-Rabbi Elazar be-Rebbi Shimon* — the halacha does not follow Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's position that even secondary uses of the nailed sandal are forbidden. The *Gemara* notes this ruling might have seemed unnecessary, since Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon is an individual (*yachid*) arguing against a majority (*rabim*), and the presumptive rule is *yachid ve-rabim, halacha ke-rabim*. The reason the ruling needed to be stated explicitly is that one might have supposed Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is particularly compelling in this case: since the prohibition is commemorative rather than functional, perhaps the sandal should be made an even more complete taboo than a standard *kli she-melachto le-issur*. The *Gemara* rejects this: the sandal is treated no worse than a hammer — forbidden for its primary function on *Shabbat* but usable for a permitted secondary purpose.

## Babylonian Leniency

### Social Pressure

Rabbi Chiya declares: *ei lav de-karu li Bavla'ei sharei* — if not for the fear that people would call me the permitting Babylonian, *sharena bah tuva* — I would have permitted a far wider range of cases. Rabbi Chiya had come from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, and he was evidently aware of his reputation as potentially lenient in the eyes of his new peers. The remark is telling: an inclination to rule permissively in this area clearly existed, but social and institutional pressures operated as a check on expressing it.

### Regional Variation

How far would he have gone? *Be-Pumbedita amrin esrin ve-arba* — in Pumbedita they say up to twenty-four nails. *Be-Sura amrin esrin ve-tarein* — in Sura, twenty-two. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a mnemonic: *ad de-ati mi-Pumbedita le-Sura, chasar tartei* — in the time it takes to walk from Pumbedita to Sura, two of your nails will fall out.

### Gezeirah Narrowing

One might note that if sandals of the period commonly had five to seven nails, the practical scope of the *gezeirah* was already narrow on any of the opinions discussed. The progression from five to seven to thirteen to twenty-two to twenty-four represents a systematic effort to narrow the decree's application further and further.

This dynamic is precisely what this *Gemara* illustrates throughout. What began as a declaration in the immediate aftermath of a tragedy — *al yeitzei adam be-sandal ha-mesumar* — was progressively circumscribed: by excluding ornamental nails, by permitting more nails for structural correction, by allowing the sandal once it has been transformed by a lining, by permitting hook-shaped fasteners, by allowing cleated soles, by permitting secondary non-wearing uses, and by establishing thresholds low enough that most sandals fell outside the prohibition's scope. The *Gemara* never formally abrogates the *gezeirah* — a *takkanah* cannot simply be reversed without a comparable rabbinic authority — but through careful redefinition of categories, it achieves a result in which the decree has diminishingly little practical application. The difference between the Babylonian academies and the practice in Eretz Yisrael reflects the degree to which the original event retained emotional and communal resonance: in Babylon, where it had little direct resonance, the thresholds were set even higher.
