# Hullin 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meat & Tumah

### Opening Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא יָדַיִם תְּחִלּוֹת? אִין, דְּתַנְיָא: הִכְנִיס יָדָיו לְבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע – יָדָיו תְּחִלּוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָדָיו שְׁנִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are there** cases where **hands** assume **first-degree** impurity? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Yadayim* 3:1): If **one inserts his hands into a leprous house** (see Leviticus 14:33–53) **his hands** assume **first-degree** impurity as though his entire body entered the house; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say: His hands** assume **second-degree** impurity.

The *gemara* on today's daf (34a) opens in the middle of 33b, approximately twenty lines before the end of the page, at the line beginning with the word *sheniyot*. The context is the *mishnah*'s ruling that if blood does not emerge from a slaughtered animal, the meat does not become susceptible to *tumah*. The *gemara* has been puzzling over why susceptibility even matters: the meat is *chulin*, and *chulin* food cannot attain a status higher than *sheni* in any case. If a person's hands are *tamei* at the level of *sheni* — the standard rabbinic *tumah* of hands — the meat they touch would become, at most, a *shlishi*, which is a level that carries no practical consequence for *chulin*. So what is the *mishnah* worried about?

The *gemara* has already worked through several answers. The most recent resolution is that the case involves hands that have attained the status of *rishon* — a heightened state possible when, for example, one entered a house afflicted with *tzara'at*. In that scenario, the hands as *rishon* could render touched meat a *sheni*, which is meaningful. The *gemara* now takes up a further question.

### Hands as Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, בּוֹ בְּיוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״וְכׇל כְּלִי חֶרֶשׂ וְגוֹ׳ יִטְמָא״, ״טָמֵא״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״יִטְמָא״ – לְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים, לִימֵּד עַל כִּכָּר שֵׁנִי שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בְּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Sota* 27b): **On that day,** when Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya was appointed *Nasi* in Yavne, **Rabbi Akiva taught: "And every earthenware vessel** into which any of them falls, whatever is in **it shall be impure,** and you shall break it" (Leviticus 11:33). With regard to the item rendered impure in the vessel, **it is not stated:** It **is impure [*tameh*]; rather,** the term **"it shall be impure [*yitma*]"** is stated, indicating that the item has the capacity **to transmit impurity to other** items. **This** verse **teaches about a loaf** with **second-degree** impurity that had been rendered impure in the airspace of an earthenware vessel in which there was a creeping animal, **that** through contact **it renders non-sacred** food impure with **third-degree** ritual impurity.

Even granting that the hands were a *rishon* and could make the meat a *sheni*, the *gemara* presses on: and even if the hands were only a *sheni*, is there not yet another answer? The *gemara* proposes that the *mishnah* could be teaching us the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *chulin* itself can become a *shlishi* — in apparent violation of the standard assumption that only *terumah* and *kodshim* can reach that level.

### Rabbi Akiva & Vessels

The *gemara* grounds Rabbi Akiva's position in the *mishnah* of *bo bayom* — the famous day on which Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah was appointed *nasi*, when Rabbi Akiva expounded a series of teachings. One of them was a *drasha* on the verse *vechol kli cheres asher yipol mehem el tocho kol asher betokho yitma* (Vayikra 11:33): any earthenware vessel into which a creeping thing falls becomes *tamei*, as does everything inside it. The creeping thing renders the vessel a *rishon*, and the vessel in turn renders the food inside it a *sheni*. This much is standard.

Rabbi Akiva, however, focuses on the word *yitma* — "will become *tamei*" — rather than the simple form *tamei*. The future-oriented formulation, he argues, implies *letamei acherim*: it will make other things *tamei* in turn. This teaches that the *kikar* (loaf of bread) inside the vessel, itself a *sheni*, will make yet another object *tamei* — rendering it a *shlishi*. Thus Rabbi Akiva derives from the verse that a *sheni* can produce a *shlishi* in *chulin*. If the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Akiva, then *chulin* meat susceptible to *tumah* could indeed become a *shlishi*, and the *mishnah*'s concern is well founded.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּטוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲבָל בִּדְרַבָּנַן לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Perhaps this statement,** that non-sacred food becomes impure with third-degree ritual impurity, applies only **with regard to impurity by Torah law,** e.g., a creeping animal; **but with regard to** impurity by **rabbinic law,** e.g., impurity of hands, that is **not** the *halakha*.

The *gemara* immediately qualifies this solution: *dilma hani milei betumah de'oraita* — perhaps Rabbi Akiva made his ruling only with respect to biblical *tumah*, where the *sheni*-to-*shlishi* chain is fully operative. The case of *tumah* of hands, however, is a purely rabbinic enactment. We have no basis to assume that Rabbi Akiva extended his principle to rabbinic *tumah*, granting it the same capacity to propagate through levels. This distinction forecloses the solution, and the *gemara* moves to yet another answer.

## Chulin w/ Sanctity

### Chulin & Kodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הָכָא בְּחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ עָסְקִינַן, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הָאוֹכֵל אוֹכֶל רִאשׁוֹן – רִאשׁוֹן, שֵׁנִי – שֵׁנִי, שְׁלִישִׁי – שְׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Hoshaya said** a third explanation of the mishna: **Here we are dealing with** a case of **non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of sacrificial** food. **And** the mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Teharot* 2:2): **Rabbi Eliezer says: One who eats food** with **first-degree** ritual impurity assumes **first-degree** impurity. One who eats food with **second-degree** ritual impurity assumes **second-degree** impurity. One who eats food with **third-degree** impurity assumes **third-degree** impurity.

Rabbi Elazar citing Rabbi Oshaya now offers a fundamentally different framework: *hacha bechullin shena'asu al taharat hakodesh askinan* — the *mishnah* is dealing with *chulin* that was treated with the purity standards of *kodashim* (sacrificial food). The practice of elevating one's ordinary, non-sanctified food to the *tahara* requirements of *terumah* or even sacrifices was known in the period of the *beit hamikdash* and afterward. The question is whether food so treated acquires the *tumah* and *tahara* laws of the higher category — and that question, the *gemara* notes, is precisely what is debated. The answer is offered in contradistinction to the position of Rabbi Yehoshua: *udelo k'Rabbi Yehoshua*.

### Rabbi Yehoshua's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: הָאוֹכֵל אוֹכֶל רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי – שֵׁנִי, שְׁלִישִׁי – שֵׁנִי לַקֹּדֶשׁ, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי לַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Yehoshua says: One who eats food** with **first-degree or second-degree** impurity assumes **second-degree** impurity. One with second-degree impurity who comes into contact with *teruma* disqualifies it, but does not render it impure. One who eats food with **third-degree** impurity assumes **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis sacrificial** food, i.e., his contact with sacrificial food renders it impure with the capacity to transmit impurity to other sacrificial food, **but** does **not** assume **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis *teruma*,** and his contact with *teruma* does not disqualify it.

A *baraita* lays out the dispute. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the person who eats *tamei* food takes on the *tumah* level of that food: eat an *ochel rishon* and you become a *rishon*; eat a *sheni* and you become a *sheni*. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees and holds that regardless of what level you eat, you essentially become a *sheni*. The *baraita* concludes with the phrase *uvechulin shena'asu al taharat terumah* — and regarding *chulin* treated with the purity of *terumah*. The *gemara* infers from this that Rabbi Yehoshua allows *chulin* treated like *terumah* to carry the *tumah* levels of *terumah* (including *shlishi*), but *al taharat hakodesh lo* — if one treats *chulin* like sacrifices, that is too far-fetched to carry halachic weight, and the *chulin* does not acquire the *tumah* laws of *kodshim*.

The logic is that *terumah* is something *kohanim* routinely eat at home; a non-sanctified bread could realistically be confused with *terumah* bread, and therefore treating *chulin* like *terumah* is a meaningful and legally cognizable act. Treating *chulin* like sacrifices, on the other hand, is so removed from ordinary reality — no one eats sacrifices outside of Yerushalayim — that the practice lacks halachic significance.

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת תְּרוּמָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ? לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי ״בָּשָׂר״, דְּאִי בִּתְרוּמָה – בָּשָׂר מִי אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **with regard to** the case of **non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of *teruma* and** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua.** The Gemara responds: **It** should **not enter your mind** that the mishna is referring to a case of the level of purity of *teruma*, **as** the mishna **teaches** a case of the slaughter of animals and birds and consumption of their **meat.** And **if** the mishna is taught **with regard to** non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of ***teruma*, is there meat** eaten with the level of purity of *teruma*? The practice of preparing non-sacred food items on the level of purity of *teruma* is done only so that one will not treat actual *teruma* in the correct manner, and *teruma* is separated only from produce that grows in the ground.

This creates an apparent problem for the resolution offered by Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Oshaya: if the *mishnah*'s case involves *chulin* treated like *kodshim*, and Rabbi Yehoshua says that such treatment has no halachic force, then the *mishnah* must be following specifically Rabbi Eliezer (who presumably accepts both *terumah*-level and *kodshim*-level treatment as significant), not Rabbi Yehoshua.

The *gemara* then asks the obvious follow-up: *v'liokma bechullin shena'asu al taharat terumah* — why not explain the *mishnah* as treating the *chulin* like *terumah*, a position that even Rabbi Yehoshua accepts, and thereby bring the *mishnah* into accord with all opinions? The *gemara* answers: *lo salka da'atach d'katni basar* — this cannot work, because the *mishnah* is specifically discussing meat. Meat is never *terumah*. Treating meat as though it were *terumah* would be every bit as absurd as treating it like a sacrifice, since *terumah* takes the form of grain, flour, bread, oil, and the like — never meat. So the act of treating meat like *terumah* would carry no halachic significance whatsoever. *basar biterumah meika?* — when is there ever meat as *terumah*?

### Meat vs. Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי, בְּקָדָשִׁים? חַיָּה בְּקָדָשִׁים מִי אִיכָּא? בָּשָׂר בְּבָשָׂר מִיחַלַּף, בָּשָׂר בְּפֵירֵי לָא מִיחַלַּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what** is the case in the mishna? Is it a case of non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of **sacrificial** food? **Is there an undomesticated animal** that can be sacrificed as an offering and its meat is **sacrificial** food? The Gemara answers: Although undomesticated animals cannot be sacrificed as an offering, there are those who would undertake to eat their meat only when prepared on the level of purity of sacrificial food because **meat** of an undomesticated animal is sometimes **interchanged with meat** of a domesticated animal. No one would undertake to eat meat only when prepared on the level of purity of *teruma*, because **meat** would **not** be **interchanged with produce.**

The *gemara* then entertains the possibility that treating the meat like *kodshim* is equally absurd, citing the *mishnah*'s mention of *chaya* (non-domestic animals such as deer), which are never sacrificed. The *gemara* resolves this by noting degrees of absurdity: *basar b'basar michallef* — one kind of meat can be confused with another kind of meat. It is therefore meaningful for a person to treat all meat as though it were sacrificial meat, and that act of treating carries halachic significance. By contrast, *basar b'peiri lo michallef* — no one confuses meat with the grain and produce that constitute *terumah*. Treating meat like *terumah* remains entirely without significance.

The upshot is that the *mishnah* is dealing with meat treated like sacrifices, which can accordingly attain the *tumah* levels of *kodshim* — including *shlishi* — but this follows only the opinion that rejects Rabbi Yehoshua's restriction (or, as the *gemara* will clarify, perhaps even Rabbi Yehoshua agrees in this case).

### Ulla's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: חַבְרַיָּא אָמְרִין בְּחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא חוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת קֹדֶשׁ דַּחֲמִירִי דְּאִית בְּהוּ שְׁלִישִׁי, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי אִית בְּהוּ שְׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** My **colleagues say** that the mishna is referring to the case **of non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of sacrificial** food, **and** the mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** who says: Non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of *teruma* assume third-degree impurity, but non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of sacrificial food do not assume third-degree impurity.
>
> Ulla continues: **And I say** that the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** and when he said that non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of *teruma* assume third-degree impurity, he **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to say that in the case of **non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of sacrificial** food, **which is stringent, that they have** the capacity of assuming **third-degree** impurity. **Rather, even non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of *teruma* also have** the capacity of assuming **third-degree** impurity.

Ulla now enters with his own reading. He reports that his colleagues (*chavraya*) hold the standard reading: the *mishnah* treats *chulin* as *kodshim*, and this is not the position of Rabbi Yehoshua, who limits halachic significance to the case of *chulin* treated like *terumah*. But Ulla himself argues: *rabbi yehoshua hee* — even Rabbi Yehoshua would agree in the case of *kodshim*. His reasoning is that Rabbi Yehoshua employed a *lo mibaiya* formulation: he did not need to say that treating *chulin* like *terumah* is significant — a fortiori, treating *chulin* like the weightier *kodshim* would certainly be significant. Rabbi Yehoshua mentioned the *terumah* case precisely to tell us that even the lesser degree of treatment carries halachic weight; one need not infer that the greater case (*kodshim*) is excluded.

The debate, then, is whether Rabbi Yehoshua's silence about *kodshim* means he rejects it as too far-fetched, or whether it means the *kodshim* case is so obviously valid that it required no explicit statement.

## Eating Tamei Food

### Channah's Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן חַבְרַיָּא? רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי אַהְדַּרוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לַהֲדָדֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who are the colleagues** to whom Ulla referred? **It is Rabba bar bar Ḥana, as Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What did Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua reply to each other?** The differences between their opinions are twofold. First, Rabbi Eliezer holds that one who eats food with first-degree impurity assumes first-degree impurity, while according to Rabbi Yehoshua he assumes second-degree impurity. Second, Rabbi Eliezer holds that one who eats food with third-degree impurity assumes third-degree impurity, while according to Rabbi Yehoshua he assumes second-degree impurity vis-à-vis sacrificial food but not vis-à-vis *teruma*.

The *gemara* now pivots to the substance of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, asking: *man chavraya?* — who specifically are the colleagues who hold the more limited reading of Rabbi Yehoshua? The answer is Rabbah bar bar Channah, who said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, reporting what Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua said to one another in their exchange. The *mishnah* or *baraita* recorded only their conclusions; Rabbah bar bar Channah transmits the logic and give-and-take behind those conclusions.

### You Are What Eaten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מָצִינוּ אוֹכֵל חָמוּר מִן הָאוֹכֶל, דְּאִילּוּ נִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר בַּחוּץ – לֹא מְטַמֵּא, וְאִילּוּ אוֹכְלָהּ מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, וְאָנוּ הֵיאַךְ לֹא נַעֲשֶׂה אוֹכֵל כַּמַּאֲכָל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said to Rabbi Yehoshua:** The basis for my opinion that one assumes the level of impurity of that which he ate is that **we found** a case where the *halakha* of **the one who eats** a food item **is more stringent than** the *halakha* of **the food** itself. **As, the carcass of a kosher bird** on **the outside,** i.e., when one comes into contact with it, **does not impart ritual impurity, while** one who **eats** the carcass of the kosher bird **renders** his **garments impure** when the food is **in** his **throat. And we,** in light of that, **how will we not deem one who eats** the impure item to be on a level of impurity at least **like** that of **the food** that he ate?

Rabbi Eliezer's opening argument to Rabbi Yehoshua runs as follows: *matzinu ochel chamur min ha'ochel* — we find that the eater can be in a stricter status than the food eaten. The paradigmatic case is *nivlat of tahor*, the carcass of a kosher bird. This carcass does not transmit *tumah* through touch — *bachutz lo metamei* — yet if one eats it, it transmits *tumah* through the act of swallowing: *metamei begadim beivit habeli'ah*, rendering the eater's clothes *tamei* as the food descends the throat. Here, then, is a case where the eater is in a more severe position than mere contact with the food would produce. From this Rabbi Eliezer derives the general principle: just as eating is more powerful than touching in the case of the dead bird, so too when one eats food that is *tamei*, the resulting *tumah* of the eater matches, rather than descends from, the level of the food consumed. Eat a *rishon*, become a *rishon*; eat a *sheni*, become a *sheni*; eat a *shlishi*, become a *shlishi*. You are, in effect, what you eat.

### Food is Stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִנִּבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר לָא גָּמְרִינַן, דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא. אֶלָּא מָצִינוּ שֶׁהַמַּאֲכָל חָמוּר מִן הָאוֹכֵל, דְּאִילּוּ מַאֲכָל בִּכְבֵיצָה, וְאוֹכֵל עַד דְּאָכֵיל כַּחֲצִי פְרָס, וְאָנוּ הֵיאַךְ נַעֲשֶׂה אוֹכֵל כַּמַּאֲכָל?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua** responded that **we do not derive** other cases **from the** case of the **carcass of a kosher bird, because it is a novel** ruling that cannot serve as a paradigm. **Rather, we found that** the *halakha* of **food is more stringent than** the *halakha* of **the one who eats** it, **as food** becomes impure if its measure is **that** of **an egg-bulk, and** one who **eats** impure food does not become impure **until he eats half of a half-loaf. And we,** in light of that, **how will we deem one who eats** the impure item to be on a level of impurity **like** that of **the food** that he ate?

Rabbi Yehoshua rejects the proof: *minivlat of tahor lo gamrinan* — one cannot derive principles from the carcass of a kosher bird, because *de'chiddush hu* — it is an anomaly. Nowhere else in the laws of *tumah* does a source of impurity fail to transmit through touch; that the dead kosher bird does so is a unique exception from which no broader inference may be drawn.

Rabbi Yehoshua then advances his own counter-principle: *matzinu she'hama'achal chamur min ha'ochel* — the food is often stricter than the eater. The evidence: food requires only the size of an egg (*ke'beitzah*) to become *tamei*. An eater, however, does not incur *tumah* through eating until he consumes the equivalent of half a *pras* — approximately two egg-volumes, or four *kzaytim*. Since eating *tamei* food is a rabbinic enactment (with some dispute whether there is a biblical component, though *rashi* notes one view that it is biblical), the rabbis specified that the threshold for *tumah* through eating is this larger quantity. The upshot, Rabbi Yehoshua argues, is that the eater is in a *less* severe position than the food itself, and therefore the eater cannot attain the same *tumah* level as the food — the eater must descend one level relative to what was eaten.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: טוּמְאָה מִשִּׁיעוּרִין לָא גָּמְרִינַן, וְעוֹד – לִדְבָרֶיךָ שֶׁאַתָּה אוֹמֵר עַל רִאשׁוֹן שֵׁנִי – יָפֶה אַתָּה אוֹמֵר, שֵׁנִי שֵׁנִי לְמָה?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer** responded: **We do not derive** the relative stringency of ritual **impurity from** the relative size of halakhic **measures,** as measures are not indicative of stringency or leniency. **And furthermore, according to your statement, that** which **you say with regard to one who eats food** with **first-degree** ritual impurity, that he assumes **second-degree** impurity, what **you say** is **well.** But **that** which **you say** with regard to one who eats food with **second-degree** impurity, that he assumes **second-degree** impurity, **why** is that the case? It contradicts your reasoning.

Rabbi Eliezer responds: *tumah m'shiurim lo gamrinan* — one cannot draw inferences about the severity of *tumah* from questions of quantity. The required measure for food becoming *tamei* and the required measure for a person becoming *tamei* through eating reflect their respective internal logics (what constitutes "food" versus what constitutes "a meal"), not a hierarchy of strictness. Quantities are incommensurable with levels of *tumah*.

Rabbi Eliezer then presses Rabbi Yehoshua with an internal inconsistency. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that eating a *rishon* makes you a *sheni* (going down one level, as if you had touched it) — that much is coherent. But if the rule is that you always go down one level from the food you eat, why does eating a *sheni* also make you a *sheni*? Should you not go down further?

### Liquids & Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מָצִינוּ שֶׁהַשֵּׁנִי עוֹשֶׂה שֵׁנִי עַל יְדֵי מַשְׁקִין. אָמַר לוֹ: וְהָא מַשְׁקִין נָמֵי תְּחִלָּה הָווּ, דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַפּוֹסֵל בִּתְרוּמָה מְטַמֵּא מַשְׁקִין לִהְיוֹת תְּחִלָּה, חוּץ מִטְּבוּל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to** Rabbi Eliezer: **We found that** food with **second**-**degree** impurity **renders** other food impure with **second-degree** impurity **by means of liquids.** Liquids that come into contact with food with second-degree impurity render other food with which they come into contact impure with second-degree impurity.
>
> Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: But aren't** those **liquids also** impure with **first-degree** ritual impurity through contact with an item impure with second-degree impurity? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Para* 8:7): **Any** item with second-degree ritual impurity that **disqualifies *teruma* renders liquids impure** with **first-degree** ritual impurity. These liquids assume a degree of impurity greater than that of the item that rendered them impure. This rabbinic decree applies to all people and items with second-degree impurity **except** for **one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed. Liquids with which he comes into contact follow the standard course of transmitted impurity and assume third-degree impurity, one level below his own impurity.

Rabbi Yehoshua's answer invokes the principle of *mashkin* (liquids): *matzinu she'hasheni oseh sheni al yedei mashkin* — we find that a *sheni* can produce another *sheni* through the medium of liquids. The rabbis granted liquids an elevated susceptibility: when any *tamei* object — even a *sheni* — comes into contact with a liquid, the liquid becomes a *rishon*. A liquid that is a *rishon* then renders whatever food it touches a *sheni*. Consequently, if a *sheni* piece of food touches an apple that has moisture on it, the moisture becomes a *rishon* and the apple becomes a *sheni*. Thus, contact with a *sheni* can, under some circumstances, produce another *sheni* (via the liquid intermediary). Rabbi Yehoshua applies this analogically to eating: since touching a *sheni* can sometimes yield a *sheni* (when liquid is present), eating a *sheni* likewise yields a *sheni*. His rule is consistent — he treats eating exactly like touching — but accounts for the fact that in the world of *tumah*, touching a *sheni* does not always produce a lower level.

As proof that *sheni* affects liquid in this way, the *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: *kol haposel biterumah metamei mashkin lihyot techila* — anything that invalidates *terumah* (i.e., a *sheni*) renders liquid *tamei* to be a *rishon*. The one exception is the *tvul yom* — a person who has immersed in a *mikvah* that day but has not yet waited for nightfall. This person has the status of *sheni*, but it is a weakened *sheni*, essentially a residual *tumah* that will vanish at nightfall; such a *sheni* does not affect liquids in this elevated way. Every other *sheni*, however, does.

### Shlishi to Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, שְׁלִישִׁי שֵׁנִי לָמָה? אָמַר לוֹ: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא בִּתְרוּמָה, שֶׁטׇּהֳרָתָהּ טוּמְאָה הִיא אֵצֶל הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer questioned the next segment in the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua: **And furthermore,** that which you say with regard to one who eats food with **third-degree** impurity, that he assumes **second-degree** impurity vis-à-vis sacrificial food, **why** is that the *halakha*? It contradicts your reasoning.
>
> Rabbi Yehoshua **said to** Rabbi Eliezer: **I too stated** that one who eats food with third-degree impurity assumes second-degree impurity vis-à-vis sacrificial food **only with regard to** one who eats non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of ***teruma*, as its** state of **purity is impurity vis-à-vis sacrificial** food.

Rabbi Eliezer poses a further challenge to Rabbi Yehoshua: *shlishi lama sheni?* — if eating a *rishon* makes you a *sheni* and eating a *sheni* makes you a *sheni*, why does eating a *shlishi* make you a *sheni*? If anything, a *shlishi* is a lesser degree of *tumah* (and exists only in the world of *terumah* and above), so one would expect you to become a *revi'i* at most, or perhaps nothing at all in the world of *chulin*.

Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *af ani lo amarti ela biterumah* — I was only speaking about *terumah*. The *shlishi* in question is not ordinary *chulin* becoming a *shlishi*; it is food treated with the purity of *terumah* that has become a *shlishi* — the very category we have been discussing throughout.

### Kodesh Perspective


Now comes the crux of Rabbi Yehoshua's reasoning for why eating such a *shlishi* makes you a *sheni*. The principle he invokes is: *shetahartah terumah hi etzel hakodesh* — what is pure from the standpoint of *terumah* is considered *tamei* from the standpoint of *kodashim*. This reflects the hierarchical world of *tahara* in the Second Temple period. A person eating *kodashim* maintains a higher standard of purity than a person eating *terumah*, who in turn maintains a higher standard than one who merely treats his *chulin* with *tahara*. Each level of the hierarchy does not trust the level below it: the *terumah*-eater does not trust the *chulin b'tahara* person, and the *kodashim*-eater does not trust the *terumah*-eater. This was not merely theoretical — it shaped eating groups, social boundaries, and the daily interactions of *chaverim* and *amei ha'aretz* in that era.

Applying this principle: even if one is perfectly scrupulous about *terumah* and one's *chulin*-treated-as-*terumah* has attained only the *shlishi* level of *tumah*, from the vantage point of a person eating sacrifices, one is considered as a *sheni* — because *terumah*-level purity is insufficient for *kodashim*. And Rabbi Yehoshua already established that eating a *sheni* makes you a *sheni*. Therefore: you ate a *shlishi* (of food treated as *terumah*), but evaluated from the perspective of *kodashim* — the relevant perspective for the final halachic determination — you ate something treated as a *sheni*, and so you become a *sheni* vis-à-vis *kodashim*. The *baraita* makes explicit that this status as *sheni* applies with respect to *kodashim*, not with respect to *terumah* itself.

## Eating Shlishi

### Zeira's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הָאוֹכֵל שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁל חוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ – נַעֲשָׂה גּוּפוֹ שֵׁנִי לַקֹּדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **Rabbi Yannai says:** With regard to **one who eats non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of sacrificial** food, and these items had become impure with **third-degree** impurity, **his body becomes** impure with **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis sacrificial** food. He renders sacrificial food with which he comes into contact impure with third-degree impurity, and that food in turn disqualifies sacrificial food with which it comes into contact.

The *gemara* now records a chain of tradition: Rabbi Zeira said in the name of Rabbi Assi, who said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, who said in the name of Rabbi Yannai — *ha'ochel shlishi shel chulin shena'asu al taharat hakodesh* — one who eats a *shlishi* of *chulin* that was treated with the purity of *kodashim*: *na'aseh gufo sheni lakodesh* — that person's body becomes a *sheni* with respect to *kodashim*. This is the halachic conclusion: eating a *shlishi* (in this framework) results in the status of *sheni* vis-à-vis sacrifices. No fully articulated reason is given here for why eating a *shlishi* specifically produces a *sheni* in the *kodshim* context, though it follows from the framework Rabbi Yehoshua laid out — the *kodashim*-perspective transforms a *terumah*-level *shlishi* into an effective *sheni*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אַסִּי: שְׁלִישִׁי שֵׁנִי לַקֹּדֶשׁ, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי לַתְּרוּמָה, בְּחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת תְּרוּמָה. עַל טׇהֳרַת תְּרוּמָה – אִין, עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raised an objection to** the statement of **Rabbi Asi** from the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua: One who eats food with **third-degree** impurity assumes **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis sacrificial** food **but** does **not** assume **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis *teruma*.** This is stated **in** the case of **non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of *teruma*.** The Gemara infers: **Yes,** one is able to prepare items **on the** level of **purity of *teruma*,** but one is **not** able to prepare items **on the** level of **purity of sacrificial** food, as in the latter case the non-sacred food does not become impure at all.

Rabbi Zeira then challenges Rabbi Assi: the *baraita* states *shlishi sheni lakodesh ve'ein sheni leterumah* (if you eat a *shlishi*, you are a *sheni* for *kodashim* but not a *sheni* for *terumah*) — and this ruling applies specifically when the food eaten was *chulin* treated like *terumah*, *not* when it was *chulin* treated like *kodashim*. How, then, can the tradition just cited apply that same ruling to *chulin* treated with the purity of sacrifices? If anything, *chulin* treated like *kodashim* seems to be an entirely different — and arguably more absurd — category, which Rabbi Yehoshua's reasoning did not address.

### Ulla on Shlishi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. אָמוֹרָאֵי נִינְהוּ, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Asi **said to** Rabbi Zeira: That inference is incorrect, as Rabbi Yehoshua **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary.** It is not necessary to say that in the case of non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of sacrificial food, which is stringent, they have the capacity of assuming third-degree impurity. Rabbi Yehoshua teaches that non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of *teruma* also have the capacity of assuming third-degree impurity.
>
> The Gemara objects: **But didn't** Rabbi Yehoshua **say** to Rabbi Eliezer according to the explanation that Rabba bar bar Ḥana said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: **I too stated** this **only with regard to** non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of ***teruma*,** indicating that he was not referring to non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of sacrificial food. The Gemara explains: Rabba bar bar Ḥana and Rabbi Asi **are *amora'im*, and** disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** and they disagree about whether the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua was stated specifically with regard to non-sacred food items that were prepared on the level of purity of *teruma*, or whether it includes even those prepared on the level of purity of sacrificial food.

The resolution is: *amora'ei ninhu ve'aliva de'Rabbi Yochanan* — there is in fact a dispute among the *amoraim* as to how to understand Rabbi Yochanan's own reading of Rabbi Yehoshua's position. Both the tradition reported by Rabbah bar bar Channah and the tradition just cited by Rabbi Zeira go back to Rabbi Yochanan, yet they appear to conflict. In one understanding, Rabbi Yehoshua's logic applies only to *chulin* treated as *terumah* (since that is the case where eating a *shlishi* of *terumah*-level food, which is evaluated as a *sheni* from the perspective of *kodashim*, makes one a *sheni*). In another understanding, the same logic extends to *chulin* treated as *kodashim* — even there, eating the *shlishi* renders one a *sheni*, because allowing *chulin* to attain the *shlishi* level at all implies it can propagate the resulting consequences. This second position holds that even Rabbi Yehoshua agrees that treating *chulin* as *kodashim* carries full halachic weight, and that eating a *shlishi* in that context makes one a *sheni* for sacrifices — though the precise logical basis is not fully spelled out.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הָאוֹכֵל שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁל חוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת תְּרוּמָה – נִפְסַל גּוּפוֹ מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla says: One who eats non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of *teruma*** and that are impure with **third-degree** impurity, **his body is disqualified from** the right **to partake of *teruma*.**

Ulla then offers an additional teaching: *ha'ochel shlishi shel chulin shena'asu al taharat terumah* — one who eats a *shlishi* of *chulin* treated as *terumah* not only becomes a *sheni* vis-à-vis *kodashim* (as we knew), but also *nifsal gufo mil'echol biterumah* — one's body is disqualified from eating *terumah*. This is a distinct and significant point. Being a *sheni* for the purposes of *kodashim* does not mean one is a *sheni* for *terumah*; rather, eating the *shlishi* renders one a *shlishi* in the world of *terumah*, which still carries the consequence that one may not eat *terumah* (since a *shlishi* may not eat *terumah*, even though a *shlishi* does not disqualify *terumah* by touch, for *terumah* cannot become a *revi'i*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: שְׁלִישִׁי שֵׁנִי לַקֹּדֶשׁ, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי לַתְּרוּמָה, בְּחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת תְּרוּמָה. שֵׁנִי הוּא דְּלָא הָוֵי, הָא שְׁלִישִׁי הָוֵי. אִי מֵהַהִיא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לָא שֵׁנִי הָוֵי וְלָא שְׁלִישִׁי הָוֵי, וְאַיְּידֵי דְּאָמַר ״שֵׁנִי בַּקֹּדֶשׁ״ – אָמַר נָמֵי ״אֵין שֵׁנִי בִּתְרוּמָה״, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Ulla **teaching us? We** already **learn** in the mishna cited earlier (33b) from tractate *Teharot*: One who eats food with **third-degree** impurity assumes **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis sacrificial** food, **but** does **not** assume **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis *teruma*.** This is stated **in** the case of **non-sacred** food items **that were prepared on the** level of **purity of *teruma*.** The Gemara infers: It is **second-degree** impurity **that he does not assume, but third-degree** impurity **he assumes,** and he is therefore disqualified from partaking of *teruma*.
>
> It was necessary for Ulla to teach this *halakha*, **as if** it was learned **from that** mishna, **I would say** that vis-à-vis *teruma* **he assumes neither second-degree nor third-degree** impurity, as there is no reason to deem the status of one who eats like that of the food that he ate. **And** perhaps it was only **since** Rabbi Yehoshua **said** that he assumes **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis sacrificial** food that he **also said** that he does **not** assume **second-degree** impurity **vis-à-vis *teruma*.** Therefore, Ulla **teaches us** that he assumes third-degree impurity and is disqualified from partaking of *teruma*.

The *gemara* objects that this seems obvious, since the *mishnah* already implies it — if eating a *shlishi* makes you a *sheni* for *kodashim* and *not* a *sheni* for *terumah*, the natural implication is that you are a *shlishi* for *terumah*, and a *shlishi* cannot eat *terumah*. Ulla responds that without his explicit teaching, one might have thought: *lo sheni havi velo shlishi havi* — you are neither a *sheni* nor a *shlishi* for *terumah*; you are simply nothing, since there is no coherent level in the *terumah* world to which eating a *shlishi* maps. The statement in the *mishnah* that you are not a *sheni* for *terumah* might simply mean you have no status at all for *terumah*, rather than that you retain a *shlishi* status. Ulla therefore teaches explicitly that eating the *shlishi* does render you a *shlishi* for *terumah*, with the practical consequence that you are forbidden from eating *terumah*.

### Hamnuna's Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא לְעוּלָּא: הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁבַּחוּלִּין טָמֵא וּמְטַמֵּא, וְהַשֵּׁנִי פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי נֶאֱכָל בִּנְזִיד הַדֶּמַע. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ נִפְסַל גּוּפוֹ מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה, סָפֵינַן לֵיהּ מִידֵּי דְּפָסֵיל לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raised an objection to** the statement of **Ulla** from a mishna (*Teharot* 2:3): **Non-sacred** food that is impure with **first-degree** impurity is **impure and renders** *teruma* **impure** in the sense that this *teruma* disqualifies other *teruma* with which it comes into contact. Non-sacred food that is impure with **second-degree** impurity **disqualifies** *teruma*, **but** it does **not** render *teruma* **impure,** meaning that it renders the *teruma* itself impure, but not to the extent that the *teruma* can render other *teruma* impure. **And** food that is impure with **third-degree** impurity may be **eaten** by a priest **in a stew** that is **a mixture** containing spices of *teruma*.
>
> **And if you say** that by partaking of food that is impure with third-degree impurity **his body is disqualified from** the right **to partake of *teruma*,** do **we feed him a matter that invalidates his body** from the right to partake of *teruma*? According to this, when eating the stew that contains food that is impure with third-degree impurity, he is disqualified from partaking of the spices of *teruma* that the mishna permits him to add to the stew. Ulla **said to him: Leave** the matter of **stew** that is **a mixture** containing spices of *teruma*,

Rav Hamnuna poses an objection to Ulla's ruling. The *baraita* states: *harishon shebachulin tamei umitamei* — a *rishon* among *chulin* (treated as *terumah*) is *tamei* and transmits *tumah*; *vehasheni posel ve'eino metamei* — a *sheni* invalidates but does not transmit *tumah*; *vehasheni oseh shlishi* — a *sheni* makes the next item a *shlishi*. So far, so standard for a *terumah*-regime. Then the *baraita* says: *vehashlishi ne'echal b'nezid hadema* — a *shlishi* can be eaten together with a *nezid hadema*, a stew that contains a mixture of ordinary food with actual *terumah* (e.g., *terumah* spices, *terumah* onion, and *terumah* garlic mixed into a stew of *chulin*). This makes sense on its face: a *shlishi* cannot make the *terumah* ingredients into a *revi'i*, so there is no *tumah* concern about mixing them.

But if Ulla is correct that eating a *shlishi* renders your body a *shlishi* for *terumah* — meaning you may not eat *terumah* — then how can you eat the stew? The *chulin* portion of the stew is a *shlishi*, you eat it, you become a *shlishi*, and now you are forbidden from eating *terumah*. Yet you are in the middle of eating a stew that contains *terumah* ingredients. *maskinan lei midi d'pasul lei l'gufeih?* — are we seriously going to feed you something that will render you unable to eat the very *terumah* that is also in the pot?

### Solution & Proportions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַנַּח לִנְזִיד הַדֶּמַע, דְּלֵיכָּא כְּזַיִת בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to him: Leave** the matter of **stew** that is **a mixture** containing spices of *teruma*,
>
> **as there is not an olive-bulk** of *teruma* in the amount of stew that he eats **in** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread.

Ulla's resolution: *hanach l'nezid hadema d'leit k'zayit bik'dei achilat pras* — do not trouble yourself with the case of *nezid hadema*, because that ruling applies to a case where the proportion of *terumah* in the stew is so small that there is no *k'zayit* of *terumah* distributed within the quantity eaten in the time it takes to eat a *pras*. A *pras* is roughly half a loaf of bread, equivalent in volume to approximately eight or nine *kzaytim*; the ratio of *terumah* to *chulin* in the stew is less than one part in nine, so no matter how one eats through the stew, one will never consume a *k'zayit* of *terumah* within the halachically combined eating-time of a *pras*. Since the *tumah* incurred by eating the *shlishi chulin* is purely rabbinic — not biblical — and since one is never actually consuming a *k'zayit* of *terumah* in any single halachically significant eating, the rabbinic prohibition does not come into play. (A biblical *tumah*, by contrast, would entail concern even for *chatzi shiur*, a fraction of the prohibited amount; but since we are dealing here with a rabbinic enactment, the threshold of a *k'zayit* remains operative, and falling below it means no prohibition is transgressed.)

The *sugya* breaks off here, to be continued. The fundamental question — whether eating a *shlishi* of *chulin* treated as *terumah* renders one a *shlishi* with the practical consequence of being forbidden to eat *terumah*, and how that squares with the permissibility of *nezid hadema* — has been resolved by Ulla with the stipulation of sufficiently small proportions of *terumah* in the mixture, leaving the full elaboration for the following session.
