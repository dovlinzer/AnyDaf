# Sukkah 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lulav Preparation

### Atziba Storage

The daf begins at the very bottom of 44b, continuing the discussion of taking the *lulav* in the *Beis HaMikdash* and the *aravah* ritual. The *Mishnah* had taught that on *erev Shabbos*, the *lulavim* would be arranged at the *itztaba* — a kind of covered public plaza, apparently furnished with rows of benches or chairs. A *tanna* taught before Rav Nachman: *sodrin agag ha'itztaba* — they would arrange the *lulavim* on top of the *itztaba*. Rav Nachman objected: would one do this to dry them out? Placing them on the roof exposes them to the sun and they will wilt. The text is therefore emended: read *agaf ha'itztaba* — on the top of the benches or chairs, not on the exposed roof.

### Har HaBayis Structure

Rechava said in the name of Rav Yehuda: *Har HaBayis kaful hayah, stav lifnim mistav* — the *Har HaBayis* had a double *stav*, a double colonnade, rows of columns within rows of columns. This is reminiscent of the Cardo in Yerushalayim, with its double colonnade and covered walkways. It was presumably in this area that the *lulavim* were stored. With that, the *Gemara* moves on to the next *Mishnah*.

## Aravah Ritual

### Mishnah Aravah

The *Mishnah* now describes in detail the *mitzvas aravah* in the *Mikdash*. *Mitzvas aravah keitzad?* — How was the *aravah* ritual performed? There was a place below Yerushalayim called Motza. They would descend there, gather large branches of *aravah* (*murbiyot shel aravah*), bring them back, and stand them up along the sides of the *mizbe'ach* (*zokfin otan l'tzidei hamizbe'ach*). Their tops would arch over and bend above the *mizbe'ach* — a canopy of willow branches hovering over the altar. We will discuss the mechanics of this overhang shortly.

### Tekiah & Circumambulation

When the *aravah* branches were inserted in position, they would sound *tekiah teruah tekiah*. This is consistent with the broader pattern at the *Mikdash* during *Sukkos*, where *tekiah teruah tekiah* accompanied the *nisuch hamayim* as well. The *aravah* connection to water is obvious — *arvei nachal* grow by streams — and the trumpet blasts are part of the overall rejoicing and ritual surrounding this water-centered celebration.

Every day during *Sukkos*, they would then circumambulate the *mizbe'ach* once, saying *Ana Hashem hoshia na, Ana Hashem hatzlicha na* — Please God save us, please God grant us success. It is worth noting that according to one opinion cited earlier in the *Gemara*, this daily procession around the *mizbe'ach* was performed not with the *aravah* but with the entire *lulav*. And indeed, this maps directly onto contemporary practice: we perform *hakafos* with the full *lulav*, and the shaking of the *lulav* is done specifically at the *Ana Hashem hoshia na* refrain. It all coheres as a single unified ritual complex.

### Ana Hashem Recitation

The connection to *Ana Hashem hoshia na* is not accidental. *Tosafot* points to a verse in *Divrei Hayamim* — *az yeranenu atzei haya'ar*, the trees of the forest will burst into song — and they will say *hoshienu Elohei yishenu*, save us, God of our salvation. This ties the *aravah* ritual to *shirah* and joy, but also to gratitude for past salvation. The *simcha* of *Sukkos* has both a forward-looking dimension — the approaching rainy season, the prayer for rain — and a backward-looking one, giving thanks for the agricultural blessing just received. Both are present in the recitation of *Ana Hashem hoshia na*.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees about the precise text of the recitation. He holds that instead of *Ana Hashem hoshia na*, they would say *ani vahu hoshia na* — "I and *Vahu*, save us." This is a striking and cryptic formulation. The *Gemara* does not unpack it here; we will return to it shortly.

### Seven Circuits

On the seventh day — *Hoshana Rabbah* — the circumambulation was performed seven times rather than once. This is unmistakably evocative of Yericho, where Bnei Yisrael marched around the city once each day for six days and then seven times on the seventh, after which the walls fell. What exactly is being evoked by this parallel is a matter for reflection: presumably not the destruction of the *mizbe'ach*, but perhaps the breaking down of the barriers between us and God, or the theme of salvation from distress that pervades the entire *Yericho* narrative.

### Taking Leave

When they finished and were departing the *mizbe'ach*, they would recite a formal leave-taking. This is itself a striking concept — a ritual of farewell directed at the altar. What would they say? *Yofi lach mizbe'ach, yofi lach mizbe'ach* — beauty is to you, O altar, beauty is to you, O altar. Rabbi Eliezer records a different formulation: *l'Ko v'lach mizbe'ach* — to God and to you, O altar. The implications of this formulation will be addressed later by the *Gemara*, which raises the question of whether combining God with any created entity, even in a word of praise, is problematic.

### Erev Shabbos Prep

The *Mishnah* notes that what was done on a weekday was done on *Shabbos* as well — *k'maasehu bechol kach maasehu beShabbat* — except that the preparation had to be done in advance. The seven circuits and the insertion of the *aravah* branches at the *mizbe'ach* could be performed even on *Shabbos* in the *Mikdash*. But to ensure the *aravah* branches would not wilt overnight, they would gather them on Friday and place them in golden barrels — *gigiyot shel zahav* — filled with water, as Rashi explains. Gold, being chemically inert, does not rust or react with the water, making it an ideal vessel for preservation.

### Chariyos Ritual

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka adds another dimension. He records that they would bring large palm branches — *chariyot shel dekel* — and would bang or insert them into the ground beside the *mizbe'ach* (*v'chovtin otan bakarka betzidei hamizbe'ach*). That day was accordingly called *yom chibbut chariyot* — the day of the striking or insertion of the palm sticks. The word *chovet* here seems clearly to mean banging or inserting into the ground — which, as was noted in the previous session, aligns with the Rambam's understanding of *chibbut aravah* in our own practice of striking the *aravah* against the ground.

*Tosafot* raises two questions about Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's statement. First, does he mean that this *chariyot* ritual was performed only on *Hoshana Rabbah* — as the phrase *oto hayom* ("that day") suggests — or every day? Second, is he denying the basic *aravah* ritual and substituting the *chariyot* in its place, or is he saying that the *chariyot* were done in addition to the *aravah*? The text is ambiguous. It seems more plausible that he is not disputing the *aravah* ritual but rather supplementing it, since he gives a certain primacy to the *chariyot* — the day was called not *shevi'i shel aravah* but *yom chibbut chariyot*.

The same ambiguity noted earlier about whether the daily procession was performed with the *aravah* or the *lulav* is reflected here. Evidently, rituals were performed around the *mizbe'ach* involving both *aravah* and *lulav* — and the palm branches as well — all deeply connected to the theme of water. Indeed, the date palm itself requires enormous quantities of water, so all of these species evoke the prayer for rain that lies at the heart of the *Sukkos* season.

### Post-Hoshana Rabbah

The final line of the *Mishnah* records a charming detail. *Miyad*, immediately, the children would discard their *lulavim* and eat their *esrogim*. The sense is that once *Hoshana Rabbah* was completed — after the extended and elaborate service — the relief of being done prompted children to cast down their *lulavim* and eat their *esrogim*.

Rashi, however, reads the passage differently. He understands *miyad tinokos* as "from the hands of children" — meaning the adults would take the *lulavim* out of the children's hands and eat the *esrogim* from them. Rashi's final comment on the *Mishnah* is revealing: *v'ein bo davar mishum gezel v'lo mishum darchei shalom — shekach nahagu machmas simcha*. It was not considered theft or even a violation of *darchei shalom* because that was simply the custom — all done *machmas simcha*, on account of joy.

This *machmas simcha* framing opens a broader theme. It connects to the idea of *simchas Yom Tov* and ultimately to the origins of *Simchas Torah*. Where does the extraordinary joy that marks the culmination of *Sukkos* come from? The *Maharsha* and the *Ramah* both speak of things done *machmas simcha* — including even the suspension of biblical prohibitions in certain contexts, such as cross-dressing during *Purim*, understood by the *Ramah* to be permissible when performed as part of the communal celebration. Similarly, acts during *Simchas Torah* that might otherwise constitute damage to property are often covered by a presumed *mechilah* rooted in the shared festive joy. Rashi is already embedding that sensibility into the *Mishnah*'s closing image.

## Ani Vahu Meaning

### Gematria Analysis

The phrase *ani vahu hoshia na* — Rabbi Yehuda's alternative recitation during the circumambulation — demands unpacking. The *Gemara* itself does not explain it, but Rashi steps in with several approaches. The first approach is *gematria*. Consider the phrase *Ana Hashem* — the standard opening of the recitation. The divine name — *Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei* — has a *gematria* of 26: 10 plus 5 plus 6 plus 5. Now take *Ana*: the *alef* equals 1, the *nun* equals 50, the *alef* equals 1 — total 52. Add the 26 of the divine name and you get a combined value. But more directly: *ani* — *alef-nun-yud*, 1 plus 50 plus 10 equals 61 — and *vahu* — *vav-alef-vav-heh* — has its own numerical value, and together they match the *gematria* of *Ana Hashem*. The point Rashi is demonstrating is that *ani vahu* is numerically equivalent to *ana Hashem*, and therefore serves as a kind of cryptic encoded form of the same appeal.

Rashi also cites a separate approach based on three *pesukim* in *Parashas Beshalach* — the verses beginning *vayisa malach Elokim*, each of which contains exactly 72 letters. By combining letters from these three verses in various sequences, one can derive the 72 three-letter divine names. *Ani* and *vahu* are two of those names.

### Kabbalistic Exile

*Tosafot* accepts Rashi's *gematria* and the derivation from the 72 names, but presses further: *adayin tzarich ta'am la-zeh lamma nishtanu shenei shemos halalu* — we still need to understand why specifically these two names were chosen. If there are other three-letter combinations derivable from the divine names, why *ani vahu* in particular?

*Tosafot* answers with a deeply striking *kabbalistic* interpretation drawn from *Eichah Rabbati*. The verse in Yechezkel reads *v'Ani b'soch hagolah* — "I am in the midst of the exile." The verse in Yirmiyah states *v'hu asur bazikim* — "He is bound in chains." *Ki-vyachol* — as it were — God Himself is trapped in exile together with Israel. *V'hainu hoshana she-yoshia es atzmo*: what we are saying when we cry out *hoshana* is: save yourself. *Ani* — I, *Bnei Yisrael*, as in *v'Ani b'soch hagolah* — and *vahu* — He, God, as in *v'hu asur bazikim* — save us both. Our fate and God's fate are bound together, and the cry for salvation is simultaneously a cry on behalf of Israel and on behalf of God's own redemption from exile.

This idea is not entirely foreign to our liturgy. At the end of the *Hoshanos* there appears the phrase *v'hotzeisi eschem*, which can be read not only as "I will take you out" but as "I will go out with you" — *v'hotzeisi it'chem* — God departing from exile together with Israel. The same theology underlies the reading of *v'Ani b'soch hagolah* as a description of God's own exile.

One might ask: why would this recitation have been used in the *Mikdash* itself, when the *Beis HaMikdash* was standing and Israel was not in exile? The difficulty is noted and left without a fully satisfying resolution. But the theme clearly pervades the *Hoshana Rabbah* liturgy as we practice it today, post-*churban*.

### Pronunciation Debate

Some authorities are troubled by the theological boldness of this *kabbalistic* interpretation and have accordingly adjusted the pronunciation. The question is whether one says *ani vehu* — with *hu* clearly meaning "He," God — or *ani veho*, where *veho* lacks an obvious meaning and therefore avoids the implication of directly identifying God as a party to the cry for salvation.

Different *siddurim* reflect this debate. The Koren *siddur* prints *ani vehu*, while the Artscroll *siddur* prints *ani veho*. Artscroll's note identifies the phrase as two of the 72 divine names but declines to comment on the reason for the *veho* reading over *vehu*. That silence itself reflects the discomfort with the radical *kabbalistic* reading that *Tosafot* presents.

## Motza Etymology

### Motza Tax Exemption

A *baraita* is cited to explain the name of the village from which the *aravah* branches were gathered. *Makom Kalanya hava* — the place was actually called Kalanya. Rashi explains that *kalanya* is an Aramaic term meaning "free" or "not enslaved." Why then did the *Mishnah* call it Motza? *Aidi demipik mikarga demalka karei lei Motza* — because it was exempt from the royal tax, it was called Motza, which likewise means "exempted" or "released." Both names — *Kalanya* and *Motza* — carry the same meaning: a place freed from the burden of royal taxation.

## Aravah Dimensions

### Branch Height

The *Gemara* now turns to the mechanics of how the *aravah* branches were positioned at the *mizbe'ach*. A *baraita* teaches: the branches were numerous, long, and eleven *amot* tall, so that they would arch over and overhang the *mizbe'ach* by one *amah* — *gochachot al hamizbe'ach amah*.

### Base Placement

Mareimar said in the name of Mar Zutra: *shema mina, al yesod manchi lehu* — we can infer from this that the branches were not placed on the ground but on the base (*yesod*) of the *mizbe'ach*. The reasoning: if they had been placed on the ground, eleven *amot* would not be sufficient to overhang the top of the *mizbe'ach* by one *amah*, given the angle required.

### Altar Architecture

To understand the inference, the *Gemara* cites the *Mishnah* that describes the structure of the *mizbe'ach*: *ala ama ve-chanas ama zehu yesod* — ascending one *amah* and receding one *amah*, that is the base. *Ala chamesh ve-chanas ama zehu sovev* — ascending five *amot* and receding one *amah*, that is the *sovev*, the walkway. *Ala shalosh zehu makom hakranot* — ascending three more *amot*, that is the level of the corners. The total height of the *mizbe'ach* body comes to nine *amot*, with the corners bringing it to ten.

### Height Calculations

With this structure in mind, the geometry becomes clear. The *mizbe'ach* is nine *amot* tall to the top surface, receding by an *amah* at the base and another *amah* at the *sovev*. Rashi understands *gochachot amah* to mean that the branch must both bend over by an *amah* and be suspended an *amah* above the top — not dragging across it. For a branch starting at ground level and positioned at an angle, eleven *amot* of length would not be sufficient to reach nine *amot* of height, bend over, and still be suspended an *amah* clear of the top. The slight but real additional length required by the angle means the branch would have to be more than eleven *amot* long. But the *baraita* says eleven *amot*. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the branches must have been placed on the *yesod* — the one-*amah* base — rather than on the ground. From that elevated starting point, eleven *amot* would suffice.

This raises its own practical question: the *mizbe'ach* was paved with stone, offering no soft earth in which to insert the branches. They must therefore have been held in some kind of stand or support structure. Whether that stand was on the *yesod* or elsewhere is the precise question the *Gemara* is resolving.

## Isru Chag Verse

### Isru Chag Drasha

Rabbi Abahu asks: what is the scriptural source for the *aravah* branches arching over the *mizbe'ach*? He cites the verse *isru chag ba'avotim ad karnot hamizbe'ach* (Tehillim 118:27). This verse is ordinarily read as an instruction to bind the festival sacrifice with ropes to the horns of the altar. But Rabbi Abahu reads it differently: *isru* can mean "surround," and *avotim* can mean trees — from the resonance with *anaf etz avos*, the dense-leafed tree of the *hadassim*. Thus: surround the *mizbe'ach* on the festival with trees, up to the horns of the altar. This gives a direct scriptural basis for placing the *aravah* branches around the *mizbe'ach*. The emphasis on trees is also notable: *Tosafot* had already cited *az yeranenu atzei haya'ar* — the trees of the forest will sing — in the context of the *aravah* ritual, reinforcing the association between trees, the *mizbe'ach*, and the *simcha* of *Sukkos*.

### Altar Sacrifice

Rabbi Abahu then quotes Rabbi Elazar with a remarkable *drasha* that draws on the same verse. *Kol hanotel lulav be'igudo* — anyone who takes the *lulav* bound in its *agged*, *ve'hadas ba'avoto* — and the *hadas* in its *avos*, its dense branches (*anaf etz avos*, the source of the word *avotim*) — the Torah regards them, *ma'aleh alav hakatuv*, as if they had built a *mizbe'ach* and offered a *korban* upon it: *ke'ilu bana mizbe'ach vehikriv alav korban*, as the verse says, *isru chag ba'avotim ad karnot hamizbe'ach*.

This is a powerful statement. The connection between the *lulav* and the *korban* has appeared earlier in the tractate: the *esrog* is compared to a *korban* in terms of the requirements of *temimim* and the prohibition of *baalei mumim*; the waving of the *lulav* parallels the *tenufah* of the *shtei halechem*. Here the connection is made even more explicit — and the *Maharsha* presses it further. The verse does not merely say you offered a *korban*; it says you *built* a *mizbe'ach*. The binding together of the *lulav*, the preparation, the construction of the *agged* — all of this is like the act of constructing the altar. Only once the altar is built can the sacrifice be offered. This layered resonance is particularly meaningful in a post-*Mikdash* reality: we have no *mizbe'ach* on which to sacrifice. But the *drasha* tells us that when we bind our *lulav*, we are building one — and when we wave it, we are offering upon it.

Then follows a parallel statement transmitted by Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai (and Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon HaMachozi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan HaMakuti): *kol ha'oseh issur lachag ba'achila uveshtia* — anyone who makes a binding for the *chag* through eating and drinking — is similarly regarded as having built a *mizbe'ach* and offered a *korban*, as the verse says *isru chag ba'avotim*. Here *avotim* is read as connoting something thick and substantial — a large, well-fed animal — and *isru* means to bind or surround the holiday with festive eating. The verse thus yields a second teaching: joyous eating on *Yom Tov* is itself compared to bringing a sacrifice.

### Eating Restraint

Rashi offers two explanations of *issur lachag ba'achila uveshtia*. The first is simply eating well on *Yom Tov* in honor of *simchas Yom Tov*. The second refers to the day after *Yom Tov* — what we call *Isru Chag* — the practice of extending the joy of the festival into the following day. But neither explanation fully captures why such eating would be compared to offering a sacrifice.

The *Maharsha* proposes a third reading that is particularly elegant. The word *issur* means restraint — to hold back, to bind oneself. If one does not become a glutton on *Yom Tov*, if one holds oneself back from purely appetite-driven indulgence and eats in a religious key — to honor the *chag*, to experience *simchas hachag* — then one has truly transformed the table into an *altar*. This is the familiar concept that *shulchanam shel tzaddikim mechaperes aleihem* — our table atones for us, as the altar once did. But the *Maharsha* sharpens the metaphor: what makes a *korban* a *korban* is that you are giving something of your own to God. If you restrain yourself from taking all that sits before you and dedicate your eating to God, you are offering something of yourself. That is what makes the table into a *mizbe'ach*. The eating is not ascetic — one must eat and drink and rejoice — but the restraint within the eating is what elevates it to an act of *avodah*.

This entire cluster of *drashot* works to bring the *Mikdash* reality into the present. The theme is not merely historical: here is what they did in the *Mikdash*, now forgotten. Rather, the *Gemara* is insisting that the taking of the *lulav*, the festive meal, the binding of the *agged* — these are the altar and the sacrifice, available to us right now.

## Mishkan Connection

### Natural Direction

The *Gemara* continues with another statement by Chizkiyah in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *kol mitzvos kulan ein adam yotzei bahen ela derech gdilasan* — one does not fulfill any *mitzvah* with an item unless it is used in the direction in which it naturally grows. The source is the verse *atzei shittim omdim* — standing acacia wood — which was used in the *Mishkan*. The wood had to be erected in the orientation it grew, not inverted. Similarly, the *lulav* and *esrog* must be taken in the direction they grow; one is not *yotzei* if they are held upside down.

A question naturally arises: when an *esrog* grows on the tree, the *pitom* faces downward, pulled by the weight of the fruit. Yet we hold the *esrog* with the *pitom* facing up. The answer is that when the fruit first begins to form, it grows upward; only later does the weight pull it down. The initial direction of growth is considered the natural orientation.

Note the association being constructed: the taking of the *lulav* and *esrog* is being connected not merely to the *Mikdash* but to the *Mishkan* itself. We are not talking about some derivative post-biblical custom; we are placing this *mitzvah* in continuity with the original construction of God's dwelling place.

### Eternal Standing

A second and third interpretation of *atzei shittim omdim* are offered. *Davar acher*: *omdim* — they stand in the sense that they support their gold coating, holding it firm. Another *davar acher*, which is a celebrated teaching: lest you say *avad sivram u'vitel sikuyam* — their hope is destroyed and their prospects are nullified, meaning: the *Beis HaMikdash* is destroyed, the *mizbe'ach* is gone, perhaps it is over forever — *talmud lomar atzei shittim omdim*: they are standing, eternally standing, *she'omdim l'olam u'l'olmei olamim*. There will be a future *Beis HaMikdash*; the hope is not lost. Perhaps the *Mishkan* itself, in some metaphysical sense, continues to stand even now.

The resonance with everything preceding it is unmistakable. Here we are, post-*churban*, and the *Gemara* has told us: take your *lulav* and you have built the altar; restrain your eating and you have offered a sacrifice; hold the *lulav* in the direction it grows and you are participating in the *Mishkan*. And the wood of the *Mishkan* itself stands eternally. The entire passage constitutes a sustained meditation on how *Sukkos* practice in the post-*Mikdash* world preserves, reimagines, and perpetuates the *Mikdash* reality.

## Righteous Individuals

### Merit Statements

The *Gemara* now introduces a series of statements by Chizkiyah in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — the same chain of transmission used for the preceding teachings. *Yachol ani liftur es kol ha'olam kulo min hadin miyom shenivresi v'ad atah* — I am capable of exempting the entire world from divine judgment from the day I was created until now. My merits are sufficient to cover the accumulated transgressions of all humanity throughout my lifetime. *V'ilmalei Elazar bni imi* — and if my son Elazar were with me, we could extend that exemption from the day the world was created until now. And *v'ilmalei Yotham ben Uzziah imanu* — if the righteous king Yotham ben Uzziah joined us, the exemption would extend from the day the world was created until its very end. It is not entirely clear what Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai intends to demonstrate with this statement; the *Gemara* allows it to stand without extended commentary.

Similarly: *ra'isi bnei aliyah v'hein mu'atin* — I have seen those of elevated stature, the truly righteous, and they are few. *Im elef hein, ani u'vni meihen* — if there are a thousand such people in the world, I and my son are among them. *Im me'ah hein, ani u'vni meihen* — if there are only a hundred, we are in that number. *V'im shnayim hein, ani u'vni hein* — and if there are only two, it is I and my son.

### 18,000 Righteous

The *Gemara* presses back: is the number really so small? Could there truly be only two? Rava cites the verse in Yechezkel — *saviv shmonah asar elef* — eighteen thousand — and reads it as the number of those constituting God's innermost circle: the row of those who stand before the *Kadosh Baruch Hu*, the eighteen thousand who are in God's presence. Surely the number is eighteen thousand, not two or a hundred or a thousand.

### Lit Lens Vision

The *Gemara* continues to press: even among those who see through a lit lens — *aspaklaria hameira* — is it really so few? Could there truly be only two who see with complete clarity? The *Maharsha* addresses the implicit comparison to Moshe Rabbeinu, of whom the *Gemara* elsewhere says that he alone saw through a *lit* lens while the prophets saw through a dim one. He argues that the distinction between Moshe and the prophets applies specifically to *prophecy*, but in the domain of wisdom and *Torah* scholarship, sages can attain levels comparable even to Moshe. That is a bold claim — but it is the *Maharsha*'s reading of the *Gemara*'s implicit logic here.

### Lamed-Vavniks

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between "only two" and "eighteen thousand" by introducing Abaye's statement: *lo pachat alma mitlatim v'shita tzadikei d'mikablei apei shechina b'chol yoma* — the world has no fewer than 36 righteous people who receive the face of the *Shechinah* every single day. This is the classical foundation of the concept of the *lamed-vavniks* — the 36 hidden righteous ones. The proof: the verse says *ashrei kol chochei lo* — and *lo*, whose *gematria* is 36, is the key word. *Tlatim v'shita havei* — it comes to 36.

The resolution of the apparent contradiction between "two" and "36" is then offered: *lo kashya — ha d'aily b'bar, ha d'aily b'lo bar*. Those who enter *b'bar* — with permission, by formal admission into God's innermost chamber — are indeed only two. Those who are part of the 36 righteous who greet the *Shechinah* daily enter without needing special permission; they are always there. Alternatively: *bar* means "son" in Aramaic. Those who enter together with their sons — father-and-son teams of supreme righteousness — that is only two: Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and his son Elazar. The 36 are righteous individuals, but not necessarily accompanied by righteous sons.

## Leave-Taking

### Praising Altar

Returning to the *Mishnah*'s description of the leave-taking from the *mizbe'ach*, the *Gemara* raises a pointed challenge. Rabbi Eliezer's formulation — *l'Ko v'lach mizbe'ach* — "to God and to you, O altar" — appears to combine God's name with another entity. And a *baraita* explicitly states: *kol hamishtateif sheim shamayim v'davar acher ne'ekar min ha'olam* — anyone who combines the divine name with something else is uprooted from the world. The principle is derived from the verse *bilti laHashem levado* — to God alone, exclusively, nothing else combined. The prohibition applies to oaths, praise, and declarations: equating any created thing with God is heresy.

How then could Rabbi Eliezer's formulation be permitted? The *Gemara* answers by re-parsing the phrase. *L'Ko v'lach mizbe'ach* does not mean "to God and also to you, O altar" as a combined statement. Rather, it is an elliptical construction with two separate clauses: *l'ka anu modim* — to God we give thanks — and then a separate implicit verb — *v'lach at hamizbe'ach anu m'kalsin* — and to you, O altar, we give praise. The two praises are rendered unto two distinct addressees, not combined into one utterance equating them.

One might still wonder: why would one praise the altar at all? Rashi explains that *modim* here means gratitude — we are grateful for the existence of the altar, which provides atonement for our sins. But as was noted, this still involves a kind of direct address to an inanimate object, treating the *mizbe'ach* as a being capable of receiving thanks. This anthropomorphizing of the altar, alongside the *ani vahu* formulation discussed earlier, reflects a dimension of the *Sukkos* liturgy and ritual that pushes against conventional theological categories. These are challenging, powerful ideas — and the *Gemara* handles them with care but does not dissolve their force.

## Palm Tree Symbol

### Lulav vs Aravah

Rav Huna asks: what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, who emphasized the *chariyot shel dekel* — palm branches — rather than (or in addition to) the *aravos*? The verse says *kapot* in the context of the four species — *kefes*, dual form — and the *chariyot* custom was built around the palm. What is the *derash*?

The verse in *Vayikra* says *kapot temarim*, and the plural form of *kapot* suggested to some that there are two distinct *lulav*-related acts in the *Mikdash*: *achas lulav ve'achas mizbe'ach* — one the general *mitzvah* of taking the *lulav*, and one a special ritual around the *mizbe'ach*. It is this second act that Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka is describing, built around the palm. The *Rabbanan*, however, read *kapat* as written deficiently — without the *vav* — meaning only one act, not two.

### One Heart Teaching

Levi offers a completely different approach, bypassing the *derash* of the verse altogether. *Kitamar*, he says — like the palm tree. *Mah tamar zeh ein lo ela lev echad* — just as the palm tree has only one heart. Rashi explains that the marrow and internal structure of the palm tree is centralized, like a human heart, rather than distributed across branches as in other trees. *Af Yisrael ein lahem ela lev echad la'avihem shebashamayim* — so too Israel has only one heart directed toward their Father in Heaven.

The palm tree grows with a singular, straight, undivided thrust — no branching, no spreading, just one continuous upward column. That visual unity symbolizes a unified, undivided devotion to God. This evokes the statement of Rabbi Akiva in an earlier part of the tractate: one *lulav*, one *esrog*, one *hadas*, one *aravah* — all expressing the singular fidelity of Israel to God. When the *chariyot shel dekel* are brought to the *mizbe'ach*, they embody this message: we have one heart, and it is turned entirely toward You.

*Af Yisrael ein lahem ela lev echad la'avihem shebashamayim.* Israel has only one heart to their Father in Heaven. That is the final word of this section of the *daf* — a fitting culmination to a passage that has moved from the mechanics of willow branches and altar dimensions to the deepest expressions of Israel's relationship with God.
