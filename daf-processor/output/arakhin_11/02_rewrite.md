# Arakhin 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Temple Vessels

### Cymbals & Mortar

The Gemara on 10b, roughly twelve lines from the bottom of the page, opens with *elu shenei kelim* — these two vessels. The discussion concerns two instruments that were carried over from the First Temple: the cymbals and the mortar and pestle used to grind the aromatic spices. The previous lines had noted that these may even have dated back to the time of Moshe Rabbeinu, but the Gemara here clarifies: it was the reed flute that dated to Moshe Rabbeinu, while the cymbals and the mortar are identified as having been preserved from the First Temple. *Nishtayru miMikdash Rishon* — they remained from the First Temple. *Venispagmu* — they were damaged. *Velo haya lahem arucha* — and they were never truly able to be repaired; every attempt to fix them made them worse.

*Ve'alehem amar David* — regarding them, David said, *nechoshet memurat* or *nechoshet maruk*, a form of beaten or refined copper. And another verse says: *bichli nechoshet mutzav, shenayim chamudot* — copper vessels *mutzav*, meaning golden-looking or gold-plated, and they are two that are desirable.

### Vessel Value Dispute

*Rav veShmuel* dispute the interpretation of *mutzav shenayim*. One holds that *kol echad ve'echad shakol kishnayim shel zahav* — each one of the two vessels was worth as much as two of its weight in gold. Though they were copper, they were so perfectly crafted as to be worth twice their weight in gold. The other holds *sheneihem shekulim ke'echad shel zahav* — the two combined were worth as one of them in gold, meaning half their weight in gold.

In any case, this is a *drash* on the *pasuk* regarding these two copper vessels. The observation that copper instruments over a thousand years old remained usable is itself noteworthy, since copper corrodes over time.

*Tani Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef taught a *baraita* in accordance with the second position, that the two together were worth as one of gold. *Tanya*, Rav Natan says *sheniyim hayu* — they were doubled, meaning there were two of each, two of the grinders and two of the cymbals. The *Shita* switches the *girsa* to read *shenayim* simply as meaning there were duplicates. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel derives this with an *al tikrei*: do not read *shenayim* but rather *sheniyim*.

### Water Tunnel Lesson

The Gemara now returns to the theme that animated the discussion on the previous daf: sometimes attempting to fix something only makes it worse. *Tani Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*: *shiloach haya mekalach mayim be'issar* — there was a water system that funneled water into Yerushalayim, and the water poured through an opening the size of a small coin, *issar*. *Tziva hamelech vehirchivuhu* — the king ordered the opening widened, *kedai she-yirbu meimeiha*, so that more water would flow. *Ve'nistamu* — but the reverse occurred; less water came out, perhaps because widening the passage reduced the water pressure. *Ve'chazru lechomuto* — they restored it to its original dimensions, and the water flowed as it had before.

*Lekayem mah shene'emar*: the conclusion drawn returns to the verse cited yesterday: *al yithalel chacham b'chochmato ve'al yithalel gibor bigvurato* — let not the wise man glory in his wisdom, nor the mighty man in his strength. The end of that *pasuk*, *ki im b'zot yithalel hamithalel haskel v'yadoa oti*, points to knowing God as the primary purpose of life, not worldly achievement. But the immediate moral here has a more practical register: if something isn't entirely broken, do not assume that your technology or ingenuity will improve it. Sometimes intervention makes things worse.

### Bells Excluded

*Vekhen Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer: hardulin lo haya b'Mikdash* — these *hardulin* were not present in the Temple. *Mai hardulin? Amar Abaye: tavla gurgadna* — Rashi identifies this as something like a bell. *Mipnei she-kolo arev* — because its sound was sweet or, according to another reading, *av*, deep and sonorous — *u'mearvev et hane'ima* — and it would interfere with the singing. The principle is that musical accompaniment should support the singing, not compete with it. Not every musical addition is beneficial; some sounds, even if rich in tone, can overwhelm the voice and undermine the *ne'ima*.

### Magrefa Instrument

The final line of 11b introduces another instrument: *amar Rabbah bar Shila amar Rav Matna amar Shmuel: magrefa hayta b'Mikdash* — there was a *magrefa* in the Temple. Normally *magrefa* denotes a shovel used for clearing ashes; Rashi takes the name literally, while Tosafot holds that the same name was applied to an instrument that merely resembled one. *Asara nekavim hayu bah* — it had ten holes. *Ve'chol echad ve'echad motzi asara minei zemer* — each hole produced ten different musical sounds. *Nimtzet kula motzia me'ah minei zemer* — the instrument in total produced a hundred types of sound. Rashi describes it as something like a square frame with ten holes, each connected to a tube resembling a flute with additional holes — not unlike an organ in structure. There was also a handle that extended from it, which may have been used to pump air through the mechanism.

*Be'matnita tana*: a *baraita* teaches that the instrument was one *ama* wide and one *ama* tall, with a handle emerging from it, and ten holes — but in this version, *kol echad ve'echad motzi me'ah minei zemer* — each hole produced a hundred sounds, and *nimtzet kula motzia elef minei zemer* — a thousand sounds in total.

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak ve'simanach: matnita guzma* — the *baraita* exaggerates. This is offered as a mnemonic to distinguish between the two versions. Rashi explains that it is the *derech* of the *baraita* to exaggerate, though one might observe that exaggeration seems more characteristic of *Amoraim* than of *Tannaim* — and in that sense Rashi's remark is itself somewhat surprising.

## Who Are Musicians?

### Three Positions

The Gemara turns to the question of who actually played the musical instruments in the Temple. This discussion connects to the physical location of the *duchan* — the platform on which the Levites stood to sing. The *duchan* was situated at the border between the *Ezrat Yisrael* and the *Ezrat Kohanim*, directly in front of the *Mizbeach*, within the *Azara* proper.

Three positions exist regarding the identity of the musicians who provided the instrumental accompaniment: *de'avdei kohanim hayu sham* — they were the servants of the *kohanim*; or they were full-status Levites; or they were Israelites from genealogically distinguished families (*meyuchas* families). The Gemara attempts to correlate these positions with the underlying dispute about the primary function of the musical service: *ikar shira b'peh* — the essence of the *shira* is vocal singing — or *ikar shira b'kli* — the primary service is with instruments.

*De'man de'amar avadim hayu, kasavar ikar shira b'peh* — if the real service is vocal, then the instrumental accompaniment merely sweetens the singing, *levasumei kala hu d'avida*, and it need not be performed by a Levite; even a servant could do it. *U'man de'amar Leviyim hayu, kasavar ikar shira b'kli* — the one who holds that only Levites may play holds that the instrumental service is itself primary. Rabbi Yossi, who requires *meyuchas* Israelites, presents a puzzle: if it is about the voice, servants should suffice; if about the instruments, Levites should be required. What middle ground justifies his position?

### Circular Logic

The Gemara resolves this with a striking observation: *le'olam kasavar ikar shira b'peh* — in truth, all opinions may agree that vocal singing is primary. The debate is not about the nature of the service itself, but about what assumptions people will draw from the *duchan*. *Bema'alin miduchan l'yuchasin ul'ma'asrot kamiflagi* — the dispute concerns whether we allow inferences from the fact that someone stood on the *duchan*.

This reasoning is admittedly circular. The identity of who is permitted on the *duchan* is being shaped by the assumptions we expect people to make from seeing who stands there — and those assumptions depend in turn on who we decide is permitted.

*Man de'amar avadim hayu, kasavar ein ma'alin miduchan lo l'yuchasin ve'lo l'ma'asrot* — if we allow servants, we must hold that no one draws inferences from the *duchan* regarding lineage or the right to receive *ma'asrot*. *Man de'amar Leviyim hayu, kasavar ma'alin miduchan bein l'yuchasin bein l'ma'asrot* — if only Levites are permitted, then people do draw such inferences, and we must restrict access accordingly so that *ma'asrot* are not misdirected. *U'l'man de'amar Yisraelim hayu, kasavar ma'alin miduchan l'yuchasin, ve'lo l'ma'asrot* — the position requiring distinguished Israelites holds that people draw genealogical inferences from the *duchan* but not inferences regarding Levitical entitlement to *ma'asrot*; therefore distinguished Israelites are sufficient, and Levites are not required.

One might resolve the circularity by saying the Gemara is describing not what we prescribe people to assume, but what people naturally assume in practice — and since perception is difficult to change, we must shape reality to match prevailing perception. In that framing, the practical constraints of public inference determine the requirements for who may stand on the *duchan*.

The Gemara's conclusion is that in principle all positions may agree: the fundamental service is vocal singing, and the instrumental accompaniment could theoretically be performed by anyone. The debate is entirely downstream, about what safeguards must surround that service given social inference patterns.

## Levite Singing Role

### Song Validity

Having established that the heart of the Temple music is vocal *shira*, the Gemara now asks a more fundamental question: where in the Torah do we find any basis for *shira* accompanying the *korbanot* at all? The basic Levitical function described in the Torah is carrying the components of the *Mishkan* and guarding the surrounding encampment. Once the Temple was built and there was no more carrying, the Levites took on the roles of *sho'arim* (gatekeepers) and *meshorerim* (singers), as is clearly documented in *Divrei HaYamim* — but what is the Torah-level basis?

The Gemara opens with the *baraita*: *hashir me'akev et hakorban* — the song prevents the *korban* from being valid if not performed. This applies primarily to the fixed communal *korbanot* — the morning and afternoon *Tamid*, and the *Musaf* — and it is done during the time of the *nisuch hayayin*, the pouring of the wine libation. The principle *ein shira ela al hayayin* — song is said only over wine — will be developed shortly.

Rabbi Meir holds that without the song one is not *yotzei*; the Sages hold one is. Tosafot raises a sharp objection: since *shira* is linked to the *nisuch hayayin*, and one who omits the *nisuch* entirely is still *yotzei* the *korban*, how can Rabbi Meir say that omitting the *shira* invalidates the offering? Tosafot therefore limits the statement: without the *shira* one is not *yotzei* the *nisuch hayayin* specifically, but one is *yotzei* the *korban* itself. This interpretation is technically forced by the language, but the question is powerful.

*Mai ta'ama deRabbi Meir?* The verse states: *venatati et haLevi'im netunim leAharon ulevanav mitoch Bnei Yisrael ulechaper al Bnei Yisrael* — I will give the Levites to Aharon and his sons from among Bnei Yisrael, to atone for Bnei Yisrael. Rabbi Meir reasons: *mah kapara me'akevet* — just as the atoning act (the sprinkling of blood) is indispensable to the *korban*, *af shira me'akevet* — so too the *shira* of the Levites, which is identified here as their participation in the *kapara*, is indispensable. The Levites' singing accompanies the *korban* and is thereby part of the atonement it achieves; if it is omitted, the *kapara* is incomplete.

The Sages respond: *hahu lechid-Rabbi Eliezer* — that verse is expounded for a different purpose, as Rabbi Eliezer teaches: *mah kapara ba-yom af shira ba-yom* — just as the *korbanot* are performed by day, the *shira* must also be performed by day. This is relevant because the *nesachim* (libations) that accompany a daytime *korban* may in certain circumstances be offered at night; the verse comes to teach that even then, the *shira* is performed only when the *nesachim* are done by day. But from this verse one cannot derive that *shira* is *me'akev*; it speaks only to timing.

### Torah Proofs

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: minayin le'ikar shira min haTorah?* How do we establish the very concept of *shira* accompanying the *korban* from the Torah? From the verse: *lesharet beshem Hashem Elokav kechol echav haLevi'im ha'omdim sham lifnei Hashem* — to serve in the name of Hashem his God, like all his Levite brothers who stand there before Hashem. The verse speaks of a Levite coming to serve in the Temple. What service involves invoking God's name? *Eizehu sherut she-beshem? Hevei omer zeh shira* — it must be the singing, which contains *pesukim* in which God's name is pronounced.

The Gemara asks: perhaps *lesharet beshem* refers to *nesiat kapayim*, the Priestly Blessing? The reply: another verse states *lesharto ulevarch bishmo* — to serve Him and to bless with His name — implying that serving and blessing with His name are two distinct acts. *Mikhlal de-Birkat Kohanim lav sherut hi* — the Priestly Blessing is therefore not categorized as *sherut* in the sense of service of the *korbanot*; it is a blessing directed toward Bnei Yisrael. The *sherut* that involves God's name is the *shira* of the Levites.

*Rav Matna amar mehacha*: from the verse in the *kelala* section: *tachat asher lo avadta et Hashem Elokecha besimcha uvtuv levav* — because you did not serve Hashem your God with joy and with goodness of heart. *Eizehu avoda she-besimcha uvtuv levav? Hevei omer zo shira* — singing, which, as Rashi notes, emerges from an inner feeling of love, joy, and gladness.

The Gemara challenges this derivation: perhaps *besimcha uvtuv levav* refers to Torah study? The verse in Tehillim says: *pikudei Hashem yesharim mesamchei lev* — the statutes of God are upright, they gladden the heart. The Gemara responds: yes, Torah is called *mesamchei lev* — it brings joy to the heart — but *tuv* it is not called. Tosafot objects: Torah is called *tov* throughout — *lekach tov natati lakhem*. Tosafot therefore qualifies: Torah is not called *tuv levav*, goodness of heart — that particular formulation belongs uniquely to *shira*. It is admittedly difficult to articulate a fully substantive distinction between the joy that *shira* brings and the joy that Torah brings. One might observe that both phenomena — Torah and *shira* — occupy a singular place in the world of *Chazal* as modes of direct engagement with the divine. Torah may be understood as listening to God, while *tefilla* and *shira* represent speaking to God. The Gemara here is deliberating between these two on the basis of which better embodies *simcha* and *tuv levav*, which suggests that the joy of *shira* carries a quality of gratitude and outward-directed expression that distinguishes it from the inward intellectual joy of Torah study. That nuance, however, is difficult to extract cleanly from the technical formulation.

### Joy & Service

The Gemara continues: perhaps *besimcha uvtuv levav* refers to the bringing of *Bikkurim*? For it is written *vesamachta bechol hatov* — you shall rejoice in all the goodness. The reply: *tov ikri, tuv levav lo ikri* — *Bikkurim* is indeed called *tov*, but not *tuv levav*. The concept of *tuv levav* — a goodness emerging from deep within — is reserved for *shira*. One could suggest that *Bikkurim* evokes gratitude, which might explain why it is called *tov*, but *shira* represents a more profound inwardness of joy that the formulation *tuv levav* uniquely captures.

Having said this, the Gemara — which one might call the *Breslov daf* — raises a related question: *Amar Rav Matna: minayin leBikkurim shete'unim shira?* Now that we have established that *shira* accompanies the *korbanot*, how do we know that *Bikkurim* also require *shira*? *Atya tov tov mehacha* — a *gezeirah shavah*: since *Bikkurim* uses the word *tov* and the verse about serving God with *tuv levav* (which we have identified as *shira* of the *korbanot*) also uses *tov*, we derive that *shira* accompanies *Bikkurim* as well.

### Song Over Wine

The Gemara immediately challenges: *amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: minayin she-ein omrim shira ela al hayayin?* How do we know that *shira* is said only over wine? From the *mashal* in Shoftim, where the vine is asked to be king and replies: *hechadalti et tiroshi hamesameach Elohim va'anashim* — shall I leave my wine that brings joy to people and to God? We understand how wine gladdens people; how does it gladden God? *Mikan she-ein omrim shira ela al hayayin* — from here we learn that the singing in the Temple, which accompanies the wine libation, is how wine brings joy in the service of God. The *shira* is sung at the time of the *nisuch hayayin*, and in that sense wine and song are inseparable in the Temple service.

This principle has resonances beyond the Temple. The reason *Kiddush* is said over wine, the reason later *minhag* attached *Sheva Berachot*, *Birkat Erusin*, and *Birkat Brit Milah* to wine — even though the Gemara itself does not mandate wine for these — is the sense that wine elevates the ritual significance and adds a dimension of joy. Even where the Gemara does not require wine for these blessings, later practice associated them with wine precisely because wine carries this quality of sanctified joy.

But if *shira* requires wine, how can *Bikkurim* require *shira*? There is no wine brought with *Bikkurim*. The Gemara resolves this: *meshkachat lah ke-detanya Rabbi Yossi* — there is a way to find wine in *Bikkurim*. The verse says to bring *reshit pri ha'adama* — the first fruits — from which one might infer that only fruit is brought, not fruit juices. Yet if one brought grapes, one may press them into wine, as the extra word *tavi* allows the inclusion of classic liquids — wine and olive oil — as *Bikkurim*. Tosafot reads the *maskana* as *lechatchila*: one may bring wine as *Bikkurim*. Once *shira* accompanies *Bikkurim* when wine is present, the institution of *shira* for *Bikkurim* was established broadly. This does not mean *shira* is never said without wine in other contexts — *Hallel*, for example, is recited without wine — but it underscores wine's centrality to the formal *shira* of the Temple service.

## When Singing Required

### Chizkiyahu Story

*Chizkiya amar mehacha* — from the following verse: *ve-Chananyahu sar haLevi'im yassur bemassa ki mevin hu* — Chananyahu the officer over the Levites will *yassur* regarding the burden, for he is understanding. The Gemara reads *al tikrei yassur ela yashir* — do not read *yassur* (he will direct) but *yashir* (he will sing). The word *bemassa* — in the burden — connects to the Levitical *massa*, the carrying, which is the Torah's own term for the Levites' service. In this reading, the verse tells us that the Levites sing, and links that singing to their defined *avodah*. Tosafot notes that there are explicit verses in *Divrei HaYamim* identifying specific Levitical families as *meshorerim*; one might simply quote those. But Tosafot explains that the earlier derivations are preferred because they connect *shira* to the concept of *avoda* rooted in the Torah itself — particularly through the word *massa* — rather than merely noting historically that Levites happened to sing. That connection gives *shira* the character of an obligatory *avoda* rather than an incidental practice.

*Bavati amar Rabbi Yochanan mehacha*: from the verse *la'avod avodat avoda ve-avodat massa* — to perform the work of work and the work of carrying. *Eizo avoda she-tzricha avoda?* What work serves another work? *Hevei omer zu shira* — it is the singing that serves the *avoda* of the *korbanot*. This derives directly from a Torah verse and identifies *shira* as a service that enables and accompanies another service.

*Rabbi Yitzchak amar mehacha: se'u zimrah, u'tnu tof, kinor na'im im nevel* — lift up song, give the drum, harp and lyre. Tosafot notes that the keyword *se'u* (lift up) echoes the word *massa* and thereby connects this verse about singing to the Levites whose defining Torah-term is *massa*.

*Amar Rav Papa Rav Yitzchak amar mehacha: hemmah yis'u kolam yaronnu big'on Hashem tzahalu miyam* — they lift their voices, they sing joyously to God's greatness, the waters burst forth. Again the keyword *yis'u* (they lift) links the singing to the Levitical *massa*.

*Ve-tanna maiti lah mehacha*: the *baraita* derives it from *ki avodat hakodesh aleihem ba-katef yis'u* — the sacred work is upon them, they shall carry on the shoulder. The Gemara asks: if the verse already says *ba-katef* (on the shoulder), why add *yis'u*? *Ein yis'u ela lashon shirah* — *yis'u* is the language of song. This is then linked back to the verse *se'u zimrah u'tnu tof*. The *baraita* is essentially connecting the same dots as the preceding *Amoraic* derivations, just anchored more firmly in the Torah text.

*Chananya ben akhi Rabbi Yehoshua omer mehacha: Moshe yedabber veha-Elokim ya'anennu ve-kol* — Moshe spoke and God answered him with voice. Since Moshe was a Levite, the Gemara reads this as God informing Moshe about the Levitical responsibility of *kol* and of song. Other authorities evidently found this derivation difficult; Rav Ashi therefore brings a verse from *Divrei HaYamim* directly: *vayehi ke-echad la-mehatzetzrim ve-la-meshorerim, le-hashmi'a kol echad* — the trumpeters and the singers were as one, making one unified sound. As Tosafot notes, this is the most explicit source, but its strength lies in direct attestation rather than in connecting *shira* to the Levites' foundational *avoda* in the Torah. *Rabbi Yonatan amar mehacha: ve-lo yamutu gam hem gam attem* — and they shall not die, neither they nor you. Just as the *kohanim* (you) perform a service at the *mizbe'ach*, so too the Levites (they) perform a service at the *mizbe'ach* — and that service is singing.

## Role Boundaries

### Death for Wrong Role

The Gemara now develops the implications of the verse *ve-lo yamutu gam hem gam attem* in a striking direction. *Tanya nami hakhi: attem be-shelahem, ve-hem be-shelakhem, be-mitah* — if the *kohanim* perform Levitical duties or the Levites perform priestly duties, they are *chayav mitah*. The case of a Levite doing priestly service is understandable: the *halacha* is *zar she-shimmesh be-mitah*, that a non-*kohen* who performs the Temple service is liable. But the verse now establishes the parallel: just as they are liable for doing your job, you are liable for doing theirs.

More striking still: *hem be-shelahem* — if Levites perform Levitical duties that are not their assigned role. The Levitical functions in *Divrei HaYamim* were family-specific: certain families were *sho'arim* and others were *meshorerim*. Performing the function assigned to a different Levitical family is not simply an administrative irregularity — it carries the status of *chayav mitah*. This is derived from the verse *ve-hazar ha-karev yumat*: *mai zar?* *Ilima zar mamash* — if it means literally a non-Levite, we already know that from elsewhere. *Ela lav, zar de'osa avoda* — it must mean one who is foreign to that particular service, even if a Levite. Performing the role you were not assigned makes you a *zar* with respect to that service.

Rav Soloveitchik, in his *Shiurim Lezecher Abba Mari*, develops this point into a broader philosophical distinction between *kohanim* and *Levi'im*. A *kohen* is defined by his personal status — *kehuna* is a matter of who he is. A Levite, by contrast, is defined by his *avodah* — by the role to which he is assigned. His identity in the Temple context is constituted by his job, not the reverse. This is why a Levite who performs a different Levitical function is a *zar* relative to it: the assignment is not incidental to his Levitical identity but constitutive of it. Among the *kohanim*, by contrast, we never find a parallel prohibition against performing a priestly function outside one's specific assignment — because for *kohanim*, all priestly functions flow from a unified personal status.

### Crossing Roles Story

*Maaseh be-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya she-halach le-sayei be-agafat daltot* — there was an episode involving Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya, who was a Levite and a singer, and who went to assist with the closing of the Temple gates. *Matza'o Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada encountered him. *Amar lo: beni, chazor le-achorecha, she-atah min ha-meshorerim, va-ani min ha-sho'arim* — my son, go back; you are among the singers, and I am among the gatekeepers. One must not perform the role belonging to the other.

## Role Boundaries…

### Assisting Debate

The Gemara draws on this episode to address the question of whether *assisting* in another Levitical role triggers the same concern as performing it. The *baraita* rules: *meshorer she-shiyeir, ve-sho'er she-shorer, einam be-mitah ela be-lav* — a singer who performs gating duty, or a gatekeeper who performs singing duty, is not *chayav mitah* but transgresses a prohibition. Abaye rules more stringently: *naktinan, meshoreir she-shiyeir beshel chaveiro, be-mitah* — our ruling is that a singer who performs a gatekeeper's duty is in fact liable, like a non-Levite doing Levitical service.

The Gemara notes that this is a dispute of Tannaim; the episode of Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada reflects this underlying disagreement. Rabbi Yehoshua may have believed that assisting was permissible; Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada corrected him because he held otherwise. *Mai lav be-ha kamiflagi: de-mar savar mitah hi ve-gazru bah rabanan* — one opinion holds the underlying transgression is *chayav mitah*, and therefore the Sages made a safeguard prohibiting even the act of assisting, lest it lead to actually performing the role. *U-mar savar lav hi, ve-lo gazru bah rabanan* — the other holds it is only a prohibition, and no safeguard against assisting was decreed.

The Gemara then suggests an alternative: *de-kulei alma lav hi* — perhaps everyone agrees it is merely a prohibition, and the debate is only whether we decree against *assisting* when the transgression is a simple *lav*, not *mitah*. One holds that the Sages did make such a decree; the other holds they did not. Importantly, the Gemara takes it as given that merely assisting is not itself the transgression — it is only a question of rabbinic safeguard. This is a qualitatively different analysis from the case of two people jointly performing a *melacha* together.

## When Singing Required…

### Free-Will Offerings

*Ba'ei Rabbi Avin*: now that we have established that *shira* accompanies *korbanot*, the question is which *korbanot* require it. The assumption is that obligatory communal *olot* — the *Tamid* and the *Musaf* — are accompanied by *shira*. Rabbi Avin asks: *olat nidvat tzibur, te'unah shirah o einah te'unah shirah?* Does a voluntary communal *olah* require *shira* or not?

The case in question is the *olat kaitz ha-mizbe'ach*: a communal fund existed from which *olot* were brought on slow days in the Temple when no *korbanot* were being offered, so as to keep the *mizbe'ach* active. This was a communal *olah* drawn from communal funds, but it was not obligatory and had no fixed time. Does *shira* accompany it?

The Gemara cites the verse *oloseichem amar Rachmana* — the Torah uses the plural, *your olot*. Does this plurality indicate *achas olas chova ve'achas olas nedava* — both the obligatory and the voluntary — or does *oloseichem* simply mean the entirety of the Jewish people's obligatory *olot*, without extending to free-will offerings?

*Ta shema*: from the episode of Chizkiyahu, *vayomer Chizkiyahu le'haalos ha'olah la-mizbe'ach u'veit heichel ha'olah heichel shir Hashem veha-chatzotzrot* — Chizkiyahu directed that the *olah* be brought to the altar, and when the offering began, so did the singing and the *chatzotzrot*. The verse implies deliberation before this decision. If this were a standard obligatory *olah*, why was there consultation? It seems he was deliberating about a non-standard case. *Ela lav de'olas nedava* — it must have been a voluntary offering, and the deliberation concluded that *shira* and *chatzotzrot* accompany even a voluntary communal *olah*.

### Offering Identity

However, the identification of Chizkiyahu's offering remains unresolved. *Amar Rav Yosef: lo, olas Rosh Chodesh hava* — it was the *Rosh Chodesh olah*, and the deliberation concerned whether *Rosh Chodesh* had been properly established that day. Abaye objects: *ve-chasiv beyom shisha asar lachodesh harishon* — the verse explicitly states it was the sixteenth of Nissan, the second day of Pesach — so it cannot have been *Rosh Chodesh*.

*Ela amar Rami brei de-Rav Yeiva: keves haba im ha'omer ka mibaya lei* — it was the lamb accompanying the *Korban Omer*, and his question was whether *Rosh Chodesh* had been established in time for the *Omer* to be brought. But since this was the sixteenth of Nissan — the second day of Pesach — one would expect the timing of *Rosh Chodesh* Nissan to have been resolved long before. The Gemara asks: *ve-leichezi Pesach heicha avi, matza heicha achal* — when did they bring the *Korban Pesach*? When did they eat *matza*? These events would have anchored the calendar unambiguously.

*Ela amar Rav Ashi: midi de-hava a-shlicha de-tzibura de-mimloch* — the deliberation was not about calendrical uncertainty but about the formal ritual procedure. Just as the *shliach tzibur* performs a ritual act of requesting permission from the community before representing them — a practice still observed on *Yom Kippur* before *Musaf* — so too Chizkiyahu performed a formal consultation before bringing the *olah* on behalf of the community.

The Gemara concludes: *hashta de-asis le-hacha, afilu teima olas chova* — now that we have this explanation, one could equally read the verse as referring to an obligatory *olah*, where the deliberation was simply the customary ritual of communal consultation before representative sacrifice. If so, the story of Chizkiyahu does not prove that *shira* accompanies a *nidvat tzibur* — and the question raised by Rabbi Avin remains unresolved, to be addressed in the continuation of the *sugya*.
