# Gittin 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Modeh BeMiktzat

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר, כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם נִשְׁבָּע עַל טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ. כֵּיצַד? ״מָנֶה לְאָבִיךְ בְּיָדִי, וְהֶאֱכַלְתִּיו פְּרָס״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע. וְזֶה הוּא שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּע עַל טַעֲנַת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yitzḥak **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Shevuot* 5:10) that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** There are **times when a person takes an oath about his own claim. How so?** One says to another: **One hundred dinars of your** deceased **father's was in my possession,** as I had borrowed that sum from him. **And I** already **paid him part [*peras*]** of it, but I still owe you fifty dinars. In this case, he is not believed unless **he takes an oath** that he repaid the half, like anyone who admits to part of a claim. **And this is** an example of a case **where** one **takes an oath about his own claim.** Although nobody has claimed anything from him, he still takes an oath on the basis of his own statement.

The discussion picks up approximately halfway through 51b, continuing the *machlokes* between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *rabanan* regarding the circumstances under which one can compel another to take a *shvuah* in the cases we have been examining — and specifically, the question of *kemeshiv aveidah*: whether a minor can force someone to take a *shvuah*.

### Three Shvuot Categories

There is an interesting *machlokes rishonim* about which *shvuot* this exemption applies to. Three distinct categories of *shvuah* are relevant to the discussion. The first is *shvuas ed echad*: where a single witness testifies that a debt is owed, the witness cannot extract the debt but his testimony compels the defendant to take a *shvuah*. The second is *shvuas hapikadon*: where a *shomer* — someone watching over property — can be compelled to swear that he did not mishandle what was entrusted to him. The third, and the one the *gemara* now addresses directly, is *shvuas modeh bemiktzas*: where a person admits to part of a claim and is consequently compelled to take a *shvuah* regarding the remainder.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלָּא בִּדְרַבָּה קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: מוֹדֶה מִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה – יִשָּׁבַע? חֲזָקָה – אֵין אָדָם מֵעִיז פָּנָיו בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל חוֹבוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** the following explanation given by **Rabba, as Rabba says: For what reason did the Torah say** that one who **admits to part of the claim** brought against him **takes an oath** with regard to the rest of the claim, which he denies, whereas one who denies the entire claim is not required to take an oath? Rabba answers: The oath of partial admission is based on a **presumption** with regard to the defendant's behavior. There is a presumption **that a person would not be so brazen** as to stand **before his creditor** and deny his debt when his creditor knows that he is lying.

The *gemara* states: *ella keRabbah kamipalgei* — this *machlokes* is actually grounded in a principle articulated by Rabbah.

### Rabbah's Chazakah

Rabbah asks: *mipnei mah amrah Torah modeh bemiktzas hata'anah yishava* — why does the Torah require someone who admits to a partial debt to take a *shvuah*? One might wonder why Rabbah needs to inquire into the rationale at all; if it is *halacha*, it is *halacha*. But the reason Rabbah's rationale matters here is that it becomes the operative principle for determining how *modeh bemiktzas* intersects with the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *rabanan*.

Rabbah's answer: *chazakah ain adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — there is a *chazakah*, a presumption, that a person will not brazenly contradict someone to his face with a bald-faced lie. Tosafot formulates this sharply: *ain adam meiz kol makom she'oso kenegdo yode'a shehu meshaker* — a person will not be so brazen as to lie to someone who knows with certainty that he is lying. This *chazakah* is, admittedly, a claim about human psychology, and one might wonder whether it applies uniformly across all people and eras. Nonetheless, since *modeh bemiktzas* is a *halacha de'oraisa*, the operative rule certainly remains in force regardless.

### Delay Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי, בְּכוּלֵּיהּ בָּעֵי לְמִכְפְּרֵיהּ לֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּלָא כַּפְרֵיהּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין אָדָם מֵעִיז פָּנָיו בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל חוֹבוֹ; וּבְכוּלֵּיהּ בָּעֵי דְּלוֹדֵי לֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּלָא אוֹדִי לֵיהּ – אִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי הוּא דְּקָא מִשְׁתְּמִיט לֵיהּ – סָבַר: עַד דְּהָווּ לִי זוּזֵי וּפָרַעְנָא לֵיהּ; וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: רְמִי שְׁבוּעָה עִילָּוֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלוֹדֵי לֵיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **And this** one who admits to part of the claim would **want to deny all of it, and** the only reason **he does not deny** all of **it is because a person would not be so brazen before his creditor. And** in fact, he would **want to admit** to **all** of the claim **to him. And** the reason **that he did not admit** the whole claim **to him** and say that in fact he owes him the entire sum is that **he was evading** his obligation temporarily. The debtor is short of money and **he thinks:** I will pay my creditor as much as I can afford now, and I will evade paying the rest **until I have** enough **money, and** then **I will repay him** the rest, to which I have not yet admitted. Therefore, **the Merciful One states: Impose an oath on** the debtor **in order** to induce him **to admit all** of the debt **to** the creditor.

The *gemara* then traces the psychological logic of the partial admission. Since *ain adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo*, an honest person ought to have admitted to the entire debt. Why, then, does he admit only to part? Because *demishta­mutei hu dekamishta­mit lei* — he is trying to delay. He knows he owes the full amount, but he lacks the funds to pay it all right now, so he admits to part, pays that immediately, and hopes to buy time until he has the rest: *ad dehavu li zuzei ufarna lei*. He intends to pay eventually, but uses the mechanism of *modeh bemiktzas* to force a delay — against the lender's will.

It is this manipulation of the system that the Torah addresses. *Ve'amar Rachmana remei shvuah aleih* — the Torah imposes a *shvuah* on him, as a way of pressuring him to admit the full amount rather than engineer a delay. The *shvuah* is designed to make the partial denial untenable.

### Eliezer's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: לָא שְׁנָא בּוֹ וְלָא שְׁנָא בִּבְנוֹ – אֵינוֹ מֵעֵיז, וְהִילְכָּךְ לָאו מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הוּא; וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: בּוֹ הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ מֵעֵיז, אֲבָל בִּבְנוֹ מֵעֵיז, וּמִדְּלֹא מֵעֵיז – מֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Following Rabba's reasoning, the difference of opinion between Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov and the Rabbis can be explained as follows: **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov holds** that **there is no difference between** the creditor **himself and** the creditor's **son,** as in all cases the debtor **would not be so brazen** as to deny his debt. **Therefore,** the debtor **is not** considered like **someone who is returning a lost item.** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov maintains that Rabba's reasoning applies in this case as well, so he is required to take an oath. **And the Rabbis hold** that **he would not be so brazen** as to deny a debt before the creditor **himself, but** toward **his** creditor's **son he would be brazen** and deny the claim completely. **And since he was not so brazen** as to deny the entire claim, but admitted to part of it, **he is** considered like **someone returning a lost item,** and therefore he is exempt from taking an oath.

Having established Rabbah's rationale, the *gemara* explains the positions of the *tanna'im*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds *lo shna bo velo shna bivno* — it makes no difference whether one is confronting the original creditor or that creditor's heir. *Eino meiz* — the debtor will not be brazen even in the presence of the son. The psychological restraint remains operative whether the opposing party is the original lender or his child, and therefore the same *shvuah* logic applies.

### Rabanan Counter

The *rabanan* disagree. In their view, *bo de'eino meiz aval livno meiz* — a person is indeed restrained from lying to the creditor himself, but he will be far more willing to stand up against the creditor's child, who was not party to the original transaction. Since the psychological impediment is diminished or absent, the very fact that the debtor admits to *part* of the claim is no longer surprising — he could easily have denied everything. His partial admission therefore resembles the conduct of a *meshiv aveidah*, someone voluntarily returning a lost object. Because the internal psychological pressure that would normally explain the partial admission is not in play, we give him greater benefit of the doubt, do not compel him to take a *shvuah*, and treat him as the honest person he appears to be.

## Apotropsin Powers

### Types of Apotropsin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יְתוֹמִין שֶׁסָּמְכוּ אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, אוֹ שֶׁמִּינָּה לָהֶן אֲבִיהֶן אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס – חַיָּיב לְעַשֵּׂר פֵּירוֹתֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **orphans who are living with a homeowner** who takes care of all their needs and affairs, even if neither their father nor the court officially appointed him to this task, **or if their father appointed a steward [*apotropos*] for them,** this person **is obligated to tithe their produce.**

With the beginning of the new *mishnah* at the top of 52a, the topic shifts entirely to *apotropsin* — appointed guardians who oversee the estates of orphans. The *mishnah* and subsequent *gemara* distinguish among three types of *apotropos*. The first is appointed by the father himself — anticipating his own death, a father may designate someone to administer the estate on behalf of his minor children. This has clear resonance for any case where a person can foresee his own death in advance; it is an interesting precedent for instruments akin to living wills and advance directives. The second type is self-appointed — someone who simply steps forward and assumes responsibility for the property. The third is appointed by *beit din*.

This immediately raises a question. The underlying issue is one of *zekhiyyah* and its relationship to ownership: who has the right to separate *terumah* and *ma'aser* from produce that belongs to someone else? The *gemara* in *Kiddushin* in the second *perek* distinguishes three different mechanisms by which land was apportioned when the Jewish people entered *Eretz Yisrael* with Yehoshua: *shelikhut* — formal agency; *zekhiyyah* — automatically doing something beneficial for another, with a *machlokes rishonim* as to whether *zekhiyyah* operates through the mechanism of *shelikhut*; and *apotropos* — a distinct category unto itself. The *apotropos* is not the owner of the produce. A *shaliach* can separate *terumah* from another's produce based on a special *drashah* from a *pasuk*, but the *pasuk atem* — "you," implying only owners — would seem to exclude an *apotropos* from this power.

One might ask: why does this matter so urgently, given that *katanim* are only obligated in *mitzvot* through *chinuch* anyway, making any *issur* at worst *derabanan*? Yet someone must manage their religious and financial lives, and that is precisely what motivates this entire institution of *apotropus* — the *yetomim* need someone to oversee their financial dealings and ensure their proper upbringing.

Regarding the underlying mechanism by which *apotropsus* functions: Rabbeinu Tam maintains that the *drashah* from *atem* is only an *asmachta*, and the principle excluding *apotropsin* from separating *terumah* is merely *derabanan*. The exception allowing the *apotropos* to separate *terumah* in the case of actively feeding the *yetomim* is itself a *derabanan* creation designed to protect them. Other *rishonim* propose different approaches. As we will see later, Tosafot Rid invokes *hefker beit din hefker* — the principle that a *beit din* can divest and reassign ownership — as the underlying mechanism that grants the *apotropos* his powers. That principle will resurface shortly.

### Tithes Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא שׁוּתָּפִין; ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא אֲרִיסִין; ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא אַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין, וְלֹא הַתּוֹרֵם אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a steward is obligated to tithe the produce of the orphans in his charge. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* that expounds a verse dealing with the *teruma* of the tithe, given by a Levite to a priest: "Thus you, also you, shall offer a gift to the Lord" (Numbers 18:28). The emphasis placed on the word "you" teaches as follows: **"You"** separate *teruma*, **but not partners,** meaning that one partner may not separate *teruma* on behalf of the other. **"You"** separate *teruma*, **but not sharecroppers. "You"** separate *teruma*, **but not stewards,** and "you" separate *teruma*, **but not one who separates *teruma* from** produce **that is not his.** How, then, can the mishna say that a steward is able to, and is even required to, tithe the produce of the orphans in his charge, when that produce does not belong to him?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לְהַאֲכִיל, כָּאן לְהַנִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** that this is **not difficult: Here** the mishna is dealing with a steward who tithes the orphans' produce in order **to feed** it to them. Since he is not permitted to feed them untithed produce, the Sages allowed him to tithe that which he gives them to eat. **There** the *baraita* is referring to produce that is not needed for the orphans' sustenance; rather, the steward wishes **to put** it **aside** in a tithed state. Since he is not the owner of the produce, he lacks the authority to tithe it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הָאַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין תּוֹרְמִין וּמְעַשְּׂרִין – לְהַאֲכִיל וְלֹא לְהַנִּיחַ. וּמוֹכְרִין לָהֶן בְּהֵמָה, עֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת, בָּתִּים, שָׂדוֹת וּכְרָמִים – לְהַאֲכִיל, אֲבָל לֹא לְהַנִּיחַ. וּמוֹכְרִין לָהֶן פֵּירוֹת, יֵינוֹת, שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת – לְהַאֲכִיל אֲבָל לֹא לְהַנִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 1:10) that this distinction is made based on whether the steward intends to feed the produce to the orphans or store it: **Stewards can separate *teruma* and tithes** from the produce of the orphans in their charge in order **to feed** the produce to them, **but not** with the intention **to put** it **aside. And** stewards **may sell** the orphans' possessions **for them** as follows: **Cattle, male and female slaves, houses, fields, and vineyards,** in order **to feed** the orphans, so that they will have something to eat immediately, **but not** with the intention **to put aside** the proceeds for future use. **And they may** also **sell produce, wine, oil, and flour for them** in order **to feed** them, **but not** with the intention **to put aside** the proceeds for a later date.

The *gemara* raises an apparent contradiction: *uvreminhu* — a *beraisa* that seems to exclude *apotropsin* from separating *terumah*. Rav Chisda resolves the tension with a distinction: *kan le-hachil, kan le-haniach* — one case concerns feeding the *yetomim* now, while the other concerns storing produce for the future. When the *apotropos* is separating *terumah* and *ma'aser* so as to actively feed the *yetomim* from the produce at hand, this is permitted. But to separate *terumah* from produce that will simply sit in storage for a decade until the orphans mature — that is not permitted, because they will be fully capable of separating it themselves when they are grown. The *gemara* confirms: *ve-hatanya apotropsin tormin u-maasrin le-hachil aval lo le-haniach* — the *beraisa* supports Rav Chisda's distinction, and Rashi notes that *ve-hatanya* here is used *be-nichuta*, as a supporting citation rather than a challenge.

### Braita's Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹשִׂין לָהֶן לוּלָב וַעֲרָבָה וְסוּכָּה וְצִיצִית, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קִצְבָה; לְאֵיתוֹיֵי שׁוֹפָר. וְלוֹקְחִין לָהֶם סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, תְּפִילִּין וּמְזוּזוֹת, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קִצְבָה; לְאֵתוֹיֵי מְגִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** stewards **make** the following items that are required for the fulfillment of a mitzva **for the** orphans, from their property: **A *lulav*, a willow branch, a *sukka*, ritual fringes, and any item** used for a mitzva **that involves a fixed** expense. The Gemara notes that the words: Any item used for a mitzva, serve **to include a *shofar*. And they may purchase a Torah scroll, phylacteries, *mezuzot*, and any** other **item** used for a mitzva **that involves a fixed** expense. The Gemara comments that these last words serve **to include a Megilla,** the Scroll of Esther, read on Purim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין פּוֹסְקִין עֲלֵיהֶם צְדָקָה, וְאֵין פּוֹדִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַשְּׁבוּיִין, וְלֹא כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קִצְבָה; לְאֵתוֹיֵי תַּנְחוּמֵי אֲבֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But** stewards **may not undertake** to give **charity on behalf of** orphans, **and they may not redeem captives on their behalf** with their property. **Nor** may they do **anything** with the orphans' property **that does not involve a fixed** expense. The Gemara explains that this last phrase serves **to include** that which is brought to **comfort mourners.** A steward may not use property belonging to the orphans in his charge for this purpose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין אַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין רַשָּׁאִין לָדוּן – לָחוּב וְלִזְכּוֹת – בְּנִכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים. לִזְכּוֹת – אַמַּאי לָא? אֶלָּא ״לָחוּב עַל מְנָת לִזְכּוֹת״ בְּנִכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And stewards are not permitted to involve** themselves **in litigation,** if the purpose is **to accept an obligation or to secure gain for the property of the orphans.** The Gemara asks: **Why** may they **not** enter into litigation **to secure gain?** The Gemara clarifies: **Rather,** this means that stewards are not permitted **to** involve themselves in litigation in which they will **accept** financial **obligation** upon the orphans' estate, even if they do so **in order to** ultimately **secure gain for the property of the orphan.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין אַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין רַשָּׁאִין לִמְכּוֹר בְּרָחוֹק וְלִגְאוֹל בְּקָרוֹב; בְּרָעָה וְלִגְאוֹל בְּיָפָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּדִלְמָא מִשְׁתַּדְּפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And stewards are not permitted to sell** a field belonging to the orphans that is located **in a distant** place **and** use the proceeds to **redeem** a field that their father had sold **in a nearby** place, although this is ordinarily considered to be a favorable exchange. Similarly, stewards are not permitted to sell **a bad** field and use the proceeds to **redeem a good** one. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? The reason is **that perhaps** the bought fields **will become blighted,** and it will turn out that the steward has caused the orphans a loss with his purchase.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין אַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין רַשָּׁאִין לִמְכּוֹר שָׂדוֹת – וְלִיקַּח עֲבָדִים, אֲבָל מוֹכְרִין עֲבָדִים – וְלוֹקְחִים בָּהֶן שָׂדוֹת. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף לֹא לִמְכּוֹר עֲבָדִים וְלִיקַּח שָׂדוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּלְמָא לָא מְשַׁפְּיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And stewards are not permitted to sell fields** belonging to the orphans **and** use the proceeds **to purchase slaves. But they may sell** the orphans' **slaves and** use **their** proceeds **to purchase fields,** as land is considered to be a more stable asset. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** Stewards **may not even sell slaves and** use the proceeds **to purchase fields.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? **Perhaps** it will turn out that the property **is not secure [*meshappeyan*],** the ownership of the fields being contested by others, and consequently the steward will have made matters more complicated for the orphans as a result of his purchase.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין אַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין רַשָּׁאִין לְהוֹצִיא עֲבָדִים לְחֵירוּת; אֲבָל מוֹכְרִין אוֹתָן לַאֲחֵרִים, וַאֲחֵרִים מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן לְחֵירוּת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, אַף הוּא – נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי עַצְמוֹ וְיוֹצֵא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמוֹכְרוֹ לוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And stewards are not permitted to free slaves** belonging to the orphans in their charge, even if it is necessary to do so for any reason. **But they may sell them to others, and** then those **others may free them. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that **even he,** the slave himself, **may give his own monetary value** to the steward **and** thereby **go** free, **due to** the fact **that it is as if** the steward **sold** the slave **to** the slave himself. It is irrelevant whether it is some other person or the slave himself who pays his purchase price.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצָרִיךְ לְחַשֵּׁב עִמָּהֶן בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. אֵין עוֹשִׂים אַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים וּקְטַנִּים, וְאִם מִינָּן אֲבִי יְתוֹמִין – הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** the steward must **calculate with** the orphans **in the end,** when they reach adulthood and he hands over the property to them. At that time, he calculates all of the expenses and income generated by their property during the period that he served as steward. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He is not required** do so, as the court relies on his integrity. The court **does not appoint women, slaves, or minors as stewards, but if the father of the orphans appointed** one of **them** as his orphans' steward, **he has permission** to do so.

The *beraisa* continues with a comprehensive list of what *apotropsin* may and may not do. They may sell animals, slaves, houses, fields, and vineyards — *le-hachil*, to actively support the *yetomim*, but not *le-haniach*, not to build up a long-term reserve. Similarly, they may sell fruits, wine, oil, and flour for immediate support but not for storage. The language *u-mochrin lahem* encompasses both selling on behalf of the *yetomim* and purchasing for them — the *apotropos* manages active financial support in either direction.

The *beraisa* proceeds to enumerate *mitzvah* objects that may be purchased: the *apotropos* may make for the orphans a *lulav*, *arava* — notable since *arava* on its own is a *minhag* rather than a biblical *mitzvah*, though the *Rif*'s version of the text omits *arava* — a *sukkah*, and *tzitzit*. The *beraisa* then adds a general category: *kol davar sheyesh lo kitzvah* — anything with a fixed and defined cost; the *gemara* notes this includes *shofar* and, separately, *megillah*. Further, the *apotropos* may purchase for the orphans a *Sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot*. Regarding a *Sefer Torah* in particular: there is a *mitzvah* for every Jewish man to write one, and beyond the ritual dimension, in antiquity a *Sefer Torah* was the means of Torah study itself — effectively, purchasing a *Sefer Torah* amounted to providing an education. The Maharam Shiff examines each item in this list individually, treating each as a *chiddush* in its own right.

### Court & Property Limits

Corresponding to these permissions, the *beraisa* specifies what *apotropsin* may not do: *ein poskin aleihen tzedakah* — they may not donate *tzedakah* from the orphans' inheritance; *ein podin aleihen et hashvuyim* — they may not use orphan funds to redeem captives; and *lo kol davar she'ein lo kitzvah* — nothing in the category of indefinite, open-ended expense. The paradigm case is *tanchumei aveilim* — bringing food to a house of mourning — since communal and charitable claims of this nature could arise indefinitely and drain the estate without limit. The governing principle is that any expenditure must be capped and predictable; anything unlimited falls outside the *apotropos*'s authority.

This typology raises an interesting question: does it apply uniformly to all types of *apotropos*, whether father-appointed, self-appointed, or *beit din*-appointed? The *mishnah* will address that shortly.

The *beraisa* further states: *ein apotropsin resha'im ladun lachov velizkos benichsei yetomim* — *apotropsin* may not litigate, whether the outcome would be adverse or favorable, with regard to orphan property. The *gemara* asks: if it is an entirely beneficial case, why may they not litigate? The answer: *ela lachov al menas lizkos* — even a case with long-term benefit may involve short-term detriment, and that suffices to prohibit it. But something that is unambiguously and entirely beneficial — that they may pursue. The practical difficulty, of course, is that no one can predict with certainty how litigation will resolve before the case is decided, which is why the *rishonim* struggle to define precisely what situations are contemplated here. The broader principle is clear: *katanim* cannot be brought to court, and active litigation of claims against their property must generally await their majority.

### Field Sale Restrictions

The *beraisa* further prohibits: *ein apotropsin resha'im limkor berachok velikhol bekarov* — they may not sell distant property and acquire nearby property in its stead, nor sell inferior property to acquire superior property. The rationale given by the *gemara*: *dilma mishta­dfin* — the newly acquired property might be struck by blight, flooding, or some other calamity. One thought it was good property; it may turn out to be ruinous. This reflects a broader awareness in *Chazal* — visible across several of the recent *dapim* — of the tension between objective assessments of property quality and the reality that property values fluctuate wildly over time.

### Slaves for Fields

The *beraisa* adds: *ein apotropsin resha'im limkor sadot velikaach avadim* — they may not sell fields to acquire slaves. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel goes further: *af lo limkor avadim velikaach sadot* — even selling slaves to acquire fields is prohibited. His rationale: *dilma lo mashpyan* — the fields may not produce abundantly. The *apotropos* may have thought that converting liquid assets into permanent productive land was a sound investment; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel warns that the field may yield little, rendering the transaction a net loss — and the *apotropos* is only authorized to act for the *yetomim*'s clear benefit.

### Freeing Slaves Prohibiti…

The *beraisa* states: *ein apotropin resha'im lehotzi avadim lacherut* — *apotropsin* may not free slaves; *aval mocherin otan la'acherim* — but they may sell them to others, *ve'acherim motzi'in otan lacherut* — and those others may then free them. Note that the *tanna kamma* permits selling slaves to acquire real estate, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disputes this.

Rashi and Tosafot Rid dispute the underlying reason for the prohibition on freeing slaves. Rashi holds that the *apotropsin* simply lack the requisite legal power — *ein gufan kanui lahen leshachreran* — freeing slaves is not within their authority. Tosafot Rid presses the point: if they can sell slaves, why can they not free them, since the two transactions are functionally nearly equivalent? Tosafot Rid's answer is grounded in the principle of *hefker beit din hefker*: the *apotropos*'s powers derive from a rabbinic framework in which *beit din* effectively takes the orphans' property and re-vests authority in the *apotropos* to act on their behalf — but only for the specific transactions within that authorization. Freeing a slave, though similar to selling, falls outside the scope of what the rabbinic framework empowers the *apotropos* to do. The alternative conceptual framework — that *apotropsus* operates through *zekhiyyah* or *shelikhut*, perhaps as a superpowered form of *zekhiyyah* capable of encompassing even temporary loss for long-term gain — would have different implications.

### Rabbi's Self-Redemption

Rabbi interjects with his own view: *omer ani af hu noten demei atzmo veyotze mipnei shehu kemochro lo* — even the slave himself may pay for his own freedom, since a slave buying his own freedom is legally equivalent to someone else selling the slave to him. This is an interesting extension of the earlier discussions regarding *avadim*.

### Accounting Requirements

The *beraisa* states: *vetzarich lechashev imahem ba'achronah* — the *apotropos* must render a full accounting to the orphans at the conclusion of his tenure, documenting how much was spent on their upkeep, food, and other expenditures, to ensure complete transparency and honesty.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: *eino tzarich* — no accounting is required. His reasoning, which the *gemara* will develop: if you impose the burden of detailed accounting, no one will be willing to serve as *apotropos*. This tension — between protecting the *yetomim* through oversight and ensuring that capable people are willing to take on the responsibility — runs throughout this entire discussion.

### Ineligible Apotropsin

The *beraisa* concludes: *ein osin apotropsin nashim va'avadim uketannim* — women, slaves, and minors are ineligible to serve as *apotropsin*. It is important to note that these three categories are disqualified for very different reasons. Rashi explains regarding women: *she'ein darchan latzeis ulavo ulitrach* — it was not the custom of women in that era to go out into the marketplace, engage in business dealings, and manage court cases. Rashi's formulation implicitly acknowledges that this rationale is circumstantial rather than inherent: were the relevant social conditions different, the application of this *halacha* might well look different.

However, there is a significant qualification: *ve'im minahu avi yatmei harreshut beyado* — if the father himself designated such a person as *apotropos*, that appointment stands. The father's authority over his own estate and his knowledge of those he trusts give him power to override what *beit din* would otherwise require. It is his estate, and he may exercise his own judgment as to whom he entrusts it.

## Apotropsin Case Studies

### Rabbi Meir's Dream

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס דַּהֲוָה בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דַּהֲוָה קָא מְזַבֵּין אַרְעָתָא וְזָבֵין עַבְדֵי, וְלָא שַׁבְקֵיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אַחְווֹ לֵיהּ בְּחֶלְמֵיהּ ״אֲנִי לַהֲרוֹס וְאַתָּה לִבְנוֹת?!״ אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא אַשְׁגַּח, אֲמַר: דִּבְרֵי חֲלוֹמוֹת לֹא מַעֲלִין וְלֹ מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that there was **a certain steward who was in Rabbi Meir's neighborhood who was selling land** belonging to the orphans **and purchasing slaves** with the proceeds, **and Rabbi Meir did not allow him** to do this, as the practice is contrary to *halakha*. **They showed him in his dream** the words: **I wish to destroy and you build?** He understood this as a sign that God wanted the orphans to suffer financial collapse, and therefore it would be preferable to allow the steward to continue his practice. **Even so,** Rabbi Meir **paid no heed** to his dream, and **said: Words** appearing in **dreams do not bring up and do not take down;** they should not be taken into consideration.

The *gemara* now turns to a series of narrative case studies illustrating *apotropsin* in practice. *Hahu apotropos dehava bishivvusei deRabbi Meir* — there was an *apotropos* in the town of Rabbi Meir who was selling real estate and selling slaves. Rabbi Meir did not intervene. *Itlai be-chelma* — the deceased father appeared to Rabbi Meir in a dream, crying: *ani leharos ve'atah livnot* — you are letting him destroy everything and I am left having to rebuild? Yet even after this dream, Rabbi Meir still did not act. *Amar divrei chalomot lo maalin velo moridin* — dreams carry no halakhic weight; they neither elevate nor diminish the legal standing of a matter. The *gemara* records many conflicting attitudes toward dreams across *Shas* — in *Yevamos*, *Nedarim*, and *Berakhot* dreams are sometimes taken with great seriousness — but Rabbi Meir here represents a position of firm dismissal.

### Satan Possession Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בֵּי תְרֵי דְּאִיגָּרִי בְּהוּ שָׂטָן, דְּכֹל בֵּי שִׁמְשֵׁי הֲווֹ קָא מִינְּצוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. אִיקְּלַע רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְהָתָם, עַכְּבִינְהוּ תְּלָתָא בֵּי שִׁמְשֵׁי, עַד דַּעֲבַד לְהוּ שְׁלָמָא. שַׁמְעֵיהּ דְּקָאָמַר: וַוי, דְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא מִבֵּיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos an incident involving Rabbi Meir, the Gemara relates another story about him: **There were two** people **who, incited by Satan, would argue with each other every** Friday afternoon **at twilight. Rabbi Meir happened** to come **to** the place where they argued. **He stopped them** from fighting **three** Friday afternoons **at twilight, until** finally **he made peace between them. He** then **heard** Satan **say: Woe, that Rabbi Meir removed that man,** Satan, **from his house.** This indicates that Satan himself lives among those who have discord.

The *gemara* interjects a famous story about Rabbi Meir. *Hanehu betrei de'igrei behu satana* — there were two men who were seized by *satana*, such that every *erev Shabbat* they would erupt in conflict. Rabbi Meir came and stayed with them for three consecutive *Shabbat* evenings until he brought peace between them. Upon his departure, he heard Satan cry out in frustration: *vai de'afkei Rabbi Meir lehahu gavra mibeisei* — woe, for Rabbi Meir has driven that man out of his house. The *satana* brings *machlokes*; it cannot coexist in a space where *shalom* prevails. The story reveals Rabbi Meir as a person who went out of his way to engage with people and intervene in their conflicts — a picture consistent with other accounts of him elsewhere in *Shas*.

### Oxen Sale Uncontested

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס דַּהֲוָה בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דַּהֲוָה קָא מְזַבֵּין אַרְעָא וְזָבֵין תּוֹרֵי, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי; סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִיָּמַי לֹא קָרִיתִי לְאִשְׁתִּי ״אִשְׁתִּי״ וּלְשׁוֹרִי ״שׁוֹרִי״, אֶלָּא לְאִשְׁתִּי ״בֵּיתִי״, וּלְשׁוֹרִי ״שָׂדִי״.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that there was **a certain steward** of orphans **who was in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi who was selling land and buying oxen** on behalf of the orphans, **and he did not say anything to** the steward to the effect that he was acting improperly. The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said: In all my days, I did not call my wife: My wife, nor my ox: My ox. Rather,** I called **my wife: My home,** because she is the essence of my home, and I called **my ox: My field,** because the primary force behind enhancements to the field is the ox that plows it. Similarly, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi maintains that purchasing oxen to work the land is considered like purchasing land itself and that consequently a protest should not be raised against the steward, who sold land belonging to the orphans in order to purchase oxen with the proceeds.

A parallel case: *hahu apotropos dehava bishivvusei deRabbi Yehoshua ben Levi dehava kamazvin ar'a u-zavin tori velo amar lehu lei velo midi* — an *apotropos* in the domain of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was selling land and oxen, and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said nothing to him. The *gemara* explains that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi followed the opinion of Rabbi Yosi. As the *beraisa* records, Rabbi Yosi said that in his life he never called his wife "my wife" but rather "my house," for she oversaw the entire household, and he never called his ox "my ox" but rather "my field," for the ox was indispensable to working all his fields. In other words, Rabbi Yosi regarded the ox as equivalent in importance to the field itself — and consequently Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi did not object to the *apotropos* selling the orphans' oxen.

### Widow's Ox Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ יַתְמֵי דַּהֲווֹ סְמִיכִי גַּבֵּי הָהִיא סָבְתָּא. הֲוָה לְהוּ תּוֹרְתָּא, שְׁקַלָה וְזַבֵּינְתַּהּ נִיהֲלַיְיהוּ. אֲתוֹ קְרוֹבִים לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבִידְתַּהּ דִּמְזַבְּנָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״יְתוֹמִים שֶׁסָּמְכוּ אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** It is further related that **certain orphans who lived with an old woman had an ox,** and **she took it and sold it on their behalf. Relatives** of those orphans **came before Rav Naḥman** and **said to him: What is she doing selling** the orphans' property? Who authorized her to do so? Rav Naḥman **said to them: We learned** from the phrase in the mishna: If **orphans are living with a homeowner,** that official appointment as a steward is not necessary. The fact that the orphans lived with the woman and she took care of them sufficed to bestow upon her the authority of a steward.

Another case: *hanehu yasmei dehavu samchei gabei hahu sabta* — orphans who were under the care of an older woman. She had an ox belonging to them and sold it. The relatives came before Rav Nachman and protested. She invoked the case of *yetomim shesamkhu etzel ba'al habayit* from our *mishnah* — orphans who had taken up residence with a householder — arguing that she was acting within her authorized capacity to sell the ox for their support.

### Property as Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיַּיקַּר! בִּרְשׁוּתָא דְלוֹקֵחַ אִיַּיקַּר. וְהָא לָא נְקִיטִי דְּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The relatives continued with their objection to the sale: **But didn't** the animal afterward **increase in value,** which is reason to invalidate the transaction? Rav Naḥman answered: The animal **increased in value while in the possession of the buyer,** and this is not considered as a loss suffered by the orphans. The relatives said to him: **But they did not** yet **receive** the **money,** and consequently the sale was incomplete.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִם כֵּן, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר רַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נִכְסֵי יְתוֹמִין הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְלָא מִקְּנֵי אֶלָּא בְּכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to them: If so, this is** what **Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi says** that **Shmuel says,** as **Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi says** that **Shmuel says: Orphans' property is like consecrated** property **and is** fully **acquired only with** the transfer of **money.** Since they did not yet receive the money, they can raise the purchase price or renege on the entire sale.

The *gemara* then notes: *veha ikar bireshusa delokeach ikakar* — but the ox had appreciated in value once it was in the buyer's possession. Moreover, *ve'ada lo nekiti damei* — the money had not yet been paid over. What could or should be done?

Rav Nachman ruled on the basis of a principle transmitted by Rav Chanilai bar Idi in the name of Shmuel: *nichsei yetomim harei hen kehekdesh velo makni ela bechespa* — the property of orphans is like *hekdesh*, consecrated property, in that it can be acquired only through the transfer of money. This generates an important practical rule: just as a transaction involving *hekdesh* is not finalized until money changes hands, so too a transaction involving orphans' property.

This contrasts with the general rule for *metaltelin*, where there is a *machlokes* between Rav Yochanan and Reish Lakish — *ma'os konos* versus *eino nikneh ela bemeshicha* — whether acquisition of movable property takes effect through payment of money or through the act of *meshicha*, physically taking possession. For orphans' property, the rule is fixed: only cash completes the transaction, akin to *hekdesh*.

This principle operates in both directions, always to the *yetomim*'s benefit: if the price rises after *meshicha* but before payment, the *yetomim* collect at the higher price. The system is, in effect, rigged in their favor — whatever arrangement yields them the better outcome is the operative one.

### Wine Price Fluctuation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַמְרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא עוּקְבָא יַתְמָא – מַשְׁכוּהּ בְּאַרְבְּעָה אַרְבְּעָה, וְאִיַּיקַּר וְקָם בְּשִׁיתָּא שִׁיתָּא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַיְינוּ דְּרַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידִי – דְּאָמַר רַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נִכְסֵי יְתוֹמִין הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְלָא מִקְּנֵי אֶלָּא בְּכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, it is related that those who came to buy **the wine of Rabbana Ukva the orphan pulled it** into their possession after having agreed **on** a purchase price of **four** dinars per barrel. **But** before the buyers actually paid for the wine, **it increased in value and** its price **stood at six** dinars per barrel. The parties **came before Rav Naḥman** for a ruling on the matter. **He said to them: This is** subject to what **Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi** said, **as Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi says** that **Shmuel says: Orphans' property is like consecrated** property, **and is** fully **acquired only with** the transfer of **money.** Consequently, the buyers pay the current, higher price or the sale can be rendered void.

The *gemara* works through several scenarios. *Chamra de-rabna Ukva yatmei mashkhuhu be-arba'ah arba'ah* — wine belonging to the estate of the orphans of Rabna Ukva was drawn (*meshicha* was performed) when it was worth four per barrel. The price then rose to six per barrel. Rav Nachman invoked the ruling of Rav Chanilai bar Idi: since *nichsei yetomim* are like *hekdesh* and are acquired only through money, and money had not yet been paid, the transaction is resolved at the higher price — a very substantial windfall for the *yetomim*.

Conversely: if *meshicha* was performed on behalf of the *yetomim* when they were purchasing produce and the price then dropped — *lo yehei koach hedyot chamur mehekdesh* — the ordinary mechanism of sale must not work worse for them than *hekdesh* does. We give them the benefit of either form of acquisition, whichever is more advantageous.

### Merchant Incentives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשׁוּךְ פֵּירֵי מִיַּתְמֵי; אִיַּיקּוּר – הַיְינוּ דְּרַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידִי. זוּל – לֹא יְהֵא כֹּחַ הֶדְיוֹט חָמוּר מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records several additional laws relating to this topic: If the buyers **pulled** into their possession **produce belonging to orphans** but did not yet pay for it, and afterward the produce **increased in value, this is** subject to what **Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi** said: Orphans' property is fully acquired only with the transfer of money, and until payment is made the transaction can still be rendered void. But if the produce **decreased in value,** and the buyers wish to renege on the sale, one can argue: Just as it is said that **the power of an ordinary person should not be greater than** that of the **Temple** treasury, so too, the power of orphans should not be less than that of other people. Since the buyers pulled the orphans' property into their possession, thereby performing an act of acquisition, the sale is valid and the buyers cannot retract.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִמְּשִׁיכוּ לְהוּ פֵּירֵי לְיַתְמֵי; אִיַּיקַּר – לֹא יְהֵא כֹּחַ הֶדְיוֹט חָמוּר מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ. זוּל – סְבוּר מִינָּה, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידִי;
>
> **Translation:** If the steward **pulled produce** into his possession **on behalf of the orphans,** but did not yet pay for it, and afterward it **increased in value,** then just as **the power of an ordinary person should not be greater than** that of the **Temple** treasury, so too, the power of orphans should not be less than that of other people. Therefore, the orphans acquire the produce based on its price at the time the steward pulled it into their possession. But if **the produce decreased in value,** and now the steward wishes to cancel the sale, the students in the study hall **understood from here** that **this is** included in what **Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi** said, that transactions involving orphans are completed only with the transfer of money, and therefore the steward can renege on the sale and acquire the produce at the lower price.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: הָא רָעָה הִיא לְדִידְהוּ – דְּזִמְנִין דְּמִצְטַרְכִי לְפֵירֵי, וְלֵיכָּא דְּיָהֵיב לְהוּ עַד דְּיָהֲבִי זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said to them: This would be bad for** the orphans were they treated in this manner, **as** they might at **times need produce and nobody will give** it **to them before they** actually **pay the money.** It is preferable for them to be treated like other buyers, who finalize their acquisition when they pull the produce into their possession.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָהֲבִי יַתְמֵי זוּזֵי אַפֵּירֵי; זוּל – לֹא יְהֵא כֹּחַ הֶדְיוֹט חָמוּר מִן הֶקְדֵּשׁ. אִיַּיקַּר – סְבוּר מִינַּהּ, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב חֲנִילַאי בַּר אִידִי;
>
> **Translation:** If **the orphans gave money for** the purchase of **produce** but did not yet physically transfer it into their possession, and afterward the produce **decreased in value,** then one applies the principle that **the power of an ordinary person,** the seller, **should not be greater than** the **Temple** treasury. Therefore, the orphans can renege on the purchase, as an act of acquisition has not yet been performed. But if the produce **increased in value,** and the orphans wish to uphold the sale, the students in the study hall **understood from here** that **this is** included in what **Rav Ḥanilai bar Idi** said, that transactions involving orphans are completed with the transfer of money, and therefore the orphans acquire the produce at the lower price.

This raises a practical concern: if sellers know that the price will always resolve against them when dealing with *yetomim*, people will simply refuse to do business with them. Moreover, applying the *hekdesh* principle in both directions — such that *yetomim* always pay the lower price when buying and receive the higher price when selling — creates a system that merchants will circumvent by waiting until payment and delivery coincide exactly. Rav Shisha, son of Rav Idi, identifies the countervailing risk on each side: if *yetomim* pay cash first before taking possession, the seller can always invoke *nisrefu chitechem ba'aliya* — your wheat burned in the attic — claiming the goods were destroyed before delivery, leaving the *yetomim* with no recourse. The *gemara* works through these tensions carefully, ultimately leaving the general principle of protecting the *yetomim* intact while acknowledging the practical complications.

Rav Ashi adds: he and Rav Kahana signed a document authorizing the sale of a field belonging to orphans to pay a tax, without performing *achrazata* — the public announcement calling for the best possible buyer that is normally required before selling orphan property. The *Nehardai* ruled that for three categories of pressing need — paying taxes, providing food, or funding a burial — *achrazata* is not required. Tosafot notes that the case of burial seems so self-evidently urgent as to be unworthy of explicit mention; the *gemara* must therefore be referring not to purchasing a burial plot itself but to repaying a debt incurred for a burial.

### Amram Tzeva Accusations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַמְרָם צַבָּעָא אַפּוֹטְרוֹפָּא דְיַתְמֵי הֲוָה, אֲתוֹ קְרוֹבִים לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: קָא לָבֵישׁ וּמִכַּסֵּי מִיַּתְמֵי! אֲמַר לְהוּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִישְׁתַּמְעָן מִילֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Amram the dyer was a steward for orphans,** and the orphans' **relatives** once **came before Rav Naḥman** and **said to him: He dresses and covers** himself with clothing purchased **from** the property of **the orphans.** Rav Naḥman **said to them:** Perhaps he does this **in order that his words be heard,** meaning that he dresses himself in finery at the orphans' expense in order to better manage their property, as he will be ignored if he appears to be a poor person.

*Amram Tzeva* was serving as *apotropos* for orphans. Relatives came before Rav Nachman claiming he was dressing and clothing himself from the orphans' property. Rav Nachman's response was measured: perhaps he is eating and drinking from his own means — and if someone found an unexpectedly valuable lost object (*metzia*), one gives him the benefit of the doubt.

### Removing Negligent Guard…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָאָכֵיל וְשָׁתֵי מִדִּידְהוּ, וְלָא אֲמִיד! אֵימוֹר מְצִיאָה אַשְׁכַּח. וְהָא קָא מַפְסֵיד! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַיְיתוֹ לִי סָהֲדִי דְּמַפְסֵיד, וְאֵיסַלְּקִינֵּיהּ; דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא חַבְרִין מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס דְּמַפְסֵיד, מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ. דְּאִיתְּמַר: אַפּוֹטְרוֹפָּא דְּמַפְסֵיד, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ; דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרִי: לָא מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ. וְהִלְכְתָא: מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** They said to him: **He eats and drinks** lavishly **from** the orphan's property **and is not** known to be **rich** enough to be able to afford such large expenditures from his own money. Rav Naḥman said to them: **Say** that perhaps **he found a lost item,** with the help of which he can allow himself to maintain a high standard of living. They said to him: **But he is damaging** the orphans' property and thereby causing them financial loss. Rav Naḥman **said to them: Bring me witnesses that he is damaging** their property **and I will remove him** from his stewardship, **as our colleague Rav Huna said in the name of Rav** that in the case of **a steward who damages** the property of the orphans, the court **removes him. As it was stated:** With regard to **a steward who damages** orphans' property, **Rav Huna** says that **Rav says:** The court removes him. **In the school of Rabbi Sheila they say:** The court **does not remove him.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that the court **removes him.**

The *gemara* then asks: but if he is in fact depleting their estate, that is a serious problem. Rav Nachman replied: bring me witnesses that he is causing loss, and I will remove him. The *apotropos* is entitled to a presumption of good faith — and that presumption serves a practical function. If an *apotropos* can be removed on bare accusation, no one will be willing to accept the responsibility.

Rav Huna stated in Rav's name: *apotropos demafsid mesalkinan lei* — an *apotropos* who causes loss to the orphans is removed. This is the subject of a direct *machlokes* among *amora'im*: Rav Huna citing Rav rules that he is removed; the school of Rabbi Shila says he is not removed. The *halacha* follows Rav Huna: he is removed.

## Shvuah for Apotropsin

### Tanna Kamma Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס שֶׁמִּינָּהוּ אֲבִי יְתוֹמִים – יִשָּׁבַע. מִינּוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – לֹא יִשָּׁבַע. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a steward who was appointed by the orphans' father,** when he returns all of the property to the orphans upon their reaching adulthood, **he takes an oath** that he took nothing of theirs for himself. By contrast, if **the court appointed him** to serve as a steward for them, then **he is not** required to **take** such **an oath. Abba Shaul says: The matters are reversed.** A steward appointed by the court takes an oath, but a steward appointed by the orphans' father is not required to do so.

The *mishnah* rules: *apotropos sheminahu avi yetomim yishava* — an *apotropos* appointed by the father must take a *shvuah*; *minahu beit din lo yishava* — one appointed by *beit din* does not.

### Incentive Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי לָאו דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ – לָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס; וּמִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעָה לָא אָתֵי לְאִמְּנוֹעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? **If** the steward **had not received** some **benefit from** the father during his life, **he would not have** agreed to **become a steward** for his children. **And he will not come to avoid** becoming a steward merely **because of an oath** that the court will impose upon him some time in the future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִינּוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – לֹא יִשָּׁבַע: מִלְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּעָבֵיד לְבֵי דִינָא; וְאִי רָמֵית עֲלֵיהּ שְׁבוּעָה אָתֵי לְאִמְּנוֹעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **the court appointed him** to serve as a steward for them, then **he is not** required to **take** such **an oath.** The Gemara explains: The reason for this is that **it is merely a favor that** the steward **does for the court,** as he derives no benefit from accepting the position. **And if you** also **cast** an obligation upon him to take **an oath, he will come to avoid** becoming a steward, and the orphans will suffer a loss, as people will not be willing to administer their affairs.

The *gemara* explains the *tanna kamma*'s reasoning. The father-appointed *apotropos* is asked to take a *shvuah* precisely because *i lav de'is lei hana'ah minei lo hava lei apotropos* — if he were not receiving some benefit from the arrangement, he never would have agreed to take it on. The father must have offered him compensation or some special inducement. Accordingly, *mishum shvuah lo ati le'amnuyei* — the prospect of taking an oath at the conclusion of his tenure will not deter him from having accepted the position in the first place; the benefit still outweighs the burden. Therefore the *shvuah* can be imposed without creating a disincentive.

Conversely, the *beit din*-appointed *apotropos* does not take a *shvuah*. His motivation is simply *milta be'alma hu de'avid le'bei dina* — he is doing a favor for the court. *Ve'i ramit lei shvuah ati le'amnuyei* — if we burden him with the obligation of an oath at the end, the negatives will outweigh whatever public benefit he derives, and no one will agree to serve. The *beit din* does not pay its *apotropos*, making the deterrent effect of the *shvuah* far more pronounced.

This discussion invites reflection on how courts today think about foster care arrangements — with relatives versus non-relatives — and the degree to which financial incentives and public recognition affect people's willingness to step into caregiving roles for orphans.

### Abba Shaul Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים: מַאי טַעְמָא מִינּוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין – יִשָּׁבַע? בְּהַהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא נָפֵיק עֲלֵיהּ קָלָא דְּאִינִישׁ מְהֵימְנָא הוּא – דְּהָא סָמֵיךְ עֲלֵיהּ בֵּי דִינָא – מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעָה לָא אָתֵי לְאִמְּנוֹעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna then teaches: **Abba Shaul says: The matters are reversed;** it is not a steward appointed by the orphans' father but a steward appointed by the court who takes an oath. The Gemara clarifies Abba Shaul's opinion: **What is the reason** that if **the court appointed** the steward, **he takes an oath? Since** he derives **a certain benefit** from his appointment, **that publicity is generated about him that he is a trustworthy man, as** is demonstrated by the fact that **the court relies upon him** and appoints him to a position of responsibility, **he will not come to avoid** becoming a steward merely **because of an oath** that the court will impose upon him.

*Abba Shaul omer chiluf hadvarim* — Abba Shaul reverses the ruling entirely.

### Public Reputation Logic

Abba Shaul's rationale for his reversal: *minuhu beit din yishava be'hahi hana'ah de-nafik lei kala de'inish meheman hu* — when *beit din* appoints you, your appointment becomes publicly known. You become recognized in the community as a person of trustworthiness whom the court has seen fit to select. *Deha samchu lei beit din* — the court has placed its confidence in you. The public reputation you thereby acquire is itself a form of *hana'ah*, and it is sufficient to keep you motivated. Therefore *mishum shvuah lo ati le'amnuyei minehon* — the prospect of taking a *shvuah* will not deter you from having accepted the appointment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִינָּהוּ אֲבִי יְתוֹמִים, לֹא יִשָּׁבַע – מִילְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּעָבְדִי לַהֲדָדֵי, וְאִי רָמֵית עֲלֵיהּ שְׁבוּעָה – אָתֵי לְאִמְּנוֹעֵי. אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר אַמֵּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הִלְכְתָא כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **the orphans' father appointed him** as a steward, he is **not** required to **take an oath.** Why so? **It is merely a favor that** these people **do for each other. And if you cast** an additional obligation upon the steward to take **an oath, he will come to avoid** accepting the position, and the orphans will suffer a loss, as people will not be willing to administer their affairs. **Rav Ḥanan bar Ami says** that **Shmuel says: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul.**

Regarding the father-appointed *apotropos*, Abba Shaul argues the opposite: *milta be'alma hu de'avid le-hahu gavra* — he is doing a favor for a man who is now dead. A private appointment by a deceased father does not generate the same public recognition as a *beit din* appointment. The reputational reward is minimal, and therefore *ve'i ramit lei shvuah ati le'amnuyei* — the burden of a *shvuah* will indeed deter him from having agreed to serve.

### Shmuel's Ruling

Rav Chanan bar Ami reports in the name of Shmuel: *hilchata ke-Abba Shaul* — the *halacha* follows Abba Shaul. The *Rif* indeed rules accordingly.

### Rabbi Elazar's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה יִשָּׁבַע, וַהֲלָכָה כִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** Both **this** one, a steward appointed by the father, **and that** one, a court-appointed steward, **take an oath, and the *halakha*** is **in accordance with his statement.**

However, Tosafot raises a difficulty: a subsequent *beraisa* records that Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov says *zeh vezeh yishava* — both types of *apotropos*, whether appointed by the father or by *beit din*, must take a *shvuah*. The *beraisa* itself concludes: *halacha kidvarav* — the *halacha* is in accordance with his view. This appears to contradict the ruling of Shmuel following Abba Shaul, and Tosafot grapples with how to resolve the tension.

### Paid Guardian Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר מַעְרְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס שֶׁמִּינָּהוּ אֲבִי יְתוֹמִים – יִשָּׁבַע, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ אַיְיתֵת קַבָּא וְכָיְילַתְּ לֵיהּ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥalifa, from the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Abbahu:** In the case of **a steward who was appointed** to that position **by the father of the orphans, he takes an oath** when the orphans reach adulthood and he returns the property to them, **because he receives payment,** i.e., he derives a certain benefit from managing the orphans' property. Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: Did you bring a *kav* and measure** how much the steward earns from his efforts? **Rather, say** that he takes an oath **because it is as if he received payment.** Presumably he agreed to serve as the orphans' steward because of a benefit that he derived from their father during his lifetime, and consequently it is as if he received payment for accepting the stewardship.

Rav Tachlifa bar Ma'arava taught before Rabbi Abbahu: the reason the father-appointed *apotropos* takes a *shvuah* is *mipnei shehu nosei sachar* — because he is a *shomer sachar*, a paid guardian. Rabbi Abbahu challenges the precise formulation: he is not literally a *shomer sachar*; rather, he is *kenosei sachar* — like a paid guardian. He derives a certain *hana'ah* from the arrangement that places him in the functional position of someone who benefits from the *shmirah*, even if not technically a hired watchman.

There is a great deal more to be said about this *daf*. The *Ran* and the *Ra'avad* and *Rambam* on the *dapei ha-Rif* engage in a rich *machlokes* about how the institution of *apotropus* interacts with the various *halakhot* surveyed here. It is, in all, a remarkably dense and jam-packed *daf*.
