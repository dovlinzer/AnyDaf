# Gittin 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Modeh BeMiktzat

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabanan

The discussion picks up approximately halfway through 51b, continuing the *machlokes* between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *rabanan* regarding the circumstances under which one can compel another to take a *shvuah* in the cases we have been examining — and specifically, the question of *kemeshiv aveidah*: whether a minor can force someone to take a *shvuah*.

### Three Shvuot Categories

There is an interesting *machlokes rishonim* about which *shvuot* this exemption applies to. Three distinct categories of *shvuah* are relevant to the discussion. The first is *shvuas ed echad*: where a single witness testifies that a debt is owed, the witness cannot extract the debt but his testimony compels the defendant to take a *shvuah*. The second is *shvuas hapikadon*: where a *shomer* — someone watching over property — can be compelled to swear that he did not mishandle what was entrusted to him. The third, and the one the *gemara* now addresses directly, is *shvuas modeh bemiktzas*: where a person admits to part of a claim and is consequently compelled to take a *shvuah* regarding the remainder.

The *gemara* states: *ella keRabbah kamipalgei* — this *machlokes* is actually grounded in a principle articulated by Rabbah.

### Rabbah's Chazakah

Rabbah asks: *mipnei mah amrah Torah modeh bemiktzas hata'anah yishava* — why does the Torah require someone who admits to a partial debt to take a *shvuah*? One might wonder why Rabbah needs to inquire into the rationale at all; if it is *halacha*, it is *halacha*. But the reason Rabbah's rationale matters here is that it becomes the operative principle for determining how *modeh bemiktzas* intersects with the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *rabanan*.

Rabbah's answer: *chazakah ain adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — there is a *chazakah*, a presumption, that a person will not brazenly contradict someone to his face with a bald-faced lie. Tosafot formulates this sharply: *ain adam meiz kol makom she'oso kenegdo yode'a shehu meshaker* — a person will not be so brazen as to lie to someone who knows with certainty that he is lying. This *chazakah* is, admittedly, a claim about human psychology, and one might wonder whether it applies uniformly across all people and eras. Nonetheless, since *modeh bemiktzas* is a *halacha de'oraisa*, the operative rule certainly remains in force regardless.

### Delay Mechanism

The *gemara* then traces the psychological logic of the partial admission. Since *ain adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo*, an honest person ought to have admitted to the entire debt. Why, then, does he admit only to part? Because *demishta­mutei hu dekamishta­mit lei* — he is trying to delay. He knows he owes the full amount, but he lacks the funds to pay it all right now, so he admits to part, pays that immediately, and hopes to buy time until he has the rest: *ad dehavu li zuzei ufarna lei*. He intends to pay eventually, but uses the mechanism of *modeh bemiktzas* to force a delay — against the lender's will.

It is this manipulation of the system that the Torah addresses. *Ve'amar Rachmana remei shvuah aleih* — the Torah imposes a *shvuah* on him, as a way of pressuring him to admit the full amount rather than engineer a delay. The *shvuah* is designed to make the partial denial untenable.

### Eliezer's Position

Having established Rabbah's rationale, the *gemara* explains the positions of the *tanna'im*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds *lo shna bo velo shna bivno* — it makes no difference whether one is confronting the original creditor or that creditor's heir. *Eino meiz* — the debtor will not be brazen even in the presence of the son. The psychological restraint remains operative whether the opposing party is the original lender or his child, and therefore the same *shvuah* logic applies.

### Rabanan Counter

The *rabanan* disagree. In their view, *bo de'eino meiz aval livno meiz* — a person is indeed restrained from lying to the creditor himself, but he will be far more willing to stand up against the creditor's child, who was not party to the original transaction. Since the psychological impediment is diminished or absent, the very fact that the debtor admits to *part* of the claim is no longer surprising — he could easily have denied everything. His partial admission therefore resembles the conduct of a *meshiv aveidah*, someone voluntarily returning a lost object. Because the internal psychological pressure that would normally explain the partial admission is not in play, we give him greater benefit of the doubt, do not compel him to take a *shvuah*, and treat him as the honest person he appears to be.

## Apotropsin Powers

### Types of Apotropsin

With the beginning of the new *mishnah* at the top of 52a, the topic shifts entirely to *apotropsin* — appointed guardians who oversee the estates of orphans. The *mishnah* and subsequent *gemara* distinguish among three types of *apotropos*. The first is appointed by the father himself — anticipating his own death, a father may designate someone to administer the estate on behalf of his minor children. This has clear resonance for any case where a person can foresee his own death in advance; it is an interesting precedent for instruments akin to living wills and advance directives. The second type is self-appointed — someone who simply steps forward and assumes responsibility for the property. The third is appointed by *beit din*.

The *mishnah* opens: *yetomim shesamkhu etzel ba'al habayit* — orphans who have taken up residence with a householder, *o sheminah lahen avihen apotropos* — or whose father designated an *apotropos* for them — *apotropos chayav le'aser peroteihem* — the *apotropos* is obligated to separate *ma'aser* from their produce.

This immediately raises a question. The underlying issue is one of *zekhiyyah* and its relationship to ownership: who has the right to separate *terumah* and *ma'aser* from produce that belongs to someone else? The *gemara* in *Kiddushin* in the second *perek* distinguishes three different mechanisms by which land was apportioned when the Jewish people entered *Eretz Yisrael* with Yehoshua: *shelikhut* — formal agency; *zekhiyyah* — automatically doing something beneficial for another, with a *machlokes rishonim* as to whether *zekhiyyah* operates through the mechanism of *shelikhut*; and *apotropos* — a distinct category unto itself. The *apotropos* is not the owner of the produce. A *shaliach* can separate *terumah* from another's produce based on a special *drashah* from a *pasuk*, but the *pasuk atem* — "you," implying only owners — would seem to exclude an *apotropos* from this power.

One might ask: why does this matter so urgently, given that *katanim* are only obligated in *mitzvot* through *chinuch* anyway, making any *issur* at worst *derabanan*? Yet someone must manage their religious and financial lives, and that is precisely what motivates this entire institution of *apotropus* — the *yetomim* need someone to oversee their financial dealings and ensure their proper upbringing.

Regarding the underlying mechanism by which *apotropsus* functions: Rabbeinu Tam maintains that the *drashah* from *atem* is only an *asmachta*, and the principle excluding *apotropsin* from separating *terumah* is merely *derabanan*. The exception allowing the *apotropos* to separate *terumah* in the case of actively feeding the *yetomim* is itself a *derabanan* creation designed to protect them. Other *rishonim* propose different approaches. As we will see later, Tosafot Rid invokes *hefker beit din hefker* — the principle that a *beit din* can divest and reassign ownership — as the underlying mechanism that grants the *apotropos* his powers. That principle will resurface shortly.

### Tithes Separation

The *gemara* raises an apparent contradiction: *uvreminhu* — a *beraisa* that seems to exclude *apotropsin* from separating *terumah*. Rav Chisda resolves the tension with a distinction: *kan le-hachil, kan le-haniach* — one case concerns feeding the *yetomim* now, while the other concerns storing produce for the future. When the *apotropos* is separating *terumah* and *ma'aser* so as to actively feed the *yetomim* from the produce at hand, this is permitted. But to separate *terumah* from produce that will simply sit in storage for a decade until the orphans mature — that is not permitted, because they will be fully capable of separating it themselves when they are grown. The *gemara* confirms: *ve-hatanya apotropsin tormin u-maasrin le-hachil aval lo le-haniach* — the *beraisa* supports Rav Chisda's distinction, and Rashi notes that *ve-hatanya* here is used *be-nichuta*, as a supporting citation rather than a challenge.

### Braita's Restrictions

The *beraisa* continues with a comprehensive list of what *apotropsin* may and may not do. They may sell animals, slaves, houses, fields, and vineyards — *le-hachil*, to actively support the *yetomim*, but not *le-haniach*, not to build up a long-term reserve. Similarly, they may sell fruits, wine, oil, and flour for immediate support but not for storage. The language *u-mochrin lahem* encompasses both selling on behalf of the *yetomim* and purchasing for them — the *apotropos* manages active financial support in either direction.

### Fixed vs. Indefinite Pri…

The *beraisa* proceeds to enumerate *mitzvah* objects that may be purchased: the *apotropos* may make for the orphans a *lulav*, *arava* — notable since *arava* on its own is a *minhag* rather than a biblical *mitzvah*, though the *Rif*'s version of the text omits *arava* — a *sukkah*, and *tzitzit*. The *beraisa* then adds a general category: *kol davar sheyesh lo kitzvah* — anything with a fixed and defined cost; the *gemara* notes this includes *shofar* and, separately, *megillah*. Further, the *apotropos* may purchase for the orphans a *Sefer Torah*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot*. Regarding a *Sefer Torah* in particular: there is a *mitzvah* for every Jewish man to write one, and beyond the ritual dimension, in antiquity a *Sefer Torah* was the means of Torah study itself — effectively, purchasing a *Sefer Torah* amounted to providing an education. The Maharam Shiff examines each item in this list individually, treating each as a *chiddush* in its own right.

### Court & Property Limits

Corresponding to these permissions, the *beraisa* specifies what *apotropsin* may not do: *ein poskin aleihen tzedakah* — they may not donate *tzedakah* from the orphans' inheritance; *ein podin aleihen et hashvuyim* — they may not use orphan funds to redeem captives; and *lo kol davar she'ein lo kitzvah* — nothing in the category of indefinite, open-ended expense. The paradigm case is *tanchumei aveilim* — bringing food to a house of mourning — since communal and charitable claims of this nature could arise indefinitely and drain the estate without limit. The governing principle is that any expenditure must be capped and predictable; anything unlimited falls outside the *apotropos*'s authority.

This typology raises an interesting question: does it apply uniformly to all types of *apotropos*, whether father-appointed, self-appointed, or *beit din*-appointed? The *mishnah* will address that shortly.

The *beraisa* further states: *ein apotropsin resha'im ladun lachov velizkos benichsei yetomim* — *apotropsin* may not litigate, whether the outcome would be adverse or favorable, with regard to orphan property. The *gemara* asks: if it is an entirely beneficial case, why may they not litigate? The answer: *ela lachov al menas lizkos* — even a case with long-term benefit may involve short-term detriment, and that suffices to prohibit it. But something that is unambiguously and entirely beneficial — that they may pursue. The practical difficulty, of course, is that no one can predict with certainty how litigation will resolve before the case is decided, which is why the *rishonim* struggle to define precisely what situations are contemplated here. The broader principle is clear: *katanim* cannot be brought to court, and active litigation of claims against their property must generally await their majority.

### Field Sale Restrictions

The *beraisa* further prohibits: *ein apotropsin resha'im limkor berachok velikhol bekarov* — they may not sell distant property and acquire nearby property in its stead, nor sell inferior property to acquire superior property. The rationale given by the *gemara*: *dilma mishta­dfin* — the newly acquired property might be struck by blight, flooding, or some other calamity. One thought it was good property; it may turn out to be ruinous. This reflects a broader awareness in *Chazal* — visible across several of the recent *dapim* — of the tension between objective assessments of property quality and the reality that property values fluctuate wildly over time.

### Slaves for Fields

The *beraisa* adds: *ein apotropsin resha'im limkor sadot velikaach avadim* — they may not sell fields to acquire slaves. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel goes further: *af lo limkor avadim velikaach sadot* — even selling slaves to acquire fields is prohibited. His rationale: *dilma lo mashpyan* — the fields may not produce abundantly. The *apotropos* may have thought that converting liquid assets into permanent productive land was a sound investment; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel warns that the field may yield little, rendering the transaction a net loss — and the *apotropos* is only authorized to act for the *yetomim*'s clear benefit.

### Freeing Slaves Prohibiti…

The *beraisa* states: *ein apotropin resha'im lehotzi avadim lacherut* — *apotropsin* may not free slaves; *aval mocherin otan la'acherim* — but they may sell them to others, *ve'acherim motzi'in otan lacherut* — and those others may then free them. Note that the *tanna kamma* permits selling slaves to acquire real estate, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disputes this.

Rashi and Tosafot Rid dispute the underlying reason for the prohibition on freeing slaves. Rashi holds that the *apotropsin* simply lack the requisite legal power — *ein gufan kanui lahen leshachreran* — freeing slaves is not within their authority. Tosafot Rid presses the point: if they can sell slaves, why can they not free them, since the two transactions are functionally nearly equivalent? Tosafot Rid's answer is grounded in the principle of *hefker beit din hefker*: the *apotropos*'s powers derive from a rabbinic framework in which *beit din* effectively takes the orphans' property and re-vests authority in the *apotropos* to act on their behalf — but only for the specific transactions within that authorization. Freeing a slave, though similar to selling, falls outside the scope of what the rabbinic framework empowers the *apotropos* to do. The alternative conceptual framework — that *apotropsus* operates through *zekhiyyah* or *shelikhut*, perhaps as a superpowered form of *zekhiyyah* capable of encompassing even temporary loss for long-term gain — would have different implications.

### Rabbi's Self-Redemption

Rabbi interjects with his own view: *omer ani af hu noten demei atzmo veyotze mipnei shehu kemochro lo* — even the slave himself may pay for his own freedom, since a slave buying his own freedom is legally equivalent to someone else selling the slave to him. This is an interesting extension of the earlier discussions regarding *avadim*.

### Accounting Requirements

The *beraisa* states: *vetzarich lechashev imahem ba'achronah* — the *apotropos* must render a full accounting to the orphans at the conclusion of his tenure, documenting how much was spent on their upkeep, food, and other expenditures, to ensure complete transparency and honesty.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: *eino tzarich* — no accounting is required. His reasoning, which the *gemara* will develop: if you impose the burden of detailed accounting, no one will be willing to serve as *apotropos*. This tension — between protecting the *yetomim* through oversight and ensuring that capable people are willing to take on the responsibility — runs throughout this entire discussion.

### Ineligible Apotropsin

The *beraisa* concludes: *ein osin apotropsin nashim va'avadim uketannim* — women, slaves, and minors are ineligible to serve as *apotropsin*. It is important to note that these three categories are disqualified for very different reasons. Rashi explains regarding women: *she'ein darchan latzeis ulavo ulitrach* — it was not the custom of women in that era to go out into the marketplace, engage in business dealings, and manage court cases. Rashi's formulation implicitly acknowledges that this rationale is circumstantial rather than inherent: were the relevant social conditions different, the application of this *halacha* might well look different.

However, there is a significant qualification: *ve'im minahu avi yatmei harreshut beyado* — if the father himself designated such a person as *apotropos*, that appointment stands. The father's authority over his own estate and his knowledge of those he trusts give him power to override what *beit din* would otherwise require. It is his estate, and he may exercise his own judgment as to whom he entrusts it.

## Apotropsin Case Studies

### Rabbi Meir's Dream

The *gemara* now turns to a series of narrative case studies illustrating *apotropsin* in practice. *Hahu apotropos dehava bishivvusei deRabbi Meir* — there was an *apotropos* in the town of Rabbi Meir who was selling real estate and selling slaves. Rabbi Meir did not intervene. *Itlai be-chelma* — the deceased father appeared to Rabbi Meir in a dream, crying: *ani leharos ve'atah livnot* — you are letting him destroy everything and I am left having to rebuild? Yet even after this dream, Rabbi Meir still did not act. *Amar divrei chalomot lo maalin velo moridin* — dreams carry no halakhic weight; they neither elevate nor diminish the legal standing of a matter. The *gemara* records many conflicting attitudes toward dreams across *Shas* — in *Yevamos*, *Nedarim*, and *Berakhot* dreams are sometimes taken with great seriousness — but Rabbi Meir here represents a position of firm dismissal.

### Satan Possession Case

The *gemara* interjects a famous story about Rabbi Meir. *Hanehu betrei de'igrei behu satana* — there were two men who were seized by *satana*, such that every *erev Shabbat* they would erupt in conflict. Rabbi Meir came and stayed with them for three consecutive *Shabbat* evenings until he brought peace between them. Upon his departure, he heard Satan cry out in frustration: *vai de'afkei Rabbi Meir lehahu gavra mibeisei* — woe, for Rabbi Meir has driven that man out of his house. The *satana* brings *machlokes*; it cannot coexist in a space where *shalom* prevails. The story reveals Rabbi Meir as a person who went out of his way to engage with people and intervene in their conflicts — a picture consistent with other accounts of him elsewhere in *Shas*.

### Oxen Sale Uncontested

A parallel case: *hahu apotropos dehava bishivvusei deRabbi Yehoshua ben Levi dehava kamazvin ar'a u-zavin tori velo amar lehu lei velo midi* — an *apotropos* in the domain of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was selling land and oxen, and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said nothing to him. The *gemara* explains that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi followed the opinion of Rabbi Yosi. As the *beraisa* records, Rabbi Yosi said that in his life he never called his wife "my wife" but rather "my house," for she oversaw the entire household, and he never called his ox "my ox" but rather "my field," for the ox was indispensable to working all his fields. In other words, Rabbi Yosi regarded the ox as equivalent in importance to the field itself — and consequently Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi did not object to the *apotropos* selling the orphans' oxen.

### Widow's Ox Sale

Another case: *hanehu yasmei dehavu samchei gabei hahu sabta* — orphans who were under the care of an older woman. She had an ox belonging to them and sold it. The relatives came before Rav Nachman and protested. She invoked the case of *yetomim shesamkhu etzel ba'al habayit* from our *mishnah* — orphans who had taken up residence with a householder — arguing that she was acting within her authorized capacity to sell the ox for their support.

The *gemara* then notes: *veha ikar bireshusa delokeach* — but the ox had appreciated in value once it was in the buyer's possession. Moreover, *ve'ada lo nekiti damei* — the money had not yet been paid over. What could or should be done?

### Property as Hekdesh

Rav Nachman ruled on the basis of a principle transmitted by Rav Chanilai bar Idi in the name of Shmuel: *nichsei yetomim harei hen kehekdesh velo makni ela bechespa* — the property of orphans is like *hekdesh*, consecrated property, in that it can be acquired only through the transfer of money. This generates an important practical rule: just as a transaction involving *hekdesh* is not finalized until money changes hands, so too a transaction involving orphans' property.

This contrasts with the general rule for *metaltelin*, where there is a *machlokes* between Rav Yochanan and Reish Lakish — *ma'os konos* versus *eino nikneh ela bemeshicha* — whether acquisition of movable property takes effect through payment of money or through the act of *meshicha*, physically taking possession. For orphans' property, the rule is fixed: only cash completes the transaction, akin to *hekdesh*.

This principle operates in both directions, always to the *yetomim*'s benefit: if the price rises after *meshicha* but before payment, the *yetomim* collect at the higher price. The system is, in effect, rigged in their favor — whatever arrangement yields them the better outcome is the operative one.

### Wine Price Fluctuation

The *gemara* works through several scenarios. *Chamra de-rabna Ukva yatmei mashkhuhu be-arba'ah arba'ah* — wine belonging to the estate of the orphans of Rabna Ukva was drawn (*meshicha* was performed) when it was worth four per barrel. The price then rose to six per barrel. Rav Nachman invoked the ruling of Rav Chanilai bar Idi: since *nichsei yetomim* are like *hekdesh* and are acquired only through money, and money had not yet been paid, the transaction is resolved at the higher price — a very substantial windfall for the *yetomim*.

Conversely: if *meshicha* was performed on behalf of the *yetomim* when they were purchasing produce and the price then dropped — *lo yehei koach hedyot chamur mehekdesh* — the ordinary mechanism of sale must not work worse for them than *hekdesh* does. We give them the benefit of either form of acquisition, whichever is more advantageous.

### Merchant Incentives

This raises a practical concern: if sellers know that the price will always resolve against them when dealing with *yetomim*, people will simply refuse to do business with them. Moreover, applying the *hekdesh* principle in both directions — such that *yetomim* always pay the lower price when buying and receive the higher price when selling — creates a system that merchants will circumvent by waiting until payment and delivery coincide exactly. Rav Shisha, son of Rav Idi, identifies the countervailing risk on each side: if *yetomim* pay cash first before taking possession, the seller can always invoke *nisrefu chitechem ba'aliya* — your wheat burned in the attic — claiming the goods were destroyed before delivery, leaving the *yetomim* with no recourse. The *gemara* works through these tensions carefully, ultimately leaving the general principle of protecting the *yetomim* intact while acknowledging the practical complications.

Rav Ashi adds: he and Rav Kahana signed a document authorizing the sale of a field belonging to orphans to pay a tax, without performing *achrazata* — the public announcement calling for the best possible buyer that is normally required before selling orphan property. The *Nehardai* ruled that for three categories of pressing need — paying taxes, providing food, or funding a burial — *achrazata* is not required. Tosafot notes that the case of burial seems so self-evidently urgent as to be unworthy of explicit mention; the *gemara* must therefore be referring not to purchasing a burial plot itself but to repaying a debt incurred for a burial.

### Amram Tzeva Accusations

*Amram Tzeva* was serving as *apotropos* for orphans. Relatives came before Rav Nachman claiming he was dressing and clothing himself from the orphans' property. Rav Nachman's response was measured: perhaps he is eating and drinking from his own means — and if someone found an unexpectedly valuable lost object (*metzia*), one gives him the benefit of the doubt.

### Removing Negligent Guard…

The *gemara* then asks: but if he is in fact depleting their estate, that is a serious problem. Rav Nachman replied: bring me witnesses that he is causing loss, and I will remove him. The *apotropos* is entitled to a presumption of good faith — and that presumption serves a practical function. If an *apotropos* can be removed on bare accusation, no one will be willing to accept the responsibility.

Rav Huna stated in Rav's name: *apotropos demafsid mesalkinan lei* — an *apotropos* who causes loss to the orphans is removed. This is the subject of a direct *machlokes* among *amora'im*: Rav Huna citing Rav rules that he is removed; the school of Rabbi Shila says he is not removed. The *halacha* follows Rav Huna: he is removed.

## Shvuah for Apotropsin

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *mishnah* rules: *apotropos sheminahu avi yetomim yishava* — an *apotropos* appointed by the father must take a *shvuah*; *minahu beit din lo yishava* — one appointed by *beit din* does not.

### Incentive Rationale

The *gemara* explains the *tanna kamma*'s reasoning. The father-appointed *apotropos* is asked to take a *shvuah* precisely because *i lav de'is lei hana'ah minei lo hava lei apotropos* — if he were not receiving some benefit from the arrangement, he never would have agreed to take it on. The father must have offered him compensation or some special inducement. Accordingly, *mishum shvuah lo ati le'amnuyei* — the prospect of taking an oath at the conclusion of his tenure will not deter him from having accepted the position in the first place; the benefit still outweighs the burden. Therefore the *shvuah* can be imposed without creating a disincentive.

Conversely, the *beit din*-appointed *apotropos* does not take a *shvuah*. His motivation is simply *milta be'alma hu de'avid le'bei dina* — he is doing a favor for the court. *Ve'i ramit lei shvuah ati le'amnuyei* — if we burden him with the obligation of an oath at the end, the negatives will outweigh whatever public benefit he derives, and no one will agree to serve. The *beit din* does not pay its *apotropos*, making the deterrent effect of the *shvuah* far more pronounced.

This discussion invites reflection on how courts today think about foster care arrangements — with relatives versus non-relatives — and the degree to which financial incentives and public recognition affect people's willingness to step into caregiving roles for orphans.

### Abba Shaul Reversal

*Abba Shaul omer chiluf hadvarim* — Abba Shaul reverses the ruling entirely.

### Public Reputation Logic

Abba Shaul's rationale for his reversal: *minuhu beit din yishava be'hahi hana'ah de-nafik lei kala de'inish meheman hu* — when *beit din* appoints you, your appointment becomes publicly known. You become recognized in the community as a person of trustworthiness whom the court has seen fit to select. *Deha samchu lei beit din* — the court has placed its confidence in you. The public reputation you thereby acquire is itself a form of *hana'ah*, and it is sufficient to keep you motivated. Therefore *mishum shvuah lo ati le'amnuyei minehon* — the prospect of taking a *shvuah* will not deter you from having accepted the appointment.

Regarding the father-appointed *apotropos*, Abba Shaul argues the opposite: *milta be'alma hu de'avid le-hahu gavra* — he is doing a favor for a man who is now dead. A private appointment by a deceased father does not generate the same public recognition as a *beit din* appointment. The reputational reward is minimal, and therefore *ve'i ramit lei shvuah ati le'amnuyei* — the burden of a *shvuah* will indeed deter him from having agreed to serve.

### Shmuel's Ruling

Rav Chanan bar Ami reports in the name of Shmuel: *hilchata ke-Abba Shaul* — the *halacha* follows Abba Shaul. The *Rif* indeed rules accordingly.

### Rabbi Elazar's View

However, Tosafot raises a difficulty: a subsequent *beraisa* records that Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov says *zeh vezeh yishava* — both types of *apotropos*, whether appointed by the father or by *beit din*, must take a *shvuah*. The *beraisa* itself concludes: *halacha kidvarav* — the *halacha* is in accordance with his view. This appears to contradict the ruling of Shmuel following Abba Shaul, and Tosafot grapples with how to resolve the tension.

### Paid Guardian Status

Rav Tachlifa bar Ma'arava taught before Rabbi Abbahu: the reason the father-appointed *apotropos* takes a *shvuah* is *mipnei shehu nosei sachar* — because he is a *shomer sachar*, a paid guardian. Rabbi Abbahu challenges the precise formulation: he is not literally a *shomer sachar*; rather, he is *kenosei sachar* — like a paid guardian. He derives a certain *hana'ah* from the arrangement that places him in the functional position of someone who benefits from the *shmirah*, even if not technically a hired watchman.

There is a great deal more to be said about this *daf*. The *Ran* and the *Ra'avad* and *Rambam* on the *dapei ha-Rif* engage in a rich *machlokes* about how the institution of *apotropus* interacts with the various *halakhot* surveyed here. It is, in all, a remarkably dense and jam-packed *daf*.
