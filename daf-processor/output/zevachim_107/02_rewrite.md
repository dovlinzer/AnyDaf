# Zevachim 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechita Chutz

### Shechita—No Explicit Ver…

The daf under discussion opens mid-sugya, picking up from 106b, approximately ten lines down, with the words *ela amar Rav Avin* — though some versions read *ela amar Abaye*. The Gemara's driving question concerns the prohibition of *shechutei ve-ha'alot chutz*: the forbidding of slaughtering or burning an animal as a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* on a private altar. Performing either act renders one liable to *kares*, even when the offering is directed to God. The punishment is stated explicitly in the verses, but the difficulty is that while the Torah explicitly prohibits the act of *ha'ala'ah* — offering up — with the words *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha*, "be careful lest you offer up your *olot*" — no parallel explicit prohibition is stated regarding *shechita* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

This creates a significant legal problem. The principle is that punishment presupposes a prior prohibition, and the implication of the Mishnah's language — "when are you *chayav*?" meaning when would you bring a *korban* for an unintentional transgression — requires both a negative prohibition and *kares* for the willful act. The Gemara has already attempted various approaches and found them inadequate. We now turn to what emerges as the operative solution.

### Rav Avin's Kal Vachomer

*Ela amar Rav Avin* — or *ela amar Abaye* — proposes deriving the prohibition from a *kal vachomer*. The reasoning proceeds as follows. An animal sanctified as a *korban* before the *Mishkan* was built and then slaughtered outside after the *Mishkan* was established is prohibited, even though the offender does not incur *kares* — since the animal was consecrated during a period when private altars were permitted. This is the *mah bimkom she-lo anash hizhir* case: even where there is no *kares*, there is still a prohibition. All the more so, then, *makom she-anash eino din she-yizhir*: in the case where we know *kares* applies — where the animal was sanctified after the *Beit HaMikdash* was built and is then slaughtered outside — surely there must also be a prohibition. The *kal vachomer* appears compelling.

### Ravina's Chelev Challenge

*Amar lei Ravina le-Rav Ashi*: Ravina immediately challenges this line of reasoning. If the Torah can communicate a prohibition through *kal vachomer*, why did it need to explicitly prohibit eating *chelev* — forbidden animal fat — which carries the punishment of *kares*? Let it simply state the *kares* and allow us to infer the prohibition by *kal vachomer* from *neveilah*, the improperly slaughtered animal, which is prohibited but carries no *kares*. Just as the lighter case of *neveilah* is prohibited, the weightier case of *chelev* should certainly be prohibited. *Teisi be-kal vachomer* — let it be derived logically. The fact that the Torah chose to state the prohibition of *chelev* explicitly suggests that *kal vachomer* is insufficient for establishing a prohibition.

### Rava's Chelev Analysis

*Amar lei* Rava: in fact, you cannot derive the prohibition of *chelev* from *neveilah* even if you tried, because the *kal vachomer* can be challenged. *Mah le-neveilah sheken mitama* — *neveilah* causes *tumah*, which is a weightier dimension not shared by *chelev*, so the *kal vachomer* does not hold. The Gemara then pursues a series of alternative bases for the *kal vachomer*: *shratzim temeim*, forbidden rodents that cause *tumah* — also disqualified because they cause *tumah*. *Shratzim tehorim*, forbidden creatures that do not cause *tumah* — disqualified because their prohibition applies even to a quantity smaller than a *ke-zayit*, making them in some respects weightier. *Orlah* and *kilayim* — disqualified because they are forbidden even in benefit. *Shevi'it* — disqualified because its sanctity transfers to money paid for it. *Teruma* — disqualified because there is never a circumstance in which *teruma* is permitted to a non-*kohen*.

And *chelev* itself, though it carries *kares*, is actually more lenient than all these other prohibitions in one respect: the *chelev* of a *chayah* — a deer, for example — is entirely permitted. So *chelev* has a dimension of leniency absent from every comparison case, and therefore *mah le-chelev she-lo hutra miklalo* — the prohibition was never fully universal — and no *kal vachomer* to *chelev* can succeed.

Rava then raises a broader concern: *ei kashya li ha kashya li*. What truly bothers him about using *kal vachomer* to establish a prohibition is the case of *Pesach* and *milah*, which carry *kares* but involve only a failure to perform a positive commandment, not a negative prohibition. With Rav Avin's logic, one might construct a *kal vachomer*: since *notar* — leaving over the *korban Pesach* — is prohibited without *kares*, then *Pesach* itself, which carries *kares*, should certainly generate a negative prohibition. But that is plainly not so. Rav Ashi reports that when he raised this with Rav Kahana, Rav Kahana responded that you cannot construct the *kal vachomer* from *motir* because it too can be challenged: *de-ika li-mifrach mah le-motir sheken ein lo takana* — once food has become *notar* it is ruined with no remedy, whereas the *Pesach* obligation has a remedy in the form of *Pesach sheni*. So again the *kal vachomer* fails on technical grounds.

### Ein Mazhirin Principle

The Gemara steps back from the local debates and states the overarching principle directly: *ve-chi mazhirin min ha-din?* Do we derive that something is prohibited from a *kal vachomer*? *Afilu le-man de-amar onshin min ha-din* — even according to the opinion that punishment may be derived from a *kal vachomer*, once you know something is prohibited you can perhaps determine the severity of the consequence logically. But the basic prohibition itself — *mazhirin min ha-din* — you cannot derive from logic alone.

There is something philosophically apt about this principle. A prohibition, in the framework of Torah law, is a divine command — it derives its force from God saying "do not do this." The use of *kal vachomer* is a human logical operation. While human reasoning has an authorized role in elaborating details and even in deriving consequences, the foundational commandedness of a prohibition cannot rest on human logical inference alone. It must proceed directly from the divine text. We are thus back to the beginning: how do we know *shechita* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is negatively prohibited?

### Gezerah Shavah Solution

*Ela ke-Rabbi Yochanan*, the Gemara concludes: the prohibition is derived through *Rabbi Yochanan*'s principle of *atya havayah havayah* — a *gezerah shavah* linking the two *parshiyot* in Vayikra 17.

The first *parasha* (17:3–4) addresses *shechita*: *ish ish mi-beit Yisrael asher yishchat shor kesev o ez... v'el petach ohel moed lo hevi'o l'hakriv korban la-Shem... dam yechashev la-ish ha-hu dam shafach v'nichrat*. This passage states the *kares* for slaughtering outside but contains no explicit prohibition. The second *parasha* (17:8–9) addresses *ha'ala'ah*: *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach... v'el petach ohel moed lo yavi'enu v'nichrat ha-ish ha-hu me-amav*. This passage, by contrast, is preceded by the explicit prohibition *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha*.

The link is forged through the shared language of *havi'ennu* and *hevi'o* — both expressing the act of bringing to the *ohel moed*. *Ma le-halan lo anash ela im ken hizhir af kan lo anash ela im ken hizhir*: just as by *ha'ala'ah* there is no punishment without a prior prohibition, so too by *shechita* there is no punishment without a prior prohibition — and the *gezerah shavah* transfers that prohibition to the case of *shechita*. Rava offers a parallel derivation *ke-Rabbi Yonah*, through a different *gezerah shavah* based on the word *olatecha*.

The conceptual distinction between this solution and the failed *kal vachomer* is significant. A *gezerah shavah* is understood as encoded within the text itself — it is God's own textual design that links these two passages. A *kal vachomer*, by contrast, is pure human logic applied externally to the text. The prohibition, derived through the *gezerah shavah*, therefore meets the requirement of originating in the divine word rather than in human inference.

## Burning Offerings

### Muktarei Pnim/Chutz

With the prohibition of *shechita* established, the Gemara now turns to a question arising from the debate in the Mishnah. We have established, the Gemara notes, *ashkechan muktarei pnim she-he'elah ba-chutz*: we understand the liability for burning outside things that were fit to be offered inside — i.e., an animal slaughtered within the *Beit HaMikdash* and then burned on an outside altar. In that case, the animal was *ra'uy li-fnim*, fit for the inside, and so the act of burning it outside is clearly a violation.

But the Mishnah's debate with Rabbi Shimon concerns the case of *muktarei chutz she-he'elah ba-chutz*: an animal slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and subsequently burned outside. At the moment of the burning, this animal is no longer fit for the *Beit HaMikdash* — it was invalidated by being slaughtered outside. Rabbi Shimon accordingly rules *patur*. The Gemara asks: *minayin* — from where does the majority position derive that one is nonetheless *chayav*?

### V'Aleihem Tomar

*Amar Rav Kahana amar kra*: the verse says *v'aleihem tomar* (17:8). Reading *aleihem* as *aleihem* — "regarding those" rather than "to them" — Rav Kahana derives *al ha-semuchim tomar*: the prohibition of *ha'alah* stated in the second *parasha* applies also "regarding" the very things described in the preceding *parasha* — namely, animals slaughtered outside. The *vav* of *v'aleihem tomar* connects the two *parshiyot*, indicating that the prohibition of burning outside applies even to animals that were slaughtered outside and thus are not fit for inside.

### 37th Kareis Question

*Maskif lei Rava*: Rava challenges this. *Mi ksiv v'aleihem? V'aleihem ksiv* — the word is actually *va'aleihem*, "and to them," not *v'aleihem* in the sense of "regarding them," so the textual basis for Rav Kahana's reading is questionable. The Gemara therefore pivots to the formulation taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *v'aleihem tomar le-arev parshiyot* — the *vav* connecting the two sections links them, so that the prohibition of burning outside encompasses also the case of animals slaughtered outside.

Rabbi Yochanan offers an alternative derivation through a *gezerah shavah* on the word for "bringing": *lo yavi'enu* in the second *parasha* and *lo hevi'o* in the first. *Mah le-halan muktarei chutz* — just as the first *parasha* concerns animals slaughtered outside, so too the second *parasha*, through this link, includes animals slaughtered outside.

Rav Bibi then raises an objection. The Mishnah states that there are 36 categories in the Torah for which one incurs *kares*. If we now distinguish between burning something slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash* (derived from the plain meaning of the verse) and burning something slaughtered outside (derived through a separate *derasha*), these should constitute two distinct prohibitions — and the count should reach 37. *D'ika ha-ma'aleh ve-ha-ma'aleh* — there is one *ma'aleh* for each sub-category, and treating them as separate derivations implies they are separate sins.

### One Prohibition Unity

The Gemara marks this difficulty with *kashya*, acknowledging the force of the objection. The underlying resolution implicit in the discussion is that although the two cases are derived from different textual sources, they constitute a single prohibition with two applications: the verse — read through the connecting *vav* or through the *gezerah shavah* — encompasses both within the scope of one prohibition. This is consistent with Rabbi Shimon's position as the background to the whole discussion: the Rabbanan hold that the *derasha* extends the prohibition to include *muktarei chutz*, but the extension belongs to the same prohibition, not a new one.

## Sprinkling Blood

### Zerika—New Category

The Gemara now introduces a new question. We have established *chiyuv* for *shechita* outside and *ha'alah* outside. But what about *zerika* — sprinkling the blood — performed outside the *Beit HaMikdash*? The next Mishnah will teach that one who sprinkles some of the blood outside is *chayav*. *Mina lan* — what is the source?

### Rabbi Yishmael—Dam

*Nafkha lei mi-de-is-tanya*: it is derived from a *baraita*. In the verse concerning *shechita* (17:4), it states *dam yechashev la-ish ha-hu dam shafach* — it is reckoned to him as the spilling of blood. Rabbi Yishmael focuses on this emphasis on *dam* — blood — and derives from it *le-rabbot ha-zorek*: to include the one who sprinkles the blood outside. The textual emphasis on blood in the context of *shechita* extends the prohibition to encompass the blood-ritual of *zerika* as well. This categorizes *zerika* together with *shechita*.

### Rabbi Akiva—Or

Rabbi Akiva derives *zerika* from a different verse entirely — the *parasha* of *ha'alah* (17:8–9): *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach*. The word *o* ("or") before *zavach* is read as an inclusion: *le-rabbot et ha-zorek* — to include the act of sprinkling blood. This derivation categorizes *zerika* together with *ha'alah*.

*Ve-Rabbi Yishmael hai o zavach mai avid lei?* What does Rabbi Yishmael do with the word *o zavach*? *Le-chalek* — it serves its normal grammatical function, indicating that one is *chayav* for either an *olah* or a *shelamim* independently, not only if both are offered together. Rabbi Yishmael has no need to derive *zerika* from this word because he already has *dam yechashev*.

### Zerika Grouping

*Ve-Rabbi Akiva le-chalek mena lei?* If Rabbi Akiva uses *o* for *zerika*, where does he derive that one is *chayav* for either offering independently? *Nafka lei mi-lo yavi'enu* — the verse says *lo yavi'enu* in the singular rather than *lo yevi'am* in the plural, teaching that even a single *korban* brought outside generates liability, without requiring two.

Rabbi Yishmael, in turn, uses *lo yavi'enu* for a different lesson: *al ha-shalem hu chayav ve-eino chayav al ha-chaser* — one is liable only if he burns a whole animal, not an incomplete one. And Rabbi Akiva derives the "wholeness" requirement from the phrase *la'asot oto* — "to make it" — which implies completeness.

This generates a further distinction between the two positions. Since Rabbi Yishmael uses both *lo yavi'enu* and *la'asot oto* to derive the "wholeness" requirement, he applies it in two distinct scenarios. *Ve-Rabbi Yishmael chad be-muktarei pnim she-chisru ve-he'elu ba-chutz ve-chad be-muktarei chutz she-chisru ve-he'elu ba-chutz* — one verse excludes the case of an animal fit for inside that was rendered incomplete and then burned outside; the other excludes the case of an animal fit only for outside that was rendered incomplete and then burned outside. In both scenarios, liability requires that the animal be whole.

### Shalem—Wholeness

A *baraita* elaborates Rabbi Yishmael's position. *Yachol muktarei pnim she-chisru ve-he'elu ba-chutz chayav* — one might have thought that for an animal slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, even if it became incomplete before being burned outside, there would still be *chiyuv*. Rashi explains why: within the *Beit HaMikdash*, if a piece of an *olah* falls off the altar it is replaced; the norm for *muktarei pnim* is to continue burning even partial pieces. One might have thought this logic carries over to the case of burning outside. Therefore the Torah teaches: *talmud lomar la'asot oto* — liability applies only to burning the complete animal. Whether it was slaughtered inside or outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, one is exempt if only a partial animal is burned outside.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. Having used *lo yavi'enu* to derive "one rather than two" and having only *la'asot oto* for the wholeness requirement, he has only one verse covering only one scenario. He would therefore rule that for *muktarei pnim* — an animal slaughtered inside — if it was incomplete and burned outside, one would still be *chayav*. The two Tannaim thus dispute both the derivation of *zerika* and the scope of the "wholeness" requirement.

The accounting of verses continues further. Rabbi Akiva uses *dam yechashev* to include *shechitat ha-of* — slaughtering a bird sacrifice outside — since one might have thought that since birds are normally processed by *melika* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, *shechita* of a bird outside would generate no liability. Rabbi Yishmael derives this from the repetition of the verb *yishchat* in the verse (*asher yishchat ba-machaneh o asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machaneh*), the doubled usage being unnecessary and therefore coming to include bird *shechita*.

Both agree, however, that *melika* of a bird performed outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is exempt: Rabbi Yishmael derives this from *zeh ha-davar asher yishchat* — "this is the matter, of slaughtering" — the word *zeh* restricting the prohibition to *shechita* alone. Rabbi Akiva derives it from *lo yavi'enu* read as *al ha-shochet hu chayav ve-lo al ha-moleik*.

## Multiple Violations

### Shachat Vezarak

Rabbi Abbahu now draws out the practical implications of the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva regarding the categorization of *zerika*. The question is: if someone, in a single state of forgetfulness that the prohibition existed, performed both *shechita* and *zerika* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, how many *chatat* offerings must he bring upon realizing his error?

*Shachat ve-zarak le-divrei Rabbi Yishmael chayav achat le-divrei Rabbi Akiva chayav shtayim.* Since Rabbi Yishmael derives *zerika* from the *parasha* of *shechita* — specifically from *dam yechashev* — he categorizes *zerika* together with *shechita* as a single prohibition. Performing both in one state of forgetfulness constitutes one sin committed twice, and only one *korban* is required. Rabbi Akiva, who derives *zerika* from the *parasha* of *ha'alah*, categorizes it together with burning — and therefore *shechita* and *zerika* constitute two distinct prohibitions, requiring two *korbanot*.

### Abaye's Unification

Abaye dissents from this framing of the Rabbi Akiva position. *Afilu le-divrei Rabbi Akiva eino chayav ela achat* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, one brings only one *korban* for *shechita* and *zerika* combined. Although Rabbi Akiva derives *zerika* from the verse of *ha'alah*, he nevertheless agrees on the conceptual categorization: *amar kra sham ta'aseh ve-sham ta'aleh* — the verse in Devarim states "there you shall perform your *korbanot*" and "there you shall offer up," distinguishing between a general category of ritual performance (*ta'aseh*) and the specific act of burning (*ta'aleh*). *Shechita* and *zerika*, being ritual acts other than burning, fall within the *ta'aseh* category and are thus grouped together as one type of prohibition, regardless of which verse derives *zerika*. *Kasav asiya le-khulhu avoda achat* — the Torah's formulation of *asiyah* makes all acts other than burning into a single *avoda* category.

### Zarak Veha'ela

The same reasoning applies in reverse. *Zarak ve-he'ela* — one who sprinkled blood and then burned the offering outside in a single state of forgetfulness: *le-divrei Rabbi Yishmael chayav shtayim* — for Rabbi Yishmael, since *zerika* is categorized with *shechita* and *ha'alah* is its own category, these are two distinct prohibitions and require two *korbanot*. *Le-divrei Rabbi Akiva eino chayav ela achat* — for Rabbi Akiva (on the non-Abaye reading), since *zerika* is derived from the *parasha* of *ha'alah*, the two are effectively one category, and only one *korban* is required.

Abaye again corrects: *afilu le-divrei Rabbi Akiva chayav shtayim* — even Rabbi Akiva holds that *zerika* and *ha'alah* are two distinct sins. The verse *sham ta'aleh ve-sham ta'aseh* separates *ha'alah* from all other ritual acts. However *zerika* is derived textually, it conceptually belongs to the *ta'aseh* category, not to *ta'aleh*, and therefore *zerika* and *ha'alah* remain two distinct prohibitions. *Ve-hachi pelaginhu kra* — this is how the verse itself divides them.

### All Three Violations

The final case: *ve-shachat ve-zarak ve-he'ela* — one who performed all three acts in a single state of forgetfulness. *Le-divrei ha-kol chayav shtayim* — according to all opinions, he brings two *korbanot*. Wherever *zerika* is categorized — whether with *shechita* (Rabbi Yishmael, or Rabbi Akiva per Abaye) or with *ha'alah* (Rabbi Akiva on the non-Abaye reading) — the three acts span both categories. In either case, one category is represented twice and the other once, yielding two distinct sins committed, and therefore two *korbanot*.

## Geographic Scope

### Three Camps Question

With the categories of prohibited acts now fully analyzed, the Gemara turns to the geographic question: where must the act take place for liability to arise? The relevant verse reads *asher yishchat ba-machaneh o asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machaneh* — "who slaughters in the camp or who slaughters outside the camp." The phrase *mi-chutz la-machaneh* normally denotes a location fully outside all three *machanos* — the *machaneh Shechinah*, *machaneh Leviyah*, and *machaneh Yisrael*, corresponding in the context of Jerusalem to the *azarah*, *Har HaBayis*, and Jerusalem itself. But the verse also includes *ba-machaneh* — in the camp — which implies that even slaughtering within the camp environs generates *chiyuv*, as long as it is not at the *petach ohel moed*.

### Shechita Ba'Darom

A *baraita* is introduced: *asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machaneh — yachol chutz li-shlosh machanos?* Perhaps *mi-chutz la-machaneh* means only fully outside all three camps, and one who slaughters within any of them is exempt? The verse therefore also says *ba-machaneh* — even within the camp — to establish that liability extends to locations within the camp perimeter. But this raises the converse question: *yachol ha-shochet olah ba-darom yei chayav?* Perhaps the inclusion of *ba-machaneh* means one is even *chayav* for slaughtering an *olah* in the southern section of the *azarah*?

This is not as strange as it sounds. The southern section of the *azarah* is not a valid location for the *shechita* of *kodshei kodshim*, including an *olah*, though it is valid for *kodshim kalim* like *shelamim*. One might have thought: since the verse says *ba-machaneh* to include locations within the camp, perhaps slaughtering an *olah* in the south — a place unfit for it — should generate liability for *shechutei chutz*. The *baraita* answers: *mi-chutz la-machaneh meyuchad she-ein ra'uy li-shchitat kodshim u-le-shchitat kol zevach* — the model for liability is a location unfit for the slaughter of *any* sacrifice whatsoever. The south, while unfit for *kodshei kodshim*, remains fit for *kodshim kalim*. Therefore it does not fall within the prohibition, and one who slaughters an *olah* there is not liable for *shechutei chutz* — though the *korban* is invalidated.

### Roof of Heichal

*Amar Ulla: ha-shochet al gago shel Heichal chayav.* Ulla proposes that if someone slaughters a *korban* on the roof of the *Heichal*, he is *chayav*. The roof of the *Heichal* is certainly not a valid location for any *shechita*, and therefore it would seem to fit the criterion of a place unfit for the slaughter of all sacrifices — thus generating liability for *shechutei chutz*. Ulla adds: *ho'il ve-ra'uy li-shchitat kol zevach* — since, had the act been performed inside, it would have been valid for any sacrifice.

### Petach Ohel Moed

*Matkif lah Rava*: Rava challenges Ulla. *Im ken nichtov kra mi-chutz la-machaneh ve-lo ba'ei petach ohel moed* — if geographic location outside any permitted zone were the sole criterion, the Torah should have simply stated "outside the camp" and had no need to add the phrase *v'el petach ohel moed lo hevi'o* — "he did not bring it to the entrance of the *ohel moed*." The fact that the verse specifies *petach ohel moed* indicates that the operative question is not merely where the act occurred but whether the animal was brought to the *ohel moed*. An animal on the roof of the *Heichal* is within the airspace and zone of the *ohel moed* — it was, in a meaningful sense, brought there. Therefore *petach ohel moed lamah li? Lav le-mi'utei gagot* — the reference to *petach ohel moed* is precisely to exclude the roof, teaching that one who slaughters on the roof is *patur*.

### Animal in/out Scope

*U-le-Rava im ken nichtov petach ohel moed ba-machaneh u-mi-chutz la-machaneh lamah li?* The Gemara presses back on Rava: if *petach ohel moed* does the work on its own, why does the verse need to specify both *ba-machaneh* and *mi-chutz la-machaneh*? What do those geographic qualifications add?

*Amar Rav Mari: la'atuyei kullah bifnim ve-tzavara ba-chutz* — they come to include the case where the body of the animal is standing inside the *azarah* while its neck protrudes outside, where the *shechita* occurs. In such a case, one might have argued that since the animal was brought into the *ohel moed*, there is no liability. The *ba-machaneh u-mi-chutz la-machaneh* teaches that one is nonetheless *chayav*.

The Gemara objects: *tzavara ba-chutz peshita* — that case is obvious. The act of *shechita* occurs at the neck, and the neck is outside. Even with the *petach ohel moed* verse alone, one would know that *shechita* occurring outside is the operative act. *Ela la-atuyei kullah ba-chutz ve-tzavara bifnim* — rather, the geographic qualifications come to include the reverse scenario: the body of the animal stands outside while its neck is inside the *azarah*. Without *ba-machaneh u-mi-chutz la-machaneh*, one might have argued that since the *shechita* itself occurs inside — at the neck, which is within the *ohel moed* — the offender should be exempt. The verse teaches that even here, since the bulk of the animal remains outside, one is *chayav*.

In summary, the geographic parameters work together: the references to *ba-machaneh* and *mi-chutz la-machaneh* clarify that *chiyuv* applies whether the location of the act is close to the *Beit HaMikdash* or far — even on *Har HaBayit* or in Jerusalem itself, the act is prohibited — and they further teach the edge cases involving an animal straddling the boundary of the *azarah*. The *petach ohel moed* language, for its part, excludes acts performed fully within the sanctified precinct, including, according to Rava, those performed on the roof of the *Heichal*, which falls within the zone of the *ohel moed*. Whether Ulla or Rava is correct on that final point remains the subject of the *itmar* that will be taken up in the next daf.
