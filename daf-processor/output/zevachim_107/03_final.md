# Zevachim 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechita Chutz

### Shechita—No Explicit Ver…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא עָנַשׁ – הִזְהִיר, מְקוֹם שֶׁעָנַשׁ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהִזְהִיר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Avin says:** The prohibition against slaughtering an offering outside the Temple is derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as in a case** in **which** the Torah **did not prescribe punishment** for a certain action, it nevertheless **prohibited** it, as is the case with regard to sacrificing outside the Temple an offering consecrated while there was permission to sacrifice on private altars, so too, in **a case** in **which** the Torah **did prescribe punishment** for a certain action, as is the case with regard to slaughtering outside the Temple an offering consecrated while it was prohibited to sacrifice on private altars, **is it not logical that** the Torah **prohibited** the action?

The daf under discussion opens mid-sugya, picking up from 106b, approximately ten lines down, with the words *ela amar Rav Avin* — though some versions read *ela amar Abaye*. The Gemara's driving question concerns the prohibition of *shechutei ve-ha'alot chutz*: the forbidding of slaughtering or burning an animal as a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* on a private altar. Performing either act renders one liable to *kares*, even when the offering is directed to God. The punishment is stated explicitly in the verses, but the difficulty is that while the Torah explicitly prohibits the act of *ha'ala'ah* — offering up — with the words *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha*, "be careful lest you offer up your *olot*" — no parallel explicit prohibition is stated regarding *shechita* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

This creates a significant legal problem. The principle is that punishment presupposes a prior prohibition, and the implication of the Mishnah's language — "when are you *chayav*?" meaning when would you bring a *korban* for an unintentional transgression — requires both a negative prohibition and *kares* for the willful act. The Gemara has already attempted various approaches and found them inadequate. We now turn to what emerges as the operative solution.

### Rav Avin's Kal Vachomer

*Ela amar Rav Avin* — or *ela amar Abaye* — proposes deriving the prohibition from a *kal vachomer*. The reasoning proceeds as follows. An animal sanctified as a *korban* before the *Mishkan* was built and then slaughtered outside after the *Mishkan* was established is prohibited, even though the offender does not incur *kares* — since the animal was consecrated during a period when private altars were permitted. This is the *mah bimkom she-lo anash hizhir* case: even where there is no *kares*, there is still a prohibition. All the more so, then, *makom she-anash eino din she-yizhir*: in the case where we know *kares* applies — where the animal was sanctified after the *Beit HaMikdash* was built and is then slaughtered outside — surely there must also be a prohibition. The *kal vachomer* appears compelling.

### Ravina's Chelev Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִם כֵּן, לֹא יֵאָמֵר לָאו בְּחֵלֶב – וְתֵיתֵי קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִנְּבֵילָה: מָה נְבֵילָה שֶׁלֹּא עָנַשׁ – הִזְהִיר, חֵלֶב שֶׁעָנַשׁ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהִזְהִיר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: If so,** that whenever the Torah states a punishment for a certain action, there is no need for it to state the prohibition, then **let** the Torah **not state a prohibition with regard to** eating forbidden **fat** of a kosher animal, **and** then **derive** the fact that it is prohibited through **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the prohibition against eating **an unslaughtered animal carcass: Just as** with regard to **a carcass,** even though the Torah **did not prescribe punishment** for consuming it, it nevertheless **prohibited** consuming it, so too, with regard to animal **fat,** for **which** the Torah **did prescribe punishment, is it not logical that** the Torah **prohibited** its consumption?

Ravina immediately challenges this line of reasoning. If the Torah can communicate a prohibition through *kal vachomer*, why did it need to explicitly prohibit eating *chelev* — forbidden animal fat — which carries the punishment of *kares*? Let it simply state the *kares* and allow us to infer the prohibition by *kal vachomer* from *neveilah*, the improperly slaughtered animal, which is prohibited but carries no *kares*. Just as the lighter case of *neveilah* is prohibited, the weightier case of *chelev* should certainly be prohibited. *Teisi be-kal vachomer* — let it be derived logically. The fact that the Torah chose to state the prohibition of *chelev* explicitly suggests that *kal vachomer* is insufficient for establishing a prohibition.

### Rava's Chelev Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִנְּבֵילָה נָמֵי לָא אָתְיָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לִנְבֵילָה שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא! מִשְּׁרָצִים טְמֵאִין. מָה לִשְׁרָצִים טְמֵאִים – שֶׁמְּטַמְּאִין בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ! מִשְּׁרָצִים טְהוֹרִים. מָה לִשְׁרָצִים טְהוֹרִים – שֶׁאִיסּוּרָן בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ! מֵעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם. מָה לְעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם – שֶׁכֵּן אֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה! מִשְּׁבִיעִית. מָה לִשְׁבִיעִית, שֶׁכֵּן תּוֹפֶסֶת דָּמֶיהָ! מִתְּרוּמָה – שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלָהּ! מִכּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי – שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתְּרוּ מִכְּלָלָן!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **came before Rava** and **said to him:** The prohibition against eating animal fat **can also not be derived from** the prohibition of **a carcass, as** the *a fortiori* inference **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is notable **about a carcass?** It is notable **in that** it **renders** other items **ritually impure** through contact with it. Forbidden fat does not share this stringency. Can one derive it **from** the prohibition against eating carcasses of **ritually impure creeping animals** (see Leviticus 11:41)? One cannot, as **what** is notable **about ritually impure creeping animals?** They are notable in **that they render** other items **ritually impure though** contact with **any amount** of them. Forbidden fat does not share this stringency. Can one derive it **from** the prohibition against eating the carcasses of **ritually pure creeping animals** (see Leviticus 11:41), which do not have the capacity to render other items ritually impure? One cannot, as **what** is notable **about ritually pure creeping animals?** They are notable in **that** with regard to **their prohibition** there is liability **for** consuming **any amount** of them. Forbidden fat does not share this stringency, as one is liable only if he eats an olive-bulk of it. Can one derive it **from** the prohibition against eating **the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*] or** from the prohibition against eating **diverse kinds in a vineyard,** for which one is liable only if he eats an olive-bulk of them, just like with regard to animal fat? One cannot, as **what** is notable **about *orla* and diverse kinds in a vineyard?** They are notable **in that they are items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.** Forbidden fat does not share this stringency. Can one derive it **from** the prohibition against eating **Sabbatical** Year produce, from which benefit is permitted just like with regard to animal fat? One cannot, as **what** is notable **about Sabbatical** Year produce? It is notable **in that it transfers** its sanctity to the **money** with which **it** is purchased. Forbidden fat does not share this stringency. Can one derive it **from** the prohibition of a non-priest partaking of **the portion of the produce that is designated for the priest [*teruma*]?** One cannot, **as** in the case of *teruma* there are **no** circumstances in which **its general prohibition was permitted.** The prohibition concerning *teruma* applies broadly, unlike in the case of forbidden fat, where the prohibition applies only to the fat of domesticated animals but not to the fat of undomesticated animals. Rava notes that the possibility of deriving the prohibition against eating forbidden fat **from all of those** prohibitions can **also** be refuted based on this claim, **as** in those cases, too, there are **no** circumstances in which **their general prohibition was permitted.**

*Amar lei* Rava: in fact, you cannot derive the prohibition of *chelev* from *neveilah* even if you tried, because the *kal vachomer* can be challenged. *Mah le-neveilah sheken mitama* — *neveilah* causes *tumah*, which is a weightier dimension not shared by *chelev*, so the *kal vachomer* does not hold. The Gemara then pursues a series of alternative bases for the *kal vachomer*: *shratzim temeim*, forbidden rodents that cause *tumah* — also disqualified because they cause *tumah*. *Shratzim tehorim*, forbidden creatures that do not cause *tumah* — disqualified because their prohibition applies even to a quantity smaller than a *ke-zayit*, making them in some respects weightier. *Orlah* and *kilayim* — disqualified because they are forbidden even in benefit. *Shevi'it* — disqualified because its sanctity transfers to money paid for it. *Teruma* — disqualified because there is never a circumstance in which *teruma* is permitted to a non-*kohen*.

And *chelev* itself, though it carries *kares*, is actually more lenient than all these other prohibitions in one respect: the *chelev* of a *chayah* — a deer, for example — is entirely permitted. So *chelev* has a dimension of leniency absent from every comparison case, and therefore *mah le-chelev she-lo hutra miklalo* — the prohibition was never fully universal — and no *kal vachomer* to *chelev* can succeed.

### Further Challenges to Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לִי הָא קַשְׁיָא לִי – הָא דִּתְנַן: פֶּסַח וּמִילָה מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה, תֵּיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִמּוֹתִיר: מָה מוֹתִיר שֶׁלֹּא עָנַשׁ – הִזְהִיר, פֶּסַח וּמִילָה שֶׁעָנַשׁ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהִזְהִיר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If** there is **a difficulty for me** in accepting Rabbi Avin's claim that the prohibition against slaughtering an offering outside the Temple can be derived via an *a fortiori* inference, **this** is **difficult for me: That which we learned** in a mishna (*Karetot* 2a), which enumerates the thirty-six cases for which one is liable to receive *karet*. The end of that mishna lists: One who neglects sacrificing the **Paschal offering and** one who does not undergo **circumcision,** which are **positive mitzvot,** unlike the other cases enumerated in the mishna, which are all prohibitions. Rava explains his difficulty: If Rabbi Avin is correct, **derive** that there is a prohibition against neglecting the sacrifice of the Paschal offering and not undergoing circumcision **via an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **one who leaves over** sacrificial meat beyond the allotted period for its consumption (see Leviticus 22:30): **Just as** in the case of **one who leaves over** sacrificial meat, **where** the Torah **did not prescribe punishment** but nevertheless **prohibited** this act, so too, with regard to neglecting the sacrifice of the Paschal offering and not undergoing circumcision, for **which** the Torah **did prescribe punishment, is it not logical that** the Torah actually **prohibited** neglecting them as well?

Rava then raises a broader concern: *ei kashya li ha kashya li*. What truly bothers him about using *kal vachomer* to establish a prohibition is the case of *Pesach* and *milah*, which carry *kares* but involve only a failure to perform a positive commandment, not a negative prohibition. With Rav Avin's logic, one might construct a *kal vachomer*: since *notar* — leaving over the *korban Pesach* — is prohibited without *kares*, then *Pesach* itself, which carries *kares*, should certainly generate a negative prohibition. But that is plainly not so. Rav Ashi reports that when he raised this with Rav Kahana, Rav Kahana responded that you cannot construct the *kal vachomer* from *motir* because it too can be challenged: *de-ika li-mifrach mah le-motir sheken ein lo takana* — once food has become *notar* it is ruined with no remedy, whereas the *Pesach* obligation has a remedy in the form of *Pesach sheni*. So again the *kal vachomer* fails on technical grounds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְאָמַר לִי: מִמּוֹתִיר לָא אָתְיָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְמוֹתִיר – שֶׁכֵּן אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה, תֹּאמַר פֶּסַח – יֵשׁ לוֹ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stated this** very **discussion before Rav Kahana, and he said to me: One cannot derive** a prohibition against neglecting the sacrifice of the Paschal offering **from** the case of **one who leaves over** sacrificial meat via an *a fortiori* inference, **as it can be refuted: What** is notable **about one who leaves over** sacrificial meat? He is notable **in that there is no remedy** once the prohibition has been violated. **Shall you say** the same about the neglect of the **Paschal offering,** for which **there is a remedy?** One who fails to bring it on the first *Pesaḥ* must bring it on the second *Pesaḥ*.

### Ein Mazhirin Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין?! אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין, אֵין מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the very premise of Rabbi Avin's claim: **But can one** derive that the Torah **prohibits** an action **via** an *a fortiori* **inference? Even the one who says** that the court **administers punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference** concedes that **one does not** derive **a prohibition from** an *a fortiori* **inference.** Therefore, Rabbi Avin's claim is refuted.

The Gemara steps back from the local debates and states the overarching principle directly: *ve-chi mazhirin min ha-din?* Do we derive that something is prohibited from a *kal vachomer*? *Afilu le-man de-amar onshin min ha-din* — even according to the opinion that punishment may be derived from a *kal vachomer*, once you know something is prohibited you can perhaps determine the severity of the consequence logically. But the basic prohibition itself — *mazhirin min ha-din* — you cannot derive from logic alone.

There is something philosophically apt about this principle. A prohibition, in the framework of Torah law, is a divine command — it derives its force from God saying "do not do this." The use of *kal vachomer* is a human logical operation. While human reasoning has an authorized role in elaborating details and even in deriving consequences, the foundational commandedness of a prohibition cannot rest on human logical inference alone. It must proceed directly from the divine text. We are thus back to the beginning: how do we know *shechita* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is negatively prohibited?

### Gezerah Shavah Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: אָתְיָא הֲבָאָה–הֲבָאָה; מָה לְהַלָּן לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר, אַף כָּאן לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the prohibition against slaughtering an offering outside the Temple courtyard can be derived **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says:** It is **derived** from the prohibition against offering up outside the Temple through a verbal analogy between the reference to **bringing** stated with regard to slaughtering outside the Temple, and the reference to **bringing** stated with regard to offering up outside the Temple. With regard to slaughtering, it is stated: "Or that slaughters it outside the camp, and he did not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 17:3–4), and with regard to offering up, it is stated: "That offers up a burnt offering or sacrifice, and he will not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 17:8–9). The verbal analogy teaches that **just as there,** with regard to offering up, the Torah **did not prescribe punishment** for an action **unless** it also explicitly **prohibited** the action, **so too here,** with regard to slaughtering, the Torah **did not prescribe punishment unless** it also **prohibited** it. Therefore, even though the Torah does not explicitly state the prohibition, it is evident that it is prohibited.

*Ela ke-Rabbi Yochanan*, the Gemara concludes: the prohibition is derived through *Rabbi Yochanan*'s principle of *atya havayah havayah* — a *gezerah shavah* linking the two *parshiyot* in Vayikra 17.

The first *parasha* (17:3–4) addresses *shechita*: *ish ish mi-beit Yisrael asher yishchat shor kesev o ez... v'el petach ohel moed lo hevi'o l'hakriv korban la-Shem... dam yechashev la-ish ha-hu dam shafach v'nichrat*. This passage states the *kares* for slaughtering outside but contains no explicit prohibition. The second *parasha* (17:8–9) addresses *ha'ala'ah*: *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach... v'el petach ohel moed lo yavi'enu v'nichrat ha-ish ha-hu me-amav*. This passage, by contrast, is preceded by the explicit prohibition *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha*.

The link is forged through the shared language of *havi'ennu* and *hevi'o* — both expressing the act of bringing to the *ohel moed*. *Ma le-halan lo anash ela im ken hizhir af kan lo anash ela im ken hizhir*: just as by *ha'ala'ah* there is no punishment without a prior prohibition, so too by *shechita* there is no punishment without a prior prohibition — and the *gezerah shavah* transfers that prohibition to the case of *shechita*. Rava offers a parallel derivation *ke-Rabbi Yonah*, through a different *gezerah shavah* based on the word *olatecha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כִּדְרַבִּי יוֹנָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָה: אָתְיָא ״שָׁם״–״שָׁם״; מָה לְהַלָּן לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר, אַף כָּאן לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The prohibition can be derived **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yona, as Rabbi Yona says:** It is **derived** from the verse: "But in the place that the Lord will choose in one of your tribes, there you shall offer up your burnt offerings and there you shall do all that I command you" (Deuteronomy 12:14), through the juxtaposition of the word **"there"** in the first part of the verse to the word **"there"** in the second part of the verse. This serves to juxtapose the offering up, mentioned in the first part, to the sacrifice of an offering, mentioned in the second part, which includes slaughtering it. Accordingly, it teaches that **just as there,** with regard to offering up, the Torah **did not prescribe punishment unless** it also **prohibited** it, **so too here,** with regard to slaughtering, the Torah **did not prescribe punishment unless** it also **prohibited** it. Therefore, even though the Torah does not explicitly state the prohibition, it is evident that it is prohibited.

The conceptual distinction between this solution and the failed *kal vachomer* is significant. A *gezerah shavah* is understood as encoded within the text itself — it is God's own textual design that links these two passages. A *kal vachomer*, by contrast, is pure human logic applied externally to the text. The prohibition, derived through the *gezerah shavah*, therefore meets the requirement of originating in the divine word rather than in human inference.

## Burning Offerings

### Muktarei Pnim/Chutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן מוּקְטְרֵי פְּנִים שֶׁהֶעֱלָן לַחוּץ; מוּקְטְרֵי חוּץ שֶׁהֶעֱלָן לַחוּץ מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara (106a) states that the source for the liability for offering up outside the Temple is the verse: "Any man…that offers up a burnt offering or sacrifice, and he will not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, to sacrifice it to the Lord, and that man shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 17:8–9). The Gemara asks: **We have found** that one is liable only for offerings that are fit to be **burned inside** the Temple courtyard, having been appropriately slaughtered inside the Temple courtyard, **which** one then **offered up outside** the courtyard. The verse states: "He will not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting," which indicates that they were fit to be brought to the Temple. **From where** is it derived that one is also liable with regard to offerings that are unfit and are to be **burned outside** the Temple as they were unlawfully slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, **that** one then **offered up outside?**

With the prohibition of *shechita* established, the Gemara now turns to a question arising from the debate in the Mishnah. We have established, the Gemara notes, *ashkechan muktarei pnim she-he'elah ba-chutz*: we understand the liability for burning outside things that were fit to be offered inside — i.e., an animal slaughtered within the *Beit HaMikdash* and then burned on an outside altar. In that case, the animal was *ra'uy li-fnim*, fit for the inside, and so the act of burning it outside is clearly a violation.

But the Mishnah's debate with Rabbi Shimon concerns the case of *muktarei chutz she-he'elah ba-chutz*: an animal slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and subsequently burned outside. At the moment of the burning, this animal is no longer fit for the *Beit HaMikdash* — it was invalidated by being slaughtered outside. Rabbi Shimon accordingly rules *patur*. The Gemara asks: *minayin* — from where does the majority position derive that one is nonetheless *chayav*?

### V'Aleihem Tomar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וַאֲלֵהֶם תֹּאמַר״ – עַל הַסְּמוּכִין תֹּאמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** The beginning of that **verse states: "And to them [*va'alehem*] you shall say"** (Leviticus 17:8). The term "*alehem*," to them, written with the letter *alef*, is phonetically similar to the term *alehem*, about them, written with the letter *ayin*. Therefore, the verse can be understood as saying: **About that which is** written in the **adjacent** passage **you shall say.** The preceding passage discusses offerings that were slaughtered outside the Temple, so the liability for offering up outside the Temple mentioned in this verse is also referring to those offerings.

*Amar Rav Kahana amar kra*: the verse says *v'aleihem tomar* (17:8). Reading *aleihem* as *aleihem* — "regarding those" rather than "to them" — Rav Kahana derives *al ha-semuchim tomar*: the prohibition of *ha'alah* stated in the second *parasha* applies also "regarding" the very things described in the preceding *parasha* — namely, animals slaughtered outside. The *vav* of *v'aleihem tomar* connects the two *parshiyot*, indicating that the prohibition of burning outside applies even to animals that were slaughtered outside and thus are not fit for inside.

### Further Derivations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: מִי כְּתִיב ״וַעֲלֵיהֶם״?! ״וַאֲלֵיהֶם״ כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וַאֲלֵהֶם תֹּאמַר״ – לְעָרֵב פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this: Is it written: And about them? "And to them" is written,** and it means that the command should be relayed to Aaron, his sons, and the Jewish people, who are mentioned in the beginning of the passage. **Rather,** liability in this case is derived **as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The opening phrase: **"And to them you shall say,"** starts with the conjunction "and" **to mix** the **passages** of slaughtering outside the Temple and offering up outside the Temple, in order to teach that one is liable for the latter even after having done the former.

*Maskif lei Rava*: Rava challenges this. *Mi ksiv v'aleihem? V'aleihem ksiv* — the word is actually *va'aleihem*, "and to them," not *v'aleihem* in the sense of "regarding them," so the textual basis for Rav Kahana's reading is questionable. The Gemara therefore pivots to the formulation taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *v'aleihem tomar le-arev parshiyot* — the *vav* connecting the two sections links them, so that the prohibition of burning outside encompasses also the case of animals slaughtered outside.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אָתְיָא הֲבָאָה–הֲבָאָה; מָה לְהַלָּן מוּקְטְרֵי חוּץ, אַף כָּאן מוּקְטְרֵי חוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Liability in this case **is derived** through a verbal analogy between the reference to **bringing** stated with regard to slaughtering (see Leviticus 17:4), and the reference to **bringing** stated with regard to offering up (see Leviticus 17:9). The verbal analogy teaches that **just as there,** with regard to slaughtering, one is liable for offerings that are going to be **burned outside** the Temple, since one slaughtered them there, **so too here,** with regard to offering up, one is liable even for offerings that are unfit and so will be **burned outside** the Temple, having been slaughtered there.

Rabbi Yochanan offers an alternative derivation through a *gezerah shavah* on the word for "bringing": *lo yavi'enu* in the second *parasha* and *lo hevi'o* in the first. *Mah le-halan muktarei chutz* — just as the first *parasha* concerns animals slaughtered outside, so too the second *parasha*, through this link, includes animals slaughtered outside.

### 37th Kareis Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב בִּיבִי, הָא דִּתְנַן: שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשֵׁשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת בְּתוֹרָה – תְּלָתִין וְשַׁב הָוְיָין, דְּאִיכָּא הַמַּעֲלֶה וְהַמַּעֲלֶה! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai objects to** these suggestions: But what about **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Karetot* 2a): There are **thirty-six** cases **in the Torah** for which one is liable to receive ***karet*.** The mishna enumerates all thirty-six, and offering up outside the Temple is counted as only one of them. According to these suggestions, **there are thirty-seven, as there is** one case of **one who offers up** an offering that was slaughtered inside the Temple, **and** the other case of **one who offers up** an offering that was slaughtered outside the Temple, which are considered two independent prohibitions. The Gemara concedes: This is **difficult.**

Rav Bibi then raises an objection. The Mishnah states that there are 36 categories in the Torah for which one incurs *kares*. If we now distinguish between burning something slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash* (derived from the plain meaning of the verse) and burning something slaughtered outside (derived through a separate *derasha*), these should constitute two distinct prohibitions — and the count should reach 37. *D'ika ha-ma'aleh ve-ha-ma'aleh* — there is one *ma'aleh* for each sub-category, and treating them as separate derivations implies they are separate sins.

### One Prohibition Unity

The Gemara marks this difficulty with *kashya*, acknowledging the force of the objection. The underlying resolution implicit in the discussion is that although the two cases are derived from different textual sources, they constitute a single prohibition with two applications: the verse — read through the connecting *vav* or through the *gezerah shavah* — encompasses both within the scope of one prohibition. This is consistent with Rabbi Shimon's position as the background to the whole discussion: the Rabbanan hold that the *derasha* extends the prohibition to include *muktarei chutz*, but the extension belongs to the same prohibition, not a new one.

## Sprinkling Blood

### Zerika—New Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָדִתְנַן: הַזּוֹרֵק מִקְצָת דָּמִים בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, מְנָלַן? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּתַנְיָא: ״דָּם יֵחָשֵׁב״ – לְרַבּוֹת הַזּוֹרֵק. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אוֹ זָבַח״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַזּוֹרֵק.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara considers the source for other prohibitions. **And** concerning **that which we learned** in a mishna (110a): **One who sprinkles part of** the **blood** of an offering, e.g., if he sprinkles one sprinkling instead of four, **outside** the Temple courtyard, is **liable; from where do we** derive this? The *tanna* **derives it from that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to one who slaughters outside the Temple courtyard: **"Blood shall be imputed** to that man, he has shed blood, and that man shall be cut off from among his people" (Leviticus 17:4). This serves **to include** liability for **one who sprinkles** sacrificial blood outside the Temple courtyard; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** It is derived from the verse: "Any man…that offers up a burnt offering **or sacrifice"** (Leviticus 17:8). The term "or" serves **to include** liability for **one who sprinkles** blood outside the Temple courtyard.

The Gemara now introduces a new question. We have established *chiyuv* for *shechita* outside and *ha'alah* outside. But what about *zerika* — sprinkling the blood — performed outside the *Beit HaMikdash*? The next Mishnah will teach that one who sprinkles some of the blood outside is *chayav*. *Mina lan* — what is the source?

### Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva's Approaches

*Nafkha lei mi-de-is-tanya*: it is derived from a *baraita*. In the verse concerning *shechita* (17:4), it states *dam yechashev la-ish ha-hu dam shafach* — it is reckoned to him as the spilling of blood. Rabbi Yishmael focuses on this emphasis on *dam* — blood — and derives from it *le-rabbot ha-zorek*: to include the one who sprinkles the blood outside. The textual emphasis on blood in the context of *shechita* extends the prohibition to encompass the blood-ritual of *zerika* as well. This categorizes *zerika* together with *shechita*.

Rabbi Akiva derives *zerika* from a different verse entirely — the *parasha* of *ha'alah* (17:8–9): *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach*. The word *o* ("or") before *zavach* is read as an inclusion: *le-rabbot et ha-zorek* — to include the act of sprinkling blood. This derivation categorizes *zerika* together with *ha'alah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הַאי ״אוֹ זָבַח״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? לְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yishmael, what does he do with this** term "or" in "a burnt offering **or sacrifice"?** It serves **to divide** them into two independent cases, such that liability is incurred even if one offered up only one of them.

*Ve-Rabbi Yishmael hai o zavach mai avid lei?* What does Rabbi Yishmael do with the word *o zavach*? *Le-chalek* — it serves its normal grammatical function, indicating that one is *chayav* for either an *olah* or a *shelamim* independently, not only if both are offered together. Rabbi Yishmael has no need to derive *zerika* from this word because he already has *dam yechashev*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לְחַלֵּק מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״לֹּא יְבִיאֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva, from where does he** derive the *halakha* **to divide** them into two cases? **He derives it from** the next verse: "And **he will not bring it** to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 17:9). The term "it" is written in the singular to indicate that one is liable even if he offered up only one of them.

### Wholeness Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: עַל הַשָּׁלֵם הוּא חַיָּיב, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל הֶחָסֵר. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״לַּעֲשׂוֹת אוֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rabbi Yishmael,** why does he not derive that *halakha* from the term "it"? The Gemara answers: According to him, **that** term **is necessary** as the source for the *halakha* that one **is liable for** offering up **a complete** animal, **but** one **is not liable for** offering up **an incomplete** animal. The term "it" indicates an animal in its entirety. **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva, he derives** this **from** the repetition of "it" in the continuation of the verse: "He will not bring it…**to sacrifice it** to the Lord" (Leviticus 17:9).

*Ve-Rabbi Yishmael le-chalek mena lei?* If Rabbi Akiva uses *o* for *zerika*, where does he derive that one is *chayav* for either offering independently? *Nafka lei mi-lo yavi'enu* — the verse says *lo yavi'enu* in the singular rather than *lo yevi'am* in the plural, teaching that even a single *korban* brought outside generates liability, without requiring two.

Rabbi Yishmael, in turn, uses *lo yavi'enu* for a different lesson: *al ha-shalem hu chayav ve-eino chayav al ha-chaser* — one is liable only if he burns a whole animal, not an incomplete one. And Rabbi Akiva derives the "wholeness" requirement from the phrase *la'asot oto* — "to make it" — which implies completeness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – חַד בְּמוּקְטְרֵי פְּנִים שֶׁחָסְרוּ וְהֶעֱלָן בַּחוּץ, וְחַד לְמוּקְטְרֵי חוּץ שֶׁחָסְרוּ וְהֶעֱלוּ בַּחוּץ. וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל מוּקְטְרֵי פָּנִים שֶׁחָסְרוּ וְהֶעֱלוּ בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לַעֲשׂוֹת אֹתוֹ״ – עַל הַשָּׁלֵם חַיָּיב וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל הֶחָסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rabbi Yishmael,** how does he explain the repetition of "it"? He holds that each mention teaches about a different case: **One** is referring **to** offerings that are fit to be **burned inside** the Temple courtyard **that became incomplete and were** then **offered up outside. And** the other **one** is referring **to** offerings that are unfit and so will be **burned outside** the Temple as they were unlawfully slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, **that became incomplete and were offered up outside.** In both cases one is exempt. **And so it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael says:** One **might** have thought that with regard to offerings that were fit to be **burned inside** the Temple courtyard and **that became incomplete and were** instead **offered up outside,** one would be **liable.** To dispel this notion, **the verse states: "To sacrifice it,"** which indicates that **for** offering up **a complete** animal one is **liable, but** one is **not liable for an incomplete** animal.

This generates a further distinction between the two positions. Since Rabbi Yishmael uses both *lo yavi'enu* and *la'asot oto* to derive the "wholeness" requirement, he applies it in two distinct scenarios. *Ve-Rabbi Yishmael chad be-muktarei pnim she-chisru ve-he'elu ba-chutz ve-chad be-muktarei chutz she-chisru ve-he'elu ba-chutz* — one verse excludes the case of an animal fit for inside that was rendered incomplete and then burned outside; the other excludes the case of an animal fit only for outside that was rendered incomplete and then burned outside. In both scenarios, liability requires that the animal be whole.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – מוּקְטְרֵי פְּנִים שֶׁחָסְרוּ וְהֶעֱלוּ בַּחוּץ, חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva,** who has already expounded both mentions of "it," disagrees with Rabbi Yishmael and holds that one is **liable** for offerings that are fit to be **burned inside that became incomplete and were** instead **offered up outside.**

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. Having used *lo yavi'enu* to derive "one rather than two" and having only *la'asot oto* for the wholeness requirement, he has only one verse covering only one scenario. He would therefore rule that for *muktarei pnim* — an animal slaughtered inside — if it was incomplete and burned outside, one would still be *chayav*. The two Tannaim thus dispute both the derivation of *zerika* and the scope of the "wholeness" requirement.

### Further Applications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַאי ״דָּם יֵחָשֵׁב״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? לְרַבּוֹת שְׁחִיטַת הָעוֹף. וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אוֹ אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחָט״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael derives the liability for sprinkling part of the blood of an offering outside the Temple courtyard from the verse "Blood shall be imputed to that man." The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva, what does he do with this** verse: **"Blood shall be imputed** to that man"? He expounds it **to include** liability for the **slaughter of a bird** offering outside the courtyard. One is liable despite the fact that inside the Temple, a bird is sacrificed by pinching the nape of its neck, not by slaughtering it. **And** as for **Rabbi Yishmael, he derives** this *halakha* **from: "Or that slaughters** it outside the camp" (Leviticus 17:3).

The accounting of verses continues further. Rabbi Akiva uses *dam yechashev* to include *shechitat ha-of* — slaughtering a bird sacrifice outside — since one might have thought that since birds are normally processed by *melika* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, *shechita* of a bird outside would generate no liability. Rabbi Yishmael derives this from the repetition of the verb *yishchat* in the verse (*asher yishchat ba-machaneh o asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machaneh*), the doubled usage being unnecessary and therefore coming to include bird *shechita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אָמַר לָךְ, הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: עַל הַשּׁוֹחֵט הוּא חַיָּיב, וְלֹא עַל הַמּוֹלֵק. וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״זֶּה הַדָּבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva** could have **said to you** that he does not derive it from **that** verse, as he holds that it is **necessary to** teach that only **one who slaughters** a bird offering outside **is liable, but** one is **not liable for pinching its nape** outside. **And** as for **Rabbi Yishmael,** from where does he derive that one is exempt if he pinches? **He derives it from** the phrase at the beginning of the passage about slaughtering outside: **"This is the matter"** (Leviticus 17:2), which indicates that one is liable only for slaughtering and not for any other method of killing.

Both agree, however, that *melika* of a bird performed outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is exempt: Rabbi Yishmael derives this from *zeh ha-davar asher yishchat* — "this is the matter, of slaughtering" — the word *zeh* restricting the prohibition to *shechita* alone. Rabbi Akiva derives it from *lo yavi'enu* read as *al ha-shochet hu chayav ve-lo al ha-moleik*.

## Multiple Violations

### Shachat Vezarak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שָׁחַט וְזָרַק – לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל חַיָּיב אַחַת, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cited a dispute concerning the source for the liability for sprinkling part of the blood of an offering outside the Temple courtyard. According to Rabbi Yishmael, it is derived from the verse in the passage about slaughtering outside the Temple: "Blood shall be imputed to that man" (Leviticus 17:4). According to Rabbi Akiva, it is derived from the verse in the passage about offering up outside the Temple: "Any man…that offers up a burnt offering or sacrifice" (Leviticus 17:8). **Rabbi Abbahu says:** One who **slaughtered** an offering **and sprinkled** its blood outside the Temple in a single lapse of awareness, **according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael,** is **liable** to bring **one** sin offering, as liability for each transgression is derived from the same passage and they are subcategories of the same prohibition. **According to the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** one is **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings, as the liability for each transgression is derived from a different passage and they are considered independent prohibitions.

Rabbi Abbahu now draws out the practical implications of the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva regarding the categorization of *zerika*. The question is: if someone, in a single state of forgetfulness that the prohibition existed, performed both *shechita* and *zerika* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, how many *chatat* offerings must he bring upon realizing his error?

*Shachat ve-zarak le-divrei Rabbi Yishmael chayav achat le-divrei Rabbi Akiva chayav shtayim.* Since Rabbi Yishmael derives *zerika* from the *parasha* of *shechita* — specifically from *dam yechashev* — he categorizes *zerika* together with *shechita* as a single prohibition. Performing both in one state of forgetfulness constitutes one sin committed twice, and only one *korban* is required. Rabbi Akiva, who derives *zerika* from the *parasha* of *ha'alah*, categorizes it together with burning — and therefore *shechita* and *zerika* constitute two distinct prohibitions, requiring two *korbanot*.

### Abaye's Unification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ [לְדִבְרֵי] רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״שָׁם תַּעֲשֶׂה״ – הַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאָן לְכוּלָּן עֲבוֹדָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** he is **liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, **as the verse states** with regard to performing sacrificial rites outside the Temple courtyard: "But in the place that the Lord will choose in one of your tribes, there you shall offer up your burnt offerings, and **there you shall do** all that I command you" (Deuteronomy 12:14). Since the first part of the verse makes reference to offering up, by inference, the term "you shall do" in the second part must be referring to all the other sacrificial rites. Therefore, **the verse has regarded all of** the sacrificial rites apart from offering up as **one rite** with regard to the prohibition against performing them outside the Temple courtyard.

Abaye dissents from this framing of the Rabbi Akiva position. *Afilu le-divrei Rabbi Akiva eino chayav ela achat* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, one brings only one *korban* for *shechita* and *zerika* combined. Although Rabbi Akiva derives *zerika* from the verse of *ha'alah*, he nevertheless agrees on the conceptual categorization: *amar kra sham ta'aseh ve-sham ta'aleh* — the verse in Devarim states "there you shall perform your *korbanot*" and "there you shall offer up," distinguishing between a general category of ritual performance (*ta'aseh*) and the specific act of burning (*ta'aleh*). *Shechita* and *zerika*, being ritual acts other than burning, fall within the *ta'aseh* category and are thus grouped together as one type of prohibition, regardless of which verse derives *zerika*. *Kasav asiya le-khulhu avoda achat* — the Torah's formulation of *asiyah* makes all acts other than burning into a single *avoda* category.

### Zarak Veha'ela

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָרַק וְהֶעֱלָה – לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu also said: One who **sprinkled** the blood of an offering **and offered** it **up** outside, in a single lapse of awareness, **according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael** is **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings. **According to the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** he is **liable** to bring **only one** sin offering.

The same reasoning applies in reverse. *Zarak ve-he'ela* — one who sprinkled blood and then burned the offering outside in a single state of forgetfulness: *le-divrei Rabbi Yishmael chayav shtayim* — for Rabbi Yishmael, since *zerika* is categorized with *shechita* and *ha'alah* is its own category, these are two distinct prohibitions and require two *korbanot*. *Le-divrei Rabbi Akiva eino chayav ela achat* — for Rabbi Akiva (on the non-Abaye reading), since *zerika* is derived from the *parasha* of *ha'alah*, the two are effectively one category, and only one *korban* is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ [לְדִבְרֵי] רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם; לְהָכִי פַּלְגִינְהוּ קְרָא: ״שָׁם תַּעֲלֶה״ וְ״שָׁם תַּעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva** he is **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings, as it is **for this** reason that **the verse differentiated** between the offering up and the slaughtering, as it states: **"There you shall offer up** your burnt offerings, **and there you shall do,"** in order to teach that one who performs both is liable for each one.

Abaye again corrects: *afilu le-divrei Rabbi Akiva chayav shtayim* — even Rabbi Akiva holds that *zerika* and *ha'alah* are two distinct sins. The verse *sham ta'aleh ve-sham ta'aseh* separates *ha'alah* from all other ritual acts. However *zerika* is derived textually, it conceptually belongs to the *ta'aseh* category, not to *ta'aleh*, and therefore *zerika* and *ha'alah* remain two distinct prohibitions. *Ve-hachi pelaginhu kra* — this is how the verse itself divides them.

### All Three Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט וְזָרַק וְהֶעֱלָה – לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, Rabbi Abbahu adds the logical conclusion that with regard to one who **slaughtered** an offering **and sprinkled** its blood **and offered** it **up** outside, in a single lapse of awareness, **all agree** that he is **liable** to bring **two** sin offerings.

The final case: *ve-shachat ve-zarak ve-he'ela* — one who performed all three acts in a single state of forgetfulness. *Le-divrei ha-kol chayav shtayim* — according to all opinions, he brings two *korbanot*. Wherever *zerika* is categorized — whether with *shechita* (Rabbi Yishmael, or Rabbi Akiva per Abaye) or with *ha'alah* (Rabbi Akiva on the non-Abaye reading) — the three acts span both categories. In either case, one category is represented twice and the other once, yielding two distinct sins committed, and therefore two *korbanot*.

## Geographic Scope

### Three Camps Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בַּמַּחֲנֶה״ – יָכוֹל הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹלָה בַּדָּרוֹם יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "Any man of the house of Israel that slaughters an ox, or lamb, or goat, in the camp, or that slaughters it outside the camp" (Leviticus 17:3). Based on the first part of the verse: "That slaughters…**in the camp,"** one **might** have thought that **one who slaughters a burnt offering in the south** of the courtyard, instead of in the north, where it should be slaughtered, **will be liable,** as he has slaughtered it outside the area in which it should be slaughtered. To counter this, **the** next part of the **verse states:** "That slaughters it **outside the camp,"** indicating that liability is incurred only for slaughtering an offering outside the Temple courtyard.

With the categories of prohibited acts now fully analyzed, the Gemara turns to the geographic question: where must the act take place for liability to arise? The relevant verse reads *asher yishchat ba-machaneh o asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machaneh* — "who slaughters in the camp or who slaughters outside the camp." The phrase *mi-chutz la-machaneh* normally denotes a location fully outside all three *machanos* — the *machaneh Shechinah*, *machaneh Leviyah*, and *machaneh Yisrael*, corresponding in the context of Jerusalem to the *azarah*, *Har HaBayis*, and Jerusalem itself. But the verse also includes *ba-machaneh* — in the camp — which implies that even slaughtering within the camp environs generates *chiyuv*, as long as it is not at the *petach ohel moed*.

### Shechita Ba'Darom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת? (תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אוֹ עֵז בַּמַּחֲנֶה״.) מִנַּיִן אַף בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּמַּחֲנֶה״. אִי בַּמַּחֲנֶה, יָכוֹל הַשּׁוֹחֵט עוֹלָה בַּדָּרוֹם יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״. מָה חוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה מְיוּחָד – שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לִשְׁחִיטַת קָדָשִׁים וְלִשְׁחִיטַת כׇּל זֶבַח; יָצָא דָּרוֹם – שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לִשְׁחִיטַת קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, רָאוּי לִשְׁחִיטַת קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Three concentric areas, so-called camps, arranged according to decreasing level of sanctity, encompassed the Tabernacle and Temple: The camp of the Divine Presence comprised the courtyard of the Tabernacle, and later on, of the Temple. Surrounding that was the Levite camp and then the Israelite camp. If the verse had stated only: "That slaughters it outside the camp," one **might** have thought that a person would be liable only for slaughtering an offering **outside** all **three** of the **camps.** To dispel this notion, the first part of **the verse states:** "That slaughters an ox, or lamb, **or goat, in the camp,"** which indicates that liability is incurred even for slaughtering inside these camps. **From where** is it derived that one is liable **even** if he slaughters **inside the Levite camp? The verse states:** "That slaughters…**in the camp,"** which indicates that liability is incurred even for slaughtering inside the three camps. **If** the verse stated only the term **"in the camp,"** which indicates that one is liable even for slaughtering inside the three camps, one **might** have thought that **one who slaughters a burnt offering in the south** of the courtyard, which is regarded as being outside of the holier northern section of the courtyard, where such an offering should be slaughtered, **will** also **be liable.** To counter this, **the verse states: "Or** that slaughters it **outside the camp."** This indicates that **just as** the phrase **"outside the camp"** is **distinctive** in that it is referring to an area **that is not fit for slaughtering offerings** of the most sacred order **or for slaughtering any** other type of **offering,** so too, the term "in the camp" is referring to an area unfit for the slaughter of any offering. This **excludes** the **south** of the courtyard, **as even though** it is an area that is **not fit for slaughtering offerings of the most sacred order,** it is **fit for slaughtering offerings of lesser sanctity.** Therefore, no liability is incurred for slaughtering an offering in the south of the courtyard, even if it should have been slaughtered in the north.

A *baraita* is introduced: *asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machaneh — yachol chutz li-shlosh machanos?* Perhaps *mi-chutz la-machaneh* means only fully outside all three camps, and one who slaughters within any of them is exempt? The verse therefore also says *ba-machaneh* — even within the camp — to establish that liability extends to locations within the camp perimeter. But this raises the converse question: *yachol ha-shochet olah ba-darom yei chayav?* Perhaps the inclusion of *ba-machaneh* means one is even *chayav* for slaughtering an *olah* in the southern section of the *azarah*?

This is not as strange as it sounds. The southern section of the *azarah* is not a valid location for the *shechita* of *kodshei kodshim*, including an *olah*, though it is valid for *kodshim kalim* like *shelamim*. One might have thought: since the verse says *ba-machaneh* to include locations within the camp, perhaps slaughtering an *olah* in the south — a place unfit for it — should generate liability for *shechutei chutz*. The *baraita* answers: *mi-chutz la-machaneh meyuchad she-ein ra'uy li-shchitat kodshim u-le-shchitat kol zevach* — the model for liability is a location unfit for the slaughter of *any* sacrifice whatsoever. The south, while unfit for *kodshei kodshim*, remains fit for *kodshim kalim*. Therefore it does not fall within the prohibition, and one who slaughters an *olah* there is not liable for *shechutei chutz* — though the *korban* is invalidated.

### Roof of Heichal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט עַל גַּגּוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל – חַיָּיב, הוֹאִיל וְאֵין רָאוּי לִשְׁחִיטַת כׇּל זֶבַח. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, נִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״(אֶל) מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״, וְלָא בָּעֵי ״אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״; ״אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ לְמָה לִי? לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי גַּגּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** Based on this *baraita*, **Ulla says: One who slaughters** an offering **on the roof of** the **Sanctuary** is **liable** for slaughtering outside the Temple courtyard, **since** the roof is an area that is **not fit for the slaughter of any offering. Rava objects to this: If so, let the verse write** only: "Or that slaughters it **outside the camp," and** it would **not** be **necessary** to write: "And he did not bring it **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 17:4). **Why do I** need the verse to also write: "And he did not bring it **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"?** Is it **not to exclude the roof of** the Sanctuary, as in the Temple the Sanctuary corresponded to the Tent of Meeting? Although the roof is an area that is not fit for slaughtering any offering, one should not be liable for slaughtering there since the offering was nevertheless brought to the Tent of Meeting.

*Amar Ulla: ha-shochet al gago shel Heichal chayav.* Ulla proposes that if someone slaughters a *korban* on the roof of the *Heichal*, he is *chayav*. The roof of the *Heichal* is certainly not a valid location for any *shechita*, and therefore it would seem to fit the criterion of a place unfit for the slaughter of all sacrifices — thus generating liability for *shechutei chutz*. Ulla adds: *ho'il ve-ra'uy li-shchitat kol zevach* — since, had the act been performed inside, it would have been valid for any sacrifice.

### Petach Ohel Moed

*Matkif lah Rava*: Rava challenges Ulla. *Im ken nichtov kra mi-chutz la-machaneh ve-lo ba'ei petach ohel moed* — if geographic location outside any permitted zone were the sole criterion, the Torah should have simply stated "outside the camp" and had no need to add the phrase *v'el petach ohel moed lo hevi'o* — "he did not bring it to the entrance of the *ohel moed*." The fact that the verse specifies *petach ohel moed* indicates that the operative question is not merely where the act occurred but whether the animal was brought to the *ohel moed*. An animal on the roof of the *Heichal* is within the airspace and zone of the *ohel moed* — it was, in a meaningful sense, brought there. Therefore *petach ohel moed lamah li? Lav le-mi'utei gagot* — the reference to *petach ohel moed* is precisely to exclude the roof, teaching that one who slaughters on the roof is *patur*.

### Animal in/out Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא – אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב: ״אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״; ״בַּמַּחֲנֶה״ ״(וְאֶל) מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ לְמָה לִי? לָאו לְאֵתוֹיֵי גַּגּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rava's claim: **But according to Rava, if** that is **so** and one is exempt if he slaughtered on the roof of the Sanctuary as ultimately the offering was brought to the Tent of Meeting, then **let** the verse **write** only: **"And** he did not bring it **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting." Why do I** need the verse to also write **"in the camp"** and **"outside the camp"?** Is it **not** that the term "in the camp" indicates that there is a case where one is liable even when slaughtering inside the courtyard, which serves **to include** liability for slaughtering on **the roof of** the Sanctuary? According to this, the term "outside the camp" would then be necessary to exclude liability for slaughtering a burnt offering in the south of the courtyard.

*U-le-Rava im ken nichtov petach ohel moed ba-machaneh u-mi-chutz la-machaneh lamah li?* The Gemara presses back on Rava: if *petach ohel moed* does the work on its own, why does the verse need to specify both *ba-machaneh* and *mi-chutz la-machaneh*? What do those geographic qualifications add?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: לְאֵיתוֹיֵי כּוּלָּהּ בִּפְנִים וְצַוָּארָהּ בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said** in defense of Rava: No, the term "in the camp" is written **to include** liability for slaughtering an offering in a case where **its** body is **entirely inside** the courtyard **but its neck** is **outside** the courtyard.

*Amar Rav Mari: la'atuyei kullah bifnim ve-tzavara ba-chutz* — they come to include the case where the body of the animal is standing inside the *azarah* while its neck protrudes outside, where the *shechita* occurs. In such a case, one might have argued that since the animal was brought into the *ohel moed*, there is no liability. The *ba-machaneh u-mi-chutz la-machaneh* teaches that one is nonetheless *chayav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* [צַוָּארָהּ] בַּחוּץ – פְּשִׁיטָא! אַמַּאי קָפֵיד רַחֲמָנָא – אַשְּׁחִיטָה; וּשְׁחִיטָה בַּחוּץ הִיא! אֶלָּא לְאֵיתוֹיֵי כּוּלָּהּ בַּחוּץ וְצַוָּארָהּ בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara modifies Rav Mari's statement: If **its neck** is **outside** the courtyard, it is **obvious** that one is liable, as **about what** is **the Merciful One particular?** The Merciful One is particular **about** the **slaughter. And** in this case, the **slaughter is** done **outside** the courtyard. **Rather,** say instead that the term "in the camp" is written **to include** liability for slaughtering an offering where **its** body is **entirely outside** the courtyard **but its neck** is **inside** the courtyard.

The Gemara objects: *tzavara ba-chutz peshita* — that case is obvious. The act of *shechita* occurs at the neck, and the neck is outside. Even with the *petach ohel moed* verse alone, one would know that *shechita* occurring outside is the operative act. *Ela la-atuyei kullah ba-chutz ve-tzavara bifnim* — rather, the geographic qualifications come to include the reverse scenario: the body of the animal stands outside while its neck is inside the *azarah*. Without *ba-machaneh u-mi-chutz la-machaneh*, one might have argued that since the *shechita* itself occurs inside — at the neck, which is within the *ohel moed* — the offender should be exempt. The verse teaches that even here, since the bulk of the animal remains outside, one is *chayav*.

In summary, the geographic parameters work together: the references to *ba-machaneh* and *mi-chutz la-machaneh* clarify that *chiyuv* applies whether the location of the act is close to the *Beit HaMikdash* or far — even on *Har HaBayit* or in Jerusalem itself, the act is prohibited — and they further teach the edge cases involving an animal straddling the boundary of the *azarah*. The *petach ohel moed* language, for its part, excludes acts performed fully within the sanctified precinct, including, according to Rava, those performed on the roof of the *Heichal*, which falls within the zone of the *ohel moed*. Whether Ulla or Rava is correct on that final point remains the subject of the *itmar* that will be taken up in the next daf.
