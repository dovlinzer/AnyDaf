# Shabbat 152 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Old Age Metaphors

### Intro to Aging

The final pages of *Maseches Shabbos* draw to a close with an extended *aggadeta* — a sustained and richly textured discourse on the infirmities of old age, the nature of the soul after death, and the fate of the righteous and wicked in the world to come. The *gemara* builds much of this discussion on a close reading of *pesukim* from Kohelet, interpreting each image as a precise metaphor for the physical deterioration that accompanies aging.

### Guards of House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיּוֹם שֶׁיָּזוּעוּ שׁוֹמְרֵי הַבַּיִת וְהִתְעַוְּתוּ וְגוֹ׳״. ״בַּיּוֹם שֶׁיָּזוּעוּ שׁוֹמְרֵי הַבַּיִת״ — אֵלּוּ הַכְּסָלִים וְהַצְּלָעוֹת. ״וְהִתְעַוְּתוּ אַנְשֵׁי הֶחָיִל״ — אֵלּוּ שׁוֹקַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to interpret verses from the Book of Ecclesiastes. The verse states: **"On the day when the keepers of the house shall tremble,** and the strong men **shall bow themselves,** and the grinders cease because they are few, and those that look out the windows shall be dimmed" (Ecclesiastes 12:3). **"On the day when the keepers of the house shall tremble"; this** is referring to **the flanks and ribs** that surround and protect a person's internal organs. **"And the strong men shall bow themselves"; these are the thighs,** which support a person's strength.

The *gemara* identifies each element of the verse with a part of the aging body. *Shomrei habayit*, the watchmen or guards of the house, are *haksalim veha'atzla'ot* — the ribs and sides, which protect the inner organs as a guard protects a house. *Anshei hachayil*, the strong men who become bent, refer to *shokayim* — the thighs, once powerful, now weakened and stooped.

### Grinders & Eyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָטְלוּ הַטּוֹחֲנוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ שִׁינַּיִם. ״וְחָשְׁכוּ הָרוֹאוֹת בַּאֲרוּבּוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ עֵינַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **"And the grinders cease"; these are the teeth,** which decay and fall out. **"And those that look out the windows shall be dimmed"; these are the eyes,** which become dimmer.

The verse continues: *ubatlu hatochenot* — the grinders cease — which the *gemara* identifies as *shinayim*, the teeth. And *vachoshchu haro'ot ba'arubot* — those who see through the skylights grow dark — refers to *einayim*, the eyes. Each faculty of the body, in turn, fails.

### Caesar Dialogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָתֵית לְבֵי אֲבִידָן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טוּר תְּלַג סַחְרָנוֹהִ גְּלִידִין. כַּלְבוֹהִי לָא נָבְחִין. טָחֲנוֹהִי לָא טָחֲנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The Roman **emperor said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya: What is the reason you did not come to the House of Avidan?** This was a place in which dialogues and debates were conducted. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to him** enigmatically: **The snowy mountain is surrounded with ice,** meaning that his hair had turned white; **his dogs do not bark,** meaning that his voice could no longer be heard; **his grinders have ceased grinding,** meaning that his teeth had fallen out.

The *gemara* then relates that Caesar said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya: *mai ta'ama lo asit levei avidan* — why did you not come to *bei avidan*? *Bei avidan* is a house of disputation — whether between Rabbis and members of various Jewish sects or between philosophers — to which different Sages responded differently, some confident in their capacity to handle intellectual or other challenges, others reluctant to engage. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya had not attended, and Caesar pressed him for an explanation.

### Yehoshua's Decline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּי רַב אָמְרִי: אַדְּלָא אַבֵּידְנָא, בָּחֵישְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** In **the school of Rav they say** that he added: **I am searching for that which I have not lost,** because an old man walks bent over and appears to be searching for something.

Rabbi Yehoshua's response is a cascade of vivid metaphors: *tur talag* — the mountain is snowy, meaning his hair has gone white. *Sachronei glidin* — it is surrounded with frost, which Rashi interprets as his beard and mustache having also whitened. *Kalvei lo navchin* — the dogs are no longer barking, meaning, according to Rashi, that he has lost the clarity and volume of his speech. *Tachneit lo tachnin* — the grinders are not grinding, that is, his teeth no longer function. He pleads old age, couched in precisely the same vivid figurative language the *gemara* has just been unpacking.

### Bei Rav Saying

The Sages in the school of Rav offer another pointed image of old age: *ad d'lo avidna bacheshna* — I search for something I have not lost. Because the elderly become bent over, they appear perpetually to be searching the ground for something they have dropped — though they have lost nothing at all. It may also capture the mental dimension of aging, the sense of reaching for something that is no longer there.

### Youth vs Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר קִיסְמָא אוֹמֵר: טָבָא תְּרֵי מִתְּלָת. וַוי לַהּ לַחֲדָא דְּאָזְלָא וְלָא אָתְיָא. מַאי הִיא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: יַנְקוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei bar Kisma says:** The **two** feet of one's youth **are better than the three** of old age, when one walks with a cane. **Woe to the one who goes and does not come** back. **What is this** referring to? **Rav Ḥisda said: Youth.**

A *baraisa* is cited in the name of Rabbi Yosi bar Kisma: *tava try me-telasa* — two is better than three. This is the riddle of the Sphinx inverted: a person walks on two legs in the prime of life and three — with a cane — in old age. *Vai lah lechada d'azla v'lo atya* — woe to that one which has gone and does not return. The *gemara* asks: what is that one thing? *Yankusa* — youth. Woe to the loss of youth, which, once gone, does not return.

### Roses & Thorns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: יַנְקוּתָא — כְּלִילָא דְּוַורְדָּא, סָבוּתָא — כְּלִילָא דְחִילְפָא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said: Youth is a crown of roses; old age is a crown of thorns.**

Rav Dimi elaborates: *yankusa klila d'varda* — youth is a crown of roses. *Savusa klila d'chilfei* — old age is a crown of thorns. The contrast could hardly be starker.

### Meir's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: דּוֹק בְּכַכֵּי וְתַשְׁכַּח בְּנִיגְרֵי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַנִּשְׂבַּע לֶחֶם וַנִּהְיֶה טוֹבִים וְרָעָה לֹא רָאִינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **taught in the name of Rabbi Meir: Grind** food **with your teeth and** you will **find in your feet** the strength to carry your body, **as it is stated: "For we were sated with our bread and were well, and saw no evil"** (Jeremiah 44:17).

A teaching transmitted in the name of Rabbi Meir advises: *dok b'kakhei vetishkach b'nigrei* — grind with your teeth and you will find it in your legs. The meaning is that if one eats well and takes care of one's body in youth, the benefit will be felt in old age — in one's legs, one's mobility, one's strength. The verse cited in support is *vnisba lachem v'nihye tovim vera'ah lo rainu* — we will be sated with bread, we will be well, and we will not see evil — implying that even in later years, one who has maintained proper nutrition will be spared suffering.

### Food & Drink

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, שְׁרֵי שַׂקָּיךָ וְעַיֵּיל לַחְמָךְ. עַד אַרְבְּעִין שְׁנִין — מֵיכְלָא מְעַלֵּי, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — מִשְׁתְּיָא מְעַלֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: Large-toothed one; untie your sack,** that is, your mouth, **and insert your food. Until** the age of **forty years, food is beneficial; from here and on, drinking is beneficial.**

Shmuel says to Rav Yehuda — whom he addresses as *Shinana*, the sharp one — *sharei kayecha v'ayyel lachmech*: open your mouth and let the bread come in. Eat well while you are young. *Arba'in shanim michla ma'aly* — until forty years of age, food is what principally benefits your health. *Mikan v'eilach mishty ma'aly* — from forty onwards, what you drink becomes the more significant factor for your well-being.

## Eunuch Exchange

### Animal Insults

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא גּוֹזָאָה לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: מֵהָכָא לְקַרְחִינָא כַּמָּה הָוֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּמֵהָכָא לְגוֹזַנְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּרְחָא קַרְחָא בְּאַרְבְּעָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עִיקְרָא שְׁלִיפָא בִּתְמָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Having quoted some aphorisms of the Sages, the Gemara relates the following conversation: **A certain eunuch** who was an apostate **said** the following **to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** as a provocation: **How** far is **it from here to Karḥina?** The provocateur's intention was to hint to the fact that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa was bald [*kere'aḥ*]. **He said to him: It is the same as** the distance **from here to** the mountains of **Gozen,** hinting at the eunuch's castration, which in Aramaic is *goza* (Rav Ya'akov Emden). The apostate **said to him: A bald buck** is sold **for four** *dinar*. **He said to him: A castrated goat [*ikkara shelifa*]** is sold **for eight.**

A certain *guza'ah* — a eunuch — who appears according to Rashi to have also been a *Tzadoki*, a sectarian of some kind, said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *meihacha l'Karchina kama* — how far is it from here to Karchina? Karchina was plainly chosen as a play on *karcha*, meaning bald, and so the question was a veiled insult directed at the Rabbi's baldness.

### Counter Insult

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַזְיֵיהּ דְּלָא סָיֵים מְסָאנֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּעַל סוּס — מֶלֶךְ, דְּעַל חֲמוֹר — בֶּן חוֹרִין, וּדְמַנְעָלֵי בְּרִיגְלוֹהִי — בַּר אִינִישׁ. דְּלָא הָא וְלָא הָא — דַּחֲפִיר וּקְבִיר טָב מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גּוֹזָא גּוֹזָא, תְּלָת אֲמַרְתְּ לִי, תְּלָת שָׁמְעַתְּ: הַדְרַת פָּנִים — זָקָן, שִׂמְחַת לֵב — אִשָּׁה, ״נַחֲלַת ה׳ — בָּנִים״. בָּרוּךְ הַמָּקוֹם שֶׁמְּנָעֲךָ מִכּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The apostate **saw that** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa **was not wearing shoes.** He **said to him:** One who rides **on a horse** is a **king.** One who rides **on a donkey** is **a free man. And one who wears shoes is** at least **a human being.** One who does **neither this nor that,** someone **who is buried** in the earth **is better than him. He said to him: Eunuch, eunuch, you said to me three things,** and now **hear three** things: **The glory of a face is the beard, the joy of the heart is a wife,** and **"the portion of the Lord is children"** (Psalms 127:3); **blessed is the Omnipresent who has denied you all of them,** for a eunuch does not have a beard, a wife or children.

Rabbi Yehoshua responded in kind: *kemeihacha l'Guzina* — the same distance as it is from here to Guzina. *Guzina* echoing *guza*, a eunuch — you call me bald, I call you a eunuch. The *Tzadoki* then said: *barcha karcha b'arba'ah* — a sheared goat costs only four *zuz*, implying that without its hair it is worth almost nothing. Rabbi Yehoshua countered: *ikra shalifa b'tamanya* — a castrated sheep fetches eight *zuz*. The point, as noted from the Sefaria commentary, is that even with the concession of baldness, a castrated animal commands a higher price than a bald one — making the eunuch's situation comparatively worse, not better.

### Barefoot Shame

The eunuch then observed that Rabbi Yehoshua was not wearing shoes. He said: *d'al sus melech* — one who rides on a horse is a king; *d'al chamor ben chorin* — one who rides on a donkey is at least a free man; *dimna'aly b'riglohi bar inish* — one who has shoes is at least human. But *d'lo ha v'lo ha dachafir v'kavir tav mineih* — one who has neither beast nor shoes, who goes about barefoot — it would be better for him to be dug into the earth and buried. This last comment perhaps explains why this exchange appears in the context of a discussion about death and the fate of the body.

### Three Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קָרְחָא מְצַוֵּי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עִיקְרָא שְׁלִיפָא תּוֹכֵחָה!
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him:** Does **a bald man quarrel? He said to him:** Does **a castrated male goat** speak words of **rebuke?**

Rabbi Yehoshua responded: *guza guza, tlat amart li, tlat shamat* — castrated one, you have said three things to me; now hear three things in return. The three things he received were the three insults: the crack about baldness, the comparison of animals, and the remark about the shoes. The three responses: *hadrat panim zakan* — the glory of a man's face is his beard, which the eunuch, being castrated, does not have; *simchat lev isha* — the joy of a man's heart is his wife; *nachalat Hashem banim* — the inheritance from God is children. *Baruch hamakom shemena'acha mikulam* — praised be God who has prevented you from possessing any of these three. When the eunuch challenged Rabbi Yehoshua — *karcha metziyana*, are you really picking a fight? — Rabbi Yehoshua replied: *ikra shalifa tochecha* — is a eunuch going to deliver me rebuke?

## Shimon & Rabbi

### Festival Absence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן חֲלַפְתָּא: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא הִקְבַּלְנוּ פָּנֶיךָ בָּרֶגֶל, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִקְבִּילוּ אֲבוֹתַי לַאֲבוֹתֶיךָ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סְלָעִים נַעֲשׂוּ גְּבוֹהִים, קְרוֹבִים נַעֲשׂוּ רְחוֹקִים, מִשְׁתַּיִם נַעֲשׂוּ שָׁלֹשׁ, מֵשִׂים שָׁלוֹם בַּבַּיִת בָּטַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again addresses old age: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to Rabbi Shimon ben Ḥalafta: For what** reason **did we not greet you during the Festival the way that my fathers greeted your fathers?** This was a polite way of asking Rabbi Shimon ben Ḥalafta why he had not come to visit Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. He **said to him:** Because I have grown old, and **the rocks** on the road **have become tall,** and destinations that are **near have become far away,** and my **two** feet **have been made into three** with the addition of a cane, and **that which brings peace to the house,** namely, the sexual drive which motivates a couple to make peace, **is no more.**

Rabbi said to Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta: *mipnei mah lo hikbalnu panecha baregel* — why did we not welcome you on the festival, the way my fathers welcomed your fathers? The expectation was that a student would visit his Rebbe on the *regel* to show proper respect to the *Nasi*. Rabbi Shimon's reply, like that of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya before him, is a catalogue of the infirmities of age.

### Obstacle Shift

The obstacles and distances of life have transformed for the elderly. *Slaim naasu gevohim* — the rocks have become very high. Even the modest inclines and small obstacles of daily life now loom impossibly large. *Krovim naasu rehokim* — things that used to be close now feel very distant; even short distances seem enormous. Rashi adds the interpretation that this may also refer to loss of hearing: people speaking right next to him now seem as if they are speaking from far away. This image resonates with contemporary experience as well. A speaker on disability access once described to a group of rabbis that if they wished to understand why people with physical disabilities did not come to their synagogues, they needed simply to step outside and count the steps — obstacles invisible to those unaffected, but each one a mountain to those who are.

### Cane & Loss

*Mishtayim naasu shalosh* — from two I have become three. He must now walk with a cane. *Umesim shalom babayis batel* — and the one who makes peace in the house has been nullified. Rashi interprets this as a reference to the loss of sexual desire or sexual capacity in old age, though other explanations have been offered relating to digestion or other functions. The plain sense of *mesim shalom babayis* — the one who makes peace in the house — lending itself to other readings as well.

## Kohelet Ch. 12

### Closed Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְסֻגְּרוּ דְלָתַיִם בַּשּׁוּק וְגוֹ׳״ — אֵלּוּ נְקָבָיו שֶׁל אָדָם. ״בִּשְׁפַל קוֹל הַטַּחֲנָה״ — בִּשְׁבִיל קוּרְקְבָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ טוֹחֵן. ״וְיָקוּם לְקוֹל הַצִּפּוֹר״ — שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ צִפּוֹר מְנַעַרְתּוֹ מִשְּׁנָתוֹ. ״וְיִשַּׁחוּ כׇּל בְּנוֹת הַשִּׁיר״ — שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ קוֹל שָׁרִים וְשָׁרוֹת דּוֹמוֹת עָלָיו כְּשִׂיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to expound the verses of the final chapter of Ecclesiastes. The verse states: "And the doors shall be shut in the marketplace when the sound of the grinding is low, and one shall start up at the voice of a bird, and all the daughters of music shall be brought low" (Ecclesiastes 12:4). The Sages expounded: **"And the doors shall be shut in the marketplace"; these are a person's orifices,** which cease to function normally. The interpretation continues: **"When the sound of the grinding is low"; because the stomach is not grinding** and digesting one's food. **"And one shall start up at the voice of a bird";** because one is unable to sleep deeply such **that even a bird will wake him from his sleep. "And all the daughters of music shall be brought low";** this means **that even the voices of male and female singers will seem to him like** mere **conversation,** and he will no longer derive pleasure from song.

The *gemara* returns to the *pesukim* of Kohelet 12. *Vesigru dlasayim bashuk* — and the doors have been closed in the marketplace. This refers to *nekavav shel adam* — the body's orifices — which no longer function as they once did. The reason given is *bishvil korkevan she-eino tochen* — the stomach no longer grinds as it should, which is a different form of grinding than the failure of the teeth discussed earlier.

### Bird Wake

The bird's chirp awakens the elderly from their restless sleep. *Veyakum lekol hatzippur* — and one rises at the sound of a bird. The *gemara* explains: *she-afillu tzippur menaarato mishnato* — even a bird's chirping is enough to rouse the elderly person from sleep. The slightest sound, which a younger person would sleep through entirely, awakens the old.

### Song Failure

*Veyishchu kol bnos hashir* — and all the daughters of song are laid low. Even the sound of male and female singers, which once brought pleasure, now falls upon the elderly person *ke-shucha* — like a burden or something laid low — no longer giving joy.

### Barzillai Truth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף בַּרְזִילַּי הַגִּלְעָדִי אָמַר לְדָוִד: ״בֶּן שְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם הַאֵדַע בֵּין טוֹב לְרָע״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁדַּעְתָּן שֶׁל זְקֵנִים מִשְׁתַּנּוֹת. ״אִם יִטְעַם עַבְדְּךָ אֶת אֲשֶׁר אוֹכַל וְאֶת אֲשֶׁר אֶשְׁתֶּה״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁשִּׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל זְקֵנִים מִתְרַפְּטוֹת. ״אִם אֶשְׁמַע עוֹד בְּקוֹל שָׁרִים וְשָׁרוֹת״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁאׇזְנֵיהֶם שֶׁל זְקֵנִים מִתְכַּבְּדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And even Barzilai the Gileadite said to David: "Today I am eighty years old, can I discern between good and bad?** Can your servant taste what I eat or what I drink? Can I hear any more the voice of singing men and singing women?" (II Samuel 19:36). The Gemara explains: "Can I discern between good and bad"; **from here** we derive **that the minds of the elderly change** and they no longer discern properly. **"Can your servant taste what I eat or what I drink"; from here** we derive **that the lips of the elderly crack and wither. "Can I hear any more the voice of singing men and singing women"; from here** we derive **that the ears of the elderly become heavy.**

The *gemara* now raises the case of Barzillai the Giladi, who said to David: *ben shmonim shana anochi hayom, hayada bein tov lera* — I am eighty years old today; do I any longer know between good and evil? The *gemara* notes: *mikan shedeoseihen shel zekenim mishtanos* — here one sees that the intellect, or more broadly the sensory and experiential faculties, of the elderly change.

The passage continues to elaborate on what Barzillai meant. *Im yit'am avdecha et asher ochal ve'et asher eshteh* — can your servant even taste what he eats or drinks? From this the *gemara* derives: *mikan she'siftoteihem shel zekenim mitrapot* — that the lips of the elderly become cracked, or that their sense of taste diminishes, so that they can no longer properly taste what they eat or drink. *Im eshma od bekol sharim vesharos* — will I still be able to hear the sound of singers? From this: *mikan she'ozneihem shel zekenim mitkavdot* — their ears grow heavy, meaning they lose the acuity of their hearing. Whether the *gemara* understands Barzillai's statement as referring primarily to the intellectual and ethical capacity to distinguish between good and evil, or more to the experiential sense of pleasure and taste, is not entirely clear from the *peshat* of the verse — though the *gemara* leans toward the latter, understanding his complaint as one about failing senses rather than failing moral judgment.

### Maidservant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: בַּרְזִילַּי הַגִּלְעָדִי שַׁקָּרָא הֲוָה. דְּהַהִיא אַמְּתָא דַּהֲוַאי בֵּי רַבִּי בַּת תִּשְׁעִים וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין, וַהֲוָת טָעֲמָא קִידְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: Barzilai the Gileadite was a liar** and he merely wanted to avoid joining David upon his return to Jerusalem, for an eighty-year old man is not usually this debilitated. **For there was a particular maidservant in the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **who was ninety-two years old, and she would taste** the food that was cooking **in the pots.**

Rav challenges the reliability of Barzillai's testimony: *Barzilai HaGiladi shakra hava* — Barzillai the Giladi was a liar. As evidence, Rav points to a maidservant in the house of Rebbi who was ninety-two years old and who continued to taste the pots to determine the spices — demonstrating that old age does not necessarily rob everyone of their sense of taste. Of course, this does not mean that Barzillai's experience was not real, only that it was not universal.

### Licentious Fall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בַּרְזִילַּי הַגִּלְעָדִי שָׁטוּף בְּזִמָּה הֲוָה, וְכׇל הַשָּׁטוּף בְּזִמָּה — זִקְנָה קוֹפֶצֶת עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Barzilai was speaking the truth, but **Barzilai the Gileadite was steeped in promiscuity, and anyone who is steeped in promiscuity is overtaken by old age** before his time.

Rava offers a different explanation: *Barzilai HaGiladi shatuf be'zimah hava* — Barzillai the Giladi was immersed in licentiousness. *Vekol hashatuf be'zimah, zikna kofetzet alav* — anyone who is given over to uncontrolled sexual behavior finds that old age jumps upon him with particular force. The suggestion seems to be that Barzillai's suffering in old age was more severe than most, which explains why his description sounds so extreme — even harsher than what the *gemara* itself had already described. Rebbi's maidservant represents the norm; Barzillai was a special and self-inflicted case.

## Wisdom vs Folly

### Scholars Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּזְקִינִין — חָכְמָה נִתּוֹסֶפֶת בָּהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בִּישִׁישִׁים חׇכְמָה וְאוֹרֶךְ יָמִים תְּבוּנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says: As Torah scholars grow older, wisdom is increased in them, as it is stated: "With aged men is wisdom; and length of days brings understanding"** (Job 12:12).

A *baraisa* — which also appears as a *mishnah* at the end of *Maseches Kinim* — is cited in the name of Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi. *Talmidei chachamim, kol zman shemazkinin, chochmah nitvosefet bahem* — Torah scholars, the older they become, the wiser they grow. The verse cited in support is *biyeshishim chochmah ve'orech yamim tevunah* — in the elderly is wisdom, and with length of days comes understanding.

### Folk Folly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמַּזְקִינִין — טִפְּשׁוּת נִתּוֹסֶפֶת בָּהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מֵסִיר שָׂפָה לְנֶאֱמָנִים וְטַעַם זְקֵנִים יִקָּח״.
>
> **Translation:** **And as ignoramuses grow older, foolishness is increased in them, as it is stated: "He removes the speech of men of trust and takes away the understanding of the aged"** (Job 12:20).

*Ve'amei ha'aretz*, however — the people of the land, those less versed in learning — *kol zman shemazkinin, tipshus nitvosefet bahem* — as they grow older, they become more foolish. The verse invoked here is *mesir safah lene'emanim* — God removes speech from those who were trustworthy, those who could ordinarily be depended upon for meaningful discourse — *veta'am zekenim yikach* — and the taste of elders He takes away.

### Scripture Split

One might ask: these two *pesukim* seem to be in direct contradiction — one says aging brings wisdom, the other says aging brings foolishness. Rashi resolves the contradiction by splitting the verses along the divide between *talmidei chachamim* and *amei ha'aretz*: one verse describes the trajectory of those devoted to Torah learning, the other describes those who were not.

### Mind Exercise

Beyond the theological framing, there is something genuinely compelling here from a modern perspective. The principle that sustained intellectual engagement across decades preserves mental sharpness in old age finds some support in contemporary research. There is evidence, for instance, that activities like crossword puzzles are associated with delayed onset of dementia and Alzheimer's. More broadly, there is an ongoing debate about whether significant cognitive decline is inevitable in people who do not develop dementia — and some evidence suggests that the more a person has exercised their mind over a lifetime, the more robustly those faculties persist. This maps quite naturally onto what the *gemara* is saying: decades of *talmud Torah* constitute sustained mental exercise of the highest order, and the Sages observed that this produced precisely the effect of preserving and even deepening wisdom in old age.

## Almond & Desire

### Almond Blossom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גַּם מִגָּבוֹהַּ יִירָאוּ״ — שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ גַּבְשׁוּשִׁית קְטַנָּה דּוֹמָה עָלָיו כְּהָרֵי הָרִים. ״וְחַתְחַתִּים בַּדֶּרֶךְ״ — בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁמְּהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ נַעֲשׂוּ לוֹ תְּוָהִים. ״וְיָנֵאץ הַשָּׁקֵד״ — זוֹ קְלִיבוֹסֶת. ״וְיִסְתַּבֵּל הֶחָגָב״ — אֵלּוּ עֲגָבוֹת. ״וְתָפֵר הָאֲבִיּוֹנָה״ — זוֹ חֶמְדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues interpreting verses from Ecclesiastes. The verse states: "Also when they shall be afraid of that which is high and terrors shall be on the road, and the almond tree shall blossom, and the grasshopper shall drag itself along, and the caper berry shall fail, for a person goes to his eternal home, and the mourners circle the marketplace" (Ecclesiastes 12:5). The Gemara explains: **"Also when they shall be afraid of that which is high";** this means **that even a small knoll** on the road **seems to him,** the elderly, **like the highest of mountains. "And terrors shall be on the road";** this means **that while he is walking on the road he will have terrors,** i.e., he will fear falling or otherwise suffering injury. **"And the almond tree shall blossom"; this is the hip bone** that protrudes from the skin of an elderly person. **"And the grasshopper [*ḥagav*] shall drag itself along [*yistabbel*]";** by replacing the letter *ḥet* of *ḥagav* with an *ayin*, **this** can be understood as referring to the **buttocks [*agavot*]** which become heavy [*sevel*]. **"And the caper berry shall fail"; this is** sexual **desire** that ceases.

The *gemara* returns to Kohelet 12, now picking up from *gam migavo'ah yira'u* — even from a height they will fear — which is understood as: *she'afilu gushushit k'tanah domeh lo keharei harim* — even a small mound looks to the elderly person like the tallest of mountains. *Vechatchatin baderech, besha'ah sheholech baderech na'asu lo tohin* — they are fearful when they walk on the road, perceiving every step as a potential danger, afraid they might fall and injure themselves.

*Veyanats hashaked* — the almond tree blossoms. *Zu klivosos* — this is the hip bone, which protrudes from the body in old age.

### Grasshopper Drag

*Veyistabel hachagav* — the grasshopper drags itself along. *Elu agavos* — this refers to the buttocks, which begin to sag.

### Caper Bush

*Vetafer ha'aviyonah* — the caper bush fails. *Zu chemdah* — this is sexual desire, which is lost in old age. The derivation of *chemdah* from *aviyonah* appears to involve taking the *taf* from *vetafer* together with *aviyonah* to yield *ta'avah*, desire — though Rashi clarifies it refers specifically to *chemdat tashmish*, sexual desire.

### Rav's Sigh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא הֲוָה פָּסֵיק סִידְרָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב. כִּי מְטָא לְהַאי קְרָא, נְגֵיד וְאִתְּנַח. אֲמַר, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בְּטֵל לֵיהּ חֶמְדֵּיהּ דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Kahana was reading** biblical **verses before Rav. When he got to this verse,** Rav **sighed.** Rav Kahana **said:** We can **derive from this that Rav's desire has ceased.**

Rav Kahana was reciting the *pesukim* before Rav. When he reached *vetafer ha'aviyonah*, Rav gave a great sigh. Rav Kahana understood from this: *shma mina bital lei chemdeh deRav* — I see that Rav has lost his sexual drive. This is a striking moment, not least because Rav was known to have taken temporary wives when he traveled — a practice attested elsewhere in the *gemara* — and so the loss of that drive would have been felt all the more acutely by him.

### God's Command

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הוּא אָמַר וַיֶּהִי״ — זוֹ אִשָּׁה. ״הוּא צִוָּה וַיַּעֲמוֹד״ — אֵלּוּ בָּנִים. תָּנָא: אִשָּׁה חֵמֶת מָלֵא צוֹאָה, וּפִיהָ מָלֵא דָּם — וְהַכֹּל רָצִין אַחֲרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana** also **said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written:** "For He spoke and it was, He commanded and it stood" (Psalms 33:9)? He understands this to mean that God created man with desires that push him to do things he would not do if he acted purely on the judgment of his intellect, and Rav Kahana therefore interprets the verse in the following manner: **"For He spoke and it was"; this is a woman** that a man marries. **"He commanded and it stood"; these are the children** who one works hard to raise. A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman is** essentially **a flask full of feces,** a reference to the digestive system, **and her mouth is full of blood,** a euphemistic reference to menstruation, yet men are not deterred **and they all run after her** with desire.

Rav Kahana then expounds: *mai dechtiv ki hu amar vayehi, zu ishah* — what is meant by the verse "He spoke and it was" — this refers to the woman. *Hu tziva vaya'amod, elu banim* — He commanded and it stood firm — these are children. The point is that God Himself implanted sexual desire in human beings, which leads men to seek marriage and ultimately to produce children. Without that divinely commanded desire, the rational case for investing in marriage and child-rearing would be far weaker — a point that resonates with evolutionary accounts of why male investment in family formation requires such a powerful biological drive.

### Women Baraita

A *baraisa* states: *ishah chemat meleia tzo'ah* — a woman is like a flask filled with excrement. Rashi reads this as a comment about the shared nature of the human body, not specifically targeting women. *Upiha malei dam* — her mouth is filled with blood, a euphemism for menstruation. *Ve'hakol ratzin achareha* — and yet everyone runs after her. The *baraisa* is making the point about the sheer power of sexual desire: that in spite of everything that might rationally diminish attraction, the drive implanted by God is so strong that men pursue women regardless. One might object that the excrement image need not be specific to women — though the menstruation reference immediately following makes it difficult to read it otherwise. What is theologically interesting is the juxtaposition: the *gemara* has just described the loss of sexual drive in old age as something to be mourned, and now frames the drive itself as something divinely commanded and essential to human continuity — without offering a purely religious justification. Rav's sigh over the loss of his desire was a very human moment, and the *gemara* does not shy away from its profundity.

## Eternal House

### Individual Dwellings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי הוֹלֵךְ הָאָדָם אֶל בֵּית עוֹלָמוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל צַדִּיק וְצַדִּיק נוֹתְנִים לוֹ מָדוֹר לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ. מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ שֶׁנִּכְנָס הוּא וַעֲבָדָיו לָעִיר, כְּשֶׁהֵן נִכְנָסִין — כּוּלָּן בְּשַׁעַר אֶחָד נִכְנָסִין, כְּשֶׁהֵן לָנִין — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נוֹתְנִין לוֹ מָדוֹר לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets the continuation of the verse cited above: **"For a person goes to his eternal home"** (Ecclesiastes 12:5). **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: This teaches that each and every righteous person is given a dwelling place** in the World-to-Come **in accordance with his honor.** The Gemara offers **a parable** in which **a king enters a city along with his servants. When they enter, they all enter through a single gate;** however, **when they sleep, each one is given a dwelling place in accordance with his honor.** So too, although everyone dies, not everyone receives the same reward in the World-to-Come.

The *gemara* now turns to Kohelet 12:5: *ki holech adam el beit olamo* — because a person goes to his eternal house. Rav Yitzchak derives from this that every righteous person receives their own particular dwelling place in *olam ha-ba*. The use of *beit olamo* in the singular — each person's own eternal house — rather than a collective *beit olamim*, suggests individual, differentiated accommodations commensurate with each person's spiritual standing.

### King Parable

The passage has already presented a *mashal* (parable): a king and his servants enter a city together. *Keshen nichnasin kulam be-sha'ar echad nichnasin* — when they enter, they all pass through the same gate. Everyone enters *olam ha-ba* through the same portal. But *u-keshen lanin kol echad ve-echad notnin lo mador le-fi kevodo* — when it is time to retire, each person is given lodging according to their honor. So while all enter together, each righteous person occupies their own unique and appropriately calibrated place in *Gan Eden*.

### Youth Darkens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הַיַּלְדוּת וְהַשַּׁחֲרוּת הָבֶל״ — דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָדָם עוֹשֶׂה בְּיַלְדוּתוֹ מַשְׁחִירִים פָּנָיו לְעֵת זִקְנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "For childhood and youth [*shaḥarut*] are vanity"** (Ecclesiastes 11:10)? Sinful **things that a person does in his youth darken [*mashḥirim*] his face** with shame **as he grows old.**

Rav Yitzchak further expounds: *ki ha-yaldut ve-ha-shachrut havel* — because youth and black hair are vanity. *Devarim she-adam oseh be-yalduto mashchirin panav le-et ziknato* — the things a person does in his youth will darken his face in old age. Rashi connects this to the earlier discussion of sexual licentiousness: excessive indulgence in one's youth comes back to haunt a person in old age, bringing shame and consequence.

## Worm & Soul

### Maggot Needle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: קָשָׁה רִימָּה לַמֵּת כְּמַחַט בַּבָּשָׂר הַחַי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַךְ בְּשָׂרוֹ עָלָיו יִכְאָב״. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מִתְאַבֶּלֶת עָלָיו כׇּל שִׁבְעָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנַפְשׁוֹ עָלָיו תֶּאֱבָל״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַשׂ לְאָבִיו אֵבֶל שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: The maggots** that eat the flesh of the deceased **are as painful to the dead as a needle in the flesh of the living, as it says** with regard to the dead: **"But his flesh is in pain for him,** and his soul mourns over him" (Job 14:22). **Rav Ḥisda said: A person's soul mourns for him** during **all seven days** of mourning following his death, **as it is stated: And his soul mourns over him," and it is** also **written: "And he mourned his father seven days"** (Genesis 50:10).

Rav Yitzchak states: *kashah rimmah la-met ke-machat be-vasar ha-chai* — the maggot eating away at a dead body is as painful to the dead person as a needle piercing living flesh. The dead, it turns out, are not entirely beyond sensation. The verse cited is *ach besaro alav yichav* — even his flesh will pain upon him.

### Soul Mourning

Rav Chisda adds: *nafsho shel adam mitabelet alav kol shivah* — a person's soul mourns for its body for the entire seven days of *shivah*. The soul does not immediately ascend and detach; it remains present, mourning the loss of the body it inhabited. The verses cited are *ve-nafsho alav te'evel* — his soul mourns for him — and *va-ya'as le-aviv evel shiv'at yamim* — establishing that *avel* is a seven-day period.

### Communal Shiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מֵת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מְנַחֲמִין — הוֹלְכִין עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם וְיוֹשְׁבִין בִּמְקוֹמוֹ. הָהוּא דִּשְׁכֵיב בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, לֹא הָיוּ לוֹ מְנַחֲמִין, כׇּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה דָּבַר רַב יְהוּדָה בֵּי עַשְׂרָה, וְיָתְבִי בְּדוּכְתֵּיהּ. לְאַחַר שִׁבְעָה יָמִים אִיתְחֲזִי לֵיהּ בְּחֶילְמֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּנוּחַ דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁהִנַּחְתָּ אֶת דַּעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** In the case of **a deceased person who has no comforters,** i.e., he has nobody to mourn for him, **ten people should go and sit in his place** and accept condolences. The Gemara relates the story of **a certain person who died in Rav Yehuda's neighborhood** and **who did not have** any **comforters,** i.e., mourners; **every day** of the seven-day mourning period, **Rav Yehuda would take ten** people **and** they would **sit in his place,** in the house of the deceased. **After seven days** had passed the deceased **appeared** to **Rav Yehuda in his dream and said to him: Put your mind to rest, for you have put my mind to rest.**

Rav Yehuda teaches: *met she'ein lo menachamim, holchin asarah benei adam ve'yoshvin bi-mekomo* — if a dead person has no mourners, ten people go and sit in the place where that person used to live. The practice of assembling a *minyan* in the home of someone who dies without family — sometimes observed even today — is rooted in the *gemara*'s understanding that the soul of the deceased is still present and sitting *shiva* as it were, in mourning for its own body. The practice reflects a profound respect: no one should depart this world without others experiencing and marking the loss, and the dead person himself is included in that mourning.

### Dream Gratitude

The *gemara* relates that a certain person died in the neighborhood of Rav Yehuda without any mourners. Every day Rav Yehuda gathered ten people and sat in the place where that person had lived. After seven days, the deceased appeared to Rav Yehuda in a dream and said: *tanuach da'atach shehenachta et da'ati* — may your mind be at ease, for you have set my mind at ease. The gratitude expressed by the deceased in the dream affirms the *gemara*'s underlying conviction: the soul of the departed is present, aware, and genuinely affected by whether it is mourned. Beyond the halakhic dimension, there is something profound here — a statement that no one should leave the world without their absence making some impact, without others pausing to register the loss.

### Hearing Eulogies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כׇּל שֶׁאוֹמְרִים בִּפְנֵי הַמֵּת, יוֹדֵעַ, עַד שֶׁיִּסָּתֵם הַגּוֹלֵל. פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי. חַד אָמַר: עַד שֶׁיִּסָּתֵם הַגּוֹלֵל, וְחַד אָמַר: עַד שֶׁיִּתְעַכֵּל הַבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: Everything people say before the deceased, he knows, until** the tomb **is sealed** with **the top-stone. Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **disagreed with regard to** the meaning of **this** statement. **One** of them **said** that the deceased is aware **until** the tomb **is sealed** with **the top-stone,** which is referring to the covering of the grave (*Tosafot*). **And one** of them **said** that it is **until the flesh decomposes.**

Rabbi Abahu states: *kol she'omrin bifnei hameis, yodeiah* — whatever is said in the presence of a dead body, the deceased is aware of it, *ad sheyistom hagolel* — until the coffin is sealed. This has direct implications for the *hesped*, the eulogy. Is a eulogy delivered solely for the comfort of the mourners, or does the deceased also hear it and derive something from it? According to Rabbi Abahu, the deceased hears every word until the moment the coffin is closed. The *gemara*'s treatment of *kavod hameis* — respect for the dead — is thus grounded in a quite literal metaphysical assumption: the soul remains present and aware.

### Awareness Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר עַד שֶׁיִּתְעַכֵּל הַבָּשָׂר, דִּכְתִיב: ״אַךְ בְּשָׂרוֹ עָלָיו יִכְאָב וְנַפְשׁוֹ עָלָיו תֶּאֱבָל״. מַאן דְּאָמַר עַד שֶׁיִּסָּתֵם הַגּוֹלֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיָשׁוֹב הֶעָפָר עַל הָאָרֶץ כְּשֶׁהָיָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The one who said** that it is **until the flesh decomposes** can support his position based on that **which is written** in the following verse: **"But his flesh grieves for him, and his soul mourns over him"** (Job 14:22). This indicates that the deceased is aware of the pain of his flesh in the grave. **The one** who **said** that the deceased is aware only **until** the tomb **is sealed** with **the top-stone** can support his view based on that **which is written** in a different verse: **"And the dust returns to the earth as it was,** and the spirit returns to God who gave it" (Ecclesiastes 12:7). This indicates that when the body returns to the earth, the spirit also returns to its place and is no longer aware of what is happening to the body.

Rav Chiya and Rabbi Shimon *berabbi* dispute the precise duration of this awareness. One opinion: *ad sheyistom hagolel* — until the coffin is sealed. The other: *ad sheyitachel habasar* — until the flesh is consumed, which could be a considerably longer period. The one who says until the flesh is consumed derives it from *ach besaro alav yichav venafsho alav te'eval* — his flesh pains him, and his soul mourns — implying that the soul remains present and aware as long as the flesh is being consumed by decomposition. The one who says until the coffin is sealed derives it from *veyashov he'afar al ha'aretz keshehayah veharuach tashuv el ha'elohim asher netanah* — once the dust returns to the earth, the spirit returns to God, no longer hovering near the body. The question of whether this occurs the moment the body enters the ground or only when the coffin is sealed is itself a point of subtlety.

## Soul Fates

### Garments Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהָרוּחַ תָּשׁוּב אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים אֲשֶׁר נְתָנָהּ״ — תְּנָהּ לוֹ כְּמוֹ שֶׁנְּתָנָהּ לְךָ, בְּטׇהֳרָה — אַף אַתָּה בְּטָהֳרָה. מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁחָלַק בִּגְדֵי מַלְכוּת לַעֲבָדָיו. פִּקְחִין שֶׁבָּהֶן, קִיפְּלוּם וְהִנִּיחוּם בְּקוּפְסָא. טִפְּשִׁים שֶׁבָּהֶן, הָלְכוּ וְעָשׂוּ בָּהֶם מְלָאכָה. לְיָמִים בִּיקֵּשׁ הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶת כֵּלָיו, פִּקְחִין שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶחֱזִירוּם לוֹ כְּשֶׁהֵן מְגוֹהָצִין, טִפְּשִׁין שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶחֱזִירוּם לוֹ כְּשֶׁהֵן מְלוּכְלָכִין. שָׂמֵחַ הַמֶּלֶךְ לִקְרַאת פִּקְחִין, וְכָעַס לִקְרַאת טִפְּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"And the spirit returns to God who gave it"** (Ecclesiastes 12:7) that the words: Who gave it, mean: As it was given. In other words, **give it to Him as He gave it to you;** just as He gave it to you **in purity, you too** should return it to God **in purity.** The Gemara cites **a parable of a king of flesh and blood who distributed royal garments to his servants. The wise ones folded them and placed them in a box [*kufsa*]** to protect them, whereas **the foolish ones went and worked in them.** After **a period of time the king requested** that **his garments** be returned to him. The **wise ones returned them to him pressed,** as they were when the servants received them, and **the foolish ones returned them dirty. The king was happy to greet the wise ones and angry to greet the foolish ones.**

The *gemara* now presents a *baraisa* on the fate of souls, framed by a powerful *mashal*. A human king distributed royal garments to his servants. The wise ones (*pikechim*) folded them carefully, placed them in a box, and preserved them. The foolish ones (*tipshim*) went out and did their work in them, soiling and wearing them out. When the king called for his garments back, the wise ones returned them freshly laundered and gleaming; the foolish ones returned them filthy. The king rejoiced to receive the garments of the wise and was furious at those of the foolish.

### Righteous Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל פִּקְחִין אָמַר: יִנָּתְנוּ כֵּלַי לָאוֹצָר, וְהֵם יֵלְכוּ לְבָתֵּיהֶם לְשָׁלוֹם. וְעַל טִפְּשִׁין אָמַר: כֵּלַי יִנָּתְנוּ לְכוֹבֵס, וְהֵן יֵחָבְשׁוּ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to the wise ones he said: My garments shall be given** back **to the storehouse, and let them go to their homes in peace. And with regard to the foolish ones he said: My garments shall be given to the launderer, and they,** the fools, **will be locked up in prison** as a punishment for degrading the king's garments for their own purposes.

For the wise ones he said: *yinatnu kelai le'otzar* — let my garments be placed in the storehouse where they belong — and *vehem yelchu levateihem leshalom* — and they shall go to their homes in peace. For the foolish ones: *kelai yinatnu lechoves* — my garments must first be sent to the launderer — with the clear implication that the soul will require cleansing in *Gehennom* — *vehem yitchabshu beveit ha'asurim* — and they shall be bound in prison.

### Righteous Bodies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, עַל גּוּפָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים אוֹמֵר: ״יָבֹא שָׁלוֹם יָנוּחוּ עַל מִשְׁכְּבוֹתָם״, וְעַל נִשְׁמָתָן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיְתָה נֶפֶשׁ אֲדוֹנִי צְרוּרָה בִּצְרוֹר הַחַיִּים״. עַל גּוּפָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֵין שָׁלוֹם אָמַר ה׳ לָרְשָׁעִים״, וְעַל נִשְׁמָתָן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֵת נֶפֶשׁ אוֹיְבֶיךָ יְקַלְּעֶנָּה בְּתוֹךְ כַּף הַקָּלַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, also** acts in this way. **With regard to the bodies of the righteous,** which are likened to the royal garments that are well kept, **it states: "He enters into peace, they rest on their beds** each one that walks in his uprightness" (Isaiah 57:2). **And with regard to their souls, it states: "And the soul of my lord shall be bound in the bundle of life** with the Lord your God" (I Samuel 25:29). And conversely, **with regard to the bodies of the wicked, it states: "There is no peace, says the Lord, for the wicked"** (Isaiah 57:21), **and with regard to their souls, it states: "And the souls of your enemies He shall sling out in the hollow of a sling"** (I Samuel 25:29).

God says of the bodies of the righteous: *yavo shalom yanuchu al mishkevotam* — they shall come in peace and lie down in their rest. And of their souls: *vehayetah nefesh adoni tzerurah betzror hachayim* — the soul of my master will be bound up in the bundle of life, held under the *Kise HaKavod*, as the *gemara* will elaborate.

Of the bodies of the wicked: *ein shalom amar Hashem lerasha'im* — there is no peace, says God, for the wicked. Precisely what this means for the physical fate of their bodies is not spelled out. Of their souls: *ve'et nefesh oyvecha yekale'enah betoch kaf hakela* — the soul of your enemies shall be slung as from the pocket of a slingshot, shot across the world.

### Eliezer Souls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נִשְׁמָתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים גְּנוּזוֹת תַּחַת כִּסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיְתָה נֶפֶשׁ אֲדֹנִי צְרוּרָה בִּצְרוֹר הַחַיִּים״. וְשֶׁל רְשָׁעִים זוֹמְמוֹת וְהוֹלְכוֹת, [וּמַלְאָךְ אֶחָד עוֹמֵד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם, וּמַלְאָךְ אַחֵר עוֹמֵד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם, וּמְקַלְּעִין נִשְׁמָתָן זֶה לָזֶה,] שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵת נֶפֶשׁ אוֹיְבֶיךָ יְקַלְּעֶנָּה בְּתוֹךְ כַּף הַקָּלַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The souls of the righteous are stored beneath the Throne of Glory, as it is stated: "And the soul of my lord shall be bound in the bundle of life"** (I Samuel 25:29). **And** the souls **of the wicked are continuously tied up, and one angel stands at** one **end of the world and another angel stands at** the other **end of the world and they sling the souls** of the wicked back and forth **to one another, as it is stated: "And the souls of your enemies He shall sling out in the hollow of a sling"** (I Samuel 25:29).

Rabbi Eliezer elaborates: *nishmatan shel tzadikim genuzot tachat Kise HaKavod* — the souls of the righteous are hidden under God's heavenly chair, as derived from *vehayetah nefesh adoni tzerurah betzror hachayim*. Of the wicked: *zomemot ve'holchot* — they are stretched out and sent across the world. One angel stands at one end of the world and another at the other end, and they slingshot the souls of the wicked back and forth between them — *umekal'in nishmatan zeh lazeh* — as from the pocket of a slingshot.

### Dumah Souls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה לְרַב נַחְמָן: שֶׁל בֵּינוֹנִים מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיכּוֹ שָׁכֵיבְנָא, לָא אֲמַרִי לְכוּ הַאי מִילְּתָא, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ לְדוּמָה נִמְסָרִין. הַלָּלוּ, יֵשׁ לָהֶן מָנוֹחַ. הַלָּלוּ, אֵין לָהֶן מָנוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said to Rav Naḥman: What** happens to the souls **of middling people,** who are neither righteous nor wicked? Rav Naḥman **said to him:** It is good that you asked me this question, for even **if I were dead I would not** have been able **to tell you** that. As **Shmuel said as follows: These and those,** the souls of the wicked and of the middling people, **are handed over to Duma,** the angel in charge of spirits. But **these,** the souls of the middling people, **have rest, and these,** the souls of the wicked, **do not have rest.**

Rabbah then asks Rav Nachman: what about the *beinoni*, the average person — neither fully righteous nor fully wicked? Rav Nachman demurs: *ei k'ma deshchivna lo amri lacho* — if I were to die, I would not be able to tell you, because he is not himself a *beinoni*, implying he would have access to the answer only from the vantage point of having died as one. Shmuel, however, does offer an answer: *elu ve'elu leDumah nimseru* — both the wicked and the average are handed over to *Dumah*, the angel appointed over souls. But among those given to *Dumah*: *halalu yesh lahem manoach* — some have rest — *halalu ein lahem manoach* — and some do not. The wicked have no rest; the average do, though they are not elevated to the place beneath God's heavenly chair that the righteous occupy. This is a striking *gemara*: the righteous alone attain *olam ha-ba* in the fullest sense; the wicked suffer; the average occupy a neutral, restful but undistinguished state — not punished but not rewarded in any active sense either.

## Dust & Resurrection

### Mari Dust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: עֲתִידִי צַדִּיקֵי דְּהָווּ עַפְרָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיָשׁוֹב הֶעָפָר עַל הָאָרֶץ כְּשֶׁהָיָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said:** Even the bodies of **the righteous will** not be preserved and will **become dust, as it is written: "And the dust returns to the earth as it was"** (Ecclesiastes 12:7).

Rav Mari states: *atidi tzaddiki d'havu afra* — in the end, even the righteous will return to dust, derived from *veyashov he-afar al ha-aretz k'she-hayah* — and the dust shall return to the earth as it was. This appears to be a challenge to the assumption, implicit in the earlier *mashal* of the king, that the bodies of the righteous are preserved intact.

### Grave Diggers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ קָפוֹלָאֵי דַּהֲווֹ קָפְלִי בְּאַרְעָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן. נְחַר בְּהוּ רַב אַחַאי בַּר יֹאשִׁיָּה. אֲתוֹ וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן: נְחַר בַּן גַּבְרָא. אֲתָא וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן נִיהוּ מָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא אַחַאי בַּר יֹאשִׁיָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָאו אָמַר רַב מָרִי: עֲתִידִי צַדִּיקֵי דְּהָווּ עַפְרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמַנִּי מָרִי דְּלָא יָדַעְנָא לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״וְיָשׁוֹב הֶעָפָר עַל הָאָרֶץ כְּשֶׁהָיָה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related story: **The diggers who were digging in Rav Naḥman's land** came upon a grave, and **Rav Aḥai bar Yoshiya,** who was buried there, **rebuked them. They came and said to Rav Naḥman: A** deceased **person** just **rebuked us.** Rav Naḥman **came and said to** the person buried there: **Who is the Master,** i.e., who are you? He **said to him: I am Aḥai bar Yoshiya.** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** How has your body been preserved? **Didn't Rav Mari say** that **the righteous will turn to dust?** Rav Aḥai **said to him: And who is Mari, whom I do not know?** Why should I be concerned about what he says? Rav Naḥman **said to him:** Even without Rav Mari's statement, **there is an** explicit **verse which is written: "And the dust will return to the earth as it was"** (Ecclesiastes 12:7).

The *gemara* then relates a remarkable story. Grave diggers were digging in the territory of Rav Nachman when Rav Acha bar Yoshiya, who had been buried there, rebuked them from within his grave. They came to Rav Nachman and reported: *nachar ban gavra* — a person rebuked us. Rav Nachman came and asked: who are you? The response: *ana Acha bar Yoshiya* — I am Acha bar Yoshiya. The story rests on the assumption — consistent with what was said of the righteous in the earlier discussion — that not only are the souls of *tzadikim* preserved beneath the *Kise HaKavod*, but their bodies too remain intact in their graves.

### Nachman Challenge

Rav Nachman pressed him: *velav amar Rav Mari atidi tzaddiki d'havu afra* — but did not Rav Mari say that even the righteous will return to dust? How can your body still be present?

### Acha Jealousy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאַקְרְיָיךְ קֹהֶלֶת, לָא אַקְרְיָיךְ מִשְׁלֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּרְקַב עֲצָמוֹ קִנְאָה״. כָּל מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קִנְאָה בְּלִבּוֹ — עַצְמוֹתָיו מַרְקִיבִים, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קִנְאָה בְּלִבּוֹ — אֵין עַצְמוֹתָיו מַרְקִיבִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥai **said to him: Whoever taught you** the book of **Ecclesiastes did not teach you** the book of **Proverbs, for it is written** in Proverbs: "A tranquil heart is the life of the flesh, **but envy is the rotting of the bones"** (Proverbs 14:30). This means that **anyone who has envy in his heart** during his lifetime, **his bones rot** in the grave, and **anyone who does not have envy in his heart, his bones do not rot.**

Rav Acha bar Yoshiya replied: *u-mani Mari d'lo yadeina lei* — who is this Rav Mari? I never encountered him, and so I do not *pasken* like him. Rav Nachman then pressed him with the *pasuk* itself: *veyashov he-afar al ha-aretz k'she-hayah* — the dust shall return to the earth as it was. Rav Acha responded: *de-akrayakh Koheles lo akrayakh Mishlei* — whoever taught you Kohelet did not teach you Mishlei. For Mishlei says: *u-rekav atzmos kinah* — and the rotting of bones is jealousy. *Kol mi She-yesh lo kinah b'libo, atzmosav markivim* — anyone who has jealousy in his heart, his bones rot. *Kol she-ein lo kinah b'libo, ein atzmosav markivim* — if one has no jealousy in his heart, his bones do not rot. Because Rav Acha bar Yoshiya harbored no jealousy, his body remained preserved. The verse from Kohelet speaks only of those whose bones are subject to this rotting — not of the *tzadik* who has purged himself entirely of envy.

### Resurrection Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּשְּׁשֵׁיהּ, חַזְיֵיהּ דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מְשָׁשָׁא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵיקוּם מָר לְגַוֵּויהּ דְּבֵיתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּלֵּית אַדַּעְתָּךְ דַּאֲפִילּוּ נְבִיאֵי לָא קְרֵית, דִּכְתִיב: ״וִידַעְתֶּם כִּי אֲנִי ה׳ בְּפִתְחִי אֶת קִבְרוֹתֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **touched him** and **saw that he had substance.** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Let the Master arise** and come **into my house.** Rav Aḥai **said to him: You have revealed** that **you have not even studied Prophets,** and it is not just the Writings of which you are ignorant, **for it is written: "And you shall know that I am God when I open up your graves** and lift you up from your graves, My nation" (Ezekiel 37:13). As long as the dead have not been instructed to leave their graves, leaving of their own accord is prohibited.

Rav Nachman then touched him — *gasheshiyei* — and felt actual substance: *chazie d'is bei mashasha* — he saw that there was real physical matter there, that this was not a hallucination or a dream. He said: why not come to my house? Rav Acha replied: *galise da'atakh d'afilu nevi'ei lo kares* — you have revealed that you have not even studied the prophets. For it says: *vi-yedatem ki ani Hashem bifishi es kivroseichem* — you shall know that I am God when I open your graves. Only God — only at the time of *techiyas hameisim* — will the dead be removed from their graves. And the end of the verse: *uvehaalosi eschem mikivroseichem ami* — when I raise you up out of your graves, My people. I can sit up, I can speak, but I cannot leave my grave.

### Second Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְהָכְתִיב: ״כִּי עָפָר אַתָּה וְאֶל עָפָר תָּשׁוּב״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman once again asked Rav Aḥai about the preservation of the flesh, and **he said: But it is written: "For you are dust and you shall return to dust"** (Genesis 3:19), so why has your body not turned into dust? He **said to him: That** verse applies to the righteous only **one hour before the resurrection of the dead,** so that they, too, may be created anew (Maharsha).

One might ask: how do we reconcile the preservation of the body with the verse *ki afar ata veal afar tashuv* — for dust you are and to dust you shall return? Rav Acha answers: *hahu sha'ah achas kodem techiyas hameisim* — that moment of returning to dust is one second before the resurrection. The body is preserved intact until that final instant, at which point it returns to dust — and then God reconstitutes the dust and resurrects the body. This resolution is elegant: the bodies of the righteous are genuinely preserved, Rav Mari's statement stands, and the apparent contradiction dissolves. Furthermore, one who attended to the earlier discussion will note a significant implication: if the *tzadik's* body does not decompose until a single moment before *techiyas hameisim*, then the earlier opinion — that the deceased remains aware until the flesh is consumed — means that the *tzadik* is aware of everything occurring around his grave until the very moment before resurrection.

## Witch Samuel

### Heretic Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אָמְרִיתוּ נִשְׁמָתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים גְּנוּזוֹת תַּחַת כִּסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד. אוֹבָא טַמְיָא, הֵיכָא אַסְּקֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל בִּנְגִידָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ הֲוָה, דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ גּוּפוֹ קַיָּים, וְנִשְׁמָתוֹ עוֹלָה וְיוֹרֶדֶת. לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ הַגּוּף בָּטֵל
>
> **Translation:** **A certain apostate** once **said to Rabbi Abbahu: You say** that **the souls of the righteous are stored beneath the Throne of Glory,** but if so, **how did the oracle woman raise up Samuel using necromancy** if his soul was no longer in this world? Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: There, it was within twelve months** of his death, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: For **a full twelve months** a deceased person's **body remains and his soul ascends and descends,** such that it is sometimes in this world with its body. **After twelve months, the body ceases** to exist, and the passage continues to:

A *min* posed a challenge to Rabbi Abahu: *amrisu nishmasan shel tzadikim genuzos tachas kisei hakavod* — you say that the souls of the righteous are hidden under God's heavenly chair. *Uva'alas ov heicha askiya l'Shmuel* — so how did the necromancer at Ein Dor raise up the soul of Shmuel? If his soul was beneath the *Kise HaKavod*, far beyond this world, how could she possibly have accessed it? The question is not trivial: it presupposes an internal tension between the doctrine of the soul's elevation and the biblical narrative of Shmuel's apparition.

### Twelve Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִשְׁמָתוֹ עוֹלָה, וְשׁוּב אֵינָהּ יוֹרֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **and his soul ascends** to its place beneath the Throne of Glory, **and does not descend anymore.**

Rabbi Abbahu responded: *hasam besoch shneim asar chodesh hava* — that was still within twelve months of Shmuel's death. A *baraisa* teaches: *kol shneim asar chodesh gufo kayam venishmaso ola veyoredes* — for the first twelve months, the body remains preserved and the soul ascends and descends. During that period the soul is still somehow connected to this world, moving between the heavenly realm and the earthly, not yet having fully departed. The necromancer was therefore able to encounter the soul on one of its return visits, when it was accessible in this world.

### After Months

After twelve months: *haguf batel* — the body ceases, it begins to decompose — and *venishmaso ola ve'shuv eina yoredes* — the soul ascends and no longer comes back down. It remains permanently beneath the *Kise HaKavod* and is no longer accessible from below. The witch at Ein Dor could raise Shmuel precisely because she acted within that first year; had she attempted it afterward, she would have found no soul within reach. This brings the *masechta's* final discussion to a fitting close, weaving together the themes of bodily preservation, the soul's postmortem journey, and the limits of what lies within human — or demonic — power to access.
