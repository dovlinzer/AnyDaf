# Shekalim 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tables & Menorahs

### Tables Orientation

The daf opens by continuing the discussion of thirteens in the *beis hamikdash*, which serves as the framework leading into the thirteen *shofaros* — the collection boxes — that are the central subject of our *masechta*. Having already treated thirteen prostrations and thirteen *shulchanot*, the Gemara now addresses the exact placement and orientation of those tables. The backdrop is that in the time of *Shlomo*, ten additional *shulchanot* were added — five to the right and five to the left of *Moshe*'s original *shulchan* — and a parallel set of ten *menorot* was likewise added. A *baraisa* is cited recording a dispute on the matter: *mizrach u'ma'arav hayu netunim* — *Rebbi* holds that the *shulchanot* were oriented east-west; *Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon* holds they were placed north-south, *tzafon ve-darom*.

### Beis Hamikdash Diagram

To appreciate what is at stake, consider the layout of the *heichal*. The interior space runs forty *amot* from the entrance to the *kodesh kodashim*, with an additional twenty *amot* for the *kodesh kodashim* itself. The *shulchan* stood to the north and the *menorah* to the south, each positioned within the inner half of the forty-*amah* space.

The Gemara notes the difficulty with the north-south position: *nimtza shulchan ba'darom* — if the ten additional *shulchanot* are lined up north to south, *Moshe*'s *shulchan* would no longer be the northernmost. Since halachic significance attaches to the northernmost position, this creates a problem. The same issue applies to the *menorah*, which the *pasuk* places specifically to the south (*temana*): were the additional *menorot* arranged north-south, *Moshe*'s *menorah* would no longer be the southernmost. By contrast, if the *shulchanot* and *menorot* are aligned east-west — that is, with each additional vessel running parallel to *Moshe*'s, neither protruding further north nor further south — all the vessels are equally accessible and the primacy of *Moshe*'s originals is uncompromised.

### Shulchan Placement

A *baraisa* provides direct textual evidence in favor of the east-west orientation. *Ha-shulchan hayah natun me-chatzi ha-bayit u'vifnim* — the *shulchan* was positioned on the inner half of the house, meaning from the halfway point of the forty-*amah* space inward. It was drawn *mashuch min ha-kotel shtei amot u'mechetza k'lapei tzafon* — two and a half *amot* from the northern wall. The reason a gap of two and a half *amot* was needed between the *shulchan* and the wall is functional: the *lechem hapanim* had to be approached from both sides, with the old bread being removed and the new bread inserted simultaneously, requiring access from each flank.

The *menorah*, the *baraisa* continues, was situated opposite the *shulchan* on the southern side of the *heichal*. Given that there are only two and a half *amot* between the *shulchan* and the northern wall, there is simply no room for any additional *shulchanot* between it and the wall. This *baraisa* thus clearly presupposes an east-west arrangement and constitutes strong evidence against the north-south view.

The *Korban HaEdah* and the *Tachlin Chadatin* both note the significance of how the *Yerushalmi* handles this evidence. Rather than engaging in elaborate *ukimtot* to salvage the north-south position — as the *Bavli* might do — the *Yerushalmi* simply presents the *baraisa* and moves on. The *Tachlin Chadatin* makes this observation explicit: *v'gam stam matnitin de'Menachot kevata de'Rebbi v'lav orcha d'Yerushalmi le'ha'arich be'pilpula shelo aliba de'hilchata* — the plain *mishna* of Menachot accords with *Rebbi*'s position, and it is not the way of the *Yerushalmi* to extend its dialectic in support of a view that does not reflect the *halacha*. This reflects a genuine and pronounced methodological difference between the two Talmuds.

### Golden Altar

The *baraisa* continues with the *mizbeach ha-zahav*: *haya natun be-emtza ha-bayit* — it was positioned exactly in the middle of the *heichal*, dividing the house at its midpoint, *cholek et ha-bayit mechatziya u'lifnim*. Interestingly, the *Yerushalmi* notes that it was *meshuch kima k'lapei chutz* — drawn slightly outward — so that it was not positioned directly between the *menorah* and the *shulchan* but slightly toward the entrance, leaving an open space between the two vessels. The sole function performed at the *mizbeach ha-zahav* was the burning of the *ketoret*: twice daily, in the morning and afternoon, a *kohen* would offer the incense there. Since the entire *heichal* is roofed, animal sacrifices would not be appropriate in that space. On *Yom Kippur*, blood from the inner sacrifices was also applied to the corners of the *mizbeach*, but the regular daily rite was exclusively the burning of *ketoret*.

The *baraisa* notes that all these vessels — *shulchan*, *menorah*, and *mizbeach ha-zahav* — were *me-shlish ha-bayit u'lifnim*, positioned from a third of the house inward. This apparent contradiction with the earlier statement of "half" is resolved by the *Bavli*: "half" counts from the entrance to the *kodesh kodashim* — twenty *amot* of forty; "a third" counts the entire structure including the *kodesh kodashim* — twenty *amot* of sixty.

### Solomon's Menorahs

The Gemara now turns to the ten *menorot* that *Shlomo* added: *eser menorot asah Shlomo*, as the *pasuk* in *Divrei Hayamim* states — *vayasas menorat ha-zahav eser ke-mishpatam vayitein ba-heichal chamesh mi-yamin u'chamesh mi-smol*, five on the right and five on the left. The question of what "right" and "left" mean here parallels the problem of "north" and "south" discussed above. If one faces east — the standard orientation, reflected in the word *kedem* meaning both "east" and "in front," and in *temana* (south) deriving from *yamin* (right) — then right is south and left is north. That would once again leave *Moshe*'s *menorah*, which is required to be the southernmost, surrounded by others to its south. The Gemara resolves this: *chamisha mi-yamin menorato shel Moshe v'chamisha mi-smolo* — five on each side of *Moshe*'s *menorah*, but oriented east-west, not north-south.

### Menorah Lighting

Even after establishing their placement, a second debate emerges: were all ten additional *menorot* actually lit in practice, or only *Moshe*'s original? One view holds that only *Moshe*'s *menorah* was kindled: *lo haya mavir ela b'shel Moshe bilvad*. The basis is a *pasuk* in *Divrei Hayamim* that refers to the *menorah* in the singular — *menorat ha-zahav ve-neiroteha le'va'er ba-erev ba-erev* — the golden *menorah* and its lamps to kindle every evening. *Rabbi Yosei be-Rabbi Yehuda* takes the opposing view: *al kullan haya mavir* — all of them were lit. His proof is from another *pasuk* in *Divrei Hayamim* that uses plural language — *ve-et ha-menorot ve-neiroteihen le'va'aram ke-mishpat lifnei ha-dvir zahav sagur* — the *menorot* and their lamps to kindle them as required before the *dvir* (the *kodesh kodashim*).

This is a striking dispute. The instinct to say that *Shlomo*'s additional vessels were not actually used for their intended ritual purposes is understandable. The *Torah*'s entire account of the *mishkan* seems to invest the specific *keilim* and their arrangement with profound significance — indeed, the *Torah* begins its description with the *aron* and works outward to the structure itself. To multiply the primary vessels and operate them all in parallel would represent something qualitatively different from constructing the *Beis Hamikdash* at a grander scale. One can therefore appreciate the inclination to read the singular *pasuk* as indicating that, in practice, only *Moshe*'s original *menorah* was ever kindled.

### Gold Purification

The *pasuk*'s description of *Shlomo*'s *menorot* as being made of *michlot zahav* — the purest of gold — introduces a *baraisa* that illuminates the extraordinary refining process involved. *Tani Rabbi Yehuda be-shem Asi*: *haya Shlomo notel elef kikarei zahav u'machnisam la-kur u'motzian ad she'hu ma'amidam al echad* — *Shlomo* would take a thousand talents of ore, place it in a furnace, and reduce it until only one talent of pure gold remained. This is not as paradoxical as it sounds; the process of smelting gold ore to remove impurities is, in fact, how gold is refined even today, and raw ore can contain a very high proportion of dross. The phrase *michlot zahav* is thus read as *kilei zehavo shel Moshe* — they exhausted, through this intense purification, the gold of *Moshe*'s original *menorah* and then some, so great was the quantity processed. The purpose: *likayem mah she-ne'emar kikar zahav tahor asah otah* — to fulfill the Torah's requirement that the *menorah* be of pure gold, which demanded that all impurities be burned away.

### Moshe's Menorah

A *baraisa* adds a remarkable detail about *Moshe*'s own *menorah*. *Maaseh bi-menorat zahav she-asah Moshe ba-midbar* — when *Moshe* fashioned the golden *menorah* in the wilderness, *ve-hayata yeteira dinar zahav* — it came out one *dinar* heavier than the prescribed *kikar*. It was run through the furnace eighty times — *ve-hikhnisuha la-kur shmonim pa'am* — and yet *lo chasra klum*: nothing could be reduced from it. The Gemara explains: *ad de-lo yakum al burarei havi chasar sagi* — before gold reaches its absolutely pure state, repeated smelting continues to drive off impurities and reduce the mass; *min de-kayaum al burarei lo chasar klum* — once it has reached its ultimate purity, there is nothing left to burn away. The furnace does not consume gold itself, only its impurities. *Moshe*'s *menorah*, having been fashioned at the level of ultimate purity, could not be reduced further no matter how many times it was fired. The extra *dinar* was an irreducible part of the purest gold itself. This is, as noted, a rather striking passage — the suggestion that *Moshe*'s *menorah* came out slightly heavier than expected is puzzling, but the Gemara's resolution transforms it into a testament to the extraordinary purity of that vessel.

## Thirteen Shofaros

### Shofaros Categories

The Gemara now arrives at the immediate subject of our *perek*: the thirteen *shofaros* that stood in the *beis hamikdash*. These were collection boxes of a distinctive pyramidal shape — narrow at the top, widening downward — designed so that money could be dropped in but could not be retrieved by hand. Each *shofar* bore an inscription indicating the designated purpose of funds deposited in it.

The thirteen designations were as follows. Two boxes were for *shekalim*: one inscribed *taklin chadatin* (new shekels) for the current year's half-shekel obligation, and one inscribed *taklin atikin* (old shekels) for those paying in arrears from the prior year. Two boxes were for *kinim* — bird sacrifices: one labeled *kinim* generically, and one labeled *gozlei olah* for chick *olos*. One box was for *etzim* (wood for the altar). One was for *levonah* (frankincense). One was for *zahav la-kaporet* (gold for the *kaporet*, the cover of the *aron*, although the *mefarshim* understand this broadly as gold for the *klei shares* generally). The remaining six were designated for *nedavah* — free-will offerings — which would be used for *kayitz ha-mizbeach*, the fund that kept the altar continuously active when private *korbanot* were not being brought.

The existence of boxes for *levonah* and *etzim* reflects a significant halachic development. While the *Torah* specifies *olah*, *shelamim*, and the various types of *mincha* as forms of voluntary sacrifice, the *Chachamim* recognized two additional categories not explicitly mentioned in the *Torah*: the *minchat nesachim* offered independently of any animal sacrifice, and *ketoret* as a private donation. Moreover, the *etzim* are treated by *Chazal* with something approaching the status of a *korban* — *mipnei she-hu ke-korban* — as the *pasuk* uses the term *korban ha-etzim*. The *Bavli* even raises the question of whether privately donated wood should have salt applied to it, as required of all sacrificial offerings — *al kol korbanecha takriv melach*. This expanded range of voluntary offerings is thus not derived from the explicit text of the *Torah* but articulated by rabbinic tradition.

### Old & New Shekels

The inscriptions *taklin chadatin* and *taklin atikin* served a clear administrative function. New shekels (*taklin chadatin*) were for the current year's contribution, which would be collected, stored in the *lishkah*, and drawn upon three times yearly in the *terumat ha-lishkah* to fund the communal *korbanot*. Old shekels (*taklin atikin*) — *mi she-lo heivi eshtakad shokel li-shanah ha-ba'ah* — were for those who had not contributed in the prior year and were now making up the arrears.

### Bird Sacrifices

The two bird *shofaros* introduce a dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Chachamim*. *Rabbi Yehuda* held that both the *kinim* box and the *gozlei olah* box were designated for *olot* exclusively — one for mature pigeons (*torim*) and one for young doves (*benei yonah*), simply two forms of voluntary bird *olot*. The *Chachamim*, however, drew a sharp distinction: the box labeled *gozlei olah* was indeed for voluntary *olot*, but the box labeled *kinim* was split — *echad chatas ve-echad olah* — half for *chatas* and half for *olah*. This reflects the common purification scenario in which a woman — a *zivah* or a *yoledes* — is obligated to bring two birds, one as a *chatas* and one as an *olah*. Rather than personally acquiring, transporting, and presenting live birds to the *beis hamikdash*, she could simply deposit the monetary equivalent into the *kinim* shofar, leave, and be assured that by the end of the day the priests would have offered the appropriate number of sacrifices on behalf of all who had contributed. This system presupposes a fixed market price for birds, so that the total amount deposited could be evenly divided between *chatas* and *olah* obligations.

### Wood & Incense

The minimum for a wood donation is addressed in the *mishna*: *harei alai etzim, lo yifchos mi-shnei gezirin* — one who accepts upon himself to donate wood may not bring less than two logs. The minimum for frankincense is: *lo yifchos mi-kometz* — no less than one handful. These minima govern not only physical donations but also the monetary value that must be deposited in the respective *shofaros*.

### Gold & Kaporet

For gold, the minimum is: *lo yifchos mi-dinar zahav* — no less than one gold *dinar*. The *shofar* for *zahav la-kaporet* — gold for the *kaporet* — raises a conceptual question, since the *kaporet* (the cover of the *aron*) was already constructed and in place. The *mefarshim* therefore understand *kaporet* here generically, as referring to the *klei shares* more broadly, notwithstanding the somewhat unusual terminology.

### Free-Will Offerings

The six remaining *shofaros* were for *nedavah*. The Gemara explains: *lokchim bah olos* — the funds collected in these boxes were used to purchase animal *olot*. The meat went entirely to God (*ha-basar la-Shem*), as is the nature of an *olah*, while the hides went to the *kohanim* (*ha-orot la-kohanim*). These *olot* served as *kayitz ha-mizbeach* — keeping the altar fires occupied at all times, even when no private *korbanot* were being brought.

The legal basis for diverting surplus *chatas* and *asham* funds into this *olah* account is derived from a *midrash* of *Yehoyada ha-kohen gadol*: *asham hu asham asham la-Hashem* — the *pasuk* in *Melachim* states that the silver of *asham* and *chatas* funds shall not be brought into the *beis Hashem* but shall belong to the *kohanim* (*kesef asham ve-kesef chatot lo yuva beis Hashem la-kohanim yihyu*), while another passage indicates that such funds go *la-Shem*. The resolution: one brings an *olah* from such funds, such that the meat goes entirely to God while the hides go to the *kohanim* — *nimtze'u shnei kesuvim kayamim*, both verses are fulfilled simultaneously. This is the source for the rule that *mosar chatas* — money set aside for a *chatas* that exceeded the actual cost — is redirected to *kayitz ha-mizbeach*. A parallel instance of *kohanim* independently interpreting *pesukim* for halachic purposes appears earlier in the *masechta* (3b), where the *kohanim* derived from *ve-chol minchat kohen kalil tihyeh* an exemption from the half-shekel obligation.

## Chatas Money Debate

### R. Yehuda's View

A *baraisa* records *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position: *lo haya shofar shekalim bi-Yerushalayim mipnei ha-ta'aroves* — there was no *shofar* designated for *chatas* bird money in Jerusalem, because of the problem of mixture. This is a striking claim, since it amounts to a rejection of what the *mishna* describes as established practice. *Rabbi Yehuda*, living roughly a century after the *churban*, is arguing that the system described by the *Chachamim* could not have operated as stated, because it is fundamentally incompatible with the laws of *chatas*.

The concern is specific: *shema tamus achas meihen* — suppose one of the donors dies after depositing money into the *kinim* shofar but before it is spent. The money she deposited for a *chatas* is now *dmei chatas meisa* — money designated for a *chatas* whose owner has died — and such money must be allowed to be lost (*yamus*). Once it is mixed with the other funds in the shofar, the entire sum is compromised. *Ve-nimtzu dmei chatayot meisos me'uravot bahen* — money of a *chatas meisa* is now indistinguishably mixed throughout the collection, rendering the whole fund unusable.

*Rabbi Yehuda* does not invoke principles of *bitul* or *rov* as solutions, presumably because he holds *min be-min lo batil* — like-kind is not nullified by like-kind. Even the *Chachamim* who disagree regarding *bitul* accept that *oilin lo mevatlin zeh et zeh* — sacrificial coins do not nullify one another. There is also the conceptual possibility of simply disregarding the eventuality (*lo chayish*), since persons have a *chazakah* of being alive, but *Rabbi Yehuda* does not accept this as sufficient grounds to permit the entire arrangement in principle.

### Baraisa Evidence

Against *Rabbi Yehuda*, the Gemara cites a *baraisa*: a woman who has an obligation to bring bird sacrifices — either as a *yoledes* or as a *zavah* — may bring the monetary value, *dmei kan*, deposit it in the *shofar*, and thereafter eat *kodashim* that same evening. *Ve-einah chosheshet shema nis'atzlu bahen* — she need not be concerned that the *kohanim* were negligent and failed to offer the sacrifices that day. And, crucially, *ve-ein kohen choshesh shema dmei chatas meisa me'ureves bahen* — the *kohen* officiating need not be concerned that the money of a *chatas meisa* is mixed into the fund. This *baraisa* directly contradicts *Rabbi Yehuda* on two counts: it presupposes that *chatas* money was in fact deposited in a *shofar*, and it explicitly dismisses the concern about a deceased donor's funds being mixed in.

### Bittul Solutions

The Gemara proposes a possible solution: *nivrar arba zuzei ve-nishdi be-nahara* — designate four *zuzim* (representing the compromised *chatas* funds) and cast them into a river, thereby segregating and removing the tainted money. This would be analogous to the method sometimes employed in cases of *avodah zarah* that has become mixed with other items, where one symbolically separates and discards the forbidden element once *bitul* has already taken effect.

### No Breirah

The Gemara rejects this approach on behalf of *Rabbi Yehuda*: *Rabbi Yehuda leisa lei breirah* — *Rabbi Yehuda* does not accept *breirah*. It should be noted that the term *breirah* here is applied somewhat loosely: classic *breirah* involves making an act whose content is retroactively defined by future events (as in designating *terumot u'maasrot* to be the portion that will remain at a future date). What is described here is closer to a declaratory fiction — asserting that the four *zuzim* being cast away are specifically the ones that were compromised. Whether or not this constitutes *breirah* in the formal sense, *Rabbi Yehuda*'s non-acceptance of it as a legal mechanism leaves him without a remedy. The conclusion is therefore that according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *chatas* money could not be placed in a communal *shofar*; individuals obligated in a bird *chatas* must bring the sacrifice directly and personally.

## Wood Donation Minima

### One Log vs. Two

Having established that the minimum wood donation is two logs, the Gemara raises a related question: if someone said *harei alai eitz* — "I hereby obligate myself to bring a single log" — can one log constitute a valid minimum, or is the floor always two? The question turns on whether a single log (*gezer echad*) has independent halachic standing as a *korban* unit.

### Yoma Evidence

*Rav Elazar* resolves the question from the *mishna* itself: *masnisa amrah kein — shezeh korban bifnei atzmo ve-zeh korban bifnei atzmo* — each log has independent status as a *korban*. The proof is from a *mishna* in Yoma: *shnayim be-yadam shnei gizrei etzim* — when wood was brought onto the *mizbeiach* each morning as part of the *tamid* service, two *kohanim* participated, each carrying one log independently. The fact that the *Torah*-ordained morning service assigns each *kohen* an individual log demonstrates that a single log is a functionally complete sacrificial unit. Accordingly, one who stipulates *harei alai eitz* is obligated in one log and no more.

### Mizbeach Dimensions

A digression addresses the physical dimensions of the wood on the *mizbeiach*. *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaches: the logs were an *amah* in thickness — *uvyan amah be-amah be-amah sochaktes*, measured in the generous *amah sochaktes* (a loosely measured *amah*, somewhat larger than exact) — but less than an *amah* in length, *amah gedumah*, a truncated *amah*. This produces an unusual proportionality in which the logs were essentially cubic or even wider than they were long. *Rav Chuni be-shem Rav Ami* adds a comparison to the arm of a balance scale (*turtani*), suggesting a specific shape. The *amoraim* then explain the unusual dimensions.

### Log Length Requirement

*Rabbi Shmuel be-Rabbi Yitzchak* provides the rationale: *mipnei she-lo hayah makom ha-ma'arachah ela amah* — the designated space for the woodpile on the *mizbeiach* was only one *amah* by one *amah*. Therefore the logs could not protrude beyond that square, requiring that they be shorter than one *amah* in length. This is illustrated by a *baraisa* enumerating the successive *amah*-wide sections of the *mizbeiach*'s upper surface: *amah ha-yesod*, *amah ha-soveiv*, *amah ha-karkov*, *amah ha-keranot*, and *amah ha-ma'arachah*. Working inward, with one *amah* given to the *keranot* on each side and one *amah* for the *karkov*, one arrives at a one-by-one *amah* central space for the *ma'arachah* — the woodpile. Because the logs must fit entirely within this space, they are cut to less than a full *amah* in length.

## Incense Minima

### Incense Handful

For *levonah* donated to the *beis hamikdash*, the minimum is one *kometz* — one handful. The Gemara derives this through a *gezeirah shavah* linking the *azkarah* of *lechem hapanim* to the *azkarah* of the *mincha*.

### Azkarah Connection

The connection works as follows. By *lechem hapanim*, the *pasuk* states: *ve-nasata al ha-ma'arachah levonah zakah ve-hayetah la-lechem le-azkarah* — the *levonah* placed in spoons upon the *shulchan* alongside the showbread serves as its *azkarah*, its memorial portion. By the *mincha*, the *pasuk* states: *ve-kamatz misham melo kumtzo mi-soltah u'mi-shamnah al kol levonatah ve-hiktir ha-kohen et azkaratah* — the *kohen* takes a *kometz*, a full handful, of the *mincha* and burns it as the *azkarah*. Since both contexts use the term *azkarah*, a *gezeirah shavah* connects them: *mah azkarah ha-amurah le-halan milo kometz, af azkarah ha-amurah kan milo kometz* — just as the *azkarah* of the *mincha* is a full handful, so too the *azkarah* of the *lechem hapanim* consists of a full *kometz* of *levonah* per arrangement, burned weekly when the old bread is removed and the new is set in place.

### Two Spoons Question

This raises an immediate difficulty. By *lechem hapanim* there were two arrangements (*ma'arachot*), each accompanied by its own spoon of *levonah*, yielding a total of two *komatzim* of *levonah* burned each week. If the minimum unit is derived from the *azkarah* of *lechem hapanim*, should not the minimum be two *komatzim* rather than one — *af azkarah ha-amurah kan shnei komatzim*?

### Each Kometz Independent

*Rabbi Ila* resolves this by demonstrating that each *kometz* of *levonah* by the *lechem hapanim* is halachically independent. The argument proceeds through an internal analysis of the *lechem hapanim* itself: the *halacha* is that a *kometz* of the *mincha* that is *chaser* — lacking even a minimal amount — is invalid. If one of the two spoons of *levonah* by the *lechem hapanim* were slightly deficient while the other were slightly in excess, they could not be combined to make up the shortage, because if we treated them as a single unit we would never be able to say that one alone is invalid. The very fact that a deficient *kometz* is invalid on its own proves that each spoon is evaluated independently, as a self-contained unit. Consequently, one *kometz* is a complete and valid minimum — just as each *kohen* who carried a single log of wood in the morning performed a complete act, so too each *kometz* of *levonah* stands on its own.

### Kohen's Handbreadth

A final question concerns the measuring standard for the *kometz*: since a *kometz* is literally a handful measured by the *kohen*'s fingers, whose hand determines the measure? *Rabbi Yosei* in the name of *Rabbi Ila* says: *ha-mitnadev levonah meivi k'kumtzo shel kohen* — the donor must bring an amount corresponding to the handbreadth of the *kohen* who will offer it. According to some, this means the *kohen gadol*; according to others, it means the largest *kohen* available, ensuring that however large the officiating *kohen*'s hand, the amount donated will suffice. *Rabbi Chizkiyah be-shem Rabbi Yirmiyah* offers a lenient view: *afilu be-kometz ba'alim* — the donor may measure by his or her own handful, without regard for the *kohen*'s hand size.

## Gold Donation Minima

### Gold Coin Amount

For gold donations, the *mishna* establishes: *lo yifchos mi-dinar zahav* — one may not give less than one gold *dinar*. *Rabbi Elazar* clarifies: this minimum applies *ve-hu she-hizkir tzurah* — when the donor explicitly specifies coins, i.e., refers to a particular minted form of gold. In that case, the minimum denomination is one gold *dinar*, which in the Roman period represented a substantial sum.

### Generic Gold

However, if the donor did not specify a form — *im lo hizkir tzurah* — but simply said "I will give gold" generically, then *meivi afilu tzinora* — even a tiny amount, such as a gold needle, is acceptable. The determination of the minimum thus turns on whether the donor's language invoked a specific monetary unit or referred to gold as a material substance without further specification.
