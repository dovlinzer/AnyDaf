# Keritot 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Woman's Doubtful Birth

### Mishnah Setup

The daf begins with a *mishnah* whose opening case concerns a woman who has brought a *chatas ha'of* under conditions of doubt. In the context of childbirth, a woman is required to bring both an *olas ha'of* and a *chatas ha'of* at the conclusion of her period of *tuma*. Where there is uncertainty whether what she gave birth to constitutes a halachically recognized birth — for instance, whether the fetus had been conceived for forty days or whether other qualifying conditions were met — she faces a difficulty. Bringing an *olas ha'of* under conditions of doubt poses no real problem: one simply declares it either an *olas chiyuv* if obligated, or an *olas nedava* if not. A *chatas*, however, cannot be brought as a *nedava*, so the ordinary solution is unavailable.

Nevertheless, there is a tradition that in this particular case a woman may bring a *chatas ha'of misafek* — a bird sin-offering conditioned on doubt — in order to become *tehora*. Because of the manner in which a bird offering is slaughtered (from the back of the neck), the meat would be considered *neveila* if the offering turns out not to have been obligatory, and therefore it is not eaten. The *mishnah* does not address a possible *chulin ba'azara* concern head-on; the resolution is either that the need to restore the woman's *tahara* overrides that concern, or that there exists a *masora* — perhaps a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — that specifically permits this practice.

### Safek Chatas Ha'of

The case of the *chatas ha'of misafek* generates its own internal question, which the *mishnah* passes over somewhat quickly: what happens if she becomes certain of her obligation *after* the bird has already been slaughtered? It would seem logical that once certainty is established she ought to be able to eat the offering even if *shechita* has already taken place. Surprisingly, neither the *mishnah* nor the *gemara* explores this scenario directly, leaving the question effectively open.

### Certainty Before Melikah

What the *mishnah* does address explicitly is the case where she becomes certain *before* the *melikah* is performed. The *mishnah* states: *Im ad shelo nimlok noda lah sheyalda vaday, ta'aseh vaday* — if before the *melikah* she learns that she definitely gave birth in the halachically relevant sense, then the offering proceeds as a certain *chatas*, and it may be eaten. The reasoning is that the very same type of offering she would bring for a doubt she now brings for a certainty — *shemin hamin shehevia al hodam mevia al lo hodam* — and therefore nothing prevents the offering from being treated as fully valid and consumed.

## Chaticha Mixtures

### Chalal & Kodesh

The *mishnah* now transitions to the central cases of this section, which revolve around the classic *asham taluy* scenario and its more complex variations. The paradigmatic case: there are two pieces of meat before you, one of *chulin* and one of *kodesh* (sanctified property). You ate one but do not know which. This creates a situation of *safek me'ila* — a doubtful violation of the prohibition against deriving personal benefit from sanctified property. *Rabbi Akiva mechayev asham taluy*: Rabbi Akiva holds that one brings an *asham taluy* even for *safek me'ila*, consistent with his position elaborated in earlier discussions.

### Eating the Second

If the same person then ate the second piece as well, certainty is established: one of the two pieces was definitely *kodesh*, so *me'ila* was definitely committed. All opinions agree that one now brings an *asham vadai*. It is worth noting that a residual doubt remains — over *which* piece the *me'ila* was performed — but this does not constitute a sufficient *safek* to interfere with the obligation. The analogy is to one who knows he performed a *melacha* on *Shabbos* but cannot recall which *av melacha* it was: since the identity of the sin is certain, the uncertainty about which specific object it was performed on does not affect the *korban* obligation. One similarly brings an *asham vadai* here, and the question of which piece was *kodesh* — which bears on the repayment obligation — does not affect the *korban*.

### Reuven & Shimon

The *mishnah* then introduces the scenario where two different individuals eat the two pieces: Reuven ate the first piece and Shimon ate the second. Each one is now in a state of *safek me'ila* individually.

### R. Akiva: Two Asham

Rabbi Akiva rules: *zeh mevi asham taluy ve-zeh mevi asham taluy* — each brings an *asham taluy* independently. Two further implications are embedded in this ruling. First, Rabbi Akiva holds that an *asham taluy* is brought for *safek me'ila* at all. Second — and this connects back to a debate from the opening of the *masechet* — by ruling that Shimon also brings an *asham taluy*, Rabbi Akiva implicitly holds that one need not have eaten from two pieces simultaneously in order to incur the obligation of *asham taluy*. When Shimon ate the second piece, only one piece remained before him; there was no longer a pair of pieces from which the doubt arose in the moment of eating. Yet Rabbi Akiva requires him to bring nonetheless.

### R. Shimon: Joint

Rabbi Shimon proposes an alternative: *sheneihem mevi'im asham echad* — let both bring a single *asham* jointly, with stipulation. Reuven declares: if I sinned, this is my *asham vadai*; if I did not, I relinquish my portion to Shimon. Shimon makes the reverse declaration. The *korban* is thus brought without a definitively identified owner, which raises questions about *shechita lishmo ba'alim*, though a *shetam* slaughter would suffice. The technical issue of sanctification and ownership can in principle be resolved through appropriate *kinyan* and conditional *hakdasha*. Rabbi Shimon's real innovation is the claim that stipulated joint ownership is a viable mechanism even for an *asham*.

### R. Yosi: No Joint

Rabbi Yosi rejects Rabbi Shimon's solution: *ein shenayim mevi'im asham echad*. Two people cannot jointly bring one *asham* — or, as becomes explicit later, one *chatas* brought for sin. This is not merely a technical objection about the mechanics of sanctification, but a principled position: a *chatas* or *asham* that comes to atone for sin must be specifically identified with a known owner. One might distinguish this from cases where animals have become physically mixed up — where the *kohen* simply brings the offering for whoever owns it — because in that scenario the uncertainty is about which animal is whose, not about whether a given person is the owner at all. Here the very identity of the obligated party is indeterminate from the outset, and that, for Rabbi Yosi, is disqualifying.

One might wonder whether the same concern applies to a *chatas* brought for *tahara* purposes rather than for sin — for instance, the *chatas* of a *yoledes*. Rabbi Yosi's formulation, elaborated most explicitly later in the *mishnah*, is *kol chatas sheba'ah al cheit ein shenayim mevi'im osah* — any *chatas* that comes on account of sin cannot be brought by two people jointly. This language suggests his objection is limited to sin-atoning offerings, leaving open the theoretical possibility that a *chatas* for *tahara* might be different.

An interesting related question is raised regarding *smicha* and *viduy*: if one does not know whether one sinned, how can one perform *smicha* and make a proper *viduy*? Although *smicha* is generally not *me'akeiv*, there is a *Tosafos* that addresses the case of *tme'im* who are *meshalei'ach* their *korbanos* when they cannot perform *smicha* themselves, since a *shaliach* cannot perform *smicha* on one's behalf — a topic worth studying further, but one the *gemara* does not raise in this context.

## Chelev Combinations

### Chelev & Chulin

Having established the framework, the *mishnah* now presents a series of cases that vary the nature of the prohibition involved. The first is the standard *asham taluy* scenario: two pieces before you, one *cheilev* (forbidden fat) and one *chulin*. You ate one and do not know which. This is the classic case — *mevi asham taluy*. If you then eat the second piece as well, you have certainly eaten *cheilev* — *mevi chatas*. Here we have moved entirely away from the *safek me'ila* context and into the paradigmatic case of doubtful consumption of a forbidden substance.

When Reuven eats the first piece and Shimon the second, each brings an *asham taluy*. The *mishnah* is implicitly relying on the assumption that one need not have two pieces in front of oneself at the moment of eating — *chatichah mishtei chatichot* is not required — since Shimon ate when only one piece remained.

Rabbi Shimon again applies his principle: *sheneihem mevi'im chatas achas* — they bring one *chatas* jointly with stipulation. Rabbi Yosi again objects: *ein sheneihem mevi'im chatas achas*.

### Chelev & Nosar

The *mishnah* then considers: one piece is *cheilev* (one *aveira*, one *chatas*) and the other is *cheilev venotar* (forbidden fat that is also leftover sacrifice — two violations, two *chataos*, with *me'ila* bracketed as we shall explain). You ate one and do not know which — *mevi chatas ve'asham taluy*: you certainly ate *cheilev*, and you may have also committed the violation of *nosar*, so you bring a *chatas* and an *asham taluy*.

If you eat the second piece as well: *mevi gimel chatasos* — three sin-offerings — one for each of the two *cheilev* violations and one for *nosar*, assuming a *yedi'ah* separated the two acts of eating. The question of why no *asham* for *me'ila* is brought in the *nosar* case is addressed by the *gemara* directly: the *nosar* piece is generally not worth a *shaveh perutah*, the minimum value required for *me'ila* liability to apply. The reasoning is that once meat becomes *nosar* it loses nearly all its practical value — though it is not categorically excluded from *me'ila*, as Rashi notes that so long as it could theoretically be left on the *mizbe'ach* it retains some residual value to *hekdesh*.

### Chelev & Kodesh

The next variation: one piece is *cheilev chullin* (ordinary forbidden fat) and the other is *cheilev kodesh* (forbidden fat of a sanctified animal). Both pieces are forbidden, but for different reasons: the first gives rise to a *chatas*, and the second gives rise to a *chatas* for *cheilev* plus, potentially, an *asham* for *me'ila*. You ate one and do not know which: *mevi chatas* — you certainly ate *cheilev* — and now the question of *me'ila* is in doubt. *Rabbi Akiva omer af asham taluy*: Rabbi Akiva says you additionally bring an *asham taluy* for the doubtful *me'ila*. The other *tanna'im* do not require this.

If you eat the second piece: *mevi shtei chataos ve'asham vadai* — two *chataos* for the two *cheilev* violations, and an *asham vadai* for the certain *me'ila*.

Where Reuven and Shimon each eat one piece: each brings a *chatas* for the certain *cheilev*, and *Rabbi Akiva omer ze veze mevi'im asham taluy* — Rabbi Akiva says each also brings an *asham taluy* for the doubtful *me'ila*.

Rabbi Shimon: each brings his own *chatas* individually, and for the *asham* — since between them they are certainly liable for one — *ushneihem mevi'im asham echad*, they bring one joint *asham* with stipulation. Rabbi Yosi: *ein sheneihem mevi'im asham echad* — you cannot do this.

### Chelev Kodesh Mix

The most complex case: both pieces are *cheilev*, but both are also *kodesh* — one is simply *cheilev kodesh*, while the other is *cheilev kodesh venotar* (sanctified forbidden fat that is also leftover). Here the full complexity of overlapping prohibitions comes into view. One piece carries two violations (*cheilev* and *me'ila*), while the other carries three (*cheilev*, *me'ila*, and *nosar*). The *me'ila* from the *nosar* piece is again potentially excluded on the grounds of insufficient value.

When Reuven and Shimon each eat one piece, each certainly ate *cheilev*, each is in *safek* regarding *me'ila*, and each is in *safek* regarding *nosar*. Each brings a *chatas* for *cheilev*, and each brings an *asham taluy* for the doubtful *nosar* — assuming, again, that one need not have had two pieces before oneself. Rabbi Shimon: each brings a *chatas* individually, and *sheneihem mevi'im chatas achas* jointly for the *nosar* with stipulation. Rabbi Yosi: *kol chatas sheba'ah al cheit ein shenayim mevi'im osah* — the most fully articulated statement of his position. Any sin-atoning *chatas* requires a specific, identified owner; it cannot be brought as a joint stipulated offering.

The two assumptions running through this entire *mishnah* — beyond Rabbi Akiva's *asham taluy* for *safek me'ila* — are worth highlighting. First, the *mishnah* consistently treats the second person in the Reuven-Shimon scenarios as bringing an *asham taluy* even though he ate from only one piece, not two: *chatichah mishtei chatichot* is not required. Second, an *asham taluy* is brought even in cases where *both* options involved a sin — one just does not know which sin. This is somewhat surprising, since an earlier *mishnah* seemed to endorse the view that *asham taluy* is limited to cases where one *might not have sinned at all*. The present *mishnah* qualifies this: if the two possible violations would have given rise to *different* kinds of *korban* (a *chatas* on one hand, an *asham* on the other), the uncertainty is sufficient to warrant an *asham taluy* for the doubtful *chatas*-type violation.

## Issur Chal Al Issur

### Nosar's Value

The *gemara* (Rava's question to Rav Nachman) focuses on several of the surprising features of the preceding *mishnah*. The first issue concerns the *nosar* case: when both pieces have been eaten and we say *mevi gimel chatasos*, why is no *asham vadai* brought for *me'ila* on the *nosar* piece? Rava raises this question explicitly, and Rav Nachman answers: *de-leika shaveh perutah* — the *nosar* piece is not worth a *shaveh perutah*. Since *me'ila* liability requires that the benefit derived be worth at least a *perutah*, and *nosar* has so little practical value, no *me'ila* obligation arises.

This answer does not reflect a categorical exclusion of *nosar* from *me'ila*. Rashi notes that meat which has become *nosar* retains some minimal connection to *kodesh* value because it could theoretically remain on the *mizbe'ach*. Rather, the claim is that its value in practice is so diminished — since you cannot use it for anything permitted — that a typical piece of *nosar* would not reach the *shaveh perutah* threshold.

The *gemara* then challenges this: but earlier cases in the *mishnah* (involving *cheilev kodesh* that was not *nosar*) assumed the piece *was* worth a *perutah*, as evidenced by the fact that an *asham vadai* for *me'ila* was required. Can the same piece be worth a *perutah* in one case and not in another? The answer is that the two cases involve structurally different objects: a piece of sanctified *cheilev* that has not become *nosar* is worth a *perutah* — *kohanim* could eat the non-fat portions of the animal, and the fat itself has value to be placed on the *mizbe'ach* — whereas *nosar* of the same physical size is worth so little in its degraded state that it falls below the *shaveh perutah* threshold.

The *gemara* then raises a further challenge: but we have a *mishnah* that describes a single piece of meat combining multiple violations — among them *nosar* and *me'ila* together — where four *chataos* and one *asham* are brought. That case demonstrates that *nosar* is not categorically excluded from *me'ila*. The resolution: it depends on size — *hahi begassah, v'hacha bedakkah* — or, alternatively, on the season: in summer, when meat spoils quickly, the already-minimal value of *nosar* drops below a *perutah*, while in winter it might retain marginal value. The upshot is that *nosar* is not inherently outside the category of *me'ila*, but in most practical cases its value will not meet the *shaveh perutah* minimum.

This discussion is also notable for a broader reason: the question of *me'ila*'s *shiur* — whether it is measured by *kezayit* (as for eating violations) or *perutah* (as for theft-like violations) — is implicitly touched upon here. By defining *me'ila* liability in terms of *perutah* even in the context of eating, the *gemara* signals that *me'ila* carries a distinctive monetary dimension alongside its character as a prohibition against personal benefit.

### R. Shimon's View

The *gemara* now turns to a deeper structural question. Several cases in the *mishnah* involve a single piece of meat that is subject to multiple prohibitions simultaneously — for example, *cheilev* that is also *kodesh*, or *cheilev* that is also *nosar*. Rabbi Shimon agrees to these cases (he counts the multiple violations), yet Rabbi Shimon is famously the *tanna* who holds *ein issur chal al issur* — that a second prohibition cannot take effect upon an object already subject to a prior prohibition. The question Rava poses to Rav Nachman is: does Rabbi Shimon really hold *issur chal al issur* in cases of *issur kolel* and *issur mosif*?

### Fat & Me'ilah

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *Rabbi Shimon omer, ha'ochel neveilah b'Yom HaKippurim — patur*. One who eats *neveilah* on Yom Kippur is exempt (from the Yom Kippur prohibition). Yom Kippur is clearly an *issur kolel* — it applies to all foods, not just *neveilah* — and yet Rabbi Shimon says its prohibition cannot take effect on something already forbidden as *neveilah*. By the same logic, Rabbi Shimon would seemingly reject *issur mosif* as well. And even more strikingly, the *gemara* notes that even *issur chamur* — a weightier prohibition — does not, in Rabbi Shimon's view, overcome *ein issur chal al issur*: Yom Kippur carries *kares* (weightier) while *neveilah* does not, yet Rabbi Shimon still says *patur*. So the question sharpens: how does Rabbi Shimon agree that *cheilev* of *kodesh* meat also carries *me'ila*, since the *issur me'ila* would be taking effect on top of the already-established *issur cheilev*?

### Simultaneous Effect

The resolution: *ella b'kodshim gali rachmana d'ati issur chal al issur* — by sanctified property, the Torah itself has revealed that a second prohibition *can* take effect on top of a prior one. A *baraita* derives from the phrase *asher l'Hashem* that even one who eats the *imurim* (the fats designated for the *mizbe'ach*) while *tamei* — already subject to *me'ila* and *cheilev* — also incurs an obligation for the *tumah* violation. Three prohibitions thus simultaneously govern one piece of fat: *cheilev*, *me'ila*, and *tumah*. By *kodashim*, the Torah has created a special exception to *ein issur chal al issur*.

Rebbi (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi), who generally holds that *issur chal al issur* does apply — but only when the second prohibition is weightier (*issur chamur al issur kal*) — is shown to hold an even broader principle within the domain of *kodashim*: even a lighter prohibition can take effect on a weightier one. The proof is the *issur me'ila*, which carries only *mita b'yedei shamayim* and is thus lighter than *issur cheilev* (which carries *kares*), yet the Torah explicitly states that *me'ila* takes effect on the fat. From this Rebbi derives — and the *gemara* confirms — that by *kodashim* even a lighter prohibition can take effect on a heavier one.

(Why is *mita b'yedei shamayim* considered lighter than *kares*? Various explanations are offered. Some suggest that *kares* involves premature death *plus* dying childless — *kares ariri* — making it more severe. Rashi here argues that the fact that *kares* leads to an obligation to bring a *chatas* gives it greater halachic weight, whereas *me'ila* leads to an *asham*, which is seen as less weighty — though this reasoning has its difficulties, as Rabbi Akiva would apply *me'ila* to *asham taluy* as well.)

Yet despite Rebbi's expansive view within *kodashim*, Rabbi Shimon explicitly disagrees even with the limited *kodashim* exception: *Rabbi Shimon omer, ein pigul be-olim ve-ein nosar be-olim* — the portions that go on the *mizbe'ach* cannot become *pigul* or *nosar*, since they are already under the prohibition of *me'ila* and, in Rabbi Shimon's view, no additional prohibition can take effect on them even in the *kodashim* context. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela tanna'i hi* — it is a dispute among *tanna'im* whether the *kodashim* exception to *ein issur chal al issur* applies. According to the position that it does (represented by our *mishnah* insofar as Rabbi Shimon agrees to the multiple-violation cases there), Rabbi Shimon holds the exception. According to the stricter position — Rabbi Shimon's ruling about *olim* — he does not.

For the view that even by *kodashim* there is no *issur chal al issur*, the verse *kol chelev l'Hashem* establishing *me'ila* on the fat must be explained differently: it refers specifically to an animal born to a sanctified mother (*yaldei kodashim*), where the *issur cheilev* and the *issur me'ila* take effect simultaneously at the moment of birth — *b'havayatan hen kedoshim* — so there is no sequence of one prohibition following another, and the question of *issur chal al issur* does not arise at all.

To summarize the conclusions of this extended analysis: *nosar* is not categorically excluded from *me'ila*, but in many practical cases will not meet the *shaveh perutah* threshold. The principle of *ein issur chal al issur* has a special *kodashim* exception according to one view among the *tanna'im*, explaining how a single piece of meat can simultaneously carry violations of *cheilev*, *me'ila*, and *nosar*. Rabbi Shimon's position is internally complex: in the *mishnah* regarding Reuven and Shimon's stipulation he appears to accept the *kodashim* exception, but his explicit ruling about *olim* suggests he does not — and the *gemara* resolves this as a genuine *machlokes tanna'im*.

## Asham Taluy Resolved

### Didn't Sin Case

A new *perek* begins, building directly on a hint from the previous *mishnah*: the case of the *yoledes* who brought her *chatas ha'of* under conditions of doubt and then discovered she was definitively obligated. The new chapter systematically addresses the parallel scenario for an *asham taluy*: one brings an *asham taluy* and subsequently discovers either that one definitely did not sin, or that one definitely did. What is the status of the *asham* at various stages of its processing?

### Before Shechita

The *mishnah* opens: one who brought an *asham taluy* and then discovered that he had not sinned — *Im ad shelo nishchat* — if this is discovered before *shechita*, *yetzei v'yirei ba'eder*, the animal is released back to the flock. This is the ruling of Rabbi Meir. The *gemara* will explain: Rabbi Meir holds that sanctification requires actual knowledge of need — *keivan delo tzarich lei lo makdish lei* — so if it turns out there was no obligation, the original *hakdasha* is retroactively void.

### After Shechita

If the discovery comes *after shechita*, Rabbi Meir rules *yishapeich hadam vehabasar yeitzei l'veit hasreifa* — the blood is spilled and the meat goes to be burned. Since the *hakdasha* was invalid according to Rabbi Meir, the slaughter was that of *chulin ba'azara*. One might ask whether this should require burial (*kevura*) rather than burning (*sreifa*), since burning is typically reserved for *kodshim she'nifsalu* while *chulin ba'azara* are buried. The distinction is that in the *asham taluy* case the doubt was genuine at the time — the offering has more of the character of *kodshim* — so the *chumra* of *sreifa* is applied. We will see below that the comparable case of an *asham vadai* where one discovers no sin was committed is treated differently, calling for *kevura*.

The *chachamim* disagree with Rabbi Meir. Their position — *mitoch libo nokfo, gamar umakdish* — is that because one's heart troubled one, because the psychological reality of doubt was present at the moment of sanctification, the *hakdasha* was valid and complete. The fact that the doubt resolves in either direction afterward does not undo a valid act of sanctification made under genuine uncertainty. This is the key conceptual divide of the chapter.

### Blood Sprinkled

If the blood has already been sprinkled on the altar and one then discovers one had not sinned, *yei'acheil* — even Rabbi Meir agrees the meat may be eaten. At the moment of *zerikat hadam* the doubt was fully present, the offering was validly processed, and the *korban* reached completion in a state of *kashrut*. The subsequent discovery of innocence cannot retroactively invalidate what was done under legitimate doubt.

### R. Yosi: Blood OK

Rabbi Yosi extends this principle one step further: *afilu hadam bekos yizarek vehabasar yei'acheil* — even if the discovery comes while the blood is already collected in the vessel and has not yet been sprinkled, one may still go ahead and sprinkle the blood, and the meat may be eaten afterward. Rabbi Yosi holds *kol ha'omeid lizarek kezaruak dami* — whatever stands ready to be thrown is halachically considered as if already thrown. Since the blood is in the vessel and about to be sprinkled, it is treated as if *zerikat hadam* has already occurred, and the same rule applies as in the post-sprinkling case.

## Other Conditional Cases

### Asham Vadai

The *mishnah* draws a contrast with the *asham vadai* — the definite guilt-offering. If one sanctified an animal for an *asham vadai* or a *chatas* and then discovered one had not sinned, this is an instance of *hekdesh b'ta'ut* — a mistaken sanctification. The assumptions of fact that prompted the *hakdasha* were simply wrong, not merely uncertain. Accordingly, *ad shelo nishchat — yetzei v'yirei ba'eder*: before *shechita*, it goes back to the flock, with *universal* agreement (not just Rabbi Meir). If *shechita* has already occurred: *harei ze yikaver* — it is buried, not burned. This is because it never had valid *kedusha* — it was *chulin ba'azara* from the start — and *chulin ba'azara* is buried. After blood sprinkling: *habasar yeitzei l'veit hasreifa* — the meat is burned, because the sprinkling of the blood of something that was never a valid *korban* renders the meat akin to *kodshim she'nifsalu*.

### Shor Haniskal

The *shor haniskal* — the ox sentenced to be stoned — follows the same general pattern as the *asham vadai*, not the *asham taluy*. If the witnesses are found to be lying before the ox is executed (*ad shelo niska*), it is released: *yetzei v'yirei ba'eder*. The entire *gmar din* was a mistake, and the ox never had the status of *shor haniskal*. If it was already stoned and the deception is then discovered: *mutar behana'ah* — it is permitted for use, since it no longer bears the status of *shor haniskal* once the mistaken judgment is exposed. It has the status of *neveilah* but can be used for benefit.

### Egla Arufa

The *egla arufa* — the heifer whose neck is broken in the valley when a murder victim is found and the killer is unknown — is the case most structurally similar to the *asham taluy*, since it too is brought because of an unresolved doubt (the identity of the murderer). Yet the *mishnah* groups it with the *asham vadai* and *shor haniskal*, not with the *asham taluy*, for the pre-breaking stage.

*Ad shelo ne'erfah* — before the neck is broken — if the murderer is identified, *teitzei v'teira'eh ba'eder*: she goes back to the flock. Even the *chachamim* agree to this. This is initially puzzling: is the *egla arufa* not brought because of genuine *safek*, just like the *asham taluy*? The *gemara* will explore this. One possible distinction is that the *egla arufa* does not have *kedusha* in the same way an *asham taluy* does, so the question of sanctification under doubt does not arise in the same manner.

*Mishene'erfah* — after the neck is broken — if the murderer is then identified, *tikaver bimkomah*: she is buried where the neck was broken. The reason given: *she'safek ba mitechilah, kaperas sefeikah vehalchah lah* — it was brought originally for a doubt, it atoned for that doubt at the moment the neck was broken, and it has fulfilled its purpose. The discovery of the murderer afterward does not undo what was done under valid *safek*. In this respect the *egla arufa* post-breaking closely parallels the *asham taluy* after *zerikat hadam*: once the act of atonement was performed at a moment when the doubt was genuinely present, the subsequent resolution of the doubt is irrelevant.

The precise question of why the *chachamim* agree to Rabbi Meir on the pre-breaking stage for *egla arufa* but disagree on the pre-*shechita* stage for *asham taluy* is left for the *gemara* to address — whether it turns on the presence or absence of *kedusha*, or on some deeper difference in the nature of the two atonements.

## Sanctification Theory

### R. Meir: Knowledge

The *gemara* articulates the theoretical basis of the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* in precise terms. Rabbi Meir's position: *keivan delo tzarich lei lo makdish lei* — since it turns out he did not need it, he did not truly sanctify it. Rabbi Meir locates the validity of *hakdasha* in the knowledge of actual need. If the need is retrospectively absent, the intention to sanctify was, in his view, never sufficiently grounded to create valid *kedusha*.

### Sages: Intent

The *chachamim* counter with a principle that is almost psychological in character: *mitoch libo nokfo, gamar umakdish* — because his heart troubled him, because the subjective experience of doubt was genuinely present, he made up his mind and fully sanctified the animal. The *kedusha* was real at the moment it was declared, because the doubt that motivated it was real. The resolution of that doubt in either direction thereafter cannot unravel what was validly done.

### When Sin Confirmed

The *gemara* notes that the *baraita* extends the dispute of Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* to both directions: *bein shenoda shelo chata uvein shenoda shechata pligi* — they disagree whether one discovers one did not sin *and* whether one discovers one did sin. In either case, once certainty is established, the *asham taluy* has become superfluous, and the question of whether its *hakdasha* was valid arises equally.

Why state both cases? *L'hodia kocho d'Rabbi Meir*: to demonstrate the full force of Rabbi Meir's position. Even if one discovers one *did* sin — meaning the worst-case scenario has been confirmed and one will need to bring a *chatas* — Rabbi Meir still rules that the *asham taluy* animal is released (*tetzei v'tireh ba'eder*), because at the moment of sanctification the person did not know this, and therefore *keivan d'be'idna d'afrasha lo yada* — since at the time of setting it apart one did not know — the *hakdasha* is retroactively void.

Conversely, *l'hodia kocho d'Rabbanan*: to demonstrate the full force of the *chachamim*'s position. Even if one discovers one did *not* sin — even though one is entirely off the hook — the *chachamim* hold the *asham taluy* retains its *kedusha*, because *keivan d'be'idna d'afrasha lo yada, libo nokfo, hilkach gamar umakdish* — since at the time of setting it apart one did not know and one's heart was troubled, one fully and validly sanctified it. That psychological reality at the moment of *hakdasha* is what gives the *kedusha* its force, and no subsequent discovery can dissolve it.
