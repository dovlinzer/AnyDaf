# Kiddushin 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reish Lakish Kal Vachomer

### Reish Lakish's Claim

The *daf* picks up in the middle of the ongoing discussion of Reish Lakish's claim that an *amah ivriyah* goes free when her father dies. The argument, framed as a *kal vachomer*, runs as follows: she goes free when her father dies while she is still under her father's control; therefore, by logical inference, she should similarly go free when her father dies even after she has been sold and is under her master's control. The *Gemara* has been lining up proofs against this position, but Reish Lakish has managed to deflect each one. The present passage introduces yet another challenge.

### Rav Amram's Challenge

Rav Amram raises a challenge from a *braisa*. The *braisa* states that the following are the categories of slaves who receive the *hanaka* — the gifts with which the master must load the slave upon release: one who goes free after six years (*shanim*), one freed by the arrival of *yovel* during those six years, one freed through *mitat adon* (death of the master, in the case of a *nirtza*), and an *amah ivriyah* who goes free upon the emergence of *simanim* (signs of puberty). If Reish Lakish's position is correct that the *amah ivriyah* also goes free when her father dies, the *braisa* should have mentioned *mitat ha'av* (death of the father) as an additional case.

### Fixed vs. Uncertain

One might respond that the *braisa* is merely an abbreviated list and does not purport to be exhaustive. To this, the *Gemara* has previously offered the counter-argument that the *braisa* uses the formulation "and these," implying a complete enumeration. An alternative reconciliation was proposed: the *braisa* lists only those cases of liberation that have a fixed or predictable character, and the death of the father — which may occur at any point — lacks that fixed quality. The *Gemara* then pushes back: *simanim* also lack a fixed time, yet the *braisa* includes them. The prior answer to that objection — associated with Rav Safra — was that *simanim* have at least a minimum age threshold, lending them some degree of temporal predictability.

### Reish Lakish Rejected

Now, however, the *braisa* explicitly mentions *mitat adon* — the death of the master — which is likewise entirely unpredictable and has no fixed time. Yet the *braisa* does not mention *mitat ha'av*. This closes off any remaining line of defense. The *Gemara* declares: *tyuvta d'Reish Lakish tyuvta* — a complete and unqualified rejection of Reish Lakish's position.

### Simanim vs. Father's Death

The *Gemara* then addresses the underlying *kal vachomer* itself. Even setting aside the *braisa*, the *Gemara* argues that the *kal vachomer* was inherently flawed from the outset. The challenge (*pircha*) to the inference is this: the case of *simanim* carries with it an intrinsic change — *nishtana ha-guf*, the body itself has been transformed. The *amah ivriyah* has moved from childhood into adulthood, and it is precisely this independent transformation of her identity that is sufficient to sever the relationship with her master, even though the same *simanim* may not suffice to free her from her father's domain (where she must reach *bogeres*, not merely *na'arah*). The death of the father, by contrast, changes nothing about her — neither physically, nor in her relationship to the master. The master's authority over her is entirely unaffected by what happens to her father. The death of the father is relevant to her connection to her father, not to her connection to the master. Accordingly, the *kal vachomer* fails, and there is no basis to conclude that *mitat ha'av* grants freedom.

## Hanaka Distribution

### Contradictory Baraitot

Having dispensed with the *kal vachomer*, the *Gemara* turns to revisit the question of the *hanaka*. A tension emerges between two *baraitot*. One *braisa* states that when the *eved ivri* receives the *hanaka*, he keeps it for himself — and likewise the *amah ivriyah* keeps her *hanaka* for herself. A second *braisa*, however, states that the *hanaka* of the *amah ivriyah*, as well as her found objects (*metzi'ot*), go to her father; the master receives only *sechar battalah* — compensation for the time she took away from her service in order to retrieve those found objects, since while the master is entitled to her *ma'aseh yadayim* (her productive earnings), the category of *metzi'ah* is treated differently under *halacha* and remains associated with the father's rights. In fact, one can see a certain internal logic here: if while she is still in service, her found objects go to the father, then when the master releases her and gives the *hanaka*, one might equally say it flows to the father — except that one could also argue that the entire purpose of the gift is to benefit the person who actually did the labor.

The contradiction between the two *baraitot* must be resolved.

### Father Alive Case

The *Gemara* initially proposes a straightforward reading: perhaps one *braisa* refers to the case where she goes free by *simanim* (so she is now a *na'arah* and her property belongs to her father), while the other refers to the case where she goes free because the father died (in which case there is no father to collect it, and she keeps it herself). This reading, however, would seem to provide support for Reish Lakish's rejected position — namely, that *mitat ha'av* is itself a mechanism of liberation. The *Gemara* therefore rejects this reading. In truth, both *baraitot* speak of the same case: she goes free by virtue of *simanim*. The question is not the mode of liberation but the status of the father. In one *braisa*, the father is alive, and therefore the *hanaka* — like the earnings of a *na'arah* — goes to him. In the other, the father has already died, and therefore she retains it.

### Father Dead Case

This resolution, though technically satisfying, does raise a question of what the *chiddush* is. If the father is dead, it is self-evident that there is no one for the money to go to — why does a *braisa* need to tell us she keeps it? The *Gemara* clarifies: the *chiddush* is specifically to exclude the brothers. One might have thought that just as a father has rights in his daughter's earnings and found objects during her minority, those rights pass to his sons after his death. The *braisa* teaches that this is not so. A person does not transmit the rights he holds in his daughter to his sons. The source for this is the verse regarding *avadim kena'ani* — "you shall bequeath them to your children after you" — from which the *Gemara* derives that one bequeaths real property, an *eved kena'ani*, to one's children, but not one's rights in a daughter. Those rights belong exclusively to the father and do not pass to the brothers. Hence, if the father has died, the *hanaka* belongs to the *amah ivriyah* herself, and the brothers have no claim.

### Hebrew Slave's Hanaka

The *Gemara* then turns to the apparently obvious ruling in the *braisa* that the *eved ivri* keeps his own *hanaka*. This seems self-evident — who else would receive it? Rav Yosef uses the idiomatic expression that Rashi explains as meaning one has made a mountain out of a molehill, or a large city out of a tiny *yud*. Nevertheless, Abaye cites Rav Sheshet, who quotes a *tanna* named Tuta'i to the effect that the *hanaka* goes to the *eved ivri* himself and not to his creditor. This is the genuine *chiddush*: even if the *eved ivri* has outstanding debts, the *hanaka* cannot be seized by his creditors. It belongs to him, and he alone decides when and how to use it to satisfy his obligations.

## Four Cases of Hanaka

### Four Cases Listed

The *Gemara* now returns to the *braisa* itself: the four categories of those who receive the *hanaka* — one who goes free through *shanim* (the six-year term), through *yovel*, through *mitat adon*, and the *amah ivriyah* through *simanim*.

### Mitat Adon Logic

The case of *mitat adon* deserves attention. For a regular *eved ivri* serving out a six-year contract, death of the master does not terminate service — the slave continues working for the master's son, as he has simply sold a block of labor. But for a *nirtza* — one who had his ear pierced and committed to serve indefinitely — *mitat adon* does terminate service and brings the *hanaka*. This is precisely because the *nirtza*'s status is closer to genuine subjugation, like real property. And the *chiddush* is that *yovel* overrides even this extended commitment, and death of the master likewise ends it. The point is to underscore that even the *nirtza* is not an *eved kena'ani* — he is not heritable property. Similarly, the *amah ivriyah* goes free upon *mitat adon* in every case, without any scenario of *nirtza* applying to women.

### Exclusions: Who Doesn't…

Two categories are explicitly excluded from the *hanaka*: the *boreach* (runaway slave) and the *yotzei begiraon kesef* (one who redeems himself by paying back the remaining value of his service). The *Gemara* derives the inclusive cases from the phrase *"lo teshalchennu reikam"* — "you shall not send him away empty-handed" — read in conjunction with the doubled language *"hanek ta'anik"*, which implies the obligation applies whenever there is a sending-away, under all such circumstances. The exclusions are derived from the phrase *"vechi tishalchennu chafshi me'imach"* — "when you send him free from you." This phrase highlights the requirement that the release be a sending "from you" — meaning, initiated or effectuated from the master's side. The *boreach* ran away on his own; the *yotzei begiraon kesef* is redeeming himself.

### Self-Redemption Logic

The exclusion of the *yotzei begiraon kesef* is at first glance surprising. If anything, self-redemption seems like the most empowering and dignified form of liberation — the slave has managed to gather funds and buy back his freedom. Why should he be the one to forfeit the *hanaka*? The *Gemara*'s answer turns on the phrase *"me'imach"* — "from you." Whose act is this release? When the six years expire, or when *yovel* arrives, the Torah compels the master to release the slave — it is a release "from you." When the slave redeems himself, by contrast, the initiative comes from the slave; it is he who is effectively freeing himself. As Rashi explains in this context, even though the master is halachically obligated to accept the payment, the process is fundamentally one in which the slave is the active party. This is the same conceptual framework as was encountered earlier in the discussion of *ishah konah et atzmah* and *gevoah ka-mazkei leih* — where Heaven itself is said to effect the transfer, making it not a release "from you" but something external to the master-slave relationship. What drives the distinction is not whether the master acts willingly, but whether the freedom is externally imposed upon the relationship or whether the slave himself is the one dissolving it.

### Boreach and Yovel

The *boreach* case is more intuitively understandable: one who ran away should not be rewarded with a *hanaka* for service that was never rendered. The precise formulation of the exclusion is *"mi sheshilucho me'imach"* — "one whose sending is from you" — which does not apply to someone who departed without authorization. However, the *Gemara* clarifies the exact scenario in which the *boreach* is excluded.

### Boreach Repayment

When a slave runs away and is later recaptured, the principle of *hashlama* applies — he must make up the time he was absent. This is derived from the verse *"shesh shanim ya'avod"* — "he shall work six years" — meaning a full six years of actual service, not merely six calendar years from the date of purchase. If the slave runs away but then returns and completes his full six years before *yovel*, he would have fulfilled his obligation and would presumably be entitled to the *hanaka*. The specific case of exclusion, as Rav Sheshet explains, is where the slave ran away and *yovel* intervened before he could make up the lost time. In such a case, he genuinely stole service from his master — the *yovel* arrived during what should have been makeup time, cutting short his obligation to repay. One might have thought that since ultimately it was *yovel* that freed him — and *yovel* is a release "from you" in the broad sense — he should receive the *hanaka*. The *Gemara* rejects this: the fact remains that he failed to provide the service he owed, and therefore he does not receive the *hanaka*.

### Sick Leave Rules

A related question arises: does illness count against the slave the same way that running away does? The *Gemara* derives from the verse *"uvashvi'it yeitze"* — "in the seventh year he shall go free" — that involuntary absence does not carry the same consequence as willful flight. Two *baraitot* appear to contradict one another on this point. One states that if the slave was ill for three years and worked three, he does not have to make up the time. The other states that only if he was ill for all six years must he make up the time. Rav Sheshet reconciles them: the operative rule is that if the slave was ill for a majority of the period — more than three years — he must make up the excess. If he was ill for no more than half the time, the illness is absorbed as sick leave and he need not make up anything. However, if during the period of illness he was still performing some form of service — the *Gemara* invokes the image of needlework performed while bedridden — then even a majority of illness may be effectively reduced, since he was not entirely idle. The *Gemara* resolves the apparent internal contradiction of the *braisa* as follows: the threshold of three years and a day into illness is treated as if it were illness for the entire six, and requires *hashlama*.

### Tosafos: Slaves vs. Teac…

Tosafos raises a practically significant question: can one learn from these rules about the *eved ivri* to determine the sick leave entitlement of hired workers, specifically *melamdei tinokos* — schoolteachers contracted for a year? Some authorities had argued that just as the *eved ivri* is granted sick leave up to half his term without obligation to compensate, so too a hired teacher ill for up to half the contracted period need not make up the time. Tosafos rejects this comparison. The reason the *eved ivri* receives generous sick leave is rooted in *gufo kanui la-adono* — there is an element of bodily acquisition in the relationship. The master, in effect, has purchased the slave, paid a purchase price upfront, and as long as the slave is not willfully withholding service, the sale remains in force even if no labor is rendered. The situation resembles a *kinyan* model rather than a payment-for-services model. A teacher, by contrast, has merely hired out his services for a defined period. If services are not rendered, there is nothing to pay for — just as one does not pay a plumber who sends a bill without performing the repair. Therefore, a teacher who is ill may collect only for the time he actually taught, with no sick leave credit.

This distinction — *gufo kanui* versus *sechirus* — sits at a conceptually intermediate point between full property ownership (as with an *eved kena'ani*) and an ordinary employment arrangement. It is precisely because the *eved ivri* occupies this unusual middle ground that the sick-leave rules applicable to him cannot be generalized to other hired workers.

## Hanaka Amount Dispute

### Rabbi Meir: Fifteen

The *Gemara* now takes up the question of how much the *hanaka* must be. *Tanu rabanan*: five *sela'im* of each category mentioned in the verse — grain, flock, and wine — for a total of fifteen *sela'im*. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. A *sela* is equivalent to four *zuz*, so fifteen *sela'im* equals sixty *zuz* — roughly a tenth of the value of a *kesuvah*, a not inconsiderable sum.

### Meir's Gezeira Shava

Rabbi Meir derives his figure through a *gezeira shava* on the word *reikam* ("empty-handed"). The verse here says *"lo teshalchennu reikam"* — you shall not send him away empty-handed. The word *reikam* also appears in the context of *pidyon ha-ben*, where it is written *"velo yera'u fanai reikam"* — they shall not appear before Me empty-handed. By the *pidyon ha-ben*, the fixed amount is five *shekel*. Rabbi Meir therefore learns: just as there it is five, so here it is five.

### Five of Each Category

One might ask: why five of each category rather than five total, distributed among all three? The *Gemara* answers that this depends on the placement of the word *reikam* in the verse. Since the verse places *"lo teshalchennu reikam"* before enumerating the three categories — grain, flock, and wine — each category independently carries the force of "not empty-handed." Accordingly, the slave must receive the minimum from each category individually, such that the total must be at least fifteen *sela'im*. The *Gemara* clarifies that Rabbi Meir's figure of five per category is not rigid in its composition — one may give less from one category and more from another — but the aggregate must reach fifteen.

The *Gemara* then asks why we do not make the *gezeira shava* instead from the *olat re'iyah*, where *reikam* also appears but the minimum implied is only two *shekel*. The answer: the verse here says *"asher berachecha Hashem Elokecha"* — "as God has blessed you" — indicating generosity. When there is a choice between two possible *gezeira shava* links, the more generous reading, supported by the language of blessing, is preferred.

### Rabbi Yehudah: Thirty

Rabbi Yehudah says the *hanaka* is thirty *shekel*, corresponding to the thirty *shekel* paid when an *eved kena'ani* is gored by an ox.

### Yehudah's Source

Rabbi Yehudah derives this through a *gezeira shava* on the word *netinah* ("giving"). The verse here uses *"titein lo"* — "you shall give him" — and the passage regarding a gored *eved kena'ani* also uses the language of giving: *"kesef shloshim shekalim yitein la-adonav"* — thirty *shekel* of silver shall be given to his master. Although one involves an *eved kena'ani* and the other an *eved ivri*, Rabbi Yehudah learns one from the other: just as the giving there is thirty, so too here.

The *Gemara* challenges: why not instead make the *gezeira shava* of *netinah* from *erchin*, where *netinah* also appears and the amount can reach fifty *shekel*? Two responses are offered. First, *tafasta meruba lo tafasta* — if you reach too high you may not hold on to it; when there is uncertainty, the safer and more modest claim is preferred. Second, it is more apt to learn slave from slave: the *eved kena'ani* and the *eved ivri* share the common context of *avdus*, making that *gezeira shava* the more natural one.

### Rabbi Shimon: Fifty

Rabbi Shimon says the *hanaka* is fifty *shekel*, corresponding to the highest *erech* value in *erchin* — the fixed valuations the Torah prescribes for a person by age and sex, the maximum of which is fifty *shekel*.

### Shimon's Gezeira Shava

Rabbi Shimon likewise uses a *gezeira shava* on *netinah*, but links it to *erchin* rather than to the gored slave. The *Gemara* asks: why not simply say fifty is too much and apply *tafasta meruba lo tafasta*? Furthermore, and why not learn from the slave (thirty) rather than from *erchin* (fifty)? The *Gemara* answers that Rabbi Shimon's *gezeira shava* is not on the word *netinah* at all. Rather, he employs a different *gezeira shava* — on the word *mach* ("poor"): by the *eved ivri* it says *"vechi yamuch achicha"* — "if your brother becomes impoverished [and sells himself]," and by *erchin* it says *"ve'im mach hu"* — "if he is poor." Since the shared word is *mach*, and it links specifically to *erchin*, there is only one possible *gezeira shava* to make, and one cannot redirect it elsewhere. That is how Rabbi Shimon arrives at fifty.

### Gezeira Shava Tradition

This discussion opens onto a broader principle regarding how *gezeira shava* functions, articulated here by Rashi. The rule is *ein adam dan gezeira shava me-atzmo* — one cannot construct a *gezeira shava* on one's own initiative; it must rest on a tradition transmitted from one's teachers, ultimately going back to Sinai (*mesurah hi lahem me-raboteihem u-mi-Sinai*). Yet the *Gemara*'s back-and-forth — asking why Rabbi Yehudah doesn't use the *gezeira shava* of *erchin*, or why Rabbi Shimon doesn't use that of the slave — seems to presuppose that the Tanna has some latitude to choose which *gezeira shava* to apply.

Rashi resolves this by clarifying what exactly is transmitted as tradition. The tradition is not a fully specified pairing — "this word links to that word and produces this *halacha*." Rather, the tradition conveys only that a particular *halacha* is derivable through *gezeira shava* involving a particular word. The specific link — which other passage that word connects to — is left for the scholar to reason through. Thus, for example, Rabbi Yehudah knew that the *hanaka* amount was to be derived through a *gezeira shava* on *netinah*; but whether to link it to the *netinah* of *erchin* or the *netinah* of the gored slave was a question he had to work through using his own analysis, guided by principles such as *tafasta meruba* or *eved me-eved*. Similarly, Rabbi Shimon's tradition was built on the word *mach* rather than *netinah*, which is why the challenge of redirecting it to the slave's thirty does not apply to him — his link is unique.

This represents, as Rashi emphasizes — and the Ramban makes the same point in his *Sefer Ha-Mitzvot* — a nuanced middle ground between pure *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* and free-form rabbinic derivation. The anchor in tradition is the word that opens the *gezeira shava*, but the application of that anchor to derive a specific ruling retains a genuine human analytical dimension.

## Blessing Requirement

### Things That Increase

The *Gemara* now asks: given that Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon hold that the *hanaka* is a single fixed sum (thirty or fifty *shekel*) and not broken down by category, why does the Torah bother to mention specifically grain (*goren*), flock (*tzon*), and wine (*yekev*)? For Rabbi Meir the enumeration is easily understood — five from each. But what work do these categories do for the other opinions?

The *Gemara* answers from a *braisa*: one might have thought that the *hanaka* must come specifically from these three. The verse then says *"asher berachecha Hashem Elokecha"* — indicating that the master should give from whatever God has blessed him with, not limited to these three. But if the enumeration of grain, flock, and wine is not meant to restrict the categories, why mention them? To teach a limiting principle going in the other direction: just as these three share the characteristic that they are *bechlal bracha* — things that experience natural increase — so too, whatever is included in the *hanaka* must be something of that nature. Grain, sheep, and wine all increase naturally: flocks reproduce, crops grow abundantly, grapes yield wine in quantity. It is this capacity for blessing-driven increase that qualifies them as the paradigm.

### Money & Mules Excluded

From this principle of *bechlal bracha* the *Gemara* derives two exclusions. Rabbi Shimon holds that money is excluded — coins lying in a vault do not naturally grow; they have no intrinsic increase. Mules are excluded by Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — mules, being sterile hybrids, do not reproduce and therefore do not experience the blessing of increase. Each of these Tannaim, however, offers a response on behalf of the other category: Rabbi Shimon would allow mules, arguing that they do become stronger and more valuable through use and feeding — a form of increase even without reproduction. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov would allow money, noting that money productively invested does yield increase. Thus the two opinions disagree about which of the two potential exclusions is the correct one.

A further question arises from this discussion that the *Gemara* leaves somewhat open: is the requirement that the *hanaka* come from *bechlal bracha* items a restriction on what the master may use to fulfill his obligation, or is it a *lechatchila* directive that he give from each such category in his possession? The *Rambam*'s formulation on this point was not entirely transparent, and whether the master must give representatively from every qualifying category or simply from any one of them remains somewhat ambiguous.

The *Gemara* then establishes the logical necessity of the threefold enumeration. Had the Torah mentioned only *tzon*, one might have thought the *hanaka* must come from living creatures. Had it mentioned only *goren* (grain), one might have restricted it to products of the earth. The inclusion of both expands the category to all things that experience natural increase. The specific mention of *yekev* (wine) then adds the further limiting function: to underscore through these paradigmatic examples that the category is one of *bechlal bracha*, thereby excluding money and mules.

### Discretionary Approach

The *braisa* then presents a different reading of the verse altogether. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah reads *"asher berachecha Hashem Elokecha"* at face value: if the master's household was genuinely blessed through the slave's service — if the master's fortunes grew as a result of the slave's work — then he must give the *hanaka*. If the master was not so blessed, there is no obligation. One can anticipate the practical problem this creates: who determines whether the blessing was "because of" the slave? The potential for abuse is obvious. Nevertheless, Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah maintains that this is the plain meaning of the verse.

### Chazal's Quantification

Against this, the first opinion holds that the doubled language *"hanek ta'anik"* — "you shall surely grant him gifts" — indicates the obligation is unconditional, regardless of whether the master prospered. The verse's reference to *"asher berachecha"* then becomes not a condition but a calibration: you are obligated to give in any event, but how much you give should reflect how much you were blessed. If the slave brought great prosperity to the household, the master should be commensurately generous beyond the minimum.

What this discussion lays bare is the gap between the verse's apparently open-ended, discretionary formulation and the fixed quantities established by the Tannaim. The simple reading of the verse does not suggest a specific amount — it evokes generous giving proportional to divine blessing. It is *Chazal* who are uncomfortable leaving the obligation undefined and who therefore quantify it into a halachically actionable minimum. In this sense the structure parallels that of *terumah*, where the Torah likewise leaves the amount open and the rabbis define a recommended quantity. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, one may infer, would reject the fixed amounts of fifteen, thirty, or fifty and treat the entire matter as proportional and discretionary — though even he would presumably acknowledge that the rabbinic figures represent a reasonable concrete floor.

## Service to Heir

### Son vs. Daughter

The final section of the *daf* discusses the question of whether an *eved ivri* continues his service after the master's death, and for whom. The rule established in the *braisa* is: an *eved ivri* works for the son of a deceased master but not for the daughter. An *amah ivriyah*, by contrast, goes free upon the master's death in any case — she does not continue in service for either a son or a daughter. Similarly, an *eved ivri* sold to a non-Jew would not continue working for the non-Jew's heirs upon his death.

### Textual Source

The derivation proceeds from two verses. One verse says *"va-avadcha shesh shanim"* — "and he shall work for you six years" — which implies working *for you*, not for your heir. The other verse says *"shesh shanim ya'avod"* — "he shall work six years" — without specifying for whom, implying the six years must be completed regardless. The *Gemara* reconciles these: the second verse, which speaks unconditionally of six years, applies to the case of the son. The first verse, which specifies "for you," excludes other heirs.

### Son as Continuation

This raises the question: on what basis is the son singled out as the one heir for whom the slave continues to work, rather than some other heir? The *Gemara* answers: *"sheken kam tachas aviv"* — the son stands in the place of his father; he is the primary continuation of the father's identity. Two proofs are cited for the principle that the son serves as the father's continuation. First, *yi'ud*: the Torah permits the master's son to perform *yi'ud* — betrothing the *amah ivriyah* — if the father himself did not do so. This demonstrates that the son steps directly into the father's place in the relevant legal relationships. Second, *sdeh achuzah*: if a man's ancestral field is sold and his son purchases it, the field does not go to the *kohanim* at *yovel* but reverts to the original owner. This rule, derived through *drashot* on the relevant verses, is grounded in the son's unique status as extension of the father.

### Brother Excluded

Could one argue that the brother, not the son, is a stronger continuation of the deceased — on the grounds that the brother performs *yibum*, which is explicitly described as "standing in the place of his brother"? The *Gemara* rejects this: *yibum* applies only when there is no son. The very fact that *yibum* is required only in the son's absence demonstrates that the son is the primary and natural continuation of the father, and the brother's role through *yibum* is merely a secondary substitute when the primary continuation is absent. Therefore it is the son, not the brother, who is considered to "stand in the father's place" for the purposes of continued service.

### Brother Excluded

The *Gemara* adds one further clarification. One might argue that even if the son is a stronger candidate than the brother, the son still has *two* connections to his father that establish continuation — *yi'ud* and *sdeh achuzah* — while the brother has only one (*yibum*). So why does the *Gemara* rest its entire argument on the logical priority demonstrated by *yibum* yielding to the son's existence, rather than simply saying the son wins on the basis of two factors to one? The *Gemara* explains that the *sdeh achuzah* precedent is itself only knowable through the same logical inference: the *pesukim* governing *sdeh achuzah* do not explicitly specify which heir causes the field to revert to the father. The assignment of that role to the son (and not the brother) is derived precisely because we already know the son is the primary continuation — which we know from the principle that *yibum* exists only where there is no son. Both proofs, in other words, ultimately converge on this single foundational point: the son's primacy as continuation of the father is a given, from which all else flows. And it is on this basis that the *eved ivri* continues to work for the son, but for no other heir.
