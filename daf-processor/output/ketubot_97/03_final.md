# Ketubot 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Widow Selling Fields

### Introduction to Selling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד מוֹכֶרֶת?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **How does** a widow **sell** property to earn money for her support?

The daf opens with the word *keitzad* — "how does she sell?" — which signals that the Gemara is not merely continuing a narrative but is asking a structural question: what exactly is the mechanism by which a widow, dependent on her late husband's heirs for sustenance, goes about selling off their fields? This is daf 97a, and the scenario is one in which the heirs are failing to provide the widow with her *mezonot*, forcing her to take matters into her own hands by selling fields that belong to the estate — fields that could otherwise have been used to pay the *ikar ketubah* or to support the *yorshim* themselves.

The complication is immediate and practical. The moment she remarries, her right to *mezonot* from the estate vanishes. If she has already sold a field to a buyer, and then gets married the following month, the heirs will turn to that buyer and demand the field back, arguing that the widow had no right to convey what was not truly hers to give. The buyer, naturally, is left in an awkward position. So the question of *keitzad mocheret* — how exactly does this sale work — is not a theoretical one.

### Rav Huna's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי דָּנִיאֵל בַּר רַב קַטִּינָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מוֹכֶרֶת אַחַת לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, וְלוֹקֵחַ מְפַרְנֵס אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Daniel bar Rav Ketina said** that **Rav Huna said: She sells** her late husband's property **once every twelve months and the buyer** who purchased the property from her **provides** her with money **once every thirty days.**

Rav Daniel bar Rav Ketina, a name that appears rarely in the Talmud, cites Rav Huna, the *Rosh Yeshiva* of Sura, as follows: *mocheret achat lishneim asar chodesh velokeach mefarnes achat lishloshim yom* — she sells enough to cover twelve months of sustenance, and the buyer pays her in monthly installments of one month at a time. The logic is structural rather than commercial. The buyer does not hand over the full value of the field at once, because doing so would immediately vest the property in him regardless of what happens to her *mezonot* rights. Instead, by paying month by month, the arrangement functions more like a rental with an option to purchase: each monthly payment covers one month's worth of *hana'ah* from the field, and the *yorshim* retain a lien on the property for as long as she remains entitled to *mezonot*. Should she remarry mid-course, the buyer returns whatever portion he has not yet paid, and the field reverts to the heirs.

Rashi's language makes this explicit: *she'im tinasei yachazir mah shebeyado leyorshim* — if she remarries, he returns what remains in his hand to the heirs. The total sum, *kol hama'os*, represents the full purchase price of the field outright; the reason the buyer does not simply pay that sum upfront is precisely to preserve the heirs' lien against the eventuality of her remarriage. Once the full price has been paid out, the *yorshim* lose their claim entirely.

### Rav Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: מוֹכֶרֶת לְשִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים, וְלוֹקֵחַ מְפַרְנֵס אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda said: She sells** once **every six months and the buyer provides** her with money **once every thirty days.**

Rav Yehuda takes a more conservative position: *mocheret lishisha chodashim velokeach mefarnes achat lishloshim yom* — she sells for only six months at a time, with the same monthly payment structure. Where Rav Huna permits her to sell up to a twelve-month horizon, Rav Yehuda limits her to six months. In both opinions, however, the monthly installment arrangement is the same.

### Baraitot & Ameimar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: מוֹכֶרֶת לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, וְלוֹקֵחַ מְפַרְנֵס אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: מוֹכֶרֶת לְשִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים, וְלוֹקֵחַ מְפַרְנֵס אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna: She sells** once **every twelve months, and the buyer provides** her with support **once every thirty days.** So too, it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda: She sells** once **every six months, and the buyer provides** her with money **once every thirty days.**

The Gemara presents a *baraita* in support of Rav Huna's twelve-month position, and separately a *baraita* supporting Rav Yehuda's six-month position: *tanya kavatei deRav Huna… tanya kavatei deRav Yehuda*. Having two *baraitot* supporting two contradictory opinions does not resolve the question of which is authoritative. Ameimar, however, rules: *hilcheta mocheret leshisha chodashim velokeach mefarnes achat lishloshim yom* — the *halacha* follows Rav Yehuda.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר, הִלְכְתָא: מוֹכֶרֶת לְשִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים וְלוֹקֵחַ מְפַרְנֵס אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: דְּרַב הוּנָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, כְּלוֹמַר: לָא סְבִירָא לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said: The *halakha*** is that **she sells** once **every six months and the buyer provides** her with money **once every thirty days,** in accordance with the opinion of Rav Yehuda. **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: What** do you have to say about the opinion **of Rav Huna? He said to him: I did not hear** about this statement; **that is to say, I do not hold** in accordance with it.

Rav Ashi challenges Ameimar: *veRav Huna mai?* — what do you make of Rav Huna? Both Rav Huna, the *Rosh Yeshiva* of Sura, and Rav Yehuda, the *Rosh Yeshiva* of Pumbedita, are contemporaries of roughly equal authority, and we have a *baraita* behind each of them. Why rule definitively like one over the other?

Ameimar responds: *lo shemi'a lei* — I have not heard it. The Gemara clarifies that this expression should be understood as *kelomar lo sevira lei* — that is, not that Ameimar was simply unaware of Rav Huna's ruling, but rather that he does not accept it. This is a recognized idiom in *Chazal*: because everything is oral tradition, saying "I have not heard" functions the way we might say in English "I don't hear you" — meaning, I don't agree. Ameimar is registering a substantive disagreement with Rav Huna's position, not pleading ignorance, and therefore *paskens* like Rav Yehuda.

## Liens & Ketubah

### Rav Sheshet Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מוֹכֶרֶת לִמְזוֹנוֹת, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּחֲזוֹר וְתִטְרוֹף לִכְתוּבָּה?
>
> **Translation:** § The students **raised a dilemma to Rav Sheshet:** If a woman **sells** property **for** her **sustenance, what is** the *halakha*? **Can she return and seize** those very properties that she had sold, as payment **for** her **marriage contract?**

The next question is addressed to Rav Sheshet, and it is a genuinely intricate one: *mocheret lemezonot mahu shetachzor vetitrof lekhetuvatah?* — if a widow sells estate property in order to fund her *mezonot*, can she later return and seize that same property to collect her *ketubah*?

### Liens Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ בִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף. דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אַרְמַלְתָּא דְּזַבֵּין — אַחְרָיוּת אַיַּתְמֵי, וּבֵי דִינָא דְּזַבֵּין — אַחְרָיוּת אַיַּתְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **They raised this dilemma in** reference to a *halakha* established **by Rav Yosef, as Rav Yosef said:** In the case of **a widow who sold** liened property to a third party, **the** property **guarantee** rests **upon the orphans.** If the property was seized from the purchasers in payment of a previous debt, then the purchasers are reimbursed by the orphans. **And** so too, in the case of **a court that sold** property belonging to the deceased, **the** property **guarantee** rests **upon the orphans.**

To appreciate the difficulty, one must understand the mechanics of *shiabud* — the mortgage or lien that attaches to property in the context of debt. When a person takes a loan, all of his landed property becomes mortgaged to that debt. If he subsequently sells that property and then defaults, the creditor may approach the buyer and seize the land, leaving the buyer to seek reimbursement from the debtor. This is equally the case with a *ketubah*: when we write *ketubot*, we declare that everything the husband owns — even, as the formula puts it, the coat on his back — is mortgaged to the *ketubah*. If the husband dies insolvent, the widow may pursue property he sold during the marriage and seize it to satisfy her *ketubah*, leaving the purchaser to recover his money from the estate.

### Ketubah vs. Mezonot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאַחְרָיוּת אַיַּתְמֵי, טָרְפָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא מָצֵי אָמְרִי לַהּ: נְהִי דְּאַחְרָיוּת דְּעָלְמָא לָא קַבֵּילְתְּ עִילָּוָךְ, אַחְרָיוּת דְּנַפְשָׁךְ מִי לָא קַבּוֹלֵי קַבֵּילְתְּ?
>
> **Translation:** Is the *halakha* that **since the** property **guarantee** rests **on the orphans, she** is able to **seize** the property? **Or perhaps** the buyers **are able to say to her: Granted, you did not accept upon yourself** a property **guarantee for everyone,** and it is the heirs and not you who have to reimburse us if our property is seized; however, **did you not accept a guarantee** about **your own** actions, that you as the seller will not return and seize the property from us?

The critical distinction here is that while the *ketubah* carries this power of *tereifah* — the ability to seize mortgaged property from a third-party purchaser — *mezonot* does not. A widow can sell property to fund her *mezonot*, but she cannot seize property as a lien for *mezonot*. The question is therefore whether this asymmetry creates a trap: she sells the field herself to obtain *mezonot*, the *yorshim* are bankrupt and cannot pay her *ketubah*, and she now wants to seize that very same field — the one she sold — to satisfy her *ketubah*. Is that permissible?

### Rav Yosef Ruling

The question is framed in terms of a ruling by Rav Yosef: *almanta dezabnah achrayut ayitmei* — when a widow sells land, the *achrayut* for that sale, meaning the obligation to make the buyer whole if the property is subsequently seized by a creditor, falls on the *yatmei*, the heirs of the original estate, not on her. The same applies to a *beit din* that sells property: the heirs bear the *achrayut*. This principle seems to suggest that she stands outside the transaction in a meaningful sense — she acted on their behalf, the responsibility rests with them, and therefore she should be free to step back in as a creditor in her own right and seize the property for her *ketubah*.

### Two Objections

The Gemara presents two possible lines of argument, pulling in opposite directions.

The first argument is that since the *achrayut* falls on the *yatmei* rather than on her, she is essentially acting as their agent in making the sale. Having not accepted responsibility herself, she remains a stranger to the transaction in the sense that matters, and she may therefore turn around and seize the property as a *ketubah* creditor.

The second argument, however, is the one the Gemara articulates with particular sharpness: *mineih de'achrayut de'alma lo kabalt alach, achrayut denafshach mi lo kabalt?* — You have not accepted responsibility for the general debts of the estate; but did you not accept responsibility for your own sales? The force of this objection is that the two transactions — selling the field for *mezonot* and then seeking to seize it for the *ketubah* — are both her doing. If she intended to preserve that field as collateral for her *ketubah*, she should not have sold it. Having sold it herself, she cannot now present herself as an innocent creditor exercising a lien. That is her business on both ends, and she should bear the consequences.

There is something intuitively compelling about the widow's counter-position. One might argue that from the beginning it was the heirs' obligation to provide her with *mezonot* and to honor her *ketubah*. If they failed in both, she was forced to act as their agent to feed herself, and she should not be penalized for having discharged their obligation on their behalf. The buyer, moreover, is not uninvolved in this calculus: anyone purchasing property that is known to be heavily mortgaged and sold under these circumstances is, in a sense, a venture capitalist — buying at a discount precisely because the title is uncertain, counting on the property eventually becoming fully his, and knowing that if it is seized he has a legal avenue to recover his funds from the party bearing *achrayut*.

### Sheshet's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: מוֹכֶרֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת עַד כְּדֵי כְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְסֶמֶךְ לָהּ שֶׁתִּגְבֶּה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מִן הַשְּׁאָר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to** the one who raised the dilemma: **You learned** in a *baraita*: A widow **sells** the deceased's property for her sustenance, and **she continues** to do so **until** there is nothing left except **the value of her marriage contract, and she relies upon** the fact **that she will collect** payment of **her marriage contract from the remainder** of the property.

Rav Sheshet resolves the question by citing a *tanna* who taught: *mocheret veholechet ad kedei ketuvatah* — she may continue selling fields for *mezonot* until only enough property remains to cover her *ketubah*. *Vesamach lah sheteigbeh ketuvatah min hashe'ar* — and she relies on collecting her *ketubah* from what remains. The *tanna* thus envisions a self-limiting principle: she has the ongoing authority to sell, but she must preserve a residuum of property sufficient to satisfy her *ketubah*. The grammatical subject of *samach* — who exactly "relies" — is uncertain in the text; the masculine singular does not obviously refer to her, nor does it clearly refer to *beit din*, which would usually take a plural form.

### Eitza Tova or Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא עֵצָה טוֹבָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּלָא לִיקְרוֹ לַהּ הַדְרָנִיתָא. אִם כֵּן, לִיתְנֵי: "גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מִן הַשְּׁאָר", מַאי "סֶמֶךְ לָהּ"? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שַׁיַּירָא — אִין, לָא שַׁיַּירָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **But perhaps** the *baraita* **teaches us good advice, so that they will not call her a retractor** and say that she is an untrustworthy individual who goes back on agreements into which she entered. However, legally, she is able to seize the property from the buyers. The Gemara answers: **If** that is **so,** and the *baraita* intended only to give advice, **let it** simply **teach: She collects** payment of **her marriage contract from the remainder. What** is the purpose of the added emphasis of: **She relies? Learn from** this that the *baraita* is worded in a precise manner and teaches that if **she left** property, **yes,** she can collect payment of her marriage contract. If **she did not leave over, no,** she cannot collect payment of her marriage contract.

The Gemara immediately raises the question of whether this *baraita* constitutes binding *din* or merely *eitza tova* — good practical advice. Perhaps the *tanna* is not establishing a legal rule but simply counseling wisdom: she ought to leave over enough property to cover her *ketubah* so that people will not call her unreliable, so that she will not acquire a reputation for doubling back on buyers. This echoes a theme from the prior day's learning, where *eitza tova derabnutah* was invoked in a similar context.

The Gemara resists the *eitza tova* reading. If the *baraita* were merely advisory, it would have been sufficient to say "and she collects her *ketubah* from the remainder." The specific language — *vesamach lah sheteigbeh ketuvatah min hashe'ar*, with its implication of reliance rather than mere permission — suggests a binding legal rule. The inference drawn is: *shayra in, la shayra la* — if she leaves over sufficient property, she may collect her *ketubah*; if she does not, she may not.

## Rescission of Sale

### Rescission Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: זַבֵּין וְלָא אִיצְטְרִיכוּ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי, הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי, אוֹ לָא הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars: If someone **sold** properties because he needed money for a certain purpose **and** in the end **he did not need the money** for that purpose, is this considered a sale conducted in error, so that the seller can renege on the deal and **the sale is reversed? Or, is the sale not reversed** and what is done is done?

The Gemara now broadens its inquiry: *zevnah delo itzterichah lei — zuzei hadrei zevini o lo hadrei zevini?* — If someone sells property and it turns out that they no longer need the money for which they sold it, can the sale be rescinded? This is framed as a general question arising in connection with the widow's situation, though it quickly takes on a life of its own as the Gemara canvasses concrete cases.

### Oxen Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּזַבֵּין אַרְעָא לְרַב פָּפָּא, דְּאִצְטְרִיכוּ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְמִיזְבַּן תּוֹרֵי. לְסוֹף לָא אִיצְטְרִיכוּ לֵיהּ, וְאַהְדְּרַיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof: There was **a certain man who sold land to Rav Pappa because he needed money to buy oxen. In the end, he did not need** the money and regretted having sold the land, **and Rav Pappa returned his land to him.**

The first example: a man needed oxen to work his land. Without livestock — or, in modern terms, without machinery — land in the ancient world was of limited productive value. He therefore sold a portion of his property to Rav Pappa in order to raise funds to purchase oxen. *Lesof lo itzterichah lei* — ultimately, it turned out he did not need the money; the oxen were cheaper than he had anticipated, or he found another source of funds.

### Rav Pappa Piety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** This is not a proof, as **Rav Pappa acted** in a manner that was **beyond the letter of the law.**

*Ahadrei nihalei Rav Pappa lara'ei* — Rav Pappa returned the land. The natural assumption might be that this is what the law requires, but the Gemara corrects that: *Rav Pappa lifnim mishurat hadin hu de'avad* — Rav Pappa acted beyond the letter of the law, out of personal piety. He recognized that the man had sold land he did not ultimately need to sell, and out of generosity returned it. This is therefore not a proof for or against the legal rule; it establishes only that Rav Pappa was a *tzaddik*, not that the law compels rescission.

### Famine Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא בִּצּוּרְתָּא דַּהֲוָת בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא, זַבְּנִינְהוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְאַפַּדְנַיְיהוּ, לְסוֹף אֲתוֹ חִיטֵּי. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב נַחְמָן: דִּינָא הוּא דְּהָדְרִי אַפַּדְנֵי לְמָרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another proof: There **was a certain drought in Neharde'a** during which **everyone sold his mansion [*appadna*]** in order to buy wheat. **In the end, wheat arrived,** driving down the price, rendering their sale unnecessary. **Rav Naḥman said to them: The *halakha* is that the mansions are returned to their** previous **owners.** It is evident that he holds that a sale that was prompted by the need for money is voided if it becomes clear that the seller no longer needs the money.

The second case is more instructive. There was a local famine in Nehardea. Rashi explains that the river rose, making it difficult for boats carrying grain to navigate, and the resulting shortage drove the price of wheat dramatically upward. In response, *zvinu kulei alma laafdanaihu* — everyone began selling off their *apadna*, their substantial homes, in order to purchase food. *Lesof atu chiti* — eventually, the grain boats did arrive, the artificial shortage collapsed, and wheat prices fell immediately.

### Nachman Ruling

Rav Nachman ruled: *dina hu dehadrei apadnei lemarihu* — it is the law that those mansions should be returned to their original owners.

### Mekach Taaut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי: זְבִינֵי בְּטָעוּת הֲווֹ, דְּאִיגַּלַּאי מִילְּתָא דְּאַרְבָּא בְּעִקּוּלֵי הֲוָה קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There too, the sale was** conducted **in error, as it became known that the ship** with the wheat **was** already **in the bays of the river** at the time when the mansions were sold. Had they known that the ship was so close, they would not have sold their property. This is a case of an error at the time of the sale, which is different from a case where the circumstances changed after the sale.

The Gemara explains the basis for this ruling: *hatam nami zvinei beta'ut havu de'iglai milta de'arba bekilei havah kayma* — this too was a sale made in error, because it subsequently became clear that the boats were already stationed just around the bend, waiting for the river to subside. Had the sellers known the boats were nearby, they would not have sold their homes. The transaction was therefore predicated on a false factual premise, making it a *mekach ta'ut*.

### Rashi Clarification

Rashi draws a careful distinction here. The ruling applies specifically because the boats were nearby and discoverable — their presence was knowable, even if the sellers did not know it. This is a different situation from one in which grain arrived from a genuinely unexpected source: for example, if a merchant from a distant region with a wheat surplus happened to arrive in Nehardea, flooding the market in a way no one could have anticipated. In that case, Rashi indicates, there would be no *mekach ta'ut* and the sales would stand, because the change in circumstances was truly unforeseeable. The rule turns on the distinction between what was knowable and what was genuinely unknowable.

### Buyer Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב נַחְמָן: אִם כֵּן נִמְצֵאתָ מַכְשִׁילָן לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ כׇּל יוֹמָא בִּצּוּרְתָּא שְׁכִיחָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers proof that the error was already present at the time of the transaction: **If so, this is what Rami bar Shmuel said to Rav Naḥman** when he questioned his ruling: **If** this is **so,** and the mansions need to be restored to their previous owners, **you find yourself obstructing them for the future.** As a result of this ruling, people will not want to buy land because they will worry that the seller will change his mind. Rav Naḥman **said to him: Is that to say** that it is so **common,** that there is **a drought every day?**

Rami bar Shmuel raises a concern before Rav Nachman: *im ken nimtzeit machshilan le'atid lavo* — if you rule this way, you will end up creating obstacles for future transactions. Buyers, fearing that their purchases will be reversed whenever an artificial shortage dissipates, will decline to participate in such markets at all. In a time of famine, when people most need to convert assets into food, the very mechanism by which that conversion occurs will be paralyzed.

### Nachman Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ כׇּל יוֹמָא בִּצּוּרְתָּא שְׁכִיחָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, בִּצּוּרְתָּא בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא מִשְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Is that to say** that it is so **common,** that there is **a drought every day?** I said that the properties are returned only in these specific circumstances. **He said to him: Yes, in Neharde'a drought is a frequent** occurrence.

Rav Nachman responds: *atu kol yoma betzorta shchicha?* — does such an artificial shortage occur every day? Do we really encounter these conditions so frequently that buyers will be deterred from ordinary commerce by the possibility of rescission? The other party in the conversation replies, yes, in fact, in Nehardea it does occur with some regularity — something about Nehardea's geography makes it susceptible to these supply disruptions. But Rav Nachman's point stands: the ruling addresses a specific and unusual situation, and the concern about chilling future sales does not override the basic claim of justice on behalf of people who sold their homes under conditions of artificial and transient crisis.

### Final Hilcheta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: זַבֵּין וְלָא אִיצְטְרִיכוּ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי — הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is** that if one **sold** properties for a certain purpose **and** in the end **did not need the money** for that reason, **the sale is reversed.**

The *hilcheta* is then stated: *zevin velo itzterichah lei zuzei hadar zevina* — if one sells property and it turns out that he did not need the money, the sale is reversed. This represents the standing law. The *hilcheta* formulas are, as is well known, the contributions of the *Rabanan Savorai*, the post-*Amoraic* scholars who edited the Talmud and appended definitive rulings to conclude *sugyot*; they carry full legal weight even if they arrive after the Amoraic discussion itself is concluded.

The underlying principle is one of legislated *rachmanus*: land was not mere commodity in the ancient world. To sell land was, in a sense, to give up a part of oneself, and the pain of that transaction did not disappear simply because the seller failed to announce his reasons at the time of sale. The fact that no explicit stipulation was made — that the sale appeared *stam*, without stated conditions — does not eliminate the equitable claim that a man who sold his land unnecessarily should be able to recover it upon returning the money.

## Widow Authority

### Tanna Kama Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַלְמָנָה, בֵּין מִן הָאֵירוּסִין בֵּין מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין — מוֹכֶרֶת שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A widow, whether** widowed **from betrothal or from marriage, sells** her husband's property **when not in court.**

The next *mishna* addresses the procedural requirements for a widow's sale of estate property. The *tanna kama* rules: *almana bein min ha'erusin bein min hanisu'in mocheret shelo b'veit din* — a widow, whether widowed from actual marriage or from *erusin* (engagement), may sell the heirs' property without the formal sanction of a *beit din*.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין מוֹכֶרֶת שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין, מִן הָאֵירוּסִין לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** A widow **from marriage sells when not in court,** but a widow **from betrothal may sell only in court, because she does not receive sustenance** from her husband's property. She receives only her marriage contract.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees and introduces a distinction: *min hanisu'in mocheret shelo b'veit din, min ha'erusin lo timkor ella b'veit din, mipnei she'ein la mezonot*. A widow from actual marriage may sell without *beit din*, but a widow from engagement may not — she must go through *beit din* — because she has no right to *mezonot*. The widow from marriage sells regularly because she has an ongoing need for sustenance, assuming the *yorshim* are failing to provide it. A woman widowed from engagement, however, has only a *ketubah* claim, not a *mezonot* claim; Rabbi Shimon therefore requires her to proceed through official channels.

### Question Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין — מִשּׁוּם מְזוֹנֵי, אֶלָּא מִן הָאֵירוּסִין מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara elaborates: **Granted,** a widow **from marriage** may sell when not in court **due to** the fact that **her sustenance** is a pressing concern, so one does not make her wait until she finds a court that will oversee her sale. **However, what is the reason** that a widow **from betrothal** may sell property when not in court?

The Gemara asks: the case of the widow from marriage — we understand why the *tanna kama* permits her to sell without *beit din*, since she has an ongoing need for sustenance. But the widow from engagement — what is the reason that the *tanna kama* permits her to sell without *beit din* as well?

### Ulla/Chen Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מִשּׁוּם חִינָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: Due to desirability.** The Sages enacted several ordinances on behalf of women, so that men will want to marry them.

Ulla answers: *mishum china* — because of *chen*. There is a *machloket* between Rashi and Tosafot as to exactly what *chen* means in this context, and the two interpretations yield meaningfully different analyses throughout the discussion.

### Rashi on Chen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁתִּתְבַּזֶּה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Because a man does not want his wife to be disgraced** by being involved **in court** proceedings.

Rashi explains *china* as follows: *sheyiheyu ba'aleihen lechen be'eineihen* — so that their husbands will be pleasant in their eyes, meaning so that the woman will retain a fond memory of the marriage. *Velo yimne'u nashim milinsa la'anashim* — and women will not be deterred from remarrying by the prospect of a cumbersome collection process. The idea is that if we make it burdensome for a widow to collect what she is owed, women may become averse to marriage altogether, knowing what awaits them if they are widowed. By streamlining the process, we make marriage a more attractive proposition and thereby serve a social interest. Rashi's reading has daughters in mind, one might say — a man ensuring that the women in his community do not face bureaucratic difficulty in their hour of need.

This interpretation becomes difficult in the continuation of the *sugya*, as will become clear.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different rationale: *lefi she'ein adam rotze shetitbaze ishto b'veit din* — because no man wants his wife to be embarrassed by having to appear in *beit din*. The leniency is not for her future benefit but reflects the *kavod* a husband feels toward his wife even after his death: the social norms of the community are such that a woman appearing in *beit din* to wrangle over money is regarded as undignified, and the institution is designed to spare her that indignity. This connects to a broader theme running through *Masechet Ketubot*, where there is a recurring concern that daughters not be reduced to going *bichozrot al hapetachim* — going door to door begging — and that the honor of families not be compromised.

### Grushah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ גְּרוּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the two opinions? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is in the case of **a divorcée.**

The Gemara identifies the practical difference between Ulla and Rabbi Yochanan: *ika beinaihu grushah* — the case of the *grushah*, the divorced woman. She too has a *ketubah* to collect, but she is neither a widow who needs to be encouraged toward remarriage nor a woman whose husband cares about her dignity in court.

### Chen Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם חִינָּא — גְּרוּשָׁה נָמֵי בָּעֲיָא חֵן, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁתִּתְבַּזֶּה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין — גְּרוּשָׁה לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to the one who says** that it is **due to desirability, a divorcée also requires desirability.** But **according to the one who says** that it is **because a man does not want his wife to be disgraced in court,** a man **does not care** if his **ex-wife** is disgraced.

According to the view that the leniency exists *mishum china* — because of *chen* in Rashi's sense, to encourage remarriage — a *grushah* should also benefit, since she too may want to remarry and we similarly wish to make the process attractive for her.

According to the view that the leniency exists because a man does not want his wife embarrassed in *beit din*, the *grushah* should not benefit: *grushah mai ikpat leh?* — what does he care about her? Once she is divorced, his concern for her dignity dissolves. There is no longer a husband who would be pained by her public appearance in court.

### Grushah Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וּגְרוּשָׁה לֹא תִּמְכּוֹר אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna (97b): **And a divorcée may sell only in court.**

The very next *mishna* in the tractate states explicitly: *vegrushah lo timkor ella b'veit din* — and a divorced woman may not sell except through *beit din*.

### Grushah Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: מָכְרָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, מִשְׁכְּנָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, עָשְׂתָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי לְאַחֵר — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the **mishna?** The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **she sold** all of **her marriage contract,** or **mortgaged her marriage contract,** or if **she made her marriage contract designated repayment to another, she does not** receive **sustenance** any longer; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *mishna*'s ruling that a *grushah* requires *beit din* seems to confirm Rabbi Yochanan's position and to align with Rabbi Shimon's approach. However, the Gemara works through the implications carefully. If the *mishna* reflects the *chen* rationale, one would need to explain why a *grushah* does not need *chen*. The Gemara suggests: *almana min ha'erusin hu dela nafshuh lechen didah* — a widow from engagement has less *chen*, and it is for that reason that Rabbi Shimon requires her to go to *beit din*, while a *grushah* has more *chen* because she was involved in a real relationship — *aval grushah de'nafshuh chen didah*.

Here Rashi's interpretation begins to strain. Why would a widow from engagement have less *chen* in the sense of needing encouragement toward remarriage? Rashi suggests she was not as emotionally attached to her husband in the first place. This is acknowledged to be somewhat forced. Rabbeinu Chananel, cited in Tosafot, offers two alternative readings. First, that *nafish chen didah* in the case of the *grushah* means she has a larger *ketubah* because she typically receives the *tosefet* in addition to the *ikar ketubah*, and therefore has more at stake in the collection process — giving her more need for the facilitation that *chen* provides. Second, and more persuasively, that a *grushah* is in a more socially vulnerable and stigmatized position than a widow — *hi bzuya yoter min ha'almana* — and therefore she has a greater need for *chen* in the sense of assistance and social support, not that she has more of it inherently. The phrase *nafish chen didah* then means she needs more *chen* rather than that she possesses more of it.

## Heirs Selling

### Heirs Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמּוֹכֶרֶת שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין, כָּךְ יוֹרְשֶׁיהָ יוֹרְשֵׁי כְתוּבָּתָהּ מוֹכְרִים שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof: **Just as** the widow **sells when not in court, so too, her heirs,** those who **inherit her marriage contract, sell when not in court.**

A *baraita* is cited: *keshem shemocheret shelo b'veit din, kach yorsheha yorshei ketubatah mocherin shelo b'veit din* — just as the widow herself may sell without *beit din*, so too those who inherit her *ketubah* may sell without *beit din*. This rule extends the widow's procedural leniency to whoever steps into her position as a creditor with a *ketubah* claim.

### Male Heirs Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁתִּתְבַּזֶּה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּתִתְבַּזֵּי — יוֹרְשֶׁיהָ נָמֵי לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלִיבַּזּוּ, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם חִינָּא, יוֹרְשֶׁיהָ מַאי חֵן אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** that the reason why she may sell out of court is **because a man does not want his wife to be disgraced** by having to appear **in court,** it is possible to say that **just as he is not amenable** to the idea **that she will be disgraced, he also is not amenable** to the idea **that her heirs will be disgraced. However, according to the one who says** that she sells out of court **due to desirability, what desirability do her heirs** need to **have?**

This creates an immediate problem for the embarrassment rationale. If the reason a widow sells without *beit din* is that no man wants his wife to be embarrassed in court, why would the same exemption apply to her *yorshim*? One could argue that the concern extends to the *yorshim* as well — *ki heichi de'i'lei de'nicha lei de'titbazye yorshei nami de'nicha lei de'libazu* — just as he did not want his wife to appear in *beit din*, so too he did not want his *yorshim* subjected to the indignity of public litigation over money. On this reading, *bizayon* attaches to anyone — male or female — who has to go and haggle over an inheritance in court. It is simply beneath the dignity of the family.

### Grace Problem

The *chen* rationale, however, faces its own difficulty here. If the leniency exists to ease the widow's path toward remarriage or to maintain her favorable memory of the deceased, what does that have to do with male heirs? Sons who inherit their mother's *ketubah* claim have no need of the kind of *chen* being invoked on her behalf.

### Female Heirs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ עוּלָּא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁיְּרָשַׁתָּה בִּתָּהּ אוֹ אֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla interpreted it:** This could take place, **for example,** when **her daughter or her sister inherited** from **her,** and they too need desirability.

Ulla resolves the problem with a *targum* — a reinterpretation of the case: *tirgmah Ulla kegon sheyirshtah bitah o achotah* — the *baraita* is referring to a case where the widow's heirs are her daughter or her sister. These female heirs do have a need for *chen* in exactly the way the widow herself did. It is not *banin dichrin* — the usual case of sons inheriting through the *ketubot* provision — but rather female relatives who have stepped into the widow's position as creditors, and who similarly benefit from having the collection process made accessible and dignified. The daf concludes here.
