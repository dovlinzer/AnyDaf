# Pesachim 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechita Shelo Lishmo

### Pesach Scheduling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפָּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, אוֹ לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וְלִשְׁמוֹ — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The Paschal lamb that** the priest **slaughtered not for its** own **purpose,** i.e., at the time of slaughter he said that his intent was to slaughter it as a different offering, e.g., a peace-offering or burnt-offering, rather than as a Paschal lamb; or if the priest **received** the blood, or **carried** the blood to the altar, or **sprinkled** the blood on the altar while saying that it was **not for the purpose** of the Paschal lamb; **or** if the priest performed the rites both **for its** own **purpose and not for its** own **purpose; or not for its** own **purpose and for its** own **purpose;** in all these cases, the Paschal lamb is **disqualified.**

The daf begins at the very bottom of 59b, with the *mishnah* that addressed the schedule of *Erev Pesach*: when the afternoon *tamid* is normally brought, and how it is adjusted on *Erev Pesach* to allow time for the *korban Pesach*. Although the general rule prohibits bringing any *korbanot* after the afternoon *tamid*, the *Pesach* was the exception, brought later in the afternoon — though care was still taken to leave sufficient time for everyone to bring their *korban Pesach*.

The *mishnah* now turns away from scheduling and toward the question of how the *korban Pesach* was slaughtered — specifically, the effect of improper intention during the slaughter.

### Zevachim Analogy

This discussion is closely parallel to the very opening *mishnah* of *Zevachim*, which is fitting, given that the next several *prakim* of *Pesachim* could easily belong to *Seder Kodashim*. *Zevachim* opens with the question of what happens when one slaughters a *korban shelo lishmo* — not for its intended purpose — whether because one thought it belonged to a different owner, or, more commonly, because one thought it was a different type of *korban* than it actually was.

### Shelo Lishmo Rule

The general rule stated in that *mishnah* is: *kol hazvachim sheshchatan shelo lishman ksheirin ela shelo alu labaalim lesheim chovah* — any *korban* slaughtered for the wrong purpose is still valid as an object, but does not count toward the owner's obligation. If the *kohen* had in mind the wrong type of *korban*, the sacrifice itself may proceed — the blood may be sprinkled, the meat eaten, the fats burned — but the owner has not discharged his obligation. The *korban* as an object is legitimate; the owner's personal fulfillment is not.

### Chatat Exception

The *chatat* presents a more severe case. The entire purpose of the sin offering is to atone for a specific sin of a specific owner; the *chatat* is inseparable from its owner's relationship to the transgression. Once *shelo lishmo* severs that relationship, there is no longer any valid *chatat* at all. A *chatat shelo lishmo* is therefore completely invalid, not merely insufficient for the owner's obligation.

### Pesach Strictness

Our *mishnah* will show that the same stringent rule applies to the *korban Pesach*. Although the *Pesach* is in many ways a lighter *korban* — akin to a *shelamim*, eaten by the owners — it shares with the *chatat* the principle that *shelo lishmo* renders it completely invalid, not merely unhelpful for the obligation. The reason for this will become clearer as the discussion unfolds, but it is connected to the unique centrality of eating in the *korban Pesach*.

### Pigul vs Shelo Lishmo

One might ask whether this is the same as *pigul*. It is not. *Pigul* arises when one intends to eat the *korban* at the wrong time — or in a variation, in the wrong place — and that too invalidates. But *shelo lishmo* is distinct: it refers to thinking in one's mind that the *korban* is a different type altogether — a *chatat* instead of a *shelamim*, or an *olah* instead of a *chatat*. Both *pigul* and *shelo lishmo* depend on the *kohen*'s intentions, but they are conceptually separate categories.

### Articulated Thought

It is worth noting at this stage — as it arises at the very beginning of *Zevachim* as well — that when the *halachah* speaks of intention (*machshavah*) invalidating a *korban*, a significant view among the *Rishonim* holds that this means articulated thought, that is, thought which was verbalized. Whether silent, purely internal thought has any legal effect is itself a subject of debate. The discussion below proceeds in terms of stated intentions, which makes the analysis more tractable, though the underlying question of silent thought carries its own implications.

### Four Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ? לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח וּלְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים. שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וְלִשְׁמוֹ — לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים וּלְשֵׁם פֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does one perform the rites **for its** own **purpose and not for its** own **purpose?** It is in a case where the priest said that his intent is **for the purpose of the Paschal lamb and for the purpose of a peace-offering.** And how does one perform rites **not for its** own **purpose and for its** own **purpose?** It is in a case where the priest says that the offering is **for the purpose of a peace-offering and for the purpose of a Paschal lamb.**

The *mishnah* reads: *hapesach sheshchatan shelo lishmo* — if one slaughtered the *korban Pesach shelo lishmo*, for a different purpose. The next *mishnah* will address the case of wrong owners; here the concern is a different type of *korban*. *Vekibel vehilach vezarak shelo lishmo* — or if one caught the blood, walked it to the altar, or threw it *shelo lishmo*. The *vav* here clearly functions as "or," not "and."

There are four primary *avodot* involved from *shechitah* through the application of the blood: (1) *shechitah*, the slaughter; (2) *kabbalah*, catching the blood in a vessel; (3) *holachah*, carrying the blood to the altar; and (4) *zrika*, throwing the blood on the altar. An improper intention at any one of these four acts can invalidate the *korban*.

### Mishnah Structure

The *mishnah* goes on: *o lishmo veshelo lishmo* — or if one had a mixed intention, partly for the correct purpose and partly for the wrong one. This is the formulation the *Gemara* will focus on closely. The rule stated is: at any of the four *avodot*, whether the intention was entirely *shelo lishmo* or was a hybrid of the two, if any part of the intention was for the wrong *korban*, the *Pesach* is invalid — just as with the *chatat*.

### Mixed Intentions

The *mishnah* then asks: what is the case of *lishmah u'shelo lishmah*? The example given: *leshem Pesach u'leshem shelamim* — one slaughtered it thinking it was a *Pesach* and then thinking it was a *shelamim*. And the reverse: *shelo lishmah u'lishmah* — thinking it was a *shelamim* and then a *Pesach*. The *mishnah* clearly states both hybrid orderings as invalidating. How and when these two intentions occurred — simultaneously, sequentially during one *avodah*, or across two separate *avodot* — is precisely the question the *Gemara* now takes up.

## Rav Pappa's Query

### One Act or Two?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת תְּנַן, אוֹ בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת תְּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma:** The mishna says: For its own purpose and not for its own purpose. **Did we learn** in the mishna concerning a case where the priest had both intentions while performing **one rite,** i.e., he stated both intentions while slaughtering? **Or, did we learn** in the mishna concerning a case where the priest had the two intentions while performing **two rites,** i.e., during one rite he said that it was for its own purpose and in another of the four rites he said that it was not for its own purpose?

Before entering the *Gemara*'s formal question, it is worth pausing on why *shelo lishmo* is particularly strict by the *korban Pesach*. The *korban Pesach* is unique among all *korbanot* in that its primary purpose is eating. In most *korbanot*, the focal moment is the *zrikat hadam* — the throwing of the blood. The eating by the *kohanim* or by the owners is a secondary dimension. By the *korban Pesach*, however, everything is *leshem achilah* — oriented toward the eating. This will emerge in several important *halachot* as the *masechta* continues.

Furthermore, the *pasuk* states *ish lefi ochlo tachosu al haseh* — one counts the group that will eat from it and slaughters accordingly. The designated eaters are bound up with the act of *shechitah* itself. Just as the *chatat* cannot exist without an owner — because its entire purpose is the owner's atonement — so too the *korban Pesach* cannot exist without designated eaters, because its entire purpose is the eating by the designated group. *Shelo lishmo* severs the relationship between the *korban* and its owners, and without that relationship the *Pesach* has no purpose. Hence it is invalid entirely, not merely insufficient for the obligation.

With this context established, the *Gemara* presents the question of *Rav Pappa*: *ba'ei Rav Pappa* — does our *mishnah* speak of a case where the two thoughts occurred *b'avodah achat*, during one *avodah*, or *bishtei avodot*, during two separate *avodot*?

The four *avodot* — *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, *holachah*, and *zrikat hadam* — each constitute a distinct act. The question is whether the hybrid statement of "for a *Pesach* and for a *shelamim*" was made as a single composite utterance during one act, or whether it represents two separate statements at two different acts.

### Rabbi Meir vs Yose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת תְּנַן וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אַף בִּגְמַר דְּבָרָיו אָדָם נִתְפָּס. דְּאִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר — הָא אָמַר: תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן,
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa elaborates on the difference between these two different explanations. Shall we say that **we learn** in the mishna **with regard to one rite,** and therefore our mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said that a person is held** accountable **even for the conclusion of his statement?** Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Meir disagree in the case of one who says: This animal is a burnt-offering and a peace-offering. Rabbi Yosei says that not only the beginning but also the conclusion of his statement is significant, and the animal has been consecrated as both a burnt-offering and a peace-offering. It is therefore unfit to be offered on the altar; rather, it must be sold when it develops a defect and the money used to purchase animals for both a burnt-offering and a peace-offering. And similarly in the case of our mishna, where the priest says that his intent is for the purpose of a Paschal lamb and for the purpose of a peace-offering, Rabbi Yosei would say that the animal has been slaughtered for both a Paschal lamb and a peace-offering, and is therefore disqualified. **For if** our mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** surely **he said: Hold** one accountable for **the first expression,** and what he says after that is of no consequence.

To understand the stakes of Rav Pappa's question, the *Gemara* invokes a *machlokes* between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yose that was already discussed earlier in the tractate. If a person says *harei ze tmuras olah tmuras shelamim* — this is a substitute for an *olah* and a substitute for a *shelamim* — the statement is self-contradictory. Something cannot simultaneously be both. How does one interpret such a hybrid utterance?

Rabbi Meir holds *tfos lashon rishon* — one grabs hold of the first part of the statement, reads the sentence as ending after the first clause, and disregards the remainder as incoherent. Rabbi Yose holds *af bigmar devarav adam nitpas* — a person is held accountable even for the conclusion of his words. Even if the end of the sentence creates a contradiction with the beginning, one must grapple with the entirety of what was said, not truncate it.

### Shechita Debate

If our *mishnah*'s hybrid case — where one said both *leshem Pesach* and *leshem shelamim* — refers to a single act of *shechitah*, then we are dealing with a single contradictory statement during one *avodah*. In that case, Rabbi Meir would say: simply read only the first part. If the first part was *lishmo* — for the sake of a *Pesach* — then the *korban* stands, and the trailing *shelamim* is disregarded. If the first part was *shelo lishmo*, it invalidates immediately. Rabbi Yose, by contrast, would hold the person accountable to the entirety of the hybrid statement, and since part of it was *shelo lishmo*, the *korban* is invalid.

### Two Acts Agreement

If, however, the two thoughts were expressed at two separate *avodot* — say, *lishmo* at *shechitah* and *shelo lishmo* at *zrika* — then there is no single contradictory sentence to parse. Each act stands alone. The *shechitah* was valid, and the *zrika* was *shelo lishmo*, which invalidates the *korban*. In this scenario, even Rabbi Meir would agree that the *korban* is invalid, because the rule of *tfos lashon rishon* applies to a single contradictory utterance; it does not rescue an entirely separate act performed with a wrong intention.

### Mishnah Interpretation

The *Gemara*'s interest, then, is not in what the *halachah* is — that is clear. If two separate *avodot* are involved with separate improper intentions, everyone agrees the *korban* is invalid. If a hybrid thought occurs during one *avodah*, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yose disagree. The question is simply: which scenario does our *mishnah* actually describe? The *Gemara* will now attempt to resolve this through careful reading of the *mishnah* itself.

### Shelo Lishmo First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אַהֵיָיא? אִי נֵימָא אַשֶּׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וְלִשְׁמוֹ, בֵּין בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת בֵּין בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, בֵּין לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — אִיפְּסִיל לֵיהּ מִקַּמָּיְיתָא, דְּהָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נָמֵי, אַף בִּגְמַר דְּבָרָיו אָדָם נִתְפָּס אִית לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא: אַלִּשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **They said: To which** part of the mishna **does this** discussion refer? **If we say** that the discussion pertains to the case in the mishna of slaughtering an offering first **for a different purpose and** then **for its own purpose,** then **whether** these intentions are verbalized **during one rite or during two rites, whether according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir or according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** the offering **is disqualified from the first** statement, **as also Rabbi Yosei holds that a person is held accountable even for the conclusion of his statement.** That does not mean that the final statement is primary but that it too has value and complements the previous one. Therefore, since one initially intended to offer the Paschal lamb for a different purpose, it was immediately disqualified. **Rather,** the question is concerning the case of slaughtering an animal first **for its own purpose and** then **for a different purpose; what** exactly are the parameters of that case?

Before analyzing the hybrid case, the *Gemara* first clarifies a simpler matter. If the *mishnah*'s hybrid refers to *shelo lishmo u'lishmo* — where the wrong intention came *first* — then there is no need for any debate at all. Everyone agrees, regardless of whether it is one *avodah* or two, and regardless of whether one follows Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Yose, that the *korban* is invalid from the moment the first word — *shelamim* — is out of one's mouth. After all, both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yose agree that the beginning of a statement binds; Rabbi Yose merely adds that even the end of the statement binds. So *shelo lishmo* first invalidates immediately and comprehensively. The interesting case — and the one Rav Pappa's question targets — is the hybrid where the *kosher* intention comes first: *lishmo u'shelo lishmo*.

## First vs Hybrid Case

### Mishnah First Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַפֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא כִּדְקָתָנֵי, לְמָה לִי עַד דִּמְחַשֵּׁב לְכוּלְּהוּ? מִקַּמָּיְיתָא אִיפְּסִיל לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a series of sources in order to resolve this question. **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the question from what is taught in the mishna: **A Paschal lamb** that one **sacrificed for a different purpose, and he received** the blood, **and carried** it to the altar, **and sprinkled** it **for a different purpose,** is disqualified. This needs clarification: **What** exactly **are the circumstances? If we say** that the case is exactly **as it was taught,** that these four rites are all performed for a different purpose, **why do I need** to say that **he intended** to perform **all of them,** i.e., the slaughter, receiving of the blood, carrying the blood to the altar, and sprinkling it upon the altar, for a different purpose? The offering has been **disqualified from the first** improper intention during slaughter.

The *Gemara* now reads the *mishnah* carefully to determine which scenario it describes. The *mishnah* opens: *hapesach sheshachto shelo lishmo, vekibel vehilach vezarak shelo lishmo* — the *Pesach* that one slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, or caught the blood, or walked it, or threw it *shelo lishmo*.

### Vav Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַפֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, אִי נָמֵי שְׁחָטוֹ לִשְׁמוֹ וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ. אִי נָמֵי שְׁחָטוֹ וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ לִשְׁמוֹ, וְזָרַק שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ. דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ שְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is this not what** the mishna **is teaching: A Paschal lamb** that one **slaughtered for a different purpose, or alternatively,** he **slaughtered it for its own purpose, but received** the blood, **and carried** the blood, **and sprinkled** the blood **for a different purpose, or alternatively, he slaughtered it, and received** the blood, **and carried** the blood **for its own purpose, but sprinkled** the blood **for a different purpose,** in all these situations the offering is disqualified? If so, **it is** a case of different intentions in **two rites,** and the mishna can also be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

The *vav* connecting these acts cannot mean "and" — as if one had to commit all four *avodot shelo lishmo* to invalidate the *korban*. That reading is untenable: if the first *avodah* was already *shelo lishmo*, the *korban* was invalidated at that point. Why would the *mishnah* require all four? Rather, the *vav* here means "or." The *mishnah* is saying: if any one of these acts was performed *shelo lishmo*, the *korban* is invalid.

### Different Avodot Proof

Reading it this way, the *mishnah*'s first clause actually covers the case of different intentions at different *avodot*. The *mishnah* is saying: whether you did the *shechitah shelo lishmo* (with the rest performed correctly), or the *kabbalah shelo lishmo*, or the *holachah*, or the *zrika* — any single act *shelo lishmo* invalidates. This includes the case where, say, the *shechitah* was *lishmo* but the *zrika* was *shelo lishmo*: a case of one correct *avodah* followed by one incorrect *avodah*.

### One Avodah Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת — הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא! אֶלָּא לָאו, בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אַף בִּגְמַר דְּבָרָיו אָדָם נִתְפָּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: **For its own purpose and for a different purpose.** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that one had these two intentions **during two** different **rites, that is** precisely the meaning of **the first clause** and there is no need to repeat it. **Rather, must we not** say that in this part of the mishna, one had both intentions **during one rite,** and the mishna is **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said that a person is held accountable even for the conclusion of his statement?**

If that is so, then when the *mishnah* subsequently discusses the hybrid of *lishmo u'shelo lishmo* and *shelo lishmo u'lishmo*, it cannot again be referring to different thoughts at different *avodot* — that was already covered. The hybrid case at the end of the *mishnah* must therefore describe two thoughts occurring during a *single avodah*: a composite intention at the moment of, say, the *shechitah*. And that case reflects the position of Rabbi Yose — *af bigmar devarav adam nitpas* — that even when both thoughts are expressed in one utterance, the *shelo lishmo* component invalidates.

### Alternative Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, וְרֵישָׁא — דְּקָאֵי בִּשְׁחִיטָה וְקָא מְחַשֵּׁב בִּשְׁחִיטָה, אִי נָמֵי, קָאֵי בִּזְרִיקָה וְקָא מְחַשֵּׁב בִּזְרִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** This proof is refuted: **No, actually** it is possible to explain that even the latter clause of the mishna is referring to a case in which one has conflicting intentions **during two** different **rites, and** the difference between the first clause and the latter clause of the mishna is as follows: **The first clause** is referring to a case in which **one is standing at** the time of **the slaughter and thinking about slaughtering** the animal as a different offering; **alternatively, he is standing** at the time of the **sprinkling** of the blood **and thinking about sprinkling** for a different purpose.

The *Gemara* considers a rebuttal. Perhaps the *mishnah* in its entirety addresses only cases where the wrong intention and the right intention occur at *separate* acts — and the hybrid at the end is not truly a single compound intention at one act.

### Thought During vs Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא — דְּקָאֵי בִּשְׁחִיטָה וְקָחָשֵׁיב בִּזְרִיקָה, דְּאָמַר: הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַפֶּסַח לִשְׁמוֹ לִזְרוֹק דָּמוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ. וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דִּמְחַשְּׁבִין מֵעֲבוֹדָה לַעֲבוֹדָה, וְהַיְינוּ בַּעְיָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The latter clause** of the mishna, on the other hand, is referring to a case in which **one is standing at** the time of **the slaughter and thinking about sprinkling** for a different purpose. For example, where **he said: I am hereby slaughtering the Paschal lamb for its own purpose,** but my intent is **to sprinkle its blood for a different purpose. And** the mishna **teaches us** a new *halakha*, **that one can intend from one rite to another rite,** meaning that one can disqualify an offering through an improper intention about an upcoming rite while performing an earlier rite. **And this was the question** raised **by Rav Pappa** on a different occasion. He asked if intent during one rite concerning the performance of a different rite can disqualify an offering.

To sustain this reading, the *Gemara* distinguishes between two ways of having different intentions at different *avodot*. In the first case — covered by the *mishnah*'s opening — the *kohen* has a *machshavah* about the act he is currently performing. When he slaughters, he thinks: "I am slaughtering this *shelo lishmo*." Or when he throws the blood, he thinks: "I am throwing this blood as a *shelamim*." Each act is accompanied by a *machshavah* about that act.

### Hybrid Example

The second way — which would correspond to the *mishnah*'s hybrid case — is that the *kohen* during one act harbors an intention about a *different, future* act. In this reading, the hybrid statement is: *hareini shocheit es haPesach lishmo lizrok damo shelo lishmo* — "I am slaughtering this *korban Pesach* for its proper purpose, with the intention that I will throw its blood for the sake of a *shelamim*." Here, the *shechitah* itself is designated correctly, but the *kohen* articulates a *machshavah* that the future *zrika* will be *shelo lishmo*. These are not contradictory statements about a single act; they are two distinct, self-consistent statements. There is no incoherent hybrid sentence that would prompt Rabbi Meir to truncate it. Both statements stand independently, and even Rabbi Meir would hold the *kohen* accountable to both.

### Machshavah Transfer

This reading also teaches an important principle: *machshevina me'avodah le'avodah* — a *machshavah* expressed during one *avodah* regarding a future *avodah* is halachically significant and can invalidate the *korban*. One does not need to entertain the wrong intention precisely at the moment of the disqualifying act; having the wrong intention during the *shechitah* about the *zrika* is sufficient.

### Unresolved Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וְלִשְׁמוֹ פָּסוּל. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, הַשְׁתָּא לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ אָמְרַתְּ פָּסוּל, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וְלִשְׁמוֹ מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Since our question has not been resolved, it is necessary to bring another proof: **Come** and **hear** that which is taught in the last clause of the mishna: **Or for a different purpose and for its own purpose,** the offering **is disqualified.** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **What** exactly **are the circumstances? If we say** that it speaks **about two rites,** there is a difficulty: **Now** that **you** have already **said that** if the priest first performed a rite while intending that the offering be **for its own purpose, and** then performed a rite with the intention that it be **for a different purpose,** the Paschal lamb **is disqualified, is it necessary to say** that it is disqualified when the priest first performed a rite while intending that the offering be **for a different purpose, and** then performed a rite with the intention that it be **for its own purpose?** It is obvious that the offering is disqualified immediately with the first expression of intent.

On this reading, Rav Pappa's question can be rephrased as follows: does the *mishnah*'s hybrid case describe a single contradictory utterance at one *avodah* — in which case the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Yose's position — or does it describe a non-contradictory compound statement at one *avodah* about a future *avodah* — in which case even Rabbi Meir could accept it? The *Gemara* at this stage leaves the question open.

## Owners Case

### Second Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת. וּמִדְּסֵיפָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת — רֵישָׁא נָמֵי בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't** the mishna addressing a case in which the priest has two intentions **during one rite? And since the last clause** of the mishna talks **about one rite, the earlier clause** of the mishna must **also** speak **about one rite,** and the mishna is according to Rabbi Yosei.

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*, which concerns intention regarding the wrong owners rather than the wrong type of *korban*. The basic distinction here is important.

### Owners Rule

When one slaughters the *Pesach* with the sole intention of the wrong group of owners — the wrong *le'ochlav* — the *Pesach* is invalid, much as it would be if the wrong type of *korban* was intended. However, when the intention is a mixture — partly for the right owners and partly for the wrong ones — the *Pesach* is *kasher*. This stands in direct contrast to the rule for wrong-*korban* intention, where even a hybrid thought is sufficient to invalidate. The next *mishnah* thus teaches an asymmetry: hybrid intention regarding the type of *korban* invalidates; hybrid intention regarding the *eaters* does not.

### Wrong Owners Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא?! הָא כִּדְאִיתָא וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא: סֵיפָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת, וּמִדְּסֵיפָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת — רֵישָׁא נָמֵי בָּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof as it refuted the previous one: **Are** the two cases **comparable?** Perhaps **this** case is **as it is and that** case is **as it is.** There is no reason to equate the cases, as it is possible to say that **the latter clause** speaks **about one rite,** whereas **the first clause,** the current mishna, speaks **either about one rite or about two rites.** Therefore, there is no conclusive resolution to Rav Pappa's question.

The *mishnah* states: *shachto shelo le'ochlav ushelo limnuyav* — one slaughtered it for those who cannot eat it, or for people registered to a different group. In both cases the *Pesach* is *pasul*.

### Owners Definitions

*Shelo le'ochlav* may refer to those who, though they are part of the designated group, are physically incapable of eating — they are ill, elderly, or unable to consume the requisite amount. *Shelo limnuyav* refers more straightforwardly to those belonging to an entirely different group — men who are uncircumcised for whatever reason, or those who are *tamei*. In either case, a pure intention directed at the wrong eaters invalidates.

### One Avodah Clear

Crucially, the text frames this case entirely in terms of *shachto* — the *shechitah* alone. This is clearly a case involving one *avodah*, not a spread of intentions across multiple acts.

### Proof from Seifa

The *Gemara* attempts a proof: since the *seifa* — the *mishnah* about eaters — is unambiguously about one *avodah*, we might infer that our *mishnah* about *shelo lishmo* is also about one *avodah* when it describes the hybrid case.

### Separate Mishnas

The *Gemara* rebuts: *ha kede'ita veha kede'ita* — each *mishnah* operates according to its own parameters. The fact that one *mishnah* discusses a single *avodah* tells us nothing about the scope of a different *mishnah*. The question about our first *mishnah* remains unresolved.

## Machshevet Ochlim

### Two Avodot Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לְאוֹכְלָיו וְשֶׁלֹּא לְאוֹכְלָיו — כָּשֵׁר. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, וְטַעְמָא דְּחַשֵּׁיב בִּזְרִיקָה, דְּאֵין מַחְשְׁבֶת אוֹכְלִין בִּזְרִיקָה —
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further proposes: **Come** and **hear** a solution based on what was taught later in that other mishna: If one slaughtered the Paschal lamb with the intent that it be **for those who can eat it and for those who cannot eat it, it is valid.** This ruling must be clarified: **What** exactly **are the circumstances? If we say** that one had two intentions **during two** different **rites,** e.g., he slaughtered it for those who can eat it and sprinkled the blood for those who cannot eat it, **then the reason** that the sacrifice is valid **is that he had the** disqualifying **intention at** the time of the **sprinkling,** which does not invalidate the offering **because there is no intent concerning those who can eat it during the sprinkling.** That is, intent to feed the Paschal lamb to those who are unable to eat it invalidates the offering only during the slaughter but not during the sprinkling.

The *Gemara* now attempts a stronger proof from the same *mishnah* about owners. It focuses on the *mishnah*'s positive case: *le'ochlav ushelo le'ochlav kasher* — if one slaughtered with intention both for those who can eat and for those who cannot, the *Pesach* is *kasher*. If this refers to two separate *avodot* — the *shechitah* with intention for the right eaters and the *zrika* with intention for the wrong eaters — why is it *kasher*? Surely *zrika shelo le'ochlav* would be a problem.

### Ein Machshevet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת, כְּגוֹן בִּשְׁחִיטָה דְּמַהְנְיָא בַּיהּ מַחְשְׁבֶת אוֹכְלִין — פָּסוּל, וְהָא קַיְימָא לַן מִקְצָת אוֹכְלִין לָא פָּסְלָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But** if one had both intentions **during one rite, such as the slaughter, during which intent with regard to those who will eat** the offering **is effective,** the sacrifice **is disqualified.** However, this conclusion is difficult, **for we maintain that** if only **some of those who are to eat** the Paschal lamb are unable to do so, such as in this case, the offering is **not disqualified.**

The answer is a striking principle: *ein machshevet ochlin b'zrika* — the thought about the eaters is not legally relevant during the act of throwing the blood.

### Pesach Uniqueness

This is distinctive to the *korban Pesach*. By all other *korbanot*, the *machshavah* of the officiating *kohen* during any of the four *avodot* can affect the *korban's* status. By the *Pesach*, when it comes to the question of *who* will eat it, only the *shechitah* matters. The other three *avodot* are simply irrelevant for purposes of this particular form of *machshavah*.

### Torah Source

The reason traces back to the *pasuk*: *ish lefi ochlo tachosu al haseh* — one counts the eaters and slaughters accordingly. The *Torah* links the designated eaters specifically to the act of *shechitah*. The *shechitah* is what transforms the animal into meat; it is the moment most intimately connected to eating. Hence it is at *shechitah* — and only at *shechitah* — that one's thoughts about the eaters carry legal weight.

### Hybrid at Shechita

Given this principle, if the *mishnah*'s case of *le'ochlav ushelo le'ochlav* were a hybrid thought during the *shechitah* itself — both the right and wrong eaters in mind simultaneously during the one *avodah* where eater-related thought is relevant — it would indeed be *pasul*. The *machshavah* about wrong eaters during *shechitah* would invalidate.

### Mixed Valid Only If

This means the reason the *mishnah* declares the hybrid case *kasher* cannot be that hybrid thoughts about eaters are generally permissible. Rather, it must be *kasher* specifically because the bad thought about wrong eaters occurred at an *avodah* other than *shechitah* — for example, at the *zrika* — where that thought is entirely irrelevant.

### Eaters Leniency

We do have a received tradition that a mixed thought regarding eaters is not problematic in the same way that a mixed *shelo lishmo* thought is. But the *Gemara* reasons that this leniency only operates when the bad thought about eaters does not occur during *shechitah*.

### Seifa One Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו: בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת, וּמִדְּסֵיפָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת — רֵישָׁא נָמֵי בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, doesn't it** refer to a case in which one had both intentions during **one rite,** and nevertheless it is valid? **And since the latter clause** speaks about **one rite, the first clause,** the current mishna, must **also** speak about **one rite,** and yet the offering is disqualified, because even the conclusion of one's statement is significant, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei.

Consequently, when the *mishnah*'s case of *le'ochlav ushelo le'ochlav* is declared *kasher*, it can only be referring to one *avodah*: a mixed thought during *shechitah* itself. Only there does the thought about eaters matter at all. And there, the tradition is that a partially-wrong thought about eaters is acceptable.

### Reisha One Avodah

The *Gemara* draws the inference: since the *seifa* of the owners' *mishnah* is necessarily discussing a hybrid thought during one *avodah* — the *shechitah* — we can argue that the *reisha* of our *mishnah* about *shelo lishmo* is similarly discussing a hybrid thought during one *avodah*. The parallel structure supports the reading that the entire discussion concerns single acts, not sequences of separate *avodot*.

### No Final Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא?! הָא כִּדְאִיתָא וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא. סֵיפָא בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת, וְרֵישָׁא אוֹ בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת, אוֹ בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof as it refuted the previous one: **Are** the two cases **comparable?** Perhaps **this** case is **as it is and that** case is **as it is.** There is no reason to equate the cases, as it is possible to say that **the latter clause** speaks **about one rite,** whereas **the first clause,** the current mishna, speaks **either about one rite or about two rites.** Therefore, there is no conclusive resolution to Rav Pappa's question.

However, the *Gemara* once again resists this inference: *ha kede'ita veha kede'ita*. Each *mishnah* stands by its own internal logic. The owners' *mishnah* necessarily refers to one *avodah* because only *shechitah* is relevant for eater-related *machshavah*. That tells us nothing definitive about whether the *shelo lishmo* *mishnah* describes one *avodah* or two. Rav Pappa's question is left unresolved.

What is never in doubt is the *halachah* itself: if two separate intentions were expressed at two separate *avodot*, the *korban* is invalid and everyone agrees. If a single *avodah* was performed with a hybrid intention, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yose disagree, with the *mishnah* lending itself more naturally to Rabbi Yose's reading. The sole unresolved issue is which scenario the *mishnah* actually addresses.

## Pesach Other Days

### Pesach Transformed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, מַהוּ? מִי אָתֵי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ מִידֵי לִשְׁמוֹ וּמַכְשִׁיר לֵיהּ, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **A Paschal lamb that one slaughtered on the rest of the days of the year** other than the eve of Passover **for its own purpose and for a different purpose, what is its** status? The Gemara clarifies the two sides of the issue: **Does** the intent to offer the sacrifice **for a different purpose come and nullify** the intent to offer the sacrifice **for its own purpose** and thereby **make it valid, or not?** The law is that a Paschal lamb that was slaughtered for a different purpose at a time other than Passover eve attains the status of a peace-offering and is therefore valid. The question raised here is whether or not partial intent to slaughter the animal as a peace-offering rather than as a Paschal lamb suffices to validate the offering.

The *Gemara* now raises a fascinating new question. There is a well-known *halachah*: a *korban Pesach* that was slaughtered not on *Erev Pesach* — but at another time of year — takes on the status of a *shelamim*. If *Pesach* has already passed and the animal was designated but never slaughtered, it can no longer be held until the following year (since a *korban Pesach* must be under one year old), so its status is automatically reassigned as a *shelamim* even without any particular intention.

But consider a different scenario: it is still before *Pesach* — say, around *Purim* — and one slaughters the designated *Pesach* at that time. Its natural status remains a *korban Pesach* because it could yet be brought as such in a few weeks. However, if one slaughters it *with intentional* intention of a *shelamim*, the animal is transformed into a *shelamim* by that designation.

### Mixed on Purim

The *Gemara* now poses the question: what if on *Purim* one slaughtered the designated *Pesach* with a *mixed* intention — *lishmo ushelo lishmo* — partly for a *Pesach* and partly for a *shelamim*? Which intention governs?

### Conflicting Thoughts

The situation is the inverse of the *mishnah* we have been studying. In the *mishnah*, *shelo lishmo* is the bad thought and *lishmo* is the good thought; *shelo lishmo* wins, invalidating the *korban*. Here, on *Purim*, *lishmo* is the bad thought — thinking of it as a *Pesach* being brought at the wrong time is what would make it invalid as a *Pesach* — while *shelo lishmo* (thinking of it as a *shelamim*) is what validates it. The question is: does the *shelo lishmo* thought still win out even when it is the validating thought? Or is it always the *bad* component of a hybrid thought that wins?

### Which Wins

The question cuts to the heart of what the principle really is. If the rule is simply that in any hybrid thought the *shelo lishmo* component always dominates — regardless of whether it is the validating or invalidating element — then on *Purim* the *shelo lishmo* thought should validate the *korban* even in a hybrid. If, on the other hand, the rule is that a bad thought always spoils — and on *Purim* the *lishmo* thought is the bad one — then the hybrid would leave the *korban* invalid as both a proper *Pesach* and a proper *shelamim*.

### Rav Dimi Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר, אַמְרִיתָא לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הוֹאִיל וְלִשְׁמוֹ מַכְשִׁירוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ מַכְשִׁירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ: מָה לִשְׁמוֹ הַמַּכְשִׁירוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ — אֵין מוֹצִיאוֹ מִידֵי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, אַף שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ הַמַּכְשִׁירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ — אֵין מוֹצִיאוֹ מִידֵי לִשְׁמוֹ, וּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said: I raised this question in the presence of Rabbi Yirmeya** and expressed my opinion: **Since** intent to offer the sacrifice **for its own purpose validates it at its proper time,** i.e., on the eve of Passover, **and** intent to offer the sacrifice **for a different purpose validates it not at its proper time,** i.e., the rest of the year, there is room to equate the two situations. **Just** as intent **for its own purpose, which validates it at its proper time, does not nullify** the intent to offer the sacrifice **for a different purpose,** i.e., if one intends to offer the sacrifice both for its own purpose and for a different purpose it is disqualified, **so too,** intent to offer the sacrifice **for a different purpose, which validates it not at its proper time, does not nullify** the intent to offer the sacrifice **for its own purpose, and it is disqualified.** It can be concluded from here that whenever one's thoughts concerning an offering are a combination of validating and disqualifying intentions, the offering is disqualified.

When *Rav Dimi* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported the following reasoning that he had presented to Rabbi Yirmiyah. He argued: *hoil ulishmo machshiro bizmano ushelo lishmo machshiro shelo bizmano* — since *lishmo* validates on *Erev Pesach* and *shelo lishmo* validates the rest of the year. Now, on *Erev Pesach*, when a hybrid thought is present — *lishmo* combined with *shelo lishmo* — the good thought of *lishmo* is not strong enough to overcome the bad thought of *shelo lishmo*. That is the principle established by our *mishnah*: the *shelo lishmo* wins and invalidates. By the same logic, he argued, on the rest of the year when *shelo lishmo* is the validating thought: just as *lishmo* could not overcome *shelo lishmo* on *Erev Pesach*, so too *shelo lishmo* cannot overcome *lishmo* on *Purim*. In both cases, the presence of a bad thought is what ruins the hybrid, regardless of which element of the hybrid is bad. The *korban* on *Purim* with a mixed intention would therefore be invalid.

This seems like the straightforward extension of the principle. Rabbi Yirmiyah, however, disagreed: *lo*. He argued that the two cases are not symmetric. *Shelo lishmo* on *Erev Pesach* is a universal invalidity — *sheken noheig bechol hazvachim*. The rule that *shelo lishmo* is problematic applies to every *korban*; it is a pervasive, powerful disqualification. That is why it wins out even in a hybrid. But *lishmo* is almost never an invalidating thought. The only *korban* for which thinking of it as what it actually is could ever be a problem is the *Pesach* brought at the wrong time — *sheken eino noheig bechol hazvachim ela bePesach bilvad*. Such a weak and context-specific disqualification does not carry the force to overcome the validating *shelo lishmo* thought in a hybrid.

### Rava's Turn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לִי: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ שֶׁכֵּן נוֹהֵג בְּכׇל הַזְּבָחִים, תֹּאמַר בְּלִשְׁמוֹ שֶׁכֵּן אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בְּכׇל הַזְּבָחִים אֶלָּא בַּפֶּסַח בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Yirmeya **said to me: No,** this reasoning is not to be relied upon. **If you say** this rule concerning the intent to offer a Paschal lamb applies when it is sacrificed **for a different purpose,** it is **because** this disqualification **applies to all offerings,** as slaughtering an offering for a purpose other than its own disqualifies all offerings to one extent or another. Therefore, intent for the sacrifice's own purpose does not nullify intent for a different purpose. But **can you** necessarily **say** the same thing **about** the intent to slaughter a Paschal lamb **for its own purpose** at other times of the year, a disqualification **that does not apply to all offerings but to the Paschal lamb alone?** Only the Paschal lamb is disqualified if it is slaughtered for its own purpose not at its proper time, and therefore intent for a different purpose can indeed nullify intent for its own purpose.

The question is left open at this point, and the *Gemara* now turns to *Rava* for a resolution.

## Rava's Position

### Rava Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — כָּשֵׁר. דְּהָא סְתָמוֹ לִשְׁמוֹ קָאֵי, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, כִּי שָׁחֵיט לֵיהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — כָּשֵׁר,
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi's argument having been refuted, the Gemara asks: **What** conclusion was reached **about** Rav Dimi's question? **Rava said: A Paschal lamb that one slaughtered on the rest of the days of the year** with the intent that it be **for its own purpose and for a different purpose is valid.** The reason for this is as follows: **In an** otherwise **undetermined** situation, the animal **stands** to be offered **for its own purpose,** for one did not rescind its status as a Paschal lamb either verbally or by intent. **But nevertheless, when he slaughters it for a different purpose, it is valid.**

*Rava* rules: *Pesach sheshachto bish'ar yemot hashana lishmo ushelo lishmo* — a *korban Pesach* slaughtered at another time of year with a mixed intention is *kasher*. The *shelo lishmo* component prevails and validates it as a *shelamim*.

### Normal Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא: אָתֵי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ מִידֵי לִשְׁמוֹ. כִּי שָׁחֵיט נָמֵי לִשְׁמוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — אָתֵי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ וּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ מִידֵי לִשְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently,** then, the intent to offer the sacrifice **for a different purpose comes and nullify** the presumed intent to offer it **for its own purpose;** the statement that one makes to slaughter the offering as a peace-offering rather than as a Paschal lamb has the power to remove it from its previous status as a Paschal lamb. Accordingly, we should **also** say that **when** the priest **slaughters** the animal both **for its own purpose and for a different purpose,** the intent to offer it **for a different purpose** carries more weight and **comes and nullify** the stated intent to offer it **for its own purpose.**

*Rava* grounds this ruling in an analogy. In the normal case — on *Purim*, without any hybrid thought — when one slaughters the designated *Pesach* as a *shelamim*, this is *kasher*. The *shelo lishmo* thought transforms it. But note: the animal was already designated as a *korban Pesach*. It carries that identity.

### Pesach Status

The designated *Pesach* on *Purim* represents, in effect, a *lishmo* status: it is a *Pesach* in waiting, sanctified as such, with no other identity. When one slaughters it and thinks *shelamim*, the *shelo lishmo* thought overcomes the *korban*'s own entrenched status as a *Pesach* and transforms it. This is remarkable: a simple statement of *shelo lishmo* wins against the *korban*'s entire, immutable status as a *Pesach* — *stamei dePescha ushemo ka'ei*, its standing designation is that of a *Pesach* — and the *shelamim* thought wins out and validates it.

### Rava's Logic

Now, *Rava* reasons, if *shelo lishmo* can overcome even the *korban*'s own standing identity as a *Pesach* — not just a competing verbal intention, but the *korban*'s very designation — then surely when one says both *lishmo* and *shelo lishmo* aloud, the *shelo lishmo* will prevail. After all, saying "this is a *Pesach*" is no more weighty than the *korban*'s own inherent designation, which *shelo lishmo* already overcomes in the normal case.

### Shelo Lishmo Trumps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְרָבָא: דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר מֵהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר. דְּהָא לְאוֹכְלָיו וְשֶׁלֹּא לְאוֹכְלָיו כָּשֵׁר, וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּשָׁחֵיט לֵיהּ שֶׁלֹּא לְאוֹכְלָיו לְחוֹדֵיהּ — פָּסוּל, וְאַמַּאי? הָא סְתָמָא לְאוֹכְלָיו קָאֵי. אֶלָּא שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר מֵהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר. הָכִי נָמֵי, שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר מֵהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to Rava: Perhaps** a case **where** one explicitly **said** that he was slaughtering the offering for its own purpose and for a different purpose **is different from** a case **where he did not say** this. The proof of this is that if one slaughtered the Paschal lamb **for those who can eat it and those who cannot eat it, it is valid, but wherever he slaughters it only for the sake of those who cannot eat it, it is disqualified. Now, why** should this be? **Surely in an** otherwise **undetermined** situation, the animal **stands** to be offered **for those who can eat it.** This indicates that presumed intention cannot be equated with intention that is explicitly verbalized. **Rather,** a case **where** one explicitly **said** that he is slaughtering the animal for those who can eat it and those who cannot eat it **is different from** a case **where he did not say** this. **So too,** a case **where he** explicitly **said** that he is slaughtering the offering for its own purpose and for a different purpose **is different from** a case **where he did not say** this.

The implication is that *shelo lishmo* carries an inherent dominance — it trumps even in the direction of validation, not only in the direction of invalidation. Just as on *Erev Pesach shelo lishmo* wins and invalidates a hybrid thought, on *Purim shelo lishmo* wins and validates a hybrid thought.

### Mixed Irrelevant

Therefore, the mixed intention does not leave the *korban* in an indeterminate state. *Shelo lishmo* still determines the outcome: the *korban* is *kasher* as a *shelamim*.

## Rav Adda Objection

### Speaking Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם כַּמָּה דְּלָא עָקַר לֵיהּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה — סְתָמֵיהּ וַדַּאי לִשְׁמוֹ קָאֵי. אֶלָּא הָכָא — סְתָמֵיהּ לְאוֹכְלָיו קָאֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Are** the two cases **comparable? Granted, there,** with regard to the Paschal lamb and the purpose for which it is offered, **as long as** one **did not uproot** the animal's status as a Paschal lamb **at** the time of **slaughter, in an** otherwise **undetermined** situation, **it certainly stands** to be offered **for its own purpose;** its standing as a Paschal lamb can be changed only if it is explicitly designated as something else. **But here,** with regard to a Paschal lamb and the people for whom it is slaughtered, **in an** otherwise **undetermined** situation, **does it stand** to be slaughtered **for those who** are registered to **eat it?**

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* challenges *Rava*. He distinguishes between a scenario where one *says* a hybrid thought and a scenario where one *says nothing* and the *korban*'s own standing status fills in the other half. When one says *shelamim* alone and the *korban* represents *Pesach*, one's explicit statement of *shelamim* contends against the *korban*'s status — and wins. But when one says both *Pesach* and *shelamim*, one's own statement is internally divided and weakened. Perhaps a compromised, self-contradicting utterance does not carry the same force as a clean, unambiguous declaration.

### Shelo L'Ochlav Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא מִימַּשְׁכִי הָנֵי וְאָתֵי אַחֲרִינֵי וּמִימְּנִי עִלָּוֵיהּ, (אַחֲרִינֵי) דִּתְנַן: נִמְנִין וּמוֹשְׁכִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶם מִמֶּנּוּ, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחַט.
>
> **Translation:** This is not the case, for in the instance of every Paschal lamb, there is the possibility that **perhaps these will withdraw and others will come and register for it. For we learned** in a mishna: People can **register** for a particular Paschal lamb **and withdraw from it until it is slaughtered.** Therefore, it is possible that this Paschal lamb will not be suitable for its subscribers at the time of its slaughter, and it makes sense to distinguish between a situation in which one explicitly stated that it is for people who can eat it, in which case it is valid even if he intended it also for people who cannot eat it, and a situation in which he did not make an explicit statement. This is different from slaughtering a Paschal lamb not in its time, for in that case the offering maintains its previous status as a Paschal lamb until its status is explicitly changed through a verbal statement.

*Rav Adda* supports this from the eaters case. We know that if one thinks *shelo le'ochlav* alone — only of the wrong eaters — the *Pesach* is invalid. Yet the *korban Pesach* naturally represents its designated eaters: *stamei le'ochlav ka'ei*. Why does the *korban*'s inherent association with the right eaters not combine with the wrong-eaters thought to create a valid hybrid? Logically, one might think: the *korban* represents the right eaters; the *kohen* said the wrong eaters; together it is a hybrid, and by the principle we have seen, a hybrid in the eaters case is *kasher*.

### No Combination

This does not happen. One's explicit statement of wrong eaters does not get rescued by the *korban*'s natural association with the right ones. The *korban*'s status cannot supplement one's stated intention to create a hybrid.

### Said vs Silent

The distinction is between what one *says* and what one leaves implicit. When one says nothing and the *korban*'s status supplies the *lishmo*, one's explicit *shelo lishmo* can overcome it. But when one has *said* only the wrong thing, one cannot invoke the *korban*'s status as an unspoken addition to one's own declaration to construct a valid hybrid retroactively.

### Here Too Weak

The same logic applies here. When one says *shelamim* alone and says nothing else, one's clear declaration of *shelamim* wins against the *korban*'s identity. But when one says both *Pesach* and *shelamim*, one's own utterance is compromised — it no longer presents a clean, decisive declaration of *shelamim*. A divided statement is weaker than a unified one.

### Can't Supplement

Therefore, *Rav Adda* concludes, one cannot invoke what the *korban* naturally represents to shore up one's own hybrid statement. The situations are genuinely different: when one says one thing, the *korban*'s status is irrelevant because one's own clear statement determines the outcome. When one's own statement is already mixed, the *korban*'s status does not rescue it.

## Rava Responds

### Different Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר מֵהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר. דְּהָא לְאוֹכְלָיו וְשֶׁלֹּא לְאוֹכְלָיו כָּשֵׁר, וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּשָׁחֵיט לֵיהּ שֶׁלֹּא לְאוֹכְלָיו לְחוֹדֵיהּ — פָּסוּל, וְאַמַּאי? הָא סְתָמָא לְאוֹכְלָיו קָאֵי. אֶלָּא שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר מֵהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר. הָכִי נָמֵי, שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר מֵהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Perhaps** a case **where** one explicitly **said** that he was slaughtering the offering for its own purpose and for a different purpose **is different from** a case **where he did not say** this. The proof of this is that if one slaughtered the Paschal lamb **for those who can eat it and those who cannot eat it, it is valid, but wherever he slaughters it only for the sake of those who cannot eat it, it is disqualified. Now, why** should this be? **Surely in an** otherwise **undetermined** situation, the animal **stands** to be offered **for those who can eat it.** This indicates that presumed intention cannot be equated with intention that is explicitly verbalized. **Rather,** a case **where** one explicitly **said** that he is slaughtering the animal for those who can eat it and those who cannot eat it **is different from** a case **where he did not say** this. **So too,** a case **where he** explicitly **said** that he is slaughtering the offering for its own purpose and for a different purpose **is different from** a case **where he did not say** this.

*Rava* rejects the comparison. The eaters case and the *Purim* case are fundamentally different, and the asymmetry explains why the *korban*'s status can supplement one's declaration in the *Purim* scenario but cannot do so in the eaters scenario.

### Mutable Group

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא מִימַּשְׁכִי הָנֵי וְאָתֵי אַחֲרִינֵי וּמִימְּנִי עִלָּוֵיהּ, (אַחֲרִינֵי) דִּתְנַן: נִמְנִין וּמוֹשְׁכִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶם מִמֶּנּוּ, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחַט.
>
> **Translation:** This is not the case, for in the instance of every Paschal lamb, there is the possibility that **perhaps these will withdraw and others will come and register for it. For we learned** in a mishna: People can **register** for a particular Paschal lamb **and withdraw from it until it is slaughtered.** Therefore, it is possible that this Paschal lamb will not be suitable for its subscribers at the time of its slaughter, and it makes sense to distinguish between a situation in which one explicitly stated that it is for people who can eat it, in which case it is valid even if he intended it also for people who cannot eat it, and a situation in which he did not make an explicit statement. This is different from slaughtering a Paschal lamb not in its time, for in that case the offering maintains its previous status as a Paschal lamb until its status is explicitly changed through a verbal statement.

By the eaters case, the *korban* does not have a fixed, fully determinate identity with respect to who its eaters are. The *Torah* itself provides for fluidity: *nimnin umoshchin yadeihem mimenu ad sheyishachet* — one may be added to the group or withdraw from it until the moment of *shechitah*. The membership of the group is a moving target, variable right up to the final moment.

### Status Not Fixed

Precisely because the identity of the eaters is not locked in and can change, the *korban Pesach* does not fully and immutably represent a specific set of eaters. It therefore cannot supply the other half of a hybrid thought about eaters by itself. When one's statement refers to wrong eaters, there is no sufficiently firm counter-designation standing behind the *korban* to create a meaningful hybrid. To express the right owners, one must articulate them explicitly; the *korban* cannot do it for you.

### Fixed Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה בְּשִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים, מַהוּ? שִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים כְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ דָּמֵי — וּמַכְשַׁיר לֵיהּ, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **a Paschal lamb that one slaughtered on the rest of the days of the year with a change of owner,** i.e., he slaughtered it not for the sake of its original owner, **what is its** status? **Is a change of owner like a change of sanctity?** A Paschal lamb whose sanctity is changed to that of a peace-offering now has the status of a peace-offering, and so too, a Paschal lamb that is slaughtered not for the sake of its original owner, an intention that at its proper time would disqualify the sacrifice, now has the status of a peace-offering. **And it validates it,** for a peace-offering that was slaughtered not for the sake of its original owner is valid. **Or,** perhaps **not,** for it remains a Paschal lamb and is disqualified.

On *Purim*, however, the situation is entirely different. The *korban*'s identity is fully fixed and immutable: it is a *korban Pesach*, sanctified as such, and nothing about that designation fluctuates. There is no way for its status to change except through an intentional act of the *kohen* at the time of *shechitah*. *Stamei dePascha ushemo ka'ei* — its standing designation is that of a *Pesach*, completely and unambiguously.

### Full Status Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אַמְרִיתָא לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא: הוֹאִיל וְשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ פּוֹסְלוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וְשִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים פּוֹסְלוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ. מָה שִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ שֶׁפּוֹסְלוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ — מַכְשִׁירוֹ לְאַחַר זְמַנּוֹ, אַף שִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים שֶׁפּוֹסְלוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ — מַכְשִׁירוֹ לְאַחַר זְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: I raised this question in the presence of Rava** and expressed my opinion: **Since a change in sanctity invalidates it** as an offering **in its proper time,** as if one intended it in its proper time as a peace-offering it is invalid, **and a change in owner,** i.e., one intended that the offering be for someone other than its owner, also **invalidates it in its proper time,** the following analogy is made: **Just as a change in sanctity, which invalidates it in its proper time, validates it** as an offering **after its proper time, a change in owner, which invalidates it in its proper time, also validates it** as an offering **after its proper time.**

When one declares *shelamim* and nothing else, this single, clear declaration overcomes the *korban*'s fully committed identity as a *Pesach* and transforms it. This is remarkable: a simple statement of *shelo lishmo* wins against the *korban*'s entire, entrenched, immutable status as a *Pesach* — not just a competing verbal intention, but the *korban*'s very designation — and the *shelamim* thought wins out and validates it.

### Rava Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר לִי: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ — שֶׁכֵּן פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בְּאַרְבַּע עֲבוֹדוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **said to me: No,** this analogy is not to be accepted: **If you say** this **about a change in sanctity,** it is **because the disqualification it** causes **is in** the offering **itself,** for the disqualification relates to the very sanctity of the offering, **and it applies to all four of the rites,** for the disqualification caused by a change in sanctity can occur not only during the slaughter but also during any of the four rites performed with an offering.

If *shelo lishmo* is powerful enough to overcome the *korban*'s fully committed *Pesach* status when nothing else is said, then adding the word *Pesach* to one's own utterance — thereby creating a hybrid — cannot make things worse. The *korban*'s own designation already provided a *lishmo* element against which *shelo lishmo* already wins. Saying *lishmo* explicitly adds nothing beyond what the *korban*'s status already supplied. Therefore *shelo lishmo* still wins, and the *korban* is *kasher* as a *shelamim*.

### Debate Ends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֶשְׁנוֹ לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בְּצִבּוּר כְּבַיָּחִיד. תֹּאמַר בְּשִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים — דְּאֵין פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ בְּאַרְבַּע עֲבוֹדוֹת, וְאֵינוֹ לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְאֵינוֹ בְּצִבּוּר כְּבַיָּחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, the disqualification following from a change in sanctity **applies after death.** If one consecrates an offering and dies, his son must bring the offering in his place, and it can be invalidated through a change in sanctity. Moreover, **it applies to communal** offerings **as it does to** the offerings of **an individual.** However, **can you** necessarily **say** the same thing **with regard to a change in owner** that does not have these characteristics? **Its disqualification is not in** the offering **itself; and it does not** apply **to all four rites** but only to the sprinkling of the blood on the altar; **and it does not** apply **after death,** for after the owner has died there is no true owner of the offering, and therefore, if the priest intends to offer it for someone else the offering remains valid; **and it does not** apply **to communal** offerings **as it does to** the offerings of **an individual,** since it is not possible to have in mind a different owner, as it is owned by the whole community.

The debate between *Rava* and *Rav Adda bar Ahava* thus turns on a precise and elegant distinction. *Rav Adda* argues that there is a meaningful difference between saying something and saying nothing — that one's own hybrid statement is weaker than a clean unilateral declaration, and that the *korban*'s status cannot make up the difference. *Rava* responds by distinguishing between the eaters case, where the *korban*'s status with respect to its designated group is genuinely mutable and therefore cannot supply a fixed counterweight, and the *Purim* case, where the *korban*'s *Pesach* identity is fully immutable and the *shelo lishmo* declaration already prevails against it in the simple case — making the addition of an explicit *lishmo* statement by the *kohen* carry no additional weight against the *shelo lishmo* that was already dominant.
