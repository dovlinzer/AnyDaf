# Gittin 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyan Peirot Debate

### Kinyan Peirot Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ לְפֵירוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § **One who sells his field for** just its **produce,** meaning that he retains ownership over the field itself and he sells the rights to all of its produce to someone else, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The purchaser **brings** first fruits from this field to the Temple **and recites** the verses in the Torah associated with the bringing of the first fruits, in which he thanks God for: "The land which You, Lord, have given me" (Deuteronomy 26:10). **Reish Lakish says:** Although the buyer **brings** the first fruits, **he does not recite** the verses, since it is not his field.

The daf begins nine lines from the bottom of 47b, at the word *eitvei*. We are in the midst of an ongoing debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, which grew out of the Mishnah's discussion of *bikkurim* — specifically, what happens to the status of fruit for purposes of *bikkurim* when a field is sold to a non-Jew. From there the Gemara moved to a broader dispute: if someone sells a field *le-peirot* — meaning the purchaser acquires not actual title to the land but only the right to its produce — does that fruit-ownership entitle him to be *mevi ve-korei*, to bring *bikkurim* and recite the declaration *ha-adamah asher natata li*?

Rabbi Yochanan holds that it does. Since the fruit belongs to the purchaser, he has sufficient ownership and title to the land to declare *ha-adamah asher natata li*. Reish Lakish disagrees: although such a person brings the *bikkurim* as his own fruit, he cannot recite the declaration, for *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* — the ownership of land for the sake of its fruit is not equivalent to actual ownership of the land itself.

### Nichsei Milog Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה בָּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וּבִיכּוּרֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּיָדוֹ, וְשָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא. מֵתָה – אִין, לֹא מֵתָה – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **raised an objection to** Rabbi Yoḥanan based on a *baraita*: If **he was traveling on the road** to Jerusalem **and the first fruits of his wife's** field **were in his possession, and he heard that his wife died,** then **he brings** the first fruits **and recites** the verses. One can infer: If **she died, yes,** he brings the fruits and recites the verses, as he has now inherited the field itself; but if **she did not die,** then **no,** he does not recite the verses, because acquisition of a field's produce is not considered to be like the acquisition of the field itself.

The Gemara now brings a *beraisa* as a potential proof: a man is traveling and carries with him fruit from his wife's property. This is a case of *nichsei milog* — the wife holds title to the land while the husband holds the right to its produce. The husband thus has exactly what we have been discussing: *kinyan peirot* without *kinyan haguf*. While he is on the road, he hears that his wife has died. Since he inherits from her, he now acquires full title to the land. The ruling is that he may bring the fruit and recite the declaration.

The Gemara draws the obvious inference: *meisah in, lo meisah lo* — the fact that he recites only because his wife died implies that had she not died, he could not have recited it. This supports Reish Lakish: *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and one who holds the field only for its fruit is *mevi* but *eino korei*.

### Baraisa Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הַדִּין דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא מֵתָה; וּמֵתָה אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ – סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא לִיגְזוֹר מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The same is true** in that case, **that even though she did not die** he also brings the fruits and recites the verses, **but** it **was necessary for** the *baraita* to mention the possibility that **she died,** because **it might enter your mind to say that there should be** a rabbinic **decree** in this case **due to** the statement **of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina** concerning a similar *halakha*.

Rabbi Yochanan rejects this inference. He maintains that even had his wife not died, he could have recited *ha-adamah asher natata li*. The reason the *beraisa* presents the case where she dies, he explains, is to teach a novel ruling even in that scenario — namely, that even after the wife's death, when a status change has occurred, he may still recite the declaration.

One might wonder: what is the significance of a status change here, if according to Rabbi Yochanan his relationship to the fruit was unaffected? The answer is that the status change, however subtle, might have been thought to disqualify the recitation because it resembles a case discussed by Rabbi Yose ben Chanina.

### Shaliach Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: בְּצָרָן, וְשִׁגְּרָן בְּיַד שָׁלִיחַ, וּמֵת שָׁלִיחַ בַּדֶּרֶךְ – מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ״–״וְהֵבֵאתָ״ – עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא לְקִיחָה וַהֲבָאָה בְּאֶחָד; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** If one **harvested** his first fruits **and sent them in the possession** of **an agent, and the agent died on the way,** then someone else **brings** the fruits **but does not recite** the verses, **as it is stated: "And you shall take…and you shall bring"** (see Deuteronomy 26:2–3), and this juxtaposition teaches that the verses are not recited **until the taking and bringing will be** accomplished **by one** person. One might have said that since the husband took the fruits as the owner of the fruits alone and not the field, as the field was owned by his wife, and then he became the owner of the field as well and is bringing the fruits as the full owner, he should not recite the verses. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that he is considered to be the full owner before his wife's death, because the acquisition of a field's produce is considered to be like the acquisition of the field itself.

Rabbi Yose ben Chanina taught: if the owner harvests the fruit and sends them via a *shaliach*, and the *shaliach* dies along the way, and the owner then retrieves the fruit and completes the delivery to Jerusalem — the owner brings the fruit but does not recite the declaration. The reason: *ve-lakachta ve-heiveta* — the Torah requires that the taking and the bringing be performed by one person. When the *shaliach* was involved, there were two statuses: the owner performed part of the process, the *shaliach* performed part. That interruption, or that divided agency, is enough to preclude the *keriah*.

The cases are not entirely parallel. In the *shaliach* case there is a genuinely meaningful change in status, since a *shaliach* cannot recite the declaration in any event — the fruit is not ultimately the *shaliach*'s property. In our case, by contrast, according to Rabbi Yochanan the husband could have recited from the outset. Nevertheless, the Gemara explains that Rabbi Yochanan reads the *beraisa* as teaching that even the relatively minor status change caused by the wife's death does not disqualify the recitation — *kamashma lan*. As a straightforward reading of the *beraisa*, this is certainly not compelling evidence; it simply shows how Rabbi Yochanan would respond to it.

## Yovel Extension

### Both Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish **follow their** standard line of **reasoning** with regard to the issue of the acquisition of a field's produce. **As it was stated** that they had a dispute in the following case as well: **One who sells his field in** the **time when the Jubilee** Year **is practiced,** and every sale of land is only for its produce, because the land returns to its original owners in the Jubilee Year, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The purchaser **brings** the first fruits **and recites** the verses. **Reish Lakish says:** The purchaser **brings** the first fruits **but** he **does not recite** the verses.

The Gemara notes that this dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish *azlu le-ta'amaihu* — follows their respective positions in a parallel debate stated elsewhere. There it was taught: one who sells a field during the period when *yovel* is in effect — and during *yovel* any sold field ultimately reverts to its original owner — does he have sufficient ownership to be *mevi ve-korei*? Rabbi Yochanan says yes, *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami*. Reish Lakish says no, *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and therefore one is *mevi* but *eino korei*.

It is worth pausing to note that in the *yovel* case the argument for full ownership is considerably stronger than in the *peirot* case. When a field is sold during *yovel*, the purchaser intends to acquire the land outright; it is only an external legal mechanism — the institution of *yovel* — that causes eventual reversion. The purchaser has made a genuine land purchase, not merely a contract for fruit rights. One would therefore expect it to be easier to say *ha-adamah asher natata li* here.

### Peirot Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא. דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָהִיא, בְּהָהִיא קָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּכִי קָא נָחֵית – אַדַּעְתָּא דְפֵירָא קָא נָחֵית; אֲבָל בְּהָךְ – דְּאַדַּעְתָּא דְגוּפֵיהּ קָא נָחֵית, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to state the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish in both cases. **Because if it was stated** only **in that** case, with regard to one who acquires a field for its produce, one might say that it is only **in that** case **that Reish Lakish says** that he does not recite the verses, **since** already **when** he **descended** to the field, i.e., took possession of the land, **he descended with the intention of** acquiring only **the produce,** as stipulated at the time of the sale; **but in this** case, with regard to one who purchases the field when the Jubilee Year is practiced, **when he descended** to the field **with the intention** of acquiring the land **itself, say** that **he concedes to Rabbi Yoḥanan** that he recites the verses. Therefore, it is necessary to state explicitly that Reish Lakish holds that he does not recite the verses in this case as well.

The Gemara accordingly states that both cases are necessary, *tzricha*. Had the Gemara only stated the *peirot* case, one might have thought Reish Lakish's ruling is limited to that scenario — where from the very outset, *dachi nachit ada'ata de-peirei nachit*, the purchaser's entire relationship to the land was conceived as fruit-rights only. But in the *yovel* case, *da-ada'ata de-gufa nachit* — the purchaser intended to acquire the land itself, and one might have thought even Reish Lakish would agree with Rabbi Yochanan.

### Yovel Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָא, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it was stated** only **in this** case, with regard to one who purchases the field when the Jubilee Year is practiced, one might say that it is only **in this** case that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is like the acquisition of the item itself and recites the verses, as he purchased the field to fully own it; **but in that** case, where the sale was only with regard to the produce, **say** that **he concedes to Reish Lakish** that he does not recite the verses. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state the dispute in both cases.

The full scope of the dispute thus emerges: Rabbi Yochanan is willing to call even a purchaser of fruit-rights an owner sufficient to recite *ha-adamah asher natata li*, while Reish Lakish maintains a standard so demanding that even a purchaser who intended to acquire the land itself, yet whose ownership is temporally bounded by *yovel*, cannot be regarded as a full owner for purposes of *keriah*.

### Tree Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַקּוֹנֶה אִילָן וְקַרְקָעוֹ – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין הַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a proof in support of Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna (*Bikkurim* 1:11): **One who acquires a tree and its** surrounding **land brings** the first fruits of those trees **and recites** the verses, even though he is required to return the land in the Jubilee Year. The Gemara responds: **With what are we dealing here** according to the opinion of Reish Lakish? The mishna is referring to one who acquires a tree and its surrounding land **in** the **time when the Jubilee** Year **is not practiced,** so the acquisition is permanent.

The Gemara attempts to bring proofs. *Ta shema* — one who purchases a tree along with its underlying ground may bring *bikkurim* and recite the declaration. This would seem to show that even a purchaser, when *yovel* is practiced, may be *mevi ve-korei*. The Gemara deflects: *hacha be-mai askinan bi-zman she-ein ha-yovel noheg* — the case refers to a time when *yovel* is no longer in effect, which was true toward the end of the First Temple period when the twelve tribes were no longer all settled on their respective portions of the land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַקּוֹנֶה שְׁנֵי אִילָנוֹת בְּתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא. הָא שְׁלֹשָׁה – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא! הָכָא נָמֵי בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין הַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof in support of Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna (*Bikkurim* 1:6): **One who acquires two trees in another's field brings** the first fruits of those trees **but does not recite** the verses, as he acquires only the trees but not the land. **But** if one acquires **three** trees, he **brings** the first fruits of those trees **and recites** the verses, because he also acquires the land surrounding the trees, despite the fact that the land is returned in the Jubilee Year. The Gemara rejects this: **Here too,** Reish Lakish would explain that the mishna is referring to one who acquires three trees **in** the **time when the Jubilee** Year **is not practiced.**

A further attempt: one who purchases two trees within another's field is *mevi* but *eino korei*, since two trees do not convey rights to the land beneath them; but if he purchases three trees — which, as established elsewhere, carries a presumption that the land between them is included — he is *mevi ve-korei*. Again, the Gemara answers: *hacha nami bi-zman she-ein yovel noheg*. Any Mishnaic case in which a purchaser is *mevi ve-korei* can simply be referring to a time when *yovel* does not apply. It is therefore difficult to bring a counterproof from such sources.

## Yovel Rishon

### Pre-Yovel Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּיּוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי, אֲבָל בְּיּוֹבֵל רִאשׁוֹן – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא, דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא סְמַךְ דַּעְתַּיְיהוּ; לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּיוֹבֵל רִאשׁוֹן, הָא בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And now that Rav Ḥisda said:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish is with regard to land sold **during the second Jubilee,** after the Jewish people already practiced the Jubilee Year once and people could trust that the land would be returned in the Jubilee Year, **but** with regard to land sold **during the first Jubilee,** which was practiced by the Jews immediately following their entry into Eretz Yisrael, **everyone agrees** that **he brings** the first fruits **and recites** the verses, **as they did not yet rely** on the fact that the fields would be returned, there is no need to claim that according to Reish Lakish these *mishnayot* are referring to when the Jubilee Year was not practiced. Instead, one could answer that it is **not difficult: This,** the *mishnayot* that state that he brings the first fruits and recites the verses, are referring to land sold **during the first Jubilee. That,** where Reish Lakish rules that he brings the first fruits but does not recite the verses, is referring to land sold **during the second Jubilee.**

Against this backdrop, Rav Chisda offers a significant teaching: the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish applies only from *Yovel sheni* — the second *yovel* cycle and beyond. But regarding *Yovel rishon* — the first *yovel* cycle ever practiced in the Land of Israel — *divrei ha-kol meivi ve-korei*. Everyone agrees that a purchaser during the first *yovel* cycle may bring and recite.

### Perception Principle

Why? Because *d-achti lo samcha da'ataihu* — people's minds had not yet settled into confidence that the reversion would actually take place. The institution of *yovel* had been proclaimed in the Torah, but it had never been practiced. Neither the buyer nor the seller truly felt certain it would be enforced. In their minds, the sale might well turn out to be permanent. And since their psychological relationship to the land was that of full and final ownership, that perception was sufficient to grant the purchaser the status of *kinyan haguf*.

This is a striking principle. Legally, on paper, the sale was absolutely time-bound and the reversion fully operative. What makes the ownership incomplete in Reish Lakish's view is not a formal legal deficiency but the lived awareness that the land will not remain permanently in the purchaser's hands. And when that awareness is absent — because the institution of *yovel* had never yet been tested — the halakhic assessment shifts accordingly. Perception reshapes the legal relationship to the land.

### Yovel Divide

This teaching also resolves the evidentiary problem. Any source that appears to show *meivi ve-korei* during *yovel* can be explained as referring to *Yovel rishon*, where everyone agrees. Any statement that one is *mevi* but *eino korei* applies from *Yovel sheni* onward, and that is the context of Reish Lakish's ruling. *Lo kashya: ha bi-Yovel rishon, ha bi-Yovel sheni.*

## Tannaim Parallel

### Hekdesh Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: מִנַּיִן לַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה מֵאָבִיו, וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָבִיו – מִנַּיִין שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** The *halakha* is that if one consecrated but did not redeem his ancestral field, and the Temple treasury sold it to another Jew, it becomes the property of the priesthood in the Jubilee Year. However, if one purchases a field from another Jew and consecrates it, it reverts back to the original owner in the Jubilee Year. The *baraita* taught: **From where** is it derived **that one who purchases a field from his father** in the time when the Jubilee Year was practiced **and consecrated it, and afterward his father died, from where** is it derived **that it should be** considered **before him like an ancestral field,** and it does not revert to the son's ownership in the Jubilee Year?

The Gemara now asks whether this debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish parallels a *tanna'im* dispute. Before examining the parallel, it is worth noting that Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish originally spoke only about the narrow question of *mevi ve-korei* in *bikkurim*. It is the Gemara that has generalized their positions into the broader principle of *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf* or *lav ke-kinyan haguf*, a principle that could have implications throughout *halakha*. Accordingly the Gemara now tries to connect this to other debates.

### Timing Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם אֶת שְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר לֹא מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״ – שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, יָצְתָה זוֹ שֶׁרְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **The verse states:** "And **if** he sanctify to the Lord **a field which he has bought, which is not of the field of his ancestral field"** (Leviticus 27:22). The addition of the phrase: "Which is not of the field of his ancestral field," teaches that the *halakha* that the field reverts to the original owner applies specifically to **a field that is not fit to be an ancestral field,** meaning that he would not inherit it in the future. **This** field, which the son was entitled to inherit after he had consecrated it, **is excluded, as it is fit to be an ancestral field,** although the son had purchased it. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon.**

The *beraisa* in question asks: how do we know that if someone purchases a field from his father, sanctifies it, and then his father dies — all before *yovel* arrives — the field is treated as a *sdei achuzah*, an ancestral field? The mechanics here are important. When a person sanctifies a *sdei miknah*, a purchased field, to the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, that field reverts at *yovel* to its original owner, because the sanctifier could not dedicate more than he owned. But when a person sanctifies a *sdei achuzah*, his ancestral land, the Torah provides that at *yovel* the field goes to the *kohanim* and does not revert to the original owner — the *Beit ha-Mikdash* retains it permanently.

In our case: the son purchased the field from his father during *yovel*. As a purchased field, it would have reverted to the father had *yovel* come. But the son sanctified it, and before *yovel* arrived, the father died. The son now inherits the land outright. When *yovel* comes, does it revert — returning to the "father," who is now dead, and passing from there to the son as heir? Or does it stay with the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, since by now the son has full title to it? The *beraisa* rules it is treated as a *sdei achuzah* and stays with the *Beit ha-Mikdash*.

The derivation comes from the verse: *im sdei miknaso asher lo mi-sdei achuzaso* — if he sanctifies a field he purchased, which was not a field of his inheritance. The phrase *asher lo mi-sdei achuzaso* — that it was not from his ancestral holdings — is seen as excluding from the reversion category any field that was either actually ancestral land or had the potential and actualized fitness to become ancestral land.

### Rabbi Meir View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה מֵאָבִיו וּמֵת אָבִיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁהּ, מִנַּיִין שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם אֶת שְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר לֹא מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״ – שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, יָצְתָה זוֹ שֶׁהִיא שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה. וְאִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מֵת אָבִיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁהּ – לָא צְרִיכָא קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Meir says: From where** is it derived **that one who purchases a field from his father** at the time when the Jubilee Year was practiced **and his father died, and afterward he consecrated it, from where** is it derived **that it should be** considered **before him like an ancestral field,** and does not revert to the son in the Jubilee Year? **The verse states:** "And **if** he sanctify to the Lord **a field which he has bought, which is not of the field of his ancestral field."** The addition of the phrase: "Which is not of the field of his ancestral field," teaches that the *halakha* that the field reverts to the original owner applies specifically to **a field that is not an ancestral field,** meaning that he did not inherit it. **This** field, which the son inherited before he consecrated it, **is excluded, as it is an ancestral field, while according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, a verse is not required** to teach that in a case where **his father died, and he consecrated it afterward,** it is considered an ancestral field, as this is obvious. The Gemara explains why the other *tanna'im* do not require a verse to teach the *halakha* in the case discussed by Rabbi Meir:

Rabbi Meir, by contrast, asks a different question: how do we know that if someone purchases a field from his father, the father then dies, and only afterward does the son sanctify it — that this constitutes a *sdei achuzah*? The verse teaches this explicitly.

But why would Rabbi Meir need a verse for this apparently obvious case? If the father died before the sanctification, the son clearly inherits the land fully; how could one think it is still a *sdei miknah*? The Gemara explains: Rabbi Meir holds *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami*. From the moment the son purchased the field from the father during *yovel*, he was already considered the full owner. When the father subsequently died, there was, in a sense, nothing new for the son to inherit with respect to this land — *hu de-lo yaris ve-lo midi*, it is as if he inherited nothing additional. Since he was already treated as the full owner, the father's death does not produce a change in his legal status. One might therefore have thought that the *sdei miknah* status established at the time of purchase persists and is not superseded by the father's death. The verse is therefore needed to tell us that the *sdei achuzah* status does prevail — that the fact of actual inheritance supersedes the earlier purchase-status, and therefore the sanctification thereafter is of a *sdei achuzah*.

This requirement for a verse demonstrates *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami* — Rabbi Meir's position aligns with Rabbi Yochanan.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי? דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, וּבְהָא – בְּמִיתַת אָבִיו הוּא דְּלָא יָרֵית וְלָא מִידֵּי, וְהִלְכָּךְ מֵת אָבִיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁהּ – צְרִיךְ קְרָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What, is it not** that **they disagree about this, as Rabbi Meir holds:** The **acquisition of** an item for **its produce is considered** to be **like** the **acquisition of the item itself, and in this** case, when he purchased the field, i.e., the rights to the produce, from his father before his death, **it is** the case **that he inherits nothing when his father dies,** as he had already taken ownership of the field when he purchased it from his father, and nothing changed with his father's death; **and therefore,** if **his father died and he consecrated it afterward,** then **a verse is necessary** to teach that it is treated like an ancestral field, as one might have thought that the field is his entirely as a result of the purchase, and not because of an ancestral inheritance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבְרִי: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, וּבְהָא – בְּמִיתַת אָבִיו הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּקָא יָרֵית, וְהִלְכָּךְ מֵת אָבִיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁהּ – לָא צְרִיכָא קְרָא; וְכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא – לְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָבִיו הוּא דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** While **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon hold** that the **acquisition of** an item for **its produce is not considered** to be **like** the **acquisition of the item itself, and in this** case, when he had purchased the field from his father before his father died, **with the death of his father he now inherits** the field as well, since until now he owned only the rights to the produce; **and therefore** if **his father died and he consecrated** the field **afterward, a verse is not necessary** to teach that it is an ancestral field, as it is obvious that he now owns it due to his inheritance. **And when a verse was necessary,** it is **for** a case where **he consecrated** the field **and his father died afterward that it was necessary.**

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon hold *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*. Therefore, when the son purchased from the father during *yovel*, he was not considered the full owner — only the owner of fruit-rights. When the father died, the son then truly inherited and became the full owner. In that sequence — purchase, then inheritance before sanctification — it is entirely obvious that the sanctification is of a *sdei achuzah*, and no verse is needed. The verse can therefore be deployed for the more novel case: that even if the son sanctified it while it was still only a *sdei miknah*, if the father subsequently dies and the potential for ancestral status is actualized, it is still treated as a *sdei achuzah*.

This positions Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon as paralleling Reish Lakish.

### Alt. Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ, בְּעָלְמָא קָסָבְרִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי. וְהָכָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן קְרָא אַשְׁכַּחוּ וּדְרוּשׁ – לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״אִם אֶת שְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר לֹא אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״; מַאי ״מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״? שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה – יָצְתָה זוֹ, שֶׁרְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation of the dispute. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Actually, I** could **say to you** that **in general Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon hold** that the **acquisition of** an item for **its produce is considered** to be **like** the **acquisition of the item itself,** and they agree that the verse is necessary to teach the *halakha* concerning a case where he consecrated the field after his father's death. **And here, Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon found** another element of the **verse and** they **expounded** it. They maintain that if the verse is teaching the *halakha* only in the case where he consecrated the field after his father's death, then **let the Merciful One write** in the Torah: And **if** he sanctify to the Lord **a field which he has bought, which is not his ancestral field. What** is the meaning of the expression: **"Of his ancestral field"** (Leviticus 27:22)? This emphasizes: **A field that is not fit to be an ancestral field,** meaning that he would not inherit it in the future. **This** field, which the son was entitled to inherit after he had consecrated it, **is excluded, as it is fit to be an ancestral field,** although the son had purchased it.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes an alternative reading. He maintains that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon actually hold *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami* — they agree with Rabbi Meir on that underlying principle, and thus align with Rabbi Yochanan. According to this reading, the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon is not about *kinyan peirot* at all.

### Pasuk Language

How then does Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explain why Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon could skip directly to the more complex case? They found a doubly redundant formulation in the verse. The Torah could have simply said *mi-sdei miknaso asher lo achuzaso*. Instead it says *asher lo mi-sdei achuzaso* — the added prefix *mi-* makes the phrase *mi-sdei achuzaso* even more expansive, as if to say not even *derived from* the category of ancestral land. This double extra language allows Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon to perform two derivations simultaneously — one for the case where the son inherits before sanctifying (which, according to this reading, also requires a verse even for them, because of the *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf* principle), and one for the case where the potential was present and actualized after the fact.

## Rabbi Yosef

### Brothers Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אִי לָאו דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, לֹא מָצָא יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ – לָקוֹחוֹת הֵן, וּמַחְזִירִין זֶה לָזֶה בְּיוֹבֵל;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: If not** for the fact **that Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the **acquisition of** an item for **its produce is considered** to be **like** the **acquisition of the item itself, he would not find his hands or his feet in the study hall,** i.e., there would be a contradiction between Rabbi Yoḥanan's statements. **As Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Brothers who divided** property received as an inheritance **are** considered to be **purchasers** from one another, **and** as purchasers of land **they must return** the portions **to each other in the Jubilee** Year, at which point they may redistribute the property.

Rabbi Yosef offers a striking observation: were it not for Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami*, one could not even get started in learning, *lo matza yadav ve-raglav be-veit ha-medrash*. The reason lies in a further ruling of Rabbi Yochanan, cited by Rabbi Assi: brothers who divide an inherited estate are considered as purchasers of one another's portions — *lakuchos hein u-machzirin zeh la-zeh ba-yovel*. At *yovel*, they must return the property and renegotiate the division.

The conceptual basis of this ruling is that when brothers inherit jointly, each acquires an undivided half-interest in every property. When they subsequently divide — one taking the northern field, one the southern — this is legally conceived as a swap: each transfers his half-interest in one field in exchange for the other's half-interest in another. They have, in effect, purchased from one another.

This produces an almost startling consequence: come *yovel*, everything reverts. Not only formal commercial sales but every inheritance that has ever been divided among siblings would need to be reconsolidated and re-divided. One wonders how society could function under such a system, but that is the logical consequence of the ruling.

### Bikkurim Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ דְּמַיְיתֵי בִּיכּוּרִים אֶלָּא חַד בַּר חַד עַד יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** to say that the legal status of the acquisition of an item for its produce **is not like** that of the **acquisition of the item itself,** then according to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion **you will find that** one **brings first fruits** by Torah law **only** when he is **an only** child **the son of an only** child, and so forth, dating back **to** the time of **Joshua, son of Nun.** Only in such a case does the child fully inherit the land. In any other case, the children inherit only the rights to the produce, as they must return the actual land to each other in the Jubilee Year, and would not be able to recite the verses connected with the first fruits, since they could not refer to the land that the Lord has given them. Since Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that the acquisition of an item for its produce is considered to be like the acquisition of the item itself, anyone who inherits land may recite the verses.

Now the problem for *bikkurim* becomes apparent. If *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and if brothers are *lakuchos* — considered purchasers of one another during the *yovel* period — then virtually no landowner could ever declare *ha-adamah asher natata li*, since virtually every plot of land has passed through at least one inheritance division somewhere in the family line. *Lo meshkachat de-maisi bikkurim ela chad bar chad ad Yehoshua bin Nun* — one would never find anyone who could bring *bikkurim* with the declaration unless he could demonstrate a completely uninterrupted father-to-single-son chain of descent going all the way back to Joshua bin Nun. The absurdity of this conclusion demonstrates why Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is indispensable.

### Tosafos Fix

Tosafos grapples with this, since the bottom-line *psak* is not clearly settled. If we rule like Reish Lakish — *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* — and we also rule *ein breira* (which generally prevents retroactive clarification of ownership), we seem to be back to the same problem. Tosafos resolves this by distinguishing between the technical concept of *kinyan peirot* in the context of commerce and the practical realities of inheritance. Even if one holds *ein breira* in general, that principle does not extend so far as to treat the division of an inheritance as a purchase that triggers the *yovel* reversion. The Torah's reversion mechanism applies to genuine commercial sales; the division of an estate, even if theoretically conceptualized as a swap of partial interests, would not fall within the category of sale subject to reversion. This allows one to hold *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* without generating the absurd consequence that no one could ever bring *bikkurim*.

## Rava Proofs

### Yovel Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קְרָא וּמַתְנִיתָא מְסַיְּיעִי לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. קְרָא – ״בְּמִסְפַּר שְׁנֵי תְבוּאוֹת יִמְכׇּר לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: A verse and a *baraita* support** the opinion of **Reish Lakish. A verse,** as it is written: **"According to the number of years of the crops he shall sell to you"** (Leviticus 25:15), meaning that it is not the field itself that is sold, but rather, what is sold is the right to consume the produce for a specific number of years.

Rava brings independent support for Reish Lakish from both a verse and a *beraisa*. The verse: *be-mispar shnei tevuot yimkor lach* — in the sale of a field during *yovel*, the price is calculated based on the number of years of produce remaining until *yovel*. More pointedly, the verse concludes *ki mispar tevuot hu mocher lach* — for it is years of produce that he is selling you. The Torah itself frames the transaction as a sale of produce-years rather than a sale of land, which supports Reish Lakish's view that the purchaser's relationship to the land is that of a *kinyan peirot*, not a *kinyan haguf*.

### Bechor Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתָא – דְּתַנְיָא: בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּשָׂדֶה הַחוֹזֶרֶת לְאָבִיו בְּיוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **A *baraita*** supports the opinion of Reish Lakish, **as it is taught: A firstborn son takes a double** portion **in a field that returns to his father in the Jubilee** Year, i.e., a field that his father sold, and he subsequently died, and the field therefore returns to his sons in the Jubilee Year. The *halakha* is that a firstborn son receives a double portion only of property that the father possessed at the time of his death, but not of property that was due to the father. If the acquisition of the produce is considered to be like the acquisition of the item itself, then the field the father sold would not be considered to have been in his possession at the time of his death.

The *beraisa* proof: a *bechor*, a firstborn son, receives a double portion of the estate. The rule is that he receives a double portion of assets that were under the father's control at the time of death, but not of assets that had passed out of the father's hands. The *beraisa* teaches that a *bechor* receives a double portion of a field that had been sold by the father and that will revert to the father's estate at *yovel*.

### Father Ownership

This is a direct proof for Reish Lakish. The fact that the field counts as part of the father's estate for purposes of the *bechor*'s double portion demonstrates that even after the sale, the father retains a meaningful ownership interest in the land. The purchaser has only *kinyan peirot* — rights to the produce for the interim period — while the father retains *kinyan haguf*. Reish Lakish's position is thereby supported: the two categories are legally distinct.

## Abaye Proof

### Harsha'ah Need

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, נָקְטִינַן: בַּעַל בְּנִכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ צָרִיךְ הַרְשָׁאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We have a tradition** that **a husband requires authorization with regard to his wife's property,** meaning that if there is a legal dispute between the husband and another person with regard to the usufruct property the husband received from his wife, he requires authorization from his wife that he may act on her behalf in order to present himself in court as a litigant. If he does not receive such authorization, the other litigant has the right to claim that he is not legally answerable to the husband, and may insist that the wife come before the court herself. This indicates that the acquisition of the produce is not considered to be like the acquisition of the item itself, and therefore the husband is not the owner of the property.

Abaye contributes a further proof from an accepted practical ruling: *naktinan*, we have received as settled law, that a husband who wishes to litigate on behalf of his wife to protect her ownership of a field requires a *harsha'ah* — a formal power of attorney — to represent her interests in court. In a *nichsei milog* marriage, the wife holds title to the land (*kinyan haguf*) while the husband holds the right to the produce (*kinyan peirot*). If *kinyan peirot* were equivalent to *kinyan haguf*, the husband would himself be considered a full owner and would have standing to litigate without any special authorization. The requirement of *harsha'ah* therefore demonstrates *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*.

### Fruit Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא נָחֵית אַפֵּירֵי, אֲבָל נָחֵית אַפֵּירֵי, מִיגּוֹ דְּמִשְׁתַּעֵי דִּינָא אַפֵּירֵי – מִשְׁתַּעֵי דִּינָא אַגּוּפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And we said** this *halakha* **only when** the other litigant **did not go down** to court with a claim **concerning the produce,** and the case addresses only the ownership of the land itself. **But** if **he went down** to court with a claim **concerning the produce,** with regard to which the husband is definitely the interested party, then **since** the husband **speaks** in front of the court **about the law concerning the produce, he** also **speaks** in front of the court **about the law concerning the** land **itself,** and therefore he does not require authorization from his wife.

Abaye adds a qualification: this applies *delo nachit a-peirei* — only where the litigation concerns title to the land alone. But if the dispute also implicates the fruit-rights — *nachit a-peirei* — then *migo de-mishtei dina a-peirei mishtei dina a-gufa*: since the husband can litigate in his own right to defend his fruit interests, he simultaneously acquires standing to defend his wife's ownership interests as well. This is a limited exception to the general principle, and the underlying rule remains that *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*.

## Tikkun Olam

### Quality Grades

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנִּיזָּקִין שָׁמִין לָהֶן בְּעִידִּית, וּבַעַל חוֹב בְּבֵינוֹנִית, וּכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיבּוּרִית. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה בְּבֵינוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The court **appraises land of superior quality [*iddit*] for payment to injured** parties. **And a creditor** collects his debt **from** the debtor's **intermediate-quality land. And** payment of **a woman's marriage contract** is collected **from** her husband's **inferior-quality land. Rabbi Meir says:** Payment of a woman's **marriage contract** is **also** collected **from intermediate-quality land.**

The *perek* of *ha-shole'ach* is now complete, and the Gemara moves into the following *perek*, which continues with matters of *tikkun olam*. The Mishnah opens: *ha-nizakin shamin lahen be-iddit* — when assessing damages in a tort case, the injured party collects from the highest-quality property of the one who caused the damage. The halakha recognizes three grades of land quality: *iddit* (grade A, finest), *beinonis* (grade B, intermediate), and *ziboris* (grade C, lowest). As Rashi notes, people would far rather receive an acre of prime land than many acres of inferior land; high-quality real estate is far more liquid and valuable per unit, making it significantly more desirable as a form of compensation.

### Collection Order

The Mishnah's hierarchy: the tort victim (*nizak*) collects from *iddit*, the finest land of the tortfeasor. A creditor collecting on a loan collects from *beinonis*, intermediate quality — and this applies equally when collecting from property that has been sold to third parties. The wife collecting her *ketubah* collects from *ziboris*, the lowest grade. Rabbi Meir holds that even the *ketubah* is collected from *beinonis*.

### Encumbered Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ נְכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵן זִיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** Payment of a debt or other obligation **is not collected from liened property** that has been sold to a third party **when** the debtor still **has unsold property, even** when this unsold property is **inferior-quality land.** The creditor cannot collect his debt from liened property that the debtor has sold to another person as long as the debtor is still in possession of other property, even if the remaining assets are inferior to those to which the creditor would otherwise have been entitled.

Another rule: one may not collect from encumbered property — property over which one holds liens because it was sold by the debtor — when unencumbered property remains in the debtor's own possession, even if that unencumbered property is of inferior quality. So if the debtor retains only *ziboris* land in his possession but has sold *beinonis* land to third parties, the creditor cannot invoke his liens against those third-party purchasers. He must accept the *ziboris*. This protects innocent purchasers who acquired property from the debtor before the debt was called in.

### Heirs Collect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִין אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** If one who owed money died and his children inherited his property, the father's debt **can be collected from the property of the orphans only from inferior-quality land.**

Similarly, when one collects from the estate of a deceased debtor — from the heirs — one may only collect *ziboris*, the lowest grade. Though ordinarily a creditor collects *beinonis*, the heirs stand in a position analogous to third-party purchasers who have come into ownership of the property, and the protection of *tikkun olam* requires that collection against them be limited to the lowest grade.

## Indeterminate Amounts

### Improvements Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹצִיאִין לַאֲכִילַת פֵּירוֹת, וְלִשְׁבַח קַרְקָעוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** The court **does not appropriate** liened property that has been sold to a third party **for the consumption of produce or for the enhanced value of land.** If one appropriated a field and sold it, and the buyer worked the land, enhanced it, and grew produce on it, and then the initial owner from whom the field had been stolen took back the land and the produce from the buyer, compensating him only for his expenses, then the buyer may go back to the seller, i.e., the robber, and collect his losses. He can collect the purchase price of the field even from property that the robber sold to another person. By contrast, the value of the produce and the enhancement in the value of the field, which resulted from his actions, may be collected only from the robber's unsold property.

The Mishnah introduces a further category of *tikkun olam*: one may not collect *achilas peirot* or *shevach karka'os* from encumbered property that has been sold. *Achilas peirot* and *shevach karka'os* refer to the improvement value of land — the enhancement in value produced by labor and investment. The scenario: one purchases land with a guarantee (*achrayus*). Another party then proves that the seller had no valid title, and seizes the land along with all improvements. The buyer is compensated for his labor by the one who seized the land — retroactively treated as a hired laborer — but not fully. He then turns to the seller to invoke the insurance (*achrayus*).

For the base purchase price of the land, the buyer may invoke his liens against property the seller sold to third parties, if the seller has no unencumbered assets. But for the value of *achilas peirot* and *shevach karka'os* — the improvements and produce — he may collect only directly from the seller, not from third parties who purchased the seller's other lands. The reason: purchasers of the seller's lands cannot protect themselves against this exposure, because the amount of future improvements on the contested property is inherently indeterminate at the time of their purchase. It would be unjust to subject them to liens of unknown and unpredictable magnitude.

### Support Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִמְזוֹן הָאִשָּׁה וְהַבָּנוֹת – מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִין, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, payment **for the sustenance of** a man's **wife and daughters** cannot be collected **from** his **liened property.** One of the stipulations included in a marriage contract is that after the husband dies, his widow and daughters are entitled to sustenance from his estate. This sustenance cannot be collected from the husband's liened property that has been sold to another person, but only from his unsold property inherited by his heirs. All of these enactments were made **for the betterment of the world.**

The same principle applies to a widow's and daughters' right of support from the estate. When a husband dies, his widow and daughters are entitled to be sustained from the estate. However, they may not collect this support from property the husband sold during his lifetime — only from unencumbered property remaining in the estate. Again, the reason is *tikkun olam*: the total amount of support over time is indeterminate, and purchasers of the husband's property could not have calculated and protected themselves against an open-ended lien of this nature.

## Found Property

### Finder Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמּוֹצֵא מְצִיאָה – לֹא יִשָּׁבַע, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it was further instituted that **one who finds a lost item** and returns it to its rightful owner is **not** required to **take an oath** that he did not keep any part of the lost item for himself. This ordinance was also instituted **for the betterment of the world.**

The Mishnah adds a qualitatively different case of *tikkun olam*: one who finds a lost object and returns it to its owner is exempt from taking an oath if the owner disputes how much was in the wallet or what the object contained. Ordinarily, a litigant in this type of dispute would be required to swear. But if finders were obligated to take oaths, people would simply decline to return lost objects rather than expose themselves to the burden and risk of oath-taking. The exemption is therefore a *tikkun olam* — an enactment for the welfare of society that encourages the return of lost property.

## Tannaim Sources

### Torah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם?! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: When the mishna says: For the betterment of the world, it seems to be referring to all of the cases in the mishna. The Gemara therefore asks: The first clause of the mishna states that compensation for damage is collected from superior-quality land. Is this ordinance also only **for the betterment of the world?** This **is by Torah law, as it is written** with regard to one who damages another person's property: **"Of the best of his own field, and of the best of his own vineyard, shall he pay"** (Exodus 22:4).

The Gemara now investigates the Torah source for the rule that tort victims collect from the finest land of the tortfeasor. The verse: *meitav sadehu u-meitav karmo yeshallem* — he shall pay from the best of his field and the best of his vineyard. The context is the case of an animal that goes out and grazes in another's field (*shen va-regel*), but the principle is extended to all cases of damage. This, says the Gemara, is a biblical obligation. Why then does the Mishnah frame it as *tikkun olam*?

### Rabbi Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּאָמַר מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בִּדְנִיזָּק שָׁיְימִינַן; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם שָׁיְימִינַן בִּדְמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This statement **is necessary only according to Rabbi Yishmael,** who **said** that **by Torah law we appraise** the property **of the injured** party, i.e., the injured party can collect payment only from property equal in quality to the best of his own, even if the one who caused the damage owns property of higher quality. Therefore, the *tanna* of the mishna **teaches us** that **for the betterment of the world we appraise** the property **of the one who caused the damage.**

Abaye clarifies: the framing as *tikkun olam* is necessary only according to Rabbi Yishmael. The *beraisa* records: *meitav sadehu shel nizak u-meitav karmo shel nizak divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — the verse refers to the finest field of the *nizak*, the injured party, not of the *mazik*, the tortfeasor. On this reading, the measure is not the objective quality of the tortfeasor's land but rather what counts as finest quality for the damagee. If the damagee's own best land rates, say, 80 on a scale of 100, and the tortfeasor's land ranges from 90 to 100, the tortfeasor need not give his 90-to-100 quality; he can satisfy his obligation with his 80-quality land, since that is what constitutes *meitav* from the damagee's subjective vantage point.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He reads the verse as establishing the right of the injured party to collect from the finest property of the tortfeasor regardless of the relative quality of the damagee's own land, *lo ba ha-katuv ela ligvot li-nezikin min ha-iddit*. And from this principle of tort law he derives by *kal va-chomer* that the same applies — and even more so — with respect to *hekdesh*.

### Abaye Doubtful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? דְּתַנְיָא: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לַגְבּוֹת לַנִּיזָּקִין מִן הָעִידִּית, וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is this statement of **Rabbi Yishmael** alluded to by Abaye? It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"Of the best of his own field, and of the best of his own vineyard, shall he pay,"** teaches that the appraisal is of **the best quality of the field of the injured** party **and** of **the best quality of the vineyard of the injured** party. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: The verse comes only to** allow **injured** parties **to collect** compensation **from superior-quality land** belonging to the one who caused the damage, in the event that he has no money or movable property. **And** by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference one can derive that the **Temple** treasury collects from superior-quality land.

The *baraita* initially presents Rabbi Yishmael's position in a way that seems to mean something different: the tortfeasor must pay as if his animal ate from the finest of the damagee's fields, even if it actually ate from inferior land. Taken literally, this would mean: if the animal ate from grade C land, the tortfeasor pays as though it ate from grade A. Abaye proposes that the case is one of uncertainty — the animal ate a row of crops and it is not known whether it consumed from the prize grapes worth ten thousand or the ordinary grapes worth one thousand — and in that case of doubt, one pays based on the finest.

### Rava Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָכַל שְׁמֵנָה – מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁמֵנָה; אָכַל כְּחוּשָׁה – מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁמֵנָה?! אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכְלָה עֲרוּגָה בֵּין הָעֲרוּגוֹת, וְלָא יָדְעִינַן אִי כְּחוּשָׁה אָכַל אִי שְׁמֵנָה אָכַל, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם לֵיהּ מִמֵּיטַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** if one's animal **ate** from **a rich** garden bed, it is understandable that he **must pay** the injured party the value of **a rich** garden bed. But if it **ate** from **a poor** garden bed, is it reasonable that he **pays** the value of **a rich** garden bed? **Rav Idi bar Avin said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the animal that caused the damage **ate** from one **garden bed among** other **garden beds, and we do not know whether it ate** from **a poor** one **or it ate** from **a rich** one. In such a case, the animal's owner **pays** the injured party **the** value of the latter's **best quality** property.

Rava rejects Abaye's reading. If we knew the animal ate from the lesser quality, it would pay for the lesser quality. How then could the Torah's verse cause us to pay the higher amount merely because we are uncertain? This would violate the fundamental principle of *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah* — the burden of proof lies with the claimant. In a case of doubt, one cannot extract a larger sum than what can be affirmatively proven.

### Collection Resolution

The Gemara thus arrives at what will be the final resolution, to be developed further: Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva agree that the amount owed is determined by the actual damage caused — not inflated by doubt or by hypothetical enhancement. Their dispute is about *which property* of the tortfeasor the damagee is entitled to collect from. Rabbi Yishmael holds that the biblical measure entitles the damagee to collect from tortfeasor's land that is comparable in quality to the damagee's own finest land — even if that is not the absolute finest land the tortfeasor owns. Rabbi Akiva holds the damagee is entitled to collect from the genuinely finest land of the tortfeasor. The Rabbis' *tikkun olam* — framed as a rabbinic enactment — is in fact to adopt Rabbi Akiva's standard and allow collection from the actual *iddit* of the tortfeasor, providing a stronger deterrent against negligence.
