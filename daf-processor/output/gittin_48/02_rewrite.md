# Gittin 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyan Peirot Debate

### Kinyan Peirot Intro

The daf begins nine lines from the bottom of 47b, at the word *eitvei*. We are in the midst of an ongoing debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, which grew out of the Mishnah's discussion of *bikkurim* — specifically, what happens to the status of fruit for purposes of *bikkurim* when a field is sold to a non-Jew. From there the Gemara moved to a broader dispute: if someone sells a field *le-peirot* — meaning the purchaser acquires not actual title to the land but only the right to its produce — does that fruit-ownership entitle him to be *mevi ve-korei*, to bring *bikkurim* and recite the declaration *ha-adamah asher natata li*?

Rabbi Yochanan holds that it does. Since the fruit belongs to the purchaser, he has sufficient ownership and title to the land to declare *ha-adamah asher natata li*. Reish Lakish disagrees: although such a person brings the *bikkurim* as his own fruit, he cannot recite the declaration, for *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* — the ownership of land for the sake of its fruit is not equivalent to actual ownership of the land itself.

### Nichsei Milog Case

The Gemara now brings a *beraisa* as a potential proof: a man is traveling and carries with him fruit from his wife's property. This is a case of *nichsei milog* — the wife holds title to the land while the husband holds the right to its produce. The husband thus has exactly what we have been discussing: *kinyan peirot* without *kinyan haguf*. While he is on the road, he hears that his wife has died. Since he inherits from her, he now acquires full title to the land. The ruling is that he may bring the fruit and recite the declaration.

The Gemara draws the obvious inference: *meisah in, lo meisah lo* — the fact that he recites only because his wife died implies that had she not died, he could not have recited it. This supports Reish Lakish: *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and one who holds the field only for its fruit is *mevi* but *eino korei*.

### Baraisa Proof

Rabbi Yochanan rejects this inference. He maintains that even had his wife not died, he could have recited *ha-adamah asher natata li*. The reason the *beraisa* presents the case where she dies, he explains, is to teach a novel ruling even in that scenario — namely, that even after the wife's death, when a status change has occurred, he may still recite the declaration.

One might wonder: what is the significance of a status change here, if according to Rabbi Yochanan his relationship to the fruit was unaffected? The answer is that the status change, however subtle, might have been thought to disqualify the recitation because it resembles a case discussed by Rabbi Yose ben Chanina.

### Shaliach Status

Rabbi Yose ben Chanina taught: if the owner harvests the fruit and sends them via a *shaliach*, and the *shaliach* dies along the way, and the owner then retrieves the fruit and completes the delivery to Jerusalem — the owner brings the fruit but does not recite the declaration. The reason: *ve-lakachta ve-heiveta* — the Torah requires that the taking and the bringing be performed by one person. When the *shaliach* was involved, there were two statuses: the owner performed part of the process, the *shaliach* performed part. That interruption, or that divided agency, is enough to preclude the *keriah*.

The cases are not entirely parallel. In the *shaliach* case there is a genuinely meaningful change in status, since a *shaliach* cannot recite the declaration in any event — the fruit is not ultimately the *shaliach*'s property. In our case, by contrast, according to Rabbi Yochanan the husband could have recited from the outset. Nevertheless, the Gemara explains that Rabbi Yochanan reads the *beraisa* as teaching that even the relatively minor status change caused by the wife's death does not disqualify the recitation — *kamashma lan*. As a straightforward reading of the *beraisa*, this is certainly not compelling evidence; it simply shows how Rabbi Yochanan would respond to it.

## Yovel Extension

### Both Cases Needed

The Gemara notes that this dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish *azlu le-ta'amaihu* — follows their respective positions in a parallel debate stated elsewhere. There it was taught: one who sells a field during the period when *yovel* is in effect — and during *yovel* any sold field ultimately reverts to its original owner — does he have sufficient ownership to be *mevi ve-korei*? Rabbi Yochanan says yes, *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami*. Reish Lakish says no, *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and therefore one is *mevi* but *eino korei*.

It is worth pausing to note that in the *yovel* case the argument for full ownership is considerably stronger than in the *peirot* case. When a field is sold during *yovel*, the purchaser intends to acquire the land outright; it is only an external legal mechanism — the institution of *yovel* — that causes eventual reversion. The purchaser has made a genuine land purchase, not merely a contract for fruit rights. One would therefore expect it to be easier to say *ha-adamah asher natata li* here.

The Gemara accordingly states that both cases are necessary, *tzricha*. Had the Gemara only stated the *peirot* case, one might have thought Reish Lakish's ruling is limited to that scenario — where from the very outset, *dachi nachit ada'ata de-peirei nachit*, the purchaser's entire relationship to the land was conceived as fruit-rights only. But in the *yovel* case, *da-ada'ata de-gufa nachit* — the purchaser intended to acquire the land itself, and one might have thought even Reish Lakish would agree with Rabbi Yochanan.

### Peirot Intent

Conversely, had only the *yovel* case been stated, one might have inferred that Rabbi Yochanan permits the recitation there precisely because the purchaser conceived of himself as acquiring the land. But in the *peirot* case, where from the start the acquisition was framed as fruit-rights only, one might have thought even Rabbi Yochanan concedes to Reish Lakish. Hence both cases must be taught.

### Yovel Intent

The full scope of the dispute thus emerges: Rabbi Yochanan is willing to call even a purchaser of fruit-rights an owner sufficient to recite *ha-adamah asher natata li*, while Reish Lakish maintains a standard so demanding that even a purchaser who intended to acquire the land itself, yet whose ownership is temporally bounded by *yovel*, cannot be regarded as a full owner for purposes of *keriah*.

### Tree Proofs

The Gemara attempts to bring proofs. *Ta shema* — one who purchases a tree along with its underlying ground may bring *bikkurim* and recite the declaration. This would seem to show that even a purchaser, when *yovel* is practiced, may be *mevi ve-korei*. The Gemara deflects: *hacha be-mai askinan bi-zman she-ein ha-yovel noheg* — the case refers to a time when *yovel* is no longer in effect, which was true toward the end of the First Temple period when the twelve tribes were no longer all settled on their respective portions of the land.

A further attempt: one who purchases two trees within another's field is *mevi* but *eino korei*, since two trees do not convey rights to the land beneath them; but if he purchases three trees — which, as established elsewhere, carries a presumption that the land between them is included — he is *mevi ve-korei*. Again, the Gemara answers: *hacha nami bi-zman she-ein yovel noheg*. Any Mishnaic case in which a purchaser is *mevi ve-korei* can simply be referring to a time when *yovel* does not apply. It is therefore difficult to bring a counterproof from such sources.

## Yovel Rishon

### Pre-Yovel Agreement

Against this backdrop, Rav Chisda offers a significant teaching: the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish applies only from *Yovel sheni* — the second *yovel* cycle and beyond. But regarding *Yovel rishon* — the first *yovel* cycle ever practiced in the Land of Israel — *divrei ha-kol meivi ve-korei*. Everyone agrees that a purchaser during the first *yovel* cycle may bring and recite.

### Perception Principle

Why? Because *d-achti lo samcha da'ataihu* — people's minds had not yet settled into confidence that the reversion would actually take place. The institution of *yovel* had been proclaimed in the Torah, but it had never been practiced. Neither the buyer nor the seller truly felt certain it would be enforced. In their minds, the sale might well turn out to be permanent. And since their psychological relationship to the land was that of full and final ownership, that perception was sufficient to grant the purchaser the status of *kinyan haguf*.

This is a striking principle. Legally, on paper, the sale was absolutely time-bound and the reversion fully operative. What makes the ownership incomplete in Reish Lakish's view is not a formal legal deficiency but the lived awareness that the land will not remain permanently in the purchaser's hands. And when that awareness is absent — because the institution of *yovel* had never yet been tested — the halakhic assessment shifts accordingly. Perception reshapes the legal relationship to the land.

### Yovel Divide

This teaching also resolves the evidentiary problem. Any source that appears to show *meivi ve-korei* during *yovel* can be explained as referring to *Yovel rishon*, where everyone agrees. Any statement that one is *mevi* but *eino korei* applies from *Yovel sheni* onward, and that is the context of Reish Lakish's ruling. *Lo kashya: ha bi-Yovel rishon, ha bi-Yovel sheni.*

## Tannaim Parallel

### Hekdesh Case

The Gemara now asks whether this debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish parallels a *tanna'im* dispute. Before examining the parallel, it is worth noting that Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish originally spoke only about the narrow question of *mevi ve-korei* in *bikkurim*. It is the Gemara that has generalized their positions into the broader principle of *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf* or *lav ke-kinyan haguf*, a principle that could have implications throughout *halakha*. Accordingly the Gemara now tries to connect this to other debates.

The *beraisa* in question asks: how do we know that if someone purchases a field from his father, sanctifies it, and then his father dies — all before *yovel* arrives — the field is treated as a *sdei achuzah*, an ancestral field? The mechanics here are important. When a person sanctifies a *sdei miknah*, a purchased field, to the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, that field reverts at *yovel* to its original owner, because the sanctifier could not dedicate more than he owned. But when a person sanctifies a *sdei achuzah*, his ancestral land, the Torah provides that at *yovel* the field goes to the *kohanim* and does not revert to the original owner — the *Beit ha-Mikdash* retains it permanently.

In our case: the son purchased the field from his father during *yovel*. As a purchased field, it would have reverted to the father had *yovel* come. But the son sanctified it, and before *yovel* arrived, the father died. The son now inherits the land outright. When *yovel* comes, does it revert — returning to the "father," who is now dead, and passing from there to the son as heir? Or does it stay with the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, since by now the son has full title to it? The *beraisa* rules it is treated as a *sdei achuzah* and stays with the *Beit ha-Mikdash*.

The derivation comes from the verse: *im sdei miknaso asher lo mi-sdei achuzaso* — if he sanctifies a field he purchased, which was not a field of his inheritance. The phrase *asher lo mi-sdei achuzaso* — that it was not from his ancestral holdings — is seen as excluding from the reversion category any field that was either actually ancestral land or had the potential and actualized fitness to become ancestral land.

### Timing Issue

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon interpret the verse to mean: the field reverts only if it was a purchased field lacking even the potential to become ancestral. If it had the potential to become ancestral — as in our case, where the son's eventual inheritance of the field was a genuine possibility — and that potential was actualized by the father's death, then even if it was sanctified before the father died, it is treated as a *sdei achuzah*.

This raises a structural question: according to Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, why did they need the verse to tell them about the case where the son sanctified first and the father died afterward? Why not begin with the simpler teaching that if the father dies *before* the son sanctifies, the sanctification is certainly of a *sdei achuzah*? The fact that they go directly to the more complex case — sanctification first, then the father's death — suggests they regarded the simpler case as obvious and requiring no verse.

### Rabbi Meir View

Rabbi Meir, by contrast, asks a different question: how do we know that if someone purchases a field from his father, the father then dies, and only afterward does the son sanctify it — that this constitutes a *sdei achuzah*? The verse teaches this explicitly.

But why would Rabbi Meir need a verse for this apparently obvious case? If the father died before the sanctification, the son clearly inherits the land fully; how could one think it is still a *sdei miknah*? The Gemara explains: Rabbi Meir holds *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami*. From the moment the son purchased the field from the father during *yovel*, he was already considered the full owner. When the father subsequently died, there was, in a sense, nothing new for the son to inherit with respect to this land — *hu de-lo yaris ve-lo midi*, it is as if he inherited nothing additional. Since he was already treated as the full owner, the father's death does not produce a change in his legal status. One might therefore have thought that the *sdei miknah* status established at the time of purchase persists and is not superseded by the father's death. The verse is therefore needed to tell us that the *sdei achuzah* status does prevail — that the fact of actual inheritance supersedes the earlier purchase-status, and therefore the sanctification thereafter is of a *sdei achuzah*.

This requirement for a verse demonstrates *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami* — Rabbi Meir's position aligns with Rabbi Yochanan.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon hold *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*. Therefore, when the son purchased from the father during *yovel*, he was not considered the full owner — only the owner of fruit-rights. When the father died, the son then truly inherited and became the full owner. In that sequence — purchase, then inheritance before sanctification — it is entirely obvious that the sanctification is of a *sdei achuzah*, and no verse is needed. The verse can therefore be deployed for the more novel case: that even if the son sanctified it while it was still only a *sdei miknah*, if the father subsequently dies and the potential for ancestral status is actualized, it is still treated as a *sdei achuzah*.

This positions Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon as paralleling Reish Lakish.

### Alt. Reading

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes an alternative reading. He maintains that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon actually hold *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami* — they agree with Rabbi Meir on that underlying principle, and thus align with Rabbi Yochanan. According to this reading, the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon is not about *kinyan peirot* at all.

### Pasuk Language

How then does Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explain why Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon could skip directly to the more complex case? They found a doubly redundant formulation in the verse. The Torah could have simply said *mi-sdei miknaso asher lo achuzaso*. Instead it says *asher lo mi-sdei achuzaso* — the added prefix *mi-* makes the phrase *mi-sdei achuzaso* even more expansive, as if to say not even *derived from* the category of ancestral land. This double extra language allows Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon to perform two derivations simultaneously — one for the case where the son inherits before sanctifying (which, according to this reading, also requires a verse even for them, because of the *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf* principle), and one for the case where the potential was present and actualized after the fact.

## Rabbi Yosef

### Brothers Division

Rabbi Yosef offers a striking observation: were it not for Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami*, one could not even get started in learning, *lo matza yadav ve-raglav be-veit ha-medrash*. The reason lies in a further ruling of Rabbi Yochanan, cited by Rabbi Assi: brothers who divide an inherited estate are considered as purchasers of one another's portions — *lakuchos hein u-machzirin zeh la-zeh ba-yovel*. At *yovel*, they must return the property and renegotiate the division.

The conceptual basis of this ruling is that when brothers inherit jointly, each acquires an undivided half-interest in every property. When they subsequently divide — one taking the northern field, one the southern — this is legally conceived as a swap: each transfers his half-interest in one field in exchange for the other's half-interest in another. They have, in effect, purchased from one another.

This produces an almost startling consequence: come *yovel*, everything reverts. Not only formal commercial sales but every inheritance that has ever been divided among siblings would need to be reconsolidated and re-divided. One wonders how society could function under such a system, but that is the logical consequence of the ruling.

### Bikkurim Problem

Now the problem for *bikkurim* becomes apparent. If *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and if brothers are *lakuchos* — considered purchasers of one another during the *yovel* period — then virtually no landowner could ever declare *ha-adamah asher natata li*, since virtually every plot of land has passed through at least one inheritance division somewhere in the family line. *Lo meshkachat de-maisi bikkurim ela chad bar chad ad Yehoshua bin Nun* — one would never find anyone who could bring *bikkurim* with the declaration unless he could demonstrate a completely uninterrupted father-to-single-son chain of descent going all the way back to Joshua bin Nun. The absurdity of this conclusion demonstrates why Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is indispensable.

### Tosafos Fix

Tosafos grapples with this, since the bottom-line *psak* is not clearly settled. If we rule like Reish Lakish — *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* — and we also rule *ein breira* (which generally prevents retroactive clarification of ownership), we seem to be back to the same problem. Tosafos resolves this by distinguishing between the technical concept of *kinyan peirot* in the context of commerce and the practical realities of inheritance. Even if one holds *ein breira* in general, that principle does not extend so far as to treat the division of an inheritance as a purchase that triggers the *yovel* reversion. The Torah's reversion mechanism applies to genuine commercial sales; the division of an estate, even if theoretically conceptualized as a swap of partial interests, would not fall within the category of sale subject to reversion. This allows one to hold *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami* without generating the absurd consequence that no one could ever bring *bikkurim*.

## Rava Proofs

### Yovel Pasuk

Rava brings independent support for Reish Lakish from both a verse and a *beraisa*. The verse: *be-mispar shnei tevuot yimkor lach* — in the sale of a field during *yovel*, the price is calculated based on the number of years of produce remaining until *yovel*. More pointedly, the verse concludes *ki mispar tevuot hu mocher lach* — for it is years of produce that he is selling you. The Torah itself frames the transaction as a sale of produce-years rather than a sale of land, which supports Reish Lakish's view that the purchaser's relationship to the land is that of a *kinyan peirot*, not a *kinyan haguf*.

### Bechor Proof

The *beraisa* proof: a *bechor*, a firstborn son, receives a double portion of the estate. The rule is that he receives a double portion of assets that were under the father's control at the time of death, but not of assets that had passed out of the father's hands. The *beraisa* teaches that a *bechor* receives a double portion of a field that had been sold by the father and that will revert to the father's estate at *yovel*.

### Father Ownership

This is a direct proof for Reish Lakish. The fact that the field counts as part of the father's estate for purposes of the *bechor*'s double portion demonstrates that even after the sale, the father retains a meaningful ownership interest in the land. The purchaser has only *kinyan peirot* — rights to the produce for the interim period — while the father retains *kinyan haguf*. Reish Lakish's position is thereby supported: the two categories are legally distinct.

## Abaye Proof

### Harsha'ah Need

Abaye contributes a further proof from an accepted practical ruling: *naktinan*, we have received as settled law, that a husband who wishes to litigate on behalf of his wife to protect her ownership of a field requires a *harsha'ah* — a formal power of attorney — to represent her interests in court. In a *nichsei milog* marriage, the wife holds title to the land (*kinyan haguf*) while the husband holds the right to the produce (*kinyan peirot*). If *kinyan peirot* were equivalent to *kinyan haguf*, the husband would himself be considered a full owner and would have standing to litigate without any special authorization. The requirement of *harsha'ah* therefore demonstrates *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*.

### Fruit Exception

Abaye adds a qualification: this applies *delo nachit a-peirei* — only where the litigation concerns title to the land alone. But if the dispute also implicates the fruit-rights — *nachit a-peirei* — then *migo de-mishtei dina a-peirei mishtei dina a-gufa*: since the husband can litigate in his own right to defend his fruit interests, he simultaneously acquires standing to defend his wife's ownership interests as well. This is a limited exception to the general principle, and the underlying rule remains that *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*.

## Tikkun Olam

### Quality Grades

The *perek* of *ha-shole'ach* is now complete, and the Gemara moves into the following *perek*, which continues with matters of *tikkun olam*. The Mishnah opens: *ha-nizakin shamin lahen be-iddit* — when assessing damages in a tort case, the injured party collects from the highest-quality property of the one who caused the damage. The halakha recognizes three grades of land quality: *iddit* (grade A, finest), *beinonis* (grade B, intermediate), and *ziboris* (grade C, lowest). As Rashi notes, people would far rather receive an acre of prime land than many acres of inferior land; high-quality real estate is far more liquid and valuable per unit, making it significantly more desirable as a form of compensation.

### Collection Order

The Mishnah's hierarchy: the tort victim (*nizak*) collects from *iddit*, the finest land of the tortfeasor. A creditor collecting on a loan collects from *beinonis*, intermediate quality — and this applies equally when collecting from property that has been sold to third parties. The wife collecting her *ketubah* collects from *ziboris*, the lowest grade. Rabbi Meir holds that even the *ketubah* is collected from *beinonis*.

### Encumbered Property

Another rule: one may not collect from encumbered property — property over which one holds liens because it was sold by the debtor — when unencumbered property remains in the debtor's own possession, even if that unencumbered property is of inferior quality. So if the debtor retains only *ziboris* land in his possession but has sold *beinonis* land to third parties, the creditor cannot invoke his liens against those third-party purchasers. He must accept the *ziboris*. This protects innocent purchasers who acquired property from the debtor before the debt was called in.

### Heirs Collect

Similarly, when one collects from the estate of a deceased debtor — from the heirs — one may only collect *ziboris*, the lowest grade. Though ordinarily a creditor collects *beinonis*, the heirs stand in a position analogous to third-party purchasers who have come into ownership of the property, and the protection of *tikkun olam* requires that collection against them be limited to the lowest grade.

## Indeterminate Amounts

### Improvements Limit

The Mishnah introduces a further category of *tikkun olam*: one may not collect *achilas peirot* or *shevach karka'os* from encumbered property that has been sold. *Achilas peirot* and *shevach karka'os* refer to the improvement value of land — the enhancement in value produced by labor and investment. The scenario: one purchases land with a guarantee (*achrayus*). Another party then proves that the seller had no valid title, and seizes the land along with all improvements. The buyer is compensated for his labor by the one who seized the land — retroactively treated as a hired laborer — but not fully. He then turns to the seller to invoke the insurance (*achrayus*).

For the base purchase price of the land, the buyer may invoke his liens against property the seller sold to third parties, if the seller has no unencumbered assets. But for the value of *achilas peirot* and *shevach karka'os* — the improvements and produce — he may collect only directly from the seller, not from third parties who purchased the seller's other lands. The reason: purchasers of the seller's lands cannot protect themselves against this exposure, because the amount of future improvements on the contested property is inherently indeterminate at the time of their purchase. It would be unjust to subject them to liens of unknown and unpredictable magnitude.

### Support Limit

The same principle applies to a widow's and daughters' right of support from the estate. When a husband dies, his widow and daughters are entitled to be sustained from the estate. However, they may not collect this support from property the husband sold during his lifetime — only from unencumbered property remaining in the estate. Again, the reason is *tikkun olam*: the total amount of support over time is indeterminate, and purchasers of the husband's property could not have calculated and protected themselves against an open-ended lien of this nature.

## Found Property

### Finder Exemption

The Mishnah adds a qualitatively different case of *tikkun olam*: one who finds a lost object and returns it to its owner is exempt from taking an oath if the owner disputes how much was in the wallet or what the object contained. Ordinarily, a litigant in this type of dispute would be required to swear. But if finders were obligated to take oaths, people would simply decline to return lost objects rather than expose themselves to the burden and risk of oath-taking. The exemption is therefore a *tikkun olam* — an enactment for the welfare of society that encourages the return of lost property.

## Tannaim Sources

### Torah Source

The Gemara now investigates the Torah source for the rule that tort victims collect from the finest land of the tortfeasor. The verse: *meitav sadehu u-meitav karmo yeshaleim* — he shall pay from the best of his field and the best of his vineyard. The context is the case of an animal that goes out and grazes in another's field (*shen va-regel*), but the principle is extended to all cases of damage. This, says the Gemara, is a biblical obligation. Why then does the Mishnah frame it as *tikkun olam*?

### Rabbi Yishmael

Abaye clarifies: the framing as *tikkun olam* is necessary only according to Rabbi Yishmael. The *beraisa* records: *meitav sadehu shel nizak u-meitav karmo shel nizak divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — the verse refers to the finest field of the *nizak*, the injured party, not of the *mazik*, the tortfeasor. On this reading, the measure is not the objective quality of the tortfeasor's land but rather what counts as finest quality for the damagee. If the damagee's own best land rates, say, 80 on a scale of 100, and the tortfeasor's land ranges from 90 to 100, the tortfeasor need not give his 90-to-100 quality; he can satisfy his obligation with his 80-quality land, since that is what constitutes *meitav* from the damagee's subjective vantage point.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He reads the verse as establishing the right of the injured party to collect from the finest property of the tortfeasor regardless of the relative quality of the damagee's own land, *lo ba ha-katuv ela ligvot li-nezikin min ha-iddit*. And from this principle of tort law he derives by *kal va-chomer* that the same applies — and even more so — with respect to *hekdesh*.

### Abaye Doubtful

The *beraisa* initially presents Rabbi Yishmael's position in a way that seems to mean something different: the tortfeasor must pay as if his animal ate from the finest of the damagee's fields, even if it actually ate from inferior land. Taken literally, this would mean: if the animal ate from grade C land, the tortfeasor pays as though it ate from grade A. Abaye proposes that the case is one of uncertainty — the animal ate a row of crops and it is not known whether it consumed from the prize grapes worth ten thousand or the ordinary grapes worth one thousand — and in that case of doubt, one pays based on the finest.

### Rava Rejects

Rava rejects Abaye's reading. If we knew the animal ate from the lesser quality, it would pay for the lesser quality. How then could the Torah's verse cause us to pay the higher amount merely because we are uncertain? This would violate the fundamental principle of *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah* — the burden of proof lies with the claimant. In a case of doubt, one cannot extract a larger sum than what can be affirmatively proven.

### Collection Resolution

The Gemara thus arrives at what will be the final resolution, to be developed further: Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva agree that the amount owed is determined by the actual damage caused — not inflated by doubt or by hypothetical enhancement. Their dispute is about *which property* of the tortfeasor the damagee is entitled to collect from. Rabbi Yishmael holds that the biblical measure entitles the damagee to collect from tortfeasor's land that is comparable in quality to the damagee's own finest land — even if that is not the absolute finest land the tortfeasor owns. Rabbi Akiva holds the damagee is entitled to collect from the genuinely finest land of the tortfeasor. The Rabbis' *tikkun olam* — framed as a rabbinic enactment — is in fact to adopt Rabbi Akiva's standard and allow collection from the actual *iddit* of the tortfeasor, providing a stronger deterrent against negligence.
