# Bekhorot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blemish & Non-Altar Sanctity

### Blemish Before Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם מֵתוּ יִפָּדוּ. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת לֹא הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if they died they may be redeemed.** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: This is the statement of Rabbi Shimon, who says:** Items **consecrated to** be sacrificed on the **altar were included in** the requirement of **standing and valuation,** but items with the **sanctity of** those consecrated for **Temple maintenance,** such as the red heifer, **were not included in** the requirement of **standing and valuation.**

We pick up at the bottom of 14b, two lines before the lines widen. The subject is a *korban* that had a *mum* before it was sanctified. Because the blemish preceded the sanctification, the *kedusha* was never oriented toward the altar — never *kedushas mizbe'ach*. It was always destined to be redeemed, with the redemption money then directed toward the altar, but the animal itself was never going to be brought as an offering. We are thus dealing with *kedushas damim* — sanctity of monetary value only — and the *gemara* explores several consequences that flow from this non-altar sanctity.

### Temura from Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא יַחֲלִיפֶנּוּ וְלֹא יָמִיר וְגוֹ׳״. הַשְׁתָּא רַע בְּטוֹב אָמְרַתְּ לֹא, טוֹב בְּרָע מִיבַּעְיָא? אֶלָּא טוֹב מֵעִיקָּרוֹ עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, רַע מֵעִיקָּרוֹ אֵין עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** sacrificial animals whose blemish preceded their consecration **do not render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them sacred. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this ruling? It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "He shall not exchange it, nor substitute it,** a good for a bad, or a bad for a good" (Leviticus 27:10). The Gemara infers: **Now** that **you have said** that substituting **a bad** animal **for a good** animal is **not** permitted, **is it necessary** to state that substituting **a good** animal **for a bad** animal is **not** permitted? **Rather,** the verse is teaching that an animal which is **good at the outset renders** an animal that was **a subsitute** for it sacred, but an animal that is **bad at the outset,** i.e., whose blemish preceded its consecration, **does not render** an animal that was **a subsitute** for it sacred.

The first consequence stated is *ve'eino ose tmura*: one cannot effect an exchange (*tmura*) with such an animal. More precisely, if one attempts to exchange another animal for it, the second animal does not acquire *kedusha*. The laws of *tmura* simply do not apply. *Mai ta'ama* — what is the reason? The verse states *lo yachalifenu velo yamir oso tov b'ra o ra b'tov*: one may not exchange good for bad or bad for good. Now, in the context of *korbanos*, *Chazal* understand "bad" (*ra*) as the standard designation for a blemished animal. The *gemara* reasons: since the verse already tells us *ra b'tov* — you may not even exchange a blemished animal for an unblemished one — it is self-evident that *tov b'ra*, exchanging an unblemished animal for a blemished one, is likewise forbidden. Why then did the Torah state the prohibition of *tov b'ra* at all?

### Good vs. Bad from Start

*Ella* — the answer is that the phrase *tov b'ra* is not merely restating an obvious prohibition. It is defining the conditions under which *tmura* can operate at all. Only *tov me'ikara ose tmura* — only an animal that was originally unblemished, that began with *kedushas mizbe'ach*, can generate an exchange. Even if it subsequently acquired a *mum*, the original altar-sanctity it carried means that *tmura* can apply to it. But *ra me'ikara* — something that began with a blemish, as in our *mishna* — never had *kedushas mizbe'ach* to begin with, and therefore *eino ose tmura*: it cannot effect a *tmura* at all.

To state the reading precisely: even when the verse speaks of *ra b'tov* — a blemished animal potentially making a *tmura* — the qualifier from the opening phrase *tov* still applies. The blemished animal that can participate in *tmura* is only one that started as *tov*. If it started with a *mum* and its *kedusha* was never fit for the *mizbe'ach*, it stands entirely outside the category of *tmura*.

## Redemption & Alive Requirement

### Rabbi Shimon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, אִם מֵתוּ — יִפָּדוּ. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁנִּפְדֶּה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹתָהּ״ ״אוֹתָהּ״ — לְמַעוֹטֵי בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Temura* 32a): **Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to animals **consecrated for the Temple maintenance, if they died they may be redeemed,** because animals consecrated for the Temple maintenance are not included in the obligation of standing and valuation. **And Rabbi Shimon concedes with regard to** an animal that was **blemished from the outset,** i.e., before it was consecrated, **that it may be redeemed** even if it was consecrated for the altar. **What is the reason?** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states:** "Then he shall stand the animal before the priest. And the priest shall appraise **it"** (Leviticus 27:11–12). The emphasis on **"it"** serves **to exclude** an animal that was **blemished from the outset.**

The *mishna* then moves to animals that had a *mum* from the start — *kedushas damim* — and teaches that if they die, they may still be redeemed. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: this position reflects the view of *Rabbi Shimon*, who holds that *kodshei mizbe'ach hayu bichlal ha'amada veha'aracha* — altar-sanctified animals fall within the Torah's requirement of *ha'amada veha'aracha*, the obligation to stand the animal upright before the kohen for appraisal, which necessitates that the animal be alive at the time of redemption. This requirement, however, applies only to *kodshei mizbe'ach*. *Kodshei bedek habayis lo hayu bichlal ha'amada veha'aracha* — animals sanctified for the Temple treasury were never included in that verse's scope, and therefore even if they die, they can be redeemed.

A *mishna* in *Temura* makes this explicit: Rabbi Shimon says that *kodshei bedek habayis im mesu yipadu* — Temple-treasury consecrations may be redeemed even after death. And yet Rabbi Shimon *umodei b'ba'al mum me'ikara shenifda* — he concedes that an animal that began with a blemish and was then sanctified ostensibly for the altar, because it was blemished from the start and therefore never truly fit for the *mizbe'ach*, is treated like a treasury-consecration and may likewise be redeemed even dead. This is the underlying logic of our *mishna*: animals with original *kedushas mizbe'ach* must be alive for redemption; animals whose *kedusha* was only ever of monetary value may be redeemed in death.

The *gemara* asks for the verse that supports this distinction. The answer: the Torah uses the word *osah* — "it" — in the passage *ve'he'emid osah*, "and he shall stand it," to indicate that only a true *kedushas mizbe'ach* animal — one genuinely fit for the altar — is included in the requirement of being alive. An animal that was nominally sanctified for the altar but carried a blemish from the outset, and whose *kedusha* was therefore never really *kedushas mizbe'ach*, is excluded.

### Tana d'Bei Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִם מֵתוּ — יִקָּבֵרוּ. מַאן חֲכָמִים? תָּנָא דְּבֵי לֵוִי הוּא, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי לֵוִי: הַכֹּל הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But the Rabbis say:** With regard to both animals consecrated for the altar and animals consecrated for Temple maintenance, **if they died they must be buried.** The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is that of **the Rabbis** in the mishna? It is the opinion of the ***tanna* of the school of Levi, as the school of Levi taught: All** animals **were included in** the obligation of **standing and valuation, even** an animal that was **blemished from the outset.**

The *gemara* then asks: who are the *chachamim* that disagree with Rabbi Shimon in the *Temura mishna*? The answer is that they are the tradition of *tana d'bei Levi*. The teaching of the House of Levi holds that *hakol hayu bichlal ha'amada veha'aracha* — everything is subject to the requirement of being alive for valuation and redemption, even *v'afilu ba'al mum me'ikara*, even an animal that began with a blemish. The scope of the verse extends not only to altar-sanctified animals but to all consecrated animals, even a *chaya*, even a bird — any creature sanctified even for the treasury must be alive in order to be redeemed. The question *v'haksiv osah* — but doesn't the Torah say "it," implying limitation? — is noted, and the *gemara* leaves it as a difficulty for the *tana d'bei Levi* without resolution.

### Sages' Position Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: רַב כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר לְרַבָּנַן: קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, קׇדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ לֹא הָיוּ בִּכְלַל הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה, וְלָא מִיתּוֹקְמָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְאִם מֵתוּ יִקָּבֵרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in response that **Rav** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, who says: According to the Rabbis,** animals **consecrated for the Temple maintenance were included in** the obligation of **standing and valuation,** whereas animals **consecrated for the altar were not included in** the obligation of **standing and valuation. And** therefore it is **not** possible to **interpret the mishna in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, as the latter clause teaches: And if** animals consecrated for the altar **died, they must be buried** even if their consecration preceded their blemish, as they are included in the obligation of standing and valuation.

The *gemara* then asks about the *rabanan* in that *Temura mishna* who argue against Rabbi Shimon — what exactly is their position? Could they be arguing in the same direction as our *mishna*, namely that blemished-from-the-start animals may be redeemed even dead? If so, our *mishna* would accord with the *rabanan* as well, and the statement "this is the position of Rabbi Shimon" would be incomplete.

*Rav* (as against *Rabbi Yochanan*) maintains that the *rabanan* who argue on Rabbi Shimon take precisely the reverse position: they say *kodshei bedek habayis hayu bichlal ha'amada veha'aracha, kodshei mizbe'ach lo hayu bichlal ha'amada veha'aracha* — treasury-sanctified animals require being alive, while altar-sanctified animals do not. That is the exact inversion of Rabbi Shimon's ruling. This cannot be the position of our *mishna*, which holds that an animal that *did* start with *kedushas mizbe'ach* cannot be redeemed dead — precisely because, as the *seifa* states, *im mesu yikaveru*, if they died they must be buried. Since the *rabanan*'s position would permit redeeming dead *kodshei mizbe'ach*, they are entirely incompatible with our *mishna*'s *seifa*. Therefore our *mishna* must indeed reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: מִמַּאי דְּהָא ״מֵתוּ יִקָּבֵרוּ״ מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵי הַעֲמָדָה וְהַעֲרָכָה הוּא? דִּלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים הוּא! אָמְרִי: אִם כֵּן, נִיתְנֵי: ״אִם נַעֲשׂוּ טְרֵיפָה יִקָּבֵרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **says: From where** is it known **that this** ruling of the mishna: If **they died they must be buried, is due to** the fact **that** they **require standing and valuation? Perhaps it is because one may not redeem sacrificial** animals in order **to feed them to dogs.** The Sages **said** in response: **If** that were **so, let** the mishna **teach: If** one of **them became an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], it must be buried.** Since a *tereifa* can be stood before the priest, the only reason to require its burial must be due to the prohibition against redeeming a sacrificial animal in order to feed it to dogs.

The *gemara* raises the possibility of attributing our *mishna* to the *rabanan* by arguing that the prohibition on redeeming the dead *kodshei mizbe'ach* derives not from the verse requiring *ha'amada veha'aracha* but from a separate concern: *ein podin et hakodshim l'ha'achilan l'klavim* — one may not redeem consecrated animals to feed to dogs. If that is the reason, perhaps the *rabanan* could still maintain their reversed position on *ha'amada veha'aracha* while agreeing with our *mishna* on the practical outcome for *kodshei mizbe'ach* due to this secondary concern. The *gemara* challenges this: if that were the rationale, the *mishna* should have formulated the case using *treifah* — an animal that became a *treifah* — not one that died, since a *treifah* equally cannot be eaten and equally would implicate the prohibition of redeeming for dog food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם רַב כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וּתְנִי: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וּמַחְלוּקְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually, Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who says that according to the Rabbis, animals consecrated for the altar were included in the obligation of standing and appraising, and therefore the mishna can be explained in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And** in fact Rav **taught: This is the statement of Rabbi Shimon and those who disagree with him.**

Ultimately, the *gemara* notes that *rav k'Rebbe Yochanan sevira lei* — one could resolve the matter by saying that Rav follows Rabbi Yochanan and that when we say "this is the view of Rabbi Shimon," we mean Rabbi Shimon *and his disputants* in that *mishna* — on the understanding that the *rabanan* there actually hold the same substantive position as Rabbi Shimon, differing perhaps only in formulation, and therefore our *mishna* does reflect both.

### Two Reasons Alive

This discussion clarifies that there are in fact two distinct reasons why a consecrated animal must be alive in order to be redeemed. The first is the *gezeiras hakasuv* of *ha'amada veha'aracha* — the Torah's requirement that the animal stand before the kohen for appraisal, which by definition requires it to be alive. The scope of this requirement is the subject of the debate: Rabbi Shimon limits it to *kodshei mizbe'ach*; the *tana d'bei Levi* extends it to all consecrated animals.

The second reason is independent of that verse entirely: *ein podin et hakodshim l'ha'achilan l'klavim* — one may not redeem consecrated property if the result will be that it is used as dog food. This concern differs from the first in several important ways. It may be in nature more of a *lechatchila* concern rather than a *bediavad* invalidation. It has a different scope: it applies even to a *treifah* — an animal that is alive and thus satisfies the requirements of *ha'amada veha'aracha*, but which, once slaughtered, cannot be eaten by a human and would wind up as dog food. Such an animal, on the grounds of the second concern, could not be redeemed. The first reason — the *gezeiras hakasuv* — would not apply to it because it is alive; but the second reason would. The practical difference between the two reasons thus emerges clearly: the verse about standing and appraisal speaks to whether the act of redemption is formally valid, while the prohibition against redeeming for dog food speaks to whether redemption is appropriate given the destination of the animal.

The *gemara*'s conclusion is that our *mishna*, which permits redemption of an animal that started with a blemish and forbids it for one that started unblemished, is most cleanly explained by Rabbi Shimon's position. When the *kedusha* was *kedushas damim* from the start — because the blemish preceded the sanctification — there is, according to Rabbi Shimon, neither the *ha'amada veha'aracha* requirement nor the dog-food concern in the same way. When the *kedusha* was *kedushas mizbe'ach*, both concerns apply in their full force.

## Residual Sanctity

### Deer/Hart Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קָדַם הֶקְדֵּשָׁן וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״צְבִי״, מָה צְבִי פָּטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, אַף פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין פְּטוּרִין מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But** if **their consecration preceded** their blemish, they are exempt from the mitzva of the firstborn and from the priestly gifts. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** with regard to a verse discussing disqualified consecrated animals: "However, as you eat the **gazelle** and the hart, so shall you eat thereof" (Deuteronomy 12:22). **Just as a gazelle is exempt** from the mitzva of the **firstborn,** as this obligation does not apply to undomesticated animals, **so too, disqualified consecrated** animals **are exempt** from the mitzva of the **firstborn.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַמַּתָּנוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַיָּל״, מָה אַיָּל פָּטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, אַף פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין פְּטוּרִין מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Based on this derivation, **I will exclude** consecrated animals that developed a blemish from the mitzva of **the firstborn but I will not exclude** them **from** the mitzva of **the gifts.** From where is it derived that such animals are not subject to the mitzva of the gifts either? The Gemara answers that **the verse states:** "However, as you eat the gazelle and the **hart."** This teaches that **just as a hart is exempt** from the mitzva of the **firstborn and from** the mitzva of **the gifts, so too, disqualified consecrated** animals **are exempt** from the mitzva of the **firstborn and from** the mitzva of **the gifts.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה צְבִי וְאַיָּל חֶלְבָּן מוּתָּר, אַף פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין חֶלְבָּן מוּתָּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַךְ״ — חִלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** so, then it follows that **just as the fat of a gazelle and a hart is permitted, so too, the fat of disqualified consecrated** animals should be **permitted.** In fact, the *halakha* is that the fat of disqualified consecrated animals is forbidden, like that of other domesticated animals. The *baraita* answers that **the verse states: "However,** as you eat the gazelle and the hart." The term "however" **differentiates,** i.e., it serves to limit the analogy between these animals to the mitzvot of the firstborn and the gifts, and not forbidden fat.

We now turn to the second category: an animal that was sanctified without a blemish — full *kedushas mizbe'ach* — and subsequently acquired a *mum*. This is *kedushas haguf*, genuine altar-sanctity. Once it is redeemed, it retains *residual kedusha* by virtue of having started as a true *korban*. The question is: what are the practical consequences of that residual sanctity?

A *braisa* is cited. The Torah states that one should eat this animal just as one eats the *tzvi* and the *ayal* — the deer and the hart. *Chazal* understand this verse as addressing *pesulei mukdashin* — animals with full original *kedusha* that were subsequently blemished and redeemed. The comparison to these wild animals yields two immediate *psurim*. First: *ma tzvi patur min habechora* — a deer is not subject to the firstborn (*bechor*) obligation, because *bechor* applies only to cattle, sheep, and goats. So too, *af pesulei mukdashin peturim min habechora*, even a redeemed blemished sacrifice is exempt from the *bechor* obligation. Second: one might have thought that we exclude only the *bechora*, since that has a narrower scope (*she'ein shaveh bakol* — it does not apply to all animals, only firstborn males). But *matanot* — the priestly gifts of the shoulder, cheeks, and stomach — have a broader scope (*shaveh bakol*, applying to all animals regardless of sex or birth order), and one might therefore not have excluded them. The Torah says both *tzvi* and *ayal*, and since we have two words for two distinct *chayos*, we derive from the second that *pesulei mukdashin* are also exempt from *matanot*: *af pesulei mukdashin peturim min hamatanot*.

### Limits of Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אוֹצִיא אֶת הַבְּכוֹר, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַמַּתָּנוֹת. מַאי שְׁנָא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַבְּכוֹר — שֶׁאֵין שָׁוֶה בַּכֹּל, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַמַּתָּנוֹת — שֶׁשָּׁווֹת בַּכֹּל. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַיָּל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said: I will exclude** consecrated animals that developed a blemish from the mitzva of **the firstborn but I will not exclude** them from the mitzva of **the gifts.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about a firstborn **that I** would **exclude** only the mitzva of **the firstborn,** in the first stage of this interpretation? Why not exclude the mitzva of the gifts as well? The Gemara answers **that** the mitzva of the firstborn **does not apply equally** to all animals but only to males, **and** therefore **I do not** initially **exclude** the mitzva of **the gifts, as they do apply equally to all** domesticated animals, including females. Therefore, when **the verse states "a hart"** in addition to the gazelle, this is derived as teaching that disqualified consecrated animals are excluded from the mitzva of the gifts as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אִי מָה צְבִי וְאַיָּל, אֵין אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ נוֹהֵג בָּהֶן, אַף פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין אֵין אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ נוֹהֵג בּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** another question **to Abaye: If** one accepts the analogy between disqualified consecrated animals and the animals mentioned in the verse, then one can claim as follows: **Just as** the prohibition against slaughtering an animal **itself and its offspring** on the same day **does not apply to a gazelle and a hart, so too,** the prohibition against slaughtering an animal **itself and its offspring** should **not apply to disqualified consecrated** animals. Why does the *baraita* not address this issue?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְמַאי מְדַמֵּית לְהוּ? אִי לְחוּלִּין — ״אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ״ נוֹהֵג בּוֹ, וְאִי לְקָדָשִׁים — ״אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ״ נוֹהֵג בּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: **To what are you comparing** disqualified consecrated animals? **If** you are comparing them **to non-sacred** domesticated animals, the prohibition against slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** on the same day **applies to them. And if** you are comparing them **to sacrificial** animals, the prohibition against slaughtering an animal **itself and its offspring** also **applies to them.** Since all domesticated animals are subject to the prohibition against slaughtering a mother and its offspring, one cannot derive the exclusion of disqualified consecrated animals from this verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי חֶלְבּוֹ נָמֵי נֵימָא הָכִי: לְמַאי מְדַמֵּית לְהוּ? אִי לְחוּלִּין — חֶלְבָּן אָסוּר, וְאִי לְקָדָשִׁים — חֶלְבָּן אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa **said to him: If so,** then **with regard to its fat as well, let us state** a claim **like this: To what are you comparing** disqualified animals? **If** you are comparing them **to non-sacred** animals, **their fat is forbidden. And** if you are comparing them **to sacrificial** animals, **their fat is** also **forbidden.** Since the prohibition of fat also applies to both consecrated and non-sacred animals, by the same reasoning there is no need for the verse to teach that the prohibition of fat applies. Nevertheless, the *baraita* derives this *halakha* from the word "however."

The *braisa* continues: since we are comparing *pesulei mukdashin* to deer and hart, might we not go further and say *af pesulei mukdashin chelban mutar* — that their fat (*chelev*) is also permitted, just as the fat of a deer is permitted? The Torah responds with *ach* — "however" — indicating that the comparison should not be taken to its logical extreme. The fat of *pesulei mukdashin* remains forbidden.

What is striking is how seriously *Chazal* do take this comparison. There is in fact an opinion that if one mates two animals of *pesulei mukdashin* together, one would be liable for *kilayim* — the prohibition of interbreeding — because they are treated as a hybrid species akin to the *tzvi ve'ayal*. The verse is thus taken with great force, and the *hava amina* that fat might be permitted is not as far-fetched as it sounds. The *ach* is what draws the line.

*Rav Papa* then poses a sharper question to *Abaye*. If we take the comparison to *tzvi ve'ayal* seriously enough that we needed *ach* to exclude fat, why do we not also say *k'tzvi v'ayal ein oto v'beno noheig bahen* — the prohibition of slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day should not apply, since that prohibition (*oto v'beno*) is limited to *behemah* and does not apply to *chaya*? Surely we should apply the same verse to exclude *oto v'beno* from *pesulei mukdashin*?

*Abaye* responds: *l'mai midamset lah* — what are you comparing it to? This animal, wherever we place it, falls either in the category of *chulin* or in the category of *kodshim*. If it is like *chulin*, then *oto v'beno noheig bo*. If it is like *kodshim*, then *oto v'beno noheig bo* as well. Since *oto v'beno* applies in both categories, there is no way to use the *tzvi ve'ayal* comparison to exclude it. One should not take the comparison so far as to render this animal worse — more lenient — than *both* categories simultaneously.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לָאו מִי אָמְרַתְּ ״אַךְ״ וְלֹא חֶלְבָּן? אֵימַר נָמֵי ״אַךְ״ וְלֹא אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, didn't you say** in the *baraita*: **"However," but not their fat? Say also: "However," but not a mother and its offspring.** With regard to both *halakhot*, which apply to domesticated but not undomesticated animals, the reason that the analogy is not extended is not due to Abaye's claim, as the juxtaposition between the animals would teach that their *halakhot* are the same. Instead, both exclusions are derived equally from the word "however," which serves to differentiate disqualified consecrated animals from the gazelle and the hart both with regard to forbidden fat and with regard to the prohibition against slaughtering a mother and its offspring. Interpreting the exclusion in this manner means that disqualified consecrated animals are at least comparable to sacrificial animals in that they are excluded from the mitzva of the firstborn and the priestly gifts. Had the exclusion been interpreted with regard to the mitzva of the firstborn and the priestly gifts, disqualified consecrated animals would not be comparable to any type of animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״אַךְ״ לְאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ הוּא דַּאֲתָא, וְחֶלְבּוֹ מִ״דָּמוֹ״ נָפְקָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״רַק אֶת דָּמוֹ לֹא תֹאכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a different interpretation. **Rava says:** The word **"however" comes to** exclude the prohibition against slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** from the comparison between disqualified consecrated animals and the gazelle and hart, **and** the exclusion of **its fat is derived from** the term **"its blood." As it is written** with regard to a blemished firstborn animal: "You shall eat it…as the gazelle, and as the hart. **Only you shall not eat its blood"** (Deuteronomy 15:22–23).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּמוֹ״? אִילֵּימָא דָּמוֹ מַמָּשׁ, לָא יְהֵא אֶלָּא דָּמָן דִּצְבִי וְאַיָּל, אַטּוּ דָּמָן דִּצְבִי וְאַיָּל מִי שְׁרֵי? אֶלָּא ״דָּמוֹ״ — חֶלְבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava elaborates: **What** is **"its blood"** referring to? **If we say** that it is referring to **its actual blood,** the Torah would not have had to state this explicitly, as even if **it were merely** similar to **the blood of a gazelle and a hart, is that to say** that **anyone permits the blood of a gazelle and a hart?** Since the blood of all animals is forbidden, it is already established that the blood of a blemished firstborn animal is forbidden. **Rather,** the term **"its blood"** is referring to **its fat.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֶלְבּוֹ״! אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֵלֶב״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אַהֲנִי הֶיקֵּישָׁא וְאַהֲנִי קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** in that case, **let the Merciful One write: Its fat,** instead of "its blood." The Gemara explains: **If the Merciful One had written fat, I would say: The juxtaposition** between disqualified consecrated animals and a gazelle and a hart **is effective** with regard to fat, **and the verse** itself **is** also **effective,** i.e., it can be derived from the juxtaposition and the verse together that the prohibition of fat applies to disqualified consecrated animals to a certain extent, but not fully.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהֲנִי הֶיקֵּישָׁא לְמַעוֹטֵי מִכָּרֵת, דְּכִי כְּתַב כָּרֵת — אַאוֹכֵל חֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה הוּא דִּכְתִיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי כׇל אוֹכֵל חֵלֶב מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The juxtaposition is effective in** that it **excludes** one who eats the fat of consecrated animals **from** the punishment of **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*].** The reason is **that when** the Merciful One **writes "*karet*," it is written with regard to one who eats the fat of a domesticated animal, as it is stated: "For anyone who eats the fat of the domesticated animal…**even the soul that eats it shall be excised from his people" (Leviticus 7:25). The juxtaposition to a gazelle and a hart would teach that the punishment of *karet* does not apply in the case of disqualified consecrated animals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַהֲנִי קְרָא לְמֵיקַם עֲלֵיהּ בְּלָאו בְּעָלְמָא, לְהָכִי אַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן ״דָּמוֹ״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה דָּמוֹ בְּכָרֵת — אַף חֶלְבּוֹ בְּכָרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** at the same time **the verse** in Deuteronomy 15:23 would also be **effective, as,** if it were written: Only you shall not eat its fat, it would **establish** the consumption of the fat of disqualified consecrated animals **as a prohibition** for which one is liable **merely** to receive lashes. **For this** reason, **the Merciful One expresses** the prohibition against eating the fat using **the language "its blood,"** in order **to tell you** that **just as** consumption of **its blood** is punishable **by *karet*, so too,** consumption of **its fat** is punishable **by *karet*.** By using the term "its blood," the Torah conveys that one is liable to receive *karet* for eating the fat of disqualified consecrated animals, i.e., that with regard to this particular *halakha* the juxtaposition does not apply at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנָא ״אַךְ״, וְלֹא חֶלְבּוֹ, קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** Rava stated that the *halakha* with regard to the consumption of the fat of a disqualified consecrated animal is derived from the verse: "Only shall you not eat its blood." The Gemara asks: **But didn't** the ***tanna*** of the *baraita* **say** that the word **"however"** limits the juxtaposition so that disqualified consecrated animals are not included in the mitzvot of the firstborn and the gifts, **but** they are **not** excluded from the prohibition against eating **its fat?** How can Rava suggest a different source than the one cited in the *baraita*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר ״דָּמוֹ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר ״אַךְ״ — וְלֹא חֶלְבּוֹ, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״דָּמוֹ״, לְאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that according to Rava, **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: Had** the term **"its blood" not been stated, I would have said** that the word **"however"** teaches: **But not its fat.** But **now that it is stated: "Its blood,"** the word "however" **comes to** teach that the prohibition against slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** on the same day applies to disqualified consecrated animals.

*Rav Papa* presses back on *Abaye*: if that argument were sufficient, it would have been equally sufficient for fat. Fat too is forbidden by both *chulin* and *kodshim* — so why did the *braisa* need the word *ach* at all? The Torah could simply have been silent and we would have known the fat is forbidden. Yet the *braisa* plainly takes the comparison seriously and requires *ach* to limit it. The same logic that requires *ach* for fat should require a verse to limit *oto v'beno*. *Aval lo mi amart ach velo chelbo* — we used *ach* to exclude fat; the same *ach* should then also exclude *oto v'beno*. This leaves a difficulty: can *ach* bear the weight of excluding both?

*Rava* then offers his resolution: *ach le'oto ve'et beno hu de'ata* — in truth, the word *ach* is coming to exclude *oto v'beno*, not fat. If so, how do we know that fat is forbidden? *Midamo nafka* — it is derived from the prohibition of blood. The Torah says *rak et damo lo tochal* — "only its blood shall you not eat." Now, it is puzzling why the Torah needs to prohibit the blood of *pesulei mukdashin*. Once the animal has been compared to *tzvi ve'ayal*, we already know its blood is forbidden — the blood of all animals is forbidden. So why state it?

*Mai damo* — the Torah cannot literally mean blood. *Ilima damo mamash, atu dama detzvi ve'ayal mi shari* — is the blood of a deer permitted? Obviously not. So the Torah's mention of "blood" must be pointing to something else. It means: the *chelev* — the fat — is being governed by the same rule as the blood. *Ma damo bekaret af chelban bekaret*: just as the prohibition of blood carries the penalty of *karet*, so too does the prohibition of fat in *pesulei mukdashin* carry *karet*. This is necessary because one might otherwise have reasoned from the comparison to *tzvi ve'ayal* that if fat is prohibited at all, it carries only a simple *lav* — since the classic *karet* prohibition of fat applies to *behemah* (*ki kol ochel chelev min habehemah*), not to deer. The Torah therefore uses the language of blood to signal that the fat here retains the full severity, including *karet*, of its original status.

The *braisa* is then reread in light of *Rava*'s interpretation: *ilu lo ne'emar damo, hayit omar ach velo chelban* — had the Torah not said "its blood," I would have used *ach* to exclude the fat. But since *damo* teaches us about the fat, *oto ve'et beno hu de'ata* — the word *ach* is freed to serve its true function of excluding *oto ve'et beno*. The *braisa*'s mention of fat and *ach* is thus a conditional reading: had we lacked the *damo* verse, *ach* would have done that work; since we have *damo*, *ach* addresses *oto ve'et beno* instead.

It is worth pausing on *Abaye*'s underlying logic, which at the end of the day captures the essential reason for the exemptions from *bechor* and *matanot*. The comparison to *tzvi ve'ayal* is the mechanism, but the deeper reason that *pesulei mukdashin* are exempt from *bechor* and *matanot* is that they retain residual *kedusha* — they have not fully descended into the category of *chulin*. The same way something genuinely *kadosh* is exempt from *bechor* and *matanot*, something with residual *kedusha* remains in that exempt category. That is the substantive point of our *mishna*.

## Post-Redemption Restrictions

### Slaughter & Eat Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָם יוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״תִּזְבַּח״ — וְלֹא גִּיזָּה, ״בָּשָׂר״ — וְלֹא חָלָב, ״וְאָכַלְתָּ״ — וְלֹא לִכְלָבֶיךָ, מִכָּאן שֶׁאֵין פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** animals whose consecration preceded their blemish **do not** completely **emerge** from their sacred status and assume **non-sacred** status in order to be shorn and to be utilized for labor. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a verse discussing consecrated animals that developed a blemish and were redeemed: "Notwithstanding, after all the desire of your soul, you may slaughter and eat flesh" (Deuteronomy 12:15). The term **"you may slaughter"** teaches that slaughtering the animal is permitted, **but not shearing** it. Likewise, "you may eat **flesh"** teaches that its meat is permitted, **but not** its **milk.** Finally, **"you may eat"** teaches that you may eat the meat, **but** you may **not** feed it **to your dogs. From here** it is derived **that one may not redeem sacrificial** animals in order **to feed them to dogs.**

We now reach the most direct expression of residual *kedusha*: after a *pesulei mukdashin* animal has been redeemed, one may not work it, shear it, or milk it. The only permitted act is *shechita* followed by eating. *V'einam yotzim l'chulin l'higazez ul'he'aved*.

The source: *d'tanu rabanan*, the sages taught from the verse *tizbach ve'achalta basar* — "you shall slaughter and eat the meat." *Chazal* read this as addressing *pesulei mukdashin*. One reading parses each word separately: *tizbach* — slaughter it — *belo giza*, without shearing; *basar* — you eat the meat — *belo chalav*, but not the milk; *ve'achalta* — and you shall eat it — *velo l'kalbecha*, not for your dog. From here, *mikan she'ein podin et hakodshim l'ha'achilan l'klavim*: one may not redeem consecrated animals for the purpose of using them as dog food. The only permitted use that justifies redemption is eating.

### Benefit After Slaughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: ״תִּזְבַּח וְאָכַלְתָּ״ — אֵין לָךְ בָּהֶן הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה אֶלָּא מִשְּׁעַת זְבִיחָה וְאֵילָךְ, אֲבָל פּוֹדִין אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לְהַאֲכִילָן לִכְלָבִים, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternative exposition. **Some say** that the phrase: **"You may slaughter and eat,"** teaches that **you have permission to eat them only from the time of slaughter onward.** Similarly, no benefit may be derived from them until after their slaughter. The Gemara notes: **But** according to this interpretation, **so too,** the *tanna* **said** that **one may redeem sacrificial** animals in order **to feed them to dogs,** as this prohibition is not derived from the term "you may eat."

An alternative reading of the same verse: rather than parsing word by word, this second opinion reads *tizbach ve'achalta* holistically — slaughter and eat. The Torah is telling us that the only benefit permitted from *kodshim* that have been redeemed is benefit that comes *mishat zvicha ve'eilach* — from the point of slaughter onward. Before slaughter, while the animal is alive, no benefit is permitted — no shearing, no working, no milking. This reading, however, has a different implication for the dog-food prohibition: since benefit is only restricted while the animal is alive, once it is dead (or becomes a *treifah*) you might have thought it could be redeemed for dog food. The *gemara* explores this: if the animal is a *treifah* — alive but unable to be eaten by a human — there is no *ha'amada veha'aracha* issue since it is alive, but there is the concern of redeeming for dog food.

### No Redemption for Dog Food

This establishes the biblical basis for the principle that one may not redeem consecrated animals destined for dog food. As noted earlier, the distinction between this concern and the *ha'amada veha'aracha* requirement is meaningful: the former applies even to a *treifah*, which is alive but whose end use would be dog food; the latter applies even to an animal that is not a *treifah* but is dead. Both reasons are operative for *kodshei mizbe'ach*, and our *mishna*'s statement that *kodshei mizbe'ach* cannot be redeemed once they die reflects both underlying principles. For *kodshei damim* — the first case in our *mishna* — neither concern applies with the same force, which is why Rabbi Shimon permits their redemption even after death.

## Offspring & Milk Status

### Question on Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן אָסוּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיעַבַּר וְאִיתְיְלִיד לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — אַמַּאי? וְלַד צְבִי וְאַיָּל נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא דְּאִיעַבַּר לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן וְאִיתְיְלִיד לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to sacrificial animals: **And their offspring and their milk are prohibited after their redemption.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the offspring's birth? **If we say that** the offspring **was** both **conceived and born after** its mother's **redemption,** then **why** would it be prohibited? After all, **they are** like **the offspring** of **a gazelle or a hart,** i.e., they are completely non-sacred. **Rather,** it must be referring to a case **where** the offspring **was conceived prior to** its mother's **redemption and born after** its mother's **redemption.**

The *mishna* states that the offspring and milk of these animals (*pesulei mukdashin*) are forbidden even after redemption. This residual *kedusha* extends to what the animal produces. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If both conception and birth happened entirely after the mother was redeemed, why should the offspring be forbidden? After all, the mother has been compared to a *tzvi ve'ayal* — a wild animal — and if a deer gives birth and produces milk, those are perfectly permissible. The residual *kedusha* restricts how one may *use* the mother, but should it really extend to offspring conceived and born after redemption?

### Conception Timing

The answer: *ela d'abar lifnei pidyon v'yalda l'achar pidyon* — the case is where the fetus was conceived before the mother was redeemed, but was born after redemption. In that case, the fetus shared in the mother's full *kedusha* during the period of gestation, and that *kedusha* remains in the offspring even after the mother is redeemed. It is this overlap between the period of full *kedusha* and the period of gestation that renders the offspring forbidden. If, however, conception occurred entirely after redemption, the offspring are not affected by the mother's residual *kedusha* and are completely unrestricted — *velad tzvi ve'ayal ninhu*, they are like the young of a deer.

### Offspring Born Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן — מִיקְדָּשׁ נָמֵי קָדְשִׁי. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״זָכָר״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַוָּלָד, וּ״נְקֵבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַתְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** One can infer: **But** if they were born **before their** mothers' **redemption,** not only are they not prohibited, **they are also imbued with** inherent **sanctity.** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter,** that the offspring of a sacrificial animal is imbued with inherent sanctity, derived? It is derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And if his offering be a sacrifice of peace offerings: If he sacrifice of the cattle, whether male or female, he shall sacrifice it without blemish before the Lord" (Leviticus 3:1), that the words "male" and "female" are extraneous, as the term "cattle" includes both. Consequently, **"male"** serves **to include the offspring** of a peace offering; **"or female"** serves **to include the** female animal that is designated as **a substitute** for a peace offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא וְלַד תְּמִימִין וּתְמוּרַת תְּמִימִים, וְלַדי בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין וּתְמוּרַת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין מִנַּיִן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אִם זָכָר״ — לְרַבּוֹת וְלַד בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין, ״אִם נְקֵבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת תְּמוּרַת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And I have** derived this *halakha* **only** with regard to **the offspring of unblemished** animals **and the substitute of unblemished** animals. **From where** do I derive that the same *halakha* applies to **the offspring of blemished** animals **and the substitute of blemished** animals? **When** the verse **states: "Whether male,"** this serves **to include the offspring of blemished** animals, in a case where its consecration preceded its blemish, and when the verse states: **"Or female,"** it serves **to include the substitute of blemished** animals.

What about offspring conceived and born entirely *before* the mother was redeemed — when the mother still had full *kedushas mizbe'ach*? *Ha lifnei pidyon mikdash nami kadshi* — it goes without saying that such offspring carry their own *kedusha*, and the question becomes what its precise nature is.

The *gemara* derives from the verse by *shelamim* — which states *zachar* and *nekevah*, male or female — that derivative *kedushos* are included. *Zachar l'rabot et havelad*: the word "male" extends to include the offspring of a consecrated animal; *nekevah l'rabot et hatmurah*: the word "female" extends to include an animal that was the subject of a *tmura* exchange. Both of these are derivative *kedushos*, and both carry full *kedusha* sufficient in principle to be brought as a *korban*. The same principle applies even where the mother subsequently acquired a *mum*: *im zachar l'rabot velad baalei mumin* — additional words in the verse include the offspring of an animal that has a fixed *mum*, as long as that *mum* came after the original consecration and thus did not compromise the foundational *kedusha*.

That said, there is a *machlokes* regarding such offspring. Some say *kadshi l'hakravah* — the offspring can actually be brought as a *korban*, reflecting full derivative *kedusha*. Others say *kadshi l'reia* — the offspring must graze until they acquire a *mum* and are then redeemed, the derivative *kedusha* not being strong enough to permit direct sacrifice. The simpler reading is that they do have full *kedusha* and can be brought as a *korban*, but the disagreement exists.

## Offspring After Redemption

### Rav Kahuna's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתָן וְלָדוֹת שֶׁלְּאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן, מָה תְּהֵא עֲלֵיהֶן? לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן — מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי בְּהוּ, אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: קָדְשִׁי לִיקְרַב, וְאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: קָדְשִׁי לִרְעִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **those offspring that** were born **after their** mother's **redemption, what will be with them,** since they can be neither sacrificed nor redeemed? Before providing an answer, the Gemara notes: If they were born **before their** mother's **redemption,** the Sages **disagree with regard to** the *halakha*. **There is one who says: They are** entirely **consecrated,** to the extent that they may **be sacrificed, and there is one who says: They are consecrated to graze** until a blemish befalls them so that they can be redeemed, but they may not be sacrificed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן, מָה תְּהֵא עֲלֵיהֶן? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כּוֹנְסָן לַכִּיפָּה, וְהֵן מֵתִין. דְּהֵיכִי לֶיעְבֵּיד? לִיקְרְבִינְהוּ — מִכֹּחַ קְדוּשָּׁה דְּחוּיָה קָאָתוּ, לִיפְרְקִינְהוּ — לָא אַלִּימִי לְמִיתְפַּס פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the previous inquiry: With regard to those **that** were born **after their** mother's **redemption, what will be with them? Rav Huna says: One gathers them into a chamber, and they die** of starvation. The reason is **that what** are we **to do? Shall we sacrifice them?** They cannot be sacrificed, as **they received** their status **from** the **deferred sanctity** of the redeemed mother, and are therefore unfit for the altar. **Shall we redeem them?** They cannot be redeemed, as their sanctity **is not strong** enough **to be transferable** to money for **their redemption.**

The more complex and troubling case is offspring that were conceived before the mother was redeemed but born after. *Vladot she'le'achar pidyon mai te'hei aleihem* — what is their legal status? The problem is acute: *bema nafshach*, no straightforward approach resolves it. The offspring cannot be brought as a *korban* because they derive from *kedusha dchuya* — sanctity that was derailed when the mother was redeemed. *Mikoch kedusha dchuya ka'atu*: they come from sanctity that was pushed aside. One might think to redeem them — either directly or by letting them graze until they acquire a *mum* — but *lo alimi l'mispas pidyonam*: their sanctity is not robust enough to effectuate a transfer through redemption. The *kedusha* is so residual and so derailed that the formal mechanism of redemption, which requires a transfer of sanctity to a monetary substitute, cannot function.

*Amar Rav Kahuna*: *konsan l'kipa u'mesim* — they must be locked in a room and left to die. The well-known discomfort with *tzaar baalei chayim* is acknowledged: the *gemara*'s *rishonim* accept that where preventing transgression constitutes a sufficient overriding purpose, the principle of animal suffering does not override. This is one such case. The animal cannot be used, cannot be redeemed, cannot be sacrificed, and leaving it to roam freely would create risks of misuse. Locking it away is the only solution.

*De'heichi le'avid* — what other option is there? To bring it as a *korban*: impossible, *kedusha dchuya*. To redeem it: impossible, *lo alimi l'mispas pidyonam*. The *kedusha* that was in place while it was a fetus derived partly from the mother, and when the mother was redeemed, that derivative element was stripped away. The offspring is left with a form of *kedusha* that is neither functional enough to reach the altar nor transferable through redemption.

### In Utero Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: סָמוּךְ לְפִדְיוֹנָן מַתְפִּיסָן לְשֵׁם אוֹתוֹ זֶבַח. סָמוּךְ לְפִדְיוֹנָן? לְמֵימְרָא דִּבְנֵי פְּדִיָּיה נִינְהוּ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: סָמוּךְ לְפִדְיוֹן אִמָּן מַתְפִּיסָן לְשֵׁם אוֹתוֹ זֶבַח. טַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יְגַדֵּל מֵהֶן עֲדָרִים עֲדָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **They said in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina:** Immediately **before their redemption, one dedicates them for the sake of the same offering** for which their mother was consecrated. The Gemara asks: Immediately **before their redemption?** Is that **to say that they are fit to be redeemed?** It was established that they cannot be redeemed. **Rather, say:** Immediately **before their mother's redemption, one dedicates them for the sake of the same offering.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the offspring are not simply left as they are? **Rabbi Levi says:** It is a rabbinic **decree, lest one raise flocks and flocks of them.** In other words, if the offspring are left alive in their forbidden status, there is a concern that one might eventually derive benefit from them and thereby violate a Torah prohibition.

The *gemara* then presents a solution attributed to *Ami*, *Mar*, and the school of *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of *Rabbi Chanina*: *samuch le'pidyon imam matpisan leshem oso zevach* — just before the mother is redeemed, one should sanctify the fetus in utero for the same type of sacrifice that the mother was intended to be. This is a brilliant intervention. The entire problem arises because the fetus's *kedusha* is derivative and becomes derailed once the mother is redeemed. But if, *before* that happens — before any derailment occurs — the fetus is given its own independent *kedusha* for the same purpose (a *shelamim*, say, if that was the mother's designation), then when the mother is subsequently redeemed, the fetus's status is entirely independent. It no longer relies on derivative *kedusha* from the mother; it has its own consecration. Once born, it is a *shelamim* in its own right.

The *gemara* initially questions this: we just established that the offspring cannot be redeemed. The response clarifies: the solution is not to redeem the offspring but to sanctify it *before* the mother is redeemed, thereby giving it an independent *kedusha* and preempting the problem entirely. Once that independent *kedusha* is established, when the mother is redeemed the fetus is unaffected.

Why then would anyone ever need the *kipa* solution? Because one must act before the redemption of the mother. If the mother has already been redeemed, it is too late — the *kedusha dchuya* is already in place, and once sanctity is *dechuyah*, it cannot be restored. The Talmudic principle is that *dichuy* is permanent: once something is pushed aside, it cannot return to eligibility. At that point, *kipa* is the only option.

A further question arises: why must we not graze the offspring, let it live its life and let things resolve naturally? The answer, attributed to *Rav Levi*, is *gezeirah shema yigadel me'hen adarim adarim* — a rabbinic concern that one might end up grazing entire flocks of such animals. These offspring would reproduce, their offspring would reproduce, and soon one would have large numbers of animals in this ambiguous status with no practical resolution and a significant risk of transgression. Swift resolution — either through in-utero sanctification or the *kipa* — is therefore mandated.

### Cannot Add Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבִינָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַהוּ שֶׁמַּתְפִּיסָן לְכׇל זֶבַח שֶׁיִּרְצֶה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מַתְפִּיסָן. מַאי טַעְמָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גָּמַר ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״ ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״ מִבְּכוֹר, מָה בְּכוֹר אֵין מַתְפִּיסָן לְכׇל זֶבַח שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, דִּכְתִיב: ״אַךְ בְּכוֹר אֲשֶׁר יְבֻכַּר לַה׳ בִּבְהֵמָה וְגוֹ׳ לֹא יַקְדִּישׁ אִישׁ אֹתוֹ״, אַף הָנֵי אֵין מַתְפִּיסָן לְכׇל זֶבַח שֶׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet:** According to Rabbi Ḥanina, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the following matter: **Can one dedicate** the unborn offspring **as any offering he desires?** Rav Sheshet **said to him: He may not dedicate them** as any offering other than that for which the mother was consecrated. Ravina asked: **What is the reason?** Rav Sheshet **said to him:** It is **derived** by a verbal analogy between the phrase: **"Within** all **your gates"** (Deuteronomy 12:15), and: **"Within your gates"** (Deuteronomy 15:22), written **with regard to a firstborn. Just as one cannot dedicate a firstborn as any offering he desires, as it is written: "But the firstborn among animals, which is born as a firstborn to the Lord, no man shall sanctify it"** (Leviticus 27:26), **so too, one cannot dedicate these** offspring **as any offering he desires.**

*Ba'ei minei Ravina me'Rav Sheshet*: *Ravina* posed the following question. When we say that one sanctifies the fetus before the mother's redemption — does it matter what *type* of sacrifice one designates? May one designate it as an *olah* even if the mother was a *shelamim*? If we are giving it an independent *kedusha*, why not give it any *kedusha* one wishes?

*Rav Sheshet* replied: *ein matpisin* — no, one cannot do this. Why not? *Gemar bisharecha bisharecha mibechor* — there is a *gezeirah shavah* connecting the phrase "in your gates" by *pesulei mukdashin* and the same phrase by *bechor*. From the verse regarding *bechor* — *ach bechor asher yibaker laHashem bivehemah lo yakdish ish oto* — we know that one may not impose an additional, different *kedusha* upon a *bechor*. Even if one wished to keep the *kedusha habechor* intact and add a *shelamim* designation on top of it, this is prohibited. The verse says explicitly that you shall not sanctify the *bechor* for a different purpose.

The *gezeirah shavah* teaches that the same restriction applies to *pesulei mukdashin*: one cannot sanctify them for a different type of sacrifice. Since the fetus already has derivative *kedusha* from the mother — say, *kedushas shelamim* — one cannot now say "I am sanctifying it as an *olah*." Just as the *bechor* acquires its *kedusha* by virtue of the mother's womb, not because the mother was herself a *korban*, and yet receives no additional or different *kedusha*, so too here: the fetus has *kedusha* by virtue of the mother, and one may not impose upon it a *kedusha* of a different type. One may only give it an independent version of the *same* type of *kedusha* it already derivatively holds.

## Comprehensive Baraita Review

### Blemish-Before Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: קֳדָשִׁים שֶׁקָּדַם מוּם קָבוּעַ לְהֶקְדֵּישָׁן וְנִפְדּוּ — חַיָּיבִין בַּבְּכוֹרָה וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת, בֵּין לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן בֵּין לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — הַגּוֹזֵז וְהָעוֹבֵד בָּהֶן אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, בֵּין לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן בֵּין לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Sheshet: Sacrificial** animals **whose permanent blemish preceded their consecration and** that **were redeemed, are obligated in** the mitzva of **a firstborn, and** obligated **in the gifts. Whether before their redemption or after their redemption, one who shears** them **or utilizes them for labor does not incur the forty** lashes. Similarly, **whether before their redemption or after their redemption, they do not render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them sacred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן — מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וּלְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וּוַלְדוֹתֵיהֶן — חוֹל, וְנִפְדִּין תְּמִימִים, וּמַתְפִּיסָן לְכׇל זֶבַח שֶׁיִּרְצֶה. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הֲרֵי הֵן כְּחוּלִּין לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, אֵין לְךָ בָּהֶם אֶלָּא מִצְוַת עִלּוּי בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And before their redemption,** one who benefits from **them** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **and after their redemption,** one who benefits from **them is not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. **And their offspring are non-sacred** if they were born after their mother's redemption; **and they may be redeemed** while they are **unblemished** if they were born before their mother's redemption; **and one can dedicate** the offspring **as any offering he desires. The principle of the matter** is that animals whose permanent blemish preceded their consecration **are like non-sacred** animals **in all matters,** and **you have only the mitzva of value alone,** i.e., they must be valuated and redeemed with money, unlike non-sacred animals.

A *braisa* is now cited that systematically reviews both categories and serves as a comprehensive summary of the topics covered. *Tanya nami ke'vatah de'Rav Sheshet.*

*Kodshim she'kadam mum kavua le'hakdeshan* — if the blemish preceded the sanctification, and the animal was then redeemed, it is *chayav be'bechor u've'matanot*: the *bechor* and *matanot* obligations apply in full. There is no residual *kedusha* to exempt it. This applies both before and after redemption. Similarly, *hagozez ve'oveid bahen eino soveg et ha'arba'im* — if one shears or works with the animal before redemption, one does not receive the forty lashes, because there is no *kedushas haguf* here that makes those acts intrinsically prohibited. Before redemption, *moalin bahen* — there is *me'ila*, since the animal belongs to the Temple. After redemption, *ein moalin bahen* — no *me'ila* applies. It does not effect *tmura*. And *vladoseihem chol* — offspring born after redemption are completely *chulin*, entirely de-sanctified. Those offspring are *nifdim tmimim* — they can even be redeemed while unblemished, since their *kedusha* was never *kedushas mizbe'ach*. And *matpisin l'chol zevach she'yirtzeh* — once redeemed, one may dedicate the animal to any sacrifice one wishes. *Klalo shel davar harei hen k'chulin l'chol divreihem* — they are entirely like *chulin* in every respect. *Ein l'cha bahem ela mitzvas pidyon bilvad* — the only *mitzva* that applies to them is redemption; after that, nothing residual remains.

### Sanctification-Then-Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קָדַם הֶקְדֵּשָׁן אֶת מוּמָן, אוֹ מוּם עוֹבֵר לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן, וּלְאַחַר מִכָּאן נוֹלַד מוּם קָבוּעַ וְנִפְדּוּ — פְּטוּרִין מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, בֵּין לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן בֵּין לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן. הַגּוֹזֵז וְהָעוֹבֵד בָּהֶן סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וּבֵין לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן בֵּין לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But** if **their consecration preceded their blemish, or** they had **a temporary blemish** prior **to their consecration and afterward developed a permanent blemish, and they were redeemed, they are exempt** from the mitzva of the **firstborn and from the gifts.** Furthermore, **whether before their redemption or after their redemption, one who shears** them **or utilizes them for labor incurs the forty** lashes. **And whether before their redemption or after their redemption, they render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them sacred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן — מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וּלְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וּוַלְדוֹתֵיהֶן קוֹדֶשׁ, וְאֵין נִיפְדִּין תְּמִימִין, וְאֵין מַתְפִּיסָן לְכׇל זֶבַח שֶׁיִּרְצֶה. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, וְאֵין לָךְ בָּהֶן אֶלָּא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **Before their redemption,** one who derives benefit from **them** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **and after their redemption,** one who derives benefit from **them is not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. **And their offspring are sacred and may not be redeemed** while they are **unblemished, and one may not dedicate them as any offering he desires. The principle of the matter** is that redeemed animals whose consecration preceded their permanent blemish **are like** redeemed **sacrificial** animals **in all matters, and you have only the permission of consumption alone,** i.e., once they have been redeemed they may be eaten. Like Rav Sheshet, this *tanna* rules that the offspring of animals whose consecration preceded their blemish may not be dedicated as any offering one desires.

*Aval kodesh hikdishan ba'al mum* — if the animal was sanctified first and then acquired a fixed *mum*, or if it had a transitory *mum* at the time of consecration and later acquired a fixed one, and it was then redeemed — this is the second category, the one with residual *kedusha*. The following rules apply:

*Ptura min habechor u'min hamatanot* — exempt from *bechor* and *matanot*, both before and after redemption. *Hagozez ve'oveid bahen sofeg et ha'arba'im* — one who shears or works with the animal receives lashes, because the act itself is prohibited by virtue of the *kedushas haguf*, even after redemption; the residual *kedusha* is sufficient to generate this prohibition. It *oseh tmura* — effects a *tmura* both before and after redemption, the residual *kedusha* being sufficient for that as well. Before redemption, *moalin bahen* — full *me'ila* applies; after redemption, *ein moalin bahen* — but note that the absence of *me'ila* after redemption does not mean the animal is unrestricted. It means only that the *me'ila* framework no longer applies; the lashes-generating prohibitions remain. *Vladoseihem kodesh* — offspring are sanctified even if born after redemption. *V'ein nifdim tmimim* — they cannot be redeemed while unblemished.

### Residual Sanctity Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, וְאֵין לָךְ בָּהֶן אֶלָּא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **Before their redemption,** one who derives benefit from **them** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **and after their redemption,** one who derives benefit from **them is not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. **And their offspring are sacred and may not be redeemed** while they are **unblemished, and one may not dedicate them as any offering he desires. The principle of the matter** is that redeemed animals whose consecration preceded their permanent blemish **are like** redeemed **sacrificial** animals **in all matters, and you have only the permission of consumption alone,** i.e., once they have been redeemed they may be eaten.

The *braisa* then states its final and governing principle: *klalo shel davar harei hen k'hekdesh l'chol divreihem* — in every respect they retain the status of *hekdesh*. Even though they carry a *mum*, even after they have been redeemed, the residual *kedusha* is comprehensive. And the practical expression of that? *V'ein l'cha bahem ela heter achilah bilvad* — the only permission that exists for such an animal after redemption is the permission to eat it. Nothing more. All other uses — work, shearing, milking — remain forbidden by the force of that residual *kedusha*. The animal has traveled from full *kedushas mizbe'ach* through blemish through redemption, and what remains at the end of that journey is still genuine *kedusha* — just enough to restrict every use except the table.
