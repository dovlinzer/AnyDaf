# Niddah 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Placenta & Fetus

### Fetus in Placenta

The daf opens eight lines from the bottom of 26b, where the Gemara had been working through the halachic relationship between a *shilya* (placenta) and the fetus it is presumed to contain. The foundational principle is that a *shilya* is assumed to contain a dissolved fetus, rendering the woman *tme'ah leida* (impure with the impurity of childbirth) and, if the *shilya* is in a house, rendering that house *tamei* with the *tum'ah* of a corpse. The Gemara's preceding discussion addressed the situation where both a fetus and a placenta are present: is the placenta connected to the already-identified fetus, or does it indicate an additional fetus?

The *tanya* had established that when the first birth is a *nefel* (non-viable miscarriage), the placenta is presumed connected only if it emerges within three days; beyond that, one must be concerned that it represents an independent fetus. When the first birth is a live birth, however, no second fetus could have survived alongside it without emerging immediately — and therefore the placenta, however late it appears, is presumed connected to that live birth. The final ruling attributed to the students of Rav added a further distinction: the presumption of connection applies only when the fetus was on a path toward viability. If the fetus was of a kind that could not have been viable, the placenta may be independent.

### Viability & Connection

With this framework established, the Gemara turns to a question posed by Rav Yose ben Shaul to Rebbi: what of a fetus that looks like a raven — one of those anomalous births that bear an animal appearance? Such a fetus does not confer *tumat leida*, since it lacks a human form. Does its placenta follow the same rules? The question carries an inherent ambiguity that the Gemara does not fully resolve: when the text describes a fetus that *looks* like a raven, it may be gesturing at something more essential than mere appearance — that on some level, the Gemara regards such a birth as partaking of the nature of what it resembles.

### Animal Appearance

Rebbi's response is pointed: *ein tolin ela bedavar sheyesh bemino shilya* — one may only ascribe a placenta to a type of creature that actually produces placentas. Mammals have placentas; birds do not. Since this fetus presents as a raven, its placenta cannot logically be attributed to it. Rebbi's reasoning implicitly confirms that the Gemara treats the animal-like appearance as indicative of some underlying essence — the *shilya* simply does not belong to something of avian nature.

### Physical Connection

Rav Yose ben Shaul presses further: what if the placenta is physically intertwined (*keshurah*) with the fetus? Here the physical connection seems to supply direct evidence that they are one. Rebbi dismisses even this question, declaring it a case that simply never occurs (*davar she'eino she'altah*) — implying that his assumption about the avian nature of such a fetus made it inconceivable that a placenta would be present at all, and therefore the question of physical entwinement is moot.

### Multiple Fetuses

A *baraita* then challenges this dismissal. It teaches that if a woman miscarries something that looks like a *behemah*, *chayah*, or *of* (domestic animal, wild animal, or bird), and a placenta accompanies it, then: if the placenta is physically connected (*keshurah*) to the fetus, there is no concern that it represents a separate child — it is treated as one birth. If the placenta is not connected, one must be concerned that it is an independent fetus. The *baraita* further specifies that when there is such concern, the stringencies of two births are imposed — a significant ruling whose practical implications differ depending on whether one follows Rabbi Meir's view (that an animal-appearing fetus does produce *tumat leida*) or that of the *Chachamim* (that it does not).

This *baraita* directly contradicts Rebbi on two counts: it demonstrates both that physical connection *does* occur in such cases, and that it *does* matter halachically. The Gemara accepts this as a *tiyuvta*.

One might ask: if the placenta is not physically connected but the fetus is there, why not simply assume they belong together? The Gemara answers that one must say: *shema nimuach shapir shel shilya* — perhaps the fetus of that placenta dissolved, and separately, the placenta of the unusual fetus dissolved — leaving us with one anomalous-looking fetus and one independent placenta, which happen to be in proximity. Once they are not physically connected and the fetus is of this unusual type, the circumstantial case for connection is overcome by this alternative possibility.

## Twin Birth Timing

### Placenta Order

The Gemara next cites a ruling transmitted by Rabba bar Shmuel citing Rav Chisda citing Shmuel: there was a case in which the placenta was attributed as connected to the fetus even ten days after the birth — consistent with the principle that a live birth always retains its connection to the subsequent placenta. The Gemara then specifies a crucial boundary: *velo amru tulin ela beshilya habaa achar havlad* — this entire principle applies only when the placenta follows the fetus. If the placenta emerged before the fetus, it cannot be attributed to it; instead, one must be concerned that it was an independent birth, with a dissolved fetus inside it, and that the fetus which emerged afterward was a separate event entirely. While such a sequence is presumably very rare (hence the term "afterbirth"), the Gemara establishes that when it does occur, the presumption of connection does not apply.

### 33-Day Separation

Rabba bar bar Chana then reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that there was a case where the placenta was attributed to the birth even twenty-three days later. Rav Yosef interjects that when this tradition was transmitted to him, the figure was twenty-four days, not twenty-three — and the Gemara leaves that discrepancy without resolution.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Avira then reports in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak a case of a different kind altogether: not a placenta following a birth, but an actual second birth following the first by thirty-three days — *vlad achar chaveiro lamed gimel yom*. Again Rav Yosef objects that when the report was transmitted to him, the interval was thirty-four days. Here the Gemara sets aside the one-day discrepancy and grapples with the more fundamental puzzle: how is it possible for two viable twin births to be separated by thirty-three (or thirty-four) days?

### Month Viability

The difficulty is rooted in the Gemara's understanding of which gestational months yield viable births. The Gemara assumes that only a seventh-month baby or a full ninth-month baby can be viable. An eighth-month baby, or a ninth-month baby born before the completion of nine months (on one opinion), is not viable.

The Gemara frames this as a dispute: *hanicha leman de'amar yoledes letisha yoledes lekitain* — according to the opinion that viability in the ninth month obtains even if birth occurs partway through the month, the case is straightforward. The first twin was born toward the end of the seventh month, the second was born in the beginning of the ninth month — and both were viable. But according to the stricter opinion, that a ninth-month baby is viable only at the full completion of nine months, the arithmetic creates a problem: if the first twin was born anywhere in the seventh month, thirty-three days later places the second birth somewhere in the ninth month but not necessarily at its end.

### 3-Month Separation

The Gemara resolves the difficulty by reversing (*ipuch shmateta*) the earlier attributions: the thirty-three day interval actually referred to the placenta case, and the twenty-three day interval referred to the second-birth case. With twenty-three days separating two births, and assuming viability anywhere in the seventh month (which the Gemara grants more liberally than it does for the ninth), both twins could have been born in the seventh month.

A further case then extends the discussion dramatically: Avdimi bar Abba (or Rav Adda) cites a report from Rav Menachem of the village of Sharim that a second birth followed the first by three months — and, the Gemara adds, *ve'hareihem yoshvim lefanenu beveit hamidrash*: both children were alive and sitting right there in the *beit midrash*. The Gemara identifies them: Yehuda and Chizkiya, the sons of Rabbi Chiya, who were twins separated by three months of gestation.

### One Drop Split

This prompts an immediate difficulty: the Gemara elsewhere assumes that a woman who conceives cannot conceive again until she gives birth — so how could this be explained as anything other than a second conception three months after the first? The answer, attributed to Abaye, is: *tipa achat hayta venichalka lishnayim* — it was one drop (of seed) that divided into two. Both twins were conceived in the same act; one reached completion of form early (toward the beginning of the seventh month) and the other reached completion of form late (toward the end of the ninth month), and so they were born three months apart.

## Placenta Impurity

### Meir vs. Colleagues

Having established the rules about the *shilya*'s connection to a fetus, the Gemara turns to a distinct question: what is the *shilya* itself as a source of *tum'ah*? A *baraita* records the dispute: Rabbi Meir holds that a *shilya* in a house renders the house *tamei*, not because the *shilya* is itself a fetus, but because a *shilya* is never found without a fetus accompanying it — and that fetus has dissolved inside, conferring *tumat met* on the house. His three colleagues — Rabbi Yose, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon — all rule the house is *tahor*.

### Dissolution & Transfer

Rabbi Meir's colleagues challenge him: would he not agree that if the *shilya* were carried in a vessel into an outer chamber, that outer chamber would be *tahor*? The implication is that the act of moving it would cause the dissolved contents to fully dissolve further, eliminating the *tum'ah*. Rabbi Meir replies *aval* — yes, he concedes that point.

The colleagues press: if movement to another room causes full dissolution and thus *taharah*, why should the original room be any different? The jostling of transport would complete the dissolution. The answer is equally interesting: Rabbi Meir says the two cases are not comparable, because there is a difference between something dissolved *once* (*pa'am achas*) and dissolved *twice* (*shtei pe'amim*). In the original room, the fetus has begun to dissolve but retains enough form or integrity to still be legally "present"; transport and the additional agitation cause a second stage of dissolution that removes even that.

### Dissolution Stages

At this point the discussion within the Gemara undergoes a conceptual shift. Initially, the dispute in the Mishnah had appeared to be primarily factual: did the fetus dissolve while still in the womb (in which case it never conferred *tum'ah* upon emerging), or did it dissolve only after emerging (in which case it did)? But the *baraita*'s exchange between Rabbi Meir and his colleagues suggests something more halachic: even granting that the fetus was present when the *shilya* emerged, once dissolution is complete, the *tum'ah* ceases. The debate is not merely about timing but about the legal status of dissolved matter.

This sets the stage for the Gemara's deeper analysis.

Rav Hamnuna was delivering a *shiur* on this matter, and Rav Pappa, seated in the second row behind Rav Bibi, was following along. Rav Hamnuna asked: what is Rabbi Shimon's reason? And he answered: *kasavar kol tum'ah she-nisarvah bah mi-min acher betailah* — Rabbi Shimon holds that any *tum'ah* that becomes mixed with matter of another type is negated. It is not merely that the fetus has dissolved into nothingness; it is that the other liquids present in the *shilya* — the embryonic fluids and other contents of the placental sac — constitute a mixture, and the dissolved fetal matter is negated within that mixture.

## Rav Pappa's Lesson

### Three Sages Unite

Rav Pappa speaks up at this point to add a clarification: *hayinu nami ta'ama de-Rabbi Yehuda ve-Rabbi Yose* — this is also the reason of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose, the other two colleagues who ruled alongside Rabbi Shimon. The *baraita* had listed all three together, so of course they share the same reasoning. The other scholars laughed at him — *achichu alei* — considering it obvious: why state the reason of the other two *tannaim* separately when they had already been cited together with Rabbi Shimon? *Peshita* — self-evident.

### Speak Truth

Rav Pappa's response to being laughed at contains a memorable principle. He said: *afilu ki amilta le-vei inish velo nishka mei-rabbai* — even something that is obvious to others, one must say it, and one must not remain silent before one's *rebbe* out of embarrassment. The point Rav Pappa was making — that the reasoning articulated for Rabbi Shimon applies equally to Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose — was not necessarily self-evident. One might have thought the three sages held identical conclusions for different reasons. By not speaking up, a genuine clarification would have been lost.

Rav Pappa cites the verse: *im navalta be-hitnasei* — if you make yourself lowly in the pursuit of Torah learning, you will be elevated. Conversely, *im zamota yad la-feh* — if you are too proud to speak for fear of appearing foolish, in the end you will be silenced. The principle is the familiar one: *lo ha-bayshan lamed* — the bashful person does not learn. One must not allow concern for one's dignity to suppress genuine Torah clarification, even when the audience may find the point obvious and even when they respond with laughter.

## Decomposed Corpse

### Mixed Matter

The Gemara now pivots to an extended discussion of *rekav* — decomposed matter from a corpse — which will ultimately be brought to bear on the case of the dissolved fetus. The foundational *halacha* is that a *melo tarvad* (a full ladle-measure) of *rekav* causes *tum'ah* just as an intact corpse does, but only under extremely narrow and specific conditions. The Gemara is exploring the parallel between external matter mixed into *rekav* and external liquid mixed with a dissolved fetus.

The case: *melo tarvad rekav shenafal letocho afar kol shehu* — the prescribed minimum quantity of *rekav* into which the tiniest amount of external dirt has fallen. The *Rabbanan* rule it is still *tamei*; Rabbi Shimon rules it *tahor*.

Rava reports that he found the scholars of Rav's academy sitting and explaining Rabbi Shimon's position as follows: it is impossible, they argued, that two particles of dirt should not constitute a majority over one particle of *rekav* somewhere in the mixture. That is, if one takes any microscopic cross-section where the dirt is concentrated, the dirt particles outnumber the *rekav* particles at that location, negating a tiny portion of the *rekav* and reducing the total below the required minimum. The logic is subtle: since the *tum'ah* of *rekav* is predicated on having the full minimum amount, even a microscopic deficit caused by *bittul* in one local zone undermines the whole.

Rava, however, rejected this explanation outright: *adaraba* — on the contrary! If one looks at the mixture macroscopically rather than microscopically, the *rekav* will always constitute the majority over any small amount of external dirt. The dirt is negated into the *rekav*, not the other way around. The *rekav* in fact grows, not shrinks. The students' micro-level analysis inverts the normal application of *bittul b'rov*.

It is worth pausing here on the point raised by Tosafot, which illuminates the entire discussion. Normally, *tum'ah* from a corpse is caused by a *kezayit* of flesh, a bone of a certain size, or a limb. None of those categories apply to *rekav*. The only category that can encompass *rekav* is the *tum'ah* of a *meit shalem* — an entire corpse. But that means the *rekav* must be present in its complete minimum quantity. If even a tiny portion is negated — even halachically annulled, not physically removed — one no longer has a "whole corpse" in the relevant sense, and the *tum'ah* does not apply. This is why Tosafot's analysis is so powerful: the same logic will apply to the dissolved fetus, which also cannot be characterized as flesh or bone, only as a whole entity.

Rava then offers the true reason for Rabbi Shimon's position: *sofo ki-techilato* — the end follows the same law as the beginning. The *halacha* of *rekav* is extraordinarily restrictive from the outset: the body must be buried naked, in a marble casket or on a stone floor, with absolutely no external matter present during decomposition. This extreme restriction at the beginning, Rabbi Shimon holds, extends to the end as well: any external matter that becomes mixed in afterward likewise disqualifies the *rekav* from causing *tum'ah*. Just as the presence of a *ganguilon* — any external item (Rashi explains this as something like a belt, derived from a Latin term for a binding band) — at the time of burial prevents the *halacha* of *rekav* from ever taking effect, so too does any external matter mixed in after the fact.

### Marble Casket

The Gemara now cites the *baraita* that defines which corpse gives rise to *rekav* and which does not. A corpse buried naked in a marble casket, or placed naked on a stone floor — that is a corpse whose *rekav* causes *tum'ah*: *zeh meit sheyesh lo rekav*. A corpse buried in its shrouds, or in a wooden casket, or placed on a brick floor — any situation where organic or porous external material was present during decomposition — that is a corpse *she'ein lo rekav*, one that does not have this law.

An additional restriction: *velo amru rekav ela le-meit bilvad* — this *halacha* was stated only for a complete corpse, not for a murder victim. Rashi explains that a murder victim lacks all his blood, and the *halacha* requires the corpse to have been buried whole and complete. Tosafot notes a technical issue — the blood is not what becomes *rekav* in the relevant sense — but the *halacha lemoshe mi-sinai* requires completeness, and any deficiency prevents the law from applying.

The marble casket requirement also serves a practical function beyond the halachic one: it prevents insects and maggots from entering, which is presumably necessary for flesh to decompose into *rekav* rather than being consumed entirely. The question of where *rekav* physically comes from — given that in normal burial, insects consume the flesh and leave only bones — is real, and the requirement of a sealed marble environment addresses it.

### Cemetery Dirt

The second case in this *baraita* is striking: *melo tarvad ve'od afar beit ha-kevarot* — a measure of cemetery dirt, slightly more than the minimum, taken from a gravesite. The *Rabbanan* rule this *tamei*. Rabbi Shimon rules it *tahor*.

The *Rabbanan*'s reasoning: *lefi she'ee efshar le-melo tarvad ve'od afar beit ha-kevarot she'ein bo melo tarvad rekav* — it is impossible that such an amount of cemetery dirt not contain the full minimum measure of *rekav*. This is surprising, as one might ask: given how rare it is to achieve the conditions for valid *rekav* in the first place, how can one assume any random scoop of cemetery dirt contains the required amount? The *Rabbanan*'s position seems to be either a rabbinic extension applied as a fence around the law, or it presupposes a special case — as Tosafot suggests — where one has reason to know that a body buried under these specific conditions decomposed in this particular location. Rabbi Shimon, consistent with his principle, says that the presence of external earth mixed throughout the cemetery dirt disqualifies it from the status of *rekav* entirely.

## Fetus Dissolution

### Majority Negates

Having developed the principles around *rekav* and the role of external matter, the Gemara now draws the analogy back to the dissolved fetus in the *shilya*. *Hashta de'amarta ta'ama de-Rabbi Shimon mishum sofo ki-techilato* — now that we have explained Rabbi Shimon's reasoning in the *rekav* case as "the end follows the beginning," we must ask: *gabei shilya mai ta'ama* — what is his reason in the placenta case? The *shilya* is not governed by the special, restrictive *halacha* of *rekav*. Why should mixing with external liquid negate the *tum'ah* of the dissolved fetus?

And indeed, the logic cannot be the same: by our earlier analysis, if anything, the fetus is the majority within the *shilya* — the dissolved fetal matter would negate the other liquids, not the reverse. So one cannot say the fetus is simply nullified by a majority of external matter.

The Gemara therefore attributes to Rabbi Yochanan a distinct rationale: *mishum bittul b'rov nahagu bah* — the ruling follows the principle of *bittul b'rov*, and we must understand Rabbi Shimon as assuming that the majority of the contents of the placental sac consists of other liquids (embryonic fluid and the like), not of the dissolved fetus itself. In that case, the dissolved fetal matter is negated by the majority, loses its halachic identity, and the *tum'ah* does not apply.

### Animal Fetus

Rabbi Yochanan further maintains that Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov are expressing a fundamentally similar principle in different legal contexts — *amru davar echad*. Rabbi Shimon's ruling we have just established. Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's parallel case is found in a *Mishna* in Bechoros: if a large animal (*behemah gassa*) expelled a mass of blood (*chararas dam*), that mass is buried — because it may have been a miscarried firstborn, which cannot be used for benefit — and the animal is thereby exempted from *bechorah* for its next birth, since this may have been its *peter rechem*. Rabbi Chiya elaborates: the expelled mass does not cause *tum'ah* through contact or carrying.

The Gemara immediately seizes on the internal tension: if it does not cause *tum'ah*, why must it be buried? If it has no halachic status as a birth, there is no prohibition against deriving benefit from it, so burial would be unnecessary. The Gemara answers: *kedei lefarsem she-hi patur min ha-bechorah* — the burial is a public declaration. If the mass is simply discarded or used as fertilizer, people will assume it did not count as a birth, and they will treat the next calf as a firstborn requiring *kedushah*. The burial signals that this was treated as a birth. What we therefore observe is a remarkable halachic situation: the animal is considered to have given birth for purposes of *bechorah* (the next offspring is not a *bechor*), yet the thing it gave birth to has no independent status — it causes no *tum'ah* through contact or carrying.

### Birth Without Status

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *mishum bittul b'rov nahagu bo* — here too, as in the case of the *shilya*, the fetal matter is present but is negated by the majority of surrounding fluid. The birth has occurred — the *rechem* has opened, the *bechorah*-exemption is triggered — but the thing that emerged has lost its status as a fetus because of the majority of external matter surrounding it.

This is, as the Gemara itself makes clear, a genuinely counterintuitive result. A birth has occurred without there being, in halachic terms, anything that was born. The mother (or cow) bears the status of having given birth, but the product of that birth is halachically absent. The *tum'ah* accrues to the mother; nothing accrues to the fetus itself. Rabbi Ami then confirms that Rabbi Shimon himself would acknowledge (*modeh*) that the mother is *tme'ah leida* — precisely the conclusion that Rabbi Yochanan's framework generates.

### Verse Analysis

An elder elaborates on Rabbi Yochanan's position by anchoring it in a verse: *isha ki tazria veyalda zachar* — "if a woman gives forth seed and gives birth to a male." The textual inference is: *afilu lo yalda ela ke'ein she-hizria* — even if what she ultimately produces is only like the seed she emitted, i.e., something entirely liquefied, she is nonetheless *tme'ah leida*. The *tum'ah* of childbirth attaches to the mother based on the process — that a fetus was present and has now emerged, even in dissolved form — regardless of whether the product itself retains halachic substance. This verse-based rationale reinforces Rabbi Yochanan's framework and answers why the dissolution of the fetus does not retroactively eliminate the mother's *tum'ah*, even if it eliminates the independent *tum'ah* of the fetus.

### Form vs. Majority

Resh Lakish offers a different account of why, according to Rabbi Shimon, the dissolved fetus does not confer *tum'ah*. He does not invoke *bittul b'rov* at all. Instead: *shapir she-tarfuhu be-maymav na'asa ke-meit she-nitbalbela tzurato* — a fetus that has been scrambled together with its waters becomes like a corpse whose form has been entirely effaced. The operative concept is not majority but *tzurah* — form and appearance.

This reasoning connects naturally to the discussions earlier in the daf about animal-appearing fetuses, where the Gemara's rulings seemed to turn on whether the thing in question retained recognizable form. Resh Lakish's position is in some ways more intuitive than the majority-based analysis: why does a dissolved fetus not cause *tum'ah*? Because there is simply nothing here that looks like a corpse. The categories of *tum'ah* — flesh, bone, limb — all presuppose identifiable form. Even the category of *meit shalem* (Tosafot's framework) requires some coherent wholeness. When the form is entirely destroyed, none of these categories can apply, and it is not a technical *bittul* calculation but a fundamental loss of identity that produces this result.

### Burned Corpse

Rabbi Yochanan immediately challenges Resh Lakish: from where do we know that a corpse that has lost its form is *tahor*? He begins to introduce the ruling of Rav Shabtai in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak of Magdala (with a reverse attribution also given): *meit she-nisraf ve-shildo kayemet tamei* — if a corpse was burned but its *sheled* (outline or skeletal frame) remains intact, it is *tamei*. The question is what exactly *shildo kayemet* means: whether it refers to a charred body that still holds its general shape, or to a situation where ashes remain in the form of an outline on a surface. In either case, the ruling that form preserved in the aftermath of burning still yields *tum'ah* speaks directly to Resh Lakish's claim that loss of form removes *tum'ah*.

The daf concludes with this debate open: Rabbi Yochanan holds that *bittul b'rov* — the negation of the fetal matter by a majority of surrounding fluid — is the mechanism; Resh Lakish holds that it is the loss of *tzurah*, of identifiable form, that removes the status. The question of what *meit she-nisraf ve-shildo kayemet* proves, and what it implies about the role of form versus majority, will be the opening question of the next daf.
