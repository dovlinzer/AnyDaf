# Menachot 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Semichah on Korbanot

### Communal & Individual

The daf begins at the bottom of 92b, where the Mishnah turns from the various questions of measure to the laws of semichah — the act of laying hands upon the korban. The Mishnah states: *Kol korbanos hayachid te'unin semichah* — all individual korbanot require semichah. We had already learned that communal korbanot are essentially two: the par for helam davar, when the beis din rules incorrectly, and then there is a debate as to whether the second is the goat brought for avodah zarah when beis din rules incorrectly, or the goat for azazel on Yom Kippur. The act of semichah on the se'ir hamishtale'ach is something we recall every Yom Kippur in the Mussaf avodah, though the question of whether it constitutes classic semichah turns on whether the kohanim themselves are considered part-owners of that korban.

The Mishnah now turns to individual korbanot, stating that all require semichah — the chatas, the asham, the olah, the shelamim — with the exception of the bechor, ma'aser, and pesach. This is a striking exception. The excluded three are obligatory korbanot, yet they fall into a kind of intermediate category: they are obligatory, but they are kodshim kalim. The chatas and asham, also obligatory, do require semichah. The free-will korbanot — olah and shelamim — also require it. Only these three fall between the cracks.

### Mishnah Overview

The Gemara proceeds through a baraita: *Korbano* — the possessive form, "his korban" — is written three times in the context of semichah by shelamim, and each instance is understood as an exclusionary term. The Torah says *v'samach yado al rosh korbano* — he shall place his hand on the head of his korban — and the possessive ו signals that it refers to some offerings but not all.

From the first *korbano*: not the bechor. From the second: not ma'aser. From the third: not the pesach.

### Textual Exclusions

Before accepting the exclusions, the baraita presents kal vachomer arguments that would have argued for including each of these three. Regarding the bechor: since shelamim — which do not possess intrinsic sanctity from the womb — require semichah, the bechor, whose sanctity begins from the moment of birth without any act of designation, all the more so should require semichah. The Torah therefore writes *korbano* to exclude it.

Regarding ma'aser: shelamim do not sanctify the animals before and after them in the counting, yet require semichah. The ma'aser, however, does sanctify the animals on either side of it — if the counter errs and designates number nine or number eleven as the tenth, those animals also acquire the sanctity of ma'aser. Given this greater sanctifying power, one might have thought ma'aser should certainly require semichah. The Torah therefore writes *korbano* again to exclude it.

Regarding the pesach: shelamim, which are a free-will offering, require semichah. The pesach, which is obligatory — *b'amud v'haya* — all the more so should require semichah. Yet the Torah writes *korbano* a third time to exclude it.

### Kal Vachomer

The logic of the exclusions calls for explanation. It is not simply a matter of obligatory versus free-will korbanot, because semichah is performed on the chatas and asham, which are obligatory. Indeed, the entire act of semichah on Yom Kippur — where the kohen lays his hands on the korban and recites the viduy — is the paradigmatic example of semichah on an obligatory offering. So what is the principle?

Rashi, in a very compressed comment near the bottom of the page, explains: *korbano* — written three times in the context of semichah by shelamim — teaches that semichah applies to a korban *she'ba l'shum ritzui v'doron*, one that comes as an expression of appeasement or as a gift to God. The bechor, ma'aser, and pesach are excluded because *she'einam ba'im l'ritzui* — they are not coming as voluntary expressions of relationship — *ela chova heim alav* — they are pure obligations — and furthermore *v'lo l'kapara* — they do not serve an atoning function.

This points toward the deeper meaning of semichah. One might initially have thought that semichah is simply a formal act of identification — placing one's hands on the animal to establish that this is one's korban. But if that were its entire purpose, there would be no reason ever to exempt anything, since one always wishes to identify the korban as one's own. The fact that there are exemptions suggests that semichah is conceptualized differently.

What accompanies semichah by a chatas? Viduy — verbal confession. The act of laying hands while reciting the viduy is an act of designating the purpose of the korban: this animal is connected to my sins, it is being brought to atone for what I have done. That is the personal, specific dimension of semichah. By the shelamim and the olah, even when one is not atoning, there is something personal being expressed — one is choosing to give this offering, and the semichah moment is an opportunity to articulate what one wishes to express through the giving.

The bechor, ma'aser, and pesach fall into neither category. They are not atoning offerings where one could confess specific sins. Nor are they chosen gifts where one expresses something personal. Every Jew is obligated to give these. One does what the Torah commands, but there is no specific personal dimension to it. This, it seems, is the logic underlying Rashi's formulation.

### Ma'aser Logic

Although this understanding does not appear explicitly in the Gemara, the Rambam develops a strikingly parallel idea. He writes *yeira'eh li* — it would seem to me — that when bringing a shelamim one should also recite a verbal declaration. This is remarkable: the Rambam is introducing, without direct Talmudic backing, the principle that every korban requires some form of verbal declaration at the moment of semichah to designate its function.

What precisely does one say? For a chatas or asham, the Rambam specifies: *Chatasi, avisi, pashati, v'asisi kach v'kach, v'chazarti b'teshuvah lefanecha, v'zu kaparasi* — I have sinned, transgressed, committed iniquity, done such-and-such, I have returned in teshuvah before You, and this is my atonement. Notably, the Rambam incorporates teshuvah into the formula of the korban, which is theologically significant. For the shelamim, he says *yeira'eh li* — it appears to me — that one does not recite viduy but does recite *divrei shevach*, words of praise and thanksgiving.

The Rambam's formulation strongly supports the understanding that semichah is fundamentally about designating the personal meaning and function of the korban. This is precisely what is lacking — and therefore not required — by bechor, ma'aser, and pesach. There is nothing personal to declare.

## Logic of Exclusions

### Pesach Exclusion

The Gemara now raises a challenge to the entire structure of the kal vachomer exclusions. One could ask: why was there ever a basis to apply semichah to the bechor, ma'aser, and pesach in the first place? These korbanot cannot simply be extended from shelamim, since shelamim is distinctively different — it requires nesachim, the wine libation and mincha, and the waving of the chazeh and shok. Perhaps semichah was always unique to shelamim and those korbanot explicitly included alongside it, and there was never a basis for extending it to the others.

The Gemara accepts this point: *kra asmachto be'alma* — the verses excluding bechor, ma'aser, and pesach are merely asmachta; one would never have thought to apply semichah to them from a kal vachomer in the first place. If so, what work is the word *korbano* actually doing?

### Rashi's View

The Gemara answers: *Korbano velo korban chaveiro* — his korban and not the korban of another. One may not perform semichah on someone else's korban; the act must be performed by the owner on his own offering. Additionally, *korbano velo korban oved kochavim* — a non-Jew's korban does not require semichah. As is noted, this is not entirely distinct from the exclusion of *korban chaveiro*, since the assumption that a non-Jew does not enter the Azara — though this is only a rabbinic restriction — would mean that any non-Jew's korban would necessarily be brought by someone else, making the problem of performing semichah on another's korban automatic. The separate derivation is nevertheless needed according to the logic of the Gemara.

### Viduy & Declaration

A third use of *korbano* is the phrase *korbano lerabot kol ba'alei korban lisemichah* — if a korban is owned in partnership, the question of semichah becomes complex. When two people jointly bring an olah or shelamim — though one cannot bring a chatas in partnership — how does semichah work? This phrase is ambiguous. It could mean that any one partner may perform semichah on behalf of the group, analogous to what we saw with the kohen gadol performing semichah on behalf of all of Klal Yisrael by the se'ir hamishtale'ach. Or it could mean the opposite: no individual can represent the group, and all partners must perform semichah themselves. The Gemara will clarify this on 93b.

## Korbano Exclusions

### Question Raised

The Mishnah continues: *hayoresh somech* — an heir who inherits a korban may perform semichah on it. The scenario is one where, for example, a person's father died and left him a sanctified animal. By a chatas this question does not arise, since if the owner dies the chatas is left to die. But by an asham, if the owner dies and the animal had been set aside to graze until it develops a blemish, and it was then inadvertently slaughtered, it functions as an olah — and on an olah semichah is performed. The Mishnah rules that in such cases the heir performs semichah.

### Asmachta Only

Rabbi Chanina taught a version of this teaching before Rava with the opposite ruling: *yoresh eino somech, yoresh eino meimir* — an heir does not perform semichah, and an heir cannot make temurah. Rava challenged him: but our Mishnah explicitly says *hayoresh somech*! Rabbi Chanina asked: should I reverse my version? Rava replied: no — do not reverse it. Your version represents the position of Rabbi Yehudah, and the two texts represent a genuine dispute between tanna'im.

The baraita itself is explicit: the first opinion holds that an heir performs semichah and makes temurah; Rabbi Yehudah says the heir does neither.

### Korbano Teachings

What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehudah? He learns from the same word *korbano* — his korban, not his father's korban. The heir has inherited the animal as property, but the korban does not fully belong to him in the relevant sense. It is not the korban he sanctified; it is not connected to any personal obligation of his own. He therefore lacks sufficient connection to perform semichah, which is fundamentally about personal relationship to the offering.

Rabbi Yehudah then adds a structural argument: *u'tchilat hekdesh misof hekdesh* — we learn the beginning of the process of kedushah from its end. The end of the process is semichah, and we have established that an heir cannot perform semichah. Therefore, at the beginning of the process — the act of transferring kedushah through temurah — the heir likewise cannot do so. The heir's inability to perform semichah reflects his insufficient connection to the kedushah of the korban, and that same insufficiency means his attempt to make temurah is ineffective.

### Heir Dispute

Tosafot raises an objection: there are many ways in which the beginning and end of the process of kedushah differ, so how can we simply derive one from the other? Tosafot answers that this is not an arbitrary structural parallel. The point is not merely that what applies at the end applies at the beginning or vice versa. Rather, the question being asked in both cases is identical: are you considered the owner of this korban in the relevant sense?

This is the heart of the matter. The heir has inherited a piece of property. In monetary terms, he is the owner. But the kedushah of this animal — its sanctity as a korban, its identity as a designated offering — is something he did not create and to which he has no intrinsic link. The question of whether the heir can perform semichah and the question of whether his temurah is effective are both expressions of the same underlying question: does the heir's proprietorship over the animal extend to its status as a korban? Rabbi Yehudah says no; the chachamim say yes.

### R. Yehudah View

The chachamim derive their position differently. The Torah uses the double language *hemir yamir* — if he shall make temurah — and this amplified formulation is understood as inclusive, extending to include even the heir: *lerabot et hayoresh*. If the heir can make temurah, we reverse the direction of the learning: *v'yalif sof hekdesh mi'tchilat hekdesh* — we learn the end of the process from the beginning. Since at the beginning of the process the heir counts as an owner sufficient to make temurah, so too at the end of the process — at semichah — the heir performs semichah.

For Rabbi Yehudah's part, the double language of *hemir yamir* is needed for a different purpose entirely. Since Rabbi Yehudah does not learn from it that the heir can make temurah, he must use that language to teach the inclusion of women in the laws of temurah — as the Gemara will now explain.

## Heir Debate

### Tchilat from Sof

The entire parshah of temurah is written in the masculine. One might therefore have thought that women are excluded from the prohibition and its effects — that if a woman attempts to make temurah, it would neither be prohibited nor effective.

### Ownership Concept

The Gemara brings a baraita that asks directly: since the entire subject is discussed in masculine language, how do we include women? The answer is the double language *hemir yamir* — according to Rabbi Yehudah, it is this amplified formulation that includes women. The chachamim, however, derive the inclusion of women from *ve'im*, the word "if" that introduces the verse — the additional letter ו serving as a ribui to include women.

### Rabbis Counter

This raises an obvious question: the Torah almost universally uses masculine language, and we routinely assume women are included unless explicitly excluded. Why would we need a special derivation here to include women in temurah?

Tosafot addresses this by citing the Gemara in Temurah: the reason is that temurah is not universally applicable. There is no temurah by communal korbanot, and there is no temurah by korbanot owned in partnership. Since the prohibition and its effects are not applied universally across all cases, one might have been inclined to read the masculine language more restrictively — perhaps this too is a case where it does not apply, and women are excluded. The double language or the word *ve'im* therefore comes to explicitly include women, establishing that even though temurah is not universal, when a woman attempts it, she is prohibited and the act takes effect.

## Who Does Semichah

### Verse Resolution

The Mishnah turns from which korbanot require semichah to who may perform it. The list of those excluded is: the cheresh, shoteh, and katan; the blind person; the non-Jew; the slave; the agent; and the woman. The Mishnah then adds several important qualifications: semichah is *she'yarei mitzvah* — the remnant of the mitzvah, meaning that its omission does not disqualify the korban. It must be performed *al harosh*, on the head of the animal; with both hands; in the place where the shechita will occur; and the shechita must immediately follow.

### Women & Temurah

The Gemara identifies the underlying principle uniting the exclusions: *korbano velo korban chaveiro* — it must be his own korban, not another's. The entire list — slave, agent, woman — is read by the Gemara as referring not to these individuals performing semichah on their own korbanot, but to whether any of them may serve as a substitute for the owner, performing semichah on the owner's behalf. The answer in every case is no. The personal connection between the owner and the korban is what semichah expresses, and that cannot be delegated.

### Mishnah List

The Gemara begins with the cheresh, shoteh, and katan: *bilshama Cheresh Shoteh ve'Katan de'lav bnei de'ah ninhu* — these are not people of intellect, and therefore their exclusion is self-evident. This, however, raises a deeper question. If semichah were merely a formal ritual act — analogous to, say, placing an object — there would be no obvious reason to disqualify those lacking mental capacity, provided the physical act was performed. The fact that they are excluded points precisely to semichah being something more. The connection being forged between the person and the korban requires mental engagement; it is not a bare physical gesture.

This is illustrated by considering the scenario where someone sanctifies a korban and then loses mental capacity. Even if the korban could theoretically be brought in that state, semichah would not be appropriate — not because the person is exempt from mitzvot in that state, but because semichah is about an active, conscious connection to the korban. The question of whether a korban brought on behalf of a mentally incapacitated person achieves kapparah in some technical sense is a separate and interesting one, but semichah in any case requires the person to be *bnei de'ah*. The Gemara takes this for granted.

### Shiyrei Mitzvah

The non-Jew's exclusion from semichah is derived from the verse at the opening of Vayikra — *Daber el Bnei Yisrael* — which restricts the obligation of semichah to the children of Israel: *Bnei Yisrael somchin ve'ein ovdei kochavim somchin*.

### Mental Capacity

The exclusion of the blind person requires derivation, and two amoraim dispute its source. One holds: *atya semicha semicha mi'Ziknei ha'Edah* — we derive the law of semichah by a gezeirah shavah from the elders who perform semichah by the par helam davar. Since members of the Sanhedrin cannot be blind, and those elders perform semichah, we apply that restriction to semichah performed by individuals.

The other holds: *atya semicha semicha mi'Olat Re'iyah* — we derive it from the korban of the olat re'iyah, the obligatory olah brought on the three regalim. The Gemara there makes the famous drasha: *yireh yira'eh* — just as it says you shall appear before God, so too you must be able to see. *Keshem sheba'im leira'ot kach ba'im lirot* — just as they come to be seen, so they come to see. One who is blind, even in one eye, is exempt from olat re'iyah, and therefore exempt from the semichah associated with it. By extension, this restriction on semichah applies universally.

It is worth noting that the simple reading of the pasuk is actually *yireh et pnei Hashem Elokecha*, where *et* marks the object of the verb — meaning the person does the seeing of God's presence. The more anthropomorphic implication prompted the traditional reading as *yira'eh* — he shall appear. The drasha in a sense restores the plain meaning: the verse really does speak of the person seeing, and that requires sight.

The Gemara then explains the reasoning of each position. The one who learns from the olat re'iyah prefers it because *danin yachid miyachid* — we prefer to derive laws governing individual korbanot from other individual korbanot rather than from the communal par helam davar. The one who learns from the elders of the Sanhedrin prefers it because the par helam davar explicitly mentions semichah in the text itself, whereas the olat re'iyah never explicitly mentions semichah — it must first be derived that semichah applies to olat re'iyah at all (from the verse *va'ya'aseh ka'mishpat* — he did it according to the law of the free-will olah), making it an indirect source for a law about semichah. The elders source, by contrast, speaks of semichah directly.

Tosafot notes a difficulty: in neither the case of the Sanhedrin nor the case of olat re'iyah do we derive any other legal criteria from these contexts. We do not require that those performing semichah be eligible for Sanhedrin membership in other respects, nor do we extend all the criteria of olat re'iyah to all semichah. Only this one criterion — vision — is derived. Tosafot does not offer a fully satisfying resolution.

## Agent & Wife

### Blind Exclusion

The Gemara introduces a baraita that derives the exclusion of the slave, the agent, and the wife from the word *yado* — his hand. Three repetitions of *yado* in the context of semichah exclude each of these three candidates for substitution, and each exclusion requires its own derivation because each presents a distinct argument for why it might have been permitted.

### Non-Jew & Slave

*Yado* — his hand, and not the hand of his slave. A Canaanite slave is not obligated in positive time-bound commandments and is therefore not fully a bar mitzvah in the relevant sense. One who is not personally obligated in a mitzvah cannot discharge another's obligation through it. The slave therefore cannot stand in for his master.

### Shaliach Principle

*Yado* — his hand, and not the hand of his agent. Here the argument for inclusion is far stronger: the agent is a fully obligated Jew, and we have the general principle *shlucho shel adam kemoto* — a person's agent is like the person himself. One might have thought that precisely here, where we need a personal connection to the korban, the agent — who is legally the alter ego of the principal — could serve that function. The Torah therefore requires an explicit exclusion: even an agent cannot perform semichah in place of the owner.

### Wife Excluded

*Yado* — his hand, and not the hand of his wife. The argument for including the wife is even stronger than that for the agent. We sometimes speak of the wife as *kegufo damya* — as though she is his very body. If anyone might be considered an extension of the person himself for purposes of performing a deeply personal act like semichah, it would be his wife. The Torah nonetheless excludes her as well.

### Woman's Own Korban

The Gemara concludes that all three exclusions are necessary: each presents an independent argument for inclusion that the others do not. The underlying principle is absolute: *ani velo hashaliach* — I, and not my agent. Semichah must be performed by the owner himself, directly and without intermediary. No matter how close the relationship — slave, agent, or wife — the connection must be the owner's own, unmediated connection to his korban.

## Semichah & Kaparah

### Verse Analysis

The Mishnah describes semichah as *she'yarei mitzvah* — the remnant of the mitzvah. This phrase, which the Gemara also uses in connection with tenufah and similar acts, conveys that while semichah is a genuine and important mitzvah, its omission does not disqualify the korban. *Be'dieved*, the korban still achieves its purpose.

### Blood & Semichah

The baraita in the Gemara probes the verse *v'samach v'nirtzah* — he shall perform semichah and it shall be accepted for him. Does semichah itself achieve the atonement? The Gemara answers: *v'halo ein kaparah ela b'dam* — atonement is achieved only through the blood, as it is written *ki hadam hu banefesh yechaper*. Semichah alone is not the vehicle of kapparah. Rather, as the Gemara explains, *ela lomar lecha* — the verse teaches that if one treated the semichah as a mere remnant of the mitzvah — that is, if one omitted it — the Torah regards it as though no atonement occurred.

### Hollow Atonement

This is a subtle but important distinction. The formal statement is that omitting semichah is treated *k'ilu lo kiper* — as if atonement was not achieved. Yet the Gemara also says *v'chiper* — technically, the bare atonement does occur. The korban is not invalidated. There are thus two levels: the full and intended atonement, which is bound up with the entire experience of semichah and its accompanying viduy; and the bare technical atonement, which is achieved by the blood even when semichah was not performed.

### Tenufah Parallel

The Gemara notes an identical formulation in the laws of tenufah, the waving of the chazeh and shok. The verse says *u'tenufah l'chaper* — the waving achieves atonement — and the same analysis applies: obviously only blood achieves atonement, so the verse must mean that if tenufah was treated as a mere remnant and omitted, it is as if no atonement occurred, though technically the korban still achieves bare atonement.

It is worth pausing to note that this parallel is somewhat surprising. Semichah, understood as the moment of personal identification and purpose-designation for the korban, carries enormous weight in the experience of offering. The tenufah — the presenting of the chazeh and shok — is important but does not carry the same central symbolic weight; it is not the defining act of the korban but rather a secondary element connected to the priestly portions. Yet the Gemara gives them precisely equal weight in terms of their status as *she'yarei mitzvah*. This sheds light on how seriously the tenufah is regarded as part of the full korban experience, even if it is not the korban's essential act.

## Semichah Details

### On Head

The Mishnah states *al harosh* — semichah must be performed on the head of the animal. The Gemara elaborates through a baraita that excludes several alternative locations.

### Sides Query

The baraita specifies: *yado al harosh v'lo yado al hatzavar* — on the head and not on the neck; *v'lo al hagabayim* — not on the back; *v'lo yado al hachazeh* — not on the chest. The Gemara asks why all three need to be excluded separately. Each exclusion addresses a different argument that might have permitted the alternative location.

Had the Torah said only *al harosh*, one might have thought it excludes the neck because the neck is not at the same plane as the head. The back, however — particularly the upper back of the neck — might have been thought to be an extension of the head and at the same plane, and therefore acceptable. A second exclusion is therefore needed for the back. And had only those two been excluded, one might have thought the chest — which is waved during the tenufah of certain korbanot — has a special elevated status and might therefore be a valid location for semichah as well. The third exclusion addresses this possibility.

### Both Hands

A further question is raised: *yado al hatzdadim mahu* — what about the sides of the head, meaning the cheeks? Do they qualify as part of the head for purposes of semichah? The Gemara resolves this from a baraita in which Abba Bira in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov states: *yado al harosh v'lo yado al hatzdadim* — on the head and not on the sides. The cheeks are excluded.

### R. Elazar Story

Rabbi Yirmiyah raises a further question: if a cloth or rag intervenes between the person's hands and the animal's head, does that constitute a valid semichah, or does it constitute a chatzitzah that invalidates the act? The answer comes from a baraita: *uvilevad shelo tehei davar chotzetz beino levein hazavach* — there must not be any intervening object between the person and the animal. The hands must touch the animal's head directly.

### Location & Timing

### Reish Lakish's Derivation: Yado in Singular Context of Semichah Means Two Hands

The Mishnah states that semichah requires both hands. Reish Lakish derives this from the verse concerning Yom Kippur: *v'samach Aharon et shtei yadav* — and Aaron shall place his two hands. The word is written *yado* in the singular, yet the full phrase is *shtei yadav* — two hands. Reish Lakish establishes a paradigm: *kol makom shene'emar yado harei kan shtayim* — wherever the Torah writes *yado* in the context of semichah, it means both hands, until the Torah explicitly specifies one hand. This is a substantial chiddush: the verse governing semichah says *v'samach yado* — his hand, singular — and from this alone one would never infer two hands. Reish Lakish's drasha reverses the intuition.

### The Story of Rabbi Elazar: Taking Credit Without Attribution

Rabbi Elazar went and taught this drasha in the beit midrash without attributing it to Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish heard of this and was angered. In response, he confronted Rabbi Elazar: if every instance of *yado* in the singular means two hands, why does the Torah ever use *yadav* in the plural? He challenged him twenty-four times the Torah uses the plural form — *yadav tevi'ena*, *yadav rav lo*, *shikel et yadav* — and why would any of those have been necessary if *yado* already implies two? Rabbi Elazar had no answer and was silent.

### Reish Lakish's Challenge: Why Does Torah Ever Use Yadav in Plural?

After Reish Lakish calmed down, he told Rabbi Elazar what he should have said: *yada d'semichah ka'amri* — my drasha applies only to semichah. It is not a universal principle that *yado* means two wherever it appears. Rather, in the specific context of semichah on animals, *yado* means two hands. This is why the Torah never needs to say *yadav* in the context of semichah on animals — *yado* is sufficient to indicate two.

### Resolution: The Drasha Applies Only to Semichah on Animals, Not Universally

The Gemara raises a further challenge: but even in the context of semichah the Torah does write *yadav* in the plural — *vayismoch et yadav alav vayitzaveihu*, when Moshe performs semichah on Yehoshua. Reish Lakish narrows the drasha still further: *semicha d'behema ka'amri* — the principle applies only to semichah performed on animals. When it is semichah on a person, the Torah does use the plural. Within the specific context of semichah on animal korbanot, however, *yado* in the singular means both hands, and the verse never needs to say *yadav* in that context.

### Semichah Shechita

### The Mishnah's Statement: Semichah Happens Where Shechita Happens

The Mishnah concludes: *uv'makom shesomchin shochtin* — in the place where semichah is performed, the shechita is performed. The Gemara asks what the Mishnah is teaching with this formulation — is it two separate rules or one?

### Immediate Succession: Shechita Follows Immediately After Semichah

The Gemara explains: *hachi ka'amar* — this is what the Mishnah means: you must perform semichah in the same location where the shechita will occur, and the reason is *shetechef l'semichah shechitah* — because the shechita must immediately follow the semichah. The location requirement and the timing requirement are therefore connected: since shechita must follow immediately, you perform semichah precisely where the shechita will take place, so that no movement of the animal is required after semichah.

### The Meaning: The Relationship Defined Through Semichah Immediately Translates to Offering

The juxtaposition of semichah and shechita is not merely procedural. Whatever relationship is being articulated through the semichah — the personal identification with the korban, the designation of its purpose, the connection of the owner to this offering — is immediately, without interruption, translated into the act of bringing the korban. The semichah defines the meaning of what is about to occur, and the shechita immediately actualizes it. The two acts together constitute a single continuous gesture: this is my korban, and now it is being offered.
