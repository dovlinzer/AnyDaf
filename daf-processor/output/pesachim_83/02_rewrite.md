# Pesachim 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psul Begufо vs. Bedamo

### Psul Begufо Principle

The daf opens with a review of the foundational principle governing the burning of invalid *korbanot*. When a *korban* becomes *pasul* for an intrinsic reason — a *psul begufo* — it is burned immediately. When the invalidity is extrinsic, characterized in a *baraita* as *bedamo uviva'alim* (relating to the blood or the owners), the burning is deferred until the following day.

### R. Yochanan ben Beroka

This principle was applied in the previous *mishnah* to the *korban pesach* whose owners have died, leaving no one to eat it. Although this would seem to be an extrinsic defect, and therefore one might expect the burning to be deferred to the sixteenth, Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka maintains that even this constitutes a sufficiently intrinsic problem that the *pesach* is burned immediately. According to Rabbi Yochanan in the *Gemara*, this applies even when the owners died after the blood was sprinkled — as long as the meat has no one to eat it, that is treated as an intrinsic defect requiring immediate burning.

### Rava's Connection

The *Gemara* previously connected the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka to that of Rabbi Nechemiah, who articulated a similar principle. The daf now opens with Rava drawing a further connection — *Rava mosif af Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili* — identifying yet a third *tanna* who shares this approach.

---

## R. Yossi ha-Gelili

### Parim & Se'irim Nisrafim

Rava teaches that the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka — that even an extrinsic defect may necessitate immediate burning — reflects the view of Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili. As the *baraita* states: *Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili omer kol ha-inyan kulo eino medaber ella be-farim ha-nisrafim u-vi-se'irim ha-nisrafim lisrof psuleihem al beis ha-birah.*

The verse in Vayikra states: *ve-chol chatat asher yuva midamah le-chaper ba-kodesh lo te'achel ba-esh tisaref* — that a *chatat* whose blood is brought into the *kodesh*, into the *heichal*, may not be eaten but must be burned. The standard reading of this verse treats it as describing an invalidity: a *chatat* whose blood should have been applied on the outer *mizbe'ach* but was instead brought inside, thereby becoming *pasul*. Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili, however, reads the verse entirely differently. He maintains that the entire passage speaks of *chatat* offerings whose blood is *supposed* to be brought inside — namely the *parim ha-nisrafim* and *se'irim ha-nisrafim*, such as the *par* brought on Yom Kippur or the *par he'elem davar* brought when the *beis din* issues an erroneous ruling.

### Kohen Gadol's Par

This requires a brief review of the relevant *halacha*. When the *korban* is performed correctly — as with the *par* of the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur, whose blood is sprinkled inside the *heichal* — the carcass is taken and burned *el michutz la-machaneh*, outside the precincts of the *Beis ha-Mikdash*, as the verse states: *ve-et par ha-chatat ve-et se'ir ha-chatat asher huva et damam le-chaper ba-kodesh yotzi el michutz la-machaneh ve-sarfu va-esh et orotam ve-et besaram ve-et pirsham.*

Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili reads the verse in Vayikra as addressing precisely these *korbanot*, but teaching what is done when such a *korban* becomes *pasul*. Ironically, when performed correctly, the carcass is burned outside the *Beis ha-Mikdash*; but if something goes wrong and the *korban* is invalidated, it is burned *bakodesh* — within the grounds of the *Beis ha-Mikdash* itself. This is what Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili derives from the verse: *lisrof psuleihem al beis ha-birah*. The verse also establishes a *lo ta'aseh* against eating the meat of such offerings.

### Gemara's Challenge

The *Gemara* presses Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili: on your reading, the verse is concerned only with *parim ha-nisrafim* and *se'irim ha-nisrafim*. How then do we know that a normal *chatat* — one whose blood should have been applied on the outer *mizbe'ach* — becomes *pasul* if its blood is brought inside the *heichal*? This is the ruling that the broader tradition derives from this very verse. What is Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili's source for it?

### Aaron's Story

Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili responds by invoking the narrative of Aharon. The verse says: *hein lo huva et damah el ha-kodesh pnimah* — Moshe challenged Aharon, asking why he had burned the *chatat* rather than eating it, noting that its blood had not been brought inside. The *Gemara* infers: *michlal de'ee nafik hee... ee nami ayil dama, bisreifah* — from the very framing of Moshe's question, we derive that had the blood been brought inside, the *korban* would have been burned. Thus the invalidity of a normal *chatat* whose blood is brought inside, along with the requirement to burn it, is derived from this episode.

At this point, the *Gemara* returns to the original purpose of Rava's argument. The connection to Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili is: if even a defect relating to the blood — which might seem extrinsic — results in immediate burning (as inferred from the story of Aharon), then we see that Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili would treat even extrinsic problems as triggering immediate burning. That is the link to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who holds the same for a *pesach* without owners.

### Dam u'Vasar vs. Ba'alim

The *Gemara* then explains why Rabbi Yochanan himself declined to make this connection. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *dam u-vasar chada milsa hi, ba'alim milsa achrisei hi* — the blood and the flesh of a *korban* are one integrated matter, intrinsic to the physical reality of the *korban* itself, and a defect in either is therefore an intrinsic *psul*. A defect relating to the owners, by contrast, is genuinely extrinsic — it is a problem with the circumstances surrounding the *korban*, not with the *korban* itself. The story of Aharon and the burning of the *chatat* whose blood was brought inside is therefore no proof about a *korban* whose owners have died; those are categorically distinct situations. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka and the *chachamim* thus turns specifically on the case of the owners — the *ba'alim* — and whether that extrinsic defect suffices to require immediate burning or merely deferred burning.

## Atzmos, Gidin, Notar

### Mishnah Listing

The *mishnah* states: *ha-atzamos ve-ha-gidin ve-ha-notar yisrafu be-shishah asar* — the bones, the sinews, and the leftover meat of the *pesach* are burned on the sixteenth. Leftover meat (*notar*) is not meat that became *pasul* for some external reason, but rather the meat of a validly offered *korban* that was simply not consumed. The bones require burning because they contain marrow; since the *korban pesach* carries a prohibition against breaking its bones, the marrow cannot be extracted and burned separately. The sinews similarly require burning, as we will discuss. All of this is done on the sixteenth because burning cannot take place on the fifteenth, which is *Yom Tov*.

---

## Notar on Shabbat

### Sixteenth as Shabbat

The *mishnah* continues: *chal tet-zayin lihiyot be-Shabbos, yisrafu be-shiva-asar* — if the sixteenth falls on Shabbos, the burning is deferred to the seventeenth. In this scenario, the *pesach* was *notar* on the fifteenth, but one cannot burn on *Yom Tov*, so the burning is scheduled for the sixteenth; if the sixteenth is Shabbos, the burning must wait until the seventeenth.

### No Override Rule

The *mishnah* gives the reason: *lefi she'ein dochin lo et ha-Shabbos ve-lo et ha-Yom Tov* — burning *kodashim* does not override either Shabbos or *Yom Tov*. Even though burning *notar* is a *mitzvah*, it is insufficient to displace either of these days, as the *Gemara* will elaborate.

## Atzmos, Gidin, Notar (II)

### Rav Mari & Rav Yitzchak

The *Gemara* opens with a teaching: *Rav Mari bar Avuha amar Rav Yitzchak: atzmos kodshim she'shimshu notar metamin et ha-yadayim* — bones of *kodashim* that served *notar* convey *tumah* to the hands. The Rabbis instituted that *notar* meat imparts *tumah* of the hands as a measure to prompt *kohanim* to eat the meat before it expired: since *kohanim* were scrupulously careful about matters of *tumah*, attaching *tumah* to *notar* would motivate them not to allow meat to become *notar* in the first place. Rav Mari bar Avuha teaches that bones which "served" the *notar* — specifically by containing marrow within them — likewise convey *tumah*, since they are considered a *basis le-davar ha-assur*, a base or vessel for something forbidden.

### Marrow Status

The *mishnah* establishes that bones are to be burned on the sixteenth. A question immediately arises: *hani atzamos heichi dami?* — what exactly is the case? If the bones contain no marrow, there is no obligation to burn them; they should simply be discarded, for there is nothing of the *korban* remaining in them — a bone without marrow has no more sanctity than a piece of wood. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *mishnah* must be discussing bones that contain marrow. The marrow is *notar*, and it is that marrow which must be burned — which is why the bones are addressed in the *mishnah*. This discussion has implications for broader questions, such as whether bones of animals that never contained marrow have any *halachic* status whatsoever.

### Shimush Notar Debate

The *Gemara* then raises a structural question. Given that we are clearly burning the bones because of the marrow, why must we burn the bones together with the marrow? Why not simply break open the bones, extract the marrow, burn the marrow, and discard the bones? The *Gemara* assumes that the prohibition against breaking bones no longer applies once the *pesach* has become *notar* — so by the morning of the fifteenth or sixteenth, there should be no obstacle to breaking them. The *Gemara* therefore suggests that burning the bones whole (rather than extracting and burning only the marrow) implies something about the status of the bones themselves: *shema mina shimush notar milta hi* — we may infer that the act of "serving" *notar* confers upon the bones a status equivalent to *notar*, requiring that they too be burned.

The *Gemara* then challenges this inference by reversing the logic: *le'olam eima lach shimush notar lav milta hi* — perhaps the bones do not acquire any status from having served *notar*, and the bones themselves genuinely have no obligation to be burned. The reason they cannot simply be broken open is the *halakhic* force of the verse *ve-etzem lo tishberu bo* — do not break a bone of the *pesach*. The word *bo* is read not as a limiting term, but as applying even after the *pesach* has become *pasul*: *bo be-chasher ve-afilu be-fasul*. The prohibition against breaking bones continues to apply even the next morning when the *pesach* is *notar*.

The *Gemara* immediately objects: a *mishnah* taught that if one breaks a bone of a *tamei pesach*, one does not receive lashes, implying the prohibition does not apply to an invalid *pesach*. The *Gemara* resolves this tension by distinguishing between *sha'at ha-kosher* and its absence: *kan she-haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher, kan she-lo haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher*. A *pesach* that was validly offered and only later became *notar* — it had its *sha'at ha-kosher*, its moment of validity — retains the bone-breaking prohibition even after it has become *pasul*. But a *pesach* that was *tamei* from the outset and was therefore never valid never had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, and the prohibition therefore never applied to it and does not apply now.

### Sheviras Etzem

With this distinction in hand, the *Gemara* explains why we cannot simply break open the bones and extract the marrow: by the morning of the fifteenth, the *pesach* has become *notar*, but because it once had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, the prohibition against breaking its bones continues to apply. The bones must therefore be burned whole, together with the marrow inside them.

### Sha'at ha-Kosher

The *Gemara* then identifies the *tanna* behind this distinction. *U-man tanna de-shani leh?* — who is the *tanna* who draws this distinction? It is Rabbi Yaakov. As the *baraita* teaches: the verse *ve-etzem lo tishberu bo* prohibits breaking a bone of a valid *pesach* but not an invalid one. Rabbi Yaakov adds: *haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher ve-nifsal, yesh bo* — if it once had a moment of validity and subsequently became *pasul*, the prohibition applies. *Lo haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher, ein bo mishum sheviras etzem* — if it was never valid, there is no prohibition of bone-breaking.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh, ein bo mishum sheviras etzem* — whether or not the *pesach* once had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, once it is now *pasul*, there is no prohibition of bone-breaking at all. On Rabbi Shimon's view, even the bones of a *pesach* that was validly offered may be broken freely the next morning when it is *notar*.

*Tosafot* makes a significant structural observation: Rabbi Yaakov and Rabbi Shimon are not independently disputing one another, but are each offering their interpretation of the *tanna kamma*. The *tanna kamma* stated only that the prohibition applies to a *kasher pesach* and not a *pasul* one. The debate between Rabbi Yaakov and Rabbi Shimon is over what constitutes *pasul*: Rabbi Yaakov holds that *pasul* means only those that were never valid, while Rabbi Shimon holds that any *pesach* currently *pasul* — regardless of its prior status — is excluded from the prohibition. This pattern, where later *tannaim* debate the meaning of an earlier ruling rather than independently asserting their own views, recurs throughout the *mishnah*, as seen for instance in the opening *mishnah* of *Pesachim* itself, where Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel debate the meaning of the earlier teaching about the two rows in a cellar.

### Bones Distinction

The *Gemara* now turns to a *baraita* that presents what seems to be a difficulty: *kol atzmos ha-kodshim ein te'unim sereifah* — all bones of other *kodashim* do not require burning — *chutz me-atzmos ha-pesach, mipnei ha-takalah* — except for the bones of the *pesach*, because of the potential for transgression. What does this mean?

The *Gemara* asks: what is the case regarding these bones of other *kodashim* that need not be burned? If they contain no marrow, there is no need to burn them in the first place — they would simply be discarded. So the *baraita* must be discussing bones that do contain, or once contained, marrow. Yet it says these bones of other *kodashim* need not be burned. If the principle of *shimush notar* — that serving *notar* confers *notar*-status upon the bones — were operative, why would these bones not also require burning?

This appears to challenge the conclusion that *shimush notar milta hi*. The *Gemara* resolves the challenge by explaining that the *baraita* is not discussing bones from which the marrow was removed after the *korban* became *notar*, but rather bones that were already stripped of their marrow when found. The question then becomes: when was that marrow removed?

### Stripped Bones

Two *amoraim* offer related explanations of this scenario.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: *kegon she-metzan chalutzin* — the case is one where the bones are found already stripped of their marrow. By bones of other *kodashim*, since there is no prohibition against breaking the bones, we may assume that the owners were careful to extract the marrow before the meat became *notar* — *kamei de-hava notar chalutzinhu* — and therefore the bones never served *notar*, and need not be burned: *velo havei shimush notar, velo ba'ei sereifah*. By the *pesach*, however, since breaking the bones is prohibited, the marrow could not have been removed before the *korban* expired; therefore, it is certain that the bones served *notar* before they were stripped, and they must be burned even now that they are hollow: *leva'tar de-hava notar hu de-chaltzinhu, ve-havu lehu shimush notar u-va'u sereifah*. This reading supports the principle that *shimush notar milta hi*.

Rav Zvid offers a somewhat different reading of the *baraita*. He explains: *kegon she-metzan tziburin tziburin* — the case is one where the bones are found in a large pile, and inspection of those on top reveals that they have been stripped of their marrow. By bones of other *kodashim*, since there is no prohibition against breaking the bones, one can assume not only that the stripped bones in hand had their marrow removed before *notar* set in, but one can further extrapolate that the same is true of the entire pile — *lekulu havei chalitz lehu ve-achul lehu* — and therefore none of the bones in the pile require burning. By the *pesach*, however, even if the bones on top were stripped, one cannot make a corresponding assumption about the rest of the pile — *dilma hani de-chaltzinhu u-le-hanach lo chaltzinhu* — since the prohibition against breaking bones would have made early extraction difficult or impossible, and the bones at the bottom of the pile may still contain *notar* marrow, requiring them to be burned.

On Rav Zvid's reading, the distinction between *pesach* and other *kodashim* does not rest on whether *shimush notar milta hi*, but on whether one can make a reliable assumption about the contents of stripped bones. The bones themselves need not be burned independently; only those that actually contain marrow need burning. The difference is simply evidentiary: for other *kodashim*, one may assume early extraction; for the *pesach*, one may not.

## Gidin & Gid Nasheh

### Gidin Classification

A general teaching is cited: *kol ha-gidin basar ninhu* — all sinews are classified as meat for *halachic* purposes, with one exception: *chutz mi-gidei tzavar*, the sinews of the neck, which are essentially inedible and are not counted as meat.

### Sinew Reference

The *mishnah* states that the *gidin* must also be burned on the sixteenth. The *Gemara* asks what kind of sinews the *mishnah* has in mind. If it refers to ordinary, edible sinews — *gidei basar* — then the *chiddush* is unclear: these are simply meat that was not eaten, and they are already subsumed under the general category of *notar*. There is no reason to list them separately. And if the *mishnah* refers to the sinews of the neck, which are inedible and not classified as meat, then on one hand one might argue that the *mishnah* is teaching that even these count as meat and must be burned; but on the other hand, if they are not really meat, why would they have any *notar* status at all?

The tension becomes sharp: for the *mishnah* to be making a *chiddush* by listing the sinews separately, it must be referring to sinews that are in some sense borderline — either nearly inedible but still technically meat, or technically permissible but practically uneaten. If it refers to ordinary sinews, the mention adds nothing. If it refers to the neck sinews, one must argue that even these are considered meat — which would be the *chiddush* — and that the obligation to burn them follows from their status as *notar*.

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda offers a different identification entirely: *lo nitzrecha ela le-gid ha-nashe* — the *mishnah* is speaking of the *gid ha-nashe*, the sciatic nerve, which is forbidden to eat. But if the *gid ha-nashe* is forbidden and cannot be eaten, why would there be an obligation to burn it as *notar*? The obligation to burn *notar* is linked to the *mitzvah* of eating the *korban*: one leaves over something that one was obligated to eat, and that leftover must be burned. The *gid ha-nashe* was never in the category of things to be eaten. Rav Chisda's answer is that the explanation follows Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *eino noheg ella be-achat* — the prohibition of the *gid ha-nashe* applies to only one of the two thighs. *Ve-ha-da'at machra'at shel yamin* — reason suggests it is the right thigh that is primary. Now, does Rabbi Yehuda mean that he has resolved the question and determined definitively that it is the right thigh? Or does he mean that while it is probably the right, he remains uncertain — in which case one would refrain from eating both the right and left *gidin* out of doubt? If Rabbi Yehuda retains a *safek* and therefore forbids both as a precaution, then one of the two *gidin* — the permissible one — could have been eaten but was not, and is therefore *notar* that must be burned. This would explain why the *mishnah* mentions the *gidin* as requiring burning.

### Mixed-Up Gidim

Rav Ika bar Chinena offers a related scenario: *kegon she-hukru le-vasof ve-nitarvu* — even if Rabbi Yehuda ultimately concludes with certainty that it is the right *gid* that is forbidden, the two *gidin* may have become mixed up. Once they are mixed up, one cannot identify which is which, and therefore both are left uneaten, with the permissible one becoming *notar* and requiring burning. This explains the *mishnah*'s listing of *gidin* as a case requiring burning even if one accepts that Rabbi Yehuda definitively identified the prohibited side.

### Shuman shel Gid

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading: *lo nitzrecha ella le-shumno shel gid ha-nashe* — the *mishnah* is not referring to the *gid* itself, but to the fat (*shuman*) surrounding the *gid ha-nashe*.

### Fat Status

As the *baraita* teaches: *shumno muttar* — the fat around the *gid ha-nashe* is technically permitted by Torah law. Nevertheless, *ve-Yisrael kedoshim hem ve-nohagim bo issur* — the Jewish people are holy and have adopted the practice of treating it as forbidden. This practice has the force of at least a rabbinic prohibition. The consequence is striking: since nobody eats this fat — though the Torah permits it — it necessarily remains as *notar* after the *pesach* meal, and the *mitzvah* to burn *notar* applies to it. The *mishnah* therefore lists *gidin* as requiring burning on the sixteenth because of the fat around the *gid ha-nashe* that was left over. There is a certain irony here: the *chumra* of treating permitted fat as forbidden results, in effect, in a *chumra de-ati lidei kulla* — everyone who eats a *korban pesach* inevitably transgresses the prohibition of *notar* with respect to this fat, since they cannot eat something they have undertaken not to eat.

### Outer vs. Inner Gid

Ravina proposes a final answer: *bechitzon* — the *mishnah* refers to the outer *gid*, and the explanation follows the ruling of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *de-bei gidin hein* — there are actually two sinews. The inner one, *pnimi ha-samuch la-etzem*, running along the bone, is biblically forbidden and one is liable for its consumption. The outer one, *chitzon ha-samuch la-basar*, closer to the meat and not running the length of the bone, is only rabbinically forbidden — *ein chayavin alav*. Because of this rabbinic prohibition, the outer *gid* is left uneaten, even though it is biblically classified as meat. It therefore becomes *notar* and must be burned, and this is what the *mishnah* is teaching. The same logic applies as in Rav Ashi's explanation: a rabbinic prohibition results in something biblically permissible being left uneaten and consequently requiring burning as *notar*.

## Notar on Shabbat (II)

### Chizkiya's Proof

The *Gemara* now turns to the question raised by the *mishnah*: if the sixteenth falls on Shabbos, why can the burning of *notar* not override the prohibition of *Yom Tov*? After all, this is a positive commandment — should the *aseh* of burning *notar* not displace the *lo ta'aseh* of prohibited labor on *Yom Tov*? This question presents no difficulty with respect to Shabbos, which combines a *lo ta'aseh* with a *lo ta'aseh* and the additional severity of *chiyuv misah*, but *Yom Tov* seems more vulnerable to the override argument.

Chizkiya, and likewise the *tanna* of the school of Chizkiya, derives the answer from the verse: *ve-lo sosiru mimenu ad boker, ve-ha-notar mimenu ad boker ba-eish tisrofu*. The verse first prohibits leaving the meat until morning, and then commands that whatever remains be burned in the morning. Why does the verse need to say *boker* a second time? The first *boker* already establishes the prohibition. *Liten lo boker sheini le-sereifaso* — the second mention of *boker* teaches that the burning is assigned to a subsequent morning: do not burn the *notar* on *Yom Tov* itself; give it another morning. The Torah explicitly delays the burning to avoid conflict with *Yom Tov*.

### Abaye's Approach

Abaye derives the answer from a different verse: *olas Shabbos be-Shabbato* — the *olah* of Shabbos is offered on its own day. The verse implies: *ve-lo olas chol be-Shabbos* — the weekday sacrifice cannot be completed on Shabbos. This principle extends equally to *Yom Tov*: *ve-lo olas chol be-Yom Tov*. Even on the *mizbe'ach* itself, fat from a *korban* slaughtered on the fourteenth cannot be burned on *Yom Tov* night — it is a weekday sacrifice being completed on *Yom Tov*, which is prohibited. If one may not even complete the offering of a valid *korban* on *Yom Tov*, how much more so may one not burn *notar*, which is already an invalid remnant of the *korban*. The *kal va-chomer* establishes that burning *notar* is not permitted on *Yom Tov*.

### Rava's Logic

Rava derives the answer from yet another verse: *hu levado ye'aseh lachem* — on *Yom Tov*, only that which is directly related to eating is permitted; *hu ve-lo machshirav* — but not preparatory acts such as chopping wood for a fire; *levado, ve-lo milah shelo bi-zmanah* — and "alone" teaches that even *milah* not performed on its proper day may not override *Yom Tov*, despite the fact that *milah* performed on its prescribed eighth day overrides even Shabbos. Rava's point is that the Torah explicitly teaches, through the word *levado*, that *Yom Tov* cannot be displaced by commandments that do not pertain directly to food preparation on *Yom Tov* itself — even *mitzvot* as significant as *milah*. If that is true of *milah*, it is certainly true of burning *notar*.

### Rav Ashi Final

Rav Ashi offers what is perhaps the most direct and structurally elegant answer: *shabbaton de-Yom Tov aseh hu* — the *shabbaton* quality of *Yom Tov* is itself a positive commandment. *Yom Tov* is therefore not merely a *lo ta'aseh* but a combination of *lo ta'aseh* and *aseh*. *Ve-ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh va-aseh* — a positive commandment can override a negative commandment standing alone, but it cannot override a negative commandment reinforced by a positive commandment. The *aseh* of burning *notar* therefore cannot displace *Yom Tov*, which carries both a prohibition against *melacha* and the positive requirement of *shabbaton* — rest. This is why *notar* may not be burned on *Yom Tov*, and *a fortiori* not on Shabbos.
