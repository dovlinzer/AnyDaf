# Pesachim 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psul Begufо vs. Bedamo

### Psul Begufо Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ — יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד. בַּדָּם וּבַבְּעָלִים — תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle:** In **all** cases in which **its disqualification is in itself, it should be burned immediately.** If the disqualification is **in the blood** of the offering or **in the owners, its form** should be left to **decay, and** then **it should be taken out to the place** designated for **burning.**

The daf opens with a review of the foundational principle governing the burning of invalid *korbanot*. When a *korban* becomes *pasul* for an intrinsic reason — a *psul begufo* — it is burned immediately. When the invalidity is extrinsic, characterized in a *baraita* as *bedamo uviva'alim* (relating to the blood or the owners), the burning is deferred until the following day.

### R. Yochanan ben Beroka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים אַחַר זְרִיקָה, דְּאִיתְחֲזִי בָּשָׂר לַאֲכִילָה. אֲבָל נִטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, דְּלָא אִיתְחֲזִי בָּשָׂר לַאֲכִילָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** This **dispute** is about a case in which **the owners became ritually impure after the sprinkling** of the blood, **because the meat was fit for eating** since all its services were completed in a valid way. **But** if **the owners became ritually impure before the sprinkling** of the blood, such that **the meat was not fit for eating** even for a moment, **all agree** that **it should be burned immediately.**

This principle was applied in the previous *mishnah* to the *korban pesach* whose owners have died, leaving no one to eat it. Although this would seem to be an extrinsic defect, and therefore one might expect the burning to be deferred to the sixteenth, Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka maintains that even this constitutes a sufficiently intrinsic problem that the *pesach* is burned immediately. According to Rabbi Yochanan in the *Gemara*, this applies even when the owners died after the blood was sprinkled — as long as the meat has no one to eat it, that is treated as an intrinsic defect requiring immediate burning.

### Rava's Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה נָמֵי מַחְלוֹקֶת. וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** a case in which the owners became ritually impure **after sprinkling** the blood **is** included in **the dispute** about whether decay of form is necessary. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan follows his** line of **reasoning, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka and Rabbi Neḥemya said the same thing.** The statement of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka is that which we said.**

The *Gemara* previously connected the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka to that of Rabbi Nechemiah, who articulated a similar principle. The daf now opens with Rava drawing a further connection — *Rava mosif af Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili* — identifying yet a third *tanna* who shares this approach.

## R. Yossi ha-Gelili

### Parim & Se'irim Nisrafim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה מוֹסִיף, אַף רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָעִנְיָן כּוּלּוֹ אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בְּפָרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּבִשְׂעִירִין הַנִּשְׂרָפִין, לִשְׂרוֹף פְּסוּלֵיהֶן אַבֵּית הַבִּירָה, וְלִיתֵּן ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה״ עַל אֲכִילָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba adds** that these tanna'im were not the only ones who maintain this opinion; **even Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** agreed with them, **as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: The entire matter** that is stated in the verse: "Any sin-offering from which some blood has been brought to the Tent of Meeting, to make atonement in the sacred place, shall not be eaten; it shall be burned in fire" (Leviticus 6:23) is not stated with regard to a regular sin-offering improperly brought inside the Sanctuary; rather, it **speaks only about bulls that are burned and goats that are burned.** These are unique sin-offerings, and the Torah states that their blood should be brought inside the Sanctuary. The verse is stated with regard to these sin-offerings both in order to command the Jewish people **to burn the disqualified ones in the place of the *bira*** on the Temple Mount **and** in order **to establish a prohibition with regard to their consumption.**

Rava teaches that the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka — that even an extrinsic defect may necessitate immediate burning — reflects the view of Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili. As the *baraita* states: *Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili omer kol ha-inyan kulo eino medaber ella be-farim ha-nisrafim u-vi-se'irim ha-nisrafim lisrof psuleihem al beis ha-birah.*

The verse in Vayikra states: *ve-chol chatat asher yuva midamah le-chaper ba-kodesh lo te'achel ba-esh tisaref* — that a *chatat* whose blood is brought into the *kodesh*, into the *heichal*, may not be eaten but must be burned. The standard reading of this verse treats it as describing an invalidity: a *chatat* whose blood should have been applied on the outer *mizbe'ach* but was instead brought inside, thereby becoming *pasul*. Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili, however, reads the verse entirely differently. He maintains that the entire passage speaks of *chatat* offerings whose blood is *supposed* to be brought inside — namely the *parim ha-nisrafim* and *se'irim ha-nisrafim*, such as the *par* brought on Yom Kippur or the *par he'elem davar* brought when the *beis din* issues an erroneous ruling.

### Kohen Gadol's Par

This requires a brief review of the relevant *halacha*. When the *korban* is performed correctly — as with the *par* of the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur, whose blood is sprinkled inside the *heichal* — the carcass is taken and burned *el michutz la-machaneh*, outside the precincts of the *Beis ha-Mikdash*, as the verse states: *ve-et par ha-chatat ve-et se'ir ha-chatat asher huva et damam le-chaper ba-kodesh yotzi el michutz la-machaneh ve-sarfu va-esh et orotam ve-et besaram ve-et pirsham.*

Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili reads the verse in Vayikra as addressing precisely these *korbanot*, but teaching what is done when such a *korban* becomes *pasul*. Ironically, when performed correctly, the carcass is burned outside the *Beis ha-Mikdash*; but if something goes wrong and the *korban* is invalidated, it is burned *bakodesh* — within the grounds of the *Beis ha-Mikdash* itself. This is what Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili derives from the verse: *lisrof psuleihem al beis ha-birah*. The verse also establishes a *lo ta'aseh* against eating the meat of such offerings.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: חַטָּאת שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמָהּ לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים מִנַּיִן? אָמַר לָהֶן: ״הֵן לֹא הוּבָא אֶת דָּמָהּ אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִי נָפֵיק אִיהִי, אִי נָמֵי עָיֵיל דָּמָהּ — בִּשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: If you expound the verse in this fashion, then with regard to a regular **sin-offering whose blood entered inside** the Sanctuary, **from where** is it derived that it must be burned? **He said to them:** It is derived from the verse: **"Behold, its blood was not brought into the Sanctuary within"** (Leviticus 10:18). This proves **by inference that if** the sin-offering **is taken out** of its permitted area, **or alternatively,** if **its blood enters** the Sanctuary, it must be **burned** immediately without waiting for decay of form.

The *Gemara* presses Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili: on your reading, the verse is concerned only with *parim ha-nisrafim* and *se'irim ha-nisrafim*. How then do we know that a normal *chatat* — one whose blood should have been applied on the outer *mizbe'ach* — becomes *pasul* if its blood is brought inside the *heichal*? This is the ruling that the broader tradition derives from this very verse. What is Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili's source for it?

### Aaron's Story

Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili responds by invoking the narrative of Aharon. The verse says: *hein lo huva et damah el ha-kodesh pnimah* — Moshe challenged Aharon, asking why he had burned the *chatat* rather than eating it, noting that its blood had not been brought inside. The *Gemara* infers: *michlal de'ee nafik hee... ee nami ayil dama, bisreifah* — from the very framing of Moshe's question, we derive that had the blood been brought inside, the *korban* would have been burned. Thus the invalidity of a normal *chatat* whose blood is brought inside, along with the requirement to burn it, is derived from this episode.

At this point, the *Gemara* returns to the original purpose of Rava's argument. The connection to Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili is: if even a defect relating to the blood — which might seem extrinsic — results in immediate burning (as inferred from the story of Aharon), then we see that Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili would treat even extrinsic problems as triggering immediate burning. That is the link to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who holds the same for a *pesach* without owners.

### Dam u'Vasar vs. Ba'alim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: דָּם וּבָשָׂר חֲדָא מִילְּתָא הִיא. בְּעָלִים מִלְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** did not include Rabbi Yosei HaGelili because Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds** that there is a difference between the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and those of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka and Rabbi Neḥemya. The reason for this difference is that impurity of **blood and meat,** about which Rabbi Yosei HaGelili was speaking, **is one matter,** and impurity of the blood can therefore be considered a disqualification in the animal itself, but disqualification of the **owners is a different matter.** Therefore, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili does not necessarily agree with the *tanna'im* who allow the offering to be burned immediately in the case of disqualification of the owners.

The *Gemara* then explains why Rabbi Yochanan himself declined to make this connection. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *dam u-vasar chada milsa hi, ba'alim milta achrisei hi* — the blood and the flesh of a *korban* are one integrated matter, intrinsic to the physical reality of the *korban* itself, and a defect in either is therefore an intrinsic *psul*. A defect relating to the owners, by contrast, is genuinely extrinsic — it is a problem with the circumstances surrounding the *korban*, not with the *korban* itself. The story of Aharon and the burning of the *chatat* whose blood was brought inside is therefore no proof about a *korban* whose owners have died; those are categorically distinct situations. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka and the *chachamim* thus turns specifically on the case of the owners — the *ba'alim* — and whether that extrinsic defect suffices to require immediate burning or merely deferred burning.

## Atzmos, Gidin, Notar

### Mishnah Listing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעֲצָמוֹת וְהַגִּידִין וְהַנּוֹתָר — יִשָּׂרְפוּ בְּשִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר. חָל שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת — יִשָּׂרְפוּ בְּשִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר. לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָן דּוֹחִין לֹא אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת וְלֹא אֶת יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The bones** of the Paschal lamb that contain edible marrow but cannot be eaten because it is prohibited to break the bones of the Paschal lamb; **and the sinews; and the leftover** meat **should** all **be burned on the sixteenth** of Nisan, immediately after the first day of the Festival. If **the sixteenth occurs on Shabbat, they should be burned on the seventeenth, because** the mitzva to burn **them does not override Shabbat or the Festival.** Therefore, they are burned on the first weekday.

The *mishnah* states: *ha-atzamos ve-ha-gidin ve-ha-notar yisrafu be-shishah asar* — the bones, the sinews, and the leftover meat of the *pesach* are burned on the sixteenth. Leftover meat (*notar*) is not meat that became *pasul* for some external reason, but rather the meat of a validly offered *korban* that was simply not consumed. The bones require burning because they contain marrow; since the *korban pesach* carries a prohibition against breaking its bones, the marrow cannot be extracted and burned separately. The sinews similarly require burning, as we will discuss. All of this is done on the sixteenth because burning cannot take place on the fifteenth, which is *Yom Tov*.

---

## Notar on Shabbat

### Sixteenth as Shabbat

The *mishnah* continues: *chal tet-zayin lihiyot be-Shabbos, yisrafu be-shiva-asar* — if the sixteenth falls on Shabbos, the burning is deferred to the seventeenth. In this scenario, the *pesach* was *notar* on the fifteenth, but one cannot burn on *Yom Tov*, so the burning is scheduled for the sixteenth; if the sixteenth is Shabbos, the burning must wait until the seventeenth.

### No Override Rule

The *mishnah* gives the reason: *lefi she'ein dochin lo et ha-Shabbos ve-lo et ha-Yom Tov* — burning *kodashim* does not override either Shabbos or *Yom Tov*. Even though burning *notar* is a *mitzvah*, it is insufficient to displace either of these days, as the *Gemara* will elaborate.

## Atzmos, Gidin, Notar…

### Rav Mari & Rav Yitzchak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב מָרִי בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: עַצְמוֹת קָדָשִׁים שֶׁשִּׁימְּשׁוּ נוֹתָר — מְטַמְּאִין אֶת הַיָּדַיִם, הוֹאִיל וְנַעֲשָׂה בָּסִיס לְדָבָר הָאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Mari bar Avuh said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Bones of offerings that served** as a base for **leftover,** meaning that they have leftover meat on them or inside them after the time of eating, **render hands impure,** just as the leftover sacrificial parts themselves render hands impure. **Since** the bones **have become a base for an** intrinsically **prohibited object,** they are treated in the same manner as the prohibited object itself.

The *Gemara* opens with a teaching: *Rav Mari bar Avuha amar Rav Yitzchak: atzmos kodshim she'shimshu notar metamin et ha-yadayim* — bones of *kodashim* that served *notar* convey *tumah* to the hands. The Rabbis instituted that *notar* meat imparts *tumah* of the hands as a measure to prompt *kohanim* to eat the meat before it expired: since *kohanim* were scrupulously careful about matters of *tumah*, attaching *tumah* to *notar* would motivate them not to allow meat to become *notar* in the first place. Rav Mari bar Avuha teaches that bones which "served" the *notar* — specifically by containing marrow within them — likewise convey *tumah*, since they are considered a *basis le-davar ha-assur*, a base or vessel for something forbidden.

### Marrow Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הָעֲצָמוֹת וְהַגִּידִים וְהַנּוֹתָר — יִשָּׂרְפוּ לְשִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר. הָנֵי עֲצָמוֹת הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלֵית בְּהוּ מוֹחַ, לָמָּה בִּשְׂרֵיפָה? נִשְׁדִּינְהוּ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּאִית בְּהוּ מוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following expression in the mishna **supports him: The bones, and the sinews, and the leftover should be burned on the sixteenth** of Nisan. **What are the circumstances** in which **these bones** must be burned? **If we say** that **there is no marrow in them, why** must they be **burned? We should discard them,** as the Torah requires that leftover parts of offerings be burned only when they are edible. **Rather,** it is **obvious that** we are dealing with bones that **have marrow in them.** The marrow is part of the meat of the offering, and in other offerings one would have broken the bones in order to eat the marrow.

The *mishnah* establishes that bones are to be burned on the sixteenth. A question immediately arises: *hani atzamos heichi dami?* — what exactly is the case? If the bones contain no marrow, there is no obligation to burn them; they should simply be discarded, for there is nothing of the *korban* remaining in them — a bone without marrow has no more sanctity than a piece of wood. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *mishnah* must be discussing bones that contain marrow. The marrow is *notar*, and it is that marrow which must be burned — which is why the bones are addressed in the *mishnah*. This discussion has implications for broader questions, such as whether bones of animals that never contained marrow have any *halachic* status whatsoever.

### Shimush Notar Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר מִילְּתָא הִיא, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי בָּעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, לְמָה לְהוּ שְׂרֵיפָה? נִתְבְּרִינְהוּ וְנַחְלְצֵהּ לְמוֹחַ דִּידְהוּ וְנִשְׂרְפֵיהּ וְנִשְׁדִּינְהוּ לְדִידְהוּ. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say that** an item that **serves** as a base or container for **leftover is something** significant and becomes disqualified in the same manner as the leftover themselves, **because of this it needs burning** like the leftover itself. **But if you say that serving** as a base for **leftover is nothing** significant, **why do these** bones need to be **burned? Let us break them, and remove their marrow, and burn** the marrow, **and discard** the bones. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it that serving** as a base for **leftover is something** significant, and the bones themselves become prohibited and impart ritual impurity due to their consecrated contents?

The *Gemara* then raises a structural question. Given that we are clearly burning the bones because of the marrow, why must we burn the bones together with the marrow? Why not simply break open the bones, extract the marrow, burn the marrow, and discard the bones? The *Gemara* assumes that the prohibition against breaking bones no longer applies once the *pesach* has become *notar* — so by the morning of the fifteenth or sixteenth, there should be no obstacle to breaking them. The *Gemara* therefore suggests that burning the bones whole (rather than extracting and burning only the marrow) implies something about the status of the bones themselves: *shema mina shimush notar milta hi* — we may infer that the act of "serving" *notar* confers upon the bones a status equivalent to *notar*, requiring that they too be burned.

The *Gemara* then challenges this inference by reversing the logic:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר — לָאו מִילְּתָא, וְקָסָבַר: ״בּוֹ״ בְּכָשֵׁר, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Say** in refutation of this proof: **No. Actually, I could say to you that serving** as a base for **leftover is nothing** significant, **and** the *tanna* of the mishna **holds** that the prohibition stated in the Torah: "And you shall not break a bone **in it"** (Exodus 12:46), applies **even to a disqualified** Paschal lamb. The prohibition to break the bones of the Paschal lamb applies even when the Paschal lamb itself may no longer be eaten. Thus, the bones must be burned because there is no way to extract the marrow. They are not burned because they themselves have served as a base for the leftover marrow.

*perhaps the bones do not acquire any status from having served *notar*, and the bones themselves genuinely have no obligation to be burned. The reason they cannot simply be broken open is the *halakhic* force of the verse *ve-etzem lo tishberu bo* — do not break a bone of the *pesach*. The word *bo* is read not as a limiting term, but as applying even after the *pesach* has become *pasul*: *bo be-chasher ve-afilu be-fasul*. The prohibition against breaking bones continues to apply even the next morning when the *pesach* is *notar*.

The *Gemara* immediately objects: a *mishnah* taught that if one breaks a bone of a *tamei pesach*, one does not receive lashes, implying the prohibition does not apply to an invalid *pesach*. The *Gemara* resolves this tension by distinguishing between *sha'at ha-kosher* and its absence:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר — לָאו מִילְּתָא, וְקָסָבַר: ״בּוֹ״ בְּכָשֵׁר, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּפָסוּל. אֲפִילּוּ בְּפָסוּל סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? וְהָא תְּנַן: אֲבָל הַמּוֹתִיר בַּטָּהוֹר וְהַשּׁוֹבֵר בְּטָמֵא אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים! לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן — שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, כָּאן — שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at the previous answer: **Can** it **enter your mind** to say that the prohibition applies **even to a disqualified** Paschal lamb? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: **But one who leaves over** part of **a pure** Paschal lamb or **one who breaks** the bone of **a ritually impure** Paschal lamb **does not receive forty** lashes, which indicates that the Torah prohibition to break a bone applies only to a Paschal lamb that is still valid? The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. Here,** when the mishna indicates that it is prohibited to break even the bone of a disqualified Paschal lamb, it is referring to a case where **it had a time when it was valid,** such as leftover sacrificial meat, which was valid before it was left over, and the prohibition therefore became relevant when it was valid. **There,** when the other mishna stated that the prohibition does not apply, it is referring to sacrificial meat **that did not have a time when it was valid.**

*kan she-haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher, kan she-lo haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher*. A *pesach* that was validly offered and only later became *notar* — it had its *sha'at ha-kosher*, its moment of validity — retains the bone-breaking prohibition even after it has become *pasul*. But a *pesach* that was *tamei* from the outset and was therefore never valid never had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, and the prohibition therefore never applied to it and does not apply now.

### Shevrias Etzem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא דְּשָׁנֵי לֵיהּ בֵּין שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר לְלֹא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ בוֹ״, ״בּוֹ״ בְּכָשֵׁר וְלֹא בְּפָסוּל. רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסַל — יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, לֹא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And who is the *tanna* who differentiates between** a Paschal lamb **that had a time when it was valid and** one that **did not have a time when it was valid? It is Rabbi Ya'akov, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall not break a bone in it,"** and the emphasis of the expression **"in it"** teaches that the prohibition applies only **to a valid** Paschal lamb **and not to a disqualified** one. **Rabbi Ya'akov says:** If an offering **had a time when it was valid and** then **became disqualified, it is subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone;** if **it did not have** any **time when it was valid, it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone. Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **both this and that,** whether the offering was valid at some point or not, once it becomes disqualified **it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone.**

With this distinction in hand, the *Gemara* explains why we cannot simply break open the bones and extract the marrow: by the morning of the fifteenth, the *pesach* has become *notar*, but because it once had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, the prohibition against breaking its bones continues to apply. The bones must therefore be burned whole, together with the marrow inside them.

### Sha'at ha-Kosher

The *Gemara* then identifies the *tanna* behind this distinction. *U-man tanna de-shani leh?* — who is the *tanna* who draws this distinction? It is Rabbi Yaakov. As the *baraita* teaches: the verse *ve-etzem lo tishberu bo* prohibits breaking a bone of a valid *pesach* but not an invalid one. Rabbi Yaakov adds: *haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher ve-nifsal, yesh bo* — if it once had a moment of validity and subsequently became *pasul*, the prohibition applies. *Lo haitah lo sha'at ha-kosher, ein bo mishum shevrias etzem* — if it was never valid, there is no prohibition of bone-breaking.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh, ein bo mishum shevrias etzem* — whether or not the *pesach* once had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, once it is now *pasul*, there is no prohibition of bone-breaking at all. On Rabbi Shimon's view, even the bones of a *pesach* that was validly offered may be broken freely the next morning when it is *notar*.

*Tosafot* makes a significant structural observation: Rabbi Yaakov and Rabbi Shimon are not independently disputing one another, but are each offering their interpretation of the *tanna kamma*. The *tanna kamma* stated only that the prohibition applies to a *kasher pesach* and not a *pasul* one. The debate between Rabbi Yaakov and Rabbi Shimon is over what constitutes *pasul*: Rabbi Yaakov holds that *pasul* means only those that were never valid, while Rabbi Shimon holds that any *pesach* currently *pasul* — regardless of its prior status — is excluded from the prohibition. This pattern, where later *tannaim* debate the meaning of an earlier ruling rather than independently asserting their own views, recurs throughout the *mishnah*, as seen for instance in the opening *mishnah* of *Pesachim* itself, where Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel debate the meaning of the earlier teaching about the two rows in a cellar.

### Bones Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל עַצְמוֹת הַקֳּדָשִׁים אֵין טְעוּנִין שְׂרֵיפָה, חוּץ מֵעַצְמוֹת הַפֶּסַח מִפְּנֵי הַתַּקָּלָה. הָנֵי עֲצָמוֹת הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלֵית בְּהוּ מוֹחַ, לְמָה לְהוּ שְׂרֵיפָה? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאִית בְּהוּ מוֹחַ, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר מִילְּתָא הִיא — עַצְמוֹת קָדָשִׁים אַמַּאי אֵין טְעוּנִין שְׂרֵיפָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on what was taught in the following *baraita*: **No bones of offerings require burning except for the bones of the Paschal lamb, due to the** possibility that leaving the bones around will lead to **a mishap** because one may eventually transgress the prohibition of breaking the bones. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which **these bones** must be burned? **If we say** we are dealing with a case **where there is no marrow** in the bones, **why must they be burned?** They should simply be discarded. **Rather,** it is **obvious that there is marrow in them. And if it should enter your mind that serving** as a base for **leftover** meat of an offering **is something** significant, **why do bones of** other **offerings not require burning?** They are serving as a base for the leftover marrow in them.

The *Gemara* now turns to a *baraita* that presents what seems to be a difficulty: *kol atzmos ha-kodshim ein te'unim sereifah* — all bones of other *kodashim* do not require burning — *chutz me-atzmos ha-pesach, mipnei ha-takalah* — except for the bones of the *pesach*, because of the potential for transgression. What does this mean?

The *Gemara* asks: what is the case regarding these bones of other *kodashim* that need not be burned? If they contain no marrow, there is no need to burn them in the first place — they would simply be discarded. So the *baraita* must be discussing bones that do contain, or once contained, marrow. Yet it says these bones of other *kodashim* need not be burned. If the principle of *shimush notar* — that serving *notar* confers *notar*-status upon the bones — were operative, why would these bones not also require burning?

This appears to challenge the conclusion that *shimush notar milta hi*. The *Gemara* resolves the challenge by explaining that the *baraita* is not discussing bones from which the marrow was removed after the *korban* became *notar*, but rather bones that were already stripped of their marrow when found. The question then becomes: when was that marrow removed?

### Stripped Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁמְּצָאָן חֲלוּצִין: עַצְמוֹת קָדָשִׁים, דְּאֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם — קַמֵּי דְּנֶהְווֹ נוֹתָר חַלְצִינְהוּ, וְלָא הָווּ שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר, וְלָא בָּעוּ שְׂרֵיפָה. עַצְמוֹת הַפֶּסַח, דְּיֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם — לְבָתַר דְּנֶהְווֹ נוֹתָר הוּא דְּחַלְצִינְהוּ, וְהָווּ לְהוּ שִׁימּוּשׁ נוֹתָר, וּבָעוּ שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a special case **where one finds** bones from which the marrow had already been **removed.** Therefore, there is room to differentiate between the different cases: With regard to **bones of** other **offerings, to which** the prohibition of **breaking a bone does not apply,** one may presume that **before they became leftover one removed** the marrow from them and ate it. Therefore, these bones **were not serving** as a base for **leftover** sacrificial meat, **and they do not require burning** and may be discarded. On the other hand, **bones of the Paschal lamb, to which** the prohibition of **breaking a bone does apply,** were certainly not broken while the prohibition applied; rather, **it was after they became leftover that one removed** the marrow **from them,** when the prohibition of breaking a bone no longer applied, according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon above. In that case, the bones temporarily **served** as a base for **leftover** sacrificial meat, and therefore **they require burning.**

Two *amoraim* offer related explanations of this scenario.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: *kegon she-metzan chalutzin* — the case is one where the bones are found already stripped of their marrow. By bones of other *kodashim*, since there is no prohibition against breaking the bones, we may assume that the owners were careful to extract the marrow before the meat became *notar* — *kamei de-hava notar chalutzinhu* — and therefore the bones never served *notar*, and need not be burned: *velo havei shimush notar, velo ba'ei sereifah*. By the *pesach*, however, since breaking the bones is prohibited, the marrow could not have been removed before the *korban* expired; therefore, it is certain that the bones served *notar* before they were stripped, and they must be burned even now that they are hollow: *leva'tar de-hava notar hu de-chaltzinhu, ve-havu lehu shimush notar u-va'u sereifah*. This reading supports the principle that *shimush notar milta hi*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁמְּצָאָן צִבּוּרִין צִבּוּרִין, וּמֵהֶן חֲלוּצִין. עַצְמוֹת קָדָשִׁים דְּאֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם — לְכוּלְּהוּ הֲוָה חָלֵיץ לְהוּ וְאָכֵיל לְהוּ, וְלָא בָּעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה. עַצְמוֹת הַפֶּסַח דְּיֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם — דִּילְמָא הָנֵי דְּחַלְצִינְהוּ, וּלְהָנָךְ לָא חַלְצִינְהוּ, וּבָעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said** that one can answer in a different way: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **such as when one finds them in piles, and** he sees that **some of them** have had their marrow **removed.** If these bones are **bones of offerings to which** the prohibition of **breaking a bone does not apply,** one can assume that the owner **removed** the marrow from **all** the bones before the marrow became leftover **and ate it, and they do not require burning.** On the other hand, with regard to **the bones of the Paschal lamb, to which** the prohibition of **breaking a bone does apply, perhaps** the owner **removed** the marrow from **these, and from those he did not remove** the marrow, **and they require burning.** Therefore, although one needs to check all the bones of the Paschal lamb he finds to ensure that they do not have marrow left in them, he does not need to burn them due to the unconfirmed possibility that the marrow was removed after it became leftover. The bones would need to be burned only if there was still marrow in them or if one knew for certain that they had served as a base for leftover sacrificial meat.

Rav Zvid offers a somewhat different reading of the *baraita*. He explains: *kegon she-metzan tziburin tziburin* — the case is one where the bones are found in a large pile, and inspection of those on top reveals that they have been stripped of their marrow. By bones of other *kodashim*, since there is no prohibition against breaking the bones, one can assume not only that the stripped bones in hand had their marrow removed before *notar* set in, but one can further extrapolate that the same is true of the entire pile — *lekulu havei chalitz lehu ve-achul lehu* — and therefore none of the bones in the pile require burning. By the *pesach*, however, even if the bones on top were stripped, one cannot make a corresponding assumption about the rest of the pile — *dilma hani de-chaltzinhu u-le-hanach lo chaltzinhu* — since the prohibition against breaking bones would have made early extraction difficult or impossible, and the bones at the bottom of the pile may still contain *notar* marrow, requiring them to be burned.

On Rav Zvid's reading, the distinction between *pesach* and other *kodashim* does not rest on whether *shimush notar milta hi*, but on whether one can make a reliable assumption about the contents of stripped bones. The bones themselves need not be burned independently; only those that actually contain marrow need burning. The difference is simply evidentiary: for other *kodashim*, one may assume early extraction; for the *pesach*, one may not.

## Gidin & Gid Nasheh

### Gidin Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַגִּידִין בָּשָׂר, חוּץ מִגִּידֵי צַוָּאר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: All the sinews** in an animal are considered **like meat.** Therefore, one can fulfill his obligation to eat the Paschal lamb by eating them and one can register for them, **except for the sinews of the neck,** which are so hard that they are not considered meat.

A general teaching is cited: *kol ha-gidin basar ninhu* — all sinews are classified as meat for *halachic* purposes, with one exception: *chutz mi-gidei tzavar*, the sinews of the neck, which are essentially inedible and are not counted as meat.

### Sinew Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָעֲצָמוֹת וְהַגִּידִים וְהַנּוֹתָר יִשָּׂרְפוּ בְּשִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר. הָנֵי גִּידִין הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא גִּידֵי בָשָׂר — נֵיכְלִינְהוּ? וְאִי דְּאִיתּוֹתַר — הַיְינוּ נוֹתָר? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא: גִּידֵי צַוָּאר.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **The bones, and the sinews, and the leftover shall be burned on the sixteenth.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which **these sinews** must be burned? **If we say** they are **sinews of meat, let us eat them.** Why are they burned? **And if** they are sinews **that were left over** and not eaten for some other reason, **they are leftover,** so why does the mishna list sinews separately? **Rather, it is obvious** that the mishna is referring to **sinews of the neck,** which are different than other sinews and are therefore mentioned separately.

The *mishnah* states that the *gidin* must also be burned on the sixteenth. The *Gemara* asks what kind of sinews the *mishnah* has in mind. If it refers to ordinary, edible sinews — *gidei basar* — then the *chiddush* is unclear: these are simply meat that was not eaten, and they are already subsumed under the general category of *notar*. There is no reason to list them separately. And if the *mishnah* refers to the sinews of the neck, which are inedible and not classified as meat, then on one hand one might argue that the *mishnah* is teaching that even these count as meat and must be burned; but on the other hand, if they are not really meat, why would they have any *notar* status at all?

The tension becomes sharp: for the *mishnah* to be making a *chiddush* by listing the sinews separately, it must be referring to sinews that are in some sense borderline — either nearly inedible but still technically meat, or technically permissible but practically uneaten. If it refers to ordinary sinews, the mention adds nothing. If it refers to the neck sinews, one must argue that even these are considered meat — which would be the *chiddush* — and that the obligation to burn them follows from their status as *notar*.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בָּשָׂר נִינְהוּ, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי בָּעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָאו בָּשָׂר נִינְהוּ — לְמָה לְהוּ שְׂרֵיפָה? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְגִיד הַנָּשֶׁה, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say they are meat, due to that they require burning; but if you say they are not meat, why do they** require **burning?** One should simply discard them like other waste. **Rav Ḥisda said:** The mishna's mention of sinews **is necessary only for the sciatic nerve, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

Rav Chisda offers a different identification entirely: *lo nitzrecha ela le-gid ha-nashe* — the *mishnah* is speaking of the *gid ha-nashe*, the sciatic nerve, which is forbidden to eat. But if the *gid ha-nashe* is forbidden and cannot be eaten, why would there be an obligation to burn it as *notar*? The obligation to burn *notar* is linked to the *mitzvah* of eating the *korban*: one leaves over something that one was obligated to eat, and that leftover must be burned. The *gid ha-nashe* was never in the category of things to be eaten. Rav Chisda's answer is that the explanation follows Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בְּאַחַת, וְהַדַּעַת מַכְרַעַת שֶׁל יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said:** The prohibition to eat the sciatic nerve according to Torah law **applies only to** the sciatic nerve in **one** of the animal's thighs, and not to both, **and logic dictates** that it is **the right** thigh. However, since there is no absolute proof that this is correct, the sciatic nerve must be removed from both sides. Although in theory the forbidden sciatic nerve may be discarded and the permitted one may be eaten, since there is uncertainty as to which one is permitted, neither of them may be eaten. Both must be burned.

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *eino noheg ella be-achat* — the prohibition of the *gid ha-nashe* applies to only one of the two thighs. *Ve-ha-da'at machra'at shel yamin* — reason suggests it is the right thigh that is primary. Now, does Rabbi Yehuda mean that he has resolved the question and determined definitively that it is the right thigh? Or does he mean that while it is probably the right, he remains uncertain — in which case one would refrain from eating both the right and left *gidin* out of doubt? If Rabbi Yehuda retains a *safek* and therefore forbids both as a precaution, then one of the two *gidin* — the permissible one — could have been eaten but was not, and is therefore *notar* that must be burned. This would explain why the *mishnah* mentions the *gidin* as requiring burning.

### Mixed-Up Gidim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּסַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּאִי מִיפְּשִׁיט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, הָהִיא דְּהֶיתֵּירָא נֵיכְלֵיהּ, וּדְאִיסּוּרָא נִישְׁדְּיֵיהּ — לְמָה לֵיהּ שְׂרֵיפָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we then conclude that Rabbi Yehuda was uncertain** about which sciatic nerve is forbidden? The Sages were unsure whether Rabbi Yehuda was absolutely convinced that it is the sciatic nerve from the right side that is forbidden, or if he was saying that this would seem likely to be the case, but he was not certain. **As, if it were clear to him** that it is the sciatic nerve from the right thigh that is forbidden, the proper procedure would be different: **The one that is permitted we should eat, and** the one **that is forbidden we should discard. Why should he** require **burning?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִיקָא בַּר חִינָּנָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ika bar Ḥinnana said,** in response to this attempted proof: The mishna addresses a case **where** the two sciatic nerves **were known, but in the end** became **mixed** together. In other words, at first it was known which was the forbidden right nerve and which was the permitted left nerve. However, they were then mixed together and can no longer be identified. Therefore, due to the uncertainty, they must both be burned.

Rav Ika bar Chinena offers a related scenario: *kegon she-hukru le-vasof ve-nitarvu* — even if Rabbi Yehuda ultimately concludes with certainty that it is the right *gid* that is forbidden, the two *gidin* may have become mixed up. Once they are mixed up, one cannot identify which is which, and therefore both are left uneaten, with the permissible one becoming *notar* and requiring burning. This explains the *mishnah*'s listing of *gidin* as a case requiring burning even if one accepts that Rabbi Yehuda definitively identified the prohibited side.

### Shuman shel Gid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְשֻׁמָּנוֹ שֶׁל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה. דְּתַנְיָא: שֻׁמָּנוֹ מוּתָּר, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל קְדוֹשִׁים הֵם וְנוֹהֲגִין בּוֹ אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The mishna's ruling that the sinews must be burned **is necessary only** with regard to **the fat of the sciatic nerve, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **The fat** around the sciatic nerve **is permitted** according to Torah law, but **the Jewish people are holy and treat it as forbidden.** Since it is permitted according to Torah law, it has the status of meat and may not be simply discarded. However, since the Jewish people treat it as forbidden, they do not eat it even from the Paschal lamb. Therefore, it is left until after the time when the meat may be eaten and burned in accordance with the general *halakha* of leftover.

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading: *lo nitzrecha ella le-shumno shel gid ha-nashe* — the *mishnah* is not referring to the *gid* itself, but to the fat (*shuman*) surrounding the *gid ha-nashe*.

### Fat Status

As the *baraita* teaches: *shumno muttar* — the fat around the *gid ha-nashe* is technically permitted by Torah law. Nevertheless, *ve-Yisrael kedoshim hem ve-nohagim bo issur* — the Jewish people are holy and have adopted the practice of treating it as forbidden. This practice has the force of at least a rabbinic prohibition. The consequence is striking: since nobody eats this fat — though the Torah permits it — it necessarily remains as *notar* after the *pesach* meal, and the *mitzvah* to burn *notar* applies to it. The *mishnah* therefore lists *gidin* as requiring burning on the sixteenth because of the fat around the *gid ha-nashe* that was left over. There is a certain irony here: the *chumra* of treating permitted fat as forbidden results, in effect, in a *chumra de-ati lidei kulla* — everyone who eats a *korban pesach* inevitably transgresses the prohibition of *notar* with respect to this fat, since they cannot eat something they have undertaken not to eat.

### Outer vs. Inner Gid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: בַּחִיצוֹן, וְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁנֵי גִידִין הֵן, פְּנִימִי הַסָּמוּךְ לָעֶצֶם — אָסוּר, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו. חִיצוֹן הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר — אָסוּר, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** This discussion pertains to the **outer** nerve, **and** it is in accordance with that which **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: There are two sinews** in the sciatic nerve: The **inner** sinew **that is next to the bone is forbidden** according to Torah law, **and one is liable** to be flogged **for** eating **it. The outer** sinew **that is next to the meat is forbidden** by rabbinic law, and therefore **one is not liable** to be flogged **for** eating **it.** Since the outer sinew is permitted according to Torah law, it attains the status of leftover when it is not eaten.

Ravina proposes a final answer: *bechitzon* — the *mishnah* refers to the outer *gid*, and the explanation follows the ruling of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *de-bei gidin hein* — there are actually two sinews. The inner one, *pnimi ha-samuch la-etzem*, running along the bone, is biblically forbidden and one is liable for its consumption. The outer one, *chitzon ha-samuch la-basar*, closer to the meat and not running the length of the bone, is only rabbinically forbidden — *ein chayavin alav*. Because of this rabbinic prohibition, the outer *gid* is left uneaten, even though it is biblically classified as meat. It therefore becomes *notar* and must be burned, and this is what the *mishnah* is teaching. The same logic applies as in Rav Ashi's explanation: a rabbinic prohibition results in something biblically permissible being left uneaten and consequently requiring burning as *notar*.

## Notar on Shabbat…

### Chizkiya's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָל שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר וְכוּ׳. וְאַמַּאי? נֵיתֵי עֲשֵׂה וְיִדְחֵי לֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that if **the sixteenth** of Nisan **occurs** on Shabbat, the burning of the leftover does not override Shabbat, and they are burned on the seventeenth. The Gemara asks: **And why** isn't the leftover of the Paschal lamb burned on the Festival day itself? **The positive mitzva** to burn the leftover **should come and override the prohibition** that prohibits the performance of labor on Festivals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, וְכֵן תָּנֵי דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תוֹתִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר וְהַנּוֹתָר מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר בָּאֵשׁ תִּשְׂרֹפוּ״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַד בֹּקֶר״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַד בֹּקֶר״ — לִיתֵּן לוֹ בֹּקֶר שֵׁנִי לִשְׂרֵיפָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiya said, and similarly,** one of the Sages in **the school of Ḥizkiya taught: The verse states: "And you shall not leave any of it until morning; and that which remains of it until morning you shall burn with fire"** (Exodus 12:10). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state "until morning,"** and repeating that phrase adds nothing to the verse, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states "until morning"?** It is **to give it a second morning for its burning.** In other words, it comes to teach that the mitzva to burn the leftover does not apply on the first morning, i.e., the morning of the Festival when they become prohibited to eat, but on the second morning, the sixteenth of Nisan. However, when the sixteenth falls on Shabbat, the burning is delayed until the seventeenth.

The *Gemara* now turns to the question raised by the *mishnah*: if the sixteenth falls on Shabbos, why can the burning of *notar* not override the prohibition of *Yom Tov*? After all, this is a positive commandment — should the *aseh* of burning *notar* not displace the *lo ta'aseh* of prohibited labor on *Yom Tov*? This question presents no difficulty with respect to Shabbos, which combines a *lo ta'aseh* with a *lo ta'aseh* and the additional severity of *chiyuv misah*, but *Yom Tov* seems more vulnerable to the override argument.

Chizkiya, and likewise the *tanna* of the school of Chizkiya, derives the answer from the verse: *ve-lo sosiru mimenu ad boker, ve-ha-notar mimenu ad boker ba-esh tisrofu*. The verse first prohibits leaving the meat until morning, and then commands that whatever remains be burned in the morning. Why does the verse need to say *boker* a second time? The first *boker* already establishes the prohibition. *Liten lo boker sheini le-sereifaso* — the second mention of *boker* teaches that the burning is assigned to a subsequent morning: do not burn the *notar* on *Yom Tov* itself; give it another morning. The Torah explicitly delays the burning to avoid conflict with *Yom Tov*.

### Abaye's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״עֹלַת שַׁבָּת בְּשַׁבַּתּוֹ״, וְלֹא עוֹלַת חוֹל בְּשַׁבָּת, וְלֹא עוֹלַת חוֹל בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The reason is because **the verse states: "The burnt-offering of each Shabbat on its Shabbat in addition to the continual burnt-offering and its libation" (Numbers 28:10). This indicates that the burnt-offering of Shabbat may be offered on the altar on Shabbat or burned if it is disqualified, but** not the burnt-offering of a weekday on Shabbat, and not the burnt-offering of a weekday on a Festival.** It is derived from here that one may not burn anything on Shabbat that is not an obligation of that day.

Abaye derives the answer from a different verse: *olas Shabbos be-Shabbato* — the *olah* of Shabbos is offered on its own day. The verse implies: *ve-lo olas chol be-Shabbos* — the weekday sacrifice cannot be completed on Shabbos. This principle extends equally to *Yom Tov*: *ve-lo olas chol be-Yom Tov*. Even on the *mizbe'ach* itself, fat from a *korban* slaughtered on the fourteenth cannot be burned on *Yom Tov* night — it is a weekday sacrifice being completed on *Yom Tov*, which is prohibited. If one may not even complete the offering of a valid *korban* on *Yom Tov*, how much more so may one not burn *notar*, which is already an invalid remnant of the *korban*. The *kal va-chomer* establishes that burning *notar* is not permitted on *Yom Tov*.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא לְבַדּוֹ יֵעָשֶׂה לָכֶם״ — ״הוּא״ וְלֹא מַכְשִׁירָיו, ״לְבַדּוֹ״,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different reason: **The verse states** concerning Festivals: "And on the first day there shall be a sacred convocation; no kind of labor shall be done on them, save that which every man must eat, **only that may be done for you"** (Exodus 12:16). From this verse one can infer the following: **That,** i.e., the food itself, you may prepare, **but** you may **not** perform acts that **facilitate its** preparation, which must be done beforehand. The word **only** is also exclusive; it indicates that only food preparation is permitted,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא מִילָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנָּהּ, הַבָּאָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **but not a circumcision** that is **not at its** ideal **time**. If, for some reason, circumcision was not performed on the eighth day after birth, its performance at a later date does not override a Festival, because the obligation to perform the circumcision is not particular to that specific day. This needed to be emphasized. Otherwise, it would have **come** to be derived **through an *a fortiori*** inference that circumcision does override Shabbat and Festivals, based on the fact that it overrides the laws of leprosy. Therefore, the Torah emphasized that circumcision performed later than the eighth day does not override Festivals.

Rava derives the answer from yet another verse: *hu levado ye'aseh lachem* — on *Yom Tov*, only that which is directly related to eating is permitted; *hu ve-lo machshirav* — but not preparatory acts such as chopping wood for a fire; *levado, ve-lo milah shelo bi-zmanah* — and "alone" teaches that even *milah* not performed on its prescribed eighth day may not override *Yom Tov*, despite the fact that *milah* performed on its prescribed eighth day overrides even Shabbos. Rava's point is that the Torah explicitly teaches, through the word *levado*, that *Yom Tov* cannot be displaced by commandments that do not pertain directly to food preparation on *Yom Tov* itself — even *mitzvot* as significant as *milah*. If that is true of *milah*, it is certainly true of burning *notar*.

### Rav Ashi Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״שַׁבָּתוֹן״ דְּיוֹם טוֹב — עֲשֵׂה הוּא, וְאֵין עֲשֵׂה דּוֹחֶה לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different reason that the leftover Paschal lamb is not burned on the Festival: The obligation of **solemn rest** stated **with regard to Festivals is a positive** mitzva. Therefore, the *halakhot* of Festivals include both a positive mitzva to rest from performing prohibited labor and a prohibition to avoid such labor, **and a positive** mitzva such as burning leftover sacrificial meat **does not override** both **a prohibition and a positive** mitzva.

Rav Ashi offers what is perhaps the most direct and structurally elegant answer: *shabbaton de-Yom Tov aseh hu* — the *shabbaton* quality of *Yom Tov* is itself a positive commandment. *Yom Tov* is therefore not merely a *lo ta'aseh* but a combination of *lo ta'aseh* and *aseh*. *Ve-ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh va-aseh* — a positive commandment can override a negative commandment standing alone, but it cannot override a negative commandment reinforced by a positive commandment. The *aseh* of burning *notar* therefore cannot displace *Yom Tov*, which carries both a prohibition against *melacha* and the positive requirement of *shabbaton* — rest. This is why *notar* may not be burned on *Yom Tov*, and *a fortiori* not on Shabbos.
