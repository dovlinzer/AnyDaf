# Nedarim 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomeret Yavam Intro

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, בֵּין לְיָבָם אֶחָד, בֵּין לִשְׁנֵי יְבָמִין. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָפֵר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לְאֶחָד, אֲבָל לֹא לִשְׁנַיִם. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא לְאֶחָד וְלֹא לִשְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a widow waiting for her *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage, **whether** she is waiting **for one *yavam*,** if her late husband had only one brother, or **whether** she is waiting **for two** or more ***yevamin*,** if he had several brothers, **Rabbi Eliezer says:** A *yavam* **can nullify** her vows. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** If she is waiting **for one** *yavam*, he can nullify her vows, **but not** if she is waiting **for two. Rabbi Akiva says:** A *yavam* **cannot** nullify her vows, regardless of whether she is waiting **for one** *yavam* **or for two** or more.

The fifth *mishnah* of this *perek* continues the discussion of unusual cases — those that do not arise in the standard situation contemplated by the *Torah* — which the *perek* has chosen to focus upon: the case of the father and the *arus* acting in partnership in the annulment of vows, and now the case of the *shomeret yavam*, the woman who awaits *yibum*, levirate marriage.

### Yavam's Role

The *yavam*'s relationship with his *yevamah* is, on one hand, a carryover of the marriage of the deceased brother, which may itself have been at the level of *nesu'in*. The *yibum* relationship requires less deliberate intent to actualize into a full *nesu'in* — even an act of *bi'ah* performed without specific *kavanah* to create a marriage accomplishes it, because of the *zika*, the bond and remnant of the *kiddushin* that transfers from the dead husband to his brother. On the other hand, the relationship in its present, unrealized state is not equivalent to a full marriage: if the *yevamah* were to be with another man, it would not constitute *eshet ish*; and the release from the obligation can be achieved through *chalitzah* rather than a *get*.

### Yavam & Hafara

Given this ambiguous status, the question arises whether the *yavam* is needed and able to participate in *hafarat nedarim* together with the father. If the original marriage to the deceased husband had been at the level of *erusin*, the father remains in the picture entirely. The question then becomes whether the *yavam*'s relationship resembles *erusin* — in which case his participation in *hafara* is necessary but must be jointly with the father — or whether it rises to the level of *nesu'in*, in which case he would act alone. The *mishnah* uses the word *yafer* (he may annul), and it is not immediately clear which of these two possibilities that word entails.

## Mishnah Dispute

### Mishnah Setup

The *mishnah* presents the following case: a *shomeret yavam* — a woman awaiting *yibum* — whether she has one *yavam* or two. With two *yevamin*, the question is immediately more complex, since one cannot designate any particular one as being in a concrete relationship with her.

### R. Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מָה אִם אִשָּׁה שֶׁקָּנָה הוּא לְעַצְמוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא מֵיפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ, אִשָּׁה שֶׁהִקְנוּ לוֹ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיָּפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna then elaborates: **Rabbi Eliezer said: Just as** with regard to **a woman he acquired for himself** through betrothal, **he nullifies her vows,** so too with regard to **a woman acquired for him from Heaven,** i.e., the *yevama*, **isn't it logical that he should** be able to **nullify her vows?**

*Rabbi Eliezer omer yafer* — Rabbi Eliezer says the *yavam* may be *mefir*, whether there is one or two brothers-in-law. Whether this means he does so alone (as a *ba'al*) or together with the father (as an *arus*) remains to be clarified by the *Gemara*.

### R. Yehoshua View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: עֲקִיבָא, דְּבָרֶיךָ בִּשְׁנֵי יְבָמִין, מָה אַתָּה מֵשִׁיב עַל יָבָם אֶחָד? אָמַר לוֹ: אֵין הַיְּבָמָה גְּמוּרָה לַיָּבָם כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהָאֲרוּסָה גְּמוּרָה לְאִישָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: Akiva, your statement applies** in a situation **with two *yevamin*,** but **how do you reply** to Rabbi Eliezer **in** the case of **one *yavam*?** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: A *yevama* is not the full-fledged** wife **of the *yavam* in the way that a betrothed woman** is **her husband's full-fledged** wife, and the *yavam* is not empowered to nullify vows at all.

*Rabbi Yehoshua omer l'echad aval lo lishnayim* — Rabbi Yehoshua says this authority applies only when there is one *yavam*, not when there are two. The reason is not merely epistemic uncertainty about which brother is "really" the one in the relationship, but something more fundamental: the relationship is indeterminate at its base, and therefore neither brother genuinely occupies the role of husband. It follows that if there are two *yevamin* and neither has the status of husband, the father annuls the vow by himself — not that the parties are paralyzed, but that the indeterminacy removes the *yavam* from the equation and leaves the father as the sole authority.

### R. Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁקָּנָה הוּא לְעַצְמוֹ — שֶׁאֵין לַאֲחֵרִים בָּהּ רְשׁוּת, תֹּאמַר בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִקְנוּ לוֹ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, שֶׁיֵּשׁ לַאֲחֵרִים בָּהּ רְשׁוּת?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: No, if you say** that a husband can nullify the vows of **a woman he acquired for himself, over whom others have no authority, shall you** also **say** that this is the case with regard to **a woman acquired for him from Heaven, over whom others have authority?** If there are two *yevamin*, each *yavam* has equal authority with regard to her vows.

*Rabbi Akiva omer lo l'echad velo lishnayim* — Rabbi Akiva rules that even a single *yavam* has no such status. In his view, even with only one brother-in-law, the relationship does not carry sufficient halachic weight to confer authority over her vows, and everything reverts to the father.

### Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Eliezer challenges Rabbi Akiva with a *kal v'chomer*. When a man marries a woman on his own initiative — *isha shekana hu l'atzmo* — he acquires the right to annul her vows. How much more so, then, in the case where heaven itself has designated the relationship — *isha shehiknu lo min hashamayim* — should he be in a position to be *mefir*? The very existence of a *mitzvah* of *yibum*, the concept of *yakum shem achiv hamet*, suggests this divinely ordained bond is stronger, not weaker, than one created by purely human initiative.

### R. Akiva Response

Rabbi Akiva rejects the comparison. *Im amarta b'isha shekana hu l'atzmo, she'ein l'acherim bah reshus* — when a man marries a woman entirely on his own, no one else has any share or claim to her. *Tomar b'isha shehiknu lo min hashamayim, sheyesh l'acherim bah reshus* — in the case of *yibum*, another brother also has a connection to her, and even after one brother acts, the other is not categorically excluded. The relationship is thus not exclusive, and for that reason the *yavam* does not stand in the same position as a husband. Rabbi Yehoshua presses back — *Akiva, peratzta b'shnei yevamim, mah atah meshiv al yavam echad?* — conceding that the argument works for two brothers but asking what Rabbi Akiva would say about a single *yavam*, where Rabbi Eliezer's *kal v'chomer* seems to apply in full force. Rabbi Akiva responds: *ein hayevamah gemurah layavam k'shem she'arusah gemurah l'isha* — the *yevamah* is not fully given over to the *yavam* the way an *arusah* is given over to her husband. The nature of the relationship is not as weighty; it is not marriage in the full sense, and therefore even a single *yavam* cannot be *mefir*.

## Zika & Relationship

### R. Akiva: No Zika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר אֵין זִיקָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר יֵשׁ זִיקָה. אֶלָּא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ? אִי יֵשׁ זִיקָה, אֵין בְּרֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The latter two opinions in the mishna **make sense: Rabbi Akiva holds** that the levirate **bond is not substantial.** Since the obligation of levirate marriage does not create a marriage-like bond between the *yavam* and the *yevama*, a *yavam* cannot nullify the vows of the *yevama*. **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that the levirate **bond is substantial,** so that if there is only one *yavam*, the *yevama* is considered his wife, allowing him to nullify her vows. **However,** with regard to **Rabbi Eliezer, what is his reason?** Even **if** he holds that the levirate **bond is substantial, there is,** nevertheless, **no** retroactive **designation.** Since it has not yet been established which of them will be her husband, how can either of them nullify her vows?

The *Gemara* now provides a conceptual framework for the three-way dispute. Rabbi Akiva's position is explained as *ein zika* — he does not recognize any concrete bond. *Zika*, as familiar from *Masechet Yevamot*, refers to whether the potential relationship awaiting actualization is itself given real halachic weight, such that we speak of an actual existing bond rather than a mere future possibility. One might have thought that even Rabbi Akiva could acknowledge *zika* while nonetheless maintaining that it is not strong enough to reach the level of *eshet ish* or to function like the bond of a husband for purposes of *hafara*. However, the *Gemara* prefers to frame Rabbi Akiva's position as a denial of *zika* as a concrete relationship altogether. There is a *mitzvah*, there is an *issur* of *lo tihiyeh eishet hamet hachutza*, but none of that has yet defined a real, concrete relational status — it remains a potential and an obligation, not an actualized bond.

### Yehoshua/Eliezer: Yes Zika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לַהּ כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, דְּאָמְרִי: מַאֲמָר קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ami said:** It is a case **where** one *yavam* has already **performed levirate betrothal with her.** According to Beit Hillel, levirate betrothal does not have the full force of a regular betrothal, **but Rabbi Eliezer holds like Beit Shammai, who say: Levirate betrothal effects a full-fledged acquisition** just like a regular betrothal.

Both Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Eliezer affirm *zika* as a real, concrete bond. The distinction between them is then straightforward: Rabbi Yehoshua holds that when there are two *yevamin*, the *zika* is fundamentally indeterminate — one cannot locate where it attaches — and so neither brother can invoke it. Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein b'reirah* is not a concern here, and even with two brothers some basis remains to recognize a *yavam*'s authority. The *Gemara* presses: even granted *zika*, how can Rabbi Eliezer hold that any particular one of two brothers is in a position to annul?

### Rav Ami Ukeimta

*Amar Rabbi Ami: k'gon she'asa ma'amar* — Rabbi Ami resolves the difficulty with an *ukeimta*, a recontextualization of the *mishnah*. He holds that the *mishnah* is not describing the baseline case of two brothers where neither has done anything. Rather, it speaks of a case where one of the brothers has performed *ma'amar* — the rabbinic act of *kiddushin* performed by a *yavam* with his *yevamah*. As is well known from *Masechet Yevamot*, *ma'amar* has no *d'Oraita* effect; the only halachically effective routes to completion are *bi'ah* for *yibum* or *chalitzah* for release. A *get* accomplishes nothing *d'Oraita*, and *kiddushin* on its own does nothing *d'Oraita*. Nevertheless, the Rabbis instituted *ma'amar* as a preparatory act — demonstrating intent, providing structure to the relationship, and in the case of multiple brothers, serving to designate which one is proceeding with the *yibum*. The Rabbis actually encouraged *ma'amar* even when there is only one brother, as a way of bringing formality and intention to the process before the act of *bi'ah* that constitutes the *yibum* itself. Once one of two brothers performs *ma'amar*, he is thereby designated as the one proceeding, and on that basis Rabbi Eliezer holds he is in a position to be *mefir*. This is the *ukeimta*: the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerns a case where *ma'amar* has been performed — Rabbi Eliezer holding it is sufficient to designate one brother for *hafara*, and Rabbi Yehoshua holding it is not.

### Ma'amar & Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר לְךָ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּחַד יָבָם, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנֵי יְבָמִין — לָא. מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּכִי אָתֵי אֲחוּהִי אָסַר עֲלֵיהּ בְּבִיאָה, אוֹ בְּגִיטָּא וּמֵפֵר? וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: אֵין זִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rabbi Yehoshua** would **say to you,** Rabbi Eliezer, in response: **That** statement, that **levirate** betrothal effects a full-fledged acquisition, **applies** only to a case **with one *yavam*, but with two *yevamin*** it is **not** so. The Gemara explains why it cannot be a full-fledged acquisition where there is more than one *yavam*: **Is there anything** like this sort of betrothal that **when his brother comes,** he **can render** the betrothed woman **forbidden to** the one who performed levirate betrothal **by** engaging in **sexual intercourse** with the *yevama*, thereby performing levirate marriage, **or by** giving her **a bill of divorce,** thereby disqualifying her from levirate marriage, **and** nevertheless the betrothed **can** still **nullify** her vows? Since this betrothal can in essence be nullified, it cannot be viewed as betrothal with regard to nullification of vows. Rabbi Ami completes his analysis: **And Rabbi Akiva holds** that the levirate **bond is not substantial** at all, and there is no marital bond between a *yevama* and her *yavam* until the levirate marriage is consummated.

The *Gemara* immediately notes a difficulty: *ma'amar* is rabbinic; why should a rabbinic act create a concrete enough relationship to confer the authority to do *hafara*? The answer is that *Rabbi Eliezer savar lah k'Beis Shammai*, for Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with Beis Shammai, *de'amri ma'amar koneh kinyan gamur* — that *ma'amar* effects a complete acquisition, a *kinyan gamur*, with real *d'Oraita* consequences. The Ran elaborates: according to Beis Shammai, *ma'amar* achieves the status of *k'nusah* — as if she has been fully brought into the marriage — such that if the *yavam* subsequently changes his mind, he need not perform *chalitzah*; he can dissolve the relationship with a *get* instead. This is a significant halachic consequence that demonstrates the *ma'amar* has genuinely redefined the relationship, moving it from the track of *yibum* onto the track of regular marriage with its attendant modes of dissolution.

## Beis Shammai Debate

### R. Elazar Narrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר) [אֶלְעָזָר], דְּאָמַר: מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵין קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה. מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges Rabbi Ami's interpretation of the dispute: **And according to** the *amora* **Rabbi Elazar, who said** that **levirate betrothal, according to Beit Shammai, does not effect** a full-fledged **acquisition except** in that it **removes a rival wife** from being considered substantially bound to the *yavam* while she is a *yevama*, **what can be said?** Only the relatives of the betrothed *yevama* are then forbidden to the *yavam*.

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a complication. A certain *Rabbi Elazar* — distinct from Rabbi Eliezer of the *mishnah* — holds a narrower reading of Beis Shammai's position: *ma'amar l'Beis Shammai ein koneh ela lidchos b'tzara* — all *ma'amar* accomplishes according to Beis Shammai is to push aside the concern about the co-wife; it does not constitute a genuine *kinyan* in the broader sense. This narrow reading undercuts the premise that allowed Rabbi Eliezer's position in the *mishnah* to make sense, since without a true *kinyan gamur* from *ma'amar*, one cannot explain how one brother among two is designated as holding a concrete relationship with the *yevamah*.

### Three-Brother Example

To understand what "pushing aside the co-wife" means, consider a case with three brothers — call them Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — where two of them are married to sisters. If Shimon dies, Reuven has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* with Shimon's wife. But if he does so and then Levi dies, he is already married to the wife's sister and cannot perform *yibum* with Levi's widow. Conversely, if he does not perform *yibum* and Levi also dies, he faces the problem of *achot zekukato* — each woman is the quasi-wife's sister of the other because the *zika* to each renders the other an *achot zekukato* — and he cannot perform *yibum* with either.

### Beis Shammai Position

Now suppose Reuven performs *ma'amar* with Shimon's widow before Levi dies. When Levi subsequently dies, can Reuven say: "I have already acted upon my *zika* to Shimon's widow; Levi's widow can go free, since she is not in a genuine *zika* relationship with me in the same way"? Beis Shammai says yes — the *ma'amar* is sufficient to establish that Levi's widow need not concern him; she is the *tzarah* who is *nidchit*, pushed aside. Rabbi Elazar understands this narrowly: Beis Shammai is not saying *ma'amar* effects a full *kinyan*; it merely operates in this local context to remove the obligation with respect to the sister. Read this way, we are left without the broader *kinyan gamur* that would explain Rabbi Eliezer's position in the *mishnah*. The *Gemara* therefore turns to an alternative explanation.

## Mezonot Requirement

### Beit Din Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין, וְאִיתְחַיֵּיב לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת, וְכִדְרַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַנּוֹדֶרֶת — עַל דַּעַת בַּעְלָהּ הִיא נוֹדֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the *yavam* **stood** in court **in judgment** after the woman demanded that he marry her **and he was obligated** by the court to provide **her sustenance. And** this is **in accordance with** that which **Rav Pineḥas** stated **in the name of Rava, who said: Any** woman **who vows,** that which **she vows** is contingent **upon her husband's consent.** Since under these circumstances the *yavam* must provide for the *yevama* he betrothed, he is authorized to nullify her vows.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *hacha b'mai askinan — k'gon she'amad b'din v'nitchayev lah mezonot*. The case is not simply one of *ma'amar* alone; it is a case where one of the brothers was brought before a *beit din* — presumably the older brother, to whom the obligation of *yibum* is first directed — and the *beit din* obligated him to pay *mezonot* to the *yevamah*. The logic is that this woman has been left without a husband, and if one particular brother is expected to fulfill the obligation and has been deferring, once a full year has passed she should not be left without support. The *beit din* therefore makes him financially responsible for her maintenance for as long as she continues to wait.

### Ran's Principle

This connects to a principle the Ran articulated in the previous *sugya*, cited here in the name of Rabbi Pinchas in the name of Rava: *kol hanoderet al da'at ba'alah hi noderet* — a woman who makes a vow does so with her husband's understanding in mind. The Ran was careful to explain that this principle is not a blanket rule that any person paying someone's bills can annul her vows. The mechanism requires someone who is already in a quasi-husband relationship, and to that pre-existing relationship one adds the further reality that he is paying her food bills and she sees herself as living in a relationship with him and about to be fully married to him. Under those conditions, her vows are presumed to be made *al da'ato*, in accordance with his understanding.

### Mezonot = Nisuin

The Ran draws out the practical consequence: paying *mezonot* combined with the existing *zika* relationship places this *yavam* into the *Torah*'s category of *ba'al*. More specifically, the Ran holds that this reflects a *nesu'in*-type relationship — and therefore, according to those who say the *yavam* can annul in such a case, he does so alone, without the father, as a *ba'al* and not merely as an *arus*. This resolves the earlier question about what *yafer* in the *mishnah* means: in the case where *mezonot* have been imposed, the *yavam* functions as a *ba'al* and acts independently.

## Textual Evidence

### Kal Vachomer Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וּמָה אִם אִשָּׁה שֶׁקָּנָה לְעַצְמוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא מֵיפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ, אִשָּׁה שֶׁהִקְנוּ לוֹ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמֵּיפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ? וְאִי בְּשֶׁעָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר, קָנָה לְעַצְמוֹ הוּא! שֶׁקָּנָה לְעַצְמוֹ עַל יְדֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with Rabbi Ami's explanation of Rabbi Eliezer's opinion: **We learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer said: Just as** with regard to **a woman he acquired for himself** through betrothal, **he nullifies her vows,** so too with regard to **a woman acquired for him from Heaven,** i.e., the *yevama*, **isn't it logical that he should** be able to **nullify her vows? Now if** Rabbi Ami's interpretation is correct, and the mishna is referring **to** a case **where** a *yavam* **performed levirate betrothal,** then **it** is actually a case where **he acquired** a woman **for himself** by performing levirate betrothal. The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, it is a case **where he acquired for himself** a woman who was imposed upon him **by means of Heaven.**

The *Gemara* now re-examines the text of Rabbi Eliezer's *kal v'chomer* in the *mishnah* in light of this reading. Rabbi Eliezer said: a woman he acquired for himself — *isha shekana hu l'atzmo* — he can annul her vows; how much more so a woman whom heaven designated for him. If, as Rabbi Ami holds, the *mishnah* is describing a case where *ma'amar* was performed, then this woman is not merely *min hashamayim* — she is *also* one whom he acquired himself through *ma'amar*, making her *kanah le'atzmo al yedei shamayim*. The *Gemara* notes this apparent over-description but dismisses it: it only strengthens the argument. He is saying, "This case is designated by heaven — and parenthetically he also acted himself on top of that — so surely this should be stronger than one who acted entirely alone."

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּבָעֵי רַבָּה: מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי, אֵירוּסִין עוֹשֶׂה אוֹ נִשּׂוּאִין עוֹשֶׂה? תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּנִשּׂוּאִין עוֹשֶׂה. דְּאִי אֵירוּסִין עוֹשֶׂה — הָא תְּנַן: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה, אָבִיהָ וּבַעְלָהּ מְפִירִין נְדָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty with Rabbi Ami's interpretation, in that if it is correct, **you** can **resolve** the dilemma **that Rabba raised:** Does **levirate betrothal, according to Beit Shammai,** merely **effect betrothal, or does it effect** full-fledged **marriage?** According to Rabbi Eliezer, **you** can **resolve** the dilemma by proving **that it effects marriage.** The proof is as follows: **Because if** levirate betrothal **effects** only **betrothal,** why does the mishna mention only the *yavam* with regard to nullification of vows? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 66b) that with regard to **a betrothed young woman, her father and her husband** together **nullify her vows?** If levirate betrothal renders her betrothed to the *yavam*, the father should also be mentioned as a partner in the nullification.

The *Gemara* then observes that if we accept Rabbi Ami's reading of the *mishnah* as a *ma'amar* case, we can potentially resolve a question posed by Rava: *ma'amar l'Beis Shammai erusin oseh o nisuin oseh?* — does *ma'amar* according to Beis Shammai produce a state of *erusin* or a state of *nisuin*? This question arises because even if Beis Shammai holds *ma'amar* is a *kinyan gamur*, it is formally an act of *kiddushin*. Yet the nature of the *yibum* relationship — where *bi'ah* without any deliberate intent still consummates the marriage — suggests the entire structure might be closer to *nisuin* from the outset, such that *ma'amar* elevates it all the way to *nisuin* rather than stopping at *erusin*. The practical implications include whether *mezonot* is owed after *ma'amar* and the full range of questions attending to *nesu'in* status.

### No Father Mentioned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי ״יָפֵר״ — יָפֵר בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This does not resolve the dilemma, as **what** could be the meaning of **nullify** in the mishna? It could mean that **he nullifies** vows **in partnership** with the father.

The *Gemara* proposes a resolution: since the *mishnah* states simply *yafer* — he annuls — without mentioning the father, it implies the *yavam* acts alone, as a *ba'al*, which would indicate *nisuin* status. The working assumption is that those who grant the *yavam* any authority do so because his relationship resembles *nisuin*, and therefore the *mishnah*'s omission of the father is significant.

### Rav Yitzchak View

Rav Yitzchak, citing Rav Ami, rejects this inference: *yafer beshutfut* — *yafer* means together with the father. The *yavam*'s status in that case is like an *arus*, not a *ba'al*, and his authority is not independent. The *mishnah*'s *yafer* does not resolve whether it is *erusin* or *nisuin*, and the question remains open.

## Braiyta Evidence

### Tosefta Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי כְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, בֵּין יָבָם אֶחָד בֵּין שְׁנֵי יְבָמִין, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָפֵר, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לְאֶחָד וְלֹא לִשְׁנַיִם. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא לְאֶחָד וְלֹא לִשְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is also taught** in a *baraita* **that** Rabbi Eliezer is addressing a case where levirate betrothal has been performed, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami:** With regard to **a widow awaiting her *yavam*, whether** she is waiting for **one *yavam*,** if her late husband had only one brother, or for **two** or more ***yevamin*, Rabbi Eliezer says:** The *yavam* **can nullify** her vows, **and Rabbi Yehoshua says:** He can nullify her vows only in a case where she is waiting **for one** *yavam*, **but not** if she is waiting **for two. Rabbi Akiva says:** Nullification is not possible at all, **not** if she is waiting **for one, and not** if she is waiting **for two** or more.

The *Gemara* now introduces a *braita* — a passage from the *Tosefta* — that parallels the *mishnah* debate. *Shomeret yavam bein yavam echad bein shnei yevamin*, Rabbi Eliezer says *yafer*, Rabbi Yehoshua says only for one and not for two, and Rabbi Akiva says neither one nor two.

### R. Eliezer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וּמָה אִם אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בָּהּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא תָּבֹא לִרְשׁוּתוֹ, מִשֶּׁבָּאת לִרְשׁוּתוֹ — נִגְמְרָה לוֹ. אִשָּׁה, שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק עַד שֶׁלֹּא תָּבֹא לִרְשׁוּתוֹ, מִשֶּׁבָּאת לִרְשׁוּתוֹ — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתִּגְמוֹר לוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Eliezer said** to his disputants: **And just as if** one accepts that a man cannot nullify the vows of **a woman in whom he has no share until she enters into his jurisdiction** through betrothal, yet **once she enters into his jurisdiction, she is fully** under **his** authority for the nullification of her vows, so too with regard to **a woman** in whom **he has a share before she enters his jurisdiction,** i.e., his *yevama*, **once she enters his jurisdiction, is it not logical that she be fully** under **his** authority for the nullification of her vows?

Rabbi Eliezer's argument in the *braita* is formulated more precisely than in the *mishnah*. He says: *u'mah im isha she'ein lo chelek bah ad shelo tavo lereshuto* — a completely unrelated woman, toward whom he had absolutely no prior connection, once she enters his domain through an act of *kiddushin*, she is fully his and he can annul her vows. *Isha sheyesh lo chelek bah ad shelo tavo lereshuto* — but the *yevamah* is a woman to whom he already has a connection — the *zika*, the *mitzvah* of *yibum* — even before he has taken any additional step. How much more so, *mishebat'a lereshuto*, once she has come further into his domain, should he have that power? The Ran notes that the phrase *mishebat'a lereshuto* is chosen rather than *nichnesah lereshuto*, and suggests this reflects the language of *kiddushin* rather than *nesu'in* — the *Gemara* reads *ba'ah lereshuto* as referring to an act of *ma'amar* rather than completed *yibum*, since completed *yibum* would make her fully his wife and no one would debate it. This confirms Rabbi Ami's *ukeimta*: the case is one where *ma'amar* has been performed.

### R. Akiva Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁקָּנָה הוּא לְעַצְמוֹ, שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בָּהּ, כָּךְ אֵין לַאֲחֵרִים חֵלֶק בָּהּ. תֹּאמַר בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִקְנוּ לוֹ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עִמָּהּ חֵלֶק — כָּךְ יֵשׁ לַאֲחֵרִים חֵלֶק בָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Akiva said to him: No,** your *a fortiori* inference is refutable. **If you spoke of** a man having authority over the vows of **a woman he acquired for himself** through betrothal, **that** would be different: **Just as he has no share in her** before betrothal, **so** too **others have no share in her. Will you say** the same **with regard to a woman** who is **acquired for him from Heaven,** i.e., his *yevama*, **for whom, just as he has a share in her, so do others,** i.e., his brothers, also **have a share in her,** as they also are *yevamin*?

Rabbi Akiva's response in the *braita* is explicit. He distinguishes: *bekiddushin shekanah hu l'atzmo* — when a man performs *kiddushin* himself, *shekeshem she'ein lo chelek bah* before the marriage, *she'ein l'acherim chelek bah* either — nobody has any connection to her, and after *kiddushin* he closes out everyone else entirely. *Tomar b'isha shehiknu lo min hashamayim* — in the case of the *yevamah*, *shekeshem sheyesh lo imah chelek, ken yesh l'acher imah chelek* — just as he has a connection to her, so too another brother has a connection to her. Even after *ma'amar*, the other brother can theoretically perform *bi'ah* and complete the *yibum*; the exclusivity that characterizes an ordinary *kiddushin* is simply absent. This is the structural flaw in Rabbi Eliezer's *kal v'chomer*: ordinary *kiddushin* excludes all others; the *yavam*'s connection does not.

### R. Yehoshua Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: עֲקִיבָא, דְּבָרֶיךָ — בִּשְׁנֵי יְבָמִין, מָה אַתָּה מֵשִׁיב עַל יָבָם אֶחָד? אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם חִלַּקְנוּ עַל יָבָם אֶחָד וְעַל שְׁנֵי יְבָמִין בֵּין שֶׁעָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר? וְכִשְׁאָר דְּבָרִים כֵּן נְדָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: Akiva, your statement** fits a situation **with two *yevamin*,** but **what do you answer for** the case of **one *yavam*?** Rabbi Akiva **replied to him: Did we distinguish between one *yavam* and two *yevamin*,** regardless of **whether he performed levirate betrothal or whether he did not perform levirate betrothal? And** just **as** in **other matters** there is no such distinction, **so** too with regard to **vows.**

Rabbi Yehoshua again presses Rabbi Akiva: *Akiva, zu hi bishnei yevamin* — this reasoning about the lack of exclusivity holds when there are two brothers, but what do you say about a single *yavam*? When there is only one brother, there is no other claimant. Rabbi Eliezer's argument seems fully applicable.

### R. Akiva Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה אָמַר בֶּן עַזַּאי: חֲבָל עָלֶיךָ בֶּן עַזַּאי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁימַּשְׁתָּ אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds a comment: **Ben Azzai stated** his response to hearing this **discussion in this language: Woe [*ḥaval*] to you, ben Azzai, that you did not serve Rabbi Akiva** properly.

Rabbi Akiva's answer is deliberately terse: *u'kesha'ar devarim ken nedarim* — just as in all other laws, so too with vows. Ben Azzai famously lamented — *chavalo l'cha Ben Azzai shelo shimasht et Rabbi Akiva* — that he had not attended upon Rabbi Akiva, implying the answer was so compressed that one needed deep familiarity with Rabbi Akiva's thinking to grasp it fully.

## Key Principle

### Ma'amar Presupposed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּשְׁמְעִתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי? דְּקָתָנֵי: בֵּין שֶׁעָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר.
>
> **Translation:** **is it taught** in the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Ami?** The Gemara explains that support for Rabbi Ami's opinion is found in **that which is taught** in the *baraita*, as Rabbi Akiva mentions the following distinction: **Whether he performed levirate betrothal or whether he did not perform levirate betrothal.** This indicates that Rabbi Eliezer's statement is referring to a *yevama* with whom he performed levirate betrothal.

The *Gemara* now draws the conclusion that the *braita* confirms Rabbi Ami's *ukeimta*. The evidence: Rabbi Akiva himself says *bein asah ma'amar bein lo asah ma'amar* — whether or not he performed *ma'amar*, it makes no difference. This statement only makes sense if *ma'amar* was already on the table as a live option in the debate, which confirms that the scenario under discussion was one where *ma'amar* was part of the picture.

### Ba'at Lereshuto

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי מֵרֵישָׁא דְּקָתָנֵי: מִשֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִגְמְרָה לוֹ. וְאִי דְּלָא קַדֵּישׁ, מַאי ״נִגְמְרָה לוֹ״? תִּפְשׁוֹט מִינֵּיהּ כְּשֶׁעָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** support can be derived **from the first clause** of the *baraita*, **which teaches: Once she enters his jurisdiction, she is fully** under **his** authority. **If** the *baraita* is referring to a situation **where he did not betroth** her in levirate betrothal, in **what** sense is **she fully** under **his** authority? **Deduce from it** that the situation is one **in which he has performed levirate betrothal.**

Further confirmation comes from the *braita*'s formulation. The phrase *isha sheyesh lo chelek bah... mishebat'a lereshuto* — referring to the *yevamah* who has come further into his domain — can only refer to a state after *ma'amar*. If *ba'at lereshuto* meant the consummation of *yibum* through *bi'ah*, she would already be his wife and the debate would be meaningless. The phrase therefore presupposes that something beyond mere *zika* has occurred but that full *yibum* has not yet been completed — which is precisely the state after *ma'amar*. This confirms *ka pashit mineih d'keshe'asah bah ma'amar* — one can derive from this passage that the entire debate is framed around the *ma'amar* scenario.

### No Stoning = No Eshet Ish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְכִשְׁאָר דְּבָרִים כֵּן נְדָרִים״ דְּקָתָנֵי? אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: אִי אַתָּה מוֹדֶה שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין סְקִילָה, כְּנַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara then asks with regard to the *baraita* itself. **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **And** just **as** in **other matters** there is no such distinction, **so** too with regard to **vows, that** Rabbi Akiva **teaches** in the *baraita*? **Rava said: This is what** Rabbi Akiva **is teaching: Do you not concede that** one is **not liable to** be punished with **stoning** for adulterous relations with a *yevama* **as** he would be if she were **a betrothed young woman?** The status of the relationship is inferior to proper marriage, as one who engages in sexual intercourse with a *yevama* does not incur the death penalty. Accordingly, the authority of the *yavam* with regard to vows is also inferior.

Rabbi Akiva's clinching argument is now spelled out by Rava: *ee atah modeh she'ein chayavin sekilah k'na'arah hame'urasah* — you would have to admit that if the *yevamah* were to sleep with another man after *ma'amar*, she would not be liable to stoning as would a *na'arah hame'urasah*, the betrothed maiden. She is not *eshet ish* in the fullest sense. *U'kesha'ar devarim ken nedarim* — therefore, just as she lacks the full status of *eshet ish* in all other areas of law, so too with respect to *nedarim* she is not fully under his authority. No matter how robust the *zika*, no matter that *ma'amar* was performed, no matter that only one brother is in the picture — unless the relationship rises to the level of genuine *eshet ish*, the *yavam* cannot be *mefir*.

### Final Mishnah Phrase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא: אֵין יְבָמָה גְּמוּרָה לְאִישָׁהּ כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֲרוּסָה גְּמוּרָה לְאִישָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: The mishna** (74a) **is also precisely** formulated to indicate this, as it teaches: **A *yevama* is not her husband's full-fledged** wife **in the same manner that a betrothed woman** is **her husband's full-fledged** wife.

The *Gemara* concludes by returning to the closing words of the *mishnah* itself, as cited by Rav Ashi: *ein yevamah gemurah l'ish keshem she'arusah gemurah l'ish* — the *yevamah* is not fully this man's wife in the way that a betrothed woman is fully her husband's *arusah*. This formulation encapsulates Rabbi Akiva's position: whatever the weight of *zika*, whatever the effect of *ma'amar*, the *yevamah* remains in a categorically incomplete relationship — not a fully constituted *eshet ish* — and therefore the *yavam* cannot perform *hafara*.
