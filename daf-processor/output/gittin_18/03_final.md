# Gittin 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zman in Get Review

### Yochanan v. Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? קָסָבַר: יֵשׁ לְבַעַל פֵּירוֹת עַד שְׁעַת נְתִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan did not say in accordance with** the reason of **Reish Lakish?** The Gemara answers that **he holds the produce belongs to the husband until the time of** the **giving** of the bill of divorce, and only afterward does the woman have the rights to the produce of her property. If she attempts to collect the value of the produce sold after her divorce, she will be asked to prove when she received the bill of divorce. Therefore, in terms of assisting her to collect these monies, the dating of the bill of divorce does not serve any purpose.

The daf opens with a brief review of the central debate that occupied the preceding discussion: the argument between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning when a *get* takes effect, and what precisely is the purpose of requiring a *zman* — a date — in a *get*. The *mishna* under examination deals with the permutations that arise when a *get* is begun on one day and completed on the following night, and whether such a document is valid given that technically the writing (*ketivah*) and the signing (*chatima*) occurred on different calendar days.

### Two Reasons for Zman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְנוּת לָא שְׁכִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Adultery is infrequent,** and the Sages would not institute the dating of a bill of divorce to avoid an infrequent problem.

Two major reasons were proposed for why *chazal* instituted the requirement of a *zman* in a *get*. The first is to protect a wife from wrongful execution: if a husband were to predate a *get*, his wife could claim she was already divorced prior to any alleged act of adultery, thus escaping punishment she would otherwise deserve. Without a *zman*, she could always claim she was divorced earlier than the affair occurred. The second reason concerns the purchaser of *nichsei milog* — the wife's personal property over which the husband holds usufruct rights during the marriage. The question is until when the husband may legitimately sell the produce (*peirot*) of that property. If the *get* is predated, a buyer of those *peirot* might be disadvantaged, purchasing produce the husband was no longer entitled to sell. The later the husband's rights terminate, the more exposure the purchaser faces; the earlier the termination, the more circumscribed his ability to transact.

### Rabbi Shimon Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָא מַכְשַׁיר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – דְּמַכְשַׁיר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **Granted, according to Reish Lakish, due to that** reason **Rabbi Shimon deems valid** a bill of divorce that was signed on the day after it was written, because he holds that the rights to the produce of usufruct property revert to the woman the moment the bill of divorce is written. She is therefore within her rights to collect these monies from the date written in the bill of divorce, even if it was given at a later date. **However, according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds that the reason for writing the date is to prevent the husband from shielding his wife from punishment for her infidelity, **what is the reason that Rabbi Shimon deems** it **valid?** There is still a concern that he will have the bill of divorce written and dated earlier in order to protect her.

These two foundational reasons allow us to understand the debate between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma*. The central question is: when does the husband's right to the *peirot* end? Rabbi Shimon, who is *machshir* — who validates — what is called a *get mukdam*, a predated *get*, holds that the husband loses his rights at the earliest possible time. If someone produces a *get* bearing an earlier date, Rabbi Shimon is unconcerned, because in his view the husband was in any case already divested of his rights to the *peirot* from the time of writing. Whether one follows Rabbi Yochanan or Reish Lakish regarding the precise moment this takes effect — the *ketivah*, the *chatima*, or the *netinah* — Rabbi Shimon places that cutoff earlier than either of them, and that is the basis of his leniency.

By contrast, the *Tanna Kamma* holds that the operative moment is later. Accordingly, if a *get* bears an earlier date than when it actually took legal effect, there is a problematic gap during which the husband might have sold *peirot* he should not have. It is precisely this gap that renders the predated *get* unacceptable to the *Tanna Kamma*, regardless of whether one follows Rabbi Yochanan or Reish Lakish on the specific timing question.

## Takanah Challenges

### Psulim Gets Kelali

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִים, וְאִם נִיסַּת – הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר; מָה הוֹעִילוּ חֲכָמִים בְּתַקָּנָתָן? אַהֲנוֹ דִּלְכַתְּחִילָּה לָא תִּינָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara records a series of questions with regard to the parameters of the ordinance that bills of divorce should be dated. **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** It was taught in a mishna (86a): **Three bills of divorce are invalid, but if** a woman **married** after she received one of these bills of divorce then **the offspring is** of **unflawed** lineage, meaning that the husband and wife are divorced after the fact. One of the three bills of divorce listed is a bill of divorce that does not have a date. Abaye asks: Being that the divorce does take effect, **what did the Sages accomplish with their ordinance** requiring that the date appear on a bill of divorce? Either way the bill of divorce is valid after the fact without a date. The Gemara answers: **It is effective** in **that she may not marry *ab initio*** as a result of receiving this bill of divorce, which consequently limits the usage of such a bill of divorce.

The discussion turns to a pointed challenge against the entire framework of the *takanat chazal* requiring a *zman*. The *Gemara* introduces the teaching cited by Abaye to Rav Yosef: there are three categories of *gittin* that are *psulim* — invalidated — yet if a woman went ahead and remarried using one, the children born of that subsequent marriage are not considered *mamzerim*. One of those three defective *gittin* is a *get* that has witnesses but lacks a *zman*. This prompts the larger question: if such a *get* can in practice enable remarriage without producing *mamzerim*, what exactly did *chazal* accomplish with their *takanah* requiring a *zman*?

### Circumventing Zman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּזְיֵיהּ לִזְמַן דִּידֵיהּ, וְיַהֲבֵיהּ נִיהֲלַהּ, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְרַמַּאי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continued to ask of Rav Yosef: If the husband **cut out its date** after the dated bill of divorce was written **and gave it to her, what** is the *halakha*? May the bill of divorce be used *ab initio*? **He said to him: We are not concerned about a deceiver.** The ordinance requiring that the bill of divorce be dated does not apply in this case, as it was dated when it was written.

The *Gemara* then raises two ways a bad actor might circumvent the *takanah*, putting pressure on its effectiveness. First, one might simply instruct the *sofer* to write the *get* without any *zman* at all. The *Gemara* dismisses this: *l'chatchila* no one would do this, because the *sofer* would refuse. The requirement is well-known enough that a competent scribe would decline to write a *get* without a date. This practical deterrent gives the *takanah* real force.

But the *Gemara* presses further: what if the *sofer* writes the *get* with a proper *zman*, and then the husband physically cuts out the date and delivers the document without it? The *Gemara* responds: we do not suspect people for such games — *lo micheshvinan*. The concern does not rise to the level requiring us to legislate against every possible manipulation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב בּוֹ שָׁבוּעַ, שָׁנָה, חֹדֶשׁ, שַׁבָּת, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּשֵׁר. וּמָה הוֹעִילוּ חֲכָמִים בְּתַקָּנָתָן?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continued and asked: If the date **written in** the bill of divorce made reference only to the **seven-**year Sabbatical cycle in which it was written, or only to the **year,** or only to the **month,** or only to the **week,** but the precise date was not recorded, then **what** is the *halakha*? May such a bill of divorce be used *ab initio*? **He said to him:** It is **valid** to be used *ab initio*. Abaye asked him: If so, **what did the Sages accomplish with their ordinance** requiring the dating of the bill of divorce? Regardless of the reason for recording the date, such vague dating will not ameliorate the problem.

A third objection is then raised. What if the *zman* is written, but imprecisely — not the full date, but only the year, or the *shemita* cycle, or the month, or the week? Does such vagueness render the *takanah* ineffective? The *Gemara* rules that even such a document is *kasher*. And to the obvious follow-up — if so, what does the *takanah* accomplish if you can write something so general? — the *Gemara* answers: it at least delimits the time to the immediately preceding and immediately following units. If you wrote the *shemita* cycle but not the day, you know it was not the prior cycle and not the next one. That is meaningful precision, even if imperfect. And this is no different from the way we always write a *get* with the day but not the hour — we have always tolerated some degree of temporal imprecision. Writing the day tells us it was not yesterday and not tomorrow, and writing the *shemita* year tells us it was not the prior one and not the subsequent one. The *takanah* therefore always confers something, even when the *zman* is approximate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְנוֹ לְשָׁבוּעַ דְּקַמֵּיהּ וּלְשָׁבוּעַ דְּבָתְרֵיהּ. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, יוֹמָא גּוּפֵיהּ – מִי יָדְעִינַן אִי מִצַּפְרָא אִי מִפַּנְיָא?! אֶלָּא לְיוֹמָא דְּקַמֵּיהּ וּלְיוֹמָא דְּבָתְרֵיהּ; הָכָא נָמֵי, אַהְנִי לְשָׁבוּעַ דְּקַמֵּיהּ וּלְשָׁבוּעַ דְּבָתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef answered: **It helps for the seven-year period before it and the seven-year period after it.** If witnesses testify that the woman committed adultery before this seven-year period, then she is liable; if the husband sells produce after this seven-year period, then the woman can recover it. The reason for this is **because if you do not say so,** i.e., that a limited benefit is enough to justify the ordinance, then on that **day itself, do we know if** it was written **in the morning or in the evening?** All of the issues that were mentioned before could apply also to that day itself. **Rather,** the date is effective **for the day before it and the day after it. Here also,** writing the seven-year period **is effective for the seven-year period before it and the seven-year period after it.**

### Takanah Effectiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: כַּתְבֵיהּ וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ בְּכִיסְתֵּיהּ – דְּאִי מִפַּיְּיסָהּ תִּיפַּיַּיס, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava:** If **he writes** the bill of divorce, **and puts it in his pocket** without giving it to her, as he thought **that if she appeases [*mippayyesa*]** him **he will be appeased, what** is the *halakha* when he then does give it to her? Since the date on the document is incorrect, did the Sages institute an ordinance that such a bill of divorce should not be used?

A further challenge is posed by Ravina to Rava: what if the husband did everything correctly — wrote a proper *get* with a full *zman* — but then simply held onto it, placing it in his pocket in the hope that reconciliation might come? He thought things were rocky, wrote the *get* as a precaution, but then underwent a period of deliberation. Now the *get* is delivered much later than it was written. What did the *takanah* accomplish in this interval?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מַקְדֵּים אִינָשׁ פּוּרְעָנוּתָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: A person does not hasten a calamity on himself** and will not ordinarily prepare a bill of divorce in advance. Rather, he will wait until he is ready to divorce his wife. Since this scenario is unlikely, the Sages did not see a need to institute an ordinance pertaining to it.

Rava replies: a man does not hasten his own misfortune. If the husband genuinely hopes the marriage will work out, he would not write a *get* in the first place. The very act of writing signals resolve; one does not initiate divorce proceedings in anticipation of a trip to marriage counseling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: גִּיטִּין הַבָּאִים מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, דְּמִיכַּתְבִי בְּנִיסָן וְלָא מָטוּ עַד תִּשְׁרֵי – מָה הוֹעִילוּ חֲכָמִים בְּתַקָּנָתָם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנְהוּ, קָלָא אִית לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** When there are **bills of divorce that come from a country overseas which are written,** for example, **in** the month of **Nisan, and they do not reach** the woman **until** the month of **Tishrei, what did the Sages accomplish with their ordinance** to date the bill of divorce? All of the concerns about an undated bill of divorce still apply. **He said to him:** Concerning **these** bills of divorce from overseas, it is **public knowledge** that they do not take effect from the date on which they were written. Therefore, if there is a conflict over produce the husband has sold, the woman must bring witnesses to testify as to when she received her bill of divorce.

Then Ravina poses the question to Rav Ashi regarding *gittin* that arrive from *medinat hayam* — from overseas. A *get* written in Nisan may not be delivered until Tishrei, leaving six months or more between *ketivah* and *netinah*. How does the *takanah* help in such cases? The *Gemara* answers: *hani kala it lehu* — matters like this are publicly known. Rashi explains that the interim period raises concerns about both *zenut* and *peirot*, and in both cases the burden falls on her to bring proof of when the *get* was actually received. People will investigate and inquire, and the husband will not be able to transact freely with her property during the gap, because the community is aware of the circumstances. Whether the concern is adultery or the sale of *peirot*, public knowledge serves as a check: no one will be misled into thinking the *get* was delivered when it was not, because everyone knows it came from abroad and can calculate the timeline.

## Three-Month Wait

### Two Wives Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מֵאֵימָתַי מוֹנִין לַגֵּט? רַב אָמַר: מִשְּׁעַת נְתִינָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מִשְּׁעַת כְּתִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages decreed that it is prohibited for a woman to remarry within three months of her divorce or her becoming a widow, in order to prevent a situation of doubt concerning the paternity of a child. **It was stated: From when** does one begin **counting** the three months **with regard to a bill of divorce? Rav says: From the time of** the **giving** of the bill of divorce, **and Shmuel says: From the time of** the **writing** of the bill of divorce.

The *Gemara* now pivots to a distinct question: from what point do we begin counting the three-month waiting period that a divorced woman must observe before remarrying? The question is introduced via a case of two wives living in the same courtyard, both given *gittin* by the same husband at the same time, but whose *gittin* bear different *zmanim* because they were written at different times. This would create an odd disparity — one wife appears to be divorced a month earlier than the other, despite both receiving their *gittin* simultaneously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נָתָן בַּר הוֹשַׁעְיָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, יוֹמְרוּ: שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּחָצֵר אַחַת – זוֹ אֲסוּרָה וְזוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: זוֹ זְמַן גִּיטָּהּ מוֹכִיחַ עָלֶיהָ, וְזוֹ זְמַן גִּיטָּהּ מוֹכִיחַ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Natan bar Hoshaya objects to this: According to** the statement of **Shmuel,** now people **will say:** If **two women** who live **in one courtyard** received their bills of divorce at the same time, but the first document was written at the time of the divorce and the second one was written earlier, then **this** woman **is** still **prohibited** from remarrying, as three months have not yet passed from when her bill of divorce was written; **and that** woman is already **permitted** to remarry. This does not seem reasonable. **Abaye said to him:** This is not difficult. With regard to **this** woman, **the date of her bill of divorce proves concerning her** that she may not remarry yet; **and** with regard to **that** woman, **the date on her bill of divorce proves concerning her** that she may remarry already.

Abaye resolves this by noting that *zmana mochiach aleha* — the date on each *get* speaks for itself. Since the *zmanim* are different, people can see that they were written at different times, and there is no confusion.

### Rav Netinah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְנִשְׁתַּהָה שָׁלִיחַ בַּדֶּרֶךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, כְּשֶׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ – צְרִיכָה לְהַמְתִּין שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים; וּלְגֵט יָשָׁן אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין, שֶׁהֲרֵי לֹא נִתְיַיחֵד עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute, it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav,** and it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel.** The Gemara explains: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav** that the three-month count begins from when the bill of divorce is given: In the case of **one who sends a bill of divorce to his wife, and the agent tarries on the way for three months, once the bill of divorce reaches her hand she must wait three months** before remarrying. **And one need not be concerned that** it is **an outdated bill of divorce,** i.e., that the husband and wife were secluded after it was written, rendering the bill of divorce invalid, **because he was not secluded with her** during the time the bill of divorce was being delivered.

Rav holds that the three-month waiting period begins at the time of *netinah* — delivery. A *baraita* supports this: if an agent was entrusted with a *get* to deliver to a man's wife, and the agent was delayed three months on the road, when the *get* finally reaches the wife she must wait an additional three months from the moment of delivery. If the period began from writing, she could remarry immediately upon receiving the *get*, since three months have already elapsed. The *baraita*'s ruling that she must wait further supports Rav's position that delivery is the operative moment.

The *baraita* adds that in this case we are not concerned about *get yashan* — the problem of an "old *get*" — because the husband did not have *yichud* with his wife in the interim. The concept of *get yashan* refers not necessarily to a *get* that is old in terms of calendar time, but to the concern that if the husband wrote a *get* and then cohabited with his wife before delivering it, we may infer that he reconsidered the divorce. Even if the *get* was written and delivered on the same day, if he slept with her in between, it becomes a *get yashan*. In the case of delivery from overseas, however, the parties are clearly not together, so there is no possibility of *yichud*, and the concern is entirely absent.

### Shmuel Ketivah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: הַמַּשְׁלִישׁ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: אַל תִּתְּנֵהוּ לָהּ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, מִשֶּׁנְּתָנוֹ לָהּ – מוּתֶּרֶת לִינָּשֵׂא מִיָּד; וּלְגֵט יָשָׁן אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין, שֶׁהֲרֵי לֹא נִתְיַיחֵד עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in another *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel** that the count begins from the time of the writing: In the case of **one who** is about to travel and **deposits a bill of divorce for his wife** with a trustee **and says to him: Give it to her only after three months, once he gives it to her, she is permitted to marry immediately** and is not required to wait an additional three months. **And one need not** be **concerned that** it is **an outdated bill of divorce, as he was not secluded with her** in the interim.

Shmuel holds that the three-month period begins at the time of *ketivah* — writing. A supporting *baraita* presents the case of a man who entrusted a *get* to an agent with the instruction not to deliver it for three months. The *baraita* rules that once the agent receives the *get*, the wife may remarry immediately upon delivery — because the three months have already been counted from the time of writing. This clearly supports Shmuel's position. The *baraita* likewise adds that we are not concerned about *get yashan* because the husband had no *yichud* with her in the interim.

### Final Ruling Ketivah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב פַּפֵּי וְרַב אָשֵׁי – עָבְדִי מִשְּׁעַת כְּתִיבָה, רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ – עָבְדִי מִשְּׁעַת נְתִינָה. וְהִלְכְתָא: מִשְּׁעַת כְּתִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is told that **Rav Kahana and Rav Pappi and Rav Ashi would in practice** count three months **from the time of** the **writing,** but **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, would in practice** count these three months **from the time of** the **giving. And the *halakha*** is that the counting begins **from the time of** the **writing.**

Among the later *amoraim*, Rav Kahana, Rav Pappa, and Rav Ashi ruled in practice according to the time of *ketivah*, while Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua ruled according to the time of *netinah*. The *Gemara* concludes: *v'hilchata mishaat ketivah* — the final ruling is that the three-month period begins from the time of writing.

## Ketubah Shemittah

### Ketubah Not Cancelled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מֵאֵימָתַי כְּתוּבָּה מְשַׁמֶּטֶת?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses another dispute between Rav and Shmuel with regard to documents. According to the terms of a marriage contract, a widow is entitled to payment of its value upon the death of her husband. By Torah law, all outstanding debts are canceled at the close of the Sabbatical Year. A woman need not take her marriage settlement as soon as her husband dies. The issue at hand is at what point the marriage contract generates a concrete debt that will be canceled by the Sabbatical Year. **It was stated: From when** is the debt established by **a marriage contract canceled** in the Sabbatical Year?

The *Gemara* now raises a question that might seem surprising at first but proves remarkably relevant: is a *ketubah* cancelled by *shemita*? The *shemita* year cancels debts that are outstanding and collectable. However, not all financial obligations are treated as ordinary debts. Obligations that qualify as *tnai beis din* — conditions established by rabbinic enactment rather than purely private agreement — are not cancelled, because they are classified differently from personal loans. The *ketubah* is paradigmatically such an obligation: *chazal* instituted it as a condition of marriage, and a man is halachically forbidden to cohabit with his wife without one. It thus carries the character of a *beis din*-type imposition rather than a voluntary transaction.

In the ordinary case, therefore, a wife's *ketubah* is not cancelled by *shemita*. But what about a case in which the couple is no longer married — she is divorced or he has died — and the *ketubah* has become collectable but has not yet been paid? At that point, does it shift from a *tnai beis din* obligation to an ordinary private debt subject to *shemita*? That is the question the *Gemara* addresses.

### Rav Two Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: מִשֶּׁתִּפְגּוֹם – וְתִזְקוֹף. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: פָּגְמָה – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא זָקְפָה, זָקְפָה – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא פָּגְמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** The marriage contract becomes like a debt and will be canceled in the Sabbatical Year **from** the time **when** the woman **collects partial payment and establishes** the rest as a debt in court. **And Shmuel says:** This occurs when **she** either **collects partial payment although she did not establish** it as debt, or **she established** the entire value of the marriage contract as a debt in court **although she did not collect partial payment.**

Rav holds that a *ketubah* becomes subject to cancellation by *shemita* only when two conditions have both been met. The first is *pegimah* — she has begun to collect, having received a partial payment of her *ketubah*. The second is *zekifah* — she has explicitly converted the remaining balance into a loan, declaring that what is owed to her should be treated as a monetary debt to be repaid. Only when both conditions are satisfied does the *ketubah* lose its *tnai beis din* character and become vulnerable to cancellation by *shemita*.

The logic is that *pegimah* alone — beginning to collect — signals that she is actively pursuing payment and the *ketubah* is now "live" in the sense of being processed. But the remaining unpaid balance is still a *tnai beis din* obligation unless she affirmatively converts it into a loan. Conversely, *zekifah* alone — declaring it a loan without yet having received any payment — also does not fully transform the obligation. Only when both are present has the *ketubah* truly migrated from the domain of *beis din* enactment into the realm of ordinary personal debt.

### Shmuel One Condition

Shmuel disagrees and holds that either condition alone is sufficient. If she has begun collecting (*pegamah*), even without declaring the remainder a loan, the *ketubah* becomes subject to *shemita*. And if she has declared it a loan (*zakfah*), even without having yet received any payment, that too is sufficient to render the remaining amount cancellable. Either act of converting the obligation from its *tnai beis din* status into something more like a private transaction is enough.

### Rav Baraita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: מֵאֵימָתַי כְּתוּבָּה מְשַׁמֶּטֶת? מִשֶּׁתִּפְגּוֹם וְתִזְקוֹף. פָּגְמָה וְלֹא זָקְפָה, זָקְפָה וְלֹא פָּגְמָה – אֵינָהּ מְשַׁמֶּטֶת, עַד שֶׁתִּפְגּוֹם וְתִזְקוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav: From when is** the debt established by **a marriage contract canceled? From when she accepts partial payment and establishes** the rest as a debt in court. However, if **she accepted partial payment but did not establish** the debt, or **established** the debt **but did not accept partial payment,** then **it is not canceled until she accepts partial payment and establishes** the debt.

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that explicitly supports Rav's position. The *baraita* states: when is a *ketubah* cancelled by *shemita*? When she has both engaged in *pegimah* and performed *zekifah*. It then specifies: if she performed *pegimah* without *zekifah*, or *zekifah* without *pegimah*, the *ketubah* is not cancelled — not until she has done both. This is about as explicit as a *baraita* can be.

### Women's Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: אוֹנֶס, וּקְנָס, וּפִיתּוּי, וּכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה – שֶׁזְּקָפָן בְּמִלְוָה, מְשַׁמְּטִין; וְאִם לָאו – אֵין מְשַׁמְּטִין. מֵאֵימָתַי נִזְקָפִים בְּמִלְוָה? מִשְּׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in another *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel:** If the fine and damages paid by one who commits **rape; or** the **fine** paid by one who falsely claims his wife was not a virgin at the time of their wedding; **or** the fine paid for **seduction; or** the debt established by **a woman's marriage contract was established as a loan,** then the Sabbatical Year **cancels it. And if** one did **not** establish these payments as loans, then the Sabbatical Year **does not cancel them. From when** is it considered that these payments **have been established as a loan? From the time of standing trial,** because the abstract obligation to pay becomes a debt once the court ruled that one is required to pay.

The *baraita* cited in support of Shmuel enumerates several categories of obligations arising from situations involving women — *ones*, *knas*, *pitui*, and *ketuvat isha* — and states that these are cancelled by *shemita* when the obligation has been converted into a loan (*shezakfam b'malveh*). The process of *zekifah* is defined as *mishe'amda badin* — when the matter has been brought before a court and adjudicated, at which point the obligation is formally established as a monetary debt.

This *baraita* mentions only *zekifah* and says nothing about *pegimah*, which might appear to support Shmuel: perhaps *zekifah* alone, without *pegimah*, is sufficient to trigger *shemita* cancellation. However, the *baraita* does not explicitly say that *pegimah* is unnecessary — it simply does not address it. One could argue that the *baraita* presupposes that *pegimah* has already occurred, or at minimum that the absence of *pegimah* from the text does not prove that *zekifah* alone is enough. The *baraita* thus only partially supports Shmuel — it shows that *zekifah* without an explicit mention of *pegimah* can trigger cancellation, but it does not address whether *pegimah* alone, without *zekifah*, would also suffice. Shmuel claims both directions independently, and the *baraita* establishes only one leg of his position. Unlike the other debates discussed earlier in the *daf*, the *Gemara* here does not issue a final ruling between Rav and Shmuel.

## Ketubah Parallel

### Shmuel Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּתוּבָּה כְּמַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין דָּמְיָא – מָה מַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין נִכְתָּבִין בַּיּוֹם וְנֶחְתָּמִין בַּלַּיְלָה, אַף כְּתוּבָּה נִכְתֶּבֶת בַּיּוֹם וְנֶחְתֶּמֶת בַּלַּיְלָה. כְּתוּבְּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אִיכְּתוּב בַּיּוֹם וְאִיחֲתוּם בַּלַּיְלָה; הֲוָה רַב הָתָם וְלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses other aspects of the marriage contract. **Shmuel said: A marriage contract is** considered to be **like a court enactment. Just as court enactments may be written during the day and** then **signed at night,** and the fact that the writing and signing were not performed on the same day does not present a problem, **so too, a marriage contract may be written during the day and** then **signed at night.** It is told: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav's marriage contract was written during the day and** then **signed at night,** and **Rav was there and he did not say anything to** the people who wrote and signed it. This indicates that Rav agrees with the ruling of Shmuel.

Shmuel articulates an important principle: a *ketubah* is comparable to a *ma'aseh beis din* — an act of court. Just as documents issued by *beis din* may be written during the day and signed at night without the intervening time rendering the document invalid, so too a *ketubah* may be written during the day and signed at night. This is directly relevant to the question that has run throughout the *daf* — the concern about *get mukdam* and the gap between *ketivah* and *chatima*.

### Chiya Ketubah

A practical illustration is offered: the *ketubah* of Rabbi Chiya bar Rav was written on one day and signed on a different day, and Rav was present and raised no objection. One might infer from his silence that he agrees with Shmuel's position. However, the *Gemara* qualifies this inference.

### Same Matter Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כִּשְׁמוּאֵל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? עֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן הֲווֹ. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי צָדוֹק: לֹא שָׁנוּ, אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁאֵין עֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן, אֲבָל עֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel?** The Gemara rejects this: This cannot be proven from here because the scribe and witnesses **were** continuously **engaged in that matter** of writing the marriage contract, from when it was written in the day until they signed it at night, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok said:** The Sages **taught** that a document written during the day and signed at night is invalid **only when** the scribe and witnesses **were not** continuously **engaged in that matter, but** if they **were** continuously **engaged in that matter** until nightfall, then it is **valid.**

The reason Rav did not object in that case was not because he agreed with Shmuel's principle in the abstract, but because the parties were still *oskim b'oto inyan* — engaged in the same matter. When those writing and signing a document remain continuously involved in the same transaction or proceeding throughout, the gap between writing and signing does not introduce the kinds of concerns that would otherwise arise. A *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yosi supports this: the rule that writing in the day and signing at night is invalid applies only when the parties are no longer engaged in the same matter, but if they remain so engaged throughout, it is *kasher*. This leniency does not extend indefinitely — if the gap grows to ten days or more, or the parties are no longer involved in the same proceeding, the concern reasserts itself.

## Rabbi Shimon Scope

### Rabbi Shimon Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר: אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? קָסָבַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו לְגָרְשָׁהּ, שׁוּב אֵין לוֹ פֵּירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **Rabbi Shimon deems valid** a bill of divorce written during the day and signed at night. **Rava said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon?** He **holds** that **once** the husband **has decided to divorce her, he no longer has** the rights to **the produce** from his wife's property, and it is clear that he has decided to divorce her when he writes a bill of divorce. Consequently, the woman obtains the rights to the produce from the moment the bill of divorce was written, regardless of when it was signed.

The *Gemara* now takes up the formal analysis of why Rabbi Shimon is *machshir* a *get mukdam*, a predated *get*. Rava explains: Rabbi Shimon holds that *keivan shenatan einav l'garshah, shuv ein lo piyus* — once the husband has set his mind to divorce, he can no longer be appeased or reconciled. The act of *piyus* — reconciliation — is no longer available to him. The concrete moment that marks this irreversible decision is the writing of the *get*. From that point forward, whatever appears in the *get* as the date is treated as though the divorce effectively began at that time, because the husband's intent was firm and irrevocable. This is why predating is tolerable according to Rabbi Shimon: no practical harm flows from an earlier date, since the husband's rights over the *peirot* were already forfeit from the moment he committed to the divorce through the act of writing.

### Lakish Limits Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא הִכְשִׁיר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֶלָּא לְאַלְתַּר, אֲבָל מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים – לֹא;
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: Rabbi Shimon deems** such a bill of divorce **valid only** if it is signed **immediately,** when the witnesses signed the bill of divorce during the night after it was written. **However,** if the delay in signing was **from now,** i.e., when the bill of divorce was written, **until ten days** have elapsed, i.e., when there is an extended delay, he does **not** deem it valid.

Reish Lakish, however, limits Rabbi Shimon's leniency sharply. He holds that Rabbi Shimon spoke only about the immediate case: the *get* was written that same day and signed that same night — *l'altar*, immediately. The window between *ketivah* and *chatima* that Rabbi Shimon permits is only that narrow span of a day and the following night. Extending the gap to ten days is something Reish Lakish does not permit under Rabbi Shimon's leniency. The concern is *shema piyes* — perhaps during that longer period the husband was reconciled with his wife, in which case the *get* would no longer represent a firm and irrevocable decision but a superseded one.

### Yochanan Expands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא פִּיֵּיס. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים; אִם אִיתָא דְּפַיֵּיס – קָלָא אִית לַיהּ לְמִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Why not? Because **we are concerned** that **perhaps he appeased** his wife and engaged in sexual intercourse with her, thereby invalidating the bill of divorce. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** if the delay in signing was **from now until ten days** later Rabbi Shimon deems it valid. There is no concern that he appeased her because **if it is so** that **he appeased** her, then **the matter** becomes **public knowledge,** and all would hear of it.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and permits a window of up to ten days even within Rabbi Shimon's framework. His reasoning: *ei ita d'piyes, kala it lehu l'milta* — if the parties had truly reconciled, people would know. Reconciliation is not a private matter; it manifests in behavior that the community observes. If a couple that was known to be headed for divorce is suddenly seen together again, going out, resuming their life — people talk. Since we would hear about a reconciliation, we are not concerned that one occurred silently during the ten-day gap. Rabbi Yochanan therefore permits relying on Rabbi Shimon's leniency for that extended period.

## Ten People Get

### Ten People Command

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר לַעֲשָׂרָה ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״; אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁנַיִם מִשּׁוּם עֵדִים, וְכוּלָּם מִשּׁוּם תְּנַאי. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִשּׁוּם עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish disagreed with regard to the following case: If the husband **says to ten** men: **Write a bill of divorce for my wife, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He means that all of them should sign the bill of divorce. **Two** of them function **as witnesses, and all** the rest **of them** sign the bill of divorce only **due to** the **stipulation** that the husband had stated. He made the bill of divorce dependent upon all of them signing, but he did not mean that ten men need to actually function as witnesses. **And Reish Lakish says: All of them** function **as witnesses.**

The *Gemara* introduces a new case: a husband instructs ten people to write a *get* for his wife. This requires interpretation. It cannot mean that all ten physically write the document — that would be neither practical nor halachically coherent. What then does he intend? The answer must lie in the signing. But a *get* requires only two witnesses to sign. So what role do the other eight play?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״כּוּלְּכֶם״; וְהָא תְּנַן, אָמַר לַעֲשָׂרָה: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״ – אֶחָד כּוֹתֵב וּשְׁנַיִם חוֹתְמִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which all of them have to sign? **If we say** that **he did not say to them: All of you,** but just said to them: You should write a bill of divorce for my wife, **didn't we learn** in a mishna (66b) that if **one said to ten** people: **Write a bill of divorce for my wife,** then **one** would **write, and two** would **sign,** and not all ten of them are required to sign.

A *baraita* clarifies: if a husband says to ten people "write a *get* for my wife" without specifying *kulchem* — "all of you" — then one person writes and two sign. The other seven need not be involved at all. From this, we see that the act of addressing ten people does not inherently require all ten to participate. Rashi explains that in a case where all ten are indeed required to sign — without the word *kulchem* having been used — the husband's intention was to embarrass his wife publicly, using the gathering as a forum for the declaration of divorce. He wanted all those present to know about it. The *get* itself bears only the two standard witnesses, but the husband chose to make the matter public.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ ״כּוּלְּכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the case of their dispute is **when he said to them: All of you** write a bill of divorce for my wife, and in this instance is was taught later on in that mishna that one person writes the bill of divorce and everyone else must sign it.

Given this, the case before us must be one in which the husband explicitly said *kulchem* — all of you — thereby creating an obligation for all ten to participate in the signing. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish turns on what that participation means.

### Yochanan Two Edim

Rabbi Yochanan holds: two of the ten sign as *edim* — as the actual halachic witnesses whose signatures give the *get* its validity — and all the rest sign because of a *tnai*, a condition. The husband, by saying *kulchem*, created a condition that all ten must sign, but only two of them function as witnesses in the technical sense. The remaining eight are satisfying a condition, not serving as witnesses.

### Lakish All Edim

Reish Lakish holds that all ten are *edim* — all ten function as full witnesses. The *get* is not valid until all ten have signed, because all ten were designated as witnesses when the husband invoked *kulchem*.

### Practical Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – דַּחֲתוּם בֵּי תְרֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ בְּיוֹמֵיהּ, וְאִינָךְ מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים. מַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם תְּנַאי – כָּשֵׁר; וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם עֵדִים – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish; what is the difference if the rest of the men function as witnesses or just in order to fulfill the man's stipulation? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** in a case **where two of them signed on that day and the rest** signed not on the day it was written but **from now until ten days** later. According to **the one who says** that the other signatures are **due to the stipulation,** the bill of divorce is **valid,** because two witnesses signed the bill of divorce on the same day that it was written, and the stipulation was also fulfilled. **And** according to **the one who says** that all these people sign **due to** his wish that they all function as **witnesses,** it is **invalid** because not all the witnesses signed the bill of divorce on the day it was written.

What practical difference emerges between these two positions? The *Gemara* identifies two.

The first: if two of the ten signed on one day and the remaining eight signed up to ten days later, according to Rabbi Yochanan — who holds the other eight are signing due to a *tnai* rather than as witnesses — this is unproblematic. A *tnai* carries no defined time requirement; the condition can be fulfilled whenever the parties get around to it. But according to Reish Lakish, who holds all ten are *edim*, all ten witnesses must sign at the same time. A *get* cannot have its witnesses sign across multiple days, and so this arrangement would render the *get* invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶם קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם תְּנַאי – כָּשֵׁר; לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם עֵדִים – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** there is another difference between them, in a case **where one of these** ten people **is found** to be **a relative** of the husband or wife, **or** is otherwise found to be **disqualified** to serve as a witness. **According to the one who says** that the other signatures are **due to the stipulation,** this bill of divorce is **valid** because the disqualified person did not serve as a witness. He just signed the bill of divorce in order to fulfill the stipulation, and the bill of divorce has the signature of two qualified witnesses. **According to the one who says** that all these people sign **due to** the man's wish that they all function as **witnesses,** the bill of divorce would be **invalid,** as is the case whenever one member of a group of witnesses is disqualified.

The second difference: if one of the ten is found to be a *karov* or *pasul* — a disqualified witness — the consequences differ. According to Rabbi Yochanan, only the two designated *edim* need to be valid; if one of the other eight is disqualified, it is of no consequence, because they are signing due to a *tnai* and the *tnai* can simply be fulfilled by the remaining valid signatories. According to Reish Lakish, since all ten are *edim*, any disqualification among the ten affects the validity of the *get*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי חֲתִים בִּתְחִילָּה קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל; אָמְרִי לָהּ כָּשֵׁר, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פָּסוּל. אָמְרִי לָהּ כָּשֵׁר – תְּנַאי הוּא; אָמְרִי לַהּ פָּסוּל – אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בִּשְׁטָרוֹת דְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds another point of dispute: According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who holds that the other witnesses sign due to the husband's stipulation, **if a relative or** other **disqualified** witness **signed at the beginning,** before two valid witnesses had signed, then **some say** the bill of divorce is **valid and some say** that it is **invalid. Some say** that it is **valid** because the request for ten signatures **is a stipulation.** Since these ten people are not meant to serve as witnesses and are meant only to fulfill the stipulation, it makes no difference if they signed at the beginning or at the end. **Some say** that it is **invalid** because there is a concern that the courts **will come to confuse it with typical documents,** and they will rely on disqualified witnesses in those cases.

A further sub-question arises: what if one of the *first* two to sign is found to be *karov* or *pasul*? According to those who say this is still *kasher*, the reasoning is that since the husband did not specify *which* two are the witnesses, the identity of the witnesses is determined by who among the ten is valid, not by the order in which they signed. The first two to sign are not inherently the designated witnesses; any two valid signatories from among the ten fulfill the requirement. The fact that a *pasul* signed first does not corrupt the *get*, because we look to the valid signatures as the operative witnesses. The opposing view holds that this creates a problem of *ati l'ichlofu b'shtar d'alma* — people may mistake the *get* for an ordinary legal document and validate other documents on the strength of a *pasul*'s signature appearing first.

## Rabbi Shimon Ten Days

### Two Days Signing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ לַעֲשָׂרָה: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״; חֲתוּם בֵּי תְרֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ בְּיוֹמֵיהּ, וְאִינָךְ – מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** It is told: There was **a certain person who said to ten** men: **Write a bill of divorce for my wife. Two of them signed that day and the others** signed after a delay **from now until ten days** later. **He came before Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** to ask what the *halakha* is with regard to such a bill of divorce. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **said to him:**

The *Gemara* now describes an actual case: a man said to ten people to write a *get* for his wife, and two of them signed on one day while the remaining signatories signed up to ten days later. The case came before Rabbi Shimon ben Levi.

### Rabbi Shimon Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדַאי הוּא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לִסְמוֹךְ עָלָיו בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon,** who permitted using a bill of divorce that was written during the day and signed at night, **is worthy of being relied upon in exigent circumstances.** Therefore, once such a bill of divorce has been written and given, the woman is divorced.

Rabbi Shimon ben Levi ruled: *k'dai hu Rabbi Shimon lismoch alav b'sha'at had'chak* — Rabbi Shimon is sufficiently authoritative to rely upon in a time of pressing need. The validation requires invoking Rabbi Shimon's leniency that a *get* written and signed at different times can be valid.

### Sha'at Hadeichak

But the language of *sha'at had'chak* — a time of pressing need or hardship — is significant and calls for explanation. If Rabbi Shimon's position is a legitimate *l'chatchila* ruling, why frame reliance upon it as a concession to emergency? Rashi explains: *sha'at had'chak* here refers to cases such as where the husband has since departed and cannot be located to write a new *get*, or where the woman has already remarried on the basis of this *get* and has children — situations where retroactively invalidating the *get* would produce severe consequences, including stigmatizing her children and calling her subsequent marriage into question. These are not abstract halachic exercises but real human crises that require a practical resolution.

### Yochanan Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא הִכְשִׁיר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֶלָּא לְאַלְתַּר, אֲבָל מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים – לָא! בְּהַהִיא, כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But didn't Reish Lakish say: Rabbi Shimon deemed** such a bill of divorce **valid only** if it was signed **immediately, but** after a delay **from now until ten days, no.** The Gemara answers: **With regard to that** question, whether the others must sign immediately, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds that they may sign after a delay.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁנַיִם מִשּׁוּם עֵדִים, וְכוּלָּן מִשּׁוּם תְּנַאי! בְּהָהִיא, כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Two** of them function **as witnesses, and all** the rest **of them** sign the bill of divorce only **due to** the **stipulation?** It is clear that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is concerned about the other witnesses and deems the bill of divorce valid only due to exigent circumstances. The Gemara answers: **With regard to this,** i.e., why the additional people must sign, **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** that all of them function as witnesses.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension: Rabbi Shimon ben Levi holds like Reish Lakish regarding the scope of Rabbi Shimon's leniency — that it applies only to the immediate day and night, not to ten days. But in this case he extends the window to ten days by relying on Rabbi Yochanan's position, which permits that longer interval under Rabbi Shimon's framework. This is a compound ruling: he relies on Rabbi Shimon's leniency for the validity of the *get* despite the gap between signings, and on Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that the window extends to ten days.

Why then is this only a *sha'at had'chak* ruling rather than a *l'chatchila* one? Because Rabbi Shimon ben Levi himself does not hold all these lenient positions *l'chatchila*. He holds primarily like Reish Lakish — who would require all the signatures to be contemporaneous and would limit Rabbi Shimon's window to the immediate day and night. It is only because the case already happened, and because invalidating the *get* would cause genuine hardship — *mishum iguna akilu bah rabanan*, the rabbis were lenient for the sake of preventing women from becoming *agunot* — that he is willing to reach across and rely on the more permissive opinions. This is not rabbi-shopping in the pejorative sense, but rather the established halachic principle that where the stakes involve freeing a woman from an uncertain marital status, authorities have greater latitude to adopt lenient readings than they would otherwise exercise *l'chatchila*.
