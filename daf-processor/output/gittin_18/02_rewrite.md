# Gittin 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zman in Get Review

### Yochanan v. Lakish

The daf opens with a brief review of the central debate that occupied the preceding discussion: the argument between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning when a *get* takes effect, and what precisely is the purpose of requiring a *zman* — a date — in a *get*. The *mishna* under examination deals with the permutations that arise when a *get* is begun on one day and completed on the following night, and whether such a document is valid given that technically the writing (*ketivah*) and the signing (*chatima*) occurred on different calendar days.

### Two Reasons for Zman

Two major reasons were proposed for why *chazal* instituted the requirement of a *zman* in a *get*. The first is to protect a wife from wrongful execution: if a husband were to predate a *get*, his wife could claim she was already divorced prior to any alleged act of adultery, thus escaping punishment she would otherwise deserve. Without a *zman*, she could always claim she was divorced earlier than the affair occurred. The second reason concerns the purchaser of *nichsei milog* — the wife's personal property over which the husband holds usufruct rights during the marriage. The question is until when the husband may legitimately sell the produce (*peirot*) of that property. If the *get* is predated, a buyer of those *peirot* might be disadvantaged, purchasing produce the husband was no longer entitled to sell. The later the husband's rights terminate, the more exposure the purchaser faces; the earlier the termination, the more circumscribed his ability to transact.

### Rabbi Shimon Get

These two foundational reasons allow us to understand the debate between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma*. The central question is: when does the husband's right to the *peirot* end? Rabbi Shimon, who is *machshir* — who validates — what is called a *get mukdam*, a predated *get*, holds that the husband loses his rights at the earliest possible time. If someone produces a *get* bearing an earlier date, Rabbi Shimon is unconcerned, because in his view the husband was in any case already divested of his rights to the *peirot* from the time of writing. Whether one follows Rabbi Yochanan or Reish Lakish regarding the precise moment this takes effect — the *ketivah*, the *chatima*, or the *netinah* — Rabbi Shimon places that cutoff earlier than either of them, and that is the basis of his leniency.

By contrast, the *Tanna Kamma* holds that the operative moment is later. Accordingly, if a *get* bears an earlier date than when it actually took legal effect, there is a problematic gap during which the husband might have sold *peirot* he should not have. It is precisely this gap that renders the predated *get* unacceptable to the *Tanna Kamma*, regardless of whether one follows Rabbi Yochanan or Reish Lakish on the specific timing question.

## Takanah Challenges

### Psulim Gets Kelali

The discussion turns to a pointed challenge against the entire framework of the *takanat chazal* requiring a *zman*. The *Gemara* introduces the teaching cited by Abaye to Rav Yosef: there are three categories of *gittin* that are *psulim* — invalidated — yet if a woman went ahead and remarried using one, the children born of that subsequent marriage are not considered *mamzerim*. One of those three defective *gittin* is a *get* that has witnesses but lacks a *zman*. This prompts the larger question: if such a *get* can in practice enable remarriage without producing *mamzerim*, what exactly did *chazal* accomplish with their *takanah* requiring a *zman*?

### Circumventing Zman

The *Gemara* then raises two ways a bad actor might circumvent the *takanah*, putting pressure on its effectiveness. First, one might simply instruct the *sofer* to write the *get* without any *zman* at all. The *Gemara* dismisses this: *l'chatchila* no one would do this, because the *sofer* would refuse. The requirement is well-known enough that a competent scribe would decline to write a *get* without a date. This practical deterrent gives the *takanah* real force.

But the *Gemara* presses further: what if the *sofer* writes the *get* with a proper *zman*, and then the husband physically cuts out the date and delivers the document without it? The *Gemara* responds: we do not suspect people for such games — *lo micheshvinan*. The concern does not rise to the level requiring us to legislate against every possible manipulation.

A third objection is then raised. What if the *zman* is written, but imprecisely — not the full date, but only the year, or the *shemita* cycle, or the month, or the week? Does such vagueness render the *takanah* ineffective? The *Gemara* rules that even such a document is *kasher*. And to the obvious follow-up — if so, what does the *takanah* accomplish if you can write something so general? — the *Gemara* answers: it at least delimits the time to the immediately preceding and immediately following units. If you wrote the *shemita* cycle but not the day, you know it was not the prior cycle and not the next one. That is meaningful precision, even if imperfect. And this is no different from the way we always write a *get* with the day but not the hour — we have always tolerated some degree of temporal imprecision. Writing the day tells us it was not yesterday and not tomorrow, and writing the *shemita* year tells us it was not the prior one and not the subsequent one. The *takanah* therefore always confers something, even when the *zman* is approximate.

### Takanah Effectiveness

A further challenge is posed by Ravina to Rava: what if the husband did everything correctly — wrote a proper *get* with a full *zman* — but then simply held onto it, placing it in his pocket in the hope that reconciliation might come? He thought things were rocky, wrote the *get* as a precaution, but then underwent a period of deliberation. Now the *get* is delivered much later than it was written. What did the *takanah* accomplish in this interval? Rava replies: a man does not hasten his own misfortune. If the husband genuinely hopes the marriage will work out, he would not write a *get* in the first place. The very act of writing signals resolve; one does not initiate divorce proceedings in anticipation of a trip to marriage counseling.

Then Ravina poses the question to Rav Ashi regarding *gittin* that arrive from *medinat hayam* — from overseas. A *get* written in Nisan may not be delivered until Tishrei, leaving six months or more between *ketivah* and *netinah*. How does the *takanah* help in such cases? The *Gemara* answers: *hani kala it lehu* — matters like this are publicly known. Rashi explains that the interim period raises concerns about both *zenut* and *peirot*, and in both cases the burden falls on her to bring proof of when the *get* was actually received. People will investigate and inquire, and the husband will not be able to transact freely with her property during the gap, because the community is aware of the circumstances. Whether the concern is adultery or the sale of *peirot*, public knowledge serves as a check: no one will be misled into thinking the *get* was delivered when it was not, because everyone knows it came from abroad and can calculate the timeline.

## Three-Month Wait

### Two Wives Case

The *Gemara* now pivots to a distinct question: from what point do we begin counting the three-month waiting period that a divorced woman must observe before remarrying? The question is introduced via a case of two wives living in the same courtyard, both given *gittin* by the same husband at the same time, but whose *gittin* bear different *zmanim* because they were written at different times. This would create an odd disparity — one wife appears to be divorced a month earlier than the other, despite both receiving their *gittin* simultaneously. Abaye resolves this by noting that *zmana mochiach aleha* — the date on each *get* speaks for itself. Since the *zmanim* are different, people can see that they were written at different times, and there is no confusion.

### Rav Netinah

Rav holds that the three-month waiting period begins at the time of *netinah* — delivery. A *baraita* supports this: if an agent was entrusted with a *get* to deliver to a man's wife, and the agent was delayed three months on the road, when the *get* finally reaches the wife she must wait an additional three months from the moment of delivery. If the period began from writing, she could remarry immediately upon receiving the *get*, since three months have already elapsed. The *baraita*'s ruling that she must wait further supports Rav's position that delivery is the operative moment.

The *baraita* adds that in this case we are not concerned about *get yashan* — the problem of an "old *get*" — because the husband did not have *yichud* with his wife in the interim. The concept of *get yashan* refers not necessarily to a *get* that is old in terms of calendar time, but to the concern that if the husband wrote a *get* and then cohabited with his wife before delivering it, we may infer that he reconsidered the divorce. Even if the *get* was written and delivered on the same day, if he slept with her in between, it becomes a *get yashan*. In the case of delivery from overseas, however, the parties are clearly not together, so there is no possibility of *yichud*, and the concern is entirely absent.

### Shmuel Ketivah

Shmuel holds that the three-month period begins at the time of *ketivah* — writing. A supporting *baraita* presents the case of a man who entrusted a *get* to an agent with the instruction not to deliver it for three months. The *baraita* rules that once the agent receives the *get*, the wife may remarry immediately upon delivery — because the three months have already been counted from the time of writing. This clearly supports Shmuel's position. The *baraita* likewise adds that we are not concerned about *get yashan* because the husband had no *yichud* with her in the interim.

### Final Ruling Ketivah

Among the later *amoraim*, Rav Kahana, Rav Pappa, and Rav Ashi ruled in practice according to the time of *ketivah*, while Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua ruled according to the time of *netinah*. The *Gemara* concludes: *v'hilchata mishaat ketivah* — the final ruling is that the three-month period begins from the time of writing.

## Ketubah Shemittah

### Ketubah Not Cancelled

The *Gemara* now raises a question that might seem surprising at first but proves remarkably relevant: is a *ketubah* cancelled by *shemita*? The *shemita* year cancels debts that are outstanding and collectable. However, not all financial obligations are treated as ordinary debts. Obligations that qualify as *tnai beis din* — conditions established by rabbinic enactment rather than purely private agreement — are not cancelled, because they are classified differently from personal loans. The *ketubah* is paradigmatically such an obligation: *chazal* instituted it as a condition of marriage, and a man is halachically forbidden to cohabit with his wife without one. It thus carries the character of a *beis din*-type imposition rather than a voluntary transaction.

In the ordinary case, therefore, a wife's *ketubah* is not cancelled by *shemita*. But what about a case in which the couple is no longer married — she is divorced or he has died — and the *ketubah* has become collectable but has not yet been paid? At that point, does it shift from a *tnai beis din* obligation to an ordinary private debt subject to *shemita*? That is the question the *Gemara* addresses.

### Rav Two Conditions

Rav holds that a *ketubah* becomes subject to cancellation by *shemita* only when two conditions have both been met. The first is *pegimah* — she has begun to collect, having received a partial payment of her *ketubah*. The second is *zekifah* — she has explicitly converted the remaining balance into a loan, declaring that what is owed to her should be treated as a monetary debt to be repaid. Only when both conditions are satisfied does the *ketubah* lose its *tnai beis din* character and become vulnerable to cancellation by *shemita*.

The logic is that *pegimah* alone — beginning to collect — signals that she is actively pursuing payment and the *ketubah* is now "live" in the sense of being processed. But the remaining unpaid balance is still a *tnai beis din* obligation unless she affirmatively converts it into a loan. Conversely, *zekifah* alone — declaring it a loan without yet having received any payment — also does not fully transform the obligation. Only when both are present has the *ketubah* truly migrated from the domain of *beis din* enactment into the realm of ordinary personal debt.

### Shmuel One Condition

Shmuel disagrees and holds that either condition alone is sufficient. If she has begun collecting (*pegamah*), even without declaring the remainder a loan, the *ketubah* becomes subject to *shemita*. And if she has declared it a loan (*zakfah*), even without having yet received any payment, that too is sufficient to render the remaining amount cancellable. Either act of converting the obligation from its *tnai beis din* status into something more like a private transaction is enough.

### Rav Baraita Support

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that explicitly supports Rav's position. The *baraita* states: when is a *ketubah* cancelled by *shemita*? When she has both engaged in *pegimah* and performed *zekifah*. It then specifies: if she performed *pegimah* without *zekifah*, or *zekifah* without *pegimah*, the *ketubah* is not cancelled — not until she has done both. This is about as explicit as a *baraita* can be.

### Women's Obligations

The *baraita* cited in support of Shmuel enumerates several categories of obligations arising from situations involving women — *ones*, *knas*, *pitui*, and *ketuvat isha* — and states that these are cancelled by *shemita* when the obligation has been converted into a loan (*shezakfam b'malveh*). The process of *zekifah* is defined as *mishe'amda badin* — when the matter has been brought before a court and adjudicated, at which point the obligation is formally established as a monetary debt.

This *baraita* mentions only *zekifah* and says nothing about *pegimah*, which might appear to support Shmuel: perhaps *zekifah* alone, without *pegimah*, is sufficient to trigger *shemita* cancellation. However, the *baraita* does not explicitly say that *pegimah* is unnecessary — it simply does not address it. One could argue that the *baraita* presupposes that *pegimah* has already occurred, or at minimum that the absence of *pegimah* from the text does not prove that *zekifah* alone is enough. The *baraita* thus only partially supports Shmuel — it shows that *zekifah* without an explicit mention of *pegimah* can trigger cancellation, but it does not address whether *pegimah* alone, without *zekifah*, would also suffice. Shmuel claims both directions independently, and the *baraita* establishes only one leg of his position. Unlike the other debates discussed earlier in the *daf*, the *Gemara* here does not issue a final ruling between Rav and Shmuel.

## Ketubah Parallel

### Shmuel Parallel

Shmuel articulates an important principle: a *ketubah* is comparable to a *ma'aseh beis din* — an act of court. Just as documents issued by *beis din* may be written during the day and signed at night without the intervening time rendering the document invalid, so too a *ketubah* may be written during the day and signed at night. This is directly relevant to the question that has run throughout the *daf* — the concern about *get mukdam* and the gap between *ketivah* and *chatima*.

### Chiya Ketubah

A practical illustration is offered: the *ketubah* of Rabbi Chiya bar Rav was written on one day and signed on a different day, and Rav was present and raised no objection. One might infer from his silence that he agrees with Shmuel's position. However, the *Gemara* qualifies this inference.

### Same Matter Leniency

The reason Rav did not object in that case was not because he agreed with Shmuel's principle in the abstract, but because the parties were still *oskim b'oto inyan* — engaged in the same matter. When those writing and signing a document remain continuously involved in the same transaction or proceeding throughout, the gap between writing and signing does not introduce the kinds of concerns that would otherwise arise. A *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yosi supports this: the rule that writing in the day and signing at night is invalid applies only when the parties are no longer engaged in the same matter, but if they remain so engaged throughout, it is *kasher*. This leniency does not extend indefinitely — if the gap grows to ten days or more, or the parties are no longer involved in the same proceeding, the concern reasserts itself.

## Rabbi Shimon Scope

### Rabbi Shimon Reasoning

The *Gemara* now takes up the formal analysis of why Rabbi Shimon is *machshir* a *get mukdam*, a predated *get*. Rava explains: Rabbi Shimon holds that *keivan shenatan einav l'garshah, shuv ein lo piyus* — once the husband has set his mind to divorce, he can no longer be appeased or reconciled. The act of *piyus* — reconciliation — is no longer available to him. The concrete moment that marks this irreversible decision is the writing of the *get*. From that point forward, whatever appears in the *get* as the date is treated as though the divorce effectively began at that time, because the husband's intent was firm and irrevocable. This is why predating is tolerable according to Rabbi Shimon: no practical harm flows from an earlier date, since the husband's rights over the *peirot* were already forfeit from the moment he committed to the divorce through the act of writing.

### Lakish Limits Window

Reish Lakish, however, limits Rabbi Shimon's leniency sharply. He holds that Rabbi Shimon spoke only about the immediate case: the *get* was written that same day and signed that same night — *l'altar*, immediately. The window between *ketivah* and *chatima* that Rabbi Shimon permits is only that narrow span of a day and the following night. Extending the gap to ten days is something Reish Lakish does not permit under Rabbi Shimon's leniency. The concern is *shema piyes* — perhaps during that longer period the husband was reconciled with his wife, in which case the *get* would no longer represent a firm and irrevocable decision but a superseded one.

### Yochanan Expands

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and permits a window of up to ten days even within Rabbi Shimon's framework. His reasoning: *ei ita d'piyes, kala it lehu l'milta* — if the parties had truly reconciled, people would know. Reconciliation is not a private matter; it manifests in behavior that the community observes. If a couple that was known to be headed for divorce is suddenly seen together again, going out, resuming their life — people talk. Since we would hear about a reconciliation, we are not concerned that one occurred silently during the ten-day gap. Rabbi Yochanan therefore permits relying on Rabbi Shimon's leniency for that extended period.

## Ten People Get

### Ten People Command

The *Gemara* introduces a new case: a husband instructs ten people to write a *get* for his wife. This requires interpretation. It cannot mean that all ten physically write the document — that would be neither practical nor halachically coherent. What then does he intend? The answer must lie in the signing. But a *get* requires only two witnesses to sign. So what role do the other eight play?

A *baraita* clarifies: if a husband says to ten people "write a *get* for my wife" without specifying *kulchem* — "all of you" — then one person writes and two sign. The other seven need not be involved at all. From this, we see that the act of addressing ten people does not inherently require all ten to participate. Rashi explains that in a case where all ten are indeed required to sign — without the word *kulchem* having been used — the husband's intention was to embarrass his wife publicly, using the gathering as a forum for the declaration of divorce. He wanted all those present to know about it. The *get* itself bears only the two standard witnesses, but the husband chose to make the matter public.

Given this, the case before us must be one in which the husband explicitly said *kulchem* — all of you — thereby creating an obligation for all ten to participate in the signing. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish turns on what that participation means.

### Yochanan Two Edim

Rabbi Yochanan holds: two of the ten sign as *edim* — as the actual halachic witnesses whose signatures give the *get* its validity — and all the rest sign because of a *tnai*, a condition. The husband, by saying *kulchem*, created a condition that all ten must sign, but only two of them function as witnesses in the technical sense. The remaining eight are satisfying a condition, not serving as witnesses.

### Lakish All Edim

Reish Lakish holds that all ten are *edim* — all ten function as full witnesses. The *get* is not valid until all ten have signed, because all ten were designated as witnesses when the husband invoked *kulchem*.

### Practical Differences

What practical difference emerges between these two positions? The *Gemara* identifies two.

The first: if two of the ten signed on one day and the remaining eight signed up to ten days later, according to Rabbi Yochanan — who holds the other eight are signing due to a *tnai* rather than as witnesses — this is unproblematic. A *tnai* carries no defined time requirement; the condition can be fulfilled whenever the parties get around to it. But according to Reish Lakish, who holds all ten are *edim*, all ten witnesses must sign at the same time. A *get* cannot have its witnesses sign across multiple days, and so this arrangement would render the *get* invalid.

The second difference: if one of the ten is found to be a *karov* or *pasul* — a disqualified witness — the consequences differ. According to Rabbi Yochanan, only the two designated *edim* need to be valid; if one of the other eight is disqualified, it is of no consequence, because they are signing due to a *tnai* and the *tnai* can simply be fulfilled by the remaining valid signatories. According to Reish Lakish, since all ten are *edim*, any disqualification among the ten affects the validity of the *get*.

A further sub-question arises: what if one of the *first* two to sign is found to be *karov* or *pasul*? According to those who say this is still *kasher*, the reasoning is that since the husband did not specify *which* two are the witnesses, the identity of the witnesses is determined by who among the ten is valid, not by the order in which they signed. The first two to sign are not inherently the designated witnesses; any two valid signatories from among the ten fulfill the requirement. The fact that a *pasul* signed first does not corrupt the *get*, because we look to the valid signatures as the operative witnesses. The opposing view holds that this creates a problem of *ati l'ichlofu b'shtar d'alma* — people may mistake the *get* for an ordinary legal document and validate other documents on the strength of a *pasul*'s signature appearing first.

## Rabbi Shimon Ten Days

### Two Days Signing

The *Gemara* now describes an actual case: a man said to ten people to write a *get* for his wife, and two of them signed on one day while the remaining signatories signed up to ten days later. The case came before Rabbi Shimon ben Levi.

### Rabbi Shimon Valid

Rabbi Shimon ben Levi ruled: *k'dai hu Rabbi Shimon lismoch alav b'sha'at had'chak* — Rabbi Shimon is sufficiently authoritative to rely upon in a time of pressing need. The validation requires invoking Rabbi Shimon's leniency that a *get* written and signed at different times can be valid.

### Sha'at Hadeichak

But the language of *sha'at had'chak* — a time of pressing need or hardship — is significant and calls for explanation. If Rabbi Shimon's position is a legitimate *l'chatchila* ruling, why frame reliance upon it as a concession to emergency? Rashi explains: *sha'at had'chak* here refers to cases such as where the husband has since departed and cannot be located to write a new *get*, or where the woman has already remarried on the basis of this *get* and has children — situations where retroactively invalidating the *get* would produce severe consequences, including stigmatizing her children and calling her subsequent marriage into question. These are not abstract halachic exercises but real human crises that require a practical resolution.

### Yochanan Leniency

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension: Rabbi Shimon ben Levi holds like Reish Lakish regarding the scope of Rabbi Shimon's leniency — that it applies only to the immediate day and night, not to ten days. But in this case he extends the window to ten days by relying on Rabbi Yochanan's position, which permits that longer interval under Rabbi Shimon's framework. This is a compound ruling: he relies on Rabbi Shimon's leniency for the validity of the *get* despite the gap between signings, and on Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that the window extends to ten days.

Why then is this only a *sha'at had'chak* ruling rather than a *l'chatchila* one? Because Rabbi Shimon ben Levi himself does not hold all these lenient positions *l'chatchila*. He holds primarily like Reish Lakish — who would require all the signatures to be contemporaneous and would limit Rabbi Shimon's window to the immediate day and night. It is only because the case already happened, and because invalidating the *get* would cause genuine hardship — *mishum iguna akilu bah rabanan*, the rabbis were lenient for the sake of preventing women from becoming *agunot* — that he is willing to reach across and rely on the more permissive opinions. This is not rabbi-shopping in the pejorative sense, but rather the established halachic principle that where the stakes involve freeing a woman from an uncertain marital status, authorities have greater latitude to adopt lenient readings than they would otherwise exercise *l'chatchila*.
