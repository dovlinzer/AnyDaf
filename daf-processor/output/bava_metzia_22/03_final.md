# Bava Metzia 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yeush Shelo Midaas

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵאוּשׁ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לָא הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ. וְרָבָא אָמַר: הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one's **despair** of recovering his lost item **that is not a conscious** feeling, i.e., were he aware of the loss of his property, he would have despaired of its recovery, but he was unaware of his loss when the finder discovered the item, **Abaye said: It is not** considered **despair;** the owner maintains ownership of the item, and the finder may not keep it. **And Rava said: It is** considered **despair** and the finder may keep it.

The daf begins at the final line of 21b, with the words *ta shema*, "come and hear." We are in the midst of the debate between Abaye and Rava over the principle of *yeush shelo mida'as* — whether an owner's prospective despair, his anticipated abandonment of hope of recovering a lost object, takes effect even before he is consciously aware of his loss.

### Core Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סִימָן – כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סִימָן. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לָא הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ, דְּהָא לָא יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ. רָבָא אָמַר: הָוֵי יֵאוּשׁ, דִּלְכִי יָדַע דִּנְפַל מִינֵּיהּ – מִיָּאַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to an item in which there is no distinguishing mark. Abaye said:** Despair that is not conscious **is not** considered **despair, as he did not know that** the item **fell from him;** therefore, he cannot despair of recovering it. **Rava said:** Despair that is not conscious **is** considered **despair, as when he discovers that it fell from him, he will despair** of its recovery.

Abaye holds that *yeush shelo mida'as lo havei yeush* — prospective despair does not constitute valid *yeush*. For the transfer of ownership through *yeush* to be effective, the owner must be consciously aware of his loss and must actively despair of recovery. Rava, by contrast, holds that *yeush shelo mida'as havei yeush* — since we can determine with confidence that, upon learning of the loss, the owner would have despaired, we treat that anticipated despair as operative even now, before he has found out.

The *mishna* on the preceding daf appears to support Rava broadly. In the cases discussed there, objects without a *siman* may be kept by the finder, and the assumption is that even if the owner was unaware at the moment of loss, he would eventually despair — and that suffices. Abaye, however, explains each of those *mishna* cases as ones in which there is good reason to assume the owner was already aware that the object had fallen. The *gemara* now proceeds to examine a series of further proofs bearing on this dispute.

## Ganav, Gazlan, River

### Mishna Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגַּנָּב שֶׁנָּטַל מִזֶּה וְנָתַן לָזֶה, וְכֵן גַּזְלָן שֶׁנָּטַל מִזֶּה וְנָתַן לָזֶה, וְכֵן יַרְדֵּן שֶׁנָּטַל מִזֶּה וְנָתַן לָזֶה, מַה שֶּׁנָּטַל – נָטַל, וּמַה שֶּׁנָּתַן – נָתַן.
>
> **Translation:** **A thief who took** an item **from this** person **and gave** it **to that** person, **and likewise, a robber who took** an item **from this** person **and gave** it **to that** person, **and likewise,** in the case of the **Jordan** River or another river **that took** an item **from this** person **and gave** it **to that** person, in all those cases, **that which** the person **took, he took, and that which** the person **gave, he gave.** Likewise, that which the river took, it took, and that which the river gave, it gave. The person who received the item need not return it.

The *gemara* introduces a *ta shema* from the final line of 21b. The *mishna* states: if a *ganav* — a burglar — stole from one person and gave it to another; or if a *gazlan* — an open robber — stole from one person and gave it to another; or if the Jordan River swept away one person's object and deposited it in another person's domain — in all these cases, *ma shenatal natal u'ma shenatan natan*: what was taken was taken, and what was given was given. The acquisition stands.

This is, of course, the well-known principle of *yeush v'shinui reshus*: once the owner has despaired and there has been a change of domain, the recipient acquires the object. The cases of *ganav* and *gazlan* were treated extensively in Bava Kamma. The question the *gemara* now presses is: how do we know in each of these cases that *yeush* has occurred?

### Ganav Case Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא גַּזְלָן וְיַרְדֵּן, דְּקָא חָזֵי לְהוּ וּמִיָּאַשׁ. אֶלָּא גַּנָּב, מִי קָא חָזֵי לֵיהּ דְּמִיָּאַשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted** in the cases of the **robber and** the **Jordan** River, one could say **that** the owner **sees them** take the item **and despairs** of its recovery; **but** in the case of the **thief,** who takes the item surreptitiously, **does** the owner **see him** take the item and would **that** lead him to **despair?**

The *gemara* reasons as follows. *Bishlama gazlan v'Yarden dechazi lei u'miyash* — I understand the *gazlan* case and the river case: in both, the owner sees what is happening and is aware of it. We can therefore reasonably presume he has despaired. But regarding the *ganav* — a burglar operates covertly — how do we know the owner has even discovered the theft yet? If Reuven's house was burglarized and the stolen item sold to Shimon, we have no certainty that Reuven has learned of the theft. Yet Shimon acquires the object through *yeush v'shinui reshus*. This would seem to vindicate Rava: we apply *yeush shelo mida'as* and say that once Reuven finds out he will certainly despair, and that suffices.

### Rav Pappa Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב פָּפָּא בְּלִסְטִים מְזוּיָּן. אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ גַּזְלָן! תְּרֵי גַּוְונֵי גַּזְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Pappa interpreted** the term thief in the *baraita* to be referring to **armed bandits [*listim*];** therefore, the owner is aware that the item was taken and he despairs of its recovery. The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same as **a robber,** why mention two identical cases? The Gemara answers: The *baraita* mentioned **two types of robbers;** in both cases the owner was aware that his item was taken.

Rav Pappa resolves this by explaining that the *mishna* is not speaking of an ordinary covert burglar but of *listim mezuyan* — armed robbers, who operate openly. Thus the owner does witness the taking, and the case is one of conscious awareness, not prospective despair. The *gemara* then objects: if so, why does the *mishna* need to mention both the *ganav* and the *gazlan* separately? They are essentially the same scenario. The *gemara* answers: *trei gavni gazlan* — the *mishna* wishes to convey two types of open robbery, lest one think the rule differs between them. In any event, the case is not genuinely one of *yeush shelo mida'as*: it is a case where the owner was aware. This is admittedly a very forced reading of the *mishna*, but the *gemara* accepts it.

It is worth noting, as the *gemara* itself recognizes, that the cases of *ganav* and *gazlan* are not cases of *aveidah* at all — they are cases of *geneiva* and *gezeila*. They are brought here because the underlying principle of *yeush v'shinui reshus* is shared, and thus the question of whether *yeush shelo mida'as* is effective applies equally in both contexts.

## River Deposits

### Tosefta Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁטַף נָהָר קוֹרָיו, עֵצָיו, וַאֲבָנָיו, וּנְתָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּתְיָאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **a river swept** away **one's beams, one's wood, or one's stones and placed them into the field of another, these** items belong **to** the owner of the field **due to** the fact **that the** respective **owners despaired** of their recovery.

The *gemara* now brings a *Tosefta*: *shataf nahar korov v'etzav v'avanav* — a river flooded its banks and swept away a person's beams, timber, and stones — an entire building project — and deposited them in his neighbor's field. The *Tosefta* rules: *harei elu shelo mipnei shenityashu hab'alim* — these belong to the finder because the owners have despaired.

### Conscious Yeush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּנִתְיָאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים, הָא סְתָמָא – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from the *baraita*: **The reason** they belong to the finder is **that the owners despaired; but** in **an unspecified** case, where it is not definitively known that the owners despaired, they do **not** belong to the finder. Apparently, despair that is not conscious is not considered despair.

This language is precise and significant. The *Tosefta* does not say that the objects may be kept because we assume the owners *would have* despaired. It says the owners *have* despaired — *nityashu*, past tense, indicating active and conscious *yeush*. The *gemara* draws the inference: *ta'ama denityashu hab'alim* — the reason it is permitted is that the owners actually despaired; *hastama lo* — but by implication, without such active despair it would not be permitted. This appears to be a proof for Abaye.

### Retrieval Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּשֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַצִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where** the owners are **capable of rescuing** the beams, wood, or stones; therefore, their decision not to rescue them is a clear indication of despair.

The *gemara* deflects this reading. *Hacha bemai askinan keshe'yachol lehatzil* — we are dealing here with a case where the owner may actually be able to run after the objects and retrieve them. In such a case, even Rava would not apply *yeush shelo mida'as*, because we cannot confidently predict what the owner would do upon finding out. If recovery is possible, some owners will expend the effort to pursue it. Accordingly, the *Tosefta* is discussing a scenario where the automatic presumption of *yeush* cannot be applied, which is why conscious *yeush* is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אִם הָיוּ הַבְּעָלִים מְרַדְּפִין אַחֲרֵיהֶם – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, say the latter clause** of the same *baraita*: **If the owners were pursuing** the items, the finder is **obligated to return** them.

The *gemara* then says: *ei hachi eima seifa* — if so, let us examine the continuation of the *braita*. The *Tosefta* adds: *im hayu ba'alim mardifim achareihem chayav lehachzir* — if the owners were running after the objects, demonstrating they had not despaired, then one is obligated to return them. The *gemara* presses: if we are dealing with a case where retrieval is physically possible, why does active pursuit matter? Once we cannot presume *yeush* in the first place — because the owner might choose to run after it — even the absence of active pursuit should not suffice for *yeush*. Why does the *Tosefta* specifically require active running as the trigger for the obligation to return?

### Rava Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בִּיכוֹלִין לְהַצִּיל, מַאי אִרְיָא מְרַדְּפִין? אֲפִילּוּ אֵין מְרַדְּפִין נָמֵי! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בִּיכוֹלִין לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק. מְרַדְּפִין – לָא אִיָּיאוּשׁ, אֵין מְרַדְּפִין – אִיָּיאוֹשֵׁי מִיָּאַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is a case **where** the owners are **capable of rescuing** the items, **why** did the *baraita* **specifically** cite a case where the owners were **pursuing** the items? **Even** if they were **not pursuing** the lost items, the items **also** remain in their ownership, as they did not despair of their recovery. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where** the owners are **capable of rescuing** the items **with difficulty.** In that case, if the owners **pursue** the items, it indicates that **they did not despair** of their recovery, but if the owners **do not pursue** the items, it indicates that **they despaired** of their recovery.

The *gemara* answers: *hacha bemai askinan beyocholim lehatzil al yedei hadechak* — we are speaking of a case where retrieval is possible only with great effort. The logic then runs as follows. Because the owners could theoretically retrieve the objects but only through considerable exertion, we cannot automatically presume what they would do. *Mardifim lo yeiush yiyaish* — if they actively run after the objects, that demonstrates clearly they have not despaired. *Ein mardifim yeiush miyaish* — if they are aware and do not run after the objects, that itself communicates: it is not worth the effort to me. That is *yeush*. The case the *Tosefta* addresses is thus one of conscious awareness throughout — not a case of *yeush shelo mida'as* at all. Because the owners' reaction is genuinely uncertain when they would find out, Rava's principle cannot be applied, and the *Tosefta* accordingly requires active *yeush* or its equivalent. This reading, while strained, neutralizes the *Tosefta* as a proof against Rava.

## Terumah Shaliach

### Tosefta Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד אָמְרוּ הַתּוֹרֵם שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה – הֲרֵי שֶׁיָּרַד לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ וְלִיקֵּט וְתָרַם שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, אִם חוֹשֵׁשׁ מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, וְאִם לָאו – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 1:5): **When did** the Sages **say** that in the case where **one separates *teruma* without** the owner's **consent, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*?** It is in a case where **there was** someone **who entered another's field and gathered** produce from it **and separated *teruma* without** the owner's **permission. If** the owner is **concerned about** his actions and views it **as robbery, his *teruma* is not *teruma*, but if** he is **not** concerned, **his *teruma* is *teruma*.**

The *gemara* introduces another *ta shema*, this time from a *Tosefta* in the laws of *terumah*. The *Tosefta* asks: under what circumstances does the rule hold that *hatoram shelo mida'as trumato trumah* — one who separates *terumah* without the owner's awareness has nonetheless effected valid *terumah*?

The scenario: Shimon enters Reuven's field, harvests the grain, and decides to do Reuven a favor by separating *terumah* on his behalf — *shelo birshut*, without having been granted permission. The *Tosefta* states: *im choshesh mishum gezel ein trumaso trumah* — if Reuven, upon learning what happened, would be concerned that Shimon acted improperly with his property — whether by taking too much or by using grain of too high a quality — then the *terumah* is not valid. *V'im lav, trumaso trumah* — but if Reuven has no such concern, and it is clear he is content with what Shimon did, the *terumah* stands.

### Owner Reaction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן הוּא יוֹדֵעַ אִם חוֹשֵׁשׁ מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל וְאִם לָאו? הֲרֵי שֶׁבָּא בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וּמְצָאוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל יָפוֹת״. אִם נִמְצְאוּ יָפוֹת מֵהֶן – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה. לִיקְּטוּ הַבְּעָלִים וְהוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And from where would he know whether** the owner is **concerned about** his actions and views it **as robbery or not?** If **the owner came and found him** separating *teruma* **and said to him:** You should have **gone to** take the produce of **better** quality and separate *teruma* from that, then **if** produce of **better** quality **than** the produce he had separated **is found, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*,** since the owner is assumed to have been sincere and pleased that the other has separated *teruma* from his produce. **But if not, his *teruma* is not *teruma*,** as it may be assumed that the owner was angry at him and was speaking sarcastically. The *baraita* adds: If **the owners were gathering and adding** to the *teruma* he had separated, indicating that they agree to his act of separation, **either way,** whether or not better-quality produce was found, **his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*.**

The *Tosefta* then addresses how we determine whether Reuven approves. If Reuven arrives and finds Shimon in the act of separating *terumah* and says *kolach eitzel yafos* — "you should have taken the higher-quality grain" — this statement may be sincere or sarcastic depending on the facts. *Im nimtzu yafos mehen, trumaso trumah* — if there genuinely is higher-quality grain available, Reuven's comment is sincere: he is glad Shimon separated *terumah* and merely wishes it had been even better quality. This demonstrates approval, and the *terumah* is valid. *V'im lav, ein trumaso trumah* — if the grain Shimon used was already the finest available, then Reuven's remark is sarcastic — "you used my very best grain!" — and that reveals his dissatisfaction. The *terumah* is not valid.

The *Tosefta* continues: if, instead of making any remark, Reuven simply went ahead and harvested additional produce himself and added it to what Shimon had separated as *terumah* — *bein kach u'vein kach trumaso trumah* — in either case, Shimon's *terumah* is valid. Reuven's action demonstrates retroactively that he was fully content with what Shimon had done.

This case appears to be evidence for Rava. At the moment Shimon separated *terumah*, Reuven was unaware — and yet based on Reuven's subsequent reaction we determine retroactively whether Shimon's act was valid. That is precisely the logic of *yeush shelo mida'as*: what the owner would have thought or felt, had he been aware at the time, determines the legal reality even absent actual contemporaneous consciousness.

### Shalichus Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי נִמְצְאוּ יָפוֹת מֵהֶן תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, אַמַּאי? בְּעִידָּנָא דִּתְרַם הָא לָא הֲוָה יָדַע! תַּרְגְּמַהּ רָבָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּאַבָּיֵי: דְּשַׁוְּיֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the ruling of the *baraita*: **But why** is that the *halakha*, that **if** produce of **better** quality **than** the produce he had separated **is found his *teruma* is *teruma*? At the time that he separated the *teruma*, he did not know** that the owner would ultimately agree. The *baraita* states that the *teruma* is *teruma* from the moment he separated it, despite the fact that it was only later that he learned that the owner agreed. Apparently, in the case of despair as well, despair that is not conscious is considered despair, contrary to the opinion of Abaye. **Rava interpreted** the matter **in accordance with** the opinion **of Abaye:** This is a case **where** the owner **designated him** as **an agent.**

Rava comes to Abaye's defense — a striking inversion. The *gemara* states: *amar Rava aliba d'Abaye* — Rava explains how Abaye can answer this proof. The case, Rava argues, must be one in which Reuven had formally appointed Shimon as his *shaliach* to separate *terumah*: *d'shavyu shliach*.

### Gemara Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ דְּלָא שַׁוְּויֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ, מִי הָוְיָא תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה? וְהָא ״אַתֶּם״ ״גַּם אַתֶּם״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא לְרַבּוֹת שְׁלוּחֲכֶם. מָה אַתֶּם לְדַעְתְּכֶם, אַף שְׁלוּחֲכֶם לְדַעְתְּכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **So too, it is reasonable, as if it enters your mind that** the owner **did not designate him** as **an agent, would his *teruma* be *teruma*? But doesn't the Merciful One state:** "So you also shall set apart a gift unto the Lord of all your tithes" (Numbers 18:28)? Once the verse states **"you,"** the addition of the word "also" in the term **"you also"** serves **to include an agent.** Therefore, an agent separating *teruma* has the same *halakhot* as an owner separating *teruma*. **Just as** when **you,** the owner, separate *teruma*, it is **with your knowledge, so too** when **your agent** separates *teruma*, it must be **with your knowledge.** Evidently, in any event, one needs to be appointed as an agent to be capable of separating *teruma* for another.

The *gemara* supports this reading. It would make no sense to say that someone who acted on another's behalf entirely without authorization could have effected valid *terumah*, because the very basis for *terumah* by agency is the *pasuk*: *ken tarimu gam atem trumas Hashem* — "you too shall separate the portion of God." From the word *atem*, "you," the Rabbis derive that one's agent can separate *terumah* on one's behalf. But the *gemara* immediately draws the corollary: *mah atem l'da'atchem, af shluchachem l'da'atchem* — just as when *you* separate *terumah* you are consciously aware of the act, so too your *shaliach* must be consciously designated. An unauthorized party stepping in to perform the act on your behalf cannot do so by virtue of *shlichus*, because *shlichus* requires a conscious appointment.

This is, to be candid, an extremely difficult reading of the *Tosefta*. The *Tosefta* explicitly frames the case as *hatoram shelo mida'as* — separating *terumah* without the owner's awareness — and describes Shimon as acting *shelo birshut*, without permission. To read into this a case of formal *shlichus* strains the plain meaning considerably. One could perhaps argue that *shelo mida'as* and *shelo birshut* refer not to the absence of any agency relationship but to Shimon having exceeded or deviated from the parameters of an appointment that did not specify quality or quantity. But even so, *shelo birshut* is very difficult to reconcile with a formal appointment.

### Applied Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּשַׁוְּיֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״זִיל תְּרוֹם״, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״תְּרוֹם מֵהָנֵי״. וּסְתָמֵיהּ דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת כִּי תָּרֵים מִבֵּינוֹנִית (הוּא) תָּרֵים, וַאֲזַל אִיהוּ וּתְרַם מִיָּפוֹת, וּבָא בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וּמְצָאוֹ וַאֲמַר לוֹ: ״כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל יָפוֹת״, אִם נִמְצְאוּ יָפוֹת מֵהֶן – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where** the owner **designated him** as **an agent and said to him: Go and separate *teruma*, but** he **did not say to him: Separate *teruma* from these** specific crops. **And** when **the owner's** intent is **unspecified,** and it is unclear which of his crops are meant to be separated **when** the agent **separates *teruma*,** it is **from** the crops of **intermediate quality** that **he separates *teruma*. And** in this case, the agent **went and separated *teruma* from higher-quality** produce, **and the owner** of the field **came and found him and said to him:** You should have **gone to** take the produce of **better** quality and separate *teruma* from that. **If** produce of **better** quality **than** the produce he had separated **is found, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*. But if not, his *teruma* is not *teruma*.**

The *gemara* is therefore saying: the whole *Tosefta* is a case of *shlichus*, and what it examines is merely when a *shaliach*'s deviation from implicit parameters is ratified by the owner. This is not a case of *yeush shelo mida'as* — it is a case of agency law and the scope of an agent's authority.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר וּמָר זוּטְרָא וְרַב אָשֵׁי אִקְּלַעוּ לְבוּסְתָּנָא דְּמָרִי בַּר אִיסַק, אַיְיתִי אֲרִיסֵיהּ תַּמְרֵי וְרִימּוֹנֵי וּשְׁדָא קַמַּיְיהוּ. אַמֵּימָר וְרַב אָשֵׁי אָכְלִי, מָר זוּטְרָא לָא אֲכַל. אַדְּהָכִי אֲתָא מָרִי בַּר אִיסַק, אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲרִיסֵיהּ: אַמַּאי לָא אַיְיתֵית לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן מֵהָנָךְ שַׁפִּירָתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara digresses with a related incident: **Ameimar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi happened to** come to **the orchard [*levustana*] of Mari bar Isak. His sharecropper came and placed dates and pomegranates before them. Ameimar and Rav Ashi ate** the fruit, but **Mar Zutra did not eat** the fruit due to the concern that the sharecropper had provided them with the fruit without the approval of the owner of the field. **Meanwhile, Mari bar Isak came** and **found them** eating his fruit **and said to his sharecropper: Why didn't you bring the Sages** fruit **from those higher-quality** fruits?

The *gemara* then records a practical episode. Ameimar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi visited the orchard of Mari bar Isak. Mari bar Isak's sharecropper brought them dates and pomegranates and presented them — clearly from the owner's portion of the crop, not the sharecropper's share. Ameimar and Rav Ashi ate, reasoning that as distinguished rabbis and presumably friends of the owner, Mari bar Isak would obviously be pleased for them to eat. Mar Zutra refused to eat, unwilling to assume the owner's implicit consent.

While they were sitting there, Mari bar Isak arrived. He found them and turned to his sharecropper, saying: *amai lo ayiteis lehu l'rabbanan me'hanech shapirta* — why did you not bring these honored rabbis the better quality fruit? This reaction was parallel to the *Tosefta* case. There was indeed better-quality fruit, suggesting the owner's remark was sincere — he genuinely wanted them to have his best produce. Ameimar and Rav Ashi said to Mar Zutra: surely now you can eat. Is this not precisely the *Tosefta* case — *nimtzu yafos mehen, trumaso trumah* — the owner indicated there was something better, proving his sincerity and his retroactive approval?

### Mar Zutra Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ אַמֵּימָר וְרַב אָשֵׁי לְמָר זוּטְרָא: הַשְׁתָּא אַמַּאי לָא אָכֵיל מָר? וְהָתַנְיָא: אִם נִמְצְאוּ יָפוֹת מֵהֶן – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ, הָכִי אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא אָמְרוּ כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל יָפוֹת אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן תְּרוּמָה בִּלְבַד, מִשּׁוּם דְּמִצְוָה הוּא וְנִיחָא לֵיהּ, אֲבָל הָכָא – מִשּׁוּם כְּסִיפוּתָא הוּא דְּאָמַר הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar and Rav Ashi said to Mar Zutra: Now why is the Master not eating the fruit? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where the owner of the field came and found him and said to him: You should have gone to take the produce of better quality and separate *teruma* from that; **if** produce of **better** quality **than** the produce he had separated **is found, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*.** Here too, it is clear that Mari bar Isak approved of the actions of his sharecropper. Mar Zutra **said to them** that **this** is what **Rava said:** The Sages **said** that the statement: You should have **gone to** take the produce of **better** quality and separate *teruma*, indicates consent of the owner **only with regard to the matter of *teruma*, due to** the fact **that it is a mitzva and** the owner **is amenable** to having the mitzva fulfilled. **But here,** in this incident, it is **due to shame that he said this:** Why did you not bring these Sages fruit from those higher-quality fruits? He did not really want to give them the fruit.

Mar Zutra refused to be moved. He cited Rava's ruling: *lo amru kelapei yafot ela l'inyan terumah bilvad* — the principle that we read the owner's "you should have taken better" as sincere only when the matter concerns *terumah*. Why specifically *terumah*? *Mishum de'mitzvah hu, v'nicha lei* — because *terumah* is a *mitzvah*, and therefore we presuppose that a person wants it done in the best possible way. If better grain exists, we assume the owner would have wanted the *terumah* taken from it; accordingly, his remark is read as sincere.

But in the case of the orchard, a different factor is operative. *Mishum kesifuso hu de'amar hachi* — the owner's remark was driven by embarrassment. What was he to do — berate his sharecropper in front of distinguished guests and say he did not want them eating his fruit? Social pressure and shame compelled him to say "you should have given them something even better," regardless of what he actually felt. In that context we cannot read the remark as sincere approval. Mar Zutra therefore maintained his refusal.

## Dew on Produce

### Dew Tosefta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹדֵהוּ הַטַּל עֲלֵיהֶן וְשָׂמַח – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּכִי יֻתַּן. נִגְּבוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׂמַח – אֵינָן בְּכִי יוּתַּן.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the *baraita*: If **the dew is still upon** the produce and has not yet dried, and if the owner **was glad** that the dew moistened the produce and kept it fresh, **that** produce falls into the category of: "But **when** water **is placed** upon the seed," and the produce is susceptible to contracting ritual impurity. If the produce **had dried** when the owner found it, then **even though he was glad** that the dew had moistened the produce, the produce **is not** in the category of: "But **when** water **is placed [*khi yuttan*]** upon the seed," and the produce is not susceptible to contracting ritual impurity.

The *gemara* now brings a *ta shema* from the *Tosefta* in *Machshirin*, the tractate dealing with liquids that render produce susceptible to *tumah*. The governing halacha, derived from the *pasuk* *vechi yutan mayim al zera* — "when water is placed upon seed" — is that produce cut from the ground only becomes susceptible to *tumah* upon contact with liquid, specifically when the owner is *nicha lei*, pleased that the liquid came into contact with it. The principle is *dumya d'chi yiten*: the liquid-contact must resemble an active "placing" of liquid, in that it must be something the owner is glad about.

The *Tosefta* states: *hudach tal aleihen vesamach* — if dew condensed on fruit that was drying on the roof, and the owner came up and was happy about it, *harei zeh bechiyutan* — this constitutes valid *hechsher*, rendering the produce susceptible to *tumah*.

### Post-Drying Awareness

A more complex case follows. *Nagvu* — if the dew subsequently dried off — *af al pi shesamach*, even if the owner later expressed happiness that the dew had fallen on the fruit, *eina bechiyutan* — at the top of 22b — it does not constitute *hechsher*. The produce is not susceptible.

This appears to be a direct proof for Abaye. The owner was happy — but only after the fact, once the liquid had already dried. Because he was not actively conscious of and pleased about the situation *at the time* the dew was on the fruit, that later happiness is insufficient. This is precisely Abaye's logic: retrospective or prospective frame of mind does not substitute for actual contemporaneous consciousness.

### Retroactive Happiness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא מַאי? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּאִיגַּלַּאי מִילְּתָא דְּהַשְׁתָּא נִיחָא לֵיהּ, מֵעִיקָּרָא נָמֵי נִיחָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that if the produce dried, the fact that the owner is glad does not render it susceptible to ritual impurity? Is it **not due to** the fact **that we do not say: Since the matter was revealed that he is amenable** to the moisture **now, he was also amenable from the outset?** The same should be true with regard to despair that is not conscious. The fact that when he becomes aware of his loss he despairs of its recovery does not indicate that he despaired from the outset, contrary to the opinion of Rava.

The *gemara* articulates the principle that this *Tosefta* seems to negate. *Lav mishum delo amrinan, kivan de'igla milsa dehasta nicha lei, me'ikara nami nicha lei* — is it not because we do not say: since it is now clear that he is pleased, we treat him as having been pleased from the outset? Here the *gemara* abstracts the conceptual structure of *yeush shelo mida'as* and applies it across categories: just as Rava would say that anticipated despair counts as present despair, one might analogously argue that subsequent pleasure counts as contemporaneous pleasure. The *Tosefta* rejects this — and thus it appears to support Abaye.

### Consciousness Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי יִתֵּן״. עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן. אִי הָכִי, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי: הָתָם כִּדְרַב פָּפָּא. דְּרַב פָּפָּא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב ״כִּי יִתֵּן״, וְקָרֵינַן כִּי יוּתַּן! הָא כֵּיצַד? בָּעֵינַן ״כִּי יוּתַּן״ דֻּומְיָא ״דְּכִי יִתֵּן״. מָה ״יִתֵּן״ לְדַעַת, אַף ״כִּי יוּתַּן״ נָמֵי לְדַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: It **is different there, as** although the phrase is vocalized to mean: "When it is placed," **it is written: When one places [*ki yitten*],** from which it is derived that the produce is rendered susceptible to ritual impurity **only** if the owner **places** the liquid on the produce. The Gemara asks: **If so,** in **the first clause** of the *baraita*, **too,** the produce should not be rendered susceptible to contracting impurity, because the dew fell on the produce and was not placed there by the owner. The Gemara answers: **There,** the explanation is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Pappa, as Rav Pappa raised a contradiction:** The verse states: "But when water is placed [*vekhi yuttan*] upon the seed, and any part of a carcass falls thereon, it is ritually impure unto you" (Leviticus 11:38). The word "*yuttan*" **is written** in the defective form, as if it says **"*ki yitten*."** Accordingly, this would mean that one must actively place the water on the produce. Yet, **we read** it, based on the tradition as to its correct pronunciation, as if it is written **"*ki yuttan*,"** which includes any situation where the produce becomes wet. **How so?** How can the way the verse is written and the way it is read be reconciled? Rav Pappa explains that **we require** that the situation described by the words **"when water is placed [*ki yuttan*]"** be **similar to** the situation described by the words: **When one places [*dekhi yitten*]: Just as** the term **places [*yitten*]** indicates **that** it is **with the knowledge of** the owner that the produce becomes wet, as he himself is placing the water, **so too,** the term **"is placed [*yuttan*]"** means **that** it is **with his knowledge** that the produce becomes wet, despite the fact that he did not place the water himself. Therefore, no proof may be cited with regard to the matter of despair, where there is no Torah derivation requiring awareness from the outset.

The *gemara* deflects this as well. *Shani hasam d'chsiv ki yiten, ad she'yiten* — this domain is governed by the special requirement of the *pasuk*: the word *yiten* implies an active, conscious placing of liquid. The obligation of *hechsher* is therefore uniquely demanding: it requires not only *nicha lei* but also *l'da'as* — the owner must be conscious of the liquid's presence at the time it is on the produce. This is derived by *gezeira shava* from *ki yiten*: just as active placing requires full consciousness, so too the passive falling of liquid (*ki yutan*) must be accompanied by awareness and pleasure simultaneously.

Rav Pappa had already noted the apparent tension in the *pasuk* itself: the word is written without the *vav* — *ki yiten* — but is traditionally read as *ki yutan*. The resolution: we need both. Liquid may fall passively (*yutan*), but it must be *dumya d'ki yiten* — analogous to active placing — in two respects: the owner must be *nicha lei*, and the owner must be conscious of it while the liquid is still present. Accordingly, the *Tosefta*'s ruling is not a general refutation of Rava; it reflects a special, heightened *da'as* requirement unique to the law of *hechsher tumah*, which goes beyond even *nicha lei*.

## Aveidah River Proof

### Lost Object Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: מִנַּיִן לַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁשְּׁטָפָהּ נָהָר שֶׁהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that **which Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael ben Yehotzadak: From where** is it derived **with regard to a lost item that the river swept away that it is permitted** for its finder to keep it?

The *gemara* now brings what proves to be the decisive *ta shema*. Rabbi Yochanan taught in the name of Rabbi Yishmael ben Yehotzadak: *minayin l'aveidah sheshatafah nahar shehi muteret* — from where do we know that a lost object swept away by a river is permitted to whoever finds it?

### Lost Object Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה לַחֲמוֹרוֹ וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה לְשִׂמְלָתוֹ וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה לְכׇל אֲבֵידַת אָחִיךָ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאבַד מִמֶּנּוּ וּמְצָאתָהּ״. מִי שֶׁאֲבוּדָה הֵימֶנּוּ וּמְצוּיָה אֵצֶל כׇּל אָדָם, יָצָאתָה זוֹ שֶׁאֲבוּדָה מִמֶּנּוּ וְאֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה אֵצֶל כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from this verse, **as it is written: "And so shall you do with his donkey; and so shall you do with his garment; and so shall you do with every lost item of your brother, which shall be lost from him, and you have found it"** (Deuteronomy 22:3). The verse states that one must return **that which is lost from him,** the owner, **but is** available to be **found by any person. Excluded** from that obligation is **that which is lost from him and is not** available to be **found by any person;** it is ownerless property and anyone who finds it may keep it.

The source is the *pasuk*: *v'chein ta'aseh lachamoro v'chein ta'aseh l'simlaso v'chein ta'aseh l'chol aveidas achicha asher tovad mimenu umetzasah* — "and so shall you do with his donkey, and so with his garment, and so with any lost object of your brother that is lost from him and you find it." The *gemara* focuses on the phrase *asher tovad mimenu umetzasah* — "which is lost from him and you find it." This describes the paradigmatic case of *aveidah*: *mi she'avudah mimenu umetzuyah eitzel kol adam* — lost to him, but accessible to others. A person lost it in a public place; others can find and return it. In such a case there is an obligation to return.

*Yatzas zu she'avudah mimenu v'eina metzuyah eitzel kol adam* — this excludes the case where an object is lost from him *and* inaccessible to everyone: swept away by a river, out of reach of all people. Such an object is *avudah mimenu umikol adam* — lost to all. This completely severs the owner's connection to it. Even if there is a *siman*, even if the owner has not despaired, the object is free for the finder to keep. The objective inaccessibility itself effects the severance.

### Parallel Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיסּוּרָא דּוּמְיָא דְּהֶיתֵּירָא, מָה הֶיתֵּירָא בֵּין דְּאִית בַּהּ סִימָן וּבֵין דְּלֵית בַּהּ סִימָן – שַׁרְיָא, אַף אִיסּוּרָא בֵּין דְּאִית בַּהּ סִימַן וּבֵין דְּלֵית בַּהּ סִימָן – אֲסִירָא. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא, תְּיוּבְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **And the prohibition** written in the verse against keeping an item that is lost only to its owner is **similar to the allowance** to keep an item lost to all people that is inferred from the verse; **just as** in the case of **the allowance, whether there is a distinguishing mark and whether there is no distinguishing mark,** it is permitted for the finder to keep it, **so too** in the case of **the prohibition, whether there is a distinguishing mark and whether there is no distinguishing mark,** it is **prohibited** for the finder to keep it, until there is proof that the owner despaired of its recovery. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rava is** indeed a conclusive **refutation.**

The *gemara* then draws a formal analogy. *V'isura dumya d'heteira* — the case where keeping the object is forbidden should be structured analogously to the case where it is permitted. The permitted case — the object swept entirely out of reach — applies regardless of whether there is a *siman* or not. *Bein d'is beih siman bein d'leis beih siman shara* — with or without a *siman*, it is permitted.

By parallel inference, the forbidden case — the ordinary *aveidah* where the object is accessible to others — should similarly apply with or without a *siman*. *Af issura bein d'is beih siman bein d'leis beih siman assura* — whether there is a *siman* or not, the finder is obligated to return it. Where a *siman* exists, the obligation is obvious. But even where there is no *siman*, the obligation stands — and this can only be true if we say, like Abaye, that *yeush shelo mida'as lo havei yeush*. Without conscious *yeush* by the owner, the finder may not keep the object even absent a *siman*, because the absence of a *siman* alone does not suffice to permit taking — we need actual *yeush*, and actual *yeush* requires the owner's conscious awareness.

## Halachic Conclusion

### Rava Refuted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי בְּיַעַל קַגַּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** although in disputes between Abaye and Rava, the *halakha* is typically ruled in accordance with the opinion of Rava, the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abaye in** the disputes represented by the mnemonic: ***Yod*, *ayin*, *lamed*; *kuf*, *gimmel*, *mem*.**

The *gemara* declares: *tiyufta d'Rava tiyufta* — an absolute, decisive refutation of Rava. This is striking when one reflects on the full arc of the sugya. The overwhelming weight of the earlier proofs seemed to favor Rava, and each was deflected by the *gemara* through creative — at times quite strained — rereadings. Yet this final argument, which proceeds by analogy from one category of *aveidah* to another, is treated as a *tiyufta*, an irrefutable contradiction.

The *gemara* accordingly rules: *v'hilchisa k'vosei d'Abaye* — the *halacha* follows Abaye on *yeush shelo mida'as*. The reason the *gemara* presses so hard to arrive at this conclusion is almost certainly because of an established tradition: we follow Rava against Abaye in virtually all disputes between them — *except* for six cases encoded in the mnemonic *ya'al kegam*.

### Mnemonic Exceptions

The mnemonic *ya'al kegam* — יע"ל קג"ם — encodes the six cases in which the *halacha* follows Abaye rather than Rava.

### Exception Details

The *yud* stands for *yeush shelo mida'as*: the case of the present discussion. We rule like Abaye that prospective, unconscious despair does not constitute *yeush*. Conscious *da'as* and active *yeush* are required.

The *ayin* stands for *edim zomemim* — conspiring witnesses. The dispute concerns whether, when witnesses are found to be *zomemim*, they are disqualified retroactively from the moment of their testimony, or only from the point at which they were exposed. We rule like Abaye on this question.

The *lamed* stands for *lechi haomed me'elav* — a post that stands by itself, the question of what constitutes a valid *lechi* to permit carrying in a shared courtyard or alleyway.

The *kuf* stands for *kiddushin shelo nimseru l'biah* — a *kiddushin* that cannot halachically be consummated, whether such a betrothal takes effect at all.

The *gimmel* stands for *gilui da'as b'get* — whether implicit disclosure of intent, such as an attempt to nullify a *get* through gesture or implication, is legally operative in the context of bills of divorce.

The *mem* stands for *mumar ochel neveilos l'tei'avon* — an apostate who violates Torah prohibitions not out of ideological rejection but out of appetite or desire. The question is whether such a person is *pasul l'eidus*, disqualified as a witness. We rule in accordance with Abaye in this case as well.

In all six of these matters — *ya'al kegam* — the accepted *halacha* follows Abaye against Rava. The *gemara*'s conclusion in the present sugya is therefore that *yeush shelo mida'as lo havei yeush*: without the owner's conscious awareness and active despair, no *yeush* has occurred, and the finder acquires nothing.
