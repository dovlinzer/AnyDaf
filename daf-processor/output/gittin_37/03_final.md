# Gittin 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pruzbul Foundation

### Hillel's Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן דִּתְשַׁמֵּט, זֵכֶר לַשְּׁבִיעִית; רָאָה הִלֵּל שֶׁנִּמְנְעוּ הָעָם מִלְּהַלְווֹת זֶה אֶת זֶה, עָמַד וְהִתְקִין פְּרוֹסְבּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Sages instituted that** despite this, the Sabbatical Year still **will abrogate** debt in the present, in **remembrance of the** Torah-mandated **Sabbatical** Year. **Hillel saw that** the people of **the nation refrained from lending to each other** so **he arose and instituted** the ***prosbol*.** According to this explanation, the ordinance of Hillel did not conflict with a Torah law; rather, he added an ordinance to counter the effect of a rabbinic law.

Today's *daf* is 37, and we pick up in the middle of 36b, in the midst of the remarkable *sugya* of *pruzbul*. The preceding discussion addressed the fundamental question of how Hillel could establish *pruzbul* without undermining the *d'oraita* principle that *shmitta* annuls debts. The *Gemara* resolved this by noting that the annulment of debts nowadays is Rabbinic in nature. But that itself raised a further difficulty: how did the Rabbis create that Rabbinic annulment in the first place? The *Gemara*'s answer introduced the principle of *hefker bet din hefker* — the authority of a court to reassign questions of property ownership. For Rashi, this power was sufficient to ground *pruzbul* even if *shmitta* were biblical, since it gives Hillel the authority to restructure monetary obligations at their root.

Tosafot, however, pressed a deeper point. The problem was not merely mechanical — how *pruzbul* could circumvent a *d'oraita* — but ethical: how could Hillel have done such a thing at all? That is why it was a more satisfying answer that *shmitta* nowadays is Rabbinic. And this connects to the way Hillel himself framed the issue. Against the popular conception, Hillel did not frame *pruzbul* as a workaround because the law was too burdensome. Rather, he framed it as a weighing of one *d'oraita* against another.

### Balancing Obligations

Hillel recognized a genuine conflict: on one side, the *shmitta* annulment of debts; on the other, the prohibition against withholding loans from the poor. People were violating the latter because they feared the former. In order to prevent the ongoing violation of *that* commandment, Hillel found a means of addressing the debt annulment. The entire enterprise was framed as a *d'oraita* trade-off — one Torah obligation being preserved at the cost of circumventing another — and that framing is obviously critical when one is in the business of constructing such an institution.

### Hefker Bet Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה הוּא. רָבָא אָמַר: הֶפְקֵר בֵּית דִּין – הֶפְקֵר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן שֶׁהֶפְקֵר בֵּית דִּין – הֶפְקֵר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָבוֹא לִשְׁלֹשֶׁת הַיָּמִים כַּעֲצַת הַשָּׂרִים וְהַזְּקֵנִים, יׇחֳרַם כׇּל רְכוּשׁוֹ וְהוּא יִבָּדֵל מִקְּהַל הַגּוֹלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** This is not actual theft; **it is** an instruction to **sit** passively **and not do** anything. The Sages have the authority to instruct one to passively violate a Torah law, so long as no action is taken. **Rava says:** The Sages are able to institute this ordinance because **property** declared **ownerless** by **the court** is **ownerless. As Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that property** declared **ownerless** by **the court** is **ownerless? As it is stated: "And whoever did not come within three days according to the counsel of the princes and the Elders, all of his property shall be forfeited, and he shall be separated from the congregation of the captivity"** (Ezra 10:8).

With that conceptual foundation in place, the *Gemara* continues. The principle of *hefker bet din hefker* is what gives a *beit din* the power to reassign ownership and thereby enable the mechanism of *pruzbul*. As we will see, this principle carries significant implications not only for *pruzbul* but for broader questions about the authority of rabbinic courts — including, parenthetically, the concept of *hafk'as kiddushin*, which at least according to Rashi in the case of *kesef kiddushin* may partly rest on this foundation, though the *Gemara* ultimately grounds *hafk'as kiddushin* in the principle of *al da'ata derabbanan mekadesh*.

## Temporal Scope

### Temporary vs. Permanent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כִּי הִתְקִין הִלֵּל פְּרוֹסְבּוּל – לְדָרֵיהּ הוּא דְּתַקֵּין, אוֹ דִלְמָא לְדָרֵי עָלְמָא נָמֵי תַּקֵּין?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **When Hillel instituted** the ***prosbol*,** was it **for his generation** alone **that he instituted** it, and the custom developed to continue using it, **or** did he **perhaps institute** it **also for all generations?**

The *Gemara* now raises a new question, approximately fifteen lines down on 36b, beginning *iba'u lehu*: when Hillel established *pruzbul*, did he do so only for his generation, or for all future generations? This question is notable because it was not asked about the other *tikkunei olam* instituted earlier in the tractate. Why should it arise specifically here?

One possibility is that *pruzbul* was framed as a response to a particular observed reality — *ra'ah Hillel she-nimnu ha-am mi-lilvot zeh et zeh*, Hillel saw that people were refraining from lending to one another — and this framing suggests a situational assessment rather than a permanent institutional change. A second possibility is that because *pruzbul* involves circumventing something that looks like a *d'oraita*, it was a radical step, and perhaps Hillel did not intend it as a permanent alteration.

### The Practical Stakes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְבַטּוֹלֵיהּ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְדָרֵיהּ הוּא דְּתַקֵּין – מְבַטְּלִינַן לֵיהּ; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְדָרֵי עָלְמָא נָמֵי תַּקֵּין, הָא אֵין בֵּית דִּין יָכוֹל לְבַטֵּל דִּבְרֵי בֵּית דִּין חֲבֵרוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן גָּדוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ בְּחׇכְמָה וּבְמִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What difference is there** whether it was instituted for his generation only or for all generations when either way, it is still in use? The Gemara explains: The difference arises with regard **to nullifying** the institution of *prosbol*. **If you say** that it was **for his generation** alone **that he instituted** it, then **we can nullify it** if we desire. **But if you say** that **he instituted** it **also for all generations,** then there is a principle that **a court can nullify the action of another court only if it is greater than it in wisdom and in number.** Therefore, we would not be able to nullify the ordinance instituted by Hillel and his court.

The practical stakes of this question are made explicit: *l'mai nafka mina* — what difference does it make? The *Gemara* answers: *levatlohei*, the question is whether we can annul it. If Hillel established *pruzbul* only for his generation, then perhaps it is currently maintained only by force of habit, not by the binding authority of his enactment, and a later court could dissolve it. If, however, he established it for all generations, then no subsequent *beit din* could annul it unless it were greater in wisdom and number — *ein beit din yakhol levatel divrei beit din chavero ela im kein gadol mimenu be-chochma u-ve-minyan*.

### Shmuel's Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לָא כָּתְבִינַן פְּרוֹסְבּוּל אֶלָּא אִי בְּבֵי דִינָא דְּסוּרָא אִי בְּבֵי דִינָא דִּנְהַרְדְּעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a resolution to the dilemma: **Come** and **hear that** which **Shmuel said: We write a *prosbol* only in the court of Sura or in the court of Neharde'a,** as they were the primary centers of Torah study, but not in any other court.

The *Gemara* adduces evidence from a ruling of Shmuel: *lo katvinan pruzbul ela ei be-veit dina d'Sura ei be-veit dina d'Nahardei* — a *pruzbul* may only be written before the major *batei din* of Sura or Nahardea, the central courts of the region. The *Gemara* asks: if Hillel established *pruzbul* for all generations, why would it be limited to these central institutions? Surely Hillel wanted this tool to be broadly available, in which case he should have empowered all *batei din* to execute it.

### The Logic of Limitation

The logic of the limitation seems to point toward a specific conclusion: perhaps the original authority of Hillel's enactment is no longer operative, and *pruzbul* now functions only by virtue of the power of a current central *beit din* exercising *hefker bet din hefker*. If that is so, only a *beit din* with the authority to be *motzi mamon* — to actually extract money — would have the standing to execute it. This would suggest not merely that Hillel's *takana* can be annulled, but that it has in effect already lapsed, and *pruzbul* now requires a fresh act of rabbinic authority each time it is invoked.

### Intentional Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא כִּי תַּקֵּין הִלֵּל לְדָרֵי עָלְמָא – כְּגוֹן בֵּי דִינָא דִּידֵיהּ, וּכְרַב אַמֵּי וְרַב אַסִּי, דְּאַלִּימֵי לְאַפְקוֹעֵי מָמוֹנָא; אֲבָל לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Perhaps when Hillel instituted** the *prosbol*, he did so **for all generations,** but only for courts **such as his court,** which was the primary court of his time, **and** courts **like** those of **Rav Ami and Rav Asi, as they have the power to remove money** from someone's possession. **However, for all** other courts, which are not as authoritative, he did **not** institute this ordinance.

The *Gemara* rejects this inference, however. *Dilma ki takkin Hillel l'dorei alma, kegon beit dina didei* — perhaps when Hillel established *pruzbul* for all generations, he himself always intended it to be executed only before a *beit din* of sufficient authority to be *motzi mamon*, empowered with *hefker bet din hefker*, and not before every local court. In other words, the restriction to central *batei din* may have been part of Hillel's original design. Given how radical the institution was — circumventing what appears to be a *d'oraita* — perhaps Hillel deliberately chose to confine its use to courts of the highest caliber.

The question accordingly remains unresolved: is *pruzbul* still operative on the basis of Hillel's original enactment, or are we doing it on the independent authority of the current central *beit din*? This *Gemara* carries important implications beyond *pruzbul* — it suggests that *hefker bet din hefker* is limited to *batei din* of central and high authority, a point relevant to other applications of that principle.

## Shmuel's Opposition

### Olban Shel Dayanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָא פְּרוֹסְבֻּלָא – עוּלְבָּנָא דְּדַיָּינֵי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: **Come** and **hear that** which **Shmuel said: This *prosbol* is an *ulbena* of the judges.**

The *Gemara* now introduces another statement of Shmuel, which is fully consistent with his restrictive posture toward *pruzbul*. *De-amar Shmuel: hai pruzbul ulbana de-dayyani hu* — this *pruzbul* is an embarrassment to the judges. Shmuel characterizes the institution as a disgrace, presumably because it involves circumventing what presents itself as a Torah law — even if *shmitta* nowadays is Rabbinic, it looks like a Torah law — and the judiciary is implicated in this circumvention.

### Shmuel's Chutzpah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אֲיַישַּׁר חַיִל אֲבַטְּלִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If my strength increases I will nullify it.

Shmuel goes further still: *ve-ei yashar cheili, chaya, avatleinei* — if I had the strength, I would nullify it entirely. This statement likely motivated the *Gemara*'s earlier question about whether *pruzbul* can be annulled. Given that Shmuel expressed such displeasure and such a desire, the *Gemara* naturally wondered whether the institution was in fact susceptible to annulment.

What drove Shmuel's opposition? Several possibilities present themselves. He may have felt the weight of the appearance of circumventing Torah law. He may have believed that in his generation, people were willing to absorb the annulment of small debts and would have been prepared to do so, making *pruzbul* unnecessary. Or, much as one might observe about the institution of *mechirat chametz*, he may have felt that a well-intentioned workaround was being abused and applied far beyond the cases that genuinely warranted it — thereby undermining the very *mitzvah* it was designed to protect.

### The Implicit Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנֶּעֱלָבִין וְאֵינָן עוֹלְבִים, שׁוֹמְעִין חֶרְפָּתָן וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין, עוֹשִׂין מֵאַהֲבָה וּשְׂמֵחִין בְּיִסּוּרִין, עֲלֵיהֶן הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״וְאוֹהֲבָיו כְּצֵאת הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ בִּגְבוּרָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues discussing the meaning of the word *ulbena*. **The Sages taught: Those who are insulted [*ne'elavin*] but do not insult** others, **who hear their shame but do not respond, who act out of love and are joyful in** their **suffering, about them the verse states: "And they that love Him are as the sun going forth in its might"** (Judges 5:31).

The *Gemara* at this point introduces an *aggadic* passage that may carry an implicit response to Shmuel. The phrase *ulbana* — embarrassment or disgrace — leads the *Gemara* to the *braita*: *ha-ne'elavin ve-einam olevim, shom'in cherpatam ve-einam meshivim, osim me-ahava u-semechim be-yisurin* — those who are shamed and do not return the shame, who hear themselves being disgraced and do not respond, who serve God out of love and rejoice in affliction — on them the verse says, *ve-ohavav ke-tzet ha-shemesh bi-gvurato*, those who love God shall be as the sun going forth in its strength. One might read this as a subtle pushback against Shmuel: rather than responding to the perceived embarrassment of *pruzbul* by seeking to dismantle it, the *Gemara* gestures toward the virtue of bearing that discomfort and maintaining the institution.

### Cannot Annul Unilaterally

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אֲיַישֵּׁר חַיִל יוֹתֵר מֵהִלֵּל, אֲבַטְּלִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If my strength increases** so that I become **greater than Hillel,** then **I will nullify** the *prosbol*.

The *Gemara* then asks explicitly: Shmuel said he would annul *pruzbul* — but how could he? *Ve-chi beit din yakhol levatel divrei beit din chavero ela im kein gadol mimenu be-chochma u-ve-minyan?* A *beit din* cannot annul the legislation of an earlier one unless it is greater. So what was Shmuel thinking?

The *Gemara* clarifies: *im yashar cheili yoter me-Hillel avatleinei* — Shmuel was not saying that he could annul it. He fully recognized that he could not, because *pruzbul* remains grounded in Hillel's authority, which Shmuel did not surpass. The statement was a wishful expression: if only I had the authority greater than Hillel's, I would do away with it. The practical reality is that as long as the institution rests on Hillel's enactment, no later *beit din* of lesser standing can dislodge it. Shmuel's two statements together — limit it to major courts, and wish it could be abolished — paint a consistent picture of an authority deeply uncomfortable with the institution and doing everything within his power to curtail it.

## Rav Nachman

### Automatic Pruzbul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֲקַיְּימִנֵּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rav Naḥman said:** If my strength increases, **I will uphold** the institution of the *prosbol*.

Against Shmuel's restrictive posture, Rav Nachman says *akimnah* — I would affirm and establish it.

### Already Established

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲקַיְּימִנֵּהּ?! הָא מִיקַּיַּים וְקָאֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵימָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא כָּתוּב כְּכָתוּב דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What is meant by: **I will uphold it? Isn't** it **upheld and standing?** Why does the *prosbol* require further support? The Gemara explains: **This is what he said:** If my strength increases, **I will say something about it,** and I will institute **that even though** the *prosbol* **was not written, it is considered as though it was written.**

The *Gemara* responds with an obvious question: *ha-mekayem kayam* — what do you mean you would establish it? It's already established. Who are you to re-establish something already in force? This question, too, touches on whether *pruzbul* still rests on Hillel's original authority or whether it requires fresh affirmation.

### Rashi's View

The *Gemara* resolves this by explaining that Rav Nachman's intent was something more radical. *Eima bei milta de-af al gav de-la katav ki-katav dami* — he would declare that even if one did not write a *pruzbul*, it is treated as if it were written. As Rashi explains: *oshiv beit din ve-atkin de-stam malvin yihyu ke-mosrin shetaroteihen le-veit din de-af al gav de-la ketiv yehei ke-mi she-katav* — he would convene a *beit din* and establish that all lenders by default be considered as having transferred their documents to the *beit din*, such that even without any act of writing, a *pruzbul* would be automatically and implicitly in effect for every loan.

The contrast between the positions of Shmuel and Rav Nachman could not be starker. Shmuel wants to eliminate or radically restrict *pruzbul*; Rav Nachman wants to make it so universal that no affirmative act is required at all.

This range of opinion has direct *halachic* implications. Some *Rishonim* and *poskim* understand the ease with which certain *Amora'im* applied *pruzbul* as evidence that they regarded *shemittat kesafim* nowadays — particularly outside of *Eretz Yisrael* — as no more than *minhag chasidus*, a praiseworthy custom but not a binding obligation. On this view, even without a *pruzbul*, one may still collect one's loans. Others maintain that it retains at least Rabbinic force and requires some act, even if a minimal one. The question of how seriously to take *shemittat kesafim* today has also been explored by Rav Kook as a lens through which to understand the broader debate about the status of *shmitta* in our era, including the disputed mechanism of *heter mechira* for *shemitat karka*.

## Etymology

### Chisda's Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״פְּרוֹסְבּוּל״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: פְּרוֹס בּוּלֵי וּבוּטֵי. בּוּלֵי – אֵלּוּ עֲשִׁירִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשָׁבַרְתִּי אֶת גְּאוֹן עוּזְּכֶם״, וְתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵלּוּ בּוּלָאוֹת שֶׁבִּיהוּדָה. בּוּטֵי – אֵלּוּ הָעֲנִיִּים, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַעֲבֵט תַּעֲבִיטֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the word ***prosbol*? Rav Ḥisda said: An ordinance [*pros*] of *bulei* and *butei*. *Bulei*, these are** the **wealthy, as it is written: "And I will break the pride of your power"** (Leviticus 26:19), **and Rav Yosef taught** with regard to this verse: **These are the *bula'ot*,** the wealthy people, **of Judea. *Butei*, these are the poor,** who are in need of a loan, **as it is written:** "You shall not shut your hand from your needy brother; but you shall open your hand to him, and **you shall lend him [*ha'avet ta'avitenu*]** sufficient for his need" (Deuteronomy 15:7–8).

The *Gemara* asks: *mai pruzbul* — what does the word *pruzbul* mean? Rav Chisda explains: *pruz buli u-buti*. The *pruz*, which Rashi understands as an establishment or enactment, is for both the *buli* and the *buti*. *Buli eilu ashirim* — the *buli* are the wealthy, as derived from the verse *ve-shavarti et ge'on uzchem*, and as connected with the *bulaot she-biYehuda*, the powerful class of Judea. *Buti eilu aniyim* — the *buti* are the poor, derived from *havet ta'avitenu*, you shall surely lend them. So *pruzbul* is an establishment that benefits both the wealthy, who can collect their loans, and the poor, who will now be lent to.

### Greek Origins

The actual etymology of the word is close to Rav Chisda's reading but not identical. The word *buli* is a Greek term for a council or assembly. As Klein's dictionary records, the word *pruzbul* is probably shortened from the Greek *pros boule bouleuteon* — meaning, presented before the assembly of counselors. The *Gemara* identifies the *buli* with the *ashirim*, the wealthy class, which reflects the social reality that the council members were typically the powerful elite. But the primary meaning of the full term is one who presents a matter before a court or council. This is why the institution is called *pruzbul* — the debt is, as it were, brought before the court. The lower house of the modern Greek legislature is still known as the *boule*, the same term.

One of the *Amora'im* suggests another etymology based on a foreign language: *porsa de-milta* — something that fixes or addresses the matter. Rashi seems uncertain about this derivation, but it is recorded alongside Rav Chisda's explanation.

## Land Requirement (II)

### Mishna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין פְּרוֹסְבּוּל אֶלָּא עַל הַקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shevi'it* 10:6): **One may write a *prosbol* only on** the basis of the debtor owning **land.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishna* from *Shevi'it*: *ein kotvin pruzbul ela al ha-karka* — a *pruzbul* may only be written if the debtor has land. This requirement is the focus of the next extended discussion.

### Limiting to Large Loans

Why does *pruzbul* require the debtor to hold land? Rashi and Tosafot both offer the following reasoning: the Rabbis established *pruzbul* only for the standard, common case of lending, which typically involves a debtor who owns land — since land is what allows the creditor to collect. One does not ordinarily lend large sums to someone with no assets at all. On small loans, without land present, people may well be content to have *shmitta* annul the debt.

This rationale suggests a secondary purpose as well: the land requirement serves to limit *pruzbul* to substantial loans, precisely the cases for which the institution was designed. Small debts, for which people are borrowing without any underlying assets, are arguably meant to be absorbed by *shmitta*. By requiring land, the Rabbis ensured that *pruzbul* would be used only where there is genuine need.

There is also a second explanation, which anticipates material the *Gemara* will develop later. Among the types of debt not annulled by *shmitta* even without a *pruzbul* is a loan for which the creditor holds collateral — because in such a case the creditor is not personally exacting payment, and the debt has, in a sense, already been partially collected. The combination of *pruzbul* with *karka* may similarly be designed to give the debt a quality of partial collection: real estate cannot be hidden, cannot be moved, and can always be seized by a court. The presence of *karka* may thus transform the debt from a pure obligation into something more concretely within the creditor's reach — lending the *pruzbul* a more substantive foundation.

## Expanding the Land Requirement

### Expanded Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵין לוֹ – מְזַכֵּהוּ בְּתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. וְכַמָּה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא? אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ קֶלַח שֶׁל כְּרוּב. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ הִשְׁאִילוֹ מָקוֹם לְתַנּוּר וּלְכִירַיִם – כּוֹתְבִין עָלָיו פְּרוֹסְבּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the debtor **has no** land, then the creditor **transfers any amount of his own field to him** so that he can write a *prosbol*. The Gemara asks: **And how much** is sufficient to be classified as **any amount? Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi says** that **Rav says: Even** the amount of land sufficient to grow **a stalk of cabbage** is sufficient. **Rav Yehuda says: Even** if **he lent him a place** sufficient **for an oven and a stove, one** may **write a *prosbol* on** this basis.

Having established the *karka* requirement, the *Gemara* addresses what happens when the debtor does not own land. *Im ein lo, mezakehu be-toch sodehu* — if the debtor has no land, the creditor may transfer even a tiny parcel of his own land to the debtor, thereby enabling the writing of a *pruzbul*. The *Gemara* establishes that *kol shehu*, even the most minimal amount, suffices. Rav Chiya bar Ashi citing Rav says: *afilu klach shel kruv* — even a single head of cabbage, even a square inch's worth of ground, is sufficient.

Rav Yehuda then extends this further: *afilu hishilo makom le-tanur ve-chirayim* — even if the debtor has merely borrowed space from someone on which to place his oven, that constitutes sufficient connection to land to permit writing a *pruzbul*. Borrowed land, not even owned, counts.

## Overcoming the Requirement

### Transfer Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי הִלֵּל: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין פְּרוֹסְבּוּל אֶלָּא עַל עָצִיץ נָקוּב בִּלְבָד. נָקוּב – אִין, שֶׁאֵינוֹ נָקוּב – לָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this statement: **Is that so? But didn't Hillel teach** (*Tosefta*, *Shevi'it* 8:10): **One writes a *prosbol* only on** the basis of the debtor owning **merely a perforated pot** placed on the ground. This demonstrates that **a perforated** pot **can** serve as the basis for the writing of a *prosbol*, as it is considered to be part of the ground due to the perforation; however, a pot **that is not perforated cannot** serve as the basis for the writing of a *prosbol*.

The practical implication of the expanded definition of *karka* is that the creditor can always manufacture the land requirement by transferring a tiny parcel to the debtor. This transfer can be effected through a third party even without the debtor's active consent, since acquiring land is a *zechus* — a benefit — for the recipient, and one may confer a *zechus* upon a person in their absence. The *Gemara* does note the tension: this is ultimately a *zechus* that will be used against the debtor's interests, since it enables the *pruzbul* that will allow collection of the debt. Nevertheless, the act is effective.

### Perforated Pot

A *braita* citing Hillel himself specifies that one may write a *pruzbul* only if the debtor has an *atzis nakuv* — a perforated flower pot. A flower pot with a hole in the bottom is considered to have a connection to the soil beneath it and thus constitutes *karka*. One without a hole, *eino nakuv*, does not. The *Gemara* finds this striking: why should the unperforated pot not qualify?

### Borrowed Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? וְהָא אִיכָּא מְקוֹמוֹ! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּמַנַּח אַסִּיכֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the question: Since Rav Yehuda stated that possession of the place occupied by the oven is also considered possession of the land underneath with regard to this issue, **why** can't a non-perforated pot serve as the basis for a *prosbol*; **but isn't there** the **place** where the pot is resting? The Gemara answers: **No,** one cannot compare the case of Rav Yehuda to this case, as it is **necessary** for Hillel to state his *halakha* in a case **where** the pot **is resting on stakes** and the borrower has no possession of any land at all.

The *Gemara* raises a sharp objection: *ve-ha'ika mekomo* — wherever the debtor places his flower pot, he presumably has some right to that space of ground. If borrowed land suffices for *pruzbul*, as Rav Yehuda just ruled, then anyone who has so much as a place to set down any possession should have sufficient connection to land. Why, then, does Hillel's statement require specifically an *atzis nakuv*?

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzricha de-manach asichi* — the case is one where the pot is placed on pegs rather than directly on the ground. In such a case, the debtor has rights to use someone's table or elevated surface, but has not been granted any rights to the ground itself. Therefore, despite the physical presence of his possession, he has no connection to actual land, and the *pruzbul* cannot be written. The distinction is fine but operative: it is specifically the right to *the ground* that matters, not merely any right of placement.

What the *Gemara* is doing throughout this discussion is working both sides of a tension. On one side is the impulse — consistent with Shmuel's view — to maintain *pruzbul* as a meaningful, somewhat restricted institution that is not available to everyone. On the other side is the impulse — consistent with Rav Nachman's view — to make it as widely available as possible. The progressive expansion of what counts as *karka* reflects the triumph of the latter tendency: in practice, anyone can always come up with some connection to land.

### Palm Tree Trunk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי מַקְנֵי לֵיהּ גִּידְמָא דְּדִיקְלָא, וּכְתַב עֲלֵיהּ פְּרוֹסְבּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** When **Rav Ashi** would lend money and wish to write a *prosbol* **he would transfer** to the borrower **a stump of a palm tree** that was still attached to the ground, **and he wrote a *prosbol*** based **on this.**

Consistent with that tendency, Rav Ashi in practice transferred a *gidma de-dikla* — the trunk of a palm tree — to a debtor through a third party and then wrote a *pruzbul* upon it. A trunk of a tree serves as the minimal *karka* equivalent sufficient to ground the institution.

## Verbal Pruzbul

### Academy Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב אָשֵׁי מָסְרִי מִילַּיְיהוּ לַהֲדָדֵי. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן מְסַר מִילֵּי לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא; אֲמַר לֵיהּ: צְרִיכְנָא מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא צְרִיכַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts a related incident: **The Sages of the school of Rav Ashi would transfer their matters,** i.e., their debts, **to each other** without writing a *prosbol*, by stating: You are hereby a court, and the debt is given over to you. **Rabbi Yonatan transferred a matter** by means of such a statement **to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba.** Rabbi Yonatan **said to him: Do I need anything else? He said to him: You do not need** anything else, as this statement alone is sufficient.

The progression toward simplification reaches its endpoint in the practice of the scholars of Rav Ashi's academy. *Rabbanan d'bei Rav Ashi* did not write anything at all. They simply verbally transferred their loans to one another. In practice, one would walk over to three colleagues sitting nearby, say words to the effect of "you are my *beit din* and I am transferring my loans to you," and the matter was considered complete. No document, no transfer of land, nothing beyond a few words spoken informally among colleagues.

### Verbal Transfer

Rabbi Yochanan similarly transferred his loans verbally to Rabbi Chiya bar Abba. Having done so, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba asked whether anything further was required. Rabbi Yochanan replied: *lo tzrichas* — nothing further is needed.

The trajectory from a formal written document executed before a major *beit din*, to a verbal declaration before one's colleagues, reflects the dominant position that in practice *pruzbul* should be maximally accessible. Some *Rishonim* explain this extreme simplification by suggesting that for these *Amora'im*, the annulment of debts nowadays was regarded as no more than *midas chasidus* — a praiseworthy stringency — and therefore any minimal gesture acknowledging the institution was sufficient. Others suggest this leniency was confined to *talmidei chachamim* who could be trusted not to abuse the device.

## Cases Without Land

### Basic Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹ קַרְקַע, וְלֶעָרֵב יֵשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע – כּוֹתְבִין עָלָיו פְּרוֹסְבּוּל. לוֹ וְלֶעָרֵב אֵין לָהֶן קַרְקַע, וְלַחַיָּיב לוֹ יֵשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע – כּוֹתְבִין עָלָיו פְּרוֹסְבּוּל, מִדְּרַבִּי נָתָן –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the requirement for the debtor to have land. **The Sages taught:** If the debtor **has no land but the guarantor has land** then **one writes a *prosbol* on** the basis of the land of the guarantor. If both **he and the guarantor have no land, but** another person **who is obligated** to pay money **to** the debtor **has land,** then **one writes a *prosbol* on** the basis of this land. This *halakha* is derived **from** a statement **of Rabbi Natan.**

Returning to earlier *mishnayot* from *Shevi'it* that address situations where the debtor lacks land, the *Gemara* now outlines the basic rule: *ein lo karka v'la-arev yesh lo karka* — if the debtor himself has no land but the guarantor of the loan does, one may write a *pruzbul*, since ultimately the creditor could collect from the guarantor's landed property. *Kotvin alav pruzbul* in such a case.

### Chain Debts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לַנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵרוֹ מָנֶה, וַחֲבֵרוֹ בַּחֲבֵרוֹ, מִנַּיִן שֶׁמּוֹצִיאִין מִזֶּה וְנוֹתְנִין לָזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנָתַן לַאֲשֶׁר אָשַׁם לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: From where** is it derived that **when one lends one hundred dinars to his friend, and** that **friend** lends an identical sum **to his** own **friend, that** the court **appropriates** the money **from this one,** the second debtor, **and gives it to that one,** the first creditor, without going through the middleman, who is both the first debtor and the second creditor? **The verse states** with regard to returning stolen property: **"And gives it to him in respect of whom he has been guilty"** (Numbers 5:7).

The *Gemara* extends this further: if neither the debtor nor the guarantor has land, but a third party who owes money *to the debtor* has land, that too suffices. I have lent money to Reuven, who has no land. Shimon, who guaranteed the loan, also has no land. But Levi owes money to Reuven, and Levi has land. Since I could ultimately reach Levi's property through the chain of obligations, *kotvin alav pruzbul* — a *pruzbul* may be written.

### Rav Natan

How does one jump over an intermediate link in a chain of debts? The *Gemara* cites Rav Natan: *mi-nayin le-noshe be-chavero maneh ve-chavero be-chavero maneh she-motzi'in mi-zeh ve-notnin le-zeh* — how do we know that if A is owed money by B, and B is owed money by C, we may collect directly from C to pay A? The *Gemara* derives this from the verse *ve-natan la-asher asham lo* — the money is given to the one to whom the guilt is ultimately due. Since that money is ultimately coming to me, I may go directly to the end of the chain and collect. This ability to bypass intermediate parties is what allows the creditor to have constructive access to Levi's land, thereby satisfying the *karka* requirement for *pruzbul*.

## Shmitta's Scope

### All Annulled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַשְּׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמֶּטֶת אֶת הַמִּלְוָה, בֵּין בִּשְׁטָר בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁטָר. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בִּשְׁטָר – שְׁטָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁטָר – שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים; כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shevi'it* 10:1): **The Sabbatical** Year **abrogates debt both** for loans that were contracted **with** a promissory **note and** for loans that were contracted **without** a promissory **note.** The *amora'im* disagree with regard to the interpretation of the mishna: It is **Rav and Shmuel who both say:** Loans that were contracted **with** a promissory **note,** is referring to loans that were contracted with a promissory **note that contains a property guarantee;** loans that were contracted **without** a promissory **note,** is referring even to loans that were contracted with a promissory note **that does not contain a property guarantee. All the more so, a loan by oral** agreement is canceled by the Sabbatical Year.

The *Gemara* now surveys *Mishnayot* from the tenth chapter of *Shevi'it* — the chapter devoted entirely to *shemittat kesafim*, since there is no *Bavli* on *Shevi'it* itself. The *Mishna* states: *ha-shevi'it meshametet et ha-malveh bein be-shtar bein she-lo be-shtar* — the seventh year annuls loans, whether they were made with a document or without.

### Rav and Shmuel

Rav and Shmuel explain this *Mishna* together: *be-shtar* refers to *shtar she-yesh bo achrayut nechasim*, a document that has explicitly written into it liens on the debtor's property. *She-lo be-shtar* refers to *she-ein bo achrayut nechasim* — literally a document that lacks those explicit liens, or indeed a verbal loan with no document at all. On this reading, *shmitta* annuls everything — even a *shtar* with explicit liens, and *kal va-chomer* a purely verbal debt.

### Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בִּשְׁטָר – שְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁטָר – מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה. אֲבָל שְׁטָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, it is **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish who both say:** Loans that were contracted **with a** promissory **note** is referring to loans that were contracted with a promissory **note that does not contain a property guarantee,** whereas loans that were contracted **without** a promissory **note** is referring to **a loan by oral** agreement. **However,** the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** a loan contracted with a promissory **note that contains a property guarantee,** as it is as though the creditor had already taken possession of the debtor's land.

Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish read the *Mishna* differently. *Be-shtar* refers to *shtar she-ein bo achrayut nechasim* — a document without explicit liens. *She-lo be-shtar* means a purely verbal loan with no document whatsoever. The implication of this reading is that there exists a third category: *shtar she-yesh bo achrayut nechasim*, a document with explicit liens written in, which is *not* annulled by *shmitta*, even without a *pruzbul*. That case is omitted from the *Mishna*'s list of what *shmitta* annuls, because according to Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish, *shmitta* does not touch it at all.

## Liens as Ownership

### Right vs. Ownership

This position of Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish rests on a fundamental conceptual distinction. How does one understand the nature of a lien? Is it a right that a creditor may exercise upon the debtor's property when collection becomes necessary, which crystallizes only at the moment of collection? Or is it an actual partial ownership of the property itself — present and real from the moment the *shtar* is written, even before any collection occurs?

Some *Rishonim* imply the latter view: when a *shtar* is written with *achrayut nechasim* explicitly stated, that *shtar* functions not only as a *shtar re'ayah* — a document of proof of the debt — but also as a *shtar mecher*, a document that transfers a form of property rights. The creditor does not merely have a claim against the debtor; he has a form of partial ownership in the debtor's real estate from the moment of the loan.

### Consequence

The consequence of this understanding is direct: *shmitta* annuls debts — obligations of one person to another — but it cannot annul what is already in a sense one's own property. If the written *achrayut* has already transferred a partial ownership in the land to the creditor, there is no longer a pure debt to be annulled. The debt has been, as it were, partially absorbed into a property right, and *shmitta* cannot reach it.

### Supporting Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שְׁטַר חוֹב – מְשַׁמֵּט, וְאִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט. תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: סִיֵּים לוֹ שָׂדֶה אַחַת בְּהַלְווֹאָתוֹ, אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ כָּתַב: ״כׇּל נְכָסַיי אַחְרָאִין וְעַרְבָאִין לָךְ״ – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish:** The Sabbatical Year **abrogates a promissory note, but if** the promissory note **contains a property guarantee** the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate it.** Similarly, **it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If the debtor **specified one field for** the repayment of **his loan,** then **it is not canceled. And not only that, but even** if **he wrote: All of my property is pledged and guaranteed to you,** then the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate it,** even if he does not specify a field for the repayment of the loan.

The *Gemara* cites a *braita* in support of Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish: *shtar chov meshamet* — a loan document is annulled by *shmitta*. *Ve-im yesh bo achrayut nechasim eino meshamet* — but if it has liens written into it, it is not annulled. This *braita* is adduced independently of *pruzbul*; it reflects a baseline *halacha* that a document bearing explicit liens is not annulled by *shmitta* as a matter of principle.

A second *braita* reinforces this: *sicheid lo sadeh achat be-halvahato eino meshamet* — if the creditor specifically identified a particular field from which he would collect in the event of default, *shmitta* does not annul the debt. *Ve-lo od* — and furthermore, even if no specific field was identified but the debtor wrote *kol nechasai achra'in ve-arva'in lach*, all my property is encumbered and pledged to you — that too is not annulled by *shmitta*. This mirrors the position of Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish: the explicit written *achrayut*, whether specific or general, transforms the nature of the debt.

## Practical Hesitation

### Assi's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרִיבֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא שְׁטָרָא דַּהֲוָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ אַחְרָיוּת נְכָסִים, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְשַׁמֵּט אוֹ אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The relative of Rabbi Asi had a certain** promissory **note that had a property guarantee written in it. He came before Rabbi Asi** and **said to him:** Does the Sabbatical Year **abrogate** this loan, **or** does it **not abrogate** it? **He said to him:** It **does not abrogate** it.

A practical case now arises. A relative of Rav Assi held a *shtar* with *achrayut nechasim* explicitly written into it, and sought to know: will *shmitta* annul this debt? The person was concerned — understandably, since if *shmitta* would annul it, he needed to act promptly, either by writing a *pruzbul* or otherwise protecting his claim.

### Initial Opinion

Rav Assi, consistent with the position of Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish, initially ruled that the debt would not be annulled. The written *achrayut* gave the creditor a form of property ownership that *shmitta* could not reach. The relative, apparently pleased with this answer but wanting authoritative confirmation — since the ability to actually collect the debt depends not merely on one opinion but on a reliable *halachic* consensus — went to Rabbi Yochanan himself.

### Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁבְקֵיהּ, וַאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְשַׁמֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **He left** Rabbi Asi **and came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and asked him the same question. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: It does abrogate** it.

Rabbi Yochanan ruled: *meshamet* — the debt *will* be annulled. Rav Assi, having heard this, went to Rabbi Yochanan directly and pressed him: but did you yourself, together with Resh Lakish, not rule that a *shtar* with *achrayut nechasim* is not annulled? Rabbi Yochanan acknowledged the argument but replied: *ve-chi mi-penei she-anu medamim na'aseh ma'aseh?* — just because we can construct an argument, does that mean we should act upon it? Of course, the entire enterprise of *Shas* is precisely the construction of legal arguments — but here Rabbi Yochanan is making a more pointed claim. Where there is no explicit textual grounding for a ruling, where it rests on *svara* alone, he is not prepared to issue a practical ruling on that basis. The consequence in this case is significant: without a firm ruling in his favor, the creditor must write a *pruzbul* to protect his debt.

### Beit Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא רַבִּי אַסִּי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְשַׁמֵּט אוֹ אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְשַׁמֵּט. וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁמֵּט! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָנוּ מְדַמִּין, נַעֲשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָתַנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּמָר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּלְמָא הָהִיא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: שְׁטָר הָעוֹמֵד לִגְבּוֹת – כְּגָבוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and **said to him:** Does the Sabbatical Year **abrogate** this loan, **or** does it **not abrogate** it? **He said to him: It does abrogate** it. Rabbi Asi challenged him: **But wasn't it** the **Master** himself **who said** that the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** a promissory note that contains a property guarantee? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Because we think** that this should be the *halakha* should **we perform an action** based on this? Rabbi Asi **said to him: But isn't it taught in** a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of** the **Master? He said to him: Perhaps that** *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say** more generally: **A** promissory **note that stands to be collected is considered as though it has been collected,** and this is why the loan is not abrogated, as it is considered as though the loan had already been repaid. And the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai in that issue.

The *Gemara* pushes back on Rabbi Yochanan's reversal: but you had a *braita* supporting your position! It was not pure *svara*. Rabbi Yochanan responds: *dilma hi Beit Shammai hi* — perhaps that *braita* follows the opinion of Beit Shammai. Beit Shammai holds, in another context, that *shtar ha-omed li-gvot ki-gavuy dami* — a document ready for collection is treated as if already collected. If that principle underlies the *braita*'s ruling, then the *braita* may reflect a minority position we do not follow. According to Beit Hillel, until the money is actually paid, it is not in the creditor's possession, and no anticipatory ownership can be imputed. Rabbi Yochanan, therefore, could not rely on the *braita* to issue a practical ruling, lest it be grounded in Beit Shammai's rejected position. In the end, the wise counsel was to write a *pruzbul* and avoid the entire uncertainty.

## Other Exemptions

### Collateral and Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מָעוֹת עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, וְהַמּוֹסֵר שְׁטָרוֹתָיו לְבֵית דִין – אֵין מְשַׁמְּטִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא מוֹסֵר שְׁטָרוֹתָיו לְבֵית דִּין – דְּתָפְסִי לְהוּ בֵּי דִינָא. אֶלָּא מַלְוֶה עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shevi'it* 10:2): With regard to **one who lends money to another** based **on collateral, and one who transfers his** promissory **notes to a court,** the debt owed to them **is not canceled.** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the debt is not canceled when **one transfers his** promissory **notes to a court, as** the **court seizes** the promissory notes, and they are able to collect this debt. **But what is the reason** that the debt is not canceled for **one who lends** money based **on collateral?**

The *Gemara* returns to the *Mishna* from *Shevi'it*: *ha-malveh et chavero chov al ha-mashkon, ve-ha-moseir shtarotav le-veit din — ein meshamtin* — a loan made against collateral, and a loan whose documents were actually handed over to *beit din*, are not annulled by *shmitta*. This is independent of *pruzbul*; it reflects the baseline *halacha* that these two categories of debt are simply not subject to *shmitta*'s annulment.

### Possession Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּתָפֵס לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, הִלְוָהוּ וְדָר בַּחֲצֵרוֹ – דְּתָפֵיס לֵיהּ, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא מְשַׁמֵּט?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Due to** the fact that the creditor **has seized** an item that belongs to the debtor, it is considered as though the debt has already been collected. **Abaye said to him: If that is so,** then if a creditor **loaned money to** someone **and lives in his courtyard** as a collateral for the loan, **since** he **seizes** the courtyard, which belongs to the debtor, would you **also** say that the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** the debt? That would contradict the accepted *halakha* that in this case the debt is canceled.

*Be-shlama moseir shtarotav le-veit din* — when the documents are genuinely handed over to *beit din*, the rationale is clear: it is the *beit din* that holds the documents and is empowered to collect, and the creditor is no longer personally exacting payment. The prohibition of *lo yigosh* — the creditor shall not press the debtor — is therefore not violated, and the debt, having been placed in the court's hands, is as if already somewhat collected. This is precisely the model on which Hillel constructed *pruzbul* as a legal fiction: the *pruzbul* works *ke-mosir shtarotav le-veit din*, as if one has handed the documents to the court, even though the actual giving over is a formal fiction.

But the *mashkon*, the collateral case, requires its own explanation. Why should holding collateral exempt a loan from *shmitta*?

### Living on Property

Abaye poses a sharp question: *ella me'ata hilvehu ve-dar be-chatzeiro, d'tafsilei, hachi nami ve-lo meshamet?* — if you lent someone money and happen to be living on his property, would we also say the debt is not annulled? As Tosafot clarifies, the case is one where you are not merely a tenant but are living there by virtue of a right connected to the loan — in essence, a physical presence in the debtor's property linked to the credit relationship. If mere possession is the logic, why does living on the debtor's property not equally protect the debt from *shmitta*?

## Mashkon Status

### Distinct Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁאנֵי מַשְׁכּוֹן, דְּקָנֵי לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״ – אִם אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה, צְדָקָה מִנַּיִן? מִכָּאן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Collateral is different, as** the creditor **acquires it** for himself, as learned **from** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From where** is it derived **that a creditor acquires collateral** given to him and is considered its owner so long as the item is in his possession? **As it is stated** in the verse with regard to the obligation of a creditor to return collateral at night: **"And it shall be righteousness for you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13). Rabbi Yitzḥak infers: **If** the creditor **does not acquire** the collateral, then **from where** is the **righteousness** involved in returning it? He would be simply returning an item to its rightful owner. **From here** it is learned **that a creditor acquires the collateral.** Therefore, when he returns the collateral to the debtor he is performing an act of charity.

The *Gemara* answers through a distinction. Living on the debtor's property, even with a legal connection to the loan, does not create ownership in the property. But a *mashkon* — collateral — is different, because the creditor has a form of actual ownership over the collateral itself.

### Yitzchak Drasha

Rabbi Yitzchak derives this from a *pasuk*: *m'nayin le-ba'al chov she-koneh mashkon* — how do we know that the creditor owns the collateral to some degree? From the verse that describes the mitzvah of returning a pledge: *u-lecha tihiyeh tzedakah* — and it shall be accounted to you as righteousness. *Im eino koneh, tzedakah m'nayin?* — if you did not own it, where does the *tzedaka* come from? *Tzedaka* by definition involves giving away something of yours to another. The act of returning the collateral is described as *tzedaka* because the collateral belongs, in some sense, to the creditor. By returning it, the creditor is performing an act of generosity with property that is, at that moment, legally his.

### Already Collected

The consequence follows directly. Since the creditor has a form of ownership over the collateral, the debt has been, in a meaningful sense, already partially collected — it is no longer a pure obligation floating between two parties, but an obligation partially secured in the creditor's actual possession. Therefore, *shmitta* cannot annul it. The same *mechaber* of logic that produced the analysis of *achrayut nechasim* as partial ownership of land applies here: where a debt has been absorbed into some form of property right or actual possession, *shmitta* cannot reach it.

## Debt Ownership

### Spectrum

The *sugya* as a whole traces a spectrum of debt types, distinguished by the degree to which the creditor's claim has been absorbed into something more concrete than a pure interpersonal obligation. At one end sits the purely verbal debt — *milveh al peh* — a bare obligation with no documentation, no lien, no collateral, and no possession. *Shmitta* annuls this most readily. Moving along the spectrum: a *shtar* without explicit liens, a *shtar* with explicit *achrayut nechasim* debated by the *Amora'im*, a loan against which the creditor holds collateral and thereby has genuine ownership, and finally a debt whose documents have actually been transferred to *beit din* and are thus effectively in the court's hands. At the far end, the debt is as close to already collected as a debt can be without actually having been paid. The institution of *pruzbul* functions by positioning a debt, through legal fiction or actual transfer, somewhere toward the collected end of this spectrum — and thus out of *shmitta*'s reach.
