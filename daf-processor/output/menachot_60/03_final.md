# Menachot 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oil & Frankincense

### Types of Menachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְחַיָּיב עַל הַשֶּׁמֶן בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, וְחַיָּיב עַל לְבוֹנָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ. נָתַן עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן – פְּסָלָהּ, לְבוֹנָה – יִלְקְטֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And** if one places oil or frankincense on the meal offering of a sinner or on the meal offering of jealousy brought by a *sota*, he is **liable** to be flogged **for** violating the prohibition against placing **the oil by itself, and** he is **liable** to be flogged **for** violating the prohibition against placing **the frankincense by itself,** as these are two separate prohibitions. If **one placed oil upon** the meal offering **he has disqualified it,** but if one placed **frankincense** upon the meal offering **he should gather** the frankincense and remove **it.** In this manner, the meal offering can be salvaged.

Today's daf is 60, though we begin with the *mishnah* on 59b. The *mishnah* on 59a classified the various types of *menachot* according to their ingredients: some receive both oil and *levonah* (frankincense), some receive one or the other, and some receive neither. The two *menachot* that receive neither are the *minchat chotei* — the meal offering brought as part of a *korban oleh v'yored*, a sacrifice whose particulars vary according to the donor's economic means — and the *minchat kenaot* brought on behalf of the *sotah*. In both cases the Torah states explicitly: *lo yasim aleha shemen v'lo yiten aleha levonah* — one may not place oil upon it and may not give frankincense upon it. This constitutes a negative prohibition, a *lo ta'aseh*, and the present *mishnah* addresses the practical consequences of violating it.

### Oil vs. Frankincense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן שֶׁמֶן עַל שְׁיָרֶיהָ – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה; נָתַן כְּלִי עַל גַּבֵּי כְּלִי – לֹא פְּסָלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, one violates the prohibition only by placing oil on the meal offering prior to the removal of the handful; if **he placed oil on its remainder he does not violate a prohibition.** If **one placed a vessel** with oil **on top of a vessel** that contains a meal offering of a sinner or a meal offering brought by a *sota* **he did not disqualify** the meal offering, as the oil was not placed on the meal offering itself.

The *mishnah* establishes that one transgresses separately for the oil alone and for the *levonah* alone — even doing only one of the two is a violation. Beyond the transgression, however, placing oil on the *mincha* invalidates it entirely: *natan aleha shemen psalah*. The *levonah*, by contrast, can be remedied: *yilktenah* — one simply picks it off. Because this removal is possible, the *mincha* remains *kasher* despite the transgression having occurred. This is a striking result: one has violated a *lo ta'aseh* and yet the offering is not permanently disqualified.

### Frankincense Properties

The reason the *levonah* can be picked off while oil cannot is rooted in physical reality. *Levonah* is not merely solid — it has the character of crystals, like rock candy or pebble-like formations. It sits on the surface of the flour rather than penetrating it. Oil, by contrast, is absorbed into the flour and cannot be separated from it. Once oil enters the *mincha* it transforms the nature of the offering; what has infused itself into the *mincha* has permanently altered it, and that is why the offering is *pasul*.

### Oil on Remnants

The *mishnah* draws a further distinction: *natan shemen al shi'areha eino over b'lo ta'aseh* — if one places oil on the *shirayim*, the remnant of the *mincha* after the *kemitza* has been taken, one does not transgress the negative prohibition. This is noteworthy in contrast to the *chametz* prohibition, where making the remnant of a *mincha* leaven after it has been offered does constitute a violation of *lo te'afeh chametz chelkan*. The *mishnah* is thus highlighting a distinction in scope between these two prohibitions.

### Vessel on Vessel

A further refinement: if one takes a vessel containing oil and sets it on top of the *mincha* — without the oil coming into direct contact with the flour — this does not constitute the act forbidden by *lo yasim aleha shemen*. The prohibition requires placing the oil directly upon the *mincha* itself. Consequently, putting oil into a vessel and resting that vessel atop the *mincha* neither invalidates nor constitutes a transgression.

### Reversibility Question

One might ask: given that the *levonah* can be removed, why does placing it constitute a transgression at all? Is this not analogous to a *lav hanitak l'aseh* — a prohibition that is rectified through a subsequent positive act — which under certain frameworks would mitigate the violation? The answer is that *lav hanitak l'aseh* requires not merely that the act be physically reversible, but that a specific *mitzvat aseh* be attached to undo it. There is no such *aseh* here. Moreover, even in cases where physical reversal is possible, one has still transgressed in the moment of the act. Reversibility alone does not prevent the original violation from having occurred.

## Ground Frankincense

### Ground Frankincense Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נָתַן עָלֶיהָ לְבוֹנָה שְׁחוּקָה, מַהוּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְלׇקְטָהּ, וְהָא לָא אֶפְשָׁר לְלׇקְטָהּ? אוֹ דִילְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מִיבַּלְעָא, וְהָא נָמֵי לָא מִיבַּלְעָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar Rav Huna raised a dilemma to Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If one **placed frankincense** that had been **ground** into a fine powder, which cannot be gathered up and removed, **on** the meal offering of a sinner, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara clarifies: Is the reason that a meal offering on which frankincense was placed is generally valid **due to** the fact that it is **possible to gather** the frankincense, **and** since in **this** case it is **impossible to gather it** the meal offering is disqualified? **Or perhaps** the reason that the meal offering is usually not disqualified is **due to** the fact that the dry frankincense **is not absorbed** by the flour of the meal offering, **and** therefore, as **this** ground frankincense is **also not absorbed,** the meal offering is valid.

A question is posed: *Rav Papa bar Rav Huna ba'i minei Rabbi Yochanan* — Rav Papa bar Rav Huna asked Rabbi Yochanan: *natan lah levonah shechuka mahu* — what is the law if one ground the *levonah* and then placed it on the *mincha*? The ordinary *levonah* crystals sit on the surface and can be picked off. But if one grinds the *levonah* to a fine powder — reducing it from rock crystals to something like table salt — it will embed itself among the flour granules. Unlike oil, it has not been absorbed into the kernels themselves; rather, it sits side by side with them. But unlike whole crystals, it cannot be sifted out or picked off. Does this mixed state invalidate the *mincha*?

The question touches on a broader distinction familiar from the laws of *ta'arovot*: the difference between *lach b'lach*, where substances genuinely merge and infuse one another (like blood falling into chicken soup), and *yavesh b'yavesh*, where discrete solids sit next to one another without merging (like a *treif* piece of meat mixed among several *kasher* pieces). Ground *levonah* mixed into flour occupies an intermediate position: examined kernel by kernel it is *yavesh b'yavesh*, yet experientially the two substances are thoroughly intermingled. The *Gemara* is asking how to classify this intermediate case.

There is an additional practical complication that the *Gemara* leaves to the side: if the *levonah* powder is mixed into the flour, how could a valid *kemitza* be performed? The *kemitza* would be a *kemitza chasirah* — a deficient handful — since the handful would contain *levonah* along with the flour rather than pure flour. The *Gemara* nonetheless focuses on the prior question of invalidation.

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וּלְבוֹנָה יִלְקְטֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this dilemma from the mishna: **And** if one placed **frankincense** upon the meal offering **he should gather** and remove **it,** and it is then valid. This indicates that the status of the meal offering depends on whether or not the frankincense can be gathered up. Since ground frankincense cannot be gathered and removed from the flour, the meal offering should be disqualified.

The question reduces to identifying the operative reason why, in the ordinary case, the *mincha* remains *kasher* after the whole-crystal *levonah* is placed on it and then removed. Two reasons are possible. The first: *mishum d'efshar leloktah* — because it is possible to pick the *levonah* off; and here, with ground *levonah*, it cannot be picked off, so perhaps the *mincha* is *pasul*. The second: *mishum d'lo mivla* — because the *levonah* is not absorbed into the *mincha*; and the ground *levonah* is also not absorbed, since the granules merely sit alongside the flour granules without penetrating them, and therefore perhaps it remains *kasher* even without the ability to remove it.

### Mishnah Proof

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishnah* itself, which uses the language *yilktenah* — one picks off the *levonah*. This suggests that the act of picking off is the operative requirement, not merely the incidental description of what is possible. Similarly, the *baraita* states explicitly *she'efshar leloktah* — because it is possible to pick it off — as the stated rationale for the *mincha*'s remaining *kasher*. Both texts speak in terms of the ability to remove the *levonah*, pointing toward the first reason.

### Both Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא, דְּלָא מִיבַּלְעָא, וְעוֹד – יִלְקְטֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Perhaps** there are two reasons for this *halakha* but only one of them is explicitly stated in the mishna, as the *tanna* **states one** reason **and** adds **another. One** reason is **that** the Torah did not disqualify a meal offering upon which an improper item was placed, if that item **is not absorbed** in the flour. **And another** reason the meal offering is not disqualified by frankincense is that one can **gather** up the frankincense and restore the meal offering to its former state.

The *Gemara* responds to this proof by suggesting *chada v'od ka'amar* — perhaps the texts are stating one principle and an additional one beyond what is strictly necessary. The first principle is *d'lo mivla* — it is not absorbed. And moreover, *yilktenah* — it can even be physically removed. The texts describe the superior case, where both conditions obtain, but perhaps non-absorption alone would suffice even where removal is impossible.

The final resolution comes from Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, who cites a *baraita*: in the case of the *minchat chotei* or *minchat kenaot* upon which *levonah* was placed, *melaket et ha-levonah u'kesherah* — one picks off the *levonah* and it becomes *kasher*. The *Gemara* concludes from this that the act of removal is not merely incidental — until the *levonah* is actually removed, the *mincha* remains *pasul*. If ground *levonah* cannot be removed, the *mincha* remains permanently *pasul*. Non-absorption alone is not sufficient; removability is the essential criterion, and if removal is impossible the offering is disqualified.

## Reversible Invalidation

### Baraita Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנְיָא, מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת שֶׁנָּתַן עָלֶיהָ לְבוֹנָה – מְלַקֵּט אֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה, וּכְשֵׁרָה. וְאִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא לִיקֵּט לְבוֹנָתָהּ חִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ, בֵּין חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ בֵּין חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a meal offering of a sinner or a meal offering of jealousy** brought by a *sota* **upon which one placed frankincense,** he should **gather up the frankincense and** the meal offering is **valid. And if, before he gathered its frankincense,** the priest performing the service **had intent** to sacrifice **its** handful or eat its remainder **either beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet*,** unlike the usual case of an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time [*piggul*]. This is because at the time the priest had the improper intent the meal offering was not valid, as it had frankincense upon it at that time.

With the principle established that the *mincha* is *pasul* while the *levonah* remains on it and becomes *kasher* once the *levonah* is removed, the *Gemara* confronts a deeper difficulty: how can something that has become *pasul* become *kasher* again? There is a well-known principle — *dichuy* — that once a sacrificial object has been pushed aside or disqualified, it cannot regain its eligible status. How then does this *mincha* recover?

The *baraita* cited by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak sharpens the problem dramatically. It rules: if before the *levonah* was removed, the *kohen* entertained an improper *machshava* — either *chutz lizmano*, an intention to eat the offering at the wrong time (which generates *pigul* and incurs *karet*), or *chutz limkomo*, an intention to eat in the wrong place (which invalidates without *karet*) — then the offering is *pasul* and there is no *karet*. It does not become *pigul* because the offering is not yet valid at the time the thought is entertained: having *levonah* on it still, it is *pasul*, and an improper intention cannot generate *pigul* in an already-disqualified offering.

But if one removes the *levonah* first, and only thereafter entertains an improper *machshava*: *chutz limkomo* renders it *pasul* without *karet*, and *chutz lizmano* renders it *pigul* with liability for *karet*. So after removal the offering functions normally. This is clear. The question is what happens to the *mincha* once the *levonah* is removed, given that it was *pasul* while the *levonah* was on it — has it not been *nidcheh*, pushed aside, and should it not remain disqualified even after the *levonah* is removed?

### Tosafot Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם מִשֶּׁלִּיקֵּט לְבוֹנָתָהּ, מְחַשֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if after he gathered its frankincense** the priest **has intent** to sacrifice **its** handful or to eat its remainder **outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet*.** But if he had intent to sacrifice its handful or to eat its remainder **beyond its** designated **time,** it is ***piggul*, and** one who eats it **is liable** to receive ***karet* for it.** This *baraita* indicates that until the frankincense is removed from the meal offering, the meal offering is disqualified. This proves that the reason a meal offering upon which frankincense has been placed is valid is due to the ability to remove the frankincense, and not because it is not absorbed. This resolves Rabba bar Rav Huna's dilemma: If ground frankincense is placed on the meal offering of a sinner, the meal offering is disqualified.

*Tosafot* raises a striking textual observation at this point. The version of the *Gemara* as we have it reads *v'tehevei pach* — "let it be like a vessel" — followed by the question *v'amai pasla b'machshava*, "and why does the *machshava* invalidate?" *Tosafot* argues, however, that the original *girsa* of the *Gemara* was simply *v'tehevei dichuy* — "let it be *dichuy*" — meaning, how does something pushed aside become valid again? Subsequently, a commentator wrote in the margin, as a *peirush*, the question of why the *machshava* has any effect at all on an already-disqualified offering. The word *peirush*, abbreviated *pei yud* with a slash, was later mistaken by a copyist for the word *pach*, and the entire interpolation was incorporated into the body of the text as though it were the *Gemara*'s own language. *Tosafot* reconstructs this textual history in detail — it is a remarkably academic treatment of how a variant *girsa* came to be.

Setting the textual problem aside, then, the core question according to *Tosafot*'s reading is simply *v'tehevei dichuy*: once the *mincha* was *pasul* by virtue of the *levonah*, why does it not remain permanently pushed aside?

### Abaye: Retains Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״חַטָּאת״ קַרְיַיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** Even though the meal offering is disqualified while there is frankincense on it, nevertheless **the Merciful One calls it "a sin offering"** (Leviticus 5:11) even in that state, and therefore the improper intent has an effect with regard to it.

Abaye answers: *chatas karei rachmana* — the Torah calls this offering a *chatat*. The very *pasuk* that teaches the principle by which *levonah* does not permanently invalidate (*ki chata't hi* — "for it is a *chatat*") is simultaneously declaring that this offering retains its status as a *chatat* even during the period of its temporary disqualification. Because the Torah insists on calling it a *chatat*, it is telling us that it is never truly *nidcheh* — never genuinely cast off.

### Rava: Chananya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי? חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי הוּא, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ דְּחוּיִין. דְּתַנְיָא, חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ דָּם בַּכּוֹס, מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַגְרָלָה וּמְזַוֵּוג לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** there is a different explanation: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? It is in accordance with the opinion of **Ḥanan the Egyptian, who does not** subscribe to the *halakha* of **rejection,** but holds that a sacrifice that was rejected temporarily is not rejected entirely. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Ḥanan the Egyptian says:** In the event that the scapegoat of Yom Kippur was lost, **even** if the **blood** of its counterpart that is sacrificed to God has already been slaughtered and its blood has been collected **in the cup,** the blood is not rejected, but rather **one brings** another scapegoat as **its counterpart and pairs it with** the goat that has already been slaughtered, and the blood is sprinkled.

Rava offers a different answer: *ha mani Chananya HaMitzri hi* — this ruling follows the position of Chananya HaMitzri, *d'leis lei dichuy*, who does not hold that *dichuy* is operative at all. As a *baraita* records, Chananya HaMitzri rules that in the case of Yom Kippur, if the *se'ir hamishtaleach* dies, one may bring another goat to serve as the *se'ir hamishtaleach* and partner it with the blood of the *se'ir* already being processed — even though that blood was, for a time, without a corresponding *se'ir hamishtaleach* and therefore temporarily set aside. According to Chananya HaMitzri, this poses no problem, because he does not subscribe to the principle of *dichuy*. The ruling about the *levonah* and the *mincha* would follow that same position.

### Rav Ashi: In Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ – לָא הָוֵי דָּחוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that there is a different answer: **Any** matter **that is within one's power** to remedy **is not** deemed **rejected.**

In this case, since one can gather up the frankincense from the meal offering it is not rejected as an offering, and therefore the priest's improper intention is significant.

Rav Ashi proposes yet a third answer, one that avoids relying on either a special *pasuk* or the minority view of Chananya HaMitzri: *kol she-beyado lo havei dichuy* — anything that is entirely within one's power to fix is never considered *dichuy* in the first place. The concept of *dichuy* — being pushed aside and losing eligibility — applies when a disqualification is genuinely and irremediably imposed. But when the person himself can remedy the situation at will, the disqualification is never experienced as a true *dichuy*. The offering was always in a state of being temporarily obstructed by something that could be undone in moments. Such a situation is categorically different from a genuine *dichuy*.

### Rav Ada Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא: כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּמַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאִית לֵיהּ דְּחוּיִין? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דִּתְנַן: וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נִשְׁפַּךְ הַדָּם – יָמוּת הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, מֵת הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ – יִשָּׁפֵךְ הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda says: It stands to reason** that the correct explanation is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ashi, as whom did you hear who accepts** the principle of **rejected** offerings? **It is Rabbi Yehuda, as we learned** in a mishna (*Yoma* 62a): **And Rabbi Yehuda also says:** If **the blood** of the goat sacrificed to God was **spilled** before it was sprinkled, **the scapegoat,** its counterpart, is left **to die.** Similarly, **if the scapegoat dies, the blood** of the goat sacrificed to God **should be spilled.** In either case, two other goats must be brought and lots drawn again. This indicates that according to Rabbi Yehuda, when one of the goats dies, the remaining one is rejected.

Rav Ada endorses Rav Ashi's principle, and notes that it carries an important implication: *dichuy* as a concept belongs to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that once a sacrificial object has been set aside it cannot recover. Yet even Rabbi Yehuda, it emerges, recognizes that *beyado* — being in one's power to fix — is an exception.

### Rabbi Yehuda Dichuy

Rabbi Yehuda's position on *dichuy* is illustrated from the *mishnah* concerning the Yom Kippur goats: *nishpach ha-dam yamut hamishtaleach* — if the blood of the *se'ir* offered to God is spilled, the *se'ir hamishtaleach* must be left to die, because the blood that was designated for sprinkling can no longer be used and thus the *se'ir hamishtaleach* has no corresponding blood. Conversely, *meit hamishtaleach yishafech ha-dam* — if the *se'ir hamishtaleach* dies, the blood must be spilled, because the blood was temporarily rendered unusable while no corresponding goat existed. Rabbi Yehuda holds that this temporary unusability constitutes a genuine *dichuy* that cannot be overcome.

### Pesach Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ הֵיכָא דִּבְיָדוֹ, תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כּוֹס הָיָה מְמַלֵּא מִדַּם הַתַּעֲרוֹבוֹת, וְזוֹרְקוֹ זְרִיקָה אַחַת כְּנֶגֶד הַיְסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **And yet** concerning a case **where it is in one's power** to remedy the situation, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** After the Paschal offerings were slaughtered in the courtyard, before the floor was rinsed a priest **would fill a cup with the blood** of the many offerings brought that day **that was** now **mixed** together on the floor, and **sprinkle it with a single sprinkling against the base** of the altar, i.e., against its north and west sides, where there was a base, as is required for the Paschal offering. This was done in case the blood of one of the offerings had been spilled. The offering would be rendered valid, as some of its blood was now sprinkled on the altar. This indicates that even if the blood of an offering was spilled onto the floor, since a priest has the power to remedy this problem the offering is not rejected.

Yet even Rabbi Yehuda acknowledges the *beyado* exception. A *baraita* records that Rabbi Yehuda rules: at the conclusion of the Pesach sacrifice service, a vessel would be filled with blood from the mixture on the floor of the *Beit HaMikdash* — blood from many different *korbanot Pesach* that had accumulated — and one final *zerika* would be performed, throwing this collected blood toward the base of the *mizbe'ach*. The rationale is that some individual's blood may have accidentally spilled before being properly thrown, and by gathering all the blood together and throwing it again, one ensures that every *korban Pesach* has had its blood applied. Although that spilled blood was for a time not in a *kli shareit* and could not be brought directly to the *mizbe'ach*, the ability to gather it and re-throw it meant it was always *beyado* — within one's power to rectify — and Rabbi Yehuda accordingly does not treat it as *nidcheh*.

This distinction clarifies the Yom Kippur case as well. Going to procure an entirely new goat may technically be *beyado* in the sense that it is humanly possible, but it involves a more complex and extended process — selecting a new animal, performing the lottery, and the like — and is not equivalent to the simple act of picking *levonah* off a surface or gathering spilled blood from the Temple floor. Not every *beyado* is equivalent; the immediacy and simplicity of the fix determines whether the *dichuy* is considered never to have taken hold.

## Shiur of Oil/Frankincense

### Oil Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נָתָן מַשֶּׁהוּ שֶׁמֶן עַל גַּבֵּי כְּזַיִת מִנְחָה – פָּסַל. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״לֹא יָשִׂים״ – שִׂימָה כֹּל דְּהוּ, ״עָלֶיהָ״ – עַד דְּאִיכָּא שִׁיעוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the placement of oil upon the meal offering of a sinner or the meal offering of jealousy brought by a *sota*. **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **placed any amount** of **oil on top of an olive-bulk** of **a meal offering** of a sinner, he has thereby **disqualified** the meal offering. The Gemara inquires: **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? The verse states: **"He shall place** no oil upon it" (Leviticus 5:11). This indicates an act of **placing** that has no minimum amount, and therefore one is liable for **any** amount of oil. When the verse states **"upon it,"** this teaches that a meal offering is disqualified by the oil **only if it has** the requisite **measure** of an olive-bulk.

Having resolved the theoretical problem of *dichuy*, the *Gemara* turns to the question of the *shiur* — the minimum quantity required to constitute a violation. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *nasan mashehu shemen al gabei kezayit mincha pasul* — if one places even the smallest amount (*mashehu*) of oil upon a *kezayit*'s worth of *mincha*, it is *pasul* and one has transgressed. The reasoning: the verse uses the word *yasim* — "shall place." The act of placing carries no inherent minimum; even the most minimal act of placement satisfies the description. Hence even a *mashehu* of oil suffices for the prohibition. As for the *mincha* itself needing to be a *kezayit*: the word *aleha* — "upon it" — implies that there must be something of sufficient substance upon which the oil is placed, and the minimum measure of substance is a *kezayit*.

### Frankincense Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נָתָן כְּזַיִת לְבוֹנָה עַל גַּבֵּי מַשֶּׁהוּ מִנְחָה – פָּסַל. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״לֹא יִתֵּן״ כְּתִיב, עַד דְּאִיכָּא נְתִינָה. ״עָלֶיהָ״ –
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **placed an olive-bulk of frankincense on top of any amount** of **a meal offering,** he has thereby **disqualified** the meal offering. **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? **It is written: "Neither shall he give** any frankincense upon it" (Leviticus 5:11), which indicates that frankincense disqualifies the meal offering **only if there is** at least an amount that constitutes **giving,** which is an olive-bulk. When the verse states **"upon it,"**

For *levonah* the shiurim are reversed. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *nasan kezayit levonah al gabei mashehu mincha pasul* — if one places a *kezayit*'s worth of *levonah* upon even the smallest amount of *mincha*, one has transgressed. Here the verb is *yiten* — "shall give." Giving implies transferring something of substance, something that registers as a genuine gift. For something to constitute *netinah*, it must be a significant quantity, which the tradition understands as a *kezayit*. Conversely, the *mincha* itself need only be a *mashehu*.

### Language Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי רִבּוּי אַחַר רִבּוּי, וְאֵין רִבּוּי אַחַר רִבּוּי אֶלָּא לְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** this **is one amplificatory expression after another,** one in the context of the oil and the other in the context of the frankincense. And there is a principle that **one amplificatory expression after another** serves **only to restrict.** Consequently, the placement of frankincense on any amount of a meal offering of a sinner disqualifies the meal offering.

The linguistic distinction between *yasim* (place) and *yiten* (give) thus drives two different *shiurim* for the respective ingredients. The act of placing can be performed with any amount whatsoever; the act of giving requires something of substance. The *kezayit* as the measure for *netinah* is derived, as *Rashi* notes, from the *parasha* of *teruma* eaten in error (*kodesh b'shigaga*), where the verse says *venasan la-kohen et ha-kodesh* — he gives the *kohen* the sanctified food — and the context requires that what is given be an edible quantity, understood as a *kezayit*.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question from this derivation: if *netinah* requires a *kezayit*, why does the *mitzvah* of separating *teruma* — where the verse similarly says *titen lo* — not require a *kezayit*? A single grain of wheat suffices to exempt an entire heap of grain from *tevel* status. *Tosafot* does not resolve this difficulty. One approach, though not adopted by *Tosafot*, would be to distinguish two separate dimensions of the *mitzvah* of *teruma*: the act of exempting the grain from *tevel* (which requires only the most minimal separation) and the distinct obligation to give something substantive to the *kohen* (which may indeed require a *kezayit*). *Tosafot* treats these as one unified *mitzvah* and leaves the question open.

### Double Language

How does one learn that the *mincha* need only be a *mashehu* when the *levonah* is a *kezayit*? After all, the verse also says *aleha* — "upon it" — in the context of *levonah*, and we used *aleha* to require a *kezayit* of *mincha* for oil. The answer, on 60a, is *ribui achar ribui*: the verse uses *aleha* in connection with both the oil prohibition and the *levonah* prohibition — a double insistence on the same term. The principle *ein ribui achar ribui ela lema'et* — a repeated amplification is actually a contraction — means that this double usage diminishes the requirement rather than reinforcing it. Hence when it comes to *levonah*, even a *mashehu* of *mincha* suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף: בָּעֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נָתַן מַשֶּׁהוּ שֶׁמֶן עַל גַּבֵּי כְּזַיִת מִנְחָה, מַהוּ? מִי בָּעֵינַן שִׂימָה כִּנְתִינָה אוֹ לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** there is a different version of the discussion: **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a dilemma:** If one **placed oil** of **any amount on top of an olive-bulk** of **a meal offering** of a sinner, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara clarifies: Since the Torah states with regard to oil: "He shall place no oil upon it," whereas with regard to frankincense it states: "Neither shall he give any frankincense upon it," **do we require** that the **placing** of the oil must be **like** the **giving** of the frankincense, which must be an olive-bulk, **or not?** No answer was found, and therefore the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

A further question arises — whether *shima* (placing) should be understood through the lens of *netinah* (giving), such that even the oil prohibition would require a *kezayit*. Rabbi Yochanan himself, in one version of the tradition, posed this as an open question: *mi bayinan sima k'nesina* — do we read *yasim* as equivalent to *yiten* and thus require a *kezayit* of oil as well? The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Oil After Frankincense

### Two Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן שֶׁמֶן עַל שְׁיָרֶיהָ, תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יָשִׂים עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא יִתֵּן״, יָכוֹל בִּשְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one **placed oil on the remainder of** a meal offering of a sinner or a meal offering of jealousy brought by a *sota*, he does not violate a prohibition. Concerning this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"He shall place no oil upon it, neither shall he give** any frankincense upon it," one **might** have thought that this separation into two distinct prohibitions applies only **where two priests** perform these actions, one of whom places oil on the meal offering while the other puts frankincense. Perhaps in this case each of them is separately liable to receive lashes, but if one priest put both oil and frankincense on a meal offering, one might have thought he receives only one set of lashes.

The *Gemara* now takes up whether both prohibitions — oil and *levonah* — can apply to the same *mincha*. A *baraita* states: *lo yasim aleha shemen v'lo yiten* — do not place oil upon it and do not give *levonah* upon it. The *baraita* thus groups the two prohibitions together in reference to a single *mincha*.

### Transgress After Invalid…

The question posed is: once oil has been placed and has thereby invalidated the *mincha*, does a subsequent application of *levonah* constitute a separate transgression? One might think not — since the offering is already *pasul*, any further act performed upon it cannot constitute a meaningful violation. Indeed, we have seen that one does not transgress the prohibition of making the *mincha chametz* (*lo te'afeh chametz*) if the *mincha* is already *pasul*, because there is nothing left to ruin. Perhaps the same logic applies here.

On the other hand, one might argue that oil and *levonah* are linked by the Torah in the same verse and the same prohibition, and that the act of adding *levonah* to an already-oiled *mincha* is still a transgression of the *levonah* prohibition — just as making an already-leavened *mincha chametz* again (*mechametz achar mechametz*) may still constitute a transgression.

### Tosafot Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עָלֶיהָ״, בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל מִנְחָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, וְלֹא בְּכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "Upon it,"** with regard to both the oil and the frankincense, which indicates that **the verse is speaking of** the **meal offering itself, and** it is **not** referring **to** the **priest** who performs the service. Since both prohibited actions can be performed on the same meal offering, an individual who does both is liable to receive two sets of lashes.

*Tosafot* offers two readings of the *baraita*'s question. According to the first reading, the phrase *yachol bi-shnei kohanim* — "perhaps [this prohibition requires] two *kohanim*" — means in practice two separate *menachot*, one for each prohibition. The suggestion would be that oil and *levonah* can only each be violated on their own independent *mincha*, not on the same one. The verse responds *aleha* — "upon it," the same *mincha* — to teach that one may transgress both prohibitions by acting upon a single *mincha*. Under this reading, placing *levonah* after oil on the same *mincha* does indeed constitute an additional transgression.

*Tosafot* notes a second reading in which the conclusion runs in the opposite direction: *aleha* limits each prohibition to a *mincha* in proper individual status, implying that once the *mincha* has been invalidated by oil, the subsequent addition of *levonah* does not generate an additional violation. On this reading, each prohibition operates independently on a valid *mincha* but cannot compound upon an already-disqualified one.

### Vessel on Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יִתֵּן כְּלִי עַל גַּבֵּי כְּלִי, וְאִם נָתַן – פָּסַל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עָלֶיהָ״, בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל מִנְחָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, one **might** have thought this verse means **one may not** even **place a vessel** containing oil or frankincense **on top of a vessel** that contains the meal offering, **and** that **if one placed** such a vessel on the meal offering **he** has thereby **disqualified** it. Therefore, **the verse states "upon it,"** which indicates that **the verse is speaking about** the **meal offering itself.** One may not place oil or frankincense on the meal offering itself, but it is not prohibited to place a vessel containing oil or frankincense upon a vessel that contains the meal offering.

The *baraita* then addresses the vessel-on-vessel case: *yachol lo yiten kli agabei kli* — perhaps one may not place a vessel of oil atop the *mincha*. The verse responds *aleha* — the prohibition requires placing the oil directly *upon* the *mincha* itself, not merely positioning a vessel containing oil above it. Accordingly, placing oil in a vessel and resting that vessel on top of the *mincha* neither invalidates the offering nor constitutes a transgression.

## Hagasha without Tenufah

### Mishnah Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יֵשׁ טְעוּנוֹת הַגָּשָׁה, וְאֵין טְעוּנוֹת תְּנוּפָה. הַגָּשָׁה וּתְנוּפָה. תְּנוּפָה וְלֹא הַגָּשָׁה. לֹא תְּנוּפָה וְלֹא הַגָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There are** four categories of meal offerings: Those that **require bringing near,** a rite that requires the priests to carry the offering in their hands and bring it near the southwest corner of the altar, **but do not require waving;** those that require both **bringing near and waving;** those that require **waving but not bringing near;** and those that require **neither waving nor bringing near.**

The *mishnah* now turns from the ingredients of *menachot* to their procedural requirements, specifically the twin rituals of *hagasha* and *tenufah*. *Hagasha* — touching the *mincha* to the corner of the *mizbe'ach* — and *tenufah* — waving — are not universal requirements; the *mishnah* classifies *menachot* into four categories: those requiring *hagasha* but not *tenufah*, those requiring both, those requiring *tenufah* but not *hagasha*, and those requiring neither.

### Ten Offerings Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ טְעוּנוֹת הַגָּשָׁה וְאֵין טְעוּנוֹת תְּנוּפָה: מִנְחַת הַסּוֹלֶת, וְהַמַּחֲבַת, וּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, וְהַחַלּוֹת, וְהָרְקִיקִין, מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, מִנְחַת גּוֹיִם, מִנְחַת נָשִׁים, וּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: **And these** are the meal offerings that **require bringing near but do not require waving: The fine-flour meal offering; the** meal offering prepared in **a pan; the** meal offering prepared in **a deep pan; the** meal offering baked in an oven, which can be brought in the form of **loaves or** in the form of **wafers; the meal offering of priests; the meal offering of the anointed priest; the meal offering of gentiles; a meal offering** brought **by women; and the meal offering of a sinner.**

The *mishnah* begins with the category requiring *hagasha* alone. Just as the previous *mishnah* listed ten *menachot* with respect to oil and *levonah*, here too the list numbers ten. The ten are: *minchat ha-solet* (plain flour and oil), *hamachavat* (the pan-fried), *hamarcheshet* (the deep-fried), *ha-chalot* and *ha-rekikin* (the two types of *ma'afeh tanur* — oven-baked bread-loaves and wafers), *minchat kohanim*, *minchat kohen mashiach* (the *kohen gadol*'s daily offering, half in the morning and half in the evening), *minchat nochrim* (the meal offering of non-Jews), *minchat nashim* (the meal offering of women), and *minchat chotei* (the sinner's meal offering). All of these require *hagasha* and do not require *tenufah*.

One might note the contrast between this list and the prior one. When discussing oil and *levonah*, the standard template — oil and *levonah* together — was the starting point, and the ten were listed as requiring both. Here, the standard is *hagasha* alone, because *tenufah* is the exceptional requirement applicable only to a subset of *menachot*. The list begins with the majority case. The underlying logic governing the inclusion in this list is that any *mincha* of which a portion is burned on the *mizbe'ach* — either the entire offering (as with *minchat kohanim*) or the *kometz* (as with the rest) — and which comes on its own account (thus excluding *minchat nesachim*, which accompanies an animal sacrifice) receives *hagasha*.

The notable absence from this list, compared to the oil-and-*levonah* list, is the *minchat omer* — but this is because the *minchat omer* belongs in the next category, requiring both *hagasha* and *tenufah*. Its exclusion here does not reflect a lesser status but simply places it in the correct classification.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ אֵין בָּהֶן הַגָּשָׁה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן קְמִיצָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן קְמִיצָה אֵין בָּהֶן הַגָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: The meal offering of priests and the meal offering of the anointed priest do not require bringing** of the meal offering **near** to the altar, **due to** the fact **that there is no removal of a handful in their** sacrifice, **and** there is a principle that with regard to **any** meal offering **where there is no removal of a handful in their** sacrifice, **there is** also **no bringing near.**

Rabbi Shimon disputes the inclusion of *minchat kohanim* and *minchat kohen mashiach*. His position: *minchat kohanim u-minchat kohen mashiach ein bahem hagasha* — these do not receive *hagasha*. His principle is: *v'chol she-ein bahem kmitza ein bahem hagasha* — any *mincha* that does not have a *kemitza* does not receive *hagasha*. Since the entirety of the *minchat kohanim* is burned and no portion is taken as a *kometz* that is burned separately while the rest is consumed, Rabbi Shimon excludes it from the *hagasha* requirement. For Rabbi Shimon, the operative criterion for *hagasha* is specifically the *kemitza* — the act in which the *kohen* takes the handful that goes on the *mizbe'ach* while the remainder goes to the *kohanim* to eat. Where no such *kemitza* occurs, there is no *hagasha*.

The *Tanna Kamma*, by contrast, requires only that some portion of the *mincha* be burned on the *mizbe'ach* — whether that portion was taken via *kemitza* or whether the entire offering is burned.

### Rav Papa Ten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כֹּל הֵיכָא דִּתְנַן עֶשֶׂר – תְּנַן. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Pappa stated** a principle with regard to all the *mishnayot* in tractate *Menaḥot*: **Anywhere that we learned** in a mishna that one brings a meal offering, **we learned** that one must bring **ten** items of the same type, either loaves or wafers. The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav Pappa **teaching us?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין יָבִיא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This statement of Rav Pappa serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, who says:** One who takes a vow to bring a meal offering baked in an oven must bring ten items. If he wishes he may bring ten loaves or ten wafers, and if he wishes **he may bring half** of them as **loaves and** the other **half** as **wafers.** Rav Pappa **teaches us** that the *tanna* of the mishna maintains **that** one may **not** do so; all ten must be of the same type.

Rav Papa observes — as he observed yesterday in connection with the oil-and-*levonah* list — that whenever the *mishnah* uses a list of ten *menachot*, the precise number is deliberate: *kol heicha d'tnan esser tnan*. The question is what exactly the count of ten is excluding. Here, Rav Papa explains, it excludes Rabbi Shimon's view that one may bring a *ma'afeh tanur* consisting of five *chalot* and five *rekikin* combined in a single offering. According to Rabbi Shimon this combination would constitute an additional type, bringing the count to eleven. By listing exactly ten, the *mishnah* implicitly rejects this position.

## Hagasha Derivation

### Bringing the Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״וְהֵבֵאתָ אֲשֶׁר יֵעָשֶׂה מֵאֵלֶּה לַה׳ וְהִקְרִיבָהּ אֶל הַכֹּהֵן וְהִגִּישָׁהּ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: אֵין לִי שֶׁטָּעוּן הַגָּשָׁה אֶלָּא קוֹמֶץ בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon disagree about whether the meal offering of priests and the meal offering of the anointed priest require the ritual of bringing near. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: A verse discussing the meal offering prepared in the pan states: "And you shall bring the meal offering that is made of these to the Lord, and it shall be drawn near to the priest, and he shall bring it near to the altar" (Leviticus 2:8). **If** the verse **had stated** only: **And you shall bring that which is made of these to the Lord and it shall be drawn near to the priest and he shall bring it near** to the altar, omitting the words: The meal offering, **I would say: I have** derived **only that** the **handful** that is sacrificed on the altar **alone requires bringing near.**

The *Gemara* now asks: *mena hanei milei* — from where in the Torah is the *hagasha* requirement derived? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*. The starting point is the verse at the conclusion of the *parasha* of *menachot* in *Vayikra*: *v'heiveita et ha-mincha asher ye'aseh me'eileh la-Hashem v'hikrivah el ha-kohen v'higishah el ha-mizbe'ach* — and you shall bring the *mincha* that shall be made from any of these to God, and bring it to the *kohen*, and he shall touch it to the *mizbe'ach*.

The *baraita* reasons: had the Torah said only *v'heiveita asher ye'aseh me'eileh* — you shall bring that which shall be made from these — without explicitly saying *mincha*, one might have thought that *hagasha* applies only to the *kometz* itself, since only the *kometz* is the portion going directly to God (*me'eileh l'Hashem*). Everything else is eaten by the *kohanim* or by the donor.

### Bring and Touch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחָה, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מִנְחָה״. מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶת הַמִּנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that this *halakha* applies to the entire **meal offering? The verse states** the superfluous term: "The **meal offering,"** which indicates that one must bring the entire meal offering to the altar prior to the removal of the handful. The *baraita* further asks: **From where** is it derived that this *halakha* applies to **the meal offering of a sinner? The verse states: "The meal offering."** The addition of the definite article serves to include the meal offering of a sinner.

The Torah therefore writes *et ha-mincha v'higishah el ha-mizbe'ach* — the *mincha* as a whole shall be touched to the *mizbe'ach*. This teaches that *hagasha* applies to the entire *mincha*, not merely the *kometz*. The phrase *asher ye'aseh me'eileh* encompasses all the varieties of *mincha* enumerated in the preceding passage, and *v'higishah* applies to all of them.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: נֶאֱמַר הָבֵא
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* raises a difficulty: Why is a verse necessary to teach that the requirement of bringing near applies to the meal offering of a sinner? **But** this *halakha* **is** capable of being derived by **logical inference.** The *baraita* elaborates: The procedure of bringing a meal offering **is stated here,** in the context of the meal offering of a sinner,

The *baraita* then asks: *minchat chotei minayin* — from where do we know that the *minchat chotei* also requires *hagasha*? At this point the *baraita* invokes *kal va-homer* and analogy. The verse *et ha-mincha* with its additional *et* serves as textual support for inclusion, but the *baraita* first considers whether a logical derivation alone would suffice.

The logical comparison runs as follows. On the one hand, there is the *minchat nedava* — the free-will *mincha* — which we already know requires *hagasha*. On the other hand, there is the *minchat chova* — the obligatory *mincha*, including the *minchat chotei*. One might reason by analogy: just as a *minchat nedava* requires *hagasha*, so too should a *minchat chova*.

But this analogy is vulnerable: *mah le-minchat nedava she-kein te'unah shemen u-levonah* — the *minchat nedava* requires oil and *levonah*, which is what makes it significant, and therefore perhaps only it requires *hagasha*, while the *minchat chotei*, which lacks oil and *levonah*, does not.

The *baraita* then attempts to prove the point from *minchat sotah*, which also lacks oil and *levonah* yet — as will be shown — requires *hagasha*. But this too is deflected: *mah le-minchat sotah she-kein te'unah tenufah* — the *minchat sotah* requires *tenufah*, which makes it distinctive, and perhaps only it requires *hagasha* despite lacking oil and *levonah*.

One then tries to restore the *minchat nedava* as a proof: the *minchat nedava* does not require *tenufah*, and it does require *hagasha*, so *tenufah* is not a prerequisite for *hagasha*. But then we still cannot prove the *minchat chotei* requires *hagasha*, because the *minchat nedava* has oil and *levonah*. *Lo re'i zeh k're'i zeh* — the distinguishing feature of each is absent from the other.

At this stage the *baraita* employs the *tzad ha-shaveh* — the common denominator. Both the *minchat nedava* and the *minchat sotah*, despite their differences, share one characteristic: *shavu bi-kmitza* — both have a *kemitza*. And both require *hagasha*. From this common denominator we derive: any *mincha* that has a *kemitza* should likewise require *hagasha* — including the *minchat chotei*, which also has a *kemitza*.

### Minchat Chotei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחַת חוֹבָה, וְנֶאֱמַר הָבֵא מִנְחַת נְדָבָה. מָה מִנְחַת נְדָבָה טְעוּנָה הַגָּשָׁה – אַף מִנְחַת חוֹבָה טְעוּנָה הַגָּשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** which is **an obligatory meal offering; and it is stated there,** with regard to the deep-pan meal offering, which is **a voluntary meal offering. Just as a voluntary meal offering requires bringing near, so too an obligatory meal offering,** such as the meal offering of a sinner, **requires bringing near.**

Nevertheless, the logical derivation remains vulnerable to challenge: *mah la-tzad ha-shaveh she-bahen she-kein huchsharu lavo b'ashir k'v'oni* — the common denominator of the *minchat nedava* and *minchat sotah* is that either a wealthy or a poor person may bring them. The *minchat chotei*, however, is exclusively the offering of a poor person — it is the meal-offering variant of the *korban oleh v'yored*, accessible only to those who cannot afford an animal *chatat*. This makes it categorically distinct. Perhaps, because it cannot be brought by everyone, it does not receive *hagasha*.

Therefore a scriptural derivation is needed: *talmud lomar et ha-mincha* — the additional *et* in the verse comes to include even the *minchat chotei*. The logical derivation was compelling but not conclusive; the verse provides the necessary textual anchor, while the logical reasoning informs how we apply the verse.

### Rabbi Shimon Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְמִנְחַת נְדָבָה, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה? מִנְחַת סוֹטָה תּוֹכִיחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this inference: **What** is notable about **a voluntary meal offering?** It is notable in that **it requires oil and frankincense** upon it, rendering its *halakha* more stringent than that of the meal offering of a sinner, which does not include oil and frankincense. Therefore, it would be logical to suggest that a voluntary meal offering requires bringing near but the meal offering of a sinner does not. The *baraita* responds: **The meal offering** brought by **a *sota*,** i.e., the meal offering of jealousy, **can prove** that this factor is not decisive, as it does not include oil and frankincense either, and yet it requires bringing near.

Rabbi Shimon derives the same conclusion by different means. He uses the word *v'heiveita* — "you shall bring" — to include the *minchat omer* in the *hagasha* requirement, drawing the verbal link to the verse *v'heivetem et omer reishit ktzirchem el ha-kohen* — "you shall bring the *omer* of the first of your harvest to the *kohen*." The shared root *heivi* connects the general *mincha* verse to the *omer* specifically. For Rabbi Shimon, the word *v'hikrivah* — "and bring it" — in the same verse is used to include the *minchat sotah*, linked to the verse about the *sotah*: *v'hikriv otah el ha-mizbe'ach* — "and he shall bring it to the *mizbe'ach*."

### Minchat Sotah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְמִנְחַת סוֹטָה שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנָה תְּנוּפָה? מִנְחַת נְדָבָה תּוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this claim: One cannot derive the *halakha* of the meal offering of a sinner from the meal offering brought by a *sota*, as there is another requirement that applies to the meal offering brought by a *sota* but not to the meal offering of a sinner: **What** is notable **about the meal offering** brought by **a *sota*?** It is notable **in that it requires waving,** whereas the meal offering of a sinner does not. The *baraita* responds: **The voluntary meal offering can prove** that waving is not the decisive factor, as a voluntary meal offering does not require waving and yet it requires bringing near.

The *minchat sotah* presents a particular challenge for logical derivation, since it is in several respects the least distinguished of all *menachot*: it is made from *kemach* (coarse barley flour) rather than *solet* (fine wheat flour), it has no oil, and it has no *levonah*. A logical argument might run: if even the *minchat chotei* — which has at least *solet* — requires *hagasha*, certainly the *minchat sotah* should require it, since it also has *tenufah*. But this reasoning is challenged: the *minchat chotei* uses wheat; the *minchat sotah* uses barley. And conversely: if the *minchat omer*, which uses barley, requires *hagasha*, then so should the *minchat sotah*; but the *minchat omer* has oil and *levonah* while the *minchat sotah* does not.

### All with Kemitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁשָּׁווּ לִקְמִיצָה, וְשָׁווּ לְהַגָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the inference has reverted** to its starting point, as **the aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case; **their common element** is **that** both the *omer* meal offering and the meal offering of a sinner **are equal with regard to** the requirement of the **removal of a handful, and they are equal with regard to** the requirement of **bringing near.** I will also bring** the additional case of **the *omer* meal offering, which is equal to them with regard to** the requirement of the **removal of a handful,** and conclude that **it should** likewise **be equal to them with regard to** the requirement of **bringing near.**

Again the *tzad ha-shaveh* resolves it: the *minchat chotei* and the *minchat omer*, despite their differences, share the characteristic of *kemitza*, and both require *hagasha*. From this common denominator: any *mincha* with *kemitza* gets *hagasha*, and since the *minchat sotah* also has *kemitza*, it too gets *hagasha*. A scriptural hook — *v'hikrivah* — is still needed, since the logical derivation can be challenged by the *minchat sotah*'s distinctive use of coarse barley flour. But the underlying principle that emerges from the entire *baraita* is clear: *kemitza* is the criterion. Wherever there is *kemitza*, there is *hagasha* — and this is precisely the position of Rabbi Shimon, who explicitly states *v'chol she-ein bahem kmitza ein bahem hagasha*. The *baraita*'s formulation of the common denominator as *shavu bi-kmitza* reveals that the entire derivation proceeds along Rabbi Shimon's conceptual lines.
