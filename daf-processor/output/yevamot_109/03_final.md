# Yevamot 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'un After Get

### Mishnah Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: גֵּט אַחַר מֵיאוּן — אֲסוּרָה לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, מֵיאוּן אַחַר גֵּט — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle** concerning a minor girl who refused her husband and then married several times: **If the bill of divorce followed the refusal** and she remarried, **she is forbidden to return to him. If the refusal followed the bill of divorce, she is permitted to return to him.** Since the refusal followed the bill of divorce it is clear that she was a minor and neither the marriage nor the divorce were valid by Torah law. However, when the ultimate separation is by means of a bill of divorce, there is no indication that she was a minor at the time and there is potential for confusion with an adult divorcée.

The daf opens in the middle of 108b, continuing the discussion of *me'un* following a *get*. The broader question is whether a *me'un* performed after a *get* redefines the woman's status — specifically, whether it undoes her classification as a *gerushah* and thereby permits what would otherwise be a violation of *machzir grushato*. The *mishnah* has already established that if the same man who gave the *get* subsequently remarried her and she then performed *me'un* against him, that *me'un* does redefine her status. But if a different man married her and she performed *me'un* against him, that does not redefine her status vis-à-vis the first husband.

The *gemara* had explained this distinction not on the intuitive ground that only the same man can redefine her status, but rather on a concern that the first husband will attempt to signal her — exploiting her recognition of his gestures — and thereby manipulate her into performing *me'un* with the second husband so she can return to him. With that resolved, the *gemara* moves to a new internal difficulty in the *mishnah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְמָאֶנֶת בָּאִישׁ וְנִשֵּׂאת לְאַחֵר וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, לְאַחֵר וּמֵיאֲנָה בּוֹ, לְאַחֵר וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה הֵימֶנּוּ בְּגֵט — אֲסוּרָה לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, בְּמֵיאוּן — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If a minor girl **refuses** one **man and marries another, and he divorces her,** and then she marries **another** man **and refuses him, and** then she marries **another** man **and he divorces her, this is the principle** for this case: With regard to **anyone she leaves by means of a bill of divorce, it is prohibited for her to return to him.** With regard to anyone she leaves **by means of refusal, she is permitted to return to him.**

The *gemara* notes: *ika hashta dechavrei adechavrei kashya* — even setting aside the first problem, there remains a contradiction within the *mishnah*'s own treatment of a second man's *me'un*. The *mishnah* first describes a case in which the man performed *me'un* against her, then remarried her, then gave her a *get*, and she subsequently married someone else. The *mishnah* states that if that second man died or divorced her, she is forbidden from returning to the first husband on account of *machzir grushato*. The precise formulation — specifying death or divorce — implies that had the second man's dissolution of the marriage taken the form of *me'un* rather than death or divorce, she *would* be permitted to return to the first. This *diyuk* suggests that a second man's *me'un* does, in fact, redefine her status even vis-à-vis the first husband.

### Conclusion Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא אָתֵי מֵיאוּן וּמְבַטֵּל גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It was taught in the mishna that if the man gave his minor wife a bill of divorce but subsequently remarried her and she refused him, and then she married someone else, she is permitted to remarry the first husband when her marriage to the second is concluded. **Apparently, refusal comes and nullifies a bill of divorce.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הַמְמָאֶנֶת בָּאִישׁ וְנִשֵּׂאת לְאַחֵר וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, לְאַחֵר וּמֵיאֲנָה בּוֹ, לְאַחֵר וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁיָּצְתָה הֵימֶנּוּ בְּגֵט — אֲסוּרָה לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, בְּמֵיאוּן — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ. אַלְמָא לָא אָתֵי מֵיאוּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ וּ[מְ]בַטֵּיל גִּיטָּא דִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the end of the mishna: If a minor girl **refuses** one **man and marries another, and he divorces her,** and then she marries **another** man **and refuses him, and** then she marries **another** man **and he divorces her, this is the principle:** With regard to **anyone she leaves by means of a bill of divorce, she is prohibited from returning to him.** With regard to anyone she leaves **by means of refusal, she is permitted to return to him. Apparently, a refusal of another man does not come and nullify one's** own **bill of divorce.** If the refusal completely nullified the marriage to the second husband, there would be no obstacle to her remarrying her first husband, as an ex-wife who did not marry another man is permitted to remarry her first husband. However, the divorce, combined with the second marriage, does generate a prohibition, and she is prohibited from remarrying in this case.

Yet the concluding rule of the *mishnah* states explicitly: *kol sheyatzat mimeno beget asura lachazor lo* — anyone from whom she departed with a *get* may not take her back, even if she subsequently performed *me'un* with another husband. And anyone from whom she departed with *me'un* may return to her. This is as clear a statement as possible that a *me'un* performed with a different man does *not* undo the *gerushah* status vis-à-vis the first. So the two halves of the *mishnah* appear to contradict one another directly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תַּבְרַהּ, מִי שֶׁשָּׁנָה זוֹ לֹא שָׁנָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** This mishna is **disjointed,** and **he who taught this** *halakha*, that she may remarry her first husband if she refused him after he divorced her, **did not teach that** *halakha*, that her refusal of another man does not render her permitted to her divorced husband.

Rabbi Elazar's response is stark: *miy sheshana zu lo shana zu* — the two halves of the *mishnah* were authored by different *tanna'im* and reflect a genuine dispute. The first half operates under the assumption that a second man's *me'un* can redefine her status even vis-à-vis the first husband; the second half denies this. Two distinct positions, two *tanna'im*, and no resolution between them.

It is worth pausing here to appreciate just how seriously the *gemara* entertains the position that a second man's *me'un* should work. The intuitive assumption — that only the same man can redefine the status, not an entirely different party — is not the *gemara*'s operating premise. The *gemara* is genuinely prepared to accept that even a *me'un* performed with a second husband could, in principle, strip away the *gerushah* status the first husband created. The dispute is not about whether this is theoretically possible, but about whether we permit it in practice.

One must also keep in mind throughout this entire discussion that everything here exists in the *derabbanan* world: the *kiddushin* is *derabbanan*, the *get* is *derabbanan*, and the *me'un* operates within that framework. It is precisely because all of this is rabbinic that the question of redefining statuses is even coherent. Were we dealing with *de'oraisa* marriage and divorce, there would be no mechanism for retroactive redefinition of this kind.

### Ulla's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? וְדִלְמָא: מֵיאוּן דִּידֵיהּ מְבַטֵּל גֵּט דִּידֵיהּ, מֵיאוּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ לָא מְבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵיאוּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ דְּלָא מְבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ — אַיְּידֵי דְּמַכֶּרֶת בִּרְמִיזוֹתָיו וּקְרִיצוֹתָיו אָזֵל מְשַׁבֵּשׁ וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ, מֵיאוּן דִידֵיהּ נָמֵי לָא לִיבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ — דְּאַיְּידֵי דְּמַכֶּרֶת בִּרְמִיזוֹתָיו וּקְרִיצוֹתָיו אָזֵיל מְשַׁבֵּשׁ וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the difficulty** here? **Perhaps** her **refusal of him nullifies his bill of divorce, while** her **refusal of the other man does not nullify** the original husband's **bill of divorce.** The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** her **refusal of the other man different, that it does not nullify his bill of divorce?** Isn't it that **because she is familiar with the intimations and gestures [*keritzotav*]** of her first husband, **he will lead her astray and bring her back** to him, by causing her to refuse her new husband and then return to him? Consequently, it was decreed that she may not return to her first husband by refusing the second. But for this same reason **the refusal against** the first husband **himself also should not nullify his** own **bill of divorce, as, since she is familiar with his intimations and gestures, he will lead her astray and bring her back** to him after she has married another man.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כְּבָר שַׁבְּשַׁהּ וְלָא אִישְׁתַּבַּשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **But he already tried to** lead **her astray and she was not** led **astray.** In other words, he already remarried her after the divorce and she still refused him, which proves that he does not have sufficient influence to lead her astray.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי קַשְׁיָא, דְּחַבְרֵיהּ אַדְּחַבְרֵיהּ קַשְׁיָא: מֵיאֲנָה בּוֹ וְהֶחְזִירָהּ, נָתַן לָהּ גֵּט וְנִשֵּׂאת לְאַחֵר, וְנִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה — אֲסוּרָה לַחְזוֹר לוֹ. טַעְמָא דְּנִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה. הָא מֵיאֲנָה — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, אַלְמָא אָתֵי מֵיאוּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ וּמְבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But if there is a difficulty, it is** the contradiction **between** one *halakha* involving **another man and** a different *halakha* involving **another man** that **is difficult,** as the mishna states: **If she refused him and he** subsequently **remarried her, and** this time **he gave her a bill of divorce and she married another** man, **and she was widowed or divorced, she is prohibited from returning to** her original husband. **The reason is** specifically **that she was widowed or divorced** by the other man. **But if she had refused** the second husband, **she would be permitted to return to** the first husband. **Apparently, a refusal of the other man** would have **come and nullified his bill of divorce,** permitting her to remarry the first husband, despite her erstwhile marriage to the other man.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תִּבְרַהּ, מִי שֶׁשָּׁנָה זוֹ לֹא שָׁנָה זוֹ. עוּלָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁשִּׁלְּשָׁה בְּגִיטִּין, דְּמִיחַזְּיָא כִּגְדוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** This mishna is **disjointed,** and he **who taught this** *halakha* **did not teach that** *halakha*. **Ulla said:** The last clause, in which it says her refusal does not nullify the bill of divorce, is referring to a case **where she was divorced three times. Since** she was divorced three times, **she appears to be an adult,** and therefore the Sages did not allow her refusal to cancel the effect of the divorce.

Ulla offers an alternative resolution that avoids splitting the *mishnah* between two disputants. He argues that both halves can reflect the same *tanna* — but the final case of the *mishnah*, in which a second man's *me'un* does not redefine her status, is a specific scenario involving *shilsha begetin*: she had gone through a sequence of marriages and received three *getin* over the course of her history. After three *getin*, even if subsequent husbands performed *me'un*, the cumulative pattern of divorce documents has established her *gerushah* status so firmly — at least vis-à-vis the husbands from whom she received those *getin* — that it cannot be overridden.

The logic, as Ulla explains, is that *demichzi kegdola*: after three *getin*, she looks like an adult. Despite remaining a *ketanah* throughout, the repeated formal divorces create the appearance of full adult status, and that appearance fixes her *gerushah* identity at least with respect to those specific husbands. As a result, in this particular case, no subsequent *me'un* — even from a different man — can dislodge the *gerushah* classification.

This is a striking resolution. The ordinary rule, according to Ulla, is that a second man's *me'un* can indeed redefine her status vis-à-vis the first husband. The final case of the *mishnah* is a narrow exception: when there have been three *getin*, the accumulated weight of formal divorce creates an irreversible *gerushah* status. She may still return to husbands from whom she departed through *me'un*, but she cannot return to those from whom she received *getin*, because their *gerushah* status has become fixed and is no longer amenable to redefinition through a later *me'un*.

The counterintuitive element here is worth highlighting. One would expect the rule to be that another man's *me'un* never works to redefine her status with the first husband — that seems self-evidently correct. Instead, the *gemara* reasons in the opposite direction, treating a second man's *me'un* as ordinarily effective, with the three-*getin* scenario as the exception.

### Historical Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵימֵינוּ בְּכֶסֶף שָׁתִינוּ עֵצֵינוּ בִּמְחִיר יָבֹאוּ״? בִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה נִתְבַּקְּשָׁה הֲלָכָה זוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁיָּצְאָה מֵרִאשׁוֹן בְּגֵט וּמִשֵּׁנִי בְּמֵיאוּן, מַהוּ שֶׁתַּחֲזוֹר לָרִאשׁוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to Rabbi Elazar, who holds that the mishna is disjointed, **who** is the *tanna* that **taught** that a minor may always remarry a husband she refused but not one who divorced her? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** This can be determined based on the following incident. **What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "We have drunk our water for money; our wood comes to us for a price"** (Lamentations 5:4), implying that Torah, which is analogous to water, can be purchased with money. The Gemara explains: **During the time of danger,** i.e., religious persecution by the Romans, **this halakhic ruling was requested: If she,** a minor, **left her first husband by means of a bill of divorce and her second by refusal, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to her returning to the first?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂכְרוּ אָדָם אֶחָד בְּאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז, וְשָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְאָסַר. אֶת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה בִּנְצִיבִין, וְאָסַר.
>
> **Translation:** Those involved **hired one person for four hundred dinars** for the dangerous mission **and asked Rabbi Akiva,** who was incarcerated **in prison** by the Romans for teaching Torah, **and** he ruled that it is **forbidden. They asked Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira in Netzivin,** in Babylonia, **and** he also deemed it **forbidden.**

The *gemara* now asks: according to the first answer, which treats the two halves of the *mishnah* as a *machlokes tanna'im*, where do we find this debate represented among the *tanna'im*? The answer comes through Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, introduced with a striking verse from *Eicha*: *meimenu bechesef shatinu ateinu bimchir yavo'u* — our water we had to purchase with silver, our wood we had to buy. The *gemara* applies this as a metaphor: just as water represents *Torah* and had to be acquired at great cost, so during the period of Hadrianic persecution, the following halachic question had to be pursued at enormous expense.

The question posed was: if a woman departed from the first husband with a *get* and from the second husband through *me'un*, may she return to the first? Scholars hired an intermediary for 400 *zuz* to smuggle the question into prison to Rabbi Akiva. He ruled: *ve'asar* — forbidden. A second man's *me'un* does not redefine her status. They then traveled to ask Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira in Nitzivin, who similarly ruled *ve'asar*.

### R. Yishmael Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לְזוֹ לֹא הוּצְרַכְנוּ. לְאִיסּוּר כָּרֵת הִתַּרְתָּ, לְאִיסּוּר לָאו — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: This** question was not what they asked, as it **was unnecessary: If you** rendered **permitted a prohibition** for whose violation one is liable to receive **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*],** i.e., if the prohibition against sexual intercourse with a married woman is dissolved by the refusal, as the marriage is nullified retroactively, then is it **not** clear **all the more so** that after a refusal, **the** regular **prohibition** against remarrying one's ex-wife after she was married to another should be permitted? The opinion in the mishna that refusal does not cancel the effect of divorce is in accordance with that of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, while the opinion that she is permitted to return to her first husband after refusing the second one is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרַם כָּךְ שָׁאֲלוּ: הֲרֵי הָיְתָה אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אִמּוֹ, שֶׁהִיא שְׁנִיָּיה לוֹ, וּנְשָׂאָהּ אָחִיו מֵאָבִיו וּמֵת, מַהוּ שֶׁתְּמָאֵן הַשְׁתָּא וְתִעְקְרִינְהוּ לְנִישּׂוּאִין קַמָּאֵי, וְתִתְיַיבֵּם (צָרָתַהּ). יֵשׁ מֵיאוּן לְאַחַר מִיתָה בִּמְקוֹם מִצְוָה, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, continued: **Rather, this** is what **they asked: If** the minor **was the wife of someone's mother's brother, a secondary** forbidden relative, i.e., a relative forbidden **to him** by rabbinic law, **and** afterward **his paternal brother married her and died,** so that she became eligible to him for levirate marriage, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the following: **May she refuse now and uproot the first marriage** to the mother's brother, so that she will no longer be a forbidden relative, and likewise her rival wife will not be the rival wife of a forbidden relative, **so that her rival wife may enter into levirate marriage?** In other words, **in a case** where there is **a mitzva** of levirate marriage, **is refusal after** the husband's **death valid or not?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂכְרוּ שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם בְּאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז, וּבָאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְאָסַר. אֶת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה בִּנְצִיבִין, וְאָסַר.
>
> **Translation:** Those involved **hired two people for four hundred dinars, and they came and asked Rabbi Akiva in prison and he** deemed it **prohibited.** They asked **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira in Netzivin and he** deemed it **prohibited.**

Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose, however, takes sharp issue with this account. He insists the question itself was obvious — the answer could not possibly be in doubt — and that the actual question brought to Rabbi Akiva in prison was an entirely different one. His reasoning: *issur kares hitarta* — if *me'un* has the power to permit what would otherwise be an *issur kares* — since a *ketanah* who is betrothed is considered halachically married and *me'un* dissolves that betrothal, allowing her to go to another man who would otherwise be having relations with a married woman — then certainly *me'un* should be powerful enough to remove the much lesser prohibition of *machzir grushato*, which is merely an *issur lav*. An *a fortiori* argument: if *me'un* can eliminate a *karet* prohibition, it certainly eliminates a *lav* prohibition. Therefore, Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose maintains that a second man's *me'un* obviously does redefine her status and permits her return to the first husband.

Rabbi Yishmael's objection is noted: the *me'un* dissolves the relationship with the man she is performing *me'un* against, not vis-à-vis a different man. There is a meaningful distinction between redefining her status with respect to husband number two versus redefining her *gerushah* status with respect to husband number one. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose maintains that the power of *me'un* is broad enough to accomplish this.

The actual question, Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose insists, involved a far more elaborate scenario: where a man's brother's wife — who was a *ketanah* and therefore an *issur derabbanan* to him as *eshet achi imo* — had been married by a different paternal half-brother (who was not related to her on the maternal side and could therefore marry her without violation), and that half-brother died. Could she perform *me'un* retroactively on her first marriage — even though the first husband had already died — in order to undo the prohibited relationship and enable *yibum* with the surviving brother? This would constitute *me'un le'achar mita bimkom mitzvah*: a posthumous *me'un* aimed at enabling the fulfillment of *yibum*. The *gemara* concludes that even in Rabbi Yishmael's framework, the answer here is *assur* — a posthumous *me'un* stretching back to an already-dissolved prior marriage goes too far.

## Divorce & Remarriage

### Me'un After Death

A related question arises in connection with retroactive *me'un* after death: specifically, whether *me'un* may be performed posthumously *bimkom mitzvah* — where performing it would enable *yibum* that would otherwise be impossible. Two scholars were dispatched at great cost to bring this question to Rabbi Akiva in prison, and both he and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira ruled it forbidden. The case involved the *zikah* relationship and whether *me'un* can operate retroactively once the first husband has already died, effectively reaching back in time to unwind a prior marriage that no longer exists in any tangible form. The conclusion here is that *me'un* cannot function in that posthumous mode, even for the worthy purpose of enabling *de'oraisa yibum*.

## Me'un After Get…

### Practical Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַשְׁיָאן: וּמוֹדֶה רַב, שֶׁמּוּתֶּרֶת לְאָחִיו שֶׁל זֶה שֶׁנֶּאֶסְרָה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Ashyan said: Rav concedes that she is permitted to the brother of the man to whom she is forbidden.** Rav Yitzḥak is referring to a case of a minor who refused her husband, remarried the same man, and was subsequently divorced, and then married another man and refused him. Although she may not remarry the first husband, she may marry his brother, despite the fact that one may not ordinarily marry one's brother's divorcée.

Rav Yitzchak bar Ashian makes an important practical observation: *u'modeh Rav shemuteret le'achiv shel zeh sheniesra alav* — even Rav, who rules that the second man's *me'un* does not allow her to return to the first husband, would agree that the second man's *me'un* does permit her to marry the first husband's *brother*. This is a remarkable concession that reveals how the *gemara* conceptualizes what is happening when we forbid her return to the first husband.

The prohibition on returning to the first husband is not because the *me'un* failed to redefine her status as a non-*gerushah*. In principle, the *me'un* does work. The concern is specifically that the first husband, who is familiar with her personal signals — *d'makir be'mizatah u'v'kritzatah* — will manipulate her into performing *me'un* with the second husband in order to free her to return to him. We therefore forbid the return to the first husband as a precautionary measure. But since we genuinely view her as having had her *gerushah* status redefined — since the *me'un* is considered effective in principle — she is not a *gerushas achiv* at all, and the first husband's brother may marry her freely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, הוּא נִיהוּ דְּמַכֶּרֶת בִּרְמִיזוֹתָיו וּקְרִיצוֹתָיו, אֲבָל אָחִיו לָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיגְזַר הַאי אַטּוּ הַאי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious.** It **is he,** her former husband, **whose hints and gestures she recognizes, but not** those of **his brother,** so that there is no concern that the brother will persuade her to refuse her husband. The Gemara explains: Rav Yitzḥak bar Ashyan saw fit to point this out, **lest you say: Issue a decree** rendering it prohibited for her to marry **this** brother **due to** the risk that such a marriage would lead people to think she is permitted to marry **that** brother, her original husband. Therefore, **he teaches us** that no such decree was instituted.

The *gemara* then raises a counterpoint: one might argue that since we have already forbidden her to the first husband, we should extend that prohibition by *gezeirah* to the first husband's brothers as well. Rav Yosef bar Ashian adopts exactly this position — *keshem she'asura lo kach asura le'achiv* — reasoning that once we have forbidden her to the first husband, a *gezeirah* should cover his brothers as well, lest they enable or facilitate the first husband's improper conduct. The first opinion disagrees: the concern is specific to the first husband himself, who knows her signals. There is no basis for extending the rabbinic fence to cover people who present no such risk.

The practical implication is striking and worth stating explicitly: the *gemara* is operating on the assumption that a second man's *me'un* is genuinely effective as a matter of principle. The entire edifice of what the *gemara* forbids or permits flows not from doubts about the *me'un*'s power but from precautionary concerns about the first husband's potential misconduct. And notably, the *gemara* leaves unexplored the question of whether she could thereby marry a *kohen* — since that too would seem to be answered in the affirmative if her *gerushah* status has genuinely been erased.

## Divorce & Remarriage…

### Simple Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — מוּתֶּרֶת לַיָּבָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who divorces a woman and remarries her** and then dies childless, his wife **is permitted to** enter into levirate marriage with her ***yavam*,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר. וְכֵן הַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת הַיְּתוֹמָה וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — מוּתֶּרֶת לַיָּבָם. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר. קְטַנָּה שֶׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ אָבִיהָ, וְנִתְגָּרְשָׁה — כִּיתוֹמָה בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב. וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֲסוּרָה לַיָּבָם.
>
> **Translation:** **but Rabbi Elazar prohibits** this. **Likewise,** with regard to **one who divorces an orphaned** minor **girl** whose mother and brothers married her off **and remarries her** and subsequently dies, **she is permitted to the *yavam*** in levirate marriage, **and Rabbi Elazar prohibits** it. **A minor girl whose father married her off,** in which case the marriage is valid by Torah law, **and who was** subsequently **divorced** while she was still a minor **is like an orphan during the lifetime of** her **father,** as he no longer has the right to marry her off, and she cannot become fully married because she is a minor. **And if** the husband **remarries her** while she is still a minor and then dies childless, **everyone agrees** that **she is forbidden to the *yavam*** and may not enter into levirate marriage.

The *mishnah* now introduces a new topic: *hamigaresh et ha'isha v'yachzirah* — a man who divorces his wife and then takes her back, without an intervening marriage to anyone else. The question for our purposes is whether her status as *grushas achiv* — the brother's divorcee — carries forward and prevents *yibum* when the husband subsequently dies, or whether the remarriage redefines her status so that she is once again simply *eshet achiv hamet*, the brother's wife, triggering the ordinary *mitzvah* of *yibum*.

The question has real conceptual depth: when she received the *get*, she became *grushas achiv* vis-à-vis the surviving brother. When the first husband then remarried her, did that second marriage erase the earlier status? Or does the *grushas achiv* designation persist latently, re-emerging with full force once the husband dies?

The plain ruling of the *mishnah* is *muteres leyavam* — she is permitted to *yibum*. The remarriage redefines her status and she is no longer *grushas achiv*. But Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and rules she is forbidden.

### Adult Remarriage

The *mishnah* then presents a parallel case involving a *yesomah* — a minor whose father has died, such that her original marriage was *kiddushin de'rabbanan*. He divorced her and then remarried her. Here too the *mishnah* rules *muteres leyavam*, and here too Rabbi Eliezer forbids *yibum*. At first glance this case seems identical in structure to the first, raising the question of why it is stated separately. The answer emerges only when we reach the third case.

### R. Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עֵיפָה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר — הוֹאִיל וְעָמְדָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּאִיסּוּרָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְעֵיפָה: אִי הָכִי, חֲלִיצָה נָמֵי לָא תִּיבְעֵי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sage **Eifa said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Elazar,** who prohibits a man from entering into levirate marriage with a woman whom his brother had divorced and remarried? It is **because she was forbidden to him at one time;** when the first brother divorced her, she became forbidden to the second brother due to her status as the first brother's ex-wife. A husband's relatives are forbidden to the wife even after death or divorce. However, the mitzva of levirate marriage grants a special exemption from the prohibition against marrying one's brother's wife. In this case, if the first brother had died while they were still divorced, the mitzva of levirate marriage would not have applied, and she would have been forbidden to him. **The Sages said to Eifa: If that is so, she should also not require *ḥalitza*,** since she is a forbidden relative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמְרוּ: חוֹלֶצֶת! אֶלָּא אָמַר עֵיפָה: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי טַעְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Indeed,** Rabbi Elazar also exempts her from *ḥalitza*, **isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **It was said in the name of Rabbi Elazar** that **she performs *ḥalitza*? Rather, Eifa said: I do not know the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי מִיתָה מַפֶּלֶת, אִי נִשּׂוּאִין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים מַפִּילִים. אִי מִיתָה מַפֶּלֶת — הָא רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ לְיִיבּוּם, אִי נִשּׂוּאִין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים מַפִּילִים — הָא עָמְדָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּאִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This is the reasoning of Rabbi Elazar: He is uncertain whether the death** of the husband **determines** that she is a candidate for levirate marriage, **or** whether **the original marriage determines** it. In other words, he is uncertain as to whether the obligation to perform levirate marriage is established only at the death of one's brother or already from the beginning of the brother's marriage. **If death determines** that she is a candidate for levirate marriage, **then she has happened before him for levirate marriage,** as she is the wife of his childless brother who died. **If the original marriage determines** that she is a candidate for levirate marriage, **then she was forbidden to him for a time** as his brother's ex-wife and is consequently exempt from levirate marriage. According to Rabbi Elazar, it is due to this uncertainty that the brother may not enter into levirate marriage yet must perform *ḥalitza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם פְּשִׁיטָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְּמִיתָה מַפֶּלֶת, וּמִיהוּ הַכֹּל בְּקִיאִין בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, וְאֵין הַכֹּל בְּקִיאִין בַּחֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually, it is obvious to Rabbi Elazar that death determines** that she is a candidate for levirate marriage. **However, everyone is well informed with regard to divorces.** Everyone knows that the woman was divorced, **while not everyone is well informed with regard to remarriage,** and they do not necessarily know that she remarried him. Therefore, there is concern that people will mistakenly think that one entered into levirate marriage with his brother's ex-wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה: חֲזָרָה, כֵּיוָן דְּיָתְבָא תּוּתֵיהּ, אִית לֵיהּ קָלָא! מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּאַהְדְּרַהּ בְּאוּרְתָּא וּשְׁכֵיב בְּצַפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara argues against this point: **On the contrary, her return** to her former husband, **since she is living with him, generates publicity,** so that it is known that they are remarried. The Gemara answers: **Are we not dealing** even with **a case in which he remarried her in the evening and died in the morning?** In this instance and others like it, not everyone would know that he remarried her, and they will think that the brother took his deceased brother's ex-wife in levirate marriage. In order to avoid such situations, Rabbi Elazar decreed that she is always forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּגָזַר הָנֵי מִשּׁוּם יְתוֹמָה בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב וְהֶחְזִירָהּ. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: קְטַנָּה שֶׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ אָבִיהָ וְנִתְגָּרְשָׁה, כִּיתוֹמָה בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב. וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַיָּבָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: This is the reasoning of Rabbi Elazar: He decreed** to prohibit levirate marriage with **these,** i.e., women who were divorced and remarried, **due to** the case of a girl who is considered **an orphan in the lifetime of** her **father,** who was divorced by her husband **and he** subsequently **remarried her.** If a minor girl was married off by her father and was subsequently divorced, she is no longer subject to her father with regard to marriage and divorce, but because she is a minor, any marriage she enters into is by rabbinic rather than by Torah law. The Gemara comments: **So too, this is reasonable based on what was taught in the latter clause** of the mishna: **A minor girl whose father married her off and who was** subsequently **divorced** while she was still a minor, **is like an orphan during her father's lifetime. And if** the husband **remarries her** while she is still a minor and then dies, **everyone agrees that she is forbidden to the *yavam*** and may not enter into levirate marriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא לָאו הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְּגָזַר הָנָךְ מִשּׁוּם הַאי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? **It is obvious. Rather, is it not teaching us Rabbi Elazar's reason for decreeing that these** women who were divorced and remarried are forbidden **due to that** woman, the girl who is considered an orphan in the lifetime of her father? The Gemara concludes: **Learn from here** that this is his reason.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בִּקְטַנָּה שֶׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ אָבִיהָ וְנִתְגָּרְשָׁה — הֲרֵי הִיא כִּיתוֹמָה בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַיָּבָם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁגֵּירוּשֶׁיהָ גֵּירוּשִׁין גְּמוּרִין, וְאֵין חֲזָרָתָהּ חֲזָרָה גְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Ashi: The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Elazar concerning a minor girl whose father married her off and who was divorced, that she is like an orphan in the lifetime of** her **father, and** that **if** her husband **remarried her, she is forbidden to the *yavam*, because her divorce was a full-fledged divorce** by Torah law **while her remarriage was not a full-fledged remarriage,** as she was still a minor. This implies that Rabbi Elazar's ruling is prompted by the case of a girl who is like an orphan in her father's lifetime and that this was the reason for his decree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁגֵּירְשָׁהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה וְהֶחְזִירָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה. אֲבָל גֵּירְשָׁהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה וְהֶחְזִירָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא גְּדוֹלָה, אִי נָמֵי הֶחְזִירָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה וְגָדְלָה אֶצְלוֹ וָמֵת — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמְרוּ: חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In what** case **is this statement said?** In a case **where he divorced her while she was a minor and he remarried her while she was** still **a minor. But if he divorced her while she was a minor and remarried her when she was** already **an adult, or if he remarried her while she was a minor and she matured** to legal adulthood **while with him, and he** subsequently **died, she may either perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage. It was said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: She must perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** since he decreed that all remarried women may not enter into levirate marriage due to the case of one who is like an orphan in her father's lifetime.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: צָרָתָהּ, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הִיא גּוּפַהּ גְּזֵירָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִיגְזוֹר גְּזֵירָה לִגְזֵירָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asked Rav Naḥman: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **the rival wife of** a girl whose husband remarried her, according to Rabbi Elazar? Is the girl regarded as a forbidden relative to the extent that even her **rival wife** may not enter into levirate marriage? **He said to him: She herself is** forbidden due to **a rabbinic decree,** as explained already. **And will we** then **proceed to issue a decree to** prevent violation of **a decree?** Accordingly, her **rival wife** is permitted to enter into levirate marriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמְרוּ: הִיא וְצָרָתָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת. הִיא וְצָרָתָהּ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא לָאו: אוֹ הִיא אוֹ צָרָתָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת? לָאו תָּרוֹצֵי קָמְתָרְצַתְּ, תָּרֵיץ הָכִי: הִיא — חוֹלֶצֶת, צָרָתָהּ — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **It was said in the name of Rabbi Elazar:** Both **she and her rival wife must perform *ḥalitza*?** The Gemara asks: **Would it enter your mind** to say: **She and her rival wife?** Why should two women from the same household perform *ḥalitza*? *Ḥalitza* performed by one of them exempts the other. **Rather, should it not** say: **Either she or her rival wife must perform *ḥalitza*,** but even the rival wife may not enter into levirate marriage. The Gemara responds: **Are you not emending** the mishna? If so, **emend it as follows: She** can only **perform *ḥalitza*; her rival wife may either perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage.**

The third case illuminates everything: *ketanah she'hisi'ah aviha* — a minor who was married off by her father, making the original marriage *kiddushin de'oraisa*. He then divorced her, producing a *gerushin* of full *de'oraisa* force. After the divorce, she becomes *k'yesomah b'chayei ha'av* — like an orphan while the father is alive. She has left the father's household (since she was married and then returned), is no longer under her husband's control (since he divorced her), and is in a kind of emancipated status. If the same man then remarries her, this second marriage is only *kiddushin de'rabbanan*, because the father can no longer marry her off *de'oraisa* in this state. The *gemara* then rules *divrei hakol asurah leyavam* — everyone agrees that she is forbidden to *yibum*, because the *gerushin* was a full *de'oraisa* divorce, creating a genuine *grushas achiv de'oraisa* status, while the remarriage was only *de'rabbanan* and therefore cannot undo it.

This third case is the key to understanding Rabbi Eliezer's position throughout. The reason he forbids *yibum* even in the first two cases — the adult who remarried as an adult, and the *yesomah* who was at *de'rabbanan* level throughout — is to protect against confusion with this third case, where everyone agrees she is truly *grushas achiv*.

An interesting question arises from the structure of case three: if the father loses his *de'oraisa* authority to marry her off after the first marriage dissolves, who performs the second *kiddushin*? The *Rambam* does not mention the mother or brothers in this context, which might suggest she effectuates the *kiddushin* herself, or alternatively that the father retains *de'rabbanan* authority analogous to what the mother and brothers possess — but this remains an open question.

### Eliezer's Reasoning

The *gemara* (through Eifa) initially suggests Rabbi Eliezer's rationale is *ho'il ve'amdah lo sha'ah achas be'issura* — since she stood in a forbidden relationship for at least one moment (when she was *grushas achiv* following the *get*), that status persists even after remarriage. The rabbis challenge this: if so, she should not even require *chalitzah*, since she would simply be a full *grushas achiv de'oraisa*. But a *braisa* records that Rabbi Eliezer does require *chalitzah*, which demonstrates that he does not treat her as straightforwardly *grushas achiv de'oraisa*. He must be operating with some form of rabbinic prohibition or doubt.

Eifa then concedes: *Rabbi Eliezer lo yadan mai ta'amei* — I do not know Rabbi Eliezer's reason.

Abaye provides the answer: *misafka lei ee misah mapil ee nisuin rishonim mapilin* — Rabbi Eliezer is in doubt about what determines a woman's status for purposes of *yibum*. Is it the moment of the husband's death that triggers the assessment of her status? Or is it the moment of the original marriage — the *nisuin rishonim* — that determines all relevant classifications? If one assesses from the moment of the first marriage, then she was already *grushas achiv* at the time of the *get* and that status, once created, governs. If one assesses from the moment of death, then the relevant marriage is the second one, and she should be eligible for *yibum*. Since this is unresolved, Rabbi Eliezer requires *chalitzah* as a hedge.

Rava offers a different explanation: Rabbi Eliezer actually holds clearly that *misah mapil* — it is the death that triggers the assessment. In principle, then, she should be eligible for *yibum*. But a *de'rabbanan* concern prevents it: *hakol beki'in be'geirushin ve'ein hakol beki'in be'hazara* — everyone knows about a divorce (since the community witnessed the *get*), but not everyone knows about the remarriage (which may have been private). If the brother performs *yibum* with her, onlookers who know about the divorce but not the remarriage will think he is performing *yibum* with his brother's ex-wife, which is ordinarily forbidden. To forestall this misimpression, Rabbi Eliezer requires *chalitzah* but not *yibum*. The *gemara* challenges this on the grounds that remarriage, since they live together, tends to be publicly known — but responds that one could imagine scenarios where he remarried her at night and died the next morning, meaning the community never learned of the reconciliation.

Rav Ashi offers a third explanation that he argues is the most straightforward reading of the *mishnah*: *de'gazar hani mishum yesomah b'chayei aviha ve'hichzirah* — Rabbi Eliezer's prohibition in the first two cases is a *gezeirah* enacted to protect against confusion with the third case. The third case — father-married minor, full *de'oraisa* divorce, *de'rabbanan* remarriage — is a genuine, unanimous *grushas achiv* situation. To prevent people from mistakenly permitting *yibum* in that case by confusing it with the first two, Rabbi Eliezer extends the prohibition to all cases where the pattern of divorce-followed-by-remarriage exists, even when both stages were *de'oraisa* or both were *de'rabbanan*.

This is confirmed by a *braisa*: *modim chachamim le'Rabbi Elazar be'ketanah she'hisi'ah aviha ve'nisgarsha harei hi k'yesomah b'chayei ha'av* — the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Eliezer that in the third case she is genuinely *grushas achiv*, since *geirusheiha geirushin gemurin* — her divorce was a complete *de'oraisa* divorce — *ve'ein chazarasah chazara gemura* — and her remarriage was not a full *de'oraisa* remarriage. The *gemara* notes that the very inclusion of this agreed-upon third case in the *mishnah* serves precisely to frame Rabbi Eliezer's positions in the first two cases as a *gezeirah* extending from it.

The *braisa* further clarifies: *bamei devarim amurim* — when does everyone agree she remains *grushas achiv*? When he divorced her as a minor and remarried her as a minor, so the remarriage was only *de'rabbanan*. But if he divorced her as a minor and remarried her as an adult (*ve'hichzirah k'she-hi gedolah*), or remarried her as a minor but she reached adulthood while still married to him (*gadla etzlo*), then the second marriage attains *de'oraisa* status — presumably because marital relations after she becomes an adult constitute *bi'as kiddushin* — and *o choletzet o misyabemes*, she may either perform *chalitzah* or *yibum*. Rabbi Eliezer, however, in his *gezeirah*, still requires *chalitzah* even in those latter cases.

The *gemara* then addresses a follow-up question from Rabbah to Rav Nachman: *tzarasah mahu* — what about the co-wife? If Rabbi Eliezer forbids *yibum* with this woman, can the brother perform *yibum* with the co-wife? If Rabbi Eliezer treats her as a *de'rabbanan erva*, then she would be a *tzaras erva* and the co-wife could not perform *yibum* either. But if Rabbi Eliezer's prohibition is purely a *gezeirah* — not a genuine *ervah* classification — then the co-wife remains eligible for *yibum*. Rav Nachman's initial instinct: the whole thing is a *gezeirah*, and one does not make a *gezeirah* atop a *gezeirah*, so the co-wife may perform *yibum*.

A *braisa* appears to contradict this, stating in the name of Rabbi Eliezer that both she *and* her co-wife perform *chalitzah*. The *gemara* resolves this by reading the conjunctive *vav* as disjunctive — meaning one or the other performs *chalitzah* — and then further revises: the woman herself performs *chalitzah*, while the co-wife may perform either *chalitzah* or *yibum*. This confirms that Rabbi Eliezer's prohibition, while real, does not generate full *tzaras erva* status, and the co-wife retains access to *yibum*.

## Me'un & Yibum Conflict

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנֵי אַחִין נְשׂוּאִין לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת קְטַנּוֹת, וּמֵת בַּעְלָהּ שֶׁל אַחַת מֵהֶן — הַלֵּזוּ תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה. וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי חֵרְשׁוֹת. גְּדוֹלָה וּקְטַנָּה, מֵת בַּעְלָהּ שֶׁל קְטַנָּה — תֵּצֵא הַקְּטַנָּה מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **two brothers are married to two minor sisters, and the husband of one of them dies** childless, **this** widowed girl **shall be exempt** from levirate marriage **due to** her status as a forbidden relative, **as** one is prohibited from marrying the **sister** of **his wife. The same** *halakha* applies to **two deaf-mute women,** whose status is like that of two minors in this matter, as their marriages are valid by rabbinic law. And if two brothers were married to two sisters, one of them **an adult and** the other **a minor,** and **the husband of the minor dies, the minor shall leave due to** her status as the **sister** of **a wife,** as in the first case in the mishna.

The *mishnah* now introduces a new scenario that brings *me'un* and *yibum* into direct conflict: *shnei achim nesuim lishtei achayos ketanos* — two brothers married to two minor sisters. One of the brothers dies. Since both marriages are *de'rabbanan* (the *gemara* assumes the fathers are deceased, making these *yesomot ketanot*), all the relevant obligations — the *mitzvah* of *yibum* and the prohibition of *achos ishah* — exist at the same *de'rabbanan* level and therefore cancel one another out. She simply walks away without *chalitzah* or *yibum*, since there is no *de'oraisa* obligation that would require a formal release.

### Simple Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת בַּעְלָהּ שֶׁל גְּדוֹלָה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מְלַמְּדִין אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה שֶׁתְּמָאֵן בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** But **if the husband of the adult dies,** it generates a Torah obligation of levirate marriage, which is not abrogated by the rabbinic prohibition proscribing the *yevama* as his wife's sister. This prohibition is by rabbinic law, because marriage to a minor is rabbinic in origin. What does one do under such circumstances? **Rabbi Eliezer says: We instruct the minor,** i.e., his wife, **to refuse him,** so that her marriage is dissolved and he may then enter into levirate marriage with her adult sister, the widow of his childless brother.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אִם מֵיאֲנָה — מֵיאֲנָה, וְאִם לָאו — תַּמְתִּין עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל, וְתֵצֵא הַלֵּזוּ מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel says: If** the minor **refuses** of her own accord, **her refusal is valid. And if not, she should wait until she reaches majority,** whereupon her marriage is valid by Torah law, **and that** widowed adult sister **shall be exempt** from levirate marriage **due to** her status as the **sister** of **a wife.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אִי לוֹ עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ, אִי לוֹ עַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו. מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט — וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** When the brother married to the adult sister dies, leaving the brother married to the minor, **woe [*ee*] to him for his wife, woe to him for his brother's wife.** Under these circumstances, he loses both women: **He must release his own wife with a bill of divorce** and **his brother's wife by** performing ***ḥalitza*.** He cannot stay married to his wife because she is the sister of his *yevama*, and he cannot enter into levirate marriage with the *yevama* even after divorcing his wife, because the *yevama* is his wife's sister. The principle that one is completely absolved from levirate marriage when the potential *yevama* is a forbidden relative does not apply because Torah law does not recognize his marriage to his minor wife. That marriage's rabbinic sanction does not suffice to render the *yevama*, his wife's sister, a forbidden relative who is not a candidate for levirate marriage.

The *mishnah* then works through a series of graduated cases. If one of the women is an adult (*gedolah*) and the other is a minor (*ketanah*), the outcome depends on which husband dies. If the *ketanah*'s husband dies, the surviving brother has a *yibum de'rabbanan* obligation toward her, but she is his wife's sister at the *de'oraisa* level — *achos ishah de'oraisa*. The *de'oraisa erva* overrides the *de'rabbanan yibum*: she walks away. The *mishnah* states plainly: *teitzei ketanah mishum achos ishah*.

### The Dilemma

The genuinely difficult case arises when the *gedolah*'s husband dies. Now the surviving brother has a *de'oraisa* obligation of *yibum* — the deceased was fully and biblically married to a *gedolah* — but he is *de'rabbanan* married to the sister, creating a *de'rabbanan* prohibition of *achos ishah*. He cannot simply perform *yibum*, because he would then be perpetually violating a *de'rabbanan achos ishah* prohibition. He cannot simply perform *chalitzah* and remain married to his wife, because then she becomes *achos chalutzaso*. He cannot divorce his wife and perform *yibum*, because then she becomes *achos grushaso*. Every path creates a new forbidden relationship.

The problem cannot be resolved by invoking *be'makom mitzvah lo gazru rabbanan* — the principle that rabbinic decrees do not apply in the presence of a *mitzvah* — because *yibum* is not a momentary act that overrides the prohibition in passing. If he marries her, he will be living with her in an ongoing *de'rabbanan achos ishah* violation. This is not a situation of two competing momentary obligations; it is a structural incompatibility.

Additionally, even doing nothing creates a problem according to those authorities who hold that *zikah* — the legal bond that exists between the levir and the *yevamah* before he acts — is strong enough to create a prohibition of *achos zekukaso*. If *zikah* is real, then simply remaining married to his wife while the *zikah* to the sister-in-law persists is itself forbidden.

### R. Eliezer Solution

Rabbi Eliezer cuts the knot: *melamdin et haketanah shetime'en bo* — instruct the minor wife to perform *me'un* against her husband. If she performs *me'un*, the marriage is retroactively treated as never having taken place, she is no longer his wife, there is no *achos ishah* impediment, and he may proceed to *yibum de'oraisa* with the *gedolah*'s widow. The simplicity of the solution is elegant.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel allows a middle path: *me'anah me'anah* — if the minor herself wishes to perform *me'un*, that resolves everything. But we do not instruct her to do so. If she does not wish to do *me'un*, she waits until she becomes an adult. Once she becomes an adult, the *kiddushin* attains *de'oraisa* force, and she then constitutes a genuine *achos ishah de'oraisa*, permitting her to exit without *chalitzah*. Rabban Gamliel's position presupposes that *zikah* is not strong enough to create a prohibition of *achos zekukaso*, so there is no problem with the brother remaining in his current marriages while waiting for the minor to come of age. The minor may, of course, be effectively an *agunah* for some period of years while this plays out.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

Rabbi Yehoshua rejects both solutions. He holds that one cannot instruct a woman to perform *me'un* — that is simply not a permissible intervention. And he holds that *zikah* is real enough to create *achos zekukaso*, so the brother cannot simply remain in his current marriage while the *zikah* to the sister-in-law persists. He therefore sees no exit that does not generate a new problem. His ruling: *motzi et ishto be'get ve'eshet achiv be'chalitzah* — he must both give his wife a *get* and perform *chalitzah* with the sister-in-law. He cannot do one without the other, because doing one first makes the other *achos chalutzaso* or *achos grushaso*. In theory both must happen simultaneously — and the result is that both women are released, and he ends up with neither. *Oy li al ishto oy li al eshet achiv* — woe to his wife, woe to his brother's wife.

### Teaching Me'un

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתָנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: לְעוֹלָם יִדְבַּק אָדָם בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים, וְיִתְרַחֵק מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים. יִדְבַּק בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים: בַּחֲלִיצָה, וּבַהֲבָאַת שָׁלוֹם, וּבַהֲפָרַת נְדָרִים. וְיִתְרַחֵק מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים: מִן הַמֵּיאוּן, וּמִן הַפִּקְדוֹנוֹת, וּמִן הָעֵרְבוֹנוֹת! מֵיאוּן דְּמִצְוָה שָׁנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara inquires about Rabbi Eliezer's suggestion to instruct the minor to refuse: **Is it permitted** to instruct her to refuse? **Doesn't bar Kappara teach: A person should always cling to three things and distance himself from three things. He should cling to three things: To *ḥalitza*** rather than levirate marriage, **to bringing about peace, and to the nullification of vows. And he should distance himself from three things: From refusal; and from** accepting **deposits,** as he is then responsible for them; **and from serving as a guarantor.** The Gemara answers: **A refusal for a mitzva is different,** as this refusal is performed to allow the mitzva of levirate marriage to be fulfilled with the other sister.

The *gemara* raises the question that motivates Rabbi Yehoshua's position: is it permissible to instruct a woman to perform *me'un* at all? This challenge emerges from a teaching of Bar Kapparah, which introduces the next section.

## Rabbinic Values

### Bar Kapparah Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. תָּנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: לְעוֹלָם יִדְבַּק אָדָם בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים: בַּחֲלִיצָה — כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, דְּתַנְיָא, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: הַכּוֹנֵס אֶת יְבִמְתּוֹ לְשֵׁם נוֹי, לְשֵׁם אִישׁוּת, לְשֵׁם דָּבָר אַחֵר — כְּאִילּוּ פּוֹגֵעַ בְּעֶרְוָה, וְקָרוֹב בְּעֵינַי לִהְיוֹת הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara explains the details of **the** matter **itself. Bar Kappara taught: A person should always cling to three things: To *ḥalitza*;** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Abba Shaul said: One who marries his *yevama* for** her **beauty,** or **for the sake of matrimony** because he wants to be married to her, **or for some other reason,** such as her money, **it is as if he is having intercourse with a woman forbidden to him, and in my eyes** it is **almost as if his offspring were a *mamzer*.** Therefore, it is preferable that one performs *ḥalitza* and avoids sin.

Bar Kapparah taught: a person should cleave to three things and distance himself from three things. Cleave to: *chalitzah* over *yibum*, bringing *shalom*, and annulling vows (*haforas nedarim*). Distance from: *me'un*, accepting objects for safekeeping (*pikdonos*), and being a cosigner (*aravon*). It is from the first of these — that one should prefer *chalitzah* to *yibum* — that the *gemara* derives its answer to the question about Rabbi Eliezer's solution. The *gemara* concludes: *me'un de'mitzvah shanei* — ordinarily we do not instruct people to perform *me'un*, in keeping with Bar Kapparah's teaching. But when performing *me'un* enables a *de'oraisa* obligation of *yibum* to be fulfilled, it is an exceptional case. In that context, teaching the minor to perform *me'un* is not only permissible but appropriate.

### Abba Shaul Yibum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהֲבָאַת שָׁלוֹם, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּקֵּשׁ שָׁלוֹם וְרׇדְפֵהוּ״,
>
> **Translation:** One should cling to **bringing about peace, as it is written "Seek peace and pursue it"** (Psalms 34:15).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אָתְיָא ״רְדִיפָה״ ״רְדִיפָה״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״בַּקֵּשׁ שָׁלוֹם וְרׇדְפֵהוּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״רוֹדֵף צְדָקָה וָחָסֶד יִמְצָא חַיִּים צְדָקָה וְכָבוֹד״. בַּהֲפָרַת נְדָרִים, כְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: הַנּוֹדֵר — כְּאִילּוּ בָּנָה בָּמָה. וְהַמְקַיְּימוֹ — כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיב עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu said: It is derived** by verbal analogy from the terms **pursuit** and **pursuit. It is written here: "Seek peace and pursue it"** (Psalms 34:15) **and it is written there: "He who pursues righteousness and mercy finds life, prosperity, and honor"** (Proverbs 21:21), indicating that pursuing peace is a mitzva, just as pursuing righteousness and mercy is. As **for the nullification of vows, this is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says:** With regard to **one who vows, it is as if he built** a personal **altar** when it is prohibited to build an altar outside the Temple. **And one who fulfills that** vow, **it is as if he sacrificed an offering on this** personal altar, thereby doubling his sin. Therefore, it is preferable that he ask a halakhic authority to dissolve the vow.

The preference for *chalitzah* that Bar Kapparah cites follows the position of Abba Shaul. A *braisa* records: *ha'kones et yevamto le'shem noy, le'shem ishus, le'shem davar acher* — one who takes his *yevamah* for reasons of her beauty, for the desire for marriage, or for any other motivation other than purely *le'hakim shem achiv*, establishing his brother's name — *ke'ilu poga be'erva*, it is as if he engaged in a prohibited relationship, since the ordinarily-forbidden status of *eshet achiv* is lifted only by the *mitzvah* of *yibum* performed with proper intent. When that proper intent is absent, the override does not fully function. Moreover, Abba Shaul states *ve'karov be'einai lihyos ha'vlad mamzer* — it is almost as though the child born from such a union is a *mamzer*. This harsh formulation reflects Abba Shaul's view that *yibum* performed without its proper *le'shem mitzvah* motivation is deeply compromised. Hence Bar Kapparah's teaching that one should incline toward *chalitzah*, which carries no such risk.

### Peace Imperative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיִתְרַחֵק מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים: מִן הַמֵּיאוּנִין — דִּלְמָא גָּדְלָה וּמִיחָרְטָא בָּהּ. מִן הַפִּקְדוֹנוֹת — בְּבַר מָתָא, דְּבַיְיתֵיהּ כִּי בַיְיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי. מִן הָעֵרָבוֹן — בַּעֲרָבֵי שַׁלְצִיּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And one should distance himself from three things: From refusals,** as **perhaps she will grow up and regret** her decision, and it will turn out that she refused a husband who was suitable for her. **From deposits** entrusted to him **by an inhabitant of the same city, as** he will treat the bailee's **home as his home.** The owner might enter the bailee's house and take the deposit without the latter's knowledge, and subsequently falsely sue him for its return. **From serving as a guarantor: This is referring to Sheltziyyon guarantees,** in which the lender is entitled to demand payment from the guarantor even before the borrower defaults on the loan.

The second thing one should cleave to is the pursuit of *shalom*. *Bakesh shalom ve'rodfehu* — seek peace and pursue it. Rabbi Abahu adds a *gezeirah shavah* connecting the language of *redifah* (running after) in this verse to a second verse: *rodef tzedakah va'chesed yimtza chayim tzedakah ve'chavod* — one who runs after righteousness and kindness will find life, righteousness, and honor. Life in this world, honor in the world to come. The active, relentless pursuit of peace is thus associated with the fullest range of divine reward.

### Vows & Institutions

The third positive practice is *haforas nedarim* — annulling vows. This follows Rabbi Natan, who taught: *ha'noder ke'ilu banah bamah* — one who takes a vow is like someone who built a *bamah* (a private altar), which is forbidden when there is a central *Beis Hamikdash*. And *ha'mekaymo ke'ilu hikriv alav korban* — one who fulfills that vow is like one who offered a sacrifice on that *bamah*.

The metaphor is illuminating. The underlying impulse to make a vow is a desire for personal religious expression beyond what the established system of *mitzvos* and *halacha* provides. One is not satisfied with communal religious institutions and seeks a private avenue for devotion. Rabbi Natan frames this as analogous to building a private altar: subjectively, the person believes they are performing a sacred act of dedication; objectively, from the perspective of those committed to centralized religious institutions, it represents an undermining of the communal framework. Vows therefore should not be made — and if made, they should be annulled.

### Cosigners & Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״רַע יֵרוֹעַ כִּי עָרַב זָר״ — רָעָה אַחַר רָעָה תָּבֹא לִמְקַבְּלֵי גֵרִים, וְלַעֲרָבֵי שַׁלְצִיּוֹן, וּלְתוֹקֵעַ עַצְמוֹ לִדְבַר הֲלָכָה. מְקַבְּלֵי גֵרִים — כְּרַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: קָשִׁים גֵּרִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּסַפַּחַת בָּעוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yitzḥak said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "He who serves as a guarantor for a stranger shall suffer evil;** but he who hates those who shake hands is secure" (Proverbs 11:15)? This means: **Evil after evil will befall those who accept converts, and Sheltziyyon guarantors, and one who confounds himself in matters of *halakha*.** The Gemara clarifies. Evil will befall **those who accept converts: This is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥelbo. As Rabbi Ḥelbo says: Converts are difficult for the Jewish people like a leprous sore on the skin.**

Turning to the three things from which one should distance: *me'un* is the first. Bar Kapparah's concern is *de'ma gadla u'micharta* — perhaps she will become an adult and regret it. *Pikdonos* is the second: accepting objects for safekeeping from a neighbor (*bar mata*), because *de'veiteih ke'veiteih dami* — his house is like your house; he will come and go freely, creating conditions under which he could take the object back and then claim you lost it.

The third is *aravon*, being a cosigner — specifically in the mode of *arvei shel Tzion*, where creditors would pursue the cosigner before even turning to the actual debtor. Rabbi Yitzchak explains via a verse: *ra yero'a ki arav zar* — evil after evil because one has entangled oneself as a guarantor for an outsider. The verse yields two further readings.

### Gerim Concerns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲרָבֵי שַׁלְצִיּוֹן — דְּעָבְדִי שְׁלוֹף דּוֹץ. תּוֹקֵעַ עַצְמוֹ לִדְבַר הֲלָכָה — דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר אֵין לוֹ תּוֹרָה — אֵין לוֹ תּוֹרָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה — אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Evil shall befall **Sheltziyyon guarantors because they practice: Pull out, thrust in.** That is, they pull out the borrower and thrust the guarantor in his place as the one responsible for the loan. Evil befalls **one who confounds himself in matters of *halakha*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says: Anyone who says he has no Torah, has no Torah.** The Gemara asks: Is this not **obvious? Rather, anyone who says he has nothing other** than **Torah, has nothing other** than **Torah.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ תּוֹרָה אֵין לוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלִמַּדְתֶּם וַעֲשִׂיתֶם״ — כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בַּעֲשִׂיָּה יֶשְׁנוֹ בִּלְמִידָה, כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַּעֲשִׂיָּה אֵינוֹ בִּלְמִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** this **also obvious?** One does not receive more reward than he deserves. **Rather,** it means that **he does not even have Torah. What is the reason? Rav Pappa said: The verse states: That you may learn them and perform them,** which is an abridged version of the verse "Hear, O Israel, the statutes and the ordinances that I speak in your ears this day, that you may learn them, and take care to perform them" (Deuteronomy 5:1). The verse teaches that **anyone who is engaged in performing** mitzvot **is engaged in** Torah **study, while anyone not engaged in performing** mitzvot **is not engaged in** Torah **study;** the Torah study of one who wishes only to immerse himself in his studies without fulfilling the mitzvot is not considered to be fulfilling even the mitzva of Torah study.

One reading: *ra'ah achar ra'ah tavo le'mekablei gerim* — evil after evil should come to those who accept converts. This is, taken at face value, a strikingly negative statement about accepting *gerim*. It reads *arav zar* as referring to involving an outsider in one's religious community. Rav Chelbo's teaching is cited in support: *kashim gerim le'Yisrael ke'sapachat ba'or* — converts are as difficult for Israel as a skin affliction, based on a wordplay between *ve'nispechu* (they will attach) and *sapachat* (an affliction).

This is the most negative statement in the *gemara* about accepting converts, and it must be read carefully. *Tosafot* immediately contextualizes and mitigates: the negative statement applies specifically in two circumstances — where one is proactively proselytizing and persuading people to convert, or where one accepts a prospective convert without adequate process. But if they themselves are pressing insistently to convert, *yesh lanu le'kablam* — we should accept them, and doing so is not merely permitted but meritorious. As proof, *Tosafot* cites the *midrash* that Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov were punished for failing to accept Timna when she sought to convert, with the consequence that *Amalek* descended from her.

### Learning to Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, לְעוֹלָם כִּדְאָמְרִיתוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה — אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, לָא צְרִיכָא דְּקָא מַגְמַר לְאַחֲרִינֵי וְאָזְלִי וְעָבְדִי. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִית לֵיהּ אַגְרָא לְדִידֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say: Actually, it is as you initially said: Anyone who says he has nothing other** than **Torah has nothing other** than **Torah. Rather,** this statement **is necessary** with regard to one who **teaches others and they go and perform** the mitzvot. **Lest you say that there is reward for him in it,** Rabbi Yosei **teaches us** that since that person engaged in Torah study without the intention of observing the mitzvot himself, he does not receive a reward for the mitzvot that he taught others and which they performed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, תּוֹקֵעַ עַצְמוֹ לִדְבַר הֲלָכָה: בְּדַיָּינָא דְּאָתֵי דִּינָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, וְגָמַר הֲלָכָה וּמְדַמֵּי מִילְּתָא לְמִילְּתָא, וְאִית לֵיהּ רַבָּה וְלָא אָזֵיל מְשַׁאֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that **one who confounds himself in matters of *halakha*** is referring **to a judge who had a case come before him, and he learned** the tradition about **a ruling** in a similar case, **and he likens one matter to the other** in order to reach a conclusion; **and he has a teacher** nearby **but he does not go and ask** him. This is inappropriate, as judges must be very careful not to err in judgment.

The second reading of *arvei shel Tzion* concerns *tokeia atzmo lidvar halacha* — one who sticks himself onto a matter of *halacha*. This phrase requires unpacking.

A *braisa* records Rabbi Yose teaching: *kol ha'omer ein lo Torah ein lo Torah* — one who says "I have no interest in Torah" receives no reward from Torah. This seems obvious and barely worth stating. The *gemara* clarifies: the real case is *kol ha'omer ein lo ela Torah ein lo ela Torah* — one who says "I have only Torah, nothing else" — meaning one who learns but does not act, who remains in the *beis medrash* but never translates learning into practice, receives only the reward for learning and not the reward of *mitzvah* performance. Still, the *gemara* pushes further: even that seems obvious.

The real *chiddush* is more radical: if one's learning never translates into action, *afillu Torah ein lo* — one does not even receive the reward for Torah study. The basis is the verse *u'lemadetem va'asitem* — learn and do. *Kol she'yeshno ba'asiyah yeshno ba'limidah* — only if one is embedded in a world of action does the learning have value. *Kol she'eino ba'asiyah eino ba'limidah* — if one is not in a world of doing, the learning itself yields no reward.

An extension of this: what if one stays in the *beis medrash*, teaches others, and through teaching sends others out to perform *mitzvos*? One might think this vicarious doing should count. The *gemara* says no: if the teacher himself never acts, the fact that students go out and act does not earn the teacher reward for their actions. The teacher gets credit for the Torah taught but not for the actions performed by others. Learning must translate into personal action to be credited as such.

### Judge's Weight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: לְעוֹלָם יִרְאֶה דַּיָּין עַצְמוֹ כְּאִילּוּ חֶרֶב מוּנַּחַת לוֹ בֵּין יְרֵיכוֹתָיו, וְגֵיהִנָּם פְּתוּחָה לוֹ מִתַּחְתָּיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה מִטָּתוֹ שֶׁלִּשְׁלֹמֹה שִׁשִּׁים גִּבּוֹרִים סָבִיב לָהּ מִגִּבּוֹרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳ מִפַּחַד בַּלֵּילוֹת״, מִפַּחַד שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְלַיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: A judge should always view himself as if a sword were placed between his thighs,** so that if he leans right or left he will be injured, **and as if Gehenna was open beneath him, as it is stated: "Behold, it is the bed of Solomon; sixty mighty men are around it, of the mighty men of Israel.** They all handle the sword, and are expert in war; every man has his sword upon his thigh, **because of dread in the night"** (Song of Songs 3:7–8), i.e., **because of the dread of Gehenna, which is similar to the night.** Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani understands the mighty men of Israel in this verse to refer to the judges who sit in judgment around the bed of Solomon, i.e., in the Temple.

A final interpretation of *tokeia atzmo lidvar halacha* applies to a judge: a case comes before a *dayan* and he recognizes something vaguely analogous in what he has studied. He analogizes — *medami milsa le'milsa* — and renders a ruling based on that imperfect parallel, despite the fact that he has a *rebbe* he could consult and does not bother to do so. *Tokeia atzmo lidvar halacha*: he is stuck on the one thing he happens to know, applying it as if it were a hammer to every nail, never acknowledging the limits of his expertise. This is a grave failure.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rav Yonasan: *le'olam yehei dayan atzmo ke'ilu cherev munachas lo bein yarcheisav* — a judge should always regard himself as though a sword is placed between his thighs, *ve'Gehenom pesucha lo mitachtav* — and *Gehenom* is open beneath him. The weight of judgment demands complete seriousness and genuine *yiras shamayim*. A judge who overreaches, who applies a single learned rule without seeking guidance where guidance is needed, risks far more than a wrong ruling. The verse from *Shir Hashirim* about the sixty warriors guarding the bed of Shlomo, each with a sword on account of *pachad baleilos* — fear of nights — is read as *pachad shel Gehenom shedomeh le'layla*: the fear of *Gehenom*, which resembles the night.
