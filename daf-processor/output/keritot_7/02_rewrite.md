# Keritot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemen Hamishcha

### Intro to Shemen

The *Tosefta* enumerates the full list of offenses punishable by *karet*, and the *Gemara* — following its characteristic method — focuses on those items that receive less treatment elsewhere in *Shas*. Having addressed the making of *ketores* and the making of *shemen hamishcha* in previous discussions, the *Gemara* now turns to one who anoints himself with the *shemen hamishcha* of the *Beis Hamikdash* — not oil that he himself produced, but the actual consecrated anointing oil.

### Exemptions

A *Tosefta* is cited: *hasach b'shemen hamishcha l'vehema u'v'keilim patur* — if one uses the *shemen hamishcha* on an animal or on vessels, he is exempt from the specific prohibition. (Whether this would constitute *me'ilah* is a separate question, deliberately set aside here.) The prohibition derives from the phrase *al b'sar adam lo yisach* — "it shall not be placed on human flesh" — and since animals and vessels are not *adam*, they fall outside its scope. Similarly, applying it to a corpse (*meis*) incurs no liability, since once a person has died he is called a *meis* and is no longer classified as *adam*. The exemption for non-Jews, however, requires explanation, because non-Jews are in fact referred to as *adam* in Scripture.

### Non-Jews & Adam

This leads into one of the more striking and, as the lecturer notes, deeply unsettling discussions in *Shas* — the question of whether *chazal*, when interpreting a *pasuk* that uses the word *adam*, include or exclude non-Jews. *Tosafos* in several places in *Shas* grapples with the apparent inconsistency: sometimes the *Gemara* derives from *adam* that non-Jews are included, and sometimes it derives that they are excluded.

Here the *Gemara* begins by noting that non-Jews *are* called *adam*, then cites a *pasuk* from Yechezkel: *v'atem tzon mar'isi adam atem* — "you are my flock, the flock of my shepherding, you are *adam*" — from which it derives *atem keruyin adam v'ein ovdei kochavim keruyin adam*, that Israel is called *adam* and non-Jews are not. Two counter-proofs are then raised. First, *v'nefesh adam shisha asar eleph* — the sixteen thousand captives taken from Midyan are described as *adam*. The *Gemara* responds that this usage is merely contrastive with the animals captured alongside them, not a definitive application of the term. Second, *v'ani lo achus al Ninveh ha'ir hagedola asher yesh bah harbe adam* — the book of Yonah refers to the non-Jewish population of Nineveh as *adam*. Again, the *Gemara* responds that this too is contrastive with the *behema raba* mentioned in the same verse. In both cases, the word *adam* appears in proximity to *behema* and is understood only as a contrast to the animal category, not as a full application of the term to non-Jews.

A second, more elegant answer is then offered: *kol sheyeshno b'sicha yeshno b'val yisach* — anyone who is themselves subject to the prohibition of anointing with *shemen hamishcha* is also a prohibited object of that anointing. Since the prohibition of *shemen hamishcha* is part of the *taryag mitzvos* and does not apply to non-Jews through the *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach*, non-Jews are simply not within the framework of the prohibition at all — and therefore placing it on their flesh is also not prohibited.

The broader issue raised by this *Gemara* deserves acknowledgment. While there are real halachic distinctions between Jews and non-Jews, this must be understood in its proper context. Even Rabbi Akiva, who often emphasizes legal distinctions between Jews and non-Jews, is the same tanna who declares *chaviv adam shenivra b'tzelem* — that all human beings are precious by virtue of being created in the image of God — and who then adds *chavivim Yisrael shenikru banim lamakom*, underscoring the unique relationship of *Bnei Yisrael* with God while affirming the universal dignity of all people. The interpretation of *adam* to exclude non-Jews is a technical *derasha* serving a narrow halachic purpose, and should not be read as a broader philosophical statement about the humanity of non-Jews — a position that is both textually and theologically untenable.

## Quantity Debate

### Kings & Kohanim

The *Tosefta* continues: *l'melachim u'l'kohanim* — if one anoints kings or *kohanim gedolim* a second time, after the initial anointing has already been performed, *Rabbi Meir mechayev* and *Rabbi Yehuda poter*. This is one debate. A second debate immediately follows: how much oil must be applied in order to transgress? *Rabbi Meir omer kol shehu* — even the smallest quantity — while *Rabbi Yehuda omer kezayit*, requiring an olive's bulk.

The *Gemara* notes an apparent internal contradiction: if Rabbi Yehuda just ruled that one is *patur* when anointing a king or *kohen gadol*, how can he simultaneously require a *kezayit*? The resolution is that these are two distinct debates. The question of whether kings and *kohanim* can transgress at all is one dispute. The question of how much oil is required when anointing an ordinary person who is prohibited (*hedyot*) is a separate dispute — and in that case, Rabbi Yehuda agrees that the person is *chayav*, since the prohibition is clear from the *pasuk*. The quantity debate pertains to that second case.

### Sicha vs. Netina

Rav Yosef identifies the crux of the quantity debate. The *pasuk* uses two different verbs: *al b'sar adam lo yisach* — the prohibition employs the word *yisach*, meaning to anoint (*sicha*) — while the *karet* clause says *va'asher yiten mimenu al zar*, using the word *netina*, meaning placing or giving. These two verbs carry different halachic valences.

### Deriving Kol Shehu

Rabbi Meir holds: *ma sicha kol shehu* — the word *sicha*, anointing, has no established minimum measure elsewhere in *halacha*, so we treat even the smallest amount as actionable. And since *netina* in this context is linked to and derived from *sicha*, *af netina kol shehu* — the act of placing is also governed by the same minimum-free standard.

### Rav Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that we interpret *netina* independently, in accordance with its standard halachic usage: *yalfinan netina de-al zar mi-netina de-alma* — we derive the meaning of "placing on a foreigner" from the general meaning of "placing" elsewhere. And the general meaning of *netina* in *halacha* is a *kezayit*, as derived from the context of *terumah*, where the obligation to return *terumah* to a *kohen* is linked to an act of eating (which requires a *kezayit*), and therefore the associated act of *netina* there also requires a *kezayit*. The same measure applies here.

### Two Debates

Rav Yosef emphasizes that this quantity dispute is specifically confined to the application of *shemen hamishcha* — whether one follows the *sicha*-based reading or the *netina*-based reading. However, *netina de-alma*, the ordinary meaning of *netina* in *halacha*, is agreed upon by all to require a *kezayit*. The two debates — one about kings and *kohanim*, and one about the quantity required for an ordinary prohibited person — are thus clearly delineated, with the quantity debate applying only to the latter category.

## Kings & Kohanim Debate

### R. Meir Position

The *Gemara* now returns to the debate about kings and *kohanim* and asks what its underlying logic is. Rabbi Meir holds: *asher yiten mimenu al zar ketiv u-melech v'kohen hashta zar ninhu* — the *pasuk* says one who places it on a *zar*, a foreigner. Once a king or *kohen gadol* has already been anointed and there is no longer any *mitzvah* to anoint him again, he has become, in effect, foreign to the oil — he has no right to receive it a second time. Therefore, anointing him again would constitute a transgression.

### R. Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *be'inan ad de-ika zarut mi-techilato v'ad sofo* — the term *zar* implies complete foreignness, from beginning to end. Since kings and *kohanim*, as a class, were originally entitled to be anointed with the *shemen hamishcha*, they can never be considered fully foreign to it. The word *zar* does not apply to someone who was ever legitimately within that category.

### Individual vs. Class

This raises an important question: is Rabbi Yehuda speaking about a specific individual who was personally anointed, or about membership in a class that was once anointed? The *Gemara* does not resolve this explicitly. One might wonder whether even a king who was never personally anointed — or a *kohen* who was never personally anointed — would nonetheless be exempt according to Rabbi Yehuda, because his class was once anointed. The case of *malchei Yisrael*, who as a class were generally not anointed with *shemen hamishcha*, would likely remain subject to the prohibition even according to Rabbi Yehuda.

Rav Ika the son of Rav Ami observes that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda are consistent with their respective positions elsewhere: *va'azlu l'ta'amaihu*.

### Kohen's Daughter

A *baraita* is cited to demonstrate this consistency. Consider the case of a *bat kohen* who married a *Yisrael*. While married to a non-*kohen*, she loses her right to eat *terumah*. If she then inadvertently eats *terumah*, she pays back the principal (*keren*) but not the added fifth (*chomesh*) — because the *chomesh* applies only to a *zar*, and she is not considered a *zarah*, given that she once had the status of a *bat kohen* and may return to it if her husband dies childless. Furthermore, if she commits adultery, her execution is by *sreifah*, as befits a *bat kohen*, not *chenek*. This is the ruling in the basic case.

However, if she married someone *pasul* — someone whose marriage disqualifies her permanently from *kohen* status, making her a *chalalah* — Rabbi Meir rules that she pays both the *keren* and the *chomesh*, and her execution would be by *chenek*, not *sreifah*. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that she retained *bat kohen* status only because of the possibility of returning to it; once that possibility is eliminated, she becomes a full *zarah*. The *chachamim*, whose position aligns with Rabbi Yehuda, rule that even in that case she pays only the *keren* and not the *chomesh*, and her execution is by *sreifah* — because having once been in the category of *bat kohen*, she can never be considered a full *zarah*, regardless of whether she can ever return to that status.

The parallel to the *shemen hamishcha* debate is direct: Rabbi Meir's position is forward-looking (does she still have a path back to the status?), while the *chachamim*/Rabbi Yehuda's position is backward-looking (was she ever in the category?). The same logic governs whether kings and *kohanim* can ever be considered *zarim* with respect to the *shemen hamishcha*.

## Shemen Hamishcha…

### R. Eliezer's Derasha

Returning to the Tosefta, *tani tanna kamei d'Rabbi Eliezer*: *kol sheyeshno b'sicha yeshno b'val yisach* — whoever is within the framework of the prohibition of *sicha* is also within the framework of the prohibition of *lo yisach*. Rabbi Eliezer responds to the *tanna* with approval: *shapir kamarta* — you have stated it well. The textual basis is the unusual spelling of the word *lo yisach* with a double *yud*, rendering it susceptible to a causative reading: not only is it prohibited to anoint oneself, but it is prohibited to cause it to be placed on the flesh of one who is subject to the prohibition. This reading simultaneously excludes non-Jews, since the prohibition does not fall upon them in the first place.

### Kohen Gadol Case

*Tani Rebbe Chanina kamei d'Rava*: Rebbe Chanina taught before Rava — from where do we know that a *kohen gadol* who takes oil from the *shemen hamishcha* that is on his own head and applies it to his body is liable? The verse states *al b'sar adam lo yisach*. The *Gemara* notes that this is operating within the framework of Rabbi Meir's position — that a *kohen gadol*, once anointed, is considered a *zar* with respect to further applications of the oil.

The more striking point is the specific case: what if the very oil that was just used to anoint the *kohen gadol* is then taken from his head and applied to his abdomen? It is the same oil, in the same moment. Rav Acha the son of Rava asked Rav Ashi: how is this different from the case of a *kohen* who rubs himself with *terumah* oil? In that case, we rule that even a *ben bito Yisrael* — a grandson who is a non-*kohen* — may cuddle against him or have contact with that oil without concern, because the *kohen's* use of the *terumah* oil has already been effected.

### Trumah vs. Shemen

The answer distinguishes the two cases on the basis of separate *pesukim*. By *terumah*, the verse states *umeisu bo ki yechalelu* — "they shall die for it because they have desecrated it." The key word is *yechalelu*: once the *kohen* has used the *terumah* oil on himself, it has been put to use for a human being, and that use desacralizes it — removes it from its *kedusha* status. It is, in effect, no longer *terumah* after the *kohen* has applied it, and so contact with it by a non-*kohen* is no longer a prohibited use of *terumah*.

By contrast, the *pasuk* describing the *kohen gadol* states *ki nezer shemen mishchas Elokav alav* — "because the crown of the anointing oil of his God is upon him." Even after it has been placed on his head, the Torah continues to call it *shemen mishchas Elokav* — the anointing oil of his God. *Shemen mishcha karei Rachmana* — the Torah designates it as *shemen hamishcha* even after it has been applied. *Af al gav d'isa alav lo itchal* — even though it is already upon him, it has not been desacralized. It retains its sacred status and therefore the prohibition remains active. Conceptually, the distinction tracks accordingly: with *terumah*, the sin consists in misusing a sacred object, and once used, the object no longer has that sacred status to misuse; with *shemen hamishcha*, the prohibition is not about misusing the oil's *kedusha* per se but about the specific act identified in the *pasuk*, which the oil retains its identity as *shemen hamishcha* throughout.

## Mishnah Conclusion

### Three Categories

The *Mishnah* closes with a general summary statement: *al elu chayavin al zedonam karet, v'al shigagatam chatat, v'al lo hoda shelahem asham talui* — for all the offenses on the list, intentional violation incurs *karet*, unintentional violation requires a *chatat*, and if one is uncertain whether the violation occurred, an *asham talui* is brought.

### Tuma'at Mikdash

The *Mishnah* then states an exception: *chutz mimitamei mikdash vekodashav* — with the exception of one who is *tamei* and enters the *Beis Hamikdash* or eats *kodashim* while *tamei*. For that transgression, a *korban oleh v'yored* is brought — a variable-cost *korban* whose value depends on the sinner's means. The *Gemara* asks: *mimai mapik lei* — from what exactly is this being excluded? The answer is: *chutz mimitamei mikdash vekodashav she'ein mevi asham talui* — this category is excluded specifically from the *asham talui*. The first two categories — *karet* for intentional violation and *chatat* for unintentional violation — still apply. But the *asham talui*, which is brought when one is uncertain whether a transgression occurred at all, applies only where the underlying *chatat* is a fixed-value offering (*chatat kavua*). Where the *chatat* varies in cost, the *asham talui* does not apply.

### Yom Kippur Q

The *Gemara* then raises a further question: given that the *Mishnah* is in the business of excluding categories from the *asham talui*, why does it not also mention the case of one who has passed through *Yom Kippur*? After *Yom Kippur* atones, the underlying sin for which an *asham talui* might have been brought no longer exists — and indeed, as the *Gemara* teaches elsewhere, the *asham talui* is not brought after *Yom Kippur* has intervened. The *Gemara* offers two approaches to this question, which draw the discussion into a much deeper inquiry about the nature and conditions of *Yom Kippur*'s atoning power.

---

## Yom Kippur Debate

### Atone w/o Teshuva?

Reish Lakish explains why *Yom Kippur* was not listed as an exclusion from *asham talui*: *ki katani heicha de'iteih l'chatat Rachmana patrei* — the *Mishnah* is discussing cases where the sin remains fully present and the person is still obligated in a *chatat*, yet is nonetheless excluded from *asham talui*. In the case of *tuma'at mikdash*, the sin exists but the exemption from *asham talui* is structural (because the *chatat* varies in cost). *Yom Kippur* is different — by *Yom Kippur*, the sin itself has been wiped away: *leiteih lechata'ei dekaper lei*. It is not another way of evading the *asham talui*; it is an erasure of the underlying transgression entirely.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different explanation. *Bimva'et* — there is a type of person who explicitly declares that he does not want *Yom Kippur* to atone for him: *dekamar ein Yom HaKippurim mechaper li*. For such a person, *Yom Kippur* would not atone, and he would still be obligated in his *asham talui* even after *Yom Kippur*. The *Mishnah* therefore cannot make a blanket exclusion for *Yom Kippur*, since the exclusion does not apply to this case. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *de'ei hadar beih batar Yom HaKippurim ba'ei le'atuyei asham talui* — if, after *Yom Kippur*, that person changes his mind and wants atonement after all, he would then need to bring his *asham talui*.

The implication of Rabbi Yochanan's answer is striking — it presupposes that a person's willingness to be atoned is a condition for *Yom Kippur* to function. Reish Lakish's answer implies no such thing; he simply distinguishes the structural character of the two exclusions. The *Gemara* proceeds to note that Reish Lakish does not adopt Rabbi Yochanan's answer because Reish Lakish holds the more radical position: *miva'et nami mechaper lei Yom HaKippurim* — even a person who says he does not want *Yom Kippur* to atone for him will be atoned for anyway.

### Willingness Question

This brings the *Gemara* to a parallel debate about the *chatat*. What if a person says: *tikarev v'lo titchaper* — bring the offering, but I do not want it to atone for me? Everyone agrees that if a person refuses entirely to have the *korban* brought — *lo tikarev* — it cannot atone, as the verse *yakriv oto* implies the offering must be brought with the consent of the owner (*lirtzono*). The debate concerns one who agrees to have it brought but withholds his consent to the atonement.

### Chatat & Will

Abaye holds: *ein mechaper* — it does not atone, because he has explicitly withheld his willingness. Rava holds: *mechaper* — it does atone, because *keivan de'amar tikarev kaparah mi-meila ati* — once he agrees to have it brought, the atonement follows automatically, regardless of his stated preference.

One way of understanding Rava's position draws on a point discussed earlier in *Keritot* regarding a *tinok she'nishba*, a child raised among non-Jews who violates *Shabbos* unknowingly and must bring a *chatat* despite having no culpability. The *chatat* in such a case is not about personal guilt — it is about the metaphysical fact of a sin having been committed in the world, which requires rectification independent of the sinner's inner state. On this model, even if a person does not want atonement, the bringing of the *korban* addresses the sin itself, not merely the person's guilt. This stands in contrast to an *asham*, which the *Gemara* elsewhere associates with *ashom asham la-Hashem* — it focuses on the person's state of guilt — whereas a *chatat* is a sin offering, addressing the act.

The *Gemara* then notes: *ve-hadar bei Rava* — Rava ultimately reversed his position.

### Baraitot Conflict

The reversal is prompted by a *baraita* from the *Sifra* (and a conflicting one): *yakhol yehei Yom HaKippurim mechaper al shavim v'al she'einam shavim* — perhaps *Yom Kippur* atones even for those who have not done *teshuvah*? A *kal va'chomer* might suggest this: just as a *chatat* and *asham* atone, and *Yom Kippur* atones — and just as a *chatat* and *asham* do not atone for intentional sins but only for unintentional ones, so too *Yom Kippur* should only atone for those who have done *teshuvah*. But one might counter: *chatat* and *asham* are limited in that they do not work for intentional sins; *Yom Kippur*, which does atone even for intentional sins, might be powerful enough to work even without *teshuvah*. The *Gemara* concludes: *talmud lomar ach* — the word *ach* in the *pasuk* *ach be'asor lachodesh hashevi'i* limits its scope. *Ach chalak* — it does not atone for those who have not done *teshuvah*.

A second *baraita* from the same *Sifra* appears to contradict this: *yakhol lo yehei Yom HaKippurim mechaper ela al she'nita'nah bo v'lo asa bo melacha v'kara'o mikra kodesh* — perhaps *Yom Kippur* only atones for one who fasted, refrained from *melacha*, and observed the day properly. What of one who did not fast, performed *melacha*, and failed to treat the day as sacred? *Talmud lomar Yom HaKippurim hu mikol makom* — it atones regardless. This is a striking ruling: one who violates *Yom Kippur* is nonetheless atoned for by it.

Since both *baraitot* are from the anonymous *Sifra* (attributed to Rabbi Yehuda), they cannot simply be assigned to different *tannaim*. The *Gemara* notes they are in tension: *kashya ahadadi*. One might distinguish the cases — perhaps it atones even if one violates it, provided one still wants it to work; but the *Gemara* treats these as part of a single continuum and declines to introduce such a middle position.

### Rabbi's Opinion

Abaye resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between two presentations within *Rabbi*'s teaching. One *baraita* records *Rabbi*'s ruling *aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda*, while the other reflects *Rabbi*'s own opinion. *Rabbi* himself holds: *kol aveiros sheba-Torah bein asa teshuvah bein lo asa teshuvah Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — for all transgressions in the Torah, *Yom Kippur* atones regardless of whether one has done *teshuvah*.

### Megaleh Panim

*Rabbi* lists three exceptions: *miforek ol* — one who has thrown off the yoke of Torah entirely; *megaleh panim ba-Torah* — one who acts brazenly against the Torah; and *meifer bris basar* — one who has annulled the covenant of circumcision through physical reversal. The phrase *megaleh panim ba-Torah* appears in *Pirkei Avot* and other *mishnayot* without the appended qualifier *shelo kehalacha* found in some later sources. The *Rambam* in his *Perush Hamishnayot* on *Pirkei Avot*, citing the *Yerushalmi*, interprets *megaleh panim* as a form of *azut panim* — brazenness — directed at the Torah, aligned with the verse *devar Hashem bazah*, which describes one who openly denigrates and attacks the Torah. This fits better with the plain context than the interpretation of making false *drashot*, and the *Rambam*'s reading raises the further question of how it differs from *porek ol* — though the distinction may be one of degree. Even for these extreme categories, *Rabbi* clarifies: *she'im asa teshuvah Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — if they do *teshuvah*, *Yom Kippur* will atone even for them.

### Rava Reversal

Rava offers a different resolution: both *baraitot* reflect *Rabbi*'s own opinion, and they are not in conflict. *U'modeh Rabbi b'aveirot d'Yom HaKippurim gufei d'lo mechaper* — *Rabbi* concedes that for sins involving *Yom Kippur* itself — actual violations of the day — *Yom Kippur* does not atone unless one has done *teshuvah* for those specific transgressions. The *baraita* that requires fasting and observance is speaking about the sins of the day itself; the *baraita* that says it works regardless is speaking about other sins committed during the year.

### Karet Challenge

This resolution prompts an acute question: if *Yom Kippur* atones even for one who violates it, how could one ever incur *karet* for violating *Yom Kippur*? The day itself would immediately atone for any transgression committed upon it. The *Gemara* offers several answers. If the violation occurs at night, the atonement — *itzumo shel yom mechaper* — takes effect only during the day, so one who sins at night and dies before dawn would incur *karet* without atonement. For sins committed during the day itself, the *Gemara* proposes scenarios in which the sinner dies at the moment of the transgression before the atonement can take effect: one who chokes while eating, one who performs *melacha* right up until *shekias hachamah*, or one who is killed mid-act — in each case, *d'lo havi lei sha'os d'yimama d'lechaper lei*, there was no subsequent moment for the day to atone.

The *Gemara*'s broader conclusion is striking: it aligns all the variables — whether *teshuvah* is required, whether observance of the day is required, whether wanting the atonement is required — into a single dichotomy. Either *Yom Kippur* works entirely regardless (*Rabbi*'s position taken to its logical extreme), or all those conditions apply together. What is notably absent from the *Gemara*'s discussion is the most intuitive question: what of a person who is willing to be atoned, who fasts and observes the day, but has not undergone an internal process of *teshuvah*? The *Gemara* does not address this directly, instead reducing the *shavim/she'einam shavim* distinction to a binary of whether the person explicitly accepts or rejects the atonement. The fasting and observance of *Yom Kippur* may perhaps serve as sufficient *teshuvah* in the case of a *shogeg*, but the *Gemara* leaves this largely implicit.

## Megadef

### Yidoni Question

Returning to the list, the *Gemara* raises the following question: the *Rabbanan* heard that Rabbi Akiva, in his enumeration, lists *ba'al ov* but not *yidoni*. The *Rabbanan* ask: *mai shna de-lo maisi korban* — why does *yidoni* not bring a *korban*? The answer offered is *mishum de-leis bei ma'aseh* — because there is no significant physical act involved in *yidoni* (which requires only placing a bone in the mouth). The *Rabbanan* then respond: *megadef nami leis bei ma'aseh* — but *megadef* also involves no action. Why does *megadef* bring a *korban* but not *yidoni*? This is the force of the word *af* in the earlier statement — *af megadef* should also be excluded.

Earlier in the *Gemara*, two explanations were offered for why Rabbi Akiva distinguishes between *yidoni* and *megadef*: either he requires some form of action but sets a minimal threshold, and the verbal act of *megadef* satisfies that threshold; or he does not require action at all, and excluded *yidoni* only because it shares a single *lav* with *ov*, such that one would not bring two separate *korbanos* for *ov* and *yidoni* together, though either alone would in principle require one.

### R. Akiva View

A *baraita* is cited: *megadef meivi korban ho'il u-va bo karet divrei Rabbi Akiva* — *megadef* brings a *korban* because *karet* is mentioned in connection with it, in the view of Rabbi Akiva.

### Not All Karet

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: is it really a general principle that wherever *karet* is written, a *korban* is brought? What of *pesach* and *milah*, where *karet* is stated and yet no *korban* is brought for failing to perform them? The answer is that those are violations of a *mitzvas asei* — a positive commandment — and the general rule linking *karet* to a *chatat* applies only to prohibitions (*lo ta'aseh*).

### Juxtaposition

Rabbi Akiva's position is then more precisely stated: *megadef meivi korban ho'il u-va bo ha-karet bi-mkom korban* — it is not simply that *karet* is mentioned, but that *karet* is specifically mentioned in the same passage (*Bamidbar* 15) that discusses bringing a *korban* for unintentional sin. The structure of the *pesukim* there is as follows: verse 27 states that if one sins unintentionally, he brings a *chatat* (*v'nefesh achas techeta bi-shgagah v'hikrivah ez bas shnasah l'chatat*). Verse 30 then states: *v'ha-nefesh asher ta'aseh be-yad ramah* — one who acts intentionally, *es Hashem hu megadef*, he is blaspheming God, *v'nichresah ha-nefesh ha-hi mi-kerev ammah*, he incurs *karet*. The intentional offender who gets *karet* is juxtaposed directly to the unintentional offender who brings a *chatat*. Since *megadef* and *karet* appear in the same *parasha* as the *korban*, Rabbi Akiva derives that *megadef* brings a *chatat* when done unintentionally.

### Et Hashem

The *Gemara* then asks: *mah hu megadef — mevarech es Hashem*? Is *megadef* in this *pasuk* actually referring to one who curses God? The *chachamim* respond that it is not. The phrase *es Hashem hu megadef* in *Bamidbar* 15 refers rather to a form of *avodah zarah* — not classic *avodah zarah*, which would not require a special verse, but a subsidiary or ritual form: perhaps singing, dancing, or performing some ceremonial act before an idol. This form of service would incur *karet* and, if done unintentionally, requires a *chatat* — but it is not the act of verbally cursing or blaspheming God. How then do we know that verbal blasphemy incurs *karet*? From a *gezeira shava*: the verse by the case of the *mekalel* (*Vayikra* 24, the incident of the *ben ha-Yisraelis*) says *v'nasa chet'o*, and the verse by *Pesach Sheni* also says *chet'o yisa*. Just as the latter carries *karet*, so too the *mekalel* incurs *karet* — but without the *korban* derivation, since that *pasuk* has been reassigned to *avodah zarah*.

### Issi & R. Elazar

Two *tannaim* offer characterizations of what *megadef* means, framing the debate in vivid terms. Issi ben Yehuda says the act of *megadef* is like someone who says to a fellow: *geirasta ha-ke'ara v'chisarta* — you scraped out the bowl and actually took material away from the bowl itself. The *megadef* has, as it were, attacked God directly — a profoundly violent image of verbal assault on the Divine. On this view, *megadef* means one who curses and blasphemes God.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah offers a contrasting image: *geirasta ha-ke'ara v'lo chisarta* — you scraped out the bowl but did not actually take material away from the bowl itself. You cleaned it out without damaging it. On his view, *megadef* refers to *oved avodah zarah* — an idolater. The act of *avodah zarah* is, paradoxically, depicted as less of a direct assault on God than cursing. Both *Rashi* and *Rabbeinu Gershom* understand this as saying that *avodah zarah* does not actually harm or diminish God in any direct sense, even though it is a categorical rejection of His sovereignty.

This distinction finds a striking parallel in the *Rambam*'s *Moreh Nevuchim*, where he states explicitly that holding false theological beliefs about God is worse than *avodah zarah*. His reasoning is that *avodah zarah* — whether in the form of worshipping God through images or worshipping other deities — does not necessarily corrupt one's core understanding of the Divine. It is, in his framework, a wrong act. But a false belief about God's nature — such as attributing corporeality to Him — is a fundamental distortion of one's conception of God Himself. The *Gemara* here seems to reflect a similar intuition: cursing God (*mevarech es Hashem*) involves a direct, intimate confrontation with the Divine, whereas *avodah zarah* involves a misdirection of worship, not necessarily an assault on God per se.

### Final Tanya

A final *baraita* crystallizes the dispute: *es Hashem hu megadef* — Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah says *be'oved avodah zarah ketiv*, the verse speaks about one who worships idols. *V'chachamim omrim* — the *chachamim* hold that the verse came only to assign *karet* to one who curses God (*l'mevareikh es Hashem*). Whether a *korban* accompanies that *karet*, and what exactly the verse's juxtaposition with the *chatat* passage establishes, is the substance of the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* running throughout this entire discussion.
