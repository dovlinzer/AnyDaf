# Keritot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemen Hamishcha

### Intro to Shemen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַסָּךְ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna includes in its list of those liable to receive *karet*: **And one who applies the anointing oil** to his skin.

The *Tosefta* enumerates the full list of offenses punishable by *karet*, and the *Gemara* — following its characteristic method — focuses on those items that receive less treatment elsewhere in *Shas*. Having addressed the making of *ketores* and the making of *shemen hamishcha* in previous discussions, the *Gemara* now turns to one who anoints himself with the *shemen hamishcha* of the *Beis Hamikdash* — not oil that he himself produced, but the actual consecrated anointing oil.

### Exemptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַסָּךְ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה לִבְהֵמָה וְכֵלִים – פָּטוּר, לְגוֹיִם וּלְמֵתִים – פָּטוּר. בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל בְּשַׂר אָדָם לֹא יִיסָךְ״, וּבְהֵמָה וְכֵלִים לָאו אָדָם הוּא. מֵתִים נָמֵי פָּטוּר, דְּכֵיוָן דְּמִית לֵיהּ – מֵת מִיקְּרֵי, וְלָאו אָדָם. אֶלָּא גּוֹיִם, אַמַּאי פָּטוּר? הָא אָדָם נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who applies the anointing oil to animals or vessels** is **exempt,** and one who applies it **to gentiles or to corpses** is **exempt.** The Gemara objects: **Granted,** one is exempt in the case of **animals and vessels, as it is written: "Upon the flesh of a person it shall not be applied"** (Exodus 30:32), **and animals and vessels are not** the flesh of **a person.** It is **also** clear why one is **exempt** if he applies it to **a corpse, as once someone has died,** the body **is called a corpse and not a person. But** if one applies anointing oil to **gentiles why** is he **exempt? Aren't they** included in the meaning of the term **person [*adam*]?**

A *Tosefta* is cited: *hasach b'shemen hamishcha l'vehema u'v'keilim patur* — if one uses the *shemen hamishcha* on an animal or on vessels, he is exempt from the specific prohibition. (Whether this would constitute *me'ilah* is a separate question, deliberately set aside here.) The prohibition derives from the phrase *al b'sar adam lo yisach* — "it shall not be placed on human flesh" — and since animals and vessels are not *adam*, they fall outside its scope. Similarly, applying it to a corpse (*meis*) incurs no liability, since once a person has died he is called a *meis* and is no longer classified as *adam*. The exemption for non-Jews, however, requires explanation, because non-Jews are in fact referred to as *adam* in Scripture.

### Non-Jews & Adam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאיֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַתֵּן צֹאנִי צֹאן מַרְעִיתִי אָדָם אַתֶּם״ – אַתֶּם קְרוּיִין ״אָדָם״ וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם קְרוּיִין ״אָדָם״. וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְנֶפֶשׁ אָדָם שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר אָלֶף״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא לְאַפּוֹקֵי בְּהֵמָה. וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַאֲנִי לֹא אָחוּס עַל נִינְוֵה וְגוֹ׳״! הַהוּא לְאַפּוֹקֵי בְּהֵמָה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא – כִּדְקָתָנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּסָךְ – יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּבַל יִיסָךְ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּסָךְ – אֵינוֹ בְּבַל יִיסָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Indeed** they are not. **As it is written: "And you My sheep, the sheep of My pasture, are people [*adam*]"** (Ezekiel 34:31), from which it is derived that **you,** the Jewish people, **are called *adam*, but gentiles are not called *adam*.** The Gemara raises an objection based on a verse discussing captives taken during the war against Midian. **But isn't it written: "And the people [*nefesh adam*] were sixteen thousand"** (Numbers 31:40). This indicates that gentiles are also referred to as *adam*. The Sage who was asked this **said to** the questioner: **That** term serves **to exclude,** i.e., to distinguish between the people who were taken captive and the **animals** that were taken as spoils of war, which are also mentioned in that verse. The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But isn't it written: "And should I not have pity on Nineveh,** that great city, wherein are more than one hundred and twenty thousand people [*adam*] who cannot discern between their right hand and their left hand, and also much cattle" (Jonah 4:11). The inhabitants of Nineveh were gentiles. The Gemara answers: **That** term serves **to exclude** them from the **animals** mentioned in the verse. **And if you wish, say** instead a different reason why one who applies anointing oil to gentiles is exempt. This is **as a *tanna* teaches before Rabbi Elazar: Anyone included in** the obligation not to **apply** anointing oil to himself or others **is** likewise **included** as subject to the prohibition of: **It shall not be applied,** i.e., it is prohibited to apply the oil to him. **And anyone not included in** the obligation not **to apply** anointing oil to himself or others **is not included** as the object of: **It shall not be applied.** Only Jews are included in the prohibition against applying the anointing oil.

This leads into one of the more striking and, as the lecturer notes, deeply unsettling discussions in *Shas* — the question of whether *chazal*, when interpreting a *pasuk* that uses the word *adam*, include or exclude non-Jews. *Tosafos* in several places in *Shas* grapples with the apparent inconsistency: sometimes the *Gemara* derives from *adam* that non-Jews are included, and sometimes it derives that they are excluded.

Here the *Gemara* begins by noting that non-Jews *are* called *adam*, then cites a *pasuk* from Yechezkel: *v'atem tzon mar'isi adam atem* — "you are my flock, the flock of my shepherding, you are *adam*" — from which it derives *atem keruyin adam v'ein ovdei kochavim keruyin adam*, that Israel is called *adam* and non-Jews are not. Two counter-proofs are then raised. First, *v'nefesh adam shisha asar eleph* — the sixteen thousand captives taken from Midyan are described as *adam*. The *Gemara* responds that this usage is merely contrastive with the animals captured alongside them, not a definitive application of the term. Second, *v'ani lo achus al Ninveh ha'ir hagedola asher yesh bah harbe adam* — the book of Yonah refers to the non-Jewish population of Nineveh as *adam*. Again, the *Gemara* responds that this too is contrastive with the *behema raba* mentioned in the same verse. In both cases, the word *adam* appears in proximity to *behema* and is understood only as a contrast to the animal category, not as a full application of the term to non-Jews.

A second, more elegant answer is then offered: *kol sheyeshno b'sicha yeshno b'val yisach* — anyone who is themselves subject to the prohibition of anointing with *shemen hamishcha* is also a prohibited object of that anointing. Since the prohibition of *shemen hamishcha* is part of the *taryag mitzvos* and does not apply to non-Jews through the *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach*, non-Jews are simply not within the framework of the prohibition at all — and therefore placing it on their flesh is also not prohibited.

The broader issue raised by this *Gemara* deserves acknowledgment. While there are real halachic distinctions between Jews and non-Jews, this must be understood in its proper context. Even Rabbi Akiva, who often emphasizes legal distinctions between Jews and non-Jews, is the same tanna who declares *chaviv adam shenivra b'tzelem* — that all human beings are precious by virtue of being created in the image of God — and who then adds *chavivim Yisrael shenikru banim lamakom*, underscoring the unique relationship of *Bnei Yisrael* with God while affirming the universal dignity of all people. The interpretation of *adam* to exclude non-Jews is a technical *derasha* serving a narrow halachic purpose, and should not be read as a broader philosophical statement about the humanity of non-Jews — a position that is both textually and theologically untenable.

## Quantity Debate

### Kings & Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַסָּךְ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה לִבְהֵמָה וְכֵלִים, לְגוֹיִם וּמֵתִים – פָּטוּר, לִמְלָכִים וּלְכֹהֲנִים – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר. וְכַמָּה יָסוּךְ וִיְהֵא חַיָּיב? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּזַיִת. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פָּטוּר! כִּי פָּטַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – גַּבֵּי מְלָכִים וְכֹהֲנִים, גַּבֵּי הֶדְיוֹט מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One who applies the anointing oil to animals or vessels,** or **to gentiles or** on **corpses,** is **exempt.** If one applies the anointing oil **to kings or to priests** after they had already been anointed, **Rabbi Meir deems** him **liable and Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **exempt. And how much** oil must one **apply** to his skin **and be liable? Rabbi Meir says: Any amount. Rabbi Yehuda says: An olive-bulk.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But doesn't Rabbi Yehuda say** that he is entirely **exempt?** The Gemara answers: **When Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **exempt** that is **with regard to** applying oil to **kings and priests,** whereas in the case **of an ordinary** person Rabbi Yehuda **deems** him **liable.**

The *Tosefta* continues: *l'melachim u'l'kohanim* — if one anoints kings or *kohanim gedolim* a second time, after the initial anointing has already been performed, *Rabbi Meir mechayev* and *Rabbi Yehuda poter*. This is one debate. A second debate immediately follows: how much oil must be applied in order to transgress? *Rabbi Meir omer kol shehu* — even the smallest quantity — while *Rabbi Yehuda omer kezayit*, requiring an olive's bulk.

The *Gemara* notes an apparent internal contradiction: if Rabbi Yehuda just ruled that one is *patur* when anointing a king or *kohen gadol*, how can he simultaneously require a *kezayit*? The resolution is that these are two distinct debates. The question of whether kings and *kohanim* can transgress at all is one dispute. The question of how much oil is required when anointing an ordinary person who is prohibited (*hedyot*) is a separate dispute — and in that case, Rabbi Yehuda agrees that the person is *chayav*, since the prohibition is clear from the *pasuk*. The quantity debate pertains to that second case.

### Sicha vs. Netina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּהָא פְּלִיגִי – רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: ״עַל בְּשַׂר אָדָם לֹא יִיסָךְ״ כְּתִיב, וּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן מִמֶּנּוּ עַל זָר״, מָה סִיכָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – אַף נְתִינָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: יָלְפִינַן נְתִינָה דְּעַל זָר מִנְּתִינָה דְּעָלְמָא, מָה נְתִינָה דְּעָלְמָא כְּזַיִת, אַף נְתִינָה דְּעַל זָר – כְּזַיִת. אֲבָל סִיכָה לְמִימְשַׁח מְלָכִים וְכֹהֲנִים – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל כׇּל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Concerning what** matter do **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda disagree? Rav Yosef says** that **they disagree concerning this** issue: **Rabbi Meir holds** that this *halakha* is based on the fact that **it is written: "Upon the flesh of a person it shall not be applied"** (Exodus 30:32), **and it is written: "Or whoever places any of it upon a stranger,** he shall be cut off from his people" (Exodus 30:33). The wording of the prohibition teaches with regard to the liability to receive *karet*: **Just as** the act of **applying** the oil to the skin prohibited by the verse refers to the application of **any amount,** as there is no specific measure stated in this regard, **so too,** the act of **placing** the oil mentioned with regard to *karet* refers to **any amount.** **And Rabbi Yehuda holds: We derive** the *halakha* of **placing [*netina*] that** is stated here, which is **upon a stranger,** i.e., one upon whom there is no mitzva to place the anointing oil, **from** the *halakha* of **giving [*netina*] in general. Just as giving in general** is with the amount of **an olive-bulk, so too, placing** anointing oil **upon a stranger** is with the amount of **an olive-bulk.** The principle that giving [*netina*] in general is with the amount of an olive-bulk is derived from the verse: "And if a man eats a sacred thing in error then he shall add a fifth part in addition to it, and shall give [*venatan*] to the priest the sacred item" (Leviticus 22:14). This verse is referring to an item given for eating, and the minimum amount that must be consumed for an act to be considered eating is an olive-bulk. Rav Yosef adds: **But** with regard to the mitzva of **applying** the oil in order **to anoint kings and priests, everyone agrees** it is accomplished with **any amount.**

Rav Yosef identifies the crux of the quantity debate. The *pasuk* uses two different verbs: *al b'sar adam lo yisach* — the prohibition employs the word *yisach*, meaning to anoint (*sicha*) — while the *karet* clause says *va'asher yiten mimenu al zar*, using the word *netina*, meaning placing or giving. These two verbs carry different halachic valences.

### Deriving Kol Shehu

Rabbi Meir holds: *ma sicha kol shehu* — the word *sicha*, anointing, has no established minimum measure elsewhere in *halacha*, so we treat even the smallest amount as actionable. And since *netina* in this context is linked to and derived from *sicha*, *af netina kol shehu* — the act of placing is also governed by the same minimum-free standard.

### Rav Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that we interpret *netina* independently, in accordance with its standard halachic usage: *yalfinan netina de-al zar mi-netina de-alma* — we derive the meaning of "placing on a foreigner" from the general meaning of "placing" elsewhere. And the general meaning of *netina* in *halacha* is a *kezayit*, as derived from the context of *terumah*, where the obligation to return *terumah* to a *kohen* is linked to an act of eating (which requires a *kezayit*), and therefore the associated act of *netina* there also requires a *kezayit*. The same measure applies here.

### Two Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּנְתִינַת שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה וּבְשִׁינּוּיֵי דְּשַׁנִּינַן, אֲבָל נְתִינָה דְּעָלְמָא – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** the **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda applies **to the placing of the anointing oil, and** it is explained **by** those **answers that we answered** earlier, that according to Rabbi Meir one is liable for the placing of any amount, as the verse uses an expression of applying, whereas according to Rabbi Yehuda one is liable only if one places oil that is the volume of an olive-bulk. **But** with regard to **placing in general,** e.g., the prohibition not to place frankincense on the meal offering of a sinner (see Leviticus 5:11), **everyone agrees** one is liable for placing only the volume of **an olive-bulk.**

Rav Yosef emphasizes that this quantity dispute is specifically confined to the application of *shemen hamishcha* — whether one follows the *sicha*-based reading or the *netina*-based reading. However, *netina de-alma*, the ordinary meaning of *netina* in *halacha*, is agreed upon by all to require a *kezayit*. The two debates — one about kings and *kohanim*, and one about the quantity required for an ordinary prohibited person — are thus clearly delineated, with the quantity debate applying only to the latter category.

## Kings & Kohanim Debate

### R. Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה גַּבֵּי מְלָכִים וְכֹהֲנִים? רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: ״וַאֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן מִמֶּנּוּ עַל זָר״ כְּתִיב, וּמֶלֶךְ וְכֹהֵן הַשְׁתָּא זָרִים נִינְהוּ. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: בָּעֵינַן עַד דְּאִיכָּא זָר מִתְּחִלָּתוֹ וְעַד סוֹפוֹ, וּמֶלֶךְ וְכֹהֵן מֵעִיקָּרָא לָאו זָרִים הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef** further **says: With regard to what** matter do **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda disagree** in the case **of kings and priests** who had already been anointed? **Rabbi Meir holds** that since **it is written: "Or whoever puts any of it upon a stranger," and** right **now** the **king and priest are strangers** because the mitzva to anoint them no longer applies, they are included in the prohibition. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds:** For the purposes of this prohibition **we require that** the individual in question **be a stranger from his beginning to his end, and** the **king and priest were initially not strangers.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the debate about kings and *kohanim* and asks what its underlying logic is. Rabbi Meir holds: *asher yiten mimenu al zar ketiv u-melech v'kohen hashta zar ninhu* — the *pasuk* says one who places it on a *zar*, a foreigner. Once a king or *kohen gadol* has already been anointed and there is no longer any *mitzvah* to anoint him again, he has become, in effect, foreign to the oil — he has no right to receive it a second time. Therefore, anointing him again would constitute a transgression.

### R. Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *be'inan ad de-ika zarut mi-techilato v'ad sofo* — the term *zar* implies complete foreignness, from beginning to end. Since kings and *kohanim*, as a class, were originally entitled to be anointed with the *shemen hamishcha*, they can never be considered fully foreign to it. The word *zar* does not apply to someone who was ever legitimately within that category.

### Individual vs. Class

This raises an important question: is Rabbi Yehuda speaking about a specific individual who was personally anointed, or about membership in a class that was once anointed? The *Gemara* does not resolve this explicitly. One might wonder whether even a king who was never personally anointed — or a *kohen* who was never personally anointed — would nonetheless be exempt according to Rabbi Yehuda, because his class was once anointed. The case of *malchei Yisrael*, who as a class were generally not anointed with *shemen hamishcha*, would likely remain subject to the prohibition even according to Rabbi Yehuda.

### Consistency with Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִיקָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי: וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: בַּת כֹּהֵן שֶׁנִּישֵּׂאת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאָכְלָה בִּתְרוּמָה – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינָהּ מְשַׁלֶּמֶת אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ, וּמִיתָתָהּ בִּשְׂרֵיפָה. נִיסֵּת לְאֶחָד מִן הַפְּסוּלִין – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וּמִיתָתָהּ בְּחֶנֶק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ מְשַׁלֶּמֶת [אֶת] הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינָהּ מְשַׁלֶּמֶת [אֶת] הַחוֹמֶשׁ, וּמִיתָתָהּ בִּשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ika, son of Rav Ami, said: And** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda both **follow their** lines of **reasoning, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Terumot* 7:2): With regard to **the daughter of a priest who married an Israelite and** then unwittingly **partook of *teruma*, she pays the principal,** as a thief would, as she partook of *teruma* to which she owns no rights. **But she does not pay the** additional **one-fifth** payment, which is the fine paid by an Israelite who partakes of *teruma* unwittingly (see Leviticus 22:14). This is because she is not completely disqualified from the priesthood, since if she becomes widowed or divorced without having borne children she will again be permitted to partake of *teruma*. **And** if she commits adultery **her death** penalty is administered **by burning,** as is the *halakha* with regard to the daughter of a priest (see Leviticus 21:9). By contrast, if **she married one of those who are unfit** for her to marry due to his lineage, thereby disqualifying herself from the priesthood for the future, **she pays** the **principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and her death** is **by strangulation,** as is the *halakha* with regard to Israelite women. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** **And the Rabbis say:** In **both this** case **and that** case, whether she was married to an Israelite or to one unfit for her to marry, **she pays the principal but she does not pay the** additional **one-fifth** payment, **and her death** is **by burning,** as she previously had the status of a daughter of the priesthood. This opinion of the Rabbis is presumably that of Rabbi Yehuda, who is usually the disputant of Rabbi Meir. As explained with regard to the anointing of a king, Rabbi Yehuda requires that the individual in question must have the status of a stranger, i.e., not a High Priest or a king, from beginning to end.

Rav Ika the son of Rav Ami observes that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda are consistent with their respective positions elsewhere: *va'azlu l'ta'amaihu*.

### Kohen's Daughter

A *baraita* is cited to demonstrate this consistency. Consider the case of a *bat kohen* who married a *Yisrael*. While married to a non-*kohen*, she loses her right to eat *terumah*. If she then inadvertently eats *terumah*, she pays back the principal (*keren*) but not the added fifth (*chomesh*) — because the *chomesh* applies only to a *zar*, and she is not considered a *zarah*, given that she once had the status of a *bat kohen* and may return to it if her husband dies childless. Furthermore, if she commits adultery, her execution is by *sreifah*, as befits a *bat kohen*, not *chenek*. This is the ruling in the basic case.

However, if she married someone *pasul* — someone whose marriage disqualifies her permanently from *kohen* status, making her a *chalalah* — Rabbi Meir rules that she pays both the *keren* and the *chomesh*, and her execution would be by *chenek*, not *sreifah*. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that she retained *bat kohen* status only because of the possibility of returning to it; once that possibility is eliminated, she becomes a full *zarah*. The *chachamim*, whose position aligns with Rabbi Yehuda, rule that even in that case she pays only the *keren* and not the *chomesh*, and her execution is by *sreifah* — because having once been in the category of *bat kohen*, she can never be considered a full *zarah*, regardless of whether she can ever return to that status.

The parallel to the *shemen hamishcha* debate is direct: Rabbi Meir's position is forward-looking (does she still have a path back to the status?), while the *chachamim*/Rabbi Yehuda's position is backward-looking (was she ever in the category?). The same logic governs whether kings and *kohanim* can ever be considered *zarim* with respect to the *shemen hamishcha*.

## Shemen Hamishcha's Sacred Status

### R. Eliezer's Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּסָךְ – יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּבַל יִיסָךְ, וְכׇל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּסָךְ – אֵינוֹ בְּבַל יִיסָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרַתְּ, ״לֹא יִיסָךְ״ כְּתִיב, וּקְרִי בֵּיהּ ״לֹא יַסִּיךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself: A *tanna* teaches** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Elazar: Anyone included in** the obligation not to **apply** anointing oil to himself or others **is** likewise **included** as the object of: **It shall not be applied,** i.e., it is prohibited to apply the oil to him. **And anyone not included in** the obligation not **to apply** anointing oil to himself or others **is not included** as the object of: **It shall not be applied.** Rabbi Elazar **said to** that *tanna*: **You are saying well,** as **it is written:** "Upon the flesh of a person **it shall not be applied [*lo yisakh*]"** (Exodus 30:32), **and** you **read into** the verse: ***Lo yasikh,** he shall not apply it to others. This dual reading indicates that one who is commanded not to apply the oil is the same as the one upon whom it is prohibited to apply the oil, as stated by the *tanna*.

Returning to the Tosefta, *tani tanna kamei d'Rabbi Eliezer*: *kol sheyeshno b'sicha yeshno b'val yisach* — whoever is within the framework of the prohibition of *sicha* is also within the framework of the prohibition of *lo yisach*. Rabbi Eliezer responds to the *tanna* with approval: *shapir kamarta* — you have stated it well. The textual basis is the unusual spelling of the word *lo yisach* with a double *yud*, rendering it susceptible to a causative reading: not only is it prohibited to anoint oneself, but it is prohibited to cause it to be placed on the flesh of one who is subject to the prohibition. This reading simultaneously excludes non-Jews, since the prohibition does not fall upon them in the first place.

### Kohen Gadol Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא: מִנַּיִן לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁנָּטַל מִשֶּׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה שֶׁעַל רֹאשׁוֹ וְנָתַן עַל בְּנֵי מֵעָיו, מִנַּיִן שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל בְּשַׂר אָדָם לֹא יִיסָךְ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: כֹּהֵן שֶׁסָּךְ בְּשֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, בֶּן בִּתּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִתְעַגֵּל בּוֹ וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥananya taught** a *halakha* **before Rava: From where** is it derived **with regard to a High Priest who took from the anointing oil that is on his head and placed it on his stomach; from where** is it derived **that he is liable?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "Upon the flesh of a person it shall not be applied"** (Exodus 30:32). **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: What is different between** this case and **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a priest who applied *teruma* oil** to himself, **the Israelite son of his daughter may rub against [*mitaggel*]** this oil **without concern** that he might be deriving benefit from *teruma*?

*Tani Rebbe Chanina kamei d'Rava*: Rebbe Chanina taught before Rava — from where do we know that a *kohen gadol* who takes oil from the *shemen hamishcha* that is on his own head and applies it to his body is liable? The verse states *al b'sar adam lo yisach*. The *Gemara* notes that this is operating within the framework of Rabbi Meir's position — that a *kohen gadol*, once anointed, is considered a *zar* with respect to further applications of the oil.

The more striking point is the specific case: what if the very oil that was just used to anoint the *kohen gadol* is then taken from his head and applied to his abdomen? It is the same oil, in the same moment. Rav Acha the son of Rava asked Rav Ashi: how is this different from the case of a *kohen* who rubs himself with *terumah* oil? In that case, we rule that even a *ben bito Yisrael* — a grandson who is a non-*kohen* — may cuddle against him or have contact with that oil without concern, because the *kohen's* use of the *terumah* oil has already been effected.

### Trumah vs. Shemen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם, ״וּמֵתוּ בוֹ כִּי יְחַלְּלוּהוּ״ כְּתִיב – כֵּיוָן דְּחַלְּלֵיהּ הָא אִיתַּחַיל, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה כְּתִיב: ״כִּי נֵזֶר [שֶׁמֶן מִשְׁחַת] אֱלֹהָיו עָלָיו״ – ״שֶׁמֶן מִשְׁחָה״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיתֵיהּ עָלָיו לָא אִיתַּחַיל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: There,** with regard to *teruma* oil, **it is written: "They will die through it if they profane it"** (Leviticus 22:9), and **since** the priest **has** already **profaned** the oil by using it, it is considered **profaned. But with regard to the anointing oil it is written: "For the consecration** of the anointing oil of **His God is upon him"** (Leviticus 21:12). **The Merciful One calls it anointing oil** even at this stage, to teach **that even though it is upon** the High Priest it is **not** considered **profaned,** and instead remains sacred.

The answer distinguishes the two cases on the basis of separate *pesukim*. By *terumah*, the verse states *umeisu bo ki yechalelu* — "they shall die for it because they have desecrated it." The key word is *yechalelu*: once the *kohen* has used the *terumah* oil on himself, it has been put to use for a human being, and that use desacralizes it — removes it from its *kedusha* status. It is, in effect, no longer *terumah* after the *kohen* has applied it, and so contact with it by a non-*kohen* is no longer a prohibited use of *terumah*.

By contrast, the *pasuk* describing the *kohen gadol* states *ki nezer shemen mishchas Elokav alav* — "because the crown of the anointing oil of his God is upon him." Even after it has been placed on his head, the Torah continues to call it *shemen mishchas Elokav* — the anointing oil of his God. *Shemen mishcha karei Rachmana* — the Torah designates it as *shemen hamishcha* even after it has been applied. *Af al gav d'isa alav lo itchal* — even though it is already upon him, it has not been desacralized. It retains its sacred status and therefore the prohibition remains active. Conceptually, the distinction tracks accordingly: with *terumah*, the sin consists in misusing a sacred object, and once used, the object no longer has that sacred status to misuse; with *shemen hamishcha*, the prohibition is not about misusing the oil's *kedusha* per se but about the specific act identified in the *pasuk*, which the oil retains its identity as *shemen hamishcha* throughout.

## Mishnah Conclusion

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כּוּ׳. קָתָנֵי: חוּץ מִמְּטַמֵּא מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו. מִמַּאי מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ? הָכִי קָתָנֵי: חוּץ מִמִּטַּמֵּא מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **For** any of **these** prohibitions, **one is liable** to receive *karet* **for its intentional** violation and to bring a sin offering for its unwitting violation. And for violation in a case where it is unknown to him whether he transgressed, he is liable to bring a provisional guilt offering. The Gemara notes that the mishna further **teaches:** This is the *halakha* for all the transgressions listed above **except for one who defiles the Temple,** i.e., he enters the Temple while ritually impure **or** renders **its consecrated items** ritually impure. The Gemara asks: **From what** *halakha* **does** the *tanna* **exclude** these cases? After all, one who enters the Temple while impure or renders its consecrated items impure is also liable to receive *karet*. The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna **is teaching: Except for one who defiles the Temple or** renders **its consecrated items** ritually impure, **as** he **does not bring a provisional guilt offering.**

The *Mishnah* closes with a general summary statement: *al elu chayavin al zedonam karet, v'al shigagatam chatat, v'al lo hoda shelahem asham talui* — for all the offenses on the list, intentional violation incurs *karet*, unintentional violation requires a *chatat*, and if one is uncertain whether the violation occurred, an *asham talui* is brought.

### Tuma'at Mikdash

The *Mishnah* then states an exception: *chutz mimitamei mikdash vekodashav* — with the exception of one who is *tamei* and enters the *Beis Hamikdash* or eats *kodashim* while *tamei*. For that transgression, a *korban oleh v'yored* is brought — a variable-cost *korban* whose value depends on the sinner's means. The *Gemara* asks: *mimai mapik lei* — from what exactly is this being excluded? The answer is: *chutz mimitamei mikdash vekodashav she'ein mevi asham talui* — this category is excluded specifically from the *asham talui*. The first two categories — *karet* for intentional violation and *chatat* for unintentional violation — still apply. But the *asham talui*, which is brought when one is uncertain whether a transgression occurred at all, applies only where the underlying *chatat* is a fixed-value offering (*chatat kavua*). Where the *chatat* varies in cost, the *asham talui* does not apply.

### Yom Kippur Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: חוּץ מִמִּי שֶׁעָבַר עָלָיו יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כִּי קָתָנֵי, הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לַחֲטָאֵהּ וְרַחֲמָנָא פַּטְרֵיהּ. עָבַר עָלָיו יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים לֵיתֵיהּ לַחֲטָאֵהּ, דְּקָא כַפַּר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let** the mishna **also teach: Except for one who** sinned and **Yom Kippur passed, as** he too **does not bring a provisional guilt offering. Reish Lakish said: When** the *tanna* **teaches** these exceptions he is referring to those cases **where there is a sin and** nevertheless **the Merciful One exempts him** from bringing a provisional guilt offering. By contrast, in a case where **Yom Kippur passed, there is no** remaining **sin, as** Yom Kippur **atoned for him,** i.e., for his sin.

The *Gemara* then raises a further question: given that the *Mishnah* is in the business of excluding categories from the *asham talui*, why does it not also mention the case of one who has passed through *Yom Kippur*? After *Yom Kippur* atones, the underlying sin for which an *asham talui* might have been brought no longer exists — and indeed, as the *Gemara* teaches elsewhere, the *asham talui* is not brought after *Yom Kippur* has intervened. The *Gemara* offers two approaches to this question, which draw the discussion into a much deeper inquiry about the nature and conditions of *Yom Kippur*'s atoning power.

## Yom Kippur Debate

### Atone w/o Teshuva?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בִּמְבַעֵט, דְּקָאָמַר: אֵין יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר, דְּאִי הָדַר בֵּיהּ בָּתַר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, בָּעֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי אָשָׁם תָּלוּי. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סָבַר: מְבַעֵט נָמֵי מְכַפַּר עֲלֵיהּ יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The mishna is referring **to one who rejects** the atonement of Yom Kippur, **who says: Yom Kippur does not atone** for one's sins. In this situation Yom Kippur does not atone for him, which means **that if he retracted** from his sinful ways **after Yom Kippur** he is **required to bring a provisional guilt offering.** Therefore, it cannot be listed in the exceptions stated in the mishna. The Gemara notes: **And Reish Lakish** does not explain the mishna in this manner, as **he maintains** that **even** with regard to **one who rejects** its atonement, **Yom Kippur atones for his** sins.

Reish Lakish explains why *Yom Kippur* was not listed as an exclusion from *asham talui*: *ki katani heicha de'iteih l'chatat Rachmana patrei* — the *Mishnah* is discussing cases where the sin remains fully present and the person is still obligated in a *chatat*, yet is nonetheless excluded from *asham talui*. In the case of *tuma'at mikdash*, the sin exists but the exemption from *asham talui* is structural (because the *chatat* varies in cost). *Yom Kippur* is different — by *Yom Kippur*, the sin itself has been wiped away: *leiteih lechata'ei dekaper lei*. It is not another way of evading the *asham talui*; it is an erasure of the underlying transgression entirely.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different explanation. *Bimva'et* — there is a type of person who explicitly declares that he does not want *Yom Kippur* to atone for him: *dekamar ein Yom HaKippurim mechaper li*. For such a person, *Yom Kippur* would not atone, and he would still be obligated in his *asham talui* even after *Yom Kippur*. The *Mishnah* therefore cannot make a blanket exclusion for *Yom Kippur*, since the exclusion does not apply to this case. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *de'ei hadar beih batar Yom HaKippurim ba'ei le'atuyei asham talui* — if, after *Yom Kippur*, that person changes his mind and wants atonement after all, he would then need to bring his *asham talui*.

The implication of Rabbi Yochanan's answer is striking — it presupposes that a person's willingness to be atoned is a condition for *Yom Kippur* to function. Reish Lakish's answer implies no such thing; he simply distinguishes the structural character of the two exclusions. The *Gemara* proceeds to note that Reish Lakish does not adopt Rabbi Yochanan's answer because Reish Lakish holds the more radical position: *miva'et nami mechaper lei Yom HaKippurim* — even a person who says he does not want *Yom Kippur* to atone for him will be atoned for anyway.

### Willingness Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא: הָאוֹמֵר: ״לֹא יִתְכַּפֵּר לִי חַטָּאתִי״, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֵינָהּ מְכַפֶּרֶת, רָבָא אָמַר: מְכַפֶּרֶת. הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר: ״לֹא תִּיקְרַב״ – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא מְכַפְּרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״יַקְרִיב אוֹתוֹ״ – לִרְצוֹנוֹ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי, דְּאָמַר: ״תִּיקְרַב וְלֹא תְּכַפֵּר״, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֵין מְכַפֶּרֶת, דְּהָא אָמַר לֹא תְּכַפֵּר. רָבָא אָמַר: מְכַפֶּרֶת, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר תִּיקְרַב – כַּפָּרָה מִמֵּילָא (אָתֵי).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** their dispute is **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute** between later *amora'im*: Concerning **one who says: My sin offering,** which is sacrificed for me, **should not atone for me, Abaye said:** This sin offering **does not atone** for him. **Rava said: It atones** for him. The Gemara explains: In a case **where he said:** I do **not** want it **to be sacrificed, everyone agrees that it does not atone** for him, **as it is written: "He shall bring it in accordance with his will"** (Leviticus 1:3), which indicates that if the offering is brought against his will it is not effective. **Where they disagree** is **when he says:** The sin offering **should be sacrificed but it should not atone** for me. **Abaye said:** It **does not atone** for him, **as he said** that **it should not atone** for him. **Rava said: It does atone** for him, as **once he says** it **should be sacrificed,** the **atonement comes by itself.**

This brings the *Gemara* to a parallel debate about the *chatat*. What if a person says: *tikarev v'lo titchaper* — bring the offering, but I do not want it to atone for me? Everyone agrees that if a person refuses entirely to have the *korban* brought — *lo tikarev* — it cannot atone, as the verse *yakriv oto* implies the offering must be brought with the consent of the owner (*lirtzono*). The debate concerns one who agrees to have it brought but withholds his consent to the atonement.

### Chatat & Will

Abaye holds: *ein mechaper* — it does not atone, because he has explicitly withheld his willingness. Rava holds: *mechaper* — it does atone, because *keivan de'amar tikarev kaparah mi-meila ati* — once he agrees to have it brought, the atonement follows automatically, regardless of his stated preference.

One way of understanding Rava's position draws on a point discussed earlier in *Keritot* regarding a *tinok she'nishba*, a child raised among non-Jews who violates *Shabbos* unknowingly and must bring a *chatat* despite having no culpability. The *chatat* in such a case is not about personal guilt — it is about the metaphysical fact of a sin having been committed in the world, which requires rectification independent of the sinner's inner state. On this model, even if a person does not want atonement, the bringing of the *korban* addresses the sin itself, not merely the person's guilt. This stands in contrast to an *asham*, which the *Gemara* elsewhere associates with *ashom asham la-Hashem* — it focuses on the person's state of guilt — whereas a *chatat* is a sin offering, addressing the act.

### Baraitot Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא, כִּדְתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהֵא יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר עַל שָׁבִין וְעַל שֶׁאֵין שָׁבִין? וְדִין הוּא, וּמָה חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מְכַפְּרִין וְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר, מָה חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם אֵין מְכַפְּרִין אֶלָּא עַל הַשָּׁבִין אַף יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אֵין מְכַפֵּר אֶלָּא עַל הַשָּׁבִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rava retracted** his opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **Yom Kippur atones for those who repent and for those who do not repent.** The *baraita* elaborates: **And there is a logical inference** to negate this assertion: **Just as a sin offering and a guilt offering atone, and** likewise **Yom Kippur atones, just as a sin offering and a guilt offering atone only for those who repent, so too, Yom Kippur atones only for those who repent.**

The reversal is prompted by a *baraita* from the *Sifra* (and a conflicting one): *yakhol yehei Yom HaKippurim mechaper al shavim v'al she'einam shavim* — perhaps *Yom Kippur* atones even for those who have not done *teshuvah*? A *kal va'chomer* might suggest this: just as a *chatat* and *asham* atone, and *Yom Kippur* atones — and just as a *chatat* and *asham* do not atone for intentional sins but only for unintentional ones, so too *Yom Kippur* should only atone for those who have done *teshuvah*. But one might counter: *chatat* and *asham* are limited in that they do not work for intentional sins; *Yom Kippur*, which does atone even for intentional sins, might be powerful enough to work even without *teshuvah*. The *Gemara* concludes: *talmud lomar ach* — the word *ach* in the *pasuk* *ach be'asor lachodesh hashevi'i* limits its scope. *Ach chalak* — it does not atone for those who have not done *teshuvah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם, שֶׁאֵין מְכַפְּרִים עַל הַמֵּזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג, תֹּאמַר בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, שֶׁמְּכַפֵּר עַל הַמֵּזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג. וְהוֹאִיל וּמְכַפֵּר עַל הַמֵּזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג, מְכַפֵּר עַל שָׁבִין וְעַל שֶׁאֵין שָׁבִין! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַךְ״, חָלַק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this opinion: **No, if you said** this is the *halakha* **with regard to a sin offering and a guilt offering, which do not atone for intentional** sins **as** they do for **unwitting** sins, **shall you** also **say** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to Yom Kippur, which does atone for intentional** sins **as** it does for **unwitting** sins? Yom Kippur effects atonement even in cases where offerings do not. **And since** it is the case that the atonement of Yom Kippur is far-reaching in that **it atones for intentional** sins **as** it does for **unwitting** sins, it follows that it **may atone** both **for those who repent and for those who do not repent.** To dispel this notion, **the verse states: "Yet** on the tenth day of this month it is Yom Kippur" (Leviticus 23:27). By means of the word "yet," the verse **divided** and limited the atonement of Yom Kippur so that it atones only for those who repent. This concludes the *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר אֶלָּא עַל שֶׁנִּתְעַנָּה בּוֹ וְלֹא עָשָׂה בּוֹ מְלָאכָה וּקְרָאוֹ מִקְרָא קוֹדֶשׁ, לֹא נִתְעַנָּה בּוֹ וְעָשָׂה בּוֹ מְלָאכָה וְלֹא קְרָאוֹ מִקְרָא קוֹדֶשׁ יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא יוֹם כִּיפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים הוּא״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ סְתָם סִיפְרָא הוּא, קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of this *baraita* by comparing it to another *baraita*. **And** the Sages **raise a contradiction:** One **might** have thought that **Yom Kippur shall atone only for** one **who fasted on it and did not perform labor on it and declared it a holy convocation.** With regard to one who **did not fast on it, or performed labor on it, or did not declare it a holy convocation,** one **might** have thought that **Yom Kippur shall not atone** for him. To counter this **the verse states:** "Yet on the tenth day of this seventh month **it is Yom Kippur"** (Leviticus 23:27); the additional emphasis of "it is" serves to teach that the day atones **in any case.** The ruling of this *baraita* disputes the one cited earlier, which states that Yom Kippur atones only for those who repent, **and both** are **unattributed** *baraitot* in the ***Sifra*.** They **are difficult,** as they contradict **each other.**

A second *baraita* from the same *Sifra* appears to contradict this: *yakhol lo yehei Yom HaKippurim mechaper ela al she'nita'nah bo v'lo asa bo melacha v'kara'o mikra kodesh* — perhaps *Yom Kippur* only atones for one who fasted, refrained from *melacha*, and observed the day properly. What of one who did not fast, performed *melacha*, and failed to treat the day as sacred? *Talmud lomar Yom HaKippurim hu mikol makom* — it atones regardless. This is a striking ruling: one who violates *Yom Kippur* is nonetheless atoned for by it.

Since both *baraitot* are from the anonymous *Sifra* (attributed to Rabbi Yehuda), they cannot simply be assigned to different *tannaim*. The *Gemara* notes they are in tension: *kashya ahadadi*. One might distinguish the cases — perhaps it atones even if one violates it, provided one still wants it to work; but the *Gemara* treats these as part of a single continuum and declines to introduce such a middle position.

### Rabbi's Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – רַבִּי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא – רַבִּי אַלִּיבָּא דִּידֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל עֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, בֵּין עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה וּבֵין לֹא עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה – יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר,
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. This** first *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda,** and **that** second *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **in accordance with his own** opinion. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** For **all transgressions that are** stated **in the Torah, whether one repented or whether one did not repent, Yom Kippur atones.**

Abaye resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between two presentations within *Rabbi*'s teaching. One *baraita* records *Rabbi*'s ruling *aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda*, while the other reflects *Rabbi*'s own opinion. *Rabbi* himself holds: *kol aveiros sheba-Torah bein asa teshuvah bein lo asa teshuvah Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — for all transgressions in the Torah, *Yom Kippur* atones regardless of whether one has done *teshuvah*.

### Megaleh Panim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִפּוֹרֵק עוֹל, וּמְגַלֶּה פָּנִים בַּתּוֹרָה, וּמֵפֵר בְּרִית בָּשָׂר, שֶׁאִם עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה – יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi continues: This is the *halakha* **except for** one **who divests** himself of the **yoke** of God, by denying His existence, **and one who** impudently **reveals facets of the Torah** in a manner that departs from their true meaning, **and one who nullifies the covenant of** the **flesh,** i.e., circumcision. **With regard to** these, **if one repented, Yom Kippur atones, and if not, Yom Kippur does not atone.** This indicates that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, Yom Kippur atones even if one did not repent.

*Rabbi* lists three exceptions: *miporek ol* — one who has thrown off the yoke of Torah entirely; *megaleh panim ba-Torah* — one who acts brazenly against the Torah; and *meifer bris basar* — one who has annulled the covenant of circumcision through physical reversal. The phrase *megaleh panim ba-Torah* appears in *Pirkei Avot* and other *mishnayot* without the appended qualifier *shelo kehalacha* found in some later sources. The *Rambam* in his *Perush Hamishnayot* on *Pirkei Avot*, citing the *Yerushalmi*, interprets *megaleh panim* as a form of *azut panim* — brazenness — directed at the Torah, aligned with the verse *devar Hashem bazah*, which describes one who openly denigrates and attacks the Torah. This fits better with the plain context than the interpretation of making false *drashot*, and the *Rambam*'s reading raises the further question of how it differs from *porek ol* — though the distinction may be one of degree. Even for these extreme categories, *Rabbi* clarifies: *she'im asa teshuvah Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — if they do *teshuvah*, *Yom Kippur* will atone even for them.

### Rava Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי אַלִּיבָּא דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי בַּעֲבֵירוֹת דְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים גּוּפֵיהּ – דְּלָא מְכַפַּר. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, כָּרֵת דְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לְרַבִּי, כֵּיוָן דִּבְכֹל שַׁעְתָּא וְשַׁעְתָּא מְכַפַּר, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּילְמָא דַּעֲבַד עֲבִידְתָּא כּוּלֵּי לֵילְיָא וּבַהֲדֵי עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר מִית, דְּלָא הֲוָה יְמָמָא דִּלְכַפַּר לֵיהּ! תִּינַח כָּרֵת דְּלֵילְיָא, כָּרֵת דִּימָמָא הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּילְמָא בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָכַל נַהֲמָא חֲנַקְתֵּיהּ אוּמְצָא, דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ שְׁהוּת בִּימָמָא דִּלְכַפַּר לֵיהּ. אִי נָמֵי, דַּעֲבַד עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה. אִי נָמֵי, בַּהֲדֵי דְּעָבֵיד עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ פַּסְקֵיהּ מָרָא לְשָׁקֵיהּ וּמִית, דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ שְׁהוּת בִּימָמָא דִּלְכַפַּר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **in accordance with his own** opinion, that Yom Kippur atones even for those who do not repent, **but** even **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes** that **with regard to** the **transgressions of** violating **Yom Kippur itself,** e.g., if one ate or performed labor on Yom Kippur, **that** Yom Kippur **does not atone** for those transgressions. He must necessarily concede this point, **as if you do not say so,** then **according to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, with regard to the punishment of ***karet* for** transgressing the prohibitions of **Yom Kippur, since each and every hour** of the day **atones** for one's sins, **how can you find** the application of *karet* in this case? The Gemara questions this proof: **And what is the difficulty? Perhaps while eating bread he choked on** a chunk of **meat** he ate with it, and died, **as there was not enough time in the day** after his transgression **to atone for him. Alternatively,** it is referring to a case where **he performed labor close to sunset; alternatively,** it is referring to a case where **as he was performing labor, the hoe** with which he was working **cut his thigh and he died, as** in these cases too **there was no time** in the day after his transgression **to atone for him,** either because it was no longer Yom Kippur or because he died immediately.

Rava offers a different resolution: both *baraitot* reflect *Rabbi*'s own opinion, and they are not in conflict. *U'modeh Rabbi b'aveirot d'Yom HaKippurim gufei d'lo mechaper lei Yom HaKippurim* — *Rabbi* concedes that for sins involving *Yom Kippur* itself — actual violations of the day — *Yom Kippur* does not atone unless one has done *teshuvah* for those specific transgressions. The *baraita* that requires fasting and observance is speaking about the sins of the day itself; the *baraita* that says it works regardless is speaking about other sins committed during the year.

### Karet Challenge

This resolution prompts an acute question: if *Yom Kippur* atones even for one who violates it, how could one ever incur *karet* for violating *Yom Kippur*? The day itself would immediately atone for any transgression committed upon it. The *Gemara* offers several answers. If the violation occurs at night, the atonement — *itzumo shel yom mechaper* — takes effect only during the day, so one who sins at night and dies before dawn would incur *karet* without atonement. For sins committed during the day itself, the *Gemara* proposes scenarios in which the sinner dies at the moment of the transgression before the atonement can take effect: one who chokes while eating, one who performs *melacha* right up until *shekias hachamah*, or one who is killed mid-act — in each case, *d'lo havi lei sha'os d'yimama d'lechaper lei*, there was no subsequent moment for the day to atone.

The *Gemara*'s broader conclusion is striking: it aligns all the variables — whether *teshuvah* is required, whether observance of the day is required, whether wanting the atonement is required — into a single dichotomy. Either *Yom Kippur* works entirely regardless (*Rabbi*'s position taken to its logical extreme), or all those conditions apply together. What is notably absent from the *Gemara*'s discussion is the most intuitive question: what of a person who is willing to be atoned, who fasts and observes the day, but has not undergone an internal process of *teshuvah*? The *Gemara* does not address this directly, instead reducing the *shavim/she'einam shavim* distinction to a binary of whether the person explicitly accepts or rejects the atonement. The fasting and observance of *Yom Kippur* may perhaps serve as sufficient *teshuvah* in the case of a *shogeg*, but the *Gemara* leaves this largely implicit.

## Megadef

### Yidoni Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים אַף הַמְגַדֵּף כּוּ׳. מַאי אַף הַמְגַדֵּף שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה? רַבָּנַן שְׁמַעוּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּתָנֵי בַּעַל אוֹב וְלָא תָּנֵי יִדְּעוֹנִי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא דְּלָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה? מְגַדֵּף נָמֵי לֵית בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* is the same, i.e., there is no obligation to bring a sin offering, **even** with regard to **the one who blasphemes,** as it is stated with regard to the sin offering: "You shall have one law for him who performs the action unwittingly" (Numbers 15:29), excluding one who blasphemes, as he does not perform an action but sins with speech. The Gemara asks: **What** caused the Rabbis to specify: **Even** one who **blasphemes, as he does not perform an action?** Why do the Rabbis mention this explanation? The Gemara explains: **The Rabbis heard that Rabbi Akiva teaches** in his list of those who must bring a sin offering **a necromancer, and he does not teach a sorcerer** in his list, and therefore **they said to him: What is different** about a sorcerer **that he does not bring an offering?** It must be **due to** the fact **that** his transgression **does not involve an action.** If so, with regard to the sin of one who **blasphemes as well,** it **does not involve an action.**

Returning to the list, the *Gemara* raises the following question: the *Rabbanan* heard that Rabbi Akiva, in his enumeration, lists *ba'al ov* but not *yidoni*. The *Rabbanan* ask: *mai shna de-lo maisi korban* — why does *yidoni* not bring a *korban*? The answer offered is *mishum de-leis bei ma'aseh* — because there is no significant physical act involved in *yidoni* (which requires only placing a bone in the mouth). The *Rabbanan* then respond: *megadef nami leis bei ma'aseh* — but *megadef* also involves no action. Why does *megadef* bring a *korban* but not *yidoni*? This is the force of the word *af* in the earlier statement — *af megadef* should also be excluded.

Earlier in the *Gemara*, two explanations were offered for why Rabbi Akiva distinguishes between *yidoni* and *megadef*: either he requires some form of action but sets a minimal threshold, and the verbal act of *megadef* satisfies that threshold; or he does not require action at all, and excluded *yidoni* only because it shares a single *lav* with *ov*, such that one would not bring two separate *korbanos* for *ov* and *yidoni* together, though either alone would in principle require one.

### R. Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְגַדֵּף מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֱמַר בּוֹ כָּרֵת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְנָשָׂא חֶטְאוֹ״. וּכְלָלָא הוּא, כֹּל הֵיכָא דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ כָּרֵת מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן? וְהָא פֶּסַח וּמִילָה דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ כָּרֵת וְלָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:1): One who unwittingly **blasphemes brings an offering, since *karet* is stated with regard to it.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And** the verse **states:** "Whoever curses his God **shall bear his sin"** (Leviticus 24:15), as the Gemara will explain. The Gemara asks: **And is it** an established **principle** that **wherever it is written *karet* with regard to** a mitzva, one who violates it unwittingly **brings an offering? But there** is the case of the mitzva of the **Paschal offering, and** the mitzva of **circumcision, as** the punishment of ***karet* is written** for failing to perform **them, and** yet **one does not bring an offering** for failing to perform these mitzvot unwittingly.

A *baraita* is cited: *megadef meivi korban ho'il u-va bo karet divrei Rabbi Akiva* — *megadef* brings a *korban* because *karet* is mentioned in connection with it, in the view of Rabbi Akiva.

### Not All Karet

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: is it really a general principle that wherever *karet* is written, a *korban* is brought? What of *pesach* and *milah*, where *karet* is stated and yet no *korban* is brought for failing to perform them? The answer is that those are violations of a *mitzvas asei* — a positive commandment — and the general rule linking *karet* to a *chatat* applies only to prohibitions (*lo ta'aseh*).

### Juxtaposition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: מְגַדֵּף מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן הוֹאִיל וּבָא בּוֹ כָּרֵת בִּמְקוֹם קׇרְבָּן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. קָסָבַר: מִיגּוֹ דְּבָעֵי מִכְתַּב כָּרֵת בְּעָלְמָא, וּכְתִיב כָּרֵת בִּמְקוֹם קׇרְבָּן, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this** is what Rabbi Akiva **is saying:** One who unwittingly **blasphemes brings an offering, since its** punishment of ***karet* comes,** i.e., is written, **in a place** where the Torah discusses **an offering,** i.e., *karet* is mentioned in a passage that discusses a sin offering (see Numbers 15:27–31). This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** as **he maintains: Since** the verse **should have written *karet* in general,** i.e., without connecting it to bringing an offering, **and** yet this ***karet* is written in a place** where the Torah discusses **an offering, conclude from it** that the unwitting blasphemer **brings an offering** for his transgression.

Rabbi Akiva's position is then more precisely stated: *megadef meivi korban ho'il u-va bo ha-karet bi-mkom korban* — it is not simply that *karet* is mentioned, but that *karet* is specifically mentioned in the same passage (*Bamidbar* 15) that discusses bringing a *korban* for unintentional sin. The structure of the *pesukim* there is as follows: verse 27 states that if one sins unintentionally, he brings a *chatat* (*v'nefesh achas techeta bi-shgagah v'hikrivah ez bas shnasah l'chatat*). Verse 30 then states: *v'ha-nefesh asher ta'aseh be-yad ramah* — one who acts intentionally, *es Hashem hu megadef*, he is blaspheming God, *v'nichresah ha-nefesh ha-hi mi-kerev ammah*, he incurs *karet*. The intentional offender who gets *karet* is juxtaposed directly to the unintentional offender who brings a *chatat*. Since *megadef* and *karet* appear in the same *parasha* as the *korban*, Rabbi Akiva derives that *megadef* brings a *chatat* when done unintentionally.

### Et Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״חֶטְאוֹ יִשָּׂא״. אֲתָא[ן] לְרַבָּנַן, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבָּנַן: אָמְרִיתוּ מְגַדֵּף לֵית בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה, מַהוּ מְגַדֵּף – מְבָרֵךְ אֶת הַשֵּׁם. אֶלָּא כָּרֵת דִּכְתִיב, לְמַאי אֲתָא? אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: לִיתֵּן כָּרֵת לִמְקַלֵּל, דִּכְתִיב בִּמְקַלֵּל: (״חֶטְאוֹ יִשָּׂא הָאִישׁ הַהוּא״) [״וְנָשָׂא חֶטְאוֹ״], וּכְתִיב בְּפֶסַח שֵׁנִי: ״חֶטְאוֹ יִשָּׂא״, מָה לְהַלָּן – כָּרֵת, אַף כָּאן – כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the next clause of the *baraita*: **And** the verse **states:** "Whoever curses his God **shall bear his sin"** (Leviticus 24:15). The Gemara explains: Here we **arrive at** the opinion of **the Rabbis, and this** is what **Rabbi Akiva is saying to the Rabbis: You say** that the transgression of one who **blasphemes does not involve an action,** as **what is** the case of one who **blasphemes?** It is one who **blesses,** i.e., curses, **the Name,** i.e., God. **But** if so, then concerning the punishment of ***karet* that is written:** "That person blasphemes the Lord; and that soul shall be cut off [*venikhreta*] from among his people" (Numbers 15:30), **for what** purpose **does it come,** if not to render him liable to bring an offering? The Rabbis **say to him:** It comes **to give** the punishment of ***karet* to one who curses** God, in order to teach that the phrase: "Shall bear his sin," written in the verse: "Whoever curses his God shall bear his sin" (Leviticus 24:15), is referring to *karet*, so that one can derive by verbal analogy that an individual who was obligated to bring a Paschal offering for the second *Pesaḥ* and did not do so is likewise liable to receive *karet*. **As it is written with regard to one who curses** God: "Whoever curses his God **shall bear his sin," and it is written with regard to** one who was obligated to bring a Paschal offering for the **second *Pesaḥ*** and did not do so: "That man **shall bear his sin"** (Numbers 9:13). **Just as there,** with regard to one who curses God it is referring to the punishment of ***karet*, so too here,** with regard to the Paschal offering it is referring to the punishment of ***karet*.**

The *Gemara* then asks: *mah hu megadef — mevarech es Hashem*? Is *megadef* in this *pasuk* actually referring to one who curses God? The *chachamim* respond that it is not. The phrase *es Hashem hu megadef* in *Bamidbar* 15 refers rather to a form of *avodah zarah* — not classic *avodah zarah*, which would not require a special verse, but a subsidiary or ritual form: perhaps singing, dancing, or performing some ceremonial act before an idol. This form of service would incur *karet* and, if done unintentionally, requires a *chatat* — but it is not the act of verbally cursing or blaspheming God. How then do we know that verbal blasphemy incurs *karet*? From a *gezeira shava*: the verse by the case of the *mekalel* (*Vayikra* 24, the incident of the *ben ha-Yisraelis*) says *v'nasa chet'o*, and the verse by *Pesach Sheni* also says *chet'o yisa*. Just as the latter carries *karet*, so too the *mekalel* incurs *karet* — but without the *korban* derivation, since that *pasuk* has been reassigned to *avodah zarah*.

### Issi & R. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶת ה׳ [הוּא] מְגַדֵּף״, אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּאָדָם הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״גֵּירַפְתָּה הַקְּעָרָה וְחִיסַּרְתָּהּ״. קָסָבַר: מְגַדֵּף – מְבָרֵךְ אֶת הַשֵּׁם הוּא. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: כְּאָדָם הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״גֵּירַפְתָּה הַקְּעָרָה וְלֹא חִיסַּרְתָּהּ״. קָסָבַר: מְגַדֵּף – הַיְינוּ עוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who blasphemes, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "That person **blasphemes [*megaddef* ] the Lord"** (Numbers 15:30). **Isi ben Yehuda says:** This is **like a person who says to another: You cleaned [*geirafta*] the bowl and rendered it lacking,** i.e., the transgression of blasphemy is so severe that it is compared to one who does actual damage to God. Isi ben Yehuda **maintains** that the case of the **blasphemer is** identical to that of one who **blesses,** i.e., curses, **the Name,** i.e., God, which is a particularly severe transgression. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says** that this is **like a person who says to another: You cleaned the bowl** and removed its contents, **but did not render it lacking,** i.e., the transgression of blasphemy is not compared to one who does actual damage to God. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya **maintains** that the case of the **blasphemer is** the same as that of **an idol worshipper,** which is a less severe transgression.

Two *tannaim* offer characterizations of what *megadef* means, framing the debate in vivid terms. Issi ben Yehuda says the act of *megadef* is like someone who says to a fellow: *geirasta ha-ke'ara v'chisarta* — you scraped out the bowl and actually took material away from the bowl itself. The *megadef* has, as it were, attacked God directly — a profoundly violent image of verbal assault on the Divine. On this view, *megadef* means one who curses and blasphemes God.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah offers a contrasting image: *geirasta ha-ke'ara v'lo chisarta* — you scraped out the bowl but did not actually take material away from the bowl itself. You cleaned it out without damaging it. On his view, *megadef* refers to *oved avodah zarah* — an idolater. The act of *avodah zarah* is, paradoxically, depicted as less of a direct assault on God than cursing. Both *Rashi* and *Rabbeinu Gershom* understand this as saying that *avodah zarah* does not actually harm or diminish God in any direct sense, even though it is a categorical rejection of His sovereignty.

This distinction finds a striking parallel in the *Rambam*'s *Moreh Nevuchim*, where he states explicitly that holding false theological beliefs about God is worse than *avodah zarah*. His reasoning is that *avodah zarah* — whether in the form of worshipping God through images or worshipping other deities — does not necessarily corrupt one's core understanding of the Divine. It is, in his framework, a wrong act. But a false belief about God's nature — such as attributing corporeality to Him — is a fundamental distortion of one's conception of God Himself. The *Gemara* here seems to reflect a similar intuition: cursing God (*mevarech es Hashem*) involves a direct, intimate confrontation with the Divine, whereas *avodah zarah* involves a misdirection of worship, not necessarily an assault on God per se.

### Final Tanya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״אֶת ה׳״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: בְּעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לִיתֵּן כָּרֵת לִמְבָרֵךְ הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** This dispute as to the nature of the transgression of the blasphemer **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: "That person blasphemes **the Lord"** (Numbers 15:30), and **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: The verse is speaking of an idol worshipper. And the Rabbis say: The verse comes only to give** the punishment of ***karet* to one who blesses,** i.e., curses, **the Name,** i.e., God.

A final *baraita* crystallizes the dispute: *es Hashem hu megadef* — Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah says *be'oved avodah zarah ketiv*, the verse speaks about one who worships idols. *V'chachamim omrim* — the *chachamim* hold that the verse came only to assign *karet* to one who curses God (*l'mevareikh es Hashem*). Whether a *korban* accompanies that *karet*, and what exactly the verse's juxtaposition with the *chatat* passage establishes, is the substance of the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* running throughout this entire discussion.
