# Pesachim 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs Rav Kahana

### 50-50 Status

The daf opens, continuing from 79b, with a fundamental debate between Rav and Rav Kahana concerning the case where the *tzibur* is divided exactly fifty-fifty between *tahor* and *tamei* individuals. The question is whether such a split counts as a *rov* — a majority — for purposes of determining whether the *korban pesach* is brought as a *pesach b'tumah*.

### Rav's View

Rav holds that *palga u-palga* — an exact fifty-fifty split — is treated as a *rov*. His ruling is: *hallalu osin le-atzmam ve-hallalu osin le-atzmam* — each half acts independently. The fifty percent who are *tahor* bring on their own, and the fifty percent who are *tamei* are not pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*. The *tamei* half constitutes a sufficient *kahal* to bring *Pesach Rishon* on its own, even though it is not large enough to pull the *tahor* half into a *pesach b'tumah* framework.

### Rav Kahana View

Rav Kahana disputes this, holding that *machetza al machetza* does not qualify as a *rov*, though his position comes in two versions. The precise implications of that disagreement will be developed below.

### Mishnah Analysis

The *Gemara* attempts to derive support from the *mishnah*, noting the classic problem that many *mishnayot* present: they address the case of a *rov* and a *miut* but pass over the intermediate case of fifty-fifty entirely. The *mishnah* states that if the entire congregation or its majority — *nitmah hakahal o rubo* — became *tamei*, or if the *kohanim* were *tamei* while the congregation was *tahor*, then *ya'aseh b'tumah*, the offering is brought in a state of *tumah*. The implication appears to be that only a majority of *tamei* suffices — *rubo hu de-avdi bi-tumah* — and that a fifty-fifty split would not trigger the *pesach b'tumah* dispensation. This would seem to pose a difficulty for Rav, who treats the fifty percent *tamei* as independently entitled to bring on *Pesach Rishon*.

### Mishnah Supports Rav

Rav responds that the *mishnah* is entirely consistent with his position, provided one reads it carefully. When there is a *rov tamei*, the entire community does *pesach b'tumah* together — *avdi kulu bi-tumah*. In a fifty-fifty split, *hallalu osin le-atzmam ve-hallalu osin le-atzmam*: each half does it by itself. The *mishnah*'s silence on fifty-fifty is not an implicit rejection of this arrangement; the *mishnah* simply did not address it. The *Gemara* affirms that this reading is indeed plausible — *hachi nami mistabra* — pointing to the *seifa* of the *mishnah*, which states that if a *miut* became *tamei*, the *tahor* people bring on *Pesach Rishon* while the *tamei* people bring on *Pesach Sheni*: *tehorin osin et ha-rishon u-tme'im osin et ha-sheni*. The implication is *miut hu de-avdi be-sheni* — only a minority is pushed off to *Pesach Sheni* — whereas in a fifty-fifty case, according to Rav, the *tamei* half does not get pushed off at all but rather brings *Pesach Rishon* on its own. The *mishnah* works cleanly according to Rav: a majority means everyone does *pesach b'tumah* together; a minority means the *tamei* do *Pesach Sheni*; and the unmentioned fifty-fifty case is the one where each group acts independently.

### Rav Kahana Versions

The *Gemara* notes that this same analysis poses a difficulty for Rav Kahana, since the *mishnah*'s *seifa* implies that a *miut* is specifically what triggers the *Pesach Sheni* obligation — yet Rav Kahana, in at least one version of his position, holds that even a fifty-fifty split sends the *tamei* people to *Pesach Sheni*. The *Gemara* therefore distinguishes between Rav Kahana's two formulations.

According to Rav Kahana's second version (*lishna batra*), in a fifty-fifty case the *tamei* people bring neither *Pesach Rishon* nor *Pesach Sheni* — *einan osin lo et ha-rishon ve-lo et ha-sheni*. They are not a majority and therefore cannot bring on the first; they are not a *miut* and therefore cannot bring on the second. On this reading, the *mishnah* poses no difficulty: when the *tamei* people are a *miut*, they do *Pesach Sheni*; when they are precisely fifty percent, they fall between the two categories and lose out entirely. The *mishnah*'s formulation highlights the *miut* case precisely because it is the favorable one — the fifty-fifty case is the one in which the *tamei* people are excluded from both.

### Mishnah Reconciled

The difficulty is with Rav Kahana's first version (*lishna kama*), which holds that even in a fifty-fifty case the *tahor* people bring on *Pesach Rishon* and the *tamei* people bring on *Pesach Sheni* — *tehorin osin et ha-rishon u-tme'im osin et ha-sheni*. On this view, the *mishnah* should have illustrated its rule using the fifty-fifty case, not just the *miut* case. Rav Kahana's response is that the rule stated in the *seifa* is equally true in the fifty-fifty case — *ihu ha-din de-afilu palga u-palga nami* — but the *mishnah* chose to formulate it using the *miut* case in order to parallel the *reisha*, which speaks of a majority. Just as the *reisha* requires a *rov tamei* to trigger *pesach b'tumah* for all, the *seifa* correspondingly speaks of a *miut tamei* as the condition for *Pesach Sheni*. The middle case of fifty-fifty was omitted by the *mishnah* for stylistic reasons of contrast, but in truth even fifty percent *tamei* would do *Pesach Sheni* according to this version of Rav Kahana. On this reading, all three positions — Rav and the two versions of Rav Kahana — can be reconciled with the *mishnah*.

## Three Baraitot

### Baraita for Rav

The *Gemara* now observes that we possess three *baraitot*, each of which supports a different one of the three positions just laid out. The first *baraita* is introduced as *tanya kevata de-Rav* — a *baraita* that accords with Rav's ruling. It states: if the Jewish people were exactly half *tahor* and half *tamei* — *harei she-hayu Yisrael mechetza tehorin u-mechetza tme'im* — then *hallalu osin le-atzmam ve-hallalu osin le-atzmam*, each group brings on its own. The fifty percent *tamei* are sufficient to constitute a *kahal* for purposes of *Pesach Rishon*, but do not define the *tahor* half.

### Baraita for RK1

The second *baraita* supports Rav Kahana's first version: in a fifty-fifty case, the *tahor* people bring on *Pesach Rishon* and the *tamei* people, since they are not a majority, are treated as a *miut* and bring on *Pesach Sheni* — *tehorin osin et ha-rishon u-tme'im osin et ha-sheni*.

### Baraita for RK2

The third *baraita* supports Rav Kahana's second version, which represents the more novel ruling. In a fifty-fifty case, the *tahor* people bring *Pesach Rishon*, while the *tamei* people — being neither large enough to constitute the *kahal* nor small enough to qualify as a *miut* — fall precisely between the two categories: *einan osin lo et ha-rishon ve-lo et ha-sheni*, they bring neither.

## Reconciling Baraitot

### Explaining RK1 Baraita

The *Gemara* will not rest content with simply noting that each position has a supporting *baraita* while two *baraitot* oppose it. Since each *Amora* is committed to a single ruling, they must find a way to reconcile all three *baraitot* with their respective positions. The *Gemara* therefore undertakes this exercise systematically.

The first challenge: according to Rav and according to Rav Kahana's second version, what is to be done with the *baraita* that says that in a fifty-fifty case the *tamei* people bring *Pesach Sheni*? According to Rav they should be bringing *Pesach Rishon*; according to Rav Kahana's second version they should be bringing neither. How do these two positions explain away (*heichi matretzei lah*) the *baraita* that places them in *Pesach Sheni*?

### Women's Role

The *Gemara* answers: *kegon she-hayu Yisrael mechetza tehorim u-mechetza tme'im* — it looks like a fifty-fifty case — *ve-nashim mashlimot le-tme'im*, but what creates the appearance of fifty percent *tamei* is that women's votes were included in the count. This introduces an important distinction regarding women's obligation in the *korban pesach*. The *baraita* in question operates according to the view that women's participation in *Pesach Rishon* is *reshut* — optional, not obligatory — *nashim be-rishon reshut*. If women are not obligated, then when determining the composition of the *tzibur* for purposes of this calculation, one looks only at those who bear a genuine obligation: the men.

### Recounting Without Women

Once women are removed from the count — *dal nashim mi-tme'im* — and correspondingly removed from both columns, the true numbers emerge: among the men alone, the *tamei* group constitutes a *miut*. Thus, *havu lehu tme'im miyuta* — the *tamei* people are in fact a minority — *u-miyuta yidchu le-Pesach Sheni*, and it is on account of this minority status that they bring *Pesach Sheni*. The *baraita* is therefore not a genuine fifty-fifty case at all; it only appears that way because the women's count was initially included. In reality, among those actually obligated, the *tamei* group is a minority, and the *baraita* is fully consistent with the positions of Rav and Rav Kahana's second version.

### Rav on RK2 Baraita

The *Gemara* turns to the more difficult challenge. According to Rav and according to Rav Kahana's first version, what is to be done with the *baraita* that says in a fifty-fifty case the *tamei* people bring neither *Pesach Rishon* nor *Pesach Sheni* — *tehorim osin et ha-rishon ve-tme'im ein osin lo et ha-rishon ve-lo et ha-sheni*? According to Rav, the *tamei* half should be doing *Pesach Rishon*; according to Rav Kahana's first version, they should be doing *Pesach Sheni*. Neither position accommodates a total exclusion from both. The *Gemara* asks: *heichi mitartzei lah* — how do they explain it away?

Rav's explanation again manipulates the numbers by distinguishing between which population is counted at which moment. The scenario: it appears to be fifty-fifty, but this time — *ve-nashim odfot al ha-tehorim* — the women's presence is what makes it look like fifty-fifty on the side of the *tahor* people. Without the women, the men's vote would yield a majority *tamei*. This *baraita* operates according to the view that women are obligated in *Pesach Rishon* — *nashim be-rishon chovah* — so they are counted for that calculation. But women are not obligated in *Pesach Sheni* — *u-ve-sheni reshut* — and so are excluded from that calculation.

### Retroactive Census

This generates a striking and genuinely unusual result. For the purposes of *Pesach Rishon*, women are counted. With women included, the *tahor* side constitutes a majority and the *tamei* side a minority. Therefore the *tamei* people, being a *miut*, cannot bring *Pesach Rishon* — *rishon lo avdi de-havu lehu miuta* — and are pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*. But when *Pesach Sheni* arrives, the question becomes: who is now obligated, and what were the relevant numbers a month ago? For *Pesach Sheni*, women are not obligated, so one looks back at the composition of the community through the male-only lens. And looking back through that lens, the *tamei* people were a majority — *dal nashim minaihu, havu lehu tme'im ruba* — and *ruba lo avdi be-sheni*, a majority cannot bring *Pesach Sheni*.

The result is deeply counterintuitive. At the time of *Pesach Rishon*, the *tamei* people were counted as a *miut* because women were included in the census; so they were denied *Pesach Rishon*. At the time of *Pesach Sheni*, one looks back at the same community but now counts only the men — and through that retroactive male-only lens, the *tamei* group turns out to have been a *rov*, disqualifying them from *Pesach Sheni* as well. The numbers are genuinely the same underlying reality, but the applicable census methodology differs depending on which *Pesach* one is assessing, since women's obligation differs between the two. The result is that the same community ends up excluded from both — not because they fall between two categories, but because the category they belong to shifts depending on which obligation is being measured.

### RK on RK2 Baraita

Rav Kahana's explanation of the same *baraita* is structurally parallel but employs the inverse scenario. In this version, *ve-nashim mashlimot le-tehorim* — the women's count is what makes the *tahor* side appear to constitute fifty percent. Without the women, the men's count alone would show a minority *tahor* and a majority *tamei*. The *baraita* operates according to the view that women are obligated in *Pesach Rishon* but optional for *Pesach Sheni* — the same *sevara* as before.

For *Pesach Rishon*, counting women, the *tamei* side constitutes exactly fifty percent — *de-havu lehu palga u-palga*. For Rav Kahana, fifty percent *tamei* does not qualify for *Pesach Rishon* — *be-rishon palga lo avdi*. For *Pesach Sheni*, one looks back through the male-only lens, and now the *tamei* side turns out to have been a majority of the men — *dal nashim mineihu, havu lehu tme'im ruba* — and a majority cannot bring *Pesach Sheni*. The *tamei* people are thus excluded from both. The mechanism is the same: the census methodology changes between *Pesach Rishon* and *Pesach Sheni* because women's obligation changes, and this shifting methodology produces a double exclusion.

### RK's Challenge

The third *baraita* — the one supporting Rav's position, which allows each group to bring independently — now poses a challenge to Rav Kahana. However one adjusts the numbers using the women's count, the *baraita* explicitly envisions a scenario in which half the community brings as one *kahal* and the other half brings separately. Rav Kahana fundamentally rejects the idea that *Klal Yisrael* can be split in this way, such that one group brings *Pesach Rishon* in one mode while another group simultaneously brings in a different mode. No number manipulation can explain away a *baraita* that directly endorses that very possibility.

### RK Concedes

Rav Kahana therefore concedes that this *baraita* represents a genuinely opposing *tannaic* view — *tanna'ei hi*, it is a dispute among *Tannaim*. There is a *Tanna* who holds *mechetza al mechetza ke-rov* — that a fifty-fifty split is treated as a *rov* — which produces the result of each half acting as its own independent community. Rav Kahana himself holds *mechetza al mechetza eino ke-rov*, which leads, in his two versions, either to *Pesach Sheni* for the *tamei* half or to exclusion from both. He can reconcile the two *baraitot* that align with his position through the manipulation of the women's census; the third he must acknowledge as representing the *tannaic* view that he disputes.

## Detailed Baraita

### Majority Tamei

The *Gemara* proceeds to examine the full text of one of the *baraitot* in detail. It begins with the straightforward case: if the *tamei* people outnumbered the *tahor* — *hayu tme'im odfim al ha-tehorim* — then even if the margin was a single individual — *afilu echad* — everyone brings *pesach b'tumah*: *ya'asu be-tumah*.

### No Splitting Korban

The *baraita* grounds this rule in the principle *she-ein korban tzibbur chaluk* — a communal offering cannot be divided. This principle now carries greater logical weight in context than it did when first introduced. In the fifty-fifty case discussed above, splitting was tolerated because there was no majority to unify the *tzibur* in a single direction. But once a majority — even by one — exists, one cannot try to protect the *tahor* minority by separating them from the *tamei* majority. The principle of *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* demands that everyone proceed together, and therefore everyone brings *pesach b'tumah*.

### RE ben Matya

Rabbi Eliezer ben Matya dissents from the rule that a single individual tips the scales: *ein ha-yachid machria et ha-tzibbur le-tumah*. He derives this from the *pasuk* (cited at the beginning of 80a): *lo tuchal lizbo'ach et ha-pesach be-achad bishe'arecha* — one person cannot determine that the entire *tzibur* will bring *pesach b'tumah*.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon introduces a further dimension. Even if the *tamei* group does not constitute a majority of the entire people, if they constitute an entire *shevet* — a tribal unit — they are entitled to bring *Pesach Rishon* on their own, while the remaining *shevatim*, who are *tahor*, bring in *taharah*: *hallalu osin le-atzmam ve-hallalu osin le-atzmam*. The reason is *kasavar shevet echad ikrei kahal* — Rabbi Shimon holds that even a single *shevet* qualifies as a *kahal*, consistent with the discussion in *Horayot* regarding the *par helam davar shel tzibbur* and whether a single tribe can constitute the *kahal* that triggers that communal atonement. One *shevet* is sufficient to constitute a *kahal* that may bring *Pesach Rishon* independently, but it is not large enough to impose *pesach b'tumah* on everyone else. The arrangement therefore parallels the fifty-fifty case: each unit functions as its own *tzibur*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda takes this logic in the opposite direction. Even if only a single *shevet* is *tamei* while all the remaining *shevatim* are *tahor*, everyone brings *pesach b'tumah*: *ya'asu be-tumah* — because *she-ein korban tzibbur chaluk*. Rabbi Yehuda agrees with Rabbi Shimon that one *shevet* constitutes a *kahal*, and therefore that *kahal* is entitled to bring *pesach b'tumah*. But Rabbi Yehuda adds the principle that once any *kahal* brings *b'tumah*, the prohibition against dividing the *korban tzibbur* applies to all of *Klal Yisrael*: once one group has activated the *tumah* dispensation, the entire community must bring together in *tumah*.

## Tipping Scales

### Rav's Method

Having established that a fifty-fifty split allows each group to act independently, the *Gemara* considers what should be done practically to avoid such a split. Rav proposes that when the community is exactly evenly divided, one of the *tahor* individuals should be made *tamei* — *metam'im echad mehem be-sheretz* — thereby tipping the balance to a majority *tamei* and enabling everyone to bring together in *tumah*.

### Gemara Challenge

The *Gemara* immediately presses the question: why bother? Rav himself holds that in a fifty-fifty case *hallalu osin le-atzmam ve-hallalu osin le-atzmam* — each group brings on its own. Why go to the trouble of artificially creating a majority *tamei*? Let each half do what it does and leave it at that.

The *Gemara* initially attempts to say that perhaps the case is not perfectly fifty-fifty but rather has one extra person in the *tamei* column already — *de-hayu tme'im odfim al ha-tehorim echad* — but that one individual's vote is insufficient to tip the scales per the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Matya. The *Gemara* rejects this: if there is one extra, the *tamei* are already a *rov*; simply bring *pesach b'tumah*. And if one person doesn't count, the situation is effectively fifty-fifty again — so again, why not simply split?

### Reconciliation

The *Gemara* resolves the matter by positing a specific combination of opinions. If one holds, like Rav, that *machetza al mechetza* is treated as *ke-rov* — such that the fifty percent *tamei* is in principle entitled to bring *Pesach Rishon* — but simultaneously holds, like Rabbi Yehuda, that *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* — that the *tzibur* cannot be divided under any circumstances — then one arrives at a genuine impasse. The fifty percent *tamei* cannot be pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*, since they are treated as a *rov* entitled to bring on the first; yet they are also not large enough to draw the *tahor* half into a *pesach b'tumah*; and the principle of *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* precludes any split. The only available resolution is to engineer an actual majority: make one more person *tamei*, thereby producing an unambiguous *rov tamei* and enabling everyone to bring *pesach b'tumah* together. Once that majority is established, *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* takes full effect and the entire community brings together without difficulty.

The underlying tension is also worth noting: there is an extensive body of discussion about *tumah dechuya b'tzibur* — the view that *tumah* in the *tzibur* is tolerated rather than permitted outright — which would suggest a preference to minimize exposure to *tumah*. Yet once the decision is made to bring *pesach b'tumah*, the *Gemara* here appears to assume without further qualification that everyone may become *tamei*, without requiring any effort to minimize it. How that sits alongside the *dechuya/hutra* framework is left unexplored in this context.

### Ulla's Method

Ulla offers an alternative to Rav's approach. Rather than rendering one *tahor* person *tamei*, Ulla proposes sending one of the *tahor* individuals away on a distant journey — *meshalechin echad mehem le-derech rechoka* — thereby removing that person from the calculation. With one fewer *tahor* person in *Yerushalayim*, the remaining *tamei* group constitutes a majority, and the community brings *pesach b'tumah* as a unit.

### Ulla's Problem

The *Gemara* challenges Ulla on two grounds. First, it asks: why not simply follow Rav's solution of making a *tahor* person *tamei be-sheretz*? The *Gemara* explains that Ulla holds *shochtin ve-zorkin al tamei sheretz* — that one who is *tamei sheretz* may still have the *korban pesach* slaughtered and its blood sprinkled on his behalf, since he will become *tahor* by nightfall. In other words, making someone *tamei sheretz* does not genuinely remove them from the *tahor* side of the calculation, since they will be able to eat the *pesach* that night. To create a genuine *tamei* person who cannot bring, one would need to make them *tamei* through a *meit*, but that would prevent them from bringing their *korban chagigah* on the first day of *Yom Tov*.

The *Gemara* asks: why not simply let this person bring the *chagigah* later in the *Yom Tov* week, since there are seven days during which it may be offered? To this the *Gemara* responds: then the same objection applies to Ulla's solution — send the person away, and he can bring *Pesach Sheni*; make him *tamei be-meit*, and he can bring the *chagigah* on the seventh day of *Pesach*, when he will have completed seven days of *tumah* and become *tahor*. The *Gemara* therefore notes that making someone *tamei be-meit* is actually preferable to sending them away, since the person made *tamei* still participates in the communal *pesach b'tumah*, whereas the person sent away misses the *korban pesach* entirely.

Ulla's rationale for nevertheless preferring his solution rests on his view regarding the *chagigah*: *kullan tashlumin de-rishon ninhu* — all the subsequent days of *Yom Tov* on which the *chagigah* may be brought are understood as makeup opportunities for the first day. This means that only someone who was genuinely obligated and capable on the first day may bring on subsequent days. Someone who was *tamei* on *Rosh Yom Tov* — the first day of the festival — is, in Ulla's view, not in the category of those who could have brought that day, and therefore cannot avail themselves of the makeup days: *kol de-lo chazi be-rishon, lo chazi be-kulhu* — whoever was not fit on the first day is not fit on any subsequent day. Therefore, making someone *tamei be-meit* would permanently deny them the *chagigah*, which Ulla considers a graver consequence than simply delaying their *pesach* to *Pesach Sheni*.

### Practical Reality

Rav Nachman dismisses Ulla's solution on entirely practical grounds. He instructs his students: *zilu ve-imru lei le-Ulla* — go tell Ulla — *man tzayit lach de-akar shichei ve-masganei be-rahi* — who is going to listen to you and uproot his tent pegs and run out of *Yerushalayim* on *Erev Pesach*? Someone who has made the pilgrimage to *Yerushalayim* for the *korban pesach* is not going to simply walk away on the eve of the holiday on the strength of a theoretical legal solution. Rav Nachman regards the suggestion as impractical regardless of its theoretical validity.

## Zavin & Tamei

### Rav on Zavin Case

A new case is introduced: *hayu ruban zavin u-mi'utan tmei meitim* — the majority of the community were *zavin* and the minority were *tamei meitim*. This scenario requires some background. The dispensation of *tumah be-tzibur* — whereby *tumah* in the community overrides the normal prohibition against bringing a *korban* in *tumah* — applies specifically to the type of *tumah* that could, in principle, permit a communal offering to be brought *b'tumah*. *Zavin* — individuals with *tumah* rooted in bodily flux — represent a category of *tumas ha-guf* that is not subject to the *tumah be-tzibur* dispensation even if they constitute a majority. Their *tumah* is too severe; one does not say *tumah hutra b'tzibur* for *zavin*. Consequently, *zavin* do not count in the *tamei* column when calculating whether the *tzibur* is majority *tamei* in the operative sense. Only *tamei meitim* count, since only that category of *tumah* is potentially subject to the communal dispensation.

In this scenario, Rav rules: the *tamei meitim* bring neither *Pesach Rishon* nor *Pesach Sheni* — *ein osin lo be-rishon ve-lo be-sheni*.

### No First Pesach

The *tamei meitim* cannot bring on *Pesach Rishon* because they are a *miut* — *b'rishon lo avdi de-havu lehu miuta*. Since *zavin* are excluded from the *tamei* column, the *tamei meitim*, who constitute only a minority of the total community, do not reach the threshold of a majority that would enable a *pesach b'tumah*.

### No Second Pesach

The *tamei meitim* cannot bring on *Pesach Sheni* because *Pesach Sheni* is only available when a *tzibur* brought *Pesach Rishon* — *k'heicha de-avdi tzibur b'rishon, avdi yachid be-sheni*. In this case, no *tzibur* brought *Pesach Rishon*: the *zavin* can never bring *Pesach Rishon*, and the *tamei meitim* were unable to because they were a *miut*. Since no *tzibur* brought on the first *Pesach*, there is no *Pesach Sheni* framework into which individuals can step: *kol heicha de-lo avdi tzibur be-rishon, lo avdi yachid be-sheni*.

One might ask whether a *tzibur* need constitute a full majority of *Klal Yisrael*, or whether a smaller group — such as a *shevet* — might suffice to trigger the *Pesach Sheni* option for individuals who missed the first. In the present case, however, there was no such group at all, and so the question does not arise. The *tamei meitim* are simply a *chavura* with no *tzibur* framework behind them.

### Shmuel Challenge

Shmuel challenges Rav's ruling: *zilu amru lei le-Abba* — go tell Rav — *ve-ya'asu Bnei Yisrael et ha-Pesach be-mo'ado* — what do you do with the verse requiring the Jewish people to bring the *pesach* in its appointed time? Can Rav truly accept a scenario in which the entire applicable population brings no *korban pesach* whatsoever on the fourteenth of Nisan?

### Rav Response

Rav responds in kind: *zilu amru lei* — go tell Shmuel — *ki havu kulhu zavin, mai avdit lei* — what would Shmuel do if a hundred percent of the community were *zavin*? In that case, nobody could bring at all. Yet surely one cannot argue from the *pasuk* that they must bring regardless. *Ella keivan de-lo efshar lo efshar* — when it is genuinely impossible, it is impossible, and the *halakha* accepts that outcome. *Hacha nami lo efshar* — here too, when the applicable population is a minority incapable of bringing, and the majority cannot bring *b'tumah* under the *tzibur* dispensation, the *pasuk* cannot override that reality.

## Tamei & Zavin

### Zavin Pesach Sheni

The *Gemara* now presents the inverse case: *hayu ruban tmei meitim u-mi'utan zavin* — the majority were *tamei meitim* and the minority were *zavin*. Here the *tamei meitim*, constituting the majority, bring *pesach b'tumah* on *Pesach Rishon*. The straightforward question is whether the *zavin*, who cannot participate in a *pesach b'tumah*, may bring *Pesach Sheni* — since the *tzibur* did in fact bring *Pesach Rishon*, just in a state of *tumah*.

### Rav Huna

Rav Huna rules: *ein tashlumin le-pesach ha-ba be-tumah* — there is no makeup offering (*tashlumin*) for a *pesach* that was brought in *tumah*. For individuals to be entitled to bring *Pesach Sheni*, the *tzibur* must have brought *Pesach Rishon* in a state of *taharah*. A *pesach* brought *b'tumah*, even when permitted for the *tzibur*, does not generate the entitlement to *tashlumin* for those who were unable to participate.

### Rav Adda

Rav Adda bar Ahava disagrees: *yesh tashlumin le-pesach ha-ba be-tumah* — even when the *tzibur* brought *pesach b'tumah*, there is still a *tashlumin* framework that allows individuals who could not participate to bring *Pesach Sheni*.

### Tumah Status

The *Gemara* identifies the underlying conceptual dispute. Rav Huna holds *tumah dechuya hi be-tzibur* — the *tumah* of the *tzibur* is tolerated under duress, meaning the *pesach* brought *b'tumah* remains essentially problematic; it is not the normative *Pesach Rishon* that generates the classic framework entitling individuals to a makeup. Rav Adda bar Ahava holds *tumah hutra be-tzibur* — the *tumah* of the *tzibur* is fully permitted, meaning the *pesach* brought *b'tumah* is treated as though it were a fully valid *Pesach Rishon* for all purposes, including the entitlement to *tashlumin*. If it is as though the *tzibur* brought in *taharah*, then those who could not participate are entitled to their individual *Pesach Sheni*.

## Three-Way Split

### Rav Mani Analysis

The final case of the daf is the most intricate: *hayu shelishtan zavin u-shelishtan tehorim u-shelishtan tmei meitim* — one third *zavin*, one third *tahor*, and one third *tamei meitim*. Rav Mani bar Patti rules: *tmei meitim ein osin lo be-rishon ve-lo be-sheni* — the *tamei meitim* bring neither *Pesach Rishon* nor *Pesach Sheni*. The *tahor* third brings *Pesach Rishon*, as they are always entitled to do. The *zavin* bring neither, which goes without saying. The analysis focuses on why the *tamei meitim* are also excluded from both.

### No First Pesach

On *Pesach Rishon*, the *tamei meitim* cannot bring because the situation does not qualify as a *pesach ha-ba be-tumah*. Why not? Because *higdilu zavin al tehorim de-lo avdi be-tumah* — the *zavin* are added to, or rather shift the weight of, the *tahor* side, in the sense that they are not counted in the *tamei* column. Since *zavin* are never entitled to bring *Pesach Rishon* even in a *pesach b'tumah* scenario, they do not figure in the *tamei* count. The operative census, for purposes of determining whether the *tamei* side constitutes a majority, excludes the *zavin*. When only the *tamei meitim* are counted in the *tamei* column, they constitute only one third of the entire *Klal Yisrael* — a clear *miut*. A *miut tamei* does not bring *Pesach Rishon b'tumah*.

### Zavin Recounted

One might question whether the *zavin* ought simply to be removed from the calculation entirely, leaving a fifty-fifty split between the *tahor* third and the *tamei meitim* third. The *Gemara* does not adopt this framing. The *chiddush* of Rav Mani bar Patti is precisely that the entire *Klal Yisrael* — including the *zavin* who cannot bring — constitutes the denominator for the census. The *tamei meitim*, being one third of that total, are unambiguously a *miut*, not a fifty-fifty, and there is therefore no question of treating them as a *rov* or as equal to the *tahor* population.

### No Second Pesach

On *Pesach Sheni*, the *tamei meitim* are also excluded. The reason is that for *Pesach Sheni* to be available, the majority of the *tzibur* must have brought *Pesach Rishon*. In this case, two thirds of the community — the *zavin* and the *tamei meitim* together — did not bring *Pesach Rishon*: *nitztarfu zavin im tmei meitim de-lo avdi be-rishon, de-havu lehu ruba*. Since the majority of the *kahal* did not bring *Pesach Rishon*, there is no *Pesach Sheni* framework. The only third that did bring was the *tahor* group — and that is not a majority of the community. Therefore *rova lo maytu le-pesach sheni* — a majority that did not bring cannot bring *Pesach Sheni*, and the minority who actually did bring was too small to generate a *Pesach Sheni* entitlement for those who missed it.
