# Bava Metzia 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Magbiya Metzia

### Migo Principle

The daf opens four lines from the top with a principle that has occupied the sugya for the past several days: *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro* — if one picks up a lost object not for oneself but for another person, does that act confer ownership upon the other person? The argument in favor of allowing it rests on the concept of *migo d'zachi l'nafshei* — since the one picking up the object could keep it for himself, perhaps that entitlement also empowers him to acquire it on behalf of someone else.

### Tofes L'Baal Chov

The counterargument invokes the principle of *tofes l'baal chov b'makom shechav l'acherim*. Imagine Reuven, Shimon, and Levi are all creditors of Eliezer. If a bystander goes and seizes Eliezer's property specifically for Reuven, he is acting against Shimon and Levi, who had an equal claim to whatever assets might be available. The same concern applies to a *metzia*: by picking it up for one specific person, one is denying every other potential finder their opportunity. Since the act is not for one's own benefit, the question is whether it carries any legal force at all. This is the core tension the *gemara* is working through.

### Mishnah Interpretation

The *Mishnah* presents the following case: Reuven tells Shimon to go pick up a lost object for him. Shimon does so, but then declares he is keeping it for himself — and the *Mishnah* rules that he may. The difficulty is that the *Mishnah* does not make explicit what Shimon's intention was at the moment he picked up the object. One reading of the *Mishnah* is that Shimon originally picked it up for Reuven and then changed his mind — and even so he may keep it, because *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro lo kanah chaveiro*: even if he did it for Reuven, the act did not transfer ownership.

### Techila in Seifa

The *gemara* refines this analysis by zeroing in on the word *techila* — "originally" — which appears in the *seifa*, the latter clause of the *Mishnah*. The *seifa* addresses a case where Shimon picked up the object, already handed it over to Reuven, and then said: *ani zachiti ba techila* — "I originally acquired it for myself." The *gemara* asks: *techila b'seifa l'ma li?* Why does the *Mishnah* need to specify "originally" in this second case? It is self-evident that Shimon is not claiming to take it back *now* — he already gave it to Reuven. Obviously he means to say his *original* intent was to keep it. So why state it at all?

The *gemara* answers: *tana seifa l'gluyei reisha* — the *seifa* is formulated the way it is to illuminate the *reisha*. The word *techila* in the *seifa* signals that the entire legal framework revolves around the question of original intent. Consequently, in the *reisha* — where Shimon still holds the object — the case where Shimon can keep it is specifically where he claims his original intent was to keep it for himself. If his original intent was for Reuven, then Reuven acquired it the moment it was lifted, and Shimon may no longer unilaterally change course.

### Two Positions

This yields two rival readings of the *Mishnah*. The first holds that *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro kanah chaveiro* — the act does work — and the *Mishnah*'s permission for Shimon to keep the object rests entirely on his claim of *techila*, original intent for himself. If he had originally intended it for Reuven, Reuven owns it from the moment of lifting, and no subsequent change of mind by Shimon avails anything.

The second position — *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro lo kanah chaveiro* — holds that the act does not work at all. According to this reading, the *seifa*'s use of *techila* is not definitional for the *reisha*; rather, *tana seifa l'gluyei reisha d'lo amar techila*: the *seifa* spells out the word "originally" to draw a contrast with the *reisha*, where Shimon does *not* need to invoke original intent. Even if Shimon admits he originally lifted the object for Reuven, he may still keep it — because the act never worked in the first place. As long as he hasn't transferred it, nobody owns it yet, and he is free to take it for himself.

This debate also drew a student's observation that is philosophically significant: how can *kavana* — intention — override physical possession of an object that belongs to no one? The answer is that the act of acquisition is never purely physical; it is always a combination of a physical act and intent. Without intent to acquire, mere physical contact does not create *kinyan*. The general rule *chatzero shel adam koneh lo shelo midato* — one's yard acquires on one's behalf even without one's awareness — is itself a *chiddush*, resting on the presumption that a person would want to acquire whatever enters his domain. But that presumption of intent is precisely that — a presumption, which explicit contrary intent can override.

### Rav Nachman Position

The *gemara* now records the explicit ruling of Rav Nachman and Rav Chisda, who both state: *hamagbih metziah l'chaveiro lo kanah chaveiro* — acquiring a lost object on behalf of another does not work. The reason, they explain, is *havay tofes l'baal chov bimkom shechav l'acheirim*: the act is analogous to seizing a debtor's property for one creditor at the expense of others. When a debtor owes money to multiple people and a bystander grabs his property specifically for one of them, the others are injured. Since the act is not for one's own benefit, and since one has no independent right in the matter, the seizure does not take effect.

Rashi raises the question of whether appointing a *shaliach* — a formal agent — would change the analysis. His view is that it would: a properly designated *shaliach* can represent the principal's interests and the act would succeed. Tosafot, however, disagrees; even with formal agency, the problem remains, because one cannot arrogate to oneself the right to interfere with others' opportunities simply by virtue of an appointment. The *Mishnah*'s case — where the man on the horse sends Shimon — sounds like a form of *shlichut*, and yet the *gemara* treats it as raising the same fundamental question, which suggests that even *shlichut* may not be sufficient to overcome the *tofes l'baal chov* problem.

Tosafot further notes that it is logically possible to hold *tofes l'baal chov bimkom shechav l'acheirim lo kanah* while simultaneously holding *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro kanah chaveiro* — because the two cases are not identical. In the debt-seizure case, the person acting has no right to do so on his own behalf at all; he has no claim on the property. But by a *metzia*, he could have kept it for himself. The *gemara* articulates this distinction through the phrase *migo d'ei ba'ei zachi l'nafshei* — since I could have taken it for myself, perhaps that independent entitlement extends to allow me to take it for someone else.

Tosafot also engages a related case involving drawing water from a public well for another person, as it bears on *hilchos eiruvin*. According to Rabbeinu Tam, that case presents no problem of *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro* whatsoever, because there is plentiful water in the well and no identifiable person is being harmed by the act of drawing it — the *chav l'acheirim* concern simply does not materialize when there is no realistic injury to others. Additionally, Tosafot makes the conceptual observation that if the mechanism by which one may acquire for another is the *migo* — because one could have taken it for oneself — then the logical structure is that we view the acquirer as having first taken it for himself and then transferred it to the other person, rather than as having acquired directly on the other's behalf. This has practical consequences for *hilchos eiruvin*: the *techum* of the water would be defined by the person who drew it, not by the person for whom it was drawn.

## Po'el Metzia

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges Rav Nachman's position from a *baraita* concerning a *po'el* — a hired day laborer. The rule is *metzi'as po'el l'atzmo*: a laborer who finds a lost object while working keeps it for himself. This seems unproblematic when the laborer is working for himself. But the *baraita* specifies conditions under which the *metzia* belongs to the *ba'al habayis* — the employer — rather than to the laborer. If the employer hired him with the words *asei immi melacha hayom* — "do any work for me today" — then whatever the laborer finds belongs to the employer. This is effectively a case of *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro*: the laborer is acquiring on behalf of his employer. How, then, does this work if *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro lo kanah chaveiro*?

### Task Distinction

The *baraita* itself distinguishes between two formulations of employment. If the employer specified a particular task — *nachish immi hayom* (weed for me today) or *ador immi hayom* (plow for me today) — then at the moment the laborer picks up the diamond, he is not performing that task. His act of picking up the *metzia* lies outside the scope of his employment, and whatever he finds is his own. At most, the employer could claim compensation for the five minutes of work-time lost.

### Po'el Extension

But when the employer says *asei immi melacha hayom* — do any labor for me today — the laborer's entire workday is contracted to the employer. In that case, *yado k'yad ba'al habayis hu* — his hand is treated as the employer's hand. He is, in effect, an extension of the employer, and what he acquires he acquires for the employer. This is why the *metzia* belongs to the *ba'al habayis*. This arrangement is more than mere *shlichut*: there is an actual contractual relationship, a binding engagement, that makes the laborer's acquisitive act an act of the employer.

### Breaking Contract

The *gemara* immediately raises an apparent difficulty with this analysis. Rav states: *po'el yachol lachzor bo afilu bachatzi hayom* — a laborer can quit his job even in the middle of the day. If he can always unilaterally terminate the arrangement, how can he truly be considered an extension of the employer? Does not his retained autonomy demonstrate that he is never really subsumed under the employer's identity?

The *gemara* responds: *kol kamma d'lo hadar bei k'yad ba'al habayis hu* — as long as he has not in fact quit, he is still considered an extension of the employer. The right to exit the arrangement does not negate the arrangement while it subsists. These are two separate issues: *yado k'yad ba'al habayis* describes the status quo during active employment, while the right to break the contract is founded on an entirely independent reason — *ki hadar bei ta'ama achrina hu* — it rests on a different ground altogether.

### Ki Li Bnei Yisrael

That different ground is the verse *ki li bnei Yisrael avadim* — "for to Me the Children of Israel are servants" — which the *gemara* expounds: *avadai hem, velo avadim la'avadim* — they are My servants, not servants of servants. This is a powerful theological and moral statement: Halacha will not allow employment to become a form of servitude. Even while a laborer is fully engaged and is considered *k'yad ba'al habayis*, he retains the inviolable right to exit. This right is not a loophole or a technicality; it is a fundamental safeguard against the degradation of one person's labor being transformed into ownership over another person's will.

Tosafot adds a striking nuance: the laborer not only has the right to break the contract, but he holds the advantaged position in any renegotiation about compensation for the remaining time. This further demonstrates that the laborer is never fully subordinated, even during active employment.

### Rabbi Chiya Position

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, citing Rabbi Yochanan, takes the opposite view on the core principle: *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro kanah chaveiro* — the act does work, and the other person does acquire. Rabbi Chiya must then explain how the *Mishnah* permits Shimon to keep the object. His answer does not require Shimon to have had original intent for himself.

### Delivery vs Possession

The key, says Rabbi Chiya, lies in the specific language used by the man on the horse. He said *tnei li* — "hand it to me" — not *zachei li* — "acquire it on my behalf." The instruction was to *deliver* the object, not to take *possession* of it for him. Acquisition and delivery are distinct acts. When Shimon picks up the object, he does so with the intent to convey it, not to vest title. Since there was no instruction to take possession, no *kinyan* was effected on Reuven's behalf at the moment of lifting. Shimon is therefore free to change his mind before actually handing it over. Once he does hand it over, however, the *kinyan* is complete and irrevocable. But had Reuven explicitly said *zachei li* — acquire it for me — and Shimon had that intention when he lifted it, then Reuven would own it from that moment, and Shimon could not retain it regardless.

This generates three possible scenarios: either *magbiya metzia l'chaveiro lo kanah chaveiro* (Rav Nachman's position, under which Shimon can always keep it before handing it over regardless of his stated intention); or *kanah chaveiro* but Shimon can keep it because he claims original intent for himself (*techila*); or *kanah chaveiro* but Shimon can keep it because the instruction was framed as a request to deliver, not to acquire.

### Tosafot Nuance

Returning to the *ki li bnei Yisrael avadim* discussion, Tosafot draws a careful line. Employment of one person by another is not prohibited; what is prohibited is transforming employment into *avdut* — servitude. The specific case of an *eved Ivri*, a Hebrew indentured servant, who cannot break the contract and cannot leave before the stipulated time without a formal *shtar shichrur* or the arrival of the Jubilee year — *that* is genuine *avdut*. Ordinary employment, even though it creates a relationship in which *yado k'yad ba'al habayis*, remains permissible precisely because the laborer retains the right to exit at any moment. That right is what prevents the quasi-subordination from crossing into morally objectionable territory. Tosafot therefore concludes: *mutar adam l'haskir atzmo* — it is permitted for a person to hire himself out — as long as the right of exit remains intact.

The *Gra*, in a marginal note, cites *Hagahot Maimoni* to the effect that it is forbidden to contract oneself out for more than three years, because even with the theoretical right to exit, a multi-year commitment constitutes a form of quasi-*avdut* that approaches the threshold of genuine servitude. The underlying tension — between the legitimate need for contracted labor and the theological insistence that no Israelite may become a slave to another human being — remains one of the richest moral themes the sugya opens up.

## Ra'ah Metzia

### Falling Not Kinyan

The *gemara* turns to another scenario involving a *metzia*. *Ra'ah metziah venafal aleha* — one sees a lost object and throws himself bodily upon it. Another person — smarter and quicker — reaches under him and physically takes the object away. The ruling is *zeh shehechezik bah zachah bah* — the one who physically grasped it acquires it. Falling on an object, however total the bodily contact, does not constitute a *kinyan*. *Kinyan* requires an affirmative act of taking possession — picking up the object, drawing it toward oneself, or the like. Simply placing one's weight on top of something confers nothing.

### Arba Amot Principle

The *gemara* now introduces a statement of Reish Lakish in the name of Abba Kohen Bardela: *arba amot shel adam konot lo bechol makom* — the four *amot* surrounding a person acquire on his behalf wherever he is. The concept rests on the idea of personal space: four *amot* is the halachically defined sphere of one's immediate surroundings, analogous in some ways to one's *chatzer*. If a lost object comes within that radius, the person acquires it without any additional physical act.

The *gemara* notes that this principle is invoked primarily in two contexts: finding lost objects, and *get* (divorce). Both will be elaborated. For lost objects, the intuitive purpose is to reduce conflict: if proximity confers ownership, there is at least a clear rule about who gets priority, reducing the likelihood of physical confrontation over a *metzia*.

### Taknat Rabanan

The *gemara* states explicitly: *takanat rabanan hi d'lo ati l'intzu'ei* — this is a rabbinic enactment to prevent fighting. A student nicely observed that the rule is not merely about who is first to find the object, but about preventing the kind of pile-up that would result if *kinyan* required actually picking it up. If two people are running toward the same object, the requirement of a physical *kinyan* act could generate real physical conflict. The *arba amot* principle cuts off the race earlier and with less danger — possession is established by proximity, making physical scrambling unnecessary.

## Arba Amot Challenges

### Pe'ah Challenge

Two challenges are leveled against the *arba amot* principle. Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef challenges from a *Mishnah* about *pe'ah*. The *Mishnah* records: *natal miktzat pe'ah v'zarak al ha-shear* — a person collected some of the *pe'ah* and then threw what he had on top of the remaining grain to claim it. The *Mishnah* rules: *ein lo bah klum* — he has no claim to it. If he fell upon it — *nafal aleha* — or spread his cloak over it — *piras talito aleha* — we remove him from it, and the *pe'ah* remains available for others. Why did *arba amot* not acquire the grain for him? He was standing within four *amot* of it.

### Girsa Question

The *gemara*'s first answer is: *hacha bemai askinan d'lo amar akni* — the case is one where he did not say "I want my *arba amot* to acquire for me." The *gemara* immediately challenges this: if the Rabbis established the *kinyan* of *arba amot*, why should it matter whether one utters any formula? You clearly want to acquire — why require magic words? This response is rejected.

### Gali Da'atei

The *gemara* offers instead: *keivan d'nafal gali da'ateih d'binfilah nicha leh d'nikni* — since he fell upon it, he revealed his intent that he wanted to acquire through the act of falling. And since falling is the *kinyan* he intended to perform, *b'arba amot lo nicha leh d'nikni* — he did not intend to acquire through his *arba amot*, and that *kinyan* therefore does not activate.

### Falling Intent

This is a subtle but important principle. The person clearly wants the object — there is no ambiguity about desire. But the *kinyan* of *arba amot* is not simply an automatic consequence of proximity; it is a legal mechanism that is overridden when the person has demonstrated, through his own actions, that he intended a *different* mechanism of acquisition. His act of falling expressed his chosen mode of *kinyan*. Since *nefilah* — falling — is not a valid *kinyan*, that chosen mode failed. And since he did not intend to rely on *arba amot*, that mechanism is unavailable to rescue him.

An analogy illustrates this: suppose someone intends to perform *hagbahah* — lifting — as the act of *kinyan* for a cup, and lifts it less than three *tefachim* (an insufficiency for *hagbahah*, according to one view). In so doing, he has in fact performed *meshichah* — drawing toward himself — which is a valid *kinyan*. Can we combine his desire to acquire with the physical act of *meshichah* and say he has acquired? Or do we say: he made clear he was performing *hagbahah*, that act failed, and his non-awareness of *meshichah* means the *meshichah* does not take effect for him? The *gemara* here asserts that the act of falling similarly negates the otherwise-available *kinyan* of *arba amot*.

### Moral Not Rasha

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing on a small but striking Tosafot that addresses the moral dimension of these cases. Tosafot notes that even the person who comes and physically takes the object from under the one who fell on it — removing him from the *pe'ah* — is not called a *rasha*. Indeed, *afilla rasha lo mikrei*. This follows from another principle in *Kiddushin*: *ani mehapech b'chararah u-va acher u-netalah lo mikrei rasha* — a poor person turning over a bread roll to examine it, and another person who comes and takes it away, is called a *rasha*. But Tosafot explains: that case involved land, of which there is a finite and identifiable supply. If I am negotiating for a specific plot of land and you swoop in and take it, you are a *rasha* — because there are other plots of land you could have pursued. By contrast, with a *metzia* or *pe'ah* or *hefker*, there is no comparable alternative. One cannot tell the other finder: "go find a similar lost object somewhere else." Each *metzia* is unique, and therefore the competitive acquisition of it, even in a way that denies someone else their opportunity, carries no moral opprobrium. Even if one person pointed out the *metzia* to another who then ran ahead and took it — which seems at least more morally complicated — Tosafot does not address that scenario, though it would presumably warrant further analysis. What is clear is that Tosafot is at least sensitive to the moral dimension of these cases, even if the legal conclusion removes the stigma of *rasha* in the straightforward case.

## Context Limits

### Rav Papa Field

Rav Papa offers a contextual limitation on the *arba amot* principle, formulated (according to the *Gra*'s *girsa*) as follows: *ki takinu lei rabanan arba amot besada de'alma* — when did the Rabbis enact that *arba amot* acquires? In a field that belongs to no party with a stronger claim — an ordinary field. But *besada d'ba'al habayis* — in the field of the owner of the grain — the enactment does not apply.

### Owner Override

The concept of *arba amot* is a form of personal space. It is, in effect, a portable *chatzer* — my immediate surroundings function as my domain. But when one is standing in someone else's property, the owner's claim to that space is stronger than any claim the visitor can generate through personal proximity. The *gemara* makes this precise: *ve'af gav d'zacha lei Rachmana lehuluchi bah ulelikutei pe'ah* — even though the Torah grants the *ani* the right to enter the owner's field and collect *pe'ah* — *lemehava chatzeiro lo zacha lei Rachmana* — that right does not extend to treating the owner's field as the *ani*'s own *chatzer*. The Torah gave a right of entry and collection; it did not give a right of territorial claim. And since *arba amot* functions as a personal *chatzer*, it cannot override the owner's actual possession of the land on which the *ani* is standing.

### Rav Sheshes Simta

Rav Sheshes offers a parallel limitation from a different angle. He holds: *ki tikinu Rabbanan be'simta* — the Rabbis enacted *arba amot* specifically for a *simta*, a side street or alleyway. In such a setting, the concept of personal space is coherent: the area is not heavily trafficked, and one can meaningfully speak of a defined radius around oneself as one's own immediate sphere.

### Dachki Rabbim

But *aval be'reshus harabbim d'ka dachki Rabbim* — in the public domain, where crowds are pressing and pushing — *lo tiknu Rabbanan*: the Rabbis did not apply the enactment there. In a crowded *reshus harabbim*, the notion of personal space collapses. People are physically compressed against one another; there is no realistic sense in which four *amot* around any individual constitute his sphere. The term the *gemara* uses is *dachki Rabbim* — the pressing of the multitude — and this physical reality negates the conceptual foundation on which *arba amot* rests. This closely parallels the rule that *meshichah* — the act of drawing an object toward oneself — is also only valid in a *simta* and not in a *reshus harabbim*, where any such act lacks the clarity and definition required to constitute a meaningful *kinyan*.

The *gemara* then notes that the original statement specified *bechol makom* — everywhere — and reconciles this with the limitations articulated by Rav Papa and Rav Sheshes by explaining: *kol makom l'asuyei tziddei reshus harabbim* — the phrase "everywhere" was intended to include the sidewalks and margins of the public domain, not the *reshus harabbim* itself. The principle, then, is considerably more bounded than its initial formulation suggests.

## Ketana Chatzer

### Ketana Question

The *gemara* now introduces a further application of Reish Lakish's ruling about *arba amot*, this time in the context of *get*. The background: a man may divorce a minor girl (*ketana*) through delivery of a *get*, because her marriage was itself arranged through her father's *kinyan*. The Torah states *ve'natan beyadah* — he shall place it in her hand — and the *halacha* derives from this that *get* can be delivered not only to her person but also to her *chatzer*. The question now is: can a *ketana* acquire a *get* through her *chatzer*, or through *arba amot*?

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish holds: *ketana ein lah chatzer v'ein lah arba amot* — a minor girl has neither a *chatzer* nor *arba amot* as mechanisms for acquiring. A *get* delivered to her yard or into her four-*amot* radius does not constitute valid receipt.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *yesh lah chatzer v'yesh lah arba amot* — she has both. A *get* may be validly delivered to her *chatzer* or within her *arba amot*.

### Yad vs Shlichut

The dispute turns on a fundamental question about the nature of *chatzer* as a *kinyan* mechanism. There are two competing theoretical frameworks.

### Chatzer as Hand

The first position holds: *chatzer mishum yadah itrabai* — the concept of *chatzer* is derived from, and is an extension of, the concept of *yad* (hand). The *pasuk* says *ve'natan beyadah*, and from this we broaden *yad* to include one's *chatzer* — one's property functions as an extension of one's physical person. Just as the object placed in her hand is on her person, so too an object placed in her *chatzer* is within her sphere. Under this view, *ki heichi d'is lah yad chatzer nami is lah* — the same way she has a hand, she has a *chatzer*. A *ketana* has a hand; therefore she has a *chatzer*. This is Reish Lakish's... wait — actually this is the position that would *support* Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, since a *ketana* obviously has a hand.

### Chatzer Agency

The second position holds: *chatzer mishum shlichut* — the mechanism of *chatzer* is not based on physical extension but on agency. The *chatzer* acts *on behalf* of its owner, analogous to how a *shaliach* acts on behalf of a principal. Under this view, the *chatzer* is not merely an extension of the owner's body but an independent mechanism that operates in a representative capacity. If so, *vechi heichi d'shlichut leit lah chatzer nami leit lah* — just as a *ketana* lacks the legal capacity to appoint a *shaliach* (agency requires *da'at*, and a minor's *da'at* is insufficient for that purpose), her *chatzer* similarly cannot function as an agent on her behalf. This is Reish Lakish's position.

The two positions also account for the parallel question of *arba amot*: if *arba amot* is a form of *chatzer* — a portable personal domain — then it tracks the same dispute. If *chatzer* is an extension of *yad*, *arba amot* also works for a *ketana*. If *chatzer* is a form of *shlichut*, *arba amot* is equally unavailable to her.

## Ketana Chatzer (II)

### Chatzer Crime

If *chatzer* is understood as *shlichut* — as acting on the owner's behalf — a striking implication follows: can a *chatzer* commit a *dvar aveirah* for its owner? The principle of *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* holds that agency cannot be invoked to perform an act that constitutes a transgression — if I appoint someone to steal for me, the act of theft is attributed to the agent, not to me, and I bear no liability. But if my *chatzer* is a form of *shlichut*, the same logic should apply: if an animal enters my yard and I lock the gate, using my *chatzer* as the instrument of *gneivah*, shouldn't the principle of *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* negate my liability? This is the problem the *gemara* now confronts.

---

## Agency Crime Limits

### Gneivah Proof

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: the verse *im himatzei timatzei beyado hagneivah* — "if the stolen object is found in his hand" — teaches that liability for *gneivah* attaches to the thief's hand. From where do we know that *gago, chatzero vekarfipo* — his roof, his yard, and his enclosure — also create liability? From the words *himatzei timatzei*, which expand the scope of "being found" to any location under his control. So *chatzer* can serve as the instrument of *gneivah*. But if *chatzer* is *mishum shlichut*, why doesn't *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* protect the owner from liability — just as it would if a human agent stole on his behalf?

### Ravina Answer

Ravina answers: *heichi amrinan ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah? Heicha d'shaliach bar chiyuva hu* — the principle of *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* applies only where the agent himself is bound by the prohibition in question. A *chatzer* is *lo bar chiyuva* — it is not subject to the prohibition *lo tignov*. Since it bears no obligation, the logic of *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* does not apply to it.

### Moral Choice

The rationale underlying *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* is: *divrei haRav v'divrei hatalmid divrei mi shom'im?* — when the words of God and the words of a human master conflict, whose words do you heed? If the agent is commanded by God not to steal but by his principal to steal, the agent should have refused. Having chosen to obey the principal, the agent bears the moral and legal responsibility for his choice. This is why agency is negated — not because it is technically impossible, but because the agent's free moral choice severs the causal and legal link to the principal.

### Chatzer Inanimate

But a *chatzer* is an inanimate object. It cannot be commanded. It cannot choose. The prohibition *lo tignov* does not speak to it. Therefore, *mchayev shalcho* — the sender, the owner, is held liable, because the *chatzer* is simply carrying out the situation created by the owner's act. There is no independent moral agent interposed between the owner and the outcome.

### Woman Slave

The *gemara* presses: what about a woman or a slave? If I tell them to go steal for me, they have no money of their own to pay damages — *leit lehu lishlumei* — so are they also *lo bar chiyuva*, and should I therefore be liable for their theft? The *gemara* rejects this: *ishah ve-eved benei chiyuva ninhu* — a woman and a slave are fully subject to the prohibition against theft. *Ve-hashta miha leit lehu lishlumei* — they simply have no current assets to pay with, but the obligation and liability exist. The proof: *nitgarshah ha-ishah, nishtachrer ha-eved — chayavin lishaleim* — if the woman is later divorced and the slave later freed, they must pay. Their momentary inability to pay does not negate their substantive legal obligation.

### Rava Better

Rava offers what the *gemara* presents as a superior answer. He formulates the distinction not in terms of *bar chiyuva* but in terms of the capacity for free choice: *heichi amrinan ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah? Heicha d'i ba'ei avid, i ba'ei lo avid* — we apply *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* wherever the agent has a genuine choice, where he could have done otherwise. The agent's freedom is what makes him the responsible party. If the agent could have said no and chose not to, it is the agent's act, not the principal's.

### Ba'al Korchah

But a *chatzer* has no such choice. *Aval chatzer de-ba'al korchah* — the *chatzer* does not act voluntarily. It is an inanimate domain that simply is what it is; the animal walks in, the gate is locked, and the result follows mechanically. *Ba'al korchah motvei bah* — it acts, as it were, under compulsion. And therefore *mchayev shalcho* — the one who put it in motion, who created the situation, is the one who bears liability. There is no intervening free agent to absorb the moral or legal responsibility. The owner is fully accountable.

This resolution also illuminates the *shlichut* model of *chatzer* more precisely. Even if we say *chatzer mishum shlichut*, it is not *shlichut* in the same sense as human agency. A human *shaliach* who performs an *aveirah* severs the principal's liability because of his own free choice. A *chatzer* that performs, so to speak, the same act does not sever that liability precisely because there was no choice. The anthropomorphism of treating *chatzer* as a form of agency reaches its natural limit here: it acts on the owner's behalf, but it does so without volition, and therefore it cannot absorb responsibility. *Yesh shaliach l'dvar aveirah* — there is, in this case, agency even for an act of *aveirah* — because the agent is not a free moral actor but an instrument of the owner's will.
