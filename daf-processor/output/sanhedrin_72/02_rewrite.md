# Sanhedrin 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Haba b'Machteret Intro

### Perek 8 Overview

The eighth *perek* of Sanhedrin is frequently taught in schools, and for good reason: it contains some of the most practically and philosophically charged material in the entire tractate. Having just concluded the *mishna* of *ben sorer u-moreh*, we now turn to the *mishnayyot* dealing with *haba b'machteret* — the burglar who breaks in through a tunnel — and the laws of self-defense that emerge from that case. The *perek* will go on to address the *rodef*, the obligation to intervene when another person's life is threatened, and eventually reaches the case of a woman in mortal danger from childbirth, where the fetus itself is the threat. The philosophical and *halachic* stakes throughout are considerable.

### Nidon Al Shem Sofo

The link to *ben sorer u-moreh* is the concept of *nidon al shem sofo* — being judged based on one's future outcome. By *ben sorer u-moreh*, the *Torah* looks into the future trajectory of the rebellious son and condemns him based on what he will become. The *mishna* applies the same phrase to *haba b'machteret*. But as the *Gemara* immediately makes clear, the two applications are fundamentally different in kind.

By *ben sorer u-moreh*, we are projecting decades into the future and declaring this child a *rasha* on the basis of what he will eventually do. By *haba b'machteret*, we are not projecting a future crime at all. Rather, by understanding what the burglar is prepared to do *right now* — specifically, that he is willing to use lethal force if necessary to complete his theft — we recognize that the person whose home he is entering faces an immediate, present threat to his life. The *nidon al shem sofo* here is not a preventive judgment about a career criminal; it is a recognition that the circumstances confronting us at this very moment constitute a life-threatening situation.

### Torah Source

The *pesukim* in question read: *im ba-machteret yimatze ha-ganav ve-huka va-met ein lo damim* — if a burglar is found in a tunnel and is struck and killed, there is no blood-guilt. But then: *im zarcha ha-shemesh alav damim lo* — if the sun has risen over him, there is blood-guilt. The *peshat* reading is that while the burglar is inside the tunnel, the homeowner acts under the perception of an immediate threat to his life and may use lethal force; once the burglar has exited the tunnel — once "the sun has risen," meaning he is presumably fleeing and no longer threatening — the owner no longer has justification to kill him, and doing so would constitute murder.

This becomes the *Torah* foundation for the right of self-defense, including the use of lethal force. The *mishna* then adds an important further dimension: *haba b'machteret v'shibar et ha-chavit, im yesh lo damim chayav, im ein lo damim patur* — if the burglar broke something in the course of his entry, and it is a case of *yesh lo damim* (where killing him would be prohibited), he must pay for what he broke; but if it is a case of *ein lo damim* (where killing him is permitted), he is exempt from payment. This triggers the application of the principle of *kim lei b'derabba minei*.

## Self-Defense Principle

### Reframing Nidon

*Rava* opens by asking: *mai ta'ama de-machteret?* — what is the underlying rationale of the tunnel case? The answer: *chazakah, ein adam ma'amid atzmo al mamono* — we can reliably assume that a person will not stand passively by while another takes his property. The burglar understands this. He knows that if he enters and the owner confronts him, the owner will resist. The *Gemara* then renders the burglar's calculation explicitly: *ee azlina kaei le'apai ve-lo shavik li* — if I go in, the owner will stand before me and will not simply let me take his things. *Ve-ee kaei le'apai katlinan lei* — and if he stands before me, I will be prepared to kill him. It is precisely this calculus — the burglar's advance readiness to use lethal force — that transforms the situation into one of genuine self-defense.

*Ve-ha-Torah amrah: im ba le-horgecha hashkem le-horgo* — and the Torah says: if someone is coming to kill you, get ahead of him and kill him first. This principle is the bedrock. It is worth noting that there is a discussion as to whether killing in self-defense is a *reshut* — a permission — or a *mitzvah*. The language of the *Gemara* strongly implies the latter: *im ba le-horgecha hashkem le-horgo* reads as an obligation, not merely a dispensation. This applies both to the potential victim acting in self-defense and to bystanders who have an obligation to intervene to save the threatened person.

An important limitation bears mention: *yakhol le-hatzilo be-echad me-evarav* — if one could reasonably have stopped the attacker without killing him, one may not use lethal force. In the heat of a confrontation this remains genuinely difficult to assess, and courts must always evaluate what was reasonably available in that moment of fear. Equally, one is not obligated to surrender one's property preemptively — to say, as the *Chofetz Chaim* famously did when his home was burglarized, *ani mochel*, I forgive you, just take my things. The right to protect one's property, and by extension one's life, is what the *Torah* is affirming here.

### Qim Lei Expansion

The application of *kim lei b'derabba minei* to *haba b'machteret* raises a conceptual difficulty. Classically, *kim lei* operates as follows: when a person commits an act that simultaneously incurs both a capital punishment and a monetary obligation, the court cannot impose the monetary sanction — the greater punishment, the capital one, subsumes the lesser. The classic illustration is a murderer who, in the act of killing, also destroys the victim's garment: even if he is never executed in practice — even if the killing was *be-shogeg* and there are no witnesses — since it is the *type* of act that carries a capital penalty, no monetary liability is imposed.

The problem here is that *haba b'machteret* is not a court-imposed capital punishment. The owner who kills the burglar is not acting as an agent of *beit din*; he is acting in self-defense. The burglar cannot be brought before a court and executed after the fact. Yet the *mishna* and *Gemara* nonetheless apply *kim lei*. This can be resolved in one of two ways.

The first, more conventional reading is that *kim lei b'derabba minei* is broader than previously assumed. It is not limited to court-imposed sanctions. Rather, it reflects a more general principle: when a person is engaged in an act so severe that someone is entitled to take his life — whatever the grounds for that entitlement — no secondary monetary obligation can be simultaneously imposed. The magnitude of the life-forfeiting act overwhelms the relevance of any property damage incurred along the way.

The second reading, which is less commonly articulated but emerges, arguably, from the texture of the *Gemara* itself, is more radical: rather than seeing self-defense as merely personal protection and then expanding *kim lei* to cover it, we might instead reconceptualize self-defense itself as a form of quasi-judicial action. When someone's life is threatened and there is no *beit din* present to intervene, the threatened person — or the intervening bystander — effectively acts as a representative of the legal system. The principle of *avid inish dina l'nafshei* — that a person may take the law into his own hands to protect his own property — is understood by the *Rambam* as resting on the fact that *me-ikar ha-din* one person can serve as a *dayan*. By analogy, the defender of life is deputized, as it were, to function as an arm of the court. If the court cannot be present, someone must act in its stead, and that person acts within a framework of law rather than as a vigilante. Under this model, *kim lei* applies in the conventional sense because we conceptualize the killing as, in effect, a court-imposed judgment — just one executed at the moment of threat rather than afterward.

### Two Models

These two models — self-defense as personal right versus self-defense as court-representative action — have meaningful *halachic* consequences and will recur throughout the *Gemara's* discussion. The *Rambam's* explanation of *avid inish dina l'nafshei* anchors the second model more firmly. When the *Gemara* raises as a *hava amina* that perhaps self-defense should be prohibited on *Shabbat* because court executions are not carried out on *Shabbat*, this bizarre-sounding suggestion only makes sense if at some level the killing is being conceptualized as court-mandated. One would never think to prohibit self-defense on *Shabbat* on the grounds that it resembles a *Shabbat* execution unless one had already, at least partly, conceptualized it that way.

## Qim Lei & Damages

### Rav's Statement

*Amar Rav*: *haba ba-machteret ve-natal kelim ve-yatza patur* — a burglar who came through the tunnel, took vessels, and left is exempt. *Mai ta'ama? Bi-damei kanahu* — what is the reason? He acquired them with his blood. The wordplay is pointed: *damim* means both "blood" and "money." Since his life was forfeit — since he could have been killed — he incurs no monetary obligation, not even the obligation to return what he took.

At first glance this seems reasonable: he broke in, he could have been killed, he is spared any liability. But Rava immediately expresses discomfort. *Mistavra milta de-Rav be-shivrei* — it makes sense to limit Rav's ruling to a case where the stolen object was *broken*, *de-laisnahu*, because then the object no longer exists and the question is purely one of monetary compensation. There we can say no compensation is owed. *Aval natal lo* — but if he simply *took* the TV and it is sitting intact in his possession, surely that is still the owner's property. If the owner's property happened to roll into a stranger's yard, the stranger would have to give it back. Why should the burglar be any different?

Despite Rava's preference to limit the ruling, the *Gemara* acknowledges — with something approaching an oath — that Rav himself appears to have meant his statement even in the case where the stolen object remains intact. So the question becomes: what is the theoretical basis for Rav's extreme position?

### Rava's Limitation

Rava's own reasoning for limiting Rav's statement is clean and intuitive: when the object is broken, the question is whether the burglar owes money — a monetary obligation — and *kim lei* exempts him from that obligation. When the object is intact, it is not a question of imposing a new obligation but simply of returning someone else's property. *Kim lei* does not entitle you to keep another person's property; it merely exempts you from monetary penalties. Rava maintains this position as the operative ruling.

### Ownership vs Liability

To understand what lies behind Rav's position, the *Gemara* examines a foundational question in the laws of theft. The question is this: when a *ganav* steals an object, what precisely happens at that moment? Does he incur an obligation to *return* the object, with monetary compensation as a fallback if the object is gone? Or does he, from the very instant of taking, become the owner of the object and owe its monetary value to the victim — with returning the intact object merely being a way to discharge that monetary debt?

The evidence that seems to favor the second view is the rule that a *ganav* who steals an object must compensate the owner even if the object was subsequently destroyed through no fault of the *ganav* — even through an *oness*, a completely uncontrollable accident. If, in normal circumstances, negligence or fault would be required for liability, why should the *ganav* bear the loss from an accident that would have destroyed the object even in the owner's hands? The *Gemara* answers: *alma bi-reshutei kaimi* — this shows that from the moment the *ganav* takes the object, it is conceptually "in his possession," meaning he owns it and owes its value. That is why accidents befall him: because the monetary obligation — not the duty of return — was created at the moment of theft.

Rav builds on this: if the *ganav* owns the object and merely owes money, then by *kim lei* — since his life was forfeit in committing the act — the money obligation disappears. And since it is the money obligation that is the real legal relationship, not a duty of return, the *ganav* gets to keep the object.

The *Gemara*, however, rejects this reasoning on Rava's behalf. *Ve-lo hee* — this is incorrect. *Ki ukimna rachmana bi-reshutei, le-inyan onsin* — the *Torah* places the stolen object "in the *ganav's* possession" only for the purpose of strict liability in cases of accident. *Aval le-inyan miknei, bi-reshuta de-marei kai* — but in terms of actual ownership, the object remains the owner's. The analogy is to a *sho'el*, a borrower: a borrower must compensate even for accidents, not because he owns the borrowed object, but because *kol hana'ah shelo* — all the benefit is his — and the *Torah* therefore imposes strict liability. Similarly, the *Torah* imposes strict liability on the *ganav* for accidents, but that strict liability does not transform him into the owner. The object remains the owner's; the *ganav* merely bears an extraordinary liability. Accordingly, Rava's position stands: when the object is intact, the burglar must return it, and *kim lei* does not help him because there is no monetary obligation being imposed — only the return of the owner's own property.

## Mishnah Teaching

### Broken Vessels

The *mishna* states: *haba ba-machteret ve-shibar et ha-chavit, im yesh lo damim chayav, im ein lo damim patur* — if he broke the barrel, when it is *yesh lo damim* he pays, when it is *ein lo damim* he does not. The *Gemara* parses what this implies about the case where he *took* the barrel rather than broke it.

The implication of the *mishna* seems fairly direct: it specifies the case of *breaking* and says that in a *yesh lo damim* scenario he is liable. This implies that if he merely *took* the barrel, he would have to return it even in an *ein lo damim* case — which is precisely Rava's position against Rav. The *Gemara*, however, defends Rav by arguing that the *mishna* chose to discuss the case of breaking not because the case of taking would be different, but because the case of breaking offers a *chidush* regarding the *yesh lo damim* scenario: even though the barrel is broken, the *ganav* is still liable. One might have thought that breaking — being less direct than taking — carries a different rule; the *mishna* teaches us otherwise. The implication regarding the *ein lo damim* case is therefore *lav davka* — not precise enough to constitute a contradiction of Rav's position.

### Taking vs Breaking

The *Gemara* presses on what *chidush* the *mishna* could really be offering. If the burglar broke something, of course he has to pay — he is a *mazik*, a tortfeasor. And even if he broke it accidentally, one already knows from elsewhere that *adam muad le-olam bein be-shogeg bein be-mezid bein be-ones bein be-ratzon* — a person is always liable for damage he causes, whether intentional, unintentional, or even coerced. So what precisely is the *mishna* adding? The *Gemara* concedes: *kashya* — it is a difficulty. Rashi takes care to note that *kashya* is different from *tiyuvta*: a *kashya* means the source does not work particularly well for this position, but since the *mishna* does not explicitly say that when the burglar *takes* the barrel he must return it — only that when he *breaks* it he must pay — the *mishna's* implication is not precise enough to flatly contradict Rav's view. A difficulty, yes; a refutation, no.

## Wallet on Shabbat

### Simultaneous Violations

*Motiv Rav Bibi bar Abaye*: Rav Bibi bar Abaye raises a challenge — or, as becomes clear, what initially appears to be a challenge but actually serves as support for Rav. The case: *ha-gonev kis ba-Shabbat chayav* — someone who steals a wallet on *Shabbat* is liable for the theft. Why? Because normally the two obligations — the monetary one for theft and the capital one for *chillul Shabbat* — do not arise simultaneously. The act of theft (*akviyat ha-chefetz*, taking possession) occurs the moment he lifts the wallet inside the private domain, while the act of *chillul Shabbat* occurs when he carries it into *reshut ha-rabim*. Since the theft obligation precedes the *Shabbat* violation, *kim lei* does not apply: there was already a monetary obligation before any capital-level transgression occurred.

But: *haya megarer ve-yotze* — if instead he was dragging the wallet along the ground and only completed the act of theft at the precise moment he brought the wallet into *reshut ha-rabim* by lifting it there, then the *geneivah* and the *chillul Shabbat* occur simultaneously — *issur geneiva ve-issur skilah ba'im ke-echad*. In that simultaneous case, *kim lei* would apply and he would be exempt.

This case appears to support Rav's position: the wallet is presumably still in existence, and yet the *Gemara* implies the *ganav* would not have to return it. This would be exactly what Rav maintains.

### Wallet Must Return

The *Gemara* resolves this by issuing a practical ruling: *ve-hilchata, de-shadinu be-nahara* — the exemption in that simultaneous case applies only when the wallet has been thrown into the river, meaning the money is spent and the wallet is gone. If the wallet were still intact, of course it would have to be returned. The debate between Rav and Rava is thus preserved: it is precisely the case of the intact stolen object that divides them. Rav holds even then the *ganav* keeps it; Rava holds he must return it.

### Rava's Rams Story

The *Gemara* then relates a famous episode: *Rava ignavu lei dichrei be-machterta* — remarkably, Rava himself had rams stolen from him through a tunnel. The thieves apparently had a change of heart — perhaps moved by the approach of *Erev Yom Kippur* — and came to return the rams. *Ve-lo kablinhu* — Rava refused to accept them. *Amar: hova nafik mi-pumei de-Rav* — he said, a ruling of liability issued from Rav's mouth, and even though I think Rav is wrong and that these thieves are legally obligated to return my rams, I will not accept them back.

*Tosafot* raises the obvious question: even in a *kim lei* context, there is an acknowledged obligation *latzeit yedei shamayim* — to fulfill one's moral and divine obligations even when the court will not enforce them. Why then did Rava not accept the rams? *Tosafot* answers: the thieves were returning the rams *because* they believed they were legally obligated to do so. Rava therefore refused, saying: according to Rav, you are not legally obligated. I disagree with Rav, but if you are returning the property only because you think you are compelled to, I will not accept it. If you wish to return the rams beyond your legal obligation, as an act of genuine moral restitution, I will accept. The distinction Rava is drawing — between what the court demands and what conscience requires — is important: there is a meaningful difference between legal obligation and moral obligation even in *halachic* discourse.

At bottom, the entire debate between Rav and Rava is, as noted, fundamentally a *Bava Kama* question about the nature of the *ganav's* obligation at the moment of theft — whether he becomes the owner and owes money, or whether he owes the object itself with monetary compensation as a secondary fallback. That *Bava Kama* question is being resolved here, in *Sanhedrin*, in the context of *haba ba-machteret*. This is why students who study *Bava Kama* regularly find themselves reading this passage in *Sanhedrin*.

## Conditions for Killing

### Conflicting Baraitot

A *baraita* teaches: *ein lo damim* — there is no blood-guilt. And then: *im zarcha ha-shemesh alav damim lo* — if the sun has risen upon him, there is blood-guilt. The *Gemara* asks: does the sun shine only upon him? *Mah hu im zarcha ha-shemesh alav?* What does the sun imagery mean? The first *baraita* reads it as a standard of certainty for the *owner*: *ela im barur lecha ha-davar ke-shemesh she-ein lo shalom imach, hargehu* — if it is as clear to you as the sun that he has no peace with you and is prepared to kill you, then you may kill him. *Ve-im lav, al tahargehu* — but if there is any doubt, you may not.

A second *baraita*, however, reads the sun imagery in reference to the second half of the verse — the case where it *is* blood-guilt. *Im zarcha ha-shemesh alav damim lo* — if it is as clear as the sun that there is *peace* between you, that this person would not harm you, *al tahargehu, damim lo* — only then is killing prohibited. *Ve-im lav, hargehu* — but if any doubt exists, you may kill him.

These two *baraitot* appear to be in direct tension. The first says you may kill only when it is certain the threat exists. The second says you may not kill only when it is certain the threat does not exist. The vast gray area of genuine uncertainty falls on opposite sides of the line in the two texts.

### Father-Son Distinction

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction — *lo kashya* — with a distinction that is both legally and psychologically penetrating. *Kan be-av al ha-ben, kan be-ven al ha-av* — one *baraita* addresses the case of a father breaking in to steal from his son; the other addresses the case of a son breaking in to steal from his father.

When a father breaks into his son's home, we begin from a presumption that a father would never be prepared to kill his own child. That presumption is so strong that the son may use lethal force *only* when it is unambiguously clear — like daylight — that his father has crossed that threshold and genuinely intends to harm him. Short of that certainty, the son may not act. This is the first *baraita*.

When a son breaks into his father's home, no such presumption obtains. Children kill parents for all manner of reasons, and the evidence that one's child has descended to theft provides no reliable guarantee of restraint. There the father is permitted to use lethal force unless it is as clear as the sun that his son would not harm him. Short of that, he acts in self-defense.

The *halachic* upshot of both cases together is: in any standard case of genuine uncertainty, protect your own life. The rule requiring certainty before acting applies only where we begin with a strong presumption that the intruder would not harm you — as in a father-son relationship where paternal love is so profound that it is treated as categorically restraining. But in any ordinary case, doubt resolves in favor of self-defense.

### Love vs Threat

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a striking personal example. *Amar Rav*: *kol de-ati alai be-machteret, katlinan lei* — anyone who came through a tunnel toward me, I would kill him — *livar mi-Yoni bar Kenis ve-Rav Chanina bar Shila*, these two I would not kill even if I saw them emerging from a tunnel. The students are naturally startled.

*Eileima mishum de-tzadik hu* — perhaps because they are so righteous they would surely never use lethal force? But the *Gemara* pushes back: *hacha be-machtarta* — but he is coming out of the tunnel! Whatever I knew of him yesterday, the act of desperation that has driven him to this point makes the past character reference unreliable. This is a profound observation: moral character, however established, does not inoculate against the extremes to which desperation can drive a person.

The true reason Rav would not kill those two men: *kim li be-gaveih de-meracheim alai ke-racheim av al ha-ben* — I know from personal experience that this person's love for me is like a father's love for a son. Not piety in the abstract, but a particular and known bond of love so deep that no desperation could overcome it. *Ke-racheim av al ha-ben* — and this echoes, not incidentally, the *pasuk* of *Yom Kippur*, the divine compassion of a father for his child. The exception that proves the rule: absent such personal certainty of love, assume the worst and act accordingly.

## Shabbat Application

### Killing Permitted

A *baraita* teaches: *damim lo bein be-chol bein be-Shabbat* — when it is *yesh lo damim*, you may not kill him, whether on a weekday or on *Shabbat*. And *ein lo damim* — you may kill him to defend yourself — *bein be-chol bein be-Shabbat*, whether on a weekday or on *Shabbat*. There is no suspension of the right of self-defense because the day happens to be *Shabbat*.

### Court Punishment Model

The *Gemara* finds the *ein lo damim* case obvious and asks why the *baraita* needs to state it. The answer given — and this is remarkable — is that one might have thought to apply the logic of court-imposed executions: *midi de-havei a-harugei beit din, de-be-Shabbat lo katlinan lehu* — just as the court does not execute criminals on *Shabbat*, perhaps one may not kill the burglar in self-defense on *Shabbat* either.

This is a stunning *hava amina*. No one would say you cannot defend your life on *Shabbat* if the underlying logic were purely *pikuach nefesh* — saving a life, which overrides *Shabbat* unambiguously. The only way this *hava amina* makes sense is if, at some level, the killing of the burglar is being conceptualized as a court-imposed punishment that the defender is administering on the court's behalf. The *baraita* therefore teaches: *ka-mashma lan de-katlinan* — you kill him regardless. Whether the *kol mashma lan* entirely abandons the court-punishment framework or merely refines it — holding that this is still a quasi-judicial act but one that may and must be carried out on *Shabbat* — is left somewhat open.

### Obligation Save Life

The *yesh lo damim* clause presents a different and equally striking teaching. *Bishlama ein lo damim* — the *ein lo damim* case is the one that needs explanation, as discussed above. But *yesh lo damim bein be-chol bein be-Shabbat* — when the burglar's killing would be prohibited, this is prohibited whether on a weekday or *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* initially wonders why this needs to be stated: *hash-ta be-chol lo katlinan lei, be-Shabbat mibaya* — if you cannot kill him on Wednesday, obviously you cannot on *Shabbat*.

*Amar Rav Sheshet*: the clause addresses a different scenario entirely. Imagine the burglar was coming to rob — but it was a case like a father and son, or someone known to be incapable of harming the homeowner, so there is no right to kill him. He dug the tunnel on *Shabbat*, violating it throughout. Then the tunnel collapses on him. Must the homeowner save his life, even if doing so requires violating *Shabbat*? The answer is yes. *Lo nitzrecha ela le-fakei'ach alav et ha-gal* — the *baraita* is teaching that you must remove the rubble. The obligation to save life (*pikuach nefesh*) applies even to this man, even at this moment.

This is a morally demanding ruling: the very person who a moment ago was robbing you, desecrating *Shabbat* in the process, must be rescued on *Shabbat* if the tunnel collapses. *Yesh lo damim* means not only that it is prohibited to kill him — it means he retains his full human claim on life and on your efforts to preserve it.

Rashi notes the flip side explicitly: when the burglar was in an *ein lo damim* situation — when he could have been killed — and the tunnel collapses on him, there is apparently no obligation to save him. Rashi's reasoning seems to be that since he forfeited his life, *ein lo damim*, he is already as a dead man and one need not be *mechalel Shabbat* to save him. This is difficult to understand on pure self-defense grounds: once the tunnel collapses, the threat is neutralized, and he is simply a human being under rubble. The logic becomes more intelligible under the court-punishment model: he is someone the legal system has effectively already condemned, and therefore there is no obligation to rescue him at the cost of a Torah prohibition. But Rashi's position here remains arresting.

## Third Parties & Methods

### Third Party Intervention

The *baraita* states: *ve-huka va-met ein lo damim, be-chol adam* — when the burglar is killed in the *ein lo damim* scenario, there is no blood-guilt, and this applies to *any person* who kills him. The *Gemara* explains why this needs to be stated.

One might have thought that only the homeowner — the *ba'al ha-bayit* — has the right to kill the burglar. The logic would be: it is the homeowner whose life is in danger, because *ein adam ma'amid atzmo al mamono* — the owner will not stand by and let his property be taken — and the burglar knows the owner may confront him and has resolved to kill if necessary. But a bystander's life is not directly threatened. The burglar did not calculate that a random passerby would resist him. So what entitles a stranger to intervene with lethal force?

*Ka-mashma lan de-rodef hu* — the *baraita* teaches that the burglar has the status of a *rodef*, a pursuer. And as the next *mishna* will address in detail, anyone may — and must — intervene to stop a *rodef* from taking another's life. It is not merely a right of personal self-defense; it is a communal obligation to protect the potential victim. The intervention of a third party is thus not vigilantism but a fulfillment of *lo ta'amod al dam rei'echa*. The right of intervention is obligatory, and the question of how much personal risk the intervener must accept is itself a significant discussion among the *acharonim*.

An interesting debate among *acharonim* concerns whether the license to kill the *rodef* is *hutra* — fundamentally not an act of killing at all, as implied by *ein lo damim* — or merely *dechuya*, a prohibited act pushed aside by overriding necessity. The practical difference may bear on the requirement of *yakhol le-hatzilo be-echad me-evarav*: whether an outsider is held to a stricter standard than the victim himself in exhausting alternatives before resorting to lethal force.

### Any Means Necessary

The *baraita* continues: *ve-met be-chol mita she-ata yakhol le-hamito* — he may be killed by whatever means are available. You are not restricted to a particular method. The *Gemara* asks: why does this need to be stated?

### Murderer Execution

The *Gemara* attempts to derive this principle from the laws of executing a convicted murderer. A *baraita* states: *mot yumat ha-makeh rotzeach hu* — the killer shall surely be put to death. *Ein li ela be-mita ha-amura bo* — I know only that the prescribed method (which is the sword) is to be used. But the verse also says *mot yumat* — he shall surely die — *mi-kol makom*, from any circumstances, implying that if the prescribed execution cannot be carried out, any available means may be used.

The *Gemara* considers whether one could derive the rule for *haba ba-machteret* from this precedent. It concludes that one cannot, because the case of *rotzeach* and the case of *go'el ha-dam* (the blood-avenger) together constitute *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad* — two scriptural instances that teach the same principle — and the rule is that such paired instances do not serve as the basis for general derivation; they are treated as the exception rather than the rule. Therefore the *baraita* must explicitly state that in the case of *haba ba-machteret* as well, any available means may be used.

What is telling here again is the logic of the discussion. The natural argument for using any available means against someone who is about to kill you is simply: do whatever you must to save your life. The *Gemara's* framework, however, approaches the question through the lens of court-imposed punishments and their applicable methods — *harugei beit din* — and derives the rule by analogy to the execution of a murderer. This persistent framing reinforces the second model introduced at the outset: that the killing in self-defense is being conceptualized as quasi-judicial, with the defender acting as an arm of the legal system.

## Tunnel Warning

### Beyond Literal Tunnel

The *baraita* asks: *ein li ela machteret* — do I know only that the law applies when the entry is through a literal tunnel? What about entry through the roof, the courtyard, or other means? *Talmud lomar: im yimatze ha-ganav mi-kol makom* — "if the thief is found" — wherever he is found, however he entered. The tunnel is not the *halachically* defining feature. *Mipnei she-rov ganavim metzuyyin ba-machteret* — the *Torah* mentions the tunnel simply because it is the common case; it is descriptive, not restrictive.

### Tunnel as Warning

A second *baraita* makes the same point with identical logic, extending the law to entry through a roof, courtyard, or covered portico. But then it adds a crucial principle: *machterato zo hi hatraato* — his tunnel is his warning. This phrase deserves careful attention.

Rashi explains: by going to the extraordinary length of digging a tunnel to gain entry, the burglar has demonstrated that he is prepared to do anything, including kill, to complete his mission. The tunnel functions as the equivalent of a declaration — a warning — that lethal force is on the table. No further *hatraa* from the homeowner is needed. Since the burglar has already shown, by his actions, that he knows and accepts the risk of lethal confrontation, we may proceed to act accordingly.

### Back Door Hatraa

But what if the burglar enters through the back door without digging? In that case, Rashi holds, the presumption of readiness to kill does not automatically apply. The burglar may simply be taking advantage of an easy entry point without any intention of violence. For the homeowner to be entitled to use lethal force in that scenario, classic *hatraa* would be required: witnesses, a warning addressed to the burglar, and the burglar's acceptance of the warning — *yodea ani ve-al menat kein ani oseh* — I know the consequences and I proceed nonetheless.

This is a striking application. Classic *hatraa* is the procedural requirement for a *beit din* to impose a capital sentence: witnesses must warn the perpetrator, he must acknowledge the warning and accept it, and only then does criminal liability attach. Rashi is importing that entire framework here, into the context of personal self-defense. The homeowner, before using lethal force against a non-tunnel burglar, must essentially conduct a *hatraa* as though he were a court. This again powerfully reinforces the second model: the defender is not merely a private individual acting in extremis, but someone who, when circumstances require, operates as a representative of the legal system, with all the procedural obligations that implies.

The tunnel, then, serves a dual function. Practically, it identifies the class of burglars who are presumed to be armed with violent intent. Conceptually, it marks the threshold at which the procedural requirements of the legal system are satisfied by the very act of the burglar himself, without any further formality. *Machterato zo hi hatraato*: the tunnel is the warning, the verdict, and the sentence, all at once.
