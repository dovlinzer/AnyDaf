# Hullin 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vessel & Revi'is

### Rava's Vessel Rule

The sugya opens with a ruling of Rava: *kli she'ein bo revi'is ein notlin mimenu l'yadayim* — a vessel that does not contain a *revi'is* of water (approximately three and a half ounces) may not be used for *netilas yadayim*. The Gemara immediately notes a second, slightly different formulation of Rava's statement: *kli she'eino machzik revi'is ein notlin mimenu l'yadayim* — a vessel that cannot *hold* a *revi'is* may not be used. The distinction between these two versions is significant. The first formulation demands that the vessel actually contain a *revi'is* of liquid; the second demands only that the vessel be capable of holding that volume, even if the liquid present is less than a *revi'is*. According to the second version, the decisive criterion is the capacity of the vessel, not the quantity of water inside it at the moment of use.

This introduces two distinct requirements: the requirement of a *kli* — a proper vessel — and the minimum measure of a *revi'is*. The connection between them is not incidental. Some *Rishonim* note that the very word *netila* in the blessing *al netilas yadayim* reflects the Aramaic *natla*, meaning a vessel, so that the *kli* is in fact central to the entire institution. A vessel smaller than *revi'is* capacity would not be considered a meaningful vessel at all, and thus the size requirement is itself the basis for the *kli* requirement.

### Revi'is & Remnant

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between the two formulations by distinguishing between one person washing and two people washing: *lo kashya — ha l'chad, ha l'trei*. The vessel must begin with a full *revi'is* of liquid, but once it has been used for *netilas yadayim* by one person, a second person may wash from whatever remains, even if it is less than a *revi'is*. A *Baraisa* is cited in support: *mei revi'is notlin l'yadayim l'echad v'afilu l'shnayim* — a *revi'is* of water suffices for *netilas yadayim* for one person and even for two. The fact that two people can share three and a half ounces is striking in itself, and points toward a conception of *netilas yadayim* that may require only minimal coverage — perhaps the fingertips or a single pouring.

The Gemara then qualifies this further: *v'lo hi — shani hasam mishum d'ka'asi mishirei tahara*. The case where the second person may use less than a *revi'is* is not a general dispensation from the quantity requirement; it is a special case because the remaining water derives from *shirei tahara* — the remnant of water that was already used for *taharah* purposes, having originally met the *revi'is* standard. That prior association with *taharah* lends the remnant its efficacy.

### Rav Sheshes' Questions

Rav Sheshes then posed a series of questions to Ameimar, pressing on each of the criteria that had been established. His first question: *kapdisu amana* — do you actually care about the vessel itself? Rashi explains this as asking whether the vessel must be whole, or whether a broken vessel would suffice. The answer was *in* — yes, the vessel matters. His second question: *achazusa* — do you care about the appearance of the water? Recall that certain types of water — extremely hot water, or water that does not look like water — may be disqualified; Ameimar confirmed that the appearance of the water is indeed a requirement. His third question: *ashi'ura* — do you care about the minimum quantity, the *revi'is*? Again, the answer was yes.

A second version of this exchange is also cited. According to that version, Ameimar's response was more selective: *amana v'achazusa kapdinan — ashi'ura lo kapdinan* — I care about the vessel and the appearance of the water, but I do not require a strict *shiur* of liquid quantity. This alternative reading is consistent with the earlier conclusion that the second person may wash from less than a *revi'is*, and the Gemara brings the same *Baraisa* — *mei revi'is notlin l'yadayim l'echad v'afilu l'shnayim* — as support for the view that no fixed quantity is required, as long as the water originated from a *revi'is* used for *netilas yadayim*.

### Standard Measures

The Gemara records that Rav Yaakov of Nehar Pakod established a standard glass vessel — a *natla bas revi'isa* — that held exactly a *revi'is*, which people could use as a reference to determine whether their own vessels were large enough for *netilas yadayim*. This was a form of communal standards: a bureau of measures by which people could calibrate the capacity of their vessels. Similarly, Rav Ashi in Hutzal established a *kuza bas revi'isa* — a pottery vessel holding a *revi'is* — for the same purpose. The practical message was reinforced by the physical standard: the vessel had to hold a *revi'is*, and people should know what that looks like.

### Acceptable Vessels

Rava further teaches that one need not use an elaborate or purpose-made vessel. *Magufas chavit she-tiknah notlin mimena l'yadayim* — if one takes the stopper of a barrel, carves out an indentation capable of holding liquid, and it meets the *revi'is* capacity, one may wash *netilas yadayim* from it. Similarly, a *cheimasa* or *kifiza* — types of flask or water skin — if properly adjusted, may be used for *netilas yadayim*. The point is that a wide range of objects can qualify as a *kli* for this purpose, as long as they meet the functional criteria.

### Disqualified Items

On the other side of the line stand objects that, despite being capable of containing liquid, are not classified as proper vessels for *netilas yadayim*: *sak v'kuppa — af al pi d'mekablin, ein notlin mehem l'yadayim*. A sack used for storing grain and a box, though they may physically hold liquid, cannot be used for *netilas yadayim*. Their primary function is not as liquid vessels; they are not defined as *kelim* in the relevant sense. This raises a genuine line-drawing question: what exactly distinguishes a vessel that qualifies from one that does not? The answer is not purely functional — it is definitional. The object must be recognized as a type of vessel associated with liquid, not merely something that happens to hold water in a given circumstance.

## Wrapping & Washing

### Wrapping Question

The Gemara now takes up a different question: *mahu le'echol b'mappa* — what is the law if one eats bread wrapped in a napkin, or with one's hands wrapped in cloth? The underlying premise of *netilas yadayim* is connected to the world of *tumah v'taharah*: hands were declared *sheniyos l'tumah* and thus capable of rendering food *tamei* upon contact. The Rabbis, however, attached the requirement specifically to the act of eating rather than to touching alone — one can handle bread without washing, but once one sits down to eat, washing is required.

The Gemara's question here is the inverse: if touching is the concern, what happens when eating occurs without touching? If bread is wrapped and one's hands never come into contact with it, is *netilas yadayim* still required? The question is framed practically — *mi chashinan dilma naga bei* — are we concerned that one will inadvertently touch the bread even if it is wrapped? Or is *netilas yadayim* a formal requirement that attaches to the act of eating regardless of any concern about contact?

### Rabbi Tzaddok Case

A *Baraisa* is brought from the tractate of Sukkah: when they gave food to Rabbi Tzaddok, he ate less than a *kabeitzah*, took it in a *mappa* — a cloth — ate it outside the *sukkah*, and did not recite *Birkat Hamazon* afterward. The case illustrates that eating less than a *kabeitzah* does not obligate one to eat in the *sukkah* and does not require *Birkat Hamazon*, because the sense of *seudat keva* requires at least a *kabeitzah* — the full satisfaction of *v'achalta v'savata*. Rabbi Tzaddok also did not wash his hands, apparently on account of the fact that the bread was wrapped.

### Size vs Wrapping

The Gemara asks: *mai lav* — does this not suggest that the *kabeitzah* measure is what triggers the requirement of *netilas yadayim*? On this reading, it would only be the smaller quantity that exempted him, not the wrapping. The Gemara counters: *dilma* — perhaps not; perhaps the *kabeitzah* measure addresses only the *sukkah* and *Birkat Hamazon* obligations, and it was specifically the fact that the bread was wrapped that exempted him from *netilas yadayim*. The case remains inconclusive, but it leaves open both possibilities: that the wrapping itself is sufficient to exempt, or that the relevant factor was the quantity of bread.

## Shiur of Bread

### Kabeitzah Measure

The discussion of Rabbi Tzaddok's case raises the broader question of what minimum amount of bread triggers the requirement of *netilas yadayim*. One reading of the case suggests that a *kabeitzah* is the threshold — below that, no *netilas yadayim* is required — which would parallel the *kabeitzah* as the threshold for *Birkat Hamazon* and for eating in the *sukkah*, rooted in the concept of genuine *seudah* and *seviyah*. If *netilas yadayim* is tied to the eating of bread as a meal, it would make sense that only a *kabeitzah* — a quantity that rises to the level of meaningful eating — would trigger the obligation.

### Kezayit Ruling

The *halacha* as ruled by most authorities, however, is that *netilas yadayim* is required already from a *kezayit*. This is consistent with the ruling regarding *Birkat Hamazon*, where a *kezayit* is sufficient to require *benching*, at least *mi-d'rabbanan*, even though the biblical obligation of *Birkat Hamazon* may require the fuller satisfaction of a *kabeitzah*. The parallel logic applies to *netilas yadayim*: most authorities require it from a *kezayit* onward.

### Practical Halacha

Nonetheless, there remains a dispute regarding the status of bread between a *kezayit* and a *kabeitzah*. There is an opinion that holds the *kabeitzah* to be the proper *shiur* for *netilas yadayim*, either because that is the genuine *shiur* of a *seudah* or because the minimum quantity that can become *tamei* at a biblical level is a *kabeitzah*. The practical ruling, as recorded in the *Mishnah Berurah*, is that for less than a *kabeitzah* one should perform *netilas yadayim* without a *bracha*, though many authorities hold that once there is a *kezayit* or more, the *bracha* is recited.

## Wrapping & Washing…

### Rav & Shmuel Case

The Gemara returns to the question of wrapping. Shmuel found Rav eating with his bread wrapped in a cloth. Assuming that Rav had not washed his hands and was using the wrapping as an alternative, Shmuel challenged him: *avdin kadein* — is this how one behaves? Rav replied: *da'ati katzra alai* — I am simply fastidious and do not like to handle bread directly, but of course I did perform *netilas yadayim*. The episode establishes clearly that both Rav and Shmuel hold that even when bread is wrapped, *netilas yadayim* is required.

### Rabbi Zeira's Finding

When Rabbi Zeira went up to Eretz Yisrael, he found Rabbi Ammi and Rabbi Assi eating with their bread wrapped in worn rags. He assumed they were using this as an alternative to *netilas yadayim* and challenged them: *trei gavi raberavi kevaseichu ritu de-Rav u-Shmuel* — two such great scholars, are you departing from the established position of both Rav and Shmuel? In his own view, and as established from the story above, both Rav and Shmuel held that wrapping does not exempt one from *netilas yadayim*.

### Wrapping Terumah

Rabbi Zeira's challenge, however, was itself mistaken. He had overlooked a teaching transmitted by Rav Tachlifa bar Avimi in the name of Shmuel: *hitiru mappa l'ochlei terumah v'lo hitiru mappa l'ochlei taharos* — wrapping one's hands is permitted for those eating *terumah*, but it is not permitted for those eating ordinary food who wash merely as a rabbinic requirement. Rabbi Ammi and Rabbi Assi were *kohanim* eating actual *terumah*, and for *kohanim* eating *terumah*, who are punctilious about the laws of *tumah v'taharah*, the wrapping of the hands is a recognized alternative to *netilas yadayim*. The seriousness of *terumah* and the care that *kohanim* take in its consumption provide the justification for leniency there. For ordinary eating of bread, however, no such alternative is available, and wrapping remains insufficient.

It is worth noting the broader context: the discussion of *terumah* in the Gemara's time reflects the reality that at least some *kohanim* in the period of the *Amoraim* were actually eating *terumah*, at least *mi-d'rabbanan*. While in principle all of us today are assumed to be *tamei meis* and thus unable to eat *terumah*, the Gemara's cases reflect a different reality, in which the laws of *tumah v'taharah* were actively observed. Rabbi Ammi and Rabbi Assi, as *kohanim*, were operating within that framework, and for them the wrapping was entirely permissible.

## Eater vs Feeder

### Being Fed

The Gemara raises a new question: *ochal machmas ma'achil — tzarich netilas yadayim o lo* — if one is eating because another is feeding him, placing bread directly into his mouth, does the person being fed require *netilas yadayim*? This question sharpens the fundamental issue. It is one thing to debate wrapping when there is some residual possibility of incidental contact; here, the concern about contact is all but eliminated. If the feeder is placing the food directly into the mouth of the one eating, the eater's hands need never come near the bread. Does *netilas yadayim* still apply?

### Rav Hamnuna Case

A story is brought: Rav Huna bar Sachorah was attending Rav Hamnuna and fed him meat, and Rav Hamnuna ate it. Rav Huna bar Sachorah remarked: *ila'v de-Rav Hamnuna at, lo sefina lach* — were you not Rav Hamnuna, I would not have fed you without ensuring you had performed *netilas yadayim*. The implication seems to be that Rav Hamnuna's exceptional reliability — the confidence that he would not touch the bread — was what justified feeding him without *netilas yadayim*. The Gemara, however, rejects this reading: *lo — d'zaris kadim u'mashi yadei me'ikara*. The statement did not mean that because Rav Hamnuna could be trusted not to touch, he did not need to wash; it meant that Rav Hamnuna, with characteristic alacrity, had already washed his hands in advance. The feeding was done only because Rav Hamnuna had already performed *netilas yadayim*, not as a substitute for it.

### Waiter Rule

A further teaching is cited, transmitted by Rabbi Zeira in the name of Rav: *lo yitein adam prusa l'soch piv shel shamash ela im kein yodeia bo shenatal yadav* — one may not place a piece of bread into the mouth of a *shamash* — a waiter — unless one knows that he has already performed *netilas yadayim*. This seems to settle the matter quite clearly: even a person who is being fed and not handling the bread himself must have performed *netilas yadayim*. The Gemara, however, notes a possible qualification: *shani shamash d'tarid* — the *shamash* may be different because he is constantly distracted, constantly handling many things, and is therefore liable to inadvertently touch the bread even when being fed. Perhaps the *shamash* requires *netilas yadayim* for this practical reason, but a person in circumstances where there is truly no possibility of touching would not.

The Gemara then records a related ruling: *v'hashamash mevarech al kol kos v'chos v'eino mevarech al kol prusa u'prusa* — the *shamash* must recite a new *bracha* for each cup of wine offered to him, since such offers are infrequent and he cannot presume more will come, but he need not recite a new *bracha* for each piece of bread, since people commonly offer the waiter bread and he may assume the offers will continue. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and holds that even for bread, each piece requires a new *bracha*. Rav Papa reconciles the two positions: when there is an *adam chashuv* — an important person — at the table, the host will want to appear generous and will feed the *shamash* more regularly, so he may presume subsequent offers of bread; when there is no such person present, each offer is uncertain and a new *bracha* is required. This reconciliation resolves the dispute about *brachos* but does not resolve the underlying question about *netilas yadayim*.

### Shmuel's Case

The Gemara at last brings a decisive story. The father of Shmuel found Shmuel crying. When he asked why, Shmuel explained that his teachers had rebuked him, shaming him for having fed his child without performing *netilas yadayim* — *kasafis livrai velo mashis yadach*. Shmuel's father responded: why did you not wash? Shmuel replied: *hu achil va-ana mashina* — he is the one eating; do I have to wash? *Netilas yadayim* belongs to the eater, not to the feeder. His father's response was pointed: *lo mistayei delo gemiri — bimemcha nami machu?* It is not enough that these people are ignorant of the *halacha*; they are also berating you for it? The point being that those who do not understand the nuance are precisely the ones who raise the loudest protest.

### Halachic Conclusion

The Gemara concludes with an explicit ruling: *d'hilchata — ochal machmas ma'achil tzarich netilas yadayim, ma'achil eino tzarich netilas yadayim*. The one being fed requires *netilas yadayim*; the feeder does not. This conclusion is coherent with the entire trajectory of the sugya. *Netilas yadayim* was instituted not as a response to the act of handling bread but as a response to the act of eating it. Touching without eating does not require it; feeding without eating does not require it; but eating — even when the bread is wrapped, even when someone else places it in one's mouth — requires it. The requirement attaches to the eater alone, and this may reflect not only the *tumah v'taharah* origins of the institution but also a deeper logic: the act of eating itself, the *achat*, is what demands the sanctification that *netilas yadayim* provides, in the spirit of *ve-hitkadashtem vi'heyisem kedoshim*.

## Meat & Milk Table

### Wrapping Meat/Cheese

After the lengthy digression into *mayim rishonim* and *mayim acharonim*, the Gemara returns to the topic of separation between meat and milk. The *Mishnah* teaches: *tzorer adam basar u-gevina b'mitpachas achas u-vilvad she-lo yihyu nogi'im zeh va-zeh* — one may wrap meat and cheese together in a single cloth, provided they do not touch. This ruling is notable: on the same table during a meal, one cannot have meat and milk present simultaneously, yet here, wrapping them together in a cloth is permitted, so long as there is no contact between them. The distinction may rest on the context of eating — at the table, one might inadvertently share or consume from both; in a cloth, one is simply storing or transporting, and the concern of accidental consumption does not arise. Alternatively, as the discussion develops, the concept of the prohibition may be tied to what constitutes eating together in a meaningful sense, not mere physical proximity.

### Strangers Rule

The *Mishnah* continues with a ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *shenei achsanaim ochlin al shulchan echad — zeh basar ve-zeh gevina — v'ein chosheshin* — two *achsanaim*, strangers or chance guests, may eat at the same table, one eating meat and the other eating milk products, and there is no concern. Since they do not know one another, there is no risk that they will inadvertently offer food to one another or take from one another's plate. The prohibition of eating meat and milk at the same table is rooted in the concern for inadvertent sharing, and where that concern is absent, so is the prohibition.

### Cold vs Heat

The Gemara first addresses the *Mishnah*'s opening clause about wrapping meat and cheese in a single cloth. The question is raised: *ve-chi noge'a zeh va-zeh mai havei* — if they do touch, what is the harm? They are cold — *tzonin be-tzonin hu* — and cold-to-cold contact does not transfer taste. Abaye answers: granted, *kelipa* — the removal of a surface layer — is not required when foods are cold. Nevertheless, *hadacha mi lo ba'ei* — rinsing is still necessary. The concern is not taste transfer through heat but the presence of greasy residue on the surface of the food, which can transfer from one to the other even without heat. A rinse suffices to address this. This principle has practical implications wherever cold meat and dairy foods come into physical contact: no *kelipa* is needed, but rinsing with attention to any fatty residue may be required.

### Strangers Requirement

The Gemara then turns to the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rav Chana bar Avmi reports in the name of Shmuel: *lo shanu ela she'ein makirim zeh et zeh* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's dispensation applies only when the two diners do not recognize one another at all; they are genuine strangers. *Aval makirim zeh et zeh — assur* — if they know one another, even without being close friends, eating meat and milk at the same table is forbidden. A *Baraisa* is cited in confirmation: two guests at the same inn, one coming from the north and one from the south — true strangers — one carrying meat and the other carrying cheese, may eat at the same table without concern.

### Tfisa Achat

The *Baraisa* adds a crucial formulation: *v'lo asru ela b'tfisa achat* — the prohibition was only imposed in the case of *tfisa achat*, one bundle. The Gemara immediately asks: *tfisa achat salka da'atach* — do you literally mean that the prohibition requires the two foods to be physically bundled together? That would be an absurdly narrow reading. The Gemara therefore clarifies: *ela ke'ein tfisa achat* — the meaning is *like* one bundle. What this denotes is a situation where the two people eating are connected in some sense — they know one another, there is a shared dimension to their meal — not merely that they are physically at the same table. The prohibition on eating meat and milk at the same table requires some sense of common eating, some social or relational dimension that creates the concern for inadvertent sharing.

### No Distinctions

Rav Yeimar bar Shalmiya posed a question to Abaye: what about two brothers who know each other well but are in the midst of a feud and are stringent with one another — *makpidin zeh al zeh* — refusing to share food? Since there is no social connection and no possibility of sharing, would the prohibition apply? Abaye's response invokes the principle of *lo plug*: *yomru kol ha-serikin asurin ve-serikei Baitos mutarin* — if we permit this case, people will say all decorative *matzot* are forbidden yet the designs of Baitos are permitted. The principle of *lo plug* holds that once a formal category has been established, we do not make exceptions within it based on particular circumstances, even where the underlying rationale does not apply. The distinction between strangers and non-strangers is workable; assessing the degree of social connection or animosity within the category of non-strangers is not. Even feuding brothers, therefore, may not eat meat and milk at the same table.

A challenge is raised: the Gemara in Moed Katan (in the name of Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan) permits one who owns a single garment to launder it on *Chol ha-Moed*, even though laundering is generally forbidden. Is this not also a case of making an exception within a general rule — a violation of *lo plug*? The response, attributed to Mar bar Rav Ashi, is that in that case, *izoro mochiach alav* — his belt tells the story. When a person strips off his only garment to wash it, he does so visibly, in plain view; everyone can see why the exception applies. When an exception is self-evidently justified and apparent to all, it does not open the door to other exceptions, because the exceptional nature of the case is transparent. Other cases, however — such as brothers who happen to be feuding — involve facts that are not visible or verifiable, and cannot form the basis for categorical distinctions.

### Separations

Tosafot seizes on the Gemara's language of *ke'ein tfisa achat* to argue that even when two people know one another, the prohibition may not apply if there is some form of separation between them. Tosafot and the *Rishonim* discuss two types of separation: a physical barrier that would prevent someone from reaching across and inadvertently taking from the other's food, and a *siman* — a reminder — that signals to the participants that they are eating separately. Even without a physical barrier, something that marks off one person's eating as distinct from the other's — a placemat, a piece of paper, or any visible indicator — can serve as a sufficient reminder. The logic tracks the concept of *tfisa achat*: once it is clear that the two are not eating together in any meaningful sense, the concern that animates the prohibition does not apply.

### Table Types

The practical latitude offered by Tosafot is also informed by a basic historical observation. The *shulchan* of the Gemara and the *Mishnah* was not the large communal table of modern experience. In the world of the Talmud, eating was done Roman-style: individual trays were brought before each diner, and the *shulchan* referred to that personal tray. When the *Mishnah* in Pesachim speaks of the *shulchan* being brought before the *seder* participants containing the *matzah* and other foods, it refers to this individual tray. To eat at the same *shulchan* in that sense was to eat quite literally from the same surface, in close and intimate proximity. In contemporary life, where a large shared table serves many diners, the sense of *ke'ein tfisa achat* is considerably weaker even without any formal separation. It is on this basis that the ruling emerges that nowadays, some form of separation — whether a physical barrier or a visible *siman* — suffices to permit two people sitting at the same large table to eat meat and milk respectively.
