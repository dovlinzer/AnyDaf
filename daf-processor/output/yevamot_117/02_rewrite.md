# Yevamot 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shammai vs. Hillel

### Ketubah Collection

The daf opens in the middle of a running discussion about the foundational *chiddush* of *Chazal*: the willingness to believe a woman who testifies that her husband died, enabling her to remarry. This innovation arose from a specific historical scenario involving a husband who died during the wheat harvest, and it generated a remarkable debate in which *Beis Shammai* took the more lenient position. The *mishnah* on 117b records another such debate — again, with *Beis Shammai* on the side of leniency.

*Beis Shammai omrim tinasei v'sitol kesubasah*: once she is permitted to remarry, she may also collect her *kesubah* from her husband's estate. *Beis Hillel omrim tinasei v'lo tital kesubasah*: they are prepared to believe her testimony for purposes of personal status and remarriage, but not to allow her to collect from the estate. The concern is not only one of testimonial weight. There is a secondary worry that allowing monetary collection alongside remarriage makes the entire arrangement more attractive — not merely liberating her from the *agunah* situation, but providing a financial incentive that could color, whether consciously or unconsciously, the quality of her testimony.

### Shammai Package Deal

*Beis Shammai* presses back: *hishvitem ervah chamurah v'lo nakil mamon hakal?* You have already crossed the far more serious threshold — releasing her from the prohibition of *ervah*, treating her as no longer a married woman, permitting her to marry another man — and yet you refuse to cross what should be the easier threshold of monetary collection? *Beis Shammai*'s argument is that the permissive ruling on *ervah* and the permissive ruling on *mamon* are a package deal. If she is believed enough to remarry, she is believed enough to collect.

### Hillel Brothers Inherit

*Beis Hillel* responds with a striking argument: *matzinu she-ein ha-achim nichnasin lenachalah al piha* — we find that the brothers cannot enter into the inheritance on the basis of her testimony alone. This concession, made by *Beis Shammai* themselves, demonstrates that her belief is not across-the-board. If her testimony constituted genuine *eidus* in the full sense, the brothers — or father, or other heirs — would be entitled to divide the estate on that basis. But take that testimony to a court handling inheritance and they will demand proper witnesses; a single woman's testimony does not constitute the standard of proof required for probate. The fact that *Beis Shammai* agrees on this point shows that her believability is context-specific.

### Status vs. Monetary

What emerges is a clean distinction: her testimony is accepted in the context of the court adjudicating her personal status — whether she is a widow permitted to remarry — but not in a separate court case concerning the distribution of her husband's estate. *Beis Hillel* carries this logic further: if the testimony is not universal but court-specific, then even within the domain of personal status, one can distinguish between her status as a widow permitted to remarry and her status as a widow entitled to collect her *kesubah*. These are two different claims, even if they often travel together.

### Ketubah Text

*Beis Shammai* offers a textual counter: *v'lo mi-sefer kesubah nilmod?* Can we not learn the package-deal nature of the ruling from the *kesubah* document itself? The language of the *kesubah* as they understood it reads: *she-im tinasei le-acher titali mah she-kasuv lichi* — if you remarry someone else, you are entitled to collect what is written for you. The *kesubah* itself thus ties the permission to remarry to the right to collect, making them inseparable. When her testimony is accepted in family court, the *kesubah*'s own language makes the monetary entitlement a built-in consequence. The probate court is a separate matter — that testimony remains irrelevant there — but within the framework of her own status and remarriage, the *kesubah* creates the package.

### Hillel Concedes

*Ve'chozru Beis Hillel le'horot k'divrei Beis Shammai* — and *Beis Hillel* reversed their ruling and held in accordance with *Beis Shammai*. Even they ultimately acknowledged that once the *kesubah*'s own language creates this connection, the monetary collection follows from the permitted remarriage. The two cannot be separated when the very document at the center of the case binds them together.

The *Gemara* adds a related ruling from Rav Chisda: if the *yevamah* performs *yibum* with her brother-in-law, he enters into the inheritance on her account — *nichnas lenachalah al piha* — because the inheritance in that case is not a separate proceeding but flows directly from his status as one who has performed *yibum* and thereby stepped into his brother's role. The *Torah* itself says *yakum al shem achiv*: he shall stand in his brother's name, and with that comes the estate.

### Ketubah Derash

The *Gemara* then comments that *Beis Hillel* and *Beis Shammai* essentially treated the language of the *kesubah* as quasi-scriptural — *heim darshu midrash ketubah* — subjecting it to the same kind of textual analysis one applies to *pesukim*. The *Gemara* uses this as an occasion to note that we, too, can do *midrash pesukim*, and the *pasuk yakum al shem achiv* allows us to derive that the estate passes with the act of *yibum*.

A *Tosafos* cited in this context notes that the approach of doing *midrash ketubah* — treating the *kesubah*'s precise phrasing as determinative — is characterized in the *Gemara* in *Kesubot* as a *Beis Shammai* methodology. *Beis Hillel* does not generally engage in *midrash ketubah*. The broader question of what it means to treat a contractual document with the kind of textual weight one accords to Scripture — giving decisive weight to precise formulation rather than seeking intent or context — is a topic that will be developed extensively when the learning reaches *Maseches Kesubot*.

## Rav Nachman Intent

### Two Scenarios

Rav Nachman introduces a crucial procedural distinction. When a woman comes to *beit din* and says *meis ba'ali — hiteiruni lehinasay* — "my husband died, permit me to remarry" — the ruling follows naturally: *matirin osah lehinasay v'nosnim lah kesubasah*, we permit her to remarry and she receives her *kesubah*. The intent is clear, the case is framed in the domain of personal status, and the *kesubah* follows as a package.

But now suppose she comes to *beit din* and says *tenu li kesubasi* — give me my *kesubah* — without expressing any interest in remarriage. In that case, Rav Nachman rules: *af lehinasay ein matirin osah* — not only do we not give her the *kesubah*, we do not even permit her to remarry. The reason is *ada'ata de'kesubah asai* — she came with the intention of collecting the *kesubah*. She has framed her case as a monetary claim.

### Wrong Courtroom

Rashi's explanation is precise: *uva'afokei mamona sahadusa ba'inan* — when the case is about extracting money, we require formal *eidus*. This is not primarily a suspicion that she is lying or manipulating the system in order to collect. Rather, she has walked into the wrong courtroom. The door she entered is the door of *dinei mamonos*, and in that courtroom the standard of proof is full testimony. The special rabbinic dispensation that allows her single testimony to carry weight was created for the domain of personal status — the family-court door, so to speak — not the civil-court door of monetary claims.

### Locked In

This raises the obvious question: why can she not simply close that door and open the other one? The *sugya* strongly implies that she cannot — that once she has framed the case as a monetary claim, she is locked into that framing. The ruling does not suggest that we send her home, tell her to come back and say the right words, and then grant both permissions. She appears to be stuck. This is a striking and somewhat troubling feature of the analysis, given the lengths to which the rabbis went in creating the *agunah* dispensation in the first place. It is worth noting that the *Gemara* need not have gone down this path at all.

## Simultaneous Claims

### Both Together

The *Gemara* then raises the *ibaya*: what if she demands both simultaneously — *hiteiruni lehinasay v'tenu li ketubati*, "permit me to remarry and give me my *kesubah*"? Two possible readings present themselves. On one reading, the mere mention of *kesubah* signals that this is a *kesubah* case — *kivun de'amra ketubati, da'ata de'kesubah avda*. On the other reading, people simply put all their claims before the court at once — *kol milei de'is lei inshi amru lah le'beit dina* — and since she opened with the marriage claim, the case is framed accordingly.

### Order Matters

If the first formulation leaves some ambiguity, the *Gemara* then poses a starker scenario: she says *tenu li ketubati v'hiteiruni lehinasay* — she leads with the *kesubah* and follows with the request to remarry. Here, the *Gemara* suggests, she has *vadai* framed it as a *kesubah* case — *hecha vadai da'ata de'kesubah avda*.

### Ignorance Defense

Yet the *Gemara* raises a counterargument: *ho'il v'lo yad'a be-mai mistaryah* — perhaps she simply did not know through what mechanism she would be permitted to remarry. People commonly misunderstand procedure. Perhaps she mentioned the *kesubah* because she believed, based on popular misapprehension, that submitting the *kesubah* was part of the process of obtaining permission to remarry — *shem kasabra hu she-zo hatartah*. Her fundamental interest is in being freed to marry again, and her mention of the *kesubah* reflects a procedural misunderstanding, not an intention to frame the case as monetary.

## Rav Nachman Intent…

### Agunah Concern

This is where the *sugya* becomes genuinely troubling, and it deserves to be flagged directly. The entire enterprise of believing a woman's testimony about her husband's death was built on the extraordinary concern for the *agunah* — the rabbis went to remarkable lengths, departing from ordinary evidentiary standards, in order not to leave women bound and unable to remarry. And yet here, that entire protection becomes precarious based on how she phrases her opening words to *beit din*. If she says the wrong thing — if she mentions her *kesubah* first, or even mentions it alongside her request to remarry — the door may slam shut entirely, leaving her not merely without the *kesubah* but without permission to remarry at all.

One could have constructed the ruling differently and with far less severity. The *Gemara* could have said: if she frames it as a *kesubah* case, we deny the *kesubah* on those grounds but still permit her to remarry on the basis of her testimony. That would have been a coherent and far less punishing result. The *Gemara* instead makes it sound as if the wrong framing locks her out of everything. The practical implication — one the *Gemara* does not shy away from — is that she desperately needs good legal counsel before she ever opens her mouth in front of *beit din*. Indeed, the role of the *to'ein rabbani* in contemporary rabbinic courts exists in part precisely for this reason.

The *Gemara* does raise the possibility that a *drasha* on the *kesubah* language may be involved in all of this — a theme, as *Tosafos* notes, that connects to debates in *Maseches Kesubot* about whether *midrash kesubah* is a legitimate methodology and whose position it actually reflects.

## Who Testifies

### Seven Excluded

The *mishnah* now turns to a different dimension of the same subject: who may testify that a husband has died? The general rule, *hakol ne'emanim le'ha'idah*, is that all are believed to testify on her behalf. The exceptions, however, form a defined list: *chutz me'chamotah* — all are believed with the exception of her mother-in-law; and the mother-in-law's daughter (*bas chamotah*), meaning the husband's sister on the mother's side; her *tzarah* (co-wife); her *yevamah* (husband's brother's wife); and her *bas ba'alah* (stepdaughter). The *mishnah* also notes the distinction from the parallel ruling in *Gittin*: these same women are believed to deliver a *get* from the husband, because *she-ha-ksav mochiach* — the signed document itself provides the verification that makes their delivery reliable. Oral testimony about death is an entirely different matter.

The underlying logic of the exclusion list is that these are women who may have reason to hate the wife — and specifically, who might lie not out of a desire to help her but in order to set a trap. If she remarries on the strength of false testimony and the husband subsequently reappears, she suffers the devastating consequences that the earlier chapters of the tractate describe. The excluded witnesses, on this reading, are women whose animosity toward the wife makes them capable of engineering precisely that outcome.

### Mother-in-Law

The mother-in-law's hostility toward her daughter-in-law is easy to characterize in personal terms — no one is ever quite good enough for her son. But the *Gemara* locates a less personal and more economically grounded motive as well. Property that the wife brought into the marriage will pass to the husband and, upon his death, may ultimately leave the family entirely, enriching the daughter-in-law rather than remaining within the husband's family of origin. The mother-in-law thus has both emotional and financial reasons to bear the wife ill will.

### Co-Wife

The *tzarah*'s motive is the most direct: she is in direct competition with the wife. If she testifies that the husband died and the wife remarries on that basis, and the husband subsequently returns alive, the wife will be forced out of the marriage and the *tzarah* will remain as the sole wife. She has everything to gain from the wife's downfall and nothing to lose from testifying falsely.

### Brother's Wife

The *yevamah* — the wife of the husband's brother — presents a paradoxical situation. Her concern is that if the husband dies without children, her own husband will be obligated to perform *yibum* with the wife, making the wife a co-wife in her household. One might think she would therefore want to testify that the husband is alive and thus prevent the *yibum* scenario from ever arising. In fact, the *Gemara* suggests the opposite dynamic: she may reason that it is better to trigger the *yibum* now, on her own terms, in the hope that the husband will turn up alive shortly thereafter — at which point the *yibum* marriage would be invalidated and the threat would be permanently eliminated. There is thus an ironic quality to her testimony: she is hastening the very outcome she fears, gambling that it will be only temporary. Nevertheless, her interest is compromised, and she is excluded.

### Stepdaughter

The stepdaughter's exclusion requires little explanation. The animosity between a stepdaughter and her stepmother — the wife in question — is presumed and obvious. She has no motivation to assist the woman who has, in her view, taken her mother's place.

### Husband's Sister

The husband's sister on the mother's side (*bas chamotah*) is excluded because she absorbs her mother's animosity. The mother-in-law hates the daughter-in-law; the mother-in-law's daughter learns and adopts that attitude. *Hi nami sanyah lah* — she too comes to hate her. The animus is personal, inherited from the household atmosphere created by the mother.

### Father's Side Sister

The *Gemara* then raises a *ibaya*: what about the husband's sister on the *father's* side — *bas chamiv* — who has a different mother entirely? Here the mother-in-law's personal hatred does not enter the picture, since there is no shared mother to transmit it. Does the exclusion still apply?

Two possible reasons for the exclusion of the husband's sister are offered. The first grounds it entirely in the personal, matrilineal transmission of hatred: *mishum de'ika ima desanya lah* — it is because there is a mother who hates her, and *hi nami sanyah lah* — the daughter adopts it. On this reading, a sister with a different mother would *not* be excluded. The second grounds it in a financial concern that applies regardless of which side of the family: the sister feels that the wife is consuming the fruits of her family's labor — *ka'akhla li legeisana* — that property from her family is passing out through the marriage and will end up in the hands of this woman. On this reading, whether the family wealth at issue came from the mother or the father makes no difference; the animosity exists either way, and the exclusion applies to *bas chamiv* as well.

The *Gemara* notes in passing that the term *bei nasha* — which appears in the phrase *geisana de'bei nasha*, referring to the toil of the father's household in the *bas chamiv* scenario — is an interesting locution. Normally *bei nasha* refers to the mother's side of the family, the side that tends to be forgotten in a patriarchal system (related to the root of *nashani*, "He has caused me to forget"). It can also appear in the formulary of a *kesubah* when the property brought into the marriage comes from the mother's house rather than the father's. Its appearance here as a reference to the father's household is somewhat unusual and the precise usage remains worth examining more carefully.

The *sugya* does not resolve the question conclusively; it ends with the observation that once we understand the financial motive to be the operative concern, *lo shena bas chamotah v'lo shana bas chamiv* — it makes no difference which side the sister is from. The companion *braisa* that lists five excluded women (rather than the *mishnah*'s implicit listing) is reconciled by treating the two types of husband's sister as one category, since the underlying logic subsumes both.

## Yehudah Additions

### Implicit Reverses

The *Gemara* cites the view that a *braisa* reflects the position of Rabbi Yehudah: *Rabbi Yehuda mosif af eshet av v'ha-kallah* — Rabbi Yehudah adds the stepmother and the daughter-in-law to the list of those who may not testify. The Sages respond that these cases are already implied by the reverse relationships listed in the *mishnah*. If the *mishnah* already excludes the stepdaughter from testifying about her stepmother, then by symmetry the stepmother cannot testify about the stepdaughter. Similarly, if the mother-in-law cannot testify about the daughter-in-law, the daughter-in-law cannot testify about the mother-in-law. There is no need to enumerate them separately.

### Explicit Listing

Rabbi Yehudah maintains that the reverse relationships are not obvious and must be stated explicitly. In the case of the mother-in-law and daughter-in-law, *bishlama chamotah tanya* — we understand perfectly well why the mother-in-law hates the daughter-in-law: *d'amrah ka'achlah l'girsana'i* — she feels the daughter-in-law is consuming the fruits of her labor, or simply that nobody is good enough for her son. But *kallah mai ta'ama sanyah l'chamotah?* — what reason does the daughter-in-law have to hate her mother-in-law? That requires an explanation, and since the animosity runs in a less obvious direction, the exclusion must be stated independently.

Similarly, *bishlama bas ba'al d'sanyah l'eshet av* — we understand why a stepdaughter hates her stepmother: *d'amrah ka'achlah l'girsana'i d'immi* — she feels this woman is consuming the property her mother brought into the estate. But *eshet av mai ta'ama sanyah l'bat ba'al?* — what does the stepmother have against the stepdaughter? That too is not obvious, and therefore Rabbi Yehudah must list it explicitly rather than relying on an implied reverse.

### Secrets Exposed

The *Gemara* then provides Rabbi Yehudah's reasoning. Why does a daughter-in-law hate her mother-in-law? *D'megalya l'vnah kol d'avdah* — because the mother-in-law sees everything that goes on in the house and reports it to her son. The daughter-in-law feels that her private life is exposed, that her faults and failings are being narrated to her husband by his mother, and she therefore comes to bear the mother-in-law deep animosity. This is not a financial concern but an entirely domestic one — the fear of surveillance and of being badmouthed in one's own marriage.

### Stepmother Parallel

The same logic explains the stepmother's animosity toward the stepdaughter: *d'megalya l'aviah kol d'avdah* — the stepdaughter sees everything the stepmother does in the household and will report it to her father, the stepmother's husband. Just as the daughter-in-law fears being exposed to her husband through his mother, so the stepmother fears being exposed to her husband through his daughter. Once this dynamic is spelled out, Rabbi Yehudah's logic becomes clear: neither of these hostilities is intuitive, so both must be stated.

### Water Reflects

The Sages respond with a principle: *kamayim hapanim lapanim ken lev ha'adam la'adam* — as water reflects a face back to the one looking into it, so the heart of one person reflects back the feelings of another. The principle is that animosity is reciprocal by nature: if A bears animosity toward B, B will come to bear animosity toward A, even without a specific practical trigger. Once the *mishnah* establishes that the mother-in-law may not testify — thereby acknowledging that there is animosity running from mother-in-law to daughter-in-law — the reciprocal animosity is automatic, and no separate ruling is needed to establish that the daughter-in-law cannot testify either.

Rabbi Yehudah demurs: *hahu b'divrei Torah ketiv* — that verse is written about words of Torah, meaning that if you invest your heart in Torah you will receive back in kind. It does not describe the general psychology of interpersonal relationships, and one cannot derive from it a principle that all animosity is automatically reciprocal.

## Future Mother-in-Law

### Not Yet MIL

A question was raised in *Eretz Yisrael* (*ba'u b'ma'arava*): what is the status of a *chamotah haba'ah l'achar mikan* — a future mother-in-law? How can there be such a thing? The scenario involves *yibum*: a woman's husband is absent or has died, and there exists a brother-in-law whose mother would, in the event of *yibum*, become the woman's mother-in-law. That brother-in-law's mother is thus a *potential* future mother-in-law. The question is whether such a woman is already in the category of those excluded from testifying, or whether since she has no actual present relationship of animosity, she may testify.

*Mi mapka da'atah d'nasva v'naflah kamei yavam v'sanyah lah?* — Is this future mother-in-law already anticipating the possibility that this woman will fall before her son for *yibum*, and has she begun to hate her preemptively? Is she, so to speak, getting a head start on the inevitable animosity?

### Both Die Case

The *Gemara* brings a *ta shema* from a related ruling. If a woman comes and says: "My husband died, and then my father-in-law died" — both men having been away together, say, in *medinat hayam* — the ruling is: *tinasei v'ticul kesubasah*, she may remarry and collect her *kesubah* based on her own testimony about her husband. But her mother-in-law is *asurah* — the mother-in-law may not remarry on the basis of this same testimony. Why?

The technical objection might seem easy to dismiss: once the husband died, the father-in-law was no longer her father-in-law, and the mother-in-law was no longer her mother-in-law, so she should be believed to testify about what happened to him. But the *Gemara* does not accept this reasoning.

### Ruse Theory

*Mahu d'amrinan? — Lo ba'alah meis v'lo chamotah meis.* The concern is that perhaps this entire narrative is a ruse — that neither the husband nor the father-in-law actually died, and the wife has constructed the entire account precisely in order to harm her mother-in-law. She knows that if she testified directly about her mother-in-law she would not be believed, so she testifies first about her husband's death (which benefits herself and which she knows will be accepted), and then appends testimony about the father-in-law's death (which enables the mother-in-law to remarry and thereby creates the trap — because if the husband turns up alive, the mother-in-law will have remarried on the strength of false testimony). The entire scheme is designed to destroy the mother-in-law.

*Savrah levatar de'asya sha'atah lo seisi v'sis'aran* — her reasoning, according to Rashi, is that she wants to ensure that in the future the mother-in-law will not come and continue to cause her trouble. Even though her husband is currently absent, she is acting now based on the animosity that already exists — animosity rooted in the fact that the mother-in-law has been badmouthing her to her husband. She is not acting out of an immediate threat but out of an accumulated resentment, and that resentment is real and pre-existing.

### Pre-existing Animosity

But the *Gemara* then draws the distinction that answers the original question about the future mother-in-law. *Dilma shani hasam de'mei-ikra de'asya sanyah lah* — the case of the existing mother-in-law is different because the animosity is already there, pre-existing and rooted in a real relationship. In the hypothetical above, the daughter-in-law's willingness to harm her mother-in-law through this elaborate scheme makes sense precisely because she already harbors deep animosity from the years of the marriage. The future mother-in-law, by contrast, has no relationship with this woman whatsoever. She has not yet become anyone's mother-in-law. There is no prior history, no accumulated grievance, no existing hostility. The concern about lying for the sake of a future animosity that has not yet taken root does not apply.

The upshot is: we do not extend the exclusion to those whose animosity is merely hypothetical or future. The rule applies only where there is a pre-existing relationship of actual animosity.

## Conflict Cases

### 1v2 Early

The *mishnah* now addresses cases of conflicting testimony. First: *ed echad omer meis venisat* — one witness says the husband died and she remarries on that basis, *uva acher v'amar lo meis* — and then a second comes and says he did not die. *Harei zu lo tetze* — she does not have to leave her new marriage. The principle is that once *beit din* has accepted the testimony and acted upon it, a subsequent conflicting testimony cannot unravel what has already been ruled upon and executed.

### 2v1

If two say he died and she married, but one says he did not die: *af al pi she-niset, tetze* — even having already remarried, she must leave. Two witnesses prevail over one, and the remarriage must be undone.

Conversely, if two say he died and one says he did not: *af al pi she-lo niset, tinasei* — even before she has remarried, the two witnesses establish his death and she is permitted to proceed. The implication of the *mishnah*, left implicit until the *Gemara* makes it explicit, is that if it is one against one before she has remarried, she may not remarry.

### 1v1 Later

This implication — that one against one before remarriage leaves her unable to proceed — creates the analytical problem that the *Gemara* addresses. If it is *ed echad* saying he died against *ed echad* saying he did not, why should she not be permitted? The answer involves the principle of *ed echad*'s special weight in this domain.

### Ulla Principle

*V'amar Ulla: kol makom she-he'emina Torah ed echad harei kan shnayim v'ein davar she-echad b'makom shnayim.* Ulla's principle is that wherever the Torah gave special weight to a single witness, that testimony is treated as equivalent to two witnesses — and a single subsequent witness cannot override it, since one cannot stand against two.

There is a broader set of cases where the Torah gives special weight to a single witness — *Sotah*, *Eglah Arufah*, and others. In those cases, the witness given special weight testifies to one side of the equation, and a single witness on the other side cannot override it. Here, the *Gemara* notes, the rabbis gave special believability to the testimony that the husband died; they gave no such special weight to testimony that he did not die. Therefore, even in a one-against-one scenario, the one who says he died should carry the weight of two.

But the *Gemara* reformulates Ulla's principle more precisely: *ed echad omer meis v'hityiruha linase* — it is not merely about the testimony being given; the testimony must be acted upon. *Ba echad v'amar lo meis lo tetze me-heteirah harishon* — once *beit din* has ruled on the basis of that first testimony, it acquires the weight of two, and a subsequent single witness cannot override it. Before *beit din* rules, the testimony does not yet carry that locked-in weight. This is why one-against-one before remarriage does not permit her to proceed: the first testimony has not yet been ruled upon, and so it has not yet acquired the weight of two.

## Ulla vs. Nechemiah

### Ulla Model

Ulla's model is fundamentally one of formal testimony. When the Torah or the rabbis give special weight to a single witness in a particular domain, they are placing that witness within the framework of *eidus* — formal testimony — and elevating it to the status of two. The consequence is that the world remains one of formal evidentiary standards: one becomes two, two beats one, and only two legitimate witnesses on the other side would override it. The count never involves informal assessments or quantities of opinions; it is always about the quality and formal weight of testimony.

### Nechemiah Model

Rabbi Nechemiah articulates an entirely different model: *kol makom she-he'emina Torah ed echad halach achar rov de'os* — wherever the Torah believed a single witness, the determination follows the majority of opinions. This is a fundamentally quantitative approach. Rabbi Nechemiah holds that when the standard of proof departs from formal two-witness *eidus*, one has left the world of formal testimony entirely and entered a world of counting opinions. There is no elevation of one to two, no locking in, no special weight accorded to any one testimony. Instead, all testimonies are placed on a scale and the heavier side wins.

### Two Women Count

The practical consequence of Rabbi Nechemiah's model is stark: *v'asu shtei nashim b'ish echad kishnei anashim b'ish echad* — two women testifying against one man count the same as two men testifying against one man. Ordinarily, two women do not constitute formal *eidus* and cannot override a single male witness. But in Rabbi Nechemiah's model, when we are in the domain of *ed echad* believability, we are no longer asking about formal *eidus* at all. We are simply counting: two against one, regardless of the gender or formal qualification of the testifiers.

This has severe implications for women in the *agunah* context. A woman remarries on the strength of a single permitted testimony that her husband died. If two women — even if they are *psulin*, formally disqualified — then testify that he did not die, Rabbi Nechemiah's model would require her to leave the remarriage, because two opinions now outweigh one. This is a far more precarious protection than Ulla's model offers.

### Reconciliation

The *Gemara* offers a reconciliation of the two positions that is worth examining carefully. *Kol heicha d'asa ed echad kasher mei-ikara* — wherever the first witness who came was a formally qualified (*kasher*) male witness, we are in Ulla's world. That single testimony, once ruled upon by *beit din*, is treated as two witnesses, is locked in, and can be overridden only by two formally qualified witnesses. *Afilu me'ah nashim k'ed echad damyan* — even a hundred women count as only one, and they cannot override the locked-in testimony of that *kasher* witness.

### Woman First

The domain of Rabbi Nechemiah applies when the first witness was not a formally qualified witness — *kigon d'asa isha mei-ikara*, such as when a woman testified first. In that case, we are not in the world of formal *eidus* from the outset, and Rabbi Nechemiah's quantitative principle governs. *V'asu shtei nashim b'isha achas kishnei anashim b'ish echad* — two women against one woman is like two men against one man. We count opinions rather than weigh formal testimony.

The reconciliation thus turns on the identity of the first witness. If a formally qualified witness initiated the testimony, the case enters Ulla's framework and the testimony is locked in formally. If an informally qualified witness (a woman, a relative, or another *pasul*) testified first, the case operates under Rabbi Nechemiah's quantitative model from the start.

This represents two genuinely distinct conceptual frameworks. Ulla's model maintains that even in the special domain of *ed echad* believability, we remain within the structure of formal testimony — one has simply been elevated to two. Rabbi Nechemiah's model holds that once you have departed from the ordinary standard of two witnesses, you have left *eidus* territory entirely and are now simply doing arithmetic.

### Majority Principle

The final line of the *mishnah* — *shnayim omrim meis, echad omer lo meis* — states that if two say he died and one says he did not die, she may remarry even before having done so. The *Gemara* asks: *pshita* — isn't that obvious? Two beats one. The answer is that the *chiddush* here operates under Rabbi Nechemiah's model: the two who say he died may be *psulei eidus* — formally disqualified witnesses — and yet their two opinions outweigh the single opposing opinion. This is the domain of *rov de'os*, the majority of opinions, and the ruling teaches that the majority principle applies even to leniency, not only to stringency.

One might have thought that when the quantitative model leads to a lenient outcome — permitting remarriage — we would refuse to apply it and instead require formal *eidus*. The *mishnah* teaches that this is not the case. Whether the majority of opinions points toward stringency or leniency, the principle is applied consistently: *halach achar rov de'os*. The *Gemara*'s synthesis of Ulla and Rabbi Nechemiah thus determines not only how to handle conflicting testimony but how to understand the very nature of *ed echad* believability — whether it is an elevation within the world of formal testimony or a departure from that world altogether.
