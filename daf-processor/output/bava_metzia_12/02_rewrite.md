# Bava Metzia 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Standing by Field

### Intro & Background

The present daf is Bava Metzia 12, picking up on 11b approximately fifteen lines from the bottom, beginning with the words *makne osan shani*. The broader context is the *perek*'s sustained discussion of *meziot* — the laws of finding lost objects. Although the *perek* opened with the famous case of two people simultaneously grabbing a found object, the *mishnayot* that follow address a range of scenarios involving the acquisition of lost objects. The particular *mishnah* under discussion earlier concerned a deer running through one's yard: if the deer had a broken leg such that it could effectively be caught, the owner's field could acquire it on his behalf; if the deer was running normally and could not be caught, no acquisition took place.

### Rav Yehuda vs Yose

The *gemara* had already established that Rav Yehuda, citing Shmuel, requires a person to be standing alongside his field (*omed betzad sadehu*) in order for *kinyan chatzer* to take effect. The *gemara* then challenged this from a ruling of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina, who held that one's *chatzer* can acquire on one's behalf even without one's awareness — which seemingly implies that standing nearby is unnecessary.

### Mishtameret Distinction

The *gemara* resolved this tension by distinguishing between a *chatzer mishtameret* — a protected enclosure where objects placed within it will remain — and a *chatzer lo mishtameret*, where objects are not reliably held. If the *chatzer* is *mishtameret*, no standing is required; if it is not *mishtameret*, one must be standing alongside it for *kinyan chatzer* to operate.

### Yad vs Shlichut

Rashi explains this distinction by noting that when one is standing nearby, one provides the protection oneself, thereby rendering the *chatzer* effectively *mishtameret* through one's own presence. An alternative reading, which the *gemara* will make explicit shortly, flows from the earlier discussion about whether *chatzer* functions as an extension of the person's *yad* or as a form of *shlichut*. If one is standing beside the *chatzer*, it operates as *yad* — a direct physical extension of the person. If one is absent, it can only function as *shlichut*, operating on one's behalf. As *yad*, physical holding suffices; as *shlichut*, one requires a meaningful degree of control — that is, *mishtameret* — for the agency to be coherent.

## Rabbis on Boat

### Maaser Transfer

The *gemara* had proceeded to examine a famous case involving Rabban Gamliel and the other rabbis traveling aboard a ship. Rabban Gamliel transferred ownership of *maaser rishon* to the Levite and *maaser ani* to Rabbi Akiva, who served as *gabbai tzedakah*, by renting them the space beneath which the grain in question was located. This appears to be a *kinyan chatzer* — the recipient acquires the grain by virtue of his rented space — yet Rabban Gamliel was clearly not standing alongside any field.

### Challenge to Standing

The *gemara* challenged: does this not demonstrate that one need not be standing beside the field for *kinyan chatzer* to function? Tosafot raises the obvious counter: perhaps the grain was in his house and the *chatzer* was *mishtameret* regardless. However, Tosafot rejects this, reasoning that grain stored within a household is not clearly set apart from the rest of the household's grain — family members would treat it all as undifferentiated, so there is nothing that effectively protects its distinct status. It therefore cannot be considered *mishtameret* in any meaningful sense.

### Two Answers

The *gemara* offered two possible resolutions. The first was to reframe the entire transaction: rather than a *kinyan chatzer*, the transfer may have operated as a *kinyan agav* — whereby ownership of the contents is transferred alongside the transfer of the space itself. The second answer, with which the *gemara* concludes, is introduced by Rav Papa.

### Rav Papa's Resolution

Rav Papa declares: *da'at acheret makneh otah shani* — when another party's intent is what drives the transfer, the case is fundamentally different. The requirement that a *chatzer* be *mishtameret* or that one stand alongside it applies specifically to *metzia*, where the person must exert the full force of his own act of acquisition. When, however, someone else is actively transferring the object — when another's *da'at* is the engine of the *kinyan* — the recipient's own act of possession need not be as robust. A looser form of holding suffices, and therefore neither *mishtameret* nor *omed betzad* is required.

## Da'at Acheret

### Source: Chasing Deer

The *gemara* now asks: from where does one derive this principle of *da'at acheret makneh*? The source is the *mishnah* itself, which describes people running after a deer. The *mishnah*'s discussion of a broken-legged deer implies a meaningful condition about what constitutes sufficient holding.

### Deer Seizure Test

Rabbi Yirmeyah, citing Rabbi Yochanan, clarifies that the distinction between a broken-legged and a healthy deer is not the decisive criterion per se. What matters is whether one could run after the animal and seize it before it exits the *chatzer*. If one can, the *chatzer* acquires; if not, it does not. This is the operative test for whether an object is sufficiently "held" within the *chatzer* for *kinyan* purposes.

### Matanah Question

Rabbi Yirmeyah then raises a new question: what is the rule if the deer is being given to you as a *matanah* — a gift — and it is running through your yard? Must you also be able to run after it and seize it before it escapes, or does the act of gift-giving alter the standard? Rabbi Abba bar Kahana understood the intent of Rabbi Yirmeyah's question to point toward a distinction: in the case of *matanah*, the answer is that it works *even if* you cannot run after the deer and seize it before it exits.

### Matanah vs Metzia

What explains the difference? The *gemara* reasons: *lav mishum de-da'at acheret makneh otah shani* — is it not because, when another party is actively transferring the object to you, that is itself the distinguishing factor? When *da'at acheret* is doing the work of the transfer, your own act of taking possession need not meet the ordinary threshold. Thus, a deer running through your yard that you cannot catch suffices for *matanah*, whereas for *metzia* you require either *mishtameret* or *omed betzad sadehu*.

## Get Exception

### Rav Shimi Challenge

Rav Shimi poses a challenge to Rav Papa's principle. The case of *get* — a bill of divorce — is itself a paradigmatic instance of *da'at acheret makneh*: the husband is the one transferring it to the wife, and indeed he may do so even against her will. If anything, *da'at acheret* is at its strongest here, since it is exclusively the husband's *da'at* that drives the transaction. On Rav Papa's reasoning, then, even the loosest holding by her *chatzer* should suffice.

### Ulla on Get

Yet Ulla rules precisely the opposite: for a *get* to be received through the wife's *chatzer*, she must be *omedet betzad beita o betzad chatzerah* — standing alongside her house or alongside her courtyard. Even the strong *da'at acheret* of the husband does not relax the requirement; instead, a stricter standard applies.

### Get Forced Transfer

The *gemara* explains: *shani get de-iteih be-al karchah* — a *get* is different because it can be forced upon the woman. Since the *get* is something that can be delivered to her detriment and without her consent, one requires a stronger demonstration of her taking possession. The logic, at least on one reading, is that if the husband can do everything and she plays no active role, her act of possession must correspondingly be more pronounced.

### Kal Vachomer

Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi challenges this reasoning with a *kal vachomer* that runs in precisely the opposite direction. A *get*, which the husband can force upon her — where her participation is not required and the *Torah* itself has empowered the husband to the point of disregarding her role — and yet we still require her to be standing by. Should we not reason: if even in *get*, where she is treated almost as a passive object, we require her standing, then certainly by *matanah*, where her *da'at* and active receipt are relevant, we should require it as well? The *gemara* notes that the argument can indeed go either way with respect to *get*. The matter is clarified definitively by Rav Ashi.

## Rav Ashi Framework

### Yad or Shaliach

Rav Ashi provides the definitive framework. He states: *chatzer ishtari beh mishum yad ve-lo gara mi-shlichut* — a *chatzer* functions in two possible modes. When one is standing beside it, it functions as *yad*, a direct extension of the person's own body. Even when one is not standing beside it, it functions no worse than a *shaliach*, an agent acting on one's behalf. These are two distinct mechanisms, and which one applies depends on the circumstances.

### Ein Chavin L'Adam

The critical limiting principle is *ein chavin le-adam ella be-fanav* — one cannot act to another's detriment except in that person's presence. The reason *chatzer* can acquire on someone's behalf even without their knowledge, when it functions as *shaliach*, is rooted in the principle *zachin le-adam she-lo be-fanav*: one may act to another's benefit without that person being present, just as a third party may pick up a *metzia* on someone else's behalf, since doing so only helps that person. But where the act is to the person's detriment, an agent cannot act without explicit designation — and in any case, a *chatzer* is not a real person capable of being formally designated as an agent.

### Get Needs Yad

This is precisely why *get* is categorically different. A *get* is considered to be to the woman's detriment — she loses the rights and protections that inhere in marriage — and therefore *chatzer* cannot function as her *shaliach* in receiving it. The category of *get* is seen as detrimental regardless of whether in a particular case she desires the divorce. Since *chatzer* cannot operate as *shaliach* for something to her detriment, and since she cannot formally appoint her *chatzer* as an agent for this purpose (*chatzer* not being a legal person), the *chatzer* can only function as *yad*. And *yad* requires her to be *omedet betzad beita* — present beside the *chatzer* so that it is a direct extension of her.

### Three Categories

This yields three distinct categories. For *get*, even if the *chatzer* is *mishtameret* — even her own house — she must be standing beside it, because it can only function as *yad*. For *matanah*, which is to the recipient's benefit, *zachin le-adam she-lo be-fanav* applies, and the *chatzer* can function as *shaliach* even without the recipient present or standing nearby; moreover, since *da'at acheret* is transferring it, even the loosest holding suffices. For *metzia*, which falls between these poles, the *chatzer* can function as *shaliach*, but because no one else's *da'at* is actively driving the transfer, the recipient's own act of acquisition must be more robust — requiring either *mishtameret* or *omed betzad sadehu*.

One note that should not be overlooked: the *mishnah* itself states that the person must actually declare, "let my field acquire on my behalf." The *gemara*'s entire subsequent discussion proceeds without engaging this requirement, and Tosafot acknowledges it is puzzling. One might distinguish between *mishtameret*, where perhaps no declaration is needed, and *eina mishtameret* with *omed betzad*, where perhaps a declaration is required — but the *mishnah*'s language has been entirely bracketed in the *gemara*'s analysis, which is itself a striking omission.

## Wallet Example

### Flying Wallet

Rava poses a question: if someone throws a wallet (*arneki*) through one opening in a *chatzer* and it is flying toward the other opening such that it will exit without ever landing — is that considered to be within the *chatzer* for purposes of *kinyan*? The question is framed as: *avira she-ein sofo lanu'ach kemanu'ach dami o lo* — does airspace through which an object passes but never comes to rest count as equivalent to the object being stationary within the *chatzer*?

### Flying vs Rolling

Rav Pappa (and according to some versions, Rav Adda bar Matna or Ravina) responds to Rava: is this not addressed by the *mishnah* itself, from which Rabbi Yirmeyah derived that only if one can run after the deer and seize it does the *chatzer* acquire? That ruling implies that something passing through uncontrollably — like a wallet flying through without landing — would not constitute presence in the *chatzer* for the purpose of *metzia*. However, the person throwing the wallet is clearly doing so with intent to transfer — making it a case of *da'at acheret*. And Rabbi Yirmeyah's question about *matanah*, accepted by Rabbi Abba bar Kahana, established that with *da'at acheret* even a deer that cannot be caught suffices. So does not the same reasoning apply here?

The response draws a crucial distinction: *mitgalgel kamarta? shani mitgalgel kemanu'ach dami* — were you speaking of something rolling? Something that rolls through the *chatzer* is treated as if it were stationary. But something flying through the airspace is a different matter entirely. If an object is falling into one's airspace and will land within the *chatzer*, one acquires it at the moment it enters the airspace. But an object flying through the airspace without ever touching the ground cannot be said to be within the *chatzer* at all.

### Presence in Yard

Thus three distinct levels emerge: flying through airspace without landing — possibly not present in the *chatzer* at all; rolling through the ground — treated as stationary, and therefore present; stationary — fully present. For *metzia*, one would need *mishtameret* or *omed betzad* even for a rolling object, since the question of whether it is being "held" remains live. But for *matanah*, with *da'at acheret*, even a rolling or running object (like the deer) suffices, though a merely flying object may not clear even that lower threshold.

## Household Metzia

### Mishna Overview

The *gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*, which addresses several categories of household members whose findings belong to another party: *metziat beno uvito haktanim* — the findings of one's minor son and daughter; *metziat avdo veshifchato hakena'anim* — one's Canaanite slaves; and *metziat ishto* — one's wife. In all these cases, the *mishnah* rules: *harei elu shelo* — they belong to the husband or father.

### Wife Rationale

The rationale for the wife is rooted in the reciprocal structure of obligations that the rabbis established within marriage. The husband bears responsibility for housing, food, clothing, and a range of other provisions; in exchange, whatever the wife earns or finds belongs to him. The *gemara* elsewhere acknowledges that a wife may stipulate to retain her own earnings, which is a separate discussion, but as the default arrangement, her *metzia* goes to the husband.

### Daughter Rationale

For the minor daughter, the father has broad authority over her during her minority — to the point, as Rashi notes, of being able to sell her as a slave or arrange her marriage. Tosafot, however, explains the transfer of her *metzia* to the father on more pragmatic grounds: to preserve the relationship and prevent the father from misusing his authority. If the daughter were to keep a valuable find for herself, the father might become angry and exercise his legal powers to her detriment — arranging a disadvantageous marriage or sale. To preempt such friction, the rabbis assigned her *metzia* to the father.

### Slave Rationale

Canaanite slaves are considered an extension of their owner, analogous to the *chatzer* or *yad* discussed earlier. When they take possession of something, it is as if the owner's own *chatzer* has acquired it, and therefore the finding belongs to the owner.

### Minor Son Question

The one case that is not self-evidently explained is the minor son. The *mishnah* states that his findings go to the father, but the rationale is not immediately clear from any of the frameworks just articulated. This is taken up by the *gemara*.

---

## Shmuel Minor Son

### Runs to Father

Shmuel explains: *mipnei mah ameru metziat katan le-aviv* — why did the rabbis say the minor's find goes to his father? *She-beshaah she-motzaah miritza etzel aviv ve-eino me'acher be-yado* — because the moment the child finds it, he runs to his father and does not hold it in his own hand. He never pauses to take possession for himself; he immediately brings it to the father. As a result, the child never actually acquires the object; it goes directly to the father.

### No Zachiya Implication

This immediately raises a question: does Shmuel's reasoning imply that a minor never has *zachiya* — the ability to take legal possession — on his own as a biblical matter? If the minor ran to his father not as a factual matter but as a reflection of the legal reality that he cannot acquire, then the *mishnah*'s rule would be a consequence of the minor's general incapacity to perform *kinyan*. The *gemara* presses this question by examining a *braisa*.

## Leket Braisa

### Worker vs Sharecropper

The *gemara* cites a *braisa*: if one hires a worker to gather grain in the field, that worker — if he is a poor person — may have his child collect the *leket* (the gleanings that fall during harvest, which belong to the poor by Torah law) after him. However, *lemachatza lishlish uleravia lo yilaket beno acharav* — if the worker is a sharecropper, receiving a half, a third, or a quarter of the produce, his child may not collect the *leket* after him.

### Ani Owner Restriction

The reason is that the *halacha* treats even a partial owner of a field as someone who cannot collect *leket* from that field. The owner — even if technically impoverished — is prohibited from taking the gleanings that belong to the *aniyim*. When the son collects, the assumption is that he is collecting on his father's behalf; therefore, since the father is effectively an owner of the field in the context of the sharecropping arrangement, the collection is prohibited. By contrast, when the father is merely a day laborer with no ownership stake, he is a genuine *ani* and his child may collect after him.

### Yose Dissent

Rabbi Yose dissents: *bein kach u-vein kach yilaket beno ve-ishto acharav* — in either case, the son and the wife may collect after him. The reason is that the son and the wife are collecting for themselves, not for the father. Since they are independent *aniyim*, they have full rights to the *leket*, regardless of the father's ownership status.

### Shmuel Paskens

The *gemara* then notes: *ve-amar Shmuel halacha ke-Rabbi Yose* — Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yose. This creates a direct tension with Shmuel's own explanation of the *mishnah*. If, as Shmuel said, the minor never takes possession for himself but immediately brings everything to the father — meaning the father is the one who acquires through the child — then how can Rabbi Yose's ruling stand in the sharecropper case? The child would be acquiring on behalf of the father, and the father, as a partial owner of the field, is prohibited from taking the *leket*.

---

## Possession Transfer

### Child First

The *gemara* works through the logical possibilities. If one holds that a *katan* does have the capacity for *zachiya* on his own — that he first takes possession for himself — then the sharecropper case presents no difficulty. The child, as an *ani* independent of the sharecropping arrangement, takes possession of the *leket* in his own right. *Va-avuh minei kazachi* — the father then acquires from him as a second step, by virtue of whatever rabbinic mechanism transfers the child's *metzia* to the father. Since the child took possession first and as an *ani*, the prohibition applicable to the sharecropper-father is bypassed.

### Sharecropper Problem

This resolution depends entirely on the child taking possession first and independently. Only then can one say the *leket* was properly acquired by a qualifying *ani* before the father became involved.

### Shmuel Rejects

The problem is that Shmuel's own explanation of the *mishnah* appears to foreclose this mechanism. If the child never takes possession for himself — running straight to the father without pausing to hold the object — then no preliminary acquisition by the child occurs, and the father acquires directly through the child's act of retrieval. Under that reading, the sharecropper case is insoluble.

### Shmuel Clarified

The *gemara* resolves this by reframing Shmuel's statement: *Shmuel ta'ama de-tanna de-idan ka'amar ve-lav leih sevira leih* — Shmuel was explaining what he understood to be the internal logic of the *mishnah*'s anonymous ruling, not articulating his own personal position. In his own view, Shmuel agrees that the child first takes possession and it then transfers to the father. His explanation of the *tanna*'s reasoning in the *mishnah* reflects one possible understanding of that text, but it is not the view he himself endorses. Tosafot discusses why Shmuel chose to explain the *mishnah* in a manner inconsistent with his own held position, and that discussion is worth examining separately.

## Deaf Imbecile Minor

### Darchei Shalom Rule

The *gemara* now cites a *mishnah*: *metziat cheresh shoteh ve-katan — yesh bahem mishum gezel mipnei darchei shalom* — if a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor finds a lost object, taking it from them constitutes something of a wrongful taking by reason of *darchei shalom* — maintaining social order and peaceful relations. The underlying reasoning is that these individuals lack sufficient *da'at* to perform a fully valid legal act of acquisition; technically, therefore, they have not acquired the *metzia* in a legally operative sense. Nevertheless, to preserve a well-functioning society, the rabbis declared that one may not take the object from them.

### Yose: Gezel

Rabbi Yose says: *gezel gamur* — it is outright theft. It is not merely a matter of social policy; it is a genuine wrongful taking.

### Chisda Clarifies

Rav Chisda clarifies Rabbi Yose's position: *gezel gamur mi-divreicbem* — it is outright theft *rabbinically*. Rabbi Yose does not claim that the minor has acquired the object by biblical law. Rather, the rabbis have classified the taking as actual *gezel* — not merely a violation of the spirit of *darchei shalom*, but a legally cognizable rabbinically prohibited wrongful taking.

### Nafka Mina

The practical difference between the two positions — the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yose as explained by Rav Chisda — concerns whether the wronged party can bring suit to recover the object. According to the *tanna kamma*, the declaration that one may not take from them is a policy directive rooted in *darchei shalom*, but since the minor never truly acquired the object, there is no legally enforceable claim of ownership. If someone violated the rule and took it, the matter cannot be adjudicated in court to recover the object. According to Rabbi Yose, since the rabbis have declared it genuine *gezel*, the minor or deaf-mute has a cognizable legal claim and may sue in court to recover what was taken.

---

## Aniyim Despair

### After Scavengers

But if Rabbi Yose holds only that the minor's ownership is rabbinically grounded in *darchei shalom* — and Rav Chisda makes clear that is all Rabbi Yose is saying — then how can Shmuel rule like Rabbi Yose in the *leket* case of the sharecropper? The *leket* case requires the child to have genuine, operative ownership, not merely a *darchei shalom* entitlement. Abaye proposes an entirely different explanation of what Rabbi Yose is saying in the *leket* context. The mechanism, Abaye argues, has nothing to do with the minor's personal *zachiya* at all.

Abaye explains: *asuha ke-mi she-halchu bo nemosos de-aniyei gufaihu maschi da'ataihu* — the rabbis treated the situation as equivalent to the case where the *nemosos* — the second-round scavengers who pick up even the smallest remnants — have already passed through the field. Once the *nemosos* have gone through, the *aniyim* themselves give up; they figure there is nothing worth retrieving. At that point the *leket* is no longer restricted to the *aniyim*, and anyone may take it.

### Child Gathers All

So too in the sharecropper's case: the *aniyim* figure that since the sharecropper's son is systematically gathering everything after his father, there is nothing left for them to bother collecting. *Savri brei de-hai milaket lei* — they reason that this man's son will gather everything, so they abandon their claim to the *leket* in that field entirely.

### No Longer Restricted

Once the *aniyim* have given up, the *leket* is no longer exclusively their right. It is open to anyone — including, in effect, the sharecropper-father who would otherwise be prohibited from taking it. The mechanism does not depend on the child owning it at all; it depends on the *aniyim*'s *ye'ush* — their giving up — which removes the restriction.

---

## Lion Challenge

### Lions Kal Vachomer

Rav Adda bar Matna poses a sharp challenge to Abaye's explanation. He frames it as a *kal va-chomer*: surely it is not permitted for a person to station lions in his field in order to frighten away the *aniyim* so that they will not bother collecting the *leket*. If a landowner placed lions in the field, the *aniyim* would certainly give up — *ye'ush* of the most complete kind — yet no one would say that this renders the *leket* available for the owner to take. The *ye'ush* would be manufactured through coercion.

### Undermines Despair

If *ye'ush* alone were the operative mechanism, there would be no principled distinction between the *aniyim* naturally giving up because the sharecropper's son is thorough, and the *aniyim* being frightened away by lions that the owner deliberately installed. Yet the latter is clearly prohibited. This undermines Abaye's explanation: if the despair of the *aniyim* is generated by the sharecropper's own actions — namely, deploying his child precisely to gather everything — it is not qualitatively different from deploying lions. The mechanism cannot rest on *ye'ush* produced by the owner's own conduct.

---

## Rava's Exception

### Real Zachiya

Rava responds at the top of 12b with a fundamentally different explanation: *asu she-eino zocheh ke-zocheh* — the rabbis treated someone who lacks the inherent capacity to acquire as if he fully acquires. Rava acknowledges that Rav Chisda is correct that Rabbi Yose's general position on the deaf-mute and minor is that their ownership is only rabbinically grounded in *darchei shalom*, which does not constitute genuine acquisition. However, in the specific context of *leket*, the rabbis enacted a distinct *takkanah* granting the minor full, operative acquisition — treating one who normally cannot acquire as fully capable of doing so.

### Rabbinic Enactment

This is a targeted rabbinic enactment, not a general principle. It is not the same as the *darchei shalom* rule, which merely protects the minor's possession as a matter of social policy without granting genuine ownership. Here the rabbis made a specific decision — *asu* — that the minor genuinely acquires in this context.

### Aniyim Benefit

The reason for this enactment: *aniyim gufaihu nicha lehu* — the *aniyim* themselves benefit from this policy. *Ki heichi de-ki agri le-didi nilketu banai batrai* — for when they themselves are hired to gather grain, their children may also collect the *leket* after them, making use of the same institution. Because the *aniyim* as a class stand to benefit from a robust rule granting minors genuine acquisition rights in the *leket* context, the rabbis fashioned the rule in their interest. The distinction between this rabbinic enactment and the *darchei shalom* framework is thus consequential: here the child truly acquires, which is then transferred to the father; the *darchei shalom* rule, by contrast, creates no genuine ownership, only a protective social norm.

## Supported by Father

### Dependence Test

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba, citing Rabbi Yochanan, offers a position that differs from Shmuel's framework. Rabbi Yochanan states: *lo gadol gadol mamash ve-lo katan katan mamash* — the *mishnah* is not speaking of an adult and a minor in the literal, age-based sense. The determinative criterion is not age.

### Independent or Dependent

Rather: *gadol ha-samuch al shulchan aviv ze katan* — an adult who is supported by and living on his father's table is, for the purposes of this *halacha*, treated as a minor, and his *metzia* goes to the father. Conversely, *katan ve-eino samuch al shulchan aviv zehu gadol* — a minor who is independent and not supported by his father is, for these purposes, treated as an adult, and he keeps his own *metzia*.

### Good Relations

The rationale is entirely relational, not technical. The concern is that a supported child — regardless of age — should not antagonize his father by keeping a valuable find while the father is providing for all his needs. To maintain good relations and prevent the father from withdrawing support or acting punitively, the rabbis assigned the *metzia* to the father. An independent minor, by contrast, is not in such a relationship of dependency, and there is no corresponding concern.

### Compatible Mechanism

This framework is fully compatible with the mechanism by which the transfer occurs. The supported person — adult or minor — first takes possession of the object in his own right; the *metzia* is genuinely his. Subsequently, the rabbinic principle transfers ownership to the father, in order to preserve the relationship of mutual support. This is exactly the mechanism — child first, then father — that, as the *gemara* established, is required to resolve the *leket* case. Shmuel's alternative reading, which denies the child's preliminary acquisition, is inconsistent with this view; that is precisely why the *gemara* clarified that Shmuel was not articulating his own position but rather explaining the *tanna*'s reasoning.

## Hebrew Slave Metzia

### Slave Keeps Metzia

The *mishnah* states: *metziat avdo ve-shifchato ha-ivrim harei hu shel atzman* — the findings of one's Hebrew slaves are their own. This is striking, because the master holds rights over the slave's labor to a significant degree.

### Why Not Worse?

The *gemara* asks: *lo yehe ella po'el* — surely a Hebrew slave should be no worse than a hired hand. And we know from prior discussion that a hired hand's *metzia* goes to the employer under at least some circumstances. Since the master owns the Hebrew slave's labor more comprehensively than a typical employment contract, shouldn't the slave's *metzia* go to the master *a fortiori*?

### Hired Hand Rules

A *braisa* elaborates: if a worker is hired to do a specific task — such as hoeing or weeding — and he finds a *metzia* while performing that task, the finding is his own, because collecting the *metzia* takes him off the clock. He must reimburse the employer for the time lost from the designated work, but the *metzia* itself belongs to him. However, if he was hired generically — *asei imi melacha ha-yom*, work for me whatever I say today — then *metziato le-ba'al ha-bayit*: the *metzia* goes to the employer, because the worker is always considered on the clock and any act he performs during the workday is done on the employer's behalf.

## Specialized Slave

### Pearl Driller

Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba, citing Rabbi Yochanan, explains the *mishnah*'s ruling about Hebrew slaves by specifying the case: *hacha be-eved nokeiv margaliyot askinan she-ein rabbo rotzeh le-shanoto le-melacha acheres* — we are dealing with a slave who has a highly specialized skill, such as drilling holes in pearls, and whose master has no desire to reassign him to any other task. Because the master exclusively wants him to drill pearls, any time the slave deviates from pearl-drilling, he is operating off the designated task.

### Off-Clock Logic

If such a slave stops drilling pearls to pick up a *metzia*, he is off the clock for the duration. He must compensate the master for the value of his time away from pearl-drilling, but the *metzia* itself is his. The master's labor-ownership is complete in principle, but because it is channeled into a specific, exclusively designated task, any deviation from that task removes the slave from the scope of his service obligation for that moment.

### Rava's Alternative

Rava offers a different way of reading the case: *be-magbi'ah metzi'ah im melachto askinan* — the slave is picking up the *metzia* in the same physical act as he is performing another assigned task. For example, if the slave has been told to collect garbage from the yard, and in the course of picking up garbage he encounters a diamond and retrieves it in the same motion, that act of retrieval is considered independent of his service to the master. The service obligation covers the garbage collection; the simultaneous pickup of the *metzia* is treated as a separate act not performed on the master's behalf.

### Separate Act

This is a conceptually distinct mechanism from the pearl-drilling case. There, the slave is fully off the clock because he has stopped his designated task. Here, the slave never stops his task; he continues it while simultaneously performing an act that falls outside its scope. The logic is that the master's claim over the slave's labor is defined by the content of the designated task, not by every physical movement the slave makes. An act that falls outside the designated content — even if performed simultaneously — is not considered done for the master.

## Specialized Hiring

### Metzia Collection

Rav Pappa proposes yet another reading. He reinterprets the *braisa*'s ruling that when a worker is hired generically the *metzia* goes to the employer: that case does not mean generic employment at all. *Kegon she-secharo le-laket metzi'os* — it means the worker was specifically hired to collect *metzi'os*.

### Fish After Flood

The paradigm case: *d'akfei agmi be-chavrei* — the waters overflowed and fish are stranded on the shore. The employer hires the worker specifically to go and collect these fish. In that case, when the worker picks up the fish, he is doing precisely what he was hired to do, and the collected items go to the employer.

### Generic Hiring

Outside of such specifically designated *metzia*-collection tasks, a generically hired worker keeps his own *metzia*. The *braisa*'s language of *asei imi melacha ha-yom* — work for me whatever I say — does not mean the employer claims all of the worker's activity; it means the worker may be reassigned, but the *metzia* he finds still belongs to him unless he was specifically hired to collect found objects. This reading is somewhat in tension with the plain language of the *braisa*, as the *gemara* implicitly acknowledges, but it is Rav Pappa's resolution.

## Slave Girl Metzia

### Age Status

The *gemara* now turns to the *shifcha ivriya* — the Hebrew maidservant — and asks: what are the circumstances in which this case arises? A Jewish girl sold as a slave by her father goes free automatically upon the emergence of two pubic hairs, which marks her majority. If she has those signs, *mai ba'ya gabei* — what is she doing still in his possession?

### Father Should Get

And if she has not yet reached majority — if she is a minor — then if her father is alive, the prior ruling of the *mishnah* itself should apply: findings of a minor child go to the father. Why, then, does the *mishnah* suggest the slave girl's findings are governed by a distinct rule at all? They should simply go to the father, not to the master.

### Freed on Death

The *gemara* considers the scenario where the father has died. Reish Lakish taught that a Hebrew slave girl acquires her freedom upon the death of her father, freed from the master's authority by virtue of a *kal va-chomer*: if the death of the father releases her from the father's authority, it certainly releases her from the more derivative authority of the master. If Reish Lakish is correct, then the death of the father terminates the master's rights, and the *mishnah*'s case cannot be speaking of a post-death scenario either. However, the *gemara* notes that Reish Lakish's position was challenged and is not accepted as *halacha*, so the *mishnah* could be addressing the situation after the father's death, when the girl remains in the master's possession.

### Father Alive Case

The *gemara* ultimately resolves the question differently. The *mishnah* is indeed speaking about a case where the father is alive. The *mishnah*'s statement that the findings belong to the slave girl herself — *harei hen shelahen* — does not mean she keeps them as against the father. It means she keeps them as against the master. The master has no claim. She acquires the *metzia* first, in her own right, and then the subsequent rule — that a minor child's findings are transferred to the father — kicks in. The sequence is: slave girl acquires first, then it passes to the father. The *mishnah* is ruling out the master's claim; it is not overriding the father's claim, which remains in force via the standard rule.

## Divorced Wife

### Wife Keeps It

The final case in the *mishnah*: *metziat ishto she-geirushah* — the findings of a wife whom he has divorced belong to her, not to him, even if the *ketubbah* — the lump-sum payment due upon divorce — has not yet been paid.

### Uncertain Divorce

The *gemara* specifies that the *mishnah* is addressing a case of *megureshet ve-eina megureshet* — a woman whose divorce status is uncertain. Perhaps the *get* was thrown but it is unclear whether it landed close enough to be considered received by her. In such a case, Rabbi Zeira, citing Shmuel, rules that the husband retains all his financial obligations to her — *ba'alah chayav be-mezonoteha* — since he cannot exploit the ambiguity to escape his duties.

### Normal Rationale

The *gemara* then reflects on why, in the normal case, the wife's *metzia* goes to the husband at all. *Kedei she-lo tehei lah eivah* — so that there should be no enmity. The entire structure of transferring her earnings and findings to the husband is a rabbinic policy designed to maintain marital harmony and prevent the husband from resenting a wife who benefits from his provisions while retaining her own income independently.

### Divorce Exception

In the case of a divorce — even an uncertain one — *hacha ika eivah ve-eivah* — there is already profound enmity between them. The marital relationship has broken down to the point of divorce proceedings. Since the rationale for giving him her *metzia* was to prevent enmity, and enmity already exists at the most acute level, the rationale evaporates entirely. She has no reciprocal obligation to him; the marriage as a functional relationship has dissolved even if technically unresolved. Therefore, she keeps her *metzia*, and he retains only his support obligations until the status is definitively resolved — without any corresponding right to her findings.
