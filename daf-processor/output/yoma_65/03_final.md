# Yoma 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs R. Yochanan

### New Lottery Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דַּיְקָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, וּבָרַיְיתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. מַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, דְּקָתָנֵי: אִם שֶׁל שֵׁם מֵת — זֶה שֶׁעָלָיו הַגּוֹרָל לַשֵּׁם יִתְקַיֵּים תַּחְתָּיו, וְאִידַּךְ כִּדְקָאֵי קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The language of **the mishna is precise in accordance with** the opinion **of** the opinion **of Rav, and** the language of **the *baraita*** is precise **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** The Gemara explains: The language of **the mishna** is precise **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav, as it teaches: If** the one to be sacrificed **to God died, that** goat **upon which the lot for God** was drawn **shall stand in its stead. And the other** goat **stands as it stood** before, i.e., its status does not change, in accordance with the opinion of Rav.

The daf opens in the middle of a debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan that began at the bottom of 64b. The case is as follows: of the two goats brought on Yom Kippur — one designated *l'Hashem* and one *la-Azazel* — one of them dies, necessitating a new lottery with a fresh pair. The question is which goat you ultimately use for the designation that did not die. If, for example, the goat *la-Azazel* died and the goat *l'Hashem* from the original pair is still alive, and you now draw a new lottery, the new goat *la-Azazel* clearly fills the vacancy. But which goat do you use *l'Hashem* — the surviving original, or the one from the new pair?

### Rav's Mitzvah B'Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרַיְיתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״שֵׁנִי״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם שֵׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג רִאשׁוֹן אִם שֵׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג שֵׁנִי, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״יׇעֳמַד חַי״, וְלֹא שֶׁחֲבֵירוֹ מֵת. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? ״יׇעֳמַד חַי״, וְלֹא שֶׁכְּבָר עָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** The language of **the *baraita*** is precise **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as it teaches:** When the mishna says that the **second** goat is left to graze, **I do not know whether** it is referring to **the second** goat **of the first pair, or the second** goat **of the second pair. When** the verse **states: "It shall be stood alive** before the Lord, to make atonement over him, to send him away to Azazel into the wilderness" (Leviticus 16:10), it is **not** referring to the goat **whose counterpart died.** The Gemara asks: **From where may it be inferred,** i.e., how does the verse indicate this point? The Gemara answers that it states: **"It shall be stood alive,"** in the future tense, indicating that it is **not** referring to the one **that already stood** with its counterpart that died.

Rav holds that you use the goat from the original pair. The *Gemara* indicates that the *Mishnah* supports this position. Rav's reasoning rests on the principle of *mitzvah barishon*: as long as the original animal was the first designated for that role, you remain committed to using it. The *Gemara* notes that this position aligns with the teaching of Rabbi Yossi, who derived from an analogous case involving the *Korban Pesach* — where an animal was lost, a replacement was designated, and then the original was found — that the *mitzvah* attaches to the first animal.

### R. Yochanan Nidcheh

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He argues that the surviving goat from the original pair has become *nidcheh* — pushed away, rejected — and therefore cannot be used. The reasoning is that during the interval between one animal's death and the drawing of the new lottery, the surviving goat was in a state where it could not be used; that period of disqualification leaves a permanent mark. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the principle of *dichuy* applies, and you must therefore use the goat from the new pair.

### Ba'alei Chayim Nidchim

At the heart of this debate is a broader question: can a living animal be permanently rejected? The principle under discussion is *ba'alei chayim nidchim* — whether a live animal, once rendered temporarily unusable, is permanently stigmatized as though it were dead or otherwise disqualified. Rabbi Yochanan maintains that yes, even a living creature can be pushed away in this irrevocable sense. Rav disagrees: the ongoing vitality of a living animal resists permanent categorization, and it cannot be fully written off as rejected. There is something philosophically striking about this position — the sense that as long as something is alive, it retains a kind of irreducible openness that defies a fixed label of rejection.

### Tosafot Muvchar

Before continuing with the *Gemara*, it is worth pausing on a *Tosafot* at the bottom of 64b that is both incisive and illuminating. Recall that Rabbi Yossi's original teaching about *mitzvah barishon* included a qualification: if the second animal is of superior quality — *muvchar mimenu* — one should use the better one rather than the first. *Tosafot* raises the question of why we do not apply this same principle to the case of the two goats. If the second pair happens to consist of superior animals, should we not favor them over the first pair, even granting Rav's principle of *mitzvah barishon*?

### Tosafot Human Sphere

*Tosafot* answers with a distinction that cuts deep. The first text in *Tosafot* on 64b formulates it this way: the principle of choosing the superior animal — *muvchar mimenu* — applies only in the domain of *gvulin* (outside the Temple), or more precisely, to things that are eaten or that are performed in the *azarah* and thus visible to human beings. But for the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach*, which is sent away from the camp and is not experienced by the onlookers in any direct sense, or for the inner goat that is burned outside the three camps, even if the second animal is superior, one still adheres to the first. *Mitzvah barishon* overrides the quality consideration when the offering is beyond the human sphere of experience.

### Aesthetics Philosophy

Why is this *Tosafot* so striking? Because it addresses one of the fundamental tensions in the study of *Beit HaMikdash*: why does so much of the Temple service emphasize externals — unblemished animals, perfect priests, gleaming gold, flawless aesthetics? The usual answer is that God does not, in some ultimate sense, require these things, but human beings are profoundly shaped by what they see and experience. Beauty and perfection in the sacred space elevate our sense of the holy; a blemished offering would diminish our perception of what the service means.

What *Tosafot* is saying, in effect, is that this entire concern with superior quality is a concession to the human vantage point. It is relevant only when people are actually going to witness and experience the offering — in the *azarah*, or at table. When the offering moves beyond human experience — sent to a remote place, or brought into the innermost sanctum where no congregation follows — the calculus shifts. The priority returns to the intrinsic: which animal was first designated? Appearances and comparative quality, *Tosafot* implies, are accommodations to human psychology, not objective divine demands. This provides a quietly powerful counterweight to any overly literal reading of the Temple's aesthetic standards.

## Chatat Tzibur Dies

### Mishnah Chatat Tzibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, שֶׁאֵין חַטַּאת צִבּוּר מֵתָה. הָא דְּיָחִיד כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא — מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna **that a communal sin-offering is not** left **to die. This** indicates that the sin-offering **of an individual** in **a case like this** is left **to die.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah* to attempt to adjudicate the debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan. The *Mishnah* states: *she'ein chatas tzibur meisah* — a communal *chatat* does not die. The implication is that when a replacement is used for a communal *chatat*, the leftover animal is allowed to graze until it develops a blemish, at which point its sanctity is redeemed, rather than being left to die. This rule — that the animal must be left to die — applies specifically to a private *chatat*, not a communal one. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees, holding that even a communal *chatat* must be left to die if it was not used.

### Proving Rav Gemara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, כִּדְרַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם נִתְכַּפֵּר בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ אֲבוּדָה — אֲבוּדָה מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds that the remaining goat from the first pair is left to graze until it develops a blemish, it can be explained that the sin-offering of an individual in a comparable case is killed, **in accordance with** the opinion that **Rabbi Abba** said that **Rav said. As Rabbi Abba said that Rav said: Everyone concedes that** if the animal one designated as a sin-offering became lost, and he designated another animal in its stead and then found the first animal, **if he gained atonement through** the one **that was not lost,** i.e., the second one, **the one that was lost** is left **to die.**

The *Gemara* now attempts to use the *Mishnah* to prove Rav's position. The implication of the *Mishnah* — that a communal *chatat* does not die whereas a private one in a similar scenario would — invites us to examine how this works according to both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan.

### Rabbi Abba Lost Offering

The *Gemara* cites a teaching of Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav, found at 60a: *hakol modim she'nitkapar be'she'eina avudah, she'avudah meisah* — all agree that if atonement was made using the animal that was never lost (call it B), then the animal that had been lost (call it A) must be left to die. The scenario: a *chatat* was set aside (A), it got lost, a second animal was sanctified as a replacement (B), and then A was found before B was offered. You now have a choice of which to bring. The *machlokes* concerns what happens to the animal you do not use. But on one point all agree: if you use B — the animal that was never lost — then A, which had been lost and then recovered, must be left to die.

### Niskaper Be'Acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּמַפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הִפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת — מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶם, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תִּרְעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to** the opinion **of Rav,** who rules that the remaining goat from the first pair is the one that is sacrificed, that goat was never disqualified, and the extra goat from the second pair was designated to serve the same purpose as the remaining goat. **This is like** one who **designates two sin-offerings as a guarantee,** so that if he loses one of them he will still be able to bring the other one. **And Rabbi Oshaya said** that all agree that if **one separated two sin-offerings as a guarantee, he gains atonement through one of them, and the second** is left **to graze** until it becomes unfit.

Why must A die when B is used? Because A had already experienced a period during which it was unusable — it was *avudah*, lost. When you then choose B over it, you compound that rejection: *niskaper beacher*, atonement was achieved through another. The category of *niskaper beacher* is precisely the trigger for a *chatat* to be left to die. A was pushed away once by being lost, pushed away further by having a substitute brought in, and so it has to die.

The debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan now comes into focus. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that *ba'alei chayim nidchim*, the surviving goat from the original pair is analogous to A in this case: it was unusable for a period, a new pair was brought to replace it, and so it is rejected and must die. That is consistent with the *Mishnah*'s implication that a private *chatat* in a similar scenario dies.

### Replacement vs Backup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר רָבָא: רַב סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּאָמַר מִצְוָה בָּרִאשׁוֹן, מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּמַפְרִישׁ לְאִיבּוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since Rava said** that **Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said** that **the mitzva** should be performed **with the first** animal, when one designates the second animal, it is considered **from the beginning to be comparable to** an offering **separated** in order **to be lost.** In that case, if it is the sin-offering of an individual, the second animal is left to die.

But the *Gemara* asks: according to Rav, why would a private *chatat* die in this scenario at all? Rav holds *ba'alei chayim einam nidchim* — a living animal is not permanently pushed away. So the surviving original goat, which was never itself lost and never truly rejected, should remain fully usable. Why, then, should the scenario of the private *chatat* ending in death be the analogy?

The *Gemara* answers by drawing a critical distinction. Rabbi Oshaya teaches: *hamafresh shtei chataot le'achrayus* — if someone separates two sin offerings as mutual backups from the very outset, uses one, and the other is left over, the leftover one grazes; it does not die. The rule that a *chatat* must die in the *niskaper beacher* situation applies specifically when one animal was a replacement for another — when A was there first and B was subsequently designated to substitute for A. In that case, the original A is truly displaced. But when two animals are designated simultaneously as *le'achrayus* — backups for each other, neither subordinate to the other — using one does not condemn the other to death; it simply grazes.

### New Pair as Backup

According to Rav, this is precisely the structure of our case. Since Rav holds *ba'alei chayim einam nidchim*, the surviving original goat was never truly rejected. It remained fully valid throughout. When the new pair was brought, the new goat did not come to substitute for a rejected animal; it came alongside an animal that was still perfectly usable. The two — original and new — are thus in the relation of *le'achrayus*, mutual backups. Rav uses the original because of *mitzvah barishon*, but in principle either could have been used. In such a *le'achrayus* structure, the unused goat grazes rather than dies. This is why Rav holds that the case in the *Mishnah*, where the communal *chatat* grazes, parallels the private *chatat* case — because in his view, the private *chatat* in this scenario would also graze.

### Designated for Loss

The *Gemara* presses further. Rava offers the following resolution: since Rav holds *mitzvah barishon*, he is committed from the outset to using the first animal. That means, from the moment the new goat from the second pair is designated *l'Hashem*, everyone understands it will not actually be used. It was designated not as a genuine alternative, but as a structural necessity to complete the new lottery — *me'ikara ke'mafrish le'ibud dami*, it is as if it were separated to be lost from the start. It was never truly conceived as a viable option. And because it was never genuinely intended to be used, it is not a case of *le'achrayus* — mutual backup — at all. Rather, it is functionally equivalent to an animal that was set aside to be lost, like A in the earlier scenario. And therefore, according to the logic of *niskaper beacher*, this goat — the unused *l'Hashem* from the new pair — would die. And that is precisely why, even for a private *chatat*, the *Mishnah* implies it would die: because the structure here is not true *le'achrayus*, but rather one animal designated in name only, already consigned to irrelevance.

## Rabbi Yehudah Chatat

### Rabbi Yehudah Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תָּמוּת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר שֵׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג רִאשׁוֹן יִרְעֶה, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה יָמוּת — מִתְכַּפֵּר בַּשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says: It should** be left to **die. Granted, according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** that the **second** goat **of the first pair should** be left to **graze, according to Rabbi Yehuda,** who said **it should** be left **to die, he** nonetheless **gains atonement with** the **second** goat **of the second pair.**

The *Gemara* now turns to Rabbi Yehudah's position. Rabbi Yehudah holds that even a communal *chatat* — not just a private one — must be left to die when it is not used. The Rabbis maintain that a *chatat tzibur* is treated differently from a *chatat yachid*: the unused communal animal grazes. Rabbi Yehudah draws no such distinction: *bein yachid ubein tzibur*, whether private or communal, if the animal was not used, it dies.

### First Goat Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב דְּאָמַר שֵׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג שֵׁנִי יִרְעֶה, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה יָמוּת — לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמַאי מִיכַּפַּר? מִי סָבְרַתְּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג שֵׁנִי קָאֵי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבְּזוּג רִאשׁוֹן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to Rav, who said** that the **second** goat **of the second pair should** be left to **graze,** and **according to Rabbi Yehuda it should** be left to **die,** then **according to Rabbi Yehuda** he cannot sacrifice either of the two goats: The first goat may not be sacrificed because Rabbi Yehuda holds that disqualified animals are permanently rejected, and the second goat must be left to die. **With which** goat **will he gain atonement?** The Gemara answers: **Do you maintain** that **Rabbi Yehuda was referring to the second** goat **of the second pair** when he said it should be left to die? **Rabbi Yehuda was referring to the second** goat **of the first pair.** The second goat of the second pair is sacrificed.

One might ask: does this mean Rabbi Yehudah requires the same animal to die twice? No — the *Gemara* clarifies that Rabbi Yehudah is talking about the first goat dying, not the same animal being condemned again. The structure of his position is simply that the rule of *niskaper beacher* applies uniformly, without the exemption the Rabbis carve out for communal sacrifices.

### Reversal of Pairing

Rabbi Yehudah's position involves a second, distinct disagreement — one that concerns not whether the unused animal dies, but *which* animal is left unused. Recall that Rabbi Yochanan holds *ba'alei chayim nidchim*: the surviving original goat is rejected and cannot be used, so you use the goat from the new pair. The Rabbis in the *Mishnah* — following Rav's framework — hold the opposite: use the original goat (*mitzvah barishon*) and let the new goat from the second pair graze. Rabbi Yehudah agrees that *ba'alei chayim nidchim*, and therefore the original surviving goat is the one that is rejected and dies. You use the goat from the new pair. But — and this is where his second disagreement lies — Rabbi Yehudah then says that unused goat from the new pair also dies, rather than grazes. He reverses the pairing in terms of which animal is condemned, precisely because he applies *ba'alei chayim nidchim*, and then demands death rather than grazing even for the communal offering.

### Rav and Rabbi Yehudah

This creates the following architecture of debate. There are effectively two independent disputes. The first is between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehudah over whether a *chatat tzibur* dies or grazes when unused. The second is between the positions that hold *ba'alei chayim nidchim* (Rabbi Yochanan; Rabbi Yehudah) and those that deny it (Rav). According to Rav, both debates are live between Rabbi Yehudah and the Rabbis: Rabbi Yehudah disagrees both on *chatat tzibur* (he says it dies) and on which animal is rejected (he holds *ba'alei chayim nidchim* and thus reverses the pairing). According to Rabbi Yochanan, however, there is really only one clear point of disagreement — the *chatat tzibur* question — because on the matter of *ba'alei chayim nidchim*, Rabbi Yochanan himself agrees with Rabbi Yehudah. The *Gemara* acknowledges this and concedes: *kashya* — it is difficult to identify a clean second dispute according to Rabbi Yochanan.

### Blood Spills Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נִשְׁפַּךְ הַדָּם — יָמוּת הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, מֵת הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ — יִשָּׁפֵךְ הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rabbi Yehuda said:** If **the blood** of the goat sacrificed to God **spilled** before it was sprinkled, **the scapegoat** is left **to die. Similarly, if the scapegoat dies, the blood** of the goat sacrificed to God **should be spilled,** and two other goats are brought and lots are drawn.

The *Gemara* now turns to the scenario that demonstrates Rabbi Yehudah's position on *ba'alei chayim nidchim*. Rabbi Yehudah states: *nishpach hadam yamut hamishtalei'ach* — if the blood of the inner goat is spilled, the goat designated to be sent away must be left to die. Conversely, *mes hamishtalei'ach yishafech hadam* — if the *mishtalei'ach* dies, the blood must be spilled.

The first half is the clearer of the two. Before the blood is placed on the altar, the *mishtalei'ach* cannot be sent off — *lo avad mitzvato*, the *mitzvah* has not yet been performed. If the blood spills, there is a period during which the *mishtalei'ach* simply cannot be used, and according to Rabbi Yehudah's principle of *ba'alei chayim nidchim*, that temporary disqualification is irrevocable. The *mishtalei'ach* must die.

The second half is the more surprising one: if the *mishtalei'ach* dies, the blood must be spilled. The *Gemara* asks why: *ha avad lei mitzvato* — surely the *mitzvah* of the *mishtalei'ach* has already been accomplished? In what sense? One might think: the lot was drawn, the animal was designated. That is all that was required. But this leads to Rashi's remarkable teaching.

### Rashi Core Lottery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּשְׁלָמָא נִשְׁפַּךְ הַדָּם יָמוּת הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ — דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא אִיתְעֲבִיד מִצְוְתֵיהּ. אֶלָּא מֵת הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, אַמַּאי יִשָּׁפֵךְ הַדָּם? הָא אִיתְעֲבִיד לֵיהּ מִצְוְתֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** if **the blood** of the goat sacrificed to God **spilled, the scapegoat should** be left to **die, as the mitzva** of the blood **has not yet been performed,** as it was not sprinkled in the prescribed manner. **However,** if **the scapegoat dies, why should the blood** of the goat sacrificed to God **be spilled? The mitzva of** the scapegoat **has** already **been performed.** The only essential detail with regard to the scapegoat is the lottery, which has already been performed by the priest.

Rashi states something that at first seems shocking. On the question of what obligations are truly *me'akev* — indispensable — with respect to the *se'ir hamishtalei'ach*, he writes: *kol davar me'akev bo eina ela hagrala bilvad* — the only act that is truly indispensable is the *hagralah*, the drawing of the lot, which establishes the animal's identity as the *se'ir* designated *la-Azazel*. We know from an earlier *sugya* that Rabbi Yehudah holds that if the *viduy* — the confession — was not performed, the service is nonetheless *kasher*. Rashi extends this: not only is the *viduy* not *me'akev*, but *kol sheken deshiluach d'lo me'akev* — the sending of the goat off the cliff is also not indispensable *bedi'eved*. If the animal designated *la-Azazel* dropped dead after the *hagralah* and the blood was offered, without any *viduy* and without being pushed off the cliff, the *mitzvah* has been fulfilled.

How does Rashi know this? He derives it from the verse's use of the word *chukah*, which limits the *me'akev* requirements to those things done by the *kohen* in the white garments — the blood service in the inner sanctum — and explicitly excludes the acts performed by the designated individual (*ish iti*) who takes the goat to the wilderness. The central symbol, in this reading, is the *hagralah* — the drawing of the lot that assigns each goat its identity. Everything else, including the dramatic scene of the goat going over the cliff, is important *lechatchilah* but not ultimately *me'akev*.

This is, as Rashi frames it, a powerful and clarifying insight. The *viduy* is what the Sages emphasize as creating the *kaparah* — the confession that transforms the Temple ritual into something that speaks directly to a post-Temple reality of *teshuvah*. But according to Rabbi Yehudah, not even the *viduy* is indispensable. What remains as the absolute core is simply the *hagralah*: the designation, the lottery, the identification of each animal with its role. All the subsequent drama — the confession, the procession, the cliff — is meaningful but not constitutive.

### Scapegoat Stand Alive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אָמַר קְרָא: ״יׇעֳמַד חַי לִפְנֵי ה׳ לְכַפֵּר״, עַד מָתַי יְהֵא זָקוּק לִהְיוֹת חַי — עַד שְׁעַת מַתַּן דָּמִים שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of the house of Rabbi Yannai said** that **the verse states:** "But the goat, on which the lot fell for Azazel, **shall be stood alive before the Lord, to make atonement** over him, to send him away to Azazel into the wilderness" (Leviticus 16:10). **Until when must** the scapegoat **be alive? Until the blood of its counterpart is sprinkled,** and if it dies before, the blood is disqualified.

The *Gemara* then raises the natural question. If *bedi'eved* all that is required is the *hagralah*, and the *mishtalei'ach* then dropped dead, what obligation remains? Why should the blood need to be spilled? The answer comes from the verse: *ya'amad chai lifnei Hashem l'chaper alav* — the *se'ir* designated *la-Azazel* must stand alive before God to atone. The *Gemara* derives from this that the *mishtalei'ach* must remain alive through the moment of *matan dam* — while the blood of the other goat is being applied in the inner sanctum. The two animals must both be present and functional during the act of atonement: one *l'Hashem*, one *la-Azazel*. After the blood has been offered, it is true that the *mishtalei'ach* could *bedi'eved* drop dead without further consequence — no *viduy*, no sending off the cliff required. But during the blood service, the verse demands that it stand alive.

Therefore, if the *mishtalei'ach* dies before or during the blood offering, a new lottery must be held — not to fulfill some additional *mitzvah* with the live goat, but because the verse requires a living *la-Azazel* goat to be standing while the *kohen* performs the blood service. This also provides a resolution to the theological discomfort some feel with the entire *Azazel* ritual: by establishing that the sending off the cliff is not ultimately *me'akev*, the *Gemara* effectively decenters the dramatic wilderness scene. What matters occurs entirely within the *mikdash*.

## Next Year's Offering

### Shekalim Trumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בְּנֵי הָעִיר שֶׁשָּׁלְחוּ אֶת שִׁקְלֵיהֶן וְנִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ, אִם נִתְרְמָה תְּרוּמָה — נִשְׁבָּעִין לְגִזְבָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shekalim* 2:1): If **residents of a city sent their shekels** to the Temple with a messenger, **and** the shekels **were stolen or were lost** along the way; **if** the **funds were** already **collected,** i.e., the coins for the new year's offerings were taken from the chamber before the money was stolen, the messengers **take an oath to the treasurers** of the Temple that they did not unlawfully use the shekels, but that they were taken unbeknownst to them or by force.

The *Gemara* transitions to a related question drawn from tractate Shekalim. When a city collects its half-shekel contributions and sends them to the *Beit HaMikdash*, at what point is the city's obligation considered discharged? The answer hinges on the *trumat halishka* — the ritual scooping of funds from the chamber where the collected *shekalim* were stored. Three times a year, the treasurers would formally lift out funds from the chamber to purchase communal *korbanot*. The moment that *terumah* was taken, any money that had already been donated — even if it had not yet physically arrived — was considered registered and paid.

### Lost Money Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו — נִשְׁבָּעִין לִבְנֵי הָעִיר, וּבְנֵי הָעִיר שׁוֹקְלִין אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the funds were **not** yet collected when these coins were stolen, the coins are still considered the property of those who dedicated them to the Temple, and therefore the messengers **take an oath to the residents of the city, and the residents of the city donate other** shekels to the Temple **in their stead.**

If the money sent by the city was lost or stolen after the *terumah* was taken, the city is considered to have paid. The carriers must take an oath before the *gizbarin* (Temple treasurers) that they were not negligent, and the city is absolved. If, however, the loss occurred before the *terumah* was taken, the donation has not yet been officially registered, and the city must contribute again. In that case, the carriers take an oath to the city's residents, and the city itself must collect and send a second round of *shekalim*.

### Duplicate Shekalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצְאוּ אוֹ שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּם הַגַּנָּבִים — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ שְׁקָלִים הֵם, וְאֵין עוֹלִין לָהֶן לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עוֹלִין לָהֶן לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the shekels that were lost **are found or the thieves returned them, both these and those are shekels,** i.e., they remain sanctified, **but they do not count** toward the amount due **the following year.** The next year the members of that city must donate new shekels; they have not fulfilled the second year's obligation by having given twice the previous year. **Rabbi Yehuda says: They do count toward the following year.**

A further question arises: if the lost money is subsequently recovered — or if robbers return it — after the city has already paid a second time, the city now holds two sets of *shekalim*. The first position in the *Mishnah* holds that both sets are applied to the current year's obligation, with no possibility of applying the surplus toward the following year. You cannot prepay next year's tax with this year's designated funds.

### R. Yehudah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר רָבָא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה חוֹבוֹת שֶׁל שָׁנָה זוֹ קְרֵיבוֹת לְשָׁנָה הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda? Rava said: Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **the obligations of this year are** also **brought the following year,** and therefore it is possible to fulfill one's obligation for the next year by using the shekels of this year.

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees. He holds that *chovas hashana hazo kereivah l'shana haba'ah* — an obligation incurred for this year's service, if it was not used, can be carried forward and applied to next year's obligation. This opens the door to the *Gemara*'s next inquiry.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: פַּר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים שֶׁאָבְדוּ וְהִפְרִישׁ אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶן, וְכֵן שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁאָבְדוּ וְהִפְרִישׁ אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶן — כּוּלָּן יָמוּתוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִפְּלוּ דְּמֵיהֶם לִנְדָבָה, שֶׁאֵין חַטַּאת צִבּוּר מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to this** explanation. It was taught that if **the bull and goat of Yom Kippur were lost and one designated others in their stead, and similarly** if **the goats** which atone for a communal transgression of **idol worship** by instruction of the court **were lost and he designated others in their stead,** and the original animals were found, **all** the original animals **should** be left to **die,** and cannot be sacrificed at a later time. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon say: They should** be left to **graze until they become unfit.** Then **they are sold, and the money** received in **their** sale **will go to** the purchase of **a** public **gift-offering, as a communal sin-offering** is **not** left to **die.**

Abaye challenges this position. If Rabbi Yehudah allows last year's unfulfilled obligation to be redirected toward the following year, why does the *Mishnah* require the leftover *par* and *se'ir* of Yom Kippur — the ox and the goat — to die when not used? And similarly, communal *chataot* brought when the entire community sinned through *avodah zarah* — when a substitute was designated and then the original was found — why not simply hold the unused animal over until next Yom Kippur and use it then?

### This Year's Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִבּוּר קָא אָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִבּוּר, כִּדְרַבִּי טָבִי אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי טָבִי אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: אָמַר קְרָא: ״זֹאת עוֹלַת חֹדֶשׁ בְּחׇדְשׁוֹ״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: חַדֵּשׁ וְהָבֵא לִי קׇרְבָּן מִתְּרוּמָה חֲדָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **You speak of communal offerings? Communal offerings are different, in accordance with** what **Rabbi Tavi said** in the name of **Rabbi Yoshiya. As Rabbi Tavi said** that **Rabbi Yoshiya said** that **the verse states: "This is the burnt-offering for every New Moon upon its renewal** throughout the months of the year" (Numbers 28:14). **The Torah said: Renew and bring Me an offering from the new contribution.** This indicates that communal offerings must be brought from the donations of the current year and not from the donations of the previous year.

The initial response invokes a teaching of Rav Tavi in the name of Rav in the name of Rabbi Yoshiya. The verse states: *zos olas hachodesh bechodsho* — this is the burnt offering of each month in its month. From here the Torah teaches: renew and bring a *korban* from new funds. Once an animal has been purchased with this year's *shekalim* — drawn from this year's *trumat halishka* — it is designated as this year's offering. Its identity is bound to the year of its purchase. You cannot carry a 5774 *korban* over to 5775.

This answers the *se'ir* case. But Abaye notes that it does not fully answer the *par* case, because the *Kohen Gadol*'s ox is purchased from his own private funds, not from communal *shekalim*. And even as to the *se'ir*, the principle of buying from new funds is only *lechatchilah*, as Rav Yehudah said in the name of Shmuel: *ve'im hevi min hayashan yatza* — if the communal *korban* was bought with last year's funds, it is valid *bedi'eved*, even if a *mitzvah* was missed. So the obligation to let it die remains unexplained.

## Lottery Designation

### Rav Zeira Lottery Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַגּוֹרָל קוֹבֵעַ מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Zeira said** that the reason the bull and goat of Yom Kippur are left to die is **because the lottery does not establish** designations **from** one **year to another.**

Rav Zeira offers a more fundamental answer for the *se'ir* case. The lot — the *hagralah* — establishes the animal's identity for that year only. *Ein hagoral kovei'ah mishana lachavertah* — the lottery of one year does not carry forward to bind the next. A goat designated *la-Azazel* in year one cannot carry that designation into year two; in year two, a new lottery must be drawn. So you cannot simply hold the goat over and use it next Yom Kippur under last year's designation.

### Gezerah Old Lot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְיתֵי וְנַגְרֵיל? גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ הַגּוֹרָל קוֹבֵעַ מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But let us bring** this goat and another one **and draw lots** again. The Gemara responds: **It is a** rabbinic **decree** that was enacted **lest** people **say** that **the lottery establishes** designations **from** one **year to another.**

One might respond: so redraw the lottery! Use the surviving goat this Yom Kippur along with another goat, draw a fresh lot, and if it falls *la-Azazel* again, use it. To this the *Gemara* answers: *gezeirah shema yomru hagoral kovei'ah mishana lachavertah* — we are concerned that people will see the same goat used in the new lottery and assume that last year's *goral* somehow carried over. The appearance of continuity, even when a new lottery is technically performed, creates a misleading impression that must be avoided.

### Kohen Gadol Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תִּינַח שָׂעִיר, פַּר מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? גְּזֵירָה פַּר אַטּוּ שָׂעִיר. וּמִשּׁוּם גְּזֵירָה יָמוּתוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This works out **well** with regard to the **goat,** but with regard to the **bull** of the High Priest, which is not chosen by lottery, **what is there to say?** The bull remains valid even if it is more than a year old. The Gemara answers: There is **a** rabbinic **decree** with regard to the **bull due to** the **goat.** The Gemara challenges further: Is it right that **due to a** rabbinic **decree** the offerings **should** be left to **die** instead of being left to graze?

For the *par* — the ox of the *Kohen Gadol* — the *Gemara* eventually settles on a different reasoning. The ox is purchased from the *Kohen Gadol*'s personal funds. If a new *Kohen Gadol* is appointed by the following Yom Kippur — which, as the *Gemara* notes, was quite common during the Second Temple period — he cannot use his predecessor's ox. The animal belonged to the previous *Kohen Gadol*, and an offering must come from its owner. *Chatas she-meisu be'aleha* — a *chatat* whose owners have died cannot be used. Holding the ox over risks exactly this scenario.

### Gezerah Cycle

The *Gemara* attempts to construct a mutual *gezeirah* between the ox and the goat cases: don't use the ox because people will think the goat is also permitted to carry over, and don't use the goat because people will think the ox is permitted to carry over. The *Gemara* is visibly dissatisfied with this recursive reasoning — *umishum gezeirah yamusu?* — just because of a *gezeirah* should these animals die? The circularity of using each case as a *gezeirah* against the other is recognized as unsatisfying.

### Age Disqualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם חַטָּאת שֶׁעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ. גְּזֵירָה? הִיא גּוּפַהּ חַטָּאת שֶׁעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **a** rabbinic **decree due to** a concern that the goat will become **a sin-offering whose year has passed.** A goat may not be brought as a sin-offering once it is more than a year old, and there is a concern that the goat will be too old by the Yom Kippur of the following year. The Gemara expresses surprise: Is this **a** rabbinic **decree? It is** certainly **a sin-offering whose year has passed,** as the goat will certainly be more than a year old by the following Yom Kippur.

The *Gemara* finally arrives at what it acknowledges should have been the straightforward answer all along: *ikara de-chatas she-avrah shenasa hi* — the goat is simply, as a practical matter, too old by next Yom Kippur. A *se'ir* must be within its first year; a goat that sat out one Yom Kippur and is being brought to the next is almost certainly more than a year old. This is not a *gezeirah* — it is the direct application of the animal's *peshut*-level disqualification.

### Solar Year Technicality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, כְּרַבִּי. דְּתַנְיָא: ״שָׁנָה תְּמִימָה״, מוֹנֶה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה יוֹם כְּמִנְיָן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מוֹנֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This is not difficult,** as it is possible that the goat will not be disqualified, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to redeeming houses in a walled city the Torah states: "And if it not be redeemed within the space of **a full year"** (Leviticus 25:30), which indicates that **he counts 365 days, in accordance with the number** of **days** in a **solar year;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: He counts twelve months from day to day.** Therefore, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, if the goat was less than eleven days old on the first Yom Kippur, it will not be disqualified on the following Yom Kippur because it will still be less than 365 days old, as long as the first year was not a leap year.

The *Gemara* raises one technical qualification: this is not an absolute certainty if one follows the view of Rabbi, who holds that *shenat temimah* — a full year — is measured as three hundred and sixty-five solar days rather than twelve lunar months. Rabbi derives this from the law of *batei arei chomah* (houses in walled cities), where the Torah uses the phrase *shenat temimah*, and Rabbi understands this to mean a full solar year. If one extends this principle generally, then the disqualification of an animal that is "more than a year old" would be measured in solar terms. If the goat was very young when Yom Kippur occurred — eight days old, for example — and the following year is a standard lunar year, it is theoretically possible that by the next Yom Kippur the animal is still within three hundred and sixty-five days of age.

### Final Reasons Deaths

The overwhelming majority of cases — one hundred out of one hundred according to the Sages, and ninety-nine out of one hundred even according to Rabbi — the goat will be too old by the following Yom Kippur. That is the real reason the *se'ir* must die rather than be held over. And for the *par*, the most compelling reason is that the *Kohen Gadol* may well have died in the intervening year, in which case the ox of the previous *Kohen Gadol* is unusable for his successor. Both animals thus have sound, non-circular reasons for dying, grounded in the practical realities of what they are and who owns them. The *Gemara* will continue this discussion in the following daf.
