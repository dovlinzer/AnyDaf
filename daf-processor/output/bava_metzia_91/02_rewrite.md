# Bava Metzia 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Muzzling & Threshing

### Tanu Rabanan Muzzling

The daf begins with a *baraisa*: *hachosem et haparah vehamezaveg bekilayim* — one who muzzles a cow or who yokes together animals of different species for purposes of threshing is exempt from lashes. The lashes fall only upon the one who actually performs the threshing with the muzzled animal, or who leads the mismatched pair. This is a striking point, because the verse reads *lo sachsom shor bedisho* — do not muzzle the ox in its threshing — which one might have read as placing the prohibition on the act of muzzling itself. The *gemara*, however, understands that muzzling in isolation, without threshing, does not constitute the transgression. The violation is consummated at the moment of threshing, and it is therefore the one who performs the threshing with the muzzled ox who receives lashes.

### Act vs. Preparation

This principle — that it is the direct act, not the preparatory act, that triggers liability — runs consistently through both prohibitions. By *kilayim*, the verse reads *lo sachosh beshor uvachamor yachdav* — do not plow with an ox and a donkey together. The prohibition attaches to the act of plowing or threshing itself, not to the prior act of yoking the animals together. The one who yokes them is not the transgressor; the one who actually leads them in the work is.

### Kilayim Principle

The same logic governs both the *chasima* and *kilayim* prohibitions: preparatory actions, even when they make the eventual transgression possible, do not themselves constitute the *issur*. The *gemara* had previously addressed the case of one who muzzled the animal outside, before bringing it in to thresh — and concluded that even in that case, once the animal is used for threshing while muzzled, the one who causes it to thresh is liable. But critically, the liability accrues at the moment of the direct act, not at the prior moment of muzzling.

### Muzzle Outside Issue

The prior *gemara* had asked: *chasmah babachutz mahu* — what if someone muzzled the animal outside and then threshed with it? The resolution was that liability still attaches at the point of threshing. The present discussion clarifies that in any case, the transgressor is the one who does the threshing, not the one who did the muzzling beforehand — even when those are two different people.

## Voice as Action

### Yelling & Commands

The *gemara* now turns to a different scenario: *chasmah bekolo vehinhigah bekolo* — what if someone accomplished the muzzling or the leading of the animals not through physical contact but through voice? For instance, suppose the animal was threshing and the person yelled at it, causing it to stop eating out of fear — effectively muzzling it through sound. Or suppose the person drove mismatched animals forward by shouting commands. The *gemara* assumes that such a person has in principle transgressed the prohibition, since the muzzling and the threshing are simultaneous — unlike the case where someone physically muzzled the animal beforehand. The question, however, is whether an act accomplished through voice constitutes a *ma'aseh*, an act sufficient to incur lashes.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

*Rabbi Yochanan amar chayav* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that the person is liable. His reasoning: *akimat piv havei ma'aseh* — the movement of one's lips is itself a physical act. Why should it matter whether one moved a finger to flick a switch or moved one's lips and produced sound? Both are uses of one's body to bring about a result. There is no principled reason to distinguish between a physical limb and the lips.

### Reish Lakish Position

*Reish Lakish amar patur* — Reish Lakish holds that the person is exempt. *Kala lo havei ma'aseh* — the voice is not the body. What ultimately accomplished the effect was not the person's body but the person's voice — sound waves that traveled through the air. Since the physical work was done by the voice, which is external to the body, it does not constitute a *ma'aseh* sufficient for lashes.

### Temurah Challenge

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from the laws of *temurah*: if a person attempts to exchange a sanctified animal for another animal, the act is effective — the second animal becomes *kadosh* — and the person receives lashes, even though the entire act was verbal. Does this not demonstrate that a verbal act is considered a *ma'aseh*?

### Temurah Resolution

Reish Lakish responds: that *mishnah* follows the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that one receives lashes even for a prohibition that involves no act — *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav*. This does not prove that speech constitutes an act; it merely proves that Rabbi Yehuda allows lashes without an act.

The *gemara* pushes back: how can we say the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda? The *mishnah* opens with *hakol memirin* — anyone can perform *temurah* — which the *gemara* elsewhere interprets to include a *yoresh*, an heir who inherited a consecrated animal. But Rabbi Yehuda explicitly holds that a *yoresh* cannot perform *temurah* and cannot perform *semichah*, because he does not consider the heir to be the true owner of the *korban*. Since the *mishnah* includes the *yoresh*, it cannot be authored by Rabbi Yehuda. How then can Reish Lakish invoke Rabbi Yehuda to explain the lashes in *temurah*?

The *gemara* resolves this with the classic principle that a *tanna* may agree with Rabbi Yehuda on one matter and disagree on another. The author of the *mishnah* may reject Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding the *yoresh* while nonetheless agreeing with Rabbi Yehuda that lashes apply to a prohibition without a physical act. The challenge to Reish Lakish is therefore blunted, and the debate remains unresolved by the *gemara*.

### Tosafot Framework

*Tosafot* note a broader tension across the *gemara* regarding when speech is treated as an act, and they propose a governing distinction: whether the speech produces a real-world effect. If someone testifies in court, that speech leads to an execution — it has concrete physical consequences. Similarly, performing *temurah* verbally causes the second animal to become sanctified — another real-world effect. In our case too, yelling at the animal to prevent it from eating has a direct real-world effect: the animal stops eating and is effectively muzzled. Speech acts that produce no such effect — cursing someone, blasphemy, or the like — are less likely to be treated as *ma'aseh*. *Tosafot* thus argue that both *temurah* and *chasima* through voice belong to the category of consequential speech, which is why each raises the question of whether lashes apply.

### Physics vs. Meaning

There is, however, an additional and perhaps more fundamental distinction worth drawing out. When we speak of testimony, blasphemy, or cursing, the speech act is primarily an expression of meaning. It is a vehicle for ideas — the content of the words is what matters. These are not easily reduced to physical phenomena. By contrast, in the case of the person who yells at the ox and startles it into silence, there is no question of meaning at all. The sound waves produced by the voice physically startle the animal. It is a purely physical chain of causation: lips move, air vibrates, the animal reacts. In that sense, this case is far closer to flicking a light switch or creating ripples in water than it is to the act of testifying or cursing. From that perspective, Rabbi Yochanan's position that *akimat piv havei ma'aseh* is especially compelling here, even if one would not extend that reasoning to all speech acts.

This yields three categories: pure sound with a direct physical effect, as in the *chasima* case; speech as pure meaning with no physical effect, as in blasphemy or cursing; and speech that carries meaning but also produces a concrete real-world legal or physical consequence, as in *temurah* or testimony. *Tosafot* focus on the second category as the distinction, while the analysis here suggests the first is genuinely different from both others.

## Muzzle Liability

### Lashes & Payment

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa*: one who muzzles his cow and threshes with it receives lashes — and must also pay the owner of the cow for the food the animal was denied: four *kabbin* for a cow, three *kabbin* for a donkey. The animal had a right to eat while working, and by muzzling it, the person effectively stole the animal's food from its owner.

### Double Liability Issue

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *ein lokeh u'meshalem* — one cannot receive both lashes and be required to pay money for the same act. These are two independent forms of punishment or liability, and the principle is that the more severe subsumes the lesser. How, then, can this *baraisa* impose both lashes and a monetary obligation?

### Abaye Answer

Abaye answers: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi d'amar lokeh u'meshalem* — this *baraisa* follows the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds, in the context of *eidim zomemim* — conspiring witnesses who are exposed as false — that the witnesses receive both lashes and are required to pay the monetary penalty they sought to impose on the innocent party. Rabbi Meir allows both sanctions to operate simultaneously. The *baraisa* about *chasima* follows this minority position.

### Rava Answer

Rava offers a different answer, invoking a compact but powerful principle: *esnan isha v'afilu ba'al imo* — the prohibition against bringing a prostitute's fee as a *korban* applies even if the man who paid her committed incest with his own mother. The significance of this is as follows. If a man engages in a capital sexual transgression and later gives payment to the woman, he is technically exempt from paying under the principle of *kim leh b'derabah mineh* — when liable for a capital or corporal punishment, the lesser financial liability is subsumed. Yet the *Torah* tells us that even in such a case, if he does pay, what he gives her is defined as an *esnan*, a prostitute's fee, and cannot be brought as a *korban*. This demonstrates that *kim leh* does not erase the underlying monetary obligation — it merely means the court cannot enforce it.

### Kam Leh Principle

Rashi frames the principle precisely: *kim leh* means the court's hands are tied. If we are prepared to administer lashes, we cannot simultaneously impose a monetary payment. But the debt itself remains. The person genuinely owes the money; we simply cannot act upon it in court. If he chooses to pay — *b'va latzet yedei Shamayim*, in order to discharge his obligation before Heaven — that payment is real and is defined as such. Indeed, the *Bach* cites a version of the *gemara* in which Rava's statement is preceded by precisely that phrase, indicating that the entire discussion is about one who wishes to fulfill his moral obligation even absent court compulsion. *Tosafot* notes that the language of the *baraisa* itself, *u'meshalem*, "and he pays," rather than *yeshalem*, "he must pay," reflects this: it is not that the court imposes payment, but that ideally he should pay.

### Contractual vs. Tort

The principle of *kim leh* most naturally applies when the monetary obligation has the character of a tort or a fine — that is, where the court is imposing one sanction on top of another. But there is reason to distinguish when the monetary obligation arises not from a tort but from a contractual agreement. In the case of the prostitute, the man agreed to pay for services rendered; the obligation is contractual. In the case of the muzzled ox, when the person took another's ox to use for labor, he implicitly agreed to allow the animal to eat — that is part of what it means to use someone's animal. The obligation to compensate the owner for what the animal was denied is therefore grounded in contract, not in a court-imposed penalty. It is far less clear that *kim leh* should extinguish a pre-existing contractual obligation. Both of Rava's applications of the *esnan* principle — the prostitute's fee and the ox's food — may indeed fall into this contractual category, suggesting the principle applies with particular force there.

### Rav Papa Answer

Rav Papa offers yet a third resolution, this one by temporally separating the two liabilities. *Mishas meshicha chayiv lei b'mezonoteha* — from the moment the person took the other's cow by pulling it (*meshicha*), he became liable to feed it. The obligation to provide food arose at the point of acquisition, as an inherent condition of using someone else's animal. The lashes, on the other hand, attach only at the moment of muzzling during the threshing. Since the two liabilities arise at different moments, they do not clash under the rule of *kim leh*, and both can stand.

## Dairy Bread & Breeding

### Two Inquiries

Rav Papa then reports: two questions were brought to him from the household of Rav Papa bar Abba — a different Rav Papa — and to both he answered that the matter was forbidden. He then clarifies that in one of the cases his ruling followed the *halakha*, and in the other he was being strict beyond the strict letter of the law: *chada k'hilchasa v'chada lav k'hilchasa*.

### Dairy Bread

The first question was: *mahu l'lush es ha'issa b'chalav* — may one knead dough using milk, producing milk bread?

### Milk Bread Prohibited

Rav Papa ruled that this is forbidden, and this ruling accords with the *halakha*. A *baraisa* states explicitly: *ein lashin es ha'issa b'chalav* — one may not knead dough with milk. And if one did so, *kol hapas kullah asurah* — the entire loaf is forbidden. The reason is *mipnei hergel aveira* — it will lead to transgression, since bread is eaten at every meal, and milk bread will inevitably be consumed together with meat. The same principle applies to smearing the oven with fat from the tail of a kosher animal: any bread baked in such an oven is forbidden until the oven is fired up sufficiently to remove all residue. Milk bread and meat-greased bread are each independently prohibited even to eat on their own, precisely because of this concern. Contemporary questions about whether an external label or packaging notation — rather than a marking on the bread itself — constitutes an adequate *siman* are a matter of ongoing discussion among the *poskim*, but the core prohibition is clear.

### Kilayim Enclosure

The second question concerned the laws of *kilayim*: *mahu lehachnis min vesheno mino ladir* — may one bring two animals of different species into the same enclosure? Not performing the act of cross-breeding directly, but creating the conditions under which it might occur naturally — locking different species in a barn together and allowing events to take their course.

### Breeding Stringency

Rav Papa ruled this forbidden as well, but acknowledged that this ruling does not reflect the strict *halakha* — he was being extra strict. The *halakha*, as Shmuel states, requires direct physical involvement in the act itself before any transgression is committed. Placing animals of different species in an enclosure together, even with the intent that they breed, does not constitute a violation. Rav Papa's prohibition here was a stringency, not a strict ruling.

## Kilayim Direct Act

### Shmuel Standards

Shmuel's ruling is that to establish a prohibition or a transgression in contexts requiring witnesses to a forbidden sexual act, the observers must see the actors *kederach hamenafim* — in the manner of adulterers, meaning the physical conduct makes clear what is occurring, though perhaps without seeing the genitals directly.

### Menafim Imagery

The contrast is between *derech menafim* — seeing the actors' behavior in a way that makes intercourse evident — and *kemichol bashfopheres*, like an eyelash applicator being inserted into its tube, which denotes actually witnessing the penetrative act itself.

### Kilayim Insertion

By *kilayim*, however, Shmuel rules that the standard is more stringent: *uvekilayim ad sheyachnis kemichol bashfopheres* — to transgress the prohibition of cross-breeding, one must actually insert, directly handling the genitals. Without direct manipulation of the genitals themselves, the biblical prohibition has not been transgressed. Merely placing animals of different species in proximity, or even holding them at the moment the male mounts the female without directly handling the genitals, does not constitute the act.

### Achiza Challenge

Rav Achdaboi bar Ami raises a challenge from a *baraisa*: had the verse said only *behemtecha lo tarbia* — do not breed your animals — without the word *kilayim*, one might have thought that even holding the female animal while the male mounts her is prohibited. The word *kilayim* comes to teach that the prohibition is specifically *kilayim* — cross-species breeding — and not mere involvement in same-species breeding. But notice: the *baraisa* describes the prohibited act as *achiza*, holding — not as direct genital manipulation. Does this not imply that merely holding the animals constitutes the transgression of *kilayim*?

### Achiza Response

The *gemara* responds: *mai achiza hachnasa* — when the *baraita* says "holding," it means actual insertion. The *baraita* is describing the same act of direct genital manipulation that Shmuel specified. Why then does it use the word *achiza*, holding? *Lishna ma'alya* — the *baraita* is speaking euphemistically. The *halakha* requires direct manipulation of the genitals, yet the text uses softer language. What is perhaps surprising is the consequence: a person who holds the animals at the moment of mating, without directly handling the genitals, does not transgress the biblical prohibition — a result that cuts against intuition but is what the *gemara* establishes.

### Min BeMinoRuling

Rav Yehuda rules: *min bemino mutar lehachnis kemichol bashfopheres* — when breeding animals of the same species, it is permitted to directly facilitate the act, handling the genitals like the eyelash applicator in the tube. *Ve'afilu mishum pritzuta leika* — there is not even a concern of licentiousness. *Mai ta'ama ba'avidtei tarid* — this is the person's work; a farmer engaged in animal husbandry is focused on the task at hand, and the handling of animal genitals in a professional context does not provoke inappropriate thoughts, as Rashi explains.

### Min BeMino Holding

Rav Achdaboi bar Ami then reapplies the *baraita* to the same-species context. The *baraita* states that *kilayim* — and not same-species breeding — is what is forbidden, implying that holding the animal while the male mounts her is permitted when they are the same species. The implication would be that only *achiza*, holding, is permitted for same-species breeding — not direct manipulation.

### Euphemism Response

The *gemara* responds identically: *mai achiza hachnasa* — even here, when the *baraita* says "holding," it means direct insertion. The same euphemistic language applies. Thus, for same-species breeding, direct facilitation including full genital manipulation is permitted; and for different species, no transgression occurs unless there is direct genital manipulation. Both rulings rest on the same reading of the *baraita* as speaking euphemistically.

## Mixed Species Barn

### Two & One

Rav Ashi reports a question that was brought to him from the household of Rav Nechemiah of the *Reish Galuta*: *mahu lehachnis min umino veshe'eino mino ledir* — may one bring two animals of one species together with one animal of a different species into the same enclosure? This question assumes that placing a single animal of each of two different species in a barn together would already be problematic — though as established earlier, that is not the strict *halakha*. The question is whether two of one kind alongside one of another is somehow better.

### Animal Preference

The reasoning behind the potential leniency: *keivan de'ika mineih batar mineih garir* — since there is another animal of its own species present, the animal will be drawn to mate with its own kind and will not go to the foreign species. Perhaps the presence of a same-species partner removes the concern entirely.

### Ashi Stringency

Rav Ashi concluded that it is forbidden — *upashat leh le'issura* — but acknowledged this is not the strict *halakha*: *d'lo ke'hilchata*, just as placing one of each species in an enclosure is not the strict *halakha* but is treated as a stringency, so too here.

### Pritzuta Concern

The reason for the stringency: *mishum pritzuta de'avdei* — concerns of licentious behavior. Even if legally no transgression has been committed, bringing different species into proximity in ways that might lead to cross-breeding is considered improper, and for that reason one should not do it even when the technical *halakha* would not prohibit it.

## Worker Food Rights

### Body Parts Work

The *mishnah* establishes: *haya oseh beyadav aval lo beraglav* — if a worker was working only with his hands and not his feet, or only with his feet and not his hands, or even only with his shoulder, *harei zeh ochel* — he is entitled to eat. Any form of engagement in the work suffices.

### Both Hands & Feet

Rabbi Yose beRabbi Yehuda disagrees: *ad she'ya'aseh beyadav u'veraglav* — the worker is not entitled to eat unless he is working with both his hands and his feet. One limb alone is insufficient.

### Ox Parallel

The reasoning of the *tanna kamma* is derived from the verse *ki tavo b'kerem rei'echa* — when you come into your friend's vineyard — which implies that any manner of work in the vineyard entitles one to eat. Rabbi Yose beRabbi Yehuda, by contrast, draws an analogy to the ox: just as the ox uses all of its limbs in the act of threshing, so too the human worker must engage both hands and feet. The ox, of course, has no literal hands, but the principle is that the entire body must be engaged in the labor.

### Duck Threshing

Rava bar bar Rav Huna poses a question: *dash be'avazin ve'tarnigolin le'Rabbi Yosi berbi Yehuda mahu* — according to Rabbi Yose beRabbi Yehuda, what is the rule if one uses ducks and chickens to do the threshing? These animals have no hands. Does Rabbi Yose beRabbi Yehuda mean literally hands and feet, or does he mean that the animal must engage its full bodily capacity in the work? If it is the latter, then ducks and chickens that are actively threshing are surely using everything they have.

### Teiku Hands

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. We do not know whether Rabbi Yose beRabbi Yehuda requires literally hands and feet — in which case ducks and chickens would never be entitled to eat — or whether he requires only that the creature put its full body into the work, in which case they would be entitled.

## Grape Stompers

### Stompers Eating

Rav Nachman rules in the name of Rabba bar Avuha: workers who are stomping grapes are permitted to eat. Since grape stomping is the paradigmatic example of working with one's feet, this connects naturally to the earlier discussion. The stompers work simultaneously with grapes and with wine, and the question becomes: from which of the two — grapes or wine — may they eat, and at what point?

### Wine Stages

The *gemara* specifies: until the workers have gone *sheti va'erev* through the vat, they are considered to be working only with the grapes, not yet with the wine. During this stage, *okhlin anavim ve'ein shotim yayin* — they may eat from the grapes but may not drink from the wine, because the wine has not yet begun to flow, or because they are not yet considered to be working with the wine.

### Sheti Va'erev

Once the stomping has proceeded in both directions — *sheti va'erev*, the horizontal and vertical passes across the vat — the workers are then considered to be working with both grapes and wine: *okhlin anavim ve'shotin yayin*. Even though their job is to extract the wine from the grapes, and their direct labor is on the grapes, once the stomping has reached a sufficient degree they are considered to be engaged with the wine as well, and they may drink from it.

## Species Restriction

### Figs vs. Grapes

The *mishnah* rules: *haya oseh be'te'enim lo yokhal be'anavim* — if you are working with figs, you may not eat grapes; if working with grapes, you may not eat figs. One is restricted to the type of produce one is working with. Nevertheless, the worker has some discretion: *aval mone'a et atzmo* — even though one is entitled to eat the grapes immediately in front of oneself, one may choose to refrain and wait *ad she'yagi'a limkom yafeh*, until one arrives at the choicer part of the vineyard where the grapes are better, and eat there.

### Time Limitation

*Ve'kulan lo amru ela besha'at melakha* — all of these rights to eat apply only during the time of work itself, the *sha'at melakha*. The moment work ceases, the right to eat lapses.

### Travel Exception

However, *aval mishum hashavat aveidah l'be'alim* — in order to preserve the owner's time, there is a special arrangement: *amru po'alim okhlin behalikhatan me'uman le'uman* — workers may eat as they walk from one row to the next, and *u'vachaziratam min hagat* — as they return from the threshing floor. The logic is practical: if the worker must stop working entirely in order to eat, the owner loses time. It is better for the owner that the worker grab produce and eat while walking to the next row, not pausing the work at all. This is technically outside the *sha'at melakha* — the worker is not actively working at that moment — but the *takana* permits it precisely because it serves the owner's interest.

### Vine Identity

The *gemara* raises a question: *oseh begefen zeh mahu she'yokhal begefen acher* — if a worker is working on vine A, may he eat from vine B, which is adjacent? Both are grapes, both belong to the same employer — but they are different vines. The question is whether the right to eat is tied to the specific physical source one is working on, or merely to the species.

### Extended Vine

Rav Sheshet, son of Rav Idi, addresses an embedded difficulty: if the rule were that one can only eat from the specific source one is working on — and not merely from the same species — how could an ox ever eat while working on standing grain? The ox's work is done in one place while its mouth reaches elsewhere. Rav Sheshet answers: *besircha* or *beshatcha* — we are dealing with a long vine or extended stalk, where the animal is doing work at one end of the very same object while eating from the other end. The vine or stalk is a single continuous entity, and eating from it while working upon it satisfies even the strictest reading.

## Intertwined Vines

### Rashi Handling

Rashi explains the case of *mudeles* — a vine that is wrapped around or intertwined with another plant or vine — as follows: when two vines are physically intertwined, a worker trying to harvest from one vine must physically handle and move the other vine in order to gain access to the grapes he is picking. It is in this context that the *mishnah*'s case of figs and grapes becomes intelligible: if a fig vine and a grape vine are intertwined, and you are harvesting grapes but must physically handle the fig vine to do so, the question arises whether you may eat from the figs as well.

### Dov Question

This raises a pressing question: if handling the other vine is what creates the potential entitlement, why should the species restriction matter at all? If the act of physically moving the other vine out of the way is itself considered a form of work, then one should be permitted to eat from whatever one is handling — regardless of species.

### Harvest Rights

The resolution lies in the distinction between handling and harvesting. The right to eat is not generated merely by touching or moving the other vine; it is generated by the act of harvesting from it. Your job, in this case, is to harvest the grapes from vine A. Moving vine B out of the way is incidental — it is a means to your actual work, not the work itself. Since you are not harvesting from vine B, you have no right to eat from it, even though you are physically touching it. The *chiddush* is that handling is not harvesting: it does not matter that your hands are on the other vine if your job is not to harvest from it.

This leaves the broader question noted by the *gemara* unresolved in a different direction: if the standard is that you must be harvesting from the vine, why does the *gemara* allow eating from the same species on a different vine at all — if the rule requires it to be the same physical object? The *gemara* works through this, but the tension is acknowledged.

## Walking Work Status

### Walking is Work

The first interpretive approach to the *mishnah*'s *takana* assumes *mehalek ke'oseh ma'aseh dami* — walking from one row to the next is itself considered doing work for the owner. Going to the next row is a necessary part of the job; it is not downtime. On this reading, the reason the *mishnah* needed a special *takana* permitting eating while walking is not that the walking constitutes a break from work — it is that the worker's *melakha* has shifted. He has finished working row A and is now walking toward row B. His right to eat from row A's grapes has therefore lapsed — he is done with that vine — and the *takana* comes to permit him to continue eating those grapes as he walks, extending the right even beyond the moment his direct work on that vine ended. This interpretation, if correct, would strongly imply that *oseh begefen zeh eino ochel begefen acher* — working on one vine does not permit eating from a different vine — because the entire purpose of the *takana* is to address the problem of the species or vine restriction, not the problem of inactivity.

### Walking Not Work

The second interpretive approach begins from the opposite assumption: *mehalek lav ke'oseh ma'aseh dami* — walking is not considered doing work for the owner. It is necessary travel, but not the job itself. On this reading, the reason the worker cannot eat mid-row-transition is simply that he is not currently engaged in labor. The *takana* comes to permit eating even during this non-work interval, for the owner's benefit. And on this reading, the question of whether one can eat from a different vine while actively working would remain open: the *mishnah*'s *takana* says nothing about vine restriction, only about the work/non-work distinction.

### Vine Ambiguity

Thus the two readings of the *mishnah* yield two opposite results on the vine question, and the *gemara* leaves the matter unresolved: *le'olam* it could go either way. Whether a worker on vine A may eat from vine B remains genuinely ambiguous after the *gemara*'s analysis.

## Donkey Unloading

### Donkey Eating

The *mishnah* states: *u'vakhamor keshe'hi porekes* — a donkey may eat when it is being unloaded. The exact meaning requires clarification.

### Eating Source

The *gemara* asks: *keshe'hi porekes meheicha achla* — once the donkey has been unloaded, its work is done. What source does it eat from, and on what basis? The work is over; the right to eat during *sha'at melakha* should have ended.

### Load Eating

A *baraita* resolves this: *chamor v'gamal okhlin mimasoi she'al gabeihen* — a donkey and a camel may eat from the load they are carrying on their backs. If hay or produce is sticking out from the bags loaded on the animal, it may turn its head around and eat from what is right there on its back. This explains the *mishnah*: the donkey eats from the loaded material while the unloading is still in progress, not after the work is complete. *U'vilvad shelo yitol beyado v'yachilem* — one may not, however, take the hay from the bag and actively feed the animal; the animal must eat naturally and directly from what is accessible. Actively feeding it would constitute something beyond simply allowing it to eat in the course of its work.

### Visibility Question

*Tosafot* identify the *chiddush* of the *baraita*: one might have thought that the animal's right to eat requires that the food be visible in front of it — *she'eina ocheles ela keshi'hi ro'ah hagefen lefaneha* — just as an animal working in a vineyard sees the grapes before it. If the food is on its back and out of sight, perhaps there is no anguish at not being able to eat it, and therefore no obligation to permit eating. The *baraita* teaches otherwise: even food that is on the animal's back, behind its line of sight, counts, and the animal may eat from it. This connects to the earlier discussion about whether the prohibition of *chasima* is grounded in the animal's anguish at seeing food and being denied it, or in the simpler principle that the animal is entitled to eat while working regardless of whether it can see the food. The *baraita* demonstrates that the right to eat extends even to food the animal cannot see in front of it.
