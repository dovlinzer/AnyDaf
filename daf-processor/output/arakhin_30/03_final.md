# Arakhin 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yovel Sale Debate

### Mishnah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת שָׂדֵהוּ בִּשְׁעַת הַיּוֹבֵל — אֵינוֹ מוּתָּר לִגְאוֹל פָּחוֹת מִשְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּמִסְפַּר שְׁנֵי תְבוּאֹת יִמְכׇּר לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who sells his field during a period** when **the Jubilee** Year is in effect **is not permitted to redeem it less than two years** after the sale, **as it is stated: "According to the number of years of the crops he shall sell to you"** (Leviticus 25:15). The plural form "years" indicates a minimum of two years.

The *mishnah* on this *daf* deals with the *halachot* of redeeming one's field — the *sadeh achuza* — during the period of *yovel*. The Torah in *Parashat Behar* establishes that a seller possesses both the right and ultimately the obligation to repurchase his ancestral field, but only after the buyer has had a minimum of two years of unimpeded use. That two-year period is not merely a practical threshold — the seller is actually prohibited from exercising his right of redemption during those first two years. With that framework in place, the *gemara* introduces an *itmar*, a dispute between the *Amoraim*, concerning a more specific and provocative question.

### Rav's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל עַצְמָהּ, רַב אָמַר: מְכוּרָה וְיוֹצְאָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to **one who sells his field during the Jubilee Year itself, Rav says:** The field **is sold** in principle, but **it leaves** the buyer's possession immediately, and his money is not refunded. **And Shmuel says: It is not sold at all.**

*Itmar hamokher sadehu bishnat hayovel atzmah* — what is the *halacha* if someone attempted to sell his field during the *yovel* year itself, the very year in which all ancestral fields revert automatically to their original owners? *Rav amar mechura ve-yotza*: Rav holds that the sale does go through, but that *yovel* then immediately kicks in, causing the field to revert to the seller. The practical consequence is striking: the buyer has paid money, the sale was binding, and a moment later the field returns to the seller. The seller keeps the money. It is, in effect, a case of buyer beware — a valid transaction that is instantly undone by the operation of *yovel*.

### Shmuel's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל? קַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה מְכוּרָה כְּבָר יוֹצְאָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תִּימָּכֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What is the reasoning of Shmuel?** Shmuel derives his opinion via **an *a fortiori*** inference. **And what,** if a field that was **already sold** before the Jubilee Year **leaves** the possession of the buyer in the Jubilee Year, **is it not logical that** a field **that was not** yet **sold is not sold** at all during the Jubilee Year?

Shmuel disagrees sharply. *Einah mechura kol ikar* — the sale never took effect at all. In Shmuel's view, a *sadeh achuza* simply cannot be sold during a year whose entire legal purpose is to return ancestral fields to their owners. The very concept of *yovel* is incompatible with a sale taking effect.

### Kal Vachomer

Shmuel's reasoning is articulated as a *kal vachomer*: if a field that has already been sold goes back to its owner at *yovel*, then how much more so should a field that is being sold at that very moment simply not be sold at all? The *gemara* acknowledges that this is not a technically precise *kal vachomer* in the classical sense — a proper *kal vachomer* applies the identical law in both cases — but the intuitive force of the argument is clear. The entire logic of *yovel* is to restore fields to their owners; it would be internally contradictory for a sale to succeed during the very year that embodies that principle.

### Challenge to Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב, לָא אָמְרִינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יִמְכּוֹר אָדָם אֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to Rav, do we not say** that one may derive **an *a fortiori*** inference **in this way? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **a person may sell his daughter** as a maidservant **when she is a young woman.**

The *gemara* asks: would Rav simply reject this type of *kal vachomer* reasoning? *U-le-Rav lo amrinan kal vachomer ki hai gavna?* To test the point, the *gemara* adduces a parallel case where everyone agrees such logic applies.

### Daughter Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מְכוּרָה כְּבָר — יוֹצְאָה עַכְשָׁיו, שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תִּימָּכֵר?
>
> **Translation:** You can **say** the following ***a fortiori*** inference to reject such a possibility: **And what,** if a daughter who was **already sold, now leaves** her master upon becoming a young woman, **is it not logical that** a daughter **who was not sold is not** capable of being **sold** once she becomes a young woman?

*Yachol yimkor adam et bito keshe-hi na'arah?* — Could a father sell his daughter as a Hebrew slave once she has already reached the status of *na'arah*, twelve years and a day with signs of maturity? She goes free automatically upon reaching that status if she was already sold. The *gemara* presents a *kal vachomer*: if the fact that she was already sold is sufficient to trigger her freedom — since one cannot retain ownership over a *na'arah* — then certainly a father cannot initiate a new sale of her once she has reached that age. This *kal vachomer* has the same structure as Shmuel's argument: if the condition causes release when already in force, it should certainly prevent a new transaction from taking effect. Since everyone accepts this *kal vachomer* in the daughter case, one might think Rav should accept the same reasoning for *yovel*.

### Field vs. Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא הָדְרָא מִיזְדַּבְּנָא, הָכָא הָדְרָא מִיזְדַּבְּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the cases are not comparable. **There,** with regard to a maidservant, once she becomes a young woman **she is not** ever **sold again. Here,** the field that is returned to the seller in the Jubilee Year **may** later **be sold again.**

The *gemara* rejects the analogy. *Hatam lo hadra mi-zavna, hacha hadra mi-zavna.* In the case of the daughter, once she reaches the age of *na'arah*, she exits her father's control permanently and becomes a person in her own right — there is no future window in which he can sell her again. The logic of preventing the sale is therefore compelling. The field, by contrast, is always within the seller's sphere; even if he cannot sell it during *yovel*, he will be able to sell it again after *yovel* is over. The seller never loses fundamental control over the field, and therefore the argument that *yovel* should categorically prevent the sale from taking effect is not as forceful. This distinction allows Rav to accept the *kal vachomer* regarding the daughter while rejecting an analogous inference regarding the field.

## Biblical Sources

### Baraita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״אַחַר הַיּוֹבֵל שָׁנִים תִּקְנֶה״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמּוֹכְרִין סָמוּךְ לַיּוֹבֵל, מוּפְלָג מִן הַיּוֹבֵל מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְפִי רֹב הַשָּׁנִים... וּלְפִי מְעוֹט הַשָּׁנִים״. וּבִשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל עַצְמָהּ לֹא יִמְכּוֹר, וְאִם מָכַר — אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav's opinion from a *baraita*: The verse states: "According to the number of **years after the Jubilee you shall buy** from your neighbor" (Leviticus 25:15). The term: After the Jubilee, **teaches that** a field **may be sold** in the year **adjacent to the Jubilee** Year. **From where** is it derived that a field may be sold in a year that is **separated from the Jubilee** Year? **The verse states: "According to the multitude of the years** you shall increase the price thereof, **and according to the fewness of the years** you shall diminish the price of it" (Leviticus 25:16). The verse teaches that one may sell a field even after several years have elapsed since the last Jubilee Year. The *baraita* continues: **And in the Jubilee Year itself one may not sell** his field, **and if one sold** it, **it is not sold.** This *baraita* clearly seems to contradict the opinion of Rav.

With the theoretical debate in place, the *gemara* attempts to adduce proof. It cites the relevant *pesukim* from *Behar*, beginning with *bemispar shanim achar hayovel tikneh me'et amitecha* — according to the number of years after the *yovel* you shall purchase from your fellow. The *gemara* then turns to a *braita* that derives from these verses several rules: *achar hayovel shanim tikneh* teaches that one may sell immediately after *yovel* for the full fifty years; *muflag min hayovel minayin* — how do we know one may also sell in the middle of the cycle? From the phrase *l'fi rov hashanim u-l'fi me'at hashanim*, whether there are many years or few, a sale may occur at any point in the cycle. The *braita* then states explicitly: *u-vishnat hayovel atzmah lo yimkor* — do not sell during *yovel* itself. *Ve-im machar einah mechura* — and if one did sell during *yovel*, it is not a valid sale.

### Rav's Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה לְמִסְפַּר שְׁנֵי תְבוּאוֹת, אֲבָל מְכוּרָה הִיא וְיוֹצְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And in the Jubilee Year itself one may not sell** his field, **and if one sold** it, **it is not sold.** This *baraita* clearly seems to contradict the opinion of Rav. The Gemara responds: **Rav** could **say to you:** The *baraita* means that the field **is not sold for the number of years of the crops,** i.e., it does not remain in the buyer's possession for a minimum of two years, **rather it is sold** in principle, **and** then immediately **leaves** the buyer's possession.

This *braita* appears to prove Shmuel's position. However, the *gemara* reports Rav's response: *einah mechura l'mispar shnei tvuot aval mechura hi ve-yotza*. Rav reinterprets the *braita* as teaching not that the sale was void entirely, but that it was not valid with respect to the two-year entitlement. Under normal circumstances, a buyer is guaranteed a minimum of two years of unimpeded use before the seller can force redemption. Rav's reading of the *braita* is that selling during *yovel* does not grant the buyer those two years — the sale takes effect and *yovel* immediately causes the field to revert, but the buyer cannot claim the two-year use period.

### Two-Year Rule

This interpretation forces a conceptual reexamination of the two-year rule. Until now, one would naturally understand it as a *limit* on the seller's right to redeem — he may not exercise his right of *geulah* for the first two years. But Rav's reading suggests an alternative understanding: the two-year period is itself an *entitlement* that attaches to any valid purchase. Any buyer is owed a minimum of two years. This is a genuinely surprising idea, one that would not have been expected from the plain reading of the *halacha*.

### Pre-Yovel Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיזְדַּבּוֹנֵי מִיזְדַּבְּנָה, תֵּיקוּם בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ עַד בָּתַר יוֹבֵל, וּבָתַר יוֹבֵל נֵיכְלַיהּ שְׁנֵי תְבוּאוֹת וְנַיהְדְּרַהּ! מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: אֲכָלָהּ שָׁנָה אַחַת לִפְנֵי הַיּוֹבֵל — מַשְׁלִימִין לוֹ שָׁנָה אַחֶרֶת אַחַר הַיּוֹבֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But if** the field **is indeed sold, let it stand in** the buyer's **possession until after** the **Jubilee** Year, **and after** the **Jubilee** Year **let him consume** the field's produce for two **years of crops, and** only then **return** the field. **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If the buyer **consumed** the field's produce for **one year before the Jubilee** Year, **he completes another year after the Jubilee** Year?

To probe this further, the *gemara* raises the following challenge against Rav: if the sale during *yovel* was truly valid, why shouldn't the buyer be entitled to hold the field for two full years after *yovel* concludes? The *gemara* then invokes a *braita*: *achal shana achat lifnei hayovel* — if a buyer had one year of produce before *yovel*, *mashlimin lo shana acheret achar yovel* — he is entitled to one additional year after *yovel* to complete his two years. Why, then, if the sale on *yovel* itself was valid, would we not give him two full years after *yovel*?

### Use vs. Opportunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָחֵית לַאֲכִילָה, הָכָא לָא נָחֵית לַאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The cases are not comparable. **There,** the buyer already **entered** the field in order **to consume** the produce, and therefore he completes the minimum of two years. **Here,** the buyer **did not enter** the field **to consume** the produce at all, as ownership of the field immediately reverts to the seller.

The *gemara* resolves this by drawing a distinction. *Hatam nachit l'achila* — in the case of the pre-*yovel* purchase, the buyer actually began using the field; he had at least one year of produce. *Hacha lo nachit l'achila* — in the case of a sale on *yovel* itself, he never began using it at all. The instant the sale took effect, *yovel* caused it to revert. Since he never entered into actual use of the field, he does not acquire the entitlement to complete two years. The entitlement is triggered not by the mere fact of purchase but by having actually begun to use the property.

This distinction is significant beyond the narrow question of Rav versus Shmuel. It reveals an unexpected dimension of the two-year rule: it is neither a pure procedural limit nor a simple purchase entitlement, but something that crystallizes through the act of actual use.

## Slave Comparison

### Two Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עָנָן: שְׁמַעִית מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל תַּרְתֵּי, חֲדָא הָךְ, וְאִידַּךְ הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְגוֹיִם אוֹ בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ יָצָא לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Anan says: I learned two** *halakhot* **from Master Shmuel. One** was **this** *halakha*, that if one sells his field during the Jubilee Year the sale is ineffective. **And the other** *halakha* concerned **one who sells his** Canaanite **slave to gentiles, or to** a Jew who resides **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, that the slave **is emancipated.** A Canaanite slave is partially obligated in the fulfillment of mitzvot. By selling him to a gentile, one prevents him from fulfilling the mitzvot, and by selling him to one who dwells outside of Eretz Yisrael, one prevents him from fulfilling the mitzva of dwelling in Eretz Yisrael. The Sages therefore decreed that the Jewish master must write the slave a bill of manumission after the sale, so that if he runs away from his gentile master, he would not reenter servitude under the Jewish master.

The *gemara* now reports that Rav Anan identified two parallel teachings of Shmuel on related topics. *Tartei* — two teachings. The first is the one already discussed: that a sale of a *sadeh achuza* during *yovel* does not take effect. The second is an independent ruling: *hamokher avdo l'ovdei kochavim o l'chutz la'aretz yatza l'cherut* — if someone sells his *Kena'ani* slave to a non-Jew or to someone outside the Land of Israel, the slave goes free. The rationale is that a *Kena'ani* slave is obligated in most *mitzvot*, and subjecting him to a non-Jewish master — where he will be unable to fulfill his *mitzvot* — or removing him to *chutz la'aretz* — where far fewer *mitzvot* apply — constitutes a sufficient injustice to warrant imposing a penalty of emancipation on the master.

### Sale Efficacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי, וַחֲדָא לָא הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא הֵי מִינַּיְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Anan continues: With regard to **one** of these *halakhot*, Shmuel said that **the sale is retracted** and the money is refunded, **and** with regard to **one** of them, he said that **the sale is not retracted** and the buyer loses his money. **But I do not know** in **which of** the cases the sale is retracted and in which case it is not.

This pairing raises an immediate question: in each of these two teachings, did the underlying transaction actually take effect, or not? *Hadri zvinei* or *lo hadri zvinei* — did the sale revert, meaning it never truly worked; or did it take effect but then require some corrective response? The two cases seem to demand different answers, but it is not initially clear which is which.

### Rav Yosef Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן, מִדְּתַנְיָא בְּבָרַיְיתָא: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁיחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Let us see** if it is possible to resolve Rav Anan's dilemma. It may be resolved **from that which is taught in a *baraita*:** In the case of **one who sells his slave to** a Jew **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission from his second master.**

Rav Yosef proposes to resolve this from a *braita*: the *braita* states that when a slave is sold outside of Israel, *ha-tzarich she-yachshir menu rabbo sheni* — the second master is the one who must free him. If the second master has the power and obligation to free the slave, this implies that the sale to the second master did take effect — he is genuinely the owner. *Mi d'kari lei sheni rabbo* — since the text calls him the second master, *alma lo hadri zvinei* — the sale clearly did not revert. The sale was valid; the second master now bears the responsibility to emancipate. This, then, is the teaching where the sale took effect but required correction. In contrast, Shmuel's ruling about *yovel* is the one where the sale never took effect at all and the money must be returned.

### Money Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, מִדְּקָרֵי לֵיהּ לִשְׁנֵי ״רַבּוֹ״, אַלְמָא לָא הָדְרִי זְבִינֵי, וְכִי קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכָא: אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה וּמָעוֹת חוֹזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Conclude from** the *baraita* as follows: **Since** the *baraita* **calls the second** owner the slave's **master,** and requires him to emancipate the slave, **evidently the sale is not retracted,** and the buyer loses his money. **And** therefore, **when Shmuel says here** that the field **is not sold** during the Jubilee Year, he means that the sale does not take effect **and the money is returned** to the buyer.

The *gemara* now raises a subtler point regarding what happens to the money paid in the *yovel* case. If the sale never took effect, obviously the money should be returned. But Rav Anan had another concern: how do we interpret the money that was transferred? Was it a mistaken payment — *maot chozrin*, the money returns — or perhaps the buyer, who should have known that such a sale was impossible, effectively gave the money as a gift? *Dilma einah mechura maot matana* — perhaps the sale is void but the money is treated as a gift to the seller.

### Sister Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִשְׁמוּאֵל מִמַּאי דְּאֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה וּמָעוֹת חוֹזְרִין? דִּלְמָא אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה וּמָעוֹת מַתָּנָה, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמְּקַדֵּשׁ אֲחוֹתוֹ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֲחוֹתוֹ — רַב אָמַר: מָעוֹת חוֹזְרִין, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מָעוֹת מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And he could not resolve it from the statement** of **Shmuel** itself, that the sale of a field during the Jubilee Year is ineffective, as the meaning of Shmuel's statement is unclear. **From where** can one infer **that** Shmuel means that the field **is not sold and** the **money is returned? Perhaps** he means that the field **is not sold and** the **money is** considered **a gift, just as it is** considered a gift in his opinion **in** the case of **one who betroths his sister. As it was stated:** With regard to **one who betroths his sister,** a betrothal that is invalid, **Rav says** that **the money** with which the brother betrothed his sister **is returned,** as he knew that the betrothal was invalid and merely intended to deposit the money with her for safekeeping. **And Shmuel says** that it is assumed that he wished to give her the **money** as **a gift.**

This question gains traction from a parallel dispute involving *hamekadesh achoto* — someone who attempted to effect betrothal with his sister. Everyone agrees the *kiddushin* has no legal effect. But the question of the money remains. *Rav amar maot chozrin* — Rav holds the money must be returned, as the entire transaction was a mistake. *U-Shmuel amar maot matana* — Shmuel holds the money is treated as a gift, since anyone who attempts such a thing should obviously know it cannot work; we therefore reinterpret the giving of the money as a voluntary gift rather than a mistaken payment.

### Obvious vs. Contextual

Tosafot notes that Shmuel does not contradict himself: by *yovel*, even Shmuel agrees the money returns, whereas by *kiddushin* of one's sister, even Shmuel says the money is a gift. The difference is that the prohibition of marrying one's sister is *patently obvious* — no one can be confused about whether it is possible, so we interpret the transfer of money as a gift rather than a mistake. By contrast, the rules of *yovel* are technical and contextual; a person might genuinely mistake the situation and believe the sale can take effect. There, the transfer is properly understood as a mistaken payment, and the money is returned.

## Market Responsibility

### Who Bears Fine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי חָזֵית דְּקָנְסִינַן לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ? נִקְנְסֵיהּ לְמוֹכֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara earlier (29a) cited a *baraita* that teaches that if one sells his slave to a Jew outside of Eretz Yisrael, the buyer must free the slave. Nevertheless, his money is not refunded. With regard to this, **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: What did you see** to say, **that we penalize the buyer** and he loses his money? **Let us penalize the seller** and require him to refund the money he received, so that he loses both the money and the slave.

Abaye poses a sharp question to Rav Yosef: *mai chazit d'kansinan lei l'lokeach* — why do we impose the fine on the purchaser, who is now out his money when he must free the slave? *Niknesei l'mocher* — let us penalize the seller instead. The seller, after all, is the one who created the problematic situation by selling the slave to someone who will prevent him from fulfilling his *mitzvot*. The purchaser merely bought what was offered.

### Hole and Rat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו עַכְבְּרָא גַּנָּב, אֶלָּא חוֹרָא גַּנָּב. אִי לָאו עַכְבְּרָא, חוֹרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: It is **not the mouse** that **steals; rather,** it is **the hole** that **steals,** as a mouse cannot steal any item unless he has a hole in which to hide it. In other words, the seller could not have sold his slave outside of Eretz Yisrael had the buyer been unwilling to purchase the slave. Abaye replied: The opposite is also true, namely that **if not** for **the mouse, from where** would **the hole** obtain the stolen item?

Rav Yosef responds with a memorable formulation: *lav achbara ganav ela chura* — it is not the rat that steals, but the hole. The image is of a rat that stores stolen goods in a hole: the hole, by providing the necessary infrastructure, is itself a participant in the theft. Similarly, the purchaser, by providing a willing market for this illicit sale, is not an innocent party. Without a buyer, the seller could not ply his trade.

### Current Holder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא, הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרָא קָנְסִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Had the seller refused to sell his slave, the buyer could not have purchased him and taken him out of Eretz Yisrael. Rav Yosef replied to Abaye: Although both parties are at fault, **it stands to reason** that **we apply the penalty wherever the** subject of **the prohibition is** currently located. Since the buyer is now in possession of the slave, he is the one who is penalized.

The *gemara* immediately pushes back. *Ei lav achbara chura mina lei* — if not for the rat, what would the hole ever do with itself? The hole does not initiate anything; it is the rat that acts first. The *gemara* therefore clarifies: *heicha de'isa lidi kinsinan* — we impose the fine on whichever party currently embodies the problem. At the moment of enforcement, it is the purchaser who is holding the slave in a condition that prevents him from observing his *mitzvot*. The problem presently resides with the current holder, and so it is the current holder who must address it.

### Modern Application

The implications of this principle extend well beyond its immediate *halachic* context. The case articulates a clear doctrine of market complicity: one who provides the infrastructure, the demand, or the enabling conditions for an illicit activity cannot simply disclaim responsibility by pointing to the primary actor. The *gemara* does not excuse the seller — both parties bear responsibility — but it focuses corrective obligation on the party in whom the active harm currently resides. This speaks directly to questions of enabling and supporting wrongdoing through one's economic participation, including supply chains, financial services, and similar contexts where the connection to the ultimate harm may feel indirect but is structurally essential.

## Two-Year Use Rule

### Blight vs. Negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה שְׁנַת שִׁדָּפוֹן וְיֵרָקוֹן, אוֹ שְׁנַת שְׁבִיעִית — אֵינוּ עוֹלָה מִן הַמִּנְיָן. נָרָהּ אוֹ הוֹבִירָהּ — עוֹלָה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** If one of those years **was a year of blight or mildew, or** if it was the **Sabbatical Year,** when the buyer is unable to derive benefit from the field, that year **does not count** as part **of the tally,** and the owner must wait an additional year before redeeming the field. If the buyer **plowed** the field but did not sow it, **or** if **he left it fallow,** that year **counts** as part **of his tally,** as it was fit to produce a crop.

The *mishnah* addresses two additional scenarios concerning how the two-year period is counted. If the buyer's use of the field was disrupted by *shidafon* — blight — that year does not count toward the two years; the buyer retains his entitlement to use the field for an additional year. However, if the buyer simply chose not to plant — if he let the field lie fallow through his own decision — that year does count. He made his choice, and the seller need not bear the consequence of the buyer's inaction.

### Plowing Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא הוֹבִירָהּ עוֹלָה לוֹ, נָרָהּ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one of the two years after the sale **was a year of blight,** the buyer is entitled to an additional year's crops. If the buyer plowed the field but did not sow it, or if he left it fallow, that year counts as part of his tally, as it was fit to produce a crop. The Gemara asks: **Now** that it was taught that if the buyer **left** his field **fallow** that year **counts** as part of **his** tally, even though he did not cultivate the field at all, **is it necessary** for the mishna to teach that the year counts as part of his tally if **he plowed** the field but did not sow it?

The *gemara* observes that if negligent non-use counts toward the two years, then certainly active plowing should count. *Neirah mibaya* — is it even a question? If you let it lay waste and it counts, plowing should obviously count all the more so.

### Improvements & Compensation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָרָהּ אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ — סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: הַב לֵיהּ דְּמֵיהּ וְלִיסַּק, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach the case of a buyer who **plowed** the field but did not sow it, as it might **enter your mind to say** that **we say to** the seller: **Give the monetary** value **of** the enhancement of the field **to** the buyer, **and** then **he will leave** the field. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that the seller does not have to pay him that amount.

The *gemara* responds that the case of plowing represents a genuine *chiddush* requiring explicit statement. One might have thought: *salka da'ata amina amrinan lei, hav lei dmei pulchana* — we would tell the seller, fine, you may redeem the field, but pay the buyer for the plowing he has done. The field is now prepared for planting; the buyer invested labor and the seller will immediately benefit. Should the seller not compensate him for that improvement?

The *gemara* teaches that this is not so. The buyer had two years to use the field as he saw fit. He chose to plow it rather than plant it during those two years — that was his decision, and the unrealized opportunity to benefit from that plowing is his loss. When the seller exercises his right of redemption, he need not compensate for the plowing. This ruling opens a broader question that continues to bear on subsequent discussion: to what extent, if any, must the redeeming seller factor into the redemption price improvements that the buyer made to the land? The answer here is that at least certain categories of improvement — those representing an unrealized opportunity the buyer failed to capitalize on — do not generate a compensation obligation.

The *mishnah* further records the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer: since the two years are reckoned by calendar years rather than by harvests, a buyer who purchased just before *Rosh Hashanah* while the field was full of produce may collect up to three harvests within the span of two calendar years. Correspondingly, the redemption price is calculated proportionally based on the years sold — the seller deducts a fixed fraction of the original price for each year used — without any adjustment for the fact that the buyer happened to extract three harvests instead of two. The arithmetic of redemption is fixed and does not flex to capture windfalls in either direction.

## Multiple Sales

### High-to-Low

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָהּ לָרִאשׁוֹן בְּמָנֶה, וּמָכַר רִאשׁוֹן לַשֵּׁנִי בְּמָאתַיִם, אֵינוֹ מְחַשֵּׁב אֶלָּא עִם הָרִאשׁוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר מָכַר לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When the Jubilee Year is in effect, one may sell a field only until the Jubilee Year, at which point the field returns to its original owner. If the owner redeems the field before the Jubilee Year, the payment per annum is calculated by dividing the sale price by the number of years from the sale until the Jubilee Year. The owner returns the per annum payment multiplied by the number of years remaining until the Jubilee Year. If the owner of a field **sold it to the first** buyer **for one hundred** dinars **and the first** buyer then **sold** it **to the second** buyer **for two hundred** dinars, when the original owner redeems the field **he calculates** the payment **only** according to the price that he set **with the first** buyer, **as it is stated:** "And he calculates the years of its sale, and he returns the remainder to the man **to whom he sold it"** (Leviticus 25:27).

The *mishnah* now turns to cases involving multiple sales and fluctuating prices. The first scenario: *machrah l'rishon b'maneh u-machrah rishon l'sheni b'matayim* — the original owner sold the field to a first buyer for 100, and that buyer then sold it to a second buyer for 200. The original owner now wishes to redeem the field. The question is whether the redemption price is computed based on the 100 paid in the first sale or the 200 paid in the second sale.

### Seller Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָהּ לָרִאשׁוֹן בְּמָאתַיִם, וּמָכַר הָרִאשׁוֹן לַשֵּׁנִי בְּמָנֶה — אֵינוֹ מְחַשֵּׁב אֶלָּא עִם הָאַחֲרוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְחִשַּׁב אֶת שְׁנֵי מִמְכָּרוֹ וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הָעוֹדֵף לָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר מָכַר לוֹ״, לָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר בְּתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner of a field **sold it to the first** buyer **for two hundred** dinars **and the first** buyer then **sold** it **to the second** buyer **for one hundred** dinars, when the original owner redeems the field, **he calculates** the payment **only** according to the price that was paid **by the last** buyer, **as it is stated: "And he calculates the years of its sale, and he returns the remainder to the man to whom he sold it."** The superfluous term "to the man" indicates that the verse is referring **to the man who is** currently **in** possession of the field.

The rule, *ein mechashev ela im ha-rishon* — one computes only based on the first buyer — means the seller uses the original lower price as the basis. So if, for example, half the time until *yovel* has elapsed, the seller need only pay 50, even though the current holder paid 200 and the field's remaining use-value may itself be worth 100. The converse case follows: *machrah l'rishon b'matayim u-machrah rishon l'sheni b'maneh* — if the field was sold to the first buyer for 200 but then sold on to a second buyer for only 100. Now the *mishnah* rules *ein mechashev ela im ha-acharon* — compute based on the second, lower price. In other words, the rule consistently favors the original seller: whichever of the sale prices results in a lower redemption cost for the seller is the one used.

### Biblical Flexibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְכָרָהּ לָרִאשׁוֹן בְּמָנֶה, וּמְכָרָהּ רִאשׁוֹן לַשֵּׁנִי בְּמָאתַיִם, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחַשֵּׁב אֶלָּא עִם הָרִאשׁוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״[לָאִישׁ] אֲשֶׁר מָכַר לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** Consider the case where the owner of a field **sold it to the first** buyer **for one hundred** dinars **and the first** buyer then **sold it to the second** buyer **for two hundred** dinars. **From where** is it derived **that** when the original owner redeems the field, **he calculates** the payment **only** according to the price that he set **with the first** buyer? **The verse states:** "And he calculates the years of its sale, and he returns the remainder to the man **to whom he sold it"** (Leviticus 25:27).

The *gemara* derives this dual approach from the *pesukim*. The verse states *v'heshiv et ha-odef l'ish asher machar lo* — he shall return the remainder to the man who sold to him. The phrase *l'ish asher machar lo* points to the original sale. But elsewhere the verse says *l'ish asher betocho* — to the man who is currently within it, the current holder — which points to a later transaction. Since the *pesukim* can be read as authorizing computation based on either the original sale or the subsequent one, the *halacha* adopts whichever reading benefits the original seller.

### Redemption Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִמְכּוֹר בְּרָחוֹק וְיִגְאַל בְּקָרוֹב, בְּרָעָה וְיִגְאַל בְּיָפָה, וְלֹא יִלְוֶה וְיִגְאַל, וְלֹא יִגְאַל לַחֲצָאִין, וּבַהֶקְדֵּשׁ מוּתָּר בְּכוּלָּן. זֶה חוֹמֶר בַּהֶדְיוֹט מִבַּהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** One **may not sell** his ancestral field that is located **in a distant** area **and redeem** with the proceeds a field that he sold **in a nearby** area. Likewise, he may not sell **a low-quality** field **and redeem with** the proceeds **a high-quality** field. **And he may not borrow** money **and redeem** the field, **nor may he redeem** the field incrementally, **half** now and half at a later date. **But with regard to** redeeming a field from **the Temple** treasury, it is **permitted** to redeem the field **in any of these** ways. **This is** a *halakha* where greater **stringency** applies **with regard to** redeeming a field from **an ordinary** individual **than with regard to** redeeming it from **the Temple** treasury.

The *mishnah* also enumerates several restrictions on how a seller may exercise his right of redemption. *Lo yimkor b'rachok v'yigol b'karov* — one should not sell a distant field in order to redeem a nearby one. *B'ra'ah v'yigoel b'yafah* — one should not sell a bad field in order to redeem a good one. The seller must redeem using money he actually has, not through such leveraged maneuvering. *Ve-lo yilve v'yigoel* — one may not borrow money for the purpose of redemption. *Ve-lo yigoel l'chatza'in* — one may not redeem half the field; redemption must be of the whole. This last restriction is presumably a genuine limit on the right itself: if the seller shows up with money to redeem only half, the current holder may legitimately refuse.

### Hekdesh Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִשִּׂיגָה יָדוֹ״ — יַד עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יִלְוֶה וְיִגְאַל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But with regard to** redeeming a field from **the Temple** treasury, it is **permitted** to redeem the field **in any of these** ways. This is a *halakha* where greater **stringency** applies with regard to redeeming a field from an ordinary individual than with regard to redeeming it from the Temple treasury. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** with regard to the verse: "And he prospers and finds sufficient means to redeem it" (Leviticus 25:26), the phrase **"and he prospers"** teaches that the money he uses to redeem the field must be **his own possession,** meaning **that he may not borrow** money **and redeem** the field.

*U-vehekdesh mutar bekulan* — by *hekdesh*, all of these restrictions are relaxed. The *mishnah* notes *ze chomer b'hedyot mi-behekdesh* — dealing with a private individual is more restrictive than dealing with *hekdesh*. The rationale is asymmetric stakes: if you do not redeem a *sadeh achuza* from a private buyer, *yovel* will return it to you eventually regardless. If you do not redeem a field you consecrated to *hekdesh*, and *yovel* arrives without redemption, the field is permanently lost to you. Since the Torah invests heavily in an Israelite's connection to his ancestral land, it grants broader latitude to redeem from *hekdesh* in order to preserve that connection. The greater the risk of total loss, the more the Torah loosens the procedural constraints.

## Price Fluctuation

### Value Increase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְכָרָהּ לוֹ בְּמָנֶה, וְהִשְׁבִּיחָהּ וְעָמְדָה עַל מָאתַיִם, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחַשֵּׁב אֶלָּא בְּמָנֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הָעוֹדֵף״ — הָעוֹדֵף שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** Consider the case where the owner of a field **sold it to** the buyer **for one hundred** dinars, **and** the field **appreciated** in value while in the buyer's possession **and** its value **stood at two hundred** dinars. **From where** is it derived **that** when the seller redeems the field **he calculates** the payment **only** according **to** the **one hundred** dinars he originally received for the field?**As it is stated: "And he returns the remainder** to the man to whom he sold it," that is, the seller returns only **the remainder** of the original payment **that is in his possession.**

A *baraita* now addresses the scenario where there was only one sale but the value of the field has since changed. *Machar lo b'maneh ve-hishvicha ve-amda al matayim* — he sold the field for 100, and it improved in value so that it is now worth 200. The improvement may have come from natural changes, flooding and irrigation, or simply from market appreciation. *Minayin she-ein mechashev ela b'maneh?* — How do we know that the seller may redeem it based on the original price of 100 and not the current market value of 200? The *baraita* derives this from *ve-heshiv et ha-odef* — he returns the *odef*, the remainder. *Ha-odef she-beyado* — the remainder refers to what remains of the money in the buyer's hand, i.e., the remaining proportion of the original purchase price. The computation is anchored to the original sale.

### Value Decrease

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָהּ לוֹ בְּמָאתַיִם, וְהִכְסִיפָה וְעָמְדָה עַל מָנֶה, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מְחַשְּׁבִין אֶלָּא בְּמָנֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הָעוֹדֵף״ — הָעוֹדֵף שֶׁבַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda continues: Consider the case where the owner of a field **sold it to** the buyer **for two hundred** dinars, **and** the field **depreciated** in value while in the buyer's possession **and** its value **stood at one hundred** dinars. **From where** is it derived **that** when the seller redeems the field, the payment **is calculated only** according **to** the **one hundred** dinars the field is currently worth? **As it is stated: "And he returns the remainder** to the man to whom he sold it," i.e., he returns **that which** now **remains of the** value of the **land.**

The converse: *machar lo b'matayim ve-hichsifa ve-amda b'maneh* — the field was sold for 200 but has since declined in value to only 100. *Minayin she-ein mechashev ela b'maneh?* — How do we know the seller may redeem at the lower current price? The same phrase *ve-heshiv et ha-odef* is now read as *ha-odef she-bakarka* — the remaining value of the land itself. When value has declined, the relevant *odef* is the current worth of the property, not the original purchase price. Again, whichever reading yields the more favorable outcome for the original seller is the operative one.

### Dispute Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְכָרָהּ לוֹ בְּמָנֶה, וְהִשְׁבִּיחָהּ וְעָמְדָה עַל מָאתַיִם, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחַשֵּׁב אֶלָּא בְּמָנֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הָעוֹדֵף״ — הָעוֹדֵף שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda says** that the verse should be interpreted differently: Consider the case where the owner of a field **sold it to** the buyer **for one hundred** dinars, **and** the field **appreciated** in value while in the buyer's possession **and** its value **stood at two hundred** dinars. **From where** is it derived **that** when the seller redeems the field **he calculates** the payment **only** according **to** the **one hundred** dinars he originally received for the field?**As it is stated: "And he returns the remainder** to the man to whom he sold it," that is, the seller returns only **the remainder** of the original payment **that is in his possession.**

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda and the *tanna* of the *mishnah* (Rebbe) are operating in parallel but not necessarily agreeing. Rebbe's ruling in the *mishnah* addresses the case of multiple sales — different purchase prices paid by different buyers. Rabbi Dostai's ruling in the *baraita* addresses a single sale with fluctuating market value. Both adopt the principle of favoring the original seller, but they apply that principle to different factual configurations. The *gemara* identifies the case that distinguishes them: *deyakar u-zal u-deyakar* — the field was sold at a high price, then resold at a lower price, and has now appreciated again to the higher value. According to Rebbe, who focuses on the various sale prices, the relevant comparison is between the original sale price of 200 and the second sale price of 100; the seller may redeem at the lower second-sale price. According to Rabbi Dostai, who looks at original sale price versus current market value, both of those figures now stand at 200, and the seller would have to use 200 as the basis. Tosafot adds a simpler illustration of the divergence: if there was only one sale at 200 and the field is now worth 100 with no second sale having occurred, Rebbe — who goes by purchase prices — would use 200, while Rabbi Dostai — who also looks at current market value — would use the lower figure of 100.

## Justification

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דִּלְקוּלָּא? דִּלְמָא לְחוּמְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The aforementioned practical difference aside, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda agree that the verses are interpreted in a manner that benefits the original owner. The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it derived **that** the verses should be interpreted **as a leniency** for the seller? **Perhaps** they should be interpreted **as a stringency** for the seller.

Having established that the *halacha* favors the original seller in all these scenarios, the *gemara* poses a fundamental challenge: *u-mimai d'lekula dilma l'chumra?* Since the *pesukim* contain expressions that can be read as pointing to either the original price or the current value, why is the correct interpretation the one that benefits the seller? Why not read the ambiguity against the seller, in a direction that is more stringent for him?

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּגָמַר ״גְּאוּלָּה״ ״גְּאוּלָּה״ מֵעֶבֶד עִבְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Such a possibility should **not enter your mind, as it is learned** by means of a verbal analogy between the term **"redemption"** written in this context and **"redemption" from** the passage discussing **a Hebrew slave** that the Torah is lenient with regard to the redeemer. The verse states with regard to the redemption of a field: "And he prospers and finds sufficient means to redeem it" (Leviticus 25:26), and with regard to the redemption of a slave who was sold to a gentile, the verse states: "After he is sold he shall attain redemption" (Leviticus 25:48). Accordingly, just as the Torah is lenient with regard to the redemption of a Hebrew slave, so too the Torah is lenient with regard to redeeming a field.

The *gemara* responds: *lo salka datach* — that cannot be. The reason is that we derive the laws of *geulah* for a sold field from the laws of *geulah* for an *eved ivri* by means of a *gezeirah shavah*, a verbal analogy. The same root *geulah* — redemption — appears in both contexts: the redemption of the ancestral field and the self-redemption of a Jew who sold himself as a slave. Since the two are linked by this *gezeirah shavah*, whatever principle governs redemption for the slave governs redemption for the field. It therefore suffices to establish that the slave's redemption operates in the lenient direction — to the slave's benefit — and the same will be true for the field by virtue of this analogy.

### Slave Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: נִמְכַּר בְּמָנֶה, וְהִשְׁבִּיחַ וְעָמַד עַל מָאתַיִם, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מְחַשְּׁבִין אֶלָּא מִמָּנֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** in the case of a Hebrew slave, **from where do we** derive that the Torah is lenient with regard to his redemption? The Gemara responds: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Consider the case of a Hebrew slave who **was sold for one hundred** dinars, **and he appreciated** in value during his term of servitude **and** his value **stood at two hundred** dinars. **From where** is it derived **that** if he redeems himself, the sum he pays for the remaining years of his service **is calculated only** according **to** the **one hundred** dinars for which he was originally sold? **As it is stated:** "If there are yet many years, according to them he shall return the price of his redemption **from the money of his purchase"** (Leviticus 25:51).

A *baraita* confirms the parallel. *Nimkar b'maneh ve-hishbiach ve-amad al matayim* — a Jew sold himself as a slave for 100, and he has since become worth 200 (through improved health, increased demand, or whatever the reason). *Minayin she-ein mechasvin ela b'maneh?* — How do we know his self-redemption is computed at the original price of 100? *Shene'emar mikesef miknato* — the verse says he redeems himself from the money of his purchase, meaning the original transaction price. Conversely, *nimkar b'matayim ve-hichsif ve-amad al maneh* — sold for 200 but now worth only 100. *Minayin she-ein mechasvin ela mimaneh?* — How do we know he can redeem at the lower current value? *Shene'emar kefi shanav* — according to his remaining years, meaning the value represented by those years at current rates. The same slave-redemption principle is confirmed when sold to a non-Jew: *ve-yado al ha-elyona*, the conditions must favor the slave. A further *gezeirah shavah* of *sachir-sachir* — the word *sachir* appearing in both the context of a slave sold to a non-Jew and in the context of a slave sold to a Jew — extends the same lenient principle to both scenarios.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵינִי כְּבֶן עַזַּאי בְּשׁוּקֵי טְבֶרְיָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְאַבָּיֵי: אִיכָּא לְמִידְרְשִׁינְהוּ לְקוּלָּא, וְאִיכָּא לְמִידְרְשִׁינְהוּ לְחוּמְרָא, מִמַּאי דִּלְקוּלָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye said:** I am ready to answer any questions put to me **like** those of the intellectually sharp **ben Azzai,** who would regularly expound **in the markets of Tiberias. One of the Sages said to Abaye:** The verses discussing the redemption of a Hebrew slave **can be interpreted as a leniency** for the slave, **and they can** also **be interpreted as a stringency** for him. **From where** is it derived **that** the verses should be interpreted **as a leniency** for the slave?

Abaye, in a characteristically bold mood — invoking the phrase *hareini k'Ben Azzai b'shukei Tverya*, presenting himself as ready to take on all challengers — receives a pointed question from one of the other *Rabbanan*: *ika d'darshinhu l'kula ve-ika d'darshinhu l'chumra* — granted that the *pesukim* can be read in either direction, what justifies reading them leniently toward the slave rather than strictly against him? The *gezeirah shavah* itself does not determine the direction. The fact that the same principle is mirrored in both contexts only confirms the parallel; it does not explain why either context should be read favorably. Abaye is, at least for the moment, deflated.

### Slave Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּיךְ, מִדְּאַקֵּיל רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״ — עִמָּךְ בַּמַּאֲכָל, עִמְּךָ בַּמִּשְׁתֶּה...
>
> **Translation:** Abaye explained: Such a possibility should **not enter your mind, due to** the fact **that the Merciful One was** explicitly **lenient with regard to** a slave. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a Hebrew slave: "And it shall be, if he says to you I will not leave **you…because he fares well with you"** (Deuteronomy 15:16). The term "with you" indicates that the slave must be **with you,** i.e., treated as your equal, **with regard to food,** and **with you with regard to drink.** This means **that you should not be eating fine bread** while **he eats inferior bread [*kibbar*],** bread from coarse flour mixed with bran. Likewise, **you** should not **drink old wine** while **he drinks** inferior **new wine. You** should not **sleep on** bedding made from **soft sheets** while **he sleeps on the ground.**

The *gemara* answers that the resolution lies in the Torah's broader concern for the welfare of the *eved ivri*. A *braita* expounds: *ki tov lo imach* — it is good for him with you, or as Tosafot reads, the operative verse is *ki sachir yihyeh imach* — he shall be as a laborer with you — with the emphasis on *imach*, with you, as an equal. *Imach b'ma'achal imach b'mishteh* — his food and drink should be the same as yours. You may not eat fine white bread while he eats bran bread. You may not drink aged wine while he drinks new wine. You may not sleep on cushions while he sleeps on the ground.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיכָּן אָמְרוּ: הַקּוֹנֶה עֶבֶד עִבְרִי כְּקוֹנֶה אָדוֹן לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **From here** the Sages **stated: One who acquires a Hebrew slave** is considered **like one who acquires a master for himself,** as he must ensure that the slave's living conditions are equal to his own.

*Mikan amru, hakoneh eved ivri k'koneh adon l'atzmo* — from here the sages said: whoever acquires a Hebrew slave has, in effect, acquired a master for himself. Given this extraordinary insistence on the dignity and welfare of the slave — which itself reflects the Torah's broader antipathy to the institution of slavery expressed throughout the legislation of *Mishpatim* — it is self-evident that all redemption-related rules will be read in the slave's favor. And once that principle is established for the slave, the *gezeirah shavah* carries it over to the field.

## Degradation Narrative

### Shevi'it Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, נַחְמִיר עֲלֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: ״בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה קָשֶׁה אֲבָקָהּ שֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית״.
>
> **Translation:** That Sage raised an objection to Abaye: **On the contrary, let us impose a stringency upon** the slave, **due to** the statement **of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina. As Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** in explanation of the juxtaposition of several passages in the Torah (Leviticus, chapter 25): **Come and see how severe** is even **the hint of** violation of the prohibition of **the Sabbatical** Year, as the prohibition against engaging in commerce with produce of the Sabbatical Year is not one of the primary prohibitions of the Sabbatical Year, and yet its punishment is harsh.

The *gemara* does not fully concede, however. *Eima ge'ulah tihyeh lo she-lo yitamei be-avodas kochavim* — fine, we must redeem him so that he does not become wholly absorbed among the pagans. That obligation is accepted. *U-le-olam le-inyan pidyono nachmir bo* — but when it comes to computing the actual redemption amount, perhaps we should be strict with him. After all, we have not yet proven — from the moral concerns about treating the slave with dignity — that when the *pesukim* about valuation can be read in two directions, we must choose the more lenient one on his behalf. The protective concern may obligate redemption without necessarily mandating generous terms.

The objection is built on a teaching of Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Chanina: *bo u-re'eh kamah kashah avkah shel shevi'it* — come and see how consequential it is to be even negligent about the lighter dimensions of *shevi'it* observance. This teaching interprets the sequence of *pesukim* in *Behar* as a narrative of moral and economic decline, with each stage flowing from the previous one.

### Progressive Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם נוֹשֵׂא וְנוֹתֵן בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית, לְסוֹף מוֹכֵר אֶת מִטַּלְטְלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל הַזֹּאת תָּשׁוּבוּ אִישׁ אֶל אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְכִי תִמְכְּרוּ מִמְכָּר לַעֲמִיתֶךָ אוֹ קָנֹה מִיַּד עֲמִיתֶךָ״, דָּבָר הַנִּקְנֶה מִיָּד לְיָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, continues: If **a person has commercial dealings with produce** of the **Sabbatical** Year or of the Jubilee Year, **ultimately** he will become so poor that **he will** be compelled to **sell his movable property, as it is stated: "In this Jubilee Year you shall return every man to his possession"** (Leviticus 25:13), **and it is written** in the subsequent verse: **"And if you sell something to your neighbor or buy from your neighbor's hand,"** which is referring to **an item acquired** by passing it **from hand to hand.** The juxtaposition of the two verses indicates that if one violates the *halakhot* of the Jubilee Year or the Sabbatical Year, he will eventually have to sell his movable property.

The person being described is one who begins by dealing in the sale of *shevi'it* produce — *nosei ve-noten b'peirot shevi'it* — which is merely the *avkah*, the dust, of *shevi'it* violation, since the weightier prohibitions concern working the land. What follows is a cascade. First, *le-sof mokher et metalteleiv* — he winds up selling his *metaltelin*, his chattel. The verse *ve-khi timkeru mimkar l'amitekha o kano mi-yad amitekha* — if you sell or buy from your fellow — is read by *Chazal* as referring to *davar ha-nikneh mi-yad le-yad*, to movable goods. So the first stage of financial deterioration involves selling personal possessions. Then, *le-sof mokher sadotav* — he sells his fields, derived from the verse *ve-khi yamukh achikha u-makhar me-achuzato*. Then *le-sof mokher beito* — his house, from *ve-ish ki yimsor beit moshav ir chomah*.

### Terminology Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִרְגִּישׁ — לַסּוֹף מוֹכֵר אֶת שְׂדוֹתָיו...
>
> **Translation:** If **he does not feel** remorse and he does not repent, **ultimately he will** be compelled to **sell his fields, as it is stated** in an adjacent verse: **"If your brother becomes poor and sells part of his ancestral land"** (Leviticus 25:25).

Before the *gemara* permits the narrative to continue, it pauses to observe a terminological shift. At the first stage, the text says *lo hirgish* — he was not sensitive, he did not register the message being sent to him. At the subsequent stage, the expression changes to *lo ba'u le-yado* — it did not come to his consciousness. The *gemara* notes this as *kidei Rav Huna*, a deliberate allusion to a teaching of Rav Huna.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּאת לְיָדוֹ — לַסּוֹף מוֹכֵר אֶת בֵּיתוֹ... מַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּקָאָמַר ״לֹא הִרְגִּישׁ״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָאָמַר ״לֹא בָּאת לְיָדוֹ״? כִּדְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָבַר אָדָם עֲבֵירָה וְשָׁנָה בָּהּ, הוּתְּרָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If consciousness of his sins **does not come to him, ultimately he will** be compelled to **sell his house...** The Gemara asks: **What is different there,** in the previous clause, **where** the *tanna* **says** that the sinner **does not sense** remorse, **and what is different here, where he says** that consciousness of his sins **does not come to him?** The Gemara responds: This is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says: Once a person commits a transgression and repeats it, it is permitted to him.**

*De-amar Rav Huna: keivan she-avar adam aveirah ve-shanah bah, hutrah lo.* Once a person has sinned and repeated the sin, it becomes as if permitted to him. The *gemara* immediately clarifies: *hutrah lo salka da'atakh?* — you cannot mean it literally becomes permitted. *Ela eima na'asit lo ke-heter* — rather, it becomes as though permitted. Psychologically, the person no longer perceives that he is doing something wrong.

### Moral Message

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּתְּרָה לוֹ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ כְּהֶיתֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Can it **enter your mind** to say that **it is** actually **permitted to him** because he has transgressed twice? **Rather, say** that **it becomes as** though it were **permitted to him,** that is, after transgressing the prohibition twice he becomes accustomed to this behavior and no longer feels that it is a sin. If one transgresses the prohibition only once, he is apt to feel remorse. Once he repeats his transgression, he loses this sensibility concerning his sins and will no longer feel any remorse.

The first time he sins, the text says *lo hirgish* — he is not sensitive. He is aware on some level that something is wrong; he rationalizes, but there is still a residual awareness. After he continues sinning without *teshuvah*, the description becomes *lo ba'u le-yado* — it no longer even reaches his consciousness. He has become completely oblivious. At the same time, the divine response is escalating: each stage of loss is a louder wake-up call, and the person is becoming proportionally more deaf to it. The pattern is one of simultaneous intensification: increasing punishment and increasing moral numbness. This dynamic reflects Rabbi Yisrael Salanter's observation that a sin repeated once becomes like a *heter*, and repeated a third time takes on the quality of a *mitzvah* — one constructs ever more elaborate self-justifications until the wrongdoing seems not only acceptable but virtuous.

### Daughter & Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּאת לְיָדוֹ — לַסּוֹף שֶׁלֹּוֶה בְּרִבִּית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִי יָמוּךְ אָחִיךָ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״אַל תִּקַּח מֵאִתּוֹ נֶשֶׁךְ וְתַרְבִּית״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, continues: If consciousness of his sins **does not come to him, ultimately he** will be compelled to **borrow with interest, as it is stated: "And if your brother becomes poor** and his means fail with you, then you shall uphold him" (Leviticus 25:35), **and it is written** in the subsequent verse: **"You shall take no interest or increase from him."**

The narrative in *Behar* passes directly from selling one's house to selling oneself into servitude. The *gemara* interpolates that before reaching that ultimate degradation, a person would first sell his daughter — even though the selling of a daughter does not appear in *Parashat Behar*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּאת לְיָדוֹ — עַד שֶׁמּוֹכֵר אֶת בִּתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִי יִמְכּוֹר אִישׁ [אֶת] בִּתּוֹ לְאָמָה״, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּבִתּוֹ בְּהַאי עִנְיָנָא לֵיתַהּ, נִיחָא לְאִינִישׁ דְּלִיזַבֵּין בְּרַתֵּיה וְלָא לֵיזִיף בְּרִיבִּיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** One **does not come to** borrow with interest **until he** has already been compelled to **sell his daughter, as it is stated: "And when a man sells his daughter as a maidservant"** (Exodus 21:7). The Gemara explains: **And even though his daughter is not** mentioned **in that context** in Leviticus, nevertheless, **a person prefers to sell his daughter and not to borrow** money **with interest.**

*Ve-af al gav de-bito be-hai inyana leita* — although selling one's daughter is not mentioned in this context, the *gemara* reasons *nicha le-inish de-lizban barteh ve-lo lizaf be-ribita* — a person would prefer to sell his daughter rather than borrow at interest, because by the daughter the buy-back cost decreases over time, *de-ilu hatam migra*, whereas by borrowing at interest *kamosif ve-azil* — the debt only grows. The *gemara* thus places the sale of a daughter between the sale of the house and the borrowing at interest that the Torah mentions in the next passage (*al tikach me'ito neshech ve-tarbit*), and places borrowing at interest immediately before the selling of oneself.

### Final Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּאת לְיָדוֹ — לַסּוֹף שֶׁמּוֹכֵר אֶת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יָמוּךְ אָחִיךָ עִמָּךְ וְנִמְכַּר לָךְ״; לֹא לָךְ אֶלָּא לְגֵר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְגֵר״, וְלֹא לְגֵר צֶדֶק אֶלָּא לְגֵר תּוֹשָׁב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְגֵר תּוֹשָׁב״.
>
> **Translation:** If consciousness of his sins **does not come to him, ultimately he will** be compelled **to sell himself, as it is stated: "And when your brother becomes poor with you and sells himself to you"** (Leviticus 25:39). **Not** only will he be sold **to you,** a born Jew; **rather,** he will even be sold **to a stranger, as it is stated:** "And sells himself **to the stranger"** (Leviticus 25:47). **And not** only **to** a stranger who is **a convert, but** even **to a gentile who resides in Eretz Yisrael and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*], as it is stated:** "And sells himself **to a *ger toshav*** with you" (Leviticus 25:47).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִשְׁפַּחַת גֵּר״ — זֶה הַגּוֹי. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אוֹ לְעֵקֶר מִשְׁפַּחַת גֵּר״ — זֶה הַנִּמְכָּר וְנַעֲשֶׂה מְשָׁרֵת לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** When the verse further states: "Or to the offshoot of **a stranger's family," this is** referring to **the gentile** relatives of a *ger toshav*, who are idolaters. **When it says: "Or to the offshoot [*le'eker*] of a stranger's family," this is** referring to **one who is sold and becomes a servant to idol worship itself,** i.e., he is put to work in a temple dedicated to idolatry.

The degradation continues. If the person still remains oblivious, *le-sof she-mokher et atzmo* — he winds up selling himself into servitude, from *ki yamukh achikha imach ve-nimkar lach*. Even then, the descent is not complete. *Lo lecha ela le-ger*, not only to a Jew, but even to a *ger toshav*, a resident alien, *she-ne'emar le-ger*. *Ve-lo le-ger tzedek ela le-ger toshav*, not a full convert but a resident alien who is not an Israelite. And ultimately, *mishpachat ger* — the family of the sojourner — refers, as the *gemara* reads it, to *zeh ha-nimkar ve-na'aseh mesharet le-oved kochavim atzmo*: the person has fallen so far that he sells himself to serve in the household or temple of a pagan. This is the ultimate pit of degradation — a descent that began with something as seemingly minor as trading in *shevi'it* produce.

## Redemption Obligation

### Self-Inflicted Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אַהְדְּרֵיהּ קְרָא, וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: הוֹאִיל וְהָלַךְ וּמָכַר עַצְמוֹ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אֶידְחֶה אֶבֶן אַחֵר הַנּוֹפֵל?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** that Sage: **But the verse** subsequently **restores him,** i.e., it requires that one strive to redeem him from slavery. As the *tanna* of **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Since he went and sold himself to** a temple dedicated to **idol worship, should I throw a stone after the fallen?** In other words, perhaps he should be left to his own devices?

The *gemara* now returns to the question of the slave who has brought this fate upon himself. Given the entire preceding narrative — a man who violated *shevi'it*, ignored every divine warning, sold his children, borrowed at usury, and ultimately sold himself to a pagan — one might ask whether we are truly obligated to exert effort to redeem him. Perhaps *idchi even achar ha-nofel* — one should throw a rock on top of the person who has fallen. He engineered his own ruin; the logic of divine accountability for his circumstances suggests that his situation reflects what he deserves. This is not a casual theological claim; it resonates with a dangerous thread in certain religious traditions that equate prosperity with divine favor and suffering with divine punishment, thereby dissolving any obligation to assist those in distress. The *gemara* does not dismiss this position lightly.

### Torah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״גְּאוּלַּת עוֹלָם תִּהְיֶה לּוֹ וּבַיּוֹבֵל יֵצֵא״. אֵימָא: גְּאוּלָּה תִּהְיֶה לוֹ — דְּלָא לִיטַּמַּע בַּגּוֹיִם, וּלְעוֹלָם לְעִנְיַן פִּדְיוֹנוֹ נַחְמִיר!
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: He shall have a perpetual right of redemption, and he shall leave in the Jubilee** (see Leviticus 25:31, 48). The Gemara objects: Even so, you can **say** that **he shall have a redemption** so **that he will not be assimilated among the gentiles, but actually, with regard to his redemption we will be stringent,** as it is due only to his sins that he is enslaved.

The *gemara* answers from the verse itself: *ge'ulah tihyeh lo, echad me-echav yigalenu* — redemption shall be available to him; one of his brothers shall redeem him. Even this man, even one who sold himself to serve a pagan temple, is encompassed by the Torah's obligation of redemption. *Amar lei astaye'a de-kra* — the text itself speaks to this, precluding the option of simply abandoning him. The Torah's obligation of *geulah* is unconditional.

### Strict Computation

The *gemara* does not fully concede, however. *Eima ge'ulah tihyeh lo she-lo yitamei be-avodas kochavim* — fine, we must redeem him so that he does not become wholly absorbed among the pagans. That obligation is accepted. *U-le-olam le-inyan pidyono nachmir bo* — but when it comes to computing the actual redemption amount, perhaps we should be strict with him.

### Rav Nachman Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כְּתִיב ״אִם עוֹד רַבּוֹת בַּשָּׁנִים״, וּכְתִיב ״אִם מְעַט נִשְׁאַר בַּשָּׁנִים״. וְכִי יֵשׁ שָׁנִים מְרוּבּוֹת וְיֵשׁ שָׁנִים מוּעָטוֹת? אֶלָּא: נִתְרַבָּה כַּסְפּוֹ — ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״, נִתְמַעֵט כַּסְפּוֹ — ״כְּפִי שָׁנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** Two verses are written with regard to redeeming someone who was sold to a gentile. **It is written: "If there are yet many in the years,** according to them he shall return the price of his redemption from the money that he was bought for" (Leviticus 25:51). **And it is written** in the next verse: "And **if there remain but few in the years** until the Jubilee Year, then he shall reckon with him; according to his years he shall return the price of his redemption." Now, **are there years with much** time **and years with little** time? Every year is the same length. **Rather,** this means that if **his monetary** value **appreciated** during the years of his service, he is redeemed according to **"the money that he was bought for,"** which is the lower sum. And if **his monetary** value **depreciated** over time, one determines his value **"according to his years,"** i.e., according to his current value rather than according to his previous worth.

This is where Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak intervenes with a brilliant close reading of the *pesukim* themselves. The verse regarding redemption of a slave reads: *im od rabot ba-shanim* — if there are still many of the years — *kefi hen yashiv ge'ulato mikesef miknato*, he shall return his redemption from the money of his purchase price. And: *ve-im me'at nish'ar ba-shanim* — if there are few years remaining — *kefi shanav*, according to his years. Rav Nachman poses the question: *ve-chi yesh shanim merubbot ve-yesh shanim mu'atot?* — are some years inherently larger and some smaller? The phrase "many of the years" and "few of the years" cannot refer simply to counting years remaining, since the same verse is already prescribing computation based on remaining years. There must be another meaning.

Rav Nachman resolves this by reading the words *rabot* and *mu'at* as referring not to the number of years but to the monetary value of the years themselves. *Nitraba kaspo* — if the value has increased, the years are now *rabot* — larger, more valuable — *mikesef miknato*: the verse ties this to the original purchase price, meaning you compute the redemption based on the original (now lower) price. *Nitma'at kaspo* — if the value has diminished, the years are *mu'at* — smaller — *kefi shanav*: the verse directs us to the remaining years at their current value. In other words, the very language of the verse encodes the rule: appreciation in value directs you to the original purchase price; depreciation in value directs you to the current market rate. In either case, the result favors the slave. This is not merely a moral argument — it is derived directly from the syntax of the *pesukim*.

### Alternative Reading Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: הֵיכָא דַּעֲבַד תַּרְתֵּי וּפָיְישִׁי אַרְבְּעֵי — לִיתֵּב לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״, וְהֵיכָא דַּעֲבַד אַרְבַּע וּפָיְישִׁי תַּרְתֵּי — לִיתֵּב לֵיהּ תַּרְתֵּי ״כְּפִי שָׁנָיו״! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב ״אִם עוֹד רַבּוֹת שָׁנִים״, מַאי ״בַּשָּׁנִים״? נִתְרַבָּה כַּסְפּוֹ בַּשָּׁנִים — מִ״כֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״, נִתְמַעֵט כַּסְפּוֹ בַּשָּׁנִים — ״כְּפִי שָׁנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But say** that the verses should be interpreted as follows: "If there are yet many in the years," is referring to a case **where he served** for **two** years, **and four** years of servitude **remain.** In this situation **let him return to** his master the value of **four** years of his servitude according to **"the money that he was bought for." And** the phrase "if there remain but few in the years" is referring to a case **where he served** for **four** years **and two** years of servitude **remain.** In this situation **let him return to** his master the value of **two** years of his servitude **"according to his years."** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If so, let** the verse **write: If there are yet many years. What** is the meaning of the phrase **"in the years"?** Rather, this indicates that if **his monetary** value **appreciated in the years** of his service, he is redeemed according to **"the money that he was bought for,"** which is the lower sum. If **his monetary** value **depreciated in the years,** one determines his value **"according to his years,"** i.e., according to his current value.

The *gemara* considers one final alternative reading: perhaps *rabot* and *mu'at* refer to whether more years of service have been completed or remain. If two years have been served and four remain, use the original price; if four have been served and two remain, use the current value. The *gemara* rejects this: *im ken nichtov im od rabot shanim* — had that been the intent, the verse would simply say *shanim rabot*, many years; the unusual syntax *rabot ba-shanim*, with the multiplicity inhering *in* the years themselves, implies that the years themselves are qualitatively larger or smaller — meaning that their monetary value has risen or fallen. The derivation holds.

### Sinai Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: פָּשִׁינוּ רַב נַחְמָן לְהָנֵי קְרָאֵי כְּסִינַי.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this explanation, **Rav Yosef said: Rav Naḥman** bar Yitzḥak **interpreted these verses as** truly as if the interpretation had been transmitted to Moses from **Sinai.**

With this reading in place, the *gemara* closes with a remarkable tribute. *Amar Rav Yosef: pashinu Rav Nachman l'hanei kra'i k'Sinai* — Rav Yosef declares that Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak interpreted those verses as if from *Har Sinai*. This is a fitting coda, and particularly notable coming from Rav Yosef. In the famous dispute at the end of *Kiddushin*, Rav Yosef himself champions the position that *Sinai adif* — that comprehensive mastery of received tradition is superior to sharp analytical reasoning (*oker harim*). Here, Rav Yosef praises Rav Nachman's interpretive analysis — the product of precise, rigorous *peshat* — as achieving the equivalence of revealed tradition. The unspoken implication is that while Rav Yosef's general preference is for *masorah* over analytical derivation, this particular instance of close reading was so apt, so precise, so illuminating, that it merits being placed in the same category as what was received at *Sinai* itself.
