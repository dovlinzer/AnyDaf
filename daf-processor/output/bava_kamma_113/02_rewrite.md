# Bava Kamma 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Judgment & Testimony

### Prior Context

The daf opens in the middle of 112b, where the *gemara* had been engaged with a cluster of related questions arising from the case of someone who died leaving stolen property (*gezeila*) in the possession of his children. The discussion addressed whether those children are adults (*gedolim*) or minors (*katanim*), and whether a judgment can be rendered against minors when it is as if the opposing party (*baal din*) is absent. From there the *gemara* launched into a broader treatment of *mekabelin eidus shelo bifnei baal din* — accepting testimony in the absence of the defendant — and the range of circumstances under which this is permitted: urgency because witnesses are about to depart or fall ill, or because the defendant has been summoned and refuses to appear.

*Tosafos* raises an important question about whether these two conditions — the urgency and the refusal — must both be present simultaneously, or whether either one alone is sufficient. In certain passages the *gemara* seems to indicate that a refusal to appear is independently sufficient to proceed with the case, while other passages suggest both conditions are required. This remains a significant unresolved tension in *Tosafos*.

### Document Validation

*Rava* rules: *hilchasa mekayemin lei lishtar shelo bifnei baal din* — the *halacha* is that we may perform *kiyum hashtar*, validating the signatures on a document, even in the defendant's absence. The rationale is that the requirement to verify signatures is itself only rabbinic in origin, and therefore we may proceed even without the defendant present.

Moreover, *ve'afilu omed vetzoveach* — even if the defendant is actively protesting that the document is a forgery, we proceed with *kiyum hashtar* unless he brings evidence to support his claim. If he says, "Give me time and I will produce witnesses to invalidate the document," we give him that time. If he ultimately appears and succeeds, well and good. If not, we wait a *beis-heh-beis* — the court sitting schedule of Monday, Thursday, and Monday — and if he has still not appeared, we proceed with the case despite his forgery claim, and *vechasvinan pesicha ilaveih*: we write a writ against him. *Rashi* explains this as a *shamta*, a form of *cherem*, because he has not paid his debt.

## 90-Day Collection

### Days 1–30

After a judgment has been rendered and this writ of *cherem* is in place, the *gemara* establishes a ninety-day period before actually seizing the debtor's property. The first thirty days we do not descend upon his assets — *de'amar katarach bezuzai* — because we want to give him the opportunity to borrow the money. It is preferable to allow him to raise the funds himself rather than forcing us to seize his property.

### Days 31–60

The middle thirty days we also refrain from seizing property, even though the time to borrow has passed. The reasoning is *de'amar dilma lo ashkach lemiyzif vekatarach lezabin*: perhaps he was unable to find someone to lend him money, and we therefore give him the opportunity to sell his own property at a fair price. Forced seizure would yield him a far worse outcome than a voluntary sale.

### Days 61–90

The final thirty days we likewise wait, because even if he has found a buyer, the buyer himself may need time to raise or borrow the funds to complete the purchase. We extend this accommodation so that the debtor can achieve the best possible return and suffer the minimum possible loss.

### After 90 Days

If after all ninety days have elapsed and he has not paid, *chasvinan adrachta anichsei* — we write a document authorizing seizure of his property and proceed to collect. This entire ninety-day accommodation applies only when the debtor says *maysina* — "I am trying to produce the money." If he says *lo maysina* — he rejects the proceedings entirely — then we do not wait the *beis-heh-beis* before issuing the writ against him. He may still receive the ninety days to collect, but the preliminary *bahab* waiting period is skipped.

A further distinction: all of this applies to a loan (*milva*), where the debtor had a right to spend the money and must now reassemble it. For a deposit (*pikadon*), we do not extend the same allowance — *l'altar kasvinan* — we proceed immediately, since the debtor had no right to consume what was entrusted to him.

### Movables & Forgery

A further nuance concerns the type of property that may be seized. The *gemara* initially reasons that we should seize *mekarkei* — real property — but not *metaltelin* — movable goods. The concern is *dilma shamit v'achal lehu malve l'metalteli*: the creditor, once given the movable property, will sell or consume it, and then *v'chi atei loveh u'meiti sahadi u'marea lei lishtar* — if the debtor ultimately produces witnesses proving the document was a forgery, *lo mishkach midi l'migba* — he will have nothing to recover from the creditor. The *gemara* wants to preserve the possibility of reversal, which is important precisely because the debtor is claiming the document is forged and has not yet been proven wrong.

The *gemara* initially suggests an exception: if the creditor himself owns *karka*, we can seize *metaltelin*, since the debtor could always recover from the creditor's real estate. But the *gemara* rejects even this: *af al gav d'islei karka chashinan shema techsif* — we are concerned that the creditor's real property might decline in value. Therefore, in all circumstances where the debtor is claiming forgery, we limit seizure to *karka* alone, so that if necessary the very same land can be returned. That this claim alone — the mere assertion of forgery, without yet substantiating it — is sufficient to keep this door open is, on reflection, quite striking.

### Writing Writs

When we do write an *adrachta* — the document authorizing seizure of property — we are required to inform the debtor. *Moda'inan leh*: we notify him that such a document has been issued against him and that he should be aware of the impending action.

### Informing Defendant

This obligation to notify, however, depends on the defendant's proximity. If he is nearby, we notify him before acting. If he is far away, we do not delay the writing of the *adrachta* until he can be reached. However, if he is distant but has relatives nearby, or if there are caravans that travel between here and where he lives, *mashinan leh srei asar yarchei shata* — we wait up to a full year for the caravan to travel there and back, to ensure he has actually received word before we seize his property.

The *gemara* cites the case of Ravina, who waited a full year for Mar Acha to receive notice via the caravans from Bei Chuza'ei. However, the *gemara* qualifies this: *lo hi* — it does not mean this is the standard rule. In that particular case, Mar Acha was a powerful individual (*inish alima*), and had the *adrachta* reached his hands, *lo hava efshar le'afokei mineih* — it would have been impossible to recover the property from him. Therefore extra caution was taken. In ordinary circumstances, once the *adrachta* is ready, the court's messenger sets out on Tuesday, arrives at his destination on Wednesday, and the defendant has until Thursday to appear in court. This presupposes, of course, that the defendant is close enough to be reached within that window — which raises the puzzling question of why the notification does not come before the ninety-day period begins rather than at the moment of seizure.

## Messenger's Role

### Messenger's Word

*Ravina* rules: *hai shluacha deRabbanan meheman leh kebei srei* — a court-appointed messenger (*shliach beis din*) is believed like two witnesses. If the court sends a messenger to summon a defendant and he returns reporting that the defendant refuses to appear, we accept that report without requiring corroborating evidence. On that basis, proceedings against the defendant begin — we place him under *shamta* and issue the relevant writs.

The *gemara* here seems to reflect two distinct orientations: in some passages the court appears to operate from a position of strength, pressing forward with proceedings and giving time as an accommodation. In other passages, the court seems to be bending over backwards to maintain its jurisdiction in the face of a defendant who cannot be compelled — issuing multiple summonses, waiting through extended periods, and requiring extraordinary care before acting. Whether these represent genuinely different situations or reflect a tension between formal authority and practical power is worth considering.

### Written Writ

A distinction is drawn, however, between the oral report of the messenger and the formal issuance of the *pesicha* — the written writ of *cherem*. The *pesicha* carries financial consequences: *de-ka ba'ei lei le-meitav lei zuzei le-safra* — when the *cherem* is eventually lifted, the defendant will be required to pay the scribal costs of having it drawn up. Because this imposes a monetary penalty, the court requires more than the messenger's word alone before issuing it. Supporting evidence of the refusal is needed before we write the formal document.

### Messenger Conditions

*Avina* rules: *yehavinan zimna apuma de-itata ve-apuma de-shivevei* — we may issue a summons through a woman or through neighbors, not only through official court officers. If *Reuven* is away and one informs his neighbor or his wife to relay the summons, we assume they will do so, and if *Reuven* fails to appear on the appointed day, we treat him as in contempt.

However, this assumption applies only under narrow conditions. If *Reuven* is in the city at the time of the summons, we do not rely on neighbors or family, because *de-amrinan ashtechach shlicha de-bei dina ve-amar lei* — we say that the neighbors will assume the court's own officers found him and told him directly, and will therefore not take the responsibility seriously themselves. Similarly, even if *Reuven* is out of town, if he regularly passes by the courthouse when returning, the neighbors will again assume the court told him. And even if neither of those applies, the assumption that the neighbors informed him holds only if he was expected to return that same day — if he was not coming back that day, *eimar ishtaluyei ishtalu*, we say they simply forgot to tell him.

All of this narrowing of conditions serves *Reuven*'s interest: we want to be genuinely confident he received the summons before beginning proceedings against him in absentia.

*Rava* rules further: once a *pesicha* has been written against someone for failure to appear, we do not tear it up — even if he says he is coming — until he actually shows up. And once a writ has been issued against someone for failure to comply with a judgment, we do not tear it up even if he says he will pay — until he actually pays. Or so it first seems. The *gemara* then reverses: *velo hi* — once he says *tzyasna*, "I will comply," we do tear it up. *Rashi* explains the distinction: if someone says "I'm coming to court," he can come immediately, so we hold out for actual appearance. But if someone says "I will pay," there may be legitimate reasons for delay — he needs time to raise the money — and his declaration of willingness to comply is itself meaningful. The *gemara*'s earlier discussion of the ninety-day period already acknowledged that even a willing debtor may need extended time to gather funds.

## Summons Timing

### Mon-Thu-Mon

*Rav Chisda* rules: *kosvin zman besheini uvechamishi uvesheini* — we schedule summonses for Monday, Thursday, and the following Monday, since those are the days when *beis din* convenes. Only after the defendant has failed to appear across this full sequence — Monday, Thursday, Monday — do we write a *shamta*, a writ of contempt, against him.

### Women vs. Men

*Rav Assi* visited the *beis din* of *Rav Kahana* and observed a case in which a woman was summoned on one afternoon and by the following morning *Rav Kahana* had already written a *pesicha* against her. *Rav Assi* challenged him: does the master not hold by *Rav Chisda*'s rule that we give a full Monday-Thursday-Monday sequence before writing the writ of contempt?

*Rav Kahana* replied: *hani mili gavra de-anis* — that rule applies to a man, who may be traveling, occupied with his livelihood, or otherwise unavailable. But a woman, *keivan de-isa be-mata* — who is presumed to be at home in the city — if she has not appeared, *moredes hi*, she is evidently refusing. The *gemara* thus establishes a procedurally different standard for women on the grounds of presumed availability.

This is, admittedly, a striking ruling that reflects certain assumptions about the social roles of men and women. What makes it interesting beyond the obvious is that the *gemara* elsewhere holds precisely the opposite assumption when it comes to *tefilla*: women are exempt from time-bound positive commandments, often explained on the grounds that they are occupied with domestic responsibilities. There, the *gemara* seems to presume women are busier than men; here, it presumes they are more available. The tension is real, and worth noting.

What the ruling does illustrate clearly is that *Rav Chisda*'s rule is not a rigid formal requirement but a contextual one — its application depends on the actual circumstances of the defendant's likely availability.

### Exempted Times

*Rav Yehuda* rules in the name of *Rav*: *lo yahvinan zimna lo be-yomei Nisan ve-lo be-yomei Tishrei* — we do not issue summonses during the harvest season of Nisan or the planting season of Tishrei. People have pressing agricultural obligations during those periods, and it would be unjust to summon them to court when they cannot reasonably attend. However, one may send a summons *during* those seasons requiring the defendant to appear *after* the season ends — during Nisan, informing him to come after Nisan; during Tishrei, to come after Tishrei.

Similarly, *mi-malei Shabbos le-basar malei Shabbos lo havinan* — we do not send a summons on Friday requiring someone to appear after *Shabbos*. The reason is *mi-avadta de-Shabbos tridei* — on Fridays people are absorbed in Shabbos preparations and will not be properly attentive to a court summons.

### Religious Gatherings

*Rav Nachman* rules: *lo yahvina zimna lo livnei kala be-kala ve-lo livnei rigla be-rigla* — we do not issue summonses to those attending the *kala* (the regular Shabbos *drasha*, according to *Rashi*) while they are there for it, nor to those gathered for the period of Torah study before the *regel* while they are so gathered. Using a public assembly as an opportunity to serve legal papers on attendees would be both improper and destructive to future attendance.

When litigants would approach *Rav Nachman* during such gatherings and ask him to use the opportunity to serve process on those present, he would rebuke them: *b'cheila d'dachki knuphaichu* — do you think I assembled this crowd for your purposes? The crowd was gathered to learn Torah.

The *gemara* appends, however: *v'idna d'ika ramai chashinan* — nowadays, since there are people who are skilled at evading process, and a public gathering may be the only opportunity to find them, we do make use of the occasion. The practical tension between respect for the learning assembly and the needs of the legal system is thus acknowledged.

## Minors & Father's Honor

### Public Property

Returning to the *mishnah*, the principle that the children of a deceased thief must return stolen property is clarified. *Rebbe* taught his son *Rabbi Shimon*: the *mishnah*'s phrase "something that has *achrayus*" does not literally mean land. Rather, it refers to anything that is visibly and publicly in use — even a cow being plowed with, or a donkey being led — *chayavin l'hachzir mipnei kevod avihem* — they must return it out of respect for their father's honor. Even if technically the children may have acquired it through *yeush v'shinui reshus*, if the stolen nature of the object is obvious and public, they are obligated to return it.

### Private Items

*Rav Kahana* posed a question to *Rav*: what about items that are used regularly but in private, indoors, such as a bed one reclines on (*mita u'mesaiv aleha*) or a table one eats off of (*shulchan v'ochel alav*)? These items are seen by guests who enter the home, but they are not publicly observable in the same way a working animal is. *Rav* replied: *ten l'chacham v'yichkam od* — give it to a wise person and he will become wiser, meaning a learned person ought to be able to work it out for himself. *Rashi* understands this to mean that the same principle applies — such items must also be returned — though the answer is left implicit.

## Tax Collectors

### Stolen Funds

The *mishnah* rules: *ein pordin lo mi-teivas ha-mochsin v'lo mi-kis shel gabbai* — you may not exchange coins from the money box of a *moches* (tax collector) or from the pouch of a *gabbai* (a different type of collector). This ruling is rooted in the institution of tax farming in the Roman world, by which the government sold the right to collect taxes to private individuals, who then bore the costs and kept the revenues. The practical result was routine abuse: collectors would routinely extract more than they were legally entitled to. The general perception — shared widely in the ancient world, as reflected in the New Testament's association of tax collectors with social outcasts — was that their accumulated funds were essentially stolen money.

Since the money in the tax collector's box was presumed to derive largely from excess, unlawful collection, it was treated as stolen property. Changing your money with the tax collector would mean receiving funds that belong to whoever overpaid their taxes.

### Exceptions

The *mishnah* provides an important limitation: *aval notel hu mi-toch beito o min ha-shuk* — you may take money from the tax collector at his home or in the marketplace, where there is no reason to assume that whatever he happens to have on hand is stolen. The stolen-money presumption applies specifically to the designated collection pouch or box. The *gemara* adds further: *tanna aval nosin lo dinar ve-nosin lo es ha-sh'ar* — if your tax obligation is ten and you pay twenty, you may take ten in change even if the change comes from the pouch. *Rashi* understands this as *matzil mi-yadam* — one need not forfeit one's own money to avoid this concern.

### Tosafot's View

*Tosafos* analyzes the problem at two levels. First, according to the Rabbis who hold that ordinary stolen property is not subject to *ye'ush ha-ba'alim* — the owners have not given up hope — the prohibition is straightforward: the money genuinely still belongs to the original taxpayers. As for why one may still take change from the pouch, *Tosafos* explains that since not all the money in the pouch is necessarily stolen — some portion was legitimately collected — one may be lenient in the narrow case of recovering one's own money.

Second, and more fundamentally, *Tosafos* argues that even if we assume *ye'ush* and even *shinui reshus*, making the money technically transferable, it remains *davar meguneh* — unseemly and inappropriate — to take money from a receptacle that is effectively designated as a repository for stolen goods: *de-teiva ve-kis miyuchedes le-hatznia sham gezelos*. Technically permissible transfer is one thing; voluntarily engaging with what is universally understood to be stolen property is another.

This raises naturally the broader question — which *Tosafos* does not fully resolve and which remains practically sensitive — of whether one may accept *tzedakah* donations or institutional gifts from individuals whose wealth derives from ill-gotten gains. The *mishnah* itself narrows its prohibition to direct engagement with the designated collection pouch: money obtained by the tax collector through other means, and held elsewhere, is not subject to the same concern. The practical upshot seems to be that while direct engagement with the stolen cache itself is prohibited, more attenuated connection — buying from his profits, accepting donations from his household — falls outside the prohibition.

## Dina Demalchuta

### Exceeding Authority

The broader discussion of *dina demalchuta dina* — the rule that the law of the kingdom is legally binding — is prompted by an apparent contradiction. *Shmuel* famously rules *dina demalchuta dina*. If the government authorizes tax collection, how can those tax collectors' funds be considered stolen?

*Rav Chanina bar Kahana* answers in the name of *Shmuel*: the ruling applies to a *moches she'ein lo kitzvah* — a tax collector who operates without fixed parameters, collecting whatever he likes beyond what the law permits. The school of *Rabbi Yannai* gives a second answer: *moches ha'omed mei'elav* — a self-appointed collector with no governmental authorization at all, essentially a local criminal operation.

The *gemara* traces the same two answers through three Tannaitic sources that all assume tax collectors are effectively *gazlanim*: the *mishnah* prohibiting exchange of money with tax collectors; the ruling in *Kilayim* that one may not wear garments of *kilayim* even on top of ten garments in order to avoid paying import duties; and the ruling in the *mishnah* about *nedarim* — that one may take a vow before robbers (*hargin*), extortionists (*charamin*), and tax collectors (*mochsin*) claiming one's goods are *terumah* or belong to the royal household, even though they are not, and this does not constitute a prohibited false *neder* because the compelled context is understood by all parties, and one may in any case mentally qualify the vow.

In each case the *gemara* presses the same question: how does *Shmuel*'s *dina demalchuta dina* accommodate these Tannaitic rulings that treat tax collection as illegitimate? And in each case the answer is the same: we are speaking of collectors who exceed their authority or lack it altogether.

A historically significant observation emerges here. All three sources treating tax collectors as *gazlanim* are Tannaitic, and all emerge from the context of the Jews of *Eretz Yisrael* living under the oppressive Roman imperial system — a system in which tax collection was a byword for extortion, and in which the entire government apparatus was perceived as fundamentally hostile to Jewish interests. *Shmuel*, by contrast, is an *Amora* from *Bavel*, who had a close relationship with *Shvor Malka*, the head of the Persian government. The Babylonian government was far more hospitable to the Jewish community, and its legal system was perceived as substantially more just. The *Yerushalmi* never articulates *dina demalchuta dina* — it is a purely Babylonian, purely *Shmuel*-ian formulation. The two traditions are thus operating from very different assumptions about the legitimacy of the surrounding political order.

The analogy to *Rav Moshe Feinstein*'s famous *teshuva* on this cluster of questions is instructive. *Rav Moshe* begins by stating that of course no legitimate *yeshiva* would even be entertaining questions about cheating on the Regents examinations or misrepresenting oneself to obtain government subsidies — but to address the underlying principle: we live in a *medina shel chessed*, a government genuinely concerned with the welfare of its citizens, which is categorically different from Czarist Russia. The mentality appropriate to an oppressive regime that was itself stealing from its Jewish subjects cannot be exported wholesale into a context in which the government protects and serves its citizens. Whether or not one accepts this framing in its entirety, it captures precisely what distinguishes *Shmuel*'s *dina demalchuta dina* from the Tannaitic baseline.

*Tosafos* applies the principle about permissible vows to a case from his own time: in the Middle Ages, rulers would prohibit Jews from relocating to other regions and would compel them to take oaths not to leave. Such oaths constrained Jews' economic mobility and their ability to earn a *parnasa*. *Tosafos* rules that one may take such an oath while mentally qualifying it — *hayom* ("today"), for instance — without articulating the condition aloud, and this does not constitute a false *neder*. Even if the oath is formulated as binding for life, one may privately stipulate any condition in the mind. If the qualification was whispered under one's breath, *Tosafos* says it is *kol sheken* — all the more certainly valid.

The concern about *chilul Hashem* that might arise if this practice became known to the authorities was, by at least some accounts, understood by both sides — the government official administering the oath and the Jewish subject taking it both grasped the political theater involved. And yet the *gemara*'s warning about *chilul Hashem* remains operative: if the authorities genuinely discover that Jews are routinely circumventing their oaths, a serious *chilul Hashem* ensues. This was the social reality within which these halakhic maneuvers were practiced.

### Property vs. Conduct

*Rav Ashi* offers a third answer to the question of how tax collection can be considered *gezel* despite *dina demalchuta dina*: *bemochas goy* — when the tax collector is a non-Jew. (The text as it appears in our printed *gemaras* reads *bemochas Kena'ani*, using the term "Canaanite" — a product of medieval censorship. The original reading was *goy* or *nochri*, and that is what appears in the *rishonim* and manuscripts. The practice of substituting *akum* or *Kena'ani* for *goy* was a form of Jewish self-censorship designed to deflect church scrutiny of passages that appeared to speak disparagingly of non-Jews or to exempt Jews from certain obligations toward them.)

*Rav Ashi*'s answer, properly understood, implies something significant about the scope of *dina demalchuta dina*. One might ask: even if one is permitted under Jewish law to withhold payment from a non-Jewish creditor, surely the government itself has mandated payment to this tax collector — so why doesn't *dina demalchuta dina* require compliance? The answer that seems to emerge from a careful reading is that *dina demalchuta dina* governs *property rights* — the government has the authority to determine who owns what, to impose liens, to establish that a given debt is owed. What it does not do is impose a *halachic* obligation on the Jew to actively comply with government directives. The government can define that the money is owed; whether the Jew is obligated to pay it is a separate *halachic* question governed by the *Torah*.

This is a consequential distinction. *Dina demalchuta dina* operates analogously to *hefker beis din hefker* — just as a *beis din* can determine property rights and effect transfers, so can the government. They can say that the tax farmer owns the tax revenue and that you owe it to him. But they cannot, through their legal mandate alone, impose on the Jew the *halachic* duty to perform an act of payment. And so when the government seizes property — cutting down palm trees to build bridges, for instance — that seizure is legitimate because the government has the authority to determine property rights, and what they take is considered rightfully theirs. We therefore use those bridges without compunction. But when the government instructs the Jew to pay, that instruction creates no independent *halachic* obligation; its force derives from whatever *halachic* obligation exists toward non-Jews independently.

### Seized Property

The proof that *dina demalchuta dina* genuinely confers legitimate property rights — not merely that owners become *mityaiesh* — is derived from *Rava*'s observation: *d'katlei diklei ve'gashri gishrei ve'avrinan aleihu* — the government cuts down people's palm trees and builds bridges with them, and we use those bridges. If we considered the seized property to be *gezel*, we would have no right to benefit from those bridges. The fact that we do use them demonstrates that the government's seizure is not *gezel* but a legitimate exercise of its authority to determine property ownership.

*Abaye* challenges: perhaps we use the bridges simply because the owners are *mityaiesh* — they have given up hope of recovering their trees — and *ye'ush* alone suffices? *Rava* responds: *i lav dina demalchuta dina, heichi miyash?* — if *dina demalchuta dina* were not operative, what kind of *ye'ush* would this even be? *Rashi* clarifies: *ye'ush* alone is not sufficient; one needs *ye'ush ve'shinui reshus*, and in this case there is no *shinui reshus* — the bridges are in a public domain, the palm trees are unchanged in identity, and there has been no real change of domain or form. The legitimacy of our using the bridges must therefore rest on *dina demalchuta dina* itself.

### Tax Discretion

The *gemara* then addresses a complication: do tax collectors actually follow the king's directives? It notes that the king may instruct them to take a proportional amount from every copse (*baga*) of palm trees in the region, but the collectors — being lazy — instead take everything from one patch. Have they exceeded their authority and thus stepped outside *dina demalchuta*?

The *gemara* rules: *shlucha d'malka k'malka ve'lo tarach* — an agent of the king is like the king himself and has discretion not to shoulder the administrative burden of equal distribution. Moreover, *v'inhu afsidu anafshaihu* — the villagers bear some responsibility for the outcome themselves. They had the option to pool their resources, gather the appropriate total, and present it to the collector, who would then distribute the burden fairly among them. If they failed to organize themselves in this way, knowing how collectors operate, they cannot fully blame the collector for the result. The collector is thus acting within the parameters of his authorized discretion, and the seizure remains within *dina demalchuta*.

A related ruling from *Rava*: if a man is found at the threshing floor when the tax collector arrives, and the collector seizes from him alone the full tax owed across all the grain stored there — effectively making him pay other people's debts — that too is within the tax collector's discretion (*para manta d'malka*). The others whose grain was present must then reimburse him, but the collector's act is valid. This applies, however, only if the person at the threshing floor is a partner in the grain. If he is merely a sharecropper (*arisa*), who has no ownership stake in the grain, it would not be within the collector's authority to impose the full tax obligation upon him.

Finally, the *gemara* addresses the question of current versus back taxes. A tax collector has discretion to seize property from one person temporarily to pressure another person to pay — even using an uninvolved party's assets as leverage. This applies to current-year obligations (*dubla d'bar l'ara d'charga d'hai shata*). But for back taxes (*shata d'chalif*), the discretion is reduced: *ho'il u'fayeis malka* — since the king was already appeased with whatever was collected for the prior year, and that matter is settled, the collector's discretionary powers to apply unusual pressure are correspondingly diminished.

## Gezel Goy (IV)

### Judging Mixed Cases

A *braita* addresses the case of a Jew and a non-Jew coming to *beis din* for judgment. *Rabbi Yishmael* rules: if you can make the Jew win by applying Jewish law, apply Jewish law and tell the non-Jew "this is our law." If you can make the Jew win by applying non-Jewish law, apply non-Jewish law and tell him "this is your law." And if neither system produces a ruling in the Jew's favor, *ba'in elav be'akifin* — find some indirect device to ensure the Jew wins.

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees with the third option: *ein ba'in elav be'akifin mipnei kiddush Hashem* — you may not use deceptive devices, because if discovered, the result will be *chilul Hashem* rather than *kiddush Hashem*.

The *gemara* notes the implication of *Rabbi Akiva*'s reasoning: his hesitation is *solely* the concern for *kiddush Hashem*. Where that concern would not be present — *heicha de-leka kiddush Hashem* — he would presumably permit the use of such devices. This implies that there is no independent *gezel goy* problem with the underlying result, since the Jewish party may in fact genuinely owe the money.

### Stealing vs. Debt

The *gemara* immediately presses back: can one really say that stealing from a non-Jew is permitted? A *braita* records that *Rabbi Akiva* himself expounded, upon returning from Zafirin: *minayin le-gezel goy she-hu assur* — from where do we know that stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden? From the verse *acharei nimkar geulah tihyeh lo* — after a Jewish slave is sold, he shall have a right of redemption. The verse requires that we calculate the proper redemption price and pay it honestly to the non-Jewish master, even though there is a *mitzvah* to redeem the Jewish slave. We may not simply take him or deceive the master; we must buy his freedom at fair value. This demonstrates that *gezel goy* is forbidden.

*Rav Yosef* attempts to resolve the apparent contradiction within *Rabbi Akiva*'s positions by distinguishing between ordinary non-Jews and a *ger toshav* — a non-Jew who observes the seven Noahide laws and dwells among the Jewish community. He suggests that stealing from a *ger toshav* is forbidden, while from an ordinary non-Jew it may be permitted. *Abaye* objects: the very verse about redeeming the Jewish slave speaks about both categories together, making it impossible to confine the prohibition to *ger toshav* alone.

*Rava* therefore offers the resolution that becomes the definitive *halacha*: *lo kashya — kan be-gezel kan be-hafkakat halvato* — there is no contradiction. Direct theft (*gezel*) from a non-Jew is absolutely forbidden. But passive non-payment of a debt (*hafkakat halvato*) — failing to repay money owed — is permitted when the creditor is a non-Jew. Maneuvering in court to avoid paying a non-Jewish creditor what the Jew genuinely owes him falls into the second category, not the first; that is why *Rabbi Akiva* could countenance such a result in principle. The actual taking of the Jewish slave would, by contrast, be active *gezel*, since *Rava* holds *eved ivri gufo kanuy* — the owner has a form of property right in the person of the Jewish slave — and removing him forcibly would be directly stealing something in which the non-Jew has property rights.

This distinction — *gezel goy assur*, *hafkakat halvato mutar* — is the *halacha*. Its combination with the earlier discussion of *dina demalchuta dina* gives rise to conclusions that are, to put it plainly, ethically challenging in the context of modern civil obligations such as paying taxes. The *gemara*'s own margins, as printed, contain glosses that immediately attempt to contextualize and limit the application of these principles. One gloss explains that the rulings applied to pagans who treated others' property as *hefker*; another invokes the standard of one who is *kamocha* — like unto you — arguing that non-Jews who maintain just legal systems and respect property rights have the status of *ger toshav* and are not subject to these distinctions. These glosses reflect both the moral discomfort felt by later authorities and, at times, concerns about how the text would appear to non-Jewish readers.

There are serious *poskim* throughout the generations — not only contemporary ones — who have argued that in a society of law-abiding citizens, where property rights are respected and enforced, the general presumption should be that non-Jews have the status of *ger toshav* for purposes of monetary *halachot*, and these distinctions accordingly do not apply. *Rav Moshe Feinstein*'s position, referenced earlier, operates on this basic premise. What remains clearly true, and genuinely troubling, is that these passages have at times been invoked to justify dishonest monetary conduct toward non-Jews — precisely the abuse the *gemara*'s own emphasis on *chilul Hashem* was meant to foreclose.

## Dina Demalchuta (II)

### Current vs. Back

This section, which belongs to the *dina demalchuta* discussion, has been addressed above in its proper sequence within the "Dina Demalchuta" segment. The distinction between current-year taxes — where the collector retains broad discretion — and prior-year back taxes — where that discretion is reduced because *ho'il u'fayeis malka*, the king was already satisfied with what was collected — is the central ruling. For back taxes, while collection remains technically authorized, the extraordinary enforcement measures available for current taxes are no longer fully available.

## Gezel Goy (IV) (II)

### Lost Objects

*Rav Bibi bar Gidel* states in the name of *Rav Shimon Chasida*: *gezel goy assur* — stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden. *Aveidato muteres* — keeping his lost object is permitted. One need not return a lost object belonging to a non-Jew, as derived from the verse *le-chol aveidas achicha* — to all lost objects of your *brother* — implying the obligation extends only to a fellow Jew.

*Rav Huna* reinforces the prohibition on active theft from the verse *ve'achalta es kol ha'amim asher Hashem Elokecha nosein lach* — you shall consume all the nations God gives you — from which is derived: *bezman she'heim mesurim beyadecha* — only when they are delivered into your hand, i.e., when conquered in war, does this apply, *ve'lo bezman she'einam mesurim beyadecha* — but not otherwise, and certainly not through ordinary theft.

*Rav Chama bar Gurya* derives the permissibility of keeping a non-Jew's lost object from the verse itself: *le'achicha atah machzir ve'ee atah machzir le-goy* — you return to your brother but not to a non-Jew. One might ask: is this genuinely a less serious omission than active theft, given that there is a concrete physical object in one's possession belonging to another? The *gemara* does not address this directly, but in every case, *chilul Hashem* overrides the permissive baseline: *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* rules that wherever *chilul Hashem* is at stake, even keeping a non-Jew's lost object is forbidden — *afilu aveidato assur*.

*Shmuel* adds: *ta'uto muteres* — if a non-Jew makes a mistake in a business transaction that benefits the Jew, the Jew need not point it out. Again, this is subject to the *chilul Hashem* constraint. *Shmuel* himself is then cited as an example: he purchased what he knew to be a gold vessel from a non-Jew who believed it was copper, paying the copper price, and also apparently paid slightly less than even the agreed sum. His protection against *chilul Hashem* was his declaration to the seller: *chazi dela chasmina* — "look, I am trusting you in all of this" — thereby putting the burden of care on the seller and insulating himself from the charge of deception if the error were later discovered. *Rav Kahana* similarly purchased 120 barrels at the price of 100, also declaring *chazi dela chasmina*. In both cases the declaration of trust served to transfer responsibility to the seller and preempt any subsequent *chilul Hashem*.

The pattern of these cases — while consistent with the *halachic* framework of *hafkakat halvato* — is admittedly ethically uncomfortable in ways the *gemara* itself seems to acknowledge through the *chilul Hashem* constraint it imposes on every one of these permissions. The marginal glosses attempting to limit these rulings to an older, more hostile social context reflect a genuine awareness, throughout the tradition, that these principles require careful handling.

## Mixed Flocks

### City Grazing

*Rava* rules: shepherds who graze their animals within the *tchum* — the area near a city — *assur l'kach meihem* — it is forbidden to purchase animals from them. The reason is *mishum d'me'arvi chiyuta d'mata bahadeihu* — because animals belonging to the city's inhabitants inevitably become mixed in with the shepherd's flock when they are grazing so close to populated areas. Any given animal in such a shepherd's possession may therefore have been absorbed from someone else's herd and effectively stolen.

### Distant Grazing

Shepherds who graze exclusively outside the *tchum* are treated differently: *mutar l'kach meihem* — one may purchase from them, since the mixing of city animals with distant flocks is far less likely. However, *Ravina* qualifies: *im hayu ba'alim meradfim achareihen* — if the owners of animals were visibly pursuing this shepherd, apparently tracking their lost animals into his flock, then even outside the *tchum* one may not purchase from him, because the circumstances themselves suggest that his flock contains animals not his own.

## Gezel Goy (IV) (II)

### Ger Toshav

The *gemara*'s earlier framing of *gezel goy* involved a tacit assumption about the category of non-Jew to whom these distinctions apply. As addressed above, *Rav Yosef* had proposed distinguishing between an ordinary non-Jew and a *ger toshav* — one who accepts the seven Noahide laws. *Abaye* rejected that as a solution to the textual contradiction within *Rabbi Akiva*'s positions, since the relevant verse encompasses both categories. Nevertheless, the *ger toshav* category remains operative as a limiting principle: many authorities hold that where the non-Jew in question observes basic moral and legal norms — behaving, in the language of the gloss, *kamocha*, like unto you — the full range of permissive rulings concerning *hafkakat halvato* and lost objects does not apply.

## Non-Jewish Courts

### Volunteer Testimony

*Rava* — or in some versions *Rav Huna* — announced publicly, directed at those traveling between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Bavel*: a Jew who knows testimony that would support a non-Jew's case against a fellow Jew — *v'lo tva'u minei* (according to the textual reading that includes this phrase) and has not been summoned to testify — who nevertheless goes and testifies in a non-Jewish court on behalf of the non-Jew against the Jew, *meshamtinan lei* — is placed in *cherem*.

The reason is that non-Jewish courts of the *magista* type (the local village court, per *Rashi*) will extract money on the basis of a single witness — *de'inhu mafik mamona apuma de-chad* — whereas Jewish law requires two witnesses before money is extracted, and a single witness can at most impose an oath. By volunteering testimony in such a court, the Jew enables a monetary extraction that Jewish procedure would not permit. The *halacha* thus limits this to unsummoned testimony in courts that do not follow the two-witness standard. If the witness is one of two (so that the non-Jew would prevail in Jewish court as well), or if the court follows the same standards as a Jewish court with respect to single witnesses, testifying is not prohibited.

## Gezel Goy (IV) (III)…

### Trade Examples

The cases of *Rav Ashi* walking along the road and *Ravina* purchasing a palm tree together with a non-Jewish partner illustrate the practical texture of these principles. When *Rav Ashi* instructed his attendant to bring him grapes from a vineyard if the vineyard belonged to a non-Jew, but not if it belonged to a Jew, a non-Jew who overheard challenged him: is it permitted to simply take a non-Jew's grapes? *Rav Ashi* replied: *goy shakel damei* — if it were a non-Jew's vineyard, he would be paid; *Yisrael lo shakel damei* — a Jew would not agree to be paid after the fact and would need to be asked permission beforehand. The intent was always to pay the owner; the distinction was about whose presumed consent could be relied upon in advance.

*Tosafos* takes this at face value: the dominant position of this entire *gemara* is that *gezel goy* is *assur*, and if *Rav Ashi* believed otherwise the *gemara* would have made that clear. *Tosafos* therefore concludes that he genuinely intended to pay.

*Ravina*'s case with his non-Jewish partner involved his instruction to his attendant to take his share of a palm tree from the base rather than the top — the base yielding far thicker, more valuable wood. The non-Jew, *Ravina* observed, *minyana yada* — counts only quantity, not quality. The *gemara* presents this as permissible business acumen within the terms of their partnership agreement, since the division was by number of logs rather than by weight or value.

## Non-Jewish Courts (II)

### Single Witness

The *gemara* rules that where a witness would be one of two — such that the non-Jew would prevail even in *beis din* — there is no prohibition against testifying in a non-Jewish court if summoned. Similarly, where the non-Jewish court in question follows similar evidentiary standards to Jewish procedure — imposing an oath rather than extracting money on the strength of a single witness — testimony is also permissible.

### Prominent Witness

*Rav Ashi* reports a question that arose in the *beis din* of *Rav Kahana*: what about an *adam chashuv* — a prominent person — who is a single witness, but whose word carries such weight that the non-Jewish court will rely on his testimony alone and extract money, as if he were two witnesses? On one hand, because of his prominence he functions effectively as two witnesses, and therefore testifying would enable an extraction that Jewish procedure would not permit from a single witness. On the other hand, *lo matu mishtamit lehu* — a person of such standing cannot avoid appearing when summoned; he would face serious consequences for failing to appear, consequences that a less prominent person might not face. The question remains unresolved: *teiku*.

## Land to Non-Jews

### Excommunication

*Rav Ashi* rules: a Jew who sells a plot of land to a non-Jew, where that land abuts the property of a Jewish neighbor, is placed in *cherem* — *meshamtinan lei*. The potential basis one might suggest is *dina de-bar mitzra* — the right of first refusal that a neighboring landowner has when adjacent property is sold. However, the *gemara* dismisses that as the operative reason, since the law of *dina de-bar mitzra* does not generally apply to transactions involving non-Jews.

### Neighbor Concern

The actual reason for the *cherem* is different: by selling to a non-Jewish neighbor, the seller has effectively placed a threat next to the Jewish neighbor's property — *arveiti aryeh amitzrei* — "you have set a lion at my border." The assumption underlying this ruling reflects the broader social context: non-Jewish neighbors were perceived as likely to encroach on land, steal crops, and cause harm in ways a Jewish neighbor would not. The seller is therefore held in *cherem* until he accepts personal financial liability — *ad dekableh kol onsa de'ati machmateh* — for any harm or loss that results from the non-Jew's proximity to the Jewish neighbor's land. The laws of *dina de-bar metzra* proper remain a separate topic, treated extensively in *Bava Batra*.
