# Bava Kamma 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Judgment & Testimony

### Prior Context

The daf opens in the middle of 112b, where the *gemara* had been engaged with a cluster of related questions arising from the case of someone who died leaving stolen property (*gezeila*) in the possession of his children. The discussion addressed whether those children are adults (*gedolim*) or minors (*katanim*), and whether a judgment can be rendered against minors when it is as if the opposing party (*baal din*) is absent. From there the *gemara* launched into a broader treatment of *mekabelin eidus shelo bifnei baal din* — accepting testimony in the absence of the defendant — and the range of circumstances under which this is permitted: urgency because witnesses are about to depart or fall ill, or because the defendant has been summoned and refuses to appear.

*Tosafos* raises an important question about whether these two conditions — the urgency and the refusal — must both be present simultaneously, or whether either one alone is sufficient. In certain passages the *gemara* seems to indicate that a refusal to appear is independently sufficient to proceed with the case, while other passages suggest both conditions are required. This remains a significant unresolved tension in *Tosafos*.

### Document Validation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִלְכְתָא: מְקַיְּימִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל דִּין, וַאֲפִילּוּ עוֹמֵד וְצוֹוֵחַ. וְאִי אָמַר: נְקִיטוּ לִי זִימְנָא עַד דְּמַיְיתֵינָא סָהֲדִי וּמַרַעְנָא לֵיהּ לִשְׁטָרָא – נָקְטִינַן לֵיהּ. אִי אָתָא – אֲתָא, אִי לָא אֲתָא – נָטְרִינַן לֵיהּ שֵׁנִי וַחֲמִישִׁי וְשֵׁנִי;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The ***halakha*** is that the court **ratifies a document in the absence of a litigant, and** this is the case **even** if the other litigant **stands and screams** in protest that the document is a forgery. **And if he said: Give me time until I bring witnesses and disqualify the document, we give him** time before forcing him to pay. **If he has come** with witnesses within the time allotted to him, **he has come,** and the court reviews the case accordingly. **If he does not come** within the time allotted to him, **we give him** an additional three days on which to bring witnesses, when the court is in session: **Monday, Thursday, and Monday.**

*Rava* rules: *hilchasa mekayemin lei lishtar shelo bifnei baal din* — the *halacha* is that we may perform *kiyum hashtar*, validating the signatures on a document, even in the defendant's absence. The rationale is that the requirement to verify signatures is itself only rabbinic in origin, and therefore we may proceed even without the defendant present.

Moreover, *ve'afilu omed vetzoveach* — even if the defendant is actively protesting that the document is a forgery, we proceed with *kiyum hashtar* unless he brings evidence to support his claim. If he says, "Give me time and I will produce witnesses to invalidate the document," we give him that time. If he ultimately appears and succeeds, well and good. If not, we wait a *beis-heh-beis* — the court sitting schedule of Monday, Thursday, and Monday — and if he has still not appeared, we proceed with the case despite his forgery claim, and *vechasvinan pesicha ilaveih*: we write a writ against him. *Rashi* explains this as a *shamta*, a form of *cherem*, because he has not paid his debt.

## 90-Day Collection

### Days 1–30

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָא אֲתָא – כָּתְבִינַן פְּתִיחָא עִלָּוֵיהּ תִּשְׁעִין יוֹמִין. תְּלָתִין קַמָּאֵי לָא נָחֲתִינַן לְנִכְסֵיהּ, דְּאָמְרִינַן: קָא טָרַח בְּזוּזֵי וְנֵיזוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **If he** still **has not come, we write a document of ostracism against him** for **ninety days.** During **the first thirty** days **we do not descend to his property** to collect his debt, **as** the court **says:** Perhaps **he is occupied** with **borrowing money** to pay his debt.

After a judgment has been rendered and this writ of *cherem* is in place, the *gemara* establishes a ninety-day period before actually seizing the debtor's property. The first thirty days we do not descend upon his assets — *de'amar katarach bezuzai* — because we want to give him the opportunity to borrow the money. It is preferable to allow him to raise the funds himself rather than forcing us to seize his property.

### Days 31–60

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצִיעָאֵי נָמֵי לָא נָחֲתִינַן לֵיהּ לְנִכְסֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: דִּלְמָא לָא אַשְׁכַּח לְמֵיזַף, וְקָא טָרַח וּמְזַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** During **the middle** period of thirty days **as well, we do not descend to his property, as** the court **says: Perhaps he did not find** someone **to borrow** from and **he is** now **occupied with selling** his property in order to pay his debt.

The middle thirty days we also refrain from seizing property, even though the time to borrow has passed. The reasoning is *de'amar dilma lo ashkach lemiyzif vekatarach lezabin*: perhaps he was unable to find someone to lend him money, and we therefore give him the opportunity to sell his own property at a fair price. Forced seizure would yield him a far worse outcome than a voluntary sale.

### Days 61–90

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתְרָאֵי נָמֵי לָא נָחֲתִינַן לְנִכְסֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: לוֹקֵחַ גּוּפֵיהּ קָא טָרַח בְּזוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** During the **final** thirty days **as well, we do not descend to his property, as** the court **says:** Perhaps the debtor has found a purchaser and **the purchaser himself is occupied with** obtaining the **money** so that he can pay the debtor for the sale, and the debtor will then pay his debt.

The final thirty days we likewise wait, because even if he has found a buyer, the buyer himself may need time to raise or borrow the funds to complete the purchase. We extend this accommodation so that the debtor can achieve the best possible return and suffer the minimum possible loss.

### After 90 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא אֲתָא – כָּתְבִינַן אַדְרַכְתָּא אַנִּיכְסֵיהּ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דַּאֲמַר "אָתֵינָא", אֲבָל אָמַר "לָא אָתֵינָא" – לְאַלְתַּר כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** If the debtor still **did not come** to pay his debt after ninety days, **we write** a document of **authorization [*adrakhta*]** allowing the creditor to collect his due from any **property** belonging to the debtor. **And this statement** applies only in a case **where** the debtor **said: I am coming** to pay and then he does not actually make an appearance. **But** if **he said: I am not coming, we immediately write** a document of authorization without waiting for ninety days to pass.

If after all ninety days have elapsed and he has not paid, *chasvinan adrachta anichsei* — we write a document authorizing seizure of his property and proceed to collect. This entire ninety-day accommodation applies only when the debtor says *maysina* — "I am trying to produce the money." If he says *lo maysina* — he rejects the proceedings entirely — then we do not wait the *beis-heh-beis* before issuing the writ against him. He may still receive the ninety days to collect, but the preliminary *bahab* waiting period is skipped.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמִלְוֶה, אֲבָל בְּפִקָּדוֹן – לְאַלְתַּר כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **this matter** of a ninety-day waiting period applies only **with regard to a loan,** as the debtor requires time in order to obtain the money to pay it back. **But with regard to a deposit** that the owner demands back from a bailee, **we write** a document of authorization **immediately.** Since a bailee should have immediate access to the deposit, there is no reason to grant him an extension.

A further distinction: all of this applies to a loan (*milva*), where the debtor had a right to spend the money and must now reassemble it. For a deposit (*pikadon*), we do not extend the same allowance — *l'altar kasvinan* — we proceed immediately, since the debtor had no right to consume what was entrusted to him.

### Movables & Forgery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי כָּתְבִינַן – אַמְּקַרְקְעֵי, אֲבָל אַמִּטַּלְטְלִי – לָא; דִּלְמָא שָׁמֵיט וְאָכֵיל לְהוּ מַלְוֶה לְמִטַּלְטְלֵי, וְכִי אָתֵי לֹוֶה וּמַיְיתֵי סָהֲדִי וּמַרַע לֵיהּ לִשְׁטָרָא – לָא מַשְׁכַּח מִידֵּי לְמִיגְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **When we write** a document of authorization, the document applies only **to** the borrower's **land, but** with regard **to movable property,** the court does **not** draft such a document. The reason for this is that **perhaps the creditor will seize and consume** the borrower's **movable property, and when the borrower** later **comes and brings witnesses and** thereby **invalidates the** creditor's **document, he will not find anything to collect** in order to retrieve his money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִית לֵיהּ מְקַרְקְעֵי לְמַלְוֶה – כָּתְבִינַן. וְלָא הִיא; אַדְרַכְתָּא אַמִּטַּלְטְלִי לָא כָּתְבִינַן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית לֵיהּ מְקַרְקְעֵי; חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תַּכְסִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the creditor** owns **land, we write** a document authorizing the creditor to collect from any property that belongs to the borrower, including movable property. Since the creditor owns land, there is no concern that if the borrower manages to overturn the ruling he will have nothing from which to collect. The Gemara comments: **And** that **is not so.** The court **does not draft** a document of **authorization with regard to movable property even if** the creditor **has land.** This is because **we are concerned lest** the creditor's land **depreciate** in value and the borrower will be unable to retrieve his money.

A further nuance concerns the type of property that may be seized. The *gemara* initially reasons that we should seize *mekarkei* — real property — but not *metaltelin* — movable goods. The concern is *dilma shamit v'achal lehu malve l'metalteli*: the creditor, once given the movable property, will sell or consume it, and then *v'chi atei loveh u'meiti sahadi u'marea lei leshtar* — if the debtor ultimately produces witnesses proving the document was a forgery, *lo mishkach midi l'migba* — he will have nothing to recover from the creditor. The *gemara* wants to preserve the possibility of reversal, which is important precisely because the debtor is claiming the document is forged and has not yet been proven wrong.

The *gemara* initially suggests an exception: if the creditor himself owns *karka*, we can seize *metaltelin*, since the debtor could always recover from the creditor's real estate. But the *gemara* rejects even this: *af al gav d'islei karka chashinan shema techsif* — we are concerned that the creditor's real property might decline in value. Therefore, in all circumstances where the debtor is claiming forgery, we limit seizure to *karka* alone, so that if necessary the very same land can be returned. That this claim alone — the mere assertion of forgery, without yet substantiating it — is sufficient to keep this door open is, on reflection, quite striking.

### Writing Writs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי כָּתְבִינַן אַדְרַכְתָּא – מוֹדְעִינַן לֵיהּ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּמְיקָרַב, אֲבָל מְרַחַק – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara teaches another *halakha* with regard to this matter: **When we write** the document of **authorization, we inform** the party that is liable to pay, as a final warning before authorizing a creditor to locate and take possession of his property in repayment of the debt. **And this matter** applies only **when he is nearby, but** if he is **distant,** the court does **not** notify him before authorizing the creditor.

When we do write an *adrachta* — the document authorizing seizure of property — we are required to inform the debtor. *Moda'inan leh*: we notify him that such a document has been issued against him and that he should be aware of the impending action.

### Informing Defendant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מְרַחַק וְאִיכָּא קְרוֹבִים, אִי נָמֵי אִיכָּא שְׁיָירָתָא דְּאָזְלִי וְאָתוּ הָתָם, מְשַׁהֵינַן לֵיהּ תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא עַד דְּאָזְלָא וְאָתְיָיא שְׁיָירָא. כִּי הָא דְּרָבִינָא שְׁהָא לְמָר אַחָא תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, עַד דְּאָזְלָא וְאָתְיָיא שְׁיָירָא מִבֵּי חוֹזָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he is distant but there are relatives** of his nearby, **or** if **there are caravans that go** to the borrower's current location **and return, we delay** issuing **his** document of authorization for the **twelve months** of **the year, until the caravan goes and returns,** so that if he wishes to make a claim, he can do so. This is **like that** incident **where Ravina delayed** for the **twelve months** of **the year** before issuing a document of authorization **for Mar Aḥa, until a caravan went** to **and returned from Bei Ḥozai.**

This obligation to notify, however, depends on the defendant's proximity. If he is nearby, we notify him before acting. If he is far away, we do not delay the writing of the *adrachta* until he can be reached. However, if he is distant but has relatives nearby, or if there are caravans that travel between here and where he lives, *mashinan leh srei asar yarchei shata* — we wait up to a full year for the caravan to travel there and back, to ensure he has actually received word before we seize his property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; הָתָם – אִינִישׁ אַלִּימָא הֲוָה, אִי הַוְיָא מָטְיָא אַדְרַכְתָּא לִידֵיהּ לָא הֲוָה אֶפְשָׁר לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִינֵּיהּ. אֲבָל הָכָא – לָא נָטְרִינַן לֵיהּ אֶלָּא עַד דְּאָזֵיל שְׁלִיחָא בִּתְלָתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא וְאָתֵי בְּאַרְבְּעָה בְּשַׁבְּתָא, וּלְחַמְשָׁא בְּשַׁבְּתָא קָאֵי בְּדִינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** that **is not so. There,** Ravina delayed issuing the document because the creditor **was a violent man,** and **if** a document of **authorization would come into his possession, it would not be possible to take it from him** if the borrower would prove that the original document was invalid. **But here,** i.e., in general, **we wait for** the defendant to be informed **only** if he is close enough to be informed within a day. Consequently, if the court decided the issue on Monday, **a messenger** would **go** inform the defendant **on Tuesday, and he** could **come** from his location to the court **on Wednesday and stand trial on Thursday.** If a debtor is more than a day's travel away, however, the court does not trouble itself to notify him.

The *gemara* cites the case of Ravina, who waited a full year for Mar Acha to receive notice via the caravans from Bei Chuza'ei. However, the *gemara* qualifies this: *lo hi* — it does not mean this is the standard rule. In that particular case, Mar Acha was a powerful individual (*inish alima*), and had the *adrachta* reached his hands, *lo hava efshar le'afokei mineih* — it would have been impossible to recover the property from him. Therefore extra caution was taken. In ordinary circumstances, once the *adrachta* is ready, the court's messenger sets out on Tuesday, arrives at his destination on Wednesday, and the defendant has until Thursday to appear in court. This presupposes, of course, that the defendant is close enough to be reached within that window — which raises the puzzling question of why the notification does not come before the ninety-day period begins rather than at the moment of seizure.

## Messenger's Role

### Messenger's Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הַאי שְׁלוּחָא דְרַבָּנַן, מְהֵימְנִינַן לֵיהּ כְּבֵי תְרֵי. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לְשַׁמְתָּא, אֲבָל לִפְתִיחָא – כֵּיוָן דְּמָמוֹנָא קָא מְחַסַּר לֵיהּ, דְּקָא בָעֵי לֵיהּ לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְסָפְרָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the Gemara's discussion of summoning an individual to court by means of a messenger, the Gemara discusses this issue in a more general manner. **Ravina said: We deem the agent of the Rabbis,** who was sent to summon an individual to court, as **credible as two** witnesses if he says that the defendant refuses to come to court. **And this matter** applies only with regard **to excommunication, but** with regard **to** issuing **a document of ostracism, since it causes** the defendant **loss of money, as he must give money to the scribe** for drafting the document, **no,** the agent is not deemed credible.

*Ravina* rules: *hai shluacha deRabbanan meheman leh kebei srei* — a court-appointed messenger (*shliach beis din*) is believed like two witnesses. If the court sends a messenger to summon a defendant and he returns reporting that the defendant refuses to appear, we accept that report without requiring corroborating evidence. On that basis, proceedings against the defendant begin — we place him under *shamta* and issue the relevant writs.

The *gemara* here seems to reflect two distinct orientations: in some passages the court appears to operate from a position of strength, pressing forward with proceedings and giving time as an accommodation. In other passages, the court seems to be bending over backwards to maintain its jurisdiction in the face of a defendant who cannot be compelled — issuing multiple summonses, waiting through extended periods, and requiring extraordinary care before acting. Whether these represent genuinely different situations or reflect a tension between formal authority and practical power is worth considering.

### Written Writ

A distinction is drawn, however, between the oral report of the messenger and the formal issuance of the *pesicha* — the written writ of *cherem*. The *pesicha* carries financial consequences: *de-ka ba'ei lei le-meitav lei zuzei le-safra* — when the *cherem* is eventually lifted, the defendant will be required to pay the scribal costs of having it drawn up. Because this imposes a monetary penalty, the court requires more than the messenger's word alone before issuing it. Supporting evidence of the refusal is needed before we write the formal document.

### Messenger Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: יָהֲבִינַן זִימְנָא אַפּוּמָּא דְאִיתְּתָא וְאִפּוּמָא דְשִׁיבָבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, **Ravina said: We may give** a defendant a particular **time** to appear in court by informing him of the trial **by** word of **mouth from a woman or from his neighbors,** as these people are trusted to notify the defendant of the court's summons. The Gemara comments: **And we said** this **only when** the defendant is **not in the city.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בְּמָתָא, אֲבָל אִיתֵיהּ בְּמָתָא – לָא; דְּאָמְרִינַן: אֵימַר לָא אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ, דְּאָמְרִי: אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ שְׁלִיחָא דְּבֵית דִּין, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the defendant **is in the city,** the court does **not** ostracize him for failing to respond to a summons conveyed through a woman or a neighbor, **as we say:** Perhaps **they did not tell him** of the court's summons, **as they said** to themselves: Since the defendant is in the city, **a court agent** has already **found him and told him.** As a result, these unofficial messengers will not deliver the court's summons to the defendant at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא חָלֵיף אַבָּבָא דְּבֵי דִּינָא, אֲבָל חָלֵיף אַבָּבָא דְּבֵי דִינָא – לָא; אָמְרִי: אַשְׁכְּחוּהּ בֵּי דִינָא, וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **we said** that the court will ostracize one who does not respond to a summons conveyed through a woman or a neighbor **only** in a case **where he does not pass by the court's entrance** on his way home, **but** if he does **pass by the court's entrance,** the court does **not** ostracize him. This is because it is possible that the unofficial messengers will **say** to themselves: Since he passes by the courthouse, **the court** has already **found him and told him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּאָתֵי בְּיוֹמֵיהּ, אֲבָל לָא אָתֵי בְּיוֹמֵיהּ – לָא; אֵימָא אִישְׁתְּלוֹיֵי אִשְׁתְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **we said** that the court will ostracize one who does not respond to a summons **only** in a case **where he comes** home **on the** same **day** that the woman or neighbor is sent to deliver the court summons. **But** if he does **not come** home **on the** same **day,** he is **not** ostracized, because it is possible to **say** that **they forgot** to notify him.

*Avina* rules: *yehavinan zimna apuma de-itata ve-apuma de-shivevei* — we may issue a summons through a woman or through neighbors, not only through official court officers. If *Reuven* is away and one informs his neighbor or his wife to relay the summons, we assume they will do so, and if *Reuven* fails to appear on the appointed day, we treat him as in contempt.

However, this assumption applies only under narrow conditions. If *Reuven* is in the city at the time of the summons, we do not rely on neighbors or family, because *de-amrinan ashtechach shlicha de-bei dina ve-amar lei* — we say that the neighbors will assume the court's own officers found him and told him directly, and will therefore not take the responsibility seriously themselves. Similarly, even if *Reuven* is out of town, if he regularly passes by the courthouse when returning, the neighbors will again assume the court told him. And even if neither of those applies, the assumption that the neighbors informed him holds only if he was expected to return that same day — if he was not coming back that day, *eimar ishtaluyei ishtalu*, we say they simply forgot to tell him.

All of this narrowing of conditions serves *Reuven*'s interest: we want to be genuinely confident he received the summons before beginning proceedings against him in absentia.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דִּכְתִיב עֲלֵיהּ פְּתִיחָא עַל דְּלָא אָתֵי לְדִינָא – עַד דְּאָתֵי לְדִינָא, לָא מְקָרְעִינַן לֵיהּ. עַל דְּלָא צָיֵית לְדִינָא – עַד דְּצָיֵית, לָא מְקָרְעִינַן לֵיהּ. וְלָא הִיא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר: "צָיֵיתְנָא" – קָרְעִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** With regard to **one** who had **a document of ostracism written about him due to** the fact that **he did not come to court, we do not tear up the document for him until he** actually **comes to court,** and it is not enough for him to simply commit to appearing. Similarly, if the document of ostracism was written **due to** the fact that **he did not obey** the ruling of **the court, we do not tear it up for him until he** actually **obeys** the ruling. The Gemara comments: This second statement **is not so.** Rather, **once he** has acquiesced and **said: I will obey, we** immediately **tear up the document for him.**

*Rava* rules further: once a *pesicha* has been written against someone for failure to appear, we do not tear it up — even if he says he is coming — until he actually shows up. And once a writ has been issued against someone for failure to comply with a judgment, we do not tear it up even if he says he will pay — until he actually pays. Or so it first seems. The *gemara* then reverses: *velo hi* — once he says *tzyasna*, "I will comply," we do tear it up. *Rashi* explains the distinction: if someone says "I'm coming to court," he can come immediately, so we hold out for actual appearance. But if someone says "I will pay," there may be legitimate reasons for delay — he needs time to raise the money — and his declaration of willingness to comply is itself meaningful. The *gemara*'s earlier discussion of the ninety-day period already acknowledged that even a willing debtor may need extended time to gather funds.

## Summons Timing

### Mon-Thu-Mon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: קוֹבְעִים זְמַן שֵׁנִי וַחֲמִישִׁי וְשֵׁנִי – זִמְנָא וְזִמְנָא בָּתַר זִמְנָא, וּלְמָחָר כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The court **sets a date** for an individual to appear in court on the upcoming **Monday. And** if he does not appear, they set a date for that **Thursday, and** if he does not appear, they set a date for the following **Monday,** so that he has a second **date and** then a third **date after** the first **date. And** if he does not appear in court by the third date, then **on the next day we write** a document of ostracism.

*Rav Chisda* rules: *kosvin zman besheini uvechamishi uvesheini* — we schedule summonses for Monday, Thursday, and the following Monday, since those are the days when *beis din* convenes. Only after the defendant has failed to appear across this full sequence — Monday, Thursday, Monday — do we write a *shamta*, a writ of contempt, against him.

### Women vs. Men

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי אִיקְּלַע בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא. חֲזָא הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּאַזְמְנַהּ לְדִינָא בְּפַנְיָא, וּבְצַפְרָא כְּתֻיב עֲלַהּ פְּתִיחָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: קוֹבְעִין זְמַן שֵׁנִי וַחֲמִישִׁי וְשֵׁנִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Asi happened** to come to **the house of Rav Kahana. He saw** that there was **a certain woman whom** Rav Kahana had **summoned to** appear in **court in the evening,** but she did not appear, **and in the morning** Rav Kahana **wrote a document of ostracism concerning her.** Rav Asi said **to him:** Does **the Master not hold** in accordance with **that which Rav Ḥisda says,** that the court **sets a date** for the coming **Monday, and** then **Thursday, and** then the following **Monday** before it issues a document of ostracism?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי גַּבְרָא, דַּאֲנִיס וְלֵיתֵיהּ בְּמָתָא; אֲבָל אִיתְּתָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיתַהּ בְּמָתָא וְלָא אָתְיָא – מוֹרֶדֶת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **said to him: That matter** applies only with regard to **a man, as he is a victim of circumstance and is not** always **in the city** due to his vocational activities. **But** in the case of **a woman, since she is** always **in the city,** when **she does not come** to court the first time **she is** immediately considered **rebellious,** and the court may issue a document of ostracism right away.

*Rav Assi* visited the *beis din* of *Rav Kahana* and observed a case in which a woman was summoned on one afternoon and by the following morning *Rav Kahana* had already written a *pesicha* against her. *Rav Assi* challenged him: does the master not hold by *Rav Chisda*'s rule that we give a full Monday-Thursday-Monday sequence before writing the writ of contempt?

*Rav Kahana* replied: *hani mili gavra de-anis* — that rule applies to a man, who may be traveling, occupied with his livelihood, or otherwise unavailable. But a woman, *keivan de-isa be-mata* — who is presumed to be at home in the city — if she has not appeared, *moredes hi*, she is evidently refusing. The *gemara* thus establishes a procedurally different standard for women on the grounds of presumed availability.

This is, admittedly, a striking ruling that reflects certain assumptions about the social roles of men and women. What makes it interesting beyond the obvious is that the *gemara* elsewhere holds precisely the opposite assumption when it comes to *tefilla*: women are exempt from time-bound positive commandments, often explained on the grounds that they are occupied with domestic responsibilities. There, the *gemara* seems to presume women are busier than men; here, it presumes they are more available. The tension is real, and worth noting.

What the ruling does illustrate clearly is that *Rav Chisda*'s rule is not a rigid formal requirement but a contextual one — its application depends on the actual circumstances of the defendant's likely availability.

### Exempted Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לָא יָהֲבִינַן זִימְנָא לָא בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן וְלָא בְּיוֹמֵי תִּשְׁרֵי, לָא בְּמַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא טָבָא וְלָא בְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא. אֲבָל מִנִּיסָן לְבָתַר יוֹמֵי נִיסָן, וּבְיוֹמֵי תִּשְׁרֵי לְבָתַר תִּשְׁרֵי – קָבְעִינַן. מִמַּעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא לְבָתַר מַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא – לָא קָבְעִינַן. מַאי טַעְמָא? בַּעֲבִידְתֵּיהּ דְּשַׁבְּתָא טְרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Continuing the discussion of court dates, **Rav Yehuda says:** The court **does not set a date for** legal proceedings **during the days of Nisan, nor during the days of Tishrei,** and also **not on the eve of a Festival nor the eve of Shabbat** because these are busy times. **But during Nisan we may set** a court date to take place **after Nisan, and** likewise, **during Tishrei** we may set a court date to take place **after Tishrei.** By contrast, **on the eve of Shabbat we do not** set a court date to take place **after the eve of Shabbat. What is the reason** for this? It is because **one is preoccupied with his work** in preparation **for Shabbat** and it is possible that he will forget about the court summons.

*Rav Yehuda* rules in the name of *Rav*: *lo yahvinan zimna lo be-yomei Nisan ve-lo be-yomei Tishrei* — we do not issue summonses during the harvest season of Nisan or the planting season of Tishrei. People have pressing agricultural obligations during those periods, and it would be unjust to summon them to court when they cannot reasonably attend. However, one may send a summons *during* those seasons requiring the defendant to appear *after* the season ends — during Nisan, informing him to come after Nisan; during Tishrei, to come after Tishrei.

Similarly, *mi-malei Shabbos le-basar malei Shabbos lo havinan* — we do not send a summons on Friday requiring someone to appear after *Shabbos*. The reason is *mi-avadta de-Shabbos tridei* — on Fridays people are absorbed in Shabbos preparations and will not be properly attentive to a court summons.

### Religious Gatherings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: לָא יָהֲבִינַן זִימְנָא לָא לִבְנֵי כַלָּה בְּכַלָּה, וְלָא לִבְנֵי רִיגְלָא בְּרִיגְלָא. כִּי הֲווֹ אָתוּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְכִי לְדִידְכוּ כִּנּוּפַיְיכוּ?! וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּאִיכָּא רַמָּאֵי – חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: We do not set** a court **date for participants in the *kalla*,** the gatherings for Torah study during Elul and Adar, **during** the months of the ***kalla*, nor for participants in** the public discourses prior to **the Festival during the** period leading up to **the Festival.** The Gemara relates: **When** people **would come before Rav Naḥman** during the *kalla* period in order to make legal claims against others, **he would say to them: Did I gather you** here **for your** own needs? No, I gathered you to participate in Torah study. The Gemara adds: **But now that there are scoundrels,** who do not come to study Torah but rather to avoid trial, **we are concerned** that they will continue to evade prosecution, and therefore we summon them to court even during these time periods.

*Rav Nachman* rules: *lo yahvina zimna lo livnei kala be-kala ve-lo livnei rigla be-rigla* — we do not issue summonses to those attending the *kala* (the regular Shabbos *drasha*, according to *Rashi*) while they are there for it, nor to those gathered for the period of Torah study before the *regel* while they are so gathered. Using a public assembly as an opportunity to serve legal papers on attendees would be both improper and destructive to future attendance.

When litigants would approach *Rav Nachman* during such gatherings and ask him to use the opportunity to serve process on those present, he would rebuke them: *b'cheila d'dachki knuphaichu* — do you think I assembled this crowd for your purposes? The crowd was gathered to learn Torah.

The *gemara* appends, however: *v'idna d'ika ramai chashinan* — nowadays, since there are people who are skilled at evading process, and a public gathering may be the only opportunity to find them, we do make use of the occasion. The practical tension between respect for the learning assembly and the needs of the legal system is thus acknowledged.

## Minors & Father's Honor

### Public Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה דְבַר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם. מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרֵיהּ: לֹא דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַחְרָיוּת מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ פָּרָה וְחוֹרֵשׁ בָּהּ, חֲמוֹר וּמְחַמֵּר אַחֲרָיו – חַיָּיבִין לְהַחֲזִיר, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד אֲבִיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches, with regard to one who left a stolen item to his children, **if** the item **was something that may serve** as a legal **guarantee** of a loan, the heirs **are obligated to pay** the owner. The Gemara states that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi would **teach** this mishna to **Rabbi Shimon, his son,** and explain that it does **not** refer only to **something that can actually** serve as **a guarantee** for a loan, i.e., land. **Rather,** it refers **even** to **a cow** that **he plows with,** or **a donkey** that **he drives** by directing it from **behind,** which the heirs **are obligated to return because of the honor of their father,** so that people will not continually point out that the inheritance was stolen and thereby disgrace their deceased parent.

Returning to the *mishnah*, the principle that the children of a deceased thief must return stolen property is clarified. *Rebbe* taught his son *Rabbi Shimon*: the *mishnah*'s phrase "something that has *achrayus*" does not literally mean land. Rather, it refers to anything that is visibly and publicly in use — even a cow being plowed with, or a donkey being led — *chayavin l'hachzir mipnei kevod avihem* — they must return it out of respect for their father's honor. Even if technically the children may have acquired it through *yeush v'shinui reshus*, if the stolen nature of the object is obvious and public, they are obligated to return it.

### Private Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא מֵרַב: מִטָּה וּמֵיסֵב עָלֶיהָ, שׁוּלְחָן וְאוֹכֵל עָלָיו, מַהוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: "תֵּן לְחָכָם וְיֶחְכַּם עוֹד".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raises a dilemma before Rav:** If the robber left his heirs a stolen item that is used in relative privacy, such as **a bed that he lies on** or **a table upon which he eats,** rather than something as conspicuous as a large animal, **what is** the *halakha*? Are the heirs obligated to return it to its owner? Rav **said to him: "Give to a wise man, and he will be yet wiser"** (Proverbs 9:9), meaning that from the fact that the heirs must return a cow and a donkey, one can infer that they must also return a bed and a table.

*Rav Kahana* posed a question to *Rav*: what about items that are used regularly but in private, indoors, such as a bed one reclines on (*mita u'mesaiv aleha*) or a table one eats off of (*shulchan v'ochel alav*)? These items are seen by guests who enter the home, but they are not publicly observable in the same way a working animal is. *Rav* replied: *ten l'chacham v'yichkam od* — give it to a wise person and he will become wiser, meaning a learned person ought to be able to work it out for himself. *Rashi* understands this to mean that the same principle applies — such items must also be returned — though the answer is left implicit.

## Tax Collectors

### Stolen Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין פּוֹרְטִין לֹא מִתֵּיבַת הַמּוֹכְסִין, וְלֹא מִכִּיס שֶׁל גַּבָּאִין, וְאֵין נוֹטְלִין מֵהֶם צְדָקָה. אֲבָל נוֹטֵל הוּא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ אוֹ מִן הַשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not exchange** larger coins for smaller ones **from the trunk of customs collectors nor from the purse of tax collectors, and one may not take charity from them,** as they are assumed to have obtained their funds illegally. **But one may take** money **from** the collector's **house or from** money he has with him in **the market** that he did not take from his collection trunk or purse.

The *mishnah* rules: *ein pordin lo mi-teivas ha-mochsin v'lo mi-kis shel gabbai* — you may not exchange coins from the money box of a *moches* (tax collector) or from the pouch of a *gabbai* (a different type of collector). This ruling is rooted in the institution of tax farming in the Roman world, by which the government sold the right to collect taxes to private individuals, who then bore the costs and kept the revenues. The practical result was routine abuse: collectors would routinely extract more than they were legally entitled to. The general perception — shared widely in the ancient world, as reflected in the New Testament's association of tax collectors with social outcasts — was that their accumulated funds were essentially stolen money.

Since the money in the tax collector's box was presumed to derive largely from excess, unlawful collection, it was treated as stolen property. Changing your money with the tax collector would mean receiving funds that belong to whoever overpaid their taxes.

### Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לוֹ דִּינָר, וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁאָר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It was **taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the prohibition against exchanging money from the trunk of a customs collector: **But one may give** the customs collector **a dinar** as payment for a debt that amounts to less than a dinar, **and** when the collector **gives him change,** he may accept it.

The *mishnah* provides an important limitation: *aval notel hu mi-toch beito o min ha-shuk* — you may take money from the tax collector at his home or in the marketplace, where there is no reason to assume that whatever he happens to have on hand is stolen. The stolen-money presumption applies specifically to the designated collection pouch or box. The *gemara* adds further: *tanna aval nosin lo dinar ve-nosin lo es ha-sh'ar* — if your tax obligation is ten and you pay twenty, you may take ten in change even if the change comes from the pouch. *Rashi* understands this as *matzil mi-yadam* — one need not forfeit one's own money to avoid this concern.

### Tosafot's View

*Tosafos* analyzes the problem at two levels. First, according to the Rabbis who hold that ordinary stolen property is not subject to *ye'ush ha-ba'alim* — the owners have not given up hope — the prohibition is straightforward: the money genuinely still belongs to the original taxpayers. As for why one may still take change from the pouch, *Tosafos* explains that since not all the money in the pouch is necessarily stolen — some portion was legitimately collected — one may be lenient in the narrow case of recovering one's own money.

Second, and more fundamentally, *Tosafos* argues that even if we assume *ye'ush* and even *shinui reshus*, making the money technically transferable, it remains *davar meguneh* — unseemly and inappropriate — to take money from a receptacle that is effectively designated as a repository for stolen goods: *de-teiva ve-kis miyuchedes le-hatznia sham gezelos*. Technically permissible transfer is one thing; voluntarily engaging with what is universally understood to be stolen property is another.

This raises naturally the broader question — which *Tosafos* does not fully resolve and which remains practically sensitive — of whether one may accept *tzedakah* donations or institutional gifts from individuals whose wealth derives from ill-gotten gains. The *mishnah* itself narrows its prohibition to direct engagement with the designated collection pouch: money obtained by the tax collector through other means, and held elsewhere, is not subject to the same concern. The practical upshot seems to be that while direct engagement with the stolen cache itself is prohibited, more attenuated connection — buying from his profits, accepting donations from his household — falls outside the prohibition.

## Dina Demalchuta (II)

### Exceeding Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹכְסִין. וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא!
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that one may not exchange money from the trunks of **customs collectors,** which are assumed to include stolen funds. The Gemara questions this ruling: **But doesn't Shmuel say** that **the law of the kingdom is the law,** i.e., *halakha* requires Jews to obey the laws of the state in which they live. Accordingly, the customs are collected legally and it should be permitted to make use of the funds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר כָּהֲנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמוֹכֵס שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קִצְבָה. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמְרִי: בְּמוֹכֵס הָעוֹמֵד מֵאֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Kahana said** that **Shmuel says:** The mishna is discussing **a customs collector who does not have a limitation** placed by the governor on the amount he may collect, and he collects as he pleases. Alternatively, the Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai said:** The mishna is discussing **a customs collector who stands on his own,** i.e., he was not appointed by the government but, on his own, he forces people to give him money.

The broader discussion of *dina demalchuta dina* — the rule that the law of the kingdom is legally binding — is prompted by an apparent contradiction. *Shmuel* famously rules *dina demalchuta dina*. If the government authorizes tax collection, how can those tax collectors' funds be considered stolen?

*Rav Chanina bar Kahana* answers in the name of *Shmuel*: the ruling applies to a *moches she'ein lo kitzvah* — a tax collector who operates without fixed parameters, collecting whatever he likes beyond what the law permits. The school of *Rabbi Yannai* gives a second answer: *moches ha'omed mei'elav* — a self-appointed collector with no governmental authorization at all, essentially a local criminal operation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַהָא: לֹא יִלְבַּשׁ אָדָם כִּלְאַיִם אֲפִילּוּ עַל גַּבֵּי עֲשָׂרָה בְּגָדִים, לְהַבְרִיחַ בּוֹ אֶת הַמֶּכֶס. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּתַנְיָא: אָסוּר לְהַבְרִיחַ אֶת הַמֶּכֶס. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מוּתָּר לְהַבְרִיחַ אֶת הַמֶּכֶס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **There are** those **who teach** the statements of Rabbi Ḥanina bar Kahana and the Sages of the school of Rabbi Yannai **with regard to this** following mishna (*Kilayim* 9:2) and its attendant discussion. The customs collectors would not levy a duty for the garments one was wearing. In light of this, the mishna teaches: **A person may not wear** a garment made of **diverse kinds,** i.e., a combination of wool and linen, **even** if he wears it **on top of ten garments,** in order **to avoid** paying **customs.** It was noted that this **mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **It is prohibited to avoid** paying **customs** by wearing a garment of diverse kinds. **Rabbi Shimon says in the name of Rabbi Akiva: It is permitted to avoid** paying **customs** in this manner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעִנְיַן כִּלְאַיִם, בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין מוּתָּר, וּמָר סָבַר: דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין אָסוּר. אֶלָּא לְהַבְרִיחַ בּוֹ אֶת הַמֶּכֶס – מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, with regard to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds, they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** i.e., Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that **an unintentional act is permitted.** In this case, the prohibition is to benefit from wearing the garment, and that is not his intent, as his intention is merely to avoid paying the customs duties. Therefore, it is permitted. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., the first *tanna* in the *baraita*, **holds** that **an unintentional act is prohibited. But is** it ever **permitted to avoid customs? Doesn't Shmuel say: The law of the kingdom is the law?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר כָּהֲנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמוֹכֵס שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קִצְבָה. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמְרִי: בְּמוֹכֵס הָעוֹמֵד מֵאֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** In answer to this question, **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Kahana said** that **Shmuel says:** The dispute in the *baraita* is **with regard to a customs collector who does not have a limitation** placed on the amount he may collect. Alternatively, Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai said:** The dispute is **with regard to a customs collector who stands on his own,** i.e., who is self-appointed.

The *gemara* traces the same two answers through three Tannaitic sources that all assume tax collectors are effectively *gazlanim*: the *mishnah* prohibiting exchange of money with tax collectors; the ruling in *Kilayim* that one may not wear garments of *kilayim* even on top of ten garments in order to avoid paying import duties; and the ruling in the *mishnah* about *nedarim* — that one may take a vow before robbers (*hargin*), extortionists (*charamin*), and tax collectors (*mochsin*) claiming one's goods are *terumah* or belong to the royal household, even though they are not, and this does not constitute a prohibited false *neder* because the compelled context is understood by all parties, and one may in any case mentally qualify the vow.

In each case the *gemara* presses the same question: how does *Shmuel*'s *dina demalchuta dina* accommodate these Tannaitic rulings that treat tax collection as illegitimate? And in each case the answer is the same: we are speaking of collectors who exceed their authority or lack it altogether.

A historically significant observation emerges here. All three sources treating tax collectors as *gazlanim* are Tannaitic, and all emerge from the context of the Jews of *Eretz Yisrael* living under the oppressive Roman imperial system — a system in which tax collection was a byword for extortion, and in which the entire government apparatus was perceived as fundamentally hostile to Jewish interests. *Shmuel*, by contrast, is an *Amora* from *Bavel*, who had a close relationship with *Shvor Malka*, the head of the Persian government. The Babylonian government was far more hospitable to the Jewish community, and its legal system was perceived as substantially more just. The *Yerushalmi* never articulates *dina demalchuta dina* — it is a purely Babylonian, purely *Shmuel*-ian formulation. The two traditions are thus operating from very different assumptions about the legitimacy of the surrounding political order.

The analogy to *Rav Moshe Feinstein*'s famous *teshuva* on this cluster of questions is instructive. *Rav Moshe* begins by stating that of course no legitimate *yeshiva* would even be entertaining questions about cheating on the Regents examinations or misrepresenting oneself to obtain government subsidies — but to address the underlying principle: we live in a *medina shel chessed*, a government genuinely concerned with the welfare of its citizens, which is categorically different from Czarist Russia. The mentality appropriate to an oppressive regime that was itself stealing from its Jewish subjects cannot be exported wholesale into a context in which the government protects and serves its citizens. Whether or not one accepts this framing in its entirety, it captures precisely what distinguishes *Shmuel*'s *dina demalchuta dina* from the Tannaitic baseline.

*Tosafos* applies the principle about permissible vows to a case from his own time: in the Middle Ages, rulers would prohibit Jews from relocating to other regions and would compel them to take oaths not to leave. Such oaths constrained Jews' economic mobility and their ability to earn a *parnasa*. *Tosafos* rules that one may take such an oath while mentally qualifying it — *hayom* ("today"), for instance — without articulating the condition aloud, and this does not constitute a false *neder*. Even if the oath is formulated as binding for life, one may privately stipulate any condition in the mind. If the qualification was whispered under one's breath, *Tosafos* says it is *kol sheken* — all the more certainly valid.

The concern about *chilul Hashem* that might arise if this practice became known to the authorities was, by at least some accounts, understood by both sides — the government official administering the oath and the Jewish subject taking it both grasped the political theater involved. And yet the *gemara*'s warning about *chilul Hashem* remains operative: if the authorities genuinely discover that Jews are routinely circumventing their oaths, a serious *chilul Hashem* ensues. This was the social reality within which these halakhic maneuvers were practiced.

### Property vs. Conduct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּמוֹכֵס גּוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The mishna issues its ruling **with regard to a gentile customs collector,** whom one may deceive, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a Jew and a gentile who approach** the court **for judgment** in a legal dispute, **if you can vindicate** the Jew **under Jewish law, vindicate him, and say to** the gentile: **This is our law.** If he can be vindicated **under gentile law, vindicate him, and say to** the gentile: **This is your law. And if** it is **not** possible to vindicate him under either system of law, **one approaches** the case **circuitously,** seeking a justification to vindicate the Jew. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva** disagrees and **says: One does not approach** the case **circuitously** in order to vindicate the Jew **due to the sanctification of God's name,** as God's name will be desecrated if the Jewish judge employs dishonest means.

*Rav Ashi* offers a third answer to the question of how tax collection can be considered *gezel* despite *dina demalchuta dina*: *bemochas goy* — when the tax collector is a non-Jew. (The text as it appears in our printed *gemaras* reads *bemochas Kena'ani*, using the term "Canaanite" — a product of medieval censorship. The original reading was *goy* or *nochri*, and that is what appears in the *rishonim* and manuscripts. The practice of substituting *akum* or *Kena'ani* for *goy* was a form of Jewish self-censorship designed to deflect church scrutiny of passages that appeared to speak disparagingly of non-Jews or to exempt Jews from certain obligations toward them.)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – טַעְמָא דְּאִיכָּא קִידּוּשׁ הַשֵּׁם, הָא לֵיכָּא קִידּוּשׁ הַשֵּׁם – בָּאִין;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from this *baraita*: **And** even according to **Rabbi Akiva, the reason** that the court does not employ trickery in order to vindicate the Jew is only **because there is** the consideration of **the sanctification of God's name. Consequently,** if there **is no** consideration of **the sanctification of God's name,** the court does **approach** the case circuitously. Apparently, it is permitted to deceive a gentile.

*Rav Ashi*'s answer, properly understood, implies something significant about the scope of *dina demalchuta dina*. One might ask: even if one is permitted under Jewish law to withhold payment from a non-Jewish creditor, surely the government itself has mandated payment to this tax collector — so why doesn't *dina demalchuta dina* require compliance? The answer that seems to emerge from a careful reading is that *dina demalchuta dina* governs *property rights* — the government has the authority to determine who owns what, to impose liens, to establish that a given debt is owed. What it does not do is impose a *halachic* obligation on the Jew to actively comply with government directives. The government can define that the money is owed; whether the Jew is obligated to pay it is a separate *halachic* question governed by the *Torah*.

This is a consequential distinction. *Dina demalchuta dina* operates analogously to *hefker beis din hefker* — just as a *beis din* can determine property rights and effect transfers, so can the government. They can say that the tax farmer owns the tax revenue and that you owe it to him. But they cannot, through their legal mandate alone, impose on the Jew the *halachic* duty to perform an act of payment. And so when the government seizes property — cutting down palm trees to build bridges, for instance — that seizure is legitimate because the government has the authority to determine property rights, and what they take is considered rightfully theirs. We therefore use those bridges without compunction. But when the government instructs the Jew to pay, that instruction creates no independent *halachic* obligation; its force derives from whatever *halachic* obligation exists toward non-Jews independently.

### Seized Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא. אָמַר רָבָא: תִּדַּע, דְּקָטְלִי דִּיקְלֵי וְגָשְׁרִי גִּישְׁרֵי – וְעָבְרִינַן עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates to **the** matter of civil law **itself. Shmuel says: The law of the kingdom is the law,** and the halakhic principle is that Jews must obey the laws of the state in which they reside. **Rava said: Know** that this principle is true from the fact **that** the municipal authorities **cut down palm trees** without the consent of their owners **and construct bridges** from them, **and** yet **we cross over them.** Evidently, the wood is not considered stolen property, which one is prohibited from using, because the law of the kingdom is the law.

The proof that *dina demalchuta dina* genuinely confers legitimate property rights — not merely that owners become *mityaiesh* — is derived from *Rava*'s observation: *d'katlei diklei ve'gashri gishrei ve'avrinan aleihu* — the government cuts down people's palm trees and builds bridges with them, and we use those bridges. If we considered the seized property to be *gezel*, we would have no right to benefit from those bridges. The fact that we do use them demonstrates that the government's seizure is not *gezel* but a legitimate exercise of its authority to determine property ownership.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְדִלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיַּיאוּשׁ לְהוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ מָרַיְיהוּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי לָא דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא, הֵיכִי מִיָּיאֲשִׁי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **Perhaps** the reason the bridges may be used is **because their owners despaired of retrieving them** and not because the law of the kingdom is the law. Rava **said to** Abaye: **If not** for the fact that **the law of the kingdom is the law, how** would the **despair** of the owners of the trees allow us to use the bridges? The fact that the owners have despaired of retrieving their wood does not effect a transfer of property, and it therefore still belongs to them.

*Abaye* challenges: perhaps we use the bridges simply because the owners are *mityaiesh* — they have given up hope of recovering their trees — and *ye'ush* alone suffices? *Rava* responds: *i lav dina demalchuta dina, heichi miyash?* — if *dina demalchuta dina* were not operative, what kind of *ye'ush* would this even be? *Rashi* clarifies: *ye'ush* alone is not sufficient; one needs *ye'ush ve'shinui reshus*, and in this case there is no *shinui reshus* — the bridges are in a public domain, the palm trees are unchanged in identity, and there has been no real change of domain or form. The legitimacy of our using the bridges must therefore rest on *dina demalchuta dina* itself.

### Tax Discretion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא קָא עָבְדִי כִּדְאָמַר מַלְכָּא – מַלְכָּא אָמַר: זִילוּ וּקְטֻלוּ מִכֹּל בָּאגֵי, וְאִינְהוּ אָזְלוּ וְקָטְלוּ מֵחַד בָּאגָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rava's understanding: **But** the municipal authorities **do not act as the king said. The king said: Go and cut** down a bit of wood **from all the valleys** in the area so that each individual loses only a small amount of wood. **They,** however, disobey the king and **go and cut** down all the wood needed for the bridge **from one valley.** Therefore, even if the law of the kingdom is the law, this cannot be the reason that the *halakha* permits Jews to cross over such bridges, as the authorities are not enforcing the law of the kingdom, but rather their own unlawful inclinations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלוּחָא דְמַלְכָּא – כְּמַלְכָּא, וְלָא טָרַח; וְאִינְהוּ אַפְסִיד אַנַּפְשַׁיְיהוּ – דְּאִיבְּעִי לְהוּ לְמִינְקַט מִכּוּלֵּיהּ בָּאגֵי, וּמִשְׁקַל דְּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **An agent of a king** is **like the king** himself, and he is **not** expected to **trouble** himself to collect wood proportionally from each valley. **They,** the owners of the land where the wood is cut, **cause themselves a loss, as they should collect** compensation **from all** the other residents **of the valleys and take money** from them for this purpose. Since the land owners whose wood was used have permission to collect compensation from all the residents in the area, the authorities are acting within their rights by confiscating wood from a single location.

The *gemara* then addresses a complication: do tax collectors actually follow the king's directives? It notes that the king may instruct them to take a proportional amount from every copse (*baga*) of palm trees in the region, but the collectors — being lazy — instead take everything from one patch. Have they exceeded their authority and thus stepped outside *dina demalchuta*?

The *gemara* rules: *shlucha d'malka k'malka ve'lo tarach* — an agent of the king is like the king himself and has discretion not to shoulder the administrative burden of equal distribution. Moreover, *v'inhu afsidu anafshaihu* — the villagers bear some responsibility for the outcome themselves. They had the option to pool their resources, gather the appropriate total, and present it to the collector, who would then distribute the burden fairly among them. If they failed to organize themselves in this way, knowing how collectors operate, they cannot fully blame the collector for the result. The collector is thus acting within the parameters of his authorized discretion, and the seizure remains within *dina demalchuta*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן דְּמִשְׁתְּכַח בְּבֵי דָרֵי, פָּרַע מְנָתָא דְּמַלְכָּא. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי שׁוּתָּפָא, אֲבָל אֲרִיסָא – אֲרִיסוּתֵיהּ הוּא דְּקָא מַפֵּיק.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rava says:** When the king's agents come to collect the king's share of the grain from a field owned by several partners, the **one who is found in the granary** must **pay the king's share** for the entire property, as the agents are not expected to locate and exact payment from each individual proprietor separately. The partner who paid may later claim reimbursement from the other owners for covering their share of the tax. **And this statement** applies only to **partners** who share ownership of the field. **But a sharecropper collects his portion** from the crop but does not own a share of the land. Consequently, the tax may not be collected from his produce, and doing so would constitute robbery.

A related ruling from *Rava*: if a man is found at the threshing floor when the tax collector arrives, and the collector seizes from him alone the full tax owed across all the grain stored there — effectively making him pay other people's debts — that too is within the tax collector's discretion (*para manta d'malka*). The others whose grain was present must then reimburse him, but the collector's act is valid. This applies, however, only if the person at the threshing floor is a partner in the grain. If he is merely a sharecropper (*arisa*), who has no ownership stake in the grain, it would not be within the collector's authority to impose the full tax obligation upon him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: בַּר מָתָא אַבַּר מָתָא מִיעֲבָט. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּבֹרָלָא אַרְעָא וּכְרָגָא דְּהַאי שַׁתָּא, אֲבָל שַׁתָּא דַּחֲלֵיף – הוֹאִיל וְאִפַּיַּיס מַלְכָּא, חֲלֵיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** also **says:** Property that belongs to **a town dweller may be taken as security for** the tax owed by another **town dweller. And this statement applies** only **to the property tax and head tax of that year, but** with regard to taxes from **the previous year, since the king has** already **been appeased,** the ability to take property belonging to someone else has **passed.** The tax collector has already paid the entire amount he must pay the king for the previous year, and everything else he collects is his own profit. Although he has the right to collect this extra amount, he may not take property for one person as security for the taxes of someone else.

Finally, the *gemara* addresses the question of current versus back taxes. A tax collector has discretion to seize property from one person temporarily to pressure another person to pay — even using an uninvolved party's assets as leverage. This applies to current-year obligations (*dubla d'bar l'ara d'charga d'hai shata*). But for back taxes (*shata d'chalif*), the discretion is reduced: *ho'il u'fayeis malka* — since the king was already appeased with whatever was collected for the prior year, and that matter is settled, the collector's discretionary powers to apply unusual pressure are correspondingly diminished.

## Gezel Goy (IV) (II)

### Judging Mixed Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּמוֹכֵס גּוֹי. דְּתַנְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי שֶׁבָּאוּ לַדִּין, אִם אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְזַכּוֹתוֹ בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל – זַכֵּהוּ, וֶאֱמוֹר לוֹ: כָּךְ דִּינֵינוּ. בְּדִינֵי גּוֹיִם – זַכֵּהוּ וֶאֱמוֹר לוֹ: כָּךְ דִּינְכֶם. וְאִם לָאו, בָּאִין עָלָיו בַּעֲקִיפִין; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין בָּאִין עָלָיו בַּעֲקִיפִין, מִפְּנֵי קִידּוּשׁ הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The mishna issues its ruling **with regard to a gentile customs collector,** whom one may deceive, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a Jew and a gentile who approach** the court **for judgment** in a legal dispute, **if you can vindicate** the Jew **under Jewish law, vindicate him, and say to** the gentile: **This is our law.** If he can be vindicated **under gentile law, vindicate him, and say to** the gentile: **This is your law. And if** it is **not** possible to vindicate him under either system of law, **one approaches** the case **circuitously,** seeking a justification to vindicate the Jew. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva** disagrees and **says: One does not approach** the case **circuitously** in order to vindicate the Jew **due to the sanctification of God's name,** as God's name will be desecrated if the Jewish judge employs dishonest means.

A *braita* addresses the case of a Jew and a non-Jew coming to *beis din* for judgment. *Rabbi Yishmael* rules: if you can make the Jew win by applying Jewish law, apply Jewish law and tell the non-Jew "this is our law." If you can make the Jew win by applying non-Jewish law, apply non-Jewish law and tell him "this is your law." And if neither system produces a ruling in the Jew's favor, *ba'in elav be'akifin* — find some indirect device to ensure the Jew wins.

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees with the third option: *ein ba'in elav be'akifin mipnei kiddush Hashem* — you may not use deceptive devices, because if discovered, the result will be *chilul Hashem* rather than *kiddush Hashem*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגֶזֶל גּוֹי מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּבַר זֶה דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כְּשֶׁבָּא מִזְּפִירִין: מִנַּיִן לְגֶזֶל גּוֹי שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אַחֲרֵי נִמְכַּר, גְּאֻלָּה תִּהְיֶה לּוֹ" –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this assertion: **But is robbery** from **a gentile permitted? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said** that **Rabbi Akiva taught this matter when he came from Zephirin: From where** is it derived that it is **prohibited** to **rob a gentile?** It is derived from a verse, **as it states** with regard to a Jew who has been sold as a slave to a gentile: **"After he is sold he may be redeemed"** (Leviticus 25:48),

The *gemara* notes the implication of *Rabbi Akiva*'s reasoning: his hesitation is *solely* the concern for *kiddush Hashem*. Where that concern would not be present — *heicha de-leka kiddush Hashem* — he would presumably permit the use of such devices. This implies that there is no independent *gezel goy* problem with the underlying result, since the Jewish party may in fact genuinely owe the money.

The *gemara* immediately presses back: can one really say that stealing from a non-Jew is permitted? A *braita* records that *Rabbi Akiva* himself expounded, upon returning from Zafirin: *minayin le-gezel goy she-hu assur* — from where do we know that stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden? From the verse *acharei nimkar geulah tihyeh lo* — after a Jewish slave is sold, he shall have a right of redemption. The verse requires that we calculate the proper redemption price and pay it honestly to the non-Jewish master, even though there is a *mitzvah* to redeem the Jewish slave. We may not simply take him or deceive the master; we must buy his freedom at fair value. This demonstrates that *gezel goy* is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא יִמְשְׁכֶנּוּ וְיֵצֵא. יָכוֹל יִגְלוֹם עָלָיו? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְחִשַּׁב עִם קֹנֵהוּ" – יְדַקְדֵּק עִם קוֹנֵהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** indicating **that** one should **not take** a Hebrew slave by force **and** thereby allow him to **leave** the gentile's jurisdiction. Rather, the Jew must be freed by legal means. **One might have thought** that it is permitted to **deceive him** in order to free the Jew. **Therefore, the verse states: "And he shall reckon with him that bought him"** (Leviticus 25:50), in order to teach that one must **be precise** in the financial dealings **with** the **purchaser** of a Hebrew slave, and one must pay him the appropriate sum without employing any form of deception. This indicates that it is prohibited to steal from a gentile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא, בְּגוֹי, הָא בְּגֵר תּוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Yosef said:** It is **not difficult,** as **this** ruling that permits the court to deceive a gentile is issued **with regard to** a regular **gentile,** whereas **that** verse, which teaches that it is prohibited to deceive a gentile, is stated **with regard to a gentile who resides in Eretz Yisrael and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*].**

*Rav Yosef* attempts to resolve the apparent contradiction within *Rabbi Akiva*'s positions by distinguishing between ordinary non-Jews and a *ger toshav* — a non-Jew who observes the seven Noahide laws and dwells among the Jewish community. He suggests that stealing from a *ger toshav* is forbidden, while from an ordinary non-Jew it may be permitted. *Abaye* objects: the very verse about redeeming the Jewish slave speaks about both categories together, making it impossible to confine the prohibition to *ger toshav* alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי כְּתִיבִי: לֹא לְךָ, אֶלָּא לְגֵר – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לְגֵר". וְלֹא לְגֵר צֶדֶק, אֶלָּא לְגֵר תּוֹשָׁב – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לְגֵר תּוֹשָׁב".
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: How is it possible to differentiate between a gentile and a *ger toshav*? **Aren't both of them written next to each other,** indicating that the same *halakha* pertains to both? As it is taught in a *baraita*: One who violates the prohibitions of the Sabbatical Year will be punished by having to resort to selling himself as a slave. And he will sell himself **not to you, but to a stranger, as it is stated:** "And sell himself unto **the stranger"** (Leviticus 25:47), **and not to a stranger** who is a convert, **but to a *ger toshav*, as it is stated:** "And sell himself unto **the stranger who is a settler [*ger toshav*]** with you" (Leviticus 25:47).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "מִשְׁפַּחַת גֵּר" – זֶה הַגּוֹי; כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: "אוֹ לְעֵקֶר" – זֶה הַנִּמְכָּר לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues and states: "Or to the offshoot of a stranger's family." When it says **"a stranger's family," this** is referring to **the gentile** family members of a *ger toshav*, who are idolaters. **When it says "or to the offshoot," this** is referring to a Jew **who is sold to idol worship,** i.e., to work in a temple dedicated to idolatry. Since it is subsequently stated: "He shall reckon with him that bought him" (Leviticus 25:50), it is apparent that this reckoning applies equally to each of the above, including the gentile. This contradicts Rav Yosef's answer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בִּגְזֵילוֹ, וְכָאן בְּהַפְקָעַת הַלְווֹאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** It is **not difficult** because **here,** in the case of the slave, the *halakha* is stated **with regard to** an actual act of **robbery** committed against a gentile, **but there,** in the case of the *baraita*, where it would be permitted to employ deception if not for the desecration of God's name, the *halakha* is stated **with regard to abrogating his loan.** Abrogating a loan owed to a gentile is permitted because it does not entail actually taking money.

*Rava* therefore offers the resolution that becomes the definitive *halacha*: *lo kashya — kan be-gezel kan be-hafkakat halvato* — there is no contradiction. Direct theft (*gezel*) from a non-Jew is absolutely forbidden. But passive non-payment of a debt (*hafkakat halvato*) — failing to repay money owed — is permitted when the creditor is a non-Jew. Maneuvering in court to avoid paying a non-Jewish creditor what the Jew genuinely owes him falls into the second category, not the first; that is why *Rabbi Akiva* could countenance such a result in principle. The actual taking of the Jewish slave would, by contrast, be active *gezel*, since *Rava* holds *eved ivri gufo kanuy* — the owner has a form of property right in the person of the Jewish slave — and removing him forcibly would be directly stealing something in which the non-Jew has property rights.

This distinction — *gezel goy assur*, *hafkakat halvato mutar* — is the *halacha*. Its combination with the earlier discussion of *dina demalchuta dina* gives rise to conclusions that are, to put it plainly, ethically challenging in the context of modern civil obligations such as paying taxes. The *gemara*'s own margins, as printed, contain glosses that immediately attempt to contextualize and limit the application of these principles. One gloss explains that the rulings applied to pagans who treated others' property as *hefker*; another invokes the standard of one who is *kamocha* — like unto you — arguing that non-Jews who maintain just legal systems and respect property rights have the status of *ger toshav* and are not subject to these distinctions. These glosses reflect both the moral discomfort felt by later authorities and, at times, concerns about how the text would appear to non-Jewish readers.

There are serious *poskim* throughout the generations — not only contemporary ones — who have argued that in a society of law-abiding citizens, where property rights are respected and enforced, the general presumption should be that non-Jews have the status of *ger toshav* for purposes of monetary *halachot*, and these distinctions accordingly do not apply. *Rav Moshe Feinstein*'s position, referenced earlier, operates on this basic premise. What remains clearly true, and genuinely troubling, is that these passages have at times been invoked to justify dishonest monetary conduct toward non-Jews — precisely the abuse the *gemara*'s own emphasis on *chilul Hashem* was meant to foreclose.

### Stealing vs. Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי – הַפְקָעַת הַלְווֹאָתוֹ הוּא! רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי גּוּפוֹ קָנוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: The release of **a Hebrew slave** from his gentile master **is** akin to the **abrogation of his loan.** The purchase price paid by the master is considered as a loan that the slave pays back over the years of his servitude until he goes free at the Jubilee. Consequently, deceptively bringing about his early release is akin to abrogating a loan, yet Rabbi Akiva derives from the verse that it is prohibited to do so. The Gemara answers that **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava says: The body of a Hebrew slave is owned** by his master, and retaking him from the gentile by deceptive means would therefore constitute actual robbery.

### Lost Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: גֶּזֶל גּוֹי אָסוּר, אֲבֵידָתוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת. גְּזֵילוֹ אָסוּר – דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִנַּיִן לְגֶזֶל הַגּוֹי שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְאָכַלְתָּ אֶת כׇּל הָעַמִּים אֲשֶׁר ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ נֹתֵן לָךְ" – בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מְסוּרִים בְּיָדְךָ, וְלֹא בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינָם מְסוּרִין בְּיָדְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another statement related to stealing from a gentile. **Rav Beivai bar Giddel says** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida says:** It is **prohibited** to **rob a gentile,** but it is **permitted** to retain **his lost item,** i.e., one is not required to return it to him. The Gemara examines the basis for each of these rulings: It is **prohibited** to **rob a gentile, as Rav Huna says: From where** is it derived **that** it is **prohibited** to **rob a gentile?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And you shall consume all the peoples that the Lord your God shall deliver unto you"** (Deuteronomy 7:16), indicating that it is permitted to consume the other nations' property only **when they are delivered into your hand,** i.e., in times of war, **but not when they are not delivered into your hand.**

*Rav Bibi bar Gidel* states in the name of *Rav Shimon Chasida*: *gezel goy assur* — stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden. *Aveidato muteres* — keeping his lost object is permitted. One need not return a lost object belonging to a non-Jew, as derived from the verse *le-chol aveidas achicha* — to all lost objects of your *brother* — implying the obligation extends only to a fellow Jew.

*Rav Huna* reinforces the prohibition on active theft from the verse *ve'achalta es kol ha'amim asher Hashem Elokecha nosein lach* — you shall consume all the nations God gives you — from which is derived: *bezman she'heim mesurim beyadecha* — only when they are delivered into your hand, i.e., when conquered in war, does this apply, *ve'lo bezman she'einam mesurim beyadecha* — but not otherwise, and certainly not through ordinary theft.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבֵידָתוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת – דְּאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לַאֲבֵידַת הַגּוֹי שֶׁהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לְכׇל אֲבֵדַת אָחִיךָ" – לְאָחִיךָ אַתָּה מַחְזִיר, וְאִי אַתָּה מַחְזִיר לְגוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** It is **permitted** to retain **his lost item, as Rav Ḥama bar Gurya says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived that it is **permitted** to retain **the lost item of a gentile?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** with regard to the mitzva of returning a lost item: **"With every lost thing of your brother's"** (Deuteronomy 22:3), indicating that it is only **to your brother** that **you return** a lost item, **but you do not return** a lost item **to a gentile.**

*Rav Chama bar Gurya* derives the permissibility of keeping a non-Jew's lost object from the verse itself: *le'achicha atah machzir ve'ee atah machzir le-goy* — you return to your brother but not to a non-Jew. One might ask: is this genuinely a less serious omission than active theft, given that there is a concrete physical object in one's possession belonging to another? The *gemara* does not address this directly, but in every case, *chilul Hashem* overrides the permissive baseline: *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* rules that wherever *chilul Hashem* is at stake, even keeping a non-Jew's lost object is forbidden — *afilu aveidato assur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא אֲתַי לִידֵיהּ, דְּלָא מִחַיַּיב לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי בָּתְרַהּ; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵיהּ, אֵימָא לַיהְדְּרַהּ! אָמַר רָבִינָא: "וּמְצָאתָהּ" – דַּאֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ מַשְׁמַע. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר אוֹמֵר: בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ חִילּוּל הַשֵּׁם, אֲפִילּוּ אֲבֵידָתוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this derivation: **But say** that **this applies** only **where** the item **has not yet come into** the Jew's **hand, as he is not obligated to pursue it** in an effort to find the lost item and return it. **But** in a case **where the item had** already **come into his hand, say** that he must **return it** to the gentile. The Gemara answers that **Ravina said:** It is understood from the verse itself, as it states: "And so shall you do with every lost thing of your brother's, which he has lost, **and you have found"** (Deuteronomy 22:3), which **indicates** that the verse refers even to an item **that** has already **come into one's hand.** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Pineḥas ben Ya'ir says: In a case where there is** a concern that retention of an article lost by a gentile will result in the **desecration of** God's **name,** it is **prohibited even** to retain a gentile's **lost item.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: טָעוּתוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת. כִּי הָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל זְבַן מִגּוֹי לָקָנָא דְּדַהֲבָא בְּמַר דְּפַרְזְלָא – בְּאַרְבַּע זוּזֵי, וְאַבְלַע לֵיהּ חַד זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Shmuel says** that it is **permitted** to financially benefit from a business **error** of a gentile, i.e., it need not be returned. The Gemara notes that this is **like that** incident **where Shmuel purchased a golden bowl [*lakna*] from a gentile in exchange [*bemar*] for** the price **of an iron** bowl, which was **four dinars, and** Shmuel **included one** additional **dinar** in the payment so that the gentile would not realize his mistake.

*Shmuel* adds: *ta'uto muteres* — if a non-Jew makes a mistake in a business transaction that benefits the Jew, the Jew need not point it out. Again, this is subject to the *chilul Hashem* constraint. *Shmuel* himself is then cited as an example: he purchased what he knew to be a gold vessel from a non-Jew who believed it was copper, paying the copper price, and also apparently paid slightly less than even the agreed sum. His protection against *chilul Hashem* was his declaration to the seller: *chazi dela chasmina* — "look, I am trusting you in all of this" — thereby putting the burden of care on the seller and insulating himself from the charge of deception if the error were later discovered. *Rav Kahana* similarly purchased 120 barrels at the price of 100, also declaring *chazi dela chasmina*. In both cases the declaration of trust served to transfer responsibility to the seller and preempt any subsequent *chilul Hashem*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא זְבַן מִגּוֹי מְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים חָבְיָתָא בִּמְאָה, וְאַבְלַע לֵיהּ חַד זוּזָא; אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי, דַּעֲלָךְ קָא סָמֵיכְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **Rav Kahana purchased one hundred and twenty barrels from a gentile for** the price of **one hundred** barrels, **and he included one** additional **dinar** in the payment. Rav Kahana **said to him: Take note that I am relying upon you** to check that the transaction has been carried out properly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא זְבַן דִּיקְלָא הוּא וְגוֹי לְצַלָּחָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: קְדֵם וְאַיְיתִי מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, דְּגוֹי מִנְיָינָא יָדַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a third episode: **Ravina and a gentile purchased a palm tree** together in order **to chop it up** and split the wood between them. Ravina **said to his attendant: Hurry and precede** the gentile so that you can bring my share of the wood **from the trunk** of the tree, which is thicker than the upper part of the tree, **as the gentile knows** only the **number** of logs that he is due to receive and will not realize that you are taking thicker pieces.

The pattern of these cases — while consistent with the *halachic* framework of *hafkakat halvato* — is admittedly ethically uncomfortable in ways the *gemara* itself seems to acknowledge through the *chilul Hashem* constraint it imposes on every one of these permissions. The marginal glosses attempting to limit these rulings to an older, more hostile social context reflect a genuine awareness, throughout the tradition, that these principles require careful handling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל בְּאוֹרְחָא, חֲזָא שִׁיבְשָׁא דְגוּפְנָא בְּפַרְדֵּיסָא, וּתְלוּ בַּהּ קִיטּוּפֵי דְּעִינְבֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: זִיל חֲזִי, אִי דְּגוֹי נִינְהוּ – אַיְיתִי. אִי דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל נִינְהוּ – לָא אַיְיתִי לִי. שְׁמַע הָהוּא גּוֹי דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּפַרְדֵּיסָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּגוֹי שְׁרֵי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גּוֹי שָׁקֵיל דְּמֵי, יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא שָׁקֵיל דְּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a final anecdote: **Rav Ashi was traveling on the road** and **he saw a branch of a grapevine in an orchard, and** there were **clusters of grapes hanging on it. He said to his attendant: Go see** to whom these clusters belong. **If they are** owned by **a gentile, bring** some to me, but **if they are** owned by **a Jew, do not bring me** any. **A certain gentile who was sitting in the orchard overheard** Rav Ashi's instructions. The gentile **said to him:** Is it **permitted** to steal the property **of a gentile?** Rav Ashi **said to him: A gentile takes money** for his grapes, and I intended to pay for them, but **a Jew does not take money** for his grapes and I did not want to take them without paying for them.

*Tosafos* takes this at face value: the dominant position of this entire *gemara* is that *gezel goy* is *assur*, and if *Rav Ashi* believed otherwise the *gemara* would have made that clear. *Tosafos* therefore concludes that he genuinely intended to pay.

## Non-Jewish Courts (II)

### Volunteer Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרֵיז רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב הוּנָא: דְּסָלְקִין לְעֵילָּא וּדְנָחֲתִין לְתַתָּא – הַאי בַּר יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּיָדַע סָהֲדוּתָא דְגוֹי וְלֹא תְּבָעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ, וְאָזֵיל וּמַסְהֵיד לֵיהּ בְּדִינָא דְגוֹיִם עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל חַבְרֵיהּ – מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאִינְהוּ מַפְּקִי מָמוֹנָא
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the discussion of legal dealings between Jews and gentiles, the Gemara relates: **Rava declared, and some say** that it was **Rav Huna** who declared: All **who ascend upward** to Eretz Yisrael **and** all who **descend downward** to Babylonia agree that in the case of **a Jew who knows** of **evidence concerning** the legal claim of **a gentile, and** the gentile **did not demand from him** that he testify, **and** the Jew nevertheless **went and testified for him in a gentile court, against his fellow Jew, we excommunicate him. What is the reason** that we excommunicate him? It is **because they,** the gentile courts, **expropriate money**

*Rava* — or in some versions *Rav Huna* — announced publicly, directed at those traveling between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Bavel*: a Jew who knows testimony that would support a non-Jew's case against a fellow Jew — *v'lo tva'u minei* (according to the textual reading that includes this phrase) and has not been summoned to testify — who nevertheless goes and testifies in a non-Jewish court on behalf of the non-Jew against the Jew, *meshamtinan lei* — is placed in *cherem*.

The reason is that non-Jewish courts of the *magista* type (the local village court, per *Rashi*) will extract money on the basis of a single witness — *de'inhu mafik mamona apuma de-chad* — whereas Jewish law requires two witnesses before money is extracted, and a single witness can at most impose an oath. By volunteering testimony in such a court, the Jew enables a monetary extraction that Jewish procedure would not permit. The *halacha* thus limits this to unsummoned testimony in courts that do not follow the two-witness standard. If the witness is one of two (so that the non-Jew would prevail in Jewish court as well), or if the court follows the same standards as a Jewish court with respect to single witnesses, testifying is not prohibited.

### Single Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַפּוּמָּא דְחַד. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא חַד, אֲבָל בִּתְרֵי לָא. וְחַד נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּדִינֵי דְמָגִיסְתָּא, אֲבָל בֵּי דַוּוֹאר – אִינְהוּ נָמֵי חַד אַמּוֹמְתָא שָׁדוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** based **on the word of one** witness, which is insufficient evidence according to Jewish law. **And we said** that this is so **only** when **one** individual testifies alone against his fellow Jew, **but when two** witnesses testify against a Jew, we do **not** excommunicate them, as their testimony is sufficient evidence according to Jewish law as well, and they have not caused the defendant any unjustified financial loss even according to *halakha*. **And** in a case of **a single** witness **also, we said** that we excommunicate him **only** if he testified **in a court of villagers [*demagista*], but** if he testified in **the** official government **courthouse [*bei davar*],** he is not excommunicated. This is because **they also prescribe an oath to** the defendant based on the testimony of **a single** witness, but they do not expropriate money, in accordance with Jewish law.

The *gemara* rules that where a witness would be one of two — such that the non-Jew would prevail even in *beis din* — there is no prohibition against testifying in a non-Jewish court if summoned. Similarly, where the non-Jewish court in question follows similar evidentiary standards to Jewish procedure — imposing an oath rather than extracting money on the strength of a single witness — testimony is also permissible.

### Prominent Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינָא בֵּי רַב הוּנָא, אִיבַּעְיָא לַן: אָדָם חָשׁוּב דְּסָמְכִי עֲלֵיהּ כְּבֵי תְרֵי – מַפְּקִי מָמוֹנָא אַפּוּמֵּיהּ, וְלָא אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאַסְהוֹדֵי; אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאָדָם חָשׁוּב הוּא – לָא מָצֵי מִשְׁתְּמִיט לְהוּ, וּמָצֵי לְאַסְהוֹדֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Ashi said: When I was in the academy of Rav Huna,** the following **dilemma was raised before us:** What is the *halakha* with regard to an **important person,** whose testimony **is relied upon** by the gentile courts **as** if it were the testimony of **two** witnesses? Since the gentile court will **expropriate money** based **on his word,** should the *halakha* be that **he should not testify? Or perhaps, since he is an important person, he cannot escape** the authorities who demand his testimony, **and he may** therefore **testify.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Rav Ashi* reports a question that arose in the *beis din* of *Rav Kahana*: what about an *adam chashuv* — a prominent person — who is a single witness, but whose word carries such weight that the non-Jewish court will rely on his testimony alone and extract money, as if he were two witnesses? On one hand, because of his prominence he functions effectively as two witnesses, and therefore testifying would enable an extraction that Jewish procedure would not permit from a single witness. On the other hand, *lo matu mishtamit lehu* — a person of such standing cannot avoid appearing when summoned; he would face serious consequences for failing to appear, consequences that a less prominent person might not face. The question remains unresolved: *teiku*.

## Land to Non-Jews

### Excommunication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַאי בַּר יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ אַרְעָא לְגוֹי אַמִּצְרָא דְּבַר יִשְׂרָאֵל חַבְרֵיהּ – מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי נֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא, וְהָאָמַר מָר: זְבַן מִגּוֹי, וְזַבֵּין לְגוֹי, לֵיכָּא מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another situation where a Jew is excommunicated for causing harm to another Jew. **Rav Ashi said:** In the case of **a Jewish man who sells a gentile** a plot of **land** that is **on the border of** the property of **his fellow Jew, we excommunicate him. What is the reason? If we say** it is **because** he has ignored **the right of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor to be the first one offered the purchase of the field, **but doesn't the Master say:** With regard to **one who purchases** land **from a gentile, and one who sells** land **to a gentile, there is no right of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor to be the first one offered the purchase of the field?

*Rav Ashi* rules: a Jew who sells a plot of land to a non-Jew, where that land abuts the property of a Jewish neighbor, is placed in *cherem* — *meshamtinan lei*. The potential basis one might suggest is *dina de-bar mitzra* — the right of first refusal that a neighboring landowner has when adjacent property is sold. However, the *gemara* dismisses that as the operative reason, since the law of *dina de-bar mitzra* does not generally apply to transactions involving non-Jews.

### Neighbor Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: אַרְבַּעְתְּ לִי אַרְיָא אַמִּצְרַאי. מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ עַד דְּקַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ כֹּל אוּנְסָא דְּאָתֵי מֵחֲמָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **because we say to him** on behalf of the owner of the adjacent field: **You have placed a lion,** i.e., a dangerous individual, **on my border,** as the gentile might now cause me harm. Consequently, **we excommunicate him until he accepts upon himself** responsibility for **all harm that comes** upon the neighbor **due to** the gentile's activities.

The actual reason for the *cherem* is different: by selling to a non-Jewish neighbor, the seller has effectively placed a threat next to the Jewish neighbor's property — *arveiti aryeh amitzrei* — "you have set a lion at my border." The assumption underlying this ruling reflects the broader social context: non-Jewish neighbors were perceived as likely to encroach on land, steal crops, and cause harm in ways a Jewish neighbor would not. The seller is therefore held in *cherem* until he accepts personal financial liability — *ad dekableh kol onsa de'ati machmateh* — for any harm or loss that results from the non-Jew's proximity to the Jewish neighbor's land. The laws of *dina de-bar metzra* proper remain a separate topic, treated extensively in *Bava Batra*.
