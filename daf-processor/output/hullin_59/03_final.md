# Hullin 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Notzah/Down of Birds

### R. Yehuda & Notzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם נִיטְּלָה הַנּוֹצָה – פְּסוּלָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: הַנּוֹצָה מִצְטָרֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **Rabbi Yehuda says: If the down** covering its body **was removed,** it is a *tereifa* and **unfit** for consumption, like an animal whose hide was removed. With regard to this, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yishmael said the same thing. Rabbi Yehuda** said **that which we said** here, that the removal of the feathers is like removal of the hide. **Rabbi Yishmael** agrees, **as we learned** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael says: The down joins** the flesh to constitute an olive-bulk for purposes of *piggul*.

The daf actually begins a few lines before the *mishnah* on 58b. There the Gemara records: *rabbi yehuda omer im nitlah hanotzah pesulah* — Rabbi Yehuda said that if the down of a bird is missing, it is *pesulah*. This appears in the context of things that are not ordinarily considered *treifah* defects. The term *pesulah* here is understood to mean *treifah* — that missing down, according to Rabbi Yehuda, renders the bird a *treifah*. The Gemara notes that this position was contested, and the story of Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta, who performed an experiment to refute Rabbi Yehuda, is cited in this connection.

### R. Yochanan's Comparison


Rabbi Yochanan draws a comparison: *rabbi yehuda v'rabbi yishmael amru davar echad* — Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yishmael essentially said the same thing with respect to the *notzah* of birds. Rabbi Yehuda's position, just stated, is that the *notzah* is necessary and its absence creates a *treifah* condition. Rabbi Yishmael's parallel view appears in a *mishnah* in *taharos*: *rabbi yishmael omer hanotzah mitztarefet* — the down combines. The question is: combines for what?

### Rashi on Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דִּילְמָא לָא הִיא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָכָא אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן טְרֵפָה, דְּלֵיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּמַגֵּין עֲלֵיהּ, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן אִיפַּגּוֹלֵי – כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָתָם אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן אִיפַּגּוֹלֵי, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן טְרֵפָה – אַגּוֹנֵי לָא מַגֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Perhaps it is not so,** and they disagree. Perhaps **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the down is like the hide of an animal **here only with regard to a *tereifa*, since** when the down is removed, **there is nothing that protects** the bird, and its life is in danger; **but with regard to *piggul*, he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that the down is not treated like the hide of an animal, because *piggul* applies only if one had intent to consume an item that is normally consumed. **And** perhaps **Rabbi Yishmael says** so **there only with regard to *piggul*,** since he holds that the down is in fact fit for consumption; **but with regard to a *tereifa*,** he holds that the down **does not protect** the bird, and its removal does not endanger it.

Rashi understands this *mishnah* in *taharos* as referring to *pigul*: if a *kohen* intends to eat the meat of a bird *korban* together with its down, the down is treated as flesh, and such intent constitutes *pigul*. This reading parallels Rabbi Yehuda's position in the sense that both treat the down as functionally analogous to flesh. Rashi further connects this to the issue of a *gluda* — a skinned cow — which was discussed at the *mishnah* earlier. Just as skin is compared to flesh in the context of *pigul* (though not perfectly equivalent, since skin itself does not create *pigul*), so too missing down is analogous to a removed skin. This is where Rashi makes the comparison: if down is considered like flesh in this limited sense, its removal is akin to the animal having been stripped of its skin.

### Tosafot: Shomer


Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's reading. The *mishnah* in *taharos*, Tosafot argues, is not about *pigul* at all but about the laws of *tumah* — specifically, the minimum size requirement of *k'beitzah* for food to become *tamei*. If one has meat from a bird that does not amount to a full *k'beitzah*, can the down combine with the meat to reach that minimum? Tosafot notes that under the laws of *tumah*, a *shomer* — a protective element such as a nutshell around a nut — can combine with the food it protects to reach the required measure. Rabbi Yishmael is therefore saying that the down qualifies as a *shomer* for the flesh, protecting it, and may thus combine with the meat to reach the required size. The down cannot count on its own; it must be protecting actual flesh, and it is the meat that must constitute the majority. On this reading, the comparison to Rabbi Yehuda's position about *treifah* is more intuitive: in both cases, the down is not itself flesh, but it performs a necessary protective function for the flesh.

### Rava's Resolution


Rava then asks whether Rabbi Yochanan's comparison is actually sound: *dilma lo* — perhaps they are not saying the same thing at all. *ad kan lo ka'amar rabbi yehuda hacha ela l'inyan treifa d'leika midi d'magein alah* — Rabbi Yehuda only applies his position in the context of *treifah*, where without the down nothing is protecting the living bird. But when it comes to *pigul*, he may agree with the rabbis that the down is not considered meat. Conversely, *v'ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Yishmael hatam ela l'inyan pigulei* — Rabbi Yishmael only says his position regarding *pigul*, that the down is considered edible like meat. But when it comes to *treifah*, he might say *agonei lo magnei* — it does not protect — and therefore would not render the bird a *treifah*.

This is Rashi's version of Rava's answer. On Rashi's reading, the distinction is clear: being like meat (*pigul*) is a different question from whether the down protects (*treifah*), and the two cannot be compared. According to Tosafot, however, the *mishnah* in *taharos* is already about *shomer* — protection — which seems to make it a closer parallel to the *treifah* discussion. Tosafot's answer is that the concept of *shomer* in *tumah* law refers to something that protects the food *now*, after slaughter, as it lies there as an edible item. Rabbi Yehuda's position about *treifah*, by contrast, concerns the down's role in protecting the *living* bird. The Gemara's resolution is therefore that what serves to protect the animal while alive is not necessarily the same as what constitutes a *shomer* for the meat once the animal has been slaughtered. Hence the two cases remain distinguishable even on Tosafot's reading.

It is worth noting that Rashi's text apparently read *pigulei* at a key juncture in the Gemara — indeed, Rashi himself writes *pigulei garsinan*, signaling either that he is affirming his own version against an alternate text or that he actively emended the reading. Tosafot clearly worked with a different text, and the two *rishonim* are therefore not only offering different analyses but operating from different versions of the Gemara.

## Fatal Non-Treifah

### Final Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֲחוּזַת הַדָּם, וְהַמְעוּשֶּׁנֶת, וְהַמְצוּנֶּנֶת, וְשֶׁאָכְלָה הַרְדּוּפְנֵי, וְשֶׁאָכְלָה צוֹאַת תַּרְנְגוֹלִים, אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁתָת מַיִם הָרָעִים – כְּשֵׁרָה. אָכְלָה סַם הַמָּוֶת, אוֹ שֶׁהִכִּישָׁהּ נָחָשׁ – מוּתֶּרֶת מִשּׁוּם טְרֵפָה, וַאֲסוּרָה מִשּׁוּם סַכָּנַת נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an animal that is **congested with** excess **blood, or that** was **smoked,** i.e., that suffered from smoke inhalation, **or that** was **chilled** and subsequently became sick, **or that ate oleander,** which is poisonous, **or that ate the excrement of chickens, or that drank foul water,** although in all these cases the animal is in danger, it is **kosher.** By contrast, if the animal **ate deadly poison, or** if **a snake bit** the animal, **with regard to** the prohibition of ***tereifa*,** consumption of the animal would be **permitted, but** it is **prohibited due to the threat to** one's **life** if he eats it.

The final *mishnah* of this entire section addresses a point that has underlain the discussion throughout: *treifah* does not simply mean that an animal will die. An animal can die from old age or illness without being a *treifah*. The category of *treifah* is specifically tied to a fatal type of wounding or injury, and this *mishnah* draws the distinction explicitly.

### Fatal Conditions List


The *mishnah* enumerates several conditions that may prove fatal to an animal yet do not constitute a *treifah*: *achuzas ha-dam* — a blood clot; *ha-me'ushenes* — inhalation of smoke; *ha-metzunenah* — chills or frostbite; eating *hordufni* — some type of plant that burns through the inner organs; eating *tzoas tarnegolim* — chicken dung, presumably a concern about parasites or bacteria; and drinking *mayim ra'im* — bad water. All of these can lead to the animal's death, but they involve no wound or injury. Therefore, in all these cases, the animal remains *keshera* — it is not a *treifah*.

### Poison & Snake Bite


The *mishnah* then contrasts these with two further cases. If the animal ate *sam ha-maves* — a deadly poison — or was bitten by a snake, it is *muteres mishum treifah* — the animal is not rendered a *treifah* on account of these events, since again there is no wound of the *treifah* type. Nevertheless, *ve'asurah mishum sakanat nefashot* — it is forbidden to eat such an animal because of the health risk to humans. The poison may remain in the meat and endanger whoever consumes it.

This raises an interesting implicit distinction. The earlier cases — bad water, chicken dung, and the like — are apparently not considered a danger to humans who eat the animal, only to the animal itself. The *mishnah* seems to assume that when an animal consumes something harmful to itself, a human who eats that animal will not be poisoned. The snake bite and *sam ha-maves* cases are different: there the poison can remain in the flesh and endanger the human consumer directly. This juxtaposition is striking when placed alongside the Talmudic concern about *mayim megulim* — exposed water — which is always treated as a potential snake-poison risk. The *mishnah* here apparently holds that water contaminated through means other than direct snake contact, even if it sickens or kills the animal, does not constitute a human health risk.

## Chiltit & Spices

### Shmuel on Chiltit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הִלְעִיטָהּ חִלְתִּית – טְרֵפָה, מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּמְינַקְּבָה לְהוּ לְמַעְיָינַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says:** If one **fed** an animal **asafoetida,** a very sharp plant, it is **a *tereifa*. What is the reason? Since it perforates its intestines.**

The Gemara now proceeds to analyze specific items from the *mishnah*'s discussion of fatal conditions. Shmuel teaches: *hilitah chiltit treifah* — if an animal swallowed *chiltit* (asafoetida), it is a *treifah*. The reasoning is *d'minkevu lehu lemayeha* — because it burns holes in the animal's inner organs. This is a significant ruling: unlike the other items in the *mishnah*'s list, *chiltit* does not merely cause illness and death but actually creates an internal wound, which is precisely the type of injury that generates *treifah* status. The spice is understood to be so acrid that it perforates the intestines.

### Rav Shizbi's Kashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: אֲחוּזַת הַדָּם, וְהַמְעוּשֶּׁנֶת, וְשֶׁאָכְלָה הַרְדּוּפְנֵי, וְשֶׁאָכְלָה צוֹאַת תַּרְנְגוֹלִים, וְשָׁתָת מַיִם הָרָעִים, הִלְעִיטָהּ תִּיעָה, חִלְתִּית וּפִלְפְּלִין, אָכְלָה סַם הַמָּוֶת – כְּשֵׁרָה. הִכִּישָׁהּ נָחָשׁ, אוֹ שֶׁנְּשָׁכָהּ כֶּלֶב שׁוֹטֶה – מוּתֶּרֶת מִשּׁוּם טְרֵפָה, וַאֲסוּרָה מִשּׁוּם סַכָּנַת נְפָשׁוֹת. קַשְׁיָא חִלְתִּית אַחִלְתִּית, קַשְׁיָא סַם הַמָּוֶת אַסַּם הַמָּוֶת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizvi raises an objection** from a *baraita*: An animal that is **congested with** excess **blood, or that** suffered from **smoke** inhalation, **or that ate** poisonous **oleander, or that ate the excrement of roosters, or that drank foul water,** or if **one fed it *tia*, asafoetida, or peppers,** or if it **ate deadly poison,** it is **kosher.** But if **a snake bit** the animal, **or if a mad dog bit it, with regard to** the prohibition of ***tereifa*** its consumption is **permitted, but** it is nevertheless **prohibited due to the threat to** one's **life.** The statement of the *baraita* concerning an animal that was fed **asafoetida** poses **a difficulty to** Shmuel's statement that **asafoetida** renders the animal a *tereifa*, and the statement concerning **deadly poison** poses **a difficulty to** the mishna's statement that **deadly poison** renders the animal prohibited due to the threat to one's life.

Rav Shizbi challenges Shmuel from a *beraita* that elaborates on the *mishnah*. The *beraita* lists: blood clots, smoke inhalation, eating *hordufni*, eating chicken dung, drinking bad water — and then adds: *ve'hilitah ti'ah chiltit ve'pilpelin* — and if it swallowed *ti'ah*, *chiltit*, and peppers (presumably hot peppers). It then adds: *o sam ha-maves* — or if it ate poison. In all these cases: *kesherah* — it is kosher. It may die, but it is not a *treifah*. The *beraita* explicitly lists *chiltit* among the non-*treifah* fatal conditions, directly contradicting Shmuel's ruling that *chiltit* creates a *treifah*.

The *beraita* then continues: *hikisha nachash o she'nashcha kelev shoteh* — if the animal was bitten by a snake or by a mad dog, it is not a *treifah* but is forbidden because of *sakanat nefashot*. The question of whether rabies can be transmitted through inadequately cooked meat is genuinely unresolved, and that concern underlies the health-risk prohibition here.

The Gemara therefore identifies a double contradiction: *kashya chiltit achiltit* — there is a conflict regarding *chiltit*, since Shmuel renders it a *treifah* while the *beraita* does not; and *kashya sam ha-maves asam ha-maves* — there is also an apparent conflict regarding poison, since the *mishnah* treats it as a health risk while the *beraita* appears to permit the animal.

### Leaves vs. Seeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִלְתִּית אַחִלְתִּית לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּעָלִין, כָּאן בִּקְרָטִין. סַם הַמָּוֶת אַסַּם הַמָּוֶת לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּידַהּ, הָא דְּאָדָם. סַם הַמָּוֶת דִּבְהֵמָה הַיְינוּ הַרְדּוּפְנֵי? תְּרֵי גַּוְונֵי סַם הַמָּוֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The apparent contradiction between one statement about **asafoetida** and the other statement about **asafoetida** is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* is referring **to** a case where the animal ate asafoetida **leaves,** which are less dangerous. **There,** Shmuel is referring **to** a case where the animal ate **slivers** of asafoetida, which are very sharp. Likewise, the apparent contradiction between one statement about **deadly poison** and the other statement about **deadly poison** is **not difficult. This** statement of the *baraita* that it is permitted is referring to a case where the animal consumed poison that is deadly only **to itself. That** statement of the mishna that it is prohibited is referring to a case where it consumed poison deadly **to a person.** The Gemara asks: **Poison deadly to an animal is** the same as **oleander;** why should the *baraita* mention both? The Gemara responds: The *tanna* is referring to **two types of deadly poison.**

The resolution of the *chiltit* contradiction is: *kan be'alin kan bekartin* — in one case we are speaking of the leaves, and in the other of the seeds. The leaves of *chiltit* may be harmful and ultimately fatal, but they do not perforate the organs and therefore do not create a *treifah* condition. The seeds, however, are so intensely concentrated that they burn through the intestinal walls, creating an actual hole — and it is the seeds that Shmuel has in mind when he rules *treifah*. The analogy to chili peppers is instructive: the flesh of the pepper has a certain sharpness, but the seeds are capable of causing searing burns on contact.

### Animal vs. Human Poison


The contradiction regarding *sam ha-maves* is resolved differently: *ha deidah ha de'adam* — in one case the animal consumed a poison harmful to animals, while in the other it consumed a poison harmful to humans. Certain substances are toxic to animals but pose no health risk to humans who eat the animal's flesh. In such a case, the animal is not a *treifah* and may even be eaten. But if the animal consumed something that is a human poison, then while it still does not create a *treifah* condition, eating the meat is forbidden on account of the health risk. The Gemara then notes: *sam ha-maves debehemah haynu herdufni* — animal poison is essentially the *hordufni* already mentioned in the *mishnah*. The Gemara therefore clarifies that the *beraita*'s reference to *sam ha-maves* in the context of permitted animals denotes animal-specific poisons, of which *hordufni* is one type and other substances are others: *trei gavnei sam ha-maves* — there are multiple categories of animal poison.

### Tea (Tiah) ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תִּיעָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: עִיקָּרָא דְּמָרִירְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The above *baraita* teaches that if an animal was fed *tia*, it is kosher. The Gemara asks: **What is *tia*? Rav Yehuda said:** It is **the root of a bitter** vegetable.

The Gemara asks: *mai ti'ah* — what is this *ti'ah* listed among the harmful substances? Rav Yehudah says it is *ikra demarirta* — the root of *marirta* (or, in some versions, *maridta*), some species of bitter herb. Rashi identifies it as the root of *torah*, though the precise botanical identification remains uncertain.

## Dangerous Foods Tales

### Three Measures Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דַּאֲכַל תְּלָתָא תִּקְלֵי חִלְתִּית אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא – מִישְׁתְּלַח מַשְׁכֵיהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּדִידִי הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וַאֲכַלִי חַד תִּקְלָא חִלְתִּיתָא, וְאִי לָא דִּיתַבִי בְּמַיָּא – מִישְׁתְּלַח מַשְׁכַאי, וְקִיַּימְתִּי בְּעַצְמִי ״הַחׇכְמָה תְּחַיֶּה בְעָלֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: This** individual **who eats** the weight of **three shekel of asafoetida on an empty heart,** i.e., stomach, **his skin sheds** due to the fever he contracts. **Rabbi Abbahu said: There was an incident in which I** was involved, wherein **I ate** the weight of **one shekel of asafoetida, and had I not** immediately **sat in water** to cool off, **my skin would have shed. And I** thereby **fulfilled with regard to myself** that which the verse states: **"Wisdom preserves the life of him that has it"** (Ecclesiastes 7:12).

Rav Yehudah issues a cautionary ruling: *hai man de'achal tlata taklei chiltit aliba reikanya* — anyone who eats three measures of *chiltit* seeds on an empty stomach, *mishtalach mashchei* — will suffer to the point that his skin falls off. The physiological premise is consistent with the Gemara's earlier understanding that this substance perforates inner organs.

### Rav Avahu's Experience


Rav Avahu reports a personal experience: *uvedidi havah uvda* — this happened to me. He consumed only a single measure of *chiltit*, and *ilu dela yatvi bemaya, mishtalach mashchai* — had he not immediately immersed himself in water, his skin would have come off. He applies to himself the verse *hachochmah techayeh be'aleha* — wisdom keeps its master alive: he may not have been wise enough to avoid eating it in the first place, but he was wise enough to know the remedy.

### Rav Yosef on Sterility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי מַאן דְּאָכֵיל שִׁיתַּסְרֵי בֵּיעֵי, וְאַרְבְּעֵי אַמְגּוֹזֵי, וְשַׁב בּוּטְיָתָא דְּפִרְחֵי, וְשָׁתֵי רְבִיעֵתָא דְּדוּבְשָׁא בִּתְקוּפַת תַּמּוּז, אַלִּיבָּא רֵיקָנָא – מִתְעֲקַר תַּלְיָא דְּלִיבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: This** individual **who eats sixteen eggs and forty nuts and seven fruits of the caper bush, and he drinks a quarter-*log* of honey in the season of Tammuz,** i.e., summer, all **on an empty heart,** i.e., stomach, **his heartstrings are uprooted.**

Rav Yosef teaches: *hai man de'achal shitsar beyei ve'arba'in guzei vesheva butita defarchi veshati revi'ta deduvsha bitkufas Tamuz aliba reikana* — one who eats sixteen eggs, forty nuts, seven caper berry seeds, and drinks a *revi'it* of honey, all in a single meal, on an empty stomach, in the summer — *mitakar taya d'liba* — the membranes or sinews around the heart become uprooted, which apparently can cause some form of cardiac trauma or dislodgement.

### Reish Galuta Deer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בַּר טַבְיָא דַּאֲתָא לְבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, דַּהֲוָה (מפסק) [מְפַסְּקָן] כַּרְעִן בָּתְרָיָיתָא, בַּדְקֵיהּ רַב בְּצוֹמֶת הַגִּידִין וְאַכְשְׁרֵיהּ. סְבַר לְמֵיכַל מִינֵּיהּ בְּאוּמְצָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל: לָא חָיֵישׁ מָר לְנִיקּוּרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that if an animal is bitten by a poisonous snake, it is not a *tereifa*, but it is nevertheless prohibited for consumption due to the hazard it poses. The Gemara recounts the case of **a certain young deer that was brought to the house of the Exilarch** after slaughter **whose hind legs had been cut. Rav inspected it at the convergence of sinews** in the thigh and found them intact, **and he deemed it kosher. He thought to eat it rare,** i.e., lightly roasted. **Shmuel said to him: Is the Master not concerned for** the possibility that it may have **a snakebite?**

The Gemara now introduces a narrative: *hahu bar tavia d'ata l'vei Reish Galusa* — a certain deer that was brought to the house of the *reish galuta*, the political head of the Jewish community in Babylonia — *d'hava nifsak karaia basraisa* — whose rear legs were cut or broken. The precise nature of the injury is left somewhat open: it was not necessarily a complete amputation, and the subsequent inspection makes clear that the concern centered on the area of the *tzomas ha-gidin*, the nexus of sinews at the lower knee joint.

### Rav's Inspection


Rav examined the *tzomas ha-gidin* and found it intact — the sinews had not been severed at that critical juncture, and the injury appeared to be below that point. He therefore declared the animal *keshera*, ruling it fit. *savar l'meichal minei b'umtza* — he then proposed to eat from the meat immediately, either raw or very lightly prepared. This in itself is telling: eating from an animal one has just *pasken*ed on demonstrates genuine confidence in the ruling.

### Shmuel's Snake Bite Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי תַּקַּנְתָּא? נוֹתְבֵיהּ בְּתַנּוּרָא דְּאִיהוּ בָּדֵיק נַפְשֵׁיהּ. אוֹתְבֵיהּ, נְפַל תִּילְחֵי תִּילְחֵי. קָרֵי שְׁמוּאֵל עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב: ״לֹא יְאֻנֶּה לַצַּדִּיק כׇּל אָוֶן״. קָרֵי רַב עֲלֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: ״כׇּל רָז לָא אָנֵס לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to him: What is the rectification** for such an uncertainty? Shmuel said to him: **We shall set it in a** hot **oven, as it will** then **inspect itself.** Shmuel **set it** in the oven on a spit, and the meat **fell** off the bone **bit by bit,** a sign that a snake had bitten the young deer. **Shmuel recited about Rav** the verse: **"There shall no mischief befall the righteous"** (Proverbs 12:21), since he was saved due to his righteousness. **Rav recited about Shmuel** the verse: "And **no secret causes you trouble"** (Daniel 4:6), since he was learned even with regard to such matters.

At this point Shmuel intervened: *lo chaish mar l'neshichusa* — is my master not concerned about snakebite? His point was that the cut in the leg might not have resulted from a physical blow or amputation but from the bite of a snake, in which case venom could have been injected into the flesh. This is not a *treifah* concern — the organs were not perforated, and the *drusa* category (clawing by a predatory animal) does not apply to biting — but it does implicate the prohibition of *sakanat nefashot*. Rav asked: *mai takansa* — what should be done? Shmuel replied: *naspeiya b'sanura* — put it in an oven. Cook it thoroughly. *d'ihu badik nafshiya* — it will test itself; the result of the baking will reveal whether poison is present.

It is worth pausing on why snake bite does not constitute *drusa*. The category of *drusa* — which does render an animal a *treifah* — is the clawing of a predatory animal with presumed injection of venom through the tearing of the outer skin. The defining feature of *drusa* appears to be the clawing action itself. A snake bite, by contrast, transfers venom through a puncture but without clawing, and it therefore falls outside the *drusa* category. If the bite actually perforated an inner organ, it would create a *treifah* through that separate mechanism. But a bite that penetrates only the outer flesh creates no *treifah* condition — the health risk alone remains.

### Oven Test Result


The meat was placed in the oven: *nafal tilchi tilchi* — it fell to pieces. The disintegration of the flesh confirmed that poison had indeed been present in the meat. Rav was thereby saved from eating it.

### Rav & Shmuel Praise


*Kari Shmuel alei d'Rav* — Shmuel said of Rav: *lo ye'uneh l'tzaddik kol aven* — no harm shall befall the righteous. The providential timing — that Shmuel happened to be present at precisely the moment Rav was about to eat — was understood as divine protection. *kari rav alei d'shmuel* — Rav then reciprocated, saying of Shmuel: *kol raz lo anis lach* — no secret is hidden from you. Shmuel had both the foresight to consider the possibility of poison and the practical knowledge of how to test for it. The mutual praise between these two great contemporaries also illuminates the mode of address: Rav had called Shmuel *mar*, a term used between colleagues, not merely a student addressing a master.

With this narrative the entire *treifos* section draws to a close. The daf has traced *treifos* of *behemos*, *treifos* of birds, and the category of conditions that are fatal but not *treifah*. The final *mishnah* and its Gemara culminate in the principle that *treifah* is defined by the nature of the injury, not merely by whether the animal will die — and that some conditions, even lethal ones, fall entirely outside that category while nonetheless triggering a health-risk prohibition of their own.

## Kosher Species ID

### Simanim Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סִימָנֵי בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה נֶאֶמְרוּ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וְסִימָנֵי הָעוֹף לֹא נֶאֶמְרוּ, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: כׇּל עוֹף הַדּוֹרֵס – טָמֵא, כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אֶצְבַּע יְתֵירָה וְזֶפֶק וְקוּרְקְבָנוֹ נִקְלָף – טָהוֹר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: כׇּל עוֹף הַחוֹלֵק אֶת רַגְלָיו – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The signs** that indicate that **a domesticated animal and an undomesticated animal** are kosher **were stated in the Torah, and the signs of** a kosher **bird were not** explicitly **stated. But the Sages stated** certain signs in a bird: **Any bird that claws** its prey and eats it is **non-kosher. Any** bird **that has an extra digit** behind the leg slightly elevated above the other digits, **and a crop,** which is a sack alongside the gullet in which food is stored prior to digestion, **and** for which the yellowish membrane inside **its gizzard can be peeled,** is **kosher. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says: Any bird that splits** the digits of **its feet** when standing on a string, placing two digits on one side of the string and two on the other, is **non-kosher.**

Having concluded the discussion of *treifos*, the Gemara now transitions to a *mishnah* that in some ways precedes all the previous material conceptually: before one can inquire into whether an animal is *treifah*, one must first establish that it belongs to a *kosher* species. The *mishnah* opens: *simanim behema vechaya ne'emru min hatorah* — the identifying characteristics of domesticated and wild mammals are given explicitly in the Torah (split hooves and cud-chewing). *vesimane ha'of lo ne'emru batorah* — but the Torah provided no such characteristics for kosher birds; it simply listed the non-kosher species. This creates a significant practical problem: since the identities of the non-kosher birds are no longer known with certainty, and since no positive *simanim* are given, the permissibility of any unfamiliar bird depends entirely on received tradition.

### Kosher Bird Signs


The *mishnah* records: *aval amru chachamim kol of ha-dores tamei* — the Sages declared that any predatory bird is non-kosher. The precise definition of *dores* — predatory — is a matter of dispute among the *rishonim*. Three interpretations are offered: that the bird swoops and seizes its prey; that it holds its prey with one foot while tearing at it with the other; and that it eats from its prey while the prey is still alive. The shared core is that the bird attacks living creatures rather than foraging. Note that the word *dores* is the same root as *drisa*, the clawing that generates *treifah* status — the connection is not incidental.

By contrast, the *mishnah* provides three positive *simanim* for a kosher bird: *kol sheyesh bo etzba yeteira vezefek vekorkavano niklaf tahor* — any bird that has an extra finger, a crop, and a gizzard whose inner lining can be peeled is kosher. What "extra finger" means is itself debated: Rashi identifies it as the opposable rear talon — the small thumb-like protrusion at the back of the foot — while others hold it refers to the elongated middle finger that is disproportionate to the others.

This raises the broader question of whether possessing these three signs is *sufficient* to establish a bird as kosher, or whether the absence of the predatory character (*dores*) is also independently required. This question bears directly on the permissibility of turkeys and other birds for which there is no longstanding tradition — a matter the Gemara will address.

### Split Fingers Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַחֲגָבִים, כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע רַגְלַיִם, וְאַרְבַּע כְּנָפַיִם, וְקַרְצוּלַּיִם, וּכְנָפָיו חוֹפִין אֶת רוּבּוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: וּשְׁמוֹ חָגָב. וּבַדָּגִים, כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ סְנַפִּיר וְקַשְׁקֶשֶׁת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי קַשְׂקַשִּׂין וּסְנַפִּיר אֶחָד. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן קַשְׂקַשִּׂין – הַקְּבוּעִין בּוֹ, וּסְנַפִּירִים – הַפּוֹרֵחַ בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to grasshoppers,** whose signs were also not stated in the Torah, the Sages stated: **Any** grasshopper **that has four legs, and four wings, and** two additional **jumping legs, and whose wings cover most of its** body, is kosher. **Rabbi Yosei says: And** this applies only if the **name** of **its** species is **grasshopper. And with regard to fish,** the signs are explicitly stated in the Torah: **Any** fish **that has a fin and a scale** is kosher; **Rabbi Yehuda says: Two scales and one fin. And these are scales:** Those **that** are **fixed to its** body; **and fins** are those **with which** the fish **swims.**

Rabbi Eliezer ben Tzadok adds a negative indicator from the bird's perspective: *kol of ha-holek et raglav tamei* — any bird that splits its toes evenly — two in front and two behind — is non-kosher. This configuration characterizes birds of prey, which require the bilateral grip to seize and hold their prey. The normal kosher bird configuration, by contrast, has three toes forward and one behind.

### Grasshoppers & Fish


The *mishnah* then turns to other species. For grasshoppers: *kol sheyesh lo arba raglayim ve'arba knafayim ve'chartzulayim veknafav hofin et rubo* — four legs, four wings, jumper legs, and wings that cover the majority of the body's length. Rabbi Yossi adds the requirement that it be called a *chagav* — that it belong to the tradition-recognized category of *chagav* — which introduces the same dependency on transmitted tradition that applies to birds.

For fish: *kol sheyesh lo snapir vekaskeset* — fins and scales. Rabbi Yehudah specifies a minimum of two scales and one fin, though what species might possess only those minimum quantities is unclear.

## Derived Simanim

### Cud & Upper Teeth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵלּוּ הֵן סִימָנֵי בְּהֵמָה – ״כׇּל בְּהֵמָה מַפְרֶסֶת פַּרְסָה וְגוֹ׳״, כׇּל בְּהֵמָה שֶׁמַּעֲלַת גֵּרָה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁאֵין לָהּ שִׁינַּיִם לְמַעְלָה, וּטְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that the signs of a kosher domesticated animal are stated in the Torah. With regard to this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **These are the signs of a** kosher **domesticated animal: "Whatsoever parts the hoof,** and is wholly cloven-footed, and chews the cud, among the animals, that may you eat" (Leviticus 11:3). **Any animal that chews the cud certainly has no upper** front **teeth,** i.e., incisors, **and is kosher.**

The Gemara introduces a *beraita*: *kol behemah shema'alah gerah, beyadu'a she'ein lah shinayim lema'alah, vetehorah* — any animal that chews its cud, you may know that it has no upper front teeth, and it is kosher. The observation is empirically striking: ruminants lack upper incisors, as one can readily see by looking at a cow, which has lower front teeth but a hard upper palate where front teeth would otherwise be. The claim seems to imply a shortcut: if you observe an animal without upper front teeth, you know it is a ruminant, and if it is a ruminant, it is kosher.

### Camel Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְלָלָא הוּא? וַהֲרֵי גָּמָל, דְּמַעֲלֵה גֵרָה הוּא, וְאֵין לוֹ שִׁינַּיִם לְמַעְלָה, וְטָמֵא! גָּמָל נִיבֵי אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** this an established **principle? But** isn't there **a camel, which chews the cud, and has no upper** front **teeth, and** it is still **non-kosher** (see Leviticus 11:4)? The Gemara responds: **A camel has cuspid**-like upper incisors, one on each side.

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge: *vaharei gamal, dema'aleh gerah hu, ve'ein lo shinayim lema'alah, vetamei* — what about the camel, which chews its cud, and seemingly lacks upper front teeth, yet is not kosher? The Gemara answers: *gamal nivei is lei* — the camel does in fact have those long front teeth, sometimes called fangs or incisors — which is why it is non-kosher. Rashi notes that horses similarly have prominent upper front teeth.

### Statement Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי בֶּן גָּמָל, דְּנִיבֵי נָמֵי לֵית לֵיהּ, וְתוּ, הֲרֵי שָׁפָן וְאַרְנֶבֶת דְּמַעֲלַת גֵּרָה הֵן וְיֵשׁ לָהֶן שִׁינַּיִם לְמַעְלָה, וּטְמֵאִין! וְעוֹד, שִׁינַּיִם מִי כְּתִיבִי בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל בְּהֵמָה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ שִׁינַּיִם לְמַעְלָה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא מַעֲלַת גֵּרָה וּמַפְרֶסֶת פַּרְסָה, וּטְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there **a young camel, which does not even have cuspid**-like upper incisors and is still non-kosher? **And furthermore,** aren't there the **hyrax and** the **hare, which chew the cud, and** yet **they have upper** front **teeth, and are non-kosher? And furthermore,** one might ask: **Are teeth written in the Torah** with regard to the kosher status of an animal? **Rather, this** is what the *tanna* **is saying: Any animal that does not have upper** front **teeth certainly chews the cud and parts the hoof and is kosher.**

The Gemara now recognizes that the original statement was imprecise and inverts it. The statement was not meant to say that cud-chewing tells you about upper teeth. The point is precisely the opposite: *kol behemah she'ein lah shinayim lema'alah, beyadu'a shehi ma'alat gerah umafriset parsah* — any animal lacking upper front teeth, you may know that it chews its cud *and* has split hooves. This is a *siman* that works as a shortcut to the Torah's two criteria: one need only examine the front upper teeth. Since animals without upper front teeth are ruminants with split hooves, the absence of those teeth is sufficient to establish kosher status — subject to knowing the exceptions.

The Gemara also addresses the objection from *shafan* and *arnevet* (the hyrax and the hare), which the Torah lists as chewing their cud yet having no split hooves. These animals, the Gemara notes, do have upper front teeth — consistent with the revised formulation that no upper teeth signals both cud-chewing *and* split hooves. One might note parenthetically that modern zoology questions whether these animals are true ruminants in the technical sense, but the Gemara takes their cud-chewing as given.

### Young Camel Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיבְדּוֹק בְּפַרְסוֹתֶיהָ! כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ חֲתוּכוֹת, וְכִדְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּמִּדְבָּר וּמָצָא בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ חֲתוּכוֹת – בּוֹדֵק בְּפִיהָ, אִם אֵין לָהּ שִׁינַּיִם לְמַעְלָה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה, אִם לָאו – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּכִּיר גָּמָל. גָּמָל נִיבֵי אִית לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּכִּיר בֶּן גָּמָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why should one inspect the teeth? **Let him** simply **inspect** whether **the hooves** are cloven. The Gemara responds: One inspects the teeth in a case **where its hooves were cut** and one cannot tell whether they are cloven. **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda says:** If one **was walking in the wilderness, and he found an animal whose hooves were cut, he may inspect its mouth. If it has no upper** front **teeth, it is certainly kosher; if** that is **not** the case, **it is certainly non-kosher, provided that he recognizes** that this animal is not **a camel,** which is non-kosher even though it has no upper incisors. The Gemara asks: Why must one recognize that this is not **a camel?** A camel **has cuspid**-like upper incisors. **Rather,** say: **Provided that one recognizes** that it is not **a young camel,** which has no cuspid-like upper incisors.

The *ben gamal* — the young camel — presents a genuine difficulty. A young camel has not yet developed those characteristic incisors. It therefore lacks upper front teeth but is not kosher. This is the single exception to the rule. The Gemara responds by invoking the *beraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *v'es hagamal ki ma'aleh gerah hu* — the Torah specifies the camel precisely because it is the *only* cud-chewing animal that does not have split hooves (along with the other species enumerated there: hyrax, hare, and the few others). *shalit b'olamo yodeia she'ein lecha davar shema'aleh gerah vetamei ela gamal* — the Master of the Universe knows that the only cud-chewing non-kosher animals are those explicitly listed. Since those are the only exceptions, and since within that category the only animal whose young would lack upper incisors is the camel, the sole exception one must know in applying the teeth test is the young camel. As long as you can confirm the animal is not a young camel, the absence of upper front teeth is a reliable indicator of kosher status.

### Rav Chisda Scenario


Rav Chisda formalizes this as a practical ruling: *hayah mehalech b'midbar umatz'a behemah sheparsosav hatuchot* — you are traveling in the wilderness, you find an unknown animal, and its hooves have been severed. You cannot apply the Torah's primary *siman*. What do you do? *bodek b'piv* — examine its mouth. *im ein lo shinayim lema'alah, biyadu'a shehi tehorah* — if it has no upper front teeth, it is kosher. *im lav, biyadu'a shehi temei'ah* — otherwise, it is non-kosher. *u'vilvad sheyakir ben gamal* — provided you can identify a young camel and confirm the animal is not one.

This principle is theoretically elegant, though one might ask whether it actually holds empirically across all mammalian species. Rabbi Natan Slifkin, among others, has examined this question and noted that the Torah's lists are not necessarily exhaustive — there are in fact ruminants without split hooves beyond those the Torah enumerates. The Gemara's claim, premised on the *d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* tradition, asserts divine authority for the completeness of those lists; the scientific question remains separate.

### Split Hooves Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וּמָצָא בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ גָּמוּם, בּוֹדֵק בְּפַרְסוֹתֶיהָ: אִם פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ סְדוּקוֹת – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה, אִם לָאו – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּכִּיר חֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says:** If one **was walking on the road, and he found an animal whose mouth was mutilated, he may inspect its hooves. If its hooves are cloven, it is certainly kosher. If not, it is certainly non-kosher.** This applies **provided that he recognizes** that it is not **a pig,** which is non-kosher even though it has cloven hooves.

When the animal's teeth cannot be examined — its mouth has been damaged or removed — Rav Chisda provides the next level of the inquiry: *badik b'parsoteha* — examine its hooves. *im parsoteha sedukot, biyadu'a she'hi tehorah* — if split hooves, it is kosher. *u'vilvad sheyakir chazir* — provided you can recognize a pig.

### Pig Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אָמְרַתְּ אִיכָּא חֲזִיר? אִיכָּא נָמֵי מִינָא אַחֲרִינָא דְּדָמְיָא לַחֲזִיר! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְאֶת הַחֲזִיר כִּי מַפְרִיס פַּרְסָה הוּא״ – שַׁלִּיט בְּעוֹלָמוֹ יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁמַּפְרִיס פַּרְסָה וְטָמֵא אֶלָּא חֲזִיר, לְפִיכָךְ פָּרַט בּוֹ הַכָּתוּב ״הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **didn't you say that there is a pig,** which has cloven hooves but is still non-kosher? If so, perhaps **there is also another species that is similar to a pig.** The Gemara responds that this possibility should **not enter your mind, as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The verse states: **"And the pig, because it parts the hoof,** and is cloven-footed, but chews not the cud, it is unclean" (Leviticus 11:7). **The Ruler of His world knows that nothing other than** the **pig parts the hoof and is** still **non-kosher. Therefore, the verse singles it out** with the word **"it."**

Here again the school of Rabbi Yishmael is invoked: *v'es hachazir ki mafris parsa hu* — the Torah specifies the pig because it is the *only* animal with split hooves that does not chew its cud. *shalit b'olamo yodeia she'ein lecha davar shemafris parsa vetamei ela chazir* — the Master of the Universe knows that the pig is the unique exception. This assertion — that the pig is the *only* split-hooved non-ruminant — is in fact supported by modern zoology, unlike the parallel claim about cud-chewers.

### Meat Grain Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּמִּדְבָּר, וּמָצָא בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ גָּמוּם וּפַרְסוֹתֶיהָ חֲתוּכוֹת – בּוֹדֵק בִּבְשָׂרָהּ, אִם מְהַלֵּךְ שְׁתִי וָעֵרֶב – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה, וְאִם לָאו – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּכִּיר עַרְוָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says:** If one **was walking in the wilderness, and he found an animal whose mouth was mutilated and whose hooves were cut, he may inspect its flesh. If it runs** like **warp and woof,** i.e., part of it stretches vertically and part of it horizontally, **it is certainly kosher; and if not, it is certainly non-kosher, provided that one recognizes** that this animal is not **a wild donkey,** which is non-kosher even though its flesh runs like warp and woof.

Rav Chisda provides a final tier of the analysis, for the extreme case where neither teeth nor hooves are available: *badik bivshara* — examine the flesh itself. *im mehalech sheti va'erev, biyadu'a she'hi tehorah* — if the grain of the meat runs in two directions (as one who cuts against the grain in cooking would discover), it is kosher. The musculature of kosher animals runs in multiple directions, creating the characteristic cross-grain pattern.

### Wild Donkey Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אָמְרַתְּ אִיכָּא עַרְוָד? אִיכָּא נָמֵי מִינָא אַחֲרִינָא דְּדָמֵיא לְעַרְוָד! גְּמִירִי דְּלֵיכָּא. וְהֵיכָא בּוֹדֵק? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּכַנְפֵי הָעוּקֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't you say that there is a wild donkey,** whose flesh runs like warp and woof but who is still non-kosher? If so, perhaps **there is also another species that is similar to a wild donkey.** The Gemara responds: **It is learned** as a tradition **that there are no** similar species. The Gemara asks: **And where does one inspect** the flesh? **Abaye said, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda** said: **At the edges of the tailbone,** on the upper thighs.

As before, the Gemara raises the concern: once you admit there is a single exception — the *arod* (wild donkey), whose flesh apparently runs in one direction despite being non-kosher — are there not other potential exceptions? The Gemara responds: *gemiri* — we have a tradition — that the wild donkey is the *only* exception to the meat-grain rule. Unlike the previous two rules, which had scriptural backing through the *d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* tradition, this one rests on received tradition alone, since the direction of meat grain is not a Torah *siman* at all. *avaye* adds that the place to examine this is toward the rear flanks of the animal, where the directional pattern is most pronounced.

## Chaya vs. Behema

### Chaya/Behema Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימָנֵי חַיָּה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵלּוּ הֵן סִימָנֵי חַיָּה. חַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה הִיא לְסִימָנִין! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לְהַתִּיר חֶלְבָּהּ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵלּוּ הֵן סִימָנֵי חַיָּה שֶׁחֶלְבָּהּ מוּתָּר – כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ קַרְנַיִם וּטְלָפַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **The signs of an undomesticated animal** were stated in the Torah. With regard to this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **These are the signs of an undomesticated animal.** The Gemara interjects: Why does one need signs for an undomesticated animal? **An undomesticated animal [*ḥayya*] is included** in the category of **a domesticated animal [*behema*] with regard to signs,** as the verse states: "These are the living things [*ḥayya*] which you may eat among all the animals [*behema*] that are on the earth. Whatsoever parts the hoof" (Leviticus 11:2–3). **Rabbi Zeira said:** The signs are given **to permit its** forbidden **fat,** i.e., to deem the animal undomesticated. Fat that is forbidden for consumption in domesticated animals is permitted in undomesticated animals. **And this** is what the *tanna* **is saying: These are the signs of a** kosher **undomesticated animal,** so that one will know **that its fat is permitted: Any** animal **that has horns and** cloven **hooves** is an undomesticated animal.

The *mishnah* had stated that *simanim* for both *behema* and *chaya* are given by the Torah. The Gemara asks: why are we now discussing *simanim* that distinguish *chaya* from *behema*? For *kashrus* purposes, both are governed by the same criteria. The Gemara explains: *chaya bekhlal behema hi lesimanim* — true, for *kashrus* they are equivalent, but the distinction matters for two other halachos. First, the fat (*chelev*) of a *chaya* is permitted, while that of a *behema* is forbidden. Second, the blood of a *chaya* requires covering (*kisui ha-dam*). Therefore, the *simanim* here are not about *kashrus* but about identifying something as *chaya lehattir chelvah* — a wild animal whose fat is permitted.

### Horns & Hooves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ לָהּ קַרְנַיִם – אִי אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לַחֲזוֹר עַל טְלָפַיִם, יֵשׁ לָהּ טְלָפַיִם – צָרִיךְ אַתָּה לַחֲזוֹר עַל קַרְנַיִם, וְקֶרֶשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא קֶרֶן אַחַת – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Dosa says:** If **it has horns,** it is certainly a kosher undomesticated animal, and **you need not search for** cloven **hooves.** But if **it has** cloven **hooves, you must** still **search for horns. And** with regard to the animal called **a *keresh*, even though it has only one horn,** its fat **is permitted** for consumption.

The *beraita* states: *kol sheyesh lah karnayim utlafim* — any animal with horns and hooves is a *chaya*. Rabbi Dosa qualifies this: *yesh lah karnayim, i attah tzarikh lachzor al hatlafiym* — if it has horns, you need not check the hooves; *yesh lah tlafim, tzarikh attah lachzor lekarnayim* — if it has hooves alone, you must also check the horns. For Rabbi Dosa, the horns are the primary indicator.

Why mention hooves at all? According to Rashi, the reference to hooves in the *beraita* serves not to distinguish *chaya* from *behema* but to exclude non-kosher wild animals — like bears — that lack hooves entirely. An animal must have kosher-type hooves to be a valid *chaya*. Rabbeinu Tam offers a different reading: "hooves" here refers specifically to the *chaya*-type hoof — long and slender, like a deer's, as opposed to the broad, compact hoof of a cow. On this reading, the distinctive hoof shape is itself a marker of *chaya* status. Either way, the Gemara's focus in what follows is almost entirely on the horn characteristics.

### Goat Counterexample

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלָא הוּא? וַהֲרֵי עֵז, דְּיֵשׁ לוֹ קַרְנַיִם וּטְלָפַיִם, וְחֶלְבּוֹ אָסוּר! כְּרוּכוֹת בָּעֵינַן. וַהֲרֵי שׁוֹר, דִּכְרוּכוֹת, וְחֶלְבּוֹ אָסוּר! חֲרוּקוֹת בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is** this an established **principle? But** isn't there **a goat, which has horns and** cloven **hooves, and** still **its fat is forbidden,** as it is a domesticated animal? The Gemara responds: **We require** horns that are **layered** to designate a kosher animal as undomesticated; a goat's horns are not layered. The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there **a bull, which** has **layered** horns, **and** still **its fat is forbidden** since it is domesticated? The Gemara responds: **We require horns** that are **grooved;** a bull's horns are not grooved.

The Gemara challenges the sufficiency of horns: *vaharei ez deyesh lo karnayim utlafim, vechelban assur* — a goat has horns and hooves, yet its fat is forbidden because it is a *behema*, not a *chaya*. The Gemara therefore cannot accept the bare presence of horns as sufficient; the horns must have specific characteristics.

### Bundled Pattern


The Gemara proposes: *keruchot beinan* — the horns must be bundled, meaning they grow in layered rings, one layer deposited upon the previous as the horn extends. Looking at these horn cross-sections, one sees concentric growth rings — visually resembling the growth rings inside a tree. This, the Gemara initially suggests, is the hallmark of a *chaya*.

### Ox Counterexample


But: *vaharei shor dekeruchot, vechelban assur* — an ox's horns also grow in this layered, bundled manner, yet it is a *behema* whose fat is forbidden. Bundled growth alone is therefore insufficient.

### Ridged Texture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי עֵז, דַּחֲרוּקוֹת, וְחֶלְבּוֹ אָסוּר? מְפוּצָּלוֹת בָּעֵינַן! וַהֲרֵי צְבִי, דְּאֵין מְפוּצָּלוֹת, וְחֶלְבּוֹ מוּתָּר? חֲדוּרוֹת בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: **But** isn't there **a goat, which** has **grooved** horns, **and** still **its fat is forbidden?** The Gemara responds: **We require** horns that are **branched;** a goat's horns are not branched. The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there **a gazelle [*tzevi*],** whose horns **are not branched, and** still **its fat is permitted?** The Gemara responds: **We require** horns that are **rounded** and not broad like those of a sheep.

The Gemara proposes: *charukot beinan* — the horns must be ridged. Certain horns have visible, pronounced ridges running along their length, in contrast to smooth horn surfaces.

### Goat Ridged Issue


The problem recurs: *vaharei ez decharukot, vechelban assur* — a goat has ridged horns and yet is a *behema*. Ridging alone is also insufficient.

### Branched Structure


The Gemara proposes: *mefutzalot beinan* — the horns must be branched, like antlers. The paradigmatic image here is the antlered deer — or more dramatically, the moose — whose horns extend and divide into multiple branches. Moose, like deer, are indeed *chayot* whose fat is permitted, and they are fully kosher.

### Deer Counterexample


But now the exception runs in the other direction: *vaharei tzvi de'ein mefutzalot vechelban muttar* — a deer does not have branched horns, yet it is a *chaya* with permitted fat. Rashi himself acknowledges difficulty here, writing *lo yadana mai ka'amar d'avadei mefutzalot hein* — he does not know what the Gemara means, since deer's horns are in fact branched. Rashi then offers: *venira be'einai* — it seems to him that what we call *tzvi* is not what the Talmud called *tzvi*; the Talmudic *tzvi* may have been what is called in his day a *steinbuck* (identified variously as an ibex-type animal or a South African antelope), whose horns are ridged but not branched. The author of *sichat chullin* adds that Middle Eastern deer in the land of Israel have non-branched antlers, so the identification may simply refer to the indigenous Israeli deer species rather than requiring Rashi's more exotic identification.

### Pointed Tips

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, הֵיכָא דְּמִיפַּצְלָא – לָא דִּינָא וְלָא דַּיָּינָא; הֵיכָא דְּלָא מִיפַּצְלָא – בָּעֵינַן כְּרוּכוֹת, חֲדוּרוֹת, וַחֲרוּקוֹת, וְהוּא דְּמִיבְּלַע חִירְקַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Therefore, where** an animal's horns are **branched, there is neither judgment nor judge,** and the animal is without a doubt undomesticated. **Where** they are **not branched, we require** that they be **layered, rounded, and grooved. And it** must be grooved in a pattern such **that its grooves are absorbed** into one another.

The Gemara therefore proposes a fourth criterion: *chadurot beinan* — the tips must be pointed, or according to another reading of Rashi, the base must be perfectly circular. Rashi suggests two possible meanings: either that the horn narrows to a fine point at its tip, or that it is perfectly round in cross-section at its base, unlike the more irregular cross-sections of *behema* horns. Looking at deer antlers, the pointed tips are indeed characteristic.

### Synthesis & Rules


The Gemara synthesizes: *heicha demiftzala, lo dina velo dayana* — if the horns are branched, there is simply no question; it is a *chaya*, full stop. *heicha delo miftzala, beinan keruchot chadurot vecharukot* — if the horns are not branched, then all three remaining criteria must be satisfied: bundled growth, pointed tips, and ridged texture. Furthermore, *vehu demivla chirkayhu* — the ridges must be overlapping, close together, not widely spaced.

### Karchos Goats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ סְפֵיקָא דְּעִיזָּא כַּרְכּוּז. הָהִיא עִיזָּא כַּרְכּוּז דַּהֲוַאי בֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, דַּעֲקוּר מְלָא צַנָּא דְּתַרְבָּא מִינַּהּ. רַב אַחַאי אָסַר, רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אֲכַל מִינֵּיהּ, קָרֵי אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ: ״מִפְּרִי פִי אִישׁ תִּשְׂבַּע בִּטְנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is the uncertainty** that arose concerning the ***karkoz* goat,** i.e., whether it is considered a domesticated animal. As there was **a certain *karkoz* goat that was in the house of the Exilarch, from which** they **removed a full basket of fat** after slaughtering it. **Rav Aḥai deemed** the fat **forbidden,** because he considered it a domesticated animal. But **Rav Shmuel, son of Rabbi Abbahu, ate of it. He read** the verse **about himself: "A man's belly shall be filled with the fruit of his mouth"** (Proverbs 18:20). In other words, due to his learning, he knew it was permitted to eat the fat of the *karkoz* goat.

This synthesis leads directly into the famous case of the goats of Karchos: *hahi iza dekarchos dehavi bei Reish Galusa* — there were goats of Karchos in the household of the *reish galuta*, from which an entire basketful of fat (*chelev*) had been extracted. The question was whether these were *chaya* or *behema* — could the fat be eaten? Their horns were presumably of the non-branched variety, triggering the requirement to satisfy all three other criteria. The ridges were present but the question was whether they were sufficiently overlapping.

### Karchos Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, וְהִזָּהֲרוּ בְּרַבֵּינוּ אַחַאי, שֶׁמֵּאִיר עֵינֵי גּוֹלָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **sent** a message **from there,** Eretz Yisrael: **The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of** Rav **Shmuel, son of Rabbi Abbahu,** that a *karkoz* goat is an undomesticated animal and its fat is permitted; **but** nevertheless, **be mindful of** the honor of **our teacher Aḥai** who deems it forbidden, and do not partake of the fat in his presence, **as he enlightens the eyes of the exile.**

*Rav Acha asar* — Rav Acha ruled the fat forbidden, treating the animals as *behema*. *shmuel breih deRabbi abbahu achal minei* — Shmuel the son of Rabbi Abbahu ruled it permitted and, true to form, ate from the fat himself to demonstrate his genuine conviction. He applied to himself the verse *mipri pi ish tisba bitno* — from the fruit of your mouth your stomach will be satisfied: his own ruling enabled him to benefit. A ruling was subsequently sent from Eretz Yisrael: *hilchata kevasei deShmuel breih deRabbi Abbahu* — the *halacha* follows Shmuel the son of Rabbi Abbahu, and the goats of Karchos are *chaya*, their fat permitted.

### Respect for Rav Acha


Yet the message from Eretz Yisrael added: *vehizaharu beRabbeinu Acha* — nonetheless, show respect to Rabbeinu Acha. Do not rule against him flagrantly, and certainly not in his presence. *shemeir einei golah hu* — he is the one who illuminates the eyes of the diaspora. The honorific *meir einei ha-golah* — which would later be famously applied to the Maharam of Rothenburg — conveys the stature of a figure whose authority over the diaspora community demands deference even when the formal *halacha* runs against his ruling.
