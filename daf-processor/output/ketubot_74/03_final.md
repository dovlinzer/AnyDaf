# Ketubot 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Raba's Tnai Framework

### Rav v. Shmuel Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁגֵּירְשָׁהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה, וְהֶחְזִירָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה. אֲבָל גֵּירְשָׁהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה וְהֶחְזִירָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא גְּדוֹלָה, אוֹ שֶׁהֶחְזִירָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה וְגָדְלָה אֶצְלוֹ וָמֵת — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **In what case is this statement said? When he divorced her while she was a minor girl and remarried her while she was a minor girl. But if he divorced her while she was a minor girl and remarried her when she was an adult woman, or if he remarried her while she was a minor girl and she became an adult woman while married to him, the second marriage is binding, and she has the status of a full-fledged married woman. And therefore, if he died, either she performs ḥalitza or she enters into levirate marriage** like any other widow.

The daf opens in the middle of a foundational debate between Rav and Shmuel concerning a woman who is married subject to a *tnai* — a condition — and the condition is not ultimately met. The wrinkle is that after the *kiddushin*, the couple also proceeded to *nissuin*: they lived together and had marital relations. The question is how much the subsequent cohabitation and sexual relations, occurring in a fully marital context, operates to create a binding marriage despite the unmet condition, and how much the original *tnai* continues to frame and govern the relationship.

Rav's position is that the sexual relations subsequent to the *kiddushin* create a binding marriage regardless of the unfulfilled condition. Shmuel disagrees, holding that the *tnai* retains its framing force throughout. The Gemara at this stage is in the midst of Raba's attempt to situate this dispute.

### Raba: Tnai Always Frames

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמְרוּ: חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת. וְהָא הָכָא, דִּכְטָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** **In the name of Rabbi Eliezer they said that even in this case she performs ḥalitza, but she may not enter into levirate marriage. But here, this should be considered similar to an error concerning one woman, and yet they disagree.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשֵׁי קְטַנָּה כְּלוּם, וְגָמַר וּבָעַל לְשֵׁם קִידּוּשִׁין, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשֵׁי קְטַנָּה כְּלוּם, וְכִי קָא בָעַל — אַדַּעְתָּא דְּקִידּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים קָא בָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **There too they disagree over a possible halakhic error: One Sage, the first tanna, holds that a person knows that the betrothal of a minor girl is nothing, and consequently, after she reaches adulthood, he decides to engage in sexual intercourse for the purpose of betrothal. Therefore, if the husband dies she may enter into levirate marriage. And one Sage, Rabbi Eliezer, holds that a person does not know that the betrothal of a minor girl is nothing, and therefore when he engages in sexual intercourse with her after she reaches adulthood, he does so on the basis of the initial betrothal, which is not effective according to Torah law, and therefore she cannot enter into levirate marriage.**

Raba's proposal is that in a standard case of *kiddushin al tnai* — where the couple married subject to an explicit condition — both Rav and Shmuel would agree that the *tnai* continues to frame the ongoing marital relationship, and if the condition is not met, they are not married even if they have been living together and having relations. On Raba's reading, the dispute of Rav and Shmuel is confined to a different category of case: situations where the original marriage was somehow defective not because of a stated condition but for some other reason, such as a different set of women involved, or some other structural anomaly. The core claim is that an explicit *tnai* uttered at the outset operates continuously — it defines the meaning of every subsequent act within that marriage, including sexual relations.

This analysis carries enormous *halakhic* weight in contemporary discussions about using *tnai bekiddushin* as a potential solution to the *agunah* problem, and the Gemara will return to test and challenge it from multiple directions.

### Ketanah Becomes Adult

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁגֵּירוּשֶׁיהָ גֵּירוּשִׁין גְּמוּרִין, וְאֵין חֲזָרָתָהּ חֲזָרָה גְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Because her divorce was a full-fledged divorce, since the father has authority to receive her bill of divorce from her husband, and her return to her husband afterward is not a full-fledged return, since she remarries her husband while still a minor, and her father no longer has authority to marry her off, and marriage to a minor girl by her own consent is not considered a full-fledged marriage. She is consequently prohibited from entering into a levirate marriage based on the prohibition against a divorcée marrying her former husband's brother.**

The Gemara now raises a *baraita* to challenge Raba's framework. The case involves a woman who was married as a minor, her husband divorced her while she was still a minor, and she then returned to him — also while still a minor. After *nissuin* with the first husband, the divorce was fully effective (*geirushin gemurin*), but the re-marriage was not biblically binding (*hazaratah einah hazarah gemurah*), since she was an emancipated minor — a *yetomah be'chayei ha'av*, one who is no longer under her father's legal authority but has not yet reached adulthood, and whose capacity to effect a fully valid marriage is therefore only rabbinic at best.

When such a husband dies, the surviving brother-in-law may only perform *chalitzah* and not *yibum*, because the second marriage, being only rabbinic in nature, does not create the full legal status of *eshet ach* that would permit *yibum*, yet the original first marriage — which was fully biblical — creates a genuine *issur kares* risk for the brother. *Chalitzah* is therefore mandatory.

The *baraita* then specifies: when is this so? Only if she was divorced as a minor and re-married as a minor. But if she was divorced as a minor and re-married as an adult, or divorced as an adult and re-married as an adult, or even re-married as a minor but then *gadlah etzlo* — matured into adulthood while still living with him — then the rule is *o choletzet o mityabemes*: the surviving brother may choose between *chalitzah* and *yibum*, because by that point the marriage has acquired full biblical standing.

### Sex After Ketanah

The case of *gadlah etzlo* — the girl who was re-married as a minor but became an adult while the marriage was ongoing — is directly relevant to the central question of the sugya. Here, the second marriage began as a merely rabbinic union, yet through the continued cohabitation and sexual relations once she had matured into adulthood, it became a full *kiddushin de-oraita*. This is evidence that continued marital relations can indeed elevate and transform the status of a defective initial *kiddushin* into a binding biblical marriage.

The Gemara notes that *Rabbi Eliezer* (in the name of *Rabbi Eliezer*) dissents even in these latter cases, ruling *choletzet ve-lo mityabemes* — only *chalitzah*, not *yibum* — out of concern that permitting *yibum* in the cases where the second marriage did become fully valid will lead people to permit it even in the case where the marriage remained merely rabbinic. This is a precautionary ruling. But the *tanna kamma* and Raba's analysis both take seriously the principle that continued relations can constitute new *kiddushin* in certain circumstances.

### Tnai vs. Ta'ut Distinguished

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ עַל תְּנַאי וּבָעַל — [דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל] אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated in accordance with Raba's opinion, that there is no dispute with regard to an error concerning one woman: Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who betrothed a woman conditionally and engaged in sexual intercourse without specifying that he is voiding the condition, all agree that she does not require a bill of divorce from him.**

The Gemara now presses Raba: the case of the *ketanah* who matured during the second marriage shows that continued sexual relations can indeed create binding *kiddushin* even when the original marriage was defective. How then can Raba claim that everyone agrees that a *tnai* negates the binding force of subsequent relations?

The Gemara answers, and Raba's distinction is clarified: the debate about whether continued relations create *kiddushin* when the original marriage was defective turns on a question of awareness and intent. In the *ketanah* case, one opinion holds that people are indeed aware that *kiddushei ketanah* have no biblical force, and therefore when they continue to have relations as husband and wife, the man *gamar u'ba'al leshum kiddushin* — he has specific intent for those relations to constitute an act of *kiddushin*. The other opinion holds that people are generally unaware that *kiddushei ketanah* are void, and therefore continued relations are understood by both parties simply as an extension of the original marriage, *ada'ata de-kiddushin rishonim ba'al* — performed in the spirit of the first *kiddushin* — and therefore they do not constitute an independent act of *kiddushin*.

This precise debate — whether continued relations are a new act or a continuation of the original frame — does not apply to the case of *tnai*. When an explicit condition was stated at the outset, says Raba, the *tnai* categorically and continuously frames all subsequent relations. There is no ambiguity about what the parties intended. Everyone, including Rav, would agree that if the condition is not met, those subsequent relations cannot be construed as an independent act of *kiddushin*, because they are definitionally understood within the framework of the stated condition. The debate of *ta'ut* — error about the binding force of the initial act — simply does not arise when the parties explicitly articulated a condition.

The contemporary *halakhic* implications are significant. Rav Moshe Feinstein's approach to marriages contracted in non-Orthodox settings — where he argued that because the witnesses were affiliated with a movement that accepts *halitzah*, they are disqualified as *eidim*, rendering the original *kiddushin* void and the subsequent cohabitation legally framed by that void — closely parallels the Gemara's analysis here. If the original act was defective, the question is whether subsequent marital life cures it. Raba's framework, by distinguishing *tnai* cases from *ta'ut* cases, maps onto exactly this set of concerns.

## Tnai Validity & Limits

### Rav Acha: Tnai + Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ עַל תְּנַאי וּבָעַל — [דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל] אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ: חֲלִיצָה מוּטְעֵת כְּשֵׁירָה. אֵיזוֹ הִיא חֲלִיצָה מוּטְעֵת? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל שֶׁאוֹמֵר לוֹ ״חֲלוֹץ לָהּ, וּבְכָךְ אַתָּה כּוֹנְסָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated in accordance with Raba's opinion, that there is no dispute with regard to an error concerning one woman: Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who betrothed a woman conditionally and engaged in sexual intercourse without specifying that he is voiding the condition, all agree that she does not require a bill of divorce from him. Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, son of Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov's sister, raised an objection to his opinion from a baraita that states: A mistaken ḥalitza is valid. The amoraim asked: What is meant by the term: A mistaken ḥalitza? Reish Lakish said: Any situation when someone says to a man whose married brother died childless [yavam] not well versed in halakha: Perform ḥalitza with her and by doing so you will thereby marry her. Although he did not intend to release their bond with this ḥalitza, it is nevertheless effective.**

The Gemara now reports a teaching of Rav Acha bar Yaakov citing Rabbi Yochanan: *hamkadesh al tnai u'ba'al, divrei hakol* — if one performed *kiddushin* subject to a condition and then had sexual relations, by universal agreement she requires a *get* from him. This statement, read straightforwardly, appears to confirm Raba's view that the *tnai* continues to frame the marriage — everyone agrees the condition governs. The sexual relations do not override the *tnai*, and therefore if the condition is not met, she is not married and does not technically need a *get*, but the rabbis require a *get* as a precaution.

Wait — the Gemara immediately introduces a challenge.

### Chalitzah Mutaat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה: בֵּין שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּון הוּא וְלֹא נִתְכַּוְּונָה הִיא, בֵּין שֶׁנִּתְכַּוְּונָה הִיא וְלֹא נִתְכַּוֵּון הוּא חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה, עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוְּונוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם. וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כֹּל שֶׁאוֹמֵר לוֹ ״חֲלוֹץ לָהּ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתֵּן לְךָ מָאתַיִם זוּז״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: I teach with regard to the halakhot of ḥalitza: Whether he intended to release her through ḥalitza and she did not intend it, or whether she intended this outcome and he did not intend it, her ḥalitza is invalid; it will always be invalid until they both intend the appropriate outcome. And you say that in that case, when he intended to marry her and not to release her, that her ḥalitza is valid? Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan said that in fact, in such a case, the ḥalitza would be invalid, and the term: A mistaken ḥalitza, concerns any situation where someone says to the yavam: Perform ḥalitza with her on condition that she gives you two hundred dinars as payment, and afterward she refuses to give him the money.**

The challenge comes from the ruling that *chalitzah mutaat kesheirah* — *chalitzah* performed under mistaken circumstances is nevertheless valid. The Gemara asks: what constitutes *chalitzah* performed in error? Reish Lakish explains: where the rabbis told the *yavam* to perform *chalitzah* and told him that by doing so he would acquire her as his wife — they deceived him into performing *chalitzah* by misrepresenting it as the procedure for *yibum*. He performed *chalitzah* believing it would result in marriage, and the *chalitzah* is nonetheless valid and effective.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, objects to that reading. He rules that *chalitzah* requires mutual intent — *bein shenitkavein hu velo nitkavnah hi, bein shenitkavnah hi velo nitkavein hu, chalitzatah pesulah* — whether only he intended it as *chalitzah* but not she, or only she intended it and not he, the *chalitzah* is invalid. It requires both parties to intend the act as *chalitzah* (*ad sheyitkavnu shneihem*). How then can we say that deceiving the *yavam* into thinking he was performing *yibum* constitutes valid *chalitzah*?

Rabbi Yochanan therefore revises the definition of *chalitzah mutaat*: the case is where the rabbis told him to perform *chalitzah* on the condition that she would pay him two hundred *zuz*. He understood that this was *chalitzah* and consented to it, but the condition — the payment of two hundred *zuz* — was never fulfilled. He intended to perform *chalitzah*, he performed it validly, but there was a deception regarding the condition attached to it.

### Acts Override Tnai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא כֵּיוָן דַּעֲבַד מַעֲשֶׂה — אַחוֹלֵי אַחֲלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּבְעַל — אַחוֹלֵי אַחֲלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently, it can be seen from here that since he performed an action, the halakha views this as though he explicitly waived his condition, for although he stipulated a condition, once he actually performs ḥalitza the condition is ignored. If so, here too, with regard to a conditional betrothal, since he engaged in sexual intercourse, he has waived his condition,** which contradicts Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov's view.

This case is now highly significant for the *tnai* discussion. The *chalitzah* was performed *al menat* — on condition — that she pay two hundred *zuz*, and the condition was not met. Yet the *chalitzah* was valid. The Gemara derives from this: *kol de'avid ma'aseh acholei achil letna'ei* — whoever performs an act has implicitly waived his condition. By going through with the act, the actor retroactively annuls his own *tnai*.

If this principle is correct, then by the same logic, the man who performed *kiddushin al tnai* and then had sexual relations should equally be considered to have implicitly waived his condition through the act of *bia*. The *bia* would override the *tnai* just as the *chalitzah* overrode its condition, and the *kiddushin* would be fully binding — the exact opposite of Raba's universal-consensus claim.

One might ask: why does any *tnai* work at all? When I sell an object conditionally, or give a *get* conditionally, the act of handing over the object or the document is itself an act — why doesn't that act equally override the condition? The Gemara's implicit answer is that acts like *chalitzah* and *bia* are qualitatively different — they are deeply personal, physically involving the actor in a way that handing over a ring or a document is not. The act of performing *chalitzah*, with all of its ritual degradation — the spitting, the declaration, the shoe removal — or the act of sexual union, are acts of such a personal and total nature that by going through with them one signals complete commitment to their legal effect, regardless of any stated condition.

### Tosafot: Shaliach Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּר בֵּי רַב: שַׁפִּיר קָא אָמְרַתְּ? מִכְּדֵי כֹּל תְּנַאי מֵהֵיכָא גָּמְרִינַן — מִתְּנַאי בְּנֵי גָד וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן, תְּנָאָה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְקַיּוֹמֵהּ עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ, כִּי הָתָם — הָוֵי תְּנָאֵיהּ תְּנָאָה. דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לְקַיּוֹמֵהּ עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ, כִּי הָתָם — לָא הָוֵי תְּנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: Student of the academy, have you spoken well? After all, from where do we learn all the laws of conditions? From the conditions made with the descendants of Gad and the descendants of Reuben. A condition that can be fulfilled by means of an agent, as was done there, when Moses transferred responsibility for implementing the condition to Joshua and the Elders, and such a condition is a valid condition. Whereas a condition that cannot be fulfilled by means of an agent as was done there is not a valid condition.**

A student in the *yeshiva* raises the underlying conceptual question: where do we learn that conditions can render legal acts conditional at all? The source is the *tnai Bnei Gad u'Bnei Reuven* — Moshe's conditional assignment of the Trans-Jordanian territories. What is it about that case that serves as the paradigm? The answer is: *tnai de-efshar le-kiyumei al yedei shaliach* — a condition is legally operative only when the act which the condition governs could in principle be performed through an agent. Since Moshe could delegate the apportionment of the land — indeed, Yehoshua ultimately performed it — the act was of the type where the principal's authority can be transferred. Where such delegation is impossible, the *tnai* has no legal force.

Tosafot develops the underlying logic. Why specifically this criterion? The answer is: *ho'il ve-ha-ma'aseh kol kach biyado she-yakhol le-kayemo al yedei shaliach, svara hu she-t'nai k'ein biyado lismokh bo t'na'ei* — since the act is so thoroughly within the actor's power and control that he can transfer that power to another, he is equally empowered to determine the conditions under which the act will be binding. I sell my coffee cup: I can appoint an agent to sell it for me, which means I am genuinely the master of that transaction, and I can therefore make it conditional. I perform *kiddushin*: I can appoint an agent to perform the *kiddushin* on my behalf, which means I am genuinely the master of that act, and I can make it conditional.

### Empowerment Logic

The underlying principle Tosafot articulates is one of *empowerment versus automatic status change*. If an act is one in which I am the empowered agent — I effect the transfer, I cause the status change — then I can also control *when* and *under what conditions* that change takes effect. But if the act is one in which the law simply says: if this act occurs, the status will change — and I am not so much the effector as the occasion — then I am not empowered in the relevant sense, and I cannot impose conditions on a status change that is not mine to control.

*Chalitzah* exemplifies the latter. Nobody else can perform *chalitzah* on behalf of the *yavam*. This is not merely a physical limitation — it is a legal and conceptual one. The fact that the act cannot be delegated is evidence that the *yavam* is not empowered in the relevant sense. He performs certain actions and the law effects a status change, but he does not himself effect that change. He is therefore not in a position to condition it.

This same principle illuminates several other features of *chalitzah* and *yibum* noted in the context of tractate Yevamot. *Yibum* does not require *kavanah* — the very reality of the sexual act automatically creates the marital bond, regardless of intent — whereas normally a legal act requires the actor's conscious intention precisely because the actor is the one effecting the change. Similarly, there was a Talmudic position that even a *katan* might be able to perform *chalitzah*: if we understand that the *yavam* is not the agent effecting the change but merely the occasion through which the law effects it, the requirement of adult *da'at* is attenuated.

### Bia & Tnai Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בִּיאָה, דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לְקַיּוֹמַיהּ עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ כִּי הָתָם, וְקָא הָוֵי תְּנָאָה! הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתַּקּוּשׁ הֲוָיוֹת לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **But sexual intercourse is something that cannot be fulfilled by means of an agent as was done there, in the case of the descendants of Gad and the descendants of Reuben, and yet it is considered a valid condition. There, in the case of betrothal, there is a special reason for this law, because the different ways of becoming betrothed are juxtaposed to each other.** The Torah describes betrothal with the term becoming, as in the expression: "And she becomes another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:2). Betrothal can be performed through the transference of money or an item of value, through a document, or through sexual intercourse. All three forms are juxtaposed with one another. Conditions can be stipulated for betrothal performed via transference of money or through a document, since these methods of betrothal can be fulfilled through an agent. Therefore, a condition may also be stipulated for betrothal through sexual intercourse, although that cannot be fulfilled through an agent.

The Gemara now directly confronts the apparent inconsistency: *bia* also cannot be performed through an agent — no one can have sexual relations on another man's behalf — yet we maintain that a *tnai* attached to *kiddushei bia* remains operative. How can this be?

The Gemara answers: *hasam mishum de-iskesh havayos le-hadadi* — because all three modes of *kiddushin* (money, document, and sexual relations) are textually linked to one another by the word *ve-haytah* in the verse. Since *kiddushei kesef* and *kiddushei shtar* can unambiguously be performed through an agent — the man is genuinely empowered in those modes — and since all three forms of *kiddushin* are halakhically equated, *bia* shares in that empowerment by association, even though the physical act of *bia* itself cannot be delegated. The *hekkesh* — the textual equation — extends the empowerment of the delegable forms to the non-delegable form.

*Chalitzah*, by contrast, has no such *hekkesh* linking it to any delegable act. It stands alone, and standing alone it cannot carry conditions.

This resolution also brings into relief the deeper conceptual question running through the entire sugya: when marital cohabitation and sexual relations constitute the *de facto* reality of a couple's life together, is that reality itself the legally operative fact — automatic, objective, requiring no intent — or is it an act of *kiddushin* that the man effects with intention and over which he therefore has conditional control? The Gemara's answer, at least in the *tnai* framework, is the latter: because *bia* is equated with the other modes of *kiddushin* through the *hekkesh*, the man is understood as the empowered agent even in that context, and conditions therefore govern.

## Universal Rule Debate

### Rav Yehuda: All Need Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עוּלָּא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה וּבָעַל, עַל תְּנַאי וּבָעַל, בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה וּבָעַל — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ulla bar Abba said that Ulla said that Rabbi Elazar said: In the case of one who betroths a woman with a loan by forgiving a debt she owes him, which does not effect betrothal, and subsequently engages in sexual intercourse with her; or one who betroths a woman conditionally and the condition was not fulfilled, and he subsequently engages in sexual intercourse with her; or one who betroths a woman with an item worth less than the value of a peruta, and he subsequently engages in sexual intercourse with her, in all of these cases, all agree that she requires a bill of divorce from him. Although the initial betrothal was invalid, they are betrothed due to the subsequent sexual intercourse.**

The Gemara now introduces a strikingly different teaching. Rav Yehuda, citing Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar, rules: *hamikadesh be-milve u-va'al* — one who performs *kiddushin* using a forgiven loan and then has relations; *o al tnai u-va'al* — or with a *tnai* followed by relations; *o be-pachos mi-shaveh perutah u-va'al* — or with less than a *perutah* followed by relations — *de-kulei alma tzrichah heimenu get*: by universal agreement she requires a *get*.

This is the exact opposite of what Raba established. Raba argued that in the *tnai* case, everyone agrees no *get* is needed — the *tnai* governs and there is no binding marriage. Here Rav Yehuda rules that in all three categories — *milve*, *tnai*, and *pachos mi-shaveh perutah* — everyone agrees a *get* is required. Once sexual relations have occurred following even a defective *kiddushin*, the marriage is treated as binding enough to require dissolution. The implications for *hilchos mamzerut* and the *agunah* question are severe.

### Rav Yosef: Knowledge Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי מְנַחֵם אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה וּבָעַל, צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט. בְּהָא הוּא דְּלָא טָעֵי, אֲבָל בְּהָנָךְ טָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef bar Abba said that Rabbi Menaḥem said that Rabbi Ami said: In the case of one who betroths a woman with an item worth less than the value of a peruta and subsequently engages in sexual intercourse with her, she requires a bill of divorce from him. This is because it is in this matter that he does not err. He knows that betrothal must be performed with an item worth at least a peruta, and he therefore must have engaged in intercourse with her for the purpose of betrothal. But with regard to those other cases, i.e., a loan or a condition, he does err. He engages in intercourse based upon his initial betrothal, and therefore she does not require a bill of divorce.**

Rav Yosef bar Abba, in the name of Rav Nachman and Rav Ami, offers a narrower ruling: *hamikadesh be-pachos mi-shaveh perutah, tzarich heimenu get* — only the case of less than a *perutah* requires a *get* after subsequent relations. The reasoning is rooted in awareness. In the *pachos mi-shaveh perutah* case, everyone knows — or ought to know — that giving less than a *perutah* is not valid *kiddushin*. Because the defect is universally understood, the man's subsequent sexual relations are presumed to carry independent intent: he is aware the original act was void and therefore has *kavanah* for the *bia* to serve as a new, independent act of *kiddushin*. That intent transforms the sexual act into a binding *kiddushei bia*.

In the *tnai* and *milve* cases, however, the man may not appreciate the defect. He may believe the original *kiddushin* was binding and that the subsequent relations are simply marital relations within an existing marriage, not a new act of *kiddushin*. Absent that awareness and intent, the subsequent *bia* cannot be construed as an independent act of *kiddushin*. Rav Yosef thus holds that intent is the operative variable: only where the parties know the original act was void will subsequent relations create binding *kiddushin*.

### Rav Kahana Rejects Raba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ עַל תְּנַאי וּבָעַל — צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט. זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְלֹא הָיָה כֹּחַ בַּחֲכָמִים לְהוֹצִיאָהּ בְּלֹא גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said in the name of Ulla: In the case of one who betroths a woman conditionally and the condition was not fulfilled, and he subsequently engages in sexual intercourse with her, she requires a bill of divorce from him, in accordance with Rabbi Elazar. The Gemara relates: This was an incident that actually occurred. A man betrothed a woman conditionally and then engaged in sexual intercourse with her without specification, and the Sages did not have the power to allow her to leave her husband without a bill of divorce, since they could not definitively rule that the betrothal was invalid. Therefore, they forced him to give her a bill of divorce.**

Rav Kahana rules in the name of Rav: *hamikadesh al tnai u-va'al, tzrichah heimenu get* — one who performs *kiddushin* subject to a condition and then has relations requires a *get*. This is a direct rejection of Raba's framework. Raba had argued that in the *tnai* case, everyone agrees the *tnai* governs and no *get* is needed. Rav Kahana rules, at minimum in the name of Rav, that the *tnai* case requires a *get*, and this opinion is adopted as the operative ruling — *l'chumra*. Even if Shmuel might disagree, we rule in accordance with Rav that *tnai* followed by sexual relations does require a *get*.

### Ma'aseh: No Power

The Gemara reinforces this position with a precedent: *ve-zeh hayah ma'aseh, ve-lo hayah koach ba-chachamim le-hotziah beloa get* — this actually happened, and the rabbis did not have the power to release the woman without a *get*. A marriage had been contracted subject to a *tnai*, the couple had subsequently lived together and had relations, and when the question arose, the rabbis found themselves unable to permit the woman to walk away without a *get* from her husband.

This statement is a striking one, particularly in the context of contemporary *agunah* discussions. Those who argue that the rabbinic authorities could resolve *agunah* cases through *hafka'at kiddushin* or other mechanisms often presuppose a broad rabbinic power to declare marriages void. The language here — *lo hayah koach be-yad chachamim* — asserts the opposite: the rabbis' hands were tied. They recognized opinions on both sides, they may have been aware of Shmuel's more lenient position, but they did not feel they could rely on those positions in practice.

### Kiddushei Ta'ut Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהַאי תַּנָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְהִיא לֹא נִתְפָּשָׂה״ — אֲסוּרָה. הָא נִתְפָּשָׂה — מוּתֶּרֶת. וְיֵשׁ לְךָ אַחֶרֶת שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְפָּשָׂה, מוּתֶּרֶת. וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? שֶׁקִּידּוּשֶׁיהָ קִידּוּשֵׁי טָעוּת, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בְּנָהּ מוּרְכָּב עַל כְּתֵיפָהּ, מְמָאֶנֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara comments: This statement comes to exclude the opinion of this tanna, as Rav Yehuda said that Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: The verse states with regard to a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [sota]: "And she was not seized" (Numbers 5:13), i.e., she was not raped. That is, she is forbidden to her husband since she willingly committed adultery with another man. Consequently, a woman who in fact was seized is permitted to her husband. And you have another woman who, although she was not seized but rather engaged in sexual intercourse willingly, is nevertheless permitted to her husband and is not considered a sota. And who is this? This is referring to one whose betrothal was a mistaken betrothal, as even if her son from this marriage is riding on her shoulders, she may refuse her husband and go on her way. According to this opinion, the marriage did not take effect at all, despite the fact that they engaged in sexual intercourse, because the betrothal involved an error.**

To balance the record, the Gemara cites a tannaitic source taking the opposite extreme. Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Shmuel, in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: *ve-hi lo nitpasha, asurah* — a married woman who committed adultery willingly is forbidden to her husband; *ha nitpasha, muteret* — if she was raped, she is permitted. And Rabbi Yishmael derives: *ve-yeish lecha acheret she-af al pi she-lo nitpasha, muteret* — there is another type of woman who, even though she was not coerced, is nonetheless permitted. Who is this? *Zo she-kiddusheha kiddushei ta'ut* — a woman whose original *kiddushin* was in error. Even if she has been living with this man for years, even if *bnah murkav al ktefah* — she is carrying their toddler on her shoulder — *mema'enet ve-holechet lah*: once it is established that the original *kiddushin* was a *ta'ut*, she declares her rejection of the marriage and walks away, and she is entirely free.

This tannaitic position represents the outer pole of the opposing approach: no matter how many years of married life follow a *kiddushin* that was void from the outset, the subsequent cohabitation creates nothing, and the woman is free at any time.

The sugya thus presents three distinct positions. Raba: in a *tnai* case, everyone agrees the *tnai* governs and no *get* is needed; the debate is in other cases. Rav Yehuda citing Rabbi Elazar: in all cases — *tnai*, *milve*, *ta'ut* — everyone agrees a *get* is needed once relations have occurred. And the tannaitic teaching of Rabbi Yishmael: in a *ta'ut* case, even years of cohabitation with children create nothing, and the woman may walk away freely at any time.

## Condition Fulfillment

### Vow Annulment Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָלְכָה אֵצֶל חָכָם וְהִתִּירָהּ — מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. אֵצֶל רוֹפֵא וְרִיפֵּא אוֹתָהּ — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מָה בֵּין חָכָם לְרוֹפֵא? חָכָם עוֹקֵר אֶת הַנֶּדֶר מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, וְרוֹפֵא אֵינוֹ מְרַפֵּא אֶלָּא מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: If a man betroths a woman on condition that there are no vows incumbent upon her to fulfill, but there are vows incumbent upon her, and she goes to a halakhic authority and he dissolves her vows, she is betrothed. However, if he betroths her on condition that she has no blemishes, but she does have blemishes, and she goes to a doctor and he heals her blemishes, she is not betrothed. The Gemara inquires: What is the difference between a halakhic authority and a doctor? The Gemara explains: When a halakhic authority dissolves a vow, he uproots the vow retroactively. It is as if she had never vowed at all, and therefore she was in fact not bound by vows at the time of their betrothal. But a doctor only heals from here onward. Since she had blemishes at the time of betrothal, she is in breach of his condition.**

The Gemara now shifts to a related but distinct question: once we have established that a *tnai* governs a *kiddushin*, how do we determine whether the condition has been met or not? The *Mishnah* states that if a man betroths a woman *al tnai she-ein lah nedarim* — on condition that she has no vows — and she turns out to have vows, she is not *mekudeshes*. The Gemara now considers wrinkles in this basic rule.

A *baraita* teaches: *halchah etzel chacham ve-hitira, mekudeshes* — if after accepting the ring she went to a rabbinic sage and had the vow annulled, she is *mekudeshes*. The rationale will become clear, but the basic ruling is that the subsequent annulment satisfies the condition retroactively.

The countercase: *halchah etzel rofe ve-hirpasah, eina mekudeshes* — if she went to a doctor and was healed of physical blemishes that had been the subject of a separate condition, she is not *mekudeshes*. The healing does not retroactively satisfy the condition.

### Sage vs. Doctor

The Gemara asks: what is the difference between the sage and the doctor? The answer is: *chacham oker et ha-neder mei-ikaro* — the sage uproots the vow from its very inception. The mechanism of *hatarat nedarim* operates retroactively: the sage asks the person whether, had she contemplated the full circumstances at the time of taking the vow, she would still have taken it. When she says she would not, the ruling is that the vow was never properly formed in the first place — it was never binding from the outset. The sage does not release her from an existing obligation; he establishes that the obligation never truly arose.

Therefore, when we look back to the moment she accepted the ring, she never had a vow. The condition is satisfied retroactively.

The doctor, by contrast, *eino mirapeh ela mikan u-le-haba* — heals her only prospectively from this point forward. The blemish existed at the time of *kiddushin*, and the doctor's intervention does not change that historical fact. The condition — that she had no blemish — was not satisfied at the relevant moment, and the subsequent cure does not alter this.

### Contradictory Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵצֶל חָכָם וְהִתִּירָהּ, אֵצֶל רוֹפֵא וְרִיפֵּא אוֹתָהּ — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָא — רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁתִּתְבַּזֶּה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין. הָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם רוֹצֶה שֶׁתִּתְבַּזֶּה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught in a different baraita that if she goes to a halakhic authority and he dissolves her vows, or to a doctor and he heals her blemishes, she is not betrothed? Rava said: This is not difficult, for this baraita follows the opinion of Rabbi Meir, and that one follows Rabbi Elazar. This baraita, which states that if her vows were dissolved by a halakhic authority she is betrothed, is in accordance with Rabbi Meir, who said that a man is willing for his wife to be degraded by going to court to have her vow dissolved. That baraita follows the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, who said that a man is not willing for his wife to be degraded in court. Consequently, even if she went to a halakhic authority and he dissolved her vows, such a solution is unacceptable to her husband, as he did not want her to go to court. Therefore, the betrothal is invalid even after the dissolution of the vow.**

A contradictory *baraita*, however, teaches that *halchah etzel chacham ve-hitira, eina mekudeshes* as well — even going to a sage and having the vow annulled fails to satisfy the condition. Both the sage route and the doctor route are equally ineffective. This directly contradicts the first *baraita*.

Raba resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha Rabbi Meir, ha Rabbi Eliezer* — the two *baraitot* reflect the positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer respectively.

The question driving this dispute is not merely whether the vow was retroactively voided. Even if we accept that the sage's annulment operates retroactively and the woman technically had no vow at the moment of *kiddushin*, the further question is: was the condition satisfied in a manner the husband would have accepted? And that depends on whether the husband is assumed to object to his wife going before a *beit din* to have a vow annulled. If a man considers it unseemly — *bizayon* — for his wife to appear before a rabbinic court for *hatarat nedarim*, then even though the vow was retroactively voided, the condition he set is not considered fulfilled, because he would never have intended to accept that route as a legitimate resolution of the condition.

## Meir v. Eliezer Details

### Remarriage Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? דִּתְנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשּׁוּם נֶדֶר — לֹא יַחְזִיר. מִשּׁוּם שֵׁם רָע — לֹא יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **What is this fundamental dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar? The Gemara answers: As we learned in a mishna (Gittin 45b): If a man who divorces his wife due to a vow that she took subsequently regrets his decision and wants to take her back, he may not remarry her. Similarly, if he divorces her due to a bad reputation she has acquired, he may not remarry her. This is because if he were allowed to take her back if the vow is dissolved by a halakhic authority or after discovering that the rumors about her were false, he might say that had he known this he never would have divorced her. Such a statement would retroactively cast doubt on the validity of the bill of divorce and could potentially cause her children from a second marriage to have the status of mamzerim. He is therefore informed that if he divorces her due to a vow or a bad reputation he can never remarry her.**

The underlying debate of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer is found in a *Mishnah* dealing with a different context: a man who divorces his wife because she has vows (*mishum neder*) or because of a bad reputation (*biglal shem ra*) — *lo yachazir* — he may not remarry her. The concern is that if he is later permitted to remarry her, he will be in a position to retroactively claim the divorce was an error, thereby creating grave problems for any intervening marriage and its children.

### Rashi: Unconditional

Rashi's interpretation of *lo yachazir* focuses on a different mechanism than that of Tosafot. Tosafot understands the prohibition as removing the incentive to make retroactive claims — if he cannot remarry her, he has no reason to claim the divorce was defective. Rashi, however, reads the prohibition as establishing at the outset that the divorce was unconditional and therefore irrevocable. By telling the husband that no matter what happens later, he will never be permitted to remarry her, the rabbis ensure that when he divorces her he does so with *gemirut da'at* — full and final resolve. There can therefore be no legitimate subsequent claim that he divorced her in error, because his divorce was declared absolute and final from the moment he gave the *get*.

### Meir: Sage Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל נֶדֶר שֶׁצָּרִיךְ חֲקִירַת חָכָם — לֹא יַחְזִיר. וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ חֲקִירַת חָכָם — יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: Any vow that requires investigation by a halakhic authority, he may not remarry her, for he can claim that had he known the vow could be dissolved he would never have divorced her. And with regard to a vow that does not require the investigation by a halakhic authority, he may remarry her. There is no concern that he might impair the bill of divorce, as he knew he could dissolve the vow and yet he chose to divorce her regardless.**

Rabbi Meir rules: *kol neder she-tzarich chakirat chacham lo yachazir* — for any vow that requires the intervention of a sage to annul, he may not remarry her; *ve-she-eino tzakich chakirat chacham, yachazir* — but if the vow is one that does not require a sage — meaning, a vow the husband himself could have annulled through *hafara* — he may remarry her.

### Meir: Logic

Rabbi Meir's reasoning is as follows. If the vow was one that the husband could have annulled himself through *hafara*, and he divorced her instead, he cannot later claim he would have acted differently had he known about his ability to annul. He knew, or should have known, that he had this power, and he divorced her anyway. The divorce therefore stands firmly, and remarriage is permissible.

But if the vow required a sage to annul, then the husband could legitimately say: had I known you could go to a *chacham* and have this vow retroactively eliminated, I never would have divorced you. According to Rabbi Meir, this is a plausible and legitimate claim, because Rabbi Meir implicitly assumes that a husband would *not* object to his wife going to a sage for *hatarat nedarim*. It is a reasonable course of action in his view. Therefore, the possibility of *hatarat nedarim* is something the husband should reasonably be expected to have wanted, and we prevent remarriage precisely to prevent him from making this destabilizing retroactive claim.

### Eliezer: No Bizayon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד צָרִיךְ וְאֶחָד אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ — לֹא יַחְזִיר. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא אָסְרוּ צָרִיךְ אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דִּכְתִיב:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Both in the case of a vow that requires investigation by a halakhic authority and one that does not require it, he may not remarry her. Rabbi Elazar said in explanation of his view: They prohibited remarrying a woman who was bound by a vow that requires investigation by a halakhic authority only due to a vow that does not require such investigation. The Gemara explains the respective opinions: What is the reason for Rabbi Yehuda's statement? As it is written:**

Rabbi Eliezer takes the opposite position. He rules: *lo asru zeh ela mipnei she-eino zeh* — they only prohibited remarriage in the case requiring a sage as a *gezerah* on account of the case not requiring a sage. The fundamental case of concern for Rabbi Eliezer is the *hafara* case: where the husband himself could have nullified the vow but did not realize he had that power. In that case he can say *had I known I could have been mefer it, I never would have divorced her* — a fully legitimate claim of error. To prevent such claims, the rabbis also prohibited remarriage in the *chacham* case, even though in the *chacham* case there is no legitimate retroactive claim. Why not? Because, says Rabbi Eliezer, *ein adam rotzeh she-tit'bazeh ishto be-veit din* — a man does not want his wife to be subjected to the process of appearing before a *beit din*. It is a matter of dignity and reputation. The husband would never have wanted that scenario, and therefore he cannot come after the fact and say he would have preferred it.

### Applied to Tnai

With this debate now in hand, Raba applies it back to our *kiddushin* case. When a man betroths a woman on condition that she has no vows, and she does have a vow but subsequently obtains *hatarat nedarim* from a sage — is the condition considered satisfied?

According to Rabbi Meir, yes. Rabbi Meir holds that a husband finds it entirely acceptable for his wife to appear before a sage for *hatarat nedarim*. Since the sage's annulment operates retroactively to void the vow from inception, at the moment of *kiddushin* she effectively had no vow. And since the husband has no objection to the mechanism by which that retroactive elimination was achieved, the condition is considered fulfilled and she is *mekudeshes*.

According to Rabbi Eliezer, no. Rabbi Eliezer holds that a husband does *not* want his wife appearing before a *beit din* for *hatarat nedarim* — it involves a degree of public exposure and *bizayon* that he finds unacceptable. Therefore, even if the vow was retroactively voided through *hatarat nedarim*, the condition "she has no vows" was not satisfied in a manner the husband would have countenanced. He intended to marry a woman who had no vows *and* who would never need to seek rabbinic intervention of this kind. The condition is therefore not met, and she remains not *mekudeshes*.

The first *baraita* — which rules that *hatarat nedarim* satisfies the condition — follows Rabbi Meir. The second *baraita* — which rules it does not — follows Rabbi Eliezer. And here the sugya rests.
