# Ketubot 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Anan & Gifts

### Fish Gift Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב עָנָן אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ הַהוּא גַּבְרָא כַּנְתָּא דְגִילְדָּנֵי דְּבֵי גִילֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבִידְתָּיךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי. לָא קַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain man** who once **brought to Rav Anan a basket of small fish [*gildanei devei gilei*]. He said to him: What are you doing** here? The man **said to him: I have a case** to present before you. Rav Anan would **not accept** the basket **from him,** and **he said to him: I am disqualified from** presiding over **your case,** due to your actions.

The daf opens with a story about *Rav Anan*. A certain man brought him a basket of small fish and, when asked what brought him there, openly stated that he had a case in judgment. *Rav Anan* refused the gift. *Lo kabil minei* — he did not accept it — and told the man: "You have invalidated me from serving as your judge." This mirrors the earlier stories on the daf, where the rabbis not only recused themselves from judgment but refused to receive the accompanying gifts as well, even though in principle one could imagine doing one without the other. The man responded that he was content to have his case heard elsewhere — *dina demar lo beina* — but asked that *Rav Anan* at least accept the gift as a *mitzvah*, independent of any judicial role, because he wished to perform the act of *kiruvei bikkurim*.

### Gifts as Bikkurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא דְּמָר לָא בָּעֵינָא, קַבּוֹלֵי לְקַבֵּיל מָר דְּלָא לִמְנְעַן מָר מֵאַקְרוֹבֵי בִּכּוּרִים. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאִישׁ בָּא מִבַּעַל שָׁלִישָׁה וַיָּבֵא לְאִישׁ הָאֱלֹהִים לֶחֶם בִּכּוּרִים עֶשְׂרִים לֶחֶם שְׂעוֹרִים וְכַרְמֶל בְּצִקְלוֹנוֹ״. וְכִי אֱלִישָׁע אוֹכֵל בִּכּוּרִים הֲוָה? אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: כׇּל הַמֵּבִיא דּוֹרוֹן לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם — כְּאִילּוּ מַקְרִיב בִּכּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The man said to him: I do not need the Master's judgment.** However, **let the Master accept my gift** anyway, **so that the Master does not prevent me from presenting first fruits.** What does the mitzva of first fruits have to with this situation? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"And there came a man came from Ba'al Shalisha, and he brought the man of God bread of the first fruits, twenty loaves of barley and fresh ears of corn in his sack"** (II Kings 4:42). **But did Elisha,** the recipient of these gifts, **eat first fruits?** After all, he was not a priest. **Rather,** this verse comes **to tell you: Whoever brings a gift to a Torah scholar, it is as though he has presented first fruits.** This visitor to Rav Anan wished to fulfill this mitzva.

The man's argument invokes a *braita*: the verse in the story of *Elisha* describes a man bringing bread of *bikkurim* — twenty loaves of barley bread and *karmel* — to the *ish ha'Elohim*. The *gemara* asks: did *Elisha* eat *bikkurim*? *Rashi* attempts to prove that *Elisha* was not a *kohen*; *Tosafot* notes the simpler answer that they were not in *Yerushalayim* and this was not being brought to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Rather, *ela lomar lach* — the verse comes to teach — that anyone who brings a gift to a *talmid chacham* is as if he brought *bikkurim*. That is the *mitzvah* the man wished to perform.

### Aliyah & Rebbe

This moment opens a broader reflection on how *Chazal* dealt with the absence of the *Beit HaMikdash* and created meaningful substitutes. Most striking in this regard is the idea that just as there is a *mitzvah* of *aliyah laregel*, so too the *gemara* teaches there is a *mitzvah* of *mekabel pnei rabo baregel* — presenting oneself before one's *rebbe* on the festivals. The *rebbe* in some sense takes the place of the *Beit HaMikdash* as the destination of spiritual pilgrimage. This is already hinted at in the *pesukim* themselves. The husband of the *Shunamit* says to her: *madua at holechet elav hayom lo chodesh velo Shabbat* — why are you going to him today, it is neither *Rosh Chodesh* nor *Shabbat*? — indicating that one of the practices of the time was to travel to the *ish Elohim* on *Shabbat* and spend the day in spiritual communion. And in *Yeshayahu* the vision of the end of days includes *umidei Shabbat beshabbato yavo kol basar lehishtachavot lefanai* — each *Shabbat* all flesh will come to prostrate before God. The *ish Elohim* in *Shmuel* and the eschatological vision in *Yeshayahu* share the same rhythm, and that pattern becomes the foundation for *mekabel pnei rav* as the post-*Beit HaMikdash* substitute for *aliyah laregel*.

### Gift Accepted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַבּוֹלֵי לָא בָּעֵינַן דְּאֵיקַבֵּיל, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ לִי טַעְמָא, מְקַבֵּילְנָא. שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: נִידַיְּינֵיהּ מָר לְהַאי גַּבְרָא, דַּאֲנָא עָנָן פְּסִילְנָא לֵיהּ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Anan **said to him: I do not want to take it from you,** but **now that you have explained to me the reason** that you wish to give it to me **I will accept it** from you. Rav Anan **sent** the man **to Rav Naḥman,** and **he** also **sent him** a letter: **Let the Master judge this man's** case **because I, Anan, am disqualified from judging his** cases.

Returning to the story: the *gemara* concludes that *Rav Anan*, upon hearing the man's reasoning, accepted the gift. *Hashta d'amart li ta'ama* — now that you've given me a proper rationale — perhaps the explanation also freed the gift from any taint of *shochad* by reframing it entirely, *mikabelna*. He accepted it. But he would still not serve as the man's judge. And, as we will see, the story does not end there.

## Nachman & Bias

### Referral Letter

Because the man still needed his case heard, *Rav Anan* sent him before *Rav Nachman* and accompanied the referral with a letter: *dayneye l'mar l'hai gavra* — please hear this man's case — *d'ana Anan pasilna lei l'dina* — for I, *Anan*, am invalid to serve as his judge.

### Misinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: מִדִּשְׁלַח לִי הָכִי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָרִיבֵיהּ הוּא. הֲוָה קָאֵים דִּינָא דְיַתְמֵי קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר:
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said** to himself: **From the fact that he sent me this** letter, I can **conclude from here** that the reason he is disqualified from judging the case is because **he is his relative.** At that time, **a case involving orphans was** being heard **before** Rav Naḥman. **He said:**

There are multiple reasons one might be invalid as a judge. When *Rav Nachman* received this message, *mideshlach li hachi shma mina kriveye hu* — since he sent me this, it must be that this person is his relative — and that is why *Rav Anan* had to recuse himself.

### Two Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי עֲשֵׂה וְהַאי עֲשֵׂה — עֲשֵׂה דִּכְבוֹד תּוֹרָה עֲדִיף. סַלְּקֵיהּ לְדִינָא דְּיַתְמֵי וְאַחֲתֵיהּ לְדִינֵיהּ, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא בַּעַל דִּינֵיהּ יְקָרָא דְּקָא עָבֵיד לַיהּ, אִיסְתַּתֻּם טַעְנָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is a positive mitzva,** for judges to judge cases properly, **and this is a positive mitzva,** to honor Torah scholars and their families. Rav Naḥman concluded that **the positive mitzva of** giving **honor to the Torah takes precedence.** Therefore, **he put aside the case of the orphans and settled down to judge** the case of that man, under the mistaken assumption that he was a relative of Rav Anan. **Once the** other **litigant saw the honor being accorded to** that man by the judge, he grew nervous until his mouth, i.e., his ability to argue **his claim, became closed,** and he lost the case. In this manner, justice was perverted by Rav Anan, albeit unwittingly and indirectly.

At that moment, *Rav Nachman* had before him a case involving orphans. When the letter arrived requesting he hear this other man's case, he recognized that he faced two *mitzvot*: the obligation to adjudicate the orphans' case — a general *mitzvah* of *ushfatetem tzedek*, and as the *Maharsha* adds, also the *mitzvah* of *lo takiru panim bamishpat k'katon k'gadol tishm'un* with its particular application to vulnerable parties like orphans — and, on the other side, the *mitzvah* of *kavod Torah*, since hearing this man's case first would show honor to *Rav Anan* and thereby to *Torah* itself.

### Orphans Suspended

*Rav Nachman* resolved to set aside the orphans' case and hear this man's case first: *salik l'dina d'yasmei v'achtei l'dineye*.

### Silenced Party

The consequence was immediate and devastating. When the opposing litigant saw that *Rav Nachman* was setting aside the orphans' case to hear this man's — apparently because he had arrived with a letter from *Rav Anan* — it appeared to him as unambiguous favoritism. *Istatam tanateye* — he was silenced. He did not feel free to fully articulate his claim and position, because he perceived that the outcome was already determined.

### Bias & Perception

This story is striking in several respects. The earlier stories on the daf illustrated how a bribe, even when refused, exerts subconscious influence on the judge. Here, *Rav Nachman* received no bribe at all. He merely believed the man to be *Rav Anan*'s relative, and he was not even trying to favor the litigant himself — he was trying to honor *Rav Anan*. Yet even that action, taken for entirely legitimate reasons, was sufficient to silence the opposing party and potentially pervert the course of justice. The question is not only whether the judge will be impartial, but what messages his actions communicate and how those messages affect the people whose cases are being heard.

This dynamic appears in real-world settings as well. In cases heard before *batei din*, including those with strong reputations for fairness, similar dynamics can arise: a woman appearing before an all-male court may already feel inhibited from fully stating her case; if the *dayanim* happen to know the other party socially and exchange greetings with him at the outset, the effect on the opposing side can be immediate and silencing, regardless of whether any actual bias exists. The lesson is that it is not sufficient that the judge be impartial in his own mind; the culture, conduct, and visible signals within the courtroom must themselves be beyond reproach. *Kavod Torah* is a genuine consideration, but when it conflicts with the integrity of *din*, the *gemara* here suggests it must yield.

One might ask whether there is also an issue of *chalishut ha-da'at* — the harm caused to a party who has had their case interrupted mid-hearing. The *Maharsha* discusses this in connection with *Sanhedrin*. But the primary emphasis here is on the way in which even well-intentioned judicial conduct communicates bias and suppresses the speech of litigants.

## Anan & Eliyahu

### Eliyahu Visits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב עָנָן הֲוָה רְגִיל אֵלִיָּהוּ דְּאָתֵי גַּבֵּיהּ, דַּהֲוָה מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ סֵדֶר דְּאֵלִיָּהוּ. כֵּיוָן דַּעֲבַד הָכִי, אִיסְתַּלַּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Elijah** the Prophet **was accustomed to come** and visit **Rav Anan, as** the prophet **was teaching him** the statements that would later be recorded in the volume ***Seder deEliyahu*,** the Order of Elijah. **Once** Rav Anan **did this** and caused a miscarriage of justice, Elijah **departed.**

*Rav Anan* had a standing relationship with *Eliyahu HaNavi*: *Rav Anan havei ragil Eliyahu de-ati gabei* — *Eliyahu* would regularly come to visit him. During these visits, *Eliyahu* taught him *Seder de-Eliyahu*, that collection of stories and teachings associated with *Eliyahu HaNavi* — specifically *Seder Eliyahu Rabba* and *Seder Eliyahu Zuta*, as we will see in a moment.

### Eliyahu Withdrawn

*Keivan de-avad hachi* — once *Rav Anan* acted as he did — sending the letter, accepting the fish — *Eliyahu* withdrew. *Istalek*. This is a remarkable consequence, particularly because *Rav Anan* had not acted with malicious intent. Yet as noted, the problem is often not what one deliberately does, but what one fails to think through — the unintended consequences of one's actions.

### Fast & Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב בְּתַעֲנִיתָא וּבְעָא רַחֲמֵי וַאֲתָא. כִּי אֲתָא, הֲוָה מְבַעֵית לֵיהּ בַּעוֹתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Anan **sat in** observance of **a fast and prayed for mercy, and** Elijah **came** back. However, **when** Elijah **came** after that, **he would scare him,** as he would appear in frightening forms.

*Rav Anan* sat in a fast, *yativ be-ta'anita*, and prayed for mercy, *u-va'ei rachamei*, and *Eliyahu* returned. But when *Eliyahu* came back, *Rav Anan* was frightened of him — *havei miv-it lei be-vuta'ei* — perhaps in the wake of the withdrawal.

### Box Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַעֲבַד תֵּיבוּתָא וִיתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ עַד דְּאַפֵּיק לֵיהּ סִידְרֵיהּ. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי: סֵדֶר דְּאֵלִיָּהוּ רַבָּה, סֵדֶר אֵלִיָּהוּ זוּטָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav Anan **made a box** where he settled himself down **and he sat before** Elijah **until he took out for him,** i.e., taught him, all of **his *Seder*. And this is what** the Sages mean when **they say: *Seder deEliyahu Rabba*,** the Major Order of Elijah, and ***Seder Eliyahu Zuta*,** the Minor Order of Elijah, as the first order was taught prior to this incident and the second came after it.

To manage his fear, *Rav Anan* constructed a box and sat inside it while *Eliyahu* continued to teach him — *ve-avad teivuta ve-yativ kamei* — until *Eliyahu* finished imparting the complete *seder* to him. This is the origin of the two versions: *Seder Eliyahu Rabba* and *Seder Eliyahu Zuta*.

### Rabba vs Zuta

*Rashi* explains that *Seder Eliyahu Rabba* is what was taught *outside* the box, and *Seder Eliyahu Zuta* is what was taught *inside* the box, without explaining why one is larger than the other. *Tosafot* explains: *Eliyahu* had already taught *Rav Anan* the majority of the material before the incident, and so the first, larger portion — taught before the disruption — is called *Rabba*, and the smaller remaining portion — taught within the box — is called *Zuta*. Perhaps, too, the teaching inside the box was of somewhat lesser quality or depth.

### Mida Kneged Mida

The *Maharsha* identifies a principle of *mida keneged mida* in the two aspects of *Rav Anan*'s response. The *ta'anit* served as atonement for having accepted the fish — even though his acceptance was grounded in a legitimate argument about *bikkurim*, he now acknowledged it was a mistake. The trembling before *Eliyahu* and the need to create a barrier — the box — corresponded to what *Rav Anan*'s actions had caused the opposing litigant to experience: the silencing, the pulling back, the fear to speak freely when one senses that the dynamic in the room is working against you. *Rav Anan* now experienced, in a different register, precisely what his conduct had imposed on another.

## Yosef & Famine

### Famine Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁנֵי דְּרַב יוֹסֵף הֲוָה רִיתְחָא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב יוֹסֵף: לִיבְעֵי מָר רַחֲמֵי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה אֱלִישָׁע דְּכִי הֲווֹ רַבָּנַן מִיפַּטְרִי מִקַּמֵּיהּ, הֲווֹ פָּיְישִׁי תְּרֵי אַלְפֵי וּמָאתַן רַבָּנַן, בְּעִידָּן רִיתְחָא לָא הֲוָה בָּעֵי רַחֲמֵי, אֲנָא אֶיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **In the years of Rav Yosef there was** a divine **anger,** manifested by world hunger. **The Sages said to Rav Yosef: Let the Master pray for mercy** concerning this decree. **He said to them: Now, if** in the case of the prophet **Elisha, when the Sages would take their leave of him, 2,200 Sages would remain** behind whom he would support from his own pocket, and yet he **would not pray for mercy at a time of** divine **anger** and famine, **should I pray for mercy?**

The *gemara* shifts to a passage set in the years of *Rav Yosef*. There was *ritcha* — divine anger, meaning famine — and the rabbis asked *Rav Yosef* to pray for its removal.

### Elisha Precedent

*Rav Yosef* responded by pointing to the precedent of *Elisha*: in *Elisha*'s time there was also divine anger, *de-havi rabanan miftari mikamei* — when the rabbis would leave *Elisha*'s learning — *havei payshei trei alfein u'masan rabanan* — there would still be 2,200 rabbis remaining in the *beis medrash*. And even in a time of *ritcha*, *Elisha* did not pray to remove the divine anger — or at least there is no record in *Tanach* that he did. *Rav Yosef*'s implicit point: even at that level of Torah stature, prayer was not offered. How much more so should I not be expected to do so?

### 2200 Students

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּפָיְישִׁי הָכִי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר מְשָׁרְתוֹ מָה אֶתֵּן זֶה לִפְנֵי מֵאָה אִישׁ״. מַאי ״לִפְנֵי מֵאָה אִישׁ״? אִילֵימָא דְּכוּלְּהוּ, לִפְנֵי מֵאָה אִישׁ בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת טוּבָא הֲווֹ! אֶלָּא דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד קַמֵּי מֵאָה אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it derived **that this** number of scholars **would remain** behind with Elisha? **As it is written: "And his servant said: How should I set this before a hundred men"** (II Kings 4:43). **What** is the meaning of **"before a hundred men"? If we say that all of** the gifts that he had received, i.e., the first fruits, twenty loaves of barley, and fresh ears of corn mentioned in the preceding verse, were meant to be placed **before one hundred men, in years of drought** and famine **this was a good** deal of food, which would have sufficed for them. **Rather,** it must mean **that each and every one** of the loaves was to be placed **before one hundred men.** Since he had twenty loaves plus two meals of first-fruits and ears of corn, there must have been 2,200 people present.

One might ask: how do we know that *Elisha* had 2,200 students? The source is the verse in which *Elisha*'s attendant says *u'mah eten zeh lifnei meah ish* — how shall I set this before 100 men? The verse refers back to the same *pasuk* that opened our discussion of *bikkurim*: the man from *Ba'al Shalishah* brought twenty loaves of barley bread and *karmel*.

### Bread Math

The calculation: if those 22 loaves were distributed, one per 100 men — each loaf set before 100 people — then 22 loaves times 100 people equals 2,200 people total. That is the size of the *talmidim* who remained in *Elisha*'s *beis medrash*.

## Yeshiva Decline

### Rav: 1,200

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲווֹ מִיפַּטְרִי רַבָּנַן מִבֵּי רַב, הֲווֹ פָּיְישִׁי אַלְפָּא וּמָאתַן רַבָּנַן. מִבֵּי רַב הוּנָא, הֲווֹ פָּיְישִׁי תַּמְנֵי מְאָה רַבָּנַן. רַב הוּנָא הֲוָה דָּרֵישׁ בִּתְלֵיסַר אָמוֹרָאֵי. כִּי הֲווֹ קָיְימִי רַבָּנַן מִמְּתִיבְתָּא דְּרַב הוּנָא וְנָפְצִי גְּלִימַיְיהוּ הֲוָה סָלֵיק אַבְקָא וְכָסֵי לֵיהּ לְיוֹמָא, וְאָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: קָמוּ לֵיהּ מִמְּתִיבְתָּא דְּרַב הוּנָא בַּבְלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Incidentally, the Gemara relates: **When the Sages would take their leave from the school** of **Rav, 1,200 Sages would remain** behind to continue their studies. When the Sages would take their leave **from the school** of **Rav Huna, eight hundred Sages would remain** behind. **Rav Huna would expound** the lesson **by** means of **thirteen speakers,** who would repeat his statements to the crowds that had gathered to hear him. **When the Sages would arise** from listening to lectures **in the yeshiva of Rav Huna and dust off their cloaks, the dust would rise and block out the sun,** forming a dust cloud that could be seen from afar. **And they would say in the West,** in Eretz Yisrael: **The** scholars **have** just **arisen in the yeshiva of Rav Huna the Babylonian.**

The *gemara* traces the decline of *yeshiva* enrollment across generations. When the rabbis would leave the *yeshiva* of *Rav*, there would remain *alfa u'masan rabanan* — 1,200 rabbis still in the *beis medrash*.

### Huna: 800

When the rabbis left the *yeshiva* of *Rav Huna*, there would remain 800 rabbis. *Rav Huna* taught with 13 *amoraim* — not *amoraim* in the sense of the rabbinic generation, but in the technical sense of the role: the *amora* was the *rav*'s loudspeaker, who would repeat and project the teacher's words to different sections of the large assembly hall. With 13 such figures distributed throughout the room, the scope of *Rav Huna*'s audience can be appreciated.

### Dust Covers Sun

When the rabbis would rise from *Rav Huna*'s *masivta* and shake out their garments, *havei salik avka* — dust would rise up — *ve'kasei leh leyoma* — and cover the sun, darkening the day. In *Eretz Yisrael* they would say: *kamu lehu mimasivta deRav Huna deBavel* — the Babylonian *yeshiva* of *Rav Huna* has finished.

### Rava & Yosef: 400

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מִיפַּטְרִי רַבָּנַן מִבֵּי רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף, הֲווֹ פָּיְישִׁי אַרְבַּע מְאָה רַבָּנַן, וְקָרוּ לְנַפְשַׁיְיהוּ: ״יַתְמֵי״. כִּי הֲווֹ מִיפַּטְרִי רַבָּנַן מִבֵּי אַבָּיֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מִבֵּי רַב פָּפָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מִבֵּי רַב אָשֵׁי, הֲווֹ פָּיְישִׁי מָאתַן רַבָּנַן, וְקָרוּ נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ ״יַתְמֵי דְּיַתְמֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** **When the Sages would take their leave from the school** of **Rabba and Rav Yosef, four hundred Sages would remain** behind, **and they would refer to themselves as orphans,** as they were the only ones left from the entire crowd. **When the Sages would take their leave from the school** of **Abaye, and some say from the school** of **Rav Pappa, and some say from the school** of **Rav Ashi, two hundred scholars would remain** behind, **and they would refer to themselves as orphans of orphans.**

When the rabbis left the *yeshiva* of *Rava* and *Rav Yosef*, there remained 400 rabbis. *Ve-karu le-nafshaihu yasmei* — they called themselves orphans. Given the size of the previous generations — 2,200 under *Elisha*, 1,200 under *Rav*, 800 under *Rav Huna* — the descent to 400 felt like bereavement.

### Later: 200

When the rabbis left the *yeshiva* of *Abaye*, or according to others the *yeshiva* of *Rav Pappa*, or *Rav Ashi*, there remained only 200 rabbis. *Ve-karu le-nafshaihu yasmei de-yasmei* — orphans of orphans — so small a remnant compared to what had been.

## Temple Payments

### Blemish Inspectors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר רְדִיפָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מְבַקְּרֵי מוּמִין שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, הָיוּ נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַמְלַמְּדִין הִלְכוֹת שְׁחִיטָה לְכֹהֲנִים, הָיוּ נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the issue of those who receive their wages from public funds. **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Redifa said** that **Rabbi Ami said: Inspectors of blemishes** of consecrated animals **in Jerusalem,** who would examine all animals brought to be sacrificed in the Temple to verify that they were free of any blemishes that would disqualify them from being sacrificed on the altar, **would take their wages from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber. Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Torah scholars who teach the *halakhot* of slaughter to the priests** of the Temple **would take their wages from** the **collection of the chamber.**

The *gemara* returns to the principal theme of the chapter, which emerged from the *Mishnah*'s reference to *shnei dayanei gezerot* and the question of their payment from communal funds. *Rav Yitzchak bar Redifa* said in the name of *Avani*: those in *Yerushalayim* whose role it was to inspect animals for blemishes — *mevakrim mumin she-be-Yerushalayim* — received their salary from *terumat ha-lishka*, the communal funds collected annually for the *korbanot*. When there were surplus funds from the *lishka*, they were also available for other Temple-related or communal services. The inspection of blemishes is clearly *tzorchei korban* — directly connected to the sacrificial service.

### Shechita Teachers

*Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Shmuel*: the *talmidei chachamim* who taught the *kohanim* the *hilchot shechita* also received their pay from *terumat ha-lishka*, for this too falls under *tzorchei korban*. This calls to mind the stories at the beginning of *Yoma* about teaching the *Kohen Gadol* the laws of the *avoda*. The role of teaching the *halachot* is distinct from the physical transmission of practical skill — a *kohen* may receive the practical skill through tradition, but the legal framework governing each scenario had to be formally studied. Hence it is classified as a Temple service deserving communal support.

### Kemitza Teachers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַמְלַמְּדִים הִלְכוֹת קְמִיצָה לַכֹּהֲנִים, נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַגִּיהֵי סְפָרִים שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, הָיוּ נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: Torah scholars who teach the *halakhot* of the removal of a handful to the priests would take their wages from the collection of the chamber.** All these scholars were constantly engaged in work necessary for the functioning of the Temple, and therefore they would receive their wages from the Temple treasury. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The proofreaders of** the Torah **scrolls in Jerusalem would take their wages from the collection of the chamber.**

*Rav Giddel* said in the name of *Rav*: those *talmidei chachamim* who taught the *kohanim* the *hilchot kemitza* similarly received their salary from *terumat ha-lishka*. The same principle applies: it is the legal dimension of the service — what constitutes a valid *kemitza*, what invalidates it — that requires instruction, and that instruction is considered integral to the *korban* service.

### Torah Proofers

*Rabba bar bar Chana* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: those who proofread *sifrei Torah* in *Yerushalayim* — *maggihei sefarim she-be-Yerushalayim* — also received their payment from *terumat ha-lishka*. This is more surprising, because proofreading *sifrei Torah* is not directly connected to the *korbanot*. As we will continue to see on this daf, *terumat ha-lishka* and particularly *shiyarei terumat ha-lishka* served as a broad catch-all for a range of Temple-related and communal needs. *Rashi* here notes the relevant justification: *lev beit din matne aleihem* — the *beit din* reserved the authority to reassign these funds as it saw fit. Anyone donating the *shekalim* understood that the ultimate disposition of the money lay with the *beit din*'s discretion, even if the primary purpose was the *korbanot*.

### Parochet Weavers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב: נָשִׁים הָאוֹרְגוֹת בַּפָּרֹכוֹת, נוֹטְלוֹת שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר, מִקׇּדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת. הוֹאִיל וּפָרֹכוֹת תַּחַת בִּנְיָן עֲשׂוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rav said: The women who weave the curtains** that separate the Temple Sanctuary from the Holy of Holies **would take their wages from the collection of the chamber.** Rav Naḥman added: **But I say** that they would not be paid from the collection of the chamber; rather, their salary would come **from** the funds **consecrated for Temple maintenance.** Why? **Since the curtains served in place** of the solid construction of the **building,** they were part of the Temple itself. Therefore, any work performed for the curtains should be paid for from money allocated for building purposes, not from the funds collected to pay for offerings and the daily needs of the Temple.

*Rav Nachman* said in the name of *Rabbah bar Avuha*: the women who wove the *parochet* — the curtain for the *Beit HaMikdash* — also received their payment from *terumat ha-lishka*. This immediately prompts a question: is the *parochet* related to the *korbanot* or is it really a structural element of the *Beit HaMikdash*, which would place it under *bedek ha-bayit*?

The *gemara* cites a dissenting view: *ve'ani omer mi-kodshei bedek ha-bayit* — I say it comes from *bedek ha-bayit* — *hu parochet tachat binyan asuyot* — because the *parochet* is made underneath the roofed structure. The logic is that something hanging within a roofed enclosure becomes part of the structure itself, not merely a suspended object. This distinction has a fascinating parallel in *hilchot Shabbat*: if one hangs a curtain across an open doorway, according to the *Chazon Ish* one is adding to the structure and not merely decorating it, making it more like installing a door than hanging a picture on a wall. The *parochet* hanging within the *heichal* functions similarly — it is integral to the architectural space.

The *gemara* then raises an objection from a *braita*: the women weaving the *parochet*, the *Beis Garmo* who taught the preparation of *lechem ha-panim*, and the *Beis Avtinas* who knew how to prepare the *ketoret* — all of them received their pay from *terumat ha-lishka*, not from *bedek ha-bayit*. The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hatam be-divavi* — that *braita* refers to the curtains hanging in the gates, which were in the open, unroofed areas of the Temple complex. Those would indeed be classified differently — more like a *kli* than part of the *binyan*.

### 13 Parochet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: נָשִׁים הָאוֹרְגוֹת בַּפָּרֹכוֹת, וּבֵית גַּרְמוּ עַל מַעֲשֵׂה לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, וּבֵית אַבְטִינָס עַל מַעֲשֵׂה הַקְּטֹרֶת — כּוּלָּן הָיוּ נוֹטְלוֹת שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה! הָתָם בִּדְבָבֵי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה פָּרֹכוֹת הָיוּ בְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי, שֶׁבַע כְּנֶגֶד שִׁבְעָה שְׁעָרִים, אַחַת לְפִתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל, וְאַחַת לְפִתְחוֹ שֶׁל אוּלָם. שְׁתַּיִם בַּדְּבִיר, שְׁתַּיִם כְּנֶגְדָּן בָּעֲלִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this: **The women who weave the curtains, and the house of Garmu,** who were in charge **of the preparation of the shewbread, and the house of Avtinas,** who were in charge **of the preparation of the incense, all would take their wages from the collection of the chamber.** This contradicts Rav Naḥman's claim. The Gemara answers: **There,** it is referring to the curtains **of the gates,** which were not considered part of the actual Temple building but were decorative in purpose. **As Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said: There were thirteen curtains in the Second Temple, seven opposite,** i.e., on the inside of, **seven gates, one at the entrance to the Sanctuary, one at the entrance to the Entrance Hall, two** additional curtains **within the partition,** in the Holy of Holies in place of the one-cubit partition, **and two corresponding to them** above **in the upper chamber.**

*Rav Zeira* said in the name of *Rav*: there were thirteen *parochet* curtains in the *Beit HaMikdash*. Seven corresponded to the seven gates. One was for the opening of the *heichal*, one for the opening of the *ulam*. Two were in the *dvir* — the *Kodesh HaKodashim* — and two corresponding ones above, since the *heichal* complex was a double-story structure. The curtains within the roofed structure — the *ulam*, *heichal*, and *dvir* — fall into the category of *bedek ha-bayit*; those at the gates in the open courtyards fall under *terumat ha-lishka*. Where a *parochet* does not come under *bedek ha-bayit*, it defaults to *terumat ha-lishka*, even though *terumat ha-lishka* is in principle designated for *korbanot* rather than all of these auxiliary purposes.

## Fund Sources

### Para Aduma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָשִׁים הַמְגַדְּלוֹת בְּנֵיהֶן לַפָּרָה — הָיוּ נוֹטְלוֹת שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: נָשִׁים יְקָרוֹת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיוּ זָנוֹת אוֹתָן וּמְפַרְנְסוֹת אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **the women who raise their children for** the red **heifer,** i.e., who would raise their children in special places so that they would live their entire lives up to that point in a state of ritual purity, enabling them to draw the water for the purposes of the ritual of the red heifer, these women **would take their wages from the collection of the chamber. Abba Shaul said:** Their wages would not come from the collection of the chamber. Instead, wealthy and **prominent women of Jerusalem would sustain them and provide them with a livelihood.**

The *gemara* continues with another case. The women who raised their children for the *para aduma* service also received their support from *terumat ha-lishka*. The background: the *Tzadokim* maintained that the *kohen* performing the burning of the *para aduma* could never have been *tamei* at any point. The *Chachamim*, in a remarkable counter-move, deliberately arranged for the *kohen* to become *tamei* and then immerse in a *mikveh*, so that he would be a *tvul yom* at the time of the service — thereby demonstrating through practice that the law followed their understanding, not the *Tzadokim*'s. This very leniency on one end was balanced by extraordinary stringencies in other areas of *tahara* related to the *para aduma*. One of those stringencies involved raising children in conditions of guaranteed *tahara* from birth. Pregnant women would give birth in specially constructed spaces above caves, where the cave ceiling would block any potential *tumat ha-met* from bodies buried in the earth below. The children born there were raised entirely in those conditions, and they would assist in the preparatory stages of the *para aduma* service. The communal funds of *terumat ha-lishka* provided the financial support for the women maintaining these households.

*Abba Shaul* offers an alternative: the prominent women of *Yerushalayim* — *nashim yakirot she-be-Yerushalayim* — would voluntarily provide food, clothing, and other support for these women and children, so that the burden did not need to fall on *terumat ha-lishka* at all. *Rashi* specifies *levush u-chsut* as among the provisions — echoing the *zona u-mefarnes* language familiar from the laws of *ketuba*.

### Vessels Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב הוּנָא מֵרַב: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּעָשׂוּ מִקׇּדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת? צוֹרֶךְ מִזְבֵּחַ נִינְהוּ, וּמִקׇּדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אָתוּ? אוֹ צוֹרֶךְ קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ, וּמִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין נַעֲשִׂין אֶלָּא מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna raised a dilemma before Rav:** Concerning Temple **service vessels, what is** the *halakha* with regard to the possibility **that they may be prepared by** using money **consecrated for Temple maintenance?** The Gemara explains the two sides of the dilemma: **Are they requirements of the altar, and** therefore they **came from** money **consecrated for Temple maintenance, or are they requirements of offerings, and** therefore **they were prepared from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber?** Rav **said to him: They are prepared only from the collection of the chamber.**

*Rav Huna* asked *Rav*: what is the source for the *klei shares*, the Temple service vessels? Do they come from *kodshei bedek ha-bayit*, or not? The framing of the question reflects what we have learned: *bedek ha-bayit* funds are for structural matters, while *terumat ha-lishka* is the catch-all for everything else. The vessels could be seen as *tzorech mizbe'ach* — connected to the altar, which is so large and fixed it functions almost as part of the structure itself, suggesting *bedek ha-bayit* — or as *tzorech korban* — used in the actual bringing of *korbanot*, suggesting *terumat ha-lishka*.

### Vessels Answer

*Rav* answered: they come from *terumat ha-lishka*.

### Proof Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״וּכְכַלּוֹתָם הֵבִיאוּ לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ וִיהוֹיָדָע (הַכֹּהֵן) אֶת שְׁאָר הַכֶּסֶף וַיַּעֲשֵׂהוּ כֵלִים לְבֵית ה׳ כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת וְגוֹ׳״!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **raised an objection to** this from a verse that deals with those in charge of maintaining the Temple structure: **"And when they had made an end, they brought the rest of the money before the king and Jehoiada, of which were made vessels for the house of the Lord, vessels with which to minister,** and buckets, and pans, and vessels of gold and silver" (II Chronicles 24:14). This indicates that vessels may be prepared with money consecrated for Temple maintenance.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this from *Divrei HaYamim*: *vaya'asu keilim le-veis Hashem klei shares* — they made service vessels for the *Beit HaShem* — and the context is the leftover money from the *bedek ha-bayit* collection. This appears to show that the *klei shares* came from *bedek ha-bayit* funds.

### Melachim Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאַקְרְיָיךְ כְּתוּבֵי לָא אַקְרְיָיךְ נְבִיאֵי, ״אַךְ לֹא יֵעָשֶׂה בֵּית ה׳ סִפּוֹת וְגוֹ׳ כִּי לְעֹשֵׂי הַמְּלָאכָה יִתְּנֻהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to him: Whoever taught you the Writings did not teach you the Prophets,** as you forgot about the parallel verse in the Prophets: **"But there were not made for the house of the Lord cups** of silver, snuffers, basins, trumpets, any vessels of gold, or vessels of silver, of the money that was brought into the house of the Lord; **for they gave that to those who did the work"** (II Kings 12:14–15). This verse proves that vessels were not prepared with the money donated for Temple maintenance.

The parallel account in *Melachim* resolves this: *ach lo ye'aseh beis Hashem safos kesef... hem le-melacha yisnehu* — the money collected for the *Beit HaShem* was not to be used for vessels, but was to be given to the craftsmen who would work on the structure. This directly contradicts the *Divrei HaYamim* passage.

### Lev Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, קָשׁוּ קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן שֶׁגָּבוּ וְהוֹתִירוּ, כָּאן שֶׁגָּבוּ וְלֹא הוֹתִירוּ. וְכִי גָּבוּ וְהוֹתִירוּ מַאי הָוֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לֵב בֵּית דִּין מַתְנֶה עֲלֵיהֶן, אִם הוּצְרְכוּ — הוּצְרְכוּ, וְאִם לָאו — יְהוּ לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, the verses contradict each other,** as in one place it states that the Temple vessels may be funded with the money donated for Temple maintenance, while in the other verse it states that this money was used exclusively for those involved in the actual work of Temple maintenance. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; here** it is speaking of a case **where they collected** funds **and there was** money **left over.** These funds could be used for Temple vessels. Conversely, **here,** the verse is referring to a situation **where they collected** funds **and there was nothing left over,** and therefore all of the money was allocated to actual Temple maintenance. The Gemara asks: **And if they collected** money **and there was** some **left over, what of it?** After all, that money was consecrated for another purpose. If the Temple vessels could not be prepared with money consecrated for Temple maintenance, how were they able to use any of these funds for this purpose? **Rabbi Abbahu said: The court** initially **sets a mental stipulation about the** money collected: **If it is required** for Temple maintenance, **it is required** and is allocated accordingly, **and if not, it will be** used **for the service vessels.**

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. *Kan she-gavu ve-hosiru kan she-gavu ve-lo hosiru* — in one case they had collected and had a surplus; in the other they had collected and did not have a surplus. In principle, *bedek ha-bayit* funds are collected and designated specifically for structural purposes, not for *klei shares*. But if there were leftover funds, the *beit din* could reassign them. How is this reassignment legitimate? *Rabbi Abbahu* invokes the principle of *lev beis din matne aleihem*: the *beit din* stipulates in advance — *im hutzrechu hutzrechu, ve-im lav yehu le-klei shares* — if the money is needed for *bedek ha-bayit*, it will be used for that; if not, it will go to *klei shares*. Anyone contributing to the fund does so with the understanding that the *beit din* retains ultimate discretion over its use.

This principle consistently recurs in these discussions. The underlying assumption is that the *beit din* — conceived in the *gemara*'s framework as the rabbinic authority — holds ultimate oversight over Temple finances, an idealized picture that, historically, would have been complicated by the dominant role of the *kohanim* in actual Temple administration. As one might observe, the historical reality was that the *kohanim* ran the institution and the rabbis were largely outside its precincts, often in tension with the *Tzadokim* among the priestly class. The *gemara* retrospectively imagines an arrangement in which rabbinic authority supervises everything — *lev beit din matne aleihem*, not *lev kohanim matne aleihem* — a projection of rabbinic self-understanding onto the Temple era.

## Tannaim Debate

### Yishmael View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת בָּאִין מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת שְׁאָר הַכֶּסֶף״, אֵיזֶהוּ כֶּסֶף שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שִׁירַיִים — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה תְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The funding for the **service vessels** of the Temple **comes from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber, as it is stated: "The rest of the money"** (II Chronicles 24:14). **Which money has a remainder? You must say** that **this** is referring to **the collection of the chamber.** After the money was brought into the chamber, a certain portion of it would be set aside for the requirements of the offerings, while the remainder was used for other purposes.

*Tana debei Rabbi Yishmael* states: *klei shares ba'in mi-terumat ha-lishka* — the Temple vessels come from *terumat ha-lishka*. The proof text is *es shear ha-kesef* — the remainder of the money. In context, this verse appears in the narrative about money collected for *bedek ha-bayit*, but *tana debei Rabbi Yishmael* understands *shear ha-kesef* as a reference to the *terumat ha-lishka*.

### Shirei Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא שִׁירַיִים גּוּפַיְיהוּ? כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא: ״הָעוֹלָה״ — עוֹלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, הָכִי נָמֵי: ״הַכֶּסֶף״ — כֶּסֶף רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one can **say** that **the remainder itself** was used for the Temple vessels, and the phrase "the rest of the money" does not refer to the funds of which there is a remainder, but to the remainder of the donations left in the chamber after the first collection was removed. The Gemara answers: This is **as Rava said** elsewhere, that the phrase **"the burnt-offering"** (Leviticus 6:5), with the definite article, is referring to **the first burnt-offering; so too,** the term **"the money"** (II Chronicles 24:14) is referring to **the first money,** i.e., the money removed from the collection of the chamber.

One might ask: perhaps *shear ha-kesef* means literally the *shirei terumat ha-lishka* — the leftover surplus — rather than the main body of the *terumat ha-lishka* fund? On that reading, the vessels would come not from the primary *terumat ha-lishka* but only from any unspent remainder.

### Kesef Rishon

The *gemara* rejects this. The word *hakesef* — with the definite article — focuses attention on the money itself, not on its remainder. Drawing on *Rava*'s interpretation of *zot torat ha-olah* — where the *heh* indicates the primary *olah*, the *olat ha-tamid*, which precedes all others — so too here: *hakesef* signals *kesef rishon*, the main fund. The vessels come from the primary *terumat ha-lishka*, not merely from its surplus.

### Main Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַקְּטוֹרֶת וְכׇל קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִבּוּר — בָּאִין מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה, מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב וּלְבוֹנָה וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת — בָּאִין מִמּוֹתַר נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following source: The funds for **the incense and all communal offerings come from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber.** The funds for **the golden altar,** located inside the Sanctuary and upon which the incense was offered, **the frankincense, and the service vessels** all **come from the leftover** money of the funds set aside for **the libations.**

The list of items that come from *terumat ha-lishka* thus includes: the *ketoret*, all communal *korbanot*, the golden altar, and the service vessels.

### Motar Nesachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה, הַלְּשָׁכוֹת וְהָעֲזָרוֹת — בָּאִין מִקׇּדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת. חוּץ לְחוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה — בָּאִין מִשְּׁיָרֵי הַלְּשָׁכוֹת. זוֹ הִיא שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: חוֹמַת הָעִיר וּמִגְדְּלוֹתֶיהָ וְכׇל צׇרְכֵי הָעִיר בָּאִין מִשְּׁיָרֵי הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The funds for the upkeep of **the altar of burnt-offerings,** which was located outside the Sanctuary and on which most offerings were burned, and for **the chambers, and** for **the** various **courtyards, come from** money **consecrated for Temple maintenance.** Funds for those matters that are **outside the walls of** the Temple **courtyard come from the remainder of the chambers. And** with regard to **this we learned: The wall of the city, its towers, and all of the requirements of the city** of Jerusalem likewise **come from the remainder of the chamber.** According to this source, the funds for the sacred vessels came from the leftover money of the funds set aside for the libations, not the collection of the Temple treasury chamber.

However, a competing *braita* presents a different source for the golden altar and the *klei shares*: they come from *motar nesachim* — the surplus of the libation funds. The practice was that when one donated funds for the *nesachim* accompanying a *korban*, one would intentionally provide slightly more than the required amount to ensure sufficiency; the *Beit HaMikdash* would level off the measure, and the small excess would be set aside for *hekdesh*. Over time, all these small surpluses accumulated into meaningful sums. According to this view, it was those accumulated funds — *motar nesachim* — that financed the *klei shares*, a somewhat counterintuitive assignment that may reflect the practical reality that the *klei shares* did not need frequent replacement and the accumulated *motar nesachim* was roughly equivalent in scale to what was needed.

### Building Funds

The outer altar — *mizbe'ach ha-olah* — together with the *lishkot* and the *azarot*, the chambers and the courtyards of the Temple complex, come from *bedek ha-bayit*. This is clear: these are the fundamental structural elements.

### City Needs

Any construction outside the walls of the *azara* — but still on the Temple Mount or in its vicinity — comes from *shiyarei ha-lishka*, the surplus of the *terumat ha-lishka* fund, which was regularly used for a broader range of communal purposes. The same source provides for the walls and towers of the city of *Yerushalayim* and all the *tzorchei ha-ir* — the general municipal needs of the city. The walls of *Yerushalayim* are not merely civil infrastructure; they are bound up with the *kedushah* of the *Beit HaMikdash* itself, since it is the wall of *Yerushalayim* that defines the *kedushah* of the *Mikdash*'s environs.

## Leftover Funds

### Kayitz Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה, מָה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ? רִיקּוּעֵי זָהָב צִיפּוּי לְבֵית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: מוֹתַר פֵּירוֹת — לְקֵיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה — לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as we learned** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 6a): **What would they do with the leftover** funds **of the collection** of shekels that had not been spent on communal offerings? They would purchase **golden plates** as a **coating for** the walls and floor of **the Holy of Holies. Rabbi Yishmael says:** There were different types of remainders in the Temple, each of which had separate regulations. **The leftover produce** was used to purchase the **repletion [*keitz*] of the altar,** i.e., burnt-offerings sacrificed when the altar would otherwise be idle. **The leftover** funds **of the collection** were used **to** purchase **service vessels.**

The *gemara* addresses the *tannaitic* dispute over what to do with the leftover *truma* funds. The first opinion: *motar peirot l'kayitz ha-mizbe'ach* — the surplus was used to keep the *mizbe'ach* active, *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach*. The etymology is disputed: some derive it from *l'hakitz*, to awaken or rouse; others associate it with *kayitz*, the summer, as in something left idle — and just as one does not want fruit to sit unused in summer, so one does not want the *mizbe'ach* to stand idle. The concern was genuine: if there were periods — even within a single day — when no *korbanot* were being brought, the *mizbe'ach* would stand empty, an embarrassment to the institution. The solution was to ensure that communal *korbanot* would be offered during those intervals, funded by the surplus.

### Buy Low Sell High

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה מָה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ? לוֹקְחִין פֵּירוֹת בְּזוֹל וּמוֹכְרִין אוֹתָם בְּיוֹקֶר, וְהַשָּׂכָר — מְקַיְּצִין בּוֹ אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. וְזוֹ הִיא שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: מוֹתַר פֵּירוֹת לְקֵיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **What would they do with the leftover** funds **of the collection? They** would use it to **buy produce at a cheap** price **and** subsequently **sell** that produce **at an expensive** price, **and the profit** earned from this trade would be used **for the repletion of the altar. And** with regard to **this we learned: The leftover** funds **of produce** were used to purchase the animals **for the repletion of the altar.**

The operational model under this first opinion: the *Beit HaMikdash* would purchase fruit cheaply and sell it at higher prices, and the profits would go toward the *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach*. The *Beit HaMikdash* was in a unique position to execute this — it had something approaching a monopoly and a captive market of pilgrims arriving for the *regalim*.

### Yishmael View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה — לְקֵיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מוֹתַר נְסָכִים — לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמֵר: מוֹתַר נְסָכִים לְקֵיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת. וְזֶה וָזֶה, לֹא הָיוּ מוֹדִים בְּפֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: The leftover** funds **of the collection** of shekels were used to purchase the animals **for the repletion of the altar,** as they had originally been collected for offerings. **The leftover libations** were used **to** purchase **service vessels. Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, says: The leftover libations** were used to purchase animals for **the repletion of the altar,** while **the leftover** funds **of the collection** of shekels were used **to** purchase **service vessels.** Both **this** Sage, Rabbi Akiva, **and that** Sage, Rabbi Ḥanina, **did not agree** with Rabbi Yishmael's opinion **with regard to** the leftover **produce.**

*Rabbi Yishmael* proposed a more targeted version of this business model: buy wines, oils, and fine flours — precisely the materials that pilgrims arriving at the *Beit HaMikdash* would need for their *nesachim* and *korbanot* — and use the profits from these sales to fund the operating expenses of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The strategic logic is clear: these were items in guaranteed demand, and the institutional position of the *Beit HaMikdash* made it uniquely positioned to profit from that demand.

### Akiva Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: אֵימָא צְרָרֵי אַתְפְּסַהּ. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא אָמַר לָהּ ״צְאִי מַעֲשֵׂה יָדַיִךְ בִּמְזוֹנוֹתַיִךְ״. בְּשֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ מַאי טַעְמָא לָא — אֵין עֲנִיּוּת בִּמְקוֹם עֲשִׁירוּת. בְּשֶׁל עֲנִיִּים מַאי טַעְמָא לָא — דִּלְמָא מִתְרְמֵי לְהוּ עַנְיָא וְלֵיכָּא לְמִיתְּבָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: One may not generate profit by** selling **consecrated** property, **nor** may one profit **from** funds set aside **for the poor.** The Gemara explains the reason for Rabbi Akiva's ruling: **What is the reason** that one may **not** use **consecrated** property to generate a profit? It is because **there is no poverty in a place of wealth,** i.e., the Temple must always be run in a lavish manner. Therefore, one may not use Temple funds to generate small profits in the manner of paupers. **What is the reason** that one may **not** use funds set aside **for the poor** to make a profit? It is because **perhaps one will encounter a poor person and there will be nothing to give him,** as all of the money is invested in some business transaction.

*Rabbi Akiva* objected on principle: *ein mishtakrin be-shel hekdesh* — one does not use *hekdesh* money for commercial profit. There is something fundamentally degrading about turning the funds of the *Beit HaMikdash* into business capital. *Ein aniyut bi-mkom ashirut* — there is no poverty where there is wealth — meaning that *hekdesh* ought to embody and project an image of transcendent abundance, and subjecting it to the contingencies and indignities of the marketplace undermines that dignity.

### Poor People

*Rabbi Akiva* pressed a second objection regarding the parallel case of charity funds: *af lo be-shel aniyim* — one also does not invest funds belonging to the poor. *Dilma mitrami lehu ani ve-leika le-mitav leh* — a poor person may need the money immediately, and if the funds are tied up in an investment, they will not be available when urgently needed. The principle extends naturally to institutional funds generally: an endowment may earn returns, but its primary obligation is to the constituency it serves, and availability matters as much as yield. The *gemara* here touches on a set of questions with obvious resonance for any institution that holds funds in trust for those who depend on them.
