# Shekalim 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Six Nedavah Boxes

### Priestly Families

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁשָּׁה לִנְדָבָה. חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר. כְּנֶגֶד שִׁשָּׁה בָתֵּי אָבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** "Six for voluntary gifts." Ḥizqiah said, corresponding to the six clans165During the week of service of a "watch" of Cohanim, each of the six workdays another clan would serve, whose members then had claim to all hides of elevation offerings of that day. In B, the statement is ascribed to Rav, Ḥizqiah's cousin, and fellow student of the Elder R. Hiyya, Rav's uncle and Ḥizqiah's father.

The daf opens at the bottom of 18b, continuing the discussion of the *shofarot* — the various donation boxes in the *Beit HaMikdash* — which collected money for different types of offerings and Temple needs. We have already worked through the first seven boxes, each designated for a specific purpose, such as logs of wood, frankincense, and gold. The Gemara now turns to six additional *shofarot*, all designated for *nedavah* — voluntary offerings — also known elsewhere as *keitz hamizbeach*, referring to *olot* brought on the *mizbeach* during periods when no other sacrificial activity occupied it. This fund also received proceeds from leftover money originally designated for a *chatat* or an *asham*, as indeed the *mishnah* had discussed, citing the interpretation of *Yehoyada Kohen Gadol*.

The *gemara* opens with the question: why were there six *shofarot* for *nedavah* when every other designation had only one? The first answer comes from Rav or Chizkiya: *kineged shisha batei avot* — corresponding to the six family houses of the priests. By way of background, the *kohanim* were divided into twenty-four *mishmarot*, each serving approximately twice a year. Each *mishmar* was further subdivided into *batei avot*, with one family house operating each day of the week and all families serving together on *Shabbat*, yielding six family houses per *mishmar*.

The word *kineged* here is worth examining carefully. Sometimes *kineged* means only that a number is symbolically parallel to another — a pleasing correspondence. But here *kineged* seems to mean something more literal: each *shofar* was actually assigned to a specific day of the week. If you deposited money into the Sunday *shofar*, that money was designated for the *korbanot* brought on Sunday; the Monday *shofar* for Monday *korbanot*, and so on. Why would such a system be necessary rather than simply maintaining a single communal fund drawn upon as needed? The *Korban HaEdah* explains: if one family's day of service was particularly busy and drew heavily on the communal *nedavah* fund, while another family's day was quieter, the busier family would benefit more — in the form of hides or portions of *korbanot* belonging to the family on duty that day. To prevent disputes over the unequal distribution of these benefits, *chazal* instituted separate boxes for each day so that each family would draw only from its own day's fund and could not complain about how resources were allocated across the week.

### Six Animal Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר פְּדָיָה אָמַר. כְּנֶגֶד שֵׁשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת. פָּר וְעֶגֶל וְשָׂעִיר אֵיִל וּגְדִי וּטְלֵה.
>
> **Translation:** Bar Pedaiah said, corresponding to seven animals: bull, and calf, and he-goat, ram, and lamb, and kid goat.

Bar Pedaiah offers a different explanation: *kineged shisha behemot* — corresponding to six types of animals. The enumeration runs as follows: a cow (*par*), a calf (*egel*), a goat (*sa'ir*), a ram (*ayil*), a young goat (*gedi*), and a lamb (*taleh*). Just as with the explanation based on *batei avot*, one can ask whether *kineged* here is purely symbolic or whether it means the funds in each box were literally designated to purchase a specific type of animal. Here too, it would appear that each *shofar* was marked — or assumed — to fund *olot* of a particular species.

### Six Korban Origins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר. כְּנֶגֶד שֵׁשׁ קָרְבָּנוֹת. קִינֵּי זָבִים קִינֵּי זָבוֹת קִינֵּי יּוֹלְדוֹת חַטָּאוֹת וָאֲשָּׁמוֹת הַמְּנָחוֹת וָעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Samuel said, corresponding to six sacrifices: the nests of the sufferer from gonorrhea, the nests of the sufferer from flux, the nests of the new mother, purification sacrifices, and reparation sacrifices, flour offerings, and the Tenth of an Ephah.

Shmuel offers yet another reading: *kineged shisha korbanot* — corresponding to six types of *korbanot*, meaning six distinct origins of the funds deposited. These are: *kinei zavim*, *kinei zavot*, *kinei yoldot*, *chatatot*, *ashamot*, *menachot*, and *asirit ha-eifah*. The count requires some explanation, since several of these are obligatory rather than voluntary offerings, and the total enumeration seems to exceed six, suggesting some question about the *girsa*. The commentators understand that what the *shofar* received was not the *korban* itself but leftover money originally sanctified for these purposes. Funds remaining after a *chatat*, an *asham*, a *minchah*, or an *asirit ha-eifah* — including the *Kohen Gadol*'s daily personal *minchah* — would be deposited here. This explanation is the hardest to follow intuitively: while it is clear why you might want separate boxes based on the day of service or the species of animal, why the *origin* of the funds would necessitate distinct *shofarot* is considerably less obvious.

### Large Volume Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. עַל יְדֵי שֶׁהַנְּדָבָה מְרוּבָּה רִיבוּ לָהּ שׁוֹפָרוֹת הַרְבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, because the gifts were many, they added many horns for it.

Rabbi Yochanan provides a simpler, more pragmatic answer: *al yedei she-hanedava meruba ribbu lo shofarot harbe* — because the *nedavah* donations were so numerous and the fund so large, they simply maintained multiple *shofarot*. On this reading, there is nothing architecturally meaningful about the number six; it is merely that the volume of money flowing into this fund — from leftover *chatat* and *asham* funds, from personal donations, and from other sources — was so great that a single box could not accommodate it.

### Preventing Family Disputes

The *Korban HaEdah*'s explanation, which has already been introduced, provides the most detailed rationale for the system based on *batei avot*. The passage reads that since each family working on a given day would be entitled to whatever benefit derived from the *korbanot* brought on that day — whether hides, meat portions, or other entitlements depending on the type of *korban* — a single communal *nedavah* fund would have generated constant disputes. A family whose day was busy but which drew little from the *nedavah* fund would resent a family whose day drew heavily on those same communal resources. To forestall such conflicts entirely, each family was given its own dedicated *shofar*, and each family's entitlements were tied exclusively to what was raised and used on its own day. The logic holds even if it leaves some questions open — for instance, what happened to unclaimed funds from one family's box when the next *mishmar* rotated in — but the structural intention is clear: separate funds prevent quarrels.

It is worth noting that there is a certain tension in how we picture the *kehuna* here. On one hand, the *kohanim* are renowned for their precision and diligence in the *avodah*. On the other hand, the Talmud records their intense competitiveness and passion. These qualities are not contradictory — they are expressions of the same fervor applied in different directions.

To summarize the different explanations: some link the six *shofarot* to the six days of the priestly family rotation; others link them to the six species of sacrificial animals; still others to the six origins from which *nedavah* funds derived; and Rabbi Yochanan simply notes the large volume of donations as the practical impetus.

## Yehoyada & King's Boxes

### Two Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹנָתָן [אָמַר]. שְׁתֵּי נְדָבוֹת [עָשָׂה]. תַּנֵּי רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. נְדָבָה אַחַת. וְהָא כְתִיב וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיַּעֲשׂוּ אֲרוֹן אֶחָד וַיִּתְּנֻהוּ בְּשַׁעַר בֵּית יְי חוּצָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Samuel ben Naḥman in the name of Rebbi Jonathan [said], two voluntary gifts169The two verses refer to two separate collections.. Rebbi Ismael stated, one gift. But is it not written170*2 Chr.*24:8, while in *2K.* 12:10 it is reported that the chest was deposited in the priests' courtyard, to the right (East) of the altar., *the king commanded and they made a chest and put it at the outside of the gate of the Eternal's House.*

The *gemara* now cites the *pesukim* bearing on the collection boxes from the time of *Yehoyada HaKohen*. Two biblical passages describe the donation arrangements: *Melachim* records that *Yehoyada* took a box and placed it *etsel hamizbeach miyamin* — beside the *mizbeach* on the right — while *Divrei HaYamim* (chapter 24) records that the king ordered the box to be placed *beshaar beit Hashem hachutza* — at the outer gate of the Temple. The statement of Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rav Yonatan is that there were in fact two separate *nedavot*, two distinct collections — which accounts for the two divergent descriptions. The *pasuk* of *Divrei HaYamim* also seems to indicate that the money from one collection was used to purchase *klei shareit*, while *Melachim* says explicitly that the money was *not* to be used for *klei shareit* — further evidence that two different boxes with two different purposes are being described.

### King's Reform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חונָה. מִפְּנֵי הָטְּמֵאִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥuna said, because of those impure.

The *baraita* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael reconciles the two locations somewhat differently. Rav Huna explains: *mipnei ha-teme'im* — because of those in a state of *tumah*. The *Korban HaEdah* elaborates: there was originally only one *nedavah* box, and *Yehoyada* placed it inside, *etsel hamizbeach*, within the *azarah*. The king subsequently ordered it relocated to the outer gate — outside the *azarah* — so that those who were *temei met* or *tamei sheretz*, who were permitted to enter the *Har HaBayit* but not the *azarah*, could also contribute.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חוּנָה בְשֵׁם רַב יוֹסֵף. עַל שֵׁם אַךְ לֹא יֵעָשֶׂה בֵּית יְי סִפּוֹת כֶּסֶף. מִין סִפּוֹת כֶּסֶף לֹא יֵעָשֶׂה בֵית יי.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥuna in the name of Rav Joseph: Because of *but silver cups would not be made in the Eternal's House*171*2K.* 12:14.. Things similar to silver cups would not be made in the Eternal's House172Either there were two different calls for gifts to the Temple, or for the same collection period there were two chests, one inside and one outside, and vessels were made only from the money which never entered the sacred domain.

The *Korban HaEdah* ultimately concludes that the evidence compels the view that there were indeed two separate *nedavot* — one for interior use and one for exterior access — since the contradictions between the two biblical accounts extend not only to the location of the box but also to its designated purpose. One was used for *klei shareit*; the other was not. These simply cannot be describing the same box at two moments in time.

## Found Coins Principle

### Shekalim-Nedavah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: מָעוֹת שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ בֵּין שְׁקָלִים לִנְדָבָה קָרוֹב לַשְּׁקָלִים יִפָּלוּ לַשְּׁקָלִים לַנְּדָבָה יִפְּלוּ לַנְּדָבָה מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה יִפְּלוּ לַנְּדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: Coins which are found between the *sheqalim* and the voluntary gifts, if closer to *sheqalim* they have to be given to *sheqalim*, closer to voluntary gifts to voluntary gifts; in the middle they have to be given to voluntary gifts1As explained at the end of the Mishnah, in general coins found are assigned to the box closest to the place where the coins were found. Only if the coins are strictly in the middle between two boxes they are assigned to the more holy use. Since voluntary gifts are strictly for elevation offerings but *sheqalim* are used for all needs of the Temple, voluntary gifts represent the more holy use.

With the close of the preceding *perek*, the *gemara* transitions into a new topic: what is the halachic status of coins found in ambiguous locations — that is, between two *shofarot* designated for different purposes? The *mishnah* opens: *ma'ot she-nimtzu bein ha-shekalim la-nedavah* — coins found between the *shofar* for *shekalim* and the one for *nedavah*. The rule: if they are closer to the *shekalim* box, they go to *shekalim*; if closer to the *nedavah* box, they go to *nedavah*; and if equidistant — *mechtza l'mechtza* — they go to *nedavah*.

This invokes the halachic principle of *karov* — proximity. This principle holds that when the origin of an object is uncertain, we assume it came from the nearest source. The *Bavli* derives this principle from the passage about the *eglah arufah* — the city closest to an unidentified corpse bears the responsibility. (Famously, Rabbi Yirmiyah asked what happens if the corpse's foot is in one jurisdiction and the rest is in another — and was reportedly expelled from the *beit midrash* for that question.)

### Circular Arrangement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: מָעוֹת שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ בֵּין שְׁקָלִים לִנְדָבָה כול׳. לָא צוֹרְכָה דְלָא בֵין שְׁקָלִים לִקִינִּים. רִבִּי אָבוּן בְשֵׁם רִבִּי פִינְחָס. כְּמִין כּוֹכְלִיַים הָיוּ עֲשׂוּיִין.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: "Coins which are found between the *sheqalim* and the voluntary gifts," etc. It would only be necessary between *sheqalim* and nests5Since Mishnah 6:7 states that the horn for nests is next to the two for *sheqalim*, how can coins be found between *sheqalim* and gifts?! They were arranged like a snail6Greek κοχλίας, "snail with a spiral shell". If the horns are arranged almost in a circle, the box for new *sheqalim* is next to the last box for gifts.

A difficulty immediately presents itself: how can a coin be found *between* the *shekalim* box and the *nedavah* boxes? On a linear arrangement, these two would be at opposite ends of the row, not adjacent. The answer, provided by Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Pinchas, is that the *shofarot* were arranged in a circle — *k'min brukhliya hayu osin* — like some sort of circular vessel. In a circular arrangement, the *nedavah* boxes at one end of the arc could be adjacent to the *shekalim* box at the other, making the scenario of a coin found between them perfectly plausible.

This resolves the scenario posed by the *mishnah* but does not fully explain why the *mishnah* omits so many other possible scenarios — coins between *shekalim* and *kinnim*, between *gozlei olah* and *etzim*, between *levonah* and *zahav*, and so on. The *gemara* does not address all of these omissions, and a number of possible combinations go undiscussed.

### Other Box Pairs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין עֵצִים לִלְבוֹנָה קָרוֹב לָעֵצִים יִפְּלוּ לָעֵצִים לַלְּבוֹנָה יִפְּלוּ לַלְּבוֹנָה מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה יִפְּלוּ לַלְּבוֹנָה. בֵּין קִנִּים לְגוֹזְלֵי עוֹלָה קָרוֹב לַקִּנִּים יִפְּלוּ לַקִּינִּים לְגוֹזְלֵי עוֹלָה יִפְּלוּ לְגוֹזְלֵי עוֹלָה. מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה יִפְּלוּ לְגוֹזְלֵי עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Between wood and incense, if closer to wood they have to be given to wood, closer to incense to incense; in the middle they have to be given to incense2Incense is an offering in itself, whereas wood is only an auxiliary to offerings on the altar; incense is more holy.. Between nests and young birds for elevation sacrifices, if closer to nests they have to be given to nests, closer to young birds for elevation sacrifices to young birds for elevation sacrifices; in the middle they have to be given to young birds for elevation sacrifices3According to the rabbis, nests contain one bird as purification offering and one as elevation offering, whereas young birds for elevation sacrifices are all elevation sacrifices; the latter are more holy.

The *mishnah* then extends the same proximity principle to additional pairs of *shofarot*: between *etzim* and *levonah*, closer to either goes to that box; *mechtza l'mechtza* goes to *levonah*. Between *kinnim* and *gozlei olah* — the *kinnim* being, according to Rabbi Meir's view, pairs designated for *chatat-olah* combinations, and *gozlei olah* being birds designated exclusively as *olot* — closer to either goes to that box; *mechtza l'mechtza* goes to *gozlei olah*.

### Home Coins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין חוּלִּין לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי קָרוֹב לַחוּלִּין יִפְּלוּ לַחוּלִּין לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי יִפְּלוּ לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה יִפְּלוּ לְמָעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי. זֶה הַכְּלָל הוֹלְכִין אַחַר הַקָּרוֹב מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה לְהַחְמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Between profane money and Second Tithe\4, if closer to profane money they have to be given to profane money, closer to Second Tithe to Second Tithe; in the middle they have to be given to Second Tithe. This is the principle: one follows proximity; in the middle one is stringent.

The final case moves outside the *Beit HaMikdash* entirely: coins found at home between a person's regular funds (*chullin*) and their *maaser sheni* money — funds that must be brought to *Yerushalayim* and used to purchase food and offerings. The same proximity rule applies: closer to the *chullin* wallet goes to *chullin*; closer to the *maaser sheni* pouch goes to *maaser sheni*; and equidistant goes to *maaser sheni*. The *mishnah* articulates the underlying principle: *zeh haklal, holchin achar hakarov l'hakel; mechtza l'mechtza l'hachmir* — we follow proximity to rule leniently; when equidistant, we rule stringently.

This last case is the most intuitive: when in doubt, treat the money as consecrated in order to play it safe. The earlier cases are more complex, and the *gemara* will work to explain why the *nedavah* default — and the *gozlei olah* and *levonah* defaults — represent the *machmir* position in each of those contexts.

One might also ask why these ambiguities cannot be resolved simply through conditional *pidyon* — a stipulated redemption. One might say: if this coin is *nedavah*, transfer its sanctity to a second coin; if it is *shekalim*, transfer its sanctity to a third coin, so that each category ends up properly designated. The *mishnah* does not pursue this approach, and the *gemara* will not fully explain why.

### Why Nedavah Default

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צוֹרְכָה דְלָא מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה יִפְּלוּ לִשְׁקָלִים. אִית דְּבָעֵי מֵימַר. שֶׁמָּא יִפְּלוּ לְשִׂיֵּרֵי הַלִּשְׁכָּה. אִית דְּבָעֵי מֵימַר. מֶחֱצָה לְמֶחֱצָה כְּמִי שֶׁמֵּת. אָמַר רִבִּי יָסָא. עַד דַּאֲנָא תַמָּן שְׁמָעִית קָל רַב יְהוּדָה שְׁאַל לִשְׁמוּאֵל. הִפְרִישׁ שִׁקְלוֹ וָמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Would it not be necessary that it be given to *sheqalim*7Since "gifts" provide private sacrifices but *sheqalim* public ones, the sanctity of *sheqalim* should be greater than that of "gifts".? Some want to say, maybe they would remain with the excess of the lodge8Which are not used for sacrifices.. Some want to say, in the middle it is as if he died. 9This text to the end of the paragraph is from Halakhah 2:5, Notes 81–83. The origin of the text is there, since the last two sentences, about the High Priest's flour offering, have no connection with the topic under discussion here. Rebbi Yasa said, when I still was there, I heard the voice of Rav Jehudah asking Samuel, if somebody had set his *sheqel* apart and died?

The *gemara* asks directly: why, when coins are equidistant between *shekalim* and *nedavah*, do they go to *nedavah* and not to *shekalim*? Two answers are offered.

The first: *shema yiplu l'shirei halishka* — lest they fall to *shirei halishka*, the surplus Temple funds. If the coin is placed in the *shekalim* box and turns out to be *nedavah* money, it may end up treated as leftover *terumah* funds — which, by an established stipulation, could be used for the walls of *Yerushalayim* and other broadly civic purposes. But money genuinely consecrated as a *nedavah* for an *olah* cannot be redirected to such non-sacrificial purposes. Therefore we cannot risk placing *nedavah* money into the *shekalim* channel, since that risks its eventual misappropriation for uses incompatible with its sanctity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ. יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** He told him, they shall fall to the gift account.

The second answer invokes a halachic parallel: *mechtza l'mechtza k'mi she-met* — being equidistant is like the case of one who died. Specifically, this refers to someone who set aside a *shekel* and then died before delivering it to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *gemara* records that Rabbi Yosi reports having been present in *Bavel* and heard Rav Yehuda ask Shmuel: if someone separated his *shekel* and died — *hifrish shiklo u-met* — what is done with those funds? The answer: *yiplu l'nedavah* — they go to the *nedavah* fund. This principle is consistent with the treatment of other stranded sacred funds: just as leftover money from a *chatat* or an *asham* goes to the *nedavah* fund when the original purpose cannot be fulfilled, so too a *shekel* whose owner has died cannot be applied to the *shekel* obligation — that obligation was personal — and so it flows into the *nedavah* fund as a general consecrated resource. The *nedavah* thus serves as a halachic catchall for sanctified money that can no longer be applied to its designated purpose. The *gemara* then notes that the *mishnah*'s closing rule — *zeh haklal, holchin achar hakarov; mechtza l'mechtza l'hachmir* — is sufficient to cover all the unlisted scenarios, such as coins between *ktores* and *etzim*, or between *levonah* and *zahav l'kapara*. The principle of *machmir* governs them all, even though the *gemara* does not specify which side is *machmir* in each of these cases.

The analogy to equidistant coins follows: a coin that might be a *shekel* but cannot be established as such is analogous to a *shekel* that cannot be used — and therefore, like that *shekel*, it defaults to *nedavah*.

The first answer — concern over *shirei halishka* — reflects an asymmetry of risk: placing genuine *nedavah* money into the *shekalim* channel may ultimately lead to its use for non-sacrificial purposes, which is worse than the reverse. The second answer is more structural: *halakha* converts unusable *shekel* funds into *nedavah* funds, and the equidistant coin is treated as a case of an unusable *shekel* and converted accordingly.

## Kinnim-Olah Resolution

### Initial Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא. וְיֵשׁ חַטָּאת קְרֵיבָה עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** And it is difficult. May purification offering be brought as elevation offering10If coins lie in the middle between nests and young birds for elevation sacrifices, there is a 50% chance that the money was brought by a woman for her obligatory nests. How can one justify a rabbinical rule that assigns the money to elevation sacrifices against a possibly biblical injunction against doing this??

The *gemara* now addresses a more serious set of difficulties raised by the *mishnah*'s ruling that equidistant coins between *kinnim* and *gozlei olah* go to *gozlei olah*. The problem is twofold.

First: *v'yesh chatat kreviha olah* — if the coin was money designated for a *chatat* bird, how can it simply be applied toward an *olah*? Even granting the general principle that leftover *chatat* funds become *olah* funds, this is typically in the context of non-bird offerings. Bird *korbanot* operate under distinct rules, and using *chatat* money directly for an *olah* — especially in bird form — is not straightforward.

Second, and more urgently: what about the woman who deposited money for her *kinnim* — typically a pair of birds, one as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*, brought by a woman after childbirth or by a *zav* or *zavah* as part of a purification process? This is not a discretionary offering. Without the *chatat*, her purification is incomplete. If her money is now diverted to the *gozlei olah* fund, her *korban* will not be brought, and she will not achieve *taharah*. This is a serious problem — not the misallocation of a freewill donation, but the failure to complete an obligatory purification process.

### Conversion Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה אֲחִי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין הוּא עַל הַמּוֹתָרוֹת שֶׁיִּקְרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥizqiah (brother)11The correct reading is preserved in B, א׳ (אָמַר) "said". in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish: It is a stipulation of the Court that the excesses be brought as elevation sacrifices12Everybody bringing a gift to the Temple knows that these will be evaluated by Temple rules and the donor accepts these rules by giving. Therefore the money never was absolutely dedicated for purification offerings.

The first problem — using *chatat* money for an *olah* — is answered by Chizkiyah in the name of Reish Lakish: *tnai beit din hu al hamosarot she-yikrevu olot* — it is a standing stipulation of the rabbinic court that leftover or unresolved funds originally designated for a *chatat* should be brought as *olot*. This is an application of the broader principle *kol hamakdish ada'ata d'rabbanan makdish* — whoever consecrates does so subject to the conditions established by the Sages. Since *beit din* has issued a blanket stipulation, any money set aside for a *chatat* that cannot be used for its original purpose automatically assumes the status of *olah* funds by virtue of that standing condition.

### Supplier Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה הַזֹּאת בַּמֶּה הִיא מִתְכַּפֶּרֶת. אָמַר רִבִּי יִצְחָק. תְּנַיי בֵית דִּין הוּא. הַמְסַפֵּק אֶת הַקִּינִּים הוּא מְסַפְּק אֶת הַפְּסוּלוֹת וְאֶת הָאֲבוּדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** By what is that woman atoned for13Since the money may have been given by a woman needing purification, if it happens that her monies were used as elevation offering, how can she ever eat *sancta*? The sentence and the next are reproduced at the end of the Chapter (7).? Rebbi Isaac said, it is a stipulation of the Court that the providers of nests also provide the disqualified ones14The provider has to provide a few extra birds which are treated as possible purification offerings (a situation impossible for four-legged animals) but are not eaten.

The second problem — securing the woman's purification when her money has been reallocated — is addressed by Rav Yitzchak: *tnai beit din hu; hamefasek et ha-kinnim, hu mefasek et ha-psulot ve-et ha-avudot* — it is a standing stipulation of the rabbinic court that the supplier of the birds is obligated to make good any birds that are lost or found invalid, beyond the initial supply he delivers. The arrangement works as follows: the *beit din* holds a certain sum in its *kinnim* box and contracts with a bird supplier — in exchange for the advantages of regular, high-volume Temple business, the supplier accepts unfavorable contractual conditions, including the obligation to replace any birds that turn out to be *pasul* or that go missing. Under this standing stipulation, the box's uncertainty is included within that obligation. If there is a doubt whether a particular coin belonged to the *kinnim* fund, the supplier must provide the corresponding bird anyway. The money goes to the *gozlei olah* fund, but the woman's *korban* is secured through the supplier's contractual liability. The only party who bears the cost is the supplier — but that is the arrangement he accepted as a condition of doing business with the *Beit HaMikdash*.

This has no bearing on questions of ordering or timing of the two birds within the *kin* — those are matters for Masechet Kinnim — but it does fully resolve the concern about the woman's purification.

## Found Money Jerusalem

### Sellers' Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: מָעוֹת שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ לִפְנֵי סוֹחֲרֵי בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹלָם מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: Coins found near vendors of animals always are Tithe money16Most kosher animals sold in Jerusalem are sold to pilgrims who use their tithe money for their living expenses in Jerusalem. If not all this money is spent during their visit, the remaining coins used to be used to buy food consumed in purity according to the rules of second Tithe. Therefore the presumption is that money in the hand of resident Jerusalemites found near venders of animals for slaughter is Second Tithe money. The finder will have to use the coins according to Tithe standards.

The *gemara* now moves to a new and well-known *mishnah* — one cited frequently in the *Bavli* — dealing with money found in various parts of *Yerushalayim*. *Ma'ot she-nimtze'u lifnei sochrei beheima* — coins found in front of the animal sellers in *Yerushalayim* — are assumed, *le'olam*, at all times of the year, to be *maaser sheni* money.

### Maaser Assumption

The reason for this assumption is the sheer prevalence of *maaser sheni* funds in *Yerushalayim*. A farmer with a substantial yield might have the equivalent of thousands of dollars in *maaser sheni* money — a tenth of his produce — which he would bring to *Yerushalayim* whenever he made the pilgrimage. That sum could be far more than he could spend on food during his stay. The money would circulate: shared with friends, relatives, or given to others. But the primary and classic use of *maaser sheni* money was to purchase food consumed *lifnei Hashem*, and the paradigmatic expression of *achilah lifnei Hashem* is *shelamim*. The *mishnah* therefore assumes that the dominant use of *maaser sheni* funds in *Yerushalayim* was to purchase animals for *shelamim* — so much so that virtually all the money flowing through the animal market was *maaser sheni* funds.

### Seasonal Variation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהַר הַבַּיִת חוּלִּין. וּבִירוּשָׁלִַם בִשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה חוּלִּין וּבִשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל הַכֹּל מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** On the Temple Mount, profane. In Jerusalem, during the remainder of the year they are profane, during the holidays everything is Second Tithe.

Outside the immediate context of the animal market, the presumption differs. *Uv'har habayit* — on the *Har HaBayit* — found coins are presumed *chullin*. In the rest of *Yerushalayim*, the ruling depends on the time of year: *bish'ar kol yemot hashana*, during regular periods of the year, coins are presumed *chullin*; but *uv'sh'at haregel*, during the pilgrimage festival period, all coins found in *Yerushalayim* are presumed *maaser sheni*, because the entire city is flooded with pilgrims carrying their *maaser* funds.

### Sweeping Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: לָא צוֹרְכָה דְלָא בְּהַר הַבַּיַת קוֹדֶשׁ. רִבִּי בָּא רִבִּי חִיָיה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. חַזָּקָה שֶׁאֵין הַכֹּהֵן מוֹצִיא מִן הִַלִּישְׁכָּה מָעוֹת עַד שֶׁהוּא מְחַלְלָן עַל הַבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: Would it not be necessarily holy on the Temple Mount17Since it is forbidden to enter the Temple Mount in private business (Mishnah *Berakhot*9:8), how can there be a presumption that monies lost there are profane?? Rebbi Abba, Rebbi Ḥiyya in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan: There is a presumption that no Cohen will remove money from the lodge unless it is redeemed by an animal18The money legitimately in the hands of private persons on the Temple Mount is money received from the Temple for delivery of goods or services. While the origin of the money is from the Temple, it is legitimately profane in the hands of its owner.

The question then arises: if during the *regel* all coins in *Yerushalayim* are presumed *maaser sheni*, why does the same not apply to the *Har HaBayit*? The answer lies in a practice unique to *Yerushalayim*: the streets were swept daily. Because of this daily sweeping, any money found in the street could be assumed to have been dropped recently, and its identity could therefore be determined based on the current time of year. During the *regel*, recent money is *maaser* money. Outside the *regel*, recent money is *chullin*.

The *Har HaBayit*, by contrast, was not swept regularly. Two factors exempted it from this need: first, people were forbidden from entering the *Har HaBayit* with dust on their feet, so it accumulated little debris; second, the elevated and wind-exposed terrain contributed to natural cleaning. Because the *Har HaBayit* was not swept daily, money found there could not be assumed to be recently dropped — it might have been lying there for weeks or months. Therefore one cannot rely on the time of year to determine its status, and it defaults to *chullin* year-round.

## Found Meat Rules

### Temple Courtyard Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּמְצָא בָּעֲזָרָה אֵיבָרִים עוֹלוֹת. וַחֲתִיכוֹת חַטָּאוֹת. וּבִירוּשָׁלַיִם זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים. זֶה וָזֶה תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Of meat found in the Temple courtyard, limbs are elevation sacrifices and cuts purification sacrifices19Elevation sacrifices have to be dissected into limbs (*Lev.* 1:6,12) before being brought to the altar, but purification sacrifices have to be eaten by the Cohanim in the holy precinct and for this purpose have to be cut into portions.; in Jerusalem well-being sacrifices20Since usually meat in Jerusalem is from a family sacrifice.. In either case it has to lose its shape and be taken out to be disposed of by burning21The meat can neither be eaten nor brought to the altar since it might be impure or otherwise disqualified; it cannot be disposed of immediately since it might be pure and qualified. One has to wait until the time allotted for each kind having passed and the meat automatically becoming disqualified; this is the remainder of the day for elevation sacrifices, the remainder of the day plus the following night for purification sacrifices, and the following day for well-being sacrifices.

The *mishnah* turns from money to meat. *Basar ha-nimtza ba-azarah* — meat found in the Temple courtyard. The only meat present in the *azarah* was from offerings: *olot*, which are dismembered and burned on the *mizbeach*, and *chatat* and *asham*, the portions of which are eaten by the *kohanim* in the *azarah*. The rule is: if the meat is in the form of *eivarim* — whole limbs — it is assumed to be *olah*, since that is how an *olah* appears after slaughter (dismembered). If it is *chatichos* — cut into slices or portions — it is assumed to be *chatat*, since that is how the *kohanim*'s portion appears. *Shelamim* would not remain in the *azarah*, as that is a *kodshim kalim* and the *be'alim* take it out immediately.

### Jerusalem Meat

Meat found in *Yerushalayim* itself — outside the *azarah* — is presumed to be *shelamim*. This follows naturally from the previous discussion: if the dominant use of *maaser sheni* money is to purchase animals as *shelamim*, then the meat found in the city is most likely *shelamim* as well.

In either case, whether the meat is assumed to be *olah*, *chatat*, or *shelamim*, the *mishnah* rules: *ta'avor tzurato v'yeitzei l'beit hasreifah* — one must allow its form to pass, meaning allow a day to pass until it becomes *nosar* (*noticeably stale and no longer fit*), and then burn it at the *beit hasreifah*.

## Outside Jerusalem Meat

### Limb-Piece Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא בַּגְּבוּלִין אֵיבָרִים נְבֵילוֹת וַחֲתִיכוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת. וּבִשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל שֶׁהַבָּשָׂר מְרוּבֶּה אַף אֵיבָרִין מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If it was found in the countryside26Palestine outside of Jerusalem and its surroundings., limbs are carrion27Since the butcher did not care to cut it into slices, one must assume that it was notkosher and thrown away for the dogs., cuts are permitted28If there are no sources of non-kosher meat in the neighborhood.. On holidays, when meat is abundant, even limbs are permitted29Since for big family meals one may buy whole limbs and cut them after cooking.

Meat found outside *Yerushalayim* — in the surrounding territory — follows a different set of presumptions. If found in the form of whole *eivarim*, it is presumed to be *neveilah* — an animal that was not slaughtered through *shechitah*. The reasoning is straightforward: one does not bother to dismember a *neveilah* into neat cuts; it would be discarded whole, thrown to dogs, or sold in bulk to non-Jewish butchers. If, however, the meat is found as *chatichos* — properly cut portions — it is presumed *mutarot*, kosher and permitted. The assumption in this latter case is that one is operating within a generally Jewish context, and in the absence of any reason to suspect improper *shechitah* or *treifah*, the meat may be consumed.

## Nosar Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי לָעְזָר בְשֵׁם רִבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָה. הִסִּיעַ דַּעַת טָעוֹן צוּרָה. אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָה. מַתְנִיתָה אָמְרָה כֵן. תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar in the name of Rebbi Hoshaia: Diversion of attention requires {loss of} shape22Sacrificial meat which is left unattended even for a moment cannot be used any more; it has to be kept as is until the time of its sacrificial use has elapsed and then disposed of by burning outside the sacred domain. Babli *Pesaḥim*34a.. Rav Hoshaia said, the Mishnah says so, "it has to lose its shape and be taken out to be disposed of by burning."

The *mishnah*'s requirement to wait before burning meat found in the Temple courtyard or in *Yerushalayim* — *ta'avor tzurato* — prompts a closer discussion of what underlying concern drives that ruling.

### Invalidation Debate

The question then arises: if during the *regel* all coins in *Yerushalayim* are presumed *maaser sheni*, why does the same not apply to the *Har HaBayit*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵי וְיֵאוּת. לְאוֹכְלוֹ אֵין אַתְּ יָכוֹל שֶׁמָּא נִתְקַלקְלָה צוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** 23The rest of this Halakhah is missing in B. Rebbi Yose says, this is correct. You may not eat it for maybe its shape already is lost.

Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Hoshaya explains: *hisi'a da'at ta'un ibbur tzurah* — the act of taking one's eyes off sacred meat, *hesi'ach hadaat*, itself renders it invalid. According to the *Bavli*, *hesi'ach hadaat* from *basar kodesh* creates a *psul*. Therefore, even if the meat found was slaughtered today and would otherwise be perfectly fresh and valid, the fact that it was left unattended — that no one maintained proper attention over it — has already disqualified it. One cannot consume it or offer it as is; one must wait until it is definitively *pasul* due to age (*linah*) and then burn it.

Rabbi Yosi demurs: *veya'ot* — is that reading truly compelling? He argues that *hesi'ach hadaat* is not the operative concern. Rather, *l'ochlo ein ata yachol, shema nitkalkela tzuraso* — the reason one cannot eat it is simpler: perhaps the meat's form has already passed, meaning it may already be a day old and therefore *nosar*. Since we do not know when the meat was left there, we cannot be certain it is still within the time permitted for consumption or offering. Therefore we wait another day until it is definitively *pasul* through *linah* and can be burned. On Rabbi Yosi's view, *hesi'ach hadaat* has nothing to do with it; it is purely a question of age and the possibility that the meat is already *nosar*.

## Strung Meat & Market Rules

### Strung Meat Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי קְרִיסְפָּא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן חֲנִינָה. אִם הָיוּ מְחָרוֹזוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Crispus in the name of Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Ḥanina: If they were strung they are permitted32If all butcher shops in the neighborhood are kosher and all households eat kosher, nobody expends the work to string pieces (or sausages) together if they are not kosher and have to be fed to the dogs.

The *gemara* notes an exception to the rule that whole limbs found outside *Yerushalayim* are presumed *neveilah*. If the limbs were *machruzot* — strung together on a cord — this clearly indicates they came from a butcher's market, since *neveilah* would not be packaged and displayed in that manner. Strung limbs are therefore presumed kosher and *mutarot*.

### Nine Shops Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּשַׁע חֲנוּיוֹת מוֹכְרוֹת בְּשַׂר נְבֵילָה וְאַחַת מוֹכֶרֶת בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה נִתְחַלְּפוּ לוֹ. חוֹשֵׁשׁ. וְלַנִּמְצֵאת הוֹלְכִין אַחַר הָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** Ten stores, nine sell carrion and one sells kosher slaughtered meat, if it was switched, he worries. For what is found, one follows the majority.

The *gemara* now brings the well-known case of the nine shops: nine butcher shops sell non-kosher meat and one sells kosher meat. If a person does not remember which shop he purchased from — *nischalef lo* — he must be concerned (*choshesh*) that it came from a non-kosher shop, even though nine out of ten are non-kosher. The principle at work here is *kavuah*: when the object of doubt was associated with a fixed location — a shop that stands in its place — the halachic status is treated as an even fifty-fifty doubt regardless of the actual numerical ratio. The fixed presence of the shops prevents the ordinary application of *rov*. If, however, the meat is simply found in the street — *nimtza* — the question is abstract and one goes by *rov*: the majority of butchers is followed, and the meat is treated as if it certainly came from one of the nine non-kosher shops and is forbidden.

In the reverse configuration — nine kosher shops and one non-kosher shop — the same structural distinction applies. If the person does not remember which shop he visited, it is *kavuah* and treated as a fifty-fifty doubt, so he must be concerned even though only one of nine is non-kosher. But if the meat is found in the street, one goes by *rov* and may eat it.

### Non-Jew's Meat Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. הַנִּמְצָא בְיַד גּוֹי כְּנִמְצָא בַפַּלַּטְיָא. רִבִּי לָעְזָר בֵּירִבִּי חַגַּיי הֲוָה מְסַמֵּךְ לְרִבִּי מָנָא. חֲמָא לְחַד אֲרָמַאי מְקַטֵּעַ מִן סוּסֵיהּ וּמַפִּיק לִבְרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ. הָדָא הִיא דְאָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. הַנִּמְצָא בְיַד גּוֹי כְּנִמְצָא בַפַּלַּטְיָא. אָמַר לֵיהּ. כֵּן אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵי רִבִּי. וְהֵן שֶׂרָאוּ אוֹתוֹ יוֹצֵא מִמַּקֻּילִין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, what is in the hand of a Gentile is as found on a thoroughfare36Greek πλατεῑα (sc. 'οδός) "a wide (road)". On a thoroughfare the traffic is not local; even if all local shops are kosher, the majority of travelers come from places which are not kosher, and meat found there automatically is forbidden.. Rebbi Eleazar ben Rebbi Ḥaggai was supporting Rebbi Mana. He saw an Aramean cutting from his horse and feeding his son. He said to him, that is what Rebbi Joḥanan said, what is in the hand of a Gentile is as found on a thoroughfare37Meat at the disposal of a Gentile automatically is forbidden.. He answered him, so said the great Rebbi Yose38R. Yose ben Ḥalafta, the Tanna.: Only39That meat in the possession of the Gentile possibly is kosher. if they saw him leaving a Jewish butcher shop40Latin *macellum*, Greek μάκελλον..

Rabbi Yochanan rules that meat found in the possession of a non-Jew — *ha-nimtza b'yad nochri* — is treated like meat found in the town square, *k'nimtza b'platya*. In other words, the question of which shop the non-Jew visited is not treated as *kavuah* from the halachic questioner's perspective. Since the Jew asking the question was not the one who stood in the shops, the *kavuah* principle does not apply to him; for him, it is equivalent to finding meat on the ground and one follows *rov* accordingly.

However, a further nuance is raised: if a non-Jew simply has meat, how can one invoke the majority of butchers at all? Perhaps he did not acquire the meat from a butcher but slaughtered his own animal — a horse, for instance — in his barn. Rabbi Yosi clarifies: the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan applies specifically when one saw the non-Jew emerge from the Jewish butchers' market (*makolin shel Yisrael*). In that case, it is known that he obtained the meat from a butcher; the only question is which one. And then one follows *rov*. If one has no knowledge of where the meat came from, no such inference is available.

### Trust Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד בַּר נַשׁ בְּצִיפּוֹרִין אֲזַל בָּעֵי מְשִׁיגָּה אֵסְקוֹפְתֵּיהּ מִן טַבְּחָא וְלָא יְהַב לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַד רוֹמַיי וְאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ. לָא נָסְבִּית עַל כָּרְחֵיהּ. אָמַר. וְלָאו בְּשַׂר דִּנְבֵילָה יְהָבִית לֵיהּ. רִבִּי יִרְמְיָה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. מַעֲשֶׂה בָא לִפְנֵי רִבִּי וְאָמַר. לָא כוּלָּא מִינֵּיהּ מֵיסּוֹר מַקֻּילִין דְּצִיפּוֹרִין.

> **Translation:** A man in Sepphoris went and wanted to buy red meat from a butcher, but he did not give to him. He told a Roman, who brought him. He said to him, did I not buy against your will? He retorted, did I not give carrion to him41Disproving R. Yose's statement. The butcher is happy to sell his non-kosher meatto a Gentile since it is worthless for his Jewish clientele.? Rebbi Jeremiah in the name of Rebbi Ḥanina: A case came before Rebbi who said, it is not believable to forbid the butcher shop of Sepphoris42He doubts the genuineness of the story. Since Rebbi Joḥanan lived a generation after Rebbi, his ruling has to be followed.

The *gemara* closes with an illustrative incident from *Tzippori*. A certain person wanted to purchase meat from a *shochet* who refused to sell to him. The customer then sent a non-Jewish acquaintance to buy the meat on his behalf — and succeeded. He went back to the *shochet* and boasted that he had outsmarted him. The *shochet* replied: I knew what you were doing, and so I gave your agent *neveilah* meat. The question that arises is whether this admission undermines the trustworthiness of the *shochet* going forward — not only for this customer, but for all the butchers of *Tzippori*.

The case was brought before Rebbe. Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Chanina reports Rebbe's ruling: *lo kulam minei me-asar makolin de-Tzippori* — one cannot, on the basis of this *shochet*'s statement, forbid the butchers of *Tzippori*. The admission of one butcher selling *neveilah* to a non-Jew in a specific act of spite does not cast suspicion on the general reliability of the market. Other butchers' establishments are not implicated. As for the *shochet* himself who made this statement, his personal trustworthiness is a separate question — but that too is left for continued discussion.
