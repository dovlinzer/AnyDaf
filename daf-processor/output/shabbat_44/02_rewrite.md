# Shabbat 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Muktzah Focus

### Kira Overview

Daf 44 picks up at the bottom of 43b. It is worth pausing to note that the chapter of *Kira* is something of a motherlode for the central issues of *Shabbat*. In the course of this *perek* we have covered the *derabbanan* prohibitions of *shehiya* and *chazara*, the key aspects of the *d'oraisa* of *bishul*, all the *derabbanan* ramifications of *bishul*, including *kli sheni*, *yad soledes bo*, *bishul chama*, and *bishul ur*, and now we are in the midst of a *sugya* — extending through the remaining *mishnayot* to the end of the *perek* — devoted to what is perhaps one of the most central and practically consequential discussions in all of *hilchot* Shabbat: *muktzah*.

### Terminology

To be precise about the scope: the *perek* contains treatment of the *d'oraisa* and *derabbanan* dimensions of *bishul*, alongside the laws of *muktzah*. When one considers that *d'oraisa* and *derabbanan* distinctions within *bishul* and the full framework of *muktzah* are all concentrated here in *Kira*, it becomes clear why this *perek* is such a major locus for so much of what is practically relevant to *Shabbat* observance.

## Dead Body/TiltulMH

### Dead Body Setup

The *Gemara* at this point returns to the case that prompted the previous discussion: what does one do with a dead body in the courtyard? The discussion arose in connection with the position that one may move an object only for the sake of a non-*muktzah* item — which raised the question of how one may move a *meis* when necessary. The *Gemara* had already established that if one places an infant or a loaf of bread upon the corpse, it is permissible to move it. This is a striking allowance, because the ordinary use of placing something *mutar* alongside something *muktzah* — such as placing a loaf of bread on a tray along with the Shabbat candelabra so that the tray becomes a *basis* for both permitted and prohibited items — presupposes that the *basis* itself is not inherently *muktzah*. Here, however, the *meis* itself is obviously *muktzah*, and yet the fiction of moving it for the sake of the bread is sufficient — and this is universally agreed upon.

### Two Tiltul Types

The debate that follows concerns the case where there is no loaf of bread and no infant. May one then move the *meis* indirectly — *min hatzad* — by shifting it from one bed to another? This is the question of *tiltul min hatzad*. Before proceeding to the *Gemara*'s analysis, it is important to distinguish between two distinct categories.

The first is *tiltul k'alachar yad* — or *tiltul b'guf* — where one moves an item using a body part other than one's hand in the normal fashion: blowing on it, kicking it, moving it with one's elbow. This type of indirect movement is permitted on *Shabbat*, and crucially, it is permitted even if one is doing it for the sake of the *muktzah* object itself — for example, to prevent it from being damaged.

*Tiltul min hatzad* is a distinct category. Here one moves the *muktzah* item with one's hands, but indirectly — either through another object, as in this case where the *meis* is being carried on a bed, or incidentally, as when one reaches for a permitted object and in so doing pushes a *muktzah* item out of the way.

### L'Tzorech Principle

The governing principle for *tiltul min hatzad* is *l'tzorech davar hamuttar*: such indirect movement is permitted only when one is doing it for the sake of a permitted object. If one is reaching for an orange and one's hand incidentally pushes a wallet out of the way, that is permitted — one is moving toward the orange, a permitted item. What is not permitted is to employ *tiltul min hatzad* for the sake of the *muktzah* object itself. One may not, for instance, use an intermediary to relocate one's wallet simply because one wishes to move the wallet.

To summarize the distinction: *tiltul k'alachar yad* with a *shinui* — kicking, blowing, using one's elbow — is permitted even for the sake of the *muktzah* object. *Tiltul min hatzad* — indirect movement with one's hands, whether incidentally or through an intermediary — is permitted only *l'tzorech davar hamuttar*, for the sake of a permitted item, and not for the sake of the *muktzah* object itself.

### Gemara Analysis

This is precisely why the case of the *meis* is debated. Moving the *meis* on the bed constitutes *tiltul min hatzad*, but one is doing so for the sake of the *meis* itself. And it is that combination — *tiltul min hatzad* for the sake of the *muktzah* object — that is at issue.

The *Gemara* frames it as follows. *Ki pligi* — when do they disagree? *D'leis lei kikar o tinok* — when there is no loaf of bread and no infant. One opinion holds *tiltul min hatzad shemei tiltul* — even indirect movement of *muktzah* is considered real movement of *muktzah* and is prohibited. The other holds *lo shemei tiltul* — it does not rise to the level of genuine *tiltul* and is therefore permitted.

The *Gemara* then attempts to cast this as a *Tannaitic* dispute (*leima kitnai*), citing the following: *ein matzzilin et hamet mipnei hadleika* — one may not save a dead body from a fire. But Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish says: *shamati shematzzilin et hamet mipnei hadleika* — I have heard that one may save it from a fire. The *Gemara* initially suggests that they are debating our exact case: perhaps there is no loaf of bread and no infant, and the dispute is over *tiltul min hatzad* for the sake of a *muktzah* object. The *Gemara* then rejects this framing. Perhaps both *Tannaim* agree that *tiltul min hatzad shemei tiltul* — that indirect movement is still considered prohibited movement of *muktzah* — and that the universally agreed allowance of placing bread or an infant on the corpse is assumed. When the *Gemara* says *leima kitnai* and the response is *lo*, it means the proposed *Tannaitic* parallel is rejected.

### Cremation Concern

The *Gemara* instead explains: *dekulei alma tiltul min hatzad shemei tiltul* — everyone agrees that *tiltul min hatzad* for the sake of a *muktzah* object is still considered a prohibited form of *tiltul*. And this is how we rule in practice: one cannot move *muktzah* indirectly or through an intermediary for the sake of the *muktzah* object itself.

Why then does Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish permit saving the *meis* from a fire? *Dechasi alei* — because *mitoch she'adam bahul al meto*, a person is in an acute state of agitation over his deceased relative and will not allow it to burn. *I lo sharit lei* — if you do not give him a permissible route, even one that involves moving *muktzah*, he will simply extinguish the fire, *ati lechabuyei* — which is obviously a more serious violation. This reflects an important principle that recurs in *hilchot Shabbat*: *Chazal* recognized that even a normally observant person, under the pressure of the moment, will simply transgress. It is therefore sometimes necessary to provide a lesser violation as an outlet, so that a more serious one does not occur. A parallel case is the person who finds himself outside the city as *Shabbat* approaches with his wallet, and if given no permissible path, will simply carry it home.

### R. Yehuda Ruling

The *Gemara* then reports: *Amar Rav Yehuda ben Shila amar Rav Assi amar Rabbi Yochanan* — *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish b'meis*: the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish in the case of the dead body. The special allowance applies, even though under normal circumstances one cannot move *muktzah* by *tiltul min hatzad* for the sake of the *muktzah* object.

The *Gemara* then notes briefly, from the *mishnah* preceding the one now under discussion, that even if a vessel was placed beneath a lamp before *Shabbat* to catch the dripping oil, one may not benefit from that oil, since the oil was not *muchan* — not designated for use — and is therefore *muktzah*. A *baraisa* elaborates: the leftover oil in the lamp itself, or oil that dripped into a plate beneath it, is *asur*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees and permits it, consistent with his general position that he does not hold of most categories of *muktzah*: since the oil is now usable, he sees no basis for prohibition, even though it was designated for the lamp when *Shabbat* began.

## Muktzah Categories

### Keilim/Muktzah

The discussion of the *meis* and the question of what may be moved for the sake of a *muktzah* object invites a broader clarification of the *muktzah* framework — one that is frequently misunderstood. There are two large categories of things that can be subject to *muktzah* restrictions, and they must be kept distinct: *keilim* on the one hand, and *muktzah* proper on the other.

A *kli* — a vessel or utensil — is in principle an item that is part of one's ordinary sphere of usable objects. Within the category of *keilim*, the *Gemara* draws a fundamental distinction: *kli shemelachto l'heter* versus *kli shemelachto l'issur*. A fork or a pillow is a *kli shemelachto l'heter* — it is ordinarily used for permitted purposes. A hammer is the classic example of a *kli shemelachto l'issur*: it is primarily used for hammering nails, an act prohibited on *Shabbat*.

### Kli L'Issur

What are the practical consequences of a *kli shemelachto l'issur* like a hammer? It may be moved for its own sake — for instance, to crack nuts — and it may be moved if one needs the space it occupies. The one thing one may not do is move it merely to protect it, as when it has been left out in the rain. These restrictions exist because the hammer's primary association is with a prohibited activity; it is not, however, a thing that has been entirely pushed out of one's usable domain. It remains a *kli*.

### Atzmo Category

*Muktzah* proper refers to objects that are categorically set aside — off-limits at the onset of *Shabbat*. The first sub-category is *muktzah machmat atzmo*: objects with no ordinary use that are simply not vessels in any meaningful sense, such as a rock or a stick. If one designates a rock as a paperweight before *Shabbat*, it may acquire a use and thereby shed its *muktzah* status; absent such designation, it remains *muktzah machmat atzmo*. A dead body is another example: it has no practical use in the normal sense, and is therefore *muktzah machmat atzmo*.

### Mius Category

A second sub-category is *muktzah machmat mius* — objects that are *muktzah* because they are disgusting. The oily, malodorous pottery lamp that will be discussed momentarily is the paradigm case: while one will certainly use it again when one needs to light a lamp, it is not the sort of thing one normally handles, and it falls into this category of *mius*.

### Chisron Kis Cat.

A third sub-category is *muktzah machmat chisron kis*: items that are so expensive or fragile that one treats them as entirely off-limits — the valuable vase one would never allow to be casually handled.

### No-Move Rule

The crucial distinction between these categories of real *muktzah* and the category of *kli shemelachto l'issur* is the following: a *kli shemelachto l'issur* like a hammer can be moved for a legitimate purpose, such as using it for cracking nuts or needing its space. Real *muktzah* — a dead body, a burning lamp, a rock — cannot be moved for any purpose whatsoever. The only permissible ways of dealing with real *muktzah* are *tiltul k'alachar yad* (moving with an unusual body part) or *tiltul min hatzad* where it is for the sake of a permitted item.

This distinction is directly relevant to the earlier discussion. The principle of *ein kli nital ela l'tzorech davar hanital* — which we do not rule by — concerns a non-*muktzah* object being moved for the sake of a *muktzah* object, which the rejected opinion forbids. That is a wholly separate question from what may be done with a *muktzah* object itself. A *muktzah* object cannot be moved for any reason; the only exceptions are the indirect methods discussed above, and extraordinary cases like the *meis* where the *Gemara* provides a special dispensation.

One might ask about a *meis* in a contemporary hospital setting, where space must be cleared for another patient. Where a *sakanah* or the needs of a *choleh she'yesh bo sakanah* are present, those override other considerations. But in terms of the *muktzah* laws themselves, real *muktzah* cannot be moved for any purpose in the absence of such overriding factors — though indirect movement and extraordinary circumstances do provide recourse.

The question of frozen food's *muktzah* status is a related one worth noting: there is genuine debate among *poskim* about whether frozen food that cannot be eaten during *Shabbat* is *muktzah*, and the question arises in practical contexts such as whether a frozen *challah* may be taken from the freezer for use as *shtei lechem*. Even if one were to hold it is *muktzah*, however, one could push it aside by *tiltul k'alachar yad* or *tiltul min hatzad* to reach a permitted item.

## New/Old Lamps

### Mishnah Text

The *mishnah* states: *metaltelin ner chadash aval lo yashan* — one may move a new lamp, but not an old one. The lamps under discussion are pottery oil lamps, not candles.

### Why Old Bad

Why is an old lamp problematic? Once a pottery oil lamp has been used, it becomes oily, smelly, and generally unpleasant to handle. Under normal circumstances, one would not pick it up without reason; it is kept at a remove until one actually needs to light it again. This renders it *muktzah machmat mius* — *muktzah* on account of its repulsive character. This is the position of the *Tanna Kamma*: even if the old lamp was not burning when *Shabbat* began, it remains off-limits because of its disgusting quality.

### R. Meir View

Rabbi Shimon's position in the *mishnah* is that *kol hanerot metaltelin chutz min haner hadolek baShabbat* — all lamps may be moved, with the sole exception of one that is currently burning. Rabbi Shimon holds essentially no categories of *muktzah*, or at least does not apply them in these cases.

### Issur Category

Why would even Rabbi Shimon prohibit moving a currently burning lamp? One possible explanation has nothing to do with *muktzah* at all: moving a burning lamp might cause it to go out or to burn more quickly, raising concerns of *kibui* or *havarah*. But there is an additional possible ground — a special category of *muktzah* called *muktzah machmat issur*. This category has its primary and essentially exclusive application in the case of a burning lamp. The burning flame is itself performing a *melachah* on *Shabbat*, and therefore the lamp and the wick that support the flame are *muktzah machmat issur* — set aside by reason of the prohibited act they are currently involved in. This conceptual ground for prohibition is relevant beyond the mere concern about extinguishing, because it extends the prohibition to associated objects — such as the oil that drips from the lamp — and its existence as an independent category will have *nafka mina* implications to be explored in the *Gemara*.

---

## Burning Status

### T. Kamma/R. Meir

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraisa*. The *baraisa* reads: *metaltelin ner chadash aval lo yashan* — one may move a new lamp but not an old one: *divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — these are the words of Rabbi Yehuda. So the *Tanna Kamma* of the *mishnah* is now identified as Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *muktzah machmat mius* applies to the old, used lamp.

Rabbi Meir's position is introduced: *kol hanerot metaltelin* — all lamps, new or old, may be moved. Rabbi Meir is not concerned about *muktzah machmat mius*, whether because he categorically rejects that category or because he holds old lamps do not fall within it. The *Gemara* tends to read these disputes as categorical.

### Burning @ Start

However, Rabbi Meir does carve out an exception: *chutz min haner shehidliku bo b'oto Shabbat* — except for a lamp that was lit on that *Shabbat*. The phrase *shehidliku bo b'oto Shabbat* sounds as if it means the lamp was lit during *Shabbat* itself, but everyone understands this to mean a lamp that was burning when *Shabbat* began. Rabbi Meir holds that if a lamp was in a burning state at the onset of *Shabbat* — during *bein hashmashot* — it retains its status as *muktzah* for the entire *Shabbat*, even after it goes out. This is the principle that *muktzah* status is determined at the commencement of *Shabbat* and applies throughout. Rabbi Meir holds *muktzah machmat issur* and its implications for *status at Shabbat's outset*. This is essentially how we rule.

## New/Old Lamps…

### R. Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon's position, restated in the *baraisa*, is that all lamps may be moved — *chutz min haner hadolek baShabbat* — except one that is currently burning. And if it goes out — *kavsah mutar letaltela* — it immediately becomes permitted. Rabbi Shimon thus rejects the principle that *muktzah* status at the beginning of *Shabbat* defines status for the whole *Shabbat*. While the lamp is burning, he does not permit moving it; once it goes out, he has no objection.

---

## Burning Status…

### Goes Out OK

Rabbi Shimon's position thus stands in sharp contrast to Rabbi Meir's. Rabbi Meir says: if it was burning when *Shabbat* began, it remains off-limits all *Shabbat*. Rabbi Shimon says: while it is burning, do not handle it; once it goes out, it is immediately permitted.

The *mishnah* adds an apparently anomalous ruling: *aval kos v'keira v'ashashit lo yazizam mimkoman* — but a cup, a plate, or a lantern, may not be moved at all. This is surprising given Rabbi Shimon's otherwise narrow *muktzah* position. Why would he be more stringent about a cup, a plate, or a lantern than about a lamp?

### R. Elazar View

Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon's position is then introduced: *mistapek min haner hakaveh* — once the lamp goes out, one may take the oil from it. This is consistent with his father Rabbi Shimon's view. But he adds: *u'min hashemen hametaftef v'afilu b'shaa shehaner dolek* — one may also take oil that is dripping from the lamp even while it is still burning. Taking oil from within the lamp while it is burning is prohibited — doing so would constitute a form of *kibui*, diminishing the flame. But oil that has dripped into the plate beneath the lamp while the lamp is burning — Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon permits using that oil. He is not concerned about *muktzah* as applied to that oil.

## Abaye/R. Elazar

### Agreement

*Amar Abaye*: Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon agrees with his father Rabbi Shimon in one respect and disagrees in another. *Sevira lei k'Rabbi Shimon b'chada* — he holds like his father in one area: *d'leis lei muktzah* — he fundamentally rejects *muktzah*, or at least the relevant categories thereof.

### Disagreement

*U'palig lei b'chada* — he disagrees in one area, going further than his father: *kavsah in lo kavsah lo* — his father holds that one may use the oil only once the lamp has gone out. Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon holds *af al gav d'lo kavsah* — even if the lamp has not yet gone out, one may use the oil that dripped into the plate beneath it.

### Issur Debate

What is the underlying conceptual dispute? If the only reason one cannot move a burning lamp is the concern that doing so will cause it to go out — *kibui* or *havarah* — and if one rejects *muktzah* entirely in this context, then even the oil that dripped from the lamp while it was burning should be completely permitted. One is not moving the lamp; one has no *muktzah* concern. This is the position of Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon: his sole restriction is the physical act of moving the lamp while it burns, but he holds no *muktzah* concern at all for the burning lamp or its associated oil.

His father Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, appears to hold a category of *muktzah machmat issur*. Things involved in or associated with a prohibited act become *muktzah*. Therefore, while the lamp is burning, not only may one not move it (due to *kibui*), but the oil dripping from it is also *muktzah machmat issur* — it is designated for and associated with the burning lamp. Once the lamp goes out, that *muktzah machmat issur* status dissolves and everything becomes permitted. The practical relevance of the *muktzah machmat issur* category — over and above the *kibui* concern — is precisely this question of the dripping oil.

Rabbi Shimon further rejects the principle that *muktzah* status is fixed at the outset of *Shabbat*: he does not say that because the lamp was burning at *bein hashmashot*, it remains forbidden all *Shabbat*. The other positions, however, do hold this principle — and this is how we rule. If something is *muktzah* when *Shabbat* begins, that status applies for the entire *Shabbat*.

## Container Size

### Why Different

The *Gemara* now turns to the anomaly in Rabbi Shimon's position: *mai shna hani* — why are a cup, a plate, and a lantern treated more stringently than a lamp? The lamp, according to Rabbi Shimon, is permitted once it goes out; why not these as well?

Ulla suggests: *seifa atya k'Rabbi Yehuda* — the latter clause about the lantern and the plate reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position, not Rabbi Shimon's. But Mar Zutra challenges this: the *mishnah* uses the word *aval* — "however" — which signals a contrast within the same opinion. It reads as though Rabbi Shimon himself says: lamps are fine once out, but a cup and a plate and a lantern are not. If it were simply a switch to Rabbi Yehuda's position, the contrastive *aval* would be inappropriate.

### Attribution

*Ela amar Mar Zutra* — rather, Mar Zutra says the entire passage belongs to Rabbi Shimon.

### Small/Large

When does Rabbi Shimon allow a lamp that has gone out? *Ner zuta* — in the case of a small lamp. *D'daitei iluvei* — because one's intention rests upon it: one expects it to burn out during *Shabbat* and plans to use the oil afterward. *Aval hani d'nafishi* — but the large containers — the cup, the plate, the lantern — are different because they are large. Under normal circumstances one would never expect such a large container of oil to burn out over the course of a *Shabbat*. One would have had no thought of using it.

The underlying principle is clearer now: Rabbi Shimon does not generally hold that *muktzah* status is fixed by *bein hashmashot*. However, there is an exception: if when *Shabbat* began the object was *muktzah* in a category Rabbi Shimon himself recognizes — namely *muktzah machmat issur*, the burning lamp — and if under normal circumstances one would never have expected the opportunity to use it during *Shabbat* (because it is a very large container of oil that one assumed would burn all night), then even Rabbi Shimon agrees that its status is defined at the onset of *Shabbat* and it remains forbidden even if it goes out.

A *baraisa* is cited to confirm this: *mutar hashemen shebanair veshabekara asur, Rabbi Shimon matir* — leftover oil in the lamp and in the plate is forbidden; Rabbi Shimon permits it. The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by reading the two cases as referring to different-sized plates: in the *baraisa*, the plate is a small one functioning analogously to the lamp — Rabbi Shimon permits it once extinguished. In the *mishnah*'s clause about the cup, plate, and lantern, the plate is a large one akin to the cup — Rabbi Shimon forbids it.

### Status Fixed

To summarize the structure of opinion: according to Rabbi Shimon, a small lamp that goes out during *Shabbat* is immediately permitted, because one had it in mind that it might be used. A large container of oil that one never expected to go out during *Shabbat* is forbidden even if it does, because its *muktzah machmat issur* status was fixed at the onset of *Shabbat* and was the sort of thing that under normal circumstances would have remained inaccessible all *Shabbat*. We rule more strictly than Rabbi Shimon: even in the case of a small lamp, once it is *muktzah* when *Shabbat* begins, that status applies all *Shabbat*.

## Metal Candelabra

### Pamot Case

*Amar Rabbi Zeira*: in the case of a *pamot* — a metal candelabrum, as opposed to a pottery *ner* — *shehidliku bo baShabbat*, that was burning when *Shabbat* began, *ledivrei hamatir assur, ledivrei ha'oser mutar* — the one who normally permits would here agree it is forbidden, and the one who normally forbids would here agree it is permitted. This is a striking statement and requires unpacking.

### Two Directions

The one who normally permits is, according to Tosafot, Rabbi Shimon — or according to Rashi, Rabbi Meir, who allows even old lamps (rejecting *muktzah machmat mius*). But here, even that permissive opinion would agree the metal candelabrum is *asur*, because it is currently burning. And the one who normally forbids — who holds *muktzah machmat mius* and says an old lamp is off-limits because it is disgusting — would here agree the metal candelabrum is *mutar*, because a metal candelabrum does not become disgusting with use.

### Category Holders

The *Gemara* is now drawing out an important implication: Rabbi Yehuda, who is normally strict about old lamps because of *muktzah machmat mius*, has never been asked about a burning lamp of the non-disgusting metal variety. One might have supposed he does not hold *muktzah machmat issur* at all, and that the metal candelabrum — even while burning — would be permitted in his view. Conversely, Rabbi Meir, who does not hold *muktzah machmat mius* and permits old lamps, clearly does hold *muktzah machmat issur* (as established from the *baraisa* regarding the lamp burning when *Shabbat* began). The question was whether Rabbi Yehuda holds *muktzah machmat issur* for the metal candelabrum that is not subject to *mius*.

### Braisa Proof

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraisa* that explicitly resolves this: *Rabbi Yehuda omer kol hanairos shel matechet metaltelin* — Rabbi Yehuda says all metal lamps may be moved — because they are not disgusting — *chutz min haner shehidliku bo b'oto Shabbat* — except for one that was burning when *Shabbat* began. This demonstrates explicitly that Rabbi Yehuda does hold *muktzah machmat issur* and forbids the metal candelabrum if it was burning at the onset of *Shabbat*.

### Final Ruling

The *Gemara* therefore revises the original formulation of Rabbi Zeira's statement: *divrei hakol assur* — everyone agrees that a metal candelabrum that was burning when *Shabbat* began is forbidden, because everyone holds *muktzah machmat issur*. And *lo hidliku aleha, divrei hakol mutar* — if it was not burning when *Shabbat* began, everyone agrees it is permitted, because a metal candelabrum does not become *muktzah machmat mius*. The sole remaining exception is Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon, who holds no *muktzah* concern for the burning lamp at all.

By way of a broader principle: if something is not *muktzah* when *Shabbat* begins, it can still become *muktzah* during *Shabbat* if circumstances change — for instance, if a vessel breaks, or if an egg falls into it. But that newly acquired *muktzah* status does not persist beyond the duration of the condition that created it. The status-defining power of *bein hashmashot* operates only in one direction: *muktzah* at the start means *muktzah* all *Shabbat*. But something not *muktzah* at the start can become *muktzah* mid-*Shabbat* only for as long as the triggering condition lasts. This is the principle of *ein muktzah lechatzei Shabbat*: a *muktzah* status that originates mid-*Shabbat* does not persist once the condition that created it is removed.

## Bed/Money Basis

### Bed Designation

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: *mita sheyichada lema'os assur letaltela* — a bed that has been designated for holding money may not be moved, even if no money has yet been placed on it and no money is currently on it. The premise is unusual: who uses a bed as a money drawer? But the *Gemara* entertains it. The ruling is that the mere designation is sufficient to render the bed off-limits.

### Tosafot Q

Tosafot raises the obvious challenge: how is this worse than a *kli shemelachto l'issur* like a hammer? A hammer is designated for a prohibited purpose and yet may still be moved for legitimate uses. If we say the bed is designated for money — an *issur* use on *Shabbat* — why can it not similarly be used for other purposes like any *kli shemelachto l'issur*?

### Designation/Use

Tosafot answers by distinguishing: there are objects that, unlike a hammer, would never be used for any other purpose once designated for one role. A trumpet, for instance, is not going to be used to crack nuts; it has a single use. The *Gemara* assumes that a bed designated exclusively for holding money falls into that category — it becomes an object of a single, prohibited use, and thus entirely off-limits.

### R. Nachman Q

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenges this with the case of the new lamp: *metaltelin ner chadash aval lo yashan* — a new lamp may be moved. A new lamp was manufactured for lighting, which is its designated purpose and its only purpose. If anything, its designation for a prohibited purpose is more inherent than that of a bed. Yet it is permitted if it has never been used. How then can a designated bed be forbidden before any use?

### Must Use

The *Gemara* resolves this by revising Rav Yehuda's statement: *ela i itmar hachi itmar* — if this was said, here is how it was said. *Hiniach aleha ma'ot assur letaltela* — if one actually placed money upon the bed, thereby employing it in its designated use, then it is forbidden to move it on *Shabbat*, even if the money is not on it now. Once the bed has been put to use as a money holder, it acquires a new identity — analogous to the trumpet, which has only one use — and is permanently off-limits on *Shabbat*. *Lo hiniach aleha ma'ot mutar letaltela* — if one has designated it but has not yet placed money on it, it is still permitted, just as the new lamp is permitted before it has been used.

## Status @ Outset

### Desig+Used

The analysis now becomes more nuanced. Three cases are laid out. The first: one designated the bed for money *and* has used it in that capacity. In this case, the bed has been transformed into a money-holder with a singular identity. Even if no money is on it now, and even if no money was on it at *bein hashmashot*, one may not move it on *Shabbat*.

### Desig/NoUse

The second: one designated the bed for money but has never actually placed money on it. This is the case of the new lamp — the designation has not yet been actualized. The bed retains its status as an ordinary bed and may be moved.

### No Desig

The third: one did not designate the bed for money at all. Here it depends entirely on whether money is present. *Yesh aleha ma'ot assur letaltela* — if money is currently on the bed, one may not move it, because it is now a *basis ladavar ha'asur* — a supporter for *muktzah*. *Ein aleha ma'ot mutar letaltela* — if there is no money on it, it is permitted.

### Bein Hashem

The critical qualifier: *v'hu shelo hayu aleha bein hashmashot* — provided that the money was not on the bed when *Shabbat* began. If money was intentionally placed on the bed at *bein hashmashot*, the bed became a *basis* for the *muktzah* money at that moment, and it retains that status for the entire *Shabbat*, even after the money is removed. This is the principle of *bein hashmashot* determining status for the whole *Shabbat*.

One important nuance: for a *basis* to be created, the *muktzah* item must have been intentionally placed on the supporting object. If coins were accidentally dropped on a bed, that does not create a *basis*. If one intentionally placed a wallet on the bed before *bein hashmashot* and then forgot to remove it, the wallet was deliberately placed and the *basis* is established, even though the forgetfulness was unintentional.

The question of whether one may *use* a *muktzah* item without *moving* it — for instance, sleeping on a bed that is a *basis* — is a separate and genuinely debated question among the *Rishonim*.

## Drawer/Tumah

### Cabinet Setup

*Ulla* reports a challenge raised by Rabbi Eliezer from a *mishnah* dealing with a *shidda* — a large cabinet — and its *muchni*, a detachable element that is variously understood by Rashi (as a kind of wheel) and Tosafot (as a base or drawer). The *mishnah* is complex and its bulk concerns laws of *tumah* rather than *Shabbat*, which accounts for the extensive Rashi and Tosafot here.

### Detachable

*Bizman shehi nishmet* — if this element (the base or drawer) can be detached, *ein chibur lah* — it is not considered connected to the cabinet for purposes of *tumah*. If one of the items is rendered *tamei*, the *tumah* does not automatically transfer to the other through this connection, because they are treated as separate objects.

### Measure/Seal

*V'ein nimdedes imah* — when detachable, it is not measured together with the cabinet. This is relevant because a wooden vessel that can contain forty *seah* — the volume of a *mikvah* — cannot become *tamei*. If the drawer is detachable, its volume is not counted together with the cabinet's volume when assessing whether the forty-*seah* threshold is reached.

*V'einah metzelet imah b'ohel hamet* — it does not protect the cabinet's contents in a room containing a dead body. The mechanism here, as Tosafot explains, concerns the *tzamid patil*: a tightly sealed vessel protects its contents from *tumah*. If the cabinet is so large as to be beyond the threshold of *tumah*, and has a tight seal, objects inside are protected. However, if the drawer is detachable, its presence does not fully seal the opening where it sits, and the contents of the cabinet may still become *tamei* through that gap.

### Cannot Drag

*V'ein gorerin otah baShabbat* — one may not drag the cabinet on *Shabbat*, *bizman sheyesh aleha ma'ot* — if money is on the drawer or cabinet. Tosafot notes that when a drawer is completely fixed to a cabinet, dragging the cabinet is simply moving the cabinet and the drawer is merely incidental. But when the drawer is detachable, moving the cabinet is understood as moving two separate items — the cabinet and the drawer — and thus if money is on the drawer, one is moving the *muktzah basis* directly.

### If No Money

The *mishnah* implies: *ha ein aleha ma'ot sharya* — if there is no money on it, it is permitted, and the simple reading suggests this is true even if money was on it at *bein hashmashot*.

## BH Status Q

### Contradiction

This presents an apparent contradiction. If the *mishnah* about the *shidda* implies that the absence of money *now* renders the object permitted — even if money was present at *bein hashmashot* — then the principle that *bein hashmashot* determines status for the whole *Shabbat* would seem to be contradicted.

### R. Shimon Attr

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ha hi Rabbi Shimon hi* — that *mishnah* about the *shidda* reflects the view of Rabbi Shimon, who does not hold of *muktzah* and does not hold that *bein hashmashot* status defines *Shabbat* status. According to Rabbi Shimon, if the money is not on the object now, it is permitted now.

### Rav/R. Yehuda

*V'Rav k'Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei* — Rav, however, holds like Rabbi Yehuda, and therefore holds that *bein hashmashot* status is determinative.

### Palm Tree

The *Gemara* offers proof: *amar Rav manichin ner gabi dekla baShabbat* — Rav says one may place a lamp on a palm tree on *Shabbat*. The concern would be that having placed the lamp there, one might come to take hold of the tree when retrieving the lamp — and using a *mechubar*, an item still attached to the ground, is prohibited on *Shabbat*. Rav says this concern does not apply, because since the lamp was burning when *Shabbat* began, it is *muktzah* and one will not come to remove it during *Shabbat*.

### Yom Tov

*V'ein manichin ner gabi dekla b'Yom Tov* — but on *Yom Tov*, one may not do so. On *Yom Tov*, a lamp is not *muktzah*. Therefore there is a genuine concern that one might retrieve the lamp from the tree during *Yom Tov* and thereby make use of the tree. The critical distinction is that on *Shabbat* the lamp is *muktzah* for the whole *Shabbat* — it was burning at the onset — and one will therefore not touch it; on *Yom Tov* no such *muktzah* applies and the concern is real.

### Conclusion

*Ei amarta bishlama d'Rav k'Rabbi Yehuda sevira lei* — if Rav holds like Rabbi Yehuda, that something *muktzah* at the outset of *Shabbat* remains so all *Shabbat*, then the distinction between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* is perfectly intelligible. *Ela i amarta k'Rabbi Shimon sevira lei* — but if Rav holds like Rabbi Shimon, who permits the lamp once it goes out, then *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* would be indistinguishable. This proves that Rav holds like Rabbi Yehuda, that status is fixed at the beginning of *Shabbat*.

The conclusion is thus firmly established: Rav holds that the status of an object at *bein hashmashot* — the onset of *Shabbat* — determines its *muktzah* status for the entire *Shabbat*. A bed on which money was intentionally placed at *bein hashmashot* is a *basis* for *muktzah* and remains off-limits the entire *Shabbat*, even after the money is removed. This is the ruling we follow, in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda, against Rabbi Shimon.
