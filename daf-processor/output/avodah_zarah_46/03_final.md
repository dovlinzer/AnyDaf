# Avodah Zarah 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tfisat Yad Adam

### Mountains & Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַגּוֹיִם הָעוֹבְדִים אֶת הֶהָרִים וְאֶת הַגְּבָעוֹת — הֵן מוּתָּרִין, וְעוֹבְדֵיהֶן בְּסַיִיף, וְאֶת הַזְּרָעִים וְאֶת הַיְּרָקוֹת — הֵן אֲסוּרִין, וְעוֹבְדֵיהֶן בְּסַיִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The ***tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Sheshet:** With regard to the *halakha* in the case of **the gentiles who worship the mountains and the hills,** the mountains and hills **are permitted, but their worshippers** are punished with decapitation **by** the **sword. But** with regard to gentiles who worship **plants and vegetables,** the plants and vegetables **are forbidden and their worshippers** are punished with decapitation **by** the **sword.**

The daf opens with a review of a debate that has been unfolding at the bottom of 45b. The question is whether worshipping a mountain renders it *assur b'hana'ah*. The answer is no — mountains and hills remain permissible — but the objects on them may not. With respect to trees, the *chachamim* hold that worshipping a tree does not render it forbidden. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, however, take a stricter view: by virtue of *tfisat yad adam* — human control or shaping — a tree can acquire the status of *avodah zarah*, at least as regards the growth that sprouts after the act of worship.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאָמַר לָךְ, מַנִּי? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ — אָסוּר. וְלוֹקְמַהּ בְּאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ מִתְּחִלָּה לְכָךְ, וְרַבָּנַן! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּקָתָנֵי דּוּמְיָא דְּהַר, מָה הַר שֶׁלֹּא נְטָעוֹ מִתְּחִלָּה לְכָךְ, אַף הַאי נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא נְטָעוֹ מִתְּחִלָּה לְכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to him: Who is** the *tanna* **who said** this **to you? It** must be **Rabbi Yosei, son of** Rabbi **Yehuda, who says** that **a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped is forbidden.** **But** why not **interpret** the *baraita* as referring **to a tree that one initially planted for** idol worship, **and** then the ruling would be consistent with the opinion of **the Rabbis** as well? The Gemara answers: This should **not enter your mind, as** the *baraita* **teaches** the case of plants and vegetables as being **similar to** the case of **a mountain.** Therefore, one can extrapolate that **just as** with regard to **a mountain,** it is a case **where one did not initially plant it for this** purpose, as mountains are not planted by people, **so too, this** case of plants and vegetables is a case **where one did not initially plant it for this** purpose.

This raises a prior question: where precisely does the threshold of *tfisat yad adam* lie? If a person planted a seed — not a sapling — does the resulting tree fall under this doctrine? Rashi and Tosafot dispute this directly. Rashi holds that even planting a seed constitutes *tfisat yad adam*, and therefore Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda would regard subsequent growth as forbidden if worshipped. Tosafot disagrees: only planting an actual sapling counts, because when one plants a seed one does not yet relate to it as a tree. According to Tosafot, even Rabbi Yosi would permit what grows from a seed. Both authorities agree, however, that something which grew entirely on its own — never subject to human intervention — poses no problem for anyone. Such a tree is simply part of the landscape; no act of worship can retroactively define or control it.

It is important to note that the prohibition, when it does apply, is not a personal *k'nas* imposed only on the one who worshipped. Rather, it reflects the reality that the object has acquired the status of *avodah zarah* and is therefore forbidden to everyone.

### Stages of Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הַאי סְבָרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מְ״אַבֵּד תְּאַבְּדוּן״ — ״אַבֵּד״, וְאַחַר כָּךְ ״תְּאַבֵּדוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** derive **this opinion** about the order of priorities in the process of conquering Eretz Yisrael? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: "You shall destroy all the places where the nations that you are to dispossess served their gods, upon the high mountains, and upon the hills, and under every leafy tree." From the double-verb form of the directive **"you shall destroy [*abbed te'abedun*]"** he derives that there are two stages to the destruction of their gods: First **destroy** them **[*abbed*],** i.e., smash them; then go and conquer the land, **and** only **afterward you shall destroy [*te'abedun*]** them completely, i.e., burn or eradicate them.

The two stages of destruction found in the *pesukim* — first cutting down and then burning — are interpreted differently by the parties to this dispute. Rav Huna reads these two stages, according to the *chachamim*, as corresponding to two phases of conquest: first one destroys what is visible upon entering the land, and later, after consolidating control, one returns and completes the destruction. Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda reads the two stages differently: cutting down leaves the stump of a tree that was worshipped after it had already grown, while total burning is reserved for a tree that was planted from the outset for the purpose of worship — the classic *asherah* of the Torah.

## Uprooting & Roots

### Rav Huna's Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רְדוֹף, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שְׂרוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says: Pursue** the enemy **and then** return to **burn** them. Meaning, first smash their objects of idol worship, then conquer the land, and then return to burn the smashed items.

Picking up at the bottom of 45b, the *gemara* quotes Rav Huna: *radof achar kach srof* — pursue it and then burn it. This encapsulates the *chachamim*'s reading of the two-stage destruction: first the initial campaign of cutting down, then a return to finish the burning after the land is secured.

### Root Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְעוֹקֵר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְשָׁרֵשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what do **the Rabbis** derive from the double verb? The Gemara answers: **This** double verb **is necessary to** teach that when one **deracinates** an object of **idol worship, he needs to root out all traces of it.**

Where does Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda derive his two-stage reading? *Nafka lei mi'abbed te'abbedun* — from the doubled language "surely destroy." The first *abbed* is one pass; the *te'abbedun* is the return. And what do the rabbis (*rabbanan*) do with that doubled language, if they use the two stages differently? *Le'oker avodat kochavim shetzarich lesharesh achareiha* — it is not enough to uproot idolatry; one must dig up its roots as well. The image is both literal and metaphorical: if you deal only with surface manifestations and not root causes, the problem will simply reemerge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לְשָׁרֵשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וְּאִבַּדְתֶּם אֶת שְׁמָם מִן הַמָּקוֹם הַהוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** derive the obligation **to root out all traces of** idol worship? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: "And you shall break down their altars, and dash in pieces their pillars, and burn their *asherim* with fire; and you shall hew down the graven images of their gods; **and you shall destroy their name out of that place"** (Deuteronomy 12:3).

In turn, where does Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda source this obligation to pursue the roots? *Nafka lei mi've'ibadtem shemam min hamakom hahu* — "you shall utterly destroy their name from that place." Obliterating the name means ensuring that it never resurfaces.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא לְכַנּוֹת לָהּ שֵׁם, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְעוֹקֵר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְשָׁרֵשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִבַּדְתֶּם אֶת שְׁמָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what do **the Rabbis** derive from this verse? The Gemara answers: **That** verse teaches that it is a mitzva **to give** a derogatory **nickname to** an idol. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer says: **From where** is it derived that when one **deracinates** an object of **idol worship, he needs to root out all traces of it? The verse states: "And you shall destroy their name** out of that place."

And what do the *rabbanan* do with that phrase? *L'chanos l'shem* — they read it more literally: do not continue to use the idol's name; give it a replacement name instead.

### Eliezer vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״אַבֵּד תְּאַבְּדוּן״, אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִבַּדְתֶּם אֶת שְׁמָם מִן הַמָּקוֹם הַהוּא״? לְכַנּוֹת לָהּ שֵׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: But isn't it already stated: "You shall destroy"** (Deuteronomy 12:2)? This obviously includes rooting out all traces of idols. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "And you shall destroy their name out of that place"?** This means that it is a mitzva **to give it a nickname.**

A *baraita* elaborates this point. Rabbi Eliezer asks: how do we know that when uprooting *avodah zarah* one must dig up its roots? From the verse *ve'ibadetem et shemam* — "you shall destroy their name." Rabbi Akiva responds: but we already know the requirement of thorough destruction from *abed te'abbedun*, "surely destroy." If so, what does *ve'ibadetem et shemam* add? It must come to teach *l'chanos l'shem* — to substitute a replacement name. One might then wonder whether the replacement name should be a praiseworthy one (*l'shevach*). The *gemara* treats this as self-evidently absurd. Perhaps then a neutral name, neither praiseworthy nor derogatory? No — from *shakeitz teshaktzenu ve'ta'ev teta'avenu ki cherem hu*, "you shall surely abominate it and find it repulsive, for it is under the ban," we learn that the renaming must carry a sense of disgust.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לְשֶׁבַח? לְשֶׁבַח סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא יָכוֹל לֹא לְשֶׁבַח וְלֹא לִגְנַאי? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שַׁקֵּץ תְּשַׁקְּצֶנּוּ וְתַעֵב תְּתַעֲבֶנּוּ כִּי חֵרֶם הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that one may give it **a positive** nickname. The Gemara interjects: Would it **enter your mind** that one may give **a positive** nickname to an idol? **Rather,** the *baraita* means that one **might** have thought that the reference is to a nickname that is **neither positive nor negative.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And you shall not bring an abomination into your house, and be accursed like it; **you shall detest it, and you shall abhor it; for it is a proscribed item"** (Deuteronomy 7:26). This verse clearly indicates that the nickname should be a negative one.

### Dysphemism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתָהּ בֵּית גַּלְיָא — קוֹרִין אוֹתָהּ ״בֵּית כַּרְיָא״, פְּנֵי מֶלֶךְ — ״פְּנֵי כֶּלֶב״, עֵין כֹּל — ״עֵין קוֹץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** To what type of nickname is this referring? If the idol worshippers **would call** their house of worship **the house of elevation [*beit galya*], one should call it the house of digging [*beit karya*].** If they call it **the all-seeing eye [*ein kol*],** one should call it **the eye of a thorn [*ein kotz*].**

The *gemara* illustrates this concretely: a place called *Beit Galya* — "house of the heights" — should be renamed *Beit Karya*, a place of a ditch. *Ein Kol*, "the all-seeing eye," becomes *Ein Kotz*, "the eye with a thorn." The substituted name sounds similar to the original but carries a degrading connotation. This practice — calling something negative instead of positive, the opposite of euphemism — has an English name: dysphemism (or cacophemism in some formulations).

### Cultural Context

This renaming practice reflects a long and deeply embedded Jewish tradition of referring to the objects and institutions of other religions in derogatory terms — *beit tiflah* (house of nonsense) instead of *beit tefillah* (house of prayer), and similar coinages. Such practices are entirely understandable in historical context. A minority community subjected to cultural pressure, periodic persecution, and constant negative messaging from the dominant culture needed internal mechanisms to reinforce its own sense of truth and worth. Dysphemism served that function.

The difficulty is that these attitudes, when carried forward into contemporary contexts — particularly in more insular *chareidi* or *chassidish* communities — can become a reflexive devaluation of others' religious lives, sustained long past the conditions that originally gave rise to them.

### Memory & Irony

There is also a structural irony in this whole enterprise. *Ve'ibadetem et shemam* — "you shall destroy their name" — sits in tension with the very act of renaming. One thinks of the parallel with *timcheh et zecher Amalek lo tishkach*: "blot out the memory of Amalek, do not forget." The command to forget becomes the very mechanism that perpetuates the memory. If one genuinely wanted a name to be forgotten, the most effective method would be simply to stop using it and replace it with something entirely unrelated. Places that have been given wholly new names — Constantinople becoming Istanbul, Saint Petersburg becoming Leningrad — fade from common memory after a few generations. But if one instead coins a mocking substitute that sounds like the original, one is actually keeping the original alive in people's consciousness, precisely in order to sustain the sense of repulsion. The *gemara*'s logic, in other words, is that sustaining that feeling of disgust is considered more important than achieving total obliteration of the memory — presumably because the danger is seen as ongoing and the community needs to remain alert to it.

## Natural Objects

### Baraita & Akum

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* taught before Rav Sheshet regarding non-Jews who worship mountains, hills, plants, and vegetables. Mountains and hills themselves remain permissible for lay use, but plants and vegetables that have been worshipped become forbidden.

### Planted Intent

The question arises: whose *baraita* is this? It must follow Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *tfisat yad adam* renders planted things forbidden when worshipped. One might counter that even the *rabbanan* agree in a case where something was planted from the outset for the purpose of worship — that is the classic *asherah* case. Perhaps the seeds and vegetables here were planted specifically to be worshipped. The *gemara* rejects this: the *baraita* is structured *dumya de'har*, analogous to a mountain — just as a mountain was not planted for the purpose of worship, the case here likewise involves something not planted for that purpose but later worshipped. That it is forbidden under such circumstances is the distinctive position of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rolling Boulders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: אַבְנֵי הַר שֶׁנִּדַּלְדְּלוּ — בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, חַד אָמַר: אֲסוּרוֹת, וְחַד אָמַר: מוּתָּרוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מוּתָּרוֹת? כְּהַר — מָה הַר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ תְּפִיסַת יְדֵי אָדָם וּמוּתָּר, אַף הָנֵי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן תְּפִיסַת יְדֵי אָדָם וּמוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated:** The *halakha* in a case where a gentile worships **boulders that** naturally **became dislodged from a mountain** is subject to dispute between **the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya** on the one hand **and Rabbi Yoḥanan** on the other. **One** side **says: They are forbidden, and one** side **says: They are permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the one who says** that **they are permitted?** The Gemara answers: The status of the dislodged stones is still **like** the status of the **mountain** itself. **Just as** in the case of **a mountain, it** is **not** the product of **human involvement, and** therefore it is **permitted** to derive benefit from it even after it is worshipped, **so too, these** boulders, **which** are **not** the product of **human involvement, are permitted.**

The *gemara* now takes up a case that sits at the intersection of these categories. *Avnei har she-nidaldlu* — stones that have rolled off a mountain. Such a boulder presents a genuinely puzzling profile: it was never shaped by human hands and has no *tfisat yad adam* in that sense, yet it is no longer attached to the ground, and it is not a living animal. We have established two categories of things that, even if worshipped, do not become forbidden: *mechubar*, something attached to the ground, and *baal chai*, a living animal. A detached boulder fits neither category cleanly.

This is the subject of a dispute between the sons of Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Yochanan — one holds the boulder remains permissible if worshipped, the other holds it is forbidden.

The reasoning of the permissive position: just as a mountain has no *tfisat yad adam* and is therefore permissible, so too this boulder. But the *gemara* challenges: *mah le'har she-ken mechubar* — the mountain's permissibility may derive specifically from being attached to the ground, not merely from lacking human shaping. Response: *beheimah tochiach* — an animal proves the point. A live animal is not attached to the ground, yet if worshipped it remains permissible for lay use. But then: *mah le'beheimah she-ken baal chai* — perhaps an animal's permissibility derives from being alive, not merely from lacking human shaping. This ping-pong of *har yochiach / beheimah tochiach* — each example proving the other — leads to the classic formulation of *ha'tzad ha'shaveh she'bahen*: the common denominator between them is *she'ein bahen tfisat yad adam* — that neither has been shaped by human hands. From that common denominator: *af kol she'ein bah tfisat yad adam u'mutar* — anything not shaped by human hands is permissible even if worshipped.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַר, שֶׁכֵּן מְחוּבָּר! בְּהֵמָה תּוֹכִיחַ. מָה לִבְהֵמָה, שֶׁכֵּן בַּעֲלַת חַיִּים! הַר יוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן תְּפִיסַת יְדֵי אָדָם וּמוּתָּר, אַף כׇּל שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן תְּפִיסַת יְדֵי אָדָם וּמוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **What** is notable **about a mountain?** It is notable **in that** it is **attached** to the ground. This may be the reason for the leniency concerning it, and this does not apply to boulders. The Gemara responds: The case of a worshipped **animal can prove** that this is not relevant, as it is permitted to derive benefit from a worshipped animal even though it is not attached to the ground. The Gemara defends its objection: **What** is notable **about an animal?** It is notable **in that** it is **a living,** animate **being.** This may be the reason for the leniency concerning it, and this does not apply to boulders. The Gemara responds: The case of **a mountain can prove** that this is not relevant, as it is permitted to derive benefit from a mountain that was worshipped, even though it is not a living being. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point, and the *halakha* can be inferred from a combination of the two sources: **The aspect of this** case, that of a mountain, **is not like the aspect of that** case, that of an animal, **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case; **their common element is that they are not** products of **human involvement, and** it is **permitted** to derive benefit from them. **So too,** in **any** case of worshipped objects **that are not** the product of **human involvement,** including boulders, they are **permitted.**

This is a striking piece of reasoning. The underlying idea is that things still alive or still rooted in the ground remain, in a meaningful sense, independent of us. We shape raw materials; we do not shape the living and the rooted. A detached boulder, never touched by human hands, belongs to the same category.

### No Human Shaping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא נִשְׁתַּנּוּ מִבְּרִיָּיתָן! אֶלָּא אָתְיָא מִבְּהֵמָה בַּעֲלַת מוּם, וּמֵהַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **What** is notable **about the common element between** the case of a mountain and that of an animal that prevents utilizing it as a paradigm for other cases? These worshipped objects are notable **in that they were not altered from their natural form.** Perhaps that is why their status remains unchanged. By contrast, a dislodged boulder has been moved from its original position. **Rather,** the *halakha* that a boulder that is dislodged from a mountain is still permitted after it is worshipped **is derived from** the case of **a blemished animal and from** the case of **a mountain,** both of which are permitted after having been worshipped.

One additional challenge is raised: perhaps the common denominator between a mountain and an animal is not merely the absence of human shaping but rather *she'lo nishtanu mi'beriyatan* — that they have not undergone any transformation from their original state. A boulder that has rolled off a mountain has, in a sense, been transformed — it is no longer in its original position. The *gemara* responds by adducing a wounded animal (*beheimah ba'alat mum*): even an animal that has undergone physical change remains permissible if worshipped. Alternatively, a dried-out withered tree (*ilan yavesh*): even though it has changed from its living state, it would not become *avodah zarah* if worshipped. The operative criterion is not the preservation of original form but the absence of human shaping.

### Absence of Control

So the first position — that the boulder remains permissible — rests on the *tzad ha'shaveh*: neither a mountain nor an animal has been controlled or shaped by human hands, and the same is true of a boulder that simply rolled off naturally.

### Sheketz Teshaktzenu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָסַר, לְהָכִי כְּתִיב ״שַׁקֵּץ תְּשַׁקְּצֶנּוּ וְתַעֵב תְּתַעֲבֶנּוּ״, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָתְיָא מִדִּינָא לְהֶיתֵּרָא, לָא תֵּתְיַאּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning of **the one who deems** worshipped boulders **forbidden?** The Gemara answers: According to that opinion, it is in order **to** teach **this** *halakha*: **It is written:** "And you shall not bring an abomination into your house, and be accursed like it; **you shall detest it, and you shall abhor it;** for it is a proscribed item" (Deuteronomy 7:26). This verse teaches **that although** one may theoretically **derive, by logical inference, to permit** the boulders, **do not derive** that *halakha*.

The second position — that the boulder is forbidden — also has a rationale, and it is a remarkable one. *Lahachi kasiv shakeitz teshaktzenu ve'ta'ev teta'avenu* — this is what we learn from the verse commanding us to utterly abominate *avodah zarah*. Even if the logical derivation (*dina*) from the common denominator would suggest permissibility, that general attitude of revulsion and distance toward *avodah zarah* shapes the hermeneutic process itself. When determining the scope of a prohibition, one should not seek to narrow it; one should incline toward expansion, because the very nature of *avodah zarah* demands that we treat it as maximally repulsive.

This is a genuinely fascinating use of an aggadic-seeming principle within the halakhic process. It is worth noting that this is not the same as using *shakeitz teshaktzenu* to include something within the category of *avodah zarah* that is not actually *avodah zarah* — a use that would be more problematic, and one that figures, for example, in a dispute between Rav Ovadia Yosef and the Tzitz Eliezer regarding entry into a mosque. Rav Ovadia argues that Islam is not *avodah zarah*; the Tzitz Eliezer invokes *shakeitz teshaktzenu* to maintain the restriction. Here in the *gemara*, by contrast, someone has genuinely performed an act of worship, and the question is only how to classify its object. Still, the *gemara*'s willingness to bring this visceral attitude to bear as a halakhic principle in its own right is striking.

## Egg Question

### Egg Elevation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים, דִּבְנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּשָׁרוּ, דְּבָעֵי חִזְקִיָּה: זָקַף בֵּיצָה לְהִשְׁתַּחֲווֹת לָהּ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara introduced the case of a boulder that naturally dislodged from a mountain as the subject of a dispute between the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan, but it did not specify who subscribes to which opinion. The Gemara suggests: **It may be concluded that it is the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya who deem** it **permitted, as Ḥizkiyya,** Rabbi Ḥiyya's son, **raises a dilemma:** If one **erected an egg** in order **to bow to it** as an idol, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it then prohibited to derive benefit from the egg?

The *gemara* now attempts to determine which of the two sons of Rabbi Chiyya took which position in the boulder dispute. *Tistayem dibne Rabbi Chiyya d'sharu* — let us try to conclude that it is the sons of Rabbi Chiyya who hold the boulder permissible. The evidence: *de'ba'ei Chizkiyah* — Chizkiyah (one of Rabbi Chiyya's two sons, the other being Yehuda) posed the following question: *zakaf beitza le'hishtachavosei lah mahu* — if one took an egg and elevated it on a wall in order to bow down to it, what is the law?

### Prep vs. Worship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ לְהִשְׁתַּחֲווֹת לָהּ, וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ, וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ הַאי זְקִיפָתָהּ אִי הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה אִי לָא הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה, אֲבָל לֹא זָקַף — לָא מִיתַּסְרָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּשָׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **It enters your mind** that this dilemma is referring to a case where one erected an egg in order **to bow to it, and he** then **bowed to it. And he raises a dilemma** as to **whether this erecting** of the egg **is** considered a significant **act** of human involvement that renders the egg forbidden as an object of idol worship, or **whether** it **is not** considered a significant **act** of human involvement. **But** if he had **not erected** the egg, there would be no dilemma, as clearly it would **not become forbidden.** Accordingly, one can **conclude from** this dilemma that **the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya** are the ones **who deem** the boulder **permitted,** since it is not a product of human involvement.

The initial reading of Chizkiyah's question assumes he was asking about a case where one actually did bow down to the egg, and the question concerned the act of elevation: is *zakifah*, placing the egg on the wall, itself a sufficient *ma'aseh* — a sufficient act of human engagement — to constitute *tfisat yad adam* and thus render the egg definable as an object of worship? The implicit premise of the question is that an egg simply lying in the coop, bowed down to without any prior handling, would not be a problem — because it is, like the boulder, a natural object untouched by human hands, not attached to the ground, not alive. The question is whether the act of placement changes that.

If Chizkiyah — a son of Rabbi Chiyya — is asking this question and presupposing that an untouched egg in the coop is unproblematic, that would align with the position that such natural objects are permissible. Hence the *gemara*'s attempt to conclude that the sons of Rabbi Chiyya are the permissive ones.

### Non-Jew Worships

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּאָסְרִי, דְּהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא זְקָפָהּ אֲסוּרָה, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁזָּקַף בֵּיצָה לְהִשְׁתַּחֲווֹת לָהּ, וְלֹא הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No, actually I will say to you** that perhaps it is **the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya who deem** the dislodged boulder **forbidden** after it was worshipped, and they similarly hold **that** in this case of the egg, if **he bowed to it, even if did not erect it,** the egg is certainly **forbidden. And** the dilemma is as follows: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where he erected** the **egg** in order **to bow to it, but he** ultimately **did not bow to it.** Ḥizkiyya's dilemma was whether designation of an object as an idol renders it forbidden even without actual worship.

The *gemara* then offers an alternative reading. Perhaps the sons of Rabbi Chiyya are actually the *stricter* ones — holding that worshipping a natural untouched object *does* render it forbidden. On this reading, bowing down to an egg in the coop without touching it would already be problematic. The question Chizkiyah is raising is therefore an entirely different one: *kegon shezakaf beitza le'hishtachavosei lah ve'lo hishtachavah lah* — you elevated the egg with the intent to bow down but never actually did so. The egg was then worshipped by a non-Jew. Normally, a non-Jew cannot, through his own act of worship, render a Jew's object forbidden — since the Jew never consented. But here the Jew had already set the egg up as an object of worship, thereby signaling his willingness. Can the non-Jew's subsequent act of worship now fix its forbidden status?

### Egg vs. Brick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן? אִי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲסוּרָה מִיָּד — אֲסוּרָה, אִי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁתֵּיעָבֵד — הָא לָא פַּלְחַהּ! לָא צְרִיכָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁזָּקַף בֵּיצָה לְהִשְׁתַּחֲווֹת לָהּ, וְלֹא הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ, וּבָא גּוֹי וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ. כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁזָּקַף לְבֵינָה לְהִשְׁתַּחֲווֹת לָהּ, וּבָא גּוֹי וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ — אֲסוּרָה. וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: לְבֵינָה הוּא דְּמִינַּכְרָא זְקִיפָתָהּ, אֲבָל בֵּיצָה — לָא? אוֹ דִּלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to whom** does he raise the dilemma? **If it is according to the one who says** that any object **of a Jew** who dedicated it to **idol worship is immediately** rendered **forbidden,** there is no place for the dilemma; the egg is clearly **forbidden. If it is according to the one who says** that an object of idol worship is not forbidden **until it is** actually **worshipped,** there is also no place for the dilemma, as the egg was **not worshipped,** and is therefore permitted. The Gemara answers by reformulating the case: **No,** those are not the cases with regard to which the dilemma is raised. It is **necessary** to raise it with regard to a case **where** one **erected an egg** in order **to bow to it, but** ultimately **he did not bow to it, and** then **a gentile came** along **and bowed to it.** There is an established principle that one cannot render forbidden an item that is not his. In this case, the egg can be rendered forbidden only by a combination of the Jewish owner's indicated intention and the gentile's act of worship. This dilemma assumes **that which Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** In the case of **a Jew who erected a brick** in order **to bow to it, and a gentile came and bowed to it,** it is **forbidden. And** Ḥizkiyya **raises the** following **dilemma** with regard to the case of an egg: **Is it** specifically **a brick** that is forbidden, **as its being erect is** clearly **recognizable** and is therefore a clear indication of intent on behalf of the Jewish owner, **but** as for **an egg,** erecting it is **not** enough of an indication, and it is therefore permitted? **Or perhaps** there **is no difference,** and the egg would similarly be forbidden. The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

This connects to a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: a Jew who erected a brick (*zakaf leveina*) — standing it upright in a conspicuous manner — and a non-Jew then came and bowed down to it, the brick is forbidden. This is the case Michael had raised. The question is whether an egg is analogous. A brick is man-made; its being stood upright is a clear, visible signal of intent. An egg placed on a wall is a much less legible act.

### Intent & Form

This is precisely the *gemara*'s question: *liveinah hu deminchara zekifutsei* — it is specifically by a brick that the act of erection is visually obvious and communicates the owner's intent to any passerby, so that a non-Jew who bows down to it is acting in accordance with that publicly signaled intent. *Aval beitza lo* — but an egg on a wall sends no such clear message. *O dilma lo shana* — or perhaps the distinction is irrelevant, and since the Jew's internal intent was present, even a non-Jew's worship suffices to define the object. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved, and similarly leaves unresolved which of the sons of Rabbi Chiyya held which position on the boulder.

## Temple Use

### Mountain Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְהַר, אֲבָנָיו מַהוּ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** In the case of **one who bows down to a mountain, what is** the *halakha* with regard to the permissibility of using **its stones for** building **the altar** in the Temple?

Rami bar Chama poses a new question: *mishtachaveh le'harar rabbanan mahu le'mizbe'ach* — if one worships a mountain, we know that for ordinary people the mountain remains permissible. But can stones quarried from that mountain be used for the altar in the *Beis HaMikdash*? The parallel to the *baal chai* case is instructive: we know from a special *pasuk* that an animal that was worshipped, though permissible for ordinary use, cannot be brought as a *korban*. Does something analogous apply to worshipped objects that are *mechubar*?

### Two Status Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ נֶעֱבָד בִּמְחוּבָּר אֵצֶל גָּבוֹהַּ, אוֹ אֵין נֶעֱבָד בִּמְחוּבָּר אֵצֶל גָּבוֹהַּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: An animal that was worshipped is rendered unfit to be sacrificed as an offering in the Temple. The Gemara previously established a principle that objects attached to the ground are not rendered forbidden by idol worship. The question here is this: **With regard to** an item that is **attached** to the ground and that **was worshipped, is it** rendered unfit **for** the **Most High,** i.e., for the Temple, just like an animal? **Or** is the *halakha* that **with regard to** an item that is **attached** to the ground and that **was worshipped, it is not** rendered unfit **for** the **Most High,** just as it is not forbidden with regard to an individual's deriving benefit from it?

The *gemara* frames this as a two-part question. First: does a worshipped object that is *mechubar* acquire any forbidden status *vis-à-vis* the divine sphere (*eitzel gavo'ah*) even though it has no such status for ordinary human use? Second — assuming it does — does that prohibition extend not only to being offered as a sacrifice but also to *machshirei korban*, things that facilitate a sacrifice, such as building material for the altar itself?

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר יֵשׁ נֶעֱבָד בִּמְחוּבָּר אֵצֶל גָּבוֹהַּ, מַכְשִׁירֵי קׇרְבָּן כְּקׇרְבָּן דָּמוּ אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **if you say** that **with regard to** an item that is **attached** to the ground and that **was worshipped, it is** rendered unfit **for** the **Most High,** are the items that merely **facilitate** the sacrifice of **an offering,** such as the altar, viewed the same way **as an offering** itself, **or not?** Perhaps the principle that items worshipped in idolatrous practice are disqualified for use in the Temple applies only to offerings, and not to the items used to facilitate the bringing of offerings.

Rava argues that it is indeed forbidden, via a *kal vachomer* from *esnan zonah* — the payment given to a prostitute. The Torah prohibits bringing an *esnan zonah* or *mechir kelev* to the house of God (*lo tavi esnan zonah u'mechir kelev beit Hashem Elokecha*). Rashi notes that *beit* here extends the prohibition beyond sacrificial use to any temple purpose. Crucially, the *esnan* is never forbidden for ordinary lay use — the prostitute's client can still benefit from it in normal ways. Yet it is forbidden for the temple. And this is true whether the *esnan* was something detached or something attached to the ground — the verse does not distinguish, and Rashi reads *beit* as including even a house that was given to a prostitute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֶתְנַן שֶׁמּוּתָּר בְּתָלוּשׁ לְהֶדְיוֹט, אָסוּר בִּמְחוּבָּר לְגָבוֹהַּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תָבִיא אֶתְנַן זוֹנָה וּמְחִיר כֶּלֶב״, לָא שְׁנָא תָּלוּשׁ וְלָא שְׁנָא בִּמְחוּבָּר, נֶעֱבָד שֶׁאָסוּר בְּתָלוּשׁ לְהֶדְיוֹט — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאָסוּר בִּמְחוּבָּר לְגָבוֹהַּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The dilemma can be resolved with **an *a fortiori*** inference. The Torah prohibits the sacrifice of an animal as an offering or a donation to the Temple if it was given in payment to a prostitute or exchanged in the sale of a dog. **And if** it is so that concerning deriving benefit from an item that served as **payment** to a prostitute, **which is permitted to an ordinary** person even **in** a case where the item is **detached** from the ground, nevertheless, such an item is **forbidden** to use **for** the **Most High** even **in** a case where it is **attached** to the ground, **as it is written: "You shall not bring the payment to a prostitute or the price of a dog** into the House of the Lord your God for any vow" (Deuteronomy 23:19), where there **is no difference whether** the payment is **unattached** to the ground **and** there **is no difference whether it is attached;** concerning an object **worshipped** in idolatrous practice, **which** is rendered **forbidden in** a case where it is **detached** even **for** the use of **an ordinary** person, **is it not logical that** it should be **prohibited** to use it **for** the **Most High** even **in** the case of an object **attached** to the ground?

Now, something that was actually worshipped as *avodah zarah* is clearly a graver matter than a payment to a prostitute. If even the *esnan*, which is never forbidden to lay people, is forbidden in its *mechubar* form for temple use — how much more so something worshipped, which *is* forbidden to lay people when detached (*assur b'talush l'hedyot*). The *kal vachomer* concludes: something worshipped in its *mechubar* form is certainly forbidden for temple use as well.

The verse confirming that a worshipped mountain is permissible for lay use — *elokeihem al he'harim ve'lo he'harim elokeihem*, "their gods are upon the mountains, but the mountains are not their gods" — establishes the lay permissibility, but Rava's *kal vachomer* pushes toward forbidding temple use.

### Direction Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרָבָא: אוֹ חִילּוּף, וּמָה נֶעֱבָד שֶׁאָסוּר בְּתָלוּשׁ אֵצֶל הֶדְיוֹט — מוּתָּר בִּמְחוּבָּר לְגָבוֹהַּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עַל הֶהָרִים״ וְלֹא הֶהָרִים אֱלֹהֵיהֶם, לָא שְׁנָא לְהֶדְיוֹט וְלָא שְׁנָא לְגָבוֹהַּ; אֶתְנַן שֶׁמּוּתָּר בְּתָלוּשׁ לְהֶדְיוֹט — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמּוּתָּר בִּמְחוּבָּר לְגָבוֹהַּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rava: Or** one can **reverse** the claim. **And if** an object that **was worshipped, which is forbidden in** a case where it is **detached** from the ground even **for** the use of **an ordinary** person, is yet **permitted** for use even **for** the **Most High in** a case where it is **attached** to the ground, **as it is stated** that the mitzva is to destroy: **"Their gods, upon the** high **mountains"** (Deuteronomy 12:2), **but not the mountains** themselves that are **their gods,** and therefore with regard to such items, there **is no difference** between use **for an ordinary** person **and for** the **Most High;** concerning **payment** to a prostitute, **which is permitted** for use **for an ordinary** person **in** the case of **a detached** item, **is it not logical that** it should be **permitted** to use **for** the **Most High in** a case where the payment is an item **attached** to the ground?

Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua challenges Rava sharply. He accepts the logical structure entirely but points out that the *kal vachomer* can be run in the opposite direction. Start instead with the assumption that something worshipped in its *mechubar* form is permissible even for temple use — *elokeihem al he'harim ve'lo he'harim elokeihem* implies that worshipping a mountain is a meaningless act that changes nothing about the mountain's status. If that is true, and the lighter case (something worshipped) is permissible for the temple even when *mechubar*, then certainly the still lighter case of an *esnan* — which was never even an act of worship — should be permissible for the temple when *mechubar*. The logic runs equally well in both directions.

One might think that the phrase *beit Hashem Elokecha* in the *esnan* verse settles the matter, proving the *esnan* is forbidden for all temple purposes even when *mechubar*. But Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua argues that this phrase teaches something else entirely — as a *baraita* cited by Rabbi Elazar has it: *prat le'parah she'eina ba'ah la'bayit*, the phrase comes to exclude the *parah adumah*, which is not brought into the *Beis HaMikdash* at all, from the *esnan* prohibition. Accordingly, a *parah adumah* that was given to a prostitute as payment may still be used as a *parah adumah*. The *chachamim* dispute this, reading *beit Hashem* as extending the prohibition even to things like decorative gold plating (*rikui'in*). But on either reading, the verse does not necessarily establish that *mechubar* objects given as *esnan* are forbidden. So how does Rava know to assume that the *esnan* is forbidden in its *mechubar* form and apply that assumption to the *kal vachomer* toward stringency?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִשּׁוּם ״בֵּית ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״בֵּית ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ — פְּרָט לְפָרָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בָּאָה לַבַּיִת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָרִיקּוּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, continues: **And if** one would say that it should still be prohibited to use it in the Temple **due to** that which the verse states: "You shall not bring the payment to a prostitute, or the price of a dog, into **the House of the Lord your God,"** which indicates a blanket prohibition, this verse is already **necessary to** teach another *halakha*, namely, **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase "into **the House of the Lord your God" excludes** its use as a red **heifer, which is not brought to the Temple,** rather it is sacrificed outside the city on the Mount of Olives; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. And the Rabbis say:** It is stated **to include the** prohibition against hanging in the Temple **beaten plates** of gold that were used in the payment of a prostitute. Even though they are not used in the Temple service, it is prohibited to hang them up.

### Strict Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא קָאָמֵינָא לְחוּמְרָא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לְקוּלָּא — קוּלָּא וְחוּמְרָא לְחוּמְרָא פָּרְכִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: **I am stating** an *a fortiori* inference that leads **to a stringency, and you are stating** an opposite *a fortiori* inference that leads **to a leniency.** And the principle is that whenever there is the option to employ an *a fortiori* inference that leads to **a leniency or** one that leads to **a stringency, we infer the stringency.**

Rava responds: *ana kamina le'chumra ve'at amarta le'kula* — I am ruling strictly, and you are ruling leniently. When a *kal vachomer* can be constructed in either direction and we do not know which halakhic assumption to begin with, the hermeneutic principle is to run it in the strict direction. *Le'chumra parchinan* — we make the *kal vachomer* toward stringency. Rashi notes that *parchinan* here — "we ask" — reflects the rhetorical nature of the *kal vachomer* conclusion, which always ends with a question of the form "is it not certainly so?"

### Pesach Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא קוּלָּא וְחוּמְרָא, לְקוּלָּא לָא פָּרְכִינַן? וְהָא הַזָּאָה דְּפֶסַח, דִּפְלִיגִי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לְחוּמְרָא וְקָא מְחַיֵּיב לֵיהּ לְגַבְרָא, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְקוּלָּא וּפָטַר, וְקָא פָרֵיךְ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְקוּלָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava: And is** it so that **anywhere that there is** an option to employ an *a fortiori* inference to infer **a leniency or a stringency, we do not infer the leniency? But isn't there** the case of **sprinkling** the purifying water of a red heifer upon one who has contracted ritual impurity through contact with a corpse, in order to thereby obligate him in the sacrificing and eating **of** the **Paschal offering? As Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva disagree** with regard to whether it is permitted to do this on Shabbat, each of them employing an *a fortiori* inference to support his opinion. **As Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **a stringency** should be inferred **and he** thereby **deems the man obligated** to purify himself and bring a Paschal offering, even if he can purify himself only on Shabbat, **and Rabbi Akiva** infers **a leniency and exempts** him from the obligation to bring the offering. **And** clearly, contrary to Rava's claim, **Rabbi Akiva infers a leniency.**

Rav Pappa challenges this principle. Is it truly the case that whenever a *kal vachomer* can go either way, we always apply it strictly? He cites the famous dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva regarding *haza'ah* on *erev Pesach* that falls on *Shabbat*. Someone who became *tamei mes* has their seventh day of purification fall on *erev Pesach*, which is a *Shabbat*. Normally, sprinkling the ashes of the *parah adumah* is rabbinically prohibited on *Shabbat* as a *shevut*. The question: does the need to become *tahor* in time to bring the *korban Pesach* — which does override *Shabbat* — also override the rabbinic prohibition against sprinkling?

Rabbi Eliezer holds strictly: the sprinkling should be done, and failure to do so makes one liable for not having brought the *korban Pesach*. His *kal vachomer*: the *shechita* of the *korban Pesach* itself overrides *Shabbat* at the Torah level; certainly the sprinkling — which is only a rabbinic restriction — should override *Shabbat* as well. Rabbi Akiva goes leniently and exempts the person from liability. His rhetorical counter-argument: sprinkling, which is only a *shevut*, does not override *Shabbat*; how much more so should *shechita*, which is a Torah-level act, not override *Shabbat*! — But of course, everyone agrees that *shechita* of the *korban Pesach* does override *Shabbat*. The argument is patently absurd.

### Eliezer's Memory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, הֵשִׁיב רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אוֹ חִילּוּף, וּמָה הַזָּאָה שֶׁהִיא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת — אֵינָהּ דּוֹחָה [אֶת] הַשַּׁבָּת, שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁהִיא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (see *Pesaḥim* 65b) that Rabbi Eliezer reasoned: If slaughter, which is generally prohibited on Shabbat by Torah law, is permitted for the sake of sacrificing the Paschal offering, all the more so is it not clear that sprinkling the purifying water of a red heifer, which is prohibited on Shabbat merely due to a rabbinic decree, should override Shabbat? **Rabbi Akiva replied: Or** one can **reverse** the claim. **And if sprinkling** the purifying water on Shabbat, **which is** prohibited merely **due to rabbinic decree, does not override Shabbat,** then with regard to **slaughter, which is** prohibited **by Torah law, all the more so** is it **not** clear that it should not override Shabbat? Contrary to Rava's claim, Rabbi Akiva clearly infers a leniency instead of the stringency inferred by Rabbi Eliezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר גַּמְרֵיהּ, וְאִיַּיקַּר לֵיהּ תַּלְמוּדָא, וַאֲתָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְאַדְכּוֹרֵיהּ. וְהַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, אַל תַּכְפִּירֵנִי בִּשְׁעַת הַדִּין, כָּךְ מְקוּבָּל אֲנִי מִמְּךָ — הַזָּאָה שְׁבוּת, וְאֵינָהּ דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** Rabbi Akiva does not employ a real *a fortiori* inference. **Rabbi Eliezer** himself **taught him** this *halakha*, which he knew as a tradition, **but he had forgotten his** own **learning and Rabbi Akiva came to remind him** of it by drawing an *a fortiori* inference that would cause Rabbi Eliezer to remember that which he himself had taught. The Gemara offers a support for this interpretation from the continuation of that discussion, which is recorded in a *baraita*: **And this is** what Rabbi Akiva **said to him: My teacher, do not deny my** contention **at the time of judgement,** i.e., during deliberation of this matter, as **this** is the tradition **I received from you: Sprinkling is** prohibited by **rabbinic decree and does not override Shabbat.**

The *gemara* explains the strange exchange. *Hasam Rabbi Eliezer gemara gamrei* — Rabbi Eliezer had actually learned the *halacha* in tradition and had taught it: sprinkling does not override *Shabbat* even to enable the *korban Pesach*. But he had grown old and his learning had become heavy for him — *ve'yakar lei talmuda* — he had forgotten it. Rabbi Akiva, his student, was trying to remind him without saying so directly. By making the obviously absurd argument that *shechita* should not override *Shabbat*, he was signaling: do you see? If even *shechita* — which patently does override *Shabbat* — were to follow your logic and not override *Shabbat*, you would conclude absurdity. Your own tradition says sprinkling does not override *Shabbat*. In the end Rabbi Eliezer did not catch the hint, and Rabbi Akiva was forced to say it explicitly: *al tachpireni b'shat ha'din — kach mekubalani mimcha*, "do not embarrass me in argument — this is the very tradition I received from you."

The point the *gemara* draws from this: Rabbi Akiva's argument here was not a genuine *kal vachomer* meant to go leniently. It was a rhetorical device to jog his teacher's memory, as anyone could see it was absurd. The principle therefore stands: *le'chumra parchinan* — when the direction of a *kal vachomer* is genuinely undetermined, one goes strictly.

## Transformation

### Grain Milling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְקָמַת חִטִּים, מַהוּ לִמְנָחוֹת? יֵשׁ שִׁינּוּי בְּנֶעֱבָד, אוֹ אֵין שִׁינּוּי בְּנֶעֱבָד?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** In the case of **one who bows to a stalk of wheat, what is** the *halakha* with regard to using it **for meal-offerings?** Does the wheat lose its forbidden status after it is ground into flour, or not? **Does a change in** the form of **a worshipped** object revoke its forbidden status, **or does a change in** the form of **a worshipped** object **not** revoke its forbidden status?

Rami bar Chama poses a follow-up question. Having established (at least tentatively) that worshipping grain while it is still attached to the ground renders it forbidden for the altar, the question now is: *hamishtachaveh le'kamat chitin mahu le'minachot* — what is the law if one took that worshipped grain, milled it, and ground it into flour? Can that flour be used for a *minchah* offering?

### Shinui Question

*Yesh shinui bene'evad* — does transformation change the status of a worshipped object for purposes of the altar? *O ein shinui bene'evad* — or does it make no difference? Rashi and Tosafot both observe carefully that this question applies specifically and only to the altar-related prohibition. If grain had been detached before being worshipped, it would be forbidden for ordinary use entirely — and in that case no *shinui* would help, just as pulverizing forbidden foods does not render them permitted. But here, the grain was worshipped while still *mechubar* to the ground, was never forbidden for ordinary use, and acquired only a potential restriction *vis-à-vis* the altar. The question is whether the transformation from grain to flour is sufficient to sever that restricted status. The *gemara* will continue this discussion in the next session.
