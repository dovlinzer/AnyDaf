# Kiddushin 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava's Three Cases

### Problem Setup

The sugya opens at the very bottom of daf 6b, introducing three remarkable cases articulated by Rava involving unusual mechanisms for effecting *kiddushei kesef*. In a standard case of *kiddushin*, the man gives something of value to the woman — that object or sum of money constitutes the *kesef kiddushin* that effects the formal transactional change of status we call *kiddushin*. It is not a purchase in the simple sense, but there is nonetheless a formalized process that introduces both the prohibitive dimensions of marriage and its attendant obligations. The *sugya* here is concerned specifically with *kiddushei kesef* — and the cases Rava presents are, to put it plainly, strange.

To follow the cases clearly, three parties must be kept in mind: the *isha* (the woman), the *ba'al* (the prospective husband), and *Ploni* (a third party).

### Din Arev

The first case: *ten maneh l'Ploni ve-ekadesh lach, mekudeshet mi-din arev*. The man says to the woman — or, grammatically more precisely, she says to the man — "Give a *maneh* to Ploni, and I will be betrothed to you." The direction of the money is striking: it flows from the husband to a third party. The woman receives nothing; the husband does not give anything to his prospective wife. Yet Rava rules she is *mekudeshet*.

The Gemara explains via the analogy of an *arev* — a guarantor or co-signer on a loan. When a borrower seeks funds and the lender requires added security, the *arev* steps in to obligate himself to repay in the event of default, despite receiving no benefit whatsoever from the transaction. The Gemara applies this logic: *arev, af al gav d'lo mati hana'ah l'yadeih, kamshabd nafsheih* — the guarantor, even though no benefit reaches him, nonetheless obligates himself. So too the woman: even though no money reaches her, she obligates herself and effects the transfer of her person into the marital relationship.

### Arev Analysis

There is a major *machloket rishonim* about precisely how this case works, and indeed the underlying question touches on the most fundamental issues of what *kiddushei kesef* requires.

Rashi appears to take the position that each of these archetypical transaction-types — *arev*, *eved Kena'ani*, and the combined case — functions as a recognized transactional form, and because it works in those contexts, it can work for *kiddushin* as well. The later *rishonim* find this difficult.

The Rosh takes the view that what makes this good *kiddushei kesef* is simply the fact that money was transferred — regardless of direction or party. A formal act of monetary transfer occurred, and that transfer is what *kiddushin* attaches to. On this reading, *kiddushin* is not about the man purchasing the woman, but about a formal transactional event that gives legal content to the marital intention.

The Rambam's approach, which is perhaps the most conceptually coherent, is that the woman receives a psychological *hana'ah* — a benefit. Her agreement to do what the husband requests in a financial matter, or more precisely, the benefit she derives from the fact that he acted on her financial direction, constitutes *hana'ah* worth at least a *perutah*. This principle — that intangible psychological value can constitute *kesef kiddushin* — recurs later in the *masechta* in cases such as "do a dance for me and you will be *mekudeshet*." The difficulty with the Rambam's approach, as noted, is that none of this is articulated in the Gemara's formulation of the case itself.

Rashi and several other *rishonim* respond that for this mechanism to work, it must be articulated explicitly — otherwise, the woman handing money to a stranger is simply disconnected from *kiddushin* entirely. There is no inherent link unless something is stated to make the connection.

The Ritva stakes out a middle position: there is a monetary dimension in the sense that money was transferred, but some part of the mechanism is still anchored in the psychological value as well. The cases are genuinely difficult, and that difficulty is precisely the point — these are strange cases that push at the edges of what *kiddushei kesef* can mean.

One further clarification that emerges from discussion: in the first case, who actually gives the money? The text is ambiguous, and whether it is her money or his money being given to Ploni is itself part of the *machloket rishonim*. The textual formulation may be read either way. What is clear is that the woman is the one making the request — she says, in effect, give money to Ploni and I will be your wife — and the theoretical underpinning is the *arev* principle: a person can obligate themselves in a transaction from which they derive no direct benefit.

### Din Eved Kena'ani

The second case: *heilach maneh vehitkadshi l'Ploni, mekudeshet mi-din eved Kena'ani*. Here the directions are reversed. A third party gives the woman money, and the man — the *Ploni* here — marries her as a result, even though he has not spent a single coin. The Gemara grounds this in the law of the Canaanite slave.

When a slave seeks to purchase his own freedom, the *halacha* presents a structural problem: everything a slave owns belongs in principle to his master. Any money the slave accumulates is already the master's property. Therefore, the slave cannot technically purchase his own freedom using money in his possession. Instead, a third party must provide the purchase price, paying the master directly. Yet the transaction is the slave's — it is his freedom that is acquired, his status that changes, and the conceptual framework remains one of him acquiring himself.

*Eved Kena'ani yochiach*: the slave proves that a person can effect a change in his own status through a transaction in which he himself loses nothing — because someone else bears the financial cost. Applied to *kiddushin*: the husband does not lose money, someone else provides the funds to the woman, and yet the marriage is effected. It is a status change, not a purchase, and the status change attaches to the husband even without any financial outlay on his part.

### Din Shneihem

The third case combines both problems simultaneously: *ten maneh l'Ploni vehitkadshi ani lo, mekudeshet mi-din sheneihem*. The woman tells the husband, "Give money to Ploni, and I will be married to him." The husband gives money to a third party, and the woman becomes betrothed to that third party. Neither problem in isolation is sufficient to invalidate — the woman gets no money, and the prospective husband Ploni loses nothing — and Rava therefore invokes both principles together to validate the transaction.

The Gemara walks through the circular logic explicitly: *arev* addresses the woman's situation (she obligates herself despite receiving no *hana'ah*); *eved Kena'ani* addresses Ploni's situation (he acquires a wife without losing money). Each principle on its own would face the objection that the other half doesn't match — but together, the two principles cover all the anomalies of the transaction and the *kiddushin* is valid.

## Fourth Case & Agav

### Woman Gives to Husband

Rava then poses a question — *ba'ei Rava* — which is perhaps the most striking of all: *heilach maneh ve-ekadesh ani lach, mahu?* What is the rule when the woman gives money directly to the man and says, "I will be betrothed to you"? This is the case that Abaye had in mind when questioning the formulation of *hikadesh otach* — the language of marriage being initiated by the woman rather than the man. The Gemara takes it up as a genuine question rather than disposing of it categorically.

Mar Zutra, citing Rav Pappa, rules: *mekudeshet* — the marriage is valid.

### Agav Problem

Rav Ashi immediately objects. If the woman's giving of money to the man can effect *kiddushin*, that implies the woman is, conceptually, transferring herself to the man *agav* the money — as an incidental acquisition bundled with the primary transfer. But the *mishnah* teaches the rule of *kinyan agav* as follows: movable property (*nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut*) can be acquired along with real estate (*nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut*), not the reverse. Land is the primary vehicle; chattels ride along for free. Here, money is movable property — and the woman would be the *karka*, real estate — so the transfer is going in the wrong direction. It would be as if one were purchasing a flock of sheep and claiming the field comes along with them, which is precisely the inverse of how *kinyan agav* works.

### Adam Chashuv

Mar Zutra deflects the objection entirely: *mi savart de-amari agav? hacha be-Adam Chashuv askinan* — who said I am invoking *kinyan agav* at all? We are speaking of a case involving an *adam chashuv*, a person of considerable social standing and reputation.

Rashi explains: an *adam chashuv* is someone who, on principle, does not accept gifts, based on the *pasuk* in Mishlei, *soneh matanot yichyeh* — the one who hates gifts will live. Refusing gifts is treated by *Chazal* as a mark of honor and is regularly invoked as a normative standard for distinguished individuals. If such a person accepts a gift, the psychological benefit to the giver is measurable and real: *de-be-hahi hana'ah de-ka mekaibel hana'ah minah, gamra u-maknya lei nafshah* — from the benefit she receives from the fact that this *adam chashuv*, who does not ordinarily accept gifts, has accepted hers, she resolves to betroth herself to him completely.

The analogy is apt: if one were to pay for dinner in the company of a distinguished personage who normally declines others' hospitality, the very fact of that acceptance confers a palpable benefit on the one who treated. That benefit is worth at least a *perutah*, and it is this psychological *hana'ah* — not any principle of *kinyan agav* — that provides the mechanism for the *kiddushin*.

This parallels the Rambam's reading of the first case, where the woman's benefit from being heeded in a financial matter constitutes the *hana'ah* that makes the *kiddushei kesef* functional. The difference is that in the *adam chashuv* case, the Gemara makes this explicit — unlike the first case, where the Rambam reads it in by inference.

In practice, the *adam chashuv* principle is extremely narrow. It applies only where the recipient is genuinely known to refuse gifts, and where the psychological benefit to the giver is therefore clear and recognized. The difficulty of assessing such benefit in any given case is considerable, which is part of why the reverse ring ceremony, while theoretically available in this framework, is not practically employed.

## Application to Mamona

### Kiddushin vs Mamona

After teaching the three cases of *arev*, *eved Kena'ani*, and *din sheneihem*, Rava adds: *ve-chen l'inyan mamona* — and the same principles apply in monetary transactions. That is, if one wishes to use any of these unusual mechanisms to effect a *kinyan kesef* in a standard financial transaction — purchasing a field, for example, which requires only a *perutah* of *kesef* — those mechanisms work there too.

One might wonder why this needs to be stated. The Gemara explains: *tzricha*.

### Mamona Same Rules

Had Rava taught only the *kiddushin* cases, one might have assumed the permissibility was limited to *kiddushin* because the threshold for *kesef kiddushin* is extremely low — a single *perutah* — and moreover because of the well-known presumption of Reish Lakish: *tav le-meitav tadu mi-le-meitav armelu* — it is better to dwell in partnership than to dwell alone, meaning women generally prefer a state of marriage. Given that preference, one might assume the *kiddushin* threshold is especially easy to meet on account of the woman's own inclination. Hence we might have thought these mechanisms work only for *kiddushin* and not for ordinary monetary transactions. Rava therefore teaches: no, they work for monetary transactions as well.

### No Mechila in Kiddushin

Conversely, had Rava taught only the monetary cases, one might have assumed the permissibility there flows from the fact that in monetary contexts, there is room for *mechila* — waiver. A creditor can simply forgive a debt: *arba me'ot shekel kesef beini u-veinecha mah hi* — four hundred shekels of silver between you and me, consider it a gift. In financial matters, if the parties are satisfied, the formal adequacy of the payment mechanism matters less.

But *kiddushin* has no room for *mechila*. The formal transactional event is constitutive of the *kiddushin* itself; without it, there is no marriage regardless of mutual consent. A man and a woman who simply agree to be married, without any *kinyan*, are not *mekudeshet*. Therefore, one might have thought these unusual mechanisms — good enough for *mamona* where *mechila* smooths over gaps — would not work for the more demanding formal requirements of *kiddushin*. Rava therefore teaches both: the mechanisms are valid for both *kiddushin* and *mamona*, and each teaching is necessary because neither can be derived from the other.

## Half-Marriage Cases

### Half Marriage

Rava then turns to a different class of problems: *hitkadshi li l'chetzi* — "be betrothed to half of me." Can a man offer to marry a woman only partially? Rava rules: *mekudeshet* — she is betrothed. The marriage is valid.

### Why Half Fails

Abaye challenges: if it is valid for a man to offer half of himself in marriage, why should the inverse case — *chetziyech mekudeshet li*, "half of you be betrothed to me" — be invalid? The verse says *ki yikach ish isha*, implying a whole woman must be taken. But it also says *ish* — implying, perhaps, a whole man must be the one taking. The same logic that invalidates half-*kiddushin* of the woman should apply to the man.

Rava responds: do not invoke *pesukim* at me; I am speaking of *svarot* — substantive logical reasoning. *Ititah le-vei terei lo chazya* — a woman cannot be married to two men simultaneously. Therefore, when a man says "half of you be betrothed to me," the implicit consequence is that the other half remains available for a second man to marry — which is forbidden. The statement is thus structurally self-defeating: it presupposes an arrangement that cannot legally exist.

But a man in a marriage? *Gavra mi lo chazi le-vei terei?* — can a man not be married to two women? In the period of the Gemara, polygamy, while not normative, was technically permitted. When a man says "half of me be betrothed to you," what he is expressing — using unconventional language — is that he reserves the right within this marriage to take a second wife. That is halachically permissible, and therefore the statement does not self-destruct in the same way. The *kiddushin* is valid.

It is worth pausing to note the interesting implication: the man here is entering marriage on the explicit understanding that he may take a second wife. One might consider the extent to which, in entering a polygamous arrangement, a man must stipulate this at the outset or whether it is a reserved right independent of the *kiddushin* formulation. The Gemara does not pursue that question, but the structure of the case raises it naturally.

### Hekdesh Analogy

Mar Zutra bar Rav Mari then poses a challenge to Ravina: even if we accept that *chetziyech mekudeshet li* fails, why should the *kiddushin* not spread through her entire person? This is an importation from the laws of *hekdesh*: if a person consecrates part of an animal for the altar, the sanctity can spread (*lifshot*) through the entire animal. Does the *kiddushin* not spread similarly?

The Gemara invokes a specific teaching on the matter: *ha-omer regla shel zo olah, tehei kulah olah* — if one says "the leg of this animal is an *olah*," the entire animal becomes an *olah*. Even according to the opinion in Masechet Temurah that such spreading does not always work, that restrictive view applies only where one consecrated a part from which the animal could survive — a leg, for instance. An animal can live without a leg. But if one consecrates something that the animal's life depends on — its heart, its brain — then the entire life of the animal is encompassed, and the *hekdesh* spreads throughout. By analogy, if one says "half of you" in a way that divides the woman down the middle — a division incompatible with survival — should not the *kiddushin* spread through all of her?

### Objects vs Persons

The Gemara's answer marks a fundamental distinction: *mi dami? hasam behemah, hacha da'at acheret* — can these cases be compared? There, it is an animal; here, there is *da'at acheret* — another consciousness, another will.

When an animal is consecrated for the altar, it is designated — it does not consent. An animal has no *da'at* to resist or limit the effect of *hekdesh*. The sanctity spreads because the animal is a passive object of the transaction.

Marriage is different in the most fundamental way: the woman's *da'at* is constitutive of the *kiddushin*. We have seen already in the *masechta* that at the moment of *kiddushin*, the husband articulates a verbal declaration of intent, and the woman's acceptance is her consent to that specific declaration. If the man says *chetziyech mekudeshet li* — "half of you be betrothed to me" — her acceptance is an acceptance of precisely that formulation. She may be agreeing only to what was said; her *da'at* does not expand beyond the terms she was offered. Unlike an animal, which has no consciousness to resist or delimit the spread of holiness, the woman's active *da'at* means the *kiddushin* takes effect only to the extent she has consented — and "half of you" is what she agreed to.

The emphasis here on the woman's *da'at* as a limiting and constitutive factor in *kiddushin* is striking. Even in a case where she seems broadly willing, her agreement is agreement to a particular articulation, and *kiddushin* cannot spread beyond what that articulation encompasses.

Before leaving this section, one important dispute between Rashi and Tosafot should be noted. The *hava amina* earlier — that the woman giving money to the man might operate via *kinyan agav* — raised the question of whether a human being can be analogized to land for transactional purposes. Rashi holds that human beings are in a certain general sense comparable to *karka* in transactional contexts. Tosafot strongly disagrees: the comparison to real estate applies to Canaanite slaves, who are explicitly equated to land by a *pasuk*, but does not extend to free persons. The *Shach* in Shulchan Aruch marshals extensive proofs against Tosafot's position — as is characteristic of his method when he is firmly convinced of one view. The underlying question — whether the *hekesh* between slaves and land extends conceptually to free persons — is a significant one and worth careful consideration.

The Gemara then draws additional lessons from a case involving an animal owned by two partners, one of whom consecrates his half, then purchases the other half and consecrates that as well. Such an animal is *kadosh* but cannot be brought as a sacrifice — *eina kreiva* — because it is, in conceptual terms, two half-animals joined together rather than one whole animal. Yet it has sufficient *kedushah* to effect *temurah*, the exchange of sanctity to another animal — which requires *kdushat haguf*. And the *temurah* itself is likewise *eina kreiva*.

From this case the Gemara derives three lessons — *shma mina tlat*, as is the Talmudic convention: that living animals can be subjected to *dichui* (disqualification that persists even after the disqualifying condition resolves); that *dichui me-ikara havei dichui* — even initial-state disqualification counts; and that *yesh dichui be-damim* — *dichui* applies even in cases of *kdushat damim*, value-based sanctification, not only *kdushat haguf*.

## Teiku Cases

### Half-Perutah

Rava then poses a series of progressively more strained cases, all left as *teiku* — unresolved. The general principle is that most *rishonim*, including the Rambam, rule *lechumra* in cases of *teiku* in *kiddushin*, though of course the precise application depends on how a given Gemara is read.

The first case: *chetzyeich be-chatzi perutah ve-chetzyeich be-chatzi perutah* — "half of you with half a *perutah* and half of you with another half a *perutah*." Can two statements of *chatzi pruta* combine to constitute the minimum value? *Keivan de-amar lah chatzi perutah, chatzi paska* — by saying "half a *perutah*," he has articulated an incomplete transaction, and the two halves may simply be two separate invalid acts. Or perhaps he is *moneh ve-holeich* — building up his count incrementally, in which case the transaction adds up. *Teiku*.

If one admits that *moneh ve-holeich* applies there, a more acute case follows: *chetzyeich be-pruta ve-chetzyeich be-pruta* — "half of you with one *perutah* and the other half with another *perutah*." Now each half is stated with a full *perutah* — a significant and discrete amount. Does stating a full *perutah* indicate a self-contained transaction, thus splitting the two statements into two independently invalid acts? Or does the same *moneh ve-holeich* principle apply — he is simply counting out the *kiddushin* in pieces? *Teiku*.

And if that case admits of combination: *chetzyeich be-pruta hayom ve-chetzyeich be-pruta le-machar* — "half of you today with a *perutah* and half tomorrow with a *perutah*." At this point the absurdity is evident; it seems nearly certain that this cannot work. *Keivan de-amar lah le-machar, paska* — the word "tomorrow" already severs the transaction entirely. Or perhaps, the Gemara entertains, what he is expressing is that the *kiddushin* begins now but is only completed tomorrow — conceptually drawing out what should be an instantaneous transactional moment across two days. It is worth noting that the time-scale here is days rather than minutes and hours, which themselves are known concepts — the coarse granularity is itself interesting to observe. *Teiku*.

Finally: *shenei chetzyeich be-pruta* — "your two halves be betrothed to me with this one *perutah*." This is a single statement, made at one moment, referring to the complete person by saying "both halves." Here the Gemara asks whether this single unified statement can save the transaction — *hacha vadai be-chad zimna ka'amar lah*, he said it at one time. Or perhaps there is a more fundamental principle: *ein ishah mitkadeshet le-chatzayin klal* — a woman simply cannot be married by halves in any form. Framing the *kiddushin* in terms of halves, even adding up to a whole in the same breath, reflects a flawed conception of what marriage is about, or signals to the woman an offer she is not able to meaningfully accept. *Teiku*.

### Two-to-Two Case

Rav Pappa then raises a separate case: *shenei benotayich li-shenei banai be-perutah* — a father, acting as agent for his two sons (having been properly appointed as *shaliach*, since a father cannot unilaterally marry off a son without the son's authorization), approaches another father and says: "Your two daughters be betrothed to my two sons with a single *perutah*." Does the single *perutah* suffice for both marriages?

The question is whether we follow the people actually conducting the transaction — the two fathers — making it in some sense a single unified exchange, with the money satisfying the minimum requirement. Or do we follow the men and women who are themselves being married, meaning each pair needs a separate *perutah*, and one *perutah* divided between two marriages is insufficient? *Teiku*.

### Daughter & Cow

Rav Ashi's case pushes further into the complexity of combining different categories of transaction: *bittacha u-faratcha be-perutah* — "your daughter and your cow with a single *perutah*." Are these being split into half a *perutah* each, making the *kiddushin* invalid for lack of minimum value? Or are they fundamentally different transactions — the *kiddushin* effected by a full *perutah* and the cow acquired by *meshicha* (the appropriate *kinyan* for movable property), the two simply uttered together but legally discrete? *Teiku*.

Rav Ashi then poses the closest variant: *bittacha be-karkacha be-perutah* — "your daughter and your field with a single *perutah*." Human beings and land are, in a certain conceptual sense, closer to each other in the transactional framework than a woman and a cow are. The *hava amina* that free persons might be analogized to real estate (whatever one concludes about the Rashi-Tosafot debate) gives this case a special texture. Is the *perutah* split half and half, or does the full *perutah* go to the *kiddushin* while the field is acquired by *chazakah*? *Teiku*.

## Silk & Valuation

### Silk Case

The final section of the *daf* is introduced by a real case: *hahu gavra de-akadish be-shirai* — a man who attempted to effect *kiddushin* using a piece of silk. Silk in this period was extraordinarily valuable — the product of silkworms from China, reaching the Near East only through long and costly trade routes. The historical note is apt: Rava and Rav Yosef are late-fourth-generation Amoraim, and silk was making its appearance in the markets of Babylonia precisely in this period, making it exactly the kind of novel, expensive, and imprecisely-valued commodity whose halachic implications needed to be worked out in real time.

### Raba vs Rav Yosef

Raba says: *lo tzricha shuma* — no assessment of value is required. We know silk is certainly worth more than a *perutah*; that is sufficient for *kiddushei kesef*, and the woman's consent stands on that basis.

Rav Yosef says: *tzricha shuma* — precise assessment is required. The woman is consenting to the marriage conditional on the object being worth what she believes it is worth. If she has no way of independently verifying the value, her *da'at* — her real consent — is not fully secured.

This dispute has direct practical ramifications and underlies the requirement, well-established in later *posekim*, to use plain metal rings of determinable value for *kiddushin* — avoiding gemstones or elaborately worked pieces whose market value fluctuates widely and which a non-expert cannot independently evaluate.

### Consensus

The Gemara clarifies that there are two cases in which everyone agrees. First: if the man explicitly says *be-kulo* — "be married to me for whatever minimal value exists in this silk," indicating that only a single *perutah*'s worth is being designated as the *kesef kiddushin*. Since it is beyond question that silk is worth at least a *perutah*, everyone agrees *lo tzricha shuma* — no assessment is needed. Second: if the man explicitly stated the silk was worth 50 and it was actually not worth 50 — *ha lo shavu* — everyone agrees that the *kiddushin* is invalid. She was misled about the value, her consent was based on a false representation, and the marriage does not stand.

### Core Dispute

The actual *machloket* between Raba and Rav Yosef concerns a case where the man said the silk was worth 50 and it actually was worth 50 — *d'amar chamishin ve-shavu chamishin* — but the woman did not know this independently and had no way to verify it.

Raba rules: *lo tzricha shuma* — no assessment is required. He said it was worth 50, it was worth 50; the statement was accurate, she agreed to it, the marriage is valid.

Rav Yosef rules: *tzricha shuma* — assessment is required. *Keivan de-ittata lo bekia'ah be-shuma, lo samcha da'atah* — since a woman would not normally know how to assess the value of silk, her mind is not settled. She did not really consent in the full sense because she had no way to evaluate the claim being made. Even if it turned out to be true, the *da'at* necessary for *kiddushin* was lacking at the moment of the transaction.

### Alternative View

An alternative reading of the *sugya* — introduced as *ika de-amrei* — extends the dispute further. On this view, Raba and Rav Yosef disagree even in the case of *be-kulo*, where the man designated only a minimal *perutah*'s worth. Rav Yosef holds: *shaveh perutah harei hu ke-kesef* — an object worth a *perutah* is treated as money for purposes of *kiddushei kesef*, and therefore it must conform to the qualities of money.

### Shaveh Kesef Rules

What is the essential quality of money? *Mah kesef de-kayatz* — money is precisely and fixedly valued. A coin has a determinate worth; there is no ambiguity about how much a *perutah* is worth. Rav Yosef therefore holds: *af shaveh kesef nami de-kayatz* — when *shaveh kesef* (property used in place of money) is employed for *kiddushin*, it too must be precisely measurable in value. Silk, whose price varies and which requires expert evaluation, does not meet that standard unless it is formally assessed.

The source for this principle is a *baraita*: *mi-kesef miknato* — in the context of the *eved Ivri*, the Hebrew bondsman who wishes to buy out the remaining years of his indentured servitude, the *pasuk* establishes that the redemption must be *be-kesef* — with money. The *baraita* derives that this excludes *tvu'ah* and *keilim* — produce and implements. Not because they cannot be used in transactions generally — the *pasuk* continues, *yashiv et geulato*, which the Gemara reads as extending *shaveh kesef ke-kesef*, equating property with money for transactional purposes — but because *tvu'ah* and *keilim*, even when they clearly exceed a *perutah* in value, lack the property of *kayitz*: their value is not fixed, not precisely defined, subject to fluctuation and market variation.

The analogy to *kiddushin* is thus direct: just as the redemption of the *eved Ivri* requires *kesef de-kayatz* or *shaveh kesef de-kayatz* — either actual money or property of precisely determinable value — so too *kiddushei kesef* requires that what is given, whether money or *shaveh kesef*, have a known and fixed value at the moment of the transaction. Where value is indeterminate, the woman's *da'at* is unsettled, and the *kiddushin* cannot stand.

A final note of historical interest connects the laws of *kiddushin* to the approaching holiday: the Rama in *siman* 695 rules, strangely, that a man should not send *mishloach manot* to a widow, owing to concern about *safek kiddushin*. The *acharonim* are puzzled — for *kiddushin* to result, both the man's intent and the woman's consent are obviously required. Yet the Rama states the concern. Looking closely at this ruling in light of today's *sugya* — which has established just how expansive the mechanisms of *kiddushei kesef* can be, including psychological *hana'ah* and gifts accepted by *adam chashuv* — provides the proper context for understanding what halachic concern the Rama may have in mind.
