# Eruvin 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 2000 Amah Source

### Outside City Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ מַכִּיר אוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּקִי וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** If **one is not familiar** with a tree or any other noticeable landmark, **or if he is not an expert** in the *halakha*, unaware that residence can be established from a distance, and he said: My residence is at my current location, his presence at his current location acquires for him the right to walk two thousand cubits in each direction.

The daf begins approximately ten lines down on 51a, at the words *im eino makiro o she'eino baki* — "if he does not recognize it, or is not familiar." The context is a person who finds himself outside the city just before *Shabbos* begins, situated four thousand *amos* from home, with a tree located exactly at the midpoint. Such a person may designate that tree as the starting point of his *Shabbos*, walking two thousand *amos* from his current position to the tree, and from the tree back to his home — two thousand *amos* in each direction. However, if he does not recognize any specific tree or landmark to designate, he should at minimum begin *Shabbos* where he stands, thereby ensuring that he has at least two thousand *amos* from his current position.

### Silent Designation

Tosafot notes that when one's intention is to start *Shabbos* where he is presently standing, no formal declaration is actually required. As long as he does nothing to undermine it — that is, as long as he does not actively assert the opposite — he effectively begins *Shabbos* at his current location. This point applies even if one designates a tree or some other spot two thousand *amos* away: one who places food under the designated tree may then travel two thousand *amos* in any direction from that point.

### Where from 2000?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה הֵיכָן כְּתִיבָן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״שְׁבוּ אִישׁ תַּחְתָּיו״ — אֵלּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. ״אַל יֵצֵא אִישׁ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ״ — אֵלּוּ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a fundamental question: **These two thousand cubits, where are** they **written** in the Torah? The Gemara answers that it is **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **"Remain every man in his place"** (Exodus 16:29); **these are** the **four cubits,** which constitute the minimum Shabbat limit, e.g., for one who ventured beyond his prescribed limit. **"Let no man go out of his place"** (Exodus 16:29); **these are** the **two thousand cubits** of the Shabbat limit for one who remains in his place. Unless otherwise specified, the measure of one's place is two thousand cubits.

With the issues surrounding the designation of a tree resolved over the preceding two days of study, the *Mishna* now turns to how the two thousand *amos* are actually measured — whether in a circle, in a square, or by some other method. Before engaging that question directly, however, the *Gemara* addresses a more fundamental matter: *hani alpaim amah heichan ksivan* — where are these two thousand *amos* written? The assumption is that this figure derives from a *pasuk*, since the very concept of *eiruv techumin* and its specific parameters must have some textual basis.

### Biblical Source

The answer begins with the verse from the account of the *man* in the wilderness, where Moshe declares *shvu ish tachtav, al yetze ish mimkomo bayom hashvi'i* — "Let every person remain in his place; no one shall leave his place on the seventh day." This verse served as the basis for the restriction on travel on *Shabbos*, though its precise scope was disputed. The *Karaites* interpreted it with radical literalism, permitting no departure whatsoever. The Sages understood it more restrictively. And even among the Sages, there is a significant position — that of the Rambam — according to which the biblical prohibition applies only at twelve times the *halachic* limit set by the Rabbis, namely at twenty-four thousand *amos* or twelve *mil*, not at the single *mil* that Rabbinic law establishes. Whether the two thousand *amos* is itself biblical or merely an *asmachta* is therefore a matter of considerable weight.

### Four vs Two-Thousand

The *Gemara* derives two distinct laws from the two clauses of this verse. *Shvu ish tachtav* — sit in your place — refers to *tachtav*, literally "beneath him," which was elsewhere established to mean four *amos*: when one is outside the *techum*, he retains only four *amos* of personal space. *Al yetze ish mimkomo* — do not leave your *makom* — uses the broader term *makom*, one's "place," which is larger than the immediate ground beneath one's feet. This *makom*, the *Gemara* says, equals two thousand *amos*. But where, from this *pasuk* alone, does one arrive at the figure of two thousand? The verse does not say so explicitly.

### Daisy Chain Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָמַדְנוּ ׳מָקוֹם׳ מִ׳מָּקוֹם׳, וּ׳מָקוֹם׳ מִ׳נִּיסָה׳, וְ׳נִיסָּה׳ מִ׳נִּיסָה׳, וְ׳נִיסָּה׳ מִ׳גְּבוּל׳, וּ׳גְבוּל׳ מִ׳גְּבוּל׳, וּ׳גְבוּל׳ מִ׳חוּץ׳, וְ׳חוּץ׳ מִ׳חוּץ׳. דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמַדּוֹתֶם מִחוּץ לָעִיר אֶת פְּאַת קֵדְמָה אַלְפַּיִם בָּאַמָּה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that this is the measure of one's place? **Rav Ḥisda said: We derive** this by means of a verbal analogy between the term **place** written here: "Let no man go out of his place," and **from** the term **place** written with regard to an unwitting murderer: "Then I will appoint you a place to where he shall flee" (Exodus 21:13). This last verse mentions both place and fleeing, **and** the term **place** is derived **from** the term **fleeing. And** the term **fleeing** is derived **from** the term **fleeing,** written in a different verse with regard to the unwitting murderer: "But if the slayer shall at any time come outside the border of the city of his refuge, whither he has fled" (Numbers 35:26). **And** the term **fleeing** is derived **from** the term **border,** which appears in the same verse. **And** the term **border** is derived **from** the term **border,** as it states there: "And the avenger of blood find him outside [*miḥutz*] the borders of the city of his refuge" (Numbers 35:27). Since this verse mentions both the term border and the term outside, the term **border** is derived **from** the term **outside. And** the term **outside** is derived **from** the term **outside, as it is written** with regard to the Levite cities, which also served as cities of refuge: **"And you shall measure from outside [*miḥutz*] the city on the east side two thousand cubits,** and on the south side two thousand cubits, and on the west side two thousand cubits, and on the north side two thousand cubits" (Numbers 35:5). From this chain of identical terms, the meaning of the term place stated in connection with Shabbat is derived from the two thousand cubits mentioned with regard to the Levite cities.

Rav Chisda answers: *lamadnu makom mi-makom, u-makom mi-nisa, u-nisa mi-nisa, u-nisa mi-gevul, u-gevul mi-gevul, u-gevul mi-chutz, ve-chutz mi-chutz* — we learn *makom* from *makom*, *makom* from *nisa*, *nisa* from *nisa*, *nisa* from *gevul*, *gevul* from *gevul*, *gevul* from *chutz*, and *chutz* from *chutz*. This is a daisy chain of linked verses: verse A is connected to verse B through a shared word, B is connected to C through another shared word, and so on through the chain until one arrives at a verse that explicitly mentions two thousand *amos*.

### Chain Process

Each link in the chain is joined to the next by a verbal bridge — one word that appears in both verses. The *Gemara* itself does not quote the full chain of verses, but Rashi supplies them. The opening *pasuk* — *al yetze ish mimkomo* — contains the word *makom*. That links to a second *pasuk* containing both *makom* and *nisa* (the root of running or fleeing): *ve-samti lecha makom asher yanus shamah* — "I will set for you a place to which he shall flee" — a verse from the context of the *arei miklat*, the cities of refuge. From there, *nisa* connects to another verse also containing *nisa* and *gevul* — *mi-gevul ir miklato asher yanus* — linking the concept of fleeing to a boundary. That *gevul* links to yet another verse containing both *gevul* and *chutz*, and from there *chutz* connects to the final verse: *u-madotem mi-chutz la-ir el pe'at kedmah alpayim ba-amah* — "You shall measure outside the city to the east two thousand *amos*." This final verse speaks of the surrounding area — the *migrash* — of the cities of the Levites, which the Torah specifies as two thousand *amos* around the city.

### 2000 vs 1000

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מִ״קִּיר הָעִיר וָחוּצָה אֶלֶף אַמָּה״? דָּנִין ׳חוּץ׳ מִ׳חוּץ׳, וְאֵין דָּנִין ׳חוּץ׳ מֵ׳חוּצָה׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let us derive** instead by means of a verbal analogy between the term outside in the verse: "Outside the borders of the city of refuge," and the term outside in the verse: **"From the wall of the city outward [*vaḥutza*] a thousand cubits"** (Numbers 35:4), that the Shabbat limit measures only a thousand cubits. The Gemara answers: **One derives** the meaning of the term **outside [*ḥutz*]** by means of a verbal analogy **from** another instance of the term **outside [*ḥutz*], but one does not derive** the meaning of the term **outside from** the term **outward [*ḥutza*].**

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: the passages describing the *migrash* of the Levitic cities actually contain two different figures. One verse specifies two thousand *amos* around the city; another specifies one thousand. Since the penultimate verse in the chain — *u-matza ito goel ha-dam mi-chutz li-gevul ir miklato* — uses the word *mi-chutz*, and the two-thousand-*amah* verse says *u-madotem mi-chutz la-ir*, the words match exactly: *mi-chutz* to *mi-chutz*. The one-thousand-*amah* verse, by contrast, uses *chutza* — essentially the same concept but a different grammatical form. So the *Gemara* asks: why does one link to the verse of two thousand rather than the verse of one thousand? The words *chutz* and *chutza* are essentially synonymous, and a *gezeirah shavah* does not always require verbal precision, as is evidenced by the well-known teaching of the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ve-shav ha-kohen* and *u-va ha-kohen* — one verse says the *kohen* shall "return" and another says he shall "come," yet these semantically equivalent but verbally distinct expressions are linked together to derive laws in the laws of *tzara'at* of houses.

### Precise Matching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? הָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשָׁב הַכֹּהֵן״ ״וּבָא הַכֹּהֵן״ — זוֹ הִיא שִׁיבָה, זוֹ הִיא בִּיאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **What is** significant about **the difference** between the two terms? **Didn't the school of Rabbi Yishmael teach** a verbal analogy with regard to leprosy of houses between the verse: **"And the priest shall return [*veshav*]"** (Leviticus 14:39) and the verse: **"And the priest shall come [*uva*]"** (Leviticus 14:44), from which it is derived that **this is** the *halakha* with regard to **returning,** i.e., it is after seven days; **this is** the same *halakha* with regard to **coming;** it is after seven days. Obviously, the less pronounced difference of one letter between *ḥutz* and *ḥutza*, should not prevent the teaching of a verbal analogy.

The *Gemara* answers: *hani mili heicha de-lika midi de-dami lei* — that principle, that imprecise verbal matches are acceptable in a *gezeirah shavah*, applies only when there is no more precisely matching verse available. *Aval heicha de-ika midi de-dami lei* — but when there exists a verse whose wording is an exact match, one must use the precise match. Since *mi-chutz* appears verbatim in both the linking verse and the verse of two thousand *amos*, we learn from that verse rather than from the one-thousand-*amah* verse, which only uses the approximation *chutza*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ — מִדָּמֵי לֵיהּ יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Gemara rejects this argument: **This applies** only **when there are no terms** that are **identical to it however, where there are terms** that are **identical to it, we derive** the verbal analogy **from** terms **identical to it,** rather than from the terms that are not precisely identical.

This entire sugya uses the full technical vocabulary of *d'Oraita* derivation — the language of *gezeirah shavah*, of precision and imprecision in verbal links, of why one verse is preferred over another. That formality is striking, because the dominant position holds that *eruv techumin* is entirely *derabbanan* and that even if it has some biblical grounding, the Rambam limits that to a distance twelve times larger than the Rabbinic one. Yet the *Gemara* uses this language even for matters that are merely *asmachta*, as one sees in the parallel daisy chain at the beginning of *Pesachim* — where the *Gemara* similarly derives, through a chain of linked verses, that *bedikat chametz* is best performed by candlelight, even though the entire institution of *bedikat chametz* and the use of a candle are plainly *derabbanan*. The short conclusion is that the two thousand *amos* is modeled on the *migrash* — the surrounding field — of the Levitic cities, and so the area around one's starting point for *Shabbos* is similarly two thousand *amos*.

## Circle vs Square

### Mishnah Positions

With the source of the two-thousand-*amah* figure established, the *Gemara* turns to the question of how those two thousand *amos* are measured. The *Mishna* on 49b records two positions. The first is that of Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos: *alpayim amah le-chol ruach agulot* — two thousand *amos* in every direction, measured as circles. The second is the position of the Sages: *meruba'ot ke-tavla meruba'at* — square, like a square tablet, *kedei she-hein nischar la-zaviyot* — so that one gains the corners.

### Circle Position

Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos holds that the measurement is circular: a radius of two thousand *amos* in every direction, with the individual at the center.

### Square Position

The Sages hold that one draws a square of two thousand *amos* to a side. The phrase *ke-tavla meruba'at* — "like a square tablet" — refers to the flat writing tablets of the time. The practical implication is significant: a square provides more total space than a circle of the same radius, precisely because the corners of the square extend beyond the circle. If one's two thousand *amos* reaches the side of the square, walking at a diagonal toward a corner covers a greater absolute distance.

### Chanina's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה עֲגוּלּוֹת. וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי אִית לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה — פֵּיאוֹת כְּתִיבָן, אִי לֵית לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה — אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna'im* of the mishna disagree whether the **two-thousand-cubit** limit granted to a person in every direction is measured as a **circle** or as a square tablet. The Gemara poses a question: With regard to the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigenos** that the limit is measured as a circle, **no matter what** you say, it is difficult. **If he is of the** opinion that there is a **verbal analogy** from the verse written with regard to the Levite cities it is difficult, because **sides** is the term **written,** indicating squared boundaries. And **if he is not of the** opinion that there is a **verbal analogy, from where does he** derive that the Shabbat limit is **two thousand cubits?**

The *Gemara* asks: what is Rabbi Chanina's reasoning? His position actually requires explanation, because the natural inference from the very source that establishes the two-thousand-*amah* figure — the *migrash* of the Levitic cities — seems to support a square, not a circle. The verse about the Levitic cities uses the word *pe'ah*, referring to directions or corners — "toward the eastern *pe'ah* two thousand *amos*" — suggesting that the Torah itself conceptualizes the surrounding area in terms of corners, hence a square. So the *Gemara* frames the problem precisely: if Rabbi Chanina accepts the *gezeirah shavah* that links *Shabbos* to the Levitic cities, he ought to accept the square measurement implied by *pe'ah*; but if he does not accept the *gezeirah shavah*, he has no basis for the figure of two thousand *amos* to begin with.

### Corners Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אִית לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״זֶה יִהְיֶה לָהֶם מִגְרְשֵׁי הֶעָרִים״ — לָזֶה אַתָּה נוֹתֵן פֵּיאוֹת, וְאִי אַתָּה נוֹתֵן פֵּיאוֹת לְשׁוֹבְתֵי שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, he is of** the opinion that there is **a verbal analogy, but here,** with regard to the Levite cities, it **is different, as the verse says: "This shall be to them the open space of the cities"** (Numbers 35:5), from which it is inferred: **To this,** the open space of the city, **you** should **provide sides** and square it, **but you do not provide sides to those resting on Shabbat.** Instead, those who establish Shabbat residence are provided with a circular, two-thousand-cubit limit.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *le'olam is lei gezeirah shavah* — Rabbi Chanina does indeed accept the *gezeirah shavah*. But the verse about the Levitic cities concludes with *zeh yihyeh lahem migrashei he'arim* — "this shall be for them the *migrash* of their cities." Rabbi Chanina reads *zeh* — "this" — as a limiting term: *l'zeh attah noten pe'ah, ve-i attah noten pe'ah le-shovsei Shabbos* — for them, the Levites, you give corners; but you do not give corners to those observing *Shabbos* for purposes of *techumim*. The Levites get a square; for the *Shabbos* boundary, one measures only a circle.

### Sages' Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? תָּנֵי, רַב חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה יְהוּ כׇּל שׁוֹבְתֵי שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis** understand the emphasis placed on the word this in the verse? The Gemara answers: As **it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rav Ḥananya says: Like this** measure **shall be** the calculations of measures for **all those who rest on Shabbat,** i.e., square.

The Sages' rejoinder comes from a *beraita* of Rabbi Chananya — not to be confused with Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos. Rabbi Chananya reads the same verse differently: *ke-zeh yihyu kol shovsei Shabbos* — not "for this [group alone]," but "like this shall all observers of *Shabbos* be." The word *zeh* is expansive, not limiting: just as the Levites receive a square, so too does every observer of *Shabbos* receive a square. The dispute thus turns on a single word in the concluding clause of the Levitic cities passage — whether *zeh* is restrictive or inclusive — and the *Gemara* treats the interpretive choice with the full weight of a textual analysis, notwithstanding that this is almost certainly all *asmachta*.

### Compass Orientation

The practical ruling is that one draws a square of two thousand *amos* to a side. But a further question immediately presents itself: in which direction does one orient that square? The answer, which emerges here and becomes even more consequential when applied to city boundaries, is that the square is oriented to the compass points — north, south, east, and west. The sides of the square run due north-south and due east-west. One does not simply smooth out the edges of an irregular shape; rather, one finds the northernmost, southernmost, easternmost, and westernmost points of one's position and draws the square accordingly.

### Manhattan Example

The significance of compass-based orientation becomes vivid when applied to a city like Manhattan. Manhattan's landmass runs at a diagonal relative to true north. If one were to square off Manhattan according to the compass points — taking the northernmost, southernmost, easternmost, and westernmost tips and drawing a rectangle with sides parallel to the cardinal directions — the resulting square would encompass a substantial amount of territory well beyond the city's actual borders, including portions of New Jersey. This is not merely a theoretical curiosity; it directly affects the calculation of *techumim* for residents of any city whose physical layout does not align with the cardinal directions. The individual beginning *Shabbos* in such a city would, under this analysis, gain far more territory in the diagonal directions: walking due north or due south, one reaches exactly two thousand *amos*; walking northeast at a 45-degree angle, one could travel two thousand times the square root of two — approximately 2,800 *amos* — before leaving one's *techum*.

## Diagonal Measure

### Carrying Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: הַמַּעֲבִיר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁמַּעֲבִיר הֵן וַאֲלַכְסוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: One who carries** an object **four cubits in the public domain is only liable if he carries** it **four** cubits **with their diagonal.** The four cubits mentioned in many places is only the basic measure by which the distance beyond which it is prohibited to carry is calculated. However, in practice, a person is liable only if he carries the object the length of the diagonal of a square with four-cubit sides.

The *Gemara* now arrives at a striking teaching. Rav Acha bar Yaakov declares: *ha-me'avir arba amos bi-reshut ha-rabbim, eino chayav ad she-me'avir hen ve-alachsonan* — one who carries four *amos* in the public domain is not liable unless he carries not merely four *amos* but four *amos* plus the hypotenuse of a four-by-four square. That hypotenuse equals four times the square root of two — approximately 5.6 *amos*. After more than a tractate and a half of *Masechet Shabbos* and the entirety of *Eruvin* to this point, in which the figure of four *amos* for carrying in the public domain has been taken as axiomatic, the *Gemara* now appears to say that the actual threshold is 5.6 *amos*.

### Pole Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא בָּדֵיק לַן רָבָא: עַמּוּד בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וְרוֹחַב אַרְבָּעָה, צָרִיךְ הֵן וַאֲלַכְסוֹנָן אוֹ לָא? וְאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּרַב חֲנַנְיָה? דְּתַנְיָא, רַב חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה יְהוּ כׇּל שׁוֹבְתֵי שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that **Rava** once **tested us** by asking: With regard to **a pillar in the public domain, ten** handbreadths **high and four** handbreadths **wide, must** the width **be** four handbreadths **with their diagonal** in order to be regarded a private domain, **or not? And we said to him: Is this not** that which was taught by **Rav Ḥananya? As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rav Ḥananya says: Like this** measure **shall be** that of **all those who rest on Shabbat,** indicating that the diagonal is the determining measure for the *halakhot* of rest on Shabbat.

Rav Pappa reports that Rava posed a related question to the *talmidim*: if there is a pole (*amud*) in the public domain that is ten *tefachim* tall and four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* wide — which renders it a *reshut ha-yachid* — does that pole require the diagonal measurement (*hen ve-alachsonan*) or not? In other words, does the four-by-four specification refer to a circle with a diameter of four, or to a square with a side of four? This question probes whether the diagonal principle applies to the definition of *reshut ha-yachid* as well as to the act of carrying.

### Diagonal Discovery

The *Gemara* responds that this is precisely what Rabbi Chananya's *beraita* established: *ke-zeh yihyu kol shovsei Shabbos* — just as the Levitic cities are measured as a square, so too does the square measurement apply to all *Shabbos* calculations. The implication is genuinely unsettling: the *Gemara* seems to be saying that what we previously assumed to be four *amos* — whether in the context of carrying in the public domain, of the personal four *amos* when one is stuck outside the *techum*, or of the minimum dimensions of a *reshut ha-yachid* — may actually mean something larger and more complex than a simple linear measurement.

### Three Views

There are essentially three positions among the *Rishonim* on what this means, and they diverge significantly. Rashi's reading, the teaching of Rabbeinu Tam, and a second opinion in Tosafot each offer a distinct account.

### Direction Dependent

Rabbeinu Tam offers the most measured interpretation when it comes to the law of carrying. His view does not require that one always carry 5.6 *amos* before becoming liable; rather, liability depends on the direction of carrying. The conceptual premise is that carrying in the public domain is not merely the act of moving an object a set linear distance, but of removing it from its *space* — and that space is defined as a square oriented to the compass points. If one carries due north, due south, due east, or due west, the relevant distance is four *amos*, because that is how far one must go to exit the compass-aligned square. If one carries at a 45-degree angle — northeast, for instance — one must travel 5.6 *amos* before the object has left its square space, because the diagonal of a four-by-four square is four times the square root of two. This reading is coherent and elegant: it reframes "four *amos*" not as an arbitrary linear threshold but as the edge of a defined conceptual square, with the threshold varying depending on the direction of travel.

### Rashi's View

Rashi's reading is simpler and considerably less radical, particularly regarding *reshut ha-yachid*. In Rashi's understanding, the *Gemara*'s conclusion — *hen ve-alachsonan* — refers not to a new, larger measurement but to the shape of the required space. The question of whether a pole requires the diagonal is, on this reading, a question of whether the four-by-four specification means a circle with a four-*amah* diameter or a square with four-*amah* sides. The answer is: a square. This is consistent with the general principle throughout *halacha* that whenever a dimension is specified, it is understood to mean a square of that dimension, not a circle. Just as a *sechach*-opening of a *tefach* by a *tefach* for transferring *tumah* means a square *tefach*, not a circular one, so too a four-by-four *reshut ha-yachid* means a four-by-four square. On this reading, the *Gemara* announces nothing new; it simply confirms what had always been the underlying assumption.

### Reshut HaYachid

Tosafot's second opinion — not Rabbeinu Tam's — is the most radical: in every direction and regardless of orientation, the threshold is always 5.6 *amos*, because "four *amos*" has always been shorthand for the diagonal of a four-by-four square. This means that everything ever said about "four *amos*" of carrying liability in *Masechet Shabbos* was in fact referring to 5.6, and similarly that a *reshut ha-yachid* is not truly four by four but 5.6 by 5.6. Rabbeinu Tam's position on *reshut ha-yachid* is directional in a different sense: a *reshut ha-yachid* must be four by four aligned with the compass points. A four-by-four square rotated 45 degrees relative to the cardinal directions would not satisfy the requirement, because its northeast corner would be only four *amos* from its southwest corner — the diagonal being exactly what a compass-aligned four-by-four requires. One who draws a tilted square must be able to show that the compass-aligned square within or around it satisfies the four-by-four requirement.

### City Boundaries

As remarkable as these implications are for the theory of carrying and *reshut ha-yachid*, their greatest practical consequence lies in the calculation of city *techumim*. The question of how to draw the square, and that one draws it by the compass points, means that determining the *techum* of New York, for instance, produces a substantially larger area than a naive reading of "two thousand *amos* in every direction" would suggest. Drawn as a square with sides parallel to the cardinal directions, the *techum* of a diagonally oriented city — or of any starting point — extends considerably farther in the diagonal directions, and the resulting territory can overlap with regions that seem, on an ordinary map, to lie well outside any intuitive boundary.

## Eruv Debate

### Poor Person Eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה הוּא שֶׁאָמְרוּ הֶעָנִי מְעָרֵב בְּרַגְלָיו. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא עָנִי וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And this is** the meaning of that **which** the Sages **said: A pauper can establish an *eiruv* with his feet,** i.e., one who does not have the bread required to establish an *eiruv* may walk anywhere within his Shabbat limit and acquire residence. **We have** this leniency in effect **only** for **a pauper,** who does not have food for two meals. However, one who has bread may only establish residence with bread. Rabbi Yehuda says: This leniency is in effect for both a pauper and a wealthy person.

The *Gemara* returns now to the *Mishna*'s statement: *ve-zo-hi she-amru ha-ani me'arev be-raglav* — "and this is what they said: a poor person may make an *eruv* with his feet." The *Mishna* then records a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda: Rabbi Meir says *ein lanu ela ani* — we have only [this leniency for] a poor person; Rabbi Yehuda says it applies to *echad ani ve-echad ashir* — equally to a poor person and a wealthy person.

### Nachman's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַחֲלוֹקֶת ״בִּמְקוֹמִי״, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: עִיקָּר עֵירוּב בְּפַת,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** This **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is **with regard to** a case where the person said: My residence is in **my** current **location. As Rabbi Meir maintains: The primary** ordinance and establishment **of *eiruv*** is **with bread.**

Rav Nachman interprets the dispute as follows: the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerns the final case of the *Mishna* — where a person wishes to begin *Shabbos* at the very spot where he is physically standing, without bread and without having traveled to the designated location.

### Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָנִי הוּא דְּאַקִּילוּ רַבָּנַן עִילָּוֵיהּ, אֲבָל עָשִׁיר — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **it is** only **with regard to a pauper,** who does not have food for two meals, **that the Sages were lenient** and permitted him to establish residence merely by saying: My residence is in my current location. **However,** with regard to **a wealthy person** in his own house who has bread, **no,** they did not permit him to do so.

Rabbi Meir's position is that only an *ani* — a poor person — may make an *eruv* with his feet, and not an *ashir*.

### Meir's Rationale

Rabbi Meir holds *ikar eruv be-pas* — that the fundamental medium of *eruv techumin* is bread. This principle, well established for *eruv chatzeiros*, applies equally here. Consequently, for an *ashir* — one for whom bread was available — the Rabbis made no leniency; he is required to use bread. The leniency of making an *eruv* with one's feet was extended only to the *ani*, who had no bread. Here, importantly, the terms *ani* and *ashir* carry specific functional meanings: *ashir* does not refer to a person of particular wealth but to anyone for whom bread was available, and *ani* refers to the person caught outside without any bread or other options. Anyone stuck on the road with nothing on his person qualifies as an *ani* for this purpose.

### Yehuda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: עִיקַּר עֵירוּב בָּרֶגֶל, אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר. אֲבָל ״בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל: עָנֵי — אִין, עָשִׁיר — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda maintains: The primary** ordinance **of *eiruv* is by foot,** i.e., by going and stating that he is establishing his residence in that location, and therefore it applies to **both a pauper and a wealthy person. However,** with regard a case when the person said: My residence is **in such-and-such place,** and he is not there, **everyone,** both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, **agrees** that for **a pauper** on the road on a Shabbat eve, **yes, an** *eiruv* may be established in that manner; however, for **a wealthy person, no,** an eiruv may not be established in that manner.

Rabbi Yehuda holds *ikar eruv be-regel* — that the fundamental medium of *eruv* is one's physical location, where one's feet actually stand at the onset of *Shabbos*. Whether one is an *ani* or an *ashir*, any person may designate the spot where he physically stands.

### Nachman Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְזוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ״ מַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ — רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְאַהֵיָיא קָאֵי — אַ״אֵינוֹ מַכִּיר אוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּקִי בַּהֲלָכָה״. ״וְלֹא אָמְרוּ מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל״ מַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** And as for the mishna's statement: **And this is what** the Sages meant when **they said** that a pauper can establish an *eiruv* with his feet, **who,** which Sage, **is teaching it?** It is **Rabbi Meir. And to which** clause of the mishna **is it referring?** It refers to the previous statement: If he is **not familiar** with a tree or any other noticeable landmark, **or if he is not an expert in the *halakha*,** and therefore is unaware that a residence can be established from a distance, and said: My residence is in my current location, he acquires two thousand cubits in each direction. **And** as for the statement in the continuation of the mishna: The Sages **said** that **one establishes an *eiruv* with bread only to be lenient with the wealthy** person, **who,** which Sage, **is teaching it?** It is **Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that the option of establishing an *eiruv* by foot is available to the wealthy as well.

According to Rav Nachman, both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda are debating only this case — where one wishes to begin *Shabbos* at the spot where one stands. The earlier case in the *Mishna* — where one designates a distant spot such as a tree two thousand *amos* away without being physically present there and without having placed bread there — is a separate matter entirely.

### Stuck Only

That earlier case of designating a distant spot is a special provision available only to the person who is truly stuck: caught outside the city, unable to place bread at the distant location, unable to reach it before *Shabbos* begins. In that extreme situation, the Rabbis permitted a completely different mechanism. But this special leniency, according to Rav Nachman, is one on which Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda are in complete agreement — both would hold that it is limited exclusively to that person, the true *ani*, the person with no alternatives whatsoever.

### Parsing Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת, וְלֹא יֵצֵא עָשִׁיר חוּץ לַתְּחוּם וְיֹאמַר: ״שְׁבִיתָתִי בִּמְקוֹמִי״ — לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ מְעָרְבִין בָּרֶגֶל אֶלָּא לְמִי שֶׁבָּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְחָשְׁכָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: A *baraita* **was taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman,** who said that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is with regard to one who said: My residence is in my present location. It was stated in the *baraita*: **Both a pauper and a wealthy person establish an *eiruv* with bread; however a wealthy person may not go out beyond the** Shabbat **limit and say: My residence is in my** present **location, because** the Sages **said** that **one can establish an *eiruv* by foot only** in the case of **a person who was coming along the way and it grew dark** while **he** was traveling. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *Gemara* then parses the precise language of the *Mishna* to confirm Rav Nachman's reading. The *Mishna* on 49b reads: *ve-zo-hi she-amru ha-ani me'arev be-raglav* — "this is what they said: a poor person may make an *eruv* with his feet" — which is attributed to Rabbi Meir, who emphasizes that it applies specifically to the *ani*. Then the *Mishna* records Rabbi Yehuda's view: *lo amru me'arvin be-pas ela le-hakeil al ha-ashir* — "they only said [one uses] bread in order to be lenient on the *ashir*," implying that using one's feet is open to anyone. The *Gemara* asks: to which case does *ve-zo-hi she-amru* — this introductory phrase — refer? It refers to the later case: the person who does not recognize a tree (*eino makir*) or is unfamiliar with the relevant *halacha* (*eino baki ba-halacha*), and therefore wishes to begin *Shabbos* where he stands. In that case, Rabbi Meir asserts that only an *ani* may do so. And the closing statement, that bread was only established to be lenient on the *ashir*, belongs to Rabbi Yehuda — who holds that beginning *Shabbos* where one stands is the essential act and requires no bread. The first case of the *Mishna* — the designation of a distant spot — remains, on Rav Nachman's reading, undisputed territory: an exception all agree is limited to the truly stuck.

## Chisda's View

### Distant Place Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מַחֲלוֹקֶת ״בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: עָנִי — אִין, עָשִׁיר — לָא, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר. אֲבָל ״בִּמְקוֹמִי״, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר, דְּעִיקַּר עֵירוּב בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda,** however, disagreed with Rav Naḥman and **said:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda in the mishna is with regard to a person who said: My residence is **in such-and-such place,** in which case his residence is neither acquired by foot nor with bread. **As Rabbi Meir maintains: A pauper, yes,** he establishes residence with an *eiruv* in that manner; however, **a wealthy person, no,** he does not. **And Rabbi Yehuda maintains: Both a pauper and a wealthy person** may establish an *eiruv* in that manner. **However,** in a case where one said: My residence is **in my** present **location, everyone,** both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, **agrees** that an *eiruv* of this kind is effective **both** for **a pauper and** for **a wealthy person,** as everyone agrees agree that **the primary** ordinance **of *eiruv* is by foot.**

Rav Chisda reads the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda entirely differently. In his view, *machlokes bi-makom ploni* — their dispute concerns precisely the case of designating a specific distant location. It is not the case of beginning *Shabbos* where one stands that is contested; rather, the controversy is about whether one may, while still at home, verbally designate a far-off spot as one's *Shabbos* starting point.

### Yehuda Allows Rich

According to Rav Chisda, Rabbi Yehuda holds *echad ani ve-echad ashir* — that both an *ani* and an *ashir* may designate a distant spot. This is, to put it plainly, a striking position. Under this reading, Rabbi Yehuda does not limit the special provision of remote designation to the person who is genuinely stuck; he extends it even to someone sitting comfortably at home.

### Rich Person Can

The implication of Rav Chisda's interpretation is that even a wealthy person, with full access to bread and with every option available, could in theory remain seated at home on a Friday afternoon and verbally designate some distant location as his *Shabbos* starting point — thereby shifting his *techum* by two thousand *amos* without having traveled there or placed food there. This would render the entire *mitzvah* of actually going to the place or sending bread there largely superfluous.

### Contrast Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְזוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ״ מַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ — רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְאַהֵיָיא קָאֵי — אַהָא: ״מִי שֶׁבָּא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְחָשֵׁכָה״. ״וְלֹא אָמְרוּ מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל״ מַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** And as for the mishna's statement: **And this is what** the Sages meant when **they said** that **a pauper** can establish an *eiruv* by foot, **who is teaching it?** It is **Rabbi Meir. And to which** clause of the mishna **is it referring?** It is referring **to this** clause: **One who was coming along the way** on Shabbat eve, **and it grew dark** while **he** was traveling. According to Rabbi Yehuda, he could have established an *eiruv* even if he was in his house. And as for the statement in the continuation of the mishna: The Sages **said** that **one establishes an *eiruv* with bread only** in order **to be lenient with the wealthy** person, **who is teaching it? Everyone agrees** with this *halakha*, and it is taught according to both opinions.

On Rav Chisda's reading, what Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree upon — without any dispute — is the case of beginning *Shabbos* where one physically stands: *aval bi-mkomi, divrei ha-kol echad ani ve-echad ashir* — there, everyone would agree that any person, whether *ani* or *ashir*, may start *Shabbos* at his present location, because *ikar eruv be-regel* — the fundamental act of *eruv* is placing one's feet somewhere, and where one stands is the most self-evident expression of that principle. The dispute, on this approach, is only about whether the *ashir* can invoke the distant-designation mechanism even without being stuck.

## Two Houses Case

### Puzzle Explained

The *Gemara* cites a later *Mishna*: a person who set out to walk to a city to which an *eruv* had been established, and whose friend turned him back en route, is permitted to walk to that city on *Shabbos* — but the residents of his home city are not. This is stated as the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. The puzzle is immediately apparent: why should this individual have a greater range of travel than the residents of the city he departed from? What did he do, in the few moments he walked before being turned around, that the townspeople did not?

### Two Houses Resolve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲוֵינַן בַּהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא אִיהוּ וּמַאי שְׁנָא אִינְהוּ? וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים, וּשְׁנֵי תְּחוּמֵי שַׁבָּת בֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** this mishna and raised a difficulty: **What is different** about **him and what is different** about **them?** Why is he permitted to proceed to the other town while it is prohibited for the other residents to do so? **And Rav Huna said: We are dealing here with** a case **where he has** two **houses,** one in each city, **and** there is the distance of **two Shabbat limits,** four thousand cubits, **between them.**

Rav Huna resolves the difficulty: *hacha be-mai askinan — she-yesh lo shnei batim, u-shnei techumei Shabbat beinehem* — we are dealing here with a person who has two houses, with the two *Shabbos* boundaries falling between them. City A is his home; city B is his second home. The two cities are no more than four thousand *amos* apart. He set out Friday afternoon with the intention of spending *Shabbos* in city B.

### Traveler Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיהוּ, כֵּיוָן דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לְאוֹרְחָא — הֲוָה לֵיהּ עָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **him, since he set out on his way, his** legal status **is** that of **a pauper,** as he did not intend to return to his first house, but to continue to his other house. Therefore, he can establish residence at the end of his Shabbat limit by verbal means alone.

Once he departed with the intention of spending *Shabbos* in city B, he became *hava lei ani* — he acquired the status of an *ani* for this purpose. He was no longer considered a person "at home"; he was a traveler in transit between two places, effectively homeless for the duration of his journey. Even though his friend persuaded him to return, his *halachic* status had already been established at the moment he was caught out on the road. He was physically present at the midpoint between the two cities when *Shabbos* began — or effectively so — and therefore that midpoint functioned as his *eruv* location, granting him two thousand *amos* in each direction, sufficient to reach city B the following morning.

### Different Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנָךְ — עֲשִׁירִים נִינְהוּ. אַלְמָא כָּל ״בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ — עָנִי אִין, עָשִׁיר לָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the legal status of **these** other inhabitants of his city, **is** that of **wealthy people,** as they are in their houses and have food. Consequently, they can only establish residence at the end of their Shabbat limit by depositing food there prior the onset of Shabbat. **Apparently, everything** stated with regard to one who says: My residence is **in such-and-such place;** to **a pauper, yes,** it applies to **a wealthy person, no,** it does not apply. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that this is the case.

The residents of city A never left their homes and never entered the traveler's condition. They are thus *ashirim* in the relevant sense — people who began *Shabbos* from within the stable context of their residence — and cannot take advantage of the foot-based *eruv* mechanism without bread. This case therefore illustrates the distinction between the traveler's status and that of ordinary city dwellers: not whether one is financially wealthy or poor, but whether one's *Shabbos* commenced while one was in transit, without the anchor of a home.

### Inference From Text

The *Gemara* draws an inference from this case specifically in relation to the debate between Rav Nachman and Rav Chisda. This *Mishna* is presented as *divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda. Yet the traveler's case is accorded a special status that the city residents do not share. If Rabbi Yehuda holds universally, as Rav Chisda claims, that even an *ashir* may designate a distant spot, why would the traveler have any advantage over the city residents? They should be able to designate a distant spot too, regardless of their status. The fact that the *Mishna* assigns the traveler a distinct privilege — allowing him to reach city B when others cannot — implies that even for Rabbi Yehuda, there remains a meaningful distinction between the person who is genuinely stuck outside and the person who is not. The case of distant designation must, after all, be limited to the true *ani* — even in Rabbi Yehuda's view. This inference supports Rav Nachman's reading: the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerns only the case where one begins *Shabbos* at the spot where one stands.

## Rabbah Case

### Rabbah's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן הֲוָה רְגִיל דְּאָתֵי מֵאַרְטִיבְנָא לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan was in the habit** of **coming from** his home in **Artibbena to Pumbedita** on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* reports that Rabbah bar bar Chana was accustomed to traveling regularly from Artivna to Pumbedita over *Shabbos*. Rather than going out on Friday afternoon to place bread or to be physically present at the midpoint, he would simply sit at home and declare: *tehei shvisasi be-Tzinza* — "Let my *Shabbos* be in Tzinza" — designating that particular location from the comfort of his home.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: ״תְּהֵא שְׁבִיתָתִי בְּצִינְתָא״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּ״מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״,
>
> **Translation:** He **would declare** on Shabbat eve: **My residence is in Tzinta,** a settlement located between the Shabbat limits of the two places. **Abaye said to him: What is your opinion** that led you to act in that manner? Is it because in a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and Rav Ḥisda said:** The **dispute** between these two Sages is in a case where the person said: My residence is **in such-and-such place,** and you rely on Rabbi Yehuda and establish residence at a place between the two cities even though you are still at home?

Abaye challenged him: *mai datchach* — what are you thinking? He was apparently relying on the combination of two positions: the ruling that we follow Rabbi Yehuda, together with Rav Chisda's interpretation that Rabbi Yehuda permits even an *ashir* to designate a distant spot. Abaye rejected this reliance.

### Nachman Restated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב נַחְמָן, וְתַנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲדַרִי בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rav Naḥman** explain the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda differently, **and** furthermore, a *baraita* **was taught in accordance with his** opinion. Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan **said to him: I retract** my opinion and will no longer do so.

Against Rav Chisda's reading, Rav Nachman's position stands: when it comes to designating a specific distant location, the rule is *ani* — yes, *ashir* — no. This is not merely one opinion among equals; it is supported by the *beraita* cited earlier in the sugya, which clearly establishes that the mechanism of remote designation was a limited exception for the person caught outside with no alternatives, and that the debate of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerned only the case of beginning *Shabbos* at the spot where one physically stands.

### Retraction

Abaye's point struck home. Rabbah bar bar Chana replied: *hadar bi* — I retract. He acknowledged that he could no longer rely on Rav Chisda's reading of Rabbi Yehuda and that he would henceforth need to schlep out on Friday afternoons to be physically present at the designated location.

### Final Resolution

The *Gemara* thus concludes firmly in accordance with Rav Nachman's position. The *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda — one may begin *Shabbos* at the spot where one physically stands, whether *ani* or *ashir* — but this applies only to the case of starting *Shabbos* where one is present. To designate a distant spot without being there, without placing bread there, and without any physical connection to that location — that remains exclusively the provision of the person who is genuinely stuck outside the city with no other options. The attempt to invoke this mechanism from one's easy chair at home, designating a faraway space by declaration alone, is rejected by the *Gemara* as a misapplication of the leniency. The *ani* exception is precisely that: an exception born of necessity, not a general license to designate *Shabbos* locations at will.
