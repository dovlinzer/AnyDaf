# Yoma 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Scapegoat Procession

### Rabbi Eliezer Digression

Today's daf is 67. We pick up at the very bottom of 66b, a little before the *mishnah*. We are dealing with the previous *mishnah* concerning sending off the *sa'ir*, the goat. There had been a whole discussion about what happens if the accompanying individual falls ill — that this may be done on *Shabbat*, even carrying him on one's shoulders — and a fascinating digression regarding Rabbi Eliezer's practice of giving only indirect answers and refusing to directly respond to a woman's question. We now pick up four lines down in the wide lines.

### Bavli'im Identity Dispute

The *mishnah* mentions that they made a ramp — *kevesh asu lo* — for the man who would lead out the goat, and it also records that the *Bavli'im* would attack this man, pulling his hair and crying out, "Get out of here — take our sins away!" Rabba Bar Bar Chana comments: *lo Bavli'im hayu* — they were not actually *Bavli'im* — *ella Alexandrim hayu* — they were Alexandrian Jews, who tended toward assimilation, though it is worth noting they may have come to Jerusalem specifically for the solemn *Yom Kippur* service. *Umitoch she-son'im et ha-Bavli'im* — because the *rabbanim*, the authors of the *mishnah*, harbored a certain animosity toward *Bavli'im* — *hayu korin otan al sheman* — they would call any people they viewed as behaving badly by that name.

This is somewhat ironic, of course, since the Alexandrians were among the most acculturated Jews of the ancient world. One wonders what particular associations the *Tannaim* of *Eretz Yisrael* attached to the label *Bavli'im* — ordinarily, when one applies a name pejoratively, it is because the name carries specific connotations, not simply because one dislikes the group. What associations would *Bavli'im* have carried that made it suitable to apply to Alexandrians caught behaving in this manner? Perhaps it was ironic: these Alexandrians prided themselves on their refinement and culture, so to brand them with a term associated with the unlettered or uncultured was particularly cutting. We do find similar formulations elsewhere — the expression *hanei Bavli'ei tipshai*, "stupid Babylonians," appears in a recent context — though one must note that *Bavel* is not identical with the broader category of *Medinat HaYam*, the general Diaspora.

There is also the observation that the exclamation quoted in the text is given in Aramaic: *ma shahita tzapira dein* — why are you tarrying with this goat? — *ve-chovei dara sagi'in* — when the sins of the generation are so great? The fact that the speech is in Aramaic would seem to support the identification as *Bavli'im* rather than Alexandrians, since one would expect Alexandrian Jews to be speaking Greek. This may constitute a *kashya* on the view that they were Alexandrians.

A *baraita* is then cited: *tanu b'raita*, Rabbi Yehuda says *lo Bavli'im hayu ella Alexandrim hayu* — they were not *Bavli'im* but Alexandrians. Rabbi Yosi responds: *tanuach da'atacha she-inachta et da'ati* — your mind should be at ease, for you have set my mind at ease. Rashi explains that Rabbi Yosi himself was descended from *Bavli'im* and was therefore personally relieved by this clarification. Tosafot notes, however, that another *Gemara* records the very same exchange with the names reversed — Rabbi Yosi making the identification and Rabbi Yehuda responding — which raises a textual difficulty. What remains striking in any case is that even while insisting that these particular individuals were *not* Bavli'im, the *mishnah*'s usage of the term as a pejorative still implies something negative about *Bavli'im* in general.

### Jerusalem Procession

The *mishnah* continues: *meyakkirei Yerushalayim* — the honored, distinguished people of Jerusalem — *hayu melavin oto ad sukkah harishonah* — would accompany the man with the goat as far as the first hut. This was not the obligation of anyone in particular; in practice, only *Kohanim* were used for the actual role. Nevertheless, the honored citizens of Jerusalem would accompany the man as far as the first *sukkah* as a mark of dignity, without being the primary escorts themselves.

## Sukkot & Techumin

### 10 Sukkot Layout

There were ten *sukkot* from Jerusalem to the cliff — *eser sukkot miYerushalayim ve'ad tzuk*. The distance was 90 *ris* in total — *tish'im ris* — which, at seven and a half *ris* per *mil*, gives us twelve *mil*. A *mil* corresponds to 2000 *amos* — the distance of *techum Shabbat* — roughly a kilometer, making the total distance approximately twelve kilometers, or about seven miles, from the *Har HaBayit* to the cliff.

If there were ten *sukkot* spread over twelve *mil*, the spacing works as follows: there is one *sukkah* at every *mil* for the first ten *mil*, and then from the final *sukkah* to the cliff is a further two *mil*. Between each *sukkah* — that is, at every kilometer — there was a small hut stationed along the route.

### Techumim Constraints

The arrangement of the *sukkot* is directly tied to the laws of *techum Shabbat*. The people accompanying the man with the goat were limited to walking 2000 *amos* — one *mil* — beyond the boundary of the city. The man from Jerusalem would walk with the *sa'ir* as far as the first *sukkah*, which sat at the edge of Jerusalem's *techum Shabbat*, and then stop. A new escort would then accompany the man from there to the next *sukkah*, and so on down the line, with a fresh person available at each *sukkah* to walk him through the next *mil*.

The man leading the goat himself was granted the necessary dispensation to travel the full distance. The accompanying escorts, however, were each limited to their own *techum Shabbat*. This ensured that the man was not alone for any part of the journey — he had company through every *mil* — yet no escort was required to violate the *techum* restrictions. It should be noted that the honored people of Jerusalem accompanied him only to the first *sukkah* and then returned home; they were not the ones stationed at subsequent *sukkot* throughout *Yom Kippur*. Other individuals took on the role of remaining at each *sukkah* for the entire day, ready to escort the man for their particular segment of the route.

### Pas Be-Salo Principle

At every *sukkah*, the people stationed there would say to the man leading the goat: *harei mazon v'harei mayim* — here is food, here is water — should he need refreshment. A *baraita* records that in fact no one ever needed to avail himself of this offer — *me'olam lo hutzrach adam le-kach* — but the offer was always extended. Why? Because, as the *Gemara* explains with the well-known phrase, *eino domeh mi she-yesh lo pas be-salo le-mi she-ein lo pas be-salo* — it is not the same for someone who has bread in his basket as for someone who does not. When a person knows that food and water are available and within reach, he is far less consumed by hunger or thirst. It is only when one is told that something is unavailable or prohibited that it becomes the sole focus of attention.

This phrase, *pas be-salo*, appears elsewhere in the *Gemara* in a very different context — typically in discussions of permissible sleeping arrangements between a husband and wife when the wife is a *niddah*, or in the account of the *Amoraim* who would travel to a new town and publicly announce their availability for betrothal. In both cases the principle is the same: the awareness of legitimate future availability diminishes present desire.

A practical question arises: would this man have actually been permitted to eat and drink on *Yom Kippur* had he truly needed to? The *Gemara* does not resolve this explicitly. The *Tosafot Yeshanim* at the bottom of 68a addresses the issue. One reading there suggests that just as this *avodah* overrides *Shabbat* and *tumah*, so too it would override the eating prohibitions of *Yom Kippur* — the principle being that once a *mitzvah* overrides certain prohibitions, it may override them in the ideal manner, not merely out of *dechuyah*. A second reading, however, suggests that the food would only have been permitted had the man reached a state of genuine mortal danger — *bulmus*, an overwhelming weakness — in which case it would be permitted not because of the *avodah* itself but because of *pikuach nefesh*. The text of the *Tosafot Yeshanim* at that point is somewhat uncertain, and the two answers may in fact be distinct positions rather than a single coherent view. The word *bulmus*, incidentally, appears to be related etymologically to the word "bulimia," and in other contexts in the *Gemara* refers to a dangerous state of acute starvation or dehydration.

### Final Accompanier

The *mishnah* specifies that every escort accompanied the man from one *sukkah* to the next — *u-melavin oto mi-sukkah le-sukkah* — with one exception: *chutz me-acharon she-bahen*, the last escort. The man at the final *sukkah* would not accompany him all the way to the cliff, because the cliff was two *mil* from the last *sukkah*, and going the full two *mil* would take him beyond his own *techum Shabbat*. Instead, he would walk one *mil* — to the edge of his permitted range — and then stand at a distance and watch: *ela omed me-rachok ve-roeh et ma'asav*.

A question arises regarding how the man managed to keep the goat moving with him for twelve kilometers without a leash. The *mishnah* never mentions any restraint, and the earlier discussions about carrying on *Shabbat* did not raise the issue of a leash. It is possible the goat simply followed alongside, as animals sometimes do. If a leash had been involved, one might have expected the *Gemara* to have addressed the *Shabbat* implications of carrying it.

## Red String Ritual

### String Division

When the man reached the cliff, the *mishnah* describes what he would do: *cholek lashon shel zehorit* — he would divide the red string — *chetzyo kashar ba-sela* — tying half of it to a rock nearby — *ve-chetzyo kashar bein shnei karnav* — and the other half he would tie between the two horns of the goat. The *Gemara* explains the reasoning for this division.

Why not tie the entire string to the rock? Because since there is still a *mitzvah* to be performed — pushing the goat off the cliff — perhaps the string would turn white before the goat was pushed over. *Dilma kadim u-malbin* — perhaps it would turn white prematurely — *u-maitva dateih* — and he would feel settled, thinking the *avodah* was complete, and neglect to push the goat off. If the string is on the goat, he is actively involved with the animal and is less likely to walk away. Rashi adds that while he is in the midst of acting upon the goat, the string might not turn white yet — since the *avodah* is still in process.

Why not tie the entire string to the goat's horns? Because *zimnin de-gamish leih le-reisheih* — sometimes the goat will bend its head downward as it tumbles, tucking it beneath its body — *ve-lav adateih* — and one would not be able to see the string or notice whether it had turned white. The whole purpose of the string is its symbolic and visible efficacy; one needs to be able to observe it.

It is worth noting that the entire institution of the red string — *lashon shel zehorit* — has no explicit source in the *pesukim* of the *Torah*. It is presumably rooted in the verse *im yihyu chataeichem ka-shanim ka-sheleg yalbinu* — "though your sins be as scarlet, they shall become white as snow" — but that verse is of course a metaphor in the *Nevi'im*. Nevertheless, the practice of the red string is taken entirely for granted in the *mishnah* and *Gemara*, and considerable halachic discussion surrounds exactly where and how it should be placed.

The question of whether cutting the string constitutes a *melachah* on *Yom Kippur* is also worth noting. *Kore'a* — tearing — applies to fabrics. A string, however, is not woven fabric, and the relevant category might be *mechatech* — cutting to a precise measure — which would only be prohibited if done to an exact measurement, which this would not be. So it is not entirely clear that cutting the string is halachically problematic. Similarly, tying a knot on the goat's horn could implicate the *melachah* of *kesher*, but a non-permanent or non-expert knot would not constitute a *kesher shel kayama*. There is also the consideration of *mechamer* — using an animal to perform *melachah* — since the goat is carrying something on its body. As one of the earlier comments noted, it is far from obvious where the entire institution of the red string derives its authority or how precisely its performance was reconciled with the *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* restrictions.

### Descent & Shattering

The man would then push the goat backwards off the cliff — *ud'chafo le-achorav* — and the goat would tumble down, *v'hu mitgalgel ve-yored*. The cliff was evidently sheer, with outcroppings and rocks, because *ve-lo haya magiya la-chatzi ha-har ad she-na'aseh evarim evarim* — it would not even reach the halfway point of the descent before it shattered into its limbs. This was presumably a domesticated animal from someone's flock, not a mountain goat accustomed to rocky terrain, which would make the handling and the pushing all the more logistically interesting.

### Accompanier Returns

After the goat was pushed off, the *ish iti* — the man who had led the goat — would return and sit in the last *sukkah*: *ba ve-yashav lo tachat sukkah acharonah*. He would walk back two *techumin* — 4000 *amos* — from the cliff to the final *sukkah*. Rashi notes that this is notable: normally, when someone is granted permission to travel beyond the *techum Shabbat* for a *mitzvah* purpose such as *pikuach nefesh* or assisting in childbirth, once the task is complete, the permission ends and they are limited to 2000 *amos* from their current position. This man, however, is permitted to walk back the full two *mil*, because remaining in the *midbar* exposed him to danger — from the elements, and from whatever else might be encountered in an uninhabited wilderness — and he was weak and exhausted from the journey and the fast. There he would remain in the *sukkah* until nightfall — *ad she-techshach*.

### Impurity of Garments

The *mishnah* then raises the question of *tumah*: when during this process does the man who led the goat cause his garments to become *tamei*? The *Torah* states *ve-hameshale'ach et ha-sa'ir la-Azazel yechabes begadav* — the one who sends the goat to *Azazel* shall launder his garments — which indicates he becomes *tamei*. The question is: at what moment?

The *tanna kamma* holds that it is *mi-she-yatza chutz le-chomat Yerushalayim* — from the time he exits the walls of Jerusalem. Rabbi Shimon holds that it is only *mi-sha'at dechiyato la-tzuk* — from the moment of the actual pushing over the cliff.

This question of *tumah* is connected to a broader question about the stages of fulfillment of the *avodah*. As the *Gemara* elsewhere makes clear, there appear to be three distinct stages: first, the drawing of the lot and the *viduy*, after which the *avodah* is considered fulfilled *bedi'avad* — if the goat died at that point, the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* would not have to be repeated. Second, leaving the walls of Jerusalem, at which point *na'asit mitzvato* — the *mitzvah* of the *sa'ir* itself is considered fully accomplished. It is at this second stage that the red string would turn white and the garments would become *tamei*, according to the *tanna kamma*. Third, the actual going over the cliff, which seems to function as a final completion — not the essential threshold but the culmination of the process. We who tend to focus on the dramatic moment of the goat going over the cliff may be surprised to realize that halachically, the *mitzvah* was substantially complete much earlier.

## Sukkot Count Dispute

### Rabbi Meir Position

A *baraita* is cited: *eser sukkot u-shneim asar milim hayu, divrei Rabbi Meir* — ten *sukkot* and twelve *mil*, this being the standard position assumed by our *mishnah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda says: *tesha sukkot va-asara milim* — only nine *sukkot* and ten *mil*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the escort from the last *sukkah* could accompany the man all the way to the edge of the cliff, since the distance from the ninth *sukkah* to the end of the tenth *mil* — the cliff — is exactly one *mil*, within the *techum Shabbat* of that escort.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

Rabbi Yosi says: *chamesh sukkot va-asara milim* — five *sukkot* and ten *mil* — *ve-kulam al yedei eruv* — and all of them through the mechanism of *eruv*. Since all the escorts are traveling in one direction — away from Jerusalem toward the cliff — rather than restricting each person to one *mil*, one could make an *eruv* at the far end of the first *mil* and thereby extend one's range to two *mil* in that direction. Each escort stationed at a *sukkah* could place an *eruv* one *mil* further along the route, enabling him to accompany the man for two *mil* rather than one. This halves the number of *sukkot* needed while maintaining full coverage of the ten-*mil* route.

### Elazar Hypothetical

Rabbi Yosi then relates that his son Elazar raised a further point — *sach li Elazar beni* — speaking, Rashi notes, in something of a playful or exploratory tone: if we are going to use *eruvin* to extend coverage, *yachol ani la'asot afilu shtei sukkot va-asara milim* — I could cover the entire ten *mil* with just two *sukkot*, by placing two people at each *sukkah* and having each make an *eruv* in the opposite direction from the other.

The arrangement would work as follows. The people departing Jerusalem place their *eruv* two *mil* out, enabling them to walk two *mil* in that direction. At the first *sukkah*, two people are stationed: one places his *eruv* back toward Jerusalem and can walk two *mil* in that direction to meet the returning man; the other places his *eruv* two *mil* further along the route toward the cliff, enabling him to walk two *mil* forward. At the second *sukkah*, the same arrangement applies. The result is that with two people per *sukkah* making *eruvin* in opposite directions, the entire ten-*mil* route can be covered with only two *sukkot*.

As the *Gemara* makes clear, Elazar is not claiming this is what was actually done — *eruvin* were not used at all in practice — but is simply demonstrating the logical extreme of the principle his father introduced. The *Gemara* then asks: *ke-man azla* — like whom does the *mishnah*'s detail that the final escort does not accompany the man all the way to the cliff go? It goes like Rabbi Meir — because according to Rabbi Yehuda (nine *sukkot*, ten *mil*), the last escort could reach the cliff; and according to Rabbi Yosi (five *sukkot* with *eruv*), again the man could be accompanied to the end. Only according to Rabbi Meir, with ten *sukkot* and twelve *mil*, does the geometry require the last escort to fall short of the cliff by a full *mil*.

## Limbs Permissibility

### Limb Question

The *Gemara* raises a question: *otan evarim mah hen be-hana'ah* — after the goat shatters on its descent, what is the legal status of those limbs with respect to deriving benefit? The goat had been sanctified — it was designated for *Azazel* — and the *Gemara* had noted that *shechitato zo hi shechitato*, the manner of its death constitutes its *shechitah*. The question is whether, once *na'asit mitzvato* — once the *mitzvah* is accomplished — any sanctified status that would prohibit benefit is lifted, or whether it persists.

### Rav/Shmuel Dispute

Rav and Shmuel disagree. *Chad amar mutarin* — one says the limbs are permitted for benefit — *ve-chad amar asurin* — the other says they are forbidden. The one who says they are permitted relies on the word *midbar* — wilderness — which carries a connotation of openness and freedom from restriction, suggesting that whatever is sent there is released from prohibitions. The one who says they are forbidden relies on the word *gezeirah* — the land of cutting or restriction — which implies that the object retains some character of limitation.

Each opinion must then account for the word it does not primarily rely upon. According to the opinion of *mutarin*, what purpose does *gezeirah* serve? It serves to teach, as a *baraita* records, that the cliff must be a sheer drop — *gezeirah ein gezeirah ella chatucha*, *gezeirah* means cut, implying the face of the cliff is cut straight down, a sheer precipice rather than a gradual slope. This is in fact the textual source for the entire requirement to push the goat off a cliff rather than simply releasing it into open wilderness. According to the opinion of *asurin*, what purpose does the repeated use of *midbar* serve? It serves the *derashah* cited elsewhere: *ha-midbarah, ha-midbarah, ba-midbar* — the multiple uses of "wilderness" in the *pesukim* teach that this *avodah* applies not only during the years of the *Mishkan* in the wilderness but also at *Nov*, *Giv'on*, *Shilo*, and the permanent *Beit HaMikdash* — wherever the *Mishkan* was stationed, including the settled land of *Eretz Yisrael*.

## Limbs Permissibility (II)

### Textual Locations

The repeated use of "wilderness" in the relevant *pesukim* — *ve-shillach et ha-sa'ir ha-midbarah*, *ve-shillach oto ha-midbarah* — thus serves to include *Nov*, *Giv'on*, *Shilo*, and the permanent *Beit HaMikdash* as locations where the *avodah* of the *sa'ir* applies. This raises an interesting observation: once the *Mishkan* was in a settled land rather than an actual wilderness, the symbolic resonance of sending something "to the wilderness" shifts. The *pesukim* refer specifically to sending the goat to an uninhabited and wild place; when the *Mishkan* is in a settled territory, the significance of this act may be somewhat different in character.

One participant noted that the *Mishkan* was also stationed at *Gilgal* for several years after the entry into the land, a location in the Jordan Valley that may not have had a suitable sheer cliff nearby. This would be an interesting question: it is not listed among the locations in the *baraita*, which may hint that this period presented its own unique circumstances. In any event, the *baraita*'s inclusion of *Nov*, *Giv'on*, *Shilo*, and the permanent *Beit HaMikdash* also implies a certain foreknowledge of the *Mishkan*'s eventual itinerary — itself a striking observation.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava rules in favor of the opinion that the limbs are permitted: *mistabra ke-man de-amar mutarin*. His reasoning is: *lo amra Torah shillach le-takala* — the *Torah* does not command one to send something into the wilderness only to have it become a stumbling block. If the limbs remain forbidden for benefit, then scattered animal parts lying in a desolate place could lead people who encounter them to inadvertently misuse them — and the *Torah* would not establish a *mitzvah* that creates such a hazard. Rava invokes a similar principle in the context of the *metzora* ritual, where the bird sent away after purification — if it were to later fly into someone's domain — could not possibly remain forbidden for benefit, since no one would know its identity. The *Torah* would not prescribe a practice that routinely generates occasions of stumbling.

## Azazel Etymology

### Azazel Definition

The *Gemara* now turns to the derivation of the word *Azazel* and what the *pesukim* teach about the requirements for the location and manner of the goat's dispatch. *Tanu rabanan*: *Azazel* — *she-yehei az ve-kasheh* — the place must be strong and hard. The word encodes the requirement of a hard, rocky mountain.

### Tzuk Definition

A separate *baraita* teaches: *gezeirah ein gezeirah ella chatucha* — the word *gezeirah* means something cut, from the root *ligzor*, to cut. This is the textual basis for requiring a sheer cliff — something cut straight down — as opposed to merely rough or hard terrain. An alternative reading offered in the *baraita*: *ein gezeirah ella davar ha-mitgazer ve-yored* — something that gets chopped up as it descends — which speaks to the goat's shattering on the way down.

A further alternative reading carries a different significance entirely: *gezeirah shema tomar ma'aseh tohu hu* — lest you say this entire act is foolishness. You look at this ritual — sending a goat over a cliff to *Azazel* — and you wonder: what kind of divine service is this? What meaning can it have? To that, the *Torah* responds: *Ani Hashem gezarti* — I, God, have decreed it — *ve-ein lecha reshut le-harheir bahen* — and you have no license to question it. The word *gezeirah* thus also evokes the concept of a divine edict, a *chok* — something decreed by God that is not subject to rational justification. The verse *chukotai tishmoru* — keep my *chukim* — underscores this: these are the statutes I have set, and your obedience is itself the point.

### Unsettled Land

The *mishnah* teaches: *yachol be-yishuv* — one might think the ritual could be performed in settled territory? *Talmud lomar ba-midbar* — the *Torah* explicitly states "in the wilderness," requiring that it be performed in uninhabited, unsettled land. And how do we know it must specifically involve a cliff — a *tzuk* — and not merely hard terrain? *Talmud lomar gezeirah* — the word *gezeirah*, as derived above, teaches the requirement of a sheer drop.

A separate *baraita* offers an alternative derivation: *Azazel — kasheh she-be-harim* — it must be the hardest of mountains. The word *el* in *Azazel* connotes strength, as in the verse *ve-et eilei ha-aretz lakach* — he took the mighty ones of the land — where *eilei* denotes the strong and powerful.

### Uza/Aza'el Atonement

*Tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* offers yet another reading: *Azazel* — *she-mechaper al ma'aseh Uza ve-Aza'el* — it atones for the sin of Uza and Aza'el. Rashi identifies these as *malachei chavala*, angels of destruction, who descended to the earth in the days of Na'amah the sister of Tuval Kayin, and about whom it is said *va-yir'u bnei ha-elohim et bnot ha-adam* — the divine beings saw the daughters of man — a reference to the narrative of the fallen angels who cohabited with human women. Rashi brings this back to the concrete: *klomar al arayot mechaper* — what this means in practice is that the *Azazel* ritual atones for sexual sins. The allusion to these mythic figures thus grounds in a recognizable halachic category, namely, the sin of *gilui arayot*.

## Mishpatim & Chukim

### Rational Laws

The *Gemara* now addresses a broader classification of *mitzvot*, introduced through a *pasuk* from the Torah: *et mishpatai ta'asu ve-et chukotai tishmoru*. The *Gemara* distinguishes *mishpatim* — rational laws — from *chukim* — divine edicts. *Mishpatim* are defined as *devarim she-ilmalei nichetvu din hu she-yichtevu* — things that even had they not been written, reason demands they would have been written. They are the laws that any developed legal or moral system would arrive at independently.

The examples given are: *avodat kochavim* — idolatry, which is a rational prohibition in any monotheistic framework; *gilui arayot* — sexual prohibitions; *shefichut damim* — murder; *gezel* — theft; and *virchat Hashem* — blaspheming God. It is worth noting that every single one of these corresponds precisely to the *mitzvot sichli'yot*, the rationally derivable commandments — and they map almost exactly onto the *sheva mitzvot Bnei Noach*. This has significant implications for the medieval philosophical debate about the nature of those seven laws. Mendelssohn wished to see the *sheva mitzvot Bnei Noach* as natural law — principles derivable without revelation — and this *Gemara* would seem to support that view, explicitly listing them as things reason would have demanded even without divine promulgation. Rambam, however, in his *Mishneh Torah*, emphasizes that a non-Jew acquires the special status associated with observing the *sheva mitzvot* only if he observes them as revealed law — because God commanded them — not merely because reason dictates them. The present *Gemara* therefore appears to sit more comfortably with Mendelssohn's position.

It is also worth noting that the Rambam in *Shemonah Perakim* does not include *gilui arayot* among the *mishpatim*, placing it instead among the *chukim*. This is not necessarily a contradiction: the category of *gilui arayot* is broad, and whether adultery, incest, or *niddah* is the subject may determine whether the prohibition is rationally self-evident or more in the character of a divine edict.

### Divine Edicts

The *chukim*, by contrast, are *devarim she-ha-Satan meishiv aleihem* — things that the *Yetzer HaRa* or the forces of critique would challenge, arguing that they are nonsensical, arbitrary, or irrational. The examples given are: *achilat chazir* — eating pork and other forbidden foods; *levishat shatnez* — wearing *shatnez*, a mixture of wool and linen; *chalitzat yevamah* — the *chalitzah* ceremony for the *yevamah* (notably, the text specifies *chalitzah* rather than *yibbum* itself, since *yibbum* has ancient precedent in the broader ancient world and carries intelligible social rationale, whereas the specific act of *chalitzah* is less obviously purposive); *taharat metzora* — the purification ritual of the *metzora*, with its birds and cedar wood and scarlet thread, which can appear to be almost magical in character; and *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* — the scapegoat itself.

*Korbanot* in general can be understood as meaningful religious practice — ancient sacrifice is a near-universal religious form. But sending a goat specifically over a cliff to a place called *Azazel*? This is precisely the kind of practice that invites the critique of *ma'aseh tohu* — sheer foolishness.

### Obedience Theme

To that critique, the *Torah* responds: *Ani Hashem chakaktiv ve-ein lecha reshut le-harheir bahen* — I, God, have inscribed this, and you have no license to second-guess it. This is the meaning of *chukotai tishmoru* together with *Ani Hashem*. The *chukim* are not presented as things requiring rational vindication. They demonstrate, precisely through their resistance to rational justification, the character of pure obedience to divine command. The declaration *Ani Hashem* is what makes them binding — not their intelligibility.

## Mishpatim & Chukim (II)

### Rambam on Values

The Rambam in *Shemonah Perakim* offers a nuanced reconciliation of the rabbinic and philosophical positions on this tension. The *Gemara* elsewhere records the view that it is preferable to say: "I want to sin, but what shall I do — my Father in Heaven has forbidden it," rather than "I have no desire to sin." This formulation would seem to exalt external compliance over internalized virtue, which runs counter to the philosophical ideal — articulated by Aristotle and adopted by many medieval thinkers — that the highest ethical state is one in which a person genuinely does not desire to do wrong, having internalized correct values.

Rambam resolves the apparent conflict by drawing precisely the distinction the *Gemara* has just established: for *mishpatim* — the rational laws — the philosophical ideal holds. One should genuinely not want to murder or steal; one should internalize justice and honesty as values, not merely comply with them under external compulsion. For *chukim*, however — the divine edicts that resist rational justification — the ideal is precisely external obedience. If one wants to eat *chazir* and refrains because God said not to, that is the ideal response, because *chukim* are designed to demonstrate obedience, not to cultivate a particular internal disposition. The two models are thus not in conflict; they apply to different categories of *mitzvot*.

## Garment Impurity Dispute

### Sender Becomes Tamei

The *mishnah*'s discussion of garment impurity is now examined in the *Gemara* in greater detail. *Ha-meshale'ach metamei vegadim* — the one who actually leads the goat becomes *tamei* and must launder his garments — *ve-ein ha-shole'ach et ha-meshale'ach metamei vegadim* — but those who sent him, the dignitaries who accompanied him for portions of the journey, do not become *tamei*. Even though they walked with him, their role was escort, not the act of sending.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

One might have thought that *tumah* sets in as early as when the man leaves the *Azarah* — the Temple courtyard. The *Gemara* rejects this, deriving from the word *ha-meshale'ach* — "the one who sends" — that the act of sending must already be in process, meaning the man must already be in the wilderness. It would then not be *tamei* from departure from the *Azarah*. But one might also think: perhaps *tumah* does not set in until he reaches the *tzuk*, the cliff? The *Gemara* derives from the added *vav* in *ve-ha-meshale'ach* — "and the one who sends" — that once the process of sending toward the wilderness has begun, he becomes *tamei*, which occurs upon leaving the walls of Jerusalem. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda: *mi-she-yatza chutz le-chomat Yerushalayim* — from the time he exits beyond the walls of Jerusalem.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

Rabbi Yosi takes a different approach. He juxtaposes the word *Azazel* — as he understands it, pointing to the cliff — with the instruction to launder garments: *Azazel ve-chibbes*. Since *Azazel* is associated with the *tzuk*, he holds that *tumah* does not set in until the man reaches the cliff itself — *ad she-yihiyeh la-tzuk*.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon reads the verse most literally: *ve-ha-meshale'ach et ha-sa'ir la-Azazel yechabes begadav* — the one who sends the goat to *Azazel* shall launder his garments. For Rabbi Shimon, the sending is completed only with the actual act of pushing the goat over — *zorko be-vat achat* — in one moment, one act of hurling. Only at that point, when the goat is actually dispatched over the cliff, does the man become *tamei* and his garments require laundering. The mere arrival at the cliff is insufficient; it is the push itself that constitutes the completion of *meshale'ach*, and only then does *tumah* take effect.
