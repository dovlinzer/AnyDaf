# Horayot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Par ha-Elam Davar

### Review: Chatat Keduma

We begin today at the top of daf 9, *amud aleph*. The *sugyot* we have been traversing concern the conditions under which a *par ha-elam davar shel tzibbur* is brought, and in parallel the conditions under which the *kohen gadol* brings his *par*. Several principles emerge from the prior discussion. Both require a *psak halacha*; both are only liable through a *hora'ah* involving *kiyum miktzat u'bittul miktzat*. The *hora'ah* must concern a matter for which one would otherwise bring a *korban chatat* — not a matter governed by *oleh v'yored*. A *korban chatat* itself requires a potential *chiyuv kares*, and, flowing from this, an *asham talui* is likewise linked only to the category of *korban chatat* and not to *korban oleh v'yored*.

### Nasi & Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבִינָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: נָשִׂיא שֶׁנִּצְטָרַע, מַהוּ? מִידְחָא דְּחֵי, אוֹ מִיפְטָר פְּטִיר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּילָךְ, אוֹ דְּגַזָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak:** In the case of **a king who was afflicted with leprosy** and unfit to serve as king during his affliction, **what is** his status with regard to the sliding-scale offering? Previously, during his reign, was **he** completely **eliminated** from the obligation to bring a sliding-scale offering to the extent that even now, when he is no longer king, he remains exempt? **Or** was he merely **exempted,** so that now that he is no longer king he is obligated to bring the offering? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **said to** Ravina: Does he bring the offering from **your** property, i.e., public property, **or** does he bring the offering **from** his personal **treasure [*degazza*]?** Since he obviously would bring the offering from his own personal treasure, he remains exempt from bringing the offering.

Within the discussion of *oleh v'yored*, a question arises: does the *kohen gadol* bring a special *korban* based on *oleh v'yored*, or are his *korbanot* categorically limited to those linked to a *korban chatat*? The same question applies to the *nasi*. He possesses a special *korban*, yet that uniqueness is not conditioned on a *psak* — it reflects his personal status when he commits an ordinary transgression. Does he nevertheless participate in the *oleh v'yored* structure?

The *mishnah* that concluded yesterday did not address the *kohen gadol* directly, but it did take up the *nasi*. According to *Rabbi Akiva*, the *nasi* is generally subject to *korban oleh v'yored* — when an act falls in the category of *chatat* he brings his designated *se'ir*, but for the *oleh v'yored* transgressions he is included, with one exception: the *oleh v'yored* arising from the oath to give testimony, which has no application to a king. *Rabbi Yose HaGlili*, by contrast, excludes both the *nasi* and the *kohen gadol* from *oleh v'yored* entirely — and, notably, not because their *korbanot* are limited to a different category, but because *oleh v'yored* is structurally designed for those who could conceivably become poor. A *kohen gadol* and a king are guaranteed against poverty by virtue of their office: the community ensures the *kohen gadol*'s wealth, and the king may always levy taxes to replenish his treasury.

This is a significant point: *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* is not arguing that their special *korbanot* crowd out the *oleh v'yored*. He is arguing that the very architecture of *oleh v'yored* — a sliding scale calibrated to wealth — is simply inapplicable to individuals whose poverty is structurally impossible.

## Nasi with Tzara'at

### Midcha Dachi Query

The *Gemara* continues roughly ten lines from the top of 9a, beginning with the word *minei*: *ba'ei minei Ravina me'Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* — Ravina posed a question to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak. Given that the *nasi* is excluded from *oleh v'yored* because he can never become poor, what is the law if the *nasi* contracted *tzara'at*? Since a king afflicted with *tzara'at* cannot continue serving, the question becomes: *midcha dachi o miptar patir* — has his royal status been permanently displaced such that the exemption from *oleh v'yored* is categorically removed, or has he only been temporarily suspended, such that during this interlude his prior exemption no longer shields him and he reverts to the ordinary obligation?

### Rashi: Wealth Exempts

The response, *dilach o deganza* — "is it from yours or from the treasury?" — is cryptic on its face. Rashi reads the exchange as follows: the question is, where does the money for the *korban* come from? Were the king to sin in office, the *korban* would be funded from the royal treasury — which, per Rashi, represents his personal wealth. Since even a king afflicted with *tzara'at* retains his wealth, and since the entire rationale for excluding the *nasi* from *oleh v'yored* is that he cannot become poor, the king remains exempt even while incapacitated. His wealth is intact; the exemption therefore persists.

### Ha-Rosh Challenge

The *Rosh* raises a powerful challenge to Rashi's reading. Mere wealth, he argues, cannot by itself be the criterion for exemption. Wealthy individuals who face no structural guarantee against eventual poverty still bring an *oleh v'yored*. The rationale for excluding the *kohen gadol* and the king is not wealth per se, but the institutional mechanism that ensures they will never fall into poverty — for the *kohen gadol*, communal support; for the king, the power of taxation. But a deposed king no longer commands that power. He can no longer reach into another's pocket. So what justifies his continued exemption?

The *Rosh* therefore reads *dilach o deganza* in the opposite direction: *dilach* — will the money come from your pocket, i.e., are you still able to compel others to fund your *korban*? Since the answer is no, and the money must come *deganza*, from his own stores, the deposed king now stands in the position of a private individual. And if that is so, he is no longer exempt — he would in fact be obligated to bring the *korban*. The *Rosh* concedes in the end that he prefers Rashi's reading, but the underlying critique stands: bare wealth, without the institutional guarantee, should not suffice for exemption. The unresolved tension in the *Gemara* is whether this period of *tzara'at* represents a complete displacement of the king's status or merely a temporary suspension of it.

## Kohen Gadol & Mincha

### Rava on Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מָשִׁיחַ פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּן. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״זֶה קׇרְבַּן אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו״. זוֹ בָּאָה חוֹבָה לוֹ, וְאֵין אַחֶרֶת בָּאָה חוֹבָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: An anointed** priest **is exempt from** bringing an offering in **all** the cases where one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering. **Rava said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva?** It is as **the verse states: "This is the offering of Aaron and of his sons** that they shall offer unto the Lord on the day that he is anointed: One-tenth part of an ephah of fine flour as a meal-offering" (Leviticus 6:13). One can infer: It is **this** tenth of an ephah that **comes** as **an obligation for him, and no other** such offering **comes** as **an obligation for him.**

The *Gemara* now turns, via a *braita*, to *Rabbi Akiva*: *mashiach patur mikulan* — the *kohen gadol* is exempt from all categories of *oleh v'yored*. Rava explains the reasoning: *amar kra, zeh korban Aharon u'vanav* — the verse describing the daily *mincha* offered by the *kohen gadol*, half in the morning and half in the evening, is presented as his sole *mincha* obligation. *Zu ba-achovah lo v'ein acheres ba-achovah lo* — this, and no other *mincha*, is his obligatory offering. Since the lowest tier of *oleh v'yored* is a *mincha*, and since the *Torah* has said that a *kohen gadol* never incurs a *mincha* obligation beyond his daily offering, the bottom of the *oleh v'yored* scale is structurally inaccessible to him.

This distinguishes *Rabbi Akiva*'s position from that of *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* in a subtle but important way. *Rabbi Yose* excludes the *kohen gadol* because he cannot become poor. *Rabbi Akiva* excludes him because the *Torah* has categorically foreclosed the possibility of his ever bringing a *mincha* as an obligation. The two rationales overlap in practical outcome but diverge in conceptual basis.

### Me'achot Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: כִּי מְמַעֵט לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא – מִדַּלֵּי דַלּוּת, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה. אֲבָל עֲנִיּוּת וַעֲשִׁירוּת, לָא מַעֲטֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו הַכֹּהֵן עַל חַטָּאתוֹ אֲשֶׁר חָטָא מֵאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״. הַמִּתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת – מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּכוּלָּן, וְשֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת – אֵין מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that **when the Merciful One excludes** an anointed priest, it is particularly **from** the type of sliding-scale offering brought due to **extreme poverty? And what is it?** The **one-tenth of an ephah** meal-offering mentioned in the verse. **But the Merciful One did not exclude him** from the dove brought as a sliding-scale offering due to **poverty** and the sheep brought as a sliding-scale offering by one with **wealth.** The Gemara rejects this: That **should not enter your mind, as it is written** with regard to the sliding-scale offering: **"And the priest shall atone for him for his sin that he has committed from one of these"** (Leviticus 5:13), from which it is derived: **One who gains atonement with** every **one** of the types of a sliding-scale offering **gains atonement with any of** the types of the sliding-scale offering, **and one who** does **not gain atonement with** every **one** of the types of a sliding-scale offering does **not gain atonement with any of** the types of the sliding-scale offering.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge: perhaps we should read the verse as excluding the *kohen gadol* only from the lowest tier — the *mincha* of *dallei dalus* — while leaving him subject to the middle tier (the bird) and the upper tier (the large animal) when his means permit. Why not say simply that the *Torah* excluded him from *asiris ha'eifa* but not from the others?

The *Gemara* responds: *v'chiper alav ha-kohen al chataso asher chata mei-achas mei-eleh* — the verse states that the *kohen* shall atone for whoever sinned on "one of these." The principle derived is *ha-miskaper b'achas miskaper b'chulan* — one who can atone with any one of the three options may use all of them; *she-ein miskaper b'achas ein miskaper b'chulan* — one who cannot atone with one of them cannot use any of them. Since the *kohen gadol* is excluded from the *mincha*, he is excluded from the entirety of *oleh v'yored*.

### Me'achot vs L'achot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה כִּי יֶאְשַׁם לְאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״, הָכִי נָמֵי: דְּכׇל הַמִּתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחַת – מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן, וְשֶׁאֵין מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחַת – אֵין מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן. אַלְּמָה תְּנַן, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: נָשִׂיא חַיָּיב חוּץ מִשְּׁמִיעַת קוֹל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if that is so,** and the verse is interpreted in that manner, then that **which is written** there: **"And it shall be when he shall be guilty of one of these matters"** (Leviticus 5:5), **so too** shall be interpreted: **Anyone who becomes liable in** every **one** of the instances for which one brings a sliding-scale offering **can become liable in any of** those instances, **and** anyone **who does not become liable in** every **one** of the instances to bring a sliding-scale offering **cannot become liable in any of** those instances. **Why,** then, did **we learn** in the mishna that **Rabbi Akiva says:** The **king is liable** in all of these cases **except for** the case of **hearing of a voice,** indicating that he can become liable in the rest of the instances even if he is exempt in one?

The *Gemara* presses further. A similar formulation appears in the verse describing the three categories of sin that trigger *oleh v'yored* — the oath about testimony, *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*, and swearing falsely. That verse uses the phrase *l'achas mei-eleh* — sinned regarding "one of these." By the same logic, why not say: only if a person could be liable for any one of these sins may he be liable for any of them? If the *nasi* cannot be sworn to testify, he should be excluded from the entire *oleh v'yored*, just as *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* maintains. Yet *Rabbi Akiva* holds that the *nasi* is exempt only from the oath about testimony but remains obligated for the others. How can *Rabbi Akiva* invoke the *miskaper b'achas* principle to exclude the *kohen gadol* from the *korban*, while not invoking the same logic to exclude the *nasi* from the sin categories?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״מֵאַחַת״ מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ, ״לְאַחַת״ לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ. וּמַאי שְׁנָא ״מֵאַחַת״ דְּמַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye and Rava both say** to resolve this difficulty: Rabbi Akiva **learns** this inference from the term: **"From one"** (Leviticus 5:13). **He does not learn** anything from the term: **"Of one"** (Leviticus 5:5). The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about the term **"from one" that he learns** a *halakha* from it?

Abaye and Rava resolve this jointly: *mei-achas* and *l'achas* do not by themselves carry the implication that all possibilities must be available — the *mem* versus *lamed* is not the operative distinction. What matters is the *placement* of the word in the *pasuk*. The phrase *mei-achas* in connection with the *korban* appears specifically at the end, positioned adjacent to the *mincha* clause — *gabei asiris ha'eifa*. Had it been written alongside the bird or the large animal, it would carry no special exclusionary implication. But because the *Torah* placed it specifically at the *mincha* — the tier that a *kohen gadol* can never bring — the only way to make sense of that placement is to understand it as a signal: one who cannot bring the *mincha* is excluded from all tiers. The positioning is itself the *derashah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכַתְבֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְבַסּוֹף גַּבֵּי עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה, לְמֵימְרָא דְּכֹל דְּמִחַיַּיב בַּעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה – מִחַיַּיב בְּכוּלָּן, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחַת אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן, נִכְתְּבֵיהּ לְהַאי ״מֵאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה״ בְּדַלּוּת, אִי נָמֵי בַּעֲשִׁירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The difference is **that the Merciful One wrote it at the end** of the passage discussing the sliding-scale offering, **with regard to the one-tenth of an ephah** meal-offering, **to say that anyone who can become liable to bring the one-tenth of an ephah can become liable to** bring **any of them. As, if it enters your mind** to say that **one can become liable to** bring **one even though he cannot become liable to** bring **any** one **of them, let** the Torah **write this** phrase: **From one of these, with regard to** the offering brought due to **poverty,** or **alternatively, with regard to** the offering brought by one with **wealth.** Since this term does not appear with regard to one of the other offerings, apparently, it is specifically with regard to the offering brought due to extreme poverty that one who cannot become liable for that offering is exempt from the entire matter.

By contrast, the *l'achas* formulation governing the sin categories does not appear at a strategically chosen point that signals the exclusion of a class who cannot commit one of the sins. Therefore, the *nasi*'s inability to be sworn to testify does not cascade into a total exclusion from *oleh v'yored* — and *Rabbi Akiva* is not being internally inconsistent.

In summary: both *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* and *Rabbi Akiva* agree that the *kohen gadol* is fundamentally in the *parasha* of *oleh v'yored*, and that arguments must be marshaled to extract him. *Rabbi Yose* excludes him because he cannot become poor. *Rabbi Akiva* excludes him because the *Torah* has removed the *mincha* from his repertoire, and the *Torah*'s placement of *mei-achas* at that juncture signals a total exclusion. For the *nasi*, *Rabbi Yose* likewise excludes him from everything, while *Rabbi Akiva* retains him in the category except for the oath of testimony.

## Mishnah Classification

### Standard Chatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת שֶׁבְּתוֹרָה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָן כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָן חַטָּאת – הַיָּחִיד מֵבִיא כִּשְׂבָּה וּשְׂעִירָה, וְהַנָּשִׂיא שָׂעִיר, וּמָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין מְבִיאִין פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In summation: **For all mitzvot that are in the Torah for whose intentional** violation one is **liable** to receive ***karet* and for whose unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering, the individual brings a ewe or female goat** for their unwitting transgression, **and the king** brings **a male goat** for their unwitting transgression, **and an anointed** priest **and a court** who issued an erroneous ruling **bring a bull.**

Any transgression in the *Torah* for which willful violation incurs *kares* and inadvertent violation incurs a *chatat*: the *yachid* brings a female sheep or goat, *ha-nasi meivi se'ir* — the *nasi* brings a male goat, and *u'mashiach u'Beit Din mevi'im par* — the *kohen gadol* and *Beit Din* bring a bull.

### Avoda Zara Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – הַיָּחִיד וְהַנָּשִׂיא וְהַמָּשִׁיחַ מְבִיאִין שְׂעִירָה, וּבֵית דִּין פַּר וְשָׂעִיר; פַּר לְעוֹלָה וְשָׂעִיר לְחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And for** unwittingly engaging in **idol worship, the individual, and the king, and the anointed** priest **bring a female goat, and the court** brings **a bull and a goat: A bull for a burnt-offering and a goat for a sin-offering.**

In the case of *avoda zara*, the *yachid*, the *nasi*, and the *kohen gadol* all bring a female goat. *Beit Din*, when ruling leads the community to sin regarding *avoda zara*, brings a *par l'olah v'se'ir l'chatas* — a bull as an *olah* and a male goat as a *chatat*.

### Asham Talui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם תָּלוּי – הַיָּחִיד וְהַנָּשִׂיא חַיָּיבִין, וּמָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a provisional guilt-offering, the individual and the king are liable, and an anointed** priest **and a court are exempt.**

An *asham talui* — the conditional guilt offering brought when one is uncertain whether a sin requiring a fixed *chatat* was committed — obligates the *yachid* and the *nasi*. The *kohen gadol* and *Beit Din* are exempt. The *kohen gadol*'s exemption derives from the verse *al shegogaso asher shagag*: an *asham talui* is brought only by one whose liability arises purely from *shiggas ma'aseh* — inadvertent action alone. Since the *kohen gadol*'s obligation arises additionally from *hora'ah*, he does not fall within this category.

### Asham Vadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם וַדַּאי – הַיָּחִיד וְהַנָּשִׂיא וְהַמָּשִׁיחַ חַיָּיבִין, וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a definite guilt-offering, the individual, the king, and the anointed** priest **are liable, and a court is exempt.**

For the specified cases of *asham vadai* — a definite guilt offering — the *yachid*, the *nasi*, and the *kohen gadol* are all obligated. *Beit Din* remains exempt, as its liability arises only through the *chatat* mechanism.

### Oleh V'Yored Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל שְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל, וְעַל בִּטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם, וְעַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – בֵּית דִּין פְּטוּרִין, וְהַיָּחִיד וְהַנָּשִׂיא וְהַמָּשִׁיחַ חַיָּיבִין, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מָשִׁיחַ חַיָּיב עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וּמָה הֵן מְבִיאִין? קׇרְבַּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַנָּשִׂיא מֵבִיא שָׂעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **For hearing of a voice,** i.e., a false oath of testimony, **and for** a false oath on **an utterance of the lips, and for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **a court is exempt, and the individual, the king, and the anointed** priest **are liable. But an anointed High Priest** is **not liable for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods; this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon. And what** offering are **they** liable to **bring?** It is **a sliding-scale offering** based on their financial circumstances, as delineated in the Torah (see Leviticus 5:1–13). **Rabbi Eliezer says: The king brings a goat.**

For the three sins that trigger *oleh v'yored* — *shmiat ha-kol*, *bitui sefatayim*, and *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — *Beit Din* is exempt, as its obligation is confined to the fixed *chatat* structure. The *yachid*, the *nasi*, and the *kohen gadol* are, in principle, obligated — though as we have seen, there are grounds upon which each may be excluded from particular instances of this obligation.

## Tum'at Mikdash Issue

### Contradiction Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָיָה נוֹתֵן כְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהַיָּחִיד בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי – הַנָּשִׂיא כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, מָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין. וְכֹל שֶׁהוּא בְּאָשָׁם וַדַּאי – נָשִׂיא וּמָשִׁיחַ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon would posit a principle:** For **any** case in **which the individual** is liable **to** bring **a provisional guilt-offering,** the status of **the king** is **like** that of the individual, **and an anointed** priest **and a court are exempt. And** for **any** case in **which** an individual is liable **to** bring **a definite guilt-offering,** the status of **a king and an anointed** priest is **like** that of the individual, **and** the **court is exempt.**

The *mishnah* states: *ella she'ein kohen gadol mashiach chayav al tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — the *kohen gadol* is not liable for *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*. This is structurally parallel to the *nasi*'s exemption from the oath about testimony: each is excluded from one of the three *oleh v'yored* sins while retaining obligation for the others — according to *Rabbi Shimon*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל, וּבִטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם, וְטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – בֵּית דִּין פְּטוּרִין, נָשִׂיא וּמָשִׁיחַ חַיָּיבִין, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין הַנָּשִׂיא חַיָּיב בִּשְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל, וְלֹא מָשִׁיחַ בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו. כׇּל שֶׁהוּא בְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד – נָשִׂיא כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, מָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** He continues: For **hearing of a voice,** i.e., a false oath of testimony, **and** for a false oath on **an utterance of the lips, and** for **the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **a court is exempt, and a king and an anointed** priest **are liable. But the king is not liable in** a case of **hearing of a voice, and an anointed** priest is not liable **for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods. In general, for **any** case for **which** an individual is liable **to** bring **a sliding-scale** offering, the status of **a king is like** that of the individual, **and an anointed** priest **and a court are exempt.**

Yet the *braita* also states: *kol she-hu be'oleh v'yored, nasi kayotze vo, mashiach u'Beit Din peturin* — when it comes to *oleh v'yored* generally, the *nasi* is included but the *kohen gadol* and *Beit Din* are categorically excluded. These two positions contradict one another. The first says the *kohen gadol* is in the *oleh v'yored* category except for *tum'at mikdash*; the second says he is entirely outside it. *Hagufa kashya* — the *braita* contradicts itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גּוּפַהּ קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ: שֶׁאֵין מָשִׁיחַ חַיָּיב בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – הוּא דְּפָטוּר, אֲבָל בִּשְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל וּבִטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם – חַיָּיב. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא בְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד – נָשִׂיא כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, מָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין, קָתָנֵי: מָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין פְּטוּרִין, מָה בֵּית דִּין פְּטוּרִין מִכּוּלְּהוֹן – אַף מָשִׁיחַ פָּטוּר מִכּוּלְּהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **This** *baraita* **itself** is **difficult. You said that an anointed** priest is **not liable for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, and by inference: **It is for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods **that he is exempt, but** he is **liable for hearing of a voice and** for **a statement of the lips. Say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: For **any** case for **which** an individual is liable **to** bring **a sliding-scale** offering, the status of **a king** is **like** that of the individual, **and an anointed** priest **and a court are exempt. He teaches: An anointed priest and a court are exempt; just as a court is exempt from all of** the sliding-scale offerings, **so too, an anointed priest is exempt from all of them,** not only from the offering for the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods.

## Rabbi Eliezer Rule

### Nasi Brings She-Goat

The *braita* also teaches: *Rabbi Eliezer* rules that when the *nasi* is obligated under *oleh v'yored*, he does not bring the standard sliding-scale *korban*. Instead, *ha-nasi meivi se'ir* — the *nasi* brings a male goat, which is the *korban* he would bring for a standard *chatat*. He is included in the *oleh v'yored* category, yet the *korban* he brings when obligated is his fixed designation, not the sliding-scale animal.

### Yochanan Limits Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֶלָּא בְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֱמַר כָּרֵת בּוֹ כְּבִקְבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The king brings a goat,** and not a sliding-scale offering. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Eliezer stated** his opinion **only with regard to the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, **since *karet* is stated concerning it, as** it is stated **in** all matters where there is liability to bring **a fixed** sin-offering. Just as the king brings a goat as a sin-offering for any unwitting transgression for whose intentional violation one is liable to receive *karet*, so too, he brings a goat for the defiling of the Temple. For other unwitting transgressions for which one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering and for whose intentional violation one is not liable to receive *karet*, the king is also liable to bring a sliding-scale offering.

*Rabbi Yochanan* clarifies that *Rabbi Eliezer* made this ruling only regarding *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — *ho'il v'ne'emar kares bo k'be'kavua* — because among the three *oleh v'yored* sins, *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* is the only one that shares the structure of a standard *korban chatat*: it carries a *chiyuv kares* for willful violation. Had it not been incorporated into *oleh v'yored*, it would have been a regular *chatat*. Because of this structural kinship with *chatat*, *Rabbi Eliezer* reasons that when the *nasi* is obligated for this sin, he brings his standard *se'ir* rather than the variable *oleh v'yored* offering.

## Tum'at Mikdash Issue (II)

### Rav Huna Resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לָא קַשְׁיָא. כָּאן – בְּדַלּוּת, כָּאן – בְּדַלֵּי דַלּוּת. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בַּחֲדָא וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא: סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּדַלֵּי דַלּוּת דְּפָטוּר, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בְּדַלּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** If so, these two passages are **difficult,** as they contradict **one another.** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** This is **not difficult,** as there is a distinction between the rulings. **Here,** in the passage that deems the anointed priest liable in cases other than the defiling the Temple, it is **in** the case of an offering brought due to **poverty,** whereas **there,** in the latter clause, it is **in** the case of an offering brought due to **extreme poverty. And Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva with regard to one** *halakha* **and disagrees with him with regard to one** other *halakha*. **He holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva in** the case of **extreme poverty that** the anointed priest is **exempt** from bringing that meal-offering. **And he disagrees with** Rabbi Akiva **in** the case of **poverty,** as he does not hold that the anointed priest is completely exempt from bringing a sliding-scale offering.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua resolves the internal contradiction in the *braita*: *kan bidalus, kan bidlei dalus* — one statement speaks of the middle tier (*dalus*, the bird), and the other speaks of the lowest tier (*dallei dalus*, the *mincha*). The first clause, which says the *kohen gadol* is in the *oleh v'yored* category except for *tum'at mikdash*, refers to the bird and the large animal. The second clause, which categorically excludes the *kohen gadol*, means he is excluded from the *mincha* tier. The contradiction dissolves: the *kohen gadol* participates in *oleh v'yored* at all levels except the *mincha* — and, additionally, for the sin of *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* he is not liable even at the levels he could in principle bring.

### Rav Shimon's View

Rav Shimon agrees with *Rabbi Akiva* in one dimension and disagrees in another. He agrees — *sevira lei k'Rabbi Akiva bidlei dalus depatur* — that the verse *zeh korban Aharon u'vanav* excludes the *kohen gadol* from ever being obligated in a *mincha* as part of *oleh v'yored*. Where he diverges is in scope: *Rabbi Akiva* held that exclusion from the *mincha* tier entails exclusion from all tiers; *Rav Shimon* holds that the *kohen gadol* is excluded from the *mincha* but remains obligated at the other levels — save for *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*, from which he is separately excluded for independent reasons.

## Tum'at Mikdash Reaso (II)

### Mitoch HaKahal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מָשִׁיחַ חַיָּיב כּוּ׳. אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנִכְרְתָה הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַהִיא מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״, מִי שֶׁקׇּרְבָּנוֹ שָׁוֶה לַקָּהָל. יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ שָׁוֶה לַקָּהָל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Shimon says: **But** an anointed **High Priest is not liable** for the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods. **Ḥizkiyya said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon?** It is **as it is written** with regard to one who becomes ritually impure and enters the Temple: "And a man who shall be impure, and shall not be purified, **that soul shall be excised [*venikhreta*] from the midst of the congregation,** because he has defiled the Sanctuary of the Lord; the water of sprinkling has not been sprinkled on him: He is impure" (Numbers 19:20). It is derived from this verse that this *halakha* applies specifically to **one whose offering equals** the offering **of the congregation,** i.e., the Jewish people. This serves to **exclude** the High Priest, **as his offering does not equal** the offering **of the congregation,** as on Yom Kippur he brings a bull for his unwitting transgression, while he brings a goat to achieve atonement for the Jewish people.

Why is the *kohen gadol* specifically excluded from *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*? Chizkiyah provides the derivation: *venichresa ha-nefesh ha-hi mitoch ha-kahal* — "that soul shall be cut off from within the congregation." The phrase *mitoch ha-kahal* teaches that the *oleh v'yored* obligation for *tum'at mikdash* applies only to *mi she-korbano shaveh la-kahal* — one whose *korban* is the same as the congregation's. *Yatza zeh she-ein korbano shaveh la-kahal* — the *kohen gadol* is excluded because his *korban* for *tum'ah* differs from the community's. On *Yom Kippur*, the *Torah* emphasizes *vekiper al ha-kodesh mi-tum'ot Bnei Yisrael* — the bull and the goat atone for the *tum'ah* of the community, while the *kohen gadol*'s atonement for *tum'ah* constitutes a separate *korban* with a distinct structure. Since on *Yom Kippur* — the paradigmatic day of atonement for *tum'at mikdash* — the *kohen gadol* and the *kohanim* stand in a categorically different relationship to *tum'ah* than the rest of the community, the verse's limiting phrase *mitoch ha-kahal* excludes them from the *oleh v'yored* obligation for this sin.

### Challenge to Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, נָשִׂיא נָמֵי – אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ שָׁוֶה לַקָּהָל! שָׁוֶה בְּכַפָּרָה דְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים. אִם כֵּן, כֹּהֲנִים נָמֵי לֹא שָׁווּ לַקָּהָל בְּכַפָּרָה דְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים! כֹּהֲנִים שָׁווּ לַקָּהָל בִּשְׁאָר מִצְווֹת דְּשָׁנָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, a king too,** should be exempt, as **his offering does not equal** the offering **of the congregation,** as he brings a goat. The Gemara answers: Even so, the king **equals** the congregation **in the atonement of Yom Kippur,** as his atonement is achieved by means of the same offerings through which the rest of the congregation achieves atonement. The Gemara asks: **If so, priests too** should be exempt from bringing the offering for the defiling of the Temple, as they **do not equal the congregation in the atonement of Yom Kippur,** as their atonement is achieved by means of the bull of the High Priest. The Gemara answers: **Priests equal the congregation with regard to** atonement for **the rest of the mitzvot of the entire year.**

The *Gemara* challenges immediately: if the criterion is that the *nasi*'s *korban* differs from the community's, then the *nasi* also brings a different *korban* for a standard sin — a *se'ir* rather than a *kesba* or *se'irah*. So why isn't the *nasi* also excluded from *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*?

## Rabbi Eliezer Rule (II)

### Rav Papa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר עַל כּוּלְּהוֹן קָאָמַר, מִכְּדִי שְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא וּפַר מָשִׁיחַ בִּמְקוֹם יָחִיד לְחַטָּאת קָאֵי. נִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: מָשִׁיחַ מֵבִיא פַּר בִּשְׁמִיעַת קוֹל וּבִטּוּי שְׂפָתַיִם. אֶלָּא מִדְּלָא קָתָנֵי מָשִׁיחַ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַטּוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו קָאֵי, דְּמָשִׁיחַ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: So too, it is reasonable, as if it enters your mind** to say that **Rabbi Eliezer says** that the king brings a goat **for all** of the transgressions enumerated in the mishna, and the king brings a goat in cases where individuals bring a sliding-scale offering, then **since the goat of a king and the bull of an anointed** priest **stand in place of** liability of **an individual to** bring **a sin-offering, let** Rabbi Eliezer **also teach: An anointed** priest **brings a bull for hearing of a voice and** for **an utterance of the lips. Rather, from** the fact **that** Rabbi Eliezer **does not teach** this *halakha* with regard to **an anointed** priest, **learn from it** that his statement that the king brings a goat **stands** in reference only **to the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, with regard to **which an anointed** priest is **exempt,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

Rav Papa raises a parallel question regarding *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling that the *nasi* brings a *se'ir* when obligated under *oleh v'yored* for *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*. If the logic is that a person brings their standard *chatat* offering when this *oleh v'yored* sin is involved, then by the same reasoning, the *kohen gadol* should bring a *par* when he is similarly obligated. The structural parallel is clean: just as the *nasi* substitutes his standard *se'ir* for the sliding-scale animal, the *kohen gadol* should substitute his standard *par*.

### Why No Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אַכּוּלְּהוֹן קָאֵי, וּבְמָשִׁיחַ סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: מָשִׁיחַ פָּטוּר בְּכוּלָּן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מִי פָּטַר לֵיהּ מִפַּר? וְתוּ לָא מִידִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa: From where** do you prove this? **Perhaps** the statement of **Rabbi Eliezer stands** in reference **to all of them. And with regard to an anointed** priest, **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says: An anointed** priest **is exempt from all of** the cases where one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering. Rav Pappa **said to him: And Rabbi Akiva, does he exempt** an anointed priest **from** bringing **a bull?** Rabbi Akiva exempted him only from bringing a sliding-scale offering, but he holds that the High Priest is liable to bring the offering unique to him, the bull for an unwitting transgression he performed on the basis of his own erroneous ruling. **And nothing more** need be discussed.

The very fact that *Rabbi Eliezer* did not extend his ruling to the *kohen gadol* demonstrates, by *diyuk*, that he was indeed speaking only of *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — and even then, only in the case of the *nasi*, because the *kohen gadol* is entirely exempt from *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*. *Shma mina tumat mikdash v'kodashav ka'i de'mashiach patur* — the silence regarding the *kohen gadol* proves both that *Rabbi Eliezer*'s rule is limited to this sin and that the *kohen gadol* does not bring for it at all.

### Asham Talui Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם. תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אָשָׁם תָּלוּי בָּא עַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דַּאֲמַר לָךְ מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֱמַר בּוֹ כָּרֵת כְּבִקְבוּעָה, מַיְיתֵי נָשִׂיא שָׂעִיר עָלֶיהָ. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Eliezer concedes that** a king **does not bring a guilt-offering** for the defiling of the Temple or its sacrificial foods. **The *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **recited** a *baraita* **before Rav Sheshet:** In the case of a king, **a provisional guilt-offering comes for the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods. Rav Sheshet **said to him: Who said** this **to you? Is it Rabbi Eliezer, who said: Since *karet* is stated in its** regard **as** it is stated **in** all matters where there is liability to bring **a fixed** sin-offering, **a king brings a goat for** the defiling of the Temple? Since the status of his offering is like that of a fixed sin-offering, in cases of uncertainty, he is liable to bring a provisional guilt-offering. Rav Sheshet asks: **But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Rabbi Eliezer concedes that** a king **does not bring** a provisional **guilt-offering?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, based on the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan, the *baraita* is **difficult.**

A *tana* taught a *braita* before Rav Sheshet: *asham talui ba al tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — one brings an *asham talui* in a case of doubt regarding *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*. Rav Sheshet responds that this makes sense according to *Rabbi Eliezer*, who treats *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* as structurally equivalent to a standard *chatat* — since *kares* applies to its willful violation and the sin has the architecture of a fixed obligation. If one follows *Rabbi Eliezer*'s principle that this sin is *k'be'kavua*, it is logical that an *asham talui* would be brought in a case of doubt, just as it would for any *chatat kavua*. *Rabbi Yochanan*, however, had limited *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position: *modeh Rabbi Eliezer she-ein meivi asham talui* — *Rabbi Eliezer* would concede that an *asham talui* is not brought. In *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading, *Rabbi Eliezer* is making a narrow ruling about which animal the *nasi* brings — not a wholesale reclassification of *tum'at mikdash* as a standard *chatat*. This *braita* presents a difficulty for *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position.

## Tum'at Mikdash Reaso (II)

### Rava's Reframe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשִׁיחַ נָמֵי, הָא שָׁוֶה בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת דְּשָׁנָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, אֵימָא הָכִי: מִי שֶׁחַטָּאתוֹ שָׁוָה לִיחִידִים, וּמַאי נִיהוּ קָהָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The **anointed priest, too, equals** the congregation **with regard to** atonement for **the rest of the mitzvot of the** entire **year. Rather, Rava said: Say this: One whose sin-offering equals** that of **individuals. And who are** these individuals? They are the **congregation.** The status of a congregation that performed an unwitting transgression not on the basis of the ruling of the court is that of individuals. The High Priest's sin-offering is different, as he brings a sin-offering only for an unwitting transgression he performed on the basis of his own ruling.

Rava reframes the entire reasoning behind the *kohen gadol*'s exclusion from *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav*. Rather than grounding it in the *Yom Kippur* atonement structure and the distinction between the *kohen gadol*'s *korban* and the community's *korban*, Rava focuses on the nature of the *sinner*: *mi she-chataso shaveh li-yachidim u'mai nihu kahal* — the verse *mitoch ha-kahal* refers to one whose sin is equivalent to that of a private individual. A private individual's sin is triggered by *shiggas ma'aseh* — inadvertent action alone. The *kohen gadol*, by contrast, achieves liability through a combination of *hora'ah* and *shiggas ma'aseh*. He is never, even when sinning privately, treated as a pure *yachid*. His entire identity is constituted by his public role — he does not leave the *mikdash* even to mourn his own parents — and therefore he never occupies the position of an ordinary private individual. Since the *oleh v'yored* obligation for *tum'at mikdash* attaches specifically to one who qualifies as a *yachid* in the full sense, the *kohen gadol*, who never qualifies as a *yachid*, is excluded.

The *Gemara* notes that this reasoning, if taken to its logical conclusion, would exclude all *kohanim*, not only the *kohen gadol*, from *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* obligations — since *kohanim* also bring a distinct *korban* on *Yom Kippur*. The response is that *kohanim* are otherwise participants in the communal framework: when *Beit Din* rules and the community follows, *kohanim* are part of that community. But the *kohen gadol* stands apart even there — he is separate both in his *tum'ah* atonement and in his status vis-à-vis ordinary sin. The *Gemara* presses again: but the *kohen gadol* also participates in communal sin when *Beit Din* rules and the community acts. The discussion remains unresolved on precisely why *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* in particular is the locus of his exclusion rather than other categories.

## Deposed Leader Korban

### Sin While in Office

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁחָטָא וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָבַר מִמְּשִׁיחוּתוֹ, וְכֵן נָשִׂיא שֶׁחָטָא וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָבַר מִגְּדוּלָּתוֹ – כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ מֵבִיא פַּר, וְהַנָּשִׂיא מֵבִיא שָׂעִיר. מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁעָבַר מִמְּשִׁיחוּתוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ חָטָא, וְכֵן הַנָּשִׂיא שֶׁעָבַר מִגְּדוּלָּתוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ חָטָא – כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ מֵבִיא פַּר, וְהַנָּשִׂיא כְּהֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **an anointed priest who sinned** on the basis of his own erroneous halakhic ruling **and thereafter moved on from his anointment,** e.g., if he was disqualified due to a blemish that befell him before he brought his sin-offering, **and likewise** in the case of **a king [*nasi*] who sinned and thereafter moved on from his prominence** before he had brought an offering, **an anointed priest brings a bull** despite the fact that he is no longer the High Priest, **and the king brings a goat,** as he would have done during his reign. In the case of **an anointed** priest **who moved on from his anointment and thereafter sinned, and likewise the king who moved on from his prominence and thereafter sinned, an anointed priest brings a bull,** which he would have brought while he was High Priest, **and the** status of the **king** is **like** that of **a commoner [*kehedyot*].**

*Kohen mashiach she-chata ve-achar kach avar mi-meshichuso* — a *kohen gadol* who sinned while in office and was subsequently removed from his position: *kohen mashiach meivi par* — he brings a *par*, in accordance with his status at the time of the sin. Similarly, *ve-chen nasi she-chata ve-achar kach avar mi-gedulaso* — a *nasi* who sinned as *nasi* and then was deposed brings a *se'ir*. The governing principle is that the *korban* follows the offender's status at the moment of the transgression, not his status at the time of atonement.

### Sinned After Deposed

*Mashiach she-avar mi-meshichuso ve-achar kach chata* — a *kohen gadol* who was first removed from office and only then sinned: *kohen mashiach meivi par* — he still brings a *par*, as though he remains a *kohen gadol*. *Ve-ha-nasi ke-hedyot* — but a *nasi* who was first deposed and then sinned brings as a commoner. This asymmetry is striking. The *kohen gadol*, even after being removed from his position, retains his *korban* status indefinitely. The *nasi* does not. The *kohen gadol* never fully loses his elevated halachic category; the *nasi*'s category is entirely contingent on his holding office.

### Stylistic Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַשְׁתָּא יֵשׁ לוֹמַר: עָבַר מִמְּשִׁיחוּתוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ חָטָא מֵבִיא פַּר, חָטָא וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָבַר מִמְּשִׁיחוּתוֹ צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי גַּבֵּי נָשִׂיא, דְּכִי עָבַר מִנְּשִׂיאוּתוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ חָטָא – כְּהֶדְיוֹט מַיְיתֵי, אַהָכִי תָּנֵי גַּבֵּי מָשִׁיחַ [חָטָא וְאַחַר כָּךְ עָבַר] מֵבִיא פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the formulation of the mishna: **Now it can be said:** An anointed priest **who moved on from his anointment and thereafter sinned brings a bull.** Does the case of one who **sinned and thereafter moved on from his anointment need to be said?** It is obvious that he is liable to bring a bull. The Gemara answers: **Due to** the fact **that** the *tanna* **teaches** the *halakha* **with regard to a king, that when he moved on from his sovereignty and thereafter sinned he brings** an offering **like** that of **a commoner; therefore, he teaches** the corresponding *halakha* **with regard to an anointed** priest: If **he sinned and thereafter moved on** from his priesthood, **he brings a bull.**

The *Gemara* observes that the first ruling — that one who sinned while in office and was then deposed brings the elevated *korban* — is technically superfluous once the second ruling is stated. If a *kohen gadol* retains his *par* obligation even when he sins after being deposed, it is self-evident that he retains it when he sinned before being deposed. The *Gemara* defends the inclusion on stylistic grounds: since by the *nasi* there is a meaningful contrast — sinning while in office triggers a *se'ir*, while sinning after deposition triggers the commoner's *korban* — the *mishnah* presents the *kohen gadol*'s case in the same two-part structure for parallel clarity, even though the first clause is logically subsumed by the second.

### Ve'Hikriv Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל חַטָּאתוֹ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמֵּבִיא חַטָּאתוֹ מִשֶּׁעָבַר,
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the statement in the mishna concerning an anointed priest who sinned after he was removed from his position, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: It is **as the Sages taught:** It is written with regard to the High Priest: **"And he shall sacrifice for his sin [*ḥattato*]** that he sinned" (Leviticus 4:3); this **teaches that he brings his sin-offering [*ḥattato*]** even **after he has moved on** from his priesthood.

*D'tano rabbanan, ve-hikriv al chataso* — the *Gemara* identifies a *Torah* source for the *kohen gadol*'s retained status: the verse states *ve-hikriv al chataso*, he shall bring *upon* his sin — language oriented toward the past transgression. This past-oriented language is interpreted as *melamed she-meivi chataso mi-she-avar* — it teaches that he brings for a sin committed even under his former status. How exactly the past-tense formulation generates this ruling is not entirely transparent, but the *Gemara* takes it to mean that whatever category of *korban* was his at the time of the sin — or at the time of his *kohen gadol* status — remains the category of his atonement.

### Kal V'Chomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה נָשִׂיא שֶׁמֵּבִיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה – אֵין מֵבִיא חַטָּאתוֹ מִשֶּׁעָבַר, מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה – [לֹא] כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל חַטָּאתוֹ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמֵּבִיא עַל חַטָּאתוֹ מִשֶּׁעָבַר.
>
> **Translation:** This derivation is necessary, as one **might** have thought: **Could this not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference? And if a king, who brings** a goat as his sin-offering **for the unwitting** performance **of an action, does not bring** a goat as **his sin-offering from** the moment **that he has moved on** from his sovereignty, **an anointed** priest, **who does not bring** a sin-offering **for the unwitting** performance **of an action** alone; **rather,** he is liable **only for absence** of awareness **of the matter** by the court together **with unwitting** performance **of an action,** is it **not all the more so** that he will not bring a bull for his sin-offering once he has moved on from the High Priesthood? Therefore, **the verse states: "And he shall sacrifice for his sin [*ḥattato*]** that he sinned"; this **teaches that he brings his sin-offering [*ḥattato*]** even **after he has moved on** from his priesthood.

*Sheyachol velo dino* — one might attempt a *kal v'chomer* in the opposite direction: if the *nasi*, whose threshold for bringing a *korban* is relatively low — mere *shiggas ma'aseh* alone suffices — nevertheless loses his elevated *korban* status once deposed, then the *kohen gadol*, whose threshold is far higher — requiring both *hora'ah* and *shiggas ma'aseh* — should certainly lose his status once deposed, since the conditions that gave rise to that special *korban* are no longer present. The *Torah* therefore states explicitly *ve-hikriv al chataso* to override this *kal v'chomer*: the *kohen gadol* brings for his sin, even after his role has ended. The *kal v'chomer* would have led us to the wrong conclusion — that the *kohen gadol*'s stricter threshold means his status is more contingent, not less. The verse corrects this by anchoring his *korban* to the sin itself, not to his current office. It presumably follows that the conditions for bringing — including the requirement of a *hora'ah* — likewise attach to his former status; his *korban* as a past *kohen gadol* still demands the framework that made him a *kohen gadol*, even if he no longer holds the title.
