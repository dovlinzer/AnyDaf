# Bava Kamma 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Land Grades & Creditor Rava's Choice Rule

### Rava's Choice Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רְאוּבֵן שֶׁמָּכַר כׇּל שְׂדוֹתָיו לְשִׁמְעוֹן, וְהָלַךְ שִׁמְעוֹן וּמָכַר שָׂדֶה אַחַת לְלֵוִי, וּבָא בַּעַל חוֹב דִּרְאוּבֵן; רָצָה – מִזֶּה גּוֹבֶה, רָצָה – מִזֶּה גּוֹבֶה. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דִּזְבַן בֵּינוֹנִית,
>
> **Translation:** In a related ruling, **Rava says:** In the case of **Reuven, who sold all of his fields to Shimon** simultaneously **and Shimon proceeded to sell one field** of those purchased **to Levi, and Reuven's creditor came** to collect the debt, if the creditor so **desires, he collects from this** one, i.e., Shimon, and if **he** so **desires, he collects from that** one, i.e., Levi. **And we said this only** in a case **where** Levi **purchased intermediate-quality** land from Shimon. A creditor's right is to collect the debt from intermediate-quality land. Accordingly, in this case, Reuven's creditor can collect his loan from the intermediate-quality land even after it has been transferred into Levi's possession. Nevertheless, if he so desires, he may insist on collecting his loan from any inferior-quality land left in Shimon's possession. This is because a creditor also has the right to demand payment from a borrower's inferior-quality land instead of his intermediate-quality land, and therefore that land is also liened to the loan.

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion — begun on 8b — concerning the grades of land to which various claimants are entitled: the person who suffered damages (*nezek*) collects from the best quality, *idis*; the *bal chov*, the creditor, collects from middle-quality land, *beinonis*; and for a *kesuvah*, the wife receives the lowest grade, *ziboris*. Having established those entitlements, the *gemara* now turns to a more complex scenario involving chains of purchasers.

Rava opens with a case: Reuven sold all of his fields to Shimon, and Shimon in turn sold one of those fields to Levi. Reuven's creditor then arrives to collect. Presumably the creditor would go first to Reuven himself, but if Reuven no longer holds any *beinonis* land, the creditor must look elsewhere — either to Shimon or to Levi. The question is whether Levi can deflect the creditor by insisting that he must first go to Shimon, the earlier purchaser, or whether the creditor may simply choose whichever party is most convenient.

Rava's ruling is stated tersely: *ratza mi-zeh goveh, ratza mi-zeh goveh* — if he wishes, he collects from this one; if he wishes, he collects from that one. The creditor, having been unable to collect from Reuven directly, appears free to go to either Shimon or Levi at his discretion.

### Levi's Defense (Qualif.)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל זְבַן עִידִּית וְזִיבּוּרִית – לָא, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְהָכִי דְּיַיקִי וּזְבַנִי עִידִּית וְזִיבּוּרִית, אַרְעָא דְּלָא חַזְיָא לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if Levi **purchased superior-quality and inferior-quality** land from Shimon, **no,** the creditor can collect only from Shimon's intermediate-quality land, **as** Levi could **say to him: For this** reason **I was particular to purchase superior-quality and inferior-quality** land, which is **land that is not fit for you** to collect from as a creditor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ זְבַן בֵּינוֹנִית נָמֵי, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא שַׁיַּיר בֵּינוֹנִית דִּכְווֹתַיהּ, דְּלָא מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: הִנַּחְתִּי לְךָ מָקוֹם לְגַבֵּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֲבָל שַׁיַּיר בֵּינוֹנִית דִּכְווֹתַיהּ גַּבֵּי שִׁמְעוֹן – לָא גָּבֵי מִינֵּיהּ, דְּמָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: הִנַּחְתִּי לְךָ מָקוֹם לִגְבּוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** Levi **purchased** the **intermediate-quality** land from Shimon **as well, we said** that Reuven's creditor can collect from him **only** in a case **where he did not leave** some **similar intermediate-quality** land in Shimon's possession, **as** in that case, Levi **is unable to say to** the creditor: **I left a place for you,** i.e., a plot of land, **in Shimon's possession,** from which to collect. **But** where Levi **did leave** some **similar intermediate-quality** land **in Shimon's possession,** the creditor **does not collect from** Levi, **as** Levi **is able to say to him: I left a place for you from which to collect.**

What follows is a lengthy Aramaic passage that substantially qualifies the apparent sweep of Rava's ruling. The first qualification: this freedom of choice applies *only* if the field that Levi purchased is itself *beinonis*-quality land — the grade to which the creditor is entitled. If Levi purchased *idis* and *ziboris* — that is, first-class and third-class land — but no *beinonis*, Levi can turn the creditor away entirely. He is able to say: *lehachi deiki vezavni idis veziboris* — "I made a point of purchasing only A-grade and C-grade land precisely so that you, as a *bal chov*, would have no claim against me." Since the creditor is entitled to *beinonis* and Levi has none, the creditor cannot simply step down a grade and demand *ziboris* from Levi as a convenient substitute; he must go to Shimon.

The next qualification is even more surprising, and represents the real *chiddush* of this passage. Even if Levi did purchase *beinonis* land — so that, in principle, the creditor's entitlement matches what Levi holds — the creditor still cannot bypass Shimon if Shimon also retains *beinonis*-quality land of comparable value. In that case, Levi can tell the creditor: *hinachti lecha makom ligvos mi-Shimon* — "I left you a place to collect from Shimon." The earlier purchaser has priority. Only if Shimon does not hold *beinonis* — only then may the creditor proceed to Levi.

This amounts to a near-complete reversal of what Rava's initial statement seemed to imply. Where *ratza mi-zeh goveh, ratza mi-zeh goveh* suggested genuine discretion for the creditor, the Aramaic qualification reveals that the creditor's choice is quite constrained: he must exhaust prior-level purchasers before proceeding to subsequent ones, and Levi can always protect himself by pointing to Shimon's available *beinonis* as the proper source of collection.

The picture that emerges is a clear ordering of priority: the creditor must first pursue Reuven; failing that, he must pursue the first-level purchasers from Reuven; and only when none of them holds the appropriate grade of land may he turn to second-level purchasers like Levi.

### Achrayus & Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רְאוּבֵן שֶׁמָּכַר שָׂדֶה לְשִׁמְעוֹן בְּאַחְרָיוּת, וַאֲתָא בַּעַל חוֹב דִּרְאוּבֵן וּטְרַף מִשִּׁמְעוֹן, דִּינָא הוּא דְּאָזֵיל רְאוּבֵן וּמִשְׁתַּעֵי דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ; וְלָא מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו בַּעַל דְּבָרִים דִּידִי אַתְּ, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מַפְּקַתְּ מִינֵּיהּ – עֲלַי הָדַר.
>
> **Translation:** In another related ruling, **Abaye says:** In the case of **Reuven, who sold a field** liened to his creditor **to Shimon with a guarantee,** meaning that Reuven agreed to reimburse Shimon should Reuven's creditor collect the debt from that field, **and Reuven's creditor came and seized** the field **from Shimon, the *halakha* is that Reuven** can **go and litigate with** his creditor and claim that he had already repaid the debt, and insist that the field be returned to Shimon. **And** the creditor **is unable to say to** Reuven: **I am not legally answerable to you,** as I am not taking away your land, since you sold it to Shimon. This is **because** Reuven could **say to him: If you extract** the field **from him,** Shimon **will return to me** and demand that I reimburse him for his loss. Consequently, I am involved in this matter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּאַחְרָיוּת נָמֵי, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא נִיחָא לִי דְּתֶהְוֵי לְשִׁמְעוֹן תַּרְעוֹמֶת עֲלַי.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say: Even** in a case **where** he sold the field **without a guarantee, as well,** in which case even if the creditor seizes Shimon's land there are no legal consequences for Reuven, Reuven can litigate with the creditor, **as** he could **say to him: It is not satisfactory for me for Shimon to have a grievance against me** because he lost the field I sold him on account of the fact that I was unable to pay my debt. Consequently, I am involved in this matter.

Abaye introduces a distinct set of issues. Reuven sold a field to Shimon *be-achrayus* — with a guarantee, which functions as a lien: if some creditor ever seizes that field in satisfaction of Reuven's debts, Shimon holds a lien against Reuven's remaining property and is entitled to reimbursement. This arrangement is what makes the sale feel, from Shimon's perspective, like an insured transaction.

The scenario then unfolds: Reuven's creditor arrives and seizes the field from Shimon. Before Shimon turns to Reuven for reimbursement, the question arises whether Reuven himself has *standing* — the status of a *baal davar*, a party with a stake in the litigation — to intervene in the proceedings between the creditor and Shimon. Can Reuven appear in court alongside Shimon to contest the creditor's claim, or can the creditor say *lav baal devarim didi at* — "this is none of your business"?

The *gemara* rules that Reuven does have standing. He can answer the creditor: *ei mafkat minei alai hadar* — "if you take that field from Shimon, he will turn around and demand reimbursement from me." The downstream financial consequence to Reuven is sufficient to give him a stake in the proceeding.

A second view goes further: *ve-ika de-amrei afilu she-lo be-achrayus nami* — even if the sale was made *without* the *achrayus* guarantee, so that Shimon would have no legal claim for reimbursement, Reuven still has standing. His argument is: *lo nicha li de-leihavei le-Shimon taromas alai* — "I do not wish for Shimon to have a grievance against me." The reputational harm, the fact that Shimon will blame Reuven for his loss, suffices to make Reuven a *baal davar* with standing to appear.

One might ask whether the mere prospect of blame is ever sufficient to confer legal standing, absent a direct financial obligation. The case here is arguably stronger than a pure reputational claim: the creditor is pursuing Shimon *because* of money owed by Reuven, so Reuven is not a stranger to the dispute. It is Reuven's debt that generated the entire proceeding, and it is that underlying connection — compounded by the reputational exposure — that makes his intervention legitimate even without *achrayus*.

## Sale w/o Insurance

### Retraction Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רְאוּבֵן שֶׁמָּכַר שָׂדֶה לְשִׁמְעוֹן שֶׁלֹּא בְּאַחְרָיוּת, וְיָצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ עֲסִיקִין; עַד שֶׁלֹּא הֶחְזִיק בָּהּ – יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ, מִשֶּׁהֶחְזִיק בָּהּ – אֵין יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in another related ruling, **Abaye says:** In the case of **Reuven, who sold a field to Shimon without a guarantee,** **and claimants [*asikin*] come forth about** the ownership of the land, disputing Reuven's prior ownership, **as long as** Shimon **has not yet taken possession** of the land, **he can retract** his agreement to the transaction and is not required to pay for the land. **Once he has taken possession** of the land, **he cannot retract** his agreement to the transaction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: חַיְיתָא דְּקִטְרֵי סְבַרְתְּ וְקַבֵּלְתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that he cannot retract his agreement to the transaction once he has taken possession? **As** Reuven **says to him:** The purchase of the land was like purchasing **a tied bag [*ḥayta*]** whose contents are unknown and might not have any value. Since **you were aware** of **and accepted** that possibility, as you purchased it without a guarantee, you cannot retract your agreement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאֵימַת הֲוַאי חֲזָקָה? מִכִּי דָיֵישׁ אַמִּצְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From when is it** considered that he has **taken possession** of the property? He takes possession **from** the time **that he walks upon the boundaries** of the land to inspect it.

Abaye addresses a second scenario: Reuven sold a field to Shimon *shelo be-achrayus* — without any guarantee of reimbursement. After the sale, title challenges to the field emerged (*yatzu lah iskin*), meaning the field's ownership is now in dispute and Shimon may find himself embroiled in litigation for years. Can Shimon now retract the purchase?

The ruling: *ad shelo hechzik bah, yachol lachzor bah* — before Shimon has performed a *chazakah*, a formal act of taking possession, he may withdraw. *Mishehechzik bah, ein yachol lachzor bah* — once *chazakah* has been performed, he may not. The reason: Reuven can tell him *chaysa dekitri savat vekabalt* — you bought yourself a bag of knots. The idiom signals "buyer beware": the problems that have emerged are simply the risk Shimon assumed by making the purchase, and once *chazakah* is complete, the sale is final.

The question then becomes: what precisely constitutes the *chazakah* that closes the retraction window? The answer given is *michidayesh amitzrei* — when the buyer has walked around the borders of the field.

### Tosafot's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַוְקָא שֶׁלֹּא בְּאַחְרָיוּת, אֲבָל בְּאַחְרָיוּת – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** this is referring **specifically** to a case where the field was sold **without a guarantee. But** where it was sold **with a guarantee** this is **not** so, and Shimon can retract his agreement and refuse to pay for the land even after taking possession of it. The reason is that if the claimants succeed with their claims and seize the land, Reuven will then have to return the sum Shimon paid for it. In order to minimize judicial proceedings, the court allows Shimon to claim that there is no point in paying Reuven now only to have his money returned to him later. Therefore, he can retract his agreement to the transaction.

Rashi understands the *chazakah* here in the conventional sense: a standard act of taking possession of real property. But Tosafot raises two difficulties with this reading. First, what is the *chiddush*? We already know that a sale is finalized only upon *chazakah*. Second, walking the borders is not the standard definition of *chazakah*, which normally requires some productive act performed upon the land itself.

Tosafot therefore offers a different framework entirely. This is not a discussion of whether the sale has been finalized; the sale may already be complete. Rather, there exists a window of time after even a fully concluded sale — analogous in spirit to *toch kedei dibbur*, a brief period during which one can still reverse course — during which the buyer retains the right to withdraw if a significant problem comes to light. That window closes once the buyer has had the opportunity to survey the land by walking its boundaries. The walking of the borders, on this reading, is not the act of acquisition but the outer limit of the retraction period.

A practical parallel: when purchasing a house today, various contingency periods exist even after an agreement has been signed, during which the buyer may withdraw pending inspection, title search, or similar processes. The sale is in some sense binding, yet not irrevocably so until those steps have been completed.

### Achrayus Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחְרָיוּת נָמֵי, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַחְוִי טִירְפָךְ וַאֲשַׁלֵּם לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that **even** where the field was sold **with a guarantee,** Shimon is unable to retract his agreement to the transaction, **as** Reuven has the legal right to **say to him:** First **show me the claim authorization document,** which a court provides to a buyer when the land he purchased is seized from him by a third party who proved that he had a lien on the land, **and** only then **I will pay you.** Consequently, Shimon cannot claim that there is no point in paying Reuven now, after he agreed to the sale but before a third party seized the land, as even if the land will be seized it does not automatically follow that Reuven will have to reimburse Shimon.

The *gemara* then addresses what changes when the sale was made *with* *achrayus*. If Shimon purchased with a guarantee, he would seem to have even stronger grounds to retract during an extended period, since he could argue: if this field ultimately is seized from me, you will owe me reimbursement anyway — so let us simply unwind the transaction now. On this view, the buyer who holds *achrayus* has a longer retraction window than one who does not.

Tosafot limits this position: the extended right of retraction may apply only before the buyer has actually paid for the field. Once payment has been made, the window may close even for a buyer with *achrayus*. The *gemara* itself leaves this boundary uncertain.

A dissenting view — *ve-ika de-amrei afilu be-achrayus nami* — holds that even with *achrayus*, the buyer's retraction window is no broader than without it. Reuven can say to Shimon: *otiv tarfach ve-ashalem lach* — wait until *beit din* actually awards the field to someone else; when it is taken from you, I will pay you back. Until then, Shimon holds the field and must litigate its title himself. The *achrayus* guarantee entitles him to reimbursement after the fact, not to withdrawal in anticipation of a possible future loss.

## Payment Methods

### Rav Huna Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אוֹ כֶּסֶף אוֹ מֵיטַב.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its earlier discussion concerning the form of the payment of damages: **Rav Huna says:** Damages are paid **either** with **money or** with one's best-quality land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב הוּנָא: ״יָשִׁיב״ – לְרַבּוֹת שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף, אֲפִילּוּ סוּבִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman raised an objection to Rav Huna** from a *baraita*: The verse states: **"He shall recompense** money to its owner" (Exodus 21:34), which serves **to include** items **worth money, even bran,** a relatively inferior commodity, as valid forms of payment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּדְלֵית לֵיהּ. אִי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: זִיל טְרַח זַבֵּין, וְאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ כְּסַף; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna replied: **With what are we dealing here** in the *baraita*? We are dealing with a case **where he does not have** any money or land. The Gemara asks: **If he does not have** any other means with which to pay, isn't it **obvious** that he can pay using what he has? The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that **we** would **say to him: Go** to the **trouble** of **selling** whatever commodity you own, **and** thereby **bring him money** as payment for the damage, the *baraita* **teaches us** that it is unnecessary to sell one's possessions and pay with money.

With this discussion concluded, the *gemara* returns to a question raised approximately two *dappim* earlier: how does one pay a debt or a damage award? The tension was between *meitav sadehu* — the rule that damages must be paid with best-quality land — and the verse *kesef yashiv le-va'alav afilu subin* — "he shall return money," which seemed to permit payment even with cheap commodities like bran. The resolution reached earlier was that best land is required when paying with land, but if paying with movable property, any chattel suffices.

Rav Huna now adds a further requirement: *o kesef o meitav* — either top-quality land or cash. In his view, one cannot satisfy a *nezikin* obligation with whatever chattels happen to be available; if you have cash, you must pay cash, and if you have land, it must be the best grade. Rav Nachman challenges this from the verse that permits payment even with bran. Rav Huna resolves the challenge: the *subin* rule applies only *if* the debtor lacks both land and cash. If you genuinely have nothing else, you may pay with whatever you have, because compelling you to sell your assets in order to produce cash would be an unreasonable imposition. But if cash or top-quality land is available, that is what must be tendered.

### Three Categories

Tosafot, through the ruling of Rabbeinu Tam, articulates a tripartite framework that distinguishes among different types of payment obligations.

For *nezikin* — damage awards — the standard is *o kesef o meitav*: either the best-quality land or cash, in accordance with Rav Huna. Only when neither is available does the damaged party receive whatever the tortfeasor has.

For a *bal chov* — an ordinary creditor — the preferred mode of repayment is cash, *le-chatchila*. If the debtor has money, the creditor cannot be compelled to accept land instead, even excellent land; he lent cash and is entitled to cash in return. Only if cash is unavailable may the creditor accept chattels or land.

For a *poel* — a hired worker — the standard is the strictest of all. Even if the employer lacks cash on hand, he cannot pay the worker with goods from his warehouse or whatever else he has. The obligation to workers is specifically a cash obligation, and the employer must sell assets and raise the funds. This is derived from the principle that a worker who has labored all day is entitled to receive actual wages, not to be sent home with a hundred pairs of shoes from the employer's factory.

The hierarchy is striking: for damages, best land or cash; for debts, cash preferred; for workers, cash required absolutely.

### Cash as Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: כְּסָפִים הֲרֵי הֵן כְּקַרְקַע. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא לְעִנְיַן מֵיטַב – הַיְינוּ דְּרַב הוּנָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says: Money is equivalent to land.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** are they equivalent? **If we say** that it is **with regard to** fulfilling the requirement of paying with one's best-quality land, meaning that he can choose to pay with money instead, **this is** precisely the ruling **of Rav Huna;** and yet the ruling of Rav Asi is presented as an independent ruling.

Rav Assi introduces the principle *kesafim harei hein ke-karka* — cash is treated as equivalent to land. The *gemara* asks what the practical *halacha* turns on this formulation. If it merely restates that cash qualifies alongside top-grade land for payment of *nezek*, that is already Rav Huna's position, and Rav Assi adds nothing new. The *gemara* therefore seeks another application — and finds it in the laws governing brothers who divide an estate.

### Bros. Estate Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים שֶׁחָלְקוּ, וְנָטַל אֶחָד קַרְקַע וְאֶחָד כְּסָפִים, וּבָא בַּעַל חוֹב וְנָטַל קַרְקַע; דְּאָזֵיל הַאי, וְשָׁקֵיל פַּלְגָא בִּכְסָפִים בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav Asi is referring **to** a case of **two brothers who divided** their inheritance, **and one took land and** the other **one** took **money, and** subsequently their father's **creditor came and took** the **land,** as it was liened to his loan. Rav Asi's ruling teaches **that this** brother whose land was taken can **go and take a half** share **of the money together with** his brother.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! הַאי בְּרָא וְהַאי לָאו בְּרָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: Isn't that **obvious?** Is **this** one **a son and that** one **not a son?** Since they are both sons and equally inherited the estate of their father, they share responsibility for their father's debts. If the creditor takes repayment of the debt from one brother, the other one must reimburse him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי אַדְּרַבָּה, לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְהָכִי שְׁקַלִי כְּסָפִים, דְּאִי מִגַּנְבִי – לָא מִשְׁתַּלַּמְנָא מִינָּךְ; וּלְהָכִי שָׁקְלַתְּ אַרְעָא – דְּאִי מִטַּרְפָא, לָא מִשְׁתַּלְּמָא לָךְ מִידֵּי מִינַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that the Gemara rejects this explanation not because it is obvious, but **on the contrary,** because it is incorrect. As, one could look at it **from the opposite direction,** saying **that** one brother **could say to** the other: It was **with this** understanding that **I took the money, that if it would be stolen** from me **I would not be reimbursed from your** land, **and** it was **with this** understanding that **you took the land, that if it would be seized** from you by a creditor **you would not be reimbursed from my** money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים שֶׁחָלְקוּ, וּבָא בַּעַל חוֹב וְנָטַל חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav Asi is referring **to** a case of **two brothers who divided** the land they inherited from their father, **and** their father's **creditor came and took** the **portion of one of them.** Since the brothers carry joint responsibility for their father's debt, the one whose portion was seized has a right to be reimbursed by his brother. Rav Asi's ruling teaches that this may be done either by redistributing the remaining portion of land or by reimbursing him with money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַמְרַהּ רַב אַסִּי חֲדָא זִימְנָא – דְּאִיתְּמַר: הָאַחִים שֶׁחָלְקוּ, וּבָא בַּעַל חוֹב וְנָטַל חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן, רַב אָמַר: בָּטְלָה מַחְלוֹקֶת. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: וִיתֵּר. וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: נוֹטֵל רְבִיעַ בְּקַרְקַע, וּרְבִיעַ בְּמָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Asi** already explicitly **say that** ruling **once before?** Therefore, this cannot be the explanation of this ruling. **As it was stated:** In a case of **brothers who divided** the land they inherited from their father, **and** their father's **creditor came and took** the **portion of one of them, Rav says** that the initial **division** of the land is **voided** and any remaining land is divided equally between the brothers. **Shmuel says** that each brother, upon taking his portion, **relinquished [*viter*]** any rights to be reimbursed if his portion were lost. **And Rav Asi says** that the brother whose portion was seized has a right to receive half of the remaining inheritance: **He takes a quarter in land, and** he takes the other **quarter in money.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: בָּטְלָה מַחְלוֹקֶת – קָסָבַר: הָאַחִים שֶׁחָלְקוּ, כְּיוֹרְשִׁים הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the rationale behind each opinion: **Rav says** that the initial **division** of the land is **voided.** This is because **he holds** that **brothers who divided** their inheritance **are** considered to still be **similar to heirs** with respect to the inheritance, and therefore they continue to share joint responsibility for their father's debts. Consequently, any remaining land is divided equally between the brothers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: וִיתֵּר – קָסָבַר: הָאַחִים שֶׁחָלְקוּ לָקוֹחוֹת, וּכְלוֹקֵחַ שֶׁלֹּא בְּאַחְרָיוּת דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says** that each brother, upon taking his portion, **relinquished** any rights to be reimbursed if his portion were lost, as **he holds** that **brothers who divided** their inheritance are considered to be like **buyers** of their respective portions, **and** each one is considered to be **similar to a buyer** who bought his portion **without a guarantee** that would have provided him with a right to be reimbursed if his portion were seized by a creditor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: נוֹטֵל רְבִיעַ בְּקַרְקַע וּרְבִיעַ בְּמָעוֹת – מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי כְּיוֹרְשִׁין דָּמוּ אִי כְּלָקוֹחוֹת דָּמוּ, הִלְכָּךְ נוֹטֵל רְבִיעַ בְּקַרְקַע, וּרְבִיעַ בְּמָעוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Asi says** that the brother whose portion was seized has the right to receive half the remaining inheritance. **He takes a quarter in land and** he takes the other **quarter in money.** This is because **he is uncertain** as to **whether they are similar to heirs,** in which case the remaining land should be redistributed between them, or **whether they are similar to buyers** who bought their portions with a guarantee, in which case the brother whose portion was taken should be reimbursed by his brother with a sum of money equal to the value of the land that was seized from him. **Therefore, he takes a quarter in land and** he takes the other **quarter in money.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַאי ״הֲרֵי הֵן כְּקַרְקַע״ – לְעִנְיַן מֵיטַב.
>
> **Translation:** As Rav Asi had already stated the *halakha* of brothers who divided their inheritance, the Gemara suggests another explanation of his statement. **Rather,** with regard to **what** did Rav Asi state that money **is equivalent to land?** He stated it **with regard to** the requirement of paying with one's best-quality land, and is teaching that one may choose to pay with money instead of land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב הוּנָא! אֵימָא: וְכֵן אָמַר רַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara repeats its original rejection of this suggestion: **If so,** then **this is** precisely the ruling **of Rav Huna,** and yet Rav Asi is presented as an independent ruling. The Gemara resolves the problem: Emend the statement and **say: And similarly, Rav Asi said.**

Two brothers divide their father's estate; one takes land and one takes cash. The father's creditor then arrives and, having liens only against land, seizes the land from the brother who took it. The brother who lost his land now turns to his sibling and demands half the cash — arguing that the seized property came effectively from the estate as a whole and therefore both brothers should share the loss.

The *gemara* initially treats this as obvious: both are heirs; the debt came from the estate; of course the remaining assets should be redistributed. But a competing view inverts the conclusion entirely. The brother who took cash should be able to say: *le-hachi shakli kesafim* — that is precisely why I took cash. By choosing cash I assumed a certain risk — cash can be stolen and I could not ask you to reimburse me — and by choosing land you assumed yours, namely that a creditor might seize it. We made our respective bets; you cannot now demand equalization.

A second version of the case removes this asymmetry: both brothers took land, and the creditor seized one brother's portion while leaving the other's untouched. Here neither party can claim to have assumed a special risk, since both took the same category of asset. This case — where *kesafim harei hein ke-karka*, where the cash in the first scenario is treated analogously to land — is where Rav Assi's principle does genuine work.

A broader dispute among the Amoraim underlies the analysis. Rav holds that even after brothers divide an estate, they retain the status of *yorshim*, heirs, with respect to the land — *batla machloket*, the division is undone and must be redone to account for the seizure. Shmuel holds that division transforms the brothers into *lakochot*, purchasers of one another's shares, and a purchase made without *achrayus* carries no right of reimbursement — *vitar*, the injured brother simply absorbs the loss.

Rav Assi occupies an intermediate position: *mispeka lei i ke-yorshim damu i ke-lakochot damu* — he is uncertain whether the brothers are legally more like heirs or like purchasers. But he reasons that either way the injured brother is entitled to something: if they are heirs, the land should be redistributed; if they are purchasers, the transaction was implicitly *be-achrayus* and reimbursement is owed. Since the mode of reimbursement differs — the *yoresh* model produces redistribution of land, the *lokeach be-achrayus* model produces cash payment — Rav Assi splits the difference: the injured brother receives one quarter in land and one quarter in cash, *notel revia be-karka u'revia be-maot*.

The discussion circles back to confirm that Rav Assi's principle *kesafim harei hein ke-karka* adds nothing beyond what Rav Huna already said about *meitav*, and the *gemara* concludes that the two Amoraim are in agreement: *eima Rav Huna ve-chen Rav Assi*.

## Mitzvah Spending

### One-Third Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּמִצְוָה – עַד שְׁלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav Huna says: For** the purchase of an object with which to fulfill **a mitzva,** one should spend **up to one-third.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁלִישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: To **what** does this **one-third** refer?

The *gemara* now transitions to a topic that, while connected to the theme of monetary obligation, stands somewhat apart from the preceding discussions: how much must one spend to fulfill a *mitzvah*?

Rabbi Zeira cites Rav Huna: *be-mitzvah ad shalish* — when it comes to performing *mitzvot*, one must spend up to one-third. The question is immediately raised: one-third of what? Rashi explains: if a person has an obligation to perform a *mitzvah* — purchasing a *lulav*, acquiring *tzitzit*, writing a *sefer Torah* — he must be willing to spend up to one-third of his net worth (*shalish beito*) in order to fulfill it.

This framing raises an important preliminary point. To avoid transgressing a negative commandment, a *lo ta'aseh*, one must be willing to give up all of one's wealth before violating it. The threshold for a positive commandment, a *mitzvas aseh*, is lower — but one-third of net worth is still a significant sum and not an obviously lenient standard.

An objection is raised: if there are three *mitzvot* to be performed simultaneously, each requiring a third of one's net worth, must one give up everything? Technically, a third of the remainder could always be taken at each step, so it never reaches a hundred percent — but the concern is real. This difficulty leads to a reinterpretation.

### Third Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא שְׁלִישׁ בֵּיתוֹ, אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אִי אִיתְרְמִי לֵיהּ תְּלָתָא מִצְוָתָא – לִיתֵּיב לְכוּלֵּיהּ בֵּיתֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** it means that one should spend up to **one-third of his estate** to perform a mitzva, **but if that is so,** and **if it happened to him** that he became obligated in **three mitzvot** at the same time, **should he give his entire estate** away in order to fulfill those mitzvot? One is certainly not required to do so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּהִידּוּר מִצְוָה – עַד שְׁלִישׁ בְּמִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** what **Rabbi Zeira said** is that **for** the **embellishment** of the performance **of a mitzva,** e.g., to purchase a more beautiful item used in the performance of a mitzva, one should spend **up to one-third** more than the cost **of** the standard item used to perform **the mitzva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁלִישׁ מִלְּגָיו, אוֹ שְׁלִישׁ מִלְּבַר? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** How is this one-third calculated? Is it **one-third from within,** i.e., he calculates the cost of a standard item, adds one-third of that value, and spends the total on purchasing a more beautiful item; **or** is it **one-third from without,** i.e., one-third of the sum he ultimately spends should be the additional sum added in order to purchase a more beautiful item? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Zeira clarifies: the one-third rule applies not to the basic *mitzvah* itself but to *hiddur mitzvah* — the enhanced, beautified performance of the commandment. *Be-hiddur mitzvah ad shalish be-mitzvah*: one must spend up to one-third more than the base cost of fulfilling the *mitzvah* in order to perform it in a *mehudar* fashion.

On Rashi's reading: if a perfectly valid *esrog* costs sixty dollars, one should be prepared to spend up to one-third more — twenty additional dollars — in order to acquire a more beautiful one. The baseline, however, is not necessarily the cheapest kosher option; whether one measures from the minimal-cost item or from a standard-quality item is a question the sources leave unresolved.

### God's Backing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא: עַד שְׁלִישׁ – מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – מִשֶּׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they say in the name of Rabbi Zeira:** Of the money one spends on purchasing a more beautiful item to perform a mitzva, **up to one-third** more than the cost of a standard item comes **from his own** finances, but **from this** point **forward,** any additional sum spent on purchasing a more beautiful item comes **from** the largesse of **the Holy One, Blessed be He,** i.e., God will reimburse him for spending that additional sum.

A tradition cited in the name of Rabbi Zeira adds a dimension that extends beyond the one-third threshold: *ad shalish mishalo, mi-kan ve-eilach mishel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — the first third spent on *hiddur mitzvah* comes from one's own pocket, but beyond that, it comes from God's pocket. This does not mean that the vendor is to be told to collect payment from the Almighty; rather, it is a divine promise of reimbursement, analogous to the principle stated regarding Shabbat expenses, *luvu alai ve-ani poreia* — "borrow on My account and I will repay."

The broader question that emerges is what the obligatory minimum for performing a *mitzvas aseh* actually is. Tosafot rules that one is not required to spend the entirety of one's wealth to fulfill a positive commandment, and even a full third of net worth is more than the law demands in the end. The parallel drawn is to *tzedakah*: the ruling that one should not disperse more than one-fifth of one's assets (*al yevazbeiz yoter me-chomesh*) is invoked by many *poskim* as a ceiling on mitzvah expenditure. The *Rema*, in *Orach Chaim* siman 656 (*taf reish nun vav*), rules that one need not spend an enormous sum to perform a *mitzvah overet*, a time-bound commandment, and compares the limit to that of *tzedakah* — not more than a fifth. He is explicit that this applies to positive commandments, and that for a *lo ta'aseh* one must give up all one's money.

The distinction between income and net worth when applying the one-fifth figure is a significant practical question that the *poskim* do not fully resolve, and the number of a fifth, while frequently cited, represents an upper ceiling rather than a precise minimum obligation.

## Negligence Liability

### Core Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּל שֶׁחַבְתִּי בִּשְׁמִירָתוֹ – הִכְשַׁרְתִּי אֶת נִזְקוֹ. הִכְשַׁרְתִּי בְּמִקְצָת נִזְקוֹ – חַבְתִּי בְּתַשְׁלוּמֵי נִזְקוֹ כְּהֶכְשֵׁר כׇּל נִזְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **anything** for **which I became responsible for safeguarding it** to prevent it from causing damage, if it in fact causes damage, it is considered as if **I** actively **facilitated that damage,** and accordingly I must pay for it. In any case in which **I facilitated part of the damage it** caused, **I am liable for payments** of restitution **for damage it** caused, **as** if I were the **one who facilitated the entire damage it** caused.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְכָסִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן מְעִילָה, נְכָסִים שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל בְּנֵי בְּרִית, נְכָסִים הַמְיוּחָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** One is liable only with regard to damage caused to **property** for **which,** were he to use it for a non-sacred purpose, he **would not be** liable **for** the **misuse** of consecrated property; with regard to damage caused to **property that belongs to members of the covenant,** i.e., Jews; and with regard to **assigned property,** the meaning of which the Gemara will explain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם, חוּץ מֵרְשׁוּת הַמְיוּחֶדֶת לַמַּזִּיק,
>
> **Translation:** **And** one is liable for damage caused **in any place except for a domain designated** exclusively **for** the use of **the one responsible for the damage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק וְהַמַּזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one is liable for damage caused in **a domain** designated for the joint use **of the injured** party **and the one liable for the damage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהִזִּיק חָב הַמַּזִּיק לְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי נֶזֶק בְּמֵיטַב הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** an animal or item one is responsible to safeguard **causes damage, the one liable for the damage** caused by insufficiently safeguarding it **is obligated to pay payments** of restitution **for damage with** his best-quality **land.**

The *gemara* now turns to a new *Mishnah*, one written in an archaic, almost riddle-like style: *kol she-chavti bi-shmirato hichsharti et nizko* — everything I am obligated to watch, if I am negligent, it is as though I have prepared the damage. *Hichsharti be-miktzat nizko chavti be-tashlumei nizko ke-hechsher kol nizko* — even if I only partially prepared the damage, I bear the full liability for it.

The language of this *Mishnah* echoes the closing words of the preceding one — *chav ha-mazik le-shalem* — deploying the same archaic form of *chayav* in connection with the *mazik*, the damager. And yet the *mazik* in these cases has not personally inflicted the harm; his animal or his property did. The *Mishnah* therefore seems to answer directly the foundational question that runs through the opening of *Bava Kamma*: why is the owner of an animal that causes damage called a *mazik*? Why is he personally liable?

The *Mishnah*'s answer is: because his obligation to watch implies his responsibility for any failure to watch. When the damage occurs, it occurs because of his negligence, and the negligence transforms his inaction into a form of action — *hichsharti et nizko*, I prepared the damage. This is not strict liability in the sense of liability without fault; it is rather liability grounded in fault, specifically in the owner's failure to fulfill his *chiyuv she-mira*, his duty of supervision. The *Mishnah* commits clearly to the negligence-based explanation of liability rather than to any strict vicarious reading.

### Entrusting Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּל שֶׁחַבְתִּי בִּשְׁמִירָתוֹ – הִכְשַׁרְתִּי אֶת נִזְקוֹ. כֵּיצַד? שׁוֹר וּבוֹר שֶׁמְּסָרָן לְחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, וְהִזִּיקוּ – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם. מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** a *baraita* that elucidates the mishna's ruling: With regard to **anything** for **which I became responsible for safeguarding it** to prevent it from causing damage, if it in fact causes damage, it is considered as if **I** actively **facilitated that damage** and accordingly must pay for it. **How so?** In the case of **an ox or a pit that one transferred to** the care of **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** whose presumed limited intellectual capacity means they are deemed incapable of sufficiently safeguarding them from causing damage, **and** the ox or pit **caused damage,** the *halakha* is that since the owner of the ox or pit did not fulfill his duty to safeguard them, he is **liable to pay** for the damage, **which is not so in** a corresponding case where the damage is caused by **a fire.**

The *Baraita* elaborates: if one entrusted an ox, or the contents of a pit, to a *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* — a deaf-mute, a fool, or a minor, none of whom are legally competent actors — and damage resulted, the owner is liable. The reasoning follows directly from the *Mishnah*'s principle: having been obligated to watch and having delegated that watch to someone incapable of fulfilling it, the owner has failed his duty. Whatever harm follows is attributable to him.

The *Baraita* then notes *ma she-ein ken be-eish* — fire is an exception, and a person who entrusts fire to a *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* is exempt. This is startling: if the negligence model applies across the board, why should fire be any different?

### Fire Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵּימָא בְּשׁוֹר קָשׁוּר וּבוֹר מְכוּסֶּה, דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי אֵשׁ – גַּחֶלֶת; מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elucidates: **With what are we dealing? If we say** that the *baraita* deals with **a tethered ox or a covered pit,** which cannot cause damage in the manner in which the owner left them, then the case **in the corresponding** situation **of fire,** where he is not liable, is where one transferred **an ember** to one of limited halakhic competence, which, had it been left alone, would not have erupted into a fire and caused damage. This cannot be correct, because if so, **what is different here** in the case of an ox and a pit, where one is liable, **and what is different there** in the case of the fire, that exempts him from liability? There seems to be no reason to differentiate between them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּשׁוֹר מוּתָּר וּבוֹר מְגוּלֶּה, דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי אֵשׁ – שַׁלְהֶבֶת; ״מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּאֵשׁ״ – דְּפָטוּר?! וְהָא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּחִזְקִיָּה: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ גַּחֶלֶת – וְלִיבָּהּ, אֲבָל שַׁלְהֶבֶת – חַיָּיב! מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא בָּרִי הֶזֵּיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* must be dealing with **an untethered ox or an exposed pit,** which can cause damage in the manner in which the owner left them. If so, then the case **in the corresponding** situation **of fire,** where he is not liable, is where one transferred **a flame** to one of limited halakhic competence, which can also cause damage in this form. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: But if so, why does the *baraita* state: **Which is not so** in the case of damage caused **by a fire, as one is exempt** from paying damages? **But** this is untenable, as **didn't Reish Lakish say in the name of Ḥizkiyya: They taught** that one is exempt from damage caused by a fire **only** in a case where **he transferred an ember to** one of limited halakhic competence **who** then **fanned** it into a flame. **But** if he transferred **a flame** to him, the one who transferred the flame to him **is liable** for any damage caused. **What is the reason?** He is responsible **because the** capacity for it to cause **damage is certain.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בְּשׁוֹר קָשׁוּר וּבוֹר מְכוּסֶּה, וְדִכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי אֵשׁ – גַּחֶלֶת. וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ: מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its initial suggestion: **Actually,** the ruling of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to a tethered ox or a covered pit, and** the case **in the corresponding** situation **of fire,** where he is not liable, is where one transferred **an ember** to one of limited halakhic competence. **And** with regard to **that** which **you said** in order to reject this: **What is different here** in the case of an ox and a pit that causes him to be liable **and what is different there** in the case of the fire, that exempts him from liability? Seemingly, in all these cases the item was not able to cause damage in its current form, so he is exempt from liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹר – דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְנַתּוֹקֵי; בּוֹר – דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְנַתּוֹרֵי; גַּחֶלֶת – כַּמָּה דְּשָׁבֵיק לַהּ, מִעְמָיא עָמְיָא וְאָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: You could answer as follows: The typical **manner of an ox is to free** itself from being tethered and the typical **manner of a pit is** for its cover **to become dislodged.** Therefore, as it is negligent to transfer an ox or pit to someone who is incapable of sufficiently safeguarding them, the one who transferred the ox or flame to them is liable. By contrast, with regard to **an ember, as long as he leaves it** alone **it progressively dims,** and the only way in which it will cause damage is if someone else actively fans it into a flame; therefore, he is not liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מָסַר לוֹ שַׁלְהֶבֶת – נָמֵי פָּטוּר, דִּכְווֹתַהּ הָכָא – בְּשׁוֹר מוּתָּר וּבוֹר מְגוּלֶּה; מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says: Even** if the *halakha* is that if **one transferred a flame to** one of limited halakhic competence he **is exempt,** it is possible to say that the case **in the corresponding** situation **here** in the first clause of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to an untethered ox or an exposed pit.** The Gemara asks: But if so, **what is different here** in the case of an ox and a pit that causes him to be liable **and what is different there** in the case of the fire that exempts him from liability? Seemingly, in all these cases the item that was transferred is capable of causing damage in its current form, so he would be expected to be liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם – צְבָתָא דְחֵרֵשׁ קָא גָּרֵים, הָכָא – לָא צְבָתָא דְחֵרֵשׁ קָא גָּרֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **There,** in the case of fire, ultimately **the deaf-mute's handling [*tzevata*]** of the flame **causes** the damage, as if it were not for him moving the flame no damage would have been caused. **Here,** in the case of the ox and the pit, **the deaf-mute's handling** of them **does not cause** the damage. Rather, the damage was caused even though they remained in exactly the same dangerous state in which the one who had transferred them to the deaf-mute did so. Therefore, the damage is a result of that person's negligence, and he is liable.

The *gemara* works through the underlying cases. If the ox was tied and the pit was covered — both hazards in a currently protected state — why is the owner liable when he entrusts them to a *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan*? And what is the corresponding fire case? A *gachelet*, an ember, which is similarly contained. If we are comparing protected hazards to protected hazards, why is the owner liable in one case and exempt in the other?

The *gemara* initially proposes that we are dealing instead with an unbound ox and an exposed pit — active hazards — and the fire parallel is a *shalevet*, a live flame. But this raises the same question in reverse.

The resolution attributed to Reish Lakish in the name of Chizkiyah: *lo shanu ela she-masar lo gachelet ve-libah* — the exemption applies only when one handed over an ember and the *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* fanned it into a flame. In that situation, the escalation from ember to flame was the act — however incompetent — of the watchman, not the owner. But if one handed over a live flame (*shalevet*) and it caused damage, the owner is liable, because the damage was already virtually inevitable from the state in which the fire was handed over: *de-vari heizeika*, the harm was readily foreseeable.

The *gemara* then offers another resolution, reconciling the case where a bound ox and a covered pit generate liability when entrusted to a *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan*, while a *gachelet* under similar conditions does not. The distinction: *shor darcheih lenatukei, vor darcheih lenaturei* — an ox naturally works itself free from its bonds, and a pit cover tends to shift or be dislodged. These hazards require active, ongoing oversight to remain protected; they cannot simply be left alone. An ember, by contrast, naturally burns itself out: *gachelet kama de-shavit lah, ma'amya ma'amya ve-azel* — if left undisturbed, it simply dies down on its own. The need for active supervision is thus present in the first cases and absent in the second.

Rashi and Tosafot differ on the mechanism. Rashi holds that the *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* is guilty of nonfeasance — they fail to do the monitoring that is necessary. By a *gachelet*, no monitoring is necessary, so their incapacity causes no harm. Tosafot holds that the danger lies in the *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* actively undoing the safeguards — playing with the ox until it breaks free, disturbing the pit's cover — whereas they are unlikely to do the same with an ember. The practical difference between the two explanations shapes how one understands the role of the incompetent watchman in each case.

### R. Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan takes the most expansive position: *afilu masar lo shalevet nami patur* — even if one hands a live flame to a *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* and damage results, the owner is exempt. If that is so, the *gemara* asks, then the comparable cases involving the ox and the pit must involve an unbound ox and an exposed pit — genuinely active hazards — and the question presses harder: why the difference?

Rabbi Yochanan's answer is formulated in terms of causation: *hatam tzavta de-cheresh ka garim, hacha lav tzavta de-cheresh ka garim* — in the case of fire, it is the act of the *cheresh* himself that causes the damage; in the case of the ox and the pit, it is not the *cheresh*'s act that causes the harm, but solely the owner's negligence.

This is a striking principle. When a *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* takes a torch and applies it to something, causing a fire, they have performed an act — however incompetent, however lacking in legal culpability — and that human act, interposed between the owner's negligence and the resulting damage, is sufficient to relieve the owner of liability. The fact that the *cheresh* bears no legal responsibility for his own act does not eliminate the causal significance of the act itself. By contrast, when an ox gores or a pit swallows its victim, the *cheresh* did nothing; the damage flows directly from the owner's negligence without any intervening human action.

This is a genuinely novel principle. One might have assumed that only the intervening act of a fully competent *ben da'at* could break the causal chain between an owner's negligence and resulting damage. Rabbi Yochanan holds that even the act of a legally incompetent individual suffices — the human act itself, not the actor's legal status, is what matters.

The *sugya* of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on this point will be addressed more fully in a later section of *Gemara*; here it is introduced only in the context of the *Baraita*. What the *Mishnah* ultimately emphasizes, however, is the centrality of the owner's negligence as the grounding of his liability. The *mazik* is called a *mazik* not because his property mechanically caused harm, but because he — in failing his duty of supervision — effectively brought that harm about.
