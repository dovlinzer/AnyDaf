# Sotah 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Response to Kohanim

### Pesukim During Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים מְבָרְכִים אֶת הָעָם, מָה הֵן אוֹמְרִים?
>
> **Translation:** When the priests are blessing the people, what do the people say?

The *daf* begins at the bottom of 39b, in the midst of the discussion of *birchas kohanim* — one of the rituals that must be performed in Hebrew. The *gemara* poses the question: *bizman shehakohanim mevarchin et ha'am mah hem omrim* — when the *kohanim* bless the people, what do the people say?

Nowadays, *siddurim* print *pesukim* for the congregation to recite silently — a verse corresponding to each word of the blessing — though many people simply scan the text with their eyes. The assumption in the *gemara*, however, is more active: the congregation would say something during the blessing itself, something that relates not merely to each individual word but to the very fact of receiving a divine blessing through the *kohanim*. This is distinct from the *amen* said at the conclusion; what is described here occurs during the blessing itself.

### Shacharis Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: ״בָּרְכוּ ה׳ מַלְאָכָיו גִּבֹּרֵי כֹחַ וְגוֹ׳״, ״בָּרְכוּ ה׳ כׇּל צְבָאָיו מְשָׁרְתָיו עוֹשֵׂי רְצוֹנוֹ״, ״בָּרְכוּ ה׳ כׇּל מַעֲשָׂיו בְּכׇל מְקֹמוֹת מֶמְשַׁלְתּוֹ בָּרְכִי נַפְשִׁי אֶת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira says that Rav Ḥisda says: For each blessing that the priests recite, they respond with a blessing from the Bible: "Bless the Lord, His angels, mighty in strength that fulfill His word, hearkening unto the voice of His word" (Psalms 103:20), "Bless the Lord, all of His hosts, His ministers that do His pleasure" (Psalms 103:21), and "Bless the Lord, all of His works, in all places of His dominion, bless the Lord, O my soul" (Psalms 103:22).

Rabbi Zeira said in the name of Rav Hisda: the congregation recites the closing verses of Psalm 103 — *barechu Hashem malachav gibborei ko'ach*, praise God, His angels of great strength; *barechu Hashem kol tzeva'av meshartav osei retzono*, all His hosts, those who serve God and do His will; *barechu Hashem kol ma'asav bechol mekomos memshalto barchi nafshi et Hashem*, all His creatures in all places of His dominion should praise Him — my soul shall bless God.

The inner logic here is striking: the *kohanim* declare that God will bless the people, and the people respond, as it were, in kind — praising and blessing God in return. God blesses you, and you, in gratitude, call upon all of creation to bless God back.

### Musaf Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמוּסְפֵי דְשַׁבְּתָא, מָה הֵן אוֹמְרִים? אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: ״שִׁיר הַמַּעֲלוֹת הִנֵּה בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳ כׇּל עַבְדֵי ה׳ וְגוֹ׳ שְׂאוּ יְדֵיכֶם קֹדֶשׁ וּבָרְכוּ אֶת ה׳״, ״בָּרוּךְ ה׳ מִצִּיּוֹן שׁוֹכֵן יְרוּשָׁלִָם הַלְלוּיָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** When the priests ascend a second time to bless the congregation during the additional prayer of Shabbat, what do the people say? It is not appropriate for them to repeat the same verses of praise that they recited previously. Rabbi Asi said: They say: "A song of ascents. Behold, bless you the Lord, all you servants of the Lord, that stand in the house of the Lord in the night seasons" (Psalms 134:1), "Lift up your hands in sanctity and bless the Lord" (Psalms 134:2), and "Blessed be the Lord out of Zion, Who dwells at Jerusalem. Hallelujah" (Psalms 135:21).

For *Shabbas Musaf* — an additional *Shemoneh Esrei* — Rabbi Assi states that the congregation recites: *Shir hama'alos hinei barechu et Hashem kol avdei Hashem* (Psalm 134:1), behold, bless God all you servants of God; *se'u yedekhem kodesh uvarechu et Hashem* (ibid. 2), lift up your hands in holiness and bless God; and *baruch Hashem mitziyon shokhen Yerushalayim halleluyah* (Psalm 135:21), blessed is God from Zion, who dwells in Jerusalem, praise God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא נָמֵי ״יְבָרֶכְךָ ה׳ מִצִּיּוֹן״, דִּכְתִיב בְּהָהוּא עִנְיָינָא! אָמַר יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהִתְחִיל בְּבִרְכוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְסַיֵּים בְּבִרְכוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And let them also say the third verse that appears right after the first two blessings: "The Lord shall bless you out of Zion" (Psalms 134:3), as it is written in that same matter. Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, says: Since they began with blessings of the Holy One, Blessed be He, they must end with a blessing of the Holy One, Blessed be He, rather than reciting this verse, which is a blessing for the Jewish people.

The *gemara* notes, however, that the third *pasuk* is taken from a different *perek* in *Tehillim*. Why not use *yevarekhekha Hashem mitziyon*, which appears in the same *perek* as the first two verses? Yehudah the son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi explains: since the theme of the congregational response is blessing God — mirroring the blessing the *kohanim* channel toward the people — one must end with a verse that blesses God. *Baruch Hashem mitziyon* accomplishes this. *Yevarekhekha Hashem mitziyon*, by contrast, speaks of God blessing you — precisely what the *kohanim* are already saying. The response must be a reciprocal blessing directed toward God, not a repetition of what is being received.

One observes, as well, that just as there are three *pesukim* of *birchas kohanim*, so too there are three *pesukim* of response — one corresponding to each.

### Taanis & Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִנְחֲתָא דְתַעֲנִיתָא מַאי אָמְרִי? אָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר יַעֲקֹב: ״אִם עֲוֹנֵינוּ עָנוּ בָנוּ ה׳ עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן שְׁמֶךָ״, ״מִקְוֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל מוֹשִׁיעוֹ בְּעֵת צָרָה לָמָּה תִהְיֶה כְּגֵר בָּאָרֶץ וְגוֹ׳״, ״לָמָּה תִהְיֶה כְּאִישׁ נִדְהָם כְּגִבּוֹר לֹא יוּכַל לְהוֹשִׁיעַ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: When the priests ascend to recite the Priestly Benediction during the afternoon prayer of a fast day, what do the people say? Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: They say: "Though our iniquities testify against us, O Lord, work for Your name's sake" (Jeremiah 14:7), "The Hope of Israel, its savior in times of trouble, why should You be a stranger in the land and as a wayfaring man that turns aside to tarry for a night?" (Jeremiah 14:8), and "Why should You be like a man overcome, as a mighty man who cannot save? Yet You, O Lord, are in the midst of us, and Your name is called upon us; leave us not" (Jeremiah 14:9).

For *mincha* on a *taanis* — when *birchas kohanim* is performed because there is no concern that the *kohanim* have been drinking — Rav Acha bar Yaakov provides a markedly different set of verses: *im avonoseinu anu vanu Hashem aseh lema'an shemekha* (Jeremiah 14:7), though our sins testify against us, God act for the sake of Your name; *mikveh Yisrael moshio be'eis tzarah* (ibid. 8), O Hope of Israel, its savior in time of distress; *lamah tihyeh kger ba'aretz lamah tihyeh khe'ish nidham kegibor* (ibid. 9), why should You be like a stranger in the land, like a mighty man who cannot save?

This is notably different in character from the other responses. Rather than blessing God in return for the blessing received, the congregation uses the occasion as an opening to pray for the very purpose of the *taanis* — to importune God to answer and to act. Receiving a blessing and standing in connection with God, they seize the moment to beseech Him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּנְעִילָה דְּיוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי מַאי אָמַר? אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא: ״הִנֵּה כִי כֵן יְבֹרַךְ גָּבֶר יְרֵא ה׳״. ״יְבָרֶכְךָ ה׳ מִצִּיּוֹן וּרְאֵה בְּטוּב יְרוּשָׁלִָם כֹּל יְמֵי חַיֶּיךָ״. ״וּרְאֵה בָנִים לְבָנֶיךָ שָׁלוֹם עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** During the closing prayer [*ne'ila*] of Yom Kippur, which also includes the Priestly Benediction, what do the people say? Mar Zutra says, and some say that this was taught in a *baraita*: "Behold, surely thus shall the man who fears the Lord be blessed" (Psalms 128:4), "The Lord shall bless you out of Zion, and you shall see the good of Jerusalem all the days of your life" (Psalms 128:5), and "And see your children's children. Peace be upon Israel" (Psalms 128:6).

At *ne'ila* on *Yom Kippur*, Mar Zutra (and some say this appears in a *baraisa*) gives: *hinei ki chen yevorach gever yere Hashem* (Psalm 128:4), behold, so shall the man who fears God be blessed; *yevarekhekha Hashem miTziyon ure'eh betuv Yerushalayim kol yemei chayekha ure'eh vanim levanekha shalom al Yisrael* (ibid. 5–6), God should bless you from Zion and may you see Jerusalem's goodness all the days of your life, and see your children's children — peace upon Israel.

Here, at the close of *Yom Kippur*, the response is neither a return-blessing to God nor a petition for rain. Instead it expresses the aspiration of a *yerei Hashem*, a God-fearing person receiving blessing, looking forward to a year of life and continuity and peace. Of all the responses enumerated, only the standard *shacharis* version — where one blesses God in response to receiving blessing from God — follows the pure structure of reciprocal praise.

### Timing of Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָן אוֹמְרָן? רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בֵּין כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: בְּהַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Where does the congregation say these verses during the Priestly Benediction? Rav Yosef says: They are said between each and every blessing. And Rav Sheshet says: They are said during the mention of the name of God in each of the three blessings.

The *gemara* raises the question of where exactly within *birchas kohanim* these *pesukim* would be recited. Rabbi Elazar says: between each *bracha* — that is, after each of the three *pesukim* of the blessing. Rav Sheshet says: at the mention of God's name — *b'hazkarat Hashem* — since hearing the divine name in the context of this blessing is itself what calls forth the response of praise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב מָרִי וְרַב זְבִיד. חַד אָמַר: פְּסוּקָא לָקֳבֵל פְּסוּקָא, וְחַד אָמַר: אַכֹּל פְּסוּקָא אָמַר לְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Mari and Rav Zevid disagree about this matter. One says: The congregation recites one verse at a time, corresponding to the verse that the priests recite. And one says: For every single verse that the priests recite, the congregation says all three verses.

Rav Mar and Rav Zevid dispute the structure of the response. One holds that it was *pasuka likaval pasuka* — one response-*pasuk* corresponding to each *pasuk* of the blessing. The other holds that upon each *pasuk* of the *kohanim*, the congregation recited all three *pesukim* of the response.

## Only in Mikdash

### Chiya bar Abba View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: כׇּל הָאוֹמְרָן בַּגְּבוּלִין — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: Anyone who recites these verses in the outlying areas, i.e., outside the Temple, is nothing other than mistaken in his practice.

Having established that these *pesukim* were said, the *gemara* turns to whether they may be recited outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba rules: *kol ha'omram bigvulin eino ela to'eh* — whoever recites them outside the *Beis Hamikdash* is simply making a mistake. The rationale is that these verses were a response to the power of hearing the explicit divine name pronounced in the *Mikdash*. Whether one holds that the *pesukim* were said at *shem Hashem* or between the *brachot*, the sense of God's manifest presence bestowing blessing — which invokes this particular response — belongs specifically to the *Mikdash* context.

### Chanina bar Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: תִּדַּע דִּבְמִקְדָּשׁ נָמֵי לָא מִיבְּעֵי לְמֵימְרִינְהוּ, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ לְךָ עֶבֶד שֶׁמְּבָרְכִין אוֹתוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲזִין?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa said: You should know that in the Temple also people should not recite these verses. Do you have a servant who is being blessed and does not listen to the blessing, but rather speaks at the same time?

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa goes further and disagrees more fundamentally. He says: *teda* — know — that not only are they inappropriate outside the *Mikdash*, they should not be said even in the *Mikdash*. His reasoning: *klum yesh lecha eved shemevarchin oto vehu machzir* — is there a slave who, when receiving a blessing, talks back? Even if his words are grateful and responsive, should one not simply listen attentively to the blessing being given? The argument implies that the *pesukim* are said during the blessing itself — during the words of the *kohanim* — which makes Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa's objection coherent. One is supposed to receive the blessing, not be murmuring verses simultaneously.

This framing, however, sits in some tension with Rabbi Elazar's view that the *pesukim* are said between the *brachot*, not during them — and the *gemara* does not fully reconcile these positions.

### Yitzchak bar Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָה בַּר חֲנִינָא: תֵּדַע דְּבִגְבוּלִין נָמֵי מִיבְּעֵי לְמֵימְרִינְהוּ, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ עֶבֶד שֶׁמְּבָרְכִין אוֹתוֹ וְאֵין מַסְבִּיר פָּנִים?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says: You should know that in the outlying areas one is also required to say these verses. Is there a servant who is being blessed and his face does not brighten?

Rav Yitzchak bar Chanina takes the opposite position: *teda* — know — that it is entirely appropriate to say them even outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. His counter-argument mirrors Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa's imagery but inverts it: *klum yesh eved shemevarchin oso ve'ein mekabbel panim* — is there a slave who, when blessed, shows no acknowledgment at all? If one receives a blessing, one ought to demonstrate gratitude and respond in kind. This reasoning aligns naturally with the view that the *pesukim* are said between the *brachot* rather than during them — a moment of response, not of distraction.

### Abahu's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְהוּ, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַבָּא דְּמִן עַכּוֹ דְּלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ — אֲנָא נָמֵי לָא אָמֵינָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu says: At first, I would recite these verses, but since I saw that Rabbi Abba of Akko does not say them, I also do not recite them anymore.

Rabbi Abahu reports: *merish hava amina lehu* — I used to recite these *pesukim* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. But once he observed Rabbi Abba of Akko not saying them, he too stopped. The specific weight Rabbi Abahu gave to this behavior will become clear momentarily, in light of his deep respect for Rabbi Abba as a man of exceptional humility.

### Humility Digression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא עִינְוְתָנָא אֲנָא. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַבָּא דְּמִן עַכּוֹ דְּאָמַר אִיהוּ חַד טַעְמָא וְאָמַר אָמוֹרֵיהּ חַד טַעְמָא וְלָא קָפֵיד — אָמֵינָא לָאו עִינְוְתָנָא אֲנָא.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Abbahu says: At first, I would say to myself that I was humble. Since I saw that Rabbi Abba of Akko himself stated one reason for a matter, and his interpreter stated one other reason of his own rather than delivering the reason that Rabbi Abba stated, and yet Rabbi Abba did not mind, I say to myself that I am not humble.

Rabbi Abahu also relates: I used to think of myself as a humble person — *anvetana ana*. But once I observed Rabbi Abba of Akko's conduct, I realized that I was not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי עִינְוְותָנוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ? דַּאֲמַרָה לַהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּאָמוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ לִדְבֵיתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הָא דִּידַן לָא צְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לְדִידָךְ, וְהַאי דְּגָחֵין וְזָקֵיף עֲלֵיהּ — יְקָרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּעָבֵיד לֵיהּ. אֲזַלָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ וַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: וּמַאי נָפְקָא לִיךְ מִינַּהּ? מִינִּי וּמִינֵּיהּ יִתְקַלַּס עִילָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And what was the humility of Rabbi Abbahu? The Gemara relates that Rabbi Abbahu's interpreter's wife said to Rabbi Abbahu's wife: This one of ours, i.e., my husband, has no need for your husband Rabbi Abbahu, as he could teach everything on his own. And the fact that he bends over to listen to Rabbi Abbahu, and then stands up above him, and repeats his words to the congregants is merely to show respect for him. Rabbi Abbahu's wife went and told this to Rabbi Abbahu. He said to her: And what difference does it make to you? Through me and through him the One above will be exalted, and it does not matter which one of us is teaching.

What was Rabbi Abba's conduct? When Rabbi Abba would convey a teaching to his *amora* — together with the reasoning behind it — the *amora* would then stand up and announce to the assembled crowd his own reason instead of, or in addition to, Rabbi Abba's. This requires some explanation of the role of the *amora*. In the academies, the term *tana* referred not only to the sages of the *Mishnah* and *baraisa* era but to a specific functionary: a person who had memorized all the *mishnayos* and *baraisos* and would recite them upon request — a living repository. Similarly, the *amora* (from *amira*, speech) was a kind of loudspeaker: the rabbi would say his *shiur* quietly, and the *amora* would repeat it loudly so the entire crowd could hear.

What Rabbi Abba's *amora* would do was substitute — or supplement — Rabbi Abba's reasoning with his own. Perhaps he would say: "Rabbi Abba teaches this *halacha*, and here is my reason." Rabbi Abba *lo kapid* — he did not mind at all.

The most plausible reading, in order to reconcile this with the obligation to teach Torah accurately, is that the *amora* announced his own reasoning in his own name — explicitly — and Rabbi Abba allowed him to use the public platform to do so. The humility displayed was not just tolerance of being contradicted, but the willingness to let a subordinate elevate himself to a seemingly equal footing in public.

By contrast, Rabbi Abahu's own humility — which he now recognized as lesser — consisted of the following: the wife of Rabbi Abahu's *amora* told Rabbi Abahu's wife: "Ours doesn't need yours. My husband is capable enough to give his own *shiur* — he bends down to hear Rabbi Abahu and then stands up to announce it only as a gesture of respect, because Rabbi Abahu has connections to the government (*bei Keisar*). But he could manage entirely on his own." Rabbi Abahu's wife went and told him this. His response: *mai nafka lach minha* — what does it matter to you? *Minei uminah yitkales ila'ah* — between me and him, God's name is elevated. He was not *makpid*. Let the man think he gives just as good a *shiur* — who is harmed?

But Rabbi Abahu recognized that this was a private remark, made to a wife — not a public act of self-elevation like what Rabbi Abba's *amora* had done. The degree of humility demanded to truly not mind, as Rabbi Abba of Akko did not mind, was of a different order entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אִימְּנוֹ רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ לְמִמְנְיֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא, כֵּיוָן דְּחַזְיֵהּ לְרַבִּי אַבָּא דְּמִן עַכּוֹ דִּנְפִישִׁי לֵיהּ בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבוֹת, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִיכָּא רַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** And furthermore, in another example of his humility, the Sages were counted and reached a decision to appoint Rabbi Abbahu to be the head of the yeshiva. Since he saw that Rabbi Abba of Akko had many creditors and was impoverished, he attempted to get him out of debt. He said to them: There is a man who is greater than me, Rabbi Abba.

A further story about Rabbi Abahu's regard for Rabbi Abba: when the rabbis considered appointing Rabbi Abahu as *Rosh Yeshiva* — a position that brought with it significant material support — Rabbi Abahu saw that Rabbi Abba of Akko was burdened with many creditors and owed considerable debts. He told the rabbis: there is a greater rabbi — appoint Rabbi Abba of Akko. This would allow Rabbi Abba to pay off his debts through the income that came with the position.

### Jewels & Knickknacks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אִיקְּלַעוּ לְהָהוּא אַתְרָא, רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ דְּרַשׁ בְּאַגַּדְתָּא, רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא דְּרַשׁ בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא. שַׁבְקוּהּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, וַאֲזוּל לְגַבֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶמְשֹׁל לְךָ מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה: לִשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם, אֶחָד מוֹכֵר אֲבָנִים טוֹבוֹת וְאֶחָד מוֹכֵר מִינֵי סִידְקִית, עַל מִי קוֹפְצִין — לֹא עַל זֶה שֶׁמּוֹכֵר מִינֵי סִידְקִית?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another example of his humility: Rabbi Abbahu and Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba happened to come to a certain place. Rabbi Abbahu taught matters of *aggada*, and at the same time Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba taught *halakha*. Everyone left Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba and went to Rabbi Abbahu, and Rabbi Ḥiyya was offended. Rabbi Abbahu said to him, to appease him: I will tell you a parable: To what is this matter comparable? It is comparable to two people, one who sells precious stones and one who sells small items [*sidkit*]. Upon whom do the customers spring? Don't they spring upon the one who sells small items? Similarly, you teach lofty and important matters that do not attract many people. Everyone comes to me because I teach minor matters.

The *gemara* now relates a famous episode directly involving Rabbi Abahu and Rabbi Chiya bar Abba. The two came together to a certain town. Rabbi Abahu delivered a *shiur* in *agadata* — a *midrash*, a sermon, a meaningful message. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba delivered a serious *halacha shiur* with rigorous sources and analysis. Everyone left Rabbi Chiya bar Abba's lecture and went to hear Rabbi Abahu. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba was despondent.

Rabbi Abahu consoled him with a *mashal*: to what may this be compared? To two merchants who come to a village — one selling precious gems and pearls, the other selling small knickknacks, needles, and trinkets. *Al mi koftzin* — to whom does everyone rush? To the one selling the knickknacks, of course, because everyone can buy something small and useful. Nobody can afford the jewels. Your *shiur* is clearly at a far higher level, Rabbi Abahu was saying — your Torah is the jewels. But it is simply beyond these people. They come to me not because my Torah is greater, but because it is more accessible to them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל יוֹמָא: הֲוָה מַלְוֵה רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ עַד אוּשְׁפִּיזֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם יְקָרָא דְבֵי קֵיסָר. הָהוּא יוֹמָא אַלְוְיֵהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא עַד אוּשְׁפִּיזֵיהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא אִיתּוֹתַב דַּעְתֵּיהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that every day Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba would escort Rabbi Abbahu to his lodging place [*ushpizei*] out of respect for the house of the emperor, with which Rabbi Abbahu was associated. On that day, Rabbi Abbahu escorted Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba to his lodging place, and even so, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba's mind was not at ease with Rabbi Abbahu and he felt insulted.

Every day, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba would escort Rabbi Abahu to his lodging — *mishum yekara d'vei Keisar* — as a gesture of honor, since Rabbi Abahu's proximity to the imperial government gave him special status. On that day, in recognition of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba's true greatness in Torah, Rabbi Abahu reversed the gesture and escorted Rabbi Chiya bar Abba to his lodging instead. And even so, *lo itosev da'tei minei* — it did not fully settle Rabbi Chiya bar Abba's spirit. Being told that one possesses extraordinary Torah is gratifying, but it does not entirely substitute for feeling that people want to hear from you.

## Modim Response

### Modim Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁשְּׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר אוֹמֵר מוֹדִים, הָעָם מָה הֵם אוֹמְרִים? אָמַר רַב: ״מוֹדִים אֲנַחְנוּ לָךְ ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ עַל שֶׁאָנוּ מוֹדִים לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** While the prayer leader is reciting the blessing of: We give thanks, what do the people say? Rav says that they say: We give thanks to You, Lord our God, for the merit of giving thanks to You.

The *gemara* now transitions naturally: having discussed what the congregation says when receiving *birchas kohanim*, it asks what the congregation says when the *shaliach tzibbur* recites *modim* during *chazaras hashatz*. This is one of the two signature moments of the *chazara* — alongside *kedusha* — where communal participation is most essential. During *kedusha*, the congregation sanctifies God's name together; during *modim*, the congregation echoes the *shaliach tzibbur*'s expression of communal gratitude, each person saying their own *modim* while he says his.

So *mah hem omrim* — what does the congregation say? Rav says: *Modim anachnu lach Hashem Elokeinu al she'anu modim lach* — we thank You, God our Lord, for the fact that we thank You. This is a remarkable formulation. Rashi explains: we thank You for having given us the capacity and the closeness to You that enables us to thank You — gratitude for the very ability to be grateful. In one's own silent *Shemoneh Esrei*, one has already thanked God for all one has prayed for; now, in the repetition, the congregation says: we are so thankful to be a people who stand before You in prayer and are able to offer You thanks.

### Modim Variations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: ״אֱלֹהֵי כׇּל בָּשָׂר, עַל שֶׁאָנוּ מוֹדִים לָךְ״. רַבִּי סִימַאי אוֹמֵר: ״יוֹצְרֵנוּ יוֹצֵר בְּרֵאשִׁית עַל שֶׁאָנוּ מוֹדִים לָךְ״. נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי סִימַאי: ״בְּרָכוֹת וְהוֹדָאוֹת לְשִׁמְךָ הַגָּדוֹל עַל שֶׁהֶחֱיִיתָנוּ וְקִיַּימְתָּנוּ, עַל שֶׁאָנוּ מוֹדִים לָךְ״. רַב אַחָה בַּר יַעֲקֹב מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ הָכִי: ״כֵּן תְּחַיֵּינוּ וּתְחׇנֵּנוּ, וּתְקַבְּצֵנוּ, וְתֶאֱסוֹף גָּלִיּוֹתֵינוּ לְחַצְרוֹת קׇדְשֶׁךָ, לִשְׁמוֹר חוּקֶּיךָ וְלַעֲשׂוֹת רְצוֹנְךָ בְּלֵבָב שָׁלֵם, עַל שֶׁאָנוּ מוֹדִים לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** And Shmuel says that one should say: God of all living flesh, for the merit of giving thanks to You. Rabbi Simai says that one should say: Our Creator, Who created everything in the beginning, for the merit of giving thanks to You. The Sages of Neharde'a say in the name of Rabbi Simai that one should say: We offer blessings and praises to Your great name, for You have given us life and sustained us, for giving thanks to You. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov would finish the blessing as follows: So may You give us life, and show us favor, and collect us, and gather our exiles into Your sacred courtyards, in order to observe Your laws and to fulfill Your will wholeheartedly, for giving thanks to You.

Shmuel elaborates: *Elokei kol basar al she'anu modim lach* — God of all flesh, that we thank You. Rabbi Simai elaborates further: *Yotzreinu yotzer bereishis* — our Creator, Creator of the beginning — *al she'anu modim lach*.

The sages of Nehardea add, in the name of Rabbi Simai, something more concrete: *berachos vehoda'os lishmecha hagadol al shehecheyisanu vekiyamsanu* — blessings and thanks to Your great name for having given us life and sustained us — *al she'anu modim lach*. Now the thanks are no longer purely reflexive; something specific is named: life itself. Rav Acha bar Yaakov then appends a *bakasha* to Rabbi Simai's formulation: *ken techayeinu usechaneinu* — so too may You give us life and be gracious to us — *vetikabetzeinu ve'esof galuyoseinu lechatzros kodshecha* — gather us to Your holy courtyards — *velishmor chukecha vela'asos retzonecha belevav shalem* — to observe Your laws and do Your will wholeheartedly — *al she'anachnu modim lach*.

At this point the formulation has traveled quite far from its origin. The elegant core — *modim al she'anu modim*, thanksgiving for the capacity of thanksgiving — has been expanded into petition, and the *netziv* of *Modim Derabbanan* as commonly recited today includes this fuller material. Something of the purity of that original formulation is worth pausing over.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִילְכָּךְ נֵימְרִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said: These Sages each added a different element to the prayer. Therefore, we should combine them together and recite all of them.

Rav Pappa concludes: since all of these formulations contain beautiful and fitting content, we say all of them together — which is precisely what became our practice.

### Community Respect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם תְּהֵא אֵימַת צִבּוּר עָלֶיךָ, שֶׁהֲרֵי כֹּהֲנִים פְּנֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי הָעָם וַאֲחוֹרֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי שְׁכִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The awe of the public should always be upon you, i.e., one must always treat the public courteously. As when the priests bless the people they face the people and their backs are toward the Divine Presence, out of respect for the congregation.

Rabbi Yitzchak states: a leader should always have *eimas tzibbur* upon him — a sense of reverence for, and obligation toward, the community. The *gemara* derives this from *birchas kohanim*: when the *kohanim* bless the people, their faces are toward the people and their backs are, as it were, toward the *Shechina* — toward where the *Aron* stands, the front of the sanctuary. Such is the degree of respect shown to the congregation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״וַיָּקׇם דָּוִיד הַמֶּלֶךְ עַל רַגְלָיו וַיֹּאמֶר שְׁמָעוּנִי אַחַי וְעַמִּי״. אִם ״אַחַי״ לָמָּה ״עַמִּי״, וְאִם ״עַמִּי״ לָמָּה ״אַחַי״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר לָהֶם דָּוִד לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אִם אַתֶּם, שׁוֹמְעִים לִי — אַחַי אַתֶּם, וְאִם לָאו — עַמִּי אַתֶּם, וַאֲנִי רוֹדֶה אֶתְכֶם בְּמַקֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman said that this principle is derived from here: "Then King David stood up upon his feet, and said: Hear me, my brethren, and my people" (I Chronicles 28:2). Evidently, King David stood up to address the people rather than remain seated. If he said "my brethren," why did he say "my people"? And if he said "my people" why did he say "my brethren"? Rabbi Elazar says: David said to the Jewish people: If you listen to me, you are my brethren. And if you do not listen to me willingly, you are my people and I am your king, and I will rule over you by force with a staff. This shows that if the nation acted properly, David would relate to them respectfully.

Rav Nachman derives the same principle from a verse in Divrei Hayamim: *vayakom hamelech David al raglav vayomer shim'uni achai ve'ami* — King David rose to his feet and said, hear me, my brothers and my people. The *gemara* asks: if they are *achai*, my brothers — his equals — why call them *ami*, my people? And if they are *ami*, his subjects — why call them *achai*? Rabbi Elazar explains: David was saying, if you are prepared to listen to me willingly, then I will treat you as brothers, as equals. *Ve'im lav ami atem va'ani rodeh eschem bemakkel* — and if not, I will treat you as subjects and compel you. The honor and equality offered to the community is thus conditional on their responsiveness — but the baseline posture of a leader toward his community is one of respect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן אָמְרִי מֵהָכָא: דְּאֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים רַשָּׁאִים לַעֲלוֹת בְּסַנְדְּלֵיהֶן לַדּוּכָן. וְזֶהוּ אַחַת מִתֵּשַׁע תַּקָּנוֹת שֶׁהִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages say that the importance of showing respect for the congregation is derived from here: The *halakha* is that the priests are not permitted to ascend the platform to recite the benediction in their sandals, as is taught in a *baraita*. And this *halakha* is one of nine ordinances that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted.

The *rabbanan* then bring this discussion back to *birchas kohanim* with the teaching: *ein kohanim rashaim la'alos besandaleihem laduchan* — *kohanim* may not ascend the *duchan* wearing sandals. This is one of the nine *takanos* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakai.

## Blessing Halacha

### Kohanim Sandals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי טַעְמָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד צִבּוּר? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא, הָתָם שֶׁמָּא נִפְסְקָה לוֹ רְצוּעָה בְּסַנְדָּלוֹ וַהֲדַר אָזֵיל לְמִיקְטְרֵיהּ, וְאָמְרוּ: בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** What is the reason for this ordinance? Is it not out of respect for the congregation, as it would be disrespectful for the priests to display their dirty sandals in front of the congregants? Rav Ashi said: No, this is not the reason. There, in the *baraita*, the reason is a concern lest a strap of his sandal break, and he will therefore return to his place to go tie it and not ascend the platform in time for the benediction, and people will say that he was removed from the platform because he is disqualified from the priesthood, as he is the son of a priest and a divorced woman or the son of a priest and a *ḥalutza*.

The *gemara* asks: what is the reason for this *takana*? The obvious suggestion is *kevod tzibbur* — when the *kohanim* lift their arms and the hem of their garments rises, muddy sandals are exposed, and this is unseemly before the congregation.

Rav Ashi, however, disagrees. The real concern is different: *shema nifsika lei retzu'a besandalo* — perhaps the strap of a *kohen*'s sandal breaks on the way up to the *duchan*, and he bends down to retie it. Everyone else continues up to perform *birchas kohanim*, and he is left crouching on the ground. People will say: he is not going up because he is a *ben grusha o ben chalutza* — the son of a divorced woman or a woman who performed *chalitza* — and is therefore disqualified from performing *birchas kohanim*. To avoid this source of suspicion, the *takana* requires all *kohanim* to remove their sandals before ascending.

### One Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמִקְדָּשׁ בְּרָכָה אַחַת כּוּ׳. וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹנִים ״אָמֵן״ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna: And in the Temple, the priests recite the three verses as one blessing. The Gemara asks: And why does the practice in the Temple differ so much from outside the Temple? Because one does not answer amen in the Temple, and therefore there is no pause between the blessings.

The *mishna* states: *bamikdash bracha achas* — in the *Mikdash*, *birchas kohanim* was recited as a single uninterrupted blessing. The reason: *lefi she'ein onim amen bamikdash* — because *amen* is not said in the *Beis Hamikdash*. Since there is no *amen* following each of the three *pesukim*, they are not treated as three separate *brachot* but as a continuous unit.

This creates a notable tension with the earlier discussion in the *gemara*. The *gemara* above spoke of reciting *pesukim* in the *Mikdash* between or during the *brachot* — and that would seem to break them up. *Tosafos* resolves this by suggesting that *baruch shem kavod malchuto le'olam va'ed* — said at *shem Hashem*, in the middle of a *bracha* rather than at its conclusion — would not constitute a division between the three *pesukim*. This works well according to the opinion that the *pesukim* were recited at *shem Hashem*. According to the opinion that they were said between the *brachot*, the difficulty remains, and the *gemara* does not directly address it.

### No Amen Mikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין עוֹנִים ״אָמֵן״ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קוּמוּ בָּרְכוּ אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם מִן הָעוֹלָם עַד הָעוֹלָם״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁעַל כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה תְּהִלָּה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וִיבָרְכוּ שֵׁם כְּבֹדֶךָ וּמְרוֹמַם עַל כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּתְהִלָּה״. עַל כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה תֵּן לוֹ תְּהִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: From where is it derived that one does not answer amen in the Temple? As it is stated: "Stand up and bless the Lord, your God, from everlasting to everlasting" (Nehemiah 9:5). This blessing is recited instead of amen in the Temple. And from where is it derived that for each and every blessing in the Temple the people respond with these words of praise? As it is stated: "And let them say: Blessed be Your glorious name, which is exalted above all blessing and praise" (Nehemiah 9:5). This verse indicates that for each and every blessing, you should give Him praise. However, those present do not respond amen.

The *gemara* asks: from where do we know that *amen* is not said in the *Beis Hamikdash*? The source is the verse in Ezra: *kumu barchu et Hashem Elokeichem min ha'olam ve'ad ha'olam* — rise and bless God from this world to the next world — which encapsulates the liturgical formula *baruch shem kavod malchuto le'olam va'ed* as the response in the *Mikdash*.

And from where do we know that this response is given after each individual *bracha*? From the verse: *veyivarchu shem kevodecha umeromam al kol bracha usehilla* — and they shall bless Your glorious name, which is exalted above every *bracha* and praise — teaching that for every single *bracha*, one gives praise. That praise takes the form of *baruch shem kavod*, not *amen*.

Why is *amen* not said in the *Mikdash*? The *Maharsha* offers an explanation involving *gematria*. A more compelling approach may be conceptual: *amen* is fundamentally the response of a follower — one who hears another initiate a blessing and affirms it. There is power in this role; the *gemara* elsewhere speaks at length of the extraordinary spiritual weight of *amen*. But in the *Beis Hamikdash*, where one stands in the direct presence of God, perhaps the expectation is different. If one is going to respond to a blessing of God at all, one must do so with one's own active blessing — *baruch shem kavod* — not merely with a secondary affirmation of someone else's.
