# Avodah Zarah 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shlakot & Prooftext

### Opening & Shlakot

We begin at the bottom of 37b, nine lines from the bottom, three lines from the wide lines, with the word *vehashlakot* — "and the vegetable soups." The *shlakot* that appear in Masechet Berachot, where they carry the *bracha* of *ha'adamah*, are a kind of broth: a mixture of water and vegetables from which the vegetables have been removed, leaving behind a flavorful liquid. This food — this mixture of water and cooked substance — is the entry point into our broader discussion of *bishulei akum*, the prohibition against consuming foods cooked by non-Jews.

### Tosafot's Source

Before even entering the *gemara* proper, Tosafot poses a foundational question: *min hanei milei* — from where do we derive this? There is no explicit *pasuk*, only a kind of *pasuk*, which we will examine momentarily. Seven lines into the Tosafot, we find the following formulation: *gazru aleiha mitam chatnut ve'al hapat lo gazru mishum shelo shayach bei chatnut* — they decreed upon it on account of *chatnut*, the concern for intermarriage, and they did not decree upon bread because *chatnut* is not applicable there. According to Tosafot, the primary animating concern behind the prohibition on non-Jewish cooking is the social intimacy that shared meals create, an intimacy that can lead to intermarriage.

There are, of course, other concerns one might imagine: the possibility of poison, the mixing in of non-kosher ingredients, the general mistrust of eating food prepared by someone outside one's community. But Tosafot's stated and foregrounded reasoning is *chatnut*.

### Devarim Prooftext

The *gemara* then introduces a *pasuk* as support: *amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan, amar kra* — we have a scriptural allusion. The verse comes from Sefer Devarim, chapter 2, pasuk 27, where Moshe, recounting the episode with Sichon, King of the Amorites, says: *ochel bakesef tashbireni ve'achalti, umayim bakesef titeni veshatiti* — "food for money you shall give me, and I shall eat; water for money you shall give me, and I shall drink." The *gemara* will derive from the juxtaposition of food and water a principle about which foods are subject to the non-Jewish cooking prohibition.

### Kamayim Principle

The principle extracted is: *ma mayim shelo nishtanu, af ochel shelo nishtanu* — just as water does not change from its original nature, so too only food that does not change from its original nature falls under the prohibition. The corollary is clear: food that undergoes a fundamental transformation through the cooking process is prohibited; food that remains essentially unchanged may be treated differently.

### Roasted & Ground Grain

The *gemara* then tests this principle against concrete cases. What about wheat kernels that have been roasted — *chitin ve'asaan kliyot*? Does parching grain constitute a sufficient change to bring it under the prohibition? A *baraita* is cited: *chitin ve'asaan kliyot mutarin* — roasted grains are permitted. And what about ground grains — *chitin utechanan*? Again, a *baraita* teaches: *hakmachim vehasaltot shelahem mutarin* — their flours and fine flours are permitted. So neither roasting nor grinding, it seems, is the kind of change that triggers the prohibition.

The *gemara* therefore refines the principle: *ma mayim shelo nishtanu mibriyatan, af ochel shelo nishtana mibriyato* — just as water does not change from its original nature, so too we are looking for food that does not change from its original nature. The question that remains is: which process of change is the relevant one?

### Heating Element

The answer emerges: *ma mayim shelo nishtanu mibriyatan al yedei ha'or, af ochel shelo nishtana mibriyato al yedei ha'or* — just as water is not changed by fire, so too we are concerned only with food that is not changed by fire. The controlling factor is the action of cooking through heat, not grinding, not salting, not any other form of preparation. The prohibition on non-Jewish cooking applies specifically to foods that are fundamentally altered by the application of fire.

### Derabbanan Status

But does the verse actually say this? *Midei or ketiv?* — is fire written in the verse? It is not. The conclusion follows: *ella miderabbanan vekra asmachta be'alma* — the entire prohibition is rabbinic in origin, and the scriptural verse serves merely as an *asmachta*, a Biblical allusion lending support to a rabbinic enactment. This is confirmed by Rashi on the top of 38a.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Rashi's reasoning for the prohibition is stated clearly: *shelo yehei Yisrael ragil etzlo bema'achal uvimishteh veya'achilennu davar tamei* — so that a Jew should not become habituated to eating and drinking at the home of a non-Jew, lest he be fed something *tamei*. There are thus two distinct strands in the *rishonim*: Rashi emphasizes *regilut*, habituation, and the resulting risk of eating forbidden foods; Tosafot foregrounds *chatnut*, the fear of intermarriage. These need not be mutually exclusive — from *regilut* may come *chatnut* and vice versa — but the emphases reflect something real about the commentators and perhaps about the worlds they inhabited.

It is worth noting that Tosafot quotes the Ra'avad in this context. Tosafot cites the Ra'avad only three times in all of *Shas*, and this is one of them.

## Ra'avad Opinion

### Ra'avad Permissive

The Ra'avad — Rabbi Avraham ben Rabbi David, 1110–1180, Cordoba — offers a striking leniency. His position: *devadai shlakot asru chachamim keshe'oved kochavim mevashelam beveito* — the *chachamim* forbade *shlakot* when the *oved kochavim* cooks them in his own home. *Aval kshe-hu mevashel beveito shel Yisrael ein lachosh, lo l'chatnut velo l'shem yachilenu devarim tme'im* — but when he cooks in the Jew's home, there is no concern, neither for *chatnut* nor for being fed forbidden foods.

The Ra'avad's reasoning has a certain intuitive force. If the non-Jew is cooking in my kitchen, I know what ingredients are present, I can supervise, and the social dynamics that produce *chatnut* are far less pronounced than when I am a guest in his home.

### Rejection & Reasoning

Nevertheless, *velo hoda lo Rabbeinu Tam* — Rabbeinu Tam did not accept the Ra'avad's position, and this is the decisive voice of Tosafot. The rejection is straightforward: *devadai kemshe'oved kochavim mevashel lo chalku klal chachamim bein reshut haYisrael l'reshut ha'oved kochavim* — the *chachamim* made no distinction whatsoever between the non-Jew cooking in his own domain and in the Jew's domain. The reason: *ki le'olam yesh lachosh shema lo yizaher gam beveito shel Yisrael kmo beveito shel oved kochavim* — there is always the concern that he will not be careful even in the Jew's home, just as in his own. He might go to the market and bring outside ingredients; he might be careless about what he introduces into the cooking. Note that this second concern — about ingredients — is not a concern about *chatnut* at all, but about the integrity of what enters the food.

### Modern Context

The Ra'avad's position, though rejected, retains practical relevance for our own time. Older individuals or families with non-Jewish domestic workers who cook in the home — does one need always to be present, to light the fire, to intervene? Is there a recognized opinion that permits a non-Jew to cook in a Jew's kitchen without active Jewish participation? The Ra'avad provides at least a minority position to be aware of. Similarly, contemporary questions about restaurant supervision — a vegan restaurant with kosher certification, where all ingredients are technically permitted but a non-Jew does the cooking — engage precisely these questions about who is cooking, where, and with what degree of oversight. The issue of *hashgacha* in such settings brings together concerns about both *davar tamei* and the identity of the one cooking.

## Food Categories

### Rav Shmuel: Chi

At the top of 38a, *amar Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak amar Rav*: *kol hane'echal kemot shehu chai ein bo mishum bishulei ovdei kochavim* — anything that is eaten as it is, raw, is not subject to the prohibition of non-Jewish cooking. The underlying logic connects back to the *kamayim* principle: if something does not require cooking to be fit for consumption, cooking by a non-Jew does not implicate the prohibition.

### Pumbedita Criterion

Sura transmitted the teaching in this form. Pumbedita added a further criterion: *kol she'eino ne'echal al shulchan melachim lelapet bo et hapat ein bo mishum bishulei ovdei kochavim* — anything that is not served on royal tables to be eaten with bread is not subject to the prohibition. Pumbedita's formulation requires not only that the food can be eaten raw but also that it is the kind of food that would appear at an important table, eaten alongside bread in the manner of *lapet*.

What is the practical difference between the two formulations? *Mai beineihiu* — what falls between them? The examples given are *dagim ktanim*, small fish; *ardi*, mushrooms; and *daisa*, porridge — what we might call cream of wheat or oatmeal, which mothers still offer today. These are foods that occupy an ambiguous status: not quite eaten raw in any ordinary sense, and not quite fit for a royal table either.

### Examples & Salting

*Amar Rav Assi amar Rav*: small salted fish — *dagim ktanim mluchim* — are not subject to *bishulei ovdei kochavim*. The question raised here is what salting does. Salting is a process that transforms fish — think of ceviche, or any form of salt-curing — but it does not involve fire. The principle articulated earlier focused on change wrought by fire. Salting, by that logic, would not implicate the prohibition. This connects as well to the broader category of *kalei habishul*, foods that cook very quickly or through processes other than sustained heat, which similarly occupy a borderline status.

### Fried Fish & Harsana

*Amar Rav Yosef*: if a *oved kochavim* fried fish — *im tzl'an* — a Jew may rely on them for purposes of *eiruvei tavshilin*, treating them as cooked food. This is permitted. However, if the non-Jew made them in the manner of *ksada d'harsana* — a dish of fish prepared with fish oil, flour, and oil, cooked together — that is *assur*. The concern with *ksada d'harsana* relates to the presence of vinegar in the preparation. Vinegar raises the suspicion of wine, and wine raises the suspicion of *yayin nesech*, wine used in idolatrous practice. From there, the concern ramifies outward.

### Ikar Question

*Pshita* — this seems obvious — so the *gemara* asks what the novelty is. The answer: *mahu d'teima harsana ikar, kamashma lan kimcha ikar* — one might have thought the fish is the principal ingredient, *ikar*, and since fish can be eaten raw it should be permitted; the *gemara* teaches us that the flour is the *ikar*. And since the flour, though not cooked on its own, is now mixed into a cooked dish and cannot be eaten raw in that form, it brings the entire preparation under the prohibition of *bishulei akum*.

### Honey & Cheese

Tosafot raises the question of honey and cheese: these are raw, they are eaten with bread, they appear on royal tables — by Pumbedita's criterion they should be entirely exempt from *bishulei akum*. Tosafot notes that a later formulation was added to the first — *shena'asa bahem lashon acharon musaf al harishon u'lehakel alav* — a later language was superimposed on the earlier one in order to be lenient. There is also a question whether Tosafot requires that the Jew perform some final act — such as salting — before the non-Jew cooks: *harei shebishlan oved kochavim achar shemerchan Yisrael*, the non-Jew cooks after the Jew has salted. This would make the Jew's participation a prerequisite even in cases that might otherwise seem clearly permitted.

## Intent & Agency

### Singeing Head

*Amar Rav Beruna amar Rav*: a non-Jew who set fire to a marsh — *oved kochavim she'itzit et ha'ur be'agam* — all the grasshoppers in the marsh are *asurin*. The *gemara* unpacks what exactly the problem is. It cannot be that we do not know which grasshopper is *tahor* and which is *tamei* — that problem would arise whether it was a Jew or a non-Jew who lit the fire. So the concern must be something else. The *gemara* explores whether this is a case of *bishul ovdei kochavim*. And here a counter-principle is introduced: *ve'ha amar Rav Chanan bar Ami amar Rav Adda amar Rabbi Yochanan* — a non-Jew who singes an animal's head to remove its hair, *oved kochavim decharich reisha*, it is permitted to eat from it, *afilu meresh ozneih*, even the tip of the ear, which would be fully cooked by the singeing process. The resolution is that *alma le'avorei se'ar kamichaven* — his intent was to remove the hair, not to cook. Intent in cooking — *kavanah* — is a governing principle. Just as singeing to remove hair is not cooking with intent to cook, burning a marsh to clear it for agriculture is not cooking grasshoppers. The *gemara* concludes that the marsh case is one where the grasshoppers cannot be identified as *tahor* or *tamei* — and the non-Jew is incidental.

### Peg & Gourd

*Amar Ravina*: if a non-Jew threw a peg — *sikta* — into an oven to dry or harden it, and a Jew then placed a gourd — *kra* — in that same oven, *shapir dami*, this is fine. The *gemara* considers the possibility that one might think the non-Jew intended cooking — *l'bishulei m'na ka michavein* — but the intent was clearly to treat the peg, not to cook food. The question of who acted first — the Jew placing the gourd or the non-Jew lighting the fire — is also significant, and there are cases where *ipcha*, the reverse, will be examined.

### Kavanah Required

The last line of the Tosafot on 38a makes the principle explicit: *aval le'inyan bishulei kochavim lo chashiv ve'eino chayav ad she'itkaven lebishul gamur* — with respect to *bishulei kochavim*, the act is not significant and there is no prohibition until there is an intent directed toward full cooking. Incidental heating is not *bishul*. This is the principle of *melechet machshevet* applied to the context of non-Jewish cooking: only a thoughtful, intentional act of cooking brings the prohibition into play.

### Meat on Coals

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: if a Jew placed meat on coals and a non-Jew came and turned it over, *mutar* — it is permitted. The *gemara* probes the case. If the meat would have cooked anyway without the turning, then *pshita* — obviously there is no problem, since the non-Jew's action added nothing essential. The more interesting case is the one where the *gemara* says *lo tzricha* — the ruling is needed: where the meat would have cooked in two hours without being turned, but with turning cooked in one hour.

### Hastening Cooking

This raises the question of whether hastening the cooking process constitutes *bishul* for purposes of the prohibition. If the non-Jew's intervention meaningfully accelerated the cooking, one might think it constitutes his act of cooking. The ruling: *mahu deteima kruvei bishula milta hi, kamashma lan* — lest you say that hastening cooking is significant, the teaching tells us it is not. This is the settled position — at least for this case — and the discussion continues elsewhere in *Avodah Zarah* and beyond.

A related case: a woman left a pot on a *keirah* and a non-Jewish woman came and stirred it — *umegisah* — until the Jew returned from the bathhouse or *beit haknesset*. No concern. Similarly, the Jew putting meat on coals while the non-Jew turns it until the Jew returns from *beit haknesset* or *beit hamidrash* — *ve'eino choshesh*. There is a separate question, which we note without resolving, whether a *shomer* is required when a non-Jew is left unattended with cooking food, given the possibility that he might add something — from something simply non-kosher to something outright harmful — to what is being cooked.

The case of a Jew placing meat in a basket — *silta* — where it would not cook at all, and then the *oved kochavim* taking it and placing it in the oven, is treated as the non-Jew initiating a new act of cooking. The threshold of *ma'achal ben Drusai* becomes relevant: if the meat was cooked past a third before being placed in the basket, the non-Jew's completing act may be viewed differently than if it was barely begun.

### Techilo V'Gemaro

*Itmar nami, amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan* — and some say *amar Rav Acha bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan*: whether the non-Jew placed the food and the Jew turned it, or the Jew placed the food and the non-Jew turned it — *mutar* in both cases. *Ve'eino assur ad shetehe techilato ugemaro beyad oved kochavim* — it is forbidden only when both the beginning and the end of the cooking are in the hands of the non-Jew. This is the rule of *techilo v'gemaro*: full non-Jewish cooking from start to finish is forbidden; any meaningful Jewish participation at either end renders it permitted.

### Bread Combinations

*Amar Ravina*, and this is stated as *hilchata* — as the operative ruling: regarding bread baked in an oven, there are several permitted combinations. If a non-Jew lit the oven and a Jew baked — *shapir dami*. If a Jew lit and a non-Jew baked — *shapir dami*. If a non-Jew lit and a non-Jew baked, but a Jew came and performed *chituy* — stoking or stirring the fire — *shapir dami*. The *chituy*, a concept familiar from laws of *bishul* on *Shabbat*, is sufficient Jewish intervention to break the *techilo v'gemaro* of the non-Jew.

The practical question of how much intervention is required — whether merely throwing a peg into the fire is enough — is a subject of significant dispute among the *rishonim* and later authorities. The Rosh says this peg-throwing (*din keisam*) is forbidden as insufficient intervention, while acknowledging that one should *vehanach l'Yisrael sheyiyu shogegim* — let Jews err in this unknowingly. The Rambam permits it outright and holds that a Jew's throwing even a small object into the fire while the non-Jew cooks constitutes *bishul Yisrael*. The Shulchan Aruch reflects this dispute, and the practical outcome is that Ashkenazim are *mekilim* on this question — the throwing of the peg is sufficient — while Sefardim require more active participation: actually placing the food in the oven and being an active party to the cooking process, not merely a bystander who performed one small gesture.

## Fish & Egg Disputes

### Salted Fish

*Dag maliach* — salted fish — is the subject of a dispute: *Chizkiyah shari veRabbi Yochanan assur*. Chizkiyah permits salted fish cooked by a non-Jew; Rabbi Yochanan forbids. The logic of Chizkiyah's leniency presumably connects to the earlier discussion: salting is not a fire-based transformation, and perhaps the fish is considered eaten *chai* — in its essentially raw form — even after salting. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and maintains the prohibition.

### Roasted Egg

*Beitzat tzluyah* — a roasted egg — is similarly disputed: *Bar Kappara shari veRabbi Yochanan assur*. Bar Kappara permits an egg roasted by a non-Jew; Rabbi Yochanan forbids. *Ki ata Rav Dimi amar* — when Rav Dimi came, he summarized: both the salted fish and the roasted egg — *Chizkiyah uVar Kappara sharu veRabbi Yochanan assur*. Two authorities permit; one forbids.

### Reish Galuta Story

Rabbi Chiya Parva arrived at the home of the *Reish Galuta*, the head of the exile community. They asked him: *beitzat tzluyah mai* — what is the ruling on a roasted egg? He reported both positions: Chizkiyah and Bar Kappara permit, Rabbi Yochanan forbids. And he added: *ve'ein devarav shel echad bimkom shenayim* — the opinion of one does not prevail against two. An interesting invocation of what one might call Talmudic democracy — which is by no means always applied.

Then Rav Zevid intervened: *lo ttzitu leh* — do not listen to him. *Hachi amar Abaye hilchata kevataiah deRabbi Yochanan* — Abaye said the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan, which means the egg is forbidden. Rav Zevid was not himself expressing his own opinion but transmitting Abaye's ruling about the authority of Rabbi Yochanan — which is a different matter from simply adding a third opinion to the debate.

What happened to Rav Zevid? *Ashkyuha naguta de-chala ve-nach nafshei* — they gave him a cup of spiced vinegar to drink, and he is resting ever since. *Nach nafshei* is the *gemara*'s way of saying he died. This is a striking *gemara*. The message seems to be: enough with *chumrot* in the middle of an already complex gray area. We are discussing fish and eggs — foods that should in principle be entirely unproblematic — and someone comes to add a stringency on top of a stringency. The *gemara* does not reward him for this.

### Baraita Foods

*Tanu rabanan*: capers — *kafrisin* — leeks — *kaflotot* — *matlya*, hot water — *chamin* — and roasted grains — *kalyot* — that belong to non-Jews are all permitted. A roasted egg is forbidden. Oil — *shemen* — was reconsidered: *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi ve-beit dino nimnu alav ve-hitiruhu* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his court convened on the matter and permitted oil. The reconvening and formal permitting of oil is itself a notable act of deliberate legislative reversal.

## Matlya & Shiata

### Matlya Definition

The *baraita* mentioned *matlya*, and the *gemara* immediately asks what it is. *Ha-matlya hi pashlya hi shiata* — *matlya* is *pashlya* is *shiata*. One unknown term is explained by two other unknown terms, which requires further clarification: *mai shiata* — what is *shiata*?

### Shiata Process

*Amar Rabba bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan* — and this is the team we have followed throughout much of this *daf*, student transmitting teacher's tradition. The preparation of *shiata* involves taking seeds of *karpas* (parsley or cress), flax — *chitna* — and fenugreek — *shavlilita* — and soaking them together in lukewarm water — *b'pishurei* — leaving them until they sprout. Then one takes a new earthenware vessel — *chatzavei chadata* — fills it with water, soaks red clay in it — *gargishta* — and adheres the sprouted seeds to it.

### Shiata Use

Rabba bar bar Chana says this was done forty years after the practice emerged from Egypt; his student Rabba bar bar Chana himself says sixty years — they do not disagree, because they are speaking from different points in time, twenty years apart. The purpose of the *shiata*: one prepares this mixture and then goes to the bathhouse. *Ad nafku m'lavlavei* — by the time one emerges, the seeds are already sprouting and blossoming. *V'achli mineihu ve-kairi m'binta d'rasheihu ad tufra d'charei'eihu* — they ate from it, and it cooled the body from the top of the head to the toenails of the feet after the heat of the bathhouse.

### Rav Ashi Skeptical

*Amar Rav Ashi amar li Rabbi Chanina milin, ve'amri lah b'milin* — Rav Ashi quotes Rabbi Chanina expressing skepticism. Some say *milin* meaning the story is simply without basis, empty words. Others say *b'milin* — that it was accomplished through magical words, not through any natural process of germination. The *gemara* itself thus contains its own internal critique of the account, with one strand dismissing it as fiction and another attributing the phenomenon to magical practice. The tension between the two readings — naturalistic improbability versus deliberate magical act — is left open.

## Beverages & Boiled Food

### Kusfan Vessel Size

*Tanu rabanan*: the *kusfan* of non-Jews — an alcoholic beverage made from the soakings or refuse of dates — if it was heated in a large vessel, *yorah gedolah*, it is *assur*; if in a small vessel, *yorah ketanah*, it is *mutar*.

### Vessel Size Details

*V'eizo hi yorah ketanah* — what defines a small vessel? *Amar Rabbi Yannai*: any vessel into which a *tzipor dror*, a sparrow, cannot enter. The concern with a large vessel is evidently that things can be introduced into it — other ingredients, contaminants. The *gemara* then tightens the definition: perhaps one could chop up the bird and stuff the pieces inside? The answer: *ela kol she'ein rosh tzipor dror yachol* — a vessel is "small" for these purposes if even the head of a sparrow cannot enter its opening. Any opening large enough to admit even the head of a small bird is large enough to be a concern.

### Vessel Conflict

A conflicting *baraita* is then cited: *achat yora gedola ve'achat yora ktana mutar* — whether the vessel is large or small, the *kusfan* is permitted. *La kashya* — no contradiction. *Ha keman d'amar noten taam lifgam assur, ha keman d'amar noten taam lifgam mutar* — the two sources represent two different positions on the question of *noten taam lifgam*: whether a flavor absorbed into a vessel's walls that imparts a diminished or detrimental taste to what is subsequently cooked in it is *assur* or *mutar*. This question is not resolved here; it will continue on Daf 67.

## Noten Taam Lifgam

### Oil Forbidden

*Amar Rav Sheshet*: oil that was cooked by Arameans — *mishcha shlaka d'Armai* — is *assur*. This is stated plainly, as a ruling.

### Rav Safra's Inquiry

*Amar Rav Safra*: to what concern do we attribute this? He poses three possibilities. *Ee mishum eruvi masreseri* — if the concern is that something forbidden was mixed in, such as vinegar from wine, that would be detectable by smell. *Ee mishum bishulei ovdei kochavim* — if the concern is non-Jewish cooking, oil is *ne'echal kemo shehu chai* — it is used raw, so the prohibition does not apply. *Ee mishum ge'ulei ovdei kochavim* — if the concern is residual absorbed flavor from non-Jewish vessels, then *noten taam lifgam hu umutar* — it imparts a detrimental flavor and should therefore be permitted.

### Flavor Analysis

*Ge'ulei ovdei kochavim* refers not to cooking per se but to the residue or absorbed flavor of previous use — what remains in the walls of a vessel after it has been used. This is different from actual *bishul*. If the residual flavor detracts from rather than enhances the food, then *noten taam lifgam* — the principle that a detrimental absorbed flavor is permitted — would apply. The analogy offered is vivid: the barrels in which whiskey ages may previously have held wine, and the absorbed flavor of the wine transfers into the whiskey, changing its character. Whether such absorption improves or detracts, and whether one can identify its presence, are precisely the questions the *gemara* is navigating.

### Sfek Sfeka

Tosafot addresses this with a principle of *sfek sfeka* — double doubt. The first uncertainty: we do not know whether the vessel was used today or yesterday. The second uncertainty: even if it was used today, we do not know whether what was used in it was something that imparts a prohibited flavor or something that imparts no flavor at all — *safek nishtamshu bo hayom o etmol, ve'afilu nishtamshu bo hayom shema nishtamshu bo davar shehu pogem o davar she'ein noten taam*. With two independent doubts stacking on each other, the result is *mutar*.

## Beverages & Boiled Foo…

### Boiled Dates

A question is posed before Rav Assi: *hanei ahini shlakei d'Aramaye mahi* — what is the ruling on these boiled dates of the Arameans? The question is nuanced. *Cholei lo tiba'i lach d'vadai sharu* — if the dates are sweet, obviously they are permitted; nothing was added, since the sweetness speaks for itself. *Meriri lo tiba'i lach d'vadai assiri* — if bitter, obviously forbidden; bitterness indicates that vinegar was cooked with them, vinegar derives from wine, and wine raises the concern of *yayin nesech*. *Ki tiba'i lach mtziya mai* — the question is about the middle category, those that are neither clearly sweet nor clearly bitter.

### Dates Ruling

Rav Assi tells those asking: my *rebbe* — *Levi* — forbade the middle category. The practical upshot is that we follow the leniency of Levi on the sweet dates — they are permitted — and the stringency on the bitter — they are forbidden — and the middle category is treated stringently in accordance with the *rebbe*'s ruling.

## Shtita Dish

### Shtita Definition

*Rashi* explains: *tavshil ha'asui mi'kemach shel kliyot she'yibeshan ba'tanur* — a dish made from the flour of grains that were dried in an oven. Alternatively, *yesh osim oto mi'kemach adashim* — some make it from lentil flour. And because lentils are exceptionally sweet, *noten sham chometz* — one adds vinegar to balance the sweetness.

### Vinegar Concern

This is precisely where the concern arises. Vinegar comes from wine. Wine, in certain contexts, may be *yayin nesech*, wine associated with idolatrous practice. A dish prepared with vinegar of uncertain origin therefore implicates these concerns, and *shtita* — particularly the lentil-flour variety — becomes suspect when made with vinegar.

## Caper Plant

### Caper Parts

The *kafrisin* mentioned in our *baraita* — one of the permitted foods cooked by non-Jews — are capers. The caper plant is the *tzalaf*, and it grows throughout the land of Israel, including in Jerusalem. The plant produces multiple distinct edible parts: the caper buds are *kafrisin*; the berries are *evyonot*; the leaves are yet another part of the same plant. All three are distinct for purposes of *halacha*, and there are separate rulings about which *beracha* applies to each — a fitting botanical coda to a *daf* that has taken us through soups, roasted eggs, salted fish, sprouting seeds, date beverages, and the ever-elusive question of where, exactly, a non-Jew's cooking ends and a Jew's begins.
