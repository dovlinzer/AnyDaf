# Hullin 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Foreign Objects

### Needles in Liver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא מַחְטָא דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח בְּסִמְפּוֹנָא רַבָּה דְּכַבְדָּא, הוּנָא מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי טָרֵיף, רַב אַדָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי מַכְשַׁר. אֲתוֹ שַׁיְילוּהּ לְרָבִינָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׁקִילוּ גְּלִימָא דְּטָרוֹפָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain needle that was found in the large duct of a liver. Huna Mar, son of Rav Idi, deemed** the animal **a *tereifa*,** while **Rav Adda bar Minyumi deemed it kosher. They came** and **asked Ravina** about the issue, and **he said to them: Take the robe of those who deemed it a *tereifa*.** They must pay restitution to the owner of the animal, who was wrongfully forced to discard his kosher meat.

The *sugya* on 49a, five lines from the top, continues the discussion about needles and other foreign objects found inside organs, and the central question of how they arrived there. The working framework has been whether foreign matter entered through the trachea and its branching tubes — and thus passed through without necessarily creating a disqualifying perforation — or whether it punctured its way in from outside, indicating a tear somewhere that would render the animal *treif*. The immediately preceding discussion focused on the orientation of the needle: if the point faced outward into the body cavity, that strongly suggests it did not travel through a tube but instead pierced its way in from without.

### Dispute on Needle


The *gemara* now presents a fresh case: *hahu machta de-ishtachach be-simpona raba de-kavda* — a needle was found in the major tube leading into the liver. The anatomical assumption of the *gemara* is that the respiratory system branches not only into the lungs but also feeds into the liver and heart, so this tube would be understood as branching off from the trachea. This is actually quite favorable terrain for a lenient ruling: a hole in the trachea is only a problem if the majority (*rov*) of it is affected, and a hole in the liver is generally not problematic at all, since most of the liver can be missing without rendering the animal *treif*. The only genuine concern would be if the needle had entered by tearing through the esophagus — that would be a *treifah*. Huna Mar the son of Rav Idai ruled *treif*; Rav Adda bar Minyomi ruled *kasher*.

### Ravina's Ruling


The case was brought before Ravina, and his response was striking. He said: *shekulu glima de-trefai* — "go seize the cloak of those who declared it *treif*." In other words, not only did he rule leniently, he ruled with such force that he held the stringent *psak* to have been so clearly wrong that its authors owe financial restitution to the owner of the animal. The reasoning is that under normal presumptions we do not assume the needle entered by tearing through the esophagus; regardless of whatever holes it may have made in the trachea or liver along the way, neither of those creates a *treifah* problem. The remark about seizing the cloak was most likely meant metaphorically — the *gemara* in *sanhedrin* governs actual monetary liability — but the rhetorical force of the statement is clear: the *treif* ruling was considered manifestly incorrect.

### Stone in Gallbladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא קְשִׁיתָא דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח בְּמָרָה, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא, אָמַר: הָא וַדַּאי סִימְפּוֹנָא נְקַט וַאֲתַאי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא קָא נָפְקָא, מִירְבָּל הוּא דְּרָבֵיל לַיהּ, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּדִיקְלָא, אֲבָל דְּזֵיתָא מִיבְזָע בָּזַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there was **a certain** date **pit that was found in a gallbladder. Rav Ashi said: When we were in the house of Rav Kahana** as students, **he would say** with regard to such a case: **This** date pit **certainly took** the route **of** the **duct** connecting the liver and gallbladder **and came** through it to the gallbladder, as it is not sharp enough to have perforated the gallbladder from without. **Even though it** is large enough that it **does not exit** the gallbladder if one tries to squeeze it into the duct, one may still assume that the movements of the animal's body gradually **cause it to slip** through the duct. The Gemara adds: **And this statement** applies only to the pit **of a palm,** i.e., of a date, **but** the pit **of an olive** is pointed. Therefore, one must be concerned that it **has pierced** the wall of the gallbladder, rendering the animal a *tereifa*.

The *gemara* proceeds to a parallel case: *hahu kashishta de-ishtachach be-marta* — something like a date stone (Rashi identifies it as such, though it is entirely possible the *gemara* is in fact describing what we would call a gallstone) was found inside the gallbladder. Rav Ashi reports what he learned from Rav Kahana: *hai vadai simpona nakat ve-asai* — this definitely came via the *simpon*, the tube system. The gallbladder is near the liver; in the *gemara*'s anatomical understanding, the trachea branches into the liver, and from the liver into the gallbladder, and the stone traveled that route without creating a disqualifying hole along the way.

### Squeezing Through


One might immediately object: if the stone is too large to be squeezed back out through the tube — and the *gemara* acknowledges this explicitly with *af al gav de-lo ka nafka*, even though it would not exit through the tube — how could we assume it entered that way? The *gemara* answers: *mervel hu de-ravlei* — it worked its way through gradually. Rashi explains with the analogy of a sieve: just as one must continuously shake a sieve to coax material through, so too this stone, over time and through the constant movement of the living animal, slowly made its way through a tube that was slightly too narrow, and eventually came to rest in the gallbladder. The fact that it cannot now be extracted through that route does not prove it did not enter that way.

### Date vs. Olive


The *gemara* then qualifies: *vehani mili d'dikla* — this reasoning applies specifically to a date stone from a palm tree. *aval d'zeisa* — but if it were an olive stone, *mirza beza*, since olive stones are pointed and sharp, we would have to be concerned that it did not travel through the tube system at all but rather tore its way in, creating a hole. This is a somewhat surprising distinction, given that the *gemara* just ruled leniently even in the case of a needle found in the liver tube without worrying that it tore its way through. And one might add: an object large enough to lodge in the gallbladder, if it truly tore through, would presumably leave a visible, gaping hole — yet no such evidence is mentioned.

### Rashi/Tosafot


Rashi addresses this difficulty by suggesting the *gemara* is here speaking only according to the opinion we do not rule like — the position that assumes foreign matter in organs most likely entered by tearing rather than through the natural tube system. The olive-stone ruling belongs to that rejected opinion, not to normative *halacha*. Tosafot disagrees and finds it strange that the *gemara* would shift mid-discussion to an opinion it has already set aside. Tosafot instead argues that since an olive stone, being rough and sharp like a needle, is unlikely to travel smoothly through the trachea system, it is more reasonable to suspect it came through the esophagus. This too, however, is difficult to accept: something this large tearing through the esophagus and arriving in the gallbladder without leaving observable evidence is extremely counterintuitive. Rashi's reading at least confines the stringency to a position we do not follow, which makes it somewhat more manageable.

## Lung Focus

### Lung Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ רֵיאָה – שֶׁמְּאִירָה אֶת הָעֵינַיִם. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לַאֲכִילָה, אוֹ עַל יְדֵי סַמָּנִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its discussion of the lung: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Why is** the lung **called *rei'a*** in Hebrew? **Because it lights up [*me'ira*] the eyes** of one who eats it. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is Rabbi Yoḥanan referring **to** a lung **eaten** as is, **or** is he referring to a lung eaten only **through** its treatment with certain **substances?**

With the discussion of foreign objects in organs concluded, the *gemara* offers a brief etymological digression. Rabbi Yochanan asks: *lama nikra shemo reyah* — why is the lung called a *reyah*? The answer is a play on the letters of the word: *sheme'irah es ha'einayim* — because it gives light to the eyes. Eating from the lungs is said to be nutritionally beneficial and supports eyesight.

### Lung Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה: אֲוָוזָא בְּזוּזָא, וְרֵיאָה דִּידַהּ בְּאַרְבְּעָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לַאֲכִילָה – לִינְקֹט בְּזוּזָא וְלֵיכוֹל! אֶלָּא, עַל יְדֵי סַמָּנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that **which Rav Huna bar Yehuda says: A goose** may be purchased **for a dinar, but its lung** may be purchased **for four** dinars. **And if** it should **enter your mind** that Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring **to eating** the lung without treating it, one has no incentive to buy a lung separately for more money. **Let him buy** the whole goose **for a dinar and eat** the lung that comes included. **Rather,** it must be that the lung gains special properties only **through** treatment with certain **substances,** and this treatment is the reason for the higher price.

The *gemara* immediately raises a question: *achila o al yedei samanim* — does this health benefit come from eating the lung directly, or only when the lung is prepared together with particular medicinal ingredients?

The answer comes from a market observation reported by Rav Huna bar Yehuda: *avza bezuza* — a duck might sell for a *zuz*, but *vereyah didah b'arba* — its lung alone costs four *zuzim*. The *gemara* reasons: if the lung were beneficial simply when eaten plainly, one could just buy the whole duck for a *zuz* and eat the lung as part of the bargain. The fact that the lung commands a price four times that of the entire bird must indicate that it requires special preparation — either because the medicinal ingredients themselves are costly, or, as Rabbeinu Gershom explains, because the knowledge of how to prepare such a treatment properly is exceedingly rare, making the expertise itself extraordinarily valuable.

## Shochet-Caused Holes

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינַּקְבָה רֵיאָה הֵיכָא דִּמְמַשְׁמְשָׁא יְדֵיהּ דְּטַבָּחָא, תָּלֵינַן אוֹ לָא תָּלֵינַן? רַב אַדָּא בַּר נָתָן אָמַר: תָּלֵינַן, מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי אָמַר: לָא תָּלֵינַן, וְהִלְכְתָא: תָּלֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If **the lung is perforated where the hand of the butcher handles** it after slaughter, **do we attribute** the perforation to the butcher's handling, **or do we not attribute** the perforation to the handling, in which case the animal is a *tereifa*? **Rav Adda bar Natan says: We attribute** it to the butcher's handling, while **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, says: We do not attribute** it to the handling. **And the *halakha*** is that **we attribute** it to the handling.

The *gemara* now turns to a *she'eilah* that arises regularly in practice: *inkava reyah heicha d'mamshesha yadei d'tavcha* — if a hole is found in the lung precisely where the *shochet* typically handles it during and after *shechitah*, do we ascribe that hole to the *shochet*'s handling, or not? The question is whether we have sufficient grounds to attribute the visible defect to the *shochet* rather than treating it as a pre-existing condition that would render the animal *treif*.

### Rav Adda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַבָּא מֵרֵישֵׁי כַּלֵּי דְּרַפְרָם הֲוָה, וַאֲמַר: תָּלֵינַן. אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ לְהָא דְּמָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי, וְלָא קַבְּלַהּ. אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: כְּוָותֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ דְּאַבָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּהָא תָּלֵינַן בִּזְאֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel, son of Rabbi Abbahu, said:** My **father was** one **of the heads of the *kalla*** lectures of **Rafram, and he said: We attribute** it to the handling. **They said this** *halakha* **of Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari,** who held the opposite, **before** Rav Shmuel, **and he did not accept it.** **Rav Mesharshiyya said: It stands to reason** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Grandfather,** i.e., Rav Adda bar Natan, **as we attribute** perforations **to a wolf.** If slaughtered meat is recovered from a wolf and found to have a perforation that would have rendered the animal a *tereifa*, one may attribute the perforation to the wolf and presume that it did not exist beforehand.

Rav Adda bar Natan says *talinan* — we do attribute the hole to the *shochet*. Mar Zutra the son of Rav Mari says *lo talinan* — we do not. The *gemara* rules: *vehilchata talinan* — the *halacha* follows the lenient position that we do ascribe it to the *shochet*. This is a striking ruling: there is a visible, observable hole in the lung, and we are prepared to say that it was probably caused by the *shochet*'s handling rather than by a pre-slaughter condition. What makes this particularly notable is the contrast with the treatment of *sirchos* — adhesions that are not even visible holes — where the *gemara* has shown great stringency, treating them as indicative of an underlying hole even when the *sircha* appears to plug it. Here, with an actual visible hole, we invoke the specific, identifiable agency of the *shochet* and rule leniently.

Shmuel the son of Rabbi Abbahu adds that his father, Rabbi Abbahu, was the head gatherer (*keili*) for the *drashot* of Rafram, and would transmit in Rafram's name the same ruling: *talinan*, we do attribute it to the *shochet*. This tradition was reported before Mar Zutra the son of Rav Mari, who nonetheless did not accept it. Rav Mesharshiya then supports the position of Rav Adda bar Natan, the father of Abba: *mistabra*, it makes sense, for the same reason we attribute holes to a wolf.

### Wolf Analogy


The supporting analogy is this: *deha talinan bize'ev* — we know that if a wolf takes one of the inner organs of a slaughtered animal in its teeth and returns it with holes, we attribute those holes to the wolf rather than treating them as pre-existing perforations. The same principle applies to the *shochet*: when we have a specific, plausible cause to which we can attribute a hole, and that cause is directly associated with the location of the hole, we assign the damage to that cause. One might note that a wolf's teeth and a *shochet*'s hands are rather different instruments, and that in practice one might also consider whether the shape or character of a hole — a tear versus a circular puncture, for instance — offers additional evidence. The *gemara*, however, establishes the general principle that a specific identifiable cause, when it is reasonable and directly associated with the location of the damage, is sufficient to permit the animal.

### Worm Holes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּרְנָא, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר דּוֹסַאי וְרַבָּנַן, חַד אָמַר: קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה פָּרֵישׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה פָּרֵישׁ, וְהִלְכְתָא: לְאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה פָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, if a perforation was caused by **a worm [*murana*], Rav Yosef bar Dosai and the Rabbis disagree. One says** that the worm **emerged** from the lung and perforated it **before** the **slaughter,** and the animal is a *tereifa*, **and one says** that **it emerged after** the **slaughter,** and the animal is kosher. **And the *halakha*** is that one presumes that **it emerged after** the **slaughter.**

A related case: if a worm is found emerging from a hole in the lung, do we assume the worm bored through while the animal was alive — which would make the hole a *treifah* condition — or after *shechitah*? There was a dispute between Rav Yosef bar Dosai and the Rabbanan. The *halacha* is *le'achar shechitah parish* — we assume the worm separated, i.e., created the hole, after *shechitah*. Once again, having a specific cause — the worm itself — allows us to attribute the hole to a post-*shechitah* event and rule the animal permitted.

This raises an obvious practical question: how much time can have elapsed between *shechitah* and the discovery of the worm before this attribution becomes implausible? The *gemara* does not address this directly. One might understand the underlying logic in formal *halachic* terms: a slaughtered animal carries a presumption of permissibility (*chezkas hetter*), and when there is something specific to which a defect can be attributed, we lean on that specificity to preserve the presumption. Yet this has to be weighed against the reality that lung defects are precisely the area of *treifos* that demands the most vigilance — indeed, lungs are essentially the only organ we check routinely, precisely because of the prevalence of holes and *sirchos*. The straightforward attribution to a worm or a *shochet* may thus require more supporting context in practice than the bare *gemara* ruling suggests.

## Rabbi Shimon

### Shimon's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁתִּינָּקֵב. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: עַד שֶׁתִּינָּקֵב לְסִמְפּוֹן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **Rabbi Shimon says:** An animal with a perforated lung is not a *tereifa* **until it is perforated** through to the bronchi. With regard to this, **Rabba bar Taḥlifa says** that **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says:** Rabbi Shimon meant specifically that it is not a *tereifa* **unless it is perforated** through **to the large bronchus,** from which the smaller bronchi branch out.

The *gemara* now takes up the final opinion in the *mishnah* regarding lung defects: that of Rabbi Shimon, who adopts a dramatically more lenient position. Rabbah bar Tachlifa says in the name of Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba: Rabbi Shimon holds that a lung is only *treif* when the perforation penetrates *l'simpon gadol* — into the major central tube running through the lung. A hole that reaches only one of the smaller subsidiary tubes feeding into the individual lobes (*onot*) is insufficient to create a *treifah* condition. If this opinion were normative, it would transform the landscape of *treifos* entirely: almost all lung *treifos* — including the vast majority of *sircha*-related issues — would be eliminated, since they typically do not implicate the major central tube.

### Meloch Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: אָמַר רַבִּי מַלּוּךְ אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַלּוּךְ עַרְבָאָה קָאָמְרַתְּ? אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Aḥa bar Abba sat before Rav Huna, and he was sitting and saying: Rabbi Malokh says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Rav Huna **said to him: Are you saying** this in the name of **Malokh the Arab? He says** that the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with Rabbi Shimon.**

It is therefore of enormous practical significance to know whether Rabbi Shimon's view is the ruling *halacha*. The *gemara* records that Rav Acha bar Abba was sitting before Rav Huna and reported: Rabbi Meloch said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon*, we rule like Rabbi Shimon. This would be a radical conclusion with sweeping consequences.

### Huna Correction


Rav Huna immediately pushed back: do you mean Meloch from Arabia? I know what he says — *ein halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon*, he said the *halacha* is *not* like Rabbi Shimon. The tradition had been transmitted in precisely the opposite direction from how Rav Acha bar Abba had reported it.

### Zeira's Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סָלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב בִּיבִי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: אָמַר רַבִּי מַלּוּךְ אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַיֵּי דְּמָר, דַּאֲנָא וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא וְרַב אַסִּי אִיקַּלְעִינַן לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מַלּוּךְ, וַאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: אִי אָמַר מָר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? וַאֲמַר לַן: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֲמַרִי. וְאַתְּ מָה בִּידָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **When Rabbi Zeira went up** to Eretz Yisrael, **he found Rav Beivai, who was sitting and saying: Rabbi Malokh says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Rabbi Zeira **said to him:** On the **Master's life, I and Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba and Rav Asi visited the place** of residence **of Rabbi Malokh, and we said to him: Did** the **Master say that** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon? And he said to us: I said** that the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Rav Beivai asked Rabbi Zeira: **And you, what** more do you have **in your possession** on this matter? Rabbi Zeira **said to him: This** is what **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Ami says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

The *gemara* then records that when Rabbi Zeira went up to *eretz yisrael*, he found Rav Bibi sitting and transmitting the same tradition: Rabbi Meloch said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon*. Rabbi Zeira responded with an oath: I, together with Rabbi Chiya bar Abba and Rav Assi, traveled to the very place of Rabbi Meloch — a serious journey if, as the word suggests, he lived in Arabia — and asked him directly: did the master say we rule like Rabbi Shimon? He answered us: *ein halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon ami* — no, I said the *halacha* is not like Rabbi Shimon.

Rav Bibi then turned the question back to Rabbi Zeira: and what is your own personal position? Rabbi Zeira replied that he had a separate tradition: Rabbi Yitzchak bar Ami said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon*, the *halacha* is like Rabbi Shimon. This is the same Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi being cited, now through a different transmitter, in the opposite direction from what Rabbi Meloch is said to have actually transmitted.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rules: **And** the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Any perforation of the membranes of the lung renders the animal a *tereifa*.

The *gemara* concludes with a terse ruling: *ve'ein halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon* — we do not rule like Rabbi Shimon. Rashi explains that this line represents the *gemara*'s own final determination, issued in what he calls the last convocation (*yeshiva ha'achronah*) — the latest editorial layer of the *gemara*, in which undecided questions received their authoritative resolution.

### Halacha Process


This passage raises profound questions about how *halacha* is determined. The entire discussion revolves around recovering one tradition — what Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said through Rabbi Meloch — with rabbis undertaking long journeys to verify it. And yet at the end of the day, two equally credentialed traditions pointing in opposite directions are both attributed to the same Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and the *gemara* simply rules against Rabbi Shimon without explicit justification.

Several points compound the difficulty. First, the *bavli* is generally more willing than the *yerushalmi* to adjudicate on the basis of *sevara* — logical reasoning — rather than tradition alone. Yet here, extraordinary effort is invested in tracing a single chain of transmission, while the logical and practical implications of the question receive comparatively little discussion. Second, and more tellingly: if we were to rule like Rabbi Shimon, ninety percent of the lung *treifos* discussed throughout the preceding *sugyot* would simply dissolve. The entire prior discussion would become largely academic. The weight of all those earlier cases — the *sirchos*, the various holes, the elaborate analysis of lung anatomy — strongly implies that in practice, things were *not* ruled like Rabbi Shimon. That tacit evidence of practice may ultimately be more decisive than any single chain of oral tradition, however carefully preserved. The question of how the *gemara* arrived at its final ruling here — whether through deliberation, through the weight of accumulated practice, or through later editorial consolidation — remains genuinely open.

## Chelev Discussion

### Fat on Stomach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיקְּבָה הַקֵּבָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: חֵלֶב שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קֵבָה – כֹּהֲנִים נָהֲגוּ בּוֹ הֶיתֵּר, כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אֲבוֹתָיו. וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״יִשְׁמָעֵאל כָּהֲנָא מְסַיַּיע כָּהֲנֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: If **the abomasum was perforated,** the animal is a *tereifa*. Concerning this, **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says:** With regard to **fat that is on the abomasum,** the **priests accustomed** themselves to consider **it permitted,** and they would eat it along with the rest of the abomasum, which was given to them from non-sacred animals as one of the gifts of the priesthood. This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael that he says in the name of his forefathers. And** since there is a disagreement between the Sages with regard to this issue, **your mnemonic** to remember the lenient opinion is the idiom: **Yishmael the priest** generally **helps the priests.**

Having concluded the extended discussion of lung *treifos*, the *gemara* turns to the *keivah*, the fourth stomach, in which the digestive process is completed and which most closely corresponds to the human stomach. A hole in the *keivah* renders the animal *treif*. But before returning directly to that *treifah* issue, the *gemara* digresses into the question of *chelev* — forbidden fat — and specifically which fat associated with the *keivah* is in that category.

Rav Yitzchak bar Pinchas says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: *chelev she'al gabei hakeivah kohanim nahagu bo heter* — the *kohanim* treated the fat on the surface of the *keivah* as permissible. Rashi notes the obvious interest at play here: the *keivah* is one of the priestly gifts (*matanos kehunah*) given to the *kohen* from a slaughtered animal, and it comes together with the fat sitting on it. It was therefore very much in the *kohanim*'s interest to rule that this fat was not forbidden *chelev*. They were following the position of Rabbi Yishmael, who said so in the name of his fathers.

### Yishmael Position


Rabbi Yishmael's position is that the fat on the *keivah* does not fall within the category of biblically forbidden *chelev*, and therefore the *kohanim* could consume it lawfully together with their priestly portion.

### Yishmael Helps Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״כֹּה תְבָרְכוּ אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: לָמַדְנוּ בְּרָכָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִפִּי כֹּהֲנִים, לְכֹהֲנִים עַצְמָן לֹא לָמַדְנוּ. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַאֲנִי אֲבָרְכֵם״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּהֲנִים מְבָרְכִין לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְבָרֵךְ לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** this, i.e., where does Rabbi Yishmael help the priests? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the priestly benediction: **"So you shall bless the children of Israel"** (Numbers 6:23). **Rabbi Yishmael says: We learn** from this verse about **a blessing for Israel from the mouth of the priests,** but **we have not learned** about a blessing **for the priests themselves. When it says** afterward with regard to the priests: "And they shall put My name upon the children of Israel, **and I will bless them"** (Numbers 6:27), **you must say:** The **priests bless Israel, and the Holy One, Blessed be He, blesses the priests.**

The *gemara* offers a mnemonic: *yishmael kahana mesaya kahanei* — Rabbi Yishmael, himself a *kohen*, issued rulings that benefited the *kohanim*. The *gemara* is careful not to impugn his integrity; the point is simply that this pattern can help one remember his positions. The *gemara* then asks for a second example of this tendency, and brings a *midrashic* teaching: on the verse *koh tevorchu es benei Yisrael* — "thus shall you bless the children of Israel" — Rabbi Yishmael derives that Israel is blessed through the *kohanim*, and then reads the continuation, *va'ani avarachem* — "and I will bless them" — as God promising to bless the *kohanim* directly. The *kohanim* bless Israel; God blesses the *kohanim*. So the *kohanim* receive their blessing directly from God, while Israel's blessing is mediated through the *kohanim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לָמַדְנוּ בְּרָכָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִפִּי כֹהֲנִים, מִפִּי גְבוּרָה לֹא לָמַדְנוּ. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וַאֲנִי אֲבָרְכֵם״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּהֲנִים מְבָרְכִין לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַסְכִּים עַל יָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says** a different interpretation: **We learned** from the verse about **a blessing for Israel from the mouth of the priests,** but **we have not learned** about a blessing for Israel **from the mouth of the Almighty. When it says** afterward: "And they shall put My name upon the children of Israel, **and I will bless them," you must say: The priests bless Israel, and the Holy One, Blessed be He, affirms their** blessing. The word "them" is referring to Israel.

Rabbi Akiva reads the same verses differently. He too sees the first clause as establishing that Israel is blessed through the *kohanim*, but reads *va'ani avarachem* as God saying: I will bless the people as well. The "them" refers to Israel, not the *kohanim*. God is agreeing with and reinforcing the *kohanim*'s blessing of Israel, but the *kohanim* themselves receive no special blessing from this verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, בְּרָכָה לְכֹהֲנִים מְנָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִ״וַּאֲבָרְכָה מְבָרְכֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then **from where does Rabbi Akiva** learn about **a blessing for the priests? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** He learns it **from** God's promise to Abraham: **"And I will bless them that bless you"** (Genesis 12:3). All who bless the Jewish people are blessed themselves.

The *gemara* then asks: where, according to Rabbi Akiva, do the *kohanim* receive their blessing? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers: from the verse *me'avarchacha mevorachecha* — "I will bless those who bless you" — the blessing originally given to Avraham. Since the *kohanim* bless Israel, they are included in God's promise to bless those who bless His people. Tosafot notes parenthetically that a *yerushalmi* applies this principle even to non-Jews who bless Israel. If that is so, the *kohanim*'s inclusion in this guarantee is far less distinctive — they are merely one instance of a universal rule. This makes Rabbi Yishmael's *derasha* considerably more elegant: in his reading, the *kohanim* receive a blessing specifically tied to and commensurate with the blessing they give to Israel, as part of the same divine utterance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי מְסַיַּיע כָּהֲנֵי? דְּמוֹקֵי לֵהּ לְבִרְכַּת כֹּהֲנִים בִּמְקוֹם בְּרָכָה דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** sense does only Rabbi Yishmael **help the priests,** given that Rabbi Akiva concedes that the priests are also blessed? The Gemara responds: He helps the priests in the sense **that he establishes the blessing for the priests in the** same **place,** the same verse, that one finds **the blessing of Israel,** indicating that they receive the same blessing.

### Chelev Characteristics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אֲבוֹתָיו, מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אֶת כׇּל הַחֵלֶב אֲשֶׁר עַל הַקֶּרֶב
> וְגוֹ׳״ – לְהָבִיא חֵלֶב שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַדַּקִּין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לְהָבִיא חֵלֶב שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַקֵּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated that the priests accustomed themselves to permit the fat on the abomasum, in accordance with the statement of **Rabbi Yishmael that he says in the name of his forefathers.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** this statement? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that when the verse states with regard to forbidden fats mentioned in the Torah: **"All the fat that is upon the innards"** (Leviticus 3:3),
> the word "all" serves **to include** the **fat that is on the small intestines;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** The word "all" serves **to include** the **fat that is on the abomasum.** According to Rabbi Yishmael, the fat of the abomasum is permitted.

Returning to the substance of the *chelev* question, the *gemara* cites a *beraita* that attempts to define what constitutes forbidden *chelev*. The underlying premise is that the fat specified for the altar (*korban*) is also what is forbidden to ordinary Israelites. Rabbi Shimon (later identified as Rabbi Yishmael — the *gemara* will flag a textual inversion) says: the defining characteristic of forbidden *chelev* is that it has a *krum veniklaf* — a membrane that can be peeled away, leaving the fat mass beneath. This peelable membrane distinguishes forbidden *chelev* from the softer, more adhesive fat (*shuman*) found integrated within meat.

Rabbi Akiva adds a further criterion: *totav krum veniklaf* — forbidden *chelev* must both have a peelable membrane *and* be *totav*, meaning it cloaks or sheathes an organ from outside without adhering to it. Rashi explains *totav* as describing fat that lies over an organ like a cloak: visually surrounding it, but not stuck to it, and therefore separable as a distinct mass. Fat that has a membrane but is adhesive to the organ beneath it — like the fat on the *keivah* — would meet the first criterion but not the second, and would therefore not be forbidden *chelev* according to Rabbi Akiva.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: ״אֶת הַחֵלֶב אֲשֶׁר עַל הַקֶּרֶב״ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה חֵלֶב הַמְכַסֶּה אֶת הַקֶּרֶב קְרוּם וְנִקְלָף, אַף כֹּל קְרוּם וְנִקְלָף. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מָה חֵלֶב הַמְכַסֶּה אֶת הַקֶּרֶב תּוֹתָב קְרוּם וְנִקְלָף, אַף כֹּל תּוֹתָב קְרוּם וְנִקְלָף.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: The verse states: "The fat that covers the innards, and all **the fat that is upon the innards"** (Leviticus 3:3). **Rabbi Shimon,** i.e., Rabbi Yishmael, **says: Just as the fat that covers the innards** possesses **a membrane, and** that membrane is easily **peeled** off, **so too, all** fat possessing **a membrane and** whose membrane is easily **peeled** is forbidden, including the fat on the abomasum. **Rabbi Akiva says: Just as the fat that covers the innards is spread out** loosely over them, and it possesses **a membrane, and** the membrane is easily **peeled** off, **so too, all** fat that is **spread out** loosely, and possesses **a membrane, and** whose membrane is easily **peeled,** e.g., the fat on the intestines, is forbidden. The fat on the abomasum is fixed tightly and is therefore permitted. It would seem from this *baraita* that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael contradict themselves.

### Reversal Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח רָבִין מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּךְ הִיא הַצָּעָה שֶׁל מִשְׁנָה, וְאֵיפוֹךְ קַמַּיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravin sent** an answer **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Such is the** proper **layout of the mishna,** i.e., the *baraita*. The latter attribution of the opinions is correct, **and** one must **reverse** the attribution of opinions in **the first** *baraita*. Accordingly, Rabbi Akiva deems the fat on the intestines prohibited, while Rabbi Yishmael deems the fat on the abomasum prohibited as well.

At this point the *gemara* faces a contradiction. Earlier, Rabbi Akiva was the one who said the verse *ve'et kol hachelev asher al hakerev* comes to include the fat on the *keivah* — implying he viewed it as forbidden — while Rabbi Yishmael held it was the fat on the small intestines (*hadakin*) that was included, implicitly excluding the *keivah*. But now, with the additional *totav* criterion that Rabbi Akiva requires, the fat on the *keivah* (adhesive, not cloaking) would be *excluded* from forbidden *chelev* according to Rabbi Akiva, while under Rabbi Yishmael's single-criterion standard it would be *included*. The positions appear reversed.

The resolution, sent by Ravin in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is that the attribution in the first teaching should be flipped: *ve'ipeich kamayta* — reverse the first *beraita*. Rabbi Akiva should be the one who said the verse includes the fat on the small intestines (thereby excluding the *keivah*), and Rabbi Yishmael should be the one who said it includes the fat on the *keivah*. This aligns both *beraisos*: Rabbi Yishmael consistently applies a single criterion (membrane alone), which makes *keivah* fat forbidden; Rabbi Akiva applies two criteria (membrane *and* cloaking), which excludes *keivah* fat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חָזֵית דְּאָפְכַתְּ קַמַּיְיתָא? אֵיפוֹךְ בָּתְרָיְיתָא! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּקָתָנֵי ״מָה״, דַּוְוקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did you see that** led **you** to **reverse the first** *baraita*? Why not **reverse the second** *baraita* instead? The Gemara responds: The second *baraita* **here is different, since it teaches** using the formula: **Just as.** Because it takes care to **specifically** provide the textual justifications for the opinions, it is presumably accurate about the attributions as well.

The *gemara* asks why we reverse the first *baraita* rather than the second. The answer is that since the second *baraita* speaks in precise definitional terms — explicitly articulating specific characteristics — its attribution is more reliable. When positions are being carefully enumerated by characteristic, the tradition is more precise, and so we trust the second *baraita* and correct the first accordingly.

### Yishmael Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אֲבוֹתָיו, וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** how can Rabbi Oshaya claim above that the priests acted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael? It** was actually **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** Rabbi Oshaya meant only **that** Rabbi Yishmael **says** the lenient opinion **in the name of his forefathers, but** Rabbi Yishmael **himself does not hold accordingly,** and he considers the fat of the abomasum forbidden.

This creates a new problem. We said that the *kohanim* followed Rabbi Yishmael in ruling the *keivah* fat permissible. But after the reversal, it is Rabbi Akiva — not Rabbi Yishmael — who holds that the *keivah* fat is not forbidden *chelev*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *she'amar mishum avosav velo svira lei* — Rabbi Yishmael was transmitting his fathers' tradition (that the *keivah* fat is permissible), but he did not himself hold that view. His personal opinion was that the fat on the *keivah* is indeed forbidden. So the *kohanim* who ate that fat were relying on a family tradition reported by Rabbi Yishmael — a tradition he himself rejected. This is a striking case: Rabbi Yishmael faithfully transmits an ancestral ruling while personally disagreeing with it, and the *gemara* does not frame this as inconsistency but simply as an honest reporting of a tradition alongside his own independent ruling. It should be noted that *velo svira lei* does not mean he had no opinion; it means he actively rejected that position.

## Fat Plugging Holes

### Rav's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: חֵלֶב טָהוֹר – סוֹתֵם, טָמֵא – אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵם. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה סוֹתֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a connection between the issue of fats and that of *tereifot*: **Rav says: Kosher fat** effectively **seals** a perforation that it covers, and the animal is not rendered a *tereifa*. **Non-kosher** fat **does not** effectively **seal** a perforation, and the animal is a *tereifa*. **And Rav Sheshet says: Both this and that** fat effectively **seal** a perforation.

With the nature of *chelev* now established, the *gemara* returns to its practical implications for *treifah* law. Rav rules: *chelev tahor soteim, tamei eino soteim* — permissible fat (*chelev tahor*) can plug and seal a hole in an organ; forbidden fat (*chelev tamei*) cannot. The reasoning connects directly to the physical characteristics discussed above. Permissible fat — the kind that lacks the defining membrane and cloaking property of forbidden *chelev* — is adhesive and sticky. Because it adheres firmly to the tissue around it, it can seal a hole effectively and permanently. Forbidden *chelev*, surrounded by a peelable membrane and not adhering to the organ, will not form a lasting seal. A hole plugged by forbidden *chelev* remains, in effect, an open hole.

### Sheshet Lenient


Rav Sheshet takes a more lenient position: *echad zeh ve'echad zeh soteim* — either type of *chelev* is sufficient to plug a hole. He does not distinguish between permitted and forbidden fat with regard to their sealing capacity.

### Deer Fat Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: חֵלֶב חַיָּה מַאי? דַּוְקָא אָמַר חֵלֶב טָהוֹר סוֹתֵם, וְהַאי נָמֵי טָהוֹר הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דִּמְהַדַּק, וְהַאי לָא מְהַדַּק?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma:** With regard to the **fat of an undomesticated animal,** all of which is permitted for consumption, **what** is the *halakha*? Shall one say that Rav **specifically said** that **kosher fat seals** a perforation, **and** since **this too,** i.e., all the fat of an undomesticated animal, **is kosher,** it is all considered an effective seal? **Or perhaps** Rav specified that permitted fat of a domesticated animal seals a perforation only **because it is** firmly **attached. But this,** the fat on the innards of an undomesticated animal, **is not** firmly **attached,** and it is not an effective seal, even though it is permitted for consumption.

Rav Zeira poses a challenge: *chelev chayah mai* — what about the fat of a *chayah*, a non-domesticated animal such as a deer, where all fat is permissible? Under Rav's formulation, one might argue that since all the deer's fat is *tahor*, all of it should count as plugging holes. Or one might say the criterion is not halachic status but physical properties, and the deer's fat that corresponds to what would be forbidden *chelev* in a cow is still not sticky — making it unable to plug holes regardless of its permissibility.

### Stickiness Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? נְהִי דִּשְׁרֵי בַּאֲכִילָה, אִהַדּוֹקֵי לָא מְהַדַּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Why** does Rabbi Zeira **raise this dilemma? Granted that** the fat of the innards is **permitted for consumption,** but still, **it is not** firmly **attached** and clearly does not form an effective seal.

Abaye dismisses the question as obvious: *nihei d'shari b'achilah, bahadukei lo mehadeik* — yes, it is permissible to eat, but it is not sticky. The permissibility of deer fat is a function of the species, not a change in the fat's physical character. Fat that would be forbidden *chelev* in a cow retains the same consistency in a deer — it simply lacks the adhesive quality — and therefore cannot plug holes, regardless of its kosher status. The criterion is physical stickiness (*hadukah*), not halachic category. Tosafot adds that the same logic applies to bird fat: since bird fat that covers organs is by nature adhesive, it would be considered to plug holes effectively, and Rabbeinu Yitzchak ruled accordingly that such fat resolving a *treifah* concern in birds is valid.

## Practical Cases

### Hole/Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא נֶקֶב דְּסַתְמֵהּ חֵלֶב טָמֵא, דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אָמַר רָבָא: לְמַאי נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? חֲדָא – דְּהָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: חֵלֶב טָמֵא נָמֵי סוֹתֵם, וְעוֹד – הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: רַב, וְאִיסּוּרָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ ״הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל״?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain perforation that was sealed by non-kosher fat that came before Rava. Rava said: With regard to what** need **we be concerned? First, doesn't Rav Sheshet say: Non-kosher fat also** effectively **seals** a perforation? **And furthermore,** in general, **the Torah spares the money of the Jewish people,** and it is appropriate to rule leniently in this regard. **Rav Pappa said to Rava:** But there is also the opinion of **Rav** that non-kosher fat does not seal a perforation, **and** this dispute concerns **a prohibition by Torah law, and** yet **you say** that **the Torah spares the money of the Jewish people?** One cannot rely on this principle to rule in accordance with the lenient opinion with regard to such matters.

The *gemara* now presents a real case that came before Rava: *hahu nekev d'sasmei chelev tamei* — a hole in an organ was filled with forbidden *chelev*. Based on Rav's ruling, the forbidden fat does not effectively plug the hole, and the animal should be *treif*. Yet Rava considered ruling leniently.

Rava offered two grounds for leniency: first, invoking the opinion of Rav Sheshet that even forbidden *chelev* seals a hole; and second, the principle of *ha-Torah chasah al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah is concerned with the financial loss of Israel. Since declaring the animal *treif* would cause significant monetary loss, Rava felt the combination of Rav Sheshet's position and the principle of financial concern warranted a lenient ruling.

### Pappa Challenge


Rav Pappa challenged Rava on both counts. On the first: Rav's opinion — that forbidden *chelev* does not seal holes — is at least equally authoritative as Rav Sheshet's, and may well be the dominant view. On the second: the principle of the Torah's concern for financial loss is invoked in cases of rabbinic prohibition, not in cases of biblical prohibition (*issur de-Oraisa*). Where a genuine biblical question is at stake, one must rule stringently when there is a substantive dispute, not leniently because of financial concern. This is a pointed and significant principle: *chesah al mamonam shel Yisrael* is a genuine value in *halacha*, but it has limits — it cannot override the requirements of biblical law in a case of real doubt.

### Honey Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְיוֹמִין כַּנְדּוּקָא אִיגַּלִּי לֵיהּ בֻּסְתְּקָא דְּדוּבְשָׁא, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אָמַר רָבָא: לְמַאי נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? חֲדָא – דִּתְנַן, שְׁלֹשָׁה מַשְׁקִים אֲסוּרִים מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי: הַיַּיִן וְהַמַּיִם וְהֶחָלָב, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַמַּשְׁקִים מוּתָּרִים, וְעוֹד – הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרָבָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְסַכָּנַת נְפָשׁוֹת, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ ״הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar episode: **Manyumin the jug maker** had **a jug [*bisteka*]** full **of honey** that **was** left **uncovered,** and he was concerned it might have been contaminated by snake venom. **He came before Rava** to inquire as to the *halakha*. **Rava said: With regard to what** need **we be concerned? First, as we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 8:4): **Three liquids are prohibited due to exposure: Wine, water, and milk; and all other liquids are permitted.** Honey, therefore, is permitted. **And furthermore, the Torah spares the money of the Jewish people. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rava:** But there is also the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** that exposed honey is prohibited, **and** this dispute concerns a matter of **mortal danger, and** yet **you say** that **the Torah spares the money of the Jewish people?**

The *gemara* then pivots to a parallel situation involving a different kind of potential loss, though with the additional dimension of danger to life. A certain Minyomin Kanduka — apparently a barrel or container merchant — had a container of honey that was left uncovered (*giluy*). The concern about uncovered liquids is that snakes may have introduced venom into them, making them dangerous to drink. He came before Rava, who again leaned toward leniency on two grounds. First, the *mishnah* explicitly lists only three liquids as problematic when left uncovered: wine, water, and milk — all other liquids, including honey, are permissible (*sha'ar kol ha-mashkin mutarin*). Second, here again, the Torah's concern for financial loss: the honey would have to be discarded at significant cost.

### Nachman Counter


Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak pushed back sharply: *rabbi shimon ve-sakanas nefashos ve-at amarat ha-torah chasah al mamonam shel yisrael*? There is a dissenting opinion — that of Rabbi Shimon — who holds that honey is problematic when uncovered, and this is not merely a matter of rabbinic prohibition but a genuine question of danger to life. Financial concern cannot override the risk of mortal danger. The *gemara* then specifies what Rabbi Shimon says: a *beraita* lists five liquids that are generally not subject to the *giluy* concern — fish brine, vinegar, oil, honey, and *muris* (a type of pickling liquid). Rabbi Shimon rules that even these five are problematic when uncovered. And Rabbi Shimon himself offered evidence: *ani ra'isi nachash she-shasah tzir be-Tzidon* — I personally witnessed a snake drink fish brine in Tzidon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: חֲמִשָּׁה אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי: צִיר, וָחוֹמֶץ, שֶׁמֶן, וּדְבַשׁ, וּמוּרְיָיס. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף הֵן יֵשׁ בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי. וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי נָחָשׁ שֶׁשָּׁתָה צִיר בְּצַיְדָּן. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שָׁטְיָא הֲוָה, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַשּׁוֹטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** this opinion of **Rabbi Shimon? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Five** liquids **are not** subject to the prohibition of **exposure,** since snakes do not drink from them: **Fish brine, vinegar, oil, honey, and fish gravy [*morayes*]** made from fish brine mixed with oil and salt. **Rabbi Shimon says: Even they are** subject to prohibition **due to exposure. And Rabbi Shimon said: I** once **saw a snake that drank fish brine in Tzaidan.** The Rabbis **said to him:** That snake **was strange,** i.e., it behaved differently than other snakes, **and one does not bring a proof from strange** creatures.

### Stupid Snake


The response to Rabbi Shimon's empirical evidence is dismissive: *shoteh hayah* — that was a foolish snake, and *ein mevivin ra'ayah min ha-shotim* — we do not derive legal conclusions from anomalous cases. The anecdote from Rav Soloveitchik about the dog that ate toothpaste — whether he called it a *shoteh kelev* in the manner of the *shoteh nachash*, or instead simply asked "Who is your *rebbe* — me or the dog?" — captures exactly this methodological point: individual observed anomalies do not override established legal categories.

### Honey Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹדִי לִי מִיהַת בְּצִיר, דְּהָא רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבָּנַן, כִּי הֲוָה לְהוּ גִּילּוּיָא, שָׁדוּ לֵיהּ בְּצִיר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹדִי לִי מִיהָא בִּדְבַשׁ, דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר קָאֵי כְּוָותֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר בִּדְבַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: **Concede to me, at least, with regard to fish brine** that it is not prohibited by exposure, **as when Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Yehoshua, and the Sages would have** a case of **exposure** of some liquid, **they would cast it into fish brine.** The sharpness of the brine neutralizes the snake venom. Accordingly, just as the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon with regard to fish brine, so too it is not in accordance with his opinion with regard to honey. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **said to him: Concede to me, at least, with regard to honey** that it is prohibited by exposure, **as Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar held in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And likewise, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar would deem** exposed **honey prohibited.**

Despite this dismissal of the fish-brine evidence, Rava then asked Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak to concede at least that we do not rule like Rabbi Shimon regarding fish brine specifically — given that the very rabbis who invoked him, Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, would themselves counter the effects of *giluy* by placing suspected liquids into fish brine, indicating that fish brine itself is not a *giluy* concern. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak countered: at least admit that we *do* rule stringently regarding honey. His basis: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — a different authority from Rabbi Shimon — rules that honey is forbidden when uncovered. Since Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar independently takes the same position on honey as Rabbi Shimon, there is a second voice for stringency in this specific case, and given that we are dealing with danger to life, one cannot dismiss this ruling on financial grounds.

### Analysis


The broader principle emerging from this discussion is that *chesah al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah's concern for Jewish financial loss — is a genuine and operative value in *psak halacha*, but it is conditioned by two significant constraints. First, it does not override biblical prohibitions in cases of genuine *halachic* doubt: where the dispute is at the level of *de-Oraisa*, one cannot lean on financial concern as a tiebreaker toward leniency. Second, and even more forcefully, it does not override concerns about danger to life: when the question involves potential mortal harm, no amount of financial loss can shift the balance toward leniency. What makes the honey case particularly instructive is that had there been no opinion at all countering the *stam Mishnah* that permits honey when uncovered, Rava's position would have been quite reasonable — a *stam Mishnah* and a financial concern together constitute a genuine basis for leniency. It was precisely the existence of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's independent ruling, combined with the seriousness of the danger, that tipped the scales toward stringency in this specific instance.
