# Avodah Zarah 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oil Kashrut & Repealed D…

### Oil Prohibition Overview

The final line of 35b introduces the next topic of the *mishnah*: the *kashrut* of oil produced by idolaters. The *mishnah* records that *Rabbi u-veit dino* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his *beit din* — permitted the oil, implying that at some earlier point a prohibition had been in force and was subsequently repealed. The discussion therefore proceeds on two tracks: when was the prohibition first instituted, and why, and how and why was it later repealed.

This case is notable as one of the relatively few examples in *Shas* of a rabbinic *gezeirah* that was formally abrogated, and it becomes an important test case for understanding the scope and parameters of rabbinic legislative authority.

### Cheese Prohibition Digre…

Before turning to the oil, it is worth noting the prior discussion of why non-Jewish cheese was prohibited. The *sugyah* canvassed numerous explanations, and *Tosafot*'s conclusion appears to follow Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position that the concern is snake venom — a matter of *sakana*. Characterizing it as a *sakana* opens the question of whether, if the danger could be demonstrably eliminated, the prohibition might no longer apply, at least according to those authorities who reason that way. Indeed, some have argued that the *Rama*, the *Chazon Ish*, and even Rav Soloveitchik ruled in line with *Tosafot* in various circumstances.

By contrast, the *Rambam* and many other prominent *rishonim* hold that the prohibition stems from the *neveilah* concern: the rennet used in cheese production is derived from the stomach lining of an animal slaughtered without *shechita*, making it a piece of actual meat. Because this *ma'amid* — the coagulating agent — imparts the flavor of *neveilah* into the cheese and cannot be *batel*, the rabbis decreed against all cheese produced by non-Jewish manufacturers. On this view, even cheeses produced today with microbial or vegetable-derived curdling agents enter a debate already found among the *geonim* and *rishonim* about whether non-animal curdling agents fall under the *gezeirah* at all. The *Rambam* and many *geonim* were *machmir* on this point, though the Talmud itself leaves the matter open.

### Oil Repeal Question

Returning to the *shemen*: the discussion that follows asks both why the prohibition was originally instituted and what justification Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi had for repealing it.

---

## Rav's Daniel Theory

### Daniel Gazar Alav

*Rav amar*: *Daniel gazar alav* — it was Daniel and his *beit din*, during the period of the Babylonian exile, who decreed against the use of oil produced by idolaters. Daniel is not classified as a *navi* in the strict sense within the rabbinic tradition, but he occupies a role close to that of a prophet, and this attribution grants the *gezeirah* considerable antiquity and authority.

### Shmuel's Tamei Vessel Th…

*Shmuel*, however, offers a different rationale: *zelifatan shel keilim tmeiyim osartan* — the prohibition arose because of the seepage of *tamei* liquids from the vessels in which the oil was stored. The concern is that a jar already containing absorbed *tamei* substances would leach those substances into the olive oil placed within it, rendering the oil *tamei*.

### Tahara Not Universal

The *gemara* challenges Shmuel's formulation immediately: *atu kulei alma ochlei taharot ninhu?* — is everyone who eats food required to maintain *tahara*? Eating *chulin* in a state of *tahara* was a *midat chasidut*, a special stringency practiced by portions of the rabbinic class during the *mishnaic* period, but it was never obligatory for the general population. A *gezeirah* premised on that concern would rest on a very narrow foundation.

### Prohibited Vessels Seepa…

Shmuel accordingly revises his position: *zelifatan shel keilim assurim osartan* — the concern is not *tamei* vessels but prohibited (*assur*) vessels, that is, containers that had previously held non-kosher wine, lard, or other prohibited substances. The absorbed flavor of those prohibited materials would seep into the olive oil stored in the same vessels. One might initially object that such contamination would degrade the quality of the oil and therefore constitute *noten ta'am lifgam* — a detrimental flavor — which is ordinarily permissible. The *gemara* will address this directly.

---

## Shmuel vs. Rav Debate

### Shmuel's Logic for Repeal

Shmuel now presses his advantage. *Bishlama ledidi* — his own position explains coherently why the prohibition could later be repealed. As he notes, *Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta* reported that *Rabi Simlai* taught in *Ntzivin* that when Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his *beit din* took a vote on the oil, *nimnu alav vehitiruhu* — they voted to permit it. Their rationale, according to Shmuel, was *kasavar noten ta'am lifgam mutar*: they ruled, or reassessed, that any flavor that would be transferred from the prohibited vessels into the oil would damage rather than improve the oil's taste, and a *noten ta'am lifgam* imparts no halachic prohibition.

One might ask whether *noten ta'am lifgam* was simply not a recognized principle before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's time, or whether the earlier authorities had assessed the flavor transfer differently, or whether Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and his court were clarifying the proper application of an already-established rule. These are significant questions the *gemara* leaves open.

In any case, Shmuel's point is that if the original prohibition had a concrete and limited rationale — the seepage of non-kosher flavors into the oil — then a later *beit din* can reevaluate whether that concern still applies under current circumstances and repeal the *gezeirah* accordingly.

### Repeal Authority Challen…

Shmuel then turns the argument against Rav: *ela ledidach de'amarta Daniel gazar alav* — but on your account, Rav, that it was Daniel who decreed against the oil, how can you maintain that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi could subsequently come and repeal it? The concern here is one of institutional hierarchy and legal precedent. A *gezeirah* instituted by a figure of Daniel's stature, centuries earlier, represents a deeply embedded legal norm. And indeed, one must also consider how much of this was Daniel's personal stringency versus an actual promulgated decree — the *gemara* at this stage does not say *Daniel u-veit dino* but simply attributes the *gezeirah* to Daniel himself, which will become significant shortly.

### Ein Beit Din Yachol Leva…

The challenge is rooted in the well-known principle: *ein beit din yachol levatel divrei beit din chaveiro ela im ken gadol heimenu be-chokhma u-ve-minyan* — one court cannot abrogate the rulings of another court unless it is greater in wisdom and in number. This principle, discussed extensively in the first chapter of *Beitzah*, raises a fundamental question about the architecture of rabbinic law. There are at least two ways to read the principle. One reading addresses competing courts of equal standing within the same era, neither of which can override the other's local rulings — the remedy is to appeal to a higher authority, namely the *Sanhedrin*. The other reading, which appears to be the relevant one here, addresses the relationship between *batei din* across historical periods: can a later *Sanhedrin* repeal decrees issued by an earlier *Sanhedrin*?

The word *minyan* in the phrase *gadol heimenu be-chokhma u-ve-minyan* is itself subject to debate among the *rishonim*. It could refer literally to the number of members of the court — the *Sanhedrin* was normally seventy-one, though the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* had one hundred twenty members, which may be why certain enactments of Ezra's court are considered unrepeatable. Alternatively, *minyan* might refer not to the number of members but to the size of the court's constituency — the number of students and followers who adhere to that court's rulings, and thus a measure of its authority and reach. Both interpretations carry significant implications for how *dinim d'rabbanan* function over time.

### Ad Hominem vs. Lod

At this point in the debate, Rav attempts a different tactic. He questions whether the position that the oil prohibition was repealed really rests on a reliable source, asking: *amar lei Simlai Luda'ah ke-ha'amarta?* — is this really what Simlai from *Lod* said? And then: *shani Luda'ei de-mizalzelu* — people from *Lod* are known to be lax in their observance of rabbinic decrees, and therefore their opinions on the weight and bindingness of *dinim d'rabbanan* cannot be taken as authoritative. This is a remarkable move — an *ad hominem* attack on an entire community rather than engagement with the legal substance.

### Rav's Tradition Defense

The *gemara* indicates that this approach backfired: *ichsif* — Rav was embarrassed. The direction the argument had taken, attacking the community rather than the legal principle, was not the kind of reasoning Rav wanted to be associated with. He pivots to a substantive defense. *Amar Rav: im heim lo darshu ana lo darshinan* — if the tradition had not already interpreted the *pasuk* in this way, Rav himself would never have derived this law from the *drashah*. In other words, Rav concedes that the *pasuk* in Daniel is not independently compelling as a source for the prohibition; what makes the *drashah* meaningful is that it serves as a *mnemonic* for a law received through reliable tradition. The *drashah* is an *asmachta* that confirms what is already known from *mesorah*, not the source from which the law is derived.

---

## Daniel Proof & Binding N…

### Daniel 1:8 Citation

Rav proceeds to cite the verse: *vayasem Daniel al libo asher lo yisga'el bepas-bag hamelech uveyayin mishtav* — Daniel resolved not to defile himself with the king's bread or with his wine. The word *mishtav* is spelled *malei*, with a *yud*, which leads the *gemara* to read it as referring to two distinct types of drink: *bishtei mishta'ot hakasuv medaber*, the verse speaks of two kinds of drinking — one of wine and one of oil.

### Weak Drashah Admission

Rav openly acknowledges the weakness of this reading as an independent *drashah*. Left to its own devices, no interpreter would read the verse as referencing oil. The *drashah* is valid only because the tradition already establishes that Daniel avoided non-Jewish oil, and the verse provides a post-hoc textual anchor for that tradition.

### Personal vs. Binding Dec…

The real disagreement between Rav and Shmuel crystallizes around the question of whether Daniel's avoidance of oil constituted a personal practice or a binding legal decree. *Rav savar: al libo sam u-lechol Yisrael hora* — Rav holds that the phrase *al libo sam*, "he resolved in his heart," indicates that Daniel decided this for himself personally, but then *hora* — he issued a *hora'ah*, a legal ruling, making it binding upon all of Israel. The verb *hora* is significant: it implies a formal *psak*, not merely a personal stringency.

### Shmuel: Personal Decisio…

*Shmuel savar: al libo sam u-lechol Yisrael lo hora* — Shmuel holds that Daniel's resolution was purely personal. He decided this for himself but never promulgated it as a binding decree upon the community. On Shmuel's reading, there was therefore no *gezeirah* in the time of Daniel at all; the prohibition arose for entirely different reasons at a later period, as Shmuel explained, and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's court had full authority to reassess and repeal it.

## Eighteen Decrees Question

### Oil Among Eighteen?

A new question is raised: was the oil prohibition counted among the eighteen decrees enacted by *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* on the famous day when *Beit Shammai* outnumbered *Beit Hillel* and pushed through eighteen *gezeirot*? If oil was among those eighteen, the problem of repeal becomes even more acute.

### Daniel Rejected, Later A…

One possibility offered: Daniel did attempt to institute the decree, but the people did not accept it — *Daniel gazar velo kibeil*. Then, in a later generation, the students of *Hillel* and *Shammai* reinstated the same *gezeirah* and this time it was accepted: *va-atu talmidei d'Hillel ve-Shammai ve-gazur ve-kibeil*. If this is the case, one might ask: what was the point of Rav citing the Danielic origin of the decree if it was not accepted until the time of the students of *Hillel* and *Shammai*? The *gemara* regards this as a valid challenge.

### Urban vs. Rural Scope

An alternative resolution: *Daniel gazar alav ba'ir* — Daniel originally decreed against oil in urban settings, where the risk of adulteration or social intermingling was more acute. Later, the students of *Shammai* and *Hillel* extended the *gezeirah*: *va-atu inhu ve-gazur afilu basadeh* — they extended it to rural settings as well.

### Repeal Authority vs. Sha…

Either way, a serious difficulty remains: *ve-Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi heichi matzi l'mishrei takanta d'talmidei Shammai ve-Hillel* — how could Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi repeal a *takkanah* established by the students of *Shammai* and *Hillel*, who are of obviously far greater antiquity and seniority? The same principle reasserts itself: *ein beit din yachol levatel divrei beit din chaveiro ela im ken gadol heimenu be-chokhma u-ve-minyan*.

## Majority Acceptance Prin…

### All Decrees Repeatable E…

The *gemara* invokes an important principle from *Rabbah bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *bechol yachol levatel beit din divrei beit din chaveiro chutz mi-shmonah asar davar* — in general, any *beit din* can repeal the rulings of an earlier *beit din*, except for those eighteen specific matters.

### Eliyahu Cannot Repeal Ei…

The principle is underscored dramatically: *she-afilu yavo Eliyahu u-veit dino ein shomein lo* — even if Eliyahu himself arrives in the Messianic era and convenes a *beit din* to repeal those eighteen *gezeirot*, the ruling is not to be heeded. This raises a fascinating question: why specifically those eighteen? They were enacted on a single day when *Beit Shammai* achieved a majority over *Beit Hillel* — what makes those eighteen uniquely immune to repeal while every other *gezeirah* from earlier or later periods remains in principle reversible? The *gemara* does not pause to address this directly, but notes that Eliyahu's *beit din* would be subject to the same rule — prophetic authority and legislative authority are categorically distinct, as the *Rambam* elaborates extensively: prophets legislate only in their capacity as heads of the *Beit Din HaGadol*, never by virtue of their prophetic powers alone.

### Rav Mesharshiya: Spread…

*Rav Mesharshiya* provides the underlying rationale: *ho-il u-fashat issuro berov Yisrael* — those eighteen *gezeirot* are irreversible because they spread throughout the majority of the Jewish people and became universally accepted. *Shemen lo pashat issuro berov Yisrael* — the oil prohibition, by contrast, never achieved that broad acceptance. This is why Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was not only legally empowered to repeal it but had good reason to do so: the people were not observing it in any case.

### Investigation of Oil Acc…

This factual claim is supported by *Rabbi Shmuel bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *yashivu raboteinu badku al shemen shelo pashat issuro berov Yisrael* — the teachers of that generation — Rabbi Yochanan's era, shortly after Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — conducted an investigation and found that the prohibition against oil had not spread throughout the majority of Israel.

### Majority Uphold Principle

Upon this finding, *v'samchu raboteinu al divrei Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel ve-al divrei Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok* — they relied on the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, two *Tannaim* who stated: *ein gozrim gezeirah al ha-tzibur ela im ken rov tzibur yecholim la'amod bah* — a *beit din* should not, or perhaps cannot, decree a law upon the community unless the majority of the community is capable of upholding it.

This formulation leaves a critical ambiguity: is this a formal limitation on the legal power of the *beit din* — such that a *gezeirah* which fails to achieve majority acceptance is retroactively invalid — or is it merely a counsel of prudence, warning against enacting decrees that will generate widespread violation? If the former, then the failure of a *gezeirah* to take hold might in principle render it void. If the latter, it remains a judgment call about good legislative practice, not a juridical nullity.

Further, even if the principle does function as a formal limitation, it does not appear to operate automatically. What is required is active judicial intervention: a *beit din* must investigate, find that the *gezeirah* failed to spread, and formally repeal it. That is precisely what Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did. The law does not simply evaporate because people are not observing it; the authority of the *Beit Din HaGadol* is still necessary to give the repeal legal effect.

It is also worth noting that this position was in the minority: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* and *Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok* represent a dissenting Tannaitic view, one the majority of *Tannaim* apparently did not share. The mainstream position seems to have been that *gezeirot* may be enacted even if the community will not fully observe them. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi found it necessary to invoke this minority view as the basis for the repeal.

## Malachi Source & Rov Tzi…

### Malachi 3:9 Proof

On the last line of 36a, *Rav Adda bar Ahava* provides a Scriptural peg for the principle that a *gezeirah* requires broad communal acceptance. The verse cited is from *Malachi* 3:9: *bame'era atem ne'arim ve-oti atem kovim ha-goy kulo* — "you are cursed with a curse, and you are robbing Me, the entire nation."

### Entire Nation Requirement

The operative phrase is *ha-goy kulo* — the entire nation. The verse associates the full force of a covenantal curse with the participation of the entire nation, not a partial constituency. Analogously, the argument runs, a rabbinic decree that binds with the force of law — and whose violation carries the weight of transgression — requires the acceptance of the entire community, or at minimum the majority. Only when *ha-goy kulo* is in play does the decree carry full operative force.

### Curse Acceptance Conditi…

*Rashi* notes that *bame'era atem ne'arim* refers to accepting a *gezeirah* in the manner of a binding oath or covenantal commitment, analogous to the acceptance at *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*. The implication: *ei ika goy kulo, in; ei lo, lo* — if the entire community is present as the accepting body, the decree takes effect; if not, it does not.

## Four Categories & Daught…

### Four Things Listed

The *gemara* now takes up a teaching attributed to *Bali* in the name of *Avimi*, quoting *Rav*: *pitan ve-shamnan ve-yenan u-venoteihem, kullan mi-shmonah asar davar hen* — bread, oil, wine, and their "daughters" are all counted among the eighteen decrees of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*.

### Benoteihem Definition

This prompts the question: *benoteihem mai hi* — what does "their daughters" mean in this context?

### Non-Jewish Women as Nidd…

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* explains: *gazru benoteihem niddot me-arisotan* — the rabbis decreed that non-Jewish women are to be considered *niddot* from infancy, from the cradle. The background is that *mi-de'oraita*, the laws of *tumah ve-taharah* do not apply to non-Jews at all — with the possible exception of a non-Jewish corpse, itself subject to Tannaitic dispute. Non-Jews are not subject to *tumah* and cannot become *tamei* in the biblical sense. However, *mi-de-rabbanan* several forms of *tumah* were instituted with respect to non-Jews, presumably as an extension of the social boundaries constructed by the laws of *tumah ve-taharah* for the Jewish community itself. In this particular case, all non-Jewish women are treated rabbinically as if they were *niddot* from birth. The practical consequence is that any otherwise-prohibited sexual contact with a non-Jewish woman carries the added rabbinic violation of *niddah* as well.

### Gneiva: Avodah Zarah Rea…

*Gneiva*, however, quoting *Rav*, offers a different explanation: *kulan mishum avodah kochavim gazru bahem* — all four of these categories were decreed upon for the same underlying reason, namely *avodah zarah*, concern about idolatrous practice.

## Causal Chain of Decrees

### Bread-Oil-Wine Chain

*Rav Acha bar Adda* came from *Eretz Yisrael* and quoted *Rabbi Yitzchak* elaborating the causal chain: *gazru pitan mishum shamnan* — they prohibited the bread because of the oil. One might ask why the oil is considered a stronger basis for the decree than the bread itself, which prompts the clarification: *ela al pitan ve-shamnan mishum yenan* — in fact both bread and oil are prohibited because of wine. The connection is social: eating together leads to drinking together, and oil is part of the food that accompanies the shared meal, bringing one in proximity to the prohibited wine.

### Wine → Daughters

*Ve-al yenan mishum benoteihem* — and wine itself is prohibited because of "their daughters," meaning that sharing wine leads to social and ultimately sexual proximity with non-Jewish women.

### Daughters: Davar Acher

*Ve-al benoteihem mishum davar acher* — and the prohibition regarding their daughters is itself rooted in something referred to as *davar acher*, a rabbinic euphemism. According to *Rashi*, the first *davar acher* refers to *avodah zarah* — the concern is not so much the daughters themselves as the idolatrous practices to which proximity with them might lead.

### Davar Acher Reason Unkno…

*Ve-al davar acher mishum davar acher* — and the *avodah zarah* concern itself rests on some further reason, a second *davar acher* whose precise referent remains opaque. As *Rashi* notes, this second *davar acher* will be explained later in the *gemara*. The term *davar acher* is a well-known Talmudic euphemism that can refer variously to pork, sexual immorality, *avodah zarah*, *tzara'at*, or other sensitive matters depending on context.

## Intermarriage Prohibitio…

### Seven Nations Exception

The *gemara* probes the claim that the prohibition against non-Jewish daughters is rabbinic: *benoteihem de-oraita hi* — isn't it a biblical prohibition? The verse *lo titchaten bam* prohibits intermarriage with the Canaanite nations. The answer is that *de-oraita, zayin umot* — the Torah's explicit prohibition against intermarriage applies specifically to the seven Canaanite nations enumerated in the text. With respect to other idolaters, there is no explicit *de-oraita* prohibition against a Jewish man marrying an idolatrous woman. It was the rabbis who came and extended the prohibition to *shear ovdei kochavim*, all idolaters.

### Shimon ben Yochai: All I…

One might challenge this on the basis of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's position: the verse *ki yasir et bincha me'achari* — "for she will turn your son away from Me" — teaches that the underlying concern is the risk of religious seduction, and Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai holds that *l'rabot kol ha-masirot*, this includes any idolatrous woman, since the same risk exists with all of them. On his reading, the *de-oraita* prohibition would apply universally, not only to the seven nations.

### Chatnut vs. Znut

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *de-oraita ishut derech chatnut* — the biblical prohibition, even on Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's reading, applies to formal marriage, to the long-term relationship of *chatnut*, because it is in such a relationship that the risk of religious seduction is most acute. The later rabbis then came and extended the prohibition: *va-atu inhu ve-gazru afilu derech znut* — they prohibited even casual sexual relations between a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman, not only marriage, on the ground that even such encounters fall under the concern of social and religious contamination.

### Asymmetric Prohibition

One might then ask: but was not *znut* with a non-Jewish woman already prohibited by *Beit Dino shel Shem* — the *beit din* of Shem, which Rav Elchanan Wasserman discusses in his *Kuntras Divrei Sofrim* as the source of universal rabbinic-style enactments in the wake of the Flood? The *gemara* distinguishes: *de-oraita oved kochavim ha-ba al bat Yisrael d'mashcha batrei* — the primary biblical concern is a non-Jewish man who takes a Jewish woman into his environment, drawing her after him into his culture and away from Torah. A Jewish man going to a non-Jewish woman presents a different profile of risk. That is the asymmetry that underlies the *de-oraita* formulation.

The rabbinic extension then fills in the gap: *va-atu inhu ve-gazru afilu Yisrael ha-ba al ovedet kochavim* — the *de-rabbanan* decree addresses the case of a Jewish man with a non-Jewish woman as well. However, the *gemara* then notes that the prohibition against a Jewish man having relations with a non-Jewish woman is in fact *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, derived from the episode of Pinchas — *ha-boel Aramit kana'in pog'in bo* — where anyone who witnesses the act may take the law into his own hands and kill the transgressor. This applies *de-oraita* only *be-farhesya*, in public, like the case of Zimri, who committed the act openly before *Ohel Moed*, in full view of Moshe and the *zekenim*. The *de-rabbanan* then extends the prohibition to *bitzina*, in private, as will be seen.

## Yichud Decree Progression

### Chashmonaim: Biah Decree

The *gemara* traces a layered historical progression of prohibitions. *Beit dino shel Chashmonai* decreed against the act of *biah* — sexual relations — between a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman, formulating the violation as *nashga*: *niddah, shifcha, goya, ve-eshet ish* — the act constitutes a compound rabbinic transgression combining the prohibitions of *niddah*, *shifcha* (a slave woman), *goya* (a non-Jewish woman), and *eshet ish* (a married woman), even if she is not in fact married. *Ravin*, when he arrived from *Eretz Yisrael*, reported a slightly variant formulation of *nashga*: *niddah, shifcha, goya,* and *zonah* — a promiscuous woman — rather than *eshet ish*, on the reasoning that she may not be married but certainly falls into the category of *znut*.

### Biah vs. Yichud

The *gemara* emphasizes the precise scope of the Hasmonean decree: *ki gazru beit dino shel Chashmonai biah, aval yichud lo* — the Hasmonean court prohibited the act of *biah* itself, but did not extend the prohibition to *yichud* — seclusion — with a non-Jewish woman.

### David: Yichud with Jewis…

The prohibition of *yichud* with a Jewish woman, by contrast, was decreed by *beit dino shel David*. *Rav Yehuda* teaches that at the time of the rape of Tamar by Amnon, David's court enacted the decree against *yichud*. That decree, however, addressed seclusion between a Jewish man and a Jewish woman — *yichud de-bat Yisrael* — prompted by the specific violation that gave rise to the Tamar tragedy.

### Shammai/Hillel: Yichud E…

*Va-atu inhu ve-gazru afilu yichud de-oved kochavim* — the students of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* later extended the *yichud* prohibition to cover seclusion with a non-Jewish woman as well. David's court had not addressed this scenario, presumably because the concern at that stage was focused on violations within the Jewish community.

## Yichud Status & Drashah

### Yichud De'oraisa Source

With the prohibition of *yichud* with a Jewish woman now attributed to David's *beit din*, a question arises: is *yichud de-oraita*? The *gemara* suggests it is, or at minimum has a *de-oraita* basis — though the source is characterized only as a *remez*, a hint from the Torah rather than an explicit verse.

### Deuteronomy Remez

*Rabbi Yochanan* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak* asks: *remez le-yichud min ha-Torah minayin* — where in the Torah is there a hint to the prohibition of *yichud*? The verse cited is from the section dealing with *avodah zarah*: *ki yasitcha achicha ben imecha* — "if your brother, the son of your mother, entices you." The question is posed: does only a maternal brother entice, but not a paternal brother? The *gemara* explains: *ben misyached im imo* — a son may be secluded with his mother, because that relationship is not subject to the *yichud* concern.

### Son-Mother Exception

*Ve-ein acher misyached im kol arayot she-ba-Torah* — but no one else may be secluded with any of the *arayot* listed in the Torah. The verse specifies *ben imecha*, the son of your mother, because that is the one familial dyad for which *yichud* is permitted — a son and his mother. All other *arayot* are subject to the *yichud* prohibition. This *remez* from the Torah constitutes the basis for treating *yichud* with a Jewish woman as a *de-oraita* concern, or at least as something with a strong scriptural anchor, even though the formal enactment is attributed to David's court.
