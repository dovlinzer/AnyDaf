# Yevamot 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chiya/Bar Kappara

### Three Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, רַבִּי חִיָּיא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. בַּר קַפָּרָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. קָפַץ רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: שְׁתַּיִם. קָפַץ בַּר קַפָּרָא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a non-priest who served** in some capacity in one of the Temple services, such as the burning of a burnt-offering on the altar **on Shabbat, Rabbi Ḥiyya says: He is liable** to receive punishment on **two** counts, both because he was a non-priest serving in the Temple and because he desecrated Shabbat. **Bar Kappara says: He is liable** on **only one** count. **Rabbi Ḥiyya jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **two** counts. **Bar Kappara jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **one** count.

The Gemara on 33b picks up a dispute between Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara concerning three scenarios involving transgressions performed simultaneously or in sequence in the Temple context: a *zar* (non-kohen) who performs the *avodah* on *Shabbat*, a *baal mum* (physically blemished kohen) who performs the *avodah* in a state of *tumah* at a time when *tumah hutra betzibbur* (communal impurity is permitted), and a *zar* who eats from a bird upon which *melikah* (the nape-pinching slaughter unique to bird-offerings) was performed. The question was whether, in each scenario, the individual transgresses one prohibition or two. The Gemara sought to frame this disagreement as a debate about *issur chal al issur* — whether a second prohibition can take effect upon a preexisting one — and explored several explanatory models.

### Issur Kolel/Bevas Achas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא זָר: מֵעִיקָּרָא שְׁרֵי בִּמְלָאכָה וְאָסוּר בַּעֲבוֹדָה. אָתְיָא לַהּ שַׁבָּת, מִגּוֹ דְּקָא מִיתְּסַר בִּמְלָאכָה — מִיתְּסַר נָמֵי בַּעֲבוֹדָה. בַּעַל מוּם: מֵעִיקָּרָא שְׁרֵי בַּאֲכִילָה, וְאָסוּר בַּעֲבוֹדָה. אִיטַּמִּי לֵיהּ, מִגּוֹ דְּקָא מִיתְּסַר בַּאֲכִילָה — מִיתְּסַר נָמֵי בַּעֲבוֹדָה. אֶלָּא מְלִיקָה — בְּבַת אַחַת הִיא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, בְּאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** in the case of **a non-priest** who served in the Temple on Shabbat, **initially he was permitted to perform labor** every day **and was prohibited from** engaging in **the** Temple **service.** When **Shabbat came, since he is** now **prohibited from** engaging in **prohibited labor** in all contexts due to Shabbat, **he is also prohibited from** engaging in labors related to the Temple **service** due to Shabbat. Indeed, the additional prohibition of Shabbat was added to the previously existing prohibition against performing service in the Temple. This is a more inclusive prohibition since it also includes prohibited labor outside of the Temple. The same is true with regard to **a blemished priest** who served in the Temple while ritually impure: **Initially,** prior to his becoming ritually impure, **he was allowed to eat** from the consecrated animals **but was prohibited from** performing **the** Temple **service,** like all blemished priests. When **he was rendered ritually impure, since he is** now **prohibited from eating** of the consecrated items, **he is also prohibited from** performing the Temple **service** due to that same ritual impurity. There is, then, a more inclusive prohibition here. **However,** with regard to the case of a bird that was killed by **pinching, you find** that the prohibition against the eating of consecrated items by a non-priest and the prohibition against eating an unslaughtered animal carcass take effect **simultaneously. You do not,** however, **find a more inclusive prohibition** here as there was no original prohibition that took effect beforehand. Instead, this is a case of two prohibitions that take effect simultaneously.

The model of *issur kolel* — where a later prohibition encompasses a broader category and thus takes effect despite a prior prohibition already being in place — could account for the first two scenarios, which admit of a sequential reading: first one becomes a *zar*, and only thereafter does *Shabbat* arrive; first one is a *baal mum*, and only thereafter does *tumah* set in. The *melikah* case, however, cannot be sequential. The moment the bird's neck is pinched, it simultaneously becomes a *korban* forbidden to non-kohanim and becomes non-kosher for ordinary consumption. These two prohibitions arise at the exact same instant — *bevas achas* — making it impossible to say that one preceded the other. The Gemara therefore concluded that the underlying debate is whether *issur chal al issur* can operate *bevas achas*, and specifically whether Rabbi Shimon — who ordinarily rejects *issur chal al issur* — would concede that when two prohibitions arise simultaneously, both take effect.

### Bar Kappara's Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, קָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּאִיסּוּר בְּבַת אַחַת, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא, קָא מִישְׁתְּבַע לְאַפּוֹקֵי לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מֵחֶזְקֵיהּ. אֶלָּא לְבַר קַפָּרָא — לְמָה לֵיהּ לְאִשְׁתְּבוֹעֵי? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the previous explanation is rejected and the Gemara suggests instead that **they disagree with regard to** an additional **prohibition** that takes effect **simultaneously, and** this dispute pertains to the status of these prohibitions specifically **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** It could be suggested that when the two prohibitions take effect simultaneously, Rabbi Shimon would concede that they both take effect. However, this explanation raises a difficulty as well: **Granted, according to Rabbi Ḥiyya** it is clear why **he was swearing.** He did so in order **to exclude** the understanding of **Rabbi Shimon's** opinion that was based on **his presumed** opinion. As it was known that Rabbi Shimon holds that a new prohibition cannot take effect where another prohibition exists; this is the default understanding of his opinion. Therefore, it was incumbent upon Rabbi Ḥiyya to take an oath in order to emphasize that despite Rabbi Shimon's general opinion, in cases where the prohibitions take effect simultaneously, he would concede that both prohibitions can take effect. **However, according to bar Kappara, why was it necessary to swear?** It would have sufficed for him simply to make his statement, as it concurs with the default understanding of Rabbi Shimon's opinion. The Gemara comments: Indeed, this is **difficult.**

This conclusion, however, creates a difficulty. Both Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara swore oaths that they had heard Rebbi's ruling directly: Rabbi Chiya maintained that Rebbi said two transgressions, Bar Kappara that Rebbi said one. If the *bevas achas* case genuinely yields two transgressions even according to the most lenient opinion — even according to Rabbi Shimon — then from Rabbi Chiya's perspective, how could Bar Kappara ever have heard from Rebbi that one only transgresses once? The Gemara poses this starkly: *ela l'Rabbi Chiya, Bar Kappara shikra ka meshaker?* — is Bar Kappara simply lying?

### Misremembering Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כִּי אַתְנְיֵיהּ רַבִּי לְדִידֵיהּ, תַּרְתֵּי לִפְטוּר אַתְנְיֵיהּ, וְאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל אַתְנְיֵיהּ, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְחַזְיַאּ (לְ)בַּר קַפָּרָא לְזָר שֶׁאָכַל מְלִיקָה, וְאַיְּידֵי דְּדָמְיָא לַהּ — עָרְבַהּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, וְעַיֵּין בַּהּ, וְלָא אַשְׁכַּח אֶלָּא בְּבַת אַחַת. וְסָבַר: מִדְּהָא בְּבַת אַחַת, הָא נָמֵי בְּבַת אַחַת. וּמִדְּהָנָךְ לִפְטוּר, הָנָךְ נָמֵי לִפְטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥiyya** could have **said to you** that this is what transpired: **When Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught him,** bar Kappara, **he taught him two** cases involving **exemptions,** i.e., the cases of a non-priest who served in the Temple on Shabbat and that of the blemished priest who served while ritually impure. Both of these are cases of more inclusive prohibitions, and he was informing bar Kappara that Rabbi Shimon holds them liable on only one count, because Rabbi Shimon holds that a prohibition takes effect where another prohibition already exists only if the prohibitions take effect simultaneously, but not in cases of more inclusive prohibitions. **And he taught him** the ruling with regard to **a more inclusive prohibition,** that in such cases there is an exemption, **and** this ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And bar Kappara** then **saw** the case of **a non-priest who ate** a bird killed by **pinching, and since it was similar** to the previous cases, **he mixed them together.** Then, sometime later, it seemed to him as though he had in truth heard all of these cases together from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **but** when **he examined this** last case, **he found** that it could exist **only** if the events occurred **simultaneously. And he concluded** that **since** the case of a non-priest eating a bird killed by pinching can occur only in a scenario where the prohibitions take effect **simultaneously,** the other cases are instances of **simultaneous** prohibitions **as well. And** similarly, **since** he was taught **to exempt** one individual from a second prohibition in **those cases, these cases** were taught **to exempt** one individual from a second prohibition **as well.**

Rabbi Chiya's answer is that Bar Kappara is not lying — he genuinely misremembered. The Gemara explains: *ki asniyah Rebbi lidi-di tarti l'patur asniyah* — when Rebbi taught the ruling of "one transgression" to Bar Kappara, he was teaching only the first two cases, those of the *zar* on *Shabbat* and the *baal mum* in *tumah*. In those two cases, the sequence is that first one already has the status of *zar* or *baal mum*, and only afterward the additional prohibition of *Shabbat* or *tumah* arrives. This is a case of *issur kolel* — the later prohibition is broader in scope. According to Rabbi Shimon, who rejects *issur chal al issur*, if he even rejects *issur mosif* (a prohibition that extends the class of persons to whom the object is forbidden), he certainly rejects *issur kolel*. So Rebbi taught Bar Kappara that according to Rabbi Shimon, in these two sequential cases you transgress only once.

Then Bar Kappara encountered the third case — *vezar she'achal melicha*, the *zar* who ate from the *melikah* bird. Because it was structurally similar — again a non-kohen doing something ordinarily permitted when done correctly — he mixed it together with the first two in his mind: *ve'aydi d'damya lei arbav bahadeihu*. Now, treating all three as a single teaching, he noticed that the only common denominator across the newly combined set was *bevas achas*. He therefore concluded that the entire teaching was about *bevas achas*, and since Rebbi had exempted from a second transgression in those cases, he assumed Rebbi meant that *bevas achas* also yields only one transgression. In short, Bar Kappara misremembered — he inadvertently inserted the *melikah* case, which changed the character of the set, and drew a conclusion that Rebbi had never actually taught.

### Three Forms Summarized

This analysis occasions a useful summary of the three forms by which *issur chal al issur* can operate. *Issur mosif* is perhaps the most intuitively accessible: a second prohibition takes effect because it extends the forbidden status to a wider audience — more people, or more types of activity, are now prohibited with respect to this object. *Issur kolel* is harder: the object was already forbidden to everyone, but now more items within the same class are also forbidden, and by virtue of that expansion, the original prohibition is deemed to have been re-imposed in its fuller form. *Bevas achas* — when both prohibitions arise simultaneously — is in some respects the most conceptually straightforward, since there is no prior prohibition whose "territorial claim" would be challenged; both take effect together, and it is argued that even Rabbi Shimon would concede to this.

## Beraita Proof

### Zar/Baal Mum Beraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, וּבַעַל מוּם שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּטוּמְאָה — יֵשׁ כָּאן מִשּׁוּם זָרוּת, וּמִשּׁוּם שַׁבָּת, וּמִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם, וּמִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין כָּאן אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם זָרוּת וּבַעַל מוּם בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of bar Kappara from a *baraita*: In the case of **a non-priest who served on Shabbat and** the case of **a blemished priest who served while ritually impure, there is** liability **here, due to** the prohibition against serving as **a non-priest; and due to** the prohibition against desecrating **Shabbat; and due to** the prohibition against serving as **a blemished priest; and due to** the prohibition against serving after contracting **ritual impurity.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Shimon says: There is** liability **here only due to** the prohibition against serving as **a non-priest and due to** the prohibition against serving as **a blemished priest.**

Having established that the debate between Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara concerns whether *bevas achas* would cause even Rabbi Shimon to concede that two transgressions are incurred, the Gemara turns to a *beraita* that addresses almost precisely the same cases. The *beraita* states: *zar sheshimash b'Shabbat u'vaal mum sheshimash b'tumah* — a *zar* who performed the *avodah* on *Shabbat*, and a *baal mum* who performed the *avodah* in *tumah* even in a situation of *tumah hutra betzibbur*. In the first case, says the *beraita*, one transgresses both for being a *zar* and for *Shabbat*; in the second, for being a *baal mum* and for *tumah*. This is the position of Rabbi Yose. Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *ein kan ela mishum zar u'vaal mum bilvad* — there is only the first, original transgression of being a *zar* or *baal mum*; the subsequently arriving prohibition of *Shabbat* or *tumah* does not take further effect. This confirms that the debate is indeed between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, the very Tannaim known to disagree about *issur chal al issur*.

### Melikah Omitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ מְלִיקָה שַׁיְּירַהּ. לְמַאן שַׁיְּירַהּ? אִילֵימָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הַשְׁתָּא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל מִיחַיַּיב שְׁתַּיִם — בְּאִיסּוּר בַּת אַחַת מִיבַּעְיָא? אֶלָּא לָאו לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וּבְאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל הוּא דְּפָטַר, אֲבָל בְּאִיסּוּר בְּבַת אַחַת — מִיחַיַּיב. תְּיוּבְתָּא דָּבָר קַפָּרָא תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And yet** the case of a non-priest who ate a bird killed by **pinching was left out** and not mentioned among the cases subject to this dispute. The Gemara asks: **According to whom was this left out?** That is, according to the opinion of which *tanna* would this question arise? **If we say** that this was left out **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** it is difficult. **Now** that **Rabbi Yosei** holds, even **in** cases of **a more inclusive prohibition, that one is liable on two** counts, as was stated with regard to a non-priest who served on Shabbat, **is** it **necessary** to state that in the case **where the** additional **prohibition** takes effect **simultaneously** one would be liable on two counts? **Rather, is it not according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** that this case was left out, as Rabbi Shimon would concede that in this case one is liable on two counts? Therefore, Rabbi Shimon **exempts** one from liability for the second prohibition in cases of **more inclusive prohibitions, but in** cases of **simultaneous prohibitions** he would **deem** one **liable** on two counts. The Gemara summarizes: **The refutation** of the opinion **of bar Kappara is** indeed **a conclusive refutation,** and his statements are rejected.

Crucially, the *beraita* discusses only two of the three scenarios — and conspicuously omits the *melikah* case. The Gemara asks: for whom was it omitted, and why? If the omission is from Rabbi Yose's perspective, that makes no sense. Rabbi Yose is prepared to say two transgressions even when the prohibitions arrive sequentially — how much more so would he say so when they arrive *bevas achas*, where there is no question of one preceding the other. *Hashta Rabbi Yose be'issur kolel mechayev shtayim, be'issur bevas achas mibaya?* — if Rabbi Yose obligates twice even in a sequential *kolel* case, would he ever hesitate regarding a *bevas achas* case? Clearly not. The *melikah* case must therefore have been omitted because it would yield a *different* ruling for the other disputant — Rabbi Shimon.

### Conclusion on Bevas Achas

The Gemara concludes: *ela lav Rabbi Shimon* — it is precisely because Rabbi Shimon would rule differently in the *melikah* case that it was left out. The *beraita* records Rabbi Shimon's leniency only for the sequential cases, where *issur kolel* is the operative mechanism. *Be'issur kolel hu d'patar* — it is specifically in the *kolel* scenario that Rabbi Shimon rules exempt. But *aval be'issur bevas achas mechayev* — in the *bevas achas* case, Rabbi Shimon would concede and hold the person liable for two transgressions. The Gemara therefore declares *tiyuvta d'Bar Kappara tiyuvta* — this is a refutation of Bar Kappara's position.

One might ask whether this proof is fully decisive. The *beraita* clearly treats three cases in the prior discussion, yet mentions only two. That something distinctive about the third case caused it to be omitted is a reasonable inference, and if one accepts *bevas achas* as the distinguishing feature, this constitutes a solid proof that Rabbi Shimon concedes in the *melikah* scenario. Whether the *beraita* also establishes precisely what the original dispute between Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara was about is a more open question — but the core ruling, that Rabbi Shimon holds *bevas achas chayav shtayim*, emerges clearly.

## Zar Shechita Analysis

### Shechita Permitted?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, בְּמַאי? אִי בִּשְׁחִיטָה — שְׁחִיטָה בְּזָר כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאִי בְּקַבָּלָה וְהוֹלָכָה — טִלְטוּל בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. אִי בְּהַקְטָרָה — וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַבְעָרָה לְלָאו יָצָאתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question with regard to one of the laws mentioned above. The *baraita* mentioned the case of **a non-priest who served on Shabbat.** The Gemara wonders: **In what** form of service did he perform in the Temple? **If** this is referring to a case where a non-priest performed **the slaughtering** of the sacrificial animal, there would be no prohibition, as the **slaughtering by a non-priest is valid. And if** it is referring to **receiving** the blood **and carrying** the blood to the altar, while this is a case where he would be liable as a non-priest performing the service of a priest, insofar as Shabbat is concerned **this is a case only of moving,** and it does not entail a violation of any prohibited labor. **If** he served **by burning** the sacrificial portions on the altar, then this discussion follows the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, **but didn't Rabbi Yosei say** that the prohibition against **kindling on Shabbat was** singled **out** to teach that one who lights a fire on Shabbat merely violates **a prohibition?**

Before the Gemara moves to the next *Mishnah*, it pauses on a technical question that the *beraita*'s case raises: *bemai* — in what act exactly did the *zar* violate *Shabbat*? The problem is sharper than it first appears. If the *zar* performed *shechita* (slaughter of the *korban*), there is no *Shabbat* violation, because *shechita* of a *korban* by a non-kohen is actually *kasher*. This is a striking and perhaps unfamiliar *halacha*: even on *Shabbat*, when a communal *korban* overrides the day's restrictions, a non-kohen may perform the *shechita*, and that act is entirely valid. So there would be no secondary transgression there to speak of.

If instead the *zar* performed *kabbalah* (catching the blood) or *holachah* (carrying the blood to the *mizbeach*), this is essentially a matter of carrying within an enclosed area — a form of *tiltul b'alma*, simple moving of an object — and hardly the substantive *Shabbat* violation the *beraita* has in mind. If the act in question was *haktarah* — burning the limbs on the *mizbeach* — then, as Rav Yosef taught, *hav'ara l'lav yatza*: making a fire on *Shabbat* is, according to Rabbi Yose himself (one of the two disputants in the *beraita*), only a *lav* rather than a capital offense, because the Torah singled it out explicitly, and Rabbi Yose interprets that singling-out as a demotion in severity. This creates an inconsistency: are we seriously saying the two-transgression debate hinges on the lighter *lav* of *hav'ara*?

### Yom Kippur Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: בִּשְׁחִיטַת פָּרוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וּכְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שְׁחִיטַת פָּרוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּזָר פְּסוּלָה. אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא זָר? אֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט נָמֵי! שֶׁזָּר אֶצְלוֹ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov said:** The service referred to **in** this case is **the slaughtering of the High Priest's bull** on Yom Kippur, **and this is in accordance with the statement of the one who says** that **the slaughtering of the High Priest's bull is disqualified** if performed **by a non-priest,** for this service is designated specifically for the High Priest alone. The Gemara asks: **If so,** that this is referring to the slaughtering of the High Priest's bull, then **why specifically** state **a non-priest? The same would hold true even** for **a common priest as well.** The Gemara answers: **This is referring to one who is as a non-priest in relation to** the High Priest and not to an actual non-priest. The Hebrew word *zar* literally means foreigner, and this type of service is considered foreign to a common priest as well as a non-priest.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov resolves the difficulty by re-framing the scenario entirely: the case is not *Shabbat* in the ordinary sense but rather *Yom Kippur* — specifically the *shechita* of the High Priest's ox (*paro shel Kohen Gadol*). On *Yom Kippur*, the *avodah* is exclusive to the Kohen Gadol, and according to the opinion that the *shechita* of the Kohen Gadol's ox, when performed by a non-Kohen Gadol, is *pasul* — invalid — the act of *shechita* itself becomes a prohibited labor that the *zar* has no right to perform. Now we have a genuine *Shabbat*-category transgression through *shechita*, and it is a case where that *shechita* was specifically not *kasher b'zar*. The Gemara then pushes back: if that is the case, why specify a *zar* at all? Even a regular *kohen hedyot* would be disqualified from performing this particular *shechita* on *Yom Kippur* according to that opinion. The Gemara answers: *shezar etzlo ka'amar* — we mean someone who is like a *zar* with respect to this particular *korban*, i.e., anyone other than the Kohen Gadol. The Gemara itself acknowledges this is a stretch.

### Rav Ashi's Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִידֵּי חַטָּאוֹת קָתָנֵי, אוֹ לָאוֵי קָתָנֵי?! אֶלָּא אִיסּוּרֵי בְּעָלְמָא קָא חָשֵׁיב. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְקוֹבְרוֹ בֵּין רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi strongly objects to this:** The previous rejection that was based on the idea that the act of burning was not a full-fledged prohibited labor is in fact groundless. **Is** the *tanna* **teaching** how many **sin-offerings** would be incurred, **or is he teaching** how many **negative mitzvot** are being violated? **Rather, he merely** enumerates **the prohibitions** violated by a certain action, without detailing their severities, and therefore the desecration of Shabbat involved in the Temple service can be any Shabbat violation. The Gemara asks: **What practical difference does it make** how many prohibitions are involved? The Gemara answers: It affects the decision whether or not **to bury him among the completely wicked.** If he violated two prohibitions, he is considered wicked on two counts and consequently must be buried accordingly.

Rav Ashi cuts through the difficulty with a fundamentally clarifying observation. *Midi chatos katani? O lavin katani?* — did the *beraita* ever say anything about how many *chataot* one must bring, or how many sets of lashes? It did not. *Ela issurei be'alma ka chashiv* — the *beraita* was simply counting how many *issurim* (prohibitions) were violated, without specifying or implying anything about the legal consequences. The question of one or two is a question of how many wrong things the person did — *likro rabbeinu rasha gamur*, to assess how fully blameworthy a person is — not a question about capital liability or sin-offerings. Once that reframing is in place, there is no difficulty with *haktarah* and its *lav*-only status: we were never asked to equalize the penalties of the two transgressions, only to count them. This recalibration is not without irony: a sustained Tannaic dispute about *issur kolel*, *issur mosif*, and *bevas achas* ultimately resolves into a question about moral characterization rather than concrete legal obligation. But the Gemara had already flagged this dimension earlier, and Rav Ashi's point is that the earlier difficulty never should have arisen in the first place.

## Mixed-Up Wives

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנַיִם שֶׁקִּידְּשׁוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, וּבִשְׁעַת כְּנִיסָתָן לַחוּפָּה הֶחְלִיפוּ אֶת שֶׁל זֶה לָזֶה וְאֶת שֶׁל זֶה לָזֶה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. וְאִם הָיוּ אַחִין — מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָח. וְאִם הָיוּ אֲחָיוֹת — מִשּׁוּם ״אִשָּׁה אֶל אֲחוֹתָהּ״. וְאִם הָיוּ נִדּוֹת — מִשּׁוּם נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **two** men **who betrothed two women, and at the time that they entered the wedding canopy,** after the betrothal, the men **switched this** wife **with that** wife **and that** one **with this** one, **then these** two men **are liable for** engaging in forbidden sexual relations with **a married woman,** since each of them had intercourse with his fellow's wife. The act of betrothal is sufficient to prohibit a woman to all other men as a married woman. Therefore, when the women were switched, both men transgressed this violation. **And if they were brothers,** then they are also liable **for** forbidden sexual relations with **a brother's wife. And if** these women **were sisters,** then they are liable **for** taking **a wife and her sister** as well. **And if they were menstruating women,** they would be liable **for** intercourse with **a menstruating woman** as well.

The Gemara now arrives at the *Mishnah* that actually motivates the entire preceding discussion of *issur chal al issur*. The *Mishnah* opens: *shnayim shekidshu shtei nashim* — two men betrothed two women. The men were unrelated to each other and the women were unrelated to each other. When the time came for the *chupah* — the consummation of the marriages — *hechlifu es shel zeh lazeh* — they mixed up the wives, each man going in to the other's wife. This is, in a sense, a worse situation than the *Rachel and Leah* episode, because there each man knows whose wife is whose — here, each man is unknowingly sleeping with the other man's wife.

### Multiple Transgressions

The *Mishnah* then systematically enumerates the potentially compounding transgressions. *Harei elu chayavim mishum eishet ish* — each man has transgressed the prohibition of adultery with a married woman. *Ve'im hayu achim, mishum eishet ach* — if the two men happened to be brothers, there is an additional transgression of *eishet ach*, the prohibition of a brother's wife. *Ve'im hayu achayot* — if the two women happened to be sisters, then each man is also in violation of *isha el achosah*, the prohibition of his wife's sister (*achat u'niddah* in the shorthand). *Ve'im hayu niddot, mishum niddah* — and if the women happen to be in a state of *niddah* at the time, that is yet a fourth transgression. In the worst configuration — brothers who have married sisters who are currently *niddot* — each man potentially incurs four separate prohibitions in a single act.

A natural question arises about the woman's role in this accounting. The answer is that the prohibitions of *arayot* are formulated from the man's perspective throughout the Torah, but the woman is a full participant in the act. She is therefore liable not as one who facilitates another's sin (*lifnei iver*) but as a direct party to the same transgression. Curiously, this is so even though the substantive definitions — married woman, brother's wife, wife's sister — are all defined from the male vantage point.

### Separation/Pregnancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַפְרִישִׁין אוֹתָן שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, שֶׁמָּא מְעוּבָּרוֹת הֵן. וְאִם הָיוּ קְטַנּוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיוֹת לֵילֵד — מַחֲזִירִים אוֹתָן מִיָּד. וְאִם הָיוּ כֹּהֲנוֹת, נִפְסְלוּ מִן הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** following these forbidden sexual relations, **we separate** these women from their husbands **for three months, lest they were impregnated** by that forbidden act of intercourse. Doing so makes it possible to distinguish a child born of these relations, so that he could be rendered a *mamzer*. **And if they were female minors and unable to bear children, then we immediately return them** to their original husbands. **And if they were daughters of priests, they are** thereby **disqualified from eating of *teruma*.** By engaging in illicit sexual acts, they were rendered forbidden to priests and disqualified from eating *teruma*.

After the error is discovered, the *Mishnah* requires a period of enforced separation of three months — *shema me'ubarot hen* — lest the women be pregnant, which would create questions of *mamzerut*. There were no pregnancy tests; one waited three months to determine by external signs. If the women were minors (*ketanot*) who could not conceive, they are returned to their husbands immediately.

The Gemara establishes that the scenario described is one of inadvertent error, not deliberate wife-swapping. One proof: Rabbi Chiya teaches in a *beraita* that there would be sixteen *chataot* (sin-offerings) — four transgressions times four persons (each of the two men and each of the two women), all acting as *shogegin* (unwitting sinners). If the act had been intentional, there would be no *chata* obligation at all. Additionally, the *Mishnah*'s allowance for the women to return to their husbands after three months would be impossible if the act had been willful, since a woman who commits adultery voluntarily becomes forbidden to her husband. The Gemara therefore accepts the reading *huchlafu* — "they were exchanged" (passively, accidentally) — rather than *hechlifu*, "they exchanged."

One apparent exception: the Gemara notes that a minor's consent does not constitute legal consent — *pituy ketanah ones hu* — so even if a minor were to act willfully in such a scenario, she would be considered legally coerced and would not be forbidden to return to her husband. But that consideration does not apply here, where the entire case is one of unintentional error.

## Rabbi Meir Position

### Which Tanna?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֶחְלִיפוּ. מִידֵּי בְּרַשִּׁיעֵי עָסְקִינַן?! וְתוּ, הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת — אִי בְּמֵזִיד מִי אִיכָּא קׇרְבָּן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: תְּנִי ״הוּחְלְפוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In the mishna it states: **They switched** this one with that one. **Are we dealing with** such **wicked individuals** that they would intentionally switch their wives? **And furthermore,** consider **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** on this subject: **There are sixteen sin-offerings here,** four sin-offerings for each of the men and four for each of the women. Yet, **if** they had acted **intentionally would there be an offering?** Sacrifices are brought only for unintentional acts. **Rav Yehuda said: Teach** instead **they were switched,** which indicates that the switching was not done intentionally; rather, the women were accidentally mixed up.

With the *Mishnah*'s scenario established, the Gemara asks: *uman hai tanna d'is lei issur kolel ve'issur mosif ve'issur bas achas?* — which Tanna is it who accepts all three forms of *issur chal al issur*? The scenario involves *eishet ish* (married woman) and *eishet ach* (brother's wife) arising together — potentially *bevas achas* — and then *achos isha* (wife's sister) added through *issur kolel*, and *niddah* added through *issur mosif*. All three mechanisms must be operative for the *Mishnah* to yield four distinct transgressions. The Gemara, citing Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, identifies this as the position of Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Meir Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: יֵשׁ אוֹכֵל אֲכִילָה אַחַת וְחַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ אַרְבַּע חַטָּאוֹת וְאָשָׁם אֶחָד. טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל חֵלֶב, וְהוּא נוֹתָר מִן מוּקְדָּשִׁין, בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: This is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **There is** one who performs a single act of **eating** an olive-bulk of food, **and he is liable to bring four sin-offerings and one guilt-offering. How so? This *halakha* applies to one who is ritually impure who ate forbidden fat that was notar from a consecrated offering, i.e., it remained after the time when it was permitted to eat it, and this occurred on Yom Kippur.**

The source is a *beraita*: *yesh ochel achilah achas ve'chayav aleha arba chataot ve'asham echad* — one can perform a single act of eating and thereby incur four *chataot* and one *asham*. The case: a person who is *tamei* eats the *cheilev* (forbidden fat) of a sanctified animal that has become *notar* (leftover sacrifice, which may no longer be consumed) on *Yom Kippur*. The four *chataot* correspond to: eating *cheilev* (forbidden fat in general), being *tamei* while eating consecrated food, eating *notar*, and eating on *Yom Kippur*. The *asham* corresponds to *me'ilah* — deriving benefit from consecrated property. Rabbi Meir adds: if it was *Shabbat* and the person carried this food in his mouth, he would be liable for that as well. The other Sages object — *eino min hashem* — carrying the food in one's mouth is not the same act as eating it; the additional *Shabbat* violation arises from a conceptually distinct action and should not be counted together. Tosafot, however, notes that Rabbi Meir may have held that the act of swallowing constitutes the *hanachah* (placement) required for the *melacha* of carrying, making it technically part of the same act.

### Three Forms Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה שַׁבָּת וְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּפִיו — חַיָּיב. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינוֹ מִן הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says:** There is one more sin-offering for which he may be liable. **If it was Shabbat and he carried** this olive-bulk of food from one domain to another **in his mouth, he is liable** for carrying out on Shabbat. The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Meir: Liability for the sin-offering that you added **is not** incurred **from** violation **of the** same **type** of prohibition. He is liable for carrying out the food, not for eating it.

The *Rashi* on this passage assigns each of the three *issur chal al issur* mechanisms to a specific stage in this sequence. When the animal was plain *chullin* (unsanctified), its *cheilev* was forbidden to eat but permitted for other benefit. When it was sanctified — *hukdesha* — the *cheilev* acquired an additional dimension of prohibition: now benefit (*hana'ah*) from it is also forbidden. Rashi terms this *issur mosif*, since the sanctification extended the nature of the prohibition. It must be noted, however, that this is a somewhat unusual application of *issur mosif*: the standard understanding is that *issur mosif* means the forbidden object becomes forbidden to a wider circle of persons, whereas here it is the same persons who now face a stricter prohibition. Rashi's extension of the term to cover this case is worth flagging as genuinely questionable, even if it is the framework the Gemara works with.

When the animal became *notar*, the prohibition expanded to include the *mizbeach* itself — it can no longer be offered on the altar — and so the class of "entities" forbidden from it has grown. This is a more conventional *issur mosif*. When the person himself became *tamei*, he became forbidden from eating any consecrated food, not just this particular piece of *cheilev*; the expansion here is in the range of objects forbidden to him, not a change in the status of the object. This is *issur kolel* — the broader prohibition sweeps in this particular item along with everything else in its class. The *bevas achas* dimension, according to Tosafot, is illustrated by Rabbi Meir's addition of *Shabbat* to the case: when it is simultaneously *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat*, both the *Yom Kippur* prohibition on eating and the *Shabbat* prohibition on carrying arise together, instantiating the *bevas achas* mechanism.

## Eliezer/Yehoshua

### Alignment Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, הָאָמַר: טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה — פָּטוּר. אֶלָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to clarify the opinion of Rabbi Meir: **And in accordance with whose opinion is** the statement of **Rabbi Meir? If** you say that **it is in accordance** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** this is difficult. **Didn't** Rabbi Yehoshua **say that one who erred with regard to a mitzva is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering, i.e., one who unwittingly performed a transgression while intending to perform a mitzva is exempt from punishment? In the case in the mishna as well, the men intended to perform the mitzva of marriage but unwittingly violated a transgression because the wives were switched. Therefore, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, they should be exempt from a sin-offering. **Rather,** this must be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that even if one mistakenly transgressed while attempting to perform a mitzva, he is still obligated to bring a sin-offering.

Having established that the *Mishnah*'s scenario requires the position of Rabbi Meir as the Tanna who accepts all three forms of *issur chal al issur*, the Gemara raises a further complication. There is a separate, well-known dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua about *to'eh bidvar mitzvah* — one who errs in the course of performing a *mitzvah*. The men in the *Mishnah* were attempting to sleep with their own wives — an act of marital relations, which is itself a *mitzvah*. If Rabbi Yehoshua holds that such a person is exempt from a *chata*, then the *Mishnah*'s assumption that *chataot* are owed becomes problematic. *Le-Rabbi Meir aliba de-man?* — according to which opinion of this second debate is Rabbi Meir's position in the *Mishnah* tenable?

The Gemara initially suggests it must align with Rabbi Eliezer, who does not grant an exemption for *mitzvah* errors. But the Gemara immediately offers an alternative: *ibba'it eima afilu aliba de-Rabbi Yehoshua* — one can maintain that even Rabbi Yehoshua would not exempt here.

### Bohul/Time Pressure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה פָּטוּר, הָנֵי מִילֵּי גַּבֵּי תִּינוֹקוֹת — דִּזְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל, אֲבָל הַאי, כֵּיוָן דְּאֵין זְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish,** resolve this differently and **say: Actually,** this can be explained even **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua. When Rabbi Yehoshua says that one who erred with regard to a mitzva is exempt, this applies** only **to** the case of **babies** who were mixed up in such a way that on Shabbat the *mohel* mistakenly circumcised a baby whose time had not yet come for circumcision. Since the *mohel* did not fulfill the mitzva of circumcision, he should be liable for performing prohibited labor on Shabbat. However, Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him from a sin-offering **since** he is **pressed for time.** Because circumcision must be performed only on the day specified, he was rushed and anxious to perform the circumcision, and due to this sense of urgency he erred and thought that the proper time was on Shabbat. **In this** case in the mishna, **however,** where the mistake involved marriage, **since** the brothers were **not pressed for time** Rabbi Yehoshua does **not** deem the brothers exempt.

The key to reconciling Rabbi Meir with Rabbi Yehoshua lies in the precise conditions under which Rabbi Yehoshua grants the *mitzvah*-error exemption. The Gemara explains with a paradigmatic case: a *mohel* who has two children to circumcise on consecutive days — one whose eighth day falls on *Shabbat*, one whose eighth day falls on Sunday — accidentally performs the circumcision on *Shabbat* for the Sunday child. He has violated *Shabbat* through an act of *melacha*, yet he was attempting to perform a *mitzvah*. Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him from a *chata*.

The Gemara attributes this exemption to *bohul* — a sense of urgency or time pressure. The *mohel* operates under the pressure that today's circumcision must be done today; that ambient pressure, combined with the fact that he was acting in the service of a *mitzvah*, constitutes sufficient mitigation. The crucial point is that *bohul* and *mitzvah* together produce the exemption — neither alone is sufficient. In the case of the mixed-up wives, there is no *bohul*. A man does not face a deadline by which he must sleep with his wife on a particular night. Without time pressure, the combination that gives Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption its force is absent, and even Rabbi Yehoshua would say the man owes a *chata*.

The Gemara is careful here about what exactly is doing the exculpatory work. One reading is that *bohul* is a form of *ones* — unavoidable compulsion — and that the *mitzvah* context amplifies that *ones* to the point of exculpation. Another reading is that the *mitzvah* context is itself the primary exempting factor, but a high degree of negligence can override it; *bohul* functions to lower the threshold of apparent negligence. On either reading, the absence of *bohul* in the wife-mixing case is fatal to the exemption claim, and the *Mishnah*'s liability for a *chata* is consistent with Rabbi Yehoshua's view.

### Trumah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי תְּרוּמָה, דְּאֵין זְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל, וְקָפָטַר! דִּתְנַן: הָיָה אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And yet** in the case of eating ***teruma*, where one is not pressed for time and** nevertheless Rabbi Yehoshua **exempts** him if the act involved a mitzva, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 72b): If a priest **was partaking of *teruma* and it became known that he was the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*** and thereby disqualified from the priesthood and prohibited from eating *teruma*, **Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **liable** to pay **the** value of the **principal and** an additional **fifth,** like any non-priest who unwittingly ate *teruma*. However, **Rabbi Yehoshua exempts** him because at the time of consumption he intended to perform a mitzva, for he thought that he was a priest eating of the *teruma*.

The Gemara challenges this framework by pointing to another case in which Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua debate, one that also lacks time pressure: a kohen eating *trumah* who is informed mid-meal that he is actually a *chalal* (a disqualified kohen, in this instance the son of a divorced woman or a *chalutzah*), and therefore unauthorized to eat *trumah* — he is, with respect to *trumah*, a *zar*. Rabbi Eliezer holds him liable for principal payment plus the additional *chomesh* penalty; Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him. Yet eating *trumah* is not a *mitzvah* under time pressure. Why then does Rabbi Yehoshua exempt him?

### Erev Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בִּתְרוּמָה בְּעֶרֶב הַפֶּסַח עָסְקִינַן — דִּזְמַנָּהּ בָּהוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But wasn't it stated with regard to** that mishna: **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said: Here we are dealing with** leavened ***teruma*** that was being eaten by the priest **on the eve of Passover, for he is pressed for time.** In this case, he would rush to eat it so that it would not have to be burned.

The Gemara resolves this by the comment of Rabbi Abba bar Chiya: *hacha bitrumah be'erev pesach askinan* — the *trumah* case in question is specifically one that occurred on *erev Pesach*, when the kohen needed to eat his *trumah* (which was *chametz*) before the prohibition on *chametz* took effect. That creates a genuine *bohul* — a time-pressured context — and Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption fits the same framework as before. Alternatively, the Gemara suggests *ve'ibba'it eima* — even without that specific setting, one could resolve the *Mishnah* differently, by attributing it to Rabbi Shimon operating under a *bevas achas* framework, which would be a separate line of analysis altogether.

## Bevas Achas Reconcile

### Simultaneous Transgressions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּאִיסּוּר בַּת אַחַת, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. בִּשְׁלָמָא כּוּלְּהוּ מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ: דְּשַׁוִּינְהוּ שָׁלִיחַ, וְשַׁוּוֹ אִינְהוּ שָׁלִיחַ, וּפְגַע שָׁלִיחַ בְּשָׁלִיחַ. אֶלָּא נִדּוּת הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a different attribution of the mishna: **And if you wish, say** a different explanation: The mishna is not according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who deems one liable in cases of more inclusive prohibitions and expanded prohibitions. Rather, the mishna is referring only to cases **of simultaneous prohibitions, and in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. Granted,** with regard to **all of** the prohibitions in the mishna, **you can find that they** take effect simultaneously in the case **where** the men **appointed** a single **agent** to betroth the women for them, **and** the women **appointed an agent** to accept the betrothals for them, **and** one **agent encounters** the other **agent.** In this scenario, all the men become betrothed to all the women the moment that the agents meet, and the prohibitions all take effect simultaneously. **However,** with regard to the prohibition pertaining to **menstruating women, how can you find** the case where both women become menstruating women at the exact time of betrothal?

The final alternative the Gemara entertains is that the *Mishnah* need not be attributed to Rabbi Meir at all. Instead, one could read all four transgressions as arising literally simultaneously — *bevas achas* — in which case even Rabbi Shimon, who otherwise rejects *issur chal al issur*, would concede that all four apply. The challenge is constructing a scenario in which *eishet ish*, *eishet ach*, *achos isha*, and *niddah* can all genuinely take effect at the identical moment.

### Shlichim Synchronization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַב: בְּשׁוֹפְעוֹת מִתּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר — לְאִחַיּוֹבֵי אִינְהוּ, מִתּוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר — לְחַיּוֹבֵי אִינְהִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said that Rav said:** This is referring to a situation **where** the women **continuously discharge** menstrual blood **from within the thirteenth** year of the brothers **until after** they reached the age of **thirteen,** in order **to deem** the men **liable** for the prohibition pertaining to a menstruating woman. At the moment that the boys reached maturity and the betrothals went into effect, the sisters were forbidden as menstruating women. And the women were continuously discharging blood **from within the twelfth** year of the women **until after** the age of **twelve, in order to deem** the women **liable** for engaging in relations as a menstruating woman. Therefore, if this took place in such a way that on the exact day when the men reached maturity, i.e., their thirteenth birthday, the women reached maturity, i.e., their twelfth birthday, and at that moment they were menstruating, then all of the prohibitions would take effect simultaneously and they would be liable on all counts, according to Rabbi Shimon as well.

The synchronization of *eishet ish*, *eishet ach*, and *achos isha* is achievable through the mechanism of *shlichim* (agents). Suppose the two brothers — Reuven and Shimon — appoint a single agent, Michael, to perform two *kiddushin* simultaneously. Reuven's *kiddushin* is to Rachel, Shimon's is to Leah. Simultaneously, Rachel and Leah appoint their own agent, Devorah, to receive their *kiddushin*. Michael presents two *perutot* (coins) to Devorah in a single act — one for Rachel, one for Leah. In that exact instant, Rachel becomes Reuven's *eishet ish*, and since Shimon is Reuven's brother, she is also Shimon's *eishet ach*; Leah simultaneously becomes Shimon's wife and Rachel's sister, rendering her Reuven's *achos isha*. All three statuses crystallize in the same moment. This is the scenario the Gemara constructs: *d'shavyuhu shaliach ve'shavu inhu shaliach, u-faga shaliach b'shaliach* — the brothers made one agent, the women made another, and the two agents met.

### Niddah Age Three

The *niddah* prohibition, however, resists this synchronization. A woman is either already a *niddah* before the *kiddushin* moment — in which case the *niddah* prohibition predates *eishet ish* and is not simultaneous with it — or she becomes a *niddah* afterward, in which case it arrives later. Neither option gives us *bevas achas* for all four.

### Age Three Solution

The Gemara resolves this with a striking construction. *B'shofaat mitoch g' shanim* — we are dealing with women who began experiencing *niddah* bleeding before their third birthdays and who remained in *niddah* status continuously through their third birthday. The key is that halachic liability for acts of *biah* (intercourse) with a girl under three does not take effect — the act is not formally reckoned as *biah* under three years of age. So: the fathers of these girls (who are minors, and whose *kiddushin* may be effected by their fathers) arranged the *kiddushin* before the girls reached three. The girls were already in a state of *niddah* from before age three. For the brothers, all four prohibitions — *eishet ish*, *eishet ach*, *achos isha*, and *niddah* — crystallize at the single moment when the girls turn three, because only at that moment does the *biah* become formally actionable, and at that moment the *niddah* status is already present alongside the others. This is *bevas achas* for the brothers' liability.

For the women's own *chata* obligations, the relevant moment is when they themselves reach the age of *bat mitzvah* (twelve years). The Gemara's formulation — *v'chayiveinu mitoch shteim esrei l'achar shteim esrei l'chiyuvey inhei* — indicates that to create the women's liability, the *niddah* status established before age three (and never interrupted by *tevilah* in a *mikveh*) must persist continuously through their becoming *bat mitzvah*. At the moment they become obligated in *mitzvot*, the same set of simultaneous prohibitions is already in place, and the *niddah* component is the same continuous status that was operative from before age three. If they had immersed in the interim, the *niddah* status would have lapsed and the simultaneity would be broken. The scenario thus requires that neither woman immersed from before age three until after becoming *bat mitzvah* — a span of roughly nine years.

## Pregnancy/Tamar

### First Act Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַפְרִישִׁים אוֹתָן. וְהָא אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת בְּבִיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: שֶׁבָּעֲלוּ וְשָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that **we separate** these women from their husbands for three months, as perhaps they became pregnant. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it known that a virgin **woman does not become pregnant from her first sexual act? Rav Naḥman said that Rabba bar Avuh said:** This is referring to a situation **where they engaged in intercourse and then repeated** the act, so that it is possible that the women were impregnated during the second act of intercourse.

The *Mishnah* requires a three-month separation to ensure the women are not pregnant — *shema me'ubarot hen*. But a challenge arises: the Gemara operates on the assumption, widely held in the ancient world, that a woman cannot become pregnant from the first act of intercourse. If the men slept with the wrong wives only once, there is no risk of pregnancy. Rav Nachman, citing Rabba bar Avuha, resolves this: *shebau ve'shanu* — they had intercourse more than once. The second act is when pregnancy becomes possible.

A further objection presents itself: if we count each act separately, Rabbi Chiya's accounting of sixteen *chataot* would rapidly become incoherent, since Rabbi Eliezer holds that every distinct act of thrusting within a prolonged sexual encounter constitutes a separate act of *biah* — potentially multiplying the *chataot* enormously. The Gemara resolves this by explaining that the count of sixteen is based on the first act of *biah* alone — *b'via rishona kachashiv* — and that the three-month separation presupposes a second act having occurred. These are simply two separate framings of the scenario operating in parallel.

### Hymen Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת, תְּלָתִין וְתַרְתֵּין הָוְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if this is so, how can one explain **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: Here, then, are sixteen sin-offerings?** If there were indeed two acts of intercourse, then **there** should **be thirty-two** sin-offerings, since each prohibition was violated twice.

The Gemara then raises the biblical case of Tamar, who became pregnant by Yehuda apparently from a single encounter — *d'b'via rishona iabra*. How does this square with the assumption that pregnancy cannot result from a first act? The answer offered is that Tamar had previously removed her hymen manually: *Tamar b'etzba miacha*. Rav Yitzchak adds that this practice was known from the household of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — girls there would do so and were therefore given the name Tamar as a kind of epithet, reflecting what the original Tamar had done. The Gemara presupposes that the hymen, when intact, physically prevents conception on the first act; once it is removed, the obstacle is gone.

This connects to a broader halachic context: there is a rabbinic (post-Talmudic in its full development) treatment of *dam besulim* (hymenal blood) as resembling *niddah* blood, which historically gave rise to practices of pre-emptive hymen removal before the wedding night so as to avoid the resulting *niddah* status on the first night. The Gemara's assumptions about physiology reflect the medical understanding of the Talmudic period.

### Er/Onan Sin

But a new difficulty arises: Tamar had been married to Er and Onan before Yehuda. Was she not therefore no longer a virgin? The Gemara's answer is that *Er v'Onan shimshu shelo kedarkan* — Er and Onan had intercourse with her in a non-vaginal manner (*bi'ah shelo kedarkan*), which left her hymen intact. The Gemara then cites a *beraita*: *kol esrim ve'arba'ah chodesh dash mibifnim ve'zoreh mibachutz divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — for the twenty-four months of nursing, Rabbi Eliezer permits a form of early withdrawal (threshing on the inside, winnowing on the outside, to borrow the agricultural metaphor) to avoid impregnating the nursing mother. The Sages respond: *hallalu eino ela k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan* — this is nothing other than what Er and Onan did, and is forbidden.

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension between the *beraita* (which equates early withdrawal with the sin of Er and Onan) and its own characterization of Er and Onan's sin as *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* (non-vaginal sex, not early withdrawal): *k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan ve'lo k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan* — the nursing practice is *like* what they did in one respect (the semen is expelled outside the body, *ve'shichet artzah*) but *not* exactly what they did, because theirs was *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* while the nursing practice involves normal vaginal intercourse (*kedarkan*) with withdrawal at the end.

### Early Withdrawal Forbidden

The Gemara then asks: we understand from the verse *ve'shichet artzah* that Onan practiced this form of emission, but what is the source that it was *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* rather than early withdrawal? And separately, the verse only explicitly describes Onan's act — what is the basis for assuming Er sinned in the same way? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers from the word *gam* in *vayamot gam oto* — "he too died." The word *gam* implies "also," meaning Er died by the same cause and in the same manner as Onan. The reason given for Onan's act is explicit: *lo lo yihyeh hazera* — he knew the child would be reckoned under his deceased brother's name, not his own, and he resisted that. Er's motive, by contrast, is inferred by the Gemara as having been aesthetic: *kedei shelo titaber ve'yachahish yofyah* — he did not want Tamar to become pregnant and lose her beauty. In both cases, the act was *bi'ah shelo kedarkan*.

## Zera Levatala Debate

### Tosafot First View

The discussion of *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* and the condemnation of Er and Onan's conduct opens onto a significant halakhic question that the Rishonim debate at length: in the context of marital relations, is a husband ever permitted to have a seminal emission outside of his wife? Two distinct *Gemara* passages appear to point in different directions. Here, early withdrawal is condemned as *k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan*; elsewhere (in tractate *Nedarim*), the Gemara appears to permit *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* in the marital setting without qualification. Tosafot addresses this tension directly. The first approach offered by Tosafot reconciles the sources by limiting the *Nedarim* permission to cases where *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* does not involve seminal emission: *mekivan d'leis hashchasas zera lo havei k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan* — only because there is no destruction of seed does it not constitute the sin of Er and Onan. On this reading, *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* is permissible in marriage only so long as it does not result in the semen being expelled outside the wife.

### Ri's View

The Ri (Rabbi Yitzchak, cited within the same Tosafot) offers a second, more permissive approach. *Velo chashiv k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan ela keshemitkavein lehashchis hazera ve'ragil la'asos kein tamid* — an act is only considered to be in the category of Er and Onan's sin when the person's *intention* is the destruction of seed as such, and when this is his habitual practice. If, on the other hand, the couple engages in *bi'ah shelo kedarkan* simply as part of their marital intimacy — *aval be'akrai be'alma umis'aveh lavo le'ishto* — without this being a calculated strategy to avoid procreation or destroy seed systematically, then *shelo kedarka shary*: it is permitted. The Ri grounds this in the *Nedarim* principle that *kol mah she'adam rotzeh la'asos be'ishto ya'aseh* — within the marital relationship a man may conduct himself with his wife as he wishes. On this view, the marital context transforms the character of the act: what would otherwise be *zera levatala* (wasted seed) is not so considered when it arises naturally from the couple's intimacy.

### Rema's Ruling

The practical *halakha* is shaped decisively by the *Rema* (Rabbi Moshe Isserles), who rules in accordance with the Ri's second approach. The implication is far-reaching: according to the *Rema*, the prohibition of *zera levatala* does not apply within the marital relationship regardless of whether seminal emission occurs during vaginal intercourse or otherwise, provided it is not a deliberate and habitual practice of seed destruction. This ruling has significant relevance for how marital relations are understood within *halakha*, and its basis in this passage of the Gemara — via the Ri in Tosafot — reflects the broader principle that contextual intention and relational setting are determinative in this domain.
