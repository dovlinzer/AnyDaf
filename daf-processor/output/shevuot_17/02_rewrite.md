# Shevuot 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shehiya for Malkus

### Rava: Shehiya for Malkus

The daf opens at the very bottom of 16b. The context is the problem of entering the *mikdash* while *tamei*: one who is *tamei* and enters becomes liable for a *korban* if the transgression was unintentional, and the earlier discussion established that one can also incur liability through *shehiya* — lingering for the duration of a full prostration — even without having actively walked in *tamei*. With this backdrop, Rava poses a foundational question: *tzarich shehiya l'malkus o ein tzarich shehiya l'malkus* — does one need *shehiya* in order to incur *malkus* when standing in the *mikdash* while *tamei*?

The question operates on two distinct levels. First, there is a general principle that *malkus* requires an act — a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* does not generate lashes. This parallels a discussion in Makkot regarding one who wears *kilayim*: is the continued wearing of the garment, when one had an opportunity to remove it and did not, considered an active continuation of the original prohibited act, or merely passive non-compliance? The case here is structurally different: the person never performed an act of entering the *mikdash* while *tamei* — he became *tamei* after he was already inside. So when he remains, is that considered a sufficient act to deserve *malkus*? Second, there is a question about the nature of the prohibition itself: *malkus* would be for the *lav* of entering, but this person did not enter while *tamei* — the *tumah* came upon him afterwards.

The *gemara* frames the unresolved tension as follows: *l'korban g'miri shehiya l'malkus lo g'miri shehiya* — perhaps the principle of *shehiya* was derived only in the context of *korban*, not for *malkus*. Alternatively — *bifnim g'miri shehiya* — perhaps the principle is simply that any lingering inside while *tamei* creates liability, regardless of whether the case leads to a *korban* or to *malkus*. The *gemara* concludes: *teiku*.

### Korban vs. Malkus

The phrase *tzarich shehiya* — "does one need *shehiya*?" — sounds, at first glance, as though the alternative is that one transgresses immediately, without any waiting. In fact, Tosafot reads the question in precisely the opposite direction: the question is not whether *shehiya* is necessary for liability, but whether *shehiya* is sufficient to generate *malkus* at all. Tosafot on 17a makes this explicit: *hacha mashma d'lokei a'shehiya* — it is evident that one does get lashes for *shehiya*; the only question is whether the measure must equal the *shiur hishtachavaya* or not.

This reading leads Tosafot into a discussion of why it is so obvious that *shehiya* could produce *malkus* given the requirement of *ma'aseh*. And that leads to the heart of the distinction. The case of *korban* is one where the person became *tamei* unintentionally — *b'shogeg* — and then was informed and chose not to leave. The case of *malkus* is one where the person is willfully remaining. Given the willful nature of remaining, why would *shehiya* of any particular duration be required? Perhaps even one second of intentional staying constitutes the act of violation.

The contrast is clarified by a second Tosafot passage, which distinguishes three scenarios. In the first, the person is already *tamei* and warned not to enter the *mikdash* — he walks in, and that act of entry generates *malkus* immediately. In the second, there is a *sheretz* inside the *mikdash* and the person is warned not to touch it and become *tamei*, but he does so anyway — that act of rendering himself *tamei* generates *malkus* at once. In the third scenario — the one Rava is examining — the person accidentally touched something *tamei* while inside and is now *tamei* unintentionally, people are telling him he must leave or he will incur liability, and he willfully remains. In this third case, the question is whether willful remaining requires any minimum *shiur* before it constitutes a violation, or whether the very first moment of intentional staying suffices.

### Suspended in Air

Rava then poses a second question: *toleh atzmo b'avir azarah mahu* — what is the law for one who is suspended in the airspace of the *azarah*, hovering without touching the ground? He has waited the duration of a *hishtachavaya*. Does he incur liability?

The question cuts to the conceptual core of *shehiya*. Is the idea of *shehiya* that we treat the waiting as a virtual *hishtachavaya* — the person effectively "performed" a prostration during that time — in which case, a person physically incapable of prostrating (because he is suspended in the air, clinging to a wall) could not be said to have virtually done so, and he would be exempt? Or is *shehiya* simply a *halacha* tied to any meaningful presence inside the *mikdash* — *bifnim g'miri shehiya* — and it makes no difference whether or not the position was conducive to actual prostration: *lo shna d'vas hishtachavaya v'lo shna d'la vas hishtachavaya*?

The *gemara* again concludes: *teiku*.

### Willful Tumah

Rav Ashi now poses a further variation: *timei atzmo bemezid mahu* — what if the person willfully rendered himself *tamei* while inside the *mikdash*? The *gemara* asks: *be'ones g'miri shehiya bemezid lo g'miri shehiya* — perhaps the principle of *shehiya* applies only when the *tumah* came upon the person involuntarily, but when he deliberately made himself *tamei*, he is liable immediately, without any *shiur*. Or alternatively — *bifnim g'miri shehiya lo shna be'ones velo shna bemezid* — perhaps the principle of lingering applies regardless of how the *tumah* came about, and one always needs *shehiya* in order to be liable.

The *gemara* concludes: *teiku*.

### Tosafot's Case

Since the question involves *bemezid*, one would naturally assume it concerns *malkus*, as intentional violation ordinarily excludes the possibility of a *korban*. Tosafot, however, reads the case as still pertaining to a *korban*. Tosafot explains: *tzarich lomar desha'ah shetimei atzmo lo yada shehayah beMikdash* — it must be that when he rendered himself *tamei*, he did not know he was in the *mikdash*. He knew he was touching the *sheretz* and becoming *tamei*, but he was unaware of his location. This combination — deliberate *tumah* without awareness of being in the *mikdash* — means the violation remains *b'shogeg*, and the question is whether the *korban* requires the full *shiur shehiya* or whether his deliberate act of self-contamination changes the calculus.

The resulting typology covers three distinct cases. If the person knew he was in the *mikdash* and deliberately made himself *tamei*, he obviously incurs *malkus* with no *shehiya* required. If he became *tamei* involuntarily and is now willfully remaining, the question is whether *malkus* requires a full *shiur* or takes effect immediately. And according to Tosafot, Rav Ashi's question is a third case: he deliberately became *tamei* but was unaware of his location, and the question is whether such deliberate *tumah* affects the *shiur* required for the *korban*.

## Nazir in Cemetery

### Nazir Unintentional

Rav Ashi introduces yet another variation: *nazir bekever* — a *nazir* who finds himself inside a cemetery. For the case to parallel the prior discussion — where *tumah* came about involuntarily — Rashi explains the *nazir* must have arrived *be'ones*: he was being transported in a sealed container, the container broke, and he found himself inside the *beis hakevaros* without any volitional act of entry.

The question is whether *shehiya* is required before he incurs *malkus* for violating the prohibition against a *nazir* becoming *tamei*. The *gemara* presents the tension: *bifnim g'miri shehiya* — the concept of *shehiya* as a minimum measure makes intuitive sense in the context of the *mikdash*, where the *azarah* is the place of *hishtachavaya* and presence in the *mikdash* is the violated state. *Bachutz lo g'miri shehiya* — perhaps outside the *mikdash*, in a cemetery, we have no basis for applying a minimum duration. Alternatively — *be'ones g'miri shehiya lo shna bifnim velo shna bachutz* — perhaps the principle applies universally wherever *tumah* came about involuntarily: in any such case, a minimum duration is required before lingering constitutes a transgression.

It is worth noting an internal curiosity: if *shehiya* does apply to a *nazir* in a cemetery, the measure used would presumably be the duration of a *hishtachavaya* — the time to perform a prostration. This measure makes obvious sense in the *azarah*, where prostration is the paradigmatic act. Why it should apply in a cemetery setting is far less clear, but this is the *shiur* the tradition has attached to the concept of *shehiya*.

The *gemara* again concludes: *teiku*.

### Logic of Shiur

The underlying logic of requiring a *shiur* in cases of involuntary *tumah* deserves attention. When one eats a forbidden substance, a minimum *kezayis* is required before any liability attaches — eating a smaller quantity, while perhaps prohibited, does not generate a *korban* or *malkus*. Something analogous may be at work here. When *tumah* fell upon a person without any act of will, and he finds himself inside the *mikdash* or the *beis hakevaros*, one might ask: at what point can we say he has truly "done something" worthy of punishment? Merely standing there for an instant before he even has a chance to react seems too thin a basis for liability. Requiring a minimum duration — *shehiya* — is a way of saying that only once he has remained for a meaningful period, beyond what would be necessary to begin exiting, can we regard his presence as an act of staying, rather than simply the continuation of an involuntary situation.

### Tosafot Critique

Tosafot raises a sharp challenge to the entire framework of *shehiya* as applied to the *nazir* in a cemetery. In the *mikdash*, the rationale for *shehiya* is intelligible: a *tamei* person's presence in the *mikdash* is a continuing offense, because the *tumah* is actively violating the sanctity of the *mikdash*, and remaining there maintains and perpetuates that violation. The obligation to remove oneself is rooted in this ongoing desecration.

But in the case of a *nazir* in a cemetery, Tosafot argues, remaining does not add to the violation. A *nazir*'s problem is that he becomes *tamei*. Once he is already *tamei* from his involuntary entry, his continued presence in the *beis hakevaros* does not make him any more *tamei* than he already is. His level of *tumah* is not increasing. The offense was the becoming *tamei*, and that has already occurred. Why, then, should remaining for additional time compound his liability in any way?

Tosafot notes that this question has practical relevance for *kohanim*. When a *kohen* lawfully buries a close relative — entering the *beis hakevaros* to carry the coffin — he becomes *tamei* in the course of a permitted act. Once *tamei*, passing over additional graves does not increase his *tumah* level. The question of what obligations attend his exit from the cemetery, and whether staying beyond a certain point creates new violations, depends on the very same analytical distinction Tosafot is pressing here.

## Short vs. Long Route

### Rava's Route Rule

Having analyzed the issue of *shehiya* — the duration of waiting — the *gemara* turns to the parallel principle of taking the short versus the long route out of the *azarah*. One who is *tamei* and inside the *mikdash* is required to exit. The principle: *b'arucha chayav uviktzara patur* — if he takes the long route, he is liable; if he takes the short route, he is exempt.

Rava establishes a striking ruling: *ketzara she'amru afilu akev b'tzad godel v'afilu kol hayom kulo* — when the tradition speaks of the "short route," it means even walking heel-to-toe at the most glacially slow pace, even if it takes the entire day. As long as the person is actively walking — continuously moving, without stopping — and is on the short route, he is exempt from liability regardless of how long it takes. Pausing is measured separately by *shehiya*; route is measured separately by which path is taken. These are two independent considerations.

### Combining Intervals

Rava then asks: *shehiyot mahu she'yitzatrefu* — if a person pauses for short intervals separated by walking, do the separate intervals of *shehiya* combine? Suppose the *shiur shehiya* is three seconds. The person waits one second, walks a few steps, waits another second, walks, waits a third second. Do these combine to reach the requisite *shiur*?

One might think that Rava's own ruling answers this: if one may walk heel-to-toe for the entire day and remain exempt, then surely very slow walking already incorporates countless micro-pauses. The *gemara* responds: *hatam d'lo shahah* — walking extremely slowly is nevertheless continuous motion without actual stopping; it is categorically different from a series of genuine pauses with intervals of walking between them. The question of whether separate intervals of *shehiya* combine thus remains unresolved.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye now challenges the premise underlying Rava's formulation. Rava spoke as though the distinction between the short and long routes is self-evidently about the route itself — the path taken — rather than the time consumed. Abaye asks: *ba lo b'arucha shiur ketzara mahu* — if someone took the long route but ran it so fast that he covered it in the time it would take to traverse the short route, what is the law? Is it about the *shiur* — the duration — regardless of which physical path was taken? Or is it *davka* — specifically about which route was taken, without regard to how long it actually consumed?

### Route Determines It

Rava responds: *lo nitna arucha lehiddachot etzlo* — the long route cannot be pushed aside or "replaced" by the short route's time. Rashi reads this as a clear ruling: the long route always incurs liability, even if traversed in less time than it would take to walk the short route. Conversely, the short route always exempts, even if the person takes the entire day. The route itself — not the time — is the operative criterion. This confirms what Rava had assumed from the outset: *davka* the route, not the duration.

## Avodah While Tamei

### Rav Zevid Difficulty

Rav Zevid now raises a difficulty generated by the preceding analysis. There is a *halacha* that a *kohen* who performs *avodah* in the *mikdash* while *tamei* is liable for *mita bidei shamayim*. This is a distinct punishment from the *karet* that attaches to simply lingering in the *mikdash* while *tamei*. Rav Zevid wishes to isolate a scenario in which one incurs *mita bidei shamayim* for performing *avodah* while *tamei* without simultaneously incurring *karet* for lingering too long. But the combination of these two principles seems to foreclose any such case: *heichi mishkachat lah* — how can you find it?

### Time-Based Solution

If one performed *avodah* and then exited without pausing: *heichi avid avoda* — when did he have time to perform the *avodah* if he ran straight out? And if he paused long enough to perform the *avodah*: *bar karet hu* — he has already incurred *karet* for *shehiya* as well, not merely *mita bidei shamayim*.

The resolution depends on the prior debate. If the *shiur* is about time — if what matters is whether the person spent more than the duration of the short route — then there is a workable scenario. The *gemara* explains: the clock for *karet* liability would be the time required to traverse the short route *plus* the *shiur shehiya* (the duration of a *hishtachavaya*). Rashi reads the two figures as additive. Suppose the short route takes thirty seconds and the *shiur shehiya* is three seconds, giving a total of thirty-three seconds. A *kohen* who performs a brief act of *avodah* — say, *shechita*, which takes only a few seconds — and then rushes out through the short route within the total thirty-three second window would have performed *avodah* while *tamei* (incurring *mita bidei shamayim*) without having lingered long enough to incur *karet*.

### Route-Based Problem

But if the rule is not about time but about which route is taken, the arithmetic changes dramatically. The exemption from *karet* requires taking the short route, and independently, *shehiya* of more than three seconds incurs liability. There is no combining of time figures. The *kohen* must exit via the short route *and* must not pause for more than three seconds. Given that virtually any act of *avodah* takes more than three seconds, how can he perform *avodah* and still avoid the *shehiya*-based *karet*?

### Rashi Combines Times

As noted, Rashi's resolution on the time-based reading is that the two figures — the time of the short route and the *shiur shehiya* — are totaled together. The person is not on two independent clocks; rather, the permissible window inside the *mikdash* is the sum of both periods. This allows for the *avodah* to be performed within the combined interval.

The difficulty Rav Zevid is pressing, however, is that if the operative principle is *davka* about the route — as Rava concluded in responding to Abaye — this additive solution is unavailable. The short route and the *shehiya* are separate and non-combinable conditions, and no act of *avodah* will fit within the *shehiya* window alone.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye responds that there is no difficulty: *mishkachat lah kegon de'ba biktzara vehapach betzinora* — you find the case where the *kohen* is walking out via the short route and, in the course of walking, flips over meat on the *mizbeach* using the pitchfork (*tzinora*). He never pauses — he continues walking the entire time. The act of *avodah* is performed literally in motion, on the way out, without any *shehiya* at all. He incurs *mita bidei shamayim* for performing *avodah* while *tamei*, but since he paused not a moment — walking continuously on the short route — he does not incur *karet*.

### Rav Huna Rule

Abaye grounds this in a teaching of Rav Huna: *zar shehafach betzinora chayav mita* — a non-*kohen* who flips over the meat with the pitchfork is liable for *mita bidei shamayim*, because this constitutes an act of *avodah*. The *gemara* then asks: what is the precise case? If flipping the meat is essential — without it, the meat would not be consumed by the fire — then obviously it constitutes *avodah*. And if the fire would eventually consume it regardless, then what did the person actually do?

The *gemara* resolves: *lo tzricha de'ei lo hapich behu miachali betarti sha'ei vehashata miachali b'chada sha'ata* — the case is where without flipping, the meat would have taken two hours to be consumed, and by flipping, it is consumed in one hour. The act was not necessary but meaningfully accelerated the process. And the lesson drawn: *kol keruvei avodah avodah hi* — anything that advances or speeds up the *avodah*, even if not strictly necessary, is itself *avodah*. It is sufficient for a non-*kohen* to incur *mita bidei shamayim*, and equally sufficient for a *tamei kohen* to incur *mita bidei shamayim*. This ruling provides the halakhic foundation for Abaye's answer: the mid-stride flip of the meat is a bona fide act of *avodah*, and performing it on the way out — without pause — is precisely how one can be liable for *avodah* while *tamei* without simultaneously incurring *karet* for *shehiya*.

## Natural Entry

### Rav Oshaya House

The *gemara* now introduces an apparently separate teaching, which turns out to be directly connected to the laws of *tumah* in the *mikdash*. Rav Oshaya prefaces his statement: *beina de'eima milta umistafina mechavraya* — I want to say something but I am afraid of the reaction of my colleagues. This kind of formulation in the *gemara* signals that what follows is a significant and somewhat counterintuitive ruling.

His ruling: *ha'nichnas l'veit hamenuga derech achorav v'afilu kulo chutz mechotamo tahor* — one who enters a house afflicted with *tzara'at* walking backwards, even if his entire body is inside except for his nose, which is still facing outward toward the entrance, is *tahor*. He has not incurred *tumah*.

The source is the verse *v'haba el habayit* — "the one who comes into the house" — which the Torah uses to define when one becomes *tamei* from a *tzara'at* house. Rav Oshaya reads this as requiring *derech bi'ah* — entry in the natural, customary manner, which means face-forward. A person who enters backwards has not fulfilled the act of *bi'ah* as the Torah defines it, and therefore has not incurred the *tumah* of the house.

### Rava Support

The anticipated objection to Rav Oshaya's ruling is immediate: *ei hachi kulo nami* — if that is so, then even if the person's entire body is inside, he should also be *tahor*, since he still entered in an unnatural manner. Rav Oshaya cannot answer this objection, but Rava comes to his defense with a distinction: *kulo lo gara mikelim shebabayit* — when the person is fully inside, he is *tamei* not because of the act of *bi'ah* (entering), but by virtue of simply *being* inside, analogous to the vessels in the house, which become *tamei* through presence, not entry. The verse *v'nitma kol asher babayit* — "everything that is in the house shall become *tamei*" — covers anything *inside*, regardless of how it got there.

So Rav Oshaya's ruling stands: the act of *bi'ah* into a *tzara'at* house requires *derech bi'ah* — natural entry face-forward — and backward entry with any part of the body still outside does not constitute entry at all. But full immersion inside the house creates *tumah* by a separate principle of presence, not entry.

### Roof Entry

This framework is now applied directly to the *mikdash*. A *baraita* teaches: *gagot hallalu* — the roofs above the *azarah* and the *heichal* were not sanctified with the *kedusha* of the *heichal* or the *azarah*. One may not eat *kodshei kodashim* there, may not perform *shechitas kodashim kalim* there. The roof does not possess the *kedusha* of the interior space.

Accordingly: *v'hatamei shenichnas derech gagin l'heichal patur* — a *tamei* person who enters the *heichal* by crashing through the roof is exempt. This follows from both principles: the roof has no *kedusha* (so being on the roof is not a violation), and descending through the roof into the *heichal* is not *derech bi'ah* — it is not the natural mode of entry. The verse *v'el ha'mikdash lo tavo* — "and to the *mikdash* you shall not come" — is interpreted, like the verse about the *tzara'at* house, as requiring *derech bi'ah asrah Torah* — the Torah prohibits specifically natural, customary entry.

### Shehiya Post-Roof

Critically, however, this exemption applies only to the act of entry itself. If the person — having crashed through the roof — then remains inside the *heichal* for the *shiur shehiya*, he becomes liable through *shehiya*, even though his entry was not *derech bi'ah*. The act of entry was not prohibited, but the lingering becomes its own basis for liability. This brings the discussion full circle back to the theme of *shehiya* that has dominated the entire daf: even when the triggering act was absent or irregular, *shehiya* — remaining — can independently constitute the violation.

## Beit Din Ruling

### Horayot Rule

The *gemara* then transitions to an apparently digressive topic: the circumstances under which a *beit din* that issues an erroneous ruling incurs liability and must bring a *korban*. The context is the *mishnah* in Horayot, which states that *beit din* does not bring a *korban* for errors involving the positive and negative commandments connected to *tumah* in the *mikdash*. The *gemara* here — *zo mitzvat aseh she'be'mikdash she'ein chayavin aleha* — identifies this as the case the *mishnah* in Horayot is referencing.

For *beit din* to incur liability for an erroneous ruling, the individual sin at issue must be one for which an individual would bring a fixed-price *chatat* when he violates it unintentionally. The case of *tumat mikdash*, however, involves a *korban oleh v'yored* — a sliding-scale *korban* whose composition varies depending on the financial status of the offender.

### Variable Korban

Since the *korban* for *tumat mikdash* is an *oleh v'yored* — variable in cost — it does not meet the criterion for *beit din* liability. *Beit din* brings its communal *korban* only when the individual's sin would have generated a standard fixed-price *chatat*. The variable-price *korban*, though it contains all the structural elements of a serious prohibition — a positive commandment (*yishalchu min hamachaneh*), a negative commandment (not to enter the *mikdash* while *tamei*), *karet*, and a *korban* — nevertheless falls outside the scope of *beit din* liability on account of the *korban*'s variable nature.

### No Asham Talui

The same logic excludes *tumat mikdash* from the domain of *asham talui* — the conditional guilt offering brought when a person has concrete doubt about whether he violated a sin punishable by a *korban chatat*. The *asham talui* is brought as a temporary atonement while the matter remains uncertain. But the *asham talui* applies only where the underlying offense, if confirmed, would generate a fixed-price *chatat*. Since *tumat mikdash* generates an *oleh v'yored*, there is no *asham talui* for a doubtful violation of this prohibition either.

The *gemara* thus places the entire topic of *tumat mikdash* — with all its detailed analysis of *shehiya*, routes, airspace, and *avodah* — outside the framework of both *beit din* corporate liability and individual *asham talui* liability, solely on account of the variable nature of its *korban*.

## Niddah Parallel

### Theological Parallel

The *gemara* now pivots to the case that *does* generate *beit din* liability: the positive and negative commandments governing intercourse with a *niddah*. Before turning to the technical law, it is worth pausing on the extraordinary juxtaposition the *gemara* is drawing.

The entire daf has been occupied with the details of entering and remaining in the *mikdash* while *tamei*. Entry into the *mikdash* is referred to throughout as *bi'ah* — the same word the Torah uses for sexual intercourse. The verse about the *tzara'at* house — *haba el habayit*, *derech bi'ah asrah Torah* — is quoted in the same breath as the verse about the *mikdash*: *v'el ha'mikdash lo tavo*. The act of *bi'ah* into the sacred space — face-forward, in the natural mode, carrying the full weight of *kedusha* and its obligations — is now placed in explicit parallel with the act of *bi'ah* in the marital context.

The *gemara*'s discussions of the *Kohen Gadol* entering the *Kodesh HaKodashim* on *Yom Kippur* are shot through with imagery of ultimate intimacy with the Divine. On the other side of the parallel, the marital union — especially in the context of *taharas hamishpacha* — carries its own dimension of *kedusha*: it is the context in which new Jewish life may be created, in which husband and wife achieve a form of sacred union. The traditional literature's language of *Kedushas Yisrael* as bound up with *taharas hamishpacha* reflects precisely this parallel: what remains of *tumah v'taharah* in practical life today is the prohibition against intercourse with a *niddah*, and this single remaining application of *tumah* in the intimate domain is treated as bearing the weight of all the sanctity that *tumah v'taharah* represents.

### Niddah vs. Mikdash

The *mishnah* draws a sharp contrast: *aval chayavin al assei v'lo ta'aseh she'be'niddah* — *beit din* is liable for erroneous rulings about the positive and negative commandments relating to *niddah*. The relevant *korban* for intercourse with a *niddah b'shogeg* is a fixed-price *chatat*, which satisfies the criterion for *beit din* liability. The same principle extends to *asham talui*: if a person has a genuine doubt about whether his wife was a *niddah* when intercourse occurred, he would bring an *asham talui*.

### Intercourse Case

The specific case the *mishnah* addresses is the following: a man was having intercourse with his wife when she was *tehorah* — not yet a *niddah* — and in the course of the act she announced *nitmeiti* — I have just become a *niddah*. He is obligated to withdraw. But the *halacha* here is counterintuitive: he must *not* withdraw immediately with full erection.

### Withdrawal Transgression

If he withdraws immediately while still erect — *ufiresh miyad* — that act of withdrawal itself constitutes an act of intercourse: *mipnei sheyetz'iato hana'ah lo k'vi'ato* — the pleasure of withdrawal is equivalent to the pleasure of entry, and the act is considered a continuation of intercourse. He is therefore liable — *chayav* — for this act, which was performed when she was already a *niddah*. This is the *lo ta'aseh*, the negative commandment against intercourse with a *niddah*. And there is a positive commandment as well — derived from *v'hizartem et bnei Yisrael mitumatam* — which requires active separation from *tumah*, paralleling *v'yishalchu min hamachaneh* in the *mikdash* context.

## Two or One

### One or Two Acts

The *amoraim* dispute whether the man in this case transgresses once or twice. Abaye in the name of Rabbi Chiya bar Rav, and Rava in the name of Rav Shmuel bar Rav Shava in the name of Rav Huna, both rule *chayav shtayim* — he is liable for two transgressions. One is for the original act of entry (*achneisah*) and one is for the withdrawal (*afreishah*).

### Rava Challenge

Rava raises a sharp challenge: the case involves a man who had intercourse near the time his wife was expected to begin her period (*samuch l'vestah*). If we are speaking of a *talmid chacham* — someone who knows the relevant *halachot* — then the initial act is understandable as *shogeg*: he believed he could complete the act before she became a *niddah*, and he was wrong. That generates a *korban*. But when she announces in the middle that she has become a *niddah* — and he, as a *talmid chacham*, knows the *halacha* — he now knows that continuing or withdrawing with an erection is forbidden. At the moment of withdrawal he is *mezid*, not *shogeg*. Why should he bring a *korban* for the withdrawal, which was a fully intentional act?

### Abaye Answer

Abaye answers by reframing the case as involving an *am ha'aretz* — someone without *halachic* knowledge. When the wife announces she has become a *niddah*, the *am ha'aretz* does not recognize that this newly transforms his act into a transgression. He is not aware, even in that moment, that anything he is now doing is forbidden. His ignorance is pervasive and consistent throughout the entire episode: from the beginning, when he initiated intercourse near the time of her expected period, through to the moment of withdrawal. There is no point at which he transitions from *shogeg* to *mezid*, because he never acquires the awareness that would create that distinction.

### Korban Count

The conclusion follows from the level of knowledge and awareness throughout the act. For a *talmid chacham*: the initial act (*achneisah*) may be *shogeg* — irresponsible but unintentional — generating one *korban*. The withdrawal (*afreishah*) is *mezid* — intentional — and therefore generates no *korban* at all, though it is a serious transgression. For an *am ha'aretz*, by contrast, neither the entry nor the withdrawal is accompanied by any awareness that a sin has been committed. Both are *shogeg* throughout. However, because there is no moment of clear awareness separating the two acts — no point at which a new and distinct *shegagah* begins — the entire episode is treated as one continuous *shegagah*, and only one *korban* applies rather than two.
