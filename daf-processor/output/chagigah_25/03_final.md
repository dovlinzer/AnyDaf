# Chagigah 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Terumah & Pressing Time

### Intro: Belief Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּיהוּדָה אִין, וּבַגָּלִיל לָא, מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** In Judea, yes, but in the Galilee, no. What is the reason for this distinction between the two places?

The daf opens by cataloguing the circumstances under which an *am ha'aretz* — a common person not scrupulous about the laws of purity — is credited with claims about the status of produce and vessels. One category of believability is geographical: in Judea, *amei ha'aretz* are believed about *kodesh*, sacred Temple items, because of Judea's proximity to the *Beis Hamikdash*. A broader category, applying in all locations, concerns *terumah* — the priestly portion separated from produce — at the time of the pressing of grapes and olives.

### Pressing Season Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁעַת הַגִּיתּוֹת וְהַבַּדִּים — אַף עַל הַתְּרוּמָה. וּרְמִינְהִי: הַגּוֹמֵר זֵיתָיו — יְשַׁיֵּיר קוּפָּה אַחַת וְיִתְּנֶנָּה לְעָנִי כֹּהֵן!
>
> **Translation:** **And during the period of the winepress and olive press** *amei ha'aretz* **are trusted even with regard to** the purity of ***teruma*. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following teaching: An *am ha'aretz* **who finishes** pressing **his olives should leave over one sack** of unpressed olives, **and give it to a poor priest** as *teruma*, so that the priest himself can make ritually pure oil from it. This shows that even during the period of the olive press the *am ha'aretz* is not trusted to make pure olive oil himself.

The Mishnah states: *u'bishat hagisos ne'emanim al hatruma* — at the time of the pressing, they are believed about *terumah*. The Gemara immediately raises a contradiction from a *baraisa*: one who finishes pressing his olives should leave over one basket of unpressed olives and give it to a poor *kohen* who is a *chavér* — a scrupulous associate who observes the purity laws. The point of leaving unpressed olives is that olives which have not been pressed have not come into contact with liquid and are therefore not susceptible to *tumah*. The practical advice, then, is: if you want to give your *terumah* to a *kohen chavér*, give it in the form of olives or grapes before they have come into contact with liquid, and they will be able to accept it without concern. The implication is that you are *not* believed about *tumah va-tahara* even at the time of the pressing, which directly contradicts the Mishnah.

### Rashi's Rationale

Rashi explains the general rule: because it was the beginning of the pressing season, everyone brought their vessels to the *mikveh* and observed purity with greater care, creating a stronger presumption that everything was being handled in *tahara*. Beyond that factual basis, there is an explicit policy concern — which Rashi will spell out further below — that the *chavérim* among the *kohanim* needed to be able to receive *terumah* at this critical time, or they would lose access to the bulk of the year's *terumah*. Both considerations together undergird the rule.

### Early vs. Late Harvest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּחָרְפֵי, הָא בְּאַפְלֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: כְּגוֹן מַאי? כְּאוֹתָן שֶׁל בֵּית אָבִיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** This is **not difficult. This** case of the mishna, where *amei ha'aretz* are trusted to produce pure olive oil themselves, is referring **to** people who press their olives **early,** during the regular season of the olive press, while **that** case is referring **to** those who press their olives **later,** after the period when most people press their olives has passed. **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to him:** Such as **what** case, **for example?** Such **as those** olives **of your father's house.** Rav Naḥman's father had many olives, and he often pressed them after the regular pressing season.

To resolve the contradiction between the Mishnah and the *baraisa*, Rav Nachman states: *lo kashya ha be-charfei ha be-aflei* — there is no difficulty; the cases differ depending on whether we are dealing with an early or late harvest of the olives. The *baraisa* advises giving unpressed olives because the pressing in that case falls after the normal season — it is a late harvest — and therefore outside the window of credibility. This is an exception, not a refutation of the general rule. When asked for an example, Rav Adda bar Abba offers: like the olive orchards belonging to your father's household, which are known to ripen late and whose pressing regularly falls after the standard pressing season.

### Judea vs. Galilee

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בְּגָלִילָא שָׁנוּ. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: עֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן וְהַגָּלִיל — הֲרֵי הֵן כִּיהוּדָה, נֶאֱמָנִין עַל הַיַּיִן בִּשְׁעַת הַיַּיִן וְעַל הַשֶּׁמֶן בִּשְׁעַת הַשֶּׁמֶן, אֲבָל לֹא עַל הַיַּיִן בִּשְׁעַת הַשֶּׁמֶן וְלֹא עַל הַשֶּׁמֶן בִּשְׁעַת הַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** a different resolution of the above contradiction. The source that states that *amei ha'aretz* are not trusted **was taught** with **regard to the Galilee,** and as the mishna taught earlier concerning sacrificial wine and oil, *amei ha'aretz* are trusted only in Judea and not in the Galilee. **Abaye raised an objection to him** from a *baraita*: **Transjordan and the Galilee are like Judea,** in that **they are trusted with regard to wine** of *teruma* **during the period of wine** production, **and with regard to oil** of *teruma* **during the period of oil** production. **However,** they are **not** trusted **with regard to wine during the period of oil** production, **nor** are they trusted **with regard to oil during the period of wine** production. This *baraita* shows that with regard to *teruma* there is no difference between the trustworthiness of *amei ha'aretz* who live in the Galilee and that of those who live in Judea.

Rav Yosef proposes an alternative resolution: the *baraisa* was taught specifically with respect to the Galilee. This implies a geographic distinction in the rule of *terumah* believability at pressing time — that it applies in Judea but not in the Galilee. This is striking, because the geographic distinction we understood earlier applied to *kodesh* (since only in Judea could one readily access the *Beis Hamikdash*), but there is no obvious reason why *terumah* believability at pressing season should differ between Judea and the Galilee.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav Yosef's answer must be rejected, and **it is clear** that the correct answer is **as we answered initially,** that it is speaking of the period following the conclusion of the winepress.

Abaye challenges this position with a *baraisa*: *Ever Hayarden ve-ha-Galil harei hein ke-Yehuda* — the Trans-Jordan and the Galilee are treated the same as Judea for *terumah* purposes. They are believed that wine is *tahor* during the wine-pressing season and that oil is *tahor* during the oil-pressing season — and each only during its own respective season, not the other's. This *baraisa* explicitly extends the rule to the Galilee. The Gemara concludes: *ela mechvarta kedishni me'ikara* — the earlier answer was better. The clear principle is that the rule of believability about *terumah* at pressing time applies regardless of geographic location, and the original contradiction is resolved by distinguishing early from late harvests.

## Holding Till Next Season

### Post-Season Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ הַגִּיתּוֹת וְהַבַּדִּים וְהֵבִיאוּ לוֹ חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן — לֹא יְקַבְּלֶנָּה הֵימֶנּוּ, אֲבָל מַנִּיחָהּ לַגַּת הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Once **the** periods of the **winepress and olive press have passed,** if *amei ha'aretz* **brought to** a *ḥaver* priest a **barrel of *teruma* wine, he may not accept it from them. But** the giver **may leave it** over **for the following winepress** season, in the following year.

The Gemara continues: if the pressing season has passed — *avra hagisos* — and an *am ha'aretz* brings a *chavér* a barrel of wine, the *chavér* should not accept it. He cannot now believe the *am ha'aretz*, and accepting the wine would leave him holding *terumah temei'ah*. However, the Gemara adds a striking alternative: the *chavér* can tell the *am ha'aretz* to hold onto the barrel and bring it back at the next pressing season, perhaps ten months hence, at which point he will be able to believe him — even though it is the exact same barrel that has had ten additional months in which it could have become *tamei*. The counterintuitive nature of this rule is acknowledged: the delay only multiplies the opportunities for *tumah* to occur, yet the formal status at the time of the next pressing season governs.

### Acceptance Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: עָבַר וְקִיבְּלָהּ, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּנִּיחֶנָּה לַגַּת הַבָּאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **They raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet:** If the priest **violated** the *halakha* **and did accept** the wine from an *am ha'aretz*, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it permissible **that he should leave it** over for himself **for the following winepress** season? Since it is permissible to accept the wine and oil of an *am ha'aretz* intentionally left until that time, perhaps it is also permissible if the priest himself intentionally leaves it over until that time.

Rav Sheshet is asked: what if the *kohen chavér* already accepted the barrel — he did not say "bring it back later," he simply took it — is he now permitted to hold onto it until the next pressing season and at that point declare it *tahor*? Can the passage of time change the legal status of something already in his possession? The answer is sought from an existing teaching.

### Inheritance Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַיָּא: חָבֵר וְעַם הָאָרֶץ שֶׁיָּרְשׁוּ אֶת אֲבִיהֶם עַם הָאָרֶץ, יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: טוֹל אַתָּה חִטִּין שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, וַאֲנִי חִטִּין שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a *ḥaver* and an *am ha'aretz*, who** are brothers and **inherited** property from **their father,** who was also **an *am ha'aretz*,** the *ḥaver* **can say to** his *am ha'aretz* brother: **You take** the **wheat that is in such and such a place and I** will take the **wheat that is in such and such a place.** The *ḥaver* knows that the former batch of wheat had been made susceptible to impurity, and he would therefore have no use for it, while the latter batch had not been made susceptible to impurity.

The Gemara cites a *baraisa*: if a *chavér* and an *am ha'aretz* inherit their father's estate, and the father himself was an *am ha'aretz*, the estate would include wine, grapes, olives, grain, and similar produce. Since the father was an *am ha'aretz*, the *chavér* must presume all of it is *tamei*. The question becomes how to divide the estate between the two heirs.

### Wet/Dry Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוֹל אַתָּה יַיִן שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, וַאֲנִי יַיִן שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי. אֲבָל לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: טוֹל אַתָּה לַח וַאֲנִי יָבֵשׁ, טוֹל אַתָּה חִטִּין וַאֲנִי שְׂעוֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the *ḥaver* brother may say to the *am ha'aretz* brother: **You take** the **wine that is in such and such a place and I** will take the **wine that is in such and such a place.** The *ḥaver* knows that the latter batch of wine has not been rendered impure, and he wants to take that batch as his share. When brothers inherit a quantity of a certain item they will each end up receiving an equal share of that item. Therefore, the principle of retroactive designation applies, meaning that it is considered that whatever portion any particular brother receives in the end is the one that had been designated for him as his inheritance from the beginning. It is not considered to be a trade or a business transaction with the other brothers in exchange for their portions.
>
> **But** the *ḥaver* brother **may not say to** the *am ha'aretz* brother: **You take** the **wet** produce **and I** will take the **dry** produce. Nor may he say: **You take** the **wheat, and I** will take the **barley.** The principle of retroactive designation does not apply to objects of different types. If one brother would take wheat and the other barley it would be considered a trade. And it is prohibited for a *ḥaver* to sell or transfer impure produce or produce that is susceptible to impurity to an *am ha'aretz*, who does not strictly follow the principles of purity, as this would involve the prohibition of "You shall not place a stumbling-block before the blind" (Leviticus 19:14).

The difficulty in dividing the estate is subtle. One might think the *chavér* could simply take the dry grain — which has never come into contact with liquid and is therefore not susceptible to *tumah* — and let the *am ha'aretz* take the washed grain. But since both sons are equal partners in the whole estate, such a division would effectively mean the *chavér* is transferring his share of the *tamei* washed grain to the *am ha'aretz* in exchange for the *am ha'aretz*'s share of the *tahor* dry grain. A *chavér* is not permitted to hand *tamei* produce to an *am ha'aretz* who will not observe the restrictions on it — that amounts to facilitating a transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: אוֹתוֹ חָבֵר שׂוֹרֵף הַלַּח, וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַיָּבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to** this mishna: If the *ḥaver* receives a share that consists of some items that are wet and some that are dry, **that *ḥaver* must burn the wet** produce if it was *teruma*, as it has certainly been defiled and impure *teruma* is burned, **but he may leave the dry** produce and use it, as it can be assumed that it has not been defiled.

The *baraisa* therefore rules as follows: one may say *tol attah chittin she-bimkom peloni va-ani chittin she-bimkom peloni* — you take the wheat from over there and I will take the wheat from over here — where it happens that one location contains washed grain and the other dry grain, but the division is framed as a neutral geographic split of the same type of produce. One may similarly say: you take the wine from that place and I will take the wine from this place. However, one may not say: *tol attah lach va-ani yavesh* — you take the wet and I will take the dry — because that framing explicitly designates the *tamei* produce for the *am ha'aretz* and the *tahor* for the *chavér*, making it transparently a swap. Similarly, one may not say: you take the wheat and I will take the barley, because different species clearly constitute an exchange of portions.

This distinction rests on the principle of *yesh bereira* — that when a division is sufficiently natural and undifferentiated, we may treat it as though each party's portion was always already his, with no actual transfer occurring. When the division is instead framed in terms that make clear the *chavér* is trading the *tamei* for the *tahor*, there is no *bereira* to rely on and the arrangement is prohibited. If one holds *ein bereira* — that no division is ever truly pre-determined — every such split would be seen as an exchange, and the problem would be universal.

### Burning Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? יַנִּיחֶנָּה לַגַּת הַבָּאָה! בַּדָּבָר שֶׁאֵין לוֹ גַּת. וְיַנִּיחֶנּוּ לָרֶגֶל! בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִשְׁתַּמֵּר לָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara draws a conclusion based on this *baraita*: **Why** must he burn the wet *teruma*? **Let him leave it over until the following winepress** season, during which time *teruma* from an *am ha'aretz* is considered pure. The fact that this option is not taken into account indicates that one may not intentionally leave over *teruma* received from an *am ha'aretz* until the next winepress season, which would resolve the dilemma presented to Rav Sheshet. The Gemara rejects this proof: Here it is referring **to something that does not have a winepress,** i.e., to liquids that are never used in the Temple service, as *amei ha'aretz* are careful only with regard to liquids that may be used in the Temple service. The Gemara asks: Even so, there is another option: **And let him leave it for the** next **pilgrimage Festival,** for the mishna later teaches (26a) that *amei ha'aretz* are assumed to observe all laws of ritual purity on Festivals. The Gemara answers: It is referring **to something that would not last until the** next **pilgrimage Festival,** but which would spoil beforehand. The dilemma presented to Rav Sheshet therefore remains unresolved.

The *baraisa* rules that a *kohen chavér* in this situation burns the wet grain — since it is *terumah temei'ah* and the only permissible disposition of *terumah temei'ah* is burning — and retains the dry grain, which is *tahor*. But this prompts the central question: *amai yanichenu legas ha-ba?* — why does he have to burn it? Why not simply hold onto the wet grain until the next pressing season, at which point the general assumption of *tahara* would apply, and the need to burn it would be eliminated?

The Gemara offers two limiting answers. First: *bedavar she-ein lo gas* — perhaps this applies to something that has no pressing season. The pressing-season rule applies specifically to olive oil and grapes, which have a concentrated, well-defined period of communal processing. Grain, though it has its own seasons, does not carry the same narrowly defined pressing period with the same presumption of universal care, and arguably the leniency never applied to it in the first place. Second: *ve-yanichenu laregel* — perhaps he could hold it until the *regel* (pilgrimage festival), when everyone is also believed about *terumah*. The Gemara answers: *bedavar she-eino mishtamer laregel* — it is something that will spoil before the festival. Both answers limit the scope of the question rather than resolving it categorically: in principle, if one could wait for the next *giton* or the next *regel* and the produce would keep, it appears that waiting might indeed be a valid option.

A further implication emerges: once produce receives a stamp of *tahara* at the time of the pressing or the *regel*, it does not revert to a presumption of *tumah* once those special periods pass. The status, once established, remains. This points to the formalist underpinning of the entire system — these rules are not fine-grained empirical assessments but legal categories that operate at designated moments.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר הִפְרַשְׁתִּי לְתוֹכָהּ רְבִיעִית קֹדֶשׁ — נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** And if** the giver **says** to the priest: **I separated** and placed **into** this barrel of *teruma* **a quarter**-*log* **of sacrificial** wine or oil, **he is deemed credible.**

The Mishnah then records another case: if an *am ha'aretz* brings a barrel of wine outside of pressing season and says *hifrashti le-tokhah revi'is kodesh* — I have separated a quarter-log of wine from this barrel for *nesachim*, Temple libations — he is believed about the entire barrel. Since *amei ha'aretz* are always believed about *kodesh*, and this small portion has been designated for *kodesh* purposes, that believability extends to the whole barrel. The Gemara will return to this point later.

## Field Checking Rules

### Pesach vs. Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: מוֹדִין בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל שֶׁבּוֹדְקִין לְעוֹשֵׂי פֶסַח, וְאֵין בּוֹדְקִין לְאוֹכְלֵי תְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Oholot* 18:4): **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that one may examine** the ground **for those performing the paschal** offering, **but one may not examine** the ground **for those who eat *teruma*.**

The Gemara now introduces a Mishnah from elsewhere: *modim Beis Shammai u'Beis Hillel she'bodkin l'osei Pesach v'ein bodkin l'ochlei terumah* — both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree that one checks a field for those who are bringing the *korban Pesach*, but one does not check a field for those who eat *terumah*. The context is a *beis ha-peras* — a field in which a grave was plowed over, scattering small bone fragments, which creates a rabbinic prohibition on entering because of the risk of *tumah*. If one must cross such a field to reach Jerusalem and bring the *korban Pesach*, a method of checking is available. If the concern is merely about eating *terumah*, it is not.

### Blowing Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בּוֹדְקִין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְנַפֵּחַ אָדָם בֵּית הַפְּרָס וְהוֹלֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of: **One may examine? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: A person may blow** at the ground of **a *beit haperas* and walk** through it as he does so. A *beit haperas* is a patch of ground with a grave in it that was subsequently plowed over. The Sages were concerned that there might be small pieces of human bone scattered in the field, which would impart impurity to anyone moving them with his foot. Therefore, they decreed that whoever traverses such a field becomes impure. However, the Sages allowed one to pass through the field while maintaining his purity if he blows on the ground as he goes, the assumption being that any small pieces of bone would thereby be blown out of his path. This is the examination to which this mishna refers. The mishna teaches that this examination is sufficient to allow one to retain his purity as he goes to perform the paschal offering, but not to allow one to retain his purity with regard to the eating of *teruma*.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel explains the method of checking: *menapei'ach adam beis ha-peras ve-holeich* — one blows ahead of oneself and then takes a step, blowing any surface bone chips away before walking forward. This is permitted for those bringing the *korban Pesach*, where the urgency is acute — if one fails to bring the Pesach offering, one is liable *karet*. For *terumah*, there is no comparable urgency, and so the leniency is not extended.

### Pre-Tread Paths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא אָמַר: בֵּית הַפְּרָס שֶׁנִּדַּשׁ — טָהוֹר לְעוֹשֵׂי פֶסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said in the name of Ulla: A *beit haperas* that has been trodden** by passersby who have beaten a path through it **is** considered **pure for those who** are on their way to **perform the paschal** offering, as the assumption is that no more bone fragments remain on the surface of the ground. The examination referred to in the mishna, then, is referring to ascertaining whether a particular *beit haperas* has been trodden or not.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Ulla adds a second mechanism: *beis ha-peras she-nidash tahor* — a *beis ha-peras* that has already been trodden is treated as *tahor* for those bringing the *korban Pesach*. If a path through the field shows signs of prior foot traffic — the earth has been pressed down — one may assume that the bone chips have been dislodged from that path. Admittedly, the logic is not fully transparent: the people who previously walked the path may not have been *kohanim* and may not have cared about *tumah*. Nevertheless, the prior treading is treated as sufficient grounds for leniency when the purpose is bringing the *korban Pesach*. Here too the leniency applies only *l'osei Pesach*.

### Karet vs. Misah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הֶעֱמִידוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶן בִּמְקוֹם כָּרֵת. לְאוֹכְלֵי תְרוּמָה הֶעֱמִידוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶן בִּמְקוֹם מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The reason for this leniency is that the impure status of a *beit haperas* is only a rabbinical decree, and the Sages **did not uphold their words** decreeing the field to be impure **in a place** where this affects one's ability to perform a mitzva involving ***karet*;** and failure to bring a paschal offering is punishable by *karet*. However, with regard **to those who** wish to **eat *teruma*** after traversing a *beit haperas*, the Sages **did uphold their words** decreeing the field to be impure **in a place** of a sin involving the punishment of **death** by God's hand. The sin of eating *teruma* in a state of impurity is punishable by death by God's hand, and the Sages were therefore strict in insisting that one not eat it after traversing a *beit haperas*.

The Gemara provides a structural explanation for why the leniency applies specifically to the *korban Pesach*: *lo he'emidu divreihem bi-mikom kares* — the Sages did not insist on their rabbinic restrictions when doing so would expose a person to liability for *karet*. One who deliberately fails to bring the *korban Pesach* is liable *karet*, and therefore the Sages relaxed their stringencies to enable the offering to be brought.

By contrast, *he'emidu divreihem bi-mekom misah* — they maintained their restrictions in the case of *terumah*, even though eating *terumah* while *tamei* carries a penalty of *misah bidei shamayim*. The structure of the parallel is somewhat inverted: in the case of Pesach, waiving the restriction allows the person to fulfill an obligation and avoid *karet*; in the case of *terumah*, maintaining the restriction protects the person from accidentally incurring *misah bidei shamayim*. So the same concern — preventing the gravest consequences — drives both rulings, even though one operates by relaxing and the other by upholding the rabbinic standard.

### Pesach to Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בָּדַק לְפִסְחוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָתוֹ? עוּלָּא אָמַר: בָּדַק לְפִסְחוֹ — מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתוֹ. רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר: בָּדַק לְפִסְחוֹ — אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars: If **one examined a** *beit haperas* **for** the purpose of bringing **his paschal** offering, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to *teruma* after he has traversed the field? Is it permissible **that he** can rely on this examination to **eat his *teruma*** as well, since his passage through the field has been established as not having defiled him? **Ulla said:** If he **examined** the ground **for** purposes of bringing **his paschal** offering, **he is permitted** afterward **to eat his *teruma*,** for once he is declared pure with regard to the offerings it would be inconsistent to declare him at the same time impure with regard to *teruma*. But **Rabba bar Ulla said:** If one **examined** the ground **for** purposes of bringing **his paschal** offering, it **is prohibited** for him **to eat his *teruma*.**

This leads to the question the Gemara has been building toward: *bodek l'pischo mahu she-yochal bi-terumaso?* — if a person blew the path through the *beis ha-peras* and brought his *korban Pesach*, may he now eat *terumah* on the basis of the same *tahara* status? It would seem peculiar to say that the same person is *tahor* for purposes of the *korban Pesach* but must treat himself as *tamei* when he wishes to eat *terumah*. Yet this is precisely the question, and its resolution draws directly on the logic of the earlier ruling about the barrel of wine.

### Unified Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: לָא תִּפְלוֹג עֲלֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא, דִּתְנַן כְּווֹתֵיהּ: וְאִם אָמַר הִפְרַשְׁתִּי לְתוֹכָהּ רְבִיעִית קֹדֶשׁ — נֶאֱמָן, אַלְמָא: מִדִּמְהֵימַן אַקֹּדֶשׁ מְהֵימַן נָמֵי אַתְּרוּמָה, הָכָא נָמֵי: מִדִּמְהֵימַן אַפֶּסַח, מְהֵימַן נָמֵי אַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain older man said to** Rabba bar Ulla: **Do not argue with Ulla, as we learned** in the mishna **in accordance with his** opinion, for the mishna states: **And if** the *am ha'aretz* **says** to the priest: **I separated** and placed **into** this barrel of *teruma* **a quarter-***log* of **sacrificial** wine or oil, **he is deemed credible. Apparently, then, once** the *am ha'aretz* **is deemed credible** with regard to the **sacrificial** items in the barrel **he is also deemed credible** with regard to the ***teruma*** in it. **Here too,** the same principle should be applied, so **once he is deemed credible** and is considered pure with regard to the **paschal** offering, **he is also deemed credible with regard to *teruma*.**

Ulla rules: *bodek l'pischo mutar le-echol bi-terumaso* — if you walked the blown path and can bring your *korban Pesach*, you may also eat your *terumah*. Rabbah bar Ulla disagrees: *bodek l'pischo assur le-echol bi-terumaso* — you may not.

A certain elder rebukes Rabbah bar Ulla, citing the very Mishnah under discussion. The Mishnah had ruled that if a man declares he separated a quarter-log of wine from a barrel for *kodesh* purposes, he is believed about the entire barrel. The implication is clear: *mi-d'meheiman a-kodesh meheiman nami a-terumah* — since he is believed about the *kodesh* portion, he is also believed about the *terumah* portion. We do not say the same barrel is *tahor* for *kodesh* and *tamei* for *terumah*. By the same logic — *hacha nami mi-d'meheiman a-Pesach meheiman nami a-terumah* — since he is deemed *tahor* for the purpose of bringing the *korban Pesach*, he is also *tahor* for *terumah*. We do not assign the same person a bifurcated purity status for different purposes. The Gemara leaves this without rebuttal, indicating that Ulla's position is accepted.

## Vessels vs. Contents

### Barrel Belief

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן הַמְדוּמָּעוֹת — נֶאֱמָנִין עֲלֵיהֶם בִּשְׁעַת הַגִּיתּוֹת וְהַבַּדִּים, וְקוֹדֶם לַגִּיתּוֹת שִׁבְעִים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **that are mingled,** *amei ha'aretz* **are trusted with regard to them during the period of the winepress and the olive press, and** also up to **seventy days before the winepress,** for that is when people begin to purify their vessels in preparation for the wine-pressing season.

The Mishnah had stated that *kadei yayin u-khadi shemen ha-meduma'os* — barrels of wine and barrels of oil that are *meduma'os* — are believed about at the time of the pressing and for seventy days before. The category of *meduma'os* ordinarily refers to a mixture of *terumah* and *chulin*, or possibly *tevel*, and its significance here is not immediately clear.

### Meduma'os Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אֵין נֶאֱמָנִים לֹא עַל הַקַּנְקַנִּים וְלֹא עַל הַתְּרוּמָה. קַנְקַנִּים דְּמַאי? אִי קַנְקַנִּים דְּקֹדֶשׁ — מִיגּוֹ דִּמְהֵימַן אַקֹּדֶשׁ, מְהֵימַן נָמֵי אַקַּנְקַנִּים! אֶלָּא קַנְקַנִּים דִּתְרוּמָה — פְּשִׁיטָא, הַשְׁתָּא אַתְּרוּמָה לָא מְהֵימַן, אַקַּנְקַנִּים מְהֵימַן?
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: *Amei ha'aretz* **are not deemed credible, neither with regard to flasks nor with regard to *teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **Flasks of what? If** it is referring to **flasks of sacrificial** food, **since** an *am ha'aretz* **is deemed credible** concerning the **sacrificial** items it contains, he must necessarily be **deemed credible** concerning the **flasks as well. Rather,** it is referring to **flasks of *teruma*.** But this **is obvious: Now,** if he **is not deemed credible** concerning the ***teruma*** itself, is it possible that he would be **deemed credible** concerning the **flasks** containing it?

The *tana* glosses the Mishnah: they are believed neither about the barrels themselves nor about the *terumah* inside. The Gemara presses: what exactly is the case? If we are talking about jugs designated for *kodesh* wine or oil — for Temple use — then by the principle already established, *migo d'meheiman a-kodesh meheiman nami a-kankanim*: since one is believed about the *kodesh* contents, one is believed about the jugs as well. The two cannot be disaggregated in that direction.

### Empty vs. Filled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּרֵיקָנִים דְּקֹדֶשׁ וּבִשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, וּבִמְלֵאִין דִּתְרוּמָה וּבִשְׁעַת הַגִּיתּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring **to empty** flasks that had contained **sacrificial** food **during the rest of the days of the year,** i.e., during all the days of the year, for an *am ha'aretz* is deemed credible with regard to sacrificial food throughout the year, yet he is not deemed credible with regard to its flask once the food has been removed, **and** it is referring as well **to** flasks **full of *teruma*** wine **during the period of the winepress,** when they are deemed credible with regard to the *teruma* itself, but not the vessels.

The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing two scenarios. The case where one is *not* believed about the vessels is that of empty jugs normally used for *kodesh* wine or oil, outside of the pressing season. Even though one is believed about *kodesh* contents when present, if the jug is empty and a *chavér* wishes to borrow it for his own use, he must immerse it — the *chavér* cannot treat the empty vessel as *tahor* just because he would have been believed about the *kodesh* contents that were formerly in it. Believability about the contents does not automatically confer believability about the vessel in isolation.

Conversely, there is a case — *u-vimalaim de-trumah u-visha'as ha-gisos* — where one is believed about *terumah* wine but not about the vessel containing it. During the pressing season, the *chavér* can accept the *am ha'aretz*'s *terumah* wine as *tahor*, yet cannot treat the barrel itself as *tahor*. The practical consequence is: pour the wine into my vessel and take your barrel back.

### Wine/Vessel Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּמַשְׁקִין עַצְמָן, שֶׁהֵן טְמֵאִין. וְאַל תִּתְמַהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי לָגִין מָלֵא מַשְׁקִין — לָגִין טְמֵאִין טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, וּמַשְׁקִין טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: And Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes with regard to the liquids themselves** in the vessel **that they are impure,** for although the Sages declared the vessels to be pure they did not waive the decree that liquids touched by *amei ha'aretz* are impure. **And do not be perplexed** by this apparent contradiction, **for** there is a similar *halakha* in a case of an earthenware **pitcher full of liquid** in a room with a corpse and the pitcher is tightly sealed with another earthenware vessel of an *am ha'aretz*, where the *halakha* is that the **pitcher is impure** with a **seven**-day **impurity, while the liquids** remain **pure.**

This is admittedly paradoxical. How can the wine be *tahor* while the vessel containing it is *tamei*? The Gemara initially assumed that believability must be holistic — if you believe one, you believe the other. Here it is saying: not necessarily. We believe the *am ha'aretz* about the wine for *terumah* purposes but do not believe him about the vessel. This is an explicit legal fiction the system is prepared to maintain.

Rashi makes the policy rationale explicit. He writes: *she-lo gazru al trumasun bi-sha'as ha-gisos mishum hefsed Kohanim chaveirim de-nimtza mafsid es rov trumas Eretz Yisrael* — the Sages did not decree against accepting *terumah* at the pressing season out of concern for the loss sustained by *kohanim* who are *chavérim*, since doing so would forfeit the majority of the *terumah* of the Land of Israel. If one could not believe the *am ha'aretz* about *terumah* during the pressing season, the *kohanim* would effectively lose access to most of the year's *terumah*. That is the operative consideration — not merely that people happen to be more careful at this time of year, though that factual basis exists as well. As for the vessels: *al ha-kankanim lo heimanum*, since a *kohen chavér* does not accept vessels together with the *terumah* — he simply has the *am ha'aretz* pour the wine into his own vessel. The believability is calibrated to what is actually needed.

Rashi's comment makes visible what underlies the entire architecture of these rules: from the vantage point of strict *halacha*, an *am ha'aretz* would ordinarily be believed about everything, since all of these skepticisms are rabbinic stringencies layered upon stringencies. The Sages who erected those stringencies are equally empowered to relax them when weighty policy considerations — the unity of Israel, the economic interests of *kohanim chavérim*, the practical necessities of daily life — so require.

## Pottery Vessels

### Modi'in Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַמּוֹדִיעִים וְלִפְנִים נֶאֱמָנִין עַל כְּלֵי חֶרֶס. מִן הַמּוֹדִיעִים וְלַחוּץ — אֵין נֶאֱמָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **From Modi'im and inward** toward Jerusalem, i.e., in the area surrounding Jerusalem, up to the distance of the town of Modi'im, which is fifteen *mil* from Jerusalem, all potters, including *amei ha'aretz*, **are deemed credible with regard to** the purity of **earthenware vessels** that they have produced. Because these places supplied earthenware vessels for the people in Jerusalem, the Sages did not decree impurity for them. **From Modi'im and outward,** however, **they are not deemed credible.** The details of this ruling are specified: **How so? A potter who sells pots,** if he **entered within Modi'im** from outside it, although **the potter, and the pots, and the customers** were all previously located outside Modi'im, where he is not deemed credible with regard to purity, **he is** now **deemed credible.** And the opposite is true of the opposite case: If the same person who was deemed credible inside **left** the boundaries of Modi'im, **he is no** longer **deemed credible.**

The Mishnah next turns to pottery vessels. It states: *min ha-Modiin u-lefanim ne'emanim al klei cheres* — from Modi'in inward, *amei ha'aretz* are believed that pottery vessels are *tahor* at all times. Modi'in is located approximately fifteen *mil* — about fifteen kilometers — from Jerusalem, placing it within the immediate environs of the city.

### Kiln Location

The Gemara explains the background: kilns for pottery production were not maintained within Jerusalem itself, because they would have created smoke and pollution within the city. Kilns were therefore situated outside Jerusalem, in the surrounding areas. This meant that pottery production was concentrated precisely in the zone inhabited more heavily by *amei ha'aretz*, since the *chavérim* tended to cluster closer to the *Beis Hamikdash* and its environment of heightened *tahara*. If the testimony of the *amei ha'aretz* who produced the pottery were not credited, it would become impossible to obtain *tahor* pottery vessels at all.

### Community Needs

Rashi articulates the policy point directly: *she-lo ligzor aleihem she-ein gozrin gezeirah al ha-tzibbur she-ain rov ha-tzibbur yekholim la'amod bah* — the Sages did not apply their decree to the pottery makers because one may not impose a decree on the public that the majority of the public cannot live with. People need pottery vessels. If every vessel had to be presumed *tamei* because its maker was an *am ha'aretz*, the community could not function. The practical necessity overrides the standard presumption of untrustworthiness.

### Modi'in Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: מוֹדִיעִים — פְּעָמִים כְּלִפְנִים, פְּעָמִים כְּלַחוּץ. כֵּיצַד? קַדָּר יוֹצֵא וְחָבֵר נִכְנָס — כְּלִפְנִים. שְׁנֵיהֶן נִכְנָסִין
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **Modi'im** itself, **there are times** that it is considered **like inside** the perimeter surrounding Jerusalem, and **there are times** that it is considered **like outside** that perimeter. **How so?** If **a potter is leaving** the perimeter **and a *ḥaver* is entering** it, and they meet in Modi'im, it is considered **like inside,** and the *ḥaver* may purchase the jugs. However, if **both are entering** the perimeter

The Mishnah then distinguishes: *min ha-Modiin ve-lachutz, ein ne'emanim* — from Modi'in outward, they are not believed. The formal boundary is Modi'in itself, and the Mishnah illustrates the rule with a case of pure formalism: the same jug merchant, with the same jugs, selling to the same buyers — if encountered inside of Modi'in toward Jerusalem, *ne'eman*, he is believed; if encountered outside of Modi'in, *eino ne'eman*, he is not. The Mishnah here makes explicit the legal formalism at work: we cannot assess each transaction individually, and so the boundary is fixed.

### Direction Test

The *tanna* adds: *Modi'in pe'amim ke-lifnim u-fe'amim ke-lachutz* — Modi'in itself sometimes has the status of the inside and sometimes the outside. The Gemara explains: if the jug merchant is heading away from Jerusalem and the *chavér* is heading toward Jerusalem, this is their last opportunity to transact before parting in opposite directions, and so Modi'in is treated as *lifnim* — within the credible zone — and the purchase is permitted. If both are heading toward Jerusalem, they need not transact in Modi'in but can wait until they are fully inside the *lifnim* zone. And if both are heading away from Jerusalem — or both heading in the same direction — Modi'in is treated as *lachutz*, the outer zone, and the *am ha'aretz* is not believed.

### Thin Vessels Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: נֶאֱמָנִין בִּכְלֵי חֶרֶס הַדַּקִּים לַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** in the *Tosefta* (3:33): All people, including *amei ha'aretz*, **are deemed credible** with regard to purity from Modi'im and inward only **with regard to small earthenware vessels,** and they may be used **for sacrificial** food. Since these small vessels were needed by all, the Sages deemed the *amei ha'aretz* credible concerning them.

A *tanna* qualifies the scope of this leniency: *ne'emanim al klei cheres ha-dakim le-kodesh* — they are believed only about the thin, small pottery vessels. These are the vessels most commonly needed for daily use, and it is for their sake that the communal leniency was enacted. The larger, heavier vessels do not enjoy the same presumption.

### Size/Full Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: וְהוּא שֶׁנִּיטָּלִין בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁאֵין נִיטָּלִין בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said: It is** speaking of those vessels **that can be picked up in one hand,** but no larger. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even if they cannot be picked up in one hand,** they can still be called small vessels.

Reish Lakish adds a further restriction: the vessels must be small enough to be carried in one hand. Rabbi Yochanan is more lenient: even if they require two hands to carry, they are within the scope of the leniency, perhaps because they are thin even if somewhat large.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא רֵיקָנִין, אֲבָל מְלֵאִין — לֹא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מְלֵאִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ אַפִּיקָרְסוּתוֹ לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said** further: **They taught** in the *baraita* **only** that *amei ha'aretz* are deemed credible with regard to **empty** vessels, **but** if they are **full** of liquid they are **not** deemed credible. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** if the jugs are **full, and even** if **his garment [*apikarsuto*] is inside** the vessel, the Sages were not concerned about impurity, as they did not apply their decree to such vessels at all.

Reish Lakish also rules: *lo shanu ela reikanin aval meleim lo* — the leniency applies only to empty vessels, not to full ones. The reasoning is this: if the vessel is filled with *chulin* wine, or with *terumah* wine outside of the pressing season, the *am ha'aretz* is not believed about the contents — they must be presumed *tamei*. Can one then still treat the vessel itself as *tahor*? Reish Lakish says no: if one must presume the contents are *tamei*, one must presume the vessel is *tamei* as well. Rabbi Yochanan permits it even when full.

### Pure Vessel/Impure Content

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּמַשְׁקִין עַצְמָן, שֶׁהֵן טְמֵאִין. וְאַל תִּתְמַהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי לָגִין מָלֵא מַשְׁקִין — לָגִין טְמֵאִין טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, וּמַשְׁקִין טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: And Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes with regard to the liquids themselves** in the vessel **that they are impure,** for although the Sages declared the vessels to be pure they did not waive the decree that liquids touched by *amei ha'aretz* are impure. **And do not be perplexed** by this apparent contradiction, **for** there is a similar *halakha* in a case of an earthenware **pitcher full of liquid** in a room with a corpse and the pitcher is tightly sealed with another earthenware vessel of an *am ha'aretz*, where the *halakha* is that the **pitcher is impure** with a **seven**-day **impurity, while the liquids** remain **pure.**

Rava clarifies Rabbi Yochanan's position: *u-modeh Rabbi Yochanan be-mashkin atzman she-hein temei'im* — Rabbi Yochanan concedes that the liquid contents are indeed *tamei*; he is not saying that the vessel's status elevates the liquid. He is saying the reverse: we can simultaneously hold that the liquid is *tamei* and the vessel is *tahor*, which is the mirror image of the case discussed earlier where the *terumah* wine was *tahor* but the vessel was *tamei*. The Gemara anticipates the objection — how can two physically interacting objects have contradictory purity statuses? — and responds: *ve-al titmah*, do not be astounded, and cites the case of *lugin temei'im tumat shiva u-mashkin tehorin*. Pitchers can be *tamei* with a seven-day *tumah* due to contact with a *meis*, while the liquids inside them remain *tahor* — a case already established in earlier discussions involving Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel. Here too, the contradictory statuses of vessel and contents are legally coherent, even if counterintuitive.

The larger pattern running through this entire section is now legible: the architecture of *tahara* law accommodates deliberate legal fictions, sustained contradictions between the status of objects in physical contact, and policy-driven modifications of baseline rules — all in service of making it possible for the community to function and for *kohanim chavérim* to receive their *terumah*.

## Collectors & Thieves

### Tax Collectors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגַּבָּאִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, וְכֵן הַגַּנָּבִים שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּ אֶת הַכֵּלִים — נֶאֱמָנִין לוֹמַר: לֹא נָגַעְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of *amei ha'aretz* tax **collectors who entered a house** to collect items for a tax, **and similarly thieves who returned the vessels** they had stolen, **they are deemed credible when they say: We did not touch** the rest of the objects in the house, and those items remain pure.

The Mishnah states: *ha-gabba'im she-nichnesu le-toch ha-bayis* — tax collectors who entered a house, as well as thieves who returned stolen vessels — are believed to say *lo naganu*, that they did not touch certain items. The tax collectors referenced here are the *am ha'aretz* of the rabbinic imagination — the same type of people with whom, as the New Testament records, one does not ordinarily eat. And yet they are credited when they specify what they did and did not touch, so that the *chavér* need not treat the items they disclaim touching as *tamei*.

## Yom Tov Belief

### Festival Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁעַת הָרֶגֶל אַף עַל הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And during a pilgrimage Festival** they are deemed credible **even with regard to *teruma*.**

The Mishnah then arrives at its culminating principle: *u-bi-sha'as ha-regel af al ha-terumah* — at the time of the pilgrimage festival, everyone is believed even about *terumah*. This is the broadest and most consequential of all the leniencies catalogued in the chapter, and its basis, as the Gemara will make clear, is not primarily empirical — it is not simply that people are more careful about *tahara* on the *regel* — but is rooted in *Chazal*'s deliberate effort to create unity among the entire people of Israel when they gather together in Jerusalem. The festival leniency, more than any other, is an expression of communal policy rather than a judgment about individual trustworthiness.

## Collectors & Thieves Revisited

### Supervisor Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הַגַּבָּאִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת — הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ טָמֵא. לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִיכָּא גּוֹי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, הָא דְּלֵיכָּא גּוֹי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a different mishna (*Teharot* 7:6): If *amei ha'aretz* tax **collectors entered a house, the entire house is impure.** The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult,** as **that** mishna is referring to a situation **where there is a gentile with them,** in which case they conduct a thorough search in the whole house, and certainly will have touched everything; whereas **this** mishna deals with a case **when there is no gentile with them,** and their claim not to have touched anything is therefore accepted.

The Gemara raises a contradiction against the Mishnah's ruling about tax collectors. A *baraisa* states: *gabba'im she-nichnesu le-toch ha-bayis kulo tamei* — when tax collectors entered the house, everything is presumed *tamei*. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *ha de-ika nochri bahadeihu ha de-leika nochri bahadeihu* — the cases differ depending on whether a non-Jew is supervising them. The Mishnah, which credits their testimony, refers to a case without a non-Jewish supervisor; the *baraisa*, which presumes everything *tamei*, refers to a case where a non-Jew is present and overseeing the collection. The *tanna* elaborates: if there is a non-Jew with them, the collectors may say they did not enter the house at all, and that is believed; but they cannot say they entered yet touched nothing. Rashi explains: the reason they cannot disclaim touching when a non-Jew was present is that one must assume they inspected everything thoroughly — not because the non-Jew compelled them to lie, but because they would not have risked failing to conduct a complete inspection before their supervisor.

### Fear Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אִיכָּא גּוֹי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ מַאי הָוֵי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: אֵימַת גּוֹי עֲלֵיהֶן. וְחַד אָמַר: אֵימַת מַלְכוּת עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: **And when there is a gentile with them, what of it?** Why does this affect the *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disputed this issue. **One said: The fear of the gentile,** who is their senior, **is upon them,** for they are afraid he might punish them. **And one said: The fear of the kingdom,** i.e., the government, **is upon them,** as the gentile might report them to the authorities if they do not carry out a thorough search.

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar dispute the precise mechanism. One holds *eimas nochri aleihem* — they fear the non-Jew himself; the other holds *eimas malchus aleihem* — they fear the governmental authority he represents and the prospect that he will report their negligence to the government. The practical difference between the two positions concerns a non-Jew who is not particularly important or well-connected: according to the view that the fear is personal, a non-entity of a non-Jew would not inhibit the collectors from being selective in their search. According to the view that the fear is institutional — fear of governmental authority — even a low-status non-Jew represents a reporting channel to the government, and the collectors would still feel compelled to inspect thoroughly.

### Thieves' Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הַגַּנָּבִים שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּ אֶת הַכֵּלִים. וּרְמִינְהִי: הַגַּנָּבִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת — אֵינוֹ טָמֵא אֶלָּא מְקוֹם דְּרִיסַת רַגְלֵי הַגַּנָּבִים. אָמַר רַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כְּשֶׁעָשׂוּ תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna: **And similarly thieves who returned vessels** are deemed credible. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following mishna (*Teharot* 7:6): Concerning **the thieves who entered a house, only the place where the feet of the thieves had trodden is impure.** The implication is that all the vessels of the section of the house where they had entered are impure, and they are not deemed credible if they say that they did not touch a particular item. **Rav Pinḥas said in the name of Rav:** The mishna here is referring to a case **where** the thieves **repented,** which is why they are deemed credible, whereas the mishna in *Teharot* is referring to a case in which the thieves did not repent.

The Gemara turns to the second half of the Mishnah: *ve-chen ha-ganavim she-hechziru es ha-keilim* — thieves who returned the vessels are believed. A *baraisa* is cited in apparent tension: *ha-ganavim she-nichnesu le-toch ha-bayis eino tamei ela mekom drisat raglei ha-ganavim* — when thieves entered a house, only the places where they walked are *tamei*. This suggests that the areas they did not walk are unaffected, but Rashi reads the tension as follows: if one must presume *tamei* wherever they walked, perhaps one should also presume *tamei* whatever vessels they handled, and their testimony that they did not touch the contents of a vessel should not be credited.

Rav Pinchas in the name of Rav — or in the name of Rava — provides the resolution: *keshe-asu teshuvah* — when they have done *teshuvah*, returning the vessels is itself the act of repentance, and one who is in the process of sincere *teshuvah* is believed. When they have not done *teshuvah*, the presumption of *tumah* stands. The Gemara notes that this is supported by the precise language of the Mishnah itself: *she-hechziru es ha-keilim* — the Mishnah specifies that they returned the vessels, which is the act that signals *teshuvah*. *Shema mina.*

A further question remains implicit: why are these people — tax collectors and thieves, quintessential *amei ha'aretz* — believed about *tahara* at all, given that *amei ha'aretz* are generally distrusted in these matters? Rashi limits the believability to *kodesh*, which falls within the general rule that *amei ha'aretz* are always believed about *kodesh*. Tosafot extends it even to *terumah*. One might add that there is a meaningful distinction between believing someone about how they handled someone else's property — where they have no personal stake in misrepresenting their care — and believing them about how they handled their own produce, where the very question of whether they are a *chavér* or an *am ha'aretz* colors every claim they make.
