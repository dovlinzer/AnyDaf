# Ketubot 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Base Ketubah Amount

### Mishnah Overview

The *mishnah* we now reach has, as has been noted many times, finally arrived at what one might have expected to be among the very first *mishnayos* of the tractate: the base *ketubah* amount. The *mishnah* establishes 200 *zuz* for a *besulah* and 100 for an *almanah*. If the husband wrote less than the standard amount — or failed to write it at all — it makes no difference, because these obligations exist as a *tenai beis din*: they are imposed by rabbinic mandate regardless of what appears in the *ketubah*. Similarly, the lien encumbering all of the husband's property for payment of the *ketubah* is not subject to limitation. If the husband attempted to restrict the *shibudim* to a single field, that restriction is without effect. And further, the obligation to redeem a wife taken captive is likewise a *tenai beis din*. The first portion of the discussion concerns whether the husband can validly write a *ketubah* below the standard amount and whether the *shibudim* can be contractually limited.

### Rabbi Meir's Ruling

The *gemara* opens with a simple question: *mani* — whose view does this *mishnah* follow? The answer given is that it must be Rabbi Meir, *de'amar*: *kol hapoches libesulah mimasa'im ule'almanah mimana harei zu be'ilas zenus*. Anyone who writes a *ketubah* for less than the standard amounts — less than 200 for a *besulah* or less than 100 for an *almanah* — has rendered the union effectively one of *be'ilas zenus*: intercourse conducted without the protections of marriage.

The underlying idea is significant. One of the primary functions of the *ketubah* is to ensure that a husband cannot simply divorce his wife without cost. Knowing there will be a financial consequence causes him to pause before acting callously. If the woman lacks that protection, if she knows he can divorce her at will with little financial deterrence, then from her perspective the relationship feels like casual intimacy rather than a committed marital bond. Rabbi Meir's position is that any *ketubah* below the standard amount fails this essential protective function entirely.

### Two Interpretations

This raises a question: what exactly does Rabbi Meir mean when he calls such a union *be'ilas zenus*? Does he mean that writing a lesser amount is simply censured — a violation that renders the ongoing marital relationship objectionable — while the *ketubah* remains legally valid at the written amount? Or does he mean that the written amount is voided, and the *ketubah* automatically reverts to the standard 200 or 100?

The *gemara* assumes these two consequences go together. Since Rabbi Meir utterly opposes any *ketubah* below the standard amounts, the natural implication is that if one were written for less, we void it and replace it with the standard amount. This is what the *mishnah* reflects: a *ketubah* below the threshold cannot stand, and the standard amount applies regardless.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Approach

The *gemara* contrasts this with the position of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda taught: *rotzeh kosev libesulah shtar shel masayim vechosev hiskabalti mimech mane* — a man who wishes to effectively reduce his wife's *ketubah* may write the full 200, then have her write a receipt stating she has already received 100. Similarly, for an *almanah*, he writes 100 and she writes a receipt for 50. Two things emerge from Rabbi Yehuda's formulation. First, even he agrees that one must begin with the base amount — you cannot simply write a lesser figure from the outset. Second, however, Rabbi Yehuda has no objection in principle to the effective amount being below the threshold, provided the procedure of writing the receipt is followed. This means Rabbi Yehuda does not regard a *ketubah* that is effectively less than 200 or 100 as *be'ilas zenus*. Accordingly, a *mishnah* that prohibits writing a lesser amount from the start cannot reflect Rabbi Yehuda's view.

### Gemara Attribution

From this analysis, the *gemara* concludes that the *reisha* of our *mishnah* — the rule that the *ketubah* must be at least 200 or 100 — follows Rabbi Meir.

## Liens & Achrayus Debate

### Seifa Overview

The *gemara* now turns to the *seifa* of the *mishnah*, which presents its own difficulty. If the husband wrote that his *ketubah* obligation is secured only by a field worth 100 *zuz* — and did not write the standard language (*kol nichasin d'is li achrayin l'chtubaseich*, all my property is encumbered for your *ketubah*) — he is nevertheless *chayav*, the full *shibudim* apply, because this is a *tenai beis din*.

### Achrayus Ta'us Sofer

The *gemara* notes that this *seifa* sounds like it follows Rabbi Yehuda. Why? Because Rabbi Yehuda holds *achrayus ta'os sofer* — the absence of a written lien in a document is treated as a scribal error. This means that any written loan automatically carries *shibudim* whether or not they are written explicitly. One might think that principle applies only when the scribe simply forgot to include the lien language. But in our case the husband *explicitly* restricted the lien to a single field — there is no scribal error here at all. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda's underlying position is that a written document inherently generates liens, and therefore even an explicit restriction cannot override that. The *seifa*, which says the liens apply to all property regardless of what was written, thus follows Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rabbi Meir's View

Rabbi Meir disagrees. He holds *achrayus lav ta'os sofer* — the absence of a written lien is not a scribal error, and a document without explicitly written liens carries no liens at all. This applies even to a *ketubah*.

### Found Loan Documents

A *baraita* is cited as evidence for Rabbi Meir's position. If one finds a loan document in the street — a document recording that Reuven borrowed money from Shimon — the question arises whether to return it to Shimon. Even if Reuven acknowledges the debt and says it belongs to Shimon, may we return it?

The *mishnah* rules: *im yesh bahem achrayus nechasim lo yachzir* — if the document contains liens, do not return it, *she'beis din yifre'u meihen* — because *beis din* would then exact those liens. The concern is that Reuven has already repaid the debt, and the two parties are colluding: Shimon will present the document, pretend the debt is still owed, Reuven will claim to have no money, and Shimon will then seize encumbered property from third-party purchasers of Reuven's fields. The document, found lying in the street, lacks the evidentiary weight of a properly held document — we are suspicious about its origins, and therefore we do not return it even when Reuven says it belongs to Shimon.

*Im ein bahem achrayus nechasim, yachzir* — but if the document contains no liens, return it, because *beis din* cannot seize third-party property on the basis of such a document, and there is no mechanism for the alleged scheme to cause harm to others. This is *divrei Rabbi Meir*. Rabbi Meir's position is explicit: a document without written liens carries no liens. *Vachachamim omrim, echad zeh ve'echad zeh lo yachzir* — the Sages say do not return it in either case, because the Sages hold that any written document automatically carries liens.

The *baraita* is significant because it explicitly extends this principle to a *ketubah* as well: *kesuvas isha she'ein bo achrayus* — a *ketubah* lacking written *achrayus* is listed among documents that carry no liens. So Rabbi Meir holds that a *ketubah* without explicitly written liens has no *shibudim*, just as an ordinary loan document does not.

This creates an interesting observation. One might have expected Rabbi Meir — who is so insistent on the full standard *ketubah* amount — to be equally insistent on full *shibudim*. Yet Rabbi Meir draws a distinction, seemingly because the rights of third-party purchasers must also be protected: when someone purchases property, they are entitled to know precisely what liens encumber it. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, appears to hold the reverse: a *ketubah* can be effectively less than 200 or 100, but the *shibudim* are always full.

## Mishnah Attribution

### Rabbi Meir Distinction

The *gemara* now confronts the apparent contradiction directly: the *reisha* of our *mishnah* sounds like Rabbi Meir, the *seifa* sounds like Rabbi Yehuda. Can the *mishnah* be attributed to a single authority?

One possible resolution: perhaps the entire *mishnah* follows Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Meir simply draws a distinction between a *ketubah* and other loan documents. For ordinary documents, he holds that unwritten liens mean no liens. But for a *ketubah*, perhaps Rabbi Meir agrees that *tenai beis din* overrides, and therefore even without written liens — or even with explicitly restricted liens — the full *shibudim* apply by rabbinic mandate. However, the *gemara* is not satisfied with this resolution, as we shall see, because Rabbi Meir is not shown to make this distinction elsewhere.

---

## Five Non-Lien Items

### Mechurarin Concept

At this point the *gemara* introduces a broader *baraita* that bears on the question. The *baraita* enumerates five things that can only be collected from *mechurarin* — free, unencumbered property, meaning property still in the direct possession of the debtor — and *not* from *meshuabad* property, meaning property already sold to a third party. The word *mechurar* carries the connotation of freedom (*ben chorin*), as opposed to *shibudim*, which carries connotations of bondage — an apt contrast as *Pesach* approaches.

The significance of this list is that it identifies specific obligations which, by their nature, cannot carry liens on alienated property. These are obligations that are either indefinite in amount, ongoing, or otherwise structurally incompatible with the certainty required for liens to bind third-party purchasers.

### Peiros & Shevach

The first two items are *peiros* and *shevach peiros* — fruit and the benefit of improvements. The scenario involves a *ganav* who steals a field and sells it to a buyer. The true owner eventually reclaims the field. The buyer, who purchased in good faith, then seeks reimbursement from the *ganav*. Since the *ganav* has no ready funds, the buyer looks to enforce liens against property the *ganav* had sold to other parties.

Two components of the buyer's claim are at issue. The basic purchase price — a fixed, known amount — can indeed be collected from encumbered property, because when Reuven bought the *ganav*'s field, he knew there was a fixed, quantifiable lien that might be exercised against it. But the *peiros* and *shevach peiros* — the ongoing investment the buyer made in the stolen field over the years, plowing, fertilizing, planting — cannot be collected from encumbered third-party property. The reason is that this is an open-ended, indefinite amount: it could represent twenty years of investment, continuously accruing. It would be fundamentally unfair to the third-party purchaser to hold their property subject to such an uncertain, indefinite claim. Therefore, the buyer can only recover his invested improvements from property currently in the *ganav*'s direct possession.

### Mezonot Stepchildren

The third item is the obligation to support stepchildren. If a man undertook as a *ketubah* condition to provide *mezonos* for his wife's children from a prior marriage, that is an ongoing, indefinite obligation. Since the amount is not fixed — it accumulates continuously — it similarly cannot be enforced against property sold to third parties. One may recall from the preceding *daf* the question of daughters being supported from estate property: the same logic applies. Ongoing support obligations are not of the kind that can generate liens on alienated property.

### Documents Without Liens

The fourth and fifth items are: *get chov she'ein bo achrayus* — a loan document without written liens — and *kesuvas isha she'ein bo achrayus* — a *ketubah* without written liens. Neither carries enforceable *shibudim* on property sold to third parties.

### Conclusion on Five Items

The *baraita* thus provides explicit support for Rabbi Meir's position: a *ketubah* lacking written *achrayus* is treated like any other document without written liens, and no *shibudim* are implied. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *man tana d'amar achrayus lav ta'os sofer* — who is the *tanna* who holds that absence of written liens is not a scribal error? Rabbi Meir. And this *baraita* explicitly extends that rule to a *ketubah*.

So we now have clearly before us the full picture. Rabbi Meir: full standard *ketubah* amount required, but *shibudim* only if explicitly written, even in a *ketubah*. Rabbi Yehuda: *ketubah* can be effectively less than the standard (though you must start by writing the full amount), but *shibudim* are always full on any written document.

## Mishnah Attribution (II)

### Contradiction Setup

The difficulty stands: our *mishnah* demands both the full standard amount *and* full *shibudim*. It cannot straightforwardly follow either Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Yehuda, since each authority holds one of these requirements but rejects the other.

### Rabbi Yehuda Resolution

The *gemara* offers its first substantive resolution: *i bayis eima Rabbi Yehuda, hasam kasav lah hiskabalti, hacha lo kasav lah hiskabalti.* If you want to say our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda — consider why Rabbi Yehuda formulated his ruling so oddly. Why did he not simply say: you may write a *ketubah* of 100? Instead he said: you must write 200 and then write a receipt for 100. The obvious implication is that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the base *ketubah* must be written at the full standard amount established by the Rabbis. Only after that base has been formally established may the parties reduce the effective amount through a receipt. Writing a lesser amount from the outset is not acceptable even for Rabbi Yehuda. Our *mishnah*, which prohibits writing below 200 or 100, therefore accords with Rabbi Yehuda perfectly. And the *seifa* — which imposes full *shibudim* regardless of what was written — naturally follows Rabbi Yehuda's principle of *achrayus ta'os sofer*.

### Alternative Rabbi Meir

The *gemara* also offers an alternative reading: *ve'ibayis eima Rabbi Meir, mai chayav de'kasani mina meshu'abadi.* One could say our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Meir even in the *seifa*, if we reread the *mishnah*'s conclusion differently. When the *mishnah* says that if you wrote the lien only on one field you are still *chayav*, perhaps it does not mean that liens extend to all real estate — perhaps it means only that you are personally obligated for the full amount, collectible from whatever unencumbered property is in your possession. That would be consistent with Rabbi Meir's view that only written liens bind alienated property.

### Most Likely View

However, this is not the simple reading of the *mishnah*. The natural sense of the *mishnah*, combined with the language *tenai beis din*, implies that the *shibudim* are full and extend beyond unencumbered property. Therefore, the most satisfying conclusion is that our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda: you must start with the base amount — writing less from the outset is impermissible — and the *shibudim* are always full, consistent with *achrayus ta'os sofer*.

## Ones vs. Ratzon

### Wife of Yisroel

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*'s treatment of a wife taken captive and the husband's obligation to redeem her. Before examining the *ketubah* obligations directly, the *gemara* addresses the underlying *halachic* difference between the wife of a *Yisroel* and the wife of a *Kohen* in the event of captivity and potential rape.

*Amar Avuhu deShmuel* — the father of Shmuel declared: *eishes Yisroel shena'ansa asura leba'ala* — a non-*Kohen*'s wife who was raped is forbidden to her husband. The reason given is *chayshinan shema techilasa be'ones vesofa beratzon* — we are concerned that although she was raped, perhaps at some point she became a willing participant. This is a deeply difficult *gemara*. The suggestion that in the midst of rape a woman might become willing is one that cannot be imagined from a female perspective, and yet it is the position articulated.

### Ones Becoming Ratzon

What the *gemara* will later develop is the broader context of women held captive under ongoing threat. A woman in such a situation might act in seemingly willing ways — providing food, handing over weapons — while in fact doing so entirely out of fear for her life. Such conduct is still classified as *ones*. But the statement of the father of Shmuel here is not about captivity in the abstract; it is about a direct case of rape. The question the *gemara* is raising is whether a woman in the midst of a rape can be considered, at some point during the act, to have become a willing participant. One might suggest it refers to a longer-term psychological process — an identification with captors over time. That reading has some appeal, but as the *gemara* makes explicit, the scenario the father of Shmuel envisions is one in which a woman has ceased actively protesting during the act itself.

### Rav's Challenge

*Eisivei Rav la'Avuhu deShmuel* — Rav challenged the father of Shmuel from the *ketubah* text itself. The standard *ketubah* language reads: *im tishatvai afarkinach ve'osvinach li le'intu* — if you are taken captive, I will redeem you and restore you as my wife. This language clearly contemplates the return of a captive wife to her husband, contradicting the assertion that a *Yisroel*'s wife who was raped or taken captive is forbidden. The father of Shmuel was silent — *ishtik* — unable to respond. Rav then applied the verse *sarim atzru bemilim, vechaf yasimu lefihem* — the princes were restrained in speech and placed their hands to their mouths — noting that the father of Shmuel need not have been silent, because there was an answer available.

### Rape vs. Captivity

The answer is *bishvuya heikilu* — the Rabbis were lenient in the case of captivity. The father of Shmuel's ruling that a wife is forbidden applies specifically to a case of known, witnessed rape. In such a case we know she was raped, and we are therefore concerned that at some point she became a willing participant. But the *ketubah* language addresses captivity, in which case we do not know whether she was raped at all. Since rape is uncertain, she is permitted to return to her husband. The two cases are therefore distinguishable: certain rape raises the concern of eventual *ratzon*; mere captivity does not.

## Rava's Principle

### Rava's Ruling

*U'pliga de'Rava* — this position of the father of Shmuel is in dispute with Rava. Rava states: *kol shetechilata be'ones vesofa beratzon* — whenever something begins with force, even if it ends with willingness, the woman is permitted to her husband. Rava accepts the factual premise that a rape victim might at some point during the act express willingness. He does not dispute this characterization of the events. What he disputes is the legal conclusion drawn from it.

### Overriding Willingness

*Afilu hi omeret hanichu lo* — even if the woman herself says "leave him alone, don't stop him," *she'ilmale nizkak lah hi socharto* — even if she says "had he not forced himself upon me I would have paid him," even such explicit expressions of desire do not render her forbidden. *Mai ta'ama? Yeitzer albasha* — her *yeitzer hara* got the better of her. Since the entire process began with *ones*, even the most explicit subsequent expression of *ratzon* is regarded as a consequence of that original *ones* and cannot be legally classified as genuine *ratzon*. This is Rava's formulation of the principle of *techilata be'ones vesofa beratzon*: the original *ones* defines everything that follows.

It is worth noting the relationship to a parallel principle in the laws of torts: *techilata befoshia vesofa be'ones* — if one was initially negligent with another's property and then an accident befalls it, the initial negligence may define the outcome. Analogously here, the initial *ones* defines all subsequent states, such that no subsequent expression of *ratzon* — however sincere it may be — can override the original act of force. One might ask whether this principle extends to longer-term processes, such as a captive who over time genuinely identifies with her captors. The *gemara* does not address this directly, though it is a compelling question.

### Supporting Baraita

*Tanya k'vatei d'Rava* — a *baraita* is cited in support of Rava's position. The *baraita* interprets the *pasuk* regarding *sotah*: *vehi lo nitpasha asura* — if she was not seized [i.e., not raped] she is forbidden [to her husband if she committed adultery]. *Hanitpasha muteret* — if she was raped she is permitted. *V'yesh lach acheret sh'af al pi shelo nitpasha muteret* — and there is yet another woman who, even though she was not raped, is permitted to her husband. What is this case? *Kol shetchilata be'ones vesofa beratzon* — any case that began with force: even if it ended with willingness, she is permitted. This *baraita* directly supports Rava's ruling.

## Alternative Scenarios

### Techilata Be'ones

A second *baraita* addresses complementary cases. *V'hi lo nitpasha asura, hanitpasha muteret* — the same baseline: not raped is forbidden, raped is permitted. *V'yesh lach acheret sh'af al pi shelo nitpasha muteret* — another woman who is permitted to her husband even though she was not raped. *Kol shetchilata be'ones vesofa beratzon* — again, the case where it began with force, which defines the entire episode as *ones* regardless of what followed.

### Wife of Kohen

*V'yesh lach acheret sh'af al pi shenitpasha asura* — and there is yet another woman who, even though she was raped, is forbidden to her husband. *V'eizo zo eshet kohen* — this is the wife of a *Kohen*. Even a rape victim who is the wife of a *Kohen* is forbidden to her husband, because rape renders her a *zonah* — a category that, in *halachah*, applies not only to a prostitute but to any woman who has had intercourse with a man she is forbidden to marry, regardless of her will — and a *zonah* is forbidden to a *Kohen*.

### Alternative Teaching

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Yishmael* — a third formulation introduces yet another scenario. *V'yesh lach acheret sh'af al pi shelo nitpasha muteret* — a woman who is permitted to her husband even though she committed adultery willingly, not under compulsion. *V'eizo zo she'kidusheiha kidushei taut* — this is a woman whose marriage was based on fundamental error, *kidushei taut*. Even if she has been married for years, even if she has children — *sh'afillu bna murkav al kteifa, memaenet v'holechet la* — she may refuse the marriage and be released, because the entire marriage was founded on a misrepresentation. Since the *kiddushin* was invalid from the outset, there is no real marriage for her to have violated, and she is not forbidden. A contemporary illustration: if a husband concealed at the time of marriage something that would have been a decisive impediment to the match — the *poskim* discuss such cases — the logic of *kidushei taut* in principle should apply, even if practically the requirement for a *get* is maintained.

## Bandits vs. Government

### Rav Yehuda Ruling

*Amar Rav Yehuda: hanni nashei d'ganvi ganvei sharyon l'gavraihu* — women taken by bandits (*listos*) are permitted to return to their husbands, because if they were raped it was not done willingly, and the circumstances are regarded as *ones*.

### Willingness Objection

The Rabbis challenged Rav Yehuda: *v'ha ka mamatyan lehu nahama* — but these women appear to be willing participants; they bring the bandits their bread and provisions. *V'ha ka mashl'chin lehu giri* — they even hand them their arrows in battle. This hardly looks like unwilling captivity.

### Fear Motivation

*Machmat yira* — all of this is done purely out of fear. Every ostensibly willing act performed by a woman in bandit captivity, from providing food to handing over weapons, is motivated entirely by the threat of what will happen to her if she does not comply. That is the definition of *ones* in this context.

### Freedom and Ratzon

*Vadai shavkinhu v'azlu minafshaihu asiran* — the only case in which a woman would be considered to have acted from genuine *ratzon* is if the captors explicitly offered her freedom to leave and she chose to stay. At that point, continuing to remain is a genuine expression of will, no longer coerced by circumstance, and she would be forbidden to her husband.

### Rambam Parallel

This principle is closely paralleled in the *Rambam*'s *Iggeret HaShmad*, where he discusses people who were forced under mortal threat to convert outwardly to Islam. The *Rambam* argues that such a person may be considered an *anus* — acting under compulsion — as long as they have no viable opportunity to leave. But once a genuine opportunity to escape or relocate presents itself and the person does not take it, the *Rambam* says they can no longer claim the status of *anus*. The principle is the same: coercion defines the conduct while escape is impossible, but once freedom becomes a genuine option, remaining is an act of *ratzon*.

What emerges from all of this is a nuanced and, on the whole, relatively broad rabbinic definition of *ones*. Physically compelled rape is obviously *ones*. Acting willingly out of fear for one's life is *ones*. Even explicit verbal expressions of desire, if they arose in the wake of an initial *ones*, are treated by Rava as *ones*. The only scenario that conclusively terminates the status of *ones* is a clear, genuine, uncoerced opportunity to leave that is not taken.

## Gov't vs. Bandit Status

### Braita Contradiction

*Shvuyey malchut harey hen kishvuyin* — women taken captive by the government (*malchut*) are treated as captives whose relationship is considered *ones*, and they may return to their husbands. *Ginuvey listos einam kishvuyin* — but those taken by bandits (*listos*) are not treated as captives in the same sense, and may not return. This directly contradicts Rav Yehuda's ruling above.

A second *baraita* says the opposite. *Vatanya ipcha* — and indeed there is a contradicting *baraita*. So we have two apparent contradictions: one about *malchut* and one about *listos*.

### Achashverosh & Ben Nezar

*Malchut amalchut lo kashya* — the contradiction about government captivity is not a difficulty. *Ha bemalchut Achashverosh, ha bemalchut Ben Nezar.* Rashi explains: in the case of Achashverosh, who is of genuine royal blood, a captured woman knows she will never become his queen — she is simply a captive for his use, never a prospective wife. There is therefore no incentive for her to be a willing participant, and the act is classified as *ones*. In the case of Ben Nezar, however — a figure who was simultaneously a bandit and an emerging warlord carving out his own territory — a captured woman might reason that willing participation could lead to becoming his queen, since he lacks royal lineage and marrying her would be fully within his power. In that case, we fear she may have been a willing participant, and she is forbidden.

### Relative Status

*Listos alistos lo kashya* — similarly, the contradiction about bandits is resolved. *Ha beben Nezar* — one *baraita* that classifies *listos* as a problem refers to Ben Nezar. *Ha belistin de'alma* — the other *baraita*, which permits a woman taken by bandits to return, refers to ordinary bandits who have no prospect of offering her elevation in status. And Ben Nezar himself is assigned to different categories in different *baraisos* depending on the reference point: *hasam kari ley malka hacha kari ley listim* — in one *baraita* he is called a king, in another a bandit. *Ein gabei Achashverosh listim* — compared to Achashverosh, who is genuinely of royal blood, Ben Nezar is a mere bandit. *Hu vegabei listin de'alma malka havey* — but compared to ordinary bandits, Ben Nezar is a king.

### Humorous Aside

It is like the old story of the man who makes his first million, buys himself a yacht, puts on his captain's hat with the brass buttons, and brings his mother for a tour. Compared to actual royalty, he is just a new-money bandit; compared to the people in his old neighborhood, he is a king. The relative status is everything.

## Kohen Wife & Redemption

### Ketubah Language

The *gemara* now returns to the *ketubah* obligations themselves. For the wife of a *Yisroel*, the *ketubah* reads: *im tishatvai afarkinach ve'osvinach li le'intu* — if you are taken captive I will redeem you and restore you as my wife. For a *Kohenet* — a woman married to a *Kohen* — the language differs: *adrina l'medinateich* — I will return you to your city. The promise is of redemption and return, but not of restoration as a wife, because even a *Kohen*'s wife who was raped is forbidden to remain with him.

### Abaye's Position

*Amar Abaye: almana l'Kohen Gadol chayav lifdosah* — if a *Kohen Gadol* married an *almanah*, or a *Kohen* married a *gerushah* — a marriage that is halachically forbidden though the *kiddushin* is technically binding — he is still obligated to redeem her if she is taken captive. The reason: *she'ani korei bah u've'Kohenet adrina l'medinateich* — the *ketubah* language "I will return you to your city" applies to her. The obligation to redeem therefore exists even though he will have to divorce her after the redemption.

### Redemption Logic

This requires explanation. When a forbidden but binding marriage exists — such as a *Kohen Gadol* who married an *almanah*, or a *Yisroel* who married a *mamzeret* — the *kiddushin* takes effect and a *get* is required. Moreover, certain *ketubah* obligations continue to apply. The principle is that obligations which are not intrinsically tied to the ongoing marital relationship remain in force even in a forbidden marriage. The lump sum payment due at divorce, for example, continues to apply because it is a payment made at the *time of* divorce, not as part of ongoing married life. By contrast, obligations that presuppose and sustain the ongoing marriage — food, *refuah*, and similar day-to-day provisions — are withheld, because we wish to discourage the continuation of the forbidden marriage and thereby encourage divorce.

The obligation to redeem falls into a distinct category: for a *Kohenet* or an *almanah l'Kohen Gadol*, redemption is followed necessarily by divorce. It is not an act that sustains the forbidden marriage; if anything, it facilitates its proper dissolution. Therefore, Abaye holds that the redemption obligation applies even here.

### Mamzeres Exception

The next line sharpens the point. *Mamzeret u'netinah l'Israel eino chayav lifdosah* — if a *Yisroel* married a *mamzeret*, he does not have the obligation to redeem her if she is taken captive. Why? *Ki ein ani korei bo v'hashivotecha li le'into* — because for this couple, the *ketubah* language "I will return you to me as my wife" does not apply. According to Rashi, the key distinction is whether the relevant *ketubah* language — either the version for an *Yisroel*'s wife ("I will restore you as my wife") or the version for a *Kohen*'s wife ("I will return you to your city") — is operative in the given case. For a *mamzeret* married to a *Yisroel*, the standard language implying restoration as a wife cannot apply since they should not be living as husband and wife; and since there is no alternative *ketubah* language providing for redemption without restoration, no obligation exists.

---

## Rava's Refinement

### Specific Impediment

*Rava amar: kol she'issur shevuyah goreim lah chayav lifdosah, issur davar acheir goreim lah eino chayav lifdosah.* Rava articulates a more precise principle. When the *ketubah* formula states "I will return you to your city" — indicating an obligation to redeem even without restoring the marriage — that language applies only when the impediment to the ongoing marriage is caused by the captivity itself. That is, if the reason she cannot remain his wife is specifically *because* she was taken captive (*issur shevuyah goreim lah*), then the obligation to redeem applies. But if there is a pre-existing or independent reason they cannot be married (*issur davar acheir goreim lah*) — a reason having nothing to do with the captivity — then the fundamental redemption obligation between husband and wife is simply inapplicable, and no derivative obligation can flow from it.

### Application Cases

For a *Kohen* and a *Kohenet* whose marriage is otherwise valid: if she is taken captive, the only reason she becomes forbidden upon return is the captivity. Therefore the *ketubah* obligates him to redeem her even though she will not remain his wife. For an *almanah l'Kohen Gadol* or *gerushah l'Kohen*: the impediment to the marriage existed before the captivity and is entirely independent of it. According to Rava, no redemption obligation applies in such a case.

This is the point on which Abaye and Rava diverge. Abaye, following the reasoning that redemption is not an "ongoing marital" obligation and therefore applies even in a forbidden marriage, would obligate the *Kohen Gadol* to redeem his *almanah* wife. Rava, applying the principle that the impediment generating the forbidden status must be specifically the captivity, would not impose a redemption obligation when the marriage was already forbidden prior to the captivity.

## Redemption Debate

### Vow & Captivity

*Leima k'tana'ei* — the *gemara* suggests this dispute between Abaye and Rava may be rooted in a *tanna'itic* debate. The case: a husband takes a vow that his wife may derive no benefit from him (*hamadir es ishto*). Such a vow obligates him to divorce her, since husband and wife cannot continue to live together under such a vow. He is thus already in the process of being obligated to divorce her. Subsequently, she is taken captive.

### Rabbi Eliezer

*Rabbi Eliezer omer podeh v'nosein lah kesuvashah* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the husband must redeem her and then give her the *ketubah* upon divorcing her. Both obligations apply.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

*Rabbi Yehoshua omer nosein lah kesuvashah v'einah podeh* — Rabbi Yehoshua holds that he need only pay out her *ketubah* upon divorcing her; he has no obligation to redeem her. Since they were already headed toward divorce before the captivity, the marital framework that generates the redemption obligation no longer fully applies.

### Timing Question

*Amar Rabbi Natan sha'alti es Sumchos* — Rabbi Natan reports that he posed to Sumchos, a student of Rabbi Meir, the following question: when Rabbi Yehoshua said that the husband need only pay the *ketubah* without redeeming her, did he mean this only when *keshedirah ul'vasof nishbeys* — the vow preceded the captivity, so that the couple was already obligated to divorce at the time she was taken? Or would it apply even if *nishbeys ul'vasof pedirah* — she was taken captive first, and the vow came only afterward?

### Sumchos Answer

*V'amar li lo shamati, v'nir'in hadevarim keshedirah ul'vasof nishbeys* — Sumchos replied: I did not hear this explicitly, but it seems that Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling — that there is no redemption obligation — applies only when the vow preceded the captivity. When she was already in a state of required divorce before being taken captive, the redemption obligation does not apply. If, however, she was first taken captive and only then the husband took the vow, the obligation to redeem would remain.

### Scheme Concern

*D'ee amart nishbeys ul'vasof pedirah, asi l'arumey* — were we to say that a husband can escape the redemption obligation by taking a vow after his wife is already held captive, we would be enabling a scheme. A husband facing a redemption payment of 100,000 — far exceeding the lump sum *ketubah* payment of 10,000 — could simply take a vow after the captivity, trigger the divorce obligation, and thereby reduce his financial exposure from 100,000 to 10,000. To prevent this manipulation, the ruling applies only when the vow demonstrably preceded the captivity.

## Kohen Attribution

### Linking to Kohen

*Mai lav bemadir ishto kamiflagi* — the *gemara* asks whether the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua is precisely about a case comparable to that of the *Kohen*'s wife: a situation in which the couple is obligated to divorce for reasons unrelated to the captivity, yet the question remains whether the redemption obligation persists.

### Abaye=R. Eliezer

*Abaye amar k'Rabbi Eliezer* — Abaye's position aligns with Rabbi Eliezer. Even when there is an independent reason compelling divorce, the redemption obligation remains. Just as Rabbi Eliezer says the husband must redeem and then divorce, Abaye holds that a *Kohen* married to a woman forbidden to him must still redeem her.

### Rava=R. Yehoshua

*Rava amar k'Rabbi Yehoshua* — Rava's position aligns with Rabbi Yehoshua. Where there is an independent imperative to divorce — one that existed before and apart from the captivity — the redemption obligation does not apply. Only the *ketubah* payment upon divorce is owed.

### Conclusion

The debate, then, applies specifically to cases of forced divorce: situations in which the husband is obligated to divorce his wife for reasons independent of her captivity, yet she is subsequently taken captive. Must he redeem her? Rabbi Eliezer — and Abaye — say yes. Rabbi Yehoshua — and Rava — say the *ketubah* payment upon divorce suffices, and redemption is not required. We will continue from here.
