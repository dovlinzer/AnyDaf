# Beitzah 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Tchumim Overview

### 39 Melachos Context

We arrive at the penultimate daf of *Beitzah* — daf 39 — as the masechta draws to a close. It is worth noting that while there are 39 *melachos*, the tractate runs from daf 2 through daf 40, yielding precisely 39 *dapim* of content. Not all 39 *melachos* apply with equal force on *yom tov*, and the discussions of this daf continue to explore the intersection of *yom tov* law with the principles of *tchumim* — the boundaries within which people and objects may travel on *Shabbat* and *yom tov*.

### Tchumim on Yom Tov

Although *tchumim* applies equally on *Shabbat* and *yom tov*, the practical concern about transporting objects — and especially food — is distinctively a *yom tov* issue. On *Shabbat*, carrying outdoors is prohibited categorically, so the question of how far one may transport an object rarely arises in the same way. On *yom tov*, however, where carrying food is permitted and one need not rely on walls or an *eiruv* in the conventional sense, the concern about moving food across *tchum* boundaries becomes acute. It is in this context that the present daf continues its inquiry.

## Coal & Flame

### Coal vs. Flame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגַּחֶלֶת כְּרַגְלֵי הַבְּעָלִים, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. גַּחֶלֶת שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ — מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת — לֹא נֶהֱנִין, וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. הַמּוֹצִיא גַּחֶלֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — חַיָּיב, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A coal** that one borrowed from another on the Festival is **as the feet of the owner,** and it may be carried on the Festival to any place where its owner may walk. Since it has substance, it is associated with its owner. **But a flame** that one lit from another's flame may be taken **anywhere,** as it has no substance. This essential difference between a coal and a flame has additional halakhic ramifications: If one uses **a coal of consecrated property** for a non-consecrated purpose, he is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, since it has substance. **But** if one uses a consecrated **flame,** although according to rabbinic law **one may not** derive **benefit** from it *ab initio*, if one did benefit from it, **he is not** liable for **misuse,** since it does not have substance. Similarly, **one who takes out a coal** from a private domain **to the public domain** on Shabbat is **liable** for the prohibited labor of carrying, but one who takes out **a flame is exempt.**

The *Mishnah* opens with a fundamental distinction: a *gachelet* — a coal or ember — is subject to the *tchum* of its owner, just like any other object in one's possession. The *shalevet* — the flame — however, may be taken *bechol makom*, anywhere at all, without restriction. The practical scenario is straightforward: if one forgot to light a *yahrtzeit* candle before *yom tov* and needs to kindle a light, one may transfer a flame from someone else's candle or fire. One may not, however, take that person's physical candle beyond its owner's *tchum*. The flame, by contrast, carries no such limitation.

### Flame Nature

This raises an immediate question: why is the flame exempt? One possible explanation is that the transferred flame is considered a new entity — it is now yours, not the original owner's. But the *Mishnah*'s language of *bechol makom*, "in every place," without qualification, suggests something deeper: the flame is not considered a *davar mamash* — a real, substantial object — at all. It belongs to no one because it is not the kind of thing that can be owned. That is why there is no *tchum* limitation tied to any particular person; it is not that the flame belongs to the new recipient, but rather that flame as such is not a possessable thing.

### Hekdesh Coal & Flame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בַּגַּחֶלֶת: הַגַּחֶלֶת כְּרַגְלֵי הַבְּעָלִים, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. גַּחֶלֶת שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ — מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת — לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. גַּחֶלֶת שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — אֲסוּרָה, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת — מוּתֶּרֶת. הַמּוֹצִיא גַּחֶלֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — חַיָּיב, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת — פָּטוּר. הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ — אָסוּר בְּגַחַלְתּוֹ, וּמוּתָּר בְּשַׁלְהַבְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta* (*Beitza* 4:7): **Five things were stated with regard to a coal,** in relation to the practical halakhic differences between a coal and a flame: **(1) Coal is as the feet of the owner** with regard to its Festival resting place, **whereas a flame** may be carried **anywhere. (2) One** is liable for **misusing** property consecrated to the Temple **with a consecrated coal, whereas** with regard to **a flame,** according to rabbinic law **one may not benefit** from it, **but he is not** liable for **misusing** property consecrated to the Temple. **(3) Coal** used for **idol worship is prohibited** for one to benefit from it, **whereas** from **a flame** of this sort it **is permitted** to benefit. **(4) One who carries out a coal to the public domain is liable, whereas** one who carries out **a flame is exempt. (5) One who is prohibited by a vow** from deriving **benefit from another is prohibited from** using **his coal, but** he is **permitted** to derive benefit **from his flame.**

This same distinction carries through to the domain of *hekdesh*. A *gachelet shel hekdesh* — an ember belonging to the *Beit HaMikdash* — is subject to *me'ilah*: misappropriating it constitutes a biblical violation. The *shalevet* of *hekdesh*, however, occupies a different category: *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — one may not derive benefit from it rabbinically, yet one does not incur the biblical prohibition of *me'ilah*.

This raises a genuine conceptual difficulty. One can understand why a transferred flame might not be subject to *me'ilah* if it is regarded as a new entity no longer connected to the original coal. But what if one is simply standing near a fire of *hekdesh* and benefiting from the light or heat emanating from it? The flame is still directly rooted in the coal of *hekdesh*. Is that not equivalent to deriving benefit from the coal itself?

The difficulty is real and connects to a broader *gemara* discussion about whether one who burns wood is considered to be deriving benefit from the wood as it burns, or only from what has already been consumed — that is, whether the energy being released is still "the wood" in any meaningful sense. In contemporary terms, this is something like the relationship between matter and energy: the flame is the conversion of mass into energy, and perhaps at that point of conversion the substance of the coal is no longer the operative reality. What one derives benefit from is the flame — the energy — not the coal that gave rise to it. Though the *Gemara* does not frame the discussion in these terms, the underlying intuition tracks closely with the scientific understanding of combustion.

If one were to take a piece of straw and press it directly against a smoldering ember of *hekdesh*, that would appear to be a straightforward case of *me'ilah* — one is deriving benefit from the coal itself, before the conversion. But if one touches one's straw to the *flame* rising from that coal, the question becomes whether that flame is still the coal or whether it has become something categorically distinct.

### Carrying Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא שַׁלְהֶבֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּשַׁרְיָא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ דַּאֲסִירָא? עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּמְאִיסָה וּבְדִילִי אִינָשֵׁי מִינַּהּ — לָא גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן. הֶקְדֵּשׁ, דְּלָא מְאִיס וְלָא בְּדִילִי אִינָשֵׁי מִינֵּיהּ — גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakhot* cited in the *baraita* above, the Gemara asks: **What is different** in the case of **a flame** of **idol worship, that** one is **permitted** to use it even *ab initio*, as the *baraita* uses the term permitted in that case; **and what is different** in the case of a **consecrated** flame, in **that** it is **prohibited** to be used *ab initio*, as the *baraita* states: One may not benefit from it, but he is not liable for misuse? The Gemara explains: In the case of **idol worship, which is repulsive** to Jews **and from which** Jewish **people** inherently **maintain separation, the Sages did not decree** additional restrictions **with regard to it.** However, concerning **consecrated property, which is not repulsive and from which people do not** inherently **maintain separation,** in order to prevent its misuse, **the Sages did decree with regard to it** that it is prohibited to use the flame.

### Five Coal Rules

A *Tosefta* compiles five rules stated about the *gachelet*, presenting them in parallel: for *tchumim*, the coal follows the owner while the flame is unrestricted; for *hekdesh*, there is *me'ilah* on the coal but not on the flame; for *avodah zarah*, the coal is forbidden while the flame is permitted; for carrying on *Shabbat*, one is liable for the coal but exempt for the flame; and for *mudar hana'ah*, one who has forsworn benefit from another's possessions is forbidden from using that person's coal but may transfer and benefit from the flame.

The *Tosefta* also adds, found in the margin, that one recites *borei meorei ha'esh* only on a flame, not on a coal. The logic is consistent: a *berachah* does not require a physical object — one recites a blessing on thunder — but ownership and *halachic* substantiality do require physicality, and the flame lacks that quality.

### Avodah Zarah Fire

Among these five rules, the *avodah zarah* case is the most striking. A coal used in idol worship is *asur* — forbidden by biblical prohibition — while the flame is not only exempt from *me'ilah* (which of course does not apply here at all) but is entirely *mutar*, permitted without even a *derabbanan* restriction. The *Gemara* finds this worthy of explanation, particularly given the asymmetry with *hekdesh*, where the flame is at least rabbinically forbidden.

### Hekdesh vs. AZ

The *Gemara* resolves the asymmetry as follows: *avodah zarah demi'isa badilei inshi minah* — *avodah zarah* is intrinsically repulsive, and people naturally distance themselves from it. Because of this innate taboo, the Rabbis did not need to enact a *gezeirah* against benefiting from its flame; people will avoid it on their own. *Hekdesh*, by contrast, *delo ma'is velo badilei inshi mineih* — it is not disgusting and people do not instinctively keep away from it. People regularly enter the *Beit HaMikdash* and interact with *hekdesh*. Therefore the Rabbis enacted a protective measure, making even the flame of *hekdesh* rabbinically forbidden.

The precise contours of the scenario — whether the prohibition applies only to a transferred flame or also to direct benefit from a flame still rooted in the coal — are left unresolved by the *Gemara*. But the possibility that the ruling may depend on whether the flame remains physically anchored will become relevant in the next section.

## Flame & Shabbat

### Flame Transport

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצִיא גַּחֶלֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — חַיָּיב, וְשַׁלְהֶבֶת — פָּטוּר. וְהָא תַּנְיָא: הַמּוֹצִיא שַׁלְהֶבֶת כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — חַיָּיב! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוֹצִיאוּהוֹ בְּקֵיסָם.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the *baraita* that **one who carries out a coal to the public domain** is **liable, whereas** one who carries out **a flame is exempt.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it **taught** in another *baraita*: **One who carries out a flame of any size** on Shabbat **is liable? Rav Sheshet said:** The second *baraita* is referring to a case **where one carried out** the flame along **with a wooden chip.** Since the flame is attached to a physical object, it is considered significant.

The *Gemara* now probes the *Shabbat* liability question more carefully. The *Mishnah* states that one who carries a *shalevet* is *patur*, yet a *baraita* states that one who carries even the smallest flame is *chayav*. These appear to be a direct contradiction. *Rav Sheshet* resolves it: the *baraita* refers to a case where the flame is being carried out on a *kisam* — a small twig or toothpick. The *Mishnah*'s exemption applies only to a flame carried without any physical substrate.

### Oil & Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם קֵיסָם! בִּדְלֵית לֵיהּ שִׁעוּרָא. דִּתְנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא עֵצִים — כְּדֵי לְבַשֵּׁל בֵּיצָה קַלָּה. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּשַׁיְיפֵיהּ [לְ]מָנָא מִשְׁחָא וְאַתְלִי בֵּיהּ נוּרָא. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם מָנָא? בְּחַסְפָּא. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם חַסְפָּא! בִּדְלֵית לֵיהּ שִׁעוּרָא. דִּתְנַן: חֶרֶס — כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן בֵּין פַּצִּים לַחֲבֵירוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But** if so, **let it derive** that one is liable for carrying out in this case **due to** the **wooden chip,** and the presence of the flame is irrelevant. The Gemara responds: That *baraita* speaks of a chip **that does not have the** minimum **measure** that determines liability for carrying out, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 89b): In the case of **one who carries out wood** on Shabbat, the measure that determines liability is **enough** wood **to cook an egg** of the kind that is the **easiest** to cook, which is the egg of a chicken. Because the chip is too small to cook an egg, one is not liable for carrying it out, but one is liable for carrying out the flame attached to it. **Abaye said** a different scenario: The mishna is referring to a case **where one smeared a vessel with oil, and lit a fire on it,** and carried out that flame. The Gemara asks: If so, **let it derive** that one is liable for carrying out in this case **due to** the **vessel** itself, and the flame is irrelevant. The Gemara replies: The mishna is referring **to** a fire lit in **an earthenware** shard, not in a whole vessel. The Gemara challenges: **And** nevertheless, **let it derive** that one is liable for carrying **due to** the **earthenware** shard itself. The Gemara answers: It deals **with** a shard **that is not of** the minimum **measure** that determines liability for carrying out, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 82a): The measure that determines liability for carrying out **earthenware** is **enough to place between one** window **frame and another,** as small shards of earthenware were sometimes placed between window frames during construction. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *Gemara* immediately asks: if one is carrying the twig, why does the flame matter at all — is one not liable simply for carrying the twig? The answer is that the twig is below the *shiur* for wood: to be liable for carrying wood, one needs enough to cook a light egg, as the *Mishnah* in the relevant context teaches. A match-sized sliver of wood does not meet that threshold. However, because the flame is now *rooted in a physical object*, the flame itself acquires *halachic* significance and one becomes liable on account of the flame.

*Abaye* introduces a further case: one who smears oil on a vessel and lights it on fire. Here there is no obvious wick being consumed; what burns is a thin film of oil on what is, let us say, a shard of pottery. The *Gemara* again asks: why mention the flame — is one not liable for carrying the pottery? Again, the pottery shard is below the *shiur*: to be liable for carrying broken pottery, the piece must be large enough to wedge between beams in a house, as the *Mishnah* rules. The shard does not reach that *shiur*. But here too, because the flame is anchored in a physical object — even an oil-smeared potsherd — it gains substantiality, and one is liable.

### Flame Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא שַׁלְהֶבֶת פָּטוּר, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כְּגוֹן דְּאַדְּיֵיהּ אַדּוֹיֵי לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, if one is liable for carrying it out whenever the flame is attached to an object of substance, **that which we learned** in the mishna here: **One who carries out a flame is exempt,** under **what circumstances** can this case **be found?** The Gemara answers: The mishna is speaking of a case **where one fanned** the fire **with his hand** so that it spread **into the public domain** without its being attached to any vessel.

The *Gemara* now asks: given all this, what scenario would produce the exemption in the *Mishnah* — a case where one carries a flame and is genuinely *patur*? The answer: *kegon de'adei aduyei lirshut harabbim* — one blew or pushed the flame across the boundary.

### Blown Flame

*Rashi* explains: one has a fire on one side of the boundary between private and public domain, and blows or waves it such that something on the other side catches fire. The object that was burning in the private domain never moved, and the object now burning in the public domain was already there. There was no act of carrying — no transfer of a flame-bearing object from one domain to another. Only in this fashion does one "carry" a flame in the technical sense without it being anchored in a physical object that itself crossed the boundary.

This conclusion now circles back to the earlier discussions. If *Shabbat* liability for carrying a flame depends on whether it is rooted in a physical object, perhaps the same principle governs the *hekdesh* and *avodah zarah* cases as well. Perhaps the flame of *hekdesh* is only rabbinically forbidden — and the flame of *avodah zarah* entirely permitted — in a case where the flame has been detached from its original coal and transferred. But if one is benefiting directly from a flame still rising from its original fuel, perhaps the coal's status reasserts itself, and one would be treated as deriving benefit from the coal itself. The *Gemara* does not revisit the earlier cases in light of this conclusion, leaving the question open.

## Wells & Ownership

### Three Well Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בּוֹר שֶׁל יָחִיד — כְּרַגְלֵי הַיָּחִיד, וְשֶׁל אַנְשֵׁי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר — כְּרַגְלֵי אַנְשֵׁי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר, וְשֶׁל עוֹלֵי בָבֶל — כְּרַגְלֵי הַמְמַלֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a cistern of an individual,** water drawn from it **is as the feet of the individual** who owns the cistern, and the water may be carried only to those places where its owner is permitted to walk. **And** water drawn from a cistern belonging jointly to all **the people** dwelling in **a particular town is as the feet of the people of that town. And** water drawn from a cistern **of those who come up** to Eretz Yisrael **from Babylonia,** i.e., a public cistern, is **as the feet of whoever fills** his vessel with its water; the water has no defined boundary of its own since it is made available to all.

The next *Mishnah* turns from fire to water, introducing a parallel set of rules about wells and the *tchum* of their water. Three categories are distinguished, each governed by a different principle of ownership.

### Private vs. City

A *bor shel yachid* — a privately owned well — has its water go by the owner's *tchum*, like any other object in private ownership. A well belonging to the residents of a city goes by the *tchum* of the city collectively: even if individual residents have personally extended their *tchum* through an *eiruv tchumin*, that does not affect the city's water, which is defined by the two-thousand *amah* radius of the city as a whole.

### Olei Bavel Wells

A third category is wells designated for *olei Bavel* — travelers who came up for the *regel*. These wells were established along roads for the use of pilgrims and wayfarers, not for a defined community. They are not owned by any collective. Accordingly, the water goes *keraglei hamemallei* — by the *tchum* of whoever fills the water on *yom tov*. It is worth noting the *Mishnah*'s choice of language: even writing after the *Churban*, it speaks of *olei Bavel* as a living reality, evoking the sense that the pilgrimage remains a live institution.

### Breira & Filling

*Rashi* notes an implicit assumption in this ruling that deserves attention. At the onset of *yom tov*, this water is ownerless — *hefker*. Given that, one might have expected it either to acquire its *shvisah* in place (as *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* held for ownerless objects), giving it a two-thousand *amah* radius from the well itself, or alternatively to have no *tchum* at all, free of any restriction. Instead, the *Mishnah* rules that it goes by the filler. *Rashi* explains that this reflects the principle of *breira*: since the person ultimately acquires the water on *yom tov*, we say retroactively that from the beginning of *yom tov* this water was already under that person's effective ownership, and so it is properly governed by that person's *tchum*.

## Water Flow Debate

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רָמֵי לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן, תְּנַן: בּוֹר שֶׁל יָחִיד — כְּרַגְלֵי הַיָּחִיד, וּרְמִינְהוּ: נְהָרוֹת הַמּוֹשְׁכִין וּמַעֲיָנוֹת הַנּוֹבְעִין — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּרַגְלֵי כׇּל אָדָם! אָמַר (רָבָא): הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּמְכוּנָּסִין. וְאִתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בִּמְכוּנָּסִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava raised a contradiction to Rav Naḥman: We learned** in the mishna that the water of **a cistern of an individual is as the feet of the individual; and** Rava **raised a contradiction** from the *Tosefta* (*Beitza* 4:8): Water drawn from **flowing rivers and flowing springs are as the feet of all people. Rava said: With what are we dealing here** in the mishna? **With** cisterns that contain **collected** water, not flowing water. **And it was also said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin said** that **Shmuel said:** The mishna applies only **to collected** water.

*Rava* raises a contradiction against *Rav Nachman*. The *Mishnah* states that a private well goes by its owner's *tchum*. Yet a *baraita* teaches: *haneharos hamoshchin veha-ma'ayanos hanov'in harei hen keraglei kol adam* — rivers that flow and springs that surge go by every person, meaning they are not limited by anyone's *tchum* at all.

### Flowing Water

The resolution lies in the nature of the water. The *baraita* speaks of *moshchin* — flowing — and *nov'in* — surging. Such water begins *yom tov* not at rest in a fixed location. Since *kinyan shvisah* requires the object to be stationary, water that is continuously moving cannot acquire a *shvisah* at all. Even if it is privately owned, it never becomes *koneh shvisah*, and therefore no *tchum* applies.

### Collected Water

The *Mishnah*, by contrast, discusses a *bor* — a cistern of collected rain water. This water is stationary at the onset of *yom tov* and can acquire a *shvisah*. *Rava* states, and *Chiya bar Abba* in the name of *Shmuel* confirms: *bi'mekhunasim* — the *Mishnah* is dealing with collected water. The contrast with the *baraita* is thus resolved: the *baraita* addresses flowing water, the *Mishnah* addresses collected water.

## Hefker Dispute

### Who Owns Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל עוֹלֵי בָבֶל — כְּרַגְלֵי הַמְמַלֵּא. אִתְּמַר: מִילֵּא וְנָתַן לַחֲבֵירוֹ, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: כְּרַגְלֵי מִי שֶׁנִּתְמַלְּאוּ לוֹ, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: כְּרַגְלֵי הַמְמַלֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And** water drawn from a cistern **of those who come up** to Eretz Yisrael **from Babylonia,** i.e., a public cistern, is **as the feet of whoever fills** his vessel with its water. **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to this issue: In the case of one who **filled** a vessel with water from a public cistern on behalf of **another and gave** the water **to** him, **Rav Naḥman said:** The water is **as the feet of the one for whom they were filled; Rav Sheshet said:** It is **as the feet of the one who filled** it.

The *Mishnah*'s ruling — *keraglei hamemallei* — assumes that the *olei Bavel* well is in some sense *hefker*, or at least not collectively owned in the way a city's well is. The question that now arises concerns a specific scenario: what if one fills the water from such a well not for oneself but for someone else?

### Rav Nachman

*Rav Nachman* rules: the water goes by *mi she-nitmalu lo* — the person for whom it was filled.

### Rav Sheshet

*Rav Sheshet* rules: the water goes by *keraglei hamemallei* — the filler, not the recipient.

### Hefker Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: בֵּירָא דְהֶפְקֵרָא הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר: בֵּירָא דְשׁוּתָּפֵי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara explains: **One Sage,** Rav Sheshet, **holds** that a public **cistern is ownerless,** and the *halakha* is that one cannot take possession of ownerless property on behalf of someone else. Therefore, the water belongs to the one who drew it; it is as his feet, and this status does not change even if he subsequently gave it to anyone else. **And one Sage,** Rav Naḥman, **holds** that a public cistern **is** considered jointly owned **by** all its **partners,** namely, all of the Jewish people. Therefore, it is possible for one partner to draw water on behalf of another partner, and the drawn water immediately belongs to the person for whom it was drawn.

*Rashi* clarifies the scope of the dispute immediately: this is not a case where one first filled the water for oneself and then gave it to a friend. That case is covered by the *Mishnah* itself — once I have acquired the water, it is mine, limited by my *tchum*, and my subsequent gift to someone else does not change the *tchum* that was already fixed. The dispute concerns rather the case — analogous to *Rivkah* and *Eliezer* — where from the outset I fill the water *in order to* give it to someone else. My act of drawing is entirely in service of providing water for the other person. Whose water is it?

*Rashi* connects this to the principle of *magbia metzia l'chavero*: if I pick up an ownerless object in order to acquire it on behalf of someone else, does that other person become the owner? The general rule in *Bava Metzia* is that *magbia metzia l'chavero lo kanah chavero* — the recipient does not acquire, because by picking it up for him I am depriving others of the opportunity to take it themselves. Thus, if the well is *hefker*, I — the filler — acquire the water, and it goes by my *tchum*, not the recipient's.

### Hefker vs. Shutafut

*Rav Nachman* and *Rav Sheshet* thus disagree about the underlying nature of the *olei Bavel* well. *Mar savar bira de'hefkera hu* — one holds the well is *hefker*, ownerless. *U'mar savar bira de'shutfei hu* — the other holds the well is effectively a partnership, belonging collectively to all of *Klal Yisrael*.

If the well is *hefker*, then filling it for another person does not transfer ownership to that person, since the principle of *magbia metzia l'chavero* blocks the acquisition — and therefore it goes by the filler. If the well is a form of *shutafut* — collective partnership — then the picture changes. Every Jew is already a *shutaf*, a co-owner, in the water. When I draw it for Charlie, I am not taking something from the commons at others' expense; I am merely facilitating Charlie's access to his own portion. In that case, Charlie can acquire it, and the water goes by him.

## Noder Hana'ah

### Vow Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: הֲרֵינִי עָלֶיךָ חֵרֶם — הַמּוּדָּר אָסוּר. הֲרֵי אַתָּה עָלַי חֵרֶם — הַנּוֹדֵר אָסוּר. הֲרֵינִי עָלֶיךָ וְאַתָּה עָלַי — שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִים זֶה בָּזֶה, וּמוּתָּרִין בְּשֶׁל עוֹלֵי בָבֶל, וַאֲסוּרִין בְּשֶׁל אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a challenge to Rav Naḥman** from a mishna (*Nedarim* 47b): One who says to another: **I am hereby** prohibited **to you** by force of ***ḥerem*,** a kind of vow of prohibition, as objects declared as *ḥerem* are generally consecrated to the Temple, **the one prohibited by the vow,** the addressee, **is prohibited** to derive benefit from the person who made the vow or from his property, as the point of the vow was to prohibit the addressee from deriving any benefit from the one who made the vow. If he said to him: **You are hereby** prohibited **to me** by force of ***ḥerem*, the one making the vow is** himself **prohibited** to derive benefit from the addressee or from his property. If he said to him: **I am hereby** prohibited **to you** and **you to me** by force of *ḥerem*, **they are both prohibited** to benefit **from one another. And they are permitted** to benefit from anything belonging **to those who come up from Babylonia,** i.e., public property that is not owned by any person or group, **but they are prohibited** to benefit from property that is jointly owned by the inhabitants **of that city,** as both parties have a share in such items.

*Rabbah* challenges *Rav Nachman* — who holds the water goes by the recipient, implying the well is *shutafut* — from a *baraita* concerning *mudar hana'ah*. The case: one person says *hareini alecha cherem* — my possessions are forbidden to you — and the other reciprocates: *harei atah alai cherem* — your possessions are forbidden to me. Both vows take effect simultaneously: *shneihem asurim zeh bazeh*, each is forbidden from deriving benefit from the other.

### City Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן דְּבָרִים שֶׁל עוֹלֵי בָבֶל: הַר הַבַּיִת, הַלְּשָׁכוֹת וְהָעֲזָרוֹת, וּבוֹר שֶׁל אֶמְצַע הַדֶּרֶךְ. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר: הָרְחוֹב, וּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, וּבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ.
>
> **Translation:** That mishna provides examples: **And the following are items of those coming up from Babylonia,** i.e., publicly owned items: **The Temple Mount, the chambers, and the courtyards** on the Temple Mount, **and a cistern** situated **in the middle of the road. And these are** items jointly owned by the inhabitants **of that city: The street, and the synagogue, and the bathhouse.**

The *baraita* then specifies what is and is not included in this mutual prohibition. *V'asurin beshel ota ha'ir* — they are forbidden from the property belonging to their city collectively: the streets (*harechov*), the bathhouse (*beit hamerchatz*), and the synagogue (*beit haknesset*). The rationale is that each person holds a partial ownership in these communal institutions, and deriving benefit from them thus constitutes deriving benefit from the other. This has far-reaching practical implications: as soon as a mutual *issur hana'ah* takes effect, one cannot walk on city streets, enter the communal synagogue, or use the bathhouse.

### Olei Bavel Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בֵּירָא דְשׁוּתָּפֵי הוּא, אַמַּאי מוּתָּר? וְהָתְנַן: הַשּׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁנָּדְרוּ הֲנָאָה זֶה מִזֶּה — אֲסוּרִים לִיכָּנֵס לֶחָצֵר לִרְחוֹץ בַּבּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** Rava, having cited the mishna in full, concludes her challenge to the opinion of Rav Naḥman: **And if you say** that **a cistern** of those who come up from Babylonia, a public cistern, **is** owned jointly **by partners,** i.e., by all Jews, **why** should it be **permitted** for the one who made the vow and the addressee to use it? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 45b): Two **partners who took a vow** not to derive **benefit from one another are prohibited to enter** a joint **courtyard** in which they both have a share **to wash** themselves **in a cistern**. According to you, the same should apply to a cistern in which the two of them have a share, such as the cisterns of those who come up from Babylonia.

By contrast, *umutarin beshel olei Bavel* — they are permitted to benefit from things belonging to *olei Bavel*. These are not subject to the mutual prohibition, even though the water in such wells and the pilgrimage infrastructure generally belongs in some sense to all of *Klal Yisrael*.

### Olei Bavel Examples

The *baraita* enumerates: *Har HaBayit*, the Temple Mount; the chambers (*lishkot*); the *azarot*; and the wells along the roads. Though *Har HaBayit* belongs to all of *Klal Yisrael*, and not only to *olei Bavel* in the narrow sense, its inclusion here illustrates the category: public property of a national rather than a local character, accessible to all without the quality of partial individual ownership.

### City Examples

The city examples — streets, bathhouse, synagogue — represent a different kind of communal ownership: one where the individual members of the local community hold genuine, partial stakes, such that benefiting from them counts as benefiting from one another. The *Gemara* here touches on questions that later jurisprudence would address through the concept of corporate personality. The *halacha* has a developed sense that city property is not the same as partnership property and not the same as *hefker*, yet it still treats it as sufficiently connected to the individual members that an *issur hana'ah* between individuals extends to it.

## Partnership Analysis

### Partnership Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִרְחוֹץ הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — לְמַלּאוֹת, מָר מִדִּידֵיהּ קָא מְמַלֵּא וּמַר מִדִּידֵיהּ קָא מְמַלֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman answered: Indeed that is the case. **So too,** they are prohibited **to wash** themselves in a cistern because when bathing one uses of all the water of the cistern, part of which belongs to the forbidden partner. **But** when the *baraita* says that a cistern of those who come up from Babylonia is permitted to both parties, **with what are we dealing here?** The *baraita* is referring only **to filling** water from the cistern. This is permitted because it is considered that **this one fills from his** portion, **and that one fills from his** portion. The water that each of them draws is considered retroactively designated exclusively for him, so that the partner has no share in it at all.

The *baraita* has established that one may derive benefit from *olei Bavel* property even under *mudar hana'ah*. This becomes the basis for a logical inference: if the *olei Bavel* well were a *shutafut* — a genuine partnership among co-owners — it would be no different from any other jointly owned property. *Tosafot* makes this point directly: if the well were a partnership like city property, the *baraita* would have forbidden it just as it forbids city property. The fact that it is permitted implies the well is not a partnership.

### Breira Logic

But the *Gemara* refines this argument. The prohibition on city property does not rest simply on partnership ownership in the abstract; it rests on the way in which one *uses* the shared resource. The *Mishnah* in *Nedarim* teaches: *hashutafin shenidru hana'ah ze mizeh asurin l'hikanes l'chatzer* — partners who are mutually subject to *issur hana'ah* may not enter a jointly owned courtyard, and may not *lirchotz b'vor* — bathe in a shared well. The reason is that when one bathes in the well, one uses the entire shared space as a single unit; one is not taking one's discrete portion, but rather immersing oneself in the whole. One cannot apply *breira* to say "this part of the water is mine" when one is using all of it simultaneously.

### Water Apportionment

Drawing water from a well, however, is different. When one takes water from the well, one removes a discrete, bounded quantity. That quantity was always, retroactively, the portion destined for this person. This is the operation of *breira*: the act of drawing apportions the water, and retroactively that water was always one's own portion. Therefore, even in a *shutafut* framework, drawing water does not involve deriving benefit from the other person's share — it involves taking only one's own share, which is determined at the moment of drawing. One is not forbidden from drawing water from a jointly owned well under *mudar hana'ah*, because *breira* ensures that the water drawn was always yours.

### Rav Nachman Position

On this basis, *Rav Nachman*'s position can be maintained with the well as *shutafut*: the *mudar hana'ah* problem is avoided not because the well is *hefker* but because *breira* governs the taking of water in a *shutafut* framework. And on *yom tov*, when one draws water on behalf of another, that other person acquires their portion through the same *breira* mechanism — the water drawn was always their intended portion. Hence, according to *Rav Nachman*, the water goes by the recipient.

## Estate Division

### Brothers' Estate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַב נַחְמָן יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה? וְהָתְנַן: הָאַחִים הַשּׁוּתָּפִים, כְּשֶׁחַיָּיבִים בַּקָּלְבּוֹן — פְּטוּרִין מִמַּעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. וּכְשֶׁחַיָּיבִים בְּמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה — פְּטוּרִין מִן הַקָּלְבּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rav Naḥman hold** that **there is** retroactive **designation? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 1:7): If **brothers** divided up inherited property among themselves and subsequently joined their property again and became **partners, they are obligated to** add **a *kalbon*,** a small coin, to the obligatory half-shekel yearly Temple donation. The *kalbon* covered both the cost to the Temple of exchanging half-shekels into larger coins and the depreciation of the donated coin. Although a whole shekel given by two partners does not need to be changed into a larger coin, the Sages imposed the same *kalbon* fee on the partners as on everyone else. However, these partners **are exempt from** the **animal tithe,** in accordance with the standard *halakha* that people who own animals in partnership are exempt from the animal tithe. The quote from the mishna continues: **And** in a situation in which the brothers are **liable for the animal tithe,** as when they have not yet divided up their inheritance, and all the deceased's estate is therefore still considered a single unit and not a partnership, **they are exempt from the *kalbon*,** in accordance with the *halakha* that a father who contributes a single shekel for his two dependent sons does not need to add the *kalbon*.

The *Gemara* now questions whether *Rav Nachman* truly holds *yesh breira*. A *Mishnah* is cited concerning *achim hashutafim* — brothers who inherited an estate and then became partners in it. The question is whether their reconstitution as a partnership changes the legal status of the estate. Before division, the estate functions as a single corporate-like entity — *tfisat habayit* — rather than as a partnership of individual owners. After division, if they pool their holdings again, the estate functions as a *shutafut*, a genuine partnership. The difference between these two statuses carries significant practical consequences in two areas: *ma'aser behemah* and the *kalbon*.

### Obligations Differ

*Ma'aser behemah* — the animal tithe — requires that one drive all newborn animals through a narrow opening and consecrate every tenth one. This obligation does not apply to animals owned in partnership; it applies only to privately owned animals. As long as the estate remains undivided, its animals are owned by a single corporate entity — the estate — and the brothers are obligated in *ma'aser behemah* as one collective owner. Once they divide and reconstitute as a partnership, however, the animals are owned by multiple individuals in partnership, and the obligation of *ma'aser behemah* does not apply.

The *kalbon* — the moneychanger's surcharge — works in the opposite direction. When two partners contribute a single *shekel* between them, they are still two individuals and must pay two *pundions* (the surcharge). But if the estate remains undivided and functions as a single entity, only one *kalbon* is required, even though two brothers are involved.

Thus: when they are obligated in the *kalbon* — meaning they divided and reconstituted as a partnership — they are exempt from *ma'aser behemah*; when they are obligated in *ma'aser behemah* — meaning the estate remains as one entity — they are exempt from the *kalbon*.

### Rav Anan View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב עָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁחָלְקוּ גְּדָיִם כְּנֶגֶד טְלָאִים וּטְלָאִים כְּנֶגֶד גְּדָיִם. אֲבָל חָלְקוּ גְּדָיִם כְּנֶגֶד גְּדָיִם וּטְלָאִים כְּנֶגֶד טְלָאִים, אוֹמֵר: זֶהוּ חֶלְקוֹ הַמַּגִּיעוֹ מִשָּׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה לְכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Anan said:** The Sages **taught** that the inherited property is no longer considered a single unit after the brothers divided it and then rejoined in a partnership **only when they divided kids against lambs or lambs against kids,** i.e., if one brother took kids and the other took a corresponding value of lambs. This kind of division is considered a commercial transaction, with one brother purchasing goats and paying for them with lambs and vice versa. Therefore, when they join their animals again as partners, it is considered an entirely new partnership. **However,** if **they divided kids against kids and lambs against lambs,** meaning that each brother took an equal portion of each of the items they inherited, one can **say** of each brother's portion: **This is his portion** destined to **reach him from the first moment,** from the time of the death of the deceased. If the brothers form their partnership again, the inheritance becomes a single unit again, and they are therefore obligated in the animal tithe and exempt from the *kalbon*.

*Rav Anan* introduces a nuance: the ruling that division followed by reconstitution creates a *shutafut* applies only when the division was uneven, specifically when brothers exchanged unlike categories — *g'dayim k'neged tla'im* — goats for sheep, or sheep for goats. In such a case, the division was effectively a commercial exchange: I took all the goats and gave you my share in them in exchange for your share in the sheep. Since there was a genuine transaction at the moment of division, the estate was dissolved and what was reconstituted is a new partnership.

### Even Division

*Rav Anan* holds that if the division was even — *g'dayim k'neged g'dayim, tla'im k'neged tla'im* — each brother took exactly half of every category — then *breira* applies. This half was always what you were destined to receive; this is the portion that was always coming to you from the inheritance. There was no purchase, no exchange, no transaction; just a clarification of what was always yours. Since the division was merely the actualization of what was always each person's portion by inheritance, it did not break the corporate unity of the estate. Reconstituting it is therefore not creating a new partnership; it is reverting to the natural state that was only temporarily obscured.

### Rav Nachman View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חָלְקוּ גְּדָיִם כְּנֶגֶד גְּדָיִם וּטְלָאִים כְּנֶגֶד טְלָאִים — אֵין אוֹמֵר: זֶה חֶלְקוֹ הַמַּגִּיעוֹ מִשָּׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה לְכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rav Naḥman said: Even if they divided kids against kids and lambs against lambs,** one does **not say** that **this is his portion** destined to **reach him from the first moment.** This is because Rav Naḥman does not accept the principle of retroactive designation. Consequently, the resolution proposed previously for the issue of filling water from the cistern of those who come up from Babylonia is invalid.

*Rav Nachman* disagrees: *afilu chilku g'dayim k'neged g'dayim utla'im k'neged tla'im — ein omer ze chelko hamagia mishaa rishona lakach*. Even an even division does not admit of *breira*. Any division of an estate involves an element of purchase and exchange: by taking these specific goats rather than those specific goats, I am effectively buying out your portion in these goats and surrendering my portion in those. There is always a transactional element, and therefore no division can be said to have been predetermined by the original inheritance. Once divided, the estate cannot be reconstituted; what is formed is always a new partnership.

### No Reconstitution

The consequence of *Rav Nachman*'s position is that *ein breira* governs the estate scenario. Brothers who divide and reconstitute are always partners, never a reconstituted estate. And this reveals that *Rav Nachman* holds *ein breira* — at least at the biblical level.

## Well Resolution

### Well is Hefker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בֵּירָא דְהֶפְקֵרָא הִיא, אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: קָנָה, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara retracts its previous explanation of the disagreement between Rava and Rav Naḥman: **Rather, everyone agrees** that a cistern of those who come up from Babylonia, i.e., a public cistern, **is an ownerless cistern, but here they disagree over** a different issue: **One who picks up a found article** intending to acquire it **on behalf of his friend.** One **Sage,** Rav Naḥman, **holds** that if one picks up a found object on behalf of his friend, his friend **acquires** it through this act as though he had picked it up himself. The water of the ownerless cistern is like a found object. Therefore, if one draws water on behalf of another, the latter acquires it, and consequently the water is as his feet. **And** one **Sage,** Rav Sheshet, **holds** that when one picks up a found object for another, the latter **does not acquire it.** Rather, it belongs to the one who actually picked it up, and consequently the water is as the feet of the one who draws it.

The *Gemara* now draws the threads together. Given that *Rav Nachman* holds *ein breira*, his earlier position in the *mudar hana'ah* case — that deriving benefit from a *shutafut* well is permitted because *breira* apportions one's share — is no longer available to him. One cannot explain the permissibility of the well under *issur hana'ah* through *breira* if one holds *ein breira*. Therefore, the *Gemara* concludes: *d'kulei alma bira d'hefkira hu* — it is universally agreed that the *olei Bavel* well is *hefker*. That is what distinguishes it from city property and explains why there is no *mudar hana'ah* problem: the well does not belong to anyone, and therefore deriving benefit from it does not constitute deriving benefit from one's counterpart.

### Filled for Other

This resolution, however, creates a new problem for *Rav Nachman*'s position in the earlier dispute with *Rav Sheshet*. If the well is *hefker*, and one fills the water for someone else, then the principle of *magbia metzia l'chavero lo kanah chavero* should apply: the recipient does not acquire, and the water goes by the filler. Yet *Rav Nachman* ruled that the water goes by the recipient. How can that be reconciled?

### Magbia Dispute

The *Gemara* therefore explains: *Rav Nachman* and *Rav Sheshet* are in fact disagreeing about the application of *magbia metzia l'chavero*. Specifically, they dispute whether one who draws water from this well on behalf of another person has in fact made that other person the owner. *Rav Nachman* holds that the recipient acquires; *Rav Sheshet* holds that the recipient does not acquire, and the filler retains ownership.

### Tosafot Resolution

*Tosafot* offers an elegant explanation for *Rav Nachman*'s position. The rule that *magbia metzia l'chavero lo kanah chavero* — the recipient does not acquire — is premised on the fact that by picking up the object for one person, one deprives others of the opportunity. But is that concern relevant at a well with ample water, sufficient for all who need it? If there is enough water for every traveler, no one loses out when I draw for Charlie. In such a case, *Rav Nachman* holds, the standard objection to *magbia metzia l'chavero* does not apply, and Charlie does acquire. The water therefore goes by the recipient.

## Fruit in Another City

### Two-City Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיוּ פֵּירוֹתָיו בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת וְעֵרְבוּ בְּנֵי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר לְהָבִיא אֶצְלוֹ מִפֵּירוֹתָיו — לֹא יָבִיאוּ לוֹ. וְאִם עֵרַב הוּא — פֵּירוֹתָיו כָּמוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who had produce in a different city** beyond the Shabbat limit, **and the residents of that city** where the produce was located joined the Shabbat boundaries, enabling them to reach the owner's home on the Festival, and they wish **to bring him some of his produce, they may not bring** it **to him.** His produce is as his feet; since it is outside of his Shabbat limit, it may not be taken from its place. **However, if** the owner **placed an *eiruv*** to enable travel to that city, the legal status of **his produce is like his** status with regard to the Shabbat limit. People from that city who also placed an *eiruv* may bring the produce to him, since he himself may walk to the produce and take it.

The final *Mishnah* of the daf introduces a scenario that encapsulates the broader themes of *tchum* and food transport on *yom tov*. The case: my fruit is in City B. I am in City A. City A and City B are within reach of one another — perhaps within two thousand *amot* — but the fruit begins *yom tov* outside my *tchum*. Residents of City B wish to come to City A on *yom tov* and offer to bring me my fruit on their way.

### Fruit Transport Ban

The *Mishnah* rules: *lo yavi'u lo* — they may not bring the fruit to him. The fruit began *yom tov* outside the owner's *tchum*. Once an object begins *yom tov chutz latchum*, it may not be moved in a manner that extends its reach beyond where it could have gone from its starting point. It is not merely that the fruit cannot travel all the way to the owner; the fruit cannot be moved at all in a direction that exceeds its own two-thousand *amah* radius from its location at the onset of *yom tov*.

### Eruv Solution

However, if the owner had made an *eiruv tchumin* before *yom tov* — extending his personal *tchum* toward City B — then the fruit is considered to begin *yom tov* within his *tchum*. In that case, the fruit is treated as his object that was always within his domain, and it may be transported.

### Guest Gift Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁזִּמֵּן אֶצְלוֹ אוֹרְחִים — לֹא יוֹלִיכוּ בְּיָדָם מָנוֹת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן זִכָּה לָהֶם מָנוֹתֵיהֶם מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who invited guests to** visit **him** from a town beyond his Shabbat limit, and they joined the Shabbat boundaries to enable them to reach his house, **they may not carry in their hands** back to their town any **portions** they received from him as gifts. These portions are as the feet of the host, since they belonged to him on the eve of the Festival. This is true **unless he transferred ownership of their portions to them on the eve of the Festival,** in which case the gifts may be carried wherever the recipients may walk.

The *Mishnah* addresses a related scenario: one who invited guests from another city, and when those guests are departing on *yom tov*, wishes to send food back with them as a gift. This too is forbidden: the food is limited by the host's *tchum*, and once the guests leave the host's two-thousand *amah* boundary they may not take the host's food with them. The exception: if before *yom tov* the host formally transferred ownership of the food to the guests — *zachah lahem manoteihem me'erev yom tov* — then the food begins *yom tov* already belonging to the guests, governed by their *tchum*, and they may carry it back when they depart.
