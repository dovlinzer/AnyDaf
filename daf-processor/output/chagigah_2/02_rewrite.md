# Chagigah 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Festival Sacrifices

### Opening Remarks

We begin today with the privilege of opening *Masechet Chagiga*. Before turning to the broader introduction to the *masechet*, let us first work through the *daf* itself to ensure we cover the material fully.

### Three Korbanot Types

The *mishna* opens: *hakol chayavin bere'iyah* — everyone is obligated in the *korban re'iyah*. To understand this, it is necessary to appreciate that there are three distinct *korbanot* one brings to the *Beit HaMikdash* during a *chag*, during one of the *shalosh regalim*. The first is the *korban re'iyah*, an *olah* — a burnt offering, consumed entirely on the altar — brought simply for the act of appearing before God. It is, in essence, a "show-up" *korban*. The second is the *korban chagiga*, a sacrifice in honor of the holiday itself. The third is the *korban simcha*, brought to generate joy during the festival. Crucially, while the *korban re'iyah* is an *olah* that the offerer does not eat, the *korbanot chagiga* and *simcha* are *korbanot shelamim*, which the offerer consumes.

### Korban Re'iyah Obligation

It is this first *korban* — the *korban re'iyah* — that the *mishna* declares universally obligatory. The *mishna* then proceeds to enumerate those categories of individuals who are nonetheless exempt.

## Exemptions & Disabilities

### Exempt Categories

The *mishna* lists the following as exempt from the *korban re'iyah*: *cheireish* (a deaf person), *shoteh* (someone who lacks competence or cognitive agency), and *kattan* (a minor). To these it adds the *tumtum* — a person whose gender is indeterminate — and the *androginos*, one who possesses both sets of reproductive anatomy, not merely undetermined but positively dual. Women (*nashim*) are likewise exempt, as are *avadim she'einam meshuchararim* — slaves who have not yet been freed.

The *mishna*'s language here is notable. It says "slaves who have not yet been freed," for once a slave is freed he is no longer a slave in any halakhic sense — he becomes a convert. The *mishna* is thus referring to the *eved kena'ani* who remains in servitude. One might wonder: is there even a *hava amina* that a *kena'ani* slave would be obligated? The answer lies in the general principle that an *eved kena'ani* is exempt from *mitzvot asei shehazeman gerama* — time-bound positive commandments — but is otherwise bound by the full range of prohibitions and other obligations. Given this framework, the question of whether such a slave might be included in the *re'iyah* obligation is not entirely without basis, even if the conclusion is that he is exempt.

The *mishna* continues: *hechigeir* — one who is lame; *vehasuma* — one who is blind (a matter that will receive extensive discussion by the end of this *daf*); *vehacholeh* — someone who is ill; *vehazaken* — one who is elderly; and *umi she'eino yachol la'alot beraglav* — one who is simply unable to ascend by foot, for whatever reason.

### Minor Definition Debate

*Eizehu kattan?* asks the *mishna* — who exactly qualifies as a *kattan* for the purpose of this exemption?

*Beit Shammai* defines a *kattan* as any child who cannot ride upon his father's shoulders and ascend from Jerusalem to *Har HaBayit*. It is worth noting that this formulation implies *Har HaBayit* is a distinct location from Jerusalem proper. *Beit Hillel* defines a *kattan* as any child who cannot hold his father's hand and walk on his own feet from Jerusalem to *Har HaBayit*, deriving this from the verse *shalosh regalim* — three *regalim*, with *regel* understood by *Rashi* to mean that one must be capable of ascending *b'regel*, on one's own two feet.

The child described by *Beit Hillel* must clearly be older than the one described by *Beit Shammai*: *Beit Shammai*'s threshold is met as soon as the child can ride on his father's shoulders, while *Beit Hillel* requires that he walk independently. This may initially appear to run counter to the usual expectation that *Beit Hillel* takes the more lenient position — but here *Beit Hillel* actually exempts more children by setting a higher bar for obligation. Since a *chiyuv* is a *chumra*, *Beit Hillel*'s wider exemption is in fact the more lenient ruling.

### Shammai-Hillel Value Dis…

*Beit Shammai* further rules that the minimum expenditure for the *re'iyah* — the *olah* — is two pieces of silver, and for the *chagiga*, one silver coin. *Beit Hillel* reverses this: the *re'iyah* requires only one silver coin while the *chagiga* requires two. Here again, *Beit Hillel*'s position is the more lenient one in a practical sense. Since the *chagiga* is a *shelamim* that the offerer eats, spending more on it yields a tangible personal benefit — more food — whereas the *re'iyah* is an *olah* that is burned entirely. *Beit Hillel* thus requires a smaller outlay for the *korban* from which one derives no personal benefit.

## Hakol Inclusive Language

### Hakol Inclusion Analysis

The *gemara* now investigates the word *hakol* — "everyone" — with which the *mishna* opens. There is a well-established principle in *gemara* analysis that when a *mishna* uses inclusive language such as *zeh haklal* or *hakol*, it is not merely summarizing what has already been stated, but rather signaling that some category one might have thought was excluded is in fact included. The *gemara* therefore asks: *hakol l'atuyei mai?* — what additional case does this word come to include?

### Half-Slave Half-Free

The *gemara* offers an initial answer: *l'atuyei mi she'chetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin* — to include the person who is half slave and half free. Such a situation arises, for example, when two partners jointly purchase a slave and one of them subsequently frees his half. One might have thought that because this person is partially enslaved, he is exempt from *re'iyah*, just as a full slave is. The *gemara* answers that his partial freedom is sufficient to generate an obligation.

### Ravina's Exemption

However, *Ravina* holds expressly that *mi she'chetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin patur min ha're'iyah* — this person is in fact exempt. Since Ravina cannot accept the *gemara*'s first proposed inclusion, the question *hakol l'atuyei mai* must be answered differently.

### Healed Lameness Case

The *gemara* therefore proposes a second answer: *l'atuyei chigeir b'yom rishon v'nitpashet b'yom sheni* — to include a person who was lame on the first day of the *chag* but whose lameness healed by the second day. One might have supposed that because lame people are generally exempt, and this person was lame when the *chag* began, he has no subsequent obligation. The inclusion teaches that he is nonetheless *chayav*, since he recovered in time to fulfill the *mitzvah* during the festival.

### Which Day to Bring Korban

Yet even this answer encounters difficulty, for it depends on which of two positions one accepts in the *gemara*. According to the view that *kulan tashlumin zeh lazeh* — all the days of the festival are mutually compensating — a person may bring the *korban re'iyah* on any day of the festival if he missed a prior day, as no single day carries exclusive *chiyuv*. Under this view it is entirely intelligible that someone lame on the first day could be obligated from the second day onward.

But according to the view that *kulan tashlumin l'rishon* — all the subsequent days are merely compensatory for the first day — the *chiyuv* falls exclusively on the first day. If on the first day the person was lame and therefore not a *bar chiyuva*, he was never subject to the obligation in the first place, and there is nothing to "make up" on subsequent days. The case of healed lameness thus cannot serve as the universal inclusion intended by *hakol*, since it does not resolve the problem according to all opinions.

### One-Eye Blindness

The *gemara* therefore proposes a third answer: *l'atuyei suma b'achat mei'einav* — to include a person who is blind in one eye. Such a person is not fully blind and might be expected to be obligated; the *gemara* proposes that the word *hakol* comes to confirm this.

### Yochanan ben Dehavai Rul…

But here too there is a dissenting opinion. A *braita* records that *Yochanan ben Dehavai* rules in the name of *Rabbi Yehuda* that *suma b'achat mei'einav patur min ha're'iyah* — a person blind in one eye is exempt from the *korban re'iyah*. Given this position, Yochanan ben Dehavai cannot accept the *gemara*'s third proposed inclusion, and must therefore hold by one of the earlier positions instead.

### Two Eyes Requirement

What is Yochanan ben Dehavai's reasoning? He derives it from the verse *shene'emar yeira'eh yeireh* — "he shall be seen/he shall see." *Rashi* explains that the verse in question — *shalosh pe'amim bashana yeira'eh kol zechurcha et pnei adon Hashem* — contains a word that is written as *yireh* (he shall see) but read as *yeira'eh* (he shall be seen). From this dual reading the *gemara* derives: *kederech sheba lirot kach ba lera'ot* — just as one comes to see, so too one comes to be seen. *Ma lirot bishtei einav* — seeing is done with two eyes; *af lera'ot bishtei einav* — and therefore, to be "seen" at the Temple, one must also be capable of seeing with both eyes.

It is worth noting that this interpretive move carries an embedded assumption about what constitutes "normal" seeing — namely, binocular vision — and one sensitive to the broader halakhic treatment of disabilities will feel the weight of that assumption here.

## Ravina & Half-Slave Stat…

### Mishna Rishona Resolution

Having worked through all three proposed inclusions, the *gemara* circles back to address Ravina's position directly. The *gemara* offers a resolution: *kan bemishna rishona, kan bemishna achrona* — there are, in effect, two *mishnayot*. The *mishna* as a compiled text reflects a development of opinion, and a position can be revised even within the corpus of the *mishna*. Ravina's ruling — that the half-slave-half-free person is exempt from *korban re'iyah* — reflects the position of the *mishna rishona*, the earlier formulation. The *mishna* as we hold it *l'halacha* represents a later, different position.

### Gittin Mishna Rules

The *gemara* now cites the relevant *mishna* from *Masechet Gittin* to clarify what that later position entails. *Mi shechetzyo eved vechetzyo ben chorin* — a person who is half slave and half free — *oved et rabo yom echad ve'et atzmo yom echad*, *divrei Beit Hillel*: according to *Beit Hillel*, he works one day for his master and one day for himself, and this alternating arrangement is considered a workable and acceptable status quo.

### Shammai's Objection

*Beit Shammai* responds sharply: *tikantem et rabo, et atzmo lo tikantem* — you have solved the master's problem by ensuring his labor entitlement is met, but you have left the slave himself in an impossible situation.

### Marriage Impossibility

The problem is this: *lissa shifcha eino yachol* — he cannot marry a maidservant, because only a full slave may marry a female slave; *bat chorin eino yachol* — and he cannot marry a free woman either, because free people may not marry slaves. He is caught between two worlds, unable to form a household in either.

### Tikkun Ha'olam Freedom

Should he simply remain unmarried? The *gemara* rejects this: *lo nivra ha'olam ela l'pirya v'rivya* — the world was created for the purpose of propagation, as the verse states: *lo tohu vera'ah lashevet yetzarah* — God did not create the world for chaos but for habitation and continuity. Therefore, *mipnei tikkun ha'olam* — for the sake of *tikkun ha'olam* — something must be done.

It is important to note that *tikkun ha'olam* in *Masechet Gittin* carries a fairly precise and narrow meaning: it refers to legal arrangements that must be corrected because they generate untenable social situations. This is distinct from the broader contemporary usage of the term in the sense of general *chesed* or social activism.

### Forced Freedom & Debt

The resolution: *kofin et rabo ve'oseh oto ben chorin* — the *Beit Din* compels the master to free the slave entirely. Once freed, *vechotev lo shtar al chatzi damav* — the formerly enslaved person writes a promissory note to the master for half his monetary value, representing the half that the master effectively surrendered.

### Ravina Applied to Early…

With this context established, the *gemara* explains Ravina's position cleanly. When Ravina ruled that the half-slave-half-free person is exempt from *re'iyah*, he was operating according to the position of the *mishna rishona* — specifically, the original position of *Beit Hillel*, which contemplated this status as a potentially permanent arrangement. Under that view, such a person genuinely exists in a stable half-free state, and his partial servitude plausibly exempts him. But once *Beit Hillel* accepted *Beit Shammai*'s argument and ruled that the *Beit Din* must compel manumission, the intermediate status is no longer tenable. The *mishna achrona* reflects this revised position, and accordingly, the half-slave-half-free person — to the extent the status persists even briefly — is included in the obligation of *korban re'iyah*.

## Get Authentication Rules

### Bifnei Nechtav Rule

A related discussion arises concerning the authentication of a *get*. The question is: when is the declaration *bifnei nechtav u-bifnei nechtam* — "it was written before me and signed before me" — required of the *shaliach* who delivers the *get*?

### Geographic Requirement

This requirement applies when the *shaliach* is traveling between different provinces, or when he is bringing the *get* from *Chutz La'Aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael*, or even from one province to another within *Eretz Yisrael* itself. The concern in all these cases is that the husband may subsequently raise a *irur* — a protest — claiming that the signatures on the *get* are not his or are not those of the witnesses. Because witnesses capable of identifying those signatures may be unavailable once the *shaliach* has traveled such distances, the *shaliach* must affirm the authenticity of the document at the time of delivery.

### Same Province Exception

However, if the *shaliach* delivers the *get* within the same province in which it was written and signed, this declaration is unnecessary. In such a locale, witnesses who can recognize and verify the signatures are readily available, and the risk of an unresolvable *irur* does not arise.

## Number Eight Mysticism

### Eight & Transcendence

The number eight represents transcendence of the physical world — reaching beyond the natural order into something higher. We see this most clearly in *brit milah*, which takes place on the eighth day. The timing is not incidental: we are declaring that the bond between a Jewish child and *Hakadosh Baruch Hu* is something that surpasses the laws of nature, *l'maala min ha'teva*. The same principle appears in *Chanukah*. Among the many possible numbers, eight was chosen because *Chanukah* is precisely the time when we seek to take the *gashmius* of the physical world and elevate it toward *ruchnius* — to lift the material into the spiritual.

### Letter Etymology

There is a striking etymological connection embedded in the Hebrew. The letters of the word *shemonah* — eight — are the same letters as the word *neshamah* — soul — and the word *shemen* — oil. This is not coincidence; these three words share a common inner meaning.

### Oil Metaphor

Oil serves as the metaphor: when you place oil in water, it always rises to the top. It does not mix with the water; it remains separate and elevated. This is precisely what the *neshamah* does in relation to the material world. The aspiration of *shemonah* — of the eighth, of transcendence — is to reach that level where the *neshamah* guides us rather than the natural forces of the *sheva*, the seven, the world of nature that surrounds us.

## David & Teshuvah

### David Mekim Ol

The *gemara* teaches that David was *mekim ol teshuvah* — he was the one who raised and established the yoke of repentance. It is for this reason that *Tehillim* opens with *mizmor l'David*: David's defining contribution was his capacity to take responsibility for his actions, to say *chatati* — I have sinned — and to move forward.

### Shelah on Teshuvah

The *Shelah HaKodesh* offers a profound explanation for why David specifically was chosen to embody and establish *teshuvah*. *Teshuvah*, he explains, is not simply an apology. It is the recognition that even after a fall, even after a genuine setback, a person can rise again and be a *melech* — a king.

### King Defined

A *melech* is not someone who never fails. A *melech* is someone who knows how to deal with failure. Kingship, properly understood, is not defined by an absence of stumbling but by the capacity to rise.

### Humility & Teshuvah

The *Shelah* adds that David HaMelech possessed the specific *midah* of *anavah* — humility — and it was precisely this quality that made *teshuvah* possible for him. Without humility, one cannot admit wrongdoing. Without admitting wrongdoing, *teshuvah* is impossible. The capacity to say "I was wrong" is itself an act of greatness.

### Chatati La'Hashem

When David said *chatati L'Hashem* — I have sinned to God — that was the moment he became truly a *melech*, not merely by appointment but by character. Before that declaration, he held the throne by virtue of anointing. In that moment, he earned it by virtue of who he was.

### Teshuvah Etymology

There is a further etymological teaching: the word *teshuvah* contains within it the word *shabbat*. More precisely, *teshuvah* can be read as *tashuv heh* — return the letter *heh*. This is a deep *kabbalah* concept, but in its simplest formulation it means: return to your source, return to *Hakadosh Baruch Hu*. *Teshuvah* is not merely behavioral correction but a homecoming.

### Malchut Elevation

This is why David HaMelech is so intimately associated with *malchut* — kingship. *Malchut* is the *sefirah* that represents the capacity to take the physical world and elevate it to the spiritual, to take what has fallen and lift it higher. This is the power of *teshuvah me'ahavah* — repentance motivated by love — through which *zedonot*, intentional sins, are transformed into *zechuyot*, merits. That is the legacy David HaMelech bequeathed to us.

## Me'aimasai Time Framework

### Time & Change

The *Sefas Emes* asks a question that cuts to the heart of the entire *Shas*: why does the *mishna* begin with a question of time? *Me'aimasai* — from when? The *Sefas Emes* explains that time is, by definition, the medium of change and transition. A world without change, a state of permanent, undisturbed clarity, would leave no room for *emunah*.

### Faith in Darkness

*Emunah* — faith — is precisely the capacity to remain connected to the Divine even when circumstances are shifting, even when clarity gives way to obscurity, even when one moves from light into darkness. It is not the faith of certainty but the faith of commitment through change.

### Shas Begins Me'aimasai

It is therefore not incidental that the entire *Shas* opens with the word *me'aimasai*. The whole framework of our relationship with *Hashem*, the entire structure of *avodas Hashem*, is situated within time — within a world of constant flux. The question "from when?" is the foundational question of a life lived in relationship with the eternal.

### Finding Consistency

Our task within that changing world is to locate *kevi'us* — consistency, permanence, a point of stable connection to that which does not change. This is the paradox and the challenge of religious life: to find the eternal within the temporal, to anchor oneself in *Hashem* even as everything around shifts.

### Shema in Darkness

When the *mishna* asks *me'aimasai korin et Shema be'aravin* — from when do we recite the *Shema* in the evening — it is telling us something essential: it is specifically in the darkness, specifically in the moment of transition and diminishment, that we must declare *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad*. The recitation of *Shema* is not reserved for clarity and light. It is demanded precisely in the darkness. That is the gateway to everything. That is the beginning of Torah.

## Jacob's Revival Legacy

### Vayechi Meaning

In *Parshas Vayechi* the Torah states: *Vayechi Yaakov b'eretz Mitzrayim* — and Jacob lived in the land of Egypt. The *Chachamim* explain that *vayechi* can be read not only as "he lived" but as "he was revived." After years of grief and suffering, upon being reunited with Yosef, Yaakov found something he had lost.

### Jacob's Peace

What gave Yaakov this sense of renewal and peace? He saw that Yosef was still a *tzadik* — that the years in Egypt had not diminished him spiritually. And he saw that all the *shevatim* were united, that the family had held together.

### Legacy Continuation

From this, Yaakov understood that the legacy of Avraham and Yitzchak would continue — that what had been built and transmitted across generations would not be lost. This realization is itself what it means to truly live.

### Values Transmission

Living, in the deepest sense, is not mere biological existence. It is seeing one's values and one's faith passed forward to the next generation. *Vayechi* — he lived — because he could see the continuity of everything he stood for.

### Jacob's Berachos

It is for this reason that Yaakov gathered his children before his death and gave them *berachos* — blessings that would fortify them for the *galus* ahead. He was not simply distributing inheritance; he was transmitting the spiritual resources they would need to survive exile.

### Connection to Roots

He understood that the only way his children would remain strong through *galus* was if they remained connected to their *shoresh* — their roots. Disconnection from one's source is the great danger of exile; connection to it is the great protection.

### Chazak Chazak

As we conclude *Sefer Bereishis*, this is the message we carry forward: *chazak chazak v'nis-chazek* — we must strengthen ourselves and one another in our *emunah* and in our commitment to Torah. That is what sustains us as a people, in whatever land we find ourselves and through whatever challenges we face.
