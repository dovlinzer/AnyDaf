# Sukkah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fixed Abode & Ideal

### Fixed Sukkah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּתוֹ קֶבַע וּבֵיתוֹ עֲרַאי, כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ לוֹ כֵּלִים נָאִים מַעֲלָן לַסּוּכָּה, מַצָּעוֹת נָאוֹת — מַעֲלָן לַסּוּכָּה, אוֹכֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה וּמְטַיֵּיל בַּסּוּכָּה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״ — כְּעֵין תָּדוּרוּ, מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: כׇּל שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים עוֹשֶׂה אָדָם סוּכָּתוֹ קֶבַע וּבֵיתוֹ עֲרַאי. כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ לוֹ כֵּלִים נָאִים — מַעֲלָן לַסּוּכָּה, מַצָּעוֹת נָאוֹת — מַעֲלָן לַסּוּכָּה, אוֹכֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה וּמְטַיֵּיל בַּסּוּכָּה וּמְשַׁנֵּן בַּסּוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **All seven days** of *Sukkot*, **a person renders his *sukka*** his **permanent** residence **and his house** his **temporary** residence. **How so? If he has beautiful vessels, he takes them up to the *sukka*,** which was typically built on the roof. **If he has beautiful bedding, he takes it up to the *sukka*. He eats and drinks and relaxes in the *sukka*.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara explains that it is **as the Sages taught:** "In *sukkot* **shall you reside"** (Leviticus 23:42), and they interpreted: Reside **as you dwell** in your permanent home. **From here they said: All seven days, a person renders his *sukka*** his **permanent** residence **and his house** his **temporary** residence. **How so? If he has beautiful vessels, he takes them up to the *sukka*;** if he has **beautiful bedding, he takes it up to the *sukka*; he eats and drinks and relaxes in the *sukka* and studies** Torah **in the *sukka*.**

The *gemara* on 28b opens by establishing the foundational principle of how one dwells in the *sukkah* for the seven days of the festival. The *braita* derives this principle from the verse *teshvu* — "you shall dwell seven days in *sukkot*" — which is read through the lens of *ke'ein taduru*: dwell in the *sukkah* as you dwell in your house. From this the Rabbis formulated the rule: *kol shiv'at yamim oseh adam sukkato keva uveito arai* — for all seven days a person makes his *sukkah* fixed and his house temporary. This *braita* parallels an earlier one but adds something important: it anchors the principle in a verse, grounding the halakhic ideal in scriptural obligation rather than mere custom.

### Vessels & Furnishings

The practical translation of *sukkato keva* is spelled out concretely. If one has nice vessels — *kelim na'im* — one brings them up to the *sukkah*. If one has fine bedding — *matzo'ot na'im* — those too belong in the *sukkah*. The image is of relocating the full quality of one's domestic life into the *sukkah* for the duration of the festival. The *sukkah* is not to be furnished like a camping tent but like a proper home.

### Activities in Sukkah

The *braita* continues: *ochel veshotteh umtiyel basukkah* — one eats, drinks, and spends time in the *sukkah*. And then it adds something not present in the earlier formulation: *umeshaneh basukkah* — one even does one's learning in the *sukkah*. The ideal of *ke'ein taduru* extends not only to the physical furnishing of the space but to the full range of human activity that would normally take place in one's home.

## Learning in Sukkah

### Migras vs Iyunei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: מִקְרָא וּמִתְנֵא בִּמְטַלַּלְתָּא, וְתַנּוֹיֵי בַּר מִמְּטַלַּלְתָּא! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּמִגְרַס, הָא — בְּעַיּוֹנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to studying Torah in the *sukka*, the Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rava say: Studying Bible and studying Mishna** are undertaken **in the *sukka*;** however, **analyzing** the Mishna must be undertaken **outside the *sukka*.** This indicates that one should not analyze Torah in the *sukka*. The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, where it was taught that one studies in the *sukka*, is **with regard to extensive study,** i.e., broad study and memorization. **That** statement of Rava that one should study outside the *sukka* is **with regard to intensive study;** such study requires an environment where one can concentrate properly in order to engage in analysis of the Mishna.

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with the requirement to learn in the *sukkah*. The word *umeshaneh* suggests analytical, *talmudic*-style learning — the kind of intensive intellectual engagement one associates with *gemara* study. But doesn't Rava say explicitly: *mikra umishna bimtalulta* — *mikra*, *psukim*, *tanakh*, and *mishna*, the received traditions one recites and memorizes, those belong in the *sukkah* — *vetanuyei bar mimtalulta* — but analytical learning, *iyunei*, one does outside the *sukkah*, in a better environment, perhaps outdoors with fresh air, or in the *beit midrash* where conditions are more conducive to intense concentration? How does Rava's statement square with the *braita*'s injunction to learn in the *sukkah*?

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo kashya — ha bemigras ha bei'yunei*. Even within *gemara* study itself there are two distinct modes. *Migras* refers to memorization and recitation — running through material that has already been analyzed and received. *Iyunei* refers to the analytical work itself, the independent reasoning and probing of the text. The *sukkah* is suitable for the former but not necessarily for the latter.

### Rava & Rami Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא (דְּרַבָּה) בַּר חָמָא כִּי הֲווֹ קָיְימִי מִקַּמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא מַרְהֲטִי בִּגְמָרָא בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וַהֲדַר מְעַיְּינִי בִּסְבָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As** in **that** situation involving Rava and Rami **bar Ḥama, when they would stand before Rav Ḥisda,** after he taught them a *halakha* **they** would **quickly** review **the tradition** that they heard from him **together and** only **then analyze the rationale** of the tradition that they had received. Apparently, in the study of Mishna and the amoraic commentary on the Mishna there is a distinction between extensive and intensive study.

The *gemara* illustrates this distinction with a concrete example: *ki ha deRava veRami bar Chama ki hava kamei deRav Chisda meratzi beGemara bahadi hadadei* — when Rava and Rami bar Chama sat before their teacher Rav Chisda, they would first run through the material together, reviewing and memorizing what had been taught. That was stage one. *VeHadar ma'aynei bisevara* — only after they had the tradition firmly in hand would they then turn to independent analysis, asking what the teacher meant, what objections arose, what implications followed. The memorization phase — even of *gemara* material — is analogous to *mikra* and *mishna* in that it does not demand the same level of environmental focus. The analytical phase does.

### Sukkah as Home

This distinction also need not be framed as a competition between *talmud Torah* and *sukkah* in which *talmud Torah* wins. The more natural framing follows the logic that has governed all the *sukkah* discussions until this point: the *sukkah* is not a prison. Just as one is not required to perform in the *sukkah* activities that one would not normally perform in one's home, so too with learning. If the conditions in the *sukkah* are not conducive to a particular type of study — the kind one would typically pursue in the *beit midrash* or in a quieter setting — then the same reality applies in the *sukkah*. This coheres with the broader principle of *ke'ein taduru*, which defines the *sukkah* by analogy to genuine domestic living, not by an artificial standard of maximal enclosure.

It is worth pausing here to note what is being said. We are not merely discussing the bare minimum *d'oraita* obligation — sleeping and eating a *achilas keva* in the *sukkah*, with its various exemptions for *shomer iro* and *mahalchei drachim*. We are discussing the ideal. And even that ideal is not to be understood in narrow, quantitative terms. The fullest expression of *yoshev basukkah* is to live in the *sukkah* as one lives in one's house — and that means bringing in not only the obligations but the natural rhythms of domestic life, including one's learning, while recognizing that even in one's home there are activities one does elsewhere.

## Vessels & Environment

### Glasses vs Dishes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מָאנֵי מִשְׁתְּיָא — בִּמְטַלַּלְתָּא, מָאנֵי מֵיכְלָא — בַּר מִמְּטַלַּלְתָּא. חַצְבָּא וְשָׁחֵיל — בַּר מִמְּטַלַּלְתָּא, וּשְׁרָגָא — בִּמְטַלַּלְתָּא. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: בַּר מִמְּטַלַּלְתָּא. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא — בְּסוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה, הָא — בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to residence in the *sukka*, **Rava said: Drinking vessels** such as cups, which are usually clean, remain **in the *sukka*. Eating vessels** are taken **out of the *sukka*** after use. **An earthenware jug and a wicker basket [*shaḥil* ]** that are used for drawing water are taken **outside the *sukka*. And a lamp** remains **inside the *sukka*, and some say** it is taken **outside the *sukka*.** The Gemara comments: **And they do not disagree.** Rather, **this** opinion, that a lamp remains inside the *sukka*, is referring **to a large *sukka*,** where the lamp and its odor do not disturb those residing in the *sukka*. And **that** opinion, that the lamp is taken outside the *sukka*, is referring **to a small *sukka*,** where the lamp's odor is offensive.

Rava now turns from activities to the physical setup of the *sukkah* environment. *Manei d'mishtaya* — drinking vessels — *bimatalalta* — may be left in the *sukkah* even after one has finished drinking. They do not become objectionable or disrespectful to the space. *Manei d'michla* — eating vessels, the dishes — *bar mimatalalta* — must be removed from the *sukkah* after the meal. Dirty plates, greasy bowls, and leftover food debris are disrespectful to the sanctity of the *mitzvah*, and beyond that, if the goal is to create an environment one actually wants to inhabit — a genuine domestic space — then clearing the table is simply what one would do in any home after a meal.

### Pitchers & Buckets

Similarly, *chatzba veshichaya* — a pottery pitcher or a wooden bucket — once one has poured from them what one needs, these too should be removed from the *sukkah*. The governing principle in all of these cases is the same: keep the *sukkah* a welcoming and pleasant space. Objects that serve their immediate purpose and then become clutter or eyesores have no place lingering in the *sukkah*.

### Candles Placement

Regarding *sharaga* — the candle — the first opinion holds it should remain *bimatalalta*, inside the *sukkah*. Light encourages one to stay in the *sukkah*. A second opinion says *bar mimatalalta* — the candle should be kept outside. The *gemara* resolves: *lo pligi — ha besukkah gedolah ha besukkah ketanah*. They do not actually disagree. In a large *sukkah*, there is no concern and the candle remains inside. In a small *sukkah*, a lit candle poses a fire risk — one will fear the *sukkah* burning down and will not want to remain inside — and therefore the candle is placed outside. This discussion concerns a lit candle, distinct from the earlier discussions about objects left over after use.

### Welcoming Environment

The cumulative picture painted by these *halakhot* is striking. What would it mean to take this seriously in practice? One would set up the *sukkah* so that it is genuinely comfortable and conducive — proper lighting, furnishings that make it inviting, cleared dishes after meals, and an atmosphere that makes one want to remain. If one would read a novel at home, one reads it in the *sukkah*. If one would set up a fan or a portable air conditioner to manage the heat, that belongs in the *sukkah* too. The *mitzvah* does not demand austerity; it demands authenticity — that the *sukkah* actually function as one's home.

One powerful dimension of this is the interplay between the *sukkah* as home and the *sukkah* as a place of divine presence — the *ananei hakavod*. The model in the background is the *mishkan* and the seven days of *miluim*, when the divine presence hovered over the space in which Aaron and his sons dwelt. The insight, developed at length by Rabbi Yaakov Nagen in his work on the religious themes of Masechet Sukkah, is that the *sukkah* represents the merging of two realities that are ordinarily separate: one's home and the place of the divine presence. The *beit hamikdash* is where one goes to encounter God; home is where one lives. The *sukkah* collapses that distinction. And therefore bringing the full texture of domestic life — including the comfortable, the quotidian, even the intimate — into the *sukkah* is not a compromise of its sacred character but its fullest realization.

## Rain & Discomfort

### Spoiled Porridge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים. תָּנָא: מִשֶּׁתִּסְרַח הַמִּקְפָּה שֶׁל גְּרִיסִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: If **rain fell,** it is permitted to leave the *sukka* from the point that it is raining so hard that the congealed dish will spoil. **It was taught** in the *Tosefta*: The measure is **from when a congealed dish of pounded grain,** a dish ruined by even slight rainfall, **will spoil.**

The *gemara* turns to the question of when rain compels one to leave the *sukkah*. A *tana* teaches: *mishetyisrach hamikpah* — once the porridge, made of *grisin* (split beans or similar pounded grain, a dish that spoils quickly), is spoiled by the rain falling into it, one may go inside. This is the threshold: not the first drops, but a degree of rain that meaningfully disrupts the meal.

One might assume this flows naturally from the principle that a *mitzta'er* — someone experiencing genuine discomfort — is exempt from the *sukkah*, a principle attributed to Rava. But there is a subtlety worth noting. The *mishnah* does not use the language of *tza'ar* at all. The reason may be different: it is not merely that the person is personally distressed, but that a house with rain pouring through its roof is no longer a domicile in any meaningful sense. A leaky roof may be closer to a condemned building than to a home, and the *sukkah* then loses its identity as the kind of dwelling that *ke'ein taduru* requires. Rava's principle of *mitzta'er patur min hasukkah* may therefore be a distinct *chiddush*, addressing personal subjective distress rather than the objective habitability of the *sukkah* itself.

### Rav Yosef Fastidious

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה קָא יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בִּמְטַלַּלְתָּא, נְשַׁב זִיקָא וְקָא מַיְיתֵי צִיבְוָתָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב יוֹסֵף: פַּנּוּ לִי מָאנַי מֵהָכָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָא תְּנַן: מִשֶּׁתִּסְרַח הַמִּקְפָּה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִידִי, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲנִינָא דַּעְתַּאי — כְּמִי שֶׁתִּסְרַח הַמִּקְפָּה דָּמֵי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye was sitting before Rav Yosef in the *sukka*. The wind blew and brought** with it **splinters** from the roofing, and they fell onto the food. **Rav Yosef said to him: Vacate my vessels from here,** and I will eat in the house. **Abaye said to him: Didn't we learn** in the mishna that one remains in the *sukka* **until the congealed dish will spoil?** That is not yet the case. **He said to him: For me, since I am delicate,** this situation **is as if the congealed dish will spoil.**

The *gemara* relates: *Abaye hava yatav kamei deRav Yosef bimtalalta* — Abaye was sitting before his teacher Rav Yosef in the *sukkah*. A wind blew — *nashav zika* — and material from the *schach* began falling into the food and onto the people below. Rav Yosef said: *pinu li manei meihacha* — remove my belongings; we are going inside. Abaye questioned him: but we learned that only once the porridge has spoiled may one leave — surely small debris from the *schach* falling has not reached that threshold? Rav Yosef replied: *lididi kivan d'aninai da'ati — k'mishetitroach hamikpah dami* — for me, since I am so fastidious, so sensitive, it is equivalent to the porridge having spoiled.

This is an important statement. The criterion for *mitzta'er* is not purely objective, applied uniformly to all people. A person who is genuinely *aninei da'at* — particularly sensitive to disturbances in their environment — may reach the threshold of intolerable discomfort at a point that others would not. Rashi connects *aninei da'at* to a passage in Pesachim identifying three types of people whose lives are not truly lives: those who are overly empathetic, those who are easily angered, and those who are so fastidious that the slightest change in their environment distresses them. Rav Yosef identifies himself as having all three of those qualities.

This also shifts the discussion. The *mishnah*'s rain case may have been about the *sukkah*'s objective status as a domicile. Rav Yosef and Abaye, however, interpret the *halakha* more in terms of personal *mitzta'er* — and once it is personal, it becomes inherently more subjective. The disruption here is not even rain but debris from the *schach*, and yet it qualifies because for Rav Yosef it is genuinely intolerable.

This raises a broader question *l'halacha*: if a *sukkah* is regularly invaded by mosquitoes, or is located in a climate so cold that sleeping in it is never truly feasible, does the recurring *mitzta'er* affect not only the obligation in those moments but the identity of the *sukkah* itself? Could one argue that a space regularly unfit for habitation cannot truly be called a domicile? The tendency in *halakhic* practice has been to resist that conclusion — to evaluate each moment independently, granting exemption when *mitzta'er* is present and obligating when it is not, rather than allowing situational unfitness to undermine the *sukkah*'s status altogether.

The observation that the Talmud's climate is the Mediterranean context of *Eretz Yisrael* — where *Sukkot* falls at the transition from the dry season to the rainy season, and where rain carries deep theological weight — is relevant here. When communities moved northward to Germany, France, and eventually Poland, where the nights of *Sukkot* could be genuinely freezing, questions arose. The Rama records that the *minhag* is not to sleep in the *sukkah*, and the Mishnah Berurah addresses how much accumulation of snow inside the *sukkah* would be needed before one may leave. The question of whether the climate renders such a *sukkah* permanently unfit as a domicile remains a live theoretical concern.

### Continuity Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה אוֹכֵל בַּסּוּכָּה וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים וְיָרַד — אֵין מַטְרִיחִין אוֹתוֹ לַעֲלוֹת עַד שֶׁיִּגְמוֹר סְעוּדָּתוֹ. הָיָה יָשֵׁן תַּחַת הַסּוּכָּה וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים וְיָרַד — אֵין מַטְרִיחִין אוֹתוֹ לַעֲלוֹת עַד שֶׁיֵּאוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If **one was eating in the *sukka*, and rain fell,** and **he descended** from the *sukka* on the roof to eat in his house, **one does not burden him to ascend** back to the *sukka* once the rain ceases **until** after **he finishes his meal.** Similarly, if **one was sleeping under** the roofing of **the *sukka*, and rain fell, and he descended** to sleep in the house, **one does not burden him to ascend** back to the *sukka* once the rain ceases; rather, he may sleep in the house **until it becomes light.**

The *gemara* provides further *halakhot* regarding continuity once one has lawfully left the *sukkah*. *Haya ochel basukkah veyardu geshamim veyarad* — one is eating in the *sukkah*, it begins to rain, and one goes inside. Then the rain stops. *Ein matrikhin oto la'alot ad sheyigmor seudato* — one need not return to the *sukkah* to complete the meal. Once the exemption permitted departure, one may finish eating where one is.

Note that the *sukkah* is described as being on the rooftop — hence the language of going "down" — echoing the earlier passage about *he'alum lasukkah*, bringing things "up" to the *sukkah*. The basis of this leniency may be the concept of *mitzta'er* — interrupting a meal midway would itself be a *tza'ar* — or it may follow from *ke'ein taduru*: one does not ordinarily change locations in the middle of a meal, and so since one was legitimately where one is, one may remain there.

The same principle applies to sleep. *Haya yashen tachat hasukkah veyardu geshamim veyarad* — one is sleeping in the *sukkah* and rain comes and one goes inside. *Ein matrikhin oto la'alot* — and then it stops. Must one return? The *gemara* raises a textual question: is the limit *ad sheyei'or* — with an *ayin* — until one wakes up, or *ad sheyei'or* — with an *aleph* — until it becomes light? The distinction matters: does the exemption expire only when the person themselves wakes, or does it expire at daybreak regardless?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עַד שֶׁיֵּעוֹר אוֹ עַד שֶׁיֵּאוֹר? תָּא שְׁמַע: עַד שֶׁיֵּאוֹר וְיַעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר. תַּרְתֵּי?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: עַד שֶׁיֵּעוֹר וְיַעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is the correct reading of the *baraita*: **Until one awakens [*sheyeor*],** spelled with an *ayin*, and once he awakens he returns to the *sukka* even in the middle of the night? Or is the correct reading: **Until it becomes light [*sheyeor*],** spelled with an *alef*, and he need not return to the *sukka* until morning? **Come** and **hear** a proof that will resolve the matter from a related *baraita*: One need not return to the *sukka* **until it becomes light [*sheyeor*],** spelled with an *alef*, **and dawn** arrives. The Gemara asks: Why did the *baraita* repeat the arrival of light **two** times (Ritva)? **Rather, say** instead: **Until he awakens [*sheyeor*],** spelled with an *ayin*, **and the dawn** arrives. Both of the readings are accurate, as until one awakens and it becomes light he may remain in the house.

A *braita* provides both markers together: *ad sheyei'or veya'aleh amud hashachar* — until one wakes and the morning star rises. The *gemara* reads these conjunctively: the exemption holds until both conditions are met — the person has woken and dawn has arrived. Waking in the middle of the night does not obligate returning; and if it is already morning but one remains asleep, no one need rouse them. Only once one has woken of one's own accord and it is genuinely morning must one return to the *sukkah*. The analogy to ordinary life is apt: if one moved to a neighbor's home during construction and was told at two in the morning that the work was done, one would not pack up immediately — one would return in the morning.

## Rain Theology

### Master Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מִי שָׁפַךְ לְמִי? תָּא שְׁמַע דְּתַנְיָא: שָׁפַךְ לוֹ רַבּוֹ קִיתוֹן עַל פָּנָיו, וְאָמַר לוֹ: אִי אֶפְשִׁי בְּשִׁמּוּשֶׁךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: The Sages **told a parable: To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable to a servant who comes to pour wine for his master, and he pours a jug of water in his face. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Who poured** the water **in whose** face? **Come** and **hear** a proof, **as it is taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: **His master poured a jug** of water **on his face and said to him: I do not want your service.**

The *gemara* now addresses the theological significance of rain falling in the *sukkah*. *Mashal l'mah hadavar domeh* — a parable: a servant comes to pour for the master, and the pitcher of water is spilled on the master's face.

### Two Interpretations

The question the *gemara* poses is *mi shafach l'mi* — who is spilling the water on whom? Two readings present themselves. In the first, the servant is coming to perform the *mitzvah* — to pour for the master — and trips, causing the water to spill on the master's face. The theological message would be that one has come to perform the *mitzvah* but has somehow made a mess of it, and God is displeased. In the second reading, and the one that more naturally fits the imagery of water being thrown at a face, the master himself takes the pitcher and throws the water at the servant: *shafach lo rabbo kiton al panav v'amar lo ee efshi beshimushecha* — the master spills the water on the servant's face and says, "I have no interest in your service." This is a powerful image of rejection. One comes to perform the *mitzvah*, and God turns one away. The first interpretation attributes the problem to some failure on the servant's part; the second frames it as God's expression of anger for unrelated reasons.

### God's Presence

Why, one might ask, would rain be interpreted with such theological weight, in a way we would not apply to, say, difficulty finding a *lulav*? Two factors converge. First, as noted, rain during *Sukkot* arrives precisely at the transition to the rainy season in *Eretz Yisrael*, a time already laden with theological meaning — the time of *mashiv haruach umorid hagashem*, when rain carries the weight of divine favor or its withdrawal. Second, the nature of the *mitzvah* itself: activities like *tefillah*, blowing *shofar*, and dwelling under the *ananei hakavod* are modes of direct encounter with the divine. When something goes wrong in those moments — when the *ba'al tokeiah* cannot produce a sound, when rain drives one from the *sukkah* — we are already primed to read these events theologically. The image of the *sukkah* as standing under God's presence, under the *ananei hakavod*, means that being expelled from the *sukkah* by rain is being expelled from the master's presence. "Get out of my place." That reading lends the *mashal* its force.

## Eclipses & Signs

### Solar Eclipse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַחַמָּה לוֹקָה — סִימָן רַע לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁעָשָׂה סְעוּדָּה לַעֲבָדָיו וְהִנִּיחַ פָּנָס לִפְנֵיהֶם, כָּעַס עֲלֵיהֶם וְאָמַר לְעַבְדּוֹ: טוֹל פָּנָס מִפְּנֵיהֶם וְהוֹשִׁיבֵם בַּחוֹשֶׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the fact that rain on *Sukkot* is an indication of divine rebuke, the Gemara cites several related topics. **The Sages taught: When the sun is eclipsed it is a bad omen for the entire world.** The Gemara tells **a parable. To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a king of flesh and blood who prepared a feast for his servants and placed a lantern [*panas*] before them** to illuminate the hall. **He became angry at them and said to his servant: Take the lantern from before them and seat them in darkness.**

The *gemara* expands its theological reading of natural phenomena beyond rain to the far rarer and more dramatic events of celestial affliction. *Bizman shehachamah lokah* — when the sun is smitten, meaning an eclipse, partial or total — *siman ra l'chol ha'olam* — it is a bad sign for the entire world. The sun is universal; it belongs to everyone. The parable offered: a human king makes a feast for his servants, places a lantern before them, and then in anger commands his servant to remove the lantern and leave them sitting in darkness. The Maharsha connects this to the *midrash* on creation: God laid out the feast of creation for humanity, the sun and moon and stars set in place with the declaration *vehayu leotot* — they shall be for signs. When God removes the light, it is a statement about the entire created order.

### Lunar Eclipse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמְּאוֹרוֹת לוֹקִין — סִימָן רַע לְשׂוֹנְאֵיהֶם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּלוּמָּדִין בְּמַכּוֹתֵיהֶן. מָשָׁל לְסוֹפֵר שֶׁבָּא לְבֵית הַסֵּפֶר וּרְצוּעָה בְּיָדוֹ, מִי דּוֹאֵג — מִי שֶׁרָגִיל לִלְקוֹת בְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם הוּא דּוֹאֵג.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says: When the** heavenly **lights,** i.e., the sun and the moon, **are eclipsed, it is a bad omen for the enemies of the Jewish people,** which is a euphemism for the Jewish people, **because they are experienced in their beatings.** Based on past experience, they assume that any calamity that afflicts the world is directed at them. The Gemara suggests **a parable:** This is similar **to a teacher who comes to the school with a strap in his hand. Who worries?** The child **who is accustomed to be beaten each and every day is** the one who **worries.**

A *braita* presents Rabbi Meir's teaching: *kol zeman shehamaorot lokim* — whenever the *maorot* are afflicted — here referring not to the sun but to the lesser luminaries, primarily the moon — *siman ra leson'eihem shel Yisrael* — it is a bad sign for the Jewish people, expressed euphemistically. Why specifically the Jewish people? *Mipnei shemelumadim bemakoteihem* — because they are accustomed to being smitten; they know that when trouble comes to the world, they are often the ones it falls upon. The parable: a schoolteacher enters the classroom with a strap in hand. Which student begins to worry? The one who gets whipped every day. It is a somber metaphor.

The Maharsha softens this reading by pointing out that a teacher only disciplines children from whom discipline can produce some good. The child who is a lost cause is ignored. The implication is that Israel's suffering carries the possibility of *teshuvah* and growth — it is not mere punishment but purposeful correction directed at those who have the capacity to respond. The connection between lunar eclipses and Israel also has an astronomical dimension worth noting: since the major festivals — Pesach, Sukkot — fall on the fifteenth of the month, lunar eclipses at full moon naturally tend to cluster around these dates.

### Eclipse Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַחַמָּה לוֹקָה — סִימָן רַע לַגּוֹיִם. לְבָנָה לוֹקָה — סִימָן רַע לְשׂוֹנְאֵיהֶם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל מוֹנִין לַלְּבָנָה, וְגוֹיִם לַחַמָּה. לוֹקָה בַּמִּזְרָח — סִימָן רַע לְיוֹשְׁבֵי מִזְרָח. בַּמַּעֲרָב — סִימָן רַע לְיוֹשְׁבֵי מַעֲרָב. בְּאֶמְצַע הָרָקִיעַ — סִימָן רַע לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in another *baraita*: **When the sun is eclipsed, it is a bad omen for the** other **nations.** When **the moon is eclipsed, it is a bad omen for the enemies of the Jewish people.** This is **due to** the fact **that the Jewish people calculate** their calendar primarily based **on the moon, and the** other **nations** calculate based **on the sun.** When the sun is **eclipsed in the east, it is a bad omen for the residents** of the lands of **the east.** When it is eclipsed **in the west, it is a bad omen for the residents** of the lands of **the west.** When it is eclipsed **in the middle of the sky, it is a bad omen for the entire world.**

The *gemara* continues with further specifications. *Laka bamizrach* — if the eclipse occurs when the luminary is in the east — *siman ra leyoshvei mizrach* — it is a bad sign specifically for those who dwell in the east. *Bamaarav* — if in the west — it is a bad sign for those in the west. *Beemtza harakia* — if it occurs when the luminary is in the middle of the sky — *siman ra lechol haolam kulo* — it is bad for the entire world.

### Color & Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּנָיו דּוֹמִין לְדָם — חֶרֶב בָּא לָעוֹלָם. לְשַׂק — חִיצֵּי רָעָב בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם. לָזוֹ וְלָזוֹ — חֶרֶב וְחִיצֵּי רָעָב בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם. לָקָה בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ — פּוּרְעָנוּת שׁוֹהָה לָבֹא. בִּיצִיאָתוֹ — מְמַהֶרֶת לָבֹא. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If, during an eclipse, **the visage** of the sun **is** red **like blood,** it is an omen that **sword,** i.e., war, **is coming to the world.** If the sun **is** black **like sackcloth** made of dark goat hair, it is an omen that **arrows of hunger are coming to the world,** because hunger darkens people's faces. When it is similar both **to this,** to blood, **and to that,** to sackcloth, it is a sign that both **sword and arrows of hunger are coming to the world.** If it was **eclipsed upon its entry,** soon after rising, it is an omen that **calamity is tarrying to come.** If the sun is eclipsed **upon its departure** at the end of the day, it is an omen that **calamity is hastening to come. And some say the matters are reversed:** An eclipse in the early morning is an omen that calamity is hastening, while an eclipse in the late afternoon is an omen that calamity is tarrying.

Color matters as well. *Panav domim ledam* — if the face of the afflicted luminary appears blood-red — *cherev ba laolam* — it signals war, the redness of blood. *Lesak* — if it takes on the ashen gray of sackcloth — *chizei raav ba'in laolam* — famine is coming. Rashi's reading: the gray-black color suggests the darkened face of fasting. The Maharsha reads the sackcloth as referencing what is contained in such a sack — grain — and thus the absence of grain. A reddish-and-sacklike appearance together signals both war and famine.

Timing within the day also carries meaning. *Laka bechnisato* — if the eclipse strikes when the luminary is setting — *puranut shoah lavo* — the affliction is slow to arrive. *Beyetziato* — if it strikes when the luminary is rising — *memaheret lavo* — it is imminent. Some reverse this: Rashi explains the alternate view that an eclipse at setting, leaving no time remaining in the day, signals something coming swiftly with no time to spare.

### Lesser Deities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לְךָ כׇּל אוּמָּה וְאוּמָּהּ שֶׁלּוֹקָה, שֶׁאֵין אֱלֹהֶיהָ לוֹקֶה עִמָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְכׇל אֱלֹהֵי מִצְרַיִם אֶעֱשֶׂה שְׁפָטִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages said: **There is no nation that is afflicted whose god is not afflicted with it, as it is stated: "And against all the gods of Egypt I will mete out judgment; I am God"** (Exodus 12:12).

Finally, a sweeping principle: *ve'ein lecha kol uma vauma sheloka sheein eloheiha loka ima* — there is no nation that is struck without its presiding deity being struck as well. The proof text: *uvchol elohei mitzrayim eeseh shefatim* — "with all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments." The idea is that the forces governing the nations below — understood as angels or lesser celestial powers — are themselves afflicted when their nations suffer. The celestial events thus reflect disturbances in both the earthly and the heavenly order simultaneously.

## Righteous Protected

### God's Will Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם אֵין מִתְיָרְאִין מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ אֶל דֶּרֶךְ הַגּוֹיִם אַל תִּלְמָדוּ וּמֵאוֹתוֹת הַשָּׁמַיִם אַל תֵּחָתּוּ כִּי יֵחַתּוּ הַגּוֹיִם מֵהֵמָּה״ — גּוֹיִם יֵחַתּוּ, וְאֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל יֵחַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **When the Jewish people perform God's will, they** need **not fear any of these** omens, **as it is stated: "Thus says the Lord: Learn not the way of the nations, and be not dismayed at the signs of Heaven; for the nations are dismayed at them"** (Jeremiah 10:2). **The nations will be dismayed, but the Jewish people will not be dismayed,** provided they do not follow the ways of the nations.

Having described the threatening significance of celestial afflictions, the *gemara* introduces its crucial qualification. *Uvizman sheyisrael osim retzono shel Makom* — when the Jewish people do the will of God — *ein mityar'im mikol elu* — they need not fear any of these signs. The signs operate within the domain of natural-metaphysical forces, but those who are connected to God transcend that domain. The *gemara* invokes the famous tradition about Avraham: when Avraham complained that he saw in the stars that he would have no children, God "took him outside" — *vayotze oto hachutzah* — lifting him beyond the influence of the constellations. Those who are not doing God's will remain subject to these forces; those who do God's will are lifted above them.

### Separation Principle

The *gemara* cites a verse to anchor this: *koh amar Hashem el derech hagoyim al tilmadu — umeotot hashamayim al tichatu* — "Thus says God: do not learn from the ways of the nations, and do not be broken by the signs of heaven." *Ki yeichatu hagoyim mehemah* — let the nations be broken by them; those who separate themselves from the ways of the nations need not fear the signs. *Ovdei kochavim yeichatu ve'ein Yisrael yeichatu* — it is something for idolaters to worry about, not for Israel, when Israel is doing God's will.

## Sins & Afflictions

### Four Solar Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים חַמָּה לוֹקָה: עַל אָב בֵּית דִּין שֶׁמֵּת וְאֵינוֹ נִסְפָּד כַּהֲלָכָה, וְעַל נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה שֶׁצָּעֲקָה בָּעִיר וְאֵין מוֹשִׁיעַ לָהּ, וְעַל מִשְׁכַּב זְכוּר, וְעַל שְׁנֵי אַחִין שֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָן כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that **on account of four matters the sun is eclipsed: On** account of **a president of the court who dies and is not eulogized appropriately,** and the eclipse is a type of eulogy by Heaven; **on** account of **a betrothed young woman who screamed in the city** that she was being raped **and there was no one to rescue her; on** account of **homosexuality; and on** account of **two brothers whose blood was spilled as one.**

The *gemara* now enumerates the specific sins that bring about these celestial afflictions. *Bishvil arba'ah devarim chamah lokah* — for four things the sun is smitten. Rashi acknowledges that he does not see a clear reason why these particular sins are matched to solar affliction specifically, but proceeds with the list. First: *al av beit din shemet ve'eino nispad kahalacha* — the head of the *beit din* dies and is not mourned as is fitting, reflecting a failure to value the institution of a functioning legal system. Second: *al naara me'orasa shetzakah la'ir ve'ein moshia lah* — a betrothed maiden who cries out in the city and no one comes to her aid, an evocation of the verse in the Torah describing rape in the city and the failure of those nearby to intervene, a breakdown of public accountability for justice. Third: *al mishkav zachor* — on male homosexuality. Fourth: *al shnei achim shenishpach damam ke'echad* — two brothers whose blood is shed simultaneously. The first two cases connect readily to themes of judicial failure and communal responsibility; the latter two are harder to integrate into a single coherent framework.

### Four Lunar Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים מְאוֹרוֹת לוֹקִין: עַל כּוֹתְבֵי פְלַסְתֵּר, וְעַל מְעִידֵי עֵדוּת שֶׁקֶר, וְעַל מְגַדְּלֵי בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְעַל קוֹצְצֵי אִילָנוֹת טוֹבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And on account of four matters the** heavenly **lights** are **eclipsed: On** account of **forgers of a fraudulent document [*pelaster*]** that is intended to discredit others; **on** account of **testifiers of false testimony; on** account of **raisers of small domesticated animals in Eretz Yisrael** in a settled area; **and on** account of **choppers of good,** fruit-producing **trees.**

*Uvishvil arba devarim hame'orot lokin* — for four things the luminaries, primarily the moon, are afflicted, relating to Israel's failures. First: *al kotvei plastar* — people who write falsehood, which Rashi glosses as either false documents or libelous writings. Second: *al me'idei edut sheker* — people who give false testimony, continuing the theme of dishonesty in civic life. Third: *al megadlei behemah dakah be'Eretz Yisrael* — those who raise small livestock in *Eretz Yisrael*, because grazing animals destroy the vegetation of the land, causing harm to the communal environment. Fourth: *al kotzei ilanot tovot* — those who cut down good trees, which Rashi says applies even to trees one owns, because it destroys the beauty of the world God has given. The connection among these four is not immediately obvious; there may be a loose theme of falsehood and betrayal in the first two and wanton destruction in the latter two.

### Four Seizure Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים נִכְסֵי בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים נִמְסָרִין לַמַּלְכוּת: עַל מַשְׁהֵי שְׁטָרוֹת פְּרוּעִים, וְעַל מַלְוֵי בְּרִבִּית, וְעַל שֶׁהָיָה סִפֵּק בְּיָדָם לִמְחוֹת וְלֹא מִיחוּ, וְעַל שֶׁפּוֹסְקִים צְדָקָה בָּרַבִּים וְאֵינָן נוֹתְנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And on account of four matters the property of homeowners is delivered to the monarchy** as punishment: **On** account of those **keepers of paid** promissory **notes,** who keep these documents instead of tearing them or returning them to the borrowers, as that would allow the lender to collect money with the note a second time; **and on** account of **lenders with interest; and on** account of **those who had the ability to reprimand** sinners **and did not reprimand** them; **and on** account of **those who issued** a commitment to give **charity in public and** ultimately **do not give** the charity to which they committed.

*Bishvil arba devarim nichsei ba'alei batim nimsarin la'malchut* — for four reasons the property of wealthy homeowners is seized by the government. First: *al mash'ei shetarot pru'im* — holding promissory notes that have already been repaid, perhaps to collect twice or to use as leverage in litigation — even if one does not intend outright theft, the Maharsha notes, the retention of such documents is a form of extortion. Second: *al malvei b'ribit* — lending with *ribit*, charging interest in violation of Torah law. Third: *al she'haya sfeika b'yadam limchot v'lo michu* — when they had the standing and influence to protest wrongdoing and failed to use it; a person of wealth and authority who remains silent when they could speak is culpable. Fourth: *al she'poskim tzedakah b'rabim v'einam notnim* — those who make public pledges of charity and do not fulfill them, a betrayal of the public trust they cultivated.

A parallel teaching from Rav:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: בִּשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים נִכְסֵי בַּעֲלֵי בָּתִּים יוֹצְאִין לְטִמְיוֹן: עַל כּוֹבְשֵׁי שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר, וְעַל עוֹשְׁקֵי שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר, וְעַל שֶׁפּוֹרְקִין עוֹל מֵעַל צַוְּארֵיהֶן וְנוֹתְנִין עַל חַבְרֵיהֶן, וְעַל גַּסּוּת הָרוּחַ. וְגַסּוּת הָרוּחַ — כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: On account of four matters the property of homeowners is confiscated by the state treasury [*timyon*]: On** account of **those who delay** payment of **the salary of hired laborers** (see Leviticus 19:13; Deuteronomy 24:15); **on** account of **those who withhold the salary of hired laborers** and do not pay at all; **and on** account of **those who throw off the yoke** of communal responsibility **from their** own **necks and place** that yoke **on** the necks of **their friends; and on** account of **the arrogance** of those who, due to their wealth, exercise power over the community. **And the** punishment for **arrogance is equal to them all.**

*Bishvil arba devarim nichsei ba'alei batim yotzin l'timyon* — for four reasons the property of wealthy people is not merely seized by the government but simply disappears, swallowed up entirely: *al kovshei sachar sachir* — withholding wages from hired workers; *al oshkei sachar sachir* — an even greater degree, outright denial of their funds; *al she'porkin ol me'al tzavareihem v'notnim al chavreihem* — whenever obligations arise, they shed them and place them on others; and *al gasut haruach* — arrogance.

### Arrogance Root

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בַּעֲנָוִים כְּתִיב: ״וַעֲנָוִים יִירְשׁוּ אָרֶץ וְהִתְעַנְּגוּ עַל רֹב שָׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** However, **with regard to the humble it is written: "The humble will inherit the land and delight themselves in the abundance of peace"** (Psalms 37:11).

*V'gasut haruach k'neged kulam* — and arrogance outweighs all of them together. The Maharsha explains this not as a simple ranking but as a causal claim: arrogance is probably the underlying root from which all the other transgressions listed flow. The wealthy man who thinks himself above accountability — who says "I am wealthy, I can do what I want" — is the one who withholds wages, charges interest, sheds responsibility, and ignores the suffering of others. All of these behaviors are expressions of the same foundational *gasut haruach*.

The *gemara* concludes this section with a counterpoint: *aval ba'anavim ktiv — v'anavim yirshu aretz v'hit'angu al rov shalom* — but regarding the humble it is written: "The meek shall inherit the earth, and they shall delight in abundant peace." This verse from Tehillim — which is indeed the source of the famous phrase — carries a reassurance about the ultimate order of things: those who amass wealth through transgression and arrogance will lose it, while those who act with integrity will in the end receive their portion.

## Lulav & Etrog

### Stolen & Dried

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לוּלָב הַגָּזוּל וְהַיָּבֵשׁ — פָּסוּל. שֶׁל אֲשֵׁירָה וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת — פָּסוּל. נִקְטַם רֹאשׁוֹ, נִפְרְצוּ עָלָיו — פָּסוּל. נִפְרְדוּ עָלָיו — כָּשֵׁר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יַאַגְדֶנּוּ מִלְמַעְלָה. צִינֵי הַר הַבַּרְזֶל — כְּשֵׁירוֹת. לוּלָב שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים כְּדֵי לְנַעְנֵעַ בּוֹ — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A *lulav* that was stolen or that is** completely **dry** is **unfit** for use in fulfilling the mitzva of the four species. The *lulav* **of a tree worshipped as idolatry [*asheira*] and** a *lulav* **from a city** whose residents were **incited** to idolatry, which must be burned along with all the city's property, are **unfit. If the top** of the *lulav* **was severed or if the** palm **leaves were severed** from the spine of the *lulav*, it is **unfit. If its leaves,** although still attached, **were spread** and are no longer completely joined to the spine, it is **fit. Rabbi Yehuda says:** In that case, **one should bind** the *lulav* **from the top,** to join the leaves that spread to the spine. A *lulav* from the **palms of the Iron Mountain** are **fit** for use, although it differs from one taken from a standard palm tree, in that its leaves are shorter and do not cover the entire spine. **A *lulav* that has three handbreadths** in length, **sufficient** to enable one **to wave with it, is fit** for use in fulfilling the mitzva.

With *Hadran alach haya yashen*, the *gemara* concludes the second *perek* and opens the third, which is devoted in its entirety to the *lulav* and *etrog* and the *arba minim*. The structure mirrors the opening of the tractate: just as the first *perek* began not with the *mitzvah* of dwelling in the *sukkah* but with the physical qualities that render the *sukkah* fit or unfit, so this *perek* begins not with the *mitzvah* of taking the *lulav* and *etrog* but with the physical properties that make them *kosher* or *pasul*.

The *mishnah* opens: *lulav hagazul vehayavesh pasul* — a stolen *lulav* or a dried-up *lulav* is invalid.

### Avodah Zarah Trees

*Shel asherah v'shel ir hanidachat* — if the *lulav* comes from an *asherah*, a tree that was the object of idol worship, or from the trees of a condemned city — *ir hanidachat*, a city whose inhabitants worshipped *avodah zarah* collectively and whose property is therefore condemned to destruction — it is *pasul*. The underlying principle is that an object that is under a verdict of destruction has, in a sense, no standing — it is as though it no longer legally exists — and therefore cannot serve as the vehicle for a *mitzvah*.

### Cut/Spread Leaves

*Niktam rosho* — if the *lulav*'s head, the central spine, is cut off — *pasul*. *Nifretzu alav* — if the leaves are spread, meaning, according to Rashi, that they have been removed from the spine and reattached, or according to Tosafos, that the double leaves have all been split open along their middle crease — *pasul*. *Nifredu alav* — if the leaves fan outward from the center without being split or removed, this is actually *kasher*; Rabbi Yehudah says one should nevertheless flatten them and bind them toward the spine so it looks more whole and beautiful, but it remains valid as is. The *lulav* from the iron mountain — *tzinai har habarzel* — which has naturally short leaves, is *kasherah*.

### Height Requirement

Finally: *lulav sheyesh bo shlosha tefachim kedei lena'anea bo kasher* — a *lulav* that has three *tefachim*, sufficient to shake, is *kosher*. The *gemara* will interpret this to require three *tefachim* plus an additional *tefach* to enable shaking, yielding an effective minimum of four *tefachim*.

## Hadar Principle

### Rashi on Hadar

What is the underlying basis for these invalidities? Why does a dried *lulav* fail, or a *lulav* whose spine is cut? Rashi's formulation is: *deba'inan mitzvah mehuderet* — we require the *mitzvah* to be performed beautifully — *dikesiv ve'anveihu* — as it is written "this is my God and I will glorify him," the principle of *zeh keili ve'anveihu* that one should make the performance of *mitzvot* beautiful. Notice that Rashi himself uses the word *mehuderet*, borrowed from the root *hadar*, signaling that he is drawing on the very concept the verse in the *arba minim* passage encodes.

### Tosafos on Hadar

Tosafos raises an objection: ordinarily, the principle of *noy mitzvah* — beautifying a *mitzvah* — does not invalidate the *mitzvah* if not observed. One who performs a *mitzvah* without aesthetic enhancement has still fulfilled the obligation. Why should lack of beauty *pasul* the *lulav*? Tosafos therefore grounds the invalidity not in the general principle of *ve'anveihu* but in the specific verse *peri etz hadar* — "the fruit of a beautiful tree" — where the word *hadar* is part of the Torah's own definition of what the object must be. The requirement of aesthetic integrity is not an add-on enhancement but a constitutive element of the *mitzvah* itself.

Rashi likely does not disagree with this; his language of *mitzvah mehuderet* is itself a usage of *hadar*. The practical difference is that Rashi applies *hadar* to all four of the *arba minim* through the general language of *ve'anveihu*, while Tosafos prefers to anchor it directly in the verse *peri etz hadar*. In either case, the governing principle is the same: *hadar* — beauty and aesthetic integrity — is a substantive halakhic requirement that can render the *arba minim* invalid, not merely a recommendation for enhancement.

## First vs Other Days

### Chol HaMoed Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָא פָסֵיק וְתָנֵי, לָא שְׁנָא בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן, וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The ruling in the mishna is that a stolen *lulav* is unfit. The Gemara posits: **The mishna teaches** this *halakha* unequivocally, indicating that there is **no difference** whether the stolen *lulav* is used **on the first day of the festival** of *Sukkot*, when taking the four species is a mitzva by Torah law, **and** there is **no difference** whether the stolen *lulav* is used beginning **on the second day of the festival** of *Sukkot*, when it is a mitzva by rabbinic law.

The *gemara* observes that the *mishnah* presents these invalidities categorically — *kapaska vetani* — without distinguishing between the first day of *Yom Tov* and subsequent days. *Lo shna beyom tov rishon velo shna beyom tov sheni* — it makes no distinction. The *rishonim* understand "second day" here to refer primarily to *Chol HaMoed* rather than the second day *Yom Tov* of the diaspora. Does the same standard of invalidity apply on *Chol HaMoed* as on the first day? The question has force because the first day obligation is *d'oraita* — *u'lekachtem lachem bayom harishon* — while the taking of the *arba minim* on *Chol HaMoed* is only rabbinic, a *zecher lemikdash*, a commemoration of the Temple practice. Should the rabbinic enactment carry the same stringencies as the biblical one?

### Hadar All Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא יָבֵשׁ — ״הָדָר״ בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא. אֶלָּא גָּזוּל, בִּשְׁלָמָא יוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן, דִּכְתִיב: ״לָכֶם״ — מִשֶּׁלָּכֶם. אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי אַמַּאי לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, a dry** *lulav* is unfit both on the first day and subsequently. It is unfit for use because the term *hadar* is used with regard to the four species, from which it is derived that **we require beauty. And** since in a dry *lulav* **there is not** beauty, it is unfit. **However,** with regard to **a stolen** *lulav*, **granted,** on **the first day of the Festival** it is unfit, **as it is written:** "And you shall take **for yourselves** on the first day" (Leviticus 23:40), indicating that the four species must be taken **from your own** property. **However,** beginning **on the second day of the Festival, why** does one **not** fulfill his obligation with a stolen *lulav*?

The *gemara* proceeds to distinguish between the different grounds for invalidity. *Bishlama yavesh* — I understand why a dried *lulav* is invalid even on *Chol HaMoed*: *hadar beinan veleka* — the requirement of *hadar* plainly applies. A dried-up *lulav* is not *hadar*, and that requirement applies across all the days of the festival regardless of whether the obligation is biblical or rabbinic.

### Stolen Lulav Problem

*Ela gazul* — but a stolen *lulav*: why is it invalid on *Chol HaMoed*? *Bishlama Yom Tov Rishon ksiv lachem — mishelachem* — I understand the first day: the verse says *lachem*, you shall take "for yourselves," which is read as *mishelachem* — from what is yours — imposing a requirement of ownership. But on *Chol HaMoed*, when we know that one may even borrow a *lulav* without owning it, why should a stolen *lulav* be any worse? If ownership is not required on *Chol HaMoed*, what precisely disqualifies a stolen one? The *gemara* leaves this question open at this point, to be taken up in the continuation.
