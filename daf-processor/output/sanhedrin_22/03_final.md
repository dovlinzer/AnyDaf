# Sanhedrin 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Horses & Wealth

### Intro: Horses & Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לּוֹ סוּסִים״, אֶלָּא כְּדֵי מֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ. ״וְכֶסֶף וְזָהָב לֹא יַרְבֶּה לּוֹ מְאֹד״, אֶלָּא כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן אַפְסַנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The king **"shall not accumulate many horses for himself"** (Deuteronomy 17:16), but **only** enough **for his chariot** in war and in peace. **"Neither shall he greatly accumulate silver and gold for himself"** (Deuteronomy 17:17), but **only enough to provide** his **soldiers' sustenance [*aspanya*].**

The *mishnah* on 21b introduces a set of royal restrictions that extend beyond the prohibition on excessive wives discussed previously. The *mishnah* states: *lo yarbeh lo susim ella k'dei mirkavto v'chesef v'zahav lo yarbeh lo me'od ella k'dei litein aspanya* — the king may not accumulate too many horses beyond what is needed for his chariots and cavalry, and he may not accumulate excessive silver and gold beyond what is needed to fund his army and the operating needs of the crown. The underlying principle is that while some subjective threshold exists, once horses or wealth become genuinely extraneous — serving no present or even potential function — the prohibition applies.

### Extraneous Horses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ סוּסִים״ – יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ כְּדֵי מֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ וּפָרָשָׁיו? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לוֹ״ – לוֹ אֵינוֹ מַרְבֶּה, אֲבָל מַרְבֶּה הוּא כְּדֵי רִכְבּוֹ וּפָרָשָׁיו. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״סוּסִים״? סוּסִים הַבַּטְלָנִין. מִנַּיִין שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ סוּס אֶחָד וְהוּא בָּטֵל שֶׁהוּא בְּ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה״? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לְמַעַן הַרְבּוֹת סוּס״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"He shall not accumulate many horses [*susim*] for himself** nor return the people to Egypt for the sake of accumulating horses [*sus*]" (Deuteronomy 17:16): One **might** have thought that he shall not have **even** enough horses **for his chariot and riders.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For himself,"** teaching that only if the horses are **for himself,** for personal pleasure, **he shall not accumulate** them, **but he may accumulate** horses **for his chariot and riders. How, then, do I realize** the meaning of **"horses [*susim*]"** in the verse? It is referring to **idle horses,** which serve no purpose other than glorifying the king. **From where** is it derived **that even** if the king has **one horse that is idle, that he** transgresses **"he shall not accumulate"? The verse states: "For the sake of accumulating horses [*sus*],"** with the term for horses written in the singular.

What, then, is actually prohibited under the word *susim*? The *gemara* answers: *susim batlanin* — idle horses, horses running in front of the royal procession purely for show, serving no military or functional role whatsoever. The *gemara* further asks: how do we know that even a single idle horse, *sus echad v'hu batel*, falls under *lo yarbeh*? From the verse *l'ma'an harbot sus* — the word *l'ma'an* comes to include even one such horse. But then, once we know that even a single idle horse is already prohibited, what additional work does the plural *susim* do? The answer is *la'avor b'lo ta'aseh al kol sus v'sus* — each and every extraneous horse constitutes its own independent transgression. The accumulation of additional idle horses does not merely deepen a single *issur*; rather, each one is a separate violation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דַּאֲפִילּוּ סוּס אֶחָד וְהוּא בָּטֵל קָאֵי בְּ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה״, ״סוּסִים״ לְמָה לִי? לַעֲבוֹר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל כׇּל סוּס וְסוּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But once** the verse taught **that even one horse that is idle stands** to be included in the prohibition of **"he shall not accumulate," why do I** need the plural form **"horses"** in the first clause of the verse? The Gemara responds: Its purpose is **to** teach that a king would **transgress** the **prohibition** an additional time **for each and every** idle **horse.**

### 'Lo' Teaches Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לוֹ״, הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ כְּדֵי רִכְבּוֹ וּפָרָשָׁיו נָמֵי לָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, לְאַפּוֹשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this ruling: The specific **reason** for limiting the prohibition to idle horses is **that the Merciful One writes:** "He shall not accumulate **for himself,"** which indicates, **consequently,** that if the Torah had **not** written **this, I would say** that **even** enough horses **for his chariot and riders** are **not** permitted; and this is unreasonable, since the king needs an army. The Gemara responds: **No,** the term "for himself" is **necessary** to teach that it is permitted for the king **to add** a reasonable number of horses beyond the necessary minimum, and it is only strictly personal use that is prohibited.

The *gemara* then addresses the textual role of the word *lo* more precisely. Had the Torah not written *lo*, one might have thought the prohibition extended even to horses maintained for the army itself. The word *lo* therefore comes to carve out that military horses are entirely permitted — and indeed, the king must ensure he has adequate forces. The *lo* limits the prohibition sharply to personal, extraneous accumulation.

### Silver & Gold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכֶסֶף וְזָהָב לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ״, אֶלָּא כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן אַפְסַנְיָא. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְכֶסֶף וְזָהָב לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ״ – יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן אַפְסַנְיָא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לוֹ״ – לוֹ אֵינוֹ מַרְבֶּה, אֲבָל מַרְבֶּה הוּא כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן אַפְסַנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **"Neither shall he greatly accumulate silver and gold for himself"** (Deuteronomy 17:17), but **only enough to provide** his **soldiers' sustenance. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: From the command **"neither shall he greatly accumulate silver and gold for himself,"** one **might** have thought that he should not have **even enough to provide** his **soldiers' sustenance.** To counter this, **the verse states: "For himself,"** teaching that only if the silver and gold is **for himself,** for personal pleasure, **he shall not accumulate** it, **but he may accumulate enough** silver and gold **to provide** his **soldiers' sustenance.**

The same structure is applied to silver and gold. The *baraita* states: *lo yarbeh lo — lo eino marbeh aval marbeh hu kedei liten aspanya* — the word *lo* teaches that a personal stockpile is prohibited, but accumulation for military pay and the needs of the kingdom is permitted. The *gemara* raises the obvious question: would anyone have thought the king cannot maintain funds to pay his army? The answer is that the word *lo* comes to emphasize that the permission for expanded royal wealth is genuinely limited to those functional needs — *lo tzricha le-harchavah* — and that any accumulation beyond them, even in anticipation of future needs, is restricted to the personal sphere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לוֹ״? הָא לָאו הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן אַפְסַנְיָא נָמֵי לָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, לְהַרְוָוחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this ruling: The specific **reason** for limiting the accumulation of personal wealth is **that the Merciful One writes:** "Neither shall he greatly accumulate silver and gold **for himself,"** which indicates, **consequently,** that if the Torah had **not** written **this, I would say** that it **is not** permitted for the king to accumulate **even enough** silver and gold **to provide** his **soldiers' sustenance;** this is unreasonable, since the king needs an army. The Gemara responds: **No,** the term "for himself" is **necessary** to teach that the king is permitted **to** allow for **a liberal appropriation** to the military budget, so that the army has a comfortable financial cushion.

The *gemara* then connects this back to the earlier prohibition of *lo yarbeh lo nashim*, noting that the *lo* in that context serves a different function: *lemiut hedyotot* — to distinguish the king's restrictions from those of a commoner, who is not limited in the number of wives he may marry.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ ״לוֹ״ לִדְרָשָׁה, ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים״ מַאי דָּרְשַׁתְּ בֵּיהּ? לְמַעוֹטֵי הֶדְיוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now that you have said** that the term **"for himself"** in the verse is stated **for** the purpose of **a derivation** for practical *halakha*, which limits and narrows the verse's scope, **what do you derive from** the next phrase in the verse: **"He shall not add many wives for himself"?** The Gemara answers: That usage of "for himself" serves **to exclude ordinary** people, to specify that only the king is restricted from having many wives, but a civilian may marry as many women as he wants, provided he can support them financially.

## Solomon's Wealth

### Conflicting Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״וַיְהִי לִשְׁלֹמֹה אַרְבָּעִים אֶלֶף אֻרְוֹת סוּסִים לְמֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ״, וּכְתִיב ״וַיְהִי לִשְׁלֹמֹה אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים אֻרְווֹת סוּסִים״. הָא כֵּיצַד? אִם אַרְבָּעִים אֶלֶף אִיצְטַבְלָאוֹת הָיוּ, כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הָיוּ בּוֹ אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים אֻרְווֹת סוּסִים. וְאִם אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים אִיצְטַבְלָאוֹת הָיוּ, כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד הָיוּ בּוֹ אַרְבָּעִים אֶלֶף אֻרְווֹת סוּסִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda raises a contradiction: It is written** in one verse: **"And Solomon had forty thousand stalls of horses for his chariots"** (I Kings 5:6), **and it is written** in another verse: **"And Solomon had four thousand stalls for horses** and chariots" (II Chronicles 9:25). **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? **If there were forty thousand large stables [*itztablaot*], each and every one** of them **had in it four thousand stalls,** or rows, **for horses. And** alternatively, **if there were four thousand large stables, each and every one had in it forty thousand stalls for horses.** Therefore the two verses are reconciled.

Rabbi Yehuda notes an apparent contradiction between two verses regarding Solomon's stables. One verse states that Solomon had *arbaim elef urvot susim* — forty thousand stable-units for his horses — while another states *arba'at alafim* — four thousand. The resolution offered is that these figures describe different levels of organization: either there were forty thousand stable complexes each containing four thousand individual stalls, or four thousand complexes each containing forty thousand stalls. Either way, the total number of horses involved is staggering.

### Silver Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״אֵין כֶּסֶף נֶחְשָׁב בִּימֵי שְׁלֹמֹה לִמְאוּמָה״, וּכְתִיב ״וַיִּתֵּן שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת הַכֶּסֶף בִּירוּשָׁלִַים כָּאֲבָנִים״? לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – קוֹדֶם שֶׁנָּשָׂא שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בַּת פַּרְעֹה, כָּאן – לְאַחַר שֶׁנָּשָׂא שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בַּת פַּרְעֹה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak raises a contradiction: It is written** in one verse: **"Silver was not worth anything in the days of Solomon"** (II Chronicles 9:20), **and it is written** in another verse: **"And the king made silver in Jerusalem as stones"** (I Kings 10:27), i.e., gems. The Gemara responds: It is **not difficult: Here,** where silver was worthless, this was **before Solomon** sinfully **married Pharaoh's daughter. There,** where the silver was valuable, this was **after Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter.**

Rabbi Yitzchak raises a parallel tension regarding silver. One verse says *ein kessef nechshav bimei Shlomo le-me'umah* — silver was not considered significant in Solomon's days — implying a kind of devaluation or scarcity of prestige. Another verse states that Solomon made silver as plentiful in Jerusalem as stones. These two descriptions seem irreconcilable.

### Pre/Post Marriage

The *gemara* resolves both tensions through the same chronological key: *lo kashya kan kodem shenasa Shlomo et bat Paroh kan le'achar shenasa Shlomo et bat Paroh* — one verse describes the period before Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter, and the other describes the period after. It is worth noting that there is an additional tradition that the king may not accumulate wealth through taxation, but gifts — such as a royal dowry — would be permitted, which may help explain the dramatic shift in the kingdom's financial character following the marriage.

### Rome Foundation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנָּשָׂא שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בַּת פַּרְעֹה, יָרַד גַּבְרִיאֵל וְנָעַץ קָנֶה בַּיָּם, וְהֶעֱלָה שִׂירְטוֹן, וְעָלָיו נִבְנָה כְּרַךְ גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּרוֹמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: When Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter,** the angel **Gabriel descended** from Heaven **and implanted a pole in the sea. And** it gradually **raised up a sandbar [*sirton*]** around it, creating new, dry land, **and on it the great city of Rome was built.** This shows that the beginning of the Jewish people's downfall to Rome came with Solomon's marriage to Pharaoh's daughter.

A striking *midrash* is introduced here: Rabbi Yitzchak teaches that at the very moment Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter, the angel Gabriel descended and drove a reed into the sea. From the sandbank that arose, the great city of Rome was eventually built. The implicit reading is that Solomon's sin in this marriage planted the seed of Israel's greatest future enemy. A tradition also cited in this context holds that Pharaoh's daughter brought with her musical instruments associated with idolatrous worship, introducing practices that God would ultimately punish through the Roman destruction of the Temple. The very act by which Solomon built the Temple thus contained within it the conditions for its destruction.

### Hidden Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נִתְגַּלּוּ טַעֲמֵי תּוֹרָה? שֶׁהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּי מִקְרָאוֹת נִתְגַּלּוּ טַעְמָן, וְנִכְשַׁל בָּהֶן גְּדוֹל הָעוֹלָם. כְּתִיב: ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים״. אָמַר שְׁלֹמֹה: ״אֲנִי אַרְבֶּה וְלֹא אָסוּר״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי לְעֵת זִקְנַת שְׁלֹמֹה נָשָׁיו הִטּוּ אֶת לְבָבוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: For what** reason **were the rationales of Torah** commandments **not revealed?** It was **because the rationales of two verses were revealed,** and **the greatest in the world,** King Solomon, **failed in** those matters. **It is written** with regard to a king: **"He shall not add many wives for himself,** that his heart should not turn away" (Deuteronomy 17:17). **Solomon said: I will add many, but I will not turn away,** as he thought that it is permitted to have many wives if one is otherwise meticulous not to stray. **And** later, **it is written: "For it came to pass, when Solomon was old, that his wives turned away his heart** after other gods" (I Kings 11:4).

Rabbi Yitzchak draws a broader lesson from Solomon's failures: *mipnei mah lo nitgalu ta'amei Torah* — why are the reasons for *mitzvot* generally not revealed in the Torah? Because the two cases where reasons are given — don't multiply wives, lest your heart be led astray; don't multiply horses, lest you return to Egypt — both led to disaster. Solomon reasoned that the reasons would not apply to him: *ani arbeh velo asur* — "I will multiply and not be led astray." In both cases, the Torah's feared outcome came to pass. The lesson is that once reasons are known, individuals will construct arguments for why those reasons do not apply to their circumstances. This connects to a broader *machlokes* discussed earlier in the *masechta* about whether one *doresh ta'ama de'mitzvot*, since even where reasons are given by *Chazal* at a later stage, the same risk emerges: a person identifies with one side of a *chakira* and concludes the prohibition does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לּוֹ סוּסִים״, וְאָמַר שְׁלֹמֹה: ״אֲנִי אַרְבֶּה וְלֹא אָשִׁיב״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַתֵּצֵא מֶרְכָּבָה מִמִּצְרַיִם בְּשֵׁשׁ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is** also **written:** "Only **he shall not accumulate many horses for himself** nor return the people to Egypt for the sake of accumulating horses" (Deuteronomy 17:16), **and Solomon said: I will accumulate many, but I will not return. And it is written: "And a chariot** came up **and went out of Egypt for six** hundred shekels of silver" (I Kings 10:29), teaching that not only did Solomon violate the Torah, but he also failed in applying the rationale given for its commandments. This demonstrates the wisdom in the Torah's usual silence as to the rationale for its mitzvot, as individuals will not mistakenly rely on their own wisdom to reason that the mitzvot are inapplicable in some circumstances.

## Sefer Torah

### Own Sefer Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוֹתֵב לוֹ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה לִשְׁמוֹ. יוֹצֵא לַמִּלְחָמָה – מוֹצִיאָהּ עִמּוֹ, נִכְנָס – הוּא מַכְנִיסָהּ עִמּוֹ, יוֹשֵׁב בַּדִּין – הִיא עִמּוֹ, מֵיסֵב – הִיא כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיְתָה עִמּוֹ וְקָרָא בוֹ כׇּל יְמֵי חַיָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the king **writes himself a Torah scroll for his sake,** as stipulated in Deuteronomy 17:18. When **he goes out to war, he brings it out with him.** When **he comes in** from war, **he brings it in with him.** When **he sits in judgment, it is with him.** When **he reclines** to eat, **it is opposite him, as it is stated: "And it shall be with him and he shall read it all the days of his life"** (Deuteronomy 17:19).

The *mishnah* states that the king must write a *sefer torah* for himself — *v'chotev lo sefer torah lishmo* — and this *sefer torah* accompanies him everywhere. When he goes out to war, it goes with him; when he returns, it returns with him. When he sits in judgment, it sits beside him, as the verse states *v'haytah imo v'kara bo kol y'mei chayav* — it shall be with him and he shall read from it all the days of his life. The *sofrim* of antiquity were capable of writing with extraordinary precision in miniature form, making it plausible that the king carried something approaching a portable *sefer torah*.

### Braita: Others' Torahs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוֹתֵב סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה לִשְׁמוֹ. תְּנָא: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְנָאֶה בְּשֶׁל אֲבוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The king **writes a Torah scroll for his sake.** The Sages **taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:4): The king fulfills the mitzva **provided that he does not beautify himself with** the Torah scroll **of his ancestors** for this purpose, i.e., he must write his own scroll.

A *baraita* refines the *mishnah*'s ruling. When we say the king must write his own *sefer torah lishmo* — and that he should not make use of one that is not his — the disqualification is not of an inherited *sefer torah* but of one written for someone else. For the king (*melech*), the requirement to have his own applies; for a commoner (*hedyot*), it does not. Yet separately, the rule that one must write his own even if he inherits one applies to everyone, based on the verse *ve'ata kitvu lachem et hashirah*.

### Two Torahs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, צְרִיכָא לִשְׁתֵּי תּוֹרוֹת, וְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וְכָתַב לוֹ אֶת מִשְׁנֵה וְגוֹ׳״ – כּוֹתֵב לִשְׁמוֹ שְׁתֵּי תּוֹרוֹת: אַחַת שֶׁהִיא יוֹצְאָה וְנִכְנֶסֶת עִמּוֹ, וְאַחַת שֶׁמּוּנַּחַת לוֹ בְּבֵית גְּנָזָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara dismisses Abaye's objection: **No,** the ruling of that *baraita* is **necessary** to teach that the king is commanded to write **two Torah** scrolls; he writes one scroll as does any Jew, and he writes an additional scroll because he is king. **And** this is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"That he shall write for himself a second** Torah in a scroll, out of that which is before the priests the Levites" (Deuteronomy 17:18). This teaches that **he writes for his sake two Torah** scrolls, **one that goes out and comes in with him** at all times, **and one that is placed in his treasury.**

The *baraita* continues: the king actually requires two *torahs*, as derived from the verse *v'chatav lo et mishneh ha-Torah ha-zot*. One accompanies him in and out — a travel *sefer torah* — and one remains in his treasury, *veit genazav*.

### Torah as Amulet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתָהּ שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה וְנִכְנֶסֶת עִמּוֹ, עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָהּ כְּמִין קָמֵיעַ וְתוֹלָהּ בִּזְרוֹעוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁוִּיתִי ה׳ לְנֶגְדִּי תָמִיד כִּי מִימִינִי בַּל אֶמּוֹט״. אֵינוֹ נִכְנָס בָּהּ לֹא לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ וְלֹא לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיְתָה עִמּוֹ וְקָרָא בוֹ״ – מָקוֹם הָרָאוּי לִקְרוֹת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to **the one that goes out and comes in with him, he makes it** very small, **like an amulet, and he hangs** it **on his arm, as it is stated: "I have set the Lord always before me; He is at my right hand, that I shall not be moved"** (Psalms 16:8). This alludes to the small Torah scroll that is always on his right hand. **He does not go into the bathhouse with it, nor into the bathroom, as it is stated: "And it shall be with him and he shall read from it"** (Deuteronomy 17:19), meaning, it shall remain in **a place that is appropriate for reading from it.**

The *sefer torah* that travels with the king is fashioned into a kind of receptacle that can be attached to his arm, *kemin kame'a vetoleh bizro'o*, evoking the verse *shiviti Hashem l'negdi tamid* — I have set God before me always. It is worth noting the *tefil'in*-like character of this practice. However, this portable *sefer torah* is not taken absolutely everywhere: *eino nichnas bah lo le-veit hamerchatz velo le-veit hakisei* — it does not accompany him into the bathhouse or the bathroom.

### Inner/Outer Torah

The image of two *torahs* — one that goes out into the world and one kept in the *genizah* — invites reflection on different modes of Torah engagement. The *torah* one presents to the world and the *torah* one maintains internally need not be identical in character; there is a *torah* for public life and a *torah* for the private inner realm. The verse *v'hayeta imo v'kara bo* — he shall read from it — is limited by the phrase *makom hara'ui likro bo*, a place appropriate for such reading, which restricts the portable *sefer torah*'s use to fitting contexts.

## Torah Scripts

### Original Script

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא מָר עוּקְבָא: בַּתְּחִלָּה נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּכְתָב עִבְרִי וּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ. חָזְרָה וְנִיתְּנָה לָהֶם בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא בִּכְתָב אַשּׁוּרִי וּלְשׁוֹן אֲרַמִּי. בֵּירְרוּ לָהֶן יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּתָב אַשּׁוּרִי וּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, וְהִנִּיחוּ לַהֶדְיוֹטוֹת כְּתָב עִבְרִית וּלְשׁוֹן אֲרַמִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra says, and some say** that it is **Mar Ukva** who says: **Initially,** the **Torah was given to the Jewish people in *Ivrit* script,** the original form of the written language, **and the sacred tongue,** Hebrew. **It was given to them again in the days of Ezra in *Ashurit* script and** the **Aramaic tongue. The Jewish people selected *Ashurit* script and the sacred tongue** for the Torah scroll **and left *Ivrit* script and** the **Aramaic tongue for the commoners.**

Mar Zutra, and some attribute this to Mar Ukva, teaches: *batchila nitna torah le-Yisrael bichtav Ivri u-leshon hakodesh* — the Torah was originally given to Israel in *ktav Ivri* and *leshon hakodesh*.

### Ezra's Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזְרָה וְנִיתְּנָה לָהֶם בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא בִּכְתָב אַשּׁוּרִי וּלְשׁוֹן אֲרַמִּי. בֵּירְרוּ לָהֶן יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּתָב אַשּׁוּרִי וּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, וְהִנִּיחוּ לַהֶדְיוֹטוֹת כְּתָב עִבְרִית וּלְשׁוֹן אֲרַמִּי. מַאן הֶדְיוֹטוֹת? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כּוּתָאֵי. מַאי כְּתָב עִבְרִית? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כְּתָב לִיבּוֹנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **was given to them again in the days of Ezra in *Ashurit* script and** the **Aramaic tongue. The Jewish people selected *Ashurit* script and the sacred tongue** for the Torah scroll **and left *Ivrit* script and** the **Aramaic tongue for the commoners.** The Gemara asks: **Who are** these **commoners? Rav Ḥisda said: The Samaritans [*Kutim*].** The Gemara asks: **What is *Ivrit* script? Rav Ḥisda says: *Libona'a* script.**

In the time of Ezra, the script was changed: *chazra venitna lahem bimei Ezra bichtav Ashurit u-leshon Aramit* — it was reissued in *ktav Ashurit* with *leshon Aramit*. The refined formulation is that the Jewish people ultimately retained *ktav Ashurit* with *leshon hakodesh* — which is our current form — while *ktav Ivri* and *leshon Aramit* were left for the *hedyotot*. Who are the *hedyotot* here? Rav Chisda says: the *Kuta'ei*, the Samaritans.

### Ktav Names

What is *ktav Ivri*? Rav Chisda says it is *ktav libona* — script associated with a place called Libona, the precise identification of which remains unclear. As for *ktav Ashurit*, the *gemara* will shortly address its etymology.

### Ezra & Moses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: רָאוּי הָיָה עֶזְרָא שֶׁתִּינָּתֵן תּוֹרָה עַל יָדוֹ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, אִילְמָלֵא לֹא קְדָמוֹ מֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:5): **Rabbi Yosei says: Ezra was suitable,** given his greatness, **for** the **Torah to be given by him to the Jewish people, had Moses not come first** and received the Torah already.

A *baraita* records a remarkable statement: Rabbi Yosei says, *raui haya Ezra she-tinaten torah al yado le-Yisrael ilmalei lo* — Ezra was worthy of having the Torah given through him, had Moses not preceded him.

### Moses Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמֹשֶׁה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּמֹשֶׁה עָלָה אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים״, בְּעֶזְרָא הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הוּא עֶזְרָא עָלָה מִבָּבֶל״. מָה עֲלִיָּיה הָאֲמוּרָה כָּאן – תּוֹרָה, אַף עֲלִיָּיה הָאֲמוּרָה לְהַלָּן – תּוֹרָה. בְּמֹשֶׁה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֹתִי צִוָּה ה׳ בָּעֵת הַהִיא לְלַמֵּד אֶתְכֶם חֻקִּים וּמִשְׁפָּטִים״. בְּעֶזְרָא הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי עֶזְרָא הֵכִין לְבָבוֹ לִדְרֹשׁ אֶת תּוֹרַת ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו וְלַעֲשֹׂות וּלְלַמֵּד בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל חֹק וּמִשְׁפָּט״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **With regard to Moses** the verse **states: "And Moses went up to God"** (Exodus 19:3), and **with regard to Ezra** the verse **states: "This Ezra went up from Babylon** and he was a ready scribe in the Torah of Moses, which the Lord, the God of Israel, had given" (Ezra 7:6). **Just as** the **going up stated here,** with regard to Moses, is for the **Torah,** which he received from God and transmitted to the Jewish people, **so too,** the **going up stated there,** with regard to Ezra, is for the **Torah,** as he taught Torah to the Jewish people and was suitable to have originally merited to give it. The *baraita* continues: **With regard to Moses** the verse **states: "And the Lord commanded me at that time to teach you statutes and ordinances"** (Deuteronomy 4:14), and **with regard to Ezra** the verse **states: "For Ezra had set his heart to seek the Torah of the Lord his God and to do it and to teach in Israel statutes and ordinances"** (Ezra 7:10).

The *gemara* then develops a series of textual parallels between Moses and Ezra. Moses *alah el ha-Elohim* — went up to God; Ezra *hu oleh mi-Bavel* — ascended from Babylon. The shared language of *aliyah* in both cases implies a parallel spiritual significance, and one might further read here an early Zionist resonance: the ascent to the Land of Israel is equated with the ascent to receive Torah. Moses was commanded to teach *chukim u-mishpatim* to Israel; of Ezra it is written that he prepared his heart *lidrosh et Torat Hashem ve-la'asot u-le-lamed be-Yisrael chok u-mishpat* — virtually identical language. And yet, though the Torah was not transmitted through Ezra's hand, the script nonetheless changed through him — *nishtanah al yado ha-ktav* — as attested by verses in *Sefer Ezra* and *Daniel* that point to changes in written form, and a verse in *Devarim* which the *gemara* reads as implying a script *ra'uy le-hishtanot* — fit to be changed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה עַל יָדוֹ, נִשְׁתַּנָּה עַל יָדוֹ הַכְּתָב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּכְתָב הַנִּשְׁתְּוָן כָּתוּב אֲרָמִית וּמְתֻרְגָּם אֲרַמִּית״. וּכְתִיב: ״לָא כָהֲלִין כְּתָבָא לְמִקְרֵא וּפִשְׁרֵהּ לְהוֹדָעָה לְמַלְכָּא״. [מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם נִיתַּן] וּכְתִיב: ״וְכָתַב אֶת מִשְׁנֵה הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת״ – כְּתָב הָרָאוּי לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And even though** the **Torah was not given** literally **by him, the script** of the Torah **was changed by him, as it is stated:** **"And the writing of the letter [*hannishtevan*] was written in the Aramaic script, and set forth in the Aramaic tongue"** (Ezra 4:7). The term "*hannishtevan*" is similar to the word *nishtana*, meaning changed, alluding to the fact that the script had been changed. **And it is written** with regard to the writing on the wall of Belshazzar's palace: **"Then came in all the king's wise men. But they could not read the writing, nor make known to the king the interpretation"** (Daniel 5:8), and the reason they could not read it is that it was written in the new script that Ezra would transmit. **And it is written: "That he shall write for himself a second [*mishne*] Torah"** (Deuteronomy 17:18), where "second [*mishne*]" teaches that it is written in **a script that is apt to be changed [*lehishtannot*].**

## Script Changes

### Sin/Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בַּתְּחִלָּה בִּכְתָב זֶה נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל. כֵּיוָן שֶׁחָטְאוּ, נֶהְפַּךְ לָהֶן לְרוֹעֵץ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁחָזְרוּ בָּהֶן, הֶחְזִירוֹ לָהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוּבוּ לְבִצָּרוֹן אֲסִירֵי הַתִּקְוָה גַּם הַיּוֹם מַגִּיד מִשְׁנֶה אָשִׁיב לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:5): **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Initially, the Torah was given to the Jewish people in this script,** *Ashurit*, which is in use today. **Once** the Jewish people **sinned, it turned into an impairment for them** and they began writing with a different script, *Libona'a*. **Once they repented,** the first script **was returned to them,** and they resumed writing with *Ashurit* script, **as it is stated: "Return to the stronghold, you prisoners of hope; even today do I declare that I will render double [*mishne*] unto you"** (Zechariah 9:12), meaning that God restored to the Jewish people this script that had been changed [*nishtanna*].

*Rebbe* offers a different framing of the script changes. He argues that one should not understand these changes as innovations introduced in the time of Ezra. Rather, *bi-tchilah ktav zeh nitenah Torah le-Yisrael* — the Torah was given in this script from the beginning. When Israel sinned, the script was transformed against them; when they repented, it was restored. The proof-text is *shuvu le-vitzaron asirei ha-tikvah gam hayom magid mishneh ashiv lach* — the word *mishneh* implying a doubling back or restoration. This framing is significant: if the different scripts already existed as modes at *Har Sinai*, then Ezra's changes feel far less theologically destabilizing. One need not view them as a later editorial intervention but as a restoration of an earlier state.

### Ashurit Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ אַשּׁוּרִי? שֶׁעָלָה עִמָּהֶם מֵאַשּׁוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Why** is this script **called *Ashurit*? Because it ascended with** the Jewish people **from Ashur** when they returned from their exile in Babylonia.

The *gemara* asks: *lamma nikra Ashurit* — why is the script called *Ashurit*? Two answers emerge. One: *she-alah imahem me-Ashur* — because these letters came up with the returning exiles from Assyria. A second etymology: *she-me'usheret bi-chtav* — because it is a particularly rich, embellished script.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ אַשּׁוּרִי? שֶׁמְּאוּשָּׁר בִּכְתָבו‍ֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If this script predates the exile to Babylonia, **why is it called *Ashurit*? Because it is *meusheret*,** beautiful and straight, **in script.**

### No Change View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן פַּרְטָא, שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי: כְּתָב זֶה לֹא נִשְׁתַּנָּה כׇּל עִיקָּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָוֵי הָעַמּוּדִים״. מָה עַמּוּדִים לֹא נִשְׁתַּנּוּ, אַף וָוִים לֹא נִשְׁתַּנּוּ. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶל הַיְּהוּדִים כִּכְתָבָם וְכִלְשׁוֹנָם״ – מָה לְשׁוֹנָם לֹא נִשְׁתַּנָּה, אַף כְּתָבָם לֹא נִשְׁתַּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Perata, who said in the name of Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i: This script did not change at all, as it is stated** with regard to the construction of the Tabernacle: **"The hooks of [*vavei*] the poles"** (Exodus 27:10). This teaches that **just as the poles were not changed, so too, the hooks [*vavim*] were not changed.** The letter *vav* in *Ashurit* script has the shape of a hook. Evidently, this is why the term for hook in the Torah is *vav*. **And** the verse **states: "And to the Jews according to their script and according to their language"** (Esther 8:9). This teaches that **just as their language was not changed** over the generations but remained Hebrew, **so too, their script was not changed.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Perata, in the name of Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i, takes the strongest position: *ktav zeh lo nishtanah kol ikar* — this script never changed at all. His proof is the verse *vavei ha-amudim* — the pillars. Just as the columns of the *sefer torah* never change, so too the *vav* — the shape of the letter *vav* — never changed. A further proof: the verse in *Esther* reads *ki-chtavam ve-khilshonam* — just as their language did not change, so too their writing did not change. One must acknowledge, however, that in practice many *torahs* have different column configurations, so the absolute stability of columns is itself not uncontested.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״אֶת מִשְׁנֵה הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת״? לִשְׁתֵּי תּוֹרוֹת: אַחַת שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה וְנִכְנֶסֶת עִמּוֹ, וְאַחַת שֶׁמּוּנַּחַת לוֹ בְּבֵית גְּנָזָיו. אוֹתָהּ שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה וְנִכְנֶסֶת עִמּוֹ – עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָהּ כְּמִין קָמֵיעַ וְתוֹלָהּ בִּזְרוֹעוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁוִּיתִי ה׳ לְנֶגְדִּי תָּמִיד״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But** if the script was in fact not changed, **how do I realize** the meaning of the phrase **"a second [*mishne*] Torah"** (Deuteronomy 17:18)? This serves **to** teach the *halakha* concerning the **two Torah scrolls** that the king writes, **one that goes out and comes in with him, and one that is placed in his treasury.** With regard to **the one that goes out and comes in with him, he makes it** very small, **like an amulet, and he hangs** it **on his arm. As it is stated** by King David: **"I have set the Lord always before me;** He is at my right hand, that I shall not be moved" (Psalms 16:8). This verse alludes to the two Torah scrolls, one that is before him and one that is in his right hand.

According to this view, the verse *mishneh ha-Torah ha-zot* does not refer to a script change at all, but to the two *torahs* the king writes — one that accompanies him and one kept in storage. The portable *sefer torah* is made like an amulet, *kemin kame'a ve-toleh bi-zro'o*, based on *shiviti Hashem le-negdi tamid*. Those who do not read this verse as referring to the portable *sefer torah* apply it instead to the teaching of Rav Chana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida: *hamispallel tzarich sheyireh atzmo ke'ilu Shechina kenegdo* — one who prays must imagine the *Shechina* standing before him, as it says *shiviti Hashem l'negdi tamid*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, הַאי ״שִׁוִּיתִי״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ כִּדְרַב חָנָה בַּר בִּיזְנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב חָנָה בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּרְאֶה עַצְמוֹ כְּאִילּוּ שְׁכִינָה כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁוִּיתִי ה׳ לְנֶגְדִּי תָמִיד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the other** Sage, i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who does not hold that the king must write and affix the second scroll to his arm, **what does he expound from that** verse: **"I have set"** (Psalms 16:8)? The Gemara responds: **He requires that** verse **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Ḥana bar Bizna, as Rav Ḥana bar Bizna says** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida says: One who prays needs to see himself as if the Divine Presence is opposite him, as it is stated: "I have set the Lord always before me"** (Psalms 16:8).

### Gimatriya Code

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: ״כְּתָב זֶה לֹא נִשְׁתַּנָּה״, מַאי ״לָא כָהֲלִין כְּתָבָא לְמִקְרֵא״? אָמַר רַב: בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא אִיכְּתִיב לְהוֹן ״יטת יטת אדך פוגחמט״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to Rabbi Shimon, who says** that **this script was not changed** at all, **what** is the reason **"they could not read the writing"** (Daniel 5:8)? **Rav says:** Because **it was written for them in** the obscure code of ***gimatriyya*.** It was written: ***Yod*, *tet*, *tav*; *yod*, *tet*, *tav*; *alef*, *yod*, *dalet*, *khaf*; *peh*, *vav*, *gimmel*, *ḥet*, *mem*, *tet*.** These letters correspond with: *Mem*, *nun*, *alef*; *mem*, *nun*, *alef*; *tav*, *kuf*, *lamed*; *vav*, *peh*, *reish*, *samekh*, *yod*, *nun*; this is based on the exchange of letters known as *at bash*, or the exchanging of a letter with its counterpart in the opposite place in the alphabet, e.g., *alef*, the first letter, for *tav*, the last letter.

The *gemara* addresses how Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who holds the script never changed, handles the verse from *Daniel* — *lo kahalin kesava le-mikra* — which implies that the writing on the wall at Belshazzar's feast could not be read by the Chaldean wise men. Rav answers: *be-gimatriya* — it was a cipher. The words were written in a code in which each letter is replaced by its counterpart from the opposite end of the alphabet (*alef* for *tav*, *bet* for *shin*, *gimel* for *reish*, *dalet* for *kuf*, and so forth). The apparently nonsensical letters *itat itat adach pugachmat* thus decode into *mene mene tekel upharsin*. Various Sages — Shmuel, Rabbi Yochanan, and Rav Ashi — offer additional interpretations of the cipher according to different decoding schemes, each arriving at somewhat different readings of the underlying text.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי פָּרֵישׁ לְהוּ? ״מְנֵא מְנֵא תְּקֵל וּפַרְסִין״. ״מְנֵא״ – מְנָא אֱלָהָא מַלְכוּתָךְ וְהַשְׁלְמַהּ לָךְ. ״תְּקֵל״ – תְּקִילְתָּא בְמֹאזַנְיָא וְהִשְׁתְּכַחַתְּ חַסִּיר. ״פַּרְסִין״ – פְּרִיסַת מַלְכוּתָךְ וִיהִיבַת לְמָדַי וּפָרָס. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: ״ממתוס ננקפי אאלרן״. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״אנם אנם לקת ניסרפו״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״נמא נמא קתל פורסין״.
>
> **Translation:** **What did** Daniel **explain to them?** The letters stand for the terms: **"*Mene mene tekel ufarsin*"** (Daniel 5:25). He then explained the meaning: **"*Mene*": God has numbered [*mena*]** the days of **your kingdom and brought it to an end. "*Tekel*": You are weighed [*tekilta*] on the scale and are found lacking. "*Parsin*": Your kingdom is divided [*perisat*] and given to the Medes and Persians"** (Daniel 5:26–28). **And Shmuel says:** The writing used the correct letters for those terms, but instead of being written in order, the four words, *mene mene tekel ufarsin* were written vertically and were therefore meant to be read from the top down. If read in the usual way, from right to left, it says: **"*Mamtos nankafei a'alran*." And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Each word was written backward, so that read right to left, they spelled, ***Anem anem leket nisrapu*. Rav Ashi says:** They were written with the first two letters of each word reversed: ***Nema nema ketal pursin*.**

## Royal Dignity

### Horse & Seat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין רוֹכְבִין עַל סוּסוֹ, וְאֵין יוֹשְׁבִין עַל כִּסְאוֹ, וְאֵין מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בְּשַׁרְבִיטוֹ, וְאֵין רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא מִסְתַּפֵּר, וְלֹא כְּשֶׁהוּא עָרוֹם, וְלֹא כְּשֶׁהוּא בְּבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׂוֹם תָּשִׂים עָלֶיךָ מֶלֶךְ״, שֶׁתְּהֵא אֵימָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not ride on** the king's **horse, and one may not sit on his throne, and one may not use his scepter, and one may not see him when he is having his hair cut, nor when he is naked, nor when he is in the bathhouse, as it is stated: "You shall set a king over you"** (Deuteronomy 17:15), meaning, ensure **that his fear should be upon you.** All of these actions would lessen one's fear of and reverence for the king.

The *mishnah* continues with a series of prohibitions protecting the dignity of the king: *ein rochvin al suso* — one may not ride the king's horse; *ve'ein yoshvin al kiso* — one may not sit on his throne; *ve'ein mishtamshin be-shravito* — one may not use his scepter.

### Visibility Rules

Similarly: *ve'ein ro'in oto keshehu mistaper* — one may not observe the king while he is getting his haircut, *velo keshehu arum* — nor while he is naked, *velo keshehu be-veit hamerchatz* — nor while he is in the bathhouse.

### Eimah Principle

The source and reason for all these prohibitions is the verse *som tasim alecha melech* — you shall surely set a king upon yourself — which the *gemara* reads as *she-tehe eimaso alecha* — that his *eimah*, his awe-inspiring quality, should rest upon you. All these activities — riding his horse, sitting in his chair, seeing him in a compromised state — erode the royal dignity and undermine the subject's appropriate sense of reverence toward the king.

## Avishag Story

### Avishag & Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲבִישַׁג מוּתֶּרֶת לִשְׁלֹמֹה, וַאֲסוּרָה לַאֲדֹנִיָּה. מוּתֶּרֶת לִשְׁלֹמֹה – דְּמֶלֶךְ הָיָה, וּמֶלֶךְ מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּשַׁרְבִיטוֹ שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ. וַאֲסוּרָה לַאֲדֹנִיָּה – דְּהֶדְיוֹט הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ya'akov says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Abishag** the Shunammite was **permitted to Solomon but forbidden to Adonijah,** who in fact wanted to marry her. She was **permitted to Solomon, as he was a king, and a king may use the scepter of a king.** Abishag's status was similar to the king's scepter, as she had been designated to serve King David. She was **forbidden to Adonijah, as he** was **an ordinary** person, not a king.

Rav Yaakov, citing Rabbi Yochanan, addresses the case of Avishag HaShunamit: she was permitted to Solomon but forbidden to Adoniyah. She was permitted to Solomon because he was himself a king, and a king may make use of the property or prerogatives of a previous king — *melech mishtamesh be-shravito shel melech*. She was forbidden to Adoniyah because *hedyot hu* — he was a commoner.

### Caregiver Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבִישַׁג מַאי הִיא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד זָקֵן בָּא בַּיָּמִים וְגוֹ׳ וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ עֲבָדָיו יְבַקְשׁוּ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְבַקְשׁוּ נַעֲרָה יָפָה וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנַּעֲרָה יָפָה עַד מְאֹד וַתְּהִי לַמֶּלֶךְ סֹכֶנֶת וַתְּשָׁרְתֵהוּ״. אֲמַרָה: ״נִינְסְבַן!״ אֲמַר לַהּ: ״אֲסִירַתְּ לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: The story of **Abishag, what is it? As it is written: "Now King David was old and stricken in years…and his servants said to him, let there be sought**…a young virgin…and let her lie in your bosom, that my lord the king may get heat" (I Kings 1:1–2); **and it is written: "So they sought for a beautiful maiden…**and found Abishag" (I Kings 1:1–3); **and it is written: "And the maiden was very beautiful and she became a companion to the king and ministered to him,** but the king did not know her" (I Kings 1:1–4). Abishag **said** to King David: **Marry me.** King David **said to her: You are forbidden to me,** as I already have eighteen wives.

The *gemara* explains who Avishag was. The verses from *Melachim* describe how King David had grown old and cold, and his servants sought a young woman to serve as his companion and caretaker. Avishag was found — exceptionally beautiful — and she served the king. At some point she said to David: *ninsavin* — marry me. David refused, saying it was forbidden for him to do so. She accused him of using *halacha* as a pretext, suggesting that he was really afraid of his own diminished capacity — *chasra d'ganav v'anafshaih l'shlama nakit* — that he was hiding his weakness behind religious scruple.

### Bathsheva Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: חַסְּרֵיהּ לְגַנָּבָא, נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּשַׁלְמָנָא נָקֵית. אֲמַר לְהוּ: קִרְאוּ לִי לְבַת שֶׁבַע. וּכְתִיב: ״וַתָּבֹא בַת שֶׁבַע אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ הַחַדְרָה״. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה קִינְּחָה בַּת שֶׁבַע בִּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מַפּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Abishag **said to him: When the thief is lacking** what to steal, **he makes himself like a man of peace.** In other words, she was saying that since King David was physically unable to engage in intercourse, he devised an excuse not to marry her. King David **said to** his attendants: **Call Bathsheba to me. And it is written: "And Bathsheba went in to the king into the chamber;** now the king was very old and Abishag the Shunammite ministered to the king" (I Kings 1:15). **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: At that time, Bathsheba wiped** herself **with thirteen cloths,** corresponding to the number of words in the verse, indicating that she engaged in intercourse with King David thirteen times.

David rejected this accusation decisively. He instructed his servants: *karu li l'Batsheva* — bring Bathsheva to me. The verse records that Bathsheva came to the king in his chamber. Rav Yehuda, citing Rav, teaches: *b'ota sha'ah kincha Batsheva b'shlosh esrei mapot* — at that moment, Bathsheva cleaned herself with thirteen cloths, corresponding to the thirteen words in the verse describing the scene and indicating, per Rashi, that they had relations thirteen times in succession. David's purpose was unambiguous: to demonstrate that his earlier refusal of Avishag was entirely on *halachic* grounds and not on account of any personal failing.

### Divorce Hard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֶׁמֶן בַּר אַבָּא: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה קָשִׁין גֵּירוּשִׁין, שֶׁהֲרֵי דָּוִד הַמֶּלֶךְ הִתִּירוּ לוֹ לְיַיחֵד, וְלֹא הִתִּירוּ לוֹ לְגָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shemen bar Abba says: Come and see how severe** a matter **divorce** is, **as they** rendered it **permitted for King David to be secluded** with Abishag without marrying her, **but they did not** render it **permitted for him to divorce** one of his wives to enable him to marry Abishag.

Rav Shemen bar Abba draws a striking lesson from this episode: *bo u're'eh kamah kashin gerushin* — come and see how difficult divorce is. David, who had as many as eighteen wives, was not permitted to divorce one of them in order to marry Avishag. Instead, the authorities permitted him the more delicate arrangement of *yichud* with a woman who remained forbidden to him — rather than sanction the dissolution of an existing marriage. This illustrates how deeply resistant *halacha* is to divorce even when there might seem to be good reason for it.

## Marriage & Divorce

### First Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כׇּל הַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ רִאשׁוֹנָה, אֲפִילּוּ מִזְבֵּחַ מוֹרִיד עָלָיו דְּמָעוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֹאת שֵׁנִית תַּעֲשׂוּ כַּסּוֹת דִּמְעָה אֶת מִזְבַּח ה׳ בְּכִי וַאֲנָקָה מֵאֵין עוֹד פְּנוֹת אֶל הַמִּנְחָה וְלָקַחַת רָצוֹן מִיֶּדְכֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲמַרְתֶּם עַל מָה עַל כִּי ה׳ הֵעִיד בֵּינְךָ וּבֵין אֵשֶׁת נְעוּרֶיךָ אֲשֶׁר אַתָּה בָּגַדְתָּה בָּהּ וְהִיא חֲבֶרְתְּךָ וְאֵשֶׁת בְּרִיתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Concerning **anyone who divorces his first wife, even the altar sheds tears about him, as it is stated: "And further, this you should do: Cover the altar of the Lord with tears, with weeping and sighing, from His no longer showing regard to the offering, nor receiving it with goodwill from your hand"** (Malachi 2:13), **and it is written: "Yet you say: Why? Because the Lord has been witness between you and the wife of your youth, against whom you have dealt treacherously, though she is your companion and the wife of your covenant"** (Malachi 2:14).

Rabbi Elazar states: *kol hamgaresh et ishto rishonah afilu mizbe'ach morid alav dmaot* — whoever divorces his first wife, even the *mizbe'ach* weeps for him. The *pesukim* from *Malachi* are cited: the *mizbe'ach* is covered with tears; God testifies between a man and the wife of his youth — *eshet ne'urecha* — whom he has betrayed, and who was his companion and the wife of his covenant. These are presented as experiential and *agadah*-level observations rather than purely prescriptive *halacha*, though they are no less serious for that.

### Wife Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מֵתָה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מְבַקְּשִׁין מִמֶּנּוּ מָמוֹן וְאֵין לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם אֵין לְךָ לְשַׁלֵּם לָמָּה יִקַּח מִשְׁכָּבְךָ מִתַּחְתֶּיךָ״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁמֵּתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ רִאשׁוֹנָה, כְּאִילּוּ חָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בְּיָמָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֶּן אָדָם הִנְנִי לֹקֵחַ מִמְּךָ אֶת מַחְמַד עֵינֶיךָ בְּמַגֵּפָה לֹא תִסְפֹּד וְלֹא תִבְכֶּה וְלֹא תָבוֹא דִּמְעָתֶךָ״. וּכְתִיב: ״וָאֲדַבֵּר אֶל הָעָם בַּבֹּקֶר וַתָּמׇת אִשְׁתִּי בָּעָרֶב״. וּכְתִיב: ״הִנְנִי מְחַלֵּל אֶת מִקְדָּשִׁי גְּאוֹן עֻזְּכֶם מַחְמַד עֵינֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan, and some say Rabbi Elazar, says: A man's wife does not die unless** his creditors **ask him for money** that he owes **and he does not have** it, **as it is stated: "If you do not have with what to pay, why should he take away your bed from under you?"** (Proverbs 22:27). The bed mentioned in the verse alludes to one's wife. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** For **any man whose first wife dies, it is as if the Temple were destroyed in his days, as it is stated: "Son of man, behold, I take away from you the desire of your eyes with a stroke; yet neither shall you make lamentation nor weep, neither shall your tears run down"** (Ezekiel 24:16). **And it is written: "So I spoke to the people in the morning and in the evening my wife died"** (Ezekiel 24:18). **And it is written** in the continuation of the same passage: **"Behold I will profane My Sanctuary, the pride of your power, the desire of your eyes"** (Ezekiel 24:21), illustrating that a man's wife is as precious to him as the Temple is for the entire Jewish nation.

Rabbi Yochanan — and some attribute this to Rabbi Elazar — teaches that a man's wife dies only when he faces debts he cannot repay: *im ein lecha l'shalem lamah yikach mishkavcha mitachtecha* — if you cannot pay, why should your bedfellow be taken from beneath you. This is a striking *gemara*. Rabbi Yochanan then adds: *kol adam shemeta ishto rishonah k'ilu charav Beit HaMikdash b'yamav* — for any man whose wife dies before him, it is as if the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed in his days. The proof comes from *Yechezkel*, where the prophet is told that God will take away *machmad eineicha* — the delight of your eyes — in a plague, and where the Temple itself is called *machmad eineichem*. The death of Yechezkel's wife and the destruction of the *Mikdash* are juxtaposed explicitly in those verses. Clarifying the phrase *ishto rishonah*: it refers not to a first wife as opposed to a second, but to the wife who dies before her husband — that is, whose death he lives to experience.

### Darkness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁמֵּתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּיָמָיו, עוֹלָם חָשַׁךְ בַּעֲדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אוֹר חָשַׁךְ בְּאׇהֳלוֹ וְנֵרוֹ עָלָיו יִדְעָךְ״. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר: פְּסִיעוֹתָיו מִתְקַצְּרוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יֵצְרוּ צַעֲדֵי אוֹנוֹ״. רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר: עֲצָתוֹ נוֹפֶלֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתַשְׁלִיכֵהוּ עֲצָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Alexandri says:** For **any man whose wife dies in his days, the world is dark for him, as it is stated: "The light shall be dark in his tent and his lamp over him shall be put out"** (Job 18:6), since the word tent is commonly employed as a metonym for a wife. **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: His steps get shorter, as it is stated: "The steps of his strength shall be constrained"** (Job 18:7). **Rabbi Abbahu says: His counsel falls, as it is stated: "And his own counsel shall cast him down"** (Job 18:7).

Rabbi Alexandri adds: *kol adam shemeta ishto b'yamav olam chashach ba'ado* — for any man whose wife dies in his days, the world grows dark around him, as the verse says *or chashach b'oholo v'nero alav yidach* — the light darkens in his tent and his candle is extinguished over him. Rabbi Yosei bar Chanina adds that such a man's stride shortens — *pisiotov mitkatzrot* — one can almost picture the diminished, shuffling gait of grief. Rabbi Abahu adds that *itzato nofelet* — his counsel fails him, his judgment and capacity for wisdom become unreliable.

### Shidduchim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קָשֶׁה לְזַוְּוגָם כִּקְרִיעַת יַם סוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים מוֹשִׁיב יְחִידִים בַּיְתָה מוֹצִיא אֲסִירִים בַּכּוֹשָׁרוֹת״. אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״מוֹצִיא אֲסִירִים״ אֶלָּא ״כְּמוֹצִיא אֲסִירִים״, אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״בַּכּוֹשָׁרוֹת״ אֶלָּא ״בְּכִי וְשִׁירוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is as **difficult to match** a couple **as the splitting of the Red Sea, as it is stated: "God makes the solitary dwell in a house; He brings out prisoners into prosperity"** (Psalms 68:7). **Do not read** the verse as **"brings out prisoners"; rather,** read it as: **Like bringing out prisoners.** Accordingly, the act described in the first clause of the verse, God's causing the solitary to dwell in a house, i.e., to marry, is compared to the act described in the next clause in the verse, i.e., bringing out prisoners. And do not read the verse as **"into prosperity [*bakkosharot*]"; rather,** read it as: **Crying and singing [*bekhi veshirot*],** which alludes to the splitting of the Red Sea, when there was both crying and singing.

Rabbah bar Bar Chana, citing Rabbi Yochanan, states: *kasheh lezivugam kepriat yam suf* — making a match is as difficult as the splitting of the sea. The verse *elohim moshiv yechidim baitah* is read with *bakosharot* parsed as *bechi veshirut* — with weeping and with song. One might ask: is this really true? After all, we know that Rav Yehuda teaches in the name of Rav that forty days before a child is formed, a *bat kol* goes out and declares *bat ploni l'ploni* — the daughter of so-and-so for so-and-so. If the match is preordained at birth, how can it be as difficult as splitting the sea?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם קוֹדֶם יְצִירַת הַוָּלָד, בַּת קוֹל יוֹצֵאת וְאוֹמֶרֶת ״בַּת פְּלוֹנִי לִפְלוֹנִי״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּזִוּוּג רִאשׁוֹן, הָא בְּזִוּוּג שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so** that it is this difficult to find a match? **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: Forty days before the formation of the fetus a Divine Voice emerges and states: The daughter of so-and-so** shall be the wife **of so-and-so?** Why should matching them be so difficult, since they are prepared for this from before their birth? The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. This** latter statement, about predestined matches, **is** stated **with regard to the first match; that** former statement, about the difficulty of matchmaking, **is** stated **with regard to the second match.**

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *lo kashya ha bezivug rishon ha bezivug sheni* — there is no contradiction. The first marriage is preordained from before birth and in that sense is not hard at all. It is the *zivug sheni* — the second marriage — that is as difficult as splitting the sea. The weight of this discussion is palpable. These are texts that are difficult to read without thinking of people in one's own life — those who have lost spouses, those who are seeking a match — and that dimension of personal resonance deserves acknowledgment.

### Eshet Neurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן: לַכֹּל יֵשׁ תְּמוּרָה, חוּץ מֵאֵשֶׁת נְעוּרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵשֶׁת נְעוּרִים כִּי תִמָּאֵס״. מַתְנֵי לַהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ: אֵין אָדָם מוֹצֵא קוֹרַת רוּחַ אֶלָּא מֵאִשְׁתּוֹ רִאשׁוֹנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְהִי מְקוֹרְךָ בָרוּךְ וּשְׂמַח מֵאֵשֶׁת נְעוּרֶיךָ״. אָמַר לוֹ: כְּגוֹן מַאן? אָמַר לוֹ: כְּגוֹן אִמָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥman says: For everything** that is lost **there is a substitute, except for** one's **wife from youth** who dies, **as it is stated: "And a wife from youth, can she be rejected?"** (Isaiah 54:6). **Rav Yehuda taught Rav Yitzḥak, his son: A man finds calmness of spirit only from his first wife, as it is stated: "Let your fountain be blessed and have joy with the wife of your youth"** (Proverbs 5:18). Rav Yitzḥak, his son, **said to him: Such as whom?** Rav Yehuda **said to him: Such as your mother.**

Shmuel bar Nachman teaches: *lakol yesh tmurah chutz me'eshet neurim* — for everything there is a substitute or replacement, except for the wife of one's youth, the *eshet neurim*. The verse in Isaiah — *ve'eshet neurim ki timas* — is read as a rhetorical question: can the wife of your youth truly become despicable to you? Rav Yehuda teaches his son Rav Yitzchak that a person achieves true *korat ruach* — contentment and equanimity — only through his first wife, citing the verse *yehi mekorecha varuch usmach me'eshet neurecha*. When Rav Yitzchak asks for a concrete example, Rav Yehuda replies: *kegon imach* — like your mother. This is, one might observe, the first recorded your-mother joke in the *gemara*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא מַקְרֵי לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ: ״וּמוֹצֶא אֲנִי מָר מִמָּוֶת אֶת הָאִשָּׁה אֲשֶׁר הִיא מְצוֹדִים וַחֲרָמִים״. וְאָמַר לוֹ: כְּגוֹן מַאן? וְאָמַר לוֹ: כְּגוֹן אִמָּךְ. מִיתְקָף תַּקִּיפָא, עַיבּוֹרֵי מְעַבְּרָא בְּמִלַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **Is that so? But didn't Rav Yehuda** once **read to Rav Yitzḥak, his son,** from the verse: **"And I find more bitter than death the woman whose** heart **is snares and nets"** (Ecclesiastes 7:26)? **And** Rav Yitzḥak **said to him: Such as whom? And** Rav Yehuda **said to him: Such as your mother.** The Gemara responds: This is not a contradiction. Granted, **she is difficult** and angry, but afterward she **is conciliatory,** so she is both more bitter than death and a source of calmness and joy for him, at different times.

Rav Yitzchak then pushes back: but didn't Rav Yehuda himself teach from *Kohelet* the verse *u-motze ani mar mi-mavet et ha'isha* — I find the woman more bitter than death? Rav Yehuda's response: *kegon imach — mitzvavcha kefifa iburei ma'avra ummilla* — like your mother: there are times it is as constricting as a basket, but that too passes and the goodness returns. The picture of marriage that emerges here is not of unvarying bliss but of something that encompasses both bitterness and sweetness, and whose depth of satisfaction, over time, is irreplaceable.

## Kohanim Grooming

### Haircut Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מֶלֶךְ מִסְתַּפֵּר בְּכׇל יוֹם, כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט אֶחָד לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One may not see** the king when he is having his hair cut. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A king has his hair cut every day. A High Priest** waits **from the eve of Shabbat to the eve of Shabbat** between haircuts. **An ordinary priest** has his hair cut **once every thirty days.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* laying out the haircut schedule for the three levels of sacred personnel: the king receives a haircut every day — *melech mistaper bechol yom* — because the verse says *melech be-yofyo techazenah eineicha*, a king should be seen in his beauty. The *Kohen Gadol* receives a haircut *me'erev Shabbat le-erev Shabbat* — once a week, every Friday. The ordinary *kohen* receives one *achat lishloshim yom* — once every thirty days.

### Mishmar Rotation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֶלֶךְ מִסְתַּפֵּר בְּכׇל יוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מֶלֶךְ בְּיׇפְיוֹ תֶּחֱזֶינָה עֵינֶיךָ״. כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹאִיל וּמִשְׁמָרוֹת מִתְחַדְּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **A king has his hair cut every day, as it is stated: "Your eyes should see the king in his beauty"** (Isaiah 33:17), so his beauty must be tended to daily. **A High Priest** waits **from the eve of Shabbat** to the eve of Shabbat, as **Rav Shmuel bar Naḥman says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Since new** priestly **watches begin** every week on the eve of Shabbat, the High Priest must have his hair cut in order to look his best when the new watch arrives.

The reason the *Kohen Gadol* cuts his hair weekly is connected to the rotation of the *mishmarot* — the priestly divisions. Each week a new group of *kohanim* arrives at the *Mikdash* to begin their term of service, and the *Kohen Gadol* must appear dignified and well-groomed to receive them on that first day.

### 30-Day Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט אֶחָד לִשְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרֹאשָׁם לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ וּפֶרַע לֹא יְשַׁלֵּחוּ כָּסוֹם יִכְסְמוּ אֶת רָאשֵׁיהֶם״, וְיָלֵיף פֶּרַע פֶּרַע מִנָּזִיר. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״פֶּרַע לֹא יְשַׁלֵּחוּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״גַּדֵּל פֶּרַע שְׂעַר רֹאשׁוֹ״. מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁלֹשִׁים, אַף כָּאן שְׁלֹשִׁים. וּתְנַן נָמֵי: סְתַם נְזִירוּת שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation of the *baraita*: **An ordinary priest** has his hair cut **once every thirty days, as it is written** with regard to the priests: **"They shall not shave their heads, and long hair they shall not grow out; they shall trim only their heads"** (Ezekiel 44:20), **and derive** by verbal analogy **"long hair"** in this verse **from "long hair"** in the verse written with regard to the **nazirite. It is written here,** with regard to the priests: **"And long hair they shall not grow out," and it is written there,** with regard to the nazirite: **"Long hair growing on his head"** (Numbers 6:5). **Just as there,** nazirite hair growth is at least **thirty** days, **so too here, it is thirty** days, and the priest must cut his hair before it is called long. **And we also learn** in a mishna (*Nazir* 5a): A term of **naziriteship** of **unspecified** length is **thirty days.**

The thirty-day cycle for the ordinary *kohen* is derived from a *gezeirah shavah* using the word *pera*. In *Yechezkel*, the verse reads *rosham lo yifrau ufera lo yeshalchu kasom yichsemu et rosheihem* — they shall not grow their hair in a disordered way but shall cut it regularly. And in the laws of *nazir*, the verse uses *gadel pera se'ar rosho*. The shared word *pera* links the two contexts: just as the minimum period of *nezirut* — and thus the minimum period over which *pera* can grow — is thirty days, so too the *kohen* must cut his hair at thirty-day intervals. The basis for the thirty-day minimum of *stam nezirut* is itself derived by Rav Matnah from *gematriya*: the verse *kadosh yihyeh* — *yihyeh* being *yud-heh-yud-heh*, which equals ten plus five plus ten plus five, totaling thirty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״קָדֹשׁ יִהְיֶה״, בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא תְּלָתִין הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** in the verse about the nazirite, **from where do we** derive that thirty days is the minimum length of a term? **Rav Mattana says: As the verse states: "He shall be [*yihye*] holy"** (Numbers 6:5): The word "*yihye*" **has the numerical value of thirty.**

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֵימָא לָא לִירַבּוֹ כְּלָל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי כְּתִיב ״לֹא יְשַׁלֵּחוּ פֶּרַע״ – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״וּפֶרַע [לֹא יְשַׁלֵּחוּ]״ – פֶּרַע לֶיהֱוֵי, שַׁלּוֹחֵי לָא לִשַׁלְּחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Say** that the priests **should not grow** their hair **at all,** but shave their heads instead. Abaye **said to him: Had it been written: They shall not grow long hair,** it would be **as you said,** but **now that it is written: "And long hair"** (Ezekiel 44:20), it **can be** somewhat **long hair,** but **they should not grow it out** very long.

Rav Pappa raises a challenge to Abaye: perhaps the instruction *pera lo yishalchu* should be read as requiring haircuts more often than every thirty days, or even as prohibiting any *pera* growth at all — that is, the *kohanim* should keep their heads entirely shorn at all times, not merely trim them periodically.

### Service Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָאִידָּנָא נָמֵי? דּוּמְיָא דְּיַיִן: מָה יַיִן, בִּזְמַן בִּיאָה הוּא דְּאָסוּר, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַן בִּיאָה שְׁרֵי – אַף פְּרוּעֵי רֹאשׁ, בִּזְמַן בִּיאָה אָסוּר, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַן בִּיאָה שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that the restriction on priests growing long hair is a mitzva by Torah law and not just a regulation for its time, **now too,** after the destruction of the Temple, priests should be required to have their hair cut every thirty days. The Gemara responds: The prohibition against priests growing long hair **is similar to** the prohibition against their drinking **wine. Just as** with regard to **wine,** only **at** the **time of** their **entering** the Temple **is it forbidden** to the priests, but **when it is not** the **time of** their **entering** the Temple it is **permitted, so too,** with regard to **those with long hair, at** the **time of** their **entering** the Temple, growing their hair long is **prohibited,** but **when** it is **not** the **time of** their **entering** the Temple, it is **permitted.**

Abaye responds: the prohibition against *parua rosh* applies only *bizman biah* — at the time of entering the *Mikdash* for service. Outside of active service, it is no longer applicable. The analogy is to wine: just as wine is forbidden to a *kohen* only at the moment he is about to enter the *Mikdash* for service, and is entirely permitted to him when he is not serving, so too the prohibition on unkempt hair is tied exclusively to the moment of *biat haMikdash*.

## Wine & Service

### Wine Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיַיִן שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַן בִּיאָה שְׁרֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר, אוֹמֵר אֲנִי: כֹּהֲנִים אֲסוּרִין לִשְׁתּוֹת יַיִן לְעוֹלָם, אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁתַּקָּנָתוֹ קַלְקָלָתוֹ. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּמַאן שָׁתוּ כָּהֲנֵי חַמְרָא הָאִידָּנָא? כְּרַבִּי. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבָּנַן אָסְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this comparison: **And** is **wine permitted when** it is **not** the **time of** their **entering** the Temple? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that actually, it is **always prohibited** for **priests to drink wine,** even since the destruction of the Temple, **but what can I do, as his repair is his ruin,** meaning that it is the same destruction of the Temple that ruined and confused all priestly regulations and that also improved priests' lifestyle by enabling them to drink. **And Abaye said: In accordance with whose** opinion **do priests drink wine nowadays? In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara concludes **by inference that** if Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is singled out as a lone opinion, then **the Rabbis prohibit** it.

Since the prohibition on *parua rosh* applies only at the time of entering the *Mikdash*, the same is clearly true of wine: *kohanim* who are not currently on their *mishmar* and not entering the *Mikdash* to serve may drink freely.

### Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *omer ani Kohanim assurim lishtot yayin l'olam* — I maintain that *kohanim* are forbidden to drink wine at all times, not only during service. Yet he concedes the practical reality: *aval mah e'eseh shetakanto kalkalato* — but what can I do? The very measure that would fix the situation is what ruins it.

### Future Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, מְהֵרָה יִבָּנֶה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וּבָעֵינַן כֹּהֵן הָרָאוּי לָעֲבוֹדָה, וְלֵיכָּא. הָכָא נָמֵי, בָּעֵינָא כֹּהֵן הָרָאוּי לָעֲבוֹדָה, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **There,** with regard to drinking wine, **this is the reason** for the prohibition: **The Temple will speedily be built, and we need a priest who is fit for the** Temple **service; and** if the priests drink wine **there are none.** Therefore, none of them may drink wine. The Gemara objects: If so, **here too,** with regard to growing hair long, **I need a priest who is fit for** performing **the** Temple **service** as soon as the Temple is rebuilt, **and** if the priests grow their hair long, **there are none.**

The logic of *takanto kalkalato* is striking. *Kohanim* currently drink wine — that is the established custom — and Rabbi Yehuda acknowledges this. But if the *Beit HaMikdash* were rebuilt — which is what all Jews pray for — the ban on wine would presumably resume. The incentives are, as one might observe, entirely backward: the thing that *kohanim* would presumably be praying for would result in their no longer being able to drink wine. Abaye identifies the relevant position: it is Rabbi Yehuda's view that permits current *kohen* wine-drinking, since in his framework the restriction is absolute in principle but deferred in practice due to the absence of the Temple. The majority opinion (*rabanan*), by contrast, restricts the prohibition to times of actual service, making the question of the Temple's rebuilding somewhat less dramatic in its practical implications. The reason the *rabanan* nonetheless do not require *kohanim* to maintain their haircuts every thirty days today is the same as the logic for wine: *meheira yibaneh Beit HaMikdash u-vainan Kohen haraoy l'avoda v'leika* — the Temple may be rebuilt at any moment, and we will need a *kohen* ready to serve. He can simply get a haircut and enter — *efshar d'mistaper v'ayel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא אֶפְשָׁר דִּמְסַפַּר וְעָיֵיל. הָתָם נָמֵי אֶפְשָׁר דְּנָיֵים פּוּרְתָּא וְעָיֵיל, דְּאָמַר רַב אַחָא: דֶּרֶךְ מִיל וְשֵׁינָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מְפִיגִין אֶת הַיַּיִן. וְלָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ: אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁשָּׁתָה כְּדֵי רְבִיעִית, אֲבָל יוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי רְבִיעִית – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּדֶרֶךְ טוֹרַדְתּוֹ וְשֵׁינָה מְשַׁכַּרְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Here it is possible for him to cut his hair and enter** the Temple immediately. The Gemara objects: **There too, it is possible for him to sleep a little and go in, as Rav Aḥa says:** Walking **the distance of a *mil* or sleeping** even **a minimal amount** will **dispel the** effect of **wine** that one has drunk. The Gemara responds: **But wasn't it stated with regard to** Rav Aḥa's statement: **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: They taught** that the wine's effect is dispelled in such a way **only when he drank a quarter-***log*, at most, **but** if he drank **more than a quarter-***log*, **all the more so that** walking that **distance disturbs him and sleep makes him** more **drunk,** unless he sleeps for a long time.

### Drunkenness Cure

The *gemara* discusses the possibility of having a *kohen* sober up quickly in order to serve. Rav Acha teaches: *derech mil v'shaina kol shehu mefigin et ha-yayin* — walking a *mil* or taking even a brief nap will abate the effects of wine. However, Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, qualifies this significantly: *lo shanu ela sheshata k'dei revi'it* — this applies only if he drank up to a *revi'it*. If he drank more than a *revi'it*, walking and sleeping will in fact intensify the intoxication rather than mitigate it.

## Profaning Service

### Death Penalties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן, דִּכְתִיב: ״יַיִן וְשֵׁכָר אַל תֵּשְׁתְּ אַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ... וְלֹא תָמֻתוּ״. אֶלָּא פְּרוּעֵי רֹאשׁ, מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted,** priests entering the Temple **drunk on wine** are liable for death, **as it is written: "Drink no wine nor strong drink, you, nor your sons with you,** when you go into the Tent of Meeting, **that you not die;** it shall be a statute forever throughout your generations" (Leviticus 10:9). **But** as for priests **with long hair, from where do we** derive that they are punished with death at the hand of Heaven?

The *gemara* now establishes that entering the *Mikdash* in a state of intoxication carries the death penalty. The source is explicit: the verse in *Vayikra* states *yayin ve-shechar al tesht atah u-vanecha itach velo tamutu* — do not drink wine or strong drink, you and your sons with you, lest you die. What of *parua rosh* — entering without the proper haircut? That prohibition is explicitly stated in *Yechezkel*: *rosham lo yifrau u-fera lo yeshalchu*. The *gemara* derives that just as intoxication in the Temple carries the death penalty, so too does officiating with unkempt hair, via the *heikesh* — the juxtaposition of the two prohibitions in the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ שְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן לִפְרוּעֵי רֹאשׁ, כְּתִיב: ״וְרֹאשָׁם לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ וּפֶרַע לֹא יְשַׁלֵּחוּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְיַיִן לֹא יִשְׁתּוּ וְגוֹ׳״. מָה שְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן בְּמִיתָה, אַף פְּרוּעֵי רֹאשׁ בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It is from the fact **that** priests **drunk on wine were juxtaposed to those with long hair. It is written: "They shall not shave their heads, and long hair they shall not grow out"** (Ezekiel 44:20), **and it is written** in the next verse: **"Neither shall any priest drink wine** when they enter the inner court" (Ezekiel 44:21). **Just as** priests **drunk on wine** are liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven, **so too, those with long hair** are liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven.

### Profaning Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִינַּהּ: מָה שְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן דְּמַחֲלִי עֲבוֹדָה – אַף פְּרוּעֵי רֹאשׁ דְּמַחֲלִי עֲבוֹדָה. קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And** if so, **from this** very juxtaposition one may learn that **just as** priests **drunk on wine desecrate the** Temple **service, so too, those with long hair desecrate the** Temple **service,** which poses a difficulty for Rav Ashi's statement. The Gemara acknowledges: This poses **a difficulty.**

The *gemara* further derives that both wine and *parua rosh* profane the *avodah* — *machil avoda*. The verse draws together both prohibitions, and the parallel structure implies that just as serving drunk constitutes *chilul ha-avodah*, so too does serving with disordered hair.

### Before Yechezkel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הַאי, עַד דְּלָא אֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל, מַאן אַמְרַהּ? וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה מִתּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ, עַד שֶׁבָּא יְחֶזְקֵאל וְלִימְּדָנוּ: ״כׇּל בֶּן נֵכָר עֶרֶל לֵב וְעֶרֶל בָּשָׂר לֹא יָבוֹא אֶל מִקְדָּשִׁי לְשָׁרְתֵנִי״, עַד דְּלֹא בָּא יְחֶזְקֵאל מַאן אַמְרַהּ? אֶלָּא, גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, וַאֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל וְאַסְמְכַהּ אַקְּרָא. הָכָא נָמֵי, גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, וַאֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל וְאַסְמְכַהּ אַקְּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: This** *halakha*, **until Ezekiel came** and taught it, **who said it?** How can a *halakha* by Torah law be derived from the juxtaposition of verses in Ezekiel? Rav Ashi responds: **And according to your reasoning,** then, with regard to **this** statement **that Rav Ḥisda says: We did not learn this** following **matter from the Torah of Moses, our teacher, until Ezekiel came and taught** it to **us: "No stranger, uncircumcised in heart and uncircumcised in flesh, shall enter into My Sanctuary to serve Me"** (Ezekiel 44:9), **until Ezekiel came** and taught it, **who said it? Rather,** this *halakha* **is learned as a tradition** and therefore was observed for generations, **and Ezekiel came and gave it support by** writing **a verse. Here too,** this *halakha* **is learned as a tradition, and Ezekiel came and gave it support by** writing **a verse.**

Ravina poses a classic question to Rav Ashi: *hai ad delo ata Yechezkel man amrah* — before *Yechezkel* recorded these verses, what was the source for these *halachot*? This is a recurring concern in the *gemara* whenever a *halacha* appears to rest on a late prophetic text: were these rules simply unknown beforehand?

### Mesorah

Rav Chisda answers: *davar zeh mi-torat Moshe Rabbeinu lo lamdanu ad sheba Yechezkel ve-limdanu* — this matter was not taught to us explicitly from the Torah of Moses our teacher until Yechezkel came and taught it. The tradition itself existed — *gemara gemiri lah* — as an oral *mesorah* going back to Sinai. Yechezkel did not innovate the *halacha*; he gave it explicit textual grounding. The *gemara* draws an analogy: just as Yechezkel grounded other long-standing oral traditions in written verse, so too here — *ve'ata Yechezkel ve'asmachah akra* — he came and anchored the received tradition to a *pasuk*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי גָמְרִי הֲלָכָה לְמִיתָה, לְאַחוֹלֵי עֲבוֹדָה לָא גְּמִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And when they learned** this ***halakha*,** it was to teach that a priest who violates the restriction is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. But the fact **that** it is considered **desecration of the** Temple **service is not learned** as a tradition.

## High Priest Hair

### Lulianit Style

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כָּסוֹם יִכְסְמוּ אֶת רָאשֵׁיהֶם״? תָּנָא: כְּמִין תִּסְפּוֹרֶת לוּלְיָינִית. מַאי תִּסְפּוֹרֶת לוּלְיָינִית? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תִּסְפּוּרְתָּא יְחִידָאָה. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד עִיקָּרוֹ שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"they shall trim only their heads"** (Ezekiel 44:20)? The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **Like a *lulyanit* haircut.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **a *lulyanit* haircut? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: A unique haircut.** The Gemara asks again: **What is it like? Rav Ashi says:** The hair is cut so that **the head of** the hair in **this** row is **at the side of the bottom of that** row.

The *gemara* now asks: what exactly is the *tisporet* described in the verse *rosham lo yifrau u-fera lo yeshalchu*? The *tana* answers: *kemin tisporet lulianit* — something like the *lulianit* haircut. What is the *tisporet lulianit*? Rav Ashe explains: *rosho shel zeh betzad ikaro shel zeh* — the tip of one hair lies adjacent to the root of the next. This seems to describe a style in which the hair is so uniformly and carefully cut that each hair's end is flush with the base of the next — suggesting either an extremely precise, closely-cropped look, or perhaps a layered, curved arrangement. The *Rambam* describes it as hair cut uniformly short so that no hair folds over another, resembling a closely harvested field. Some have suggested the term *lulianit* — related to the word for a trapeze or acrobat — implies a curved, looping style where each hair arcs back toward the scalp. The precise visual remains somewhat elusive.

### Ben Alasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי: אֵיזֶהוּ תִּסְפּוֹרֶת שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל? אָמַר לָהֶן: צְאוּ וּרְאוּ מִתִּסְפּוֹרֶת שֶׁל בֶּן אֶלְעָשָׂה. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לֹא עַל חִנָּם פִּיזֵּר בֶּן אֶלְעָשָׂה אֶת מְעוֹתָיו, אֶלָּא כְּדֵי לְהַרְאוֹת בּוֹ תִּסְפּוֹרֶת שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **asked Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **What was the haircut of the High Priest? He said to them: Go out and see the haircut of ben Elasa,** who was the son-in-law of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and who sported the High Priest's hairstyle. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Not for nothing did ben Elasa disburse his money** on his haircuts, **but in order to demonstrate** on himself **the haircut of the High Priest.**

When Rabbi was asked to describe the *Kohen Gadol*'s haircut, he did not attempt a verbal description but instead directed his questioners: *tze'u ur'u mi-tisporet shel Ben Elasha* — go out and observe the haircut of Ben Alasha. Ben Alasha apparently maintained this distinctive style in his own appearance, making him a living demonstration of the *Kohen Gadol*'s haircut.

### Display Purpose

A *baraita* records Rebbe's explanation of why Ben Alasha spent lavishly on his haircuts: *lo al chinam pizer Ben Elasha mamon rav ela kedei lehar'ot bo tisporet shel Kohen Gadol* — it was not for vanity that Ben Alasha spent so much money on his hair, but specifically to display and preserve the knowledge of what the *Kohen Gadol*'s haircut looked like. In a world without a functioning Temple, without a *Kohen Gadol* actively serving, Ben Alasha took it upon himself to embody and transmit this *halacha* through his own appearance — a remarkable act of living *mesorah*. The very fact that such concerns were maintained with such care speaks to the seriousness with which the restoration of the *Beit HaMikdash* was anticipated.
