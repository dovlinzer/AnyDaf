# Berakhot 31b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hannah's Prayer

### Kavana from Heart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הִלְכְתָא גִּבָּרָווֹתָא אִיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מֵהָנֵי קְרָאֵי דְחַנָּה. ״וְחַנָּה הִיא מְדַבֶּרֶת עַל לִבָּהּ״ — מִכָּן לַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּכַוֵּין לִבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **How many significant *halakhot* can be derived from these verses** of the prayer **of Hannah? "And Hannah spoke in her heart,"** the *halakha* that **one who prays must focus his heart** on his prayer is derived.

The Gemara on 31b opens with a statement of Rav Hamnuna: *kama hilchata givarvata ika le-mishma me-hanei karei de-Channah* — how many powerful *halachot* can be inferred from the verses describing Hannah's prayer? The passage builds on the immediately preceding discussion of *kavana* in prayer, including the striking Gemara about Rabbi Akiva and the difference between how he would sway (*shokel*) when praying privately versus in the *tzibbur*. That contrast reflects two concerns simultaneously: sensitivity to others in communal prayer, and the deeper *kavana* that belongs to individual prayer. The verses of Hannah now serve as the primary model for exploring that individual dimension.

The first inference is from the phrase *ve-Channah hi medaberet al libah* — Hannah was speaking from her heart. The Gemara reads this not merely as a description of her sincerity but as a normative requirement: *mikan le-mitpallel tzarich she-yekaven libo* — from here we learn that one who prays must direct his heart. Prayer is not simply the recitation of words; it demands genuine inward engagement with what one is saying.

### Lips Moving Silently

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״רַק שְׂפָתֶיהָ נָּעוֹת״ — מִכָּאן לַמִּתְפַּלֵּל שֶׁיַּחְתּוֹךְ בִּשְׂפָתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **From "Only her lips moved,"** the *halakha* that **one who prays must enunciate** the words **with his lips,** not only contemplate them in his heart, is derived.

From *rak sefateha na'ot* — only her lips were moving — the Gemara derives *mikan le-mitpallel she-yachtoch bi-sfatav*: one must articulate with the lips, literally "cut" with them. This is a fascinating halachic balance. Normally, *halacha* requires speech to be audible — we encountered a lengthy discussion earlier in Berakhot regarding one who cannot hear his own recitation of *Shema*. Here, the rule is precisely the opposite: lips move, but the voice is not heard. Some *acharonim* raise the question of whether, given that *tefillah* is fundamentally *avodah she-ba-lev* — service of the heart — it might suffice to merely think the words. The general consensus is that it does not suffice, though there is a position (*tzad*) that *tefillah* constitutes a kind of silent speech, distinct from ordinary vocalization.

### No Raised Voice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְקוֹלָהּ לֹא יִשָּׁמֵעַ״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁאָסוּר לְהַגְבִּיהַּ קוֹלוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **From "And her voice could not be heard,"** the *halakha* that **one is forbidden to raise his voice in his** *Amida* **prayer** as it must be recited silently is derived.

From *ve-kolah lo yishama* — her voice could not be heard — comes the ruling *she-asur le-hagbi'ah kolo bi-tfilato*: it is forbidden to raise one's voice in prayer. The model of *tefillah* is thus a silent articulation — lips moving without audible sound.

### Drunk Cannot Pray

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּחְשְׁבֶהָ עֵלִי לְשִׁכֹּרָה״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁשִּׁכּוֹר אָסוּר לְהִתְפַּלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **From "So Eli thought her to be drunk,"** the *halakha* that **a drunk person is forbidden to pray.** That is why he rebuked her.

*Vayachsheveha Eli le-shikorah* — Eli presumed Hannah to be drunk. From this, the Gemara derives *she-shikor asur le-hitpallel*: an intoxicated person may not pray. Even if one might argue that minimal *kavana* could allow for *yetzia*, the fundamental problem is one of standing before God — a drunken person cannot do so appropriately. The identification of drunken prayer with *avodah zarah* is later derived from Hannah's own words: *al titein et amatecha lifnei bat beliyaal*. The Gemara reads *beliyaal* through a *gezerah shavah* with a verse in which people of *beliyaal* led a city to worship false gods. The connection may rest on the disrespect shown to God by appearing before Him in such a state, and perhaps also on the fact that certain forms of idolatrous worship historically involved intoxication and artificially induced religious frenzy.

### Duty to Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלֶיהָ עֵלִי עַד מָתַי תִּשְׁתַּכָּרִין וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן לָרוֹאֶה בַּחֲבֵרוֹ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן, צָרִיךְ לְהוֹכִיחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** On the subject of Eli's rebuke of Hannah, as it is stated: **"And Eli said to her: How long will you remain drunk?** Remove your wine from yourself"; **Rabbi Elazar said: From here** the *halakha* that **one who sees in another an unseemly matter, he must reprimand him,** is derived.

Eli's reproof of Hannah — *Ad matai tistakarin*, how long will you remain drunk? — becomes the source for another ruling. Rabbi Elazar derives: *mikan le-ro'eh be-chavero davar meguneh, tzarich le-hochiho* — if one sees another engaged in behavior that is unseemly, one is obligated to rebuke them. Tosafot makes explicit that the obligation applies even where no formal *issur* has been violated; inappropriate conduct alone is sufficient to warrant a loving correction.

### Judge Favorably

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתַּעַן חַנָּה וַתֹּאמֶר לֹא אֲדוֹנִי״. אָמַר עוּלָּא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לֹא אָדוֹן אַתָּה בְּדָבָר זֶה, וְלֹא רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ שׁוֹרָה עָלֶיךָ, שֶׁאַתָּה חוֹשְׁדֵנִי בְּדָבָר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **"And Hannah answered and she said no, my master,** I am a woman of sorrowful spirit, and I have drunk neither wine nor liquor, but I pour out my soul before the Lord." Regarding the words: "No, my master," **Ulla, and some say Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said** that **she said to him,** in an allusion: **With regard to this matter, you are not a master, and the Divine Spirit does not rest upon you, as you** falsely **suspect me of this.**

The Gemara then addresses Hannah's response. She says *lo Adoni* — no, my master. The phrase is interpreted in two ways. According to one tradition (attributed to Ulla, and some say Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Hanina), she was saying: *lo adon atta ba-davar hazeh* — you are not the master here, you do not understand what is happening. *Ve-lo Ruach HaKodesh shorah alecha* — the divine spirit was not resting upon you, because you have suspected me of this impropriety.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, הָכִי אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לֹא אָדוֹן אַתָּה? לָאו אִיכָּא שְׁכִינָה וְרוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ גַּבָּךְ, שֶׁדַּנְתַּנִי לְכַף חוֹבָה וְלֹא דַּנְתַּנִי לְכַף זְכוּת?
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** another version of her response. **She said to him,** questioning: **Aren't you a master? Aren't the Divine Presence and Divine Spirit with you that you judged me to be guilty, and you did not judge me to be innocent?**

A second version reads Hannah as saying: *lav ika Shekhinah ve-Ruach HaKodesh gavach* — the divine presence was not with you, *she-dantani le-chaf chovah ve-lo dantani le-chaf zechut* — for you judged me by tipping the balance of guilt, rather than the balance of merit. When one sees another person act in a manner that appears problematic, the obligation is to tip those scales toward merit — *le-chaf zechut* — to assume that the behavior is proper rather than improper.

There is a striking *hagah* worth noting here: how could Eli, a *kohen gadol* equipped with the *urim ve-tumim*, have made such an error? One answer (attributed to the Vilna Gaon) is that the *urim ve-tumim* worked by having the letters of the tribal names rise before the *kohen*, and that the letters *shin, chaf, resh, heh* — which together spell *shikorah* (drunk) — appeared to Eli, when in fact the divine message was *kesherah* (upright), that she was like *Sarah*, barren and praying. This is admittedly a *vort* rather than *pshat*, but it is a beautiful one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיַיִן וְשֵׁכָר לֹא שָׁתִיתִי״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן לַנֶּחְשָׁד בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ, שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Hannah's explanation that **"I have drunk neither wine nor liquor," Rabbi Elazar said: From here** the *halakha* is derived **that one who is suspected of something of which he is not** guilty cannot suffice merely with the personal knowledge of his innocence, but **must inform** the one who suspects him that he is innocent and clear himself of suspicion.

From Hannah's own correction of Eli — *yayin ve-shechar lo shatiti* — Rabbi Elazar derives a further obligation: *mikan le-nechshad be-davar she-ein bo, she-tzarich lehodio* — one who has been wrongly suspected must inform the person who suspected them. One must not allow false impressions to persist unchallenged.

### Appease Wrongly Accused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּעַן עֵלִי וַיֹּאמֶר לְכִי לְשָׁלוֹם״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן לַחוֹשֵׁד אֶת חֲבֵרוֹ בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ, שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְפַיְּיסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And Eli answered and said: May you go in peace"** (I Samuel 1:17). **Rabbi Elazar said: From here** the *halakha* is derived **that one who suspects another of something that he has not** done, **he must appease him.**

When Eli recognized his error and said *lechi le-shalom* — go in peace — Rabbi Elazar derives yet another obligation: *mikan le-choshed et chaveiro be-davar she-ein bo, she-tzarich le-fayeso* — one who has wrongly suspected another must appease that person and make peace with them.

### Bless the Wronged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְבָרְכוֹ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וֵאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יִתֵּן אֶת שֵׁלָתֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover,** the one who suspected him **must bless him,** as Eli continued and offered Hannah a blessing, **as it is stated: "And may the God of Israel grant your request** that you have asked of Him."

*Ve-lo od* — and not only that — *ela she-tzarich le-varcho*: one must go further and offer a blessing to the person wrongly accused. For Eli did not merely send Hannah away in peace; he blessed her: *ve-Elohei Yisrael yitein et she'elatcha* — may the God of Israel grant your request.

It is worth noting that while this entire passage is introduced as deriving *halachot* of *tefillah*, most of what has been extracted here concerns interpersonal obligations — the duty to rebuke, to judge favorably, to correct false impressions, to seek appeasement, and to bless the wronged. Only one or two of the *halachot* directly concern the laws of prayer itself.

## Hannah's Bold Prayer

### Tzevaot Divine Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּדֹּר נֶדֶר וַתֹּאמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִיּוֹם שֶׁבָּרָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶת עוֹלָמוֹ, לֹא הָיָה אָדָם שֶׁקְּרָאוֹ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא ״צְבָאוֹת״, עַד שֶׁבָּאתָה חַנָּה וּקְרָאַתּוּ ״צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Incidental to this discussion of Hannah's prayer, the Gemara explores related topics. In her prayer, Hannah said: **"And she swore an oath and said, Lord of Hosts [*Tzeva'ot*]** if You will indeed look upon the affliction of Your maidservant and remember me, and not forget Your maidservant and will give Your maidservant a male child, I will give him to the Lord all the days of his life, and there shall be no razor come upon his head." **Rabbi Elazar said: From the day that the Holy One, Blessed be He, created His world, there was no person who called the Holy One, Blessed be He, Lord of Hosts until Hannah came and called Him Lord of Hosts.** This is the first time in the Bible that God is referred to by this name.

The passage continues: *va-tidor neder va-tomar — Hashem Tzevaot*. Rabbi Elazar observes that from the day God created the world, no one had ever invoked the divine name *Tzevaot* — the Lord of Hosts — until Hannah did so. *Tzevaot* derives from *tzava*, meaning army or host, evoking the heavenly legions. Hannah chose this name deliberately. As the Gemara explains, she said before God: *Ribbono shel Olam, mikol tzevaei tzevaot she-barata be-olamecha, kashe be-einecha she-titein li bein echad?* — Master of the Universe, with all the hosts of hosts, all the galaxies and universes You have created, is it so difficult for You to give me one son?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה חַנָּה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִכׇּל צִבְאֵי צְבָאוֹת שֶׁבָּרָאתָ בְּעוֹלָמְךָ קָשֶׁה בְּעֵינֶיךָ שֶׁתִּתֵּן לִי בֵּן אֶחָד?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar explains that **Hannah said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe,** are You not the Lord of the Hosts, and **of all of the hosts and hosts** of creations **that You created in Your world, is it difficult in Your eyes to grant me one son?**

## Aggressive Prayer

### Poor Man Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשָׁל לַמָּה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁעָשָׂה סְעוּדָה לַעֲבָדָיו. בָּא עָנִי אֶחָד וְעָמַד עַל הַפֶּתַח, אָמַר לָהֶם: תְּנוּ לִי פְּרוּסָה אַחַת! וְלֹא הִשְׁגִּיחוּ עָלָיו. דָּחַק וְנִכְנַס אֵצֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: אֲדוֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ, מִכׇּל סְעוּדָה שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ קָשֶׁה בְּעֵינֶיךָ לִיתֵּן לִי פְּרוּסָה אֶחָת?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests **a parable: To what is this similar?** It is similar to **a flesh and blood king who made a feast for his servants. A poor person came and stood at the door. He said to them: Give me one slice** of bread! **And they paid him no attention. He pushed and entered before the king. He said to him: My lord, the King, from this entire feast that you have prepared, is it so difficult in your eyes to give me a single slice** of bread?

The Gemara offers a *mashal* to illuminate Hannah's posture: a human king made a feast for his servants, and a poor person stood at the doorway asking for a scrap of bread. When the servants ignored him, he forced his way in to the king himself and said: *Adoni ha-Melech, kol se'udah she-asita, kashe be-einecha liten li prusah achat?* — My master the king, with this entire lavish feast you have prepared, you cannot give me one piece of bread? The parallel to Hannah's prayer is clear: she forced her way, as it were, to the King Himself, bypassing every intermediary, to demand what should have been a simple request. As was noted, the image of forcing one's way into the king's presence echoes the broader theme of aggressive, insistent prayer running through this entire passage.

### Conditional Threat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִם רָאֹה תִרְאֶה״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמְרָה חַנָּה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, ״אִם רָאֹה״ — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — ״תִּרְאֶה״. אֵלֵךְ וְאֶסְתַּתֵּר בִּפְנֵי אֶלְקָנָה בַּעֲלִי, וְכֵיוָן דְּמִסְתַּתַּרְנָא מַשְׁקוּ לִי מֵי סוֹטָה, וְאִי אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה תּוֹרָתְךָ פְּלַסְתֵּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״.
>
> **Translation:** As for the double language in the verse, **"if you will look upon [*im ra'o tireh*]," Rabbi Elazar said: Hannah said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, if You will look upon [*ra'o***] me now, **fine, and if not,** in any case **You will see** [***tireh***]. What was Hannah threatening? She said: **I will go and seclude myself** with another man **before Elkana, my husband. Since I secluded myself, they will force me to drink the *sota* water** to determine whether or not I have committed adultery. I will be found innocent, **and** since **You will not make Your Torah false [*pelaster*],** I will bear children. With regards to a woman who is falsely suspected of adultery and drank the *sota* water, the Torah **says:** "And if the woman was not defiled, but was pure, then **she shall be acquitted and she shall conceive"** (Numbers 5:28).

The Gemara then addresses the phrase *im ra'oh tireh* — if You will see. Rabbi Elazar explains that Hannah was in effect issuing a conditional threat: *im ra'oh* — if You see my affliction, *mutav* — good; but *ve-im lav* — if not — *estater bifnei Elkanah ba'ali* — I will seclude myself with another man in the presence of my husband Elkanah. Once I have done so, he will bring me through the *sotah* process, and I will drink the *mei sotah*. And then — *ve-i atah oseh Toratecha palaster* — You will not be able to make Your Torah into a falsehood. The word *palaster* is of Greek origin; Steinsaltz notes that it derives from the Greek *plastos*, meaning a molder, and is used metaphorically to mean false or counterfeit.

The force of the argument rests on the verse *ve-niktah ve-nizre'ah zara* — she will be cleansed and she will conceive. If a woman suspected of adultery is innocent and drinks the waters, she will bear children.

### Sotah Waters Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִם הָיְתָה עֲקָרָה — נִפְקֶדֶת, שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר, אִם הָיְתָה יוֹלֶדֶת בְּצַעַר יוֹלֶדֶת בְּרֶיוַח, נְקֵבוֹת יוֹלֶדֶת זְכָרִים שְׁחוֹרִים יוֹלֶדֶת לְבָנִים קְצָרִים יוֹלֶדֶת אֲרוּכִּים, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** However, Rabbi Elazar's opinion **works out well** according **to the one who said** that the verse means: **If she were barren, she will be remembered** by God and granted children. That works out **well. But** according **to the one who said** that the verse means that childbearing will be easier and more successful, i.e., **if she had** previously **given birth with pain,** she now **gives birth with ease,** or if she had previously given birth to **daughters,** she now **gives birth to sons,** or if she had previously given birth to **black** children, considered to be unattractive, she now **gives birth to fair** children, or if she had previously given birth to **short,** weak children, she **gives birth to tall,** strong children, **what can be said?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה עֲקָרָה — נִפְקֶדֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִם כֵּן, יֵלְכוּ כׇּל הָעֲקָרוֹת כּוּלָּן וְיִסְתַּתְּרוּ, וְזוֹ שֶׁלֹּא קִלְקְלָה — נִפְקֶדֶת. אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה יוֹלֶדֶת בְּצַעַר — יוֹלֶדֶת בְּרֶיוַח, קְצָרִים — יוֹלֶדֶת אֲרוּכִּים, שְׁחוֹרִים — יוֹלֶדֶת לְבָנִים, אֶחָד — יוֹלֶדֶת שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that the *tanna'im* disputed the interpretation of the verse in Numbers: **"Then she shall be acquitted and she shall conceive" teaches that if she was barren, she will be remembered** by God and granted children; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva said to him: If so, all barren women will go and seclude themselves** with men who are not their husbands, **and any** woman **who did not** commit the **sin** of adultery **will be remembered** by God and granted children. **Rather,** the verse **teaches** that this is merely a promise for greater ease in childbirth; **if she has** previously **given birth with pain,** she now **gives birth with ease,** if she has previously given birth to **short** children, she **gives birth to tall** children, if she has previously given birth to **black** children, she now **gives birth to fair** children, if she has previously given birth to **one** child, she now **gives birth to two** children.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אִם רָאֹה תִרְאֶה״ — דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rabbi Akiva's explanation, **what** is derived from the double language uttered by Hannah: ***Im ra'o tireh*? The Torah spoke in the language of men,** meaning that this double language is not extraordinary and nothing may be derived from it. It is common biblical vernacular.

The Gemara immediately qualifies this: the argument only works according to the opinion that the *sotah* waters cause a barren woman to become fertile — *im hayta akara, nifkedet*. This is the position of Rabbi Yishmael, who derives it from *ve-niktah ve-nizre'ah zara*.

Rabbi Akiva objects: if this were the effect, all barren women would arrange to be secluded before their husbands and go through this process in order to conceive. He argues instead that the verse refers to improvements in the quality of childbearing: a woman who had given birth with pain would now do so with ease, one who had borne girls would now bear boys, one who had borne dark-complexioned children would bear fair-complexioned ones, one who had borne short children would bear tall ones, one who had borne only one at a time would have multiple births. These benefits, while real, are apparently not so attractive as to motivate widespread manipulation of the *sotah* procedure.

According to Rabbi Akiva, then, Hannah could not have been threatening to force God's hand into making a barren woman conceive through this mechanism. How is the double language of *ra'oh tireh* explained? The Gemara answers: *dibrah Torah ki-leshon bnei adam* — the Torah simply speaks in the manner of ordinary human expression. The doubling is not to be over-interpreted.

## Hannah's Bold Prayer…

### Three Women's Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּעֳנִי אֲמָתֶךָ״. ״אַל תִשְׁכַּח אֶת אֲמָתֶךָ״. ״וְנָתַתָּה לַאֲמָתְךָ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שָׁלֹשׁ אֲמָתוֹת הַלָּלוּ לָמָּה? — אָמְרָה חַנָּה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּדְקֵי מִיתָה בָּרָאתָ בָּאִשָּׁה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: שְׁלֹשָׁה דִּבְקֵי מִיתָה. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: נִדָּה, וְחַלָּה וְהַדְלָקַת הַנֵּר. כְּלוּם עָבַרְתִּי עַל אַחַת מֵהֶן?!
>
> **Translation:** In the oath/prayer uttered by Hannah, she refers to herself as "Your servant" [*amatekha*] three times: **"The affliction of Your maidservant…**and **not forget Your maidservant and will give Your maidservant."** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: Why** are **these three maidservants [*amatot*]** cited in the verse? They are cited to teach that **Hannah said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, You have created three crucibles** potentially leading to **death in a woman,** where she is particularly vulnerable. Alternatively, **some say:** Master of the Universe, You have created **three accelerants of death** in a woman. **They are** mitzvot that, as a rule, pertain to women: Observing the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman,** separating ***ḥalla*** from dough, **and lighting** Shabbat **candles. Have I ever violated one of them?**

The Gemara continues with the three occurrences of the word *amatcha* — your maidservant — in Hannah's prayer. Rabbi Yosei bar Hanina asks why this word appears three times. He explains that Hannah was invoking them as a response to the three *bidkei mita*, or *difkei mita*, that God created for a woman — three obligations whose violation, according to the *Mishnah* in *Bameh Madlikin*, can bring death in childbirth: *niddah*, *challah*, and *hadlakat ha-ner*, the kindling of the Shabbat candle.

These three *mitzvot* correspond in a meaningful way to the domestic sphere of a woman's life: *challah* to the preparation of food, *hadlakat ha-ner* to the light of the home, and *niddah* to the most intimate dimensions of married life. Hannah's point was: *klum avarti al achat mehen* — I have not transgressed any one of these obligations. I have fulfilled all my *mitzvot*. Now give me what I am asking for.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנָתַתָּה לַאֲמָתְךָ זֶרַע אֲנָשִׁים״. מַאי ״זֶרַע אֲנָשִׁים״? אָמַר רַב: גַּבְרָא בְּגוּבְרִין. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: זֶרַע שֶׁמּוֹשֵׁחַ שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, וּמַאן אִינּוּן — שָׁאוּל וְדָוִד. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: זֶרַע שֶׁשָּׁקוּל כִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, וּמַאן אִינּוּן — מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן בְּכֹהֲנָיו וּשְׁמוּאֵל בְּקוֹרְאֵי שְׁמוֹ״. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: ״זֶרַע אֲנָשִׁים״ — זֶרַע שֶׁמּוּבְלָע בֵּין אֲנָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **"an offspring of men"? Rav said:** Hannah prayed for **a man among men,** a son who would be outstanding and exceptional. **And Shmuel said:** This expression means **an offspring who will anoint two men** to royalty. **And who were they? Saul and David. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Hannah prayed that she would bear **an offspring who** would be **the equivalent of two** of the world's greatest **men. And who were they? Moses and Aaron. As it is stated: "Moses and Aaron among His priests, and Samuel among those who call His name"** (Psalms 99:6). In this verse, Hannah's son, Samuel, is equated to Moses and Aaron. **And the Rabbis say: "An offspring of men":** Hannah prayed for **an offspring who would be inconspicuous among men,** that he would not stand out in any way.

The Gemara then addresses the phrase *ve-natata le-amatcha zera anashim* — You shall give Your maidservant a seed of men. What is meant by *zera anashim*? Several interpretations are offered. Rav understands it as *gavra be-govrin* — a man among men, a person who stands out among others, an outstanding individual, as Rashi explains. Shmuel interprets it as *zera she-mosheach shnei anashim* — seed that will anoint two men — namely Shaul and David, whom her son Samuel would anoint as kings of Israel. Rabbi Yochanan interprets it as *zera she-shakul ke-shnei anashim* — seed that is equivalent in stature to two great people — namely Moses and Aaron, as the verse places Samuel alongside them: *Moshe ve-Aharon be-chohanav u-Shmuel be-korei shemo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אֲמַר לֹא אָרוֹךְ וְלֹא גּוּץ, וְלֹא קָטָן וְלֹא אַלָּם, וְלֹא צָחוֹר וְלֹא גִּיחוֹר, וְלֹא חָכָם וְלֹא טִפֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** in explanation: Hannah prayed that her son would not be conspicuous among men; **neither too tall nor too short; neither too small nor too fat; neither too white nor too red; neither too smart nor too stupid.**

By contrast, the anonymous *rabanan* offer the interpretation *zera she-muvla bein anashim* — seed that is absorbed among people, that does not stand out. Rav Dimi elaborates: *lo aroch ve-lo gutz* — not too tall and not too short; *ve-lo katan ve-lo ilem* — not too thin and not too fat; *ve-lo tzachor ve-lo gichor* — not too fair-skinned and not too dark; *ve-lo chacham ve-lo tipesh* — not too clever and not too dim-witted. In other words, completely average.

This is striking on multiple levels. It is worth observing that the interpreters who settled on "average" were the anonymous *rabanan* — perhaps, as one might suggest, those who were themselves not so outstanding. But more poignantly, Rashi suggests the motivation was to avoid *ayin hara* — that a child too exceptional in every respect would draw the evil eye. There is also the simpler reading: Hannah was saying she was not asking for anything extraordinary. She did not need a prodigy or a great man. She simply wanted a child, any child. That reading carries its own quiet power.

## Samuel & Paskening

### Four Cubits Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲנִי הָאִשָּׁה הַנִּצֶּבֶת עִמְּכָה בָּזֶה״, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מִכָּאן שֶׁאָסוּר לֵישֵׁב בְּתוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁל תְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** When Hannah came to the Temple with her son Samuel, she told Eli: "My lord, as your soul lives, my lord, **I am the woman who stood here with you** to pray to the Lord." **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: From here** the *halakha* **that it is forbidden to sit within four cubits of** one who is **praying** is derived. As the verse says: "Who stood here with you," indicating that Eli stood alongside Hannah because she was praying.

When Hannah returned the following year with the child, she said to Eli: *ani ha-isha ha-nitzevet imcha bazeh* — I am the woman who stood here with you at this place. From this, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derives: *mikan she-asur leshev be-toch arba amot shel tefillah* — it is forbidden to sit within four *amot* of a person who is praying. Since Hannah said she was *standing* with Eli, Eli must have been standing as well — which means one cannot sit within the four *amot* of a person in prayer.

Tosafot adds that the word *imcha* is written *malei*, spelled with the letter *heh*: *ayin, mem, chaf, heh*. The *heh* hints that one may sit in the fifth *amah*, but not within the first four. Tosafot also cites the *teshuvot* of the *Geonim* to the effect that the prohibition specifically applies when one is sitting *u-vatel* — sitting idly, doing nothing. If, however, one is engaged in *Kriat Shema* or other *tefillah*, that suffices. This is a meaningful distinction: the concern is not merely that one's sitting will distract the person praying, but that it is inappropriate to be idle in a shared space where prayer is occurring. If you are participating — even in a lesser form of *tefillah* — that resolves the impropriety. The practical implication is relevant to crowded *batei kenesiot*: someone sitting during the *chazzan*'s repetition (*Chazarat HaShatz*) and listening attentively would not violate this prohibition.

### Samuel Paskens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶל הַנַּעַר הַזֶּה הִתְפַּלָּלְתִּי״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שְׁמוּאֵל מוֹרֵה הֲלָכָה לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ הָיָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁחֲטוּ אֶת הַפָּר וַיָּבִיאוּ אֶת הַנַּעַר אֶל עֵלִי״.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, Hannah's emphasis in speaking to Eli, **"for this youth I prayed"** (I Samuel 1:27), indicates that she came to protect him from danger. As **Rabbi Elazar said: Samuel was** one who **taught *halakha* in the presence of his teacher.** Hannah wanted to pray that he not be punished by death at the hand of Heaven for his transgression, **as it is stated: "And they slaughtered the cow and they brought the youth to Eli"** (I Samuel 1:25).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אָמַר לָהֶן עֵלִי: קִרְאוּ כֹּהֵן, לֵיתֵי וְלִשְׁחוֹט. חֲזַנְהוּ שְׁמוּאֵל דַּהֲווֹ מְהַדְּרִי בָּתַר כֹּהֵן לְמִישְׁחַט, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לְמָה לְכוּ לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי בָּתַר כֹּהֵן לְמִישְׁחַט? שְׁחִיטָה בְּזָר כְּשֵׁרָה! אַיְיתוּהוּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּעֵלִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי כְּתִיב ״וְשָׁחַט הַכֹּהֵן״?! ״וְהִקְרִיבוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים״ כְּתִיב, מִקַּבָּלָה וְאֵילָךְ מִצְוַת כְּהוּנָּה, מִכָּאן לַשְּׁחִיטָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁרָה בְּזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this is what happened: **Eli said to** those who brought the offering: **Call a priest; he will come and slaughter** the offering. **Samuel saw them looking for a priest to slaughter** the animal. **He said to them: Why do you** need **to look for a priest to slaughter** it? **Slaughter** of an offering performed **by a non-priest is valid. They brought him before Eli** to clarify his statement. Eli **said to him: How do you know this?** Samuel **said to him: Is it written** in the Torah: **And the priest shall slaughter** indicating that the offering may only be slaughtered by a priest? **It is written: "And the priests shall offer,"** only **from** the stage of **receiving** the blood in the bowls **and onward is it a mitzva** incumbent upon **priests** alone. **From here** the *halakha* that **slaughter by a non-priest is acceptable** is derived.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵימָר שַׁפִּיר קָא אָמְרַתְּ, מִיהוּ מוֹרֶה הֲלָכָה בִּפְנֵי רַבָּךְ אַתְּ, וְכׇל הַמּוֹרֶה הֲלָכָה בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ חַיָּיב מִיתָה. אָתְיָא חַנָּה וְקָא צָוְוחָה קַמֵּיהּ: ״אֲנִי הָאִשָּׁה הַנִּצֶּבֶת עִמְּכָה בָּזֶה וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לַהּ: שִׁבְקִי לִי דְּאֶעֶנְשֵׁיהּ, וּבְעֵינָא רַחֲמֵי, וְיָהֵיב לָךְ רַבָּא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״אֶל הַנַּעַר הַזֶּה הִתְפַּלָּלְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** Eli **said to** Samuel: **You have spoken well** and your statement is correct, but **nevertheless, you are** one who **issued a halakhic ruling in the presence of your teacher, and anyone who issues a halakhic ruling in the presence of his teacher,** even if the particular *halakha* is correct, **is liable for death** at the hand of Heaven for showing contempt for his teacher. **Hannah came and shouted before him: "I am the woman who stood here with you** to pray to the Lord;" do not punish the child who was born of my prayers. **He said to her: Let me punish him, and I will pray** for mercy, that the Holy One, Blessed be He, **will grant you** a son who will be **greater than** this one. **She said to him: "For this youth I prayed"** and I want no other.

The Gemara proceeds to an astonishing *aggadta*. Rabbi Elazar states: *Shmuel moreh halacha lifnei rabbo hayah* — Samuel had actually ruled on a halachic matter in the presence of his teacher Eli, which as we know is always a grave transgression. Indeed, one Gemara identifies this — ruling in the presence of one's master — as the sin of Nadav and Avihu, for which they were struck dead.

How is this known? The verse states: *vayishchatu et ha-par vayavi'u et ha-na'ar el Eli* — they slaughtered the ox and brought the boy to Eli. What is the connection between those two events? Eli must have been looking for a *kohen* to perform the *shechitah* of the ox brought as a sacrifice. Young Samuel — who was perhaps three or five years old at the time — observed them searching and said: *lamah lachem le-hadurei batar kohen le-mishchat? Shechitah be-zar kesherah* — why do you need to search for a *kohen* to slaughter? A non-*kohen* (*zar*) may perform *shechitah* for a *korban*.

When Samuel was brought before Eli, Eli asked him: *mina lach ha* — from where do you know this? Samuel replied: *mi ketiv ve-shachat ha-kohen?* — Does the verse in *Vayikra* say "and the *kohen* shall slaughter"? It does not. It says *ve-hikrivu ha-kohanim* — the *kohanim* shall bring, referring to the reception of the blood and what follows from that point. The obligation of *kehunah* begins with *kabbalah*, the receiving of the blood. From the point of *shechitah* and before, the act is valid even when performed by a non-*kohen*.

Eli conceded: *meimar shapir kamart* — you have spoken correctly. But he added: *mihu moreh halacha bifnei ravcha* — nevertheless, you have ruled on a matter of *halacha* in the presence of your teacher. *Ve-chol ha-moreh halacha bifnei rabbo chayav mita* — anyone who rules in such a fashion deserves death.

Tosafot raises a natural question: how was Eli already Samuel's *Rebbe* at this moment, since this was the first time Samuel was being brought to him? Tosafot answers that Eli was the *gadol ha-dor*, and since Samuel was coming to study under him, Eli already had the status of his *Rebbe* — and that status carried its obligations from the outset, regardless of how young the student happened to be.

### Not Replaceable

Hannah came before Eli weeping, and Eli said to her: *shivki li d'aktlei* — let me invoke divine punishment upon him, and *u-veina rachamei* — I will pray, and *v-yahiv lach raba minei* — God will give you a greater son in his place.

Hannah replied: *el ha-na'ar ha-zeh hitpalalti* — I prayed for *this* child. Do not speak to me of a replacement. The emphasis on *hazeh* — this one — carries a *derasha*: she was not asking for any child in general, but for this particular child. There is a resonance here with the earlier interpretation of *zera anashim* as meaning simply a child — not a superstar, just a child. And now she says: not even a better child. This one. The one she prayed for. Somehow Samuel survived the episode, though the text does not spell out exactly how he escaped punishment.

This entire narrative foreshadows the story immediately following in the *pesukim*, in which the young Samuel hears God's voice in the sanctuary while Eli does not — a story in which Samuel begins to eclipse his master. It is not difficult to sense that dimension in the background here as well.

## Hannah's Bold Prayer……

### Body's Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְחַנָּה הִיא מְדַבֶּרֶת עַל לִבָּהּ״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן זִמְרָא: עַל עִסְקֵי לִבָּהּ. אָמְרָה לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבָּרָאתָ בָּאִשָּׁה, לֹא בָּרָאתָ דָּבָר אֶחָד לְבַטָּלָה: עֵינַיִם לִרְאוֹת, וְאׇזְנַיִם לִשְׁמוֹעַ, חוֹטֶם לְהָרִיחַ, פֶּה לְדַבֵּר, יָדַיִם לַעֲשׂוֹת בָּהֶם מְלָאכָה, רַגְלַיִם לְהַלֵּךְ בָּהֶן, דַּדִּים לְהָנִיק בָּהֶן. דַּדִּים הַלָּלוּ שֶׁנָּתַתָּ עַל לִבִּי לָמָּה? לֹא לְהָנִיק בָּהֶן?! תֵּן לִי בֵּן, וְאָנִיק בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to deal with Hannah's prayer. It is said: **"And Hannah spoke on her heart."** Several interpretations are offered to explain her use of the phrase "on her heart" instead of the common phrase to her heart. **Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Zimra:** Hannah spoke to God **concerning matters of her heart. She said before Him: Master of the Universe,** of **all** the organs **You created in a woman, You have not created one in vain.** Every organ fulfills its purpose; **eyes to see, ears to hear, a nose to smell, a mouth to speak, hands with which to perform labor, feet with which to walk, breasts with which to nurse.** If so, **these breasts that You placed upon my heart, to what** purpose did You place them? Was it **not** in order **to nurse with them? Grant me a son and I will nurse with them.**

Returning to the verse *va-Channah hi medaberet al libah*, Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Zimra explains: *al iskei libah* — regarding matters of her heart. And what were those matters? Hannah said to God: *Ribbono shel Olam, kol mah she-barata be-isha lo barata davar echad le-vatalah* — Master of the Universe, everything You created in a woman, You did not create in vain. *Einayim lirot* — eyes to see; *oznayim lishmoa* — ears to hear; *chotem le-hariach* — a nose to smell; *peh le-daber* — a mouth to speak; *yadayim la'asot bahem melachah* — hands to perform labor; *raglayim le-halech bahem* — feet to walk. And *dadim le-hanik bahem* — breasts with which to nurse a child. *Dadim elalu she-natata al libi* — these breasts You have placed on my heart, as the verse says *al libah*. *Lamah?* — Why? *Lo le-hanik bahem?* — Is it not to nurse a child? *Ten li bein ve-anik bahem* — give me a son so that I may nurse from them.

The argument is at once halachic in its structure and deeply human in its content. Hannah is pointing to her own body as evidence of God's unfulfilled purpose. You created me in a way that assumes motherhood; every aspect of a woman's body — including this — was made for a reason. If you gave me the equipment, give me the capacity to use it. It is, as noted, a profoundly poignant image: not merely the pain of barrenness in the abstract, but the specific ache of a body created for something it cannot do.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן זִמְרָא: כׇּל הַיּוֹשֵׁב בְּתַעֲנִית בְּשַׁבָּת קוֹרְעִים לוֹ גְּזַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה. וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן חוֹזְרִין וְנִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ דִּין עוֹנֶג שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** Tangentially, the Gemara also cites an additional statement that **Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Zimra: Anyone who sits** in observance of a **fast on Shabbat,** his merit is great and **they tear up** and repeal **his sentence of seventy years;** because everyone is enjoying himself and a feast is prepared, it is more difficult to fast on Shabbat than on any other day. **Nevertheless, they then hold him accountable** for failing to fulfill **the *halakha* of delight of Shabbat**.

A digression follows, connected by the name Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Zimra: anyone who observes a fast on Shabbat — which is understood to mean a *ta'anit chalom*, a fast for a disturbing dream, which must be observed immediately even if it falls on Shabbat — *kora'in lo gzar dino shel shiv'im shanah* — even a decree of seventy years' standing will be torn up. Rashi explains that this decree is one that may have been issued from one's youth. The difficulty of fasting on Shabbat, when everyone else is enjoying food and festivity, gives the act its particular power. Nevertheless, *ve-af al pi chein kofin oto le-hista'anet od pa'am achat be-Shabbat* — one must still accept a second fast as a form of consequence for having missed *oneg Shabbat*. The remedy, according to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, is *leisiv tanisa le-tanisa* — to fast for the fast, which somehow restores what was lost.

## God's Responsibility

### Aggressive Prayer Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: חַנָּה הֵטִיחָה דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְתִתְפַּלֵּל עַל ה׳״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֵטִיחָה דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** After explaining the uncommon expression, on her heart, the Gemara cites an additional statement in the matter of Hannah. **And Rabbi Elazar said: Hannah spoke impertinently toward** God **on High. As it is stated: "And she prayed onto the Lord,"** as opposed to the common phrase: To the Lord. This **teaches that she spoke impertinently toward on High.**

The Gemara now makes explicit what has been implicit throughout this extended passage: *ve-amar Rabbi Elazar: Channah hiticah devarim klapei ma'alah* — Hannah threw her words toward Heaven. The verb *hiticah* conveys something like plastering or slapping — words hurled upward, almost aggressively. The evidence is the verse *va-titpallel al Hashem* — she prayed *al* God, literally upon God, rather than *el* God, toward God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵלִיָּהוּ הֵטִיחַ דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה הֲסִבֹּתָ אֶת לִבָּם אֲחֹרַנִּית״. אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן שֶׁחָזַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְהוֹדָה לוֹ לְאֵלִיָּהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר הֲרֵעֹתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** on a similar note, **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Elijah spoke impertinently toward** God **on High** as well in his prayer at Mount Carmel, **as it is stated:** "Answer me, Lord, answer me, that this people will know that You are the Lord, God, **and You have turned their hearts backward"** (I Kings 18:37), claiming that God caused Israel to sin. On this topic, **Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Yitzḥak said: From where do we know that the Holy One, Blessed be He, ultimately conceded to Elijah** that he was correct? **As it is written** in a future prophecy: "In that day, says the Lord, I will assemble the lame, and I will gather those who are abandoned and **those with whom I have dealt in wickedness"** (Micah 4:6). God states that He caused Israel to act wickedly.

Rabbi Elazar adds: Elijah did the same — *ve-ata hasivot et libam achoranis* — and You have turned their hearts backward. Elijah blamed God directly. Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak then asks: how do we know that God went back and conceded to Elijah that He bore some responsibility? The proof is from the verse *ve-asher hare'oti* — and that which I have caused them to do evil. God acknowledges having created circumstances in which human beings are drawn toward sin.

### Three Saving Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אִלְמָלֵא שָׁלֹשׁ מִקְרָאוֹת הַלָּלוּ נִתְמוֹטְטוּ רַגְלֵיהֶם שֶׁל שׂוֹנְאֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. חַד דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר הֲרֵעֹתִי״, וְחַד דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנֵּה כַחֹמֶר בְּיַד הַיּוֹצֵר כֵּן אַתֶּם בְּיָדִי בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְחַד דִּכְתִיב: ״וַהֲסִרֹתִי אֶת לֵב הָאֶבֶן מִבְּשַׂרְכֶם וְנָתַתִּי לָכֶם לֵב בָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: Had it not been for these three verses, the legs of the enemies of Israel,** a euphemism for Israel itself, **would have collapsed, as** Israel would have been unable to withstand God's judgment. **One** is the verse just mentioned in which **it is written: "Those whom I have dealt in wickedness." And one** is the verse in which **it is written: "Behold, like clay in the potter's hand, so are you in My hand, house of Israel"** (Jeremiah 18:6). **And one** is the verse in which **it is written:** "And I will give you a new heart and a new spirit I will place within you, **and I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and I will give you a heart of flesh"** (Ezekiel 36:26).

Rabbi Hama be-Rabbi Hanina states: *ilmale shalosh mikra'ot halalu, nismotetu ragleihem shel sonei Yisrael* — were it not for these three verses, the legs of Israel (a euphemism for Israel itself) would have collapsed. The three verses are:

First, *ve-asher hare'oti* — I have caused them to do evil. God admits to having created in human beings an inclination toward evil — the *yetzer hara*.

Second, *hinei ke-chomer be-yad ha-yotzer, kein atem be-yadi Beit Yisrael* — behold, like clay in the potter's hand, so are you in My hand, O house of Israel. The word *yotzer* (potter) resonates with *yeitzer* (inclination); God has formed us, and by forming us He has created our inclination as well.

Third, *va-hasiroti et lev ha-even mi-vsarchem ve-natati lachem lev basar* — I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh. The implication is that right now, God has not yet done this — and if He has not, it is He who has left us with the heart of stone, the incapacity to fully resist temptation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְאֶת רוּחִי אֶתֵּן בְּקִרְבְּכֶם וְעָשִׂיתִי אֵת אֲשֶׁר בְּחֻקַּי תֵּלֵכוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** there is a clearer proof **from here: "And I will place My spirit within you and I will cause you to walk in My statutes,** and you will observe My decrees and do them"** (Ezekiel 36:27).

Rav Papa adds a fourth verse: *ve-et ruchi etein be-kirbekhem ve-asiti et asher be-chukotai teleikhu* — I will place My spirit within you and cause you to walk in My statutes. Again, that spirit is something God can give and has not yet given; the absence is, in some sense, His.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מֹשֶׁה הֵטִיחַ דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּתְפַּלֵּל מֹשֶׁה אֶל ה׳״ אַל תִּקְרֵי ״אֶל ה׳״, אֶלָּא ״עַל ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: Moses** also **spoke impertinently toward** God **on High, as it is stated** in the verse following the sin of those who murmured against God in the desert: **"And Moses prayed to the Lord** and the fire subsided." Do not read to [*el*] the Lord,** but rather **onto [*al*] the Lord,** which indicates that he spoke impertinently.

The Gemara then returns to Moses. Rabbi Elazar states: *Moshe hitiach devarim klapei ma'alah* — Moses too threw his words toward Heaven. The verse says *vayitpallel Moshe el Hashem* — but the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov reads *aleph* and *ayin* interchangeably, converting *el* to *al*, so that Moshe prayed *upon* God, against God.

### Prosperity & Rebellion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכֵּן דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב קוֹרִין לָאַלְפִין עַיְינִין, וְלָעַיְינִין אַלְפִין. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמְרִי, מֵהָכָא: ״וְדִי זָהָב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the basis for this interpretation: **As** the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov would** indiscriminately **read *alef* as *ayin* and *ayin* as *alef*** and in this case transforming *el* into *al*. The Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai,** however, **say** proof that Moses spoke impertinently toward God on High is derived **from here,** Moses' rebuke at the beginning of Deuteronomy: **"And Di Zahav"** (Deuteronomy 1:1).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְדִי זָהָב״? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כָּךְ אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, בִּשְׁבִיל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁהִשְׁפַּעְתָּ לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, עַד שֶׁאָמְרוּ ״דַּי״ — הוּא גָּרַם שֶׁעָשׂוּ אֶת הָעֵגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** We must clarify: **What is** the meaning of **and Di Zahav?** The Sages of **the school of Rabbi Yannai said that Moses said the following before the Holy One, Blessed be He,** to atone for Israel after the sin of the Golden Calf: **Master of the Universe, because of the gold and silver that you lavished upon Israel** during the exodus from Egypt **until they said enough [*dai*];** it was this wealth **that caused** Israel **to make the** Golden **Calf.**

The school of Rabbi Yannai then derives Moses's implicit argument from the place name *Vedi Zahav* at the opening of Devarim. The Gemara reads *Vedi Zahav* as *dai zahav* — too much gold. Moses said to God: *bishvil ha-kesef ve-ha-zahav she-hishpata lahem le-Yisrael, ad she-amru dai* — because of the silver and gold You poured upon Israel, until they said "enough" — *hu garam she-asu et ha-eigel* — that is what caused them to make the calf. The argument is not merely that human beings have a weakness built into their nature, but that the specific circumstances God created — the extraordinary wealth — made sin nearly inevitable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֵין אֲרִי נוֹהֵם מִתּוֹךְ קוּפָּה שֶׁל תֶּבֶן אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ קוּפָּה שֶׁל בָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Establishing a general moral principle, the Sages **the school of Rabbi Yannai said: A lion does not roar** standing **over a basket of straw** from which he derives no pleasure, **but** he roars standing **over a basket of meat,** as he only roars when satiated.

The school of Rabbi Yannai offers a vivid *mashal*: *ein ari noheim mi-toch kupah shel teven, ela mi-toch kupah shel basar* — a lion does not roar over a trough of straw; it roars after devouring flesh. It is satisfaction and abundance that lead to boldness, and boldness leads to rebellion. When life is lean, one stays on the straight and narrow.

### Spoiled Son Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ פָּרָה כְּחוּשָׁה וּבַעֲלַת אֵבָרִים, הֶאֱכִילָהּ כַּרְשִׁינִין וְהָיְתָה מְבַעֶטֶת בּוֹ. אָמַר לָהּ: מִי גָּרַם לִיךְ שֶׁתְּהֵא מְבַעֶטֶת בִּי — אֶלָּא כַּרְשִׁינִין שֶׁהֶאֱכַלְתִּיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Oshaya said:** This is **comparable to a person who had a lean, but large-limbed cow.** At one point, **he fed it lupines,** a choice food, **and** soon thereafter the cow **was kicking him. He said to** the cow: **Who caused you** to begin **kicking me if not the lupines I fed you?** Here, too, the sin was caused by an abundance of good.

Rabbi Oshaya offers a parallel image: a man had a weak, thin cow with its ribs showing. He fed it *karshinin* — a grain typically reserved for humans — and it began to kick and rebel against him. They said to the cow: what caused you to kick against me? Nothing but the *karshinin* I fed you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָשָׁל לְאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ בֵּן. הִרְחִיצוֹ וְסָכוֹ, וְהֶאֱכִילוֹ וְהִשְׁקָהוּ, וְתָלָה לוֹ כִּיס עַל צַוָּארוֹ, וְהוֹשִׁיבוֹ עַל פֶּתַח שֶׁל זוֹנוֹת. מַה יַּעֲשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ הַבֵּן שֶׁלֹּא יֶחֱטָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another analogy: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **comparable to a person who had a son; he bathed him and anointed him** with oil, **fed him and gave him drink, and hung a purse** of money **around his neck.** Then, he **brought** his son **to the entrance of a brothel. What could the son do to avoid sinning?**

Rabbi Yochanan, in the school of Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba, offers what is perhaps the most powerful formulation. A man had a son. He bathed him, anointed him with oil, fed him, gave him drink, hung a purse of money around his neck, and then placed him at the entrance to a brothel. *Mai ya'aseh oto bein she-lo yecheta?* — What do you expect the son to do and not sin? You have given him every inducement. You have made him feel entitled, comfortable, and without restraint. This is what God did when He gave Israel all that gold. What were they supposed to do?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: מְלֵי כְּרֵסֵיהּ זְנֵי בִּישֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Huna, said** that **Rav Sheshet said: That is what people say** in a popular maxim: **Filling his stomach is a type of sin.**

Rav Acha son of Rav Huna in the name of Rav Sheshet cites the folk saying: *malei kerisei, zanei bisha* — a full belly breeds bad kinds of sin. The *pesukim* multiply: *ke-mar'itam vayisba'u, sav'u vayaram libam, al ken shechechuni* — they grazed and were sated, their heart grew haughty, and therefore they forgot Me. *Ve-ram levavcha ve-shachachta et Hashem Elokecha* — your heart will grow haughty and you will forget God. *Ve-achal ve-sava ve-dashen u-fanah* — he will eat and be satisfied and be fat and turn away. *Vayishman Yeshurun vayivat* — Israel grew fat and kicked.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כְּמַרְעִיתָם וַיִּשְׂבָּעוּ שָׂבְעוּ וַיָּרׇם לִבָּם עַל כֵּן שְׁכֵחוּנִי״. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״וְרָם לְבָבֶךָ וְשָׁכַחְתָּ אֶת ה׳״. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי, מֵהָכָא: ״וְאָכַל וְשָׂבַע וְדָשֵׁן וּפָנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** as it is stated: "When they were fed and became full they were sated, and their hearts were lifted and they have forgotten Me" (Hosea 13:6). **Rav Naḥman said:** This principle is derived not from the verse in Hosea, but **from here: "And your heart is lifted and you forget the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 8:14). **And the Rabbis say** that this principle is derived **from here: "And they will have eaten and been sated and fattened, and they will turn** to other gods"** (Deuteronomy 31:20).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״וַיִּשְׁמַן יְשׁוּרוּן וַיִּבְעָט״. אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁחָזַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְהוֹדָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכֶסֶף הִרְבֵּיתִי לָהֶם וְזָהָב עָשׂוּ לַבָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that it is derived **from here: "And Jeshurun grew fat and kicked"** (Deuteronomy 32:15). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: From where** in the Torah is it derived that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, ultimately conceded to Moses** that the reason for the sin of the Golden Calf was indeed the riches lavished upon Israel? **As it is stated: "And I gave them an abundance of silver and gold, which they used for the Ba'al"** (Hosea 2:10).

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan then asks: and how do we know that God went back and conceded to Moses — that He acknowledged His own role in Israel's sin? The verse says: *ve-chesef hirbeiti lahem ve-zahav asu la-Baal* — I gave them much silver and gold, and they used the gold to make the Baal. The verse itself is God speaking, and the emphasis on *I gave* carries the concession: the very wealth I bestowed led them to this sin.

The model of prayer being built throughout this passage is striking in how far it departs from simple supplication. From Hannah to Elijah to Moses, the *aggadata* of this *amud* and a half presents prayer as a form of *ta'anah* — a reasoned argument, even an accusation. It calls to mind the Chassidic tradition of Rabbi Levi Yitzchak of Berditchev, who was known for defending Israel before God through precisely this mode of prayer. What is remarkable is that these models appear here at the opening of the *perek* on *kavana* — as if to say that this kind of engaged, demanding, even confrontational prayer *is* the model for *tefillah*.

## Moses Defends Israel

### Go Down Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה לֶךְ רֵד״. מַאי ״לֶךְ רֵד״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: מֹשֶׁה, רֵד מִגְּדוּלָּתְךָ! כְּלוּם נָתַתִּי לְךָ גְּדוּלָּה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְעַכְשָׁיו יִשְׂרָאֵל חָטְאוּ — אַתָּה לָמָּה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates upon additional aspects of the sin of the Golden Calf. It is stated: **"And the Lord said to Moses: Go and descend,** for your people whom you have lifted out of the land of Egypt have been corrupted." **What is** the meaning of **"go and descend"? Rabbi Elazar said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: Moses, descend from your greatness. Isn't it only for the sake of Israel,** so that you may serve as an emissary, **that I granted you prominence; and now that Israel has sinned, why do I** need **you?**

The Gemara now turns directly to the episode of the Golden Calf and Moses's prayer on Israel's behalf. *Vayidaber Hashem el Moshe: lech red* — God spoke to Moses: go down quickly. Rabbi Elazar reads the divine instruction as a demotion: *red mi-gedulatecha* — descend from your greatness. *Klum natati lecha gedulah ela bishvil Yisrael?* — Did I give you this status for any reason other than for the sake of Israel? *Ve-achshav Yisrael chatu, ata lama li?* — Now that Israel has sinned and lost their standing, what do I need you for?

This is a remarkable reversal of the surface reading of the *pesukim*, which seem to position Moses as God's beloved and Israel as expendable. The *midrash* completely inverts this: God's primary concern is Israel; Moses's greatness was always *bishvil Yisrael*, for their sake. When they fall, his status falls with them. God here is not rewarding Moses by offering to replace Israel with him — He is stripping Moses of the basis for his greatness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד תָּשַׁשׁ כּוֹחוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, וְלֹא הָיָה לוֹ כֹּחַ לְדַבֵּר. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״הֶרֶף מִמֶּנִּי וְאַשְׁמִידֵם״, אָמַר מֹשֶׁה: דָּבָר זֶה תָּלוּי בִּי! מִיָּד עָמַד וְנִתְחַזֵּק בִּתְפִלָּה, וּבִקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים.
>
> **Translation:** There is no need for an emissary. **Immediately, Moses' strength waned and he was powerless to speak** in defense of Israel. **And once** God **said** to Moses: **"Leave Me be, that I may destroy them,"** **Moses said** to himself: If God is telling me to let Him be, it must be because **this matter is dependent upon me. Immediately Moses stood and was strengthened in prayer, and asked** that God have **mercy** on the nation of Israel and forgive them for their transgression.

*Miyad tashash kocho shel Moshe* — immediately Moses's strength was sapped. *Ve-lo haya lo koach le-daber* — he had no strength to respond. Having been told he no longer had standing, he felt he had nothing to say. He was not just weakened emotionally; he was rendered halachically mute, as it were — without standing to intercede.

But then God said: *heref mimeni ve-ashmidem* — let go of Me and I will destroy them. The moment Moses heard *heref mimeni* — let go of Me — something shifted. *Amar Moshe: davar zeh talui bi* — Moses realized: this matter depends on me. If God needs me to release Him, then I have the power not to do so. *Miyad amad ve-nitchazek bi-tefillah* — immediately he stood and strengthened himself in prayer.

### King & Counselor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ שֶׁכָּעַס עַל בְּנוֹ, וְהָיָה מַכֵּהוּ מַכָּה גְּדוֹלָה. וְהָיָה אוֹהֲבוֹ יוֹשֵׁב לְפָנָיו, וּמִתְיָרֵא לוֹמַר לוֹ דָּבָר. אָמַר הַמֶּלֶךְ: אִלְמָלֵא אוֹהֲבִי זֶה שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב לְפָנַי — הֲרַגְתִּיךָ. אָמַר: דָּבָר זֶה תָּלוּי בִּי. מִיָּד עָמַד וְהִצִּילוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: This is **comparable to a king who became angry at his son** who had sinned against him, **and beat him,** administering **a severe beating.** At that moment, **a well-wisher** of the king **was sitting before him** and witnessed the entire event, **and was afraid to say anything to** the king about the excessive beating. Meanwhile, **the king said** to his son: **Were it not for this well-wisher of mine who is sitting before me, I would have killed you.** Upon hearing this, the king's friend **said** to himself: This is clearly a sign that **this matter,** rescuing the son from the hands of his father, **is dependent upon me. Immediately he stood and rescued him** from the king.

The Gemara illustrates this dynamic with a *mashal*. A king was angry at his son and was striking him severely. The king's closest companion was sitting right there before the king, pained and wishing to intervene, but afraid to say anything — who was he to interfere with the king's discipline of his own son? Then the king said aloud: if not for my friend who is sitting before me, I would have killed you. The companion understood immediately: the king was sending him a message. The king cared what the companion thought. The companion rose and intervened, and he saved the son.

So too here. *Heref mimeni* — let go of Me — was God's message to Moses that Moses could and should intervene. The instruction was not a command to step back but a hidden invitation to step forward.

### Grab Divine Collar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹמְרוֹ. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁתְּפָסוֹ מֹשֶׁה לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, כְּאָדָם שֶׁהוּא תּוֹפֵס אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בְּבִגְדוֹ, וְאָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֵין אֲנִי מַנִּיחֲךָ עַד שֶׁתִּמְחוֹל וְתִסְלַח לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** In an additional aspect of the sin of the Golden Calf, God told Moses: **"Now leave Me be, that My wrath will be enraged against them and I will consume them; and I will make of you a great nation"** (Exodus 32:10). Explaining this verse, **Rabbi Abbahu said: Were the verse not written** in this manner, **it would be impossible to utter** it, in deference to God. The phrase: Leave Me be, **teaches that Moses grabbed the Holy One, Blessed be He, as a person who grabs his friend by his garment** would, **and he said before Him: Master of the Universe, I will not let go of You until You forgive and pardon them.**

Rabbi Abbahu states: *ilmale mikra katuv i efshar le-omro* — were it not written in a verse, one could not say such a thing. *Melamed she-tafas Moshe le-Hakadosh Baruch Hu ke-adam she-tofes et chaveiro be-vigdo* — it teaches that Moses seized God, as a person might seize his companion by the cloak. *Ve-amar lefanav: Ribbono shel Olam, ein ani manichacha ad she-timchol ve-tislach lahem* — he said before Him: Master of the Universe, I will not let go of You until You forgive and pardon them.

This is one of the most extraordinary images in the Gemara. The power of prayer, in this formulation, is not petition but physical insistence — *grabbing God by the collar*. Prayer does not merely ask; it compels. The image draws its force entirely from the phrase *heref mimeni* — let go of Me — which Moses, in his prayer, refuses to do.

### Three-Legged Chair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶעֱשֶׂה אוֹתְךָ לְגוֹי גָּדוֹל וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, וּמָה כִּסֵּא שֶׁל שָׁלֹשׁ רַגְלַיִם אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד לְפָנֶיךָ בִּשְׁעַת כַּעְסֶךְ. כִּסֵּא שֶׁל רֶגֶל אֶחָד — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה! וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ בִּי בּוֹשֶׁת פָּנִים מֵאֲבוֹתַי, עַכְשָׁו יֹאמְרוּ: רְאוּ פַּרְנָס שֶׁהֶעֱמִיד עֲלֵיהֶם. בִּקֵּשׁ גְּדוּלָּה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְלֹא בִּקֵּשׁ עֲלֵיהֶם רַחֲמִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the same verse, God promised Moses: **"And I will make of you a great nation."** What was Moses' response? **Rabbi Elazar said: Moses said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, if a chair with three legs,** the collective merit of the three forefathers, **is unable to stand before You in Your moment of wrath, all the more so** that **a chair with one leg,** my merit alone, will be unable to withstand your wrath. **Moreover, but I have** a sense of **shame before my forefathers. Now** they will **say: See** this **leader that** God **placed over** Israel. **He requested greatness for himself but did not pray for** God to have **mercy upon them** in their troubled time.

When God said *ve-e'eseh otcha le-goy gadol va-atzum* — I will make you into a great and mighty nation — Rabbi Elazar records Moses's response. *Ribbono shel Olam, u-mah kisei shel shalosh raglayim eino yachol la'amod lefanecha bi-sha'at ka'asecha* — Master of the Universe, if a chair with three legs — Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — cannot stand before You in the moment of Your anger, then *kisei shel regel achat, al achat kama ve-chama* — a chair of one leg, built on my merit alone, how much more so will it be unable to stand?

And beyond that: *she-yesh bi boshet panim me-avotai* — I will be shamed before my forefathers. Because they will say: look at this leader God appointed over them — *bikeish gedulah le-atzmo ve-lo bikeish aleihem rachamim* — he grabbed at greatness for himself and did not seek compassion for the people. The point underscores the same theme as before: Moses's greatness is entirely bound up in his role as the defender of Israel. To accept an offer that abandons the people is to abandon the very thing that gave Moses his standing.

## Legal Argument Prayer

### Prayer Makes Ill

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְחַל מֹשֶׁה אֶת פְּנֵי ה׳״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָמַד מֹשֶׁה בִּתְפִלָּה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, עַד שֶׁהֶחֱלָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah continues: **"And Moses beseeched [*vayḥal*] before the Lord"** (Exodus 32:11). Many interpretations were given for this uncommon term, *vayḥal*: **Rabbi Elazar said:** It **teaches that Moses stood in prayer before the Holy One, Blessed be He, until it made him ill [***heḥelahu***]** from overexertion.

*Va-yechal Moshe et penei Hashem Elokav* — and Moses importuned God. Rabbi Elazar explains that Moses stood in prayer *ad she-hechelohu* — until he had importuned Him. Rashi reads *hechelohu* as *hiftsir bo*, meaning he overwhelmed God with his prayers. Steinsaltz, however, renders it as "made Him ill" — understanding the root as *choleh*, sickness. This is a striking image in its own right, and one that may be the more vivid reading: Moses prayed until he had, as it were, worn God out.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: עַד שֶׁהֵפֵר לוֹ נִדְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said:** Moses stood in prayer **until he nullified His vow,** as the term *vayḥal* alludes to nullification of an oath.

Shmuel offers a different reading of *vayechal*: *melamed she-masar atzmo le-misah aleihem* — it teaches that Moses surrendered himself to death for them, reading the root as *chalal*, a corpse, in connection with his later plea *mecheini na mi-sifracha* — erase me from Your book.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיְחַל״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״לֹא יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ״, וְאָמַר מָר: הוּא אֵינוֹ מֵיחֵל, אֲבָל אֲחֵרִים מְחִלִּין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here it is written *vayḥal*, and there** referring to vows, **it is written: "He shall not nullify [*lo yaḥel*] his word"** (Numbers 30:3). **And** with regard to vows, **the Master said: He** who vowed **cannot nullify** his vow, **but others,** the court, **can nullify** his vow **for him.** Here, it is as if Moses nullified the Lord's vow to destroy Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמָּסַר עַצְמוֹ לְמִיתָה עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** The term *vayḥal* **teaches that** Moses **gave his life,** from the term *ḥalal*, a dead person, **for** Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבְרָם בִּשְׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֻחְלָה עֲלֵיהֶם מִדַּת רַחֲמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava,** also interpreting this verse, **said** that **Rav Yitzḥak said:** The term *vayḥal* **teaches that he caused the Divine Attribute of Mercy to take effect [*heḥela*] upon them.**

Rabbi Avram in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak reads *vayechal* as *she-hechilah aleihem midat ha-rachamim* — he caused the attribute of divine compassion to descend upon them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! חוּלִּין הוּא לְּךָ מֵעֲשׂוֹת כַּדָּבָר הַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say** that this term constitutes the essence of Moses' claim: **It teaches that Moses said before the Holy One Blessed be He: Master of the Universe! It is a sacrilege [*ḥullin*] for You to do something like this.**

The anonymous *rabanan* offer yet another interpretation: *chulin hu lecha me'asot ka-davar ha-zeh* — it would be a desecration for You to do such a thing. This reading connects *vayechal* to *chulin* and *chalilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָמַד מֹשֶׁה בִּתְפִלָּה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַד שֶׁאֲחָזַתּוּ אֲחִילוּ. מַאי אֲחִילוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵשׁ שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** And another interpretation of the verse, **"And Moses beseeched [*vayḥal*] before the Lord." It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says: This** term **teaches that Moses stood in prayer before the Holy One, Blessed be He, until he was overcome by *aḥilu*.** Even the Sages were unfamiliar with this term. Therefore, the Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of ***aḥilu*? Rabbi Elazar,** an *amora* of Eretz Yisrael, **said** that *aḥilu* is **fire in the bones.**

A *beraita* cites Rabbi Elazar the Great: Moses stood in prayer before God *ad she-achazo achilu* — until he was seized with *achilu*. What is *achilu*? Rabbi Elazar explains: *esha de-atzmot* — a burning of the bones, a deep fever. Abaye translates into Aramaic: *eshta de-garmei*. This is not merely a metaphor for exhaustion; the prayer was so intense that it affected Moses physically.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֵשׁ שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹת? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִשָּׁתָא דְגַרְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** However, this expression was familiar in Eretz Yisrael but not in Babylonia. They asked in Babylonia: **What is** the disease that they called **fire of the bones? Abaye said** that is a disease known in Babylonia as ***eshta degarmei*,** which in Aramaic means **fire of the bones;** in other words, a fever.

### Release from Vow

Rava offers his own reading of *vayechal*. He connects it to the verse *lo yachel devaro* — a person may not desecrate his word, i.e., may not violate a vow. *Aval acherim mechilin lo* — but others can annul the vow for him. Rava therefore reads *vayechal* as: Moses convened a *Beis Din* and released God from His vow. What was God's vow? When God declares that He will destroy Israel, that constitutes something analogous to a vow; by the mechanism of *hatarat nedarim*, Moses was able to annul it.

### Patriarchs' Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זְכֹר לְאַבְרָהָם לְיִצְחָק וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל עֲבָדֶיךָ אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּעְתָּ לָהֶם בָּךְ״. מַאי ״בָּךְ״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אִלְמָלֵא נִשְׁבַּעְתָּ לָהֶם בַּשָּׁמַיִם וּבָאָרֶץ, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁשָּׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ בְּטֵלִים — כָּךְ שְׁבוּעָתְךָ בְּטֵלָה. וְעַכְשָׁו שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּעְתָּ לָהֶם בְּשִׁמְךָ הַגָּדוֹל, מָה שִׁמְךָ הַגָּדוֹל חַי וְקַיָּים לְעוֹלָם וּלְעוֹלְמֵי עוֹלָמִים — כָּךְ שְׁבוּעָתְךָ קַיֶּימֶת לְעוֹלָם וּלְעוֹלְמֵי עוֹלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** As Moses continues his prayer, he says: **"Remember Abraham, Isaac and Israel Your servants, to whom You swore in Your name"** (Exodus 32:13). **What is** the meaning of **in Your name? Rabbi Elazar said: Moses said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, had You sworn to them by the heavens and the earth, I would say: Just as the heavens and the earth will** ultimately **be no more, so too Your oath will be null and void. Now that You swore to them by Your great name, just as Your name lives and stands for all eternity, so too does Your oath live and stand for all eternity.**

Moses then invoked the oath to the patriarchs: *zechor le-Avraham le-Yitzchak u-le-Yisrael avadecha, asher nishbata lahem bach* — remember Abraham, Isaac, and Israel Your servants, to whom You swore by Yourself.

Rabbi Elazar explains the significance of *bach* — by You, by Your own name. Moses argued: *Ribbono shel Olam, ilmale nishbata lahem be-shamayim u-va-aretz* — had You sworn to them by heaven and earth, I might have said: just as heaven and earth will eventually be destroyed, so too Your oath can be nullified. *Ve-achshav she-nishbata lahem be-shimcha ha-gadol* — but now that You have sworn in Your own great name, *mah shimcha ha-gadol chai ve-kayam le-olam u-le-olmei olamim* — just as Your great name lives and endures forever, *kach shevuatcha kayama le-olam u-le-olmei olamim* — so too Your oath stands forever and cannot be annulled.

The argument is airtight in halachic terms: an oath sworn in God's own eternal name cannot be undone by any temporal contingency. Moses trapped God, as it were, within His own metaphysical permanence.

### Nations' Slander

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתְּדַבֵּר אֲלֵיהֶם אַרְבֶּה אֶת זַרְעֲכֶם כְּכוֹכְבֵי הַשָּׁמָיִם וְכׇל הָאָרֶץ הַזֹּאת אֲשֶׁר אָמַרְתִּי״, הַאי ״אֲשֶׁר אָמַרְתִּי״ — ״אֲשֶׁר אָמַרְתָּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** In this verse, Moses continues: **"And You said to them: I will make your offspring as numerous as the stars of heaven, and all this land of which I have spoken** I will give to your offspring that they shall inherit it forever." The Gemara clarifies a puzzling phrase in this verse. **That** phrase **of which I have spoken, it should** have said: **Of which You have spoken,** as Moses is referring to God's promise to the forefathers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עַד כָּאן דִּבְרֵי תַלְמִיד, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — דִּבְרֵי הָרַב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: To this** point, the verse cites **the words of the student,** Moses; **from this point,** and all this land of which I have spoken, the verse cites **the words of the Master,** God.

Moses then made a pragmatic argument from the verse *vayomru mibilti yacholet Hashem* — they will say God was not able. The word is *yacholet*, in the feminine form. Rabbi Elazar explains that Moses said: *Ribbono shel Olam, achshav yomru umot ha-olam: tashash kocho ke-nekevah* — the nations will say God's power has weakened like a woman's, and *eino yachol le-hatzilam* — He cannot save them.

God responded: *ve-lo kbar ra'u nissim u-gevurot she-asiti lahem al ha-yam?* — Have the nations not already seen the miracles I performed at the sea? Moses countered: *adayin yesh lahem lomar, melech echad yachol la'amod, u-shloshim ve-echad melachim eino yachol la'amod* — they can still say: He could stand up against one king, Pharaoh, but He could not stand up against the thirty-one kings of Canaan. The destruction of Israel before the conquest would look like a failure of the very mission God had begun.

### Divine Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁחָזַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְהוֹדָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ סָלַחְתִּי כִּדְבָרֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From where** is it derived that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, ultimately conceded to Moses? As it is said: "And the Lord said: I have forgiven according to your word"** (Numbers 14:20).

Rabbi Yochanan asks: how do we know God went back and conceded to Moses? *She-ne'emar: vayomer Hashem salachti ki-devarecha* — it says: God said, I have forgiven them *according to your words*. The phrase *ki-devarecha* — according to your words — is the concession: Moses's arguments carried the day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֲאֵל: כִּדְבָרֶיךָ, עֲתִידִים אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם לוֹמַר כֵּן. אַשְׁרֵי תַלְמִיד שֶׁרַבּוֹ מוֹדֶה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: According to your word,** it will be, as indeed **in the future the nations of the world will say this.** The Gemara concludes: **Happy is the student whose teacher concedes to him** as the Lord conceded to Moses.

The *Tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael adds: *ki-devarecha atidim umot ha-olam lomar ken* — the nations would indeed have said exactly what Moses predicted had God destroyed Israel.

*Ashrei talmid she-rabbo modeh lo* — praised is the student whose master concedes to him and admits he was right.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאוּלָם חַי אָנִי״, אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: מֹשֶׁה, הֶחֱיִיתַנִי בִּדְבָרֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Explaining the next verse, **"Nevertheless, as I live,** and the glory of the Lord fills the entire world" (Numbers 14:21), **Rava said** that **Rav Yitzḥak said:** This **teaches** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: Moses, you have given Me life with your words.** I am happy that on account of your arguments, I will forgive Israel.

The *daf* closes on a remarkable note. God says: *va-ulam chai ani* — yet as I live. And Rava in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak explains: *she-amar lo Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-Moshe: hecheyitani bi-devarecha* — God said to Moses, you have given Me life with your words. *Chai ani* — I have lived. Rashi explains this as meaning that Moses's prayer prevented the nations from claiming that God lacked the power to save — and so Moses, in a sense, restored God's honor in the world. But there is also a deeper dimension: when we pray properly, when we give God the ability to do in the world what God wants to do, we as it were give life to God. The proper prayer of a human being enables divine action in this world.

## Praise Before Request

### Praise First Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: לְעוֹלָם יְסַדֵּר אָדָם שִׁבְחוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִתְפַּלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Based on Moses' prayers, **Rabbi Simlai taught: One should always set forth praise of the Holy One, Blessed be He, and then pray** for his own needs.

Rav Simai states: *le-olam yesader adam shivcho shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu ve-achar kach yitpallel* — one should always first arrange the praise of God and only afterward pray. The word *yitpallel* here means specifically *bakasha* — petition or request — while in earlier usages the word *tefillah* referred to praise. The distinction maps onto the structure of *Shemoneh Esreh*: the first three *brachot* are praise, and the middle thirteen are *bakashot*.

### Moses Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? — מִמֹּשֶׁה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאֶתְחַנַּן אֶל ה׳ בָּעֵת הַהִיא״. וּכְתִיב: ״ה׳ אֱלֹהִים אַתָּה הַחִלֹּתָ לְהַרְאוֹת אֶת עַבְדְּךָ אֶת גׇּדְלְךָ וְאֶת יָדְךָ הַחֲזָקָה״. וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ ״אֶעְבְּרָה נָּא וְאֶרְאֶה אֶת הָאָרֶץ הַטּוֹבָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that one should conduct himself in this manner? **From Moses, as it is written** in his prayer: **"And I beseeched the Lord at that time"** (Deuteronomy 3:23). **And** immediately afterward in his prayer, **it is written: "Lord, God, You have begun to show Your servant Your greatness and Your strong hand, for what God is there in the heavens or on earth who can perform deeds such as Yours and Your might"** (Deuteronomy 3:24)? Here, Moses began with praise of God, **and it is** only **thereafter** that **it is written: "Please, let me pass over and see the good land** that is beyond the Jordan, that good hill country and the Lebanon" (Deuteronomy 3:25). Only after his praise did Moses make his personal request.

The source, as always, is Moses. *Menalan mi-Moshe* — from where do we derive this? From the verse *va-etchanan el Hashem* — and I beseeched God. And how did Moses begin? *Hashem Elokim, atah hachilota leharot et avdecha et godlecha ve-et yadcha ha-chazakah, asher mi Kel ba-shamayim u-va-aretz asher ya'aseh ke-ma'asecha u-che-gevurotecha* — Lord God, You have begun to show Your servant Your greatness and Your mighty hand; who is there in the heavens or on earth who could do as Your deeds and as Your mighty acts? First, the praise. And only then: *e'evrah na ve-er'eh et ha-aretz ha-tovah* — please let me cross over and see this good land. First the *shevach*, then the *bakasha*. This structure will be developed further as the Gemara continues.
