# Yoma 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Fit Foods/Drinks

### Intro: Unfit Foods

The daf begins slightly before the midpoint of 81b, approximately ten lines above where the lines widen on the page. The mishna introduces the rule: *akhal okhlin she-aynam re'uyim* — if one ate foods that are not fit for eating, one is *patur*. This continues the broader discussion of the prohibitions of Yom Kippur, which had included, in the preceding material, the derivation of the negative prohibition against feasting on the fast day, and two important *drashot* from the verse *u-ve-tish'ah la-chodesh ba-erev me-erev ad erev*. One of those *drashot* established the principle of *mosifin me-chol al ha-kodesh* — that the fast begins before nightfall and ends somewhat after the close of the day — which is the foundation of *tosefet Yom Kippur*. According to the Rambam, *tosefet* is *d'Oraita* specifically for Yom Kippur, even if it does not carry the same punishment. The other *drasha* taught that one who eats and drinks on the ninth of Tishrei is credited by Scripture as though he fasted on both the ninth and the tenth — emphasizing that the purpose of the day is not self-affliction per se, but freedom from eating, which begins through proper preparation the day before.

With that background established, the mishna turns to foods and drinks that are not *ra'ui* — not fit for consumption — and rules that eating them incurs no liability.

### Rava: Peppers & Ginger

*Amar Rava*: if someone chews peppercorns on Yom Kippur, he is *patur*. Similarly, if someone chews ginger on Yom Kippur, he is likewise *patur*. The reasoning is that these items, in their dried, spice form, are not ordinarily eaten straight. They function as condiments and flavorings but are not consumed as food. The note in the standard edition clarifies that Rava is speaking of pepper berries used as spices rather than peppers that are eaten plain. The fresh, pickled ginger familiar from the table — and the ordinary vegetables one might find in a salad — would present a different question, as will become clear momentarily.

### Baraita: Peppers & Orlah

The Gemara raises an objection from a *baraita*. Rabbi Meir teaches: once the verse already says *ve-araltem orlato et piryo* — that one must render the fruit of a tree subject to *orla* — we already know it is discussing an edible tree. Why, then, does the verse add the words *etz ma'akhal*, specifying a tree meant for eating? To teach that we are dealing even with a case where the taste of the fruit and the taste of the bark are the same: *she-yihyeh ta'am etzo u-firyo shaveh*. The answer typically given in the Gemara for this case is the *etrog*, whose flesh and rind share a similar flavor. However, the *baraita* continues: *hevei omer zeh palpelin* — we are speaking here of the pepper plant. The branches of the pepper plant apparently share a taste profile with the peppercorn itself. The *baraita* then draws the conclusion: *le-lamedekha she-palpelin hayavin be-orla* — peppers are subject to *orla*. And since the laws of *orla* apply only to edible fruit, the fact that peppers carry an *orla* obligation demonstrates that they are considered food. The *baraita* adds the additional point, more aggadic than halachic in character, that Israel lacks nothing — even pepper plants grow there, as the verse says *lo techsar kol bah*.

The objection is clear: if peppers are food, as the *orla* obligation proves, how can Rava say that eating them on Yom Kippur is *patur*?

### Moist vs. Dry Resolution

*Lo kashya*, says the Gemara — there is no difficulty. *Ha bi-retiva ha bi-yavesha*: it depends on whether the pepper is moist or dry. When fresh and moist, pepper can indeed be eaten directly and is considered a normal food. Once dried out and processed into a spice, it is no longer eaten straight by any standard measure of normal consumption, and one who eats it in that form is *patur* on Yom Kippur.

### Himalta Spice Case

Ravina raised a question to Rav Mareima regarding a teaching of Rav Nachman. Rav Nachman had said that *himalta* — a compound of ground spices originating from the house of Handuai (which Rashi identifies as from the land of Cush, i.e., Ethiopia), apparently a preparation of spices with honey — is permitted and does not raise the concern of *bishul nochrim*, because it is edible raw. Furthermore, one recites *borei pri ha-adamah* over it, apparently on account of the spice ingredients it contains. This seems to indicate that spices can be eaten raw and are therefore considered food. But did we not just say that such spices, being dried, are not considered normal food?

The Gemara resolves this in the same way: *lo kashya — ha bi-retiva ha bi-yavesha*. When fresh and moist, yes, these ingredients can be eaten directly and are considered food by any standard. When dried out, they are no longer regarded as fit to be eaten by ordinary people under ordinary circumstances, and eating them in that form does not constitute an act of *achila*.

### Leaves: Reed vs. Grape

The Gemara continues with a *baraita*: *achal alei kanim patur* — if one ate the leaves of reeds, one is exempt, as these are not considered human food, perhaps suitable for animals but not for people. However, *lulvei gefanim* — grape leaves — render one liable. The Gemara then asks: which grape leaves qualify? Rav Yitzchak of Magdala teaches: any that have sprouted from Rosh Hashanah until Yom Kippur — that is, leaves that have been on the vine for no more than ten days, and are still tender and edible. After that point, they harden and are no longer considered suitable food. Rav Kahana disagrees and says: any leaves up to thirty days old are still considered edible. The *baraita* sides with Rav Yitzchak of Magdala: grape leaves are those that have sprouted from Rosh Hashanah until Yom Kippur, and only those count as *lulvei gefanim* for this purpose.

### Brine & Vinegar

A parallel distinction is drawn for liquids. If one drinks fish brine or a certain type of fish fat (*murays*), one is *patur* — these are not considered drinkable in any ordinary sense. The Gemara implies that vinegar, by contrast, would render one liable, since it is more recognizable as a liquid one might actually drink. The question then arises: which tannaitic opinion does the mishna follow?

### Rebbi: Vinegar Effect

The Gemara explains that our mishna follows the view of Rebbi, as taught in a *baraita*: *Rebbi omer — chometz meshivas hanefesh* — vinegar settles the soul. This is the Hebrew equivalent of the Aramaic phrase *yituvei da'ata* that has appeared repeatedly over the preceding two days of the tractate. Since vinegar abates the sense of *inuy* — the affliction of the fast — it constitutes a problem on Yom Kippur. This is the position of Rebbi.

### Rav Gidal Retraction

Rav Gidal bar Menashya from Bei Ridna publicly expounded that we do not rule like Rebbi — that drinking vinegar does not constitute a violation of Yom Kippur. By the following year, word had spread, and people throughout the community mixed vinegar with water and drank it on Yom Kippur. When Rav Gidal heard what had happened, he was furious. He said: what did I actually say? I said it is not a violation *bedi'eved* — after the fact. I never said one may do so *lechatchila* — prospectively, from the outset. And further: when did I say even *bedi'eved* it was not a problem? Only for a small amount — *purta*. For a large amount I said no such thing. And a further precision: I was speaking only about raw vinegar — *chai*. If it is diluted with water — *mazug* — then it is genuinely potable, and I did not permit that even *bedi'eved*.

The episode is instructive quite apart from its halachic content: what a person says in public is heard as people wish to hear it, and a scholar must be extraordinarily precise, because an audience eager for leniency will hear exactly what they want and no more.

### Eating vs. Soul-Settling

This section of the Gemara invites reflection on a conceptual tension that runs through the entire discussion. The Yom Kippur prohibitions are framed around *inuy* — affliction — and the operative notion in these sugyot has consistently been *meshivat hanefesh* or *yituvei da'ata*, the settling or abating of that affliction. Yet the mishna frames the relevant categories in terms of *achila* and *shtia* — normal eating and drinking.

One might ask: is it possible for a substance to abate the *inuy* — to function as *meshivat hanefesh* — without technically constituting an act of *achila* or *shtia*? After all, Rebbi's formulation regarding vinegar was not *chometz shtia hi* — he did not say vinegar is a drink. He said *chometz meshivas hanefesh* — it settles the soul. Could something fall into a halachic grey zone: not an act of eating or drinking by conventional standards, yet effective enough in relieving the fast's discomfort to constitute a violation of the *inuy*? Or, alternatively, is it a *d'Rabbanan* extension of the prohibition — analogous to the concept of *chatzi shiur*, which is a quantitative threshold, but here applied qualitatively: something not ordinarily eaten, yet real enough in its relief-giving effect?

The question does not find a clean resolution in the Gemara, but it is worth holding in mind, particularly when the Gemara elsewhere mentions that smelling pleasant aromas on Yom Kippur is not prohibited. The reason is that *inuy* was derived specifically in the context of eating and drinking; other forms of relief — even those that genuinely settle the soul — fall outside its scope. The case of vinegar, and similar borderline substances, sits in the space between those poles: they enter the mouth, they produce a physiological effect, but they do not qualify as normal food or drink. Whether the prohibition tracks the act of eating or the experience of relief is a question the Gemara leaves, at least partially, open.

The subjective dimension also has limits. The earlier discussion in the tractate established *melo lugmav* — the minimum quantity that satisfies the *inuy* violation — as potentially a more individualized standard than the fixed *k'kosevet* measure. But that subjectivity was found only in the quantitative determination. As regards food types — whether a given substance qualifies as food at all — the Gemara consistently applies an objective, socially normative standard: is this what people ordinarily eat? The question of whether it is a specific person's preferred food does not appear to enter the analysis.

## Chinuch of Minors

### Mishna: No Affliction

The Gemara moves to the opening of the following mishna, at the top of 82a: *ha-tinokot ein ma'anin otam be-Yom HaKippurim* — young children are not subjected to *inuy* on Yom Kippur; we do not require them to fast. *Aval mechanchin otam lifnei shana ve-lifnei shnataim* — but we do train them a year or two beforehand, *bishvil she-yihyu regilin be-mitzvot* — so that they should become accustomed to performing *mitzvot*.

This is the general concept of *chinuch*. The institution of *chinuch* applies across many domains: once a child can recite *Shema*, one teaches him *Shema*; once a child can care for *tzitzit*, one provides *tzitzit*; and so on with *tefillin*, *lulav*, *etrog*, and other rituals as the child becomes capable of each. The typical cases of *chinuch* in the literature tend to involve positive ritual performance — *mitzvot aseh*. Here, however, *chinuch* is being applied to what we might more readily classify as a prohibition — the abstention from eating and drinking. Yet the fast of Yom Kippur is itself also a ritual act, a *mitzvah*, not merely a negative restraint. That duality will prove significant when the question of the conceptual scope of *chinuch* is raised later in the discussion.

Unlike some other *mitzvot* where *chinuch* begins at a fixed developmental moment — such as when the child can speak, in the case of *Shema* — fasting *chinuch* is recognized as inappropriate to begin until the child is old enough both to understand what is being asked of them and to bear the physical demand. And as the Gemara will elaborate, even then it is done gradually. The mishna says a year or two before; the precise meaning of that phrase becomes the subject of the Gemara's detailed analysis.

### Rav Chisda: Health/Const…

The opening question is almost self-answering: if one begins training two years before, surely one must begin one year before as well. So what does the mishna add by saying "a year or two"? *Amar Rav Chisda: lo kashya — ha be-chola ha be-vari*. The resolution depends not on the child's religious maturity or level of understanding, but on their physical constitution. For a healthy child (*bari*), training begins two years before; for a child with a weaker or more delicate constitution (*chola*), training begins only one year before.

### Rav Huna: Age Progression

Rav Huna then spells out the progression in more concrete terms. At eight or nine years old — *ben cheit ben tet* — one begins *chinuch* by extending the hours of fasting: the child's regular mealtime is delayed, adding a few hours before eating is permitted, but there is no expectation of completing the full fast. At ten and eleven — *ben yod ben yod-alef* — the child fasts the complete day, but this is on a *d'Rabbanan* level, a rabbinic obligation of *chinuch*. At twelve — *ben yod-bet* — the girl's fasting is biblical. This, as Rav Huna specifies, is the schedule for a girl: *ve-da le-tinoket*.

### Rav Nachman: Later Timel…

Rav Nachman shifts the entire schedule one year later. At nine and ten — *ben tet ben yod* — one adds hours. At eleven and twelve — *ben yod-alef ben yod-bet* — the child fasts the complete day rabbinically. At thirteen — *ben yod-gimel* — the obligation is biblical. And this, as Rav Nachman specifies, is the schedule for a boy: *ve-da le-tinok*.

At first glance, Rav Huna and Rav Nachman appear to disagree. But Rashi suggests, and the logic of the Gemara supports, that they are not actually in conflict. Both opinions are in fact operating with the same *bari/chola* distinction introduced by Rav Chisda. Rav Huna's earlier ages reflect the healthier child; his later ages within the same bracket reflect the child of weaker constitution. Similarly with Rav Nachman. To take the case of a girl: a healthy girl begins adding hours at eight, begins fasting the full day rabbinically at ten. A girl of weaker constitution begins adding hours at nine, begins fasting the full day rabbinically at eleven. Both Rav Huna and Rav Nachman follow this logic; the difference between them is whether they are describing the girl's progression or the boy's, with boys reaching *bar mitzvah* a year later than girls.

### Reconciling Opinions

The Gemara then faces the language of the mishna: *mechanchin otam lifnei shana ve-lifnei shnataim* — one trains them a year or two before. According to Rav Huna and Rav Nachman, *chinuch* actually begins three or four years before *bar* or *bat mitzvah* — the phase of adding hours begins well ahead of the full-day rabbinic period. So what does the mishna mean by "a year or two"?

The *Gra*'s reading resolves this. According to his text and interpretation, the mishna is not saying that *chinuch* begins one or two years before *bar mitzvah*. Rather, the hours-based *chinuch* continues until one or two years before — meaning that it runs through those intervening years and gives way to the full-day rabbinic fast in the final period. *Lifnei shana ve-lifnei shnataim* thus describes the horizon at which the preliminary training concludes, not the point at which it begins. For a healthy girl: the hours-based *chinuch* runs until two years before *bat mitzvah*, at which point she begins fasting the full day rabbinically. For a girl of weaker constitution: the hours-based *chinuch* runs until one year before, and then she begins fasting the full day.

### Rabbi Yochanan Dissent

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees with the entire framework of Rav Huna and Rav Nachman. His position is: *hashlama d'rabbanan leka* — there is no rabbinic-level *chinuch* obligation to fast the complete day before the child reaches *bar* or *bat mitzvah*. The full-day fast, in his view, is simply too demanding to impose rabbinically on a child who is not yet obligated. What Rabbi Yochanan does require is the adding of extra hours — but only in the one or two years immediately prior to *bar* or *bat mitzvah*. In practical terms: for a girl, in the years of ten and eleven, one adds hours. At twelve, the biblical obligation begins.

For Rabbi Yochanan, the phrase *lifnei shana ve-lifnei shnataim* is read differently than under the *Gra*'s interpretation. The *samuch le-parkan* — the "close to the time of their obligation" — refers to the biblical age of obligation, not some rabbinic intermediate stage. The hours-based *chinuch* takes place during that final one or two year window immediately preceding *bat* or *bar mitzvah*, and that is the full extent of what is required.

This creates a tension with the mishna's language that is now the mirror image of what was raised against Rav Huna and Rav Nachman. For them, the phrase "a year or two before" seemed too short; for Rabbi Yochanan, the phrase describes exactly the hours-based training period, which falls in those final one or two years.

The Gemara then considers a *baraita*. *Detani Rabba bar Shmuel*: children are not subjected to *inuy* on Yom Kippur, *aval mechanchin otam shana o shtayim lifnei pirkam* — but one trains them one or two years *samuch le-parkan*, close to their coming of age. This reads naturally in support of Rabbi Yochanan: there is no full-day rabbinic fast, only an hours-based *chinuch* in the year or two immediately preceding *bar* or *bat mitzvah*.

This *baraita* now creates a difficulty for Rav Huna and Rav Nachman. If the *baraita* says *chinuch* takes place one or two years before, and the Gemara has been assuming that the word *mechanchin* refers specifically to the hours-based training (as distinct from the full-day fast), then the *baraita* supports a model in which *chinuch* only begins in those final years — contradicting Rav Huna and Rav Nachman, who introduce it much earlier.

Rav Huna and Rav Nachman respond: *mai chinuch nami de-katani — hashlama*. The word *mechanchin* in the *baraita* does not refer to the hours-based *chinuch*; it refers to *hashlama*, the completion, i.e., the full-day fast. The *baraita* is saying that the full-day fasting occurs in the one or two years immediately preceding *bar* or *bat mitzvah* — the hours-based training precedes that and is not what the *baraita* is describing.

But the Gemara presses: would one really use the term *chinuch* to refer to doing the full amount? The term *chinuch* seems to imply something introductory, something less than the complete performance. The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *eizehu chinuch?* — if a child is accustomed to eating at the second hour of the day, one feeds him at the third; if at the third, one feeds him at the fourth. The word *chinuch* is used explicitly for the adding of hours, not for the full performance. So how can Rav Huna and Rav Nachman use it to mean the complete fast?

*Amar Rava bar Ulla: trei chinuchei havei* — there are two types of *chinuch*. The word *chinuch* can refer both to the introductory training — the hours — and to the *chinuch* of doing the full amount just before the age of obligation. Both are legitimately called *chinuch* in different contexts.

### Rabba bar Shmuel Braita

As noted above, the *baraita* of Rabba bar Shmuel — *tinokot ein me'anin otam be-Yom HaKippurim, aval mechanchin otam shana o shtayim lifnei pirkam* — reads naturally with Rabbi Yochanan's position, and creates the interpretive challenge for Rav Huna and Rav Nachman that leads to the analysis in the following subsection.

### Rava bar Ulla: Two Chinu…

Rava bar Ulla's resolution, as explained above, is that *chinuch* is not a monolithic concept. It encompasses two distinct activities: the introductory *chinuch* of adding hours, which may begin several years before *bar* or *bat mitzvah*, and the *chinuch* of completing the full fast, which occurs in the year or two immediately before the age of obligation. The same word is used for both, but they describe different phases of the same pedagogical process.

### Tosafot: Chinuch Concept

The Tosafot Yeshanim — found in the lower left of the page in the standard edition — raises a series of important conceptual questions about *chinuch*. The passage is attributed to Rav Elazar of Metz, who was a student of the Ri and the author of the *Yere'im*.

The first question concerns daughters. There is a *baraita* in tractate Nazir that states: a man can vow his son into *nezirut* as a form of *chinuch*, in keeping with the view of Reish Lakish that this is done *kedei le-chancho be-mitzvot* — in order to train him in *mitzvot*. But the text specifies *beno* — his son, not his daughter, because there is no obligation of *chinuch* regarding one's daughter in the context of *nezirut*. This might suggest that *chinuch* as a concept does not apply to daughters at all. Tosafot Yeshanim rejects this reading: the *baraita* is speaking specifically about *nezirut*, which may have its own reasons for excluding daughters. In general, the obligation of *chinuch* applies to daughters as well as sons, as the present Gemara makes clear by discussing both *tinok* and *tinoket*.

The second question is more conceptually striking. The broader halachic literature states: *katan ochel neveilot — ein beit din metzuvim le-hafrisho* — if a minor is eating non-*kosher* food, the rabbinical court is not obligated to stop him. Yet if parents are obligated to train their children in *mitzvot*, surely all the more so they must prevent their children from committing violations. Why would we say that one need not intervene?

Rav Elazar of Metz provides a fascinating answer: *chinuch lo shayach ela she-ya'aseh mitzvah ve-lo peirushei me-issura* — *chinuch* as a concept applies only to the performance of a *mitzvah*, not to restraining someone from a transgression. *Chinuch* is about habituation to positive activity. It is a process of training through doing, not through refraining.

This idea is admittedly counterintuitive. One might ask: if I am training my child in observance, surely I should also train him not to transgress. And indeed it is easy to think of practices — checking *hechsherim*, avoiding *muktzeh* on *Shabbat*, and the like — that express training in observance through the avoidance of prohibited acts. But the conceptual claim here is that *chinuch* as a formal category is oriented toward *mitzvot aseh* — positive performative acts — and not toward abstention.

But this raises a problem directly relevant to the present context: the obligation of Yom Kippur fasting is framed as a *lav*, a prohibition. How is *chinuch* applicable here? Tosafot Yeshanim answers: *ha-chinuch she-mechanchin oto be-mitzvat ve-anitem et nafshoteichem* — the fast of Yom Kippur can be conceived as a positive *mitzvah*, the fulfillment of *ve-initem et nafshoteichem*. The point of *chinuch* here is not the refraining from food as such, but the active performance of the *mitzvah* of affliction. This is what allows fasting to fall within the scope of *chinuch* despite its prohibitory appearance.

The secondary question raised by Tosafot Yeshanim is: upon whom does the obligation of *chinuch* rest, and does it extend to others beyond the parents? The rule that *beit din* need not stop a minor from eating *treif* is explained, in a second answer, as follows: the obligation of *chinuch* is personal — it falls upon the parent in relation to their own child, not upon bystanders in relation to other people's children. It is not that preventing a child from sinning is categorically outside the scope of *chinuch*; it is that policing other people's children is not one's obligation. A parent, by contrast, would indeed be expected to stop their own child from eating forbidden food.

The further question — whether *chinuch* falls upon the father alone or also upon the mother — is noted as an active debate among the *Rishonim*, but is not developed fully here.

Finally, the status of *chinuch* itself as a formal obligation is worth noting: it is understood to be a *d'Rabbanan* obligation, even if the precise scriptural hook is somewhat loose. The verse *chanoch la-na'ar al pi darko* provides a textual anchor, but the structured legal obligation is rabbinic in character.

### Practical Enforcement

In practice, the gradual approach to fasting *chinuch* — building from extra hours in the early years toward full-day fasting in the year immediately preceding *bar* or *bat mitzvah* — reflects a realistic account of what can reasonably be demanded of children. Rabbi Yochanan's more minimal position, which restricts *chinuch* to hours-based training with no full-day rabbinic obligation, is noted as a dissenting view. The prevailing approach, which builds toward the full day in the final years before *bar* or *bat mitzvah*, is more demanding and requires a gradual ramp-up.

In practice, some parents begin encouraging their children to extend fasting voluntarily a year or more before the formal obligation. The pedagogical logic of permitting the experience to fall short — allowing the child to test their limits in a year when failure carries no consequence — serves the *chinuch* process well, as it prepares the child to succeed when the obligation is real. Whether *chinuch* applies on a *d'Rabbanan* level to the child directly, or falls as an obligation upon the parent, affects how one assesses the limits of enforcement; and whether the obligation rests on the father alone or on both parents equally is an open question in the *Rishonim*.

## Pikuach Nefesh & Mar (II)

### Pregnant Woman Case

The Gemara now turns to a new mishna, which opens the topic of *pikuach nefesh* on Yom Kippur. The mishna states: *ubra she-hericha* — a pregnant woman who smelled food and felt an overwhelming need to eat — *machilin otah ad she-tashiv nafshah* — one feeds her until her sense of distress settles. The precise phrase *tashiv nafshah* is strikingly close to the *meshivat hanefesh* language that has appeared throughout the last two days of the sugya. The use of this phrase in the mishna is very likely the source from which Rebbi drew when he described vinegar as *meshivas hanefesh*, and the Aramaic equivalent *yituvei da'ata* carries the same conceptual weight. The mishna thus anchors that language in the concrete scenario of the pregnant woman.

### Sick Person Protocol

The mishna continues: *chola* — a sick person — one feeds based on the assessment of experts (*beki'in*). If no medical experts are available, one follows the sick person's own account of their condition and feeds them accordingly, until the person says they have had enough.

### Gradations of Relief

A *baraita* elaborates on the scenario of the pregnant woman who smelled sacred meat or pig meat and craved it. The procedure is graduated. First, one dips something like a fork or toothpick into the sauce of the dish and touches it to her lips — perhaps that small taste will be enough to settle her. If she calms down, good. If not, one gives her the sauce itself, not the meat. If she calms, good. If not, one gives her the fat itself, and then the meat itself. The underlying principle guiding this progression is that even when a prohibition must be violated for the sake of life, one seeks to commit the lesser transgression first.

### Nothing Before Pikuach

The *baraita* then states the overarching rule: *she-ein lecha davar she-omed bifnei pikuach nefesh* — nothing stands before the preservation of life. However, there is an important exception: *chutz me-avodah zarah ve-gilui arayot u-shfichut damim* — except for the three cardinal sins of idolatry, forbidden sexual relations, and murder. For these three, the rule is reversed: one must give up one's life rather than transgress. The rest of the discussion on this daf concerns the scriptural and logical derivations of this principle.

### Avodah Zarah Basis

*Avodah zarah minalan* — where is it derived that one must die rather than commit idolatry? From a *baraita* in which Rabbi Eliezer teaches: the verse commands loving God *be-chol nafshecha* — with all your soul. That phrase already implies one must love God even at the cost of one's life. Why, then, does the verse also say *be-chol me'odecha* — with all your might, understood as all your wealth?

### Rebbi Eliezer Drasha

Rabbi Eliezer's answer is that not all people are the same. *Yesh lecha adam she-gufo chaviv alav mi-mamono* — some people value their body more than their money. For such a person, commanding them to love God *be-chol me'odecha* ensures that even their wealth must be surrendered for God. Conversely, there are people who value their money more than their bodies — *mamono chaviv alav mi-gufo*. For them, the phrase *be-chol nafshecha* — even your soul — establishes that the obligation extends to life itself. Each formulation is needed to address a different human type.

### Betrayal Principle

The phrasing of the *avodah zarah* obligation in terms of love of God — rather than in terms of the technical prohibition of idolatry — has significant implications. Loving God with all one's soul suggests that what is at stake is not merely the commission of a particular transgression, but the fundamental act of betraying one's relationship with God. This raises the question: what if an act constitutes such a betrayal without technically meeting the halachic parameters of *avodah zarah*?

A relevant historical case is the forced conversion to Islam. Islam is strictly monotheistic, involves no images, and does not worship multiple deities — in the standard halachic taxonomy, it would not be classified as *avodah zarah* in the technical sense. Yet in the time of the Rambam, when communities faced forced conversion (*shmad*), the question arose whether *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* applied to this demand. The Rambam addressed this in his *Iggeres Hashmad*, and the general conclusion that emerged was that the obligation to give up one's life is rooted not in the technical parameters of *avodah zarah* but in *ve-ahavta et Hashem Elokecha be-chol levavecha u-ve-chol nafshecha* — and that accepting another exclusive monotheistic religion constitutes a betrayal of that commitment even when it does not meet the definition of *avodah zarah* per se.

A further nuance is raised by Tosafot in the context of tractate Sanhedrin. The Gemara there discusses *ha-oved me-ahavah u-mi-yira'h* — one who worships *avodah zarah* out of love or fear rather than genuine belief. Rashi explains that worshipping out of fear means doing so because a non-Jew threatens to kill you otherwise. If so, Tosafot asks, how can there ever be a scenario of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* for *avodah zarah*? Since the person doesn't actually believe in the idol, the act is done entirely under compulsion, and if compulsion voids the transgression, then no genuine act of *avodah zarah* has been committed. If so, there is nothing to die for. The answer, implicit in the discussion, is that while the technical sin of *avodah zarah* may not be committed under such circumstances, the outward act of prostrating oneself before another deity — the public communication of abandoning God — itself constitutes a fundamental betrayal that *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* is designed to prevent. The obligation to die is not contingent on the technical culpability of the specific transgression; it is tied to what the act communicates about one's relationship with God.

### Sex & Murder Derivation

*Gilui arayot u-shfichut damim minalan* — how are forbidden sexual relations and murder derived? From a *baraita* in which Rebbi teaches: the verse *ki ka'asher yakum ish al re'ehu u-retzacho nefesh kein ha-davar hazeh* is stated in the context of a betrothed woman who is raped — the Torah declares that she is like a murder victim, not at fault, just as a person murdered is blameless for their own death. This is the *peshat* of the verse.

### Mutual Derivation

But the Gemara reads the verse as establishing a bilateral teaching. *Vechi mah inyan lamadnu me-rotzeach le-na'arah ha-me'orasah?* What precisely does murder teach about the raped woman, and what does the raped woman teach about murder? The Gemara answers: *harei zeh ba le-lamed ve-nimtza lamed* — it comes to teach and ends up learning.

On one side: from the raped woman, we learn that if someone is being pursued to be raped, a bystander may kill the would-be attacker to save her — this is derived from *ein moshia lah*, from which we infer that if there *is* someone who can save her, he must, even at the cost of the attacker's life. The same principle is then extended to murder: *af rotzeach — nittan le-hatzilo be-nafsho* — one being pursued by a would-be murderer may also be saved by killing the pursuer.

On the other side: from the case of murder, we learn that one must die rather than commit the act. *U-ma rotzeach — yehareg ve-al ya'avor*, just as one gives up one's life rather than commit murder, *af na'arat ha-me'orasah — yehareg ve-al ya'avor*, the same applies to forbidden sexual relations: one must die rather than transgress.

### Rava's Story

*Rotzeach gufa minalan* — but how is the rule for murder itself derived? The Gemara says: *sevara hi* — it is a matter of pure logic. A man came before Rava with the following question: the ruler of his city had told him to kill a specific person, and if he refused, the ruler would kill him. What should he do? Rava told him: *niktlach — ve-lo tiktol* — let him kill you; do not commit murder. *Mai chazit de-dama didach sumak tefei?* — what makes you think your blood is any redder? *Dilma dama de-hu gavra sumak tefei?* — perhaps the blood of that other man is redder? The logic, as Rashi explains, is that the entire basis for overriding a prohibition to save a life is the sanctity of the life being saved. If killing another person is what is required to "save" one's own life, there is nothing to license the transgression: one life is not worth more than another, and so the license does not exist. The prohibition against murder holds.

This same logic is then applied to forbidden sexual relations: just as one may not commit murder even to preserve one's own life, the same applies to *gilui arayot*.

This does not resolve the question of precisely which sexual prohibitions are encompassed by *yehareg ve-al ya'avor*, or whether the principle extends to acts other than intercourse. Those questions require inference from other passages in the Talmud and are not fully articulated here. What is established is the foundational principle: murder, *avodah zarah*, and forbidden sexual relations are the three transgressions that override the otherwise absolute principle of *pikuach nefesh*.

## Pikuach Nefesh & Mar (II)

### Rambam: Oness Exception

The Rambam addresses a consequential question raised by the *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* framework: what is the halachic status of one who, rather than accepting martyrdom, yields and commits one of the three cardinal sins under coercion — a gun, as it were, to their head? The Rambam rules that such a person is considered an *oness* — acting under duress — and is therefore not halachically culpable for the specific transgression of *avodah zarah* or murder. He did not commit those sins voluntarily; they were extracted from him by force. The transgression he did commit was the failure to accept martyrdom when required. But the act itself — the killing of another, or the outward act of worshipping an idol — is classified as having been done *be-oness*, and the corresponding *aveirah* of murder or *avodah zarah* is not attributed to him.

### Sex: Erection Test

The Rambam carves out an exception for a man in a case of *gilui arayot*. Even with a weapon held to his head, if he proceeds and engages in a forbidden sexual act, he is not classified as a full *oness*. The reason derives from a Talmudic principle: *ein kishui ela le-da'at* — erection occurs only with the man's actual desire. Physical coercion cannot produce an erection; some degree of the man's own will must be engaged. Therefore, if the act is completed, it demonstrates that the man's will was implicated to some extent, and the full claim of *oness* cannot be sustained. This repositions the agency from the coercing party back to the man himself, at least partially, and accordingly denies him the complete exemption of *oness* that applies in the cases of murder and *avodah zarah*. Tosafot also engages with this question, though in somewhat less direct terms than the Rambam.

## Two Pregnant Stories

### First Woman: Calmed

The Gemara now recounts two aggadic narratives involving pregnant women on Yom Kippur. In the first, a pregnant woman smelled food and experienced the urgent craving described in the mishna. The people with her came before Rebbi. He instructed them: *zil u-lechushu lah de-Yoma de-Kippura hu* — go and whisper to her that it is Yom Kippur. They did so, and she was calmed — *ve-alchisha*. She settled without needing to be fed.

### Rebbi: Righteous Fetus

Rebbi then applied a verse to this case: *beterem etzarcha ba-beten yeda'ticha* — before I formed you in the womb, I knew you. The implication is that the fetus itself is responsible for the craving — and yet, when informed that it was Yom Kippur, the craving ceased. The fetus, Rebbi suggests, was already so righteous that even in the womb it deferred to the sanctity of the fast day.

### Rabbi Yochanan Birth

The Gemara identifies the woman in this story as the mother of Rabbi Yochanan. It was Rabbi Yochanan who was the fetus in question — and who, already before birth, demonstrated the spiritual sensitivity that would mark his life as one of the great sages of his generation.

### Second Woman: Not Calmed

A second story follows a parallel structure. Another pregnant woman smelled food on Yom Kippur, and again the people came before a sage — here identified as Rebbi Chanina — seeking guidance. They were again instructed to whisper to her that it was Yom Kippur. They did so, but this time it did not help — *ve-lo alchisha* — and she required feeding.

### Rebbi: Wicked Fetus

Rebbi applied a different verse to this case: *zoru resha'im me-rachem* — the wicked spread out from the womb. The inference is the inverse of the first story: a fetus that demanded to be fed on Yom Kippur even after being informed of the day's sanctity revealed a wicked character already in utero.

### Shabtai Identity

The Gemara identifies the child born from the second woman: *nafak mineh Shabtai otzar peiri* — Shabtai the fruit monopolist emerged from her. This individual held a monopoly on fruit, deliberately withheld supply to drive up prices, and thereby caused ordinary people to go hungry — a form of exploitation that earned him a place in the tradition as an exemplar of wickedness.

### Moral Tension

The juxtaposition of these two stories with the halachic material that precedes them creates a notable tension. The law is unambiguous: *she-ein lecha davar she-omed bifnei pikuach nefesh* — nothing stands before the protection of life, and a pregnant woman in genuine distress must be fed on Yom Kippur without hesitation. Yet immediately following this clear ruling, the Gemara presents stories that attach profound moral significance — and a hint of moral judgment — to which women required feeding and which did not.

The concern is not merely academic. If the moral valence of the fetus is somehow encoded in the woman's craving, a reader might be tempted to hesitate — to wonder whether the woman who cannot be calmed by a whisper is carrying a wicked soul, and whether feeding her is truly urgent. That hesitation, however subtly introduced, could undermine the force of the halachic mandate. The law requires feeding without delay and without moral accounting; the aggadic material seems to introduce precisely the kind of moral accounting that the halacha would exclude. It is a striking and genuinely unsettling editorial juxtaposition, and one does wonder to what degree the moral narrative could, in practice, make those responsible for the woman's care more reluctant to act, or more inclined to probe whether she "really" needs it — precisely the hesitation that the law is designed to prevent.
