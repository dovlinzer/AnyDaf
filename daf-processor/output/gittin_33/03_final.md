# Gittin 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shaliach Cancellation

### Two vs. Three Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה. אִיתְּמַר, בִּפְנֵי כַּמָּה הוּא מְבַטְּלוֹ? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם; רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Initially a husband who wished to render void the bill of divorce **would convene** a court, even if he had already sent the document with an agent, and render the bill of divorce void in the presence of the court. **It was stated:** When the husband would state that the bill of divorce should be void, **in the presence of how many** people **must he render it void? Rav Naḥman says:** He must render it void **in the presence of two** people. **Rav Sheshet says:** He must render it void **in the presence of three** people.

The fourth chapter of *Gittin* opens with the *mishna* of *hasholeach get l'ishto* — the case of a husband who appoints a *shaliach* to deliver a *get* to his wife, and then seeks to cancel that appointment. Since the *shaliach* is no longer authorized to deliver the *get*, his doing so would render the *get* null, leaving the wife to believe she is divorced when she is not. To prevent this, *Rabban Gamliel HaZaken* enacted a *takana*: *hitkin Rabban Gamliel HaZaken shelo yehu osim ken mipnei tikkun ha-olam* — he ordained that a husband may no longer cancel the *shlichus* of the *shaliach*, for the sake of *tikkun ha-olam*.

The *mishna* records that originally — *barishona* — the husband could convene a *beit din* elsewhere, in a place where the *shaliach* was not present, and before that court announce the cancellation of the *shlichus*. This would nullify the *get* entirely. After *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana*, this was no longer permitted.

There is a deeper conceptual point worth noting here. When a husband appoints a *shaliach*, he transfers the power to deliver the *get* to that agent. The question is: how independent does the *shaliach* then become? Does the initial command carry itself through to completion, or does the husband remain, so to speak, umbilically connected to the *shaliach* at every moment, retaining the capacity to withdraw authorization? The husband's power to cancel the *shaliach* mid-process reflects the latter understanding — that the *shlichut* continues to depend on the ongoing will of the husband. There are related *sugyot* in *Kiddushin*, in the second *perek*, that engage the question of how far a *shaliach* exercises his own independent judgment within the parameters granted to him.

The *gemara* at the bottom of 32b now takes up a new question: *bifnei kamah hu mevatlo* — in front of how many judges must the husband announce the cancellation? This refers to the era before *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana*, when cancellation was still permissible.

*Rav Nachman* says *bifnei shnayim* — two judges suffice. *Rav Sheshet* says *bifnei shlosha* — three are required.

The conceptual underpinning of *Rav Nachman*'s position is that *le-bei trei nami beis din kari lehu* — even two people can be called a *beit din*. This is at least plausible, since the standard requirement for a *beit din* of three is rooted in the principle of *ein beit din shakol* — we do not allow an evenly divided court. That concern applies when the court is voting on a case and an even split would leave it deadlocked, as would occur with two judges. But here, no judgment is being rendered; the husband is simply making a declaration before a court that oversees it. It could therefore be argued that the concern of *ein beit din shakol* simply does not apply.

### Pruzbul Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן: "מוֹסְרַנִי לִפְנֵיכֶם פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי הַדַּיָּינִין שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי". פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי הַדַּיָּינִין שֶׁבְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: From where do I say** that two people are also called a court? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 10:4): When one creates a document that prevents the Sabbatical Year from abrogating an outstanding debt [*prosbol*], he states: **I transfer** to you **in your presence,** **so-and-so and so-and-so the judges, in such and such a place,** in order that I will collect any debt that I am owed by so-and-so whenever I wish. Despite the fact that the *prosbol* mentions only two people, it nevertheless refers to them as judges, in accordance with the statement of Rav Naḥman.

*Rav Nachman* supports his position with a proof from the *mishna* in *Shevi'it* dealing with the institution of the *pruzbul*. The *pruzbul* is a legal document by which the *beit din* acquires the right to collect a loan even after the *shemita* year would otherwise cancel it. The text of the *pruzbul* as quoted in the *mishna* reads: *masrani lifneichem Ploni u-Ploni ha-dayanim she-be-makom Ploni* — "I hereby transfer [the loan] before you, so-and-so and so-and-so, the judges of such-and-such a place." Only two names appear — *Ploni u-Ploni* — suggesting that for the *pruzbul*, a court of two suffices.

*Rav Nachman* further adduces a proof from the same context: the *mishna* states that *ha-dayanim chotmin mi-lemata o ha-edim* — either the judges or the witnesses sign at the bottom of the *pruzbul*. He reasons: *mai lav dayanim dumya de-edim* — just as witnesses are two, so too judges in this context are two. The *mishna* equates the two tracks, implying that the same numerical threshold applies to both.

### Sheshet's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת – אַטּוּ תַּנָּא כִּי רוֹכְלָא לִיחְשֹׁיב וְלֵיזִיל?!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet** would respond to this: **Is that to say that the *tanna* should have continued counting** judges **like a peddler?** The *tanna* twice used the expression so-and-so because he wished to note that one should mention the judges' names; he did not intend to teach anything about the number of judges.

*Rav Sheshet* objects to the first proof. He asks rhetorically: *atu tanna ki rukhla le-meichshav ve-leizil?* — Does the *tanna* have to go listing names like a peddler in the marketplace calling out his wares? Even if three *dayanim* were present, the *mishna* would not write *Ploni u-Ploni u-Ploni*; such repetition would be superfluous. The *mishna* mentions two names not to indicate that only two are required, but simply because that is sufficient to convey the formula. The reader is presumed to know that a *beit din* requires three.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן: הַדַּיָּינִים חוֹתְמִין לְמַטָּה, אוֹ הָעֵדִים. מַאי, לָאו דַּיָּינִים דֻּומְיָא דְּעֵדִים – מָה עֵדִים שְׁנַיִם, אַף דַּיָּינִים נָמֵי שְׁנַיִם? וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת – מִידֵּי אִירְיָא?! הָא כִּדְאִיתָא, וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: From where do I say** that two judges are also considered a court? **As we learned** in a mishna with regard to a *prosbol* (*Shevi'it* 10:4): **The judges sign below** the text of the *prosbol*, **or the witnesses** do so. **What, is it not** possible to deduce: The mishna means that **judges are similar to witnesses? Just as** there are **two witnesses, so too,** there are **also two judges. And Rav Sheshet** would respond to this: **Are the cases comparable? This** case of the judges is **as it is, and that** case of the witnesses is **as it is,** each one with its respective requirement of three or two members.

To *Rav Nachman*'s second proof — the equation of judges and witnesses in the *pruzbul* — *Rav Sheshet* responds: *mi-di irya ha ke-dita ve-ha ke-dita* — the two tracks are simply independent. There is no implication that the two tracks share the same numerical requirement. Two witnesses are two; three judges are three. The *mishna* needed to mention both possibilities because the *shtar* can be drafted either in the voice of the witnesses or in the voice of the judges, and in either case the other party may sign. It is not that judges can replace witnesses with a reduced number; rather, the language of the document and the identity of the signatories are flexible, but the counts remain fixed at their respective minimums.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵא דַּיָּינִים, לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵא עֵדִים? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּלָא שְׁנָא כָּתוּב בִּלְשׁוֹן דַּיָּינִים וְחָתְמִי עֵדִים, וְלָא שְׁנָא כָּתוּב בִּלְשׁוֹן עֵדִים וְחָתְמִי דַּיָּינִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the mishna in *Shevi'it*: **Why do I** need the *tanna* **to teach** that it can be signed by **judges,** and **why do I** need him **to teach** that it can be signed by **witnesses** as well? Why is it necessary to mention both? The Gemara answers: **This teaches us that** there **is no difference** if the *prosbol* **is written in the terminology of judges and witnesses sign** it, **and** there **is no difference** if it **is written in the terminology of witnesses and judges sign** it. What is important is that the *prosbol* was written and then signed by the court, and the precise wording is not important.

The *gemara* then works out the implications of this last point more carefully. The *mishna*'s ruling that *lo shena katuv bi-leshon dayanim ve-chatmu edim ve-lo shena katuv bi-leshon edim ve-chatmu dayanim* — it makes no difference whether the document is written in the voice of the judges and signed by witnesses, or in the voice of the witnesses and signed by judges — teaches that these two formalities are interchangeable. The judges and witnesses each retain their respective roles and numerical requirements, but the language of the document need not match the identity of its signatories.

### Halachic Conclusion

The *halacha* follows *Rav Nachman*: cancellation before two judges is sufficient. This is the general rule whenever *Rav Nachman* and *Rav Sheshet* dispute a matter — the *halacha* follows *Rav Nachman*. Both the *Rambam* and the *Shulchan Aruch* rule accordingly, as does the *Rif*.

One can observe that the proofs offered on each side are not particularly decisive. *Rav Nachman*'s argument from the *pruzbul* is answerable, and *Rav Sheshet*'s objections, while cogent, are not airtight either. The deeper question — what role this *beit din* plays when it oversees a cancellation of *shlichus*, a proceeding that involves no adjudication — remains somewhat open. Both positions carry internal logic. In practice, since *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana* has closed off the entire mechanism of cancellation, the question is largely theoretical. Yet the later *amoraim* engage it seriously, and as will become clear, *Rabbi Yochanan*'s alignment with *Rav Nachman* and *Resh Lakish*'s alignment with *Rav Sheshet* gives this dispute an earlier, tannaitic resonance.

## Takanah Rationale

### Mamzerim Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם: מַאי "מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם"? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת מַמְזֵרִים. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת עֲגוּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted that one may not render a bill of divorce void in a court elsewhere **for the betterment of the world.** The Gemara asks: **What** problem did Rabban Gamliel ameliorate that this is considered to be **for the betterment of the world? Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This is **for the benefit of** potential **children born from an adulterous relationship [*mamzerim*],** as the husband might render a bill of divorce void unbeknownst to his wife. She might remarry after having received the void bill of divorce, when in fact she is still married to her first husband, and children born from the second marriage will be *mamzerim*. To prevent this, Rabban Gamliel instituted that one may not render the bill of divorce void when not in the location of his wife. **Reish Lakish says: For the betterment of deserted wives,** lest women who received their bill of divorce by means of the husband's agent refrain from remarrying out of the concern that perhaps their husband rendered the bill of divorce void.

The *mishna* states that *Rabban Gamliel* enacted his *takana mipnei tikkun ha-olam* — for the sake of fixing the world. The *gemara* now asks: what precisely is the *tikkun ha-olam* at stake?

*Rabbi Yochanan* says: *mipnei tikkun mamzerim* — to prevent *mamzerim*. When a husband secretly cancels his *shaliach* before a *beit din* of two — according to *Rav Nachman*'s position — the news of that cancellation may never reach the wife. She receives the *get* from the *shaliach*, believes she is divorced, remarries, and has children. Those children, born of what is in fact an adulterous union, would be *mamzerim*. The *takana* prevents this by blocking the husband's ability to cancel the *shlichus* altogether.

### Agunot Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת מַמְזֵרִים – סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם; וּבֵי תְרֵי לֵית לְהוּ קָלָא; וְהִיא לָא שָׁמְעָה וְלָא יָדְעָה וְאָזְלָה וּמִינַּסְבָא, וְאִיכָּא מַמְזֵרִים. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת עֲגוּנוֹת – סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, דְּאָמַר בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה; וּבֵי תְלָתָא אִית לְהוּ קָלָא; וְשָׁמְעָה וְיָדְעָה וְלָא מִינַּסְבָא; וְתַקָּנַת עֲגוּנוֹת הוּא דְּאִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who **says** the reason for this ordinance is **for the betterment of** potential ***mamzerim*, holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, who says** that the husband can render void the bill of divorce even **in the presence of two** people. **And** since matters that occur in the presence of **two people do not** generate **publicity,** it is possible that **she does not hear** that the bill of divorce was rendered void. **And** since **she does not know** that her husband rendered the bill of divorce void, **she will go and marry, and there are *mamzerim*** as a result of second marriages like these. **And Reish Lakish,** who **says** that the reason is **for the betterment of deserted wives, holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Sheshet, who says:** One can render void a bill of divorce only **in the presence of three** people. **And since** matters that occur in the presence of **three** people do generate **publicity, she does hear and know** that her husband rendered void the bill of divorce, **and she would not marry** again. Therefore, there is no concern that this will result in *mamzerim*, **but there is** a need to institute this ordinance for the **betterment of deserted wives,** as explained above.

*Resh Lakish* disagrees and says: *mipnei takkanat agunot* — to prevent *agunot*. He holds like *Rav Sheshet*, that cancellation must take place before three *dayanim*, and a formal court of three does generate a *kol* — *be-tlata it lehu kala* — a public voice sufficient to ensure that the wife hears of the cancellation before acting on the *get*. Given that, the problem is not that she will remarry and produce *mamzerim*; she will hear about it and refrain. The real danger, in *Resh Lakish*'s view, is the opposite: the husband, knowing that the court's announcement will reach her, will use the threat of cancellation to hold her hostage indefinitely. She cannot remarry even though she wishes to, and she remains an *aguna*. The *takana*, on this reading, is designed to protect women from being permanently chained.

This *machloket* recalls the broader tension in *Ketubot* between the concern for *tznuot* — women who are scrupulously careful about their marital status and may therefore wait indefinitely — and *prutzot* — women who may act on an invalid *get* and inadvertently commit adultery. *Rabbi Yochanan* is concerned with the second scenario; *Resh Lakish* with the first.

### Kol & Public Knowledge

*Tosafot* points out that the two positions are not simply in tension; they are logically nested. According to *Rabbi Yochanan*, even if the *takana* had merely changed the requirement from two witnesses to three, there would still be a concern — because even a court of three may not generate a *kol* sufficient to inform the wife in all cases. *Rabban Gamliel* therefore needed a two-stage response: first moving from two to three, and then eliminating the option entirely. The *takana* was not a minor procedural adjustment but a complete revocation of the husband's power to cancel.

A second *Tosafot*, on the phrase *be-tlata it lehu kala*, examines the broader assumption that three people sitting as a court create an effective public announcement. The suggestion is that the formality of a court proceeding — as distinct from a casual statement before two witnesses — is what generates the *kol*. *Tosafot* notes that in other areas of *halacha*, two witnesses are generally sufficient to establish public knowledge, and so the precise threshold at which something becomes widely known enough to protect the wife remains a matter of some delicacy.

## Bitulo Mevutal

### Rabbi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּטְּלוֹ – מְבוּטָּל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לֹא לְבַטְּלוֹ וְלֹא לְהוֹסִיף עַל תְּנָאוֹ, שֶׁאִם כֵּן, מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה?
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** Even after Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted that a husband cannot render void a bill of divorce when not in the presence of the wife or the agent, if **he** nevertheless **rendered it void,** the bill of divorce **is rendered void;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He is unable to render it void, and** he also **cannot add on to his condition** if the bill of divorce contained some condition, **as if so,** i.e., if he can render it void, **what advantage does the court have,** if an ordinance of the court of Rabban Gamliel can be ignored?

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita* containing two separate *machlokot* between *Rabbi* (Judah the Prince) and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. The first: *bitalo mevutal, divrei Rebbe* — if the husband cancelled the *shaliach* [after *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana*], the cancellation nonetheless works, according to *Rabbi*. Even though the husband is forbidden from cancelling the *shlichus* under *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana*, *Rabbi* holds that if he did so, the cancellation is legally effective. This is an application of the general question — *kol mah de'asrah rachmana, ee avid mehani ee lo mehani* — whether an act that is prohibited nonetheless takes legal effect when performed. In some contexts, the act works despite being forbidden; in others, the prohibition also nullifies the act. *Rabbi* holds that here the cancellation falls into the first category: forbidden but effective.

### Rabban Shimon's Position

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees on two points. First, the husband cannot cancel the *shaliach* — and second, he cannot extend a *tnai* attached to the *get*. If the husband gave a *get* conditional upon some event within thirty days and then, on the twenty-ninth day, extended the condition for another month, this would effectively create a perpetual *tnai*, circumventing the requirement of *kritut* — the complete severing of the marital bond. While a finite *tnai* such as "on condition that you do not drink wine for thirty days" is valid — because at the expiration of the term the husband has no further control — indefinite extension undermines the entire structure of *kritut*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* therefore prohibits this mechanism as well.

His overarching rationale is the principle of *mah koach beit din yafeh* — what then is the power of the court? If the husband can simply circumvent *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana* by going ahead and cancelling anyway, with the cancellation remaining legally effective, then the *takana* accomplishes nothing. The *beit din* must have the authority to make its enactments effective, not merely advisory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בָּטֵל גִּיטָּא, וּמִשּׁוּם "מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה", שָׁרֵינַן אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ לְעָלְמָא? אִין, כׇּל דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּרַבָּנַן מְקַדֵּשׁ, וְאַפְקְעִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן לְקִידּוּשִׁין מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is there anything that by Torah law renders the bill of divorce void** and the wife remains married, **and due to** the reasoning of: **What advantage does the court have, we** do not recognize that the bill of divorce is void and **permit a married woman to** marry **anyone?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, anyone who betroths** a woman **betroths** her contingent **upon the will of the Sages, and** when one fails to conform to their will in matters of marriage and divorce **the Sages expropriated his betrothal from him** retroactively.

The *gemara* presses this position hard: *umi ika de-mide'oraita batel gita, u-mishum mah koach beit din yafeh sharina eshet ish le'alma?* — Is there really a case where the *get* is invalid at the *de'oraita* level and yet, in the name of *mah koach beit din yafeh*, we permit a married woman to remarry? The husband cancelled the *shlichus*; *de'oraita*, the *get* is void; this woman is still a married woman; and yet the rabbis step in and declare her permitted? The *gemara* answers: yes — and this is the striking principle at the heart of the *sugya*.

### Rabbinic Authority

The *gemara*'s answer is: *kol de-mekadesh ada'ata de-rabbanan mekadesh, ve-afke'inhu rabbanan le-kiddushin minei* — whoever gets married, marries on the basis of the rabbis' authority, and the rabbis therefore have the power to retroactively annul the *kiddushin*. This is a foundational principle, and a consequential one. Marriage is not simply a private affair between two individuals; it is a legal status change that takes place within the framework of a society governed by *halacha*. The *kiddushin* formula itself encodes this: *harei at mekudeshet li be-taba'at zu ke-dat Moshe ve-Yisrael* — according to Mosaic law and according to *dat Yisrael*, which includes the *halacha* as it exists at the time of the marriage, including rabbinic enactments. It is thus implicit in every marriage that it is contracted under, and subject to, the authority of *chazal*.

### Kiddushin Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תִּינַח דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּכַסְפָּא, קַדֵּישׁ בְּבִיאָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? שַׁוְּיוּהָ רַבָּנַן לִבְעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** This **works out well** in a case **where he betrothed** his wife **with money,** as it is possible to say that the Sages expropriated the money used for the betrothal from the possession of its owner, resulting in a retroactive cancellation of the betrothal. But if **he betrothed** her **by means of sexual intercourse** then **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: **The Sages declared his sexual intercourse** to be **licentious sexual intercourse,** which does not create a bond of betrothal.

The *gemara* records *Ravina* posing a sharp question to *Rav Ashi*: *tenah de-kadesh be-kaspa* — this principle makes sense when a man betroths a woman with money, i.e., *kiddushei kesef*, such as a ring. Why? Because most *rishonim* invoke the principle of *hefker beit din hefker* — the court can declare property ownerless — and therefore the rabbis can retroactively nullify the monetary value used to effect the *kiddushin*, thereby undoing the *kinyan* at its root. But *kadish be-viah mai ika le-meimar* — what of *kiddushei biah*, one of the three forms of *kiddushin* recognized in the first *mishna* of *Kiddushin*, whereby marriage is effected through cohabitation? How can the rabbis retroactively declare that a sexual act did not effect *kiddushin*?

*Rav Ashi* responds: *shavveiyeh rabbanan le-beilato beilat zenut* — the rabbis recast that sexual act not as *kinyan kiddushin* but as mere *beilat zenut*, a licentious act. They strip it of its marital legal weight entirely. The mechanism is the following: there is a *chazakah* — a legal presumption — that *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut*, a man does not engage in cohabitation as a mere promiscuous act; he presumably intends it within a marital framework. The rabbis can reverse that *chazakah* and declare the opposite — that it was *beilat zenut* after all — and with that, the *kiddushin* falls away.

It is worth noting in this context that *Rav* famously placed a *cherem* on *kiddushei biah* because of its impropriety, which raises its own question: to what extent did the rabbis effectively close off this avenue of *kiddushin* entirely through their enactments, even though it is one of the three modes listed in the *mishna*?

### Rashi's Position

There is a lengthy and important *Rashi* on this page that deserves careful attention. *Rashi* cites his rabbis — the German scholars in whose *yeshivot* in Mainz and Worms he studied — who explain the principle of *kol de-mekadesh ada'ata de-rabbanan mekadesh* as follows: *de-kiddushei kesef de-rabbanan* — marriage by money is only a rabbinic institution to begin with. On this view, *kiddushei kesef* is not *de'oraita*, and therefore the rabbis' power to annul it is straightforward. The reason they give is that *kiddushei kesef* is derived through a *gezeirah shavah*, and a *gezeirah shavah* does not necessarily carry *de'oraita* force.

*Rashi* disagrees sharply and directly. He writes — and this is one of his characteristic moves in the commentary, where he explicitly parts ways with his teachers — that a *gezeirah shavah* is always *de'oraita*. He does not know what the basis for their view could be.

It is worth noting that the *Rambam* in *Hilchot Ishut* presents a related gradation. In at least some textual versions, the *Rambam* states that *kiddushei biah* and *kiddushei shtar* are *min ha-Torah*, while *kiddushei kesef* is *mi-divrei soferim*. The *Rambam*'s category of *mi-divrei soferim* occupies a middle zone in his system — learned through *drash*, but not explicitly stated in the *Torah*, and therefore not categorized as *de'oraita* in the strict sense of the *Rambam*'s taxonomy. The *Kesef Mishneh* has an important comment on this *Rambam* as well. This places the *Rambam* in a position somewhat related to, though not identical with, that of *Rashi*'s rabbis — and the debate between *Rashi* and his teachers over the *de'oraita* status of *kiddushei kesef* is directly relevant to interpreting the *Rambam*'s ruling.

## Multiple Messengers

### Ten Messengers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, אָמַר לַעֲשָׂרָה: "כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי", יָכוֹל לְבַטֵּל זֶה שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי זֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְבַטֵּל אֶלָּא זֶה בִּפְנֵי זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If a husband **said to ten** people: **Write a bill of divorce for my wife,** in which case any one of them may write the bill of divorce and two others will serve as witnesses, **he can render** his instructions **void** before **this** one, i.e., any one of them, by stating: Do not write the bill of divorce, even though it is **not before that** one, i.e., any other one of them; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He can render** his instructions **void only** when **this** one **is before that** one of them, meaning that all ten people must be present.

The *baraita* presents the second *machloket* between *Rabbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. If a husband said to ten people, *amar l'asara kitvu get l'ishti* — write a *get* for my wife — in practice this means two of them will serve as witnesses and one will write, with the others largely superfluous to the formal process. Now, can the husband go to some of them — say, two of them, without the other eight present — and cancel the *shlichus*?

*Rabbi* says yes: *yachol le-vatel zeh shelo bifnei zeh* — he can cancel one without the other knowing. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says no: *eino yachol le-vatel ela zeh bifnei zeh* — he can only cancel in the presence of all of them together.

### Witness Grouping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בְּעֵדוּת שֶׁבָּטְלָה מִקְצָתָהּ בָּטְלָה כּוּלָּהּ קָמִיפַּלְגִי; רַבִּי סָבַר: עֵדוּת שֶׁבָּטְלָה מִקְצָתָהּ לֹא בָּטְלָה כּוּלָּהּ; וְאִי אָזְלִי הָנָךְ כָּתְבִי וְיָהֲבִי – לִיכְתְּבוּ וְלִיתְּבוּ; רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: עֵדוּת שֶׁבָּטְלָה מִקְצָתָהּ בָּטְלָה כּוּלָּהּ; וְהָנָךְ לָא יָדְעִי, וְאָזְלִי וְכָתְבִי וְיָהֲבִי; וְשָׁרוּ אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ לְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: **They disagree with regard to** whether **testimony that was partially invalidated is entirely invalidated. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds: Testimony that was partially invalidated** is not entirely invalidated; and** therefore, even though the husband nullified the agency of part of the group, **if these** other people whose agency was never nullified **go** and **write** the bill of divorce **and give** it to the wife, **let them write and give** the bill of divorce. There is nothing to be concerned about, as their agency was never nullified. Since the bill of divorce that they deliver is valid, there is no reason to institute an ordinance that a husband can nullify the agency only in the presence of the entire group. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** disagrees and **holds: Testimony that was partially invalidated is entirely invalidated. And** if the agency of part of the group was nullified when those agents were not in the presence of the rest of the group, then **those** others **do not know** that their agency has been nullified as well. **And** they might then **go and write** a bill of divorce **and give** it, **and they** will cause the court to mistakenly **permit a married woman to** marry **anyone.**

The *gemara* asks: *be-mai kamipalgi?* — what is the underlying *machloket*? The answer: they dispute the principle of *eidut she-batla miktzata batla kula* — if part of a group of witnesses is cancelled, is the entire group cancelled?

*Rabbi* holds *eidut she-batla miktzata lo batla kula* — cancelling two of the ten does not cancel the mandate of the other eight. If so, telling two that their *shlichus* is revoked does not prevent the other eight from proceeding. And therefore *Rabbi* permits it: it is not harmful to the process of giving the *get*, so there is no reason to prohibit it. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, conversely, holds *eidut she-batla miktzata batla kula* — if two of the ten are cancelled, all ten are cancelled. Therefore, going to two of them and telling them the *shlichus* is revoked effectively voids the mandate of all ten. This is dangerous: the other eight do not know their mandate has been voided, and they will proceed to write and deliver the *get*, which is now invalid. Thus *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* forbids it.

### Formality Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עֵדוּת שֶׁבָּטְלָה מִקְצָתָהּ לֹא בָּטְלָה כּוּלָּהּ; וְהָכָא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – קָסָבַר: מִלְּתָא דְּמִתְעַבְדָא בְּאַפֵּי עַשְׂרָה – צְרִיכָא בֵּי עַשְׂרָה לְמִישְׁלְפַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone** agrees that **testimony that was partially invalidated is not entirely invalidated; and here, this is the reason of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** that the husband can nullify his instructions only in the presence of all of the agents: **He holds** that **a matter that is performed in the presence of ten** people **requires** the presence **of ten** people **to revoke it.** Since the husband instructed ten people to write the bill of divorce, he cannot revoke his instructions in the presence of only a portion of them.

An alternative reading is offered: *ve-iba'it eima, de-kulei alma eidut she-batla miktzata lo batla kula* — perhaps everyone agrees that cancelling part of the group does not cancel the whole. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s objection, on this reading, stems from a different principle entirely: *kasavar milta de-misavda be-apei asara tzricha be-asara le-mishlafa* — anything that was done in front of ten people requires the full ten to be present in order to undo it. This is a formality requirement: when a mandate is given to a group, it can only be revoked in front of that entire group. This is not about what will happen if the revocation is partial; it is about the inherent legal incapacity of a partial revocation to take effect at all.

*Tosafot* points out a striking consequence of this reading. Even the two to whom the husband privately declared the revocation are not actually revoked — because the announcement was not made before all ten. They remain bound by their mandate and may still proceed to write the *get*. This is a significant *chiddush*, and *Tosafot* highlights it explicitly.

### Two Messengers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: "כּוּלְּכֶם", מַהוּ? טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר: עֵדוּת שֶׁבָּטְלָה מִקְצָתָהּ בָּטְלָה כּוּלָּהּ – וְהָנֵי, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ "כּוּלְּכֶם", לָא מָצוּ כָּתְבִי וְיָהֲבִי; אוֹ דִלְמָא, טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר: כֹּל מִלְּתָא דְּמִתְעַבְדָא בְּאַפֵּי בֵּי עַשְׂרָה, צְרִיכָא בֵּי עַשְׂרָה לְמִישְׁלְפַהּ – וְהִילְכָּךְ, אֲפִילּוּ "כּוּלְּכֶם" נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the husband said to them: **All of you** should write a bill of divorce for my wife, in which case all of them must sign the bill of divorce, **what is** the *halakha* according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? The Gemara elaborates: Is the **reason of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** for prohibiting the husband from nullifying the instructions that he had given to ten people **because he holds** that **testimony that was partially invalidated is entirely invalidated,** and one should be concerned that the rest of the group will not know that their agency was nullified? **And these** people, **since he said to them** explicitly: **All of you** should write the bill of divorce, the rest of the group **are not able** to **write and give** the bill of divorce on their own. Therefore, the husband should be able to nullify his instructions when not in the presence of the entire group, as no mistake can occur because of his action. **Or perhaps the reason of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** is **because he holds** that **a matter that is performed in the presence of ten** people **requires** the presence **of ten** people **to revoke it. And therefore, even** if he said: **All of you** should write the bill of divorce, he must nullify his instructions in all ten people's presence **as well.**

The *gemara* then raises the question of the case where the husband said *kulchem* — "all of you." In the earlier case he instructed ten people and left open which of them would actually serve as witnesses and writer. But if he specified *kulchem kitvu get le-ishti* — "all of you write a *get* for my wife" — meaning all ten must sign, the analysis shifts.

If *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s reason is *eidut she-batla miktzata batla kula*, then in the *kulchem* case — where the entire group must function as a unified body — it would be even clearer that cancelling two of them cancels the entire enterprise, since the eight remaining cannot constitute the complete group the husband specified. But if *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s reason is the formality principle — that a group mandate requires a group revocation — then the *kulchem* case and the original case are equally governed by that rule.

The *nafka mina* between the two readings becomes apparent: according to the first reading, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s ruling in the *kulchem* case would rest on the same *eidut* logic, whereas according to the second reading, the formality principle applies regardless of whether the husband said *asara* or *kulchem*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: "תְּנוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי", יָכוֹל לְבַטֵּל זֶה שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי זֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְבַטֵּל אֶלָּא זֶה בִּפְנֵי זֶה. וְהָא שְׁנַיִם, דְּכִי "כוּלְּכֶם" דָּמוּ, וּפְלִיגִי! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִי בְּעֵדֵי כְתִיבָה – הָכִי נָמֵי; הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בְּעֵדֵי הוֹלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:1): If **one said to two** agents: **Give a bill of divorce to my wife, he can render** his instructions **void** before **this** one, i.e., either of them, even though it is **not before that** one, i.e., the other one; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He can render** his instructions **void only** when **this** one **is before that** one, i.e., when the two of them are together. The Gemara now states the attempted proof. **But two are equivalent to: All of you,** as the bill of divorce cannot be written and given by fewer than two people, and there is no concern that perhaps one will sign without the other. **And** yet Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel still **disagree,** proving that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's reason is that the husband can nullify his instructions only in the same manner that he gave them. **Rav Ashi said: If** the *baraita* was speaking of **witnesses of** the **writing** of the bill of divorce, both of whom must sign the bill of divorce, this would **indeed** resolve the question. However, **here we are dealing with witnesses of** the **delivery** of the bill of divorce. In that case there is no need for two agents, so there is a concern that if the husband nullifies their agency in the presence of only one of them, then the second agent could mistakenly deliver the bill of divorce to the wife.

A different formulation enters the analysis as well. The *gemara* asks: what if the husband appointed only two *shluchim* — not ten — and said *tenu get le-ishti*? Can he cancel one without the other present? *Rabbi* says yes; *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says no, only *zeh bifnei zeh*. The *gemara* asks: are two *shluchim* comparable to the *kulchem* case of ten? They are somewhat analogous, but the analysis diverges — as *Rav Ashi* notes — depending on whether we are speaking of *edei holacha* (witnesses for the delivery of the *get*) or *edei ketiva* (witnesses involved in writing the *get*). Witnesses who sign the *get* must function as a group and satisfy the requirement that *ein eidutan mitztarefet ad she-yiru shnayim ke-echad* — their testimony only combines when both witnesses perceive the act together. *Shluchim* who merely deliver the *get*, on the other hand, need not function as a group in the same way, and cancelling one independently of the other is therefore less problematic.

### Shmuel's Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה: שְׁמַעִית מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא תַּרְתֵּי – חֲדָא כְּרַבִּי, וַחֲדָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל; וְלָא יָדַעְנָא הֵי כְּרַבִּי וְהֵי כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said: I heard from Rabbi Abba two** rulings with regard to these questions; in **one** he ruled **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and** in **one** he ruled **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and I do not know which is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and which is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

*Shmuel bar Yehuda* reports: *shmi'a li minei de-Rabbi Abba tartei chad tartei* — I heard from *Rabbi Abba* [that the *halacha* is ruled] two ways, one like *Rabbi* and one like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — *chada ke-Rebbi ve-chada ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. But *ve-lo yadana hai ke-Rebbi ve-hai ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — he does not know which of the two *machlokot* is ruled like which *amora*. This uncertainty sets up the resolution that follows.

## Halachic Resolution

### Rabbi's Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן – דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה וְעָשָׂה רַבִּי כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים. אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי פַּרְטָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי פַּרְטָא הַגָּדוֹל: אִם כֵּן, מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה? וְחָזַר רַבִּי וְעָשָׂה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Let us see** ourselves which argument was decided in favor of whom based on other sources, **as when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** There was **an incident and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **acted in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis. Rabbi Perata,** the **son of Rabbi Elazar ben Perata** and the **son of the son of Rabbi Perata the Great, said before** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **If so, what advantage does the court have? And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **reversed** his ruling **and acted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** The claim of: If so, what advantage does the court have, is the reason behind Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's opinion with regard to the question of whether, in a case where the husband violates the ordinance and renders the bill of divorce void in the presence of a court but not before the agent, it is void after the fact.

*Rav Yosef* introduces a crucial piece of evidence: when *Rav Dimi* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that there had been a case in which *Rabbi* retracted his opinion and ruled like *chachamim*. The *chachamim*'s position, as explained by *Rashi*, concerns the case of *shum ha-dayanim* — a court's assessment of property value for purposes of sale. If the judges over-estimated or under-estimated by more than a sixth — more than 120% or less than 80% of market value — the ruling of *chachamim* is *michran batel*, the sale is cancelled. This implies that the court's assessment, even when erroneous, is not shielded by the principle of *mah koach beit din yafeh* in that context. *Rabbi* originally held that *mah koach beit din yafeh* should protect the court's ruling even when mistaken, but he retracted and agreed with *chachamim* that in a case of significant error the sale is voided.

*Rabbi Prata*, son of *Rabbi Elazar ben Prata*, grandson of *Rabbi Prata haGadol*, challenged this retraction: *im kein mah koach beit din yafeh* — if the sale is cancelled even when the court made the error, what power does the court retain? To this, *Rabbi* responded by reversing to the position of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — affirming that *mah koach beit din yafeh* is indeed a weighty principle — *de-chazar Rebbi ve-assak ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*.

The implication for our *sugya* is this: since *Rabbi* ultimately embraced *mah koach beit din yafeh* as a guiding principle, he must have held accordingly in the first *machloket* — i.e., that the husband cannot cancel the *shaliach* even after the *takana*, aligning with *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. Consequently, in the second *machloket* — concerning the ten messengers — *Rabbi* must have reverted to his own original position, ruling that *yachol le-vatel zeh shelo bifnei zeh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּהָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הָךְ כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And from** the fact **that this** dispute is decided **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** as Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi retracted his opinion and acted in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, by inference, in the case of **that** disagreement, with regard to one who gives instructions to ten people, the *halakha* must be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

*Rav Yosef* also cites *Rabbi Oshaya* and *Rabbi Yehoshua min Usha* as holding one *machloket* like *Rabbi* and one like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, though each is uncertain which ruling goes which way.

### Forced Get Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה דְּמִן אוּשָׁא סָבַר, חֲדָא כְּרַבִּי וַחֲדָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: חַמְשָׁה סָבֵי הֲוֵינַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה דְּמִן אוּשָׁא, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא קַמֵּיהּ, וְאַשְׁקְלֵיהּ גִּיטָּא עַל כּוּרְחֵיהּ. אֲמַר לַן: זִילוּ אִטַּמּוּרוּ וּכְתֻבוּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rabbi Yoshiya from** the city of **Usha also holds** that **one** *halakha* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and one is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. As Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: We were five elders** sitting **before Rabbi Yoshiya from Usha. A certain man came before him, and** Rabbi Yoshiya **extracted** the authorization to write **a bill of divorce from him against his will.** Rabbi Yoshiya **said to us: Go hide and write for her** a bill of divorce, so that the husband will not find you and render the bill of divorce void after it has been written.

The *gemara* then introduces a remarkable incident. Five *sages* were sitting before *Rabbi Yehoshua min Usha*, and a man was brought before them and compelled to give a *get* against his will — *ashkley gita al korchei*. This invokes the *halachic* mechanism of *kefauhu ad she'yomar rotzeh ani* — the court coerces the husband until he declares his consent, a power that *batei din* exercised in certain circumstances in that era, though not in ours. Once the husband was compelled to appoint two witnesses to write and deliver the *get*, the court instructed them: *zilu ve-itmeru ve-kitvu lah* — go, hide, and write the *get* for her. The concern was that the husband, having been coerced, would try to track down the witnesses after the court proceeding and cancel the *shlichus* before they delivered the *get*.

This is a telling detail: the court's instruction to the witnesses to hide makes sense only if the husband retains the power to cancel the *shlichus* by approaching the witnesses directly. If the husband had no power whatsoever to cancel — which would be the case if we held like *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana* applies even to the cancellation-before-witnesses scenario — hiding would be unnecessary. The husband could simply cancel by appearing before a different court. Thus the instruction to hide tells us something about how this court understood the mechanism of cancellation.

### Case Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כְּרַבִּי, כִּי מִיטַּמְּרִי מַאי הָוֵי? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the inference as to the ruling of Rabbi Yoshiya: **And if it enters your mind** that he rules **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, that if a husband renders a bill of divorce void when not in the presence of the agent then the bill of divorce is void, then **if they hide, what of it?** The man could simply render the bill of divorce void in the presence of others. **Rather, learn from it** that **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** that one can render the bill of divorce void only in the presence of the agents.

The *gemara* draws the inference: *ve-i salkha da'atach ke-Rabbi, ki mitmarei mai havvei?* — If we held like *Rabbi* that the husband can cancel even after *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana*, why would hiding help? The husband could simply convene two other people and cancel the *shaliach* before them. The fact that hiding was the prescribed remedy implies that we must be holding like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* in this context — that the husband cannot cancel the *shlichus* at all after the *takana*. Since the cancellation itself is blocked, the only way the husband could interfere is by physically locating the witnesses and cancelling in their presence; hiding prevents that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אִידַּךְ נָמֵי כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, לְמָה לְהוּ אִטַּמּוֹרֵי? לִיבַּדְּרוּ אִיבַּדּוֹרֵי! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ – חֲדָא כְּרַבִּי, וַחֲדָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the second element: **And if it enters your mind** to say that with regard to **the other** dispute, where one instructs ten people to write the bill of divorce, he **also** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** then **why would they need to hide? Let them scatter,** and since the husband will not find all of them together, he will be unable to render the bill of divorce void. **Rather, learn from it** that he holds in **one** case **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and** in **one** case **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** This is one opinion with regard to the *halakha* in the two disputes of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

But this immediately raises a contradiction: *Rav Nachman* himself stated in the name of *Shmuel* that in a case of *yetomim* who come to divide their father's estate, the *beit din* appoints an *apitropus* for each of them and selects optimal portions on their behalf — *borin lahem chelek yafeh* — and *Rav Nachman* himself ruled that once the *yetomim* reach adulthood they cannot contest the division: *higdilu ein yecholim limchot*. This implies that *Rav Nachman* does hold *mah koach beit din yafeh* in monetary matters — because otherwise why would the court's assessment be binding on the adult orphans?

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן. וְלֵית לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן "מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה"?! וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְתוֹמִין שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי אֲבִיהֶן, בֵּית דִּין מַעֲמִידִין לָהֶן אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, וּבוֹרְרִים לָהֶן חֵלֶק יָפֶה. הִגְדִּילוּ – אֵין יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת; דְּאִם כֵּן, מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman** says: The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **in both** cases. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rav Naḥman** accept the argument that **the court** should **have an advantage? But doesn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Shmuel says,** with regard to the *halakhot* of a steward who cares for the estate of orphans: In the case of **orphans who came to divide their father's property, the court appoints a steward** [***apotropos***] **for them, and they select for them,** i.e., for each of the orphans, **a fine portion.** When the orphans **have grown up, they cannot protest** the division; **for if so, what advantage** does **the court have?** This demonstrates that Rav Naḥman agrees with the principle of: If so, what advantage does the court have?

*Rava* now delivers the authoritative ruling: *halacha ke-Rabbi bi-shteihen* — the *halacha* follows *Rabbi* in both cases. First, even after *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takana*, if the husband cancelled the *shlichus*, the cancellation is effective (*bitalo mevutal*). Second, if the husband appointed ten messengers and cancelled before two of them without the others present, that cancellation is effective as well.

This ruling directly overrules the analysis attributing one *machloket* to *Rabbi* and one to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. *Rava* also specifies that *Rav Nachman* does not hold *mah koach beit din yafeh* in this context — which is why he rules consistently like *Rabbi*.

### Monetary vs. Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם מָמוֹנָא, הָכָא אִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There is no contradiction between Rav Naḥman's statement concerning the inheritance of orphans and his statement with regard to rendering a bill of divorce void. **There,** in the former case, it is in the realm of **monetary matters,** and the preservation of the court's honor is more important than the accurate distribution of the property. **Here,** in the case of divorce, it is in the realm of matters of **prohibition,** and one would not permit a married woman to remarry in order to strengthen the authority of the court.

The *gemara* resolves: *hatam mamona, hacha issura* — there is a fundamental distinction between monetary cases and cases of *issurim*, matters of ritual prohibition and personal status. *Rav Nachman* accepts *mah koach beit din yafeh* in purely monetary contexts, where it is the court's role to manage and adjudicate property affairs with finality. But in matters of *issurim* — including marital status, which is governed not merely by convention and mutual agreement but by the direct laws of the *Torah* — *mah koach beit din yafeh* does not override the underlying *de'oraita* reality.

### Rambam's Ruling

The *sugya* concludes with a notable observation about the *Rambam*. The *Ran* on the *Rif* points out that on the one hand, the *Rambam* rules like *Rabbi* in both *machlokot* — consistent with *Rava*'s conclusion. Yet in the case of *shlichus she-batla miktzata batla kula* — the parallel principle to *eidut she-batla miktzata batla kula* but applied to *shlichut* rather than testimony — the *Rambam* rules against *Rabbi* and in accordance with *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. This appears to contradict the *gemara*'s explicit statement: *halacha ke-Rabbi bi-shteihen*.

The *Ran* resolves this by drawing a principled distinction between *eidus* and *shlichut*. The *gemara*'s ruling — *halacha ke-Rabbi bi-shteihen* — addresses the *machloket* as it applies to testimony: *eidut she-batla miktzata la batla kula*. But the same principle does not automatically carry over to *shlichut*. *Shlichus she-batla miktzata lo batla kula* — cancelling one *shaliach* out of two does not cancel the other — is a separate proposition, and the *Rambam* holds that by *shlichut*, the parallel principle does not apply in the same way.

The *Ran* leaves this as a question without fully resolving the conceptual distinction, but it is possible to suggest one. A *shaliach* can operate as a *yachid* — a single individual agent. There is nothing in the nature of *shlichut* that requires two agents to function as a unified group. *Eidut*, by contrast, is structurally a group enterprise: the *halacha* of testimony requires that witnesses act together — *ein eidutan mitztarefet ad she-yiru shnayim ke-echad* — and the principle *eidut she-batla miktzata batla kula* flows naturally from that structural requirement. Since the two witnesses constitute a single composite unit of testimony, breaking part of that unit breaks the whole. *Shlichut* carries no equivalent structural requirement of group unity, and therefore the same principle does not follow. The *Ran* leaves the question hanging, but this conceptual distinction — that *eidus* demands collective integrity in a way that *shlichut* does not — offers a coherent basis for the *Rambam*'s position.
