# Bava Kamma 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shvuat Edut

### Three Scenarios

The *daf* begins on 105b with a *baraita* citing Ben Azzai, who identifies three types of false oaths one might take when asked to testify regarding a lost object. The underlying case is this: a person approaches another and asks whether he has any knowledge of who took or found his lost object, demanding that the witness come and testify. The witness, however, swears he knows nothing of the matter — and the question is whether this false oath of testimony (*shvuat edut*) incurs a *korban*.

Ben Azzai frames the analysis around three scenarios of partial or complete ignorance. In the first, the witness knew the identity of the object — that the wandering animal was indeed his friend's — but did not know who found it (*hikira bah velo bimotzaah*). He nonetheless swore he knew nothing. In the second, he knew that someone had found a lost object, but was not certain it was his friend's (*bimotzaah velo bah*); he too swore he knew nothing. The third scenario, taken at face value, would be that he knew neither the object nor the finder (*lo bah velo bimotzaah*) — but the *Gemara* immediately objects: if he truly knew neither, his oath is truthful. *Lo bah velo bimotzaah kushta ka shava* — he is swearing the truth. The *Gemara* therefore redirects: *eima seifa* — consider instead the concluding case, which is the exact opposite: he knew both the identity of the object and who found it (*bah ubimotzaah*), and swore he knew nothing. This is the paradigmatic false oath of testimony.

### Rav Ammi vs. Shmuel

The question of *lemai hilcheta* — for what practical purpose are these cases enumerated — yields a dispute between Rav Ammi and Shmuel. Rav Ammi, citing Rabbi Chanina, holds that in all these cases the witness is *patur* — exempt from bringing a *korban* for *shvuat edut*. This is a striking position, particularly in the final case where the witness knew everything and still swore falsely. Shmuel, by contrast, holds *lechayev* — the witness is liable. Even if he knew only one piece of the relevant facts, his oath may have indirectly deprived the claimant of a legal remedy: a single witness cannot establish liability directly, but his testimony could have compelled the opposing party to take an oath, and that in itself constitutes a meaningful legal consequence.

---

## One Witness

### Tanaic Debate

The *Gemara* identifies this dispute as rooted in a broader *tannaitic* disagreement. The case in question is *hamashbia ed echad* — one who causes a single witness to swear falsely. The clear case contemplated by the *Torah* involves two witnesses whose combined denial of knowledge definitively deprives the claimant of his case. When only one witness is involved, the analysis becomes more complex: one witness, at best, can only compel the other party to take an oath; he cannot by himself establish any monetary liability. A *baraita* rules: *ed echad patur* — one witness is exempt. But Rabbi Elazar berabbi Shimon holds *mechayiv* — he is liable.

### Indirect Causation

The *Gemara* explains the crux of their dispute. The first opinion holds that *davar hagerum lemamon*, an act that only indirectly causes financial loss, does not carry the weight of direct causation — *lav kemamon dami*. Rabbi Elazar berabbi Shimon disagrees: indirect causation is sufficient to create liability — *kemamon dami*. This parallels a discussion from the prior *daf* regarding an oath about *chametz she-avar alav ha-Pesach*, where the oath does not directly cause a monetary loss but may indirectly do so. The parallel structure underscores the conceptual unity of the principle: where the causal chain to financial harm is attenuated, the *tannaim* disagree as to whether liability attaches.

## Denial & Gazlan

### Rav Sheshet

The *Gemara* now transitions to a new subject with the ruling of Rav Sheshet: *ha-kofer be-pikadon* — one who denies that he is holding another's *pikadon* — *na'aseh lo gazlan*, becomes a *gazlan* immediately. Before turning to the oath entirely, the *Gemara* examines the consequences of bare denial, even without any subsequent *shvuah*. The scenario is straightforward: Michael entrusted his phone to Dov, and when Michael asks for it back, Dov says flatly, "You never gave me anything." According to Rav Sheshet, at the moment of that denial, Dov becomes a *gazlan* and is *chayav b'onsin* — liable even for accidents — because his act of denial constitutes an appropriation of the object, even without any physical act.

### Baraita Consequences

A *baraita* is cited to examine this claim through the *pesukim* in *Parshat Vayikra*. The *baraita* works through the stages of the case: *ve-khichesh bah* — he denied having the *pikadon* — and then *v'nishba lashaker* — he swore falsely. The *baraita* first establishes the *onesh* — the punishment — for denial, and then the prohibition — *azhara* — derived from *lo techachashu*. The *Gemara* asks: does the *baraita*'s reference to punishment for the initial denial (*reisha*) mean there is a monetary consequence for mere denial, even without an oath? That would directly support Rav Sheshet. Or does the *baraita* speak exclusively of cases where an oath was also taken, and the two halves of the *baraita* address two different consequences of a false oath — one where the person subsequently admits (*hoda*), and one where witnesses appear (*ba'u edim*)?

The *Gemara* articulates the distinction precisely: *kan shehoda kan sheba'u edim*. If witnesses came, the consequence is that one is *chayav b'onsin* — liable as a *gazlan*. If one admitted on his own, *oduyei odei*, the consequences are more severe in a different dimension: he pays the principal (*keren*), adds a fifth (*chomesh*), and brings a *korban* (*asham*). The surprising feature is that admission, which is normally a path to *kapara*, actually generates greater formal obligations than the mere arrival of witnesses — because admission triggers the *teshuvah* mechanism of the *Torah* with its attendant payments. The *Gemara* ultimately concludes that the *baraita* does not provide proof for Rav Sheshet's position: it may be speaking throughout of cases where a *shvuah* was taken, with the two halves describing post-oath consequences depending on whether one admits or witnesses arrive.

### Mishna Challenge

Rami bar Chama presses the question from a *mishna* in *Shevuot*. The *mishna* discusses a case where a person who is required to take an oath is disqualified from doing so — for instance, because he is already known to be a *gazlan*. The *mishna* specifies that this disqualification applies whether he is suspect of *shvuat edut* or *shvuat ha-pikadon*. The implicit question: if Rav Sheshet is correct that mere denial of a *pikadon* makes one a *gazlan*, then one should be disqualified from oaths from the moment of denial — before any *shvuah* is ever taken. Yet the *mishna* seems to tie the disqualification specifically to having taken a false oath. Why would the *mishna* refer to *shvuat ha-pikadon* as the disqualifying event if simple denial already made him a *gazlan*?

### Possession Requirement

The *Gemara* resolves this by introducing a critical qualification. The ruling of Rav Sheshet — that denial constitutes *gazlanut* — applies only *dekai be-agma*, when the object is directly in the denier's possession at the time of the denial. If the object is elsewhere — say, the cow is grazing in a swamp — then denial alone does not constitute an act of appropriation. In that case, the person denying is merely *ishtamitina*, buying time, intending to retrieve the object and return it. His lie is not an act of seizure because he has no present physical control over the object. It is only when the object is in his possession and he denies it that his denial functions as an act of *kinyan* over someone else's property.

This is further supported by a statement of Rav Idi bar Avin: *ha-kofer be-milveh kasher le-edut* — one who denies a loan is still qualified to testify. But *ha-kofer be-pikadon pasul le-edut* — one who denies holding a *pikadon* is disqualified. The distinction: when denying a loan, the person may simply be buying time, since the money has already been transferred and is no longer the lender's specific property. When denying a *pikadon*, the object remains the owner's specific property, and denial is tantamount to claiming it for oneself — but only, as established, when it is physically in one's possession.

---

## Milveh vs. Pikadon

### Intent Distinction

The *Gemara* now formally articulates the distinction between denial of a *milveh* (loan) and denial of a *pikadon* (deposit). When a borrower denies a loan, his denial can be understood as an attempt to postpone repayment rather than a claim of outright ownership — *ishtamitina*, pushing the creditor off while intending ultimately to pay. The loan itself is an abstract obligation: once the money has been transferred, it belongs to the borrower and his debt is a legal abstraction. Denial of that abstraction, while dishonest, does not carry the same immediate character of misappropriation as denial of a *pikadon*, where the specific object remains the owner's property and any act of claiming it amounts to theft.

### Shvua Konah

At this juncture the *Gemara* invokes the statement of Ilfa — *shvuah konah* — an oath "acquires." In the context of the preceding discussion, this phrase was understood to mean that the oath, rather than the bare denial, is the act that transforms the holder into a *gazlan*. The oath is the moment of formal appropriation. However, the *Gemara* signals that we are about to encounter a radically different interpretation of this same phrase: *ke-de'Rav Huna*, as Rav Huna explains it. The phrase *shvuah konah* is about to be reread not as "the oath creates *gazlanut*" but as something far more counterintuitive.

---

## Rav Huna Position

### Rav Huna's Claim

Rav Huna cites Rav with what the *Gemara* immediately recognizes as a striking and difficult position. The case: *maneh li be-yadcha* — I claim you owe me a *maneh*. The defendant says *ein lecha be-yadi klum* — you have nothing with me. He takes an oath to that effect. Then witnesses come and prove he was lying. The ruling of Rav: *patur* — exempt. He does not have to pay anything.

The source is the *pasuk*: *ve-lakach ba'alav ve-lo yishalem* — "the owners take and he does not pay." Rav reads this not as describing the normal case where the oath is truthful and therefore extinguishes the claim, but as a general principle: once the owners have accepted the defendant's oath against their claim, he is no longer liable to pay, even if it subsequently emerges that the oath was false. *Kivan she-kiblu ha-be'alim shvuah shuv ein meshalem mamon* — once the claimants have received an oath against their claim, the monetary obligation is discharged. This is Rav Huna's reading of *shvuah konah*: the oath "acquires" finality — it constitutes, in effect, a form of payment.

### Rava's Limitation

Rava immediately attempts to limit this position to the case of a loan (*milveh*). His reasoning: *de-le-hotza'ah nitnah* — money that is lent is given to be spent. It becomes the borrower's property and generates an abstract debt. One can at least conceptually speak of an oath cancelling an abstract obligation, in the way that *shemita* cancels debts. But a *pikadon* — a specific object entrusted for safekeeping — never stops belonging to its owner. *Birshutei de-mari kai* — it always remains in the owner's domain. How can taking an oath about a *pikadon* cause it to cease belonging to him? The object is right there in the bailee's possession.

### Rava on Pikadon

However, Rava immediately reverses himself: *afilu be-pikadon* — even a *pikadon* falls under Rav's rule. His basis is textual: the *pasuk* of *ve-lakach ba'alav ve-lo yishalem* is written explicitly in the context of a *pikadon*. If the *Torah* itself says that accepting an oath against a *pikadon* claim extinguishes liability, then this cannot be limited to loans. The *Gemara* does not pause here to supply any further logical justification; it simply takes note of the ruling and turns immediately to testing it against the known cases.

---

## Baraita Challenges

### Avad Case

Rav Acha bar Minyomi challenges Rav Nachman, who has been expounding Rav's position, from a case in what appears to be a *baraita*. The owner asks: where is my *pikadon*? The bailee responds: *avad* — it was lost. The owner demands an oath; the bailee swears. Then witnesses testify that the bailee had in fact consumed it — *she-achalo* — he had appropriated it for himself. In that case, *meshalem es ha-keren* — he pays the principal. If he had admitted on his own, *hodeh me-atzmo*, he would pay principal, *chomesh*, and bring an *asham*. But in any event, he pays. How does this square with Rav's position that a false oath extinguishes liability?

### Beit Din Distinction

Rav Nachman defends Rav by drawing a distinction: *k'gon de-nishba chutz le-veit din* — Rav's rule applies only to an oath taken before the court (*beit din*) in the formal judicial context. An oath taken outside *beit din* does not carry the same halakhic weight as a court-imposed oath and therefore does not constitute "payment." The cases in the *baraita* where one continues to pay after a false oath are cases where the oath was taken outside *beit din*.

### Nignav Case

The questioner immediately presses further by raising the *seifa* — the second case of the same *baraita* or *mishna*. The owner asks: where is my *pikadon*? The bailee responds: *nignav* — it was stolen. He takes an oath to that effect. Witnesses then testify that he himself was the thief — *she-ganavo*. In that case, *meshalem tashlumei kefel* — he pays the double payment (*kefel*). If he admitted before the witnesses came, *hodeh me-atzmo*, he pays principal, *chomesh*, and *asham* — but not *kefel*, since one who admits liability for a penalty (*kenas*) is exempt from the *kenas* itself.

### Kefel Problem

Here the challenge sharpens decisively. The payment of *kefel* for *gneivah* is well established as a penalty that applies only *be-veit din* — in the formal court context. The *pasuk* itself (*ad ha-Elohim yavo dvar shneihem*) implies adjudication before judges. One cannot pay *kefel* for an oath taken outside *beit din*. Therefore, the *nignav* scenario — where *kefel* is paid — must be a case where the oath was taken *in* court. But if it was taken in court, why doesn't Rav's rule apply? Why does the bailee still have to pay *kefel* after taking a court-imposed oath?

---

## Distinctions

### Split Solution

The *Gemara* first considers a simple solution: split the two cases along the *beit din* line. The *reisha* — the *avad* case where one pays the principal — occurred outside *beit din*, so Rav's rule doesn't apply. The *seifa* — the *nignav* case where one pays *kefel* — occurred inside *beit din*, but the *kefel* penalty is itself the exception to Rav's rule. However, the *Gemara* rejects this approach as *shinuya dachika* — a strained resolution — and declines to offer it as a serious answer.

### Kofas/Kafatz

The better answer, the *Gemara* says, is *eidi ve-eidi be-veit din* — both cases took place in *beit din*. The distinction is not about location but about the nature of the oath. In the case where one continues to pay, the oath was *kafatz* — the defendant volunteered it spontaneously, or alternatively (*Rashi*) it was the litigant rather than the court who demanded the oath before the judges had formally imposed it. Rav's rule — that a court-imposed oath constitutes payment — applies only when the court itself (*kofas*) imposed the oath upon the defendant. If the oath was volunteered (*kafatz*), the court's imposition is lacking and the oath does not carry the weight of a formal judicial discharge of the claim.

This answer is acknowledged as formally better than the split approach — at least it keeps both cases within *beit din* — but it is manifestly strained. We are now required to read the *baraita*'s cases as distinguishable on the basis of whether the oath was *kofas* or *kafatz*, which is precisely the difference that Rav would need to be preserved. The *Gemara* does not pretend this is fully satisfying.

---

## Multiple Oaths

### Five Oaths

Rav Hamnuna now challenges Rav from a different direction. The case: I compelled David to take five successive oaths, each time swearing that he does not have my money or object. He takes all five. The ruling is *chayav al kol achas ve-achas* — he is liable for each and every oath, whether the oath was taken before the court or not, *bein bifnei veit din bein shelo bifnei veit din*. The liability in question is bringing a *korban* (*asham*) and adding a *chomesh* for each false oath. The principal (*keren*), of course, is paid only once.

### R. Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon explains why each successive oath generates a new liability: *ho'il ve-yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot* — since David could have admitted between each oath, every subsequent oath represents a fresh decision to lie. At any moment after the first oath, David might have confessed and paid. His failure to do so, combined with taking another oath, constitutes an independent renewed act of denial. It is therefore a new sin and a new *shvuah* requiring its own *asham*.

### Hamnuna Challenge

This ruling poses a direct challenge to Rav's position. If Rav is right that a court-imposed oath extinguishes liability, then after David's first oath before the court, he should owe Michael nothing. In that case, how does his second oath make him liable for anything? He is not "stealing" anything further — according to Rav, his first oath already settled the matter. Moreover, the *baraita* explicitly specifies *bein bifnei veit din bein shelo bifnei veit din* — "whether before the court or not" — which forecloses the *beit din* distinction, and the language *hishbia alav* — "he was made to swear" — forecloses the *kafatz* distinction, since the oath was clearly imposed by the claimant.

---

## Rava's Limit

### Exclude Admission

Rava attempts to constrain Rav's position by excluding cases of admission. Rava argues that any scenario in which the defendant subsequently admits — *hodeh* — falls outside the scope of Rav's ruling. The *Torah* is explicit: *ve-hitvadda* — and he shall confess — followed by *ve-heishiv es ha-gezelah*, *chamishato*, and an *asham*. Since the *Torah* itself mandates payment of principal and *chomesh* upon admission, Rav cannot have been asserting that an earlier oath eliminates this obligation. *Lo shna ta'anat avad ve-lo shna ta'anat ganav lo amar Rav* — whether the bailee lied that the object was lost or that it was stolen, if he subsequently admits, Rav's exemption does not apply.

### Exclude Gneiva

Similarly, Rava excludes the *nignav* case where witnesses arrive. When a bailee falsely claims the *pikadon* was stolen and witnesses come to disprove this, the *Torah* imposes *kefel* — *yeshalem kefel*. Since the *Torah* explicitly mandates *kefel* in this case, Rav's rule cannot apply. *Nami lo amar Rav de-chsiv yeshalem kefel* — Rav also did not say his ruling in a case of *nignav* with witnesses, because the *Torah* says he pays *kefel*.

### Avad Only

After eliminating every case that the *Torah* explicitly addresses — admission (*hitvadda*), and *nignav* with witnesses (*yeshalem kefel*) — the only scenario remaining is the one where the bailee claimed the object was lost (*ta'anat avud*), took an oath, did not subsequently admit, and witnesses then arrived. This is precisely the case the *Torah* does not address explicitly in terms of a payment obligation following a false oath. Rava concludes: *ki ka'amar Rav ke-gon she-to'en ta'anat avud ve-nishba ve-lo hoda u-ba'u edim* — Rav's rule applies exclusively to this one case: the bailee claimed the object was lost, swore falsely, did not admit, and witnesses arrived.

---

## Hamnuna Objection

### Rav Gamda Report

Rav Gamda came before Rav Ashi and reported Rava's explanation — that Rav's position is limited to the *avad* case with witnesses, and does not apply where the defendant later admitted.

### Rav Ashi Objection

Rav Ashi immediately objects. Rav Hamnuna was Rav's own student — *talmidei de-Rav* — and would have known precisely what Rav meant. When Rav Hamnuna challenged Rav from the *baraita* about multiple oaths, his challenge was explicitly framed around the case where the defendant *could have admitted* between oaths — *yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot*. If Rav had never applied his ruling to cases of *hodeh*, Rav Hamnuna — who surely knew Rav's position from direct transmission — would never have posed a challenge about admission. Clearly, Rav Hamnuna understood that Rav's ruling extended to cases where the defendant admits. *Ve-yada de-amar Rav hoda* — he knew that Rav stated his ruling even in a case of admission.

### Abaye Defense

Abaye comes to Rava's defense. He addresses Rav Ashi's objection by reframing what Rav Hamnuna was actually asking. It was not about admission at all. The reference to *yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot* — the ability to admit — was not the substance of the challenge but the mechanism by which multiple oaths each constitute independent sins. The *reason* each oath is a separate sin is that the defendant could have admitted before each one; that is what makes each oath a fresh decision to lie. But the *substance* of the challenge is different.

### Hamnuna's Real Q

What Rav Hamnuna was actually asking, Abaye explains, is this: *ei amarta bishlama nishba ki atu edim mechayiv* — if you say that when witnesses arrive the defendant is liable, then I understand why each oath generates a new *korban shvuah*. Each oath is denying money that witnesses could come and prove is owed. *Amti lehachi mechayvinan lei korban shvuah ba-tras* — that is why we hold him liable for each subsequent oath: because at every moment he is lying about a real debt that witnesses could substantiate. *Ho'il ve-yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot* is simply the mechanism showing each oath is discrete — because at any moment an admission was available. But the real force is that witnesses could have come and established the debt.

But *ela ei amarta ki atu edim patur* — if, as Rav says, the arrival of witnesses after an oath leaves the defendant exempt, then *mi ika midi* — is there any such thing where witnesses can testify against you and you are off the hook? *Ve-anan nekum ve-nechayvei korban shvuah* — and we should nonetheless hold you liable for a *korban* for each successive oath? *Hashta miha lo odi* — but right now you have not admitted. The logic would collapse: on Rav's view, the oath before the court extinguishes liability even if witnesses come. If so, there is nothing being "stolen" by subsequent oaths — the first oath already discharged the claim. Each subsequent oath cannot be a new act of denial of money owed, because on Rav's own view, no money is owed after the first oath.

The challenge from Rav Hamnuna, properly understood, is thus about witnesses (*edim*), not about admission (*hodah*). This allows Rava's limitation to stand: Rav's ruling applies in the *avad*-with-witnesses case, and Rav Hamnuna's challenge, when correctly read, does not require extending Rav's position to cases of *hodeh*.

---

## Final Summary

### Conclusion

After an extended and multi-layered *sugya*, the *Gemara* emerges with Rav's position radically curtailed. The radical claim of Rav — transmitted through Rav Huna — that a false oath extinguishes all monetary liability, even after witnesses prove the oath was false, cannot be reconciled with the explicit *pesukim* of the *Torah* in the cases of *hodeh* or *ta'anat ganav* with witnesses. The *Torah* is clear in both those scenarios that payment is required. The only surviving domain for Rav's position is the narrow case of *ta'anat avud*: the bailee falsely claimed the object was lost, swore to that effect in court under judicial compulsion, did not subsequently admit, and witnesses then arrived to disprove him. In that one case — and only that case — Rav holds that the prior oath constitutes a form of discharge, and the defendant is exempt from further payment.

This is evidently a very constrained reading of what began as a sweeping and counterintuitive ruling. The *Gemara* makes no secret of the difficulty. As Rami bar Chama observed to Rav Nachman, one need not "mortgage one's soul" for Rav's position; even Rav Nachman himself did not hold by it. The extended discussion serves the purpose of *Torah* learning — understanding what Rav could have meant and what space, if any, the sources leave for his view — even as the practical *halacha* appears to sit very uncomfortably with it.
