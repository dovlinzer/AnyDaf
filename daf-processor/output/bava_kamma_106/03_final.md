# Bava Kamma 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shvuat Edut

### Three Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר בֶּן עַזַּאי: שָׁלֹשׁ שְׁבוּעוֹת הֵן – הִכִּיר בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּמוֹצְאָהּ, בְּמוֹצְאָהּ וְלֹא בָּהּ, לֹא בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּמוֹצְאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § After discussing a false oath taken by a robber in which he admits to the primary feature of a claim, the Gemara discusses a false oath taken by witnesses. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: When a witness takes an oath that he has no knowledge of the status of a lost item, **ben Azzai says** that **there are three** possible false **oaths,** i.e., the oath may be false for one of three reasons: The witness may have **recognized** the lost item **but** did **not** recognize **its finder,** or he may have recognized **its finder but** did **not** recognize the item, or he may have recognized **neither** the item **nor its finder.** In each of these cases, the witness took a false oath that he had no knowledge of the lost item, when in fact he had limited knowledge of it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּהּ וְלֹא בְּמוֹצְאָהּ?! קוּשְׁטָא אִשְׁתְּבַע! אֵימָא: בָּהּ וּבְמוֹצְאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In the third case cited above, where the witnesses recognized **neither** the item **nor its finder, he took his oath in truth** when he claimed that he did not have knowledge of the lost item. Why is this cited as an example of a false oath? The Gemara emends the *baraita*: **Say** that the reference here is to a case where he recognized both the item **and its finder.**

The *daf* begins on 105b with a *baraita* citing Ben Azzai, who identifies three types of false oaths one might take when asked to testify regarding a lost object. The underlying case is this: a person approaches another and asks whether he has any knowledge of who took or found his lost object, demanding that the witness come and testify. The witness, however, swears he knows nothing of the matter — and the question is whether this false oath of testimony (*shvuat edut*) incurs a *korban*.

Ben Azzai frames the analysis around three scenarios of partial or complete ignorance. In the first, the witness knew the identity of the object — that the wandering animal was indeed his friend's — but did not know who found it (*hikira bah velo bimotzaah*). He nonetheless swore he knew nothing. In the second, he knew that someone had found a lost object, but was not certain it was his friend's (*bimotzaah velo bah*); he too swore he knew nothing. The third scenario, taken at face value, would be that he knew neither the object nor the finder (*lo bah velo bimotzaah*) — but the *Gemara* immediately objects: if he truly knew neither, his oath is truthful. *Lo bah velo bimotzaah kushta ka shava* — he is swearing the truth. The *Gemara* therefore redirects: *eima seifa* — consider instead the concluding case, which is the exact opposite: he knew both the identity of the object and who found it (*bah ubimotzaah*), and swore he knew nothing. This is the paradigmatic false oath of testimony.

### Rav Ammi vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? רַב אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לִפְטוּר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְחִיּוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For which *halakha*** did ben Azzai cite these examples? **Rav Ami says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina** says: They were cited **to** teach that there is **exemption** in these cases from bringing an offering for taking a false oath of testimony. **And Shmuel says** that these examples were cited **to** teach that the witness incurs **liability** to bring an offering in these cases.

The question of *lemai hilcheta* — for what practical purpose are these cases enumerated — yields a dispute between Rav Ammi and Shmuel. Rav Ammi, citing Rabbi Chanina, holds that in all these cases the witness is *patur* — exempt from bringing a *korban* for *shvuat edut*. This is a striking position, particularly in the final case where the witness knew everything and still swore falsely. Shmuel, by contrast, holds *lechayev* — the witness is liable. Even if he knew only one piece of the relevant facts, his oath may have indirectly deprived the claimant of a legal remedy: a single witness cannot establish liability directly, but his testimony could have compelled the opposing party to take an oath, and that in itself constitutes a meaningful legal consequence.

## One Witness

### Tanaic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: הַמַּשְׁבִּיעַ עֵד אֶחָד – פָּטוּר, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Ami and Shmuel disagree **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute** between **these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who administers an oath** to **a single witness** and the witness takes a false oath that he has no awareness of the monetary matter at hand, the witness is **exempt** from bringing a sin-offering; **and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, deems** the witness **liable** to bring a sin-offering.

The *Gemara* identifies this dispute as rooted in a broader *tannaitic* disagreement. The case in question is *hamashbia ed echad* — one who causes a single witness to swear falsely. The clear case contemplated by the *Torah* involves two witnesses whose combined denial of knowledge definitively deprives the claimant of his case. When only one witness is involved, the analysis becomes more complex: one witness, at best, can only compel the other party to take an oath; he cannot by himself establish any monetary liability. A *baraita* rules: *ed echad patur* — one witness is exempt. But Rabbi Elazar berabbi Shimon holds *mechayiv* — he is liable.

### Indirect Causation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן – כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree?** One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **an item that causes financial** loss **is considered** to have **monetary** value. Similarly, the potential monetary benefit that the witness can generate by causing the defendant to admit to the debt and pay the owner is considered to have monetary value. Therefore, the witness is considered to have taken a false oath with regard to a monetary matter. **And** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that an item that causes financial loss **is not considered** to have **monetary** value. Similarly, the potential monetary benefit that the witness can generate by causing the defendant to admit to the debt and pay the owner is not considered to have monetary value. Therefore, the witness is not considered to have taken a false oath with regard to a monetary matter.

The *Gemara* explains the crux of their dispute. The first opinion holds that *davar hagerum lemamon*, an act that only indirectly causes financial loss, does not carry the weight of direct causation — *lav kemamon dami*. Rabbi Elazar berabbi Shimon disagrees: indirect causation is sufficient to create liability — *kemamon dami*. This parallels a discussion from the prior *daf* regarding an oath about *chametz she-avar alav ha-Pesach*, where the oath does not directly cause a monetary loss but may indirectly do so. The parallel structure underscores the conceptual unity of the principle: where the causal chain to financial harm is attenuated, the *tannaim* disagree as to whether liability attaches.

## Denial & Gazlan

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַכּוֹפֵר בְּפִקָּדוֹן – נַעֲשֶׂה עָלָיו גַּזְלָן, וְחַיָּיב בָּאוֹנָסִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the *halakha* concerning a bailee who denies having a deposit in his possession. **Rav Sheshet says:** A bailee **who** falsely **denies** a claim **concerning a deposit becomes** as **a robber with regard to it, and** he is therefore **liable** to pay even **for** damage that is the result of **accidents.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to a new subject with the ruling of Rav Sheshet: *ha-kofer be-pikadon* — one who denies that he is holding another's *pikadon* — *na'aseh lo gazlan*, becomes a *gazlan* immediately. Before turning to the oath entirely, the *Gemara* examines the consequences of bare denial, even without any subsequent *shvuah*. The scenario is straightforward: Michael entrusted his phone to Dov, and when Michael asks for it back, Dov says flatly, "You never gave me anything." According to Rav Sheshet, at the moment of that denial, Dov becomes a *gazlan* and is *chayav b'onsin* — liable even for accidents — because his act of denial constitutes an appropriation of the object, even without any physical act.

### Baraita Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא תּוּנָא: ״וְכִחֵשׁ בָּהּ״ – לָמַדְנוּ עוֹנֶשׁ. אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תְכַחֲשׁוּ״. מַאי, לָאו לְעוֹנֶשׁ מָמוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **the *tanna*** also **taught** this *halakha* in a *baraita*. The verse states: **"And deals falsely with it"** (Leviticus 5:22–24), and **we learned** from this verse the **punishment** for one who denies having a deposit. **From where** is the **prohibition** itself derived? **The verse states: "Neither shall you deal falsely"** (Leviticus 19:11). The Gemara explains: **What, is** the *baraita* **not** referring **to** the **punishment of** having to pay **money** due to his denial even for accidental damage, in accordance with the statement of Rav Sheshet?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹנֶשׁ שְׁבוּעָה. הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא וְאִישְׁתְּבַע, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּבַע – דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״וְנִשְׁבַּע עַל שָׁקֶר״ – לָמַדְנוּ עוֹנֶשׁ. אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תְשַׁקְּרוּ״. וּמִדְּסֵיפָא דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע, רֵישָׁא דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּבַע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the *baraita* is referring **to** the **punishment** for taking a false **oath,** i.e., the additional one-fifth payment and the bringing of the offering. The Gemara challenges: **From** the fact **that the latter clause** of the *baraita* **teaches** a case **where he took an oath, it may be inferred that the first clause** teaches a case **where he did not take an oath,** and the punishment referred to by the *baraita* is the imposition of liability for accidental damage. **As the latter clause teaches** that the verse states: **"And swear to a lie"** (Leviticus 5:22), and **we learned** from this that there is **a punishment** for taking a false oath. **From where** is the **prohibition** itself derived? **The verse states: "Nor lie"** (Leviticus 19:11). **And from** the fact **that the latter clause** teaches the *halakha* **where he took an oath, the first clause** must be teaching the *halakha* in a case **where he did not take an oath.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע; כָּאן שֶׁהוֹדָה, כָּאן שֶׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים. אֲתוֹ עֵדִים – חַיָּיב בְּאוּנְסִין. אוֹדוֹיֵי אוֹדִי – חַיָּיב בְּקֶרֶן וְחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The Sages **say** in response that both **this** clause **and that** clause are referring to a case **where he took an oath,** but **here,** in the latter clause, it is **where** the bailee **admitted** his false oath, while **there,** in the first clause, it is **where witnesses came** and testified that the deposit is in his possession. Where **witnesses came,** he is **liable** only **for** damage that is a result of **accidents,** and **where he admitted** his false oath, he is **obligated** to pay for the **principal and** an additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** to bring **a guilt-offering.** Since the *baraita* is referring in both clauses to a case where the bailee took an oath, it offers no support for Rav Sheshet's statement.

The *Gemara* articulates the distinction precisely: *kan shehoda kan sheba'u edim*. If witnesses came, the consequence is that one is *chayav b'onsin* — liable as a *gazlan*. If one admitted on his own, *oduyei odei*, the consequences are more severe in a different dimension: he pays the principal (*keren*), adds a fifth (*chomesh*), and brings a *korban* (*asham*). The surprising feature is that admission, which is normally a path to *kapara*, actually generates greater formal obligations than the mere arrival of witnesses — because admission triggers the *teshuvah* mechanism of the *Torah* with its attendant payments. The *Gemara* ultimately concludes that the *baraita* does not provide proof for Rav Sheshet's position: it may be speaking throughout of cases where a *shvuah* was taken, with the two halves describing post-oath consequences depending on whether one admits or witnesses arrive.

### Mishna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: וְשֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה – כֵּיצַד? אֶחָד שְׁבוּעַת הָעֵדוּת, וְאֶחָד שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן, וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא.
>
> **Translation:** After dismissing the attempted proof for Rav Sheshet's statement that a bailee who denies possession of a deposit is considered a robber even without having taken a false oath, the Gemara now attempts to disprove the statement itself. **Rami bar Ḥama raises an objection** to Rav Sheshet's statement: Though it is usually the defendant who is required to take an oath in order to avoid having to pay a claim, there are cases in which the plaintiff takes an oath to receive payment. The mishna in *Shevuot* (44b) lists these cases: **And** one such case is **where** the plaintiff's **opponent,** i.e., the defendant, **is suspect with regard to an oath,** i.e., there is reason for the court to believe that he would take a false oath. **How** would the defendant have become suspect? It would be by having taken a false oath with regard to **either an oath of testimony or an oath on a deposit, or even** for **an oath** taken in **vain.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, בִּכְפִירָה הוּא דְּאִיפְּסִיל לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Since the mishna states that he becomes suspect only as a result of having taken a false oath, it can be inferred that he does not become suspect by virtue of denial of a claim alone. **And if it is so** that a bailee who denies having taken the deposit is considered a robber even without having taken an oath, then **it is with** his **denial that he** ought to be **disqualified,** as a robber does not have the credibility to take an oath.

Rami bar Chama presses the question from a *mishna* in *Shevuot*. The *mishna* discusses a case where a person who is required to take an oath is disqualified from doing so — for instance, because he is already known to be a *gazlan*. The *mishna* specifies that this disqualification applies whether he is suspect of *shvuat edut* or *shvuat ha-pikadon*. The implicit question: if Rav Sheshet is correct that mere denial of a *pikadon* makes one a *gazlan*, then one should be disqualified from oaths from the moment of denial — before any *shvuah* is ever taken. Yet the *mishna* seems to tie the disqualification specifically to having taken a false oath. Why would the *mishna* refer to *shvuat ha-pikadon* as the disqualifying event if simple denial already made him a *gazlan*?

### Possession Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּקָאֵי בַּאֲגַם, דְּלָאו כְּפִירָה הוּא; דְּסָבַר: אִשְׁתְּמִיטְנָא לֵיהּ אַדְּאָזֵילְנָא וּמַיְיתֵינָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in defense of Rav Sheshet's opinion: **With what are we dealing here,** in the case of the mishna in *Shevuot*? We are dealing with a case **where** the deposit **stands in** a place not in the bailee's possession, e.g., **a swamp, as** in this case his statement **is not** considered a complete **denial, because he thinks** to himself: **I will evade** the owner by claiming that I never took the deposit **until I go and bring it to him.** Therefore, he is not considered to be a robber. By contrast, Rav Sheshet is referring to a case where the bailee denied responsibility for the item while it was in his possession, as he intended to keep it for himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּדַּע, דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הַכּוֹפֵר בְּמִלְוָה – כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Know** that one who denies possession of an item only so that he may later return it is not considered a robber, **as Rav Idi bar Avin says: One who denies** a claim **concerning** having taken **a loan is fit to bear witness,** but one who denies a claim **concerning** having taken **a deposit is disqualified from bearing witness,** because if it is clear that the deposit is in his possession, yet he denies it, he must be lying.

The *Gemara* resolves this by introducing a critical qualification. The ruling of Rav Sheshet — that denial constitutes *gazlanut* — applies only *dekai be-agma*, when the object is directly in the denier's possession at the time of the denial. If the object is elsewhere — say, the cow is grazing in a swamp — then denial alone does not constitute an act of appropriation. In that case, the person denying is merely *ishtamitina*, buying time, intending to retrieve the object and return it. His lie is not an act of seizure because he has no present physical control over the object. It is only when the object is in his possession and he denies it that his denial functions as an act of *kinyan* over someone else's property.

This is further supported by a statement of Rav Idi bar Avin: *ha-kofer be-milveh kasher le-edut* — one who denies a loan is still qualified to testify. But *ha-kofer be-pikadon pasul le-edut* — one who denies holding a *pikadon* is disqualified. The distinction: when denying a loan, the person may simply be buying time, since the money has already been transferred and is no longer the lender's specific property. When denying a *pikadon*, the object remains the owner's specific property, and denial is tantamount to claiming it for oneself — but only, as established, when it is physically in one's possession.

## Milveh vs. Pikadon

### Intent Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפִקָּדוֹן – פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** but one who denies a claim **concerning** having taken **a deposit is disqualified from bearing witness,** because if it is clear that the deposit is in his possession, yet he denies it, he must be lying.

The *Gemara* now formally articulates the distinction between denial of a *milveh* (loan) and denial of a *pikadon* (deposit). When a borrower denies a loan, his denial can be understood as an attempt to postpone repayment rather than a claim of outright ownership — *ishtamitina*, pushing the creditor off while intending ultimately to pay. The loan itself is an abstract obligation: once the money has been transferred, it belongs to the borrower and his debt is a legal abstraction. Denial of that abstraction, while dishonest, does not carry the same immediate character of misappropriation as denial of a *pikadon*, where the specific object remains the owner's property and any act of claiming it amounts to theft.

### Shvua Konah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר אִילְפָא: שְׁבוּעָה קוֹנָה: שְׁבוּעָה הִיא דְּקָנְיָא, אֲבָל כְּפִירָה לָא קָנְיָא! הָכָא נָמֵי – דְּקָיְימָא בַּאֲגַם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty with regard to Rav Sheshet's opinion: **But didn't Ilfa say** that **an oath effects acquisition,** meaning that once he testified falsely that he did not become a bailee, the item in question becomes his property, rendering him liable as a robber even for damage or loss resulting from accidents? The Gemara infers: **It is** the taking of **an oath that effects acquisition, but** mere **denial** of having become a bailee **does not effect acquisition.** The Gemara rejects this distinction: **Here too,** Ilfa must be referring to a case **where** the deposit **stands in** a place not in the bailee's possession, e.g., **a swamp,** and as long as he does not take an oath, it can be assumed that his intention is simply to be evasive. But if the animal was standing in his house, then he acquires it as soon as he denies the claim.

At this juncture the *Gemara* invokes the statement of Ilfa — *shvuah konah* — an oath "acquires." In the context of the preceding discussion, this phrase was understood to mean that the oath, rather than the bare denial, is the act that transforms the holder into a *gazlan*. The oath is the moment of formal appropriation. However, the *Gemara* signals that we are about to encounter a radically different interpretation of this same phrase: *ke-de'Rav Huna*, as Rav Huna explains it. The phrase *shvuah konah* is about to be reread not as "the oath creates *gazlanut*" but as something far more counterintuitive.

## Rav Huna Position

### Rav Huna's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי ״שְׁבוּעָה קוֹנָה״ – כִּדְרַב הוּנָא; דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי כְּלוּם״; וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלָקַח בְּעָלָיו וְלֹא יְשַׁלֵּם״ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ הַבְּעָלִים שְׁבוּעָה, שׁוּב אֵין מְשַׁלֵּם מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **What** is the meaning of Ilfa's statement that **an oath effects acquisition?** It is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says** that **Rav said** that if one party says: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other** party **says: You do not** have any money **in my possession, and** the latter **took an oath** to that effect, **and later, witnesses came** and testified that his oath was false, he is **exempt, as it is stated:** "The oath of the Lord shall be between them both, to see whether he has not put his hand unto his neighbor's goods; **and the owner thereof shall accept it, and he shall not make restitution"** (Exodus 22:10); teaching that **once the owner received an oath,** the one who took the oath **no longer pays monetary restitution.** Ilfa's statement that an oath effects acquisition means that the bailee will no longer have to pay monetary restitution, and it does not relate to Rav Sheshet's statement that the bailee becomes responsible for damage resulting from accidents as soon as he denies the claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי כְּלוּם״; וְנִשְׁבַּע, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלָקַח בְּעָלָיו וְלֹא יְשַׁלֵּם״ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ בְּעָלִים שְׁבוּעָה, שׁוּב אֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned Rav's ruling, the Gemara addresses **the** matter **itself: Rav Huna says** that **Rav says** that if one party says: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other** party **says: You do not** have any money **in my possession, and** the latter **takes an oath** to that effect, **and later, witnesses came** and testified that his oath was false, he is **exempt, as it is stated: "And the owner thereof shall accept it, and he shall not make restitution"** (Exodus 22:10); teaching that **once the owner received an oath,** the one who took the oath **no longer pays monetary restitution.**

Rav Huna cites Rav with what the *Gemara* immediately recognizes as a striking and difficult position. The case: *maneh li be-yadcha* — I claim you owe me a *maneh*. The defendant says *ein lecha be-yadi klum* — you have nothing with me. He takes an oath to that effect. Then witnesses come and prove he was lying. The ruling of Rav: *patur* — exempt. He does not have to pay anything.

The source is the *pasuk*: *ve-lakach ba'alav ve-lo yishalem* — "the owners take and he does not pay." Rav reads this not as describing the normal case where the oath is truthful and therefore extinguishes the claim, but as a general principle: once the owners have accepted the defendant's oath against their claim, he is no longer liable to pay, even if it subsequently emerges that the oath was false. *Kivan she-kiblu ha-be'alim shvuah shuv ein meshalem mamon* — once the claimants have received an oath against their claim, the monetary obligation is discharged. This is Rav Huna's reading of *shvuah konah*: the oath "acquires" finality — it constitutes, in effect, a form of payment.

### Rava's Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתֵיהּ דְּרַב – בְּמִלְוָה, דִּלְהוֹצָאָה נִיתְּנָה; אֲבָל פִּקָּדוֹן – בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דְּמָרֵיהּ קָאֵי. וְהָאֱלֹהִים – אָמַר רַב אֲפִילּוּ בְּפִקָּדוֹן; דְּכִי כְּתִיב קְרָא – בְּפִקָּדוֹן כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rav's statement is reasonable in** the case of one who denies having taken **a loan, which is intended for expenditure.** Since the money is no longer in his possession, the Torah exempts him from monetary restitution once he takes an oath. **But** in the case of one who denies having accepted **a deposit, it remains in the owner's possession** and must be returned intact, and it is not considered to be monetary restitution. Rava continues: **But, by God, Rav said** his statement **even in** the case of one who denies having accepted **a deposit, as when** this **verse** exempting him from payment **is written, it is written in** the case of one who denies having accepted **a deposit.**

Rava immediately attempts to limit this position to the case of a loan (*milveh*). His reasoning: *de-le-hotza'ah nitnah* — money that is lent is given to be spent. It becomes the borrower's property and generates an abstract debt. One can at least conceptually speak of an oath cancelling an abstract obligation, in the way that *shemita* cancels debts. But a *pikadon* — a specific object entrusted for safekeeping — never stops belonging to its owner. *Birshutei de-mari kai* — it always remains in the owner's domain. How can taking an oath about a *pikadon* cause it to cease belonging to him? The object is right there in the bailee's possession.

However, Rava immediately reverses himself: *afilu be-pikadon* — even a *pikadon* falls under Rav's rule. His basis is textual: the *pasuk* of *ve-lakach ba'alav ve-lo yishalem* is written explicitly in the context of a *pikadon*. If the *Torah* itself says that accepting an oath against a *pikadon* claim extinguishes liability, then this cannot be limited to loans. The *Gemara* does not pause here to supply any further logical justification; it simply takes note of the ruling and turns immediately to testing it against the known cases.

## Baraita Challenges

### Avad Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב נַחְמָן וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי לְרַב נַחְמָן: ״הֵיכָן פִּקְדוֹנִי?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״אָבַד״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״, וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן. הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that **Rav Naḥman was sitting and saying this *halakha*. Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from that which was taught in a mishna (108b): If the owner asked the bailee: **Where is my deposit, and** the bailee **said to him:** It is **lost,** and the owner said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the bailee **said: Amen,** thereby accepting the oath, **and the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he consumed** the deposit, then he must **pay the principal.** If he **admitted of his own** accord that he had taken a false oath, then he must **pay** the **principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** bring **a guilt-offering.** This indicates that one is required to pay monetary restitution for a deposit even after taking an oath.

Rav Acha bar Minyomi challenges Rav Nachman, who has been expounding Rav's position, from a case in what appears to be a *baraita*. The owner asks: where is my *pikadon*? The bailee responds: *avad* — it was lost. The owner demands an oath; the bailee swears. Then witnesses testify that the bailee had in fact consumed it — *she-achalo* — he had appropriated it for himself. In that case, *meshalem es ha-keren* — he pays the principal. If he had admitted on his own, *hodeh me-atzmo*, he would pay principal, *chomesh*, and bring an *asham*. But in any event, he pays. How does this square with Rav's position that a false oath extinguishes liability?

### Beit Din Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּנִשְׁבַּע חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to him: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where one takes an oath outside of court,** which does not exempt him from paying restitution.

Rav Nachman defends Rav by drawing a distinction: *k'gon de-nishba chutz le-veit din* — Rav's rule applies only to an oath taken before the court (*beit din*) in the formal judicial context. An oath taken outside *beit din* does not carry the same halakhic weight as a court-imposed oath and therefore does not constitute "payment." The cases in the *baraita* where one continues to pay after a false oath are cases where the oath was taken outside *beit din*.

### Nignav Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״הֵיכָן פִּקְדוֹנִי״? אָמַר לוֹ: ״נִגְנַב״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״, וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁגְּנָבוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi **said to him: If so, say the latter clause** of the mishna: If the owner asked the bailee: **Where is my deposit,** and the bailee **said to him:** It was **stolen,** and the owner said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the bailee **said: Amen,** thereby accepting the oath; **and the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he stole it,** he must **pay** the **payment of double** the principal. If he **admitted of his own** accord that he had taken a false oath, then he must **pay** the **principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** bring **a guilt-offering.**

The questioner immediately presses further by raising the *seifa* — the second case of the same *baraita* or *mishna*. The owner asks: where is my *pikadon*? The bailee responds: *nignav* — it was stolen. He takes an oath to that effect. Witnesses then testify that he himself was the thief — *she-ganavo*. In that case, *meshalem tashlumei kefel* — he pays the double payment (*kefel*). If he admitted before the witnesses came, *hodeh me-atzmo*, he pays principal, *chomesh*, and *asham* — but not *kefel*, since one who admits liability for a penalty (*kenas*) is exempt from the *kenas* itself.

### Kefel Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, מִי אִיכָּא כֶּפֶל?
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** that this oath was taken **outside of court, is there** an obligation of **double payment** in such a circumstance?

Here the challenge sharpens decisively. The payment of *kefel* for *gneivah* is well established as a penalty that applies only *be-veit din* — in the formal court context. The *pasuk* itself (*ad ha-Elohim yavo dvar shneihem*) implies adjudication before judges. One cannot pay *kefel* for an oath taken outside *beit din*. Therefore, the *nignav* scenario — where *kefel* is paid — must be a case where the oath was taken *in* court. But if it was taken in court, why doesn't Rav's rule apply? Why does the bailee still have to pay *kefel* after taking a court-imposed oath?

## Distinctions

### Split Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, יָכֵילְנָא לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי לָךְ: רֵישָׁא חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, וְסֵיפָא בְּבֵית דִּין; מִיהוּ שִׁינּוּיָא דְּחִיקָא לָא מְשַׁנֵּינָא לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: I am able to answer you** by saying that **the first clause** of the mishna is referring to one who took an oath **outside of court, and the latter clause** of the mishna is referring to one who took an oath **in court, but we will not answer you with a forced answer.**

The *Gemara* first considers a simple solution: split the two cases along the *beit din* line. The *reisha* — the *avad* case where one pays the principal — occurred outside *beit din*, so Rav's rule doesn't apply. The *seifa* — the *nignav* case where one pays *kefel* — occurred inside *beit din*, but the *kefel* penalty is itself the exception to Rav's rule. However, the *Gemara* rejects this approach as *shinuya dachika* — a strained resolution — and declines to offer it as a serious answer.

### Kofas/Kafatz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּבֵית דִּין – וְלָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בְּקָפַץ, כָּאן בְּשֶׁלֹּא קָפַץ.
>
> **Translation:** Instead, I will explain it in this manner: Both **this** clause **and that** clause are referring to an oath taken **in court, and** it is **not difficult. Here,** the ruling of the first clause is **with regard to** one who **leaped** to take an oath as soon as the other litigant stated his claim, even though the court had not yet required him to do so, which is not considered a full-fledged oath. **There,** the ruling of the latter clause is **with regard to** one **who did not leap** but took an oath only when required by the court.

The better answer, the *Gemara* says, is *eidi ve-eidi be-veit din* — both cases took place in *beit din*. The distinction is not about location but about the nature of the oath. In the case where one continues to pay, the oath was *kafatz* — the defendant volunteered it spontaneously, or alternatively (*Rashi*) it was the litigant rather than the court who demanded the oath before the judges had formally imposed it. Rav's rule — that a court-imposed oath constitutes payment — applies only when the court itself (*kofas*) imposed the oath upon the defendant. If the oath was volunteered (*kafatz*), the court's imposition is lacking and the oath does not carry the weight of a formal judicial discharge of the claim.

This answer is acknowledged as formally better than the split approach — at least it keeps both cases within *beit din* — but it is manifestly strained. We are now required to read the *baraita*'s cases as distinguishable on the basis of whether the oath was *kofas* or *kafatz*, which is precisely the difference that Rav would need to be preserved. The *Gemara* does not pretend this is fully satisfying.

## Multiple Oaths

### Five Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הִשְׁבִּיעַ עָלָיו חֲמִשָּׁה פְּעָמִים, בֵּין בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין, וְכָפַר עָלָיו – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** to Rav's statement from a different mishna (*Shevuot* 36b), which states: If an owner requested the return of his deposit and the purported bailee denied having received it, and the owner **administered an oath** to the bailee **about** the deposit **five times, whether before the court or not before the court, and** the bailee **denied** the claim each time, **he is liable for each and every one** of the oaths and must pay the additional one-fifth payment and bring a guilt-offering for each one. **And Rabbi Shimon says: What is the reason? Since he is able to retract and admit** after each oath, at which point he would be liable to pay, therefore each subsequent false oath is a denial of that liability.

Rav Hamnuna now challenges Rav from a different direction. The case: I compelled David to take five successive oaths, each time swearing that he does not have my money or object. He takes all five. The ruling is *chayav al kol achat ve-achat* — he is liable for each and every oath, whether the oath was taken before the court or not, *bein bifnei veit din bein shelo bifnei veit din*. The liability in question is bringing a *korban* (*asham*) and adding a *chomesh* for each false oath. The principal (*keren*), of course, is paid only once.

### R. Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon explains why each successive oath generates a new liability: *ho'il ve-yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot* — since David could have admitted between each oath, every subsequent oath represents a fresh decision to lie. At any moment after the first oath, David might have confessed and paid. His failure to do so, combined with taking another oath, constitutes an independent renewed act of denial. It is therefore a new sin and a new *shvuah* requiring its own *asham*.

### Hamnuna Challenge

This ruling poses a direct challenge to Rav's position. If Rav is right that a court-imposed oath extinguishes liability, then after David's first oath before the court, he should owe Michael nothing. In that case, how does his second oath make him liable for anything? He is not "stealing" anything further — according to Rav, his first oath already settled the matter. Moreover, the *baraita* explicitly specifies *bein bifnei veit din bein shelo bifnei veit din* — "whether before the court or not" — which forecloses the *beit din* distinction, and the language *hishbia alav* — "he was made to swear" — forecloses the *kafatz* distinction, since the oath was clearly imposed by the claimant.

## Rava's Limit

### Exclude Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה רַב הַמְנוּנָא, תַּלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַב – וְיָדַע דְּאָמַר רַב הוֹדָה, וְקָמוֹתֵיב הוֹדָה; וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ: הוֹדָה לָא אָמַר רַב?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: Now** consider: **And even Rav Hamnuna,** who was **Rav's student and knew that Rav says** that one who takes a false oath is exempt from paying restitution even in a case where he **admitted** it; **and** Rav Hamnuna therefore **challenged** him from a mishna that teaches that one is not exempt in a case where he **admitted** it. **And you say** that **Rav did not say** his *halakha* in a case where one **admitted** that he took a false oath?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, רַב הַמְנוּנָא הָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa the Elder said to Rav Ashi: Rav Hamnuna** did not raise a difficulty against Rav with regard to the case of one admitting to having taken a false oath, rather, with regard to the case where witnesses came after he took the oath, and **this** is what **is difficult for him:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא נִשְׁבַּע – כִּי אֲתוֹ עֵדִים מִיחַיַּיב, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי מְחַיְּיבִינַן לֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן אַשְּׁבוּעָה בָּתְרָיְיתָא, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת;
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that when **one takes** a false **oath** and **then witnesses come, he is liable** to pay, **that is why we hold him liable** for a guilt-**offering for the final oath, since he can** still **retract and admit** his liability and incur an obligation to pay. Consequently, the fact that he took another false oath constitutes an additional false denial of his monetary liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ כִּי אֲתוֹ עֵדִים פָּטוּר, מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּאִילּוּ אָתוּ סָהֲדִי וּמַסְהֲדִי בֵּיהּ – פָּטוּר, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּ נִיחַיְּיבֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן אַשְּׁבוּעָה הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת?! הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת לָא אוֹדִי!
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say,** as Rav did, that even **when witnesses come he is exempt** from paying, then the mishna's ruling is difficult, as **is there any** similar case **where if witnesses come and testify with regard to it, he would be exempt, and** yet **we would stand and obligate him** to bring a guilt-**offering for a** later **oath,** for no reason other than **since he can** still **retract and admit** his liability? As of **now, in any event, he has not admitted** to any liability and has no extant debt, and any further oaths that he takes are not denials of monetary liability. It follows, then, that Rava's explanation of Rav's opinion cannot be undermined by Rav Hamnuna's challenge.

Rava attempts to constrain Rav's position by excluding cases of admission. Rava argues that any scenario in which the defendant subsequently admits — *hodeh* — falls outside the scope of Rav's ruling. The *Torah* is explicit: *ve-hitvadda* — and he shall confess — followed by *ve-heishiv es ha-gezelah*, *chamishato*, and an *asham*. Since the *Torah* itself mandates payment of principal and *chomesh* upon admission, Rav cannot have been asserting that an earlier oath eliminates this obligation.

### Exclude Gneiva

Rava also excludes the *nignav* case where witnesses arrive. When a bailee falsely claims the *pikadon* was stolen and witnesses come to disprove this, the *Torah* imposes *kefel* — *yeshalem kefel*. Since the *Torah* explicitly mandates *kefel* in this case, Rav's rule cannot apply.

### Avad Only

Rava concludes by examining what remains after excluding these cases. After eliminating every case that the *Torah* explicitly addresses — admission (*hitvadda*), and *nignav* with witnesses (*yeshalem kefel*) — the only scenario remaining is the one where the bailee claimed the object was lost (*ta'anat avud*), took an oath, did not subsequently admit, and witnesses then arrived. This is precisely the case the *Torah* does not address explicitly in terms of a payment obligation following a false oath.

---

## Hamnuna Objection

### Rav Gamda Report

Rav Gamda came before Rav Ashi and reported Rava's explanation — that Rav's position is limited to the *avad* case with witnesses, and does not apply where the defendant later admitted.

### Rav Ashi Objection

Rav Ashi immediately objects. Rav Hamnuna was Rav's own student — *talmidei de-Rav* — and would have known precisely what Rav meant. When Rav Hamnuna challenged Rav from the *baraita* about multiple oaths, his challenge was explicitly framed around the case where the defendant *could have admitted* between oaths — *yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot*. If Rav had never applied his ruling to cases of *hodeh*, Rav Hamnuna — who surely knew Rav's position from direct transmission — would never have posed a challenge about admission. Clearly, Rav Hamnuna understood that Rav's ruling extended to cases where the defendant admits.

### Abaye Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, רַב הַמְנוּנָא הָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa the Elder said to Rav Ashi: Rav Hamnuna** did not raise a difficulty against Rav with regard to the case of one admitting to having taken a false oath, rather, with regard to the case where witnesses came after he took the oath, and **this** is what **is difficult for him:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא נִשְׁבַּע – כִּי אֲתוֹ עֵדִים מִיחַיַּיב, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי מְחַיְּיבִינַן לֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן אַשְּׁבוּעָה בָּתְרָיְיתָא, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת;
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that when **one takes** a false **oath** and **then witnesses come, he is liable** to pay, **that is why we hold him liable** for a guilt-**offering for the final oath, since he can** still **retract and admit** his liability and incur an obligation to pay. Consequently, the fact that he took another false oath constitutes an additional false denial of his monetary liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ כִּי אֲתוֹ עֵדִים פָּטוּר, מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּאִילּוּ אָתוּ סָהֲדִי וּמַסְהֲדִי בֵּיהּ – פָּטוּר, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּ נִיחַיְּיבֵיהּ קׇרְבָּן אַשְּׁבוּעָה הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת?! הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת לָא אוֹדִי!
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say,** as Rav did, that even **when witnesses come he is exempt** from paying, then the mishna's ruling is difficult, as **is there any** similar case **where if witnesses come and testify with regard to it, he would be exempt, and** yet **we would stand and obligate him** to bring a guilt-**offering for a** later **oath,** for no reason other than **since he can** still **retract and admit** his liability? As of **now, in any event, he has not admitted** to any liability and has no extant debt, and any further oaths that he takes are not denials of monetary liability. It follows, then, that Rava's explanation of Rav's opinion cannot be undermined by Rav Hamnuna's challenge.

Abaye comes to Rava's defense. He addresses Rav Ashi's objection by reframing what Rav Hamnuna was actually asking. It was not about admission at all. The reference to *yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot* — the ability to admit — was not the substance of the challenge but the mechanism by which multiple oaths each constitute independent sins. The *reason* each oath is a separate sin is that the defendant could have admitted before each one; that is what makes each oath a fresh decision to lie. But the *substance* of the challenge is different.

What Rav Hamnuna was actually asking, Abaye explains, is this: *ei amarta bishlama nishba ki atu edim mechayiv* — if you say that when witnesses arrive the defendant is liable, then I understand why each oath generates a new *korban shvuah*. Each oath is denying money that witnesses could come and prove is owed. *Amti lehachi mechayvinan lei korban shvuah ba-tras* — that is why we hold him liable for each subsequent oath: because at every moment he is lying about a real debt that witnesses could substantiate. *Ho'il ve-yakhol lachazor le-hodo'ot* is simply the mechanism showing each oath is discrete — because at any moment an admission was available. But the real force is that witnesses could have come and established the debt.

But *ela ei amarta ki atu edim patur* — if, as Rav says, the arrival of witnesses after an oath leaves the defendant exempt, then *mi ika midi* — is there any such thing where witnesses can testify against you and you are off the hook? *Ve-anan nekum ve-nechayvei korban shvuah* — and we should nonetheless hold you liable for a *korban* for each successive oath? *Hashta miha lo odi* — but right now you have not admitted. The logic would collapse: on Rav's view, the oath before the court extinguishes liability even if witnesses come. If so, there is nothing being "stolen" by subsequent oaths — the first oath already discharged the claim. Each subsequent oath cannot be a new act of denial of money owed, because on Rav's own view, no money is owed after the first oath.

The challenge from Rav Hamnuna, properly understood, is thus about witnesses (*edim*), not about admission (*hodah*). This allows Rava's limitation to stand: Rav's ruling applies in the *avad*-with-witnesses case, and Rav Hamnuna's challenge, when correctly read, does not require extending Rav's position to cases of *hodeh*.

---

## Final Summary

### Conclusion

After an extended and multi-layered *sugya*, the *Gemara* emerges with Rav's position radically curtailed. The radical claim of Rav — transmitted through Rav Huna — that a false oath extinguishes all monetary liability, even after witnesses prove the oath was false, cannot be reconciled with the explicit *pesukim* of the *Torah* in the cases of *hodeh* or *ta'anat ganav* with witnesses. The *Torah* is clear in both those scenarios that payment is required. The only surviving domain for Rav's position is the narrow case of *ta'anat avud*: the bailee falsely claimed the object was lost, swore to that effect in court under judicial compulsion, did not subsequently admit, and witnesses then arrived to disprove him. In that one case — and only that case — Rav holds that the prior oath constitutes a form of discharge, and the defendant is exempt from further payment.

This is evidently a very constrained reading of what began as a sweeping and counterintuitive ruling. The *Gemara* makes no secret of the difficulty. As Rami bar Chama observed to Rav Nachman, one need not "mortgage one's soul" for Rav's position; even Rav Nachman himself did not hold by it. The extended discussion serves the purpose of *Torah* learning — understanding what Rav could have meant and what space, if any, the sources leave for his view — even as the practical *halacha* appears to sit very uncomfortably with it.
