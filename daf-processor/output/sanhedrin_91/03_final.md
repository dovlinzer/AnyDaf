# Sanhedrin 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chapter Order & Olam HaBa

### Perek Order Correction

We begin the last *perek* of Sanhedrin, *Perek Chelek*. Before proceeding, a correction is in order regarding something mentioned before *Yom Tov*, when we started *Elu Hen Hanhenakim*: at that time it was suggested that some authorities list *Chelek* as the tenth *perek* and *Elu Hen Hanhenakim* as the eleventh, but that logically *Chelek* should be seen as the final *perek*, because after working through all the *misos beis din* there is a natural transition to discussing who lacks a *chelek l'olam haba*, and the *masechet* then concludes with a broader *aggadic* sweep. That is the position of Rashi, and it explains our received order. It was also noted that the *Yerushalmi* has the order reversed.

On further reflection — and confirmed by examining the *Tosefta*, where the sequence is more firmly embedded — it is clear that the correct order is actually the reverse of what we have in our editions. The reason is structural: there is one of the *arba misos* that had not yet been treated, namely *Ir Nidachas*, which falls under the category of *hereg*. And *Ir Nidachas* is in fact treated at the very end of *Chelek*. This means *Chelek* must be the second-to-last *perek*, not the last. The sequence runs: *srefa* is covered, then *chenek*, then *hereg* — and under *hereg* we have *retzicha* and *Ir Nidachas*. *Chelek* ends with *Ir Nidachas*, completing *hereg*, and then *Elu Hen Hanhenakim* follows as the final *perek* on *chenek*. This is clearly the order of both the *Yerushalmi* and the *Tosefta*.

This still raises an obvious question: why insert such an extensive *perek* about who has a *chelek l'olam haba* before one has even finished discussing all the *arba misos beis din*? A partial answer is that *Ir Nidachas* itself generates the question of whether the inhabitants of the *ir nidachas* have a *chelek l'olam haba* — so there is a sense of building toward that discussion. Beyond that, the section on *rotzeach* had already broadened the discussion beyond formal *misos beis din* to include the *ba b'machteret*, the *rodef*, people executed under special circumstances despite lack of formal proof, those who received repeated *malkus*, and *kana'im pogin bo*. There was even a mention of *misah bidei shamayim*. So it appears that once the *gemara* was already ranging into punishments that are not technically *misos beis din*, a decision was made to include the discussion of *olam haba* as a natural extension. It remains a somewhat puzzling editorial choice, but structurally *Chelek* is evidently the penultimate *perek*, not the final one.

### Rambam vs Gemara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּנְתַתֶּם מִמֶּנּוּ [אֶת] תְּרוּמַת ה׳ לְאַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן״. וְכִי אַהֲרֹן לְעוֹלָם קַיָּים? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נִכְנַס לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין לוֹ תְּרוּמָה! אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָתִיד לִחְיוֹת, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹתְנִין לוֹ תְּרוּמָה. מִכָּאן לִתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From where** is the **resurrection of the dead** derived **from the Torah?** It is derived from this verse, **as it is stated** with regard to *teruma* of the tithe: **"And you shall give the *teruma* of the Lord to Aaron the priest"** (Numbers 18:28). **And does Aaron exist forever** so that one can fulfill the mitzva by giving him the *teruma* of the tithe? **But is it not** so that Aaron **did not enter Eretz Yisrael,** the only place **where** the people would **give him *teruma*? Rather,** the verse **teaches that** Aaron is destined **to live in the future and the Jewish people** will **give him *teruma*. From here** it is derived that **the resurrection of the dead is from the Torah.**

The major theme of this *perek* is *olam haba* — who receives a portion in *olam haba* — and it is important to clarify at the outset precisely what the *gemara* means when it uses that term.

Commonly, we assume *olam haba* refers to the world of souls, the realm to which the soul proceeds after death — not a future world that does not yet exist, but a parallel world of souls that already exists, subjectively "the next world" for each individual. That is the Rambam's conception of *olam haba*, and it is emphatically not the *peshat* of this *gemara* or of this *perek* as a whole.

The evidence is immediate: the *perek* opens by identifying among those who have no *chelek* in *olam haba* the *kofer be-techiyat hameitim*, one who denies resurrection of the dead. The *gemara* then asks: why should denying *techiyat hameitim* cost one a portion in *olam haba*? And it answers: one who denies *techiyat hameitim* will not receive it. As the Ritva observes, we were speaking of *olam haba* — so what has the denial of resurrection to do with *olam haba*? The answer embedded in the *gemara* is that *techiyat hameitim* is understood as the necessary precursor to *olam haba*. Deny it, and you forfeit *olam haba* as well.

The *peshat* of the *gemara*, then — and this is explicit throughout the *mishna'yot* and the *gemara* — is that *olam haba* refers to the world that will follow this world, not subjectively but objectively, for all of humanity, in time. After this-worldly history reaches its conclusion, *mashiach* will come, the dead will rise, and a qualitatively different world will be created. The *gemara* even records a tradition that for a period of one thousand years God will wipe the slate entirely clean before creating a new world.

### Eschatological Terms

It is worth mapping the principal terms that appear in the *gemara* and how they relate to one another. The major categories are: *gan eden* and *gehennom*, *mashiach*, *techiyat hameitim*, and *olam haba*.

According to most *rishonim*, reading the *gemara* in its plain sense — beginning with the Ra'avad, the Ramban, the Ritva, and others — the sequence is: *mashiach* arrives, *techiyat hameitim* follows, and then *olam haba* begins. A central question among those *rishonim* is what the nature of *olam haba* will be. The *gemara* states that in *olam haba* there is no eating or drinking, but the *tzaddikim* sit with their crowns and derive benefit from the radiance of the *shekhinah*. Does this mean purely disembodied souls? Or, since *techiyat hameitim* precedes *olam haba*, will existence be embodied? Many *rishonim* hold that bodies will exist but will be elevated to such a spiritual level that they will require no physical sustenance — analogous to *Moshe* on *Har Sinai*, sustained entirely by proximity to the divine, yet still embodied.

### Torah Rewards Problem

One further dimension of this *sugya* requires mention: the relationship between the this-worldly rewards described in the *Torah* and the otherworldly framework the *gemara* develops. The *Torah* itself is strikingly this-worldly in its promise of reward and punishment — *venasati gishmeichem be-ito* and the entire system of *blessings* and *klalos* in the *parshiyot* are all couched in terms of rain, prosperity, and national flourishing. The problem of *tzadik v'ra lo* and *rasha v'tov lo* became increasingly acute, particularly in periods of persecution and martyrdom, when this-worldly reward seemed manifestly absent.

The Ramban's approach is that the *pesukim* of reward and punishment are addressed at the national level — when all of *Klal Yisrael* collectively observes the *mitzvos*, the national framework functions. The Rambam takes a different approach: the *blessings* described in the *Torah* are not the ultimate reward for *mitzvos* but rather instrumental — if one performs *mitzvos*, God provides the material conditions that make continued performance easier; if one sins, God allows adversity to multiply, making further observance more difficult. The *brachot* and *klalos* are thus a divine pedagogical mechanism, not an ultimate ledger of reward.

Either way, neither Rambam nor Ramban treats the this-worldly *pesukim* as the final accounting for *mitzvos* and *aveiros*. The shift to an otherworldly reckoning is where the *gemara*'s emphasis lies.

### Rambam & False Messiahs

The Rambam's conception of *mashiach* is also quite distinctive and deserves attention. The Rambam rules in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel that *ein bein olam hazeh limot hamashiach* — the only difference between the present era and the messianic period is whether Israel is subject to foreign domination. The Rambam in *Mishneh Torah* cites this position as if it were the consensus voice of the Sages — *amru chachamim ein bein olam hazeh limot hamashiach* — whereas in the *gemara* itself Shmuel's position reads more like a minority opinion. One of the Rambam's *nosei kelim* pointedly notes: *Shmuel amrah* — it is Shmuel who said this, not the *chachamim* collectively.

The broader point is that the Rambam systematically de-miraculizes the messianic era, seeking to redirect attention away from speculation about future wonders and toward present Torah observance and *mitzvos*. The Rambam was particularly impatient with the kinds of questions the *gemara* itself raises — will the resurrected wear clothes or not? Will they be the same age they were at death? If a man was married twice, which wife will he be reunited with? — dismissing such speculation as idle.

David Berger observed decades ago, however, that the Rambam's de-miraculization of messianic times produced a paradoxical effect: by establishing that the messianic era need not involve obvious miracles, the Rambam inadvertently furnished intellectual cover for claimants to messianic status. Three movements or figures illustrate this. First, Shabbetai Zvi — or more precisely, his prophet Natan — explicitly cited the Rambam to argue that the absence of miracles posed no obstacle to the messianic claim. Second, religious Zionism and the concept of *atchalta de'geulah* — that sovereignty without miracles can be read as the beginning of redemption — similarly draws on this framework. Third, those associated with the Lubavitcher Rebbe likewise cite the Rambam extensively in contexts that de-emphasize the miraculous as a criterion.

The irony Berger identifies is precise: an argument designed to suppress messianic speculation and focus Jewish attention on this world wound up lowering the bar for messianic claims.

## Proofs for Resurrection

### Romans & Deut 31:16

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ רוֹמִיִּים אֶת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה: מִנַּיִין שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְחַיֵּה מֵתִים, וְיוֹדֵעַ מַה שֶּׁעָתִיד לִהְיוֹת? אֲמַר לְהוּ: תַּרְוַויְיהוּ מִן הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה הִנְּךָ שֹׁכֵב עִם אֲבֹתֶיךָ וְקָם הָעָם הַזֶּה וְזָנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Romans asked Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, revives the dead, and** from where is it derived that **He knows what is destined to be?** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to them: Both of those** matters are derived **from this verse, as it is stated: "And the Lord said to Moses, Behold, you shall lie with your fathers and arise; this people will go astray"** (Deuteronomy 31:16). This indicates that Moses will die and then arise from the dead and that the Holy One, Blessed be He, knows what the children of Israel are destined to do.

The *gemara* records that Romans posed a question to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya: from where do we know that the Holy One resurrects the dead and that He knows the future? Rabbi Yehoshua replied that both can be derived from a single *pasuk*: *vayomer Hashem el Moshe hinecha shochev im avotecha ve-kam* — "And God said to Moshe, you will lie down with your fathers and you will rise up" (Devarim 31:16). The verse thus contains both a prediction of the future and an indication that *Moshe* will arise — hence *techiyat hameitim* and divine foreknowledge together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא ״וְקָם הָעָם הַזֶּה וְזָנָה״? אֲמַר לְהוּ: נְקוּטוּ מִיהָא פַּלְגָא בִּידַיְיכוּ, דְּיוֹדֵעַ מַה שֶּׁעָתִיד לִהְיוֹת. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: מִנַּיִין שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְחַיֵּה מֵתִים וְיוֹדֵעַ מַה שֶּׁעָתִיד לִהְיוֹת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הִנְּךָ שֹׁכֵב עִם אֲבֹתֶיךָ וְקָם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Romans asked: **But perhaps** the verse should be divided in a different manner, and it should be read: "Behold, you shall lie with your fathers **and this people will arise and go astray** after the foreign gods of the land." Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to them: Take at least** a response to **half** of your question **in your hands** from that verse, **that** God **knows what is destined to be.** The Gemara comments: **It was also stated** on a similar note by an *amora* citing a *tanna*, as **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, revives the dead, and** from where is it derived that **He knows what is destined to be?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "Behold, you shall lie with your fathers and arise."**

The Romans countered: perhaps *ve-kam* does not refer to *Moshe* rising, but to *ha-am hazeh ve-zanah* — "this people will rise and go astray." Rabbi Yehoshua conceded the point on *techiyat hameitim* but replied: *nekutu miha palga bi-yadeichu* — at least you have half of the proof in your hands. Even read your way, God predicts what the people will do in the future, establishing divine foreknowledge, *yodea mah she-atid lihyot*. It is noted that this seems almost superfluous given the breadth of prophetic prediction throughout *Nevi'im*, but the *gemara* cites it nevertheless. Rabbi Yochanan similarly transmitted this tradition in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai.

### R. Yochanan's Version

The *gemara* adds that this teaching was also transmitted independently: Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai that the same *pasuk* — *hinecha shochev im avotecha ve-kam* — serves as the source for both the resurrection of the dead and divine foreknowledge of the future.

### Minim & Forgery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בְּדָבָר זֶה זִיַּיפְתִּי סִפְרֵי כוּתִים, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים אֵין תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אָמַרְתִּי לָהֶן: זִיַּיפְתֶּם תּוֹרַתְכֶם וְלֹא הֶעֱלִיתֶם בְּיֶדְכֶם כְּלוּם, שֶׁאַתֶּם אוֹמְרִים אֵין תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה. הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַהִיא עֲוֹנָהּ בָּהּ״. ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״עֲוֹנָהּ בָּהּ״ – לְאֵימַת? לָאו לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, says: With this** following **matter, I refuted the books of the Samaritans, as they would say** that **there is no** source for **the resurrection of the dead from the Torah. I said to them: You falsified your torah and you accomplished nothing, as you say there is no** source for **the resurrection of the dead from the Torah,** and the Torah **states: "That soul shall be excised; his iniquity shall be upon him"** (Numbers 15:31). You interpret the phrase "that soul **shall be excised"** to mean that a sinner will be punished with death **in this world.** If so, with regard to the phrase **"his iniquity shall be upon him," for when** is that destined to be? Is it **not for the World-to-Come,** i.e., the world as it will exist after the resurrection of the dead? Apparently, there is a World-to-Come and there is an allusion to it in the Torah.

A *baraita* records that Rabbi Eliezer the son of Rabbi Yosi declared: with this very matter I exposed the books of the *minim* as fraudulent. The *minim* in this context were asserting that *techiyat hameitim* has no basis in the *Torah* — a claim that makes them an unusual fit for the typical identification of *minim* with Jewish Christians, who generally accepted resurrection. It appears more likely that these are Jewish sectarians of another variety — possibly *tzadokim*, or, as one authority suggests, *kutim* (Samaritans). The Samaritan identification is intriguing because the *gemara*'s language of forged or corrupted scripture fits naturally in a context where two groups each claim the authentic text of the *Torah*: Rabbi Eliezer says *ziyaftem toratchem* — you have corrupted your *Torah* — and since the Samaritans maintained their own version of the *Torah*, the charge of textual corruption lands with particular force. Whether the original text read *minim* or *kutim* would require a manuscript investigation, but the substantive point is that some form of Jewish sectarians rejected *techiyat hameitim min haTorah*, and it is against them that the following arguments are marshaled.

Rabbi Eliezer's proof comes from the *pasuk*: *hikaret tikaret hanefesh hahi avonah bah* (Bamidbar 15:31). The verse uses a doubled verb — *hikaret tikaret* — and concludes with *avonah bah*, "its sin remains upon it." He reads *hikaret* as referring to this world and *tikaret* as referring to *olam haba*, while *avonah bah* — "its sin remains with it" — implies there must be a future period in which the soul still carries its sin, i.e., a future reckoning. Since *karet* in *olam haba* implies the existence of *olam haba*, and since *olam haba* in the *gemara*'s understanding presupposes *techiyat hameitim*, the *pasuk* implicitly affirms resurrection.

### Karet Exegesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְלֵימָא לְהוּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מֵ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״! אִינְהוּ הֲווֹ אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: And let** Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, **say to** the Samaritans that **both of those** matters can be derived **from** the phrase **"shall be excised [*hikkaret tikkaret*]."** "*Hikkaret*" indicates that the sinner is excised from this world, and "*tikkaret*" indicates that the sinner is excised from the World-to-Come. Abaye answered: Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, preferred not to cite proof from the compound verb, because the Samaritans **would say: The Torah spoke in the language of people,** and the compound verb is merely a stylistic flourish.

Rav Papa raised a challenge to Abaye: why not derive both *techiyat hameitim* and *olam haba* directly from the doubled verb *hikaret tikaret* alone, without requiring *avonah bah*? Abaye responds that the *minim* would not have been persuaded by double language — they would have invoked the principle of *dibrah Torah kilshon bnei adam*, that the *Torah* sometimes uses emphatic doubling without intending two separate referents. Hence *avonah bah* is needed as an additional pointer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״ – ״הִכָּרֵת״ בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״תִּכָּרֵת״ לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״אֶת ה׳ הוּא מְגַדֵּף וְנִכְרְתָה״, וְכִי שְׁלֹשָׁה עוֹלָמִים יֵשׁ? אֶלָּא ״וְנִכְרְתָה״ בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״הִכָּרֵת״ לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״ – דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כִלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: These derivations of Rabbi Eliezer and Rav Pappa are **parallel** to a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to **"*hikkaret tikkaret*,"** as follows: **"*Hikkaret*"** indicates that the sinner is excised **in this world,** and **"*tikkaret*"** indicates that the sinner is excised **in the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yishmael said to him: Isn't it already stated** in the previous verse: **"That person that blasphemes the Lord, that soul shall be excised [*venikhreta*]"** (Numbers 15:30), **and are there three worlds** from which the sinner is excised? **Rather,** from the term **"*venikhreta*"** it is derived that the sinner is excised **in this world,** from **"*hikkaret*"** it is derived that the sinner is excised **in the World-to-Come,** and from the compound verb **"*hikkaret tikkaret*"** nothing is derived, as **the Torah spoke in the language of people.**

A *baraita* then records a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael on this matter. Rabbi Akiva reads *hikaret tikaret* as referring to two worlds — *hikaret* for this world and *tikaret* for *olam haba* — in addition to the import of *avonah bah*. Rabbi Yishmael objects: there is another *pasuk* regarding *megadef* that also uses the expression *ve-nichretah* alongside a form of *karet* — yielding potentially three "worlds," which is impossible. He therefore reads *ve-nichretah ba-olam hazeh* and *hikaret l'olam haba* as covering the two worlds, while attributing the doubling simply to the stylistic principle of *dibrah Torah kilshon bnei adam*. Both authorities, however, agree that one *pasuk* addresses this world and the other addresses *olam haba*. The question of what each derives from *avonah bah* is then resolved by appeal to a separate *baraita*.

## Disputes w/ Rulers

### Cleopatra Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלָה קְלֵיאוֹפַּטְרָא מַלְכְּתָא אֶת רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמְרָה: יָדַעְנָא דְּחָיִי שָׁכְבֵי, דִּכְתִיב ״וְיָצִיצוּ מֵעִיר כְּעֵשֶׂב הָאָרֶץ״. אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהֵן עוֹמְדִין, עוֹמְדִין עֲרוּמִין אוֹ בִּלְבוּשֵׁיהֶן עוֹמְדִין? אֲמַר לַהּ: קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵחִיטָּה. וּמָה חִיטָּה שֶׁנִּקְבְּרָה עֲרוּמָּה יוֹצְאָה בְּכַמָּה לְבוּשִׁין, צַדִּיקִים שֶׁנִּקְבָּרִים בִּלְבוּשֵׁיהֶן – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Queen Cleopatra asked Rabbi Meir** a question. **She said: I know that the dead will live, as it is written: "And may they blossom out of the city like grass of the earth"** (Psalms 72:16). Just as grass grows, so too, the dead will come to life. **But when they arise,** will they **arise naked or** will **they arise with their garments?** Rabbi Meir **said to her:** It is derived ***a fortiori* from wheat. If wheat, which is buried naked,** meaning that the kernel is sown without the chaff, **emerges with several garments** of chaff, **all the more so** will **the righteous, who are buried with their garments,** arise with their garments.

The *gemara* records that Cleopatra the queen asked Rabbi Meir a question — though chronologically the dates present a difficulty, since Cleopatra died in 30 BCE while Rabbi Meir flourished around 150 CE. She stated that she already knew the dead would come to life — citing the verse *ve-yatzitzu me-ir ke-esev ha-aretz*, "they will sprout from the city like the grass of the earth" — but her question was practical: when they rise, will they be naked or clothed?

### Grain Analogy

Rabbi Meir responded with a *kal va-chomer* from a grain of wheat: if a grain of wheat, which is buried naked, emerges covered in multiple layers — kernel, chaff, and all its various coverings — then the *tzaddikim*, who are buried in their garments, how much more so will they arise clothed. The *kal va-chomer* moves from the simple case of a grain buried without covering yet emerging with covering, to the more richly endowed case of the righteous who are already interred with garments.

### Caesar & Dust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: אָמְרִיתוּ דְּשָׁכְבֵי חָיִי. הָא הָווּ עַפְרָא, וְעַפְרָא מִי קָא חָיֵי?! אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ בְּרַתֵּיה: שִׁבְקֵיהּ, וַאֲנָא מַהְדַּרְנָא לֵיהּ. שְׁנֵי יוֹצְרִים יֵשׁ בְּעִירֵנוּ: אֶחָד יוֹצֵר מִן הַמַּיִם, וְאֶחָד יוֹצֵר מִן הַטִּיט. אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן מְשׁוּבָּח? אָמַר לָהּ: זֶה שֶׁיּוֹצֵר מִן הַמַּיִם. אָמְרָה לוֹ: מִן הַמַּיִם צָר, מִן הַטִּיט לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Roman **emperor said to Rabban Gamliel: You say that the dead will live. Aren't they dust? And does dust come to life?** **The daughter of** the emperor **said** to Rabban Gamliel: **Leave him, and I will respond to him** with a parable. She said: **There are two craftsmen in our city; one fashions** vessels **from water, and one fashions** vessels **from mortar. Which is more noteworthy?** The emperor **said to her:** It is **that** craftsman **that fashions** vessels **from water.** His daughter **said to him:** If **he fashions** a vessel **from the water, all the more so** is it **not** clear that he can fashion vessels **from mortar?** By the same token, if God was able to create the world from water, He is certainly able to resurrect people from dust.

A separate exchange involves Caesar and Rabban Gamliel. Caesar challenged: you say the dead will come to life — but by then they will be dust, and how can dust live? Rabban Gamliel's daughter intervened, telling her father to leave the response to her. She posed a rhetorical question to Caesar: in our city there are two potters — one fashions vessels from water alone, and one from clay. Which is greater? Caesar answered: the one who fashions from water. She replied: if God can fashion a human being from water — from semen — then from clay, which is far better building material, how much more so. The first human being was fashioned from earth, so if God could create life from the liquid medium of conception, the reconstitution of a body from existing clay poses no difficulty whatsoever.

### Glass Kal Va-Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִכְּלֵי זְכוּכִית, מָה כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית שֶׁעֲמָלָן בְּרוּחַ בָּשָׂר וָדָם - נִשְׁבְּרוּ יֵשׁ לָהֶן תַּקָּנָה, בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁבְּרוּחוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that resurrection of the dead ***a fortiori* from glass vessels: If** concerning **glass vessels, which are fashioned by the breath of** those of **flesh and blood,** who blow and form the vessels, and yet if **they break they can be repaired,** as they can be melted and subsequently blown again, then with regard to those of **flesh and blood, whose** souls are a product of **the breath of the Holy One, Blessed be He, all the more so** can God restore them to life.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught a parallel *kal va-chomer* from glass vessels: glass vessels, which are made merely by human breath, can be broken and then refashioned by melting and reblowing. Human beings, who were created by the breath of the Holy One — as the *parasha* we had read describes the divine *neshima* — how much more so can God reconstitute them after they decompose.

### Palace Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי: אָמְרִיתוּ דְּשָׁכְבֵי חָיִי. וְהָא הָווּ עַפְרָא, וְעַפְרָא מִי קָא חָיֵי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל. לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁאָמַר לַעֲבָדָיו: לְכוּ וּבְנוּ לִי פַּלְטֵרִין גְּדוֹלִים בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַיִם וְעָפָר. הָלְכוּ וּבָנוּ אוֹתָן. לְיָמִים נָפְלוּ. אָמַר לָהֶם: חִזְרוּ וּבְנוּ אוֹתָן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָפָר וָמַיִם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין אָנוּ יְכוֹלִין. כָּעַס עֲלֵיהֶם, וְאָמַר לָהֶן: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַיִם וְעָפָר בְּנִיתֶם, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַיִם וְעָפָר – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a certain heretic said to Rabbi Ami: You say that the dead will live. Aren't they dust? And does dust come to life?** Rabbi Ami **said to him: I will tell you a parable. To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a flesh-and-blood king who said to his servants: Go and construct for me a great palace [*palterin*] in a place where there is no water and earth** available. **They went and constructed it. Sometime** later, the palace **collapsed.** The king **said to them: Return** to your labor **and construct** the palace **in a place where there is earth and water** available. **They said to him: We are unable** to do so. The king **became angry at them and said to them:** If **in a place where there is no water and earth** available **you constructed** a palace, **now that there is water and earth** available **all the more so** should you be able to do so. Similarly, concerning man, whom God created *ex nihilo*, all the more so will God be able to resurrect him from dust.

A *min* posed a similar challenge to Rabbi Ami: you claim the dead will live, but they become dust — how can dust come back to life? Rabbi Ami responded with a parable: a mortal king commanded his servants to go and build him a great palace in a place where there was no water and no dirt. They did so. It fell. He said to them: go and rebuild it in a place where there is water and dirt. They declared: impossible. The king grew angry and said: if you could build it in a place with no materials at all, how much more so can you rebuild it where you have water and dirt. The parable maps directly onto resurrection: God created human life originally from essentially nothing — *ex nihilo* — so when God now has actual material to work with, reconstituting a body from existing dust is, if anything, easier.

### Mouse & Squirrel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִי אַתָּה מַאֲמִין, צֵא לַבִּקְעָה וּרְאֵה עַכְבָּר שֶׁהַיּוֹם חֶצְיוֹ בָּשָׂר וְחֶצְיוֹ אֲדָמָה, לְמָחָר הִשְׁרִץ וְנַעֲשָׂה כֻּלּוֹ בָּשָׂר. שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה? עֲלֵה לָהָר וּרְאֵה שֶׁהַיּוֹם אֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא חִלָּזוֹן אֶחָד, לְמָחָר יָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים וְנִתְמַלֵּא כּוּלּוֹ חֶלְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you do not believe** that a being can be created from dust, **go out to the valley and see an *akhbar*,** a creature **that today is half flesh and half earth,** and **tomorrow** the being will **develop and all of it will become flesh. Lest you say** that creation of living creatures is a matter that develops **over an extended period, ascend a mountain and see that today there is only one snail there;** then ascend **tomorrow,** after **rain** will **have fallen, and** see that **it** will be **entirely filled with snails.**

To further reinforce the point, Rabbi Ami adds: if you do not believe me, go to the valley and observe the *achbar* — what appears to be a squirrel or field mouse — which today is half flesh and half earth, and by tomorrow will be entirely flesh, having emerged from the soil. Here in this present world we already observe life generated from earth, without a miracle. The objection that such a process takes a long time rather than happening instantaneously is then addressed by the next example.

### Snail Evidence

To demonstrate that transformation can occur rapidly, the *gemara* points to the *chalazon*: if you ascend a mountain where there is but one snail, and rain falls overnight, by morning the mountain will be filled with snails. Rashi explains this by noting that the eggs of the snails all hatch simultaneously — so the operative phenomenon is that eggs hatch overnight. The argument being made is that God can bring life into being swiftly, within a single night, demonstrating that *techiyat hameitim* need not require an extended gradual process. There is a note that the Rashi on this passage has been questioned as perhaps not being the authentic Rashi text — this requires further investigation.

### Ex Nihilo Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לִגְבִיהָא בֶּן פְּסִיסָא: וַוי לְכוֹן חַיָּיבַיָּא, דְּאָמְרִיתוּן מִיתֵי חָיִין! דְּחָיִין מָיְתִי, דְּמִיתֵי חָיִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וַוי לְכוֹן חַיָּיבַיָּא, דְּאָמְרִיתוּן מִיתֵי לָא חָיִין! דְּלָא הֲווֹ חָיִי, דַּהֲווֹ חַיֵּי – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַיָּיבַיָּא קָרֵית לִי? אִי קָאֵימְנָא, בָּעֵיטְנָא בָּךְ וּפָשֵׁיטְנָא לְעַקְמוּתָךְ מִינָּךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן, רוֹפֵא אוּמָּן תִּקָּרֵא, וְשָׂכָר הַרְבֵּה תִּטּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a certain heretic said to Geviha ben Pesisa: Woe unto you, the wicked, as you say: The dead will come to life.** The way of the world is that **those** who are **alive die.** How can you say **that the dead will come to life?** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to him: Woe unto you, the wicked, as you say: The dead will not come to life.** If **those who were not** in existence **come to life, is it not** reasonable **all the more so** that **those who were** once **alive** will come to life again? The heretic **said to** Geviha ben Pesisa angrily: **You called me wicked? If I stand, I will kick you and flatten your hump,** as Geviha ben Pesisa was a hunchback. Geviha ben Pesisa **said to him** jocularly: **If you do so, you will be called an expert doctor and will take high wages** for your services.

The section concludes with an exchange involving Gviha ben Psisa — apparently a hunchback — and a *min*. The *min* says: woe to you sinners who claim the dead will come to life — if the living die, how will the dead reverse entropy and return to life? Gviha ben Psisa replied in kind: woe to you sinners who say the dead will not come to life — if one who never existed can come to life (as every new birth demonstrates, life being drawn from essentially nothing), then one who once already lived — *kal va-chomer* — how much more so can that person return to life. The *min* responded angrily — you call me a sinner? — and threatened to kick Gviha so hard as to straighten his hunchback. Gviha replied calmly that if he could do that, he would be a master physician and would deserve a generous fee.

## Alexander Disputes

### African Claim Refute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה בְּנִיסָן אִיתְנְטִילוּ דֵּימוֹסְנָאֵי מִיהוּדָה וּמִירוּשָׁלַיִם. כְּשֶׁבָּאוּ בְּנֵי אַפְרִיקִיָּא לָדוּן עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן שֶׁלָּנוּ הִיא, דִּכְתִיב ״אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן לִגְבֻלֹתֶיהָ״, וּכְנַעַן אֲבוּהוֹן דְּהָנְהוּ אִינָשֵׁי הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Geviha ben Pesisa and his cleverness in debate, the Gemara cites additional incidents where he represented the Jewish people in debates. **The Sages taught** in *Megillat Ta'anit*: **On the twenty-fourth** day **in Nisan** it is a joyous day, since the **usurpers [*dimusana'ei*] were expelled from Judea and Jerusalem. When the people of Afrikiya came to judgment with the Jewish people before** the emperor, **Alexander of Macedon, they said to him: The land of Canaan is ours, as it is written:** "This is the land that shall fall to you as an inheritance, **the land of Canaan according to its borders"** (Numbers 34:2). **And** the people of Afrikiya said, referring to themselves: **Canaan is the forefather of these people.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a series of disputes adjudicated before Alexander the Great, connected to the narrative by the figure of Gviha ben Psisa. A *baraita* from *Megillat Taanit* records that on the 24th of Nissan certain legal attacks against Judah and Jerusalem were dismissed. The *gemara* proceeds to describe the cases.

The people of Africa (*bnei Afrikaya*) came before Alexander to dispute with Israel. They claimed: the land of Canaan is ours — it is written *Eretz Knaan*, the land of Canaan, and Canaan was our ancestor. Gviha ben Psisa asked the Rabbis to grant him permission to go and dispute on their behalf before Alexander. His framing was tactically shrewd: if they defeat him, simply say he was a commoner and that the sages have not yet spoken; if he wins, attribute the victory not to himself but to the *Torat Moshe*, which prevailed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ גְּבִיהָא בֶּן פְּסִיסָא לַחֲכָמִים: תְּנוּ לִי רְשׁוּת וְאֵלֵךְ וְאָדוּן עִמָּהֶן לִפְנֵי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן. אִם יְנַצְּחוּנִי, אִמְרוּ: הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁבָּנוּ נִצַּחְתֶּם, וְאִם אֲנִי אֲנַצֵּחַ אוֹתָם, אִמְרוּ לָהֶם: תּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה נִצְּחַתְכֶם. נָתְנוּ לוֹ רְשׁוּת, וְהָלַךְ וְדָן עִמָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Geviha ben Pesisa said to the Sages: Give me permission and I will go and deliberate with them before Alexander of Macedon. If they will defeat me, say** to them: **You have defeated an ordinary** person **from among us,** and until you overcome our Sages, it is no victory. **And if I will defeat them, say to them: The Torah of Moses defeated you,** and attribute no significance to me. The Sages **gave him permission, and he went and deliberated with them.**

Permission granted, Gviha asked the Africans for their proof. They cited the *Torah*'s designation of the land as *Eretz Knaan*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: מֵהֵיכָן אַתֶּם מְבִיאִים רְאָיָיה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָבִיא לָכֶם רְאָיָיה אֶלָּא מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אָרוּר כְּנָעַן עֶבֶד עֲבָדִים יִהְיֶה לְאֶחָיו״. עֶבֶד שֶׁקָּנָה נְכָסִים – עֶבֶד לְמִי וּנְכָסִים לְמִי? וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁהֲרֵי כַּמָּה שָׁנִים שֶׁלֹּא עֲבַדְתּוּנוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to them: From where are you citing proof** that the land of Canaan is yours? **They said to him: From the Torah.** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to them: I too will cite proof to you only from the Torah, as it is stated: "And he said: Cursed will be Canaan; a slave of slaves shall he be to his brethren"** (Genesis 9:25). And with regard to **a slave who acquired property,** the **slave** belongs **to whom** and the **property** belongs **to whom?** The slave and his property belong to the master. **And moreover,** it is **several years** now **that you have not served us.** Therefore, not only are you not entitled to the land, there are additional debts that must be repaid, as well as a return to enslavement.

He responded: I will also bring proof from the *Torah*. It is written, *vayomer arur Canaan eved avadim yihyeh l'echav* — "Cursed is Canaan, he shall be a slave of slaves to his brothers." The principle is straightforward: a slave who acquires property — to whom does the slave belong, and to whom do the slave's acquisitions belong? To the master. Since Canaan was declared a slave, whatever land he possessed belongs to his masters. Furthermore, there are many years of unpaid service owed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מַלְכָּא: הַחְזִירוּ לוֹ תְּשׁוּבָה! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: תְּנוּ לָנוּ זְמַן שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים. נָתַן לָהֶם זְמַן. בָּדְקוּ וְלֹא מָצְאוּ תְּשׁוּבָה. מִיָּד בָּרְחוּ, וְהִנִּיחוּ שְׂדוֹתֵיהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן זְרוּעוֹת, וְכַרְמֵיהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן נְטוּעוֹת. וְאוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alexander the king said to** the people of Afrikiya: **Provide** Geviha ben Pesisa with **a response** to his claims. **They said to** Alexander: **Give us time;** give us **three days** to consider the matter. The emperor **gave them** the requested **time** and **they examined** the matter **and did not find a response** to the claims. **Immediately, they fled and abandoned their fields when they** were **sown and their vineyards when they** were **planted.** The Gemara adds: **And** since **that year was a Sabbatical** Year, with the accompanying restrictions on agricultural activity, this benefited the Jewish people, as they were able to consume the produce of those fields and vineyards.

Alexander turned to the Africans: give him an answer. They requested three days. He granted the time. They found no response. They immediately fled, leaving behind fields that were planted and vineyards that were tended — which the Jews then came into possession of. The *gemara* adds that it was a *shemitah* year. The *Maharsha* clarifies that this does not mean the Africans abandoned their landholdings in Africa — they abandoned the lands they had been claiming in *Eretz Yisrael*, and since it was *shemitah*, the windfall came at a propitious moment.

### Egyptian Reparations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוּב פַּעַם אַחַת בָּאוּ בְּנֵי מִצְרַיִם לָדוּן עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וַה׳ נָתַן אֶת חֵן הָעָם בְּעֵינֵי מִצְרַיִם וַיַּשְׁאִלוּם״. תְּנוּ לָנוּ כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁנְּטַלְתֶּם מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: On **another occasion, the people of Egypt came to judgment with the Jewish people before Alexander of Macedon.** The Egyptian people **said to** Alexander: **It says** in the Torah: **"And the Lord gave the people favor in the eyes of Egypt, and they lent them"** (Exodus 12:36). **Give us the silver and gold that you took from us;** you claimed that you were borrowing it and you never returned it.

The Egyptians came next before Alexander, invoking the *pasuk*: *va-Hashem natan et chen ha-am hazeh b'einei Mitzrayim va-yashi'ilum* — God gave the people favor in Egyptian eyes and they lent them silver and gold. The Egyptians demanded: you borrowed from us, now return the silver and gold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר גְּבִיהָא בֶּן פְּסִיסָא לַחֲכָמִים: תְּנוּ לִי רְשׁוּת וְאֵלֵךְ וְאָדוּן עִמָּהֶן לִפְנֵי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס. אִם יְנַצְּחוּנִי, אִמְרוּ לָהֶם: הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁבָּנוּ נִצַּחְתֶּם, וְאִם אֲנִי אֲנַצֵּחַ אוֹתָם, אִמְרוּ לָהֶם: תּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ נִצְּחַתְכֶם. נָתְנוּ לוֹ רְשׁוּת וְהָלַךְ וְדָן עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Geviha ben Pesisa said to the Sages: Give me permission and I will go and deliberate with them before Alexander** of Macedon. **If they will defeat me, say** to them: **You have defeated an ordinary** person **from among us,** and until you overcome our Sages, it is no victory. **And if I will defeat them, say to them: The Torah of Moses, our teacher, defeated you,** and attribute no significance to me. The Sages **gave him permission, and he went and deliberated with them.**

Gviha ben Psisa again sought and received permission from the Rabbis to dispute on their behalf, with the same framing as before. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: מֵהֵיכָן אַתֶּם מְבִיאִין רְאָיָיה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָבִיא לָכֶם רְאָיָיה אֶלָּא מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמוֹשַׁב בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יָשְׁבוּ בְּמִצְרָיִם שְׁלֹשִׁים שָׁנָה וְאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה״. תְּנוּ לָנוּ שְׂכַר עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁל שִׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא שֶׁשִּׁיעְבַּדְתֶּם בְּמִצְרַיִם שְׁלֹשִׁים שָׁנָה וְאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to them: From where are you citing proof** that you are entitled to the silver and gold? **They said to him: From the Torah.** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to them: I too will cite proof to you only from the Torah, as it is stated: "And the sojourning of the children of Israel, who dwelt in Egypt, was four hundred and thirty years"** (Exodus 12:40), during which they were enslaved to Egypt, engaged in hard manual labor. **Give us the wages for the work** performed **by** the **600,000** men above the age of twenty (see Exodus 12:37) **whom you enslaved in Egypt** for **four hundred and thirty years.**

He asked the Egyptians for their source; they cited the *Torah*. He replied: I too will bring proof only from the *Torah*. The verse states, *umoshav bnei Yisrael asher yashvu b'Mitzrayim shloshim shana v'arba me'ot shana* — the Jewish people dwelt in Egypt for 430 years. He therefore demanded: give us back wages for 600,000 men enslaved over 430 years. He did not even compute the interest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן: הַחְזִירוּ לוֹ תְּשׁוּבָה! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: תְּנוּ לָנוּ זְמַן שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים. נָתַן לָהֶם זְמַן. בָּדְקוּ וְלֹא מָצְאוּ תְּשׁוּבָה. מִיָּד הִנִּיחוּ שְׂדוֹתֵיהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן זְרוּעוֹת, וְכַרְמֵיהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן נְטוּעוֹת, וּבָרְחוּ. וְאוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה שְׁבִיעִית הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alexander of Macedon said to** the people of Egypt: **Provide** Geviha ben Pesisa with **a response** to his claims. **They said to him: Give us time;** give us **three days** to consider the matter. The emperor **gave them** the requested **time** and **they examined** the matter **and did not find a response** to the claims. **Immediately, they abandoned their fields when they** were **sown and their vineyards when they** were **planted, and fled.** The Gemara adds: **And that year was a Sabbatical** Year.

Alexander turned to the Egyptians: give him an answer. They requested three days. He gave them the time. They found no answer, abandoned their planted fields and vineyards, and fled. Again it was a *shemitah* year, and the Jews benefited from the windfall.

### Ishmaelite Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוּב פַּעַם אַחַת בָּאוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וּבְנֵי קְטוּרָה לָדוּן עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן שֶׁלָּנוּ וְשֶׁלָּכֶם, דִּכְתִיב ״וְאֵלֶּה תֹּלְדֹת יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֶּן אַבְרָהָם״ וּכְתִיב ״אֵלֶּה תּוֹלְדֹת יִצְחָק בֶּן אַבְרָהָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **And** on **another occasion, the descendants of Ishmael and the descendants of Keturah came to judgment with the Jewish people before Alexander of Macedon. They said to** the Jewish people before Alexander: **The land of Canaan is** both **ours and yours, as it is written: "And these are the generations of Ishmael, son of Abraham,** whom Hagar the Egyptian, Sarah's maidservant, bore unto Abraham" (Genesis 25:12), **and it is written: "And these are the generations of Isaac, son of Abraham"** (Genesis 25:19). Therefore, the land should be divided between Abraham's heirs.

The descendants of Yishmael and the children of Ketura came before Alexander with a more moderate claim: the land of Canaan is ours *and* yours — let us partition it. Their proof: it is written *ele toldot Yishmael ben Avraham* and *ele toldot Yitzchak ben Avraham* — both Yishmael and Yitzchak descend from Avraham, so both have a claim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן גְּבִיהָא בֶּן פְּסִיסָא לַחֲכָמִים: תְּנוּ לִי רְשׁוּת וְאֵלֵךְ וְאָדוּן עִמָּהֶם לִפְנֵי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן. אִם יְנַצְּחוּנִי אִמְרוּ: הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁבָּנוּ נִצַּחְתֶּם, וְאִם אֲנִי אֲנַצֵּחַ אוֹתָם אִמְרוּ לָהֶם: תּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ נִצְּחַתְכֶם. נָתְנוּ לוֹ רְשׁוּת, הָלַךְ וְדָן עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Geviha ben Pesisa said to the Sages: Give me permission and I will go and deliberate with them before Alexander of Macedon. If they will defeat me, say** to them: **You have defeated an ordinary** person **from among us,** and until you overcome our Sages, it is no victory. **And if I will defeat them, say to them: The Torah of Moses, our teacher, defeated you,** and attribute no significance to me. The Sages **gave him permission, and he went and deliberated with them.**

Gviha ben Psisa again came forward with the same procedural framing and received permission. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: מֵהֵיכָן אַתֶּם מְבִיאִין רְאָיָיה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָבִיא רְאָיָיה אֶלָּא מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּתֵּן אַבְרָהָם אֶת כׇּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ לְיִצְחָק. וְלִבְנֵי הַפִּילַגְשִׁים אֲשֶׁר לְאַבְרָהָם נָתַן אַבְרָהָם מַתָּנֹת״. אָב שֶׁנָּתַן אֲגִטִּין לְבָנָיו בְּחַיָּיו וְשִׁיגֵּר זֶה מֵעַל זֶה, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ לָזֶה עַל זֶה כְּלוּם? מַאי מַתָּנוֹת? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמָּסַר לָהֶם שֵׁם טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to** the descendants of Ishmael: **From where are you citing proof** that the land of Canaan belongs to both you and the Jewish people? **They said to him: From the Torah.** Geviha ben Pesisa **said to them: I too will cite proof to you only from the Torah, as it is stated: "And Abraham gave all that he had to Isaac. But to the sons of the concubines that Abraham had, Abraham gave gifts,** and he sent them away from his son, while he yet lived, eastward, to the east country" (Genesis 25:5–6). In the case of **a father who gave a document of bequest [*agatin*] to his sons during his lifetime and sent one** of the sons **away from the other, does** the one who was sent away **have any claim against the other?** The father himself divided his property. The Gemara asks: **What** were these **gifts** that Abraham gave to the sons of the concubines? **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says:** This **teaches that** Abraham **provided them** with the **name** of the supernatural spirit of **impurity,** enabling them to perform witchcraft.

He asked for their proof; they cited the *Torah*. He replied: I will also bring proof from the *Torah*. It is written, *vayiten Avraham et kol asher lo l'Yitzchak* — Avraham gave everything he had to Yitzchak. And it is further written, *v'livnei hapilagshim asher l'Avraham natan Avraham matanot* — to the sons of the concubines Avraham gave gifts, and while he was still alive he sent them away from his son. The legal principle: a father who gives a bequest (*agtin* — likely a Latin term for bequest) to his children during his lifetime and sends them away, thereby making clear that this portion is their entirety — does the other creditor (*ba'al chov*) retain any further claim? He does not. Avraham made it explicit that Yitzchak receives everything, and the sons of the concubines received their severance gifts and were dismissed.

The Ishmaelites and children of Ketura found no answer, abandoned their planted fields and vineyards, and fled. Again it was a *shemitah* year.

### Witchcraft Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַתָּנוֹת? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמָּסַר לָהֶם שֵׁם טוּמְאָה.

The *gemara* pauses to ask: what exactly were the *matanot* — the gifts — that Avraham gave the sons of the concubines? Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba taught: this teaches that he transmitted to them a *shem tum'ah* — names of impurity, i.e., the names and knowledge used for *kishuf*, for witchcraft. The *Maharsha* notes that this would accord with the view that *bnei Noach* were not forewarned against *kishuf*. It remains unclear why Avraham would specifically bequeath this knowledge to them, and the *gemara* does not elaborate further.

## Body-Soul Judgment

### Dualism Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַנְטוֹנִינוּס לְרַבִּי: גּוּף וּנְשָׁמָה יְכוֹלִין לִפְטוֹר עַצְמָן מִן הַדִּין. כֵּיצַד? גּוּף אוֹמֵר: נְשָׁמָה חָטָאת, שֶׁמִּיּוֹם שֶׁפֵּירְשָׁה מִמֶּנִּי הֲרֵינִי מוּטָּל כְּאֶבֶן דּוּמָם בַּקֶּבֶר. וּנְשָׁמָה אוֹמֶרֶת: גּוּף חָטָא, שֶׁמִּיּוֹם שֶׁפֵּירַשְׁתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ הֲרֵינִי פּוֹרַחַת בָּאֲוִיר כְּצִפּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos exchanges with prominent gentile leaders, the Gemara cites an exchange where **Antoninos,** the Roman emperor, **said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **The body and the soul are able to exempt themselves from judgment** for their sins. **How so? The body says: The soul sinned, as from the day** of my death **when it departed from me, I am cast like a silent stone in the grave,** and do not sin. **And the soul says: The body sinned, as from the day that I departed from it, I am flying in the air like a bird,** incapable of sin.

Antoninus posed to Rabbi a fundamental problem about divine justice in light of body-soul dualism: can the body and the soul each argue their way out of judgment separately? The body says: it was the soul that sinned — proof being that from the moment the soul departed, the body lies motionless as a stone in the grave, incapable of any act. The soul says: it was the body that sinned — proof being that from the moment it separated from the body, the soul hovers like a bird through the air, incapable of action on its own. Each party, taken alone, has a colorable defense.

### Dualism Philosophy

This *sugya* raises a broader philosophical point worth noting. The body-soul dualism so familiar to us — the idea that the soul is capable of autonomous existence apart from the body, and that the body is merely a vessel — is deeply Greek in origin. Biblical Judaism is considerably less committed to this framework. In fact, the requirement for body-soul dualism in the Jewish metaphysical picture arises precisely and almost exclusively from the need to account for the intermediate stage: the period between a person's death and the future *yom ha-din*. If one conceives of the soul as naturally continuous with the body, and resurrection as the moment of re-ensoulment, the intermediate stage still requires some framework for the soul's existence between death and resurrection — and that is where body-soul dualism becomes functionally necessary. Strip away the intermediate stage and the dualism is far less compelled. The *gemara* here engages with this intermediate state directly, as each party's argument presupposes a period of separate existence.

It is also worth noting that the question of what happens to the soul during this intermediate period — whether it hovers, enters *gan eden*, or undergoes some form of judgment — is addressed in various *gemarot*, though perhaps less extensively than one might expect given how central that question is to popular Jewish understanding of what happens after death.

### Lame & Blind Tale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל. לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ פַּרְדֵּס נָאֶה, וְהָיָה בּוֹ בַּכּוּרוֹת נָאוֹת, וְהוֹשִׁיב בּוֹ שְׁנֵי שׁוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד חִיגֵּר וְאֶחָד סוֹמֵא. אָמַר לוֹ חִיגֵּר לַסּוֹמֵא: בַּכּוּרוֹת נָאוֹת אֲנִי רוֹאֶה בַּפַּרְדֵּס. בֹּא וְהַרְכִּיבֵנִי וּנְבִיאֵם לְאׇכְלָם. רָכַב חִיגֵּר עַל גַּבֵּי סוֹמֵא, וֶהֱבִיאוּם וַאֲכָלוּם. לְיָמִים בָּא בַּעַל פַּרְדֵּס. אָמַר לָהֶן: בַּכּוּרוֹת נָאוֹת הֵיכָן הֵן? אָמַר לוֹ חִיגֵּר: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ לִי רַגְלַיִם לְהַלֵּךְ בָּהֶן? אָמַר לוֹ סוֹמֵא: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ לִי עֵינַיִם לִרְאוֹת? מָה עָשָׂה? הִרְכִּיב חִיגֵּר עַל גַּבֵּי סוֹמֵא, וְדָן אוֹתָם כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: I will tell you a parable. To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a king of flesh and blood who had a fine orchard, and in it there were** **fine first fruits** of a fig tree, **and he stationed two guards in** the orchard, **one lame,** who was unable to walk, **and one blind.** Neither was capable of reaching the fruit on the trees in the orchard without the assistance of the other. **The lame** person **said to the blind** person: **I see fine first fruits** of a fig tree **in the orchard; come and place me** upon your shoulders. I will guide you to the tree, **and we will bring** the figs **to eat them. The lame** person **rode upon** the shoulders of **the blind** person **and they brought** the figs **and ate them.** **Sometime** later **the owner of the orchard came** to the orchard. **He said to** the guards: **The fine first fruits** of a fig tree that were in the orchard, **where are they? The lame** person **said: Do I have any legs with which** I would be able **to walk** and take the figs? **The blind** person **said: Do I have any eyes** with which I would be able **to see** the way to the figs? **What did** the owner of the orchard **do? He placed the lame** person **upon** the shoulders of **the blind** person just as they did when they stole the figs, **and he judged them as one.**

Rabbi's response to Antoninus takes the form of a *mashal*: a mortal king had a beautiful garden with magnificent ripe figs, and he appointed two guardians — one lame and one blind. The lame guardian said to the blind: I can see the fine figs in the garden; come carry me, and let us take them to eat. The blind man carried the lame man, and together they ate the figs. When the owner of the garden came and asked for his figs, each party pleaded innocence: the lame man said he had no legs to walk there, the blind man said he had no eyes to see. What did the owner do? He placed the lame man back upon the blind man and judged them together as a single unit — since neither could have acted alone, and the act required their conjunction, they are judged as one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵבִיא נְשָׁמָה וְזוֹרְקָהּ בַּגּוּף, וְדָן אוֹתָם כְּאֶחָד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִקְרָא אֶל הַשָּׁמַיִם מֵעָל וְאֶל הָאָרֶץ לָדִין עַמּוֹ״. ״יִקְרָא אֶל הַשָּׁמַיִם מֵעָל״ – זוֹ נְשָׁמָה, ״וְאֶל הָאָרֶץ לָדִין עַמּוֹ״ – זֶה הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **So too, the Holy One, Blessed be He, brings the soul** on the day of judgment **and casts it** back **into the body,** as they were when they sinned, **and He judges them as one, as it is stated: "He calls to the heavens above and to the earth that He may judge His people"** (Psalms 50:4). **"He calls to the heavens above"; this is the soul,** which is heavenly. **"And to the earth that He may judge His people"; this is the body,** which is earthly.

So too God: each party alone neither sinned nor can be judged, but God will restore the soul to the body and judge them as a unified entity. The parable makes the crucial point that the very partnership that enabled the sin is what must be reconstituted for judgment.

### Heaven & Earth Verse

Rabbi concludes with a *pasuk* as proof: *yikra el hashamayim me-al ve-el ha-aretz la-din immo* — He calls to the heavens above and to the earth below, to judge His people (Tehillim 50:4). *Yikra el hashamayim me-al* refers to the soul, called down from the heavens; *ve-el ha-aretz la-din immo* refers to the body, summoned from the earth. Together — heaven and earth, soul and body — they are brought for judgment.

This *pasuk* carries an important implication that reinforces the broader framework of the *perek*: God does not judge the soul alone, nor the body alone. What is judged is the reconstituted person, the body and soul once reunited. This is the *yom ha-din* of the future time — a judgment that presupposes *techiyat hameitim* as its prerequisite. The *mashal* is thus not merely a response to Antoninus's logical puzzle about divine justice; it is simultaneously an affirmation of why *techiyat hameitim* is theologically necessary.

## Sun & Bowing

### Sun Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַנְטוֹנִינוּס לְרַבִּי: מִפְּנֵי מָה חַמָּה יוֹצְאָה בַּמִּזְרָח וְשׁוֹקַעַת בְּמַעֲרָב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוָה אִיפְּכָא נָמֵי הָכִי הֲוָה אָמְרַתְּ לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ: מִפְּנֵי מָה שׁוֹקַעַת בַּמַּעֲרָב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another exchange. **Antoninos said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **For what** reason **does the sun emerge in the east and set in the west?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: If it were the reverse, you would have also said that to me,** as the sun must emerge from one direction and set in the other. Antoninos **said to him: This is what I am saying to you: For what** reason **does** the sun **set in the west** and not occasionally deviate and set elsewhere?

After this extended discussion of body-soul unity and judgment, the *gemara* records another exchange between Antoninus and Rabbi, this one ranging into natural philosophy. Antoninus asked: why does the sun rise in the east and set in the west? Rabbi replied: if it were the reverse — rising in the west and setting in the east — you would ask the same question. This answer establishes that the direction itself is not the real question.

### Midday Arc

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן שָׁלוֹם לְקוֹנָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּצְבָא הַשָּׁמַיִם לְךָ מִשְׁתַּחֲוִים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְתֵיתֵי עַד פַּלְגָא דְרָקִיעַ, וְתִתֵּן שְׁלָמָא, וְתֵיעוֹל! מִשּׁוּם פּוֹעֲלִים וּמִשּׁוּם עוֹבְרֵי דְרָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him:** The sun always sets in the west **in order to greet its Creator, as it is stated: "And the hosts of heaven worship You"** (Nehemiah 9:6). Setting is a form of worship; it is as though the sun is bowing to God. The Divine Presence rests in the west, as is evident from the fact that the Holy of Holies in the Temple, in which the Ark, the resting place of the Divine Presence, is located in the west. Antoninos **said to him:** If so, **let** the sun **come until the midpoint of the sky,** set slightly **and greet** its Creator, and return **and enter** its place of origin in the east and set there. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi answered him: The sun sets in the west **due to workers and due to travelers,** as, if the sun did not proceed from east to west with the light of day gradually waning, they would not know that it is time to return home or to find an inn.

Antoninus pressed: that is not my question. What I am asking is: why does the sun set in the west specifically? Rashi's reading seems to suggest that the underlying question is why the sun does not simply reverse its course and return the way it came, rather than completing a full arc to the west. Antoninus sharpened this further: why does the sun not travel to the midpoint of the sky, bow toward its Creator there, and then retreat back in the direction it came? That would still accomplish the apparent purpose of showing deference — the way one properly takes leave of a king by backing out rather than turning away.

### Workers & Travel

Rabbi responded: *mishum po'alim u'mishum ovrei drachim* — on account of workers and travelers. If the sun reversed its course at midday and darkness fell in the middle of the day, workers in the field and travelers on the road would be caught without light and unable to complete their journeys or tasks. The value of the continuous arc from east to west — full daylight until a proper nightfall — is that it ensures uninterrupted productive hours. One listener raised the fair point that even if the sun reversed direction, it would not set instantly — it would gradually darken just as it gradually darkens in the ordinary course of a sunset — which suggests the question may have a slightly different nuance. The discussion on this point remains open, and the *sugya* closes there.
